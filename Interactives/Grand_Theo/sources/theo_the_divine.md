Spectacles of Change

\~Stone and Metal:

Bartop the Rock Sentinel trudged down the halls of the Temple of Memories, so slowly  
that if you were to see the poor creature, he would seem to be as solid and stationary as any great  
stone. Being slow hardly concerns Rock-Men, who live so long a human might expire before a  
limestone specimen like Bartop had time to introduce himself. He would not introduce himself,  
of course. No human has seen the inside of the Temple of Memories, with its smooth marble  
walls carved into the south face of Mount Nebo, for eons. Bartop remembers when the temple’s  
guests roamed the Seven Heavenly Corridors of the Temple, each which tells a different story  
depending on if one travels upwards or downwards.  
Brok, a little metal drone, buzzed and whizzed, while he swept the wooden floors of any  
fallen twigs. His two eyes, both the size of dinner plates, kept Brok attentive to his duty – to  
always keep the Forest Temple nice and tidy. Leaves from the vaulted ceiling of sycamores made  
perfect little boats to sail down the enchanted stream flowing through the great temple’s grounds,  
when Brok was done tending to his duties, of course. Other metal creatures roamed the  
dreamlike halls, and each had a duty, no matter how small. Brok’s hands and feet were nimble,  
and all four helped him climb the lofty heights to the highest branches of the temple’s  
magnificence.  
Mount Nebo itself was the Parallax of Life, and its majesty soared to heavenly heights.  
Beings of Light inhabit its spires, attended by faeries of Sanctified Fires.

\~The Least of Us:

Theo knew he was at a gate. He did not know the gate had a name. “The Forest Gate,” if  
Theo could open it somehow, would reveal the Chartreuse Forest in the Elysian Kingdom. The  
gate’s great redwood planks soared ten stories tall and were dwarfed by walls of alabaster stone  
that ran to Eternity in both directions.  
Pushing and shoving did nothing. Yelling did less. The impossibly ancient pales were  
fitted so tightly together, even Theo’s pinky nail failed to gain ground. In the center of the gate,  
just below eye level, was an ornate silver plate, adorned with emeralds and a keyhole in the  
center. But Theo had no key. He sat in the dirt, back to the gate, unable to imagine his way out of  
this fate.  
“Help\!”  
Theo jumped to his feet, heart in his throat.  
“Help\!”  
This time Theo yelped. Voices need a voice box, and Theo’s was not working.  
“Help\! Down here\!”  
Just a thumb’s width tall near Theo’s shoes stood a pillbug, if one can imagine a pillbug  
standing on his four back legs to ask you a question, while the ten remaining legs make the most  
mesmerizing dance of what is most likely pillbug culture’s form of hand gestures.  
Theo regained his composure and his seat, bending low to ask, “Who are you?”  
“Phil, at your service, as it were, or rather were not, since one so great needs nothing  
from one so small. I beg a pittance, good sir, if you would only lift me to the keyhole so that I  
may get back home to the Chartreuse Forest.”  
If talking pillbugs were not enough, complete sentences and favors were simply too much  
for Theo. “Uh. Um. Ummm.” With five pairs of legs opened towards the giant, Phil looked  
pitiful. “Sure, I guess.”  
“Praise the King\!”  
“The Who?” Theo gently placed the back of his hand to the dusty ground, and Phil  
crawled into the center of his palm.  
As Phil was lifted to the great heights of the keyhole, he said, “The Elysian Kingdom  
must have a King\! That is why it is called a Kingdom. Should you meet His Majesty, I beg you  
to ask for me, His favor. Thank you for aiding in my cause, Theo.” With that, Phil dashed  
through the keyhole into the Chartreuse Forest.

Theo stood confused, head tilted and eyes crossed, unaware the creature used his name.  
“Hmmph.” He reached his hand to the silver plate and gently touched one of the emeralds.  
Without sound or friction, the Invisible Door revealed its frame of Golden Light. It opened just  
enough to admit only one.  
Theo stepped through.

\~Trust in Glory:

Bartop the Rock-Man’s thoughts flow like the waters in the deep Ocean, and over  
countless ages he has traversed the forty-nine miles of the Seven Corridors in both directions  
twelve times. A niche Bartop had been passing by for several years reminded him of an  
interesting decade nine millennia ago. The ancient stone mind remembered a knight armed with  
a falcon’s claw and wearing a pentacle etched with dragon’s blood, who, hearing a legend of a  
Great Beast at the center of the Temple of Memories, sought his trophy.  
The knight searched high and low, even in the many smaller hallways, chambers, and  
passages connecting the mysteries of the Seven Corridors. He came to a niche painted with a  
Maiden kneeling in prayer, and was so enchanted by the image, he made it the center of his  
exploration. Each day he set out to find his prey, and each night he came back to the Maiden,  
sleeping under her solemn gaze. A great stone sat directly across from the Maiden’s painting. It  
was pitted and scratched, taller that it was wide, and from the right angle, resembled a man. The  
peculiar stone had no particular significance as far as the knight could see.  
“Think, Gunnin, think\!” The knight often talked to himself, a conceit left behind in  
Bartop’s youthful days. The name let the sentinel of the Temple imagine Gunnin’s path, and each  
niche, fresco, mural, and sculpture along the way. The countless signs and wonders told stories  
for those with wisdom to see.  
Bartop watched the knight with great interest. Visitors were getting rarer and rarer in  
those days. While the little human scurried about during the day, the caretaker of the Temple of  
Memories contemplated the Maiden, the recesses of his eyes never changing. At night, he  
watched the man sleep. Bartop could not imagine the dreams of a knight, whose thoughts must  
trickle like a bubbly spring that only flows during a spring thaw. Adventures took Gunnin with  
his falcon’s claw and dragon’s blood all through the Temple. By the time he searched all Seven

Corridors in both directions, his hair was beginning to gray and his face was lined with worry.  
The aging knight spent time reading a book of his people’s Wisdom, and when his sight began to  
fail, he fashioned a pair of spectacles from the wire frame of the dragon’s blood pentacle. One  
lens was of sea glass and the other of pale moonstone. Treasures untold could be found  
throughout the Temple, but these were tokens won by Gunnin’s might and prowess long ago.  
Time took the knight’s youth and vitality. The days spent staring at the Maiden  
outnumbered the days hunting a Beast whose existence he doubted. At sunset on the Day of the  
Meteor, the old knight knelt, as if in prayer, his right hand extended to touch the Maiden. The  
next morning, Bartop began the long process of carrying Gunnin to his final rest, after placing  
the priceless spectacles at the Maiden’s feet.

\~Hospitality Found:

The leaves in the Chartreuse Forest shine a kaleidoscope of green upon great and small.  
Theo could barely stay on the subtly worn path through the soft grass and sage-moss of the forest  
floor, as his eyes spied deep emerald trellises, bright jade leaves, and the sparkling Chartreuse  
colored buds twinkling like living stars on every ancient branch. The young man’s path led to a  
fork in the road, and at the fork stood a little house in disrepair, or a very nice shack, depending  
on one’s point of view. Theo knocked on the door, but he was not sure if he had decided to knock  
on the door. As the knob turned, Theo assumed the Elysian Kingdom simply took some getting  
used to. Then the door opened.  
Mr. and Mrs. Lock both stood in the doorway with disapproving looks on their furry  
faces. The couple were badgers, you see. When standing on his hind legs, Mr. Lock stood about  
as tall as a short man, which is why he adopted the habit of wearing big purple boots while  
meeting strangers at his door.  
“What do you want?” Husband and wife said this in such perfect unison, significant  
practice could have been the only cause.  
“Well, uh, I just got here and I’m lost. And um. I’m Theo by the way. It’s. It’s nice to  
meet you.” Theo, not quite full grown, seemed nervous, but he got the words out and even  
managed a timid grin.

Both badgers smiled. “I see, young man. The forest is an enchanting place, but not  
without its dangers. Come in, come in, we haven’t much, and none to share.” Mrs. Lock gestured  
for Theo to follow behind, as Mr. Lock led the way into their home.  
Theo’s senses were returning to normal or adjusting to this strange new world, but he  
could not tell which. The smile of a badger, he had just learned, was not a comforting sign of  
reassurance. However, a roof over anything makes an inside, which is exactly what Theo needed.  
He followed the odd couple until Mr. Lock gestured for the boy to sit on the floor. Mrs. Lock  
took her seat on a rickety old rocking chair, broken bits repaired with the everpresent foliage of  
the Chartreuse Forest. Mr. Lock sat in a sturdy little chair of his own construction, pieced  
together from the precious few limbs that fall from the sacred trees. The one room of the shack  
contained a small stool and a bamboo mat under Mr. Lock’s feet. An old cupboard along the far  
wall was the only fine thing in the home of the badger family.  
Everyone was silent for an agonizingly long time. Theo took the opportunity to formulate  
all sorts of questions, but he dare not speak. Mr. Lock only glanced at the boy occasionally, and  
otherwise gazed out the window or at his wife. Mrs. Lock was absorbed in her knitting, but often  
looked up to smile at Theo, which did not improve his anxiety.  
After precisely one hour passed since Mr. Lock had taken his seat, and just as Theo began  
to purse his lips as if to speak, the serious old badger said, “Well, I suppose you want to know if  
you should take the left path or the right path?”  
“Well, yes sir, I suppose I do.”  
“Supposedness is not what I asked for, but I will tell you which way to go for my own  
purpose. Take the left path. And good day, young man.” Mr. Lock gestured to the door.  
Puzzled, Theo stood and politely nodded. “Thank you both.” Then he left the abode of  
Mr. and Mrs. Lock. Unbeknownst to all three of them, Phil the pillbug was nestled in Mrs.  
Lock’s favorite potted plant, digesting her most splendid and gracious meal.

\~Into Life:

Brok, the busy metal man, was using his big keen eyes to collect each and every perfectly  
golden dandelion petal that occasionally fell from the Chapel of Gold and Silver’s heights. He  
was far, far below such a high and important place. On his favorite clover patch just outside the

Forest Temple’s northwest entrance, Brok spied something in the distance more curious than the  
precious petals. He saw a young man dressed in a red tunic and rough, brown pants, coming up  
the road. It would be some time before the youth would reach Brok, so he waited and watched.  
The traveler did not appear to be a knight, so perhaps he was a pilgrim? Brok had not seen a  
pilgrim in so long he had forgotten the protocols. Even the Rock-Men in the Temple of  
Memories took their own time to remember the faces of visitors. A simple, black leather belt and  
soft, lamb-skin slippers were the only other adornments Brok’s great eyes could see.  
As Theo approached the northwest gate of the Forest Temple, his mind retreated as the  
grandeur of something so unimaginable presented itself to him. The Temple, you see, is not a  
building of stone or even of wood. It is a grand structure cultivated by the most beautiful trees in  
the most sacred grove of the Chartreuse Forest. Theo walked uphill into the Pilgrim’s Glade,  
which held multitudes of pilgrims in its open expanse so long ago. His jaw was slack and mouth  
open as he beheld the Forest Temple. Such trees exist nowhere else in creation. Thick,  
gray-silver trunks rise and fork, twist and meet. The trunks become great branches, and branches  
become stems. Every Chapel, Hall, Palace, and room in the Forest Temple is not made of walls,  
but of Nature converging to create Space.  
The frame of the northwest entrance is a great, slender archway rising the height of  
twelve men and made of Starwood, which reflects the light of the Moon. Theo did not know if he  
could enter.  
“Hi there.”  
Small creatures appearing from nowhere appear to be a feature of the Elysian Kingdom,  
thought Theo, as he collected his composure once again. A small metal man, half Theo’s height,  
with seven-fingered hands and six-fingered feet, had darted out from behind a lush wall of ivy  
near the frame of the northwest door. “Uh. Hi?”  
“The protocols for pilgrims are not active. What do you seek?”  
“A badger. Oh\! I mean, Mr. Lock sent me this way.”  
For the first time in a long time, Brok did not know what to think. He stood, as if  
transfixed, for thirty-three seconds. His dinner plate eyes blinked twice in the process, each blink  
requiring five seconds. “Come with me.” Theo followed his little metal guide through the  
northwestern entrance of the Forest Temple during the New Moon. He exited the southeastern  
passage the second Full Moons after.

\~Above and Below

Once upon a time, whether in the future or past only the Wise know, a young girl stood at  
a fork in the road of her journey. To the left, the path pointed up to heights unseen, trees with  
rough, rocky bark subtly marking out the path of ascent. To the right, the path went down into  
depths unknown, with warm shadows of deep green and earthen brown cast by the golden light  
filtered through the canopy so high above.  
At the juncture was an ancient elm ringed by everyoung pines. The Chapel-elm emerged  
from the ground in the center of the ring, its width stretching out to meet the tops of the pines.  
During the quarter-moon, the leaves were yellow and gold, the oldest of which only hinted at the  
browns and oranges to come. Legends, or prophecies, depending on who tells the tale, speak of  
the girl visiting with the keepers of the Chapel at the Fork. The husband and wife, ever cautious,  
give fitting guidance to those who ask.  
After the pleasantries and with the utmost of politeness, the maiden was escorted by the  
couple to a comfortable spot – an alcove in the base of the great tree in which all three could sit  
comfortably. The gracious smiles of the Tenders to the Chapel warmed the girl’s heart and eased  
her mind. She humbly asked, “Dear Keepers, I am lost in this enchanted place\! The paths up and  
down both call to me, and yet this place is so serene, I could spend my days here as your servant  
with no regret. Tell me, friends, what should I do?”  
The wife spoke: “Brave girl, seek inside yourself and travel up, or travel down to serve.  
Do not stay here long, although your charming countenance might keep us company for a  
fortnight. If you seek counsel, take the downward path, and tend your garden there.” Her  
husband smiled a soft smile at the maiden, and he nodded.  
After two weeks learning ancient songs and even more ancient rites, the young girl took  
the path down into the belly of the Chartreuse Forest.

\~Gifts and Boons

Theo’s long stay in the Forest Temple began as a dream. He walked through living  
palaces where enchanted flowers sang and crisp drops of honey-water dripped from a hanging

ceiling of wonder. He slept on a bed of the softest roots in the womb of one of the Temple’s  
many crypts below its countless places of worship. Theo’s eyes were forced open by the  
magnificence of his surroundings. Then he awoke.  
Mapping out even a fraction of the Temple would be impossible for a human, but Theo  
tried. He contemplated his position at the center of a crossroads. Four choices, all reasonable.  
Clank\! After many days of dreamlike bliss, Theo was reminded that the Elysian Kingdom  
had played such tricks before. After several minutes, when his heart was beating at its proper  
rate, Theo glanced back. On the crossroads center lay three staves: ash, willow, and yew,  
arranged like a wheel on its side. Each was ordinary, and were exactly as long as Theo was tall.  
Perplexed, he took a step.  
“We must keep the Temple tidy\!” Before Theo could think, Brok buzzed by, grabbed up  
the staves, and hopped away on his one unencumbered leg. The now slightly annoyed Theo  
decided he was not surprised, but determined to get those staves and set out for his Prize.  
Hunting is not the same as mapping. Theo hunted for Brok three days and three nights.  
He slept in cozy branches wherever the day led, and looked carefully both ways, anywhere he  
tread. Theo’s wide eyes spotted a sycamore leaf, oddly folded, sailing down the stream near his  
feet. He plucked it out of the cold, clear stream and wondered. Upstream is where he found Brok.  
Quiet as Theo could be, he crept to the edge of a grove at the source of the stream, which  
bubbled up from one of the many deep places in the Temple. At the stream’s edge, Brok was  
neatly folding another leaf into a little boat. The metal man’s saucer eyes were transfixed on his  
novelty when Theo pounced.  
“ALERT\!” Brok’s voice pierced the young man’s ears, and in short order, Theo was  
pinned to the ground by all four of the machine’s nimble yet powerful limbs. “We must keep the  
Temple tidy, yes?”  
“Yes,” Theo whined.  
“YOU will keep the temple tidy, yes?”  
“Yes, ouch, yes. I’ll need your help, though, because I do not know how.”  
Brok would have been confused, if he were capable of such a thing. He calculated for  
several minutes, and said, “Yes.”  
Theo spent the remainder of his days and nights following Brok and keeping the Temple  
tidy. The splendor of the Temple returned to his eyes, while he fulfilled his promise to Brok. The

only thing he lacked, thought Theo, was someone to talk to. “We must keep the Temple tidy,” as  
response to any query did not count as conversation in Theo’s reckoning. On the morning of the  
second Full Moon after Theo’s arrival, he followed his metal companion to the southeastern  
passage.  
“Southeastern passage to Chartreuse Forest. Down and up to southwest slope of Mount  
Nebo. Please take one.” Brok’s voice, ever monotonous, had surprised Theo for the first time  
since his ill-fated hunt. He looked to the floor of the circle marking the entrance to the  
southeastern passage and saw his Prize. Staves of ash, willow, and yew. Take one. He picked up  
each in turn, and each would suffice as walking staff or worse in a pinch. The yew staff, pale  
yellow-brown and more flexible than the others, called to Theo.  
“Thank you, Brok. I choose this one.” The young man smiled at his unusual friend. Then  
he turned and took a step.  
“Wait.”  
Theo turned, and the little metal man slowly walked to his helper. Brok held in the palm  
of his right hand a leather pouch. The ancient eyes appeared as those of a great falcon, and Theo  
knew it was a gift. He took the pouch, and they parted ways. In his eons tidying up the Forest  
Temple, Brok had seen many treasures, and he remembered where each was hidden. The pouch  
was made from the hide of Hafthor the Yak-God, and its inside was lined with the fur of the  
flying goats who climb the summits of Mt. Nebo’s northeastern cliffs. As he left the Forest  
Temple through the southeastern passage, he tied the pouch to his belt and walked with his staff  
into the ravines leading to the center of all things.

\~Memories and Duties

All Seven Corridors in the Temple of Memories, near their highest points, allow access to  
the Porch of Sacred Fire, a plateau above the Temple, carved into the side of the mountain and  
plated in bronze. When the Sun begins His descent, golden light from behind Mt. Nebo’s most  
sacred peaks bathes the bronze expanse and the entire southeast timberline of Chartreuse Forest  
in Sacred Fire, as a group of Holy Nuns on pilgrimage named the phenomena in a time forgotten  
by most. Once every few thousand years, Bartop travels to the hidden places at the far reaches of  
the Seven Corridors, and with unbounded memory, performs the rites to access the Porch. He

stands at the edge and remembers the line of devoted women making their way to the Parallax,  
but never learned if they completed their journey. As the sacred fire turned to a red dusk, a bat  
landed on the Rock-Man’s shoulder and told him of signs and portents new in the world since  
Bartop’s last visit to the Porch of Sacred Fire. The creature squeaked that the Elysian Kingdom  
had a visitor. The first one in a very long time.  
When Bartop was young and the pillars of Mt. Nebo were newly settled into place, the  
artisan of stone was quick, if not agile. His kind were tasked with carving the grottos and the  
network of chambers between the Seven Corridors. The Rock-Man’s hands ground pillars and  
arches into being which his brethren would smooth and soften with a more delicate touch. When  
his work day was done, Bartop visited the Corridors, adding their symbols to his memory. He  
walked the Seven Corridors, and by the time his first millennium of life passed, he had tread all  
forty-nine miles upwards and downwards. In the deepest darkness of the innermost Corridor,  
Bartop found an already ancient grotto with a deep, black pool. He walked and sank to the  
bottom to discover a sphere of starstone, emitting its holy light in the darkness. When it was time  
to perform his duties, Bartop thought, I would rather stay here and learn the secrets of the  
Starstone. And so he did.  
A century later, when the water-laden laborer of limestone emerged from the dark pool,  
he was sluggish and slow, even by the standards of the Rock-Men. His brothers and sisters  
finished their work, as Bartop trekked his way up and down the Seven Corridors, contemplating  
their Secrets. Now at the height of the Temple of Memories and all of its wonders, the solemn  
keeper of memories waited.

\~Illusion and Reality

Mack was slim and sharp, dressed in a yellow vest and lime green pants. A pocket-watch  
dangled from his pocket, and he held a staff, but did not seem to need it for balance. In one of the  
countless ravines scratched along the southwest expanse of Mount Nebo’s ridgeline, Mack gazed  
into a pool fed by a crystal clear stream. “Fool or sage, they say\! Serpent or dove? Tricky  
questions, indeed, old chap.” Quick as lightning, the vulpine linguist shed his persona and  
donned his leonine aspect and struck the surface of the pool with his staff just hard enough to

cause a splash. His physical eyes soon refocused on lion’s eyes staring back at him from the  
deep.  
The ears of a lion are almost as good as those of a fox, and Mack heard something –  
someone? – approaching loudly from the northwest. With catlike quiet, Mack sank into the  
shadows, firm to the ground. Noticing is planning, and Mack was a noticer before he was a  
planner, making him a quadruple favorite against poor Theo in a match of almost any imagining.  
By the time Theo followed the crystal stream to Mack’s pool, a stage was set.  
“And low he’s high, a friend to all, our hero, protector of the small–.” Mack’s  
mesmerizing singing voice cut off suddenly when Theo came plodding near. “Oh\! Pardon, sir.”  
Theo was confused but not afraid, and not startled, as had so often been the case with  
pillbugs and metal men. Yew staff in hand, and walking carefully every step of the way, Theo  
had seen the odd fellow singing while sitting on a big red mushroom, oblivious to our  
adventurer’s approach. “No need for pardon,” said Theo, graciously. Then he paused.  
“Thank you dear sir\! I don’t mean to be pitiful, but times are hard. I’m not against asking,  
but you’re a young lad with only a staff to your name. As you see, I’m old and infirm, but I do  
have my staff, and it’s a good one.”  
“Well I don’t only have a staff.” Theo nodded his head towards Mack.  
“Praise the King\! You must be a generous adventurer indeed. I do see you have a nice  
leather pouch. How about this? Donate your riches in the pouch to this poor beggar, or if that be  
too much, donate the pouch instead.”  
Later on, with pouch in hand, Mack sang his tune with a fox’s smile on his face, “And  
low he’s high, a friend to all, our hero, protector of the small.” Noticing makes for good  
planning. “Young men want to be heroes and protectors of old beggars at ponds. Give them what  
they want, old chap,” said the Master of Illusions, to himself. Mack traveled to a place where his  
perception of the real and mastery of illusion was inverted, seeing through illusion while  
enchanted by reality. As time gave way to dream, and as the Chartreuse Forest gave way to the  
gentle, sloping terrain near Mt. Nebo’s southface, Mack found his way to a cottage in the midst  
of a garden, between three little hills. A simple purple tunic, green pants, and his willow staff,  
was Mack’s attire when he knocked on the door of the cottage. Eventually, he heard rustling  
inside. “My Lady, I have a gift for you\!”

The rustling stopped, and the Maiden opened the door. “Good Sir, how are you? And  
gifts you say?” She smiled into Mack’s crystal eyes. “From a friend or a neighbor?”  
“Not gifts, my love\! A single gift. It is not a chess piece nor a bribe. It was given to me  
freely and I give it to you freely, if you would have it.” The face of a man can be a fox, or a lion,  
or sometimes sincere. Mack’s true guise could be all three, and he would never lie to the Maiden.  
“Only one, ‘my’ love? My home has room enough for us, and three days walk to the  
Temple of Memories. Settle in this Kingdom, and you will be my husband, for my heart is here. I  
accept your gift, dear friend, as you knew I would.”  
Mack tossed the pouch of Hafthor the Yak-God to his beloved. “Never only one, but only  
one today.” He smiled, then sighed. “My heart lies beyond the lands of the goats whose fur lines  
that gentle mortar in your hands. A dainty thing for you, or it might make a wonderful gift. I will  
find another.”  
“A gift and a goodbye, then?”  
“No, Love. I beseech your blessing as always. And if you see the King, give him my  
kindest regards.” Mack’s eyes were the pale green of the endless lands east of Mt. Nebo, where  
mysteries are deep in slumber.  
“You have it, Master, as always.”
