Key Concepts

Markup on Cost

A common pricing method where a percentage is added to the direct cost of a product to determine its selling price. This markup covers operating expenses and generates profit.

Successive Markdowns

Applying multiple discounts sequentially to an item's price. It's crucial to understand that these discounts are applied to the reduced price at each step, not to the original price cumulatively.

Important Terms (1/3)

Markup

The amount added to the cost of a product to arrive at its selling price.

Markup rate on cost

The markup amount expressed as a percentage of the product's cost.

Markdown

A reduction in the original selling price of an item, typically used for sales or clearance.

Successive Markdowns

Multiple price reductions applied one after another, each to the previously reduced price.

Fixed Costs

Business expenses that remain constant regardless of the volume of goods or services produced.

Important Terms (2/3)

Variable Costs

Business expenses that change directly in proportion to the volume of goods or services produced.

Break-Even Point

The level of sales (in units or revenue) at which total costs equal total revenue, meaning a business neither makes a profit nor incurs a loss.

Contribution Margin

The difference between the selling price per unit and the variable cost per unit. This amount contributes to covering fixed costs and generating profit.

Profit

The financial gain made when total revenue exceeds total costs.

Supply Chain

The entire network of businesses and activities involved in creating and delivering a product or service to a consumer.

Important Terms (3/3)

High-low pricing

A retail strategy where prices are initially set high, followed by frequent sales and promotions to attract customers.

Everyday Low Pricing (EDLP)

A retail strategy offering consistently low prices without frequent sales events, often used by warehouse clubs.

Direct-to-Consumer (DTC)

A business model where a company sells its products directly to end-consumers, bypassing traditional intermediaries like distributors and retailers.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the primary challenge businesses face when making pricing decisions?

The challenge is setting a price that is not too high (driving customers away) and not too low (failing to cover costs and generate profit).

How do you calculate the selling price of an item given its cost and a markup rate on cost?

Selling Price = Cost × (1 + markup rate)

Jamal buys T-shirts for $14.00 each and uses a 60% markup on cost. What is the retail price and the markup amount per shirt?

Retail Price = $14.00 × (1 + 0.60) = $22.40. Markup Amount = $22.40 - $14.00 = $8.40.

What is a 'markdown' in retail pricing, and when is it typically used?

A pair of shoes originally priced at $110.00 receives a 25% markdown, and then an additional 15% off the reduced price. What is the final clearance price?

First markdown: $110.00 × (1 - 0.25) = $82.50. Second markdown: $82.50 × (1 - 0.15) = $70.13.

What is the 'break-even point' for a business?

The break-even point is the level of sales (in units or revenue) where total revenue equals total costs, resulting in zero profit and zero loss.

What formula is used to calculate the break-even quantity (Q)?

Q = Fixed Costs / (Price per unit - Variable Cost per unit)

Maria's Tacos has fixed monthly costs of $3,800. Each taco costs $1.75 in variable costs and sells for $5.50. How many tacos must Maria sell to break even?

Q = $3,800 / ($5.50 - $1.75) = $3,800 / $3.75 = 1,014 tacos (rounded up).
