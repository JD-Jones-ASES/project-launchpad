Linear Inequalities in Two Variables

Unlike equations, inequalities represent a range of solutions, graphically shown as a shaded region on a coordinate plane.

Graphing a Single Linear Inequality

Involves graphing a boundary line (solid for $\le, \ge$; dashed for $<, >$) and using a test point (like the origin) to determine which side of the line to shade.

Important Terms (1/2)

Linear Inequality in Two Variables

A mathematical statement in the form $ax + by < c$, $ax + by \le c$, $ax + by > c$, or $ax + by \ge c$, where $a, b, c$ are real numbers and $x, y$ are variables.

Solution (of an Inequality)

Any ordered pair $(x, y)$ that makes the inequality true.

System of Linear Inequalities

Two or more linear inequalities considered together.

Feasible Region

The set of all points that satisfy every inequality in a system simultaneously; it is the solution set for the system.

Corner Point (Vertex)

A point on the boundary of the feasible region where two or more boundary lines intersect.

Important Terms (2/2)

Objective Function

A linear expression, typically $Z = ax + by$, that a linear programming problem seeks to maximize or minimize.

Linear Programming

A mathematical technique used to determine the optimal allocation of resources by maximizing or minimizing a linear objective function subject to linear constraints.

Non-negativity Constraints

The inequalities $x \ge 0$ and $y \ge 0$, which ensure that decision variables represent non-negative quantities, commonly used in real-world applications.
