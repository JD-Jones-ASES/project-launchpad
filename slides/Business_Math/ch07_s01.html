Key Concepts

Depreciation

The process by which an asset's value decreases over time due to wear and tear, obsolescence, or market factors. Understanding depreciation is crucial for business accounting and tax planning.

Linear Decrease (Straight-Line Depreciation)

A depreciation model where an asset loses the same, constant dollar amount of value in each equal time period. When graphed, the value over time forms a straight, downward-sloping line.

Book Value

The current value of an asset as recorded in a company's accounting records, calculated as the original cost minus the total accumulated depreciation taken to date.

Important Terms (1/3)

Depreciation

The decrease in value of an asset over time.

Linear Decrease (Straight-Line Depreciation)

A method of depreciation where the asset loses a constant dollar amount each period.

Exponential Decrease (Declining-Balance Depreciation)

A method of depreciation where the asset loses a fixed percentage of its current value each period.

Original (Initial) Value ($V_0$)

The cost of the asset when it was new.

Salvage Value ($V_{end}$)

The estimated value of an asset at the end of its useful life, after all depreciation has been accounted for.

Important Terms (2/3)

Depreciation Amount ($d$)

The constant dollar amount an asset loses each period in linear depreciation.

Rate of Decrease ($r$)

The constant percentage an asset loses each period in exponential depreciation (expressed as a decimal).

Decay Factor

In exponential decrease, the term $(1-r)$, which represents the percentage of the asset's value that remains after one period.

MACRS (Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System)

The specific depreciation method required by the IRS for most business assets for tax purposes.

Property Classes

Categories defined by the IRS for MACRS, determining the number of years over which an asset can be depreciated (e.g., 5-year property, 7-year property).

Exponential Decrease (Declining-Balance Depreciation)

Definition

A quantity decreases exponentially when it loses a fixed percent of its current value each period.

Key Characteristic

The *dollar amount* of the decrease gets smaller over time, even though the *percent* loss remains constant.

Formula

Decay Factor

The expression $(1 - r)$ is known as the decay factor. It represents the proportion of value remaining after one period.

Comparing Linear and Exponential Decrease

Linear Decrease

"Loses $$X$ per year" means a constant *dollar amount* is subtracted annually.

Exponential Decrease

"Loses Y% per year" means a constant *percentage* is applied to the *current value*, leading to a changing dollar amount of depreciation.

Reading Depreciation Tables (MACRS) (1/2)

Purpose

MACRS provides a structured way to depreciate assets for tax deductions.

How it Works

The IRS categorizes assets into property classes (e.g., 3-year, 5-year, 7-year) and provides tables with specific percentages for each year of depreciation.
