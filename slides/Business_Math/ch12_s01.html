Sinking Fund

A dedicated fund created by making regular deposits over time to accumulate a specific amount of money by a future date, typically for a large expense or debt.

Important Terms (1/2)

Exponential Growth

An increase in a quantity over time, where the rate of increase is proportional to the current amount, leading to faster growth as the quantity gets larger.

Growth Factor

In compound growth, the multiplier `(1 + r)` that indicates how much the principal grows by each period, where `r` is the growth rate as a decimal.

Rule of 72

An approximation method to find the doubling time of an investment by dividing 72 by the annual interest rate (expressed as a whole number percentage).

Annuity

A series of fixed payments or receipts occurring at regular intervals for a defined period.

Ordinary Annuity

An annuity where payments are made at the end of each period. This is the default assumption unless stated otherwise.

Important Terms (2/2)

Annuity Due

An annuity where payments are made at the beginning of each period. These typically accumulate slightly more interest than ordinary annuities.

Future Value (FV)

The value of an investment or an annuity at a specific point in the future, considering the effects of compound interest.

Present Value (PV)

The current worth of a future sum of money or a series of future payments, discounted at a specific rate of return.

Time Value of Money

The economic principle that a dollar today is worth more than a dollar in the future because a dollar today can be invested and earn interest.

Sinking Fund

A financial mechanism where regular contributions are made into a fund to accumulate a target amount of money by a specific future date, often for planned expenditures or debt repayment.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What two powerful financial planning concepts are introduced as extensions of compound interest?

Annuities and Present Value.

What is the formula for the future value of a single lump sum undergoing compound growth?

A = P(1 + r)^t, where A = future value, P = present value, r = growth rate per period (as a decimal), and t = number of periods.

In the compound growth formula, what does the quantity (1 + r) represent?

It is called the growth factor, meaning the amount is multiplied by this factor each period.

A business earns $200,000 in revenue this year and expects 4% annual growth. What will its revenue be in 6 years?

A = $200,000(1.04)^6 = $200,000 1.265319 = $253,063.82.

Explain the Rule of 72.

The Rule of 72 is an estimation method to determine how long it takes for money to double, by dividing 72 by the annual interest rate (as a whole number).

What is the formula for the Rule of 72?

Doubling time = 72 / rate (as a percent).

Key Concepts (2/2)

Using the Rule of 72, approximately how long will it take $5,000 to grow to $10,000 at an 8% annual interest rate?

Doubling time = 72 / 8 = 9 years.

Define an annuity.

An annuity is a series of equal payments made at equal time intervals.

What is the difference between an ordinary annuity and an annuity due?

An ordinary annuity has payments made at the end of each period, while an annuity due has payments made at the beginning of each period.
