Application of Financial Formulas

The core idea is to use formulas from Section 12.1 (Future Value, Present Value, Annuity, Sinking Fund) to make informed decisions about saving, investing, and comparing financial options.

Present Value of an Annuity

The current value of a series of equal payments to be received or paid in the future. This concept is vital for comparing an ongoing stream of income (like rent) to a lump sum amount available today.

Future Value of an Annuity

The total value of a series of equal payments accumulated over time, including interest. This is used to determine how much a consistent saving plan will yield by a future date.

Important Terms (1/2)

Future Value (FV)

The value of an asset or money at a specified date in the future, assuming a certain rate of return or interest.

Present Value (PV)

The current value of a future sum of money or stream of cash flows, given a specified rate of return.

Annuity

A series of equal payments made or received at regular intervals over a period of time.

Sinking Fund

A fund created by setting aside money at regular intervals in order to pay off a future obligation (e.g., a debt, a capital expenditure, or retirement).

Compound Interest

Interest calculated on the initial principal and also on the accumulated interest of previous periods. It’s "interest on interest."

Important Terms (2/2)

Compounding Period

The frequency at which interest is calculated and added to the principal (e.g., monthly, quarterly, annually).

Quick Practice (Future Value of Annuity Proportionality)

Scenario

If David deposited $300/month instead of $200/month.

Answer

Since the Future Value of an Annuity formula is directly proportional to the payment amount (PMT), you can simply multiply David's original FV by the ratio of new PMT to old PMT ($300/$200 = 1.5).
