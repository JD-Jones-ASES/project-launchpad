Important Terms (1/3)

Metaphysics (Book XII/Lambda)

The specific book within Aristotle's Metaphysics where the concept of the Unmoved Mover is most fully developed. Scholars refer to it by the Greek letter Lambda.

First Philosophy

Aristotle's term for the highest science, which studies being as being and seeks the first causes and principles of all things; later known as metaphysics.

Being as Being

The study of existence in its most fundamental and universal sense, not just specific kinds of being.

Substance

That which underlies accidents and can exist independently; the primary kind of being. In Aristotle's view, form is the primary sense of substance.

Form

The essential nature or structure of a thing, what makes it the kind of thing it is, distinct from its matter.

Important Terms (2/3)

Actuality

The state of being real, fully realized, or in act; the fulfillment of potentiality.

Potentiality

The capacity or power of something to become something else, to change or develop.

Pure Actuality (Actus Purus)

A being that has no unrealized potential, no capacity for change, and no matter. This describes Aristotle's Unmoved Mover.

Unmoved Mover (First Mover)

The eternal, immaterial, purely actual being that is the ultimate cause of all motion and change in the universe, without itself being moved.

Efficient Causation

The agent or force that brings something about (e.g., a sculptor making a statue).

Important Terms (3/3)

Final Causation

The purpose or goal for which something is done; the end toward which a thing strives (e.g., health is the final cause of medicine). The Unmoved Mover acts as a final cause.

Theoria

Greek term for contemplation, intellectual inquiry, or theoretical understanding. Aristotle considers it the highest form of human activity and the sole activity of the divine.

The Argument for the Existence of the Unmoved Mover

Observation of Motion and Change

The observable world is characterized by constant motion and change (things coming into being, passing away, moving, growing, decaying).

Principle of Causation

Every instance of motion requires a cause.

Avoidance of Infinite Regress

If every cause of motion is itself in motion, this would lead to an infinite regress of causes, which Aristotle deems impossible.

Conclusion

Therefore, there must be a first cause of all motion that is itself unmoved. This is an eternal, immaterial substance. It is the root cause holding everything in existence and order.

The Life of the Divine (Reading 6.3)

Pure Activity

As pure actuality, the First Mover's existence is one of continuous, eternal activity. It cannot sleep, rest, or wait.

Highest Possible Activity

Given its supreme status, its activity must be the highest possible activity. Aristotle identifies this as theoria (intellectual contemplation).

Thought Thinking Itself

God as Living, Eternal, Most Good

Aristotle describes God (the Unmoved Mover) as a living being whose life is the eternal actuality of thought, a continuous and eternal state of supreme goodness and pleasure.

Key Concepts (1/2)

Which book of Aristotle's Metaphysics is considered the most celebrated and theologically significant, acting as a culmination of his earlier arguments?

Book XII, also known as Lambda.

What fundamental principle, established in Book I of the Metaphysics, drives Aristotle's search for the ultimate causes?

Wisdom seeks the first causes and principles of all things.

According to Aristotle's argument in Metaphysics Book VII, what is the primary sense of substance?

Form is the primary sense of substance.

What key idea from Metaphysics Book IX is crucial for understanding the nature of Aristotle's First Mover?

Actuality is prior to potentiality, and being is fundamentally energetic and active.

What observable phenomenon in the world is the starting point for Aristotle's argument for the existence of a First Mover?

The existence of motion and change in the observable world.

What logical conclusion does Aristotle draw regarding the cause of motion that leads to the First Mover?

Motion requires a cause, and the regress of causes cannot go on forever; thus, there must be a first cause of all motion that is itself unmoved.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How does Aristotle fundamentally describe the First Mover in terms of its nature?

It is an eternal, immaterial, unmoved substance.

What are the three kinds of substance that Aristotle distinguishes at the beginning of Metaphysics Book XII?

Perishable substances (e.g., plants, animals), eternal substances that are in motion (e.g., heavenly bodies), and an eternal substance that is itself unmoved...

Which types of substance are the subject of 'physics' according to Aristotle, and why?

Perishable and eternal moving substances, because they both imply movement.

Why must the First Mover be described as 'pure actuality' (actus purus)?

If it had any potentiality or unrealized capacity, something prior would be needed to actualize it, contradicting its nature as the first mover.

What does being 'pure actuality' imply about the First Mover's composition and potential?

It has no matter, no potentiality, no unrealized capacity, and no composition of any kind. It simply and eternally is what it is.
