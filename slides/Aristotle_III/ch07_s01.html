Key Concepts (1/3)

Eudaimonia (Flourishing Life)

Aristotle's concept of the good life, not as a feeling but as an activity performed in accordance with human nature, characterized by virtue, friendship, and intellectual contemplation (theoria).

Theoria (Intellectual Contemplation)

For Aristotle, the highest human activity, involving the exercise of reason on the highest objects, most closely resembling the life of the divine and leading to the happiest life.

Politikon Zōon (Political Animal)

Aristotle's view of human nature, asserting that humans are naturally suited for self-governing political community (the polis), which provides the conditions for living virtuously.

Mimesis (Creative Representation)

The fundamental nature of art for Aristotle, particularly in the Poetics, involving not mere copying but a transformative representation of human action that reveals universal truths.

Katharsis

The purgation or purification of pity and fear experienced by an audience during a well-constructed tragedy.

Key Concepts (2/3)

Ethos, Pathos, Logos

The three modes of persuasion in rhetoric: appeal through the speaker's trustworthy character (ethos), the audience's emotions (pathos), and reasoned argument (logos).

On he on (Being as Being)

The subject of "first philosophy" (Metaphysics), which seeks to understand what is true of everything that exists simply because it exists, rather than a particular kind of being.

Ousia (Substance)

The primary kind of being, identified by Aristotle as the fundamental "things" that possess properties and undergo processes, distinct from qualities or relations.

Dynamis (Potentiality) and Energeia (Actuality)

Twin concepts used to explain change without contradiction, where potentiality refers to what something can be, and actuality refers to what something is.

Unmoved Mover (Noesis Noeseos)

Aristotle's ultimate first cause, a being of pure actuality whose eternal self-contemplation ("thought thinking thought") is the first principle of all motion and reality.

Key Concepts (3/3)

The Four Causes

Aristotle's framework for complete explanation: Material (what it's made of), Formal (its structure/form), Efficient (what brought it about), and Final (what it's for, its end).

Unity of Aristotle's Inquiry

The overarching idea that all of Aristotle's works, from ethics and politics to art, rhetoric, and metaphysics, form a cohesive system exploring human nature, the world, and the divine.

Principle of Non-Contradiction

The most certain of all truths: nothing can both be and not be at the same time and in the same respect. It is a foundational principle of both logic and reality.
