Important Terms (1/3)

Al-Fārābī (Alpharabius)

An influential Islamic philosopher (c. 872–950) who synthesized Aristotelian and Platonic philosophy, impacting later Islamic thinkers.

Ibn Sīnā (Avicenna)

A Persian philosopher and physician (980–1037) whose encyclopedic work, *The Book of Healing*, built on Aristotelian foundations and influenced both Islamic and Christian scholasticism.

Ibn Rushd (Averroes)

A philosopher from Muslim Spain (1126–1198), known as "the Commentator" for his exhaustive commentaries on Aristotle, which were instrumental in transmitting Aristotle's thought to the Latin West.

"The Philosopher"

The honorific title given to Aristotle in the medieval Latin West, signifying his supreme authority in philosophical matters.

_Noesis Noeseos_

Aristotle's concept of God as "thought thinking itself," a self-contemplating deity, which created tension with the Abrahamic concept of a personal God.

Important Terms (2/3)

_Gratia non tollit naturam sed perficit_

A foundational principle of Thomas Aquinas, meaning "Grace does not destroy nature, but perfects it." It reconciled faith and reason by positing a harmonious, hierarchical relationship between them.

_Il maestro di color che sanno_

Dante's famous description of Aristotle in *Inferno IV.131*, meaning "the master of those who know," acknowledging his supreme intellectual achievement based on natural reason.

_School of Athens_

A fresco by Raphael (c. 1511) that symbolically places Plato and Aristotle at its center, illustrating their fundamental philosophical differences through their gestures (Plato pointing up, Aristot...

_Hamartia_

An Aristotelian concept referring to a tragic flaw, error of judgment, or failure of practical wisdom in a protagonist, leading to catastrophe in a tragedy.

_The Name of the Rose_

A 1980 novel by Umberto Eco, which uses the lost second book of Aristotle's *Poetics* (on comedy) as a central plot device, highlighting Aristotle's immense cultural authority.

Important Terms (3/3)

_Human Telos_

Aristotle's concept of the natural end or purpose of human beings, which G. E. M. Anscombe argued was essential for a coherent moral philosophy.

G. E. M. Anscombe

A British philosopher (1919–2001) whose 1958 essay "Modern Moral Philosophy" critically revived Aristotelian virtue ethics by arguing for the necessity of a *telos*.

Martin Heidegger

A 20th-century German philosopher who deeply engaged with Aristotle's concept of being, arguing that the Western tradition had largely forgotten the depth of Aristotle's original question.
