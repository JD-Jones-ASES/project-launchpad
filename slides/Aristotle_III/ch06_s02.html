Important Terms (1/3)

_Meditations on First Philosophy_

René Descartes' seminal philosophical work, in which he presents his arguments for God's existence and the nature of reality.

"I think, therefore I am" (_Cogito, ergo sum_)

Descartes' foundational indubitable certainty, establishing his own existence as a thinking being. This serves as the starting point for his "inside-out" philosophical inquiry.

Trademark Argument

Descartes' argument that the idea of God within him serves as a "trademark" or "signature" of God, implying divine authorship.

Ontological Argument

An argument for the existence of God based on the concept of God's being, claiming that existence is inherent to the definition of a perfect being. (Originally formulated by Anselm of Canterbury).

Clear and Distinct Perception

A key Cartesian criterion for truth; ideas perceived "clearly" (present and manifest to the attentive mind) and "distinctly" (precisely separated from all other ideas) are considered true.

Important Terms (2/3)

Perfection

In Descartes' ontological argument, a quality that makes something greater or more complete. Existence is considered a perfection.

Natural Theology

The attempt to gain knowledge of God through natural human reason and observation, without the aid of divine revelation.

_Summa Theologica_

Thomas Aquinas's monumental work of systematic theology, which includes his famous "Five Ways" for proving God's existence.

First Mover

The ultimate uncaused cause of all motion, a concept central to Aristotle's and Aquinas's arguments for God.

Efficient Cause

That which brings something into being or causes a change. In a chain of causes, it is the active agent.

Important Terms (3/3)

Potentiality and Actuality

Aristotelian concepts where potentiality is the capacity to be something, and actuality is the state of being that something. Motion is the reduction from potentiality to actuality.

Revelation

The disclosure of divine truth to humans, usually through scripture or direct communication, which surpasses what can be known by natural reason alone.
