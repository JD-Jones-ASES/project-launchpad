Aristotle’s Metaphysics: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Study Slides

The Accidental Title

Etymology

Derived from the Greek ta meta ta physika, meaning "the things after the Physics."

Origin

Following Aristotle's death in 322 BC, editors organizing his manuscripts placed his treatises on fundamental reality after those regarding natural science (the Physics).

Meanings of "Meta"

The prefix can mean "after," "beyond," or "about." All three interpretations have shaped how the field of metaphysics is understood today.

Composition and Structure

Nature of the Text

Compiled from lecture notes and research treatises written over several decades.

Internal Consistency

Because it was written across different stages of Aristotle’s career, some books repeat material or contain minor contradictions. It represents an ongoing inquiry rather than a finished system.

Translation

The standard scholarly English translation is by W. D. Ross (1924), noted for its philosophical precision.

The Core Books and Recurring Questions

Central Philosophical Questions

• What are the first causes and principles of everything that exists?

• What does it mean for something simply to "be"?

• What is substance (ousia)—the primary kind of being?

• What is the relationship between potentiality and actuality?

• Is there an eternal, unchanging, first principle of all motion?

Biographical Context: Aristotle (384–322 BC)

Early Life

Born in Stagira; son of Nicomachus, physician to the Macedonian court.

The Academy

Entered Plato’s Academy at age seventeen, staying for twenty years as a student and teacher.

Royal Tutor

Served as the tutor to the young Alexander the Great in 343 BC.

The Lyceum

Founded his own school in Athens in 335 BC. It was known as the Peripatetic school because Aristotle reportedly taught while walking (peripatein).

Legacy

His work covered logic, biology, ethics, politics, and poetics, with the Metaphysics representing the culmination of his inquiry into the first principles of reality.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Lyceum

A gymnasium outside Athens where Aristotle founded his school; the origin of the term "Peripatetic school."

Meta

A Greek prefix meaning "after," "beyond," or "about."

Ousia

Frequently translated as "substance" or "being"; the central concept of the Metaphysics naming what makes a thing what it is.

Peripatein

Greek for "to walk about"; the source of the name for the Peripatetic school of philosophy.

Prote Philosophia

"First philosophy"; Aristotle’s term for the study of the most fundamental principles of reality.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Theologike

"Theology" or the "science of the divine"; the term Aristotle used for the branch of philosophy that studies the Unmoved Mover.

Theoria

Contemplation; identified in the Ethics as the highest human activity and in the Metaphysics as the nature of the divine mind.

Unmoved Mover

The first cause of all motion and being; an eternal, self-thinking intellect.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the literal translation of the Greek phrase $\tau\alpha\ \mu\epsilon\tau\alpha\ \tau\alpha\ \phi\upsilon\sigma\iota\kappa\alpha$?

The things after the Physics.

What was the original purpose of the label "metaphysics" before it became a philosophical term?

It was a librarian's organizational note used for shelving Aristotle's works after his writings on natural science.

Which three meanings of the Greek prefix $\mu\epsilon\tau\alpha$ have historically influenced the interpretation of metaphysics?

After, beyond, and about.

Term: $\pi\rho\omega\tau\eta\ \phi\iota\lambda\omicron\sigma\omicron\phi\iota\alpha$ (First Philosophy)

Definition: Aristotle’s name for the inquiry into being as being, first causes, and the highest principles.

In what sense is "first philosophy" considered "first" according to Aristotle?

It is the most fundamental study because it investigates what everything else depends on.

Why did Aristotle sometimes refer to his inquiry as $\theta\epsilon\omicron\lambda\omicron\gamma\iota\kappa\eta$ (theology)?

It ultimately arrives at the study of the divine, the eternal, and the unmoved mover.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How many books comprise Aristotle's "Metaphysics"?

Fourteen.

Which Greek letters are used to label the fourteen books of the "Metaphysics"?

$\text{Alpha}$ through $\text{Nu}$.

What is the scholarly consensus regarding the composition of the fourteen books of the "Metaphysics"?

They were compiled from lecture notes and research treatises composed across several decades of Aristotle's career.

The second book of the "Metaphysics" is commonly referred to by what name?

Little alpha.

What is the primary function of Book $\Delta$ (Delta) in the "Metaphysics"?

It serves as a philosophical lexicon that defines key terms.

Which book is considered the "theological climax" of the "Metaphysics"?

Book $\Lambda$ (Lambda or XII).
