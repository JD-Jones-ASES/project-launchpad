Aristotle’s Metaphysics: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Study Slides

The Accidental Title

Etymology

Derived from the Greek *ta meta ta physika*, meaning "the things after the Physics."

Origin

Following Aristotle's death in 322 BC, editors organizing his manuscripts placed his treatises on fundamental reality after those regarding natural science (*the Physics*).

Meanings of "Meta"

The prefix can mean "after," "beyond," or "about." All three interpretations have shaped how the field of metaphysics is understood today.

Composition and Structure

Nature of the Text

Compiled from lecture notes and research treatises written over several decades.

Internal Consistency

Because it was written across different stages of Aristotle’s career, some books repeat material or contain minor contradictions. It represents an ongoing inquiry rather than a finished system.

Translation

The standard scholarly English translation is by **W. D. Ross (1924)**, noted for its philosophical precision.

Biographical Context: Aristotle (384–322 BC)

Early Life

Born in Stagira; son of Nicomachus, physician to the Macedonian court.

The Academy

Entered Plato’s Academy at age seventeen, staying for twenty years as a student and teacher.

Royal Tutor

Served as the tutor to the young Alexander the Great in 343 BC.

The Lyceum

Founded his own school in Athens in 335 BC. It was known as the **Peripatetic school** because Aristotle reportedly taught while walking (*peripatein*).

Legacy

His work covered logic, biology, ethics, politics, and poetics, with the *Metaphysics* representing the culmination of his inquiry into the first principles of reality.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Lyceum

A gymnasium outside Athens where Aristotle founded his school; the origin of the term "Peripatetic school."

Meta

A Greek prefix meaning "after," "beyond," or "about."

Ousia

Frequently translated as "substance" or "being"; the central concept of the *Metaphysics* naming what makes a thing what it is.

Peripatein

Greek for "to walk about"; the source of the name for the Peripatetic school of philosophy.

Prote Philosophia

"First philosophy"; Aristotle’s term for the study of the most fundamental principles of reality.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Theologike

"Theology" or the "science of the divine"; the term Aristotle used for the branch of philosophy that studies the Unmoved Mover.

Theoria

Contemplation; identified in the *Ethics* as the highest human activity and in the *Metaphysics* as the nature of the divine mind.

Unmoved Mover

The first cause of all motion and being; an eternal, self-thinking intellect.
