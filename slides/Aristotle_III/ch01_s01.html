The Poetics: The Good Word and Artistic Representation

Tragedy

The highest poetic form. It aims to produce a katharsis (purgation) of pity and fear.

The Tragic Hero

A person of high character who experiences a reversal of fortune due to a hamartia (an error in judgment).

Anagnorisis

Glossary of Key Terms (1/3)

Anagnorisis

Recognition; the moment in a tragedy where a character moves from ignorance to knowledge.

Arete

Virtue or excellence; the full development of a human capacity.

Ethos

The rhetorical appeal based on the character or credibility of the speaker.

Eudaimonia

Happiness or flourishing; the active exercise of the soul in accordance with virtue over a complete life.

Hamartia

An error of judgment that leads to the fall of a tragic hero.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/3)

Katharsis

Purgation or purification; the emotional release of pity and fear produced by tragedy.

Logos

The rhetorical appeal to reason, evidence, and logical structure.

Mimesis

Imitation or creative representation; the fundamental nature of all poetry and art.

Pathos

The rhetorical appeal to the audience’s emotions.

Phronesis

Practical wisdom; the intellectual virtue that enables one to see what a particular situation requires.

Glossary of Key Terms (3/3)

Politikon Zoon

"Political Animal"; the concept that humans are naturally social and fitted for life in a polis.

Theoria

Intellectual contemplation; the highest form of human activity.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the ultimate goal of all human actions according to the $\text{Nicomachean Ethics}$?

The ultimate goal is $\text{eudaimonia}$, or flourishing.

How does Aristotle define $\text{eudaimonia}$?

The active, excellent exercise of rational activity over a complete life.

According to Aristotle's 'function argument,' what is the distinctively human function?

The exercise of reason.

What is the meaning of the Greek term $\text{aret\acute{e}}$?

Virtue or excellence, representing the full development of a human capacity.

How do intellectual virtues differ from moral virtues in their development?

Intellectual virtues develop through instruction, while moral virtues develop through habit.

According to Aristotle, how does a person become courageous or just?

By repeatedly performing courageous or just actions until they become second nature.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Define the Aristotelian 'Doctrine of the Mean.'

The principle that every moral virtue is a middle point between the vices of excess and deficiency.

In the Doctrine of the Mean, what are the two vices associated with the virtue of courage?

Cowardice (deficiency) and rashness (excess).

In the Doctrine of the Mean, what are the two vices associated with the virtue of generosity?

Stinginess (deficiency) and prodigality (excess).

Which intellectual virtue enables an individual to discern the 'mean' in specific circumstances?

Practical wisdom, or $\text{phronesis}$.

How does Aristotle describe the role of a true friend in the pursuit of the good life?

A true friend is 'another self' who shares in the pursuit of genuine virtue.

What activity does Aristotle consider the highest form of human happiness?

$\text{Theoria}$, or intellectual contemplation.
