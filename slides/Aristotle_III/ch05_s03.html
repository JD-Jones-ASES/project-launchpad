Aristotle's Potentiality and Actuality: Understanding Change

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Potentiality (*dynamis*)

A genuine capacity or power to be or do something, representing a real but incomplete mode of being. It's not mere absence but an inherent capability, like an acorn's potential to become an oak.

Actuality (*energeia*)

The full realization, fulfillment, or "being-at-work" of a potentiality. It is the complete state towards which a potentiality is ordered, giving meaning to that potentiality.

The Problem of Change

The philosophical challenge posed by Pre-Socratics like Parmenides (change is impossible as something cannot come from non-being) and Heraclitus (everything is in constant flux, no stable being).

Aristotle distinguishes between capacities that act in only one direction (irrational, e.g., fire heats) and those that can act in opposite ways (rational, e.g., a doctor can heal or harm).

Important Terms (1/2)

_dynamis_

(Greek) Potentiality, capacity, or power. It signifies a genuine capacity to be or do something, not merely an absence.

_energeia_

(Greek) Actuality, activity, or being-at-work. A term coined by Aristotle to describe something actively operating or fully realized.

_entelecheia_

(Greek) Full realization, "having its end within itself." A related term emphasizing the completeness and self-sufficiency of actualized being.

Parmenides of Elea

A Pre-Socratic philosopher (c. 515–450 BC) who argued that genuine being is one, eternal, and unchanging, making change an illusion.

Heraclitus of Ephesus

A Pre-Socratic philosopher (c. 535–475 BC) who held that the universe is in constant flux, famously stating, "You cannot step into the same river twice."

Important Terms (2/2)

_proairesis_

(Greek) Rational choice or deliberate decision. For Aristotle, it's the uniquely human mode of action, choosing based on reasoned deliberation about what is good.

Substantial form / Final cause

Aristotelian concepts referring to the essence or nature of a thing (substantial form) and its purpose or end goal (final cause). Descartes found these terms "meaningless."
