Three Kinds of Substance

Eternity of Motion

A fundamental premise for Aristotle, arguing that motion and time have always existed and will always exist, a point of divergence from creationist theological views.

Everything in Motion is Moved by Something Else

Aristotle asserts that for anything to transition from potentiality to actuality (i.e., to move or change), it requires an external cause already in actuality.

No Infinite Regress

To avoid an endless chain of movers that ultimately explains nothing, Aristotle argues there must be a first cause of motion that is itself unmoved.

The Unmoved Mover (akineton kinoun)

This is the eternal, immaterial, and purely actual ultimate cause of all cosmic motion, standing at the highest level of reality.

Important Terms (1/3)

Perishable Substance

Things that come into being and pass away, such as animals, plants, humans, and artifacts.

Eternal Moving Substance

Bodies that are eternal and in continuous, perfect motion, such as the sun, moon, planets, and fixed stars in Aristotle's cosmology.

Eternal Unmoved Substance

The highest form of substance, which is eternal and the ultimate source of all motion, but is itself completely motionless. This is the Unmoved Mover.

Unmoved Mover (akineton kinoun)

Aristotle's term for the ultimate cause of all motion in the universe; it is eternal, immaterial, and purely actual.

Potentiality

The capacity for something to become something else; what something could be.

Important Terms (2/3)

Actuality

The state of being real or in existence; what something is.

Efficient Cause

The agent or force that directly brings something about (e.g., a sculptor making a statue).

Final Cause

The purpose or end toward which something strives; that for the sake of which something is done (e.g., health is the final cause of medicine).

Infinite Regress

A chain of causes or reasons that extends infinitely backward, without a first or ultimate starting point. Aristotle argues against this for motion.

Pure Actuality (actus purus)

A being that has no potentiality whatsoever, existing only as fully realized activity. The Unmoved Mover is pure actuality.

Important Terms (3/3)

Noesis Noeseos (Thought Thinking Itself)

Aristotle's description of the Unmoved Mover's highest activity – the divine intellect contemplating its own perfect intelligibility, where the thinking and the object of thought are identical.

Theoria

Intellectual contemplation or theoretical activity, which Aristotle considers the highest and most divine form of activity.

Cosmological Argument

A type of argument for the existence of God that begins with observed features of the universe (like motion, causation, or contingency) and reasons back to a first cause or necessary being.

Not an Efficient Cause
