The Life of Being (Aristotle III)

Chapter 5, Section 1

Aristotle's Solution to the Problem of Change

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

The Problem of Change

The philosophical dilemma arising from Parmenides' view (no real change, only appearance) and Heraclitus' view (everything is constant change, no stability).

Potentiality (dynamis)

A real capacity, power, or undeveloped state of being that a thing possesses to become something else. It is not nothing, but an incomplete mode of being.

Actuality (energeia)

The realization, fulfillment, or exercise of a potentiality. It is the complete state toward which potentiality is directed.

Rational Powers

Capacities unique to human beings that are two-directional (e.g., medical knowledge can heal or harm). These require deliberate choice for their exercise.

Irrational Powers

Capacities found in nature that are one-directional (e.g., fire heats, ice cools). They are exercised automatically under the right conditions.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Proairesis

Rational choice or deliberate decision, essential for directing rational powers. It is central to Aristotle's ethics as the distinguishing mark of virtuous action.

Entelecheia

A specific form of actuality meaning "having its end within itself," signifying the complete fulfillment of a capacity or the full realization of a thing's purpose (telos).

Analogy (as a way to define actuality)

Since actuality is too fundamental to be directly defined, Aristotle explains it through comparative examples (e.g., waking is to sleeping) to evoke an immediate intellectual perception.

The Priority of Actuality

Aristotle's claim that actuality is superior to potentiality in three fundamental ways: definition, time, and substance (or telos).

Important Terms (1/2)

Parmenides of Elea

An ancient Greek philosopher who argued that real change is impossible, as being cannot come from non-being.

Heraclitus of Ephesus

An ancient Greek philosopher who held the opposite view, that everything is constantly changing and nothing is stable.

Potentiality (dynamis)

The capacity or power for something to be or become.

Actuality (energeia)

The realization, completion, or exercise of a capacity.

Proairesis

Rational choice or deliberate decision; the act of choosing to exercise rational powers in a specific direction.

Important Terms (2/2)

Entelecheia

The complete fulfillment or full realization of a thing's inherent potential or purpose, meaning "having its end within itself."

Telos

The end, goal, purpose, or ultimate aim of a thing. For Aristotle, everything in nature has a telos towards which it is ordered.

The Ancient Problem of Change

Parmenides' View

Heraclitus' View

Contended the opposite, that everything is in a constant state of flux; "you cannot step into the same river twice." Nothing is ever truly stable.

The Paradox

Both thinkers had insights, but their extremes made ordinary experience—where things both change and persist—incomprehensible.

Aristotle's Solution: Potentiality and Actuality

Potentiality (dynamis)

This is not non-existence, but a real capacity or power to be or do something. An acorn genuinely has the capacity to become an oak in a way a stone does not. It is rooted in the thing's nature...

Actuality (energeia)

This is the realization or exercise of that capacity. When the acorn grows into an oak, its potential is actualized.

Change

For Aristotle, change is simply the transition from potential being to actual being. Something doesn't come from nothing; it moves from a state of "being-potentially" to "being-actually."

Understanding Potentiality and Actuality

Potentiality (dynamis)

The raw capacity. Examples: an acorn is potentially an oak; marble is potentially a statue; a child is potentially an adult. It's an incomplete, dependent mode of being.

Actuality (energeia)

The fulfillment of that capacity. Examples: the oak, the statue, the adult. It is the complete and realized state.

Rational vs. Irrational Powers

Irrational Potentialities

These are one-directional. When conditions are right, they always act the same way. Examples: fire always heats, ice always cools, heavy objects always fall. There is no choice involved.

Entelecheia: Full Realization

Energeia

Refers to ongoing activity or the exercise of a capacity ("being at work").

Entelecheia

Refers to the complete fulfillment of a capacity, the state where a thing has fully realized its inherent end or purpose ("having its end within itself").

Actuality is Defined by Analogy

Why not directly defined?

Actuality is more fundamental than potentiality; potentiality is defined by reference to actuality. Actuality is a basic feature of existence.

Connecting the Concepts

Biology

Explains growth, development, and generation (e.g., seed to plant, embryo to animal).

Ethics (Volume I)

Virtue is the actualization of the soul's rational capacities, and eudaimonia (flourishing) is the full actualization of human nature. Proairesis is key to virtuous action.

Theology (Chapter 6)

God is described as pure actuality (actus purus), the ultimate source of all actualization in the universe, the Unmoved Mover.
