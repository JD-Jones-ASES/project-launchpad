To ti en einai

Literally "the what it was to be," translated as "essence." It is the formula that captures what a thing truly and necessarily is, stripped of all incidental properties.

Hyle

Greek for "matter." The physical stuff or substratum from which a thing is made.

Substratum

The underlying material or subject that remains constant through change and upon which properties are predicated. One of Aristotle's candidates for substance.

Important Terms (2/2)

Universal

A general concept, property, or characteristic that can be predicated of many individual things (e.g., "redness" can apply to many red objects).

Genus

A broader category or class under which more specific species fall (e.g., "mammal" is a genus for "dog").

Propter se

Latin for "by its very nature" or "in itself." Aristotle uses this to distinguish essential attributes from accidental ones. The essence of a thing is what it is propter se.

Substance as the Root of All Categories

Priority in Definition

The definition of a thing's substance must be present in the definition of any other predicate applied to it.

Priority in Knowledge

We know something most fully when we know what it is (its substance), rather than just its qualities or quantities.

Priority in Time/Existence

Other categories cannot exist independently; only substance can. You can't have "whiteness" without something being white.

The Concept of Essence (To Ti En Einai)

• The thing itself as a composite of form and matter.

• A mere description of surface properties.

• A combination of attributes that aren't inherently one (e.g., "white surface" is not the essence of a surface, because "white" is added to "surface").

Form and Matter in the Process of "Coming-to-Be"

Matter (hyle)

is the raw material, the "out of which" something is made (e.g., bronze for a statue, bricks and timber for a house).

Form (eidos)

is the blueprint, the structure, the "into which" or the goal that guides the process of creation (e.g., the human figure for the statue, the structure for sheltering humans for the house).

What is Aristotle's ultimate conclusion about what substance primarily is?

Substance is primarily form (eidos) — the defining structure that makes a thing what it is.

What does Aristotle mean by 'eidos' (form) in the context of substance?

'Eidos' refers to the defining structure or pattern that makes a thing what it is, distinguishing it from mere matter and giving it its identity.
