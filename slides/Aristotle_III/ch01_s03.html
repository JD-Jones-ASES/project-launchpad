Key Concepts and Comparisons

Feature. Thomas Aquinas (The Builder)

René Descartes (The Challenger)

Philosophical Goal. To synthesize reason (Aristotle) with faith (Scripture).

To find absolute certainty through systematic doubt.

View of Aristotle. Viewed him as "The Philosopher" to be embraced and perfected.

Viewed his system as vague and unable to provide mathematical rigor.

Foundational Principle. Gratia non tollit naturam, sed perficit (Grace perfects nature).

Cogito, ergo sum (I think, therefore I am).

Nature of Reality. A hierarchy of being, purpose, forms, and final causes.

A mechanical world of two substances: mind and matter.

The Material World. Populated by substances and potentiality.

A vast machine governed by mathematical laws.

The Method of Systematic Doubt

The Meditations

In his Meditations on First Philosophy, Descartes systematically doubted everything—the senses, the physical world, and even mathematics—to see what remained certain.

The Cogito

He discovered that the act of doubting itself proved the existence of a thinker. This led to his foundational truth: Cogito, ergo sum ("I think, therefore I am").

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Averroes (Ibn Rushd)

An Arabic commentator from Córdoba whose works were vital in transmitting Aristotle to the medieval West; known as "The Commentator."

Avicenna (Ibn Sīnā)

A Persian philosopher and physician whose Aristotelian-influenced works shaped the medieval European reception of philosophy.

Cartesian

Relating to the philosophy or methods of René Descartes.

Cogito, ergo sum

"I think, therefore I am"; the foundational certainty of Descartes' philosophy.

Meditations on First Philosophy

Descartes’ 1641 masterwork focused on systematic doubt and the rebuilding of knowledge.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Res extensa

"Extended thing"; Descartes’ term for physical matter, defined by its existence in space.

Res cogitans

"Thinking thing"; Descartes’ term for the mind or soul, defined purely by the activity of thought.

Summa Theologica

Aquinas’s comprehensive synthesis of Christian theology and Aristotelian philosophy.

Thomistic (or Thomist)

Relating to the philosophy or theological system developed by Thomas Aquinas.

Unmoved Mover

Aristotle’s concept of an eternal intellect that moves the universe by being an object of desire; a concept later adapted by Aquinas.

Key Concepts (1/2)

Who are the two primary "inheritors" of Aristotle's metaphysics discussed in the text?

Thomas Aquinas and René Descartes.

Which 13th-century philosopher sought to build upon and deepen Aristotle's arguments?

Thomas Aquinas.

Which 17th-century philosopher sought to challenge and rebuild Aristotle's philosophical system?

René Descartes.

Aquinas understood the world through purpose, form, and the hierarchy of being, while Descartes understood it through _____ and mechanism.

mathematics

In what year and region was Thomas Aquinas born?

1225 near Aquino in southern Italy.

Where did Thomas Aquinas first encounter the works of Aristotle in his early twenties?

The University of Naples.

Key Concepts (2/2)

During which centuries was the full corpus of Aristotle recovered in Western Europe?

The twelfth and thirteenth centuries.

From which language were Aristotle's works first translated into Latin during the Middle Ages?

Arabic.

Who was the Arabic scholar known in the medieval West as "the Commentator"?

Averroes (Ibn Rushd).

Which Persian philosopher's works were translated into Latin and helped shape the reception of Aristotle in medieval Europe?

Avicenna (Ibn Sīnā).

What unqualified honorific title did medieval scholars use for Aristotle?

The Philosopher.

What was Aquinas's basic approach to the pagan philosophy of Aristotle?

Embrace it, correct it where necessary, and build on it.
