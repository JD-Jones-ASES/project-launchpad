The Cartesian Revolution: From Forms to Extension

Rejection of Substantial Forms

Scholastics used "substantial forms" to explain the nature and behavior of things (e.g., why fire rises or acorns become oaks). Descartes dismissed these as obscure and unscientific.

Contingency and Participation

Because a creature’s existence is not part of its essence, its existence must be received from an exterior agent. Creatures are "beings by participation."

Ipsum Esse Subsistens

This refers to God as "Subsistent Existence Itself." In God alone, essence and existence are identical. God does not have existence; God is existence.

Divine Simplicity

Aquinas argued that God is "altogether simple," meaning He is not composed of parts—neither quantitative parts, matter and form, nor essence and existence.

Glossary of Important Terms

Key Concepts (1/2)

What was the dominant philosophical doctrine used by medieval Scholastics to explain the nature of physical objects?

Aristotle's doctrine of substantial forms and matter.

In Aristotelian philosophy, what is the role of a 'substantial form'?

It is the principle that makes each thing the specific kind of thing it is.

How did medieval scientists explain why an acorn becomes an oak tree instead of an elm?

They appealed to the substantial form of the oak, which was present potentially in the acorn.

How did Rene Descartes characterize the traditional Aristotelian framework in his 'Meditations on First Philosophy'?

He viewed it as obscure and unscientific.

What single property defines the material world in the Cartesian philosophical system?

Extension, which includes length, breadth, and depth.

Term: res extensa

Definition: Descartes' term for material substance, defined entirely by its spatial properties.

Key Concepts (2/2)

According to Descartes, the scientific understanding of physical objects reduces to what two factors?

The geometry of an object's extension and the mechanics of its motion.

Which of Aristotle's four causes did Descartes retain as the primary explanation in natural science?

The efficient cause, described as the mechanical push-and-pull of matter.

Which two Aristotelian causes were eliminated from Descartes' new picture of the physical world?

Formal causes and final causes.

In the Cartesian view, what replaced the idea of nature having 'inherent purposes' or 'built-in directions'?

Matter moving according to mathematical laws.

In Meditation III, which properties does Descartes say he perceives clearly and distinctly in a piece of wax?

Magnitude (extension), figure, situation, movement, substance, duration, and number.

Why does Descartes consider qualities like color, sound, and scent to be 'obscure and confused'?

He cannot determine if these ideas represent real objects or are merely 'chimeras'.
