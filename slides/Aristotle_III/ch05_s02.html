Important Terms (1/2)

Teleological

Directed toward an end or purpose. From Greek telos (end, purpose) and logos (account, reason). A teleological explanation answers "what is it for?"

Efficient causation

In Aristotle's framework, the source of motion or change. For Descartes, it's the sole type of causation relevant to understanding the physical world (particles acting on other particles).

res cogitans

Latin for "thinking thing." Descartes's term for mind or soul, defined by the capacity for thought; a non-spatial, non-extended substance.

res extensa

Latin for "extended thing." Descartes's term for matter or body, defined entirely by spatial extension (length, breadth, depth); a purely mechanical system.

The mind-body problem

The philosophical question of how mind and body relate and interact, especially challenging in Descartes's dualistic system where they are completely distinct substances.

Important Terms (2/2)

actus purus

Latin for "pure act" or "pure actuality." Aquinas's term for God, signifying a being without any potentiality, fully and perfectly actualized.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is Descartes's term for the 'thinking thing' or mind, and what is its primary characteristic?

Res cogitans (thinking thing). It is defined entirely by the capacity for thought and is a non-spatial, non-extended substance.

What is Descartes's term for 'matter' or 'body,' and what is its primary characteristic?

Res extensa (extended thing). It is defined entirely by spatial extension (length, breadth, depth) and is a purely mechanical system operating by physical laws.

What is the key difference between mind and body, according to Descartes in his Meditations?

The body is by nature always divisible, while the mind (or soul) is entirely indivisible, apprehending itself as clearly one and entire.

How does Descartes's concept of 'essence' differ from Aristotle's?

What major intellectual consequence resulted from Descartes's program of dismantling the teleological universe?

The birth of modern science, which shifted its inquiry from asking 'what things are for?' to 'how do they work?' by focusing on mechanical laws.
