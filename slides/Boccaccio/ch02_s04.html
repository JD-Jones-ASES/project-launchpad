Chapter 2, Section 4

The Parable of the Three Rings (The Decameron, Day I, Novel 3)

Study Slides

Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: Why did Saladin need to approach Melchisedech for money?

Saladin had spent his treasure on various wars and "lavish displays of magnificence." He faced an emergency that required a large sum of money with a "celerity adequate to his necessity."

Q: What specific trap did Saladin set for Melchisedech?

Saladin asked Melchisedech to judge which of the "three laws"—the law of the Jews, the Saracens, or the Christians—was the "true law." He intended to use any answer Melchisedech gave as a pretext to harass him and extort money.

Q: How did the father in the parable ensure that all three of his sons felt loved?

He promised the ring to each son individually. To fulfill these promises without showing favoritism, he had two copies made that were so identical that even the maker could hardly distinguish the original.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/2)

Q: What was the outcome of the dispute between the three sons in the parable?

The dispute remained "pendent." Because the rings were indistinguishable, the true heir could not be determined, and the suit remained unsettled.

Q: How did Saladin react when he realized Melchisedech had evaded his snare?

Saladin felt admiration for the Jew’s adroitness. He decided to be honest about his financial needs and his original plan. Ultimately, he repaid the borrowed money and granted Melchisedech an honorable position in his court.

Key Concepts (1/2)

Who narrates the third tale of Day I in Boccaccio's Decameron?

Filomena.

In the third tale of Day I, which historical figure serves as the Sultan of Egypt?

Saladin (Salah ad-Din).

What specific event in 1187 is associated with the historical Saladin in the text?

The recapture of Jerusalem.

How was Saladin generally characterized in medieval Western literature?

As a model of chivalric virtue.

What immediate problem causes Saladin to seek out Melchisedech?

He needs a large sum of money quickly to meet an emergency.

What is Melchisedech's profession in the city of Alexandria?

He is a wealthy Jewish moneylender.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Why does Saladin avoid simply demanding money from Melchisedech?

He is not disposed to use force and prefers a "colorably reasonable pretext."

What character trait of Melchisedech makes Saladin believe he will not lend money voluntarily?

His extreme miserliness.

What is the specific "trap" question Saladin asks Melchisedech?

Which of the three laws—Jewish, Saracen, or Christian—is the true one?

How does Melchisedech perceive Saladin's religious question?

He sees it as a snare meant to entangle him in his speech for harassment.

In Melchisedech's parable, what physical object designates the father's rightful heir?

A ring of extraordinary beauty and value.

Why does the father in the parable have two duplicate rings created?

He loves his three sons equally and cannot choose a sole legatee among them.
