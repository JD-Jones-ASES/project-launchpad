The Parable of the Goslings (Papere)

The Experiment

After his wife's death, Filippo raises his son in total isolation in a cell on Monte Asinaio, exposing him only to religious teachings and "holy orisons."

The Encounter

When the son is eighteen, they visit Florence. Despite his father’s attempts to dismiss a group of beautiful women as "naughty things" called "goslings" (*papere*), the boy is instantly captivated.

The Defense of the Muses and Style

Literary Precedent

He cites great poets like Dante Alighieri, Guido Cavalcanti, and Cino da Pistoia, who all wrote of love in their old age.

The Muses as Women

He posits that the Muses themselves are "ladies," and while women are not their peers, they bear a "likeness" to them. He credits women as the inspiration for thousands of his verses.

Simple Style

He defends his use of the "vulgar Florentine" (the common vernacular) and a "homely and simple style," arguing that even in this humble form, his work is subject to the "blasts of envy."

Filostrato’s Reign

Etymology

The name *Filostrato* is derived from the Greek *philos* (lover) and the Latin/Italian *strato* (struck down or prostrated), meaning "vanquished by love."

Thematic Mandate

He decrees that the stories for the day must concern those whose love came to a "tragic conclusion." This marks a dramatic shift from the cleverness and triumphs celebrated in Day Three.
