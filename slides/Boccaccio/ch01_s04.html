The Three Crowns of Italian Literature (Tre Corone)

Author. Primary Contribution

Key Work

Dante Alighieri (1265–1321). Supreme achievement of medieval literature; developed the highest form of Italian verse.

*The Divine Comedy* (*Commedia*)

Francesco Petrarch (1304–1374). "Father of Humanism"; perfected the lyric tradition and the sonnet form.

*Canzoniere*

Giovanni Boccaccio (1313–1375). Master of prose narrative; creator of the first great work of European prose fiction.

*The Decameron*

Inherited Medieval Traditions

The Exemplum

Originally short stories used in sermons to provide moral lessons. Boccaccio utilized this form but often subverted it by providing "morals" that were ironic or ambiguous.

The Fabliau

Humorous, often bawdy French verse tales featuring archetypes like cuckolded husbands and lustful clergy. Boccaccio refined these crude plots by adding psychological depth and social commentary.

The Romance

Narratives of chivalry and courtly love. Boccaccio shifted these from the "ideal and marvelous" to grounded, realistic settings like the markets and bedrooms of Florence and Naples.

Classical Literature

Boccaccio incorporated the rhetorical models and narrative crafts of Roman authors such as Ovid, Cicero, Livy, Plautus, and Terence.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/3)

Q: What specific rhyme scheme did Dante invent for his *Divine Comedy*?

Dante invented *terza rima*, an interlocking three-line rhyme scheme.

Q: How did Petrarch contribute to the intellectual foundations of the Renaissance?

Petrarch was the first great humanist; he spent much of his life seeking out, copying, and studying classical Latin texts.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/3)

Q: In what way does Boccaccio "subvert" the tradition of the exemplum?

While a traditional exemplum is meant to teach a clear moral lesson, Boccaccio’s narrators often provide "morals" that are ironic, ambiguous, or deliberately beside the point.

Q: Name three Roman authors whose works influenced Boccaccio’s humanist education.

Boccaccio was influenced by the comedies of Plautus and Terence, the poetry of Ovid (*Metamorphoses*), the histories of Livy, and the rhetorical models of Cicero.

Q: Compare the narrators in Boccaccio’s *Decameron* to those in Chaucer’s *Canterbury Tales*.

Boccaccio’s narrators are a group of refined young aristocrats (the *brigata*), whereas Chaucer’s narrators represent a broad cross-section of English society, ranging from knights to millers.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (3/3)

Q: Which specific story from the *Decameron* serves as the basis for Chaucer’s "The Clerk’s Tale"?

The story of Patient Griselda (found in Decameron X.10).

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Allegory

A narrative where characters and events represent abstract ideas or moral principles (e.g., Dante's journey representing the soul's path to God).

Canzoniere

Meaning "songbook," this is Petrarch’s collection of 366 poems, primarily sonnets, dedicated to his beloved Laura.

Exemplum (pl. exempla)

A short story used by medieval preachers to illustrate a moral point during a sermon.

Fabliau (pl. fabliaux)

A humorous, often bawdy verse tale popular in France, typically featuring tricks and common social archetypes.

Frame Narrative

An outer story that provides the context and motivation for a series of inner stories (e.g., the *brigata* fleeing the plague in the *Decameron*).

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Humanism

An intellectual movement characterized by a passion for recovering and studying classical Greek and Latin texts, of which Petrarch was a founding figure.

Novella

From the Italian for "new" or "news," it is a short prose tale typically focused on a single incident or turning point.

Novellino

An anonymous 13th-century collection of very short, unsophisticated Italian tales.

Terza Rima

An interlocking three-line rhyme scheme invented by Dante for the *Divine Comedy*.

Tre Corone

"The Three Crowns"; the collective name for Dante, Petrarch, and Boccaccio, the three primary figures of medieval Italian literature.
