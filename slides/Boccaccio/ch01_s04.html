Reading the Decameron

Study Slides

Analyze the Narrator

Narrator Personalities

Different narrators favor different themes. For example, Dioneo is an irreverent joker, while Filomena prefers stories of fortune and adventure.

Reliability

Identify Pervasive Irony

Contradiction

A narrator may praise a character who is clearly meant to be condemned.

False Morals

The "moral" stated at the end of a tale may be the exact opposite of the lesson the reader is intended to draw.

Subtext

If a situation appears too simple or "neat," it is likely a signal of Boccaccio’s ironic intent.

Embrace Moral Complexity

Complex Characters

Figures like the wicked Ser Ciappelletto (a monster yet a brilliant performer) or the patient Griselda (admirable yet disturbing) exemplify this complexity.

Controversial Figures

The "clever wives" of Day VII are presented as both sympathetic and transgressive.

Recognize the Architecture

Thematic Progression

The themes of the Days develop over time, moving from comedy and satire toward nobility and virtue.

Internal Echoes

Tales within a single Day often echo or contrast with one another.

The J.M. Rigg Translation

Style

It is faithful to the original Italian but utilizes formal Edwardian English.

Archaic Language

Readers will encounter words such as "whither" (where), "hath" (has), and "prithee" (I pray you).

Satire and Social Critique

Bawdy Tales

These stories deal frankly with sexual desire and the "appetites of the body," following the tradition of the fabliau (comic tale).

Anticlericalism

A central motif is the beffa, or practical joke, which celebrates cleverness while punishing the gullible.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Beffa

A central motif in the Decameron involving a practical joke or elaborate trick; it often involves comic cruelty and celebrates the cleverness of the trickster.

Brigata

The group of narrators who flee the plague and tell the stories that comprise the Decameron.

Edwardian English

The formal style of English used in the 1903 J.M. Rigg translation, characterized by archaic terms and a stiff, rhythmic prose.

Exemplum (Comic)

A short story used to illustrate a moral point, though Boccaccio often subverts these to create irony.

Fabliau

A literary tradition of comic, often bawdy, tales that use humor to explore human nature and bodily appetites.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Irony

A mode of expression where the intended meaning is the opposite of the literal meaning; used by Boccaccio to critique characters or subvert stated morals.

Prithee

An archaic Edwardian English term meaning "I pray you."

Whither

An archaic term meaning "where."

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to the text, what should be a reader's primary focus when first encountering the tales of the Decameron?

Enjoying them as stories crafted to entertain, surprise, and delight.

What collective term refers to the group of ten narrators who tell the stories in the Decameron?

The brigata.

Which member of the brigata is characterized as an irreverent joker?

Dioneo.

What specific types of stories are favored by the narrator Filomena?

Stories of fortune and adventure.

How does Boccaccio use his narrators in a way that mirrors a playwright’s use of characters?

By making them characters who are not always reliable.

Where does the "real interest" often lie regarding the narrator's role in a tale?

In the gap between what the narrator says a tale means and its actual meaning.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Concept: Irony

Definition: A mode of expression where the intended meaning is different from or opposite to the literal meaning.

How might Boccaccio use irony when a narrator praises a character's virtue?

The praise may actually be exposing the character's foolishness.

Under what condition is Boccaccio likely being ironic at the end of a tale?

When a stated moral is the exact opposite of the lesson the reader is meant to draw.

What is a common sign that a situation in the Decameron is being treated ironically?

The situation or moral seems too simple or too neat.

Why does the text advise readers to resist looking for a clear "lesson" in the Decameron?

Because the tales are designed to be morally complex and ambiguous.

How did medieval audiences' reaction to moral ambiguity differ from that of modern readers?

Medieval audiences found ambiguity less troubling and trusted their own judgment.
