Chapter 6, Section 1

Boccaccio’s Decameron, Day the Fifth – Love Rewarded

Study Slides

The Tradition of Courtly Love

The Idealized Lady

A woman of higher social station who is the object of intense devotion.

The Suffering Lover

A nobleman who serves his lady with humility, patience, and generosity.

The Ennobling Power of Devotion

The belief that love makes the lover a better person, regardless of whether the love is returned.

Codification

Andreas Capellanus's *De Amore* (c. 1185) established the formal rules for courtly behavior.

The Novella Form

Realism

Unlike the *romance*, which features supernatural marvels and idealized heroes, the novella focuses on recognizable people in realistic settings.

Human Behavior

Unlike the *exemplum*, which is designed to illustrate a specific moral lesson, the novella explores the complexities of human behavior and often leaves the reader to draw their own conclusions.

Nastagio degli Onesti (V.8)

Contrapasso

The punishment in the vision mirrors the sin. A woman who was cruel to her suitor is eternally hunted, while the suitor who died of despair pursues her.

The Moral Dilemma

Nastagio uses this divine vision as a tool of manipulation, staging a dinner so his beloved will witness the horror and be frightened into marriage.

Summary of Additional Tales

Tale. Title/Characters

Theme/Plot Summary

V.1. Cimone and Efigenia

Love transforms a boorish man into an intelligent, refined individual. It celebrates love’s power to "civilize" the lover.

V.2. Gostanza and Martuccio

Believing her lover dead, Gostanza sets out to die at sea but is carried to Tunis. She finds Martuccio alive and prosperous; they are reunited by fortune.

V.3. Pietro and Agnolella

After eloping, the couple is separated by bandits in a forest. After various misadventures, they are reunited and married with family blessings.

V.4. Ricciardo and Caterina

Lovers caught together on a balcony are forced into marriage by the girl’s father, resolving a scandalous situation with pragmatic humor.

V.5. Guidotto and Giacomino

Two rivals for the same woman cease their violence when her noble parentage is revealed, leading to a resolution through marriage and family identity.

V.6. Gianni and Restituta

Lovers captured by pirates and condemned to death are saved when a high-ranking official recognizes the man's noble blood.

V.7. Teodoro and Violante

A servant is condemned to death for impregnating his master’s daughter, only to be saved when he is revealed to be the lost son of Armenian nobility.

Comparative Perspective: Boccaccio and Chaucer

Chaucer’s Approach

Presents a chain of generosity (husband to wife, suitor to wife, magician to suitor) and asks the reader, "Which was the most generous?"

Boccaccio’s Approach

In the case of Federigo, generosity is absolute and uncalculating. He sacrifices his last treasure without any hope of reward, representing the supreme act of courtly love.

Glossary of Terms (1/2)

Beffa

A comic trick or prank, often featured in Boccaccio’s more realistic or cynical tales.

Contrapasso

A punishment that mirrors the sin; used to describe the supernatural vision in Nastagio’s tale.

Courtly Love (Fin'amors/Amor Cortese)

A medieval literary tradition of idealized, often unrequited romantic devotion.

Crudeltade

A medieval term for "cruelty," specifically used to describe a lady’s refusal to return the affection of a faithful suitor.

Exemplum

A short story told to illustrate a moral point or provide an example of proper behavior.

Glossary of Terms (2/2)

Novella

A short prose narrative focused on realistic characters and human behavior, a form perfected by Boccaccio.

Provence

A region in southeastern France where troubadour poets invented the conventions of courtly love.
