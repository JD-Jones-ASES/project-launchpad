Tales of the Decameron (Boccaccio)

Chapter 2, Section 1

Day I of the Decameron: Wit, Wisdom, and the Human Comedy

Study Slides

Key Concepts and Engines of Comedy

Concept. Definition

Function in the Text

Motto. A clever, pointed remark deployed at a precise moment.

Used to correct, shame, or delight. It can transform character or defeat institutional power.

Beffa. A sustained practical joke or deception.

Involves elaborate planning and deception; acts as the primary engine of comedy throughout the work.

Ingegno. Human intelligence and resourcefulness.

The quality celebrated through both the motto and the beffa.

Major Religious Themes

The Problem of Credulity

In the tale of Ciappelletto (I.1), a wicked man exploits the assumption of sincerity required for social life. The friar who hears his confession is not depicted as stupid, but as unable to imagine...

Clerical Hypocrisy

Several tales (I.4, I.6) highlight members of the clergy who fail to live up to their vows. Boccaccio suggests that those who judge others must first be capable of judging themselves.

The Complexity of Truth

Through the character of Abraham the Jew (I.2), Boccaccio uses ironic logic to suggest that the very corruption of the Church in Rome is proof of divine support, as the religion flourishes despite ...

Religious Tolerance

The parable of the three rings (I.3) posits that the "true" faith cannot be settled by human argument. It advocates for humility and mutual respect over dogmatism.

Summary of the Tales of Day I

Tale. Primary Characters

Summary of Action

I.1. Ciappelletto

A wicked man gives a false confession on his deathbed and is venerated as a saint.

I.2. Abraham the Jew; Jehannot

Abraham visits Rome, sees the corruption of the papacy, and converts because the religion survives despite it.

I.3. Melchisedech; Saladin

Melchisedech avoids a financial trap by telling a parable about three identical rings given by a father to his sons.

I.4. Monk and Abbot

A monk caught with a woman avoids punishment by proving his abbot is guilty of the same sin.

I.5. Marchioness of Monferrato; King of France

The Marchioness rebuffs the King’s advances by serving only chicken, noting that women are all the same regardless of status.

I.6. A "Good Man"; An Inquisitor

A man accused of heresy uses a witty remark about wine to expose an inquisitor's own greed.

I.7. Bergamino; A Miserly Lord

Bergamino tells an allegory about a hungry poet to shame a host into providing hospitality.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Beffa

A sustained practical joke or elaborate deception that serves as a primary source of comedy in the Decameron.

Confession

A sacramental practice involving the confession of sins to a priest for absolution; required annually for Christians by 1215.

Ingegno

The quality of intelligence, resourcefulness, and verbal cleverness.

Inquisition

A powerful human institution within the Church intended to suppress heresy, often depicted by Boccaccio as hypocritical.

Motto

A cleverly turned phrase or pointed remark used to correct, shame, or delight.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Overture

A term used to describe Day I, as it introduces nearly every major theme of the entire collection without a set theme.

Parable

A simple story used to illustrate a moral or spiritual lesson, such as Master Alberto’s leeks or Melchisedech’s rings.

Key Concepts (1/2)

In the structure of the Decameron, what is the primary function of the ten tales told on Day I?

They serve as an overture, providing a panoramic introduction to Boccaccio's world and major themes.

Why are the narrators on Day I free to tell stories on any subject they choose?

Pampinea, the queen of the first day, sets no specific theme for the stories.

What Italian term describes Boccaccio's concept of a cleverly turned phrase used at a precise moment to correct or shame?

Motto

How does the Italian usage of the term 'motto' differ from its common English definition?

It is broader, encompassing any clever phrase deployed at the right moment rather than just a slogan.

What term refers to a sustained practical joke or deception that serves as a major engine of comedy in the Decameron?

Beffa

Which specific tale is cited as the 'supreme beffa' of the entire Decameron?

Ciappelletto's false confession (Novel I.1).

Key Concepts (2/2)

What abstract quality, encompassing intelligence and resourcefulness, does Boccaccio value above almost all others?

Ingegno

What is the specific target of Boccaccio's religious satire in Day I?

Human institutions and the hypocrisy of the clergy rather than faith or God.

In Novel I.1, how does the wicked Ciappelletto manage to be venerated as a saint after his death?

He performs a completely false confession on his deathbed that deceives a holy friar.

The _____ of 1215 established the requirement for all Christians to undergo annual confession.

Fourth Lateran Council

In Novel I.2, what ironic reasoning leads Abraham the Jew to convert to Christianity after visiting Rome?

He concludes that a religion so corrupt at its center must be supported by God to still flourish.

What is the central claim of Melchisedech's parable of the three rings in Novel I.3?

The question of which faith is the 'true' one cannot be settled by human argument.
