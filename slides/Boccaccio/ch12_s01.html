Giovanni Boccaccio and the Renaissance: A Study Guide

Study Slides

A Bridge Between Two Worlds

Social Mobility

A shift from fixed feudal hierarchies to a society of merchants, bankers, and artisans.

Ingegno

A reliance on individual wit and intelligence rather than a predetermined social order.

Urban Dynamism

The rise of cities like 14th-century Florence, which were governed by elected councils rather than hereditary lords.

The Foundations of Realism

Democratization of Subject

Literature was no longer confined to the court or cloister. Subjects included horse-dealers, innkeepers, and the wives of artisans.

Concrete Detail

Stories are set in real cities with named streets and recognizable neighborhoods.

Psychological Specificity

Characters are not mere representations of "virtue" or "vice" but are recognizable human types with complex motivations.

Comparison: Boccaccio and Chaucer

Feature. Boccaccio’s *Decameron*

Chaucer’s *Canterbury Tales*

Narrators. Elegant young aristocrats.

A boisterous cross-section of society (Knight, Miller, Wife of Bath).

Tone. Urbane and polished.

Earthy and encyclopedic.

Sources. Original and folk traditions.

Often derived directly from Boccaccio (e.g., Patient Griselda).

Focus. Italian social transition.

English social comedy and character portraiture.

Influence on Drama and the Short Story

William Shakespeare

Utilized Boccaccio’s plots for *All’s Well That Ends Well* (from the tale of Gillette of Narbonne) and *Cymbeline*. He likely accessed these through William Painter’s *Palace of Pleasure*.

The Novella Tradition

Italian writers like Franco Sacchetti and Matteo Bandello imitated and extended Boccaccio’s style, eventually influencing the development of fairy tales through Straparola and Basile.

The Modern Short Story

Boccaccio’s insight that short prose could be a vehicle for serious art paved the way for nineteenth-century masters such as Guy de Maupassant and Anton Chekhov.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Brigata

The company of young people who flee the city and provide the frame narrative for the storytelling in the *Decameron*.

Democratization of the Subject

The revolutionary literary practice of making common people (merchants, servants, etc.) the central focus of serious literature.

Frame-narrative

A literary technique where a main story organizes a set of shorter stories within it (e.g., a group of travelers telling tales).

Ingegno

Individual wit, cleverness, or genius; a key trait for success in Boccaccio’s transitional society.

Novella

A short prose tale. The *Decameron* founded the Italian tradition of this genre, which eventually evolved into the modern short story.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Teseida

An earlier work by Boccaccio that served as the source for Chaucer’s *The Knight’s Tale*.
