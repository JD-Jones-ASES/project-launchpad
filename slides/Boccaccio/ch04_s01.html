Moral Ambiguity and Satire

Anticlerical Satire

Several tales (III.3, III.4, III.8, III.10) target institutional hypocrisy, depicting members of the clergy as either gullible dupes or predatory tricksters.

The Limits of Cleverness

The text raises questions about whether ingegno is an unqualified good. It asks readers to consider if cleverness is admirable when used for deception or to serve desire rather than justice.

Summary of Tales: Day III

Novel. Primary Characters

Narrative Summary

III.1. Masetto and the Nuns

Masetto pretends to be deaf and mute to gain work at a convent. His deception allows him to fulfill the secret desires of the nuns, resulting in prosperity for all involved.

III.2. A Clever Groom and King Agilulf

A horse-servant (groom) disguises himself as the King to visit the Queen at night. When discovered, his quick thinking in a dark dormitory saves his life.

III.3. A Lady, a Friar, and a Lover

A married woman uses a naive friar as an unwitting messenger to communicate with her lover, convincing the friar he is helping her resist temptation.

III.4. Dom Felice and Fra Puccio

A monk tricks the devout Fra Puccio into performing a grueling, stationary penance in the garden so the monk can visit Puccio’s wife inside.

III.5. Zima and Francesco’s Wife

Zima is granted permission to speak to a woman on the condition she remains silent. His rhetoric is so persuasive that she is moved to respond, using words as an instrument of seduction.

III.6. Ricciardo and Catella

Ricciardo tricks the faithful Catella into believing her husband is unfaithful, leading her to a darkened bathhouse where she mistakes Ricciardo for her husband.

III.7. Tedaldo and Ermellina

Tedaldo, believed to be dead, returns to Florence disguised as a friar. He uses this deception to reclaim his lover and save an innocent man from execution.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Beffa

A trick or practical joke, often involving a complex deception (noted in the context of Novel III.8).

Bed Trick

A literary device where one person is substituted for another in a bed, typically in the dark, so that a character unknowingly has sexual relations with someone other than their intended partner.

Fortuna

The personification of luck or fate; the primary driving force of the tales in Day II of the Decameron.

Groom

In the medieval context of the Decameron, a servant who tends to horses, rather than a man about to be married.

Ingegno

An Italian term meaning intelligence, wit, resourcefulness, or ingenuity. It is the defining human quality celebrated in Day III.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Purgatory

In medieval Catholic theology, a state after death where souls are purified before entering Heaven. Boccaccio uses it as a comedic device in Novel III.8.

"Putting the Devil in Hell"

Boccaccio’s most famous sexual metaphor, used in Novel III.10 to satirize religious language through sustained double entendre.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What Italian term refers to the intelligence, wit, and resourcefulness celebrated in Day III of the Decameron?

Ingegno

In the structural progression of the Decameron, Day II focuses on the power of Fortune, while Day III focuses on _____.

Cleverness (or Ingegno)

Why is the virtue of ingegno considered a "Florentine" value in the context of the 14th century?

It reflects a commercial city where wit and enterprise were more important than noble blood.

According to the text, what quality does Boccaccio value most consistently throughout the hundred tales?

Ingegno (intelligence and resourcefulness)

How do the characters of Day III differ from those in Day II regarding their reaction to circumstances?

Instead of waiting for circumstances to change, they change circumstances through the power of their minds.

In the world of the Decameron, ingegno is valued above birth, wealth, physical strength, and _____.

Moral virtue

Key Concepts (2/2)

How did medieval literary convention typically portray the role of women?

Women were usually passive roles, such as prizes to be won, damsels to be rescued, or temptresses.

Which female protagonist in Day III is cited as the most developed example of female agency?

Gillette of Narbonne

What is unique about the way Gillette of Narbonne achieves her victory within the medieval social hierarchy?

She succeeds by understanding and using the rules of the system rather than rebelling against them.

The deceptive device by which Gillette consummates her marriage to Beltramo is known as the "_____ trick."

Bed

Which character from Chaucer’s Canterbury Tales is compared to Gillette of Narbonne for her persistence and intelligence?

The Wife of Bath

How does the Wife of Bath's subversion of patriarchal authority differ from Gillette's?

The Wife of Bath is louder and more openly subversive, while Gillette is quieter and more indirect.
