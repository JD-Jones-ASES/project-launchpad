Tales of the Decameron (Boccaccio)

Chapter 4, Section 3

Boccaccio’s Tale of Masetto da Lamporecchio (The Decameron, Day III, Novel 1)

Study Slides

Anticlerical Satire

Professed Virtue

The nuns’ vows of virginity and their "repute for sanctity."

Actual Behavior

Their "all too human" desires and their willingness to abandon vows when presented with a risk-free opportunity.

The "Nature of a Stone"

The narrator mocks the foolish belief that taking a veil causes a woman to cease having natural human cravings.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Abbess

The female head of a community of nuns. In this tale, she is the final one to succumb to Masetto’s presence, eventually promoting him to steward to keep their secrets safe.

Contadino

A peasant or husbandman; Masetto is described as a "hardy fellow" and "handsome for a contadino."

Ingegno

Italian for "wit," "genius," or "cleverness." It represents the ability to use one's intellect to overcome obstacles or seize opportunities.

Lamporecchio

The home village of Masetto and Nuto.

Masetto da Lamporecchio

The protagonist of the tale; a young gardener who successfully deceives a convent by pretending to be a deaf-mute.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Nuto

The gardener who preceded Masetto at the convent; his complaints about the nuns provide Masetto with the information needed to form his plan.

Steward

The official in charge of managing the convent's estate and labor. Masetto eventually takes over this position after the original steward dies.

The Decameron

The larger work by Giovanni Boccaccio from which this tale is taken, consisting of 100 stories told over ten days.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What was the name of the gardener who worked at the convent before Masetto?

Nuto

Why did Nuto quit his job as the gardener for the nuns?

The wages were low and the nuns were constantly nagging and bothering him.

What physical handicap does Masetto decide to fake to gain employment at the convent?

Deaf-muteness

Why does Masetto believe that being 'dumb' will help him succeed in his plan at the convent?

It ensures that no one will fear he might speak about any illicit activities that occur.

How does Masetto first prove his physical strength to the convent's steward?

By quickly splitting firewood that the previous gardener could not handle.

What reason does the steward give the abbess for why a mute gardener is a safe hire?

He will not be able to 'crack jokes' or flirt with the young women.
