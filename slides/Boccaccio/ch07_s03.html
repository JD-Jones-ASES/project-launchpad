Study Slides

The Horseback Metaphor

A Good Story

Like a well-bred horse, it carries the listener effortlessly to their destination, making the journey pleasant.

A Poor Story

Like a horse that "trots too hard," it causes the listener physical and mental distress.

Elements of Failed Storytelling

Repetition

Using the same words or phrases multiple times (e.g., repeating a word "three, four or six times").

Confusion and Inconsistency

"Harking back" or correcting oneself mid-story by saying "I said not well."

Factual Errors

Mishandling the names of characters or setting one in place of another.

Inappropriate Delivery

A mode of delivery that does not match the character of the persons or the incidents being described.

Key Concepts (1/2)

Who is the narrator of the first tale of Day VI in the Decameron?

Filomena

What distinguishes the first tale of Day VI regarding its length in the Decameron?

It is the shortest complete tale in the book.

In the context of Boccaccio's work, what is the symbolic role of the first tale of Day VI?

It serves as a manifesto on the art of narrative.

Who is the primary female protagonist of the first tale of Day VI?

Madonna Oretta

Where does Madonna Oretta live?

Florence

What is the name of Madonna Oretta's husband?

Messer Geri Spina

Key Concepts (2/2)

What specific offer does the knight make to Madonna Oretta during their journey?

To "carry her on horseback" with a story.

The Italian expression portare a cavallo con una novella literally translates to _____.

To carry on horseback with a story

In Boccaccio's metaphor, what does a "good horse" represent in storytelling?

A tale that carries the listener effortlessly to their destination.

According to the text, why does Boccaccio highlight the knight's failure as a storyteller?

To remind the reader that storytelling is a skill and that doing it poorly is a social offense.

Which ancient philosopher's work argues that the arrangement of incidents determines a story's power?

Aristotle

Concept: Aristotelian Poetics in the Decameron

The principle that the arrangement of incidents, not just content, creates a story's impact.
