Fortuna (Fortune)

The Wheel of Fortune

Narrative Function

In the *Decameron*, Fortune acts as the "great plot-maker," creating the high-stakes situations that test a character's nature.

Ingegno (Intelligence/Wit)

Human Agency

Ingegno is the ability to think quickly, adapt to shifting circumstances, and turn misfortune into an advantage.

The Heroic Ideal

The heroes of Day II are not those who avoid Fortune’s wheel, but those who possess the intelligence to "ride it."

Summary of Fortune’s Reversal

II.1. Martellino

A jester feigns a miracle but is nearly hanged when caught; he is saved by a second deception.

II.2. Rinaldo d'Asti

A merchant is robbed and left in the cold, only to find unexpected shelter and comfort with a widow.

II.3. Three Brothers

Noble brothers lose their inheritance but are restored to wealth after discovering a king's daughter in disguise.

II.4. Landolfo Rufolo

A merchant turns to piracy, is shipwrecked twice, but finds a chest of gems that makes him the richest man in his region.

II.6. Madam Beritola

A noblewoman loses her family during the Sicilian Vespers but is eventually reunited with her sons after years of exile.

II.8. Count of Antwerp

A count is falsely accused and exiled, living as a servant for years before his innocence is proven and his status restored.

II.9. Bernabò of Genoa

A husband is tricked into believing his wife, Zinevra, was unfaithful; she disguises herself as a man to eventually expose the liar.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Boethius (c. 480–524)

A Roman senator and philosopher whose *Consolation of Philosophy* introduced the metaphor of Fortune's wheel to medieval thought.

Fortuna

A Latin and Italian term meaning both "wealth" (fortune) and "fate" (chance); personified as a capricious goddess.

Ingegno

Intelligence, resourcefulness, or native wit; the supreme human quality required to adapt to Fortune.

Picaresque

A genre of fiction featuring a resourceful protagonist (a *picaro*) of low birth who lives by their wits while moving through a corrupt or dangerous world.

Sicilian Vespers (1282)

A historical rebellion in Sicily against French Angevin rule, resulting in a massacre; used as a backdrop for tale II.6.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

The Angevin Court

The royal court in Naples where Boccaccio lived from approximately 1327 to 1340, providing him with the intimate knowledge of the city displayed in his writing.
