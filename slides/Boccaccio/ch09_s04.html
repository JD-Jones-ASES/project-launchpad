Tales of the Decameron (Boccaccio)

Chapter 9, Section 4

The Scholar’s Revenge (Decameron, Day VIII, Novel 7)

Study Slides

The Seasonal Mirror

The Winter Offense

Elena lures the scholar, Rinieri, to wait in a snow-covered courtyard for a rendezvous that never happens. He nearly dies from the cold while she and her lover mock him.

The Summer Retribution

Rinieri tricks Elena into standing naked on a tower roof under a July sun. She suffers from extreme dehydration, sunstroke, and insect stings, nearly dying from heat and exposure.

Central Themes

The Limits of the Beffa

While many tales in the Decameron use the beffa (trick) for comedy, this story pushes trickery to a point of extreme psychological and physical cruelty.

Justice vs. Proportionality

The story forces a confrontation with medieval concepts of vendetta (just vengeance) and excessive cruelty. While the narrator, Pampinea, suggests the retribution is "just," the text highlights t...

The Danger of Ingegno

Intelligence and wit are usually celebrated in the Decameron as tools for love or survival. Here, Boccaccio explores ingegno placed in the service of hatred.

The Power of the Pen

The scholar explicitly threatens Elena with his ability to ruin her reputation through writing, asserting that the "might of the pen is greater far than they suppose."

Key Characters and Entities

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Beffa

A trick or prank, often elaborate, played upon a character; a core narrative device in the Decameron.

Cornemuse

A type of bagpipe; mentioned by the lady when mocking the scholar's chattering teeth.

Ingegno

Intelligence, cleverness, or wit. In this tale, it is used for destruction rather than creation.

Indite

To write or compose, particularly formal documents or literary works.

Necromancy

Black magic involving communication with the dead or spirits; the "lore" Rinieri pretends to use to trick Elena.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Orison

A prayer; specifically the "gibberish" written by the scholar for Elena to recite on the tower.

Tierce/None/Vespers

Traditional markers of time based on canonical hours (Tierce is morning, None is mid-afternoon, Vespers is evening).

Vendetta

The medieval concept of just vengeance or blood-feud, governed by specific moral and legal distinctions.

Withies

Flexible branches (like willow) used for binding or tying; used by the husbandman to repair the ladder.

Key Concepts (1/2)

Who is the narrator of the seventh tale of Day VIII in the Decameron?

Pampinea

Which story is identified as the longest single tale in the entire Decameron?

Day VIII, Tale 7 (The Scholar and the Widow)

What is the primary difference between the trick in Tale VIII, 3 (Calandrino) and the trick in Tale VIII, 7?

Tale VIII, 3 is lighthearted comedy, while Tale VIII, 7 involves extreme revenge and ethical limits.

According to the primary source text, what is the name of the Florentine scholar who studied at Paris?

Rinieri

What is the name of the beautiful widow with whom the scholar falls in love?

Elena

In the analysis section, why is the scholar's initial anonymity considered an unusual choice for Boccaccio?

Boccaccio typically names his characters, but here the scholar represents an intellectual "type."

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the specific seasonal setting of the trick Elena plays on the scholar?

A bitterly cold winter night

How does Elena lure the scholar to wait in her courtyard for hours in the snow?

She promises him a rendezvous that never comes.

Why is the winter setting considered essential to the structure of Tale VIII, 7?

It makes the humiliation physical and genuinely dangerous rather than merely emotional.

What specific seasonal inversion does Boccaccio use to structure the scholar’s revenge?

The scholar is frozen in winter, so he burns Elena in the summer.

In the context of the Decameron, what is "ingegno"?

Intelligence, cleverness, or wit.

What ethical tension is raised by the narrator Pampinea’s endorsement of the scholar's revenge?

The revenge appears disproportionately cruel despite the narrator's claim that it is just.
