Important Terms (1/3)

Black Death / Mortal Pestilence

The devastating pandemic that swept through Europe in 1348, causing widespread death and societal upheaval. Boccaccio specifically refers to it as "the late mortal pestilence."

Proem

The introductory section of a literary work, in which Boccaccio dedicates *The Decameron* and explains his purpose.

Brigata

The Italian word for "company" or "squad," referring to the group of seven ladies and three young men who flee Florence and tell the hundred tales in *The Decameron*.

Gavoccioli

The specific term Boccaccio uses for the characteristic tumors or swellings (buboes) that appeared in the groin or armpits of plague victims, described as being the size of an apple or an egg.

Discourse

Conversation; a formal discussion or treatise on a subject. In the context of the *Decameron*, it refers to the exchange of stories.

Important Terms (2/3)

Prudence

One of the four cardinal virtues; the ability to judge wisely and act accordingly. Boccaccio mentions it in contrast to uncontrollable desire.

Wont

Accustomed; in the habit of. Also used as a noun meaning "custom" or "habit."

Perchance

Perhaps; by chance.

Fortune

Fate or chance, often personified as a goddess (Fortuna) who arbitrarily raises and casts down mortals.

Wherefore

For what reason; why. (Not to be confused with "where.")

Important Terms (3/3)

Commend

To entrust; to praise. "Commend me to" means "give my regards to."

Boccaccio's Description of the Black Death

Boccaccio's distinctive approach

It combines clinical observation (detailing symptoms and spread) with moral reflection (exploring the devastating impact on family, friendship, religion, and civic life).

"What to Watch For" - Key Themes

Shift from General to Particular

Boccaccio moves from the overall physical effects of the plague to its specific social and moral consequences.

Breakdown of Human Duties

He highlights the collapse of ordinary responsibilities: parents abandoning children, priests refusing the sick, and the dead left unburied.

Author's Personal Motivation

Gratitude and Purpose

His own suffering has now abated, leaving only pleasant memories. Driven by gratitude (a virtue he highly commends, unlike ingratitude), he resolves to offer solace to others who might need it.
