Chapter 8, Section 4

Boccaccio’s Decameron, Day VII, Tale 9

Study Slides

Central Characters

Lydia

The young, beautiful, and courageous wife of Nicostrato who falls passionately in love with Pyrrhus.

Nicostrato

A wealthy, elderly nobleman of Argos. He is a distinguished man who is "mightily addicted to the chase" (hunting) and possesses an extraordinary capacity for self-deception.

Pyrrhus

Nicostrato's most trusted servant and companion. Initially skeptical of Lydia’s advances, he demands proof of her sincerity.

Lusca

Lydia’s loyal chambermaid and confidante who acts as the messenger between the lovers.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: Why does Lydia fall in love with Pyrrhus?

Lydia is in the prime of her youth but is married to a man whose years far outnumber her own. She deems Pyrrhus "more worthy than any other man" and claims she is "ever ill at ease" when he is not present.

Q: What is Pyrrhus’s initial reaction to Lydia’s message of love?

He is surprised and doubts the sincerity of the message, fearing that Lydia is merely "putting him to the proof" or that the message did not truly come from her. He initially refuses to wrong his master, who trusts him above all others.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/2)

Q: How does Lydia justify killing the sparrow-hawk to Nicostrato’s guests?

She claims the bird has "cheated" her of time with her husband, as Nicostrato rises at dawn to hunt with it, leaving her alone in bed.

Q: What psychological tactic does Lydia use to get Nicostrato to let her pull his tooth?

She tells him his breath "stinks shrewdly" and has instructed his servants to turn their heads away from him. She then pretends to find a "rotten" tooth and offers to pull it herself to spare him the "cruel way" of a professional surgeon.

Q: What is the outcome of the pear-tree incident?

Nicostrato is convinced that the tree causes anyone in its branches to see "marvels" or "false shows." He apologizes to Lydia for doubting her and orders Pyrrhus to cut the tree down to "vindicate" her honor.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Beffa

A trick or practical joke, often elaborate, played upon a character in Italian literature.

Chirurgeon

An archaic term for a surgeon.

Feign

To pretend or give a false appearance of something (e.g., Lydia feigning sickness to go to the garden).

Jesses

Short leather straps fastened around the legs of a hawk or falcon.

Lewd

In the medieval context used in the text, it refers to being ignorant or unlearned (though the modern sexual connotation was beginning to develop).

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Samite

A rich medieval silk fabric, often interwoven with gold or silver threads.

Troth

Faith, loyalty, or a pledge; often used in the oath "by my troth."
