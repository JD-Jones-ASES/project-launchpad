Tales of the Decameron (Boccaccio)

Chapter 8, Section 4

Lydia’s Triple Proof of Love (Decameron, Day VII, Novel 9)

Study Slides

Core Themes

The Beffa (The Trick)

The story is a supreme example of the "beffa," where a victim is led to believe something patently false, often despite the evidence of their own senses.

Inversion of Courtly Love

Boccaccio parodies romance literature. Typically, a lady sets tasks for a knight; here, the lady (Lydia) must perform impossible feats to prove her devotion to a social inferior (Pyrrhus).

Self-Deception

The story explores the psychology of a fool, demonstrating how trust and a desire to believe can lead a person to reject the testimony of their own eyes.

Fortune vs. Reason

Panfilo introduces the tale by noting that Lydia owed more to the "favour of Fortune" than to the "guidance of reason."

Character Profiles

Character. Role

Key Traits & Motivations

Lydia. Wife of Nicostrato

Courageous, charming, and highly intelligent. She is "mightily enamoured" with Pyrrhus and willing to perform violent or shocking acts to prove her passion.

Nicostrato. Nobleman of Argos

Wealthy and distinguished, but verging on old age. He is "wise and wary" in business but a "fool" in his domestic life, showing an extraordinary capacity for self-deception.

Pyrrhus. Servant/Companion

A "gallant of no mean accomplishment" trusted by Nicostrato. Initially skeptical and cautious, he demands three "impossible" proofs of Lydia's love to ensure he is not being set up.

Lusca. Chambermaid

Lydia's loyal confidante and messenger. She is sharp-tongued (calling Pyrrhus a "blockhead") and instrumental in coordinating the deceptions.

The Climax: The Enchanted Pear-Tree

The Setup

Lydia feigns illness and asks to be taken to the garden. She sits at the foot of a pear-tree and asks Pyrrhus to climb it and shake down fruit.

The Vision

From the tree, Pyrrhus "sees" Nicostrato and Lydia engaging in carnal intercourse and berates them for their indecency.

The Experiment

Nicostrato, confused, climbs the tree to see if it is "enchanted." While he is in the branches, Lydia and Pyrrhus actually engage in the act.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Beffa

A complex trick or practical joke played on a victim, central to many tales in the Decameron.

Chirurgeon

An archaic term for a surgeon.

Feign

To pretend or give a false appearance of something (e.g., Lydia feigning sickness).

Jesses

Short leather straps fastened around the legs of a hawk or falcon.

Lewd

In the medieval context of the source, this referred to the ignorant or unlearned (of the common people), though the modern sexual connotation is also beginning to emerge.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Samite

A rich medieval silk fabric, often interwoven with gold or silver threads.

Troth

Faith, loyalty, or a pledge; used as a common oath ("By my troth").

Key Concepts (1/2)

In the context of the Decameron's Seventh Day, what is the unifying theme of the stories told?

The stories focus on the various tricks that wives play upon their husbands.

How does Lydia's scheme in Tale VII, 9 differ from Peronella's trick in Tale VII, 2?

Lydia's scheme is a calculated campaign of escalating daring rather than a spontaneous improvisation born of necessity.

What is the primary narrative setting for the tale of Lydia and Nicostrato?

The tale is set in the ancient Greek city of Argos.

In the literary analysis of Tale VII, 9, what purpose does the non-Italian setting serve for the audience?

The Greek setting provides an air of the exotic and distances the story from the Florentine audience's immediate social reality.

Who is Pyrrhus in relation to Nicostrato?

Pyrrhus is Nicostrato's most trusted servant and companion.

What three seemingly impossible feats does Lydia offer to perform to prove her love to Pyrrhus?

She must kill Nicostrato's sparrow-hawk, send Pyrrhus a lock of Nicostrato's beard, and send one of Nicostrato's best teeth.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How does Tale VII, 9 subvert the traditional conventions of courtly love?

It inverts the tradition by having the lady perform difficult tasks to prove her devotion rather than the knight.

What is the specific pattern of 'increasing outrageousness' found in Lydia's three tests?

The first is merely shocking, the second is physically destructive, and the third requires the husband to disbelieve his own eyes.

In the narrator Panfilo's introduction, he warns that Lydia's success owed more to _____ than to the guidance of reason.

the favour of Fortune

Why is Pyrrhus initially hesitant to accept Lydia's declaration of love conveyed by Lusca?

He fears that Nicostrato is using Lydia to test his loyalty.

According to Lusca, what rule of loyalty should servants observe toward their masters?

Servants should behave toward their masters as their masters would behave toward them in similar circumstances.

How does Lydia justify killing Nicostrato’s prized sparrow-hawk in front of his guests?

She claims the bird robs her of the time her husband should spend pleasuring her in the mornings.
