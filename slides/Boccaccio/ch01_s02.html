The Decameron

Medieval Narrative Traditions

The Exemplum

Originally short stories used by preachers to illustrate moral lessons. Boccaccio adopted this form but often subverted it by providing ironic or ambiguous "morals."

The Fabliau

Bawdy, humorous verse tales from France. Boccaccio refined these crude plots by adding psychological depth and social commentary.

The Romance Tradition

Tales of chivalry and courtly love. While Boccaccio utilized these themes, he grounded them in realistic settings like the markets and bedrooms of Florence and Naples.

Classical Influence

Boccaccio’s humanist education allowed him to incorporate rhetorical models and themes from Roman authors such as Ovid, Cicero, and Livy.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Allegory

A narrative where characters and events represent abstract ideas or moral principles (e.g., Dante’s journey representing the soul’s path to God).

Canzoniere

Italian for "songbook"; Petrarch’s collection of 366 poems, primarily sonnets addressed to Laura.

Exemplum (pl. exempla)

A short story used in medieval sermons to illustrate a moral point.

Fabliau (pl. fabliaux)

A short, humorous, and often bawdy French verse tale featuring tricks and lustful characters.

Frame Narrative

A "story within a story" structure where an outer narrative provides the context for the inner tales.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Humanism

An intellectual movement focused on the recovery and study of classical Latin and Greek texts; Petrarch is considered its "father."

Novella

Derived from the Italian for "new" or "news"; a short prose tale focused on a realistic setting and a single turning point.

Novellino

An anonymous 13th-century collection of approximately one hundred very brief Italian tales.

Terza Rima

An interlocking three-line rhyme scheme invented by Dante for The Divine Comedy.

Tre Corone

"The Three Crowns"; refers to the trio of Dante, Petrarch, and Boccaccio as the pillars of Italian literature.

Key Concepts (1/2)

Who are the three writers known as the "tre corone" (three crowns) of Italian literature?

Dante Alighieri, Francesco Petrarch, and Giovanni Boccaccio.

Which Italian dialect was established as the literary language of the nation by the Three Crowns?

The Tuscan dialect.

What is the supreme achievement of medieval literature written by Dante Alighieri?

The Divine Comedy (Commedia).

Term: Allegory

Definition: A narrative where characters and events represent abstract ideas or moral principles.

What interlocking three-line rhyme scheme did Dante invent for his epic poem?

Terza rima.

Which three realms are described in Dante's journey during the Divine Comedy?

Hell (Inferno), Purgatory (Purgatorio), and Paradise (Paradiso).

Key Concepts (2/2)

Dante proved that the _____ language could be used for the highest literary art instead of just Latin.

Italian

Who are the two figures that guide Dante through the afterlife in his epic poem?

The Roman poet Virgil and his beloved Beatrice.

In which city did Dante Alighieri die in 1321 after being exiled from Florence?

Ravenna.

Which writer is celebrated as the "poet of the inner life" and the "father of humanism"?

Francesco Petrarch.

What is the title of Petrarch's collection of 366 poems, primarily sonnets?

The Canzoniere (or "songbook").

Who was the idealized beloved to whom Petrarch addressed most of his poems?

Laura.
