Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/3)

Q: Who is crowned queen of Day VI, and what is her reputation among the brigata?

> Queen Elissa (also known as Dido) is the queen of Day VI. She is described as one of the more reserved and thoughtful members of the company.

Q: What is the specific theme decreed for the stories of Day VI?

> The theme focuses on individuals who used an "apt retort" (a *motto*) to avoid loss, danger, or scorn.

Q: How does the length of Day VI reflect its thematic focus?

> Day VI is the shortest day with the briefest tales, reflecting the precision and conciseness required for a successful witty remark or *motto*.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/3)

Q: What does the term *eloquenza* signify in the context of this text?

> *Eloquenza* is the art of speaking well. For Boccaccio, it is a form of intelligence made visible through language.

Q: What is unique about the interruption involving Licisca and Tindaro?

> It is noted as something that "had not happened before"—a loud, external domestic dispute from the kitchen staff reaching the ears of the company and interrupting the start of the formal storytelling session.

Q: What is Licisca’s primary argument during her dispute with Tindaro?

> She argues that women are not as simple or virtuous as Tindaro believes, asserting that they do not wait for the "caprice of father or brothers" to lose their virginity and that they frequently deceive their husbands.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (3/3)

Q: How does Dioneo resolve the argument between the servants?

> He immediately sides with Licisca, declaring her right and Tindaro a fool, which prompts Licisca to gloat and eventually allows the Queen to dismiss them.
