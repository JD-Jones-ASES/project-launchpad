Which classical figures does the narrator use to emphasize Fra Cipolla's extraordinary, albeit unlearned, rhetorical abilities?

A. Tully and Quintilian

B. Solomon and Aristotle

C. Seneca and Cato

D. Plato and Socrates

Hint: Focus on the Roman masters of eloquence mentioned early in the description of the friar's character.

Quiz Question 2

What specific distraction allows Giovanni and Biagio to successfully infiltrate Fra Cipolla's room and steal the 'relic'?

A. Guccio's infatuation with the cook, Nuta, in the inn's kitchen.

B. A public dispute over the authenticity of the parrot feather.

C. Fra Cipolla's lengthy afternoon nap after his breakfast.

D. The ringing of the church bells for the afternoon service.

Hint: Look for the character description of the servant and his inability to resist the 'green boughs' of the kitchen.

Quiz Question 3

In his improvised travelogue, Fra Cipolla claims to have visited 'India Pastinaca.' What absurdity does he witness there as proof of his journey?

A. Pruning-hooks that fly like birds.

B. Men and women who wear pattens on mountains.

C. Bread that is carried in staves and wine in sacks.

D. A river that flows upward instead of downward.

Hint: Consider the linguistic pun the friar makes regarding agricultural tools and feathers.

Quiz Question 4

What is the satirical significance of the relic Fra Cipolla calls 'the rib of the Verbum Caro hie thee to the casement'?

A. It is a linguistic mangling of a standard Latin liturgical phrase.

B. It refers to a physical bone taken from the ruins of Solomon's Temple.

C. It is a relic supposedly given to him by the Sieur de Chatillon.

D. It represents a literal rib from the Angel Gabriel.

Hint: Think about how medieval clergy might use the common people's lack of Latin to create nonsense 'holy' objects.

Quiz Question 5

How does Fra Cipolla rhetorically resolve the discovery of charcoal instead of the feather in his casket?

A. He claims God swapped the boxes because the feast of St. Lawrence was approaching.

B. He accuses his servant Guccio of being a thief and a rascallion.

C. He argues that the feather has been miraculously transformed into charcoal to test their faith.

D. He admits he was robbed but claims the charcoal was blessed by the Patriarch of Jerusalem.

Hint: Recall the calendar-based excuse he provides to explain why the charcoal is actually relevant to the current date.

Quiz Question 6

The 'Ladies' Vale' is described as having a very specific geometric and architectural layout. Which of the following best describes its center?

A. A diminutive lake with a bottom of fine gravel and visible fish.

B. A grand palace modeled after the theatres of antiquity.

C. A dense thicket of firs and cypresses that blocks all sunlight.

D. An altar dedicated to St. Lawrence and the Holy Spirit.

Hint: Consider the feature that prompted the ladies to undress and enjoy a diversion from the heat.
