Tales of the Decameron (Boccaccio)

Chapter 1, Section 5

The Literary Architecture of Boccaccio’s Decameron

Study Slides

Key Structural Facts:

The Brigata

The group consists of seven women and three men of noble birth.

The Setting

Fleeing the chaos of the Black Death, the group retreats to a series of country villas in the hills outside Florence.

The Routine

To pass the time and combat despair, they tell stories. Each day, a king or queen is elected to preside over the group and set a theme for that day's tales.

The Total

Ten storytellers, telling one tale each per day for ten days, totaling 100 tales.

The Thematic Arc of the Ten Days

Day. Theme

Description

I. No set theme

Establishes the tone and concerns of the work.

II. Fortune’s reversals

Tales of unexpected happiness following adversity.

III. Cleverness triumphant

Tales of those who use wit to obtain their desires.

IV. Love and tragedy

Tales of love with unhappy endings.

V. Love rewarded

Tales of lovers finding happiness after terrible adventures.

VI. Quick wit

Tales of escaping danger or mockery through clever remarks.

VII. Tricks of wives

Tales of tricks wives play on their husbands.

The Brigata: The Storytellers (1/2)

Pampinea

The eldest woman; practical, authoritative, and the first queen.

Filomena

Favors tales of fortune and adventure.

Neifile

Prefers tales of cleverness and ingenuity.

Fiammetta

Boccaccio’s literary alter ego of his beloved; focuses on love.

Filostrato

Meaning "vanquished by love"; king during the day of tragic love.

The Brigata: The Storytellers (2/2)

Panfilo

Meaning "all-loving"; tells the very first and very last tales.

Dioneo

The "rebel" and "provocateur." He is exempt from the daily themes and always tells his story last. Despite his irreverence, he tells the final, morally serious tale of Patient Griselda.

Elissa, Lauretta, and Emilia

Other members of the group with less sharply defined preferences.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Brigata

Italian for "company" or "brigade"; the organized group of ten young people who flee the plague and narrate the tales.

Cornice

Italian for "frame"; the scholarly term for the frame narrative that surrounds and connects the one hundred stories.

Dioneo

A name derived from Dione (mother of Venus); the name of the most complex storyteller, the "rule-breaker" and "provocateur" of the group.

Proem

An introduction or preface; in the *Decameron*, this contains Boccaccio's dedication to women and his explanation for the work.

Pseudonym

A fictitious name; used by the ten storytellers to hide their real identities.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Magnanimity

Greatness of spirit or generosity; the focus of the tales on the final day of the retreat.
