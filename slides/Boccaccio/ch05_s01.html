Summary of Plot and Theme

IV.1. Tancred & Ghismonda

Tancred kills his daughter's lover, Guiscardo. Ghismonda delivers a powerful speech defending love as natural and Guiscardo as noble in virtue before taking her own life.

IV.2. Fra Alberto & Madonna Lisetta

A corrupt friar pretends to be the Angel Gabriel to seduce a woman. This is the only comic tale of the Day, serving as an ironic counterpoint.

IV.3. Three Couples in Crete

Three Florentine couples elope; jealousy and misfortune eventually lead to the destruction of all three relationships.

IV.4. Gerbino

A Sicilian prince attacks a ship to rescue the daughter of the King of Tunis. The princess is killed by her own attendants, and Gerbino is executed for treaty violations.

IV.5. Lisabetta

Lisabetta’s brothers murder her lover, Lorenzo. She buries his head in a pot of basil, watering it with her tears until her brothers steal it, leading to her death from grief.

IV.6. Andreuola & Gabriotto

Gabriotto dies of a sudden seizure in a garden. Andreuola is accused of poisoning him but is proven innocent; both lovers are ultimately mourned.

IV.7. Simona & Pasquino

Pasquino dies after rubbing a sage leaf on his teeth. Accused of murder, Simona demonstrates his actions with the same leaf and dies, proving her innocence through accidental death.

Boccaccio vs. Chaucer

Boccaccio

His tragic tales are direct, passionate, and emotionally intense, demanding the reader feel the injustice of the situation.

Chaucer

In works like *Troilus and Criseyde*, Chaucer is more meditative and philosophical, pulling back from raw emotion to contemplate the nature of suffering.

Glossary of Important Terms

Cornice

The "frame" story of the *Decameron*; the narrative involving the storytellers fleeing the plague.

Natural Law

The philosophical principle that certain moral truths are inherent in nature and discoverable by reason, independent of human laws or social conventions.

Nobiltà (Nobility)

In the context of Day IV, the state of being noble, argued by Boccaccio to be a product of character and virtue rather than social rank or bloodline.

Vidas

Brief prose biographies of Provençal troubadours that often blended fact with legend; the source of the "eaten heart" motif.
