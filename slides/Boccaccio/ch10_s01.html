Chapter 10, Section 1

Boccaccio’s Decameron, Day IX (Day the Ninth)

Study Slides

Key Characteristics

Theme

Like Day I, Day IX has no prescribed subject, allowing stories to range freely across various settings such as monasteries, bedrooms, marketplaces, and roads.

Tone

The stories are generally shorter and less morally troubling than the preceding days. Several tales are brief comic anecdotes or scenes depending on a single reversal.

Structural Function

Day IX acts as a reprise, returning to familiar ground and revisiting the Decameron’s deepest concerns—such as clerical hypocrisy and the comedy of fortune—before the work’s conclusion.

Anticlerical Satire

Target of Satire

The satire targets hypocrisy rather than Christianity itself. It focuses on the failure of religious institutions to live up to their own ideals (e.g., vows of chastity vs. reality).

Evolution of the Satire

Boccaccio employs different modes of satire throughout the work:

Corrective Nature

The satire is intended to be corrective rather than destructive, laughing at the Church to hold it accountable to its own standards.

The Bed-Trick and the Fabliau Tradition

The Fabliau

Originally Old French verse tales of 300–400 lines, these stories featured tradesmen and clerics in comic, often bawdy, situations.

Boccaccian Transformation

Boccaccio transforms the verse *fabliau* into prose *novelle*, adding psychological depth and narrative sophistication.

Summary of Day IX Tales

Novel. Title/Subject

Key Plot Elements

IX.1. Madonna Francesca

Francesca rids herself of two unwanted suitors by tricking one into posing as a corpse and the other into carrying it away.

IX.2. The Abbess’s Breeches

An abbess, rushing to catch a nun with a lover, accidentally wears a priest’s breeches on her head, exposing her own hypocrisy.

IX.3. Calandrino’s Pregnancy

Bruno and Buffalmacco trick the gullible Calandrino into believing he is pregnant; he pays them for a fake "cure."

IX.4. Cecco di Fortarigo

A historical poet, Cecco Angiolieri, gambles away his companion's money and clothes, then shamelessly blames the companion.

IX.5. Calandrino in Love

Calandrino is tricked into believing a magic scroll will make a young woman love him; he is eventually caught and beaten by his wife.

IX.6. The Inn at Forlì

A complex "bed-trick" involving a misplaced cradle that leads multiple characters into the wrong beds during the night.

IX.7. Talano d'Imolese

A man dreams of a wolf attacking his wife; she ignores his warning and is attacked exactly as foreseen.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Anticlerical Satire

A genre of literature that mocks the clergy or religious institutions, typically focusing on the gap between religious ideals and the actual behavior of priests, monks, and nuns.

Bed-Trick

A narrative device in which characters switch beds or sleep with the wrong person due to darkness, deception, or mistaken identity.

Fabliau (pl. Fabliaux)

A short, comic, often bawdy verse tale popular in medieval France, typically featuring middle- or lower-class characters.

Mimesis

A term used by critic Erich Auerbach to describe the representation of reality in literature; in Boccaccio, it refers to the realistic portrayal of everyday life.

Novella

A short prose narrative; the format Boccaccio used to adapt older verse traditions like the *fabliau*.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Reprise

A return to earlier themes or styles; in the *Decameron*, Day IX acts as a lighter return to the "free theme" of Day I.

Spatial Choreography

The precise arrangement and movement of characters within a specific physical setting to achieve a narrative or comic effect.
