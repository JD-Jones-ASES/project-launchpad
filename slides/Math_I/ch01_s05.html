Exponents and Powers

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Exponential Notation

A shorthand method for expressing repeated multiplication of the same number by itself. Instead of writing `b × b × b`, we write `b^3`.

Base and Exponent

In an expression `b^n`, `b` is the base (the number being multiplied), and `n` is the exponent (the number of times the base is used as a factor).

Special Powers

Zero Exponent Rule

For any nonzero number `b`, `b^0 = 1`. This is a fundamental rule in mathematics.

Order of Operations

Exponents are evaluated *after* operations inside parentheses but *before* multiplication, division, addition, and subtraction. This hierarchy is crucial for accurate calculations.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Powers of 10

Expressions like `10^n` are especially useful for representing very large or very small numbers (as seen in scientific notation), where `10^n` is a 1 followed by `n` zeros.

Important Terms

Exponent (or Power)

The number `n` in `b^n` that indicates how many times the base `b` is used as a factor in multiplication.

Base

The number `b` in `b^n` that is being multiplied repeatedly.

Squared

An exponent of 2 (e.g., `b^2`). It literally means the base multiplied by itself twice.

Cubed

An exponent of 3 (e.g., `b^3`). It literally means the base multiplied by itself three times.

Zero Exponent

The rule that states any nonzero number raised to the power of 0 equals 1.

Understanding the Parts of an Exponential Expression

Base (`b`)

This is the number that is being multiplied.

Exponent (`n`)

This number, written as a smaller superscript, tells you how many times to use the base as a factor in the multiplication. It is also sometimes called the "power."

Special Powers and Their Meanings

`b^2`

Read as "b squared." For example, `5^2` means `5 × 5`. This term comes from the formula for the area of a square, which is `side × side`.

`b^3`

Read as "b cubed." For example, `4^3` means `4 × 4 × 4`. This term comes from the formula for the volume of a cube, which is `edge × edge × edge`.

`b^1`

Any number raised to the power of 1 is simply the number itself. For example, `7^1 = 7`.
