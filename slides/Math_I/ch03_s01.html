Greatest Common Factor and Least Common Multiple Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Factor

A whole number that divides another whole number evenly, leaving no remainder.

Multiple

The product of a whole number and any other whole number (0, 1, 2, 3, ...).

Greatest Common Factor (GCF)

The largest factor that two or more whole numbers share. It's used primarily for simplifying fractions.

Least Common Multiple (LCM)

The smallest positive number that is a multiple of two or more whole numbers. It's primarily used for finding common denominators when adding or subtracting fractions.

Relationship between GCF and LCM

For any two numbers, *a* and *b*, the product of their GCF and LCM is equal to the product of the numbers themselves: `GCF(a,b) * LCM(a,b) = a * b`.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Distinguishing GCF and LCM

The GCF is always less than or equal to the smallest of the numbers, while the LCM is always greater than or equal to the largest of the numbers.

Important Terms

Factor

A whole number that divides another whole number without leaving a remainder.

Greatest Common Factor (GCF)

The largest number that is a factor of two or more given numbers. Often denoted as `GCF(a,b)` or simply `(a,b)` in some contexts.

Multiple

The result of multiplying a given whole number by another whole number (e.g., 0, 1, 2, 3, ...).

Least Common Multiple (LCM)

The smallest positive number that is a multiple of two or more given numbers. Often denoted as `LCM(a,b)` or simply `(a,b)` in some contexts.

Prime Factorization

The process of breaking down a composite number into its prime number components. This method is highly efficient for finding GCF and LCM of larger numbers.

The Importance of GCF and LCM

GCF

is crucial for **simplifying fractions** (dividing both numerator and denominator by their greatest common factor).

LCM

is essential for finding the **least common denominator (LCD)** when adding, subtracting, or comparing fractions with different denominators.

Understanding Factors and Multiples

Factors

When we talk about factors, we're looking for numbers that can divide a given number exactly.

Multiples

Multiples are the results of multiplication. You can think of them as skipping counting.
