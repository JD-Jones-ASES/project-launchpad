Chapter 3, Section 5: Adding and Subtracting Fractions

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Common Denominators are Essential

You can only add or subtract fractions if they represent pieces of the same size, which means they must have the same denominator. Think of the denominator as the "name" of the pieces.

Adding and Subtracting Fractions with Like Denominators

When fractions already share the same denominator, you simply add or subtract their numerators and keep the common denominator.

Adding and Subtracting Fractions with Unlike Denominators

Least Common Denominator (LCD)

The smallest common multiple of the denominators of two or more fractions. It's crucial for rewriting fractions so they can be combined.

Simplifying Fractions

After adding or subtracting, always reduce the resulting fraction to its simplest form, where the numerator and denominator have no common factors other than 1.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Do Not Add or Subtract Denominators

A common mistake is to add or subtract the denominators as well as the numerators. Always remember that the denominator stays the same once a common one is found.

Important Terms (1/2)

Numerator

The top number in a fraction, representing the number of parts being considered.

Denominator

The bottom number in a fraction, representing the total number of equal parts into which the whole has been divided.

Like Denominators

Denominators that are the same.

Unlike Denominators

Denominators that are different.

Least Common Denominator (LCD)

The smallest number that is a multiple of two or more denominators. It is used to create equivalent fractions with the same denominator.

Important Terms (2/2)

Simplify (a fraction)

To reduce a fraction to its lowest terms by dividing both the numerator and the denominator by their greatest common factor.

Improper Fraction

A fraction where the numerator is greater than or equal to the denominator (e.g., 7/5).

Mixed Number

A number consisting of a whole number and a proper fraction (e.g., 1 and 2/5).

Important Considerations and Common Mistakes

Always Simplify

Make it a habit to simplify your final fraction. For example, `4/10` should always be simplified to `2/5`.
