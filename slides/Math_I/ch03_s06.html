Adding and Subtracting Mixed Numbers: A Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Separating Whole and Fraction Parts

When adding or subtracting mixed numbers, the whole number portions and the fraction portions are handled distinctly.

Least Common Denominator (LCD)

Before fractions can be added or subtracted, they must have a common denominator. The LCD is the smallest common multiple of the denominators, making calculations simpler.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Alternative Method

Mixed numbers can also be converted to improper fractions first, then added or subtracted, and finally converted back to a mixed number. This method bypasses borrowing but can involve larger numbers.

Important Terms

Mixed Number

A number consisting of an integer (whole number) and a proper fraction, for example, $2\frac{2}{3}$.

Least Common Denominator (LCD)

The smallest common multiple of the denominators of two or more fractions. It is used to create equivalent fractions with a common denominator for addition and subtraction.

Improper Fraction

A fraction in which the numerator is greater than or equal to the denominator, for example, $\frac{17}{12}$.

Borrowing (Regrouping)

Simplify

To reduce a fraction to its lowest terms by dividing both the numerator and denominator by their greatest common factor.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the fundamental strategy for adding and subtracting mixed numbers?

The key idea is to handle the whole-number parts and the fraction parts separately.

What is the first step for adding or subtracting mixed numbers with unlike denominators?

Rewrite the fraction parts with their Least Common Denominator (LCD).

List the main steps for adding mixed numbers after finding a common denominator.

Add the fraction parts. Add the whole-number parts. If the fraction part is improper, convert it and add the extra whole number. Simplify the fraction part.

What should you do if the sum of the fraction parts when adding mixed numbers results in an improper fraction?

Convert the improper fraction to a mixed number, then add the resulting whole number to the existing whole-number sum.

When is 'borrowing' (or regrouping) necessary in subtracting mixed numbers?

Borrowing is necessary when the fraction part you are subtracting from is smaller than the fraction part you are subtracting.

What is a common error to avoid when borrowing to increase the fraction part of a mixed number?

Make sure to add $d/d$ (one whole written with the common denominator) to the fraction part, not just $1/d$. For example, if the LCD is $12$, add $12/12$.

How do you subtract a mixed number from a whole number (e.g., $8 - 3\,5/8$)?

Rewrite the whole number as a mixed number by borrowing $1$ (e.g., $8 = 7\,8/8$), then proceed with subtraction. So, $7\,8/8 - 3\,5/8 = 4\,3/8$.
