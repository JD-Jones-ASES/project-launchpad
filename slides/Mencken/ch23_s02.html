According to Mencken, what is the fundamental nature of democracy itself?

Mencken asserts that democracy is, in essence, a form of 'wholesale bribery'.

How does Mencken view corruption in relation to the democratic system?

Mencken sees corruption not as a disease of democracy, but as its inevitable and essential operating principle.

What type of evidence does Mencken use to support his claim that democratic corruption is universal and predictable?

He draws on a wide range of historical examples, from Friedrich von Gentz and Tocqueville to the Grant and Wilson administrations.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What 'Nietzschean' idea does Mencken evoke regarding the 'herd' (the democratic populace)?

Mencken suggests that the 'herd' is not merely stupid but actively complicit in its own exploitation.

Describe H.L. Mencken's adopted persona in the 'CH23_ANALYSIS' section.

He adopts the persona of a worldly-wise observer, a veteran journalist who has seen it all, explaining the obvious to an audience too genteel to acknowledge it.

Name the three distinct rhetorical registers Mencken uses to construct his argument in this section.

The three registers are: the clinical register of the political scientist, the historian's register, and the satirist's register.

What is the purpose of Mencken's 'clinical register' (e.g., 'democracy is itself a form of wholesale bribery')?

It establishes a blunt, undeniable thesis statement that refuses to be misunderstood, often simple and direct.

How does Mencken's 'satirist's register' typically conduct its argument?

It conducts the argument through vivid, often humorous or cynical images rather than pure logic, making the argument felt as well as understood.
