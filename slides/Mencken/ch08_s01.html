The Nature of the Free Man vs. The Common Man

Attribute. The Free Man

The Common Man ("Homo boobiens")

Primary Driver. Self-reliance and resolution

Envy and the desire for security

Status. A "sort of god" in his own world

An "ox" or "anthropoid" subspecies

Capacity. Capable of "doing without" and facing "dreadful loneliness"

No talent for self-reliance; comparable to an animal

Relationship to Power. Independent and defiant

Submissive, "licking the butcher behind the ear"

Historical Context: 1920s America

The Eighteenth Amendment (Prohibition)

Personal freedom restricted by majority morality.

The Palmer Raids (1919–1920)

Targeted political dissidents.

The Scopes Trial (1925)

Scientific inquiry pitted against popular religious sentiment.

Diction and Imagery

Elevated Diction

Words like "magnificent," "resolution," and "enterprise" are reserved for liberty.

Animal Imagery

The proletariat is described as "anthropoid" or an "ox." This suggests the relationship between the ruler (the butcher) and the ruled (the ox) is biological rather than political.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Anthropoid

Resembling a human being but not quite one; used by Mencken to reduce the proletariat to a subspecies.

Antithesis

The pairing of opposites for rhetorical effect (e.g., liberty vs. security).

Grandiloquently

Speaking in a lofty, pompous style; used to describe the mob’s empty talk of liberty.

Homo boobiens

Mencken’s cynical term for the common man or the "boob."

Mountebanks

Charlatans or frauds; Mencken’s term for democratic politicians.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Poltroonery

Utter cowardice; applied to the democratic electorate to contrast them with the chivalric tradition.

Votaries

Devoted followers, often of a cult; Mencken uses this to describe the few genuine lovers of liberty.

Who does Mencken credit with first formulating the ideas of freedom that prevail today?

Country gentlemen, aided by poets and philosophers, with occasional help from an eccentric king.

Provide an example Mencken uses to illustrate that even absolute monarchs could support aspects of liberty.

Frederick the Great, an absolute monarch, gave the first legal support in law for free speech.

When a city mob fights and wins, what two actions does Mencken say it takes regarding freedom and 'professional libertarians'?

First, it destroys every form of freedom not directed wholly to its immediate needs. Second, it butchers all professional libertarians.

What example does Mencken use to show the danger for 'professional libertarians' during city revolutions?

He suggests Thomas Jefferson, if living in Paris in 1793, would have made an even narrower escape from the guillotine than Thomas Paine.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How does Mencken differentiate between 'false forms of liberty' and 'true liberty' in the mind of the 'inferior man'?

The inferior man can imagine 'false forms' like choosing between two political 'mountebanks.' However, 'true liberty' is incomprehensible to him.

What essential quality does Mencken argue 'genuine liberty' demands of its adherents?

Courage. The man who loves liberty must be willing to fight for it and, even more arduously, endure it.

List some of the key characteristics Mencken associates with 'true liberty.'

Self-reliance, resolution, enterprise, and the capacity for doing without.
