Study Slides

Rhetorical Context and Purpose

Target Audience

Educated Americans who are skeptical of politics and suspect that democracy is a "sham."

Primary Purpose

To demolish the reader's assumptions about democracy so thoroughly that they can no longer view the system with "innocence." Mencken seeks to frame democracy as a "confidence trick" played on the "...

Diction and Register Mixing

Example

"A stale Christian bilge ran through their veins."

Effect

The collision of the coarse word "bilge" with the high-flown "ran through their veins" suggests that what others view as idealism is actually "rancid piety."

Theological Vocabulary

Mencken applies words like seraphic, mystical, and omniscience to democracy. This implies that democracy is not a political system, but a "credulous" religion.

Syntax and Rhythm

Musical Cadence

Short, rhythmic sentences create a sense of pleasantness that Mencken then reveals to be a lie.

Antithesis

He uses parallel structures to contrast opposing ideas, such as "no harsh bawling from below; there was only a dulcet twittering from above." This forces the reader to see the relationship between ...

Sentence Variation

He alternates between long, "periodic" sentences that suggest scholarly deliberation and short, declarative sentences that "puncture" that atmosphere with sharp verdicts.

The Musical Metaphor

Artifice

It suggests democracy was "composed" and "rehearsed" rather than being a natural right or an achievement of the people.

Seduction

The alliterative, "hissing" s-sounds in "sweet, soft" suggest a "whispering sound of seduction" rather than intellectual conviction.

Performance

It frames democracy as a sales pitch or a performance intended to deceive a gullible audience.

Glossary of Key Terms

Term. Definition

Context in Text

Antithesis. The placing of opposing ideas in parallel grammatical structures.

Used to contrast "bawling from below" with "twittering from above."

Bilge. Foul water in a ship's hull; worthless or contemptible talk.

Used to describe the "stale Christian" influence on democratic thought.

Dulcet. Sweet and pleasant to the ear.

Describes the "twittering" of those who introduced democracy.

Ineffable. Too great to be expressed in words (usually reserved for the divine).

Used ironically to describe the "virtues" of the common man.

Irony. A discrepancy between appearance and reality; meaning the opposite of the literal word.

The structural foundation of Mencken’s "mock-historian" narrative.

Jerkin. Working-class clothing of a previous era.

Used to dress Rousseau’s "noble savage" as a comic figure.

Register Mixing. The use of both elevated/scholarly and earthy/colloquial language.

Used to create "explosions of meaning" by contrasting high ideals with low reality.
