Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/3)

Q: According to the text, why is the "inferior man" unable to understand the desire for liberty in his superiors?

The inferior man is a "natural slave" who cannot apprehend liberty except as an appetite for a good he is incapable of possessing. This leads to envy and a desire to suppress those who harbor a genuine desire for liberty.

Q: What historical examples does Mencken provide to show that the American public opposed beneficial government bureaus?

Mencken notes that the American Department of Agriculture was almost unanimously opposed by farmers when it began in 1830. Additionally, he mentions that John Adams was denounced as a "scoundrel" for proposing a Weather Bureau.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/3)

Q: What did Sir Henry Maine argue would have happened if universal suffrage had been established during the eras of scientific invention?

Maine argued that universal suffrage would have prohibited the spinning-jenny, the power loom, and the threshing-machine. It would have prevented the adoption of the Gregorian Calendar and led to the proscription of Roman Catholics and Dissenters.

Q: Why does the text claim that "Fundamentalism" is popular among the "peasantry"?

Fundamentalism is popular because it offers a simple cosmogony (such as the first chapter of Genesis) that a "yokel" can grasp instantly, whereas the cosmogonies of educated men are inordinately complex and require a special habit of thought.

Q: What role did the American Legion play in the "pogrom against Reds" and the suppression of the arts?

The American Legion provided "heroic services" in destroying the rights of fellow proletarians during the post-war crusade against "Reds" and used "clubs against fiddlers" who played the music of Beethoven and Bach in the Middle West.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (3/3)

Q: How is the censorship of literature in Boston described?

Censorship in Boston is led by "Methodist anthropoids" and the "Chandala of the Latin rite," who carry out a violent crusade against books of sound quality, forcing citizens to procure literature by stealth.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the primary shift in Mencken's argumentative strategy in Section I.8 of "The Effects Upon Progress"?

Mencken shifts from focusing on the character of "democratic man" to examining the consequences for civilization when this creature is given political power.

What is Mencken's central thesis regarding democracy's impact on human progress in Section I.8?

His central thesis is that democracy consistently retards human progress at every turn.

How is Mencken's rhetorical situation described in Section I.8, and what does he aim to present?

He is described as a prosecutor delivering a closing argument, presenting "evidence of harm" after having established the defendant's character.

Who is Mencken's intended audience for Section I.8?

The educated reader who fancies himself "above the herd," invited to survey the "wreckage that democracy has made."
