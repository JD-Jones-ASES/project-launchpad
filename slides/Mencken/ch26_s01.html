The Decline of Influence

The Great Depression

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is 'Notes on Democracy' by Mencken, beyond being a simple treatise?

It is described as a sustained performance, tracing an arc that is as much tonal as it is logical.

What does Mencken mean by the 'inferior man' in Part I of 'Notes on Democracy'?

It is Mencken's Nietzschean term for the average citizen, whose intellectual limitations, as he sees them, make genuine self-government impossible.

According to Mencken, what are some key characteristics of the 'inferior man'?

Credulous, envious, hostile to excellence, and easily led by demagogues.

What is the primary rhetorical strategy Mencken employs in Part I of 'Notes on Democracy'?

Diagnosis, adopting the persona of a clinical observer using mock-scientific vocabulary and taxonomic metaphors.

List some of the key rhetorical tools Mencken uses in Part I.

Irony, antithesis, extended metaphor, hyperbole, and register mixing.

How does Mencken's coinage 'Homo boobiens' exemplify his rhetorical work in Part I?

The coinage itself does the rhetorical work by reducing a political category to a zoological joke.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What principle does Mencken argue governs the institutions of the democratic state in Part II?

The selection of mediocrity; the democratic state does not merely tolerate incompetence, it requires it.

How does the democratic state, according to Mencken, treat able individuals versus demagogues?

The able man is punished for his ability, while the demagogue is rewarded for his willingness to flatter the mob.

How does Mencken's rhetorical strategy change from Part I to Part II?

It shifts from diagnosis (detached naturalist) to prosecution (prosecuting attorney building a case).

Describe how Mencken's syntax reflects the rhetorical shift in Part II.

The sentences grow more elaborate, the catalogues longer, and the periodic constructions more punishing.

Name a few new rhetorical terms that emerge in Part II.

Zeugma, parallelism, and the catalogue as a rhetorical device.

What is Mencken's main argument about democracy and liberty in Part III?

Democracy, far from protecting liberty, is liberty's most dangerous enemy because the mob enforces intolerance through the state.
