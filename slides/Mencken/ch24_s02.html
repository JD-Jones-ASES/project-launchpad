Key Concepts (1/2)

In "The Future of Democracy" Coda, what is Mencken's primary rhetorical challenge regarding a solution?

To close a sustained polemic without offering a remedy, and to make the absence of a remedy feel like an honest and courageous position.

How does Mencken redefine the terms of his argument about democracy's future?

He suggests democracy may be a self-limiting disease, consumed by its own contradictions, rather than being overthrown by enemies.

What literary device does Mencken use to maintain a clinical stance while delivering a bleak prognosis for democracy?

The medical metaphor, borrowed from pathology, allowing him to treat democracy as a patient with a self-limiting disease.

According to Mencken, how does democracy function similarly to a religion?

It has dogmas, miracles, saints, heretics, and sacraments, making political belief a form of faith immune to rational criticism.

What is the significance of Mencken's admission "I don't know" in the opening sentence of the Coda?

It's a rare admission for him, signaling the final phase of his rhetorical performance and implicitly rebuking the certainty of democratic true believers.

Describe the shift in Mencken's voice and tone in this section compared to earlier parts of the book.

The satirist lowers his volume, adopting a more measured authority and deliberate tone, like a man delivering a verdict rather than enjoying a fight.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What are the two competing registers of vocabulary Mencken uses in this section?

Medical/pathological (e.g., diagnosis, therapeutics) and theological (e.g., faith, dogma, miracles).

What is the purpose of Mencken's deliberate collision of medical and theological vocabulary?

To diagnose a faith, applying the cold instruments of pathology to something its adherents consider sacred, suggesting political belief is a form of faith.

Define "ratiocination" as Mencken uses it.

The process of logical reasoning, which Mencken deploys to describe something democracy, in his view, renders unnecessary for the mob.

Define "puissant" as used in the text.

Powerful, mighty; an archaic word Mencken uses with faint ironic edge to describe modern democratic states.

What is "usufructs" in Mencken's context?

The legal right to enjoy the use and advantages of another's property; Mencken uses it to describe what democratic man loses when equality strips distinction...

Explain "brummagem" as Mencken applies it.

Cheap, showy, counterfeit; Mencken applies it to democratic titles and honors, suggesting they are knockoffs of genuine aristocratic distinction.
