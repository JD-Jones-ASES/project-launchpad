Study Slides

I. Key Concepts and Themes (1/2)

Primary Drives

Rage, fear, and a craving for physical pleasure.

Incapacity for Empathy

An inability to imagine the good of others, which Mencken argues inevitably leads to a spirit of persecution.

The Peasant Archetype

Mencken identifies the peasant farmer as the purest specimen of the inferior man, whose theology and politics are driven by unmitigated self-interest and a desire for violence against dissenters.

A Tom cat or rooster

Representing crude sexuality and barnyard morality.

A wolf

Representing inherent cruelty.

I. Key Concepts and Themes (2/2)

A boar

Representing base biological functions.

Quiz Question 1

In the analysis of Mencken's rhetorical strategy, what is the primary purpose of reframing political behavior as a result of 'glandular secretion'?

A. To provide a scientific foundation for a new theory of democratic governance.

B. To strip the 'democratic man' of his status as a rational agent.

C. To align political science with the emerging medical discoveries of the 1920s.

D. To argue that biological evolution will eventually fix the flaws of democracy.

Hint: Consider how the shift from philosophy to endocrinology affects the 'dignity' of the subject.

Quiz Question 2

Mencken utilizes the theories of John B. Watson primarily because they allow him to do which of the following?

A. Explain the complex inner consciousness of the 'inferior man'.

B. Treat human beings as though they were laboratory specimens.

C. Support the idea that education can modify behavior in a democratic society.

D. Reconcile the differences between Freudian psychology and biological determinism.

Hint: Think about the role of the 'mock-clinician' persona mentioned in the text.

Quiz Question 3

What is the rhetorical effect of Mencken's use of the term 'tumescence' when describing an infant's behavior?

A. It provides a clinical justification for infant healthcare reform.

B. It softens the harshness of his psychological critique through medical jargon.

C. It uses medical precision to create discomfort and shock in the reader.

D. It indicates a genuine shift in Mencken's interest from politics to pediatrics.

Hint: Recall how Mencken mixes high-register medical terms with 'low' subjects.

Quiz Question 4

According to the analysis, how does Mencken's syntax change when he shifts his focus to the 'peasant farmer'?

A. The sentences become longer and more flowery to mirror rural tradition.

B. The rhythm becomes more staccato, with shorter and more declarative sentences.

C. The use of colons increases to provide detailed scientific evidence for the farmer's behavior.

D. He adopts a passive voice to maintain clinical neutrality.

Hint: Look for the description of 'sentences growing shorter' and feeling like 'verdicts'.

Quiz Question 5

In the close reading of the passage regarding the 'anthropoid appetite,' what does the construction 'the not true' imply about the 'inferior man'?

A. It suggests a sophisticated philosophical commitment to nihilism.

B. It implies that self-deception is a biological necessity rather than an intellectual error.

C. It indicates that the common citizen is incapable of understanding any truth at all.

D. It shows Mencken's struggle to find the correct English word for 'falsehood'.

Hint: Consider the link between a physical 'thirst' and an abstract concept.

Quiz Question 6

A. To provide a humorous relief from the dense clinical vocabulary.

B. To suggest that rural life is more 'natural' and therefore superior to urban life.

C. To systematically dehumanize the democratic citizen by isolating specific crude traits.
