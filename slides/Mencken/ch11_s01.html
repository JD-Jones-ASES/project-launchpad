Key Concepts (1/2)

According to Mencken, what is the significance of the distinction between representative and direct democracy?

Mencken concludes, with characteristic finality, that the distinction between representative democracy and direct democracy does not matter.

In Mencken's view, who is the ultimate 'boss' and source of sovereignty in democratic states?

The 'lowly Christian' or the mob, which holds actual sovereignty, sometimes both actually and legally.

Why does Mencken believe the mob is often misled by 'mountebanks and scoundrels'?

Because its 'credulity and imbecility' (stupidity) cover a wider area than its simple desires, making it susceptible to manipulation.

How does Mencken categorize different forms of government, such as constitutional monarchy, representative republic, or pure democracy?

He states they are 'always essentially the same,' regardless of their specific labels.

What two primary elements does Mencken identify as defining the political process in democratic states?

Despite being susceptible to manipulation, what power does Mencken attribute to the mob in deciding between rival factions?

The mob remains 'quite free to decide between them' and has the power and means to 'throw them off at will, and even at whim.'

Key Concepts (2/2)

Why, according to Mencken, does the mob often 'stick to the thieves' (corrupt politicians) in common practice?

Because their 'words are words it understands and their ideas are ideas it cherishes.'

How does Mencken characterize the academic and political debate surrounding the differences between representative and direct democracy?

He dismisses it as 'a great deal of paper and ink...wasted,' calling it a favorite theme of 'university pundits' and 'stall-fed Rousseaus.'

What common slogan encapsulates the Liberal argument for improving democracy?

'The cure for the evils of democracy is more democracy.'

How does Mencken react to the idea that 'purer democracy' mechanisms like the initiative and referendum are effective solutions?

He labels it 'simply rhetoric,' stating that 'every time anything of the kind is tried it fails ingloriously,' with no evidence of past or present success.
