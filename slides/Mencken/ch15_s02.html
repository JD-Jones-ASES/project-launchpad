Chapter 15, Section 2

H.L. Mencken’s Critique of Democratic Reform: A Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts and Thematic Analysis (1/2)

Mountebankery

Using tricks and "balderdash" to soothe the masses.

Moral Anesthesia

Prioritizing political advantage over points of honor.

Submission to Mob-Masters

Making terms with the agents of "prehensile minorities" who manipulate public opinion.

Theodore Roosevelt

Cited as a "violent and shameless demagogue" who initially entered politics to fight demagoguery.

Henry Cabot Lodge

Described as a "reductio ad absurdum" of the gentleman turned messiah; a man whose private convictions became indistinguishable from his political dodges.

Key Concepts and Thematic Analysis (2/2)

Political Appointments

Judges are often chosen for political reasons or through popular election, favoring "shysters" over reputable lawyers.

Constitutional Neglect

Most judges have acquiesced to the destruction of various Constitutional Amendments (1st, 2nd, 4th, 5th, 6th, 14th, and 15th).

Lack of Theoretical Contribution

Very few American judges contribute to legal theory, instead enforcing laws provided by "ignorant and corrupt legislators."

Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: According to Mencken, what is the "grim dilemma" faced by an office-holder regarding the Constitutional oath?

An office-holder must swear to uphold the Constitution (including Prohibition laws). If they provide wine to a dying relative, they become a "liar" (violating their oath); if they refuse, they become a "pig" (lacking human decency).

Q: Why does Mencken believe the English judiciary is superior to the American one?

The English system is protected by a "governing oligarchy" that maintains high standards for the bench, preventing "ignoramuses" and "shysters" from attaining office, whereas the American system relies on popular elections and political patronage.

Q: What happens to a "haughty amateur" who enters politics with rectitude but refuses to use the "cologne spirits of the trade"?

They are "swiftly heaved over the fence" and their career in statecraft comes to an end after their first battle.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/2)

Q: How did Theodore Roosevelt differ from other American Presidents in his social habits, according to the text?

Roosevelt was the only President since Washington to attempt to break down barriers between politics and other classes, inviting people who "actually knew something" (intellectuals and artists) to his table.

Q: What is the "remedy" advocated by professional Liberals that Mencken finds absurd?

The idea that it is the "duty of a gentleman to go into politics" as a way to cure the "quagmire" of the system.
