The Maker of Laws (Mencken’s Critique of the American Legislator)

Study Slides

The Residency Requirement and the Cult of Mediocrity

The Intent

To ensure every electoral unit has a direct voice in government.

The Reality

Mencken argues this rule fills legislative bodies with "puerile local politicians" who lack the intelligence to grasp complex governmental problems.

The Comparison

The Senate: Independence vs. Political Necessity

The Two-Year House Term

Forces Congressmen into a state of "continuous bootlicking" of local bosses to ensure re-election.

The Six-Year Senate Term

Allows a Senator to potentially forget local bosses for a few years and exercise genuine statesmanship or intellectual dignity.

The "Eternal Tragedy"

Despite the potential for independence, Mencken notes that the average Senator remains a "party hack" who will abandon private rectitude for political necessity when pressured.

The Influence of Corrupt Agencies and Fanatics

The Eighteenth Amendment

Cited as a primary example of legislation forced through by "ignoble fellows" and "degraded noodles" from backward states.

The Anti-Saloon League

A specific agency mentioned as having immense power over incompetent legislators like the fictional "Congressman John J. Balderdash."

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Bootlicking

Characterized in the text as the submissive behavior of a Congressman toward local bosses to secure re-election.

Hedge School

A term used to describe the rudimentary or inferior early education of many Southern legislators.

Junket

A trip taken by a politician at the taxpayers' expense, described by Mencken as a "drunken holiday."

Kleagle

A rank within the Ku Klux Klan; used here to categorize the low character of certain legislators.

Noodle

A derogatory term for a person lacking intelligence; specifically applied to those in State Legislatures who ratified the 18th Amendment.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Poltroon

A spiritless coward. Senator Reed used this term to describe colleagues who fear losing votes more than they value their duty.

Puerile

Childishly silly or trivial; used to describe the nature of local politicians.

Rectitude

Morally correct behavior or thinking; righteousness.

Scrub Stock

A term for inferior livestock, applied metaphorically to the low-quality individuals found in the House of Representatives.

Venality

The quality of being open to bribery or corruption.

Mencken targets the democratic politician, specifically the legislator, and the structural conditions (like the residency requirement) that produce him.

According to Mencken, what negative consequences arise from the American residency requirement for legislators?

It confines each district to its local talent pool, ensuring mediocrity and parochialism, especially in areas where the talent pool is shallow.

How did the American residency rule for legislators differ from that of countries like Great Britain (in Mencken's time)?

Unlike the U.S., Great Britain historically allowed constituencies to choose distinguished figures from anywhere in the nation, without a residency requirement.
