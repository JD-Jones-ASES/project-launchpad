Chapter 19, Section 2

The Phenomenon of the Defeated Politician in Democracy

Study Slides

Historical Case Studies: The "Warlocks" of American Politics

Figure. Political Context

Consequences of Defeat

Aaron Burr. Failed to defeat Jefferson in 1800; failed to succeed Adams.

Became a misanthrope; engaged in a duel with Hamilton, a conspiracy with Blennerhassett, and a trial for treason.

Henry Clay. Three-time presidential candidate (1824, 1832, 1840).

Turned toward "the Interests" (Mellons, Morgans); advocated for centralization and protective tariffs.

John C. Calhoun. Failed to reach the White House/succeed Jackson.

Became a primary architect of Nullification; worked to break up the Union and prepare the way for the Civil War.

James G. Blaine. Defeated in 1876 and 1884.

Perpetuated Civil War-era hatreds ("waving the bloody shirt") and engaged in a succession of anti-social acts.

Theodore Roosevelt. Defeated in 1912.

Became a "political killer"; reversed his convictions on Germany to spite Woodrow Wilson; helped force the U.S. into WWI to seek personal military command.

William Jennings Bryan. Three-time presidential candidate (last defeat in 1908).

Attacked the "intelligent minority" via a campaign against evolution; prioritized hurting his opponents over public education or tranquility.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Bloody Shirt

A political tactic of arousing post-Civil War animosity for partisan gain.

Centralization

The concentration of administrative power in a central government, a policy favored by Henry Clay.

Lame Duck

A defeated politician whose loss has left them bitter, vengeful, and potentially dangerous to the state.

Maison de Santé

A private asylum or health establishment; mentioned as a place a Crown Prince might wish to confine a King.

Misanthrope

A person who dislikes humankind and avoids human society; used to describe Aaron Burr after his political failures.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Nullification

The legal theory that a state has the right to invalidate any federal law it deems unconstitutional; a doctrine championed by John C. Calhoun.

Protective Tariff

A duty imposed on imports to protect domestic industries; viewed by the author as a lingering "blessing" of Henry Clay’s influence.

The Interests

Powerful financial and industrial figures (e.g., Mellon, Morgan, Schwab) for whom defeated politicians may become agents.

Warlock

A term used by the author to describe destructive, trouble-making politicians on both national and local levels.

A. He became an advocate and attorney for 'the Interests' and centralization.

C. He devoted himself to the peaceful preservation of the Union.

D. He became a misanthrope who withdrew from public life entirely.

Hint: Think about the shift from a democratic candidate to a representative for powerful financial entities like the 'Mellons' or 'Morgans'.

Quiz Question 3

A. His personal bitterness over failing to reach the White House.

B. A deep-seated philosophical commitment to states' rights.

C. Pressure from his Southern constituents to protect the institution of slavery.

D. An attempt to mitigate the influence of the 'Interests' in Washington.

Hint: Look for a motivation rooted in personal disappointment rather than legal theory.

Quiz Question 5
