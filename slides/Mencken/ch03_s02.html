The Intellectual Divide: Superior vs. Inferior

Superior Minds

Possess an extraordinary capacity for development and function. With proper training, these individuals can acquire vast knowledge and reach the highest levels of human skill.

Democracy as Theology

Reliance on Phantasms

Democrats depend on the unproven theory that the "inferior man" possesses inherent wisdom and virtue.

Rejection of Facts

When confronted with uncomfortable scientific evidence regarding intelligence, democrats appeal to "highest sentiments" and the "dignity of man."

Moral Judgment

Critics of democracy are not viewed as merely being in error, but as "wicked."

Fundamentalist Roots

Democratic belief is likened to "Fundamentalism," relying on "gratuitous, transcendental assumptions" similar to those found in the Book of Genesis.

The Nature of Homo Stultus

Susceptibility to Delusion

While they struggle to grasp truth, they absorb "palpable nonsense" and "impostures" with little resistance.

Victimization

Historically, they are the perpetual victims of "quacks," including priests and politicians.

Hatred of Progress

They instinctively hate those who work for the progress of the race, viewing honest intellectual labor as "unsound, immoral and of the devil."

Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: According to the text, what are the primary drivers of the development and operation of the human mind?

> The mind is primarily a function of physical and chemical phenomena, governed by the same natural laws that regulate the human lungs or kidneys.

Q: How does the author characterize the "inferior man"?

> The inferior man is the average citizen whose intellectual limitations make self-government impossible. He is described as having no more inherent wisdom or virtue than the superstitious notion that Friday is an unlucky day.

Q: Why do "Liberals" and democrats oppose intelligence tests?

> They recognize that these tests offer a scientific means of demonstrating the natural inequality of intelligence, which poses a direct threat to the democratic theory that all men possess the same capacity for wisdom.

Mencken argues that human beings differ vastly in intelligence, and the vast majority are too stupid to govern themselves.

Who is Mencken's 'immediate target' in this section, and what does he mean by the term 'democrat'?

His immediate target is anyone who believes in the fundamental competence of the common man. 'Democrat' refers to this belief, not a political party member.

Who is Mencken's 'secondary target' in 'Varieties of Homo Sapiens'?

Critics of the intelligence testing movement, whom he identifies as Liberals clinging to a 'sentimental faith in human equality.'

He shifts from the mock-historian of Chapter 2 to a mock-scientist. He writes as a detached, clinical naturalist examining 'specimens' of a new species.
