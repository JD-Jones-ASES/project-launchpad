Chapter 12, Section 2

The Popular Will and the Nature of Democracy

Study Slides

The Paradox of Popular Sovereignty

Theoretical vs. Practical Democracy

Theoretical democracy—where the popular will is completely energized—is deemed impossible due to "insuperable impediments" in the common mind.

Democracy "In Being"

Democracy exists not when the masses are constantly involved in every decision, but when the threat of their sudden, devastating intervention makes politicians "walk a bit warily."

The Absolute Sovereignty of the "Mob"

Historical Comparisons

Ancient monarchs, such as the Merovingian kings or Japanese emperors, were limited by mayors of the palace, shoguns, or the Church.

The Nature of Disfranchisement and Indifference

Presumptive Incompetence

If a group is disfranchised, it is often because they are not "actively eager" for the ballot. Lack of interest is presented as evidence of political incompetence.

The Origins of Democracy

Democracy was not won by the masses but was "forced upon its beneficiaries" by small groups of visionaries from outside the benefited class.

The Impact of Franchise Extension

The Prussian Example

After the abolition of the three-class voting system in Prussia, government honesty and efficiency allegedly declined.

Women’s Suffrage

The text claims women's suffrage brought none of the promised reforms, stating that most women vote "unwillingly and without clear purpose," often succumbing to the same sentimentalities as men.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is H.L. Mencken's central argument regarding 'the popular will' in this section?

Mencken argues that 'the people' lack a coherent will, and therefore, democratic government cannot truly execute it.

Who is Mencken's primary audience for this section?

The educated skeptic, whom he challenges to follow an extended, complex argument.

How does Mencken's persona and tone evolve throughout this section?

He shifts from a dispassionate analyst (observing 'sheep') to a dark humorist, and finally to a prophet of doom, often moving between contempt, admiration, a...

What is the effect of Mencken's unstable tone on the reader?

It keeps the reader off balance, preventing them from settling on a fixed understanding of Mencken's exact position, thus complicating the picture.

What three animal metaphors does Mencken use as 'graduated insults' for the American people?

Sheep, donkeys, and goats, representing docility, stupidity/stubbornness, and gullibility/willing victimhood, respectively.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What does Mencken imply by adding 'to borrow from their own dialect' to his animal insults?

It adds a layer of class contempt, suggesting that even the vocabulary of abuse must be simplified for the people being insulted to understand it.

Define 'prehensile soothsayers' in Mencken's context.

It describes politicians as 'grasping' (prehensile) for power while 'pretending to see the future' (soothsayers), essentially half primate, half oracle.

According to Mencken, who are 'blacklegs'?

Swindlers or cheats, especially those preying on gamblers. Mencken applies it to Prohibition agents, equating law enforcement with criminal enterprise.
