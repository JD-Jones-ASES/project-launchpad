The Sage of Baltimore (Mencken)

Chapter 13, Section 1

Analysis of Mencken’s "Disproportional Representation"

Study Slides

Diction and Vocabulary

Technical/Constitutional

Phrases like "one-man-one-vote formula" or "proportional representation."

Colloquial/Barnyard

Derisive terms such as "yokels," "bucolic imbecilities," and "kine in the byres."

Medical/Anatomical

The use of Latin anatomical terms to describe political behavior (e.g., the hamstrings).

Syntactic Strategies

Parallelism and Anaphora

Mencken uses the repetitive "They could..." structure to create a "rhetorical avalanche." This highlights the scope of popular power by giving equal weight to both reasonable and insane propositions.

Periodic Sentences

Mencken often delays the main point of a sentence until the final clause, forcing the reader to absorb his descriptive characterizations before reaching the ultimate conclusion.

Argumentative Modes

Reductio ad absurdum

Mencken extends the logic of popular sovereignty to its most ridiculous extremes (e.g., making it a crime to drink coffee) to demonstrate the inherent instability or absurdity of the concept.

Structural Irony

The entire argument is built on the paradox that the more power Mencken attributes to the people, the more he highlights their failure to use it wisely.
