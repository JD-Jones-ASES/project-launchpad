H.L. Mencken’s Critique of Democratic Moralism: A Study Guide

Study Slides

The Motives of Democratic Moralism

Envy

The desire to punish others for enjoying pleasures the Puritan cannot experience.

Sadism

The derivation of pleasure from inflicting pain, inconvenience, or disgrace on those who are perceived as free or "having a better time in the world."

Cowardice

Mencken suggests the Puritan forbids what he fears or is incapable of doing himself, describing Puritan morality as being "against every human act that he is incapable of himself — safely."

Historical Context: Prohibition

• Driven alcohol consumption underground.

• Fueled the explosion of organized crime.

• Eroded respect for the law.

• Empowered an "army of bravos" (enforcement officers) whom Mencken characterized as professional criminals.

Rhetorical Strategies

The Three-Register Collision

The simultaneous use of theological (e.g., grace, ascetic), clinical (e.g., psychopathological, sadism), and colloquial (e.g., buncombe, fraud) language to reduce moral crusading to a p...

Accumulation (Congeries)

Piling up lists of examples—such as the Mann Act, Prohibition, and anti-evolution laws—to create an overwhelming indictment that makes denial impossible.

Voice Oscillation

Shifting between the "cool diagnostician" (the detached theorist) and the "angry polemicist" (the disgusted observer) to lead the reader from a calm analysis to a state of justified outrage.

Syntactic Rhythms

Using complex, labyrinthine sentences to mirror the "machinery of persecution," followed by short, blunt sentences (e.g., "It is a democratic invention") to deliver a forensic verdict.

Glossary of Key Terms

Key Concepts (1/2)

How does H.L. Mencken define "Puritanism" in his rhetorical context?

The impulse to regulate the private behavior of others through the force of law.

According to Mencken, what two psychological motives drive democratic moralism?

Envy and sadism.

What constitutional amendment ratified in 1919 banned alcoholic beverages in the U.S.?

The Eighteenth Amendment.

What 1919 legislation provided the enforcement mechanism for Prohibition?

The Volstead Act.

In Mencken's view, what does the Puritan seek to punish in others?

Pleasures that the Puritan himself is unable to enjoy.

Mencken describes the result of democratic moralism as a system of "_____ persecution."

psychopathological

Key Concepts (2/2)

What amendment repealed Prohibition in 1933?

The Twenty-first Amendment.

According to the text, Mencken's persona in Section III.2 oscillates between which two modes?

The cool diagnostician and the angry polemicist.

Why does Mencken shift his tone from analytical to denunciatory?

To make the reader feel that evidence has mounted until outrage is the only logical conclusion.

How does Mencken characterize the army of Prohibition enforcement officers?

As an army of bravos mainly made up of professional criminals.

What is the primary difference between Mencken's attack on moralism and an attack on morality itself?

Mencken objects specifically to using law to enforce private moral codes rather than rejecting morality as such.

What are the three distinct registers of vocabulary Mencken uses in this section?

Theological, clinical, and colloquial.
