Analysis of H.L. Mencken’s "The Politician Under Democracy": Study Guide

Study Slides

Vocabularies of Exposure

Legal/Political

Using terms like "altruist" and "honour" with "invisible quotation marks" to highlight the politician's false self-image.

Vernacular/Physical

Using earthy, "gutter" language (e.g., "butter his parsnips," "ear to the ground") to drag the politician down to reality.

Zoological/Clinical

Categorizing the politician as a "species" or "specimen" driven by "congenital" instincts rather than conscious choice.

Rhetorical Devices and Syntax

Extended Analogy

The politician is compared to a courtier who flatters to victimize and a chorus girl who trades her person for a job.

Periodic Sentences

Mencken builds suspense by delaying the main clause or "verdict" of a sentence until the very end.

The Semicolon

Used to chain parallel clauses together, creating an "oppressive catalogue" of evidence against the subject.

Zeugma

A figure of speech that yokes incompatible ideas (the literal/physical and the figurative/abstract) to force the reader to experience the politician's debasement.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: Who was Robert Michels, and why does Mencken cite him?

Mencken cites Robert Michels, a German-Italian sociologist known for the "iron law of oligarchy," to lend academic respectability to his argument that democratic organizations inevitably become oligarchies.

Q: Define Mencken’s original coinage, "demaslave."

A "demaslave" is a hybrid of Latin and Greek terms. Unlike a demagogue (a leader of the people), a demaslave is one who pretends to follow the people, highlighting the incompatible roles democracy forces upon a man.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/2)

Q: How does Mencken use the term "armamentarium"?

Borrowing from medical terminology, Mencken uses "armamentarium" to describe the collection of deceptions and methods a politician uses, giving political fraud the appearance of professional competence.

Q: Describe the structural relationship between Mencken's long and short sentences.

Mencken often uses a long, complex sentence to pile up evidence or clauses of an argument, followed by a short, blunt sentence that acts as a "gavel" or final verdict on the matter.

Q: What distinction does Mencken make between "vanity" and "pride"?

Through the chorus girl analogy, Mencken suggests that politicians retain "vanity" (pleasure in how others see them) but lose "pride" (internal self-respect that is independent of others).
