The Unresolved Paradox

The book concludes with a circular question: "How can any man be a democrat who is sincerely a democrat?" This creates a loop with no exit, leaving the reader in a state of intellectual suspension.

Diction and Vocabulary

"Charming"

By calling democracy "charming," Mencken reduces it from a serious political system to a mere aesthetic decoration.

"Confess"

This word implies intimacy and a shared secret between the author and the reader, suggesting that enjoying the folly of democracy is a slightly shameful act.

Glossary of Important Terms

Term. Definition

Contextual Usage

Maudlin. Weakly and effusively sentimental.

Used to describe the "herding" of citizens after "rogues and mountebanks."

Palpably. Obviously; in a way that is readily perceived.

Used to dismiss democratic propositions as "palpably not true."

Dunderheads. Stupid people.

A blunt, Anglo-Saxon word used to describe those "exalted" by democracy.

Contumacy. Stubborn refusal to obey or comply (especially a legal order).

Applied to the intellectual independence of a dissenting "pedagogue."

Litotes. A figure of speech using understatement for emphasis, often through negation.

The dominant rhetorical strategy of Mencken’s "Last Words."

Catechism. A set of questions and answers used for instruction.

Refers to the rhythmic syntax Mencken uses to dismiss charges against democracy.

Polemic. A strong verbal or written attack on someone or something.

The overarching genre of *Notes on Democracy*.
