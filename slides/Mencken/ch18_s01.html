The Rewards of Virtue (An Analysis of H.L. Mencken)

Study Slides

I. Key Concepts and Central Arguments (1/2)

Defiance of the Mob

Reed was known for his fierce independence, opposing the League of Nations, Prohibition, and Progressive-era legislation.

Survival through Talent

Mencken notes that Reed survived in office despite defying his party and the mob because of his "amazing talents as a popular orator."

The Political Price

Despite his talents, Reed represents the "political price" paid by those who refuse to flatter the public.

Autobiographical Turn

He recounts personal experiences as a journalist, such as being accused of taking money from the "Medical Trust," the Vatican, brewers, and distillers.

Purpose

This shift transforms abstract political theory into "personal witness," using controlled indignation to highlight the impossibility of a critic being viewed as disinterested by the public.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: According to Mencken, why is the "honest man" at a disadvantage compared to the "crook" in public life?

> **Answer:** The mob assumes all public men are corrupt. The "actual crook" knows how to play the game of corruption, whereas the honest man suffers because his disinterested motives are misunderstood and treated as hidden self-interest.

Q: What role does "Katzenjammer" play in Mencken's description of the post-World War era?

> **Answer:** Mencken uses the German word (meaning "cat’s wailing" or a hangover) to describe the national mood as a collective "hangover from patriotic excess." Using a German word in the 1920s was also an act of defiance, as German culture was...

Q: How does Mencken define the "eternal tragedy of democracy"?

> **Answer:** It is the sincere yet performatively stated belief that democracy inherently punishes the good and rewards the expedient, driven by the mob's inability to imagine virtue.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/2)

Q: What are the two stages of the legislative process described by Mencken, and who is responsible for each?

> **Answer:** The two stages are **invention** and **enactment**. Laws are invented by "frauds and fanatics" and put upon the statute books (enacted) by "poltroons and scoundrels."

Q: What is the significance of the "periodic sentence" in Mencken’s writing?

> **Answer:** It is a sentence that withholds its main point or clause until the end, building suspense through subordinate clauses. Mencken uses it to deliver "devastating conclusions" after a rhythmic build-up.
