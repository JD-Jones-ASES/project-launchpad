The Sage of Baltimore (Mencken)

Chapter 14, Section 2

The Politician Under Democracy

Study Slides

I. Key Concepts and Themes (1/2)

Cognitive Limitations

An inability to grasp complex issues, responding only to the simple and banal.

Emotional Volatility

A tendency to succumb to "preposterous alarms," envy, and hatred of superiors.

Fear of Liberty

A "congenital incapacity" for independent action and a preference for the "warmth and safety of the herd."

Venality

A willingness to be bought with simple material rewards, such as a job or a load of coal.

The Demagogue

One who preaches doctrines he knows to be false to an audience he considers idiots.

I. Key Concepts and Themes (2/2)

The Demaslave

One who listens to the "idiots" and pretends to believe their delusions.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: According to Mencken, what is the "technical equipment" of a politician?

> **Answer:** It consists simply of an "armamentarium of deceits." His skill lies in lying to hold his job, embracing new truths if lying fails, and detecting popular clamor before the people themselves are even aware of it.

Q: How does Mencken describe the moral code of the democratic politician?

> **Answer:** The politician’s code deems anything moral if it furthers his main concern: keeping his place at the public trough. This includes getting into office under false pretenses or changing convictions overnight to follow popular trends (...

Q: Why does Mencken argue that an educated man is unlikely to be elected to office?

> **Answer:** An educated man stating "elementary notions" plainly would arouse fears in the mob. The politician’s business is not to be frank, but to arouse specific fears that will run in his favor.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/2)

Q: What comparison does Mencken use to describe the "old birds" (experienced politicians) who have compromised their honor?

> **Answer:** He compares them to "chorus girls of long experience" who have resignedly accepted that they must compromise themselves (in the politician's case, by swallowing convictions) as the price of keeping their jobs.

Q: What is the distinction between "vanity" and "pride" in the surviving politician?

> **Answer:** By the time a politician has successfully navigated the democratic system, his dignity and pride have vanished, leaving only a "vacuum" in his subconscious; however, his vanity remains.
