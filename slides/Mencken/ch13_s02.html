The Sage of Baltimore (Mencken)

Chapter 13, Section 2

Disproportional Representation and the Democratic Will

Study Slides

Core Concepts and Analysis (1/2)

The Federal Model

The United States Senate is the primary example, where small states (e.g., Delaware, Nevada) possess the same voting power as large states (e.g., Pennsylvania, New York).

State-Level Disparity

This model is replicated in state legislatures, where upper houses are often organized by geography rather than population, leading to the under-representation of congested urban centers.

The "Make-Weight" Theory

Judicial Responsiveness

Supreme Court justices are described as "practical politicians" who keep their "ears close to the grass-roots." Their decisions often reflect the "popular rages of the hour" rather than immutable law.

The Malleability of the Constitution

The text asserts that a determined majority can achieve any outcome—from expropriating all private property to abolishing the jury system—without departing from the constitutional framework.

Core Concepts and Analysis (2/2)

The Electorate

Driven by a desire for safety, security, and "rough entertainment," the typical voter is willing to trade theoretical freedoms for tangible advantages like jobs or business gains.

The Politicians

Described as "jackals" who "hoodwink and swindle" the public, politicians provide the appearance of what the people want in exchange for power and the fulfillment of their own interests.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: According to the text, why does reapportionment in the lower houses of state legislatures often lag behind population growth?

> **Answer:** Because cities grow in population much faster than country districts, and the legislative process of reapportionment cannot keep pace with this rapid urban expansion.

Q: How does the text describe the typical democrat's attitude toward "natural rights" during economic "boom times"?

> **Answer:** The typical democrat becomes impatient with natural rights and is willing to exchange the theoretical boons of freedom for something usable, such as a job or business advantage.

Q: What is the primary reason given for why the American public generally rejects "direct action" or revolution?

> **Answer:** The public believes that revolution is unnecessary because any reform advocated by a respectable majority can be achieved peacefully and legally through constitutional amendments and the existing political framework.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/2)

Q: In what way does the text suggest the Supreme Court is influenced by the President and the Senate?

> **Answer:** When filling vacancies, the President and Senators act as practical politicians, selecting justices who adhere to doctrines that are currently fashionable among the general public (the "cross-roads grocery-stores and barber-shops").

Q: What does the text identify as the three things the populace wants most from its leaders?

> **Answer:** Safety and security, to be soothed with "mellifluous words," and heroes to worship.
