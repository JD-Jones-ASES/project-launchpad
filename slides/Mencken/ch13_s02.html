State Level

Upper houses of state legislatures are often organized by geographical boundaries rather than population, leading to the gross under-representation of "congested urban centres."

The Urban-Rural Dynamic

Urban Voters

Characterized as a "compact mob" that acts quickly and effectively due to physical and mental proximity.

Rural Voters

Described as "dispersed and largely inarticulate," unable to compete with the "city stampede" without the structural advantage of disproportional representation.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What familiar complaint about democratic representation does Mencken take as his starting point?

The complaint that the U.S. Senate gives equal representation to unequal states (e.g., Wyoming and New York), and that state legislatures underrepresent cities.

What is Mencken's core argument about disproportional representation?

It is not a flaw in democracy but a feature—a voluntary sacrifice of pure theory in favor of workable practice.

What is Mencken's rhetorical task in this section regarding the perceived unfairness of apportionment?

Describe the dominant tone Mencken adopts at the beginning of this section.

Sardonic patience, adopting the persona of a practical political observer explaining something that ought to be obvious.

How does Mencken's tone evolve as the section progresses?

It shifts from sardonic explanation to reckless exhibition, becoming increasingly giddy as he demonstrates the absurdity of unlimited popular sovereignty.

What does Mencken's use of the word 'fuglemen' imply about democratic reformers?

It reduces them to mere 'parade-ground performers'—all show, no substance—who advocate for 'pure democracy' with 'complicated remedies'.
