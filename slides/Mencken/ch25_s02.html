H.L. Mencken’s "Notes on Democracy" – Section IV.2: Last Words

Study Slides

The Psychology of the "Mob Man"

A Sense of Power

It makes the citizen feel as though they are "genuinely running things," a feeling Mencken compares to the satisfaction felt by archbishops or police sergeants.

The Illusion of Wisdom

It convinces the citizen that their views are taken seriously by their "betters."

Consciousness of Duty

It provides a sense of "high duty triumphantly done."

The Spectator vs. The Democrat

The Democrat

Suffers from the "naïve delusion" that happiness is achieved by taking it from others. This quest for happiness inevitably ends in disaster and unhappiness.

The Spectator

Finds democracy to be a "show of the first cut and calibre." To the observer, the "fraud" and "obvious imbecilities" of democracy are a source of high entertainment and "exhilarating" spectacle.

The Self-Devouring Nature of Government

Abandonment of Philosophy

Under national strain, democratic leaders (such as Lincoln, Roosevelt, and Wilson) frequently convert themselves into "despots" to protect national safety.

War on Liberty

While liberty is the theoretical cornerstone of democracy, the system often jails citizens for exercising it, such as reading the Bill of Rights.
