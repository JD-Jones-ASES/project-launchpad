The Dedicatory Letter to the Sorbonne

Demonstration via Natural Reason

Descartes argues that while faith is sufficient for the "faithful," philosophical arguments based on "natural reason" are required to persuade "infidels" of God’s existence and the soul's immortality.

Refuting Atheism

He claims that the "principal reason" for impiety is the belief that these two facts—God's existence and the distinction between soul and body—have never been demonstrated.

Synopsis of the Six Meditations

Meditation. Primary Theme

Key Developments

I. Universal Doubt

Sets out reasons to doubt all things, especially material objects, to deliver the mind from prejudice and senses.

II. The Nature of Mind

The mind uses its liberty to doubt everything, concluding that it is impossible for the mind itself not to exist. Establishes the mind as a "thinking thing."

III. Existence of God

Provides a principal argument for God’s existence based on the idea of a "supremely perfect being" within the human mind.

IV. Truth and Error

Defines the nature of truth and error in speculative matters. Explains that clear and distinct perceptions are always true.

V. Corporeal Nature

Explains the nature of bodies generally and offers a second, "new" proof of God's existence. Notes that geometrical certainty depends on knowing God.

VI. The External World

Distinguishes understanding from imagination. Proves the real distinction between mind and body, their close union, and the existence of material things.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Le Monde (The World)

Descartes' unpublished treatise on physics and cosmology that defended a heliocentric view; it was suppressed following Galileo's condemnation.

Light of Nature (Natural Reason)

The innate human capacity to perceive truth clearly and distinctly, independent of divine revelation or sensory input.

Objective Reality

The degree of reality or perfection represented by an idea. For example, the idea of an infinite being (God) has more objective reality than the idea of a finite substance.

Sorbonne

The Faculty of Theology at the University of Paris; a powerful body with the authority to approve or condemn philosophical works in 17th-century France.

Substance

According to Descartes, that which exists in such a way as to depend on nothing else for its existence.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Thinking Thing (Res Cogitans)

The essence of the mind or soul, characterized by the faculty of thinking, willing, and perceiving, distinct from the physical extension of the body.

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to Descartes, which two questions are best demonstrated through philosophical rather than theological argument?

The questions respecting God and the human soul.

Why does Descartes argue that natural reason is necessary to persuade 'infidels' of God's existence?

Because they do not accept the authority of faith or Holy Scripture.

What is the logical 'circle' Descartes aims to avoid when proving God's existence to non-believers?

Believing in God because of Scripture, while believing in Scripture because it comes from God.

According to the Wisdom of Solomon cited by Descartes, why are those who fail to find the 'Lord' of the world considered 'not to be excused'?

Because their understanding was great enough to discern the world and its creatures.

What did the Lateran Council under Leo X ordain Christian philosophers to do regarding the soul?

Refute arguments claiming the soul perishes with the body and employ their powers to make the truth known.

What was Descartes' primary motive for presenting his work to the Faculty of Theology at the Sorbonne?

To obtain their protection and authority to preempt censorship and satisfy the 'impious'.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How does Descartes compare his philosophical demonstrations to those found in Geometry?

Both involve lengthy, interdependent arguments that require a mind free of prejudice and devoted to their consideration.

What is the specific requirement for a mind to adequately understand Descartes' metaphysical speculations?

The mind must be free of all prejudices and capable of detaching itself from the affairs of the senses.

Why did Descartes choose not to publish the Meditations in French, the language of his earlier Discourse on the Method?

To prevent 'feebler minds' from mistakenly attempting to follow a path of doubt they might not handle correctly.

What is the first major objection to the Discourse on the Method that Descartes addresses in his preface?

That perceiving oneself as a thinking thing does not necessarily prove one's essence consists only of thinking.

How does Descartes distinguish an idea taken 'materially' from an idea taken 'objectively'?

Materially refers to the idea as an act of understanding; objectively refers to the thing represented by that act.

Why can an idea be 'more perfect' than the person who has it, according to Descartes' response to his critics?

Because of the essence of the thing the idea represents, rather than the idea being an act of the mind.
