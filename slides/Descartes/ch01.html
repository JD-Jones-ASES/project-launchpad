Key Concepts (1/2)

René Descartes (1596-1650)

Often considered the "father of modern philosophy," his work profoundly influenced Western thought, particularly in epistemology (theory of knowledge) and metaphysics.

Meditations on First Philosophy (1641)

Descartes' seminal work, presented as six "meditations" or days of thought, designed to provide philosophical proofs for the existence of God and the immortality/distinction of the human soul.

Dedicatory Letter to the Faculty of Sacred Theology at the Sorbonne

A prefatory piece where Descartes addresses the esteemed theological institution, seeking their approval and protection for his work. It reveals his awareness of the political and religious climate.

Synopsis of the Six Meditations

A brief summary preceding the Meditations, outlining the logical progression of Descartes' argument from radical doubt to the re-establishment of knowledge.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Le Monde ("The World")

Descartes' unpublished treatise on physics and cosmology, which advocated a heliocentric model. Its suppression demonstrates his fear of conflict with Church authority.

Philosophical vs. Theological Proofs

Descartes' central aim was to demonstrate God's existence and the soul's nature using "natural reason" (logic and intellect), rather than relying solely on faith or Holy Scriptures. This was intend...

Reasoning in a Circle

Descartes highlights the logical fallacy of using the Holy Scriptures to prove God's existence to unbelievers, as unbelievers would not accept the Scriptures as authoritative in the first place.

Lateran Council under Leo X (Eighth Session)

A historical Church council that condemned the idea of the soul perishing with the body, providing a theological mandate for Descartes to philosophically prove the soul's distinction and immortality.

Metaphysical Speculations

Descartes uses this term to describe his philosophical arguments, acknowledging they are demanding, requiring a mind free from prejudice and sensory distractions, making them challenging for many.

Important Terms (1/2)

Sorbonne

The Faculty of Theology at the University of Paris, a highly influential theological institution in Descartes' era, with significant power to approve or condemn intellectual works.

Inquisition

A historical institution within the Catholic Church responsible for combating heresy. Its actions against Galileo served as a stark warning to other thinkers.

Heliocentric

The astronomical model that places the Sun at the center of the solar system, with the Earth and other planets revolving around it. (Contrasts with the geocentric model.)

Natural Reason

The capacity for rational thought and deduction inherent in human beings, independent of divine revelation or sensory experience. Descartes sought to use this as the foundation for his proofs.

Infidels

A term used by Descartes to refer to non-believers or those who do not accept religious truths based on faith alone, for whom rational arguments were necessary.

The Synopsis: Mapping the Argument (1/2)

Meditation I

Establishes a foundation of universal doubt, questioning all previously held beliefs.

Meditation II

Discovers the certain existence of the "self" as a thinking thing ("I think, therefore I am").

Meditation III

Presents the first proof of God's existence.

Meditation IV

Addresses the nature of error and distinguishes between truth and falsity.

Meditation V

Offers a second, distinct proof of God's existence (the ontological argument).

The Synopsis: Mapping the Argument (2/2)

Meditation VI

Reconstructs knowledge of the external world and the distinction between mind and body, based on the certainty established in prior meditations.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What significant title is René Descartes often given in the history of thought?

He is often called the father of modern philosophy.

What was the title of Descartes' influential work published in 1641?

Meditations on First Philosophy.

How did Descartes' Meditations profoundly impact Western thinking?

It reshaped how the Western world thinks about knowledge, God, and the self.

In what language and year were the Meditations originally published?

In Latin, in Paris, in 1641.

What two prefatory pieces did Descartes include before the Meditations themselves?

A dedicatory letter addressed to the Faculty of Sacred Theology at the Sorbonne and a brief synopsis summarizing each Meditation.
