Descartes’ Meditation I and the Method of Doubt

Study Slides

The Three Stages of Doubt

Stage. Name

Key Argument

Stage 1. The Senses Deceive

The senses have misled us before (e.g., a stick appearing bent in water or a distant tower appearing round).

Stage 2. The Dream Argument

There is no reliable criterion to distinguish waking life from a vivid dream. Dreams often counterfeit the "stamp" of reality.

Stage 3. The Evil Genius

A supremely powerful, malicious deceiver (an "anti-God") might be feeding the meditator misleading experiences and compromising logic itself.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: What is the specific standard Descartes uses to determine which beliefs to set aside?

Descartes does not require proof that a belief is false. Instead, his standard is the "possibility of doubt." If there is any chance a belief could be wrong, he treats it as uncertain for the purpose of building a certain foundation for philosophy.

Q: Why does Descartes argue that it is "imprudent" to trust the senses entirely?

He bases this on the observation that the senses have deceived him in the past, such as making a large object appear small from a distance or a straight stick appear bent when submerged in water.

Q: In the Dream Argument, what category of knowledge appears to survive even if the meditator is dreaming?

Mathematical truths and the rules of arithmetic and geometry (such as "two plus three equals five" or a triangle having three angles) seem to hold true regardless of whether one is dreaming or awake.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/2)

Q: What is the "Evil Genius" (malin génie) and what is its role in the Meditation?

The Evil Genius is a thought experiment describing an all-powerful deceiver. Its role is to serve as a radical instrument of doubt that allows Descartes to question even the most self-evident truths of mathematics and logic.

Q: How does Descartes distinguish his methodological doubt from psychological doubt?

Methodological doubt is a controlled philosophical tool, similar to a mathematician assuming a variable is even to derive a contradiction. It is not a state of panic, despair, or a genuine belief that the world does not exist.

Q: At the end of Meditation I, what has Descartes established as certain?

Nothing. The first Meditation ends in a state of radical uncertainty, which Descartes likens to a man who has fallen into deep water and cannot find the bottom with his feet.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Evil Genius Hypothesis

A thought experiment involving a supremely powerful, malicious being who seeks to deceive the meditator about every experience and logical truth.

Method of Doubt

A systematic procedure for finding certainty by treating any belief that admits of even the slightest doubt as if it were false.

Prudence

In the context of the first Meditation, the cautious approach of not fully trusting a source (like the senses) that has deceived you even once.

Skepticism

In philosophy, the view that certainty or knowledge is impossible or very difficult to achieve.

Thought Experiment

A hypothetical scenario (like the Evil Genius) constructed to test a principle or isolate a variable rather than to describe actual events.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Treating as Uncertain

The act of setting aside a belief not because it is proven false, but because it is not 100% certain.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the primary goal of Descartes' Method of Doubt in Meditation I?

To find a certain foundation for building philosophy.

What is the specific standard Descartes uses to set aside a belief in Meditation I?

Whether the belief admits of even the slightest possible doubt.

Descartes treats as false any belief that is merely _____, rather than proving it is actually false.

uncertain

Concept: The Method of Doubt

Definition: A procedure for finding certainty by treating any doubtable belief as if it were false.

What are the three distinct stages of Descartes' method of doubt?

The argument from the senses, the dream argument, and the evil genius hypothesis.

Why does Descartes argue that it is 'imprudent' to trust the senses entirely?

Because the senses have deceived him in the past.

Key Concepts (2/2)

In the argument from the senses, what shape does a square tower appear to be from a distance?

Round.

What happens to the appearance of a straight stick when it is partially submerged in water?

It looks bent.

In the first stage of doubt, what happens to the perceived size of a large object viewed from far away?

It looks small.

Term: Skepticism

Definition: The philosophical view that certainty or knowledge is impossible or extremely difficult to achieve.

How does Descartes' use of skepticism differ from that of the ancient Pyrrhonian skeptics?

Descartes uses doubt as a tool to find certainty, whereas ancient skeptics suspended judgment as a final position.

Why is the first stage of doubt (the senses) considered relatively weak or 'mild'?

Because many sensory illusions can be compensated for once they are known.
