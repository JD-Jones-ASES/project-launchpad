God as the Guarantor of Truth

For Descartes, only a benevolent God, one who is not a deceiver, can ultimately assure the reliability of his clear and distinct perceptions and thus validate objective knowledge.

Classification of Thoughts

Descartes categorizes his mental states into three kinds: ideas (images), volitions/affections (acts of will), and judgments (affirmations/denials).

Important Terms (1/3)

Cogito (Cogito Ergo Sum)

Latin for "I think, therefore I am." Descartes' first undeniable truth, establishing his existence as a thinking substance.

Clear and Distinct Perception

The standard of truth for Descartes; a perception is clear when it is present and manifest to the attentive mind, and distinct when it is so precisely and sharply separated from all other perceptio...

Deceiver God (or Evil Demon)

A hypothetical, omnipotent being capable of deceiving Descartes about everything, even seemingly obvious truths like mathematical axioms or his own existence. The possibility of such a being drives...

Idea (Cartesian sense)

A thought, image, or representation in the mind. Descartes considers them as modes of thought, not necessarily having external referents.

Volitions/Affections

Acts of the will, such as willing, desiring, fearing, or affirming/denying. These are modes of thought that add something to a simple idea.

Important Terms (2/3)

Judgments

Mental acts of affirming or denying something about an idea. According to Descartes, this is the primary source of human error when the mind mistakenly asserts a correspondence between an idea and ...

Innate Ideas

Ideas that are inherent to the mind itself, not derived from experience or fabrication (e.g., the ideas of "thing," "truth," "God").

Adventitious Ideas

Ideas that appear to come from outside the mind through the senses (e.g., perceptions of light, sound, heat).

Fabricated/Invented Ideas

Ideas constructed by the mind from other ideas (e.g., chimeras, hippogriffs).

Natural Light (Lumen Naturale)

An infallible faculty of reason that allows one to recognize certain truths immediately and without doubt (e.g., that one cannot be thinking without existing).

Important Terms (3/3)

Natural Impulse/Inclination

A spontaneous, uncritical tendency to believe something, often based on sensory experience or habit. Descartes argues this is an unreliable guide to truth and can lead to error.

The Need for God's Existence

Starting Point: The Cogito's Limitation

Descartes has proven he exists as a thinking thing. This certainty relies on the principle that "what I perceive clearly and distinctly is true."

The Deceiver God Problem

However, this general rule itself is under threat from the possibility of a powerful, deceptive God (or evil demon) who could make him believe even clear and distinct perceptions are false.
