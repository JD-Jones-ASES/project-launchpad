Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the central philosophical question addressed in Descartes' Meditation IV?

If a perfect God made us, why do we ever go wrong or make mistakes?

According to Descartes, what is the immediate consequence of God being supremely perfect?

God cannot be a deceiver, because deception is an imperfection.

Why does Descartes argue that deception is an imperfection?

To deceive is to create a false impression, an act of a being that is either malicious or weak, neither of which qualities belongs to a supremely perfect being.

What crucial epistemological conclusion does Descartes draw from God's non-deceiving nature?

What the mind genuinely perceives 'clearly and distinctly' is true, and not false perceptions planted by an evil genius.

What 'serious problem' arises if God is perfect, non-deceiving, and made us, yet we still make mistakes?

This is a version of the 'Problem of Evil' applied to knowledge: how can an all-good, non-deceptive God allow intellectual error?

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is Descartes' ultimate goal in Meditation IV regarding the source of error?

To show that error is entirely our own fault, not God's, through rigorous philosophical analysis.

What are the two mental faculties Descartes distinguishes in his solution to the problem of error?

The intellect (intellectus) and the will (voluntas).

How does Descartes explain the origin of intellectual error based on the intellect and will?

Error occurs when the infinite will rushes ahead of the finite intellect, meaning we affirm or deny something we have not clearly and distinctly grasped.

What is Descartes' prescribed remedy for avoiding intellectual error?

Withhold judgment whenever your perception is not clear and distinct. This is an act of intellectual discipline.
