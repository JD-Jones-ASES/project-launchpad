Important Terms (1/2)

Continuous Quantity / Extension

A fundamental, primary attribute of material things, referring to their spatial properties of length, breadth, and depth. Descartes sees this as distinctly knowable by the mind.

Essence

The fundamental, intrinsic nature or "whatness" of a thing, which defines its identity and properties. Descartes argues these essences are knowable by reason.

True and Immutable Natures

Ideas or concepts (especially mathematical and geometric ones) whose properties are eternal, unchanging, and necessarily true, independent of whether they exist physically or are invented by the mind.

Supremely Perfect Being

Descartes' conception of God, understood as possessing all possible perfections, including existence.

Ontological Argument

A type of philosophical argument for the existence of God that proceeds from the definition or concept of God alone, typically by arguing that existence is a necessary attribute of a perfect being.

Important Terms (2/2)

Clarity and Distinctness

Descartes' epistemological criterion for certainty. A clear idea is present and manifest to the attentive mind, while a distinct idea is clear and also precisely separated from all other ideas.

Sophism

A subtly false or misleading argument, especially one used to deceive. Descartes uses this term to refer to potential objections that might seem logical but are flawed upon closer examination.

Factitious

Something artificial or made up; not natural or genuine. Descartes distinguishes the idea of God as a true and immutable nature from "factitious" ideas.

The Ontological Argument for God's Existence (Second Proof) (1/2)

Transition from Figures to God

If clearly and distinctly perceived properties truly belong to an object's idea, then this principle should apply to the idea of God.

The Idea of God

Descartes finds the idea of God – a "supremely perfect Being" – within himself as clearly and distinctly as the idea of any figure or number.

Existence as a Perfection

He knows no less clearly and distinctly that "actual and eternal existence pertains to this nature" than he knows properties belong to a figure.

Initial Apparent Sophism

At first, it might seem like a "sophism" because we usually distinguish existence from essence in other things. This might lead one to believe God's existence can be separated from His essence.
