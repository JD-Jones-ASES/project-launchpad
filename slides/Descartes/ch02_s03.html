Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: Why does Descartes wait until he is at a "mature" age to begin this project?

He believed the enterprise was so great that he needed to reach an age where he could not hope to be better fitted for the task later in life.

Q: Does Descartes feel it is necessary to prove all his former opinions are false?

No. He believes that withholding assent from matters that are not "entirely certain and indubitable" is sufficient. If he finds any reason to doubt a principle, he can reject the entire foundation.

Q: What is the "painting" analogy used to explain?

It explains that even if our dreams represent fictitious things (like sirens or satyrs), they are formed from "real and true" elements (simples), just as a painter uses real colors to create an imaginary creature.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/2)

Q: According to the text, which sciences are more certain: Physics or Geometry? Why?

Geometry is more certain because it deals with very simple and general things (like shapes and numbers) without concern for whether they exist in reality, whereas Physics deals with "composite things" that are more easily doubted.

Q: What is the purpose of the "Evil Genius" hypothesis?

It is a mental tool used to maintain a state of doubt. It prevents Descartes from falling back into his "ancient and commonly held opinions" by pretending that all external things are illusions.

Q: How does Descartes describe the difficulty of maintaining this level of doubt?

He describes it as a "laborious" task and compares himself to a "captive" who enjoys an imaginary liberty in sleep and fears awakening to a difficult reality.

Glossary of Important Terms

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is Descartes' primary motivation for undertaking the general upheaval of his former opinions?

To establish a firm and permanent structure in the sciences.

Instead of proving every opinion false, what criterion does Descartes use to justify rejecting a belief?

Finding any reason to doubt the belief, no matter how small.

Why does Descartes choose to attack the "principles" of his beliefs rather than each individual opinion?

Because the destruction of the foundations necessarily brings down the rest of the edifice.

According to the text, what is the primary source of the beliefs Descartes previously accepted as "most true and certain"?

The senses (either directly or through them).

What general rule does Descartes adopt regarding things that have deceived us at least once?

It is wiser not to trust them entirely.

Under what specific conditions does Descartes admit the senses typically deceive us?

When perceiving things that are very far away or hardly perceptible.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How does the "Dream Argument" challenge the certainty of immediate sensory experiences, such as sitting by a fire?

It asserts there are no certain indications to clearly distinguish wakefulness from sleep.

What specific realization causes Descartes to feel "lost in astonishment" regarding his state of consciousness?

The fact that dreams can mimic waking sensations so perfectly that he might currently be dreaming.

In the "Painter Analogy," what must remain real even if the specific images in a dream are false?

The simpler and more universal components from which images are formed.

According to the Painter Analogy, even if a painter invents a completely novel creature, what element must still be real?

The colors of which the image is composed.

List three examples of the "simple and universal" things that Descartes considers real even if composite objects are imaginary.

Corporeal nature, extension, and magnitude (or quantity, number, place, and time).

Why does Descartes categorize Physics, Astronomy, and Medicine as "dubious and uncertain"?

They depend on the consideration of composite things which may not exist.
