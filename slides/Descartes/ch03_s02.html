Important Terms (1/2)

Cogito

Latin for "I think." Part of Descartes' famous phrase Cogito, ergo sum ("I think, therefore I am").

Res Cogitans

Latin for "thinking thing" or "thinking substance." Descartes' term for the mind or soul.

La Fleche

The Jesuit college where Descartes received his education, influenced by Scholastic philosophy.

Scholastic Philosophy

A medieval philosophical tradition, prominent in European universities, that sought to reconcile classical philosophy (especially Aristotle) with Christian theology (e.g., through figures like Aqui...

Empiricism

A philosophical theory that states that knowledge comes primarily from sensory experience. David Hume was a rigorous empiricist.

Important Terms (2/2)

Rationalism

A philosophical theory that states that reason, rather than experience, is the primary source and test of knowledge. Descartes is a key figure in rationalism.

Critique of Pure Reason

Immanuel Kant's seminal work, which attempts to investigate the limits and structure of human reason and knowledge, mediating between rationalism and empiricism.

Si fallor, sum

Latin for "if I am mistaken, I exist." An argument made by Augustine of Hippo over a thousand years before Descartes, anticipating the cogito.

According to Descartes, what constitutes the 'self'?

Descartes identifies the self with the thinking substance — the mind or soul. For him, 'I' just am my mind.

How does Descartes distinguish between the mind and the body regarding the self?

For Descartes, the mind is what 'I am,' while the body, if it exists, is something 'I have,' not something 'I am.'

What defines the soul in Descartes' philosophy?

The soul, on Descartes' view, is defined entirely by its one activity it can never stop: thinking.

What happens, according to the Thomistic view, if the soul is removed from a human body?

If the soul is removed, you do not have a body plus a missing soul; you have something that is no longer a human body at all, only an organic corpse.
