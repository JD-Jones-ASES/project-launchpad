Matter as Pure Extension

Definition

Descartes' radical answer is that matter is extension. This means it is the property of occupying space in three dimensions: length, breadth, and depth.

Essential Properties

To be a material thing is to be extended. This implies having a determinate shape, size, and position. Material things can move, be divided, and possess measurable, mathematical properties.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What are the two primary tasks Descartes accomplishes in Meditation V?

He establishes what matter is (before proving its existence) and offers a second, independent proof of God's existence.

What underlying method connects Descartes' two tasks in Meditation V?

The recognition that the mind can grasp essential truths by pure reason, without any appeal to the senses.

According to Descartes, what is the defining characteristic of all physical things (matter)?

Extension: the property of occupying space in three dimensions (length, breadth, and depth).

What properties does Descartes attribute to material things as a result of being extended?

Determinate shape, size, position, motion, divisibility, and measurable, mathematical properties.

What qualities does Descartes argue are not inherent properties of matter itself, but rather sensations in our minds?

Color, smell, taste, warmth, or sound.

How does Descartes explain the 'redness' of an apple?

It is not in the apple; it is a sensation produced in the mind when light of a certain wavelength strikes the eye, caused by the motion of extended particles.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How does Descartes arrive at his understanding of matter, according to the text?

Not by looking at things, but by thinking; he finds the clear and distinct idea of extension in his own mind and analyzes it purely by reason.

What is the 'ontological argument' for God's existence?

An argument that proceeds purely from the concept of God—what God is defined to be—without appealing to observation or experience.

How does Descartes define God in his ontological argument in Meditation V?

As a supremely perfect being, possessing all perfections to an unlimited degree.

Why does Descartes claim 'existence' is a perfection in his ontological argument?

A being that exists is 'more' than a being that is merely possible. Lacking existence would mean lacking a perfection, so a supremely perfect being must exist.
