Translation and Historical Context

Translator Profile

Elizabeth S. Haldane (1862–1937) was a Scottish philosopher and the first woman appointed as a Justice of the Peace in Scotland.

Language Evolution

The Meditations was originally written in Latin (the language of international scholarship) and later revised in French in 1647. Haldane’s translation is based on the original Latin.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Edwardian English

The formal English style of the early 20th century (specifically the era of Elizabeth S. Haldane's translation); it may include archaic phrasing that requires careful reading.

Elizabeth S. Haldane

A Scottish writer and philosopher whose 1911 translation of Descartes became a standard English version for the 20th century.

Evil Genius Hypothesis

A thought experiment in which one imagines a powerful, deceitful being to test if any beliefs are truly beyond doubt.

The Meditator

The first-person narrator of the Meditations who undergoes the journey from doubt to certainty; distinct from Descartes the author.

Thinking Thing

The first certainty reached by the meditator; the realization of one's own existence as a conscious, mental entity.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Synopsis

A section of the book where Descartes provides an overview and recommends that readers do not move forward until they fully understand the current argument.

Key Concepts (1/2)

Why does the author suggest reading the Meditations slowly?

It is an argument where each step depends on the reasoning of previous steps.

According to Descartes' Synopsis, when should a reader move on to a new section of the text?

Only when they are fully satisfied with the preceding material.

What is the consequence of reading the Meditations as a narrative or description rather than an argument?

The reader will likely experience confusion.

What action should a reader take upon reaching a passage they do not understand?

Stop and reread the passage instead of continuing.

How does Descartes explicitly invite the reader to interact with his work?

By conducting the meditations themselves rather than just reading about them.

In the context of the Meditations, what is the 'evil genius' hypothesis?

A thought experiment designed to test the limits of doubt.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the primary risk for a reader who 'simply watches from the outside' while reading the Meditations?

They will miss the elements that make the work philosophically interesting.

Term: The Meditator

Definition: The first-person narrator of the text, distinct from Descartes the historical author.

How does the Meditator's state of knowledge at the beginning of the book differ from the author's?

The Meditator begins in uncertainty, while the author already knows the final conclusions.

What literary device describes the gap between the Meditator’s struggle and Descartes’ established conclusions?

Dramatic irony

According to the text, when should a reader be most skeptical of the Meditator’s argument?

When the progression of the argument seems too easy.

When the Meditator appears most uncertain, the text advises the reader to _____ the argument.

trust
