Mind (*Res Cogitans*)

Defined as a thinking substance. It encompasses thought, doubt, will, imagination, feeling, and perception. Crucially, it has no spatial properties (no location, size, or shape) and is indivisible.

Body (*Res Extensa*)

Defined as an extended substance. It occupies space, has shape and size, is divisible, and is incapable of thought.

Important Terms (1/2)

Real Distinction

Descartes' argument that mind and body are two fundamentally different kinds of substance that can exist independently of each other.

Substance Dualism

The philosophical position that reality consists of two distinct kinds of substance: mental (mind, soul) and material (body). Often called "Cartesian Dualism."

Cartesian Dualism

Refers specifically to Descartes' two-substance view of mind (*res cogitans*) and body (*res extensa*), characterized by their radical difference and real distinction.

Res Cogitans

Latin for "thinking thing"; Descartes' term for the mind or mental substance, which thinks, doubts, wills, and perceives, but has no spatial extension.

Res Extensa

Latin for "extended thing"; Descartes' term for the body or material substance, which occupies space, has shape, and is divisible, but does not think.

Important Terms (2/2)

Cartesian

Of or relating to René Descartes or his philosophy.

Pineal Gland

A small structure in the brain that Descartes identified as the point of interaction between the immaterial mind (soul) and the material body.

Interaction Problem

The persistent philosophical challenge of explaining how an immaterial, non-extended mind can causally influence, and be influenced by, a material, extended body.
