Significant Figures in the Intellectual Revolution

Figure. Role

Key Contribution/Impact

Nicolaus Copernicus. Astronomer

Proposed the heliocentric model, suggesting the earth moves around the sun.

Galileo Galilei. Scientist

Known as the "father of modern science." Used the telescope to observe moons orbiting Jupiter and craters on the Moon; forced by the Inquisition to recant his support for Copernicanism in 1633.

Johannes Kepler. Astronomer

Demonstrated that planetary orbits are ellipses rather than the perfect circles required by Aristotelian physics.

Sextus Empiricus. Philosopher

An ancient Greek whose preserved accounts of Pyrrhonism were printed in Latin in 1562, sparking the Renaissance "skeptical crisis."

Michel de Montaigne. Essayist

Inventor of the essay; used Pyrrhonian skepticism to argue that human knowledge is conditioned by custom, habit, and accident. His motto was "Que sais-je?" (What do I know?).

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Epoche

A Greek term meaning "suspension of belief." This was the motto of the Pyrrhonists, who argued one should suspend judgment on all questions.

Heliocentrism

The scientific theory (supported by Copernicus and Galileo) that the earth and planets revolve around the sun, contradicting the Aristotelian/Ptolemaic model.

Pyrrhonism

An ancient school of radical skepticism named after Pyrrho of Elis. It holds that because there are equally strong arguments for any claim, the only wise course is to suspend judgment entirely.

Que sais-je?

French for "What do I know?" The famous motto of Michel de Montaigne, expressing the core of his skeptical outlook.

Scholasticism

The dominant philosophical and theological tradition of medieval universities. It used formal disputations to harmonize the works of Aristotle with Christian theology.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Skeptical Crisis

A period in the Renaissance sparked by the printing of Sextus Empiricus's works, leading to a widespread questioning of human reason's ability to achieve certainty.

Thirty Years' War (1618–1648)

A massive conflict in Europe, largely driven by the religious fractures of the Reformation, occurring during Descartes' most productive years.
