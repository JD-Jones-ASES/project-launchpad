Important Terms (1/2)

Evil genius (Latin: malignus daemon)

Descartes' hypothetical supremely powerful and deceitful being, introduced in Meditation I to push doubt to its extreme.

Clear and distinct perception

Descartes' criterion for truth; an idea fully present to the attentive mind (clear) and sharply separated from all other ideas (distinct).

Causal adequacy principle

The principle that the cause of an idea must possess at least as much reality as the idea represents.

_a se_

Latin for "from itself" or "from oneself." A being that exists through its own nature, not through any external cause. Descartes holds that God alone is _a se_.

Objective reality

The reality an idea has *as representing* something. In Descartes' usage, an idea of God has infinite objective reality because it represents an infinitely real being.

Important Terms (2/2)

Eminent (Latin: eminenter)

To contain something in a higher degree or more perfect form than it appears in the effect.

Cartesian Circle

The objection that Descartes' argument for God's existence and the validation of clear and distinct perception are circular, each depending on the other.

Antoine Arnauld (1612–1694)

French theologian and philosopher who raised the Cartesian Circle objection in the Fourth Objections to Descartes' Meditations.

Degrees of Reality: Formal, Objective, and Eminent

Formal Reality

This is the reality a thing has *in itself*—its actual, independent existence. Things possess different degrees of formal reality based on their complexity and perfection.

Objective Reality

This is the reality an *idea* has *as representing something*. It refers to the subject matter or content of the idea.
