Important Terms (1/2)

A posteriori

(Latin for "from what comes after") Knowledge that depends on experience, derived from observation of the world.

A priori

(Latin for "from what comes before") Knowledge independent of experience, derived from reason alone.

Natural Theology

The attempt to prove God's existence and characteristics through reason and observation of nature.

Efficient Causation

The agent or force that brings something into being or causes a change. In Aquinas's Second Way, it refers to the sequence of causes and effects in the world.

Contingency

The quality of being possible but not necessary; something that could either exist or not exist. Aquinas's Third Way argues from the contingency of things to a necessary being.

Important Terms (2/2)

Substance

(Descartes' definition) That which exists in such a way as to depend on nothing else for its existence.

The Philosopher

Medieval term for Aristotle, frequently used by Aquinas in the Summa Theologica.

The fool

A reference from Psalm 14:1, "The fool says in his heart, 'There is no God'," used by Anselm and Aquinas to represent someone who denies God's existence.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Briefly describe Aquinas's First Way (from motion).

Everything that moves is moved by something else; an infinite regress of movers is impossible; therefore, there must be an unmoved first mover, which is God.

Briefly describe Aquinas's Second Way (from efficient causation).

Nothing can be the efficient cause of itself; a chain of causes cannot regress to infinity; therefore, there is a first uncaused cause, which is God.

Briefly describe Aquinas's Fourth Way (from degrees of perfection).

We observe things that are more or less good, true, and noble; things that admit of degrees require a maximum; this maximum is God.

Briefly describe Aquinas's Fifth Way (from design).

Natural things that lack intelligence behave for ends, as if directed by knowledge; this direction requires an intelligent being, which is God.
