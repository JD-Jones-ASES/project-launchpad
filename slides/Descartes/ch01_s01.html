The Dedicatory Letter to the Sorbonne

Demonstration via Natural Reason

Descartes argues that while faith is sufficient for the "faithful," philosophical arguments based on "natural reason" are required to persuade "infidels" of God’s existence and the soul's immortality.

Refuting Atheism

He claims that the "principal reason" for impiety is the belief that these two facts—God's existence and the distinction between soul and body—have never been demonstrated.

Synopsis of the Six Meditations

Meditation. Primary Theme

Key Developments

I. Universal Doubt

Sets out reasons to doubt all things, especially material objects, to deliver the mind from prejudice and senses.

II. The Nature of Mind

The mind uses its liberty to doubt everything, concluding that it is impossible for the mind itself not to exist. Establishes the mind as a "thinking thing."

III. Existence of God

Provides a principal argument for God’s existence based on the idea of a "supremely perfect being" within the human mind.

IV. Truth and Error

Defines the nature of truth and error in speculative matters. Explains that clear and distinct perceptions are always true.

V. Corporeal Nature

Explains the nature of bodies generally and offers a second, "new" proof of God's existence. Notes that geometrical certainty depends on knowing God.

VI. The External World

Distinguishes understanding from imagination. Proves the real distinction between mind and body, their close union, and the existence of material things.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Le Monde (The World)

Descartes' unpublished treatise on physics and cosmology that defended a heliocentric view; it was suppressed following Galileo's condemnation.

Light of Nature (Natural Reason)

The innate human capacity to perceive truth clearly and distinctly, independent of divine revelation or sensory input.

Objective Reality

The degree of reality or perfection represented by an idea. For example, the idea of an infinite being (God) has more objective reality than the idea of a finite substance.

Sorbonne

The Faculty of Theology at the University of Paris; a powerful body with the authority to approve or condemn philosophical works in 17th-century France.

Substance

According to Descartes, that which exists in such a way as to depend on nothing else for its existence.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Thinking Thing (Res Cogitans)

The essence of the mind or soul, characterized by the faculty of thinking, willing, and perceiving, distinct from the physical extension of the body.
