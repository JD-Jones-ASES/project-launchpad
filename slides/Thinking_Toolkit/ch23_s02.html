The Overfitting Problem

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Overfitting

— Calibrating a model so precisely to past data that it captures random noise rather than genuine signal, producing excellent historical performance but poor future performance

Signal vs. noise

— Signal refers to genuine, repeatable patterns; noise refers to random fluctuations specific to the training data that have no predictive value

Out-of-sample testing

— Testing a model on data it has never seen; the key diagnostic for detecting overfitting

Robustness vs. optimization

— The antidote to overfitting: prefer strategies that work reasonably well across many conditions over strategies that work perfectly in one specific condition

Important Terms

Training data

The historical data used to build and calibrate a model; overfitting produces perfect performance on training data but failure on new data

Parameters

The adjustable elements of a model; more parameters allow a model to fit any dataset perfectly, including random noise — this is why "fewer parameters" is the antidote

Backtesting

Testing a trading strategy against historical market data; a strategy that backtests brilliantly may have simply found patterns specific to that historical period

Maginot Line

France's elaborate WWI-era fortification system, an example of military overfitting: perfectly optimized for the last war's conditions, irrelevant for the next war's

Generalization

A model's ability to perform well on new data beyond its training set; the goal that overfitting destroys
