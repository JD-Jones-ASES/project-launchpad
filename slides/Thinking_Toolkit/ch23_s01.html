Chapter 23, Section 1

Gall's Law

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Gall's Law

— Working complex systems always evolve from working simple systems; complex systems designed from scratch consistently fail

Emergent behavior

— Properties that arise from the interaction of system components and cannot be predicted from the components alone; the reason designed complexity fails

Incremental development

— The reliable path to working complexity: build something simple that works, test it, then add one layer at a time

Waterfall model

— The failing design approach: specify everything upfront, build the complete system, then test — produces spectacular failures with remarkable regularity

Important Terms

John Gall

American pediatrician whose 1975 book Systemantics drew on decades of observations about how systems actually behave (as opposed to how designers expect them to)

Working prototype

A simple, functional version of a system that serves as the foundation for incremental complexity — the required starting point, per Gall's Law

Emergent failure

System failure that results not from any single defective component but from unpredicted interactions between components that each function correctly in isolation

Hubris in design

The temptation to design a perfect, comprehensive system from the ground up — what Gall's Law identifies as nearly always fatal to the project

Who formulated Gall's Law and in what foundational work?

John Gall, in his book "Systemantics: How Systems Really Work and How They Fail" (1975).

What was John Gall's professional background, and how did it influence his observations on systems?

He was a pediatrician whose insights on systems grew from decades of experience with medical, bureaucratic, and organizational systems.

Why is Gall's Law frequently ignored in fields like engineering, management, and institutional design?

Because of the nearly irresistible temptation to design a perfect, comprehensive system from the ground up, which almost always proves fatal to the project.

What is the fundamental reason complex systems designed from scratch fail, even if their designs seem logically sound on paper?

Complex systems have emergent behaviors—properties that arise from the interaction of their components and cannot be predicted from the components alone.

What is the only reliable method for discovering emergent behaviors in a system?

To build a simple version, run it, observe what actually happens, and then iterate.
