Chapter 22, Section 1

Feedback Loops — Reinforcing and Balancing

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Reinforcing (positive) feedback loop

— A process where output circles back to intensify the input, producing exponential growth or exponential collapse; "positive" means amplifying, not good

Balancing (negative) feedback loop

— A process where output circles back to counteract the input, producing stability, equilibrium, or oscillation around a set point

Feedback

— The fundamental mechanism by which the output of a process loops back to influence its own input

Dominance

— At any given moment, either reinforcing or balancing loops may dominate a system, determining whether it accelerates, stabilizes, or oscillates

Echo chamber

— A real-world reinforcing loop where algorithmic content delivery amplifies engagement in one direction without a built-in balancing mechanism

Important Terms

Systems thinking

An approach to analysis that focuses on the way system components interrelate and work over time within a larger context

Cybernetics

The formal study of feedback and control systems, originating with Norbert Wiener's 1948 work; the intellectual origin of feedback loop analysis

Set point

The target value a balancing loop maintains (e.g., the temperature a thermostat aims for)

Exponential growth

Growth produced by a reinforcing loop where each gain produces proportionally more gain

Equilibrium

The stable state maintained by a balancing loop, which the system returns to after disturbances

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the fundamental definition of "feedback" in systems thinking?

Feedback simply means that the output of a process loops back to influence the input.

Who is considered a key figure in the formal study of feedback systems, particularly in the mid-twentieth century?

Norbert Wiener, through his work in cybernetics (e.g., Cybernetics, 1948).

What are the two main types of feedback loops?

Reinforcing feedback loops and Balancing feedback loops.

Define a "reinforcing feedback loop."

A reinforcing feedback loop (also called positive feedback) is a process where change in one direction produces more change in the same direction.

What kind of change do reinforcing feedback loops produce, and what are they considered "engines" of?

They produce amplifying change and are engines of acceleration, leading to exponential growth or exponential collapse.

Explain why the term "positive feedback" for a reinforcing loop doesn't necessarily mean it's good.

"Positive" in this context means amplifying or intensifying the change, not that the outcome is desirable or good.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Provide an example of a reinforcing loop leading to collapse.

A bank run: depositors' withdrawals intensify fear, leading to more withdrawals and accelerating the bank's collapse.

Provide an example of a reinforcing loop leading to growth.

Compound interest: your interest earns interest, which earns more interest, leading to exponential growth of capital.

How does a popular social media post demonstrate a reinforcing loop?

More engagement leads the algorithm to show it to more people, which generates even more engagement, creating a self-amplifying cycle.

Define a "balancing feedback loop."

A balancing feedback loop (also called negative feedback) is a process where change in one direction produces a counterforce that resists the initial change.

What are balancing feedback loops the "engines" of?

They are engines of stability and equilibrium, keeping a system within bounds.
