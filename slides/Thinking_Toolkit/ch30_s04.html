Key Concepts (1/2)

Motte and Bailey

The Bailey

The desirable but indefensible position — a strong, controversial claim that attracts attention and advances the speaker's argument but cannot withstand scrutiny.

The Motte

The safe, defensible fallback position — a modest claim that is almost impossible to dispute but that does not actually support the bold claim the speaker was making.

Rhetorical Retreat

The move from bailey to motte when challenged, which feels like a response to the objection but actually avoids answering it.

Unconscious Deployment

The motte and bailey is often used without deliberate intent; the speaker may genuinely believe they have answered the objection by retreating to defensible ground.

Key Concepts (2/2)

The Remedy

Hold the speaker to their original claim. Acknowledge the motte, then ask directly whether they still maintain the stronger bailey and, if so, invite them to defend it on its own terms.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the origin of the term "Motte and Bailey" in rhetoric?

It was named by philosopher Nicholas Shackel in a 2005 paper, borrowing from medieval castle architecture.

Describe the architectural "motte" in a medieval castle context.

The motte is a heavily fortified stone tower on a hill; it is cramped and austere but nearly impregnable, serving as a last refuge for defenders.

Describe the architectural "bailey" in a medieval castle context.

The bailey is the larger, less defensible open courtyard below the motte; it is spacious and desirable, where people live, work, and graze animals.

What characterizes the "bailey" claim in the Motte and Bailey rhetorical maneuver?

It is a strong, bold, controversial claim that is hard to defend (e.g., 'Science is just another belief system').

What characterizes the "motte" claim in the Motte and Bailey rhetorical maneuver?

It is a much weaker, modest, true, and almost indisputable claim (e.g., 'Scientists are human and can make mistakes').
