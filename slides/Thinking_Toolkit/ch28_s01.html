The Monty Hall Problem — Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

The Monty Hall Problem

A probability puzzle in which switching doors after the host reveals a goat doubles your winning probability from 1/3 to 2/3

Conditional Probability

Probability updated by new information — the host's action changes the probability distribution over the remaining doors

Information Asymmetry

The host knows where the car is; his constrained, non-random action transfers information to the remaining door

The 50/50 Illusion

The intuition that two remaining doors are equally likely is wrong because it ignores how the host's constrained behavior concentrates probability

Probability Concentration

The 2/3 probability that the car is not behind your original door concentrates entirely on the door the host did not open

Key Concepts (2/2)

Knowledgeable Agent Effect

Whenever a knowledgeable agent filters options, the surviving options are not equally likely

Important Terms

Monty Hall

American television host of Let's Make a Deal (1963–1986), after whom the problem is named; he did not invent the puzzle

Marilyn vos Savant

American columnist who published the correct answer (always switch) in her 1990 Parade magazine column, receiving approximately 10,000 letters — many from professional mathematicians — insisting sh...

Constrained action

The host cannot open the door you chose, cannot open the door hiding the car, and must open a goat door — this constraint makes his action informative

Probability mass

The total probability assigned to all possibilities; the 2/3 mass originally distributed between the two unchosen doors moves entirely to the single remaining unchosen door after the host acts

100-door version

A clarifying variant — you pick one of 100 doors, Monty opens 98 goat doors, one remains; should you switch? Intuitively yes (99/100 vs. 1/100); the same logic applies to three doors
