The 10/10/10 Rule — Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

10/10/10 Rule

A decision framework by Suzy Welch that asks three temporal questions before deciding: How will I feel about this in 10 minutes? In 10 months? In 10 years?

Three Time Horizons

The framework's power comes from forcing consideration of short-term, medium-term, and long-term perspectives simultaneously — revealing gaps between what feels right now and what will matter later.

Emotional Fog

The rule is specifically designed for decisions made under emotional pressure — anger, excitement, fear, infatuation, social pressure — where the ten-minute perspective is badly distorted.

Short-term Emotion vs. Long-term Value

The core insight is that decisions optimized for the ten-minute window frequently produce outcomes that look very different at ten months and especially ten years.

Pattern Revelation

Comparing all three answers reveals whether a decision is guided by temporary emotion or durable preference — and often exposes which concerns are real and which will fade.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Not Always a Clear Answer

The framework does not guarantee a single correct choice; its value is that it reliably produces better thinking, not that it always resolves ambiguity.

Important Terms (1/2)

10/10/10 Framework

The three-question decision tool asking how you will feel about a choice in ten minutes, ten months, and ten years — designed to break through short-term emotional pressure.

Short-term Gratification

The immediate pleasure or relief a decision provides, which can dominate in the ten-minute window but may not reflect what serves long-term interests.

Temporal Discounting

The cognitive tendency to weight near-term outcomes more heavily than distant outcomes, even when the distant outcomes are objectively more significant; the 10/10/10 framework explicitly counteract...

Emotional Immediacy

The quality of strong present-tense emotions — anger, excitement, fear, infatuation — that makes them feel more important than they are relative to long-term considerations.

Buyer's Remorse

The dissatisfaction that follows an impulse purchase, representing the shift from a ten-minute perspective (retail therapy) to a ten-month perspective (actual utility).

Important Terms (2/2)

Cumulative Effect

The aggregate impact of decisions made consistently over time — small habits of exercise, saving, or avoidance that add up to transformative outcomes over ten-year timescales.
