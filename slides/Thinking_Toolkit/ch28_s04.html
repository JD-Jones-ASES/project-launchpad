The Gambler's Fallacy and the Hot Hand — Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Gambler's Fallacy

The false belief that past outcomes in a random, independent process influence future outcomes — that a streak of one outcome makes the opposite more likely

Independence

Events are independent when the outcome of one does not affect the probability of future events; coin flips and roulette spins are independent

Law of Large Numbers

The true principle that long-run proportions converge to theoretical probabilities — operates by dilution, not correction

Hot Hand

The belief that recent athletic success elevates the probability of further success — potentially real in skill contexts, unlike random processes

Two Opposite Errors

The Gambler's Fallacy assumes streaks will reverse; the Hot Hand belief assumes they will continue — both errors stem from failing to assess independence

Key Concepts (2/2)

The Key Question

Are the events independent? This determines which analysis applies

Important Terms (1/2)

Gambler's Fallacy

The erroneous belief that past independent random outcomes affect future probabilities; also called the Monte Carlo Fallacy

Monte Carlo incident (1913)

Black appeared at a roulette wheel an extraordinary number of consecutive times; gamblers lost millions betting on red, believing the streak must end

Law of Large Numbers

As trials increase, the observed proportion of outcomes converges to the theoretical probability; this works by diluting past imbalances, not correcting them

Hot hand

The belief that athletes who have recently made multiple successful shots are more likely to make the next; originally dismissed as a fallacy, now considered partially real

Thomas Gilovich, Robert Vallone, Amos Tversky

Authors of the influential 1985 study concluding the hot hand was an illusion — shots were essentially independent

Important Terms (2/2)

Joshua Miller and Adam Sanjurjo

Economists whose 2018 reanalysis identified a subtle statistical bias in the original study; their correction found evidence of a small real hot-hand effect

Independence

Two events are independent if the outcome of one does not change the probability of the other
