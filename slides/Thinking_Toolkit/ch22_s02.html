The Butterfly Effect

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Sensitive dependence on initial conditions

— The defining property of chaotic systems: tiny differences in starting states produce vastly different outcomes over time through exponential amplification

Deterministic chaos

— A system that is fully deterministic (each state follows from the previous by fixed rules) yet practically unpredictable over long time horizons because of sensitivity to initial conditions

Prediction horizon

— The time beyond which specific prediction becomes impossible; for weather, approximately ten days

Robustness over precision

— The practical response to chaos: design plans that perform adequately across many scenarios rather than betting on a single forecast

Important Terms

Chaos theory

The mathematical study of systems that exhibit sensitive dependence on initial conditions; founded largely by Lorenz's work in the 1960s

Lorenz attractor

The butterfly-shaped pattern traced by Lorenz's equations in three-dimensional space, which became an icon of chaos theory

Weather vs. climate

Weather refers to specific conditions on specific days (unpredictable beyond ~10 days); climate refers to long-term statistical patterns (more predictable)

Cascading interactions

The mechanism by which the Butterfly Effect works — small perturbations ripple through interconnected system components, amplifying at each step
