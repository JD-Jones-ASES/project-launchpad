Key Concepts (1/2)

Who developed Prospect Theory and when was it introduced?

Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky, in their 1979 paper “Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk.”

What classical economic assumption did Prospect Theory challenge?

It challenged the classical assumption that people evaluate outcomes in absolute terms (total wealth) rather than relative to a reference point.

According to Prospect Theory, how do most people choose between a guaranteed gain of $500 and a 50% chance of gaining $1,000?

Most people are risk-averse for gains and choose the guaranteed gain of $500 (the sure thing).

According to Prospect Theory, how do most people choose between a guaranteed loss of $500 and a 50% chance of losing $1,000?

Most people are risk-seeking for losses and choose the gamble (50% chance of losing $1,000) to avoid a sure loss.

What is the 'core insight' of Prospect Theory regarding decision-making under risk?

The framing of a choice as a gain or a loss, relative to a reference point, completely reverses people's preferences, even if the expected value is the same.

What is a 'reference point' in the context of Prospect Theory?

It is a starting point or baseline against which people evaluate outcomes as either a gain or a loss, rather than in absolute terms of total wealth.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the fundamental psychological asymmetry identified by Prospect Theory regarding gains and losses?

The psychological impact of moving away from a reference point is asymmetric: losses hurt more than equivalent gains feel good.

Roughly how much more intense is the pain of losing a given amount compared to the pleasure of gaining the same amount?

Kahneman and Tversky found the pain of losing to be roughly twice as intense as the pleasure of gaining an equivalent amount.
