Falsifiability — Popper's Criterion — Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Falsifiability

A claim is scientific if and only if it makes predictions specific enough that some conceivable observation could prove it wrong

Demarcation Criterion

Popper's answer to the question separating science from non-science — not truth, utility, or careful observation, but exposure to potential refutation

A Theory That Explains Everything Explains Nothing

When a theory can accommodate any possible outcome, it makes no real prediction and provides no genuine scientific knowledge

Risky Predictions as Scientific Virtue

Good scientific theories make specific, testable predictions that could fail — this exposure to falsification is what makes them informative

Unfalsifiable Does Not Mean Worthless

Moral principles and metaphysical claims may be unfalsifiable but still meaningful — they are simply not playing the scientific game

Key Concepts (2/2)

Contrast Cases

Einstein's general relativity (falsifiable, confirmed but could have failed) vs. Freudian theory (explains any outcome, immune to falsification)

Important Terms (1/2)

Karl Popper

Austrian-British philosopher (1902–1994); born in Vienna, fled Nazism; settled at the London School of Economics; published The Logic of Scientific Discovery (1934) and The Open Society and Its Ene...

Falsifiability

The property of a claim that it makes predictions specific enough to be potentially refuted by some conceivable observation

Demarcation problem

The philosophical question of what distinguishes scientific from non-scientific claims; Popper's answer is falsifiability

Ad hoc modification

An adjustment made to a theory specifically to protect it from a falsifying observation; a sign the theory is being immunized rather than tested

General relativity

Einstein's 1915 theory predicting that gravity bends starlight by a specific amount; confirmed by Eddington's 1919 solar eclipse expedition, but could have been refuted

Important Terms (2/2)

Freudian psychoanalysis

Popper's example of an unfalsifiable framework — any patient outcome (improvement or worsening) can be accommodated within the theory

Corroboration

Popper's preferred term for a theory's track record of surviving genuine tests — not "confirmation" (which implies proof) but evidence the theory has not yet been falsified
