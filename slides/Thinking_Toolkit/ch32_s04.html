Inversion — Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Inversion

A problem-solving technique that asks not "how do I achieve the outcome I want?" but "how would I guarantee the outcome I want to avoid?" — and then systematically avoids those things.

Jacobi's Principle

The mathematician Carl Jacobi's advice to "invert, always invert" — when a problem is hard to solve forward, work backward from the endpoint. Munger generalized this into practical reasoning.

Failure Cataloging

Inversion is powerful because the causes of failure are often more predictable, more universal, and easier to identify than the causes of success. Success is complex; failure has recurring patterns.

Dual-Direction Analysis

A robust strategy survives both forward reasoning ("here is how we succeed") and inverted reasoning ("here is how we fail, and we have addressed each failure mode").

Everyday Application

Inversion applies to presentations, relationships, personal finance, engineering — any domain where avoiding specific failure modes contributes more to good outcomes than positive brainstorming.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Relationship to Pre-Mortem

Inversion and the pre-mortem are closely related; the pre-mortem is essentially inversion applied to a specific upcoming project.

Important Terms (1/2)

Inversion

The problem-solving approach of reasoning backward from an undesired outcome to identify what would cause it, then systematically avoiding those causes.

Forward Reasoning

The conventional approach of asking "how do I achieve the outcome I want?" — starting from the present state and reasoning toward a desired future.

Backward Reasoning

The inverted approach of starting from an undesired outcome and reasoning backward to its causes — particularly useful when the causes of failure are clearer than the causes of success.

Failure Mode

A specific way in which a system, plan, or endeavor can go wrong; inversion is a method for cataloging failure modes before they materialize.

Margin of Safety

A related concept — the engineering practice of designing systems to handle loads far beyond their expected use, which is itself an application of inverted thinking: what would cause this to fail, ...

Important Terms (2/2)

Redundancy

The engineering practice of duplicating critical systems so that a single failure cannot cause a total failure — an application of inverted reasoning to system design.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the fundamental principle of 'Inversion' as a problem-solving technique?

Instead of asking how to achieve a desired outcome, inversion asks how to guarantee avoiding the outcome you want to prevent.

Who is credited with the advice, 'Invert, always invert'?

The mathematician Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi, a principle later adopted by Charlie Munger.

How did Carl Jacobi originally apply the principle of inversion in mathematics?

He used it as a problem-solving technique, working backward from a solution when a problem was difficult to solve forward.

How did Charlie Munger generalize Jacobi's mathematical inversion principle?

He expanded it into a principle of practical reasoning for decision-making and life, focusing on avoiding failure to improve outcomes.

What is the typical approach most people take when solving problems, and how does inversion differ?

Most people ask, 'How do I achieve the outcome I want?' Inversion asks the opposite: 'How would I guarantee the outcome I want to avoid?'

According to Munger's application of inversion, what behaviors would guarantee a miserable life?

Chronic dishonesty, resentment, substance abuse, envy, self-pity, and unreliability.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Applying inversion to business, what factors would guarantee failure?

Ignoring customers, mismanaging cash, hiring untrustworthy people, and refusing to adapt.

Why is inversion often considered a powerful problem-solving technique?

It is often easier to identify what leads to failure than what leads to success, as failure often has recurring, predictable patterns.

What is the nature of failure patterns compared to success patterns?

Success is complex and context-dependent, while failure often has recurring, predictable, and more universal patterns.

What is the primary benefit of cataloging and systematically eliminating the causes of failure?

While it may not guarantee success, it dramatically improves your odds of achieving a desired outcome.

How does inversion serve as a check on forward-looking plans or strategies?

After developing a strategy, one can invert it by asking, 'What could make this strategy fail?' to identify potential weaknesses.

What is the connection between inversion and a 'pre-mortem'?

Inversion, when used to check plans by identifying failure modes, is essentially a pre-mortem framed as 'how we fail'.
