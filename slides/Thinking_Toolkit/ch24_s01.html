Chapter 24, Section 1

The Sorites Paradox — The Heap

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Sorites Paradox

— The logical puzzle showing that no single grain of sand can make the difference between a "heap" and a "non-heap," yet heaps clearly exist; derived from the Greek word for "heap" (soros)

Vague predicates

— Terms like heap, bald, tall, rich, and old that admit borderline cases where applying the term is neither clearly correct nor clearly incorrect

The paradox's structure

— A logically valid argument with seemingly true premises that produces an obviously false conclusion, revealing a genuine flaw in our system of categories

Sharp vs. vague boundaries

— The contrast between categorical distinctions that have a precise threshold and those that are inherently gradual

Important Terms (1/2)

Eubulides of Miletus

4th-century BC Greek philosopher who formulated both the Sorites Paradox and the Liar Paradox

Epistemicism

The response that there is a precise boundary (exactly N grains make a heap) but we cannot know where it is; preserves classical logic but seems implausible

Supervaluationism

The response that vague predicates can be "precisified" in multiple legitimate ways; a statement is true if true on all precisifications; requires abandoning bivalence

Fuzzy logic

The response that replaces true/false with a continuous scale from 0 to 1 (a heap might be "0.7 true" with 5,000 grains); matches intuition but raises the question of where specific numbers come from

Bivalence

The classical logical principle that every statement is either true or false; supervaluationism requires abandoning it in borderline cases

Important Terms (2/2)

Bright line

A legally or institutionally stipulated sharp boundary (18 years old is an adult) that resolves vagueness for practical purposes, even though the underlying concept remains vague

Why It Generalizes

Baldness

Removing one hair from a full head does not make someone bald. But eventually, with enough removed hairs, the person is bald.

Tallness

There is no specific height that marks the exact boundary between "tall" and "not tall."

Richness, oldness, redness

All have the same structure — gradual transitions without a determinate threshold.

Philosophical Responses

Epistemicism

Sharp boundaries exist but are unknowable. Preserves classical logic but why would exactly 4,327 grains be the magic number?

Supervaluationism

Multiple legitimate "precisifications" exist; truth holds across all of them. Elegant, but requires abandoning bivalence.

Fuzzy logic

Truth comes in degrees (0.7 true, 0.3 true). Matches intuition, but why 0.7 specifically? The sharp boundary returns at a different level.
