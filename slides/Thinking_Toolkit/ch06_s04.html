Important Terms (1/2)

Fundamental Attribution Error (FAE)

A cognitive bias where people tend to overemphasize dispositional (character-based) explanations for others' behavior while underemphasizing situational explanations.

Correspondence Bias

Another name for the Fundamental Attribution Error, highlighting the tendency to assume that behavior "corresponds" directly to an underlying disposition.

Disposition

An internal, stable characteristic of a person, such as a personality trait, attitude, or belief.

Situational Pressures

External factors, circumstances, or environmental influences that can affect a person's behavior.

Trait Theorists

Individuals who believe that behavior is primarily determined by stable, internal personality traits or dispositions.

Cross-Cultural Variations

Individualistic vs. Collectivist Cultures

Research suggests the FAE is stronger in individualistic cultures (like the U.S.) that emphasize personal agency.

Collectivist Cultures

In collectivist cultures (like Japan or India), people are more attuned to situational and relational explanations, meaning the bias is still present but generally less pronounced.

"Fundamental" as an Overstatement

The cross-cultural variation implies that the term "fundamental" might be an overstatement globally, though it accurately reflects its pervasiveness in Western thought.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the 'Curse of Knowledge'?

Once you know something, you cannot imagine not knowing it, making it difficult to understand the perspective of someone who doesn't possess that knowledge.

Who coined the term 'Curse of Knowledge' and when?

Economists Colin Camerer, George Loewenstein, and Martin Weber in 1989.

What experiment vividly demonstrated the Curse of Knowledge?

Elizabeth Newton's 1990 'tapping experiment' at Stanford.

Describe Elizabeth Newton's tapping experiment.

Tappers chose a well-known song, tapped its rhythm, and asked a listener to guess it. Before the listener answered, tappers estimated the odds of success.

What were the estimated vs. actual success rates in Newton's tapping experiment?

Tappers estimated a 50% success rate, but the actual success rate was only 2.5%.

Why did tappers in Newton's experiment so vastly overestimate the listener's success rate?

Tappers could hear the melody in their heads, making the song seem obvious, while listeners only heard a series of disconnected taps.
