Important Terms

Anchoring

A cognitive bias where an initial piece of information (the anchor) biases subsequent judgments toward that anchor, even if the anchor is irrelevant.

Heuristics and Biases

Mental shortcuts (heuristics) that can lead to systematic errors in judgment (biases). Anchoring is one such bias.

Insufficient Adjustment

One proposed mechanism for anchoring, where people make an initial estimate based on the anchor but fail to adjust sufficiently away from it.

Selective Accessibility

Another proposed mechanism for anchoring, where the anchor makes information consistent with it more accessible in memory, making the anchor seem more plausible.

Introduction to Anchoring

Pioneering Research

Anchoring was experimentally demonstrated in 1974 by Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman, as part of their influential work on heuristics and biases.

Widespread Replication

The effect has since been replicated hundreds of times globally, across various fields and demographics, confirming its universality and robustness.

What is Anchoring?

Definition

Anchoring describes the phenomenon where the first number or piece of information encountered serves as a reference point that influences subsequent judgments, pulling them toward that initial anchor.

Nature of the Anchor

The anchor's influence persists even when it is arbitrary, irrelevant, or known to be meaningless.

Robustness of the Effect

Broad Impact

Anchoring affects both experts and novices alike.
