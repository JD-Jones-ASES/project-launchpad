Important Terms

Cunningham's Law

The maxim stating, "The best way to get the right answer on the internet is not to ask a question; it is to post the wrong answer."

Ward Cunningham

The programmer and inventor credited with formulating Cunningham's Law and creating the first wiki in 1995.

Wiki

A website that allows users to collaboratively add, modify, or delete content and structure. Wikipedia is the most famous example.

Engagement

In the context of online platforms, the level of interaction users have with content, often measured by comments, likes, shares, and corrections.

Provocation

An action or statement that is intended to make someone angry or react strongly, often used in the context of deliberately posting wrong information to elicit a response.

Introduction to Cunningham's Law

Origin

Attributed to Ward Cunningham, the creator of the wiki concept.

Formulation

"The best way to get the right answer on the internet is not to ask a question; it is to post the wrong answer."

Observation

Asking a question online may result in a slow response, while posting a confidently incorrect answer typically generates immediate corrections.

Underlying Principle

This law captures a fundamental aspect of human motivation and knowledge-sharing psychology.

The Psychological Mechanism

Questions vs. Wrong Answers

Human Tendency

Most people will ignore a question, but few can resist correcting a wrong answer.

Motivations

The impulse to correct is not always noble; it often includes a desire to demonstrate one's own knowledge, not just to help the person who is wrong.

Reliability

Regardless of noble intent, wrong answers reliably provoke corrections that are frequently more detailed, thorough, and helpful than direct answers would have been.

Practical Applications Beyond the Internet

Educational Settings

Teachers use this by making deliberately provocative or slightly incorrect statements to stimulate class discussion. Students who might stay silent for a question will speak up to correct an error.

Professional Environments

The Shadow Side of Cunningham's Law

Blurring Lines

In modern information environments, the distinction between a genuine error and a deliberate provocation (e.g., "clickbait" or "outrage bait") can become unclear.

Exploitation by Platforms

Social media platforms have learned that outrage, often triggered by wrong or misleading claims, drives user engagement more effectively than accuracy or genuine curiosity.

Ethical Concerns

While Cunningham's Law describes a useful human tendency, exploiting this tendency through deliberate misinformation is not always benign or ethical.

Wrong Answers Provoke Corrections (Summary from Key Concept Block)

Core Principle

People are more strongly motivated to correct wrong answers than to respond to questions.

Real-World Example: Wikipedia's Correction Dynamics

Implicit Reliance

Ward Cunningham's wiki concept, allowing anyone to edit, inherently leverages Cunningham's Law.

Self-Healing Mechanism

Wikipedia functions not because every contributor is an expert, but because errors attract corrections.

Process

Vandalism or well-intentioned but incorrect edits are often quickly noticed and fixed by other editors.

Accuracy Sustained

Wikipedia's overall accuracy, despite its imperfections, is maintained by this dynamic, harnessing the human compulsion to correct errors for collaborative knowledge building.

Connections (Related Concepts)

The Streisand Effect

Another psychological phenomenon where attempts to hide or suppress information inadvertently draw more attention to it.

Confirmation Bias

The tendency to search for, interpret, favor, and recall information in a way that confirms one's preexisting beliefs or hypotheses.
