The Nature of the Planning Fallacy

Identification

First identified by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky in 1979, the planning fallacy is a systematic, rather than random, error in judgment.

Core Tendency

It describes our consistent habit of underestimating how much time, money, and risk a future project will require, while simultaneously overestimating its potential benefits.

Common Experience

This phenomenon is a universally recognized experience: projects consistently take longer and cost more than initially expected (e.g., essays, home renovations, software launches).

Not Just Bad Luck

It's not a matter of occasional misfortune or laziness, but a fundamental aspect of how the human mind approaches predictions about the future.

Inside View vs. Outside View

Beyond Optimism

While optimism plays a role, the planning fallacy is a distinct cognitive error rooted in how we construct our estimates.

Kahneman's Personal Illustration

• In his book Thinking, Fast and Slow, Kahneman recounts a curriculum-design project where he participated.

• When he asked an expert colleague about how long similar projects typically took, the expert estimated 7 to 10 years.

• However, the team's own internal estimate (likely an inside view) was just two years.

• The project ultimately took eight years, and the curriculum was never even used. This anecdote powerfully illustrates that even individuals aware of the planning fallacy can still fall prey to it.

Universality and Real-World Impact

Megaprojects

Famous examples include the Sydney Opera House (estimated 4 years, $7 million; took 16 years, $102 million) and Boston's "Big Dig" highway project (budgeted $2.8 billion, delivered at $14.6 billion).

Everyday Life

Students consistently underestimate assignment completion times, even when reminded of past experiences. Software developers frequently miss deadlines despite extensive industry data.

What is Status Quo Bias?

A disproportionate preference for the current state of affairs that goes beyond what rational analysis would justify.

Who named the concept of Status Quo Bias and in what year?

William Samuelson and Richard Zeckhauser in 1988.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Define the 'default effect'.

The tendency for whatever option is pre-selected to win, not because it is objectively the best choice, but because it is effortless.

According to Samuelson and Zeckhauser's experiments, why might someone keep an inherited stock portfolio they wouldn't have chosen?

Because the current arrangement exerts a 'gravitational pull' simply by being current, even if it's not optimal, illustrating Status Quo Bias.

What 'opportunity' does understanding the default effect present to system designers?

Designers can harness the default effect for good by making the beneficial option the default in systems like retirement plans or software settings.

What term did Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein use in 'Nudge' to describe preserving freedom of choice while structuring the choice environment to make the easiest path the best path?

Libertarian paternalism.
