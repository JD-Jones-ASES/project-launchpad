The Gish Gallop — Study Guide

Key Concepts (1/2)

Asymmetry of Argumentation

Brandolini's Law

The "Bullshit Asymmetry Principle" — the amount of energy needed to refute nonsense is an order of magnitude larger than the energy needed to produce it. This is structural, not rhetorical.

Duane Gish

The biochemist and creationist debater whose technique gave the tactic its name; his effectiveness came from the volume and speed of claims, not their accuracy.

Disciplined Focus as Defense

The correct response to a Gish Gallop is not to chase every claim but to identify the strongest or most central claim, address it thoroughly, and name the tactic.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Volume Over Quality

The Gish Gallop deliberately substitutes quantity of claims for quality of argument, exploiting time constraints and audience attention.

Important Terms (1/2)

Gish Gallop

A debate tactic involving the rapid delivery of numerous arguments — regardless of accuracy — to overwhelm an opponent's ability to respond adequately within the available time.

Brandolini's Law (Bullshit Asymmetry Principle)

The observation that refuting misinformation requires dramatically more effort than producing it — an order of magnitude more, by some estimates.

Refutation

The act of disproving or demonstrating the falsity of a claim; refutations typically require citing evidence, addressing the underlying misunderstanding, and explaining the error — far more effort ...

Talking Points

Pre-prepared arguments that can be delivered quickly and efficiently, often used in Gish Gallop-style debates to maintain a high rate of claim generation.

Factual Error

A claim that contradicts established evidence; in a Gish Gallop, factual errors are often mixed with half-truths and rhetorical questions to create a plausible surface appearance.

Important Terms (2/2)

Selective Focus

The recommended counter-strategy — choosing the most important claim to address thoroughly rather than attempting to refute every claim and doing justice to none.
