Important Terms (1/2)

Brooks's Law

"Adding manpower to a late software project makes it later." The foundational idea discussed.

The Mythical Man-Month

The concept that challenges the notion of linear scalability between personnel and time; a "man-month" of work isn't always divisible across multiple people to shorten the calendar time.

Communication Overhead

The non-productive time spent communicating and coordinating among team members, which increases disproportionately with team size.

OS/360

IBM's large-scale operating system project from the 1960s, managed by Fred Brooks, which served as the primary case study and inspiration for Brooks's Law.

Partitionable Task

A task that can be effectively divided into smaller, independent sub-tasks, allowing parallel work.

Important Terms (2/2)

Non-Partitionable Task

A task that cannot be easily divided or run in parallel due to inherent dependencies or sequential requirements.

Nuances and Exceptions

• Projects that are already late.

• Projects where the remaining work is tightly interconnected and complex.

• Situations where the deadline is near, leaving little room for integration costs.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the core principle of Parkinson's Law?

Work expands so as to fill the time available for its completion.

Who formulated Parkinson's Law and in what year was it first published?

C. Northcote Parkinson, in The Economist in 1955.

What type of observation did Parkinson present his law as?

A serious empirical observation, backed by data.

What striking historical evidence from the British Royal Navy did Parkinson use to support his law?

Between 1914 and 1928, the number of ships and sailors decreased significantly, but the number of Admiralty officials increased by 78%.

How did the British Colonial Office demonstrate Parkinson's Law?

Its staff grew even as the number of colonies it administered declined.

What are the two primary driving forces behind bureaucratic expansion identified by Parkinson?

The 'Law of Multiplication of Subordinates' and the 'Law of Multiplication of Work.'
