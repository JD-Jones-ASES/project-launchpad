Key Concepts (1/2)

Prisoner's Dilemma

A situation where two individuals, acting in their own self-interest, choose to betray each other, resulting in a worse outcome for both than if they had cooperated.

A table used in game theory to represent the outcomes (payoffs) for each player based on all possible combinations of their strategic choices. Higher numbers typically indicate better outcomes.

Dominant Strategy

A strategy that yields a better outcome for a player regardless of what the other player does. In the Prisoner's Dilemma, defection is the dominant strategy for each individual.

Important Terms (1/2)

Prisoner's Dilemma

A game theory scenario where individual rational choices lead to a collectively suboptimal outcome for all parties involved.

Payoff Matrix

A visual tool in game theory that displays the potential outcomes (payoffs) for each player given the choices made by all players in the game.

Dominant Strategy

A strategy that is always the best choice for a player, irrespective of the strategies chosen by other players.

Nash Equilibrium

A concept where no player can benefit by changing their strategy while the other players keep theirs unchanged, representing a stable outcome.

Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma

A version of the Prisoner's Dilemma where the game is played multiple times, allowing players to learn, build reputations, and base decisions on past interactions.

The Genesis of the Prisoner's Dilemma

Origin

Developed by Merrill Flood and Melvin Dresher at RAND Corporation in 1950, formalized by Albert W. Tucker.

Significance

The most influential model in game theory and a highly studied problem in social sciences.

The Stable Outcome: Nash Equilibrium

Definition

A state where no player can improve their outcome by changing their strategy *unilaterally*.

In Prisoner's Dilemma

The outcome where both players defect is the Nash equilibrium.

Why it's stable

If both are defecting, neither can switch to cooperation alone without getting the worst possible outcome (0 "years avoided") while their partner gets the best (10 "years avoided").

Arms Races

Price Wars

Companies would profit more by keeping prices high (cooperation), but each can gain market share by cutting prices (defection). Both cutting prices reduces overall profits.

Environmental Degradation

Countries benefit from collective emissions reductions, but each has an incentive to "free-ride" on others' efforts, leading to continued pollution.

Students benefit if everyone studies honestly, but an individual cheater gains an advantage. If many cheat, an honest student falls behind.

Properties of Successful Strategies (Axelrod's Findings)

Implication

Under repeated interaction, the ability to punish and forgive, and transparency, cooperation becomes the rational strategy. Trust becomes a competitive advantage.

When Cooperation Breaks Down

A Known Endpoint (Backward Induction Problem)

If players know the exact round the game will end, rational defection unravels from the last round backward to the first.

Anonymity

If players cannot identify each other or track past behavior, reputation cannot form, and strategies like tit-for-tat become impossible.

Large Groups

In large groups, individual defection is harder to detect and punish. This is a key reason for issues like the **Tragedy of the Commons**, where shared resources are depleted.
