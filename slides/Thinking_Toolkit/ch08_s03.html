The Origin of the Paradox

Plutarch's Account

The paradox originates with the Greek philosopher Plutarch in the 1st century AD, documented in his Life of Theseus.

The Scenario

Athenians preserved the ship Theseus sailed to Crete. Over time, as planks rotted, they were systematically replaced with new timber. Eventually, every single original plank was replaced.

The Core Question

Plutarch asked: Is it still the "Ship of Theseus" after all its original parts have been replaced? This question explores what fundamentally constitutes the identity of an object across time.

Arguments for "Same" (Continuity)

The "Yes" Argument

Those who argue it is still the same ship appeal to the concept of continuity.

Explanation

The ship maintained an unbroken chain of existence; it was never destroyed, only repaired and maintained. Its continuous presence and function define its identity.

Real-World Analogy

This is the common-sense view many apply to themselves – though every cell in your body is replaced over years, you still perceive yourself as the same person.

Arguments for "Different" (Material Identity)

The "No" Argument

Those who argue it is a different ship appeal to material identity.

Explanation

An object's identity is fundamentally tied to its original parts. Once the original parts are gone, the original thing is gone too.

Real-World Analogy

If every single component of a car is replaced with different components, it might seem illogical to still call it the "same" car.

Hobbes's Twist: The Reassembled Ship

The Added Complexity

Thomas Hobbes later proposed an additional layer to the paradox.

The Scenario

Imagine someone collects all the discarded original planks and uses them to reassemble a second ship.

The New Question

Now there are two ships. Which one is the "real" Ship of Theseus?

Plutarch's Contribution (Personbox)

• Plutarch was not a philosopher focused on building grand systems but rather a humanist.

• He used biography and anecdotes to explore questions of human character, virtue, and identity.

• His well-posed question of the Ship of Theseus has proven more durable and influential than many formal philosophical systems of his era.

Modern Relevance: Digital Identity (Real-World Application)

The Question

If a mind is perfectly uploaded to a computer (a copy of all neural connections) and the biological brain is destroyed, is the "upload" still you?

Conflict

The upload has memories, personality, and thought patterns (similar to form/function), but it lacks continuity with the biological existence. It's a copy, not a direct continuation.

Challenge

The puzzle makes us question if continuity is merely a "prejudice" attached to biological systems. It helps frame these complex questions precisely.

Key Concepts (1/2)

In which of Plato's works does the story of the Ring of Gyges appear?

Plato's Republic, Book II.

Who tells the story of the Ring of Gyges in the Republic, and to whom?

Glaucon tells the story to challenge Socrates.

What is the basic premise of the Ring of Gyges story?

A shepherd named Gyges finds a ring that grants him invisibility and uses it to seize power without consequences.

What actions did Gyges take with the power of the invisibility ring?

He seduced the queen, killed the king, and seized the throne.

What core question about justice does Glaucon pose to Socrates using the Ring of Gyges?

If one could act with complete impunity (like with the ring), would they still behave justly?

What is Glaucon's suspicion about how both a 'just' and 'unjust' person would behave with the Ring of Gyges?

Glaucon suspects both would use it for personal gain, stating "No man would keep his hands off what was not his own when he could safely take what he liked."

Key Concepts (2/2)

The Ring of Gyges thought experiment goes beyond magic rings to explore what fundamental question?

The foundations of justice: do we behave well because we want to, or out of fear of consequences?

What is the distinction between two theories of justice highlighted by the Ring of Gyges?

Instrumental justice and Intrinsic justice.

Define 'Instrumental Justice.'

Behaving well because of external incentives, such as reputation, punishment avoidance, or rewards.

Define 'Intrinsic Justice.'

Behaving well because justice is good in itself, regardless of external consequences, and is constitutive of a good life; an unjust soul is a disordered soul.

What view of justice suggests it is merely a 'social contract'?

The idea that justice is a deal we make because the alternative (a "war of all against all") is worse.

According to the 'social contract' view, when does the contract bind?

Only when enforcement is possible; if enforcement is removed, the contract dissolves.
