Two-Way vs. One-Way Doors — Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

One-Way Door

An irreversible decision — once made, it cannot be undone or easily reversed. These decisions deserve careful analysis, deliberation, and consultation.

Two-Way Door

A reversible decision — if it does not work out, you can walk back through and choose differently. These decisions deserve speed rather than extensive deliberation.

Reversibility as a Decision Variable

The most important first step in any decision is not to evaluate options but to classify the decision itself: is it reversible or irreversible?

Organizational Paralysis

Most organizations treat two-way doors as one-way doors, creating approval layers and committees for decisions that could be made quickly — at the cost of speed and opportunity.

Psychological Barriers to Speed

Loss aversion, status quo bias, and the organizational incentive to never be blamed for a wrong decision all push toward treating every decision as a one-way door.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Bezos's Framework

Jeff Bezos articulated this distinction in Amazon shareholder letters, calling them "Type 1" (irreversible) and "Type 2" (reversible) decisions.

Important Terms (1/2)

Irreversible Decision (Type 1 / One-Way Door)

A decision whose consequences cannot reasonably be undone — selling a business, having a child, publishing a sensitive document. These warrant thorough deliberation.

Reversible Decision (Type 2 / Two-Way Door)

A decision that can be rescinded or changed at manageable cost if it proves wrong — launching a product feature, trying a new hobby, choosing a first major. These warrant speed.

Loss Aversion

The cognitive tendency to weight potential losses more heavily than equivalent potential gains; causes people to over-invest in deliberating reversible decisions.

Status Quo Bias

The preference for the current state of affairs over any alternative, even when the alternative is objectively preferable; reinforces avoidance of two-way doors.

Opportunity Cost

Important Terms (2/2)

Inaction Cost

The cost of failing to act, often invisible in decision analysis; in organizations, nobody is blamed for a decision never made, creating systematic incentives for inaction.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the primary objective of a pre-mortem decision tool?

The primary objective is to examine what could go wrong before a project begins, rather than reacting after it fails.

Who introduced the concept of the pre-mortem as a structured decision tool?

Gary Klein, in his 2003 book 'The Power of Intuition' and related work on naturalistic decision-making.

How does a 'pre-mortem' differ from a 'post-mortem'?

A post-mortem examines what went wrong after a project has failed, while a pre-mortem examines what could go wrong before the project begins.

What is the first step in conducting a pre-mortem exercise?

Assume failure: Imagine that you are one year in the future and the project has failed completely and spectacularly.

After assuming failure, what is the second step in the pre-mortem process?

Explain why: Each person independently writes down the reasons for the failure, including what went wrong, what wasn't anticipated, and what risks materialized.

What is the third step of a pre-mortem after explanations of failure are gathered?

Compile and prioritize: Gather all explanations and identify the most plausible, frequently mentioned, and catastrophic failure modes.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the final step in the pre-mortem process, once high-priority failure modes are identified?

Mitigate: For each high-priority failure mode, determine what can be done now to prevent it, reduce its likelihood, or limit its impact.

Name one powerful cognitive bias that the pre-mortem is designed to counteract.

Optimism bias.

How does optimism bias affect decision-making without a pre-mortem?

Once committed to a plan, group members tend to focus only on reasons for success and downplay potential failures, suppressing concerns.

How does the pre-mortem counteract optimism bias?

By asking people to explain a hypothetical failure, it gives them explicit permission to raise concerns they might otherwise suppress.

What is the second powerful bias addressed by the pre-mortem tool?

Groupthink.

Describe how groupthink can hinder effective risk identification in a team.

In a team setting, dissent is socially costly; individuals may suppress doubts to avoid 'raining on the parade' or appearing critical.
