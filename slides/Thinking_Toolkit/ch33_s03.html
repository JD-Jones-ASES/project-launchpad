Important Terms (1/2)

Map

Any mental model, framework, heuristic, or simplification used to represent and navigate reality; useful and necessary, but always incomplete relative to the territory it represents.

Territory

The actual, complex reality that mental models attempt to represent; always more complex than any map can fully capture.

Metacognition

Thinking about your own thinking — the capacity to observe, evaluate, and correct your own reasoning processes; the skill the three questions are designed to cultivate.

Vigilance

The active, ongoing effort required to catch yourself in cognitive errors; the section describes it as necessary because structural biases do not disappear with knowledge of them.

Important Terms (2/2)

Provisional Knowledge

Knowledge held open to revision — the epistemic attitude recommended by the closing reflection and by the intellectual history of the course's thinkers.

Latticework

The interconnected structure of multiple mental models, each reinforcing and checking the others — the ideal toolkit for navigating complex reality.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What central metaphor does the book use to explain the relationship between mental models and reality?

The map is not the territory.

In the metaphor 'the map is not the territory,' what do 'maps' represent?

Mental models, heuristics, biases, and paradoxes—simplified representations of a reality too complex to grasp entirely.

Why are 'maps' (mental models, biases, etc.) considered essential, even though they are not the absolute truth?

Without them, we would be lost, unable to navigate or understand reality.

What is the 'most dangerous feeling' a reader might develop after finishing a book on cognitive biases?

The temptation to believe they have assembled a 'complete atlas' and are now invulnerable to the errors these models describe.

Why is believing one is immune to cognitive biases after reading about them a sign of having 'learned nothing'?

Cognitive biases are structural features of the human mind—your mind, not just other people's—and knowing about them doesn't grant immunity.

Name two specific cognitive biases mentioned that do not spare those who can identify them.

The Dunning-Kruger Effect, Confirmation bias, and the Planning fallacy are all mentioned. (Any two are sufficient).

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the 'real value' of this book, beyond just a catalog of mental models?

The cultivation of a specific habit of mind: pausing, in the middle of a judgment or a decision, to reflect on one's thinking.

What is the first of the three crucial questions to ask when practicing the recommended 'habit of mind'?

What model am I using?

What is the purpose of asking 'What model am I using?' when making a judgment or decision?

To make the underlying framework, assumptions, or simplification of reality explicit, which is the first step toward evaluating it.

What is the second crucial question to ask about a mental model?

Where does this model apply?

Why is it important to ask 'Where does this model apply?'?

To identify the model's domain of validity—a range of situations where it illuminates more than it obscures—and prevent stretching it past its breaking point.

Which of the three questions is considered the 'most important' and the one we are most inclined to skip?

Where does this model break down?
