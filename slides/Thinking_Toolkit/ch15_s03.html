The Core of the Game of Chicken

If both swerve

They avoid a crash, ending in a minor draw. There might be some embarrassment, but no catastrophe.

If one swerves and the other keeps going straight

The one who held firm "wins" and is seen as courageous, while the swerver is the "chicken" and is humiliated.

If neither swerves

This is the worst possible outcome – a mutual, catastrophic crash.

Real-World Manifestations

Labor Negotiations

When a union threatens a costly strike and management threatens equally costly layoffs. Each side hopes the other will concede first.

Geopolitical Standoffs

Nations engaged in territorial disputes, trade wars, or, most critically, nuclear deterrence, often signal resolve, hoping the other will "blink."

Everyday Interactions

Even simple scenarios like two people approaching each other on a narrow path, each waiting for the other to step aside, can reflect the Chicken dynamic.

The Danger of Mutual Commitment

Catastrophe if Both Commit

If both players credibly commit to holding firm—if both throw away their steering wheels—the inevitable outcome is mutual destruction.

The Need for Asymmetry

The strategy only works if one side's commitment is more credible or if the other side still retains the option (and willingness) to back down.

A Real Threat

This is not a hypothetical concern; it was precisely the terrifying logic that nearly brought about the end of civilization during the Cuban Missile Crisis.

Game of Chicken vs. Prisoner's Dilemma

Prisoner's Dilemma

The optimal (dominant) strategy for each player is to defect, regardless of what the other player does, even if mutual cooperation would yield a better collective outcome.
