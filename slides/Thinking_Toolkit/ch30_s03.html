Hanlon's Razor — Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Hanlon's Razor

"Never attribute to malice that which is adequately explained by incompetence." Before assuming deliberate wrongdoing, consider the simpler explanation that the behavior resulted from carelessness,...

Default Hypothesis

The razor functions as a starting point for interpretation, not a final verdict; it does not deny that malice exists, but says not to begin there.

Fundamental Attribution Error

The cognitive bias toward explaining others' behavior through character or intent while explaining our own behavior through circumstances — Hanlon's Razor corrects for this asymmetry.

Constructive vs. Escalatory Response

Assuming incompetence leads to problem-solving responses; assuming malice leads to defensive or retaliatory responses that tend to worsen conflicts.

Historical Roots

Similar observations appear in Goethe, Napoleon, and other thinkers — the insight is ancient, but it requires constant rediscovery because human pattern-recognition is biased toward detecting threats.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Threat Detection Bias

The evolved tendency to see intentional agency and conspiratorial patterns in what are often organizational failures, errors, or oversights.

Important Terms (1/2)

Malice

The deliberate intention to cause harm or harm another's interests; the hypothesis that Hanlon's Razor asks us to consider only after simpler explanations have been ruled out.

Incompetence

Lack of skill, knowledge, attention, or training sufficient for a task — the more common explanation for many behaviors that initially appear hostile or intentional.

Fundamental Attribution Error

The systematic tendency to over-attribute other people's behavior to their character or intentions while under-attributing it to situational factors; Hanlon's Razor is a practical corrective.

Charitable Interpretation

The practice of assuming the most benign plausible explanation for another person's behavior before considering more negative ones.
