Key Concepts (2/2)

Supererogation

Acts that are morally praiseworthy but not morally required; they go "above and beyond the call of duty." Thomson's argument suggests that sustaining the violinist is supererogatory, not obligatory.

Important Terms (1/2)

Thomson's Violinist

The specific thought experiment designed by Judith Jarvis Thomson to explore the relationship between the right to life and bodily autonomy.

Right to Life

The fundamental entitlement of a person not to be killed without justification.

Right to Use Another's Body

The contentious claim that an individual's right to life implies an obligation for others to provide their body (or bodily resources) to sustain that life. Thomson argues against this expansive int...

Bodily Autonomy

The principle that individuals have the right to control their own bodies and make decisions regarding their physical being without coercion.

Supererogation

(From Latin supererogare, "to pay beyond what is due.") A morally praiseworthy act that is not morally required; it goes beyond the call of duty.

Important Terms (2/2)

Good Samaritan

In Thomson's framework, someone who goes far beyond what duty requires, performing acts of extraordinary generosity.

Minimally Decent Samaritan

Someone who performs the bare minimum that morality demands, fulfilling basic obligations.

Rights, Obligations, and the Limits of Duty

Claim 1: A has a right to life.

This is generally accepted and means others may not kill A without justification.

Claim 2: A has a right to whatever is needed to sustain A's life.

This is a much stronger and more controversial claim, implying that others are obligated to provide resources, labor, or even bodily support.

The Good Samaritan and the Minimally Decent Samaritan

Good Samaritan

Someone who goes above and beyond what is morally required, performing acts of extraordinary selflessness.

Minimally Decent Samaritan

Someone who fulfills the basic, minimal requirements of morality.

Real-World Applications

• The state generally cannot compel individuals to donate blood or organs, even if it could save lives.

• Parents are not legally required to donate a kidney to a dying child, despite the close relationship and potential for enormous benefit.

It does not follow that they have a right to use your body to sustain that life.

She distinguishes between actively killing someone and declining to sustain someone at significant personal cost.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What three key factors in the violinist scenario does Thomson use to support her argument against an obligation to stay connected?

You did not create the violinist's condition, you did not volunteer, and you were kidnapped.

How does Thomson characterize the act of staying connected to the violinist for nine months in terms of obligation?

She argues it would be a generous and admirable act, but generosity is not the same as obligation.

In the context of 'rights,' what does the claim 'A has a right to life' generally mean?

It means others may not kill A without justification.

What is the potential consequence if the concept of 'right to life' expands into 'a right to whatever is needed to sustain A's life'?

It expands into a right to commandeer other people's bodies, which Thomson argues is untenable.

What significant distinction in moral philosophy does the violinist scenario clearly illustrate?

The distinction between active killing and withholding aid (or declining to provide extraordinary sacrifices).
