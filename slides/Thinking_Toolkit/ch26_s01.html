Hilbert's Grand Hotel — Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Hilbert's Hotel

A thought experiment demonstrating that an infinite hotel, even when completely full, can accommodate additional guests through rearrangement

Infinity vs. Large Finite Numbers

Infinite sets behave qualitatively differently from very large finite sets — the whole can equal one of its proper parts

One-to-One Correspondence

The mathematical definition of equal "size" (cardinality) for infinite sets — two sets have the same cardinality if their elements can be paired exactly

Countable Infinity

The cardinality of the natural numbers (aleph-null, ℵ₀) — the natural numbers, even numbers, odd numbers, integers, and rational numbers all share this cardinality

Uncountable Infinity

A strictly larger infinity — the real numbers are uncountably infinite, proved by Cantor's diagonal argument

Key Concepts (2/2)

Hierarchy of Infinities

Cantor's discovery that there is not just one infinity but a tower of strictly increasing infinite cardinalities

Important Terms (1/2)

David Hilbert

German mathematician (1862–1943) who proposed the Hotel thought experiment in a 1924 lecture to illustrate Cantor's mathematics of infinity

Georg Cantor

German mathematician who developed the mathematics of infinite sets in the 1870s–1880s; defined cardinality by one-to-one correspondence and proved different sizes of infinity exist

Cardinality

The mathematical "size" of a set; for infinite sets, defined by whether a one-to-one correspondence can be established with another set

Aleph-null (ℵ₀)

The cardinality of the natural numbers — the smallest infinite cardinal; any set in one-to-one correspondence with the natural numbers has this cardinality

Cantor's diagonal argument

The proof that real numbers between 0 and 1 cannot be listed — for any proposed list, one can construct a real number differing from the nth entry at the nth decimal place

Important Terms (2/2)

Uncountably infinite

A set with strictly greater cardinality than the natural numbers; the real numbers are uncountably infinite

Countably infinite

A set in one-to-one correspondence with the natural numbers; the even numbers, the integers, and the rationals are all countably infinite
