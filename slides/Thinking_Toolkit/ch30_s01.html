First Principles Thinking — Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

First Principles Thinking

The practice of stripping a problem back to its most basic, independently verifiable truths and building reasoning upward from those foundations rather than from analogy or convention.

Reasoning by Analogy

Extending patterns from past experience, conventional wisdom, or what others are doing into new situations — efficient but bounded by the accuracy of the analogy.

Aristotle's Archai

The ancient Greek concept of foundational truths — the "first basis from which a thing is known" — from which all other knowledge in a field is derived.

Ceiling of Conventional Wisdom

The limit beyond which analogical reasoning cannot take you; when the underlying situation changes, borrowed patterns lead confidently in the wrong direction.

Cost Decomposition

The first principles approach of breaking a product or problem into its fundamental constituent parts (raw materials, processes, overhead) to identify where true costs originate.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Contextual Tradeoff

First principles thinking is not always superior to analogical reasoning; its value is greatest when stakes are high, conventional wisdom may be wrong, and sufficient time and expertise are available.

Important Terms

Archai

The Greek term used by Aristotle for the foundational principles of a field — truths that cannot be derived from anything more fundamental and upon which all other knowledge rests.

Analogical Reasoning

Reasoning by comparison to prior cases, existing conventions, or the practices of others; faster and less effortful than first principles thinking but limited by the validity of the analogy.
