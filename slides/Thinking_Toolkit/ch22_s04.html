Chapter 22, Section 4

The Law of Unintended Consequences

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Unintended consequences

— Effects of an action beyond its intended scope; these can be positive, negative, or neutral

Complexity

— The fundamental reason for unintended consequences: social and natural systems have too many interacting variables for any actor to model completely

Reversibility

— A key design principle for managing unintended consequences: the most dangerous interventions are those that cannot be undone

Merton's mechanisms

— Sheer complexity, ignorance, error, imperious immediacy of interest, and self-defeating prophecy all contribute to unanticipated consequences

Important Terms

Robert K. Merton

American sociologist (1910–2003) whose 1936 essay "The Unanticipated Consequences of Purposive Social Action" gave the concept its most rigorous formulation

Imperious immediacy of interest

Merton's term for focusing on short-term goals while ignoring long-term effects — one of the main drivers of unintended consequences

Self-defeating prophecy

A prediction that changes behavior in ways that make the prediction false (the opposite of a self-fulfilling prophecy)

Positive unintended consequences

Beneficial effects that were not part of the original plan (e.g., the internet arising from defense research, penicillin from a contaminated petri dish)
