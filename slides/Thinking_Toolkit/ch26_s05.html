The St. Petersburg Paradox — Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Expected Value

The probability-weighted average of all possible monetary payoffs — the St. Petersburg game has infinite expected value

Expected Utility

What rational decision-makers actually maximize — the subjective value (utility) of outcomes, not their raw dollar amounts

Diminishing Marginal Utility

Each additional dollar is worth less than the previous one; the utility of money increases with wealth but at a decreasing rate

The Core Paradox

A game with infinite expected monetary value is worth only a modest finite amount to any real person — standard expected-value theory fails

Logarithmic Utility

Daniel Bernoulli's proposed solution — utility is proportional to the logarithm of wealth, producing finite expected utility for the St. Petersburg game

Key Concepts (2/2)

Risk Aversion

The preference for a certain outcome over a gamble with the same expected value, explained by diminishing marginal utility

Important Terms (1/2)

Nicolaus Bernoulli

Swiss mathematician (1687–1759) who proposed the St. Petersburg game in 1713 in a letter to Pierre de Montmort

Daniel Bernoulli

Swiss mathematician (1700–1782) who analyzed the paradox and proposed the expected utility resolution in a 1738 paper to the Imperial Academy of Sciences in St. Petersburg, giving the paradox its name

Expected value

The sum of each possible outcome multiplied by its probability; for the St. Petersburg game, this sum diverges to infinity

Expected utility

The sum of the utility of each outcome multiplied by its probability; finite under logarithmic utility even when expected monetary value is infinite

Diminishing marginal utility

The economic principle that each additional unit of wealth produces less additional utility than the previous unit; explains why people are risk-averse

Important Terms (2/2)

Risk aversion

Preference for a certain outcome over a gamble with equal or even higher expected monetary value; rational under expected utility theory

Tail risk

Low-probability, high-magnitude events; St. Petersburg-like payoff structures appear in financial markets and contributed to the 2008 financial crisis

Who proposed and analyzed the St. Petersburg Paradox, and when?

Proposed by Nicolaus Bernoulli in 1713 and analyzed by his cousin Daniel Bernoulli in a 1738 paper.

Describe the game mechanics of the St. Petersburg Paradox.

A fair coin is flipped repeatedly until it lands tails. If tails first appears on flip 'n', you win $2^n$ dollars.

If tails first appears on flip 5 in the St. Petersburg game, how much do you win?

You win $2^5 = $32.

What is the probability of the first tails appearing on flip 'n'?

The probability is (1/2)^n, or 1/2^n.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Why does the St. Petersburg game have an infinite expected monetary value?

What is the typical amount people are willing to pay to play the St. Petersburg game, and how does it compare to the game's expected value?

Most people would pay between $5 and $25, which is a stark contrast to the game's infinite expected monetary value.

According to standard decision theory, how much should one be willing to pay for a game with an infinite expected value?

Standard decision theory suggests one should be willing to pay any finite amount.

What is the fundamental conflict or paradox that the St. Petersburg game highlights?

It highlights the conflict between the mathematically calculated infinite expected value of the game and people's intuitive, finite willingness to pay.

Who proposed the resolution to the St. Petersburg Paradox?

Daniel Bernoulli proposed the resolution in 1738.

What did Daniel Bernoulli propose as the true determinant of a decision-maker's choice, instead of expected value?

He proposed that what matters is 'expected utility' (the satisfaction or usefulness of money), not just the expected monetary value.
