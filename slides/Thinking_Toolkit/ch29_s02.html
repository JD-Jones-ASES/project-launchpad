The Paradigm Shift — Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Normal Science

The everyday work of researchers puzzle-solving within an accepted paradigm — extending and refining the framework, not questioning its foundations

Paradigm

The shared assumptions, methods, exemplary problems, and standards that define a scientific field and determine what counts as legitimate work

Anomalies

Observations the paradigm cannot explain; initially dismissed as errors, but accumulate over time until they trigger a crisis

Scientific Revolution

The wholesale replacement of one paradigm by another — a transformation of foundational assumptions, methods, vocabulary, and even the meanings of key terms

Science Is Not Smoothly Cumulative

Progress alternates between long periods of normal science and disruptive episodes of revolution; it does not simply add facts to facts

Key Concepts (2/2)

Incommensurability

Scientists working in different paradigms are in an important sense working in different conceptual worlds

Important Terms (1/2)

Thomas Kuhn

American historian and philosopher of science (1922–1996); The Structure of Scientific Revolutions (1962) is one of the most cited academic books of the 20th century

Paradigm

In Kuhn's precise usage, the shared framework of assumptions, methods, standards, and exemplary solved problems that defines what counts as science in a given field at a given time

Normal science

Puzzle-solving within an established paradigm; most scientific work at any time is normal science; it extends the paradigm without questioning it

Anomaly

An observation that resists accommodation within the current paradigm; initially set aside as error or "one for later," but accumulates toward a crisis

Crisis

The phase in which anomalies have accumulated enough that the paradigm's inadequacy becomes undeniable; precedes revolution

Important Terms (2/2)

Paradigm shift

A revolution in which one paradigm is replaced by another; changes not just theories but the questions asked, methods used, and meanings of key terms

Incommensurability

Kuhn's claim that paradigms before and after a revolution are not fully translatable — the same words can mean different things in different paradigms

Copernican Revolution

Kuhn's central example; the shift from Ptolemaic geocentrism to Copernican heliocentrism took decades, was genuinely contested, and transformed the meaning of key concepts

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the common, overused meaning of "paradigm shift" today?

It has come to mean little more than "a big change" in popular usage.

How did Thomas Kuhn's original concept of a "paradigm shift" differ from the idea of smooth, cumulative scientific progress?

Kuhn argued science does not progress by smooth, cumulative addition of facts, but alternates between two fundamentally different modes of activity.

What did Kuhn call the first mode of scientific activity?

Normal science.

What is the primary activity of scientists during "normal science"?

Puzzle-solving, where the paradigm provides the rules, and scientists work to extend and refine it.

Provide examples of scientific activities that represent "normal science."

A Newtonian physicist calculating planetary orbits, a Darwinian biologist tracing lines of descent, or a plate tectonics geologist mapping fault lines.
