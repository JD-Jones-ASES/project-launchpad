Chapter 29, Section 4

Feynman's Cargo Cult Science

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Cargo Cult Science

Research that mimics the outward forms of science — hypotheses, data, statistics, peer review — without the essential substance: a ruthless commitment to not fooling yourself.

Feynman's First Principle

You must not fool yourself, and you are the easiest person to fool. Scientific integrity begins with radical self-honesty.

Selective Reporting

Publishing only positive or favorable results while suppressing negative or null findings, distorting the scientific record.

Replication Crisis

The widespread failure, discovered especially from the 2010s onward, of published findings in psychology, medicine, and other fields to hold up under independent replication.

Data-Dredging

Testing large numbers of correlations and reporting only those that happen to reach statistical significance, producing results that appear meaningful but reflect chance.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Publication Incentives

The academic reward structure that prizes novel, positive findings over replication and null results, creating systematic pressure against scientific integrity.

Radical Honesty in Science

Feynman's standard requiring scientists to report all results, describe experiments precisely enough to be reproduced, and actively seek out evidence against their own theories.

Null Finding

A result showing no effect or no significant relationship; frequently suppressed in favor of positive findings, contributing to distorted scientific literature.

Replication

The process of repeating an experiment to verify whether the original results hold under independent testing — a cornerstone of scientific validity.

P-value

Priming Effect

A psychological finding (often cited in the context of replication failures) where prior exposure to one stimulus influences responses to subsequent stimuli.

Important Terms (2/2)

Data-Dredging (p-hacking)

The practice of repeatedly testing different variables or subgroups in a dataset until a statistically significant result appears, then presenting it as a confirmatory finding.

Open Science Collaboration

A 2015 project that attempted to replicate 100 published psychology studies; fewer than half reproduced the original results.
