Bayesian Probability Theory

A mathematical framework for updating the probability of a hypothesis as more evidence or information becomes available. It formally demonstrates why the "prior probability" (base rate) is crucial.

Prior Probability (Base Rate)

The initial probability of an event or hypothesis occurring before any new evidence is considered. For example, the prevalence of a disease in the general population.

Sensitivity

The ability of a test to correctly identify individuals who *do* have the condition (true positive rate).

Specificity

The ability of a test to correctly identify individuals who *do not* have the condition (true negative rate). A low specificity implies a higher false-positive rate.

Key Concepts (2/2)

False-Positive Rate

The probability that a test will incorrectly indicate a positive result for someone who does not have the condition.

The Prior Matters

A core insight from Bayesian reasoning, emphasizing that even a highly accurate test can produce many false positives if the condition it screens for is rare.

Important Terms (1/2)

Base Rate Neglect

A cognitive bias causing people to disregard the statistical frequency of an event (the base rate) when making judgments, instead relying too heavily on specific, individual information.

Bayesian Probability Theory

An 18th-century mathematical framework developed by Thomas Bayes, which provides a rigorous method for calculating how new evidence should update our beliefs about the likelihood of a hypothesis.

Prior Probability

The probability of an event occurring *before* any new evidence is taken into account; it represents our initial belief or the known prevalence. Also known as the base rate.

Sensitivity

The proportion of actual positives that are correctly identified as such (e.g., the percentage of sick people correctly identified by a test).

Specificity

The proportion of actual negatives that are correctly identified as such (e.g., the percentage of healthy people correctly identified by a test).

Important Terms (2/2)

False-Positive Rate

The probability of a test incorrectly indicating the presence of a condition when it is, in fact, absent. Calculated as 1 minus specificity.

Posterior Probability

The updated probability of an event occurring *after* new evidence has been considered. This is what Bayesian reasoning aims to calculate.

Connections to Other Concepts

Availability Heuristic

Memorable or recent events (e.g., a specific disease outbreak) can distort our perception of their frequency, leading us to overestimate their base rate.

Gambler's Fallacy

Another instance where people misunderstand probability, believing past events influence future independent probabilities (e.g., after a series of reds on a roulette wheel, black is "due").

Simpson's Paradox

A phenomenon where a trend appears in several different groups of data but disappears or reverses when these groups are combined, showing how aggregation can mask important underlying patterns.
