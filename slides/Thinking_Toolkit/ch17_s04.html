Key Concepts

Unreliable Communication Channel

A medium through which messages can be lost, delayed, or corrupted, without the sender being guaranteed of delivery or receipt by the intended recipient.

Need for Simultaneous Action

The core of the problem, where two parties must perform an action at the exact same time, and failure to do so results in undesirable consequences for one or both.

Important Terms (1/2)

Two Generals' Problem

A thought experiment in computer science and game theory that illustrates the difficulty of coordinating an action between two parties when their only communication channel is unreliable.

Messenger

In the context of the problem, a metaphor for any unit of communication (e.g., a packet on a network) that can be lost or delayed.

Protocol

A defined set of rules or procedures for how communication and actions should occur between parties.

Simultaneous Attack

The coordinated action required by the two generals, where success depends on both attacking at the same time.

Acknowledgment (ACK)

A message sent to confirm the receipt of a previous message.

Important Terms (2/2)

Confirmation of Acknowledgment

A message sent to confirm the receipt of an acknowledgment, which then itself requires an acknowledgment, leading to the infinite regress.

Distributed Systems

Computer systems where components are located on different networked computers and communicate with each other to achieve a common goal.

TCP Three-Way Handshake

A practical, highly reliable (but not perfectly certain) protocol used in the Internet's Transmission Control Protocol to establish a connection between two devices.

Redundancy

The practice of duplicating critical components or information paths to increase reliability and provide backup in case of failure.

The Communication Dilemma and Infinite Regress

• General A sends: "Attack at dawn."

• General A doesn't know if General B received it.

• General B sends an acknowledgment: "Message received. I will attack at dawn."

• General B doesn't know if General A received that acknowledgment.

• General A sends a confirmation of the acknowledgment.

• General A doesn't know if General B received that confirmation.
