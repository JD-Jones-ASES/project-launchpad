Simpson's Paradox — Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Simpson's Paradox

A trend that appears in every subgroup can reverse (or disappear) when the subgroups are combined — aggregation can produce a picture that is the opposite of the truth

Confounding Variable

An unmeasured variable correlated with both the grouping variable and the outcome; the hidden engine of Simpson's Paradox

Aggregation Distortion

Combining groups with different baseline rates, without controlling for the confounder, generates a misleading overall statistic

Disaggregation as Solution

Always examining subgroups separately before drawing conclusions from combined data is the primary safeguard

Berkeley Case Study

Women applied disproportionately to harder departments; the resulting aggregate appeared to show gender discrimination where none existed at the department level

Important Terms (1/2)

Edward H. Simpson

British statistician who described the paradox formally in a 1951 paper; Karl Pearson (1899) and Udny Yule (1903) noted related phenomena earlier

Simpson's Paradox

The statistical phenomenon in which a relationship present within each subgroup reverses when the subgroups are combined into an aggregate

Confounding variable

A variable that is correlated with both the grouping variable (e.g., sex) and the outcome variable (e.g., admission rate), but is not accounted for in the analysis

Lurking variable

An alternative term for confounding variable — a variable "lurking" in the background that drives the apparent relationship in the aggregated data

UC Berkeley admissions study (1973)

The famous real-world example: overall data suggested women were admitted at lower rates, but departmental analysis showed no discrimination — the confounder was which departments women applied to

Important Terms (2/2)

Randomization

A technique in clinical trials that balances confounders across treatment groups, preventing Simpson's Paradox from distorting results

Stratified analysis

Dividing data into subgroups defined by the confounder and examining each separately, rather than collapsing to an aggregate

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is Simpson's Paradox?

Simpson's Paradox occurs when a trend that appears in every subgroup of data disappears or reverses when the subgroups are combined.

What is the most famous real-world example of Simpson's Paradox?

The most famous example comes from a 1973 study of graduate admissions at the University of California, Berkeley, which was accused of sex discrimination.

What did a department-by-department analysis reveal about UC Berkeley's admissions rates?

In most individual departments, women were admitted at the same rate as men, or even at higher rates, indicating no departmental-level discrimination.

What was the confounding variable that explained Simpson's Paradox in the UC Berkeley admissions case?

The confounding variable was the departments women applied to. Women disproportionately applied to smaller programs with lower admission rates overall.
