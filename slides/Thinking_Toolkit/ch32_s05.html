Key Concepts (1/2)

Mr. Market

Price vs. Value

The central distinction the allegory teaches: price is what Mr. Market offers today; value is what the underlying business is actually worth. The two frequently diverge.

No Obligation to Trade

You are never obligated to accept Mr. Market's offer. You can buy when his price is absurdly low, sell when it is absurdly high, or ignore him entirely and focus on the underlying business.

Independent Judgment

The discipline the allegory cultivates — evaluating underlying reality rather than reacting to market sentiment, public opinion, or social pressure.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Graham's Legacy

Benjamin Graham is widely regarded as the father of value investing; his most famous student, Warren Buffett, has called The Intelligent Investor the best book on investing ever written.

Important Terms (1/2)

Mr. Market

The allegorical figure from Graham's The Intelligent Investor representing the collective mood of financial markets — useful as a source of prices but unreliable as a source of valuations.

Intrinsic Value

The actual worth of a business based on its assets, earnings, cash flows, and prospects — independent of what the market currently offers for shares in it.

Market Sentiment

The collective emotional state of market participants — which can cause prices to deviate dramatically from intrinsic value in either direction.

Value Investing

The investment approach pioneered by Graham, based on identifying businesses whose intrinsic value significantly exceeds their current market price — then buying and holding until the gap closes.

Volatility

The degree of variation in an asset's price over time; Mr. Market's mood swings represent high volatility that is often disconnected from the actual performance of the underlying business.

Important Terms (2/2)

Fundamental Analysis

The evaluation of a business based on its actual economic characteristics — revenues, earnings, assets, competitive position — as distinct from its market price.

Social Proof

The psychological tendency to use others' behavior as evidence of what is correct; in the market context, the temptation to treat rising or falling prices as evidence of rising or falling value.
