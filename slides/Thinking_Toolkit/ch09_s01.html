Important Terms (1/3)

Agrippa the Skeptic

A Greek philosopher from the 1st century AD, to whom the trilemma is attributed.

Sextus Empiricus

A Roman physician and philosopher who reported Agrippa's Trilemma in his writings.

Münchhausen Trilemma

An alternative name for Agrippa's Trilemma, coined by Hans Albert (1968), referencing Baron Münchhausen pulling himself out of a swamp by his own hair.

Epistemology

The branch of philosophy concerned with the theory of knowledge, including its nature, scope, and justification.

Justification

The reasons, evidence, or arguments offered in support of a belief or claim.

Important Terms (2/3)

Infinite Regress

A philosophical problem that arises when a statement or theory requires an endless sequence of prior justifications or causes, none of which can be definitively established.

Circular Reasoning (Petitio Principii)

A logical fallacy in which the premise of an argument assumes the truth of the conclusion, instead of offering independent evidence for it.

Dogmatism

The tendency to lay down principles as undeniably true, without consideration of evidence or the opinions of others; in philosophy, the uncritical acceptance of certain beliefs as self-evident.

Foundationalism

An epistemological theory which holds that certain beliefs are basic or foundational, meaning they are justified without relying on any other beliefs, and thus serve as the ultimate basis for all o...

Coherentism

An epistemological theory that states that a belief or a set of beliefs is justified if and only if it coheres with a larger system of beliefs. Justification is holistic, like a web.

Important Terms (3/3)

Infinitism

An epistemological theory that suggests an infinite regress of justification is not problematic and can, in fact, provide a legitimate form of justification for beliefs.
