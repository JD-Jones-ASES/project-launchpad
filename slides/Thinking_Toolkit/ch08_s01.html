Important Terms (1/2)

Aristotle (384–322 BC)

An influential ancient Greek philosopher and polymath. A student of Plato and tutor to Alexander the Great, his Nicomachean Ethics is a foundational text in moral philosophy, introducing concepts...

Nicomachean Ethics

Aristotle's most comprehensive work on ethics, where he explores the nature of the good life, virtue, happiness, and moral character.

Eudaimonia

A Greek term often translated as "happiness," "flourishing," or "living well." For Aristotle, it's the highest human good, achieved through virtuous activity over a complete life.

Virtue (aretē)

A Greek term meaning "excellence" or "moral virtue." In Aristotle's ethics, virtues are character traits that enable individuals to live a good life.

Golden Mean

The philosophical concept proposed by Aristotle that moral virtue is a middle state between two extremes of vice – one of excess and one of deficiency.

Important Terms (2/2)

Excess

Too much of a particular quality or action; one of the two vices that flank a virtue.

Deficiency

Too little of a particular quality or action; the other vice that flanks a virtue.

Phronesis (Practical Wisdom)

An intellectual virtue that involves the ability to reason correctly about what is good for human beings and how to achieve it in specific situations. It's crucial for discerning the Golden Mean.

Courage

Generosity

Honesty

is positioned between self-deprecation (deficiency in asserting one's worth) and boastfulness (excess in asserting one's worth).

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the central philosophical question posed by the Ship of Theseus puzzle?

The puzzle asks what makes something the 'same' thing across time when all its constituent parts have been replaced.

Who originally posed the Ship of Theseus puzzle?

Plutarch, in his work 'Life of Theseus' (1st century AD).

What argument supports the idea that the Ship of Theseus remains the 'same' ship even after all its planks are replaced?

The argument of continuity, which suggests an unbroken chain of existence where the ship was merely repaired, not destroyed.

How is the concept of continuity commonly applied to human identity, despite bodily changes?

Most people feel like the same person over time, even though all their cells are replaced, appealing to an unbroken chain of personal existence.

What argument supports the idea that the Ship of Theseus becomes a 'different' ship after all its planks are replaced?

The argument of material identity, which holds that the thing is its parts, and when the original parts are gone, the original thing is gone too.

Provide a common-sense example that illustrates the principle of material identity.

If every component of your car is replaced with different parts, it eventually seems illogical to call it 'your' original car.
