Important Terms (1/2)

Aristotle (384–322 BC)

An influential ancient Greek philosopher and polymath. A student of Plato and tutor to Alexander the Great, his *Nicomachean Ethics* is a foundational text in moral philosophy, introducing concepts...

Nicomachean Ethics

Aristotle's most comprehensive work on ethics, where he explores the nature of the good life, virtue, happiness, and moral character.

Eudaimonia

A Greek term often translated as "happiness," "flourishing," or "living well." For Aristotle, it's the highest human good, achieved through virtuous activity over a complete life.

Virtue (aretē)

A Greek term meaning "excellence" or "moral virtue." In Aristotle's ethics, virtues are character traits that enable individuals to live a good life.

Golden Mean

The philosophical concept proposed by Aristotle that moral virtue is a middle state between two extremes of vice – one of excess and one of deficiency.

Important Terms (2/2)

Excess

Too much of a particular quality or action; one of the two vices that flank a virtue.

Deficiency

Too little of a particular quality or action; the other vice that flanks a virtue.

Phronesis (Practical Wisdom)

An intellectual virtue that involves the ability to reason correctly about what is good for human beings and how to achieve it in specific situations. It's crucial for discerning the Golden Mean.

The Golden Mean: Virtue as Balance

Courage

, for example, is the virtue found between **cowardice** (a deficiency of boldness) and **recklessness** (an excess of boldness).

Generosity

lies between **stinginess** (deficiency of giving) and **extravagance** (excess of giving).

Honesty

is positioned between **self-deprecation** (deficiency in asserting one's worth) and **boastfulness** (excess in asserting one's worth).
