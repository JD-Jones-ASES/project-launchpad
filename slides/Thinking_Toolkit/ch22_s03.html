Chapter 22, Section 3

Second-Order Effects

Study Slides

Key Concepts

First-order effects

— The direct, immediate, intended results of an action; the visible, surface-level consequences

Second-order effects

— The indirect, delayed, often unintended results that emerge when the system (and its actors) respond to the first-order effects

Second-order thinking

— The discipline of asking not just "what happens if I do this?" but "what happens after everyone else responds to what I did?"

The seen and unseen

— Bastiat's formulation: good economic reasoning accounts for both the visible immediate effects (seen) and the invisible indirect effects (unseen)

Important Terms

Frederic Bastiat

French economist (1801–1850) whose 1850 essay contrasted the bad economist (considers only visible effects) with the good economist (considers invisible second-order effects)

System response

The way a complex system — including other actors with their own incentives — adapts to a change, often producing effects that offset or reverse the first-order intent

Price effect

In economics, when a price changes, behavior changes in response — second-order effects cascade through the system

Confounding variable

An unmeasured factor that drives both a cause and an effect, distorting simple before-and-after comparisons
