Key Concepts (2/2)

Socrates' Disordered Soul Argument

Socrates' counter-argument that even if an unjust person gains wealth and power, their soul is in chaos, ruled by appetite rather than reason, making them truly unfree and unhappy.

Human Nature

The Ring of Gyges directly probes what we believe about fundamental human morality – whether humans are inherently self-interested or possess an innate capacity for intrinsic goodness.

Important Terms (1/2)

Plato (c. 428–348 BCE)

Ancient Greek philosopher, student of Socrates, and teacher of Aristotle. Author of The Republic.

Republic

Plato's seminal work of political philosophy, which explores justice, the ideal state, and the nature of reality.

Glaucon

Plato's brother, who acts as an interlocutor in The Republic and presents the challenge of the Ring of Gyges to Socrates.

Socrates (c. 470–399 BCE)

Greek philosopher, teacher of Plato, and a foundational figure in Western philosophy. He developed the Socratic method of inquiry.

Gyges

The shepherd in Glaucon's story who finds an invisibility ring, uses it for personal gain (seducing the queen, killing the king, seizing the throne).

Important Terms (2/2)

Impunity

Exemption from punishment or freedom from the injurious consequences of an action.

Social Contract

A theory in political philosophy that individuals implicitly agree to give up some freedoms and abide by certain rules in exchange for the benefits of an organized society (e.g., security, order).

Disordered Soul

Socrates' concept for the state of an unjust person, where reason is not in control, and the individual is enslaved by desires and appetites, leading to internal unhappiness and lack of true freedom.

Instrumental vs. Intrinsic Justice

Instrumental Justice

Behaving well because of external rewards (good reputation, praise) or punishments (fines, imprisonment, social ostracization). This view sees justice as a tool to achieve other desired outcomes.

Intrinsic Justice

Behaving well because justice is inherently good, necessary for a flourishing life, and creates a harmonious soul. This view sees justice as an end in itself.

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to Aristotle, what is 'virtue' (aretē)?

Virtue (aretē) is a disposition to act at the mean between two extremes — one of excess and one of deficiency.

What deceptively simple question does Aristotle open the Nicomachean Ethics with?

Aristotle opens the Nicomachean Ethics with the question: 'what is the good life?'

What is Aristotle's 'Golden Mean'?

The Golden Mean is the idea that every virtue is a mean — a sweet spot — between two corresponding vices, one of excess and one of deficiency.

How does Aristotle illustrate the Golden Mean using the virtue of courage?

Courage is the mean between the vice of deficiency (cowardice) and the vice of excess (recklessness).

Provide an example of a virtue and its corresponding vices of excess and deficiency, according to the Golden Mean.

Generosity is the mean between stinginess (deficiency) and extravagance (excess).
