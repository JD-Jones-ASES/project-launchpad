Peter Singer (b. 1946)

Drowning Child Argument

A thought experiment proposed by Peter Singer to illustrate the moral imperative to prevent suffering, even when the victims are distant, provided the cost to the helper is not morally significant.

Identifiable Victim Effect

A psychological phenomenon where people respond more strongly and offer more help to specific, identifiable individuals in need than to anonymous, statistical victims.

Diffusion of Responsibility

A socio-psychological phenomenon where individuals feel less personal responsibility for taking action when others are present, assuming someone else will step in.

Impartiality

The moral principle of treating all individuals and their interests equally, without bias or preference for certain people or groups.

Important Terms (2/2)

Partiality

The moral stance that allows or requires giving special consideration or priority to the interests of certain individuals (e.g., family, friends, fellow citizens) over others.

The Drowning Child Scenario and Its Core Implication

Initial Thought Experiment

You encounter a child drowning in a shallow pond. You can easily save them, but it would ruin your expensive shoes and make you late for a meeting.

Obvious Moral Answer

The overwhelming consensus is that you *must* save the child. The child's life vastly outweighs the cost of shoes or being late. To do otherwise would be seen as morally monstrous.

Peter Singer's Core Argument

The Principle

Singer's argument, paraphrased, states: "If it is in our power to prevent something very bad from happening, without thereby sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance, we ought to do it."

Moral Calculus

The argument asserts that the moral weight of a child's life remains the same whether they are at your feet or in a distant country. Distance does not alter the moral obligation.
