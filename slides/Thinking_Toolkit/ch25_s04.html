The Unexpected Hanging Paradox — Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

The Unexpected Hanging Paradox

A prisoner reasons by backward elimination that a judge's "surprise" execution sentence is self-contradictory — yet is genuinely surprised when the execution occurs

Backward Elimination

The prisoner's step-by-step reasoning that eliminates each day of the week as a possible execution day, concluding the hanging cannot occur

Self-Undermining Argument

The conclusion (no hanging can happen) restores surprise and thereby defeats itself — the argument saws off the branch it sits on

Self-Reference in Epistemology

The judge's sentence refers to the prisoner's own future state of knowledge, which depends on the sentence itself, creating a feedback loop

Knowledge Problem

The prisoner's argument depends on knowing the hanging will occur, but the elimination destroys that knowledge, making the argument self-undermining

Key Concepts (2/2)

Logic vs. Experience

The paradox illustrates that formally valid-looking chains of reasoning can lead to conclusions that reality ignores

Important Terms (1/2)

Unexpected Hanging Paradox

The puzzle in which a prisoner's apparently valid backward reasoning proves no surprise execution can occur — yet the prisoner is surprised when the executioner arrives

Surprise Examination Paradox

The classroom variant in which a professor announces a surprise quiz one day next week; structurally identical to the unexpected hanging

Backward induction

The logical technique of reasoning from the last possible case backward to earlier ones, used (apparently validly) by the prisoner

Epistemology

The branch of philosophy concerned with knowledge, belief, and justification; the paradox is fundamentally epistemological because the judge's sentence is about what the prisoner can know

Martin Gardner

American mathematician and puzzle writer who popularized the Unexpected Hanging Paradox in his Scientific American column

Important Terms (2/2)

Feedback loop

A situation in which a statement or rule refers to the state it generates, making stable resolution impossible; the judge's sentence creates a feedback loop with the prisoner's knowledge
