Important Terms (1/2)

Price Signal

The economic information conveyed by a change in the price of a good or service, indicating shifts in its relative scarcity or desirability.

Classical Economics

An economic school of thought, prominent in the 18th and 19th centuries (e.g., Adam Smith), that laid foundations for understanding markets, though the explicit idea of prices as information system...

Hayek's "The Use of Knowledge in Society" (1945)

An influential essay by economist F.A. Hayek that explicitly detailed how market prices function as a powerful mechanism for communicating and aggregating dispersed knowledge.

Dispersed Knowledge

The vast amount of local, specific, and often tacit information held by individuals throughout an economy, which is difficult or impossible for any central authority to collect and process.

Scarcity

A fundamental economic problem of having seemingly unlimited human wants and needs in a world of limited resources. Prices rise as goods become scarcer.

Important Terms (2/2)

Substitutes

Goods or services that can be used in place of another. The availability of substitutes influences how consumers and producers react to price changes.

Commodity Exchange

A market where commodities (raw materials like tin, oil, wheat) are bought and sold, and their prices are determined by supply and demand.

Information Engineering

A term used by Hayek to describe the unplanned, emergent efficiency of the price system in gathering and transmitting information.

Coordination

The harmonious organization of different elements of a complex system. In economics, prices facilitate coordination among diverse actors.

The Price Signal as Information: A Core Economic Insight

Historical Context

The idea that prices carry information was implicitly present in the work of classical economists like Adam Smith (1776). However, it was explicitly articulated and highlighted by F.A. Hayek in his...

Centrality in Economics

This concept is considered one of the most significant insights of 20th-century economics, shifting the understanding of prices from mere accounting tools to dynamic information systems.

What Information Do Prices Encode?

Beyond Cost and Revenue

A price is more than just a cost for the buyer and revenue for the seller.

Compressed Summary

It serves as a highly compressed summary of all the collective knowledge within the market about the relative scarcity and desirability of a good.

Key Information Components

The Aggregation of Dispersed Knowledge

Unique Capability

The price system achieves something that no central committee, advanced computer, or sophisticated algorithm has been able to replicate.

Integrating Local Knowledge

It effectively aggregates the vast, dispersed, and often implicit knowledge of millions of individuals, each possessing unique information about their local conditions, preferences, and capabilities.

Actionable Summary

This dispersed knowledge is then distilled and transmitted in a single, easily understandable number (the price) that anyone can act upon.

"Information Engineering" by No One

Hayek recognized this system as a "marvel of information engineering" precisely because it evolved organically without any deliberate design or central planning.
