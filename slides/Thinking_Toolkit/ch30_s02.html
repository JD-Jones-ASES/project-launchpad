Occam's Razor — Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Occam's Razor

The principle of parsimony — when multiple explanations account for the same evidence, prefer the one that requires the fewest assumptions.

Parsimony

The quality of an explanation that introduces no more entities, causes, or assumptions than are necessary to account for the observed evidence.

William of Ockham

The 14th-century Franciscan friar and logician whose relentless application of parsimony throughout his philosophical work permanently associated the principle with his name.

Razor as Heuristic

Occam's Razor is a guide for choosing among explanations, not a guarantee of truth; nature is not obligated to be simple, and complexity should be added when evidence demands it.

Scientific Application

Major scientific advances — Copernicus, Newton, Darwin — have often involved replacing more complex explanatory systems with simpler, more unified ones.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Limits of Simplicity

Quantum mechanics and climate science demonstrate that the most accurate explanations are sometimes highly complex; simplicity is a starting point, not a destination.

Important Terms (1/2)

Principle of Parsimony

The methodological guideline to prefer explanations that invoke the minimum necessary number of assumptions, entities, or causal mechanisms.
