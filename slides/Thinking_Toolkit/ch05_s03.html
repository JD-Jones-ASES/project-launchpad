Important Terms (1/2)

Prospect Theory

A psychological theory explaining how individuals make decisions when faced with uncertain outcomes, particularly focusing on their perceptions of gains and losses relative to a reference point.

Loss Aversion

The disproportionate psychological impact of losses compared to equivalent gains, where losses are felt more acutely.

Reference Point

The specific benchmark or starting level from which an individual evaluates whether an outcome constitutes a gain or a loss.

Risk-averse

Describing a preference for sure outcomes over uncertain ones, especially when facing potential gains.

Risk-seeking

Describing a preference for uncertain outcomes (gambles) over sure outcomes, especially when facing potential losses.

Important Terms (2/2)

Disposition Effect

An investor bias characterized by the premature selling of assets that have increased in value and the prolonged holding of assets that have decreased in value.

Framing

The way in which a choice or situation is presented to an individual, which can significantly affect their perception and decision.

Endowment Effect

The phenomenon where individuals demand a higher price to sell an object they own than they would be willing to pay to acquire the same object.

Introduction to Loss Aversion and Prospect Theory

Origins

Introduced by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky in their seminal 1979 paper, "Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk."

Challenge to Classical Economics

Prospect Theory directly contradicted the classical economic assumption that people evaluate outcomes in absolute terms, focusing solely on total wealth.

Core Idea

Individuals evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point, not in absolute terms. The psychological impact of moving away from this reference point is asymmetric.

The Asymmetry of Loss Aversion

Pain vs. Pleasure

Prospect Theory highlights that losses "hurt more" than equivalent gains "feel good."

Kahneman and Tversky's Finding

Research indicates that the pain of losing a given amount is roughly twice as intense as the pleasure of gaining the same amount. For example, losing $100 feels as bad as finding $200 feels good.

Fundamental Feature

This asymmetry, known as loss aversion, is not an anomaly but a deep, widespread feature of human psychology observed across diverse populations and even in other primates.
