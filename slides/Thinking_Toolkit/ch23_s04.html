Pre-Mortem Analysis

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Pre-mortem

— A planning technique that asks a team to imagine a project has already failed and to explain the reasons for failure, conducted before execution begins

Post-mortem

— The traditional analysis conducted after failure; the pre-mortem asks the same questions beforehand

Optimism bias

— The tendency to focus on reasons a plan will succeed and downplay reasons it might fail; one of the primary biases the pre-mortem is designed to counteract

Reframing dissent

— The pre-mortem's mechanism: instead of asking people to criticize the plan (socially risky), it asks them to explain a failure that has already occurred (a creative exercise)

Important Terms

Gary Klein

American psychologist specializing in naturalistic decision-making; developed the pre-mortem as a practical planning tool in 2007

Hindsight bias

Our tendency to view past events as having been predictable; the pre-mortem harnesses this cognitive tendency (our skill at constructing plausible narratives for known outcomes) and redirects it to...

Groupthink

The suppression of dissent in group settings; the pre-mortem is specifically designed to overcome groupthink by changing the social framing

Independent generation

A critical step in the pre-mortem procedure — each team member writes down failure reasons independently before discussion, to prevent groupthink from shaping the results

Red teaming

The military equivalent — assigning a group to argue against a plan and identify vulnerabilities; used before the 2011 operation that found Osama bin Laden

What is the first step in the pre-mortem procedure?

Gather the project team after the plan has been developed but before execution begins.

Why do team members write down reasons for failure independently during a pre-mortem?

Independent thinking prevents groupthink and allows for a broader range of uninfluenced ideas.
