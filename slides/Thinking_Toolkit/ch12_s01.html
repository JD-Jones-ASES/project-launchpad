Important Terms (1/2)

Non-Identity Problem

A philosophical challenge concerning moral obligations to future people, where the identity of those people is contingent on the very decisions being evaluated.

Derek Parfit

A prominent British philosopher, author of Reasons and Persons (1984), who articulated the Non-Identity Problem and considered it one of the most important and difficult challenges in ethics.

Future Generations

The people who will live in the future, often centuries from now, whose well-being and existence are affected by current decisions.

The Core Scenario

Policy A (Clean & Expensive)

Preserves the environment, leading to a high quality of life for future generations.

Policy B (Cheap & Dirty)

Causes significant environmental degradation, lowering the quality of life for people born in the next century.

The Problem: No Identifiable Victim

• The people born under Policy A would be different people from those born under Policy B.

• People living in the degraded world of Policy B cannot complain that they have been harmed by Policy B. Their only alternative was not a better life, but non-existence.

• As long as their lives under Policy B are at least "marginally worth living" (i.e., not so terrible that non-existence would have been preferable), it's hard to claim they were made "worse off" by ...

The Structure of the Puzzle

Environmental Policies

Influence economic conditions, migration, social interactions, and thus who meets and has children with whom.

Medical Advances

Enable individuals to survive and reproduce who otherwise wouldn't, altering the genetic makeup of future populations.

Personal Choices

Even decisions like when to have children or where to live can affect the specific individual conceived.

Why It Is So Hard to Solve: Proposed Approaches and Their Challenges

Impersonal Value

This approach suggests that what matters is whether the overall state of the world is better or worse, regardless of the identity of its inhabitants. Policy A produces a better world than Policy B.

Rights-Based Approaches

This posits that future people have a right to a certain quality of life, irrespective of whether they would have existed under alternative policies.

Contractualism (T. M. Scanlon)

This framework suggests an action is wrong if it violates principles that no one could reasonably reject. One might argue that no one could reasonably reject a principle requiring a habitable planet.

Key Concepts (1/2)

Who authored 'A Theory of Justice,' a foundational work in 20th-century political philosophy?

John Rawls.

What is the central question explored by John Rawls's most famous thought experiment?

How would you design a just society if you did not know who you would be in it?

Define Rawls's 'Veil of Ignorance.'

A hypothetical condition in which rational people designing a society do not know their own race, sex, intelligence, wealth, religion, or social position.

What is the primary purpose of the Veil of Ignorance in Rawls's theory?

To ensure impartiality by preventing individuals from rigging societal rules in their own favor.

According to Rawls, what are the two principles of justice that rational people behind the Veil of Ignorance would converge upon?

1) Equal basic liberties for all, and 2) Social and economic inequalities must benefit the least advantaged (the difference principle).

Explain Rawls's First Principle of Justice.

Each person should have equal basic liberties, such as freedom of speech, religion, assembly, and the right to vote.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the 'difference principle' in Rawls's theory of justice?

The principle that social and economic inequalities are acceptable only if they make the worst-off group better off than they would be under strict equality.

What core human behavior does Rawls's reasoning for the difference principle rely on?

Risk aversion; people would want to insure against the worst possible outcome.
