The Straw Man, Red Herring, and False Dilemma — Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Straw Man

A fallacy in which a distorted or exaggerated version of an opponent's argument is attacked instead of the actual argument, creating the illusion of engagement while avoiding it.

Red Herring

A fallacy in which an irrelevant topic is introduced to divert attention away from the original issue, exploiting the audience's interest in the new topic to leave the original question unanswered.

False Dilemma (False Dichotomy)

A fallacy that presents only two options when more exist, eliminating middle ground and forcing the audience into a binary choice that does not reflect the full range of possibilities.

Misdirection as Distortion

All three fallacies distort the terms of a debate so subtly that participants and audiences may not notice in real time — making them among the most practically important fallacies to learn.

Common Defense

All three fallacies share the same basic remedy: slow down, restate the original claim or question, and check whether the response actually addresses it.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Emotional Amplification

The false dilemma is especially effective in emotional contexts where the simplicity of binary thinking feels reassuring and the complexity of multiple options feels overwhelming.

Important Terms (1/2)

Straw Man

The rhetorical practice of misrepresenting an opponent's position — making it weaker, more extreme, or more easily attacked — and then refuting the misrepresentation rather than the actual claim.

Red Herring

The introduction of an irrelevant consideration to distract from the actual issue under discussion; named for the practice of dragging a smoked fish across a trail to throw hunting dogs off the scent.

False Dilemma (False Dichotomy)

A presentation of a situation as if only two mutually exclusive options exist, when in fact additional options are available.

Steel-Manning

The opposite of straw-manning — representing an opponent's argument in its strongest possible form before critiquing it; the recommended intellectual practice.

Ad Hominem

A related fallacy in which the character or motives of the person making an argument are attacked rather than the argument itself.

Important Terms (2/2)

Binary Thinking

The cognitive tendency to categorize situations as either/or rather than recognizing spectrums, gradations, and multiple alternatives — the condition that makes false dilemmas effective.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What are the three "workhorses of bad argument" discussed in the text?

The Straw Man, Red Herring, and False Dilemma.

Why are the Straw Man, Red Herring, and False Dilemma considered "workhorses of bad argument"?

They are easy to deploy, hard to spot in real time, and effective on inattentive audiences.

How do the Straw Man, Red Herring, and False Dilemma primarily corrupt the terms of a debate?

They do so through misdirection, subtly distorting the debate so that the audience (and sometimes participants) may not notice.

Define the Straw Man fallacy.

A straw man is a distorted, weakened version of an opponent's argument, set up to be easy to knock down instead of addressing what was actually said.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the recommended remedy or defense against a Straw Man argument?

Before criticizing a position, restate it in a way your opponent would accept. If they say, "That is not what I claimed," you've built a straw man.

Define the Red Herring fallacy.

A red herring introduces an irrelevant topic to divert attention from the original issue.

What is the origin of the term "red herring"?

It comes from the practice of dragging a smoked herring across a trail to throw hounds off the scent.

Provide an example of a Red Herring fallacy based on the question: "How do you plan to reduce the deficit?"

A red herring response would be: "What we really need to focus on is supporting our troops." (Diverting from the deficit to troop support).

Why are Red Herrings often effective in diverting attention?

The diversion is often to a topic the audience cares about, making it harder to notice the subject has changed and to steer the conversation back.

What is the defense against a Red Herring fallacy?

Explicitly state, "That is an important topic, but it is not the question I asked. Can we return to the original question?"
