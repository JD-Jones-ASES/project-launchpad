Underdetermination — Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Underdetermination

For any body of evidence, multiple theories can fit the data equally well — evidence alone never fully determines which theory is correct

Duhem's Insight

No hypothesis is ever tested in isolation; every experiment relies on auxiliary background assumptions, and an anomalous result may indicate a failed auxiliary rather than a failed main hypothesis

Quine's Extension

Our entire web of beliefs faces experience as a whole; any belief can in principle be maintained in the face of contrary evidence by adjusting other beliefs in the network

Duhem-Quine Thesis

The combined claim that evidence underdetermines theory and that hypotheses can always be protected by modifying auxiliary assumptions

Theoretical Virtues

The non-evidential criteria scientists use to choose among empirically equivalent theories: simplicity, elegance, unifying power, fruitfulness

Key Concepts (2/2)

Science Is Not Arbitrary

Underdetermination does not make theory choice random — theoretical virtues have an excellent practical track record

Important Terms (1/2)

Underdetermination

The thesis that for any body of evidence, more than one theory is compatible with all of it; empirical evidence alone cannot uniquely identify the correct theory

Pierre Duhem

French physicist and philosopher (1861–1916); argued in 1906 that hypotheses cannot be tested in isolation but only as part of a larger theoretical system

W. V. O. Quine

American philosopher (1908–2000); extended Duhem's insight in "Two Dogmas of Empiricism" (1951) to argue that any belief can be retained given sufficient adjustments elsewhere in one's web of beliefs

Duhem-Quine thesis

The joint thesis combining Duhem's holism about testing with Quine's holism about belief revision

Auxiliary hypothesis

A background assumption supporting an experimental test — about instrument calibration, absence of confounders, the validity of a measurement technique; anomalous results may indicate a failed auxi...

Important Terms (2/2)

Theoretical virtue

A property of theories valued in theory selection beyond mere empirical fit: simplicity, elegance, unifying power, predictive fruitfulness, internal consistency

Ptolemaic model

The geocentric model with epicycles; accurately predicted planetary positions and could not be definitively refuted by early modern astronomical data alone

Copernican model

The heliocentric model; initially less accurate than Ptolemy's but ultimately preferred for its greater simplicity, elegance, and unifying power
