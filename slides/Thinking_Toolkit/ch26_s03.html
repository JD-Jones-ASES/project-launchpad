The Banach-Tarski Paradox — Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Banach-Tarski Paradox

A proven mathematical theorem that a solid sphere can be decomposed into a finite number of pieces and reassembled into two spheres each identical in size to the original

Non-Measurable Sets

The "pieces" in the decomposition — mathematical objects so wildly scattered that no consistent notion of volume applies to them; not physically constructible

Axiom of Choice

The set-theoretic axiom whose non-constructive power guarantees the existence of non-measurable sets, making the theorem possible

Volume Intuition Failure

The paradox reveals that our assumption "the volume of pieces sums to the volume of the whole" fails for non-measurable sets

Mathematical vs. Physical Reality

The theorem describes a feature of abstract mathematical space, not a recipe for duplicating physical objects (which are made of atoms)

Key Concepts (2/2)

Rigorous Theorem

This is not a trick or approximation — it is proven with the same certainty as the Pythagorean theorem

Important Terms (1/2)

Stefan Banach

Polish mathematician (1892–1945), one of the most important analysts of the 20th century; proved the theorem with Tarski in 1924

Alfred Tarski

Polish logician and mathematician (1901–1983); co-proved the Banach-Tarski theorem and also developed Tarski's hierarchy of languages for the Liar Paradox

Non-measurable set

A set of points so wildly scattered and interleaved that no consistent notion of volume can be assigned to it; exists only in abstract mathematical space, not in physical reality

Axiom of Choice

States that given any collection of non-empty sets, exactly one element can be selected from each, even with no explicit selection rule; enables non-constructive existence proofs

Measurable set

A set to which a well-defined, consistent volume (or area, or length) can be assigned; all ordinary geometric figures are measurable

Important Terms (2/2)

Decomposition

The act of dividing a mathematical object into pieces; in Banach-Tarski, five non-measurable pieces suffice

Rotation and translation

The only operations used to reassemble the Banach-Tarski pieces — no stretching, squishing, or filling of gaps
