Key Concepts (1/2)

What central challenge did Zeno's paradoxes of motion pose?

They questioned how motion could be possible if it involved completing an infinite number of tasks (e.g., crossing infinite sub-distances).

Name two of Zeno's most famous paradoxes of motion.

Achilles and the Tortoise, and the Dichotomy Paradox (having to cross half a distance, then half the remainder, infinitely).

Why were the ancient Greeks unable to resolve Zeno's paradoxes?

They lacked the mathematical machinery, specifically calculus, which was developed in the 17th century.

What "profound idea" from calculus resolves Zeno's paradoxes?

The idea that an infinite sum can have a finite total (i.e., an infinite series can converge).

How can Zeno's dichotomy paradox (crossing a room of length 1) be represented as an infinite mathematical series?

$1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 + 1/16 + \dots$

What is the exact sum of the infinite series $1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 + \dots$?

Exactly 1.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Define the key properties (first term 'a' and common ratio 'r') for the geometric series $1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 + \dots$.

The first term $a = 1/2$, and the common ratio $r = 1/2$.

What is a "partial sum" ($S_n$) in the context of an infinite series?

The sum of the first 'n' terms of the series. For Zeno's series, $S_n$ gets closer to 1 as 'n' increases.

What is the general formula for the partial sum $S_n$ of a geometric series?

$S_n = a \frac{1 - r^n}{1 - r}$

How does the formula for $S_n$ demonstrate that the series $1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 + \dots$ converges to 1?

As $n$ approaches infinity, $r^n$ (which is $(1/2)^n$) approaches 0. So $S_n$ approaches $a \frac{1 - 0}{1 - r} = \frac{1/2}{1 - 1/2} = \frac{1/2}{1/2} = 1$.

What was Zeno's critical, incorrect assumption that led to his paradoxes?

He assumed that completing an infinite number of tasks must necessarily take an infinite amount of time.
