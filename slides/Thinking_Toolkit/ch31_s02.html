The Pre-Mortem (Decision Tool) — Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Pre-Mortem

A structured technique, developed by Gary Klein, in which a team imagines that a project has already failed completely — before it begins — and then generates explanations for why it failed.

Prospective Hindsight

The cognitive mechanism that makes pre-mortems effective; imagining that failure has already happened makes potential failure modes feel concrete and specific rather than abstract and hypothetical.

Groupthink Counteraction

In team settings, dissent is socially costly. The pre-mortem makes raising concerns the expected behavior — reframing the pessimist as the realist who is helping stress-test the plan.

30% Improvement

Klein's research found that pre-mortems increase the ability to identify reasons for failure by roughly 30% compared to simply asking "what could go wrong?"

Key Concepts (2/2)

Personal Application

The technique works equally well for individual decisions — imagining regret after a job acceptance or a major purchase surfaces concerns that optimism and excitement would otherwise suppress.

Important Terms (1/2)

Pre-Mortem

A prospective failure analysis conducted before a project begins, in which participants assume the project has failed and generate explanations for why.

Post-Mortem

A retrospective analysis of what went wrong after a project has already failed; the pre-mortem performs the same function before execution, when corrections are still possible.

Optimism Bias

The systematic tendency to overestimate the probability of positive outcomes and underestimate the probability of negative ones — especially pronounced once a group has committed to a plan.

Groupthink

The tendency of cohesive groups to suppress dissent and converge prematurely on consensus, reducing the quality of decision-making by excluding critical perspectives.

Prospective Hindsight

The technique of imagining that a future event has already occurred in order to reason more concretely about how it came to pass — the cognitive mechanism underlying pre-mortem effectiveness.

Important Terms (2/2)

Failure Mode

A specific way in which a system, plan, or decision can go wrong; pre-mortems are designed to surface failure modes before they materialize.

Mitigating Action

A step taken in response to an identified failure mode to prevent it, reduce its likelihood, or limit its impact if it occurs.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What distinction does Jeff Bezos make regarding decisions?

Bezos distinguishes between 'Type 1' (irreversible) and 'Type 2' (reversible) decisions.

Define a 'one-way door' decision.

A one-way door is an irreversible decision. Once you walk through it, you cannot walk back.

Define a 'two-way door' decision.

A two-way door is a reversible decision. If it does not work out, you can walk back through and try something else.

Provide examples of a 'two-way door' decision.

What is the key implication of distinguishing between one-way and two-way doors?

The distinction profoundly impacts how you should decide, dictating the appropriate level of analysis and deliberation.

Key Concepts (2/2)

According to Bezos, what common mistake do most organizations make regarding decisions?

Most organizations treat 'Type 2' (reversible) decisions with 'Type 1' (irreversible) caution, even though most decisions are two-way doors.

What is the negative consequence of treating most decisions as if they are one-way doors?

The result is organizational paralysis, leading to missed opportunities due to excessive committees, studies, and layered approval processes.

What psychological biases contribute to treating two-way doors as one-way doors?

Loss aversion and status quo bias are key psychological factors. Loss aversion overweights the downside, and status quo bias makes the current state feel safer.

How does organizational incentive structure contribute to inaction on two-way doors?

What is the practical framework suggested for approaching any decision?

Before investing significant time, ask: Is this a one-way door or a two-way door? Then calibrate deliberation accordingly.

How should one approach a 'one-way door' decision?

One-way doors deserve careful analysis, deliberation, and consultation, because the consequences are long-lasting.
