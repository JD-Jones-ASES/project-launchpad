Two-Way vs. One-Way Doors — Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

One-Way Door

An irreversible decision — once made, it cannot be undone or easily reversed. These decisions deserve careful analysis, deliberation, and consultation.

Two-Way Door

A reversible decision — if it does not work out, you can walk back through and choose differently. These decisions deserve speed rather than extensive deliberation.

Reversibility as a Decision Variable

The most important first step in any decision is not to evaluate options but to classify the decision itself: is it reversible or irreversible?

Organizational Paralysis

Most organizations treat two-way doors as one-way doors, creating approval layers and committees for decisions that could be made quickly — at the cost of speed and opportunity.

Psychological Barriers to Speed

Loss aversion, status quo bias, and the organizational incentive to never be blamed for a wrong decision all push toward treating every decision as a one-way door.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Bezos's Framework

Jeff Bezos articulated this distinction in Amazon shareholder letters, calling them "Type 1" (irreversible) and "Type 2" (reversible) decisions.

Important Terms (1/2)

Irreversible Decision (Type 1 / One-Way Door)

A decision whose consequences cannot reasonably be undone — selling a business, having a child, publishing a sensitive document. These warrant thorough deliberation.

Reversible Decision (Type 2 / Two-Way Door)

A decision that can be rescinded or changed at manageable cost if it proves wrong — launching a product feature, trying a new hobby, choosing a first major. These warrant speed.

Loss Aversion

The cognitive tendency to weight potential losses more heavily than equivalent potential gains; causes people to over-invest in deliberating reversible decisions.

Status Quo Bias

The preference for the current state of affairs over any alternative, even when the alternative is objectively preferable; reinforces avoidance of two-way doors.

Important Terms (2/2)

Inaction Cost

The cost of failing to act, often invisible in decision analysis; in organizations, nobody is blamed for a decision never made, creating systematic incentives for inaction.
