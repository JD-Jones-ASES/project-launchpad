Market Economy

An economic system where decisions about production, investment, and distribution are guided by the forces of supply and demand, and prices are determined by voluntary exchange between private owners.

Private Ownership of the Means of Production

The legal right of individuals or non-governmental entities to own and control productive assets (e.g., factories, land, tools). This is seen as essential for the formation of genuine market prices.

Important Terms (2/2)

Socialist Commonwealth

A hypothetical or actual economic system where the means of production are owned and controlled by the community as a whole, often through the state, rather than by private individuals.

The Genesis of the Calculation Problem

Mises's Core Argument

In 1920, Ludwig von Mises published "Economic Calculation in the Socialist Commonwealth," arguing that rational economic planning in a socialist system is logically impossible without market prices.

Common Unit of Comparison

Market Prices' Role

The Link Between Private Ownership, Exchange, and Prices

Origin of Prices

Genuine market prices emerge from the process of voluntary exchange between private owners of goods and services, including the means of production.

Absence in Central Planning

In a system without private ownership of the means of production (e.g., a socialist economy), there is no genuine exchange of these productive assets.

Consequence

Without exchange, there are no market prices for these fundamental resources.

The Missing Information

The central planner is not just faced with a difficult calculation; they are missing the crucial information—market prices—that would make the calculation possible.

Mises's Fundamental Insight: Knowledge and Signals

Real-World Illustration: The Soviet Union

Case Study

The economic history of the Soviet Union provides a stark example of the calculation problem in action.

Planners' Goals

Central planners in Moscow set production targets for countless goods.

Lack of Value Measurement

Without genuine market prices, they could not objectively determine if the resources used to produce one good (e.g., tractors) might have yielded more value if used for something else.
