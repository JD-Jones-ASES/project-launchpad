Key Concepts (1/2)

Slippery Slope Argument

A claim that a seemingly small initial action or decision will inevitably lead to a chain of increasingly dramatic, and usually undesirable, consequences.

Valid Slippery Slope Argument

Occurs when a specific, plausible causal mechanism connects each step in the chain of consequences. This mechanism explains how one step makes the next one more likely.

Causal Mechanism

The specific process, reason, or force that explains how one event or state leads to another. It is the crucial differentiator between a valid and fallacious slippery slope.

Important Terms (1/2)

Slippery Slope

An argument asserting that a minor action will lead to a significant and often undesirable outcome through a series of intermediate steps.

Valid Argument Form

A pattern of reasoning where if the premises are true, the conclusion must logically follow.

Logical Fallacy

An error in reasoning or a flaw in the structure of an argument that renders it invalid or unsound, even if its premises are factually correct.

Causal Mechanism

A concrete, identifiable process or principle that explains the cause-and-effect relationship between sequential events.

Regulatory Precedent

A government or legal action that establishes a rule or principle that can then be used as a basis for similar actions or decisions in the future, often expanding in scope.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is a slippery slope argument?

A claim that a small first step will inevitably lead, through a chain of consequences, to a dramatic and usually undesirable endpoint.

What is the key factor that determines if a slippery slope is a valid argument or a logical fallacy?

The difference is whether a specific causal mechanism is specified that links each step in the chain of consequences.

What makes a slippery slope argument a valid form of reasoning?

It is valid when a specific, plausible causal mechanism is identified that links each step to the next, explaining how each step makes the subsequent one mor...

When does a slippery slope argument become a logical fallacy?

It becomes a fallacy when the chain of consequences is asserted without any specific causal mechanism explaining how one step leads to the next.

What is the crucial question to ask when evaluating any slippery slope argument?

Is the slope actually slippery? (Meaning, is there a genuine, explained causal mechanism linking the steps?)

What is a key indicator that a slope is genuinely slippery?

The presence of a specific, well-understood causal mechanism that links each step to the next, rather than just an assertion of the outcome.
