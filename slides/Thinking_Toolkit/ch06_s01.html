Important Terms (1/2)

Confirmation Bias

A cognitive bias that causes people to search for, interpret, favor, and recall information in a way that confirms or strengthens their prior personal beliefs or hypotheses.

Falsification

The act of proving a theory or hypothesis to be false, as opposed to proving it true. It is a critical component of the scientific method, championed by philosophers like Karl Popper.

How Confirmation Bias Operates at Different Stages

Seeking Confirming Evidence

We actively search for information that aligns with our beliefs. *Example:* A person believing vaccines are harmful will specifically search online for anti-vaccine content.

Noticing Confirming Evidence

We are more attuned to information that supports our views. *Example:* A sports fan convinced referees are biased against their team will interpret every marginal call as proof of that bias.

Interpreting Ambiguous Evidence as Confirming

Remembering Confirming Evidence More Readily

Our memory tends to favor information that reinforces our beliefs. Studies show people recall supportive information with greater accuracy and detail than contradictory information.

The Danger of Invisibility

Subtle and Insidious

One of the most perilous aspects of confirmation bias is its invisibility to the individual experiencing it.

False Sense of Rationality

You don't feel like you're ignoring contrary evidence; instead, you feel like you're being thorough, rational, and objective.

Rigged Search

The evidence you've gathered genuinely supports your view, but only because the process of gathering evidence was biased from the start. From an internal perspective, it appears to be honest inquiry.
