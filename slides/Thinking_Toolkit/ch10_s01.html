Important Terms (1/2)

Euthyphro Dilemma

A philosophical quandary, originating from Plato's dialogue Euthyphro, which explores whether moral acts are good because they are commanded by God, or commanded by God because they are good.

Socrates

An influential ancient Greek philosopher (c. 470–399 BC) known for his method of questioning (Socratic method) to probe fundamental concepts and expose contradictions.

Euthyphro

A character in Plato's dialogue who is a self-proclaimed expert on piety and prosecuting his own father for manslaughter, believing it to be the pious thing to do.

Piety

In the context of the Euthyphro dialogue, this term refers to righteousness, holiness, or actions deemed morally right, particularly in relation to religious duty and the gods.

Divine Command Theory

An ethical theory that posits an action's moral status (right or wrong) is determined by God's commands.

Important Terms (2/2)

Arbitrary

Based on random choice or personal whim, rather than any reason or system; lacking intrinsic reason or justification.

Divine Sovereignty

The concept of God's supreme power, authority, and independence over all things, including the creation and definition of morality.

Key Concepts (1/2)

Who were Socrates and Euthyphro, and what was the context of their meeting?

Socrates met the prophet Euthyphro outside the Athenian law courts. Euthyphro was prosecuting his own father for impiety, believing he had a clear understand...

What is Socrates' original formulation of the Euthyphro Dilemma?

Socrates asks: 'Is the pious loved by the gods because it is pious, or is it pious because it is loved by the gods?'

How is the Euthyphro Dilemma typically phrased in modern ethical terms regarding God and goodness?

The modern question is: 'Is something good because God commands it, or does God command it because it is good?'

Why is the Euthyphro Dilemma considered a highly consequential question in philosophy?

It is consequential because the two options lead to radically different conclusions about the fundamental nature of morality and its foundations.

What is the philosophical name for the view that 'Something is good because God commands it'?

Divine Command Theory.

What are the perceived advantages of Divine Command Theory?

Morality is grounded in divine authority, making it stable, absolute, and beyond human dispute.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the significant 'cost' or disadvantage of accepting Divine Command Theory?

Goodness becomes arbitrary. If God commanded cruelty, cruelty would be good. Goodness is merely a label applied by divine fiat, not an inherent quality.

Describe the second option of the Euthyphro Dilemma concerning God's relationship to good.

The second option is: God commands it because it is good. This implies that goodness is a real, objective quality that exists independently of God's commands.

What important intuition does the second option ('God commands it because it is good') preserve?

It preserves the intuition that goodness is a real, objective quality, meaning actions possess goodness inherently.

What is the 'severe cost' of the second option ('God commands it because it is good')?

Why do both options of the Euthyphro Dilemma present a challenge for those who want morality to be both objective and divinely grounded?

The dilemma forces a choice: either God is the source of an arbitrary morality, or morality is independent of God and thus not ultimately founded by Him.

What is the most influential philosophical response attempting to resolve the Euthyphro Dilemma?

The Divine Nature Theory.
