Machiavelli’s Classification of States and Principalities: A Study Guide

Study Slides

The Fundamental Division of States

Republics

States governed by a form of representative or collective rule.

Principalities

States governed by a single ruler or prince.

Taxonomy of Principalities

Hereditary Principalities

States where the prince’s family has been established as the ruling power for a long period.

New Principalities

States that have recently come under the control of a prince.

Sub-categories of New Principalities

Entirely New

A state created or seized where no prior dynastic rule existed for that prince (e.g., Francesco Sforza in Milan).

Annexed Members

Territory added to an existing hereditary state (e.g., the Kingdom of Naples being annexed to the King of Spain).

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is Machiavelli's fundamental division of all political authority?

All states are either republics or principalities, with no middle ground, exceptions, or hybrid forms.

What does Machiavelli's 'blunt opening' reveal about his method in The Prince?

He classifies what states are rather than what they ought to be, practicing what he calls 'the effectual truth of the matter'.

Which type of political entity does The Prince primarily focus on, and where are others discussed?

The Prince focuses exclusively on principalities; republics are reserved for his other great work, the Discourses on Livy.

Why might it be a mistake to conclude Machiavelli favors tyranny if one only reads The Prince?

His silence on republics is a deliberate narrowing of focus, not an indication he favors tyranny; his views on republics are found in the Discourses on Livy.

How does Machiavelli immediately subdivide principalities after establishing his focus?

Principalities are either hereditary (ruled by a family long established in power) or new.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Why does the distinction between hereditary and new principalities matter to Machiavelli?

The problems a prince faces—and the solutions available to him—differ dramatically depending on which type of state he rules.

What is the 'most important distinction' Machiavelli introduces regarding the acquisition of new states?

New states are acquired either 'by the arms of the prince himself, or of others, or else by fortune or by ability'.

What are the two opposing forces that shape political outcomes throughout The Prince?

Virtù (ability, skill, determination, energy) and Fortuna (fortune, luck, circumstance, timing).
