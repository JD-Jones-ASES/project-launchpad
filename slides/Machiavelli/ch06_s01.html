Powers and Principalities (Machiavelli)

Chapter 6, Section 1

Governing Conquered Free Cities

Study Slides

The Three Options for Rule

Strategy. Description

Practical Outcome

Destruction. Dismantling the city’s institutions and dispersing its population.

Most Secure: Removes the organizational capacity for resistance and rebellion.

Personal Residency. The prince moves to the conquered city to govern in person.

Demanding but Workable: Allows for immediate response to issues and relationship building, but only effective while the prince remains.

Indirect Rule. Allowing the city to keep its laws while extracting tribute and installing a friendly local oligarchy.

Inherently Weak: The memory of past freedom eventually leads to rebellion against the collaborators.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Indirect Rule

A governing strategy where the conqueror allows a city to maintain its own laws and customs but requires the payment of tribute and the installation of a local governing body loyal to the conqueror.

Memory of Liberty

The enduring cultural and political recognition of a city's past self-governance, which Machiavelli identifies as a primary driver of rebellion in conquered territories.

Oligarchy

A small group of people having control of a country, organization, or institution; in this context, local collaborators installed by a prince to govern on his behalf.

Political Realism

A political philosophy or method characterized by the prioritization of practical, effective power dynamics over moral or ethical considerations.

Principality

A state or territory ruled by a prince or monarch.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Republican Freedom

A tradition of self-governance where a city or state is ruled by its own laws and institutions rather than a foreign or absolute monarch.

Tribute

A wealth or tax paid periodically by one state or ruler to another as a sign of dependence or to ensure protection.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is Machiavelli's uncompromising assertion about governing cities accustomed to freedom?

He states that such cities are almost impossible to govern, and destruction is the only truly reliable solution.

According to Machiavelli in The Prince, Chapter V, what are the three courses for a prince to hold states accustomed to freedom?

1) Ruin them (destroy them), 2) Reside there in person, 3) Permit them to live under their own laws, drawing tribute, and establishing a friendly oligarchy.

Key Concepts (2/2)

According to Machiavelli, what is the only safe way to retain a city accustomed to freedom?

Ruin them (destroy them), as a city accustomed to freedom will otherwise always rally around liberty and ancient privileges.

What is the constant rallying point for rebellion in a conquered city accustomed to freedom?

The watchword of liberty and its ancient privileges.

Why, according to Machiavelli, is it so difficult to make free cities forget their former liberty?

Neither time nor benefits will cause them to forget, and they will always rally to their name and privileges unless disunited or dispersed.

What historical example does Machiavelli use to illustrate a city's enduring memory of liberty even after long subjugation?

Pisa, which rebelled after a hundred years of being held in bondage by the Florentines.

How do cities accustomed to living under a prince differ from free cities when their ruler's family is exterminated?

Cities under a prince are accustomed to obey and cannot agree on making a new ruler themselves, nor do they know how to govern themselves.

Why are cities accustomed to living under a prince considered easier for a conqueror to secure?

They are slow to take up arms because they lack a natural leader and the experience of self-governance, making them easier for a prince to gain and secure.
