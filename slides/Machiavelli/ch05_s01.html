The Kingdom of Darius and the Structures of Power

Comparison Table: Centralized vs. Decentralized States

Feature. Centralized Model (e.g., Persia, Ottoman Empire)

Decentralized Model (e.g., France)

Structure of Power. One supreme ruler with appointed officials and ministers.

A king ruling through hereditary nobles (barons/lords).

Source of Authority. Power flows top-down; officials owe positions entirely to the ruler.

Nobles have ancient rights and local loyalties independent of the king.

Ease of Conquest. Hard: No independent nobles to bribe; the state resists as a unified whole.

**Easy:** Can be invaded by finding one discontented baron to betray the ruler.

Ease of Holding. Easy: Once the ruler is defeated, there are no natural leaders left to organize resistance.

**Hard:** Numerous centers of resistance (nobles) remain with their own armed retainers.

Population Loyalty. Accustomed to central obedience; transfers loyalty to the new victor.

Loyal to local lords rather than just the central monarch.

Historical Context: Alexander and Darius

Timeline

Alexander the Great (356–323 BC) conquered the Persian Empire between 334 and 323 BC.

Key Battles

Alexander defeated Darius III at the battles of Issus (333 BC) and Gaugamela (331 BC).

The Persian Outcome

Because the Persian nobles under Darius did not have independent military followings, they submitted once Darius fell. Alexander adopted Persian customs and dress to facilitate the transition.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Barons/Lords

Hereditary nobles in a decentralized system who possess ancient rights, their own land, and their own armed retainers.

Centralized Rule

A system of government where power is concentrated in a single supreme ruler who governs through appointed servants rather than independent nobles.

Decentralized Rule

A system where power is shared or overlapped between a central monarch and various local authorities with independent loyalties.

Fifth Column

A group within a country that assists an invading enemy (referenced in the context of "discontented barons").

Macedonian Successors

The leaders who divided Alexander the Great's empire after his death in 323 BC and continued to rule the Persian core.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

The Turk

Machiavelli’s contemporary term for the Ottoman Empire, used as the primary example of a centralized state governed by a sultan and his appointed pashas.
