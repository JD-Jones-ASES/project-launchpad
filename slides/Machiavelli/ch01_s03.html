The "Mirror for Princes" Tradition

Core Philosophy

Rulers were encouraged to cultivate classical and Christian virtues: justice, piety, generosity, and mercy.

Fundamental Assumption

Good governance is a direct consequence of the ruler’s moral character. A virtuous man will be a successful king.

Primary Sources

Machiavelli’s Political Realism

Political Realism

The study of how power works in practice, not how it "ought" to work in an ideal world.

The "Effectual Truth" (*Verità effettuale*)

Machiavelli’s goal was to identify what actually works in the real world of politics, where a ruler is often surrounded by enemies and conflicting interests.

The Necessity of Vice

He argued that a ruler cannot always be virtuous. To survive and succeed, a prince must occasionally be prepared to be cruel, deceptive, or ruthless.

Case Study: Cesare Borgia (Duke Valentino)

Moral Assessment

By traditional standards, Borgia was a "monster" who used deception, betrayal, and execution of allies to maintain power.

Machiavelli’s Assessment

Machiavelli admired Borgia’s **methods** rather than his character. He praised Borgia’s strategic genius, decisiveness, and his skill at managing both force and appearances.

Conclusion

Borgia serves as a model of political intelligence. Even though his career ended in failure, his calculated use of power is presented as a template for effectiveness.
