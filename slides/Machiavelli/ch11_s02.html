Powers and Principalities (Machiavelli)

Chapter 11, Section 2

Measuring the Strength of Principalities

Study Slides

The Metric of Princely Strength

Self-Sufficient Princes

Those who possess enough men or money to assemble a sufficient army and engage any aggressor in open battle.

Dependent Princes

Those who cannot face an enemy in the field and are forced to retreat behind city walls for defense.

The Strategy of Defensive Fortification

Physical Preparation

Fortifying the town with ditches, walls, and sufficient artillery.

Resource Management

Maintaining public depots with a one-year supply of "eating, drinking, and firing" (food, water, and fuel).

The German Model of Independent Cities

• They yield to the emperor only when it suits them.

• Their fortifications make assault appear "tedious and difficult."

• They provide work for the community in industries that represent the "life and strength of the city."

• They hold military exercises in high repute and maintain strict ordinances to uphold them.

The Psychology of the Siege

Tempering Emotions

Offering hope that the hardship will be brief while simultaneously instilling fear of the enemy’s cruelty.

Managing Dissent

Adroitly handling subjects who become too bold or disruptive.

Glossary of Important Terms

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is Machiavelli's fundamental prior question before discussing a prince's behavior?

How strong is the state the prince governs?

Describe Machiavelli's 'stark and practical test' for a state's strength.

Can the prince fight a pitched battle against any attacker, or must he retreat behind walls and wait for relief?

What distinguishes a 'stronger category' prince from a 'precarious position' prince according to Machiavelli's strength test?

A stronger prince can hold the field in open battle; a precarious prince must rely on fortifications and popular endurance.

Define 'Military Self-Sufficiency' as Machiavelli uses it.

A prince is self-sufficient when he has enough men and money to face any enemy in open battle.

What characterizes a 'dependent' prince?

A dependent prince lacks the capacity for military self-sufficiency, needing help from allies, mercenaries, or greater powers.

Why does Machiavelli regard dependency as dangerous for a prince?

A dependent prince is at the mercy of others, losing control over his own fate, as opposed to the self-sufficient prince who controls his.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Which chapters does Machiavelli indicate will further explain the dangers of dependency, particularly regarding soldiers?

Chapters XII–XIII, which discuss mercenaries and auxiliaries.

What strategy does Machiavelli recommend for a prince who cannot meet an enemy in open battle?

Fortify the city, build up reserves of supplies, and cultivate the loyalty of the people to withstand a prolonged siege.

How can a prolonged siege benefit a weaker prince?

Armies on campaign consume enormous resources; a long siege can wear out attackers, making negotiation more profitable than persistence.

What is the critical factor for a weak prince's strategy to succeed during a siege?

The genuine love and loyalty of the people inside the walls, enabling them to endure great hardship and resist surrender.

What is the danger for a prince who has oppressed and alienated his people, even if his city walls are strong?

His subjects may open the gates from the inside, nullifying the physical defenses and betraying him.

Which historical example does Machiavelli use in this chapter to illustrate secure cities?

The free imperial cities of Germany, such as Nuremberg, Augsburg, and Frankfurt.
