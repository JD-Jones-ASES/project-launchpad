Governing Conquered Free Cities

Study Slides

The Vitality of Republics vs. Principalities

Feature. States Accustomed to a Prince

Republics (Free Cities)

Ease of Conquest. Easier to secure once the ruling family is exterminated.

Difficult to hold without extreme measures.

Self-Governance. They do not know how to govern themselves and cannot agree on a new leader.

Possess a "rallying point" in the memory of ancient privileges.

Response to Change. Slow to take up arms.

High vitality, greater hatred, and a desire for vengeance.

Memory of Liberty. Not applicable.

Never forgotten, regardless of time or benefits received.

Historical Case Studies

The Spartans (Athens and Thebes)

The Spartans attempted to hold Athens and Thebes by establishing oligarchies. Despite this indirect rule, they eventually lost both cities.

The Romans (Capua, Carthage, and Numantia)

The Romans chose to "dismantle" these cities. By ruining them, they successfully maintained control and did not lose the territories.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Bondage

A state of being held under the control of another; used to describe Pisa’s hundred-year subjection to Florence.

Dismantle

To pull down or destroy the structures (physical or political) of a city to prevent it from being used as a base for rebellion.

Free City

A state accustomed to living under its own laws and exercising self-governance.

Oligarchy

A small group of people having control of a country, organization, or institution. In this context, it is a local government created by a prince to maintain his interests.

Principality

A state or territory ruled by a prince or monarch.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Republic

A state where power is held by the people or their representatives, characterized by "vitality" and a deep-seated memory of liberty.

Tribute

Periodic payment made by one state to another as a sign of dependence or to ensure protection.

Watchword of Liberty

The symbolic name or concept of "freedom" used as a rallying cry for rebellion in formerly free states.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What are the advantages of a prince choosing to govern a conquered city in person?

The prince can respond to problems immediately, build personal relationships with local leaders, and prevent abuses by distant governors.

What is the main drawback or limitation of a prince choosing to govern a conquered city by living there personally?

This approach is only effective as long as the prince remains present. The underlying problems reassert themselves the moment he withdraws.

What advice did Machiavelli give elsewhere in The Prince regarding a ruler moving to a conquered territory?

He recommended this approach, notably criticizing Louis XII for not moving to Italy.

What is Machiavelli's third option for governing a conquered free city, besides destruction or direct personal rule?

Allowing it to continue under its own laws, extracting tribute, and governing through a friendly oligarchy of local collaborators.

What 'fatal weakness' does Machiavelli identify in the strategy of governing a conquered city through a friendly oligarchy?

The memory of the city's previous freedom persists among the population and eventually becomes a rallying cry for rebellion.

Why might a collaborating oligarchy in a conquered city eventually lose its position or even lead resistance?

They may be removed by their own people who remember freedom, or they may find it more advantageous to lead the resistance themselves against the foreign ruler.
