Powers and Principalities (Machiavelli)

Chapter 14, Section 2

Machiavelli on Auxiliaries and National Defense

Study Slides

The Nature of Auxiliaries

The Double Bind

If the auxiliaries lose the battle, the prince is ruined. If they win the battle, the prince becomes their prisoner or remains under their control.

Unified Obedience

Unlike mercenaries, auxiliaries are a united force that owes its total obedience to someone else (their original ruler), making them a ready-made source of ruin.

The Danger of Valor

Machiavelli argues that while the greatest danger in mercenaries is their cowardice (*dastardy*), the greatest danger in auxiliaries is their courage (*valour*).

The Hierarchy of Military Forces

One’s Own Forces (Highest Reliability)

Composed of a prince’s own subjects, citizens, or dependents. This is the only way to achieve "real victory" and long-term security.

Mixed Forces

A combination of national and mercenary/auxiliary forces. While superior to purely mercenary or auxiliary armies, they are significantly inferior to a fully national force.

Mercenaries

Paid soldiers who are not unified and require time to turn against a prince.

Auxiliaries (Lowest Reliability)

Unified foreign forces that are dangerous even when they are successful.

Historical Case Studies

Figure/State. External Force Used

Outcome

Pope Julius II. Spanish Auxiliaries

He was saved only by "good fortune" when the Switzers drove out the victors, preventing him from becoming a prisoner of his allies.

Florentines. French Auxiliaries

Attempted to take Pisa and faced more danger than at any other point in their history.

Emperor of Constantinople. Turkish Auxiliaries

Once the war ended, the Turks refused to leave, beginning the "servitude of Greece."

Cesare Borgia. French/Orsini/Vitelli

He moved from auxiliaries to mercenaries, found both unfaithful, and finally established his own forces, which led to his highest reputation.

Hiero of Syracuse. Mercenaries (*condottieri*)

Found them useless and dangerous; had them destroyed so he could fight with his own forces.

King Louis XI of France. Swiss Mercenaries

By enlisting Switzers and abolishing French infantry, he made his own army dependent on foreign help and weakened the kingdom.

Roman Empire. Goths

The enlisting of the Goths marked the beginning of the empire's decline and the transfer of Roman "valour" to others.

Glossary of Terms (1/2)

Auxiliaries

Foreign troops borrowed from another ruler to assist in a conflict; described as the most dangerous form of military assistance.

Condottieri

Professional mercenary military leaders in Renaissance Italy hired by city-states to lead their wars.

Dastardy

Cowardice or lack of spirit; identified as the primary risk when using mercenary forces.

Mixed Forces

An army composed of both national soldiers and hired mercenaries or auxiliaries.

One's Own Forces

A military comprised of a prince’s own subjects, citizens, or dependents; the only basis for true security and power.

Glossary of Terms (2/2)

Orsini and Vitelli

Mercenary leaders employed and eventually destroyed by Cesare Borgia when he found them unfaithful.

Principality

A state or territory ruled by a prince.

Switzers

Swiss mercenary soldiers, known for their infantry, upon whom the French became dangerously dependent.

Valour

Great courage or bravery, particularly in battle; cited as the quality that makes auxiliaries dangerous to the prince who employs them.
