Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Balance of Power

The distribution of political and military strength among multiple states to prevent any single power from becoming dominant.

Colonization

The practice of sending a prince’s own subjects to reside in a newly annexed territory to ensure loyalty and reduce the cost of control.

Garrison

A body of troops stationed in a fortress or town to defend it; Machiavelli views these as expensive and a source of resentment.

Hereditary Principality

A state ruled by a prince whose family has been in power for a long time; described as easier to govern than mixed principalities.

Italian Wars (1494–1559)

A series of conflicts for control of Italy involving major European powers and Italian city-states, marking the end of the balanced Italian political system.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Louis XII

King of France (1498–1515) used by Machiavelli as a primary example of a ruler who failed to maintain a mixed principality due to strategic errors.

Mixed Principality

A territory newly annexed to a prince’s existing state, often possessing different cultural and linguistic traditions.

Temporal Authority

The worldly, political power held by a religious figure, such as the Pope’s control over lands and armies.

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to Machiavelli, what is a 'principality'?

A state ruled by a single prince or sovereign, as opposed to a republic.

Which historical figure's failures does Machiavelli use as a primary case study for governing annexed territories?

Louis XII of France in Italy.

What is the inherent difficulty in all new principalities that leads people to rebel?

Men willingly change rulers hoping to better themselves, but then find they have gone from bad to worse.

Name two types of enemies a new prince typically creates when acquiring a new principality.

Those he injured in seizing the principality, and those who helped him but whose expectations he could not satisfy.

What fundamental necessity does a new prince impose, leading to hardship for the newly acquired state?

He must burden them with his soldiery and other hardships necessary for the new acquisition.

Why did Louis XII quickly lose Milan the first time?

Those who had opened the gates to him, finding themselves deceived in their hopes of future benefit, would not endure the ill-treatment of the new prince.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Why is it generally harder to lose rebellious provinces after acquiring them a second time?

The prince takes the opportunity of rebellion to punish delinquents, clear out suspects, and strengthen weak places without reluctance.

What two conditions make it easier to hold newly acquired dominions?

When they are of the same country and language, especially if they have not been accustomed to self-government.

For dominions of the same country and language, what two considerations are essential for a prince to hold them securely?

Extinguish the family of their former lord, and do not alter their laws or taxes.

What is Machiavelli's primary recommendation for a prince seeking to hold states acquired in a country differing in language, customs, or laws?

The prince should go and reside there personally.

List three benefits of a prince residing in a newly acquired foreign state.

Disorders are seen and remedied quickly; the country is not pillaged by officials; subjects have prompt recourse to the prince, fostering love or fear.

Besides residing, what is another 'better course' Machiavelli suggests for holding foreign states?

Sending colonies to one or two key places.
