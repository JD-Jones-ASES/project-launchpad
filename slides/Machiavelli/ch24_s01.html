Powers and Principalities (Machiavelli)

Chapter 24, Section 1

Machiavelli on Counsel and the Danger of Flattery

Study Slides

The Flattery Trap

• Risks are downplayed.

• Problems are hidden until it is too late to address them.

• The prince is left unprepared when reality eventually breaks through the facade of courtly praise.

Structured Honesty

Selective Advisors

The prince selects a small circle of capable, loyal men known for good judgment.

Mandated Frankness

These advisors are explicitly required to speak the truth, but only when the prince asks for it.

Specific Inquiry

The prince asks about specific matters, listens carefully, and then deliberates independently.

Restriction of Unsolicited Advice

Outside of this designated circle and specific timing, the prince does not invite criticism, thereby preserving his public authority.

Glossary of Key Terms

Key Concepts (1/2)

What primary danger does Machiavelli identify that princes find difficult to avoid?

The danger of flatterers, who are prevalent in courts because men are self-complacent and easily deceived.

What dilemma does a prince face when trying to guard against flatterers, according to Machiavelli?

If he lets everyone tell him the truth, respect for him abates; if he doesn't, he falls prey to flattery.

What 'structural' solution does Machiavelli propose for a prince to obtain honest advice without sacrificing authority?

To build a system that makes honesty possible by carefully managing who can speak truth and under what conditions.

What is Machiavelli's 'third course' for a wise prince to manage counsel and avoid flatterers?

Choosing a select group of wise men in his state and granting only them the liberty of speaking the truth, and only on matters of his inquiry.

To whom should a prince grant the liberty of speaking the truth?

Only to the wise men he has specifically chosen and designated as counselors.

On what specific topics should the chosen advisors be allowed to speak the truth?

Only on those things of which the prince inquires, and no others.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What active role must a prince take in seeking advice from his chosen counselors?

He ought to question them upon everything and listen to their opinions.

After receiving counsel, whose conclusions should a prince ultimately form?

His own conclusions.

How should a prince treat his trusted counselors to encourage them to speak freely and honestly?

He should treat them in a way that each knows the more freely he speaks, the more he shall be preferred.

Beyond his chosen group of wise men, who else should a prince listen to for counsel?

No one else; he should listen to no one outside his select group of counselors.

What should a prince do once he has formed his resolutions based on counsel?

He should pursue the thing resolved on and be steadfast in his resolutions.

What are the two negative consequences for a prince who fails to follow Machiavelli's advice on managing counsel?

He is either overthrown by flatterers or is so often changed by varying opinions that he falls into contempt.
