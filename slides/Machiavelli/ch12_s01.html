Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: According to Machiavelli, what sustains ecclesiastical principalities regardless of the prince's behavior?

> **Answer:** They are sustained by the ancient ordinances of religion, which are all-powerful and maintain the state regardless of the prince’s conduct.

Q: How does Machiavelli’s tone change when he discusses the role of God in maintaining these states?

> **Answer:** His tone becomes ironic. He claims it would be "presumptuous" to analyze states upheld by God, yet he immediately begins a historical and political analysis of how those states were actually built through force and strategy.

Q: Which two popes are credited with turning the Papal States into a major political and military power?

> **Answer:** Pope Alexander VI and Pope Julius II.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/2)

Q: What role did Cesare Borgia play in the rise of the Papal States?

> **Answer:** As the son of Pope Alexander VI, he used political violence, cunning, and treacy to expand and consolidate Church territories into a unified state.

Q: Why was Julius II known as the "Warrior Pope"?

> **Answer:** He earned the nickname (*il Papa Terribile*) because he personally led military campaigns, such as the siege of Mirandola in 1511, where he directed artillery fire and commanded troops in armor.

Q: In Machiavelli’s view, why is a prince who holds power through religious authority "enviable"?

> **Answer:** Because the legitimacy of their rule is not conditional on their performance; they do not need to be just or competent to maintain the obedience of their subjects.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Ancient Ordinances of Religion

The established religious traditions and laws that provide the foundation and stability for ecclesiastical states, independent of the ruler's actions.

Civil Religion

A concept anticipated by Machiavelli where rulers utilize religious symbolism, ceremonies, and institutions to legitimize their political authority.

Ecclesiastical Principality

A state ruled by a religious leader (such as the Pope) where political power is intertwined with religious authority.

Il Papa Terribile

The nickname for Pope Julius II, reflecting his formidable personality and his aggressive, military-led approach to the papacy.

Mirandola (1511)

The site of a military siege where Pope Julius II personally commanded troops, symbolizing the papacy's peak as a military power.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Papal States

The territories in central Italy under the direct sovereign rule of the Pope.

Vicar of Christ

The theological title for the Pope, which Machiavelli uses ironically to suggest the gap between spiritual claims and the political realities of ruling a state.
