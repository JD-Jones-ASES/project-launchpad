Powers and Principalities (Machiavelli)

Chapter 22, Section 2

Gaining Renown and Building Princely Reputation

Study Slides

Great Enterprises and Fine Examples

Strategic Distraction

During the war in Granada, he occupied the minds of the barons with military concerns, preventing them from noticing his internal acquisition of power.

Resource Management

He sustained his armies using the wealth of the Church and the people.

Continuous Action

By launching a succession of military campaigns (Africa, Italy, and France), he kept his subjects in a state of "suspense and admiration," ensuring enemies had no time to organize against him.

Internal Affairs and Public Image

Extraordinary Rewards and Punishments

Following the example of Messer Bernabo da Milano, a prince should reward or punish citizens for extraordinary deeds in civil life in a manner that becomes a public talking point.

Engagement with the People

A prince should patronize ability, honor the proficient, and entertain the public with festivals and spectacles.

Maintaining Majesty

While engaging with guilds and societies, a prince must never abate the majesty of his rank.

The Rejection of Neutrality

The Victor’s Perspective

A victor does not want "doubtful friends" who failed to assist during a trial.

The Loser’s Perspective

A loser will not offer shelter to a neutral party who refused to share their fate "sword in hand."

The Result of Indecision

Irresolute princes often follow the path of neutrality to avoid immediate danger, which typically leads to their ruin.

Prudence in Alliances

Avoid Powerful Allies

Unless compelled by necessity, a prince should avoid joining a more powerful state to attack others. If the alliance is successful, the prince remains at the mercy of the more powerful partner.

The Venetian Example

The Venetians’ unnecessary alliance with France against the Duke of Milan led to their downfall.
