Chapter 13, Section 2

The Military Foundations of the State and the Critique of Mercenary Forces

Study Slides

The Core Foundations of a State

Good Laws

The legal and civic framework that governs the conduct of citizens and the administration of the state.

Good Arms

The military means of offense and defense.

The Critique of Mercenary Forces

The Dilemma of Mercenary Captains

The Capable Captain

If the captain is skillful, he cannot be trusted. He will inevitably seek his own greatness, either by oppressing his employer or by pursuing objectives contrary to the prince’s intentions.

The Unskilled Captain

if the captain lacks skill, he will lead the state to ruin through incompetence.

Successes of Nationalized Arms

Rome and Sparta

Both remained free and armed for many centuries by using their own citizens.

The Switzers

Highly armed and entirely free.

Early Venice

The Venetians acted safely and gloriously as long as they fought at sea with their own gentlemen and plebeians.

Failures of Mercenary Reliance

Carthage

Oppressed by their own mercenary soldiers after the first war with Rome.

Thebes

After the death of Epaminondas, they made Philip of Macedon their captain; after victory, he stripped them of their liberty.

Milan

Enlisted Francesco Sforza against the Venetians. After defeating the enemy at Caravaggio, Sforza betrayed Milan and allied with the Venetians to crush his former masters.

Naples

Queen Johanna was forced to surrender her kingdom to the King of Aragon after being abandoned by her mercenary captain, Sforza (father of Francesco).

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Alberigo da Conio

A Romagnian captain who first gave renown to mercenary soldiery; the teacher of Braccio and Sforza.

Auxiliaries

Military forces borrowed from another ruler or state to assist in war.

Bracceschi

A mercenary faction often in opposition to the forces of Sforza.

Carmignuola

A valiant captain for the Venetians who was murdered by them after he became "lukewarm" in his efforts during land-based expansion.

Francesco Sforza

A mercenary captain who betrayed his Milanese employers to become the Duke of Milan.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Giovanni Acuto

An English mercenary captain (John Hawkwood) whose fidelity to the Florentines was never truly tested because he did not achieve a decisive victory.

Mercenary

A professional soldier hired to serve in a foreign army, motivated by pay rather than political or national loyalty.

Stipend

The small, fixed salary paid to mercenary soldiers.

Temporal Power

The political and worldly authority held by the Church, as opposed to its spiritual authority.

Vaila

The site of a battle where the Venetians lost in one day what had taken eight hundred years of effort to acquire.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What does Machiavelli consider the material foundation of all political power?

Machiavelli believes military force is the material foundation of all political power.

According to Machiavelli, what are the two fundamental foundations of all principalities?

Good laws and good arms are the two foundations of all principalities.

Why does Machiavelli argue that 'good laws cannot exist without good arms'?

He argues that military strength (good arms) is the prior condition for everything else; law without force is empty.

What is Machiavelli's primary criticism of mercenary forces?

He criticizes them for their lack of commitment, loyalty, and their self-serving motivations, which makes them unreliable and dangerous.

What motivates mercenaries, according to Machiavelli, that makes them unreliable?

Mercenaries fight for money; their loyalty evaporates when money runs out or when an enemy offers more.

How do mercenaries' incentives, such as prolonging wars and minimizing casualties, impact their employers?

These incentives lead mercenaries to prolong conflicts for profit and avoid decisive battles, ultimately weakening the state that hires them.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How does Machiavelli describe mercenaries, summarizing his view?

He writes that mercenaries are 'useless and dangerous'.

In Machiavelli's view, what was the direct cause of Italy's military weakness and political fragmentation in the fifteenth century?

The condottieri, the mercenary captains who dominated Italian warfare, were the direct cause.

What is a 'citizen army' in Machiavelli's philosophy?

A citizen army consists of soldiers who fight because they have a personal stake in the outcome, defending their own homes, families, and city.

What key difference makes citizen soldiers more reliable than mercenaries?

Citizen soldiers have a personal stake in the outcome (property, family, city) and cannot be bought off, unlike mercenaries who fight only for pay.

What practical experiment did Machiavelli undertake to implement the idea of a citizen army?

As Secretary of the Florentine Republic, he organized a citizen militia in 1505–06 to reduce Florence's dependence on mercenaries.

Who were the 'condottieri' in Renaissance Italy?

Condottieri were professional military commanders who led hired companies of soldiers, relied upon by Italian city-states from the fourteenth century onward.
