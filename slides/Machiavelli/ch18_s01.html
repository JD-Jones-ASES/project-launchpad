Powers and Principalities (Machiavelli)

Chapter 18, Section 1

Machiavelli on Cruelty and Clemency: Loved or Feared?

Study Slides

Human Nature and Political Reliability

The Nature of Love

Love is maintained by a "link of obligation." Because men are "base," they will break this link whenever it serves their advantage. Love is conditional and depends on the subjects' choice.

The Nature of Fear

Fear is maintained by the "dread of punishment." Unlike love, the dread of punishment "never fails" because it is a constant motivator. Fear depends on the prince and his capacity to act.

Historical Applications of Severity

Example. Action Taken

Outcome

Cesare Borgia. Used "cruelty" to subdue the Romagna, including public executions and the display of a dismembered body.

The territory was consolidated, unified, and brought to peace.

The Florentines. Exercised "clemency" by refusing to use harsh force to stop factional conflict in Pistoia.

The city was eventually destroyed by civil violence and disorder.

Hannibal. Employed "inhuman cruelty" and ruthless discipline, including executions of failing soldiers.

He held together a diverse army (Africans, Gauls, Iberians) for 15 years in hostile territory.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Baseness

The perceived tendency of human beings to be self-interested, unreliable, and willing to break obligations for personal gain.

Clemency

Mercy or leniency; Machiavelli warns that excessive clemency can lead to negligence and social disorder.

Idealist Tradition

The philosophical approach (represented by Plato and Aristotle) that focuses on how a ruler "ought" to behave according to moral ideals.

Link of Obligation

The bond created by love or mutual benefit, which Machiavelli argues is fragile in political contexts.

Patrimony

Inherited property or wealth; Machiavelli identifies the loss of patrimony as a more lasting grievance for men than the death of a relative.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Realist Tradition

Machiavelli’s approach to politics, which prioritizes practical survival and the effective exercise of power over moral or religious ideals.

Severity

The calculated use of harshness or punishment to maintain discipline and prevent widespread social chaos.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the central question Machiavelli addresses in Chapter XVII of The Prince?

Whether it is better for a prince to be loved or to be feared.

According to Machiavelli, what quality should a prince desire to be considered?

Clement (merciful).

How does Machiavelli qualify a prince's desire to be considered clement?

A prince must take care not to misuse this clemency.

How did Cesare Borgia's actions, though considered cruel, ultimately demonstrate a form of mercy according to Machiavelli?

His cruelty reconciled the Romagna, unified it, and restored it to peace and loyalty, which Machiavelli considered more merciful than allowing disorder.

What mistake did the Florentine people make, according to Machiavelli, by trying to avoid a reputation for cruelty?

They permitted Pistoia to be destroyed by factional violence, resulting in more widespread harm than a prince's decisive 'cruel' actions would have caused.

Under what condition does Machiavelli argue a prince 'ought not to mind the reproach of cruelty'?

So long as he keeps his subjects united and loyal.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Why is it impossible for a 'new prince' to avoid the imputation of cruelty?

New states are full of dangers, necessitating firm actions to establish and maintain order and loyalty.

What is Machiavelli's ideal answer to the question of whether it is better to be loved or feared?

A prince should wish to be both.

When a prince cannot be both loved and feared, which does Machiavelli conclude is safer?

It is much safer to be feared than loved.

Name three characteristics Machiavelli attributes to men in general.

Ungrateful, fickle, false, cowardly, covetous (any three).

Why does Machiavelli argue that friendships obtained by promises or payments are unreliable?

Such friendships are not secured by greatness or nobility of mind and cannot be relied upon in times of need; men will turn against a prince when danger appr...

How does Machiavelli describe the difference in how love and fear are preserved?

Love is preserved by a link of obligation (which is easily broken for personal advantage), while fear is preserved by a dread of punishment (which never fails).
