Powers and Principalities (Machiavelli)

Chapter 13, Section 1

Machiavelli on Military Power and Mercenaries

Study Slides

The Military Foundation of Political Power

The Primacy of Force

Good laws cannot exist without good arms. Military strength is the prior condition that allows laws and political skill to have meaning.

The Defense of the State

Regardless of how a prince acquires a state or what governing methods they use, the ability to defend the territory is the ultimate measure of political worth.

The Structural Critique of Mercenaries

Motivation and Loyalty

Mercenaries are motivated solely by wages. Because they lack a personal stake in the state’s survival, their loyalty evaporates when the money runs out or when they face a more lucrative offer.

Economic Drain

In peacetime, mercenaries deplete the state's treasury. In wartime, they often fail at crucial moments.

Incentives for Inefficiency

Political Danger

Strong mercenary leaders may use their power to seize control of the state (as seen with Francesco Sforza), while weak leaders simply lose the state to its enemies.

The Citizen Army Ideal

Personal Stake

Citizens who own property and have families in the city they defend will fight with a commitment that paid foreigners cannot match.

Incorruptibility

Because victory ensures their own freedom and defeat means their subjugation, citizen soldiers are harder to bribe or buy off.

Historical Implementation

Machiavelli practiced this theory by organizing a citizen militia in Florence between 1505 and 1506 to reduce the city’s reliance on hired captains.

Glossary of Key Terms

Key Concepts (1/2)

What does Machiavelli identify as the chief foundations of all states?

Good laws and good arms.

According to Machiavelli, why are good arms more fundamental than good laws?

Good laws cannot exist where the state is not well-armed; where a state is well-armed, it has good laws.

What are the four categories of arms a prince may use to defend his state, according to Machiavelli?

His own, mercenaries, auxiliaries, or mixed.

What is Machiavelli's overall judgment of mercenary and auxiliary forces?

They are useless and dangerous; a state based on them will stand neither firm nor safe.

List three negative characteristics Machiavelli attributes to mercenaries.

Disunited, ambitious, without discipline, unfaithful, valiant before friends, cowardly before enemies, lacking fear of God or fidelity to men. (Any three)

Why does Machiavelli claim mercenaries are unwilling to die for their employer?

Their only motivation is a 'trifle of stipend,' which is not enough to make them willing to die.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What happens to a state relying on mercenaries during times of peace and during war?

In peace, one is robbed by them; in war, one is robbed by the enemy (as mercenaries desert or flee).

What historical event does Machiavelli cite as direct proof of Italy's ruin due to reliance on mercenaries?

Charles, King of France, was 'allowed to seize Italy with chalk in hand,' demonstrating Italy's weakness from years of relying on mercenaries.

What is the dilemma (infelicity) associated with mercenary captains, whether they are capable or not?

If capable, they aspire to their own greatness, potentially oppressing their master. If not skillful, the state is ruined by losing battles.

How should a prince or a republic, respectively, address military command to avoid the pitfalls of mercenaries?

A prince should lead in person. A republic should send its own citizens, recalling unsatisfactory ones and holding worthy ones by law.

Name two ancient states Machiavelli mentions as examples of making great progress and remaining free by using their own arms.

Rome and Sparta (he also mentions the Switzers).

Provide an example Machiavelli uses to illustrate a mercenary captain betraying his employer.

Francesco Sforza, hired by the Milanese against the Venetians, allied with the Venetians to crush the Milanese, his masters.
