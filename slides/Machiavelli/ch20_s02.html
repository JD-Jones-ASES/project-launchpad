Chapter 20, Section 2

Machiavelli’s The Prince: Avoiding Contempt and Hatred (Study Guide)

Study Slides

The Survival of the Prince

Avoiding Hatred

This is achieved primarily by abstaining from the property and women of subjects.

Avoiding Contempt

This is achieved by projecting an image of greatness, courage, gravity, and fortitude.

The Two Fears

Internal Threats

Conspiracies by subjects. The best defense is the goodwill of the people. If the people are satisfied, a conspirator fears the "sequel to the crime," knowing the populace will turn against them.

External Threats

Foreign powers. The defense against these includes being well-armed and having reliable allies.

The Management of "Humours"

The People

Generally love peace and unaspiring princes.

The Nobles

Possess ambition that must be curbed.

The Soldiers (Roman Context)

Historically loved warlike, cruel, and rapacious princes who allowed them to vent their greed upon the people.

Historical Laboratory: The Roman Emperors

Emperor. Outcome

Machiavelli’s Analysis

Marcus Aurelius. Lived and died honored.

Succeeded by hereditary title; possessed virtues that kept both the people and soldiers in their places. Neither hated nor despised.

Pertinax. Overthrown and murdered.

A "new prince" who tried to force an "honest life" on soldiers accustomed to licentiousness. Despised for his old age.

Alexander. Murdered by the army.

Extremely good and just, but despised as "effeminate" and for being governed by his mother.

Severus. Reigned successfully.

A "new prince" who combined the qualities of the lion and the fox. His supreme renown and valour awed the people and satisfied the soldiers.

Antoninus Caracalla. Murdered by a centurion.

Admired by soldiers for being warlike, but his extreme ferocity and "unheard of" cruelties made him hated by the whole world.

Commodus. Conspired against and killed.

Inherited the empire but fell into contempt by descending to the theatre to fight gladiators; hated for his rapacity and cruelty.

Maximinus. Murdered by his own army.

Despised for his low birth (a sheep-keeper) and hated for his barbarity and the cruelties of his prefects.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Contemptible

To be viewed as weak, fickle, or frivolous; a state that invites conspiracy and rebellion.

Effeminate

A term used by Machiavelli to describe a prince perceived as soft or easily influenced (e.g., Alexander being governed by his mother), leading to contempt.

Fox and Lion

A metaphor for the two natures a prince must imitate: the fox to recognize snares (cunning) and the lion to frighten wolves (force/valour).

Humours

The differing temperaments and desires of social classes (the people, the nobles, and the soldiers).

Malcontent

A dissatisfied subject. Machiavelli warns that a conspirator must rely on malcontents, who are often unreliable because they can benefit from denouncing the conspiracy.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Rapacious

Characterized by greed and the forceful taking of property; a primary cause of being hated.

Reproach

Blame or criticism directed at a prince, often for favoring one social class over another.

Valour

Great courage and skill, especially in battle; for Severus, this quality was so great that it protected him from the hatred of the people.

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to Machiavelli, what are the primary sources from which 'hatred' arises for a prince?

Hatred arises primarily from seizing the property of subjects and interfering with their women.

How does Machiavelli define 'contempt' and what specific traits in a prince lead to it?

Contempt arises when a prince is perceived as irresolute, effeminate, cowardly, or weak, often due to an inability to make up his mind or enforce decrees.

What is the significant danger to a prince who is held in contempt by his subjects and foreign powers?

A prince held in contempt is not feared, making him vulnerable to conspiracies, factions, and ambitious nobles within his state.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What did Machiavelli conclude about the specific circumstances that allowed Marcus Aurelius to succeed as a virtuous ruler?

His birth and character aligned perfectly with his circumstances; he inherited power in a stable state, which permitted him to be Marcus Aurelius.

Which Roman emperor does Machiavelli present as a successful ruler who came to power through force and cunning, demonstrating the 'fox-and-lion' method?

Septimius Severus, who secured power through superior military skill and ruthless political cunning, prevailing despite difficult circumstances.

What was the key difference in the circumstances faced by Marcus Aurelius versus Septimius Severus?

Marcus Aurelius came to power legitimately in a stable state, while Severus seized power through a military coup during chaos and civil war.

According to Machiavelli, why did Commodus fail as an emperor?

He was hated for personal cruelty and contemptible for eccentric behavior (like appearing as a gladiator), leading to his assassination by his own household.
