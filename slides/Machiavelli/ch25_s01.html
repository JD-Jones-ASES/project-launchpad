Important Terms (1/2)

Italian Princes

The various rulers of independent city-states and principalities across the Italian peninsula during Machiavelli's era, many of whom lost power to stronger French, Spanish, or Imperial forces.

Mercenaries

Hired soldiers, often foreigners, who fight for money rather than loyalty to a specific state or ruler. Machiavelli consistently condemned their use as unreliable and dangerous.

Auxiliaries

Troops borrowed from a more powerful ally. Machiavelli also criticized their use, viewing them as a risk to a prince's independence.

Popular Loyalty

The genuine support and allegiance of the people (subjects) to their ruler, which Machiavelli argues is essential for a state's stability and defense.

Adversity

Difficult or unfavorable circumstances; in this context, primarily referring to foreign invasion, military defeat, and loss of state.

The Prince

Niccolò Machiavelli's influential 16th-century political treatise, offering advice on how to acquire, maintain, and wield political power.

The Verdict on Italy

Machiavelli's Core Argument

Chapter XXIV serves as Machiavelli's direct application of his political theories to the contemporary crisis of Italian states collapsing under foreign invasion.

Harsh Judgment

He delivers a severe "verdict," concluding that Italian princes were "inadequate" rather than merely unlucky. They violated fundamental principles of good governance.

Neglect of Key Principles

Their failures stemmed from neglecting their armies, failing to secure popular loyalty, and not taking enemies seriously, all of which contradict the advice given in The Prince.

"Do Not Blame Fortune"

Rejection of Excuses

Machiavelli directly refutes the common self-exculpatory response of defeated princes who blamed bad luck or overwhelming odds.

Fortune vs. Free Will

He argues that while fortune might govern "roughly half" of what happens, the other half is firmly "within human control."

Moral and Political Judgment

This isn't just a historical observation; it's a judgment. The princes were not tragic victims but rather responsible individuals who had warnings and resources but "chose not to act."

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is Machiavelli's harsh verdict on the Italian princes who lost their states?

They have no one to blame but themselves.

According to Machiavelli, why are the actions of a new prince more closely observed than those of an hereditary one?

Because a new prince is establishing his rule, people pay closer attention, and if his actions are able, he gains more loyalty than ancient blood.

How do men typically react to a new prince's good actions, according to Machiavelli?

Men are attracted more by the present than the past; if they find the present good, they enjoy it, seek no further, and will defend the prince.

What does Machiavelli consider 'double glory' for a new prince?

To have established a new principality, and adorned and strengthened it with good laws, good arms, good allies, and with a good example.

What does Machiavelli consider 'double disgrace' for a prince born to power?

To lose his state by want of wisdom.

What was the primary common defect found in Italian seigniors who lost their states, regarding their military?

A defect in regard to arms.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Besides defects in arms, what two other critical failures did losing Italian princes exhibit concerning their internal relations?

Either having the people hostile, or if the people were friendly, not knowing how to secure the nobles.

Under what conditions does Machiavelli state that states with enough power to keep an army in the field cannot be lost?

In the absence of defects regarding arms, having the people hostile, or failing to secure the nobles.

Why does Machiavelli advise princes not to accuse fortune for the loss of their principalities?

Because it is rather their own sloth; they failed to make any provision in quiet times against the tempest.

What 'common defect in man' does Machiavelli highlight regarding times of peace?

Not to make any provision in the calm against the tempest (i.e., neglecting preparation for bad times).

What mistaken strategy did princes often adopt when 'bad times came' after neglecting preparation?

They thought of flight instead of defending themselves, hoping the people, disgusted with conquerors, would recall them.
