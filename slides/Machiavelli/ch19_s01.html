Chapter 19, Section 1

The Fox and the Lion (Machiavelli’s The Prince, Chapter XVIII)

Study Slides

The Dual Nature of Combat: Law and Force

By Law

This method is natural to men and involves reason and established rules.

By Force

This method is characteristic of beasts and involves raw power.

The Strategic Breaking of Faith

Historical Precedent

Great princes who have achieved significant things are those who "set little store by their promises" and used cunning to circumvent those relying on loyalty.

Reciprocity and Human Nature

Because other men are often dishonest and will not keep their promises to the prince, the prince is not bound to keep his promises to them. To do otherwise is viewed not as virtue, but as self-dest...

The Primacy of Appearances

Public Perception

The majority of people judge by what they see. They lack the proximity to experience a ruler’s true character.

The Mask of Power

A skillfully constructed public persona creates a political reality that facilitates obedience and trust, regardless of the ruler’s private motivations.

The Few vs. The Many

While a few individuals may see through the prince's facade, they lack the standing to challenge the perception held by the masses, who are protected by the "majesty of the state."

Glossary of Important Terms

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to Machiavelli in The Prince, what is his controversial stance on a ruler's promise-keeping?

A prince need not, and often should not, keep his word if doing so is detrimental to his interests or the state's; he must learn when to break it.

Machiavelli describes two ways of contesting power. What are they, and to what are they compared?

By law (proper to men) and by force (proper to beasts).

Why does Machiavelli argue that a prince must resort to force, even if law is preferred?

The first method (law) is frequently not sufficient, necessitating the use of the second (force).

What does the ancient fable of Achilles being nursed by the Centaur Chiron signify for a prince, according to Machiavelli?

It signifies that a prince must understand how to utilize both human (law) and bestial (force) natures, as one without the other is not durable.

When adopting the 'beast' nature, which two animals should a prince emulate, and why?

The fox and the lion. The fox is needed to discover snares, and the lion to terrify wolves.

Under what two conditions does Machiavelli argue a wise prince should not keep faith?

When such observance may be turned against him, and when the original reasons that caused him to pledge it exist no longer.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is Machiavelli's core justification for a prince not being bound to keep faith with others?

If men were entirely good, the precept wouldn't hold, but because men are bad and will not keep faith, the prince is not bound to observe it with them either.

Beyond merely breaking promises, what skill must a prince master regarding his true intentions?

He must know how to disguise his true characteristic, and be a great pretender and dissembler.

According to Machiavelli, why does a prince who seeks to deceive often succeed?

Because 'men are so simple, and so subject to present necessities, that he who seeks to deceive will always find someone who will allow himself to be deceived.'

Whom does Machiavelli cite as a modern example of a prince who successfully employed deception, always finding victims despite his false oaths?

Alexander the Sixth (Pope Alexander VI).

What Italian proverb succinctly captures the difference between Alexander the Sixth's and Cesare Borgia's methods of cunning?

'Alexander never did what he said, Cesare never said what he did.'

Is it more important for a prince to possess good qualities or to appear to possess them, according to Machiavelli?

It is very necessary to appear to have them. To actually have and always observe them can be injurious, but to appear to have them is useful.
