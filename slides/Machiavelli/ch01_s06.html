Powers and Principalities (Machiavelli)

Chapter 1, Section 6

Machiavelli’s The Prince

Study Slides

Structural Framework

Chapters I–XI

An analysis of different types of states and principalities, focusing on how they are acquired and maintained.

Chapters XII–XIV

An examination of military power, emphasizing the necessity of a prince having his own army rather than relying on mercenaries or auxiliary forces.

Chapters XV–XXIII

A discussion of the prince’s personal qualities and "virtues," including the balance between generosity and parsimony, and cruelty and mercy.

Final Chapters

A focus on the contemporary crisis in Italy, concluding with a call for a leader to liberate the region from foreign domination.

The Scandal and Legacy

The "Machiavel"

The author's name became a byword for cunning and amoral manipulation, appearing as a stock villain in the plays of Shakespeare and Marlowe.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What was Niccolò Machiavelli's political and employment status when he wrote The Prince and its dedicatory letter?

He was without political influence, unemployed, and had no hope of official rehabilitation.

To whom did Machiavelli dedicate The Prince?

To Lorenzo de' Medici.

What was the primary purpose of Machiavelli's dedicatory letter in The Prince?

To present his book as a gift to Lorenzo de' Medici, summarizing his knowledge of how rulers succeed, hoping to influence his own fate.

Describe the tone Machiavelli adopted in his dedicatory letter.

Humble but not servile; frank but calculated.

How did Machiavelli describe the value of his gift, The Prince, in the dedicatory letter?

Its value lay in its usefulness, similar to a map or a hunting falcon, rather than the wealth or status of the giver.

What did Machiavelli genuinely believe was the most valuable aspect of his gift, The Prince?

His years of experience and rigorous analysis of that experience, which he considered more valuable than anything money could buy.

Key Concepts (2/2)

According to Machiavelli's dedicatory letter, what kinds of gifts were traditionally presented to princes?

Horses, arms, cloth of gold, precious stones, and similar ornaments worthy of their greatness.

What did Machiavelli consider his most precious possession to offer Lorenzo de' Medici?

The knowledge of the actions of great men, acquired through long experience in contemporary affairs and a continual study of antiquity.

How did Machiavelli describe the literary style and embellishment of The Prince in his dedicatory letter?

What was Machiavelli's ultimate hope or desire for Lorenzo de' Medici, as expressed in the dedicatory letter?

That Lorenzo would attain the greatness which Fortune and his other attributes promised.

What personal hardship did Machiavelli mention suffering at the end of his dedicatory letter to Lorenzo?

He suffered a 'great and continued malignity of fortune.'
