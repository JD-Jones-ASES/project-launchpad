Key Concepts and Themes

Glossary of Important Terms and Figures (1/2)

Adversity

Difficulties or misfortune; Machiavelli argues a prince must be prepared for this so that "fortune" does not find him unprepared.

The Chase

Hunting; recommended as a way for a prince to harden his body and learn the "nature of localities."

Cyrus

The founder of the Persian Empire; his life, as recorded by Xenophon, served as the model for Scipio’s virtues.

Francesco Sforza

A man who rose from a private station to become Duke of Milan through his military skill.

Ignominy

Public shame or disgrace; specifically the "despised" status of a prince who is unarmed.

Glossary of Important Terms and Figures (2/2)

Philopoemen

Prince of the Achaeans, praised by writers for his constant mental preparation and reasoning regarding military tactics during times of peace.

Scipio (Scipio Africanus)

A Roman general who defeated Hannibal; noted for imitating the qualities of Cyrus to achieve glory.

Xenophon

A Greek historian and soldier whose work Cyropaedia (the life of Cyrus) was a key source for Machiavelli regarding the qualities of a great leader.

What is Machiavelli's most sweeping claim regarding a prince's singular focus?

He claims that 'a prince ought to have no other aim or thought, nor select anything else for his study, than war and its rules and discipline.'

Is Machiavelli's claim about war as a prince's sole profession an exaggeration?

No, Machiavelli means it literally, considering the art of war to be the prince's entire art, science, and philosophy.

According to Machiavelli, why is exclusive dedication to military affairs essential for a prince?

Because political power is ultimately coercive, and coercive power depends on the credible threat of organized force.
