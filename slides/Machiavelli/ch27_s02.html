Important Terms (1/3)

Exhortation

A communication, speech, or discourse that strongly urges or encourages someone to do something. In this context, Machiavelli's fervent plea for action.

Propitious

Giving or indicating a good chance of success; favorable. Machiavelli sees the current state of Italy as a propitious time for a new prince.

Virtù (Machiavellian)

A concept encompassing a prince's political skill, strength, courage, drive, and ability to act decisively and effectively to achieve goals and maintain the state. Distinct from conventional moral ...

Fortune

The unpredictable element of chance, luck, or fate. Machiavelli saw fortune as a powerful force but believed a virtuous prince could mitigate its effects.

Moses, Cyrus, Theseus

Biblical and historical figures cited by Machiavelli as examples of great leaders who liberated their people from oppression during times of extreme crisis.

Important Terms (2/3)

Illustrious House

Refers to the powerful and prominent Medici family, who were the rulers of Florence and to whom The Prince was dedicated. Machiavelli saw them as having the potential to unite Italy.

Lombardy, Tuscany, Kingdom (of Naples)

Regions of Italy that were suffering under foreign occupation or influence during Machiavelli's time, highlighting the widespread nature of the oppression.

Native Forces

An army composed of a state's own citizens, as opposed to mercenary (foreign hired) soldiers. Machiavelli believed these were essential for a strong, stable state.

Swiss Infantry

Renowned mercenary soldiers, famous for their pike formations, considered highly effective in Machiavelli's time.

Spanish Infantry

Another highly respected infantry force of the era, known for their discipline and use of swords and shields in close combat.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What significant shift occurs in Machiavelli's writing style in Chapter XXVI of The Prince?

Machiavelli shifts from the voice of a detached, systematic analyst to a passionate appeal for his country's liberation.

What is the overarching purpose of Machiavelli's final exhortation in Chapter XXVI?

To appeal for the liberation of Italy from foreign domination and internal disunity.

How does Machiavelli generally characterize his writing and analysis in the first twenty-five chapters of The Prince?

As cool, systematic, unsentimental, often ruthless, describing how states are conquered, held, and lost, and how to manage fortune.

According to Machiavelli in Chapter XXVI, what makes the moment 'propitious' for a new prince to emerge in Italy?

Italy's profound suffering, its enslaved and oppressed condition, has prepared the people for liberation, creating a genuine hunger for order, unity, and ind...

What historical figures does Machiavelli revisit in Chapter XXVI to support his argument about opportunity arising from suffering?

Moses, Cyrus, and Romulus, who each succeeded because they encountered a people whose suffering had prepared them for a liberator.

At its deepest level, what does Chapter XXVI reveal The Prince to be, beyond a handbook for political strategy?

It is a plea for Italian national liberation, intended as 'medicine for a sick state.'

Key Concepts (2/2)

To whom is all of Machiavelli's advice in The Prince ultimately addressed, according to the final chapter?

To an Italian prince (like the Medici, if capable) who possesses the virtù to unite the peninsula and drive out foreign powers.

What specific historical context is Machiavelli referring to when he mentions foreign armies devastating Italy?

The period since 1494, when foreign powers frequently invaded and controlled Italian territories.

How does Machiavelli's invocation of Moses in Chapter XXVI differ from earlier references in The Prince?

Earlier, Moses was an 'armed prophet' and strategic model; in Chapter XXVI, he is a 'type,' mirroring Italy's situation and suggesting a liberator for suffer...

What literary device does Machiavelli use at the end of Chapter XXVI to elevate his appeal?

He quotes Petrarch's patriotic poem, calling for Italy's liberation with 'ancient valor.'

How does the passionate appeal in Chapter XXVI challenge the 'cynical reading' of The Prince?

It shows Machiavelli is not a detached observer of amoral power politics, but deeply cares about Italy's fate, demonstrating moral and political stakes.

According to Chapter XXVI, what is the ultimate purpose behind Machiavelli's previously described ruthless advice (e.g., cruelty, deception)?

The ruthlessness is in service of Italian liberation and the creation of political conditions for freedom, not tyranny for its own sake.
