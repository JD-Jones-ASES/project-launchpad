Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: According to the text, why is it dangerous for a new prince to disarm his subjects?

> Disarming subjects offends them by showing distrust, which breeds hatred. It also forces the prince to rely on mercenaries, who are unreliable and insufficient for defense.

Q: Why are factions considered a "weakness" in a prince?

> Factions are only manageable in peace. In war, they cause a state to fall quickly because the weaker internal party will assist outside enemies.

Q: Why might a former enemy be more faithful to a prince than a longtime supporter?

> Former enemies often need the prince's support to maintain their own positions and feel a strong need to prove their loyalty through deeds to erase the prince's initial bad impression of them.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/2)

Q: Under what specific condition should a prince build a fortress?

> A prince should build a fortress only if he has more to fear from his own people than from foreign invaders.

Q: What happened to the Countess of Forli's defensive strategy when Cesare Borgia attacked?

> Her fortresses were of little value because the people, who were her enemy, allied themselves with the foreign attacker (Borgia).

Key Concepts (1/2)

What four practical questions does Machiavelli address regarding a prince's strategies in this section?

Should a prince arm his subjects or disarm them? Should he encourage factions? Should he try to win over former enemies? And should he build fortresses?

What is Machiavelli's general verdict on a prince arming his subjects?

It is generally wise.

According to Machiavelli, why is disarming subjects a serious mistake?

It signals distrust, breeds resentment, humiliates the people, and makes the prince dependent on untrustworthy hired soldiers or foreign auxiliaries.

What is Machiavelli's verdict on fostering factions among a prince's subjects?

It is generally dangerous and negative.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Why does Machiavelli argue against fostering factions, despite some princes believing it divides subjects to prevent rebellion?

He believes factions may be manageable in peacetime but cause cities to collapse in the face of an external enemy, with one faction often siding with the inv...

What broader principle of security does Machiavelli connect to his views on factions?

A prince's security ultimately rests on unity, not division, as a divided people cannot resist invasion, sustain war, or provide a stable tax base.

What is Machiavelli's nuanced verdict on building fortresses?

It depends; sometimes they are useful, sometimes not, and the decisive factor is the relationship between the prince and his people.

When does Machiavelli suggest a fortress can be useful?

When the prince fears his own subjects more than foreign enemies, providing a temporary refuge to organize a response.

Under what circumstances does Machiavelli consider fortresses useless or even detrimental?

If the prince is hated by his people, they will actively help external enemies reduce the fortress, turning it into a prison rather than a defense.

What does Machiavelli famously identify as 'the best fortress a prince can have'?

Not to be hated by the people.
