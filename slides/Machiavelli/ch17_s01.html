Powers and Principalities (Machiavelli)

Chapter 17, Section 1

Machiavelli on Liberality and Meanness

Study Slides

Parsimony as Functional Virtue

• Does not exhaust his resources.

• Does not need to oppress subjects with heavy taxation.

• Can fund the defense of the state.

• Can afford "true" generosity during genuine crises or civic celebrations.

Historical Applications

Figure. Strategy

Outcome

Pope Julius II. Used generosity to win the papacy (bribing/rewarding cardinals).

Once in power, he abandoned generosity for parsimony to govern effectively and fund his wars.

Moses, Cyrus, Romulus, Scipio. Distributed "spoils of conquest"—wealth seized from enemies rather than his own subjects.

Maintained a reputation for generosity without depleting his own treasury or oppressing his people.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Effectual Truth

The reality of how things function in practice, as opposed to idealized or imaginary versions of how things "ought" to be.

Liberality

The quality of being generous or giving away wealth freely.

Meanness (Parsimony)

Frugality or "stinginess"; the practice of strictly guarding and husbanding resources.

The Paradox of Liberality

The concept that the more a prince practices generosity, the less he is able to continue practicing it without becoming oppressive.

Spoils of Conquest

Wealth, property, or resources taken from an enemy during war, which Machiavelli suggests can be distributed freely without harming the prince’s own fiscal standing.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Unsustainable Fiscal Policy

A modern term used to describe the Machiavellian trap where a ruler's spending exceeds their long-term ability to gather resources without causing systemic harm.

He exercises liberality towards all those from whom he does not take (who are numberless), by not burdening them with taxes, thus leaving them unmolested.
