Machiavelli’s Dedicatory Letter to The Prince

Study Slides

The Context of the Dedication

Politically Isolated

He possessed no official influence or employment.

Seeking Rehabilitation

The letter serves as a formal "gift" to Lorenzo de' Medici, the man who held the power to restore Machiavelli’s career.

Strategically Humble

The tone is described as humble but not servile, frank yet highly calculated.

The Nature of the "Gift"

The Content

The "knowledge of the actions of great men."

The Source

This knowledge was acquired through two primary methods: "long experience in contemporary affairs" and a "continual study of antiquity."

Stylistic Choices

Unembellished

Void of "swelling or magnificent words" or "extrinsic allurements."

Substantial

He intends for the work to be judged solely on the "truth of the matter" and the "weightiness of the theme" rather than its linguistic decoration.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Antiquity

The ancient past, specifically the classical civilizations of Greece and Rome, which Machiavelli studied to understand political patterns.

Contemporary Affairs

The current political events and diplomatic maneuvers of Machiavelli's own time.

Diligence

Persistent and careful effort; Machiavelli emphasizes his "prolonged diligence" in reflecting on the actions of great men.

Extrinsic Allurements

External decorations or rhetorical flourishes added to a text to make it more attractive, which Machiavelli purposefully omitted.

Fortune

A personified force of luck or fate. Machiavelli references it both as a provider of opportunity for Lorenzo and as a source of "malignity" (hardship) for himself.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Magnificence

A formal title of respect used to address a high-ranking noble or ruler, specifically Lorenzo de' Medici.

Malignity

The state or character of being malevolent or having ill-will; used by Machiavelli to describe his persistent bad luck.

Presumption

Behavior that is perceived as overstepping one’s bounds or being overconfident; Machiavelli addresses the potential "presumption" of a commoner advising a prince.

Rounded Periods

Highly structured, rhythmic, and complex sentences common in formal oratory and literature, which Machiavelli avoided for the sake of clarity and directness.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What crucial political lesson did Machiavelli learn from the rise and fall of Savonarola?

Enthusiasm and moral authority are no substitute for organized force, military power, allies, and an institutional base of power.

What position did Machiavelli secure in the new Florentine republic after Savonarola's execution in 1498?

Secretary to the Second Chancery and to the Ten of Liberty and Peace.

What was the primary function of the 'Ten of Liberty and Peace' in the Florentine republic?

It was one of the key governing committees responsible for military affairs and foreign policy.

How did Machiavelli's diplomatic missions contribute significantly to his political education?

Name two powerful rulers or figures Machiavelli observed during his diplomatic missions.

King Louis XII of France, Cesare Borgia (Duke Valentino), Pope Julius II ('Warrior Pope'), or Emperor Maximilian.

What characteristics of Cesare Borgia particularly fascinated (and repelled) Machiavelli?

His military skill, calculated cruelty, and masterful deception in carving out a new principality.
