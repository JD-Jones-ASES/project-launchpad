Ferdinand II of Aragon: The Case Study of a Model Prince

The Conquest of Granada (1492)

This final campaign of the Christian Reconquista allowed Ferdinand to build a formidable military and unify his subjects under a shared religious cause.

The Expulsion of the Jews (1492)

While presented as a religious act, Machiavelli identifies this as a strategic use of religion to seize wealth and provide political cover for state actions.

Expansionism in North Africa and Italy

Military campaigns in Oran, Tripoli, and the Kingdom of Naples (1503–04) ensured Spain's dominance in Italy and kept the nobility occupied with military service rather than domestic conspiracy.

The Politics of Spectacle

Prevention of Conspiracy

When a prince is constantly achieving something spectacular, his enemies are too busy observing or fearing him to plot against him.

The Danger of Neutrality

The Problem of Suspicion

Neutrality pleases no one. Both the victor and the loser will view a neutral neighbor with suspicion.

The Benefit of Commitment

Decisive action, even if the chosen side loses, builds a reputation for reliability and courage. If the chosen side wins, the prince gains a powerful, indebted ally.

Strategic Patronage and Domestic Management

Honor Talent

Recognize excellence in arts, commerce, and industry.

Provide Entertainment

Organize festivals and spectacles to entertain the populace during appropriate seasons.

Maintain Visibility

Associate with guilds and professional associations to ensure the prince is seen as a protector and patron, rather than an isolated figure in a palace.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: Why did Machiavelli consider Ferdinand II of Aragon to be the "model prince" of his era?

Ferdinand was considered the model prince because he was bold, decisive, and highly skilled at using religion as a political instrument to transform fragmented kingdoms into the most powerful state in Europe.

Q: What was the strategic benefit of the conquest of Granada for Ferdinand’s internal politics?

The conquest allowed Ferdinand to build his military machine and unify his diverse kingdoms under a singular, common religious cause.

Q: According to Machiavelli, what happens the moment a prince stops performing "impressive" acts?

The moment a prince stops being impressive and the "spectacle" of his leadership fades, the political space for conspiracy and plotting by rivals reopens.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/2)

Q: Why does Machiavelli argue that a prince who chooses a losing side in a conflict is still better off than one who remains neutral?

Choosing a side, even a losing one, earns the prince a reputation for reliability and courage. It demonstrates that his commitments are real, which potential allies value in the long term.

Q: How do festivals and public spectacles serve a prince’s political interests?

These events provide collective pleasures that bind the population to the state and ensure the prince is seen as a visible, active patron of the city’s civic life.
