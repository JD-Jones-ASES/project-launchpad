Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: Why is Chapter XV considered the "philosophical turning point" of *The Prince*?

Chapter XV is where Machiavelli stops his preliminary analysis and directly declares his unique method and purpose. It is the moment he explicitly rejects previous political traditions to establish a new framework for political realism.

Q: What is Machiavelli’s primary criticism of the "mirrors for princes" literature?

He argues that these books are useless because they describe how an ideal ruler *should* behave rather than how actual rulers *do* behave. He believes that following such idealist advice in a world of non-virtuous rivals leads to destruction.

Q: According to the text, what happens to a ruler who tries to be good at all times?

A ruler who attempts to practice all virtues in a world where others do not follow those same norms will be exploited and eventually destroyed.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Effectual Truth (*verità effettuale*)

The actual, practical truth of how things work in reality, as opposed to how they are imagined or how they ought to be.

Imaginary Version

Philosophies or theologies that describe ideal states and rulers (e.g., Plato’s Republic) that have never actually existed.

Mirrors for Princes

A genre of Renaissance literature intended to provide moral and behavioral guidance to rulers, which Machiavelli critiques as unrealistic.

Necessity (*necessità*)

The external pressures and requirements of political survival that may force a ruler to act against traditional moral virtues.

Political Science

A discipline, emerging with Machiavelli, that analyzes political stability, security, and power as independent from moral or religious prescriptions.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Virtue (Classical Context)

Qualities such as mercy, faith, and sincerity that traditional philosophers (like Aquinas and Aristotle) argued were essential for a good ruler.
