Hereditary Principalities in Machiavelli’s The Prince

Study Slides

The Prince-Subject Relationship

Reduced Necessity to Offend

Unlike a new ruler who must often use force or disruptive changes to establish authority, a hereditary prince has less reason to harm his subjects.

Natural Disposition

Because the prince has fewer reasons to "offend," he is more likely to be loved. Subjects are naturally well-disposed toward a family that has ruled them for generations.

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to Machiavelli, why does he dedicate so little discussion to hereditary principalities in The Prince?

Because, in his view, they present very few problems and are much easier to hold, requiring less detailed commentary.

What is the first interlocking reason Machiavelli gives for the stability of hereditary states?

Custom and legitimacy. People have grown up under the ruling family, viewing their rule as a natural fact woven into daily life.

Define "legitimacy" as a political concept, according to the text.

Legitimacy is the general belief among the governed that the ruler has the right to rule.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What level of ability does Machiavelli suggest is sufficient for a hereditary prince to maintain his state?

"Ordinary ability" is sufficient, provided the prince does not actively give his subjects reason to hate him.

What does Machiavelli identify as the greatest danger for a hereditary prince?

His own bad judgment, rather than external enemies.

What practical rule does Machiavelli draw for hereditary princes regarding established customs?

The hereditary prince should not change established customs unless forced to by circumstance.

What is the hereditary prince's primary job regarding the inherited system of governance?
