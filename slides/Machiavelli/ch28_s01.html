Important Terms (1/2)

Congress of Vienna (1814–1815)

A historical conference that reshaped Europe after Napoleon, cited as an example of the "balance-of-power politics" that Machiavelli analyzed.

Principalities

The various types of states (hereditary, mixed, acquired by force or fortune, etc.) that The Prince analyzes in terms of how they are acquired and maintained.

Mercenaries and Auxiliaries

Foreign or hired soldiers; Machiavelli considered them useless or dangerous, advocating instead for national armies composed of citizens.

Citizen-soldiers

An army composed of a prince's own citizens, loyal to their state, which Machiavelli argued was the most secure military foundation.

Effectual Truth

Machiavelli's term for the actual, practical reality of how politics functions, distinct from theoretical ideals or moral prescriptions.

Important Terms (2/2)

Virtù

In Machiavelli's context, refers to a leader's skill, energy, courage, foresight, and ability to adapt to circumstances; not necessarily moral virtue.

Fortuna

The unpredictable force of fortune, chance, or fate that influences political events, often beyond human control.

Political Realism

The school of thought, founded by Machiavelli, that analyzes political behavior and phenomena as they are, rather than as they morally ought to be.

The Discourses on Livy

Another significant work by Machiavelli, which presents a more favorable view of republican government and free citizens, offering a contrast to the crisis-driven focus of The Prince.
