The Models of Success: Marcus Aurelius vs. Septimius Severus

Emperor. Tenure

Method of Success

Marcus Aurelius. AD 161–180

Legitimate succession, philosophical restraint, and military competence.

Septimius Severus. AD 193–211

Military coup, ruthless cunning, and the "fox-and-lion" method.

The Models of Failure

Commodus (180–192)

The son of Marcus Aurelius, he was hated for his personal cruelty and despised for behavior seen as beneath imperial dignity, such as performing as a gladiator.

Caracalla (211–217)

The son of Severus, he was brilliant in battle but recklessly cruel in peace (e.g., massacring Alexandria), leading to his murder by his own guard.

Maximinus (235–238)

A soldier of humble origins who was despised by the Senate and the people because he was seen as an outsider without a legitimate claim to dignity.

Pertinax (193)

Attempted to restore order and discipline but was killed by soldiers who were unwilling to submit to his authority.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Contempt

A state of being despised; in a political context, it is the result of a leader appearing weak, indecisive, or cowardly.

Fox-and-Lion Method

A strategy of governance involving the use of both cunning/deception (the fox) and overwhelming force (the lion).

Hatred

Active fury or resentment from subjects, typically triggered by the prince seizing property or violating the honor of their families.

Legitimacy

The quality of having a recognized right to rule, often through birth or legal succession, which can protect a prince from the need to use cruelty.

Prudence

The practical wisdom to navigate political tensions and choose the correct course of action based on current circumstances.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Soldier-Emperors

Roman rulers whose power was primarily derived from the support and loyalty of the military legions rather than traditional civic institutions.

Virtù

The skill, energy, and strength of character required for a prince to maintain his state and achieve great things, often distinct from traditional moral virtue.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is Machiavelli's central argument in Chapter XIX of The Prince?

A prince who is hated cannot survive, and a prince who is despised invites conspiracies that will destroy him.

According to Machiavelli, what specific actions make a prince hated?

Being rapacious and violating the property and women of his subjects.

What personal qualities make a prince contemptible?

Being considered fickle, frivolous, effeminate, mean-spirited, or irresolute.

What are the two main fears a prince ought to have?

One fear from within, on account of his subjects, and the other from without, on account of external powers.

How does a prince defend against external threats?

By being well armed and having good allies.
