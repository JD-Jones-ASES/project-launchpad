The Nature of Mixed Principalities

The Deception of Change

Subjects often willingly change their rulers in the hope of improving their condition. However, they are frequently deceived, finding through experience that their situation has worsened.

Necessary Offense

A new prince is forced to burden new subjects with the presence of his military and other hardships incidental to the acquisition.

The Roman Model (Success)

• They established colonies.

• They maintained friendly relations with minor powers (the Achaeans and Ætolians) without allowing them to increase their strength.

• They humbled major powers (the kingdom of Macedonia and Antiochus).

• They never deferred war to avoid immediate trouble, preferring to fight Philip and Antiochus in Greece rather than eventually having to fight them in Italy.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Alexander (the Great)

King of Macedon (356–323 BC) used by Machiavelli as a model for how centralized governments are hard to conquer but easy to hold once the ruling family is extinguished.

Barons

Hereditary nobility in France; Machiavelli identifies them as the reason France is easy to conquer (via bribery) but hard to hold (due to their constant rebellions).

Cesare Borgia (Duke Valentino)

The son of Pope Alexander VI; described by Machiavelli as bold, ruthless, and strategically brilliant, despite being eventually undone by bad fortune.

Composite State

A state composed of multiple parts, such as an ancient state that has annexed new territories.

France

Machiavelli’s model of a decentralized state governed by a king and barons.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Mixed Principality

A principality that is not entirely new but is added as a "member" to an existing state.

Pisa

A Florentine subject city that rebelled in 1494; its prolonged resistance was a major focus of Machiavelli’s military and political career.

Principality

A state ruled by a single prince or sovereign, distinguished from a republic.

Romagna

A region in north-central Italy seized by Cesare Borgia between 1499 and 1503.

Turk (The Ottoman Empire)

Machiavelli’s model of a highly centralized state; difficult to conquer because of its unity, but easy to hold once the central ruler is defeated.
