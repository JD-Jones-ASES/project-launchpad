Ineffective Mercy

The Superiority of Fear over Love

Human Nature

Men are generally ungrateful, fickle, false, cowardly, and covetous.

The Fragility of Love

Love is maintained by a "link of obligation" which men break whenever it serves their advantage.

The Stability of Fear

Fear is maintained by a "dread of punishment" which is a constant and reliable deterrent.

Avoiding Hatred

The Priority of Property

Machiavelli notes that men forget the death of a father more quickly than the loss of their inheritance (patrimony).

Military Leadership and Discipline

Hannibal

His "inhuman cruelty" and "boundless valour" kept his diverse army united and free from dissension, even in bad fortune.

Scipio

Despite his virtues, his "too great forbearance" led to army rebellion in Spain. His "easy nature" was criticized by the Senate as a corrupting influence on military discipline.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the most famous question Machiavelli poses about a ruler's relationship with their subjects?

Is it better to be loved or feared?

What is Machiavelli's initial, conventional answer to whether it's better to be loved or feared?

It would be best to be both loved and feared.

If a prince must choose between being loved and feared, which does Machiavelli argue is safer?

It is safer to be feared.

What is Machiavelli's 'clear-eyed assessment' of human nature that underlies his argument for fear?

People are primarily self-interested, not simply evil.

According to Machiavelli, why is love less reliable than fear as a foundation for a ruler's security?

Love is conditional, based on mutual benefit and obligation which, 'owing to the baseness of men,' is broken for their advantage. Fear is preserved by the dr...

What core distinction does Machiavelli make about the source of love versus the source of fear?

Love depends on the other person's choice, while fear depends on the prince's capacity to punish. A prince's security should rest on what he controls.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What does Machiavelli mean by saying effective governance cannot be founded on 'contingent goodwill'?

It means that relying on the voluntary affection or good intentions of others is too unstable and unreliable for a ruler's security, as goodwill can change b...

How did Cesare Borgia's actions in subduing the Romagna demonstrate Machiavelli's argument about the effectiveness of cruelty?

Borgia used decisive, severe methods like public executions and murder, which Machiavelli argued produced a more humane and stable outcome than extended diso...

What lesson did Machiavelli draw from the Florentines' 'clemency' in Pistoia?

The Florentines' avoidance of cruelty led to escalating factional conflict and widespread civil violence, causing more suffering than Borgia's decisive force.

What is Machiavelli's conclusion about a prince who avoids necessary harshness to gain a reputation for mercy?

Such a prince does not earn the title of merciful, but rather negligent, as their hesitation leads to greater suffering from disorder.

Who was Hannibal Barca, and how did Machiavelli use him as an example?

Hannibal was a Carthaginian general whose 'inhuman cruelty' and ruthless discipline held his diverse army together for 15 years through harsh campaigns.

What is the crucial 'critical limit' to Machiavelli's argument for a prince cultivating fear?

A prince must never be hated. Fear is useful, but hatred is fatal.
