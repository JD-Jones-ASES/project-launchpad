Glossary of Key Terms

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to Machiavelli, what is the primary reason hereditary princes have the easiest time governing?

Long-established custom does most of the governing for him.

What is the main responsibility of a hereditary prince to maintain his state?

He needs only to avoid disrupting the order his ancestors built and not transgress their customs.

In Machiavelli's view, how do hereditary states compare to new principalities in terms of difficulty to govern?

There are fewer difficulties in holding hereditary states than new ones.

What two actions are sufficient for a hereditary prince of 'average powers' to maintain his state?

Not transgressing the customs of his ancestors and dealing prudently with circumstances as they arise.

Under what specific condition can a hereditary prince be deprived of his state?

Only by some extraordinary and excessive force.

If a hereditary prince is unlawfully deprived of his state, how does Machiavelli suggest he might regain it?

He will regain it whenever anything sinister happens to the usurper.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Who does Machiavelli provide as an example of a hereditary prince who maintained his rule due to long establishment?

The Duke of Ferrara, who withstood attacks from the Venetians and Pope Julius.

Why does a hereditary prince typically have less cause and less necessity to offend his subjects?

Because long-established customs simplify governance, reducing the need for new, potentially unpopular actions.

What is the natural disposition of subjects towards a hereditary prince, provided he avoids 'extraordinary vices'?

They will be naturally well-disposed towards him and tend to love him.

What happens to the 'memories and motives that make for change' in a state with antiquity and duration of rule?

They are lost, contributing to the stability of the hereditary principality.

What warning does Machiavelli give about allowing 'change' in a state?

One change always leaves the 'toothing' for another, implying that change can breed further instability.

What type of state does Machiavelli explicitly state he will not discuss in this part of 'The Prince'?

Republics, as he has written about them at length elsewhere.
