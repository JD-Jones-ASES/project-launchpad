The Pre-Expansion Era

Glossary of Key Terms and Figures (1/2)

Alexander VI

The Pope who first demonstrated the effectiveness of using money and arms to expand the Church's temporal power.

Capacity (Virtù)

One of the two methods (alongside fortune) by which a principality is initially acquired.

Duke Valentino

The agent and instrument used by Alexander VI to carry out military and territorial expansions.

Ecclesiastical Principality

A state held by the Church, unique because it is maintained by religious tradition rather than military or political effort.

Julius II

A successor to Alexander VI who further strengthened the Church, gained Bologna, and worked to drive the French out of Italy.

Glossary of Key Terms and Figures (2/2)

Leo X

The Pope at the time of the text's conclusion, noted for inheriting a powerful pontificate and expected to enhance it through virtue.

Orsini and Colonnesi

The two primary rival factions of Roman barons whose constant conflict historically kept the Papacy weak.

Temporal Power

The secular, political, and military power of the Church, as opposed to its spiritual authority.

Venetians

A major Italian power that, along with the Roman barons, served to restrain the territorial ambitions of the Pope prior to the reign of Alexander VI.

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to Machiavelli, what is the fundamental difference between ecclesiastical principalities and all other forms of states?

Ecclesiastical principalities are maintained without either ability or fortune, sustained by the ancient ordinances of religion, unlike all other states.

How does Machiavelli describe the acquisition and maintenance of ecclesiastical principalities?

They are acquired by ability or fortune but maintained without either, upheld by the powerful ordinances of religion.

What unique characteristic allows the prince of an ecclesiastical principality to retain power regardless of their conduct?

What is the significance of Machiavelli's "understated but unmistakable" conclusion that ecclesiastical states are "exalted and maintained by God"?

Who are the two popes Machiavelli credits with transforming the papacy into a major military-political power in Renaissance Italy?

Pope Alexander VI and Pope Julius II.

How did Pope Alexander VI contribute to the expansion and consolidation of the Papal States?

He used his son, Cesare Borgia, to expand and unify the Papal States through force, cunning, and treachery, achieving what legitimate governance could not.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What role did Cesare Borgia play in the consolidation of the Papal States under Alexander VI?

He acted as Alexander VI's agent, using political violence to unify and expand loosely controlled territories into a unified, well-defended state.

How did Pope Julius II build upon the gains made by Alexander VI and Cesare Borgia?

He enlarged the Papal States further, moving against Venice, France, and other powers that threatened papal territory, often by leading armies himself.

What specific action exemplifies Pope Julius II's role as the "Warrior Pope"?

He personally commanded troops at the siege of Mirandola in 1511, directing artillery fire while in armor.

What was Pope Julius II's nickname, and what did it signify?

He was known as the "Warrior Pope" (il Papa Terribile), signifying his personal leadership of military campaigns to defend and expand the Papal States.

How did Machiavelli regard Julius II's leadership style, particularly concerning the concept of Fortune?

Machiavelli saw him as an example of a ruler whose impetuosity matched the demands of his moment, a theme developed in Chapter XXV on Fortune.

What did Julius II's military campaigns demonstrate about the Church's power?

They showed that the Church could project real political and military power, not merely spiritual authority.
