Powers and Principalities (Machiavelli)

Chapter 21, Section 1

Machiavelli on Fortresses and State Strategy

Study Slides

The Arming of Subjects

Trust and Resentment

Disarming people signals a lack of trust, which breeds resentment and humiliation among the populace.

Dependence on Outsiders

A prince who disarms his subjects must rely on hired mercenaries or foreign auxiliaries, neither of whom are fully reliable.

Mutual Dependence

Arming citizens creates a bond where the people become partners in the state's defense. A prince leading his own armed citizens is more formidable and secure.

The Dangers of Factionalism

Ineffectiveness in War

Factions may be manageable during peacetime, but they cause a state to collapse during an external invasion.

The Invitation to Conquest

In a divided city, one faction will invariably side with an invader to defeat their internal rivals.

Foundation of Security

A prince’s security rests on unity. A divided people cannot provide a stable tax base or the collective strength needed to resist a long war.

The Utility of Fortresses

When Useful

Fortresses are useful only if a prince fears his own subjects more than foreign enemies, providing a temporary refuge to organize a response.

The Limitation

If the people hate the prince, no fortress can save him. Physical walls are vulnerable to being scaled, undermined, or starved out.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Auxiliaries

Foreign troops borrowed from an ally to assist a prince in war; regarded by Machiavelli as untrustworthy.

Civil Principality

A state where the prince has ascended to power with the favor of his fellow citizens.

Faction

A small, organized, dissenting group within a larger one, often based on family or district loyalties, which Machiavelli argues weakens the state.

Fortress

A military stronghold or physical fortification; Machiavelli argues its utility is secondary to popular loyalty.

Mercenaries

Soldiers who fight for pay rather than for loyalty to a state or prince.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Mutual Dependence

The relationship created when a prince arms his subjects, making them partners in the defense of the state.
