Major Literary Works (1512–1527)

*The Prince* (1513)

A short treatise on the nature of principalities and the methods required for a ruler to acquire and maintain power.

*Discourses on Livy* (1513–1517)

A lengthy analysis of the first ten books of Titus Livy’s history, focusing on republicanism and political stability.

*The Art of War* (1521)

A study regarding the organization and necessity of military force.

*Florentine Histories* (1525)

A commissioned historical account of the city of Florence.

*La Mandragola*

A comedy regarded as one of the finest plays of the Italian Renaissance.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Cesare Borgia (Duke Valentino)

A ruthless leader who carved out a principality in central Italy; Machiavelli found his combination of military skill and deception both fascinating and repelling.

Girolamo Savonarola

A Dominican friar who ruled Florence (1494–1498) through religious fervor but was eventually executed due to his lack of military and institutional support.

Lorenzo de' Medici ("The Magnificent")

The de facto ruler of Florence (1449–1492) and a major patron of the arts whose death ended a period of stability in Italy.

Palazzo Vecchio

The seat of government in Florence where Machiavelli served as a civil servant.

Pope Julius II

Known as the "Warrior Pope," he personally led armies and was noted by Machiavelli for his audacity and ability to bully European powers.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Sant'Andrea in Percussina

The location of Machiavelli’s family farm where he lived in exile and wrote his most famous works.

Second Chancery

The government office where Machiavelli served as Secretary, focusing on military affairs and foreign policy.

Tanto nomini nullum par elogium

The Latin inscription on Machiavelli's tomb, meaning "No praise can be adequate to so great a name."

The Ten of Liberty and Peace

A key governing committee of the Florentine republic responsible for foreign policy and military matters.
