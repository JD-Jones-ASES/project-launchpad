Chapter 1, Section 4

The Argument Unpacked

Study Slides

The Diagnosis: A Society Gone Soft

The Prescription: Martial Virtue

• He is not arguing that any particular war was just

• He is not claiming that the cause must be right or the outcome favorable

• His claim is more radical: the act of faith itself — regardless of cause or outcome — is the highest thing a human being can do

What Holmes Leaves Out

Who profits from war

The economic dimension — who pays, who benefits, who bears the cost — is entirely absent. This is precisely the question Smedley Butler will place at the center of his argument forty years later.

The politics of specific wars

Holmes treats war as a permanent feature of human existence, not as a political decision that can be evaluated on its merits.

The suffering of common soldiers

Holmes' battlefield descriptions emphasize courage and transcendence, not pain and futility. The soldier in his speech is a figure of nobility, not a victim.

The officer-enlisted gap

Holmes was a Harvard-educated officer from a wealthy family. His experience, while genuinely dangerous, was not identical to that of the working-class soldiers who made up the bulk of the army.

Key Concepts

Ethos

The rhetorical appeal based on the speaker's authority or credibility

Martial virtue

The qualities cultivated by combat — courage, obedience, faith — that Holmes argues are essential to civilization

Unstated assumption

A premise an argument depends on but does not openly defend

Smedley Butler

Future critic of war whose argument will focus on economic exploitation rather than spiritual transcendence

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is Holmes' 'diagnosis' of American society in 1895?

It has become devoted to comfort, profit, and the avoidance of pain — trading hard warrior virtues for soft material pleasures.

What three specific targets does Holmes critique in his cultural diagnosis?

The worship of wealth (Vanderbilt), the revolt against suffering (socialism, cruelty-prevention societies), and the corrosive effects of scientific materialism.

What is Holmes' 'prescription' for the disease of Gilded Age complacency?

The virtues that war cultivates: courage in the face of death, obedience to duty, and the capacity for faith — acting on belief one cannot prove.

What exactly is 'the soldier's faith' as Holmes defines it at the core of his argument?

The willingness to die for a cause one does not fully understand, in a plan one did not choose, under orders one cannot question.

Is Holmes arguing that any particular war was just?

No — he deliberately avoids judging specific wars. His claim is that the act of faith itself, regardless of cause or outcome, is the highest human act.

What are the two principal sources of evidence Holmes uses?

1) His own combat experience (the authority of the witness); 2) Cultural and literary tradition (Beowulf, Tennyson, Swinburne, Spenser).

Key Concepts (2/2)

What rhetorical term describes the authority Holmes gains as a wounded veteran speaking from experience?

Ethos — the authority of the witness.

What is Holmes' core argument claim as summarized in the argument map?

War cultivates virtues that a peaceful, commercial society cannot produce — and those virtues are essential to a civilization's health.

What is the unstated assumption underlying Holmes' entire argument?

That the virtues developed through war cannot be cultivated by any other means.

What economic dimension does Holmes leave entirely out of his analysis?

Who profits from war — who pays, who benefits, who bears the cost.

Whose argument forty years later will place economic profit at the center of a critique of war?

Smedley Butler.

Does Holmes treat war as a political decision that can be evaluated on its merits?

No — he treats war as a permanent feature of human existence, making the justice of any particular war beside the point.
