War: A Debate in Primary Sources

Chapter 4, Section 3

Smedley Butler’s Three Proposals for Ending the War Racket

Study Slides

I. Summary of the Three Proposals (1/3)

The Claim

The government must conscript the nation’s capital, industry, and labor before drafting any individuals for military service.

The Mechanism

All incomes—including those of corporate executives, bankers, politicians, and generals—must be limited to thirty dollars per month, the same pay received by a soldier.

The Logic

If the financial incentive is removed, the pressure to lobby for war disappears.

Key Evidence

During World War I, corporate profits skyrocketed by hundreds or thousands of percent, while soldiers earned only thirty dollars a month (half of which was often deducted).

The Claim

The decision to declare war should be restricted to a direct vote by those who will actually face combat.

I. Summary of the Three Proposals (2/3)

The Mechanism

Only men of military age who are physically fit for service would be permitted to vote on whether the nation goes to war.

The Logic

Those who bear the ultimate consequence of the decision (death or injury) should be the ones to make it, rather than older individuals who profit from the conflict.

Key Evidence

In World War I, four million American men were sent to fight by a Congressional declaration without being consulted.

The Claim

The United States military should be legally restricted to home defense.

The Mechanism

Naval vessels would be limited to operating within 200 miles of the coastline; aircraft would be limited to 500 miles; the Army would never leave American territory.

I. Summary of the Three Proposals (3/3)

The Logic

A nation that physically cannot project power beyond its borders cannot engage in offensive or provocative warfare.

Key Evidence

The explosion of the *U.S.S. Maine* in Havana Harbor (1898) triggered the Spanish-American War; Butler argues that if the ship had been restricted to American waters, the war might have been avoided.

II. Critical Analysis and Debates

Feature. Smedley Butler's View

Woodrow Wilson's View

Military Scope. Restricted strictly to home defense.

Capable of projecting power abroad.

Definition of Threat. Threats are manufactured by profiteers; aggression is the goal of the racket.

Genuine threats (like autocracy or submarine warfare) originate far from U.S. shores.

National Interest. No legitimate interest exists beyond the immediate coastline.

Security is served by defending democratic principles and global commerce.

Moral Stance. Foreign engagement is always a racket.

Neutrality in the face of aggression is complicity.

V. Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Conscript Capital

The policy of seizing control of a nation’s wealth and industry for war purposes, specifically by capping all incomes and profits.

Feasibility

The practical capability of a plan to be implemented, taking into account political, economic, and human constraints.

Limited Plebiscite

A direct vote on a specific issue (in this case, war) that is restricted to a certain segment of the population (those of military age).

Policy Proposal

A specific, concrete plan of action designed to solve a public problem, moving beyond general principles to define the "who, what, and how."

Profit Motive

The desire for financial gain, which Butler identifies as the primary driver of military conflict.

V. Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

War Racket

Butler’s term for a system where private interests profit from military conflict while the general population and soldiers bear the costs.
