Rhetorical Analysis of Woodrow Wilson’s 1917 Address to Congress: A Study Guide

Study Slides

The Presidential Address as Genre

The U.S. Congress

Required to vote on the proposal.

The American Public

Required to provide popular support for the war.

Allied Governments

Required to see proof of genuine American commitment.

The German Government

Required to understand America’s intent to fight.

Posterity

The historical lens through which the decision will be judged.

The Rhetorical Tool of Omission

Economic Interests

Wilson avoids mentioning that American banks had loaned approximately $2.3 billion to Allied powers compared to only $27 million to Central Powers.

Practical Costs

There is no mention of the military draft, potential casualty rates, or the expected duration of the conflict.

Strategic Purpose

By omitting these details, Wilson avoids the "racket" thesis later proposed by Butler, focusing instead on abstract moral principles.

The Three Forms of Authority (Ethos)

Oliver Wendell Holmes (1895)

The authority of the veteran who found nobility in suffering ("I have been there, and it was worth it").

Smedley Butler (1935)

The authority of the veteran who feels disillusioned ("I have been there, and it was a fraud").

Woodrow Wilson (1917)

The authority of the peacemaker who has exhausted all alternatives ("I have tried everything else, and nothing worked").

Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: How does the "Presidential Address" genre differ from a campaign speech?

The presidential address is a formal act of state and a request for legislative action, whereas a campaign speech is not an official act of government.

Q: Why was it rhetorically necessary for Wilson to sound "reluctant" to enter the war?

He needed to avoid alarming the "peace faction" in Congress and the millions of American voters who supported him specifically because he had previously kept the country out of the war.

Q: What specific economic data does the text mention that Wilson omitted from his speech?

Wilson omitted the fact that American banks had loaned $2.3 billion to the Allies and only $27 million to the Central Powers.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/2)

Q: What is the "weakest link" in Wilson’s logical chain, according to the analysis?

The transition from step 2 to step 3: the leap from a specific dispute over shipping rights and telegrams to a universal struggle between democracy and autocracy.

Q: How does Wilson's use of pathos differ from that of Holmes or Butler?

Wilson’s pathos is "architectural" and controlled. He does not use vivid pictures of heroism or maimed veterans; instead, he asks the audience to feel the weight of the decision and the justice of the cause.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Ethos

The personal authority or credibility of a speaker. In Wilson’s case, his ethos is built on his reputation as a "reluctant warrior" who exhausted all peaceful options.

Logos

The logical structure or "architecture" of an argument. Wilson’s logos relies on a chain of necessity leading from German aggression to a moral duty to defend democracy.

Omission (Rhetorical)

The deliberate exclusion of specific information or perspectives to shape the audience's understanding.

Pathos

The use of emotional appeal. Wilson uses "controlled pathos," weaving emotion into the structural gravity of the speech rather than using graphic imagery.

Presidential Address

A formal speech by a head of government to a legislative body or the nation, functioning as an official act of state and a request for action.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Reluctant Warrior

A rhetorical persona used by Wilson to position himself as a man of peace driven to fight only by forces beyond his control.
