Chapter 1, Section 2

Analysis of "The Soldier’s Faith" by Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr.

Study Slides

Key Concepts and Philosophies

The Ruling Values

Holmes observes that commerce and wealth have replaced honor as the primary measures of human value.

The Rejection of Suffering

He targets the "comfortable classes" and specific social movements—such as socialism and societies for the prevention of cruelty—as evidence of a widespread revolt against suffering and danger.

Scientific Materialism

Holmes argues that scientific materialism has eroded religious certainties without providing a new moral foundation, contributing to a corrosive effect on the national character.

Definition

Martial virtue includes courage, discipline, self-sacrifice, loyalty, and the ability to act decisively under conditions of extreme uncertainty.

Argument Structure and Evidence

Critical Omissions

Economics

He does not discuss the financial costs of war or who profits from conflict.

Politics

He treats war as a permanent human condition rather than a result of specific political decisions or justifications.

The Common Soldier

His descriptions emphasize transcendence and nobility rather than the physical pain, futility, or victimization of the rank-and-file soldier.

Class Disparity

As a Harvard-educated officer, Holmes does not address whether his conclusions apply to working-class soldiers whose experiences differed significantly from his own.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Ethos

A rhetorical appeal based on the speaker's character, credibility, or authority.

Gilded Age

A period in American history characterized by rapid economic growth, the worship of wealth, and a perceived decline in traditional moral values.

Martial Virtue

Qualities such as courage, discipline, and self-sacrifice believed to be cultivated through military service.

Scientific Materialism

A worldview focusing on physical matter and laws, which Holmes believed dissolved traditional religious and moral certainties.

The Soldier’s Faith

The supreme expression of nobility defined by acting on belief without certainty and sacrificing without the guarantee of reward.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Warrior Ideal

The cultural inheritance of Western civilization that values the code of the gentleman and the ethic of honor.
