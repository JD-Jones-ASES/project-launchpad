Chapter 2, Section 1

Smedley Butler’s "War Is a Racket" (Chapters 1–2)

Study Slides

I. Executive Summary and Central Thesis

The Nature of the Racket

A racket is defined as something that is not what it seems to the public. War is the only racket where profits are measured in dollars and losses in human lives.

Economic Disparity

While the general public pays the bill through taxation, "blood gains," and physical suffering, a handful of industrialists, bankers, and speculators reap massive fortunes.

The Cycle of Conflict

Victorious nations acquire territory that is then exploited by the same few who profited from the war, while the public shoulders the ongoing economic instability.

The Human Toll

Butler contrasts the "bloodless" profits of the elite with the "horrible accounting" of the soldier: gravestones, mangled bodies, shattered minds, and broken homes.

III. Examples of Military Waste and Profiteering (1/2)

Footwear

The government purchased 35,000,000 pairs of hobnailed shoes for 4,000,000 soldiers. After the war, 25,000,000 pairs remained unused.

Cavalry Equipment

Hundreds of thousands of McClellan saddles were purchased for use overseas, despite the fact that there was no American cavalry in France.

Mosquito Netting

20,000,000 nets were sold to the government for soldiers in France, where there were no mosquitoes. An additional 40,000,000 yards of netting were also sold.

Aviation

$1,000,000,000 was spent on airplane engines that never left the ground; not a single plane or motor ordered with that money reached a battle in France.

Nautical Waste

$635,000,000 was spent on wooden ships that were unable to float and eventually sank.

III. Examples of Military Waste and Profiteering (2/2)

Miscellaneous

6,000 buckboards (carriages) were purchased for colonels but never used; twelve dozen 48-inch wrenches were purchased for which only one nut in the country (at Niagara Falls) was large enough.

VI. Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Dollfuss (Engelbert)

Chancellor of Austria who was assassinated by Austrian Nazis in 1934, an event Butler cites as a precursor to war.

Jugoslavia (Yugoslavia)

A kingdom in southeastern Europe that was a source of tension in the 1930s following the assassination of King Alexander.

McClellan Saddles

A specific military saddle design; Butler uses them as an example of wasteful government spending for non-existent cavalry.

Nye Committee

A Senate committee that probed the munitions industry and its wartime profits, which Butler mentions as having "hardly scratched the surface."

Racket

Butler’s central metaphor; a scheme for private profit conducted under the guise of public service.

VI. Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Rhetorical Question

A question asked to make a point rather than elicit an answer. Butler uses these frequently to challenge the reader's assumptions about who fights and who profits.

Temerity

Excessive boldness or audacity; Butler uses this to describe diplomats who claim war is not in the making despite 40,000,000 men being under arms.
