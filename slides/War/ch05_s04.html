Chapter 5, Section 4

The Political Clash—Wilson vs. Butler

Study Slides

Wilson’s Political Argument: The Necessary War

Particularity vs. Generality

Wilson distinguished between "war-in-general" (which may be unjust) and a specific war fought for specific reasons.

Immediate Provocations

Wilson cited Germany’s policy of unrestricted submarine warfare and the Zimmermann Telegram—an attempt by Germany to recruit Mexico into a war against the United States—as direct threats to America...

Moral Imperative

Wilson argued that the autocratic governments of Central Europe threatened the survival of democracy. He famously claimed the war was necessary to make the world "safe for democracy."

Defense of Principles

Wilson believed a nation must be willing to fight for the principles upon which it was founded, or it would not deserve them.

Butler’s Political Argument: The Profitable War

Profit as the Primary Motive

Butler argued that the real reason for war was to protect the investments of American banks and the profits of manufacturers who sold supplies to the Allied powers.

Economic Disparity

Butler highlighted the gap between the earnings of ordinary soldiers ($30 a month) and the massive profits of corporations like Du Pont, Bethlehem Steel, U.S. Steel, and Anaconda Copper.

Rhetoric as "Window Dressing"

In Butler’s view, high-minded language about freedom is a tool used by those who profit from war to convince the public to support it.

Structural Incentives

Butler’s analysis focuses on "following the money," suggesting that those who benefit financially from war are the ones who lobby for its occurrence.

Comparison: Reasons for War

Perspective. Primary Motivation

View of Rhetoric

Woodrow Wilson. Defense of principles and democracy.

Sincere justification of moral obligations.

Smedley Butler. Economic gain and corporate profit.

"Window dressing" to hide financial motives.

The Timing Problem: Context and Hindsight

Wilson (1917)

Spoke in real time under extreme pressure. He did not know how the war would end or that the Treaty of Versailles would eventually fail to secure a lasting peace.

Butler (1935)

Wrote with eighteen years of hindsight. He could see the economic data of the war's aftermath and the failure of the peace settlement to produce the democratic order Wilson had promised.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Autocratic

Relating to a system of government where one person or a small group has absolute power; Wilson viewed this as a threat to democracy.

Hindsight

The understanding of a situation or event only after it has happened.

Sovereignty

The authority of a state to govern itself; Wilson argued Germany’s actions threatened American sovereignty.

Unrestricted Submarine Warfare

A naval policy where submarines sink vessels such as freighters and tankers without warning; a key provocation for Wilson.

Window Dressing

A misleading display or presentation designed to create a deceptively favorable impression of something.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Zimmermann Telegram

A historical document revealing Germany’s attempt to draw Mexico into a war against the United States.
