Woodrow Wilson: The Ethos of Reluctance

Background

A scholar and university president who became President of the United States. He had no personal experience with weapons or combat.

Erich Maria Remarque: The Ethos of Typicality

Background

Conscripted (drafted) at eighteen into the Imperial German Army in 1916. He was wounded by shrapnel on the Western Front in 1917 and spent the rest of the war in a hospital.

The Common Experience

Unlike the volunteer Holmes or the professional Butler, Remarque represents the millions of young men swept up by forces beyond their control.

Rhetorical Function

He speaks for the "common experience." His ethos is that of the man who was there and survived to tell the truth that leaders and professionals often omit.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Appeal to Authority

An argument that draws persuasive force from a speaker’s credentials, experience, or position rather than logic or evidence alone.

Conscript

An individual who is drafted or legally required by the government to serve in the military, rather than enlisting voluntarily.

Ethos

The rhetorical appeal based on the character, credibility, and authority of the speaker.

Martial Virtue

The qualities or behaviors (such as courage and faith) associated with soldiers and military service.

Position Authority

Credibility derived from the office or rank one holds (e.g., the Presidency) rather than personal experience.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Reluctant Warrior

A rhetorical figure who claims to seek peace and only resorts to violence or war out of a profound sense of unavoidable duty.

Representativeness

The quality of being typical of a larger group; in rhetoric, the ability to speak for the "common" or "ordinary" person’s experience.

The Insider

A speaker whose authority comes from having been a high-ranking or long-term member of the institution they are now describing or criticizing.
