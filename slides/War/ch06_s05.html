Analysis of Rhetoric: Understanding the Spectrum of Propaganda

Study Slides

Defining Propaganda

Propaganda

Subordinates truth to effect.

Legitimate Persuasion

Subordinates effect to truth.

Common Propaganda Techniques (1/2)

Selective Presentation

Choosing specific facts while omitting others to favor a specific conclusion.

Emotional Manipulation

Using intense emotion to bypass critical thinking.

Sloganeering

The use of repetitive, simplified phrases.

Demonization

Portraying opponents as inherently evil or subhuman.

Appeals to Fear or Identity

Leveraging group loyalty or anxiety to drive behavior.

Common Propaganda Techniques (2/2)

Suppression of Counterarguments

Ignoring or silencing opposing viewpoints.

Case Studies: Applying the Analysis

Writer. Primary Work Type

Key Rhetorical Features

Oliver Wendell Holmes. Memorial Day Address

Ceremonial; uses religious/martial imagery; omits physical horrors of war.

Smedley Butler. Pamphlet

Uses vivid imagery of wounded veterans; presents selective profit data.

Woodrow Wilson. Address to Congress

Frames war as "Democracy vs. Autocracy"; creates moral necessity for war.

Erich Maria Remarque. Novel (*All Quiet on the Western Front*)

Unflinching depiction of war's horrors; no explicit policy argument.

Glossary of Terms

False Dilemma (False Dichotomy)

A logical fallacy that presents only two options when more actually exist (e.g., "Either we fight or we surrender our freedom"). This creates artificial urgency.

Propaganda

Communication intended to influence behavior and attitude over rational informing, often by prioritizing effect over truth.

Straw Man

A fallacy where an opponent's position is misrepresented or distorted to make it easier to attack (e.g., claiming Wilson "enjoyed" war rather than addressing his argument for it as a last resort).

The Zimmerman Telegram

A real-world event cited as part of the genuine crisis Wilson addressed in his rhetoric.
