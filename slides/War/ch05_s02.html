The Reception History of War Arguments

Study Slides

The Four Frameworks of War

Framework. Key Proponent

Central Argument

Moral Internationalism. Woodrow Wilson

The U.S. has a moral obligation to defend democracy; military power serves moral ideals.

Economic Skepticism. Smedley Butler

War is a "racket" served by economic interests and profits disguised as patriotism.

Martial Idealism. Oliver Wendell Holmes

Military service and combat build character and reveal the best in human beings.

Realistic Testimony. Erich Maria Remarque

War is defined by mud, terror, and waste rather than glory or heroism.

Glossary of Important Terms

Internationalism

The principle or policy of cooperation among nations, specifically the belief that the United States should actively engage in world affairs rather than remain isolated.

Martial Idealism

The belief that war serves as a "forge of virtue," testing individuals and strengthening the moral fabric of society through sacrifice and discipline.

Reception History

The study of how a text, speech, or idea was interpreted and used by audiences over time, focusing on how later generations applied or rejected the work regardless of the author's original intent.

Rhetoric

The art of effective or persuasive speaking or writing, especially the exploitation of figures of speech and other compositional techniques.

The "Racket"

A term used by Smedley Butler to describe war as a scheme for profit, where the costs are borne by the working class while corporations earn record profits.
