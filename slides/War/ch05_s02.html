Butler's Counter: The Profitable War

• American banks had loaned billions of dollars to Britain and France.

• American manufacturers (Du Pont, Bethlehem Steel, U.S. Steel, Anaconda Copper) were selling weapons and supplies at enormous markups.

• If the Allies lost, those loans would never be repaid and those profits would evaporate.

• Soldiers earned $30/month; corporations earned hundreds of millions.

The Timing Problem

Sincerity vs. accuracy

Wilson may have genuinely believed his moral case. That sincerity does not prove his justification was the true cause of American entry into the war.

Two Methods of Political Analysis

Key Concepts for Review

The Zimmermann Telegram

(1917): German attempt to draw Mexico into war against the U.S.; key in Wilson's justification for American entry into WWI.

Unrestricted submarine warfare

Germany's policy of attacking all ships in British waters, including American civilian and commercial vessels.

Reception and context

Reading Wilson's 1917 speech and Butler's 1935 pamphlet side by side requires acknowledging that one was written under wartime pressure and the other with the benefit of hindsight.

Wilson's omission

Wilson's address to Congress never mentioned money — not loans, not profits, not trade. Butler regarded this as the most revealing silence in the debate.
