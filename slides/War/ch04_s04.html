The Critique of Woodrow Wilson

Butler challenges the narrative that the U.S. entered World War I for democratic ideals. He characterizes the war as a "collection operation" for American bankers who had lent billions to the Allies.

Key Concepts and Rhetorical Themes (2/2)

Simplification of Complexity

Butler avoids engaging with Wilson’s specific arguments (such as submarine warfare). Instead, he treats the presence of profit as the sole proof of the absence of a legitimate cause for war.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Ethos

An appeal to the character or authority of the speaker. Butler relies on his status as a decorated soldier to grant weight to his arguments.

Imperative Mood

A grammatical mood that forms a command or request. Butler uses this to demand specific actions, such as the conscription of capital.

Logos

An appeal to logic and reason. In this context, it refers to the profit data and statistics provided in the early chapters of Butler's work.

Pathos

An appeal to the audience's emotions. Butler utilizes this by describing the suffering of veterans to provoke anger and sympathy.

Peroration

The concluding part of a speech or written argument intended to inspire the audience and summarize the main themes.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Plain Style

A method of writing characterized by short, direct sentences and a lack of ornate or classical allusions.
