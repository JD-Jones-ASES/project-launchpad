Chapter 1, Section 5

Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. and “The Soldier’s Faith”

Study Slides

Historical Context: 1895 and the Gilded Age

Feature. Civil War Era

Gilded Age (1895)

Primary Focus. Honor, duty, and national survival.

Profit, commerce, and industrial growth.

Economic Landscape. Agrarian and early industrial.

Steel mills, railroads, and massive factories.

Key Influencers. Soldiers and military leaders.

Industrialists like Carnegie, Rockefeller, and Vanderbilt.

Social Priority. Sacrifice for a cause.

Material comfort and professional security.

Memorial Day (Decoration Day)

Parades and Speeches

Organized by groups such as the Grand Army of the Republic.

Harvard Tradition

An annual address by a distinguished speaker, frequently a veteran, discussing the war's meaning and the duties of the living.

Core Themes and Arguments

The Indispensability of War Virtues

Holmes argues that the virtues cultivated in war—courage, faith, and the willingness to act without guarantees—are necessary for a healthy civilization.

Challenge to Materialism

Holmes expresses concern that a society focused entirely on "the sound of commerce" and material comfort will lose its capacity for sacrifice.

The Value of Risk

He advocates for the willingness to risk everything for something larger than oneself, contrasting the lives of prosperous professionals with the "soldier’s faith."

The "Experience" of the Speech

Holmes does not just want to make a logical argument; he seeks to create an emotional experience for his audience through his rhetorical style.

Rhetorical Style: The Periodic Sentence

Definition

A sentence where the main idea or central point is withheld until the very end.

Structure

Subordinate clauses and phrases are "stacked" or piled up, building suspense.

Effect

The sentence creates an emotional climax, arriving at its destination with significant force, much like a "wave breaking on shore."
