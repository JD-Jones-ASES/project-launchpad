Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Deductive Reasoning

A logical process in which a conclusion is drawn from a set of premises; if the premises are true and the structure is valid, the conclusion must be true.

Ethos

The rhetorical appeal based on the authority or credibility of the speaker.

Experiential Testimony

Evidence based on first-hand, sensory experience; often used to convey the physical or emotional reality of an event.

Inductive Generalization

A logical method that reaches a general conclusion based on specific observations or instances.

Logos

The use of logic, reasoning, and evidence to support a claim; the "backbone" of an argument.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Pathos

The rhetorical appeal to the audience’s emotions.

Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc

A logical fallacy meaning "after this, therefore because of this"; the mistaken assumption that because one event followed another, the first event caused the second.

Syllogism

A form of deductive reasoning consisting of a major premise, a minor premise, and a conclusion.

Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. utilizes a logical method known as inductive generalization. Which of the following best describes the inherent vulnerability of this specific logical framework as applied...

A. The reasoning is circular because it assumes the virtues of war are the cause of the soldier's faith rather than the result.

B. The transition from specific personal observations to universal principles relies on a narrow and potentially unrepresentative sample.

C. The argument relies on deductive premises that cannot be verified through empirical observation or historical data.

D. The evidence provided consists of abstract philosophical claims that lack concrete, lived experience to support them.

Hint: Consider how the size and scope of one's personal experience affect the validity of a 'universal' conclusion.
