Chapter 7, Section 3

The Enduring Debate on War and Peace

Study Slides

Core Concepts: The Four Perspectives

Wilsonian Logic

The belief that democracies must fight to preserve the international order, resist aggression, and promote self-determination or democracy.

Holmesian Martial Virtue

A vision of war as a site of idealism, purposeful military action, and individual or national virtue.

Butler’s Skepticism

The argument that war is often a "racket" driven by profit, benefiting industrialists and contractors while questioning the strategic or moral justifications for conflict.

Remarque’s Realism

An unflinching focus on the gap between patriotic rhetoric and the grim reality of the battlefield, emphasizing the lived experience of the soldier.

Historical Applications

Conflict. Wilsonian / Holmesian Elements

Butler / Remarque Elements

World War II. Seen as "the good war"; a clear defense of democracy against Nazi and Fascist threats; vindication of martial virtue.

Fortunes made by industrialists; injustices like Japanese American internment; a gap between rhetoric and battlefield reality.

Korean War. Framed as a defense of the free world against communist expansion; a test of international order.

Questions about the costs of global leadership; concerns over a military budget that stayed high even in peacetime.

Vietnam War. Initially justified as making Southeast Asia safe for self-determination and preserving American credibility.

The war was called a "racket"; the Pentagon Papers revealed official deception; writers like Tim O'Brien echoed Remarque’s honesty.

Gulf War. A coalition enforcing international law against aggression; a restoration of purposeful, decisive military action.

Skeptics noted the presence of Kuwaiti oil reserves, asking if the US would intervene without strategic resources.

Iraq & Afghanistan. Initial self-defense after 9/11; later justified as "spreading democracy" when WMDs were not found in Iraq.

Contractors made billions; a "revolving door" between defense firms and government; soldiers served multiple tours while the public was detached.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Butler's Heir

A term used to describe modern skeptics who believe war primarily benefits industrial or political interests rather than the public good.

Cold War Context

The geopolitical environment following WWII where military interventions (like Korea) were framed as necessary to resist communist expansion and preserve the free world.

Historical Legacy

The lasting influence of an idea or argument on subsequent generations; measured by its ability to shape how people think about similar questions in the future.

International Order

The system of laws and norms among nations that Wilsonian logic seeks to defend through collective security and, if necessary, military force.

Martial Virtue

The Holmesian belief in the inherent moral or national value of military service and decisive, purposeful action.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Pentagon Papers

A set of documents published in 1971 that revealed systematic government deception regarding the Vietnam War, fueling public distrust.

Revolving Door

The movement of personnel between roles as government officials and roles in private defense firms, often cited by Butler-style skeptics as a conflict of interest.

Stalemate

A situation in which no further action or progress by opposing or competitive parties seems possible; specifically used to describe the conclusion of the Korean War.
