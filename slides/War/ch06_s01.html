Key Terms

Audience analysis

Identifying who is being addressed and how that shapes the argument's content, tone, and omissions.

Genre

The form of a text (oration, pamphlet, address, novel) that determines what kinds of arguments are possible within it.

Rhetorical silence

What a writer chooses not to say, which can reveal strategic choices as clearly as the arguments themselves.

Bearing witness

Remarque's mode — presenting experience and inviting judgment, rather than advancing a thesis.

What genre does Remarque use, and how does it differ from the others?

A novel — he does not argue in the conventional sense but places the reader inside an experience and lets the experience do the work.

What audience does Holmes address, and what does that allow him to assume?

Harvard graduates, many of them veterans. He can assume a baseline of sympathy and build on shared values.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What challenge does Butler face with his audience?

He speaks to ordinary Americans who may not yet see the connection between corporate profits and their sons' graves. He must shock them into attention.

What rhetorical challenge faces Wilson when addressing Congress?

He must persuade politicians who must cast a vote with enormous consequences — being persuasive without appearing reckless.

What is Remarque's dual audience challenge?

He writes for a generation that lived through the war and a younger generation that did not — he must make the incomprehensible somehow comprehensible.

What does Holmes choose NOT to discuss?

Who profits from war.

What does Butler choose NOT to concede?

That any war might be justified.

What does Wilson choose NOT to dwell on?

The physical horrors his listeners' constituents will endure.
