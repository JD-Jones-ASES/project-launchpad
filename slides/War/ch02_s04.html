War: A Debate in Primary Sources

Chapter 2, Section 4

The Argument Unpacked

Study Slides

What Butler Leaves Open

• Is every war a racket, or only some? His evidence comes almost entirely from WWI.

• Can a necessary war be fought without enriching the wrong people?

• Does the existence of enormous profit prove that profit was the purpose of the war? This is a crucial logical distinction — correlation is not causation — that Butler does not always make explicit.

Key Terms

Logos

— Rhetorical appeal to reason and evidence; Butler uses documented profit data.

Pathos

— Rhetorical appeal to emotion; Butler uses catalogs of human suffering.

War profiteering

— Making excessive profits from wartime government contracts.

Blood gains

— Butler's term for wartime profits derived from the suffering and death of soldiers.

Asymmetry of cost and profit

— The principle that those who profit from war are not those who fight and die in it.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the asymmetry of cost and profit in Butler's argument?

How does Butler use the rhetorical questions about the millionaires to demonstrate the asymmetry?

He asks how many of the war millionaires shouldered a rifle, dug a trench, were wounded or killed — and the answer in every case is none.

Why does Butler place the human cost accounting after the financial evidence?

So the reader has already absorbed the obscenity of the profits before being confronted with the price — giving the moral reckoning maximum impact.

What makes Butler's use of evidence 'logos at its most concrete'?

What rhetorical appeal does Butler primarily employ in Stage 2?

Logos — the appeal to documented facts and logic.

What rhetorical appeal does Butler primarily employ in Stage 3?

Pathos — moral outrage at the suffering paid by soldiers and families who receive none of the profits.
