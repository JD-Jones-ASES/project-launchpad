Reading a Modern Novel: The Great Gatsby

Chapter 5, Section 2

Chapter 4: Literary Analysis

Study Slides

Key Concepts

• Identity in the novel is fundamentally performative — characters construct public selves that conceal private realities

• Gatsby's autobiography follows the conventions of the self-made American hero, yet fails to convince precisely because it is too polished

• Physical objects carry more persuasive power than narrative coherence in a culture obsessed with surfaces

• Meyer Wolfshiem parallels Gatsby — both perform respectability while concealing criminal activity

• Jordan Baker's account of Gatsby's Louisville romance provides the emotional core that Gatsby's own autobiography strategically omits

• Three competing narrative layers structure the chapter: Gatsby's autobiography, Wolfshiem's reminiscences, and Jordan's backstory

Key Terms

performative identity

The idea that selfhood is constructed through outward acts and performances rather than being an authentic inner essence

sawdust

Nick's metaphor for Gatsby's autobiography — a conventionally stuffed figure that leaks its artificial filling under pressure

Orderi di Danilo

The Montenegrin military medal Gatsby produces as material proof of his wartime heroism

gonnegtion

Wolfshiem's pronunciation of "connection," referring to a criminal business partnership he attempts to offer Nick

somnambulatory abstraction

The trancelike, watchful distraction Wolfshiem lapses into in public — a behavioral sign of a man accustomed to surveillance
