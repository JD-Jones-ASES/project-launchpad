F. Scott Fitzgerald (1896-1940)

Study Slides

Key Concepts

• Fitzgerald was born in St. Paul, Minnesota, into a family caught between middle-class and upper-class worlds — his father's business failures and his mother's prosperous Irish immigrant background ...

• He attended the Newman School and Princeton University but never graduated, devoting himself to writing rather than academics

• His courtship with Zelda Sayre — who broke off their engagement because he lacked financial prospects — directly shaped the backstory of Gatsby and Daisy in the novel

• The publication of This Side of Paradise (1920) launched Fitzgerald and Zelda into Jazz Age celebrity; their life was glamorous but ultimately tragic, marked by Zelda's mental breakdowns and Fitz...

• Fitzgerald can be identified with both Jay Gatsby (the Midwesterner who reinvented himself) and Nick Carraway (the moralist who sees corruption beneath the glamour)

• At the time of his death in 1940, The Great Gatsby was out of print and Fitzgerald was considered a relic; his full literary vindication came posthumously

Key Terms

Jazz Age

The term Fitzgerald himself coined for the 1920s, a decade of cultural vitality, economic expansion, and social transformation

This Side of Paradise

Fitzgerald's debut novel (1920), whose bestseller success enabled his marriage to Zelda and launched his public celebrity

Tender Is the Night

Fitzgerald's 1934 novel about the disintegration of an American couple on the French Riviera, published as his reputation was declining

The Last Tycoon

Fitzgerald's final, unfinished novel, begun during his Hollywood screenwriting years
