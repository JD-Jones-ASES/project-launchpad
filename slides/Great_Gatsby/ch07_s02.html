Reading a Modern Novel: The Great Gatsby

Chapter 7, Section 2

Chapter 6: Literary Analysis

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

• Gatsby "sprang from his Platonic conception of himself" — a philosophical framework central to understanding his character

• Platonic idealism: the belief that abstract ideal Forms are more real than the imperfect material world; Gatsby applies this to his own identity

• Gatsby's cry — "Can't repeat the past? Why of course you can!" — is not naivety but the logical conclusion of his worldview

• Tom Buchanan functions as a foil to Gatsby: old money vs. new, inherited status vs. self-made myth

• Daisy's discomfort at the party signals that social reality is already pushing back against Gatsby's idealized vision

• The chapter reveals the fatal flaw in Gatsby's grand conception: it depends on others — especially Daisy — accepting his version of reality

Key Concepts (2/2)

• Fitzgerald uses the kiss scene to show the moment Gatsby "wed his unutterable visions to her perishable breath," making Daisy the vessel for an idea that transcends any real person

Key Terms (1/2)

Platonic conception

Derived from Plato's philosophy of ideal Forms; Gatsby's "platonic conception of himself" is a perfect, abstract self-image to which he tries to make reality conform

incarnation

The embodiment of an idea or quality in physical form; when Gatsby kisses Daisy, the "incarnation was complete" — his ideal found its material vessel

foil

A character whose contrasting qualities highlight the traits of another; Tom Buchanan's inherited wealth, social certainty, and contempt for Gatsby's new-money world sharpen the reader's understand...

ineffable

Too great or extreme to be expressed in words; used to describe the "universe of ineffable gaudiness" spinning in young Gatz's imagination

appalling

Causing shock or dismay; also used in the sense of diminishing — Fitzgerald uses it to describe both Daisy's reaction to West Egg and the "appalling sentimentality" in Gatsby's account of the past

Key Terms (2/2)

meretricious

Apparently attractive but having in reality no value or integrity; applied to the "vast, vulgar, and meretricious beauty" that Gatsby has devoted himself to serving
