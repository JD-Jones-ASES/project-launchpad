Important Terms (1/2)

Indirect Characterization

The revelation of a character's personality through their actions, speech, thoughts, or what other characters say about them, rather than through direct authorial description. (e.g., Daisy's "voice...

Symbolism

The use of objects, people, or ideas to represent something else, often a deeper meaning or abstract concept. (e.g., the oppressive heat symbolizing emotional tension).

Metaphor

A figure of speech that directly compares two unlike things without using "like" or "as," stating one thing *is* another. (e.g., Daisy's voice *is* full of money).

Parallelism

The use of similar or identical grammatical structures or thematic situations to show a connection or comparison between different elements. (e.g., Tom's and Wilson's discoveries of infidelity).

Power Dynamics

The interplay of power and influence among individuals or groups within a relationship or social setting. (e.g., how the car arrangements reflect who is trying to assert control).

Important Terms (2/2)

Complacency

A feeling of uncritical satisfaction with oneself or one's achievements, often coupled with a lack of awareness of potential dangers or problems. (e.g., Tom's initial unawareness of Daisy's affair).

Fantasy vs. Reality

A central thematic conflict in the novel, particularly evident in Gatsby's idealized vision of Daisy colliding with the actualities of her life.
