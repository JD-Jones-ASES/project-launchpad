Reading a Modern Novel: The Great Gatsby

Chapter 9, Section 1

Chapter 8: The Novel

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

• Nick cannot sleep after Myrtle's death and visits Gatsby at dawn

• Gatsby reveals the full story of his romance with Daisy in Louisville in 1917 — a penniless young officer courting a wealthy girl under false pretenses

• Gatsby insists Daisy never truly loved Tom, dismissing the Plaza confrontation as a misunderstanding

• Nick urges Gatsby to flee but Gatsby refuses to leave until he knows what Daisy will do

• Nick tells Gatsby: "You're worth the whole damn bunch put together" — his only genuine compliment

• George Wilson, grief-stricken and mentally unraveling overnight at the garage, confuses the billboard eyes of Dr. T. J. Eckleburg with the eyes of God

Key Concepts (2/2)

• Wilson traces the yellow car to Gatsby's estate and shoots Gatsby in his pool, then kills himself

• Gatsby had never used the pool all summer; he is using it for the first time on the day he dies

• Daisy never calls; the butler waits for a phone message that never arrives

Key Terms

Dan Cody

The wealthy copper magnate who took in the young James Gatz, gave him his first taste of wealth, and shaped the Gatsby persona; revealed in Chapter 6, referenced here as context for Gatsby's origins

Humidor

A container for storing cigars or tobacco at constant humidity; one of the objects Nick and Gatsby search for in the dark, dusty house — suggesting the mansion has already lost its vitality

Pneumatic mattress

The air-filled pool float Gatsby uses on the day of his death; an object of summer leisure that becomes a deathbed

Holocaust

Used in the chapter's final sentence in its original sense — a complete destruction; "the holocaust was complete" refers to the total ruin of Gatsby, Wilson, and Myrtle

Grail

Nick compares Gatsby's pursuit of Daisy to a medieval knight's quest for the Holy Grail — a sacred, all-consuming mission that gives his life meaning but may be unattainable
