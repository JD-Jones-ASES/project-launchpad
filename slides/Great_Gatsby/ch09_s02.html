Reading a Modern Novel: The Great Gatsby

Chapter 9, Section 2

Chapter 8: Literary Analysis

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

• Chapter 8 is the catastrophe of Fitzgerald's tragedy — the point of the protagonist's irreversible destruction

• Fitzgerald narrates Gatsby's death with extreme restraint: the actual shooting is never witnessed; the reader arrives after the fact alongside Nick

• The technique of narrative deflation (presenting catastrophic violence through peripheral details) converts spectacle into elegy

• George Wilson's conflation of the Eckleburg billboard with the eyes of God is the novel's central theological statement

• Michaelis's flat correction — "That's an advertisement" — compresses the novel's spiritual vision into a single exchange

• The novel's structure intercuts two parallel vigils: Gatsby waiting at the pool for Daisy's call, Wilson waiting in the garage to act on his grief

Key Concepts (2/2)

• Both men are destroyed by fixation on a woman already beyond their reach; both are casualties of the Buchanans' carelessness

• Classical tragic structure: the catastrophe follows the climax (Ch. 7 Plaza confrontation) and the reversal of fortune

• Anticlimax is deployed deliberately: Gatsby's death, the novel's culminating event, is rendered through ripples in water and a drifting mattress

Key Terms

Catastrophe

In classical tragic structure, the final event of the falling action — the protagonist's destruction; corresponds to the fifth act of Elizabethan tragedy

Anticlimax

A moment of high drama that resolves with unexpected flatness; Fitzgerald deploys it deliberately so that the reader supplies the emotional weight the prose withholds

Narrative deflation

Presenting catastrophic events through peripheral details and understatement, making violence more powerful through restraint

Parallel structure

The intercut vigils of Gatsby (waiting for Daisy's call) and Wilson (grieving in the garage) — a formal device that underscores their shared fate as casualties of the Buchanans' carelessness
