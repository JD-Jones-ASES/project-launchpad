Entertainment

A "whole pitful of oboes and trombones and saxophones," implying a massive, professional orchestra rather than a small band.

Logistics

The Rolls-Royce runs as an "omnibus" to transport guests. Eight servants are needed every Monday to repair the damage from the parties.

The Nature of Gatsby's Guests and Hospitality

Uninvited Crowds

Most guests are not formally invited but arrive through loose connections, "introduced by somebody who knew Gatsby."

Entitled Behavior

Nick observes them behaving "according to the rules of behavior associated with amusement parks," suggesting a lack of decorum, a sense of anonymity, and a focus on fleeting entertainment.

Rumors and Gatsby's Mysterious Identity

Whispers and Speculation

Guests at Nick's table eagerly share wild, unsubstantiated rumors about Gatsby: he "killed a man once," he was "a German spy during the war," or that he "grew up" in Germany.

Lack of Knowledge

The significant point is that *none* of these people actually know Gatsby personally. Their speculation highlights his deliberate inscrutability and the superficiality of their interest.

Myth-Making

These rumors create a romantic, almost mythic, aura around Gatsby, deepening his mystique and underscoring the gap between his public image and his hidden reality.

The Owl-eyed Man and Gatsby's Illusion

Discovery in the Library

In Gatsby's library, a drunken "owl-eyed man" marvels that Gatsby's books are "absolutely real" with "pages and everything." He had expected "a nice durable cardboard."

Belasco Comparison

The owl-eyed man compares Gatsby to "a regular Belasco," acknowledging the thoroughness and realism of Gatsby's "performance"—his estate as a meticulously constructed stage set.
