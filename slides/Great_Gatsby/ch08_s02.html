Reading a Modern Novel: The Great Gatsby

Chapter 8, Section 2

Chapter 7: Literary Analysis

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

• Chapter 7 functions as both the novel's climax (Plaza Hotel confrontation) and its peripeteia — the moment of tragic reversal

• The oppressive summer heat operates as both an atmospheric device and a symbol of mounting pressure and moral collapse

• The confrontation between Gatsby and Tom is fundamentally about class, not love

• Tom wins the confrontation through the "gravitational weight" of his social position and inherited legitimacy, not superior virtue

• Tom and Gatsby function as foils: both are morally compromised, but Tom's old money insulates him from consequences

• The theme of carelessness — introduced here, made explicit by Nick later — governs the chapter's final movement

Key Concepts (2/2)

• Dramatic irony: Gatsby believes he is protecting Daisy, but Nick sees through the window that she has already returned to Tom

• Fitzgerald's use of the Plaza Hotel setting (Wedding March drifting up, whiskey sweating on the table) reinforces the sense of a world about to crack

Key Terms (1/2)

Climax

The moment of highest dramatic tension; in Chapter 7, the Plaza Hotel confrontation

Peripeteia

The tragic reversal of fortune; the moment when Gatsby's dream becomes irretrievable

Foil

A character who contrasts with another to highlight qualities; Tom serves as Gatsby's foil — both morally compromised, but differently positioned by class

Dramatic irony

When the audience (or narrator) understands something a character does not; Nick sees that Gatsby's vigil protects nothing

Carelessness

Nick's moral verdict on the Buchanans — their wealth insulates them from consequences, enabling destruction without responsibility

Key Terms (2/2)

Old money vs. new money

The distinction between inherited wealth (East Egg, Tom) and recently acquired wealth (West Egg, Gatsby) — a class barrier Gatsby cannot cross regardless of his fortune
