Reading a Modern Novel: The Great Gatsby

Chapter 6, Section 2

Chapter 5: Literary Analysis

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

• The green light undergoes a crucial symbolic transformation: from beacon of longing to ordinary dock light

• The chapter dramatizes the central irony of the novel: achieving a dream diminishes the dream itself

• Gatsby's five-year illusion has grown "beyond" Daisy — no actual person could sustain the weight of his idealization

• Material objects (shirts, the mansion, the gold toilet set) serve as proxies for emotional expression that direct speech cannot carry

• Fitzgerald leaves Daisy's tears over the shirts deliberately ambiguous — mourning, joy, or both

• The "overwound clock" metaphor captures Gatsby's emotional collapse after the reunion's peak

Key Concepts (2/2)

• Nick's final observation of bewilderment on Gatsby's face foreshadows the novel's tragic trajectory

Key Terms

green light

The light at the end of Daisy's dock; in earlier chapters a symbol of longing and aspiration, in Chapter 5 it loses its enchantment when Daisy becomes physically present

colossal vitality of his illusion

Fitzgerald's phrase for the outsized, all-consuming nature of Gatsby's idealization of Daisy, which has grown beyond any real person's capacity to fulfill

overwound clock

The metaphor for Gatsby's emotional state after the reunion — wound to maximum tension for five years, now running down in the aftermath of achieved desire

enchanted objects

Gatsby's term, implicit in Nick's narration, for the meaningful symbols (green light, Daisy's voice) whose power diminishes as the dream approaches reality

ecstatic patron of recurrent light

Nick's description of Gatsby's joy when the rain stops — hyperbolic and slightly comic, suggesting Gatsby's emotions are still at an extreme pitch
