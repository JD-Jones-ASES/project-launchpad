Reading a Modern Novel: The Great Gatsby

Chapter 8, Section 1

Chapter 7: The Novel

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

• The climax of the novel occurs in the Plaza Hotel confrontation between Gatsby, Tom, and Daisy

• Gatsby replaces his servants with Wolfsheim associates to prevent gossip about Daisy's visits

• The oppressive summer heat intensifies the tension throughout the chapter

• Tom forces a group trip to New York, swapping cars with Gatsby as an act of dominance

• Daisy falters when Gatsby demands she declare she never loved Tom — she admits she loved both men

• Tom exposes Gatsby's bootlegging and criminal associations, destroying his credibility

Key Concepts (2/2)

• On the drive home, Daisy (driving Gatsby's yellow car) strikes and kills Myrtle Wilson

• Tom directs Wilson's suspicion toward Gatsby and the yellow car

• The chapter ends with Gatsby standing vigil outside the Buchanan house while Tom and Daisy sit together at the kitchen table, conspiring and reconciled

Key Terms

Trimalchio

A reference to a vulgar social climber in the ancient Roman novel Satyricon; Fitzgerald uses it to describe Gatsby's showy party-hosting phase, which ends abruptly before Chapter 7

Vigil

A period of watchful waiting; Gatsby's all-night surveillance outside the Buchanan house after Myrtle's death

Valley of ashes

The desolate industrial wasteland between West Egg and New York, home to the Wilsons; the site of Myrtle's death

Yellow car

Gatsby's ostentatious automobile, which becomes the instrument of Myrtle's death and the object of Wilson's revenge
