Important Terms (1/2)

Valley of Ashes

A desolate, industrial wasteland between West Egg and New York City, characterized by dust and ash, symbolizing poverty, industrial decay, and moral desolation.

Doctor T. J. Eckleburg

A faded billboard advertising an oculist, featuring giant, spectacled eyes. It acts as a symbolic, watchful presence over the valley, often interpreted as representing an absent God, indifferent ju...

West Egg

The "new money" district of Long Island, where Nick and Gatsby reside, representing flashy wealth and a less established social standing.

East Egg

The "old money" district, home to Tom and Daisy Buchanan, signifying inherited wealth, established aristocracy, and often, moral complacency.

Condescending

Exhibiting an attitude of superiority; talking down to someone. Tom Buchanan's behavior towards George Wilson is highly condescending.

Important Terms (2/2)

Domineering

Asserting one's will over another in an arrogant or arbitrary way. Tom's control over the conversation and his threats toward Wilson show his domineering nature.

Anaemic

Lacking in color, spirit, or vitality; sickly. Used to describe George Wilson, suggesting his physical and emotional depletion.

Vitality

The state of being strong and active; energy. Myrtle Wilson is described as having "immediately perceptible vitality," contrasting sharply with her husband.

The Eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg

Physical Description

A massive, faded billboard advertising an oculist. It features enormous blue eyes, "gigantic," with irises "a yard high," looking out from a pair of yellow spectacles over a nonexistent nose.

Origin Story

The sign was placed by an oculist who either went blind or moved away, leaving the advertisement to slowly decay. This backstory adds to its sense of abandonment and obsolescence.

Symbolic Interpretations

Tom Buchanan's Behavior and Power Dynamics

Condescending and Domineering

Tom treats George Wilson with a mixture of false geniality and outright contempt. He slaps Wilson on the shoulder and makes empty promises about selling him a car.

Exerting Control

When Wilson attempts to press Tom about the car, Tom quickly becomes cold, threatening to sell it elsewhere. He controls the conversation, dismisses Wilson's concerns, and later calls Wilson "dumb"...

Abuse of Power

Tom uses his wealth and social standing to intimidate Wilson and exploit him, highlighting the vast class divisions and the ruthlessness of the upper class.

George Wilson's Characterization

Physical and Emotional State

Submissive and Powerless

He answers "unconvincingly" and his voice fades off under Tom's pressure. He is clearly intimidated by Tom and unable to assert himself, blending into the ash-gray walls of his garage.

Contrast to Myrtle

His lack of vitality and submissive nature provide a sharp contrast to his wife's vibrant entrance.

Myrtle Wilson's Entrance and Vitality

Striking Presence

Myrtle enters with "immediately perceptible vitality," a stark contrast to her husband's listlessness and the dreary surroundings. She carries herself sensuously and commands attention.

Assertiveness

She is forceful and assertive, described as walking through her husband "as if he were a ghost." Her presence is powerful, and she is clearly not content with her life in the valley of ashes.

Escape from Drabness

Her vibrancy suggests a yearning for a life beyond the valley, mirroring her affair with Tom as an attempt to escape her economic and social circumstances.
