Reading a Modern Novel: The Great Gatsby

Chapter 1, Section 2

The Jazz Age and the 1920s

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

• The Great Gatsby is set during the summer of 1922, in a decade Fitzgerald himself named "the Jazz Age"

• World War I (1914–1918) left approximately seventeen million dead and shattered faith in progress, producing postwar disillusionment that runs through every major character in the novel

• Prohibition (Eighteenth Amendment, ratified 1919) banned alcohol but created a vast underground economy of bootleggers, speakeasies, and organized crime — the likely source of Gatsby's fortune

• The 1920s economic boom generated a new class of self-made wealthy Americans; the novel maps this social reality onto its geography: East Egg (old money) versus West Egg (new money)

• The automobile transformed American life and appears in the novel as both a symbol of freedom and an instrument of destruction

• The Great Migration, the rise of jazz, and the Nineteenth Amendment (women's suffrage, 1920) contributed to broader social change; "flappers" challenged Victorian conventions

Key Concepts (2/2)

• Fitzgerald was both a participant in and a critic of the Jazz Age — attracted to its glamour but aware of its moral emptiness

Key Terms (1/2)

Jazz Age

Fitzgerald's name for the 1920s, a decade of cultural energy, economic expansion, and loosened social conventions

Prohibition

The ban on manufacture, sale, and transportation of alcohol established by the Eighteenth Amendment (ratified 1919), which inadvertently created a large underground economy

Bootlegger

A person who illegally manufactures or distributes alcohol during Prohibition; the novel implies Gatsby built his fortune this way

Speakeasy

An illegal establishment selling alcohol during Prohibition

East Egg / West Egg

The novel's fictional Long Island locations representing old inherited wealth and new self-made wealth, respectively

Key Terms (2/2)

Great Migration

The movement of African Americans from the rural South to northern cities during the early twentieth century

Flappers

Young women of the 1920s who challenged traditional expectations about dress, behavior, and independence

Nineteenth Amendment

Ratified in 1920, it granted women the right to vote in the United States
