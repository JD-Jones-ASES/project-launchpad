Reading a Modern Novel: The Great Gatsby

Chapter 1, Section 3

The Novel and Its Place in Literature

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

• The Great Gatsby was published by Charles Scribner's Sons on April 10, 1925; Fitzgerald considered it his best work

• Initial reception was mixed and sales were modest — roughly 20,000 copies in the first year; the novel went out of print during Fitzgerald's lifetime

• The novel was revived during World War II when the Council on Books in Wartime distributed approximately 155,000 copies to American soldiers through the Armed Services Editions

• By the 1960s the novel had entered the American literary canon; today it sells approximately 500,000 copies per year

• The novel participates in multiple literary traditions simultaneously: modernism, social realism, classical tragedy, social satire, and romance

• Its central subject — the American Dream — gives it lasting resonance; Fitzgerald both celebrates and interrogates the belief that anyone can rise from humble origins through determination

Key Terms (1/2)

Armed Services Editions

Lightweight paperbacks distributed free to American soldiers during World War II; the edition that revived The Great Gatsby's readership

American Dream

The belief that anyone can achieve prosperity and success through hard work and determination, regardless of birth or social standing; the novel's central subject

Modernism

A literary movement of the 1910s–1930s characterized by formal experimentation, unreliable narration, subjective experience, and disillusionment with traditional values

Unreliable narrator

A narrator whose account cannot be taken entirely at face value; Nick Carraway is one of the most discussed examples in American literature

Tragic hero

A figure of exceptional qualities destroyed by a fatal flaw; Gatsby's flaw is his insistence that time can be reversed and the idealized past recovered

Key Terms (2/2)

Romance (literary)

A quest narrative in which a hero pursues an ideal; Gatsby's pursuit of Daisy follows this structure, with the green light as a grail symbol

Symbolism

The use of concrete objects or images to represent abstract ideas; key symbols include the green light, the eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg, and the valley of ashes
