Describing People and Things in Spanish

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Adjective Agreement

The most critical rule for using adjectives in Spanish is that they must always agree in both **gender** (masculine/feminine) and **number** (singular/plural) with the noun they describe.

Expanding Descriptive Vocabulary

This section introduces a new set of common adjectives to describe physical appearance, personality traits, and characteristics of things.

Connecting Multiple Adjectives

When using two or more adjectives to describe the same noun, connect them with `y` (and). Both adjectives must individually agree with the noun.

The Foundation: Adjective Agreement

Masculine Singular Noun

Use the masculine singular form of the adjective (usually ending in -o, or remaining unchanged if it ends in -e or a consonant).

Feminine Singular Noun

Use the feminine singular form of the adjective (usually ending in -a, or remaining unchanged if it ends in -e or a consonant).

Masculine Plural Noun

Use the masculine plural form of the adjective (usually ending in -os, or -es if it ends in -e or a consonant).

Feminine Plural Noun

Use the feminine plural form of the adjective (usually ending in -as, or -es if it ends in -e or a consonant).

New Descriptive Adjectives (1/2)

bonito/a

(pretty, nice-looking): *La flor es **bonita**.* (The flower is pretty.) *El coche es **bonito**.* (The car is nice-looking.)

feo/a

(ugly): *El monstruo es **feo**.* (The monster is ugly.) *La pintura es **fea**.* (The painting is ugly.)

divertido/a

(fun, entertaining): *El juego es **divertido**.* (The game is fun.) *La fiesta es **divertida**.* (The party is fun.)

aburrido/a

(boring): *El libro es **aburrido**.* (The book is boring.) *La clase es **aburrida**.* (The class is boring.)

simpático/a

(friendly, nice): *Mi amigo es **simpático**.* (My friend is friendly.) *Mi maestra es **simpática**.* (My teacher is nice.)

New Descriptive Adjectives (2/2)

antipático/a

(unfriendly, unpleasant): *El vecino es **antipático**.* (The neighbor is unfriendly.) *La película es **antipática**.* (The movie is unpleasant.)

gordo/a

(fat, chubby): *El gato es **gordo**.* (The cat is fat.) *La bebé es **gorda**.* (The baby is chubby.)

delgado/a

(thin, slender): *El modelo es **delgado**.* (The model is thin.) *La chica es **delgada**.* (The girl is slender.)

nuevo/a

(new): *Mi teléfono es **nuevo**.* (My phone is new.) *La camisa es **nueva**.* (The shirt is new.)

viejo/a

(old): *Mi abuelo es **viejo**.* (My grandfather is old.) *La bicicleta es **vieja**.* (The bicycle is old.)

Cultural Nuance: `Gordo/a` and `Viejo/a`

Key takeaway

While these words are common and often neutral, context and tone are still crucial, just as with any language. Always strive to be respectful when describing real people.
