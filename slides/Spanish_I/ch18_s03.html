Telling Your Story: Combining Preterite Verbs and Emotions

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

The Preterite Tense

Used to describe completed actions or events in the past, often with a clear beginning and end. It's essential for recounting specific occurrences.

Three Preterite Verb Patterns

Special `yo` forms

Both -ar verbs (`-é`) and -er/-ir verbs (`-í`) in the `yo` form require an accent mark to indicate the preterite tense.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Sequence Words

Words like `ayer` (yesterday), `primero` (first), `después` (afterwards), and `al final` (in the end) are vital for organizing events in a chronological story.

Important Terms (1/3)

El pretérito

The preterite tense; used for actions completed in the past.

-ar verb

A verb ending in -ar in its infinitive form (e.g., *hablar* - to speak).

-er verb

A verb ending in -er in its infinitive form (e.g., *comer* - to eat).

-ir verb

A verb ending in -ir in its infinitive form (e.g., *escribir* - to write).

Estar + adjective

A construction used to express temporary states or emotions (e.g., *estar contento/a* - to be happy).

Important Terms (2/3)

Ayer

Yesterday.

Primero

First, firstly.

Después

Afterwards, then.

Al final

Enojado/a

Annoyed, angry.

Useful Words for Storytelling (Time Connectors)

Ayer

(Yesterday) - Establishes the general timeframe.

Primero

(First/Firstly) - Indicates the initial event in a sequence.

Después

(Afterwards/Then) - Connects subsequent events.

Al final

(In the end/Finally) - Concludes the sequence of events.
