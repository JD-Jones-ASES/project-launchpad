Encountering Spanish

Chapter 2, Section 2

Los Artículos (Articles)

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Gender and Number Agreement

Spanish articles are not singular like English ("the," "a/an"). Instead, they change form to match the gender (masculine/feminine) and number (singular/plural) of the noun they accompany.

Definite Articles

These mean "the" and have four forms: `el` (masculine singular), `la` (feminine singular), `los` (masculine plural), and `las` (feminine plural).

Indefinite Articles

These mean "a," "an," or "some" and also have four forms: `un` (masculine singular), `una` (feminine singular), `unos` (masculine plural), and `unas` (feminine plural).

Noun Gender Rules of Thumb

Most nouns ending in `-o` are masculine, and most ending in `-a` are feminine. Nouns ending in `-e` or a consonant can be either gender, requiring memorization with their article.

Making Nouns Plural

Spanish nouns become plural by adding `-s` if they end in a vowel, or `-es` if they end in a consonant. Nouns ending in `-z` change the `z` to `c` before adding `-es`.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Learn Nouns with Articles

Due to exceptions to gender rules, it is crucial to always learn a noun together with its article (e.g., `el libro` instead of just `libro`) to ensure correct gender assignment.

Important Terms (1/2)

Artículo (Article)

A word used with a noun to specify its grammatical definiteness, gender, and number.

Artículo Definido (Definite Article)

Means "the" (el, la, los, las).

Artículo Indefinido (Indefinite Article)

Means "a," "an," or "some" (un, una, unos, unas).

Género (Gender)

The grammatical category that classifies Spanish nouns as either masculine (m.) or feminine (f.).

Número (Number)

The grammatical category that classifies Spanish nouns as either singular or plural.

Important Terms (2/2)

Sustantivo (Noun)

A word that refers to a person, place, thing, idea, or quality.

Masculino (Masculine)

The grammatical gender assigned to certain nouns, often accompanied by `el` or `los`.

Feminino (Feminine)

The grammatical gender assigned to certain nouns, often accompanied by `la` or `las`.

Singular

Referring to one item.

Plural

Referring to more than one item.

Helpful Shortcuts for Noun Gender:

• Most nouns ending in -o are masculine.

• Most nouns ending in -a are feminine.

• Nouns ending in -e or a consonant can be either masculine or feminine. For these, you must learn the noun along with its article.

Forms of the Definite Article ("the"):

el

Masculine singular (e.g., `el libro` - the book)

la

Feminine singular (e.g., `la silla` - the chair)

los

Masculine plural (e.g., `los libros` - the books)

las

Feminine plural (e.g., `las sillas` - the chairs)

Forms of the Indefinite Article ("a, an, some"):

un

Masculine singular (e.g., `un libro` - a book)

una

Feminine singular (e.g., `una silla` - a chair)

unos

Masculine plural (e.g., `unos libros` - some books)

unas

Feminine plural (e.g., `unas sillas` - some chairs)

Special Rule for Nouns Ending in -z:

• Example: `el lápiz` (the pencil) → `los lápices` (the pencils)

• `el libro` → `los libros`

• `la mesa` → `las mesas`

• `el lápiz` → `los lápices`

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the primary difference in how articles work in Spanish compared to English?

Spanish articles must match the gender and number of the noun they accompany, unlike English articles.

What two grammatical properties must a Spanish article agree with for its noun?

Gender (masculine/feminine) and Number (singular/plural).

How many forms does the definite article 'the' have in Spanish?

Four forms: el, la, los, las.

What are the four forms of the Spanish definite article, meaning 'the'?

el (masculine singular), la (feminine singular), los (masculine plural), las (feminine plural).

How many forms does the indefinite article 'a, an, or some' have in Spanish?

Four forms: un, una, unos, unas.

What are the four forms of the Spanish indefinite article, meaning 'a, an, or some'?

un (masculine singular), una (feminine singular), unos (masculine plural), unas (feminine plural).

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the general rule for determining the gender of most Spanish nouns ending in -o?

Most nouns ending in -o are masculine.

What is the general rule for determining the gender of most Spanish nouns ending in -a?

Most nouns ending in -a are feminine.

How do you determine the gender of Spanish nouns ending in -e or a consonant?

You must learn them with their articles, as they can be either masculine or feminine.

How do you make a Spanish noun plural if it ends in a vowel (e.g., libro, mesa)?

Add -s to the end of the noun.

How do you make a Spanish noun plural if it ends in a consonant (e.g., borrador)?

Add -es to the end of the noun.

What special rule applies when making a Spanish noun ending in -z plural (e.g., lápiz)?

The -z changes to -c before adding -es (e.g., lápiz → lápices).
