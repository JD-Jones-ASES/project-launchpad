Expressing Age

When stating someone's age in Spanish, `tener` is always used, and the word `años` (years) must always be included after the number. For example, "I am twelve years old" is `Tengo doce años`.

Important Terms (1/3)

`tener`

To have. The core verb used in these expressions.

`hambre`

Hunger. Used in `tener hambre` (to be hungry / to have hunger).

`sueño`

Sleepiness, sleep. Used in `tener sueño` (to be sleepy / to have sleepiness).

`frío`

Cold. Used in `tener frío` (to be cold / to have cold).

`calor`

Heat. Used in `tener calor` (to be hot / to have heat).

Important Terms (2/3)

`sed`

Thirst. Used in `tener sed` (to be thirsty / to have thirst).

`miedo`

Fear. Used in `tener miedo` (to be afraid / to have fear).

`razón`

Reason, right. Used in `tener razón` (to be right / to have reason).

`años`

Years. Always included when expressing age with `tener`.

`mucho/a`

Much, a lot of, very. Used to intensify `tener` expressions, agreeing in gender with the noun.

Important Terms (3/3)

`muy`

Very. Not used to intensify `tener` expressions; instead, it modifies adjectives or adverbs.

`tú` (you, informal). `tienes`

`Tú tienes frío.`

`Ella tiene sueño.`

`Nosotros tenemos sed.`

Common `Tener` Expressions (1/3)

• `tener hambre` (to be hungry)

• `tener sed` (to be thirsty)

• `tener frío` (to be cold)

• `tener calor` (to be hot)

• `tener sueño` (to be sleepy)

• `tener miedo` (to be afraid)

Common `Tener` Expressions (2/3)

• `tener razón` (to be right)

• `no tener razón` (to be wrong / not to be right)

• `tener prisa` (to be in a hurry)

• `tener ganas de` (+ infinitive) (to feel like doing something)

• `tener éxito` (to be successful)

• `tener vergüenza` (to be ashamed/embarrassed)

Common `Tener` Expressions (3/3)

• `tener suerte` (to be lucky)

Expressing Age

• `Tengo doce años.` (I am twelve years old.) NOT `Tengo doce.`

• `Mi hermana tiene veinte años.` (My sister is twenty years old.)

Intensifying `Tener` Expressions: `Mucho/Mucha` vs. `Muy`

`Tengo mucha hambre.`

(I am very hungry.) — `hambre` is a feminine noun, so `mucha` is used.

`Ella tiene mucho sueño.`

(She is very sleepy.) — `sueño` is a masculine noun, so `mucho` is used.

`Tenemos mucho frío.`

(We are very cold.) — `frío` is a masculine noun, so `mucho` is used.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is a key difference in expressing many feelings and physical states in Spanish compared to English?

Spanish uses the verb 'tener' (to have) plus a noun, while English uses 'to be' plus an adjective.

How do you say 'I am hungry' in Spanish using the 'tener' construction?

Yo tengo hambre. (Literally: 'I have hunger.')

What grammatical structure is generally used with 'tener' to express feelings and states?

tener + a noun (e.g., hambre, frío, sueño).

Should you use 'estar' + adjective for feelings like 'hungry' in Spanish?

No, you should use 'tener' + noun (e.g., tener hambre) instead of 'estar hambriento'.

When using 'tener' expressions, which part changes to match the subject, and which part remains constant?

The verb 'tener' conjugates to match the subject, but the noun (e.g., hambre, frío) does not change form.

How do you say 'She is hungry' in Spanish?

Ella tiene hambre.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How do you say 'We are hungry' in Spanish?

Nosotros tenemos hambre.

How is age expressed in Spanish using 'tener'?

Use 'tener' + number + 'años' (e.g., Tengo doce años).

Is it correct to say 'Tengo doce' to mean 'I am twelve' in Spanish?

No, you must always include 'años'. The correct phrase is 'Tengo doce años'.

What word should you use to say 'very' with 'tener' expressions?

Use 'mucho/a' (not 'muy').

How does 'mucho/a' agree with the noun when used with 'tener' expressions?

'Mucho/a' agrees in gender with the noun (e.g., mucha hambre for feminine, mucho sueño for masculine).

How do you say 'I am very hungry' in Spanish?

Tengo mucha hambre.
