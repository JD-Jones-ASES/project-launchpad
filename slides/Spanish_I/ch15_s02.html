Los Mandatos Irregulares (Irregular Tú Commands)

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Eight Irregular Tú Commands

A specific, finite group of eight verbs (*venir, tener, salir, hacer, decir, ser, poner, ir/ver*) that have unique, non-standard *tú* command forms.

Deviation from Regular Pattern

Unlike most affirmative *tú* commands, these irregular forms do not match the *él/ella* present-tense conjugation of their infinitives (e.g., *venir* → *ven*, not *viene*).

Importance of Memorization

Due to their high frequency in everyday Spanish, these eight commands must be learned by heart.

Context for *ve*

The command form *ve* can come from either *ir* (to go) or *ver* (to see). Contextual cues, such as following *a* + a place for *ir* or an object for *ver*, help differentiate their meanings.

Memorization Aids

Effective techniques like rhythmic repetition ("The Command Song") and the "story method" (linking commands to a vivid narrative) are presented to facilitate learning.

Important Terms (1/2)

Mandatos irregulares

Irregular commands; specifically referring to the eight *tú* commands that do not follow the standard conjugation pattern.

Tú commands

Informal affirmative commands directed at "you" (singular, informal).

Infinitivo

The base form of a verb (e.g., *venir*, *hacer*).

Presente de indicativo (él/ella)

The third-person singular (he/she/it) form of a verb in the present indicative tense. This is the form that regular *tú* commands are based on.

Reflexive pronoun

A pronoun that reflects the action of the verb back to the subject (e.g., *se*).

Important Terms (2/2)

Imperative mood

The grammatical mood used to express commands, requests, or prohibitions.

Poeta

Poet.

Familia multigeneracional

A family structure where multiple generations (e.g., grandparents, parents, children) live together or in close proximity.

El almuerzo

The midday meal, often the main meal of the day in Spanish-speaking cultures, and a significant family gathering time.

Objeto directo

The noun or pronoun that receives the action of the verb directly.

Accent Marks

SÉ (be!)

Has an accent mark to distinguish it from the reflexive pronoun *se*.

DI (say!/tell!)

While historically it sometimes had an accent mark, in modern Spanish, it is generally written without one.

Distinguishing "Ve" from "Ir" and "Ver"

From *ir* (to go)

Often followed by "al/a la" and a place.

From *ver* (to see/watch)

Typically followed by an object that is being seen or watched.

La familia venezolana (The Venezuelan Family)

Close-Knit Structure

Venezuelan families are typically multigenerational and close-knit, with grandparents often living nearby or in the same home, playing an active role in raising grandchildren.

Children's Responsibility

Children are expected to contribute to household chores from an early age, viewing it as a way to learn responsibility and contribute to the family's well-being, not just a burden.
