El Verbo Gustar — "To Like" (Reversed!)

Key Concepts (1/2)

Reversed Structure

Indirect Object Pronouns

These pronouns (`me, te, le, nos, les`) indicate who is pleased by something. They always precede the verb gustar.

Subject-Verb Agreement

The form of gustar (`gusta` or `gustan`) must agree with the subject of the sentence, which is the thing or action that is pleasing, and not with the indirect object pronoun.

Clarifying `le` and `les`

Since `le` and `les` can refer to several different people, an `a + [person/name/pronoun]` phrase can be added at the beginning of the sentence for clarity or emphasis (e.g., A ella le gusta).

Key Concepts (2/2)

Forming Negatives

To make a gustar sentence negative, simply place `no` before the indirect object pronoun (e.g., No me gusta).

Cultural Context

Understanding verbs like gustar helps connect to cultural expressions, such as traditional songs and games that highlight community and shared enjoyment.

Important Terms (1/3)

Gustar

To like; literally, "to be pleasing to."

Pronombre indirecto

(Indirect object pronoun) The pronoun that indicates to whom something is pleasing (e.g., me, te, le, nos, les).

Sujeto

(Subject) The noun or infinitive after gustar that "does the pleasing" and determines if gusta or gustan is used.

Infinitive

The basic, unconjugated form of a verb (e.g., leer - to read, bailar - to dance). When an action is liked, the infinitive is used as the subject of gustar.

Víbora

(Snake) Referenced in the traditional children's game song "A la víbora de la mar."

Important Terms (2/3)

La mar

(The sea) An older or poetic term for el mar (the sea), also featured in the song.

Manzana

(Apple) One of the choices players make in the "A la víbora de la mar" game.

Limón

(Lemon) The other choice players make in the "A la víbora de la mar" game.

Los fines de semana

(Weekends) Common phrase used in conversational practice.

¡Qué divertido!

(How fun!) An exclamation indicating enjoyment.

Important Terms (3/3)

Prefiero

(I prefer) A verb for expressing preference, often used in conjunction with discussing likes.

The "Reversed" Nature of Gustar

English

Subject (person) + verb ("like") + object (thing/action)

Spanish

Indirect Object Pronoun (person) + gustar (pleases) + Subject (thing/action)

Indirect Object Pronouns

Pronoun. English Equivalent

Example

`me`. to me

`Me gusta` (It pleases me / I like)

`te`. to you (informal singular)

`Te gusta` (It pleases you / You like)

`le`. to him/her/you (formal singular)

`Le gusta` (It pleases him/her/you / He/She/You like)

`nos`. to us

`Nos gusta` (It pleases us / We like)

`les`. to them/you (plural)

`Les gusta` (It pleases them/you / They/You like)

Clarifying `le` and `les`

• `A él le gusta el fútbol.` (He likes soccer.)

• `A ella le gusta la música.` (She likes music.)

• `A usted le gusta leer.` (You [formal] like to read.)

• `A Juan le gustan los libros.` (Juan likes books.)

• `A mis amigos les gusta la fiesta.` (My friends like the party.)

• `A nosotros nos gusta la pizza.` (We like pizza. - This is for emphasis, as 'nos' is already clear.)

Making Gustar Sentences Negative

• `No me gusta el café.` (I don't like coffee.)

• `No les gustan las verduras.` (They don't like vegetables.)

• `No nos gusta trabajar los fines de semana.` (We don't like to work on weekends.)

Cultural Insight: "A la víbora de la mar"

Traditional Game Song

"A la víbora de la mar" is a well-known Mexican children's game song, played at celebrations.

Game Description

Two children form an arch, and others pass underneath, like a snake (la víbora) through water (la mar). When trapped, a player chooses a side (manzana or limón) for a tug-of-war.

Themes

The game embodies suspense, laughter, and community – things that "les gustan a todos" (everyone likes). This connects back to the idea of shared enjoyment using the verb gustar.

Key Concepts (1/2)

How does the Spanish verb "gustar" fundamentally differ in structure from "to like" in English?

In Spanish, "gustar" literally means "to be pleasing to," so the structure is reversed: the thing that pleases is the subject, and the person who is pleased ...

When should you use the form "gusta" with the verb "gustar"?

You use "gusta" when one singular thing is pleasing or when an action (expressed by an infinitive verb) is pleasing.

When should you use the form "gustan" with the verb "gustar"?

You use "gustan" when multiple things are pleasing (i.e., the subject is plural).

In a sentence like "Me gusta el fútbol," what is the true grammatical subject of the verb "gustar"?

The true subject is "el fútbol" (soccer), as it is the thing doing the pleasing. "Me" is the indirect object.

Are the pronouns "me, te, le, nos, les" the subjects of "gustar"?

No, these are indirect object pronouns. They indicate who is pleased, but not the subject doing the pleasing.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What are the indirect object pronouns used with "gustar" and what do they generally mean?

Me (to me), Te (to you informal), Le (to him/her/you formal), Nos (to us), Les (to them/you all).

What is the general grammatical structure for sentences using "gustar"?

[Indirect object pronoun] + gusta/gustan + [subject].

How do you clarify who "le" or "les" refers to in a "gustar" sentence if it's unclear or ambiguous?

You add "a + [person/name/pronoun]" at the beginning of the sentence (e.g., 'A María le gusta leer').

How do you make a "gustar" sentence negative?

Add "no" directly before the indirect object pronoun (e.g., 'No me gusta el fútbol').
