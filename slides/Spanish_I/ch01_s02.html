Mastering Spanish Pronunciation: The Alphabet and Vowels

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Phonetic Consistency

Spanish is almost perfectly phonetic, meaning each letter or letter combination generally produces one consistent sound, making pronunciation highly predictable once the rules are learned.

The Spanish Alphabet (El Alfabeto Español)

Comprises 27 letters, including the 26 English letters plus the unique Spanish letter `ñ`.

Consistent Vowel Sounds (Las Cinco Vocales)

Unlike English, each of the five Spanish vowels (`a`, `e`, `i`, `o`, `u`) has a single, unchanging sound, which is fundamental to correct pronunciation.

Silent 'h' (La h es siempre muda)

The letter `h` is always silent in Spanish, 100% of the time, with no exceptions.

Key Concepts (2/2)

The Special Letter 'ñ' (La eñe)

The `ñ` is a distinct letter with its own unique "ny" sound (like in "canyon"), and its tilde (wavy mark) should never be dropped.

Cognates (Cognados)

Words that look similar and have the same meaning in both Spanish and English, offering a quick way to expand vocabulary due to shared linguistic roots.

False Cognates (Falsos Amigos)

Words that appear similar in Spanish and English but have different meanings, requiring careful attention to avoid misunderstandings.

Important Terms (1/2)

Fonético (Phonetic)

Describes a language where spelling closely matches pronunciation, with letters consistently representing the same sounds.

El alfabeto español

The Spanish alphabet, which includes all letters from the English alphabet plus the `ñ`.

Las vocales

The five vowels in Spanish (`a, e, i, o, u`), each having a singular, consistent sound.

Muda (Silent)

An adjective used to describe a letter that is not pronounced, such as the Spanish `h`.

Acento (Accent Mark)

A diacritical mark (`´`) placed over a vowel in Spanish to indicate primary stress or to distinguish homographs.

Important Terms (2/2)

La eñe (ñ)

The 27th letter of the Spanish alphabet, pronounced like the "ny" in "canyon."

Tilde

The wavy mark (~) placed above the `n` to form the letter `ñ`.

Cognados (Cognates)

Words that share a common origin and similar spelling and meaning across two languages (e.g., *important* - *importante*).

Falsos amigos (False Cognates)

Words in different languages that look or sound similar but have different meanings (e.g., *embarazada* - pregnant, not embarrassed).

Las Cinco Vocales (The Five Vowels) - The Key to Pronunciation

Memory Trick

To master the Spanish vowel sounds, practice saying them in order: **a - e - i - o - u**.

¡Ojo! La H es Siempre Muda (Watch Out! The H is Always Silent)

Practical Application

When you see an 'h' in a Spanish word, simply pretend it's invisible.
