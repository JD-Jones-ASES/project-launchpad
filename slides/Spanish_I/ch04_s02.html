Los Adjetivos (Adjectives)

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Adjective Agreement (Concordancia)

In Spanish, adjectives must always match the noun they describe in both gender (masculine/feminine) and number (singular/plural). This is a core rule for correct Spanish grammar.

Gender and Number

Nouns have a grammatical gender (masculine or feminine) and can be singular or plural. Adjectives change their endings to reflect the gender and number of the noun they modify.

Adjectives Ending in -o/-a

Most descriptive adjectives follow this pattern. Their endings change to -o (masculine singular), -a (feminine singular), -os (masculine plural), or -as (feminine plural).

Adjectives Ending in -e or a Consonant

These adjectives generally do not change for gender. They have the same form for masculine and feminine singular. To make them plural, you add an -s (for -e endings) or -es (for consonant endings).

Adjective Placement

Unlike in English, descriptive adjectives in Spanish almost always come after the noun they describe.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Using Adjectives with "Ser"

The verb "ser" (to be) is commonly used with adjectives to describe inherent qualities or characteristics of people and things.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the most important rule of Spanish adjectives regarding the nouns they describe?

Adjectives must agree with the nouns they describe in both gender (masculine/feminine) and number (singular/plural).

What is 'la concordancia de los adjetivos' in Spanish grammar?

It is the agreement of adjectives with the nouns they describe in both gender and number.

How do most descriptive adjectives ending in -o change their form to agree with masculine singular, feminine singular, masculine plural, and feminine plural nouns?

They change their ending: -o (masculine singular), -a (feminine singular), -os (masculine plural), -as (feminine plural).

Give an example of an adjective ending in -o/-a changing for all four forms (masc. sing., fem. sing., masc. plural, fem. plural).

alto (masc. sing.), alta (fem. sing.), altos (masc. plural), altas (fem. plural).

How do adjectives ending in -e (like 'grande' or 'inteligente') change to agree with nouns?

They do not change for gender. They only change for number by adding -s to form the plural (e.g., grande -> grandes, inteligente -> inteligentes).

How do adjectives ending in a consonant (like 'azul' or 'gris') change to agree with nouns?

They do not change for gender. They only change for number by adding -es to form the plural (e.g., azul -> azules, gris -> grises).

Key Concepts (2/2)

In Spanish, where are descriptive adjectives typically placed relative to the noun they describe?

Descriptive adjectives almost always come AFTER the noun, unlike in English.
