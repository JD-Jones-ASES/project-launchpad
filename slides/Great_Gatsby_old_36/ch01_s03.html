Important Terms (1/2)

Privy

Having secret or private knowledge of something; admitted to a confidence. (e.g., Nick was privy to the secret griefs of wild, unknown men.)

Feigned

Simulated or pretended; not genuine. (e.g., Nick sometimes feigned sleep to avoid intimate revelations.)

Levity

Humor or lack of seriousness, especially during a serious occasion; lightness of manner. (e.g., Nick feigned hostile levity.)

Plagiaristic

Resembling or suggestive of plagiarism; here used loosely to mean imitative or borrowed rather than original, often referring to the unoriginal nature of young men's confessions.

Wan

Pale and giving the impression of illness or exhaustion; lacking vitality. (e.g., Nick felt "wan" after returning from the East.)

Important Terms (2/2)

Teutonic Migration

Relating to the ancient Germanic peoples; here used metaphorically to describe a pattern of migration, specifically referring to Nick's participation in World War I ("the Great War").

Midas, Morgan, Maecenas

Allusions to figures representing extreme wealth and patronage: King Midas (myth), J.P. Morgan (financier), and Gaius Maecenas (Roman patron of the arts).

Courtesy Bay

The body of water (Long Island Sound) that separates West Egg and East Egg, implying a superficial politeness despite deeper social divisions.

Supercilious

Behaving as though one is superior to others; disdainfully proud. (e.g., Tom Buchanan's manner.)

Fractiousness

The quality of being easily irritated or quarrelsome; unruly behavior. (e.g., conveyed by Tom Buchanan's voice and demeanor.)

Nick Carraway's Introduction and Philosophy

Father's Advice

Nick begins by recounting his father's advice: "Whenever you feel like criticizing any one... just remember that all the people in this world haven't had the advantages that you've had."

Impact of Advice

This counsel leads Nick to "reserve all judgments," a habit that has both advantages (opening up "curious natures" to him) and disadvantages (making him a "victim of not a few veteran bores").

College Experiences

In college, his reserved nature led him to be "unjustly accused of being a politician" because he became privy to many private confessions, often unsought.

Discomfort with Confidences

He sometimes tried to avoid these "intimate revelations," finding them "plagiaristic and marred by obvious suppressions."

Carraway Family Background and Nick's Move East

Midwestern Roots

The Carraways are "prominent, well-to-do people in this Middle Western city for three generations," with a tradition of descent from the Dukes of Buccleuch.

Family Business

Nick's great-uncle founded a wholesale hardware business, which his father now runs.

Nick's Path

He graduated from New Haven (Yale) in 1915 and participated in "that delayed Teutonic migration known as the Great War" (WWI).

Restlessness and Move

After the war, he felt "restless," perceiving the Middle West as the "ragged edge of the universe." He decided to go East to learn the bond business, a common profession among his acquaintances.

Arrival in the East

His family reluctantly financed him for a year, and he arrived "permanently," he thought, in the spring of 1922.

Settling in West Egg

Finding a House

Initially planned to share a house with a colleague, but ended up alone in a "weather-beaten cardboard bungalow" in West Egg for eighty dollars a month.

Initial Loneliness and Transformation

He experienced loneliness briefly until he helped a newcomer, becoming a "guide, a pathfinder, an original settler" and shedding his solitude.

Sense of New Beginnings

With the summer, he felt "life was beginning over again," filled with hope and health.

Intellectual Aspirations

Description of West Egg and East Egg

Geographical Layout

Nick describes the "strangest communities in North America" on a "slender riotous island" (Long Island), featuring "two enormous eggs" separated by a "courtesy bay."

Physical Similarities, Fundamental Differences

While physically similar in shape and size, their "dissimilarity in every particular except shape and size" is "a more arresting phenomenon."

West Egg

The "less fashionable" of the two, representing "new money." Nick's "small eyesore" of a house is located at the tip, "only fifty yards from the Sound," squeezed between two huge mansions.

Gatsby's Mansion

The "colossal affair" next to Nick's, a "factual imitation of some Hotel de Ville in Normandy," with elaborate features like a tower, marble swimming pool, and forty acres of lawn.

East Egg

The "fashionable" side, characterized by "white palaces" glittering along the water, representing "old money" and established aristocracy.
