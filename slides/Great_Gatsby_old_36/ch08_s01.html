Important Terms (1/3)

Dejection

A state of low spirits or depression. *Example: Gatsby leans against a table “heavy with dejection” after his fruitless vigil.*

Pavilions

Large, tent-like curtains. *Example: Nick describes pushing aside curtains “that were like pavilions” in Gatsby's empty mansion—emphasizing the house's theatrical excess.*

Stratum

A level or class in society. *Example: Gatsby had given Daisy the false impression that he belonged to “much the same stratum as herself.”*

Redolent

Strongly suggestive or reminiscent of something. *Example: Daisy's world was “redolent of orchids” and social activity—fragrant with wealth.*

Important Terms (2/3)

Garrulous

Excessively talkative, especially about trivial things. *Example: A “garrulous man” at the scene of Myrtle's death retells the story until it loses its reality.*

Pneumatic

Filled with or operated by compressed air. *Example: Gatsby takes a “pneumatic mattress” to the pool—an inflatable raft that becomes his deathbed.*

Fortuitously

By chance or accident. *Example: The “poor ghosts” of Gatsby's dissolving world drift “fortuitously about”—the word underscores the randomness of his fate.*

Amorphous

Without a clearly defined shape or form. *Example: The trees appear “amorphous” as Gatsby's dream-world collapses into raw, unfamiliar reality.*
