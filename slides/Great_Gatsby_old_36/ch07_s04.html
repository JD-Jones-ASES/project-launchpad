Important Terms (1/2)

Armistice

A formal agreement of warring parties to stop fighting; a truce. Gatsby mentions the opportunity given to officers after the Armistice of World War I to attend universities.

Mint Julep

A cocktail consisting primarily of bourbon, sugar, water, crushed or shaved ice, and fresh mint. Daisy suggests making one for Tom.

Libertine

A person, especially a man, who behaves without moral principles or a sense of responsibility, especially in sexual matters. Nick uses this term to describe Tom's past behavior.

Prig

A self-righteously moralistic person who behaves as if superior to others. Nick observes Tom's transition from "libertine to prig" during his moralizing outburst.

Bootlegger

A person who makes, distributes, or sells alcoholic liquor illegally. Tom exposes Gatsby as a bootlegger.

Important Terms (2/2)

Grain Alcohol

Ethanol, typically produced by fermentation of grain, used here to refer to a component of illegal alcoholic beverages sold during Prohibition.

Kapiolani / Punch Bowl

Specific locations in Hawaii associated with romantic memories between Tom and Daisy, brought up by Tom to remind Daisy of their shared past.

Michaelis

The young Greek man who runs the coffee joint next to the ash-heaps. He becomes a witness to George Wilson's deteriorating state.

Ash-heaps (Valley of Ashes)

A desolate, industrial area separating West Egg and New York City, symbolic of the moral and social decay hidden beneath the glittering facade of the Jazz Age.

Inquest

A judicial inquiry to ascertain the facts relating to an incident, such as a death. Michaelis is described as the principal witness at the inquest, hinting at an upcoming death investigation.

Tom "automatically" calls Gatsby "crazy!" and insists Daisy loved him then and loves him now, acknowledging he "go[es] off on a spree" but always returns.

Daisy calls Tom "revolting" and reveals he "left Chicago" due to a "little spree," demonstrating his past infidelity.
