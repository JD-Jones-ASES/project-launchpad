Shakespeare's Sonnets 23-26

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

The Inadequacy of Spoken Language

Sonnet XXIII explores how overwhelming emotion can hinder verbal expression, leading to a "tongue-tied" state.

The Power of Written Language

As an alternative, written words are presented in Sonnet XXIII as a more effective and enduring medium for conveying deep love.

"Hearing with Eyes"

This phrase from Sonnet XXIII highlights the idea that true understanding of love can come from carefully reading written declarations, a meta-textual nod to the sonnet itself.

The Eye as Artist

Sonnet XXIV uses the elaborate conceit of the poet's eye acting as a painter, capturing and preserving the beloved's image within the heart.

Limits of Visual Perception

Despite the artistic skill of the eye, Sonnet XXIV concludes that sight can only capture external appearance, not the inner essence or "heart."

Key Concepts (2/2)

Fickleness of Public Favor

Sonnet XXV critiques public honor, titles, and patronage, likening their instability to marigolds that wither at a frown or a warrior's fame erased by a single defeat.

Security of Mutual Love

In contrast to public acclaim, Sonnet XXV champions the unwavering nature of being "love and am belov'd" as a stable and genuinely fulfilling condition.

Feudal Language of Love

Sonnet XXVI employs terms like "Lord of my love" and "vassalage" to frame the poet's relationship with the beloved as one of duty and humble service.

Poem as "Embassage"

The act of sending the sonnet itself is presented in Sonnet XXVI as a formal, dutiful message ("embassage") rather than a display of wit.

Conditional Declaration

Sonnet XXVI suggests that the poet's full declaration of love is postponed until an auspicious "star" or favorable circumstance allows his "tatter'd loving" to be deemed worthy.

Important Terms (1/4)

unperfect actor (Sonnet XXIII)

An actor who has not fully mastered his role or who falters under pressure. In Elizabethan usage, "unperfect" refers to incompleteness or imperfect preparation.

o'ercharg'd with burthen (Sonnet XXIII)

Overloaded with weight; too full of feeling to express oneself clearly. "Burthen" is an archaic form of "burden."

dumb presagers (Sonnet XXIII)

Silent heralds or messengers; here, referring to the written poems that announce the poet's love without vocal sound.

recompense (Sonnet XXIII)

A reward or compensation, specifically for the poet's written plea for a return of love.

stell'd (Sonnet XXIV)

Engraved or fixed, often with a connotation of permanence and luminosity (from Latin *stella*, star). A rare word suggesting the beloved's image is permanently fixed.

Important Terms (2/4)

table (Sonnet XXIV)

A writing or painting tablet; a flat surface used for inscriptions or images. Here, the heart is likened to such a tablet.

perspective (Sonnet XXIV)

The art of creating the illusion of three-dimensional depth on a flat surface, or an optical device that distorts images which then "correct" when viewed from a specific angle. Suggests the portrai...

cunning (Sonnet XXIV)

Skill, expertise; but also craftiness or guile. The word's dual meaning hints at both the eye's artistry and its limitations or potential deceptiveness.

favour with their stars (Sonnet XXV)

To be lucky or fortunate, implying public success is determined by arbitrary astrological fate.

marigold (Sonnet XXV)

A sun-tracking flower (heliotropic) that opens towards the sun and closes in shadow. A common emblem for those who rely on a prince's or patron's favor.

Important Terms (3/4)

razed (Sonnet XXV)

Erased or scratched out. Used to describe a warrior's name being removed from "the book of honour," signifying the loss of public glory.

painful (Sonnet XXV)

Laborious, full of effort and suffering. Refers to a warrior who earned fame through genuine toil, not just experiencing pain.

vassalage (Sonnet XXVI)

The state of being a subject, servant, or slave; servitude, often within a feudal system. It highlights the poet's dutiful submission.

merit (Sonnet XXVI)

Worthiness or deservingness. The poet's duty is strongly tied to the beloved's intrinsic merit.

embassage (Sonnet XXVI)

A written embassy or message from an ambassador; a formal communication. The poem itself is sent as such a message.

Important Terms (4/4)

wit (Sonnet XXVI)

Intelligence, understanding, cleverness, or mental ability. The poet humbly claims his "wit" is too poor to adequately express his great duty.

aspect (Sonnet XXVI)

Appearance, look; also an astrological term referring to the position of planets relative to each other, influencing fate.
