Important Terms (1/2)

Fair Youth

The young male subject of the first 126 sonnets, whose beauty and virtues are often praised, and who is central to the speaker's emotional journey.

Sonnet Sequence

A collection of sonnets linked by theme, subject, or narrative. Shakespeare's sonnets are a prime example.

Procreation Sonnets (Sonnets 1-17)

The initial group of sonnets that urge the Fair Youth to marry and have children to preserve his beauty.

Devotional Mode

A style of writing characterized by adoration, praise, and profound reverence, often directed towards a beloved or a divine figure.

Shadow Metaphor

Used in Sonnet 33 to represent the obscuring of the Fair Youth's perfection and the onset of emotional pain in the relationship.

Important Terms (2/2)

Self-Recrimination

The act of blaming oneself for a fault or wrongdoing. The speaker extensively engages in this in Sonnet 35.

Rhetorical Contortion

The skillful and often elaborate manipulation of language and argument to justify a position or express complex emotions, sometimes bending logic to fit a desired outcome.

Sophistic Argument

A specious or deceptive argument, especially one used for the sake of winning an argument rather than pursuing truth. The speaker's argument in Sonnet 42 is described as such.
