Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 3, Section 2

Shakespeare's Sonnets 1-3 – The Case for Posterity

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

The Procreation Imperative

The central argument that beauty is not a personal possession but a gift that must be passed on through offspring. This is presented as a moral and biological duty.

Time as an Adversary

Time is personified as a destructive force that will inevitably diminish beauty and life. Procreation is presented as the primary defense against this decay.

Self-Love as a Vice

The Youth's refusal to procreate is portrayed as a form of narcissism and "self-substantial" consumption, which is detrimental to himself and the world.

Immortality through Offspring

Having children is depicted as a way to achieve a kind of immortality, allowing one's beauty and "blood" to continue living even after one's own death.

Economic and Agricultural Metaphors

The sonnets frequently use language of treasure, increase, husbandry, and accounting to frame beauty as a valuable resource that should be invested and multiplied rather than wasted.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Reversal of Petrarchan Convention

Unlike traditional love sonnets where the poet adores an unattainable beloved, Shakespeare begins with a "counsellor" rebuking a "self-indulgent youth," setting a unique tone for the sequence.

The "Rose" as an Emblem

The rose symbolizes transient beauty that needs to be preserved, implicitly through reproduction.

Important Terms (1/4)

Increase

Offspring or progeny; also, the broader principle of natural multiplication and growth.

Contracted

Pledged or betrothed (as in marriage); also, narrowed or shrunk, turned inward.

Self-substantial

Self-consuming; made of the same material as itself, suggesting a closed, destructive circuit.

Niggarding

Miserly hoarding; failing to give generously. From "niggard," a miser.

Churl

A rude, miserly, or peasant-like person; one who is mean-spirited.

Important Terms (2/4)

Livery

An outward garment or uniform; here, the Youth's youthful appearance.

Lusty

Full of vigor and vitality; energetic and healthy (not primarily sexual desire).

Thriftless

Wasteful, profitless; producing no gain.

Succession

The legal and biological passing of inheritance from parent to child.

Sum my count

To settle a financial account; to provide the final balance or profit.

Important Terms (3/4)

Glass

A mirror; a looking glass.

Fresh repair

Renewal, restoration, or good condition.

Beguile

To charm or deceive; to while away time; to cheat.

Unear'd

Unplowed, uncultivated.

Tillage

The cultivation of land by plowing.

Important Terms (4/4)

Husbandry

Farming; also, the management of a household or marriage.

Posterity

Future generations; descendants.
