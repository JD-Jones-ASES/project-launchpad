Important Terms (1/4)

Canker

A plant disease causing rot; also a caterpillar or grub that destroys flowers from within. Used as punishment for the rose's "theft."

Canzoniere

The title given to Petrarch's collection of poems, influential in establishing the use of passing seasons as a central structural device for mapping emotional states.

Conceit

An extended metaphor or elaborate comparison that forms the controlling idea of a poem or passage.

Fleeting year

The passing year; the year in its swift movement through seasons.

Figures of delight

Images or representations of pleasure; copies of something that exists elsewhere in a more perfect form.

Important Terms (2/4)

Forward

Bold; presumptuous; premature. Used to describe the violet in Sonnet 99.

Grossly dy'd

Colored too deeply; stained crudely. Implies a clumsy or excessive theft of color.

Heavy Saturn

The planet Saturn was associated in Renaissance astrology with melancholy, old age, and heaviness of spirit. Used to highlight spring's extraordinary vivacity.

Lays

Songs; the musical calls of birds.

Marjoram

A fragrant herb with small, soft leaves and a delicate scent.

Important Terms (3/4)

Proud-pied April

Brilliantly and variously colored April, strutting like someone in fine clothes. "Pied" means multicolored; "proud" suggests both beauty and self-display.

Purple pride

The violet's vivid purple color, described as arrogance or boasting.

Reverdie

A poetic tradition ("re-greening") in which spring's return was the occasion for celebrating love renewed.

Shadow

A reflection, image, or copy; something without the substance of the original.

Teeming

Full; pregnant; productive. Autumn is "teeming" with fruit.

Important Terms (4/4)

Unfather'd

Without a father; fatherless. Describing the fruit of autumn without the "father" (the beloved/summer).

Vermilion

A bright red, specifically the red of the rose.

Volta

The "turn" or shift in thought or emotion in a sonnet, typically occurring between the octave and the sestet in an Italian sonnet, or before the final couplet in an English sonnet.

Wanton burden of the prime

The unrestrained, freely given offspring of spring.

Widow'd wombs

Wombs made barren by the death of the "lords" (the summer sun) who caused their fertility. Refers to harvest fruit as orphaned.
