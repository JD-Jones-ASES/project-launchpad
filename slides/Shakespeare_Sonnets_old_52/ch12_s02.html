Important Terms (1/4)

fair

Used with a double meaning throughout Sonnet 127: both `beautiful` and `light-complexioned`. Shakespeare highlights the collapse of the natural equation between light skin and loveliness.

successive heir

A legitimate heir who inherits by right of succession. Shakespeare uses this to present natural dark beauty as the rightful inheritor of beauty's throne, usurped by artificial fairness.

bower

A lady's private chamber or a shaded garden retreat, here symbolizing the sacred, sheltered place that true beauty should occupy but has been driven from.

fairing the foul with Art's false borrowed face

A phrase describing the use of cosmetics to make the ugly beautiful through artificial means. `Art` refers to skill or craft, and `borrowed` emphasizes the lack of genuine beauty.

raven black

Refers to the deep black color of a raven, a traditional symbol of darkness. Here, it describes the mistress's eyes, presented as authentically mourning the corruption of beauty.

Important Terms (2/4)

jacks

The keys of a virginal or harpsichord. Shakespeare also uses the colloquial sense of `impudent fellows` in "saucy jacks," personifying the keys as presumptuous rivals.

wiry concord

The harmonious sound produced by the wire strings of a keyboard instrument. `Concord` means musical agreement; `wiry` evokes the physical strings.

inward of thy hand

The sensitive inner palm of the hand, which the speaker imagines the keys kissing intimately.

chips

Small pieces of wood, used dismissively to describe the keys of the instrument, ironically highlighting their perceived advantage over the speaker's "living lips."

tickled

Lightly touched, but also carrying an erotic suggestion of pleasurable sensation in Sonnet 128.

Important Terms (3/4)

expense of spirit

The spending or exhausting of vital energy. In Elizabethan physiology, `spirit` referred to the animating vital force, and `expense` meant expenditure. Sexual activity was believed to diminish this...

waste of shame

A deliberate ambiguity meaning both `a squandering that results in shame` and `a shameful desert`—a ruined landscape left by the expenditure of vital energy.

perjur'd

Having broken a solemn oath or guilty of false swearing. Lust is depicted as a moral criminal, guilty of multiple offenses before gratification.

past reason hunted

Pursued beyond the bounds of rational thought, illustrating how desire overrides deliberation and good judgment.

a bliss in proof, and proved, a very woe

`In proof` means while being tested or experienced; `proved` means once the experience is over. This chiastic structure (bliss becoming woe) dramatizes the reversal.

Important Terms (4/4)

heaven / hell

The final words of Sonnet 129, enclosing the trap of lust: it presents itself as heaven but inevitably leads to hell, a path willingly chosen.
