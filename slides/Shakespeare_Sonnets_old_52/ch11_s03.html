Important Terms (1/5)

tables (Sonnet 122)

Writing tablets; flat surfaces coated in wax or chalk for making temporary notes, common as personal gifts.

full character'd (Sonnet 122)

Fully inscribed; written through and through. "Character" meant both a written letter and the act of engraving.

idle rank (Sonnet 122)

Useless grade or category; mere external position among objects.

tallies (Sonnet 122)

Notched sticks used for keeping numerical records before widespread literacy; a tally was split lengthwise and one half kept by each party as proof of a transaction.

adjunct (Sonnet 122)

Something attached as a supplement; an accessory.

Important Terms (2/5)

pyramids (Sonnet 123)

Ancient monuments; spectacular structures representing human attempts at permanence, not limited to Egyptian structures but any towering monument.

dressings of a former sight (Sonnet 123)

New costumes on old sights; the familiar disguised as the new, suggesting theatrical costume.

foist (Sonnet 123)

To thrust or pass off (something spurious) as genuine; to deceive someone into accepting an inferior or false article as real.

registers (Sonnet 123)

Official records; historical documents that claim to record truth.

scythe (Sonnet 123)

The curved blade traditionally carried by Father Time or the figure of Death, symbolizing the cutting down of lives.

Important Terms (3/5)

child of state (Sonnet 124)

A product of circumstances; something contingent on political fortune rather than intrinsic worth. "State" here means both political condition and personal fortune.

Fortune's bastard (Sonnet 124)

An illegitimate offspring of chance; something without stable parentage, implying no legal standing and easy disownment.

thralled discontent (Sonnet 124)

Enslaved resentment; grievance that has become a kind of bondage. "Thralled" means held in thrall, captive.

policy, that heretic (Sonnet 124)

Worldly cunning or shrewd self-interest, labeled a heretic because it operates only by short-term calculation, without faith in anything beyond immediate advantage.

hugely politic (Sonnet 124)

Supremely wise in strategy; powerfully calculating. Ironically, love achieves this by refusing to play politics.

Important Terms (4/5)

fools of time (Sonnet 124)

Possibly: those who are fooled by time's reversals, or those who are time's subjects; also possibly those who repent of a misspent life only at the moment of death.

bore the canopy (Sonnet 125)

Carried the ceremonial cloth held over a king, noble, or dignitary in procession, a formal honor but also a service role.

extern (Sonnet 125)

External appearance; outward show, used as a noun.

obsequious (Sonnet 125)

Devoutly attentive; devoted. In Elizabethan English, it did not necessarily carry the modern connotation of fawning servility but could mean "dutiful in service."

oblation (Sonnet 125)

An offering; specifically a religious offering or sacrifice, elevating the speaker's devotion to a quasi-sacramental act.

Important Terms (5/5)

not mix'd with seconds (Sonnet 125)

Unadulterated; not blended with inferior material. "Seconds" were lower grades of goods mixed in to bulk out a product.
