Key Concepts (2/2)

Political Allusion in Sonnets

Sonnet 107 stands out for its strong suggestion of topical historical events, possibly referring to the anxieties surrounding the succession of the English crown and the subsequent era of peace.

Important Terms (1/4)

Idolatry

The worship of idols; excessive devotion to something that does not deserve it. The charge addressed in Sonnet 105, implying that repetitive praise of one person might be seen as a false religion.

Wondrous

Wonderful; astonishing; beyond ordinary understanding. Emphasizes genuine admiration rather than mere flattery in Sonnet 105.

Argument

Subject; theme. Used in a rhetorical sense, referring to the core subject matter of the poem.

Invention

Creative power; poetic imagination. The speaker's "invention" is "spent" on the singular theme of "fair, kind, and true" in Sonnet 105.

Chronicle of wasted time

The historical record of the past; "wasted time" refers to time that has passed and been used up, not squandered. Appears in Sonnet 106.

Important Terms (2/4)

Wights

Persons; creatures. An archaic word for human beings used in medieval and Renaissance poetry, found in Sonnet 106.

Blazon

A detailed, itemized description of beauty in poetry, often enumerating features like hand, foot, lip, eye, brow. Derived from heraldry. Discussed in Sonnet 106.

Prefiguring

Foreshadowing; representing in advance. Used in Sonnet 106 to describe how past poets unknowingly sketched the beloved.

Divining eyes

Prophetic eyes; eyes with the gift of foreseeing the future. Used in Sonnet 106 to characterize the predictive vision of past poets.

Prophetic soul

A soul capable of foreseeing the future; the world's capacity for dread prophecy, as mentioned in Sonnet 107.

Important Terms (3/4)

Lease

A limited period; a contract of limited duration. In Sonnet 107, love's "lease" was supposed to be "confined" to a short, doomed term.

Mortal moon

Most probably an allusion to Queen Elizabeth I, whose symbol was the moon. In Sonnet 107, her "eclipse" could refer to her death in March 1603.

Sad augurs

Gloomy prophets; those who predicted disaster. Refers to pessimists whose prophecies of strife were disproven in Sonnet 107.

Balmy time

A mild, healing, pleasant time. Suggests both physical gentleness and medicinal properties, indicating a time of recovery or peace in Sonnet 107.

Subscribes

Yields; submits; acknowledges defeat. In Sonnet 107, Death "subscribes" to the speaker, conceding that poetry will have the final word.

Important Terms (4/4)

Character

A mark made by writing; a written letter or figure. Found in the partial Sonnet 108, referring to what the brain may capture in ink.
