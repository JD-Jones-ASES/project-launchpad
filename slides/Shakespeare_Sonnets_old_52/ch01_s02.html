Key Concepts (1/2)

Prolific and Diverse Output

Shakespeare's career produced a staggering volume and variety of work, including 37 plays, two long narrative poems, and 154 sonnets, demonstrating immense range without formal university training.

Histories as Political Sagas

The history plays form a cycle examining English kingship, power, legitimacy, and the human cost of ambition, often drawing on historical sources like *Holinshed's Chronicles*.

Tragedies of Intense Exploration

The great tragedies (e.g., *Hamlet, Othello, King Lear, Macbeth*) delve into profound philosophical and emotional territories such as revenge, jealousy, madness, and corruption.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Late Romances as Reconciliation

The final phase of plays, the late romances, are characterized by archaic plots, long separations, and supernatural elements, ultimately leading to themes of loss, forgiveness, and hard-won wisdom.

Narrative Poems and Literary Reputation

Shakespeare's two long narrative poems, *Venus and Adonis* and *The Rape of Lucrece*, were crucial in establishing his literary fame among contemporaries, predating his dominance on the stage.

Porous Boundary Between Plays and Sonnets

There is significant thematic and stylistic overlap between Shakespeare's plays and sonnets, sharing obsessions like the cruelty of time, the power of art, and the complexities of love and desire.

Dating the Sonnets

The precise composition date of the sonnets is debated but largely placed in the 1590s, with some later revisions, supported by references from contemporaries and early publications.

Sonnets as a Crafted Sequence

The sonnets are understood not as spontaneous outpourings but as a carefully crafted sequence by a master poet, whether based on real experiences, literary personae, or a combination.

Important Terms (1/2)

Commercial Stage

The public theaters for which Shakespeare primarily wrote, contrasting with writing solely for literary reputation or aristocratic patronage.

Holinshed's Chronicles

A multi-volume historical work that served as a major source of historical information and plot inspiration for many of Shakespeare's history plays.

Tetralogies

A series of four plays, specifically referring to Shakespeare's two groups of history plays that chronicle sequential periods of English monarchy.

Late Romances

The category for Shakespeare's final plays (*Pericles, Cymbeline, The Winter's Tale, The Tempest*), characterized by a blend of tragic and comic elements, magical interventions, and themes of redem...

Valediction

A farewell statement or gesture, often used to describe *The Tempest* as Shakespeare's metaphorical good-bye to his dramatic career.

Important Terms (2/2)

Narrative Poems

Long poems that tell a story, like Shakespeare's *Venus and Adonis* and *The Rape of Lucrece*, which were significant for his early literary renown.

Ovid's Metamorphoses and Fasti

Ancient Roman poetic works by Ovid that served as key sources for the myths and stories retold in Shakespeare's narrative poems.

Pirated Anthology

An unauthorized collection of literary works, such as *The Passionate Pilgrim*, which published some of Shakespeare's sonnets without his permission.

Terminus

A final or concluding point; the date of the first authorized publication of the full sonnet sequence (1609) acts as a *terminus* for their composition.

Literary Personae

A character or identity created by an author to speak in a poem or story, distinct from the author's own voice or experiences.

The Narrative Poems

Early Literary Reputation

Shakespeare first established his literary fame through these poems.

Venus and Adonis (1593)

Context

Published when London theaters were closed due to plague, turning Shakespeare's energy to print.

Dedication

To the Earl of Southampton, whom Shakespeare called "the first heir of my invention" (suggesting he initially valued poems over plays).

Content

Retells Ovid's myth of the goddess Venus's ardent pursuit of the mortal Adonis, who scorns her and is killed by a boar.

Style

Lush, witty, and at times "bracingly comic" (e.g., Venus's absurdity, Adonis's horse).

Success

A runaway bestseller, with ten editions in Shakespeare's lifetime, securing his fame as an author.

The Rape of Lucrece (1594) (1/2)

Dedication

Also to Southampton, with a warmer tone indicating a deepening relationship.

Contrast with *Venus and Adonis*

Grave and tragic (vs. comic and erotic).

Content

Retells the Roman story of Tarquin's violation of the chaste Lucrecia, and the political consequences (expulsion of Tarquin kings, founding of the Roman Republic).

Themes

Honor, shame, political power.

Technique

Long rhetorical set-pieces (Tarquin's monologue, Lucrece's lament) serve as "soliloquy-exercises," honing dramatic techniques for the stage.

The Rape of Lucrece (1594) (2/2)

Sources

Both poems drew heavily on Ovid's *Metamorphoses* and *Fasti*.

Anticipated Themes for Sonnets

Beauty inviting destruction, tyranny of desire, the consolation of art.
