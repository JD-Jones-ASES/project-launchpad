Important Terms (1/3)

Sonnet

A 14-line poem, typically written in iambic pentameter, with a specific rhyme scheme and often exploring a single theme or argument.

Iambic Pentameter

A poetic meter consisting of five iambs per line, totaling ten syllables, with an unstressed syllable followed by a stressed syllable (da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM).

Iamb

A metrical foot in poetry consisting of one unstressed syllable followed by one stressed syllable.

Trochee

A metrical foot in poetry consisting of one stressed syllable followed by one unstressed syllable, often used to create emphasis at the beginning of a line.

Spondee

A metrical foot in poetry consisting of two stressed syllables in a row, used to create weight or halt the forward movement of a line.

Important Terms (2/3)

Pyrrhic

A metrical foot in poetry consisting of two unstressed syllables, often used to create lightness and speed.

Rhyme Scheme (Shakespearean Sonnet)

A pattern of rhymes at the end of each line, specifically ABAB CDCD EFEF GG (three quatrains followed by a rhyming couplet).

Quatrain

A stanza or section of four lines, typically with an alternating rhyme scheme in Shakespearean sonnets.

Couplet

A pair of successive lines of verse, especially when they rhyme and have the same meter; in Shakespearean sonnets, the final two lines (GG).

Volta

The "turn" or shift in thought, argument, or emotion within a sonnet, often occurring at the beginning of the third quatrain (line 9) or the couplet (line 13).

Important Terms (3/3)

Archaic Language

Words or phrases that are no longer in common use but were characteristic of an earlier historical period, such as Shakespeare's Elizabethan English.

Thou/Thee/Thy/Thine

Elizabethan second-person pronouns: `thou` (subject form, like "you"), `thee` (object form, like "you"), `thy`/`thine` (possessive forms, like "your").

-est/-eth

Archaic verb endings: `-est` for the second person singular ("thou dost"), and `-eth` for the third person singular ("he doth").

Read Aloud

Why it's Crucial

Shakespeare wrote for performance; his language is inherently theatrical. Reading aloud allows you to experience the sonnets as they were meant to be heard.

Benefits

Practical Tip

If self-conscious, try whispering. The goal is to engage your voice and perceive the poem as a physical, temporal event.

Feel the Meter—But Do Not Force It

Iambic Pentameter

The foundational rhythm of Shakespeare's verse: five iambs (unstressed, stressed) per line, for a total of ten syllables (da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM).

How to Approach

Do not count syllables. Instead, read naturally, focusing on the meaning, and notice where the natural rhythm of speech seems to "resist" the underlying iambic pulse.

Identify the Rhyme Scheme

The Shakespearean Pattern

Dedicate a moment to confirm the rhyme scheme: ABAB CDCD EFEF GG.

Audible Architecture

The rhyme scheme acts as a map, delineating the poem's structure into three four-line quatrains and a final two-line couplet.

Structural Awareness

Recognizing the pattern allows you to anticipate the end of a quatrain and feel the particular force of the concluding rhyming couplet, which often signals a resolution or definitive statement.

Read the Couplet Last—and Again

Weight of the Couplet

The final two lines of a sonnet carry the full weight of the preceding twelve.

Strategy

Read the couplet once within the natural flow of the poem, then pause and reread it in isolation.

Questions to Ask

Does it resolve, reverse, or suspend the argument? Is the speaker confident or ironic? Does the rhyme feel triumphant or muted?

Complexity

Shakespeare's couplets can seem simple but often open into deeper complexity upon rereading, as seen in the conditional "if" of Sonnet 116.

Pause at Punctuation, Not Line Ends

Common Mistake

New readers often pause at the end of every line.

Line vs. Sense

In metered poetry, a line is a rhythmic unit, but not necessarily a complete grammatical unit. The sense often flows across line breaks.

Guidance

Follow the punctuation: pause at commas, stop at periods and semicolons, and let colons invite you to continue. This ensures you grasp the grammatical sense and avoid an awkward, stuttering reading.

Deal Patiently with Archaic Language

A Familiar Stranger

Shakespeare's English is an older version of your own, requiring patience.
