Important Terms (1/3)

Sonnet

A fourteen-line poem, usually in iambic pentameter, with a specific rhyme scheme.

Iamb

The smallest rhythmic unit in poetry, consisting of an unstressed syllable followed by a stressed syllable (da-DUM).

Pentameter

A line of verse containing five metrical feet (in this context, five iambs).

Iambic Pentameter

A line of poetry composed of five iambs, totaling ten syllables, with an alternating unstressed/stressed rhythm.

Octave

The first eight lines of a Petrarchan sonnet, typically presenting a problem or situation.

Important Terms (2/3)

Sestet

The final six lines of a Petrarchan sonnet, typically responding to or resolving the octave.

Quatrain

A stanza of four lines, as found in the Shakespearean and Spenserian sonnet forms.

Couplet

A pair of rhyming lines, especially the final two lines of a Shakespearean or Spenserian sonnet, often providing a conclusive statement or twist.

Volta

Petrarchan Sonnet

Also known as the Italian sonnet, structured as an octave (abbaabba) and a sestet (cdecde or cdccdc).

Important Terms (3/3)

Shakespearean Sonnet

Also known as the English sonnet, structured as three quatrains (abab cdcd efef) and a concluding couplet (gg).

Spenserian Sonnet

A variation of the English sonnet, with an interlocking rhyme scheme (abab bcbc cdcd ee) and a final couplet.

The Italian Form: Petrarch's Inheritance

Structure

It is distinctly divided into two main parts: an **octave** (the first eight lines) and a **sestet** (the final six lines).

Rhyme Scheme

The octave consistently rhymes `abbaabba`. The sestet, however, offers more flexibility in its rhyme scheme, typically following `cdecde`, `cdccdc`, or similar variants.

The English Form: Shakespeare's Innovation

Structure

The **Shakespearean** (or English) sonnet consists of three **quatrains** (four-line stanzas) followed by a concluding **couplet** (two-line stanza).

Rhyme Scheme

Its distinct rhyme scheme is `abab cdcd efef gg`.
