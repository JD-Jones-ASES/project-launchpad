Chapter 6, Section 4

Shakespeare's Sonnets 53-55

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Universal Beauty

The idea that the beloved's beauty is so profound and archetypal that all other beautiful things in the world are merely reflections or imitations of it. (Sonnet 53)

Constant Heart (Fidelity)

While external beauty can be found universally, the beloved's unique and most cherished quality is their steadfastness and unwavering affection. (Sonnet 53)

Truth and Inner Virtue

The argument that physical beauty alone is insufficient; it is inner character, integrity, or "truth" that gives beauty its true value and allows it to leave a lasting impact. (Sonnet 54)

Transience of Physical Beauty

The recognition that physical forms, whether human or natural, are subject to decay and fading over time. (Sonnet 54, 55)

Immortality Through Verse

The powerful assertion that poetry (the "powerful rhyme") can defy the destructive forces of time, war, and oblivion, preserving the beloved's memory and praise for all future generations. (Sonnet 55)

Important Terms (1/4)

Substance

The essential nature or deepest level of what something is made of; the opening question of Sonnet 53 asks about the beloved's fundamental composition.

Strange Shadows

Images or reflections belonging to other beings, implying that the beloved casts reflections that appear as other beautiful forms.

Counterfeit

A copy or imitation; also implies a forgery. Used to describe other beautiful figures as imperfect imitations of the beloved.

Grecian Tires

Classical Greek clothing and adornments, referring to the attire of Helen of Troy.

Foison

Abundance, harvest, the fruitfulness of autumn; generosity.

Important Terms (2/4)

Constant Heart

Steadfast, faithful, unwavering in affection; described as the beloved's most unique and valuable quality.

Ornament

That which adorns or decorates; but in Elizabethan usage, it could also denote something that gives genuine value.

Canker Blooms

Wild roses (dog-roses) without scent, often associated with corruption or infestation; contrasted with cultivated, fragrant roses.

Perfumed Tincture

Scented essence; "tincture" suggests a dissolved essence, giving the rose's perfume an almost alchemical quality.

Wantonly

In a free, undisciplined, or careless way, without specific cultivation or purpose.

Important Terms (3/4)

Unrespected

Disregarded, unvalued; referring to the fate of wild roses that are not admired.

Vade

To fade, to pass away; an archaic poetic verb emphasizing the ephemeral nature of physical beauty.

Distills

Extracts and concentrates the essence; verse is seen as this process, preserving beauty's fragrance.

Gilded Monuments

Monuments covered in gold leaf; hints at the artificiality of worldly splendor that cannot truly preserve.

Powerful Rhyme

Refers to the sonnet itself, boasting of its ability to outlast physical structures.

Important Terms (4/4)

Sluttish Time

Time personified as a slovenly, neglectful housekeeper who allows monuments to decay and gather dust.

Broils

Quarrels, battles, conflicts; also burning fires. Gathers both meanings in the context of war's destruction.

All-Oblivious Enmity

Hostility that brings complete forgetfulness; death and time personified as enemies erasing memory.

Pace Forth

To walk forward, to proceed; implies a stately, unhurried progression through time.

Ending Doom

The Last Judgement, the final day at the end of the world.

Sonnet 53: Universal Beauty and Constant Heart

Adonis

The archetype of male beauty in classical mythology, beloved of Venus, is described as "poorly imitated" after the beloved.

Helen of Troy

The legendary standard of female beauty, even when depicted in "Grecian tires" (classical Greek dress), is merely a "counterfeit" of the beloved.

Nature's Beauty

Even the beauty of spring ("spring, and foison of the year") and the generosity of autumn ("bounty") are presented as mere reflections or shadows of the beloved's attributes.

Sonnet 54: Truth, Beauty, and the Fragrance of Virtue

The Sweet Rose

A cultivated, fragrant rose, whose beauty is enhanced and valued because of its "sweet odour" (inner essence or truth). Even in its death, it provides a gift – its perfume – that endures.
