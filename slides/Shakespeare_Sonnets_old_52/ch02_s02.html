Important Terms (1/5)

Sonnet

A lyric poem of fourteen lines, typically written in iambic pentameter, with a defined rhyme scheme, focusing on a single theme or idea.

Giacomo da Lentini

A Sicilian poet from the 13th century, widely credited with the invention of the sonnet form.

Hendecasyllabic lines

Eleven-syllable lines, characteristic of early Italian sonnets.

Octave

The first eight lines of a sonnet, typically presenting a problem, question, or argument.

Sestet

The final six lines of a sonnet, typically offering a resolution, answer, or commentary on the octave.

Important Terms (2/5)

Francesco Petrarch (1304–1374)

An influential Italian Renaissance poet whose *Canzoniere* established the dominant themes and conventions of the sonnet for centuries.

*Canzoniere*

Petrarch's collection of 366 poems, mostly sonnets, largely addressed to an idealized woman named Laura.

Petrarchan conventions

A set of poetic tropes and themes popular in Renaissance love poetry, derived from Petrarch, including the idealized mistress, the suffering lover, blazon, and oxymoron.

Blazon

A poetic device in which a poet describes a beloved's physical features by cataloguing and comparing them to beautiful objects (e.g., eyes like stars, lips like rubies).

Oxymoron

A figure of speech that combines contradictory terms, often used in Petrarchan poetry to express the lover's conflicting emotions (e.g., "freezing fire," "living death").

Important Terms (3/5)

Sir Thomas Wyatt (1503–1542)

One of the first English poets to introduce the sonnet to England, primarily through translations and imitations of Petrarch.

Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey (c. 1517–1547)

An English poet who further adapted the sonnet, introducing the English (or Shakespearean) rhyme scheme (abab cdcd efef gg) and helping to establish iambic pentameter as the dominant meter in Engli...

*Tottel's Miscellany* (*Songes and Sonettes*, 1557)

A landmark anthology that published the works of Wyatt and Surrey, popularizing the sonnet in England.

Elizabethan Sonnet Craze

A period in the late 16th century (1580s-1590s) marked by a widespread enthusiasm for writing sonnet sequences in England.

Sir Philip Sidney (*Astrophil and Stella*, 1591)

An influential Elizabethan poet whose sonnet sequence revitalized the form and introduced greater psychological complexity and self-awareness.

Important Terms (4/5)

Fair Youth

The unnamed young man who is the primary addressee of Shakespeare's first 126 sonnets, a departure from the traditional female beloved.

Dark Lady

A mysterious, non-idealized woman who is the object of the speaker's complex and often degrading desire in Shakespeare's Sonnets 127–154.

Couplet

A pair of rhyming lines, especially the final two lines of an English (Shakespearean) sonnet, often used by Shakespeare to provide a summary, twist, or dramatic intensification.

Volta

The "turn" or shift in thought or argument within a sonnet, typically occurring between the octave and sestet in Petrarchan sonnets, or before the final couplet in English sonnets.

John Milton (1608–1674)

An English poet who revived the sonnet in the 17th century, expanding its thematic scope beyond love to include civic and personal themes.

Important Terms (5/5)

Iambic Pentameter

A common meter in English poetry consisting of five metrical feet, each comprising one unstressed syllable followed by one stressed syllable (e.g., "da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM").

Origins: Sicily and the Thirteenth Century

Structural Basis

Likely derived from the Sicilian *strambotto*, an eight-line folk lyric.

Content

Drew heavily on the refined conventions of Provençal troubadour poetry, which featured an idealized, often unattainable beloved and an introspective, suffering lover.

The Sonnet Arrives in England

Sir Thomas Wyatt (1503–1542)

Translated several of Petrarch's sonnets directly and penned original sonnets in the Petrarchan style.

The Elizabethan Sonnet Craze

Edmund Spenser's *Amoretti* (1595)

charted a courtship that, uniquely, ended in happy marriage, deviating from the Petrarchan norm of unrequited love.

Samuel Daniel's *Delia* (1592)

brought a melancholy grandeur to the form, exploring themes of time and poetic immortality, which Shakespeare would later intensify.

Michael Drayton's *Idea* (1594, revised through 1619)

included some of the era's finest individual sonnets, notably the famous sixty-first ("Since there's no help, come let us kiss and part") with its stunning **volta**.
