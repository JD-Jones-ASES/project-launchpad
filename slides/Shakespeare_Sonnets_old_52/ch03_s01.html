Shakespeare's Sonnets 1-17 – The Case for Posterity

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Procreation as a Moral Imperative

The speaker insists that the Fair Youth's beauty is a gift held in trust for the world, not solely for his personal enjoyment. He has a moral obligation to pass on his likeness to a new generation ...

Transience of Beauty and Mercilessness of Time

A recurring motif is the fleeting nature of beauty and the destructive power of time, which relentlessly leads to decay and oblivion.

Children as a Defense Against Oblivion

Having a child is presented as the primary means to combat time's destruction, allowing the father's beauty and likeness to live on.

The Poet's Verse as a Second Strategy for Immortality

Sonnet 15 introduces the poet's own work as another powerful method to defeat time, "grafting" the Youth's image into verse.

The Speaker's Evolving Role

The speaker begins as a gentle reproacher and counsellor, evolving into a friend, and finally, a poet who offers a unique form of immortality.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Double Immortality (Child and Poem)

By Sonnet 17, the procreation argument is joined by the promise of poetic immortality, offering the Youth two ways to live beyond his natural lifespan: "in it [a child], and in my rhyme."

Important Terms (1/2)

Sonnet Sequence

A series of sonnets linked by theme, subject, or narrative. Shakespeare's sonnets are famous for their interconnectedness.

Petrarchan Tradition

A style of sonnet popularised by the Italian poet Petrarch, typically focusing on the speaker's unrequited love for an idealised woman (e.g., Laura) and the suffering it causes.

Fair Youth

The unnamed, beautiful young man to whom the first 126 of Shakespeare's sonnets are addressed. In Sonnets 1-17, he is the target of the procreation argument.

Procreation

The act of producing offspring; having children. This is the central theme of Sonnets 1-17, presented as a duty.

Transience

The state or fact of lasting only for a short time; impermanence. This describes the fleeting nature of human beauty and life.

Important Terms (2/2)

Oblivion

The state of being forgotten or unknown. The sonnets argue that children (and later, poetry) can save one from oblivion.

Immortality

The ability to live forever or to have endless life. The sonnets propose two avenues to immortality: through one's children and through enduring poetry.
