Three Branches, One Government: A Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Separation of Powers

The fundamental principle of dividing government authority among three distinct branches (Legislative, Executive, Judicial). This design ensures that no single branch or group holds all the power, ...

Legislative Branch (Congress)

The branch primarily responsible for *making* laws. It is established by Article I of the U.S. Constitution.

Executive Branch (The President)

The branch primarily responsible for *enforcing* and *carrying out* laws, as well as managing the day-to-day operations of the government. It is established by Article II of the U.S. Constitution.

Judicial Branch (The Courts)

The branch primarily responsible for *interpreting* laws and deciding whether they comply with the Constitution. It is established by Article III of the U.S. Constitution.

Prevention of Tyranny

The core motivation behind the separation of powers; by distributing authority, the Founders aimed to prevent any single entity from gaining unlimited power, echoing their experience under a king.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Distinct Roles

Each branch has specific, limited responsibilities, and is explicitly prevented from performing the duties assigned to another branch.

Important Terms (1/2)

Legislative (LEJ-ih-slay-tiv)

Having to do with making laws. (From Latin: *lex* “law” + *latio* “bringing”)

Executive (eg-ZEK-yuh-tiv)

Having to do with carrying out and enforcing laws. (From Latin: *exsequi* “to carry out”)

Judicial (joo-DISH-ul)

Having to do with courts, judges, and interpreting the law. (From Latin: *judex* “judge”)

Separation of Powers

The division of government authority among three separate branches (Legislative, Executive, Judicial) so that no single person or group holds all of the power.

Congress

The body that forms the Legislative Branch of the U.S. government, responsible for making laws.

Important Terms (2/2)

President

The head of the Executive Branch, responsible for enforcing laws and running the government.

Courts

The institutions that form the Judicial Branch, responsible for interpreting laws.

Veto

(Latin for "I forbid") The power of the President to refuse to approve a bill or joint resolution and thus prevent its enactment into law.

Word Origins: A Deeper Look

Legislative

From Latin *lex* ("law") + *latio* ("a bringing or proposing") — signifying the branch that brings or proposes laws.

Executive

From Latin *exsequi* ("to carry out" or "to follow through") — indicating the branch that carries the law into action.

Judicial

From Latin *judex* ("judge," literally "one who speaks the law") — pointing to the branch that determines what the law means.

Veto

Directly from the Latin word *veto* ("I forbid") — a power held by the executive to reject proposed legislation.
