Important Terms (1/2)

Amendment

A formal change or addition to a constitution. The U.S. Constitution has been amended 27 times.

Bill of Rights

The first ten amendments to the U.S. Constitution, ratified in 1791. They protect individual rights and limit governmental power.

Freedom of Speech

The constitutional right to express one's opinions and ideas without fear of government punishment or censorship.

Freedom of Religion

The constitutional right to practice any faith, or no faith at all, without government interference or coercion regarding religious beliefs.

Petition

A formal written request, often signed by many citizens, asking the government to take specific action or address a problem.

Important Terms (2/2)

Anti-Federalists

A political faction that opposed the ratification of the U.S. Constitution, primarily due to concerns that it granted too much power to the federal government at the expense of individual liberties...

Ratification

The formal approval or adoption of a document, such as a constitution or treaty, by an authorized body. In this context, it refers to the states' approval of the Constitution and its amendments.

The Unsettled Constitution and the Call for Rights

• Where did the Constitution state that the government could not censor newspapers?

• Where did it guarantee that individuals would not be forced to adhere to a specific religion?

• Where did it prohibit the quartering of soldiers in private homes without consent?

What the Bill of Rights Accomplishes

• The government cannot dictate what individuals must believe (Freedom of Religion).

• The government cannot suppress individual expression (Freedom of Speech).

• The government cannot conduct unreasonable searches of homes or persons (implied by the Fourth Amendment).

• The government cannot claim powers not explicitly granted to it by the Constitution, reinforcing the principle of limited government.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is an 'amendment'?

A formal change or addition to a constitution. The U.S. Constitution has 27 amendments.

What is the 'Bill of Rights'?

The first ten amendments to the U.S. Constitution, ratified in 1791. They protect individual rights from government interference.

Define 'freedom of speech'.

The right to express your opinions without the government punishing you for what you say.

Define 'freedom of religion'.

The right to practice your faith — or no faith — without the government telling you what to believe.

What is a 'petition'?

A formal written request to the government asking it to take action or fix a problem.

Why were many Americans nervous after the Constitution was ratified?

The Constitution stated what the government could do, but not what it could not do, raising concerns about potential government overreach.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What was the main concern of the Anti-Federalists regarding the original Constitution?

They argued the Constitution needed a list of rights that the government was forbidden to violate to protect individual liberties.

Who drafted the series of amendments that became the Bill of Rights?

James Madison.

When was the Bill of Rights ratified by the states?

December 15, 1791.

What was James Madison's initial stance on the need for a Bill of Rights?

He initially argued against the need for one during ratification but later changed his mind and drafted the amendments.

What crucial role did the promise of a Bill of Rights play in the Constitution's ratification?

Without this promise, several states would not have ratified the Constitution.

What is the primary purpose of the Bill of Rights?

It protects individuals from the federal government by listing what the government cannot do.
