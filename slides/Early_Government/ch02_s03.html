Important Terms (1/2)

democracy

A system of government in which the people hold power. In ancient Greek, this concept combined demos (the people) and kratos (power or rule).

assembly (ekklesia)

In ancient Athens, a regular gathering of all eligible male citizens to discuss and vote on public matters, including laws, treaties, and declarations of war. It typically met on the Pnyx hillside.

citizen (Athenian sense)

Specifically in Athens, a free, adult male born to Athenian parents. This definition excluded women, slaves, and foreigners from political rights and participation.

direct democracy

A form of democracy in which citizens themselves vote on laws and decisions directly, without electing representatives to make choices on their behalf. This was the system practiced in ancient Athens.

aristocrats

Noble families who inherited wealth, land, and political power in early Athens before the reforms that led to democracy.

How the Athenian Assembly Worked

Meeting Place

The assembly typically met about forty times a year on a specific hillside called the Pnyx.

Participants

Any male Athenian citizen who was at least 18 years old had the right to attend, speak, and vote.

Process

Proposals were presented, debated vigorously by citizens, and then put to a direct vote. This was the purest form of direct democracy, where citizens made decisions themselves without elected r...

Powers

The assembly held immense power, deciding critical issues such as whether to go to war or make peace, approve treaties with other cities, and enact new laws that governed Athenian life.

The Limits of Athenian Democracy

Limited Participation

Only a small percentage of Athens's total population, roughly 10–15%, was eligible to participate in the government.

Exclusion of Women

Women, regardless of their social status or wealth, were completely excluded from political rights and could not become citizens in the political sense.

Enslaved People

A large portion of Athens's workforce consisted of enslaved individuals. They had no rights whatsoever and were entirely outside the political system.

Foreigners (Metics)

Even foreigners who had lived in Athens for generations, contributed to its economy, and served in its military, could not become citizens or participate in governance.

Cultural Achievements

Pericles oversaw the construction of monumental public works, most famously the Parthenon, and fostered an environment where arts, philosophy, and learning thrived.

Key Concepts (1/2)

Define 'democracy' based on its Greek origin.

A government in which the people hold power. From Greek: demos ('the people') + kratos ('power' or 'rule').

What was an 'assembly' in ancient Athens?

A gathering of citizens to discuss and vote on public matters, open to all male citizens.

Who qualified as an 'Athenian citizen' during the democratic period?

A free, adult male born to Athenian parents. Women, slaves, and foreigners were excluded from citizenship.

Explain the concept of 'direct democracy'.

A form of democracy in which citizens vote on laws and decisions themselves, rather than electing representatives to decide for them.

How was early Athens governed before the reforms that led to democracy?

It was ruled by aristocrats, noble families who inherited power, leaving most citizens with little say.

What major social problem did Solon's reforms address around 594 BC?

Debts were crushing, and poor farmers could be sold into slavery for unpaid debts.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What were Solon's key contributions to the development of Athenian democracy?

He canceled many debts, freed enslaved debtors, and opened the courts to all citizens, laying the groundwork for future democratic reforms.

Who is often called the 'father of Athenian democracy'?

Cleisthenes.

What were Cleisthenes' major reforms around 507 BC?

He reorganized Athenian society into ten new tribes (mixing rich and poor) and created a Council of 500 men chosen by lot to prepare laws for the assembly.

What was the specific name for the Athenian assembly?

The ekklesia.

Where and how often did the Athenian assembly typically meet?

It met about forty times a year on a hillside called the Pnyx.

Who was allowed to attend and speak at the Athenian assembly?

Any male Athenian citizen.
