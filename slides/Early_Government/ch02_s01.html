Important Terms (1/2)

pharaoh (FAIR-oh)

The king of ancient Egypt, who was also considered a living god. His word was the law of the land, embodying political, religious, and legal authority.

dynasty (DY-nuh-stee)

A series of rulers from the same family. In ancient Egypt, power typically passed from one generation to the next within these royal families.

monarchy (MON-ar-kee)

A form of government ruled by one person, usually a king or queen, who inherits their power, often for life. Ancient Egypt was a monarchy.

absolute rule

A system of government where one ruler has complete and unchecked control over all aspects of the state, with no limits on their power.

vizier

High-ranking government officials who assisted the pharaoh in managing the Egyptian state, often serving as chief ministers.

Important Terms (2/2)

scribe

Professionals responsible for writing, keeping records, and administering the bureaucracy in ancient Egypt.

Ra

The ancient Egyptian sun god, from whom the pharaoh was believed to descend, solidifying his divine status.

Hittites

An ancient people who established an empire in Anatolia; Ramesses II famously signed one of the world's earliest known peace treaties with them.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is a pharaoh in ancient Egypt?

The king of ancient Egypt, who was also considered a god, and whose word was the law of the land.

Define "dynasty."

A series of rulers from the same family, where power passes from one pharaoh to a son or other relative upon death.

What type of government is a monarchy?

A government ruled by one person, usually a king or queen, who inherits their power.

Explain the concept of "absolute rule."

A system where one ruler has complete control with no limits on their power, and no one to check that power.

How old was Ramesses when he became a powerful ruler, and what kind of legacy did he leave?

He was about ten years old and built enormous stone temples covered with his own carvings, led armies, and was called a living god.

What is the most crucial natural feature for Egypt's existence, and why?

The Nile River, because its annual floods leave behind rich black soil essential for crops, without which Egypt would be a desert.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How did the Nile River's characteristics necessitate a strong central government in ancient Egypt?

The floods had to be managed, canals dug, and grain stored for dry years, requiring a central ruler (the pharaoh) to organize people and keep them fed.

Beyond being a political leader, what other key roles did the pharaoh fulfill?

The pharaoh was also the chief priest and the chief judge.

What was the divine basis for the pharaoh's authority in Egyptian belief?

Egyptians believed the pharaoh was the son of the sun god Ra, meaning his commands were considered divine orders, not just laws.

What was the consequence of disobeying the pharaoh's commands?

To disobey the pharaoh was considered an act of disobeying the gods themselves.

How did the pharaoh manage the extensive governance of Egypt, and who assisted him?

The pharaoh relied on officials such as viziers (who helped run the government), scribes (kept records), tax collectors (gathered grain and goods), and pries...

How was power typically transferred between rulers in ancient Egypt?

Power was typically passed down through dynasties, where royal families kept power from one generation to the next.
