Important Terms (1/4)

republic

A government in which citizens elect representatives to govern for them. (From Latin: *res publica* – "the public thing" or "the people's affair").

senate

A governing council. In Rome, it was made up of wealthy, experienced citizens who advised and governed. (From Latin: *senex* – "old man").

consul

One of two chief executives elected each year in the Roman Republic. Their shared power and mutual veto prevented one person from having too much control.

empire

A government in which one ruler—an emperor—controls many peoples and territories, often by force.

veto

Latin for "I forbid." The power of one consul to reject the decisions of the other, an early form of checks and balances.

Important Terms (2/4)

patricians

The wealthy, noble families of ancient Rome who initially held most of the political power.

plebeians

The ordinary citizens of ancient Rome, including farmers, artisans, and merchants, who eventually gained more political rights through their tribunes.

tribunes

Officials elected by the plebeians in the Roman Republic to protect their rights and interests.

The Twelve Tables

Rome's first written legal code, inscribed on twelve bronze tablets around 450 BC, making laws public and accessible to all citizens.

Gaul

The ancient region that corresponds to modern-day France, famously conquered by Julius Caesar.

Important Terms (3/4)

Rubicon River

A small river in Italy that Julius Caesar famously crossed with his army in 49 BC, defying the Senate and initiating a civil war.

dictator perpetuo

Latin for "dictator forever," the title granted to Julius Caesar after his victory in the civil war, signifying permanent rule.

Ides of March

March 15th, the date in 44 BC when Julius Caesar was assassinated by a group of senators.

Augustus

The title meaning "the revered one" bestowed upon Octavian by the Senate in 27 BC, marking his official recognition as Rome's first emperor.

Princeps

A Latin title meaning "first citizen," which Augustus used to describe himself to maintain the illusion of republican continuity, despite holding absolute power.

Important Terms (4/4)

Pax Romana

Latin for "Roman Peace," a period of widespread peace and minimal expansion by military force experienced by the Roman Empire in the 1st and 2nd centuries AD, initiated by Augustus.
