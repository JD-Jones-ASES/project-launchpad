The Purpose of Courts

Courts serve three vital functions: resolving disputes fairly, protecting individual and constitutional rights, and punishing wrongdoing according to established laws and rules.

Important Terms (1/3)

Court

A place where judges and sometimes juries hear cases and decide whether laws were broken or disputes should be settled.

Judge

An official who presides over a court, applies the law, rules on evidence, and ensures fair procedures are followed.

Jury

A group of citizens called to listen to evidence presented in a trial and decide whether someone is guilty or not guilty (or liable in civil cases).

Verdict

The official decision of a jury or judge at the end of a trial.

Innocent until proven guilty

The fundamental principle that a person accused of a crime is assumed to be innocent unless the government can prove guilt in court "beyond a reasonable doubt."

Important Terms (2/3)

Criminal cases

Legal proceedings where the government brings charges against someone accused of breaking a law.

Civil cases

Legal disputes between people or organizations, usually over money, property, or rights, where no one faces jail time.

Prosecutor

The government's attorney who brings charges against a defendant in a criminal case.

Defendant

The person accused of a crime in a criminal case, or the person being sued in a civil case.

Plaintiff

The person or organization who brings a civil case against another party.

Important Terms (3/3)

Attorneys (Lawyers)

Legal professionals who represent either the prosecution/plaintiff or the defense, presenting evidence and arguments.

Evidence

Facts, documents, objects, or testimony from witnesses used in court to support an argument.

Appeal

To ask a higher court to review a lower court's decision, usually based on a belief that a legal mistake was made.

The Principle of Innocent Until Proven Guilty

Burden of Proof

The government (prosecution) must prove that the accused person broke the law. The accused **does not** have to prove their innocence.

Standard for Conviction

In criminal cases, the prosecution must prove guilt "beyond a reasonable doubt" – a very high standard designed to protect the innocent.

Importance

This principle acts as a vital safeguard against potential government abuse and tyranny. Without it, individuals could be easily convicted on mere accusation, threatening fundamental freedoms.

Levels of Courts

Lower Courts

Handle most cases initially.

Appeals

If a party believes a lower court made a legal error, they can **appeal** to a higher court to review the decision.
