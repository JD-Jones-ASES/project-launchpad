How Citizens Participate

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Citizen Participation

Taking an active role in the community or government, rather than passively observing. A republic cannot function without active citizen involvement.

Informed Citizenry

The necessity for citizens to stay updated on government actions, community issues, and political candidates to make sound decisions, especially when voting.

Voting as a Right and Responsibility

Jury Duty as Civic Service

Serving on a jury is a direct and vital form of civic participation, upholding the justice system and ensuring fair trials, which is a key protection in America.

Obeying Laws and Paying Taxes

These are fundamental duties that ensure a safe, fair, and functional society by funding public services (schools, roads, defense) and protecting everyone's rights and property.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Peaceful Legal Change

While citizens have a duty to obey laws, they also have the right and means to work peacefully and legally (through voting, petitioning, advocacy) to change laws they believe are unjust.

Volunteering and Community Building

Civic participation extends beyond government to non-paid efforts that strengthen communities from the ground up, such as helping neighbors, cleaning parks, or assisting those in need.

Important Terms

participation

Taking an active role in the community or government, not just watching from the sidelines.

informed citizen

A citizen who stays up to date on what government is doing and what issues the community faces.

volunteer

A person who gives time and energy to help others without being paid.

jury duty

The responsibility of an eligible citizen to serve on a jury when called by the court.

The Foundation of a Republic

• A republic, unlike a monarchy, relies entirely on its citizens to be active and engaged.

• Benjamin Franklin's famous quote, "A republic — if you can keep it," emphasizes that the survival and strength of a self-governing nation depend on its citizens' continuous support and participation.

• This participation goes beyond understanding the three branches of government; it's about individual citizens doing their part.

Comprehensive List of Ways Citizens Participate in Self-Government (1/2)

• Vote in elections and encourage others to vote.

• Stay informed about current issues and candidates.

• Serve on a jury when called for duty.

• Obey the law and work peacefully to change unfair laws.

• Pay taxes honestly and on time.

• Volunteer time and energy to serve the community.

Comprehensive List of Ways Citizens Participate in Self-Government (2/2)

• Contact elected officials to share opinions and concerns.

• Run for office or actively support those who do.

• Teach children about their civic responsibilities and the importance of participation.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is "participation" in the context of community or government?

Taking an active role in the community or government, not just watching from the sidelines.

Define an "informed citizen."

A citizen who stays up to date on what government is doing and what issues the community faces.

What is a "volunteer"?

A person who gives time and energy to help others without being paid.

What is "jury duty"?

The responsibility of an eligible citizen to serve on a jury when called by the court.

According to Benjamin Franklin, what is required to "keep" a republic?

Active participation from its citizens, as a self-governing republic is only as strong as the citizens who support it.

What is considered the most basic duty of citizens in a democracy?

Voting.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Is voting a right or a responsibility?

It is both a right (protected by the Constitution) and a responsibility (something citizens owe to their community).

What is a significant issue regarding voter turnout in the United States compared to other democracies?

Voter turnout in the U.S. is often lower, meaning many elections are decided by a minority of eligible voters.

Why is it important for citizens to be informed when voting?

To vote wisely, citizens must know who is running, what they stand for, and what issues matter.

What important protection relies on ordinary citizens' willingness to serve as jurors?

The right to a jury trial.

What is one of the most direct forms of civic service possible for a citizen?

Serving on a jury, helping decide whether a law was broken and ensuring justice.

What is considered one of the simplest and most important acts of citizenship?

Following the law.
