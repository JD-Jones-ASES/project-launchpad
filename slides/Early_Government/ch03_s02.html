Symbolic vs. Governing Power

Important Terms (1/2)

Absolute Monarchy

A government in which one ruler has complete power with no legal limits. The ruler's word is the law.

Constitutional Monarchy

A government in which a king or queen is the head of state but must follow the country's laws and constitution. A parliament or legislature makes the actual laws.

Heir

The person next in line to inherit a throne or title.

Parliament

A legislature, especially in countries with a British-style government. From French: *parler* ("to speak"), highlighting the role of discussion and debate.

Monarch (Word Origin)

From Greek *monos* ("one") + *arkhein* ("to rule") — literally meaning "one ruler."

Important Terms (2/2)

Magna Carta ("Great Charter")

A historic document signed by King John of England in 1215, which for the first time legally limited the power of the monarch, establishing that even kings must follow the law.

Divine Right

The idea that God chose the ruler, and therefore obedience to the monarch is a religious duty. This was a common justification for absolute power.

The Core Difference: Absolute vs. Constitutional

Ancient Egypt's Pharaohs

Were absolute monarchs. Their word was law, with no documents or councils to limit or overrule them.

The United Kingdom's King

Is a constitutional monarch. While he appears on stamps and lives in palaces, he does not make laws; elected members of Parliament do.

Absolute Monarchy

Definition

A system where the ruler has no legal limits on their power. They control the military, courts, and all major government decisions. Laws exist at their discretion and can be changed at will.

Historical Prevalence

This was the most common form of government for most of human history, seen in ancient Egypt, medieval Europe, and many parts of Asia and Africa.

The United Kingdom Example

How the United Kingdom Changed: A Shift from Absolute to Constitutional

Key Turning Point (1215): The Magna Carta.

English nobles compelled King John to sign this document, which was the first written limitation on a king's power, establishing the principle that the monarch was also subject to the law.

Centuries of Development

Over the centuries following the Magna Carta, the English Parliament steadily gained more power.

By the 1700s

The balance had shifted significantly, with the monarch reigning primarily as a figurehead while the elected Parliament governed.

Modern Examples

Today, constitutional monarchies include countries like the United Kingdom, Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Japan, and Spain.
