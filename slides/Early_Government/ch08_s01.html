Important Terms (1/2)

parliamentary system

A form of government where the legislature (parliament) chooses the head of government; the executive and legislative branches work together.

prime minister

The head of government in a parliamentary system; usually the leader of the largest party in parliament.

presidential system

A form of government where voters directly elect a separate president as head of the executive branch.

constitution

The written plan of government that all other laws must follow.

federal system

A system where governmental power is divided between a national government and smaller regional governments (e.g., states).

Important Terms (2/2)

monarch

A sovereign head of state, especially a king, queen, or emperor. In a constitutional monarchy, their role is largely symbolic.

legislature

The law-making branch of government (e.g., Congress in the U.S., Parliament in the U.K. and India, National Assembly in France).

impeachment

The process by which a legislative body levels charges against a government official, which may lead to a trial and removal from office.

The United States: Presidential Democracy (1/2)

System Type

Presidential System and Federal System.

Executive Selection

Voters directly elect the President.

Executive Term

The President serves a fixed four-year term.

Separation of Powers

The President (executive branch) is elected separately from Congress (legislative branch).

Removal of Executive

Congress cannot easily remove the President except through a difficult impeachment and trial process. The President cannot dissolve Congress.

The United States: Presidential Democracy (2/2)

Division of Power

A federal system divides power between the national government and fifty state governments.

The United Kingdom: Parliamentary Monarchy (2/2)

Word Origin: Parliament

The word "parliament" comes from the Old French "parler," meaning "to speak," highlighting its historical function as a place for debate and discussion. The English Parliament dates back to the 1200s.

France: Presidential-Parliamentary Mix

System Type

A unique hybrid system, combining elements of both presidential and parliamentary government. This is the "Fifth Republic" system, established in 1958.

Executive Structure

France has both a President and a Prime Minister who share executive power.

President

Directly elected by French voters for a five-year term. The President is typically responsible for foreign policy and national security.

Prime Minister

Appointed by the President, usually from the legislature (the National Assembly). The Prime Minister manages domestic policy.

Accountability

Both the President and Prime Minister are accountable to the legislature to some degree.

India: The World's Largest Democracy (1/2)

Scale and Diversity

India is the world's most populous country and its largest democracy, managing immense diversity in languages and cultures.

System Type

Parliamentary System and Federal System. Its parliamentary structure is modeled on the British system due to historical ties as part of the British Empire.

Legislature (Parliament)

The law-making body is called Parliament.

Head of Government (Prime Minister)

The Prime Minister is the head of government, leading the executive branch, similar to the British system.

Head of State (President)

India also has a President, but this role is largely ceremonial, similar in function to Britain's monarch.

Comparison Points

Executive Selection

Direct election of president (U.S., France) vs. election by legislature (U.K., India).

Executive Term

Fixed term (U.S., France) vs. term dependent on parliamentary confidence (U.K., India).

Head of State Role

Active governing role (U.S.) vs. largely ceremonial (U.K. monarch, Indian President).

Power Distribution

Federal (U.S., India) vs. more centralized (U.K., though with devolved administrations).
