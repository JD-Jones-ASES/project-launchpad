Key Concepts

Citizenship as a Daily Practice

Citizenship is not a status you gain at a certain age (like 18) but a set of actions and choices you make continuously. Every habit and skill of an adult citizen starts with practices in childhood.

Civic Virtue as Character

Beyond just following rules, civic virtue is deeply tied to personal character – the kind of person you choose to be, as exemplified by historical figures like George Washington.

Important Terms

citizen

A member of a country or community who has both rights and responsibilities.

civic engagement

Active participation in the life of a community, school, or government. This means getting involved and contributing.

student government

An elected body of students that represents student concerns and practices leadership in a school setting, mimicking adult legislative bodies.

respectful disagreement

Expressing a different opinion in a polite and constructive way, without using insults or personal attacks. It's about discussing ideas, not attacking people.

Practicing Citizenship at School

Democracy in Action

Classes often vote on activities, projects, or rules, providing direct experience with democratic decision-making.

Representation

Student councils or student governments are elected by students to represent their peers' concerns to teachers and administrators. This teaches about representative democracy and advocacy.

Rule of Law

Classroom rules are established to ensure everyone's right to learn and be safe, demonstrating how laws protect individual rights and promote the common good.

Authority and Common Good

Teachers, like governors, hold authority, but they are expected to use it for the benefit of all students, teaching about responsible leadership.
