Important Terms (1/4)

Constitution

A written plan of government that describes the structure of government, the powers of each branch, and the rights of citizens.

Confederation

A loose alliance of independent states or nations that cooperate on certain matters but each keep their own sovereignty.

Compromise

An agreement in which both sides give up something to reach a solution both can accept.

Ratify

To officially approve and put into effect. For the Constitution, nine of thirteen states had to ratify it for it to become law.

Articles of Confederation

America's first constitution, ratified in 1781, which established a very weak central government.

Shays' Rebellion

Constitutional Convention

Virginia Plan

A proposal at the Constitutional Convention, favored by larger states, suggesting representation in Congress be based on a state's population.

New Jersey Plan

Important Terms (3/4)

Separation of Powers

The division of governmental power into distinct branches (legislative, executive, judicial) to prevent tyranny and ensure checks and balances.

Legislative Branch

The branch of government responsible for making laws (Congress).

Executive Branch

The branch of government responsible for enforcing laws (President).

Judicial Branch

The branch of government responsible for interpreting laws (courts).

Federalists

Supporters of the new Constitution who advocated for a strong central government. Key figures included James Madison and Alexander Hamilton.

Important Terms (4/4)

Anti-Federalists

Opponents of the new Constitution who feared a strong central government and argued for greater protection of individual rights.

The Federalist Papers

A series of essays written by James Madison, Alexander Hamilton, and John Jay to defend and explain the Constitution and encourage its ratification.

The Articles of Confederation: A Failed Experiment

Limited Power of Congress

The central government (Congress) could not collect taxes, making it impossible to fund national operations or pay debts incurred during the Revolution.

No National Army

Congress lacked the authority to raise a standing army, leaving the nation vulnerable and unable to enforce laws or suppress rebellions.

State Sovereignty

Each state operated almost like an independent country, often leading to conflicts over trade and other issues that the national government could not resolve.

Difficult Lawmaking

Laws required the agreement of 9 out of 13 states to pass, making it nearly impossible to enact effective national policies.

Lack of Executive and Judicial Branches

There was no national president to enforce laws or a national court system to interpret them, further crippling the government's ability to function.
