Important Terms

Direct Democracy

A form of government in which citizens vote on every law themselves and participate directly in decision-making.

Representative Democracy

A form of government in which citizens elect representatives to make laws and decisions on their behalf; also called an indirect democracy.

Republic (political sense)

A government in which citizens hold ultimate power through their elected representatives, and there is no hereditary monarch; the public, not a royal family, owns the government.

Legislature

The branch of government that makes laws; in a republic, its members are elected by citizens.

Scale and Practicality

The vast size and population of the nascent United States made a direct democracy utterly impractical. A republic provided a workable framework for governing a large and diverse nation.

Comparison: Direct Democracy vs. Republic

Feature. Direct Democracy

Republic (Representative Democracy)

Decision Making. Citizens vote directly on all laws/policies.

Citizens elect representatives who make laws/policies.

Participation. High, constant, direct participation required.

Indirect participation through elections of representatives.

Suitability. Only practical for very small communities.

Practical for large, populous nations.

Complexity. Can struggle with complex issues due to lack of widespread expertise.

Representatives can specialize, leading to more informed decisions.

Minority Rights. Vulnerable to "tyranny of the majority."

Constitution and elected representatives can provide checks and balances to protect minority rights.

Accountability. Immediate accountability (direct vote).

Accountability through periodic elections (voting out officials).
