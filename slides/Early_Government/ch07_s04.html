Key Concepts (1/2)

Contradiction of Ideals

The institution of slavery, a fundamental denial of human rights, was finally abolished nationwide with the **Thirteenth Amendment** after the Civil War.

Important Terms (1/3)

suffrage

The right to vote in public elections.

equality

The state of having the same rights and opportunities as everyone else.

segregation

The practice of keeping people separate based on their race.

civil rights movement

The organized effort, especially in the 1950s and 1960s, to win equal rights for all Americans regardless of race.

Thirteenth Amendment (1865)

Constitutional amendment that abolished slavery and involuntary servitude in the United States.

Important Terms (2/3)

Fourteenth Amendment (1868)

Constitutional amendment that granted citizenship to all persons born or naturalized in the U.S. and guaranteed "equal protection of the laws" to all citizens.

Fifteenth Amendment (1870)

Constitutional amendment that prohibited denying a citizen the right to vote based on "race, color, or previous condition of servitude."

Jim Crow laws

State and local laws enacted after the Civil War in the Southern and some Midwestern states that enforced racial segregation and discrimination, primarily against Black Americans.

women's suffrage movement

The social and political movement in the United States advocating for the right of women to vote.

Nineteenth Amendment (1920)

Constitutional amendment that granted women the right to vote.

Important Terms (3/3)

Brown v. Board of Education (1954)

A landmark Supreme Court case that ruled state-sponsored segregation in public schools unconstitutional.

Civil Rights Act of 1964

A comprehensive federal law that outlawed discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin in public accommodations, employment, and education.

Voting Rights Act of 1965

A federal law that prohibits racial discrimination in voting and eliminated discriminatory practices such as literacy tests.

Women's Struggle for Suffrage

Original Exclusions

The original Constitution did not grant women the right to vote, leaving them disenfranchised for over 130 years.

Women's Suffrage Movement

Women organized and campaigned for decades through protests, petitions, and legal arguments to win the right to vote.

Nineteenth Amendment (1920)

This landmark amendment was finally added to the Constitution, ensuring that the right to vote could not be denied based on sex.

The Work Continues: The Arc of Justice

Adaptability of the Constitution

The Constitution is not static; it can be changed and amended to correct past wrongs and expand rights.

Citizen Power

Ordinary citizens have the power to organize, protest peacefully, challenge unjust laws in courts, and advocate for new legislation.

Tools for Correction

Amendments, legislative action by Congress, and judicial rulings by courts are essential mechanisms for a republic to address its own failures and bring its laws in line with its ideals.
