Important Terms

Civic Virtue

The qualities of character that help a citizen contribute to the common good; examples include honesty, courage, responsibility, and justice.

Patriotism

Love of and loyalty to one's country, expressed through both pride and a commitment to making it better.

Common Good

What is best for the whole community, not just for one person or group.

Public Service

Work done for the benefit of the community, whether paid (as a government official) or unpaid (as a volunteer).

The Story of Cincinnatus: An Example of Civic Virtue

Acceptance and Action

Cincinnatus, found plowing his field, immediately accepted the call to duty.

Swift Resolution

Within 15 days, he successfully organized the army, rescued Roman forces, defeated the enemy, and returned in triumph.

Remarkable Resignation

Crucially, Cincinnatus then did something extraordinary: he resigned his dictatorial power and returned to his farm. He believed that a good man should not hold onto power beyond what is necessary.

Illustrating Civic Virtue

This act epitomizes civic virtue, demonstrating selflessness and a commitment to the common good over personal gain or continued authority.

Key Civic Virtues Explained (1/2)

Honesty

Telling the truth, even when difficult. This builds the trust necessary for society to operate effectively, as dishonesty from citizens or officials damages this trust.

Responsibility

Fulfilling one's duties to family, community, and country without needing to be forced. It's about proactive engagement and accountability.

Courage

Standing up for what is right, even when there's a personal cost or risk. Examples include Ruby Bridges during the Civil Rights Movement and the suffragists advocating for women's voting rights.

Respect for Law

Adhering to laws, even those one dislikes. If a law is considered unjust, citizens should work through legal means to change it, rather than disregard it.

Concern for the Common Good

Thinking beyond personal benefit to consider what is advantageous for the entire community and nation.

Key Civic Virtues Explained (2/2)

Fairness

Treating all individuals as one would wish to be treated, without prejudice or bias based on who they are.

George Washington: A Model of Civic Virtue

Revolutionary War Leader

He commanded the Continental Army for eight arduous years during the Revolutionary War, leading them to victory.

Refusal of Kingship

After the war ended in 1783, despite calls from soldiers and citizens to become king, Washington famously refused, choosing instead to return to his farm at Mount Vernon.

First President and Second Resignation

Six years later, he became the first President of the United States. After serving two terms, he voluntarily resigned again, setting a precedent.

Civic Virtue in Everyday Life

Acknowledging Mistakes

Telling the truth after making an error.

Advocacy for Others

Standing up for a classmate who is being treated unfairly.

Community Care

Picking up litter in a park, even if one didn't cause it.

Respectful Dialogue

Listening respectfully to individuals who hold different opinions.

Active Citizenship

Learning about one's community and understanding its needs.
