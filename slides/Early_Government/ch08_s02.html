Monarchies: Old and New

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Monarchy as an Ancient Form of Government

Monarchy is one of the oldest systems of government, with roots tracing back to ancient civilizations.

Constitutional Monarchy

A system where the monarch's power is legally limited by a constitution and laws. Real governing authority resides with elected officials, and the monarch often serves a largely ceremonial role as ...

Absolute Monarchy

A system where the monarch holds nearly unrestricted power, with few legal or institutional checks on their authority. The ruler is typically both head of state and head of government.

Hereditary Rule

The practice of power being passed down within a family, meaning a ruler inherits their position from a parent or other relative.

Divine Right

An old, historical belief that a monarch's authority comes directly from God, making their rule unquestionable and absolute.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Magna Carta's Significance

A foundational English document from 1215 that was the first to legally limit the power of a king, planting the idea that even rulers are subject to the law.

Ceremonial vs. Governing Power

Modern constitutional monarchs primarily perform ceremonial duties, representing their nation, while elected bodies and officials handle the actual governance.

Important Terms (1/2)

Constitutional Monarchy

A form of government where a monarch acts as head of state within the parameters of a written or unwritten constitution, meaning their power is limited by laws and elected officials.

Absolute Monarchy

A form of government in which the monarch exercises ultimate governing authority as head of state and government, with no legal or constitutional limits on their power.

Hereditary (heh-RED-ih-tair-ee)

Pertaining to a characteristic, office, or right passed down from parent to child within a family; a hereditary ruler inherits their position.

Divine Right

The traditional doctrine that a monarch's right to rule is derived directly from God, not from the consent of the people, and is therefore absolute and unquestionable.

Magna Carta

A charter of liberties agreed to by King John of England in 1215, which limited the king's power and established certain rights for English citizens.

Important Terms (2/2)

Sharia

Islamic religious law, based on the teachings of the Quran and the traditions of the Prophet Muhammad, used as the legal system in some countries, including Saudi Arabia.
