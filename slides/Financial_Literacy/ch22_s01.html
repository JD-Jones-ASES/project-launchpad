Not a Victimless Crime

Identity theft goes beyond financial loss, leading to damaged credit, legal liabilities, immense time spent resolving issues, and significant emotional distress for victims.

Prevalence Due to Data Breaches

Identity theft is extremely common, largely fueled by massive data breaches that expose billions of personal records, often without victims' immediate knowledge.

Diverse Forms of Theft

Identity theft manifests in many ways beyond just financial, including tax, medical, criminal, and child identity theft, each with unique impacts.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Long-term Consequences

The damage from identity theft extends over months or years, requiring hundreds of hours to resolve and causing lasting credit damage and psychological strain.

Prevention is Key

Proactively protecting personal information is significantly less burdensome and more effective than dealing with the aftermath of identity theft.

Important Terms (1/3)

Identity Theft

The fraudulent acquisition and use of a person's private identifying information, usually for financial gain.

Data Breach

An incident where sensitive, protected, or confidential data is copied, transmitted, viewed, stolen, or used by an individual unauthorized to do so.

Phishing

A type of social engineering where an attacker sends a fraudulent message (e.g., email, text, call) designed to trick a person into revealing sensitive information or deploying malicious software.

Social Engineering

The psychological manipulation of people into performing actions or divulging confidential information.

Skimming Devices

Small electronic devices illegally attached to card readers (like ATMs or gas pumps) to steal credit or debit card information and PINs.

Important Terms (2/3)

Financial Identity Theft

The most common type, involving the use of someone's information to open new credit accounts, take out loans, or access existing bank accounts.

Tax Identity Theft

Filing a fraudulent tax return using someone else's Social Security number to claim their refund.

Medical Identity Theft

Using another person's health insurance information to receive medical care, prescriptions, or equipment, potentially creating false medical records.

Criminal Identity Theft

When an individual provides another person's name and information to law enforcement during an arrest, leading to false criminal records for the victim.

Child Identity Theft

The use of a child's Social Security number or other identifying information to open accounts or obtain services, often going undiscovered for many years.

Important Terms (3/3)

Credit Score

A numerical expression of a person's creditworthiness, used by lenders to assess risk. Identity theft can severely damage it.
