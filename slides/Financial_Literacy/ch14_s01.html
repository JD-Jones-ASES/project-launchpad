How Credit Cards Work: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Credit Card

A revolving line of credit that allows you to make purchases up to a set limit. It's a short-term loan where you spend the bank's money, which you are expected to repay.

Credit Limit

The maximum amount you are permitted to charge on your credit card. Exceeding this limit typically results in a declined transaction.

Billing Cycle

A regular period, usually 30 days, during which credit card activity is tracked. It includes the cycle opening, statement date (when the cycle closes), and payment due date.

Grace Period

The window of time (typically 21-25 days) between your statement date and your payment due date. If you pay your full statement balance by the due date within this period, you pay zero interest on ...

Minimum Payment

The least amount you must pay on your credit card bill by the due date to avoid late fees. It's usually a small percentage of your balance or a flat dollar amount, whichever is greater.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Credit Card vs. Debit Card

Credit cards use the bank's money as a loan, while debit cards draw directly from your own bank account.

Cash Advance

Using a credit card to withdraw cash. These transactions have no grace period, accrue interest immediately at a higher APR, and incur upfront fees, making them very costly.

Important Terms (1/2)

Credit Card

A financial instrument allowing purchases on credit up to a specified limit, representing a short-term loan from the issuer.

Credit Limit

The maximum amount of money a credit card holder is allowed to borrow or charge on their card.

Billing Cycle

The period, typically 30 days, over which a credit card company tracks a user's purchases, payments, and other transactions before generating a statement.

Statement Date

The date on which a credit card billing cycle closes and your official statement, detailing all charges and credits, is generated.

Payment Due Date

The final date by which your credit card payment must be received by the issuer to avoid late fees and, if the full balance isn't paid, interest charges.

Important Terms (2/2)

Grace Period

The interest-free period between the statement date and the payment due date, applicable to new purchases if the full balance from the previous cycle was paid.

Minimum Payment

The smallest amount required by the credit card company to be paid by the due date to keep the account in good standing and avoid late fees.

Cash Advance

A service offered by credit card issuers that allows cardholders to withdraw cash, typically from an ATM, against their credit line.

The Power of the Grace Period

Zero Interest Opportunity

If you pay your **full statement balance** by the payment due date within this grace period, you will pay **zero interest** on all new purchases made during that billing cycle.

Credit Card vs. Debit Card

Credit Card

You are borrowing money from the bank. It's a short-term loan that must be repaid.

Debit Card

You are spending your own money that is directly linked to your bank account.

The Dangers of Cash Advances

No Grace Period

Unlike purchases, interest on cash advances begins accruing immediately from the moment you take the cash. There is no interest-free period.

Higher APR

The Annual Percentage Rate (APR) for cash advances is usually much higher than the standard purchase APR, sometimes ranging from 25% to 29%.

Upfront Fees

There is typically an immediate fee for taking a cash advance, usually 3-5% of the amount withdrawn, with a minimum fee (e.g., $5-$10).
