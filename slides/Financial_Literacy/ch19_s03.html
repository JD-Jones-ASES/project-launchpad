Understanding the Roth IRA: Your Path to Tax-Free Retirement Growth

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

After-Tax Contributions

Money is contributed to a Roth IRA *after* you have already paid income tax on it.

Tax-Free Growth

Once funds are in a Roth IRA, all investment earnings (dividends, interest, capital gains) grow without being subject to annual taxes.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Income Limits

There are income thresholds that limit or phase out the ability to contribute directly to a Roth IRA for higher earners, though these are typically not a concern for young workers.

Custodial Roth IRA

Minors with earned income can open a Roth IRA, usually requiring a parent or guardian to establish a custodial account on their behalf.

Important Terms (1/2)

Roth IRA

An individual retirement account funded with after-tax dollars, where contributions are not tax-deductible but all investment growth and qualified withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free.

After-Tax Dollars

Money on which income taxes have already been paid before it is contributed to an investment or retirement account.

Tax-Free Growth

The accumulation of investment earnings (such as interest, dividends, or capital gains) within an account without being subject to taxation as it occurs.

Qualified Withdrawals

Withdrawals from a Roth IRA that are entirely tax-free because the account owner has reached age 59½ and the Roth IRA has been open for at least five years (known as the "five-year rule").

Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

Custodial Roth IRA

Earned Income

Money received from working, such as wages, salaries, tips, or net earnings from self-employment. Investment income or gifts do not count as earned income for IRA contribution purposes.

Income Limits

Specific adjusted gross income (AGI) thresholds set by the IRS that determine whether an individual can contribute directly to a Roth IRA and, if so, how much.
