Key Concepts (2/2)

Danger of Panic-Selling

Overestimating your risk tolerance can lead to selling investments during market downturns, locking in losses. The "best" investment strategy is one you can stick with through good times and bad.

Important Terms (1/3)

Time Horizon

The duration you plan to keep your money invested before needing to use it.

Risk Tolerance

Your financial and emotional ability to handle potential losses in your investment portfolio.

Financial Capacity (for risk)

Your ability to afford to lose a portion of your invested money without it jeopardizing your immediate financial plans or needs.

Emotional Tolerance (for risk)

Your psychological ability to remain calm and rational when your investment balance drops, without making impulsive decisions like panic-selling.

Stocks

Shares of ownership in a company, generally offering higher growth potential but also higher volatility and risk compared to bonds.

Important Terms (2/3)

Bonds

Debt instruments issued by governments or corporations, generally considered less risky than stocks but with lower potential returns.

Volatility

The degree of variation of a trading price over time, indicating how much an investment's value might fluctuate.

Age-in-Bonds Rule

A general guideline for asset allocation suggesting that the percentage of your portfolio invested in bonds should equal your age.

Emergency Fund

Money set aside for unexpected expenses, typically held in highly liquid, low-risk accounts.

High-Yield Savings Account

A type of savings account that typically offers a higher interest rate than a traditional savings account.

Important Terms (3/3)

Money Market Fund

A type of mutual fund that invests in short-term, low-risk debt securities, offering modest returns and high liquidity.

Panic-Selling

The act of selling investments rapidly during a market downturn out of fear, often locking in losses that might have recovered over time.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is your 'time horizon' in investing?

Your time horizon is the length of time you plan to keep your money invested before you need to use it.

How does a longer time horizon affect the level of risk an investor can take?

A longer time horizon allows you to take more risk because you have more time to recover from potential market downturns.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What factor makes an investor have higher financial capacity for risk?

Having an emergency fund and no immediate need for the invested money increases financial capacity for risk.

What characterizes someone with high emotional tolerance for risk?

They can stay calm watching their account value drop significantly without panicking or making impulsive decisions like selling.

What is a common and costly mistake investors make regarding risk tolerance?

Overestimating their risk tolerance, which leads to panic-selling during market downturns and locking in losses.

According to the text, what is the 'best' investment strategy?

The 'best' investment is one you will actually stick with, avoiding panic-selling during downturns.

Explain the 'age-in-bonds' rule of thumb for asset allocation.

Invest a percentage equal to your age in bonds (safer) and the remainder in stocks (higher growth potential).

Are the 'age-in-bonds' percentages strict rules?

No, they are guidelines. Your actual mix depends on personal goals, other income sources, and individual risk tolerance.
