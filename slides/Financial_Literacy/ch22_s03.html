Protecting Your Digital Identity: A Study Guide

Important Terms (1/2)

Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

A security process requiring two distinct verification methods (e.g., a password and a code sent to your phone) to confirm your identity before granting access to an account.

Password Manager

Software application that stores and organizes all your passwords securely in an encrypted database, typically protected by a single master password.

Spear Phishing

A highly targeted form of phishing attack where scammers research their victim to create personalized and convincing fake messages designed to trick them into revealing sensitive information.

Virtual Private Network (VPN)

A service that encrypts your internet connection and masks your IP address, providing a secure and private connection, especially useful on public Wi-Fi networks.

Skimming Device

A clandestine electronic device illegally attached to card readers (like at ATMs or gas pumps) to steal credit or debit card information when a user swipes their card.

Important Terms (2/2)

HTTPS

(Hypertext Transfer Protocol Secure) An internet communication protocol that encrypts data exchanged between your browser and a website, indicating a secure connection.

Strong Passwords

First Line of Defense

Passwords are the primary barrier for online accounts.

Characteristics of a Strong Password

Passphrase Strategy

A highly effective method for creating strong, memorable passwords is to use a string of four or more random words (e.g., "BlueTruckRainyHammer").

Password Managers

Purpose

To solve the challenge of remembering numerous complex, unique passwords.

Functionality

Software that securely stores all your passwords in an encrypted "vault."

Ease of Use

You only need to remember one strong master password for the manager, which then auto-fills other account credentials.

Impact

Using a reputable password manager is one of the most significant steps for improving digital security.

Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Definition

A security feature that requires two distinct types of verification to access an account.

Two Factors

Benefit

Even if your password is stolen, criminals cannot access your account without the second factor.

Social Media Privacy

Risk of Oversharing

Publicly available personal information (hometown, employer, birthday, pet names) can be used by scammers to create convincing, personalized phishing attempts.

Social Media Safety Practices

Spear Phishing

Oversharing makes you a prime target for spear phishing, where scammers use personal details to make their fake messages more believable and effective.

Public Wi-Fi Risks

Convenience vs. Risk

Public Wi-Fi networks (coffee shops, airports) are convenient but can be insecure.

Data Interception

On unsecured networks, others might be able to intercept data you send and receive.

Best Practices for Public Wi-Fi

Physical Security

Beyond Digital Threats

Physical security measures are also crucial for protecting your digital identity.

Shred Sensitive Documents

Shred bank statements, credit card offers, medical bills, pay stubs, and any documents containing account numbers or your Social Security number before discarding them.

Collect Mail Promptly

Mail theft is a common method for obtaining financial information. Consider a locked mailbox or P.O. box.

Watch for Skimming Devices

Inspect ATM and gas pump card readers for anything loose, out of place, or suspicious. Wiggle the card reader before use.

Shield Your PIN

Always cover the keypad with your hand when entering your Personal Identification Number (PIN) at ATMs, point-of-sale terminals, or gas pumps.

Monitor Your Accounts

Ongoing Vigilance

Even with strong protective habits, continuous monitoring is essential for early detection of identity theft.

Regular Statement Review

Check your bank and credit card statements at least monthly for unfamiliar or suspicious charges and report them immediately.

Annual Credit Reports

Request your free credit reports annually from each of the three major bureaus (Experian, Equifax, TransUnion) via AnnualCreditReport.com. Look for unfamiliar accounts or inquiries.

Account Alerts

Sign up for text or email notifications from your bank for transactions above a certain amount, withdrawals, or suspicious activity.
