Important Terms (1/2)

FAFSA (Free Application for Federal Student Aid)

The standardized form used to apply for federal student aid, and often for state and institutional aid as well. It is free to file.

Grants

A type of "gift aid" or free money for college that does not need to be repaid. Grants are typically awarded based on financial need.

Pell Grant

The primary federal need-based grant available to eligible lower-income students, which does not need to be repaid.

Scholarships

A type of "gift aid" or free money for college that does not need to be repaid. Scholarships are usually awarded based on academic merit, athletic ability, community service, specific talents, back...

Work-Study

A federal financial aid program that provides part-time jobs, usually on campus, to help students earn money to pay for their educational expenses. Earnings are paid directly to the student.

Important Terms (2/2)

Loans

Money borrowed to help pay for college expenses that *must* be repaid, typically with interest. Federal loans are generally preferred over private loans due to more favorable terms.

Financial Aid: Making College Accessible

Crucial Point

Financial aid is not automatic; you must actively apply for it.

The FAFSA: Your Starting Point

What it is

The **Free Application for Federal Student Aid (FAFSA)** is the essential form for determining eligibility for federal financial aid.

Broad Use

Most states and many individual colleges also use FAFSA data to award their own institutional aid.

Scholarship Search Strategies (1/2)

Start Early

Begin your scholarship search in your junior year of high school, or even earlier.

Apply Widely

Don't dismiss smaller scholarships ($500, $1000). Many have fewer applicants, and they add up quickly.

Look Locally and Niche

Check sources like:

Utilize School Counselors

High school counselors often have information on local scholarships that are only publicized through school offices.

Customize Essays

Tailor each scholarship essay to the specific prompt and organization. Generic essays are less likely to succeed.

Scholarship Search Strategies (2/2)

Reapply Annually

Most scholarships are awarded yearly, so you can often reapply in subsequent years of college.

Beware of "Full Scholarship" Language (Warning)

Always Verify

Ask for the specific dollar amount and precisely what expenses the scholarship covers (e.g., tuition only, tuition and fees, room and board, books, personal expenses).

Context Matters

A $10,000 scholarship is generous, but it's not a "full" ride if the annual cost of attendance is $60,000, leaving a $50,000 gap. Understand the actual financial benefit.
