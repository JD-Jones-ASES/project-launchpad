Important Terms (1/2)

Inheritance

Money, property, or assets received from the estate of a deceased person.

Short-term disability insurance

Insurance that typically covers a portion of your salary (e.g., 60-70%) for a limited period (e.g., 3-6 months) if you are temporarily unable to work due to disability.

Long-term disability insurance

Insurance that provides income replacement for an extended period, often years or until retirement age, after short-term disability benefits expire.

Beneficiary designation

The naming of individuals or entities who will receive assets (like life insurance proceeds or retirement account balances) upon the account holder's death.

Cost of living

The amount of money needed to sustain a certain standard of living in a particular place, including basic expenses such as housing, food, taxes, and healthcare.

Important Terms (2/2)

Relocation assistance

Financial or logistical support offered by an employer to help an employee move to a new location for a job.

Financial plan

A comprehensive roadmap outlining an individual's financial goals and strategies for achieving them, including budgeting, saving, investing, and risk management.

Receiving an Inheritance (1/2)

Do Not Rush

Immediately after receiving inherited funds, park them in a secure savings account. Avoid making any major financial decisions for at least 30–90 days to allow emotional processing and clear thinking.

Prioritize High-Interest Debt

Use the inheritance to pay off credit card debt or high-rate personal loans first. This is a guaranteed, tax-free return on your money, as it eliminates high-interest payments.

Build Your Emergency Fund

Ensure your emergency fund contains 3–6 months' worth of living expenses. If it's incomplete, allocate inheritance funds to fully build it.

Invest Thoughtfully

An inheritance is often a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity. Integrate these funds into your existing, well-thought-out financial plan rather than making impulsive investment decisions.

Divorce and Financial Separation

Immediate Account Separation

As soon as separation begins, separate all joint bank accounts, credit cards, and open individual accounts to prevent unauthorized use or new joint debt accumulation.

Update Beneficiary Designations

Review and update beneficiaries on all retirement accounts, life insurance policies, and other financial accounts to reflect your new marital status.

Review Credit Reports

Obtain your credit reports to identify all joint debts and understand your individual credit standing. This helps in negotiating debt division and managing your financial future.

Create a New Solo Budget

Develop a realistic budget based solely on your individual income and expenses. This transition from two incomes to one is a major adjustment.

Work with an Attorney

Divorce law varies significantly by state, particularly regarding property division, spousal support (alimony), and child support. Legal counsel is essential to protect your financial interests.
