Chapter 1, Section 3

The Three Functions of Money

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Money's Utility

Money is useful because of what it *does* (its functions), not what it *is made of* (paper, metal, electrons).

Medium of Exchange

Money simplifies trade by being universally accepted, eliminating the need for a "double coincidence of wants" inherent in barter.

Properties of Good Money

For money to work effectively as a medium of exchange, it must be widely accepted, portable, divisible, and durable.

Store of Value

Money allows individuals to save their wealth and transfer purchasing power from the present to the future, making long-term planning possible.

Unit of Account

Money provides a common standard for measuring and comparing the value of different goods, services, and debts, simplifying pricing and financial record-keeping.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Interdependence of Functions

A stable and effective monetary system relies on money performing all three functions simultaneously. Failure in one area (e.g., due to hyperinflation) can cause severe economic problems.

Important Terms (1/2)

Medium of Exchange

Anything that is widely accepted as payment for goods and services, facilitating trade by breaking transactions into two parts: selling for money and then buying with money.

Store of Value

Something that retains its worth over time, allowing people to save it today and use it to purchase goods and services in the future.

Unit of Account

A standard numerical unit used to measure and express the value of goods, services, debts, and wealth, providing a common language for prices.

Barter

The direct exchange of goods and services for other goods and services without the use of money.

Inflation

A general increase in prices and fall in the purchasing value of money. Rapid inflation can erode money's store of value and unit of account functions.

Important Terms (2/2)

Purchasing Power

The amount of goods and services that can be bought with a unit of currency.
