Chapter 26, Section 3: Buying Basics

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Down Payment

An upfront cash payment representing a portion of the home's purchase price. A 20% down payment is traditionally sought to avoid Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI), though many programs allow less.

Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI)

A monthly fee charged by lenders when a down payment is less than 20%. It protects the lender, not the homeowner, in case of default and is eliminated once 20% equity is reached.

Closing Costs

Various fees paid at the time of sale, in addition to the down payment. These can range from 2-5% of the loan amount and cover expenses like legal fees, title insurance, and appraisal fees.

Mortgage Payment Calculation

The monthly payment for principal and interest, determined by the loan amount, interest rate, and loan term using a specific financial formula.

Total Monthly Homeownership Costs

The comprehensive monthly expenses that include the mortgage payment, property taxes, homeowner's insurance, utilities, and a dedicated budget for ongoing maintenance.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Long-Term Cost of a Mortgage

The total amount of principal and interest paid over the entire loan term, which can significantly exceed the original loan amount, underscoring the impact of interest.

Maintenance Budget

An essential ongoing expense for home upkeep, commonly estimated at 1-2% of the home's value per year, which should be set aside in a dedicated fund.

Important Terms (1/2)

Down Payment

The initial portion of a home's purchase price that a buyer pays in cash upfront.

Mortgage

A loan provided by a bank or other financial institution to purchase real estate, with the property itself serving as collateral.

Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI)

An insurance policy that protects the mortgage lender if the borrower defaults on their loan, typically required when the borrower puts down less than 20% of the home's purchase price.

Closing Costs

A collection of fees and expenses incurred by buyers and sellers during a real estate transaction, paid at the time of closing (the final transfer of property).

Loan Principal

The original amount of money borrowed in a loan, excluding interest.

Important Terms (2/2)

APR (Annual Percentage Rate)

The annual rate charged for borrowing, expressed as a single percentage number that represents the actual yearly cost of funds over the term of a loan.

Equity

The portion of a property's value that the homeowner owns outright, calculated as the market value of the home minus the outstanding mortgage balance.

Sinking Fund

A fund created by periodically setting aside money for a specific future purpose, such as anticipated home maintenance or repairs.

The Down Payment

Definition

The initial sum of money you pay upfront in cash toward the purchase price of a home. The remaining balance is financed through a mortgage.

Traditional Target (20%)

Less Than 20% Down Payment

Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI)

What it is

A monthly fee charged by the lender when your down payment is below 20%.

Cost

Typically ranges from $50 to $200+ per month.

Purpose

PMI protects the *lender* in case you default on your mortgage payments, not you, the homeowner.

Benefit to Buyer

Provides no direct financial benefit to the homeowner.

Elimination

PMI is removed once your home equity reaches 20% of the home's original appraised value or current market value, depending on the loan terms and lender policies.

Closing Costs

Definition

Various fees and charges that are due at the time the sale is finalized ("closing"), paid in addition to your down payment.

Typical Range

Generally 2-5% of the *loan amount*.

Examples of Closing Costs

Example Calculation

For a $200,000 home with a $160,000 loan, 3% in closing costs would be $4,800 ($160,000 * 0.03).

Total Cash Needed at Closing

This combines the down payment and closing costs. In the example above, $40,000 (down payment) + $4,800 (closing costs) = approximately $44,800 cash needed on closing day.

Money Tip: Budget for Maintenance

Necessity

Home maintenance is not optional. All homes require ongoing care, including HVAC servicing, roof repairs, appliance replacements, plumbing issues, and general upkeep.

Rule of Thumb

A common guideline is to budget 1–2% of the home's value per year for maintenance.

Example

On a $200,000 home, this means budgeting $2,000–$4,000 annually, or approximately $167–$333 per month.

Preparation

This money should be saved in a dedicated "sinking fund" *before* you purchase a home, rather than being an afterthought or emergency expense.
