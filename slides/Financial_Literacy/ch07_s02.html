Key Concepts (1/2)

Zero-Based Budgeting (ZBB)

A budgeting method where every single dollar of income is given a specific job or assignment (spending, saving, or giving) so that your income minus all allocations equals exactly zero.

Income - Expenses - Savings - Giving = $0

The fundamental formula and goal of zero-based budgeting, highlighting that all income is fully allocated.

Prioritization

In ZBB, spending categories are typically prioritized: fixed expenses come first, followed by variable needs, then savings goals, and finally wants.

Net Income Focus

Budgets should always be based on your *net pay* (what you actually take home) rather than your gross pay, to ensure realism and avoid overspending.

Important Terms (1/2)

Zero-Based Budgeting

A budgeting method in which every dollar of income is assigned to a specific category—spending, saving, or giving—so that the total of all categories equals total income, resulting in a zero balance.

Net Income

Your total take-home pay for the month after all deductions (taxes, insurance, retirement contributions) have been withheld. This is the amount that actually hits your bank account.

Gross Pay

Your total earnings before any deductions are taken out. This amount is generally not used for budgeting purposes in ZBB.

Expenses

All categories of money you spend, including fixed costs (rent, car payments) and variable costs (groceries, utilities, entertainment, clothing).

Savings

Money set aside for future goals, including contributions to an emergency fund, long-term savings goals (e.g., down payment), retirement accounts, and sinking funds.

The Zero-Based Budget Formula

Net Income

This is your total take-home pay and any other predictable income for the month. It is crucial to use *net pay* (what you actually receive) rather than *gross pay* (your income before deductions).

Expenses

This encompasses all your spending categories, such as housing, food, transportation, utilities, entertainment, and clothing.

Savings

This includes contributions to your emergency fund, specific savings goals (e.g., a down payment), retirement accounts, and sinking funds for future planned expenses.

Giving

This category covers charitable donations, tithing, or personal gifts.

Budget Conservatively
