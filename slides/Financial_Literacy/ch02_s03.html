The Hidden Opportunity Cost

• A "sneaky" aspect of opportunity cost involves saving vs. spending.

• When you spend money today (e.g., $100), the opportunity cost isn't just that $100. It's also what that $100 could have grown into if it had been saved or invested, thanks to concepts like compound...

• Spending today has long-term financial implications that extend into the future.

Warning: "It was on sale!" is not a savings.

• A common misconception is that buying something at a discount (e.g., a $100 shirt for $60) means you "saved" the $40 difference.

• In reality, you spent $60. The opportunity cost is what else you could have done with that $60.

• "Saving" money refers to putting money away, usually for future use or investment, not purchasing something at a reduced price.

Did You Know: Historical Context

• The formal concept of opportunity cost was developed by economists in the late 1800s.

• However, the reality of facing trade-offs has existed throughout human history, from ancient farmers choosing crops to modern consumers choosing products.

Key Concepts (1/2)

Define opportunity cost.

The opportunity cost of a decision is the value of the best alternative option that you gave up when you made that choice.

When calculating opportunity cost, is it the value of all alternatives you gave up, or just one?

It is the value of the best alternative option you gave up, not all of them combined or a lesser alternative.

Does opportunity cost only apply to financial decisions involving money?

No, opportunity cost applies to decisions involving time and energy just as much as money.

Give an example of an opportunity cost related to time.

If you spend three hours playing video games, your opportunity cost might be the homework you could have finished, or the sleep your body needed.

Why is time often considered more limited than money in the context of opportunity cost?

You can always earn more money, but you cannot get time back once it's spent.

Maria has $200. She can buy new shoes or put the money towards a bicycle that saves her $200/month in bus fare. If Maria buys the shoes, what is her opportunity cost?

Her opportunity cost is the bicycle and the ongoing monthly commuting savings she would have gained by completing the bike purchase.

Key Concepts (2/2)

In Maria's $200 decision (shoes vs. bike), if she chooses to complete the bicycle purchase, what is her opportunity cost?

Her opportunity cost is the new pair of shoes she would have enjoyed.

What is the primary benefit of identifying opportunity cost, as shown in Maria's example?

It makes decisions informed by comparing the real value and consequences of alternatives, rather than just making them automatic.

What are the first two steps when comparing dollar values of alternatives to determine opportunity cost?

1. List your options. 2. Estimate the dollar value (or cost) of each option.

After listing options and estimating values, what are the final two steps to identify the opportunity cost?

3. Identify the best alternative you would give up. 4. That best alternative's value is your opportunity cost.

Darius can work an 8-hour shift for $9/hour or go to a concert with friends for $45. If Darius chooses to work, what is his opportunity cost?

His opportunity cost is the concert, including the $45 ticket cost and the experience of going with friends.

Darius can work an 8-hour shift for $9/hour or go to a concert with friends for $45. If Darius chooses to go to the concert, what is his financial opportunity cost?

His financial opportunity cost is the $72 he would have earned by working the shift.
