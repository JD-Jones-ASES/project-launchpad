The "Cost" of Waiting

The financial opportunity lost by delaying investments. This cost is not just the missed contributions, but primarily the missed compound growth on those contributions and their subsequent earnings.

Consistency of Contributions

The strategy of making fixed, regular contributions (e.g., monthly) to an investment account, which is a common and effective way to build wealth over time.

Important Terms (1/2)

Compound Interest

Interest calculated on the initial principal and also on all accumulated interest from previous periods. It’s "interest on interest."

Future Value (FV)

The value of a current asset or sum of money at a specified date in the future, based on an assumed rate of growth.

Annuity (Future Value of Monthly Contributions)

A series of equal payments or contributions made at regular intervals. In this context, it refers to the formula used to calculate the future worth of these regular monthly investments.

PMT (Payment)

The fixed amount of money contributed or paid each period (e.g., monthly contribution to an investment account).

Monthly Interest Rate (r)

The annual interest rate divided by 12, used for calculations involving monthly periods.

Important Terms (2/2)

Number of Monthly Payments (n)

The total count of monthly contributions made over the investment period, calculated as the number of years multiplied by 12.

Exponent (`y^x` or `x^y` key)

A mathematical operation indicating how many times a base number is multiplied by itself. It's a critical component in compound interest calculations.

The Formula for Monthly Contributions

FV

The final total value of the account at the end of the investment period.

PMT

The fixed dollar amount contributed each month.

r

The monthly interest rate. This is calculated by taking the *annual interest rate* and dividing it by 12. (e.g., 8% annual return means `r = 0.08 / 12 ≈ 0.006667`).

n

The total number of monthly payments. This is calculated by taking the *total number of years* and multiplying it by 12. (e.g., 47 years means `n = 47 * 12 = 564` months).

The Most Important Financial Takeaway

Start with whatever you have

Even small amounts like $25 or $50 per month can make a significant difference.

Time is the critical factor

The math relies on the duration of compounding, not necessarily large initial sums.

Example

A student investing just $50/month at 8% from age 18 to 65 will accumulate approximately **$310,000** at retirement.

Conclusion

The most expensive financial mistake you can make is **waiting** to start investing.
