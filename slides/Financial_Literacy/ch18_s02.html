Bonds: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Bonds as Loans

Unlike stocks which represent ownership, bonds are debt instruments where an investor lends money to an issuer for a fixed period.

Fixed Income

Bonds typically provide regular, predictable interest payments (coupon payments) to the investor.

Key Bond Terms

Understanding "face value" (principal), "coupon rate" (interest rate), "maturity" (loan end date), and "bond yield" (actual return) is crucial for bond investors.

Bond Yield Calculation

The current yield reflects the actual annual return an investor receives, considering the bond's purchase price, which can differ from its face value.

Bond Ratings

Independent agencies assess the creditworthiness of bond issuers, influencing the interest rate bonds offer; higher risk generally means higher interest.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Inverse Relationship with Interest Rates

Bond prices move in the opposite direction to prevailing interest rates. When rates rise, existing bond prices fall, and vice versa. This introduces "interest rate risk."

Portfolio Stability

Bonds generally offer less volatility than stocks and can act as a stabilizing component within a diversified investment portfolio.

Bond Ratings

AAA (highest quality) down through BBB (investment-grade)

These bonds are considered relatively safe with low default risk. They typically offer lower interest rates because the risk is minimal.

Below BBB (high-yield or "junk" bonds)

These bonds carry a higher risk of default. To compensate investors for this increased risk, these bonds must offer significantly higher interest rates.
