Budget Review and Updating

A budget is not a static document. Regularly (e.g., monthly) reviewing and adjusting your plan is crucial as your income, expenses, and financial goals change over time.

Important Terms (1/2)

Take-Home Pay

Your net income; the amount of money you actually receive after taxes and deductions.

Fixed Expenses

Regular, consistent payments that occur monthly, such as rent, car payments, and insurance.

Variable Expenses

Costs that change month-to-month, like groceries, entertainment, and gas. These offer more flexibility for adjustments.

Zero-Based Budgeting

A budgeting approach where income minus expenses equals zero, meaning every dollar has been assigned a specific job or category.

Savings Goals

Financial targets you are working towards, such as an emergency fund, a down payment, or future education.

Important Terms (2/2)

Net Worth

The total value of your assets (what you own) minus your liabilities (what you owe). A financial plan aims to increase your net worth.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the primary goal of building a monthly personal financial plan?

To move your net worth in the right direction by managing income, expenses, savings, and debt.

What are the five key questions a personal financial plan should answer?

How much money is coming in? Where does the money go (fixed and variable expenses)? How much goes to savings goals? How much goes to paying down debt? Does e...

When calculating your monthly income for budgeting, what specific amount should you use?

Your take-home pay, which is the amount that actually lands in your bank account after taxes and deductions, not your gross salary.

Define 'fixed expenses' and give a characteristic of them.

Fixed expenses are costs that are the same amount every month. They usually must be paid, and skipping them often has serious consequences.

How do 'variable expenses' differ from 'fixed expenses'?

Variable expenses change month to month, offering more control and flexibility, whereas fixed expenses remain constant.

Why are variable expenses typically the first place to look for savings when trying to reduce spending?

Because they change month to month, you have more control over them and can adjust your spending habits to find cuts.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What principle should guide your approach to assigning money to savings goals?

The principle of 'Pay yourself first,' meaning you prioritize allocating funds to savings before other discretionary spending.

How should savings goals be treated within your personal financial plan?

Savings goals should be treated like bills – they are not optional and must be assigned money just like other essential payments.

Explain the core concept of 'zero-based budgeting.'

In a zero-based budget, if you have money remaining after assigning funds to fixed expenses, variable expenses, and savings goals, what must you do with it?

You must give every remaining dollar a specific job; it cannot just 'float.' This ensures all income is accounted for.

What is the first action you should take if you discover your estimated expenses exceed your income when building your budget?

First, look at your variable expenses for potential cuts, as these are generally easier to adjust.

If cutting variable expenses isn't enough to balance your budget, what other category should you then examine for reductions?

Fixed expenses. You might look for ways to reduce them, such as finding a cheaper phone plan or getting a roommate to split rent.
