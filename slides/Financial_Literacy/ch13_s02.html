Chapter 13, Section 2: The Five Factors of Your FICO Score

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

FICO Score Calculation

Credit scores are not arbitrary; they are calculated based on five weighted factors that predict repayment behavior.

Payment History (35%)

The most significant factor, emphasizing the importance of paying bills on time. Late payments severely damage scores.

Amounts Owed / Credit Utilization (30%)

The second most important factor, measuring how much of your available credit you are using. A low utilization ratio (ideally under 30%) is best for your score.

Length of Credit History (15%)

Reflects the age of your credit accounts; longer histories generally indicate more experience managing credit.

New Credit / Hard Inquiries (10%)

Shows how often you apply for new credit. Hard inquiries temporarily lower your score, while soft inquiries do not.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Credit Mix (10%)

Demonstrates your ability to manage different types of credit, such as revolving accounts (credit cards) and installment loans (mortgages, auto loans).

Strategic Credit Management

Actions like setting up autopay, keeping utilization low, avoiding closing old accounts, and being mindful of new applications are critical for a healthy FICO score.

Important Terms (1/3)

FICO Score

A numerical representation of your creditworthiness, calculated by Fair Isaac Corporation, used by lenders to assess risk.

Payment History

Records of whether you've made payments on your debts on time.

Late Payment

A payment made 30, 60, 90, or more days past its due date, which severely damages your credit score.

Amounts Owed / Credit Utilization

The amount of money you currently owe on your credit accounts compared to your total available credit.

Credit Utilization Ratio

A percentage calculated by dividing your total current balances by your total credit limits.

Important Terms (2/3)

Length of Credit History

The average age of all your credit accounts, from the oldest to the newest.

New Credit / Hard Inquiries

The number of recent applications for new credit, which result in a "hard inquiry" on your credit report.

Hard Inquiry (Hard Pull)

Occurs when a lender checks your credit report as part of a credit application, temporarily lowering your score by a small amount.

Soft Inquiry

Occurs when you check your own credit, an employer conducts a background check, or a company pre-screens you for an offer. Soft inquiries do not affect your credit score.

Credit Mix

The variety of credit accounts you have, including revolving credit and installment loans.

Important Terms (3/3)

Revolving Credit

Credit that can be used repeatedly up to a certain limit, with balances changing month to month (e.g., credit cards, lines of credit).

Installment Loans

Loans with a fixed payment amount over a set period (e.g., auto loans, student loans, mortgages).

Factor 1: Payment History (35%)

On-Time Payments

Consistent on-time payments are crucial for building and maintaining a good score over time.

Late Payments

Even a single late payment (30 days or more past due) can significantly harm your score, potentially dropping it by 50–100 points.

Severity of Lateness

The longer a payment is late (e.g., 60 days, 90 days), the more severe the damage to your score.

Reporting Period

Late payments remain on your credit report for seven years, impacting your score for that duration.

Protection Strategy

Using autopay for at least the minimum payment on all accounts is highly recommended to prevent accidental missed due dates and safeguard your score.

Impact on Young Individuals

People new to credit often have lower scores because they simply haven't had credit accounts open for very long. Time and consistent credit management are the only remedies for a short credit history.

Factor 4: New Credit / Hard Inquiries (10%)

Hard Inquiry

When you apply for new credit (like a credit card or loan), the lender performs a "hard inquiry" (or "hard pull") on your credit report.

Soft Inquiry

This occurs when you check your own credit, an employer performs a background check, or a credit card company pre-screens you for an offer. Soft inquiries **do not** affect your credit score.

Types of Credit

Demonstrating Creditworthiness

While having only one type of credit isn't detrimental, managing both revolving and installment credit types demonstrates broader financial responsibility and can slightly boost your score.

Important Caveat

Never take out a loan solely to improve your credit mix. Only borrow money you genuinely need for a legitimate purpose.
