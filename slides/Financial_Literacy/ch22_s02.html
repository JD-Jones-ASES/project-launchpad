Chapter 22, Section 2

Common Scams

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Emotional Manipulation

Scammers primarily target emotions (fear, urgency, greed, sympathy) to bypass rational thinking.

Phishing

Fraudulent communication (emails, messages, websites) impersonating legitimate entities to steal sensitive information. Key characteristic: often uses urgency and directs to fake login pages.

Vishing (Voice Phishing)

Phone scams where criminals impersonate organizations (IRS, banks, tech support) to trick victims into revealing personal information or sending money.

Smishing (SMS Phishing)

Text message scams designed to make recipients click malicious links, call fake numbers, or disclose personal details.

Other Common Scams

Key Concepts (2/2)

Universal Red Flags

Common warning signs across all scam types, including urgency, requests for unusual payment methods (gift cards, wire transfers), too-good-to-be-true offers, and demands for secrecy.

Verification Rule

Always verify suspicious communications by contacting the organization directly using official contact information, never by clicking links or calling numbers provided in the suspicious message.

The Universal Red Flags

Urgency

Scammers always pressure you to "act right now or it's too late." Legitimate organizations provide ample time for verification and decision-making.

Gift Cards or Wire Transfers

Legitimate businesses or government agencies will **never** demand payment via gift cards, wire transfers, or cryptocurrency. These methods are preferred by scammers because they are nearly impossi...

Too-Good-To-Be-True Offers

If you didn't enter a contest, you didn't win a prize. Be highly skeptical of unexpected winnings, inheritances, or investment opportunities that promise unrealistic returns.

Requests to Keep it Secret

Legitimate requests or emergencies do not require secrecy from your family or friends. Scammers want to isolate you to prevent others from identifying the fraud.
