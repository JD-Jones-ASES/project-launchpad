Key Concepts (1/2)

W-2 Employee

Employment Categories by Duration/Hours

Jobs can be full-time (35+ hours/week, most benefits), part-time (<35 hours/week, fewer benefits), seasonal (specific time of year), or temporary (short-term, often via staffing agencies).

Key Concepts (2/2)

Hourly Wage vs. Salary

An hourly wage is pay per hour worked (often subject to overtime rules), while a salary is a fixed annual amount regardless of hours worked.

Calculating Annual Income from Hourly Wage

A crucial skill for comparing different types of job offers, using the formula `Hourly Rate × Hours per Week × 52`.

Total Compensation

When comparing jobs, it's vital to consider not just salary or hourly pay, but also the monetary value of benefits, as they can significantly impact overall financial well-being.

Commission

Pay based on the value of sales made, which can be the sole form of income or an addition to a base salary.

Important Terms (1/3)

W-2 Employee

A worker whose employer controls their work and withholds taxes from their pay, also paying half of FICA taxes.

Benefits

Non-wage compensation provided by an employer, such as health insurance, paid time off, and retirement plan contributions.

1099 Contractor (Independent Contractor/Freelancer)

A self-employed worker hired for a specific task or project, responsible for all their own taxes, including self-employment tax.

FICA (Federal Insurance Contributions Act)

Taxes that fund Social Security and Medicare. W-2 employees split FICA with their employer; 1099 contractors pay both halves as self-employment tax.

W-2 Form

A tax form sent by employers to W-2 employees at year-end, reporting total wages and taxes withheld.

Important Terms (2/3)

1099-NEC Form

A tax form sent by clients to 1099 contractors who earned $600 or more, reporting payments made.

Full-time employment

Typically working 35 or more hours per week; most likely to receive benefits.

Part-time employment

Generally working fewer than 35 hours per week; benefits are less common.

Seasonal employment

Work that lasts only during a particular time of year (e.g., holidays, summer).

Temporary employment

Short-term work to fill a specific need, often through a staffing agency.

Important Terms (3/3)

Salary

A fixed amount of pay for a year's work, paid in equal installments, regardless of hours worked.

Hourly Wage

Pay based on the number of hours worked, with earnings calculated by multiplying the hourly rate by hours worked. Overtime rules usually apply.

Commission

Pay based on the value or volume of sales made.

1099 Independent Contractors (2/2)

No Benefits

Generally, 1099 contractors do not receive benefits from the companies they work for. They must purchase their own health insurance, save for retirement, etc.

Full-Time, Part-Time, Seasonal, and Temporary Employment

Full-time employment

Typically involves working 35 or more hours per week. Full-time W-2 employees are the most likely to receive a full package of benefits.

Part-time employment

Generally involves working fewer than 35 hours per week. Benefits are less common for part-time workers, though some employers may offer limited benefits.

Seasonal employment

Work that lasts only during a particular time of year, such as retail during holiday seasons or agricultural work during harvest.

Temporary employment

Short-term work designed to fill a specific need, often arranged through a staffing agency. Temporary workers may be W-2 employees of the agency, rather than the company where they perform the work.

Calculating Annual Income from an Hourly Wage

Salary

A fixed amount of pay for a year's work, divided into equal paychecks, regardless of the exact hours worked.

Hourly Wage

Pay based on the number of hours worked. Hourly employees are typically subject to overtime rules (extra pay for hours beyond 40 per week), which usually don't apply to salaried employees.
