Legal and Tax Basics for Entrepreneurs

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Business Structures

The legal framework chosen for a business (e.g., Sole Proprietorship, LLC) impacts liability, taxation, and administrative requirements.

Sole Proprietorship

The simplest and default business structure, where the individual and business are legally inseparable, leading to unlimited personal liability but minimal setup hassle.

Limited Liability Company (LLC)

A structure that creates a separate legal entity for the business, offering limited liability to the owner, thereby protecting personal assets from business debts and lawsuits.

Self-Employment (SE) Tax

The Social Security and Medicare taxes that self-employed individuals must pay, covering both the employer and employee portions (a total of 15.3%) on their net business earnings.

Tax Deductions

Legitimate business expenses that can be subtracted from a business's gross income to reduce its taxable profit, thereby lowering the overall tax burden.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Separate Business Finances

Maintaining distinct bank accounts for business income and expenses is crucial for accurate record-keeping, legal protection, and clear financial analysis.

Important Terms (1/2)

Sole Proprietorship

A business structure where an individual and their business are considered the same legal entity. The owner reports all business income and expenses on their personal tax return.

Unlimited Personal Liability

A characteristic of sole proprietorships where the owner is personally responsible for all business debts and legal obligations, meaning personal assets can be at risk.

Limited Liability Company (LLC)

A flexible business structure that combines elements of a partnership or sole proprietorship with the limited liability protection of a corporation.

Limited Liability

A legal protection offered by structures like LLCs, where the owner's personal assets are generally shielded from business debts and lawsuits.

S-Corporation

A more complex corporate structure designed for businesses with significant revenue, offering specific tax advantages not typically relevant for small side hustles.

Important Terms (2/2)

FICA Taxes

Federal Insurance Contributions Act taxes, which fund Social Security and Medicare. Employees and employers each pay half (7.65% each, totaling 15.3%) through payroll deductions.

Self-Employment (SE) Tax

The Social Security and Medicare taxes (totaling 15.3%) that self-employed individuals pay on their net earnings, covering both the employer and employee portions.

Net Earnings/Profit

The amount of money a business has left after subtracting all legitimate business expenses from its total revenue.

Quarterly Estimated Tax Payments

Payments made to the IRS throughout the year by self-employed individuals and others who do not have taxes withheld from their income, to avoid penalties for underpayment.

Tax-Deductible Expenses

Costs incurred solely for the purpose of operating a business that can be subtracted from gross income when calculating taxable profit, thereby reducing the amount of tax owed.

How does the calculation of FICA taxes differ for an employee versus a self-employed individual?

As an employee, your employer pays half (7.65%) of FICA taxes, and you pay the other half. As self-employed, you pay both halves, totaling 15.3%.

What percentage of net earnings does the IRS use to calculate Self-Employment tax, and why?

The IRS calculates SE tax on 92.35% of net earnings because you are allowed to deduct the 'employer half' of SE tax from your net earnings before calculation.

Set aside 25–30% of your business profit for taxes, which generally covers both Self-Employment tax and regular income tax across most scenarios.
