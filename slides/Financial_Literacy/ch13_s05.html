Checking and Monitoring Your Credit Score: A Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

The Importance of Monitoring

Regularly checking your credit report and score is a fundamental financial habit for identifying errors, preventing identity theft, and understanding your financial standing.

Free Annual Credit Reports

Federal law (Fair Credit Reporting Act) entitles every American to one free credit report annually from each of the three major credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) via AnnualCreditReport...

Staggering Reports

Instead of pulling all three reports at once, it's advised to request one report every four months from a different bureau to monitor your file more frequently throughout the year for free.

What to Look For

Key items to check on your credit report include accounts you didn't open, incorrect payment history, wrong account details, outdated negative information, and duplicate accounts.

Disputing Errors

If you find an error, you have the right to dispute it in writing with the credit bureau, which must investigate and correct verified errors within approximately 30 days.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Hard vs. Soft Inquiries

Only "hard inquiries" (typically from applying for new credit) affect your credit score; "soft inquiries" (like checking your own score or pre-approvals) do not.

Free Score Monitoring Options

Many banks, credit card issuers, and some websites now offer free access to your credit score (often a FICO score or similar model) through their online portals or mobile apps.

Building a Monitoring Habit

A simple routine includes pulling one free report every four months, checking your score monthly, setting payment reminders, and immediately investigating unrecognized accounts or inquiries.

Starting Early

It's beneficial to start building credit history as a young adult, even before it's urgently needed, to establish a solid financial foundation.

Important Terms (1/2)

Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA)

A federal law that promotes the accuracy, fairness, and privacy of consumer information contained in the files of consumer reporting agencies (credit bureaus). It grants the right to a free annual ...

Credit Bureaus (Major Three)

The three main national credit reporting agencies that collect and maintain credit information on consumers: Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion.

AnnualCreditReport.com

The *only* official, government-mandated website to obtain your truly free annual credit reports from each of the three major credit bureaus.

Hard Inquiry

An inquiry into your credit file that typically occurs when you apply for new credit (e.g., a loan, credit card). These can temporarily lower your credit score.

Soft Inquiry

An inquiry into your credit file that does not affect your credit score. Examples include checking your own credit score, pre-approved credit offers, or employment background checks.

Important Terms (2/2)

Identity Theft

The fraudulent acquisition and use of a person's private identifying information, usually for financial gain. Checking your credit report helps detect this.

Credit Report Errors

Incorrect or outdated information found on your credit report, such as wrong account balances, late payments reported incorrectly, or accounts you didn't open.

Credit Repair Services

Companies that claim to improve your credit score for a fee. Many are legitimate, but some are scams that promise illegal actions like removing accurate negative information.

Your Free Annual Credit Report

Federal Mandate

Official Website

What to Look for in Your Credit Report

Accounts you did not open

A strong indicator of identity theft or fraud.

Incorrect payment history

For instance, on-time payments mistakenly listed as late.

Wrong account information

Check for incorrect balances, credit limits, or account statuses (e.g., an open account listed as closed).

Outdated negative information

Most negative items (like late payments, collections) must be removed after seven years. Bankruptcies must be removed after ten years.

Duplicate accounts

The same account listed multiple times, which can skew your report.

Free Score Monitoring Options

Banks and Credit Unions

Many financial institutions display your credit score directly in their mobile apps or online banking portals.

Credit Card Issuers

A significant number of credit card companies provide free monthly credit score access to their cardholders.

Other Websites

Some websites offer free score access, often in exchange for displaying targeted credit card or loan offers.

Red Flags to Watch For

Genuine Credit Repair

The *only* things that genuinely repair your credit are consistent on-time payments, maintaining lower credit balances, and the passage of time.
