Chapter 15, Section 2

Fixed vs. Variable Interest Rates

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Fixed-Rate Loan

An interest rate that remains constant throughout the entire life of the loan, ensuring consistent monthly payments.

Variable-Rate Loan (Adjustable-Rate Loan)

An interest rate that can change over time, typically tied to a market benchmark, leading to fluctuating monthly payments.

Market Benchmark

A publicly published interest rate (e.g., U.S. Prime Rate, SOFR) that variable rates are indexed to, causing them to rise or fall with market conditions.

Predictability vs. Flexibility/Risk

The core trade-off between fixed (predictable but less flexible if rates fall) and variable (potentially lower initial payments and savings if rates fall, but with significant risk if rates rise).

Payment Shock

The sudden, substantial increase in monthly payments on a variable-rate loan due to a sharp rise in interest rates.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Adjustable-Rate Mortgages (ARMs)

A common type of variable-rate loan for mortgages, often featuring an initial fixed "teaser" rate before adjusting periodically.

Important Terms (1/2)

Fixed-Rate Loan

A loan where the interest rate is locked in and does not change for the entire duration of the loan, resulting in consistent monthly payments.

Variable-Rate Loan (Adjustable-Rate Loan)

A loan where the interest rate can change periodically based on movements in a specified market index, leading to fluctuating monthly payments.

Market Benchmark

A standard, publicly available interest rate (such as the U.S. Prime Rate or SOFR) that serves as a reference point for calculating the interest rate on variable-rate loans.

U.S. Prime Rate

A key benchmark interest rate used by banks in the United States, often influencing various consumer and commercial loans.

Secured Overnight Financing Rate (SOFR)

A broad measure of the cost of borrowing cash overnight collateralized by Treasury securities, increasingly used as a benchmark for variable-rate loans.

Important Terms (2/2)

Payment Shock

A severe and unexpected increase in a borrower's monthly loan payment, typically occurring with variable-rate loans when interest rates rise significantly.

Adjustable-Rate Mortgage (ARM)

A type of home loan where the interest rate is fixed for an initial period and then adjusts at regular intervals according to a market index.

Teaser Rate

An artificially low initial interest rate offered on some adjustable-rate mortgages to attract borrowers, which will later reset to a higher, market-based rate.

Refinance

The process of paying off an existing loan with a new loan, often to secure a lower interest rate, change loan terms, or convert a variable rate to a fixed rate.

Fixed-Rate Loans

Predictability

Borrowers have the distinct advantage of knowing the exact amount of their monthly payment for the full term of the loan. This makes personal budgeting straightforward and reliable.

Protection from Rate Increases

If general market interest rates rise after you've secured your loan, your locked-in rate prevents your payments from increasing. You continue to pay at the original, potentially lower, rate.

Variable-Rate Loans

Often Starts Lower

Variable rates are frequently lower than comparable fixed rates at the time of origination. This can result in lower initial monthly payments, making the loan more affordable in the short term.

Potential Savings if Rates Fall

If the market benchmark rates decline during your loan term, your interest rate will automatically decrease, leading to lower monthly payments and potential overall savings on interest.

Uncertainty

The primary drawback is the lack of predictability. If market interest rates rise, your payments will increase, potentially making budgeting difficult.

Adjustable-Rate Mortgages (ARMs)

The first number (e.g., 5)

Indicates the number of years the initial interest rate is fixed. In a "5/1 ARM," the rate is fixed for the first 5 years.

The second number (e.g., 1)

Indicates how frequently the rate will adjust after the initial fixed period ends. In a "5/1 ARM," after the first 5 years, the rate will adjust every 1 year based on the prevailing market index.
