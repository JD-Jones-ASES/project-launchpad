Key Concepts (1/2)

The Financial Literacy Gap

This refers to the significant difference between what people need to know about managing money and what they actually know. It affects individuals across all age and income levels.

Financial Literacy Defined

It is the ability to understand and effectively use various financial skills, including personal financial management, budgeting, and investing, to make informed decisions.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Course Objective

The course aims to bridge this gap by providing practical knowledge and fostering essential financial habits, empowering individuals to confidently manage their money.

Important Terms (1/4)

Financial Literacy

The ability to understand and use financial skills, including personal financial management, budgeting, and investing, to make informed decisions.

Interest Rates

The cost of borrowing money or the return earned on invested money, expressed as a percentage of the principal.

Inflation

The rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services is rising, and subsequently, the purchasing power of currency is falling.

Investment Risk

The possibility of losing money or earning less than expected on an investment.

Living Paycheck to Paycheck

A situation where an individual spends their entire income, leaving little to no money for savings or unexpected expenses before their next paycheck.

Important Terms (2/4)

Credit Card Debt

Money owed on a credit card, often accruing high interest if not paid in full each month.

Student Loan Debt

Money borrowed to finance education, which must be repaid, often with interest.

Gross Pay

The total amount of money an employee earns before any deductions are taken out.

Take-Home Pay

The amount of money an employee receives after all deductions (taxes, benefits, etc.) have been subtracted from their gross pay.

Emergency Fund

A personal bank account containing money set aside to cover unexpected expenses, such as medical emergencies, job loss, or car repairs.

Important Terms (3/4)

Compound Interest

Interest calculated on the initial principal and also on the accumulated interest from previous periods. It causes money to grow at an accelerating rate.

Credit Score

A three-digit number representing an individual's creditworthiness, based on their payment history and debt levels. Lenders use it to assess risk.

Stocks

Shares of ownership in a company.

Bonds

Loans made to a company or government, which pay interest to the bondholder.

Mutual Funds

Investment vehicles that pool money from multiple investors to invest in a diversified portfolio of stocks, bonds, or other securities.

Important Terms (4/4)

Retirement Accounts

Tax-advantaged accounts designed to help individuals save for retirement (e.g., 401(k), IRA).

Insurance

A contract in which an individual or entity receives financial protection or reimbursement against losses from an insurance company.

Wills

Legal documents that declare a person's wishes regarding the distribution of their assets and the care of any minor children after their death.

Beneficiary Designations

Instructions specifying who will receive the benefits of a financial asset (like an insurance policy or retirement account) upon the owner's death.

Time Horizon

The length of time an investment is held or the period over which financial decisions are expected to have an impact.

The Reality of the Financial Literacy Gap

Definition

The "financial literacy gap" is the disconnect between what people *should know* about money and what they *actually know*.

Core Concept

Financial literacy is the ability to understand and apply financial skills like personal financial management, budgeting, and investing.

Impact

Not knowing these answers costs individuals significant amounts of money over their lifetime, sometimes tens of thousands of dollars.

What This Course Will Teach You: A Unit-by-Unit Preview (1/2)

Unit 1: Foundations of Money and Decision-Making (Chapters 1–3)

Understanding money's role in the economy, inflation, trade-offs, and a lifelong goal-setting framework.

Unit 2: Earning Income and Understanding Paychecks (Chapters 4–6)

Differentiating gross vs. take-home pay, reading pay stubs, and understanding the tax system.

Unit 3: Budgeting and Everyday Money Management (Chapters 7–9)

Building monthly budgets, tracking spending, and establishing an emergency fund for financial security.

Unit 4: Banking and Saving (Chapters 10–12)

Strategic use of checking and savings accounts, avoiding fees, and harnessing the power of compound interest.

Unit 5: Credit, Debt, and Borrowing (Chapters 13–16)

Understanding credit scores, credit cards, evaluating loans, and making careful student and car loan decisions.

What This Course Will Teach You: A Unit-by-Unit Preview (2/2)

Unit 6: Investing and Growing Wealth (Chapters 17–20)

Basics of stocks, bonds, mutual funds, retirement accounts, and the math behind long-term investing.

Unit 7: Protecting What You Have (Chapters 21–23)

Using insurance to protect against risks (accidents, medical, identity theft) and the importance of wills and beneficiary designations.

Unit 8: Consumer Skills and Life Planning (Chapters 24–27)

Becoming a smarter consumer, evaluating college costs, renting vs. buying, and exploring entrepreneurship.

Unit 9: Big-Picture Financial Planning (Chapters 28–30)

Integrating all learned concepts into a personal financial plan, adapting to life events, and considering generosity.

Essential Financial Habits and Perspectives

Think before you spend

Combat impulsive purchases by pausing and assessing needs and affordability.

Save before you spend

Prioritize savings as a non-negotiable expense to build wealth effectively.

Understand before you sign

Read and comprehend the fine print of financial agreements (loans, credit cards, insurance) to avoid costly surprises.

Plan, but stay flexible

Create a dynamic financial plan that can be revisited and adjusted as life circumstances change.
