Understanding Types of Investment Risk

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Market Risk

The risk that broad economic events cause the entire investment market to decline. It cannot be eliminated but can be managed over a long time horizon.

Inflation Risk (Purchasing Power Risk)

Interest Rate Risk

The risk that rising interest rates will reduce the value of certain investments, particularly existing bonds.

Concentration Risk

The danger of having too much of one's money tied up in a single investment, company, or industry, making the portfolio vulnerable to the poor performance of that specific asset.

Liquidity Risk

The risk of being unable to quickly sell an investment at a fair price when cash is needed.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Diversification

A critical strategy to mitigate concentration risk and reduce overall portfolio risk by spreading investments across many different assets.

Time Horizon

A long-term investment horizon is presented as a key factor in managing market risk, allowing investors to ride out market downturns and benefit from eventual recoveries.

Important Terms (1/2)

Market Risk

The risk that the entire investment market declines due to broad economic events (e.g., recession, financial crisis, pandemic, war).

Inflation Risk (Purchasing Power Risk)

The risk that an investment's return does not keep up with the rate of inflation, causing your money to buy less in the future.

Interest Rate Risk

The risk that rising interest rates will decrease the value of existing investments, primarily bonds.

Concentration Risk

The risk of investing a disproportionately large amount of money in a single investment, company, or industry.

Liquidity Risk

The risk that an investment cannot be sold quickly, or at a fair price, when you need access to your money.

Important Terms (2/2)

Portfolio

The entire collection of investments held by an individual or institution.

Diversification

The strategy of spreading investments across a range of different assets to reduce overall risk.

Market Risk

Definition

The risk of a broad market decline impacting all investments, regardless of individual company performance.

Causes

Major economic events such as recessions, financial crises, pandemics, or wars can trigger widespread selling.

Effect

Even well-performing companies see their stock prices fall as investors broadly divest.

Management

Inflation Risk (Purchasing Power Risk)

Definition

The risk that the returns on your investments will not outpace the rate of inflation, leading to a decrease in your money's purchasing power over time.

Impact

Even if the dollar amount of your savings grows, if inflation is higher than your return, your money will buy less in the future than it does today.

Interest Rate Risk

Definition

Primary Impact

Concentration Risk

Definition

The risk that comes from allocating an excessive portion of one's investment capital to a single investment, company, or industry.

Consequence

If that particular investment performs poorly or fails, it can severely damage the entire investment portfolio.

Example

Employees who invested a large share of their retirement savings in their own company's stock experienced significant losses or even bankruptcy when their company failed.

Solution

**Diversification** is the direct answer to concentration risk. It emphasizes the principle of "never putting all your eggs in one basket."

Liquidity Risk

Definition

The risk that you will be unable to sell an investment quickly, or at its fair market value, when you need access to cash.

Investor Consideration

Investors need to consider their potential need for quick access to funds when choosing investments.

Putting It Together: The Power of Diversification

Key Strategy

Diversification is a fundamental principle for managing investment risk.

Benefits

Research consistently shows that spreading investments across a wide variety of different assets significantly reduces overall portfolio risk.

Return Impact

This reduction in risk is achieved while giving up very little of the potential investment return.

Conclusion

Investors do not need to accept high concentration risk to achieve good returns; diversification offers a more prudent path.

Portfolio Origin

The term "portfolio" originated from the Italian word "portafoglio," meaning a case for carrying documents, and evolved to describe a collection of investments.
