Key Concepts (2/2)

Savings vs. Checking Accounts

Most individuals benefit from having both types of accounts: a checking account for daily spending and transactions, and a savings account for long-term storage and growth of funds.

Important Terms (1/2)

Savings Account

A type of deposit account at a bank or credit union specifically designed to hold money you are saving for future use, earning interest on your balance.

Interest

The money paid to you by a financial institution for depositing your funds with them, usually expressed as a percentage of your balance.

APY (Annual Percentage Yield)

The real rate of return earned on a savings deposit over a full year, taking into account the effect of compounding interest.

Compounding

The process of earning interest on both the initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods. (Though mentioned briefly, it's a key component of APY).

High-Yield Savings Account

A savings account, typically offered by online-only banks, that pays a significantly higher interest rate (APY) than traditional savings accounts.

Important Terms (2/2)

Regulation D

A former federal rule that limited certain types of withdrawals and transfers from savings accounts to six per month. While suspended, some banks may still maintain similar policies.

What is a Savings Account?

Purpose

A savings account serves as a dedicated place to store money that is not intended for immediate spending. It is ideal for accumulating funds towards specific financial goals.

Common Goals

These can include building an emergency fund, saving for a down payment on a car or home, funding a vacation, or planning for future education expenses.

Key Benefit

Unlike a checking account, a savings account rewards you for keeping money deposited by paying interest on your balance, allowing your money to grow over time.

How a Savings Account Earns Interest

Bank's Use of Funds

When you deposit money into a savings account, the bank doesn't just hold it passively. They use these deposits to fund loans to other customers (e.g., mortgages, business loans).

Your Compensation

In return for using your money, the bank pays you a small percentage of your balance as interest. This interest is typically calculated and credited to your account once a month.

APY Ranges and What to Look For

Varying Rates

Savings account APYs are not uniform across all banks and can change with economic conditions. It's essential to check current rates when shopping for an account.

Traditional Banks

Brick-and-mortar banks typically offer lower APYs, often ranging from approximately 0.5% to 1%.

Online High-Yield Savings Accounts

Online-only banks often provide significantly higher APYs, frequently in the range of 4% to 5% or even more.

Withdrawal Limits

Historical Context

Federally, Regulation D previously limited certain withdrawals and transfers from savings accounts to 6 per month. This rule was suspended in 2020.

Current Practice

Despite the suspension of the federal rule, individual banks may still choose to impose their own limits on the number of withdrawals or transfers allowed from a savings account each month.

Savings Account vs. Checking Account: The Key Difference

Checking Account

Primarily for daily spending, bill payments, and frequent transactions. Offers easy access to funds through debit cards, checks, and online transfers.

Savings Account

Primarily for storing money, accumulating funds for goals, and earning interest. Access to funds is generally less immediate or more restricted to discourage frequent withdrawals.

Coexistence

Most individuals maintain both types of accounts to manage their finances effectively – a checking account for routine expenses and a savings account for growing their long-term funds.
