Chapter 14, Section 2

Interest Rates and APR

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Carrying a Balance Incurs Interest

If you don't pay your full credit card statement balance each month, the credit card company will charge you interest on the amount you owe.

APR (Annual Percentage Rate)

This is the yearly interest rate applied to any outstanding balance. It's crucial for determining how much extra you'll pay.

Interest Accrues Daily

Credit card companies technically calculate interest based on your "average daily balance" using a daily periodic rate, rather than just a simple monthly calculation.

Monthly Interest Calculation (Simplified)

For most practical purposes and this course, monthly interest can be calculated by dividing the APR by 12 (to get a monthly rate) and multiplying it by your outstanding balance.

Daily Periodic Rate

This is the actual daily interest rate applied to your balance. It's calculated by dividing the APR by 365.

Key Concepts (2/2)

The High Cost of Debt

Credit card interest rates are significantly higher than typical savings account interest rates, making paying off high-interest debt a critical financial priority.

Important Terms

APR (Annual Percentage Rate)

The annual interest rate charged on any credit card balance carried from month to month. Expressed as a percentage.

Balance

The total amount of money you owe on your credit card at a given time.

Monthly Interest

The amount of interest charged on your balance for one billing cycle, typically one month.

Daily Periodic Rate

The daily interest rate applied to your credit card balance, calculated by dividing the APR by 365.

Average Daily Balance

The method credit card companies use to calculate interest, tracking your balance each day of the billing cycle and applying the daily periodic rate to the average.

The High Cost of Debt vs. Value of Savings

Debt is expensive

$1,000 carried on a credit card at 22% APR costs you about $220 in interest per year.

Savings grow slowly (comparatively)

The same $1,000 in a savings account at 4.5% APR would only earn you about $45 in interest per year.
