Chapter 20, Section 4

What If You Start Late?

Study Slides

Key Concepts

The Power of Starting Now

Regardless of age, the best time to begin investing is immediately, as waiting further only exacerbates the impact of lost compound growth.

Significant Achievability for Late Starters

Even starting in one's 40s or 50s can lead to substantial retirement savings, far exceeding not starting at all.

Compound Growth and Time

Compound interest is a powerful force, but its effectiveness is severely diminished by delay. More time means more growth, less time means less growth, even with similar total contributions.

Aggressive Strategies for Late Starters

To compensate for lost time, individuals starting late must employ more intensive strategies, such as maximizing contributions, utilizing catch-up provisions, and reducing expenses.

The Cost of Inaction

The most significant financial mistake is failing to start due to perceived inability to invest "enough." Any investment, no matter how small, is better than none.

Important Terms (1/2)

Compound Growth/Compound Interest

The process where an asset's earnings (interest or returns) are reinvested to generate additional earnings over time, leading to exponential growth.

401(k)

A retirement savings plan sponsored by an employer, allowing employees to save and invest a portion of their paycheck before taxes are taken out.

IRA (Individual Retirement Account)

A personal savings plan that allows individuals to save for retirement with tax-free growth or on a tax-deferred basis.

Catch-Up Contributions

Expense Ratio

The annual fee charged by a fund (like a mutual fund or ETF) to cover management and operating costs, expressed as a percentage of the fund's assets.

Important Terms (2/2)

Low-Cost Index Funds

Investment funds that passively track a market index (e.g., S&P 500) and typically have very low expense ratios compared to actively managed funds.

Living Below Your Means

Spending less money than you earn, thereby saving or investing the surplus.
