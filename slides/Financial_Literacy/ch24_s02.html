Important Terms (1/2)

Unit Price

The cost of one standard measurement unit of a product (e.g., $ per ounce, $ per count, $ per pound, $ per fluid ounce).

Quantity

The amount of product, expressed in standard units, used in the unit price calculation.

Comparison Shopping

The act of researching prices for a specific item at multiple retailers or sources to secure the most favorable cost.

Dollar Discount

The specific amount of money saved from an original price due to a discount.

Sale Price

The final price of an item after a discount has been applied.

Important Terms (2/2)

Discount %

The percentage by which the original price of an item has been reduced.

Important Considerations Beyond Unit Price:

Expiration

Will you consume the entire product before it expires? Food waste means lost money, regardless of the initial unit price.

Storage

Do you have sufficient and appropriate space to store a larger or bulk item?

Quality

Is the quality consistent across different brands or package sizes, especially between name brands and store brands? Sometimes, cheaper bulk items might not meet your quality expectations.

Percentage Discount Calculations:

Dollar Discount = Original Price (Discount % / 100)

Sale Price = Original Price - Dollar Discount

Discount % = ((Original Price - Sale Price) / Original Price) 100%

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is a unit price?

The cost of one standard unit of a product, such as one ounce, one count, one pound, or one fluid ounce.

What is the primary benefit of using unit pricing for purchasing decisions?

It allows you to compare any two sizes or brands of a product on an equal footing to determine the best value.

What is the formula for calculating unit price?

Unit Price = Total Price / Quantity (where Quantity is expressed in standard units like oz, lb, count, fl oz).

When should you calculate the unit price yourself, even if stores display them?

Always calculate it yourself when in doubt about the displayed unit price on shelf tags to ensure accuracy.

A 12 oz box of cereal costs $3.99, and a 20 oz box costs $5.49. Which is the better value per ounce?

12 oz box: $3.99 / 12 oz = $0.3325/oz. 20 oz box: $5.49 / 20 oz = $0.2745/oz. The 20 oz box is the better value.

Is it always true that 'bigger is cheaper' when buying products?

No, 'bigger' is not always cheaper. You must calculate the unit price to determine the actual best value.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Besides unit price, what three important factors should you consider before buying a larger-sized product?

1. Will you use it all before it expires? 2. Do you have space to store it? 3. Is the quality the same as smaller packages?

Why is buying food with the cheapest unit price not actually cheaper if you throw some away?

Because you are paying for product you don't use, negating any per-unit savings.

Brand X detergent is 50 fl oz (50 loads) for $8.99. What is its cost per load?

$8.99 / 50 loads = $0.180 per load.

Store Brand detergent is 64 fl oz (64 loads) for $7.49. Brand Y is 92 fl oz (92 loads) for $14.99. Which has the best unit price per load?

Store Brand: $7.49 / 64 loads = $0.117 per load. Brand Y: $14.99 / 92 loads = $0.163 per load. The Store Brand is cheapest per load.

How does 'comparison shopping across stores' differ from using unit pricing?

Unit pricing helps compare sizes/brands of a product. Comparison shopping helps compare the price of the exact same product across different retailers.

What is the crucial first step for effective comparison shopping across stores?

Identify the exact item you want (brand, size, quantity).
