Student Loan Repayment Plans: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Federal vs. Private Loans

Federal loans offer multiple flexible repayment options, while private loans are significantly less flexible and lack federal protections.

Standard Repayment Plan

The default federal plan, featuring fixed monthly payments over 10 years, resulting in the lowest total interest paid and fastest payoff.

Graduated Repayment Plan

A federal plan where payments start lower and gradually increase every two years over a 10-year period, designed for borrowers expecting income growth.

Income-Driven Repayment (IDR) Plans

Federal plans that cap monthly payments based on a percentage of your discretionary income, offering potential loan forgiveness after 20-25 years (though the forgiven amount may be taxable).

Key Concepts (2/2)

Grace Period

A period, typically six months after leaving school or dropping below half-time enrollment, during which you are not required to make loan payments.

Deferment and Forbearance

Options to temporarily postpone or reduce loan payments due to specific circumstances like unemployment or economic hardship, with varying rules about interest accrual.

Refinancing Federal Loans

Converting federal student loans into a new private loan, which can offer a lower interest rate but permanently forfeits all federal loan benefits and protections.

Important Terms (1/3)

Standard Repayment Plan

The default federal loan plan with fixed payments over 10 years.

Graduated Repayment Plan

A federal loan plan where payments begin low and increase over a 10-year period.

Income-Driven Repayment (IDR)

A category of federal repayment plans that adjust monthly payments based on the borrower's income and family size.

Discretionary Income

The portion of a borrower's income that is above a certain percentage (e.g., 150%) of the federal poverty line, used to calculate IDR payments.

Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF)

A federal program that forgives the remaining balance on Direct Loans for borrowers working in qualifying public service jobs after 120 qualifying monthly payments.

Important Terms (2/3)

Grace Period

A temporary period after leaving school when federal loan payments are not required.

Deferment

A temporary postponement of student loan payments due to specific situations (e.g., in-school, unemployment, economic hardship), where interest may or may not accrue depending on the loan type.

Forbearance

A temporary reduction or postponement of student loan payments for up to 12 months at a time, during which interest always accrues on all loan types.

Refinancing

The process of taking out a new loan to pay off one or more existing loans, often to secure a lower interest rate or different terms.

Unsubsidized Loans

Federal student loans for which interest begins to accrue immediately after disbursement, even during deferment or grace periods.

Important Terms (3/3)

Qualifying Employer (for PSLF)

A government organization (federal, state, local, tribal) or a 501(c)(3) non-profit organization.

Qualifying Payments (for PSLF)

Monthly payments made under an IDR plan while working full-time for a qualifying employer.

Choosing a Repayment Plan: A Strategic Decision

Current Income and Income Trajectory

If your income is low or you expect it to grow significantly, Graduated or IDR plans might be beneficial initially.

Career Path

If you're pursuing a career in public service, PSLF could offer substantial forgiveness.

Financial Comfort

If you can comfortably afford the Standard Plan's higher monthly payments, it's generally the financially optimal choice due to lower overall interest costs.
