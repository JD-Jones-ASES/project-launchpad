Key Concepts (2/2)

The Decision-Making Pause

Deliberately slowing down purchasing decisions, especially for non-essential or larger items (e.g., the 24-48 Hour Rule), is a powerful defense against impulsive buying driven by advertising urgency.

Important Terms (1/3)

Advertising

A marketing communication that employs an openly sponsored, non-personal message to promote or sell a product, service or idea.

Scarcity

A psychological tactic where advertisers create the impression that a product's supply is limited, increasing its perceived value and urgency to buy.

Social Proof

The psychological phenomenon where people assume the actions of others reflect correct behavior for a given situation; advertisers use this through testimonials, reviews, and popularity claims.

Authority

A persuasion principle where people are more likely to comply with requests made by an authority figure; advertisers use experts or implied expertise to build trust.

Anchoring

A cognitive bias where an individual's first piece of information (the "anchor") skews subsequent judgments. In advertising, it's often the first price seen influencing perception of value.

Important Terms (2/3)

FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)

Anxiety that an exciting or interesting event might currently be happening elsewhere, often aroused by posts seen on social media. Advertisers use it to imply you're missing experiences if you don'...

Lifestyle Aspiration

An advertising strategy that sells an identity or desired way of life rather than just the product itself, connecting the product to a consumer's ideal self.

Emotional Appeal

Advertising that targets a consumer's emotions (e.g., happiness, sadness, patriotism) to create memorable and persuasive messages that bypass critical analysis.

Reciprocity

A social norm where if someone does something for you, you then feel obligated to return the favor. Advertisers use free samples or trials to evoke this feeling.

Targeted Advertising (Algorithmic Ad)

Advertisements specifically tailored to an individual based on their online behavior, demographic data, and stated preferences, often delivered via complex algorithms.

Important Terms (3/3)

Influencer Marketing

A type of social media marketing that uses endorsements and product mentions from individuals who have a dedicated social following and are viewed as experts in their niche.

Impulse Purchases

Unplanned purchases, items bought spontaneously without prior consideration, often triggered by immediate desires or advertising tactics.

The Pervasive Nature of Advertising

• Advertising is everywhere: social media, videos, stores.

• It's a deliberate, engineered science designed to influence purchasing decisions, not just inform.

• Understanding these tactics provides consumers with power to make informed choices.

The Algorithmic Ad (Targeted Advertising)

Data Collection

Every online action (searches, clicks, purchases) is tracked.

Profile Building

This data is used to create a detailed profile of your interests, habits, and preferences.

Real-time Bidding

Advertisers bid in real-time to show you ads precisely matched to your profile.

The "Product" is Your Data

You get free access to platforms and services in exchange for your attention and personal information, which is then sold to advertisers.

Advertising Channels (1/2)

Social Media

Sponsored posts, content from paid influencers, story ads.

Streaming Services

Pre-roll (before content) and mid-roll (during content) video ads.

Search Engines

Paid search results appear prominently at the top of search pages.

Physical Retail

Product placement within stores, eye-catching end-cap displays, strategic shelving at eye-level.

Email and Text Marketing

Promotional messages from brands with which you've previously interacted.

Advertising Channels (2/2)

Out-of-Home (OOH)

Billboards, transit ads (on buses, trains), and even packaging design.

Volume

The average person sees 4,000 to 10,000 advertisements daily, most processed subconsciously.

Influencer Marketing and Disclosure

Paid Promotion

Many social media recommendations from creators are paid promotions, not independent opinions.

FTC Requirements

The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) mandates that paid promotions be clearly disclosed (e.g., "#ad", "#sponsored").

Consumer Vigilance

Disclosures can be subtle or hard to spot. Consumers should always question if a recommendation is genuine or if the person is being compensated.

Countering Advertising: The Decision-Making Pause

Advertisers Create Urgency

A primary goal of advertising is to prevent rational thought by creating an immediate need.

Your Defense: Time

Deliberately slowing down the purchasing process is the most effective counter-strategy.

The 24–48 Hour Rule

Impact of Impulse Purchases

Americans spend significant amounts on impulse buys (nearly $2,000/year per household), which can add up to substantial lost wealth over time if not managed.
