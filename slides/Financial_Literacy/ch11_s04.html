The Rule of 72: A Quick Guide to Doubling Your Money

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Quick Estimation Tool

The Rule of 72 provides a rapid way to approximate doubling time without needing a financial calculator or complex formulas. It's designed for quick, back-of-the-envelope calculations.

The Formula

The core of the rule is `Years to Double = 72 / r`, where `r` is the annual interest rate expressed as a whole number (e.g., use 6 for 6%).

Approximation

It's important to remember that the result is an estimate, usually within about one year of the exact answer for typical interest rates.

Universal Application (Exponential Growth)

The Rule of 72 is not limited to investments; it can be applied to any phenomenon experiencing exponential growth, such as inflation, population growth, or even the growth of debt.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Inflation and Purchasing Power

When applied to inflation, the rule helps illustrate how quickly the cost of goods can double, highlighting the erosion of purchasing power if your money doesn't grow at a rate faster than inflation.

Financial Due Diligence

The rule serves as a useful tool for quickly evaluating the realism of investment offers. Returns that suggest an exceptionally fast doubling time should be met with skepticism.

Important Terms (1/2)

Rule of 72

A simplified formula used to estimate the number of years required for an investment to double in value at a given annual rate of compound interest.

Compound Interest

Interest calculated on the initial principal and also on the accumulated interest of previous periods of a deposit or loan. The Rule of 72 is an approximation for calculations based on compound int...

Interest Rate (r)

The percentage charged by a lender to a borrower for the use of assets, or the percentage paid on an investment. In the Rule of 72, `r` is used as a whole number (e.g., 8 for 8%).

Annual Percentage Yield (APY)

The effective annual rate of return taking into account the effect of compounding interest. Often used for savings accounts.

Inflation

The rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services is rising, and subsequently, the purchasing power of currency is falling.

Important Terms (2/2)

Purchasing Power

The financial ability to buy products and services. Inflation erodes purchasing power, meaning the same amount of money buys fewer goods over time.

Understanding the Formula

`r` = Interest Rate as a Whole Number

This is crucial. If your interest rate is 6% per year, you use `6` in the formula, not `0.06`. Similarly, for 8%, you use `8`.

Approximate Result

The answer you get is an approximation, typically accurate within about one year of the exact compound interest calculation for common interest rates.
