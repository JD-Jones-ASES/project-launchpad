Chapter 4, Section 3

The Gig Economy: Understanding Modern Work and Income

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Gig Economy

A labor market where temporary, flexible jobs are commonplace, and companies tend to hire independent contractors and freelancers instead of full-time employees.

Independent Contractors (1099 Workers)

Individuals who provide services to clients under a contract, rather than being employees of a company. They are responsible for their own taxes, benefits, and expenses.

Flexibility

A major advantage of gig work, allowing individuals to set their own hours and balance multiple commitments.

No Employer Benefits

A significant downside for gig workers, who typically do not receive employer-sponsored health insurance, paid time off, or retirement contributions.

Variable Income

Earnings in the gig economy can fluctuate greatly depending on work availability, making budgeting challenging.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Self-Employment Taxes

Gig workers are responsible for paying the full 15.3% self-employment tax (Social Security and Medicare), in addition to income taxes, as employers do not cover half.

Handling Your Own Taxes

Set Aside Funds

A reasonable rule of thumb is to set aside **25-30% of every payment** received specifically for taxes.

Separate Savings Account

Open a dedicated savings account for taxes and consistently transfer money there after each payment. This prevents accidental spending of funds earmarked for tax liabilities.

Quarterly Estimated Taxes

Depending on earnings, gig workers may be required to pay estimated taxes quarterly to the IRS to avoid penalties.

Strategic Use of Gig Work

Supplemental Income

It's an effective way to earn extra money alongside a main job or during periods of study.

Skill Development and Portfolio Building

For students or those transitioning careers, gig work in fields like graphic design or web development provides income, practical experience, and a professional portfolio simultaneously.

Entrepreneurial Stepping Stone

It can offer a low-risk way to test out business ideas or build a client base before committing to full-time entrepreneurship.
