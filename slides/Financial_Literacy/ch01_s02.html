Important Terms (1/3)

Barter

A system of exchange where goods or services are directly traded for other goods or services without the use of money.

Commodity Money

A form of currency whose value comes from the material it is made of or its usefulness as a good itself.

Salary

Derived from the Latin word *salarium*, meaning "salt payment," reflecting the historical use of salt as currency.

Debasement

The practice by governments of reducing the intrinsic value of coins (e.g., by mixing in cheaper metals) while maintaining their face value, often leading to inflation.

Flying Money

Early forms of paper certificates developed in Tang Dynasty China, allowing merchants to deposit money in one city and redeem it in another without carrying physical coins.

Important Terms (2/3)

Gold-backed Currency

Money whose value is directly tied to a fixed amount of gold, meaning it can be exchanged for a specific quantity of gold on demand.

Fiat Money

Currency that has value because a government declares it to be legal tender and because the public trusts it, rather than being backed by a physical commodity. The word "fiat" means "let it be done...

Legal Tender

Currency that, by law, must be accepted for all debts, public and private.

Digital Currency

Money that exists only in electronic form, representing a digital record of value.

Cryptocurrencies

Decentralized digital currencies that use cryptography for secure transactions and typically operate on a blockchain, independent of central banks.

Important Terms (3/3)

Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs)

Digital forms of a country's national currency, issued and backed by its central bank.

Stage 4: Fiat Money (Trust Without Gold)

Shift in the 20th Century

Most countries moved away from gold-backed currency. The U.S. dollar became fully fiat in 1971 when President Nixon ended its direct conversion to gold.

Definition

Currency that has value because a government declares it **legal tender** and because the public trusts it, not because it's backed by a physical commodity.

Value of the U.S. Dollar (Example)

Foundation of Fiat Value

The dollar's value rests on the authority of the U.S. government, the trust of its users, and the strength of the underlying economy, military, and institutional stability.
