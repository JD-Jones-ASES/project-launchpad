Key Concepts (1/2)

Budget Categories

General groupings for different types of expenses that help organize and track spending, ensuring all financial outflows are accounted for.

Sinking Funds

A method of saving small, regular amounts over time for predictable, non-monthly expenses (like annual subscriptions, car registration, or holiday gifts) to avoid financial surprises and debt.

Budget Prioritization

A strategic approach to making spending cuts when income is insufficient to cover expenses, prioritizing cuts from variable wants, then fixed wants, then variable needs, and finally fixed needs.

Understanding Sinking Funds

How it works

Divide the total anticipated cost of a future expense by the number of months until it's due. Save that calculated amount each month.

Examples

Benefit

Sinking funds help you avoid debt, reduce financial anxiety, and budget more accurately for all upcoming costs.

Fixed Expenses

Characteristics

Consistent amount, difficult to adjust quickly.

Examples

Rent or mortgage payments, car loan payments, phone plans with a set monthly rate, internet service, subscriptions with a fixed monthly fee, student loan payments, insurance premiums.

Budgeting Strategy

Because they are locked in, fixed expenses should be budgeted first. They are the non-negotiable costs that you must cover.

Goal

The exercise of classifying helps you identify which expenses are non-negotiable and which could be reduced or eliminated if necessary, promoting clearer financial thinking.

Why Classification Matters: Prioritizing Cuts

Fixed vs. Variable

Tells you where you can cut. Variable expenses offer immediate flexibility, while fixed expenses require bigger life changes (e.g., moving, refinancing) to adjust.

Needs vs. Wants

Tells you what you should consider cutting first. Wants are always the first to go, as they don't impact your basic livelihood.

What is the primary purpose of classifying expenses in a budget?

To help you make smarter financial decisions, especially when money is tight, by understanding where your money goes and where you have control.

What is a "fixed expense" in a budget context?

A spending category where the dollar amount stays the same every month, over which you have little or no control once committed.

Give three examples of fixed expenses.

Rent/mortgage payment, car payment, phone plan, internet service, subscriptions with a set monthly rate.

What is a "variable expense" in a budget context?

A spending category where the dollar amount changes from month to month, over which you have more control.

Give three examples of variable expenses.

Groceries, gas, electricity (varies by season), dining out, clothing, entertainment, personal care.

Define a "need" in a budget context.

An expense required to maintain basic functioning: shelter, basic food, transportation to work/school, essential utilities, and necessary healthcare.

Give three examples of needs in a budget.

Rent/mortgage (shelter), basic groceries (food), gas for commuting (transportation), essential utilities (water, basic electricity).

Define a "want" in a budget context.

An expense that improves your life or enjoyment but is not essential for basic functioning.

Give three examples of wants in a budget.

Dining out, streaming services, entertainment, new clothing beyond necessities, hobby supplies, and upgrades to existing items.
