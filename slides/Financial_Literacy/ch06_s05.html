Key Concepts (1/2)

A crucial document provided by employers by January 31, detailing an employee's annual wages and taxes withheld. It's essential for completing your tax return.

Form 1040 (U.S. Individual Income Tax Return)

The primary federal income tax form used by individuals to report income, claim deductions and credits, and calculate their final tax liability or refund.

Filing Status

Your personal situation (e.g., Single, Married, Head of Household) that determines your standard deduction amount and the income thresholds for tax brackets.

Tax Withholding

The amount of income tax employers deduct from your paychecks throughout the year and send to the IRS on your behalf.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Tax Refund vs. Tax Due

If your withholding exceeds your actual tax owed, you get a refund. If your withholding is less than your tax owed, you must pay the difference. A refund is your own overpaid money returned.

Form W-4 (Employee's Withholding Certificate)

A form you submit to your employer to instruct them on how much federal income tax to withhold from each paycheck. Properly filling this out helps match your withholding to your actual tax liability.

Tax Scams

Awareness of fraudulent attempts to steal money or personal information by criminals impersonating the IRS. The IRS will never demand immediate payment by unusual methods or threaten arrest.

Gross Income

The total amount of money earned from all sources before any deductions or taxes are taken out.

Important Terms (2/2)

Standard Deduction

A fixed dollar amount that taxpayers can subtract from their adjusted gross income (AGI) to arrive at their taxable income, if they choose not to itemize deductions. It varies by filing status.

Tax Refund

An amount of money the government returns to a taxpayer when they have paid more in taxes than they actually owe for the year, usually due to excessive withholding from paychecks.

Form W-4 (Employee's Withholding Certificate)

A form completed by an employee and submitted to their employer to inform the employer how much federal income tax to withhold from each paycheck.

Tax Credits

Specific amounts that reduce a taxpayer's actual tax liability dollar-for-dollar. Some credits are refundable, meaning you can get money back even if you owe no tax.

Deadline

Employers must provide this form to employees by **January 31** of the year following the tax year (e.g., by January 31, 2025, for the 2024 tax year).

Key Boxes

Multiple Jobs

If you work multiple jobs in a year, you will receive a separate W-2 from each employer. You must combine the wages and withholding from all W-2s when filing your tax return.

The 1040 Form

Purpose

Form 1040, the U.S. Individual Income Tax Return, is the main federal income tax return form that you (or a tax preparer) complete and submit to the IRS each spring.

The Tax Calculation Path (as outlined in Section 6.4)

Filing Status

Single

For unmarried individuals. This is the most common status for young people in their first years of independence.

Married Filing Jointly

For married couples who combine their income on one return. Usually the most financially advantageous.

Married Filing Separately

For married couples who choose to file individual returns. Rarely beneficial, often resulting in higher overall tax.

Head of Household (HOH)

For unmarried individuals who pay more than half the cost of maintaining a home for a qualifying person (e.g., a child). This status provides a larger standard deduction than "Single."

Most Students

For most students starting out, "Single" will be the correct filing status.

Reasons to File Even If Below Threshold

Important Note

Many part-time student workers earn below the filing threshold but still have taxes withheld. **Always file a return if taxes were withheld** to claim your refund, as it could be $100s.

Tax Refund vs. Tax Due

Comparison

At the end of the year, you compare:

Tax Refund

If the tax withheld is *greater than* the tax owed, you receive a tax refund. This means the government is returning your overpayment.

Tax Due

If the tax withheld is *less than* the tax owed, you owe additional tax and must send a payment to the IRS by the filing deadline (typically April 15).

Adjusting Your Withholding: The W-4 Form

Purpose

When you start a new job, you complete Form W-4 (Employee's Withholding Certificate). Your responses on this form tell your employer how much federal income tax to withhold from each paycheck.

Importance

Filling out your W-4 accurately helps ensure your withholding closely matches your actual tax liability, reducing the chance of a large refund or a large bill at tax time.

Tips

Free Tax Filing Options

IRS Resources

The IRS offers free filing options for eligible individuals, often based on income thresholds.

VITA

Volunteer Income Tax Assistance (VITA) sites provide free in-person tax preparation help at community centers, libraries, and schools.

Free Online Tools

Various free online filing software options are available through IRS.gov.

E-filing

Electronic filing is the norm (over 94% of returns). It's faster, reduces errors, and typically results in quicker refunds (two weeks or less) compared to paper returns.
