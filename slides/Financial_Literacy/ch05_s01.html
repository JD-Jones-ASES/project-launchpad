Important Terms (1/3)

Gross Pay

The total amount of wages or salary earned by an individual before any deductions are made.

Net Pay (Take-Home Pay)

The actual amount of money an employee receives in their paycheck after all taxes and deductions have been subtracted from their gross pay.

Deductions

Any amount of money withheld from an employee's gross pay for taxes, insurance premiums, retirement contributions, or other benefits.

Required (Mandatory) Deductions

Deductions that are legally mandated and cannot be opted out of, primarily including various federal, state, and local taxes.

Voluntary Deductions

Optional deductions that an employee chooses to have withheld from their paycheck, usually for benefits like health insurance, retirement plans, or flexible spending accounts.

Important Terms (2/3)

Federal Income Tax

A tax levied by the U.S. federal government on individual and corporate income, used to fund national defense, federal programs, and services.

State Income Tax

A tax levied by individual state governments on income earned within that state, used to fund state-specific services like public schools and roads. (Not applicable in all states).

FICA Taxes (Federal Insurance Contributions Act)

Mandatory payroll taxes that fund Social Security and Medicare programs.

Health Insurance Premiums

Regular payments made to an insurance company or an employer's health plan to maintain health insurance coverage.

Retirement Contributions

Money regularly deducted from a paycheck and invested into a retirement savings plan (like a 401(k) or 403(b)) to provide income in retirement.

Important Terms (3/3)

Taxable Income

The portion of an individual's gross income that is subject to income tax after allowed deductions and exemptions.

Explain the fundamental difference between gross pay and net pay.

Gross pay is your total earnings before any deductions, while net pay is the actual amount you receive after all deductions. Gross pay is always larger than ...

Why is there a gap between your gross pay and your net pay?

The gap exists because two categories of deductions—required (mandatory) and voluntary—are taken from every paycheck.

What are the two main categories of deductions taken from a paycheck?

Required (mandatory) deductions and Voluntary deductions.

List three types of required (mandatory) deductions.

Federal income tax withholding, state income tax withholding (in most states), and FICA taxes (Social Security and Medicare).

Key Concepts (2/2)

Can you opt out of required deductions like federal income tax or FICA?

No, required deductions are mandated by law and cannot be opted out of.

Give examples of voluntary deductions.

Health insurance premiums, retirement contributions (e.g., 401(k)), dental and vision insurance, and flexible spending account contributions.

What are two effects of making voluntary deductions?

Voluntary deductions reduce your taxable income and your take-home pay.

Write out the simple formula for calculating net pay.

Net Pay = Gross Pay - Federal Tax - State Tax - FICA - Other Deductions.

What does 'FICA' stand for in the net pay formula?

FICA stands for Federal Insurance Contributions Act, which includes Social Security tax and Medicare tax.

What is included in 'Other Deductions' in the net pay formula?

Other Deductions include health insurance, retirement contributions, and any other voluntary deductions.
