Important Terms (1/2)

Opus Dei

Latin for "work of God"; refers to the communal prayer of the Divine Office, the central liturgical activity in monastic life. The Cluniacs emphasized its splendor and length.

Lectio divina

Latin for "sacred reading"; the slow, prayerful reading of Scripture, prescribed by Saint Benedict as a daily practice. Cistercians restored its prominence.

Cîteaux (Cistercium)

The marshy, desolate site in Burgundy where Robert of Molesme and his monks founded the "new monastery" in 1098, giving the Cistercians their name.

Important Terms (2/2)

Clairvaux (Clara Vallis)

St. Bernard's abbey in the Champagne region of France, founded in 1115. It became the most famous Cistercian monastery and a major center of expansion.

Doctor of the Church

A title conferred by the Catholic Church on saints recognized for their outstanding contributions to theology and doctrine. St. Bernard of Clairvaux was declared one in 1830.

Vigils (Matins)

The first liturgical hour of the Divine Office, typically prayed in the deep hours of the night (e.g., around 2:00 a.m.).

Lay brothers (conversi)

A class of Cistercian monks who took monastic vows but were not ordained priests and focused primarily on manual labor, especially agricultural work, to support the monastery.

The Problem with Success: Cluny's Magnificence

Cluniac Achievement

By the late 11th century, Cluny had grown from a single monastery (founded 910) to a network of over a thousand houses across Europe.

Wealth and Liturgy

Cluniac abbeys were renowned for their splendor, glittering churches, and an elaborate *Opus Dei* (Divine Office) that occupied most of the monks' waking hours.

St. Bernard of Clairvaux (1090–1153)

Background

Born into a noble Burgundian family, Bernard was brilliant, ambitious, and deeply devout.

Entry to Cîteaux (1112)

At age twenty-two, Bernard entered Cîteaux with approximately thirty companions, including four of his brothers. This influx saved the struggling community.

Founding Clairvaux (1115)

Within three years, Bernard was sent to found a new monastery at Clairvaux ("Bright Valley") in the Champagne region, where he served as abbot for 38 years.

Explosive Growth

Turning Point

Bernard's arrival in 1112 marked the beginning of rapid expansion.

Scale

From 1112 to Bernard's death in 1153, the Cistercian order grew to 339 monasteries across Europe. By 1200, it exceeded 500.
