Important Terms (1/2)

Cenobitic

From the Greek koinos bios ("common life"). Describes monks who live in a community under a common rule and superior.

Eremitic

From the Greek eremos ("desert" or "wilderness"). Describes monks (hermits) who live alone, in solitude and silence.

Monastic Rule (Regula)

A written guide for the daily life of a religious community, governing practices, prayers, and conduct.

Abbot/Abbess

The superior of a monastic community, responsible for directing the monks or nuns and interpreting the monastic rule.

Night Office (Vigils or Matins)

The first prayer service of the monastic day, traditionally sung in the hours before dawn.

Laura (Lavra)

A semi-eremitic community where monks live in individual cells but gather for common prayer and meals on certain days.

Carthusian Model

A monastic form that balances communal and solitary living, with monks having individual cells/houses but sharing certain prayers and meals.

Conclusion

• This book will primarily focus on cenobitic and eremitic monasticism in the Western, Latin tradition.

• A key aspect to observe is the creative tension between solitude and community.

• Monastic founders consistently wrestled with the question: "How much solitude? How much community?" Their answers profoundly shaped the history of the Western Church.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What are the two fundamental forms of monastic life recognized in the tradition?

The cenobitic life and the eremitic life.

Define 'cenobitic' and state its Greek origin.

'Cenobitic' describes monks who live together in a community. It comes from the Greek 'koinos bios,' meaning 'common life.'

What are the defining characteristics of a cenobitic monastery?

Monks (or women) live in a community, sharing meals, prayers, work, and possessions, under a common rule and a common superior (abbot/abbess).

What is a 'monastic rule' (regula)?

A written guide that governs the daily life of a religious community, with the Rule of Saint Benedict being the most famous example.

How did the cenobitic form of monasticism become the dominant form in the Western Church?

Largely through the influence of Saint Benedict of Nursia and his famous Rule of Saint Benedict.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Name some well-known cenobitic monasteries mentioned in the text.

Monte Cassino, Cluny, Cîteaux, Gethsemani.

What is the 'night office' in monastic life?

Also called Vigils or Matins, it is the first prayer of the monastic day, typically sung in the hours before dawn.

Define 'eremitic' and state its Greek origin.

'Eremitic' describes monks who live alone, in solitude and silence. It comes from the Greek 'eremos,' meaning 'desert' or 'wilderness.'

Who is widely regarded as the father of Christian monasticism, and what form of monastic life did he practice?

Saint Anthony of Egypt (c. 251–356), who spent decades living alone in the Egyptian desert, practicing the eremitic life.

What is an advantage of the eremitic life for spiritual growth?

Solitude strips away distractions and excuses, allowing for extraordinary depths of prayer and spiritual insight by confronting God alone.
