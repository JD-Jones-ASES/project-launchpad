Chapter 7, Section 2

The Carthusian Path: Guigo II's Ladder of Monks and St. Bruno's Solitary Ideal

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Lectio Divina

A traditional monastic practice of sacred reading, aiming for spiritual transformation rather than mere information. Guigo II's *Ladder of Monks* provides the classic articulation of this practice.

The Four Rungs of *Lectio Divina

*:

Interdependence of the Rungs

Guigo emphasizes that the four stages are inextricably linked; one cannot truly progress without engaging all of them.

Carthusian Solitude

A core tenet of the Carthusian order, involving monks living largely apart in individual cells (small houses) for prayer, reading, writing, and manual labor, with strict silence.

The "Desert" Ideal

In monastic terms, a remote wilderness or secluded place, offering an environment conducive to spiritual focus, far from worldly distractions.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Silence as a Spiritual Vessel

Carthusian silence is not empty but a deliberate practice to create space for profound prayer and inner listening.

Self-Sufficiency

Monastic communities like the Carthusians often maintained gardens, orchards, and pastures to support themselves, integrating manual labor into spiritual life.

Important Terms (1/2)

*Scala Claustralium

*: Latin for "The Ladder of Cloistered Monks," the title of Guigo II's influential treatise. It evokes Jacob's ladder from Genesis.

*Lectio Divina

*: Latin for "sacred reading," a slow, prayerful engagement with Scripture aimed at spiritual transformation.

*Lectio

*: Latin for "reading," specifically the slow, attentive reading of sacred texts in a monastic context.

*Meditatio

*: Latin for "meditation," involving the active intellectual engagement with and internal pondering of what has been read.

*Oratio

*: Latin for "prayer," referring to heartfelt, spontaneous conversation with God arising from meditation.

Important Terms (2/2)

*Contemplatio

*: Latin for "contemplation," a grace-filled, direct, and experiential knowledge of God's presence that transcends intellectual effort.

Cell (Carthusian usage)

Not merely a small room, but a monk's private dwelling, typically a small house with its own garden, workshop, and covered walkway.

Sacred Offices / Divine Office

The canonical hours of daily liturgical prayer (e.g., Matins, Lauds, Vespers), chanted in common by Carthusians only on Sundays and great feasts.

Desert (monastic meaning)

A remote, wild, or secluded place chosen for spiritual retreat and solitude, not necessarily a sandy wasteland.

Bridegroom

A spiritual metaphor for Christ, often used in monastic theology (e.g., from the Song of Songs) to describe the soul's intimate relationship with God.
