Important Terms (1/3)

Divine Office (Officium Divinum) / Opus Dei (Work of God) / Liturgy of the Hours

The cycle of daily communal prayer that structures a monk's day, consisting of psalms, hymns, scripture readings, and prayers chanted at fixed times.

Canonical Hours

The eight fixed times of daily prayer prescribed by the Rule of Saint Benedict: Matins, Lauds, Prime, Terce, Sext, None, Vespers, and Compline.

Psalter

The Book of Psalms, which contains 150 psalms, chanted frequently in monastic life.

Purgatory

The Catholic doctrine that souls who die in God's grace but still bear the effects of sin undergo a process of purification before entering heaven.

All Souls' Day

A solemn day of prayer for all the faithful departed, instituted by Abbot Odilo of Cluny in 998 and celebrated on November 2 in the Western Church.

Important Terms (2/3)

Communion of Saints (Communio Sanctorum)

Oratores

The "those who pray" order in the medieval three-orders model of society, primarily referring to monks and clergy.

Bellatores

The "those who fight" order, referring to knights and nobility.

Laboratores

The "those who work" order, referring to peasants and laborers.

Gregorian Reform

A broad reform movement in the Catholic Church during the 11th century, advocating for papal supremacy, clerical celibacy, and freedom from secular interference, deeply influenced by Cluniac ideals.

Important Terms (3/3)

Simony

The buying or selling of church offices or spiritual goods, a practice vigorously opposed during the Gregorian Reform.

Lay Investiture

The practice of secular rulers (laymen) appointing bishops and other church officials, which the Gregorian Reform sought to eliminate.

Saint Benedict

Author of the Rule of Saint Benedict, which guided Western monasticism.

Abbot Odilo

Cluniac abbot who instituted All Souls' Day in 998.

Bernard of Clairvaux

A prominent Cistercian abbot who criticized Cluny's splendor and emphasis on elaborate worship in the 12th century.

Cluny's Fate

Despite its original charter aiming for freedom, Cluny eventually succumbed to some dangers of wealth and influence, leading to calls for new reforms (e.g., the Cistercians).

The Legacy of Cluny

The Gregorian Reform

Deeply influenced by Cluny's ideal of a Church free from secular control. Many reform popes had Cluniac ties.

The Crusades

Pope Urban II, who called the First Crusade in 1095, was a former Cluniac monk. The Cluniac network supported the movement.
