Contemplation as a Divine Gift

While the first three rungs of *lectio divina* are within human effort, contemplation is a gift of divine grace from God, not something that can be achieved through human will or technique.

Semi-Eremitic Life

The unique Carthusian monastic structure that blends elements of both solitary (eremitic) and communal (cenobitic) life, allowing for deep solitude within the protection and support of a community.

The Carthusian Cell

A self-contained hermitage within the monastery walls where the monk lives, prays, eats, and works alone, fostering profound silence and detachment.

Discipline of Silence

For Carthusians, silence is not merely the absence of noise but a positive spiritual reality—a space for listening to God and emptying oneself of worldly distractions.

Contemplative vs. Active Life

Intercessory Prayer as Carthusian Work

Carthusians embrace the contemplative life in its purest form, seeing their hidden life of prayer and intercession as a vital service to the Church and the world.

Guigo II's Legacy

His framework for *lectio divina* has profoundly influenced Catholic spiritual tradition, extending beyond the cloister to be recommended for all believers.

Important Terms (1/4)

Lectio Divina

A traditional monastic practice of prayerful Scripture reading, developed into a four-step framework by Guigo II.

Lectio

The first rung of *lectio divina*, involving slow, attentive reading of a Scripture passage.

Meditatio

The second rung, where one ponders and "chews" (ruminates) on the words of Scripture, extracting their meaning and asking what God is saying.

Ruminatio

The ancient monastic metaphor of "chewing the cud," referring to the slow, reflective pondering of Scripture.

Oratio

The third rung, a spontaneous heart-felt prayer rising in response to what has been read and meditated upon.

Important Terms (2/4)

Contemplatio

The fourth and highest rung, a gift from God where the soul rests in God's presence, overwhelmed by divine love and knowledge beyond words.

Ascetical effort

Human actions and disciplines undertaken for spiritual growth, such as reading, meditation, and prayer.

Mystical grace

A supernatural gift from God, such as contemplation, that transcends human effort.

Semi-eremitic

A monastic lifestyle combining elements of both solitary (hermitic) and communal (cenobitic) life, notably practiced by the Carthusians.

Eremitic

Pertaining to the life of a hermit, characterized by solitude.

Important Terms (3/4)

Cenobitic

Pertaining to monastic life lived in community.

Charterhouse

A Carthusian monastery.

Divine Office

The public prayer of the Church, consisting of psalms, readings, and prayers, recited at various times throughout the day.

Spatiamentum

A weekly communal walk taken by Carthusian monks outside their cells.

Contemplative life

A life dedicated primarily to prayer, silence, and direct attention to God, often within a monastic setting.

Important Terms (4/4)

Active life

A life dedicated primarily to service, good works, and charity in the world.

The Contemplative Life and the Active Life

Martha

is traditionally seen as a figure of the **active life**: dedicated to good works, service, and charity.

Mary

represents the **contemplative life**: focused on prayer, silence, and attentive listening to God's word.
