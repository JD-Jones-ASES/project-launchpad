Important Terms (1/5)

Cluniac Reform

A movement originating from the Abbey of Cluny in the 10th and 11th centuries, which reformed monasticism by emphasizing elaborate liturgy, centralized authority, and independence from secular lord...

Desert Fathers

Early Christian hermits, ascetics, and monks who lived in the deserts of Egypt and Syria, renowned for their pursuit of spiritual purity through solitude, fasting, and prayer. They served as an ins...

Pope Gregory VII (c. 1015–1085)

A pivotal pope whose Gregorian Reform sought to assert papal authority, free the Church from secular control (e.g., lay investiture), and restore clerical discipline.

St. Bruno of Cologne (c. 1030–1101)

Founder of the Carthusian order, a renowned scholar and teacher who abandoned a prestigious career to seek a life of eremitical austerity.

Reims Cathedral School

One of the most important cathedral schools in 11th-century Europe, where Bruno was educated and later served as headmaster. These schools were precursors to medieval universities.

Important Terms (2/5)

Manasses de Gournay

The corrupt and simoniacal Archbishop of Reims who Bruno opposed, leading to his disillusionment with the institutional Church.

Simony

The buying or selling of spiritual things, especially Church offices or sacraments, considered a grave sin. Named after Simon Magus (Acts 8:18–20).

St. Hugh of Grenoble (1053–1132)

Bishop of Grenoble, a close friend and protector of Bruno who led him and his companions to the Chartreuse valley.

Cartusia (Chartreuse)

The Latin name for the remote, desolate valley in the French Alps where St. Bruno founded the first Carthusian hermitage. The order takes its name from this location.

Grande Chartreuse

The original and mother house of the Carthusian order, located in the French Alps.

Important Terms (3/5)

Carthusian Cell

A small, self-contained dwelling for a Carthusian monk, typically including a workroom, bedroom, small garden, and covered walkway. Monks spend most of their lives here in solitude.

Matins

The first and longest of the canonical hours, traditionally prayed in the middle of the night (around 2:00 a.m.) by Carthusian monks communally in church.

Prior

The head of a Carthusian monastery (charterhouse). Carthusian superiors are called priors, not abbots, reflecting their emphasis on humility and service rather than grandeur.

Pope Urban II (c. 1042–1099)

A former student of St. Bruno at Reims, he later summoned Bruno to Rome as an advisor and is famous for calling the First Crusade.

Guigo I (1083–1136)

The fifth prior of the Grande Chartreuse, who, around 1127, composed the *Consuetudines Cartusiae*, formalizing the Carthusian customs and way of life.

Important Terms (4/5)

Consuetudines Cartusiae ("Customs of the Chartreuse")

A detailed description of the daily routine, liturgical practices, and way of life of the early Carthusian monks, serving as the foundation for their future legislation.

Spatiamentum

The weekly communal walk taken by Carthusian monks, during which they are permitted to converse freely. It is one of the few breaks in their habitual silence.

Guigo II (d. c. 1188)

A later prior of the Grande Chartreuse who wrote *Scala Claustralium* (The Ladder of Monks), an influential treatise on *lectio divina*.

Scala Claustralium ("The Ladder of Cloistered Monks")

Lectio Divina ("Sacred Reading")

A slow, prayerful method of reading Scripture, central to monastic tradition, involving the four stages described by Guigo II.

Important Terms (5/5)

Cartusia numquam reformata quia numquam deformata

The unofficial motto of the Carthusian order: "The Carthusian order was never reformed because it was never deformed," signifying its remarkable fidelity to its original way of life.

Stat crux dum volvitur orbis

The official motto of the Carthusian order: "The cross stands while the world turns," expressing their conviction in the unchanging nature of Christ's cross amidst a changing world.
