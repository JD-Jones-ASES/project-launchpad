Important Terms (1/5)

Anthony of Egypt (c. 251–356)

Considered one of the founders of Christian monasticism, known for his literal interpretation of the Gospel and retreat into the desert.

Desert Fathers and Mothers

Early Christian monks and nuns who lived in the deserts of Egypt, Palestine, and Syria from the third to fifth centuries, known for their spiritual wisdom.

Apophthegmata Patrum ("Sayings of the Fathers")

A collection of short, memorable sayings and anecdotes from the Desert Fathers and Mothers.

John Cassian (c. 360–435)

A monk trained in Egypt who founded a monastery in Gaul and transmitted Eastern monastic teachings to the West through his *Institutes* and *Conferences*.

Institutes and Conferences

John Cassian's two major works, which became foundational texts for Western monasticism, explaining the practices and spiritual insights of the Egyptian desert monks.

Important Terms (2/5)

Acedia

Spiritual listlessness, sloth, or boredom, sometimes called the "noonday devil," which Cassian analyzed as a significant temptation for monks.

Benedict of Nursia (c. 480–547)

Author of the influential *Rule of Saint Benedict*, a practical guide for communal monastic life.

Rule of Saint Benedict (c. 530)

A moderate and flexible guide for monastic community life that became the standard for most Western monasteries, covering daily prayer, work, and hospitality.

Opus Dei ("the work of God")

In Benedictine usage, this refers to the communal chanting of psalms and prayers at set hours, also known as the Divine Office or Liturgy of the Hours.

Pope St. Gregory the Great (c. 540–604)

Pope and Doctor of the Church, whose *Dialogues* (Book II) provide the only early source for the life of St. Benedict.

Important Terms (3/5)

Celtic Monasticism

A distinct monastic tradition that flourished in Ireland, Scotland, and the North Atlantic islands, known for its asceticism, scholarship (illuminated manuscripts), and missionary voyages.

The Voyage of Saint Brendan (Navigatio Sancti Brendani)

A medieval Latin narrative describing the legendary sea voyage of St. Brendan the Navigator and his monks.

Columbanus (c. 540–615)

An Irish missionary monk who carried Celtic monasticism to the European continent.

Cluny

A monastery founded in Burgundy in 910 with a revolutionary charter placing it under direct papal protection, becoming the center of a vast monastic reform movement.

Carthusian Order

An austere, contemplative monastic order founded by Bruno of Cologne in 1084, characterized by hermitic life within a community.

Important Terms (4/5)

Stat crux dum volvitur orbis ("The cross stands while the world turns")

The Carthusian motto, expressing their conviction in the enduring nature of the contemplative life.

Guigo II's Ladder of Monks

A classic guide to the practice of *lectio divina*, describing its four "rungs": reading, meditation, prayer, and contemplation.

Lectio divina ("sacred reading")

A slow, prayerful reading of Scripture or spiritual texts aimed at communion with God rather than mere information.

Cistercians

Monks who emerged in the twelfth century, dedicated to a stricter observance of the *Rule of Saint Benedict*.

Bernard of Clairvaux (1090–1153)

A Cistercian abbot, Doctor of the Church, and one of the most influential churchmen of the twelfth century, known for his writings on the love of God.

Important Terms (5/5)

Thomas Merton (1915–1968)

A twentieth-century American Trappist monk whose autobiography, *The Seven Storey Mountain*, introduced millions to monastic life.

Our Lady of Clear Creek Abbey

A modern Benedictine monastery in Oklahoma, serving as an example of contemporary monastic life.
