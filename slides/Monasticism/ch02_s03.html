Important Terms (1/2)

Fuga Mundi

Latin for "flight from the world"; a deliberate withdrawal from worldly distractions to seek God more freely.

Evangelical Counsels

The counsels of poverty, chastity, and obedience, rooted in Christ's teachings in the Gospels, embraced by those called to a more radical form of discipleship.

Miles Christi

Latin for "soldier of Christ"; refers to the monk engaged in spiritual warfare against invisible enemies.

Logismoi

Greek for "thoughts" or "thought-patterns"; Evagrius Ponticus's term for the eight recurring temptations that assail the monk.

Acedia

One of the eight deadly thoughts; a deep spiritual listlessness, boredom, and restlessness, often called the "noonday devil."

Important Terms (2/2)

Apatheia

Greek for a state of inner freedom where the passions are rightly ordered, allowing pure love for God and neighbor, not the absence of emotion.

Jesus Prayer

A short, rhythmic prayer from the Eastern Christian tradition ("Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner") whose roots lie in the Desert Fathers' practice of unceasing prayer.

Opus Dei

Latin for "the Work of God"; in monastic usage, refers to the communal prayer of the Divine Office, or Liturgy of the Hours, a structured cycle of worship.

Apatheia: Freedom of the Heart

Goal of the Struggle

*Apatheia* is the ultimate internal state resulting from the conquest of disordered passions.

True Meaning

It does *not* mean modern "apathy" (indifference or lack of feeling). Instead, it signifies a deep, settled freedom of heart and the "health of the soul" as Evagrius described it.

Ordered Love

A monk achieving *apatheia* is not emotionless but loves more deeply and purely, free from the tyranny of selfishness, fear, and compulsive desires. His love is ordered and directed toward God.

Unceasing Prayer

Positive Goal

Alongside *apatheia* (freedom *from* disordered passions), unceasing prayer was the positive goal—communion *with* God.

Pauline Command

Monks took St. Paul's injunction to "pray without ceasing" (1 Thess. 5:17) literally, aiming to make every waking moment an act of prayer.
