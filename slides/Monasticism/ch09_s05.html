Why Monasticism Matters

Key Concepts

Universal Relevance of Monasticism

Monastic practices are not exclusive to monks and nuns but represent an intensified way of living the Gospel that offers invaluable lessons and tools for every Christian.

Six Monastic Practices for All Christians

Benedictine Oblates

A formal path for laypeople to affiliate with a Benedictine monastery and live according to the spirit of the Rule of Saint Benedict in their daily lives.

"Prefer Nothing to the Love of Christ" (Nihil amori Christi praeponere)

The central, unifying message of the monastic tradition and the ultimate call to every Christian, advocating for placing God first in all things.

Important Terms (1/2)

Divine Office (Liturgy of the Hours/Opus Dei)

The Church's official daily prayer, consisting of psalms, hymns, readings, and intercessions prayed at set times throughout the day. Abbreviated forms are available for laypeople.

Lectio Divina

An ancient method of spiritual reading, particularly of Scripture, often described as a four-rung ladder: reading (lectio), meditation (meditatio), prayer (oratio), and contemplation (contemplatio).

Evangelical Counsel of Poverty

A voluntary renunciation of material possessions and attachment to worldly goods for the sake of the Kingdom of God, one of the three evangelical counsels (poverty, chastity, and obedience).

Stabilitas

From the Rule of Saint Benedict, the vow of stability is a commitment to remain in one particular monastic community and geographical location for life, fostering deep roots and commitment.

Cenobitic Tradition

A form of monasticism where monks or nuns live together in a community, sharing common facilities and a regulated daily life, in contrast to hermitic (solitary) life.
