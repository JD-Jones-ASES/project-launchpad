Key Concepts (1/2)

Monasticism as a Living Tradition

The monastic way of life, from its ancient origins with the Desert Fathers to figures like Saint Benedict, continues to be practiced by thousands of men and women globally in active communities.

The Solesmes Congregation and Gregorian Chant

Many Benedictine monasteries, including Clear Creek's mother house, belong to the Solesmes congregation, known for its deep devotion to Gregorian chant and the traditional Latin liturgy.

The *Opus Dei* (Work of God)

Communal chanting of the Divine Office, central to Benedictine life, is performed multiple times a day and night, reflecting Saint Benedict's priority for liturgical prayer.

Benedictine Vow of *Stabilitas* Embodied

The act of building a monastery in stone, designed to last for centuries, physically manifests the Benedictine vow of stability and permanence in a transient world.

Important Terms (1/2)

Divine Office

The public prayer of the Church, recited at various canonical hours throughout the day and night by religious communities and clergy. Also known as the Liturgy of the Hours.

Gregorian Chant

A body of monophonic (single-line melody), unaccompanied sacred Latin song of the Roman Catholic Church, historically used in the Divine Office and Mass.

Solesmes Congregation

A network of Benedictine monasteries established by Dom Guéranger in 1833, known for its revival and devotion to Gregorian chant and traditional Latin liturgy.

Opus Dei

Latin for "the work of God," Saint Benedict's term for the communal chanting of the Divine Office, which he considered the monk's highest priority.

Romanesque

An architectural style prevalent in Western Europe from roughly the 10th to the 12th century, characterized by rounded arches, thick walls, massive towers, and a sense of permanence.

Important Terms (2/2)

Stabilitas

The Benedictine vow of stability, committing a monk or nun to a particular monastic community for life, symbolizing spiritual steadfastness and rootedness.

Trappists (Order of Cistercians of the Strict Observance)

A contemplative Roman Catholic religious order of monks and nuns, forming a branch of the Cistercian order, known for rigorous silence, manual labor, and asceticism.

Discalced Carmelites

A contemplative Roman Catholic religious order of friars and nuns, founded by Saint Teresa of Ávila, known for strict enclosure, prayer, and devotion to Marian spirituality.

Poor Clares

A contemplative Roman Catholic religious order for women in the Franciscan tradition, founded by Saint Clare of Assisi, characterized by evangelical poverty, prayer, and enclosure.

Valliscaulian Monks

A small, short-lived French monastic order founded in the 12th century, related to the Carthusians, which followed a rigorous contemplative life.
