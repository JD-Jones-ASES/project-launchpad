A written guide that outlines the daily life, practices, and principles for a religious community, ensuring structure, discipline, and common purpose. The Rule of Saint Benedict is a famous example.

St. Benedict of Nursia

Often called the "father of Western monasticism," his Rule of Saint Benedict was instrumental in making the cenobitic form dominant in the Western Church.

Intermediate Monastic Forms

Variations that combine elements of both cenobitic and eremitic life, such as the *laura* (individual cells, common worship) and the *Carthusian model* (individual houses, shared communal aspects).

Important Terms (1/2)

Cenobitic

From the Greek *koinos bios* ("common life"). Describes monks who live in a community under a common rule and superior.

Eremitic

From the Greek *eremos* ("desert" or "wilderness"). Describes monks (hermits) who live alone, in solitude and silence.

Monastic Rule (Regula)

A written guide for the daily life of a religious community, governing practices, prayers, and conduct.

Abbot/Abbess

The superior of a monastic community, responsible for directing the monks or nuns and interpreting the monastic rule.

Night Office (Vigils or Matins)

The first prayer service of the monastic day, traditionally sung in the hours before dawn.

Important Terms (2/2)

St. Benedict of Nursia (c. 480–547)

The highly influential figure considered the father of Western monasticism, whose Rule became the standard.

St. Anthony of Egypt (c. 251–356)

The widely recognized father of Christian monasticism, known for his decades as a hermit in the Egyptian desert.

Laura (Lavra)

A semi-eremitic community where monks live in individual cells but gather for common prayer and meals on certain days.

Carthusian Model

A monastic form that balances communal and solitary living, with monks having individual cells/houses but sharing certain prayers and meals.
