Important Terms (1/2)

Aqueduct

A channel or pipe system for carrying water, typically over long distances. Roman aqueducts, often built on stone arches, used gravity to deliver water from springs to cities with precise downhill ...

Opus Caementicium

The Latin term for Roman concrete, a revolutionary building material that used pozzolana volcanic ash, lime, and seawater, allowing for immense, durable structures.

Pozzolana

A volcanic ash, found near Pozzuoli, that was a key ingredient in Roman concrete, giving it unique properties, including the ability to harden underwater.

Post-and-lintel

An architectural construction method used by the Greeks, consisting of two vertical columns supporting a horizontal beam. Its span was limited by the tensile strength of the beam.

Compression

The force that pushes materials together. The Roman arch leveraged compressive strength, making stone incredibly strong and allowing for larger spans than post-and-lintel construction.

Important Terms (2/2)

Tension

The force that pulls materials apart. Stone is weak in tension, which limited the span of Greek post-and-lintel structures.

Annona

The grain dole provided by the Roman government to the urban poor, a critical component of the "bread and circuses" strategy.

Panem et Circenses

Latin for "bread and circuses," a phrase coined by Juvenal to describe the Roman government's method of appeasing the populace with food and entertainment.

Milliarium Aureum

The "Golden Milestone" erected by Augustus in the Roman Forum, from which all distances on Roman roads were measured, symbolizing Rome's centrality.

Plutarch's Parallel Lives

A collection of biographies of famous Greeks and Romans, serving as a primary historical source for many ancient events, including those depicted in Shakespeare's plays.
