Divergent Models

Literary and Moral Ideals

The Homeric Ideal (Aristocratic)

Focused on **kleos** (glory/fame) won through heroic deeds in war. The *Iliad* (the wrath of Achilles) and the *Odyssey* (the resourcefulness of Odysseus) served as the "Bible" of Greek culture.

The Hesiodic Ideal (Farmer/Citizen)

Found in *Works and Days*, this tradition emphasized justice, honest labor, and the "Iron Age" reality of toil. It argued that the gods favor those who work hard and treat neighbors fairly.

Glossary of Important Terms

Key Figures to Know (1/2)

Homer (c. 750 BC)

The traditional poet of the *Iliad* and *Odyssey* who defined Greek heroic values.

Hesiod (c. 700 BC)

Poet of *Works and Days*; emphasized justice and the dignity of the working farmer.

Draco (621 BC)

Athenian lawgiver who published the first written code, noted for its extreme severity.

Solon (594 BC)

Reformer who prevented civil war in Athens by canceling debts and restructuring the social classes.

Thales of Miletus (c. 585 BC)

The first philosopher; predicted a solar eclipse and sought rational origins of the universe.

Key Figures to Know (2/2)

Cleisthenes (508 BC)

The reformer who established the democratic institutions of Athens.

Achilles

The greatest Greek warrior of the *Iliad*, whose pride and rage drive the epic's plot.

Odysseus

The clever hero of the *Odyssey* who survives a ten-year journey home through ingenuity.

Penelope

The wife of Odysseus, celebrated for her loyalty and matching her husband's cleverness.
