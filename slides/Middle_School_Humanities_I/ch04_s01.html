Important Terms (1/2)

*Opus Caementicium

*: The Latin name for Roman concrete, known for its unique composition and durability.

*Pozzolana

*: A specific type of volcanic ash, found near Pozzuoli, that was a key ingredient in Roman concrete, giving it its distinctive properties.

Arch

A curved structural element that spans an opening and supports weight, primarily by redirecting forces through compression into its supports.

Post-and-Lintel Construction

An architectural system where two vertical posts support a horizontal beam (lintel), common in ancient Greek architecture, but limited by the tensile strength of the beam.

Aqueduct

A channel or pipe system designed to carry water, often over long distances, typically utilizing arches and a gentle downhill gradient to move water by gravity.

Important Terms (2/2)

*Annona

*: The Roman grain dole, a state-sponsored distribution of free or subsidized grain to the urban poor of Rome.

*Panem et Circenses

*: A Latin phrase meaning "bread and circuses," coined by the poet Juvenal, referring to the practice of providing food and entertainment to keep the populace content and prevent unrest.

Milliarium Aureum (Golden Milestone)

A monument in the Roman Forum erected by Augustus, from which all distances on Roman roads were measured.

B. The Mastery of the Arch

Beyond Invention

The Romans did not invent the arch, but they became its most skilled practitioners.

Structural Advantage

The arch revolutionized construction by transferring the weight of a structure sideways into supporting columns, primarily through **compression**.

Enabling Scale

This fundamental engineering breakthrough allowed for much larger spans and greater heights, making structures possible that the Greeks could never have attempted with their methods.

D. Aqueducts: Lifelines of the Empire

Purpose

Channel or pipe systems for transporting water over long distances, vital for urban sanitation, public baths, and fountains.

Construction

Typically ran on massive stone arches at a precise, gentle downhill gradient (often just one inch per hundred feet) to allow gravity to move water.

Impact

By the late empire, Rome had eleven aqueducts, providing more water per person than many modern cities, a testament to Roman planning and engineering.

E. Engineering and Imperial Expansion

Infrastructure for Empire

Roman engineering didn't just build monuments; it built the infrastructure necessary to support an empire spanning from Hadrian's Wall to the Euphrates, and from the Rhine to the Sahara.
