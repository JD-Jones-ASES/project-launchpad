_Virtus_

A core Roman virtue meaning courage, manliness, and excellence, particularly in military or public service. It implies action and bravery in the service of the state, often at great personal cost.

_Pietas_

A deep sense of duty, devotion, and respect towards the gods, one's family, and the state. It encompasses loyalty and a recognition of obligations.

_Fides_

The virtue of faithfulness, trustworthiness, and keeping one's word. Romans considered _fides_ foundational to all social contracts and alliances, essential for civil society to function.

Important Terms (1/2)

_res publica_

Latin for "the public thing," referring to the Roman Republic or the common good. The welfare of the _res publica_ was paramount for virtuous Romans.

_kleos_

An ancient Greek concept meaning "glory" or "fame," especially that achieved in battle and immortalized in poetry. It was a primary motivation for Greek heroes like Achilles.

Dictator

In the Roman Republic, a temporary magistrate appointed with absolute power during times of extreme emergency. By tradition, dictators were expected to resign their power once the crisis was resolved.

Consul

One of two annually elected chief magistrates who governed the Roman Republic. They held significant military and civil authority.

Etruscans

An ancient civilization located in Etruria (modern Tuscany), north of Rome. They were powerful neighbors and frequent adversaries of early Rome.

Important Terms (2/2)

Pons Sublicius

Rome's oldest bridge, a wooden structure spanning the Tiber River. It played a crucial role in the story of Horatius Cocles.

First Punic War (264–241 BC)

The first of three major conflicts between the Roman Republic and Carthage, primarily fought for control of Sicily and naval dominance in the Mediterranean.

The Purpose of History

Both Livy and Plutarch viewed history not just as a record but as a tool for moral instruction.

Livy: _Ab Urbe Condita_ (From the Founding of the City)

Author and Work

Titus Livius (c. 64 BC – AD 17) composed a monumental history of Rome from its legendary founding to his own era (142 books, only 35 survive).

Historical Approach

Livy was a master storyteller with a clear moral agenda. He openly acknowledged that the earliest accounts were often legendary but deemed them valuable for their moral lessons.
