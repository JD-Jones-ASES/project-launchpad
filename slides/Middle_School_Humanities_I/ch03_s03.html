Important Terms (1/6)

Romulus and Remus

Twin brothers, nursed by a she-wolf, legendary founders of Rome. Romulus killed Remus and became the first king.

ab urbe condita

Latin for "from the founding of the city," referring to Rome's traditional founding date of 753 BC.

Aeneas

Trojan prince, a hero of a second Roman founding legend, whose journey to Italy symbolized Roman *pietas* and divine destiny.

Pietas

A core Roman virtue meaning dutiful devotion to the gods, to family, and to the state; subordinating personal desires to obligations.

Tarquinius Superbus (Tarquin the Proud)

The tyrannical seventh and last king of Rome, whose abuses led to the overthrow of the monarchy.

Important Terms (2/6)

Lucretia

A noble Roman woman whose rape by Tarquin's son and subsequent suicide sparked the rebellion against the monarchy.

Lucius Junius Brutus

Roman nobleman who led the revolt against Tarquin the Proud and was instrumental in founding the Republic.

Republic (res publica)

Literally "the public thing"; Rome's system of shared government designed to prevent permanent power in one man's hands, led by two consuls.

Consuls

Two annually elected chief magistrates of the Roman Republic, who commanded the army, presided over the Senate, and held *imperium*.

Intercessio (Veto)

The power of a consul to block the decisions of the other consul, or a tribune to block a magistrate's action affecting a plebeian.

Important Terms (3/6)

Imperium

The supreme executive power in the Roman Republic, including military command and legal authority, held by consuls and praetors.

Dictator

A single magistrate appointed by the Senate in military emergencies, holding supreme power for a maximum of six months, bypassing the consular veto.

Senate

The advisory body of experienced former magistrates in the Roman Republic, serving for life, controlling state finances and foreign policy. Its decrees (*senatus consulta*) held great moral weight.

Tribunes of the Plebs

Ten annually elected officials whose persons were *sacrosanct* (inviolable) and who could veto any magistrate's action affecting a plebeian citizen.

Sacrosanct

Legally inviolable; a capital crime to harm the Tribunes of the Plebs.

Important Terms (4/6)

Cursus Honorum

The "path of honors"; the required sequence of political offices a Roman nobleman held to progress in public service (quaestor, aedile, praetor, consul, censor).

Patricians

The hereditary noble families who dominated the early Roman Republic, controlling the Senate and major priesthoods.

Plebeians

The ordinary free citizens of Rome, who made up the vast majority of the population and struggled for equal political rights.

Conflict of the Orders

The long struggle (c. 500-287 BC) between patricians and plebeians for political, legal, and social equality.

Secession

The plebeians' tactic of withdrawing from Rome and refusing to participate in its defense until their demands for rights were met.

Important Terms (5/6)

Twelve Tables

Rome's first written law code, produced in 451–450 BC and displayed publicly, making law knowable and binding on all citizens.

Licinian-Sextian Laws (367 BC)

Legislation that opened the consulship to plebeians.

Lex Hortensia (287 BC)

Law that declared resolutions of the plebeian assembly binding on all Romans, patrician and plebeian alike.

Mixed Constitution

A form of government combining elements of monarchy, aristocracy, and democracy, believed by Romans to provide balance and stability.

Virtus

A core Roman virtue meaning courage, self-discipline, and excellence in the service of the community (from *vir*, man).

Important Terms (6/6)

Gravitas

A core Roman virtue meaning seriousness, weight, and dignity appropriate for public responsibility. The opposite of *levitas* (frivolity).

Latin Rights

Partial Roman citizenship offered to some defeated cities, granting legal protections for merchants but not the full vote, as part of Rome's policy of incorporation.

The Stories Rome Told About Its Origins

Purpose of Legends

Before written history, Romans told legends not just to record events, but to explain *why* Rome deserved to rule, revealing its core values.

Romulus and Remus Legend

Aeneas Legend

Why Origin Stories Matter

They answer "Who are we, and what do we stand for?" Even if legendary, they convey real values and civic identity (e.g., Aeneas carrying his father represents honoring parents, gods, and obligations).

Livy's Approach

As a historian, Livy often acknowledged uncertainty in legends (e.g., about the Senate's original size), a valued quality in historical sources.
