Important Terms (1/5)

Pax Romana

Latin for "Roman peace." The period of relative stability from Augustus (27 BC) to Marcus Aurelius (AD 180). Its conditions — safe roads, shared language, single legal system — proved essential to ...

Augustus Caesar (27 BC – AD 14)

The first Roman emperor; ended the civil wars and established the Pax Romana. Jesus was born during his reign.

Herod the Great (d. 4 BC)

Rome's client king of Judea; rebuilt the Jerusalem Temple; ruled with notorious cruelty. Jesus was born during his reign (hence the calendar correction placing Jesus' birth c. 4 BC).

Pontius Pilate

Roman governor (procurator) of Judea who presided over Jesus' trial and ordered his crucifixion. His existence is confirmed by both Josephus and Tacitus.

Josephus (c. AD 37–100)

First-century Jewish historian whose Antiquities of the Jews contains the earliest non-Christian reference to Jesus — a "wise man" whose followers reported he appeared alive after crucifixion.

Important Terms (4/5)

Edict of Milan (AD 313)

Joint decree by Constantine and Licinius granting religious toleration to all religions throughout the Empire, restoring confiscated Christian property, and ending official persecution.

Chi-Rho

The first two Greek letters of Christos (Christ): X and P. Constantine reportedly had his soldiers paint this symbol on their shields before the Battle of Milvian Bridge.

Arius (c. AD 256–336)

Alexandrian priest who taught that the Son of God was the greatest of created beings but was created — "there was a time when he was not." His teaching was declared heretical at Nicaea.

Homoousios

Greek for "of one substance." The key word of the Nicene Creed, affirming that the Son is of the same substance as the Father — fully divine, not merely similar to God or a created being.
