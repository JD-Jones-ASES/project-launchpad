Important Terms (1/3)

Confessions

Augustine's groundbreaking autobiographical work, a prayer, confession of sin, and philosophical journey.

Consolation of Philosophy

Boethius's profound philosophical dialogue, written in prison, exploring themes of fortune, evil, and true happiness.

Manichaeism

A dualistic religion that Augustine experimented with, which attributed evil to the material world.

Rhetoric

The art of effective or persuasive speaking or writing; a highly prestigious profession in the Roman world, pursued by Augustine.

Tolle, lege

Latin for "Take up and read," the phrase a child's voice uttered, prompting Augustine's pivotal conversion experience.

Important Terms (2/3)

Monica

Augustine's devout Christian mother, whose persistent prayers for his conversion are celebrated in the *Confessions*.

Donatists

A Christian sect in North Africa that Augustine opposed, which held that clergy who had renounced faith during persecution could not administer valid sacraments.

Pelagians

A group whose teachings Augustine combated, asserting that humans could achieve salvation through their own efforts and free will, largely independent of divine grace.

Amor Sui

Latin for "love of self," which Augustine identifies as the foundational principle of the earthly City of Man.

Amor Dei

Latin for "love of God," which Augustine identifies as the foundational principle of the City of God.

Important Terms (3/3)

Eschatology

The part of theology concerned with death, judgment, and the final destiny of the soul and humankind.

Lady Philosophy

An allegorical figure who appears to Boethius in prison in *Consolation of Philosophy*, guiding him through philosophical arguments.

Theodoric

The Ostrogothic king of Italy who imprisoned Boethius on charges of treason.

Boethius's *Consolation of Philosophy

*: Unique as a philosophical dialogue written by a man condemned to death, debating life's meaning when all external goods are lost.

Shared Question

Both authors address the existential crisis of finding meaning and stability amidst the collapse of the Roman world.

Shared Answer

They conclude that true goods—virtue, wisdom, and (for Augustine) God—are internal and cannot be seized by external forces.

Augustine: *Confessions* (AD 397–400)

Literary Significance

The first great autobiography in Western literature, structured as a direct prayer to God.

The Restless Heart

Its opening lines encapsulate its core theme: "Thou madest us for Thyself, and our heart is restless, until it repose in Thee."

Sin as Misplaced Love

Augustine reflects on how he searched "abroad" for God who was "within," loving "fair forms" (created things) instead of the Creator. This defines sin as loving the gifts rather than the Giver.
