Important Terms (1/3)

Confessions

Augustine's groundbreaking autobiographical work, a prayer, confession of sin, and philosophical journey.

Consolation of Philosophy

Boethius's profound philosophical dialogue, written in prison, exploring themes of fortune, evil, and true happiness.

Manichaeism

A dualistic religion that Augustine experimented with, which attributed evil to the material world.

Rhetoric

The art of effective or persuasive speaking or writing; a highly prestigious profession in the Roman world, pursued by Augustine.

Tolle, lege

Latin for "Take up and read," the phrase a child's voice uttered, prompting Augustine's pivotal conversion experience.

Important Terms (2/3)

Monica

Augustine's devout Christian mother, whose persistent prayers for his conversion are celebrated in the Confessions.

Donatists

A Christian sect in North Africa that Augustine opposed, which held that clergy who had renounced faith during persecution could not administer valid sacraments.

Pelagians

A group whose teachings Augustine combated, asserting that humans could achieve salvation through their own efforts and free will, largely independent of divine grace.

Amor Sui

Latin for "love of self," which Augustine identifies as the foundational principle of the earthly City of Man.

Amor Dei

Latin for "love of God," which Augustine identifies as the foundational principle of the City of God.

Important Terms (3/3)

Eschatology

The part of theology concerned with death, judgment, and the final destiny of the soul and humankind.

Lady Philosophy

An allegorical figure who appears to Boethius in prison in Consolation of Philosophy, guiding him through philosophical arguments.

Theodoric

The Ostrogothic king of Italy who imprisoned Boethius on charges of treason.

Both authors address the existential crisis of finding meaning and stability amidst the collapse of the Roman world.

They conclude that true goods—virtue, wisdom, and (for Augustine) God—are internal and cannot be seized by external forces.

Key Concepts (1/2)

How did Augustine's Confessions differ from previous historical or biographical writings?

It was simultaneously a prayer, a confession of personal sin, and a philosophical autobiography tracing a single human soul's journey toward truth, creating ...

What fundamental question did both Augustine and Boethius address that resonated with people in the collapsing Western Empire?

What do you hold onto when the world falls apart? Or, does life have any meaning when Fortune has stripped away everything?

Despite different approaches (theology vs. philosophy), what central point did Augustine and Boethius agree upon regarding true goods?

The things that can be taken from you were never truly yours; only virtue, wisdom, and (for Augustine) God himself are the only real goods.

Where and when was Augustine of Hippo born, and who were his notable parents?

He was born in AD 354 in Thagaste, Numidia (modern-day Algeria). His father Patricius was a pagan, and his mother Monica was a devout Christian.

Before his conversion, what were some key aspects of Augustine's intellectual and personal journey?

He pursued a career in rhetoric, studied at Carthage, took a concubine, and experimented with Manichaean religion, teaching in Rome and Milan.

What two significant events in Milan led to Augustine's intellectual and spiritual turning point?

Hearing Bishop Ambrose preach (which showed him Christianity was intellectually respectable) and reading the letters of Paul.

What is the famous opening line and central theological psychology of Augustine's Confessions?

'Thou madest us for Thyself, and our heart is restless, until it repose in Thee.' It signifies that the human heart seeks satisfaction only found in God.

According to Augustine, what does the 'restlessness' of the human heart signify?

It is a built-in feature, a hunger that will not be satisfied by anything less than God, for whom the heart was designed, not by wealth, fame, or pleasure.
