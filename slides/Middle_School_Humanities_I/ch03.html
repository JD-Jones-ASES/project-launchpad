Important Terms (1/5)

Res publica

Literally "the public thing" or "the public affair" — the Roman name for their system of shared government. The direct ancestor of the English word "republic."

Romulus

Legendary founder of Rome (753 BC by Roman reckoning); twin brother of Remus; traditional first king. His story embeds the founding of Rome's institutions in a divine narrative.

Aeneas

Trojan hero who escaped burning Troy carrying his father and household gods; ancestor of the Roman people in legend. His story, told by Virgil in the Aeneid, embodies pietas in its fullest form.

Pietas

Dutiful devotion to gods, family, and state. The most fundamental of Roman virtues — embodied in Aeneas carrying his father from Troy. Close to the Christian virtue of honor to parents and obedience.

Virtus

From vir (man). Active courage, self-discipline, and excellence in the service of the community. Directed toward the res publica, not toward personal glory. Demonstrated in battle, in the Senate, i...

Important Terms (2/5)

Gravitas

The quality of seriousness, weight, and dignity appropriate to a man of public responsibility. Its opposite, levitas (frivolity), was considered deeply un-Roman.

Fides

Faithfulness; trustworthiness; keeping one's word regardless of cost. Romans considered it the foundation of all social life: without fides, no agreement holds, no court can function, civilization ...

Tarquinius Superbus (Tarquin the Proud)

Last king of Rome; overthrown in 509 BC after his son violated the noblewoman Lucretia. His tyranny — executing citizens without trial, ignoring the Senate — became the permanent warning against mo...

Intercessio

The right of each consul (and later tribunes) to block or veto the action of another magistrate. The constitutional mechanism that prevented any single person from holding unchecked power.

Imperium

The right to command — held by consuls and military commanders. Full authority to lead armies, execute criminals, and govern provinces.

Important Terms (3/5)

Consul

One of two annually elected chief magistrates of the Roman Republic. Held full imperium but each could veto the other. The highest regular office in the cursus honorum.

Tribune of the Plebs

One of ten annually elected officials protecting plebeian citizens. Their persons were legally sacrosanct (inviolable); they could veto any magistrate's action affecting a plebeian. The 'common cit...

Cursus Honorum

The "path of honors" — the required sequence of offices (quaestor, aedile, praetor, consul, censor) that Roman nobles had to hold in order, with minimum ages and waiting periods between each post.

Senate

The advisory body of experienced former magistrates. Controlled state finances, foreign policy, and military commands. Its decrees were not formally laws but carried overwhelming moral authority.

Patricians

The hereditary noble families of Rome who originally monopolized the Senate, major priesthoods, and the consulship.

Important Terms (4/5)

Plebeians

The ordinary free citizens of Rome — the majority of the population — who struggled for two centuries to gain political rights equal to the patricians.

Conflict of the Orders

The long struggle (c. 494–287 BC) by which plebeians gained equal political rights: creation of the tribune (494 BC), Twelve Tables (451–450 BC), opening of consulship (367 BC), Lex Hortensia (287 ...

Twelve Tables

Rome's first written law code (451–450 BC), inscribed on twelve bronze tablets displayed publicly in the Forum. Made law knowable and consistent for all citizens.

Livy (Titus Livius, c. 64 BC – AD 17)

Roman historian who wrote 142 books (35 survive) on Rome from its founding to his day. Openly a moral historian using exemplary stories to form readers.

Plutarch (c. AD 46–120)

Greek biographer under the Roman Empire. Wrote the Parallel Lives, pairing 23 Greeks and 23 Romans in moral portraits. One of Shakespeare's most important sources.
