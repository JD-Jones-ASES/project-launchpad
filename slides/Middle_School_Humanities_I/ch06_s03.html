Important Terms (3/4)

Sack of Rome (AD 410)

The three-day plunder of the city of Rome by the Visigoths under Alaric, marking a profound psychological blow to the Roman world.

Attila the Hun (r. AD 434–453)

The powerful leader who united the Hunnic tribes and terrorized both Eastern and Western Roman Empires, earning the title "Scourge of God."

Battle of the Catalaunian Plains (AD 451)

A major battle in Gaul where a combined Roman and Visigothic army under General Aetius fought Attila's Huns to a stalemate, considered the last great Roman military victory in the West.

Pope Leo I

Bishop of Rome who famously met Attila the Hun at the Mincio River in AD 452 and persuaded him to turn back from invading Rome.

Vandals

A Germanic tribe that crossed into North Africa, established a kingdom, and sacked Rome in AD 455, stripping it more thoroughly than Alaric had.

The Long Decline: Seeds Planted Before AD 376

Not a Single Event

The fall of the Western Roman Empire was a gradual process spanning over two centuries, not just the sack of Rome in AD 410 or the deposition in AD 476.

Augustine's Question

The philosopher Augustine questioned whether Rome had ever truly lived up to Cicero's ideal of a republic founded on justice, or if Roman virtue was rooted in collective pride.

Political Instability

Frontier Pressure

Stabilization

Reunification

Founding Constantinople

Pressure on the Frontiers: The Visigoths and Huns (1/2)

Structural Weaknesses

The 4th-century Roman Empire, despite its scale, harbored deep weaknesses.

Arrival of the Huns (AD 370s)

Fearsome horse-warriors from Central Asia swept westward, driving Germanic tribes into Roman territory.

Visigothic Plea (AD 376)

Fleeing the Huns, the Visigoths begged Emperor Valens to let them cross the Danube and settle within Roman lands as refugees.

Roman Corruption and Exploitation

Roman administrators failed to provide promised grain and fair treatment. They exploited the Visigoths, selling dog-meat at exorbitant prices and demanding children as slaves.

Visigothic Revolt

The exploitation led to a Visigothic rebellion.

Key Concepts (1/2)

How is the 'fall' of the Roman Empire best described?

It was not a single dramatic moment, but a slow process of collapse, particularly of the Western imperial administration, army, and tax base.

What key aspects of Roman civilization endured in Western Europe around AD 500 despite the collapse of imperial administration?

Roman roads, Latin (as language of Church and scholarship), adapted Roman law in Germanic kingdoms, and the bishop of Rome as a stable institution.

What was the most consequential change for ordinary people's lives during the fall of Rome?

The collapse of trade and Roman economic integration.

What archaeological evidence supports the claim of trade collapse in Western Europe between AD 400 and 600?

A dramatic decline in the variety and quantity of goods available to ordinary people.

How did the Eastern Roman (Byzantine) Empire differ from the Western Empire in AD 476?

The Eastern Empire, with its capital at Constantinople, remained intact, was larger and wealthier, and included key regions like Egypt (the 'breadbasket').

For whom was the 'Roman Empire' still very much alive in AD 500?

Someone living in Constantinople, Alexandria, or Antioch (cities within the Eastern Roman Empire).

Key Concepts (2/2)

Until what year did the Eastern Roman (Byzantine) Empire continue to exist?

AD 1453, when it fell to the Ottoman Turks.

What is the key question to ask when studying the fall of Rome, to avoid fatalism?

'What was worth preserving, and who preserved it?'

What institution is credited with preserving civilization through the collapse of the Western Empire?

The monastery.
