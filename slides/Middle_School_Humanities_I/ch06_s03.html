Important Terms (2/4)

Huns

Nomadic, horse-riding warriors from Central Asia whose westward migration in the late 4th century AD triggered the movement of Germanic tribes into Roman territory.

Visigoths

A Germanic people who, fleeing the Huns, sought refuge within the Roman Empire and eventually revolted, decisively defeating the Romans at Adrianople and later sacking Rome.

Battle of Adrianople (AD 378)

A pivotal battle in which the Visigoths inflicted a devastating defeat on the Roman army, killing Emperor Valens and demonstrating the vulnerability of the Empire's frontiers.

Foederati

Alaric

A Visigothic leader and former Roman military officer who famously led the Visigoths in the sack of Rome in AD 410.

Important Terms (3/4)

Sack of Rome (AD 410)

The three-day plunder of the city of Rome by the Visigoths under Alaric, marking a profound psychological blow to the Roman world.

Attila the Hun (r. AD 434–453)

The powerful leader who united the Hunnic tribes and terrorized both Eastern and Western Roman Empires, earning the title "Scourge of God."

Battle of the Catalaunian Plains (AD 451)

A major battle in Gaul where a combined Roman and Visigothic army under General Aetius fought Attila's Huns to a stalemate, considered the last great Roman military victory in the West.

Pope Leo I

Bishop of Rome who famously met Attila the Hun at the Mincio River in AD 452 and persuaded him to turn back from invading Rome.

Vandals

A Germanic tribe that crossed into North Africa, established a kingdom, and sacked Rome in AD 455, stripping it more thoroughly than Alaric had.

Flavius Odoacer

A Germanic chieftain who led a mutiny, deposed Romulus Augustulus in AD 476, and sent the imperial regalia to Constantinople, ruling Italy without appointing a new emperor.

Byzantine Empire

The name given by historians to the Eastern Roman Empire after the fall of the West, which continued to thrive for another thousand years with its capital at Constantinople.

The Long Decline: Seeds Planted Before AD 376

Not a Single Event

The fall of the Western Roman Empire was a gradual process spanning over two centuries, not just the sack of Rome in AD 410 or the deposition in AD 476.

Augustine's Question

The philosopher Augustine questioned whether Rome had ever truly lived up to Cicero's ideal of a republic founded on justice, or if Roman virtue was rooted in collective pride.

The Crisis of the Third Century (AD 235–284)

Political Instability

Over fifty emperors in fifty years, most killed by their own soldiers. This led to constant civil war across the provinces.

Frontier Pressure

Germanic tribes exerted continuous pressure on the Rhine and Danube frontiers.

Economic Collapse

Diocletian's Reforms (AD 284–305)

Stabilization

Emperor Diocletian implemented drastic reforms to stabilize the crumbling Empire.

Tetrarchy (Rule of Four)

Divided the Empire into two administrative halves (East and West), each with a senior emperor (Augustus) and a junior emperor (Caesar).

Constantine I (AD 306–337)

Reunification

After civil wars, Constantine I reunited the Empire and ruled alone.

Founding Constantinople

He established a new capital in the East, Constantinople, in AD 330.

Understanding the Term "Barbarian"

Greek Origin

The term *barbaros* (Greek) or *barbarus* (Latin) originally meant someone who didn't speak Greek, whose speech sounded like "bar-bar-bar."

Not Primitive

By Rome's fall, Germanic peoples (Visigoths, Franks, Ostrogoths) were not primitive.

Pressure on the Frontiers: The Visigoths and Huns (1/2)

Structural Weaknesses

The 4th-century Roman Empire, despite its scale, harbored deep weaknesses.

Arrival of the Huns (AD 370s)

Fearsome horse-warriors from Central Asia swept westward, driving Germanic tribes into Roman territory.

Visigothic Plea (AD 376)

Fleeing the Huns, the Visigoths begged Emperor Valens to let them cross the Danube and settle within Roman lands as refugees.

Roman Corruption and Exploitation

Roman administrators failed to provide promised grain and fair treatment. They exploited the Visigoths, selling dog-meat at exorbitant prices and demanding children as slaves.

Visigothic Revolt

The exploitation led to a Visigothic rebellion.

Alaric's Demands

Visigothic leader Alaric, a former Roman officer, repeatedly demanded land, recognition, and a senior Roman command.

Sack of Rome

After repeated refusals and broken promises from the Western government, Alaric invaded Italy and, after two attempts, sacked Rome for three days in AD 410.

Psychological Impact

The psychological shock was immense. Rome had not fallen to an enemy since the Gauls in 390 BC (800 years earlier).

Alaric and the Sack of Rome (AD 410) (2/2)

Alaric's Burial

Alaric died shortly after and was reputedly buried with treasure in the diverted bed of the Busento River, a story that has long fascinated.

Attila the Hun: "The Scourge of God"

Continued Hunnic Threat

The Hunnic threat persisted after the Visigoths settled.

Attila (r. AD 434–453)

United the Hunnic tribes into a formidable military force.

Battle of the Catalaunian Plains (AD 451)

A combined Roman and Visigothic army under General Aetius fought Attila to a standstill in modern France. This was the last great Roman military victory in the West.

Invasion of Italy (AD 452)

Attila invaded Italy, sacking Aquileia so thoroughly that its refugees founded what would become Venice.
