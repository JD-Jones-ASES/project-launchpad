Important Terms (1/4)

Marathon (490 BC)

The plain north of Athens where Athenian and Plataean forces defeated the Persian army of Darius without Spartan reinforcements. The name has been a symbol of courageous resistance ever since.

Thermopylae

The mountain pass where 300 Spartans under King Leonidas held a Persian army for three days, buying time for Greece. They died; Greece survived.

Salamis (480 BC)

The naval battle in which Themistocles lured the Persian fleet into narrow straits and destroyed it. Greece was saved, and Athens emerged as the dominant city-state.

Delian League

Peloponnesian War (431–404 BC)

Philip II of Macedon (382–336 BC)

Father of Alexander. Inventor of the sarissa (18-foot pike) and the Macedonian phalanx. Conquered most of Greece at Chaeronea in 338 BC.

Alexander the Great (356–323 BC)

Son of Philip II; tutored by Aristotle. Conquered Persia, Egypt, Mesopotamia, and the edge of India in thirteen years. Died in Babylon at age 32.

Koine Greek

The "common" dialect of Greek that became the shared language of the eastern Mediterranean after Alexander's conquests. The New Testament and the Septuagint were both written in koine.
