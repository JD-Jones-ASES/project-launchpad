Important Terms (1/4)

Dionysus

The Greek god of wine, fertility, ritual madness, and theatre, in whose honor dramatic festivals were held.

Tragedians

Playwrights who wrote tragedies; the three great tragedians mentioned are Aeschylus, Sophocles, and Euripides.

Aeschylus (c. 525–456 BC)

One of the earliest Greek tragedians, author of the *Oresteia* trilogy, which includes *Agamemnon*.

Agamemnon

The first play in Aeschylus's *Oresteia*, depicting King Agamemnon's return from Troy and his murder by his wife, Clytemnestra.

Clytemnestra

Agamemnon's wife, who murders him as revenge for his sacrifice of their daughter, Iphigenia.

Important Terms (2/4)

Sophocles (c. 497–406 BC)

A highly regarded Greek tragedian, author of *Antigone*.

Antigone

The protagonist of Sophocles' play, who defies King Creon's decree to bury her brother, arguing for the supremacy of divine law.

Creon

The king of Thebes in *Antigone*, who insists on the absolute authority of state law.

Polynices

Antigone's brother, whom King Creon declares a traitor and forbids burial.

Natural Law

The philosophical concept that there are universal moral principles inherent in nature, discoverable by human reason, that are not dependent on human legislation.

Important Terms (3/4)

Socrates (469–399 BC)

An Athenian philosopher, known for his method of questioning (Socratic method) and his belief in the "examined life." He wrote nothing himself.

Plato

Socrates' most famous student, whose dialogues (e.g., *The Apology*) are our primary source for Socrates' teachings and life.

The Apology

Plato's account of Socrates' defense speech during his trial before an Athenian jury.

Examined Life

Socrates' dictum that "the unexamined life is not worth living," emphasizing critical self-reflection and philosophical inquiry.

Logos

A Greek term meaning "word," "reason," or "divine principle," used by Justin Martyr to connect Socrates' philosophical guidance to the divine Word.

Important Terms (4/4)

Arete

A Greek concept of excellence or virtue. The section notes its evolution from Homeric martial prowess to Sophoclean moral integrity and adherence to higher laws.
