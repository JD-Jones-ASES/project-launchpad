Important Terms (1/4)

Mycenaean Civilization

The dominant Bronze Age Greek culture (c. 1600–1100 BC) centered at Mycenae, known for its warrior aristocracy and Linear B writing system.

Bronze Age Collapse

A widespread and rapid societal collapse around 1200 BC that affected most major civilizations in the Eastern Mediterranean, including the Mycenaeans.

Greek Dark Age

The period in Greek history from roughly 1100 to 800 BC, characterized by depopulation, loss of literacy (Linear B), and fragmented communities following the Bronze Age Collapse.

Polis (plural: poleis)

A self-governing Greek city-state, the basic unit of Greek political and social life, consisting of an urban center and its surrounding territory.

Acropolis

A fortified hilltop within a Greek city-state, often serving as a religious center and a refuge.

Important Terms (2/4)

Agora

The open marketplace and public gathering space in an ancient Greek city, central to civic, commercial, and social life.

Helots

The enslaved population of Sparta, who greatly outnumbered Spartan citizens and were tied to the land, necessitating Sparta's permanent military readiness.

Agoge

Sparta's rigorous state-mandated education and military training system for boys, starting at age seven, designed to produce disciplined citizen-soldiers.

Draco

An Athenian lawgiver (621 BC) who authored Athens' first written law code, known for its extreme harshness (hence "draconian").

Solon

An Athenian statesman and lawgiver (594 BC) who instituted significant reforms, including the cancellation of debt slavery and reorganization of society based on wealth, mitigating social conflict.

Important Terms (3/4)

Cleisthenes

An Athenian reformer (508 BC) who further developed democratic institutions, establishing citizen assemblies and the principle of *isonomia*.

Isonomia

The principle of equality before the law, a foundational concept in Athenian democracy.

Olympic Games

Pan-Hellenic athletic and religious festivals held every four years at Olympia in honor of Zeus, where warfare between city-states was suspended.

Thales of Miletus

Considered the first philosopher (c. 585 BC), he sought rational explanations for natural phenomena without recourse to divine intervention, proposing water as the fundamental substance.

Natural Philosophy

The early Greek intellectual tradition of seeking rational, observable, and discoverable principles to explain the workings of the natural world, a precursor to modern science.

Important Terms (4/4)

Kleos

The Greek concept of "glory" or "fame," often achieved through heroic deeds in battle or athletic victory, considered immensely valuable.
