Important Terms (1/5)

Acropolis

The rocky hill at the center of Athens, site of many important religious and civic buildings, including the Parthenon.

Parthenon

The iconic temple dedicated to the goddess Athena, built on the Acropolis between 447–432 BC, known for its marble construction and optical corrections.

Ictinus and Callicrates

The architects responsible for the design and construction of the Parthenon.

Pheidias

The renowned Greek sculptor who created the monumental ivory-and-gold statue of Athena housed inside the Parthenon.

Doric Order

The plainest and sturdiest of the three Greek architectural orders, characterized by simple, unfluted columns and capitals. Used on the exterior of the Parthenon.

Important Terms (2/5)

Ionic Order

A Greek architectural order characterized by slender columns with scroll-like (volute) capitals.

Corinthian Order

The most elaborate of the three Greek architectural orders, featuring capitals decorated with acanthus leaves.

Delian League tribute

Financial contributions demanded by Athens from its allied city-states, which helped fund many of Athens' public works and festivals.

Plato

Influential Greek philosopher (c. 428–348 BC), student of Socrates, founder of the Academy, known for his theory of Forms.

The Academy

The philosophical school founded by Plato in Athens, which operated for nearly a thousand years.

Important Terms (3/5)

Forms (Platonic Forms)

Plato's concept of perfect, eternal, unchanging blueprints or essences that exist in a higher realm, of which physical objects are imperfect copies.

Philosopher-kings

The ideal rulers in Plato's *Republic*, who possess wisdom and have glimpsed the eternal Forms.

Aristotle

Influential Greek philosopher (384–322 BC), student of Plato, founder of the Lyceum, known for his emphasis on observation and practical ethics.

The Lyceum

The philosophical school founded by Aristotle in Athens.

Virtue (Aristotelian)

For Aristotle, virtue is a character trait cultivated through habitual practice and moderate action, not an innate quality or mystical vision.

Important Terms (4/5)

Zeno of Citium

Founder of the Stoic school of philosophy.

The Stoa (Stoicism)

A school of Hellenistic philosophy that taught that virtue is based on knowledge, and that the wise live in harmony with the divine reason (Logos) that governs the universe.

Logos (Stoic)

The divine rational principle or reason that Stoics believed governs the entire universe, of which human reason is a part.

Theater of Dionysus

The great outdoor theater in Athens where plays were performed at public festivals, capable of holding approximately 17,000 spectators.

Tragedy

A genre of Greek drama that explored serious themes, often depicting the downfall of a heroic character due to pride or fate, intended to evoke pity and fear.

Important Terms (5/5)

Comedy

A genre of Greek drama that used humor and satire, often making fun of politicians, social customs, or everyday life.

Agora

The central public space in ancient Greek city-states, serving as a marketplace and a forum for political, social, and religious activity.

"Graecia capta ferum victorem cepit"

A Latin saying by Horace, meaning "Captive Greece captured its fierce conqueror," illustrating Rome's adoption of Greek culture despite military conquest.
