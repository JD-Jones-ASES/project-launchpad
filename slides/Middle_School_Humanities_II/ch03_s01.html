Important Terms (1/2)

Eyewitness

A person who has directly observed an event or incident. In history, their accounts are invaluable but require critical assessment due to inherent biases.

Primary Source

An original document or artifact created at the time of an event. Letters, chronicles, and histories written by participants are primary sources for the Crusades.

Historia Iherosolimitana

("History of the Jerusalem Journey") - Robert the Monk's influential account of Pope Urban II's speech at Clermont and the First Crusade, written around 1107.

Plenary Indulgence

A full indulgence, granting complete remission of all temporal punishment for sins.

Important Terms (2/2)

La Conquête de Constantinople

("The Conquest of Constantinople") - Geoffrey of Villehardouin's eyewitness account of the Fourth Crusade, noteworthy as one of the first extended prose works in the French language.

The Alexiad

Anna Komnene's biography of her father, Byzantine Emperor Alexios I Komnenos, which also details the arrival of the First Crusade from a Byzantine perspective.

Franks

A term commonly used by the Byzantines to refer to all Western Europeans, particularly the Crusaders, often carrying a connotation of foreignness or barbarity.

Introduction: The Problem of the Eyewitness

• The Crusades generated a wealth of vivid eyewitness writings (letters, chronicles, histories).

• However, eyewitnesses don't just report; they interpret events through their personal and cultural "lenses."

• This means two people can describe the same event very differently.

• The section introduces three distinct voices to illustrate this problem: Robert the Monk, Geoffrey of Villehardouin, and Anna Komnene.

• The historian's task is not to trust none, but "all of them, carefully, together."

Robert the Monk and the Call to Crusade

Who he was

A French Benedictine monk, present at Pope Urban II's speech at Clermont in 1095.

His work

Historia Iherosolimitana ("History of the Jerusalem Journey"), written around 1107, becoming a widely read account.

His account of Urban's speech

Not a verbatim transcript, but a literary reconstruction based on memory, hearsay, and his own strong faith. It's polished, powerful, and reflects his belief that the Crusade was God's will.

Geoffrey of Villehardouin and the Fourth Crusade

Who he was

A French nobleman and marshal of Champagne, active participant in the Fourth Crusade from its inception.

His work

La Conquête de Constantinople ("The Conquest of Constantinople"), one of the first extended French prose works and a major eyewitness account.

Anna Komnene and the Byzantine View (1/2)

Who she was

Daughter of Byzantine Emperor Alexios I Komnenos, exceptionally educated in philosophy, mathematics, rhetoric, and medicine.

Her work

The Alexiad, a biography of her father that provides the Byzantine perspective on the First Crusade.

Her perspective

The Crusaders were not heroes but "barbarians"—violent, undisciplined, greedy, and theologically unsophisticated. She saw them as a destabilizing force.

Description of Crusaders

Viewed them as a "second deluge" of men (and families) from the West, a "great ocean of people without order or discipline." Even the noble lords were "alien" in customs, language, and theology.

Critical Reading

Anna had her own agenda (to glorify her father and defend the Byzantine Empire), but her distinct perspective offers crucial information missing from Latin sources.

Reading Across the Three Voices

Robert the Monk

Crusade is God's command, violence is justified, the cause is holy.

Geoffrey of Villehardouin

Fourth Crusade is a glorious military adventure, rich in booty, with moral problems invisible to him.

Anna Komnene

Crusaders are unstable, greedy foreigners whose presence in the East threatened stability rather than defending it.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What was the theological significance of Urban II's offer of remission of sins for Crusaders?

Urban was not offering salvation by works, but rather offering the Crusade as a legitimate form of penance.

It means 'God wills it!' and became the unanimous battle cry of the First Crusade.
