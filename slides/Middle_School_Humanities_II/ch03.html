Important Terms (1/3)

pilgrimage

A journey to a holy place as an act of religious devotion. Jerusalem, Rome, and Santiago de Compostela were the most important medieval destinations.

Seljuk Turks

Central Asian converts to Sunni Islam who built an empire across Persia and Anatolia, defeating Byzantium at Manzikert (1071) and triggering the Crusade movement.

Crusade

A holy war called by the Pope; participants wore a red cross and were promised remission of sins.

People's Crusade

The unofficial, disorganized mass movement led by Peter the Hermit, nearly destroyed in Anatolia before the organized First Crusade set out.

Crusader states

The four Christian principalities established after 1099 — Edessa, Antioch, Tripoli, and Jerusalem — constantly under threat and dependent on Europe.

Important Terms (2/3)

Knights Templar

Warrior-monks (founded c. 1119) defending pilgrims and Crusader territories; later became wealthy bankers; dissolved by France in 1307.

Knights Hospitaller

Military order that began as a hospital order caring for pilgrims; later took up arms; built Krak des Chevaliers.

Saladin

Kurdish general and Sultan of Egypt and Syria (1137–1193) who unified Muslim resistance, defeated the Crusaders at Hattin, and recaptured Jerusalem with remarkable mercy.

Battle of Hattin (1187)

Saladin's decisive ambush of the combined Crusader army — surrounded and cut off from water, nearly all were captured or killed.

Treaty of Jaffa (1192)

End of the Third Crusade: Crusaders kept coastal strip; Jerusalem remained Muslim; Christian pilgrims could visit freely.

Important Terms (3/3)

indulgence

A remission of temporal punishment for sins already forgiven; Urban II offered a plenary (full) indulgence to Crusaders who died in the campaign.
