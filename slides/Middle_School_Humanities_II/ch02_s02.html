Important Terms (1/3)

The Song of Roland (La Chanson de Roland)

The oldest surviving major work of French literature, composed around 1100 AD, detailing a heroic battle from Charlemagne's reign.

Roncevaux

The mountain pass where Charlemagne's rearguard, led by Roland, was ambushed in 778 AD, serving as the historical basis for *The Song of Roland*.

Roland

Charlemagne's greatest knight and commander of the rearguard in *The Song of Roland*, known for his extreme sense of honor and courage.

Ganelon

Roland's stepfather, who betrays Roland to the Muslim king Marsile in *The Song of Roland*.

Marsile

The Muslim king who conspires with Ganelon to ambush Roland's rearguard.

Important Terms (2/3)

Olifant

Roland's ivory horn, which he initially refuses to blow to summon Charlemagne, viewing it as an act of dishonor.

Durendal

Roland's legendary sword, which he attempts to break on a stone before his death to prevent it from falling into enemy hands.

William of Malmesbury (c. 1095–1143)

A prominent medieval English historian of mixed Norman and English descent, known for his objective and complex account of the Norman Conquest.

Chronicle of the Kings of England

William of Malmesbury's comprehensive historical work, written around 1125, covering English history from the Anglo-Saxons to his own time.

Magna Carta (1215)

A landmark English legal document forced upon King John by his barons, which established specific limits on royal power and introduced fundamental principles of justice and law.

Important Terms (3/3)

King John

The English monarch (reigned 1199–1216) whose arbitrary rule, military losses, and taxation policies led to the baronial rebellion and the signing of the Magna Carta.

Runnymede

The meadow where King John met with his rebellious barons and agreed to the terms of the Magna Carta in 1215.

Free Man

Villeins (Serfs)

The majority of the medieval English population, unfree peasants legally bound to their lord's land, who were initially excluded from the protections offered by the Magna Carta.

The Ideals

*The Song of Roland* illuminates the chivalric and Christian ideals of the age, particularly the concept of the saintly warrior.

The Costs

William of Malmesbury's account reveals the human and cultural costs of conquest, depicting a displaced people and a historian's effort to preserve threatened heritage.
