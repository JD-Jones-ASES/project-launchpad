Important Terms (1/4)

Feudalism

The organization of society around the exchange of land for military service, prevalent in medieval Europe.

Fiefs

Large estates of land granted by a king to his great lords.

Vassal

A person who holds land from a lord in exchange for loyalty and military service, sealed by homage and an oath of fealty.

Homage

A ceremony in which a vassal knelt before his lord, placed his hands between the lord's, and swore an oath of fealty.

Oath of Fealty

An oath of faithfulness sworn by a vassal to his lord during the ceremony of homage.

Important Terms (2/4)

Barons / Earls

Great lords who received large land grants (fiefs) directly from the king in exchange for military service.

Knights

Professional warriors who formed the backbone of medieval armies, holding land from barons in exchange for military service.

Villeins / Serfs / Peasants

The vast majority of the population in medieval society, legally bound to the land of a lord's manor, owing labor and dues. They were not slaves but lacked freedom of movement and marriage without ...

Manor

The basic unit of medieval economic life; a lord's estate including his residence, a village, farmland, and essential services.

Manorialism

The system by which manors were organized and operated, centered on the labor and payments owed by peasants to the lord.

Important Terms (3/4)

Three-field system

An agricultural method dividing farmland into three sections, rotating winter crops, summer crops, and fallow land to maintain soil fertility.

Fallow

Agricultural land left unplanted for a season or year to recover fertility, often used for grazing.

Demesne

The lord's own fields on a manor, which villeins were required to work for a specified number of days each week.

Motte-and-bailey

An early type of Norman castle with a wooden tower on an earthen mound (motte) and an enclosed courtyard (bailey), quick to build and crucial for conquest.

Stone Keep

A massive square or rectangular stone tower that became the standard form of castle after motte-and-bailey structures, offering much stronger defense.

Important Terms (4/4)

Domesday Book

A comprehensive survey commissioned by William the Conqueror in 1086, recording land ownership, resources, and value across England before and after the Conquest.

Angevin Empire

The collection of territories, including England and vast parts of France (Normandy, Anjou, Aquitaine, etc.), controlled by Henry II and his successors.

The World the Normans Brought

Beyond a New King

The Norman Conquest by William the Conqueror at Hastings did more than replace the king; it imposed an entire new social and economic system: feudalism.

Feudalism's Core

Society was organized around the exchange of land for military service, a highly structured system developed by the Normans.

Total Transformation

Within a generation, feudalism thoroughly transformed England, affecting land ownership, military obligations, judicial administration, and the daily lives of ordinary people.

The Feudal Pyramid

Hierarchical Structure

Medieval society was visualized as a pyramid, detailing who owed what to whom.

Vassalage and Homage

Interlocking Loyalties

This system created a complex web of duties, theoretically ensuring everyone had a lord above and tenants below (if they held land).

Decentralization

While the king was at the top, a baron's primary loyalty was often to his immediate lord, not always the distant king. This made medieval kingdoms less unified than ancient empires.
