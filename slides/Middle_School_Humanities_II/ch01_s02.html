Important Terms (1/3)

Beowulf (Poem)

The longest surviving epic poem in Old English, depicting heroic deeds in pre-Christian Scandinavia.

Beowulf (Character)

The Geat warrior and later king, protagonist of the poem, known for his strength, courage, and loyalty.

Old English

The language in which Beowulf and early entries of the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle were written, spoken in Anglo-Saxon England.

Heorot

The great mead-hall of the Danish King Hrothgar, central to the initial conflicts in Beowulf.

Grendel

The monstrous descendant of Cain who terrorizes Heorot in Beowulf.

Important Terms (2/3)

Grendel's Mother

The second monster Beowulf defeats, seeking revenge for her son.

Geatland

Beowulf's homeland, a region in Scandinavia (part of modern Sweden).

Anglo-Saxon Chronicle

A collection of historical records, begun during Alfred's reign, providing year-by-year accounts of events in Anglo-Saxon England, often terse and factual.

Asser

A Welsh monk and scholar, King Alfred's biographer, author of The Life of King Alfred.

Life of King Alfred

A near-contemporary biography of King Alfred, offering a personal and admiring portrait of the king.

Important Terms (3/3)

Viking Age

The period from the late 8th to the mid-11th century, characterized by Viking raids and settlements across Europe.

Pagan Setting/Themes

Refers to the pre-Christian world and values depicted in Beowulf.

Christian Frame/Themes

The Christian perspective and references woven into Beowulf by its monastic scribe, and the explicit Christian values in Asser's biography.

Heroic Virtue

The traditional values celebrated in epic poetry, such as courage, loyalty to one's lord and people, generosity, and strength in battle.

Oral Traditions

Stories, poems, and histories passed down verbally through generations before being written down, likely influencing Beowulf.

Introduction: Reading across the Viking Age

Three Distinct Voices

This unit examines three texts (Beowulf, Anglo-Saxon Chronicle, Asser's Life of King Alfred) from the same historical period but with very different styles and purposes.

Internal Perspective

These texts offer a unique opportunity to understand the Viking Age from the viewpoint of those who lived through it, rather than through later interpretations.

Beowulf (1/2)

Oldest Surviving Old English Poem

Over 3,000 lines, likely composed between the 8th and 10th centuries.

Oral Traditions and Christian Scribe

Written down by a Christian monk, drawing on much older Germanic oral traditions.

Pre-Christian Scandinavian Setting

The poem is set among Germanic tribes in Denmark and Geatland (modern Sweden) before the arrival of Christianity.

The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle

Origin and Purpose

Begun under King Alfred's command, continued by monks until the mid-12th century. It is a vital historical document for understanding English history.

Style: Terse and Factual

Entries are typically brief, recording raids, battles, deaths, and treaties in a year-by-year format (e.g., "In this year the Danes came...").

Occasional Detailed Accounts

While generally concise, some entries open up to reveal the human impact and dramatic reality of events.

Asser's Life of King Alfred (1/2)

Author and Context

Written by Asser, a Welsh monk recruited by Alfred around 885, completed around 893 while Alfred was still alive.

Significance

One of the few near-contemporary biographies of a medieval ruler, offering a rare, personal glimpse into Alfred's character.

Admiring and Personal Tone

Asser clearly admired Alfred, resulting in a warm, detailed, and positive portrayal of the king.

What is Beowulf notable for in English literature?

It is the longest surviving poem in Old English, running to over 3,000 lines.

Who is believed to have written down Beowulf, and what older traditions did it draw upon?

A Christian monk wrote it down, drawing on older oral Germanic traditions.

Where is the poem Beowulf set, and what kind of world does it depict?

It is set in Scandinavia (Denmark and Geatland, part of modern Sweden) among the pre-Christian Germanic tribes.

Summarize Beowulf's three major battles in the poem.

Beowulf kills the monster Grendel, then Grendel's mother, and finally, as an old king, fights and kills a dragon, dying of his wounds.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What are some of the older Germanic virtues celebrated in Beowulf?

Courage, loyalty, generosity, and the willingness to fight for one's people.

How does Beowulf defeat Grendel, and why does he choose this method?

Beowulf wrestles Grendel bare-handed, tearing off his arm, because Grendel uses no weapons.

What does the ending of Beowulf with the hero's funeral pyre and lament signify?

It serves as a meditation on loyalty, the passing of great men, and the uncertainty of the future.

When was The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle begun, and how long was it updated?

It was begun during Alfred's reign (probably at his command) and continued to be updated by monks until the mid-twelfth century.

Describe the typical style of entries in The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle.

The entries are generally terse, factual, and year-by-year, recording events like raids, battles, and treaties.
