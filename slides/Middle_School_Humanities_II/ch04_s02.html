A World Building Toward Heaven: The High Middle Ages (1150–1350)

Monumental Architecture

The building of soaring cathedrals like Notre-Dame de Paris, showcasing unprecedented engineering feats like the flying buttress.

Intellectual Advancement

The establishment of universities where reason pursued divine truth, and the development of complex philosophical systems.

Economic Expansion

The growth of extensive trade networks.

Sudden End

This period of flourishing was abruptly curtailed in 1347 with the arrival of the Black Death.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What overarching characteristic defined the literature of the High Middle Ages, as described in the introduction?

It was ambitious, attempting to map moral/spiritual universes, answer profound questions, or describe vast, new worlds.

Who are four prominent literary figures from the High Middle Ages discussed in this section?

Dante Alighieri, Thomas Aquinas, Marco Polo, and Francis of Assisi.

How did the literary approaches of Dante, Aquinas, Marco Polo, and Francis of Assisi differ despite living in the same era?

Who was Dante Alighieri, and what personal event profoundly influenced the writing of The Divine Comedy?

He was a Florentine poet, philosopher, and politician, banished from Florence in 1301. His exile deeply colored his poem with longing and fury.

What is Dante Alighieri's most famous work, and what are its three main sections?

The Divine Comedy, structured as a journey through Hell (Inferno), Purgatory (Purgatorio), and Heaven (Paradiso).

What was Dante's revolutionary linguistic choice in writing The Divine Comedy, and what was its significance?

Key Concepts (2/2)

In The Divine Comedy, who serves as Dante's initial guide through Hell and Purgatory?

The Roman poet Virgil.

What does Virgil symbolize in The Divine Comedy, and why can he not lead Dante into Heaven?

Virgil symbolizes classical reason. He cannot enter Heaven because he lived before Christ, signifying that reason can guide through sin and repentance, but f...

Where does Dante place virtuous non-Christians, such as Virgil and Homer, in the afterlife?

In Limbo, the first circle of Hell, a place for virtuous pagans who lived before Christ and cannot be saved.

What famous warning is inscribed above the Gate of Hell in Dante's Inferno, Canto III?

“All hope abandon, ye who enter in!”

Explain the fundamental principle behind the organization of punishments in Dante's Hell.

The punishments strictly fit the crimes; they are logical consequences of the sinner's own choices, extended into eternity.

How does Dante define sin, which informs the structure of his Hell?

Dante believed sin is a disorder of the soul, a turning away from God toward a lesser good.
