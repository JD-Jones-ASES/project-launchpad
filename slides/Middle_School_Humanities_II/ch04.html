Important Terms (1/3)

flying buttress

An arched stone support transferring vault thrust to an outer pier, freeing the wall for windows.

Gothic architecture

The style of the great medieval cathedrals — pointed arch, ribbed vault, flying buttress — allowing extraordinary height and large windows.

university

From Latin universitas; a corporate body of scholars chartered by pope or king. First appeared late 12th and early 13th centuries.

trivium

Grammar, rhetoric, and logic — the three language arts forming the first stage of medieval university education.

quadrivium

Arithmetic, geometry, music (as mathematical proportion), and astronomy — the four mathematical arts of medieval education.

Important Terms (2/3)

scholasticism

The medieval intellectual method of systematic argumentation using philosophical reasoning (especially Aristotelian logic) to explore theology.

Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274)

Dominican friar, greatest scholastic theologian; author of the Summa Theologica; demonstrated that faith and reason are partners.

Summa Theologica

Aquinas's masterwork — a systematic treatment of virtually every question of Christian theology, in millions of words.

Five Ways

Aquinas's five rational arguments for God's existence from observable natural phenomena.

friar

A member of a mendicant religious order (Franciscans, Dominicans) living by charity and preaching in the world.

Important Terms (3/3)

Francis of Assisi (c. 1181–1226)

Founder of the Franciscan Order; preached joyful poverty and engagement with the world; wrote the Canticle of the Sun.

Mongol Empire

Founded by Genghis Khan (1206); the largest contiguous land empire in history; paradoxically destructive and trade-enabling.

Pax Mongolica

The period of relative Eurasian stability under unified Mongol control during the later 13th century.

Divine Comedy

Dante Alighieri's masterpiece (c. 1308–1321) in Italian; journey through Hell, Purgatory, and Heaven guided by Virgil and Beatrice.

Connections

• Aquinas used Aristotle, recovered via Arabic translations, which came to Europe partly through Crusade contact (Chapter 3).

• Roger Bacon as a Franciscan friar combines Chapter 4's two themes: the Franciscan spirituality of engaged learning and the scholastic tradition's willingness to investigate the natural world.

• The Black Death (Chapter 5) will end the High Middle Ages abruptly; the civilization of cathedrals and universities is about to be shattered.

• Dante's choice to write in Italian anticipates the Reformers' vernacular Bible translations (Chapter 7) and connects to Petrarch's Renaissance humanism (Chapter 6).

• The Mongol Pax Mongolica that enabled Marco Polo also set up the trade routes whose disruption after the Ottoman conquest (Chapter 5) drove the Age of Exploration (Chapter 6).
