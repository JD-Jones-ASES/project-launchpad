Filippo Brunelleschi's Breakthrough (c. 1420)

Mathematical Rules of Linear Perspective

Dissemination of Knowledge

Leon Battista Alberti formalized these rules in his treatise *On Painting* (1435), making them accessible to a wider artistic community.

Impact

Within two generations, linear perspective transformed European painting, sculpture, and architecture, turning flat surfaces into windows onto convincingly three-dimensional scenes.

The Printing Press: Movable Type Changes Everything

Gutenberg's Invention (c. 1440)

Johannes Gutenberg developed the printing press with movable type in Mainz, Germany, which became the most transformative invention of the Renaissance.

Before Gutenberg: The Scriptorium

Gutenberg's Revolutionary Impact

Rapid Expansion

By 1500, only 45 years after Gutenberg's Bible, there were presses in over 200 European cities, and an estimated 8 million books had been printed, transforming the literate world.

The Printing Press as a Weapon of Ideas

It played a crucial role in the Reformation. Martin Luther's 95 Theses spread across Germany within two weeks of publication in 1517, a scale of dissemination impossible before the press.

The Renaissance Man: Leonardo da Vinci (1/2)

Uomo Universale (Universal Man)

The Renaissance ideal of a person skilled in all arts and sciences, articulated by Leon Battista Alberti.

Leonardo da Vinci (1452–1519)

Epitomized the *uomo universale*. He was a renowned painter (*Mona Lisa*, *The Last Supper*), sculptor, architect, engineer, musician, and scientist.

Scientific and Artistic Foresight

His notebooks contained designs for inventions anticipating the helicopter, tank, parachute, and solar energy concentrator by centuries.

Unifying Principle

Leonardo's diverse interests were unified by his conviction that both artists and scientists were engaged in "learning to see accurately."

Anatomy and Art

His anatomical drawings (from dissecting corpses) provided unmatched knowledge of the human body, enhancing his realism.
