Important Terms (1/2)

Renaissance

From the French for "rebirth," this term denotes the period (roughly 1350-1600) when European thinkers consciously revived ancient Greek and Roman literature, art, and philosophy.

Media Aetas ("Middle Age")

A term coined by Renaissance thinkers to describe the centuries between the fall of Rome and their own time, implying a historical lull or period of dormancy.

Dark Ages

Petrarch's term for the post-Roman centuries, viewed as a time when learning was "buried." Modern historians generally consider this term misleading due to significant medieval achievements.

City-state

An independent political unit consisting of a city and its surrounding controlled territory, common in Renaissance Italy and resembling the polis system of ancient Greece.

Important Terms (2/2)

Studia Humanitatis

The curriculum of humanist studies, including grammar, rhetoric, poetry, history, and moral philosophy, believed to be essential for a good and virtuous life.

Patron

A wealthy individual or family (like the Medici) who financially supports artists, scholars, or writers, commissioning projects and fostering cultural development.

Linear Perspective

A mathematical system, rediscovered by Brunelleschi and codified by Alberti, for accurately representing three-dimensional space and depth on a two-dimensional surface in art.

What Is the Renaissance?

Meaning of "Renaissance"

Coined by later historians, meaning "rebirth" in French. It refers to a period (c. 1350-1600) of conscious revival of ancient Greek and Roman literature, art, and philosophy.

Origin of the Idea

Around 1350 in northern Italy, scholars and artists began looking back to classical antiquity, seeking forgotten knowledge and viewing their own time as a "waking up" from historical slumber.

"Middle Age" and "Dark Ages"

Renaissance thinkers called the preceding centuries the media aetas ("middle age") or, as Petrarch did, the "Dark Ages," implying a decline in learning.

The Italian City-States

Why Italy First?

Italy was not a unified country but a collection of rich, independent city-states (Florence, Venice, Milan, Naples, Rome) that served as the initial centers of the Renaissance.

Wealth from Trade and Banking

These cities prospered from Mediterranean trade and sophisticated banking systems, creating a wealthy merchant class with disposable income to invest in art and scholarship.

Humanism: The Revival of Classical Learning

Definition

The core intellectual movement of the Renaissance, focused on the scholarly study of ancient Greek and Roman texts in grammar, rhetoric, poetry, history, and moral philosophy (studia humanitatis).

Goals of Humanism

Humanists believed that studying these classical texts in their original languages would cultivate more eloquent, virtuous, and civically engaged individuals.

Shift from Medieval Art

Medieval painting featured flat, symbolic forms, often with gold backgrounds and hierarchical sizing, lacking realistic three-dimensional space.

The most dramatic transformation was the development of linear perspective, a mathematical system for creating the illusion of depth on a flat surface.
