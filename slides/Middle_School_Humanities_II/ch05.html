Important Terms (1/3)

Black Death

The epidemic of bubonic plague (Yersinia pestis) that swept Europe 1347–1353, killing 30–50% of the population.

Yersinia pestis

The bacterium that caused the Black Death, living in fleas on rats and other rodents.

serfdom

The condition of peasants legally bound to a lord's land; it largely collapsed in Western Europe after the Black Death due to labor shortages.

flagellants

Religious groups who publicly whipped themselves during the Black Death, believing self-punishment would end the plague.

Hundred Years' War

The series of conflicts between England and France (1337–1453) over the English claim to the French throne.

Important Terms (2/3)

longbow

The English and Welsh war bow; 10–12 arrows per minute, penetrating armor at 300 yards. Decisive at Crécy, Poitiers, and Agincourt.

Joan of Arc (c. 1412–1431)

French peasant girl who claimed divine visions, lifted the siege of Orléans, and was burned as a heretic; later canonized as a saint.

Avignon Papacy (1309–1377)

The period when popes resided in Avignon under French influence; called the 'Babylonian Captivity of the Church.'

Great Western Schism (1378–1417)

The division in which two then three rival claimants each claimed to be pope; resolved by the Council of Constance.

conciliarism

The doctrine that a general council of the Church has authority over even the pope.

Important Terms (3/3)

John Wycliffe (c. 1330–1384)

Oxford theologian who argued for Scripture alone, attacked transubstantiation, began English Bible translation. Followers called Lollards.

Lollards

Wycliffe's followers who advocated Church reform, Scripture supremacy, and vernacular Bible access.

Jan Hus (c. 1369–1415)

Czech theologian influenced by Wycliffe; burned at the Council of Constance; sparked the Hussite Wars.

Ottoman Empire

Turkish Muslim empire that under Mehmed II captured Constantinople in 1453, ending the Byzantine Empire.
