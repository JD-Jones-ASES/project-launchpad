Humanism

Prince

In Machiavelli's context, the ruler or leader of a state, particularly a principality.

Utopia

An imagined community or society that possesses highly desirable or nearly perfect qualities for its citizens. The word, coined by Thomas More, means "no place" or "good place" in Greek.

A literary technique used to expose and criticize foolishness and corruption of an individual or a society by using humor, irony, exaggeration, or ridicule.

Irony

A literary device in which a statement, situation, or event seems to mean one thing but actually means the opposite.

Important Terms (2/2)

Satirical Persona

A fictional character or voice adopted by an author to deliver satirical commentary, often to provide a layer of distance between the author and potentially controversial criticisms.

Sonnets

A poem of fourteen lines using any of a number of formal rhyme schemes, typically having ten syllables per line. Petrarch popularized the sonnet form.

Canzoniere

Petrarch's collection of 366 poems, mostly sonnets, written in Italian, primarily addressed to a woman named Laura.

Introduction: Writing in a New Key

Engagement with Antiquity

They meticulously studied the works of ancient Greek and Roman authors, drawing inspiration and knowledge.

Focus on Human Nature and Politics

Their writings often explored the complexities of human behavior, morality, and the practicalities of political leadership and societal structure.

Challenge to Received Wisdom

They were willing to question and critique the established ideas and institutions of their time, sometimes subtly and sometimes with direct force.

Name four prominent writers of the Renaissance mentioned in this unit.

Niccolò Machiavelli, Erasmus, Thomas More, and Francesco Petrarch.

Who wrote 'The Prince' and when?

Niccolò Machiavelli, in 1513.

What was the main purpose of Machiavelli's 'The Prince'?

To provide an unsparing analysis of how princes gain and keep power, focusing on the 'real truth' of politics rather than ideal states.

According to Machiavelli, is it better for a prince to be loved or feared?

It is much safer to be feared than loved, because men are generally ungrateful, fickle, false, and covetous, making friendships based on love unreliable.

What animal metaphors did Machiavelli use to describe how a prince should act, and what did each represent?

A prince should be a fox (to discover snares) and a lion (to terrify wolves).
