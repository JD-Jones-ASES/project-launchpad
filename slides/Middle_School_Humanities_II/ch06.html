Important Terms (1/3)

Renaissance

From French, 'rebirth.' Roughly 1350–1600; the revival of ancient Greek and Roman learning, art, and philosophy.

city-state

An independent self-governing state consisting of a city and surrounding territory. Florence, Venice, Milan, Naples, and Rome were the major Renaissance city-states.

humanism

The Renaissance scholarly programme centered on studying ancient Greek and Roman texts; studia humanitatis. Most humanists were devout Christians.

patron

A wealthy individual who supports artists and scholars by commissioning work and providing resources. Patronage was the economic engine of Renaissance art.

Medici

The Florentine banking family who became the greatest patrons of the Renaissance. Key figures: Cosimo de' Medici and Lorenzo the Magnificent.

Important Terms (2/3)

linear perspective

A mathematical system of representing three-dimensional space through converging parallel lines meeting at a vanishing point. Developed by Brunelleschi c. 1420; codified by Alberti (1435).

movable type

Gutenberg's system of individually cast metal letters arranged into words, locked into a frame, and pressed onto paper — making printing vastly faster than hand-copying.

caravel

A small, maneuverable Portuguese sailing ship with lateen sails that could sail into the wind; the primary vessel of the Age of Exploration.

Machiavellian

Calculating and willing to use deception or ruthlessness to achieve political ends; derived from Machiavelli's The Prince.

Christian humanism

The Renaissance movement combining humanist scholarship with Christian devotion to reform the Church from within; exemplified by Erasmus and More.

Important Terms (3/3)

Utopia

Thomas More's 1516 work describing an imaginary island society with common property and religious toleration; from Greek for both 'good place' and 'no place.'

Connections (1/2)

• The Renaissance began when Greek scholars fled Constantinople after its fall (Chapter 5) with manuscripts — directly connecting the Late Medieval Crisis to the Renaissance.

• Petrarch's dismissal of the 'Dark Ages' is implicitly corrected by Chapters 1–4, which show the real achievements of the period he dismissed.

• Erasmus's Greek New Testament showing that the Latin Vulgate contained errors directly created the textual credibility crisis that Luther exploited (Chapter 7).

• The printing press that spread Luther's theses (Chapter 7) was described in this chapter as the 'engine of the Reformation.'

• Thomas More's execution for refusing the Oath of Supremacy connects directly to Henry VIII and the English Reformation (Chapter 7).

• Machiavelli's analysis of power as separate from moral authority anticipates the secular political thought that would develop further after the Reformation.
