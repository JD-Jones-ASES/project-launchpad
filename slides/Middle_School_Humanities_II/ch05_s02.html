Important Terms (1/3)

Black Death

A pandemic of bubonic plague that swept through Europe from 1347 to 1353, killing an estimated 30-40% of the population and recurring in subsequent waves.

Labor Scarcity

A shortage of available workers relative to the amount of work needed. After the Black Death, labor became scarce, giving surviving peasants unprecedented economic bargaining power.

Statute of Laborers (1351)

An English law passed in response to labor scarcity, attempting to fix wages at pre-plague levels and prevent peasants from leaving their villages. It largely failed.

Peasants' Revolt (1381)

A major uprising in England, sparked by attempts to suppress wages and implement a new poll tax. Led by Wat Tyler and John Ball, it challenged social hierarchies, though it was ultimately suppressed.

Wat Tyler

A leader of the English Peasants' Revolt of 1381, who marched on London with peasant forces.

Important Terms (2/3)

John Ball

A radical priest associated with the Peasants' Revolt, famous for his sermon questioning social inequality ("When Adam delved and Eve span, who was then the gentleman?").

Hundred Years' War (1337-1453)

A series of conflicts between England and France, which served as a proving ground for new military technologies and tactics.

Longbow

A powerful English bow, capable of piercing plate armor at range, which proved devastatingly effective against French knights in battles like Crécy, Poitiers, and Agincourt.

Plate Armor

Full body armor made of shaped metal plates, developed in the 14th and 15th centuries for increased protection. While effective against some weapons, it was vulnerable to longbows and gunpowder wea...

Gunpowder

An explosive mixture invented in China, introduced to Europe via Mongol trade routes. Its use in cannons and later firearms revolutionized warfare, making castles and armored knights obsolete.

Important Terms (3/3)

Bombards

Early, large cannons used in the 14th century, demonstrating the initial impact of gunpowder weapons on siege warfare.

Fall of Constantinople (1453)

The capture of the Byzantine capital by the Ottoman Turks, marking the end of the Eastern Roman Empire and a pivotal moment in European history.

Silk Road

An ancient network of trade routes connecting East and West, disrupted by the Ottoman control of the eastern Mediterranean after 1453.

Age of Exploration

A period from the 15th to 17th centuries when European ships traveled the world, exploring and mapping new trade routes, often motivated by the search for alternatives to the disrupted Silk Road.

Counting the Dead: What the Numbers Mean

Pre-Plague Europe

Europe's population peaked at roughly 75 million before the Black Death (1347). This growth had strained resources, leaving many peasants on marginal land and weakened by famine.

Demographic Collapse

By 1400, the population had plummeted to 45-50 million, a loss of 30-40%. The impact was immense, with villages losing half their inhabitants, farms abandoned, and entire settlements disappearing.

The Peasants' Revolt (1381)

Causes

The attempt to suppress wages and the imposition of a new poll tax fueled widespread discontent, culminating in the Peasants' Revolt in England.

Leaders and Actions

Led by Wat Tyler and the radical priest John Ball, peasants from Essex and Kent marched on London. They burned property, opened the Tower of London, and executed high-ranking officials.

John Ball's Message

Ball's sermon, "When Adam delved and Eve span, who was then the gentleman?", articulated a powerful challenge to social hierarchy, arguing that inequality was a human creation, not divine.
