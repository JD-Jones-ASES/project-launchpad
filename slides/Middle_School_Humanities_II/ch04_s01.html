Important Terms (1/3)

Dante Alighieri

Florentine poet (1265-1321), author of The Divine Comedy.

The Divine Comedy

Dante's epic poem describing a journey through Hell (Inferno), Purgatory (Purgatorio), and Heaven (Paradiso).

Tuscan Dialect

The everyday language of central Italy that Dante chose for The Divine Comedy, which became the basis for standard Italian.

Virgil

The ancient Roman poet who guides Dante through Hell and Purgatory in The Divine Comedy, representing classical reason.

Limbo

A region of Hell in Dante's Inferno where virtuous pagans, like Virgil and Homer, who lived before Christ and could not be saved, reside without torment but also without hope of Heaven.

Important Terms (2/3)

Thomas Aquinas

Dominican friar, philosopher, and theologian (1225-1274), author of Summa Theologica.

Summa Theologica

Aquinas's comprehensive theological treatise that systematically addresses major philosophical and theological questions using logical argumentation.

The Five Ways (quinque viae)

Aquinas's five logical arguments for the existence of God, derived from observable facts about the natural world (motion, causation, contingency, etc.).

Unmoved Mover

A concept from Aristotle, adopted by Aquinas in the First Way, positing a first cause of motion that is itself unmoved, which Aquinas identifies with God.

Marco Polo

Venetian merchant and traveler (1254-1324), author of The Travels of Marco Polo.

Important Terms (3/3)

The Travels of Marco Polo

A detailed account of Polo's journey and experiences in the Mongol Empire and across Asia, dictated in 1298.

Kublai Khan

The Emperor of the Mongol Empire and founder of the Yuan dynasty in China, whom Marco Polo served for many years.

Khanbaliq

The capital city of Kublai Khan's empire (modern-day Beijing), vividly described by Marco Polo.

Francis of Assisi

Italian Catholic friar and mystic (1181/1182-1226), known for his love of nature and God's creation, and his "hymn to creation."
