Important Terms (1/3)

Pilgrimage

A journey made to a holy place as an act of religious devotion, a crucial practice in the Middle Ages.

Seljuk Turks

A Central Asian Turkic people who converted to Sunni Islam, built a powerful empire, and whose conquests were a primary cause of the First Crusade.

Crusade

A holy war called by the Pope, in which participants wore a red cross and were promised remission of their sins if they died.

Knights Templar

The Poor Fellow-Soldiers of Christ and of the Temple of Solomon; a major military order of warrior-monks founded to defend pilgrims and territories, later becoming wealthy bankers.

Saladin

Salah ad-Din Yusuf ibn Ayyub; a Kurdish general and Sultan who unified Muslim forces, defeated the Crusaders at Hattin, and recaptured Jerusalem.

Important Terms (2/3)

Church of the Holy Sepulchre

The church in Jerusalem built over the sites traditionally believed to be where Jesus was crucified, buried, and resurrected.

Battle of Manzikert (1071)

A decisive battle where the Seljuk Turks defeated the Byzantine army, opening Anatolia to Turkish expansion and prompting the Byzantine Emperor's appeal for aid from the West.

Pope Urban II

The Pope who famously called for the First Crusade at the Council of Clermont in 1095.

Council of Clermont

The ecclesiastical council in France where Pope Urban II delivered his influential speech calling for the First Crusade.

"Deus lo volt!"

Latin for "God wills it!", the enthusiastic response and rallying cry of the First Crusade participants.

Important Terms (3/3)

Crusader States

The four Latin Christian principalities (County of Edessa, Principality of Antioch, County of Tripoli, and Kingdom of Jerusalem) established in the Holy Land following the First Crusade.

Knights Hospitaller

A military order that originated as a hospital caring for sick pilgrims in Jerusalem, later taking up arms for defense.

Bernard of Clairvaux

A highly influential Cistercian monk and orator who passionately preached and inspired the Second Crusade.

Battle of Hattin (1187)

A catastrophic military defeat for the Crusader states at the hands of Saladin, leading to the loss of Jerusalem and most Crusader territories.

Richard I (the Lionheart)

King of England, a prominent and skilled military leader of the Third Crusade, known for his campaigns against Saladin.

The Holy Land Before the Crusades

Jerusalem's Centrality

Jerusalem held immense religious significance for medieval Christians as the place of Jesus's crucifixion, burial, and resurrection, marked by the Church of the Holy Sepulchre.

Pilgrimage Tradition

For centuries, Christian pilgrimage to Jerusalem and other holy sites (Rome, Santiago de Compostela) was a deeply important act of religious devotion.

Muslim Rule

Since 638 CE, Jerusalem had been under Arab Muslim rule. Generally, early Arab rulers tolerated Christian pilgrims and access to holy sites, as Christianity, Judaism, and Islam all revered the city.

The Seljuk Turk Threat and Byzantine Appeal

Shifting Dynamics

This period of relative coexistence changed in the late eleventh century with the rise of the Seljuk Turks.

Seljuk Expansion

Having converted to Sunni Islam, the Seljuks built a powerful empire stretching from Persia into Anatolia (modern Turkey).

Battle of Manzikert (1071)

The Seljuks decisively defeated the Byzantine army, leading to their rapid conquest of much of the Byzantine heartland.

Byzantine Appeal

By the 1090s, the Seljuks were dangerously close to Constantinople. Byzantine Emperor Alexios I Komnenos sent an urgent appeal to Pope Urban II in Rome for military assistance.

The Call: Urban II at Clermont (1095)

The Council of Clermont

In November 1095, Pope Urban II convened a major council in Clermont, France.

Promise of Remission of Sins

Urban offered that those who died on Crusade would receive remission of their sins. This was understood as a form of penance, not salvation by works, and was a powerful motivator for many.

"Deus lo volt!"

The crowd's enthusiastic, unanimous response—"God wills it!"—became the battle cry of the First Crusade.

The Military Orders: Knights Templar and Knights Hospitaller

Need for Defenders

The Crusader States required permanent military forces to survive in a hostile environment.

Warrior-Monks

This need led to the creation of military orders, which combined monastic vows (poverty, chastity, obedience) with military service.

Knights Templar

Knights Hospitaller

Originally a hospital order caring for sick pilgrims in Jerusalem, they later took up arms.

Fortifications

Both orders were instrumental in building impressive stone castles across the Holy Land, such as the formidable Krak des Chevaliers.

Bernard of Clairvaux and the Second Crusade

Fall of Edessa (1144)

The northernmost Crusader state, the County of Edessa, fell to the Muslim lord Zengi, sparking shock in Europe.

Preaching of Bernard of Clairvaux

The Second Crusade (1147–1149) was powerfully preached by Bernard of Clairvaux, a highly influential Cistercian monk.

Disastrous Outcome

The Second Crusade was a complete failure. The German and French armies failed to coordinate, attacked the wrong targets, and achieved nothing. Bernard attributed its failure to the Crusaders' sins.

Name two significant mathematical contributions that entered Europe from the Islamic world.

Arabic numerals (which we still use today) and algebra (from the Arabic 'al-jabr').

Who was Ibn Sina (Avicenna) and what was his major contribution to medicine?

Ibn Sina was an Islamic physician (c. 980–1037) who wrote a medical encyclopedia that remained a standard text in European universities for centuries.

What astronomical instrument, developed and refined by Arab scientists, was adopted in Europe and used to determine latitude and tell time from stars?

The astrolabe.
