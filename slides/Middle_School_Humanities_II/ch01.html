Important Terms (1/3)

Viking

From Old Norse vikingr, likely meaning 'sea raider.' Scandinavian peoples (Norse, Danes, Swedes) who raided, traded, and settled across Europe from the late eighth century onward.

Great Heathen Army

The large Danish force that invaded England in 865 and conquered Northumbria, East Anglia, and Mercia over the next fourteen years.

fyrd

The Anglo-Saxon militia of free men who served when summoned by the king, representing ordinary freemen rather than professional warriors.

Battle of Edington (878)

Alfred's decisive defeat of the Danish king Guthrum, which reversed the Viking conquest of Wessex.

Treaty of Wedmore (878)

The agreement by which Guthrum converted to Christianity (with Alfred as godfather) and withdrew north and east of the Danelaw boundary.

Important Terms (2/3)

Danelaw

The area of northern and eastern England under Danish law and custom, bounded roughly from London to Chester. Danish place names still mark this region today.

burh

A fortified town built or strengthened by Alfred as part of a defensive network. The word survives in English place names ending in -bury or -borough.

Asser

Welsh monk and bishop (died c. 909) who served Alfred's court and wrote the Life of King Alfred, our most important near-contemporary biography of any medieval English king.

Anglo-Saxon Chronicle

The year-by-year record of English history begun under Alfred, written in Old English, continued until the mid-twelfth century.

Aethelstan

Alfred's grandson who in 927 unified all of England by defeating the last Scandinavian king of York.

Important Terms (3/3)

Cnut

Danish king who ruled England 1016–1035, also king of Denmark and Norway; maintained Alfred's laws and supported the Church.

Beowulf

The longest surviving Old English poem, written by a Christian monk drawing on older Germanic oral traditions; the hero fights the monster Grendel, Grendel's mother, and a dragon.
