Important Terms (1/2)

Primary Sources

Original documents or artifacts from a historical period, like letters, speeches, books, or laws. Crucial for understanding the Reformation directly from the reformers' perspectives.

Theses

A statement or theory put forward as a premise to be maintained or proved. Luther's Ninety-Five Theses were propositions for academic debate.

Indulgences

In the Catholic Church, a remission of the temporal punishment due to sins already forgiven. In the 16th century, the sale of indulgences for money was widely criticized.

Repentance

A sincere turning away from sin and toward God; a change of mind and heart. Luther argued the entire Christian life should be one of repentance, not just a one-time act.

The Three Walls

Luther's term for the barriers the Roman Church erected to protect itself from reform: (1) spiritual power over temporal, (2) Pope as sole interpreter of Scripture, (3) Pope alone can call a council.

Important Terms (2/2)

Predestination (Election)

The doctrine, particularly associated with John Calvin, that God has eternally determined whom He will save (elect) and whom He will condemn to destruction. It emphasizes God's sovereign grace in s...

Sola Gratia

Latin for "by grace alone," a core Reformation doctrine asserting that salvation is entirely a gift of God's grace and not earned by human merit or works.

Liturgy

A fixed form of public worship used in a church, often including specific prayers, readings, and rituals.

Collect

A short, general prayer used in Christian worship, often specific to a particular day or occasion. Cranmer's Book of Common Prayer contains many famous collects.

General Confession

A prayer in a liturgical service where the entire congregation confesses its sins to God.

Context

In October 1517, Martin Luther, a German monk and professor, wrote ninety-five arguments (theses) to challenge the theological foundation of selling indulgences.

Initial Audience

He wrote them in Latin, intending to spark an academic debate among scholars and the Archbishop of Mainz.

Wider Impact

The printing press ensured the theses reached a much broader audience, spreading rapidly throughout Germany and beyond.
