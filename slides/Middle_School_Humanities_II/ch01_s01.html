Important Terms (1/3)

Shallow Draft

The minimal depth of water a ship requires to float without touching the bottom. Viking longships had an extremely shallow draft, enabling river navigation and beach landings.

Fjord

A long, narrow, deep inlet of the sea between high, steep cliffs, common in Norway, which fostered seafaring.

Portage

The act of carrying boats or goods overland between two navigable bodies of water. The lightweight longship could be portaged across narrow land bridges.

Vinland

The name given by the Norse to a part of North America, where Leif Eriksson established a short-lived settlement (L'Anse aux Meadows, Newfoundland).

Varangians

Scandinavian (primarily Swedish) traders and mercenaries who traveled along the river routes of Eastern Europe, establishing trade networks and settlements in modern Russia, Ukraine, and Belarus.

Important Terms (2/3)

Varangian Guard

An elite corps of Norse (and later Anglo-Saxon) mercenaries who served as the personal bodyguards of the Byzantine Emperor in Constantinople.

Constantinople

The capital of the Byzantine Empire, a major destination for the Varangians via the Black Sea.

Normandy

A region in northern France settled by Norse (Northmen) Vikings, from which William the Conqueror later launched his invasion of England in 1066.

Danelaw

The historical region in England, primarily in the north and east, where Danish law, customs, and culture predominated following the Viking invasions and subsequent peace treaties, notably the Trea...

Shire

An administrative division of Anglo-Saxon England, roughly equivalent to a county, administered by a royal officer (ealdorman/earl). Many modern English counties retain their shire names.

Important Terms (3/3)

Hundred

A subdivision of an Anglo-Saxon shire, typically comprising enough land to support approximately one hundred households.

The World the Vikings Came From

Norway

Characterized by rugged mountains and deep, narrow fjords that cut into the coast, leaving very little flat, arable land.

Denmark

Largely low-lying and marshy, offering limited fertile ground.

Sweden

Dominated by vast forests and extensive coastlines, but with restricted areas of fertile soil suitable for agriculture.

The Longship: A Technological Revolution

Shallow Draft

Requiring less than a meter of water, the longship could navigate extremely shallow rivers and could be beached directly onto sandy shores or riverbanks without the need for a harbor.

Flexibility

Capable of being both rowed and sailed, it allowed Vikings to maintain speed and direction even against unfavorable winds.

Symmetric Design

Its identical bow and stern meant the ship did not need to be turned around in narrow waterways; it could simply be rowed in the opposite direction.

Lightweight Construction

The longship's relatively light build allowed it to be portaged—carried overland—across narrow strips of land between different river systems, effectively connecting previously disparate waterways.

Key Concepts (1/2)

When did the first recorded Viking raid on the British Isles occur and where?

The first Viking raid occurred on June 8, 793, at the island monastery of Lindisfarne.

What is the likely origin and meaning of the term 'Viking'?

From the Old Norse 'vikingr,' likely meaning 'one who comes from the fjords' or 'sea raider.'

How did the monk Alcuin interpret the Viking raid on Lindisfarne?

Alcuin interpreted it as a sign of divine judgment, believing God permitted heathens to punish England for its sins.

What does Alcuin's reaction to the Lindisfarne raid tell us about the medieval worldview?

It reveals a common medieval belief that national disasters were God's response to national sin.

List the three primary reasons why the Vikings began raiding across Europe.

Reasons included economics (limited Scandinavian farmland), technology (the revolutionary longship), and culture (prized courage, glory, and treasure).

Key Concepts (2/2)

What was the 'Great Heathen Army' and when did it invade England?

The Great Heathen Army was a large coalition of Danish forces that invaded England in 865, systematically conquering Anglo-Saxon kingdoms.

By 870, which Anglo-Saxon kingdom was the only one remaining unconquered by the Danes?

Wessex was the only remaining unconquered Anglo-Saxon kingdom.

What notable intellectual trait did Alfred demonstrate as a child?

He loved poetry so much that he memorized an entire book of Old English verse before he could read, by having it read aloud repeatedly.

Who was Asser, and why is he important for understanding Alfred the Great?

Asser was a Welsh monk and scholar at Alfred's court who wrote 'The Life of King Alfred,' the most important biographical source for Alfred.

Where was Alfred driven into hiding by the Danes in 878, and what famous legend is associated with this period?

Alfred fled to the marshes of Somerset at Athelney. The famous legend is Alfred burning the cakes in a peasant woman's hut.

What was the 'fyrd' in Anglo-Saxon England?

The fyrd was the Anglo-Saxon militia, made up of free men obligated to serve the king in defense of their land.
