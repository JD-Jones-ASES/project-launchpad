Aristotle’s Theory of Tragedy: A Comprehensive Study Guide

The Definition of Tragedy

The Genus of Tragedy

It is a form of mimesis, specifically imitating an action rather than a person or abstract idea.

Magnitude and Completeness

Medium and Manner

Tragedy uses "embellished language" (meter, song, and diction) and is presented in the form of action (drama) rather than narrative (epic).

The Function (Catharsis)

The purpose of tragedy is to effect the "proper purgation" of pity and fear. This suggests that tragedy engages emotions in a controlled, artistic way, potentially refining or purging them.

The Six Elements of Tragedy

Rank. Element

Description

1. Plot (*Mythos*)

The "soul of a tragedy"; the arrangement of incidents into a causal, unified whole.

2. Character (*Ethos*)

The moral qualities of the agents that make them the kinds of people they are.

3. Thought (*Dianoia*)

The intellectual content, including arguments and demonstrations made by characters.

4. Diction (*Lexis*)

The verbal expression and metrical arrangement of words.

5. Song (*Melos*)

The musical composition, primarily involving the choral parts.

6. Spectacle (*Opsis*)

The visual staging, including costumes and machinery; considered the least essential element.

Poetry vs. History

History

records **particulars**: specific events involving specific people (e.g., what actually happened to Alcibiades).

Poetry

expresses **universals**: what a certain *type* of person will probably or necessarily do in a given situation.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/3)

Anagnorisis (Recognition)

A transition from ignorance to knowledge, often involving the discovery of a character's true identity or a change in relationship status (friendship or enmity).

Catharsis (Purgation/Purification)

The emotional end-goal of tragedy; the process of purging or clarifying the emotions of pity and fear in the audience.

Dianoia (Thought)

The intellectual or rhetorical content of a play; the arguments or maxims presented by characters to demonstrate a point.

Ethos (Character)

The moral element of a person in a play that defines their choices and the kind of person they are.

Hamartia (Error)

Literally "missing the mark"; a mistake, frailty, or misjudgment that leads to the hero's downfall. It is distinct from deep-seated vice or depravity.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/3)

Lexis (Diction)

The specific metrical and verbal expression of the drama; how the thoughts of the characters are translated into words.

Melos (Song)

The musical element of the tragedy, primarily found in the choral odes.

Mimesis (Imitation)

The representation or "imitation of an action" that serves as the basis for all poetry/tragedy.

Mythos (Plot)

The most essential element of tragedy; the logical and causal arrangement of incidents.

Opsis (Spectacle)

The visual aspects of a production, such as masks, costumes, and stage effects.

Glossary of Important Terms (3/3)

Peripeteia (Reversal)

A sudden shift in the protagonist's fortune to its opposite, occurring in accordance with probability or necessity.

Universal

A statement or representation of what a certain kind of person will "probably or necessarily say or do," as opposed to the "particular" facts of history.
