The Evolution of Mimesis—Averroes and Sir Philip Sidney

Study Slides

Averroes and the Cognitive Shift

Moral Framework

Averroes reframed Aristotle’s categories of tragedy and comedy into a binary system of moral evaluation: praise (panegyric) and blame (satire).

Muhakat

He translated mimesis as muhakat (assimilation or likening), shifting the focus from dramatic enactment to a cognitive operation of making one thing resemble another.

The Qasida Lens

The dominant Arabic poetic form, the qasida (ode), replaced the Greek play as the primary model for poetic structure.

Sir Philip Sidney and the Golden World

Improvement on Nature

Sidney argued that while nature provides a "brazen world" (imperfect and corrupt), the poet creates a "golden world" that shows humanity at its best possibilities.

To counter the charge that poets are liars, Sidney argued that because poets do not affirm their fictions as literal facts, they cannot be said to lie.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Averroes (Ibn Rushd)

(1126–1198) A Cordoban philosopher and jurist whose commentaries on Aristotle influenced Jewish and Christian thought, including that of Thomas Aquinas.

Brazen World

Sidney’s term for the real world—imperfect, mixed, and full of corruption.

Golden World

Sidney’s term for the fictional world created by the poet, where virtue is rewarded and human potential is fully expressed.

Madh (Panegyric)

The Arabic tradition of praise poetry, which Averroes equated with Aristotelian tragedy.

Muhakat

The Arabic translation of mimesis, meaning "assimilation" or "correspondence"; it emphasizes intellectual resemblance over dramatic action.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Poietes

The Greek word for "poet," literally meaning "maker."

Qasida

A classical Arabic ode structured in three parts; the dominant prestige form of Arabic poetry.

Sir Philip Sidney

(1554–1586) An Elizabethan poet and soldier who wrote The Defence of Poesy, defining the poet as a creator of moral ideals.

Tragedy and Comedy (Averroes’ Definition)

Reframed as tools for moral instruction via the celebration of virtues (tragedy) and the mockery of failings (comedy).

How did Averroes adapt the Aristotelian concept of 'tragedy' to fit Arabic culture?

He identified tragedy with panegyric (madh), the Arabic tradition of praise poetry that celebrated rulers, heroes, and their virtues.

How did Averroes adapt the Aristotelian concept of 'comedy' to fit Arabic culture?

He identified comedy with satirical verse (hija), the Arabic tradition of poetry that mocked enemies and exposed their failings.

Key Concepts (2/2)

According to Averroes' interpretation, what was the primary function of poetry?

Poetry was understood as fundamentally a matter of praise and blame, serving as a tool for moral instruction and social evaluation.

What classical Arabic poetic form became the lens through which Averroes refracted Aristotle's categories?

The qasida, a long monorhyme ode.

He translated it as 'muhakat,' meaning 'assimilation,' 'likening,' or 'correspondence.' It emphasized cognitive resemblance rather than dramatic enactment.
