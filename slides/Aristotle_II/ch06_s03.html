Study Slides

Anger and Calmness

Anger

An impulse, accompanied by pain, for conspicuous revenge for a conspicuous slight directed without justification toward oneself or one's friends.

Calmness

The settling down or quieting of anger. Men become calm toward those who humble themselves, those who admit their faults, or when time has passed and the anger is no longer fresh.

Friendship and Enmity

Friendly Feeling

Wishing for someone what you believe to be good things, not for your own sake but for theirs.

Enmity (Hatred) vs. Anger

Fear and Confidence

Fear

A pain or disturbance due to a mental picture of some destructive or painful evil in the future. For fear to exist, the danger must seem imminent, not remote.

Confidence

The opposite of fear; the expectation associated with a mental picture of the nearness of what keeps us safe and the absence or remoteness of what is terrible.

Character Portraits (Ethos of the Audience)

Feature. Youth

Elderly (Past Prime)

Primary Drive. Passion, sexual desire, honor, and victory.

Utility and the "love of gain."

Disposition. Sanguine, hopeful, and trustful.

Cynical, distrustful, and suspicious.

View of Life. Lives in expectation (the future).

Lives in memory (the past).

Temperament. Hot-tempered, excessive, and brave.

Chilly, cowardly, and "small-minded."

Reasoning. Guided by moral feeling and nobility.

Guided by reasoning and utility.

The Maxim (*Gnome*)

Soundness

Sound maxims display the speaker as having a sound moral character.

Appropriateness

Using maxims is most appropriate for elderly men speaking on subjects in which they are experienced.

The Enthymeme (*Enthymema*)

Demonstrative

Proves a proposition by joining compatible propositions.

Refutative

Disproves a proposition by joining incompatible propositions.

Standard patterns of argument, including:

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

A Fortiori

A line of argument suggesting that if a fact is true in a less likely case, it is even more likely to be true in a more probable case (or vice versa).

Enthymeme (*Enthymema*)

A rhetorical syllogism; a deductive argument based on probabilities or signs rather than absolute certainties.

Ethos

The mode of persuasion based on the character or perceived trustworthiness of the speaker.

Example (*Paradigma*)

An inductive rhetorical argument that uses specific instances (historical or invented) to support a general conclusion.

Insolence

A form of slighting that involves shaming a victim for the sole purpose of feeling superior.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Maxim (*Gnome*)

A short, pithy statement expressing a general truth about human conduct or practical life.

Pathos

The mode of persuasion that appeals to the emotions of the audience.

Slighting

The act of treating someone or something as unimportant; the primary cause of anger.

Sophist

Itinerant teachers in ancient Greece who taught rhetoric for pay; Aristotle distinguishes his systematic approach from theirs.

Topos (plural *Topoi*)

Literally "place"; a recurring logical pattern or "line of argument" used to construct enthymemes.
