Anger and Calmness

Anger

An impulse, accompanied by pain, for conspicuous revenge for a conspicuous slight directed without justification toward oneself or one's friends.

Calmness

The settling down or quieting of anger. Men become calm toward those who humble themselves, those who admit their faults, or when time has passed and the anger is no longer fresh.

Friendship and Enmity

Friendly Feeling

Wishing for someone what you believe to be good things, not for your own sake but for theirs.

Enmity (Hatred) vs. Anger

Fear and Confidence

Fear

A pain or disturbance due to a mental picture of some destructive or painful evil in the future. For fear to exist, the danger must seem imminent, not remote.

Confidence

The opposite of fear; the expectation associated with a mental picture of the nearness of what keeps us safe and the absence or remoteness of what is terrible.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

A Fortiori

A line of argument suggesting that if a fact is true in a less likely case, it is even more likely to be true in a more probable case (or vice versa).

Enthymeme (Enthymema)

A rhetorical syllogism; a deductive argument based on probabilities or signs rather than absolute certainties.

Ethos

The mode of persuasion based on the character or perceived trustworthiness of the speaker.

Example (Paradigma)

An inductive rhetorical argument that uses specific instances (historical or invented) to support a general conclusion.

Insolence

A form of slighting that involves shaming a victim for the sole purpose of feeling superior.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Maxim (Gnome)

A short, pithy statement expressing a general truth about human conduct or practical life.

Pathos

The mode of persuasion that appeals to the emotions of the audience.

Slighting

The act of treating someone or something as unimportant; the primary cause of anger.

Sophist

Itinerant teachers in ancient Greece who taught rhetoric for pay; Aristotle distinguishes his systematic approach from theirs.

Topos (plural Topoi)

Literally "place"; a recurring logical pattern or "line of argument" used to construct enthymemes.

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to Aristotle, what is the core definition of 'Emotions'?

Feelings that change men so as to affect their judgments, attended by pain or pleasure.

What three factors must an orator understand to successfully arouse a specific emotion in an audience?

The state of mind of the people, the persons they feel it toward, and the grounds on which it is felt.

How does Aristotle define 'Anger'?

An impulse, accompanied by pain, for a conspicuous revenge for a conspicuous slight directed toward oneself or friends without justification.

What specific pleasure is associated with the emotion of anger?

The pleasure arising from the expectation of revenge.

In Aristotle's theory of anger, what is 'slighting'?

The actively entertained opinion that something is obviously of no importance.

What are the three distinct kinds of slighting identified by Aristotle?

Contempt, spite, and insolence.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How is 'Spite' defined as a form of slighting?

Thwarting another person's wishes not to benefit oneself, but solely to prevent the other from getting what they want.

What is the defining characteristic of 'Insolence' in the context of anger?

Doing or saying things that cause shame to the victim for the sake of the pleasure of feeling superior.

Why are wealthy or young men often prone to insolence?

They believe their superiority is demonstrated by ill-treating others.

Which general frame of mind makes a person most prone to anger?

A state in which any pain is being felt, such as sickness, poverty, or unsatisfied desire.

Why are people particularly angry with their friends compared to strangers?

They feel that friends owe them good treatment rather than bad.

How does Aristotle define 'Growing Calm'?

A settling down or quieting of anger.
