The Art of Imitation: A Study Guide to Aristotle’s Theory of Mimesis

Study Slides

The Three Dimensions of Mimesis

Dimension. Definition

Examples from the Text

Medium. The material or tools the art uses to imitate.

Flute music uses rhythm and harmony; dancing uses rhythm and movement; poetry uses language; tragedy uses all three.

Objects. The types of people and actions being represented.

People can be depicted as "better than average" (Tragedy/Epic), "worse than average" (Comedy/Lampoon), or "the same as us."

Manner. The method or voice used to present the imitation.

Pure narrative (poet’s own voice), pure drama (characters speaking/acting), or a mix of both (as seen in Homer’s epics).

Comparison of Specific Works

Oedipus Rex

Uses language, rhythm, and music (Medium); represents noble characters in serious action (Object); uses the dramatic manner where characters speak and move directly (Manner).

The Iliad

Uses verse language (Medium); represents heroic characters (Object); employs a mixed manner where the narrator speaks and characters give extended speeches (Manner).

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Comedy

An imitation of people "worse than average" (phauloi). It focuses on the "ludicrous"—a subdivision of the ugly that is distorted but not painful or destructive.

Lampoon

A form of satirical poetry that involves personal attacks on the ignoble or base; the precursor to generalized comedy.

Margites

A lost comic poem attributed to Homer; it serves as an early example of the transition from personal lampoons to generalized comic fiction.

Mimesis

Creative artistic representation; the selective and skillful re-creation of human action.

Panegyric

A speech or poem of elaborate praise, originally delivered at public assemblies, celebrating heroic deeds.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Phauloi

Literally the "lowly" or "base." It refers to characters in comedy who are morally or intellectually inferior to the norm, but in a ridiculous rather than wicked way.

Spoudaioi

Literally the "serious" or "worthy." It refers to characters of elevated moral character and social standing who are the objects of imitation in tragedy.

Tragedy

An imitation of people "better than average" (spoudaioi). It involves serious action and invites the audience to feel pity and fear.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What Greek noun, from which we derive "mime" and "mimicry," does Aristotle use for imitation?

$mimesis$

Beyond "imitation," what richer range of meanings does the word $mimesis$ carry?

Representation, re-creation, and artistic portrayal.

According to Aristotle, is $mimesis$ a passive, mechanical copying of life?

No, it is a creative, selective, and purposeful re-creation of human action.

How does a playwright's work resemble life without being a transcript of it?

She crafts a representation that brings into focus something true and important about human experience.

List the three dimensions in which the arts differ as modes of imitation.

Medium, Objects, and Manner.

In the context of $mimesis$, what does the "Medium" refer to?

The specific material or means the art uses, such as rhythm, language, or harmony.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What materials constitute the medium of flute music?

Rhythm and harmony.

What is the sole medium used in dancing?

Rhythm and bodily movement.

What medium is utilized by poetry?

Language, whether in prose or verse.

Which three elements are combined in the medium of Tragedy?

Language, rhythm, and harmony.

In Aristotle’s framework, what are the "Objects" of artistic imitation?

People in action.

What are the three ways the "Objects" (people) in art can be depicted relative to ordinary humans?

As better than average, worse than average, or roughly the same.
