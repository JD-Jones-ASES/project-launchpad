Historical Interpreters and Commentary

Longinus (1st Century AD)

Introduced the concept of the **sublime** (*hypsos*), focusing on what makes literature great and immortal rather than just structurally sound.

Averroes (1126–1198)

Translated Aristotelian concepts for the Arabic-speaking world. Lacking a theatrical tradition, he reinterpreted tragedy as "praise poetry" and comedy as "satirical verse."

Castelvetro (1505–1571)

Transformed Aristotle’s observations into rigid prescriptive rules, most notably the **three unities**, which dominated European drama for two centuries.

Sidney (1554–1586)

Defended poetry against critics by arguing that poets create a "golden world" superior to nature's "brazen world," showing life as it ought to be.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/3)

Anagnorisis

Recognition; the transition from ignorance to knowledge.

Antistrophos

Counterpart; the relationship between rhetoric and dialectic.

Catharsis

Purgation or purification of emotions, specifically pity and fear, through art.

Dianoia

Thought; the intellectual content or themes of a work.

Ethos

The moral character or credibility of a speaker/agent.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/3)

Eudaimonia

Often translated as happiness; the "good life" or flourishing that is the aim of virtue.

Hamartia

A fatal error or tragic flaw.

Hypsos

The sublime; height or elevation in literature (associated with Longinus).

Logos

The logical argument or reason within a speech.

Mimesis

Imitation or artistic representation of human action.

Glossary of Important Terms (3/3)

Mythos

Plot; the arrangement of incidents and the "soul" of narrative.

Pathos

The emotional state of the audience.

Peripeteia

Reversal of fortune.

Sapheneia

Clarity; a primary virtue of style.

To Prepon

Appropriateness; matching the style to the subject and occasion.
