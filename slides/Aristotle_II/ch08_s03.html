Historical Interpreters and Commentary

Longinus (1st Century AD)

Introduced the concept of the sublime (hypsos), focusing on what makes literature great and immortal rather than just structurally sound.

Averroes (1126–1198)

Translated Aristotelian concepts for the Arabic-speaking world. Lacking a theatrical tradition, he reinterpreted tragedy as "praise poetry" and comedy as "satirical verse."

Castelvetro (1505–1571)

Transformed Aristotle’s observations into rigid prescriptive rules, most notably the three unities, which dominated European drama for two centuries.

Sidney (1554–1586)

Defended poetry against critics by arguing that poets create a "golden world" superior to nature's "brazen world," showing life as it ought to be.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/3)

Anagnorisis

Recognition; the transition from ignorance to knowledge.

Antistrophos

Counterpart; the relationship between rhetoric and dialectic.

Catharsis

Purgation or purification of emotions, specifically pity and fear, through art.

Dianoia

Thought; the intellectual content or themes of a work.

Ethos

The moral character or credibility of a speaker/agent.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/3)

Eudaimonia

Often translated as happiness; the "good life" or flourishing that is the aim of virtue.

Hamartia

A fatal error or tragic flaw.

Hypsos

The sublime; height or elevation in literature (associated with Longinus).

Logos

The logical argument or reason within a speech.

Mimesis

Imitation or artistic representation of human action.

Glossary of Important Terms (3/3)

Mythos

Plot; the arrangement of incidents and the "soul" of narrative.

Pathos

The emotional state of the audience.

Peripeteia

Reversal of fortune.

Sapheneia

Clarity; a primary virtue of style.

To Prepon

Appropriateness; matching the style to the subject and occasion.

Key Concepts (1/2)

In Aristotle's $Poetics$, what is the definition of $mimesis$?

The imitation or artistic representation of human action.

What are the three criteria Aristotle uses to distinguish between different types of imitative arts?

What they imitate, how they imitate, and the medium used.

According to the $Poetics$, poetry originates from the deep human instincts for _____ and _____.

imitation; rhythm

Why does Aristotle believe we take pleasure in the imitation of painful or ugly things?

We enjoy the recognition involved in the depiction, learning "what it is to be human."

How does Aristotle define tragedy in relation to its emotional effect on the audience?

It achieves $catharsis$, a purification or purgation of pity and fear.

List the six elements of tragedy in Aristotle's ranked order of importance.

Plot, character, thought, diction, song, and spectacle.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Which element of tragedy did Aristotle famously call the "soul of a tragedy"?

Plot ($mythos$).

The Aristotelian term for a "reversal of fortune" within a narrative is _____.

$peripeteia$

In the context of literary criticism, what does the term $anagnorisis$ represent?

A moment of recognition.

Define the concept of $hamartia$ as used in Aristotelian tragedy.

The error or flaw that leads to the hero's undoing.

Why did Aristotle argue that poetry is "more philosophical than history"?

Poetry deals with universal truths (what might happen), while history deals with particulars (what did happen).

According to Aristotle, why is the command of metaphor a "sign of genius"?

It implies an intuitive perception of the similarity in dissimilars.
