The Life of Words (Aristotle II)

Chapter 7, Section 1

Aristotle’s Theory of Style and Arrangement

Study Slides

The Role of Metaphor

Proportional Metaphors

These establish a four-term ratio (A is to B as C is to D). For example, describing fallen soldiers as the "spring taken out of the year."

Vividness (Energeia)

The best metaphors give "life to lifeless things," setting events before the eyes of the audience as if they are in motion.

Prose Rhythm and Style

The Paean

Aristotle recommends this metrical foot for prose because it provides a pleasing movement without a predictable pattern.

Periodic Style (Lexis Katestrammene)

Unlike the "running style" (a simple string of clauses), the periodic style treats the sentence as a self-contained unit with a clear beginning and end.

The Structure of a Speech

Part. Term

Purpose

Introduction. Prooimion

Prepares the audience; secures attention and goodwill.

Statement. Prothesis

States the case or the proposition to be proved.

Argument. Pistis

The proof; uses logos, ethos, and pathos to demonstrate truth.

Epilogue. Epilogos

Consolidates the proof, arouses emotion, and refreshes the memory.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/3)

Asteia (Smart Sayings)

Witty, polished remarks (from astu, meaning "city") that convey new understanding through a sudden act of recognition.

Energeia (Vividness/Actuality)

The quality of making an audience "see" something by representing it in action or giving life to inanimate objects.

Epilogos (Epilogue)

The conclusion of a speech; literally "after the speech."

Lexis Eiromene (Running Style)

A style of prose where clauses are loosely connected by simple conjunctions like "and."

Lexis Katestrammene (Periodic Style)

A structured prose style where sentences are formed as self-contained units with a clear sense of development and closure.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/3)

Paean

A Greek metrical foot (one long and three short syllables, or vice versa) recommended by Aristotle for prose because it is rhythmic but not metrical.

Pistis (Argument/Proof)

The section of the speech dedicated to demonstrating the truth of the statement.

Prooimion (Introduction)

The prelude or "before the path" section of a speech intended to prepare the audience.

Prothesis (Statement)

The necessary part of a speech where the speaker identifies what they propose to prove.

Sapheneia (Clarity)

The primary virtue of style; the transparency of language that ensures meaning is conveyed.

Glossary of Key Terms (3/3)

To Prepon (Appropriateness)

The fitness of language to the speaker, subject, and audience; known in Latin as decorum.

To Psychron (Frigidity)

The stylistic vice of being overly elaborate, strained, or affected; the opposite of naturalness.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the primary practical focus of Book III of Aristotle's Rhetoric?

The actual writing and construction of a speech.

What does Aristotle identify as the supreme virtue of prose style?

$sapheneia$ (clarity).

In the context of style, what is the literal meaning of the Greek term $sapheneia$?

Clarity or transparency.

Why does Aristotle argue that clarity is a logical requirement of speech?

Because speech exists to convey meaning, and failing to be clear means the speech has failed its essential purpose.

What is the stylistic result of a speech that achieves perfect clarity but lacks distinction?

It becomes flat and prosaic.

Define the rhetorical concept of $to\ prepon$.

Appropriateness or fitness—the match between style and subject, audience, speaker, and occasion.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the Latin equivalent for the Greek term $to\ prepon$?

Decorum.

How does appropriateness ($to\ prepon$) function as an 'epistemic virtue'?

It makes the argument more believable by preventing the language from distracting the audience from the subject.

How is the virtue of clarity primarily achieved in prose?

By using current, ordinary words for the things they commonly name.

When is a prose style considered 'appropriate' regarding its emotional register?

When the language used matches the elevation and emotion of the subject matter.

According to Aristotle, what is the 'third quality' of distinguished prose beyond clarity and appropriateness?

Distinction (character and force).

What is the single most powerful device for achieving distinction in prose style?

Metaphor.
