Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Arete

Virtue or excellence. It refers to the full excellence of any kind of thing, not just moral goodness (e.g., courage, justice, wisdom).

Doctrine of the Mean

The principle that every virtue is a middle point between a vice of excess and a vice of deficiency.

Eudaimonia

Happiness or flourishing; the highest human good achieved through the active exercise of the soul’s capacities.

Philia

Friendship; characterized as one of the highest human goods where a friend is seen as "another self."

Phronimos

The practically wise person who possesses the discernment to find the "mean" or right action in various situations.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Polis

The city-state or political community; the basic unit of Greek political life intended to facilitate the good life.

Politikon Zōon

"Political animal"; the term used to describe the nature of humans as beings who must live in a community to be fully human.

Polity

Aristotle’s preferred "mixed government" involving rule by the many (specifically a broad middle class) for the common good.

Theoria

Intellectual contemplation; the highest activity of the human soul.

Visio Dei

The "direct vision of God"; identified by Thomas Aquinas as the supernatural ultimate end of human life, surpassing Aristotle’s natural eudaimonia.

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to the Nicomachean Ethics, what is the ultimate 'highest good' that all human activities aim toward?

$Eudaimonia$ (happiness or flourishing).

The Greek word $eudaimonia$ is a compound of 'eu' (good/well) and 'daimon', which refers to the _____.

Spirit or inner self.

How does Aristotle define $eudaimonia$ in terms of human capacity?

The active, excellent exercise of one's distinctly human capacities.

What is the specific 'function' of a human being according to Aristotle?

The active exercise of the soul in accordance with reason and virtue ($aret\bar{e}$).

Term: $Aret\bar{e}$

Definition: Virtue or excellence, signifying the full excellence of any kind of thing.

Aristotle argues that virtue is not an innate feeling or a mechanical rule, but a _____ acquired through practice.

Habit (or settled disposition).

Key Concepts (2/2)

According to Aristotle, how is a person's character primarily formed?

By what they repeatedly choose to do (habituation).

Explain Aristotle's 'doctrine of the mean'.

Every virtue is a middle point between the two vices of excess and deficiency.

In the context of the doctrine of the mean, courage is the middle point between _____ and _____.

Recklessness (excess) and cowardice (deficiency).

In the doctrine of the mean, generosity is the middle point between _____ and _____.

Prodigality (excess) and miserliness (deficiency).

What term does Aristotle use for the 'practically wise person' capable of finding the mean in any situation?

The $phronimos$.

Why does Aristotle consider justice the 'most complete' of the virtues?

It concerns our relations with others in a community rather than just ourselves.
