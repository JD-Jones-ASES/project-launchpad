Aristotle’s Poetics: The Elements of Tragedy Study Guide

Study Slides

The Six Formative Elements

Rank. Element

Definition and Function

1. Plot

The "soul" of tragedy; the arrangement of incidents and the imitation of action.

2. Character

The qualities ascribed to agents; it reveals moral purpose and choices.

3. Thought

The faculty of saying what is possible and pertinent; proven statements or general truths.

4. Diction

The expression of meaning in words, whether in verse or prose.

5. Song

The chief among the artistic embellishments.

6. Spectacle

The visual representation; the least artistic element, as it depends on stage machinery rather than poetry.

Unity and Magnitude

Beginning

Does not follow anything by necessity but leads naturally to what follows.

End

Follows something by necessity or rule but has nothing following it.

Magnitude

A play must be long enough to allow a change from good fortune to bad (or vice versa) within a sequence of probability, yet short enough to be "easily embraced by the memory."

Universal vs. Particular

History

relates the particular (what actually happened, e.g., what Alcibiades did).

Poetry

relates the universal (what may happen according to the laws of probability or necessity).

The Mechanics of the Tragic Effect

Peripeteia (Reversal of the Situation)

A change by which the action veers round to its opposite (e.g., a messenger intended to cheer a character but produces the opposite effect).

Anagnorisis (Recognition)

A change from ignorance to knowledge, creating love or hate between characters.

Scene of Suffering

A destructive or painful action, such as death or bodily agony on stage.

The Ideal Hero

• Highly renowned and prosperous (e.g., Oedipus or Thyestes).

• Not eminently good or just, yet not depraved.

• A person whose misfortune is brought about by hamartia—an error or frailty—rather than vice.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Anagnorisis (Recognition)

The transition from ignorance to knowledge, often involving the identity of persons or the truth of past actions.

Catharsis (Purgation)

The emotional discharge through which the audience is purged of pity and fear.

Deus ex Machina

A plot device where a seemingly unsolvable problem is abruptly resolved by an unexpected and unlikely intervention (literally "god from the machine").

Diction

The expression of meaning through the metrical arrangement of words.

Hamartia

A "missing of the mark"; a fatal error or frailty in a character that leads to their downfall.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Imitation (Mimesis)

The fundamental principle of poetry; tragedy is the imitation of an action.

Magnitude

The appropriate scale or length of a work, necessary for beauty and the audience's comprehension of the whole.

Peripeteia (Reversal)

A sudden change in circumstances or fortune to its opposite, occurring according to probability or necessity.

Spectacle

The visual elements of a play, including costumes and stage effects; considered the least artistic element of tragedy.

Thought

The intellectual element of a play; the ability of characters to prove a point or enunciate general truths.

Key Concepts (1/2)

How does Aristotle define the fundamental principle of poetry?

Imitation (mimesis).

According to Aristotle, why is tragedy considered the "perfected form" of poetic imitation?

It is the art at its most rigorous, most unified, and most emotionally powerful.

Tragedy is an imitation of an action that is serious, complete, and of a certain _____.

Magnitude

What two specific emotions is tragedy intended to purge through the process of catharsis?

Pity and fear.

By what medium is tragedy performed, as opposed to narrative forms?

The form of action.

What does Aristotle mean by "language embellished"?

Language that includes rhythm, harmony, and song.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Which two elements of tragedy constitute the "medium of imitation"?

Diction and Song.

Which element of tragedy constitutes the "manner of imitation"?

Spectacle.

Which three elements of tragedy constitute the "objects of imitation"?

Plot, Character, and Thought.

What is Aristotle's definition of Plot?

The arrangement of the incidents.

What is the primary function of Character in a tragedy?

To reveal moral purpose by showing what kind of things a person chooses or avoids.

In what specific context is "Thought" required within a tragedy?

Wherever a statement is proved or a general truth is enunciated.
