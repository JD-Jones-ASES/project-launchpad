Aristotle’s Analysis of Poetic Diction, Epic, and the Defense of Poetry

The Mechanics of Metaphor

From Genus to Species

Using a broad category to describe a specific item (e.g., "lying" for "lying at anchor").

From Species to Genus

Using a specific item to describe a broad category (e.g., "ten thousand" for "a large number").

From Species to Species

Swapping two specific items within the same category (e.g., "drew away the life" vs. "cleft the water," both forms of "taking away").

The Perfection of Poetic Style

Clarity

Achieved through the use of "current" or "proper" words. However, a style using *only* common words is "mean" and artistically flat.

Distinction/Loftiness

Achieved through "unusual" words, including rare terms, metaphors, and modified word forms (lengthened, contracted, or altered).

The Risk of Excess

Overuse of metaphors results in a **riddle**; overuse of rare words results in **jargon**.

The Balance

Great poetry requires a "certain infusion" of unusual elements to raise the style above the commonplace, while maintaining enough proper words to ensure the text remains perspicuous (clear).

Epic Poetry (Epos)

Structure

Epics must follow dramatic principles, focusing on a **single, whole, and complete action** with a beginning, middle, and end.

Unity

Unlike historical compositions, which cover a single period regardless of how many unrelated events occurred, an epic must have the unity of a "living organism."

Scale

Epics can be much longer than tragedies because the narrative form allows for the presentation of simultaneous actions, which add "mass and dignity" and prevent satiety.

Metre

The "heroic measure" (hexameter) is the only appropriate metre for epic because it is the stateliest and most massive, easily admitting rare words and metaphors.

The Defense of Poetry

Different Standards

Correctness in poetry is not the same as correctness in politics, medicine, or science.

Artistic Ends

A "probable impossibility" is preferable to an "unconvincing possibility" if it serves the striking effect of the poem.

Idealism vs. Realism

Poets may represent things as they are, as they are said to be (tradition/myth), or "as they ought to be" (the ideal).

Linguistic Context

Apparent contradictions can often be resolved by examining rare word usages, metaphors, punctuation, or regional accents.
