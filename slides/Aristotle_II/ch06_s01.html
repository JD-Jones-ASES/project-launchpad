Key Emotions in Detail: Anger and Fear

Emotion. Definition

Key Requirements

Anger (Orge). The desire for apparent retaliation for an apparent slight done to oneself or friends, accompanied by pain.

1. Belief in a slight/disrespect. <br> 2. Belief the offender acted by choice. <br> 3. Pain from the slight and pleasure in anticipated revenge.

Fear (Phobos). Pain or disturbance caused by a mental picture of a destructive or painful evil in the future.

1. Belief that something bad may happen. <br> 2. Proximity (the evil is near, not remote). <br> 3. Uncertainty (certainty produces despair, not fear).

The Three Stages of Life

The Young

Driven by passion and hope. They are trustful, generous, and quick to anger but also quick to forgive. Lacking experience, they tend toward excess and have not yet learned the "doctrine of the mean."

The Old

The inverse of youth. Shaped by disappointment, they are cynical, suspicious, and hesitant. They live by memory rather than hope and tend toward small-mindedness and acquisitiveness.

Forms of Rhetorical Argument

Term. Type

Description

Example (Paradigma). Inductive

Drawing a general conclusion from particular cases. Can be historical (real events) or invented (fables/analogies).

Enthymeme (Enthymema). Deductive

The "body of persuasion." A rhetorical syllogism based on probabilities and signs. It often omits a premise that the audience supplies.

Maxim (Gnome). Premise

A pithy general statement about human conduct (e.g., "Fortune favors the bold"). Maxims serve as the building blocks for enthymemes.

The Topoi (Lines of Argument)

Arguing from Opposites

If X is good, its opposite must be bad.

Arguing from Degree

If a lesser case is true, the greater case is likely true as well.

Arguing from Definition

Using the literal meaning of a word to determine what falls under its category.

Arguing from Analogy

Asserting that what is true in one domain applies to a parallel domain.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Enthymeme (Enthymema)

A deductive rhetorical argument that moves from probable premises to a conclusion, often leaving one premise unstated.

Example (Paradigma)

An inductive rhetorical argument where a general rule is inferred from specific instances.

Maxim (Gnome)

A short, memorable statement of a general truth regarding human conduct.

Orge (Anger)

A painful desire for retaliation caused by a perceived slight from a rational agent.

Phobos (Fear)

Disturbance or pain arising from the mental image of a threatening future evil.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Spoudaios

A person of practical wisdom who has internalized the "mean" and possesses a stable, virtuous disposition.

Topoi

"Topics" or universal lines of argument that provide a mental toolkit for generating persuasive points on any subject.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What did Aristotle produce that is considered the first systematic psychology of human emotion in Western philosophy?

A rigorous and precise account of what emotions are, how they arise, and their purposes.

What are the three interconnected questions Aristotle uses to analyze each emotion?

What state of mind produces it, toward whom is it directed, and for what reasons does it arise?

How does Aristotle organize the fourteen emotions he analyzes in Book II?

He organizes them into contrasting pairs to illuminate each term by its opposite.

What is the contrasting emotion for anger in Aristotle's list of fourteen emotions?

Calmness.

What is the contrasting emotion for fear in Aristotle's list of fourteen emotions?

Confidence.

What is the contrasting emotion for pity in Aristotle's list of fourteen emotions?

Indignation.

Key Concepts (2/2)

According to Aristotle, why is an 'apparent' slight sufficient to trigger anger?

Perceived reality, rather than objective truth, determines a person's emotional response.

Why does Aristotle state that humans do not get angry at inanimate forces like thunderstorms?

Anger requires a rational agent who could have chosen to act differently.

What specific pleasure accompanies the pain of anger according to Aristotle?

The anticipated pleasure of retaliation or revenge.

To what does Aristotle compare an angry person's anticipation of retaliation?

A person who has just sat down to a banquet.

How does Aristotle define fear ($phobos$)?

Pain or disturbance due to a mental picture of some destructive or painful evil in the future.

Why does fear require the belief that a bad event is 'not too distant'?

Humans generally do not fear evils that are merely remote.
