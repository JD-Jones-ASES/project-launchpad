Biographical Context: Aristotle (384–322 BC)

Origins and Education

Born in Stagira, a Greek colony in northern Macedonia. At age seventeen, he moved to Athens to study at Plato’s Academy, remaining there for twenty years as a student, teacher, and researcher.

Professional Life

After Plato's death in 347 BC, Aristotle eventually became the tutor to Alexander of Macedon (Alexander the Great). In 335 BC, he returned to Athens to found his own school, the Lyceum.

Tragedy

The surviving Book I of the Poetics focuses on tragedy and epic poetry. Aristotle identifies six elements of tragedy, ranking plot as the most important, followed by character.

Katharsis

The distinctive emotional goal of tragedy is to achieve the katharsis (purification or purgation) of pity and fear.

Structural Elements

Aristotle analyzes the role of reversal, recognition, and the tragic hero. He famously identifies the mastery of metaphor as the greatest aspect of a poet's craft.

The Three Modes of Persuasion

Mode. Definition

Focus

Ethos. Character

The credibility, competence, and integrity of the speaker.

Pathos. Emotion

Moving the audience by arousing relevant emotions (fear, anger, pity).

Logos. Reason

The rational content, evidence, and logical structure of the argument.

Logic and Structure

The Enthymeme

Known as the “rhetorical syllogism,” the enthymeme is the "body of persuasion." Unlike a logical syllogism that uses universal truths, an enthymeme argues from what is probable or generally true.

The Study of Emotions

Book II of the Rhetoric contains the first systematic study of emotions in Western philosophy, analyzing feelings such as shame, envy, and indignation.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What central question does Aristotle's "Poetics" attempt to answer?

What is art and how does it work?

What central question does Aristotle's "Rhetoric" attempt to answer?

How do words persuade and how should they be used?

Why does Aristotle consider both poetry and rhetoric essential to a political community?

Because citizens must be moved by words for the community to function.

When was Aristotle's "Poetics" approximately written?

c. 335 BC.

Which work is considered the earliest surviving example of literary criticism in the Western tradition?

Aristotle's "Poetics".

Which genres of literature are the primary focus of the surviving Book I of the "Poetics"?

Tragedy and epic poetry.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What was the intended subject of the lost second book of Aristotle's "Poetics"?

Comedy.

How does Aristotle define "mimesis" in the context of poetry?

The imitation or creative representation of human beings in action.

According to Aristotle, how does the poet's task differ from the historian's task?

The poet shows what could happen, while the historian records what did happen.

Why does Aristotle describe poetry as "more philosophical and higher" than history?

Poetry reveals universal truths about the human condition rather than specific past events.

How many elements of tragedy did Aristotle identify?

Six.

Which element of tragedy does Aristotle rank as the most important?

Plot.
