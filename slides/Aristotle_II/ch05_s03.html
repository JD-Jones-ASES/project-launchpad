The Three Modes of Persuasion (Technical)

Mode. Definition

Mechanism

Ethos. Character

Achieved when the speech makes the speaker appear credible and good. Aristotle considers character "almost the most effective means of persuasion."

Pathos. Emotion

Achieved by putting the audience into a specific frame of mind. Judgments differ based on whether the hearer feels friendly or hostile, pleased or pained.

Logos. Logic/Proof

Achieved through the speech itself when a truth or "apparent truth" is proved using suitable arguments (enthymemes).

Political (Deliberative) Oratory

Audience

Members of a public assembly.

Time

The Future.

Purpose

To urge a course of action or dissuade from one.

End Goal

Establishing **expediency** (utility) or harmfulness.

Key Subjects

Ways and means (revenue/expenditure), war and peace, national defense, imports/exports (food supply), and legislation.

Forensic (Legal) Oratory

Audience

Jurymen or judges.

Time

The Past.

Purpose

To attack (accuse) or defend.

End Goal

Establishing **justice** or injustice.

Ceremonial (Epideictic) Oratory

Audience

Observers.

Time

The Present (primarily).

Purpose

To praise (encomium) or censure.

End Goal

Establishing **honour** (the noble) or shame (the base).

Glossary of Important Terms (1/3)

Aristocracy

A form of government based on an education qualification; the "rule of the best."

Deliberative Rhetoric

Political oratory used to advise for or against a future course of action based on utility.

Democracy

A form of government where citizens distribute offices by lot; its end is freedom.

Dialectic

The art of reasoning through question and answer to seek truth; the counterpart to rhetoric.

Encomium

A speech of praise based on a person’s actual deeds.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/3)

Enthymeme

The "body of persuasion"; a logical argument used by orators that functions as a rhetorical syllogism.

Ethos

The persuasive appeal based on the perceived character and credibility of the speaker.

Forensic Rhetoric

Legal oratory concerned with the justice or injustice of past actions.

Logos

The persuasive appeal based on the logic, proof, or apparent proof provided by the speech itself.

Magnanimity

The virtue that disposes one to do good to others on a large scale.

Glossary of Important Terms (3/3)

Monarchy

A constitution where one person has authority; includes kingship (limited) and tyranny (unlimited).

Oligarchy

A form of government based on a property qualification; its end is wealth.

Pathos

The persuasive appeal based on stirring the emotions of the audience to influence their judgment.

Prudence

A virtue of the understanding enabling wise decisions regarding happiness and the balance of goods and evils.

Virtue

A faculty of providing and preserving good things; a faculty of beneficence. Examples include justice, courage, and temperance.
