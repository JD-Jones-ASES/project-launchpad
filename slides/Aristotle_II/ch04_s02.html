The Nature of the Sublime (Hypsos)

Definition

Longinus uses the term hypsos to describe a quality of height, elevation, or sublimity in literature and oratory.

Effect

The sublime does not merely produce pleasure; it produces "transport"—a sense of being lifted above oneself and experiencing something greater than ordinary human measure. It is analogous to divine...

Attainability

Longinus argues that while much of the sublime depends on natural genius, it can be supported through study and art.

The Five Sources of the Sublime

Source. Category

Description

1. Grandeur of Thought. Natural

The capacity for great conceptions; the most important source.

2. Vigorous Passion. Natural

A spirited treatment of the passions; genuine emotion that sounds like "the voice of a god."

3. Artifice in Figures. Art

The effective use of figures of thought and figures of speech.

4. Dignified Expression. Art

Noble diction, including the proper choice of words and the use of metaphors.

5. Majesty of Structure. Art

The elevated composition and arrangement of the entire work.

The Sublime vs. The Pathetic

Distinction

The sublime is not always emotional, and the emotional is not always sublime. Low passions like pity, grief, and fear are not "lofty."

Parenthyrsus

This is a rhetorical vice described as "false passion." It occurs when a writer strains for greatness but produces only "empty swelling" or bombast, leading to absurdity rather than transport.

The "Golden World" vs. The "Brazen World"

The Brazen World

Nature as it actually is—imperfect and constrained by reality.

The Golden World

The world created by the poet, where virtue is rewarded, excellence is portrayed at its height, and the moral order of things is made visible.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Astrophil and Stella

Sidney’s sequence of 108 sonnets and eleven songs (c. 1582), depicting a speaker's unsuccessful love for "Stella."

Brazen World

Sidney’s term for nature as it actually is—imperfect and brass-like.

Golden World

The poet’s creation; a world in which virtue is rewarded and excellence is portrayed at its highest, raising the reader toward a "golden age."

Hypsos

Height, elevation, or sublimity; the quality that overwhelms the reader and produces divine inspiration.

Parenthyrsus

False or misplaced passion; "empty swelling" or bombast that fails to achieve the sublime and falls into absurdity.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Pathos

Emotion or suffering; the emotional content of tragedy and a mode of persuasion.

Sublime

Derived from the Latin sublimis ("under the lintel"); refers to literary greatness so powerful it produces transport.

Transport

The sudden sense of being lifted above oneself, experiencing something greater than ordinary human measure.

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to the text, what quality in literature produces transport rather than mere pleasure?

The sublime (or $hypsos$).

What is the literal meaning of the Latin word $sublimis$?

Under the lintel.

Which ancient critic wrote the treatise 'On the Sublime'?

Longinus.

Longinus identifies the sublime as an experience analogous to divine inspiration or _____.

Ecstasy.

According to Longinus, how many principal sources of the sublime exist?

Five.

What preliminary gift is required before any of the five sources of the sublime can be employed?

Command of language.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the first and most important source of the sublime identified by Longinus?

Grandeur of thought.

What is the second source of the sublime in Longinus's hierarchy?

A vigorous and spirited treatment of the passions.

Which two sources of the sublime depend primarily on natural endowments?

Grandeur of thought and treatment of the passions.

What is the third source of the sublime?

Artifice in the employment of figures (of thought and speech).

What is the fourth source of the sublime?

Dignified expression.

Into which two sub-divisions is 'dignified expression' categorized?

Proper choice of words and use of metaphors/ornaments.
