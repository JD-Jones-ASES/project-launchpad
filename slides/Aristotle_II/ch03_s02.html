Longinus and the Sublime

Hypsos (The Sublime)

A quality of greatness that does not merely persuade but "transports" or produces ecstasy (*ekplexis*). It is described as a "lightning-flash" that illuminates an entire subject in a single moment.

Aesthetic vs. Structural

Where Aristotle provides a structural explanation of tragedy (catharsis through plot), Longinus provides an aesthetic experience of it (a shattering, overwhelming feeling of greatness).

The Character of the Writer

Longinus argued that sublimity stems from the power of forming great conceptions, meaning great writing is a reflection of the writer's mind and soul, not just technical skill.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Catharsis

Aristotle’s term for the purgation or purification of the emotions of pity and fear produced by tragedy.

Ekplexis

A state of transport or ecstasy produced by encounter with the sublime.

Frigidity

A fault in writing identified by Longinus, often resulting from over-elaboration or the pursuit of "childish absurdities" and novel thoughts.

Hypsos

The Greek term for "the sublime"; a quality of loftiness and excellence in language that overwhelms the reader.

Neoclassicism

A movement in 17th- and 18th-century literature seeking to revive the standards of ancient Greece and Rome, specifically the Three Unities in drama.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Puerility

A "pedantic habit of mind" that ends in frigidity; a failing of feeble minds aiming for brilliancy but resulting in affectation.

The Three Unities

A set of dramatic rules (Action, Time, and Place) derived and expanded from Aristotle by Renaissance commentators.

Verisimilitude

From the Latin *verisimilis* (“resembling truth”); the principle that a play should be believable and true to life to maintain the audience's illusion.
