Longinus and the Sublime

Hypsos (The Sublime)

A quality of greatness that does not merely persuade but "transports" or produces ecstasy (ekplexis). It is described as a "lightning-flash" that illuminates an entire subject in a single moment.

Aesthetic vs. Structural

Where Aristotle provides a structural explanation of tragedy (catharsis through plot), Longinus provides an aesthetic experience of it (a shattering, overwhelming feeling of greatness).

The Character of the Writer

Longinus argued that sublimity stems from the power of forming great conceptions, meaning great writing is a reflection of the writer's mind and soul, not just technical skill.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Catharsis

Aristotle’s term for the purgation or purification of the emotions of pity and fear produced by tragedy.

Ekplexis

A state of transport or ecstasy produced by encounter with the sublime.

Frigidity

A fault in writing identified by Longinus, often resulting from over-elaboration or the pursuit of "childish absurdities" and novel thoughts.

Hypsos

The Greek term for "the sublime"; a quality of loftiness and excellence in language that overwhelms the reader.

Neoclassicism

A movement in 17th- and 18th-century literature seeking to revive the standards of ancient Greece and Rome, specifically the Three Unities in drama.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Puerility

A "pedantic habit of mind" that ends in frigidity; a failing of feeble minds aiming for brilliancy but resulting in affectation.

The Three Unities

A set of dramatic rules (Action, Time, and Place) derived and expanded from Aristotle by Renaissance commentators.

Verisimilitude

From the Latin verisimilis (“resembling truth”); the principle that a play should be believable and true to life to maintain the audience's illusion.

Key Concepts (1/2)

Around what year did Aristotle write the Poetics?

335 BC

How did the reception of Aristotle's Poetics change during the Renaissance?

Humanist scholars transformed its descriptive observations into binding prescriptions.

Which ancient critic evaluated tragedy based on its emotional and spiritual effect rather than structural features?

Longinus

What is the only unity that Aristotle explicitly prescribes for tragedy?

Unity of action

According to Aristotle, what structural elements must a plot possess to maintain unity of action?

A beginning, middle, and end that are causally connected.

What was Aristotle's descriptive observation regarding the time span of a tragedy?

Tragedy endeavors to confine itself to a single revolution of the sun.

Key Concepts (2/2)

The Greek word $peiratai$ in Aristotle’s observation on time translates to what English concept?

It means "tries" or "tends to," indicating a tendency rather than a command.

What does Aristotle's Poetics say regarding the unity of place?

Nothing; unity of place is entirely absent from the text.

Who was the Italian humanist scholar responsible for the 1570 commentary Poetica d'Aristotele Vulgarizzata et Sposta?

Lodovico Castelvetro

Term: Verisimilitude

The principle that art should appear true to life (from the Latin verisimilis).

Why did Castelvetro argue that a play must take place in a single location?

He believed audiences could not suspend disbelief if the stage represented multiple distant locations.

How did Castelvetro define the Unity of Time in a prescriptive sense?

A play must take place within the 12 to 24 hours of the performance itself.
