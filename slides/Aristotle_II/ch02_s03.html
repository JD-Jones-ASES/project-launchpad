The Evolution of Genres

Serious Spirits

Focused on noble actions and good men, leading from hymns and praises to Epic poetry, and eventually to Tragedy.

Trivial Spirits

Focused on the actions of "meaner persons," leading from personal satires (lampoons) to Comedy.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/3)

Q: Where and when did Aristotle compose the *Poetics*?

Aristotle composed the *Poetics* around 335 BC in Athens, during the years he led his school, the Lyceum.

Q: How did Aristotle’s view of poetry differ from Plato’s?

Plato viewed poetry with suspicion as a dangerous imitation of appearances twice removed from reality. Aristotle approached it scientifically and philosophically, viewing it with curiosity and admiration as a natural human activity.

Q: What are the three dimensions by which Aristotle distinguishes different arts?

The three dimensions are the medium (how they imitate), the objects (what they imitate), and the manner or mode (how the imitation is presented).

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/3)

Q: According to Aristotle, what is the primary difference between Tragedy and Comedy?

The difference lies in the objects of imitation: Comedy aims to represent men as "worse" than in actual life (lower types), while Tragedy aims to represent them as "better" (higher types).

Q: Why do humans find pleasure in representations of objects that would be painful to see in person?

The pleasure is derived from the act of learning. Contemplating a likeness allows people to infer and understand what an object is, and Aristotle asserts that learning gives the "liveliest pleasure" to humans.

Q: What distinguishes Epic poetry from Tragedy in terms of "manner" and "time"?

Epic poetry is narrative in form and has no specific limits of time. Tragedy is dramatic (characters acting) and endeavors to confine its action to a "single revolution of the sun" or slightly longer.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (3/3)

Q: Who was the first Athenian writer to abandon personal lampooning in favor of generalized comic plots?

Crates was the first Athenian writer to move away from the "iambic" or lampooning form to generalize themes and plots in comedy.
