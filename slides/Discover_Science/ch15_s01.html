Important Terms (1/4)

Producer

An organism, typically a plant, that produces its own food using energy from the sun (e.g., photosynthesis).

Consumer

An organism that obtains energy by feeding on other organisms.

Food Chain

A sequence illustrating how energy and nutrients are transferred from one organism to another, starting from a producer.

Water Cycle

The continuous process by which water circulates Earth's atmosphere, land, and oceans through evaporation, condensation, precipitation, and collection.

Evaporation

The process by which water changes from a liquid into a gas (water vapor).

Important Terms (2/4)

Condensation

The process by which water vapor in the air changes back into liquid water, forming clouds.

Precipitation

Water released from clouds in the form of rain, snow, sleet, or hail.

Hydrosphere

All the water on Earth's surface, underground, and in the air.

Atom

The basic unit of a chemical element; the smallest particle of matter that retains an element's chemical identity.

Molecule

A group of two or more atoms held together by chemical bonds.

Important Terms (3/4)

Glucose (C6H12O6)

A simple sugar molecule, a key product of photosynthesis and a primary source of energy for living organisms.

Photosynthesis

The process used by plants and other organisms to convert light energy into chemical energy, which is stored in glucose molecules.

Energy

The capacity to do work or produce heat; exists in various forms like light, chemical, kinetic, and heat.

Light Energy

Energy from electromagnetic radiation, such as sunlight.

Chemical Energy

Energy stored in the bonds of chemical compounds (e.g., in food molecules).

Important Terms (4/4)

Kinetic Energy

The energy of motion.

Heat Energy

Energy associated with the random motion of atoms and molecules; often a byproduct of energy transformations.

Conservation of Atoms

The principle that atoms cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction; they are merely rearranged.

Conservation of Energy

The principle that energy cannot be created or destroyed, but can only be transformed from one form to another.

Life Science: Animals Eat to Survive

Producers

Organisms like the apple tree, which makes its own food using sunlight through photosynthesis. Plants are the base of most food chains.

Consumers

Organisms that get energy by eating other organisms.

Food Chain

A sequence illustrating the flow of energy: Sun → Plant → Animal → You. Each organism in the chain relies on the one before it for energy.

Biodiversity in Food Chains

Food chains involve both **vertebrates** (like turkeys, cows, humans) and **invertebrates** (like earthworms that help decompose and enrich soil for plants).

Chemistry: Atoms in Every Bite

Atomic Composition

All matter, including your food, is made of tiny **atoms** like carbon (C), hydrogen (H), and oxygen (O). These atoms bond together to form **molecules**.

Glucose

The sugar in an apple is a molecule called glucose, with the chemical formula **C6H12O6**. This means it's composed of six carbon atoms, twelve hydrogen atoms, and six oxygen atoms.

Photosynthesis (Chemical Perspective)

Plants perform photosynthesis by taking carbon from carbon dioxide (CO2) in the air and hydrogen and oxygen from water (H2O) in the soil. They then rearrange these atoms to create glucose molecules.

Atoms in Your Body

When you eat an apple, your body breaks down the glucose molecules, using the liberated atoms as building blocks for your own cells.

Physics: Energy Flows Through the Chain

The Sun as the Energy Source

The sun is the ultimate origin of all energy in most food chains, emitting **light energy** to Earth.

Energy Transformation in Plants

Plants capture this light energy during photosynthesis and convert it into **chemical energy**, stored within the bonds of glucose molecules.
