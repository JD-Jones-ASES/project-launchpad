Important Terms (1/3)

Weather

What the atmosphere is doing at a particular place and time; changes quickly.

Climate

The average weather pattern of a place over a long period of time, usually 30 years or more.

Tropical Zone

The area near the equator (between the Tropic of Cancer and the Tropic of Capricorn) characterized by warm temperatures all year round and often heavy rainfall.

Temperate Zone

The areas between the tropics and the polar regions, known for having four distinct seasons with significant temperature changes throughout the year.

Polar Zone

The areas near the North and South Poles, characterized by very cold temperatures for most of the year and usually little precipitation.

Important Terms (2/3)

Equator

An imaginary line around the middle of Earth, equidistant from the North and South Poles, dividing Earth into the Northern and Southern Hemispheres.

Tropic of Cancer

The most northerly circle of latitude on Earth at which the Sun can be directly overhead.

Tropic of Capricorn

The most southerly circle of latitude on Earth at which the Sun can be directly overhead.

Altitude

The height of an object or point in relation to sea level or ground level. Higher altitudes are generally colder.

Important Terms (3/3)

Fujita Scale (F-Scale)

A scale developed by Tetsuya Fujita to rate the intensity of tornadoes based on the damage they cause, ranging from F0 (light damage) to F5 (incredible damage). An updated version is still used today.

Microbursts

Powerful downward blasts of air from thunderstorms that can be dangerous to airplanes, discovered by Tetsuya Fujita.

Why Different Places Have Different Climates

Distance from the Equator

Altitude

As elevation increases, air temperature decreases. Therefore, high places like mountains are typically colder than valleys, even if they are at the same latitude.

Distance from the Ocean

Large bodies of water like oceans heat up and cool down more slowly than land.

Ocean Currents

Moving masses of ocean water can transfer heat around the globe.

Real-World Application: Dressing for Your Climate Zone

Tropical Climates

People wear light, breathable clothing to stay cool.

Temperate Climates

People need clothes for all four seasons.

Polar Climates

Many layers and heavily insulated gear are necessary.

Housing

Flat roofs are common in dry climates, while steep roofs in snowy climates help shed snow.

A Famous Scientist Who Studied Storms: Tetsuya “Ted” Fujita

Background

Tetsuya “Ted” Fujita (1920–1998) was a Japanese-American meteorologist who became a world-renowned expert on severe storms.

Tornado Research

After moving to the U.S. in 1953, Fujita extensively studied tornadoes, examining damage patterns to understand their destructive power.

Fujita Scale

In 1971, he created the Fujita Scale (F-Scale), which rates tornadoes from F0 (light damage) to F5 (incredible damage) based on the observed destruction. An updated version of his scale is still us...

Discovery of Microbursts

Fujita also discovered microbursts, powerful downdrafts from thunderstorms that pose a significant danger to aircraft. His research greatly improved air travel safety.

Legacy

Known as “Mr. Tornado,” Fujita demonstrated the critical role of careful observation in scientific discovery and how it can lead to life-saving knowledge.
