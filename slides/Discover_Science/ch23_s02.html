Chapter 23, Section 2 - Acids and Bases

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Acids

Chemicals typically characterized by a sour taste, the ability to dissolve certain metals, and a pH below 7. They are common in foods like citrus fruits and vinegar, and also in your stomach.

Bases

Chemicals typically characterized by a slippery feel, a bitter taste, and a pH above 7. Examples include soap, baking soda, and antacid tablets.

The pH Scale

A numerical scale ranging from 0 to 14 used to measure how acidic or basic a substance is. A pH below 7 indicates an acid, a pH above 7 indicates a base, and a pH of 7 is neutral.

Neutral Substances

Substances that are neither acidic nor basic, having a pH of exactly 7. Pure water is a common example of a neutral substance.

Important Terms

Acid

A chemical substance that often tastes sour, dissolves some metals, and has a pH value below 7. Examples include lemon juice and vinegar.

Base

A chemical substance that often feels slippery, tastes bitter, and has a pH value above 7. Examples include soap and baking soda.

Neutral

Describing a substance that is neither an acid nor a base, possessing a pH of 7. Pure water is a neutral substance.

Neutralization

A chemical reaction between an acid and a base that results in a solution becoming less acidic and less basic, moving closer to a neutral pH of 7.

Neutralization: When Acids Meet Bases

Antacid tablets

When you take an antacid for heartburn, the basic compounds in the tablet react with and neutralize the excess hydrochloric acid in your stomach, reducing discomfort.

Toothpaste

The mild bases in toothpaste neutralize the acids produced by bacteria in your mouth, which helps prevent cavities and protect your tooth enamel.
