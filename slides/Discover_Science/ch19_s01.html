Understanding Work and Machines

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Scientific Definition of Work

In science, work is performed only when a force causes an object to move over a distance. If there is no movement, no work is done, regardless of how much force is applied.

Two Components of Work

For work to occur, there must be both a **force** (a push or a pull) and **movement** of the object in the direction of the force.

Machines Make Work Easier

Machines are tools that help us do work more conveniently. They do not create energy or do the work for us; they simply modify how we apply force.

Force-Distance Trade-off

When a machine reduces the amount of force required, it always increases the distance over which that force must be applied. The total amount of work done remains the same.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Simple Machines

These are basic tools with few or no moving parts. There are six fundamental types of simple machines.

Compound Machines

These are more complex machines made by combining two or more simple machines working together.

Important Terms

Work (in science)

Using a force to move an object over a distance. No movement means no work is done.

Machine

A tool that makes work easier by changing the amount, direction, or speed of the force you apply.

Simple machine

A basic tool with few or no moving parts that makes work easier by changing the size or direction of a force.

Compound machine

A machine made of two or more simple machines working together.

What Is Work?

Work is using a force to move an object over a distance.

If the object does not move, no work is done.

Even if you push against a brick wall with all your strength and feel tired, scientifically, you have done no work because the wall did not move.

What Do Machines Do?

Machines do not create energy out of nothing.

Machines cannot do the work for you.

They simply change *how* you apply your force.
