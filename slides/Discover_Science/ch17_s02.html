Key Concepts

Temperature as a Measure of Heat Energy

Temperature indicates how much heat energy is present in the air. High temperatures mean more heat energy, making us feel warm, while low temperatures indicate less heat energy and make us feel cold.

Wind as Moving Air

Wind is air in motion, primarily caused by the Sun's uneven heating of Earth's surface. Warm air, being lighter, rises, and cooler air rushes in to take its place, creating wind.

Important Terms (1/3)

Temperature

A measure of how hot or cold something is; it indicates the amount of heat energy in the air.

Degrees Celsius (°C)

The unit of measurement used by scientists to quantify temperature.

Thermometer

An instrument used to measure temperature, typically by observing the expansion or contraction of a liquid inside a sealed tube.

Wind

Air that is moving from one place to another.

Wind Vane (Weather Vane)

An instrument that shows the direction from which the wind is blowing.

Important Terms (2/3)

Anemometer

An instrument that measures wind speed, often using small cups that spin in the wind.

Precipitation

Any form of water (liquid or solid) that falls from clouds to Earth's surface.

Rain

The most common form of precipitation, consisting of liquid water drops.

Snow

Precipitation composed of delicate, six-sided ice crystals (snowflakes) that form when water vapor freezes in cold air.

Sleet

Small pellets of ice that form when rain falls through a layer of freezing-cold air and turns to ice before hitting the ground.

Important Terms (3/3)

Hail

Balls or lumps of ice that form inside tall thunderstorm clouds as ice pellets are repeatedly pushed up and down, accumulating layers of ice.

Rain Gauge

A simple instrument used to collect and measure the amount of rain that has fallen, typically in millimeters.

Meteorologist

A scientist who studies weather and makes weather forecasts.

Precipitation — Water From the Sky

Definition

Precipitation is any form of water that falls from clouds to Earth's surface.

Formation

It occurs when water droplets or ice crystals within a cloud grow large enough that gravity overcomes air resistance, pulling them down.
