Staying Safe in Science: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Prioritize Safety

Always remember that safety is paramount in science to protect yourself and others.

Follow Instructions

The most fundamental rule is to listen carefully and always follow your teacher's directions.

Wear Protective Gear

Use safety goggles to shield your eyes from splashes and flying debris when instructed.

Chemical Handling

Never taste or smell unknown chemicals; always get teacher permission first to avoid illness.

Good Hygiene

Wash your hands before and after every science activity to maintain cleanliness and prevent germ spread.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Accident Protocol

Know what to do if a spill, cut, eye injury, or broken glass occurs – the immediate step is always to inform your teacher.

Maintain a Safe Environment

Keep your work area tidy, handle sharp objects carefully, put materials away, and avoid running or playing during activities.

Seek Adult Help

If you are ever unsure about anything, the most important safety rule is to ask an adult (teacher or parent/guardian) for help; there are no "silly" safety questions.

Home Science Safety

When doing experiments at home, always ask for permission, work in a cleanable space, keep young children away, and clean up afterwards.

Important Terms (1/2)

Safety Goggles

Protective eyewear designed to shield your eyes from chemicals, splashes, and flying objects.

Chemical

Any substance with a definite composition; in science, this often refers to substances used in experiments, some of which can be harmful.

Spill

An accidental overflow or release of a liquid or substance, requiring immediate attention and proper cleanup.

Hazard

Something that has the potential to cause harm or danger.

Guardian

A person legally responsible for the care and management of another person, especially a child; often a parent.

Important Terms (2/2)

Experiment

A scientific procedure undertaken to make a discovery, test a hypothesis, or demonstrate a known fact.

What to Do If Something Goes Wrong

Spill

Immediately tell your teacher. Do not try to clean up chemicals on your own, as they may require special handling.

Cut or Scrape

Inform your teacher right away. Even small cuts should be properly cleaned and bandaged to prevent infection.

Something in Your Eye

Alert your teacher immediately. Do not rub your eye, as this can make the situation worse. Your teacher will guide you on flushing your eye safely.

Broken Glass

Do not touch broken glass with your bare hands. Tell your teacher, who will safely clean up the fragments using appropriate tools.

Safety at Home

Get Permission

Always ask a parent or guardian for permission before you start any science activity or experiment at home.

Choose a Safe Work Area

Work in a space that is easy to clean, such as a kitchen table or counter, away from carpets or delicate furniture.

Keep Others Safe

Make sure younger brothers, sisters, or pets are kept away from your experiment area to prevent accidental interference or injury.

Clean Up Thoroughly

After you finish, clean up all materials and your workspace completely. Dispose of waste properly as instructed by your parent or guardian.
