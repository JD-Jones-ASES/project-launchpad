Important Terms (1/2)

Evaporation

The process by which liquid water changes into water vapor (a gas) and rises into the atmosphere, often driven by heat from the sun. (Example: Dewdrop lifting off the Pacific Ocean.)

Condensation

The process by which water vapor in the air cools down and changes back into tiny liquid droplets, often forming clouds. (Example: Dewdrop cooling and becoming a droplet in a cloud.)

Precipitation

Any form of water that falls from clouds to Earth's surface, such as rain, snow, sleet, or hail. (Example: Dewdrop falling as a raindrop.)

Collection

Groundwater

Water that has soaked into the ground and is stored beneath the Earth's surface in soil and rock layers. It can remain there for weeks, years, or centuries.

Important Terms (2/2)

Glacier

A large, slow-moving mass of ice formed over many years from compacted snow, typically found in mountainous or polar regions. Water can be stored in glaciers for thousands of years.

Transpiration

The process in which plants release water vapor through tiny openings (stomata) in their leaves into the atmosphere. It's like plants "sweating."

Every Drop Has a Story: Other Routes for Water

Groundwater

A raindrop can soak into the soil, becoming **groundwater**. It might stay underground for extended periods (weeks, years, even centuries) before resurfacing through a spring.

Glacier Ice

A snowflake can land on a mountain and become part of a **glacier**. Water can remain frozen in a glacier for thousands of years before the glacier melts and releases it into a river.

Ocean Recirculation

A raindrop might fall directly into the ocean and stay there, mixing with salt and minerals, until it eventually evaporates again.

Water Is a Traveler

Ancient Water

The water present on Earth today is the same water that has existed for billions of years. No new water is being created, and no old water is being lost.

Continuous Recycling

Nature constantly recycles all the water on Earth. Every glass of water we drink has been part of countless cycles.

Activities and Extensions

Creative Writing (Think About It)

Students can imagine themselves as a water drop and write a short narrative describing their journey through the water cycle, incorporating key vocabulary.

Transpiration Demonstration (Extension)

To demonstrate transpiration, place a plastic bag over a leafy branch on a sunny day. Water droplets will collect inside the bag, showing the water vapor released by the plant's leaves.
