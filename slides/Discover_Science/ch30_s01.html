Life Science in Urban Environments

Cities host diverse life forms that exhibit specific life cycles and remarkable adaptations (behavioral and structural) to thrive in urban ecosystems.

Earth Science and Urban Resources

Cities are built from and depend on both nonrenewable (e.g., metals, fossil fuels) and renewable (e.g., wood, water, solar energy) natural resources, making resource conservation critically important.

Chemistry of City Materials

Everything in a city, from buildings to the air we breathe, is composed of atoms bonded into molecules and compounds, following the principles of chemical composition and the conservation of matter.

Physics of City Power and Machines

Electricity and magnetism are fundamental to powering a city's infrastructure through generators and motors, while simple machines are integrated into everyday urban objects.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Conservation and Sustainability

The significant resource consumption in cities underscores the importance of practices like reducing, reusing, and recycling, as well as understanding environmental impacts like the greenhouse effect.

Important Terms (1/3)

Life Cycle

The series of changes in the life of an organism, including reproduction.

Adaptation

A trait that allows an organism to survive and reproduce in its environment.

Urban Ecosystem

An ecosystem found in a city or heavily populated area, characterized by human-made structures and modified natural processes.

Natural Resources

Materials or substances such as minerals, forests, water, and fertile land that occur in nature and can be used for economic gain.

Conservation

The protection of animals, plants, and natural resources, and the careful use of natural resources to prevent depletion.

Important Terms (2/3)

Atoms

The basic building blocks of all matter.

Molecules

A group of two or more atoms held together by chemical bonds.

Compounds

A substance formed when two or more chemical elements are chemically bonded together.

Conservation of Matter

The principle that matter cannot be created or destroyed, but can only change form; also known as the Law of Conservation of Mass.

Electricity

A form of energy resulting from the existence of charged particles (such as electrons or protons), either statically as an accumulation of charge or dynamically as a current.

Important Terms (3/3)

Magnetism

A physical phenomenon produced by the motion of electric charge, resulting in attractive and repulsive forces between objects.

Generator

A device that converts mechanical energy into electrical energy, typically by rotating a coil of wire in a magnetic field.

Electric Motor

A device that converts electrical energy into mechanical energy, typically through the interaction of magnetic fields and current-carrying conductors.

Simple Machines

Basic mechanical devices for applying a force and doing work (e.g., levers, inclined planes, wheels and axles, wedges, pulleys, screws).
