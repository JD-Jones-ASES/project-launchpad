Chapter 12, Section 4: Fresh Water, Salt Water, and Taking Care of Water Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Fresh Water vs. Salt Water Distribution

While water is abundant on Earth, about 97% is undrinkable salt water in the oceans. Only 3% is fresh water, with most of it locked in glaciers and ice caps.

Scarcity of Accessible Fresh Water

A mere fraction of fresh water is readily available in lakes and rivers for human use, highlighting its extreme scarcity compared to global demand.

Consequences of Drinking Salt Water

Ocean water's high salt content actively dehydrates the body, making it unsuitable for drinking.

Desalination as an Alternative

Desalination is a process to remove salt from ocean water, but it is expensive and requires significant energy, making it a limited solution.

Water Conservation

The careful use and protection of natural resources like water are essential to ensure they last for future generations, preventing depletion faster than natural recycling can replenish them.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Practical Conservation Methods

Simple actions in daily life, such as taking shorter showers, fixing leaks, and watering plants efficiently, can significantly reduce water waste.

Important Terms

Fresh water

Water that contains very little dissolved salt, found in lakes, rivers, groundwater, and glaciers.

Salt water

Water that contains a large amount of dissolved salts, found mostly in the oceans.

Desalination

The process of removing salt and other minerals from salt water to make it drinkable.

Conservation

The careful use and protection of natural resources so they last for the future.

Why Can't We Drink Salt Water?

Dehydration Effect

Drinking salt water makes you even thirstier. Your body has to use its own water to process and flush out the excess salt, leading to dehydration.

Desalination

This is a technological process used to remove salt and other minerals from salt water to make it drinkable.

Syukuro Manabe: Linking Water to Climate

Pioneering Climate Scientist

Syukuro Manabe (born 1931) is a Japanese-American scientist whose groundbreaking work revolutionized our understanding of Earth's climate system.

Early Climate Models

In the 1960s, Manabe developed one of the very first computer models of Earth's atmosphere.

Greenhouse Effect

His models demonstrated how atmospheric gases, particularly carbon dioxide (CO2), trap heat and contribute to warming the planet.
