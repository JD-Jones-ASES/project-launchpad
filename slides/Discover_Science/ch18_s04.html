Key Concepts (1/2)

Ubiquity of Chemical Changes

Chemical changes are constant and fundamental processes happening all around us, from cooking and natural phenomena to our biological functions.

Definition of Chemical Change

A process where one or more substances are transformed into new and different substances with new properties. These changes are generally hard or impossible to reverse.

Important Terms (1/3)

Chemical Change

A process that involves rearrangement of the structure of molecules or ions, resulting in the formation of new substances with different properties.

Baking Soda

Sodium bicarbonate, a chemical commonly used in baking that reacts with acids to produce carbon dioxide gas.

Carbon Dioxide (CO2)

A gas produced during baking (making dough rise), respiration, and released during combustion.

Proteins

Complex molecules essential for living organisms, which undergo chemical changes (like denaturation) when heated during cooking.

Caramelization

A chemical change that occurs when sugar is heated slowly, causing its molecules to break apart and recombine into new molecules, resulting in a golden-brown color and distinct flavor.

Important Terms (2/3)

Rust (Iron Oxide)

A flaky reddish-brown substance formed when iron reacts with oxygen and water, a common example of corrosion.

Photosynthesis

The process by which green plants and some other organisms use sunlight to synthesize foods from carbon dioxide and water, producing sugar and oxygen.

Decomposition

The process by which organic substances are broken down into simpler matter, often by microorganisms, returning nutrients to the environment.

Enzymes

Biological catalysts (special proteins) that speed up the rate of specific chemical reactions in living organisms, such as in digestion.

Law of Conservation of Matter

A fundamental scientific law stating that in an isolated system, matter can neither be created nor destroyed by chemical reactions or physical transformations. The total mass remains constant.

Important Terms (3/3)

Physical Change

A type of change in matter where the substance's chemical identity remains the same (e.g., melting, freezing, boiling). Usually reversible.

Antoine Lavoisier and the Law of Conservation of Matter

"Father of Modern Chemistry"

Antoine Lavoisier (1743–1794), a French scientist, is renowned for his systematic approach to chemistry.

Key Discovery

Lavoisier demonstrated that matter is never created or destroyed during a chemical change.

Law of Conservation of Matter

This principle states that atoms are simply rearranged during a chemical reaction; none are lost, and none are created.

Impact

Lavoisier's emphasis on careful measurement transformed chemistry from a speculative practice into an evidence-based science.
