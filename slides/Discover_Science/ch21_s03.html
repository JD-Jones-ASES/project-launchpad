Food Chains and Food Webs: How Energy Flows in Ecosystems

Key Concepts (1/2)

Food Chain

A simple, linear diagram showing a single pathway of energy transfer from one organism to the next, starting with a producer and ending with a top predator.

Food Web

A more complex and realistic diagram that shows many interconnected food chains within an ecosystem, illustrating that most organisms eat and are eaten by multiple types of organisms.

Energy Flow

Energy from the Sun is captured by producers (through photosynthesis) and then transferred unidirectionally (one way) to consumers.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Predator-Prey Relationships

A core interaction where one animal (the predator) hunts and eats another animal (the prey).

Decomposers

Organisms (like mushrooms and earthworms) that break down dead organic matter from all trophic levels, recycling nutrients back into the soil for producers to use.

Important Terms (1/2)

Food Chain

A diagram showing a single path of energy transfer from one organism to the next, beginning with a producer.

Food Web

A diagram that shows many connected food chains in an ecosystem, providing a more complete picture of who eats whom.

Primary Consumer

An animal that eats producers (plants); also known as a herbivore.

Secondary Consumer

An animal that eats primary consumers; often a carnivore or omnivore.

Top Predator

The consumer at the very end of a food chain; no other animal in that ecosystem regularly hunts it.

Important Terms (2/2)

Predator

An animal that hunts and eats other animals.

Prey

An animal that is hunted and eaten by a predator.

Producer

An organism (like a plant) that produces its own food, usually through photosynthesis, forming the base of a food chain.

Consumer

An organism that obtains energy by feeding on other organisms.

Decomposer

An organism that breaks down dead organic material, returning nutrients to the ecosystem (e.g., bacteria, fungi, earthworms).

What Is a Food Chain?

Analogy

Think of a food chain like a line of dominoes, where each organism passes energy to the next.

Starting Point

Every food chain begins with energy from the Sun, which producers (like plants) capture through photosynthesis.

Direction of Energy Flow

Arrows in a food chain diagram indicate the direction of energy transfer, meaning "gives energy to."

Links in the Chain (Trophic Levels)

Producer

An organism that makes its own food (e.g., grass, oak tree, wildflowers). These form the base of the food chain.

Primary Consumer

An animal that eats producers. They are herbivores.

Secondary Consumer

An animal that eats primary consumers. They can be carnivores (meat-eaters) or omnivores (eat both plants and animals).

Top Predator

The consumer at the very end of a food chain. No other animal in that specific food chain regularly hunts it.

Short Food Chains

Because so much energy is lost at each step, food chains are typically short, usually only three, four, or five links long. There isn't enough energy remaining to support many more trophic levels.

What Is a Food Web?

Definition

A food web is a diagram that shows *many interconnected food chains* within an ecosystem.

Complexity

Unlike a simple food chain, a food web looks like a "tangled net of arrows" because most organisms are part of multiple food chains simultaneously.

Realism

Food webs provide a more accurate picture of how energy flows and how interconnected life truly is in an ecosystem.

The Role of Decomposers

Breaking Down Dead Material

Decomposers (like mushrooms, earthworms, and bacteria) break down dead organisms (producers and consumers) and waste products.
