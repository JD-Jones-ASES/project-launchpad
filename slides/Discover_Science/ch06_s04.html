Chapter 6, Section 4: Photosynthesis: How Plants Make Food

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Photosynthesis is Plant Food Production

Plants are unique in their ability to make their own food (sugar/glucose) using simple ingredients: sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide.

The Photosynthesis "Recipe"

Sunlight + Water + Carbon Dioxide → Sugar (Glucose) + Oxygen. This process literally means "putting together with light."

Chlorophyll Captures Light

A green substance called chlorophyll, found in leaves, is responsible for absorbing the sun's energy, which powers photosynthesis.

Outputs of Photosynthesis

The plant keeps the sugar (glucose) for energy and growth, and releases oxygen into the air.

Plants Are Essential for Life

Plants produce the oxygen we breathe, are the foundation of nearly all food chains, provide shelter, prevent soil erosion, and supply numerous materials for human use.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Farming Transformed Human Society

The ability to grow plants intentionally (farming) allowed humans to settle, build communities, and develop civilizations.

Seeds Store Energy

Before a plant's leaves can photosynthesize, the seed itself contains stored food (energy) to fuel the initial growth of roots and stems.

Science and Plants Solve Problems

Scientists like George Washington Carver demonstrated how understanding plants can lead to innovations that benefit agriculture, communities, and the environment.

Important Terms (1/2)

Photosynthesis

The process by which plants use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to make glucose (sugar) and oxygen.

Glucose

A type of sugar that plants produce during photosynthesis; it serves as their primary source of energy and building material for growth.

Chlorophyll

The green pigment found in plant leaves that absorbs sunlight and is essential for photosynthesis.

Carbon Dioxide (CO2)

A gas from the atmosphere that plants take in through their leaves as an ingredient for photosynthesis.

Oxygen (O2)

A gas produced by plants during photosynthesis and released into the air; vital for the survival of most animals, including humans.

Important Terms (2/2)

Food Chain

A sequence showing how energy is transferred from one living organism to another, typically starting with producers (plants) and moving through consumers.

Farming

The practice of growing plants (and raising animals) on purpose for food, materials, or other products; it revolutionized human history.

Why Plants Are Essential for Life on Earth

Oxygen Producers

Every breath you take contains oxygen that plants have produced through photosynthesis. Without plants, the atmosphere would lack sufficient oxygen to support most animal life.

Foundation of Food Chains

Plants are the primary producers. They convert sunlight into food energy. Herbivores eat plants, carnivores eat herbivores, and so on. Without plants at the base, entire food chains would collapse.

Shelter and Habitat

Plants provide homes and protection for countless species, from birds nesting in trees to insects living in flowers, and large animals relying on forests for cover.

Soil Stabilization

Plant roots grow deep into the ground, holding soil particles together. This prevents valuable topsoil from being washed away by rain or blown away by wind, which is crucial for healthy ecosystems ...

Source of Materials

Plants provide us with a vast array of useful materials, including wood for building, cotton for clothing, rubber for tires, and countless medicines derived from their leaves, bark, and roots.

Observing Plant Growth: A Hands-On Activity

Materials

Clear plastic cup, dried bean seeds, paper towels, water, plastic wrap, rubber band, sunny windowsill.

Process

Bean seeds are tucked between a damp paper towel and the side of a clear cup, allowing observation of growth. The cup is covered to maintain humidity and placed in sunlight.

Observations

Students can watch the seed swell, a root emerge and grow downward, and then a stem push upward with tiny leaves unfolding.
