How Plant Parts Work Together Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Interdependence of Plant Parts

All plant parts are essential and work together; no single part can sustain the plant alone.

Roots: Anchoring and Absorption

Roots physically secure the plant in the soil and are responsible for taking in water and dissolved minerals through specialized root hairs.

Stem: Support and Transport

The stem holds the plant upright to reach sunlight and acts as a central transport system, moving water and minerals upwards via xylem and sugars downwards via phloem.

Leaves: Photosynthesis and Gas Exchange

Leaves are the primary sites for photosynthesis, the process of converting sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide into glucose (sugar) for energy. They also "breathe" through stomata, exchanging gases...

Reproduction: Flowers, Fruit, and Seeds

These parts form a reproductive team. Flowers attract pollinators, fruit protects developing seeds, and seeds contain the embryo for a new plant, ensuring species survival.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Plants as the Base of Food Chains

Plants are primary producers, creating their own food through photosynthesis, which forms the foundation of nearly all food chains on Earth.

Important Terms (1/4)

Roots

The underground parts of a plant that anchor it and absorb water and minerals from the soil.

Root Hairs

Tiny, hair-like extensions on roots that increase surface area for absorbing water and dissolved minerals.

Taproot

A single, thick main root that grows straight down (e.g., carrot, dandelion).

Fibrous Roots

Many thin roots that spread out in all directions (e.g., grass).

Stem

The main body or stalk of a plant that supports leaves, flowers, and fruit, and contains the transport system.

Important Terms (2/4)

Xylem (ZY-lem)

Tubes inside the stem that carry water and dissolved minerals *up* from the roots to the rest of the plant.

Phloem (FLOW-em)

Tubes inside the stem that carry sugars (food) *down* from the leaves to other parts of the plant.

Leaves

The primary organs of a plant responsible for photosynthesis and gas exchange.

Photosynthesis

The process by which green plants use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to create glucose (sugar) for energy.

Glucose

A type of sugar that plants produce during photosynthesis and use as their main source of energy.

Important Terms (3/4)

Stomata (sto-MAH-tah)

Tiny pores, usually on the underside of leaves, that allow for the exchange of gases (carbon dioxide in, oxygen out) and the release of water vapor.

Flowers

The reproductive structures of many plants, often brightly colored or fragrant to attract pollinators.

Pollinators

Animals (like bees, butterflies, hummingbirds) that transfer pollen from one flower to another, aiding in plant reproduction.

Pollen

Fine, powdery substance produced by flowers, essential for fertilization and seed development.

Fruit

The mature ovary of a flowering plant, which protects the developing seeds and aids in their dispersal.

Important Terms (4/4)

Seeds

Reproductive units of a plant, containing an embryo and stored food, capable of developing into a new plant under favorable conditions.

Reproduction

The biological process by which new individual organisms are produced from their parents.

Roots: The Anchor and the Straw

Taproots

A single, thick root that grows straight down, often storing food (e.g., carrots, dandelions).

Fibrous Roots

Many thin roots that spread out horizontally, forming a dense network (e.g., grass). Both types are effective at their jobs.
