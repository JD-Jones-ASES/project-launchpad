Thinking Like a Scientist: Understanding the Scientific Method

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

The Scientific Method as a Process

A systematic, seven-step approach (Observe, Question, Hypothesize, Test, Record, Conclude, Share) that provides a framework for logical inquiry and problem-solving.

Hypothesis Formation

A crucial step where an observation is turned into a testable prediction, often stated as "I think... because..."

Controlled Experimentation

The "Test It!" phase involves designing an experiment that carefully changes one variable (the one being tested) while keeping others constant, to accurately observe its effect.

Importance of Data Collection

Scientists meticulously record all results, using various formats like tables or drawings, to ensure accuracy and prevent details from being forgotten, forming the basis for reliable conclusions.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Iterative Nature of Science

The scientific method is not a linear path with a definitive end; conclusions often lead to new questions, prompting scientists to restart the process and deepen their understanding.

Everyday Application

The principles of the scientific method extend beyond formal scientific research, serving as a practical "thinking tool" for individuals to approach and solve problems in their daily lives.

Important Terms (1/2)

Scientific method

A step-by-step process scientists use to investigate questions, involving observing, questioning, hypothesizing, testing, recording, concluding, and sharing.

Observe

To use your senses (sight, hearing, touch, smell) to notice something interesting or puzzling in the world.

Ask a Question

To formulate a specific inquiry based on an observation, which the scientific method aims to answer.

Make a Hypothesis

To form a testable prediction or educated guess about the answer to your question, usually based on prior knowledge or reasoning.

Test It!

To design and conduct an experiment to see if your hypothesis is correct.

Important Terms (2/2)

Record Results

To meticulously write down or document all observations and data gathered during an experiment.

Draw a Conclusion

To analyze the recorded results and determine if the hypothesis was supported or refuted, summarizing what was learned.

Share What You Learned

To communicate your findings and conclusions to others, allowing for peer review, further learning, and replication.

Experiment

A test designed to answer a scientific question by carefully changing one specific factor (variable) and observing the outcome.

Conclusion

The answer or summary a scientist writes after studying their experimental results, stating whether the hypothesis was correct or incorrect and explaining what was discovered.

Applying the Scientific Method: The Paper Airplane Experiment (1/2)

Observation

Paper airplanes fly differently based on their shape.

Question

Does the shape of a paper airplane affect how far it flies?

Hypothesis

You'd predict which shape (e.g., a dart, a glider) would fly farthest and why.

Test

You'd fold three different paper airplane designs, throw each one three times from the same spot with the same force, and measure the distance flown.

Record

You'd create a table to log the distances for each throw and calculate the average for each design.

Conclude

You'd identify which design flew the farthest on average and determine if your hypothesis was correct.

Share

You'd discuss your findings with others.

Why the Scientific Method Matters in Everyday Life

Observe

You notice your keys are missing.

Question

Where are my keys?

Hypothesize

I think they might be on the kitchen counter because I last used them when I came home and put groceries away.

Test

You look on the kitchen counter.

Record/Conclude

If they're there, your hypothesis was correct. If not, you form a new hypothesis and continue testing.

The first step is Observe. It involves using your senses (sight, hearing, touch, smell) to notice something interesting in the world around you.

After making an observation, what is the next step in the scientific method?

The next step is to Ask a Question, turning your observation into a specific question that can be investigated.

What is a hypothesis in the scientific method?

A hypothesis is a prediction you can test, an educated guess about the answer to your question.

What happens during the 'Test It!' step of the scientific method?

During 'Test It!', you design and conduct an experiment to test your hypothesis.
