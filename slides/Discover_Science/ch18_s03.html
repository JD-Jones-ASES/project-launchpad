Clues That a Chemical Change Happened: A Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Chemical Change

A process where one or more substances are converted into different substances with new properties. This is the fundamental outcome we are looking for.

Five Main Clues

Scientists look for a change in color, the formation of gas bubbles, the production or absorption of heat/light, a new smell, or a change that is hard to reverse.

Distinguishing from Physical Changes

Many physical changes (like boiling water or mixing paint) can exhibit similar signs (bubbles, color change), so it's crucial to consider if a *new substance* has formed.

Energy Changes

Chemical reactions often involve the release (like burning) or absorption (making things feel cold) of energy in the form of heat or light.

Irreversibility

Most chemical changes are difficult or impossible to reverse, serving as a strong indicator that new chemical bonds have formed.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Multiple Clues

Observing several clues simultaneously provides stronger evidence that a chemical change has occurred, rather than relying on a single observation.

Wafting

A safe technique for smelling unknown substances by gently fanning air towards the nose.

Important Terms (1/2)

Chemical Change

A change that results in the formation of new chemical substances with different properties.

Physical Change

A change in the form or appearance of a substance, but not in its chemical composition (no new substances are formed).

Rusting

A common example of a chemical change where iron reacts with oxygen and water to form iron oxide, typically reddish-brown.

Tarnishing

A chemical reaction, often seen on silver, where the metal reacts with sulfur compounds in the air to form a dark layer.

Wafting

A safety procedure for smelling chemicals by gently waving a hand over the opening of a container to direct fumes toward the nose, preventing direct inhalation.

Important Terms (2/2)

Carbon Dioxide (CO2)

A gas often produced during chemical reactions, such as the reaction between vinegar and baking soda, or in fermentation/baking.

Exothermic Reaction

A chemical reaction that releases energy, often in the form of heat and/or light (e.g., burning a match).

Endothermic Reaction

A chemical reaction that absorbs energy from its surroundings, often causing the temperature of the surroundings to drop (e.g., some cold packs).

Clue 3: Heat or Light Is Produced (or Absorbed)

Energy Release

Reactions that give off heat or light are called *exothermic*.

Energy Absorption

Some reactions absorb heat from their surroundings, making the container feel cold to the touch. These are called *endothermic* reactions.
