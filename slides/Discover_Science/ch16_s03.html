Chapter 16, Section 3 Study Guide: Muscles and Digestion

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Muscles are Movers

Muscles contract (get shorter and thicker) to pull on bones, causing movement.

Muscles Work in Pairs

Muscles can only pull, never push. Therefore, they work in opposing pairs; when one muscle contracts, its partner relaxes, allowing for movement in both directions (e.g., biceps and triceps).

Voluntary vs. Involuntary Muscles

Digestion's Purpose

The digestive system breaks down food into tiny pieces (nutrients) that the body can use for energy, growth, and repair.

The Digestive Journey

Food travels through a specific path, with each organ playing a crucial role in breaking down food and absorbing nutrients.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Nutrient Absorption

The small intestine is the primary site where nutrients from digested food pass into the bloodstream to be delivered to cells throughout the body.

Important Terms (1/2)

Muscle

A body tissue that can contract (get shorter) to produce movement.

Tendon

A strong, flexible cord that connects a muscle to a bone.

Voluntary muscle

A muscle you can control on purpose, like the muscles in your arms and legs.

Involuntary muscle

A muscle that works on its own, without you choosing to use it. The heart is the best example.

Digestion

The process of breaking down food into nutrients your body can absorb and use.

Important Terms (2/2)

Nutrients

Useful substances in food—like vitamins, minerals, proteins, and sugars—that your body needs to grow, repair itself, and have energy.

The Muscular System (1/2)

More than 600 Muscles

Your body contains over 600 muscles, which are essential for all movement.

Connection to Bones

Muscles are attached to bones by tough, rope-like bands called **tendons**.
