Temperature: How Hot or Cold - A Study Guide

Key Concepts

Temperature vs. Subjective Feelings

Scientists use temperature as an objective numerical measurement (in degrees) because feelings like "cold" or "warm" are personal and can vary between individuals.

The Celsius Scale

The standard unit for temperature in science is degrees Celsius (°C). This scale is defined by two easily observable points: 0°C, where water freezes, and 100°C, where water boils.

Important Terms (1/2)

Temperature

A measurement that tells us how hot or cold something is, typically using a thermometer and expressed in units called degrees.

Degrees Celsius (°C)

The standard unit of temperature measurement used in science, part of the Celsius scale.

Celsius Scale

A temperature scale where 0°C is the freezing point of water and 100°C is the boiling point of water, with 100 equal divisions (degrees) between them.

Thermometer

A scientific tool used to measure temperature, often consisting of a thin tube with a liquid that expands and contracts.

Expands

To take up more space; when the liquid in a thermometer gets warmer, it expands and rises.

Important Terms (2/2)

Contracts

To take up less space; when the liquid in a thermometer gets cooler, it contracts and sinks.

Freezing Point

The temperature at which a liquid turns into a solid; for water, this is 0°C.

Boiling Point

The temperature at which a liquid turns into a gas (vaporizes); for water, this is 100°C.

Anders Celsius (1701–1744)

The Swedish astronomer and scientist who created the Celsius temperature scale.

Why Temperature Matters in Science

Life Science

Temperature dictates where different plants and animals can survive. Polar bears need cold climates, while cacti thrive in deserts. Body temperature is also vital for the health of many organisms.

Earth Science

Temperature is a major driver of weather patterns and the **water cycle**. For instance, when water warms up enough, it evaporates, forming clouds that eventually lead to precipitation.

Chemistry

Physics

Temperature is directly linked to **energy**. The hotter something is, the more kinetic energy its tiny particles possess, causing them to move faster and vibrate more.
