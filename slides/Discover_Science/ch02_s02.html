Key Concepts (1/2)

Earth's "Spheres"

Earth's main parts are called "spheres" because of its spherical, ball-like shape.

Lithosphere: The Rocky Layer

This is the solid outer shell of Earth, comprising all landforms, the ground beneath our feet, mountains, and even the ocean floor. Its name comes from the Greek word "litho," meaning "rock."

Interconnectedness of Spheres

The three spheres are not isolated but constantly interact and overlap, influencing each other in dynamic ways, such as water (hydrosphere) falling through air (atmosphere) onto land (lithosphere).

Key Concepts (2/2)

Greek Roots

The names of the spheres derive from Greek words that directly describe their primary composition or state (rock, water, vapor).

Important Terms (1/2)

Sphere

In the context of Earth, a major division or part of the planet, named for Earth's spherical shape.

Lithosphere

The solid, rocky outer layer of Earth, including all landforms like continents, mountains, and the ocean floor.

Hydrosphere

All the water found on Earth, encompassing oceans, lakes, rivers, glaciers, groundwater, and atmospheric water vapor.

Atmosphere

The protective layer of gases, primarily nitrogen and oxygen, that surrounds Earth and extends hundreds of kilometers above its surface.

"Litho-"

A Greek prefix meaning "rock."

Important Terms (2/2)

"Hydro-"

A Greek prefix meaning "water."

"Atmo-"

A Greek prefix meaning "vapor" or "gas."

Glaciers

Large masses of ice that move slowly over land, formed from compacted snow. (Important as they hold most of Earth's freshwater).

Water Vapor

Water in its gaseous state, invisible, and found in the atmosphere.

The Lithosphere: Earth's Solid Foundation

Definition

The lithosphere is the rigid, rocky outer layer of Earth. It is the solid ground we live on and all the rock formations that make up our planet's surface and subsurface.

Etymology

The term "lithosphere" comes from the Greek word "**litho**," which means "rock." This etymology directly points to its primary composition.

It is the firm, unmoving base for many life forms and processes, though it undergoes very slow changes due to geological activity. To help remember, think of "litho" sounding like "little rocks."

The Hydrosphere: Earth's Water Systems

Definition

The hydrosphere encompasses all the water on Earth, in all its forms and locations. It is crucial for life and plays a vital role in Earth's climate and weather patterns.

Etymology

The term "hydrosphere" originates from the Greek word "**hydro**," meaning "water." This connection is evident in related words like "hydrant" (a source of water).

Importance

Water is essential for all known life and is a powerful agent of erosion and shaping the lithosphere.
