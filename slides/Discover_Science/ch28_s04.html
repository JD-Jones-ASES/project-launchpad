Key Concepts

States of Matter (Atomic View)

The physical state of matter (solid, liquid, gas) is determined by how fast its atoms or molecules are moving and how closely packed they are.

Heating and Cooling (Phase Changes)

Temperature changes affect the energy and movement of atoms, leading to changes in the state of matter.

Importance of Conservation of Matter

This law allows scientists to predict the products and quantities in chemical reactions, as no atoms are lost or gained. It underpins our understanding of chemical changes.

Important Terms (1/2)

Solid

A state of matter where atoms or molecules are packed closely together and vibrate in fixed positions, giving it a definite shape and volume.

Liquid

A state of matter where atoms or molecules are close together but can slide past one another, allowing it to flow and take the shape of its container while maintaining a definite volume.

Gas

A state of matter where atoms or molecules move quickly and are far apart, spreading out to fill any container and having no definite shape or volume.

Melting

The process by which a solid changes into a liquid due to the addition of heat energy.

Boiling

The process by which a liquid changes into a gas due to the addition of heat energy.

Important Terms (2/2)

Condensing

The process by which a gas changes into a liquid due to the removal of heat energy (cooling).

Freezing

The process by which a liquid changes into a solid due to the removal of heat energy (cooling).

Conservation of Matter (Law of Conservation of Mass)

The scientific principle that states matter cannot be created or destroyed, only rearranged, during any physical or chemical change. The total mass of substances involved remains constant.
