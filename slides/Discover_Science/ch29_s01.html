Static Electricity: Understanding Charge and Its Effects

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Electric Charge

A fundamental property of the tiny particles inside atoms (protons and electrons). There are two types: positive (+) and negative (-).

Protons and Electrons

Protons carry a positive charge and are found in the atom's nucleus. Electrons carry a negative charge and orbit the nucleus.

Neutral Objects

Most objects are neutral because they have an equal number of protons and electrons, causing their positive and negative charges to balance out.

Static Electricity Defined

It is the accumulation of electric charge on the surface of an object, where the charges remain "static" or in one place rather than flowing.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Rule of Charges

Opposite charges (positive and negative) attract (pull toward each other), while like charges (two positives or two negatives) repel (push away from each other).

Real-world Manifestations

Static electricity is responsible for everyday phenomena like hair standing on end, clothes clinging together, and the surprising zap from a doorknob.

Lightning

A massive and dramatic natural display of static electricity, where charges build up in storm clouds and release as a giant spark between the cloud and the ground, or between clouds.

Practical Applications

Static electricity is harnessed in technologies such as spray painting, photocopiers, and air purifiers.

Important Terms (1/2)

Electric Charge

A basic property of protons (+) and electrons (-).

Proton

A subatomic particle found in the nucleus of an atom, carrying a positive electric charge.

Electron

A subatomic particle that orbits the nucleus of an atom, carrying a negative electric charge.

Neutral

Describing an object or atom that has an equal number of positive and negative charges, resulting in no overall electric charge.

Static Electricity

The buildup of electric charge on the surface of an object, where the charges are not flowing.

Important Terms (2/2)

Attract

To pull toward each other; this occurs between opposite electric charges.

Repel

To push away from each other; this occurs between like electric charges.

Lightning

A sudden, visible discharge of electricity that occurs during a thunderstorm, resulting from a massive buildup of static charge.

Thunder

The sound caused by the rapid expansion of air heated by a lightning bolt.

The Basics of Static Electricity

Protons

, located in the center of the atom, carry a positive charge (+).

Electrons

, which orbit around the atom's center, carry a negative charge (-).

How Objects Become Electrically Charged

Example: Rubbing a balloon on your hair

The Rule of Charges: Attract and Repel

Opposite charges attract

A positively charged object and a negatively charged object will pull toward each other.

Like charges repel

Two positively charged objects will push each other away. Similarly, two negatively charged objects will push each other away.

Static Electricity in Everyday Life

Sock Shuffle

Walking across a carpet in socks can rub electrons from the carpet onto your body, making you negatively charged. When you touch a metal doorknob, the excess electrons jump from you to the doorknob...

Laundry Cling

Clothes tumbling in a dryer rub against each other, causing electrons to transfer. This leaves some clothes positively charged and others negatively charged, making them stick together.

Clingy Plastic Wrap

Peeling plastic wrap off the roll creates static charges, which allow it to cling tightly to bowls or even to itself.

Applications of Static Electricity

Spray Painting

Factories use static charges to ensure paint particles are evenly distributed and stick well to surfaces like cars and furniture.

Photocopiers and Laser Printers

These devices use static charges to precisely place toner (ink powder) onto paper, forming images and text.

Air Purifiers

Some air purifiers employ static charges to attract and remove dust, pollen, and other tiny particles from the air.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is static electricity?

Static electricity is a buildup of electric charge on the surface of an object.

What is electric charge?

Electric charge is a basic property of the tiny particles inside atoms. There are two kinds: positive (+) and negative (-).

What type of charge do protons carry and where are they found in an atom?

Protons carry a positive charge (+) and are found in the center of the atom.

What type of charge do electrons carry and where are they found in an atom?

Electrons carry a negative charge (-) and zip around the outside of the atom.

What does it mean for an atom to be 'neutral'?

A neutral atom has the same number of protons and electrons, so its positive and negative charges balance out, resulting in no overall charge.

What happens to an object that gains extra electrons when rubbed?

The object that gains extra electrons becomes negatively charged.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What happens to an object that loses electrons when rubbed?

The object that loses electrons becomes positively charged.

What does the word 'static' mean in the term 'static electricity'?

The word 'static' means staying in one place, indicating that the charges sit on the object instead of flowing.

When you rub a balloon on your hair, which object gains electrons and what charge does it become?

The balloon gains electrons and becomes negatively charged.

When you rub a balloon on your hair, what charge does your hair acquire and why might it stand up?

Your hair loses electrons and becomes positively charged. It stands up because each strand has the same (positive) charge, and like charges repel.

What is the fundamental rule about how electric charges interact?

Opposite charges attract, and like charges repel.

What does it mean for electric charges to 'attract'?

To attract means to pull toward each other, as opposite charges (positive and negative) do.
