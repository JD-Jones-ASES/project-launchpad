Invertebrate Variety and Animal Adaptations

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Invertebrate Dominance

The vast majority (95%) of animal species are invertebrates, meaning they lack a backbone.

Major Invertebrate Groups

Key groups include Insects (most diverse), Arachnids, Crustaceans, Mollusks, Worms, and Cnidarians, each with distinct characteristics.

Adaptation for Survival

An adaptation is any body feature or behavior that helps an animal survive by finding food, staying safe, or reproducing in its specific environment.

Evolutionary Development

Adaptations develop gradually over many generations through natural selection.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Charles Henry Turner's Contributions

A pioneering African American scientist who demonstrated that insects are capable of learning, challenging earlier beliefs that their actions were solely instinctual.

Important Terms (1/3)

Invertebrates

Animals that do not have a backbone.

Insects

The largest group of animals; characterized by six legs, three body parts (head, thorax, abdomen), and often wings.

Arachnids

A group of invertebrates with eight legs and two body parts, including spiders, scorpions, and ticks.

Crustaceans

Primarily aquatic invertebrates often with hard exoskeletons, such as crabs, lobsters, and shrimp.

Mollusks

Soft-bodied invertebrates, many of which produce a shell, including snails, clams, octopuses, and squid.

Important Terms (2/3)

Worms

Long, soft-bodied invertebrates with no legs, like earthworms.

Cnidarians

Water-dwelling invertebrates with stinging cells, such as jellyfish, sea anemones, and coral.

Adaptation

A body feature or behavior that helps a living thing survive in its environment.

Camouflage

An adaptation where an animal blends in with its surroundings to avoid predators or ambush prey.

Blubber

A thick layer of fat found under the skin of some marine mammals, providing insulation against cold.

Important Terms (3/3)

Behavior Adaptation

An action or pattern of behavior that helps an animal survive, distinct from a physical body part.

Migration

The seasonal movement of animals from one region to another, typically in search of food or more favorable climates.

Hibernation

A state of minimal activity and metabolic depression in endotherms, characterized by lower body temperature, slower breathing, and lower metabolic rate, typically during winter months.

Body Adaptations (1/2)

Camouflage

The ability to blend in with surroundings, making an animal hard to see.

Sharp Claws and Teeth

Used for hunting, defense, or processing food/materials.

Thick Fur or Blubber

Provides insulation against cold temperatures.

Wings

Enable flight, which is crucial for escaping predators, migrating, or finding food.

Bright Colors

Often serve as a warning signal to potential predators, indicating toxicity or danger.

Body Adaptations (2/2)

Shells and Armor

Hard outer coverings that provide protection from physical harm and predators.

Behavior Adaptations

Migration

The long-distance seasonal movement of animals, typically in response to changes in food availability, climate, or breeding grounds.

Hibernation

A state of inactivity and metabolic depression in endotherms during cold periods, allowing them to conserve energy when food is scarce.

Hunting in Groups

Cooperating to catch prey that would be too large or difficult for an individual animal.

Playing Dead

A defensive behavior where an animal feigns death to deter a predator, hoping the predator will lose interest.

Building Shelters

Constructing structures for protection, nesting, or storage.
