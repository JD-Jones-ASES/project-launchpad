The Rock Cycle Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Continuous Transformation

Rocks are not permanent; they constantly change from one type to another through various processes over geological timescales.

Three Rock Types

Interconnectedness

Any type of rock (igneous, sedimentary, or metamorphic) can be transformed into any other type, or even back into its original type, through different pathways in the cycle.

Key Processes

The cycle is driven by processes such as weathering, erosion, deposition, compaction, cementation, burial, heating, pressure, melting, and cooling.

Key Concepts (2/2)

No Single Start or End

The cycle is truly circular, meaning there is no fixed beginning or end point, and rocks can enter or exit various stages at any time.

Important Terms (1/3)

Rock Cycle

The continuous process by which rocks are created, transformed, destroyed, and reformed, changing from one type to another over very long periods of time.

Igneous Rock

Rock formed from the cooling and solidification of molten rock (magma beneath the surface or lava on the surface).

Sedimentary Rock

Rock formed from the accumulation and compaction of sediments (fragments of other rocks, minerals, or organic matter) that have been transported and deposited.

Metamorphic Rock

Rock that has undergone a change in its structure, texture, or mineral composition due to intense heat, pressure, or chemical processes, typically deep within the Earth's crust.

Magma

Molten rock material found beneath the Earth's surface. When magma erupts onto the surface, it is called lava.

Important Terms (2/3)

Weathering

The process by which rocks are broken down into smaller pieces (sediments) by physical (e.g., wind, ice, water) or chemical (e.g., dissolution) means.

Erosion

The process by which weathered rock fragments (sediments) are transported away from their original location by agents like wind, water, or glaciers.

Deposition

The process by which sediments are laid down or settle in a new location, often in layers.

Compaction

The process by which sediments are pressed together under the weight of overlying layers, reducing pore space.

Cementation

The process in which dissolved minerals precipitate in the spaces between sediment particles, binding them together to form solid sedimentary rock.

Important Terms (3/3)

Heat and Pressure

Intense forces deep within the Earth that can cause existing rocks to change their mineral structure and texture, leading to the formation of metamorphic rocks.

Multiple Pathways and Interconnections

Any rock can melt

Any existing rock type—igneous, sedimentary, or metamorphic—can be subjected to sufficient heat to melt and become magma, which then cools to form new igneous rock.

Any rock can be weathered

Direct transformation

While often going through a sedimentary stage, igneous or metamorphic rocks can also be directly altered into different metamorphic rocks if subjected to new conditions of heat and pressure.

Cross-Connections to Other Sciences

Chemistry

Involves matter changing state (solid to liquid and back) and chemical reactions (e.g., mineral formation, weathering).

Physics

Deals with forces (pressure), energy (heat driving melting and metamorphism), and the mechanics of erosion and transport.

Earth Science

Directly connects to topics like soil formation (weathering of rock creates soil) and the processes of erosion and deposition that shape landscapes.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the rock cycle?

The continuous process in which rocks change from one type to another over very long periods of time.

True or False: Rocks stay the same type forever.

False. Rocks change from one type to another over very long periods through the rock cycle.

What is the typical timeframe for changes within the rock cycle?

Thousands, millions, or even billions of years.

Does the rock cycle have a single starting point or end?

No, the rock cycle is continuous and has no single starting or ending point.

Can any type of rock become any other type in the rock cycle?

Yes, any type of rock can transform into any other type.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How does sedimentary rock transform into metamorphic rock?

Sedimentary rock gets buried deep underground, where heat and pressure change it into metamorphic rock.

Explain how metamorphic rock can become igneous rock.

If metamorphic rock gets hot enough, it melts into magma. When this magma cools, it becomes igneous rock.

What general process forms igneous rock from any other rock type?

Any rock type can melt into magma if it gets hot enough. This magma then cools and solidifies to form new igneous rock.

A mountain made of granite (igneous rock) is weathered. What type of rock might form from its sediments?

Sedimentary rock, such as sandstone, could form from the compacted sediments of the granite.

What natural forces are primarily responsible for breaking down existing rocks into smaller pieces?

Wind, rain, and ice (weathering and erosion).

What are the two primary factors that change sedimentary rock into metamorphic rock?

Heat and pressure.
