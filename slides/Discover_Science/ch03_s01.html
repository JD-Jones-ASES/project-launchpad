Chapter 3, Section 1: What Is Matter? Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Definition of Matter

Matter is the physical substance of which everything in the universe is made. For something to be considered matter, it must have mass and take up space (occupy volume).

Definition of Mass

Mass is a measure of the amount of matter an object contains. It is typically measured in units like grams. A heavier object has more mass than a lighter object.

Matter is Ubiquitous

Everything around us, from solid objects like a desk or a pencil, to liquids like water, and even gases like the air we breathe, is composed of matter.

Proving Air is Matter

While invisible, air demonstrably takes up space (fills a balloon) and has mass (an inflated balloon is slightly heavier than an empty one). These two properties confirm that air is matter.

Matter vs. Energy

Not everything is matter. Forms of energy, such as light, heat, or sound, do not have mass and do not take up space, therefore they are not classified as matter.

Important Terms

Matter

Any substance that has mass and takes up space. It is the material from which all physical objects are made.

Mass

A fundamental property of matter, representing the amount of "stuff" an object contains. It is a measure of an object's resistance to acceleration and is commonly measured in grams.

What Is Matter?

Physical Presence

When you pick up a pencil, you can feel its weight and observe that it occupies a certain amount of space. This illustrates the two key characteristics of matter.

Air as Matter

Even seemingly empty space is often filled with matter. Air, though difficult to feel, also takes up space.

Defining Matter and Mass

Examples of Matter

Your desk, your shoes, the water you drink, and even the air in the room are all examples of matter because they all have mass and take up space.

Is Air Matter?

Conclusion

Since air has both mass and takes up space, it fits the definition of matter.

Think About It: Is Light Matter?

Answer

No, light is not matter.
