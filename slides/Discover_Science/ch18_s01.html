Chapter 18, Section 1

Understanding Physical Changes

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Definition of Physical Change

A change that affects the size, shape, or state of matter, but crucially, does not alter the fundamental composition of the substance. The material stays chemically identical.

Examples of Physical Changes

Common activities like cutting, melting, freezing, dissolving, folding, and breaking are all physical changes because they don't produce a new type of matter.

No New Substance

The most important takeaway is that after a physical change, you still have the original material. For instance, melted ice is still water, just in a liquid state.

Reversibility

Many physical changes can be reversed, serving as a helpful clue that no new substance was created. You can freeze melted water, or recover dissolved sugar by evaporating the water.

Connection to States of Matter

Changes in state (solid to liquid, liquid to solid, etc.) are classic examples of physical changes, as the substance (e.g., water) remains the same despite its physical form.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Connection to Separating Mixtures

Techniques like filtering and evaporation, used to separate mixtures, are also physical changes because they don't alter the individual components of the mixture.

Important Terms (1/2)

Physical Change

A change in the size, shape, or state of matter that does not create a new substance; the material's chemical identity remains the same.

State of Matter

One of the distinct forms that matter can take, primarily solid, liquid, or gas. Changes between these states are physical changes.

Solid

A state of matter characterized by a definite shape and a definite volume.

Liquid

A state of matter characterized by an indefinite shape (takes the shape of its container) but a definite volume.

Gas

A state of matter characterized by an indefinite shape and an indefinite volume (expands to fill its container).

Everyday Examples of Physical Changes (1/2)

Cutting

When you slice a banana, you change its shape and create smaller pieces. Each piece, however, is still banana. The same applies to cutting fabric or chopping vegetables.

Melting

A wax candle melting near a flame illustrates a change in state from solid wax to liquid wax. The wax itself hasn't transformed into a new substance; it can harden back into solid wax if cooled.

Everyday Examples of Physical Changes (2/2)

Breaking

Dropping a glass and having it shatter into many pieces changes its size and shape dramatically. Yet, every single piece is still glass. No new material has been formed from the original glass.

The Big Clue: Reversibility

• Melted ice can be frozen again.

• Dissolved sugar can be recovered by evaporating the water.

• Crumpled paper can be flattened out (though it might not be perfectly smooth again, it's still paper).

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the definition of a physical change?

A physical change is a change in the size, shape, or state of matter that does not create a new substance. The material stays the same.

What happens to the chemical identity of a substance during a physical change?

The chemical identity of the substance does not change; the material remains the same. No new substance is created.

Give two common examples of physical changes from everyday life.

Tearing a sheet of paper in half and an ice cube melting into water are two common examples.

When a substance changes from a solid to a liquid, but is still the same material (e.g., ice to water), what type of change has occurred?

A physical change, specifically a change in the state of matter.

You slice a banana into pieces. Is this a physical change? Explain why.

Yes, it is a physical change. Cutting changes the shape and size of the banana, but each piece is still banana; no new substance is formed.

A wax candle melts near a flame. Why is this considered a physical change?

The wax changes from a solid to a liquid, but its chemical composition remains the same; it is still wax.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What type of physical change occurs when juice is poured into a mold and frozen to make a popsicle?

Freezing. The juice changes from a liquid to a solid state, but it is still juice.

Why is dissolving sugar in water classified as a physical change, even though the sugar seems to disappear?

Even though the sugar seems to vanish, it is still present in the water. Its chemical identity hasn't changed, and it can be recovered.

What is a helpful clue to determine if a change is physical?

In most cases, physical changes are reversible. You can often get the original substance back or restore its previous state.

How can you reverse the physical change of ice melting?

You can reverse it by putting the water back in the freezer to refreeze it into ice.

How can you prove that sugar is still present after it dissolves in water?

You can let the water evaporate (e.g., on a sunny windowsill), and the sugar crystals will reappear.

You crumple a piece of paper. Is this a physical change? Why or why not?

Yes, it is a physical change. The paper's shape changes, but it is still paper. Its chemical composition has not been altered.
