Continents, Oceans, and Poles

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Earth's Surface Composition

Earth's surface is primarily composed of large land areas called continents and huge bodies of saltwater known as oceans.

Seven Continents

There are seven distinct continents, each with unique geographical and biological features. These are Africa, Antarctica, Asia, Australia, Europe, North America, and South America.

Five Oceans

Earth is covered by five major oceans, which are interconnected bodies of saltwater. These include the Pacific, Atlantic, Indian, Southern, and Arctic Oceans.

The Poles

The Earth has two extreme points, the North Pole and the South Pole, which are characterized by extremely cold temperatures due to receiving very little direct sunlight.

Earth's Spheres

The Earth can be understood in terms of interacting "spheres": the lithosphere (land/rock), hydrosphere (water), and atmosphere (air).

Important Terms (1/2)

Continent

A very large area of land.

Ocean

A huge body of salt water.

North Pole

The northernmost point on Earth, located at the very top.

South Pole

The southernmost point on Earth, located at the very bottom.

Lithosphere

The solid, outer part of Earth, including the crust and upper mantle (represented by sand/dirt in the model).

Important Terms (2/2)

Hydrosphere

All the water on Earth's surface, such as oceans, lakes, and rivers (represented by water in the model).

Atmosphere

The layer of gases surrounding Earth (represented by moving air in the model).

Mid-Atlantic Ridge

An enormous underwater mountain range running down the middle of the Atlantic Ocean, discovered by Marie Tharp.

Plate Tectonics

The scientific theory that Earth's outer layer is made up of large, moving pieces called plates, which helps explain the movement of continents and geological events.
