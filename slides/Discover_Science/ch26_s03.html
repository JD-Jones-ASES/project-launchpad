Key Concepts

Adaptation Defined

An adaptation is any characteristic (physical or behavioral) that helps an organism survive, find food, stay safe, and reproduce in its specific environment.

Structural Adaptations

These are physical body features or parts of an organism that aid in its survival. Examples include fur color, beak shape, presence of spines, or specialized organs like gills.

Habitat Specificity

Adaptations are always tailored to the challenges and opportunities presented by an organism's particular habitat. An adaptation beneficial in one environment might be detrimental in another.

Important Terms (2/2)

Camouflage

The ability of an organism to blend in with its surroundings, either to hide from predators or to sneak up on prey.

Being active primarily during the night, often as an adaptation to avoid daytime heat, competition, or predators.

Adaptations Match the Habitat

Desert Challenges

Deserts have very little water. Adaptations like a cactus storing water in thick stems or animals having specialized kidneys to conserve water are essential.

Arctic Challenges

The Arctic faces extreme cold. Polar bears' thick fur and a layer of fat are perfect for insulation, but these would cause overheating in a tropical climate.

Ocean Challenges

The primary challenge in the ocean for land animals is breathing underwater. Fish overcome this with gills that extract oxygen from the water.

Forest Challenges

In dense forests, competition for sunlight is intense. Vines adapt by climbing tall trees to reach the canopy where sunlight is abundant.

Grassland Challenges

Open grasslands offer little cover, making animals vulnerable to predators. Zebras' stripes provide camouflage in tall grass, making it difficult for predators to single out individuals in a herd.
