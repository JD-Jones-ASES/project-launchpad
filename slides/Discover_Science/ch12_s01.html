Chapter 12, Section 1: Where Is Earth's Water? Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

The Hydrosphere

All the water on Earth, covering about 71% of its surface, is collectively known as the hydrosphere.

Diversity of Water

Earth's water exists in different forms—salty or fresh, liquid, solid (ice), or gas (water vapor).

Oceans as Major Reservoirs

Oceans hold approximately 97% of all Earth's water, making them the largest water reservoir. This water is salty due to dissolved minerals like sodium chloride.

Glaciers and Ice Caps - Fresh Water Storage

About 2% of Earth's water is locked up in glaciers and ice caps. These massive ice formations represent the largest store of fresh water on the planet.

Lakes, Rivers, and Streams

These surface bodies of water contain fresh water but make up less than 1% (specifically 0.03%) of Earth's total water, making them a tiny fraction despite being commonly seen.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Groundwater - Hidden Fresh Water

A significant amount of fresh water (0.7%) is found underground in spaces between rocks and soil, called groundwater, which is a crucial source for human use.

Water Vapor in the Atmosphere

A tiny fraction of water (0.001%) exists as an invisible gas called water vapor in the atmosphere, forming clouds and contributing to humidity.

Important Terms (1/2)

Hydrosphere

All the water on Earth, including oceans, lakes, rivers, glaciers, groundwater, and water vapor in the atmosphere.

Ocean

A very large body of salt water that covers much of Earth's surface.

Salt water

Water that contains dissolved minerals, especially sodium chloride, giving it a salty taste; found primarily in oceans.

Glacier

A huge mass of ice that forms on land when snow builds up and packs together over many years, slowly turning into ice.

Fresh water

Water that is not salty; found in glaciers, ice caps, lakes, rivers, streams, and underground.

Important Terms (2/2)

Lake

A large body of fresh water surrounded by land.

River

A long, flowing body of fresh water that moves toward an ocean, a lake, or another river.

Groundwater

Water found underground in the spaces between rocks and soil.

Water vapor

Water in the form of an invisible gas found in the air.

Introduction to Earth's Water

A Watery Planet

Earth is unique for its abundance of water, with about 71% of its surface covered by it. From space, Earth appears mostly blue.

The Hydrosphere

Scientists refer to all of Earth's water, in all its forms and locations, as the hydrosphere.

Diversity of Water

Not all water is the same. It can be salty or fresh, liquid or frozen solid (ice), or an invisible gas (water vapor) in the air. This section explores where these different types of water are found.

Oceans — The Big Blue

Vastness of Oceans

Oceans are enormous bodies of salt water that dominate Earth's surface.

Dominant Water Source

Oceans contain approximately 97% of all the water on Earth. If you imagined 100 cups representing all Earth's water, 97 of them would be ocean water.

Salt Water Composition

Ocean water is salty due to dissolved minerals, primarily sodium chloride, which is the same chemical compound found in table salt.

Earth's Five Oceans

Earth has five major oceans: the Pacific, Atlantic, Indian, Southern, and Arctic.

Glaciers and Ice Caps

Definition of Glacier

A glacier is a massive body of ice that forms on land. It develops over hundreds or thousands of years as snow continually falls, accumulates, and compresses into solid ice.

Amount of Water

Glaciers and ice caps hold about 2% of Earth's total water.

Location

They are found in polar regions like Antarctica and Greenland, as well as on high mountain tops around the world.

Primary Fresh Water Reservoir

Despite being only 2% of the total water, glaciers store the majority of Earth's fresh water.

Environmental Impact

If all glaciers were to melt, there would be a dramatic rise in global sea levels.

Lakes, Rivers, and Streams

Fresh Water Bodies

Lakes, rivers, and streams are all bodies of fresh water, meaning they do not contain significant amounts of salt.

Definition of Lake

A lake is a large body of water that is completely surrounded by land.

Definition of River

A river is a long, flowing channel of water that moves across land, typically flowing towards an ocean, a lake, or another river.

Small Percentage

Despite being the most visible and often used sources of liquid fresh water, lakes and rivers hold less than 1% (specifically 0.03%) of all the water on Earth.

Underground Water

Formation of Groundwater

Definition of Groundwater

This water found underground in the spaces between rocks and soil is called groundwater.

Vital Fresh Water Source

Groundwater is an extremely important source of fresh water for many people. Wells are dug to pump groundwater to the surface for drinking and other uses.

Springs

Springs occur when groundwater naturally flows out of the ground at the surface.

Amount

Groundwater accounts for about 0.7% of Earth's total water.

Water in the Air

Invisible Gas

Water is present in the air around us in the form of an invisible gas called water vapor.

Definition of Water Vapor

Water vapor is water that has heated up and changed into its gaseous state.

Humidity

While invisible, water vapor can be felt on humid days, making the air feel thick and sticky.

Cloud and Fog Formation

When water vapor cools down, it transforms into tiny liquid water droplets or ice crystals, which then become visible as clouds. Fog is essentially a cloud that forms very close to the ground.

Smallest Reservoir

The atmosphere holds the smallest fraction of Earth's water, approximately 0.001%.
