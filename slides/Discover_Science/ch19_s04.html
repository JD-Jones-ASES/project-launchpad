Important Terms

Compound machine

A device made of two or more simple machines combined into one.

The Big Trade-Off

The fundamental principle that states less force requires more distance, and more force requires less distance, while the total work done remains constant.

Friction

A force that opposes motion between two surfaces in contact, converting some energy into heat and reducing a machine's efficiency.

Archimedes screw

An ancient device invented by Archimedes, consisting of a large screw inside a tube, used for lifting water or other materials from a lower to a higher place.

What Is a Compound Machine?

Definition

A compound machine is created when two or more simple machines are joined together to form a single, more sophisticated device.

Examples of Compound Machines

The Big Trade-Off

Core Principle

Machines don't reduce the total amount of work you do; they change *how* you do it. The "Big Trade-Off" is:

Work = Force × Distance

This equation is key. If a machine reduces the force you need to apply, it must increase the distance over which you apply that force to keep the total work constant.
