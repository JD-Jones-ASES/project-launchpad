Chapter 27, Section 1: Renewable Resources

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Natural Resources

Anything from nature that people use to meet their needs. These can range from the air we breathe to the metals in our devices.

Renewable Resources

Natural resources that can be replaced or regrown by nature within a relatively short period, typically a human lifetime (years or decades). This allows for their continued use if managed properly.

Renewable Does Not Mean Unlimited

A crucial warning that even though a resource is renewable, it can still be depleted if used faster than nature can replace it. Sustainable practices are essential to maintain renewable resources.

Sunlight as Energy

The Sun provides a constant and vast amount of energy, which can be harnessed directly for electricity using solar panels without creating pollution. It's an inexhaustible resource.

Wind Power

Moving air is a powerful resource used historically and, today, for generating electricity through wind turbines. Wind is a renewable resource driven by uneven solar heating.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Hydropower (Water Power)

The energy from flowing water can be captured using dams and turbines to generate electricity. The continuous nature of the water cycle makes flowing water a renewable energy source.

Trees and Plants as Resources

Wood, fruits, nuts, and other plant products are renewable if forests are replanted and managed sustainably. Tree farms help ensure a continuous supply.

Important Terms (1/2)

Natural Resource

Something from nature that people use to meet their needs.

Renewable Resource

A natural resource that can be replaced or regrown within a human lifetime.

Solar Panels

Devices that convert sunlight directly into electricity.

Wind Turbines

Large structures with blades that spin in the wind to generate electricity.

Hydropower

Electricity generated by the force of flowing water, typically through dams and turbines.

Important Terms (2/2)

Water Cycle

The continuous movement of water on, above, and below the surface of the Earth, which ensures rivers are constantly refilled.

Introduction to Natural Resources

Natural resources

are defined as anything from nature that people use to meet their needs.

What Makes a Resource Renewable?

Example

Trees are renewable. If you cut down a tree, you can plant a new one, and it will grow to maturity in 20-30 years.

Crucial Warning

"Renewable" does not mean "unlimited." If we use a renewable resource faster than nature can replenish it (e.g., cutting down forests faster than they can grow back), we will still run out.

Sunlight

Technological Use

Scientists and engineers have developed **solar panels** to convert sunlight directly into electricity.

Environmental Benefits

Solar panels do not burn fossil fuels or create pollution.

Sustainability

As the Sun is expected to shine for billions more years, sunlight is an inexhaustible resource.

Real-world Application

Solar panels are increasingly used on rooftops of schools, homes, and businesses to generate clean electricity, making solar power one of the fastest-growing energy sources globally.

Wind

Historical Uses

Wind has powered sailboats, grinding stones, and water pumps.

Modern Use

Today, large **wind turbines** capture the energy of moving air to generate electricity. A single turbine can power hundreds of homes.

Origin of Wind

Wind is caused by the Sun's uneven heating of Earth's surface, so as long as the Sun shines, wind will blow.

Soil

Slow Renewability

While technically renewable, soil forms very slowly. It can take 500 to 1,000 years to create just one inch of topsoil (as discussed in Unit 22).

Importance of Care

To ensure soil remains a productive renewable resource, it must be cared for. This includes preventing erosion (the loss of topsoil) and adding organic matter to maintain its fertility.

Risk of Depletion

If soil is treated carelessly, it can be lost through erosion much faster than it can form, essentially depleting this slow-to-renew resource.
