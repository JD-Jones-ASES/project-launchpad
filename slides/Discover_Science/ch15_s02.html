Important Terms (1/7)

Vertebrate

An animal possessing a backbone, which is part of an internal skeleton. Examples include fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals.

Invertebrate

An animal that does not have a backbone. This diverse group includes insects, spiders, worms, jellyfish, and snails.

Fish

Cold-blooded aquatic vertebrates characterized by gills for breathing underwater and scales covering their bodies.

Amphibian

Cold-blooded vertebrates that typically begin their lives in water (often as larvae) and undergo metamorphosis to live on land as adults, though they usually return to water to breed (e.g., frogs, ...

Reptile

Cold-blooded vertebrates with dry, scaly skin that typically lay their eggs on land (e.g., lizards, snakes, turtles).

Important Terms (2/7)

Bird

Warm-blooded vertebrates distinguished by feathers, wings for flight (though some are flightless), beaks, and laying hard-shelled eggs.

Mammal

Warm-blooded vertebrates typically covered in hair or fur, known for feeding their young with milk produced by mammary glands.

Adaptation

A specific physical feature or a behavioral trait that has evolved to help an animal survive and thrive in its particular environment.

Cold-blooded

Describes an animal whose internal body temperature fluctuates and largely matches the temperature of its surrounding environment.

Warm-blooded

Describes an animal that can maintain a relatively stable internal body temperature, regardless of the temperature of its external surroundings.

Important Terms (3/7)

Water cycle

The continuous, unending movement of water on, above, and below the surface of Earth, driven by solar energy, involving processes like evaporation, condensation, precipitation, and collection.

Evaporation

The phase change process where liquid water absorbs heat (usually from the sun) and transforms into water vapor (a gas), rising into the atmosphere.

Condensation

The phase change process where water vapor (gas) in the atmosphere cools down and changes back into tiny liquid water droplets or ice crystals, forming clouds, fog, or dew.

Precipitation

Any form of water—such as rain, snow, sleet, or hail—that falls from clouds and reaches Earth's surface.

Collection

The process where precipitated water gathers on Earth's surface, filling oceans, lakes, rivers, and seeping into the ground to become groundwater.

Important Terms (4/7)

Fresh water

Water containing a very low concentration of dissolved salts. It is found in sources like rivers, lakes, glaciers, and underground aquifers, making up only about 3% of Earth's total water supply.

Salt water

Water that contains a significant amount of dissolved salts. It is predominantly found in oceans and seas, comprising about 97% of Earth's water.

Conservation

The careful management and protection of natural resources to ensure their continued availability and sustainability for current and future generations.

Atom

The smallest unit of an element that retains the chemical identity of that element. Atoms are submicroscopic and are the fundamental building blocks of all matter.

Molecule

A group of two or more atoms held together by chemical bonds. Molecules can consist of atoms of the same element (e.g., O2) or different elements (e.g., H2O).

Important Terms (5/7)

Element

A pure substance consisting only of atoms that all have the same number of protons. Elements cannot be broken down into simpler substances by ordinary chemical means. Over 100 different elements ar...

Periodic table

A systematic chart that organizes all known chemical elements based on their atomic number, electron configurations, and recurring chemical properties, allowing scientists to predict their behaviors.

Chemical formula

A shorthand notation using chemical symbols and numbers to represent the types and quantities of atoms present in a molecule or compound (e.g., H2O indicates two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom).

Compound

A substance formed when two or more *different* elements are chemically bonded together in a fixed ratio. Water (H2O) is a compound made of hydrogen and oxygen.

Energy

The capacity or ability to do work or cause change. A fundamental law of physics states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another.

Important Terms (6/7)

Kinetic energy

The energy an object possesses due to its motion. Any object that is moving, from a rolling ball to a flowing river, has kinetic energy.

Potential energy

Stored energy that an object has due to its position, state, or chemical composition, which has the potential to be converted into other forms of energy. Examples include a stretched spring, a ball...

Heat energy

A form of energy associated with the random motion of atoms and molecules within a substance. Heat energy always transfers from warmer objects to cooler objects, causing temperature changes.

Light energy

A form of electromagnetic radiation that travels in waves and is visible to the human eye. The sun is Earth's primary natural source of light energy.

Sound energy

Energy produced by vibrations that travel as waves through a medium (like air, water, or solids) and can be detected by the ear.

Important Terms (7/7)

Energy transformation

The process by which energy changes from one form to another. For example, a toaster converts electrical energy into heat energy, or a lamp converts electrical energy into light and heat energy.
