Chapter 14, Section 4: The Sun, Food, and Energy

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

The Sun as Earth's Main Energy Source

The Sun provides nearly all the light and heat energy that powers processes on Earth, from warming the planet to creating wind and enabling plant growth.

Photosynthesis and Chemical Energy

Plants use sunlight to perform photosynthesis, converting light energy into chemical energy stored in their tissues (leaves, stems, fruits, seeds).

Energy Transfer Through Food Chains

Other Energy Sources

While the Sun is dominant, Earth also has geothermal (heat from inside Earth), tidal (Moon's gravity), and nuclear (atomic) energy sources. Even fossil fuels store ancient solar energy.

Forms of Energy

Energy exists in various forms, including heat, light, sound, kinetic (motion), and potential (stored) energy. Energy can transform from one form to another.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Law of Conservation of Energy

Discovered by James Joule, this fundamental law states that energy can change forms but is never lost or created.

Important Terms (1/3)

Sun

A giant ball of hot, glowing gas that is the main source of light and heat energy for Earth.

Light energy

A form of electromagnetic radiation visible to the human eye, emitted by sources like the Sun, used by plants for photosynthesis.

Heat energy

A form of energy associated with the movement of atoms and molecules; it can warm objects.

Chemical energy

Energy stored inside a substance, such as food, batteries, or fuels, which can be released through chemical reactions.

Photosynthesis

The process by which plants use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to create their own food (sugars) and release oxygen.

Important Terms (2/3)

Geothermal energy

Heat energy derived from the interior of the Earth.

Tidal energy

Energy created by the gravitational pull of the Moon and Sun on Earth's oceans, resulting in tides.

Nuclear energy

Energy released from the nucleus of an atom, typically through fission or fusion.

Fossil fuels

Natural fuels formed over millions of years from the remains of ancient plants and animals (e.g., coal, oil, natural gas), which store chemical energy originally from the Sun.

Joule (J)

The standard international unit of energy, named after James Prescott Joule.

Important Terms (3/3)

Law of Conservation of Energy

A scientific law stating that energy cannot be created or destroyed, but only transformed from one form to another.

Location and Composition

The Sun is a massive ball of hot, glowing gas, approximately 150 million kilometers from Earth.

It continuously emits enormous amounts of light and heat energy in all directions.

Food: Stored Energy for Your Body

Plants as Energy Converters

Plants are unique in their ability to capture light energy from the Sun and convert it into chemical energy, which they store within their leaves, stems, fruits, and seeds.

Energy for Humans

Definition of Chemical Energy

Energy stored within a substance, which can be released through chemical reactions. Food, batteries, and fuels are examples of things containing chemical energy.

Other Energy Sources on Earth

Geothermal Energy

Heat originating from deep within the Earth's interior, powering phenomena like hot springs and geysers.

Tidal Energy

Energy derived from the gravitational pull of the Moon (and to a lesser extent, the Sun) on Earth's oceans, creating tides.

Nuclear Energy

Energy released from the nucleus (center) of atoms, used in power plants to generate electricity.

James Prescott Joule (1818–1889)

Key Discovery

Joule, an English scientist, revolutionized the understanding of energy by proving that heat is a form of energy, rather than a distinct substance.

Experimental Evidence

Through meticulous experiments, such as using falling weights to spin paddles in water and precisely measuring the resulting temperature increase, Joule demonstrated that mechanical energy (motion)...

Contribution to Energy Conservation

His work was instrumental in formulating the Law of Conservation of Energy, which states that energy can change forms but is never created or destroyed.

Legacy

The standard unit for measuring energy, the joule (J), is named in his honor. One joule is roughly the energy required to lift a small apple one meter off the ground.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is Earth's primary source of energy?

The Sun.

How far away is the Sun from Earth?

Approximately 150 million kilometers.

Name two ways the Sun's energy directly influences Earth.

It warms the planet, powers the water cycle, creates wind, and feeds plants.

What vital process do plants use to convert the Sun's light energy into food?

Photosynthesis.

How is the energy from the Sun stored within plants?

Plants convert light energy into chemical energy, which is stored in their leaves, stems, fruits, and seeds.

Define 'chemical energy'.

Chemical energy is energy stored inside a substance, such as food, batteries, and fuels, that can be released.
