Important Terms (1/7)

Roots

The part of a plant that anchors it in the soil and absorbs water and nutrients.

Stem

The part of a plant that supports the leaves and flowers and carries water and nutrients between roots and leaves.

Leaves

The part of a plant that captures sunlight and makes food through photosynthesis.

Flowers

The part of a plant involved in reproduction; they attract pollinators and produce seeds.

Fruit

The part of a plant that surrounds and protects the seeds.

Important Terms (2/7)

Seeds

Tiny packages containing a baby plant and stored food; they can grow into new plants.

Photosynthesis

The process by which plants use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to make sugar (food) and release oxygen.

Xylem

Tiny tubes inside a plant's stem that carry water upward from the roots to the leaves.

Phloem

Tiny tubes inside a plant's stem that carry sugar downward from the leaves to the rest of the plant.

Rock

A solid natural material made of one or more minerals.

Important Terms (3/7)

Mineral

A natural, nonliving solid with a specific chemical makeup and crystal structure.

Igneous rock

Rock formed when melted rock (magma or lava) cools and hardens.

Sedimentary rock

Rock formed when tiny pieces of rock, sand, or once-living things are pressed and cemented together over time.

Metamorphic rock

Rock that has been changed by heat and pressure deep underground.

Rock cycle

The never-ending process by which rocks change from one type to another over very long periods of time.

Important Terms (4/7)

Magma

Melted rock found underground.

Lava

Melted rock that has reached Earth's surface.

Mixture

A combination of two or more substances that are physically combined but not chemically changed. Each substance keeps its own properties.

Solution

A special type of mixture in which one substance dissolves completely in another.

Dissolve

To mix completely into a liquid so that the substance seems to disappear (but is still there).

Important Terms (5/7)

Solute

The substance that dissolves in a solution (e.g., sugar in lemonade).

Solvent

The substance that does the dissolving (e.g., water in lemonade).

Filtering

Separating a mixture by pouring it through a material with tiny holes that catch larger particles.

Evaporation

The process of a liquid turning into a gas, often used to separate a dissolved solid from a liquid.

Concentration

How much solute is dissolved in a given amount of solvent; a concentrated solution has a lot of solute.

Important Terms (6/7)

Measurement

Using numbers and units to describe the size, amount, or condition of something.

Precision

How close together repeated measurements are; careful measurements are more precise.

Length

How long something is, measured in centimeters (cm) or meters (m).

Mass

The amount of matter in an object, measured in grams (g) or kilograms (kg).

Volume

The amount of space something takes up, measured in milliliters (mL) or liters (L).

Important Terms (7/7)

Graduated cylinder

A tall, narrow container used to measure the volume of liquids.

Temperature

How hot or cold something is, measured in degrees Celsius (°C).

Life Science: The Plant Kingdom (Unit 6) (1/2)

Roots

Anchor the plant firmly in the soil and are essential for absorbing water and dissolved nutrients from the soil.

Stem

Provides structural support for the plant, holding up the leaves and flowers. It also acts as a transport highway, carrying water and nutrients (through **xylem**) from the roots to the leaves, and...

Leaves

The primary sites for **photosynthesis**. Their broad, flat surface helps capture maximum sunlight.

Flowers

The reproductive organs of many plants. They often have bright colors or scents to attract pollinators, which help in the formation of **seeds**.

Fruit

Develops from the flower and encloses and protects the seeds, often aiding in seed dispersal.

Life Science: The Plant Kingdom (Unit 6) (2/2)

Seeds

Contain a tiny embryonic plant and a food supply, allowing the potential for a new plant to grow.

Photosynthesis

The chemical process where plants convert light energy, water, and carbon dioxide into glucose (sugar, their food) and oxygen. This process is fundamental to life on Earth.

Xylem

Specialized vascular tissue that forms continuous tubes within the plant, conducting water and dissolved minerals from the roots upwards.

Phloem

Another type of vascular tissue that transports sugars produced during photosynthesis from the leaves to all other parts of the plant where they are needed for energy or storage.

Earth Science: Rocks and Minerals (Unit 7) (1/2)

Rock

A naturally occurring solid aggregate composed of one or more minerals or mineraloids. Rocks are the main building blocks of the Earth's solid surface.

Mineral

A naturally occurring, inorganic solid with a definite chemical composition and a characteristic crystal structure. Minerals are the basic components of rocks.

Igneous rock

Forms when molten rock cools and solidifies. This can happen underground (forming intrusive igneous rocks from **magma**) or on the Earth's surface (forming extrusive igneous rocks from **lava**).

Sedimentary rock

Metamorphic rock

Forms when existing rocks are subjected to intense heat, pressure, or chemical alteration deep within the Earth's crust. This process changes the rock's texture, mineralogy, or chemical composition.

Rock cycle

Magma

Molten rock material that originates beneath the Earth's surface. When it cools and solidifies, it forms intrusive igneous rocks.

Lava

Molten rock that has erupted onto the Earth's surface through volcanoes or fissures. When it cools and solidifies, it forms extrusive igneous rocks.

Chemistry: Mixing and Separating (Unit 8) (1/2)

Mixture

A substance containing two or more different pure substances which are physically combined but not chemically bonded. The individual substances retain their chemical identities and properties. Exam...

Solution

A homogeneous mixture where one substance (the **solute**) is uniformly dispersed or dissolved in another substance (the **solvent**). Solutions appear clear and do not separate over time. Examples...

Dissolve

The process by which a solute mixes completely with a solvent to form a solution. The solute particles become surrounded by solvent particles and are no longer individually visible.

Solute

The substance that is present in a lesser amount and dissolves in a solvent to form a solution (e.g., salt in saltwater).

Solvent

The substance that is present in a greater amount and dissolves the solute to form a solution (e.g., water in saltwater). Water is often called the "universal solvent."

Chemistry: Mixing and Separating (Unit 8) (2/2)

Filtering

A separation technique used for heterogeneous mixtures (where particles are large enough to be trapped). It involves passing a mixture through a porous material (like filter paper) that retains sol...

Evaporation

A separation technique used for solutions where a solid is dissolved in a liquid. The liquid solvent is heated and turns into a gas, leaving the solid solute behind.

Concentration

A measure of the amount of solute present in a given amount of solvent or solution. A high concentration means a large amount of solute, while a low concentration means a small amount.

Physics: Measuring Our World (Unit 9) (1/2)

Measurement

The process of assigning a numerical value to a physical quantity, typically by comparison with a standard unit. It allows us to quantify observations and make them objective.

Precision

Refers to the closeness of two or more measurements to each other. High precision indicates that repeated measurements under the same conditions yield very similar results, even if they are not nec...

Length

A measure of the linear extent of an object from one end to the other. Common units include centimeters (cm) and meters (m).

Mass

A measure of the amount of matter in an object. It is a fundamental property of an object and is typically measured in grams (g) or kilograms (kg).

Volume

A measure of the amount of three-dimensional space an object or substance occupies. Common units include milliliters (mL) and liters (L).

Physics: Measuring Our World (Unit 9) (2/2)

Graduated cylinder

A cylindrical laboratory tool with markings (graduations) along the side, used for precisely measuring the volume of liquids.

Temperature

A physical quantity that expresses the degree of hotness or coldness of a substance. It is measured in degrees Celsius (°C), Kelvin (K), or Fahrenheit (°F).
