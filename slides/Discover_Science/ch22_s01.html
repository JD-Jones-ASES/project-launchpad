What Is Soil?

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Soil Definition

Soil is a dynamic mixture of broken rock (minerals), decomposed organic material (humus), water, and air, essential for plant growth and covering much of Earth's land surface.

Four Main Ingredients

Soil consists of four crucial components: mineral particles from broken rock, nutrient-rich humus from decomposed organisms, water held in soil pores, and air also found in soil pores.

Humus

This dark, crumbly, nutrient-rich material is formed from the decomposition of dead plants and animals, significantly enhancing soil fertility.

Weathering

The process by which wind, rain, ice, and even plant roots break down rocks into smaller pieces, forming the mineral component of soil.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Soil Particle Sizes

The mineral component of soil is classified by particle size into sand (largest), silt (medium), and clay (smallest), each impacting soil texture and water retention differently.

Loam

An ideal type of soil for plant growth, characterized by a balanced mixture of sand, silt, and clay, offering good drainage, water retention, and nutrient availability.

Soil Biodiversity

Healthy soil is teeming with an immense diversity of living organisms (bacteria, fungi, worms, insects) that play crucial roles in decomposition and nutrient cycling.

Important Terms (1/2)

Soil

A complex, living mixture of broken rock, decomposed organisms, water, and air that covers much of Earth's land surface and supports plant life.

Humus

The dark, nutrient-rich material in soil that forms when dead plants and animals decompose; it improves soil structure and fertility.

Weathering

The natural process by which rocks are broken down into smaller pieces by physical (e.g., wind, ice) and chemical (e.g., acid rain) forces over time.

Sand

The largest type of soil particle, feeling gritty and allowing water to drain quickly.

Silt

Medium-sized soil particles; feels smooth and powdery when dry, falling between sand and clay in terms of size and water-holding capacity.

Important Terms (2/2)

Clay

The tiniest type of soil particle; feels sticky when wet and hard when dry, known for holding water very well.

Loam

A type of soil considered ideal for growing plants because it contains a balanced mixture of sand, silt, and clay particles, providing good drainage, aeration, and water retention.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the scientific definition of soil?

Soil is a mixture of broken rock, decomposed organisms, water, and air that covers much of Earth's land surface.

How is soil fundamentally different from 'dirt'?

Name the four main ingredients of soil.

Broken rock (minerals), decomposed organisms (humus), water, and air.

What component of soil provides plants with many of the essential nutrients they need to grow?

Broken rock, which consists of tiny mineral pieces from weathered rocks.

What is humus, and how does it form?

Humus is the dark, nutrient-rich material in soil that forms when dead plants and animals decompose.

What gives rich garden soil its characteristic dark brown or black color?

The presence of humus, which is packed with nutrients.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Explain the role of water as an essential ingredient in soil.

Soil holds water in tiny spaces between particles, which plant roots then soak up for survival and growth.

Explain the role of air as an essential ingredient in soil.

Tiny pockets of air exist between soil particles, providing oxygen that plant roots and soil animals need to breathe.

What negative effect does overly compacted soil have on plant growth?

If soil is packed too tightly, there is no room for air, which suffocates plant roots and prevents them from growing well.

Approximately how long does it take for even a thin layer of soil to form?

Hundreds or even thousands of years.

What is the very first step in the natural process of soil formation?

Rocks break apart into smaller and smaller pieces.

What is the term for the process where wind, rain, ice, and plant roots slowly crack and crumble rocks?

Weathering.
