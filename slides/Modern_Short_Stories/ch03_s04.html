Key Concepts

Tradition and the Past

The Morkan sisters' annual dance is a cherished, decades-old event, symbolizing a fading way of life in Dublin. The sisters themselves embody an older generation.

Discomfort and Alienation

Gabriel frequently experiences moments of unease and discomposure, both from external interactions (Lily's remark) and his own self-critical thoughts. This foreshadows a deeper sense of alienation.

Appearance vs. Reality

Gabriel maintains a composed exterior, but internally, he is plagued by self-doubt and overthinking, particularly regarding his intellect and social standing.

Important Terms (1/2)

Caretaker’s Daughter

Lily, a young working-class girl employed by the Morkan sisters to assist with the party. Her bitterness hints at deeper societal issues.

Usher’s Island

The location of the Morkan sisters' "dark gaunt house" in Dublin, a setting that emphasizes the old and perhaps fading nature of their lives.

Haddington Road / Antient Concert Rooms

Locations associated with Mary Jane's musical career, signifying her professional success and role as the "main prop" of the household.

Adam and Eve’s

The church where Aunt Julia is the leading soprano, reflecting her past achievements and connection to the community.

Diamond-bone sirloins, three-shilling tea, bottled stout

Specific items mentioned to illustrate the Morkan sisters' commitment to modest but good living, indicative of a particular historical period and lifestyle.

Setting the Scene: The Morkan Sisters' Annual Dance

A Lively Household

The story opens at the Morkan sisters' annual dance, a bustling event that has been a tradition for "thirty years." The house on Usher's Island, though "dark gaunt," comes alive with guests.

The Morkan Sisters (Miss Kate and Miss Julia)

Elderly, fussy, but ultimately warm and hospitable. Kate is described as "more vivacious," while Julia is "quite grey" and seems more passive. They live with their niece, Mary Jane.

Mary Jane

Lily, the Caretaker's Daughter

Overworked and "literally run off her feet" attending to guests. She is a slim, pale girl with "hay-coloured hair," providing a glimpse into the working-class life within the household.

Gabriel and Gretta's Arrival

A Late Entrance

Gabriel and Gretta arrive late, attributing Gretta's prolonged dressing as the cause. Gabriel scrapes snow from his "goloshes," indicating the winter weather.

Gabriel's Interaction with Lily

Gabriel's Physical Description

He is a "stout tallish young man" with a "hairless face," polished glasses, and glossy black hair. His appearance suggests a refined, intellectual air.

Gabriel's Inner Turmoil and Social Anxieties

Discomposure and Self-Doubt

Lily's sharp remark leaves Gabriel feeling "discomposed" and casts a "gloom over him." He immediately feels he "had made a mistake."

The Impending Speech

Gabriel is preoccupied with a speech he is to give later. He reviews his notes, particularly questioning a quotation from Robert Browning.

Family Interactions and Social Commentary

Aunts' Affection for Gabriel

Kate and Julia warmly greet Gabriel, their "favourite nephew," son of their dead sister Ellen.

The "Goloshes" Conversation

Plans for the Night

Gabriel and Gretta have booked a room at the Gresham Hotel and arranged for a caretaker, Bessie, to look after their children. This indicates a degree of independence from the aunts' household.

Aunt Kate's Concern for Lily

Kate remarks that Lily "is not the girl she was at all," suggesting a recent change or problem with Lily, which Gabriel almost asks about before being interrupted.

Aunt Julia's Wandering

The section ends with Aunt Julia wandering off down the stairs, prompting Aunt Kate to call out to her. This suggests Julia's increasing frailty or mental disengagement.
