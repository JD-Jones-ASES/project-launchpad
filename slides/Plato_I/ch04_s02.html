Justice, Law, and the Social Contract

Study Slides

Thrasymachus' Challenge to Justice

The Thesis

Justice is nothing more than the "interest of the stronger."

The Mechanism

Rulers (whether in a democracy, tyranny, or oligarchy) design legal systems to benefit themselves. They label obedience to these self-serving laws as "justice" and punish dissenters.

The Outcome

Thrasymachus claims the "perfectly unjust man"—specifically the successful tyrant—is happier than the just man because he satisfies his own interests through force and fraud.

The Socratic Counter

Plato’s Republic spends nine additional books answering this challenge, arguing that justice is a "harmony of the soul" and that the just person is inherently happier.

The Evolution of Social Contract Theory

Philosopher. Historical Context

View of the Social Contract

Thomas Hobbes. English Civil War

People surrender freedom to a sovereign for security; life without government is "nasty, brutish, and short."

John Locke. Constitutionalism

Government rests on consent to protect natural rights (life, liberty, property).

Jean-Jacques Rousseau. Enlightenment

Laws are legitimate only if they express the "general will" aimed at the common good.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Allegory

A literary form where characters and events represent abstract ideas (e.g., Lady Philosophy in Boethius' work).

Divine Law

According to Aquinas, the law revealed by God in Scripture to guide humans toward eternal life.

General Will

Rousseau’s concept of a collective will that aims at the common good, serving as the only legitimate basis for law.

Natural Law

The participation of rational creatures in the eternal law; moral principles discoverable through reason.

Prudence (Phronesis)

Practical wisdom; the ability to judge the right course of action in specific circumstances. Aquinas called it the "charioteer of the virtues."

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Prosopopoeia

The rhetorical technique of giving speech to an abstraction or an absent person.

Social Contract

The theory that a person's moral and political obligations are dependent upon a contract or agreement among them to form the society in which they live.

Sophist

A traveling professional teacher in ancient Greece who taught rhetoric and persuasion, often criticized by Socrates for prioritizing winning arguments over finding the truth.

State of Nature

A hypothetical condition of humanity without any government or central authority.

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to Thrasymachus in Book I of the Republic, what is the definition of justice?

Justice is nothing else than the interest of the stronger.

In Thrasymachus' view, what is the primary purpose of laws made by a ruling group?

To benefit the rulers themselves.

Term: Sophist

Definition: A traveling professional teacher in ancient Greece who charged fees for instruction in rhetoric and public speaking.

Why were Socrates and Plato critical of the sophists?

They accused them of teaching persuasion without regard for truth.

Thrasymachus argues that a democracy makes democratic laws and a tyranny makes tyrannical laws to ensure obedience to _____.

Self-serving laws.

To whom does Thrasymachus compare rulers who "fatten or tend" their subjects?

Shepherds or neatherds who look after sheep or oxen for their own profit.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How does Thrasymachus describe the outcome for a just man versus an unjust man in a private contract partnership?

The unjust man always ends up with more and the just man with less when the partnership dissolves.

In dealings with the State, why is the just man considered a "loser" by Thrasymachus regarding income tax?

The just man pays more than the unjust man on the same amount of income.

What does Thrasymachus identify as the "highest form of injustice"?

Tyranny, which takes property wholesale by fraud and force.

According to Thrasymachus, why do people censure injustice?

They fear being victims of injustice rather than shrinking from committing it.

What is the "Social Contract" theory in political philosophy?

The idea that government rests on an agreement or consent among the governed.

Which English philosopher argued that without a strong government, life would be "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short"?

Thomas Hobbes.
