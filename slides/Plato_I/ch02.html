Important Terms (1/4)

Piety (to hosion)

"The holy" or "the pious" in Greek; acting in accordance with what the divine law requires. Socrates' central question in the dialogue is what makes all pious acts pious.

Impiety (asebeia)

A failure to honor the gods properly; a criminal offense in Athens that could carry the death penalty. The formal charge brought against Socrates by Meletus.

Elenchus (elenchos)

Socrates' method of philosophical cross-examination. He draws out his interlocutor's beliefs, then uses questioning to reveal contradictions or inadequacies. The goal is not to win an argument but ...

Aporia

A Greek term meaning "no passage" or "puzzlement." The state of genuine intellectual impasse reached when a question you thought you understood proves more complex than expected. Many Socratic dial...

Theophiles

"Dear to the gods" — the quality Euthyphro invokes in Definitions 2 and 3 of piety. Socrates argues this describes a feature pious things happen to have, not the essential nature of piety.

Euthyphro Dilemma

Theory of Forms

Plato's metaphysical theory that abstract, eternal Forms (Justice, Beauty, Goodness, Piety) are more real than physical objects. True knowledge is knowledge of the Forms; perception of physical thi...

Doxa

Greek for "opinion." In Plato's epistemology, the kind of awareness we have of the physical world — changeable, unreliable, and inferior to true knowledge.

Episteme

Greek for "knowledge." In Plato, the certain grasp of eternal truths, especially the Forms, achieved through reason and dialectic.

Important Terms (3/4)

Divine Command Theory (DCT)

The ethical theory that moral obligations are grounded entirely in God's commands. An action is right if and only if God commands it. Associated with William of Ockham.

Natural Law

The moral law discoverable by human reason, which Aquinas defined as "the rational creature's participation in the eternal law." God commands the good because it is good, and that goodness is built...

Moral Realism

The view that moral facts (e.g., "murder is wrong") exist objectively, independently of what anyone thinks or commands.

Voluntarism

The view that moral truths are grounded in will — either God's will (theological voluntarism) or human will.

King Archon (archon basileus)

The Athenian magistrate responsible for cases involving religion and homicide, outside whose court the dialogue takes place.

Important Terms (4/4)

Mantis (diviner)

A person who claims to interpret the will of the gods through signs, dreams, or inspired speech. Euthyphro's profession.

Petitio principii ("begging the question")

A circular argument that assumes the very thing it is supposed to prove. Euthyphro's fifth definition commits this error.

The Setting and Dramatic Context

Socrates

Euthyphro

has come to the same court for a startling reason: he is prosecuting his own father for murder.

The irony is deliberate

Socrates, charged with impiety, meets a man who claims expert knowledge of piety — and who is willing to prosecute his own father in its name.

Dramatic arc

This dialogue takes place before Socrates' trial; the Apology covers the trial itself; the Crito takes place in prison afterward. Together they form a continuous narrative.

The Characters

Euthyphro

A professional religious expert and diviner (mantis) who interprets omens and claims to understand the gods' wishes.

Socrates

The questioner, approaching Euthyphro with apparent admiration. "Teach me what piety is," he says — using Socratic irony to draw Euthyphro into cross-examination.
