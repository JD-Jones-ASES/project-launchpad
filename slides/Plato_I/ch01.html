Key Concepts (1/2)

Socratic Dialogue as Drama

These texts are not textbooks but theatrical conversations featuring characters with distinct personalities, motives, and flaws that influence the arguments presented.

Socratic Irony

Socrates' deliberate pose of ignorance, often saying the opposite of what he means, to provoke interlocutors, expose false confidence, and foster genuine philosophical inquiry.

Following the Argument

Socratic dialogues follow a structured pattern of question, proposed definition, rigorous testing, and subsequent rejection or refinement, rather than being random conversations.

Pre-Socratic Philosophy

A diverse group of early Greek thinkers who, unlike previous generations, sought rational, natural explanations for the world's phenomena instead of relying on mythological accounts.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Arche

The fundamental, underlying substance or first principle from which all things in the universe originate and to which they ultimately return, a central concern for the Milesian philosophers.

Important Terms (1/3)

Philosophy

The "love of wisdom"; a discipline that explores fundamental questions about existence, knowledge, values, reason, mind, and language.

Socratic Dialogue

A literary genre in which Socrates engages one or more interlocutors in a philosophical discussion, often leading to a state of aporia rather than a definitive conclusion.

Socratic Irony

A rhetorical device used by Socrates where he feigns ignorance or expresses admiration for an interlocutor's supposed wisdom, only to then dismantle their arguments.

Eironeia

An ancient Greek term for "irony" or "dissimulation," used by rhetoricians like Aristotle to describe an understatement of one's abilities; Socratic irony applies this as a philosophical tool.

Aporia

A Greek term meaning "being without a path" or "puzzlement." In philosophy, it refers to a state of intellectual impasse or perplexity, considered a productive starting point for deeper inquiry.

Important Terms (2/3)

Arche (archē)

The "beginning," "origin," or "first principle" of all things; the fundamental substance or principle sought by the Pre-Socratics to explain the universe.

Apeiron

A concept proposed by Anaximander, meaning the "boundless" or "indefinite"; an infinite, formless substance considered to be the arche from which all definite things emerge.

Rarefaction

The process of a substance becoming thinner or less dense, as used by Anaximenes to explain how air transforms into fire.

Condensation

The process of a substance becoming thicker or more dense, as used by Anaximenes to explain how air transforms into wind, cloud, water, earth, and stone.

Pre-Socratics

A collective term for Greek philosophers who lived and taught before Socrates, notable for their attempts to understand the cosmos through reason and natural observation rather than myth.

Important Terms (3/3)

Benjamin Jowett

A Victorian scholar (1817-1893) whose widely read and literary English translations of Plato's dialogues are used in this textbook.
