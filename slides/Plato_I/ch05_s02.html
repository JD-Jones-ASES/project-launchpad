Key Historical Figures and Contributions

Figure. Period

Major Contribution

Justin Martyr. c. 100–165 AD

Developed the concept of logos spermatikos ("seeds of the Word"); viewed Socrates as a "Christian before Christ."

Augustine of Hippo. 354–430 AD

Integrated the Theory of Forms into the mind of God (rationes aeternae); established the doctrine of Divine Simplicity.

Boethius. c. 477–524 AD

Wrote The Consolation of Philosophy; argued that true happiness is found only in the Good (God); preserved Aristotelian logic.

Thomas Aquinas. 1225–1274 AD

Created the "Medieval Synthesis"; established the doctrine of Natural Law; argued that faith and reason are allies.

Marsilio Ficino. 1433–1499 AD

Translated the complete Platonic corpus into Latin; founded the Platonic Academy of Florence.

Major Philosophical Themes

The Theory of Forms in Theology

Early Christians, specifically Augustine, relocated Plato’s eternal Forms from a separate realm into the mind of God. These "eternal ideas" became the blueprints for creation.

Natural Law

Developed most fully by Aquinas, this is the idea that human reason can discern moral truths because human reason participates in the divine reason (the eternal law).

The Logos

The bridge between Greek philosophy and Christianity. For philosophers, it was the "Reason" of the universe; for Christians, it was the "Word" of God made flesh in Christ.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Divine Simplicity

The theological doctrine that God is not composed of parts (such as will, knowledge, and goodness) but that God’s essence and attributes are one and the same.

Logos Spermatikos

Literally "seeds of the Word"; the idea that God scattered fragments of truth throughout the world and throughout the minds of pagan philosophers.

Manichaeism

A dualistic religion that taught the world is a battleground between equal forces of good (light/spirit) and evil (darkness/matter).

Natural Law

The rational creature's participation in the eternal law of God; moral principles that can be discovered through natural reason without the need for special revelation.

Neoplatonism

A philosophical tradition (led by figures like Plotinus) that emphasized the ascent of the soul to a pure, immaterial Being or "The One."

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Platonic Corpus

The complete collection of approximately thirty-six dialogues attributed to Plato.

Prosopopoeia

A literary device involving the personification of an abstract idea, such as Boethius’s "Lady Philosophy."

Rationes Aeternae

"Eternal ideas"; the Platonic Forms as understood by Augustine to exist within the divine mind.

Scholasticism

The dominant medieval intellectual movement that used rigorous logical methods to reconcile Greek philosophy with Christian theology.

Skepticism (Academic)

A philosophical school that doubted the possibility of attaining certain knowledge; a stage in Augustine’s intellectual development before his conversion.
