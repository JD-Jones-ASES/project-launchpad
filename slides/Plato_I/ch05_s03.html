Part 1: Key Concepts and Further Reading

Author. Work

Key Themes

Aristotle. *Nicomachean Ethics*

Focuses on *eudaimonia* (happiness) as an activity of the soul in accordance with virtue; more practical than Plato.

Marcus Aurelius. *Meditations*

Stoic reflections on duty, death, and self-control; written as a private journal in the Socratic spirit.

Augustine. *Confessions*

A synthesis of Greek philosophy and Christian theology; explores the journey from restlessness to truth.

Boethius. *The Consolation of Philosophy*

A dialogue with "Lady Philosophy" written in prison; explores fortune and God's goodness, mirroring Socrates' calm in the *Crito*.

Part 2: Interdisciplinary Connections

Literature

Philosophical questions regarding justice and truth appear in the works of Shakespeare (*Hamlet*) and Dostoevsky (the *Euthyphro* dilemma).

History

Philosophical ideas fueled the American Revolution (Plato, Aristotle, Cicero), the French Revolution (Enlightenment philosophy), and the civil rights movement (natural law).

Theology

The dialogue between philosophy and theology has lasted two millennia, influencing thinkers like Aquinas, Calvin, and the early Church Fathers.

Science

Modern science relies on philosophical assumptions about the reliability of reason and the intelligibility of nature, originating from the pre-Socratics and Plato’s mathematical focus.

Part 6: Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Allegory of the Cave

A famous narrative in Plato's *Republic* used to illustrate his philosophical ideas (related to the Theory of Forms).

Eudaimonia

A Greek term for happiness, defined by Aristotle as an activity of the soul in accordance with virtue.

Examined Life

A practice of questioning, reflecting, and seeking truth through conversation, honesty, courage, and humility.

Natural Law Theory

A philosophical concept that influenced the civil rights movement.

Philosopher-Kings

The ideal rulers proposed by Plato in the *Republic*.

Part 6: Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Recollection

The theory in the *Meno* that learning is actually the soul remembering truths it already knows.

Socratic Method

The practice of questioning and testing assumptions to pursue truth, as demonstrated by Socrates throughout the dialogues.

Stoic Philosophy

A school of thought focused on duty, self-control, and impermanence, exemplified by Marcus Aurelius.

Theory of Forms

A central Platonic doctrine developed in the *Republic* concerning the nature of reality.
