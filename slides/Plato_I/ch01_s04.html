Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: Why does the text argue that a Socratic dialogue should be read like a play rather than a textbook?

The dialogues are dramatic works where the personalities and motives of the characters shape the arguments. A claim’s validity or meaning may change based on who is speaking and their specific situation.

Q: Define "Socratic Irony" as it is used in the dialogues.

It is a deliberate pose of ignorance where Socrates claims to know nothing or praises the wisdom of his interlocutor to draw them into a test of their knowledge, eventually exposing their false confidence.

Q: What was Aristotle's view of eironeia?

Aristotle described it as an understatement of one’s own abilities, which he considered a more graceful social manner than boasting.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/2)

Q: What are the four possible reasons a definition might be shown to be inadequate during a Socratic inquiry?

A definition may be shown to be too narrow, too broad, self-contradictory, or otherwise insufficient to cover the essence of the concept.

Q: How should a reader react if a dialogue ends without a clear resolution to the central question?

The reader should embrace the "puzzlement" or aporia. This state is considered the beginning of genuine philosophical progress and the first step toward wisdom.

Q: What advice does the text give for dealing with the Victorian English of Benjamin Jowett?

Readers are encouraged to read the text aloud to capture its rhythms and to use footnotes for archaic terms. While modern translations exist, Jowett’s are used for their literary quality and public domain status.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What literary genre best describes a Socratic dialogue?

A dramatic work involving characters with distinct personalities and motives.

Why is it important to consider the personality of the characters in a Socratic dialogue?

A character's situation and traits can change the meaning of the arguments they make.

How does the text describe Socrates' personality in the dialogues?

Witty, relentless, and occasionally maddeningly evasive.

What are the personality traits of Euthyphro as described in the text?

Confident and a little pompous.

How is the character Crito characterized in terms of his personality?

Loyal, anxious, and practical.

What instance of dramatic irony occurs between Socrates and Euthyphro?

The reader knows Socrates is facing an impiety charge, but Euthyphro is unaware of the connection while discussing piety.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Define the concept of Socratic Irony.

A deliberate pose of ignorance used to draw others into philosophical examination.

What is the intended purpose of the "philosophical trap" set by Socrates' professed ignorance?

To expose false confidence and replace it with genuine inquiry.

Term: $\text{eironeia}$

Definition: An ancient rhetorical technique meaning irony or dissimulation.

How did Aristotle describe the technique of irony in relation to social grace?

As an understatement of one's own abilities, which he deemed more graceful than boasting.

What is the strategic reason behind Socrates calling an interlocutor "wise"?

He is inviting them to put their supposed knowledge to a public test.

What is the typical first step in the structured pattern of a Socratic dialogue?

Socrates asks a question, typically seeking a definition such as "What is piety?"
