Overview of the Dialogue

Character. Background

Primary Motivation

Socrates. Philosopher recently condemned to death.

Driven by reason and the pursuit of a just life; remains calm in the face of death.

Crito. A wealthy, practical friend of Socrates whose name means "judge" (*krites*).

Driven by loyalty, fear for his friend, and concern for his own public reputation.

Socrates’ Philosophical Response

Laws as Parents and Teachers

The Laws provided the framework for Socrates’ birth, education, and life. To disobey them now would be as shameful as striking a parent.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Analogy

A comparison between two things used to explain or clarify a point (e.g., the athlete and the trainer).

Hemlock

A poisonous plant extract used as the standard method of execution in ancient Athens.

Krites

The Greek word for “judge” or “one who decides”; the root of the name Crito.

Prosopopoeia

A rhetorical device in which an abstract concept (like the Laws) is personified and given a voice.

Psyche

The soul; for Socrates, this refers to the inner self, the seat of reason and moral character.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Social Contract

The implicit agreement between citizens and their government, where the citizen obeys the law in exchange for the protections and benefits of the state.

Sunium

The southern tip of Attica where the sacred ship was sighted before arriving at Athens.

Thessaly

A region in Greece where Crito’s friends offered to provide a safe house for Socrates if he escaped.
