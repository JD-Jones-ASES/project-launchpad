Important Terms (1/4)

Socrates (c. 470–399 BC)

Athenian philosopher, father of Western philosophy, known for his Socratic method, famously wrote nothing, and was executed by the Athenian state.

Plato (c. 428–348 BC)

Athenian philosopher, student of Socrates, founder of the Academy, and author of influential philosophical dialogues featuring Socrates.

Socratic Problem

The scholarly difficulty in discerning the true historical teachings of Socrates from Plato's literary portrayal.

Elenchus

The Greek term for Socrates' method of "cross-examination" or "refutation," involving questioning to expose contradictions in beliefs.

Interlocutor

A person who takes part in a dialogue or conversation; in Plato's dialogues, these are the individuals Socrates engages with.

Important Terms (2/4)

Maieutike

"The art of midwifery," Socrates' self-description of his philosophical method of helping others develop their own ideas.

Peloponnesian War (431–404 BC)

A major conflict between Athens and Sparta, which provides a significant historical context for Socrates' life and trial.

Agora

The central public space in ancient Greek city-states, serving as a marketplace and civic hub, where Socrates conducted many of his philosophical discussions.

Xenophon, Aristophanes, Aristotle

Other ancient authors whose writings provide some insight into the life and philosophy of Socrates, alongside Plato.

Aporia

Literally "without a path"; a state of perplexity or impasse that often concludes Plato's early dialogues, intended to stimulate further thought.

Important Terms (3/4)

The Academy

The school founded by Plato in Athens around 387 BC, dedicated to philosophical and scientific inquiry.

King Archon

An Athenian magistrate responsible for religious affairs and cases involving impiety, before whom Socrates' charges were formally brought.

Meletus, Anytus, Lycon

The three primary accusers who brought charges of impiety and corrupting the youth against Socrates.

Euthyphro

One of Plato's early dialogues, depicting Socrates discussing the nature of piety with a self-proclaimed religious expert.

Apology

Plato's account of Socrates' defense speech during his trial before an Athenian jury.

Important Terms (4/4)

Crito

A Platonic dialogue where Socrates discusses whether he should escape from prison with his friend Crito.

Phaedo

A Platonic dialogue that describes Socrates' final hours, his arguments for the immortality of the soul, and his death by hemlock.

Hemlock

A poisonous plant used as the method of capital punishment in Athens, which Socrates drank.

The Dramatic Setting: Three Dialogues, One Story (1/2)

Narrative Arc

The *Euthyphro*, *Apology*, and *Crito* form a connected narrative taking place over a few weeks in 399 BC, telling the story of Socrates' trial and death.

The Indictment

Charges brought against Socrates by Meletus, Anytus, and Lycon for impiety (not believing in the city's gods, introducing new divine beings) and corrupting the youth.

The Euthyphro

Socrates, on his way to his preliminary hearing, meets Euthyphro and discusses the nature of piety. This dialogue is dramatically ironic given Socrates' own indictment for impiety.

The Trial (Apology)

Socrates defends himself before a jury of 501 Athenian citizens, is found guilty (280-221 vote), and sentenced to death.

The Imprisonment (Crito)

While awaiting execution, Socrates refuses his wealthy friend Crito's offer to help him escape, arguing that breaking the law—even an unjust one—is morally wrong.

The Dramatic Setting: Three Dialogues, One Story (2/2)

The Execution

Socrates drinks hemlock and dies, a scene described in the *Phaedo*.

Urgency of the Questions

The impending death of Socrates imbues the philosophical questions about piety, justice, and obedience to law with intense, real-world urgency, making them far from abstract exercises.
