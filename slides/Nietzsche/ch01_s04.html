The Works and Philosophy of Friedrich Nietzsche

Study Slides

The Apollonian and Dionysian

Apollonian

Represents order, form, beauty, and individuation. It provides shape and meaning to existence.

Dionysian

Represents chaos, ecstasy, the dissolution of the self, and a terrifying encounter with formless reality.

Synthesis

Nietzsche argued that Greek tragedy reached the sublime by bringing these two opposing drives together.

Chronology of Major Works

Year. Title

Primary Focus

1872. *The Birth of Tragedy*

Greek tragedy; Apollonian vs. Dionysian; defense of Wagner.

1878. *Human, All Too Human*

Anti-romanticism; psychological suspicion of morality/religion.

1882/87. *The Gay Science*

"Gai saber"; first appearance of the "Death of God" and "Eternal Recurrence."

1883–85. *Thus Spoke Zarathustra*

Philosophical prose-poetry; the Ubermensch; the prophet Zarathustra.

1886. *Beyond Good and Evil*

Critique of philosophers; noble vs. slave morality.

1887. *On the Genealogy of Morals*

Systematic inquiry into the origins of guilt, ressentiment, and asceticism.

1888. *The Antichrist*

A sustained attack on the institutional religion built by St. Paul.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Aphorism

A brief, pithy statement or self-contained paragraph used by Nietzsche to provoke thought and avoid the "intellectual dishonesty" of rigid logical systems.

Ascetic Ideal

The practice of self-denial and discipline, analyzed by Nietzsche as a tool used by priests and scientists to give meaning to suffering.

Eternal Recurrence

The thought experiment asking if one could bear to live their entire life over again, identically, for all eternity.

Gai Saber

Literally "the gay science"; a philosophy of lightness, poetry, and artistic flair.

Philology

The study of language in oral and written historical sources; Nietzsche’s original academic profession before becoming a philosopher.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Ressentiment

A sense of hostility directed toward an object that one identifies as the cause of one's frustration; according to Nietzsche, the foundational emotion of "slave morality."

Ubermensch (Overman)

A central figure in *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* representing a new stage for humanity following the "death of God."

Will to Power

A concept used throughout Nietzsche’s published works (not to be confused with the posthumous book of the same name) to describe a fundamental drive in human nature and the world.
