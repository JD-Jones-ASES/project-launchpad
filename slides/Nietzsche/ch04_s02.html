Sigmund Freud: Civilization and the Superego

Internalized Aggression

When aggression is renounced, it does not vanish but is redirected inward.

The Superego

This internalized aggression is taken up by the "superego," which punishes the "ego" with feelings of guilt.

The Paradox of Virtue

Freud observes that the more a person renounces their aggression, the more intensely the superego punishes the self, leading to greater feelings of guilt.

Michel Foucault: Discipline and the Modern Prison

Shift in Technology of Power

Foucault argues that the transition from public torture to the modern prison is not "humanitarian progress" but a change in how power is exercised.

Docile Bodies

Modern discipline targets the "soul" rather than the body. Through constant surveillance and regimented schedules, individuals learn to monitor and punish themselves, becoming "docile bodies."

Internalization of Norms

This reflects Nietzsche's "internalization of instinct" on a societal scale, where power operates by getting individuals to impose constraints upon themselves.

Criminal Justice: Retributive vs. Restorative Models

The Retributive Model

This model assumes punishment leads to moral improvement. Nietzsche argues this is a psychological error, claiming punishment instead produces resentment and cunning.
