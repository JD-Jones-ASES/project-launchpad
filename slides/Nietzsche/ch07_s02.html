Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: How does Dostoevsky’s character Ivan Karamazov frame the problem of atheism?

Ivan argues that if there is no immortality of the soul, then there is no virtue and "everything is lawful." Dostoevsky uses this as a *reductio ad absurdum* to argue that faith is necessary for morality.

Q: What did Kierkegaard find "fierce" or objectionable about the Danish state church?

He accused institutional Christianity of "domesticating" God, draining religion of genuine faith, and replacing the "terrifying encounter with the infinite" with a comfortable, routine social institution.

Q: Define Charles Taylor’s concept of the "buffered self."

The "buffered self" is a condition of the modern individual who is self-sufficient and closed off from transcendence, as opposed to the "porous self" of previous eras who was open to enchantment and transcendence.

Q: What is the primary difference between the "leaps" of Kierkegaard and Nietzsche?

Both demand authenticity and an individual confrontation with ultimate questions, but Kierkegaard "leaps toward God" while Nietzsche "leaps away from God" toward the human creation of meaning.

Q: Who was Dietrich Bonhoeffer, and what was his contribution to this discourse?

Bonhoeffer was a German Lutheran theologian who wrote about "religionless Christianity" and argued that the church must learn to live in a "world come of age" without using God as a mere hypothesis.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Amor Fati

Literally "love of fate"; Nietzsche’s formula for human greatness, involving a joyful acceptance of all that happens in life, including suffering.

Buffered Self

A term coined by Charles Taylor to describe the modern individual who is closed off from the transcendent and finds meaning within a self-sufficient, secular framework.

Death of God Theology

A movement in the 1960s (associated with Altizer and Hamilton) that attempted to affirm the death of God as a specific theological event.

Everything is Permitted (Lawful)

The famous objection to atheism found in *The Brothers Karamazov*; the idea that without God or an immortal soul, there is no ultimate basis for moral law.

Leap of Faith

Kierkegaard’s concept of an individual’s commitment to God that is not based on rational proof or institutional dogma but is made in the "teeth of uncertainty."

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

New Atheism

A 21st-century movement that treats religion as a superstition to be defeated by reason and science, often criticized for failing to recognize the moral implications of God’s absence.

Porous Self

In Charles Taylor’s framework, the pre-modern self that was open to enchantment, spirits, and the direct influence of the transcendent.

Reductio ad Absurdum

A form of argument that attempts to show a statement is true by showing that a false or absurd result follows from its denial (used by Dostoevsky regarding faith and morality).

Religionless Christianity

A concept developed by Dietrich Bonhoeffer suggesting that faith should not rely on God to fill the gaps in human knowledge or serve as a "hypothesis."
