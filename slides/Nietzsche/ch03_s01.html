The Dangerous Perhaps (Nietzsche)

Chapter 3, Section 1

Nietzsche’s Master and Slave Morality

Study Slides

Genealogy vs. Advocacy

Genealogy

This is a historical and psychological tracing of where values come from. It examines the needs, conditions, and human types that produced specific moral frameworks.

Advocacy

This is the endorsement or recommendation of a specific value system.

Master Morality vs. Slave Morality

Feature. Master Morality

Slave Morality

Core Orientation. Self-affirming: "I am good."

Reactive: "He is evil, therefore I am good."

Primary Traits. Vitality, honesty, creative power, vigorous.

Humility, pity, self-denial.

Psychological Origin. Aristocratic spirit.

*Ressentiment* (resentment).

Contribution. Beauty and vitality.

Depth, "soul," and the interior life.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Genealogy

The study of the origins and historical development of values, rituals, or beliefs.

Genetic Fallacy

The logical error of asserting that a belief is false or invalid simply because its source or origin is considered suspect or "all-too-human."

Internalization of Instinct

The process by which slave morality forced human instincts inward, creating the "soul" and the "interior life."

Master Morality

A value system rooted in the aristocratic spirit, characterized by self-affirmation, vitality, and creative power.

Ressentiment

A French term for resentment; the psychological state of the "slavish spirit" that defines itself by negating and labeling the powerful as "evil."

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Revaluation of Values

The historical process, initiated during the "slave revolt," where traditional aristocratic values were overturned and replaced by a new moral framework.

Slave Morality

A reactive value system born from the needs of the powerless, emphasizing virtues like humility, pity, and self-denial.
