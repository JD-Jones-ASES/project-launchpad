The Cosmic Metaphor

The death of God is likened to unchaining the earth from the sun. It results in a lack of direction (no "up" or "down") and the onset of "infinite nothingness."

The Timing

The madman concludes he has "come too early." The event has occurred, but its full impact—the collapse of European morality—is still traveling like the light of distant stars.

The Shadow of God (GS 108-109)

Persistence of Values

Modern secular beliefs—such as human rights, equality, the dignity of the individual, and the belief in "progress"—are essentially Christian values surviving without their foundation.

The Inversion of Plato

While Plato’s philosopher leaves the cave to find the "sun" of Truth, Nietzsche’s philosopher discovers the sun was a shadow, and the "cave" (the material world) is all there is.

The Religious Mood and "Religious Neurosis" (BGE 46-56)

Rung. Subject of Sacrifice

Context

First Rung. Human Beings

Ancient religions sacrificing loved ones to a deity.

Second Rung. Natural Instincts

Asceticism; sacrificing sensuality and "nature" to God.

Third Rung. God Himself

The ultimate sacrifice: giving up the source of meaning for "nothingness" (intellectual honesty).

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Amor Fati

"Love of fate"; the ability to affirm life exactly "as it was and is" for all eternity.

Ascetic Ideal

A regimen of solitude, fasting, and sexual abstinence used in the "religious neurosis" to test the strength of the will through self-subjugation.

Chaos (of Nature)

The general character of the world in the absence of order, structure, form, beauty, or wisdom; nature as "undeified."

Death of God

A cultural event marking the collapse of the shared belief in the Christian God as the foundation for Western morality and meaning.

Free Spirit

An adventurous thinker capable of contemplating the "period of gloom" following God's death with "cheerfulness" and a sense of liberation.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Nihilism (Nihilismus)

The condition resulting from the collapse of old values when no new values have been created; the feeling that "everything is meaningless."

Religious Neurosis

Nietzsche’s term for the psychological patterns of self-denial and "anti-naturalness" found in religious life.

Will to Truth

The drive for intellectual honesty, cultivated by Christianity, which eventually led to the deconstruction of Christian metaphysics.
