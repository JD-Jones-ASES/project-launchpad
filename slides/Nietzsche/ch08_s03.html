The Dangerous Perhaps (Nietzsche)

Chapter 8, Section 3

A Study Guide to Nietzsche’s The Antichrist: The Transvaluation of All Values

Study Slides

Nietzschean Definitions of Morality

What is good?

Anything that increases the feeling of power, the will to power, and power itself in man.

What is evil?

Anything that originates from weakness.

What is happiness?

The feeling that power is increasing and that resistance is being overcome.

Virtue (*Virtù*)

Excellence and mastery in the Renaissance sense, free from Christian moral connotations or "moral acid."

The Critique of Pity

As a Depressant

Pity drains strength and multiplies the suffering in the world. A man loses power when he pities.

Anti-Evolutionary

Pity thwarts natural selection by preserving that which is "ripe for destruction." It protects the "botched" and the weak at the expense of the healthy.

The Schopenhauer Connection

Nietzsche agrees with Schopenhauer that pity denies life, though Nietzsche views this denial as an illness to be purged.

Christianity vs. Buddhism

Actuality

Christianity relies on imaginary causes (God, soul, free will) and imaginary effects (sin, grace, salvation). Buddhism deals with the reality of suffering and provides techniques to reduce it.

Nature of Suffering

Buddhism is a "world-weary" response to the end of a civilization, whereas Christianity creates exhaustion through guilt.

The Concept of God

Nietzsche argues that a healthy nation projects its power into its God. Christianity "castrated" God into a deity of pure goodness—a "democrat among gods" and a "staff for the weary."

The Redeemer Type (Jesus)

Non-Resistance

The core of Jesus's practice was "resist not evil." He did not fight, judge, or condemn.

The Inner Kingdom

To Jesus, the "kingdom of God" was not a future event or a place beyond death, but a "state of the heart" and an inner experience of love and equality.

The Saintly Anarchist

Nietzsche views Jesus as a political criminal in the eyes of the Jewish hierarchy because he rejected all castes, privileges, and formalisms.

The Dysangelist (Paul)

The Invention of Dogma

Paul shifted the focus from Jesus’s *life* to his *death* and "resurrection."

The Lie of Immortality

Paul used the doctrine of personal immortality and "judgment" as a means to gain power over the masses.

Ressentiment

Paul’s theology is framed as a "revolt of all creatures that creep on the ground" against everything noble and high-spirited.

The Renaissance and the Reformation

The Renaissance

Nietzsche views this era as the great "transvaluation of Christian values"—an attempt to enthrone noble values at the heart of the Church (symbolized by the image of "Cæsar Borgia as pope").

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Chandala

The lowest caste in Hindu society; used by Nietzsche to describe the "lowly" and resentful masses who use Christianity to revolt against the noble.

Décadence

A condition of declining vitality and exhaustion where an organism or culture chooses what is harmful to its own survival.

Macht (Power)

Comparable to the Greek *dynamis*; potency, the capacity to act, create, and overcome resistance.

Pathos of Distance

The aristocratic feeling of superiority and the recognition of the gap between higher and lower types of humanity.

Ressentiment

A deep-seated, poisonous hatred and resentment felt by the weak toward the strong, which creates new "values" (like humility) to justify its own weakness.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Sirocco

A hot, oppressive wind; used metaphorically for the stifling effect of modern virtues and "virtuous dirtiness."

Umwertung aller Werte

The Transvaluation of All Values; the project of overturning Christian morality in favor of life-affirming values.

Wille zur Macht

The Will to Power; identified by Nietzsche as the fundamental instinct of life—the drive for growth, survival, and the accumulation of forces.
