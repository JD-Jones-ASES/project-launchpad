The Dangerous Perhaps (Nietzsche)

Chapter 2, Section 1

The Prejudices of Philosophers

Study Slides

Specific Philosophical Critiques

Target. Core Critique

Nietzsche's Argument

The Dogmatists. Failed "courtship" of Truth.

Dogmatists approach Truth with "clumsy importunity," assuming it can be won through rigid, heavy-handed logic rather than subtlety.

The Stoics. Living "according to Nature."

Nature is indifferent and purposeless. The Stoics projected their own values (order, rationality) onto Nature and then claimed to "discover" them there.

Descartes. Cogito ergo sum ("I think, therefore I am").

Descartes assumes "I" is the cause of thought. Nietzsche argues "a thought comes when 'it' wishes," and the "I" is merely a grammatical habit or interpretation.

Metaphysicians. Belief in Antitheses of Values.

Metaphysicians assume truth and error are opposites. Nietzsche suggests they may be "essentially identical" or that "untruth" might have higher value for life.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/3)

Aphorism

A short, pithy statement expressing a general truth; in Nietzsche’s work, these are compressed arguments or thought experiments.

Causa Prima

Latin for "first cause"; the ultimate or original cause of everything.

Cogito ergo sum

"I think, therefore I am"; the foundational certainty of Cartesian philosophy that Nietzsche critiques as a nest of assumptions.

De omnibus dubitandum

"Everything is to be doubted"; the motto of Cartesian philosophy.

Dogmatism

The tendency to assert principles as undeniable truths without critically examining underlying assumptions.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/3)

Logos

A Greek term meaning "word," "reason," or "principle of order"; used by Stoics to describe the rational principle pervading the cosmos.

Perspectivism

The view that all knowledge and "truth" are filtered through the specific perspective and interests of the knower.

Puerilism

Childish or immature thinking.

Synthetic judgments a priori

Knowledge or reasoning independent of experience; Nietzsche refers to these as "logical fictions" necessary for life.

Tyronism

The state of being a beginner or novice; amateurishness.

Glossary of Important Terms (3/3)

Wille zur Wahrheit

"The will to truth"; the drive that compels philosophers to seek objective knowledge.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is an aphorism in the context of Nietzsche's writing?

A short, pithy statement that serves as a compressed argument, provocation, or thought experiment.

According to Nietzsche, what do great philosophers like Plato and Kant mistake for universal reason?

Their own hidden desires, unconscious needs, and deeply personal commitments.

What provocative metaphor does Nietzsche use at the beginning of "Beyond Good and Evil" to describe Truth?

He supposes that Truth is a woman who has not allowed herself to be won by dogmatists.

Term: Wille zur Wahrheit

Definition: The "Will to Truth," or the drive that compels philosophers to seek truth above all else.

Instead of asking if a claim is objectively true, what "prior question" does Nietzsche ask about the Will to Truth?

He asks about the value of this Will and why we do not prefer untruth or uncertainty instead.

The belief that things of highest value must have a separate, "pure" origin is what Nietzsche calls the typical prejudice of _____.

metaphysicians

Key Concepts (2/2)

Nietzsche describes the biased, limited viewpoints of metaphysicians as "_____ perspectives."

frog

Under what condition does Nietzsche believe a "false" opinion might be more valuable than a true one?

When the opinion is life-furthering, life-preserving, or species-preserving.

Nietzsche argues that every great philosophy is actually a form of "involuntary and unconscious _____."

auto-biography

What does Nietzsche identify as the "true vital germ" from which every philosophical system grows?

The moral (or immoral) purpose of the originator.

How does Nietzsche characterize the difference between a "scholar" and a "philosopher" regarding their personal involvement in their work?

Scholars are often impersonal "clock-work," while a philosopher's morality reveals the deep order of their nature.

Why does Nietzsche find the Stoic command to "live according to nature" self-contradictory?

Nature is boundlessly indifferent, whereas living requires valuing, preferring, and being limited.
