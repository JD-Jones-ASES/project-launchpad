The Life and Times of Friedrich Nietzsche: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Study Slides

Early Life and Family Background

Paternal Influence

Nietzsche's father died when Friedrich was four years old from "softening of the brain." This event haunted Nietzsche, leading to a lifelong fear that he had inherited a neurological weakness.

The Naumburg Household

Following his father's death, Nietzsche was raised in a household of women, including his mother, sister Elisabeth, grandmother, and two aunts.

Academic Excellence and Health

Schulpforta

At fourteen, he attended the prestigious Schulpforta boarding school, which emphasized a rigorous classical curriculum in Greek and Latin. This training permanently shaped his prose style.

The University Years

He studied classical philology at the University of Bonn and later Leipzig under Friedrich Ritschl.

Chronic Illness

During his student years, Nietzsche began suffering from migraines and digestive ailments. He produced most of his philosophical work while in significant physical pain.

Arthur Schopenhauer

The Will

Schopenhauer argued that reality is a blind, purposeless, irrational "Will" that drives nature.

Nietzsche’s Departure

While Nietzsche adopted Schopenhauer's analysis of suffering, he eventually rejected Schopenhauer's pessimistic conclusion (denying life) in favor of life-affirmation.

Richard Wagner

Early Adulation

Nietzsche’s first book, *The Birth of Tragedy* (1872), was heavily influenced by Wagner’s artistic vision of redeeming German culture through art.

The Break

Nietzsche eventually grew repulsed by Wagner’s increasing German nationalism, anti-Semitism, megalomania, and his opera *Parsifal*, which Nietzsche viewed as a "capitulation to Christianity."

Lou Salomé

The "Holy Trinity"

Nietzsche, Rée, and Salomé formed an intellectual commune. A famous staged photograph from this era depicts Salomé holding a whip while Nietzsche and Rée are harnessed to a cart.

Rejection

Nietzsche proposed marriage multiple times; Salomé refused, preferring intellectual friendship. The relationship ended in late 1882, leaving Nietzsche devastated.

The Collapse into Madness

Wahnsinnsbriefe

Following his collapse, he sent "madness letters" to friends, signing them as "Dionysos" or "The Crucified."

Final Years

Nietzsche spent his last eleven years in a state of mental decline, cared for by his mother and sister. He died on August 25, 1900.

Posthumous Legacy and the "Sister Problem"

Glossary of Key Terms and Figures (1/2)

Bernhard Förster

Nietzsche’s brother-in-law; an anti-Semitic agitator who founded a failed "pure" Aryan colony in Paraguay.

Classical Philology

The study of ancient Greek and Latin texts; Nietzsche’s original academic profession.

Dionysos

One of the names Nietzsche used to sign his "madness letters" following his 1889 collapse.

Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche

Nietzsche’s sister; responsible for editing his work to fit nationalist agendas and for managing his literary estate.

Friedrich Ritschl

The professor at Leipzig who recognized Nietzsche’s brilliance and recommended him for the Basel professorship.

Glossary of Key Terms and Figures (2/2)

Lou Salomé

A brilliant Russian intellectual and author who rejected Nietzsche's marriage proposals but remained a significant intellectual figure of her generation.

Parsifal

Richard Wagner’s final opera, which Nietzsche viewed as a betrayal of his earlier artistic ideals and a return to Christian themes.

The Will to Power

A posthumous book compiled by Elisabeth from Nietzsche's notebooks; often incorrectly viewed as his definitive systematic work.

Wahnsinnsbriefe

The "madness letters" sent by Nietzsche in early 1889, marking the final stage of his mental health.

The World as Will and Representation

The seminal work by Arthur Schopenhauer that served as a major intellectual "thunderbolt" for the young Nietzsche.
