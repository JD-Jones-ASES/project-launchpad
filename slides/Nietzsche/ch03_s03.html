Nietzsche’s Two Moralities: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Study Slides

Master Morality (*Herrenmoral*)

Origin

The ruling aristocratic class.

Primary Distinction

"Good" vs. "Bad."

Core Values

Strength, health, beauty, pride, self-affirmation, and a "pathos of distance."

Direction of Valuation

Spontaneous and active. It begins with a "yes" to oneself and one's own power. The "bad" is merely a secondary afterthought—a pale foil representing that which is common, weak, or contemptible.

Key Traits

The noble man regards himself as a "creator of values." He honors what he recognizes in himself and acts out of a "super-abundance of power" rather than pity.

Slave Morality (*Sklavenmoral*)

Origin

The oppressed, the suffering, and the powerless.

Primary Distinction

"Good" vs. "Evil."

Core Values

Humility, pity, patience, diligence, friendliness, and utility.

Direction of Valuation

Reactive and negative. it begins with a "no" to the "other" (the master/oppressor). It defines the powerful as "evil" and only then, by negation, defines the self as "good."

Key Traits

Driven by *ressentiment*—a creative hatred that seeks imaginary revenge. It views the "good" man as the "safe" man: good-natured, easily deceived, and perhaps a little stupid.

The Parable of the Lambs and Birds of Prey

The Lambs

Dislike the birds of prey for eating them and conclude that birds of prey are "evil." They further conclude that anyone who is the opposite of a bird of prey—namely, a lamb—is "good."

The Birds of Prey

Do not bear a grudge against the lambs; in fact, they like them because they are "tasty."
