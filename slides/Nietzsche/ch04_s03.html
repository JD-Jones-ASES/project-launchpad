Nietzsche’s Genealogy of Morals, Second Essay

Study Slides

The Sovereign Individual and the "Animal That Can Promise"

Active Forgetfulness

Forgetfulness is not merely a passive "force of inertia" but an active, positive power of obstruction. It allows the mind to clear space for "noble functions" like foresight and predetermination.

Memory of the Will

To keep a promise, the human animal had to develop an "active refusal to get rid of" a pledged word, creating a "long chain of the will" that connects an original intention to a future act.

The Sovereign Individual

The "ripest fruit" of social discipline. This individual is autonomous, "super-moral" (meaning they have moved beyond the morality of custom), and possesses a proud consciousness of power and freed...

Mnemotechnics: The Technology of Memory

The Axiom of Pain

"Only that which never stops hurting remains in his memory." Nietzsche describes "mnemotechnics" as the historical use of blood, torture, and sacrifice to fix ideas into the human mind.

The Morality of Custom (*Sittlichkeit der Sitte*)

This prehistoric work of man on himself used "social strait-waistcoats" to make humans regular, uniform, and calculable.

Historical Cruelty

The severe penal laws of history (stoning, breaking on the wheel, boiling in oil) were the means by which humanity eventually attained "reason" and mastery over emotions.

The Origin of Guilt: Debt and Compensation

The Debtor-Creditor Relationship

This is the oldest human relationship. It predates social organization and religion. It is based on the idea that "everything has its price" and "all can be paid for."

Suffering as Compensation

In early history, if a debtor could not pay, the creditor was granted the right to inflict pain on the debtor. The "pleasure of the violation" served as the equivalent exchange for the debt.

Justice

Initially, justice was the "goodwill" between people of equal power to come to terms through settlements and to compel the less powerful to do the same.

The Fluid Meaning of Punishment

Fluidity of Meaning

The procedure is relatively stable and old, but the meaning is constantly re-interpreted by superior forces (the Will to Power).

The Failure of Remorse

Nietzsche argues that punishment rarely produces "bad conscience" or remorse. Instead, it "hardens and numbs," sharpening the criminal’s feeling of alienation.

Bad Conscience: The Internalization of Instinct

Internalization

When instincts for aggression, cruelty, and adventure could no longer be discharged outwardly (due to the "bulwarks" of social peace), they turned inward.

The Origin of the "Soul"

The "whole inner world" expanded as man's external outlets were obstructed. Man became his own "torture-chamber."

The State

The Moralization of Guilt and the Concept of Sin

Ancestral Debt

Primitive tribes felt a debt to their ancestors, paying them back through sacrifices and obedience. Over time, these ancestors were transfigured into gods.

Infinite Debt

With the rise of the Christian God (the "record god"), the sense of guilt reached its "sublime zenith." The debt became "inexpiable" (unpayable).

The Stroke of Genius

Christianity provides a "paradoxical and awful expedient": God sacrifices himself to pay the debt of man. The creditor plays the "scapegoat" for the debtor out of "love."
