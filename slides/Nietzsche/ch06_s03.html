Avoidance of Martyrdom

Nietzsche warns philosophers against suffering for "truth's sake." He argues that martyrdom "animalizes and brutalizes" the thinker, turning them into a "stage-and-tribune-bawler."

Morality as a Symptom of Timidity

Conventional morality is seen as a refusal to face the danger of existence, providing ready-made answers to avoid independent thought.

The Strength of Mind

A mind's strength is measured by how much "truth" it can endure without needing it "attenuated, veiled, sweetened, damped, and falsified."

The Discipline of Great Suffering

Suffering as Elevation

He asserts that all human elevation has come from the "discipline of great suffering."

Creature vs. Creator

In man, there is both the "creature" (the matter, clay, and chaos that must suffer) and the "creator" (the sculptor and the hardness of the hammer).

Reverse Sympathy

Nietzsche’s sympathy is not for social distress but for how man "dwarfs himself" by pursuing comfort and well-being.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Esoteric

Knowledge or perspectives intended for or likely to be understood by only a small number of people with specialized knowledge or high "rank."

Exoteric

Knowledge or perspectives viewed from the "outside" or "from below," suitable for the general populace.

Freier Geist (Free Spirit)

A thinker who has liberated themselves from inherited values and thinks from a position of true independence.

Freigeist (Freethinker)

A term Nietzsche uses pejoratively for Enlightenment rationalists who remain dogmatic despite their rejection of traditional religion.

Intellectual Conscience (intellektuelles Gewissen)

The demand for absolute honesty in thinking and the refusal to accept "veiled" or "sweetened" truths.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Martyrdom of the Philosopher

The act of sacrificing oneself for "the truth," which Nietzsche views as a "deterioration" into an agitator or actor.

Philosophical Workers

Scholars (like Kant and Hegel) who organize and formalize existing values rather than creating new ones.

Tartuffery

Hypocrisy, particularly religious hypocrisy; a term derived from Molière’s character Tartuffe.

Will to Power

The underlying drive that Nietzsche identifies in the act of "knowing" as "creating" and "law-giving."

Key Concepts (1/2)

In Nietzsche's terminology, how does a "free spirit" differ from a "freethinker"?

The free spirit has outgrown inherited values, while the freethinker is a shallow rationalist who merely adopts new dogmas.

What is the primary characteristic of the "great emancipation" that defines a free spirit?

A violent and painful severing of bonds from once-revered traditions, piety, and values.

According to Beyond Good and Evil 24, what is the "granitelike foundation" upon which human knowledge is built?

A far more powerful will to ignorance, simplification, and falsification.

Nietzsche argues that language is often awkward because it speaks in terms of opposites rather than _____.

Degrees and refinements of gradation.

What does Nietzsche define as "Tartuffery" in the context of morality?

Hypocrisy, specifically the religious variety.

Why does Nietzsche warn philosophers to "beware of martyrdom" for the truth?

It destroys the innocence and neutrality of the conscience and makes the thinker headstrong against objections.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Nietzsche suggests that there is more truthfulness in a philosopher's _____ than in their favorite doctrines.

Interrogative marks (questions).

What is the purpose of the "masks" and "ruses" Nietzsche advises the free spirit to use?

To ensure they are mistaken for something else or feared, protecting their independence from common misunderstanding.

How does Nietzsche describe the "good solitude" necessary for the free spirit?

A free, wanton, and lightsome solitude that allows one to remain "good" in a personal sense.

According to Nietzsche, what is the unfailing sign that a philosopher has lost their "philosophical humour"?

The presence of moral indignation.

Why does Nietzsche label thinkers like Spinoza and Giordano Bruno as "refined vengeance-seekers"?

He believes their philosophies were poisoned by the long, personal fear and persecution they endured as recluses.

To whom does Nietzsche attribute the privilege of being "independent"?

The strong (the very few).
