Important Terms (1/5)

Audacious

Bold and daring in a way that is cheeky or shocking. (e.g., "they are that audacious, they'll be walking in with their heads knocked off...")

Forrard

A dialect pronunciation of “forward,” meaning ahead in their work. (e.g., "...soon as we've got a bit forrard with our work.")

Battlements

The notched wall along the top of a castle, with raised sections and gaps that soldiers could shoot through while staying protected. (e.g., "...with battlements and lancet windows...")

Lancet windows

Tall, narrow windows with pointed arch tops, common in medieval castles and churches. (e.g., "...with battlements and lancet windows...")

Moat

A deep, wide ditch filled with water surrounding a castle, making it harder for enemies to attack. (e.g., "...across the moat that now lay where the garden had been...")

Important Terms (2/5)

Steel-caps

Helmets made of steel, worn by soldiers. Here, specifically referring to the rounded helmets from the English Civil War (the 1640s). (e.g., "...two men in steel-caps were coming towards him.")

Halidom

An old oath meaning “by my holy relic” or “by all that is holy.” The sort of thing knights said. (e.g., "`By my halidom,' said one, `a brave varlet this!'")

Varlet

An old word for a young servant or attendant. Also used as a mild insult meaning “rascal.” (e.g., "`...a brave varlet this!'")

Belike

An old English word meaning “probably” or “perhaps.” (e.g., "Some outlandish treachery, belike.'")

Hither

An old word meaning “to this place” or “here.” “Come hither” means “come here.” (e.g., "`what brings thee hither?'")

Important Terms (3/5)

Zooks

A mild old-fashioned oath, short for “Gadzooks,” itself a softened version of “God's hooks.” (e.g., "`...none hindereth, and nought lets us to follow. Zooks!'")

Knave

Originally a boy or servant, but by the Middle Ages it meant a dishonest person. (e.g., "`Where dwellest thou, young knave?'")

Forthwith

Immediately, right away, without any delay. (e.g., "And to the leader Robert was dragged forthwith - by the reluctant ear.")

Peninsular War

A war fought between 1807 and 1814 in Spain and Portugal, where British forces fought against Napoleon's French army. (e.g., "...the sword was of the pattern used in the Peninsular War.")

Cuirass

A piece of armour covering the chest and back, made of a front plate and a back plate buckled together. (e.g., "The cuirass was of the time of Charles I...")

Important Terms (4/5)

Heraldry

The system of designing and identifying coats of arms — the special symbols and colours used by noble families. (e.g., "...knew no more of heraldry or archaeology...")

Archaeology

The study of human history and prehistory through the excavation of sites and the analysis of artifacts and other physical remains. (e.g., "...knew no more of heraldry or archaeology...")

Cromwellian

Relating to Oliver Cromwell, who led the Parliamentary side in the English Civil War (1642–1651). (e.g., "...men in Cromwellian steel-caps had said a few low eager words.")

Scathe

Harm, injury, or damage. (e.g., "`Have no fear; thou shalt take no scathe,' he said.")

Men-at-arms

Professional soldiers, especially medieval knights' armed followers. (e.g., "...among these rough men-at-arms?")

Important Terms (5/5)

Grammercy

An old exclamation meaning “grant me mercy” or “thank you very much.” (e.g., "`Grammercy,' began Robert, 'for thy gracious words.'")

The Day's Punishment and Robert's Task

• The children are kept indoors as a punishment for the previous day's disastrous wish involving wings.

• Martha, the caregiver, believes she's doing her duty, seeing their actions as "naughtiness" rather than misfortune, though she dislikes punishing them due to the noise and her own sympathy.

• She plans a treat (cake for tea) and later play with Baby to ease the punishment.

• Robert is the only one allowed out, for a mere half-hour, specifically to make the day's wish.

Encountering the Psammead

• Robert easily finds the Sand-fairy, which is unusually out in the open due to the hot weather, stretching and cleaning itself in a sand-pool.

• The Psammead greets Robert, expressing relief that the children weren't "smashed up" by the wings, but immediately inquires about the others.

• Robert explains their punishment and the limited time he has to make a wish.

Robert's Difficult Wish

• Under pressure, Robert struggles to think of a suitable wish for everyone, especially since they are stuck indoors.

• His mind fills with selfish, trivial wishes (toffee, stamp album, clasp-knife, football, leg-guards, beating up a school bully).

• Desperate, he wishes for the Psammead to grant a wish to one of the others without them having to come to it.

• The Psammead grants this "tremendously hard" remote wish with great effort, collapsing like a pricked bubble. It warns Robert to hurry home to prevent a "silly" wish.

The Journey Home and the Shocking Transformation

• Robert races home, filled with anxiety about what the others might have wished for in his absence. He considers possibilities like pets, chocolate, or even a wasted wish for him to "hurry up."

• He contemplates wishes that would be amusing indoors but finds few options.

• As he approaches home, he discovers his house and garden are gone, replaced by a massive, stately medieval castle with battlements, lancet windows, and eight towers.

First Contact with the Besieging Army

• Robert sees a pale, dust-colored handkerchief waving from a castle window, identifying it as Cyril's.

• Waving back proves unwise, as two men in "steel-caps" (Cromwellian-era helmets) approach him.

• Robert decides not to run, fearing it would be futile and irritate his "foe."

• The men speak in archaic English, calling him a "brave varlet," which Robert, familiar with historical romances, interprets as a compliment despite the potential insult.

• They question his "garb" and "intent," suspecting him of treachery or bearing tidings to the besieged. Robert simply states he wants to go home.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What accidental wish does Robert make at the sand-pit?

He wishes that the others could have their wish without coming to the sand-pit — and the Psammead grants it immediately, using up tremendous effort.

What do the other children wish for while Robert is away?

They wish for a castle, and their house is instantly replaced by a full medieval castle complete with battlements, a moat, and a besieging army.

Who is Sir Wulfric de Talbot?

The magnificent leader of the besieging army outside the castle. He speaks in the language of historical romances and is entirely a product of the Psammead's...

What are battlements, and what purpose do they serve?

Battlements are the notched wall along the top of a castle, with raised sections (merlons) to hide behind and gaps (crenels) to shoot through.

Define 'portcullis' as it appears in the chapter.

A heavy gate made of iron or wood that slides up and down in grooves at a castle entrance. The children's castle has one blocking the gateway.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is a drawbridge, and what is its status in the story?

A bridge over a castle's moat that can be raised to prevent enemies from crossing. The children's drawbridge is up, providing an important defense.

Why can't Sir Wulfric understand Robert's explanation that the army isn't real?

Because Sir Wulfric himself is part of the wish — he cannot comprehend that he doesn't truly exist. The knight believes Robert is mad.

How does Robert get inside the castle?

He uses a second wish from the Psammead: 'I wish I was with the others.' The Psammead, exhausted from the earlier wish, grants it with great effort.

How does Cyril explain why the servants can see the house but the children see the castle?

What critical thing did the children forget to wish for when they wished for a castle?

They forgot to wish for food (provisions) and soldiers (a garrison) to defend it, leaving them hungry and unprotected inside a besieged fortress.
