Important Terms (1/3)

Epidermis

The outermost protective layer of the skin, including the scalp, responsible for barrier function.

Dermis

The layer beneath the epidermis, rich in connective tissue, blood vessels, nerves, hair follicles, and glands.

Subcutaneous Tissue (Superficial Fascia/Connective Tissue Layer)

A dense, fibrous layer beneath the dermis, housing major blood vessels and nerves.

Galea Aponeurotica (Epicranial Aponeurosis)

A strong, fibrous sheet that forms the tendinous connection between the frontalis and occipitalis muscles, allowing superficial scalp movement.

Loose Areolar Tissue

A delicate, highly mobile connective tissue layer between the galea and pericranium, known as the "danger zone" due to its potential for rapid spread of infection.

Important Terms (2/3)

Pericranium

The periosteum (fibrous membrane) covering the external surface of the skull bones, providing nourishment and aiding in bone repair.

Hair Follicle

An epidermal invagination extending into the dermis, responsible for producing hair.

Sebaceous Gland

Glands associated with hair follicles that secrete sebum (oil) to lubricate hair and skin.

Arrector Pili Muscle

Small smooth muscles attached to hair follicles, causing hair to stand erect ("goosebumps").

Alopecia

The medical term for hair loss, which can have various causes and patterns.

Important Terms (3/3)

Dandruff (Pityriasis Capitis)

A common scalp condition characterized by flaky skin, often due to an overgrowth of a yeast-like fungus (Malassezia).

Seborrheic Dermatitis

A common inflammatory skin condition causing red, scaly, and itchy patches, frequently affecting the scalp.

Tinea Capitis (Ringworm of the Scalp)

A fungal infection of the scalp and hair shafts, typically seen in children, leading to scaly patches and hair loss.

Definition

The soft tissue covering the cranium, extending from the supraorbital ridges anteriorly to the superior nuchal lines posteriorly, and laterally to the temporal lines.
