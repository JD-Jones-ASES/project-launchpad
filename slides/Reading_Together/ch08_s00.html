Bigger Than the Baker's Boy

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

The Ineffectiveness of Deliberate Wishing

The children realize that their carefully planned wishes for things like wealth or beauty have not brought them lasting satisfaction or truly useful outcomes.

Cyril's "Spontaneous Wish" Theory

Cyril hypothesizes that the best wishes come unexpectedly during normal play, rather than through strained effort. This introduces a new approach to dealing with the Psammead.

Consequences of Impulsivity

Robert's quick temper and impulsive actions (like ambushing the baker's boy and then wishing in anger) consistently lead to trouble and often to comical or inconvenient outcomes.

Literal Interpretation of Wishes by the Psammead

The Psammead, as usual, grants wishes literally, transforming Robert into a giant when he expresses a desire to be "bigger" than his opponent, without considering the practical implications.

The Narrator's Self-Awareness

Key Concepts (2/2)

Childhood Conflict and Fair Play

The chapter explores themes of childhood squabbles, the unfairness perceived in fights, and the differing interpretations of "chivalry" or gentlemanly conduct.

Important Terms (1/4)

Jackape

Short for "jackanapes," meaning a cheeky, impertinent person or a mischievous troublemaker. Robert is called this for being sarcastic.

Humbugging

Joking around, being dishonest, or talking nonsense. Cyril assures Robert he isn't "humbugging" about his idea.

Water-butt

A large barrel placed outside to collect rainwater from the roof gutters. Cyril stands on one to make his important speech.

The Conquest

Refers to the Norman Conquest of 1066, when William the Conqueror invaded England from France. Cyril compares recalling history facts to thinking of wishes.

Pax

A Latin word meaning "peace." British schoolchildren used it as a truce call during fights. Cyril calls for "pax" after the skirmish with Robert.

Important Terms (2/4)

Cricket ball

The hard leather ball used in cricket, England's most popular summer sport. It's roughly baseball-sized but heavier. Robert claims to have a lump "as big as a cricket ball" over his eye.

Brigandish

Looking like a brigand, which is a bandit or robber. The children's improvised hats with turkey feathers are described as having a "brigandish" effect.

Mail-cart

A sturdy baby carriage originally modeled after the carts used to deliver mail. The Lamb's mail-cart is used as a baggage-wagon in their game.

Banditti

The Italian plural of "bandit," meaning a gang of robbers or outlaws. This term is used to describe the children in their game.

Baker's boy

A young man employed by a bakery to deliver bread and goods door to door—home delivery was the norm in 1902. He becomes the unexpected antagonist.

Important Terms (3/4)

Skipping-rope

A jump rope. British children call it a "skipping-rope" because the activity is called "skipping." Robert uses Jane's skipping-rope to lasso the baker's boy.

The Boys of England

A popular adventure magazine for boys, published weekly from 1866 to 1899. The narrator references it when discussing how hero stories usually go.

Chivalry

The medieval code of honour followed by knights—being brave, courteous, and protective of the weak. The baker's boy is described as lacking this quality.

Mansion House

The official residence of the Lord Mayor of London. There was a famous busy crossing nearby. Robert's giant size is compared to a big policeman who used to be at this crossing.

Norfolk suit

A style of boys' clothing with a belted jacket and matching knee-length trousers, named after the Duke of Norfolk. Robert's Norfolk suit grows with him.

Important Terms (4/4)

Eton collar

A broad, stiff white collar worn outside the jacket, named after Eton College. Robert's large size combined with his small Eton collar makes him look comical.
