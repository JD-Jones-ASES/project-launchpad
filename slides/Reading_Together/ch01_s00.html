Important Terms (1/3)

Fly

A one-horse carriage available for hire, similar to a taxi in modern times.

Chalk-quarry

A large excavation in the ground where chalk, a soft white rock, has been dug out.

Gravel-pit

A large hole or area in the ground from which gravel (small stones and sand) has been extracted. This is a crucial location for the children's adventures.

Coping

The top row of stones or bricks along the edge of a wall or roof; in this context, referring to the decorative ironwork along the roofline.

Excursion train

A special, often cheaper, train trip organized for a day out, typically to popular destinations like the seaside.

Important Terms (2/3)

Maskelyne and Cook's

A famous magic show theatre in London, popular in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, known for stage magicians.

Jasmine

A climbing plant known for its small, often white or yellow, highly fragrant flowers.

Camden Town

A district in north London, where the family lived before moving to the country.

Limekilns

Large ovens used to heat limestone rock to produce lime, a powder used in construction and agriculture.

Oast-houses

Distinctive buildings, often with cone-shaped roofs, used for drying hops, a key ingredient in brewing beer. Common in the Kent countryside.

Important Terms (3/3)

Arabian Nights

A celebrated collection of ancient Middle Eastern folk tales, including famous stories like Aladdin, Ali Baba, and Sindbad the Sailor.

Sand-martins

Small brown-and-white birds known for digging tiny burrow-nests into sandy banks, often found near gravel pits.

The Journey to the White House

Initial Impatience

The children are eager to arrive at their new home, constantly asking "Aren't we nearly there?"

Location

The house is three miles from the station, located at the top of a hill, past a chalk-quarry and before a gravel-pit.

First Impressions

Upon arrival, the children immediately notice the house's white color, roses, and plums, finding it "rather decent." The Baby expresses a desire to "go walky."

Adult vs. Child Reaction

The children scramble out excitedly, while their mother takes her time, focusing on practicalities like luggage and paying the driver.

The Discovery in the Gravel-Pit (1/2)

Parents' Departure

Father leaves suddenly for business, and Mother goes to stay with Granny, who is unwell. This leaves the children feeling "dreadfully quiet and empty."

Idea to Dig

Cyril suggests going to dig in the gravel-pits with their spades, pretending it's the seaside, recalling Father's mention of ancient shells there.

Previous Hesitation

They had previously avoided going into the gravel-pit or chalk-quarry due to fear of parental disapproval, but now, with parents absent, they venture in.

Character Nicknames

The children are introduced with their nicknames: Anthea ("Panther"), Jane ("Pussy"), and the Baby ("Lamb").

Gravel-Pit Description

It's "very large and wide," resembling a "giant's wash-hand basin," with mounds of gravel, holes in the sides, and "sand-martins' little houses."
