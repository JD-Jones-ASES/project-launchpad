Salutation Guidelines

Complimentary Close Choice

The polite sign-off before the signature. Selection depends on formality, with "Sincerely," being universally safe. It is always followed by a comma, and only the first word is capitalized.

Importance of End Notations

These optional but important additions (Enclosure, CC notation, Typist initials) provide crucial context and ensure all necessary parties and documents are accounted for.

Formatting Consistency

It is vital to choose either Block or Modified Block format and use it consistently throughout a single letter. Mixing formats is unprofessional.

Important Terms (1/2)

Block format

A letter layout in which every line—date, address, greeting, body, and closing—starts at the left margin.

Modified block format

A letter layout in which the date, closing, and signature are centered or shifted to the right, while the body paragraphs remain left-aligned.

Salutation

The greeting that opens a letter (e.g., “Dear Ms. Rivera:”).

Complimentary close

The polite sign-off before the signature (e.g., “Sincerely,” or “Regards,”).

Enclosure

A document included with a letter; noted at the bottom with “Enclosure” or “Enc.”

Important Terms (2/2)

CC notation

A note at the bottom of a letter indicating who else receives a copy (from “carbon copy”).

Sincerely

The most common and universally safe complimentary close in business letters.

Regards

A slightly less formal complimentary close, appropriate for ongoing professional relationships.

Professional reference

A person who can speak to your work ethic, skills, and character in a professional context.

The Importance of Letter Formats

• Readers can quickly and easily find key information.

• The writer appears professional and credible.

• The communication carries appropriate weight and authority.

When to Use a Letter Instead of an Email

Legal Significance

For complaints, claims, formal requests, or contractual matters where a verifiable, formal record is essential.

Permanent Record

When you need a document on company letterhead that can be filed physically or electronically as an official record.

Higher Formality

For critical communications such as job applications, resignation letters, official responses to complaints, or formal invitations.

Recipient Expectation

Some organizations (especially government agencies) and individuals (older professionals) still prefer or expect formal letters.

Weight and Authority

To convey a message with more gravity and official status than a digital message.

Crafting Effective Salutations

Punctuation

Always use a colon (:) after the salutation in a business letter, not a comma.

Specificity

Always try to address the recipient by name and appropriate title.

Formatting Consistency

Choose One

Always select either Block format or Modified Block format and use it consistently throughout the entire letter. Never mix elements from different formats within the same document.

Employer/School Guidelines

If your employer or academic institution has a preferred format, always adhere to it.

Default Option

When in doubt about which format to use, Block format is always a safe and widely accepted choice.
