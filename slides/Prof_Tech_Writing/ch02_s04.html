Important Terms (1/2)

Request Email

An email sent to ask someone for specific information, a document, a decision, or a meeting.

Response Email

An email sent in reply to a request, acknowledging it and either fulfilling it or providing a timeline for fulfillment.

Follow-Up Email

An email sent as a reminder or to seek an update on a previous communication or action item, typically after a reasonable waiting period.

Introduction Email

An email sent to present oneself to a new contact, team member, mentor, or professional connection.

Thank-You Email

A brief and genuine email expressing gratitude for assistance, feedback, or a positive interaction.

Important Terms (2/2)

Apology/Correction Email

An email sent to acknowledge a mistake, provide the correct information, and briefly explain the discrepancy.

Scheduling Email

An email used to coordinate dates, times, locations, and preparation details for meetings, appointments, or events.

Passive-Aggressive

A style of communication that indirectly expresses negative feelings or demands, often through veiled sarcasm or subtle hostility (e.g., using phrases like "Per my last email").

Requesting Information

Purpose

To ask someone for a document, an answer, a decision, or a meeting.

Core Principle

Make it easy for the recipient to agree or fulfill the request.

Anatomy of a Strong Request

Example Comparison

A vague request ("Can you send me that report?") contrasts sharply with a specific one, which includes all four elements and sets clear expectations.

Responding to a Request

Core Principle

Respond promptly, even if you can't fulfill the request immediately. A quick acknowledgment is always better than silence.

Elements of a Good Response

Following Up

Importance

A critical professional skill. People are busy, and emails can get overlooked or buried. A polite follow-up is expected, not rude.

Writing Tip: The Polite Follow-Up

Model Example (After a Meeting)

A good follow-up summarizes decisions made, lists clear action items with owners and deadlines, and reiterates the next meeting details.

Introducing Yourself

Contexts

New team members, students to mentors, networking contacts.

Key Rule

Keep it brief and focus on the recipient's relevance, not solely on your life story.

Structure

One sentence about who you are, one sentence about why you are writing to them.

Thanking Someone

Purpose

To build goodwill and strengthen professional relationships.

Key

Ensure the thank-you is genuine and specific, not a generic form letter.

Structure

Apologizing or Correcting

Reality

Mistakes happen in professional settings.

Approach

Address them directly, briefly, and professionally, without excessive self-deprecation or apologies.

The Professional Correction Method

Example Comparison

An over-apologetic email is lengthy and emotional, whereas a professional correction is concise, factual, and solution-oriented.

Scheduling

Purpose

To coordinate meeting times, deadlines, and events efficiently.

Key Elements

Be specific and comprehensive.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How long should you typically wait before sending a follow-up email for a non-urgent request?

You should wait at least 48 hours (two business days) before following up on a non-urgent request.

When writing a follow-up email, what are two key pieces of information you should reference or restate?

You should reference your original email (e.g., 'Following up on my email from Tuesday...') and briefly restate what you need.

What phrases should you specifically avoid when writing a follow-up email, and why?

Avoid phrases like 'Per my last email' or 'As I already mentioned' because they sound passive-aggressive and undermine your professionalism.
