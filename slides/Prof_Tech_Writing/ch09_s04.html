Key Concepts

Executive Summary's Purpose

A concise overview of a report's key points, designed for busy readers who may not read the full document. It acts as a miniature version of the entire report.

Essential Components

An executive summary must include the report's purpose, key findings (important facts/data), conclusions (what the findings mean), and recommendations (what should be done next, if applicable).

The "Send Only This" Test

A self-assessment method where you imagine sending only the executive summary to your boss. If they can understand the situation and make a decision based solely on it, the summary is effective.

Important Terms

Executive Summary

A brief, stand-alone overview placed at the beginning of a report that summarizes its purpose, key findings, conclusions, and recommendations for busy readers.

Key Findings

The most important facts, data, or observations discovered during the research or analysis presented in a report.

Conclusion

An interpretation of what the findings or evidence presented in a report mean, offering insights drawn directly from the data.

Recommendation

A suggestion for a specific action or course of action that should be taken, based on the findings and conclusions of a report.

Informational Report

A type of report that primarily presents facts, data, and findings without making explicit recommendations, leaving the decision-making to the reader.

What is an Executive Summary?

Placement

Always at the very beginning of the report.

Timing

Should be written last, after the entire report is complete and you know all the key points. Writing it early leads to vague summaries.

Writing Connection: Report Structure and Essay Structure

Similarities

Both reports and essays follow a fundamental structure of introduction, body, and conclusion.

Key Difference

Reports explicitly label their sections (e.g., "2.0 Findings"), guiding the reader directly. Essays typically do not use visible headers.
