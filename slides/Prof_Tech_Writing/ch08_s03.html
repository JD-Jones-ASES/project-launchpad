Important Terms (1/3)

Meeting Minutes

The official written record detailing what was discussed, decided, and assigned during a meeting.

Agenda

A list of topics to be discussed during a meeting, serving as a plan.

Action Items

Specific tasks or assignments given to individuals, along with a deadline for completion, arising from a meeting.

Decision

A conclusion or resolution reached and agreed upon by the group during a meeting, often after discussion or a vote.

Key Information Shared

Critical updates, data, or facts presented during a meeting that are important for attendees to remember or act upon.

Important Terms (2/3)

Notetaker

The person responsible for documenting the proceedings of a meeting, specifically creating the minutes.

Third Person

A grammatical perspective where the writer refers to others as "he," "she," "it," or "they," or by name, maintaining an objective distance.

Past Tense

A grammatical form used to describe actions or states that have already occurred at a definite time in the past.

Quorum

The minimum number of members required to be present at a meeting for valid business to be conducted or decisions to be made.

Motion

A formal proposal made by a member of a group during a meeting, requiring discussion and a vote.

Important Terms (3/3)

Seconded

To formally agree with a motion proposed by another member, indicating support for it to be brought to a vote.

What to Capture (and What to Skip)

Past Tense

Minutes describe events that have already occurred. For example, "The committee voted to approve..." not "The committee votes..."

Third Person

The notetaker acts as a neutral observer. Use names or "he/she/they" instead of "I" or "we." For example, "Marcus reported..." not "I reported..." This maintains professionalism and objectivity.

What are the three essential components that every action item needs to prevent it from falling through the cracks?

Every action item needs a 'What' (specific task), a 'Who' (single person responsible), and a 'When' (deadline for completion).

In the 'What/Who/When' framework, what defines an effective 'What' component?

The 'What' must be a specific task clear enough for the person to start immediately without needing clarification.

Who should be assigned an action item, even if a task requires multiple people?

A single person should be assigned as the owner. That person is responsible for ensuring the task gets done, even if they delegate parts of it.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the first guideline for writing clear action items regarding the task owner?

Start with the person's name (e.g., 'Aisha will draft'). This puts the owner front and center, making it harder to ignore the task.

Why is it important to use strong, specific verbs when writing action items?

What type of deadline should always be included in an action item?

A specific date, reflecting urgency if necessary (e.g., 'by end of day Wednesday, October 23'), not vague terms like 'ASAP' or 'when possible'.

What is the guideline regarding the number of tasks per action item?

One task per item. If an item describes multiple actions, break it into separate action items so each can be tracked individually.

Define 'accountability' in the context of tracking action items.

Accountability is the obligation to take responsibility for completing assigned tasks and reporting on progress.
