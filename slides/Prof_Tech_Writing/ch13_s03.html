Context for Numbers

Always provide context for numerical data, answering: What is it? How does it compare? Is it going up or down? This ensures the reader understands the significance of the numbers.

Important Terms (1/2)

Finding

A specific data point, observation, or measurement collected during research; a verifiable fact.

Conclusion

An interpretation of findings, explaining what the data means, identifying patterns, or drawing inferences.

Recommendation

A proposed action or specific step that should be taken, based on the conclusions drawn from the findings.

The Sorting Test

A methodology to categorize statements in a report as findings, conclusions, or recommendations based on whether they are verifiable facts, interpretations, or proposed actions.

Benchmark

A standard or reference point used for comparison to give context to data.

Important Terms (2/2)

Metric

A quantifiable measure used to track and assess performance or progress.

Findings: What the Data Shows

Factual

No interpretation, opinion, or judgment. Present only the raw information.

Specific

Include precise numbers, dates, sources, and quantities. Avoid vague language.

Verifiable

The information can be confirmed by referring back to the original data or source.

Conclusions: What the Findings Mean (1/2)

Supported

Every conclusion must be directly traceable to one or more specific findings. If you can't point to the data, it's an opinion, not a conclusion.

Logical

The interpretation must follow directly and reasonably from the data without overreaching or making unsupported leaps.

Clearly Distinguished

Use phrases like "This suggests," "These figures indicate," or "The data points to" to signal that you are moving from fact to interpretation.

Incorrect (Finding disguised as Conclusion)

"138 students drive themselves to school." (This is a fact, a finding.)

Correct (Finding)

"138 students (16.3%) drive themselves, with 63% of those drivers in twelfth grade."

Recommendations: What to Do About It

Specific

Clearly state what needs to be done.

Supported

Directly linked to the conclusions.

Practical

Feasible and realistic to implement.

Prioritized

If multiple recommendations, indicate their order of importance or urgency.

The Sorting Test: A Practical Tool for Clarity

Can I verify it with raw data?

If yes, it's a Finding.

Does it interpret what the data means?

If yes, it's a Conclusion.

Does it propose an action someone should take?

If yes, it's a Recommendation.

What is the guideline for choosing between vertical and horizontal bar charts?

Use vertical bars for a few categories with short labels. Use horizontal bars for many categories or categories with long labels.

When should you use a line chart?

To show trends over time, answering questions like 'Is this going up, down, or staying flat?' (e.g., monthly revenue, enrollment over several years).

Plot several lines on the same chart, using distinct colors for each, and include a clear legend to differentiate them.
