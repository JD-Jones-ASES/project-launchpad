Chapter 9, Section 1

Types of Reports

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Purpose of Reports

Reports universally aim to present organized information for specific purposes like decision-making, tracking progress, or understanding events.

Choosing the Right Report

Identifying the correct type of report is crucial before writing to effectively meet the situation's needs.

Progress Reports

These documents track what has been accomplished, what is currently underway, and what remains to be done on a project, providing regular updates without requiring meetings.

Three Sections of a Progress Report

Always cover: 1) Completed work (since last report), 2) Current work (actively underway, including challenges), and 3) Upcoming work (what's next, with deadlines and responsibilities).

Honesty in Progress Reports

It's critical to report problems (delays, budget overruns) factually and early, along with explanations and proposed solutions, to maintain credibility.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Incident Reports

Factual accounts of unexpected events (accidents, failures, breaches) used to prevent recurrence, satisfy legal requirements, support claims, and identify patterns.

The Five W's of an Incident Report (+1)

Essential questions to answer: Who was involved? What happened? When did it happen? Where did it happen? Why did it happen? What actions were taken?

Fact-Based Incident Reporting

Incident reports must be strictly factual, avoiding opinions or assigning blame, allowing facts to speak for themselves.

Status Reports

Similar to progress reports but provide a "snapshot" of the current state of a project or activity, typically shorter and used for quick scanning by managers.

Common Informational Reports

Beyond the main three, many other types exist, such as trip reports, lab reports, inspection reports, and compliance reports, each with a specific informational purpose.

Important Terms (1/4)

Executive summary

A brief overview of a report's key points, written for busy readers who may not read the full document.

Findings

The factual results discovered through research, investigation, or observation.

Scope

The boundaries of what a report covers—and what it does not cover.

Methodology

The process or methods used to gather information for a report.

Appendix

A section at the end of a report containing supplementary material (raw data, forms, detailed charts) that supports the main text.

Important Terms (2/4)

Data

Facts, numbers, measurements, or observations collected for analysis.

Evidence

Information that supports or proves a claim or finding.

Attribution

Giving credit to the source of information, data, or ideas.

Citation

A formal reference to a source, following a specific format (APA, MLA, etc.).

Table

A grid of rows and columns used to organize data.

Important Terms (3/4)

Figure

Any visual element in a report—charts, graphs, diagrams, photographs—that is not a table.

Recommendation

A suggested course of action based on the report's findings and conclusions.

Conclusion

An interpretation of what the findings mean, drawn from the evidence presented.

Abstract

A short summary of a report's purpose, methods, findings, and conclusions—similar to an executive summary but more common in academic and scientific writing.

Status report

A regular update on the current state of a project or activity.

Important Terms (4/4)

Progress report

A document that describes what has been accomplished on a project, what is currently underway, and what remains to be done.

Incident report

A factual account of an unexpected event (accident, equipment failure, security breach) documenting what happened, when, and what actions were taken.

Progress Reports

Completed Work

Details what has been finished since the last report. This section requires specificity, including dates, quantities, and clearly defined milestones.

Current Work

Describes tasks or activities that are actively underway. It's crucial to mention any challenges, obstacles, or delays encountered.

Upcoming Work

Outlines what is planned to happen next. This should include future deadlines and assign responsibility for specific tasks to individuals or teams.

Incident Reports (1/2)

Prevent recurrence

By understanding what happened, measures can be put in place to stop similar incidents in the future.

Satisfy legal or regulatory requirements

Many industries have specific reporting obligations for certain types of incidents.

Support insurance claims

Detailed reports provide essential evidence for claims processes.

Identify patterns

If similar incidents occur repeatedly, the reports can help identify underlying systemic issues.

Status Reports (1/2)

Recurring meetings

Like "Monday morning status updates."

Organizations managing multiple simultaneous projects

Enabling managers to quickly scan and assess the current state of various initiatives.

Current status

(e.g., On track, At risk, Behind schedule, Complete)

Key metrics or numbers

Status Reports (2/2)

Blockers

Any obstacles or issues preventing further progress.

Next steps

Planned immediate actions.

Other Informational Reports

Trip Reports

Summarize the purpose, activities, and outcomes of business travel, conferences, or site visits.

Lab Reports

Document the purpose, methods, results, and conclusions of scientific experiments (a common report type in academic settings).

Meeting Summaries

Capture key decisions, discussions, and action items from meetings, especially important or formal ones.

Inspection Reports

Record the condition of equipment, facilities, or products after a formal inspection process.

Compliance Reports

Document an organization's adherence to required standards, laws, or regulations.
