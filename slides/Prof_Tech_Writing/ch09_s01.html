Chapter 9, Section 1: Data and Evidence

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

The Importance of Evidence

The core difference between a useful report and a useless one is whether claims are supported by evidence. Reports rely on facts, not opinions.

The Evidence Rule

Every significant claim made in a report must be supported by verifiable data. If a claim cannot be proven with data, it should either be supported or softened.

Types of Evidence

Effective Data Presentation

Instead of burying numbers in text, use tables and figures to present data clearly and accessibly.

Rules for Tables and Figures

Key Concepts (2/2)

Attribution and Credibility

Always give credit to the source of information, data, or ideas, especially when using outside sources. This is typically done with brief in-text references.

Important Terms (1/2)

Data

Facts, numbers, measurements, or observations collected for analysis.

Evidence

Information that supports or proves a claim or finding—numbers, dates, measurements, observations, or references to documented sources.

Table

A grid of rows and columns used to organize data, best for precise numerical comparisons.

Figure

Any visual element in a report—charts, graphs, diagrams, photographs—that is not a table, best for showing trends or relationships.

Attribution

Giving credit to the source of information, data, or ideas.

Important Terms (2/2)

Citation

A formal reference to a source, following a specific format (e.g., APA, MLA), used for verification and to avoid plagiarism.

Presenting Data Effectively: Tables and Figures

When to use a Table

Tables are ideal for presenting precise values and allowing readers to easily look up specific data points or compare numbers across different categories. They excel at organizing exact figures.

When to use a Figure

Figures, such as charts, graphs, diagrams, or photographs, are best for illustrating trends, showing proportions, or highlighting relationships within data. They tell a visual story.
