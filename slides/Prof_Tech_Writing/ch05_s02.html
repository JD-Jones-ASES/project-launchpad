Important Terms (1/2)

Specification

A document that defines the exact standards or requirements a product, system, or material must meet. It is a blueprint for something that will be created.

Shall

A keyword in specifications indicating a mandatory requirement that must be met. Failure to comply means the product does not meet the specification.

Should

A keyword in specifications indicating a recommended requirement that ought to be met. Non-compliance requires justification but isn't an automatic disqualification.

May

A keyword in specifications indicating an optional requirement or feature that can be included but is not mandatory.

Performance Specification

A type of specification that defines what a product must do, such as its speed, capacity, accuracy, or durability.

Important Terms (2/2)

Material Specification

A type of specification that defines what a product must be made of, detailing the specific substances or alloys.

Dimensional Specification

A type of specification that defines a product's size and shape, including precise measurements and geometric features.

Quality Specification

A type of specification that defines standards for a product's appearance, finish, and consistency, ensuring visual and tactile qualities.

Tolerance

The acceptable range of variation from a nominal (target) measurement, typically expressed as a plus-or-minus value (e.g., $\pm 0.1$ mm).

What is a Specification?

Education

A school district specifies minimum dimensions for new desks.

Software

A software company specifies performance criteria (e.g., search speed) for new features.

Civil Engineering

A city specifies the compacted thickness of asphalt for road construction.

The Language of Specifications: Shall, Should, and May

Shall (Mandatory)

This indicates an absolute requirement. The product must meet this condition without any flexibility. Failure to comply means the product is unacceptable.

Should (Recommended)

This indicates a recommended requirement. The product ought to meet this condition, and any deviation requires a strong justification. Non-compliance does not automatically disqualify the product...

May (Optional)

This indicates an optional feature or requirement. The designer or builder has the choice to include it but is not obligated to.

Writing Tip: Make Every Requirement Testable

Not Testable

"The screen shall be bright enough."

Testable

"The screen shall produce a minimum luminance of 500 nits at maximum brightness."

Tolerances and Ranges

Notation

A tolerance is usually written as a plus-or-minus value ($\pm$) after the nominal measurement.

Tighter Tolerances (smaller variation)

More expensive and time-consuming to achieve, requiring specialized equipment and processes. Often critical for high-precision components (e.g., jet engine parts).

Looser Tolerances (larger variation)

Less expensive and faster to produce, suitable for components where exactness is not critical for function (e.g., a pen cap diameter).

What is a crucial characteristic every requirement in a specification should possess to be useful?

Every requirement must be testable. You should be able to describe how to prove it is true.

Transform the non-testable requirement 'The screen shall be bright enough' into a testable one.

'The screen shall produce a minimum luminance of 500 nits at maximum brightness.'
