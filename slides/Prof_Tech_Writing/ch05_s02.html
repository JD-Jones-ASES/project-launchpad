The Language of Specifications

Testable Requirements

Every requirement in a specification must be measurable and verifiable. If you can't describe how to test it, it's too vague.

Types of Specifications

Tolerances

The acceptable range of variation from a nominal (target) measurement. Tighter tolerances increase cost and time; it's crucial to specify the loosest tolerance that still meets functional needs.

Important Terms (1/2)

Specification

A document that defines the exact standards and requirements a product, system, or material must meet. It tells you what something *must be*.

Technical Description

A document that explains what an object *is*, how it looks, and how it works.

Shall

A keyword used in specifications to denote a mandatory requirement that absolutely must be met.

Should

A keyword used in specifications to denote a recommended requirement that ought to be met, but non-compliance can be justified.

May

A keyword used in specifications to denote an optional requirement that can be included or omitted at the designer's or builder's discretion.

Important Terms (2/2)

Performance Specification

A type of specification that defines the operational characteristics and capabilities a product must exhibit.

Material Specification

A type of specification that defines the type, composition, and properties of the substances from which a product is to be constructed.

Dimensional Specification

A type of specification that defines the precise size, shape, and spatial arrangement of a product's components.

Quality Specification

A type of specification that defines the standards for a product's aesthetic characteristics, finish, and consistency.

Tolerance

The permissible range of variation from a specified dimension or value. It indicates how much a measurement can deviate from the ideal without affecting functionality or acceptability.

Writing Tip: Make Every Requirement Testable

Core Principle

A specification is only valuable if its requirements can be objectively tested and verified.

How to Achieve Testability

For every requirement, ask: "How would I prove this is true?" If a clear, objective test cannot be described, the requirement is too vague.

Tolerances and Ranges

Definition

In manufacturing, perfect precision is impossible. A tolerance specifies the acceptable range of deviation from a target (nominal) measurement. It's usually expressed as a plus-or-minus value.

Notation

For example, `$10.0 \pm 0.1$ inches` means the actual measurement must fall between 9.9 and 10.1 inches.
