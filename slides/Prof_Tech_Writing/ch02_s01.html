CHAPTER 2, SECTION 1: ANATOMY OF AN EMAIL

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Professional Email as Strategic Communication

Unlike casual emails, professional emails require careful thought and deliberate use of each component to convey professionalism and ensure clarity for the recipient.

The Seven Parts of a Professional Email

Avoiding "Reply-All" Disasters

A critical best practice is to only use "reply-all" if your response is genuinely relevant to *every* recipient on the original email thread, preventing unnecessary clutter in others' inboxes.

Effective Use of Attachments

Always mention any attached files in the body of the email, clearly stating what the file is and why it's included, and double-check that the file is actually attached before sending.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Stating Purpose First

In professional emails, it is crucial to state your main purpose or request in the very first sentence of the body to respect the recipient's time and ensure your message is understood quickly.

Important Terms (1/3)

Subject line

The brief title of your email; the first thing a recipient sees, indicating the email's content.

CC (carbon copy)

Sends a copy of an email to additional recipients; everyone can see who was CC'd, used for keeping others informed.

BCC (blind carbon copy)

Sends a copy to additional recipients without revealing their addresses to others, used for privacy in large group communications.

Greeting

The opening line that addresses the recipient (also called a salutation), setting the email's tone.

Salutation

A formal term for the greeting at the start of an email or letter.

Important Terms (2/3)

Closing

The sign-off phrase at the end of an email (e.g., "Best regards," "Thank you").

Signature block

The formatted block at the bottom of an email containing your name, title, and contact information.

Attachment

A file sent along with an email message.

Thread

A chain of related email messages grouped together by subject.

Reply-all

Responding to every recipient on the email, not just the sender.

Important Terms (3/3)

Forward

Sending a received email to someone who was not an original recipient.

Follow-up

A subsequent message sent to check on a previous request or continue a conversation.

Tone

The attitude or feeling conveyed by the words you choose in your message.

Stakeholder

Any person who has an interest in or is affected by a project, decision, or communication.
