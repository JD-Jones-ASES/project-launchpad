Important Terms

Action item

A specific task assigned to a specific person with a specific deadline; the basic unit of accountability in professional communications.

Directive memo

A memo whose primary purpose is to issue an official instruction or order, requiring specific action from the recipients.

Informational memo

A memo that provides information to the reader but does not require any specific action from them.

Passive voice

A grammatical construction where the subject of the sentence receives the action rather than performing it (e.g., "The reports were submitted"). It often obscures the actor.

Active voice

A grammatical construction where the subject of the sentence performs the action (e.g., "John submitted the reports"). It clearly identifies the actor.

Formatting for Maximum Visibility

Bold the deadline

Make the date jump out immediately. If a reader only sees one thing, it should be the "when."

Use bullet points or numbered lists

Never bury multiple action items within a paragraph. Lists break up text and make items easy to scan.

Start each item with the responsible person's name

This immediately tells the reader who is accountable for each task, improving readability and accountability. "Marcus: Schedule the conference room" is much clearer than a generic sentence.

Separate action items with a clear heading

Use headings like "Action Required" or "Next Steps" to signal exactly where readers should look for tasks.

Common Mistake: The Passive-Voice Action Item

Weak (Passive)

"The quarterly reports should be submitted by the end of the month." (Who submits them?)

Strong (Active)

"All team leads: submit your quarterly reports to Finance by March 31." (Clearly states who, what, to whom, and when.)

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to the textbook, what three essential questions should every professional message answer for the reader?

1. What do I need to know? 2. What do I need to do? 3. By when?

How does the textbook define an 'action item'?

A specific task assigned to a specific person with a specific deadline; the basic unit of accountability in professional communications.

What are the three essential components of an effective action item?

What (the specific task), Who (the person/group responsible), and When (the deadline or timeframe).

Provide an example of a complete and effective action item.

"Submit your updated project timeline (what) to Karen Walsh (who) by Friday, March 14 at 5:00 PM (when)."

What is the consequence if the 'what' component is missing from an action item?

The reader will be confused about the specific task they need to complete.

What is the consequence if the 'who' component is missing from an action item?

It leads to confusion about who is responsible for completing the task, potentially resulting in no one taking action.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the consequence if the 'when' component is missing from an action item?

The reader won't know the required deadline, making it unclear when the task needs to be completed.

Describe a 'vague' action item.

A vague action item lacks a clear owner, a clear task, or a clear deadline, leaving room for confusion and inaction.

Describe a 'specific' action item.

A specific action item assigns an explicit task to an identified person with a definite deadline, leaving no room for misunderstanding.

Why is it crucial to write specific action items rather than vague ones?

Specific action items prevent confusion, ensure accountability, and clarify who does what by when, making them impossible to misunderstand or ignore.

Why should action items be visually distinct from the surrounding text in a professional message?

Readers often scan messages quickly, and visually distinct action items prevent them from being missed or overlooked when buried in a paragraph.
