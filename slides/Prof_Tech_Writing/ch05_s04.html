Key Concepts

Necessity of Visual Aids

Visuals are crucial for showing details like surface texture, internal components, or organized data that would be cumbersome or impossible to describe adequately with words alone.

Diverse Types for Specific Purposes

Different visual aids (photographs, diagrams, schematics, cross-sections, exploded views, tables) serve distinct communicative functions, and choosing the correct type is paramount for clarity.

Tables for Organization and Comparison

Tables are powerful tools for presenting specifications, comparisons, and lists of data in a scannable, organized format, allowing readers to quickly find specific information.

Bill of Materials (BOM)

A common and vital application of tables in technical documentation, a BOM provides a structured list of all parts and materials required to build or assemble a product.

Important Terms (1/2)

Visual Aid

Any graphic element, such as a photograph, diagram, or table, used to supplement written text and enhance understanding.

Photograph

A visual aid that displays the actual appearance of an object or scene, best for showing surface finish, color, or real-world context.

Diagram

A simplified drawing used to highlight specific features, components, or relationships, often depicting internal parts or spatial arrangements.

Schematic

A highly simplified diagram that uses standardized symbols to represent components and their connections, commonly found in electrical or plumbing documentation.

Cross-section

A view that shows the interior of an object as if it has been sliced open, revealing internal structure, layers, and wall thickness.

Important Terms (2/2)

Exploded View

A diagram that shows all parts of an assembly separated along an axis, as if gently pulled apart, often used for assembly instructions.

Table

An organized arrangement of data in rows and columns, ideal for presenting specifications, comparisons, or lists.

Caption

A brief, descriptive title or explanation that accompanies a figure or table.

Column Headers

Labels at the top of columns in a table, indicating the type of data contained within that column.

Bill of Materials (BOM)

A comprehensive, structured list detailing every part and material needed to construct a product, often presented as a table.

Definition

A structured, comprehensive list of every part, sub-assembly, and raw material required to manufacture or assemble a product.

Purpose

Importance of Notes

A BOM often includes a "Notes" section for additional critical information like quality standards (e.g., plywood grade), tolerances (e.g., dimension accuracy), or specific assembly instructions.

What is the consequence of choosing the wrong type of visual aid?

Choosing the wrong visual aid wastes the reader's time and may confuse, rather than clarify, the information.

Why is it crucial to reference visual aids in the text?

A visual aid not referenced in the text is often skipped by readers. Referencing ensures readers notice and understand the visual's relevance to the content.

According to the rules for integrating visuals, when should a figure or table be mentioned in the text?

Every figure and table must be referenced in the text before the reader encounters it on the page.

Beyond simply referencing a visual aid, what else should a technical writer do?

Explain what to notice in the visual and why it matters, rather than just saying 'See Figure X.' Guide the reader to the key information.

What labeling elements are essential for figures and tables?

Every figure needs a number and a descriptive caption. Every part of a diagram needs a label. Every table needs clear column headers.
