Important Terms

Weasel Words

Vague, hedging words that sound meaningful but convey no specific information, forcing the reader to guess (e.g., "fairly," "somewhat," "relatively," "pretty much").

Words and phrases that describe measurable, verifiable facts without the writer's opinion or interpretation. This is the foundation of technical writing.

Subjective Language

Words and phrases that express the writer's personal opinion, feelings, or interpretation. Generally avoided in technical writing.

Eliminating Weasel Words (1/2)

Definition

Weasel words are vague, hedging terms that offer no specific information, forcing the reader to infer meaning.

Examples

Common weasel words include "fairly," "somewhat," "relatively," "pretty much," "approximately" (though "approximately" has specific acceptable uses).

The Weasel Word Test

To identify weasel words, ask: "Could two reasonable people disagree about what this means?" If the answer is yes, the sentence needs revision to be more specific.

Purpose

Weasel words often signal a writer's lack of specific knowledge or insufficient effort in research and articulation.

What are 'weasel words' in technical writing?

Vague, hedging words (e.g., 'fairly,' 'somewhat,' 'pretty much') that sound meaningful but convey no specific information, forcing the reader to guess.

Describe 'The Weasel Word Test' for identifying vague language.

Read your sentence and ask: 'Could two reasonable people disagree about what this means?' If yes, the sentence contains weasel words and needs revision.

Why is eliminating weasel words crucial in professional and technical writing?

Vague language wastes time, causes mistakes, and can cost real money by forcing the reader to guess or make assumptions.

What is the first convention for presenting numerical information clearly in technical writing?

Always include units. A number like '12' is meaningless; '12 inches,' '12 volts,' or '12 seconds' are specific.

When using units in technical writing, what is the best practice for abbreviations?

Use standard abbreviations. Spell out the unit at first use, then abbreviate consistently (e.g., in. for inches, kg for kilograms).

Key Concepts (2/2)

Why should technical writers provide dual units (e.g., '4.0 inches (101.6 mm)')?

To accommodate a mixed or international audience, especially in specifications, by showing both US customary and metric units.

What additional information is needed to make a performance number like 'The motor produces 150 horsepower' a complete specification?

The conditions under which the performance is achieved (e.g., 'The motor produces 150 horsepower at 6,500 RPM').

Why is consistent decimal precision important in numerical data (e.g., '12.0 inches' and '8.0 inches' instead of '8 inches')?

Consistent decimal places signal consistent precision in measurements.
