Writing Process Descriptions

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Qualities of an Effective Process Description

A strong process description is logically organized, employs clear transitions between steps, and divides complex processes into distinct, manageable stages.

Chronological Order

The most common and natural way to organize a process description, presenting events in the sequence they actually occur. This mirrors reality and is highly reader-friendly.

Tense Usage

Effective Transitions

Words and phrases that act as the "connective tissue" of a description, showing how steps relate in terms of time, cause and effect, simultaneity, or duration. They improve flow and coherence.

Partitioning Complex Processes

Dividing a process with many steps into smaller, related groups or "stages," each with its own heading. This makes long descriptions less overwhelming, easier to read, and simpler to reference.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Audience-Appropriate Detail

Writers should match the level of detail to their audience's needs, avoiding the inclusion of every tiny sub-step for a general audience. Too much detail can obscure the main process.

Important Terms (1/3)

Chronological

Organized in the order that events occur in time, from first to last.

Sequential

Following a fixed order where each step depends on the previous one.

Simultaneous

Happening at the same time; used to describe parts of a process that occur in parallel.

Transition

A word or phrase that connects one idea to the next, showing the relationship between steps or stages (e.g., "next," "as a result," "while").

Partition

To divide a complex process into smaller, more manageable sections or stages.

Important Terms (2/3)

Stage

A distinct phase or step within a larger process, often serving as a logical grouping of related actions.

Phase

A period within a process that has its own characteristics, similar to a stage.

Flowchart

Schematic

A simplified diagram that shows how parts of a system connect and interact. (Similar to flowchart, a visual aid).

Abstract

Existing as an idea rather than a physical thing; difficult to visualize or experience directly.

Important Terms (3/3)

Concrete

Specific, tangible, and observable; the opposite of abstract.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What are the three essential qualities of an effective process description?

It is organized logically, it uses clear transitions, and it partitions complex processes into manageable stages.

Define 'chronological order' in the context of process descriptions.

Organized in the order that events occur in time, from first to last; this is the most natural and reader-friendly organization because it mirrors reality.

What does it mean to 'partition' a complex process?

To divide a complex process into smaller, more manageable sections or 'stages' (groups of related steps) to make it easier to read and reference.

When should you use present tense in a process description? Provide an example.

Use present tense for processes that are ongoing, repeatable, or general. Example: 'Water evaporates from the ocean surface.'

When should you use past tense in a process description? Provide an example.

Use past tense for processes that happened once, are historical, or are completed. Example: 'The Titanic struck the iceberg at 11:40 p.m.'

Why is consistency in verb tense important within a single process description?

Mixing tenses confuses readers and can disrupt the clarity and flow of the description.
