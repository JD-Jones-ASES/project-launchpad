Important Terms (1/3)

Accessibility (in technical writing)

The quality of being easily understood or appreciated by a broad audience, especially when dealing with complex subjects, without requiring prior specialized knowledge.

Oversimplification

The act of making something seem simpler than it really is, often by omitting crucial details or nuances, which can lead to a loss of accuracy.

Familiar-to-Unfamiliar Approach

A pedagogical strategy where new or complex concepts are introduced by first connecting them to ideas or experiences the learner already understands.

In-context Definition

The practice of explaining a technical term or piece of jargon immediately at the point where it is introduced in the text, rather than relying on a separate glossary.

Public-key Cryptography

An encryption system that uses two different, mathematically linked keys: a public key for encryption and a private key for decryption.

Important Terms (2/3)

Public Key

In public-key cryptography, the key that is openly shared and can be used by anyone to encrypt messages or verify digital signatures.

Private Key

In public-key cryptography, the secret key known only to its owner, used to decrypt messages encrypted with the corresponding public key.

Plaintext

Unencrypted, readable data or information.

Ciphertext

Encrypted data; the unreadable, scrambled output of an encryption process.

Encryption

The process of converting information or data into a code to prevent unauthorized access, making it unreadable without the correct key.

Important Terms (3/3)

Decryption

The process of converting encrypted data back into its original, readable form (plaintext) using the appropriate key.

TLS (Transport Layer Security)

A cryptographic protocol designed to provide secure communication over a computer network, widely used for internet communications like web browsing (HTTPS).

The Core Principle: Accessible, Not Simple

The Challenge

Technical writers often deal with intrinsically difficult subjects (e.g., encryption, gene expression, climate feedback loops).

The Goal

Make these topics **accessible** without **dumbing them down**.

A. Start with What the Reader Already Knows (Familiar-to-Unfamiliar)

Foundation Building

The most effective explanations connect new concepts to something the reader already understands.

Anchoring New Information

Before diving into technical specifics, provide a relatable anchor.

Example (Encryption)

Explain encryption using the analogy of a combination lock, moving from the concrete idea of a physical lock to the abstract concept of digital scrambling.

"Before" vs. "After" Example (Public-Key Cryptography)

B. Define Terms in Context

Immediate Explanation

When a technical term is introduced, define it immediately, ideally within the same sentence.

Reader Flow

This prevents the reader from needing to stop, search for definitions, and potentially lose comprehension.

"Before" vs. "After" Example (DNS Server)

Control Your Level of Detail

Balancing Act

Determining how much detail to include is one of the hardest decisions in technical writing.

The Audience Test for Detail

Rule of Thumb

Explain enough for the reader to answer "How does this work?" in their own words, focusing on the key stages and the logic connecting them, rather than every minute sub-step.

Model Document: How Encryption Protects Your Password

Introduction

Sets the stage by explaining the problem (unprotected passwords online) and the solution (encryption).

Stage 1: Your Device Encrypts the Password

Explains TLS, ciphertext, and uses the analogy of a "locked box" for clarity.

Stage 2: The Encrypted Data Travels the Network

Describes the journey of encrypted data, emphasizing that intermediate devices and hackers cannot read it.

Stage 3: The Server Decrypts and Verifies

Explains the role of the private decryption key and the use of a "hash" for secure password verification, noting that actual passwords are not stored.

Why It Matters

Summarizes the importance of this process for secure online activities.

Writing Tip: The “Explain Like I'm a Smart Fifteen-Year-Old” Standard

Target Audience Mindset

Imagine explaining your topic to an intelligent, curious 15-year-old who has no background knowledge.

Expectations

This reader is smart enough to follow logical reasoning and learn new terms but will not tolerate unexplained jargon or assumptions.

Effectiveness

If your explanation works for this reader, it is likely accessible and clear enough for almost any general audience.
