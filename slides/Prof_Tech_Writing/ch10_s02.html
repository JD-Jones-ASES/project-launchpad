Important Terms (1/2)

Persuasive Professional Tone

The balanced, credible, and influential writing style required for proposals, characterized by confidence, professionalism, and honesty, avoiding both aggressive hype and timid uncertainty.

Overselling (Too aggressive)

A detrimental tone in proposals marked by making exaggerated claims, dismissing risks, using superlatives without evidence, or pressuring the reader, which ultimately erodes trust.

Underselling (Too passive)

A detrimental tone in proposals characterized by constant hedging, apologizing for the proposal, or failing to clearly articulate benefits, which signals a lack of conviction and confidence.

Assertion

A statement or claim made without accompanying proof or supporting evidence.

Evidence

Factual information, data, examples, or expert opinions used to support and validate a claim, making it credible and persuasive.

Analyzing the Model Proposal: Community Tutoring Services

Executive Summary

Concise, presents key outcomes (15–20% improvement) and funding request with specific figures ($4,800, $3,000, $1,800).

Problem Statement

Uses hard data (40% of freshmen, 87 of 218 students) to quantify the problem and establishes a compelling need.

Proposed Solution

Detailed specifics (three days/week, 3:30–5:00 PM, 15 National Honor Society students, 6-hour training) for clarity and feasibility.

Qualifications

Provides specific organizational experience (three years, 340 students, 18% average improvement) and individual expertise (David Park's master's degree and five years of management experience).

Conclusion

States the case clearly, emphasizes urgency ("Every semester of inaction..."), offers a specific start date, and includes clear calls to action with contact information.
