The Order of Refinement Matters

Always revise first to solidify your ideas and structure, then edit for sentence-level improvements, and finally proofread for minor errors. Working out of order leads to wasted effort.

Revision Transforms Vague to Specific

Effective revision involves moving from general statements to concrete details, specific problems, and evidence-based solutions, significantly enhancing a document's clarity and persuasiveness.

Important Terms (1/2)

Revision

The deepest level of writing improvement, involving rethinking and rewriting to enhance ideas, structure, clarity, and audience awareness. It addresses the document's overall purpose.

Editing

The middle level of writing improvement, focusing on refining individual sentences for clarity, tone, word choice, grammar, and flow.

Proofreading

The final pass before submission, focusing on catching surface-level errors such as spelling, punctuation, capitalization, and formatting.

Four-Pass Revision Method

A systematic approach to reviewing a document in four separate stages, each focused on a specific aspect of writing quality (purpose/audience, structure/flow, sentences/word choice, surface accuracy).

Peer Review

A process where another person reads and provides constructive feedback on your writing before you finalize it, offering an external perspective.

Important Terms (2/2)

Rubric

A scoring tool that describes different levels of quality for an assignment, from excellent to beginning, providing specific criteria for evaluation and consistent feedback.
