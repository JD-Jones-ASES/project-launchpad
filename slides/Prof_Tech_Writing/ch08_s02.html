Understanding and Managing Action Items

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Action Item

A specific, measurable task assigned to a single person with a definite deadline. It transforms vague intentions into concrete plans, driving progress in any group setting.

The What/Who/When Framework

The three indispensable components of every effective action item.

Accountability

The obligation to take responsibility for completing assigned tasks and reporting on progress. It is fostered by transparent tracking and review of action items.

Diffusion of Responsibility

A common pitfall where, if a task is assigned to a group rather than an individual, each group member assumes someone else will handle it, leading to inaction. Always assign a single owner.

Important Terms (1/2)

Action Item

A specific, actionable task assigned to an individual with a clear deadline, typically originating from a meeting.

What/Who/When Framework

A structured approach to defining action items, ensuring clarity on the task, the responsible person, and the completion date.

Accountability

The state of being responsible for completing a task and being able to explain one's actions and progress.

Diffusion of Responsibility

A psychological phenomenon where a person is less likely to take responsibility or act when others are present, often occurring when tasks are assigned to groups instead of individuals.

Deliverable

The tangible output or result that proves an action item has been completed (e.g., a report, an email, an updated webpage).

Important Terms (2/2)

Vague Intention

A general idea or wish for something to happen, lacking the specific details (What, Who, When) to become an actionable task.

Writing Clear Action Items

Start with the Owner's Name

Placing the person's name first (e.g., "Aisha will draft...") immediately identifies responsibility and makes the task harder to ignore.

Use Strong, Specific Verbs

Employ active verbs that clearly describe the required action.

Define the Output (Deliverable)

Clearly state what the completed task will produce. This defines what "done" looks like for everyone.

Include the Deadline

Always provide a specific date for completion. For urgent tasks, make the deadline reflect that urgency (e.g., "by end of day Wednesday").

One Task Per Item

Break down complex actions into individual, distinct tasks. A single action item should represent one discrete piece of work.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What are meeting minutes?

Meeting minutes are the official written record of what was discussed, decided, and assigned during a meeting, documenting decisions, tasks, and key points.

What is the primary purpose of meeting minutes?

Their primary purpose is to document the outcomes of a meeting (decisions made, tasks assigned, key information shared) and serve as proof of what was accomp...

From what Latin word does 'minutes' derive, and what does it mean?

The word 'minutes' comes from the Latin 'minutia,' meaning 'small details,' because good minutes capture small but important details.

What is the biggest mistake new notetakers make when preparing meeting minutes?

The biggest mistake is trying to write down everything, treating minutes as a transcript rather than a concise record of outcomes.

What are the three essential elements that every set of meeting minutes must capture?

Meeting minutes must capture: 1. Decisions made. 2. Action items assigned (what, to whom, and by when). 3. Key information shared.

When recording a decision that involved a vote, what should be captured in the minutes?

The decision made and the result of the vote should be captured, but not who argued for which side.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What specific details should be captured for each action item in meeting minutes?

For each action item, the minutes should record what the task is, who it is assigned to, and by when it is due.

Is it appropriate to include personal opinions, jokes, or off-topic conversations in meeting minutes?

No, minutes should remain factual and neutral, excluding personal opinions, jokes, and off-topic conversations.

In what tense and perspective should meeting minutes be written?

Meeting minutes should be written in past tense ('The committee voted') and third person ('Marcus reported') as a neutral observer.

Why is an 'Action items summary' included at the end of meeting minutes?

A consolidated action items summary at the end makes it easy for anyone to quickly scan the document and find their assigned tasks, owners, and deadlines.

What is a helpful strategy for preparing your document before a meeting to streamline note-taking?

Use the agenda as a template by copying the agenda items into your minutes document with space under each one to fill in details during the meeting.
