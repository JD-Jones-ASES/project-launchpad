Important Terms (1/2)

Fresh-Eyes Test

A method of testing instructions by having a novice user attempt to follow them without assistance, allowing the writer to observe difficulties and identify areas for revision.

Usability Testing

A general method for evaluating how easy and effective a product, system, or set of instructions is to use, typically by observing users interacting with it.

Imperative Mood

The grammatical mood used for commands and instructions (e.g., "Place," "Turn," "Remove"). (Mentioned in the introductory paragraph as a characteristic of good instructions).

Troubleshooting

The systematic process of identifying, diagnosing, and solving problems or errors, often included in instructions to help users resolve common issues.

Revision

The comprehensive process of reviewing and improving the content, clarity, organization, and completeness of a piece of writing, distinct from mere proofreading.

Important Terms (2/2)

Result Statements

Brief descriptions within or after a step that inform the user what the outcome of that step should look like or feel like, helping them confirm they have performed the action correctly.

Ambiguous Language

Vague, unclear, or open-to-interpretation phrasing that can confuse readers, especially those unfamiliar with the task.

Assumed Knowledge

Information, terms, or skills that the writer presumes the reader already possesses but may not, leading to gaps in understanding.

Common Problems Revealed by Testing

Missing Steps

This is the most frequent issue. Writers, due to their familiarity, inadvertently omit crucial steps that are essential for a novice.

Assumed Knowledge

Instructions may use technical jargon without defining it, or assume the reader possesses prior knowledge (e.g., how to "Open Terminal") that they do not.

Ambiguous Language

Vague phrasing like "Attach the piece to the side" leaves the reader guessing which piece and which side, leading to confusion and errors.

Wrong Order

Sometimes, a step is placed too early or too late in the sequence. Testing can reveal that gathering materials, for instance, should be a prerequisite rather than a mid-process step.

Missing Result Statements

Readers complete a step but have no way to verify if they did it correctly. They proceed, only to discover much later that an earlier step was performed incorrectly, wasting time and effort.

Revision: Improving Your Instructions (1/2)

• Are all necessary steps included? Are any steps missing?

• Does each step describe a single, clear action?

• Are the steps logically ordered?

• Are all technical terms or unfamiliar concepts clearly defined?

• Are warnings and cautions positioned immediately before the potentially dangerous or critical steps they apply to?

• Do important steps include result statements so the reader knows if they succeeded?

Revision: Improving Your Instructions (2/2)

• Could someone with absolutely no prior experience successfully follow these instructions?

Key Concepts (1/2)

Why are clear, well-organized numbered steps crucial in instructions?

They make even complex tasks manageable. Vague or poorly ordered steps can turn simple tasks into nightmares for the user.

What is the imperative mood in instructions?

The imperative mood is a direct command that tells the reader exactly what to do. It begins with a verb and speaks directly to the reader.

Why is the imperative mood the natural and preferred voice for instructions?

It is clear, concise, and action-oriented, making it impossible to misunderstand the required action.

Rewrite the following passive step in the imperative mood: 'The power button should be pressed and held for three seconds.'

Press and hold the power button for three seconds.

Rewrite the following wordy step concisely in the imperative mood: 'At this point, what you are going to want to do is take the Phillips-head screwdriver... and use it to go ahead and remove the four screws.'

Remove the four Phillips-head screws from the bottom of the unit using the screwdriver.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How many actions should each numbered step contain?

Each numbered step should contain exactly one action.

What is the risk of packing multiple actions into a single numbered step?

Readers can lose their place, complete one action, and then not know if they have finished the step or might skip subsequent actions within the same step.

What is a 'run-on step' and why should it be avoided?

What are substeps in the context of instructions?

Substeps are smaller actions nested within a main step, typically lettered or indented beneath the main step.
