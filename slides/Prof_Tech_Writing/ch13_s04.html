Chapter 13, Section 4

Recommendation Reports

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Purpose of Recommendation Reports

Unlike analytical reports that explain data, recommendation reports tell the reader what to do with that data by proposing specific actions.

Logical Chain Structure

Recommendation reports are built like a staircase, where each section (findings, analysis, conclusions, recommendations) logically supports the next, ensuring coherence and credibility.

Comprehensive Report Components

They include all elements of an analytical report (data, analysis, conclusions) plus a final section dedicated to proposing specific actions.

Credible Methodology

A strong methodology section is crucial for establishing the trustworthiness of the report's findings by detailing how information was collected and analyzed, including any limitations.

Qualities of Effective Recommendations

Recommendations must be specific, supported by evidence, practical (considering feasibility and resources), and often prioritized if multiple actions are proposed.

Important Terms (1/3)

Recommendation Report

A type of analytical report that not only presents data and analysis but also proposes specific actions based on the conclusions.

Executive Summary

A concise, one-page overview of the entire report, placed at the beginning, that includes the main recommendation. It is typically written last.

Introduction

The initial section that sets the stage for the report, outlining its purpose, scope, background, and methodology.

Methodology

The section that explains how the information was gathered and analyzed, including sources, collection methods, analysis techniques, and limitations.

Findings

The raw data, facts, observations, and objective information collected during the research phase, often organized by criteria or topic.

Important Terms (2/3)

Analysis / Conclusions

The section where the findings are interpreted, explaining what the data means, identifying patterns, causes, and significance.

Recommendations

The core of the report, presenting specific, actionable steps or solutions derived directly from the conclusions, always with justification.

Appendices

Supplementary materials placed at the end of the report, such as raw data, surveys, detailed tables, or supporting documents.

Logical Progression/Chain

The sequential flow of ideas and information through the report, where each section builds upon and is supported by the preceding ones.

Specific (Recommendation)

A recommendation that is precise, clear, and actionable, avoiding vague language.

Important Terms (3/3)

Supported (Recommendation)

A recommendation that is directly backed by evidence from the report's findings and conclusions.

Practical (Recommendation)

A recommendation that is realistic and feasible to implement, considering factors like cost, time, and resources.

Prioritized (Recommendation)

When multiple recommendations are given, they are ranked by importance or urgency.

Writing the Methodology Section (1/2)

What sources did you use?

(e.g., surveys, interviews, published research, direct observation, experiments, existing reports)

How did you collect the data?

(e.g., "An online survey sent to 150 employees," "Interviews with 8 department heads," "Analysis of three years of sales records")

How did you analyze the data?

(e.g., "Calculated averages," "Compared percentages," "Looked for patterns over time," "Sorted by category")

What are the limitations?

(Honest acknowledgment of factors that might affect the reliability or generalizability of the findings, e.g., low survey response rate, limited sample size).

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the primary purpose of 'Findings' in an analytical report?

Findings present the specific data points, observations, and measurements collected. They answer: “What did the data show?”

What is the primary purpose of 'Conclusions' in an analytical report?

What is the primary purpose of 'Recommendations' in an analytical report?

Recommendations propose specific actions the reader should take, based on the conclusions drawn from the findings. They answer: “What should we do?”

What is 'The Sorting Test' used for in report writing?

Define 'benchmark' as it applies to analytical reports.

A benchmark is a standard of comparison or a reference point that provides context for data, such as an industry average or historical performance.

Define 'metric' in the context of analytical reports.

A metric is a specific, measurable value used to track performance, such as customer satisfaction score, graduation rate, or cost per unit.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is identified as the single most common error in analytical and recommendation reports?

The single most common error is confusing findings, conclusions, and recommendations.

Outline the three-step logical chain that every analytical or recommendation report should follow.

The three-step logical chain is: Findings (facts) → Conclusions (interpretation) → Recommendations (action).

Why is it crucial to clearly distinguish between findings, conclusions, and recommendations, even though they are related?

According to the text, what is the consequence of having 'Findings without conclusions' in a report?

Findings without conclusions are merely a 'data dump' and lack interpretation or meaning.

What is the consequence of presenting 'Conclusions without findings' in a report?

Conclusions without findings are just opinions, as they lack factual support.

What happens if a report contains 'Recommendations without conclusions'?

Recommendations without conclusions are just guesses, lacking a logical basis derived from data interpretation.
