Recommendation Reports Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Recommendation vs. Analytical Reports

While analytical reports explain what data means, recommendation reports go further by telling readers *what to do* about it, directly influencing decisions.

Logical Progression

A recommendation report follows a clear, sequential chain: data collection (findings) leads to interpretation (analysis/conclusions), which then logically dictates specific actions (recommendations...

Comprehensive Structure

These reports adhere to a standardized structure designed to present information clearly and persuasively, moving from an overview to detailed evidence, analysis, and finally, actionable steps.

Trustworthy Methodology

A well-explained methodology section is vital for readers to assess the credibility and reliability of the report's findings and, consequently, its recommendations.

Effective Recommendations

Recommendations must be specific, directly supported by the report's findings and conclusions, practical (considering feasibility and resources), and often prioritized to guide implementation.

Important Terms (1/2)

Recommendation Report

A type of analytical report that, in addition to presenting data, analysis, and conclusions, proposes specific actions or solutions.

Analytical Report

A report that interprets data to explain what it means, often forming the basis for a recommendation report.

Executive Summary

A concise, one-page overview of the entire report, placed at the beginning, that includes the report's main recommendation(s). It is typically written last.

Introduction

The opening section of a report that outlines its purpose, scope, background, and the methodology used.

Methodology

The section detailing *how* information was gathered and analyzed, including sources, collection methods, analysis techniques, and limitations.

Important Terms (2/2)

Findings

The raw data, facts, observations, and results collected during the research phase, presented objectively.

Analysis / Conclusions

The section where the findings are interpreted to reveal patterns, causes, significance, and what the data truly means.

Recommendations

Specific, actionable steps proposed based on the conclusions, aimed at addressing the report's central problem or achieving its objectives.

Appendices

Supplementary materials (e.g., raw data, surveys, detailed tables, interview transcripts) that support the main report but are too lengthy or detailed for the body.

Writing the Methodology Section (1/2)

What sources were used?

(e.g., surveys, interviews, published data, direct observation, experiments, existing reports)

How was the data collected?

(e.g., "Online survey sent to 150 employees," "Interviews with 8 department heads," "Analysis of three years of sales records")

How was the data analyzed?

(e.g., calculated averages, compared percentages, looked for patterns over time, sorted by category)

What are the limitations?

Honesty about any limitations (e.g., low survey response rate, limited sample size) helps maintain credibility.

Writing Recommendations (1/2)

Specific

Avoid vague statements. Instead of "Reduce paper use," write "Switch to double-sided default printing on all shared printers by January 15."

Supported

Every recommendation *must* directly link back to your findings and conclusions. If your data doesn't support it, remove it.

Practical

Consider feasibility, cost, and timeline. A recommendation like "rebuild the entire school" is impractical; "add two vegetarian options to the lunch menu within the current food budget" is practical.

Prioritized

If you have several recommendations, rank them. Which actions are most urgent or impactful?
