Important Terms (1/2)

Data

Raw facts, numbers, measurements, or observations collected for analysis. It is the raw material from which evidence is derived.

Evidence

Information that supports or proves a claim or finding. It can include numbers, dates, measurements, observations, or references to documented sources.

Table

A grid of rows and columns used to organize and present data, typically for precise values and comparisons.

Figure

Any visual element in a report that is not a table, such as charts, graphs, diagrams, or photographs. Used to illustrate trends, relationships, or conceptual information.

Attribution

The act of giving credit to the source of information, data, or ideas presented in a report. Usually done with brief in-text references.

Important Terms (2/2)

Citation

A formal reference to a source, following a specific formatting style (e.g., APA, MLA). Citations allow readers to locate and verify the original source and protect against plagiarism.

Tables and Figures for Data Presentation

Tables

are ideal when readers need to look up specific values, compare numbers across different categories, or require precise data.

Figures

(such as charts, graphs, diagrams, or photographs) are best used to show trends, proportions, or relationships, helping to tell a visual story with the data.

Model Document: Incident Report - Chemistry Lab Spill

Factual, Neutral Tone

The report objectively describes events without assigning blame (e.g., it doesn't call the student "careless").

Specific Details

It includes precise measurements, dates, and times (e.g., "10:22 AM on October 17, 2025," "500 mL beaker," "400 mL solution," "3-foot area," "12-minute interruption").

Documentation of Compliance

Mentioning that "Student A was wearing required safety goggles and a lab apron" confirms that proper precautions were in place.

Focus on Contributing Factors

The report identifies systemic issues (narrow bench, beaker placement) rather than solely blaming an individual, which helps in preventing future incidents.

Clear Recommendations

Based on the identified factors, actionable recommendations are provided.

When is a table the most appropriate visual element for presenting data?

Use tables when readers need to look up specific values or compare numbers across categories. They are best for precise data.

When is a figure (chart, graph, diagram) the most appropriate visual element for presenting data?

Use figures when you want to show trends, proportions, or relationships. They are best for telling a visual story with data.

List two rules for numbering tables and figures in reports.

1. Number every table and figure (e.g., Table 1, Figure 1). 2. Number tables and figures in separate sequences.
