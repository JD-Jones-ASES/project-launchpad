Clear and Effective: Professional & Technical Writing

Chapter 9, Section 3

Types of Reports: A Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Universal Purpose of Reports

All reports aim to present organized information to enable decisions, track progress, or provide understanding of an event or situation.

Progress Report's Core Questions

Progress reports answer "What have we done?", "What are we doing?", and "What still needs to happen?" to keep stakeholders informed of project advancement.

Three Sections of a Progress Report

These reports are structured around completed work, current work, and upcoming work, detailing achievements, challenges, and future steps.

Purpose of Incident Reports

These reports create an accurate, factual record of unexpected events to prevent recurrence, meet legal/regulatory needs, support claims, and identify patterns.

The Five W's (plus one) of an Incident Report

Effective incident reports answer Who, What, When, Where, Why, and crucially, What was done in response to the incident.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Factual Basis of Incident Reports

Incident reports must be strictly factual, avoiding opinions or blame, and allowing the facts to speak for themselves.

Status Report as a Snapshot

Unlike progress reports that cover a timeline, status reports provide a current, "snapshot" view of a project or activity's immediate state.

Honesty in Reporting

Regardless of report type, it's vital to be transparent about problems, delays, or issues, providing clear explanations and proposed solutions.

Important Terms (1/4)

Executive summary

A brief overview of a report's key points, written for busy readers who may not read the full document.

Findings

The factual results discovered through research, investigation, or observation.

Scope

The boundaries of what a report covers—and what it does not cover.

Methodology

The process or methods used to gather information for a report.

Appendix

A section at the end of a report containing supplementary material (raw data, forms, detailed charts) that supports the main text.

Important Terms (2/4)

Data

Facts, numbers, measurements, or observations collected for analysis.

Evidence

Information that supports or proves a claim or finding.

Attribution

Giving credit to the source of information, data, or ideas.

Citation

A formal reference to a source, following a specific format (APA, MLA, etc.).

Table

A grid of rows and columns used to organize data.

Important Terms (3/4)

Figure

Any visual element in a report—charts, graphs, diagrams, photographs—that is not a table.

Recommendation

A suggested course of action based on the report's findings and conclusions.

Conclusion

An interpretation of what the findings mean, drawn from the evidence presented.

Abstract

A short summary of a report's purpose, methods, findings, and conclusions—similar to an executive summary but more common in academic and scientific writing.

Status report

A regular update on the current state of a project or activity.

Important Terms (4/4)

Progress report

A document that describes what has been accomplished on a project, what is currently underway, and what remains to be done.

Incident report

A factual account of an unexpected event (accident, equipment failure, security breach) documenting what happened, when, and what actions were taken.

The Three Sections of a Progress Report

Completed work

This section details accomplishments since the last report. It should be specific, including dates, quantities, and milestones reached.

Current work

Here, the report describes tasks actively underway, noting any challenges, delays, or unexpected issues encountered.

Upcoming work

This outlines the next steps and future activities, often including deadlines and assigning responsibilities.

The Five W's (plus one) of an Incident Report (1/2)

Who was involved?

(Include names, roles, and any witnesses.)

What happened?

(Provide a detailed, factual description of the event.)

When did it happen?

(Specify the exact date, time, and duration of the incident.)

Where did it happen?

(Indicate the precise location.)

Why did it happen?

(Describe known contributing factors, avoiding speculation.)

The Five W's (plus one) of an Incident Report (2/2)

What was done?

(Detail the immediate actions taken in response to the incident.)

Other Informational Reports

Trip reports

Summarize the objectives, activities, and outcomes of a business trip, conference, or site visit.

Lab reports

Document the purpose, methods, results, and conclusions of a scientific experiment.

Meeting summaries

Record key decisions made, discussions held, and action items assigned during a meeting (especially for formal meetings).

Inspection reports

Detail the condition of equipment, facilities, or products following a formal inspection.

Compliance reports

Document an organization's adherence to required standards, regulations, or policies.
