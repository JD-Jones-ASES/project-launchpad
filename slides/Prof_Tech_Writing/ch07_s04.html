Important Terms (1/3)

Chronological

Organized in the order that events occur in time, from first to last.

Sequential

Following a fixed order where each step depends on the previous one.

Simultaneous

Happening at the same time; used to describe parts of a process that occur in parallel.

Transition

A word or phrase that connects one idea to the next, showing the relationship between steps or stages.

Partition

To divide a complex process into smaller, more manageable sections or stages.

Important Terms (2/3)

Stage

A distinct phase or step within a larger process, often grouped with other related steps.

Phase

A period within a process that has its own characteristics, similar to a stage.

Flowchart

A diagram that visually shows the steps of a process using shapes and arrows to indicate sequence and decisions.

Schematic

A simplified diagram that shows how parts of a system connect and interact, focusing on relationships rather than exact physical layout.

Abstract

Existing as an idea rather than a physical thing; difficult to visualize or experience directly (e.g., a concept).

Important Terms (3/3)

Concrete

Specific, tangible, and observable; the opposite of abstract (e.g., a physical object or action).

Tense in Process Descriptions

Present Tense

Use for processes that are ongoing, repeatable, or describe general truths and scientific principles.

Past Tense

Use for processes that occurred once, are historical events, or have been completed.

Consistency

Avoid mixing tenses within a single description, as this can confuse your readers and undermine clarity.

Types of Transition Words and Phrases:

Time Sequence

Indicate the order of events.

Cause and Effect

Show that one action leads to another.

Simultaneous Actions

Describe events happening concurrently.

Duration

Specify how long an action or stage lasts.

Benefits of Partitioning:

Readability

Breaks up long texts into manageable sections.

Comprehension

Helps readers grasp the process incrementally.

Referencability

Allows readers to quickly find specific parts of the process they are interested in.

Clarity

Provides a high-level overview before delving into details.
