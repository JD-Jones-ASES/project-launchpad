Important Terms (1/2)

Primary Source

Original, firsthand evidence such as data you collected, interviews you conducted, experiments you ran, or official records.

Secondary Source

An analysis, interpretation, or summary of primary sources, like textbook chapters, review articles, or news reports.

Paraphrase

To restate someone else's ideas in your own words while preserving the original meaning and giving credit to the source.

Quote Directly

To use the exact words from a source, enclosed in quotation marks, and providing a citation.

Citation

A reference to a source that tells the reader where a piece of information, data, or idea came from, typically including author, title, publication, and date.

Important Terms (2/2)

Reference (entry)

An entry in a reference list or bibliography that provides the full publication information for a source cited in the text, allowing readers to locate the original.

Plagiarism

Presenting someone else's words, ideas, data, or creative work as your own, whether intentionally or accidentally, by failing to give proper credit to the source.

Self-plagiarism

Reusing your own previously published work without disclosure or proper attribution.

Common Knowledge

Facts so widely known that they do not need attribution (e.g., "The sun rises in the east"). If unsure, it's best to cite.
