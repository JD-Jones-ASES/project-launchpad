Key Concepts

Purpose of Data Visualization

Choosing the Right Chart

Selecting the appropriate visualization type (bar, line, pie, or table) is critical for effective communication, as each serves a distinct purpose based on the kind of question the data answers.

Designing Honest Charts

Visualizations must be created ethically to ensure they represent data accurately and do not mislead readers through skewed scales, missing labels, or distorting effects.

Referencing Visuals in Text

Charts and tables should always be integrated into the report's narrative by introducing them, directing the reader's attention to key findings, and interpreting their meaning.

Correlation vs. Causation

It is essential to differentiate between two things that change together (correlation) and one thing directly causing another (causation) to avoid making unsubstantiated claims in reports.

Important Terms (1/2)

Data Visualization

The practice of translating numerical data into visual representations (like charts, graphs, tables, and diagrams) to make complex information more accessible and understandable.

Bar Chart

A chart used to compare quantities across distinct categories, answering questions like "Which is biggest?" or "How do these categories compare?".

Line Chart

A chart primarily used to show trends or changes in data over a continuous period, answering "Is this going up, down, or staying flat?".

Pie Chart

A circular chart divided into slices to illustrate numerical proportion, best used for showing parts of a whole when there are 2-6 categories that sum to 100%.

Table

A structured arrangement of rows and columns used to present precise, exact values, particularly useful when specific numbers are more important than visual patterns.

Important Terms (2/2)

Y-axis

The vertical axis on a chart, typically representing the dependent variable or the quantity being measured.

Data Source

The origin or provider of the data presented in a visualization, which should always be cited to ensure credibility and verifiability.

Correlation

A statistical relationship between two or more variables that indicates how they tend to move together; it does not imply that one causes the other.

Causation

A relationship where one event or variable (the cause) directly leads to the occurrence of another event or variable (the effect).

Designing Honest Charts (1/2)

Start the Y-axis at zero (for bar charts)

Truncating the Y-axis can exaggerate small differences, making them appear enormous and distorting the visual comparison.

Label everything

Every chart needs a clear title, properly labeled axes with units, and a data source. Without these, the chart is ambiguous and unreliable.

Use consistent scales

When presenting multiple charts for comparison, ensure all charts use the same scale to enable valid comparisons and prevent false impressions.

Do not use 3D effects

Three-dimensional charts, especially pie and bar charts, can distort the visual perception of data, making some sections appear larger than they are. Stick to flat, two-dimensional designs.

Include the data source

Always cite where the data originated, typically in a caption or footnote. This provides credibility and allows readers to verify the information.

Designing Honest Charts (2/2)

Correlation

Means two things change together (e.g., ice cream sales and drowning deaths both rise in summer).

Causation

Means one thing directly makes the other happen (e.g., increased sunlight exposure causes increased plant growth).
