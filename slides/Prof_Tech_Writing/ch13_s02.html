Important Terms (1/2)

Data Visualization

The process of translating numerical data into graphical representations (charts, graphs, tables, diagrams) to reveal patterns, trends, and insights that the human eye can grasp quickly.

Bar Chart

A chart that uses rectangular bars of varying heights (or lengths) to compare quantities across distinct categories. Best for answering "Which is biggest?" or "How do these categories compare?"

Line Chart

A chart that uses points connected by lines to show trends or changes in data over a continuous period, typically time. Best for answering "Is this going up, down, or staying flat?"

Pie Chart

A circular chart divided into slices, where each slice represents a proportion or percentage of a whole. Best for showing parts of a whole when there are 2-6 categories that sum to 100%.

Table

A display of data in rows and columns, used to present precise values when exact numbers are more important than visual patterns or trends.

Correlation

A statistical relationship where two or more variables tend to change together. It indicates a connection but does not necessarily imply that one causes the other.

Causation

A relationship where one event or variable directly leads to another event or variable. Establishing causation requires stronger evidence and specific methodologies than simply observing correlation.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the primary role of the Findings section in a recommendation report?

The Findings section presents the raw data, facts, and observations collected, typically organized by criteria or topic.

What is the purpose of the Analysis / Conclusions section?

This section interprets the findings, explains what the data means, and identifies patterns, causes, and significance.

What is the purpose of the Methodology section?

The Methodology section explains how information was gathered and analyzed, allowing readers to judge the trustworthiness of the findings.

List at least three questions a strong Methodology section should answer.

What sources were used? How was the data collected? How was the data analyzed? What are the limitations of the data?

A recommendation must be 'Specific.' What does this mean?

A specific recommendation is precise and actionable, avoiding vagueness. It clearly states what action should be taken, by whom, and by when if applicable.
