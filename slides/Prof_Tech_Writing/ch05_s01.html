Avoiding Assumptions

A critical principle is to never assume the reader has prior knowledge of the object. Every detail necessary for understanding or replication must be explicitly stated.

Important Terms (1/4)

Specification

A detailed description of the requirements, dimensions, materials, or performance standards that a product or system must meet.

Requirement

A specific condition or capability that a product, system, or document must satisfy.

Tolerance

The acceptable range of variation in a measurement or dimension (e.g., $\pm 0.5$ mm).

Nominal

The intended or named value of a measurement, as opposed to the actual measured value.

Dimension

A measurable physical quantity such as length, width, height, or diameter.

Important Terms (2/4)

Schematic

A simplified diagram that shows the arrangement and connections of components in a system.

Cross-section

A view of an object as if it has been cut through to reveal its internal structure.

Bill of materials

A complete list of parts, components, and raw materials needed to build a product.

Shall/Should/May

Formal terms used in specifications: "shall" denotes a mandatory requirement, "should" indicates a recommended action, and "may" signifies an optional action.

Compliance

The act of meeting all the requirements stated in a specification or standard.

Important Terms (3/4)

Metric

A standard unit of measurement, or a measurable indicator used to evaluate performance.

Parameter

A measurable characteristic or variable that defines the boundaries of a system or process.

Threshold

The minimum or maximum acceptable value for a measurement or performance indicator.

Criteria

The standards or benchmarks used to evaluate whether something meets requirements (singular: criterion).

Technical Description

A written explanation of an object, mechanism, or system that conveys its physical characteristics, components, and function with enough detail for the reader to understand or visualize it.

Important Terms (4/4)

General-to-Specific Organization

A structural pattern in which the writer begins with an overview of the complete object or system, then describes each component or subsystem in detail.

Mechanism

A system of parts working together to perform a function, such as a lock, a gear train, or a hydraulic press.

Name the three core characteristics (pillars) of an effective technical description.

Objective, Precise, and Organized.

What does it mean for a technical description to be 'objective'?

It describes what is, not what the writer feels about it, avoiding opinions or emotional language.

What does it mean for a technical description to be 'precise'?

It uses exact measurements, specific materials, and concrete details rather than vague or relative terms.

What does it mean for a technical description to be 'organized'?

It follows a logical structure, typically moving from the whole object to its individual parts, or from general characteristics to specific details.

Define 'Specification' in a technical context.

A detailed description of the requirements, dimensions, materials, or performance standards that a product or system must meet.

Define 'Tolerance' as it relates to technical descriptions.

The acceptable range of variation in a measurement or dimension (e.g., ± 0.5 mm).

Explain the difference between 'Nominal' and 'Actual' values in technical descriptions.

'Nominal' is the intended or named value of a measurement, whereas 'Actual' is the real, measured value.

What is a 'Schematic'?

A simplified diagram that shows the arrangement and connections of components in a system.

In specifications, what do the terms 'shall,' 'should,' and 'may' formally indicate?

'Shall' indicates a mandatory requirement; 'should' indicates a recommended condition; 'may' indicates an optional action or condition.
