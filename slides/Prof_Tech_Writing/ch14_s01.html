Key Concepts (2/2)

Key Message as Thesis Statement

The central "one thing" you want your audience to remember from your presentation is analogous to a strong thesis statement in an essay, requiring clarity, specificity, and supporting evidence.

Important Terms (1/2)

Q&A (Question-and-Answer Period)

The designated time following a presentation during which the audience asks questions and the speaker provides responses.

Bridge (Rhetorical)

A technique used in the three-step answer to connect a specific answer back to the presentation's overall key message or main point, reinforcing the speaker's agenda.

The Three-Step Answer

A structured method for responding to audience questions: acknowledge, answer directly, and connect back to the key message.

Audience Engagement

Techniques and strategies employed by a speaker to capture and maintain the audience's attention and participation throughout a presentation.

Handout

A printed or digital document given to the audience, typically after a presentation, that summarizes its key points, provides supporting information, and includes contact details or next steps.

Important Terms (2/2)

Key Message

The single most important takeaway or central idea that the speaker wants the audience to remember from the presentation, similar to an essay's thesis statement.

The Three-Step Answer

Question

"What happens if not enough students volunteer to be tutors?"

Step 1 (Acknowledge)

"That's a fair concern – we need enough tutors to make the program work."

Step 2 (Answer)

"Based on our informal survey, 40 students said they would be interested in tutoring. We only need 15 for the pilot program. Even if half of those students don't follow through, we still have enough."

Step 3 (Bridge)

"And that's actually another benefit of the program – it gives student tutors leadership experience and community service hours, which means there's a real incentive to participate."

When someone disagrees with you

Defensive

Professional

Audience Engagement Beyond Q&A

Ask a question early

E.g., "How many of you have ever struggled...?" (Makes the problem relatable).

Use a brief poll

E.g., "Raise your hand if you've ever been tutored... Keep it raised if it helped." (Creates instant, visible evidence).

Tell a short story

A 30-second anecdote is often more memorable than statistics.

Invite a prediction

E.g., "Before I show you the results, how much do you think the school would save?" (Builds curiosity).

Handouts and Follow-Up Materials

Timing

Distribute handouts *after* your talk, not before, to prevent audience members from reading ahead and disengaging.
