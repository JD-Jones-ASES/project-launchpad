Key Concepts (2/2)

Demonstration of Growth

The portfolio should ideally tell a story of improvement over time. Including both earlier and later works can effectively illustrate your measurable progression in writing skill.

Clear Structure and Organization

A well-organized portfolio with a cover page, table of contents, and brief introductions makes it easy for the reader to navigate, understand the context of each piece, and appreciate your work.

Important Terms (1/2)

Portfolio

A curated collection of your best professional work, organized to demonstrate your skills and growth as a writer.

Evidence-Based Self-Presentation

The act of demonstrating your skills and abilities through tangible examples (like writing samples) rather than simply stating you possess them.

Range of Document Types

Refers to the variety of different writing formats and purposes (e.g., email, report, instructions, proposal) included in a portfolio to show versatility.

Quality of Writing

The degree to which a document adheres to established principles of effective communication, such as clarity, conciseness, precision, and appropriate tone.

Demonstration of Growth

Measurable improvement in a skill or ability over a period of time, often shown by comparing earlier and later work.

Important Terms (2/2)

Cover Page

The initial page of a portfolio that provides essential identifying information, such as the author's name, the portfolio title, and the date.

Table of Contents

A list that outlines all the documents included in the portfolio, along with their respective titles and page numbers, aiding navigation.

Brief Introductions

Short, concise explanations (1-3 sentences) preceding each document in a portfolio, providing context about the assignment, the document's purpose, and why it was chosen for inclusion.

Practical Tips for Portfolio Building

Visualizing Your Work

Spread out all your potential pieces (either printed or digitally) to see everything at once. This helps you better evaluate the range and quality of your work objectively.

Peer Feedback

Ask a classmate to review your potential selections. Others can often identify your strongest work more objectively than you can.

Revision as Growth

If you have some weaker pieces but want to show growth, dedicate time to substantially revise one or two. The revision process itself can be highlighted as evidence of your improved skills.

Quality Over Quantity

For students with many strong pieces, resist the urge to include everything. Be selective and choose only the absolute best, ensuring a high-quality, impactful collection.
