Agendas: Your Meeting Roadmap

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Purpose of an Agenda

An agenda is crucial for focusing discussions, enabling participant preparation, and holding individuals accountable for their contributions and the meeting's progress.

Four Core Agenda Questions

An effective agenda clarifies what will be discussed, who needs to attend, how long each topic will take, and what outcome is expected from each item.

Essential Agenda Elements

A professional agenda includes a clear meeting title, date and time, location, expected attendees, specific agenda items (topic, time, lead, outcome), and any necessary preparation notes.

Specificity in Agenda Items

Vague agenda items lead to unfocused meetings; specific items clearly state what needs to be accomplished, by whom, and with what expected result.

Structured Agenda Flow

Organizing agenda items logically (e.g., welcome, minutes approval, old business, new business, announcements, next meeting, adjournment) helps maintain order and efficiency.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Timely Distribution

Agendas must be sent to all attendees well in advance (24-48 hours) to allow for adequate preparation and to confirm attendance.

Important Terms (1/3)

Agenda

A written outline of topics to be discussed at a meeting, including time allocations and expected outcomes.

Attendee

A person who participates in a meeting.

Facilitator

The person who leads a meeting, keeps discussion on track, and ensures the agenda is followed.

Notetaker

The person assigned to record meeting decisions, action items, and key discussion points.

Quorum

The minimum number of members who must be present for a meeting's decisions to be official.

Important Terms (2/3)

Table (a discussion)

To postpone a topic for discussion at a later meeting.

Consensus

General agreement among all or most members of a group.

Stakeholder

A person who has an interest in or is affected by a decision or project.

Deliverable

A specific product, result, or output that must be completed by a certain date.

Deadline

The date or time by which something must be completed.

Important Terms (3/3)

Action item

A specific task assigned to a specific person with a specific deadline.

Accountability

The obligation to take responsibility for completing assigned tasks and reporting on progress.

Follow-up

Communication after a meeting to confirm decisions, remind people of tasks, or check on progress.

Minutes

The official written record of what was discussed, decided, and assigned during a meeting.

Parts of a Professional Meeting Agenda (1/2)

Meeting Title

A clear, descriptive name for the meeting (e.g., "Weekly Team Check-In," "Homecoming Committee Planning Meeting").

Date and Time

The specific date, start time, and crucially, an end time, which signals respect for participants' schedules.

Location

Where the meeting will physically take place, a video conference link, or both for hybrid meetings.

Attendees

A list of who is expected to attend, sometimes differentiating between required and optional participants.

Parts of a Professional Meeting Agenda (2/2)

Preparation Notes

Any documents, readings, or thoughts participants should complete or bring before the meeting.

Distributing the Agenda

Timing

Send the agenda to all attendees at least 24 hours before the meeting; 48 hours is even better.

Inclusions

Attach any necessary pre-reading documents or materials.

Confirmation

Ask attendees to confirm their attendance.

Templates

For recurring meetings, using a standardized template for the agenda can save time and help attendees know what to expect structurally.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the primary problem a follow-up email aims to solve?

People forgetting meeting decisions, action items, and agreements after a meeting.

Define a 'follow-up email' as described in the text.

A short message sent to all meeting attendees (and sometimes absent members) within 24 hours to confirm decisions and remind everyone of their tasks.

How does a follow-up email differ from full meeting minutes?

A follow-up email is a 'highlight reel' focusing on key decisions and action items, while full meeting minutes are a complete, detailed record.

What is the first main goal of a follow-up email?

To confirm shared understanding, ensuring everyone leaves the meeting with the same understanding of what was decided.

How does a follow-up email contribute to accountability?

It documents accountability by listing each person's action items in writing with their name attached, making commitments visible and increasing follow-through.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What key information should always be included in the subject line of a follow-up email?

It should be clear, specific, include the meeting name/topic, and the date (e.g., 'Meeting Summary: Environmental Club, Oct. 17').

What should the opening line of a follow-up email convey?

A single sentence stating the purpose of the email, such as 'Here is a summary of today's meeting and the action items we discussed.'

How should 'Key Decisions' be presented in a follow-up email?

As a brief, bulleted list, with each bullet point containing one or two concise sentences summarizing a decision.

Which part of the follow-up email is considered 'the most important'?

The Action Items section.

What essential details must be included for each action item in a follow-up email?

Each action item must clearly state the owner's name and the deadline, formatted to be easily noticeable (e.g., bolding names or using a table).

What logistical information should be included towards the end of a follow-up email?

The date, time, and location of the next meeting.
