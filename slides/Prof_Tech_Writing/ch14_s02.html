Mastering Slide Design Principles

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Slides as Visual Aids

Slides are meant to reinforce what the speaker is saying, not to be read as a document or script. They serve as a backdrop to your presentation.

Less Is More / Restraint

The fundamental principle of slide design. Audiences cannot simultaneously read dense text and listen effectively. Keep slides concise to prevent the audience from tuning out the speaker.

The Slide Purpose Rule

Each slide should have a single, clear purpose: to reinforce the specific point being made at that moment. If a slide attempts to convey multiple ideas, it should be split into separate slides.

The 6x6 Guideline

Visual Hierarchy

The intentional arrangement of elements on a slide to guide the viewer's eye to the most important information first. This ensures clarity and immediate understanding of the main message.

Key Concepts (2/2)

One Idea Per Slide

A crucial principle encouraging presenters to dedicate each slide to a single core idea, even if it means having more slides overall. This prevents clutter and makes information easier to process.

Visual Evidence Over Text

The most effective slides replace lengthy text with powerful visuals such as high-quality images, charts, diagrams, or striking numbers to communicate information more efficiently and memorably.

Important Terms

Slide deck

The complete collection of slides used as visual support during a presentation.

Bullet point

A short phrase or sentence in a list, marked by a dot or symbol, used to organize and present information concisely on slides.

Visual hierarchy

The arrangement of elements on a slide (e.g., text, images) so that the most important information stands out first and guides the viewer's attention.

Template

A pre-designed slide layout that provides consistent formatting, including fonts, colors, and overall design, across an entire presentation.

Less Is More: The Power of Restraint

Audience Attention

Your audience cannot read a dense slide and simultaneously listen intently to you. When presented with too much text, they will instinctively read, making you background noise.

The Slide Purpose Rule

Every slide must serve one specific purpose: to support the point you are making right now. If a slide tries to do more, split it. If it doesn't support the current point, remove it.

Visual Organization: Guiding the Eye

Visual Hierarchy

Arrange elements so the most important information is noticed first. The viewer's eye should follow a predictable path (e.g., title, then key point, then details).

Creating Visual Hierarchy

One Idea Per Slide

• If you find yourself trying to cram a lot of content onto one slide, it's a sign that the slide is trying to do too much. Split it into multiple slides.

• There's no penalty for having more slides. A presentation with many clear, focused slides is always better than one with fewer, cramped, and confusing slides.
