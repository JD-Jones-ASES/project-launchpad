Speaker Notes: Your Guide to Confident Presentations

Benefits of Speaker Notes

They encourage natural speech, maintain eye contact, allow for audience interaction, facilitate flexible pacing, and enable quick recovery if a presenter loses their train of thought.

Essential Components of Speaker Notes

For each slide, notes should include the main point, key data/details, a transition sentence to the next slide, a timing note, and optional reminders (e.g., "show demo," "ask question").

Prompt-Style Notes

These are concise, bulleted reminders of facts and talking points, designed to prompt memory rather than be read verbatim. They are superior to script-style notes for dynamic presentations.

Effective Transitions

These are "glue" sentences that logically connect one slide's content to the next, ensuring a smooth flow and helping the audience follow the presentation's narrative.

Important Terms

Speaker Notes

Written prompts visible only to the presenter, used to guide delivery without reading slides. They consist of key phrases, transitions, data points, and reminders.

Script

A word-for-word manuscript that a presenter reads aloud during a presentation, often leading to a monotonous tone and lack of audience engagement.

Transition Sentence

A phrase or sentence used in speaker notes to smoothly connect the content of the current slide to the content of the upcoming slide, ensuring logical flow.

Prompt-Style Notes

A method of writing speaker notes using concise bullet points, key phrases, and data points, designed to trigger the presenter's memory rather than be read verbatim.

Script-Style Notes

A method of writing speaker notes as full paragraphs or sentences, intended to be read aloud, which often results in a less engaging and natural presentation.

The Difference: Notes, Not a Script (1/2)

Writing Effective Transitions

Cause-Effect

Explains how one point leads to another. Example: "Because the data shows peer tutoring works, the next question is: what would it cost us?"

Contrast

Highlights differences or comparisons. Example: "Professional tutors cost $12,000 a year. Let me show you how we can get similar results for under $800."

Sequence

Guides the audience through a step-by-step process or timeline. Example: "Now that we've seen the evidence, let's walk through how the program would actually work."

Question

Poses a question that the next slide will answer. Example: "So the program works and it's affordable. The next question is: how do we get started?"

What is the primary function of "speaker notes" in a presentation?

Speaker notes are written prompts visible only to the presenter, used to guide delivery without reading slides.

How do "speaker notes" fundamentally differ from a "script"?

A script is a word-for-word manuscript to be read aloud, while speaker notes are brief prompts (key phrases, data points, reminders) to help speak naturally.

How do speaker notes help a presenter sound natural and conversational?

Because notes are brief prompts, the presenter naturally fills in details in their own words, making delivery sound unrehearsed and conversational.

What advantage do speaker notes offer if a presenter loses their place during a talk?

A quick glance at brief bullet points (notes) allows a presenter to get back on track much faster than searching through a long paragraph (script).

A concise phrase summarizing what the slide is about.
