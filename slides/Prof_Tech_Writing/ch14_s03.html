Slide Design Principles

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Slides as Visual Aids

Slides are meant to be seen and support the speaker's message, not to be read as a full document or script. They are a backdrop, not a replacement for the presenter.

Less Is More / Restraint

The most critical principle in slide design is to use restraint. Overly dense slides force the audience to choose between reading and listening, often resulting in them disengaging from the speaker.

Key Concepts (2/2)

One Idea Per Slide

A crucial principle for clarity. Each slide should focus on a single, clear idea. It's better to have more slides with focused content than fewer, cluttered slides.

Templates

Pre-designed slide layouts that provide consistent formatting (fonts, colors, layouts) across an entire presentation, ensuring a unified and professional look.

Visuals over Text

Highly effective slides replace lengthy text with powerful images, charts, diagrams, or striking numbers. Visuals communicate information more efficiently and memorably.

Don't Read Your Slides Aloud

Since audiences read faster than speakers can talk, reading slides aloud leads to audience boredom and disengagement. Slides should serve as a launchpad for discussion, not a script.

Important Terms

Slide deck

The entire collection of slides used as visual support during a presentation.

Bullet point

A short phrase or sentence in a list, marked by a dot or symbol, used to organize information on slides.

Visual hierarchy

The arrangement of elements on a slide so that the most important information stands out first, guiding the viewer's attention.

Template

A pre-designed slide layout that provides consistent formatting across a presentation, including matching fonts, colors, and overall design.

Introduction to Effective Slide Design

Slides are visual aids, not documents

The fundamental error many presenters make is treating slides as a report to be read instead of a visual backdrop.

The Principle of Restraint: "Less Is More"

Audience attention span

People cannot effectively read dense text on a screen and listen to a speaker simultaneously.

Impact of dense slides

When a paragraph appears on screen, the audience will read it, making the speaker "background noise" to their own presentation.

The Slide Purpose Rule

Singular focus

Every single slide should have one specific purpose: to reinforce the exact point being made *at that moment*.

Actionable advice

If a slide attempts to convey two different ideas, split it into two slides. If a slide doesn't directly support your current point, remove it.

Templates for Consistency

Purpose

A template provides a pre-designed layout that ensures a unified and consistent look throughout your presentation (matching fonts, colors, and overall design).

Benefits

Professional appearance, saves design time, and maintains visual coherence.

Selection

Choose a template that suits your presentation's purpose (e.g., minimalist for a business proposal, bolder for a club pitch).

Images, Charts, and Visual Evidence

Power of visuals

The most effective slides often replace extensive text with powerful visuals like photographs, charts, diagrams, or even a single striking number. Visuals are more engaging and memorable.

Guidelines for using visuals

Common Mistake: Reading Your Slides Aloud

Audience pace

Audiences can read much faster than a speaker can speak.

Consequence

If slides contain full sentences, the audience will finish reading before you finish speaking, leading to boredom and disengagement.

Solution

Use slides as a launchpad for your spoken content, not as a script to be read verbatim. Keep them brief and visually focused.
