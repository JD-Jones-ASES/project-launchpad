Chapter 10, Section 4

Budgets and Timelines in Proposals

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Practical Planning Foundation

Budgets and timelines transform proposals from "wish lists" into actionable plans, showing respect for resources and thorough preparation.

Budget Transparency

A detailed financial plan organized by category, making it clear where every dollar goes. Key principles include specificity, honesty, completeness, showing calculations, and including a contingency.

Project Timeline

A schedule outlining project phases, key checkpoints (milestones), and tangible results (deliverables).

Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA) / Return on Investment (ROI)

Methods for justifying project costs by demonstrating that the expected benefits outweigh the expenditures, often by quantifying benefits or comparing costs to alternatives.

Stakeholder Analysis

Identifying all individuals or groups affected by a project and considering their specific interests and concerns to create a more persuasive proposal.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Complete Proposal Flow

A structured process for developing a proposal, from initial research and solution design to budget creation, timeline mapping, drafting, review, and preparing an elevator pitch.

Important Terms (1/2)

Milestone

A significant checkpoint or achievement in a project's timeline, marking a point where something important is completed or a decision must be made.

Deliverable

A tangible product or result that must be provided at the end of a project or project phase, serving as concrete proof of progress.

Cost-Benefit Analysis

An analysis comparing the costs of a project to its expected benefits to determine its overall value.

ROI (Return on Investment)

The benefit gained compared to the cost spent, often expressed as a percentage, used to evaluate the efficiency of an investment.

Stakeholder

Anyone who is affected by or has an interest in a project's outcome, whose concerns should be addressed in a proposal.

Important Terms (2/2)

Implementation

The process of putting a plan into action or carrying it out.

Example Budget Analysis (After-School Math Tutoring Program):

Detail and Verification

Each line item clearly states the quantity and unit cost, allowing readers to easily verify the math.

Categorization

Costs are logically grouped (e.g., Materials, Training, Operations) for clarity.

Contingency

A 15% contingency is included, showing foresight for unforeseen expenses.

Funding Sources

The budget separates funding sources from costs, providing a complete financial picture.

Small Cushion

Total funding slightly exceeds total costs, offering a modest financial safety net.

Making the Practical Case: Cost-Benefit and ROI

Cost-Benefit Analysis

This involves comparing the project's costs against its expected benefits.

Return on Investment (ROI)

A more specific measure, often quantitative, showing the benefit gained relative to the cost spent.

Justifying Costs Effectively:

Per-unit Cost

Break down the total cost into a per-student, per-hour, or per-unit cost. (e.g., "$4,800 for 90 students = $53 per student").

Comparison to Alternatives

Show how your project's cost compares favorably to other options (e.g., "$53 per student vs. $45-$65 per hour for private tutoring").

Examples

Key Concepts (1/2)

Why are budgets and timelines crucial components of a proposal?

They demonstrate practical planning, show respect for the reader's resources, and answer 'How much will it cost?' and 'How long will it take?'

What is the primary goal of writing a proposal budget?

Transparency, allowing the reader to see exactly where every dollar goes through a detailed plan of expected costs, organized by category.

What does it mean to 'be specific' when writing a proposal budget?

Each line item should show quantity and unit cost (e.g., 'Workbooks (90 copies @ $4.50 each): $405') rather than vague totals.

Why is honesty a key principle in budget creation?

Underestimating costs can backfire, leading to a project running out of money. It's better to round up than down to reflect realistic costs.

What is meant by 'include everything' in a proposal budget?

Account for all potential costs, including often-overlooked items like printing, transportation, or substitute coverage, to ensure professionalism.

Why should you 'show your math' in a proposal budget?

When readers can see the calculations for each line item, it builds trust in the overall total and demonstrates thoroughness.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is a 'contingency' in a budget, and what is its typical percentage?

A contingency is a buffer (typically 10-15%) for unexpected costs, showing realistic planning and foresight.

What is the purpose of listing funding sources separately in a proposal budget?

It shows the reader how the project's costs will be covered and provides clarity on financial backing.

Describe the 'Explain to Your Principal' test for reviewing a budget.

Imagine your principal asking about any line item's cost. If you can provide a clear, specific reason for every expense without guessing, your budget is strong.

What is the main purpose of a proposal timeline?

It shows when each phase of the project will happen, from start to finish, answering 'what happens tomorrow? Next month? At the end of the year?'

Define 'milestone' in the context of a project timeline.
