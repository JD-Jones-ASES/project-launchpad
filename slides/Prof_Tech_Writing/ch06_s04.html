Formatting Consistency

It is essential to choose either Block or Modified Block format and use it consistently throughout the entire letter. Mixing formats is unprofessional.

Important Terms (1/2)

Block format

A letter layout in which every line—date, address, greeting, body, and closing—starts at the left margin.

Modified block format

A letter layout in which the date, closing, and signature are centered or shifted to the right, while the body paragraphs remain left-aligned.

Salutation

The greeting that opens a letter (e.g., “Dear Ms. Rivera:”).

Complimentary close

The polite sign-off before the signature (e.g., “Sincerely,” or “Regards,”).

Enclosure

A document included with a letter; noted at the bottom with “Enclosure” or “Enc.”

Important Terms (2/2)

CC notation

A note at the bottom of a letter indicating who else receives a copy (from “carbon copy”).

Sincerely

The most common and universally safe complimentary close in business letters.

Regards

A slightly less formal complimentary close, appropriate for ongoing professional relationships.

Professional reference

A person who can speak to your work ethic, skills, and character in a professional context.

The Purpose of Standardized Letter Formats

Quick Information Retrieval

A consistent structure helps readers easily locate key information within the letter.

Professionalism and Credibility

Using a recognized format signals that the writer is professional, detail-oriented, and credible.

When to Choose a Letter Over an Email

Legal Significance

For matters like formal complaints, claims, contractual agreements, or other legally binding communications.

Permanent Record

When a physical, official record on company letterhead is required.

High Formality

For important communications such as job applications, resignations, official responses to complaints, or formal invitations.

Recipient Expectation

Some organizations, government agencies, or individuals (especially older professionals) prefer or expect formal letters.

Weight and Authority

To convey a higher level of importance, seriousness, or authority than a digital message might.

Block Format

Description

The most widely used business letter format in the United States. It presents a clean, modern, and easy-to-type appearance.

Layout

Every single line of text—from the date to the closing and signature—begins precisely at the left margin.

Paragraphs

Paragraphs are not indented. Instead, they are separated by a blank line to improve readability.

Modified Block Format

Description

A more traditional letter format that is still commonly used, particularly in fields like law, government, and established corporations.

Layout

The date, complimentary close, and signature block are shifted to the center or slightly to the right of the center of the page.

Left-Aligned Elements

The inside address, salutation, and body paragraphs remain left-aligned.

Paragraph Indentation (Optional)

Some variations of the modified block format include indenting the first line of each body paragraph by half an inch, though this is not mandatory.

Salutation Details

Purpose

The formal greeting at the beginning of the letter.

Punctuation

Always followed by a colon (:) in business letters, never a comma.

Examples

Avoid "To Whom It May Concern"

This phrase suggests a lack of effort to identify the recipient and can be perceived as impersonal or unprofessional. Always try to find a specific name, title, or department.

Complimentary Close Details

Purpose

The polite closing phrase before your signature.

Punctuation

Always capitalize only the first word and follow it with a comma (,).

Choosing the Right Close

End Notations

Enclosure (Enc.)

Indicates that additional documents are included with the letter.

CC Notation

Stands for "carbon copy" and lists the names of other individuals who also received a copy of the letter.

Typist Initials

In some traditional or specialized offices (e.g., legal, medical), the writer's initials (capitalized) followed by the typist's initials (lowercase) are included.

Writing Tips

Consistency is Key

Once you select a format (Block or Modified Block), use it uniformly throughout the entire letter. Do not mix elements from different formats within a single document.

Follow Guidelines

If your employer or school specifies a preferred letter format, always adhere to those guidelines.

When in Doubt, Use Block

Block format is widely accepted and generally considered a safe choice for most business communications.
