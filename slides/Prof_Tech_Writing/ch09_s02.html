Executive Summaries

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Definition and Purpose of an Executive Summary

Importance for Busy Readers

Executives, managers, and decision-makers often rely solely on executive summaries to understand complex reports, make decisions, and identify areas requiring deeper investigation.

Essential Components

An effective executive summary must include the report's purpose, key findings, conclusions, and any recommendations.

Writing Strategy: Write Last

The executive summary should only be written after the entire report is complete, ensuring accuracy and completeness of the summary's content.

Proportional Length

The length of an executive summary should be proportional to the full report, typically no more than 10% of the total length (e.g., one paragraph for a 5-page report, one page for a 20-page report).

Key Concepts (2/2)

Leading with the Bottom Line

Start the summary with the most critical finding or conclusion to immediately convey the report's main point.

Inclusion of Key Data

Effective summaries provide specific facts and data, not just general statements, to give readers concrete information.

Matching the Report Content

Every claim or piece of information in the executive summary must be present and supported in the full report; no new information should be introduced in the summary.

The "Send Only This" Test

A practical method to assess an executive summary's effectiveness by imagining if a reader could understand the situation and make decisions based on the summary alone.

Informational Reports

Reports that primarily present facts, findings, and conclusions without making explicit recommendations, leaving decision-making to the reader (as seen in the model document).

Important Terms (1/2)

Executive Summary

A brief overview, typically placed at the beginning of a report, that highlights the document's key points, findings, conclusions, and recommendations for busy readers.

Key Findings

The most important facts, data, or observations derived from the research or analysis presented in a report.

Conclusion

An interpretation of what the findings mean, drawn logically from the evidence and data presented in the report.

Recommendation

A suggested course of action or next step, often based on the report's findings and conclusions.

Informational Report

A type of report that primarily presents data, facts, and analyses without offering specific recommendations, allowing the reader to draw their own conclusions and make decisions.

Important Terms (2/2)

Proportionality

The concept that the length of the executive summary should be scaled appropriately to the length of the full report it summarizes.

Bottom Line

The most important result, finding, or conclusion of a report; often referred to as the main takeaway.

Writing an Effective Executive Summary: Guidelines

Write it last

Complete the entire report before attempting to summarize it. This ensures accuracy and avoids vague generalizations.

Keep it proportional

The summary's length should correspond to the full report's length. A common rule is no more than 10% of the total report length. For instance, a 5-page report might have a 4-6 sentence summary, wh...

Lead with the bottom line

Start with the most important finding or conclusion. Do not bury the main point under background information.

Include key data

Move beyond general statements. Provide specific facts, figures, and quantities (e.g., "$1.34 million" instead of "sales increased").

Match the report

Ensure every piece of information, claim, or conclusion in the summary is directly supported and elaborated upon in the main body of the report. Do not introduce new information in the summary.

Weak vs. Strong Executive Summaries

Weak Example

General, vague statements ("went pretty well," "collected a lot," "a few issues") that lack specific data or clear actionable insights. It could describe almost any event.

Strong Example

Specific, data-rich, and conclusive ("2,847 pounds of food," "exceeding the 2,000-pound goal by 42%," "two logistical issues," "recommend repeating the drive annually, beginning planning in August ...

Model Document: Short Informational Report with Executive Summary

Executive Summary

Clearly states the purpose, key findings (speed issues, dead zones, outages), and an estimated impact (lost instructional minutes). It immediately informs the reader of the core problems.

Introduction

Outlines the report's purpose, scope (what it covers and doesn't), and methodology (how data was collected).

Findings

Presents the detailed results, broken down into sections (Network Speed, Coverage Gaps, Reliability), often supported by tables or bullet points.

Conclusion

Interprets the findings, explaining *why* the issues exist (e.g., old access points, exceeding device capacity).

Appendix

Shows how to handle large datasets by referencing them, keeping the main report concise and readable.
