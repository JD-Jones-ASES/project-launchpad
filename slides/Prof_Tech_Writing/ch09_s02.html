Key Concepts (2/2)

Numbered Sections

For longer reports, a numbering system (e.g., 1.0, 2.1) helps readers navigate complex documents and reference specific parts easily.

Important Terms (1/3)

Executive Summary

A concise, one-paragraph to one-page overview that summarizes the entire report, allowing busy readers to grasp the main points quickly.

Introduction

The opening section of a report that outlines its purpose, scope, and necessary background information.

Purpose

The "why" of the report; its reason for existing or what it aims to achieve.

Scope

The boundaries of what a report covers and, equally important, what it does not cover, preventing misunderstandings about the report's focus.

Background

The contextual information a reader needs to understand the report's content and significance.

Important Terms (2/3)

Findings

The factual results, data, observations, or discoveries presented in the main body of a report, obtained through research or investigation.

Chronological Organization

Arranging report content in the order in which events occurred, commonly used for progress, incident, or trip reports.

Topical Organization

Arranging report content by distinct subjects, categories, or themes, suitable for inspection or status reports covering multiple areas.

Most-Important-First Organization

Structuring the report so that the most critical information, key finding, or urgent issue is presented at the beginning, ideal when immediate action is required.

Headers

Short, descriptive titles used to introduce sections and subsections of a report, making the document easier to scan and understand.

Introduction

This section sets the stage by explaining why the report exists, what it covers (and doesn't cover), and what context the reader needs to understand the content.

Body / Findings

This is the core of the report, where all the detailed information, data, analysis, and observations are presented. It must be well-organized using descriptive headers and subheaders.

Conclusion

This section interprets the findings, explaining what they mean and their significance. It doesn't introduce new information but synthesizes what has already been presented.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the primary purpose of structuring a report with labeled sections?

To allow readers to quickly find the information they need and make scanning easy, as most professionals do not read reports cover-to-cover.

How do most professionals typically read reports?

They scan the executive summary, jump to the section that matters to them, and then move on.

List the six standard sections found in most informational reports.

Title page, Executive Summary, Introduction, Body / Findings, Conclusion, and Appendix (if needed).

Which standard report sections may be omitted in short reports (1-3 pages)?

The title page and the appendix.

What additional sections might be included in long reports?

A table of contents, list of figures, and a references section.

What three key questions does the introduction of a report answer for the reader?

Purpose, Scope, and Background.

Key Concepts (2/2)

In a report's introduction, what does 'Purpose' clarify?

Why the report exists (e.g., 'This report summarizes the results of the October safety inspection of Building C.').

Define 'Scope' in the context of a report's introduction.

The boundaries of what the report covers—and what it does not cover—to prevent misunderstandings (e.g., specifying a project's weeks covered or incident deta...

What information should be included under 'Background' in a report's introduction?

Any context the reader needs to understand the report's content (e.g., a project's overall timeline or last service date of equipment).

What are 'findings' in the body of a report?

The factual results discovered through research, investigation, or observation.

Describe the 'Chronological' organization method for a report's body and when it is best used.

Organized by time (e.g., Week 1, Week 2). Best for progress reports, incident reports, and trip reports.

Describe the 'Topical' organization method for a report's body and when it is best used.

Organized by subject or category (e.g., Electrical systems, Plumbing, HVAC). Best for inspection reports and status reports covering multiple areas.
