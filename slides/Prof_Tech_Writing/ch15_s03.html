Purposes of Citations

Citations serve to establish credibility by showing evidence support, allow readers to verify information independently, and ethically give credit to original researchers.

The Simple Rule for Avoiding Plagiarism

If an idea, fact, statistic, argument, or phrase did not originate in your own mind, it must be cited. The only exception is widely known "common knowledge." When in doubt, always cite.

Important Terms (1/2)

Primary Source

Original, firsthand evidence, such as data you collected, experiments you ran, or official records.

Secondary Source

An analysis, interpretation, or summary of primary sources, like textbook chapters or review articles.

Paraphrase

To restate someone else's ideas in your own words while preserving the original meaning and giving credit to the source.

Quote Directly

To use the exact words from a source, enclosed in quotation marks, with proper attribution.

Citation

A reference within the text that tells the reader where a piece of information, data, or idea came from, typically including author and date.

Important Terms (2/2)

Reference

An entry in a reference list or bibliography at the end of a document that provides full publication information for a source cited in the text.

Plagiarism

Presenting someone else's words, ideas, data, or creative work as your own, intentionally or accidentally, by failing to give proper credit.

Common Knowledge

Facts so widely known that they do not need attribution (e.g., "The sun rises in the east").

The Imperative of Evidence

Foundation of Argument

White papers and technical proposals require every significant claim to be supported by credible evidence. Without it, these documents are merely opinions or wish lists.

Section Focus

This section guides you through finding, evaluating, using, and citing evidence effectively to build strong arguments.

Primary vs. Secondary Sources

Defining Primary Sources

These are original, firsthand pieces of evidence.

Defining Secondary Sources

These sources analyze, interpret, or summarize primary sources.

Writing Tip: Lead with Primary, Support with Secondary

Prioritize primary sources for your main arguments and specific findings. Use secondary sources to offer context, show larger patterns, or explain background information.

Evaluating Source Credibility

Importance

Not all sources are suitable; scrutinizing their credibility is essential before inclusion.

Five Key Criteria

Common Mistake: Citing Everything You Find

Including weak, irrelevant, or outdated sources detracts from your argument. Quality over quantity is crucial; a few strong sources are more persuasive than many mediocre ones.

Citation Basics

What is a Citation?

A citation is a reference that informs the reader of the source of information, data, or ideas.

Three Purposes of Citations

Citation Styles

Various styles exist (e.g., APA, MLA, Chicago, IEEE). Consistency in using one style throughout your document is more important than the specific style chosen.

Common Format (Author-Date)

The Reference List

Definition

A complete list of all sources cited in your text, appearing at the end of the document.

Two-Way Correspondence

Every source cited in your text must have an entry in the reference list, and every entry in the reference list must be cited somewhere in the text. This ensures integrity and verifiability.

The annotated reference list provides examples of how to format different types of sources (journal articles, technical reports, web resources) in a consistent style.

Avoiding Plagiarism

Definition

Presenting someone else's words, ideas, data, or creative work as your own, whether intentionally or accidentally, by failing to give proper credit.

Serious Consequences

Plagiarism is a severe professional and academic offense that can lead to career termination, legal action, and permanent damage to reputation.

Forms of Plagiarism

Common Knowledge

The only exception to the citation rule. This refers to facts so widely known they require no attribution (e.g., "Water freezes at 32°F"). If unsure, cite it.
