Chapter 11, Section 1

Audience Analysis: Beyond the Basics - Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Deep Audience Analysis

Moving beyond basic questions like "who will read this?" to understand the specific skill level, context, and goals of diverse users for a product or system.

Three Audience Skill Levels

Categorizing users as Novice, Intermediate, or Expert, each requiring different approaches to content, detail, and guidance.

Layering Information

A writing strategy that places the most essential information first, followed by details and advanced notes, allowing different users to consume content efficiently based on their needs.

Context of Use

The circumstances (physical location, available time, device) under which a user accesses documentation, which significantly influences how the information should be presented.

User Goals (Learning vs. Quick Reference)

Recognizing that users may seek documentation either to gain a comprehensive understanding (learning) or to find a specific answer quickly (quick reference).

Key Concepts (2/2)

Task-Oriented Documentation

Organizing content around what the user wants to *accomplish* (tasks) rather than around the product's features, making it more intuitive and user-friendly.

Strategies for Multiple Audiences

Techniques to design a single documentation set that effectively caters to various skill levels and goals, such as clear navigation, labeled advanced sections, and glossaries.

Important Terms (1/4)

user

The person who actually uses a product or system in their daily work; not the developer or purchaser.

documentation

Written materials that explain how to use, maintain, or understand a product or system (e.g., user guides, help files, FAQs).

user guide

A comprehensive document explaining product usage from start to finish, more detailed than a quick-reference card but more practical than a technical manual.

knowledge base

A searchable collection of articles answering common product questions, often found online (e.g., app help centers).

onboarding

The process of helping a new user get started with a product or system, aiming for a smooth first experience.

Important Terms (2/4)

getting started

A short guide that walks a new user through initial setup and basic tasks, often the first section of a user guide.

FAQ (Frequently Asked Questions)

A list of common questions and their answers, used to save time by addressing recurring issues.

troubleshooting

The process of identifying and solving problems; a troubleshooting guide helps users fix issues independently.

cross-reference

A pointer from one part of a document to another related part, directing readers to more information (e.g., "See Section 3.2 for details").

navigation

The system that helps readers find information within a document (e.g., table of contents, headings, hyperlinks).

Important Terms (3/4)

hierarchy

An organized system of levels, from general to specific, used in document structure (e.g., heading hierarchy).

chunk

A small, self-contained unit of information covering one topic or task, making documentation easier to scan and write.

task-oriented

Documentation organized around what the user wants to accomplish, rather than around the product's features.

screenshot

A captured image of a computer or phone screen, used to show the user what they should see for verification or instruction.

callout

A label, arrow, or numbered marker on a screenshot that draws attention to a specific element, providing instruction.

Important Terms (4/4)

tooltip

A small pop-up text that appears when a user hovers over or taps an on-screen element, offering brief help without changing screens.

Audience Skill Levels: Novice, Intermediate, Expert

Novice

Users who have never used the product. They need step-by-step guidance, definitions of basic terms, and reassurance. Do not assume prior knowledge of menus or icons.

Intermediate

Users who regularly perform basic tasks but want to explore advanced features. They require clear instructions without excessive hand-holding and can follow cross-references.

Expert

Users who know the product well and need quick answers to specific questions. They prefer reference-style information (tables, parameter lists) and can be frustrated by lengthy introductions.

Context of Use

At a desk, with time (e.g., onboarding)

Users can read comprehensive "getting started" guides. Longer paragraphs and detailed explanations are appropriate.

At a desk, in a hurry (e.g., troubleshooting)

Users need quick answers. Headings, bullet points, and a robust search function are paramount.

On a phone, in the field (e.g., technician)

Requires short steps, large text, and minimal scrolling for ease of use in practical, hands-on situations.

In a meeting or class (e.g., teaching)

Quick-reference cards and projected screenshots are more effective than lengthy manuals.

Goals: Learning vs. Quick Reference

Learning

Users new to the product who want tutorials, examples, and step-by-step explanations to build understanding.

Quick Reference

Users who know the basics but need a reminder for a specific task. They seek tables, numbered steps, and easily searchable headings.
