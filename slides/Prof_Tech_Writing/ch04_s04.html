The Memo Format

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Purpose of Memos

Memos are formal internal documents used to announce policies or changes, request action, summarize decisions, and create official records within an organization.

When to Use a Memo

Use a memo when the message is directed to a group, is important enough to be filed or referenced later, announces a formal policy, decision, or request, or requires the visual structure of a heade...

The Header Block

Every memo begins with a standardized header block containing "TO," "FROM," "DATE," and "RE" (Regarding or Subject) fields, providing essential context before the body text.

Specificity in RE Line

The "RE" line functions as the subject line and must be specific and include key details (like effective dates) to help readers quickly understand the memo's content and for future reference.

Three-Part Memo Body

The body of a memo follows a clear structure: a direct purpose statement in the first paragraph, detailed information in the middle paragraphs, and a final paragraph for required actions, deadlines...

Key Concepts (2/2)

Direct Purpose Statement

The first sentence of a memo's body should immediately state the reason for writing, answering "Why am I reading this?" and avoiding unnecessary background information or pleasantries upfront.

Important Terms

Memorandum (Memo)

A formal written message used for internal communication within an organization, signaling content that is important enough to be organized, formatted, and kept on file.

Header Block

The formatted section at the top of a memo containing the "To," "From," "Date," and "Subject" (or "RE") fields, providing key contextual information.

RE Line

Shorthand for "Regarding" or "Subject," this line in the header block specifies the topic of the memo and should be clear and specific for easy identification and filing.

Purpose Statement

The crucial first paragraph of a memo's body that immediately states the reason for writing and the main point of the message.

What Is a Memo?

Announcing policies or changes

E.g., a new time-tracking system.

Requesting action

E.g., submitting budget proposals by a deadline.

Summarizing decisions

E.g., reporting a committee's vendor selection.

Creating a record

E.g., documenting a project timeline for official reference.

"Before" version

Wastes words with generic background, delaying the main point.

"After" version

Immediately states the policy, its effective date, and what the memo will cover. This is concise, respectful of the reader's time, and highly effective.
