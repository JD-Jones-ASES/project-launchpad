Every Career Requires Writing (1/3)

Communication Documents (Chapters 2, 4, 8)

Essential for daily workplace interaction (e.g., emails, memos, meeting notes).

Technical Documents (Chapters 3, 5, 7, 11)

Critical in fields like engineering, healthcare, technology, and science (e.g., instructions, specifications, user guides).

Formal Correspondence (Chapter 6)

Represents you and your organization externally (e.g., business letters).

Analytical Documents (Chapters 9, 13)

Used for data interpretation and decision support (e.g., reports).

Persuasive Documents (Chapters 10, 15)

Aim to argue for specific actions (e.g., proposals, pitches, white papers).

The Skills That Transfer Everywhere (1/2)

Audience Awareness (Chapter 1)

Tailoring your message to your reader's needs and background.

Clarity and Conciseness (Chapters 2, 4)

Expressing ideas directly and efficiently, avoiding unnecessary words.

Logical Structure (Chapters 3, 9, 13)

Organizing information coherently so readers can easily follow your argument or information.

Appropriate Tone (Chapters 6, 10)

Matching your language and style to the purpose and audience of your communication.

Precision (Chapters 5, 7)

Using specific, accurate, and measurable language rather than vague generalities.

The Skills That Transfer Everywhere (2/2)

Evidence over Assertion (Chapters 13, 15)

Supporting claims with facts, data, and credible examples.

Revision as Process (This Chapter)

Understanding that good writing is iterative; first drafts are starting points, not finished products.

Setting Writing Goals

Specific

Clearly defined (e.g., "I will proofread every email" rather than "I will write better").

Measurable

Quantifiable, allowing you to track progress (e.g., "I will reduce my average sentence length to under 20 words").

Realistic

Achievable within a reasonable timeframe (e.g., "I will revise one document per week" rather than "I will become a perfect writer").
