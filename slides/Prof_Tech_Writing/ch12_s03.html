Important Terms (1/2)

Resume

A one- or two-page document that summarizes your education, experience, skills, and qualifications for a specific job.

Curriculum Vitae (CV)

A comprehensive document listing all academic and professional accomplishments; longer and more detailed than a resume, used mainly in academia and international job markets.

Chronological

A resume format organized by time order, typically listing the most recent experience first.

Functional

A resume format organized by skill categories rather than by time, emphasizing what you can do rather than when you did it.

Objective

A brief statement at the top of a resume describing the specific position or goal the applicant is seeking. Must be specific to be effective.

Important Terms (2/2)

Summary

A short paragraph at the top of a resume highlighting key qualifications and strengths; more common than objectives in modern resumes.

Reference

A person who can speak to your qualifications, character, or work ethic when contacted by a potential employer. (Note: "References available upon request" should NOT be included on a resume.)

Reverse Chronological Order

A common organizational method, especially for experience and education sections, where the most recent entry is listed first, followed by previous entries in descending date order.

Choosing the Right Resume Format

When in doubt, use the chronological format.

It is the safest choice for most situations, especially for a student's first resume.

Contact Information

Placement

Prominently at the top.

Content

Your full name, phone number, professional email address, and city/state.

What NOT to include

Full street address (outdated).

Common Mistake

Unprofessional email addresses (e.g., "sk8rboi2009@email.com"). Create a professional email address using your name (e.g., firstname.lastname@email.com).

Objective vs. Summary

Objective

States what *you are looking for* (e.g., "Seeking a part-time position at a veterinary clinic...").

Summary

States what *you offer* (e.g., "Reliable student with two years of pet-sitting experience...").

For Students

Either works.

Key

Be specific, not generic. A generic objective ("Seeking a challenging position in a dynamic company") tells the reader nothing. A specific one names the company, position, and relevant qualities.

What NOT to Include on a Resume (1/2)

"References available upon request."

Employers assume this.

A photograph

Creates legal complications for U.S. employers (discrimination concerns).

Personal information

Age, marital status, religion, Social Security number.

Generic objective

If it doesn't add specific value, skip it.

Every job you've ever held

Include only what's relevant to the target position.

What NOT to Include on a Resume (2/2)

Irrelevant hobbies

Only include hobbies if they directly relate to the job (e.g., moderating a book review channel for a bookstore job).

Resume vs. Curriculum Vitae (CV)

Resume

Concise (1-2 pages), targeted for specific jobs, used for most non-academic positions in the U.S.

CV

Comprehensive (multiple pages), lists all academic/professional accomplishments, used mainly in academia, medicine, and international job markets.

Specific Objective

Action Verbs

Quantification
