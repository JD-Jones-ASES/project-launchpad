The Virtues and Justice

Study Slides

Voluntary vs. Involuntary Action

Voluntary Action

Originates within the agent; the agent has knowledge of the particular circumstances. These actions express a person's deliberate character.

Involuntary Action

Done under **compulsion** (the moving principle is external, and the agent contributes nothing) or through **ignorance** of relevant circumstances.

Proairesis (Deliberate Choice)

This is the specific act of will involving rational deliberation. It distinguishes a morally responsible agent from someone acting on mere impulse.

Forms of Justice

Type. Focus

Principle

Distributive Justice. Allocation of shared goods (offices, honors, civic benefits).

**Proportional:** Based on merit, contribution, or worth.

Corrective Justice. Restoring fairness after a wrong (crime, contract breach).

**Arithmetic:** Stripping the wrongdoer of gain and compensating the victim.

Natural vs. Conventional Justice

Natural Justice (Physis)

Standards of justice that hold everywhere by nature, independently of human enactment.

Conventional Justice (Nomos)

Laws, customs, or decrees that vary by community and are established by agreement.

Modern Perspectives on Justice

John Rawls (*A Theory of Justice*)

Uses the "Veil of Ignorance" to argue for principles that protect the least advantaged. This abstracts from the concrete community Aristotle emphasized.

Robert Nozick (*Anarchy, State, and Utopia*)

Argues for "entitlement theory," emphasizing individual rights and just acquisition, though largely abandoning Aristotle’s focus on the common good (teleology).

Glossary of Important Terms (1/3)

Akrasia

Weakness of will; acting against one's knowledge of what is right due to desire.

Andreia

Courage; the mean between cowardice and rashness.

Arete

Virtue or excellence; the quality that enables a thing to perform its function well.

Cardinal Virtues

The four principal moral virtues: Prudence, Justice, Fortitude, and Temperance.

Dikaiosyne

Justice; the virtue concerned with our relationships with others.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/3)

Eudaimonia

Happiness or human flourishing; the highest good and activity of the soul in accordance with virtue.

Hexis

A stable state or settled disposition of character (translated by Aquinas as *habitus*).

Logos

Reason, speech, or rational account; the basis of human political community.

Nomos

Law, custom, or convention; established rules of a specific community.

Phronesis

Practical wisdom; the intellectual virtue required to identify the mean in particular cases.

Glossary of Important Terms (3/3)

Physis

Nature; the inherent character of a thing or what holds true universally.

Positive Law

Law enacted by human authority (statutes, decrees).

Proairesis

Deliberate choice; the union of rational deliberation and the act of will.

Telos

The end, purpose, or goal of a thing.

Veil of Ignorance

John Rawls’s thought experiment for choosing principles of justice without knowing one’s social standing.
