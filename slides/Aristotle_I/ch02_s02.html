Natural vs. Supernatural Happiness

Type of Happiness. Description

Source/Means

Imperfect Happiness. *Eudaimonia*: Virtuous activity over a complete life; involves health, friendship, and external goods.

Achieved through natural powers, habituation, and practical wisdom.

Perfect Happiness. *Beatific Vision*: The direct contemplation of God as He is in Himself; the final rest of the restless human heart.

Exceeds natural capacity; given as a gift of divine grace.

The Nature of Human Action

Human Actions

These proceed from a deliberate will and reason. Man is "master of his actions" only in this category.

Actions of a Man

These are actions found in a person that are not proper to "man as man" (e.g., involuntary or irrational movements).

Q: How does Aquinas redefine the Aristotelian concept of the "end" as a cause?

While an "end" is the last thing in the order of execution (the finish line), Aquinas argues it is the *first* thing in the "order of the agent's intention." In this way, the goal acts as a final cause that moves the person to act.

Q: What is the "capacity for God" (*capax Dei*) mentioned by Bernard of Clairvaux?

It is the intuition that the human heart has a vacuum or a capacity that nothing less than God can fill, echoing Augustine’s sentiment that the heart is "restless" until it rests in God.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/2)

Q: According to Aquinas, why is a human being "more noble" than an animal, even though an animal can reach its end through natural powers alone?

A nature that can reach a perfect, infinite good (but needs help/grace to do so) is superior to a nature that can only reach a lower, imperfect good on its own. Dependence on God for perfect happiness is a sign of human dignity, not a defect.

Q: What triggered the 20th-century revival of virtue ethics?

The revival began with Elizabeth Anscombe’s 1958 article "Modern Moral Philosophy," which argued that Kantian deontology and Utilitarianism were incoherent without the teleological (goal-oriented) framework of Aristotle.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Beatific Vision (*visio Dei*)

The direct vision or contemplation of God as He is, which Aquinas identifies as the only source of perfect human happiness.

Deontology (Kantian Ethics)

A moral framework that grounds ethics in the rational principle of duty rather than the pursuit of happiness or human nature.

Eudaimonia

Aristotle’s term for happiness, defined as the soul's activity in accordance with virtue over a complete life.

Grace

The free gift of God that elevates the soul beyond its natural capacity to participate in divine life.

Natural Powers

The inherent capacities of a human being (reason and will) to achieve goods in the present life without direct divine intervention.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Positive Psychology

A modern psychological movement focused on human "flourishing"—meaningful relationships and the exercise of strengths—rather than just the treatment of mental illness.

Summa Theologica

Thomas Aquinas's masterwork, which provides a comprehensive synthesis of Christian theology and Aristotelian philosophy.

Teleology

A framework that understands things in terms of their "end," "goal," or "purpose."

Utilitarianism

The moral philosophy that defines the right action as that which produces the greatest happiness (defined as pleasure) for the greatest number.
