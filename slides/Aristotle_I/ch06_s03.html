Q: According to Aristotle, what is the defining difference between "government" and "laws"?

Government is the ordering of the state, particularly regarding the regulation of magistrates and the placement of supreme power. Laws are the rules that direct the conduct of those magistrates in their office and the punishment of offenders.

Glossary of Important Terms

Key Concepts (1/2)

How did Aristotle's approach to political science differ from pure ideal theory?

He reasoned from empirical data gathered from observing 158 different real-world constitutions.

Which of the 158 constitutions compiled by Aristotle and his students still survives today?

The Constitution of Athens.

Why does Aristotle compare a political theorist to a physician in Book IV?

Both must understand not only the ideal state of health/justice but also what is possible for actual subjects.

Term: $phronesis$

Definition: Practical wisdom, or the capacity of a statesman to judge the best course of action in specific circumstances.

According to Aristotle, what is the relationship between a city's laws and its constitution?

Laws must be framed to suit the constitution, rather than the constitution to the laws.

In Aristotle's definition, what is the primary function or 'office' of the laws?

To direct the conduct of magistrates and the punishment of offenders.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How does Aristotle define 'government' as distinct from 'laws'?

The ordering of a state regarding how magistrates are regulated, where supreme power is placed, and the community's final object.

What are the three 'regular' forms of government identified by Aristotle?

Kingly (monarchy), Aristocratical, and Free State (polity).

According to Aristotle's classification, what is the 'excess' or perversion of a Kingly government?

Tyranny.

According to Aristotle's classification, what is the 'excess' or perversion of an Aristocracy?

Oligarchy.

According to Aristotle's classification, what is the 'excess' or perversion of a Free State (Polity)?

Democracy.

What ethical principle from Aristotle's 'treatise on Morals' serves as the foundation for his ideal practical constitution?

The doctrine of the mean, asserting that the middle life is the happiest and most virtuous.
