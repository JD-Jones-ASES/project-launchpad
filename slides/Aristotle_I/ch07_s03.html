Aristotle on the Ideal State and Education: A Study Guide

Study Slides

The Three Sorts of Goods

External Goods

Wealth, property, power, and reputation. These have a natural limit; excess can be hurtful or useless.

Goods of the Body

Health and strength.

Goods of the Soul

Fortitude, temperance, justice, and prudence. These are the most valuable; the higher the degree of these qualities, the more useful and noble they become.

Education (Paideia) as a Political Necessity

Constitutional Preservation

Every government is preserved by the "manners" or character of its citizens. A democracy requires democratic manners, and an oligarchy requires oligarchic manners.

Legislative Duty

The legislator must prioritize the education of youth because neglect of education "is hurtful to the city."

Public vs. Private

Since the city has a single "end" (the best life), education ought to be "one and the same" for all citizens and managed as a common care rather than left to private choice.

The Curriculum and the Liberal Arts

Work, Play, and Noble Leisure (Schole)

Labor/Work

Effort directed toward a necessary end that one does not yet possess. It is often attended by pain or continued exertion.

Play/Amusement

A "medicine" for the exhaustion of labor. It provides relaxation but is not an end in itself.

Leisure (Schole)

The highest form of activity, performed for its own sake. It consists of pleasure, happiness, and an agreeable life.

Glossary of Important Terms

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to Aristotle, what is the primary instrument by which a state secures the best life for its citizens?

Education.

What Greek term describes the entire formation of a human being, including character, taste, and civic virtue?

$paideia$.

Aristotle argues that the best life for both a city and an individual is a life of active virtue and _____.

Reason.

According to the text, why can a city not be genuinely happy through wealth or military power alone?

Because happiness requires the exercise of virtue rather than just external goods.

Aristotle divides the 'goods' of human life into three categories: external goods, goods of the body, and goods of the _____.

Soul.

Why does Aristotle argue that external goods are less valuable than goods of the soul?

External goods have a limit and can be hurtful in excess, while spiritual goods are more useful the higher their degree.

Key Concepts (2/2)

According to Aristotle, does virtue produce external goods, or do external goods produce virtue?

Virtue produces external goods.

Aristotle claims that a city's happiness is exactly the same as the happiness of a(n) _____.

Individual.

What structural claim does Aristotle make regarding the duty of a legislator?

The legislator must greatly interest himself in the care and education of the youth.

How does education serve to preserve a specific form of government, such as an oligarchy?

It produces citizens with the manners and character peculiar to that specific government.

Aristotle argues that because every city has one shared 'end' or goal, education should be _____.

One and the same for all (a common care).

Why does Aristotle believe education should be public rather than left to private choice?

Because children belong to the city and their development affects the city's overall health.
