Aristotle and Plato: A Comparison of Perspectives

Feature. Plato (Idealism)

Aristotle (Empiricism)

Ultimate Reality. Exists in eternal, immaterial archetypes called Forms. The physical world is a shadow or copy.

Exists in **Substance**—concrete, individual things (this horse, this tree).

Source of Knowledge. Grasping Forms through reason; the senses are unreliable.

Grounded in sensory experience and careful observation of the world.

Location of Form. Exists in a separate, higher realm above the physical world.

Exists within things themselves, not floating above them.

Literary Form. Dialogues: Dramatic, witty, and often inconclusive conversations featuring Socrates.

**Treatises**: Systematic, analytical, and organized by topic, moving from definitions to premises.

The Life of Aristotle (384–322 BC) (1/2)

Early Life

Born in Stagira; son of Nicomachus, a court physician. This medical background likely influenced his passion for natural observation.

The Academy

Spent twenty years (367–347 BC) as a student and teacher under Plato.

The Naturalist

After Plato's death, he traveled and conducted extensive biological research, cataloging animals and dissecting sea creatures.

Tutor to Greatness

In 343 BC, he began tutoring the young Alexander of Macedon (Alexander the Great).

The Lyceum

Founded his own school in Athens in 335 BC. It was known as the **Peripatetic** school because he reportedly walked while teaching in the *peripatos* (covered walkways).

The Life of Aristotle (384–322 BC) (2/2)

Final Years

Following Alexander’s death in 323 BC, Aristotle fled Athens to Chalcis to prevent the city from "sinning twice against philosophy" (referencing Socrates’ execution). He died there in 322 BC.

The Teleological Method

Function (Ergon)

Aristotle asks what a thing does well. An acorn's *telos* is to become an oak; a knife's *telos* is to cut.

Human Telos

The central question of his ethics is identifying the human *telos*, which he defines as happiness (*eudaimonia*) achieved through virtuous activity.

Philosophical Concepts (1/3)

Akrasia

Weakness of will; knowing what is right but acting against it due to desire.

Arete

Virtue or excellence; the quality that allows a thing to perform its function (*ergon*) well.

Autarkeia

Self-sufficiency; a mark of the highest good.

Bios Theoretikos

The contemplative life; the highest form of human activity.

Eudaimonia

Happiness or flourishing; an activity of the soul in accordance with virtue.

Philosophical Concepts (2/3)

Hexis

A stable state or disposition of character acquired through habit.

Logos

Reason, speech, or rational account; the capacity that distinguishes humans from animals.

Megalopsychia

Greatness of soul or proper pride; the mean between vanity and pusillanimity.

Mesotes

The mean; the virtuous middle ground between extremes of excess and deficiency.

Phronesis

Practical wisdom; the ability to deliberate well about what is good in specific situations.

Philosophical Concepts (3/3)

Physis

Nature; the inner principle of growth and development in natural things.

Telos

The end, purpose, or goal of a thing.

Political and Social Terms (1/2)

Dikaiosyne

Justice; the "complete virtue" because it is exercised toward others.

Ekklesia

The assembly of citizens; the primary deliberative body in Athens.

Nomos

Law or convention; established by human agreement rather than nature (*physis*).

Oikos

The household; the basic social unit consisting of family, property, and slaves.

Polis

The city-state; the highest form of natural community, existing for the sake of the "good life."

Political and Social Terms (2/2)

Politikon Animal

"Political animal"; the idea that human nature is only fulfilled in a community governed by law.

Schole

Leisure; time set apart for activities pursued for their own sake, such as philosophy.

Stasis

Civil strife or revolution; often caused by perceived injustice in the distribution of power.

Constitutions and Governance (1/2)

Aristocracy

Rule by the virtuous few for the common good.

Democracy

In the Aristotelian sense, a deviant form where the poor majority rules in its own interest.

Monarchy

Rule by a single virtuous person for the common good.

Oligarchy

A deviant form where the wealthy few rule for their own benefit.

Polity (Politeia)

A constitutional blend of oligarchy and democracy, usually favoring the middle class; the best practical state.

Constitutions and Governance (2/2)

Tyranny

Rule by one person in their own interest; the worst deviant form of government.

Thomistic Terms (Aquinas)

Cardinal Virtues

Prudence, Justice, Fortitude, and Temperance.

Eternal Law

God’s rational governance of all creation.

Natural Law

The rational creature's participation in the eternal law, discoverable through reason.

Respondeo

"I answer that"; the core of Aquinas's arguments in the *Summa Theologica*.

Sed Contra

"On the contrary"; the section where Aquinas cites an authority (like "The Philosopher") to signal his direction.
