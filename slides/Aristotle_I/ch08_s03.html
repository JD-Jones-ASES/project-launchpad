Aristotle’s Ethics and Politics: A Comprehensive Study Guide

The Ethics: Happiness and Individual Virtue (1/2)

Eudaimonia (Happiness)

Happiness is defined not as a fleeting emotion or a passive state of contentment, but as an activity of the soul in accordance with virtue. It is "living and acting well."

The Nature of Virtue

The Doctrine of the Mean

Virtue involves finding the balance between two extremes: excess and deficiency. For example, courage is the mean between the deficiency of cowardice and the excess of rashness.

Phronesis (Practical Wisdom)

This intellectual virtue is the capacity for good judgment in specific, practical situations, allowing an individual to identify the "mean" in practice.

Friendship

A critical component of the good life. While friendships of utility and pleasure exist, the "friendship of character"—based on mutual recognition of goodness—is the most enduring and rare.

The Ethics: Happiness and Individual Virtue (2/2)

The Contemplative Life

While the active life of moral virtue is essential, Aristotle identifies the life of the mind (contemplation of truth) as the highest and most divine form of human happiness.

The Politics: Citizenship and the State

The Political Animal

Human beings are naturally social and political. A person who requires no community is considered either a "god or a beast."

The Purpose of the State

The state does not exist merely for protection or trade; it exists to enable the "good life" and human flourishing for its citizens.

Classification of Constitutions

Aristotle categorizes governments based on who rules and whether they rule for the common good or private interest.

Education

The primary responsibility of the state is the education of its citizens. This includes "liberal formation"—cultivating the soul’s capacity for virtue, wisdom, and noble leisure.

Critical Insights on Political Stability

The Best Practical Constitution

The "Polity" is considered the most stable because it is a mixed constitution that draws on the middle class, balancing oligarchic order with democratic participation.

Causes of Revolution

Constitutions fail due to internal corruption: oligarchies fall to greed, democracies to demagogues, and tyrannies to the failure of fear.

The Role of Habit

Because character is formed by habit, the institutions that shape those habits—most importantly, education—are the most vital political entities.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Aristocracy

A correct form of government where a few rule for the common good.

Doctrine of the Mean

The principle that virtue consists of a middle ground between the extremes of excess and deficiency.

Eudaimonia

Often translated as "happiness," it refers to a state of flourishing and "acting well."

Logos

The human capacity for reason and speech.

Noble Leisure

The cultivation of the soul's higher capacities beyond mere labor or utility, supported by education.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Phronesis

Practical wisdom; the intellectual virtue required to apply moral virtues to specific circumstances.

Polis

The Greek city-state; the primary political unit in Aristotle's experience.

Polity

A mixed form of government and the "best practical" constitution, relying on the stability of the middle class.
