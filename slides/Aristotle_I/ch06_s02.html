Aquinas on the Nature and Derivation of Law

Derivation by Conclusion

Opposed to Divine Good

Laws that command acts contrary to divine law (e.g., idolatry). These must never be observed, as one must "obey God rather than man."

Principles of Legal Change

The Cost of Change

Altering a law diminishes its binding power by disrupting the habit of obedience.

Justification for Change

Laws should only be changed if the "common weal" is compensated by a "very great and evident benefit" or in cases of "extreme urgency" where the existing law is clearly unjust or harmful.

Aristotelian Influence on the American Founding

Mixed Government

Aristotle’s "polity" (blending democratic, oligarchic, and aristocratic elements) was reformulated into the American doctrine of separation of powers and checks and balances.

Factions

In Federalist No. 10, James Madison echoes Aristotle’s diagnosis that factions are "sown in the nature of man."

Structural vs. Social Solutions

While Aristotle relied on a large middle class for stability, Madison relied on structural mechanisms (size of the republic, staggered elections) to moderate passions.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Common Weal

The public good or general welfare of the community.

Conscience

The internal "Divine court" where moral obligations are recognized; Aquinas argues just human laws bind here.

Derivation by Conclusion

The process of deriving a specific law as a logical consequence of a general natural principle (e.g., no murder follows from no harm).

Derivation by Determination

The process of particularizing a general natural law principle into a specific rule (e.g., deciding which side of the road to drive on).

Equality of Proportion

The principle that social burdens (taxes, etc.) must be distributed fairly and equitably among citizens based on their relation to the whole.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Factions

Groups of citizens united by a common passion or interest adverse to the rights of others or the permanent interests of the community.

Polity

Aristotle’s term for a stable constitution that balances democratic and aristocratic elements.

Scandal

In a legal/moral context, the disruption or social disorder that might occur if a law—even an unjust one—is publicly defied.

Soft Despotism

Tocqueville’s term for a modern form of state control that is not brutal but diminishes civic energy by making citizens concerned only with private comforts and dependent on an administrative state.

Tyranny of the Majority

A situation in democracy where the majority uses its power to oppress minorities or ignore the common good, treating its will as the ultimate standard of justice.

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to Thomas Aquinas, when does a human law genuinely bind the conscience of a citizen?

When the law is just.

What are the four types of law identified by Thomas Aquinas?

Eternal, natural, human, and divine law.

In Aquinas's view, from what source does every human law derive its authority?

The natural law.

What is the status of a human law that deflects from the law of nature?

It is no longer a law but a perversion of law.

What are the two modes by which a human law can be derived from the natural law?

As a conclusion from premises and by way of determination of generalities.

How is a law derived 'as a conclusion from premises' similar to the sciences?

Demonstrated conclusions are drawn directly from established principles.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Provide an example of a human law derived as a conclusion from the natural law principle 'one should do harm to no man.'

The law stating that 'one must not kill.'

Explain the 'determination of generalities' mode of law derivation using the analogy of a craftsman.

Just as a craftsman determines a general house form into a particular shape, a legislator determines general natural principles into specific rules.

How does the derivation of the law that 'an evil-doer should be punished' differ from the determination of 'how' he is punished?

The need for punishment is a general principle, while the specific method is a human determination.

Which mode of law derivation results in human laws that retain force from the natural law itself?

Derivation as a conclusion from premises.

Laws derived by way of _____ have no other force than that of human law.

Determination

Why does a law against murder have a deeper moral gravity than a parking regulation in Aquinas's theory?

Laws against murder are derived as conclusions from natural law, whereas parking regulations are mere determinations.
