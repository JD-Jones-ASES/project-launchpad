The Aquinian Framework of Law

Eternal Law

The foundational "plan of government" by which God's wisdom directs all of creation toward its proper ends.

Natural Law

The way rational creatures participate in the eternal law. It is not a written code but an orientation of human reason toward the good.

Human (Temporal) Law

Laws created by human governors that derive their legitimacy and force from natural law. According to Augustine, nothing is just in temporal law unless it is drawn from the eternal law.

Prudence and Governance

The Purpose of Education: Modern Perspectives

John Dewey

View education as "life itself," focusing on democratic citizenship, problem-solving, and adaptability through experience.

E. D. Hirsch

Advocated for "cultural literacy," a shared body of knowledge necessary for communication and participation in a shared society.

Aristotle’s Middle Path

Aristotle prioritizes the formation of character (soul) over both Dewey’s civic utility and Hirsch’s cultural knowledge, though he acknowledges the value in both.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Artes Liberales

The "arts befitting a free person." A curriculum intended for the formation of rational and moral capacities rather than vocational training.

Eternal Law

The rational governance of all creation by God's wisdom; the ultimate plan from which all other laws flow.

Hugh of St. Victor

A 12th-century Scholastic theologian who argued in the Didascalicon that learning restores the damaged rational image of God in humans.

Natural Law

The rational creature's participation in the eternal law; an orientation of reason toward the good that allows humans to discern right conduct.

Phronesis (Prudentia)

Practical wisdom; the master intellectual virtue used to discern the good and choose the correct means to achieve it.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Prudentia Regnativa

"Kingly" or "regnative" prudence; the most complete form of practical wisdom proper to those who govern a city or kingdom.

Quadrivium

The four mathematical arts of the medieval curriculum: arithmetic, geometry, music, and astronomy.

Trivium

The three language arts of the medieval curriculum: grammar, logic, and rhetoric; the foundational arts of discourse.

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to Aquinas, what is the 'eternal law'?

The rational governance of all creation by God's wisdom.

How does Aquinas define 'natural law'?

The rational creature's participation in the eternal law.

What is the relationship between temporal law and eternal law according to Augustine (cited by Aquinas)?

Temporal law is only just and lawful if it is drawn from the eternal law.

Under what condition is the 'fomes' considered to have the nature of law?

When it is viewed as a punishment resulting from Divine justice.

Why is the 'fomes' not considered law in the context of sin?

Because in that respect it denotes a proneness to sin, which is contrary to Divine law.

In Aquinas's framework, what determines the content of a school curriculum?

The nature of humans as rational creatures made for truth, beauty, and goodness.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Term: Phronesis (translated by Aquinas as prudentia)

Definition: The master intellectual virtue of practical wisdom used to discern the good and choose right means to achieve it.

How does phronesis (practical wisdom) differ from theoretical wisdom?

Phronesis is directed at action in particular cases rather than grasping universal truths.

According to Aquinas, what is the orientation of 'genuine' prudence?

It is oriented toward the common good rather than merely private benefit.

Why is the person who only reasons well about private affairs not fully prudent?

They have achieved only a partial form of prudence that lacks orientation toward the community.

Aquinas argues that seeking only one's own good is contrary to which theological virtue?

Charity.

What is 'monastic economy' in the context of medieval prudence?

Prudence concerned with things affecting the good of only one person.
