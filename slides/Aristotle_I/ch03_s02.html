Chapter 3, Section 2

The Evolution of Virtue and Justice from Aristotle to Aquinas and Beyond

Study Slides

The Nature of Virtue (Habitus)

Definition

A deep, reliable orientation of the soul that enables one to act, feel, and choose well.

The Theological Dimension

While Aristotle saw virtue as fitting us for earthly life, Aquinas argues that rightly ordered virtues open humanity toward a final supernatural end (God).

The Mean of Virtue

Real Mean

A quantitative midpoint (like arithmetic).

Rational Mean

A mean determined by "right reason" relative to the individual and the circumstances.

The Extremes

To reason, virtue is an "extreme" of conformity and excellence. To the matter (passions/operations), virtue is a "mean" between excess and deficiency.

Exception

Virtues like justice and prudence have no "excess" because they are defined by what reason requires; one cannot be "too just."

Key Characteristics of Justice

Other-Directed

Unlike other virtues, justice essentially requires a relationship between distinct persons; one cannot be strictly unjust to oneself.

The Will

Justice is not a feeling or sentiment but a formed commitment of the rational appetite (the will).

General vs. Particular

Aquinas maintains the distinction between general justice (virtuous living within a community) and particular justice (rendering specific dues to others).

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Cardo

Latin for "hinge"; the root of "cardinal" virtues.

Efficient Cause

The agent that brings a thing (like infused virtue) into being.

Habitus

The Latin equivalent of the Greek hexis; a stable state of character.

Hexis

A stable disposition to respond well or badly.

Infused Virtue

Virtues (like the theological virtues) given by God's grace rather than through human habituation.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Ius suum cuique tribuere

"To render to each his right"; the classic definition of justice.

Mean of Virtue

The point of conformity with reason, situated between the extremes of excess and deficiency.

Teleological Framework

A system of thought, like Aristotle’s, centered on the "telos" or final end/purpose of a community or individual.

Veil of Ignorance

Rawls’s thought experiment designed to determine fair principles of justice by removing knowledge of one's personal circumstances.

Key Concepts (1/2)

In what decade did Thomas Aquinas begin writing the "Summa Theologica"?

The 1260s.

What concern did some 13th-century theologians have regarding the recovery of Aristotle's works?

They worried pagan philosophy would corrupt Christian doctrine.

How did Aquinas characterize the relationship between faith and the truths Aristotle reached by reason?

Faith completes rather than contradicts reason.

What is the Latin equivalent of Aristotle's $hexis$ used by Aquinas?

$Habitus$.

Concept: $Hexis$

Definition: A stable state or settled disposition of character, rather than a fleeting mood.

According to Aquinas, what distinguishes the ultimate end of human life from Aristotle's view?

It is a supernatural end that transcends earthly life.

Key Concepts (2/2)

In the definition of virtue provided by Augustine and Aquinas, what is the "genus" of virtue?

Quality.

What does Aquinas identify as the "proximate genus" of virtue to improve its definition?

Habit.

Where does the "subject" of virtue reside according to Aquinas's general definition?

In the mind.

What is the "end" or purpose of an operative habit like virtue?

Operation.

How does virtue differ from habits like "opinion" regarding its reference to good or evil?

Virtue is always referred to the good, while opinion can be true or untrue.

In the definition of virtue, what is the significance of the phrase "of which no one makes bad use"?

It distinguishes virtue from habits that are sometimes directed toward evil.
