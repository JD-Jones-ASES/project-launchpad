Aristotle’s Ethics: The Path to Eudaimonia

Study Slides

Aristotle’s Method: Dialectical Analysis

Treatment of Opinion

Aristotle treats common beliefs as data containing genuine insight.

Refinement

His goal is to scrutinize, clarify, and refine these intuitions to identify what is correct and correct what is confused.

Connection to Experience

This method ensures ethics remains grounded in ordinary human experience rather than being detached or purely theoretical.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Arete

Virtue or excellence; the quality that allows a thing to perform its function well.

Endoxa

Reputable or received opinions; the starting point of Aristotle’s dialectical method.

Ergon

Function or task; the characteristic activity of a thing (e.g., the ergon of a knife is cutting).

Eudaimonia

Often translated as "happiness," but more accurately meaning "flourishing" or "living well" in an objective, stable sense.

Hedone

Pleasure, specifically sensory pleasure.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Hexis

A stable disposition or settled state of character.

Phronesis

Practical wisdom; the ability to discern the mean and act rightly in specific situations.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the Greek term for the "reputable opinions" that Aristotle uses as the starting point for his ethical analysis?

$endoxa$

How does Aristotle's method of starting with common opinion differ from a mathematician's approach?

He begins with received opinions rather than deducing from a first principle.

Aristotle treats common opinions as _____ rather than mere prejudice.

data

What is the primary goal of Aristotle's "dialectical" approach in ethics?

To identify what is correct in ordinary intuitions and correct what is confused.

According to Aristotle, what is the "highest good" that everyone agrees upon?

Happiness

Aristotle defines happiness as the "activity of soul in accordance with _____."

virtue

Key Concepts (2/2)

Concept: The Function Argument (Step 1)

Definition: Every craft, art, and profession has a characteristic function ($ergon$), or a purpose for which it exists.

In the function argument, what does it mean for a thing to be "good"?

It means performing its characteristic function ($ergon$) excellently.

Why does Aristotle conclude that the human function cannot be "mere life" (growth and nutrition)?

Because plants share this function, and the human function must be distinctive to humans.

Why does Aristotle exclude "sensation" as the characteristic function of human beings?

Because sensation is a function shared with animals.

What is the distinctive function of human beings according to the function argument?

The activity of the soul in accordance with reason.

The Greek term for excellence or virtue is _____.

$arete$
