The Root 'Jus'

Derived from the Latin noun *jus* (genitive *juris*), meaning "law" or "right." This root is the source of many English words related to law, fairness, and legal authority.

The Root 'Judic'

Originating from the Latin *judex* ("a judge"), which itself is a combination of *jus* ("law") and *dicere* ("to say"). This root primarily relates to the act of judging, courts, and judges.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Jurisprudence

The philosophy or science of law, combining *juris* (of law) with *prudentia* (wisdom, foresight). This term reflects the deep intellectual engagement required for legal understanding.

Important Terms (1/3)

Jus

(Latin) Meaning "law" or "right"; the fundamental Latin root for many legal terms.

Twelve Tables

The first codified (written) laws of the Roman Republic, publicly displayed in the Roman Forum around 450 BCE.

Justice

The quality of being just, fair, and righteous; derived from Latin *justitia*.

Jury

A group of citizens sworn (*jurare*, "to swear") to honestly judge the facts in a legal case.

Jurisdiction

The official power or authority to "declare the law" (*jus* + *dicere*) within a particular territory or over specific types of legal cases.

Important Terms (2/3)

Judex

(Latin) Meaning "a judge"; derived from *jus* (law) + *dicere* (to say).

Judicial

Relating to courts of law, judges, or the administration of justice.

Adjudicate

To make a formal judgment or decision about a problem or disputed matter, typically in a legal context.

Perjury

The criminal offense of lying under oath or making a false statement in a legal proceeding. The prefix *per-* here denotes something done wrongly or to destruction.

Prejudice

A preconceived opinion or judgment made without adequate knowledge, thought, or reason; derived from *pre-* (before) + *judicium* (judgment).

Important Terms (3/3)

Jurisprudence

The theory or philosophy of law; literally "wisdom about the law" (*juris* + *prudentia*).

The Enduring Influence of the Root `Jus`

Justice

This fundamental concept comes from *justitia*, meaning "the quality of being just or fair." It encapsulates the ideal that law aims to achieve.

Jury

A cornerstone of many legal systems, a jury consists of citizens who take an oath (*jurare*, "to swear") to judge a case honestly and impartially.

Jurisdiction

This term refers to the authority to "declare the law" within a specific territory or area of responsibility. It combines *jus* (law) with *dicere* (to say or declare).

The Related Root `Judic`: Understanding the Role of a Judge

Composition of `Judex`

Interestingly, *judex* itself is built from *jus* (law) + *dicere* (to say). This etymology reveals the core function of a judge: literally, "one who says what the law is."

Judicial

This adjective describes anything pertaining to courts, judges, or the administration of justice. For example, the "judicial system" refers to the network of courts and legal processes.

Adjudicate

To "adjudicate" means to formally "judge toward" a decision, settling a dispute officially and legally. It implies a formal process of evaluation and resolution by an authority.
