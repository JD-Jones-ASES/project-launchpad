The Latin Root *portare*

The fundamental Latin verb *portare*, meaning "to carry," formed the basis for an entire vocabulary related to movement and conveyance.

Etymological Development in Latin

Romans built upon *portare* to create specific terms for various types of movement, such as goods "carried in" (*importata*), "carried out" (*exportata*), individuals "carried away" (*deported*), a...

Preservation in English

The original sense of "carrying or moving" from the root *port* is remarkably preserved in dozens of modern English words, making their meanings intuitively understood even centuries later.

Everyday Etymology

Many common English words, from "portable" to "report," directly reflect their Latin origins, demonstrating a continuous linguistic lineage from ancient Rome to contemporary usage.

Important Terms (1/2)

portare

(Latin verb) Meaning "to carry." The foundational root discussed in the section.

transport

(English verb) To carry or move something from one place to another. Derived from Latin *trans-* (across) + *portare* (to carry).

imported

(English adjective/past participle) Goods or products "carried in" from another country or region for sale. From Latin *importata*.

exported

(English adjective/past participle) Goods or products "carried out" or sent to another country for sale. From Latin *exportata*.

deported

(English verb) To "carry away" or expel someone from a country or place, often as punishment.

Important Terms (2/2)

portus (ports)

(Latin noun, pluralized as English "ports") Places, typically harbors, where ships load and unload cargo; places where things are "carried to and from."

portable

(English adjective) Capable of being easily carried or moved. Combines *portare* with the suffix *-able*.

portfolio

(English noun) A flat case for carrying loose papers. Historically, from *portare* (to carry) + *folium* (leaf or sheet).

report

(English verb/noun) To "carry back" information, deliver news, or provide an account of something.
