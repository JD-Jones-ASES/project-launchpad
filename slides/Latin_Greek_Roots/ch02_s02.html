Words as Authority in Ancient Rome

In ancient Rome, words, particularly official decrees (`edictum`), held significant power. A magistrate's words were binding law, illustrating how "saying something officially was an act of power."

Evolution of Meanings

Many words derived from `dict` retain their original Latin sense of "saying" or "telling" but have evolved to include nuances of authority, accusation, or style, reflecting their Roman origins.

Important Terms (1/2)

`dicere`

The Latin verb meaning "to say" or "to tell," the origin of the root `dict`.

`edictum`

An official decree or pronouncement in ancient Rome, from which the word `edict` is derived, signifying a binding order.

Dictate

(verb) To speak words aloud for someone else to write down; to command or order.

Dictator

(noun) Originally, "one who speaks with authority"; a ruler with absolute power, typically for a limited time in ancient Rome, now generally meaning an absolute ruler.

Predict

(verb) To "say before" an event happens; to forecast or prophesy.

Important Terms (2/2)

Verdict

(noun) Literally something "truly said" (`vere + dictum`); the final decision of a jury in a legal case.

Edict

(noun) An official order or proclamation issued by a person in authority, literally "spoken out."

Contradict

(verb) To "speak against" someone or something; to deny the truth of a statement.

Diction

(noun) The choice and style of words a speaker or writer uses; how something is said.

Indict

(verb) To formally accuse someone of a crime, tracing back to "declare against" a person.
