Chapter 6 — SENS/SENT (To Feel and Perceive)

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Root meaning and origin

"sens" comes from the Latin past participle sensus; "sent" comes from the verb stem sentire (to feel, perceive, judge)

How the root appears

As "sens" in words relating to physical perception (sense, sensory, sensitive) and as "sent" in words relating to opinion and agreement (consent, dissent, assent, sentiment, sentence)

Important morpheme patterns

The prefixes con- (together), dis- (apart), and as-/ad- (toward) create a trio of agreement/disagreement words

Important Terms (1/2)

sense

a faculty of perception; also good judgment (sens)

sensory

relating to the senses or sensation (sens + -ory)

sensitive

easily affected by feelings or physical stimuli (sens + -itive)

consent

to agree; to feel together with another person (con- + sent)

dissent

to disagree; to hold a different opinion (dis- + sent)

Important Terms (2/2)

assent

to agree or express approval (as-/ad- + sent)

sentiment

a feeling, opinion, or attitude (sent + -ment)

sentence

a complete thought in words; OR a judge's declared punishment (from Latin sententia)

Word Family Analysis: Two Clusters (1/2)

• sense: any of the five faculties of physical perception; also "good sense" (judgment)

• sensory: relating to the physical senses (-ory = relating to)

• sensitive: easily moved by physical or emotional stimuli (-itive = having the quality of)

• consent (con- = together): to feel together; to agree

• dissent (dis- = apart): to feel apart; to disagree

• assent (as-/ad- = toward): to feel toward; to nod in agreement

Word Family Analysis: Two Clusters (2/2)

• sentiment: the result of feeling; an emotional opinion

The Most Surprising Word: Sentence

Grammar

A grammatical sentence is a complete expression of thought — a "finished judgment" in words

Law

A judge's sentence is the opinion she pronounces after weighing the evidence

Agreement Words: A Prefix Pattern

con-

(together) + sent = consent (agree)

dis-

(apart) + sent = dissent (disagree)

as-/ad-

(toward) + sent = assent (approve)

Key Concepts (1/2)

What does the root 'sens / sent' mean?

To feel, to perceive — from Latin 'sentire' (to feel, perceive, judge) and its past participle 'sensus'

What language does the root 'sens/sent' come from?

Latin — from 'sentire' (to feel, perceive, judge); 'sens' from the past participle 'sensus,' 'sent' from the verb stem

sense

sens (feel) + -e — a faculty of perception (sight, hearing, touch, taste, smell); also good judgment

sensory

sens (feel) + -ory (relating to) — relating to the senses or sensation

sensitive

sens (feel) + -itive (having the quality of) — easily affected by feelings or physical stimuli

consent

con- (together) + sent (feel) — to agree; to feel together with another person

Key Concepts (2/2)

dissent

dis- (apart) + sent (feel) — to disagree; to hold a different opinion from others

assent

as-/ad- (toward) + sent (feel) — to agree or express approval; to feel toward something

sentiment

sent (feel) + -i- + -ment (result of) — a feeling, opinion, or attitude

sentence

sent (feel) + -ence (state of) — a complete thought in words; OR a judge's declared punishment. Both come from Latin 'sententia' (considered opinion).

What does the prefix 'dis-' mean in 'dissent'?

Apart — 'dissent' means to feel apart from the group; to hold a different opinion

What does the prefix 'con-' mean in 'consent'?

Together — 'consent' means to feel together; to share the same judgment as another person
