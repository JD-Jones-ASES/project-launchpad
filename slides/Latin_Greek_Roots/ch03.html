Chapter 3 — AUD (To Hear and Listen)

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Root meaning and origin

"aud" comes from Latin *audire* (to hear); *audit* is the past-participle form of the same root

How the root appears

As "aud" in most words (audible, audience, inaudible) and as "audit" in words derived from the past participle (auditorium, audition, audit)

Important morpheme patterns

The suffix *-ible* means "able to be" (audible = able to be heard); *-orium* marks a place (auditorium = a place for hearing); *in-* negates (inaudible = not able to be heard)

Important Terms (1/2)

audience

a group of listeners or spectators (*aud* + *-ience*)

auditorium

a large hall designed for an audience to hear (*audit* + *-orium*)

audible

loud enough to be heard (*aud* + *-ible*)

audit

an official examination of records; originally a formal hearing of accounts (*audit*)

audition

a trial performance to demonstrate talent (*audit* + *-ion*)

Important Terms (2/2)

inaudible

too quiet to be heard (*in-* + *aud* + *-ible*)

Word Family Analysis

audience

the group gathered to hear something

audible / inaudible

whether a sound can or cannot be heard (the prefix *in-* negates the root)

auditorium

the physical space built for hearing (*-orium* = place for)

audition

a trial performance evaluated through listening (*-ion* = act of)

audit

a formal hearing or review (*-it* = Latin past participle form)

Suffix Focus: -ible and -orium

-ible

means "able to be": *audible* = able to be heard; *inaudible* = not able to be heard (also in Ch. 1: *vociferous* uses *-ous*)

-orium

means "a place for": *auditorium* = a place for hearing. You will see this suffix again in words like *emporium* (a place for trading).
