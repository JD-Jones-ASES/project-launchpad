Key Concepts

Definition of a Word Family

A collection of words derived from the same base or root word through the addition of prefixes (at the beginning) and/or suffixes (at the end). These words often share a core meaning, though their ...

Core Meaning Recognition

The ability to identify the central meaning of a root word is crucial for understanding all words in its family. This skill aids in deciphering unfamiliar vocabulary.

Vocabulary Expansion

Learning word families is an efficient strategy for vocabulary building. Instead of memorizing individual words, students can learn a root and immediately gain access to a cluster of related terms.

Impact on Reading and Writing

A strong grasp of word families improves reading comprehension by enabling readers to quickly understand complex words. In writing, it allows for greater precision and variety in word choice.

Important Terms (1/2)

Word Family

A group of words that share a common root or base word and are related in meaning and often in form. Examples: *write, writer, rewrite, unwritten, writing*.

Root Word (or Base Word)

The primary part of a word that carries its essential meaning and cannot be broken down into smaller meaningful units. It is the core around which a word family is built. Examples: *write, act, for...

Prefix

A morpheme added to the beginning of a root word to create a new word with a modified meaning. Examples: *un-* (unhappy), *re-* (rewrite), *pre-* (preview).

Suffix

A morpheme added to the end of a root word to create a new word, often changing its grammatical function (e.g., from verb to noun) or slightly altering its meaning. Examples: *-er* (worker), *-tion...

Morpheme

The smallest meaningful unit in a language. Words are composed of one or more morphemes (e.g., *un-*, *break*, *-able* are three morphemes in "unbreakable").

Important Terms (2/2)

Derivational Morpheme

An affix (prefix or suffix) that creates a new word with a new meaning or changes the grammatical category of the base word (e.g., *teach* (verb) + *-er* (suffix) = *teacher* (noun)).

Prefixes: Modifying Meaning

Negation

*un-* (unhappy), *dis-* (disagree), *in-/im-/il-/ir-* (inactive, impossible, illegal, irregular).

Direction/Position

*re-* (return), *pre-* (preview), *post-* (postpone), *sub-* (submarine), *super-* (supervise).

Time

*pre-* (precede), *post-* (postdate).

Quantity

*uni-* (unicycle), *bi-* (bicycle), *multi-* (multicolor).

Suffixes: Changing Word Function and Nuance

Noun-forming suffixes

*-tion* (action), *-ment* (movement), *-ness* (kindness), *-er/-or* (teacher, actor).

Adjective-forming suffixes

*-able/-ible* (readable, visible), *-ful* (beautiful), *-less* (careless), *-ous* (courageous).

Verb-forming suffixes

*-ize/-ise* (memorize, criticise), *-ify* (beautify), *-en* (widen).

Adverb-forming suffixes

*-ly* (quickly, happily).

Practical Application

Identify Root Words

When learning new vocabulary, try to isolate the base word.

Recognize Affixes

Pay attention to prefixes and suffixes and understand their common meanings or grammatical functions.

Create Word Maps

Visualize word families by drawing a central root and branching out to its related words.

Practice with Context

See how different words from a family are used in sentences to grasp their specific nuances.
