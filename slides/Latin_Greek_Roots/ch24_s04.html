The Mental Lexicon and Word Families

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Mental Lexicon

The brain's internal dictionary, a vast and organized storehouse of all the words a person knows, including their meaning, pronunciation, spelling, and grammatical properties.

Word Family

A group of words that share a common base or root morpheme and are often related in meaning, though not always transparently (e.g., *happy, unhappy, happiness*; *teach, teacher, teaching*).

Morphology

The study of the internal structure of words and how they are formed. It examines how morphemes (the smallest meaningful units of language) combine to create words.

Morpheme

The smallest unit of a language that carries meaning. Morphemes can be free (stand alone as words, like "cat") or bound (must be attached to other morphemes, like "-s" for plural or "un-").

Important Terms (1/3)

Lexical Access

The process of identifying and retrieving a word's information from the mental lexicon.

Morpheme

The smallest unit of language that has meaning or a grammatical function.

Root Morpheme

The core part of a word that carries its primary meaning and to which affixes can be added (e.g., "walk" in *walking*).

Affix

A bound morpheme that is attached to a word stem to form a new word or word form. Affixes include prefixes (e.g., "un-" in *unhappy*) and suffixes (e.g., "-ness" in *happiness*).

Inflectional Morpheme

An affix that changes the grammatical function of a word but not its core meaning or part of speech (e.g., "-s" for plural, "-ed" for past tense).

Important Terms (2/3)

Derivational Morpheme

An affix that creates a new word with a new meaning or changes the word's part of speech (e.g., "-er" turns *teach* into *teacher*, a noun).

Decompositional Model

A theory of morphological processing proposing that complex words are broken down into their constituent morphemes during lexical access (e.g., *unlockable* is processed as *un- + lock + -able*).

Full Listing Hypothesis

A theory suggesting that all forms of a word (e.g., *walk, walks, walked, walking*) are stored as separate, complete entries in the mental lexicon.

Important Terms (3/3)

Lexical Ambiguity

The situation where a single word has multiple meanings (e.g., "bank" can refer to a financial institution or the side of a river).
