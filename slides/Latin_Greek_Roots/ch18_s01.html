Important Terms (1/2)

Epigraph

A short inscription, quotation, or saying at the beginning of a book, chapter, or section, intended to suggest its theme or provide a relevant context.

Latin

An ancient Italic language spoken in the Roman Republic and Empire, the language from which many modern European languages derive.

Gladiator

(from Latin *gladius*, "sword") An armed combatant who entertained audiences in the Roman Republic and Roman Empire by fighting other gladiators, wild animals, or condemned criminals.

Emperor

The sovereign ruler of an empire; in this context, the head of the Roman Empire, possessing immense political, military, and often religious authority.

Morituri te salutant

(Latin) "Those who are about to die salute you." A traditional, albeit debated, gladiatorial salute to the Roman Emperor.

Important Terms (2/2)

Mortality

The state of being subject to death; the inevitability of death.

Sacrifice

The act of giving up something highly valued (often one's life or well-being) for the sake of something else regarded as more important or worthy.

Foreshadowing

A literary device in which a writer gives an advance hint of what is to come later in the story, often used to build suspense or prepare the reader for future events.

Deconstructing "Morituri te salutant"

"Morituri"

This is a future active participle, meaning "those who are about to die." The use of a participle emphasizes the *state* of being on the verge of death, highlighting the imminent and unavoidable na...

"Te"

This is the accusative form of the second-person singular pronoun, meaning "you." It directly addresses the Emperor, signifying a personal acknowledgment of his presence and authority.

"Salutant"

This is the third-person plural present indicative of "salutare," meaning "they salute" or "they greet." The present tense makes the action immediate and ongoing.
