Key Concepts (1/2)

Latin Roots for Death

The roots *mort* (from *mors*, *mortis*) and *mor* (from *mori*) are fundamental to understanding many English words related to death.

Roman Awareness of Death

Roman society had a profound and ever-present awareness of mortality, evidenced by phrases like *Memento mori* ("Remember that you will die") and *Morituri te salutant* ("Those who are about to die...

Life and Death Roots

The *mort/mor* root family forms a natural linguistic pair with *vit/viv* (life), highlighting the human condition as both "mortal beings" seeking "vital experiences."

Prefixes and Meaning

Prefixes like *im-* (not) create antonyms (e.g., *mortal* vs. *immortal*), and *re-* (again) can suggest revival (e.g., *revive*).

Evolution of Word Meanings

Some words derived from *mort/mor* have evolved from their literal meaning of "to put to death" to more figurative senses, such as *mortify* meaning extreme embarrassment rather than actual killing.

Key Concepts (2/2)

The "Death Pledge" of Mortgage

The word *mortgage* has a surprising etymology, literally meaning "death pledge," where the pledge "dies" upon repayment or forfeiture.

Important Terms (1/3)

mors (genitive mortis)

Latin noun meaning "death."

mori

Latin verb meaning "to die."

Memento mori

Latin phrase meaning "Remember that you will die," a reminder of mortality.

Morituri te salutant

Latin phrase meaning "Those who are about to die salute you," famously used by gladiators addressing the emperor.

mortal

Adjective describing any creature that will eventually die; subject to death.

Important Terms (2/3)

immortal

Adjective describing something that will never die or endure forever; not subject to death. Formed by adding the prefix *im-* (not).

mortuary

A place dedicated to the care of the dead; a funeral home.

mortify

In its root sense, to "put to death"; now commonly means to make someone feel extreme humiliation or embarrassment.

moribund

Adjective describing someone or something in a dying state, or nearing an end.

post-mortem

An examination conducted "after death," such as an autopsy. From the Latin *post mortem*.
