Understanding the Latin Root "vis" / "vid"

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Primary Root Forms

The main forms of this root found in English words are **vis** and **vid**.

Core Meaning

Both "vis" and "vid" universally convey the meaning "to see" or concepts related to sight, perception, or appearance.

Latin Origin

The roots stem directly from the Latin verb *videre* ("to see") and its past participle *visus*. This etymological connection provides a clear foundation for their meaning in English.

Variant Form "view"

A notable variant, "view," evolved from *videre* through Old French (*veoir*), demonstrating how roots can adapt and change form across languages while retaining their core meaning.

Vocabulary Building

Recognizing these roots is a powerful tool for deciphering unfamiliar words, as their presence often indicates a connection to sight or visual perception.

Important Terms (1/2)

vis

A Latin root form meaning "to see."

vid

A Latin root form meaning "to see."

*videre

*: The original Latin verb meaning "to see," from which "vis" and "vid" are derived.

*visus

*: The past participle of the Latin verb *videre*, meaning "seen." This form is particularly influential in the development of words containing "vis."

view

A variant form of the root, derived through Old French, also meaning "to see" or "to look at."

Important Terms (2/2)

visible

(adjective) Capable of being seen; perceptible to the eye. (Derived from `vis` + `-ible`)

evidence

(noun) Information or objects that help you see the truth or confirm a fact. (Derived from `e-` + `vid` + `-ence`)

video

(noun) The recording, reproducing, or broadcasting of moving visual images. (Derived from `vid` + `-eo`)

supervise

(verb) To oversee, to watch over, or direct a task or person. (Derived from `super-` + `vis` + `-e`)

Detailed Notes (1/3)

Visible

This word combines "vis" (to see) with the suffix "-ible" (able to be). Thus, something `visible` is "able to be seen." Its opposite, `invisible`, means "not able to be seen."

Vision

Meaning "the faculty or state of being able to see," or "the ability to think about or plan the future with imagination or wisdom." Both senses relate to a form of mental or physical seeing.

Revise

Here, "re-" means "again," and "vise" (from `vis`) means "to see." So, to `revise` is literally "to see again," implying a critical review and correction of something previously written or done.

The prefix "pro-" means "before" or "forward." To `provide` means "to see something before it is needed" and make it available. It implies foreseeing a need and making arrangements.

Related to "provide," `provident` means "making or indicative of timely preparation for the future." It connects to the idea of foresight.
