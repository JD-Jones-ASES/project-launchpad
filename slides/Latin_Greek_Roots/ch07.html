Chapter 7 — COGN/GNOSC (To Know and Recognize)

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Root meaning and origin

"cogn" from Latin cognoscere (to get to know, recognize); "gnosc/gnos" from Greek gignoskein (to know, perceive); both share a common Proto-Indo-European ancestor

How the root appears

"cogn" in everyday words (cognition, recognize, cognizant, cognitive, incognito); "gnos" in scientific/medical words (diagnose, prognosis)

Important morpheme patterns

Prefixes again drive distinct meanings — re- (again), in- (not), dia- (through), pro- (before/ahead)

Important Terms (1/2)

cognition

the mental process of knowing, thinking, and understanding (cogn + -ition)

recognize

to know again; to identify something previously known (re- + cogn + -ize)

cognizant

aware; having knowledge of something (cogn + -izant)

incognito

with one's identity hidden (in- + cognit + -o)

cognitive

relating to the process of thinking and knowing (cogn + -itive)

Important Terms (2/2)

diagnose

to identify a disease by examining symptoms thoroughly (dia- + gnos)

prognosis

a prediction about the likely course of a disease (pro- + gnos + -is)

The Latin Side: Everyday Knowing

cognition

the overall mental process of knowing — used in psychology and education

recognize

to know again — reconnecting a present perception to a past memory

cognizant

aware; in a state of knowing — "I am cognizant of the risks"

cognitive

relating to thinking processes — "cognitive skills," "cognitive science"

incognito

the prefix in- (not) + cognit (known) = unknown; with identity hidden. The word entered English through Italian, which preserved the full Latin form incognitus.

The Greek Side: Scientific Knowing

diagnose

dia- (through) + gnos (know) — to know through; a doctor reasons through all symptoms to name a disease

prognosis

pro- (before, ahead) + gnos (know) — knowing ahead; a prediction about how illness will develop

Diagnosis

= What is wrong NOW (looking through present evidence)

Prognosis

= What will happen NEXT (looking ahead)

Key Concepts (1/2)

What does the root 'cogn' mean?

To know, to recognize — from Latin 'cognoscere' (to get to know, recognize)

What does the root 'gnosc / gnos' mean?

To know, to perceive — from Greek 'gignoskein' (to know, perceive)

What is the origin of the Latin root 'cogn'?

Latin 'cognoscere' = co- (together, thoroughly) + gnoscere (to know). Related to Greek 'gignoskein' through a shared Indo-European ancestor.

cognition

cogn (know) + -ition (process of) — the mental process of knowing, thinking, and understanding

recognize

re- (again) + cogn (know) + -ize (to make) — to know again; to identify someone or something previously known

cognizant

cogn (know) + -izant (being in a state of) — aware; having knowledge of something

Key Concepts (2/2)

incognito

in- (not) + cognit (known) + -o (Italian ending) — with one's identity hidden; traveling as an unknown person

cognitive

cogn (know) + -itive (relating to) — relating to the process of thinking and knowing

diagnose

dia- (through) + gnos (know) + -e — to identify a disease or problem by examining and reasoning through symptoms

prognosis

pro- (before, ahead) + gnos (know) + -is — a prediction about the likely course of a disease or situation

What does the Greek prefix 'dia-' mean in 'diagnose'?

Through — a diagnosis is 'knowing through' the symptoms to identify what is wrong

What does the prefix 'pro-' mean in 'prognosis'?

Before or ahead — a prognosis is 'knowing before'; a prediction about the future course of illness
