Chapter 1, Section 2

Understanding the Power of the Voice – The Root "voc"

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Latin Root "voc"

The fundamental origin of many English words, stemming from the Latin `vocare` (to call) and `vox` (voice), emphasizing the act of speaking or being called.

The Power of the Human Voice

The central theme connecting all "voc" words, highlighting voice as a tool for influence, persuasion, and identity, as understood in ancient Rome and still relevant today.

Advocacy and Support

Words like `advocate` directly reflect the act of speaking on behalf of another, originating from the legal practice in Roman courts.

Life's Purpose or "Calling"

The term `vocation` captures the idea of a deeply felt summons to a particular type of work or life path.

Formal Appeal and Authority

`Invoke` signifies calling upon something (like a rule or a deity) for support, while `revoke` means to call back or withdraw a privilege or permission.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Emotional and Reactive Language

Words such as `vociferous`, `provoke`, and `evoke` illustrate the voice's capacity to express strong emotions, elicit reactions, or bring forth memories and feelings.

Importance of Etymological Verification

The example of `avocado` as a `false cognate` serves as a crucial reminder to carefully investigate word origins rather than relying on superficial similarities.

Important Terms (1/3)

vocare

(Latin) Meaning "to call." The primary root for many English words discussed.

vox

(Latin) Meaning "voice." Another foundational Latin term contributing to the "voc" family.

vocal

(Adjective) Pertaining to the voice; expressed or produced by the voice.

advocate

(Noun/Verb) A person who publicly supports or recommends a particular cause or policy; to publicly recommend or support. (Literally, "called toward" another's cause).

vocation

(Noun) A strong feeling of suitability for a particular career or occupation; a person's "calling" in life.

Important Terms (2/3)

invoke

(Verb) To call on (a deity or spirit) in prayer, as a witness, or for inspiration; to cite or appeal to (a law, rule, or authority) in support of one's actions.

vociferous

(Adjective) (Especially of a person or speech) vehement or clamorous; expressing feelings or opinions in a loud and forceful way. (Literally, "carrying its voice" loudly).

provoke

(Verb) To stimulate or give rise to (a reaction or emotion, typically a strong or unwelcome one) in someone. (Literally, "to call forth").

evoke

(Verb) To bring or recall to the conscious mind (a feeling, memory, or image). (Literally, "to call out").

revoke

(Verb) To put an end to the validity or operation of (a law, right, or agreement); to cancel or officially withdraw. (Literally, "to call back").

Important Terms (3/3)

aguacate

(Spanish) The Spanish word for avocado, derived from Nahuatl.

ahuacatl

(Nahuatl/Aztec) The original word for avocado from which the Spanish `aguacate` and subsequently English `avocado` are derived.

False Cognate

A word that appears to be related to another word (often in a different language) due to similar spelling or pronunciation, but actually has a different etymological origin.

The Root Story: The Ancient Roman Context

`vocare`

The Latin word meaning "to call," referring to the act of raising one's voice, especially in a public or formal setting like a courtroom.

`vox`

The Latin word for "voice."

Basic Descendants of "voc"

`vocal`

Simply describes anything related to the voice, such as vocal cords, vocal music, or being vocal about an opinion.
