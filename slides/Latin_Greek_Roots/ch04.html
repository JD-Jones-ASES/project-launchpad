Chapter 4 — SCRIB/SCRIPT (To Write)

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Root meaning and origin

"scrib" comes from Latin *scribere* (to write); "script" comes from the past participle *scriptum* (written)

How the root appears

As "scrib" in action-oriented words (describe, inscribe, prescribe) and as "script" in noun/result-oriented words (manuscript, transcript, postscript, scripture)

Important morpheme patterns

Prefixes change the direction or timing of the writing — *de-* (about), *in-* (into), *pre-* (before), *post-* (after), *trans-* (across)

Important Terms (1/2)

describe

to write about; to give a detailed account (*de-* + *scrib*)

manuscript

a document written by hand (*manu* + *script*)

inscribe

to write or carve words into a surface (*in-* + *scrib*)

prescribe

to write instructions in advance; especially medical orders (*pre-* + *scrib*)

postscript

an addition written after the main body of a letter (*post-* + *script*)

Important Terms (2/2)

scripture

sacred or holy writings (*script* + *-ure*)

transcript

a written copy of spoken or recorded words (*trans-* + *script*)

scribe

a person who copies or writes documents by hand (*scrib*)

Word Family Analysis (1/2)

scrib

(from the verb stem *scribere*): used in action words — *describe*, *inscribe*, *prescribe*

script

(from the past participle *scriptum*): used in result/noun words — *manuscript*, *transcript*, *postscript*, *scripture*

de-

(down/about): *describe* = to write about something in detail

in-

(into/on): *inscribe* = to write into or onto a surface

pre-

(before): *prescribe* = to write instructions before use

Word Family Analysis (2/2)

post-

(after): *postscript* = to add writing after the main text

trans-

(across): *transcript* = to carry speech across into written form
