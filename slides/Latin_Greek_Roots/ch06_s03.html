Exploring the Latin Root `sens/sent`

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Latin Root Influence

Many English words are derived from Latin roots, and understanding these roots helps decode the meaning of complex vocabulary.

The Root `sens/sent`

This specific root has two primary forms: `sens` and `sent`.

Core Meaning

Both `sens` and `sent` fundamentally mean "to feel" or "to perceive." This encompasses both physical sensations and mental awareness or judgment.

Latin Origin

The root originates from the Latin verb *sentire* (meaning "to feel, perceive, judge") and its past participle *sensus*.

Variant Forms

The two forms, `sens` and `sent`, arise from different grammatical parts of the original Latin word: `sens` from the past participle *sensus*, and `sent` directly from the verb *sentire*.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Derivative Words

Numerous English words, such as *sense*, *sentence*, *consent*, and *sentiment*, are direct descendants of this root, each carrying an aspect of its core meaning.

Important Terms (1/2)

Root

The basic part of a word that carries its primary meaning. It is often derived from an older language, such as Latin or Greek, and can be combined with prefixes and suffixes to form new words.

Etymology

The study of the origin of words and how their meanings have changed throughout history.

Derivative

A word formed from another word or root, often by adding prefixes or suffixes.

Variant Forms

Different spellings or forms of the same root that carry the same or a very similar meaning, often arising from different grammatical structures in the original language.

Latin

An ancient Italic language spoken in the Roman Republic and Empire, which has had a profound influence on the English language and many other European languages.

Important Terms (2/2)

Past Participle

A non-finite verb form that often functions as an adjective or part of a perfect verb tense. In Latin, the past participle was a key source for nouns and adjectives, and thus for English roots.

The Core Meaning: "To Feel, To Perceive"

"To Feel"

This can refer to physical sensations (like touch, taste, smell, hearing, sight), emotional states, or even an intuitive understanding.

"To Perceive"

This involves becoming aware of something through the senses or the intellect, to apprehend or understand. It also extends to forming an opinion or judgment.

Latin Origin: *sentire* and *sensus*

To feel

As in experiencing a sensation or emotion.

To perceive

As in becoming aware of something through the senses or intellect.

To judge

As in forming an opinion or making a decision based on perception.

Exploring Derivative Words (2/2)

Sensitive

(from `sens`) Quick to perceive or feel; easily affected by external influences.

Dissent

(from `sent`) To differ in opinion or feeling; to disagree. (Prefix `dis-` means "apart" or "away").

Resent

(from `sent`) To feel bitterness or indignation at (a circumstance, action, or person). (Prefix `re-` here can mean "again" or "back").

Presentiment

(from `sent`) An intuitive feeling about the future, especially one of foreboding. (Prefix `pre-` means "before").

Sentient

(from `sent`) Able to perceive or feel things. Often used to describe living beings with consciousness.
