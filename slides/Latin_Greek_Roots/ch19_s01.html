Key Concepts

Definition of Justice

Justice is explicitly defined as an active and sustained commitment, not merely a passive state or a fleeting emotion. It requires a conscious and ongoing *will* to act.

Active vs. Passive Justice

By defining justice as a "will to render," Justinian emphasizes an active, deliberative, and executive component, rather than a mere theoretical understanding or passive observation of fairness.

Important Terms (1/2)

Epigraph

A short quotation or saying at the beginning of a book, chapter, or document, intended to suggest its theme.

Justitia

Latin for "justice." In this context, it refers to the moral principle of fairness and righteousness, particularly as applied in law.

Constans et perpetua voluntas

Latin for "constant and perpetual will." This phrase highlights the unwavering and enduring commitment required for justice.

Jus suum cuique tribuendi

Justinian (Flavius Petrus Sabbatius Iustinianus Augustus, c. 482 – 565 AD)

A Byzantine (Eastern Roman) Emperor who reigned from 527 to 565 AD. He is most famous for his monumental legal reforms, particularly the codification of Roman law.
