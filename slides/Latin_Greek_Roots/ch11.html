Chapter 11 — CED / CEDE / CESS

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Root meaning

ced/cede/cess = to go, to yield (Latin *cedere*)

Three spelling variants

ced (some compounds), cede (verb endings), cess (from past participle *cessus*)

Double meaning

The root captures both moving forward/back AND giving way/surrendering — two types of motion

Prefix patterns

Direction is controlled by prefixes: pro- (forward), re- (back), ex- (beyond), con- (together/intensifying), ac-/ad- (toward), suc- (after)

Important Terms (1/2)

proceed

to go forward; to continue (pro- + ceed)

recede

to go back; to move away or withdraw (re- + cede)

concede

to admit something is true; to surrender a point (con- + cede)

succeed

to achieve a goal; to come after and accomplish (suc- + ceed)

exceed

to go beyond a limit; to surpass (ex- + ceed)

Important Terms (2/2)

access

the ability to approach or enter; a way in (ac-/ad- + cess)

predecessor

a person who held a position before the current holder (prae- + de- + cess + -or)

process

a series of steps leading to a result; a going forward (pro- + cess)

The Two Forms: -cede and -cess

-cede / -ceed

words (from the present stem): proceed, recede, concede, succeed, exceed

-cess

words (from the past participle *cessus*): access, process, predecessor, recess, excess

The Prefix as a Compass (1/2)

pro-ceed

go *forward*

re-cede

go *back*

ex-ceed

go *beyond*

ac-cess

go *toward*

suc-ceed

go *after* (achieve what comes next)

The Prefix as a Compass (2/2)

con-cede

yield *completely*

Predecessor vs. Successor

Predecessor

= prae- (before) + cess (go) → went before you

Successor

= suc- (after) + cess (go) → comes after you
