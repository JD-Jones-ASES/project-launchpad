Chapter 16, Section 3 - Root Profile

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Root Functions

Roots primarily anchor the plant, absorb water and dissolved minerals from the soil, and often serve as storage organs for carbohydrates.

Root Cap

A protective thimble-like layer of cells covering the root tip, shielding the delicate apical meristem as the root grows through the soil.

Apical Meristem (Zone of Cell Division)

Located just behind the root cap, this region contains rapidly dividing cells (mitosis) that produce new cells for root growth.

Zone of Elongation

Cells in this region increase significantly in length, pushing the root tip deeper into the soil.

Zone of Maturation (Differentiation)

Cells here fully differentiate into specialized tissues like epidermis (with root hairs), cortex, and vascular cylinder, becoming functionally mature.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Root Hairs

Tiny, hair-like extensions of epidermal cells in the zone of maturation, greatly increasing the surface area for water and mineral absorption.

Internal Root Tissues

Roots are composed of the epidermis (outer protective layer), cortex (ground tissue for storage), and the vascular cylinder or stele (containing xylem and phloem for transport).

Endodermis and Casparian Strip

The innermost layer of the cortex, the endodermis, has a waxy barrier called the Casparian strip, which regulates the passage of water and solutes into the vascular cylinder.

Vascular Cylinder (Stele)

The central part of the root, containing the xylem (transports water and minerals upwards) and phloem (transports sugars from leaves downwards) and the pericycle (involved in lateral root formation).

Important Terms (1/4)

Root Profile

The characteristic structure and organization of a plant root, including its external morphology and internal tissue arrangement.

Apical Meristem

A region of undifferentiated cells at the tip of a root (or shoot) where cell division occurs, leading to primary growth.

Root Cap

A protective layer of parenchyma cells that covers the apical meristem of a root.

Zone of Cell Division

The region of the root tip where meristematic cells divide rapidly.

Zone of Elongation

The region of the root where newly formed cells increase in length, pushing the root tip forward.

Important Terms (2/4)

Zone of Maturation (Differentiation)

The region where cells complete their specialization and become functionally mature, including the development of root hairs.

Root Hair

A fine, tubular outgrowth from an epidermal cell of a root, vastly increasing the surface area for absorption.

Epidermis

The outermost protective layer of cells of the root, which helps in absorption.

Cortex

The tissue layer located between the epidermis and the vascular cylinder, primarily involved in storage (e.g., starch).

Endodermis

The innermost layer of the cortex, surrounding the vascular cylinder, equipped with the Casparian strip.

Important Terms (3/4)

Casparian Strip

A waxy, waterproof band in the cell walls of endodermal cells that forces water and solutes to pass through the endodermal cells' plasma membranes, regulating entry into the vascular tissue.

Vascular Cylinder (Stele)

The central core of the root, containing the vascular tissues (xylem and phloem).

Xylem

Plant vascular tissue that transports water and dissolved minerals from the roots to the rest of the plant.

Phloem

Plant vascular tissue that transports sugars (produced during photosynthesis) from leaves to other parts of the plant, including roots.

Pericycle

The outermost layer of the vascular cylinder, from which lateral roots originate.

Important Terms (4/4)

Primary Root

The first root that emerges from a germinating seed.

Lateral Root (Branch Root)

Roots that branch off from the primary root, increasing the root system's reach.

Introduction to Roots

Anchorage

Firmly anchoring the plant to the substrate, providing stability against wind and water.

Absorption

Absorbing water and dissolved mineral nutrients from the soil. This is the primary function facilitated by a large surface area.

Storage

Many roots store carbohydrates (sugars) produced during photosynthesis, as well as water. Examples include carrots, beets, and sweet potatoes.

Synthesis

Roots can also synthesize certain hormones and secondary metabolites.

Types of Root Systems (Brief Mention)

Taproot System

Consists of a single, large primary root (taproot) from which smaller lateral roots branch off. Common in dicots (e.g., carrots, dandelions). Good for deep soil penetration.

Fibrous Root System

Consists of a dense network of slender roots of similar size, arising from the stem base. Common in monocots (e.g., grasses). Excellent for stabilizing soil and absorbing surface water.
