Word Families - The Root 'bio-'

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Word Families

A group of words that share a common root word and are related in meaning. They often have different prefixes and suffixes but stem from the same core concept.

Root Word

The fundamental part of a word that carries its primary meaning. Roots cannot stand alone as complete words but form the basis for many related words.

The Root 'bio-'

Derived from the ancient Greek word "bios," meaning "life" or "way of living." This root is incredibly common in scientific and everyday English vocabulary.

Vocabulary Expansion

Learning common root words and their associated word families is a powerful strategy for understanding new words and building a robust vocabulary.

Important Terms (1/3)

Word Family

A collection of words that share the same base or root word, often with different prefixes and suffixes.

Root (Word Root)

The core part of a word that contains its most basic meaning. Examples: *bio*, *aqua*, *graph*.

Prefix

A word part added to the beginning of a root word to change its meaning (e.g., *anti-* in antibiotic, *auto-* in autobiography).

Suffix

A word part added to the end of a root word to change its meaning, grammatical function, or both (e.g., *-logy* in biology, *-graphy* in biography).

Etymology

The study of the origin and historical development of words.

Important Terms (2/3)

Biology

The scientific study of life and living organisms.

Biodegradable

Capable of being decomposed by bacteria or other living organisms.

Biography

A written account of another person's life.

Autobiography

A written account of one's own life.

Antibiotic

A medicine that inhibits the growth of or destroys microorganisms.

Important Terms (3/3)

Symbiosis

A close, prolonged association between two or more different organisms of different species that may benefit one, both, or neither members.

Practical Application

Identify the root

Does it look familiar? For instance, if you see 'bio-', you immediately know the word likely has something to do with life.

Analyze prefixes

Does a prefix like 'anti-', 'auto-', or 'sym-' modify the root's meaning?

Consider suffixes

Does the suffix change the word to a noun (e.g., -logy, -graphy), an adjective (e.g., -able, -tic), or another part of speech?
