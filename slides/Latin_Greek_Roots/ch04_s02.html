The Latin Root *Scribere*

The Latin verb *scribere*, meaning "to write," is the direct ancestor of many English words related to putting thoughts or information into written form.

The Enduring Nature of Written Words

The section underscores the fundamental power of writing to preserve information and ideas over time, contrasting it with the transient nature of spoken words.

Etymology as a Tool

Understanding the origins of words (their etymology) helps clarify their meanings and connections to related terms.

Important Terms (1/3)

Scribe

(Historically) A professional writer or copyist, especially in ancient times; (Generally) one who writes.

Scribere

The Latin verb meaning "to write."

Scriptum

The past participle of *scribere*, meaning "written."

Stylus

A sharp, pointed tool used for writing on wax tablets.

Papyrus

A material similar to thick paper, made from the pith of the papyrus plant, used in ancient times as a writing surface.

Important Terms (2/3)

Describe

To "write down" or represent something in words, detailing its features.

Inscribe

To "write into" or carve words or symbols onto a surface.

Prescription

Something "written before"; specifically, instructions written by a doctor before medicine is dispensed or taken.

Manuscript

Literally "written by hand"; a book, document, or piece of music written by hand rather than typed or printed.

Transcript

A written or typed record that has been "written across" from speech or another form.

Important Terms (3/3)

Scripture

Sacred writings; texts considered holy by a religious group.

Postscript (P.S.)

Something "written after" the main body of a letter or text.
