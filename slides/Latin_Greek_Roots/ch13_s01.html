Important Terms

Epigraph

A short quotation or saying at the beginning of a book, chapter, or section, intended to suggest its theme or to comment on its content.

Cicero (Marcus Tullius Cicero)

(106–43 BCE) A highly influential Roman statesman, orator, lawyer, and philosopher. He is widely considered one of Rome's greatest prose stylists and orators.

"O blessed land, which gave birth to this man!"

The English translation of Cicero's quote, expressing intense admiration and reverence for an individual whose existence ennobles their place of origin.

The Nature and Purpose of an Epigraph

Setting the Tone

It can establish the emotional or intellectual atmosphere for the reader. In this case, a tone of admiration and gravitas.

Introducing Themes

It can subtly introduce core themes, ideas, or questions that the chapter will explore. Here, themes of leadership, national pride, or the significance of individuals seem relevant.

Foreshadowing

It might hint at plot points, character developments, or historical events, depending on the nature of the text.

Providing Context

Sometimes an epigraph offers a historical, philosophical, or cultural lens through which to interpret the upcoming content.

Evoking Thought

By presenting a compelling or intriguing statement, an epigraph encourages the reader to ponder its meaning and how it might relate to the material that follows.
