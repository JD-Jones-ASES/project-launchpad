Chapter 23, Section 2: Narrative - The Roots of Sound and Time

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Greek Root *phon

*: Derived from the Greek word *phone* (voice, sound, tone), this root relates specifically to sound as a physical phenomenon, often in a technical or descriptive sense.

Words from *phon

*: Common English words like *telephone*, *microphone*, *symphony*, and *euphony* illustrate the concept of sound, its transmission, amplification, or pleasing quality.

Distinction between *phon* and *voc/vox

*: While both relate to voice, Greek *phon* focuses on sound as a physical occurrence, whereas Latin *voc/vox* (voice, to call) is used for words related to speaking and calling.

Greek Root *chron

*: Originating from the Greek word *chronos* (time, duration, sequence), this root forms the basis for English words describing measurable, sequential, and ordered time.

Words from *chron

*: Examples include *chronology*, *synchronize*, *anachronism*, and *chronicle*, all of which deal with time in terms of order, concurrence, or historical placement.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Distinction between *chron* and *tempor

*: Greek *chron* tends to produce scientific and technical vocabulary related to time, while Latin *tempor* (time) gives us more general terms like *temporary* and *contemporary*.

Two Greek words for time

The Greeks had *chronos* (measurable, sequential time) which became a major English root, and *kairos* (the right or opportune moment), which survives in the field of rhetoric.

Important Terms (1/3)

phon

Greek root meaning "sound, voice, tone."

telephone

A device for transmitting sound over long distances; literally "far-sound" (tele- "far" + phon "sound").

microphone

A device that picks up small sounds and makes them louder; literally "small-sound" (micro- "small" + phon "sound").

symphony

A harmonious blending of many instruments or voices; literally "sounding together" (syn- "together" + phon "sound").

euphony

The quality of being pleasing to the ear, especially through a harmonious combination of words; literally "good sound" (eu- "good" + phon "sound").

Important Terms (2/3)

chron

Greek root meaning "time," specifically referring to duration and sequence.

chronology

An arrangement of events or dates in the order of their occurrence; a "time-account."

synchronize

To cause to occur or operate at the same time or rate; to make things happen "at the same time" (syn- "together" + chron "time").

anachronism

Something belonging to a period other than that in which it exists, especially a thing that is conspicuously old-fashioned; something "out of time" (ana- "against" + chron "time").

chronicle

A factual written account of important or historical events in the order of their occurrence; a record of events organized by time.

Important Terms (3/3)

kairos

A Greek word referring to the opportune or propitious moment for action or decision, especially in rhetoric.

The Root Story: *phon* (Sound, Voice) (1/2)

Telephone

A prime example, *telephone* combines *tele-* (meaning "far") with *phone* (sound) to create "far-sound," accurately describing a device that transmits sound over distances.

Microphone

This word, from *micro-* (meaning "small") and *phone* (sound), refers to a "small-sound" device. It doesn't make sounds smaller but rather captures and amplifies small or faint sounds.

Symphony

Derived from *syn-* (meaning "together") and *phone* (sound), *symphony* literally means "sounding together," perfectly describing the harmonious blending of many musical instruments.

Euphony

Combining *eu-* (meaning "good") with *phone* (sound), *euphony* denotes "good sound" – the pleasing and smooth quality often found in beautifully flowing words or musical arrangements.

Greek *phon

*: Predominantly used for words concerning sound as a physical phenomenon, its production, and transmission (e.g., *phonics*, *phonograph*).

The Root Story: *phon* (Sound, Voice) (2/2)

Latin *voc/vox

*: Primarily used for words related to calling, speaking, and vocal expression (e.g., *vocal*, *invoke*, *vocation*).

A "time-account," a *chronology* is an ordered list of events, arranged precisely in the sequence they occurred.

Synchronize
