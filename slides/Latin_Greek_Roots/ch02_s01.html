Important Terms

Epigraph

A short quotation or poem placed at the beginning of a book or chapter, intended to suggest its theme.

"Verbum sapienti sat est"

A Latin proverb meaning "A word to the wise is sufficient."

Plautus

An ancient Roman playwright (c. 254 – 184 BC), renowned for his comedies and often cited for common Latin proverbs.

*Persa

*: One of Plautus's Roman comedies, from which the epigraph "Verbum sapienti sat est" is taken (specifically, Act IV, Scene 7).

Proverb

A short, popular saying that expresses a general truth or piece of advice.

What is an Epigraph?

Setting the Tone

An epigraph can immediately establish the mood, whether it's serious, humorous, philosophical, or mysterious.

Hinting at Themes

It can introduce or foreshadow the central ideas, concepts, or conflicts that will be explored in the text.

Providing Context

Sometimes, an epigraph offers a historical, cultural, or philosophical framework for understanding the content that follows.

Encouraging Reflection

By presenting a condensed piece of wisdom or a thought-provoking statement, an epigraph invites the reader to ponder its meaning before delving into the main body of the text.

Establishing Authority or Connection

Quoting a renowned figure or a classic text can lend gravitas or connect the current work to a broader intellectual tradition.
