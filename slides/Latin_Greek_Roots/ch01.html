Chapter 1 — VOC (Voice and Calling)

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Root meaning and origin

"voc / vox" comes from Latin *vocare* (to call) and *vox* (voice), used in ancient Rome to describe everything from lawyers arguing in court to priests calling upon the gods

How the root appears

The root appears as "voc" in most words (vocal, vocation) and as "vok" in words that came through French (invoke, evoke, provoke, revoke)

Important morpheme patterns

Adding prefixes changes the direction of the calling — *ad-* (toward), *in-* (upon), *e-* (out), *pro-* (forth), *re-* (back)

Important Terms (1/2)

vocal

relating to or produced by the voice (*voc* + *-al*)

advocate

one who speaks in support of another; to publicly support a cause (*ad-* + *voc* + *-ate*)

vocation

a person's calling or profession; the work one feels summoned to do (*voc* + *-ation*)

invoke

to call upon for help or as an authority (*in-* + *vok*)

vociferous

loud and forceful in expressing feelings (*voc* + *ferre* + *-ous*)

Important Terms (2/2)

evoke

to call forth a feeling, memory, or image (*e-* + *vok*)

provoke

to call forth a reaction, often anger (*pro-* + *vok*)

revoke

to take back or withdraw a right or decision (*re-* + *vok*)

Word Family Analysis

invoke

*in-* (upon) — to call upon a rule or authority for support

evoke

*e-* (out) — to call out a memory or emotion

provoke

*pro-* (forth) — to call forth a reaction (often anger)

revoke

*re-* (back) — to call back; to take away a privilege
