Chapter 17, Section 3: The Profile of Plant Roots

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Root Functions

Roots primarily anchor the plant in the soil, absorb water and dissolved minerals, and often serve as storage sites for food produced during photosynthesis.

Root Anatomy

Understanding the internal and external structure of roots is crucial, including the root cap, apical meristem, epidermis, cortex, endodermis, and vascular cylinder with its xylem and phloem.

Root Growth

Roots grow primarily at their tips through the continuous division of cells in the apical meristem, a process protected by the root cap as the root navigates through the soil.

Types of Root Systems

Plants typically exhibit one of two main root system types: taproot systems or fibrous root systems, each with distinct growth habits and advantages.

Important Terms (1/4)

Root Cap

A protective thimble-like layer of parenchymal cells covering the tip of a root, shielding the delicate apical meristem as the root pushes through abrasive soil particles.

Apical Meristem

The region of actively dividing cells located just behind the root cap, responsible for primary growth (lengthening) of the root and producing new cells.

Epidermis

The outermost layer of cells in a young root, responsible for the absorption of water and minerals from the soil.

Root Hairs

Tiny, tubular extensions of epidermal cells that significantly increase the surface area of the root for efficient water and nutrient absorption.

Cortex

The tissue layer located between the epidermis and the vascular cylinder, primarily composed of parenchyma cells and involved in food storage (e.g., starch).

Important Terms (2/4)

Endodermis

An inner layer of cortical cells forming a cylinder around the vascular tissue, characterized by the Casparian strip. It regulates the movement of water and solutes into the vascular cylinder.

Casparian Strip

A waxy, waterproof band embedded in the cell walls of endodermal cells. It forces water and dissolved minerals to pass through the plasma membrane of endodermal cells (symplastic pathway) rather th...

Vascular Cylinder (Stele)

The central core of the root, containing the vascular tissues (xylem and phloem) and the pericycle.

Xylem

The vascular tissue responsible for transporting water and dissolved minerals absorbed by the roots upwards to the rest of the plant.

Phloem

The vascular tissue responsible for transporting sugars (food) produced during photosynthesis from the leaves to other parts of the plant, including the roots for growth and storage.

Important Terms (3/4)

Pericycle

A layer of meristematic cells located just inside the endodermis, from which lateral (branch) roots originate.

Taproot System

A root system characterized by a single, large, central primary root from which smaller lateral roots branch off (e.g., carrots, dandelions, oak trees).

Fibrous Root System

A root system consisting of many slender roots of similar size that spread out extensively, often near the soil surface (e.g., grasses, corn, wheat).

Prop Roots

Modified aerial roots that grow downwards from the stem and provide additional support and anchorage to tall or unstable plants (e.g., corn, mangroves).

Storage Roots

Roots modified to store large quantities of food (carbohydrates) or water, becoming swollen and fleshy (e.g., carrots, beets, sweet potatoes).

Important Terms (4/4)

Pneumatophores

Specialized roots that grow upwards out of water or waterlogged soil, facilitating gas exchange for roots in anaerobic conditions (e.g., cypress, mangroves).

Fundamental Functions of Roots

Anchorage

Roots firmly secure the plant in the soil, preventing it from being dislodged by wind, rain, or other environmental forces.

Absorption

They absorb water and dissolved mineral nutrients from the soil. This function is greatly enhanced by **root hairs**, which vastly increase the root's surface area for uptake.

Storage

Many plants use their roots as storage organs for carbohydrates (e.g., starch) produced during photosynthesis, providing energy reserves for growth, flowering, or dormancy.

Conduction

Roots facilitate the transport of absorbed water and nutrients upwards to the stem and leaves via the xylem, and transport sugars from leaves to root cells via the phloem.

Root Adaptations (1/2)

Storage Roots

Enlarged, fleshy roots modified to store large quantities of carbohydrates (starch) or water, serving as a food reserve (e.g., carrots, beets, sweet potatoes, radishes, yams).

Buttress Roots

Pneumatophores

Specialized roots that grow upwards out of oxygen-poor, waterlogged soil or water, allowing the root system to obtain oxygen for cellular respiration (e.g., bald cypress, black mangroves).

Parasitic Roots (Haustoria)

Roots of parasitic plants that penetrate the tissues of a host plant to absorb water and nutrients directly (e.g., dodder, mistletoe).

Root Adaptations (2/2)

Adventitious Roots

Roots that develop from non-root organs such as stems or leaves. Examples include prop roots, roots on plant cuttings, or aerial roots of ivy.
