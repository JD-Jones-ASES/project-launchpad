Chapter 4, Section 4: Function Arithmetic and Composition Study Guide

Key Concepts (1/2)

Arithmetic Combinations of Functions

Functions can be added, subtracted, multiplied, and divided similar to numbers.

Domain of Arithmetic Combinations

Composition of Functions `(f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x))`

A powerful operation where the output of the "inner" function $g(x)$ becomes the input for the "outer" function $f$. It's read as "$f$ composed with $g$" or "$f$ of $g$".

Evaluation of Composition (Inside Out)

To evaluate $(f \circ g)(x)$, first calculate $g(x)$, then use that result as the input for $f$.

Non-Commutativity of Composition

In general, $(f \circ g)(x) eq (g \circ f)(x)$. The order of composition matters.

Decomposition of Functions

The reverse process of composition, where a given complex function is broken down into two simpler functions $f$ and $g$ such that their composition $(f \circ g)(x)$ equals the original function.

Important Terms (1/2)

Sum of Functions

The function $(f+g)(x)$ formed by adding the outputs of $f(x)$ and $g(x)$.

Difference of Functions

The function $(f-g)(x)$ formed by subtracting the output of $g(x)$ from $f(x)$.

Product of Functions

The function $(fg)(x)$ formed by multiplying the outputs of $f(x)$ and $g(x)$.

Quotient of Functions

The function $(f/g)(x)$ formed by dividing the output of $f(x)$ by $g(x)$, provided $g(x) eq 0$.

Composition of Functions

An operation that combines two functions, say $f$ and $g$, to produce a new function $(f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x))$.

Important Terms (2/2)

Inner Function

In a composition $(f \circ g)(x)$, $g(x)$ is the inner function, whose output is used as the input for the outer function.

Outer Function

In a composition $(f \circ g)(x)$, $f(x)$ is the outer function, which acts on the output of the inner function.

Domain of Composition

For $(f \circ g)(x)$, it's the set of all $x$ such that $x$ is in the domain of $g$ AND $g(x)$ is in the domain of $f$.

Function Arithmetic

• For $(f+g)(x)$, $(f-g)(x)$, and $(fg)(x)$, the domain is the intersection of the domains of $f$ and $g$. That is, $D_f \cap D_g$.

• For $(f/g)(x)$, the domain is $D_f \cap D_g$, with an additional restriction: any $x$ for which $g(x) = 0$ must be excluded.

Composition of Functions

• $(f+g)(3) = f(3) + g(3) = (4(3)-1) + (3^2) = (12-1) + 9 = 11 + 9 = 20$.

• $(f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x)) = f(x^2) = 4(x^2) - 1 = 4x^2 - 1$.

• $(g \circ f)(x) = g(f(x)) = g(4x-1) = (4x-1)^2 = 16x^2 - 8x + 1$.

• $(g \circ f)(-1) = g(f(-1)) = g(4(-1)-1) = g(-5) = (-5)^2 = 25$.

Key Concepts (1/2)

How are two functions $f$ and $g$ combined arithmetically to form their sum, $(f+g)(x)$?

$(f+g)(x) = f(x) + g(x)$.

How are two functions $f$ and $g$ combined arithmetically to form their difference, $(f-g)(x)$?

$(f-g)(x) = f(x) - g(x)$.

How are two functions $f$ and $g$ combined arithmetically to form their product, $(fg)(x)$?

$(fg)(x) = f(x)g(x)$.

How are two functions $f$ and $g$ combined arithmetically to form their quotient, $(f/g)(x)$?

$(f/g)(x) = f(x)/g(x)$, provided $g(x) eq 0$.

What is the domain of $(f+g)(x)$, $(f-g)(x)$, and $(fg)(x)$ given functions $f$ and $g$ with overlapping domains?

The intersection of the domains of $f$ and $g$, denoted as $D_f \cap D_g$.

What is the domain of $(f/g)(x)$ given functions $f$ and $g$ with overlapping domains?

The intersection of the domains of $f$ and $g$ ($D_f \cap D_g$), with any $x$ values where $g(x) = 0$ excluded.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Let $f(x) = 3x+1$ and $g(x) = x^2-4$. Find and simplify $(f+g)(x)$.

$(f+g)(x) = (3x+1) + (x^2-4) = x^2+3x-3$.

Let $f(x) = 3x+1$ and $g(x) = x^2-4$. Evaluate $(f-g)(2)$.

$(f-g)(x) = (3x+1) - (x^2-4) = -x^2+3x+5$. So, $(f-g)(2) = -(2)^2 + 3(2) + 5 = -4 + 6 + 5 = 7$.

For $f(x) = 3x+1$ and $g(x) = x^2-4$, state the domain of $(f/g)(x)$.

The domain excludes values where $x^2-4=0$, so $x eq 2$ and $x eq -2$. The domain is $(-\infty, -2) \cup (-2, 2) \cup (2, \infty)$.

Define the composition of functions $f$ and $g$, written $(f \circ g)(x)$.

$(f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x))$. It means applying $g$ to $x$ first, then applying $f$ to the result.

When evaluating a composite function $(f \circ g)(x)$, which function is applied first, $f$ or $g$?

Function $g$ is applied first to $x$, and its output becomes the input for function $f$.

What is the domain of a composite function $(f \circ g)(x)$?

It consists of all $x$ values in the domain of $g$ for which $g(x)$ is also in the domain of $f$.
