Factoring Expressions

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Factoring as Reverse Multiplication

Factoring breaks down a polynomial into a product of simpler polynomials, much like division breaks down a number into its prime factors.

Importance of Factoring

It transforms polynomial equations into a product form, allowing the use of the Zero Product Property (if \(AB=0\), then \(A=0\) or \(B=0\)) to find solutions.

Systematic Factoring Strategy

Always begin by factoring out the Greatest Common Factor (GCF) from all terms. After that, count the remaining terms to determine the next factoring technique.

Factoring Trinomials ( \(x^2+bx+c\) )

For trinomials with a leading coefficient of 1, find two integers \(p\) and \(q\) that multiply to \(c\) and add to \(b\). The factored form is \((x+p)(x+q)\).

The AC Method (for \(ax^2+bx+c\) with \(a \neq 1\))

This systematic procedure involves finding two numbers that multiply to \(ac\) and add to \(b\), then splitting the middle term and factoring by grouping.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Special Product Formulas

Sum of Squares

Polynomials of the form \(a^2+b^2\) cannot be factored over the real numbers.

Always Check Your Work

Multiply your factors back together to ensure they equal the original polynomial. This catches most errors.

Important Terms (1/2)

Factoring

The process of rewriting a polynomial as a product of simpler polynomials (its factors).

Greatest Common Factor (GCF) of a Polynomial

The largest monomial (including its coefficient and variable parts) that divides every term in a polynomial.

Zero Product Property

If the product of two or more factors is zero, then at least one of the factors must be zero. This property is fundamental for solving factored polynomial equations.

Trinomial

A polynomial with three terms.

Important Terms (2/2)

Difference of Squares

A binomial in the form \(a^2-b^2\), which always factors into \((a+b)(a-b)\).

Perfect Square Trinomial

A trinomial that results from squaring a binomial, in the form \(a^2+2ab+b^2\) or \(a^2-2ab+b^2\). These factor into \((a+b)^2\) or \((a-b)^2\), respectively.
