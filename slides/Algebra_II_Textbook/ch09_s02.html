Parabolas as Conic Sections

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Geometric Definition of a Parabola

A parabola is the set of all points in a plane that are the same distance from a specific fixed point (the **focus**) and a specific fixed line (the **directrix**).

Vertex

The midpoint exactly between the focus and the directrix. It is also the turning point of the parabola.

Axis of Symmetry

A line that passes through the vertex and the focus, dividing the parabola into two symmetrical halves.

Vertical Parabolas (Vertex Form)

These parabolas have a vertical axis of symmetry and open either upward or downward. Their standard equation is $y = a(x - h)^2 + k$, where $(h, k)$ is the vertex.

Horizontal Parabolas (Vertex Form)

These parabolas have a horizontal axis of symmetry and open either left or right. Their standard equation is $x = a(y - k)^2 + h$, where $(h, k)$ is the vertex.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Converting from General Form

Parabola equations given in general form (e.g., $Ax^2 + Bx + Cy + D = 0$ or $Ay^2 + By + Cx + D = 0$) can be converted to vertex form by completing the square for the squared variable.

Important Terms (1/2)

Parabola (Conic Definition)

The set of all points in a plane equidistant from a fixed point (focus) and a fixed line (directrix).

Focus

The fixed point used in the geometric definition of a parabola. It always lies on the axis of symmetry, inside the parabola.

Directrix

The fixed line used in the geometric definition of a parabola. It is perpendicular to the axis of symmetry and lies outside the parabola.

Vertex

The turning point of the parabola, located exactly midway between the focus and the directrix.

Axis of Symmetry

The line passing through the vertex and the focus, which divides the parabola into two symmetrical halves.

Important Terms (2/2)

Vertical Parabola

A parabola whose axis of symmetry is vertical, opening upward or downward.

Horizontal Parabola

A parabola whose axis of symmetry is horizontal, opening to the left or right.

General Form (of a Parabola)

A second-degree equation with only one squared variable, such as $Ax^2 + Cx + Dy + Ey + F = 0$ (if $D \neq 0$) or $Ay^2 + By + Cx + D = 0$ (if $C \neq 0$).

Vertical Parabolas (1/2)

Vertex Form Equation

$y = a(x - h)^2 + k$

Vertex

The point $(h, k)$.

Opening Direction

Focus

Located at $(h, k + \frac{1}{4a})$. The focus is always *inside* the curve.

Directrix

The horizontal line $y = k - \frac{1}{4a}$. The directrix is always *outside* the curve.

Vertical Parabolas (2/2)

Vertex

$(1, 3)$.

Focus

$(1, 3 + \frac{1}{4(2)}) = (1, 3 + \frac{1}{8}) = (1, \frac{25}{8})$.

Directrix

$y = 3 - \frac{1}{8} = \frac{23}{8}$.

Horizontal Parabolas (1/2)

Vertex Form Equation

$x = a(y - k)^2 + h$

Vertex

The point $(h, k)$.

Opening Direction

Focus

Located at $(h + \frac{1}{4a}, k)$. The focus is always *inside* the curve.

Directrix

The vertical line $x = h - \frac{1}{4a}$. The directrix is always *outside* the curve.

Application: Parabolic Reflectors

Rays Parallel to Axis

Any incoming ray that is parallel to the parabola's axis of symmetry will reflect off the parabolic surface and pass directly through the focus.

Rays from Focus

Conversely, any light or sound originating from the focus will reflect off the parabolic surface and travel outward in a beam parallel to the axis of symmetry.

Satellite dishes

are parabolic: Incoming parallel radio waves (from satellites) converge at the receiver placed at the focus.

Flashlights and car headlights

use parabolic reflectors: A light bulb placed at the focus creates a powerful, parallel beam of light.

Telescopes and solar collectors

also utilize this property to collect and concentrate light or heat at a single point.
