Logarithmic Equations

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Definition of a Logarithmic Equation

An equation containing one or more logarithms where the variable is part of the logarithm's argument.

Domain Restriction

For any logarithm $\log_b(x)$, the argument $x$ *must be greater than zero* ($x > 0$). This is the most crucial rule for solving and checking logarithmic equations.

A systematic four-step process for solving logarithmic equations:

Logarithm Properties

Often used in step 1. The **Product Rule** ($\log_b M + \log_b N = \log_b(MN)$) is explicitly shown in an example, but the Quotient Rule and Power Rule can also be applicable.

One-to-One Property

If an equation has a single logarithm on both sides with the same base, e.g., $\log_b M = \log_b N$, then their arguments must be equal: $M = N$.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Extraneous Solutions

Solutions that arise from the algebraic steps but do not satisfy the domain restrictions of the original logarithmic equation. These *must* be rejected.

Quadratic in Disguise

Some logarithmic equations can be transformed into a quadratic form (e.g., $(\log x)^2 - 3 \log x + 2 = 0$) by using substitution (e.g., $u = \log x$).

Important Terms (1/2)

Logarithmic Equation

An equation that includes a variable within the argument of a logarithm.

Argument of a Logarithm

The expression inside the logarithm (e.g., in $\log_b(x)$, $x$ is the argument). It must always be positive.

Exponential Form

The equivalent form of a logarithm. If $\log_b M = c$, then its exponential form is $M = b^c$.

Extraneous Solution

A solution obtained through algebraic manipulation that does not satisfy the domain requirements of the original logarithmic equation, and therefore is not a valid solution.

Product Rule for Logarithms

The rule stating that the sum of two logarithms with the same base can be written as the logarithm of the product of their arguments: $\log_b M + \log_b N = \log_b(MN)$.

Important Terms (2/2)

Natural Logarithm (ln)

A logarithm with base $e$ (Euler's number), written as $\ln x$, which is equivalent to $\log_e x$.

Common Logarithm (log)

A logarithm with base 10, typically written as $\log x$, which is equivalent to $\log_{10} x$.
