Logarithms: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Definition of a Logarithm

A logarithm answers the question, "What exponent on base $b$ gives $x$?" If $b^y = x$, then $y = \log_b(x)$. Logarithmic functions are the inverse of exponential functions.

Conditions for Logarithms

For $\log_b(x)$ to be defined, the base $b$ must be positive ($b > 0$) and not equal to 1 ($b \ne 1$). The argument $x$ must be strictly positive ($x > 0$).

Converting Forms

The ability to convert an equation between its logarithmic form ($y = \log_b(x)$) and its exponential form ($b^y = x$) is fundamental to understanding and solving logarithmic problems.

Common Logarithm

This is a logarithm with base 10, denoted as $\log(x)$. It's used widely in science and engineering.

Natural Logarithm

This is a logarithm with base $e$ (Euler's number, approximately 2.71828), denoted as $\ln(x)$. It's particularly important in calculus and natural growth/decay models.

Domain of a Logarithmic Function

Because the argument of a logarithm must be strictly positive, the domain of a logarithmic function $f(x) = \log_b(g(x))$ is found by setting the argument $g(x) > 0$ and solving for $x$.

Important Terms (1/3)

Logarithm

The inverse operation to exponentiation. It finds the exponent to which a base must be raised to produce a given number.

Base (of a logarithm)

The number that is raised to a power in an exponential function, or the subscript in a logarithm (e.g., $b$ in $\log_b(x)$).

Argument (of a logarithm)

The number or expression inside the logarithm (e.g., $x$ in $\log_b(x)$). This must always be positive.

Common Logarithm

A logarithm with base 10, typically written as $\log(x)$ without an explicit base.

Natural Logarithm

A logarithm with base $e$ (Euler's number), typically written as $\ln(x)$.

Important Terms (2/3)

Euler's Number ($e$)

An irrational and transcendental mathematical constant, approximately 2.71828, which is the base of the natural logarithm.

Product Rule

$\log_b(MN) = \log_b M + \log_b N$ (The logarithm of a product is the sum of the logarithms).

Quotient Rule

$\log_b\left(\frac{M}{N}\right) = \log_b M - \log_b N$ (The logarithm of a quotient is the difference of the logarithms).

Power Rule

$\log_b(M^p) = p \log_b M$ (The logarithm of a number raised to an exponent is the exponent times the logarithm of the number).

Inverse Properties

$\log_b(b^x) = x$ and $b^{\log_b x} = x$. These demonstrate the inverse relationship between exponential and logarithmic functions.

Important Terms (3/3)

Change-of-Base Formula

A formula used to convert a logarithm from one base to another, often to evaluate it using a calculator: $\log_b(x) = \frac{\log_a(x)}{\log_a(b)}$.

Common and Natural Logarithms

Common Logarithm

This uses base 10.

Natural Logarithm

This uses Euler's number, $e \approx 2.71828$, as its base.
