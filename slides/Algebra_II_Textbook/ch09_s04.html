Hyperbolas: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Important Terms (1/3)

Hyperbola

The set of all points where the absolute difference of the distances to two fixed points (foci) is constant.

Foci (plural of focus)

The two fixed points used to define a hyperbola.

Center

The midpoint of the segment connecting the foci, and also the intersection point of the asymptotes.

Transverse Axis

The line segment connecting the two vertices of a hyperbola, passing through the foci and center. Its length is $2a$.

Conjugate Axis

The line segment perpendicular to the transverse axis, passing through the center. Its length is $2b$.

Important Terms (2/3)

Vertices

The points where the hyperbola intersects its transverse axis. They are $a$ units from the center.

Asymptotes

Two lines that the branches of a hyperbola approach as they extend indefinitely. They intersect at the center of the hyperbola.

Eccentricity ($e$)

A measure of how "open" a hyperbola is, defined as $c/a$. For hyperbolas, $e > 1$.

Central Rectangle

A rectangle used as a guide to sketch the asymptotes of a hyperbola. It is centered at $(h,k)$ with sides of length $2a$ and $2b$.

Conjugate Hyperbola

A hyperbola that shares the same asymptotes as another hyperbola but has its transverse and conjugate axes swapped.

Important Terms (3/3)

Degenerate Hyperbola

A special case where the hyperbola's equation results in a pair of intersecting lines.

Linear-Quadratic System

A system of equations consisting of one linear equation and one conic section equation.

Secant Line

A line that intersects a conic section at two distinct points.

Tangent Line

A line that intersects a conic section at exactly one point.

Definition and Fundamental Properties of Hyperbolas

Classifying Conics by the General Equation

Circle

$A=C$ (and both are non-zero and have the same sign).

Ellipse

$A \neq C$ (but both are non-zero and have the same sign).

Hyperbola

$A$ and $C$ have opposite signs.

Parabola

Either $A=0$ or $C=0$ (only one squared term).
