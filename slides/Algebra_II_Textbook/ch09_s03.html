Important Terms (1/3)

Ellipse

The set of all points in a plane for which the sum of the distances from two fixed points (the foci) is constant.

Foci (singular: Focus)

The two fixed points that define an ellipse.

Center

The midpoint of the segment connecting the foci of an ellipse.

Semi-major axis ($a$)

The distance from the center to a vertex of the ellipse. Half the length of the major axis.

Semi-minor axis ($b$)

The distance from the center to a co-vertex of the ellipse. Half the length of the minor axis.

Important Terms (2/3)

Major Axis

The longer axis of the ellipse, passing through the foci and vertices. Its length is $2a$.

Minor Axis

The shorter axis of the ellipse, perpendicular to the major axis and passing through the center and co-vertices. Its length is $2b$.

Vertices

The endpoints of the major axis.

Co-vertices

The endpoints of the minor axis.

Eccentricity ($e$)

A value that describes the shape of an ellipse, calculated as $e = \frac{c}{a}$. It indicates how much the ellipse deviates from a perfect circle.

Important Terms (3/3)

Perihelion

The point in an object's elliptical orbit where it is closest to the Sun (or other central body). For the Sun, this distance is $a-c$.

Aphelion

The point in an object's elliptical orbit where it is farthest from the Sun (or other central body). For the Sun, this distance is $a+c$.

Example 1: Identifying Parts of an Ellipse (Center at Origin) (1/2)

Center

$(0,0)$

Focal distance

$c^2 = a^2 - b^2 = 25 - 9 = 16 \Rightarrow c=4$.

Vertices

$(0 \pm a, 0) = (\pm 5, 0)$.

Co-vertices

$(0, 0 \pm b) = (0, \pm 3)$.

Foci

$(0 \pm c, 0) = (\pm 4, 0)$.

Example 1: Identifying Parts of an Ellipse (Center at Origin) (2/2)

Eccentricity

$e = \frac{c}{a} = \frac{4}{5} = 0.8$.

Example 2: Identifying Parts of an Ellipse (Shifted Center) (1/2)

Center

$(-1, 2)$.

Focal distance

$c^2 = a^2 - b^2 = 16 - 7 = 9 \Rightarrow c=3$.

Vertices

$(h \pm a, k) = (-1 \pm 4, 2)$, which gives $(3, 2)$ and $(-5, 2)$.

Co-vertices

$(h, k \pm b) = (-1, 2 \pm \sqrt{7})$.

Foci

$(h \pm c, k) = (-1 \pm 3, 2)$, which gives $(2, 2)$ and $(-4, 2)$.

Example 2: Identifying Parts of an Ellipse (Shifted Center) (2/2)

Eccentricity

$e = \frac{c}{a} = \frac{3}{4} = 0.75$.

Eccentricity and Planetary Orbits

Calculating $c$

$c = ea = 0.0167 \times 1.496 \times 10^8 \approx 2.50 \times 10^6$ km.

Calculating $b$

$b = \sqrt{a^2 - c^2} = \sqrt{(1.496 \times 10^8)^2 - (2.50 \times 10^6)^2} \approx 1.495 \times 10^8$ km. Notice $b$ is very close to $a$, indicating a near-circular orbit.

Perihelion (closest point to Sun)

Occurs when Earth is at a vertex closest to the focus (Sun). Distance is $a - c \approx 1.496 \times 10^8 - 0.0250 \times 10^8 = 1.471 \times 10^8$ km.

Aphelion (farthest point from Sun)

Occurs when Earth is at a vertex farthest from the focus (Sun). Distance is $a + c \approx 1.496 \times 10^8 + 0.0250 \times 10^8 = 1.521 \times 10^8$ km.
