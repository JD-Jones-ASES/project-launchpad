Adding and Subtracting Rational Expressions

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Common Denominators are Essential

Just like with numerical fractions, rational expressions must have a common denominator before their numerators can be added or subtracted.

Least Common Denominator (LCD)

For polynomial denominators, finding the LCD is crucial. It is the product of all distinct prime polynomial factors, each raised to the highest power it appears in any denominator.

Factoring is Fundamental

Factoring denominators completely is the first step in finding the LCD and is also necessary for simplifying the final rational expression by canceling common factors.

Steps for Same Denominators

When denominators are already the same, simply add or subtract the numerators and place the result over the common denominator. Always simplify the final expression.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Restrictions

It's important to identify values for the variable that would make any denominator (original or LCD) equal to zero, as these values are excluded from the domain of the expression.

Important Terms

Rational Expression

A fraction where the numerator and denominator are polynomials. (Though not explicitly defined in the text, it's the subject matter.)

Common Denominator

A polynomial that is a multiple of the denominators of two or more rational expressions, allowing them to be combined.

Prime Polynomial Factors

Polynomials that cannot be factored further into non-constant polynomials with integer coefficients (e.g., $x-2$, $x+1$, $x^2+1$).

Restrictions

Values of the variable for which a rational expression is undefined, typically because they make the denominator zero. These values must be excluded from the domain.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the fundamental requirement for adding or subtracting rational expressions?

A common denominator.

How do you add or subtract rational expressions that already have the same denominator, Q?

Add or subtract the numerators ($P \pm R$) and keep the common denominator ($Q$). Then simplify the result, if possible.

What is the final step after combining the numerators of rational expressions, regardless of whether they initially had the same or different denominators?

Simplify the resulting rational expression by factoring the numerator and denominator and canceling any common factors.

Define the Least Common Denominator (LCD) of two or more rational expressions.

The product of all distinct prime polynomial factors, each raised to the highest power in which it appears in any denominator.

What is the LCD of $5/(6x^2)$ and $7/(4x)$?

Factors of $6x^2$: $2 \cdot 3 \cdot x^2$. Factors of $4x$: $2^2 \cdot x$. LCD $= 2^2 \cdot 3 \cdot x^2 = 12x^2$.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the LCD of $3/(x^2-4)$ and $1/(x^2-4x+4)$?

$x^2-4 = (x-2)(x+2)$. $x^2-4x+4 = (x-2)^2$. LCD $= (x-2)^2(x+2)$.

How is the process of adding/subtracting rational expressions with different denominators analogous to adding/subtracting numeric fractions (e.g., $1/4 + 2/3$)?

Both require finding an LCD, rewriting each fraction with the LCD, combining the numerators over the common denominator, and then simplifying.

When adding $3/(x-1) + 2/(x+4)$, what is the first step and what is the resulting LCD?

Find the LCD. Since $(x-1)$ and $(x+4)$ share no common factors, the LCD is $(x-1)(x+4)$.

After finding the LCD for $3/(x-1) + 2/(x+4)$ as $(x-1)(x+4)$, how would you rewrite the first term, $3/(x-1)$, to have the LCD?

Multiply its numerator and denominator by $(x+4)$: $3(x+4)/((x-1)(x+4))$.

When subtracting $x/(x^2-x-2) - 2/(x^2-4)$, what are the completely factored denominators?

$x^2-x-2 = (x-2)(x+1)$ and $x^2-4 = (x-2)(x+2)$.
