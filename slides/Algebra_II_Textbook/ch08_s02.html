Transformations II: Stretches, Compressions, and Combined

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Vertical Stretches and Compressions

These transformations alter the height of a graph.

Horizontal Stretches and Compressions

These transformations alter the width of a graph.

Distinction Between Vertical and Horizontal Scaling

Order of Transformations

When multiple transformations are applied to a function, they must be applied in a specific sequence, typically working from the "inside out" of the function notation.

Important Terms

Scaling Transformations

Operations that stretch or compress a graph, changing its size rather than just its position.

Vertical Stretch

A transformation where a graph becomes taller, achieved by multiplying the output $f(x)$ by a constant $a > 1$.

Vertical Compression

A transformation where a graph becomes shorter, achieved by multiplying the output $f(x)$ by a constant $0 < a < 1$.

Horizontal Stretch

A transformation where a graph becomes wider, achieved by multiplying the input $x$ by a constant $0 < b < 1$.

Horizontal Compression

A transformation where a graph becomes narrower, achieved by multiplying the input $x$ by a constant $b > 1$.

Vertical Stretches and Compressions

The Rule

Given $y = f(x)$, the graph of $y = a \cdot f(x)$

Effect on Points

Each point $(x, y)$ on the original graph $y=f(x)$ is transformed to $(x, ay)$ on the new graph $y=a \cdot f(x)$.

Points Unchanged

Any point on the $x$-axis (where $y=0$) remains fixed during a vertical stretch or compression because $a \cdot 0 = 0$.

Horizontal Stretches and Compressions

The Rule

Given $y = f(x)$, the graph of $y = f(b \cdot x)$

Effect on Points

Each point $(x, y)$ on the original graph $y=f(x)$ is transformed to $(x/b, y)$ on the new graph $y=f(bx)$.

Points Unchanged

Any point on the $y$-axis (where $x=0$) remains fixed because $0/b = 0$.

Mnemonic

Think of $f(2x)$ as the function "working twice as fast." To get the same $y$-output as $f(x)$, you only need half the $x$-input. This means the graph is compressed horizontally.

Review Questions

Key Concepts (1/2)

How do scaling transformations differ from shifts?

Shifts move every point the same distance (add/subtract to input/output), while scaling transformations multiply input/output values, making the graph taller...

What is the general rule for a vertical stretch or compression of y = f(x)?

The graph of y = f(x) becomes y = a f(x), where 'a' is a positive constant.

Describe the transformation of y = f(x) when it becomes y = a f(x) for a > 1.

The graph is stretched vertically by a factor of 'a', making it appear taller and narrower.

Describe the transformation of y = f(x) when it becomes y = a f(x) for 0 < a < 1.

The graph is compressed vertically by a factor of 'a', making it appear shorter and wider.

If (x, y) is a point on y = f(x), what is the corresponding point on y = a f(x)?

Each point (x, y) on y = f(x) maps to (x, ay) on y = a f(x).

Which points on a graph are unaffected by a vertical stretch or compression?

Points on the x-axis (where y = 0) are unchanged, because a 0 = 0.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the general rule for a horizontal stretch or compression of y = f(x)?

The graph of y = f(x) becomes y = f(bx), where 'b' is a positive constant.

What is the counterintuitive property of horizontal scaling?

Multiplying the input by b > 1 actually compresses the graph horizontally, making it narrower, rather than stretching it.

Describe the transformation of y = f(x) when it becomes y = f(bx) for b > 1.

The graph is compressed horizontally by a factor of 1/b, making it narrower.

Describe the transformation of y = f(x) when it becomes y = f(bx) for 0 < b < 1.

The graph is stretched horizontally by a factor of 1/b, making it wider.

If (x, y) is a point on y = f(x), what is the corresponding point on y = f(bx)?

The point (x, y) maps to (x/b, y).

Explain the mnemonic 'the function is working twice as fast' for understanding f(2x).

For f(2x), the function reaches a given y-value at half the x-distance compared to f(x), which means the graph is compressed horizontally (narrower).
