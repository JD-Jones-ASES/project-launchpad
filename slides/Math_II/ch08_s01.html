Plotting and Identifying Points

The process of locating a point on the plane given its ordered pair, or determining the ordered pair for a given point.

Important Terms (1/2)

Coordinate Plane

A two-dimensional surface formed by the intersection of two perpendicular number lines, used to locate points using ordered pairs. Also known as the Cartesian Plane.

x-axis

The horizontal number line in the coordinate plane.

y-axis

The vertical number line in the coordinate plane.

Origin (O)

The point where the x-axis and y-axis intersect, represented by the ordered pair $(0, 0)$. It serves as the reference point for all other locations.

Ordered Pair (x, y)

A pair of numbers that uniquely identifies the position of a point on the coordinate plane. The first number is the x-coordinate, and the second is the y-coordinate.

Important Terms (2/2)

x-coordinate

The first number in an ordered pair $(x, y)$, indicating the horizontal distance and direction from the origin. Positive values mean right, negative values mean left.

y-coordinate

The second number in an ordered pair $(x, y)$, indicating the vertical distance and direction from the origin. Positive values mean up, negative values mean down.

Quadrants

The four regions into which the coordinate plane is divided by the x-axis and y-axis, labeled with Roman numerals I, II, III, and IV.

The Foundation: The Coordinate Plane

The x-axis

This is a horizontal number line. Positive numbers are to the right of the origin, and negative numbers are to the left.

The y-axis

This is a vertical number line. Positive numbers are above the origin, and negative numbers are below.

Locating Points with Ordered Pairs

• The x-coordinate ($x$) tells you how far to move horizontally from the origin.

• The y-coordinate ($y$) tells you how far to move vertically from the origin.

The Four Quadrants (1/2)

Quadrant I (QI)

Top-right region, where both $x$ and $y$ coordinates are positive $(+, +)$.

Quadrant II (QII)

Top-left region, where $x$ coordinates are negative and $y$ coordinates are positive $(-, +)$.

Quadrant III (QIII)

Bottom-left region, where both $x$ and $y$ coordinates are negative $(-, -)$.

Quadrant IV (QIV)

Bottom-right region, where $x$ coordinates are positive and $y$ coordinates are negative $(+, -)$.

A(3, 5)

Start at the origin. Move 3 units right (positive x), then 5 units up (positive y). This point is in Quadrant I.

The Four Quadrants (2/2)

B(-4, 2)

Start at the origin. Move 4 units left (negative x), then 2 units up (positive y). This point is in Quadrant II.

C(-1, -3)

Start at the origin. Move 1 unit left (negative x), then 3 units down (negative y). This point is in Quadrant III.

D(0, -2)

Start at the origin. Move 0 units horizontally (stay on the y-axis), then 2 units down (negative y). This point lies on the y-axis.

Reflections over the Axes (1/2)

Reflecting $(a, b)$ over the x-axis

The x-coordinate stays the same, and the y-coordinate changes its sign. The new point is $(a, -b)$. (Example: reflecting $(3, -5)$ over the x-axis gives $(3, 5)$).

Reflecting $(a, b)$ over the y-axis

The x-coordinate changes its sign, and the y-coordinate stays the same. The new point is $(-a, b)$. (Example: reflecting $(3, -5)$ over the y-axis gives $(-3, -5)$).

Reflecting $(a, b)$ over the origin

Both the x-coordinate and the y-coordinate change their signs. The new point is $(-a, -b)$. (Example: reflecting $(3, -5)$ over the origin gives $(-3, 5)$).

Reflected over the x-axis

Apply the rule $(a, b) \rightarrow (a, -b)$. So, $(3, -5) \rightarrow (3, -(-5)) = (3, 5)$.

Reflected over the y-axis

Apply the rule $(a, b) \rightarrow (-a, b)$. So, $(3, -5) \rightarrow (-3, -5)$.

Reflections over the Axes (2/2)

Reflected over the origin

Apply the rule $(a, b) \rightarrow (-a, -b)$. So, $(3, -5) \rightarrow (-3, -(-5)) = (-3, 5)$.

Real-World Applications

Maps and GPS

Locations are often given as coordinates (longitude and latitude, which form a type of spherical coordinate system, analogous to x and y on a flat map).

Video Games

Characters and objects move across a game screen, which is essentially a coordinate plane.

Engineering and Architecture

Designs often use coordinate systems for precise measurements and placement of components.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the primary purpose of the coordinate plane?

The coordinate plane allows you to describe the exact position of any point using two numbers.

How is the coordinate plane formed?

It is formed by two perpendicular number lines, the x-axis (horizontal) and the y-axis (vertical).

What are the names of the two perpendicular number lines in the coordinate plane?

The x-axis (horizontal) and the y-axis (vertical).

Where do the x-axis and y-axis intersect, and what are its coordinates?

They intersect at the origin, labeled O = (0, 0).

What is an ordered pair, and what does it represent?

An ordered pair (x, y) gives the coordinates of a point.

In an ordered pair (x, y), what does the x-coordinate tell you?

The first number x tells how far to move left or right from the origin.

Key Concepts (2/2)

In an ordered pair (x, y), what does the y-coordinate tell you?

The second number y tells how far to move up or down from the origin.

How do the axes divide the coordinate plane?
