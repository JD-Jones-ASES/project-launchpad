Real-World Applications

The Distance Formula can be applied to practical problems, such as finding distances on maps or between locations represented by coordinates, often requiring an additional conversion factor for units.

Common Pitfall

A frequent error is forgetting to square the differences before adding them under the radical. Always ensure the terms $(x_2 - x_1)$ and $(y_2 - y_1)$ are squared *before* being added together.

Important Terms (1/2)

Coordinate Plane

A two-dimensional plane formed by the intersection of a horizontal number line (x-axis) and a vertical number line (y-axis), used to locate points using ordered pairs $(x, y)$.

Pythagorean Theorem

Distance Formula

A formula used to calculate the distance between two points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ in a coordinate plane: $d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$.

Hypotenuse

The longest side of a right triangle, opposite the right angle. In the context of the Distance Formula derivation, it represents the distance between the two given points.

Legs (of a right triangle)

The two shorter sides of a right triangle that form the right angle. In the Distance Formula derivation, these are the horizontal and vertical segments.

Important Terms (2/2)

Simplified Radical Form

An expression with a square root that has no perfect square factors other than 1 remaining under the radical sign. For example, $\sqrt{8}$ simplifies to $2\sqrt{2}$.
