Review of Perimeter and Area

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Perimeter

The total distance around the outside edge of a two-dimensional shape. It is a linear measurement, typically in units like feet, meters, or inches.

Area

The amount of surface a two-dimensional shape covers, measured in square units (e.g., square feet, square meters, square inches).

Formulas for Basic Shapes

Understanding and accurately applying the correct formulas for common shapes like rectangles, triangles, trapezoids, and circles is crucial for solving measurement problems.

Fractional and Decimal Dimensions

The ability to apply area and perimeter formulas accurately even when measurements are given as fractions or decimals, requiring careful arithmetic with these number types.

Composite Figures

Shapes that are made up of two or more simpler geometric shapes. Their area can be found by dissecting the figure into known basic shapes and then adding or subtracting their individual areas.

Important Terms (1/2)

Perimeter

The sum of the lengths of all sides of a polygon or the distance around the circumference of a circle.

Area

The extent or measurement of a surface within a boundary, expressed in square units.

Base (b)

A side of a polygon, often used as a reference for calculating height in triangles and trapezoids.

Height (h)

The perpendicular distance from the base to the opposite vertex or side.

Radius (r)

The distance from the center of a circle to any point on its circumference.

Important Terms (2/2)

Diameter (d)

The distance across a circle through its center, equal to twice the radius ($d = 2r$).

Bases of a Trapezoid ($b_1, b_2$)

The two parallel sides of a trapezoid.

Composite Figure

A geometric shape formed by combining two or more basic geometric shapes.

$\pi$ (Pi)

A mathematical constant, approximately 3.14159, representing the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter.

Strategy

Visualize the L-shape as a large rectangle with a smaller rectangular piece removed from one corner.

Area of large rectangle

$A_{\text{large}} = (12 \text{ ft})(10 \text{ ft}) = 120 \text{ ft}^2$

Area of missing piece

$A_{\text{missing}} = (4 \text{ ft})(5 \text{ ft}) = 20 \text{ ft}^2$
