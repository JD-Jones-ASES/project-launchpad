Important Terms

Rational Number

Any number that can be expressed as a fraction $\frac{p}{q}$, where $p$ and $q$ are integers and $q eq 0$. This includes integers, fractions, and terminating or repeating decimals.

Least Common Denominator (LCD)

The smallest positive common multiple of the denominators of two or more fractions. It is used to rewrite fractions with a common base for comparison or addition/subtraction.

Numerator

The top number in a fraction, indicating how many parts of the whole are being considered.

Denominator

The bottom number in a fraction, indicating the total number of equal parts into which the whole is divided.

Decimal

A way of representing non-integer numbers, typically based on powers of 10.

Summary of Strategies for Comparing Rational Numbers

Common Denominator

Rewrite fractions with the Least Common Denominator (LCD) and then compare their numerators. This is ideal for comparing fractions directly.

Decimals

Convert all numbers to their decimal form and compare them digit by digit from left to right. This is highly versatile for mixed sets of numbers.

Number Line

Visualize the numbers on a number line. Numbers positioned further to the right are greater, and numbers further to the left are smaller. This provides a good conceptual understanding.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What are the two primary strategies for comparing and ordering rational numbers discussed in this section?

Converting to common denominators and converting to decimals.

State the "Comparing Fractions with a Common Denominator" property.

If two fractions have the same positive denominator ($d > 0$), the fraction with the larger numerator is greater (i.e., if $a < b$, then $ad/d < bd/d$).

How do you compare fractions with different denominators using the common denominator method?

Rewrite them with the least common denominator (LCD) and then compare their numerators.

Using a common denominator, compare 5/8 and 7/12. (Use <, >, or =).

The LCD of 8 and 12 is 24. 5/8 = 15/24, and 7/12 = 14/24. Since 15 > 14, 5/8 > 7/12.

Use the common denominator method to compare 3/5 and 5/9.

The LCD of 5 and 9 is 45. 3/5 = 27/45, and 5/9 = 25/45. Since 27 > 25, 3/5 > 5/9.

When is converting to decimals often a faster method for comparing rational numbers?

Especially when one or both numbers are already given as decimals, or when conversions are quick.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Order the following from least to greatest using decimals: 0.4, 3/8, 0.3.

Convert to decimals: 0.4 = 0.400, 3/8 = 0.375, 0.3 = 0.333 (as per text). Comparing: 0.333 < 0.375 < 0.400. From least to greatest: 0.3, 3/8, 0.4.

What is the most reliable approach for ordering a mixed set of rational numbers (fractions, decimals, integers)?

Convert everything to the same form, usually decimals, before ordering.

What specific caution applies when comparing negative numbers?

When comparing negative numbers, the one with the larger absolute value is actually the smaller number (e.g., -5 < -2 even though 5 > 2).

Three students measured a hallway: Ali 12 3/8 m, Bea 12.4 m, Carlos 12 1/3 m. Order their measurements from shortest to longest.

Convert to decimals: Ali: 12 3/8 = 12.375 m; Bea: 12.4 = 12.400 m; Carlos: 12 1/3 ≈ 12.333 m. The order from shortest to longest is 12 1/3, 12 3/8, 12.4.

Insert <, >, or =: 7/10 ___ 0.6.

Convert 7/10 to 0.7. Since 0.7 > 0.6, then 7/10 > 0.6.
