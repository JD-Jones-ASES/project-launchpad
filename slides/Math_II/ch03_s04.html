Chapter 3, Section 4

Simple and Compound Interest

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Interest

The money paid for the use of borrowed money, or the income earned from deposited money.

Principal (P)

The initial amount of money deposited or borrowed.

Annual Interest Rate (r)

The percentage charged or earned per year, expressed as a decimal in formulas.

Time (t)

The duration, in years, for which the money is deposited or borrowed.

Simple Interest

Interest calculated *only* on the original principal amount. The principal remains constant throughout the investment/loan period for calculation purposes.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Compound Interest (Annual)

Interest calculated on the original principal *and* on all accumulated interest from previous periods. This leads to "interest on interest" and faster growth.

Exponential Growth

Compound interest is an example of exponential growth, where the growth rate is applied to an ever-increasing base.

Units Consistency

It is critical that the annual interest rate ($r$) is expressed as a decimal and the time ($t$) is in years for both formulas.

Important Terms (1/2)

Interest

The fee charged by a lender to a borrower for the use of money, or the fee paid by a bank to a depositor for keeping money in an account.

Principal (P)

The original amount of money invested or borrowed.

Annual Interest Rate (r)

The rate at which interest is charged or earned, expressed per year. In formulas, this rate must be converted from a percentage to a decimal (e.g., 4% becomes 0.04).

Time (t)

The length of the investment or loan period, always expressed in years for these formulas.

Simple Interest (I)

A method of calculating interest where the interest earned or paid is based solely on the initial principal amount.

Important Terms (2/2)

Compound Interest (A)

A method of calculating interest where the interest earned or paid is based on the initial principal *and* all accumulated interest from previous periods.

Compounded Annually

Means that the interest is calculated and added to the principal once per year.

Final Amount (A)

The total sum of money at the end of the investment or loan period, including both the principal and the accumulated interest.

Important Considerations for Calculations

Rate as a Decimal

Always convert the annual interest rate from a percentage to a decimal before using it in the formulas. For example, 6% becomes 0.06.

Time in Years

Ensure that the time period ($t$) is expressed in years. If a time period is given in months (e.g., 6 months), convert it to years (e.g., 6 months / 12 months/year = 0.5 years).

Loan Applications

These formulas apply to both money you earn (like in a savings account) and money you owe (like a loan). For loans, the total amount calculated ($A$ or $P+I$) represents the total repayment.
