Important Terms

Inequality

A mathematical statement that compares two expressions using one of the symbols `<`, `>`, `≤`, or `≥`. Unlike equations, inequalities represent situations where quantities are not necessarily equal.

Introduction to Inequalities

• `a < b`: "$a$ is less than $b$"

• `a > b`: "$a$ is greater than $b$"

• `a ≤ b`: "$a$ is less than or equal to $b$"

• `a ≥ b`: "$a$ is greater than or equal to $b$"

Graphing Inequalities on a Number Line (1/3)

Open Circle `()`

Use an open circle for inequalities using `<` (less than) or `>` (greater than). This signifies that the boundary value itself is not part of the solution set.

Closed Circle `()`

Use a closed circle for inequalities using `≤` (less than or equal to) or `≥` (greater than or equal to). This signifies that the boundary value is part of the solution set.

Shade Left

For "less than" (`<` or `≤`) inequalities, shade the number line to the left of the boundary point.

Shade Right

For "greater than" (`>` or `≥`) inequalities, shade the number line to the right of the boundary point.

Symbol

The symbol is `≥`, which means "greater than or equal to."

Graphing Inequalities on a Number Line (2/3)

Circle

Because it includes "equal to," we use a closed circle at $-2$.

Shading

Because it's "greater than," we shade to the right.

Symbol

The symbol is `<`, which means "less than."

Circle

Because it's strictly "less than" (not equal to), we use an open circle at $1.5$.

Shading

Because it's "less than," we shade to the left.

Graphing Inequalities on a Number Line (3/3)

Answer

An open circle at $0$, shaded to the right. (Because `>` means strictly greater than, and the solutions are numbers larger than 0).

Writing an Inequality from a Graph or Application

Translation

"At least $48$ inches" means the height must be greater than or equal to $48$.

Inequality

$h \ge 48$

Graph Description

The graph would have a closed circle at $48$ and be shaded to the right.
