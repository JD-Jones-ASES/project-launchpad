Evaluating Websites, News, and Social Media: A Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Credibility

The trustworthiness and believability of a source, indicating whether its information is likely accurate.

Evidence

Facts, data, observations, or expert testimony that supports or refutes a claim. Good arguments rely on strong evidence.

Source

The origin of information, such as books, websites, news articles, or social media posts. Not all sources are equally reliable.

The CRAAP Test

A five-part checklist (Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose) used to systematically evaluate the reliability and usefulness of any information source.

Lateral Reading

A fact-checking technique where you open new browser tabs to research a source or claim by seeing what other reliable sources say *about* it, rather than just reading the source itself.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Red Flags

Specific warning signs (e.g., no author, emotional language, excessive ads) that indicate a source might be unreliable or biased.

Anecdotal Evidence

Using a single personal story or experience as proof for a general claim. This is a weak form of evidence as it doesn't represent broader data or trends.

Important Terms (1/2)

Evidence

Information—facts, data, observations, or expert testimony—that supports or goes against a claim.

Source

The origin point from which information comes, such as books, websites, news articles, interviews, research studies, or social media posts.

Credibility

The quality of being trustworthy and believable; a credible source provides good reasons to believe its information is accurate.

CRAAP Test

An acronym representing five key criteria (Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose) used to evaluate the reliability and appropriateness of information sources.

Red Flags to Watch For (1/2)

No Author Listed

If no one is willing to put their name on the content, why should you trust it?

No Date

Without a publication or update date, you can't assess the currency of the information.

Emotional Headlines

Sensationalized titles designed to grab clicks ("You WON'T BELIEVE...") often prioritize engagement over accuracy.

Too Good to Be True Claims

If something sounds unbelievably amazing or offers a miraculous solution, it's likely false.

No Sources or References

Reputable sources cite their evidence. A lack of references is a major warning sign.

Red Flags to Watch For (2/2)

Excessive Ads or Pop-ups

Websites primarily focused on generating ad revenue may prioritize sensational content over factual accuracy.

Common Misconceptions & Pitfalls

Professional Appearance Equals Trustworthiness

A well-designed website can still contain false information. Design quality and factual accuracy are separate things.

Only "Fake News" Sites Are Unreliable

Even well-known news organizations can have biases or make errors in individual articles. Always evaluate specific pieces of content.

Search Engine Rankings Indicate Truth

Search engines rank results based on popularity, keywords, and other algorithms, not necessarily accuracy or credibility.
