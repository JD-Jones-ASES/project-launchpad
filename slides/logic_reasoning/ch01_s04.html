Chapter 1, Section 4: Argument Detective Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Argument Identification

The skill of recognizing when a set of statements is intended to provide reasons (premises) to support another statement (conclusion).

Premise

A statement offered as evidence or a reason to believe the conclusion. In this section, you'll practice underlining these.

Conclusion

The statement that the argument is trying to convince you of; it's the main point or claim being supported. You'll practice circling this.

Distinguishing Arguments from Non-Arguments

Not every passage contains an argument. Some passages merely present facts, background information, or off-topic comments without attempting to persuade the reader of a particular claim.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Soundness

A characteristic of a *valid* argument that also has *all true premises*. A sound argument is one you should accept as true.

"Find the Conclusion First" Strategy

A crucial tip for identifying arguments. By first asking, "What is the speaker trying to convince me of?", you can more easily pinpoint the conclusion and then identify the supporting premises.

Important Terms (1/2)

Argument

A set of statements, one of which (the conclusion) is claimed to be supported by the others (the premises).

Premise

A statement in an argument that provides support for the conclusion.

Conclusion

The main claim or point of an argument that the premises are intended to support.

Validity

A property of an argument where the conclusion logically follows from the premises; if the premises were true, the conclusion *must* be true. It describes the argument's structure.

Soundness

A property of a *valid* argument in which all its premises are actually true. A sound argument guarantees a true conclusion.

Important Terms (2/2)

Non-Argument

A passage that presents statements or facts but does not offer one statement as a reason to believe another.

What *Not* to Mark

Background Information

Context or setup that doesn't directly support the conclusion.

Greetings

Phrases like "Good morning, everyone" or "Hey, Mom."

Off-Topic Comments

Sentences that are irrelevant to the main claim being made.

Passage 2: The Video Game Argument

Everyday Argument

This demonstrates how arguments appear in daily life.

Conclusion as a Request

The conclusion is what the speaker wants: "can I play video games for one more hour?" or "I think I've earned some extra screen time."

Premises

The reasons given are "I already finished my homework," "I cleaned my room like you asked," and "it's Friday night and there's no school tomorrow."
