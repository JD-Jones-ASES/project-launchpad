Important Terms (1/2)

Ad Hominem

Latin for “to the person.” A fallacy that attacks the person making an argument instead of addressing the argument itself.

Straw Man

A fallacy that misrepresents someone's argument to make it easier to attack, like knocking down a man made of straw instead of fighting a real opponent.

Red Herring

A fallacy that introduces an unrelated topic to distract from the original argument, named after the practice of using smoked fish to throw hunting dogs off a scent.

Sophists

A group of traveling teachers in ancient Greece (over 2,400 years ago) who taught people how to win arguments, often using clever but dishonest reasoning, regardless of the truth.

Sophistry

A term that still means "clever but dishonest reasoning" today, derived from the practices of the Sophists.

Important Terms (2/2)

Tu Quoque

Latin for “you also.” A fallacy that deflects criticism by pointing out that the other person does the same thing, essentially saying, "you do it too!"

Introduction to Relevance Fallacies

What they are

Bad arguments that try to win by introducing information that has nothing to do with the actual point. The "evidence" is irrelevant and doesn't logically connect to the conclusion.

Common tactics

Making fun of someone, changing the subject, or twisting someone's words instead of responding to the actual argument.

Ad Hominem — Attacking the Person (1/2)

Definition

An attack on the person making the argument, rather than on the argument itself. (Latin for "to the person").

Goal

To make you believe that because the person is "bad" (or flawed in some way), their argument must also be bad.

Core principle

An argument's validity depends on its logic and evidence, not on who is making it. Even a person with flaws can make a sound argument, and a respected person can make a weak one.

Key takeaway

Straw Man — Twisting Someone's Words

Definition

Misrepresenting an opponent's argument to make it easier to attack. Instead of responding to the actual argument, one attacks a weaker, distorted version.

Sneakiness

This fallacy can be subtle, as the person using it might genuinely believe they are responding fairly. They often exaggerate or slightly alter the original argument.

Mechanism

Take the real argument, distort it (make it extreme, simplified, or completely different), then easily "defeat" the distorted version, pretending to have defeated the original.

Examples

Differentiation (from Red Herring)

A straw man *changes the argument itself* by distorting it, whereas a red herring *changes the entire topic*.

Red Herring — Changing the Subject

Definition

Introducing an unrelated topic to distract from the original argument.

Origin

Named after the old trick of dragging a strongly scented smoked fish across a trail to confuse hunting dogs.

Effectiveness

The distracting topic might seem important or interesting, but if it doesn't address the original question, it's irrelevant and serves only to divert attention.

Tu Quoque — “You Do It Too!” (1/2)

Definition

A fallacy that deflects criticism by pointing out that the other person is guilty of the same thing. (Latin for "you also").

Mechanism

It's a "you too" defense, an argument version of pointing a finger back.

Problem

The hypocrisy of the accuser does not make their argument or criticism invalid. The truth of a statement is independent of the speaker's actions.

Example

Why it's tempting

It feels satisfying to expose hypocrisy.

Tu Quoque — “You Do It Too!” (2/2)

Important reminder

Whether someone is a hypocrite or not, the real question is whether their argument is logical and makes sense. A doctor who smokes is still correct that smoking is unhealthy.
