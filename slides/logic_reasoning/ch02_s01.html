Chapter 2, Section 1: Ambiguous Words and Why They Cause Confusion Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Ambiguity

The characteristic of a word, phrase, or sentence having two or more possible meanings. It's a "sneaky problem" that often occurs accidentally, leading to misinterpretations.

Context

The surrounding words, ideas, and situation that usually help clarify the intended meaning of an ambiguous word or phrase. However, sometimes context is insufficient.

Ambiguity's Impact on Arguments

When a key word in an argument changes meaning between its premises and conclusion, the argument becomes flawed and illogical, even if it appears sound on the surface.

The Ambiguity Check

A crucial step in evaluating arguments is to identify if important words maintain a consistent meaning throughout. If not, the argument is compromised.

Lexical Ambiguity

Occurs when a single word itself has multiple meanings (e.g., "bank," "bat," "duck"). The word itself is the source of confusion.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Syntactic (Structural) Ambiguity

Occurs when the way words are arranged in a sentence creates multiple possible interpretations, even if individual words are clear (e.g., "The teacher said on Monday she would give a test.").

Precision in Language

Being clear and specific with words is essential to avoid ambiguity, prevent misunderstandings, and construct strong, logical arguments. Ambiguity is fixable through careful rewording.

Important Terms (1/2)

Ambiguity

A situation in which something can be understood in more than one way; having multiple possible meanings.

Context

The circumstances that form the setting for an event, statement, or idea, and in terms of which it can be fully understood and assessed.

Lexical Ambiguity

Ambiguity that arises from a single word having multiple meanings.

Syntactic Ambiguity (Structural Ambiguity)

Ambiguity that arises from the grammatical structure or arrangement of words in a sentence, leading to multiple interpretations.

Premise

A statement or proposition from which another is inferred or follows as a conclusion. In an argument, premises are the reasons offered to support the conclusion.

Important Terms (2/2)

Conclusion

The final statement in an argument, which is affirmed on the basis of the argument's premises.

Words with Double Lives

Bank

A financial institution OR the land alongside a river.

Bat

A nocturnal flying mammal OR a piece of sports equipment.

Light

Not heavy OR illumination.

Bark

The sound a dog makes OR the outer layer of a tree.

Right

Correct OR a direction.

How Ambiguity Derails Arguments

Premise 1

Nothing is better than pizza. (Here, "nothing" means no food item surpasses pizza.)

Premise 2

A soggy sandwich is better than nothing. (Here, "nothing" means the absence of food.)

Conclusion

A soggy sandwich is better than pizza.

Types of Ambiguity

Meaning 1

The act of *you* visiting your relatives can be annoying.

Meaning 2

Your relatives who are visiting *you* can be annoying.
