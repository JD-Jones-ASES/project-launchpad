Inductive Generalization

A specific form of inductive reasoning where a conclusion is made about an entire population based on observations of a smaller group (sample) from that population.

Deductive vs. Inductive Reasoning

Deductive reasoning guarantees a true conclusion if premises are true (airtight); inductive reasoning provides a conclusion that is likely true based on evidence, but not certain.

Strength of Inductions

Inductive arguments vary in their persuasiveness. A strong induction makes the conclusion highly likely, while a weak induction provides poor support.

Important Terms (1/2)

Induction (Inductive Reasoning)

Reasoning from specific observations or examples to a general conclusion that is probably true.

Deductive Reasoning

Reasoning where if the premises are true, the conclusion must be true.

Inductive Generalization

A conclusion about an entire group (population) based on observations of some members of that group (sample).

Population

The entire group about which a generalization is being made.

Sample

The observed members of a group used to make a generalization about the entire population.

Important Terms (2/2)

Strong Induction

An inductive argument where the premises make the conclusion very likely to be true.

Weak Induction

An inductive argument where the premises do not provide great support for the conclusion.

Black Swan Event

A surprising, unexpected event that defies previous generalizations and has a major impact, often leading to a reassessment of prior assumptions.

Strong vs. Weak Inductions

Weak Induction

"I asked my two best friends, and they both hate broccoli. So kids hate broccoli."

Strong Induction
