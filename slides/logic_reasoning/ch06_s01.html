Important Terms

Cognitive Bias

A systematic pattern of thinking that causes your brain to make errors in judgment; an automatic, often unconscious, deviation from rational decision-making.

Heuristic

A mental shortcut or "rule of thumb" used by the brain to simplify complex problems and make quick decisions, saving time and energy but sometimes leading to mistakes.

Confirmation Bias

The psychological phenomenon where individuals tend to favor, remember, and interpret information in a way that confirms their pre-existing beliefs or hypotheses.

Anchoring

A cognitive bias that describes the common human tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information offered (the "anchor") when making decisions.

Availability Heuristic

A mental shortcut that relies on immediate examples that come to a person's mind when evaluating a specific topic, concept, method, or decision.

Confirmation Bias — Seeing What You Want to See

Definition

The tendency to search for, notice, and remember information that agrees with what you already believe, while ignoring or downplaying information that contradicts your beliefs.

Mechanism

It acts like "tinted glasses" for your thoughts, filtering information to match your existing views.

Example (Marcus and Teacher)

Marcus believes his teacher is unfair. Any action the teacher takes (hard test, easy test, extra help) is interpreted by Marcus as proof of her unfairness, disregarding alternative interpretations.

Availability Heuristic — If I Can Think of It, It Must Be Common

Definition

The tendency to judge the likelihood or frequency of an event based on how easily examples of it come to mind. If examples are readily available, the event is perceived as more common.

Mechanism

Dramatic, vivid, recent, or widely publicized events are easier to recall, leading the brain to overestimate their actual probability or prevalence.

Example (Sharks vs. Cows)

Shark attacks are heavily publicized and vivid, making them easy to recall, leading many to believe sharks are more dangerous than cows, even though cows cause more deaths annually in the U.S.
