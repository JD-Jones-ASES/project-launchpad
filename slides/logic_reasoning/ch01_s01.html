Chapter 1, Section 1

Understanding Arguments: Premises and Conclusions

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Logical Argument vs. Disagreement

In logic, an argument is not a quarrel but a structured way of presenting reasons to support a claim. It's about convincing through rational support, not emotional conflict.

Premise

A statement offered as a reason, evidence, or justification for believing something else. Arguments can have one or many premises.

Conclusion

The main statement or claim that the premises are intended to support or prove. Every argument has exactly one conclusion.

Argument Structure

An argument is composed of at least one premise and exactly one conclusion. Think of premises as the "fuel" or "reasons," and the conclusion as the "destination" or "claim."

Signal Words

Specific words or phrases that often indicate whether a statement is a premise or a conclusion. They act like road signs, guiding you through the argument's structure.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Identifying Conclusions (without signals)

If signal words are absent, ask yourself, "Which statement is the author trying to get me to believe?" That statement is the conclusion, and the others are premises.

Important Terms

Argument

In logic, a set of statements in which one or more statements (premises) are offered as reasons to believe another statement (the conclusion).

Premise

A statement provided as a reason or piece of evidence to support the conclusion of an argument.

Conclusion

The statement in an argument that the premises are intended to support or prove; it is the main claim being made.

Identifying Premises and Conclusions: Signal Words

Premise Signal Words

These words often introduce a reason or evidence.

Conclusion Signal Words

These words often introduce the main point or the claim being argued for.

Practice Example Breakdown

Conclusion

"We should have pizza every Friday." (This is the claim the speaker wants to persuade others to accept.)

Premise 1

"Pizza is the most popular food in the cafeteria survey." (Signaled by "since")

Premise 2

"Students perform better when they're happy about lunch." (Signaled by "since")
