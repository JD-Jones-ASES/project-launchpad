Chapter 5, Section 3: Weak Evidence Fallacies — Jumping to Conclusions Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Weak Evidence Fallacies

A group of logical fallacies where the evidence presented, while sounding logical, is actually too minimal or irrelevant to genuinely support the conclusion. These arguments "jump to conclusions."

False Cause (Post Hoc)

The mistaken belief that because one event happened *before* another, the first event *caused* the second. It confuses correlation or sequence with causation.

Slippery Slope

An argument that claims one minor action will inevitably lead to a disastrous chain of increasingly extreme consequences, without providing sufficient evidence for each step in the chain.

Hasty Generalization

Drawing a broad, universal conclusion based on too few specific examples or an unrepresentative sample. It assumes a few cases prove a general rule.

False Dilemma (False Dichotomy / Either/Or Fallacy)

Presenting a situation as having only two options or choices when, in reality, more possibilities exist. It forces a black-and-white choice and ignores other valid alternatives.

Important Terms

False Cause / Post Hoc

A fallacy that assumes temporal sequence implies causation; "after this, therefore because of this."

Slippery Slope

A fallacy where a minor initial action is claimed to lead inevitably to a series of increasingly extreme and negative consequences.

Hasty Generalization

A fallacy of faulty generalization, where a conclusion is drawn from a sample that is too small or not representative enough.

False Dilemma / False Dichotomy / Either/Or Fallacy

A fallacy that presents only two extreme options as the only choices, disregarding other potential alternatives or middle grounds.

Fallacy

From the Latin *fallacia*, meaning "deception" or "trick"; an error in reasoning that makes an argument unsound or invalid.

False Cause — “It Happened After, So It Must Be Because Of”

Definition

The fallacy of False Cause, also known by its Latin name *post hoc, ergo propter hoc*, occurs when someone assumes that because one event occurred *before* another, the first event *caused* the sec...

Explanation

Just because two things happen in sequence does not mean they are causally related. Many events occur consecutively without one causing the other (e.g., sunrise before breakfast).

Key Distinction (from Hasty Generalization)

False Cause is specifically about **cause and effect** – claiming one thing caused another.
