Important Terms (1/2)

Appeal Fallacies

Arguments that rely on human feelings or irrelevant sources to persuade rather than logic or evidence.

Bandwagon Fallacy

An argument that claims something is true, good, or right because it is popular or "everyone else is doing it." Also known as "appeal to popularity."

Appeal to Authority Fallacy

An argument that cites an "authority" who does not have expert knowledge on the specific topic being discussed, making their opinion irrelevant as evidence.

Appeal to Emotion Fallacy

An argument that manipulates the audience's feelings (e.g., pity, anger, joy, guilt) to win agreement, rather than offering sound logical reasons.

Appeal to Fear Fallacy

A type of appeal to emotion that attempts to scare an audience into agreeing with a conclusion by presenting a threat or negative outcome if they don't comply.

Important Terms (2/2)

Logical Fallacy

A flaw in the structure or content of an argument that makes it invalid or unsound, even if the conclusion seems convincing.

Introduction to Appeal Fallacies

Shift from Previous Fallacies

Unlike fallacies that attack a person or twist words, appeal fallacies are "sneakier" because they target our strong emotions.

How They Work

Our feelings (excitement, fear, sympathy) are powerful and can override logical thinking. Appeal fallacies exploit this by making us *feel* something instead of *think* about evidence.

Bandwagon — "Everyone's Doing It!"

Definition

Argues that an idea is correct or good because many people believe it or are engaged in it.

Origin of Name

Comes from the literal image of jumping onto a bandwagon in a parade, following the crowd.

Why it's a Fallacy

Popularity does not equate to truth or quality. Historically, large groups of people have held incorrect beliefs (e.g., Earth as the center of the universe).

Examples

Appeal to Authority — "A Famous Person Said So!"

Definition

Uses a famous or powerful person's opinion as proof, even when that person lacks relevant expertise on the topic.

Crucial Distinction

Example

"My favorite singer posted that pineapple juice cures sore throats. She has 30 million followers." (Popularity or talent in one field does not transfer to medical expertise.)

Spotlight: Fallacies in Political Ads

Common Fallacies Spotted

Self-Assessment Tip

When seeing a political ad, ask: "What evidence did they actually give me? Or did they just try to make me feel something?"

Appeal to Emotion — "But Think of How Sad It Is!"

Definition

Tries to win an argument by eliciting a strong emotion (pity, anger, joy, guilt) rather than providing actual evidence or logical reasons.

Emotions vs. Logic

Emotions are normal and important, but when they are used *as a substitute* for factual support, it becomes a fallacy.

Example

A commercial showing a sad puppy in the rain to solicit donations. (The emotional appeal distracts from asking critical questions about the organization's effectiveness or money usage.)

Appeal to Fear — "Something Terrible Will Happen!"

Definition

Attempts to scare an audience into agreeing with a conclusion by warning of dire consequences if they don't comply.

Impact of Fear

Fear is a powerful emotion that can quickly shut down rational thought and critical evaluation.

Examples

Common Misconceptions (from "teachernote")

Emotion in Arguments

Not all use of emotion is a fallacy. Emotions can be part of a good argument if they complement, rather than replace, evidence. The fallacy occurs when emotion is the *sole* basis for persuasion.

Citing Authority

Citing a genuine expert in their relevant field is valid. The fallacy occurs when the "authority" lacks expertise on the topic being discussed.
