Key Concepts (1/2)

Arguments vs. Non-Arguments

Finding Premises and Conclusions

To identify an argument, look for a claim that someone is trying to convince you of (the conclusion) and the reasons they offer to support that claim (the premises).

Arguments in Everyday Life

Arguments are present in various forms of communication, including casual conversations, narratives (like stories), and news reports.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Aristotle, Father of Logic

An ancient Greek philosopher who was the first to systematically study argument structure, identifying premises and conclusions and setting rules for evaluating arguments.

Important Terms

Argument

A set of statements where one statement (the conclusion) is claimed to follow from, or be supported by, one or more other statements (the premises).

Premise

A statement offered as a reason or evidence in support of a conclusion.

Conclusion

The main claim or point that an argument is trying to establish; the statement that is being supported by the premises.

Non-Argument

A statement or group of statements that does not present a claim supported by reasons. This includes descriptions, questions, commands, and unsupported opinions.

Opinion (without reasons)

A personal belief or preference stated without any supporting evidence or justification, and therefore not an argument.

Not Every Statement is an Argument

Descriptions

They tell you about something without trying to convince you of a particular point. (e.g., "The sky is blue today.")

Questions

They ask for information. (e.g., "What time is it?")

Commands

They tell someone to do something. (e.g., "Close the door.")
