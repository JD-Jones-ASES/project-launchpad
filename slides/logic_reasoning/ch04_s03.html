Important Terms (1/2)

Conditional (statement)

A statement typically expressed as "If P, then Q," where P is the antecedent and Q is the consequent. It asserts that Q is true whenever P is true.

Antecedent (P)

The "if" part of a conditional statement.

Consequent (Q)

The "then" part of a conditional statement.

Modus Ponens

A valid deduction pattern: If P then Q, P is true, therefore Q is true.

Modus Tollens

A valid deduction pattern: If P then Q, Q is not true, therefore P is not true.

Important Terms (2/2)

Affirming the Consequent

An invalid deduction pattern: If P then Q, Q is true, therefore P is true.

Denying the Antecedent

An invalid deduction pattern: If P then Q, P is not true, therefore Q is not true.

Valid Deduction Pattern

A reasoning structure where, if the premises are true, the conclusion *must* also be true.

Invalid Deduction Pattern

A reasoning structure where, even if the premises are true, the conclusion is *not guaranteed* to be true; it might be false.

Modus Ponens: The Classic Move

Meaning

Latin for "the way of affirming." It's the most intuitive form of deduction.

Pattern

Explanation

If we establish a rule that "if P happens, then Q will definitely happen," and then we observe that P *did* happen, we are logically compelled to conclude that Q must also happen.

Examples
