Chapter 3, Section 1: Categorical Statements Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Syllogism

An argument structure consisting of exactly two premises and one conclusion, where all three statements are categorical statements about categories or groups.

Categorical Statement

A statement that describes a relationship between two categories or groups, indicating whether members of one group belong to or are excluded from another group.

Four Standard Forms

Every categorical statement fits one of these four patterns:

Scope

Refers to how many members of a group the statement is talking about.

Quality

Refers to whether the statement affirms or denies a relationship between groups.

Key Concepts (2/2)

"Some" in Logic

This term specifically means "at least one." It does not imply "a few" or "not all"; it merely guarantees that the quantity is greater than zero, and could even include all members.

Important Terms (1/2)

Syllogism

A deductive argument with two premises and one conclusion, composed entirely of categorical statements.

Categorical Statement

A statement asserting a relationship of inclusion or exclusion, either total or partial, between two categories (or groups).

Universal Affirmative

A categorical statement of the form "All A are B." It asserts that every member of category A is a member of category B.

Universal Negative

A categorical statement of the form "No A are B." It asserts that no member of category A is a member of category B.

Particular Affirmative

A categorical statement of the form "Some A are B." It asserts that at least one member of category A is a member of category B.

Important Terms (2/2)

Particular Negative

A categorical statement of the form "Some A are not B." It asserts that at least one member of category A is not a member of category B.

Scope

The property of a categorical statement indicating whether it refers to all members (universal) or at least one member (particular) of a category.

Quality

The property of a categorical statement indicating whether it affirms a relationship (affirmative) or denies a relationship (negative) between categories.

Aristotle

An ancient Greek philosopher credited with developing the systematic study of logic, including the analysis of syllogisms.
