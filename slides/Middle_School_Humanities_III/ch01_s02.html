Important Terms (1/3)

Aristotle

An ancient Greek philosopher whose works were the primary authority on the natural world in medieval Europe for almost two thousand years.

Francis Bacon (1561–1626)

English lawyer, philosopher, and statesman; articulated the inductive method and critiqued reliance on ancient authority.

Idols

Bacon's term for mental habits, prejudices, and biases that distort objective observation and prevent clear understanding of the natural world.

Inductive method

The foundational approach to modern science, advocated by Bacon, which moves from specific observations and experiments to general conclusions. Also known as the "scientific method."

Deduction (Aristotelian)

The traditional method of reasoning that starts with accepted general principles and derives particular conclusions; contrasted by Bacon with the inductive method.

Important Terms (2/3)

Galileo Galilei (1564–1642)

Italian polymath who made significant contributions to astronomy (confirming Earth orbits the Sun) and the study of motion through observation and experimentation, often clashing with established a...

Royal Society (of London for Improving Natural Knowledge)

An organization founded in 1660 and chartered in 1662 by Charles II to promote scientific inquiry through observation and experiment. The oldest continuously operating scientific society in the world.

*Nullius in verba*

The Latin motto of the Royal Society, meaning "Take nobody's word for it," expressing its commitment to evidence over unquestioned authority.

*Philosophical Transactions*

The world's first scientific journal, published by the Royal Society beginning in 1665, facilitating the sharing and verification of experimental results.

Robert Boyle (1627–1691)

Early member of the Royal Society, often called the founder of modern chemistry; developed the air pump and formulated Boyle's Law.

Important Terms (3/3)

Christopher Wren (1632–1723)

Early member of the Royal Society, astronomer and England's greatest architect (e.g., St. Paul's Cathedral).

Isaac Newton (1643–1727)

English physicist and mathematician, a towering figure in the Royal Society; discovered the laws of motion and universal gravitation, invented calculus, and published *Principia Mathematica*.

*Principia Mathematica* (1687)

Isaac Newton's seminal work which presented his theory of universal gravitation and laws of motion, unifying terrestrial and celestial mechanics.

Natural philosophy

The term used in the 17th century for the study of the natural world through observation, experiment, and mathematics, which we now call "science." The word "scientist" was coined much later (1833).

Peer review

A modern scientific practice (descended from the Royal Society's *Nullius in verba*) where scientific findings are critically evaluated by independent experts before acceptance.

Rational Universe

Newton's work showed the universe not as a collection of separate mysteries, but as a single, rational, and orderly system governed by comprehensible laws.

Science and Faith

For Newton, his scientific discoveries were not in conflict with faith. He saw the orderly laws of nature as evidence of a rational Creator, viewing the study of physics as "reading God's mind."
