Key Concepts (1/2)

Elizabethan Settlement (1559–1563)

Queen Elizabeth I's religious compromise that established the Church of England as Protestant while retaining some Catholic forms, aiming for broad national unity and peace.

Defeat of the Spanish Armada (1588)

A pivotal naval victory for England against Philip II of Spain, which secured England's Protestant identity, fostered a sense of national pride, and solidified its emerging sea power.

The belief, strongly held by James I and Charles I, that monarchs derive their authority directly from God and are therefore accountable to God alone, not to Parliament or their subjects.

The Elizabethan Settlement (1558-1603)

Context of Religious Conflict

Elizabeth I inherited a kingdom exhausted by rapid religious changes under her predecessors (Henry VIII, Edward VI, Mary I). England's official religion had changed four times in 25 years.

Elizabeth's Solution

The Elizabethan Settlement (1559-1563) was a deliberate compromise.

Philosophy

Elizabeth famously stated she would not "open windows into men's souls," prioritizing outward conformity over private belief.

Outcome

Mary Queen of Scots and the Spanish Armada (1/2)

Mary Queen of Scots

Elizabeth's Catholic cousin and a claimant to the English throne, she became the focus of Catholic plots against Elizabeth. After fleeing Scotland, she was imprisoned by Elizabeth for 19 years.

Execution

After evidence emerged of Mary's involvement in a plot to assassinate Elizabeth, the queen reluctantly signed her death warrant, and Mary was executed in 1587.

Philip II's Response

The execution enraged Philip II of Spain, a powerful Catholic monarch who had long planned an invasion of England to restore Catholicism.

The English Civil War and the Commonwealth (1642-1660) (1/2)

Outbreak of War (1642)

England divided into **Cavaliers** (Royalists) and **Roundheads** (Parliamentarians).

Parliament's Victory

The **New Model Army**, reorganized and disciplined under Sir Thomas Fairfax and Oliver Cromwell, proved decisive.

Execution of Charles I (1649)

Charles was captured, tried by a special court, and publicly beheaded. This was unprecedented in European history, marking the first time a reigning monarch was executed by his own subjects.

The Commonwealth (1649-1653)

England became a republic, governed by Parliament and a Council of State, without a king.

The Protectorate (1653-1659)

Oliver Cromwell, a military hero and fervent Puritan, became **Lord Protector**. He ruled with near-kingly powers but grew increasingly authoritarian, dissolving Parliament when it opposed him.

The English Civil War and the Commonwealth (1642-1660) (2/2)

Collapse

After Cromwell's death in 1658, his son Richard proved unable to maintain authority, and the Protectorate collapsed.

Charles II's Catholic brother became king. James appointed Catholics to key positions, suspended anti-Catholic laws, and seemed intent on returning England to Rome.

Crisis of Succession

The birth of a son to James II in June 1688 guaranteed a Catholic succession, alarming leading Protestants.
