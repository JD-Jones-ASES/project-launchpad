The Spark: A Shot in Sarajevo (June 28, 1914)

The Event

Archduke Franz Ferdinand, heir to the Austro-Hungarian throne, and his wife Sophie were assassinated in Sarajevo.

The Assassin

Gavrilo Princip, a 19-year-old Bosnian nationalist, fired the fatal shots.

Immediate Aftermath

The Archduke and his wife died quickly.

Rapid Escalation: The Alliance System in Action

Austro-Hungarian Response

Blamed Serbia for the assassination and issued an ultimatum with extreme demands, designed to be refused.

Declaration of War

Serbia refused one clause, leading Austria-Hungary to declare war.

Chain Reaction

Outcome

Within six weeks, a regional Balkan quarrel became a war involving most of the world's major powers.

Why Did the Alliance System Fail?

Intended Purpose

Alliances were designed to deter war by making aggression too costly.

Reasons for Failure

Reality

The war, expected to be over by Christmas 1914, lasted over four years.

The Western Front: Stalemate and Attrition (1/2)

The Schlieffen Plan's Failure

Germany's plan for a rapid defeat of France through Belgium nearly succeeded, reaching within 30 miles of Paris.

Halted at the Marne

French and British forces stopped the German advance at the First Battle of the Marne (September 1914).

Race to the Sea

Both sides attempted to outflank each other, leading to a continuous line of trenches.

Trench Lines

By November 1914, a 700-kilometer line of trenches stretched from the English Channel to the Swiss border.

The Western Front remained largely static for four years, with minimal territorial gains despite massive casualties.
