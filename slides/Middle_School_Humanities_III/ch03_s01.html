Important Terms (2/2)

Sovereignty

The full right and power of a governing body over itself, without any interference from outside sources or bodies. It means a state has exclusive authority within its territorial boundaries.

The First Modern Peace Conference

Unprecedented Gathering

In 1643, ambassadors gathered in Osnabrück and Münster for peace negotiations, marking the first time nearly all major European powers simultaneously negotiated a comprehensive peace.

Scale and Duration

Over a hundred delegations participated in negotiations that lasted five years, culminating in treaties signed in 1648.

Impact

The treaties ended both the Thirty Years' War and the Eighty Years' War, but more importantly, they created a new framework for international politics.

Protocol

The artwork "The Ratification of the Treaty of Münster" by ter Borch illustrates the formal protocol, emphasizing the equal standing of all nations present.

Before Westphalia: Universal Authorities

Rival Claims

European politics had been dominated by two universal claims to authority:

Weakening Claims

These claims had been eroding for centuries due to the Protestant Reformation (shattering church unity) and the Habsburgs' inability to enforce imperial authority universally.

Westphalia's Role

The Peace of Westphalia delivered the final blow to these universal claims, ensuring that after 1648, neither the Emperor nor the Pope held authority over the internal affairs of sovereign states.

The Peace of Westphalia and its Core Principles

Foundational Treaties

The treaties signed in Osnabrück and Münster in 1648.

Key Principles Established

Legacy

These principles remain the bedrock of international relations and are often called the "birth certificate of the modern state system."

The Balance of Power

New Concept

Introduced at the Congress of Westphalia, it shaped European diplomacy for centuries.

Core Idea

No single nation should become so powerful that it can dictate terms to others.

Mechanism

If one power grows too strong, other nations form coalitions or alliances to check its influence, maintaining a rough equilibrium of power.

Limitations

Improvement

Despite its flaws, it was a significant improvement over the prior chaos, preventing devastation on the scale of the Thirty Years' War until 1914.

The Rise of France

Primary Beneficiary

France emerged as the dominant power in continental Europe, largely due to Cardinal Richelieu's strategy of opposing the Habsburgs.

Louis XIV (1643-1715)

Relentlessly exploited France's newfound advantage.
