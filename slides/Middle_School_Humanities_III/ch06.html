Important Terms (1/3)

Industrial Revolution

Transformation from agriculture and hand-production to machine manufacturing and factory labor; began in Britain c. 1760-1780; spread across Europe through the 19th century.

Social question

The 19th-century debate over how industrial society should respond to poverty, labor exploitation, and inequality; generated competing answers from liberal capitalism, socialism, and Catholic Socia...

Karl Marx (1818-1883)

German philosopher and author of The Communist Manifesto (1848) and Das Kapital; diagnosed industrial capitalism's exploitation accurately; prescribed catastrophically wrong solution of violent rev...

Communist Manifesto

Published February 1848 by Marx and Engels; argued all history is class struggle; predicted inevitable proletarian revolution; prescribed violent overthrow of capitalism and abolition of private pr...

Bourgeoisie

In Marxist analysis, the class owning the means of production (factories, land, capital); the capitalist ruling class.

Important Terms (2/3)

Proletariat

In Marxist analysis, the working class owning no property; must sell their labor to survive; Marx predicted they would overthrow the bourgeoisie.

Class struggle

Marx's central concept: the conflict between economic classes (owners vs. workers) is the engine of all historical change.

Revolutions of 1848

Wave of European revolutions in 1848; mostly led by liberal middle class, not factory workers; all suppressed within two years.

Rerum Novarum

Pope Leo XIII's 1891 encyclical "On the Condition of Labor"; founding document of Catholic Social Teaching; rejected both socialism and laissez-faire capitalism.

Imago Dei

Latin "image of God" (Genesis 1:27); every human being possesses inherent dignity that no economic system may violate; the theological foundation of Rerum Novarum.

Important Terms (3/3)

Catholic Social Teaching

Body of official Church teaching on social, economic, and political questions beginning with Rerum Novarum; principles include human dignity, common good, private property, option for the poor, and...

Laissez-faire

"Let it do"; economic philosophy of government non-interference in markets; rejected by Rerum Novarum because workers are persons, not commodities.

Subsidiarity

The principle that decisions should be made at the most local level capable of handling them; prevents both state overcentralization and complete market anarchy.

Pope Pius X (1835-1914)

Leo XIII's successor; focused on condemning theological "Modernism" within the Church; died days after WWI began.
