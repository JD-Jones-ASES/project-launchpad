Important Terms (1/3)

Defenestration of Prague (1618)

The act of throwing three Catholic officials from a castle window in Prague by Protestant Bohemian nobles; triggered the Thirty Years' War.

Raison d'etat

French for "reason of state"; the idea that governments may take morally questionable actions if they serve national interest; associated with Machiavelli and Richelieu.

Edict of Restitution (1629)

Emperor Ferdinand II's demand that Protestants return Catholic church property seized since 1552; appeared to signal total Catholic victory but provoked Swedish intervention.

Gustavus Adolphus

Swedish king called the "Lion of the North" who reformed the Swedish army and intervened decisively on the Protestant side; killed at the Battle of Lutzen (1632).

Peace of Westphalia (1648)

The peace settlement ending the Thirty Years' War, negotiated in Osnabruck and Munster; established sovereignty, legal equality, and non-interference as the principles of international relations.

Important Terms (2/3)

Sovereignty

The supreme authority of a government over its own territory and people, free from outside control; the central principle of international relations established at Westphalia.

International law

A body of rules governing relations between nations; founded by Grotius's Rights of War and Peace (1625), rooted in Christian natural law tradition.

Hugo Grotius (1583-1645)

Dutch legal scholar; "father of international law"; argued nations are subject to natural law in The Rights of War and Peace (1625).

Absolute monarchy

A system in which the monarch holds supreme, unrestricted power; Louis XIV of France is the classic example.

Louis XIV (1638-1715)

French king who embodied absolute monarchy; ruled 72 years; built Versailles; revoked the Edict of Nantes (1685).
