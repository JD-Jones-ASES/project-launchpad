Important Terms (1/3)

Miguel de Cervantes Saavedra (1547–1616)

Renowned Spanish novelist, playwright, and poet. A veteran of the Battle of Lepanto and former slave, he is best known for Don Quixote.

Don Quixote (the novel)

A two-part novel (1605, 1615) by Cervantes, widely considered the first modern novel and the greatest work in the Spanish language.

Alonso Quijano / Don Quixote de la Mancha

The protagonist of Cervantes's novel, an hidalgo who reads so many chivalric romances that he loses his sanity and sets out to revive knighthood.

Sancho Panza

Don Quixote's loyal squire, who often represents common sense and practicality, contrasting with Don Quixote's idealism.

Battle of Lepanto (1571)

A decisive naval battle in which a Holy League fleet (including Spanish forces) defeated the Ottoman Empire. Cervantes was wounded in this battle.

Important Terms (2/3)

Barbary Pirates

Mediterranean pirates who captured Cervantes on his voyage home from war and held him in Algiers as a slave for five years.

Tilting at Windmills

The most famous episode from Don Quixote, where the protagonist attacks windmills, believing them to be giants, a metaphor for attacking imaginary enemies or pursuing impossible ideals.

Bartolomé de las Casas (1484–1566)

A Spanish Dominican friar, historian, and social reformer who became an early advocate for the rights of indigenous peoples in the Americas.

A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies (1542)

Las Casas's groundbreaking historical work addressed to Prince Philip, detailing the atrocities committed by Spanish colonists against indigenous populations and arguing for their protection.

Imago Dei (Image of God)

A theological concept that asserts that human beings are created in God's image, used by Las Casas to argue for the inherent dignity and rights of indigenous peoples.

Introduction: Two Witnesses to an Empire

• Miguel de Cervantes and Bartolomé de las Casas both lived through the peak and beginning of the decline of the Spanish Empire.

• Their writings are essential for understanding the empire "from the inside."

• Cervantes (1547–1616) experienced military heroism, capture, slavery, and poverty, eventually writing Don Quixote.

• Las Casas (1484–1566) was a priest who championed indigenous rights, arguing against colonial exploitation.

• Together, they offer a spectrum of the Spanish experience: comedy and tragedy, laughter and grief.

Reading These Texts Together

• Cervantes and Las Casas were contemporaries (Cervantes: 1547–1616; Las Casas: 1484–1566).

• They both observed Spain's rise to greatness and the undeniable costs of its empire.

• Cervantes personally suffered for Spain's empire, while Las Casas fought its worst excesses from within the Church.

• Neither abandoned Spain or its faith; instead, they used their love for their nation and religion to challenge its evolving nature.

• Their combination of loyalty and protest, celebration and grief, makes them enduring figures in Spanish literature and the moral history of Western civilization.
