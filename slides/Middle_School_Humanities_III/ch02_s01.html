Unprecedented Reach

Territorial Scale

It controlled more land than any previous empire, knit together by Spanish ships, soldiers, priests, and administrators.

Engine of Empire

The primary economic driver was the extraction of mineral wealth, especially silver, from its American colonies.

Viceroyalties

The two main administrative divisions, New Spain (Mexico and Central America) and Peru (South America), were organized specifically for this purpose.

The Silver of Potosí

Discovery

In 1545, Spanish explorers discovered Cerro Rico ("Rich Mountain") in present-day Bolivia, which contained the largest silver deposit ever found.

City Growth

The city of Potosí rapidly grew around the mine, reaching a population of 160,000, surpassing London and any other city in the Americas at the time.

Global Connection

This annual trade route linked Acapulco (Mexico) to Manila (Philippines) and on to China, forming one of the longest-running trade routes in history (1565-1815).

Commodities Exchanged

New World silver (from Mexico and Peru) was transported across the Pacific to Manila, where it was exchanged for Chinese silk, porcelain, and spices.

The Columbian Exchange

Definition

The transfer of plants, animals, diseases, and people between the Americas and the rest of the world following Columbus's voyages in 1492, named by historian Alfred Crosby.

Impact on Global Diets

Disease Catastrophe

Unintended Consequences

The Columbian Exchange was a complex interaction of biological systems isolated for millennia, resulting in widespread and often unforeseen consequences far beyond any individual human decisions.

Society in the Spanish Empire: The Casta System

Racial Hierarchy

The Spanish colonial world was structured by a rigid system of racial classification, the casta system, which determined social status, legal rights, and economic obligations.

Hierarchy

Codified System

The casta system was formalized in law and daily social practice, regulating aspects like marriage, professions, clothing, and tribute payments.

Pinturas de Castas

Colonial Mexican artists created elaborate paintings depicting various racial mixtures and their assigned labels, highlighting the societal obsession with racial classification.

Maintaining Control

This system served to maintain Spanish colonial control by dividing society into distinct groups with differing interests and statuses, making collective resistance more difficult.
