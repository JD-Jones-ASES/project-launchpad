Key Concepts (1/2)

Who are the "three witnesses" to the age of expansion discussed in this section's literature?

Olaudah Equiano, Bartolomé de las Casas, and William Shakespeare.

What overarching themes do the three literary texts in this section invite readers to consider?

Power, conscience, and the cost of keeping one's eyes open to difficult realities.

What historical event is central to Olaudah Equiano's personal narrative and abolitionist argument?

The Middle Passage, which he survived as an enslaved person.

What intellectual tools did Olaudah Equiano use to make a moral case for abolition in his Narrative?

The tools of the Enlightenment, including reason, natural rights, and literary skill.

Besides being a memoir, what other functions did Olaudah Equiano's "The Interesting Narrative" serve?

It was a spiritual autobiography and a powerful political argument against slavery.

What was Equiano's rhetorical strategy in describing his African childhood in the ethnographic mode?

To humanize enslaved people by showing they had civilization, history, and culture, thereby implicitly answering arguments that Africans were 'barbaric.'

Key Concepts (2/2)

Describe the immediate physical and psychological impact of being brought aboard a slave ship, as recounted by Equiano.

He was filled with astonishment quickly turning to terror, handled 'to see if I were sound,' and persuaded he had entered a 'world of bad spirits.'

What two distinct traditions did Equiano combine in his argument against slavery?

Enlightenment natural rights philosophy (e.g., Locke) and the Christian understanding of human dignity rooted in imago Dei (being made in the image of God).

Who was Bartolomé de las Casas and what was his primary goal regarding the Spanish conquest of the Americas?

He was a Spanish priest who witnessed conquest and enslavement and used Christian theology to condemn it, campaigning against systems like the encomienda.
