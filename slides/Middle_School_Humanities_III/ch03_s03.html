Important Terms (1/4)

Defenestration of Prague

The 1618 event where Protestant Bohemian nobles threw three Catholic royal officials from a castle window, directly triggering the Thirty Years' War.

Holy Roman Empire

A vast, decentralized collection of Catholic and Protestant territories in central Europe, whose internal religious divisions were a primary cause of the Thirty Years' War.

Ferdinand II

Holy Roman Emperor during the Bohemian Phase, a devout Catholic determined to re-Catholicize his empire.

Edict of Restitution (1629)

Issued by Emperor Ferdinand II, demanding Protestants return all Catholic church property seized since 1552, escalating the conflict.

Gustavus Adolphus

King of Sweden, a brilliant military commander ("Lion of the North") who revolutionized army tactics and led Protestant forces during the Swedish Phase.

Important Terms (2/4)

Cardinal Richelieu

Chief minister of Catholic France, who secretly and then openly funded Protestant armies against the Catholic Habsburgs, exemplifying raison d'état.

Magdeburg

A city sacked by Imperial troops in 1631, resulting in the massacre of some 20,000 civilians and the city's destruction, becoming a symbol of the war's atrocities.

Peace of Westphalia (1648)

The treaties signed in Osnabrück and Münster that officially ended the Thirty Years' War and established fundamental principles of modern international law.

Sovereignty

The supreme authority of a government over its own territory and people, free from outside control; a cornerstone principle established by the Peace of Westphalia.

Hugo Grotius (1583–1645)

Dutch scholar, author of The Rights of War and Peace, considered the "father of international law" for his arguments on natural law binding nations.

Important Terms (3/4)

Louis XIV (1638–1715)

The "Sun King" of France, whose seventy-two-year reign embodied absolute monarchy and saw France become the dominant power in Europe.

Versailles

The spectacular palace built by Louis XIV, used to centralize power and weaken the French nobility by drawing them to court.

Jean-Baptiste Colbert

Louis XIV's chief minister, who reorganized the French economy to serve the state.

"L'État, c'est moi"

French for "The state, it is I," a phrase attributed to Louis XIV, perfectly capturing his philosophy of absolute rule.

Edict of Nantes (1598)

A decree issued by Henry IV of France, granting religious tolerance and protections to Huguenots.

Important Terms (4/4)

Huguenots

French Protestants, mostly Calvinist. Their religious freedoms were revoked by Louis XIV in 1685, leading to a massive diaspora.

Huguenot diaspora

The forced exodus of 200,000 to 400,000 Huguenots from France after Louis XIV revoked the Edict of Nantes, strengthening rival nations and weakening France.

War of Spanish Succession (1701–1714)

A major European conflict fought over the succession to the Spanish throne after the death of the childless King Charles II of Spain, against Louis XIV's ambitions.

Treaty of Utrecht (1713)

The peace treaty that ended the War of Spanish Succession, establishing a new balance of power in Europe and limiting French expansion.

The Thirty Years' War (1618–1648)

• Often called the "last of the religious wars" and the "first of the modern wars."

• Began over religious conflict but evolved into a struggle for political power and territorial advantage.

• Resulted in an estimated eight million deaths from combat, famine, and plague.

The Devastation of Germany

• Germany served as the primary battlefield for three decades, enduring constant marching armies, sieges, and widespread destruction.

• Armies from all sides looted the countryside for sustenance, leading to widespread famine.

• Crowding in cities during sieges and displacements facilitated the spread of plague.

• The sacking of Magdeburg (1631) by Imperial troops, which killed approximately 20,000 civilians and burned the city, became a notorious symbol of the war's brutality.

• By the war's end, Germany's population had declined by an estimated one-quarter to one-third, primarily from starvation and disease, with some regions losing over half their inhabitants. Recovery t...

• The war's immense human cost, especially given it was fought in the name of Christianity, raised profound moral questions about human nature and religious conflict.

Hugo Grotius and the Birth of International Law

• Hugo Grotius, a Dutch scholar, published The Rights of War and Peace (De Jure Belli ac Pacis, 1625) during the war.

• He argued that nations, like individuals, are bound by a natural law that dictates certain moral limitations even in war (e.g., forbidding unprovoked civilian killings, treaty breaches).

• Drawing on Roman law, Aquinas's natural law tradition, and Scripture, Grotius asserted a rational, moral order for humanity.

• His ideas influenced the Peace of Westphalia and earned him the title "father of international law."

Louis XIV and Absolute Monarchy

Louis XIV (1638–1715)

, reigning for 72 years, embodied a new ideal of kingship: absolute monarchy.

Revocation of the Edict of Nantes (1685)

Louis XIV, in a move of religious intolerance, revoked the 1598 edict that had protected French Protestants (Huguenots).

Wars of Expansion and Balance of Power

• Louis XIV's ambitions led to costly wars, such as the War of Spanish Succession (1701–1714), fought to prevent France from gaining control over the Spanish throne.

• The conflict ended with the Treaty of Utrecht (1713), which established a new balance of power in Europe, limiting French expansion and ensuring no single power could dominate the continent.

Key Concepts (1/2)

When and where did the Westphalian peace negotiations begin, and how long did they last?

They began in 1643 in Osnabrück and Münster and lasted five years, culminating in treaties signed in 1648.

What made the Westphalian peace negotiations unprecedented in European history?

Representatives of nearly all major European powers sat down simultaneously for the first time to negotiate a comprehensive peace.

How was European politics organized before the Peace of Westphalia?

Around two rival claims to universal authority: the Holy Roman Emperor (secular head of Christendom) and the Pope (spiritual supremacy).

What historical events had already weakened the universal authority claims of the Holy Roman Emperor and the Pope before Westphalia?

The Reformation had shattered the church's unity, and the Habsburgs had struggled to enforce imperial authority universally.

Define the Peace of Westphalia.

What foundational principles of international relations were established by the Peace of Westphalia?

State sovereignty, the legal equality of states, and non-interference in internal affairs.

The birth certificate of the modern state system.

Which nation emerged as the biggest winner of the Thirty Years' War, and why?

France, because Cardinal Richelieu's strategy of backing the Habsburgs' enemies paid off by weakening Spain and fragmenting the Holy Roman Empire.

He was Louis XIV's chief economic minister who reorganized the French economy according to mercantilism.
