Important Terms (1/2)

Bourgeoisie

According to Marx, the capitalist ruling class that owns the means of production and exploits the working class.

Proletariat

The working class, who do not own the means of production and must sell their labor to survive; the "oppressed" in Marx's framework.

Laissez-faire

An economic doctrine advocating minimal government intervention in private business and the free market.

Just Wage

A wage that is sufficient to allow a worker to live with reasonable dignity and support their family, a concept central to Catholic Social Teaching as articulated in *Rerum Novarum*.

Natural Law

A system of right or justice held to be common to all humankind and derived from nature rather than from the rules of society, providing a moral check on power according to the critique of Marx.

Important Terms (2/2)

Materialism

The philosophical view that everything in existence is ultimately physical matter; in the context of Marx, it refers to his reduction of human beings to economic units.

Utopianism

The belief in or pursuit of a state in which everything is perfect, especially in a political or social sense; criticized as a flaw in Marx's vision of a perfect society on earth.

Imago Dei

A Latin theological term meaning "image of God," referring to the belief that human beings are created in God's likeness, which forms the basis for human dignity in Catholic Social Teaching.

Rerum Novarum

A papal encyclical issued by Pope Leo XIII in 1891, addressing the condition of labor and articulating principles of Catholic Social Teaching regarding workers' rights and social justice.

The Communist Manifesto

A foundational text of communism, written by Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels in 1848, outlining the theory of class struggle and calling for proletarian revolution.

Fyodor Dostoevsky: "The Grand Inquisitor" (1880) (1/2)

Context

A chapter within Dostoevsky's acclaimed novel *The Brothers Karamazov*, told by the atheist brother Ivan to the devout Alyosha, offering a profound exploration of freedom, power, and faith.

Setting

Sixteenth-century Seville during the Spanish Inquisition, where Christ returns, performs a miracle, and is promptly arrested by the aged, cynical Grand Inquisitor.

The Inquisitor's Argument: Christ's Error

The Inquisitor explains why the Church "corrected" Christ's work. Christ's fundamental "error" was to offer human beings *freedom*.

Rejection of Temptations

The Inquisitor recalls Christ's refusal of Satan's temptations in the wilderness: bread (material security), worldly power, and miraculous authority. Christ chose freedom over these.

Humanity's Fear of Freedom

The Inquisitor argues that human beings are *not strong enough for freedom*. They *want* bread, miracles, and someone to tell them what to do. They fear the "weight of their own freedom."
