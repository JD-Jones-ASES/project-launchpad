Important Terms (1/3)

Elizabethan Settlement

The religious compromise of 1559-1563 making England Protestant while retaining Catholic worship forms; aimed at peace through outward conformity rather than enforced belief.

Divine right of kings

The belief that a monarch's authority comes from God alone and cannot be limited by Parliament; used by James I and Charles I to justify arbitrary rule.

Petition of Right (1628)

Parliament's declaration of specific royal abuses demanding Charles I acknowledge limits on his authority; a foundational constitutional document alongside Magna Carta.

Personal Rule (1629-1640)

Charles I's eleven-year period of governing without Parliament, funded by controversial taxes like ship money; ended by the Scottish rebellion.

Cavaliers and Roundheads

The two sides of the English Civil War; Cavaliers supported the king, Roundheads supported Parliament (named for their short-cropped hair).

Important Terms (2/3)

New Model Army

Parliament's reorganized army under Fairfax and Cromwell; its discipline proved decisive in the Civil War.

Commonwealth (1649-1653)

The republican government after Charles I's execution; England governed by Parliament and a Council of State without a king.

Protectorate (1653-1659)

Oliver Cromwell's government as Lord Protector; near-royal power without the crown; collapsed after his death.

Restoration (1660)

The return of Charles II and the monarchy; also restored the Church of England and the theaters.

Glorious Revolution (1688)

The nearly bloodless transfer of the crown from James II to William and Mary; permanently established Parliamentary supremacy.

Important Terms (3/3)

English Bill of Rights (1689)

The conditions Parliament imposed on William and Mary; prohibited royal suspension of laws, arbitrary taxation, and standing armies without Parliament; banned excessive bail and cruel punishments.

Parliamentary supremacy

The principle that Parliament is the supreme law-making authority and no monarch may override Acts of Parliament.

Habeas corpus

The legal protection requiring the government to charge a prisoner and bring them before a court; Latin for "you shall have the body"; strengthened by the 1679 Act.

Areopagitica (1644)

Milton's argument against pre-publication censorship; first great defense of freedom of the press in English.
