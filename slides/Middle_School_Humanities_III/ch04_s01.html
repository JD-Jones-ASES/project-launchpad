Important Terms (1/2)

Enlightenment

An influential intellectual and philosophical movement in 18th-century Europe emphasizing reason, individualism, and skepticism of tradition and religious dogma.

Divine Right of Kings

A political doctrine asserting that monarchs derive their authority directly from God and are not accountable to any earthly authority, often used to justify absolute rule.

Glorious Revolution (1688)

A bloodless revolution in England that established the supremacy of Parliament over the monarch and significantly influenced Locke's political writings.

Part II: The Method of Doubt

Key Concepts (1/2)

What was the Enlightenment?

An intellectual movement (c. 1680–1800) centered on the belief that human reason, properly applied, could improve government, society, religion, and morality.

How did the Scientific Revolution influence the Enlightenment?

Isaac Newton's discovery of universal gravitation suggested the universe was orderly, rational, and knowable, inspiring Enlightenment thinkers (philosophes) ...

What was the term for Enlightenment thinkers, particularly in France?

Philosophes.

Name some of the Enlightenment's greatest achievements and its greatest failure.

Achievements include constitutional government, religious toleration, and the doctrine of natural rights. Its greatest failure was the French Revolution.

What foundational philosophical technique did René Descartes develop?

The Method of Doubt, systematically doubting every belief until he reached something that could not be doubted.

What famous phrase encapsulated René Descartes's discovery of an undeniable truth?

“I think, therefore I am” (cogito, ergo sum).

Key Concepts (2/2)

How did Descartes's philosophy shift the focus of knowledge?

By making the individual thinking mind the starting point, it shifted focus away from God, Scripture, and tradition toward human reason.

What was John Locke's most important political work, and what question did it address?

Two Treatises of Government (1689), which asked: what is government, and why should anyone obey it?

An imagined condition before government where all humans are naturally equal and free, possessing natural rights to life, liberty, and property.
