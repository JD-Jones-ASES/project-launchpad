The American Revolution and Its Contradiction

Declaration of Independence (1776)

Proclaimed that "all men are created equal" and possess unalienable rights to "life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness," drawing heavily from John Locke's Enlightenment philosophy.

Profound Contradiction

The Abolitionist Movement (1/2)

Moral Achievement

One of the great moral achievements of the 18th and 19th centuries.

Leadership

Led significantly by Christians (Quakers were early proponents) and a broad coalition of Protestant Christians, Enlightenment rationalists, and formerly enslaved people.

Arguments

Based on both Christian moral principles (all people bear the image of God) and Enlightenment natural rights philosophy (all people are born free).

Key Figures

William Wilberforce, an evangelical Christian and Member of Parliament, led the parliamentary campaign in Britain for decades.

Powerful Testimony

The Opium Wars: Empire by Other Means (1/2)

Trade Imbalance

China had little interest in European goods, while Europeans (especially the British) coveted Chinese tea, silk, and porcelain, leading to a significant British trade deficit.

The Solution: Opium

The British East India Company began illegally selling opium, grown in India, to China.

Social Crisis

By the 1830s, widespread opium addiction became a major social problem in China.

Conflict

When the Chinese government tried to stop the trade by seizing and destroying opium shipments, Britain responded with military force.

First Opium War (1839–42)

Ended with China's defeat, forced to open five ports to British trade, pay reparations, and cede Hong Kong to Britain.
