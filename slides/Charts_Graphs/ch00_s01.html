Why Visuals Matter: A Study Guide

Key Concepts

Visual Processing Speed

The human brain can process an image in as little as 13 milliseconds, which is far faster than reading rows of numbers. This is why charts communicate patterns more quickly than data tables.

Charts Reveal Patterns

A well-made chart makes patterns in data almost instantly visible. The same data presented as a table requires much more effort to interpret.

A Brief History of Visualization (1/2)

• Ancient cave painters recorded animal herds on rock walls

• Medieval mapmakers drew the known world on parchment

• The oldest known data visualization is a 10th-century astronomical chart showing planetary positions

• Ancient bone tools may have tracked data as far back as 20,000 years ago

• William Playfair invented the bar chart (1786), line graph (1780s), and pie chart (1801)

• Florence Nightingale used charts to advocate for better conditions for soldiers

Key Concepts (1/2)

Why do charts help us understand data faster than tables?

The human brain processes visual information incredibly fast — in as little as 13 milliseconds — much faster than reading rows of numbers.

Who invented the bar chart, and when?

William Playfair, a Scottish engineer and economist, invented the bar chart in 1786.

Who invented the pie chart, and when?

William Playfair invented the pie chart in 1801.

What three chart types did William Playfair invent?

The bar chart (1786), the line graph (around the same time), and the pie chart (1801).

What was William Playfair's stated goal for his charts?

To make 'as much information as possible visible at a single glance.'

When did William Playfair live?

1759–1823. He was a Scottish engineer and economist.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How did Florence Nightingale use data visualization?

She used charts to save soldiers' lives by making health data visible and persuasive.

What is the oldest known data visualization?

A 10th-century astronomical chart discovered in a medieval manuscript, showing planetary positions across time.

How far back might data tracking with tallying tools go?

Researchers believe ancient bone tools may have been used for tallying and tracking data as far back as 20,000 years ago.

Why did Playfair start making charts?

He grew tired of looking at endless tables of trade data, so he started drawing pictures instead.
