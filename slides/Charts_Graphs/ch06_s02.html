Donut Charts: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Donut Chart Definition

A circular chart with a hole in the center, where the ring is divided into segments representing proportions of a whole, just like a pie chart.

Difference from Pie Charts

The primary distinction is the central hole, which is a design choice and does not represent missing data.

Purpose of the Center Hole

The hole is often used to display a key number, total, percentage, or label, providing immediate context or a summary metric for the chart.

Design Advantages

Many designers find donut charts visually cleaner, more modern, and less cluttered than filled pie charts, making them popular in dashboards and infographics.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Reading a Donut Chart

Involves interpreting the title, the central number/label, segment sizes, legends, and percentages to understand the data distribution.

Important Terms

Donut Chart

A circular data visualization chart with a central cutout, displaying proportional data in its segments.

Segment

Each colored section of a donut chart (or pie chart) that represents a specific category's proportion of the whole.

Legend

A key that identifies what each color or pattern in a chart represents, linking it to a specific data category.

Dashboard

A visual display of key performance indicators (KPIs) and data, often interactive, used to monitor and analyze information at a glance. Donut charts are frequently used in dashboards.

Infographic

A visual representation of information or data, designed to present complex information quickly and clearly. Donut charts are common components of infographics.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is a donut chart?

A donut chart is a circular chart with a hole in the center. The ring is divided into colored segments, each representing a proportion of the whole, exactly ...

How does a donut chart visually differ from a traditional pie chart?

A donut chart has a hole in its center, while a pie chart is a solid, filled-in circle.

What is the primary purpose of the hole in a donut chart?

The hole provides convenient space to display a key number, total, or label that helps the reader understand the chart at a glance.

What kind of information is typically displayed in the center of a donut chart?

A total sum (e.g., "Total: $500"), a key percentage (e.g., "65% Complete"), or a descriptive label.

What is one key advantage of a donut chart in terms of visual design?

Many designers feel donut charts look more modern and less cluttered than filled pie charts, especially in digital dashboards.

Do donut charts follow the same data rules as pie charts?

Yes, donut charts follow all the same rules: segments must sum to 100%, use six or fewer categories for best readability, and do not use 3D effects.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is a common misconception about the hole in a donut chart?

Students sometimes think the hole represents missing data. Clarify that the hole is purely a design choice; the ring and its segments contain all the data.

Are donut charts always better than pie charts?

No, the choice depends on context. Donut charts are popular in dashboards where a key number can be placed in the center, but both have their uses.

What is the first step when reading any donut chart?

Read the title to understand what the whole ring represents.

After reading the title, what should you check on a donut chart?

Check the center. Is there a number or label in the middle? What does it tell you?

How do you identify what each colored segment in a donut chart signifies?

By reading the legend or the segment labels.

What percentage should all the segments of a donut chart add up to?

All segments must add up to 100% to represent a complete whole.
