Important Terms

100% Stacked Bar Chart

A type of stacked bar chart where each bar, representing a category, is scaled to a uniform height of 100%. Each segment within the bar shows its value as a proportion (percentage) of that category...

Proportion/Percentage

A part, share, or number considered in comparative relation to a whole. In these charts, segments are expressed as percentages of their respective bar's total.

Segment

An individual colored section within a stacked bar, representing a specific component or sub-category of the whole.

Category

The distinct groups or classifications being compared, represented by individual bars in the chart (e.g., "Middle School," "High School").

Absolute Value/Total Count

The actual numerical quantity or sum of items within a category. This information is not directly available or comparable from a 100% stacked bar chart.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is a 100% stacked bar chart?

A stacked bar chart where every bar is scaled to reach exactly 100%. Each segment shows its value as a proportion (percentage) of the whole.

What is the primary purpose of a 100% stacked bar chart?

To easily compare how the mix of parts (proportions) differs across various categories, regardless of the total size of each group.

What is the most distinct visual characteristic of all bars in a 100% stacked bar chart?

Every bar is scaled to reach the same height, representing 100% of its respective group's total.

How does a 100% stacked bar chart differ from a regular stacked bar chart in terms of what it emphasizes?

A 100% stacked chart emphasizes proportions within groups, while a regular stacked chart allows comparison of both total counts and proportions.

What key information do you gain by using a 100% stacked bar chart compared to a regular stacked chart?

You gain an easy visual comparison of proportions across different bars because all bars are the same height, allowing focus on segment sizes.

What key information do you lose when converting a regular stacked chart to a 100% stacked chart?

You lose the ability to compare total counts or absolute sizes across different bars, as all bars are scaled to 100%.

Key Concepts (2/2)

True or False: A common misconception is that a 100% stacked chart means all groups have the same total count.

True. While all bars reach 100% height, this represents 100% of each group's count, and those counts can be very different.

Can you compare the total number of items or individuals across different categories using a 100% stacked bar chart?

No, you cannot. A 100% stacked bar chart only shows proportions, not absolute values or total counts. To compare totals, a regular stacked chart is needed.

What does the y-axis typically represent in a 100% stacked bar chart?

It represents percentages, usually ranging from 0% to 100%.

When reading a 100% stacked bar chart, how do you determine the percentage of a specific segment?

By finding where the segment starts and ends on the y-axis (or by reading data labels if provided).

How do you compare the proportion of a specific attribute (e.g., 'phone usage') across different categories shown in a 100% stacked bar chart?

You compare the size of that specific segment across different bars. A larger segment indicates a higher proportion in that category.

In the 'Device Usage' example, which age group had a higher proportion of phone users?

High schoolers (65%) had a higher proportion of phone users compared to middle schoolers (55%).
