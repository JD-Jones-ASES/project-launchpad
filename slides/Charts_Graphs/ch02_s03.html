Visual Deception

The general idea that charts, including pictographs, can be intentionally or unintentionally designed to mislead viewers by exaggerating or minimizing data differences.

The Honest Pictograph Rule (Otto Neurath's Rule)

A fundamental principle stating that to show more of something, one should always use *more symbols of the same size*, never a *bigger symbol*. This rule directly prevents area and volume distortion.

Critical Evaluation

The necessity for viewers to carefully examine pictographs (and all data visualizations) for potential tricks, distortions, and missing information, rather than simply accepting them at face value....

Other Pictograph Tricks

Beyond area distortion, other methods to mislead include using 3D icons (which distort by volume even more severely), omitting a key, using inconsistent symbols, or employing confusing half symbols.

Pictograph

Key (or Legend)

The Problem with Scaling Icons

The "Sneaky Trick"

It seems logical to make a picture bigger to show a larger number. However, this is a common form of visual deception.

How it Misleads

When an icon is made larger to represent a higher value, it can make the difference between values appear much greater (or smaller) than it actually is.

The Area Problem Explained

The Scenario

If sales double from January (100 phones) to February (200 phones), making February's phone icon twice as tall creates a problem.

Proportional Scaling

When you double the height of a picture and keep it in proportion, its width also doubles.

The Result

A picture that is twice as tall AND twice as wide has an area that is *four times* the original.

Brain's Interpretation

Your brain primarily reads the *area* of the image, not just its height. So, even if the value is only twice as much, the icon *looks* four times bigger.

Watch Out: Big Icons Do Not Mean Big Numbers!

Rule of Thumb

Be highly skeptical whenever you see a pictograph where the icons are of different sizes.

Correct Practice

In a proper pictograph, *only the number of symbols* should vary, not their individual size.

Crucial Step

Always count the symbols and carefully check the key (legend) to understand the actual data. Never rely solely on the visual size of the icons.

Other Pictograph Tricks

3D Icons

Missing Key

Without a key or legend, it's impossible to know what each symbol represents, rendering the chart unreadable and potentially deceptive.

Inconsistent Symbols

Using different types of symbols for different categories or time periods within the same chart makes fair comparison impossible and confuses the viewer.

Misleading Half Symbols

Cutting a symbol in half horizontally can convey a different impression than cutting it vertically, leading to visual confusion or misinterpretation of partial values.

Otto Neurath and the ISOTYPE System

Pioneer in Data Visualization

Otto Neurath (1882–1945) was an Austrian philosopher and social scientist dedicated to making data accessible to everyone.

ISOTYPE Development

In the 1920s and 1930s, he created ISOTYPE (International System of Typographic Picture Education), a system of clear, standardized, icon-based charts.

Neurath's Key Rule

The core principle of ISOTYPE, and the key to honest pictographs, is: "always change the *number* of symbols, never their *size*."

Impact

Neurath's work emphasized clarity, honesty, and understandability without complex math, profoundly influencing modern graphic design, data visualization, and infographics.
