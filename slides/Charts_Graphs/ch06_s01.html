Important Terms (1/2)

Pie Chart (Circle Graph)

A circular statistical graphic, divided into slices (sectors), used to illustrate numerical proportion. In a pie chart, the arc length of each slice, and consequently its area and central angle, is...

Slice (Sector)

A colored wedge or portion of a pie chart, representing a specific category or part of the whole. Its size visually indicates its proportion.

Legend (Key)

A guide, usually placed alongside the chart, that uses colors or patterns to explain which category each slice represents.

Percentage Labels

Numerical values, typically expressed as percentages, printed on or near each slice to quantify its contribution to the whole.

Title

The heading of a pie chart that succinctly describes the data being presented and what the entire circle represents.

Important Terms (2/2)

Whole

In the context of a pie chart, this refers to the entire dataset, collection, or total amount being analyzed, which is always represented as 100% by the complete circle.

When Pie Charts Are Most Effective

Limited Categories

Clear Differences

The categories should ideally have noticeable differences in their percentages. If all slices are very similar in size, it's hard to visually compare them, and a bar chart might offer more precision.

Showing Parts of a Whole

Their primary purpose is to illustrate how a single total is divided among its constituent parts. They visually emphasize the proportion of each part relative to the overall sum.

Avoiding Common Pitfalls

One Point in Time

Never use a pie chart to show how something changes over time. Its strength is showing proportions *at a single moment*.

Percentage, Not Count

The size of a slice depends on its percentage of the total, not on how many sub-items or complex categories might be within it.

Always 100%

The sum of all slices MUST be 100%. If you encounter a pie chart where the percentages don't add up to 100% (or very close, accounting for rounding), it's flawed.

Simplicity is Key

If you have too many categories (more than six), the chart becomes overcrowded and difficult to read. Consider a bar chart for clearer comparison in such cases.
