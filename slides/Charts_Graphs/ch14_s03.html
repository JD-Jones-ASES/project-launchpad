Being a Critical Reader of Charts: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Purpose of Charts

Charts are created by people with a specific purpose, which can range from purely informing to persuading, selling, or supporting a particular viewpoint.

Critical Reading

A critical reader actively interrogates charts, questioning their design, data, and presentation, rather than just accepting them at face value. Polished design does not guarantee honest data.

Misleading Chart Techniques

Common tricks include truncated y-axes, uneven time scales, cherry-picking data ranges, burying units, and using disproportionate visual elements.

Emotional vs. Mathematical Story

The emotional story is what the chart *wants* you to feel or believe, while the mathematical story is what the actual numbers objectively say. Misleading charts often make these two stories diverge...

Key Concepts (2/2)

Viewer Habits

Most people spend very little time (under eight seconds) looking at a chart, making them susceptible to misleading designs. Slowing down and applying critical questions is vital.

Important Terms (1/2)

Critical Reader

Someone who actively questions and evaluates the information presented in a chart, rather than passively accepting it.

Source

The origin of the data presented in a chart; includes who collected it, when, and their potential motivations.

Scale

The range and interval of values shown on a chart's axes, particularly the y-axis, and the units used.

Truncated Axis

A common misleading technique where the y-axis (or another axis) does not start at zero, making small differences appear much larger.

Cherry-picking

The selective presentation of data or a time range that supports a particular argument, while omitting contradictory or less favorable information.

Important Terms (2/2)

Comparison Baseline

A standard or reference point against which other data can be measured to provide context (e.g., a placebo group, industry average, previous performance).

Proportionality (Visual to Data)

The principle that the visual size or dimension of a chart element (e.g., bar height, pie slice area, bubble size) should directly correspond to the numerical value it represents.

Emotional Story

The feeling or impression a chart is designed to evoke in the viewer, often through visual manipulation.

Mathematical Story

The objective, factual narrative conveyed by the raw numerical data presented in a chart, without visual embellishment or distortion.

What is the source?

Who collected this data?

Knowing the author helps you assess credibility.

When was it collected?

Timeliness can be important; old data might not be relevant.

Is the source clearly labeled?

A chart without a source is like a rumor – impossible to verify.

Does the source have an incentive to present data in a particular way?

What is the scale?

Check all axes

Don't just glance; look closely at the numbers and labels.

Does the y-axis start at zero?

If not, even tiny differences between data points can appear dramatically large and significant. This is a very common misleading trick (a "truncated axis").

Are the tick marks evenly spaced?

An uneven time axis, for example, can stretch certain periods and compress others, artificially altering the perception of trends (making growth look faster or slower).

What are the units?

Is the y-axis in dollars, thousands of dollars, or millions? A bar labeled "2" means vastly different things if the unit is dollars versus millions of dollars. Charts that hide units in tiny print ...

What is missing?

Is this a cherry-picked time range?

Data might be selected to show a favorable trend while ignoring periods of decline.

Are some categories excluded?

Only showing data for successful groups while omitting less successful ones can create a skewed perception.

Is there no comparison baseline?

A statement like "Our product reduces headaches by 40%!" sounds impressive. But without knowing "40% compared to what?" (a placebo, an old version, doing nothing), the number is meaningless. Always...

Is the visual proportional to the data?

Check all visual elements

Bar heights, pie slice areas, icon sizes, bubble areas – they all must accurately reflect the numbers they represent.

Direct correspondence

If one bar represents a value twice as large as another, it should be exactly twice as tall.

Area vs. Radius Scaling

For shapes like bubbles or icons, if one visually *looks* nine times bigger than another, its *area* should be nine times larger, not just its radius. Misrepresenting scale by using radius instead ...

What story is being told — and is it honest?

Identify the intended message

Ask yourself, "What does this chart *want* me to believe or feel?" This is the **emotional story**.

Compare with the numbers

Then, ask, "What do the actual numbers and accurate representations *actually* say?" This is the **mathematical story**.

Worked Example: Applying the Five Questions

Q1 — Source

The company itself. They have an incentive to show success, so caution is warranted.

Q2 — Scale

The y-axis starts at $950M, not $0. This truncation makes a 1.4% increase (from $958M to $971M) *look* like a massive jump.

Q3 — What is missing?

Only three years are shown, offering no context on longer-term trends or comparison to industry averages.

Q4 — Proportionality

The 2024 bar is visually four times taller, but the data value is only 1.4% higher. The visual is grossly out of proportion.

Q5 — Story

The chart wants you to believe profits are "skyrocketing" (emotional story). The actual numbers show a small, steady increase (mathematical story). The two stories diverge.
