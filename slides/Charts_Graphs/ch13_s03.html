Network Diagrams

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Node (Vertex)

An individual item, object, or entity represented by a dot in a network diagram. Examples include people, cities, websites, or species.

Edge (Link/Connection)

A line connecting two nodes, indicating a relationship or connection between them. Edges can be directed (one-way) or undirected (two-way).

Network Diagram (Graph)

A visualization composed of nodes and edges that displays relationships or connections between objects.

Hub

A node that has a significantly high number of connections (edges). Hubs are often central and important within a network.

Isolated Node

A node that has no connections (edges) to any other nodes in the network. It represents an item with no relationships within the dataset.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Degree

The numerical count of edges connected to a specific node. A high degree indicates a hub, while a degree of zero indicates an isolated node.

Path

A sequence of connected edges that forms a route between two or more nodes in a network.

Important Terms (1/2)

Network Diagram (Graph)

A visual representation showing relationships between a set of objects, using dots (nodes) and lines (edges).

Node (Vertex)

A fundamental component of a network diagram, depicted as a dot, representing an individual item or entity within the network.

Edge (Link, Connection)

A line segment connecting two nodes, signifying a relationship, interaction, or flow between them.

Hub

A node characterized by a large number of edges, indicating its high level of connectivity and often its importance or influence within the network.

Isolated Node

A node that has no edges connected to it, meaning it has no defined relationships within the context of the network being studied.

Important Terms (2/2)

Degree

A quantitative measure referring to the total number of edges directly attached to a particular node.

Path

A contiguous sequence of edges that allows movement from one node to another through the network.

Directed Edge

An edge with an arrow, indicating a one-way relationship or flow from one node to another (e.g., A follows B).

Undirected Edge

An edge without an arrow, indicating a mutual or two-way relationship between two nodes (e.g., A is friends with B, and B is friends with A).

Common Misconceptions to Avoid

Physical Distance

In most network diagrams, the physical distance between nodes on the page has *no inherent meaning*. Nodes are placed for readability, not to represent actual geographic or conceptual distance.

Hub Importance

A node's importance as a "hub" is determined by its number of connections (its degree), not by its position (e.g., being in the center) on the page.

Network Diagrams in the Real World

Social Media

Every social media platform is a giant network. You are a node, and your followers, friends, or connections are edges. Influencers are prime examples of hubs.

The Internet

Websites are nodes, and hyperlinks are directed edges. Search engines like Google originally used the concept of hubs (websites with many incoming links) to rank the importance of web pages.

Food Webs (Ecology)

In ecosystems, species are nodes, and "eats" relationships are directed edges. A keystone species is a hub whose removal can have a cascading effect, potentially collapsing the entire network.

Airline Routes

Airports are nodes, and flights between them are edges. Major airports like Chicago O'Hare or Atlanta Hartsfield are massive hubs, central to air travel.

Disease Spread (Epidemiology)

During pandemics (like COVID-19), scientists use network diagrams to model how people interact (nodes and edges) to predict and understand how a virus might spread through a population.
