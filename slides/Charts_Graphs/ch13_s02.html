Flow Diagrams and Sankey Charts

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Visualizing Flow

Sankey charts are designed to show the movement or transfer of a quantity (like energy, money, people, or materials) from a source to one or more destinations.

Width Represents Quantity

The fundamental principle of a Sankey chart is that the width of the ribbons or arrows is directly proportional to the quantity of the flow it represents. A wider ribbon means a larger quantity.

Sources and Destinations

Charts typically show quantities originating from one or more "sources" (often on the left) and distributing to one or more "destinations" (often on the right).

Additivity of Flows

Just like a pie chart, the sum of all flows leaving a source (or arriving at a destination, if there's no loss) should equal the total quantity of the source.

Reading Labels

Good Sankey charts include labels with percentages or specific amounts on each flow, which are crucial for precise interpretation.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Aesthetic Order

The vertical order or position of ribbons is often for aesthetic clarity and does not necessarily indicate rank or importance; always refer to width and labels for data.

Applications

Sankey charts are widely used in various fields, including energy consumption, climate science (carbon footprints), business analytics (customer journeys), and supply chain management.

Historical Significance

Charles Joseph Minard pioneered this visualization technique with his famous map of Napoleon's 1812 Russian campaign, illustrating army size changes over geography and time.

Design Considerations

While powerful, Sankey charts can become cluttered and difficult to read if too many flows are represented simultaneously. Simplicity usually leads to clarity.

Introduction to Flow Diagrams and Sankey Charts

Analogy

Think of a river splitting into smaller streams; the wider the stream, the more water it carries. Sankey charts apply this concept to quantities like energy, money, or materials.

Purpose

To visually represent the flow and distribution of a quantity.

Where Sankey Charts Are Used (Real-World Applications)

Energy

Visualizing national energy consumption, from sources (coal, oil) to end uses (electricity generation, transportation). (e.g., U.S. Energy Information Administration).

Climate Science

Showing carbon footprints, detailing greenhouse gas emissions from various sectors like transportation, agriculture, and industry.

Business

Tracking customer journeys on websites (flow from homepage to product pages to checkout) or analyzing where customers drop off.

Supply Chains

Illustrating the movement of raw materials through manufacturing processes to finished products.

Historical Context: Charles Joseph Minard

Pioneer

French civil engineer (1781–1870) who created one of the most celebrated data visualizations.

Napoleon's March

His famous map depicted Napoleon's disastrous 1812 campaign to Moscow.

Legacy

Minard's map is considered a precursor to modern Sankey diagrams and a masterpiece of statistical graphics, influencing how we visualize flow data today.

Design Caution: Too Many Flows

The "Spaghetti Plate" Problem

Sankey charts are effective with a moderate number of flows (e.g., 3-8).

Issue

Too many flows (e.g., 20-30) lead to tangled, crossing ribbons, making the chart illegible.

Solution

Designers should simplify, group flows, or break the data into multiple, smaller charts for clarity.
