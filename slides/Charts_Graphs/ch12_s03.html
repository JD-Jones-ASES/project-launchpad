Chapter 12, Section 3

Evaluating Infographics Critically

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Importance of Critical Evaluation

Infographics, despite their attractive design, can be as misleading as any other data visualization. Learning to read them critically protects you from false information.

The Critical Evaluation Checklist

A structured approach using five key questions to assess the reliability and accuracy of an infographic.

Verifying Data Sources

Always check if a source is cited and if it's trustworthy. A missing source is a major red flag, indicating that the information might be made up or untraceable.

Checking Numerical Accuracy

Percentages in a pie chart must add up to exactly 100%. Comparing numbers across different parts of an infographic helps ensure consistency.

Assessing Visual Proportionality

Visual elements (like icon size, bar height, or bubble area) must accurately reflect the data they represent. Disproportionate visuals can exaggerate or downplay information.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Identifying Missing Context

Look out for "cherry-picking," where an infographic only shows data that supports a particular viewpoint while omitting contradictory or important background information.

Important Terms (1/2)

Critical Evaluation

The process of carefully examining and judging the accuracy, reliability, and potential biases of information, especially in visual formats like infographics.

Data Source

The origin or provider of the data used in an infographic. It's crucial for verifying the information's credibility.

Proportionality

The principle that visual elements in an infographic (e.g., icons, bars, areas) should be sized in direct relation to the numerical values they represent.

Cherry-picking

The act of selectively presenting only the data or information that supports a particular argument or conclusion, while ignoring data that might contradict it.

Bias

A predisposition or inclination for or against something, which can influence how data is collected, presented, and interpreted, often leading to a skewed perspective.

Important Terms (2/2)

Icon Size Distortion

A common infographic flaw where the size of an icon or visual element is not proportional to the data it represents, leading to an exaggerated or minimized visual impact.

Agenda

A plan or motive, often unstated, that influences the creation or presentation of an infographic, particularly in how data is selected and framed to persuade an audience.
