Key Concepts

Purpose-Driven Chart Selection

The most crucial step in choosing a chart is to first identify what question you are trying to answer or what story you want to tell with your data.

Five Main Data Visualization Purposes

Chart as a Tool

Just like using the right tool for a job, selecting the correct chart ensures your data communicates clearly and honestly, preventing muddy or misleading messages.

Time on the X-Axis

For any chart that displays change over time, the horizontal (x-axis) must always represent time, ordered from earliest to most recent.

Important Terms (1/4)

Comparison Chart

A visualization designed to display data so that different categories or groups can be easily measured and contrasted against each other.

Vertical Bar Chart

A standard comparison chart where bars extend vertically, ideal for 3-10 categories with short names.

Horizontal Bar Chart

A comparison chart where bars extend horizontally, best for categories with long names or when ranking items from highest to lowest.

Clustered Bar Chart

A comparison chart used to compare the same categories across multiple distinct groups (e.g., comparing sales across different regions for two different years).

Diverging Bar Chart

A comparison chart used when responses split in two opposing directions (e.g., "strongly agree" to "strongly disagree").

Important Terms (2/4)

Pie Chart

A composition chart best for showing parts of a whole with six or fewer slices of clearly different sizes, where each slice represents a percentage of a 100% total.

Donut Chart

Similar to a pie chart, but with a hollow center, often used to display a key number or total within the middle.

Treemap

A composition chart for showing many categories or hierarchical data, using nested rectangles whose sizes represent their proportion of the whole.

Waffle Chart

A composition chart that uses a grid of cells (typically 10x10, where each cell is 1%) to visually represent exact percentages in a highly readable format.

Stacked Bar Chart

A composition chart used to compare the breakdown of parts across multiple different groups or categories.

Important Terms (3/4)

Line Graph

A change over time chart that connects data points with lines, making trends (increases, decreases, plateaus) immediately visible, typically for one or a few series.

Area Chart

A change over time chart similar to a line graph, but with the region below the line filled in to emphasize the cumulative total or volume over time.

Stacked Area Chart

A change over time chart that shows how different parts of a total collectively change over time, with each category's area stacked upon the others.

Dot Plot

A distribution chart for small datasets (under 30 values) where each individual data point is shown.

Histogram

A distribution chart used for larger datasets of continuous data (like height or test scores), grouping values into "bins" to show the frequency distribution.

Scatter Plot

A relationship chart that displays two variables, one on each axis, using individual dots. It helps identify positive trends, negative trends, or no trend (random scatter).

Bubble Chart

A relationship chart that extends the scatter plot by adding a third variable, represented by the size of each "bubble" or dot.
