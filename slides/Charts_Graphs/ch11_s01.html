Important Terms

Heatmap

A data visualization that uses color intensity to represent values in a grid. Each cell in the grid corresponds to a combination of two categories (one on each axis), with color intensity indicatin...

Color Scale (Color Ramp or Color Legend)

The key that shows which colors correspond to which values in a heatmap. It typically appears as a bar graded from the minimum to the maximum value in the data.

Hot Spot

A region of a heatmap where the color is most intense (darkest), indicating the highest values within the dataset.

Cold Spot

A region of a heatmap where the colors are lightest, indicating the lowest values within the dataset.

Core Components of a Heatmap

Grid Layout

Data is organized into rows and columns, with each cell representing a unique combination of the categories defined by the axes.

Color Intensity

The primary encoding mechanism. Darker or more vibrant colors usually signify higher values, while lighter or less intense colors indicate lower values.

Axes Labels

Crucial for understanding what categories are being compared (e.g., days of the week vs. subjects).

Color Scale (Legend)

Provides the mapping between the colors used in the grid and the actual numerical values they represent. It clarifies the minimum, maximum, and intermediate values.

Worked Example: Homework Completion Heatmap (1/2)

Title

Homework completion rates by subject and day.

Axes

Rows are days (Monday-Friday), columns are subjects (Math, Science, English, History, Art).

Color Scale

Light blue (~65%) to dark blue (~95%).

Hot Spots

Art on Monday (95%) and English on Monday (92%) show highest completion.

Cold Spots

Math on Friday (65%) is the lowest completion rate. History on Monday (70%) is also low.

Worked Example: Homework Completion Heatmap (2/2)

Row Patterns

Friday is consistently lighter than Monday across most subjects, suggesting a drop in completion towards the end of the week.

Column Patterns

Art is consistently dark (85-95%), indicating high completion throughout the week. Math gradually lightens from Monday to Friday (85% to 65%), showing a significant decline.

Conclusion

The heatmap clearly shows a general decline in homework completion as the week progresses, particularly for Math. This pattern prompts further investigation into potential causes.

Important Considerations and Misconceptions

Rainbow Color Scales are Misleading

Avoid using rainbow (or "jet") color scales. They create uneven perceptual jumps, making it appear as if there are sharp boundaries in the data where none exist. Stick to **sequential color scales*...

Reading Direction

Do not read heatmaps like text (left-to-right, cell by cell). Instead, step back and scan the entire grid for overall gradients, large regions of dark or light, and distinct patterns.

Light ≠ Zero

A light cell typically means a low value, but not necessarily zero. Always check the color scale's minimum value to understand the actual range.

Real-World Applications of Heatmaps

Weather Maps

Showing temperature distribution across geographical areas using warm colors for hot and cool colors for cold.

Sports Analytics

Basketball shot charts indicate areas where a player is most effective (hot spots) or least effective (cold spots).

Website Analytics

"Click heatmaps" visualize where users click most frequently on a webpage, guiding design improvements.

Biology

Gene expression heatmaps illustrate which genes are active or inactive in different cell types or conditions.
