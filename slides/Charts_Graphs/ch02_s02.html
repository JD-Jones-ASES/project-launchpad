SCALES AND HALF SYMBOLS: A STUDY GUIDE

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Scale in Pictographs

The number of items that each full symbol in a pictograph represents. It's defined in the pictograph's key (e.g., "1 star = 5 votes" means the scale is 5).

Importance of Scale Choice

Choosing the right scale is vital for a pictograph to be clear, easy to read, and accurately represent data without being cluttered or confusing.

Criteria for a Good Scale

Problems with Poor Scales

A poorly chosen scale can lead to awkward fractional symbols (e.g., three-eighths of a symbol), making the pictograph hard to draw and interpret, or create rows with too many symbols.

Limitations of Fractional Symbols

While a half symbol can sometimes work, anything smaller (quarter, three-eighths, etc.) is generally too difficult to draw and accurately interpret, indicating a need to adjust the scale.

Important Terms

Scale

In a pictograph, the number of individual items that each single, complete symbol represents. It is always specified in the pictograph's key.

Key

The legend in a pictograph that explains what each symbol, or part of a symbol, stands for (e.g., "1 book = 10 students"). The key defines the scale.

Pictograph

A type of graph that uses images or symbols to represent data. Each symbol typically represents a certain quantity as defined by the scale.

Half Symbol

A symbol that is drawn as half of its full size to represent half of the quantity defined by the scale. Useful for showing values that are not exact multiples of the scale but can be divided by two.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the 'scale' in a pictograph?

In a pictograph, the scale is the number of items that each full symbol represents. It is usually shown in the key.

Why is choosing the right scale important when building a pictograph?

A good scale makes the pictograph clear and easy to read. A poor scale can result in confusing fractional symbols or a chart with excessively long rows.

How does the key/scale of a pictograph affect its interpretation?

The scale determines the value of each symbol, which is crucial for accurately calculating totals and understanding the magnitude of the data represented.

What is a common misconception students have about choosing a scale for a pictograph?

Students sometimes think that a larger key value is always better because it results in fewer symbols.

Why is it not always better to use a larger key value for a pictograph, even if it reduces the number of symbols?

The scale must fit the data. If data values are not multiples of the key, it will lead to awkward, hard-to-draw, and hard-to-read fractional symbols.

What is the first 'golden rule' for picking a good scale for a pictograph?

Make the data values work out evenly (or nearly so) with the chosen scale, to avoid needing fractions of symbols.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Besides making values work out evenly, what other guideline should you follow when choosing a pictograph's scale?

Avoid too many symbols per row. An excessive number of symbols in a row makes the pictograph unreadable.

What types of numbers are generally recommended for scales in pictographs, and why?

Round numbers like 2, 5, 10, 25, 50, or 100 are most common because they make the calculations easier.

If your data values are 4, 8, 12, and 16, what would be an ideal scale for a pictograph, and why?

A scale of 4 would be ideal because all values are multiples of 4, resulting in whole numbers of symbols (1, 2, 3, and 4 respectively) without any fractions.

Why would using a scale of 1 be impractical if your highest data value for a category is 200?

A scale of 1 would require 200 symbols in one row, making the pictograph unreadably long and cluttered.

When are fractions of symbols, such as a half symbol, acceptable in a pictograph?

A half symbol works fine when a data value is exactly half of the scale. It indicates the scale is a reasonable fit, even if not perfectly even for all values.
