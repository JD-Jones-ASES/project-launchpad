Showing Totals and Parts

Stacked bar charts are excellent for visualizing the total value for each category and how different sub-categories contribute to that total.

Reading Total Bar Height

The overall height of any bar can be read directly from the y-axis, representing the total value for that category.

Reading Bottom Segments

The value of the lowest segment in a stacked bar can be read directly by tracing its top edge to the y-axis.

Trade-offs with Other Charts

Stacked bar charts make comparing totals easy but can make direct visual comparison of individual series (sub-categories) harder than, for example, a clustered bar chart.

Important Terms (1/2)

Stacked Bar Chart

A type of bar chart where each bar is divided into several colored segments, each representing a sub-category. The total height of the bar is the sum of its segments.

Segment

A distinct, colored part of a stacked bar that represents a specific sub-category contributing to the bar's total value.

Sub-category

A smaller division or group within a larger category. In a stacked bar chart, sub-categories are represented by the segments.

Legend

A key, typically placed next to the chart, that explains what each color or pattern used for the segments represents.

Y-axis

The vertical axis of a chart, usually representing the numerical values or quantities being measured.

Important Terms (2/2)

X-axis

The horizontal axis of a chart, usually representing the discrete categories or groups being compared.

Subtraction Method

The technique used to find the value of an upper or middle segment in a stacked bar chart by subtracting the y-axis value at its bottom from the y-axis value at its top.

Stacked vs. Clustered Bars

It's important not to confuse stacked bar charts with clustered bar charts.

Segments Represent Sub-categories

Remember that the segments are sub-categories of the main bar category. For example, if the bars are grades, the segments might be fundraising methods, not different types of grades.
