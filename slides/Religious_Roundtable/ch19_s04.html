Chapter 28, Section 4

Thinking Religiously and the Global Roundtable

Study Slides

The Purpose of Religious Study

Rigor

Disciplined and thorough investigation.

Humility

Recognizing the limits of one's own knowledge.

Imagination

The ability to conceptualize different ways of being and understanding the world.

The Nature of "Thinking Religiously"

• The origins of the world.

• Moral obligations to others.

• The reality of human mortality.

The Diversity of the Roundtable

Contradictory Perspectives

Some traditions insist on a personal God, others explicitly deny the existence of a God, and some view the question as irrelevant.

Methodological Differences

Figures like the Buddha famously refused to answer cosmological questions, whereas others, like Karl Marx, focused on the socio-political implications of religious answers.

Historical Resilience and Evolution

Judaism

Survived the destruction of the Temple and centuries of persecution.

Islam

Transformed from a small desert community to a global force within a century.

Buddhism

Expanded from a single teacher to cover half a continent.

Norse Paganism

Survived through poetry preserved by those who displaced it and is currently undergoing reconstruction.

Communism

Began as a minor pamphlet and went on to convulse the 20th century.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: According to the text, what did Socrates mean when he stated that "the unexamined life is not worth living"?

> Answer: He meant that the value of life is found in the act of asking questions and seeking understanding, rather than necessarily possessing all the final answers.

Q: List the five "Big Questions" identified as the core of religious and philosophical inquiry.

> Answer: > 1. Where did the universe come from, and does it have a purpose? > 2. What is the deepest cause of human suffering, and is there a way through it? > 3. What do you owe to other people, and why? > 4. Does history have a direction, ...

Q: How does the text define intellectual and moral growth?

> Answer: It is defined by the process of documenting one’s current thoughts on existential questions and revisiting them years later to see how the answers have changed and the questions have deepened.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/2)

Q: What role does "the tenth voice" play in the context of the Roundtable?

> Answer: The tenth voice represents the individual reader's or student’s own tradition and perspective, requiring them to engage honestly with the same questions addressed by the historical traditions.

Q: In what form did Norse Paganism survive the Christianization of Scandinavia?

> Answer: It survived in the form of poems preserved by the very people who displaced the original tradition.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the overall purpose of the 'Going Further' section in a textbook?

To provide a starting point (doorway) into each tradition, listing primary texts and accessible secondary sources for deeper study.

Which primary text is recommended for Christianity, and from what year is the specified translation?

The Holy Bible, specifically the King James Version (1611).

For understanding the theological core of Christianity, which two biblical sections are suggested for beginners?

The Gospel of Mark (shortest and most immediate) and Paul's Letter to the Romans.

Who authored 'Mere Christianity' and what is its significance?

C.S. Lewis. It's a philosopher and literary scholar's accessible and intellectually honest case for Christian belief, distilled from radio broadcasts.

What is the primary text for Judaism, also known as the Five Books of Moses?

The Torah.

Which book of the Torah is recommended for beginners, and what key narratives does it contain?

Exodus, which contains both the Exodus narrative and the giving of the Law at Sinai.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Which author provides a practical and warm introduction to Jewish practice and belief for non-specialists?

Anita Diamant, in 'Living a Jewish Life'.

What is the primary text of Islam, and which translation is noted for its clarity and readability?

The Quran. The Abdel Haleem translation (Oxford World's Classics, 2004).

Which two Suras are recommended to begin with for understanding the theological foundations of the Quran?

Sura 1 (Al-Fatiha) and Sura 2 (Al-Baqara).

Who wrote a careful, sympathetic, and historically grounded account of Prophet Muhammad's life?

Karen Armstrong, in 'Muhammad'.

What is considered the primary text for Hinduism in this section, and which chapter is recommended for its philosophical heart?

The Bhagavad Gita. Begin with Chapter 2.

Which classic book has a chapter on Hinduism that is noted as one of the clearest introductions available?

Huston Smith's 'The World's Religions'.
