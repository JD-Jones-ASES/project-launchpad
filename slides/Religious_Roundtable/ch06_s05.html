Chapter 6, Section 5

The Influence and Legacy of Taoism: A Study Guide

Study Slides

Traditional Chinese Medicine and Cosmology

Acupuncture and Herbalism

Medical practices rooted in Taoist thought.

Qi

The concept of "vital energy" that flows through the human body.

Arts and Physical Practices

Feng Shui

The art of arranging spaces to achieve harmony with natural forces.

Internal Martial Arts

Disciplines such as tai chi, bagua, and xinyi are considered physical embodiments of the principle of wu wei.

Aesthetics

Chinese painting and poetry emphasize Taoist values of emptiness, suggestion, and naturalness.

Cross-Cultural Fusions: Zen Buddhism

• A preference for direct experience over formal doctrine.

• A distrust of elaborate, rigid systems.

• The practice of "sitting quietly with what is."

Wu Wei and Modern Productivity

• Doing more is not always the solution to a problem.

• Wisdom often involves stopping and observing.

• Allowing the natural movement of things to reveal itself is a valid and often superior course of action.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: According to the text, what is the core Taoist concept behind the practice of acupuncture?

> Answer: Acupuncture is rooted in Taoist cosmology and the concept of qi, or the vital energy that flows through the body.

Q: List three internal martial arts that serve as embodiments of wu wei.

> Answer: Tai chi, bagua, and xinyi.

Q: How did Taoism influence the development of Zen Buddhism?

> Answer: Taoism contributed an emphasis on direct experience over doctrine, a distrust of elaborate systems, and the practice of sitting quietly with reality.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/2)

Q: What is the Taoist view on the relationship between humanity and the natural world?

> Answer: Humans are not masters of nature or the "summit of creation." Instead, they are participants in a natural system where the Tao flows through everything equally.

Q: How does the text define the practical application of wu wei in the modern world?

> Answer: It is defined not as laziness, but as the recognition that not all problems are solved by doing more. It involves stopping, observing, and allowing natural movements to reveal themselves.

Glossary of Important Terms

Key Concepts (1/2)

What fundamental frameworks are Chinese medicine (acupuncture, herbalism, qi) rooted in?

Taoist cosmology and the yin-yang framework.

What is 'qi' in the context of Chinese medicine?

Qi (氣) is the concept of vital energy flowing through the body.

What Taoist art involves arranging spaces in harmony with natural forces?

Feng shui.

Which internal martial arts are described as explicit embodiments of wu wei?

Tai chi, bagua, and xinyi.

What Taoist aesthetic has shaped Chinese painting and poetry for centuries?

The aesthetic of emptiness, suggestion, and naturalness.

What significant cross-cultural fusion resulted from Taoism's encounter with Buddhism?

Chan Buddhism in China, which later became Zen in Japan.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What key aspects of Zen Buddhism owe a substantial debt to Taoist figures like Zhuangzi and Laozi?

Zen's emphasis on direct experience over doctrine, its distrust of elaborate systems, and its practice of sitting quietly with what is.

In the modern world, what area has Taoism attracted attention for as a resource?

Ecological ethics.

What is the Taoist insight regarding humans' relationship with nature?

Humans are not masters of nature but participants in it.

According to Taoist thought, what is the consequence of attempting to dominate natural systems?

It inevitably generates resistance and disorder.

How does Taoism view the natural world in relation to human use?

Taoism never taught that the natural world exists for human use, or that human beings are the summit of creation; the Tao flows through everything equally.

What is a 'quietly radical' challenge Taoism presents to modern society?

Its challenge to the modern obsession with productivity, efficiency, and ceaseless activity through the concept of wu wei.
