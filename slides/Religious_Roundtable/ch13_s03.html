Important Terms (1/3)

Poetic Edda

The older collection of Old Norse mythological and heroic poems, compiled in Iceland around 1270.

Prose Edda

A 13th-century handbook for poets, written by Snorri Sturluson, which retells Norse myths and explains poetic forms.

Snorri Sturluson (1179–1241)

A Christian Icelandic chieftain, poet, and historian who authored the Prose Edda.

Kenning

A distinctive compound poetic metaphor used in Norse skaldic verse (e.g., "whale-road" for the sea).

Völva

A Norse female seer or prophetess who practiced divination and magic (*sei*).

Important Terms (2/3)

Yggdrasil

The World Tree in Norse cosmology, connecting all the realms, on which Odin hung himself to gain knowledge.

Runes

Sacred symbols believed to contain hidden knowledge, which Odin acquired through his self-sacrifice.

Ragnarök

The prophesied ultimate battle of the gods and the end of the world in Norse mythology.

Hávamál ("Sayings of the High One")

A poem within the Poetic Edda that records Odin's own account of his acquisition of wisdom and the runes.

Völuspá ("The Seeress's Prophecy")

A prominent poem in the Poetic Edda where a völva prophesies the creation of the world, its destruction, and rebirth.

Important Terms (3/3)

Skald

An Old Norse court poet, known for composing complex verse often featuring kennings.

Ásatrú

A modern religious movement that revives and practices Norse paganism, grappling with the source texts preserved by Christian hands.

The Unique Preservation of the Eddas (1/2)

Christian Compilers

The texts we possess were not written or compiled by adherents of Norse Paganism. Snorri Sturluson was a devout Christian, and the compiler of the Poetic Edda was almost certainly Christian.

Contrast with Other Traditions

This stands in stark contrast to most other religious traditions, where sacred texts are written and preserved by believers for the faithful (e.g., the Torah, Quran, Bhagavad Gita).
