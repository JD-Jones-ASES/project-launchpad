Comparing Suffering: Buddhism, Norse Paganism, and Communism

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Buddhism: Internal Root of Suffering

Norse Paganism: Cosmic Root of Suffering

Communism (Marxist View): Structural Root of Suffering

The Core Disagreement

The central question posed is whether the primary source of human suffering is inside us, outside us (social structures), or built into the fabric of reality itself.

Implications of Diagnosis

Your answer to the root cause of suffering directly dictates what you believe must be done to address it, whether it's changing the mind, the economic system, or one's attitude towards fate.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Mutual Exclusivity

The text explores whether these perspectives are necessarily at odds, suggesting that elements of each might coexist or complement one another in a broader understanding of suffering.

Important Terms (1/2)

Craving, Grasping, Clinging (Buddhism)

The internal psychological processes, rooted in attachment and desire, that Buddhism identifies as the primary cause of suffering.

Eightfold Path (Buddhism)

The systematic pathway of ethical conduct, mental discipline, and wisdom prescribed in Buddhism for achieving liberation from suffering (nirvana).

Nirvana (Buddhism)

The ultimate goal of Buddhist practice; a state of liberation from suffering, desire, and the cycle of rebirth, characterized by profound peace.

Wyrd (Norse Paganism)

A concept of fate or destiny in Norse tradition, understood as a powerful, immutable force woven into the fabric of the cosmos before birth, affecting all beings, including gods.

The Deepest Disagreement: Inside, Outside, or Inherent?

• If internal craving is the root, transforming the mind is paramount.

• If economic exploitation is the root, changing the economic system is essential.

• If suffering is intrinsic to the cosmos, then living with honesty, courage, and acceptance becomes the most important response.

Are These Diagnoses Mutually Exclusive?

• A Buddhist could acknowledge that unjust economic systems make cultivating the Eightfold Path significantly harder. Material hardship can be a barrier to spiritual practice.

• A Marxist, even in a perfectly just economic system, would likely concede that personal grief, loss, or the fear of death would persist, as these are not solely products of economic structures.
