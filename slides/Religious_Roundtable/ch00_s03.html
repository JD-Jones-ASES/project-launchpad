Thinking Religiously: A Study Guide

Study Slides

Core Principles of Religious Thinking

Engagement with the "Big Questions"

Addressing fundamental inquiries that cannot be resolved through laboratory experiments or mathematical calculations.

Genuine Curiosity

Approaching unfamiliar traditions and difficult questions with an open desire to understand.

Intellectual Persistence

A willingness to "sit with difficulty and uncertainty" rather than settling for easy or superficial answers.

Objective Understanding

The goal is not to determine which tradition is "correct," but to understand:

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to the text, what does 'thinking religiously' mean?

It means taking the big questions seriously, approaching them with genuine curiosity, and being willing to sit with difficulty and uncertainty instead of rea...

What does 'thinking religiously' NOT entail, as clarified in the text?

It does not mean being devout, adopting someone else's faith, or pretending to believe things you do not believe.

What distinguishes the 'religious questions' that great minds grapple with?

They have a kind of gravity, a weight and seriousness, and do not yield to calculation or laboratory experiment.

Identify the three questions Immanuel Kant famously asked that are considered 'religious in structure.'

What can I know? What should I do? What may I hope for?

How did Albert Camus explore a 'religious question' through his concept of the absurd?

Although Karl Marx denied religion, how did his theory of history demonstrate an 'unmistakably religious' structure?

His theory was built around a story of fall (primitive harmony), suffering (capitalism), redemption (revolution), and paradise (the classless society).

Key Concepts (2/2)

Why do the questions religion asks 'pull serious thinkers toward them regardless of their own beliefs'?

Because these questions have a kind of gravity—a weight and seriousness—that makes them compelling to explore.

What is the student's primary job when encountering different religious traditions in this book?

To understand what each tradition is saying, why it might be compelling, and what it implies about how to live, rather than deciding which tradition is correct.

Define 'immanence' in the context of religious study.

Immanence is the presence of the divine or ultimate within the world and within human experience.

How does a religious tradition demonstrate an emphasis on immanence?

By teaching that God — or the sacred, or the ultimate — is close, accessible, present in nature, in the human heart, or in everyday life.

What term is presented as the opposite of immanence?

Transcendence.
