Religious Roundtable

Chapter 28, Section 1

A Study Guide to The Questions That Remain: Comparative Traditions and Topics

Study Slides

Cycle One: Faith and Belief

Christianity

Faith is centered on trust in a person—the incarnate God who lived and died in human history.

Judaism

Faith is a covenant relationship; it is a promise bound across generations rather than just a belief.

Islam

Faith begins with the shahada (declaration that there is no god but God) and influences all aspects of life.

Cycle Two: Creation and Origins

Hinduism

Emerges from a primordial unity called Brahman. The Nasadiya Sukta suggests that the ultimate origin may be a mystery even to the gods.

Buddhism

Rejects the search for a first cause. Existence is defined by "dependent origination," making the universe like a circle without an edge.

Taoism

Focuses on the "unnamed." The Tao that can be described or spoken is not the eternal, true Tao.

Cycle Three: The Human Person

Confucianism

Humans are social creatures defined by relationships and the capacity for ren (benevolence) and li (proper conduct).

Norse Paganism

Humans are material creatures, described metaphorically as trees given breath and sense. They are bound by a web of fate woven by the Norns.

Communism

Humans are productive beings. Under systems where production is exploitative, humans become "estranged" from their work, others, and themselves.

Cycle Four: Sacred Texts

Judaism

Truth is found in a living tradition of interpretation; the Torah serves as the "seed" and the Talmud as the "growth."

Hinduism

Utilizes a layered archive consisting of shruti (revelation that is "heard," like the Vedas) and smriti (tradition that is "remembered," like the epics).

Norse Paganism

Presents a paradox where texts were preserved by those who no longer believed them. Wisdom is tied to sacrifice, as seen in the god of wisdom's suffering on Yggdrasil to receive the runes.

Cycle Five: Morality and Ethics

Christianity

Ethics are grounded in the double commandment to love God and neighbor.

Taoism

Advocates for wu wei, or non-forcing, acting in harmony with the natural "grain" of things.

Communism

Morality is inseparable from material and economic conditions; personal justice cannot exist within a structurally unjust economy.

Cycle Six: Community and Worship

Islam

The ummah is a community defined by shared submission to God, unified by daily prayer facing a single direction.

Buddhism

The Sangha (community of practitioners) is one of the "Three Jewels."

Confucianism

Li (ritual) is the architecture of community, making trust and order visible.

Cycle Seven: Suffering

Buddhism

Suffering is rooted in tanha (craving or relentless wanting).

Norse Paganism

Suffering is an inevitable condition. Liberation is not promised, but courage is emphasized. As the Hávamál suggests, while life and kin die, the "fame of a dead man's deeds" lives on.

Communism

Suffering is structural, the result of a system built on exploitation.

Cycle Eight: Transcendence and Immanence

Islam

Primarily emphasizes the absolute transcendence of Allah, though the Sufi tradition explores intimate closeness.

Hinduism

Holds both simultaneously through Brahman nirguna (featureless/beyond) and Brahman saguna (accessible/with qualities).

Taoism

The Tao is a paradox, described as being everywhere and nowhere at once.

Cycle Nine: History and Hope

Christianity

History is linear, moving toward a Kingdom, judgment, and consummation.

Judaism

Hope is messianic but focused on this world through tikkun olam (repairing the world).

Confucianism

Hope is found in the civilizational ideal of the junzi (exemplary person) through self-discipline.

Part IV: Glossary of Important Terms

Term. Tradition

Definition

Brahman. Hinduism

The primordial unity from which all differentiation flows.

Brahman nirguna. Hinduism

The divine as featureless and beyond all description.

Brahman saguna. Hinduism

The divine as full of qualities and accessible (e.g., as Krishna).

Dependent Origination. Buddhism

The concept that the cosmos arises without a first cause; a circle with no edge.

Junzi. Confucianism

The "exemplary person" who serves as a model for human society.

Li. Confucianism

Proper conduct or ritual; the architecture that makes trust and order visible.

Nasadiya Sukta. Hinduism

An ancient text acknowledging that even the gods may not know the world's origin.

The questions themselves never changed. What changed, radically and sometimes shockingly, were the answers provided by different traditions.

What does Taoism identify as the origin of the world?

Taoism pointed to the unnamed: the Tao that can be spoken is not the eternal Tao.

How does Confucianism define the human person?

Confucianism answered: we are social creatures, constituted by our relationships, capable of ren (benevolence) and li (proper conduct) when we are at our best.

They become estranged—from their work, from each other, and from their own humanity.
