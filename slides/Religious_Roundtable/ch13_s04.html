Key Concepts (1/2)

Revelation at Sinai (Judaism)

The idea that the Torah was given by God at a specific historical moment (Sinai) to Moses and the people of Israel, making it a document tied to a particular date, location, and audience.

Shruti (Hinduism)

Meaning "heard," referring to the Vedas and Upanishads as timeless truths received by seers in deep meditation, rather than being composed at a particular historical moment.

Eternal Teaching (Hinduism)

The concept, exemplified by the Bhagavad Gita, that sacred knowledge is an eternal truth that may be periodically lost and then restored through a chain of teacher-student transmission.

Earned Knowledge (Norse Paganism)

The unique Norse perspective that sacred knowledge (like the runes acquired by Odin) is not freely given but must be earned through suffering, distinct from divine bestowal or meditative reception.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Text as Cultural Memory

The understanding of the Norse Eddas not as direct scripture but as a preserved memory of a lost world, compiled by outsiders (Christians) and requiring reconstruction rather than simple reception.

Relationship between Text and Reality

The overarching philosophical question explored by the section: Does a text record a past event, reveal an eternal truth, or serve as a memory pointing to a tradition to be pieced together?

Important Terms (1/2)

Torah

The central scripture of Judaism, traditionally understood as divine revelation given to Moses at Mount Sinai.

Oral Torah

The rabbinic tradition of interpretation and commentary on the written Torah, considered by tradition to be co-revealed at Sinai.

Vedas

Ancient sacred texts of Hinduism, considered *shruti* (heard).

Upanishads

A collection of philosophical texts forming the theoretical basis for the Hindu religion, also considered *shruti*.

Shruti

A Sanskrit term meaning "what is heard," referring to the divinely revealed and timeless texts of Hinduism (Vedas, Upanishads) that were not composed by humans.

Important Terms (2/2)

Bhagavad Gita

An important Hindu scripture, part of the epic Mahabharata, which presents eternal teachings through a dialogue between Krishna and Arjuna.

Eddas

Two collections of Old Norse writings (the Poetic Edda and the Prose Edda) that are primary sources for Norse mythology and legends.

Yggdrasil

In Norse cosmology, the immense sacred tree that connects the nine worlds. Odin's self-sacrifice on Yggdrasil is described as how he acquired the runes.

Hermeneutical

Pertaining to the theory and methodology of interpretation, especially of texts.

The Philosophical Heart of the Comparison

Judaism

A record of revelation that becomes a **vehicle of continued revelation** through ongoing interpretation.

Hinduism

A **window onto eternal truth** that exists independently of human discovery.

Norse Paganism

A **memory of a lost world**, imperfectly preserved and requiring active reconstruction.
