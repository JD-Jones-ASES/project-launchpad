Important Terms (1/4)

Brahman

The ultimate, uncaused, unlimited, eternal reality in Hindu theology; the ground of all being. It is generally understood as impersonal, infinite reality underlying all existence.

Upanishads

A collection of philosophical texts (800-200 BCE) forming the philosophical heart of Hindu scriptures, exploring the nature of Brahman, Atman, and reality through dialogues. The word means "sitting...

Atman

The innermost self, soul, or essential spiritual core of every individual being.

Brahman Nirguna

Brahman "without qualities"; the utterly transcendent, formless, attribute-less ground of being that cannot be described and is approached through negation.

Brahman Saguna

Brahman "with qualities"; the divine as it manifests with personality, form, and relationship, appearing as various gods and goddesses (e.g., Krishna, Shiva, Devi, Vishnu).

Important Terms (2/4)

Neti neti

Sanskrit phrase meaning "not this, not this," used to describe the approach to understanding Nirguna Brahman by negating all limited descriptions.

Krishna, Shiva, Devi, Vishnu

Prominent deities in the Hindu pantheon, understood as manifestations or aspects of Brahman Saguna, embodying divine qualities and stories.

Bhagavad Gita

A revered Hindu scripture, part of the epic Mahabharata, which features the dialogue between Arjuna and Krishna, including Krishna's revelation of his cosmic form.

Vishvarupa

The "cosmic form" of Krishna, as revealed to Arjuna in the Bhagavad Gita, wherein all of creation and time are simultaneously present within a single divine figure.

Tat tvam asi

One of the four "great sayings" (mahavakyas) from the Upanishads, meaning "That thou art" or "Thou art That," asserting the essential identity of Atman with Brahman.

Important Terms (3/4)

Mahavakyas

The "Great Sayings" or principal aphorisms found in the Upanishads, which encapsulate profound philosophical truths.

Panentheism

The belief that God pervades and is immanent in the universe, but is also transcendent and greater than the universe. The world is *within* God, but God is *more than* the world.

Pantheism

The belief that God is identical with the universe, or that the universe is a manifestation of God.

Advaita Vedanta

A major non-dualistic school of Hindu philosophy, primarily associated with the philosopher Shankara, which asserts the absolute identity of Atman and Brahman.

Maya

In Advaita Vedanta, the concept of illusion or the fundamental misunderstanding of the true nature of reality, leading to the perception of separation between Atman and Brahman.
