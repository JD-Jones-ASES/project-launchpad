Chapter 7, Section 2

Classical, Neo-Confucian, and New Confucian Thought

Study Slides

Classical Confucianism (5th Century BCE – 2nd Century CE)

Mencius

Argued that human nature is fundamentally good. He believed that virtues such as compassion and moral judgment are innate qualities that must be cultivated.

Xunzi (310–235 BCE)

Served as a contrarian to Mencius, teaching that human nature is fundamentally chaotic. He argued that virtue is not innate but must be imposed through rigorous education and ritual.

Shared Goal

Despite their disagreement on the starting point of human nature, both Mencius and Xunzi agreed on the vital importance of "cultivation."

Neo-Confucianism (Song Dynasty, 960–1279 CE)

Key Figures

Cheng Yi, Cheng Hao, and most significantly, Zhu Xi (1130–1200 CE).

The Synthesis

Zhu Xi integrated Confucian ethics with a metaphysical framework, creating a system that addressed human nature, self-cultivation, and the nature of reality.

Regional Impact

New Confucianism (Xin Rujia) (20th Century)

Core Objectives

To engage with Western philosophy, democracy, science, and human rights.

The Central Debate

Scholars question if a system based on ritual hierarchy and social roles can adapt to modern values of individual rights and equality.

Arguments for Reform

Some scholars suggest Confucianism requires radical reform, particularly regarding its historical treatment of women and its strict emphasis on hierarchy.

Arguments for Continuity

Others argue that Confucian values—such as social harmony and character cultivation—offer a unique "Asian path" to democracy.
