Important Terms (1/2)

Ummah (OOM-mah)

The global community of Muslim believers, a foundational social and theological concept in Islam that transcends race, nationality, language, and class.

Salat (sah-LAHT)

The five daily ritual prayers, the second of the Five Pillars of Islam. They involve specific physical postures and prescribed Arabic recitations.

Jumu'ah (JOO-moo-ah)

The special congregational prayer held on Friday afternoons, which includes a sermon (khutba). Attendance is obligatory for Muslim men.

Shahada (shah-HAH-dah)

The declaration of faith ("There is no god but God, and Muhammad is the messenger of God"), the first of the Five Pillars. Its public recitation is the act of entering the Muslim community.

Zakat (zah-KAHT)

Obligatory almsgiving, the third Pillar of Islam. A fixed portion of a Muslim's wealth given annually to those in need (e.g., the poor, the indebted).

Important Terms (2/2)

Hajj

The pilgrimage to Mecca, the fifth Pillar of Islam, which gathers millions of Muslims from around the world annually.

Khutba

The sermon delivered during the Jumu'ah prayer, which often connects faith to current community life and moral challenges.

Adhan

The call to prayer, recited aloud by a muezzin to announce the time for each of the five daily prayers.

Ihram

The plain white garments worn by pilgrims during the Hajj, symbolizing equality and humility before God.

Allahu Akbar

An Arabic phrase meaning "God is greater," used in various Islamic contexts, including the beginning of the Salat.

What is the "Ummah" in Islam?

The global community of Muslim believers, transcending race, nationality, language, and class. It is one of Islam's most foundational social concepts.

What is "Jumu'ah" and who is required to attend?

The Friday congregational prayer, which includes a sermon (khutba). Attendance is obligatory for Muslim men.

Explain "Zakat" and its purpose.

Obligatory almsgiving, the third Pillar of Islam. It is a fixed portion of a Muslim's wealth given annually to the poor, the indebted, and others in need.

Why is Islam considered a 'community religion' from a theological perspective?
