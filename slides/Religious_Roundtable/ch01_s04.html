Roman Catholicism

Authority

Emphasizes the Pope and bishops as the direct successors of the apostles.

Sacraments

Recognizes seven sacraments. A central element is the Eucharist, in which the bread and wine are believed to truly become the body and blood of Christ.

Tradition

Scripture is interpreted through the lens of Church tradition, and there is a strong emphasis on the veneration of Mary and the saints.

The Common Core

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Apostolic Succession

The belief in Roman Catholicism that the Pope and bishops are the direct successors of the original apostles.

Eucharist

A central Christian sacrament; in Catholicism, it is the belief that bread and wine become the actual body and blood of Christ.

Great Schism

The formal split in 1054 between the Western (Roman Catholic) and Eastern (Orthodox) churches.

Icons

Sacred images used in worship, particularly revered within the Eastern Orthodox tradition.

Indulgences

Historically, payments made to the Church that were intended to reduce a soul's time in purgatory; a major point of contention for Martin Luther.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Sola Fide

"Faith alone"; the Protestant belief that salvation is achieved through faith rather than through works or sacraments.

Sola Scriptura

"Scripture alone"; the Protestant belief that the Bible is the final and sole authority for Christian faith and practice.

Theosis

The Eastern Orthodox theological concept of being gradually transformed to share in the divine nature.

Trinity

The core Christian doctrine regarding the nature of God, shared by all three major branches.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What are the three major branches of Christianity discussed in this section?

Catholic, Orthodox, and Protestant.

What was the first major split in Christianity called, and when did it occur?

The Great Schism, which occurred in 1054.

Which two Christian leadership centers were divided during the Great Schism?

The bishop of Rome (the Pope) in the West and the patriarch of Constantinople in the East.

What were the main factors that led to the Great Schism?

Tensions over the Pope's claimed authority over all Christians, theological differences, and centuries of cultural drift.

What were the two distinct Churches that emerged from the Great Schism?

The Western Roman Catholic Church and the Eastern Orthodox Church.

Name two distinctive features of Eastern Orthodoxy mentioned in the text.

It kept the ancient Greek liturgical tradition, a deep reverence for icons (sacred images), and the theological concept of theosis.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the theological concept of 'theosis' in Eastern Orthodoxy?

The belief that human beings can be gradually transformed to share in the divine nature through prayer, sacrament, and holy living.

In which regions is Eastern Orthodoxy the dominant tradition today?

Russia, Greece, and much of Eastern Europe.

Who initiated the second great rupture in Christianity, and when did it happen?

Martin Luther in 1517, leading to the Protestant Reformation.

What specific Church practice was Martin Luther troubled by, prompting his ninety-five objections?

The sale of indulgences, which were payments supposedly reducing a soul's time in purgatory.

What was Martin Luther's deeper critique beyond indulgences?

He argued that the Church had buried the simple Gospel truth beneath layers of tradition and institutional power.

What were Martin Luther's two primary rallying cries during the Protestant Reformation?

Sola scriptura (Scripture alone as the final authority) and sola fide (faith alone, not works or sacraments, as the means of salvation).
