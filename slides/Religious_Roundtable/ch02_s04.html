Core Concepts: The Branches of Judaism

Branch. Core Philosophy

Approach to Jewish Law (Halakha)

Orthodox. Tradition provides the stable ground to navigate modernity; Torah is the literal word of God.

Holds that both Written and Oral Torah are divinely revealed and the 613 commandments are binding in every age.

Conservative. Occupies a middle position; affirms the binding nature of law while acknowledging historical context.

Laws are binding but allow for flexibility in interpretation and application.

Reform. Emphasizes the ethical heart of Judaism over ritual details; focus on "prophetic Judaism."

Law is a human response to divine revelation shaped by history; individuals discern which laws carry moral weight today.

Reconstructionist. Views Judaism as an evolving civilization rather than a supernaturally revealed religion.

Practice is an expression of culture and identity rather than divine command.

Shared Foundations

Shared Texts

All movements recognize the authority and importance of the Torah and the Talmud.

Language and Prayer

The Hebrew language remains a central component of prayer across the traditions.

The Sacred Calendar

All branches observe the cycle of holy days, including Passover, Rosh Hashanah, and Yom Kippur.

Covenantal Identity

A shared history and a sense of belonging to a "family of traditions" link Jews across the ideological spectrum.

The Value of Argument

The tradition views internal debate and argument as a vital form of belonging and a means of thriving.

Zionism and the State of Israel

Definition

Zionism is a 19th-century political movement—not a branch of Judaism—that sought to establish a Jewish homeland in the ancient land of Israel.

History

The movement led to the founding of the modern State of Israel in 1948, following catastrophic persecution in Europe.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Halakha

Literally "the way of walking"; the body of Jewish law that provides a structured way of living.

Kashrut

The Jewish dietary laws that distinguish permitted (kosher) foods from forbidden ones and require the separation of meat and dairy.

Messiah

In the context of Zionism, the figure some Orthodox Jews believed was the only one with the authority to restore the land of Israel.

Mordecai Kaplan

The 20th-century rabbi who founded Reconstructionist Judaism.

Prophetic Judaism

A Reform Jewish emphasis on the demands for justice made by the biblical prophets, prioritized over ritual or dietary rules.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Rabbinic Decisors

Authoritative figures in Orthodox Judaism who provide guidance on how to apply Jewish law to new or modern questions.

Shabbat

The Sabbath, observed from Friday sundown to Saturday night as a time of rest from work, commerce, and the use of electricity.

Talmud

One of the shared central texts of Judaism, alongside the Torah.

Torah (Written and Oral)

The foundational text of Judaism, believed by Orthodox Jews to have been revealed by God to Moses.

Zionism

A 19th-century political movement focused on establishing a Jewish homeland, leading to the creation of the State of Israel in 1948.

What is the core belief of Orthodox Judaism concerning the Torah?

Orthodox Judaism holds that the Written and Oral Torah was revealed by God to Moses exactly as received, and its 613 commandments are binding in every age.

Describe typical Shabbat observance for Orthodox Jews.

Orthodox Jews observe Shabbat (Friday sundown to Saturday night) by refraining from work, using electricity, and engaging in commerce.

What are kashrut laws in Judaism, and what do they require?

Kashrut are dietary laws that distinguish permitted (kosher) from forbidden foods and require the separation of meat and dairy.

What is Conservative Judaism's perspective on the historical development of the Torah?

Conservative Judaism accepts that the Torah developed over time and in historical context, but insists this does not diminish its authority.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the primary emphasis of Reform Judaism?

Reform Judaism emphasizes the ethical heart of Jewish teaching over its ritual details.

Name three characteristic practices found in Reform synagogues.

Reform synagogues often conduct services in the local language alongside Hebrew, seat men and women together, and ordain women and LGBTQ+ individuals as rabbis.

What is 'prophetic Judaism' in the context of Reform Judaism?

Prophetic Judaism is Reform's watchword, emphasizing that justice—what the prophets demanded—is more fundamental than any dietary rule or ritual.

Who founded Reconstructionist Judaism, and in what century?

Reconstructionist Judaism was founded in the 20th century by Rabbi Mordecai Kaplan.

How does Reconstructionist Judaism fundamentally define Judaism?

Reconstructionist Judaism views Judaism as an evolving civilization rather than a supernaturally revealed religion.
