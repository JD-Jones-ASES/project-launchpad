Important Terms (1/3)

Brahman

The ultimate, undifferentiated reality underlying all of existence; the ground of being itself.

Nasadiya Sukta (Hymn of Creation)

An ancient philosophical poem (Rigveda 10.129) that questions the origins of the universe and expresses profound uncertainty.

Upanishads

A collection of ancient Sanskrit texts that contain some of the central philosophical concepts and ideas of Hinduism, including the concept of Brahman.

sat-chit-ananda

A compound Sanskrit term used in the Upanishads to describe the nature of Brahman as absolute being (sat), consciousness (chit), and bliss (ananda).

Atman

The individual self or soul; in Hindu philosophy, it is understood to be identical with Brahman at its deepest level.

Important Terms (2/3)

Kalpas

Immense spans of time in Hindu cosmology, each lasting 4.32 billion years, representing one cycle of cosmic creation and destruction.

Trimurti

The "three forms" of the divine in Hinduism, comprising Brahma (the creator), Vishnu (the preserver), and Shiva (the destroyer), who represent the cyclical processes of the cosmos.

Brahma

The creator deity within the Trimurti, distinct from Brahman (the ultimate reality).

Vishnu

The preserver deity within the Trimurti, often associated with maintaining cosmic order.

Shiva

The destroyer deity within the Trimurti, associated with dissolution and transformation.

Important Terms (3/3)

Lila

The concept of divine play, suggesting that the creation and activity of the universe are a spontaneous, joyful, and free expression of the divine, not driven by necessity.

Rigveda

One of the four canonical sacred texts of Hinduism known as the Vedas; it contains hymns, prayers, and philosophical treatises, including the Nasadiya Sukta.

Beyond a Single Creation

Hindu cosmology does not propose a singular, instantaneous moment of creation by an individual creator figure. Instead, it posits a continuous, dynamic process.

Sat-Chit-Ananda

Identity with Atman

A core philosophical insight is that the individual self (atman) is, at its deepest, truest level, identical with Brahman. The spiritual journey often involves realizing this fundamental unity.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How long does a single kalpa last in Hindu cosmology?

A single kalpa lasts 4.32 billion years.

How does Hindu thought characterize the cosmos?

It is described as a living, breathing, pulsating process, rather than a machine built once and left to run.

Who are the three divine figures that form the Trimurti, and what are their respective roles?

Brahma the creator, Vishnu the preserver, and Shiva the destroyer. Together they represent the endless rhythm of creation, sustenance, and dissolution.

Explain the difference between 'Brahman' and 'Brahma.'

Brahman is the ultimate, undifferentiated reality and ground of being, while Brahma is a specific divine figure, the creator god within the Trimurti.

How should the Trimurti be understood in relation to Brahman?

The Trimurti are not three separate gods competing, but rather three aspects or faces of the single divine process that is Brahman expressing itself in time.
