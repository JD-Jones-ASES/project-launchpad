Radical Monotheism: The Shema

Absolute Unity

There are no rival divine powers or equal forces of evil.

The Nature of God

God is described as intensely personal—capable of speaking, hearing prayer, and grieving—yet remains utterly unlike anything human.

Prohibition of Idolatry

Judaism strictly forbids making images of God to prevent the human tendency to reduce the divine to something manageable or controllable.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/2)

Q: What are the six Hebrew words of the Shema, and what is their English translation?

> Answer: The words are Shema Yisrael, Adonai Eloheinu, Adonai Echad. They translate to: “Hear, O Israel, the Lord is our God, the Lord is One.”

Q: How does Jewish theology view the use of physical images or statues of God?

> Answer: It strictly forbids them. This is intended to resist the human impulse to reduce God to something manageable or controllable.

Q: Define the "Brit" and identify the primary roles of each partner.

> Answer: The Brit is a binding, mutual covenant. God (the senior partner) provides protection, guidance, and redemption. Israel provides obedience to the commandments and the practice of justice.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/2)

Q: Why does the Talmud record debates that arrive at no resolution?

> Answer: Because Judaism treats the conversation and engagement with the questions as sacred. It suggests that if God’s word is inexhaustible, multiple valid perspectives can coexist.

Q: How does the concept of Tikkun Olam change the perception of human agency in the world?

> Answer: It posits that the world is not "fixed" but is "being fixed." Humans are not passive observers but active partners with God in the work of restoring and healing creation.
