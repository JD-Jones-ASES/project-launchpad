A Living, Arguing Tradition: A Study Guide to the Branches of Judaism

Study Slides

Core Concepts and Movement Profiles

Movement. View of Torah and Law

Key Practices and Characteristics

Orthodox. Torah (Written and Oral) was revealed by God to Moses; all 613 commandments are binding.

Observe strict Shabbat and kashrut; pray in Hebrew; gender-separated worship; guidance from rabbinic decisors.

Conservative. Law is binding but allows for flexibility in interpretation; Torah developed within a historical context.

Middle position; many observe Shabbat and kashrut but with more leniency than the Orthodox branch.

Reform. Law is a human response to divine revelation shaped by history; emphasis on the ethical over the ritual.

Largest US movement; services in local languages; gender-neutral seating; ordains women and LGBTQ+ rabbis; focuses on "prophetic Judaism."

Reconstructionist. Judaism is an evolving civilization rather than a supernaturally revealed religion.

God is viewed as a non-personal force; practice is an expression of culture and identity rather than divine command.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Halakha

Translated as "Jewish law," it literally means "the way of walking"; a structured way of living in relationship with God.

Kashrut

Jewish dietary laws that distinguish permitted (kosher) foods from forbidden ones and require the separation of meat and dairy.

Messiah

In the context of Zionism, some Orthodox Jews believed only this figure could legitimately restore Israel.

Prophetic Judaism

A Reform Jewish emphasis stating that the pursuit of justice is more fundamental than ritual or dietary observance.

Rabbinic Decisors

Authoritative figures to whom Orthodox Jews look for guidance on new legal questions.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Shabbat

The Sabbath; observed from Friday sundown to Saturday night as a time to refrain from work, commerce, and the use of electricity.

Talmud

One of the shared central texts of Judaism, alongside the Torah.

Torah

The central reference of the religious Judaic tradition, consisting of the Written and Oral Torah.

Zionism

A 19th-century political movement seeking to establish a Jewish homeland in the land of Israel.
