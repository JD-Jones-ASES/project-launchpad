Buddhism: Dukkha and the Path to Nirvana Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

The Four Noble Truths

The foundational framework of Buddhist teaching, comprising the diagnosis of suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path to its cessation.

Dukkha (Suffering/Unsatisfactoriness)

A Pali word referring to the pervasive unsatisfactoriness, pain, grief, loss, and the impermanent nature of all conditioned existence. It's the inherent unreliability of life as ordinarily lived.

Tanha (Craving/Thirst)

The specific kind of desire that causes dukkha; a clinging, grasping attachment to things, people, or ideas, driven by the illusion that they can provide lasting satisfaction.

Anicca (Impermanence)

The Buddhist doctrine that all conditioned phenomena are in a constant state of flux and change; nothing lasts forever.

Anatta (No-Self)

The doctrine that there is no fixed, permanent, or immutable self or soul; what we perceive as "self" is a collection of impermanent processes.

Centrality of Mind

The Centrality of the Mind: Insights from the Dhammapada

• "All that we are is the result of what we have thought: it is founded on our thoughts, it is made up of our thoughts."

• This means that our mental states directly determine our experience of happiness or suffering. Evil thoughts lead to pain, while pure thoughts lead to happiness.

• The text further illustrates how harboring negative thoughts ("He abused me, he beat me...") perpetuates hatred, whereas letting go of such thoughts allows hatred to cease.

What do the terms 'anicca' and 'anatta' signify in Buddhism?

Anicca means impermanence, and anatta means without a fixed, permanent self. They describe the fundamental nature of reality that craving attempts to deny.

The Third Noble Truth states that cessation of suffering is possible, claiming that suffering is not the permanent condition of humanity.
