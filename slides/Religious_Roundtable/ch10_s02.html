The Covenant (*Brit*)

Covenant vs. Contract

Unlike a transactional contract, which ends once services are rendered and paid for, a covenant is relational. It creates a bond that persists through time, failure, and repair.

The Shema and Embodied Faith

Recitation

Observant Jews recite it twice daily and traditionally before death.

The Commandment to Love

It commands followers to love God with all their heart, soul, and might.

Daily Life

The *Shema* provides instructions for daily embodiment: teaching children, discussing the words at home and on the road, and physically binding the words to the hand and doorposts.

Orthopraxy vs. Orthodoxy

The Fundamental Question

While Christianity often asks, "Do you believe?", Judaism asks, "What do you do?"

Action as Enacted Belief

Belief is performed through the *mitzvot* (commandments) and the treatment of the vulnerable, such as the poor, the stranger, the widow, and the orphan.

Doubt vs. Observation

A Jew who struggles with doubt but observes the commandments is considered observant.

Ongoing Revelation

Oral Torah

The Talmud, a compendium of rabbinic discussion, is considered part of God’s revelation.

Wrestling with Text

Each generation continues the revelation by "wrestling" with the text, a tradition linked to the ancestor Jacob, who wrestled with God to receive a blessing.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (1/3)

Q: What is the literal meaning of the Hebrew word *brit*?

> **Answer:** It means a binding agreement with mutual obligations.

Q: How does the text distinguish between a "contract" and a "covenant"?

> **Answer:** A contract is transactional and ends when the transaction is complete. A covenant is relational, creating a bond between persons that persists through time and failure.

Q: What are the two different translations provided for the name God gives Moses (*Ehyeh asher ehyeh*)?

> **Answer:** "I am that I am" or "I will be what I will be."

Short-Answer Practice Questions (2/3)

Q: When do observant Jews traditionally recite the *Shema*?

> **Answer:** Twice daily—upon waking and before sleeping—and as the last prayer spoken before death.

Q: Define "orthopraxy" in the context of Jewish religious thought.

> **Answer:** Orthopraxy refers to "correct practice." It is the emphasis on what one does (observing commandments/mitzvot) rather than solely what one believes.

Q: What is the *am Yisrael*?

> **Answer:** It means "the people of Israel," the collective identity formed through the covenant at Sinai.

Short-Answer Practice Questions (3/3)

Q: According to the text, how is the Oral Torah (Talmud) viewed in relation to the revelation at Sinai?

> **Answer:** The Oral Torah is understood as being part of the same revelation at Sinai, signifying that revelation is ongoing rather than a finished event.
