Key Figures and Primary Texts

Entity. Description

Significance

Snorri Sturluson. An Icelandic chieftain, historian, and poet (1179–1241).

Author of the Prose Edda and Heimskringla; essential for preserving Norse mythology.

Prose Edda. Compiled around 1220 by Snorri Sturluson.

Served as a handbook for Norse mythology and the technical aspects of skaldic poetry.

Poetic Edda. A collection of older poems compiled by an unknown author around 1270.

Preserves oral traditions and heroic poems that predate the written text by centuries.

Heimskringla. A historical work written by Snorri Sturluson.

Provides a history of the Norwegian kings.

Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Heimskringla

A history of the Norwegian kings written by Snorri Sturluson.

Kenning

A riddle-like, compound poetic image used in skaldic verse to describe a common object or concept (e.g., "whale-road" for sea).

L'Anse aux Meadows

The oldest known European settlement in the Americas, located in Newfoundland and established by Leif Eriksson around 1000 CE.

Poetic Edda

A 13th-century collection of ancient mythological and heroic poems that preserve centuries of oral tradition.

Prose Edda

A 13th-century handbook of mythology and poetic technique compiled by Snorri Sturluson.

Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Skald

A Norse court poet and singer who maintained historical and sacred records through complex oral poetry.

Snorri Sturluson

An influential 13th-century Icelandic chieftain and scholar who recorded the most comprehensive surviving accounts of Norse myths.

Viking

A term specifically referring to Norse people who engaged in sea-raids, though often used to describe the era of Norse expansion (793–1066 CE).

Key Concepts (1/2)

How did Norse Paganism originate and develop?

It grew slowly over centuries and millennia among Germanic and Scandinavian peoples, without a single founder, founding moment, or written revelation, passed...

Which modern peoples are descended from the Germanic and Scandinavian ancestors who practiced Norse Paganism?

Norwegians, Swedes, Danes, Icelanders, and Anglo-Saxons.

What was the role of 'skalds' in pre-textual Norse society?

Skalds were poet-singers who composed and performed elaborate verse, preserving sacred and historical memory, often around fires in chieftains' halls.

Define 'skald'.

A Norse court poet, responsible for composing and performing elaborate verse celebrating the deeds of gods and heroes; a keeper of sacred and historical memory.

What poetic techniques made skaldic verse a reliable form of oral record?

Complex forms, dense with alliteration, internal rhyme, and riddle-like kennings, which made the poems difficult to alter during transmission.

Explain what a 'kenning' is in Norse poetry.

A compound poetic image used in skaldic verse, such as 'the whale-road' for the sea.

Key Concepts (2/2)

When did Norse Paganism reach its 'most dramatic expression,' and what was this period called?

During the Viking Age, approximately 793–1066 CE.

What was the specific meaning of the term 'Viking' during the Viking Age?

It referred specifically to those who went on sea-raids; not all Norse people were Vikings.

Describe the geographical range of Norse exploration and trade during the Viking Age.

From Newfoundland in the west to Constantinople in the east.

What were the key elements of the Norse mythological imagination carried by these voyagers?

A cosmos organized around a vast world-tree, populated by gods and giants, and headed toward a catastrophic — and ultimately regenerative — end.
