Study Slides

The Tao Te Ching (Daodejing)

Structure

The text is composed of eighty-one short chapters, some of which are only a single paragraph in length.

Thematic Duality

The content alternates between the cosmic—describing the Tao as the silent ground of all being—and the political—offering guidance to rulers on how to govern through minimal intervention.

Purpose

These paradoxes are intended as spiritual practices rather than puzzles. They are meant to disrupt the mind's tendency to seek easy answers and encourage a deeper, more contemplative state.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Chuang Tzu (Zhuangzi)

The second foundational text of Taoism, consisting of parables and essays; also the name of its attributed 4th-century BCE author.

Daozang

The expansive Taoist "Canon" which includes hundreds of volumes of ritual, meditative, and alchemical writings.

Laozi

The figure to whom the Tao Te Ching is traditionally attributed.

Tao

The "Way"; described as the silent ground of all being and the movement between all things.

Tao Te Ching (Daodejing)

"The Classic of the Way and Its Power," the 81-chapter foundational scripture of Taoism.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Te

Virtue or power; a central concept explored in the Tao Te Ching.

Wu Wei

Effortless action; the practice of living and acting in such harmony with the Tao that one finds "the space that is already there," exemplified by the story of Cook Ding.

Who is the Tao Te Ching traditionally attributed to?

Laozi.

How is the Tao Te Ching structured and what is its typical language style?

It consists of eighty-one short chapters, some barely a paragraph long, and uses deliberately paradoxical language.

What two main themes does the Tao Te Ching alternate between?

The cosmic, describing the Tao as the silent ground of all being, and the political, advising rulers on wise governance by doing less.

What is the purpose of the deliberate paradoxes in the Tao Te Ching?

They are not puzzles to be solved but practices to be sat with, designed to unsettle the mind's habit of grabbing for easy answers.
