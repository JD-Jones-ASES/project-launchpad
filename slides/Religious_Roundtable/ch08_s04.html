Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Ásatrú

A modern religious movement reviving pre-Christian Norse Paganism; the name translates to "loyalty to the Æsir" in Icelandic.

Blót

A seasonal ritual or festival celebrated by modern Ásatrú practitioners.

Continental Germanic Religion

The branch of Germanic paganism practiced by various tribes in Central Europe prior to Christianization.

Eddas

Medieval Icelandic texts that serve as the primary written record of Norse mythology and tradition.

Heathens

A common term used to describe practitioners of modern Norse or Germanic paganism.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Runes

Ancient Germanic alphabetic characters often used as symbols; currently a point of contention regarding their appropriation by extremist groups.

The Troth

An international Heathen organization known for its formal statements affirming that Norse Paganism is open to all ethnicities.

Þórr (Thor) / Þunor

The Norse and Anglo-Saxon names, respectively, for the god of thunder.

Key Concepts (1/2)

Was ancient Norse Paganism a single, unified religion with a central authority?

No, it was a family of related traditions that varied significantly by region, period, and community, without a central authority, creed, or fixed scripture.

Name three major regional variations of ancient Germanic religion mentioned in the text.

Norse religion (Scandinavia, Iceland), Anglo-Saxon religion (England), and Continental Germanic religion (Central Europe).

What did the ancient regional variations of Germanic religion share in common?

They shared the same basic family of gods and a similar cosmological framework.

How did the ancient regional variations of Germanic religion primarily differ?

They differed in emphasis and in the specific names they used for shared deities (e.g., Norse Odin vs. Anglo-Saxon Woden, Norse Þórr vs. Anglo-Saxon Þunor).

What historical sources provide most of our knowledge about the Norse tradition specifically?

The Eddas, as remembered in medieval Iceland.

Which English day of the week is named after the Norse god Týr?

Tuesday (named for Tiw, the Old English form of Týr).
