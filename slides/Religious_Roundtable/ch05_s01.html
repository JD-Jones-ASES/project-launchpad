Chapter 5, Section 1

Core Beliefs of Buddhism—The Four Noble Truths and the Eightfold Path

Study Slides

I. The Three Marks of Existence

Mark. Term

Definition and Description

First Mark. Dukkha

Often translated as "suffering," it more precisely refers to the pervasive unsatisfactoriness and instability of conditioned experience. This includes physical pain, the loss of loved ones, and the subtle dissatisfaction of knowing that pleasure is temporary.

Second Mark. Anicca

Impermanence. The teaching that nothing in the physical or mental world is permanent or fixed; every person, feeling, and empire will eventually change or disappear.

Third Mark. Anatta

Non-self. The radical concept that there is no permanent, unchanging self or "solid I" at the center of experience. The self is viewed as a dynamic, shifting bundle of physical and mental events rather than a fixed entity.

III. The Noble Eightfold Path (1/2)

Right View

Developing a deep understanding of the Four Noble Truths and the true nature of reality.

Right Intention

Cultivating mental states focused on renunciation, goodwill, and non-harming.

Right Speech

Engaging in communication that is truthful, kind, and helpful.

Right Action

Behaving in ways that avoid causing harm to oneself or others.

Right Livelihood

Choosing a profession or means of earning a living that does not cause harm.

III. The Noble Eightfold Path (2/2)

Right Effort

The active practice of cultivating wholesome mental states while releasing unwholesome ones.

Right Mindfulness

Maintaining a clear, present-moment awareness of the body, feelings, mind, and mental objects.

Right Concentration

Developing the ability to maintain deep meditative focus.

IV. Karma, Samsara, and the Noble Silence

Karma

Refers to action and its consequences. Actions motivated by greed, hatred, or delusion generate karma that continues the cycle of existence. Conversely, actions driven by wisdom and compassion lead...

Samsara

The cycle of death and rebirth. The ultimate goal of Buddhism is to escape this cycle entirely to reach the "unconditioned freedom" of nirvana, rather than simply seeking a better subsequent life.

VII. Glossary of Key Terms (1/2)

Anatta

Non-self; the teaching that there is no permanent, unchanging soul or self underlying experience.

Anicca

Impermanence; the fact that all conditioned phenomena are constantly changing.

Dukkha

The unsatisfactory and unstable quality of conditioned experience; ranges from physical pain to subtle dissatisfaction.

Karma

Action and consequence; the moral law wherein actions motivated by wisdom lead to liberation and those motivated by greed lead to further suffering.

Nirvana

The cessation of suffering; a state of unconditioned freedom reached when craving is extinguished.

VII. Glossary of Key Terms (2/2)

Samsara

The cycle of death and rebirth that individuals seek to escape.

Tanha

Craving or "thirst"; the grasping habit of the mind that serves as the root cause of suffering.
