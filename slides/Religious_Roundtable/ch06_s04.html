Chapter 6, Section 4

Philosophical and Religious Taoism

Study Slides

Philosophical Taoism (Daojia)

Focus

Understanding the nature of the Tao and cultivating personal wisdom.

Methodology

Contemplative practices, releasing rigid thinking, and seeking alignment with the Tao.

Core Principle

Wu wei, or "effortless action," which involves finding the response that a situation naturally calls for rather than forcing an outcome.

Primary Text

The Tao Te Ching is the most well-known representative of this stream.

Religious Taoism (Daojiao)

Structure

Includes a formal priesthood, liturgy, temples, festivals, and a complex pantheon of divine figures.

Focus

Practical and communal concerns, such as healing the sick, protecting communities from harmful spirits, and marking life transitions (birth, marriage, and death).

Influences

Combines philosophical texts with folk religion, shamanic traditions, and Chinese cosmology.

Longevity and Cultivation

• Breathing exercises.

• Meditation and ritual practices.

• The goal of extending life or transcending death entirely.

Glossary of Important Terms (1/2)

Daojia

Philosophical Taoism; the "School of the Way" focused on contemplation and wisdom.

Daojiao

Religious Taoism; the "Teaching of the Way" focused on ritual, community, and longevity.

Gong fu

Disciplined practice over time; the root of the term "kung fu."

Laozi and Zhuangzi

The foundational philosophers whose texts form the basis of Philosophical Taoism.

Tai chi (taijiquan)

A Taoist martial art characterized by slow, flowing, circular movements.

Glossary of Important Terms (2/2)

Tao

The "Way"; the fundamental nature of reality that practitioners seek to align with.

Wu wei

"Effortless action"; the practice of aligning one’s actions with the natural flow of a situation.

Yin-yang

A core principle of Chinese cosmology representing balance and the interplay of opposing forces; a foundational concept for Tai Chi.

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to scholars, what are the two broad streams into which Taoism is often divided?

Philosophical Taoism and Religious Taoism.

What is the Chinese term for Philosophical Taoism and what does it mean?

Daojia (daojia), meaning 'the school of the Way.'

What is the Chinese term for Religious Taoism and what does it mean?

Daojiao (daojiao), meaning 'the teaching of the Way.'

Which ancient Taoist texts are Philosophical Taoism rooted in?

The texts of Laozi and Zhuangzi.

What is the primary focus of Philosophical Taoism?

Understanding the nature of the Tao and cultivating personal wisdom and freedom by aligning oneself with it.

Describe the general approach or tendency of Philosophical Taoism.

It tends toward the contemplative: stepping back from social roles, releasing rigid thinking, and finding effortless action.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Which famous Taoist text, familiar to Western readers, is associated with Philosophical Taoism?

The Tao Te Ching.

Approximately when did Religious Taoism begin to develop as an organized religion?

Roughly from the 2nd century CE onward.

Name three defining features of Religious Taoism as an organized religion.

Priests, liturgy, temples, festivals, a rich pantheon of divine figures, and elaborate ritual practices (any three).

Besides philosophical texts, what other traditions does Religious Taoism draw upon?
