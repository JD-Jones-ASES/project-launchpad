Important Terms (1/3)

Ragnarök

In Norse mythology, the prophesied final battle where the gods will die, the world will be destroyed, and a new world will rise from the sea. It signifies both destruction and renewal.

Wyrd

The Norse concept of fate, destiny, or the cosmic pattern woven by the Norns. It encompasses the accumulated weight of what has been, shaping what can be.

Iðunn

A goddess whose golden apples the Æsir (gods) must regularly eat to maintain their youth and prevent aging.

Baldr

The most beloved of the gods, whose death, brought about by Loki's trickery, cannot be undone, highlighting the irreversibility of loss even for divine beings.

Fenrir

A monstrous wolf, destined to swallow Odin during Ragnarök.

Important Terms (2/3)

Midgard Serpent (Jörmungandr)

A colossal sea serpent, Thor's arch-nemesis, who he will kill but then die from its venom at Ragnarök.

Surtr

A fire-giant from Muspelheim who will defeat Freyr during Ragnarök and bring fire to destroy the world.

Norns

Three female beings (Urðr, Verðandi, and Skuld) who are said to weave the web of fate (`wyrd`) for gods and humans alike.

Hávamál

Meaning "Sayings of the High One," a collection of Old Norse poems from the Poetic Edda, attributed to Odin, offering practical wisdom and advice on life, conduct, and mortality.

Yggdrasil

The immense sacred ash tree that connects the nine worlds in Norse cosmology. Odin famously hung himself from its branches to gain knowledge of the runes.

Important Terms (3/3)

Valhalla

Odin's majestic hall in Asgard, where half of those who die heroically in battle are taken by the Valkyries to feast, train, and prepare to fight alongside Odin at Ragnarök.

Valkyries

Female figures who choose those slain in battle worthy of going to Valhalla, transporting them to Odin's hall.

Key Concepts (1/2)

According to Norse Paganism, what is the fundamental nature of suffering in reality?

Suffering is woven into the structure of reality, including for the gods, and is not an accident or flaw.

How does Norse Paganism's response to suffering differ from that of Buddhism or Communism?

Norse Paganism advocates meeting suffering with courage, rather than seeking escape (Buddhism) or overthrowing the system that causes it (Communism).

Define 'Ragnarök' in Norse mythology.

Ragnarök is the prophesied final battle at which the gods will die, the world will be destroyed, and crucially, a new world will rise from the sea.

Name three prominent gods and their prophesied fates during Ragnarök.

Odin will be swallowed by the wolf Fenrir; Thor will kill the Midgard Serpent and then die from its venom; Freyr will fall to the fire-giant Surtr.

What is the 'crucial' aspect of Ragnarök beyond simple catastrophe?

It signifies the death of the old world and the birth of a new one, though the current gods will not survive to see it.

In the Norse worldview, what does suffering signify about a life lived fully?

Suffering is considered the price of a life lived fully.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Define the Norse term 'wyrd.'

Wyrd refers to fate, destiny, or the cosmic pattern woven by the Norns.

Is one's 'wyrd' entirely fixed in Norse belief? Explain.

No, one's wyrd is not entirely fixed; it is the accumulated weight of what has been, shaping what can be. One cannot escape it, but can choose how to face it.

How does facing one's 'wyrd' offer an alternative to Buddhist liberation or Marxist revolution?

It offers acceptance with dignity, rather than escaping a cycle (Buddhism) or changing a system (Communism).
