What is the New Testament Greek word for faith, and what does it encompass?

The Greek word is 'pistis', meaning trust, conviction, and loyalty, encompassing a total orientation of the self (mind, will, affections) toward God.

How does Christian faith (pistis) differ from credulity?

Pistis is not carelessly accepting things without thought simply because an authority says so.

What analogy does the text use to explain faith as trust extended toward something real but not fully visible?

Key Concepts (2/2)

Quote the famous single-sentence definition of faith from Hebrews 11:1.

"Now faith is the substance of things hoped for, the evidence of things not seen."

In Hebrews 11:1, what is significant about the word 'substance' in relation to faith?

It means faith is not merely a feeling or a wish, but has substance, being real in a way that precedes and grounds ordinary visible reality.

According to Thomas Aquinas, what is the relationship between faith and reason?

Faith is not against reason, but goes beyond reason.

Which specific Christian claims, according to Aquinas, cannot be reached by reason alone and require faith?

The Trinity, the Incarnation, and the resurrection.

How did Thomas Aquinas define faith?

Faith is the intellect assenting to divine truth on the authority of God who reveals.
