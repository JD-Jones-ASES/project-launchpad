Important Terms (1/2)

De (duh)

Often translated as "virtue" or "power." In Taoism, it's the natural, effortless virtue that flows from a life aligned with the Tao, emerging when one stops forcing.

Wu wei (woo-WAY)

Literally "non-action" or "effortless action." The Taoist principle of achieving success and harmony by not forcing, not dominating, and not striving, but rather by aligning with the natural flow o...

Pu (poo)

"The uncarved block." A Taoist metaphor for the original, undistorted, simple nature of a person before social conditioning and artificial virtues alter them.

Ziran (tsih-RAN)

"Naturalness" or "self-so-ness." The quality of being spontaneous, authentic, and unforced, following one's own nature without strain or pretense.

Tao (Dao)

The fundamental, unnamable principle that underlies the universe, representing the natural order, the path, or the way.

Important Terms (2/2)

Tao Te Ching

The foundational text of Taoism, traditionally attributed to Laozi, containing teachings on the Tao, virtue, and ideal governance.

Laozi

The revered ancient Chinese philosopher traditionally credited with writing the Tao Te Ching and founding Taoism.

The Water Metaphor (Tao Te Ching, Chapter 8)

Benefits all things and does not compete

Water nourishes everything without demanding recognition or striving for supremacy.

Flows to low places

It takes the path of least resistance, occupying places others reject, symbolizing humility and acceptance.

Is like the Tao

Its qualities of non-competition, humility, flexibility (flowing around obstacles rather than fighting them), and life-giving nature perfectly embody the principles of the Tao.

Wu Wei: Effortless Action, Not Inaction

Skilled craftsman

A craftsman exhibits `wu wei` by allowing the knife to follow the natural grain of the wood, rather than fighting against it.

Good ruler

A ruler practicing `wu wei` governs so subtly and lightly that the people feel they have accomplished things "themselves."
