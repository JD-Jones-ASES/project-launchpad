Important Terms (1/3)

Subduction

A geological process where one tectonic plate slides beneath another, typically an oceanic plate beneath a continental plate, at a convergent boundary.

Seismic Waves

Energy waves generated by an earthquake, radiating outward from the focus. Types include P-waves (compressional), S-waves (shear), and surface waves (most damaging).

Focus

The point within the Earth where an earthquake originates.

Inertia

The tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion (Newton's First Law). In earthquakes, a building's mass resists the ground's sudden movement.

Base Isolation System

An engineering technique using rubber-and-steel pads between a building and its foundation to absorb seismic energy and reduce the acceleration transmitted to the structure.

Important Terms (2/3)

Ductile

A material property indicating the ability to deform plastically (bend, stretch, or drawn into a wire) without fracturing. Steel is ductile.

Brittle

A material property indicating the tendency to fracture with little or no plastic deformation when subjected to stress. Unreinforced concrete and brick are brittle.

Metallic Bonding

A type of chemical bonding characteristic of metals, involving a "sea" of delocalized electrons shared among positively charged metal ions, giving rise to properties like ductility.

Reinforced Concrete

Concrete that has steel bars (rebar) embedded within it to provide tensile strength and ductility, complementing concrete's compressive strength.

Inter-story Drift

The relative lateral displacement between one floor of a building and the floor below it during an earthquake, a key factor in structural damage and human injury.

Important Terms (3/3)

Tuned Mass Damper

A passive device, often a large pendulum, installed in tall structures to reduce dynamic response to seismic activity and wind by swinging in opposition to the building's motion.

Earth Science: Understanding Why Earthquakes Happen

Location

Chile is located on a convergent plate boundary where the Nazca Plate subducts beneath the South American Plate.

Mechanism

Enormous stress builds up along the fault as the oceanic plate dives into the mantle. When this stress exceeds rock strength, plates suddenly slip, releasing energy as seismic waves.

Seismic Waves

Engineering Application

Engineers use plate tectonics to identify earthquake zones, seismology to predict shaking intensity, and understanding wave types to anticipate directional forces on buildings.
