Important Terms (1/2)

Atmosphere

The layer of gases surrounding Earth, held in place by gravity, which provides air, regulates temperature, protects against radiation, and drives weather.

Troposphere

The lowest layer of the atmosphere (surface to ~12 km), where nearly all weather occurs, and temperature decreases with altitude.

Tropopause

The upper boundary of the troposphere, where temperature stops decreasing.

Stratosphere

The second layer of the atmosphere (12 km to 50 km), characterized by increasing temperatures with altitude due to the ozone layer.

Ozone layer

A region within the stratosphere (15-35 km) with a high concentration of ozone (O3) molecules that absorb most of the Sun's harmful ultraviolet (UV) radiation.

Important Terms (2/2)

Mesosphere

The layer above the stratosphere (50 km to 85 km), where temperature decreases with altitude, reaching the coldest temperatures in the atmosphere. Most meteors burn up here.

Thermosphere

The outermost layer of the atmosphere (85 km to 600 km or beyond), where temperatures can be extremely high due to absorption of solar radiation, despite very low air density. Auroras occur here.

Atmospheric pressure

The force exerted by the weight of the air above a given point on Earth's surface, which decreases with increasing altitude.

The Atmosphere: Earth's Gaseous Blanket

• The atmosphere is a thin layer of gas surrounding Earth, held in place by gravity.

• It's crucial for life, providing breathable air, regulating temperature, protecting from harmful radiation, and driving weather and climate.

• Air density decreases rapidly with altitude; at 100 km, it's almost a vacuum.

List three essential roles of Earth's atmosphere for life.

It provides the air we breathe, regulates temperature, and protects against harmful radiation (also drives weather and climate).

What are the two primary gases that compose Earth's atmosphere, and what are their approximate percentages by volume?

Nitrogen (N2) at approximately 78% and Oxygen (O2) at approximately 21%.

What is the primary role of nitrogen (N2) in the atmosphere?

Nitrogen makes up the bulk of the air and plays a critical role in the nitrogen cycle, though it is relatively unreactive in the atmosphere itself.

Why is oxygen (O2) essential for life, and how was it produced in Earth's atmosphere?

Oxygen is essential for aerobic respiration in animals and most other organisms. It was produced over billions of years by photosynthetic organisms.

Name three trace gases in the atmosphere that have a significant influence on Earth's temperature through the greenhouse effect.

Carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), and water vapor.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Why is water vapor considered especially important among the trace gases?

Its concentration varies widely (0-4%), it is the most abundant greenhouse gas, and it plays a central role in weather by forming clouds and precipitation.

On what basis do scientists divide the atmosphere into distinct layers?

Scientists divide the atmosphere into layers based on how temperature changes with altitude.

Describe the troposphere, including its altitude range and the general temperature trend within this layer.

The troposphere is the lowest layer, extending from Earth's surface to about 12 km altitude. Temperature decreases steadily with altitude in this layer.

What significant atmospheric phenomena occur almost exclusively in the troposphere, and what percentage of the atmosphere's mass does it contain?

Nearly all weather takes place in the troposphere. It contains about 75% of the atmosphere's total mass.

Describe the stratosphere, including its altitude range and the general temperature trend within this layer.

The stratosphere is the second layer, extending from about 12 km to 50 km altitude. Temperature increases with altitude in this layer.
