Important Terms

Organic compound

A compound that contains carbon atoms bonded to other carbon atoms and usually to hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, or other elements.

Inorganic compound

A compound that does not contain carbon-carbon bonds or carbon-hydrogen bonds. A few carbon-containing compounds (CO$_2$, CO, carbonates, and cyanides) are traditionally classified as inorganic.

Hydrocarbon

An organic compound composed entirely of carbon and hydrogen atoms. They are classified by the types of bonds between carbon atoms: single (alkanes), double (alkenes), or triple (alkynes).

Structural formula

A diagram that shows the arrangement of atoms within a molecule. Each covalent bond is drawn as a line between the bonded atoms.

Isomer

One of two or more compounds that have the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements of atoms. Isomers can have very different physical and chemical properties.

Carbon's Bonding Versatility

Valence Electrons

Carbon is in Group 14 and has four valence electrons.

Octet Rule

To achieve a stable octet, carbon needs four more electrons. It typically forms four covalent bonds by sharing electrons, rather than gaining or losing them due to its intermediate electronegativity.

Hydrocarbons: The Simplest Organic Compounds

Definition

Compounds composed entirely of carbon and hydrogen atoms.

Significance

Hydrocarbons are fundamental to fossil fuels, plastics, and countless other materials.

Examples

Alkanes

Hydrocarbons containing only single bonds between carbon atoms.

General Formula for Straight-Chain Alkanes

C$_n$H$_{2n+2}$, where 'n' is the number of carbon atoms. This formula accounts for the hydrogen atoms needed to complete each carbon's four bonds.

Scientist Spotlight: Dorothy Hodgkin

Pioneer in X-ray Crystallography

British chemist Dorothy Hodgkin revolutionized the determination of complex molecular structures.

Key Achievements

Solved the structures of penicillin (1945), vitamin B$_{12}$ (1954), and insulin (1969). These were monumental tasks requiring immense skill and perseverance.

Significance

Her work demonstrated that knowing the precise three-dimensional arrangement of atoms is essential for understanding how molecules function, particularly in biological systems.

Nobel Prize

Awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1964.

Think Deeper: Carbon vs. Silicon for Life

Bond Strength

C-C bonds (~348 kJ/mol) are significantly stronger and more stable than Si-Si bonds (~226 kJ/mol), allowing carbon to form longer, more stable chains.

Double/Triple Bonds

Carbon forms strong, stable C=C and C≡C bonds, which are crucial for the diversity and reactivity of organic molecules. Silicon-silicon double bonds are very weak and rare under normal conditions.

Oxides

Carbon dioxide (CO$_2$) is a gas, easily exchanged with the atmosphere by living organisms. Silicon dioxide (SiO$_2$) is a solid (quartz, sand), making "exhaling" difficult for a silicon-based life...

Diversity of Compounds

Carbon forms strong bonds with a wider variety of elements, leading to greater molecular diversity and functional group possibilities essential for life's complexity.
