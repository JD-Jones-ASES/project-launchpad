Important Terms (1/3)

Wave

A disturbance that transfers energy from one place to another without transporting matter.

Medium

The material through which a wave travels.

Transverse Wave

A wave in which the particles of the medium vibrate perpendicular to the direction the wave travels.

Crest

The highest point of a transverse wave.

Trough

The lowest point of a transverse wave.

Important Terms (2/3)

Rest Position

The equilibrium position of the medium's particles when no wave is passing through.

Longitudinal Wave

A wave in which the particles of the medium vibrate parallel to the direction the wave travels.

Compression

A region in a longitudinal wave where particles are pushed close together, resulting in higher pressure.

Rarefaction

A region in a longitudinal wave where particles are spread apart, resulting in lower pressure.

Wavelength ($\lambda$)

The distance between two consecutive corresponding points on a wave (e.g., crest to crest, compression to compression), measured in meters (m).

Important Terms (3/3)

Frequency ($f$)

The number of complete wave cycles that pass a given point per unit time, measured in hertz (Hz). ($1 \text{ Hz} = 1 \text{ cycle/second}$)

Amplitude

The maximum displacement of a particle from its rest position, indicating the energy carried by the wave.

Period ($T$)

The time required for one complete wave cycle to pass a given point, measured in seconds (s).

Hertz (Hz)

The SI unit of frequency, equal to one cycle per second.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is a wave?

A disturbance that transfers energy from one place to another without transporting matter.

What does a wave transfer, and what does it NOT transfer?

A wave transfers energy, but it does not transport matter. The medium (material) through which the wave travels oscillates in place.

What is the medium in the context of waves?

The material (e.g., rope, water, air) through which a wave travels.

Define a transverse wave.

A wave in which the particles of the medium vibrate perpendicular (at right angles) to the direction the wave travels.

Give examples of transverse waves.

Waves on a rope, water surface waves, and all electromagnetic waves (e.g., light).

What are the highest and lowest points of a transverse wave called?

The highest point is the crest, and the lowest point is the trough.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Define a longitudinal wave.

A wave in which the particles of the medium vibrate parallel (in the same direction) to the direction the wave travels.

Give a common example of a longitudinal wave.

Sound waves in air.

What are the regions of a longitudinal wave where particles are bunched together and spread apart called?

Regions where particles are bunched together are compressions; regions where they are spread apart are rarefactions.

How does pressure change in the compressions and rarefactions of a longitudinal wave?

Pressure is higher than normal in compressions and lower than normal in rarefactions.

Define frequency, and state its symbol and SI unit.
