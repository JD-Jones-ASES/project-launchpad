Important Terms (1/2)

Electrical power

The rate at which electrical energy is converted into other forms of energy (light, heat, motion, sound). Measured in watts (W).

Watt (W)

The unit of electrical power, equivalent to one joule per second (J/s).

Kilowatt-hour (kWh)

A unit of energy equal to the energy used by a 1 kW device operating for one hour. ($1kWh = 3.6 \times 10^6 J$)

Fuse

A safety device containing a thin strip of metal designed to melt and break a circuit if the current exceeds a safe level.

Circuit breaker

An electromagnetic switch that automatically opens a circuit when the current exceeds a safe level; it can be reset manually.

Important Terms (2/2)

Grounding

The process of connecting the metal case of an appliance or a circuit to the Earth via a low-resistance path, providing a safe route for current in case of an electrical fault.

Applying Concepts: Building Circuits (Investigation)

Series Circuits

Current is the same at all points; voltage drops add up to the total battery voltage. Removing one component breaks the entire circuit.

Parallel Circuits

Voltage is the same across each branch; branch currents add up to the total current. Removing one component does not affect others in parallel.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is electrical power?

Electrical power is the rate at which electrical energy is converted into other forms of energy (light, heat, motion, sound).

What are the SI unit for electrical power and its equivalent?

The SI unit for electrical power is the watt (W), where 1W = 1J/s (one joule per second).

State the primary equation for electrical power and explain the role of its variables.

The primary equation is P = IV, where P is power (W), I is current (A), and V is voltage (V). Voltage (V) indicates energy per coulomb, and current (I) indic...

How can Ohm's law be used to derive another form of the electrical power equation involving current and resistance?

By substituting V = IR (from Ohm's law) into P = IV, you get P = I^2 R.

How can Ohm's law be used to derive another form of the electrical power equation involving voltage and resistance?

By substituting I = V/R (from Ohm's law) into P = IV, you get P = V^2 / R.

When solving a problem, which form of the power equation (P=IV, P=I^2R, or P=V^2/R) should you use?

All three forms are equivalent. You should choose the one that uses the quantities you already know or are given in the problem.
