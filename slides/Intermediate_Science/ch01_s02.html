Key Concepts (1/2)

Two Fundamental Cell Designs

All cells are classified as either prokaryotic (simpler, no nucleus or membrane-bound organelles) or eukaryotic (larger, complex, with a nucleus and various membrane-bound organelles).

Prokaryotic Cells

These are typically smaller and simpler, lacking a true nucleus (DNA in a nucleoid region) and internal membranes. Bacteria and archaea are examples, thriving in diverse environments.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Endosymbiotic Theory

This theory explains the origin of mitochondria and chloroplasts, suggesting they evolved from free-living bacteria that were engulfed by ancestral eukaryotic cells, forming a symbiotic relationship.

Common Misconception

Plant cells do have mitochondria, in addition to chloroplasts. Chloroplasts make sugar from light, and mitochondria break down that sugar to release usable energy (ATP), just like in animal cells.

The Nucleus — Command Center

Structure

Usually the largest organelle. Surrounded by a double membrane called the nuclear envelope, which has nuclear pores for molecular transport.

Contents

Contains long strands of DNA (genetic instructions). When not dividing, DNA is spread out as chromatin. Also contains the nucleolus, where ribosome components are assembled.

Function

Controls cell activities and stores genetic material.

Ribosomes — Protein Factories

Structure

Tiny, non-membrane-bound structures, visible only with electron microscopes.

Function

Read genetic messages (from DNA) and use them to build proteins. Proteins are essential for enzymes, structure, hormones, etc.

Location

Can float freely in the cytoplasm or be attached to the endoplasmic reticulum.

Lysosomes — Recycling Centers

Structure

Small, spherical, membrane-bound organelles filled with powerful digestive enzymes.

Function

Break down old or damaged organelles for recycling, digest food particles, and destroy invading bacteria or viruses. Abundant in white blood cells for immune response.

Importance of Membrane

The membrane prevents enzymes from leaking and digesting the cell itself.

Cytoskeleton — Internal Scaffolding

Structure

A dynamic network of protein fibers crisscrossing the cytoplasm.

Components

Has three main components, including microfilaments (thin fibers).

Function

Provides structural support, maintains cell shape, enables cell movement, and facilitates the transport of materials within the cell.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What are the two fundamental categories of cells based on their internal organization?

Prokaryotic cells and eukaryotic cells.

Define a prokaryotic cell and provide examples.

A cell that lacks a membrane-bound nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles. Examples include bacteria and archaea.

Define a eukaryotic cell and provide examples.

A cell that contains a membrane-bound nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles. All plants, animals, fungi, and protists are eukaryotes.

How do prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells differ in terms of complexity and internal structures?

Where is the DNA located in a prokaryotic cell?

It floats freely in the cytoplasm in a region called the nucleoid, as prokaryotic cells do not have a membrane-bound nucleus.

Identify three key differences between typical plant and animal cells.

Plant cells have a rigid cell wall, chloroplasts, and a large central vacuole. Animal cells lack these but typically possess centrioles.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the primary function of the cell wall in plant cells?

Made of cellulose, the cell wall provides structural support, protection, and gives plant cells a fixed, boxy shape.

Which organelle is responsible for photosynthesis in plant cells?

Chloroplasts.

Why do animal cells not have chloroplasts?

Animal cells obtain energy by consuming food, whereas chloroplasts are specialized to capture light energy for photosynthesis.

What is the role of the large central vacuole in plant cells?

It stores water, salts, and waste products. When full, it pushes against the cell wall, maintaining turgor pressure and cell rigidity.

Do plant cells have mitochondria? Explain.

Yes. Plant cells have both chloroplasts (for sugar production from light) and mitochondria (to break down sugar for usable energy, ATP), just like animal cells.

What is the nucleus often called and what is its main function?

Often called the 'control center' or 'command center' of the cell, its main function is to contain the cell's DNA and control cell activities.
