Important Terms (1/2)

Power

The rate at which work is done or energy is transferred; measures how quickly energy is used or delivered.

Watt (W)

The SI unit of power, equivalent to one joule of work done per second (1W = 1 J/s). Named after James Watt.

Kilowatt (kW)

A unit of power equal to 1000 watts. Commonly used for larger appliances and motors.

Horsepower (hp)

An older unit of power, still used for engines, approximately equal to 746 watts (1 hp ≈ 746W).

Kilowatt-hour (kWh)

A unit of *energy* (not power), typically used on electricity bills. $1 \text{ kWh} = 3.6 \times 10^6 \text{ J}$.

Units of Power

Watt (W)

The SI unit, $1 \text{ W} = 1 \text{ J/s}$. This means that a device with a power rating of 100 W uses or delivers 100 joules of energy every second.

Kilowatt (kW)

A larger unit, $1 \text{ kW} = 1000 \text{ W}$. Electric motors, large appliances, and car engines often have ratings in kilowatts.

Horsepower (hp)

An older, non-SI unit still used for engines (e.g., in cars). $1 \text{ hp} \approx 746 \text{ W}$.

Kilowatt-hour (kWh)

Crucially, the kilowatt-hour is a unit of *energy*, not power. It represents the energy consumed by a 1-kilowatt device operating for one hour.

Emmy Noether and the Conservation of Energy

Time Symmetry

If the laws of physics remain the same over time (they don't change from moment to moment), then **energy is conserved**.

Spatial Symmetry

If the laws of physics are the same at every location in space, then **momentum is conserved**.

Rotational Symmetry

If the laws of physics are the same regardless of orientation, then **angular momentum is conserved**.
