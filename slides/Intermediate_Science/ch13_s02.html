Important Terms (1/2)

Ionic bond

A chemical bond formed by the electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions, typically resulting from the complete transfer of one or more electrons from a metal atom to a nonmetal atom.

Cation

A positively charged ion, formed when an atom (usually a metal) loses one or more electrons.

Anion

A negatively charged ion, formed when an atom (usually a nonmetal) gains one or more electrons.

Electron transfer

The movement of one or more valence electrons from one atom to another, leading to the formation of ions and, subsequently, an ionic bond.

Formula unit

The simplest whole-number ratio of ions represented in an ionic compound, indicating the proportion of cations to anions in the crystal lattice. It does not represent a discrete molecule.

Important Terms (2/2)

Crystal lattice

A regular, repeating three-dimensional arrangement of ions in an ionic compound, designed to maximize attractive forces between opposite charges and minimize repulsive forces between like charges.

The Nature of Ionic Bonds

Sodium (Na)

A Group 1 metal with one valence electron (electron configuration: 2, 8, 1). It tends to lose this electron to achieve a stable octet, like Neon (2, 8).

Chlorine (Cl)

A Group 17 nonmetal with seven valence electrons (electron configuration: 2, 8, 7). It tends to gain one electron to achieve a stable octet, like Argon (2, 8, 8).

Magnesium Oxide (MgO)

Magnesium (Mg, Group 2) loses two electrons to form Mg$^{2+}$, while oxygen (O, Group 16) gains two electrons to form O$^{2-}$. They combine in a 1:1 ratio.

Earth Science Connection

Halite (NaCl)

Forms cubic crystals due to the cubic packing of Na$^+$ and Cl$^-$ ions.

Calcite (CaCO$_3$) and Fluorite (CaF$_2$)

Exhibit different crystal shapes because their constituent ions pack together differently within their respective lattices.
