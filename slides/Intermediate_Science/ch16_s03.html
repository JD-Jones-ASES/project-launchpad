Mendelian Genetics Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/3)

Gregor Mendel's Contribution

Mendel, a 19th-century monk, established the basic rules of inheritance long before DNA was discovered. His meticulous experiments with pea plants laid the groundwork for the science of genetics.

Advantages of Pea Plants

Mendel chose pea plants because they grow quickly, produce many offspring, and have clearly distinct traits (e.g., purple/white flowers, tall/short stems).

True-breeding Plants

Plants that consistently produce offspring identical to themselves when self-pollinated, ensuring a pure genetic lineage for specific traits.

Mendel's Experimental Findings

Alleles

Different forms of a gene. Organisms inherit two alleles for each gene, one from each parent.

Key Concepts (2/3)

Dominant Allele

An allele that expresses its trait whenever it is present, even if only one copy is inherited. Represented by an uppercase letter (e.g., $P$).

Recessive Allele

An allele that expresses its trait only when two copies are present (one from each parent) and no dominant allele is present. Represented by a lowercase letter (e.g., $p$).

Genotype

The specific genetic makeup of an organism, referring to the combination of alleles it carries for a particular trait (e.g., $PP$, $Pp$, $pp$).

Phenotype

The observable physical characteristic or trait of an organism resulting from its genotype and environmental interactions (e.g., "purple flowers," "white flowers").

Homozygous

Having two identical alleles for a given gene (e.g., homozygous dominant $PP$ or homozygous recessive $pp$).

Key Concepts (3/3)

Heterozygous

Having two different alleles for a given gene (e.g., $Pp$). A heterozygous individual will display the dominant phenotype but carries the recessive allele.

Punnett Square

A diagrammatic tool used to predict the possible genotypes and phenotypes of offspring resulting from a genetic cross, illustrating the probabilities of each outcome.

Important Terms (1/4)

Mendelian Genetics

The study of inheritance patterns that follow the principles proposed by Gregor Mendel, involving the transmission of discrete units of heredity (genes/alleles) from parents to offspring.

True-breeding

Refers to an organism that, when self-pollinated, always produces offspring identical to itself for a given trait; it is homozygous for that trait.

$F_1$ Generation

(First Filial Generation): The first generation of offspring produced from a cross between two true-breeding parental organisms differing in one or more traits.

$F_2$ Generation

(Second Filial Generation): The second generation of offspring, resulting from a cross between individuals of the $F_1$ generation.

Dominant Allele

An allele whose trait is expressed whenever it is present, masking the effect of a recessive allele if one is also present. Represented by an uppercase letter.

Important Terms (2/4)

Recessive Allele

An allele whose trait is only expressed when two copies are present (homozygous recessive) and no dominant allele is present. Represented by a lowercase letter.

Gene

A unit of heredity that is transferred from a parent to offspring and is held to determine some characteristic of the offspring.

Allele

One of two or more alternative forms of a gene that arise by mutation and are found at the same place on a chromosome.

Genotype

The genetic constitution of an individual organism; the specific set of alleles an organism possesses for a particular trait (e.g., $PP$, $Pp$, $pp$).

Phenotype

The observable characteristics or traits of an organism, resulting from the interaction of its genotype with the environment (e.g., purple flowers, white flowers).

Important Terms (3/4)

Homozygous

Having two identical alleles for a particular gene (e.g., $PP$ or $pp$).

Homozygous dominant

Having two dominant alleles for a trait (e.g., $PP$).

Homozygous recessive

Having two recessive alleles for a trait (e.g., $pp$).

Heterozygous

Having two different alleles for a particular gene (e.g., $Pp$). A heterozygous individual will express the dominant phenotype.

Punnett square

A diagram used to predict the possible genotypes and phenotypes of offspring from a genetic cross by combining the alleles from each parent.

Important Terms (4/4)

Monohybrid cross

A genetic cross that involves individuals differing in only one specific trait.

Probability

The likelihood or chance that a particular event will occur, often expressed as a fraction or percentage.

Mendel's Experimental Design

True-breeding plants

Mendel started with plants that consistently produced offspring identical to themselves when self-pollinated. This ensured that his parent plants were genetically pure for the traits he was studying.

Controlled Crosses

He then crossed two true-breeding plants with contrasting traits (e.g., purple flowers $\times$ white flowers) and carefully observed and counted the offspring across generations.
