Connecting Concepts: Change Across Scales

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Vast Range of Timescales

Scientific phenomena occur over an immense spectrum of time, from nanoseconds (electrical changes) to billions of years (astronomical changes), with biological and chemical changes falling in between.

Change as a Unifying Thread

The study of change connects all branches of science, demonstrating how seemingly disparate fields are linked by fundamental principles of transformation.

Biological Change (Evolution)

Driven primarily by natural selection, evolution typically occurs over millions of years for complex organisms but can be rapid in organisms with short generation times, like bacteria.

Astronomical Change (Deep Time)

Deals with the longest timescales, encompassing the formation of solar systems, the life cycles of stars, and the expansion of the universe over billions of years.

Chemical Change (Reactions)

Generally observable within human timescales (seconds, minutes, hours), involving rearrangement of atoms and molecules, though some chemical processes can span geological time.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Electrical Change (Speed of Circuits)

Involves the fastest timescales, with electrical signals propagating at near the speed of light, enabling rapid information processing in technology.

Universal Principles of Change

Despite differing timescales, change across all disciplines shares common attributes: it requires energy, follows natural laws, and can often be modeled and predicted by scientists.

Important Terms (1/3)

Natural Selection

The process by which organisms better adapted to their environment tend to survive and produce more offspring, driving evolutionary change.

Generation Time

The average time between two consecutive generations in a population, significantly impacting the rate of observable evolution.

Solar Nebula

A rotating cloud of gas and dust from which the Sun and the planets of our solar system formed.

Red Giant

A late stage in the evolution of a star, where it expands significantly and cools, becoming luminous.

Supernova

A powerful and luminous stellar explosion, occurring at the end of a massive star's life, or when a white dwarf accretes matter.

Important Terms (2/3)

Big Bang

The prevailing cosmological model for the universe's earliest known periods, describing how the universe expanded from an extremely hot, dense state.

Exothermic Reaction

A chemical reaction that releases energy, often in the form of heat or light.

Endothermic Reaction

A chemical reaction that absorbs energy from its surroundings, typically causing a decrease in temperature.

pH

A measure of the acidity or alkalinity of a solution, based on the concentration of hydrogen ions.

Electrical Signal

The propagation of a change in an electric field through a conductor, carrying information or energy.

Important Terms (3/3)

Drift Velocity

The average velocity attained by charged particles, such as electrons, in a material due to an electric field.

Gigahertz (GHz)

A unit of frequency equal to one billion (10^9) cycles per second, often used to measure processor speed.

Nanosecond (ns)

A unit of time equal to one billionth (10^-9) of a second.
