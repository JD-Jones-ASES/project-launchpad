Important Terms (1/3)

Differentiation

The process by which an unspecialized cell develops into a cell with a distinct structure and function, involving the activation and silencing of specific genes.

Specialized cell (Differentiated cell)

A cell that has undergone differentiation and developed a distinct structure suited to a particular function within the organism.

Stem cell

An undifferentiated cell capable of dividing to produce more stem cells (self-renewal) and of differentiating into one or more specialized cell types.

Self-renewal

The ability of stem cells to divide and produce more stem cells, maintaining their undifferentiated population.

Totipotent stem cells

Stem cells capable of giving rise to every cell type in the body, plus the cells that form the placenta.

Important Terms (2/3)

Pluripotent stem cells

Stem cells capable of becoming almost any cell type in the body but unable to form placental tissue.

Multipotent stem cells

Stem cells capable of differentiating into several related cell types within a particular tissue.

Axon

A long, thin extension of a neuron that carries electrical impulses away from the cell body.

Dendrites

Branching extensions of a neuron that receive signals from other neurons.

Hemoglobin

The iron-containing protein in red blood cells that binds and transports oxygen.

Important Terms (3/3)

Actin and Myosin

Protein filaments found in muscle cells that slide past each other to produce muscle contraction.

Microvilli

Finger-like projections on the surface of some epithelial cells (e.g., in the small intestine) that increase surface area for absorption.

Advantages of Embryonic Stem Cells

They are pluripotent, meaning they can differentiate into a much wider range of cell types compared to adult stem cells, making them potentially more versatile for various medical applications.

Ethical Concerns

These often revolve around the source of embryonic stem cells, which typically involves the destruction of early human embryos.
