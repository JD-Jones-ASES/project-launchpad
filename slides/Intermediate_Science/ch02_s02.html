Heat and Convection in Earth's Interior

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Geothermal Gradient

The observed increase in temperature with increasing depth into Earth, averaging 25-30°C per kilometer in the crust.

Radioactive Decay

The primary current source of Earth's internal heat, where unstable isotopes (like uranium, thorium, and potassium) release energy as their nuclei spontaneously break down.

Residual Heat

The thermal energy remaining from Earth's violent formation 4.6 billion years ago, including heat from planetary accretion and the iron catastrophe.

Convection

A process of heat transfer in fluids (liquids or gases) where hotter, less dense material rises, and cooler, denser material sinks, creating circulating currents.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Plate Tectonics

The theory explaining the large-scale motion of Earth's lithospheric plates, directly powered by the forces generated by mantle convection.

Convection in Everyday Life and Atmosphere

Convection is a universal principle observable in boiling water, lava lamps, and heated rooms, and it also drives global weather patterns through atmospheric circulation cells.

Important Terms (1/2)

Geothermal gradient

The rate at which temperature increases with depth within Earth.

Radioactive decay

The process by which unstable atomic nuclei release energy (radiation and heat) as they spontaneously transform into more stable forms.

Residual heat

The original heat from Earth's formation, including heat from impacts and gravitational differentiation, that remains trapped within the planet.

Iron catastrophe

An early event in Earth's history where dense iron and nickel sank to the planet's center, forming the core and releasing significant thermal energy.

Convection

The transfer of heat through the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas), involving the circulation of currents.

Important Terms (2/2)

Convection current

The continuous circulation pattern created by the rising of hotter, less dense fluid and the sinking of cooler, denser fluid.

Mantle convection

The slow circulation of solid rock within Earth's mantle, driven by heat, which powers plate tectonics.

Subduction

The process where one tectonic plate, typically an oceanic plate, sinks beneath another plate and into the Earth's mantle.

Earth's Internal Heat: The Geothermal Gradient

Observation

As one descends into Earth, temperature increases. This is known as the geothermal gradient.

Average Rate

The geothermal gradient averages about 25-30°C per kilometer in the crust.

Significance

This indicates that Earth's interior is extremely hot, powering geological processes.

Sci-Connection: Convection in the Atmosphere

Uneven Solar Heating

The Sun heats Earth's surface unevenly, with equatorial regions receiving more direct sunlight than the poles.

Atmospheric Convection Cells

This differential heating creates massive atmospheric convection cells (e.g., Hadley, Ferrel, Polar cells) that redistribute heat globally.

Weather and Winds

These cells are responsible for major wind patterns and global weather systems.

Universal Principle

The same physical principle of convection operates in both the solid mantle and the gaseous atmosphere, driven by unequal heating.

From Convection to Plate Motion

Interaction

Mantle convection currents in the asthenosphere (the plastic upper mantle) directly interact with and move the rigid plates of the lithosphere (Earth's crust and uppermost mantle).

Pushing Force

Where hot mantle material rises and spreads horizontally beneath the lithosphere, it exerts a dragging or pushing force on the overlying plate.

Pulling Force

Where cooler, denser lithospheric plate material sinks back into the mantle (a process called subduction), it pulls the rest of the plate along with it.

Plate Speeds

The combination of these pushing and pulling forces causes tectonic plates to move at rates of 1–15 centimeters per year.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What was the "iron catastrophe" and how did it generate heat?

An early event where dense iron and nickel sank to Earth's center to form the core, converting gravitational potential energy into thermal energy.

What crucial conditions do radioactive decay and residual heat maintain within Earth's interior?

Temperatures high enough to keep the outer core liquid and to drive the slow motions of mantle rock.

Name the three fundamental ways heat can be transferred.

Conduction (direct contact), radiation (electromagnetic waves), and convection (bulk movement of a fluid).

Which mechanism is the dominant way heat is transported from Earth's deep interior to the surface in the mantle?

Convection.

The transfer of heat through a fluid (liquid or gas) by the circulation of currents, where hotter, less dense material rises and cooler, denser material sinks.
