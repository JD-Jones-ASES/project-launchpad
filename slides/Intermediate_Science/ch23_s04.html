pH Formula

$pH = -\text{log}[H+]$, where $[H+]$ is in moles per liter (M). The negative sign ensures pH values are positive.

Understanding Logarithms

A logarithm (base 10) indicates the power to which 10 must be raised to get a given number. For example, log(1000) = 3 because $10^3 = 1000$.

Buffers

Solutions that resist significant changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added. They typically consist of a weak acid and its conjugate base (or a weak base and its conjugate acid).

Biological Importance of Buffers

Buffers are crucial for maintaining stable pH levels in living systems, such as human blood (carbonic acid-bicarbonate system), which is vital for enzyme function and overall health.

Important Terms (1/2)

Logarithm (base 10)

A mathematical function that answers the question "10 raised to what power gives this number?" Used to compress large ranges of numbers.

pH

A measure of the acidity or alkalinity of a solution, defined as the negative logarithm (base 10) of the hydrogen ion concentration.

Hydrogen Ion Concentration ($[H+]$)

The concentration of hydrogen ions in a solution, typically measured in moles per liter (M).

Buffer

A solution containing a weak acid and its conjugate base (or vice-versa) that resists changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added.

Acid Rain

Precipitation with a pH below 5.6, caused by atmospheric pollutants (SO2, NOx) reacting with water to form strong acids.

Important Terms (2/2)

Carbonic Acid–Bicarbonate System

The primary buffer system in human blood, composed of carbonic acid ($H2CO3$) and bicarbonate ions ($HCO3-$), which helps maintain blood pH around 7.4.

Equivalence Point

The point in a titration where the moles of acid equal the moles of base, resulting in neutralization.

Indicator

A substance (often a dye) that changes color at a specific pH range, used to signal the equivalence point in a titration or to determine the pH of a solution.

Acid Rain

Definition and Normal Rain

Acid rain is precipitation (rain, snow, fog) with a pH lower than 5.6. Normal rainwater is slightly acidic (pH ~5.6) due to atmospheric carbon dioxide ($CO2$) dissolving to form carbonic acid.

Causes

The primary cause of acid rain is the emission of sulfur dioxide ($SO2$) and nitrogen oxides ($NOx$) into the atmosphere, mainly from the burning of fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas). These gases react...

Severity

Acid rain can have a pH of 4 or even lower, making it 10 to 100 times more acidic than normal rain.

Effects

Policy Success

Regulations such as the Clean Air Act have significantly reduced SO2 and NOx emissions in many regions since the 1980s, leading to a reduction in acid rain.

Applications of Acids and Bases

Food

Used for flavor (citric acid in citrus fruits), preservation (acetic acid in vinegar), and texture (lactic acid in dairy products, phosphoric acid in soft drinks).

Medicine

Found in antacids (neutralize stomach acid), aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid), and buffer solutions for intravenous (IV) fluids.

Industry

Essential for manufacturing processes. Examples include sulfuric acid (batteries, fertilizers), sodium hydroxide (soap, paper, textiles), and hydrochloric acid (steel pickling, pH adjustment).

Agriculture

Critical for managing soil pH. Lime ($CaCO3$) is added to raise the pH of acidic soil, while sulfur can be added to lower the pH of basic soil, optimizing conditions for specific crops.

Water Treatment

Chlorine (slightly acidic) and sodium hydroxide are used to adjust the pH of drinking water, ensuring it is safe for consumption.

Scientist Spotlight: Robert Boyle (1627–1691)

Acid/Base Indicators

Experimental Method

Boyle championed the idea that scientific theories must be rigorously supported by experimental evidence, a revolutionary concept at a time when much "natural philosophy" relied on ancient texts.

Boyle's Law

Famous for describing the inverse relationship between the pressure and volume of a gas at constant temperature ($PV=k$).

Legacy

His emphasis on careful experimentation, precise measurement, and skepticism transformed alchemy into a true modern science.
