Important Terms (1/4)

Independent variable

The factor that the experimenter deliberately changes or manipulates. It is sometimes called the "manipulated variable."

Dependent variable

The factor that the experimenter observes or measures to see if it was affected by changes to the independent variable. It is sometimes called the "responding variable."

Controlled variable

Any factor that is kept the same throughout the experiment so that it does not influence the results. Also known as "constants."

Control group

The group in an experiment that does not receive the treatment or change being tested. It serves as a baseline for comparison.

Experimental group

The group in an experiment that receives the treatment or change being tested. Its results are compared to the control group.

Important Terms (2/4)

Sample size

The number of individual trials, subjects, or observations in an experiment. Larger sample sizes generally yield more reliable results.

Bias

Any factor in the design, execution, or interpretation of an experiment that systematically favors one outcome over another, compromising reliability.

Selection bias

Occurs when the groups in an experiment are not comparable from the start, leading to skewed results.

Observer bias

Occurs when the person collecting data unconsciously favors one outcome, influencing measurements or observations.

Confirmation bias

Occurs when individuals pay more attention to data that supports their hypothesis and ignore or downplay contradictory data.

Important Terms (3/4)

Blinding

A technique used to reduce observer bias, where the person measuring outcomes does not know which group each subject belongs to.

Quantitative data

Data that can be expressed as numbers and measured (e.g., temperature, mass, length, time). Amenable to mathematical analysis.

Qualitative data

Data that describe characteristics or qualities and cannot be easily reduced to numbers (e.g., color, texture, smell). Recorded through descriptions or images.

Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT)

A type of experiment, often used in medical research, where subjects are randomly assigned to an experimental group (receiving treatment) or a control group (receiving a placebo).

Placebo effect

The tendency for patients to feel better simply because they believe they are receiving treatment, even if the substance is inactive.
