Waves and Tides

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Waves Transfer Energy, Not Water

Ocean waves are disturbances that move energy through the water medium, while the water itself primarily moves in circular orbits, returning to its approximate starting point.

Wave Anatomy

Waves have distinct measurable parts, including crests, troughs, wavelength, wave height, amplitude, frequency, and period, each describing a different aspect of the wave's form or motion.

Wave Speed Equation

The speed of any wave ($v$) can be calculated using its frequency ($f$) and wavelength ($λ$) with the formula: $v = fλ$.

Deep vs. Shallow Water Waves

Wave behavior changes significantly as water depth decreases. In deep water, speed depends on wavelength. In shallow water, waves slow down, shorten, grow taller, and eventually break.

Important Terms (1/4)

Wave

A disturbance that transfers energy through a medium (like water) without permanently displacing the medium itself.

Crest

The highest point of a wave.

Trough

The lowest point of a wave.

Wavelength ($\lambda$)

The distance from one crest to the next, or one trough to the next.

Wave height

The vertical distance from a wave's trough to its crest.

Important Terms (2/4)

Amplitude

Half the wave height; the distance from the undisturbed water level (rest position) to either a crest or a trough. Relates to the energy carried by the wave.

Frequency ($f$)

The number of waves that pass a fixed point per unit time, measured in hertz (Hz).

Period ($T$)

The time it takes for one complete wave to pass a fixed point, in seconds. It is the reciprocal of frequency ($T = 1/f$).

Wave Speed Equation

The mathematical relationship $v = fλ$, where $v$ is wave speed, $f$ is frequency, and $λ$ is wavelength.

Deep-Water Wave

A wave traveling in water where the depth is greater than half its wavelength. Its speed depends primarily on its wavelength.

Important Terms (3/4)

Shallow-Water Wave

A wave traveling in water where the depth is less than half its wavelength. Its speed is affected by the bottom, causing it to slow down, shorten, and increase in height.

Breaking Wave

A wave that becomes too steep in shallow water and curls over, crashing onto the shore.

Tide

The regular, periodic rise and fall of the ocean surface caused primarily by the gravitational pull of the Moon and, to a lesser extent, the Sun.

Tidal Bulge

A bulge of ocean water created on the sides of Earth facing toward and away from the Moon, due to gravitational forces.

Spring Tides

Tides with the greatest tidal range, occurring when the Sun, Moon, and Earth are aligned (during new moon and full moon).

Important Terms (4/4)

Neap Tides

Tides with the smallest tidal range, occurring when the Sun and Moon are at right angles relative to Earth (during first and third quarter moons).

Wave Anatomy and Properties (1/2)

Crest

The highest point reached by the wave.

Trough

The lowest point of the wave.

Wavelength ($\lambda$)

The horizontal distance between two consecutive crests or two consecutive troughs. It is measured in meters (m).

Wave Height

The vertical distance from the trough to the crest of a wave.

Amplitude

Half of the wave height. It is the vertical distance from the undisturbed water level (the "rest position") to either a crest or a trough. A larger amplitude indicates more energy carried by the wave.

Wave Anatomy and Properties (2/2)

Frequency ($f$)

The number of complete waves that pass a fixed point in a given unit of time, typically one second. It is measured in hertz (Hz), where 1 Hz = 1 wave per second.

Period ($T$)

The time it takes for one complete wave to pass a fixed point. It is measured in seconds (s) and is the reciprocal of frequency ($T = 1/f$).
