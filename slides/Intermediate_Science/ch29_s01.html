Key Concepts (1/2)

Magnetism as a Fundamental Force

Magnetism is a natural force closely related to electricity, not merely a property of special rocks.

Magnetic Poles and Interactions

Every magnet has a North pole and a South pole, where the magnetic force is strongest. Like poles repel (N-N, S-S), and opposite poles attract (N-S).

Absence of Magnetic Monopoles

Unlike electric charges, individual magnetic poles cannot be isolated. Breaking a magnet always results in two smaller magnets, each with both a North and South pole.

Earth's Magnetic Field (Geodynamo)

Earth acts as a giant magnet, generating its magnetic field through convection currents in its liquid iron outer core. This field resembles a tilted bar magnet.

Magnetic Declination

The angle between true geographic North and the direction a compass needle points (magnetic North) varies by location and over time.

Magnetosphere

Earth's magnetic field extends into space, forming the magnetosphere, which protects the planet by deflecting harmful solar wind.

Magnetic Reversals

Earth's magnetic poles periodically swap positions over thousands of years, a process recorded in seafloor rocks and crucial evidence for plate tectonics.

Important Terms (1/3)

Magnetism

A fundamental force of nature arising from the motion of electric charges, characterized by attractive and repulsive forces between objects.

Magnetic Poles

The regions of a magnet where the magnetic force is strongest, designated as North and South.

Magnetic Field

The region of space around a magnet or a moving electric charge where a magnetic force can be detected. It is a vector field with both magnitude (strength) and direction.

Magnetic Monopole

A hypothetical isolated magnetic pole (either a North pole without a South pole, or vice versa). None have ever been observed.

Magnetic Field Lines

Imaginary lines used to represent the direction and strength of a magnetic field. They emerge from North poles, enter South poles, form continuous closed loops, and never cross.

Magnetic Domain

A microscopic region within a ferromagnetic material where the magnetic fields of all atoms are aligned in the same direction.

Curie Temperature

The critical temperature above which a ferromagnetic material loses its permanent magnetism, as the thermal energy disrupts the alignment of magnetic domains.

Geodynamo

The process by which convection currents in Earth's liquid iron outer core generate and sustain its magnetic field.

Magnetic Declination

The angular difference at a given location between geographic north (true North Pole) and magnetic north (the direction a compass needle points).

Important Terms (3/3)

Magnetosphere

The region of space surrounding Earth that is influenced by its magnetic field, deflecting charged particles from the solar wind.

Solar Wind

A continuous stream of charged particles (plasma) emitted from the Sun's upper atmosphere, which interacts with planetary magnetic fields.

Magnetic Reversal

A geological event in which Earth's magnetic North and South poles swap positions, a process that takes thousands of years and has occurred numerous times throughout Earth's history.

Magnetite

A naturally occurring iron oxide mineral (Fe3O4) that is strongly magnetic and found in some biological organisms, contributing to their ability to sense magnetic fields.

Magnetism is not just a property of certain rocks but a fundamental force of nature, deeply intertwined with electricity.

Earth's Magnetic Field (1/2)

The Geodynamo

Earth's magnetic field is generated deep within its interior by the "geodynamo" – convection currents of molten iron in the liquid outer core.

Resemblance to a Bar Magnet

Earth's magnetic field pattern is similar to that of a giant bar magnet tilted approximately 11 degrees from Earth's rotational axis.

Geographic vs. Magnetic Poles

The geographic North Pole (Earth's rotational axis) and the magnetic north pole are not in the same location.
