Important Terms (1/4)

Plastic

A synthetic polymer that can be molded into shape when heated and retains that shape when cooled. Most are derived from petroleum hydrocarbons.

Thermoplastics

Plastics that can be repeatedly melted and reshaped without significant degradation (e.g., polyethylene).

Thermosets

Plastics that form a permanently rigid structure once molded and cannot be remelted (e.g., epoxy, vulcanized rubber).

Polyethylene (PE)

The simplest synthetic polymer, made from ethylene monomers, used in plastic bags, bottles, and food wrap.

Polypropylene (PP)

A synthetic polymer used in containers and automotive parts.

Important Terms (2/4)

Polyvinyl chloride (PVC)

A synthetic polymer commonly used in pipes and vinyl flooring.

Polystyrene (PS)

A synthetic polymer used in foam cups and packing materials.

Fossil Fuels

Non-renewable energy sources (coal, petroleum, natural gas) formed from the ancient remains of organisms, primarily composed of hydrocarbons.

Combustion

A chemical reaction, typically involving rapid reaction with oxygen, that releases energy (often as heat and light) by breaking chemical bonds in fuel molecules.

Green Chemistry

The design of chemical products and processes that reduce or eliminate the use and generation of hazardous substances, prioritizing safety and environmental sustainability.

Important Terms (3/4)

Atom Economy

A green chemistry principle that aims to maximize the incorporation of all starting materials into the final desired product, minimizing waste.

Renewable Feedstocks

Raw materials derived from renewable sources, such as plants or biomass, rather than finite resources like petroleum.

Design for Degradation

A green chemistry principle advocating for the design of chemical products that break down into harmless substances after their intended use, preventing persistent pollution.

Biodegradable Plastics

Synthetic polymers designed to decompose into natural, harmless substances (like CO2 and water) through microbial action. Polylactic acid (PLA) is an example.

Biochemical Engineering

The application of organic chemistry and biochemistry principles to design and optimize industrial processes involving biological systems or molecules.

Important Terms (4/4)

Fermentation

An anaerobic metabolic process used in biochemical engineering to produce substances like ethanol, antibiotics, and vitamins.

Maillard Reaction

A complex chemical reaction between amino acids and reducing sugars that occurs when food is heated, responsible for the browning and savory flavors in many cooked foods.

Pharmaceuticals

Mechanism of Action

These molecules are designed to "fit" into the active sites of enzymes or receptors, much like a key fits a lock, thereby altering biological pathways.

Biochemical Engineering and Food Chemistry

Biochemical Engineering

This discipline applies organic chemistry and biochemistry principles to industrial processes, often utilizing living organisms or their components.

Food Chemistry

This field investigates the chemical and physical interactions of biomolecules (carbohydrates, proteins, fats, vitamins) within food during processing, cooking, preservation, and digestion.

Life Science Connection: The Carbon Cycle

Photosynthesis

Plants fix atmospheric CO2 into glucose (C6H12O6), a carbohydrate, through photosynthesis.

Biomolecule Synthesis

Organisms then enzymatically transform glucose into all other essential organic molecules—lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids.

Carbon Return

Carbon returns to the atmosphere as CO2 through processes like respiration, decomposition of organic matter, and the burning of biomass or fossil fuels.

Climate Impact

Every step in this cycle involves organic chemistry, and the delicate balance of these reactions directly influences the concentration of atmospheric CO2 and, consequently, the global climate.
