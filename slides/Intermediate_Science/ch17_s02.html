Important Terms (2/2)

La Niña

The opposite climate pattern to El Niño, characterized by strengthened trade winds, intensified westward flow of warm surface water, and enhanced upwelling along the South American coast.

ENSO (El Niño-Southern Oscillation)

A recurring climate cycle in the tropical Pacific that encompasses both El Niño and La Niña events, significantly influencing global weather and climate.

Coriolis effect

A force resulting from Earth's rotation that deflects moving objects (like ocean currents and winds) to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What are ocean currents?

Massive rivers of water that flow through ocean basins, carrying heat, nutrients, and dissolved gases around the globe.

What are the primary drivers of surface ocean currents?

Global wind patterns (trade winds, westerlies, and polar easterlies).

Approximately how deep do surface currents affect the ocean?

Roughly the top 400 meters.

How does the Coriolis effect influence the direction of moving ocean water?

It deflects moving water to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere.

Define a 'gyre' and identify the main factors that form these large ocean patterns.

A large, circular system of surface currents driven by wind and deflected by the Coriolis effect and continental boundaries.

Name the five major ocean gyres.

North Atlantic, South Atlantic, North Pacific, South Pacific, and Indian Ocean.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How do the rotation directions of gyres differ between the Northern and Southern Hemispheres?

In the Northern Hemisphere, gyres rotate clockwise; in the Southern Hemisphere, they rotate counterclockwise.

Compare the typical characteristics (width, depth, speed) of western boundary currents versus eastern boundary currents.

Western boundary currents (e.g., Gulf Stream) are narrow, deep, and fast. Eastern boundary currents are broad, shallow, and slow.

Provide an example of how the Gulf Stream influences climate.

The Gulf Stream carries warm water northward from the Gulf of Mexico, making western Europe's climate milder than regions at the same latitude in North America.

What is thermohaline circulation, and what two main factors drive it?

The global pattern of deep ocean currents driven by differences in water density, which depends on temperature ('thermo') and salinity ('haline').

Why does cold, salty water sink to form deep water masses?

Cold, salty water is denser than warmer, less salty water.

Approximately how long does it take for a complete circuit of the global thermohaline 'conveyor belt'?

About 1000 years.
