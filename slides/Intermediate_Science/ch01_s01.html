Important Terms (1/2)

Cell

The basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms. Cells carry out life-sustaining chemical reactions and contain genetic information for reproduction.

Microscope

An instrument that uses lenses or beams of electrons to produce magnified images of objects too small to see with the unaided eye.

Animalcules

A term coined by Antonie van Leeuwenhoek to describe the tiny, living creatures he observed under his microscope, now known as bacteria, protists, and other microorganisms.

Cell theory

A foundational concept in biology stating that (1) all living things are made of one or more cells, (2) the cell is the basic unit of structure and function in living things, and (3) all cells aris...

Light microscope (Optical microscope)

A type of microscope that passes visible light through a specimen and uses glass lenses to magnify the image.

Important Terms (2/2)

Resolution

The ability of a microscope (or other imaging system) to distinguish two objects that are very close together as separate entities.

Electron microscope

A type of microscope that uses a beam of electrons instead of light, achieving much higher resolution and magnification than light microscopes.

Transmission electron microscope (TEM)

An electron microscope that sends electrons through an ultra-thin specimen to produce detailed images of internal cell structures.

Scanning electron microscope (SEM)

An electron microscope that bounces electrons off the surface of a specimen, creating dramatic three-dimensional images of cell surfaces.

Organelle

A specialized structure within a cell that performs a specific function, analogous to organs in the body (e.g., nucleus, mitochondria, ribosomes).

Key Concepts (2/2)

Who first proposed that all plants are made of cells, and in what year?

Botanist Matthias Schleiden concluded that all plants are made of cells in 1838.

Who first proposed that all animals are made of cells, and in what year?

Zoologist Theodor Schwann reached the same conclusion about animals in 1839.

What joint conclusion did Schleiden and Schwann reach regarding living organisms?

Together, Schleiden and Schwann proposed that every living organism is composed of one or more cells, a revolutionary unifying principle for all life.
