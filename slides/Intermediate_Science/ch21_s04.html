Important Terms (1/3)

Biological species concept

A definition of species as a group of organisms that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring under natural conditions.

Speciation

The process by which one species splits into two or more distinct species.

Reproductive isolation

The inability of different species to interbreed, or to produce fertile offspring, often due to genetic differences, behavioral differences, or physical barriers.

Geographic isolation

A physical barrier (like a mountain range or ocean) that divides a population into two or more groups, preventing gene flow.

Allopatric speciation

A type of speciation that occurs when biological populations become geographically isolated from each other to an extent that prevents or interferes with gene flow.

Important Terms (2/3)

Adaptive radiation

The rapid diversification of a single ancestral species into many new species, each adapted to a different ecological niche.

Extinction

The complete disappearance of a species; occurs when the last individual of a species dies.

Background extinction

The normal, ongoing loss of species due to environmental change, competition, and other factors, occurring at a relatively constant rate over long periods.

Mass extinction events

Episodes in which a large percentage of species disappear in a geologically short time, often caused by catastrophic global events.

Phylogenetic tree (or phylogeny)

A diagram that shows the evolutionary relationships among a group of organisms, resembling a "family tree."

Important Terms (3/3)

Cladogram

A simplified branching diagram that shows the evolutionary relationships among a group of organisms based on shared derived characteristics, with each branching point representing a common ancestor.

Node

A branching point on a cladogram, representing a common ancestor shared by the groups above it.

Shared derived characters

Traits that evolved in an ancestor and are present in its descendants but absent in more distantly related groups, used to construct cladograms.
