Positive Work ($W > 0$)

Occurs when a force has a component in the direction of motion, transferring energy to the object and typically increasing its kinetic energy.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Negative Work ($W < 0$)

Occurs when a force has a component opposite to the direction of motion, transferring energy away from the object and typically decreasing its kinetic energy.

Zero Work ($W = 0$)

Occurs when a force is perpendicular to the direction of motion, or when there is no displacement, meaning no energy is transferred to or from the object.

Important Terms (1/2)

Work (Physics Definition)

The product of the force applied to an object and the displacement of the object in the direction of the force. Work is done only when a force causes an object to move.

Joule (J)

The SI unit of work and energy. One joule is equal to the work done when a force of one newton moves an object one meter in the direction of the force ($1 J = 1 N \cdot m$).

Hypotenuse

In a right triangle, the longest side, always opposite the right ($90^\circ$) angle. In the work formula, it can represent the total applied force.

Adjacent Side

In a right triangle, the side next to a given angle ($\theta$) that is *not* the hypotenuse. In the work formula, it represents the component of force acting in the direction of motion.

Important Terms (2/2)

Opposite Side

In a right triangle, the side directly across from a given angle ($\theta$). (Not directly used in the work formula discussed here, but part of the general trigonometric context).

Defining Work in Physics

Everyday vs. Physics

In everyday language, "work" can mean any effort (mental or physical), like studying or holding a heavy bag. However, in physics, work has a very specific definition.

Physics Definition

Work is done *only* when a force causes an object to move. If you push against a wall and it doesn't move, you do no physics work on the wall, even if you exert effort.

Key Requirement

For work to be done, there must be both a force *and* a displacement in the direction of that force (or a component of that force).

Units

The SI unit of work is the **joule (J)**.

Calculating Work (Worked Example)

Problem

A student pulls a sled with a force of $50 N$ along a rope that makes a $60^\circ$ angle with the horizontal ground. The sled moves $10 m$ along the ground. How much work does the student do?

Solution Steps

Interpretation

Only the horizontal component of the force ($50 N \times 0.5 = 25 N$) does work. This horizontal component is multiplied by the distance moved to get the total work done.
