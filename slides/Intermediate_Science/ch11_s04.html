Important Terms (1/2)

Autotroph

An organism that can produce its own food, usually by photosynthesis (e.g., plants, algae).

Heterotroph

An organism that obtains food by consuming other organisms or organic matter.

Chemical Energy

Energy stored in the bonds of molecules (like glucose), which can be released through chemical reactions.

Thermal Energy (Heat)

Energy that is lost from a system, often as an unusable form, during energy transformations, typically due to the inefficiency of energy transfer.

ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate)

The primary energy-carrying molecule used to power most cellular processes.

Important Terms (2/2)

Decomposers

Organisms, primarily bacteria and fungi, that break down dead organic matter, releasing nutrients and carbon dioxide back into the environment.

Fossil Fuels

Carbon-rich natural resources (coal, oil, natural gas) formed from the remains of ancient organisms over millions of years, which release CO₂ when burned.

Carbon Reservoir

A natural pool or storage location for carbon-containing compounds (e.g., atmosphere, oceans, biomass, fossil fuels).

Light Intensity

The amount of light energy falling on a given area; a key factor influencing the rate of photosynthesis.

Energy Flow: From Sun to Cells

Energy Loss

At every step of this energy transfer, some energy is inevitably lost as thermal energy (heat). This loss is dictated by the laws of thermodynamics.

No Energy Recycling

Because energy is continuously lost as heat, it is *not* recycled within ecosystems. Therefore, a constant, fresh input of energy from the Sun is required to sustain life on Earth.

The Carbon Cycle Connection

Photosynthesis and Respiration

Photosynthesis extracts carbon from atmospheric CO₂ and incorporates it into organic molecules. Cellular respiration, performed by all organisms, returns that carbon to the atmosphere as CO₂.

Other Players in the Carbon Cycle

Climate Impact

The balance between carbon entering and leaving the atmosphere directly influences the concentration of atmospheric CO₂, a key factor in global climate regulation.

Scientist Spotlight: Jan Ingenhousz (1730–1799)
