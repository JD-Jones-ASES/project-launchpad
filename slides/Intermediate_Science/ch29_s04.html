Key Concepts (1/2)

Electric Motors

Devices that convert electrical energy into mechanical (rotational) energy using the magnetic force on current-carrying wires within a magnetic field. They are essentially generators run in reverse.

Generators vs. Motors

These are mirror images; a generator converts mechanical to electrical energy, while a motor converts electrical to mechanical. They share similar components but differ in energy input and output.

Important Terms (1/2)

Electric Motor

A device that converts electrical energy into mechanical energy (rotational motion).

Alternating Current (AC)

Electric current that periodically reverses direction, typically at 50 or 60 cycles per second (hertz).

Direct Current (DC)

Electric current that flows in one direction only.

Commutator

A device used in DC motors and generators that reverses the direction of current flow in the coil every half-turn, ensuring continuous rotation in one direction.

Regenerative Braking

A braking system, particularly in electric vehicles, where the motor acts as a generator to recover kinetic energy and convert it back into electrical energy.

Maxwell's Equations

A set of four fundamental equations that describe how electric and magnetic fields are generated and altered by each other and by charges and currents. They unify electricity, magnetism, and light.

Electric Motors

Function

An electric motor converts electrical energy into mechanical energy, specifically rotational motion.

Mechanism

Applications

Motors are ubiquitous, found in household appliances (fans, washing machines), vehicles (electric cars), power tools, computer hard drives, and even vibrating mechanisms in phones.

Regenerative Braking

In electric vehicles, the motor can operate in reverse as a generator during braking, converting the car's kinetic energy back into electrical energy to recharge the battery.
