Beyond Mendel: Complex Inheritance Patterns

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Incomplete Dominance

A pattern where the heterozygous genotype results in a blended phenotype, intermediate between the two homozygous phenotypes.

Codominance

A pattern where both alleles in a heterozygote are fully and simultaneously expressed, without blending.

Multiple Alleles

A situation where more than two different allele forms exist within a population for a single gene, though each individual only carries two.

Sex-Linked Traits

Traits determined by genes located on the sex chromosomes, primarily the X chromosome, leading to different inheritance patterns in males and females.

Polygenic Inheritance

A complex pattern where multiple genes contribute to a single trait, often resulting in a continuous range of phenotypes rather than distinct categories.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Pedigree Analysis

The use of family diagrams with standardized symbols to trace the inheritance of a trait through generations and determine its genetic pattern.

Genetic Disorders

Inherited conditions caused by specific mutations in an individual's DNA, illustrating deviations from normal physiological function.

Sample Size and Probability

In genetic crosses and simulations, larger sample sizes tend to produce experimental results that more closely match predicted probability ratios.

Important Terms (1/3)

Incomplete Dominance

An inheritance pattern in which the phenotype of heterozygous individuals is intermediate between those of the two homozygous parents.

Codominance

An inheritance pattern in which both alleles for a gene are fully and equally expressed in the heterozygote phenotype.

Multiple Alleles

A condition in which a gene has more than two alleles (different forms) within a population.

Autosomes

Any chromosome that is not a sex chromosome; humans have 22 pairs of autosomes.

Sex Chromosomes

The pair of chromosomes that determine an individual's biological sex (XX for females, XY for males in humans).

Important Terms (2/3)

Sex-Linked Genes (X-linked genes)

Genes located specifically on the X chromosome.

Carrier

An individual who is heterozygous for a recessive allele that causes a genetic disorder but does not express the disorder themselves because they have a dominant normal allele.

Polygenic Inheritance

A mode of inheritance in which a trait is determined by the cumulative effect of two or more genes, often combined with environmental factors.

Pedigree

A chart that displays the inheritance pattern of a trait within a family through multiple generations, using standardized symbols.

Genetic Disorder

A condition caused by an abnormality in an individual's genome, often inherited from parents.

Important Terms (3/3)

Sickle Cell Disease

An autosomal recessive genetic disorder caused by a single nucleotide substitution mutation in the gene for hemoglobin, leading to abnormally shaped red blood cells and impaired oxygen transport.

Polygenic Inheritance

Definition

Many traits are not controlled by a single gene but are influenced by the combined action of multiple genes. This is known as **polygenic inheritance**.

Phenotype

Unlike traits controlled by single genes that often show distinct categories (e.g., tall or short), polygenic traits typically exhibit a **continuous range** of phenotypes.

Examples

Human skin color, height, and eye color are all polygenic traits. Each contributing gene has a small, additive effect on the final phenotype.
