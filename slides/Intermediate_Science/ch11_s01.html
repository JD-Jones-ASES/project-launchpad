Chapter 11, Section 1: Energy for Life

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Energy is essential for all life functions

Every cellular activity, from beating hearts to processing thoughts, requires a constant supply of energy.

The Sun is the ultimate energy source

The energy for almost all life on Earth originates from sunlight.

Autotrophs produce their own food

These organisms (like plants, algae, and some bacteria) capture energy from the environment (sunlight or chemicals) to create organic molecules. They are the "producers" of the living world.

Heterotrophs obtain energy by consuming others

These organisms (like animals, fungi, and most bacteria) must eat other organisms or organic matter to get the energy-rich molecules they need. They are the "consumers."

Key Concepts (2/2)

ATP releases energy by breaking a phosphate bond

When the bond between the second and third phosphate group in ATP is broken, energy is released, converting ATP into ADP (adenosine diphosphate) and a free phosphate group.

ATP is continuously recycled

Cells constantly recycle ADP back into ATP using energy obtained from food or sunlight.

Important Terms (1/2)

Autotroph

An organism that produces its own food from inorganic molecules, using energy from sunlight (photosynthesis) or chemical reactions (chemosynthesis). Also known as a producer.

Chemotroph

A type of autotroph that obtains energy from chemical reactions involving inorganic substances (e.g., hydrogen sulfide, iron) rather than from light.

Heterotroph

An organism that cannot produce its own food and must obtain energy by consuming other organisms or decomposing organic matter. Also known as a consumer.

Photosynthesis

The process by which autotrophs (mainly plants and algae) capture light energy and use it to build glucose (C6H12O6) from carbon dioxide (CO2) and water (H2O), releasing oxygen (O2) as a byproduct.

Important Terms (2/2)

Adenosine Diphosphate (ADP)

The molecule formed when ATP releases energy by breaking off one of its phosphate groups. It has adenine, ribose, and two phosphate groups.

Cellular Respiration

The process by which both autotrophs and heterotrophs break down glucose to release energy, which is then stored in ATP. Carbon dioxide (CO2) and water (H2O) are produced as byproducts.
