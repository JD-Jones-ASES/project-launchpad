Important Terms (1/4)

Air Mass

A large body of air that has relatively uniform temperature and humidity throughout.

Front

The boundary where two air masses with different temperatures and moisture levels meet.

Cold Front

A weather front where a cold air mass advances and pushes under a warmer air mass, forcing rapid uplift.

Warm Front

A weather front where a warm air mass advances and rides up over a retreating cold air mass.

Stationary Front

A front where neither air mass is strong enough to push the other, causing the boundary to stall.

Important Terms (2/4)

Occluded Front

A front that forms when a fast-moving cold front catches up to a slower warm front, lifting the warm air mass entirely off the ground.

Cyclone

A large-scale low-pressure system in which air spirals inward and upward, associated with stormy weather.

Anticyclone

A high-pressure system in which air sinks and spirals outward, associated with clear skies and calm conditions.

Coriolis Effect

The apparent deflection of moving objects (like air or water) due to Earth's rotation, influencing wind patterns in cyclones and anticyclones.

Thunderstorm

A storm characterized by rapidly rising warm, moist air, towering cumulonimbus clouds, heavy rain, lightning, and often gusty winds.

Important Terms (3/4)

Wind Shear

A change in wind speed or direction with altitude, crucial for the formation of tornadoes.

Tornado

A violently rotating column of air extending from a thunderstorm to the ground, with extremely high wind speeds.

Hurricane

A large, rotating storm system that forms over warm tropical ocean water, characterized by an eye and eyewall (called a typhoon in the western Pacific).

Eye

The calm, clear center of a hurricane, an area of intense low pressure.

Eyewall

The ring of the most violent winds and heaviest rain surrounding a hurricane's eye.

Important Terms (4/4)

Barometer

An instrument invented by Evangelista Torricelli that measures atmospheric pressure.

Torr

A unit of pressure, named after Torricelli, equivalent to 1 mmHg (millimeter of mercury).

Air Masses

Definition

Large bodies of air (thousands of km²) with uniform temperature and humidity.

Formation

Develop when air remains over a consistent surface (ocean or continent) for an extended period, adopting the surface's characteristics.

Thunderstorms

Formation

Rapidly rising warm, moist air, often along a cold front or due to intense surface heating.

Process

Rising air cools, water vapor condenses into towering cumulonimbus clouds. The release of latent heat from condensation further fuels uplift.

Characteristics

Heavy rain, lightning, gusty winds, and sometimes hail. Typically lasts 30-60 minutes and affects a small area.

Tornadoes

Definition

A violently rotating column of air extending from a thunderstorm to the ground.

Formation

Wind shear (change in wind speed/direction with altitude) creates horizontal spinning air. Powerful updrafts in a thunderstorm tilt this spinning air vertically, forming a tornado.

Intensity

Wind speeds can exceed 300 km/h, making them the most intense atmospheric storms.

"Tornado Alley"

Central United States region prone to tornadoes due to frequent collisions of cold `cP` air from Canada and warm `mT` air from the Gulf of Mexico.

Hurricanes (1/2)

Definition

Large, rotating storm systems that form over warm tropical ocean water (at least 26°C). Called typhoons in the western Pacific.

Formation

Warm, moist air rises from the ocean surface, creating an intense low-pressure center.

Structure

Characteristics

Can span hundreds of kilometers, sustain winds above 119 km/h.

Weakening

Rapidly weaken over land or cool water because they are cut off from the warm ocean moisture (latent heat release) that fuels them.

Hurricanes (2/2)

Scientist Spotlight: Evangelista Torricelli (1608–1647)

Contribution

Italian physicist and mathematician who demonstrated that the atmosphere has weight and can be measured.

Invention

Invented the **barometer** in 1643 by observing mercury column height in an inverted tube.

Significance

His work proved the existence of atmospheric pressure and laid the foundation for meteorology. The unit of pressure "torr" (1 mmHg) is named in his honor.
