Connecting Concepts: Information Transfer

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Information Transfer as a Unifying Theme

The central idea that information flows through systems in structured, predictable ways across all branches of science, connecting biology, Earth science, chemistry, and physics.

The Genetic Code

Biological information is stored in DNA's base sequence (A, T, G, C) and transferred through transcription (DNA to mRNA) and translation (mRNA to protein), forming the blueprint for life.

Waves as Information Carriers

Physical waves, such as seismic waves and ocean waves, carry valuable data about their environments, allowing scientists to infer properties of the Earth's interior or distant weather patterns.

Chemical Equations as Information Codes

Balanced chemical equations are compact codes that convey precise information about reactants, products, ratios, and the conservation of matter in chemical reactions.

Important Terms (1/5)

DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid)

The molecule that carries genetic instructions for the development, functioning, growth, and reproduction of all known organisms.

mRNA (messenger RNA)

A type of RNA that carries genetic information from DNA in the nucleus to the ribosomes in the cytoplasm, where it serves as a template for protein synthesis.

Transcription

The process where the genetic information from DNA is copied into an mRNA molecule.

Translation

The process where ribosomes read the mRNA sequence to synthesize a protein.

Codon

A sequence of three nucleotides on an mRNA molecule that corresponds to a specific amino acid or a stop signal during protein synthesis.

Important Terms (2/5)

Mutation

A permanent alteration in the DNA sequence that makes up a gene, which can alter the resulting protein's function.

Seismic Waves

Waves of energy generated by earthquakes or other disturbances that travel through Earth's layers.

P-waves (Primary Waves)

Longitudinal seismic waves that can travel through solids and liquids.

S-waves (Secondary Waves)

Transverse seismic waves that can only travel through solids.

Shadow Zone

An area on Earth's surface where seismographs cannot detect S-waves due to the presence of a liquid outer core.

Important Terms (3/5)

Sonar

A technique that uses sound propagation (usually underwater) to navigate, communicate with or detect objects on or under the surface of the water, such as mapping the ocean floor.

Chemical Equation

A symbolic representation of a chemical reaction using chemical formulas to show the reactants and products.

Coefficient

A number placed in front of a chemical formula in an equation to balance the number of atoms of each element on both sides.

Subscript

A number written below and to the right of a chemical symbol in a formula, indicating the number of atoms of that element in a molecule.

State Symbols

Symbols (s, l, g, aq) used in chemical equations to indicate the physical state of a substance (solid, liquid, gas, aqueous solution).

Important Terms (4/5)

Electromagnetic Waves

Waves that are propagated by simultaneous periodic variations of electric and magnetic field intensity, travelling at the speed of light. Examples include radio waves, microwaves, and visible light.

Modulation

The process of varying one or more properties of a periodic waveform, called the carrier wave, with a modulating signal, which typically contains information to be transmitted.

Doppler Effect

The change in frequency or wavelength of a wave (like sound or light) for an observer moving relative to its source. For light, it causes redshift (moving away) or blueshift (moving towards).

Encoding

The process of converting information into a specific format (code) for transmission or storage.

Transmission

The process of sending encoded information through a carrier from one point to another.

Important Terms (5/5)

Decoding

The process of converting encoded information back into its original or interpretable form at the receiving end.
