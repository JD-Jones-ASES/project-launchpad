Key Concepts (1/2)

Evolution as Change in Allele Frequencies

Evolution is fundamentally defined as a change in the relative proportions of specific alleles within a population's gene pool across generations, not changes within an individual organism.

Gene Pool and Allele Frequency

The gene pool is the total collection of all alleles for all genes in a population. Allele frequency measures how common a particular allele is within this gene pool.

Natural Selection Patterns

Key Concepts (2/2)

Mutation

The ultimate source of all new genetic variation, creating new alleles through random changes in DNA. Most mutations are neutral or harmful, but some can be beneficial.

Sexual Selection

A specific form of natural selection where individuals with traits that enhance their success in attracting mates reproduce more successfully, even if those traits reduce survival.

Important Terms (1/3)

Population

A group of interbreeding organisms of the same species living in the same geographic area.

Gene pool

The complete set of all alleles for all genes present in a population. It represents the total genetic diversity available.

Allele frequency

The relative proportion of a specific allele compared to all other alleles for that gene in a population. Changes in allele frequency indicate evolution.

Directional selection

A pattern of natural selection where individuals at one extreme of a phenotypic range have greater reproductive success.

Stabilizing selection

A pattern of natural selection where intermediate phenotypes are favored, reducing genetic variation.

Important Terms (2/3)

Disruptive selection

A pattern of natural selection where individuals at both extremes of a phenotypic range have greater reproductive success than intermediate phenotypes.

Genetic drift

Random fluctuations in allele frequencies in a gene pool, especially prominent in small populations, due to chance events rather than natural selection.

Gene flow

The transfer of alleles from one population to another, usually through the migration of individuals or the dispersal of gametes (e.g., pollen).

Important Terms (3/3)

Mutation

A random, permanent change in the DNA sequence. It is the ultimate source of new alleles and genetic variation.

Sexual selection

A form of natural selection in which individuals with certain inherited characteristics are more likely than others to obtain mates.

Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium

A theoretical state in which a population's allele and genotype frequencies remain constant across generations, implying no evolution is occurring.
