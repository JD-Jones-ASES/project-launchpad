Seismic Waves

Vibrations from earthquakes that travel through Earth and are the primary tool for studying its interior.

Key Concepts (2/2)

P-waves (Primary Waves)

Faster, compressional waves that can travel through solids, liquids, and gases.

S-waves (Secondary Waves)

Slower, shear waves that can only travel through solids and are stopped by liquids.

Shadow Zones

Regions on Earth's surface where specific seismic waves are not detected, providing critical evidence for the liquid outer core (S-wave shadow zone) and the solid inner core (P-wave shadow zone).

Inge Lehmann's Discovery

The Danish seismologist who proposed the existence of a solid inner core based on anomalous P-wave arrivals in the P-wave shadow zone.

Multiple Lines of Evidence

Besides seismic waves, evidence from meteorites, Earth's overall density, its magnetic field, and its moment of inertia also supports the layered model.

Important Terms (1/3)

Crust

The thin, rocky outermost layer of Earth, ranging from 5 km to 70 km thick.

Oceanic crust

Relatively thin (5–10 km), dense, basaltic crust found under oceans.

Continental crust

Thicker (30–70 km), less dense, granitic crust found under continents.

Mantle

The thick layer of hot, mostly solid rock between Earth's crust and core, extending from about 30 km to 2,900 km below the surface.

Lithosphere

The rigid layer comprising the crust and the uppermost part of the mantle, which breaks into tectonic plates.

Important Terms (2/3)

Asthenosphere

A partially molten, slowly flowing zone within the upper mantle, beneath the lithosphere, allowing for plate movement.

Core

Earth's innermost region, composed primarily of iron and nickel, divided into a liquid outer core and a solid inner core.

Outer core

The liquid layer of iron and nickel extending from 2,900 km to 5,150 km below the surface, generating Earth's magnetic field.

Inner core

The solid, innermost part of Earth, from 5,150 km depth to the center (6,371 km), composed of iron and nickel under immense pressure.

Seismic wave

A wave of energy that travels through Earth's interior or along its surface, generated by earthquakes or other disturbances.

Important Terms (3/3)

P-wave (Primary wave)

A compressional seismic wave that travels through solids, liquids, and gases by compressing and expanding material in the direction of wave travel. It is the fastest seismic wave.

S-wave (Secondary wave)

A shear seismic wave that travels only through solids by shaking material perpendicular to the direction of wave travel. It cannot pass through liquids.

S-wave shadow zone

An area on Earth's surface where S-waves are not detected, indicating the presence of a liquid outer core.

P-wave shadow zone

A band on Earth's surface where P-waves are not directly detected, caused by the refraction of waves passing through the liquid outer core.

Lehmann discontinuity

The boundary at 5,150 km depth separating the liquid outer core from the solid inner core, discovered by Inge Lehmann.

Mantle (1/2)

Definition

The thick layer of hot, mostly solid rock beneath the crust.

Extent

From about 30 km to 2,900 km below the surface.

Mass

Makes up about 67% of Earth's total mass.

Composition

Silicate rocks rich in iron and magnesium.

Physical State

Mostly solid, but extremely hot (up to 3,700°C near the base). Behaves plastically over long timescales, meaning it flows very slowly (centimeters per year), like "silly putty."

Unveiling the Interior: Seismic Waves

The Tool

Seismic waves, generated by earthquakes, are the primary method scientists use to "see" inside Earth.

Mechanism

When an earthquake occurs, energy is released as seismic waves that radiate outward. By analyzing how these waves travel (or don't travel) through Earth, scientists deduce its internal structure.

P-waves (Primary Waves)

Nature

Compressional waves; they push and pull material in the same direction as wave travel.

Speed

Fastest seismic waves (e.g., 6 km/s in crust, up to 13 km/s in core).

Travel Medium

Can travel through solids, liquids, and gases.

S-waves (Secondary Waves)

Nature

Shear waves; they shake material side to side, perpendicular to the direction of wave travel.

Speed

Slower than P-waves (e.g., 3.5 km/s in crust).

Travel Medium

**Crucially, S-waves can only travel through solids.** They stop when they encounter liquid because liquids lack the rigidity to transmit shear motion.

S-wave Shadow Zone

Observation

No S-waves are detected at seismometer stations more than approximately 104° from the earthquake's epicenter.

Explanation

This vast "shadow" occurs because the S-waves encounter the liquid outer core and cannot pass through it, effectively stopping their transmission beyond that boundary.

Significance

This is the strongest evidence that Earth's outer core is liquid.

Observation

Inge Lehmann and the Inner Core

Discovery

In 1929, Danish seismologist Inge Lehmann observed faint P-waves arriving within the P-wave shadow zone, which was puzzling if the entire core was liquid.

Confirmation

Her mathematical analysis and subsequent improved seismometer networks confirmed the existence of a solid inner core.

Legacy

The boundary at 5,150 km depth between the liquid outer core and the solid inner core is known as the Lehmann discontinuity.

Supporting Evidence

Meteorites

Iron meteorites, remnants of early solar system formation, have an iron-nickel composition consistent with the predicted composition of Earth's core.

Earth's Density

Earth's average density (5.5 g/cm³) is significantly higher than that of surface rocks (2.7-3.0 g/cm³), implying that the interior must contain much denser material, like an iron-nickel core.

Earth's Magnetic Field

The existence of a global magnetic field necessitates a layer of electrically conducting liquid metal (the outer core) inside the planet, whose flow generates the field.

Moment of Inertia

Measurements of how Earth rotates indicate that its mass is concentrated toward the center, exactly as expected for a dense metallic core surrounded by less dense rocky layers.
