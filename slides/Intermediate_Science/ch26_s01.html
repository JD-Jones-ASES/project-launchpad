Important Terms (1/6)

Ecosystem

A community of living organisms interacting with their nonliving environment (soil, water, air, sunlight, temperature).

Community

All the populations of different species living and interacting in the same area at the same time.

Population

All the individuals of one species living in a defined area at a given time.

Habitat

The physical environment in which a species lives, including its climate, soil type, water availability, and shelter.

Niche

The role a species plays in its ecosystem, including what it eats, what eats it, when it's active, how it reproduces, and how it interacts with other species and its physical environment.

Important Terms (2/6)

Producers (Autotrophs)

Organisms, such as plants, algae, and cyanobacteria, that convert light energy into chemical energy through photosynthesis.

Trophic level

A step in the transfer of energy through an ecosystem, defined by an organism's feeding relationship.

Primary consumers (Herbivores)

Organisms that feed directly on producers, forming the second trophic level.

Secondary consumers

Carnivores or omnivores that feed on primary consumers, forming the third trophic level.

Tertiary consumers

Predators that feed on secondary consumers, forming the fourth trophic level.

Important Terms (3/6)

Decomposers

Organisms, such as bacteria and fungi, that break down dead organic matter and waste, returning nutrients to the soil, water, and atmosphere.

10% rule

The generalization that roughly 10% of the energy at one trophic level is passed on to the next trophic level; the remaining 90% is used for life processes or lost as heat.

Food chain

A linear sequence showing how energy passes from one organism to the next through feeding relationships.

Food web

A network of interconnected food chains that shows the complex feeding relationships within an ecosystem.

Keystone species

A species whose impact on its ecosystem is disproportionately large relative to its abundance; its removal causes significant changes in community structure.

Important Terms (4/6)

Biomass

The total mass of living organic matter in a given area or trophic level.

Gross primary productivity (GPP)

The total amount of energy or biomass produced by all the producers in an ecosystem through photosynthesis over a given time period.

Net primary productivity (NPP)

The energy or biomass remaining after producers have used some of the gross primary productivity for their own cellular respiration; NPP = GPP - respiration.

Carbon cycle

The biogeochemical cycle through which carbon moves between the atmosphere, living organisms, the ocean, and Earth's crust.

Nitrogen cycle

The biogeochemical cycle that describes how nitrogen is converted into various chemical forms as it circulates through the atmosphere, terrestrial, and marine ecosystems.

Important Terms (5/6)

Nitrogen fixation

The process by which certain bacteria convert atmospheric N2 into ammonia (NH3) or ammonium (NH4^+), making nitrogen available to plants.

Nitrification

Soil bacteria convert ammonium (NH4^+) to nitrites (NO2^-) and then to nitrates (NO3^-).

Assimilation

Plants incorporate nitrates into amino acids and nucleic acids; animals obtain nitrogen by eating plants or other animals.

Ammonification

Decomposers break down dead organisms and waste, releasing ammonium back into the soil.

Denitrification

Certain bacteria convert nitrates back into N2, returning nitrogen gas to the atmosphere.

Important Terms (6/6)

Phosphorus cycle

The biogeochemical cycle in which phosphorus moves between rocks, soil, water, and living organisms; it does not pass through the atmosphere in significant amounts.

Limiting nutrient

A nutrient whose scarcity limits the growth of producers in an ecosystem.

The 10% Rule

Only about 10% of the energy from one trophic level is transferred to the next.

Implications

This rule explains why there are fewer organisms and less biomass at higher trophic levels. For example, a vast amount of plant energy is needed to support a small population of apex predators.
