Important Terms (1/3)

Arctic amplification

The observed trend in which the Arctic region warms at a rate two to three times faster than the global average, driven primarily by the ice-albedo feedback.

Ice-albedo feedback

A positive feedback loop where melting ice (which is highly reflective) exposes darker land or ocean water (which absorbs more solar radiation), leading to further warming and more ice melt.

Temperature anomaly

The difference between a measured temperature and a reference average (baseline), usually the 1951–1980 or 1850–1900 average. Positive anomalies indicate warmer-than-average conditions.

Sea level rise

The increase in the average level of the ocean surface, caused primarily by thermal expansion of warming seawater and the addition of water from melting glaciers and ice sheets.

Thermal expansion

The increase in volume of a substance (like ocean water) as it absorbs thermal energy and its particles spread farther apart.

Important Terms (2/3)

Carbonic acid (H2CO3)

A weak acid formed when carbon dioxide dissolves in water; in the ocean, it dissociates to release hydrogen ions (H+), lowering the pH.

Ocean acidification

The decrease in ocean pH caused by the absorption of excess atmospheric CO2, which reacts with seawater to form carbonic acid.

Coral bleaching

A phenomenon where corals expel the symbiotic algae (zooxanthellae) that provide them with food and color, typically due to elevated water temperatures, which can lead to coral death if prolonged.

Phenological mismatch

A disruption in the timing of ecological events (e.g., flowering, migration, insect emergence) due to changes in climate, which can negatively impact food webs and species interactions.

Positive feedback loop

A process in which an initial change triggers a response that amplifies the original change (e.g., permafrost thaw releasing greenhouse gases, leading to more warming).

Impacts on Human Systems (1/2)

Agriculture

Changing precipitation patterns, increased heat stress, and more frequent droughts and floods threaten global crop yields, despite some regions potentially benefiting from longer growing seasons.

Water Resources

Retreating glaciers threaten freshwater supplies. Altered precipitation patterns impact river flows. Sea level rise leads to saltwater intrusion into coastal aquifers.

Infrastructure

Coastal flooding damages buildings, roads, and bridges. Thawing permafrost destabilizes foundations in Arctic regions. Extreme weather events overload drainage and power systems.

Displacement

Sea level rise and extreme weather can force millions to relocate, creating **climate refugees**, with low-lying island nations facing existential threats.
