Photosynthesis Study Guide: Unpacking How Plants Make Their Food

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Photosynthesis Definition

The process used by autotrophs (self-feeders) to convert light energy, carbon dioxide (CO2), and water (H2O) into glucose (C6H12O6) and oxygen (O2). It's the primary way energy enters most ecosystems.

Overall Chemical Equation

6CO2 + 6H2O →[light] C6H12O6 + 6O2. This equation summarizes the transformation of inorganic molecules into an energy-rich sugar, with light energy stored as chemical energy.

Chloroplasts

Specialized organelles within plant and algae cells where photosynthesis takes place. They contain specific structures like thylakoids and stroma crucial for the process.

Chlorophyll

The primary green pigment embedded in thylakoid membranes that absorbs light energy, particularly red and blue wavelengths, and reflects green light, giving plants their characteristic color.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Energy Carriers (ATP and NADPH)

Molecules produced during the light reactions that temporarily store and transport chemical energy to the Calvin cycle.

Carbon Fixation

The process in the Calvin cycle where CO2 is incorporated into organic molecules, specifically by attaching to a 5-carbon molecule to start the synthesis of glucose.

Limiting Factors

Environmental conditions that can restrict the rate of photosynthesis, including light intensity, carbon dioxide concentration, temperature, and water availability.

The Overall Equation

Inputs

Six molecules of carbon dioxide and six molecules of water.

Energy Source

Light energy.

Outputs

One molecule of glucose (an energy-rich sugar) and six molecules of oxygen.
