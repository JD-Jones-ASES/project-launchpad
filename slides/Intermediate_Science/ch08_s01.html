Classification of Elements

Elements are broadly categorized into three types based on their properties:

Important Terms (1/3)

Periodic table

A chart that organizes all known elements by increasing atomic number, arranging them in rows (periods) and columns (groups) so that elements with similar properties fall in the same column.

Triads

Groups of three elements identified by Johann Döbereiner, where the middle element's atomic mass was roughly the average of the other two, and all three shared similar chemical properties.

Law of Octaves

Proposed by John Newlands, stating that every eighth element in his list shared similar properties, analogous to notes in a musical scale.

Dmitri Mendeleev

The Russian chemist who created the first widely accepted periodic table by arranging elements by atomic mass and successfully predicting the existence and properties of undiscovered elements.

Important Terms (2/3)

Henry Moseley

The English physicist who, in 1913, discovered that atomic number, not atomic mass, is the fundamental organizing principle of the periodic table.

Atomic number

The number of protons in the nucleus of an atom, which uniquely identifies an element and determines its position in the modern periodic table.

Period

A horizontal row in the periodic table. Elements in the same period have the same number of electron energy levels.

Group (or Family)

A vertical column in the periodic table. Elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons and similar chemical properties.

Metals

Elements typically found on the left and center of the periodic table, characterized by being shiny, good conductors of heat and electricity, malleable, and ductile.

Important Terms (3/3)

Nonmetals

Elements typically found on the upper right side of the periodic table, generally poor conductors, brittle as solids, and diverse in physical state and appearance.

Metalloids (Semimetals)

Elements located along the staircase-shaped boundary between metals and nonmetals, exhibiting properties intermediate between the two categories.
