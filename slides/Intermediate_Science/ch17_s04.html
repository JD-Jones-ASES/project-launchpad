Key Concepts (1/3)

Quantitative Water Cycle

Beyond evaporation, condensation, precipitation, and collection, this section focuses on the *amounts* of water in different reservoirs and the *time* water spends in each.

Residence Time

This is the average duration a water molecule stays in a particular reservoir. It varies greatly, from days in the atmosphere to thousands of years in glaciers or deep groundwater.

Groundwater

Water that infiltrates the soil and percolates downwards to fill pore spaces in rock and sediment, forming a critical underground reservoir.

Water Table

The upper boundary of the saturated zone in the ground, where all pore spaces are filled with water. It fluctuates based on recharge (precipitation) and discharge (pumping, dry periods).

Key Concepts (2/3)

Aquifer

A permeable layer of rock or sediment that can store and transmit significant amounts of groundwater. Aquifers are vital for human water supply but are often subject to over-extraction.

Watershed (Drainage Basin)

An area of land where all precipitation drains into a common body of water. Divides, like the Continental Divide, separate these basins.

Fate of Precipitation

When rain falls, it can infiltrate to become groundwater, run off into surface waters (especially on impervious surfaces), or evaporate/transpire from plants.

Eutrophication

The process where excess nutrients (like nitrogen and phosphorus from fertilizers) lead to algal blooms, depleting oxygen and creating "dead zones" in aquatic environments.

Key Concepts (3/3)

Thermohaline Circulation (Concept from Investigation)

Ocean currents driven by differences in water temperature ("thermo") and salinity ("haline"), which affect water density. Colder, saltier water is denser and sinks, driving global ocean circulation.

Important Terms (1/3)

Residence Time

The average length of time a substance (like a water molecule) remains in a particular system or reservoir.

Groundwater

Water held underground in the soil and in the pores of permeable rocks.

Water Table

The level below the Earth's surface at which the ground becomes saturated with water.

Aquifer

An underground layer of water-bearing permeable rock or unconsolidated materials (gravel, sand, silt) from which groundwater can be extracted using a water well.

Watershed (Drainage Basin)

An area or ridge of land that separates waters flowing to different rivers, basins, or seas.

Important Terms (2/3)

Divides

High points or ridges of land that separate one watershed from another.

Impervious Surfaces

Surfaces that do not allow water to infiltrate into the ground, such as pavement, concrete, buildings, and compacted soil.

Transpiration

The process by which moisture is carried through plants from roots to small pores on the underside of leaves, where it changes to vapor and is released to the atmosphere.

Eutrophication

The process by which a body of water becomes overly enriched with minerals and nutrients, which induces excessive growth of algae and leads to oxygen depletion.

Land Subsidence

The gradual sinking or sudden downward movement of the ground surface, often caused by the removal of water, oil, or gas from beneath the Earth's surface.

Important Terms (3/3)

Thermohaline Circulation

A part of the large-scale ocean circulation that is driven by global density gradients created by surface heat and freshwater fluxes.
