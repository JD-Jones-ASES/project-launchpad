Earth in Space

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Earth's Motions

Earth constantly moves in two primary ways: **rotation** (spinning on its axis) and **revolution** (orbiting the Sun).

Rotation and Day/Night

Earth's rotation on its axis every ~24 hours causes the cycle of day and night.

Revolution and the Year

Earth's revolution around the Sun every ~365.25 days defines a year and necessitates leap days.

Axial Tilt and Seasons

The 23.5-degree tilt of Earth's axis, which remains pointed in a constant direction, is the primary reason for the changing seasons, *not* Earth's varying distance from the Sun.

Solstices and Equinoxes

Specific points in Earth's orbit marking the longest/shortest days (solstices) and equal day/night lengths (equinoxes).

Key Concepts (2/2)

Time Zones

The Earth's rotation rate of 15 degrees per hour is used to divide the world into 24 time zones.

Important Terms (1/3)

Rotation

The spinning of a planet or body on its axis. Earth's rotation causes day and night.

Axis

An imaginary line running through the center of a planet from its North Pole to its South Pole, around which it rotates.

Terminator

The boundary line on a celestial body that separates the illuminated (day) side from the dark (night) side.

Revolution

The orbital motion of one celestial body around another. Earth's revolution around the Sun defines a year.

Astronomical Unit (AU)

A unit of length, roughly the average distance from Earth to the Sun (about 150 million kilometers).

Important Terms (2/3)

Perihelion

The point in Earth's elliptical orbit when it is closest to the Sun (occurs in January).

Aphelion

The point in Earth's elliptical orbit when it is farthest from the Sun (occurs in July).

Axial Tilt

The angle (approximately 23.5 degrees for Earth) at which a planet's rotational axis is tilted relative to the plane of its orbit around the Sun. This tilt is responsible for the seasons.

Solstice

Either of the two days each year when the Sun reaches its highest or lowest point in the sky at noon, marking the longest and shortest days of the year.

Equinox

Either of the two days each year when day and night are approximately equal in length everywhere on Earth.

Important Terms (3/3)

International Date Line

An imaginary line of longitude, located roughly at 180 degrees, where the calendar date changes by one day.

Rotation: Day and Night

Definition

Rotation is the spinning of a planet on its axis. Earth's axis is an imaginary line connecting the North and South Poles, passing through the planet's center.

Duration and Effect

Earth completes one full rotation approximately every **24 hours**, which produces the cycle of **day and night**.

Mechanism

As Earth rotates from west to east, different parts of its surface are exposed to the Sun's light. The side facing the Sun experiences daytime, while the side facing away experiences nighttime.

The Terminator

Because Earth is a sphere, the transition between day and night is not instantaneous. The boundary is a gradual circle called the **terminator**, creating the periods of dawn and dusk.

Revolution: The Year

Definition

Revolution is the orbital motion of one celestial body around another. Earth **revolves** around the Sun.

Duration and Effect

Earth's Orbit

Earth orbits the Sun at an average distance of about **150 million kilometers**, a distance often referred to as 1 **Astronomical Unit (AU)**.

Orbital Shape

Earth's orbit is not a perfect circle but an **ellipse** (a slightly elongated circle). This means Earth's distance from the Sun varies throughout the year.

Important Note

The varying distance from the Sun due to Earth's elliptical orbit is *not* the cause of the seasons. This is a common misconception.

Axial Tilt and the Seasons (1/2)

The Key Factor

The primary cause of Earth's seasons is its **axial tilt**. Earth's axis is tilted at approximately **23.5 degrees** relative to the plane of its orbit around the Sun.

Constant Direction

Crucially, Earth's axial tilt does not change direction as it orbits the Sun; the North Pole always points towards the star Polaris.

Mechanism of Seasons

Because of this constant tilt, different parts of Earth receive varying amounts of direct sunlight throughout the year.

Solstices

These are the two days each year when the Sun reaches its highest or lowest point in the sky.

Equinoxes

These are the two days each year when day and night are approximately equal in length across the entire Earth.

Time Zones

Earth's Rotation Rate

Since Earth rotates 360 degrees in 24 hours, it rotates **15 degrees of longitude per hour**.

Time Zone System

This rate is used to divide the world into **24 time zones**, each covering approximately 15 degrees of longitude.

Changing Time

As you travel eastward, clocks advance by one hour for each time zone crossed. When traveling westward, clocks fall back one hour per time zone.
