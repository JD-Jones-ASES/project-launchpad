Key Concepts (1/2)

Science as a Systematic Inquiry

Science is a disciplined method of studying the natural world, using careful observation, experimentation, and evidence to answer questions.

Empirical Evidence

This is information gathered directly through observation and experimentation, forming the bedrock of scientific knowledge. It's what makes science distinct from other forms of inquiry.

Openness to Revision

Scientific knowledge is not static; it's constantly updated and refined as new evidence emerges. This willingness to change is a fundamental strength.

Observations and Testable Questions

Every investigation begins with observations (qualitative or quantitative) that lead to specific, measurable questions that can be investigated through experimentation.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Hypotheses

Scientific Theories

These are well-tested, widely accepted explanations for a broad range of observations, supported by extensive evidence from many independent investigations. They explain *why* phenomena occur.

Scientific Laws

These are statements, often mathematical, that describe consistent patterns in nature, telling us *what* happens under certain conditions but not necessarily *why*.

Peer Review

A critical process where scientific work is evaluated by other experts in the same field *before* publication, helping to ensure quality, accuracy, and methodological rigor.

Important Terms

Qualitative observation

Descriptive observations (e.g., "the solution turned blue").

Quantitative observation

Numerical or measurable observations (e.g., "the solution reached 72°C in 4 minutes").
