Chapter 6, Section 2

Levels of Organization in Living Things

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Hierarchical Organization

Living organisms are organized into a series of increasingly complex levels: cells, tissues, organs, organ systems, and the complete organism.

Cell Specialization

Individual cells are specialized with unique structures and functions, but they cannot perform all necessary tasks alone.

Tissues as Functional Units

Similar specialized cells group together to form tissues, which carry out specific functions (e.g., protection, support, contraction, signal transmission).

Organs as Integrated Structures

Organs are formed by the combination of two or more different tissue types working together to perform a more complex function that no single tissue could achieve.

Organ Systems for Broad Functions

Organ systems are groups of organs that cooperate to perform major biological functions vital for the organism's survival.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Interdependence and Coordination

All levels of organization are interdependent; the proper functioning of higher levels relies on the proper functioning of lower levels, and organ systems constantly interact.

Emergent Properties

At higher levels of organization, new properties and capabilities (like consciousness or movement) emerge that are not present at the lower, simpler levels.

Important Terms (1/3)

Tissue

A group of similar cells that work together to perform a specific function.

Epithelial Tissue

Tissue that covers body surfaces, lines body cavities and organs, and forms glands; acts as a protective barrier and controls material passage.

Connective Tissue

Tissue that supports, binds, and protects other tissues and organs; characterized by cells widely separated by an extracellular matrix.

Extracellular Matrix

Non-living material that separates cells in connective tissue.

Muscle Tissue

Tissue specialized for contraction, allowing for movement and force generation.

Important Terms (2/3)

Nervous Tissue

Tissue that transmits electrical signals throughout the body.

Neurons

Cells in nervous tissue that carry electrical impulses.

Glial Cells

Support cells in nervous tissue that support, insulate, and nourish neurons.

Organ

A structure composed of two or more tissue types that work together to perform a specific, complex function.

Organ System

A group of organs that work together to perform a broad biological function essential to the organism's survival.

Important Terms (3/3)

Organism

A complete, individual living thing made up of one or more cells; in multicellular organisms, all organ systems work together to maintain life.

Cells: The Basic Building Blocks

Cell Specialization

As learned previously, cells are specialized, meaning their structure is perfectly suited for a specific function.

Organs: Tissues in Combination

Complexity

Organs represent a higher level of complexity where diverse tissues are integrated.

Organ Systems: Coordinated Teams

Integration

Organ systems bring together multiple organs that work in a sequence or in parallel to accomplish a major task.

Eleven Major Systems

The human body has eleven major organ systems (to be explored further in Section 3).

Interconnection

Organ systems do not operate in isolation; they constantly interact and depend on one another.

The Organism: All Systems United

Coordination

In a multicellular organism, all organ systems work together in a coordinated whole to maintain life.
