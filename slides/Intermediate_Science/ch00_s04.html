Scientific Models as Representations

Scientists use models, which are simplified representations, to study complex objects, systems, or processes that are too vast, too small, too slow, or too abstract to be observed directly.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Controlled Experiments

Investigations designed to test a hypothesis by manipulating one independent variable while keeping all other controlled variables constant to observe the effect on a dependent variable.

Important Terms (1/3)

Scientific Model

A simplified representation of an object, system, or process that helps scientists understand, explain, and make predictions about the real world.

Physical Model

A three-dimensional, tangible representation of an object or system, often built to scale (e.g., a globe).

Conceptual Model

A description, diagram, or analogy that helps explain how something works, often using familiar ideas to represent unfamiliar processes (e.g., a food web, electrical current as water flow).

Analogy

A comparison between two different things, used to explain something unfamiliar in terms of something familiar.

Mathematical Model

An equation or set of equations that describes the quantitative relationships between variables in a system, allowing for precise, numerical predictions (e.g., $F=ma$).

Important Terms (2/3)

Independent Variable

The variable that is intentionally changed or manipulated by the experimenter.

Dependent Variable

The variable that is measured or observed; its value changes in response to the independent variable.

Controlled Variable

A variable that is kept constant or the same throughout an experiment to ensure that only the independent variable affects the dependent variable.

Hypothesis

A testable explanation or prediction for an observation or problem.

Photosynthesis

The process by which green plants and some other organisms use sunlight to synthesize foods with carbon dioxide and water, releasing oxygen as a byproduct.

Important Terms (3/3)

Quantum Mechanical Model

The current, most sophisticated model of the atom, describing electrons as probability clouds rather than fixed orbits.

Niels Bohr

A Danish physicist famous for his atomic model, which proposed that electrons orbit the nucleus in specific energy levels, explaining the line spectra of hydrogen.

Why Models Are Useful

Understanding

They simplify complex systems, enabling scientists and students to grasp the most important aspects by ignoring less relevant details (e.g., a circuit diagram).

Communication

Models create a shared visual or conceptual language, allowing scientists worldwide to communicate and interpret ideas consistently (e.g., a geological cross-section).

Testing Ideas

Models offer a way to test hypotheses or design concepts before undertaking costly or time-consuming real-world experiments (e.g., computer modeling of bridges, biological population dynamics).

Experiment Overview: (1/2)

Question

How does the amount of light a plant receives daily affect its growth over two weeks?

Variables

Groups

Procedure

Involves consistent planting, watering, maintaining controlled variables, and applying specific light durations to each group.

Data Collection

Measure the tallest seedling's height in each pot every two days for 14 days.

Experiment Overview: (2/2)

Analysis

Calculate mean height and range for each group. Create a line graph plotting mean height versus time for all groups.

Conclusions

Based on data analysis, determine which group grew tallest, identify trends, assess hypothesis support, discuss potential errors, and suggest improvements.
