Cellular Respiration: Harvesting Energy from Food

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Energy Extraction from Glucose

Cells break down glucose (a simple sugar derived from food) through a series of chemical reactions to produce ATP, the cell's main energy currency.

Cellular Respiration vs. Breathing

Overall Equation

The simplified chemical equation is C6H12O6 + 6O2 → 6CO2 + 6H2O + Energy (ATP), essentially the reverse of photosynthesis.

Three Main Stages (Aerobic Respiration)

When oxygen is present, cellular respiration proceeds through glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain, yielding a large amount of ATP.

Glycolysis (Stage 1)

The initial breakdown of glucose into two pyruvate molecules. It occurs in the cytoplasm, does not require oxygen (anaerobic), and produces a small net gain of ATP and electron carriers (NADH).

Key Concepts (2/2)

Krebs Cycle (Stage 2)

Processes pyruvate derivatives (acetyl-CoA) in the mitochondrial matrix, releasing carbon dioxide, a small amount of ATP, and more electron carriers (NADH and FADH2). It requires oxygen indirectly.

Important Terms (1/3)

Cellular Respiration

The process by which cells break down glucose and other organic molecules in the presence of oxygen to produce ATP, carbon dioxide, and water.

ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate)

The primary energy currency of the cell.

Glycolysis

The first stage of cellular respiration, occurring in the cytoplasm, where one molecule of glucose is split into two molecules of pyruvate. It is an anaerobic process.

Pyruvate

A three-carbon molecule formed from the breakdown of glucose during glycolysis.

NADH/FADH2

Electron carriers that transport high-energy electrons to the electron transport chain.

Important Terms (2/3)

Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle)

The second stage of aerobic cellular respiration, occurring in the mitochondrial matrix, which completes the breakdown of pyruvate (via acetyl-CoA) and produces CO2, ATP, NADH, and FADH2.

Acetyl-CoA

A two-carbon molecule formed from pyruvate, which enters the Krebs cycle.

Electron Transport Chain (ETC)

ATP Synthase

An enzyme embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane that uses the flow of hydrogen ions (H+) to generate ATP.

Aerobic Respiration

Cellular respiration that requires oxygen.

Important Terms (3/3)

Anaerobic Respiration

Cellular respiration that does not require oxygen (e.g., glycolysis, fermentation).

Fermentation

An anaerobic pathway that allows glycolysis to continue when oxygen is not available by recycling electron carriers (NADH to NAD+). It produces far less ATP than aerobic respiration.

Lactic Acid Fermentation

A type of fermentation where pyruvate is converted into lactic acid (occurs in muscle cells during intense exercise and in some bacteria).

Alcoholic Fermentation

A type of fermentation where pyruvate is converted into ethanol and carbon dioxide (performed by yeast).

Introduction to Cellular Respiration

Purpose

To extract energy from food molecules (like glucose) and store it in ATP for cellular activities.

Mechanism

A series of carefully controlled chemical reactions.

Distinction from Breathing

Overall Equation for Cellular Respiration

Equation

C6H12O6 (glucose) + 6O2 (oxygen) → 6CO2 (carbon dioxide) + 6H2O (water) + Energy (≈36-38 ATP)

Three Main Stages

Glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain.

Stage 1: Glycolysis (1/2)

Definition

The initial breakdown of glucose.

Location

Cytoplasm of the cell (outside any organelle).

Oxygen Requirement

Anaerobic (does *not* require oxygen).

Evolutionary Significance

Oldest metabolic pathway, found in virtually all living organisms.

Stage 3: The Electron Transport Chain (ETC)

Definition

The final and most productive stage of aerobic respiration.

Location

Inner membrane of the mitochondria.

ATP Yield

Produces the vast majority of ATP (approximately 32-34 ATP per glucose).

Efficiency

The ETC accounts for about 90% of the total ATP yield from glucose.

Fermentation: Respiration Without Oxygen (1/2)

Definition

An anaerobic pathway that allows glycolysis to continue when oxygen is unavailable.

Purpose

To recycle NADH back into NAD+ so that glycolysis can keep producing its small amount of ATP.

Conditions

Occurs when the ETC and Krebs cycle cannot operate due to lack of oxygen.

ATP Yield

Only 2 ATP per glucose (from glycolysis alone), making it much less efficient than aerobic respiration.

Industrial Importance

Fermentation has been used for millennia for food preservation and production (bread, beer, cheese, soy sauce, kimchi) and is now used in industrial production of antibiotics, biofuels, and vitamins.
