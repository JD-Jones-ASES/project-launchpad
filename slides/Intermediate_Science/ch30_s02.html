Key Concepts

Interdisciplinary Science

The approach of combining multiple academic disciplines to address complex problems, recognizing that real-world issues rarely fit into a single scientific category.

Holistic Problem Solving

The idea that comprehensive solutions to global challenges like climate change or disease require contributions from all major branches of science, rather than relying on a single field.

Climate Change as a Multidimensional Challenge

Understanding that climate change involves atmospheric processes (Earth Science), molecular interactions (Chemistry), energy transfer (Physics), and biological impacts (Life Science).

Important Terms (1/7)

Interdisciplinary Lens

Viewing a problem or topic through the combined perspectives of multiple academic disciplines.

Paleoclimatology

The study of past climates using indirect evidence such as ice cores, tree rings, and sediment records.

Enhanced Greenhouse Effect

The increase in Earth's atmospheric temperature due to elevated levels of greenhouse gases (like CO2) caused by human activities.

Keeling Curve

A graph showing the continuous upward trend of atmospheric carbon dioxide concentrations measured at Mauna Loa Observatory since 1958.

Anthropogenic CO2

Carbon dioxide emissions originating from human activities, primarily the burning of fossil fuels.

Important Terms (2/7)

Ocean Acidification

The ongoing decrease in the pH of the Earth's oceans, caused by the absorption of excess atmospheric carbon dioxide.

Carbon Chemistry

The branch of chemistry that studies carbon compounds, including those found in fossil fuels and organic matter.

Electromagnetic Radiation

Energy that travels in waves and spans a wide spectrum, including visible light, infrared, X-rays, and radio waves.

Photosynthesis

The process used by plants and other organisms to convert light energy into chemical energy, storing it in organic molecules and removing CO2 from the atmosphere.

Carbon Sink

A natural or artificial reservoir that absorbs and stores more carbon dioxide from the atmosphere than it releases (e.g., forests, oceans).

Important Terms (3/7)

Ecosystem Dynamics

The study of how interactions between living organisms and their environment change over time.

Pathogens

Disease-causing organisms, such as bacteria, viruses, fungi, or parasites.

Gene Therapy

A technique that involves inserting, altering, or deleting genes within an individual's cells to treat disease.

Functional Groups

Specific groupings of atoms within molecules that are responsible for the characteristic chemical reactions of those molecules, crucial for drug design.

X-ray Crystallography

A technique used to determine the atomic and molecular structure of a crystal, particularly useful in understanding complex biological molecules.

Important Terms (4/7)

MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)

A medical imaging technique that uses strong magnetic fields and radio waves to generate detailed images of organs and soft tissues within the body.

Ultrasound Imaging

A medical imaging technique that uses high-frequency sound waves to create images of internal body structures.

Radiation Therapy

The use of high-energy radiation, such as gamma rays, to damage cancer cells and shrink tumors.

Homeostasis

The ability of an organism or cell to maintain a stable internal environment despite external changes.

Fossil Fuels

Naturally occurring hydrocarbons, such as coal, oil, and natural gas, formed from the remains of ancient organisms.

Important Terms (5/7)

Combustion Reactions

Chemical reactions that involve rapid reactions between a substance with an oxidant, usually oxygen, producing heat and light (e.g., burning fossil fuels).

Conservation of Energy

A fundamental principle of physics stating that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another.

Photovoltaic Effect

The process by which a material converts light energy into electrical energy, as seen in solar panels.

Kinetic Energy

The energy an object possesses due to its motion ($KE = 1/2mv^2$).

Gravitational Potential Energy

The energy an object possesses due to its position in a gravitational field ($PE = mgh$).

Important Terms (6/7)

Electromagnetic Induction

The production of an electromotive force (voltage) across an electrical conductor in a changing magnetic field, the principle behind generators.

Oxidation-Reduction (Redox) Reactions

Chemical reactions involving the transfer of electrons between reactants, crucial for battery function.

Hydrogen Fuel Cells

Electrochemical devices that convert the chemical energy of hydrogen and oxygen into electricity with water as the only byproduct.

Geothermal Energy

Energy derived from the Earth's internal heat.

Tidal Energy

Energy generated by the movement of ocean tides.

Important Terms (7/7)

Biofuels

Fuels derived from biomass (recently living organisms or their metabolic byproducts), such as ethanol from corn or biodiesel from vegetable oil.

Fermentation

An anaerobic metabolic process that converts sugar to acids, gases, or alcohol, used in producing biofuels like ethanol.
