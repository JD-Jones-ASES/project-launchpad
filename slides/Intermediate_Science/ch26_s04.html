Important Terms (1/4)

Deforestation

The large-scale removal of forest, usually to make way for agriculture, ranching, logging, or urban development.

Urbanization

The growth of cities and the conversion of natural or agricultural land into built environments.

Heat Islands

Urban areas that are significantly warmer than surrounding rural areas due to dark pavement and buildings absorbing solar energy, waste heat generation, and fewer trees for shade.

Pollution

The introduction of harmful substances or energy into the environment at rates that exceed the environment's capacity to absorb, dilute, or decompose them.

Particulate matter (PM)

Tiny solid or liquid particles from combustion, dust, and industrial processes that damage lungs and reduce visibility.

Important Terms (2/4)

Carbon monoxide (CO)

A colorless, odorless gas produced by incomplete combustion of fossil fuels that reduces the blood's ability to carry oxygen.

Sulfur dioxide (SO2) and Nitrogen oxides (NOx)

Gases released by burning coal and vehicle exhaust that react with water in the atmosphere to form acid rain.

Acid Rain

Precipitation (rain, snow, or fog) that has become acidic due to atmospheric pollutants, primarily SO2 and NOx, which react with water to form sulfuric acid (H2SO4) and nitric acid (HNO3).

Ground-level ozone (O3)

A pollutant formed when sunlight reacts with NOx and volatile organic compounds; it irritates lungs and damages crops.

Point Sources (water pollution)

Identifiable, specific locations from which pollutants are discharged, such as a factory pipe or sewage outfall.

Important Terms (3/4)

Nonpoint Sources (water pollution)

Diffuse origins of pollution, such as agricultural runoff across a large area.

Bioaccumulation

The process by which a substance increases in concentration in an organism's tissues over time because the organism absorbs it faster than it can eliminate it.

Biomagnification

The increasing concentration of a persistent substance in organisms at each successive trophic level of a food chain, because predators consume many contaminated prey organisms.

Dead Zone

A region of water with dissolved oxygen levels so low that most marine life cannot survive, typically caused by eutrophication.

Important Terms (4/4)

Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs)

Synthetic chemicals once widely used in refrigerants, aerosol sprays, and foam insulation that destroy stratospheric ozone molecules.

Montreal Protocol (1987)

An international agreement to phase out CFCs, considered a highly successful environmental treaty.

Soil Pollution

Impacts

Ozone Depletion

Stratospheric Ozone Layer

A thin layer of ozone (O3) in the stratosphere that shields Earth's surface from harmful ultraviolet (UV) radiation.

Cause

**Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs)**, synthetic chemicals formerly used in refrigerants and aerosols, were discovered to be destroying this layer.

Mechanism

In the stratosphere, UV radiation breaks CFCs apart, releasing chlorine atoms. A single chlorine atom can catalytically destroy thousands of ozone molecules (Cl + O3 → ClO + O2; ClO + O → Cl + O2)....

The Montreal Protocol (1987)

An international agreement to phase out CFCs, considered highly successful. CFC levels have declined, and the ozone layer is slowly recovering, projected to return to 1980 levels by 2060–2070.

UV Radiation

Ozone specifically absorbs dangerous UV-B and UV-C radiation (200–315 nm), which have enough energy to damage DNA and cause skin cancer, cataracts, and immune suppression.
