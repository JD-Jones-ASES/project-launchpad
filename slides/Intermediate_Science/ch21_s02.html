Important Terms (1/3)

Natural Selection

The process by which organisms with traits better suited to their environment tend to survive and reproduce more successfully than organisms without those traits. Over many generations, it causes p...

Adaptation

A heritable trait that increases an organism's ability to survive and reproduce in its specific environment. Adaptations arise through natural selection.

Fitness (Evolutionary)

An organism's ability to survive and reproduce in its specific environment. The fittest organism is the one that produces the most surviving offspring, relative to its environment.

Heritable

Capable of being passed from parent to offspring through genetic material (DNA).

Overproduction

The phenomenon where most species produce more offspring than can possibly survive due to limited resources and environmental challenges.

Important Terms (2/3)

Competition

The struggle among organisms for limited resources (food, water, shelter, mates) and survival against predators, disease, and harsh conditions.

Differential Survival and Reproduction

The concept that individuals with traits that provide an advantage in a particular environment are more likely to survive and produce more offspring than those without such traits.

Galápagos Islands

A group of islands off the coast of South America, famous for the unique species Darwin observed, which greatly influenced his theory.

HMS Beagle

The British survey ship on which Charles Darwin served as naturalist during a five-year voyage (1831-1836).

_On the Origin of Species_

Charles Darwin's seminal book, published in 1859, which presented his theory of evolution by natural selection with extensive evidence.

Important Terms (3/3)

Charles Darwin (1809–1882)

British naturalist who developed the theory of evolution by natural selection.

Alfred Russel Wallace (1823–1913)

A British naturalist who independently conceived a theory of evolution by natural selection around the same time as Darwin, prompting Darwin to publish his work.

Charles Darwin: The Man Behind the Theory

• Born into a wealthy family in 1809, Darwin initially studied medicine and theology but was more drawn to natural history.

• His five-year voyage on HMS Beagle, especially his observations in South America and the Galápagos, provided the empirical foundation for his theory.

• He spent decades after his voyage meticulously collecting evidence and refining his ideas, corresponding with scientists globally.

• He was finally prompted to publish in 1858 when Alfred Russel Wallace independently formulated a similar theory.

• On the Origin of Species, published in 1859, presented overwhelming evidence for natural selection and was profoundly impactful, despite initial controversy.

• Darwin's work, characterized by careful observation and evidence-based reasoning, revolutionized biology and established evolution by natural selection as the unifying theory of life sciences.

What was the name of the ship Charles Darwin sailed on for his famous five-year voyage?

Key Concepts (2/2)

What did the similarities between Galápagos organisms and South American mainland species suggest to Darwin?

They looked as if mainland ancestors had arrived on the islands and then changed over time, implying common ancestry and descent with modification.

When did Darwin publish his seminal work, On the Origin of Species?

1859.

Who independently arrived at the same theory of natural selection as Darwin, prompting Darwin to publish his work?

Alfred Russel Wallace.

Name the four key observations that Darwin's theory of natural selection rests upon.

Variation, Overproduction, Competition, and Differential survival and reproduction.

Explain the observation of 'variation' in the context of natural selection.

Individuals in a population differ in heritable traits (e.g., body size, coloring, speed), much of which is passed from parent to offspring through DNA.
