Chapter 12, Section 3 Study Guide: Wind and Pressure Systems

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Wind as Air Movement

Wind is the movement of air from areas of high pressure to areas of low pressure.

Unequal Heating

The Sun heats Earth's surface unevenly, with the equator receiving more direct sunlight than the poles.

Pressure Differences

Unequal heating leads to warm, less dense air rising (creating low pressure) and cool, denser air sinking (creating high pressure), establishing a pressure gradient.

Coriolis Effect

Global Wind Belts

The combination of pressure differences and the Coriolis effect creates three major wind belts in each hemisphere: Trade Winds, Westerlies, and Polar Easterlies.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Jet Streams

Narrow, fast-moving currents of air in the upper troposphere that steer large-scale weather systems and affect air travel.

Local Winds

Smaller-scale wind systems, such as sea breezes and land breezes, are generated by localized differential heating of land and water.

Important Terms (1/4)

Wind

The movement of air caused by differences in atmospheric pressure.

Atmospheric Pressure

The force exerted by the weight of air molecules on a surface.

High Pressure

An area where air is sinking and is denser, often associated with clear skies.

Low Pressure

An area where air is rising and is less dense, often associated with cloudiness and precipitation.

Coriolis Effect

The apparent deflection of moving objects (like wind and ocean currents) caused by Earth's rotation. Objects deflect right in the Northern Hemisphere and left in the Southern Hemisphere.

Important Terms (2/4)

Trade Winds

Steady easterly winds blowing from approximately 30° latitude toward the equator in both hemispheres, historically important for sailing.

Westerlies

Prevailing winds that blow from west to east between 30° and 60° latitude in both hemispheres, influencing weather patterns across much of North America.

Polar Easterlies

Cold, dry winds blowing from the east near the poles (60°–90° latitude).

Polar Front

The boundary zone where the cold polar easterlies meet the warmer westerlies, often a region of intense storm activity.

Jet Stream

A narrow, fast-moving band of air in the upper troposphere (160–400 km/h), crucial for steering weather systems.

Important Terms (3/4)

Polar Jet Stream

The stronger jet stream found near 60° latitude, influencing mid-latitude weather.

Subtropical Jet Stream

A weaker jet stream found near 30° latitude.

Sea Breeze

A local wind that blows from the sea toward the land during the day due to the land heating faster than the ocean.

Land Breeze

A local wind that blows from the land toward the sea at night due to the land cooling faster than the ocean.

Valley Breeze

An upslope wind occurring during the day as air warms over mountain slopes.

Important Terms (4/4)

Mountain Breeze

A downslope wind occurring at night as cool, dense air drains down mountain slopes.

How Pressure Differences Form

Air Movement (Wind)

Air naturally moves from areas of **high pressure** to areas of **low pressure**. This planetary-scale movement drives global wind systems.

The Coriolis Effect

Earth's Rotation and Deflection

If Earth didn't rotate, winds would blow in straight lines from poles to the equator. However, Earth's rotation deflects moving air.

Definition

The apparent deflection of moving objects (including wind and ocean currents) caused by Earth's rotation.

Direction of Deflection

Effect on Wind

The Coriolis effect does not *create* wind; pressure differences do that. It only *curves* the wind's path.

Strength of Effect

Strongest at the poles, weakest (nearly zero) at the equator. It has no noticeable impact on small-scale phenomena like bathtub drains.

The Jet Stream

Definition

Narrow bands of very fast-moving wind (160–400 km/h) in the upper troposphere, near the tropopause. They "snake" around the planet.
