Key Concepts (1/2)

Earth's Interior and Magma Formation

The Earth's layered structure, mantle convection driven by heat, and specific conditions (temperature, pressure) at plate boundaries and hotspots lead to the melting of rock to form magma.

Density and Buoyancy

Magma rises because it is less dense than the surrounding solid rock due to thermal expansion, creating a buoyant force that drives it upwards. This is a fundamental physical principle.

Important Terms (1/5)

Magma

Molten rock found beneath the Earth's surface.

Lava

Molten rock that has erupted onto the Earth's surface.

Density ($ = m/V$)

A measure of mass per unit volume; a key factor in magma's upward movement.

Buoyant force

An upward force exerted by a fluid that opposes the weight of an immersed object.

Convection currents

The circulation of material within the Earth's mantle, driven by heat.

Important Terms (2/5)

Silicate unit

The fundamental building block of most volcanic minerals, consisting of a silicon atom bonded to four oxygen atoms in a tetrahedral arrangement.

Viscosity

A measure of a fluid's resistance to flow; influenced by lava's silica content.

Atomic number

The number of protons in an atom's nucleus, which determines its element.

Valence electrons

Electrons in the outermost shell of an atom, involved in chemical bonding.

Projectile path

The parabolic trajectory followed by an object launched into the air under the influence of gravity.

Important Terms (3/5)

Kinematic equations

Mathematical formulas used to describe motion, relating displacement, velocity, acceleration, and time.

Seismic waves

Waves of energy that travel through the Earth's layers, generated by earthquakes or volcanic eruptions.

P-waves (Primary waves)

The fastest type of seismic wave, which travel through solids and liquids.

Denature

The process by which proteins lose their specific three-dimensional structure and thus their biological function, often due to extreme heat or pH changes.

Cell membrane

The phospholipid bilayer that forms the outer boundary of a cell, controlling what enters and leaves.

Important Terms (4/5)

Homeostasis

The ability of an organism or cell to maintain a stable internal environment.

Pioneer organisms

The first species to colonize newly exposed or disturbed land.

Diffusion

The net movement of particles from an area of higher concentration to an area of lower concentration.

Osmosis

The diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane from an area of high water concentration to an area of low water concentration.

Cellular respiration

The metabolic process by which cells convert nutrients into ATP (energy).

Important Terms (5/5)

Mitosis

The process of cell division that results in two daughter cells genetically identical to the parent cell.
