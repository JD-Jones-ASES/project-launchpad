Important Terms (1/3)

Electric charge

A fundamental property of matter that causes it to experience a force in the presence of other charged matter.

Positive charge

Carried by protons.

Negative charge

Carried by electrons.

Coulomb (C)

The SI unit of electric charge.

Electrically neutral

An atom or object with an equal number of protons and electrons, resulting in no net charge.

Important Terms (2/3)

Static electricity

Electric charge that accumulates on an object and stays in place rather than flowing.

Electrostatic force

The force of attraction or repulsion between charged objects.

Electric current ($I$)

The rate at which electric charge flows past a given point, measured in amperes (A).

Ampere (A)

The SI unit of electric current, equal to one coulomb per second (1C/s).

Conventional current

The historical definition of current as the flow of positive charge from positive to negative terminals.

Important Terms (3/3)

Electron flow

The actual movement of electrons (negative charge) from negative to positive terminals in a circuit.

Conductor

A material through which electric charge flows easily, typically due to free electrons.

Insulator

A material that strongly resists the flow of electric charge, as electrons are tightly bound.

Semiconductor

A material that conducts electricity under certain conditions, intermediate between conductors and insulators.

Doping

The process of adding tiny amounts of other elements to a semiconductor to control its conductivity.

Unit of Charge

The Law of Charges

Electrostatic Force

The force between charged objects is called the electrostatic force. It strengthens as charges get closer and as the amount of charge increases.

Electric Current

Definition

While static electricity involves stationary charge, most electrical phenomena involve **electric current**, which is the continuous flow of charge through a material.

Measurement

Electric current ($I$) is the rate at which electric charge ($Q$) flows past a given point over a specific time ($t$).

Formula

$I = \frac{Q}{t}$

Unit of Current

The SI unit for current is the **ampere (A)**, where 1 ampere equals 1 coulomb per second (1A = 1C/s).
