Important Terms (1/3)

Radioactivity

The spontaneous process of an unstable atomic nucleus breaking apart or reorganizing itself, releasing energy and particles.

Strong nuclear force

The powerful attractive force that holds protons and neutrons together in the nucleus, overcoming the electrical repulsion between protons.

Unstable nucleus

An atomic nucleus with an imbalanced ratio of protons to neutrons, causing it to spontaneously decay.

Radioactive decay

The process by which an unstable nucleus transforms into a more stable configuration by emitting particles or energy.

Alpha particle ($^4_2He$)

A type of radiation composed of two protons and two neutrons (a helium-4 nucleus), carrying a +2 charge.

Important Terms (2/3)

Beta particle ($e^-$)

A type of radiation consisting of a high-speed electron emitted from the nucleus when a neutron transforms into a proton.

Gamma ray

A type of high-energy electromagnetic radiation (photon) emitted from the nucleus, with no mass or charge.

Half-life

The time it takes for half of the radioactive atoms in a sample to undergo radioactive decay.

Radioisotopes

Radioactive isotopes, often used in medical, industrial, or scientific applications.

Nuclear fission

The process where a heavy atomic nucleus splits into two or more smaller nuclei, releasing a large amount of energy.

Important Terms (3/3)

Background radiation

Naturally occurring radiation from environmental sources such as cosmic rays, radioactive elements in rocks and soil, and radioactive isotopes in food.

Millisieverts (mSv)

A unit used to measure effective dose of radiation, indicating the biological effect on human tissue.

Radiation Safety

Time

Minimize the duration of exposure to radioactive sources. Less time means less accumulated dose.

Distance

Maximize the distance from radioactive sources. Radiation intensity decreases rapidly with increasing distance (inverse square law).

Shielding

Use appropriate barriers to block radiation.
