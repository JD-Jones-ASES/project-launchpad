Important Terms (1/3)

Scientific Literacy

The ability to understand basic scientific concepts and processes well enough to make informed decisions about issues that affect one's life and community.

Biomedical Researcher

A scientist who investigates the causes and treatments of diseases at the cellular and molecular levels.

Ecologist

A scientist who studies ecosystems, biodiversity, and conservation strategies to protect the natural world.

Geologist

An Earth scientist who studies rocks, minerals, and geological processes, often working in resource management, environmental remediation, or natural hazard assessment.

Meteorologist

A scientist who forecasts weather and studies atmospheric phenomena.

Important Terms (2/3)

Pharmaceutical Chemist

A chemist who designs and tests new drug molecules to develop medications.

Materials Scientist

A scientist who develops new materials with specific properties, such as stronger alloys or better batteries.

Medical Physicist

A physicist who develops and maintains medical imaging equipment (like MRI or CT scanners) and radiation therapy systems.

Data Scientist

A professional who applies mathematical and statistical methods to analyze large datasets to extract insights and inform decisions, increasingly valuable in all fields.

Interdisciplinary Careers

Professions that combine knowledge and skills from multiple scientific branches or blend science with other fields like law, journalism, or engineering.

Important Terms (3/3)

Scientific Consensus

The broad agreement among the vast majority of scientists in a particular field, based on extensive evidence gathered from numerous independent studies.

Citizen Science

Scientific research conducted, in whole or in part, by amateur or nonprofessional scientists, often volunteers, who contribute to data collection or analysis.

Careers in Science: Beyond the Lab

Life Science

These careers focus on living organisms and their systems.

Earth Science

These fields explore our planet and the cosmos.

Chemistry

These careers involve the study of matter and its properties.

Physics

These professionals study energy, matter, space, and time.

Interdisciplinary Careers

These roles combine multiple scientific disciplines or integrate science with other professional fields.

Scientific Literacy for Every Citizen

Health Decisions

Interpreting nutrition labels, evaluating medical treatments, understanding vaccinations, and making sense of genetic test results all rely on knowledge of biology and chemistry.

Environmental Decisions

Comprehending climate change, evaluating energy policies, and making choices about resource use (water, waste) require understanding Earth science, chemistry, and ecology.

Technology Decisions

Assessing the safety and effectiveness of new technologies, understanding digital privacy, and choosing energy-efficient appliances involve knowledge of physics and engineering.

How to Keep Learning (1/2)

Take advanced science courses

Deepen your understanding in biology, chemistry, physics, and Earth science.

Study mathematics

Math is the language of science; courses through Algebra 2 and trigonometry are highly beneficial.

Explore advanced options

Consider AP or honors science courses for college-level rigor.

Engage in extracurriculars

Join science clubs, participate in science fairs, and enter competitions to explore interests beyond the curriculum.

Read widely

Explore science books and magazines for general audiences from reputable authors and publishers.

How to Keep Learning (2/2)

Watch documentaries

Use visual media to bring scientific concepts to life.

Visit museums

Science museums, planetariums, and natural history museums offer interactive learning experiences.

Follow science news

Stay informed about new discoveries from credible sources.

Participate in citizen science

Contribute to real scientific research by collecting data or observing phenomena.
