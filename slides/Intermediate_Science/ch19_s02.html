Chapter 19, Section 2 - Sound Waves

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Sound as a Mechanical Wave

Sound is produced by vibrations and requires a material medium (solid, liquid, or gas) to propagate. It cannot travel through a vacuum.

Longitudinal Waves

Sound travels as a longitudinal wave, meaning the particles of the medium vibrate parallel to the direction of wave propagation, creating alternating regions of high pressure (compressions) and low...

Speed of Sound

The speed of sound depends on the medium's properties (density and stiffness) and its temperature. It travels faster in denser, stiffer materials and at higher temperatures.

Pitch and Frequency

Pitch, the perceived highness or lowness of a sound, is determined by the wave's frequency. Higher frequency means higher pitch.

Loudness and Amplitude

Loudness, the perceived intensity of a sound, is related to the wave's amplitude (the maximum pressure variation). Greater amplitude means a louder sound and more energy carried by the wave.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Decibel Scale

Sound intensity is measured logarithmically in decibels (dB), where a 10 dB increase represents a tenfold increase in intensity.

Resonance

Important Terms (1/3)

Sound Wave

A longitudinal mechanical wave caused by vibrations, propagating through a medium.

Mechanical Wave

A wave that requires a medium through which to travel, transferring energy by the vibration of particles.

Compression

A region in a longitudinal wave where the particles of the medium are crowded together, corresponding to a region of higher pressure.

Rarefaction

A region in a longitudinal wave where the particles of the medium are spread apart, corresponding to a region of lower pressure.

Vacuum

A space entirely devoid of matter. Sound cannot travel through a vacuum.

Important Terms (2/3)

Pitch

The perceived highness or lowness of a sound, primarily determined by its frequency.

Frequency

The number of wave cycles (or vibrations) that pass a point per unit of time, measured in Hertz (Hz).

Infrasound

Sound waves with frequencies below the range of human hearing (typically below 20 Hz).

Ultrasound

Sound waves with frequencies above the range of human hearing (typically above 20,000 Hz).

Loudness

The subjective perception of the intensity of a sound, related to the amplitude of the sound wave.

Important Terms (3/3)

Amplitude

The maximum displacement or distance moved by a point on a vibrating body or wave measured from its equilibrium position; for sound, it's the maximum pressure variation from normal.

Decibel (dB)

A logarithmic unit used to measure sound intensity.

Doppler Effect

The perceived change in the frequency of a wave when its source and observer are moving relative to each other.

Redshift

A phenomenon in astronomy where light from a source moving away from an observer appears to have longer (redder) wavelengths due to the Doppler effect.

Resonance

The phenomenon where an object vibrates with increased amplitude when a periodic force is applied at its natural frequency.

How Sound Travels: A Mechanical Wave

Sound cannot travel through a vacuum

because there are no particles to transmit the vibrations. This is why sound cannot be heard in outer space. Spacecraft use electromagnetic waves (like radio waves) for communication.

Speed of Sound

Comparing Sound and Light

Light travels incredibly fast (about 3 x 10^8 m/s), nearly a million times faster than sound. This is why you see lightning before you hear thunder.

Pitch and Frequency

Pitch

is the perceived highness or lowness of a sound.

Infrasound

Frequencies below 20 Hz (e.g., used by elephants and whales for long-distance communication).

Ultrasound

Frequencies above 20,000 Hz (e.g., used in medical imaging, and by bats and dolphins for echolocation).

Loudness, Amplitude, and Decibels

Loudness

is the perceived intensity of a sound.

Decibel (dB) Scale

Sound intensity is measured using the decibel scale, which is logarithmic.

Safety Note

Prolonged exposure to sounds above **85 dB can cause permanent hearing damage**. Always use ear protection in loud environments.

Resonance and Musical Instruments

Resonance

is a dramatic increase in amplitude that occurs when a vibrating force is applied at an object's **natural frequency**.

Example

Pushing a child on a swing at the correct timing (matching its natural frequency) makes the swing's amplitude grow larger.

Applications

Musical Instruments

Instruments are designed to utilize resonance to amplify and create specific sounds.
