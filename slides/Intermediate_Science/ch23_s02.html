Important Terms (1/2)

Solubility

The maximum amount of a solute that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature. It's commonly expressed in grams of solute per 100 grams of water.

Solubility Curve

A graph depicting the relationship between temperature and the maximum solubility of a substance in a particular solvent.

Saturated Solution

A solution that holds the maximum possible amount of dissolved solute for a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature. No more solute can dissolve under those conditions.

Unsaturated Solution

A solution that contains less than the maximum amount of solute that can be dissolved at a given temperature, meaning more solute could still be added and dissolved.

Important Terms (2/2)

Crystallization

The process by which solid crystals form from a solution, often occurring when a solution becomes supersaturated or when solvent evaporates.

Precipitation

The formation of a solid (precipitate) from a solution, often due to a chemical reaction or a decrease in solubility.

Henry's Law

A principle stating that the solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the partial pressure of that gas above the liquid.

Solubility of Gases

Effect of Temperature

Gases become less soluble as temperature increases. This is because higher temperatures give gas molecules more kinetic energy, making it easier for them to escape from the liquid phase.

Effect of Pressure (Henry's Law)

Gas solubility increases as the partial pressure of the gas above the liquid increases. The higher pressure forces more gas molecules into the solution.

Define solubility.

The maximum amount of a solute that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature, typically expressed in grams of solute per 100 g of ...

What information is typically represented on the horizontal and vertical axes of a solubility curve?

Temperature (typically in degrees Celsius) on the horizontal axis and solubility (g of solute per 100 g H2O) on the vertical axis.

What does each distinct line on a solubility curve represent?

Each line represents the solubility of a different substance.

How do you determine the solubility of a substance at a specific temperature using its solubility curve?

Locate the temperature on the horizontal axis, draw a vertical line up to the curve, then read across to the vertical axis to find the solubility.

What does a point on the solubility curve indicate about a solution?

It represents a saturated solution, meaning the maximum amount of solute is dissolved at that temperature.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What does a point below the solubility curve signify about a solution?

It represents an unsaturated solution, meaning more solute could still dissolve at that temperature.

What does a point above the solubility curve signify about a solution?

It represents a supersaturated solution, meaning more solute is dissolved than the curve predicts, an unstable condition.

Define a saturated solution.

A solution that contains the maximum amount of dissolved solute at a given temperature; no more solute can dissolve unless the temperature changes.

Define a supersaturated solution.

A solution that contains more dissolved solute than a saturated solution at the same temperature. These solutions are unstable.

Why are supersaturated solutions considered unstable?

Adding a seed crystal or disturbing the solution can cause the excess solute to crystallize rapidly.

What does a steep slope on a solubility curve (like KNO3) indicate about a substance's solubility?

It indicates that the substance's solubility becomes dramatically more soluble as temperature increases.
