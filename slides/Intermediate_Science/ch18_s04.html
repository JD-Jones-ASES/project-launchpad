Types of Chemical Reactions: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts

Classification Simplifies Understanding

Grouping reactions into basic types helps chemists predict outcomes and generalize patterns.

Five Main Reaction Types

Reactivity Series

This essential tool determines whether a single replacement reaction will occur, as a more reactive element will displace a less reactive one.

Energy Changes

Some reactions (like decomposition) typically require energy input (endothermic), while others (like combustion) release energy (exothermic).

Law of Conservation of Mass

Antoine Lavoisier's work highlighted that matter is neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction, meaning the total mass of reactants equals the total mass of products.

Important Terms (1/3)

Synthesis reaction

A reaction in which two or more simple substances combine to form a single, more complex product.

Decomposition reaction

A reaction in which a single compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances.

Single replacement reaction

A reaction in which one element replaces another element in a compound.

Activity series

A list of elements ranked by their reactivity, used to predict if a single replacement reaction will occur.

Double replacement reaction

A reaction in which the positive ions of two compounds switch partners.

Important Terms (2/3)

Precipitate

An insoluble solid that forms from a solution during a chemical reaction.

Combustion

A rapid, exothermic reaction in which a substance (fuel) reacts with oxygen gas (O2) to produce oxides, typically carbon dioxide (CO2) and water (H2O) when the fuel is a hydrocarbon.

Hydrocarbon

An organic compound consisting entirely of hydrogen and carbon atoms.

Exothermic

A process or reaction that releases energy, usually in the form of heat or light.

Endothermic

A process or reaction that absorbs energy, typically in the form of heat, from its surroundings.

Important Terms (3/3)

Catalyst

A substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without itself being consumed.

Law of Conservation of Mass

A fundamental law stating that matter cannot be created or destroyed in an isolated system during a chemical reaction.

Synthesis (Combination) Reactions

Definition

In a synthesis reaction, two or more simpler substances (elements or compounds) join together to form a single, more complex product.

General Pattern

$A + B \rightarrow AB$

Examples

Decomposition Reactions

Definition

A decomposition reaction is the opposite of a synthesis reaction. A single, complex compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances.

General Pattern

$AB \rightarrow A + B$

Energy Requirement

These reactions usually require an input of energy, often in the form of heat, electricity, or light, to break the chemical bonds.

Examples

Single Replacement Reactions

Definition

In a single replacement reaction, one element replaces another element that is part of a compound. The "winning" element must be more reactive than the element it displaces.

General Pattern

$A + BC \rightarrow AC + B$

Double Replacement Reactions

Definition

In a double replacement reaction, the positive ions (cations) of two different compounds switch partners with the negative ions (anions).

General Pattern

$AB + CD \rightarrow AD + CB$

Conditions

These reactions typically occur in aqueous solutions. They often result in the formation of a precipitate (an insoluble solid), a gas, or a molecule of water.

Lab Investigation: Five Reaction Types Lab

Synthesis (Station 1)

Burning magnesium ribbon produces bright light and white magnesium oxide.

Decomposition (Station 2)

Hydrogen peroxide breaks down (catalyzed by yeast) to produce oxygen gas, visible as foam.

Single Replacement (Station 3)

Zinc metal reacts with hydrochloric acid, producing hydrogen gas bubbles and dissolving zinc.

Double Replacement (Station 4)

Silver nitrate and sodium chloride solutions mix to form an immediate white precipitate of silver chloride.

Combustion (Station 5)

A burning candle demonstrates combustion, which ceases when oxygen is consumed by covering it with a jar.
