Important Terms (1/5)

Earthquake

A sudden shaking of the ground caused by the release of energy that has accumulated in Earth's crust.

Fault

A fracture or zone of fractures between two blocks of rock, where the blocks have moved relative to each other.

Seismic waves

Waves of energy that travel through Earth's layers and are caused by earthquakes, volcanoes, or explosions.

Focus (Hypocenter)

The point *inside* the Earth where an earthquake originates, and rock first breaks.

Epicenter

The point on Earth's surface directly *above* the focus of an earthquake.

Important Terms (2/5)

P-waves (Primary waves)

The fastest type of seismic wave; compressional (longitudinal) waves that can travel through solids, liquids, and gases.

S-waves (Secondary waves)

Slower than P-waves; shear (transverse) waves that move rock perpendicular to the direction of travel and can only pass through solids.

Surface waves

Seismic waves that travel along Earth's surface; slower than P-waves and S-waves but typically cause the most ground motion and damage.

Seismograph (Seismometer)

An instrument used to detect and record seismic waves.

Seismogram

The recording produced by a seismograph, showing the arrival times and amplitudes of seismic waves.

Important Terms (3/5)

Magnitude

A measure of the size of an earthquake, related to the energy it releases.

Moment magnitude scale

The most commonly used earthquake magnitude scale today, which measures the total energy released by an earthquake. It is logarithmic.

Richter scale

An older magnitude scale that measures the amplitude of the largest seismic wave on a seismogram; less accurate for very large or distant earthquakes.

S–P time interval

The time difference between the arrival of the first P-wave and the first S-wave at a seismograph station, used to calculate distance to the epicenter.

Triangulation

A method used to locate an earthquake's epicenter by drawing circles from at least three seismograph stations; the point where the circles intersect is the epicenter.

Important Terms (4/5)

Volcano

An opening in Earth's surface through which magma, gases, and ash escape from beneath the crust.

Magma

Molten rock beneath Earth's surface.

Hot spots

Plumes of unusually hot mantle material that rise independently of plate boundaries, causing volcanic activity (e.g., Hawaiian Islands).

Shield volcano

A broad, gently sloping volcano built from many thin layers of low-viscosity (runny) basaltic lava, producing gentle eruptions.

Cinder cone volcano

A small, steep, cone-shaped volcano built from fragments of lava (cinders, ash, bombs) ejected during explosive eruptions.

Important Terms (5/5)

Composite volcano (Stratovolcano)

A tall, steep, symmetrical volcano built from alternating layers of lava flows and explosive volcanic debris, known for dangerous, violent eruptions.

Ring of Fire

A horseshoe-shaped belt around the Pacific Ocean, known for its high concentration of active volcanoes and frequent earthquakes due to extensive subduction zones.
