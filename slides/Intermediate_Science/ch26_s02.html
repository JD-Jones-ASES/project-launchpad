Population Ecology Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Exponential Growth

A rapid population increase where each generation is larger than the last by a fixed proportion, leading to a J-shaped curve when resources are unlimited.

Logistic Growth

A more realistic growth pattern where initial exponential growth slows as resources become scarce, eventually stabilizing around the environment's carrying capacity, producing an S-shaped curve.

Carrying Capacity ($K$)

The maximum population size that a particular environment can sustainably support indefinitely, given its available resources.

Limiting Factors

Environmental conditions that restrict population growth, preventing it from increasing beyond a certain size. They are categorized as either density-dependent or density-independent.

Density-Dependent Factors

Limiting factors whose impact on a population intensifies as the population density increases (e.g., competition, predation, disease).

Key Concepts (2/2)

Density-Independent Factors

Limiting factors that affect a population regardless of its density (e.g., natural disasters, severe weather, human habitat destruction).

Predator-Prey Dynamics

The cyclical fluctuations in the population sizes of predator and prey species, where changes in one population directly influence the other, often with a time lag.

Population Sampling Methods

Techniques used by ecologists to estimate population sizes when counting every individual is impractical, including quadrat sampling, mark-recapture, and transect sampling.

Population Growth Models (1/2)

Definition

Occurs when a population has unlimited resources, no predators, and no disease. Each generation grows by a fixed proportion, leading to increasingly faster growth.

Curve Shape

Produces a characteristic **J-shaped curve** on a graph, starting slow and then rising steeply.

Mathematical Model

$N_t = N_0 r^t$

Real-World Examples

Can occur for short periods when a species colonizes a new habitat, such as bacteria in fresh broth, rabbits introduced to Australia, or algal blooms.

Definition

A more common pattern where initial rapid growth slows down as resources become scarce, predators increase, or disease spreads.

Predator-Prey Dynamics

Definition

The classic ecological interaction where the population sizes of predators and their prey oscillate in a cyclical pattern.

Example: Snowshoe Hare and Canada Lynx

Key Principle

These oscillations highlight that populations within a community are interconnected through food webs, and changes in one species can have cascading effects on others.

Population Sampling Methods (1/2)

Method

A square frame (quadrat) of a known area is placed randomly at multiple locations within a habitat. All organisms within the quadrat are counted.

Calculation

The average density (organisms per unit area) from multiple quadrats is used to estimate the total population.

Suitability

Best for stationary organisms like plants or slow-moving animals.

Method

Formula (Lincoln-Petersen)

$N = (M \times C) / R$

Population Sampling Methods (2/2)

Assumptions

Assumes marked and unmarked individuals mix randomly, marks don't affect survival or fall off, and there's no significant immigration/emigration or births/deaths between samples.

Example

If 40 frogs are marked, and later 50 are captured with 8 marked, $N = (40 \times 50) / 8 = 250$ frogs.

Method

Involves walking along a straight line (transect) through a habitat and recording all organisms observed within a specified distance on either side of the line.

Purpose

Especially useful for studying how species distribution or abundance changes across an environmental gradient (e.g., moving from a forest edge into a meadow).
