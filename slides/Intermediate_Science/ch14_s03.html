The Law of Conservation of Energy

• Energy can neither be created nor destroyed.

• It can only change from one form to another (e.g., chemical to electrical, potential to kinetic).

• It can be transferred from one object to another.

• Crucially, the total energy of an isolated system remains constant. This law is a cornerstone of physics, applicable across all scales and connecting various scientific disciplines.

Life Science Connection

• During cellular respiration, not all of a glucose molecule's chemical energy becomes usable ATP; about 36-38 ATP molecules are produced, with the rest "lost" as thermal energy.

• In food chains, only about 10% of the energy from one trophic level is transferred to the next. The remaining 90% is "lost" as thermal energy through metabolic processes.

Define "mechanical energy."

Mechanical energy is the sum of kinetic energy (KE) and potential energy (PE) in a system: E = KE + PE.

Under what conditions is mechanical energy conserved?

Mechanical energy is conserved in the absence of friction and other non-conservative forces (like air resistance).

State the equation for the conservation of mechanical energy when no friction or air resistance is present.

KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f, where 'i' denotes initial and 'f' denotes final states.

What is the primary benefit of using the conservation of mechanical energy equation (KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f)?

It allows you to find speeds and heights in a system without needing to know the specific forces involved, by simply tracking energy transformations.
