Key Concepts (1/2)

Acids and Bases (Arrhenius Definition)

Acids produce hydrogen ions (H+) in water, while bases produce hydroxide ions (OH-) in water. This fundamental definition helps categorize these compounds.

Characteristic Properties

Acids typically taste sour, react with metals to produce hydrogen gas, and turn blue litmus red. Bases taste bitter, feel slippery, and turn red litmus blue. Both react in neutralization reactions.

Important Terms (1/2)

Acid

A substance that produces hydrogen ions (H+) when dissolved in water, increasing the concentration of H+ ions in solution (Arrhenius definition).

Base

A substance that produces hydroxide ions (OH-) when dissolved in water, increasing the concentration of OH- ions in solution (Arrhenius definition).

pH

A numerical scale from 0 to 14 that measures the acidity or basicity of a solution, based on the hydrogen ion concentration.

Strong Acid

An acid that completely dissociates into ions when dissolved in water.

Weak Acid

An acid that only partially dissociates into ions when dissolved in water.

Important Terms (2/2)

Strong Base

A base that completely dissociates or reacts to produce hydroxide ions when dissolved in water.

Weak Base

A base that only partially dissociates or reacts to produce hydroxide ions when dissolved in water.

Indicator

A substance that changes color depending on the pH of the solution it is in, used to determine if a solution is acidic, basic, or neutral.

Neutralization

A chemical reaction between an acid and a base that produces a salt and water, typically resulting in a solution with a pH near 7.

Salt

An ionic compound formed from the cation of a base and the anion of an acid during a neutralization reaction.

Properties of Acids

Taste

Sour (e.g., lemons, vinegar). *Note: Never taste unknown chemicals in a lab.*

Reactivity

React with active metals to produce hydrogen gas (H₂).

Litmus Paper

Turn blue litmus paper red.

Neutralization

React with bases in neutralization reactions.

Common Examples

Hydrochloric acid (HCl), sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄), nitric acid (HNO₃), acetic acid (CH₃COOH - found in vinegar), citric acid (found in citrus fruits).

Properties of Bases

Taste

Bitter (e.g., unsweetened chocolate, tonic water). *Note: Never taste unknown chemicals in a lab.*

Feel

Slippery (e.g., soap).

Litmus Paper

Turn red litmus paper blue.

Neutralization

React with acids in neutralization reactions.

Common Examples

Sodium hydroxide (NaOH - lye), potassium hydroxide (KOH), calcium hydroxide (Ca(OH)₂), ammonia (NH₃), magnesium hydroxide (Mg(OH)₂ - in milk of magnesia).

The pH Scale

Neutral

A pH of 7 is considered neutral (e.g., pure water at 25°C).

Acidic

Values below 7 indicate an acidic solution. The lower the pH, the stronger the acid (higher H+ concentration).

Basic (Alkaline)

Values above 7 indicate a basic solution. The higher the pH, the stronger the base (higher OH- concentration, lower H+ concentration).

Strong and Weak Acids and Bases

Strong Acid

A strong acid completely dissociates (breaks apart into its constituent ions) when dissolved in water. For example, every molecule of hydrochloric acid (HCl) separates into H+ and Cl- ions in solut...

Weak Acid

A weak acid only partially dissociates in water. Most of its molecules remain intact, and only a small fraction produce H+ ions. Acetic acid (CH₃COOH), found in vinegar, is a common weak acid.

Strong Base

Similar to strong acids, strong bases completely dissociate or react in water to produce OH- ions. Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) is a classic example of a strong base.

Weak Base

A weak base only partially reacts with water to produce OH- ions. Ammonia (NH₃) is a common weak base.

pH of Resulting Solution

In a complete neutralization between a strong acid and a strong base, the resulting solution will have a pH near 7 (neutral).

Real-World Applications of Neutralization

Antacids

Antacid tablets contain bases like calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) or magnesium hydroxide (Mg(OH)₂) to neutralize excess hydrochloric acid (HCl) in the stomach, providing relief from heartburn.

Stomach Acid

Your stomach maintains a highly acidic environment (pH 1-2) using HCl to aid digestion and kill bacteria. A protective mucus layer prevents the acid from digesting the stomach itself.
