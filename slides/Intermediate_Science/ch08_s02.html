Important Terms (1/2)

Alkali metal

Any element in Group 1 of the periodic table (except hydrogen), characterized by being soft, highly reactive, and having one valence electron.

Transition metal

An element found in Groups 3–12 of the periodic table, typically hard, dense, good conductors, and capable of forming ions with multiple charges.

Halogen

Any element in Group 17 of the periodic table, highly reactive nonmetals with seven valence electrons.

Noble gas

Any element in Group 18 of the periodic table, characterized by full outer electron shells and extreme unreactivity.

Metalloid

An element with properties intermediate between metals and nonmetals, found along the staircase-shaped boundary on the periodic table (e.g., boron, silicon).

Important Terms (2/2)

Valence electrons

The electrons in the outermost shell of an atom, which are involved in chemical bonding and largely determine an element's chemical properties.

Ion

An atom or molecule that has gained or lost one or more electrons, resulting in a net electrical charge.

Semiconductor

A material that conducts electricity only under certain conditions, making its conductivity controllable (e.g., silicon).

Doping

The process of intentionally introducing impurities into an intrinsic semiconductor for the purpose of modulating its electrical, optical, or structural properties.

Introduction to Element Families

Definition

An element family is a group of elements located in the same vertical column (group) of the periodic table.

Shared Properties

Members of a family share characteristic chemical behaviors because they have the same number of valence electrons (electrons in the outermost shell).

Members

Lithium (Li), Sodium (Na), Potassium (K), Rubidium (Rb), Cesium (Cs), Francium (Fr). (Hydrogen is in Group 1 but is a nonmetal and not an alkali metal).

Location

Group 2: The Alkaline Earth Metals

Members

Beryllium (Be), Magnesium (Mg), Calcium (Ca), Strontium (Sr), Barium (Ba), Radium (Ra).

Valence Electrons

Each atom has two valence electrons and typically forms a +2 ion.

Properties Compared to Alkali Metals

Examples

Magnesium burns with an intense white flame; calcium reacts slowly with water at room temperature.

Importance

Calcium compounds are essential for life, forming a major component of bones and teeth (e.g., calcium phosphate).

Groups 3–12: The Transition Metals

Location

The large block of elements in the center of the periodic table.

Familiar Examples

Iron (Fe), Copper (Cu), Silver (Ag), Gold (Au), Nickel (Ni), Zinc (Zn), Platinum (Pt).

General Properties

Group 17: The Halogens (1/2)

Members

Fluorine (F), Chlorine (Cl), Bromine (Br), Iodine (I), Astatine (At).

Valence Electrons

Each atom has seven valence electrons and needs only one more to achieve a full outer shell.

Reactivity

The **most reactive nonmetals**.

States of Matter (Room Temperature)

Other Properties

All are toxic in pure form and have strong, distinctive odors. Fluorine is extremely reactive.

Reactions

Example

Sodium and chlorine react violently to form stable sodium chloride (table salt).

Group 18: The Noble Gases

Members

Helium (He), Neon (Ne), Argon (Ar), Krypton (Kr), Xenon (Xe), Radon (Rn).

Properties

Colorless, odorless, and nearly inert (unreactive).

Stability Reason

Their outer electron shells are completely full (Helium has 2 electrons, others have 8 valence electrons). This maximum stability means they have no tendency to gain, lose, or share electrons.

Reactivity

The least reactive family. For decades, they were believed to be completely inert, but a few xenon and krypton compounds have since been synthesized (starting in 1962).

Practical Applications

Metalloids: The Border Elements

Location

Along the zigzag line separating metals from nonmetals on the periodic table.

Members

Boron (B), Silicon (Si), Germanium (Ge), Arsenic (As), Antimony (Sb), Tellurium (Te), (sometimes Polonium (Po)).

Properties

Possess properties intermediate between metals and nonmetals. They can conduct electricity to some extent (like metals) but tend to be brittle rather than malleable (like nonmetals).

Silicon

The most commercially important metalloid.

Reactivity Trends (Think Deeper)

Alkali Metals (lose electrons)

As atomic size increases down Group 1, the single valence electron is farther from the nucleus and less tightly held, making it easier to *remove*. Easier to remove means more reactive.

Halogens (gain electrons)

As atomic size increases down Group 17, the nucleus's pull on *incoming* electrons weakens. This makes it harder for the atom to *gain* an electron. Harder to gain means less reactive.
