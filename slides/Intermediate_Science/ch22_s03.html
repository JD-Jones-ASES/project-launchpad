Important Terms (1/3)

Solar system

The Sun and all the celestial objects that orbit it, including eight planets, dwarf planets, moons, asteroids, comets, and meteoroids.

Solar nebula

The vast cloud of gas and dust from which our solar system is believed to have formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago.

Terrestrial planets

The four inner planets (Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars) characterized by small size, solid rocky surfaces, and metallic cores.

Gas giants

Large outer planets (Jupiter and Saturn) primarily composed of hydrogen and helium.

Ice giants

Large outer planets (Uranus and Neptune) containing significant amounts of water, ammonia, and methane ices beneath their hydrogen-helium atmospheres.

Important Terms (2/3)

Asteroid

A small, rocky body orbiting the Sun, most commonly found in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter. They are remnants from the early solar system.

Comet

A small body made of ice, dust, and rock that orbits the Sun in a highly elongated (elliptical) path. When near the Sun, it develops a glowing coma and one or two tails.

Nebula

A large cloud of gas and dust in space; some are regions of star and planet formation, while others are remnants of dying stars.

Meteoroid

A small rocky or metallic fragment in space.

Meteor

The streak of light produced when a meteoroid enters Earth's atmosphere and burns up due to friction (a "shooting star").

Important Terms (3/3)

Meteorite

A fragment of a meteoroid that survives its passage through the atmosphere and impacts the Earth's surface.

Dwarf planet

A celestial body that orbits the Sun, is massive enough to be nearly spherical due to its own gravity, but has not "cleared the neighborhood" around its orbit of other debris.

Accretion

The process by which particles collide and stick together, gradually building up larger and larger bodies, as occurred in the early solar system.

Solar Nebula Hypothesis

The scientific model that explains the formation of the solar system from the gravitational collapse of a rotating cloud of gas and dust.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the definition of our solar system?

The Sun and all the objects that orbit it, including eight planets, dwarf planets, moons, asteroids, comets, and meteoroids.

How old is our solar system and from what did it form?

Approximately 4.6 billion years old, formed from a cloud of gas and dust called a solar nebula.

Name the four inner planets and list their common characteristics.

Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars. They are small, have solid, rocky surfaces with metallic cores, few or no moons, and thin or no atmospheres.

Which inner planet has the densest atmosphere among the terrestrial planets?

Earth.

Why does Venus have the hottest surface of any planet?

Due to an extreme greenhouse effect caused by its thick CO2 atmosphere, which traps heat far more efficiently than Earth's.

Name the four outer planets and describe their primary classifications.

Jupiter, Saturn (gas giants), Uranus, and Neptune (ice giants).

Key Concepts (2/2)

What are gas giants primarily composed of?

Hydrogen and helium.

What are ice giants primarily composed of?

Large amounts of water, ammonia, and methane ices beneath hydrogen-helium atmospheres.

Which planet is the largest in the solar system?

Jupiter.

What are Saturn's rings primarily made of?

Billions of ice and rock particles ranging in size from grains of sand to houses.

What is an asteroid, and where are most found?

A small, rocky body orbiting the Sun, mostly found in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter.

What prevents asteroids in the asteroid belt from forming a planet?

Largely Jupiter's powerful gravitational influence.
