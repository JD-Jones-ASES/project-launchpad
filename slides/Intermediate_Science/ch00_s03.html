Important Terms (1/4)

Data Analysis

The process of inspecting, cleaning, transforming, and modeling data with the goal of discovering useful information, informing conclusions, and supporting decision-making.

Independent Variable

The variable that is intentionally changed or manipulated by the experimenter; plotted on the x-axis.

Dependent Variable

The variable that is measured or observed and is expected to change in response to the independent variable; plotted on the y-axis.

Line Graph

A graph used to show how a continuous dependent variable changes as a continuous independent variable changes, often revealing trends over time or other continuous scales.

Bar Graph

A graph used when the independent variable is categorical (distinct groups) to compare values across these categories.

Important Terms (2/4)

Scatter Plot

A graph showing individual data points to determine if a correlation exists between two continuous variables without connecting the points.

Slope

A measure of the steepness of a line, indicating the rate of change of the dependent variable with respect to the independent variable ($ \frac{\text{change in y}}{\text{change in x}} $).

Mean

The arithmetic average of a data set, calculated by summing all values and dividing by the number of values.

Median

The middle value of a data set when the values are arranged in order. It is less affected by outliers than the mean.

Range

The difference between the largest and smallest values in a data set, indicating the spread or variability of the data.

Important Terms (3/4)

Trend

A general pattern or direction in a data set, such as increasing, decreasing, or showing no clear change.

Significant Figures

The digits in a measurement that are known with certainty plus one estimated digit, communicating the precision of the measurement.

Error (in science)

The unavoidable uncertainty or variability in every measurement, not a mistake.

Random Error

Error that causes measurements to scatter unpredictably around the true value, often due to uncontrollable fluctuations. It can be reduced by averaging multiple trials.

Systematic Error

Error that consistently biases all measurements in one direction (either too high or too low), often due to faulty equipment or experimental design. It cannot be reduced by averaging.

Important Terms (4/4)

Percent Error

A measure of the accuracy of an experimental value, calculated as the absolute difference between the experimental and accepted value, divided by the accepted value, multiplied by 100%.
