Important Terms (1/2)

Biodiversity

The variety of life in a particular habitat or ecosystem, measured at genetic, species, and ecosystem levels.

Genetic Diversity

The range of genetic variation within a species; crucial for a species' ability to adapt to changing conditions.

Species Diversity

The number of different species in an area (species richness) combined with their relative abundance (species evenness).

Ecosystem Diversity

The variety of different ecosystems within a particular region or landscape.

Latitudinal Diversity Gradient

The observed global pattern where species diversity generally increases from the poles towards the equator.

Important Terms (2/2)

Ecosystem Services

The benefits that humans receive from natural ecosystems, categorized as provisioning, regulating, supporting, and cultural services.

Invasive Species

A non-native species introduced to a new environment where it spreads rapidly, outcompetes native species, and often causes ecological damage due to a lack of natural predators or competitors.

Bioaccumulation

The process by which the concentration of a substance (like a pollutant) increases in organisms as it moves up through trophic levels in a food chain.

Endangered Species

A species facing a very high risk of extinction in the wild in the near future due to small population size, rapid decline, or severe threats to its habitat.

Extinction

The complete and permanent disappearance of a species from Earth.

Scientist Spotlight: Edward O. Wilson (1929–2021)

Edward O. Wilson

was a highly influential American biologist and naturalist, often called the "father of biodiversity."

Early Life & Focus

A childhood accident left him blind in one eye, leading him to focus on small creatures like insects. He became the world's leading expert on ants (a myrmecologist).

Key Contributions

Legacy

Wilson's tireless work profoundly shaped conservation science and public understanding of the importance of protecting life on Earth.
