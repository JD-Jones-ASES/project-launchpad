Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 12, Section 1

Sonnet CXXVII — The Dark Lady's Black Beauty

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Antithesis

"Fairing the foul" vs. natural beauty

Personification

Beauty given a "holy bower" and dragged into "disgrace"

Metaphor

Eyes as mourners in black garments

Irony

Darkness, once excluded, becomes the definitive image of beauty

Key Concepts (1/2)

What was the conventional view of beauty in the 'old age' according to Sonnet CXXVII?

Black (dark colouring) was not counted fair, and beauty meant fair/light complexion; dark features bore no beauty's name.

What has changed in the speaker's time regarding standards of beauty?

Black is now beauty's heir -- but only because cosmetics have corrupted natural beauty, making it impossible to distinguish genuine from artificial fairness.

What is the complaint about artificial beauty in lines 5-8?

Each hand has put on Nature's power by using cosmetics to make the foul look fair -- natural beauty is profaned and lives in disgrace because of this falseness.

What is the mistress's key physical feature in Sonnet CXXVII?

Her eyes are 'raven black' -- a dark colouring that distinguishes her from the conventional fair beauty of Petrarchan sonnets.

Why do the mistress's eyes 'seem' to mourn in lines 9-10?

They appear as mourners lamenting those who use cosmetics to create false beauty -- they seem to grieve at the corruption of natural beauty standards.
