Important Terms (1/4)

temperate

Moderate, evenly balanced; implies a philosophical ideal of temperance and proper balance of qualities.

lease

A contract granting temporary possession; used metaphorically for summer's limited duration.

complexion

In Elizabethan times, referred to both outward appearance and the blend of humors determining temperament.

eternal lines

The poem's enduring verses, which weave the beloved into permanence beyond the reach of time.

ow'st

Own'st; possessest.

Important Terms (2/4)

brag

To boast.

devouring

Consuming; destroying greedily, especially referring to Time's destructive power.

phoenix

A mythical bird that burns and rises from its ashes, symbolizing immortality and renewal. By allowing Time to destroy even the phoenix, Shakespeare elevates Time's power, making the poetic claim to...

antique pen

A pun on "antique" (ancient, old) and "antic" (grotesque, clownish); refers to the wrinkles Time inscribes on a face, contrasting it with the poet's serious pen.

beauty's pattern

A model or exemplar of beauty; the beloved is the ideal by which future generations will understand beauty.

Important Terms (3/4)

master mistress

A compound term in Sonnet 20 resisting easy categorization, indicating both male gender and the emotional role of the beloved as an object of devotion.

acquainted

Familiar; known.

hue

Color, appearance, complexion; also a pun on "Hugh" or "hew," possibly suggesting a proper name.

controlling

Dominating; having power over.

fell a-doting

To fall in doting love; to become besotted.

Important Terms (4/4)

one thing to my purpose nothing

A biological feature (the penis) that makes sexual consummation between the male poet and the male youth impossible or not desired for the poet's specific form of love.

prick'd thee out

Chose or selected you; also contains a bawdy secondary meaning relating to the male sexual organ, exploited for comic effect in the couplet.

Sonnet XVIII: "Shall I compare thee to a summer’s day?"

The Blazon

Immortality Theme

Sonnet XVIII serves as a foundational statement for the recurring theme of verse immortalizing the beloved, echoed in sonnets like 55, 60, and 65.

Classical Precursors

Shakespeare draws inspiration from classical poets like Ovid, who also boasted of his work's survival, but Shakespeare makes his claim more intimate and addressed to a specific individual.

Sonnet XIX: "Devouring Time, blunt thou the lion’s paws"

Dialogue with Sonnet XVIII

Sonnet XIX is a direct companion to Sonnet XVIII. While the first sonnet sidesteps Time's power by emphasizing the poem's existence, Sonnet XIX directly challenges and defies Time.

Mythological Allusions

The sonnet draws heavily on classical mythology, particularly Ovid's *Metamorphoses*, for images of "devouring time" and the phoenix.

Extended Meditations on Time

This sonnet's confrontational stance against Time anticipates later, more elaborate meditations on Time's destructive nature in sonnets like 60 and 65.

Sonnet XX: "A woman’s face with nature’s own hand painted" (1/2)

Feminine Beauty

"A woman's face with nature's own hand painted," "An eye more bright than theirs."

Masculine Constancy

"A woman's gentle heart, but not acquainted / With shifting change, as is false women's fashion." The beloved is praised for lacking the perceived inconstancy attributed to women.

Magnetic Charm

"A man in hue all hues in his controlling, / Which steals men's eyes and women's souls amazeth." The beloved's captivating appearance (hue/Hugh) mesmerizes both men and women.
