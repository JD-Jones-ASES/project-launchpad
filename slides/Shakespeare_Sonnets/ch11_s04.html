Key Concepts (1/2)

What does the speaker use in Sonnet CXX to justify his own past transgression?

The beloved's past unkindness to the speaker -- he argues that since the beloved once hurt him, the beloved should now show more understanding.

What does 'That you were once unkind befriends me now' mean?

The beloved's previous cruelty to the speaker now serves as leverage -- the speaker uses it to minimize guilt for his own current wrongdoing.

What does the speaker confess about his own behaviour?

He was a 'tyrant' who failed to remember how deeply he once suffered from the beloved's unkindness, and therefore did not quickly offer the humble salve of a...

What is 'the humble salve, which wounded bosoms fits' (line 12)?

A gentle apology or tender acknowledgment of hurt -- the specific remedy that fits the wound of emotional betrayal.

What does the couplet mean by 'thy trespass now becomes a fee; / Mine ransoms yours, and yours must ransom me'?

Each person's past transgression serves as a ransom payment for the other's current fault -- mutual guilt creates a kind of moral balance sheet.

What is the ethical logic of Sonnet CXX?

Mutual wrongdoing produces mutual obligation -- neither party can claim full moral superiority, and both must extend forgiveness.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How does this sonnet continue the 'wandering' arc of Chapter 11?

It turns from confession and return to a more equal exchange -- both parties have hurt each other, creating a symmetrical debt rather than simple remorse.

What does 'nerves were brass or hammer'd steel' suggest?

If the speaker were made of unfeeling metal, he could ignore moral obligation -- but he is human and must bow to guilt.
