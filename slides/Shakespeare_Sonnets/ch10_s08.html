Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 10, Section 8

Sonnet CVII — Study Guide

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Allusion

The "mortal moon" most likely alludes to Elizabeth I or a major historical crisis.

Symbolism

Olives represent peace; the scythe and "speechless tribes" represent Death's domain.

Antithesis

Those who are silenced by Death vs. the poet who lives in verse.

Conceit

Verse as monument, superior to "tombs of brass."

Key Concepts (1/2)

What does the speaker claim in the opening of Sonnet CVII?

Neither his own fears nor the world's prophetic anxieties can control or end his love, which was thought to be doomed.

What does 'the mortal moon hath her eclipse endur'd' likely refer to?

A topical allusion -- possibly Queen Elizabeth I's dangerous illness or death (1603), giving way to a new peaceful era under James I.

What change in the world does the speaker celebrate in lines 7-8?

Incertainties now crown themselves assured, and peace proclaims olives of endless age -- political uncertainties have resolved into confident peace.

How does the speaker defeat Death in Sonnet CVII?

Death 'subscribes' (surrenders) to the speaker because the speaker will live in his verse while Death only triumphs over 'dull and speechless tribes.'

What will preserve the beloved better than tyrants' tombs?

This sonnet ('this poor rime') -- contrasted with tyrants' crests and tombs of brass, which will all eventually crumble.

What does 'balmy time' suggest in line 9?

A healing, gentle period -- the new era of peace that follows the resolved crisis, in which love can flourish.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the poem's tone shift from octave to sestet?

The octave is anxious and uncertain; the sestet is triumphant -- fear is replaced by confidence in love's and verse's survival.

What is the central contrast of the couplet?

Tyrants' crests and brass tombs vs. this humble poem -- physical and political monuments are 'spent' (destroyed), but verse endures.
