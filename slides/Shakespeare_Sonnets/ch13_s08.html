Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 13, Section 8

Sonnet CXLIX

Study Slides

Key Poetic Devices

Rhetorical question cascade

the octave is built of interrogatives, accumulating evidence of self-betrayal

Antithesis and inversion

Petrarchan conventions (the beloved merits worship) are inverted — he worships her "defect"

"Commanded by the motion of thine eyes"

her nonverbal power over his will, reducing him to an automaton

Ironic command "hate on"

resigned permission for her to continue as she is — a defeated acceptance

Key Concepts (1/2)

What accusation does the speaker address in Sonnet CXLIX?

The mistress's claim that he does not love her -- he responds by cataloguing all the self-betrayals he commits in her service as proof of his love.

What self-betrayals does the speaker list as proof of love?

What does 'against myself with thee partake' mean?

He takes the mistress's part against his own interests -- he sides with her in disputes, even at his own expense.

What does 'all my best doth worship thy defect' mean?

All of the speaker's best qualities are devoted to serving the mistress's faults and imperfections -- the best of him slaves for the worst of her.

What are 'Commanded by the motion of thine eyes' (line 12)?

The speaker's every action is directed by the movements of her eyes -- he is entirely under the physical and nonverbal control of her gaze.
