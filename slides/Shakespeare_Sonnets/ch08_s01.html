Important Terms (1/2)

Rival Poet

The competing poet who also writes in praise of the young man, challenging the original speaker's position and influence.

Sonnet Sequence

A group of sonnets linked together by a common theme, narrative, or addressee, forming a larger poetic work.

Patron

An individual (often wealthy or of high social standing) who provides financial or other forms of support to artists, writers, or musicians in exchange for their dedication or work. In this context...

"Saucy bark"

A self-deprecating maritime metaphor used by the speaker in Sonnet 80 to describe himself as a small, impertinent, and vulnerable boat, dwarfed by the rival's grandeur.

"Tall building"

A maritime metaphor used in Sonnet 80 to describe the rival poet's impressive and formidable presence, likened to a large, stately ship dominating the seas.

Important Terms (2/2)

"Compeers by night," "affable familiar ghost"

Phrases from Sonnet 86 that refer to the rival's alleged supernatural or divine inspiration, a claim that was potentially associated with real-life candidates like George Chapman.

Climax

In literature, the point of highest tension or the turning point in a narrative, where the main conflict comes to a head. Sonnet 86 is described as the climax of the Rival Poet sequence.

Introduction to "The Rival Poet" Sequence

His own adequacy

Does he measure up to this brilliant new talent?

His poetic identity

What is his unique voice if another can express devotion to the same subject?

The nature of the bond between patron and poet

Is his relationship with the young man secure, or can it be usurped?

A spectrum of emotions

Jealousy, profound self-doubt, a sting of wounded pride, but also a grudging, genuine admiration for a gifted competitor.
