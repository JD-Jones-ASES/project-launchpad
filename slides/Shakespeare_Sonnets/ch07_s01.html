Chapter 7, Section 1: Devouring Time

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Time as the Antagonist

Throughout the sonnets in this chapter, Time is personified as a powerful, destructive force that erodes beauty, destroys structures, and brings about ruin and death.

The Power of Poetry

The sonnets ultimately present the act of writing and the resulting verse as humanity's only enduring defense against Time's destructive march, offering a path for beauty and memory to persist.

Varied Perspectives on Time

The sonnets approach the theme of time from different angles, sometimes depicting it as a mythological, external force, and other times reflecting on the speaker's personal aging and anxieties.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Pinnacle Sonnets

Sonnets 60, 64, 65, and 73 are identified as exceptionally profound and moving explorations of time, mortality, and the potential of poetry.

Important Terms (1/2)

Antagonist

The principal opponent or adversary of the main character or speaker; in this context, Time is presented as the primary force working against beauty and life.

Mortality

The state of being subject to death; the inevitability of dying.

Decay

The process of rotting or decomposition, or the deterioration or decline of something over time.

Elegiac

Relating to or characteristic of an elegy (a mournful, melancholic, or plaintive poem, especially a lament for the dead); expressing sorrow or lamentation.

Diptych

A pair of pictures on two panels, typically hinged together; here used metaphorically to describe two sonnets (64 and 65) that are closely linked in theme and argument.

Important Terms (2/2)

Sovereignty

Supreme power or authority; in this context, Time's absolute control over all things.

Couplet

The final two lines of a sonnet, typically rhyming and providing a summary, resolution, or twist to the preceding twelve lines.

Inward Reflection on Aging and Personal Mortality

Bare boughs

Suggesting the end of growth and the approach of winter.

Fading twilight

Evoking the diminishing light of day and the approach of night.

A fire consuming its own ashes

Illustrating life burning down and feeding on its own remnants, nearing extinction.

Fleeting Diversions and Underlying Shadows

Youth's Moral Character

Sonnets like 69 and 70 might pause to examine the moral integrity of the young man addressed in the sequence.

Youth as Beauty's Treasury

Sonnets 67 and 68 position the youth as nature's last true repository of genuine beauty, a precious but ultimately transient gift.

Poet's Anxieties

Sonnets 72 and 76 reveal the poet's own self-deprecating concerns about the worth and longevity of his own verse.
