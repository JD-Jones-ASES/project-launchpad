Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 10, Section 13

Sonnet CXII — Study Guide

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Metaphor

Scandal's "impression" stamped on the brow like a brand or seal.

Horticultural metaphor

"O'er-green" — love growing over faults like living vegetation over barren ground.

Allusion

The "adder's sense" alludes to the proverbial deafness of adders, as noted in Psalm 58.

Absolutism

"All the world besides methinks are dead" collapses the entire world into one person.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What does the beloved's love and pity do for the speaker in Sonnet CXII?

It fills in the impression left by vulgar scandal on the speaker's reputation, effectively erasing public shame.

What does 'you o'er-green my bad, my good allow' mean?

The beloved covers (overgrows) the speaker's failings with fresh green growth while endorsing what is good -- like new grass covering a scar.

What is 'adder's sense' (line 10)?

The adder (snake) was believed to be deaf; having an 'adder's sense' means being wilfully deaf to criticism and flattery alike.

What does the speaker say he is deaf to?

All voices except the beloved's -- critics and flatterers both, thrown into a 'profound abysm' of indifference.

What is the effect of the beloved being the speaker's 'all-the-world'?

Everyone else becomes effectively dead to the speaker -- 'all the world besides methinks are dead.'

What does 'steel'd sense' mean in line 8?

Hardened or armoured perception -- the speaker has toughened his judgment so that only the beloved can alter his sense of right and wrong.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the theme of social isolation in Sonnet CXII?

The speaker's absolute absorption in the beloved produces radical indifference to all outside opinion -- the beloved is the only moral authority.

What danger does the extreme devotion of Sonnet CXII suggest?

Complete loss of independent moral judgment -- by silencing all voices except one, the speaker becomes entirely dependent on the beloved's approval.
