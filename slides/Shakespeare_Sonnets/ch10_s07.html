Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 10, Section 7

Sonnet CVI — Study Guide

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Allusion

References to chronicles and ancient verse locate the beloved within a long literary tradition.

Blazon

The catalogue "hand, of foot, of lip, of eye, of brow" mimics the Renaissance blazon convention.

Conceit

The reinterpretation of ancient praise as prophecy is an extended, witty argument.

Paradox (couplet)

Eyes wonder but tongues fail — the paradox of inexpressible beauty.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What does the speaker find when reading old poetry in Sonnet CVI?

Descriptions of beautiful people (ladies and knights) that he recognizes as unconscious prophecies of his beloved's present beauty.

What is 'the chronicle of wasted time' in Sonnet CVI?

The literary record of the past -- old poems and histories that recorded earlier beauties, now seen as prefigurations of the beloved.

What is a 'blazon' in line 5?

A conventional poetic form cataloguing a person's physical beauties piece by piece (hand, foot, lip, eye, brow) -- common in Elizabethan love poetry.

What does the speaker argue about ancient poets' praise?

They wrote prophetically about beauties that prefigure the beloved, but their skill was insufficient to fully praise what they could only dimly foresee.

What is the paradox of the couplet?

The speaker's own contemporaries have eyes to wonder at the beloved but lack tongues (words) to praise him adequately.

What does 'divining eyes' mean in line 11?

Prophetic or far-seeing vision -- ancient poets saw future beauty coming but could not name or fully describe what they foresaw.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How does Sonnet CVI use the theme of Time differently from other sonnets?

It reverses the usual fear of Time destroying beauty by showing Time producing the greatest beauty in the present -- history was building toward the beloved.

What literary device is used in 'beauty making beautiful old rime'?

Polyptoton -- repetition of the root word in different forms ('beauty' and 'beautiful') -- along with alliteration.
