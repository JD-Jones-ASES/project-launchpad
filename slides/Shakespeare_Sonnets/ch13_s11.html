Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 13, Section 11

Sonnet CLII

Study Slides

Key Poetic Devices

Forensic rhetoric

the accumulation of counts (her two oaths, his twenty) gives the sonnet a quasi-legal structure of indictment

Self-reversing argument

the initial accusation against her is turned against himself with greater force

"Gave eyes to blindness"

oxymoron — using the capacity to see in order to cultivate blindness, a willful self-deception

Oath/perjury language

"forsworn," "oaths," "faith," "sworn" — the poem is saturated with the vocabulary of broken sacred vows

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the speaker's central confession in Sonnet CLII?

He is forsworn (he has broken oaths) in loving the mistress -- but she is twice forsworn: she has broken her bed-vow (marriage oath) and then sworn new love ...

What does 'In act thy bed-vow broke, and new faith torn' mean?

The mistress has broken her marriage vows through their affair, and then also torn new vows of love she swore to the speaker.

What does the speaker accuse himself of in line 6?

Being 'perjur'd most' -- he has broken twenty oaths for every two of hers; all his vows were ultimately instruments of her misuse.

What does 'all my vows are oaths but to misuse thee' mean?

What has the speaker sworn in deep oaths (lines 9-10)?

Her deep kindness, her love, her truth, her constancy -- all qualities she does not actually possess, as he has now come to understand.

What does 'to enlighten thee, gave eyes to blindness' mean?

To make her seem bright (enlighten = make bright), he deliberately blinded himself -- he gave his eyes over to blindness voluntarily.
