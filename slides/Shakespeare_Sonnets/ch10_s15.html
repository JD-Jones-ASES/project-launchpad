Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 10, Section 15

Sonnet CXIV — Study Guide

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Conceit

The eye as a court taster who prepares a flattering cup for a king.

Metaphor

Alchemy represents love's power to transmute perception; the monarch's plague represents self-serving delusion.

Irony

"My great mind most kingly" — celebrating a form of self-deception with mock-regal language.

Dilemma structure

The poem is built as a formal either/or argument before the speaker picks his answer.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What two alternatives does the speaker pose at the opening of Sonnet CXIV?

Either his mind drinks up flattery like a monarch's plague, or the beloved's love has genuinely taught his eye the alchemy of making bad things beautiful.

What is the 'monarch's plague' in line 2?

Flattery -- kings are particularly susceptible to being told what they wish to hear; the speaker wonders if his perception is merely self-flattering.

What is the 'alchemy' the speaker's love has supposedly taught his eye?

The power to transform monsters and ugly things into cherubins (angelic beauties) resembling the beloved.

Which option does the speaker finally accept in line 9?

The first -- it is flattery in his seeing; his mind kingly drinks up its own pleasing illusions rather than seeing truth.

What does the couplet suggest about knowingly drinking poison?

That if the flattery is poison, the lesser sin is that the eye loves it and freely chooses to drink it first -- wilful self-deception preferred to truth.

What is the relationship between Sonnet CXIV and Sonnet CXIII?

Both explore distortion of perception by love -- CXIII shows the eye seeing only the beloved; CXIV examines whether this is genuine alchemy or mere flattery.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the metaphor of 'gust' and 'palate' in lines 11-12?

The eye is compared to a taster preparing a cup for its master's palate -- the mind sends out the eye to find what it already knows it will enjoy.

What does the sonnet reveal about the speaker's self-awareness?

He knows his perception is distorted but chooses distortion willingly -- love involves deliberate self-deception.
