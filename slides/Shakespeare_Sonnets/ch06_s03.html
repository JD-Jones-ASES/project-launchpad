Important Terms (1/5)

trifles

Something of little value. The speaker's worldly possessions are "trifles" compared to the beloved, making the beloved ironically impossible to secure by ordinary means.

truest bars

The most reliable or secure locks. "Truest" implies bars that cannot be deceived or corrupted.

vulgar thief

An ordinary, common thief. The term "vulgar" (from Latin *vulgus*, the common people) suggests that the world at large is a potential threat to the beloved's safety.

gentle closure

A soft or mild enclosure. The speaker imagines his breast (heart) as a gentle, open vault, contrasting with the secure chests for his possessions.

truth proves thievish

A paradox suggesting that fidelity, virtue, or honesty itself can become a kind of thief. The beloved's genuine worth is so overwhelming that even virtuous things are drawn irresistibly toward him.

Important Terms (2/5)

audit

A formal accounting or balancing of books. The beloved is imagined as eventually calling for a reckoning on the relationship to assess its worth.

advis'd respects

Considered, deliberate judgments. "Advis'd" suggests careful, rational reasoning rather than impulse, implying the beloved's departure would be well-thought-out.

strangely pass

To walk by as a stranger. "Strangely" means "in the manner of a stranger," indicating the beloved treating the speaker as an unknown person.

settled gravity

A composed, serious demeanor. The beloved's love, once converted from passion to reasoned seriousness, will have logical grounds for its departure.

ensconce me here

To shelter or fortify oneself in this position. The speaker takes up a defensive stance within the knowledge of his own unworthiness.

Important Terms (3/5)

mine own desert

What I deserve; the speaker's acknowledged merit or lack thereof. He knows he deserves to be left.

heavy

Both "sorrowful" and "physically weighted." The speaker's grief makes him a literal and emotional burden on his horse.

plods dully

Moves slowly and without energy. "Dully" captures both the horse's lack of spirit and the speaker's own numbness from grief.

instinct

Innate impulse; natural prompting. The horse is imagined as sensing its rider's unhappiness without being told, deepening the pathos.

the bloody spur

A pointed device to urge a horse forward, used with such force as to draw blood. It suggests the speaker's desperation and anger, yet it fails to move the horse meaningfully.

Important Terms (4/5)

my grief lies onward, my joy behind

The sonnet's governing reversal. "Onward" is the direction of travel away from the beloved, while "behind" is where joy and the beloved have been left.

dull bearer

The slow-moving horse or carrier. "Bearer" suggests not just a riding animal but something that carries the burden of the speaker's grief and separation.

posting

Travelling at speed by relay horses ("post-horses"). A post-horse journey was the fastest available means of land travel in Shakespeare's time.

swift extremity

The utmost of swiftness. Even the greatest conceivable speed will feel too slow on the return journey to the beloved.

neigh no dull flesh

Desire will not summon or call for any slow-footed animal. The verb "neigh" is brilliantly unexpected, treating desire itself as an impatient, spirited horse.

Important Terms (5/5)

jade

A worn-out, disreputable horse; a contemptible person. The speaker affectionately dismisses his horse as unnecessary for love's true speed.

wilful-slow

Deliberately slow. The horse went slowly on purpose because its rider willed it so, not wanting to leave the beloved.

blessed key

The key to open a treasure chest, symbolizing access to something precious.

sweet up-locked treasure

Something of great value kept securely locked away.

blunting the fine point of seldom pleasure

Making the sharp, intense joy of infrequent experience dull or common through constant access.
