Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 7, Section 8

Sonnet LXVI

Study Slides

Key Themes

Social and moral corruption

Time's destruction is not only physical — it extends to the moral fabric of society.

Love as the only reason to live

The couplet focuses all the sonnet's weight onto a single point: the beloved.

Weariness and existential despair

The accumulation of injustice produces a tone of total exhaustion.

Key Literary Devices

Anaphora

Repeated "And" builds an overwhelming catalog of grievances.

Chiasmus (embedded)

"Captive good attending captain ill" — goodness and evil are reversed in their proper social hierarchy.

Volta

The couplet's "Save that" creates the turn from death-wish to its single negation: love.

Vocabulary

Desert

Worthiness, merit

Forsworn

Broken by oath, perjured

Strumpeted

Treated like a prostitute; degraded

Niggard

Miserly, stingy

Simplicity

Used pejoratively here: mere plainness, stupidity

'Save that, to die, I leave my love alone.' The only reason to keep living in this unjust world is the beloved — death would mean abandoning him.
