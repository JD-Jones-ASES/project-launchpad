Procreation as a "Mightier Way" Against Time

Sonnet 16 presents having children as a more effective and "blessed" method to combat the destructive power of time compared to the speaker's "barren rhyme."

Poetic Credibility in Sonnet 17

The speaker expresses anxiety that if he were to truly capture the Youth's exceptional beauty in verse, future generations would dismiss it as exaggeration, "a poet’s rage," or "stretched metre."

The Child as Validator of Poetic Truth

Sonnet 17 proposes a resolution: a living child of the Youth would provide tangible proof of the beauty described in the poem, ensuring that the verse is believed and thus the Youth lives "twice."

(Sonnet 17) Exaggerated or overwrought verse; poetry that has been unnaturally extended or distorted to accommodate excessive praise, implying a lack of genuine truth.

(Sonnet 17) Old-fashioned or outdated poetry; a poem from an earlier, perhaps less reliable or credible era. Future readers might dismiss the sonnets as outmoded and untruthful.
