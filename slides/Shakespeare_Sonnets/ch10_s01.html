Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 10, Section 1

Sonnet C — Study Guide

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Apostrophe

The entire poem addresses the Muse as if she were a negligent servant.

Personification

Time appears as a harvester armed with "scythe and crooked knife."

Imagery

"Wrinkle graven" evokes Time as a stone-carver slowly defacing beauty.

Imperative mood

A series of commands — "Return," "Sing," "Rise," "survey," "Give" — builds urgency.

Key Concepts (1/2)

Who is the main addressee of Sonnet C?

The poet's Muse, accused of forgetting its duty and wasting effort on worthless songs.

What does 'resty Muse' mean?

'Resty' means sluggish or lazily idle -- the Muse has been neglectful and needs to rouse itself.

What task does the speaker assign the Muse regarding the beloved's face?

To survey it for wrinkles carved by Time, and if any are found, to write sharp satirical verse against decay.

What is the central command of the couplet?

Give the beloved fame faster than Time destroys life, using poetry to outrun Time's scythe and crooked knife.

What literary device dominates Sonnet C?

Apostrophe -- the speaker directly addresses his absent, personified Muse throughout the poem.

What does 'gentle numbers' mean in line 6?

Smooth, graceful verse; 'numbers' is an Elizabethan term for metrical lines or poems.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How does Shakespeare personify Time in the couplet?

As a harvesting destroyer armed with a scythe and a crooked knife -- cutting down life and beauty.

What is the main theme of Sonnet C?

The poet's duty to use verse to immortalize the beloved and defend beauty against the ravages of Time.
