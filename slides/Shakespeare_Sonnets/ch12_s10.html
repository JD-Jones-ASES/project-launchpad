Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 12, Section 10

Sonnet CXXXVI — Nothing, Something, and the Name Will

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Extended pun

Will throughout as name/desire/appetite

Paradox

nothing held sweetly becomes something

Argument from identity

name = concept = person

Comic self-effacement

I am nothing — so include me at no cost

Key Concepts (1/2)

What does the speaker ask the mistress's soul to do in Sonnet CXXXVI?

Swear to her blind soul that the speaker was her 'Will' -- he wants her conscience to accept him using the name/word 'Will' as both identity and justification.

What is 'thy blind soul' (line 2)?

The mistress's conscience or inner self, blind because it refuses to see the truth -- the speaker exploits this blindness to claim access.

What does 'Among a number one is reckon'd none' mean (line 8)?

In a large collection, one more makes no difference -- the speaker argues he should be counted for nothing (and therefore admitted without scrutiny).

What paradox does the speaker use to gain entrance?

He asks to pass untold (uncounted) in her store -- to be nothing so that she holds that nothing as a something sweet to her.

What is the request of the couplet?

'Make but my name thy love, and love that still' -- if she loves the name Will, she is loving him; the name becomes the loophole for acceptance.

What does the speaker's strategy reveal about his desperation?
