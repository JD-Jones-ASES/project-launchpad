Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 12, Section 8

Sonnet CXXXIV — The Usurer of Hearts

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Extended financial metaphor

mortgage, forfeit, surety, usurer, statute, bond, pays

Irony

the friend's kindness leads to his deepest captivity

Antithesis

"he pays the whole, and yet am I not free"

Legal vocabulary

statute, bond, surety, use (interest)

Key Concepts (1/2)

What does the speaker confess in the opening of Sonnet CXXXIV?

He has admitted that his friend (the Fair Youth) now belongs to the mistress, and that he himself is 'mortgaged to [her] will.'

What bargain does the speaker propose in lines 3-4?

He will forfeit himself (surrender himself entirely) if the mistress will restore his friend -- he offers himself as full payment to free the other.

Why does the bargain fail?

The mistress will not release the friend because she is 'covetous' -- she wants both men; the friend, being kind-natured, became entangled by vouching (stand...

What does 'he learn'd but surety-like to write for me' mean?

The friend acted as surety (guarantor) for the speaker -- he tried to help and ended up as bound by the mistress as the speaker is.

What is the 'statute of thy beauty' (line 9)?

The legal claim the mistress has over them -- her beauty is like a statute (legal document) that entitles her to collect on debts.

What does 'thou usurer, that putt'st forth all to use' mean?

The mistress is a moneylender (usurer) who lends out everything for interest -- she exploits both men for her own benefit.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the final loss acknowledged in the couplet?

Him (the friend) have I lost; thou (mistress) hast both him and me: the friend pays the whole debt and yet the speaker is still not free.

What financial metaphors dominate Sonnet CXXXIV?

Mortgage, forfeit, surety, statute, usurer, use (interest), debtor -- love is framed as a system of binding legal and financial obligations.
