Chapter 12, Section 12

Sonnet CXXXVIII — Mutual Flattery of Lies

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Pun

"lie" (deceive / lie together sexually)

Dramatic irony

both parties know they are deceiving each other

Conceit

love's best habit as a costume of seeming trust

Antithesis

"she is unjust" vs. "I am old" — the two suppressed truths

Key Concepts (1/2)

What mutual deception do the speaker and mistress practise in Sonnet CXXXVIII?

She swears she is truthful; he pretends to believe her; she pretends to think him young; he pretends not to know she is false -- both sustain a comfortable lie.

What does the speaker believe about the mistress's declarations of truth?

He knows she lies but chooses to believe her anyway, so she might think him a naive youth unlearned in the world's false subtleties.

What is 'simply I credit her false-speaking tongue' (line 7)?

The speaker straightforwardly (simply) gives credit to her lies -- he acts simple/naive as a performance to maintain the fiction of mutual trust.

What is suppressed on 'both sides' (line 8)?

Simple truth -- neither party speaks honestly; both prefer the comfortable illusion to the painful truth.

Why doesn't the mistress say she is unjust, and why doesn't the speaker say he is old?

Because love's best habit is in seeming trust, and age in love does not wish its years told -- mutual flattery is more comfortable than truth.

What is the double meaning of 'I lie with her, and she with me' in the couplet?

Both sexual ('lie with' = sleep with) and verbal ('lie' = tell falsehoods) -- in our faults by lies we are flattered, in both senses.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What does 'love's best habit is in seeming trust' mean?

The best costume (habit) love wears is the appearance of mutual trust -- love sustains itself on appearances rather than reality.
