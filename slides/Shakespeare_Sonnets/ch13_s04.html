Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 13, Section 4

Sonnet CXLV

Study Slides

Key Poetic Devices

Iambic tetrameter

CXLV is metrically anomalous — the only sonnet in the sequence written in tetrameter (four feet per line) rather than pentameter, giving it a lighter, song-like rhythm

"Hate away" / Hathaway pun

many scholars read a pun on Anne Hathaway's surname, suggesting this may be an early poem repurposed for the sequence

Day/night simile

"as gentle day / Doth follow night" maps the amended phrase onto a cosmic renewal

"Gentle doom"

Elizabethan "doom" meaning judgment, capturing her tongue's habitual kindness

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is structurally unusual about Sonnet CXLV?

It is written in iambic tetrameter (four beats per line) rather than the standard iambic pentameter (five beats) of all other sonnets in the sequence.

What did the mistress say that made the speaker languish?

She breathed the sound 'I hate' -- a devastating declaration that caused him to suffer.

What changed the mistress's words in lines 5-8?

Seeing the speaker's woeful state, mercy came straight into her heart, chiding her tongue for having spoken harshly when it was used to giving gentle judgments.

How does the mistress transform 'I hate' into something else?

She alters it with an ending -- turning 'I hate' into 'I hate [you] not you' by appending 'not you,' which follows the hate like gentle day following night.

What simile describes how 'not you' follows 'I hate'?

As gentle day follows night, which like a fiend has flown from heaven to hell -- the darkness of 'I hate' is chased away by the light of 'not you.'

What wordplay has been proposed for line 13 ('I hate, from hate away she threw')?

Scholars have suggested 'hate away' may be a play on 'Hathaway' -- Anne Hathaway, Shakespeare's wife -- making this a very personal autobiographical sonnet.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What does 'And sav'd my life, saying not you' mean?

The completion of the phrase 'I hate... not you' literally saves the speaker's life -- the addition of 'not you' transforms mortal wound into relief and rest...

What is the emotional arc of Sonnet CXLV?

Despair at 'I hate' -- suffering -- mercy entering the mistress's heart -- transformation of the phrase -- rescue by the addition of 'not you.'
