Important Terms (1/4)

forsworn

Having broken a solemn oath; perjured. Carries significant legal and religious weight, indicating a breach of faith before God.

bed-vow

The marriage vow; specifically, the oath of fidelity made in the context of the marriage bed. Breaking it signifies adultery.

perjured

Guilty of perjury; having given false testimony under oath. This word recurs to emphasize the speaker's profound self-indictment.

gave eyes to blindness

A metaphor meaning willingly blinded oneself; surrendered one's capacity for clear sight in order to maintain a desired (but false) perception.

enlighten thee

To illuminate her; to make her appear radiant or virtuous. The speaker tried to shed light on the "darkness" of the Dark Lady.

Important Terms (2/4)

brand (Cupid's brand)

A burning torch or firebrand, Cupid's traditional weapon used to ignite love in hearts.

Dian (Diana)

The Roman goddess of the hunt, the moon, and chastity. Her "votaries" or followers were sworn to virginity.

dateless lively heat

A warmth that endures without end; perpetual heat, meaning without fixed term or expiration.

seething bath

A hot spring or thermal pool, "seething" meaning boiling or bubbling, often associated with healing properties.

distemper'd

Disordered; out of proper balance; sick. Relates to the humoral medical vocabulary of the time.

Important Terms (3/4)

Love-god

Cupid, the Roman god of love (or Eros in Greek mythology), typically depicted as a winged boy with a torch and bow.

votary

A devoted follower; one who has taken a vow, often religious or quasi-religious.

general of hot desire

A military metaphor for Cupid, casting him as the commander-in-chief of erotic longing.

quenched

Extinguished; put out; cooled. Refers to the action of plunging Cupid's torch into water.

thrall

A slave; a person held in servitude; one entirely subject to another's will. The speaker describes himself as enslaved to his mistress.

Important Terms (4/4)

Love's fire heats water, water cools not love

The final aphorism of the sequence, an elegant chiasmus that states love transforms its environment but is itself untransformable.

Moral Climax

Context: The Language of Oaths and Perjury

Elizabethan Significance

In Shakespeare's era, oaths carried immense legal and spiritual weight. Breaking an oath was a sin against God and could be a crime punishable by law.

Framing Love's Failure

The speaker uses this intense vocabulary to frame his romantic failures as serious transgressions, not mere romantic follies.

Auditor's Report

The counting of oaths ("twenty" vs. "two") evokes an audit, where the speaker's moral debt is revealed to be far greater than he had imagined.

Sonnet 153: Cupid, Diana's Maid, and the Incurable Fever (1/2)

Anacreontic Epigram

This sonnet is the first of two adaptations of a classical Greek epigram, marking a shift in tone and style.

The Myth

Cupid falls asleep, leaving his "brand" (love-torch) unattended. A virgin "maid of Dian" (Diana, goddess of chastity) finds it and plunges it into a cold fountain.

Transformation

The fountain absorbs love's fire, becoming a "seething bath" – a hot spring with "dateless lively heat," renowned for its healing properties against "strange maladies."

Speaker's Plight

The speaker, "sick withal" (sick with love's fever) and "a sad distemper'd guest," seeks this miraculous cure.

Failure of Cure

Sonnet 153: Cupid, Diana's Maid, and the Incurable Fever (2/2)

Theme

Love's fever is intractable; external remedies derived from love's power cannot cure the love-sickness originating from its ultimate source.

Context: The Anacreontic Source

Greek Origin

Sonnets 153 and 154 are based on an epigram from the *Anacreontea*, a collection of poems in the style of the ancient Greek poet Anacreon.

Renaissance Knowledge

The story was known in Renaissance Europe through translations.

Debate over Significance

Scholars debate whether this coda is a graceful return to classical allusion, an editorial placement, or a functional attempt to mythologize the speaker's personal suffering.
