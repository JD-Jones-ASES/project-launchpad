Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 12, Section 13

Sonnet CXXXIX — Wound Me Directly

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Antithesis

eye vs. tongue; cunning vs. might; art vs. power

Paradox

asking for more complete wounding rather than less

Generous rationalization

turning her cruelty into concern (lines 11-14)

Hyperbole

kill me outright — end the slow torture

Key Concepts (1/2)

What specific behaviour of the mistress does the speaker object to in Sonnet CXXXIX?

Her wandering eye -- she glances sideways at other men while in the speaker's presence, wounding him with the evidence of her desire for others.

What does 'call not me to justify the wrong / That thy unkindness lays upon my heart' mean?

Don't force me to explain or defend your cruelty -- the speaker does not want to have to articulate the depth of the hurt because doing so would be unbearable.

What does 'wound me not with thine eye, but with thy tongue' mean?

Use direct power (words) to tell him the truth of her feelings, rather than the artful, silent cruelty of sideways glances at other men.

What is the contrast between 'power with power' and 'slay me not by art'?

Blunt, honest force is preferable to cunning, artistic cruelty -- direct rejection is more humane than the subtle torture of her wandering eye.

What rationalization does the speaker offer for the mistress's glancing away?

Her pretty looks are his enemies -- she turns them away from him to protect him, so they dart their injuries elsewhere rather than destroying him.

Why does the speaker reject his own rationalization in the couplet?

He realizes he is near slain anyway -- so she might as well kill him outright with her looks and end his pain rather than prolonging it with indirection.
