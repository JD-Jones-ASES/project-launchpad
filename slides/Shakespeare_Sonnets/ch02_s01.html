Key Concepts (1/2)

What are the three defining features shared by every sonnet regardless of nationality or century?

Fourteen lines, a fixed rhyme scheme, and a meter built on iambic pentameter.

What is an iamb, and how many iambs are in a line of iambic pentameter?

An iamb is an unstressed syllable followed by a stressed one (da-DUM). Pentameter means five iambs per line, giving ten syllables in a standard line.

Why did English poets modify the Petrarchan form?

English has fewer rhymes than Italian, making the interlocked ABBA octave feel strained. English poets adapted the form to suit their less rhyme-rich language.

What is the rhyme scheme of the Shakespearean (English) sonnet?

Three quatrains of four lines each (ABAB CDCD EFEF) followed by a closing couplet (GG) — requiring only seven distinct rhyme sounds.

What is the Spenserian sonnet variation, and how does it differ from the Shakespearean form?

It threads quatrains together with interlocking rhymes (ABAB BCBC CDCD GG), creating a sense of continuous flow rather than three separate blocks.

Why is learning to find the volta described as 'the single most useful skill a sonnet reader can develop'?

Because the volta is where the poem does its deepest thinking — finding it reveals the poem's architecture: setup before, response after.
