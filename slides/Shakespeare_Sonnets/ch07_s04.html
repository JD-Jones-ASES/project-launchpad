Key Concepts (2/2)

Societal Judgment

A recurring concern about how the "wise world" (society) perceives and judges individuals, particularly how it might react to the beloved's mourning for a man deemed unworthy.

Important Terms (1/4)

Mend (Sonnet 69)

To improve or repair something deficient; implies that the beloved's outward parts need no improvement even in the most critical imagination.

Confound (Sonnet 69)

To ruin, destroy, or mix in confusion; here, meaning to negate or demolish praise given in one context by criticism in another.

Churls (Sonnet 69)

Rude, rough-mannered people; peasants. Shakespeare notes that even such individuals can accurately judge inner character.

Rank (Sonnet 69)

Offensive in smell; overgrown; coarse. Used metaphorically to describe the unpleasant moral character of the beloved, contrasted with his "fair flower."

Common grow (Sonnet 69)

To become common; to lower oneself through ordinary, base, or promiscuous behavior, losing what is special about oneself.

Important Terms (2/4)

Suspect (Sonnet 70)

Suspicion; a shadow of doubt. Paradoxically presented as a natural accessory or consequence of genuine beauty.

Canker vice (Sonnet 70)

Corrosive, consuming evil; a disease that destroys plants or living tissue. Used metaphorically for corruption attacking valuable moral qualities.

Prime (Sonnet 70)

The beginning; the best time; springtime; a period of greatest vigor. Refers to the youth's uncorrupted and excellent early life.

Ambush of young days (Sonnet 70)

The dangers and temptations inherent in youth, conceived as hidden assailants.

Enlarg'd (Sonnet 70)

Set free; released from constraint. Describes envy's persistent ability to escape and return.

Important Terms (3/4)

Mourn (Sonnet 71)

To grieve; to feel sorrow for.

Surly sullen bell (Sonnet 71)

The death-knell; the bell tolled to announce a death, personified as having a bitter, brooding character.

Vile world (Sonnet 71)

A debased, morally worthless, or despicable world, often seen as a source of injustice.

Compounded am with clay (Sonnet 71)

Dead and decomposed; mixed with and become part of the earth.

Rehearse (Sonnet 71)

To repeat; to go over; to recite. The poet asks for absolute silence regarding his name.

Important Terms (4/4)

Virtuous lie (Sonnet 72)

A falsehood told for a good moral purpose; a kind deception. The poet suggests the beloved invent such a lie to protect his reputation.

Niggard truth (Sonnet 72)

Truth that is stingy or reluctant in giving praise; an honest verdict that finds nothing worthy.

Untrue (Sonnet 72)

False; dishonest. Plays on both speaking inaccurately about someone and speaking about an untrue (dishonest/unworthy) person.

Detailed Notes (1/5)

Outward Perfection

The sonnet opens by acknowledging the beloved's flawless outward appearance, which is admired by everyone, including his enemies. His physical beauty is beyond improvement.

Inner Critique

However, the "same tongues" that praise his looks also delve deeper, examining his mind and actions. Here, their judgment changes.

Moral Corruption

They add "the rank smell of weeds" to his "fair flower," symbolizing a moral corruption or base character that undermines his external beauty.

Shift in Tone

This sonnet marks a significant shift in the poet's address, moving from pure adoration to a gentle yet firm critique of the beloved's character.

Detailed Notes (2/5)

Defense of the Beloved

This sonnet serves as a defense of the youth against the criticism implied in Sonnet 69, arguing that being slandered is not a flaw but a mark of distinction.

Beauty Attracts Suspicion

The poet claims that slander always targets the beautiful and worthy. "The ornament of beauty is suspect," meaning suspicion is a natural accompaniment to genuine beauty.

Proof of Worth

If the youth remains morally "good," slander only "doth but approve / Thy worth the greater," because "canker vice" (corruption) naturally attacks the most valuable and "sweetest buds."

Overcoming Temptation

The beloved is praised for having survived "the ambush of young days," suggesting he has either avoided or triumphed over youthful temptations.

Inevitable Envy

The couplet concedes that complete praise cannot entirely suppress envy. Some "suspect of ill" will always linger; otherwise, his virtues would be so overwhelming as to capture "kingdoms of hearts."

Detailed Notes (3/5)

Plea to Be Forgotten

The poet makes an astonishingly selfless request: the beloved should mourn him only briefly, as long as the "surly sullen bell" tolls, and then forget him entirely.
