Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 10, Section 4

Sonnet CIII — Study Guide

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Paradox

The best possible subject produces the worst possible praise — because the subject overwhelms the art.

Rhetorical question

"Were it not sinful then, striving to mend, / To mar the subject that before was well?" frames the poet's restraint as ethical, not merely aesthetic.

Mirror conceit

The "glass" becomes the ultimate portrait, superior to verse.

Self-deprecation as compliment

"Blunt invention" and "poverty" are turned into tributes by being attributed to the beloved's excess of beauty.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What does the speaker lament in the opening of Sonnet CIII?

The poverty of his Muse -- his verse falls so far short of the beloved's worth that the unadorned subject surpasses any praise he can add.

What does the speaker urge the beloved to do in line 6?

Look in a mirror -- the beloved's own face exceeds anything the poet's blunt invention can describe.

What is 'blunt invention' in Sonnet CIII?

The speaker's plain, dull poetic imagination -- contrasted with the vivid, dazzling reality of the beloved's beauty.

What does 'over-goes my blunt invention quite' mean?

The beloved's beauty entirely surpasses and defeats the poet's creative capacity -- the subject outshines the art.

What would be sinful according to lines 9-10?

Striving to improve on what was already perfect and thereby marring (spoiling) it -- more verse would do damage, not justice.

What is the argument of the couplet?

The beloved's own mirror shows more than any verse can contain -- the glass is a better portrait than any poem.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How is the mirror used as a literary device here?

As a symbol of self-evident truth -- the beloved's face reflects its own worth beyond poetic embellishment.

What theme links Sonnets CIII and CI?

Both argue that the beloved's perfection exceeds the poet's ability to praise it, raising the question of whether poetry can add anything at all.
