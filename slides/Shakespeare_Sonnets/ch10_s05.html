Important Terms (1/3)

reckoning

Counting; calculation; an accounting. In Sonnet 115, "Reckoning Time" means taking Time fully into account—measuring it honestly.

million'd accidents

The countless, unpredictable events that time produces. These "creep in 'twixt vows," suggesting time's capacity to erode even the most solemn promises.

tyranny

Cruel, oppressive, arbitrary rule. Time's "tyranny" is its power to alter and destroy what was fixed.

incertainty

Uncertainty; the state of being unsure. The speaker was "certain o'er incertainty"—confident despite knowing that absolute certainty was impossible.

impediments

Obstacles; hindrances; legal bars to marriage. Drawn from the Anglican marriage service, Shakespeare's speaker refuses to admit any such barriers to the union of "true minds."

Important Terms (2/3)

alteration

A change; a modification. The central test of love in Sonnet 116 is whether it "alters when it alteration finds"—changes when circumstances change around it.

ever-fixed mark

A permanent navigational landmark; a fixed point for sailors. Love is presented as a concrete, practical, and reliable reference point.

bark

A small sailing vessel; a ship. "Every wandering bark" refers to every lost ship looking for a star to steer by, guided by love.

bending sickle

The curved blade of the grim reaper; Time's harvesting scythe. The "bending" suggests both the curve of the blade and the arc of its destructive swing.

brief hours and weeks

The short measure of mortal time. This contrasts with "the edge of doom," emphasizing love's transcendence of temporal limits.

Important Terms (3/3)

edge of doom

The last judgment; the end of the world. "Doom" retains its older meaning of a solemn decree—specifically God's final verdict at the last day.
