Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 12, Section 5

Sonnet CXXXI — Tyranny, Dark Beauty, and Moral Blackness

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Paradox

tyrannous without conventional beauty; black but fairest

Volta

couplet pivots from physical celebration to moral critique

Self-deprecating concession

"my dear doting heart" — love acknowledged as irrational

Implied syllogism

groans = witness = proof of her fairness to him

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the central paradox of Sonnet CXXXI?

The mistress is described as tyrannous (cruel) like beautiful women who make men groan, yet the speaker privately considers her the most precious jewel despi...

What do 'some say that thee behold' claim about the mistress (line 6)?

That her face has not the power to make love groan -- she lacks conventional beauty that causes men to suffer pangs of desire.

Why does the speaker dare not call those who say this wrong?

Because he can only swear it to himself -- his judgment that she is fairest is private; publicly he cannot defend it against general opinion.

How does the speaker prove his love despite others' opinion?

He produces 'a thousand groans' when thinking of her face, which witnesses that her black (dark) complexion is 'fairest in my judgement's place.'

What does 'In nothing art thou black save in thy deeds' mean (line 13)?

The mistress's only true darkness is moral -- her dark complexion is natural beauty to the speaker, but her actions (deeds) are where real moral blackness lies.

How does the couplet introduce a darker element?

The final line suggests that the slander against her (being dark and immoral) stems from her deeds -- a hint that her behaviour is genuinely troubling beyond...

Key Concepts (2/2)

What does this sonnet reveal about the speaker's emotional position?

He is caught between private devotion (she is his jewel) and public embarrassment (others do not find her beautiful or virtuous).

How does Sonnet CXXXI connect to Sonnet CXXVII?

Both deal with the Dark Lady's black beauty -- CXXVII frames it philosophically; CXXXI makes it personal and emotionally conflicted.
