Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 13, Section 3

Sonnet CXLIV

Study Slides

Key Poetic Devices

Morality play allegory

good angel / bad angel contending for a soul, drawn directly from theatrical tradition

Antithesis

fair/dark, comfort/despair, better/worser, saint/devil — the entire poem operates in binary opposition

Dark pun

"fire my good one out" alludes to venereal disease, darkening the couplet's wit

"Colour'd ill"

physical description (dark complexion) merged with moral condemnation

What does the female evil try to do in lines 5-7?

Tempt the better angel (the Fair Youth) away from the speaker's side, trying to corrupt his purity with her 'foul pride' and win the speaker quickly to hell.

What does the speaker suspect but cannot verify (lines 9-10)?

That his angel (the Fair Youth) may have been turned into a fiend by the mistress -- but he cannot directly tell whether this has happened.

What does 'being both from me, both to each friend' mean?

Both the youth and the mistress are away from the speaker and in each other's company -- suggesting they are now together.
