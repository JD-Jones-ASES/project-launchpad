Important Terms (1/4)

approveto prove; to demonstrate

To confirm or establish something by experience or evidence. In Sonnet 147, the speaker "approves" that desire is death through his own suffering.

becoming of things ill

The quality of making bad or flawed things appear attractive or fitting. In Sonnet 150, the Dark Lady has this power to transform vice into apparent virtue.

censuresjudges; evaluates critically

To make an official or critical judgment. In Sonnet 148, the speaker's judgment "censures falsely" what his eyes see correctly.

doteto be excessively and foolishly fond

To love or admire someone or something with a lack of discernment or critical judgment. In Sonnet 148, his "false eyes dote" on what may not be truly fair.

frantic-mad

In a state of wild madness; beside oneself with frenzy. Used in Sonnet 147 to describe the speaker's uncontrolled mental state after reason has abandoned him.

Important Terms (2/4)

give the lie

To contradict directly and bluntly; to accuse of lying. In Sonnet 150, the Dark Lady makes the speaker "give the lie to my true sight," denying what he plainly sees.

insufficiencyinadequacy; lack of ability

A state of lacking what is required or expected; a deficiency. In Sonnet 150, the speaker is baffled by the Dark Lady's power despite her "insufficiency."

longing still

Always craving; perpetually desirous. "Still" here means "continually." In Sonnet 147, his love is a fever "longing still" for what prolongs the disease.

lour'stfrowns; looks threateningly

To look sullen or displeased; to cast a dark or angry look. In Sonnet 149, if the Dark Lady "lour'st on me," he punishes himself.

partaketo take sides with; to join in the judgment against

To share in or participate, often by siding with someone. In Sonnet 149, he "against myself with thee partake," meaning he sides with her against his own interests.

Important Terms (3/4)

past care

Beyond the point at which anyone will care for him; also, no longer caring about anything. In Sonnet 147, the physician (reason) has given up, and the speaker himself no longer cares for his own we...

physicmedicine; medical treatment

The art of healing or medical practice. In Sonnet 147, his reason, the physician, has "prescriptions" (advice) that are not kept.

revengeto take vengeance; retaliation

To inflict punishment or injury in return for an offense. In Sonnet 149, he spends "revenge upon myself" with grief.

vexed with watching

Troubled by sleepless vigil; exhausted by keeping watch. In Sonnet 148, the speaker's eyes are physically impaired by sleeplessness and tears.

warrantise of skill

A guarantee of proficiency; an assurance of competence or expertise. In Sonnet 150, even in her "refuse of thy deeds," there seems to be a "warrantise of skill."

Important Terms (4/4)

worship thy defect

To reverence what is actually flawed or worthless; to treat the mistress's failings as virtues. In Sonnet 149, the speaker adores her imperfections.
