Important Terms (1/4)

Sovereign eye

The sun's gaze, or the Youth's, described as that of a supreme ruler, implying majesty, power, and the ability to bestow or withdraw favor.

Gilding

Covering with gold; adorning with beauty. Used of sunlight turning pale streams golden, but also suggesting the deceptive beauty of surface appearances.

Rack

A mass of wind-driven clouds; suggests clouds are seized and carried off, not just appearing.

Celestial

Of the heavens; divine. Describes the Youth's face, making his obscuration all the more devastating.

Stain

Both a mark of discoloration and a mark of moral shame. Shakespeare holds both senses simultaneously for the Youth's fault.

Important Terms (2/4)

Bravery

Finery; splendor. Here, the Youth's radiant beauty, hidden by shameful conduct.

Salve

An ointment for a wound; a remedy. Acknowledges an attempt at healing, but the speaker rejects its sufficiency.

Physic

Medicine; a remedy. Used to argue that the Youth's shame, like a doctor's grief, doesn't cure the patient.

Repent

To feel regret and turn from wrongdoing. Shakespeare distinguishes between repentance and the undoing of consequences.

Ransom

Payment to free a captive; here, reparation sufficient to cancel a debt. Transforms the Youth's tears into something of sovereign value.

Important Terms (3/4)

Loathsome canker

A caterpillar or grub that destroys rose buds from within; also a plant disease. An image combining outward beauty with inward corruption.

Trespass

A transgression; a wrong committed. Carries both legal and moral weight.

Amiss

A fault; a wrong. "Salving thy amiss" means applying a healing ointment to wrongdoing.

Sensual fault

A fault arising from physical appetite or desire. The closest the sonnet comes to naming the transgression.

Adverse party

The opposing side in a legal action; the accuser. The speaker acknowledges he should be this.

Important Terms (4/4)

Advocate

A lawyer; one who pleads a cause in court. The speaker turns his role as wronged party inside out by defending the Youth.

Accessary

An accessory to a crime; one who assists a wrongdoer without directly committing the act. The speaker's devastating self-accusation.

Solar Conceit

A common poetic convention in Elizabethan verse where a beloved or patron is compared to the sun. Shakespeare transforms this by allowing his "sun" to misbehave.

Petrarchan tradition

A poetic tradition, derived from Petrarch, where the beloved is typically a fixed point of brightness and ideal beauty. Shakespeare deviates from this.

Fair Youth sequence

The sequence of Shakespeare's sonnets (1-126) primarily addressed to a young man.

Sonnet 33: "Full many a glorious morning have I seen"

Delayed Revelation

The first eight lines establish a beautiful natural scene before the personal application. This heightens the impact of the Youth's "disgrace."

Transformed Solar Conceit

Unlike traditional Petrarchan idealization, the Youth-as-sun here actively "permits" his own obscuration, suggesting agency in his fault.

Ambiguous Forgiveness

Is it genuine forgiveness or a sorrowful rationalization? The speaker's love is unwavering, but the underlying pain is clear.

Connection

This sonnet establishes the central solar imagery that reappears throughout the chapter and sequence, often signifying the Youth's presence or absence.

Sonnet 34: "Why didst thou promise such a beauteous day"

Direct Accusation

A shift from the indirect complaint of 33, directly questioning the Youth's actions and broken promises.

Insufficient Reparation

Explores the idea that sincere remorse doesn't necessarily undo the harm caused; some losses are permanent.

Love's Triumph

Despite the strong arguments against forgiveness, the speaker's profound love for the Youth ultimately prevails in the couplet.

Connection

This sonnet introduces "legal language" ("offender," "offence's cross," "ransom") which is further developed in Sonnet 35.

Sonnet 35: "No more be griev'd at that which thou hast done"

Self-Accusation

The speaker, instead of blaming the Youth, blames himself for enabling and excusing the Youth's fault.

Extensive Legal Metaphor

This sonnet is a masterclass in using legal terms ("trespass," "amiss," "adverse party," "advocate," "plea," "accessary") to illustrate the speaker's psychological conflict.

"Civil War"

A powerful phrase to describe the internal division between love and grievance.

Complicity in Suffering

The speaker becomes an "accessary" to the harm done to him, highlighting the self-destructive power of his love.

Context: Legal Language

The pervasive use of legal terms reflects the widespread understanding of legal processes and vocabulary in Elizabethan England, enriching the sonnet's resonance.
