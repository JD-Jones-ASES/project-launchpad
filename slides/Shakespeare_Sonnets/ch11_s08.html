Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 11, Section 8

Sonnet CXXIV

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Political and legal vocabulary

"Child of state," "Fortune's bastard," "unfather'd,"

Philosophical terms

"Accident" (contingent quality, Aristotelian) vs. essence —

Metaphor of weather

Heat and showers as Fortune's extremes leave this love unmoved.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What does 'If my dear love were but the child of state' mean?

If the speaker's love were based on circumstances, fortune, or social position, it could be disowned ('unfather'd') when those circumstances change.

What is 'Fortune's bastard' (line 2)?

A child of uncertain or illegitimate origin -- love that is born of mere circumstance has no stable parentage and can be disowned by Fortune.

What does the speaker claim his love is actually built on?

It was 'builded far from accident' -- constructed on a foundation utterly removed from chance, social favour, or political circumstance.

What does 'it suffers not in smiling pomp, nor falls / Under the blow of thralled discontent' mean?

True love neither rises with flattering prosperity nor collapses under the blow of political discontent -- it is unmoved by both good and ill fortune.

What is 'policy, that heretic' (line 9)?

Self-interested political calculation, called a heretic because it operates on short-term leases -- it betrays permanent values for temporary advantage.

What does 'nor grows with heat, nor drowns with showers' mean (line 12)?

True love is not excited by warmth (flattery, prosperity) and not destroyed by rain (adversity) -- it transcends weather and circumstance.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Who are 'the fools of time' (line 13)?

Those who lived for crime and then died for goodness -- people whose lives were inconsistent, condemned by the very shifts that policy produces.

What is the central contrast of Sonnet CXXIV?

Love as child of state (circumstantial, unstable) vs. love built far from accident (autonomous, immovable) -- true love is politically and socially independent.
