Chapter 11, Section 1

Sonnet CXVII

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Legal metaphor

The vocabulary of indictment, proof, and appeal structures the entire

Nautical metaphor

"Hoisted sail to all the winds" renders the straying as deliberate

Paradox

Inviting accusation as a form of defense is rhetorically audacious — control

Key Concepts (1/2)

What does the speaker invite the beloved to do in Sonnet CXVII?

Accuse him -- of scanting (neglecting) his debt to the beloved's deserts, forgetting to call on his love, keeping company with unknown minds, and sailing away.

What does 'scanted all, wherein I should your great deserts repay' mean?

The speaker admits he has fallen far short of repaying the beloved's worth -- he has been neglectful of what love demands.

What does 'given to time your own dear-purchas'd right' mean?

The speaker has wasted the time that rightfully belonged to the beloved -- time that was dearly earned by the beloved's worth.

What is the speaker's surprising defence in the couplet?

He says he was testing the constancy and virtue of the beloved's love -- his bad behaviour was an experiment to prove the beloved's fidelity.

How sincere is the speaker's defence in Sonnet CXVII?

It is ambiguous and potentially self-serving -- the couplet turns misconduct into a virtue-testing exercise, which many readers find unconvincing.

What does 'hoisted sail to all the winds' suggest?

The speaker has been driven by every impulse and opportunity, scattering himself in all directions rather than remaining faithful.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the tone of Sonnet CXVII compared to earlier confessional sonnets?

Less remorseful -- instead of acknowledging fault fully (as in CX), the speaker attempts to reframe his behaviour as virtuous testing.

What does 'surmise, accumulate' (line 10) mean?

Pile up not just proved faults but guesses and suspicions on top of them -- the speaker gives the beloved permission to condemn him fully.
