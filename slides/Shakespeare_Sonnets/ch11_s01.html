Farewell to the Fair Youth: A Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Gradual Withdrawal and Ending

The close of the Fair Youth sequence is presented as a "gradual, rueful withdrawal" rather than an abrupt climax, marked by a series of reckonings and apologies.

Contrite but Justified Posture

The poet adopts a contrite stance, confessing neglect and "love-madness," but his apologies are often accompanied by arguments and defenses, framing guilt within the context of the bond's strength.

Legal and Commercial Metaphors

Guilt, reconciliation, and the nature of the relationship are frequently explored through metaphors of debt and repayment, using terms like "bonds," "ransoms," "adjuncts," and "audits."

Reciprocity of Pardon (Sonnet 120)

This sonnet highlights a rare instance of mutual forgiveness, where the youth's past unkindness is used to cancel the poet's own debt, creating a shared currency of pardon.

Defiance Against Slander (Sonnet 121)

One of the most defiant poems, it asserts the speaker's self-definition ("I am that I am") and rejects external judgment, turning accusations of vice back onto the accusers.

Key Concepts (2/2)

The Envoi and Formal Leave-Taking (Sonnet 126)

The sequence concludes with a unique twelve-line poem, not a strict sonnet, serving as a formal farewell (envoi) to the Fair Youth, with a poignant warning about Time's inevitable claim.

Important Terms (1/2)

Fair Youth Sequence

The collection of Shakespearean sonnets (traditionally 1-126) primarily addressed to a beautiful young man, exploring themes of love, beauty, time, and mortality.

Contrite

Feeling or expressing remorse or penitence; apologetic.

Abject

Completely without pride or dignity; self-abasing. The text notes the poet's apologies are *not* abject, implying they retain a degree of self-respect or argument.

Legal/Commercial Metaphors

Figurative language drawing from law or business (e.g., bonds, ransoms, audits) used to describe emotional or relational dynamics, such as guilt, obligation, and forgiveness.

Important Terms (2/2)

Defiant

Openly resistant; challenging authority or convention.

Envoi

A short stanza concluding a poem, used either to address an important person or to summarize the preceding text. In Sonnet 126, it serves as a formal farewell.

Audit

An official inspection of accounts or records. Here, "Nature's audit" refers to the inevitable reckoning or accounting that Time demands from all living things.

Dark Lady Sequence

The subsequent section of Shakespeare's sonnets (traditionally 127-152), addressed to a mysterious and often scornful woman.

The Poet's Contrition and Defense

Neglect (Sonnet 117)

Acknowledges periods of absence or lack of attention.

Seeking "bitter sauces" (Sonnet 118)

Suggests he pursued other, perhaps less wholesome, experiences or affections as a counterpoint to the sweetness of his love for the youth.

Love-Madness (Sonnet 119)

Admits to having been lost in an obsessive or unbalanced love before returning to a more "chastened" devotion.

Legal and Commercial Framing of Relationships

Bonds

Obligations or agreements.

Ransoms

Payments made to secure release from captivity or obligation.

Adjuncts

Something added or connected as a subordinate part.

Audits

An official examination of accounts.

The Envoi: Sonnet 126 and the Farewell

Not a Strict Sonnet

It consists of only twelve lines, structured as six rhyming couplets, lacking the characteristic fourteen lines and the final "turn" or volta of a traditional Shakespearean sonnet.

An Envoi

This twelve-line structure functions as an "envoi," a formal leave-taking or concluding address.

Address to Nature

The poem addresses Nature itself as the "sovereign mistress" of the youth. Nature is depicted as having protected her "lovely boy" from the ravages of time.

With this poignant and formal farewell, the Fair Youth "vanishes" from the sequence, signaling the transition to the "darker world of the Dark Lady" in the subsequent sonnets.
