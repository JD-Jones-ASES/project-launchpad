Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 13, Section 9

Sonnet CL

Study Slides

Key Poetic Devices

Paradox sustained throughout

insufficiency creates power; cause for hate increases love; unworthiness generates desert

"Refuse of thy deeds"

the dregs/worst of her behavior — "refuse" as waste material, not the verb

"Warrantise of skill"

a warranty of strange effectiveness even in her worst actions

"Becoming of things ill"

her ability to make ill things graceful — an aesthetic-moral oxymoron

Key Concepts (1/2)

What mystery does Sonnet CL explore?

How the mistress, with acknowledged insufficiency and even evil, has the power to make the speaker love her more -- the more cause for hate, the more he loves.

What does 'with insufficiency my heart to sway' mean?

The mistress can move or control the speaker's heart despite being insufficient (lacking in virtue and beauty) -- her inadequacy itself has a kind of pervers...

What does 'make me give the lie to my true sight' mean?

She forces him to contradict what his own eyes plainly see -- to swear that brightness does not grace the day (an obvious falsehood) just because she wills it.

What does 'in the very refuse of thy deeds / There is such strength and warrantise of skill' mean?

Even in her worst actions -- the very rubbish (refuse) of her deeds -- there is such masterful power that it surpasses all that is best in others.

What is the paradox of the octave's culmination (lines 7-8)?

In the speaker's mind, her worst exceeds everyone else's best -- a complete inversion of rational judgment, but one he cannot escape.
