Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 1, Section 3

The Sonnets in Context

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

• The 1609 Quarto was registered and published by Thomas Thorpe; Shakespeare appears to have had no public involvement

• The quarto contained 154 sonnets plus A Lover's Complaint; it was sold in St Paul's Churchyard

• The cryptic dedication to "Mr. W. H." has generated centuries of debate; its key word, "begetter," is ambiguous

• The two main candidates for Mr. W. H. are Henry Wriothesley (Earl of Southampton) and William Herbert (Earl of Pembroke)

• The sonnets divide into three major groupings: Fair Youth (1–126), Dark Lady (127–152), and Rival Poet (roughly 78–86)

• The Dark Lady sonnets are tormented and self-lacerating; the Fair Youth sonnets span a wider range of emotions

Key Concepts (2/2)

• Christopher Marlowe is the most compelling candidate for the Rival Poet

• The debate between reading the sonnets as autobiography versus literary performance is central to their interpretation

• The sonnets rose to canonical status in the Romantic period through the advocacy of Wordsworth and Keats

Key Terms (1/2)

1609 Quarto

The only collection of Shakespeare's sonnets published with any claim to authority, registered by Thomas Thorpe and printed by George Eld

A Lover's Complaint

The longer poem that followed the 154 sonnets in the 1609 Quarto

begetter

The crux of the dedication — ambiguous between "the person who inspired the sonnets" and "the person who procured the manuscript"

Fair Youth

The beautiful young man addressed in Sonnets 1–126; his identity is traditionally debated between Southampton and Pembroke

Dark Lady

The woman of dark complexion and dark hair desired in Sonnets 127–152; associated with tormented, self-lacerating emotional intensity

Key Terms (2/2)

Rival Poet

A competitor for the Fair Youth's patronage, addressed in roughly Sonnets 78–86; Christopher Marlowe is the most compelling candidate

Petrarchan blazon

The conventional form of praise that catalogued the beloved's physical features by comparison to natural phenomena; Sonnet 130 famously parodies this convention

negative capability

Keats's term for the capacity to remain in uncertainty without irritable reaching after fact — a quality he found modelled in Shakespeare's sonnets

bowdlerised

Edited to remove material considered improper; the 1640 reprint of the sonnets changed pronouns to obscure the male-to-male address
