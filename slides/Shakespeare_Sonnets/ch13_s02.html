Chapter 13, Section 2

Sonnet CXLIII

Study Slides

Key Poetic Devices

Extended simile

the entire octave is a sustained domestic allegory, unusually humble in its register for a love poem

"Will" pun

Shakespeare's signature self-referential wordplay, linking erotic desire, the speaker's name, and grammatical futurity

Bathos

comparing the speaker to a crying baby is deliberately self-deprecating and faintly comic

Conditional structure

the sestet's "if... then" logic frames desire as a negotiated exchange

Who corresponds to which figure in the simile?

The housewife = the mistress; the feathered creature = the person the mistress pursues; the neglected baby = the speaker.

What does the baby (speaker) do while the housewife (mistress) runs?

Chases her and cries to catch her -- but she is too busy pursuing her own desire to notice or care.

What does the speaker ask in lines 11-12?

If you catch what you are chasing, turn back to me and play the mother's part -- kiss me, be kind.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What does the poem suggest about the mistress's priorities?

She is absorbed in her own pursuit of desire and entirely indifferent to those who need her -- the speaker is an afterthought.

How does the domestic imagery contrast with the rest of the Dark Lady sequence?

Most of the sequence uses elevated or abstract imagery -- the farmyard/household setting of CXLIII is deliberately comic and deflating.
