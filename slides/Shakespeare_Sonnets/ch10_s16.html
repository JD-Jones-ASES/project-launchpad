Chapter 10, Section 16

Sonnet CXV — Study Guide

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Paradox

Earlier declarations of maximum love are now retrospectively false because love has grown beyond them.

Metaphor

Time as a tanner, blunter, and diverter of beauty and vows.

Simile

Love as a growing infant — never at its final stature.

Philosophical argument

The sonnet is structured as a formal logical problem about temporal certainty and love's growth.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What does the speaker confess his earlier lines have done?

They lied -- lines that claimed he could not love more deeply have been proved false because his love has since grown even stronger.

Why did the speaker write 'Now I love you best' in the past (lines 9-11)?

Because he was 'certain o'er incertainty' -- in a moment of present fullness, he crowned the present as best while doubting the future.

What does 'million'd accidents' mean in line 5?

The countless unforeseen events and changes that creep between vows and alter the course of human lives and even the decrees of kings.

What list of Time's destructive effects appears in lines 5-8?

Time tans sacred beauty, blunts sharp intentions, and diverts strong minds -- it gradually degrades everything it touches.

What is 'Love is a babe' (line 13) meant to explain?

Love is like a child still growing -- it could not have been called 'full grown' in the past because it has been developing and deepening over time.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What does 'Crowning the present' suggest?

We naturally treat the immediate moment as the absolute best, unaware that future growth is possible -- the present feels total even when incomplete.

What is the chief paradox of Sonnet CXV?

Former claims of maximum love were truthful at the time, yet false in retrospect -- love's growth makes all superlatives eventually obsolete.
