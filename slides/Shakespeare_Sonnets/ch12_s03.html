Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 12, Section 3

Sonnet CXXIX — The Anatomy of Lust

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Accumulation/Asyndeton

rapid piling of adjectives without conjunctions

Paradox

bliss in proof, woe once proved; a heaven leading to hell

Metaphor

lust as swallowed bait laid to drive the taker mad

Irony

"heaven" as the deceptive sweetness that delivers hell

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the 'expense of spirit in a waste of shame'?

Lust in action -- spending vital energy (spirit) in an act of shame; the line defines lust as a squandering of both physical and moral resources.

What adjectives does the speaker use to describe lust before it is satisfied?

Perjured, murderous, bloody, full of blame, savage, extreme, rude, cruel, not to trust.

What happens to lust the moment it is satisfied?

It is immediately despised -- 'enjoyed no sooner but despised straight'; it turns from a hunted treasure into something hated.

What is the meaning of 'past reason hunted; and no sooner had, / Past reason hated'?

Lust defies reason both in pursuit and in possession -- it is irrational before and after the act, impossible to contain or correct through logic.

What is the triple temporal structure of lines 9-11?

Mad in pursuit (before), a bliss in proof (during), and then a very woe (after) -- lust is condemned in all three phases of time.

What does 'Before, a joy propos'd; behind a dream' mean?

Before the act, lust presents itself as a promised joy; after it, the experience dissolves into a mere dream, insubstantial and disappointing.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the bitter irony of the couplet?

All this is universally known, yet no one manages to shun the heaven (apparent pleasure) that leads men to this hell -- knowledge does not prevent the fall.
