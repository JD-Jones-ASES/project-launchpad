Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the central claim of Sonnet CXXI?

'Tis better to be vile than vile esteem'd' -- it is better actually to be bad than to be judged bad when you are innocent.

What complaint does the speaker make about other people's judgment?

Others use false, adulterate (corrupt) eyes to judge his behaviour and refuse to recognize the pleasure he legitimately enjoys.

What does 'frailer spies' mean (line 7)?

Critics who are themselves more morally weak than the speaker, yet presume to spy on and judge his conduct.

What does 'No, I am that I am' echo or suggest?

It echoes God's self-declaration in Exodus ('I am that I am') -- a claim of absolute self-identity and refusal to be defined by others' opinions.

What does 'they that level / At my abuses reckon up their own' mean?

Those who aim accusations at the speaker are actually cataloguing their own faults -- their criticism reveals more about their own corruption than his.

What does 'I may be straight though they themselves be bevel' mean?

The speaker may be morally straight (upright) even though his critics are crooked (bevel = oblique, morally bent).

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the general evil cited in the final couplet?

The proposition that all men are bad and reign in their own badness -- if critics' logic holds, it condemns everyone, not just the speaker.

What is the tone of Sonnet CXXI compared to other sonnets in Chapter 11?

Defiant rather than apologetic -- the speaker refuses to accept others' moral authority and asserts his own self-definition against collective judgment.
