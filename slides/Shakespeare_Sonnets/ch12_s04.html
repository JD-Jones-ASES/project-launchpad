Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the rhetorical strategy of Sonnet CXXX?

A sustained anti-blazon -- the speaker methodically denies every conventional Petrarchan comparison, then asserts at the end that his love is genuine despite...

What conventional comparisons does the speaker explicitly reject in lines 1-8?

Eyes like the sun, lips redder than coral, breasts whiter than snow, hair like golden wire, cheeks like roses, breath sweeter than perfume, voice like music,...

What is the couplet's claim?

'And yet by heaven, I think my love as rare / As any she belied with false compare' -- his love is just as precious as any woman falsely flattered by convent...

What does 'belied with false compare' mean?

Misrepresented through dishonest comparisons -- women who are praised with impossible Petrarchan metaphors are actually lied about, not honestly described.

What does 'reeks' mean in line 8 ('the breath that from my mistress reeks')?

In Elizabethan English, 'reeks' simply means 'comes forth' or 'emanates' -- it was not necessarily offensive, though the context is deliberately deflating.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Why does Shakespeare use the phrase 'my mistress' rather than 'my love' in line 1?

How does Sonnet CXXX function as a parody of Petrarchan conventions?

It systematically inverts every cliche of the blazon tradition to expose the absurdity of stock comparisons and to celebrate real, observable love instead.
