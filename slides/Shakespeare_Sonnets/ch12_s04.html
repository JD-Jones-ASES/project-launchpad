Important Terms (1/3)

"Anchor'd in the bay where all men ride" (137)

A striking metaphor implying the Dark Lady is a woman of easy virtue, visited by many men, and the speaker's gaze is fixed there despite this knowledge.

"A several plot" / "common place" (137)

"A several plot" is privately owned land, contrasted with "the wide world's common place," a public space or common land, carrying the bawdy implication that the mistress is shared by many.

"False plague" (137)

A deadly epidemic or affliction of falsehood and deception, indicating the speaker views his infatuation as a severe, contagious, and potentially mortal threat.

"Made of truth" (138)

The Dark Lady's claim that she is entirely composed of faithfulness and absolute fidelity, which the speaker knows is a lie.

"Untutor'd youth" (138)

A young man lacking worldly experience or knowledge of deception; the role the speaker pretends to play to flatter his mistress.

Important Terms (2/3)

"Love's best habit is in seeming trust" (138)

Love's most effective guise or habitual practice is the *performance* of trust, even if the trust is not genuine. "Habit" refers to both clothing (a disguise) and customary behavior.

"I lie with her, and she with me" (138)

The powerful double entendre in the closing couplet, meaning both to tell falsehoods and to engage in sexual intimacy. Both meanings are fully intended and intertwined.

"Justify the wrong" (139)

To provide a defense, excuse, or explanation for the injury or injustice she inflicts upon him. The speaker does not want to be forced to excuse her behavior.

"Slay me not by art" (139)

Do not kill or torment me through cunning, skill, or artifice – specifically, through subtle cruelty like glancing at other men. The speaker prefers honest, direct assault.

"Tongue-tied patience" (140)

The speaker's suffering patience, which has rendered him silent and unable to express his pain directly.

Important Terms (3/3)

"Pity-wanting pain" (140)

A pain so profound and overwhelming that it is devoid of any pity or compassion, either from the mistress or from anyone witnessing his suffering.

"Ill-wresting world" (140)

A world that tends to misinterpret, distort, or twist things negatively, making it fertile ground for rumors and slander.

"Bear thine eyes straight, though thy proud heart go wide" (140)

A pragmatic plea for the Dark Lady to maintain an outward appearance of fidelity ("bear thine eyes straight") even if her heart is unfaithful ("her proud heart go wide"), to prevent public scandal.
