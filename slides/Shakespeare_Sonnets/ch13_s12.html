Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 13, Section 12

Sonnet CLIII — The Anacreontic Coda (Part 1)

Study Slides

Key Poetic Devices

Classical myth

the Anacreontic legend of Cupid's sleeping, the chaste nymph, the fire quenched — a graceful, elegant register after the intensity of CLII

Paradox of cure and cause

the bath (cure) was made by love's fire; love's fire now lives in the mistress's eye; therefore the cure is the cause

"Dateless lively heat"

the fire's immortal, perpetual quality — "dateless" meaning without end

"Sad distemper'd guest"

the speaker arrives at the spa as a melancholy, disordered patient seeking help

Key Concepts (1/2)

What myth does Sonnet CLIII retell?

A maid of Diana finds Cupid sleeping, steals his love-kindling torch (brand), and extinguishes it in a cold valley fountain, which becomes a hot healing bath.

Who are the key figures in the myth?

Cupid (Love-god), a maid of Diana (a chaste votary), and the mistress -- who re-lights Love's brand with her eye, sending the speaker to seek the bath.

What happens when Cupid's brand is quenched in the fountain?

The fountain borrows Love's holy fire and becomes a seething bath -- a sovereign cure against strange maladies, giving men healing through Love's captured heat.

What happens to the speaker personally in lines 9-12?

At his mistress's eye, Love's brand is newly fired; Love then touches the speaker's breast, making him sick; he goes to the bath for a cure but finds none.

Where does the real cure lie, according to the couplet?

'The bath for my help lies / Where Cupid got new fire; my mistress' eyes' -- the cure is in the mistress's eyes, which are the very source of the disease.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the function of the classical myth in Sonnets CLIII-CLIV?

It distances the intensely personal earlier sonnets through a mythological framework, creating a kind of elegant, literary conclusion to the sequence.
