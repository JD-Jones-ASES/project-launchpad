Important Terms (1/3)

debarre'd

Excluded or shut out. In Sonnet XXVIII, it carries a legal connotation, implying a formal, almost judicial deprivation of rest.

swart-complexion'd night

Dark-faced night. A vivid personification in Sonnet XXVIII, depicting night as a dark-skinned figure.

twire

To peep or glance furtively. In Sonnet XXVIII, it describes stars that are not peeping out due to clouds, adding an intimate touch to the night sky.

gild'st the even

To make golden or illuminate the evening. In Sonnet XXVIII, the beloved's beauty is said to provide light in the absence of stars.

bootless

Without profit; useless; in vain. In Sonnet XXIX, "bootless cries" are prayers or complaints to an unresponsive heaven.

Important Terms (2/3)

scope

Range, freedom of action, or opportunity. In Sonnet XXIX, the speaker envies others' "scope," feeling limited and deprived of possibility.

haply

By chance; perhaps; fortunately. A crucial word in Sonnet XXIX, signifying that the memory of the beloved arrives accidentally, not by conscious will.

lark

A skylark, a songbird associated with dawn, joy, and ascending flight in English poetry. In Sonnet XXIX, it symbolizes the speaker's joyful ascent from despair.

sullen

Heavy, gloomy, or slow to respond. In Sonnet XXIX, "sullen earth" refers both to the literal ground and the poet's own heaviness of spirit.

sessions

Formal sittings of a law court. In Sonnet XXX, this word establishes the central legal metaphor, portraying memory as a court where past grievances are reviewed.

Important Terms (3/3)

dateless

Endless; without a fixed term or expiry date. In Sonnet XXX, "death's dateless night" emphasizes the permanence of death.

cancell'd woe

Discharged grief; a sorrow that was supposedly settled or crossed out like a debt. In Sonnet XXX, such grief can be reopened and re-felt.

expense

Expenditure, outlay, or loss. In Sonnet XXX, it refers to the outpouring of tears and the loss of what caused them.

fore-bemoaned

Previously mourned or lamented. In Sonnet XXX, it highlights the repetitive and exhausting nature of reliving old griefs.

tell o'er

To count over or reckon up. In Sonnet XXX, it relates to the accounting metaphor, meticulously listing sorrows.
