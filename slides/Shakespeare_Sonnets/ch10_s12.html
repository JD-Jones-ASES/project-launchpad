Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 10, Section 12

Sonnet CXI — Study Guide

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Personification

Fortune is a guilty goddess who provided poorly for the speaker.

Simile

The dyer's hand is one of Shakespeare's most famous images of professional contamination.

Medical metaphor

Infection, potions, patient, and cure build a sustained conceit of moral disease and healing.

Penitential rhetoric

Echoes of religious confession and penance give the poem a devotional weight.

Key Concepts (1/2)

Who does the speaker blame for his faults in Sonnet CXI?

Fortune, 'the guilty goddess of my harmful deeds,' for not providing better circumstances than public work.

What does 'public means which public manners breeds' refer to?

The speaker's occupation in the public theatre -- his livelihood on the popular stage has exposed him to coarse public behaviour.

What is 'the dyer's hand' metaphor?

Just as a dyer's hand is permanently stained by the dye he works with, the speaker's nature has been subdued and coloured by his public occupation.

What does the speaker ask the beloved to do?

Pity him and wish he were renewed -- the speaker accepts his taint but hopes the beloved's compassion can help restore him.

What is 'eisel' (line 10) and what does drinking it signify?

Eisel is vinegar -- thought to be a purifying remedy or penance; the speaker is willing to undergo bitter self-correction.

What does 'double penance to correct correction' mean?

He is willing to accept even double punishment -- ongoing correction on top of correction -- to be cured.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What healing metaphor sustains lines 9-14?

The speaker as a willing patient -- disease of soul acquired through public life, with the beloved's pity offered as the cure.

What does the couplet claim about the beloved's pity?

That pity alone from the dear friend is sufficient to cure the speaker -- the beloved's compassion is more powerful than any medicine.
