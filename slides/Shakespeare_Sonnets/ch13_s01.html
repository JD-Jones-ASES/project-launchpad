Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 13, Section 1

Sonnet CXLII

Study Slides

Key Poetic Devices

Tu quoque argument

the speaker deflects blame by establishing her equal guilt

Legal/economic metaphor

"bonds," "revenues," "rents" frame love and fidelity as contractual obligations both parties have breached

Paradox

her virtue is grounded in the very sin she condemns

Periphrasis

"scarlet ornaments" for her lips — a classicizing circumlocution that intensifies the accusation of profanation

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the speaker's opening admission in Sonnet CXLII?

Love is his sin, and the mistress's virtue is to hate that sin -- but her hatred is grounded on her own sinful loving.

What is the tu quoque argument the speaker makes?

He tells the mistress to compare her own state to his -- she will find her conduct does not merit reproof, or if it does, not from her lips that have 'profan...

What does 'seal'd false bonds of love as oft as mine' mean?

She has made and broken oaths of love as often as the speaker has -- she is equally guilty of false swearing in love.

What does 'Robb'd others' beds' revenues of their rents' mean?

She has committed adultery -- stealing what rightfully belongs to other people's marriages (their beds' revenues).

What is the logic of 'Be it lawful I love thee, as thou lov'st those / Whom thine eyes woo as mine importune thee'?

The speaker asks for the same licence she gives others -- if she pursues others with her eyes, he should be allowed to pursue her the same way.

What does the speaker ask to grow in her heart (lines 11-12)?

Pity -- so that when it grows, her pity may deserve to be pitied (suggesting her own need for mercy from others she has wronged).

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the couplet's logic?

'If thou dost seek to have what thou dost hide, / By self-example mayst thou be denied' -- what she withholds from the speaker, she herself secretly desires;...

What moral framework does Sonnet CXLII operate within?

Reciprocity and hypocrisy -- the mistress cannot condemn in the speaker what she practises herself; mutual sinfulness demands mutual compassion.
