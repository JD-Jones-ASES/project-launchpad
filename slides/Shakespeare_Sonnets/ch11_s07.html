Key Concepts (1/2)

What does the speaker defy at the opening of Sonnet CXXIII?

Time itself -- 'No, Time, thou shalt not boast that I do change': the speaker refuses to be altered by Time's influence.

What are the 'pyramids built up with newer might' (line 2)?

New monuments or wonders of the age -- the speaker dismisses them as nothing new, merely redressed versions of things seen before.

What does 'dressings of a former sight' mean?

Mere costumes or disguises placed on old things -- what seems new in history is actually recycled from the past.

What psychological point does the speaker make about human perception of novelty?

Because our lives are brief, we marvel at what Time presents as new, preferring to think things are newly born rather than recognizing them as repeats of wha...

What does 'Thy registers and thee I both defy' mean?

The speaker defies both Time and Time's historical records -- he refuses to accept their authority or to be measured by their standards.

Why does the speaker say Time's records 'lie' (line 11)?

Time's records are distorted by Time's own 'continual haste' -- they exaggerate or diminish things, making past and present unreliable.

What is the speaker's vow in the couplet?

How does Sonnet CXXIII relate to Sonnet CXVI in its treatment of Time?

Both assert love's defiance of Time, but CXVI defines love abstractly; CXXIII makes a personal vow of constancy and dismisses Time's illusions of novelty.
