Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 10, Section 9

Sonnet CVIII — Study Guide

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Rhetorical question

The opening invites the reader to see the problem before the speaker resolves it.

Analogy

Divine prayer provides the model for constant, repeated love that never loses its meaning.

Paradox

Love's vitality is found where time indicates it should be extinct.

Religious diction

"Hallow'd," "prayers divine," and "eternal" give the love a devotional register.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What question opens Sonnet CVIII?

What remains to be written that ink has not already expressed? The speaker implies he has said everything about his true spirit.

To what does the speaker compare repeating old words of love?

To saying daily prayers -- the same sacred words repeated each day not because they are new, but because they are eternally true.

What does 'hallow'd thy fair name' (line 8) suggest?

The act of first loving the beloved was a kind of sanctification -- the beloved's name was made holy from the very beginning.

How does eternal love treat age and wrinkles according to lines 9-12?

It ignores them -- love does not weigh the 'dust and injury of age' or yield to necessary wrinkles; it makes antiquity its servant.

What is the paradox in the final couplet?

Love finds its original freshness in the place where Time and outward form would declare it dead -- love revives itself within decay.

What does 'Counting no old thing old' mean?

In the vocabulary of love, nothing becomes stale or outdated -- the lover perpetually experiences the relationship as if it were new.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How does Sonnet CVIII resolve the tension between repetition and freshness?

By arguing that love is like prayer -- repetition is not staleness but devotion; the same words carry eternal freshness through feeling.

What literary device is 'thou mine, I thine' (line 7)?

Chiasmus -- the mirrored reversal enacts the mutual possession and balance of the relationship.
