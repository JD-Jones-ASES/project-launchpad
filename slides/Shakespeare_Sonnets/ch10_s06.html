Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 10, Section 6

Sonnet CV — Study Guide

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Anaphora / refrain

"Fair, kind, and true" recurs throughout, mirroring the constancy it describes.

Allusion

The idolatry charge evokes Protestant-era debates about religious devotion and proper worship.

Platonic triad

Fair (beauty), kind (goodness), true (truth) invoke the classical triad absorbed by Renaissance humanism.

Enactment

The poem's formal repetition enacts constancy — the verse is itself "confin'd" to one thing.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What charge does the speaker pre-emptively deny in Sonnet CV?

That his love is idolatry and that he worships the beloved as an idol -- he insists his poems praise one constant subject, not an idol.

What three qualities summarize the beloved in Sonnet CV?

'Fair, kind, and true' -- these three virtues are the sole and recurring subject of all the speaker's verse.

How does the speaker justify writing only about one person?

Constancy in love requires that verse be confined to one subject; variety would be unfaithfulness in both love and art.

What does 'leaves out difference' mean in line 8?

By praising only one person, the poetry excludes comparison and variety -- a sign of absolute, undivided devotion.

What paradox appears in the couplet?

Fair, kind, and true have existed separately in history, but never before have all three lived together in one person.

What is 'one thing expressing' a defence of?

Repetition in the sonnets -- constancy of subject is presented as the highest form of artistic faithfulness, not a poverty of imagination.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What does Sonnet CV suggest about apparent monotony in the sequence?

The speaker turns always praising the same person into a theological-like devotion -- not idolatry, but pure, singular love.

What literary device is 'Fair, kind, and true' repeated three times?

Anaphora -- the triple repetition of the phrase enacts the constancy the poem describes.
