Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 10, Section 2

Sonnet CI — Study Guide

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Apostrophe

The Muse is addressed throughout as a person who must answer for her truancy.

Antithesis

The Muse's hypothetical defense ("best is best, if never intermix'd") is set against the speaker's counter-argument.

Metaphor

"Truth in beauty dy'd" fuses abstract qualities using the image of dyeing cloth.

Conceit

The "gilded tomb" represents costly but perishable material commemoration, inferior to verse.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What accusation opens Sonnet CI?

The Muse is called a 'truant' -- it has neglected truth in favour of beauty, failing its proper duty.

What argument does the Muse offer in its own defence (lines 5-8)?

Truth needs no embellishment since it has its own fixed colour; beauty needs no painting brush; best is best unmixed.

Why does the speaker reject the Muse's excuse?

Even if the beloved needs no praise now, it lies in the Muse's power to make him outlive a gilded tomb and be praised by future ages.

What is the couplet's instruction to the Muse?

Do your office: the speaker will teach the Muse how to make the beloved seem, long hence, exactly as he appears today.

What tension drives Sonnet CI?

The tension between truth (what is genuine) and beauty (outward appearance), and whether poetry can or should add to what is already perfect.

What does 'gilded tomb' signify?

An ornate but ultimately perishable monument -- contrasted with the enduring immortality that verse can provide.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How does Sonnet CI continue the theme of Sonnet C?

Both sonnets address and rebuke the Muse for neglecting the beloved, urging poetry as a weapon against Time's obliteration.

What literary device appears in 'truth in beauty dy'd'?

Metaphor -- truth is depicted as being dyed or stained by beauty, suggesting an intermingling of the two qualities in the beloved.
