Chapter 3, Section 1

Sonnet I

Study Slides

Key Literary Devices

Paradox

"Making a famine where abundance lies" — the youth's great beauty creates a scarcity because he refuses to pass it on.

Oxymoron

"Tender churl" — simultaneously affectionate and accusatory, capturing Shakespeare's flattery-with-rebuke strategy.

Extended metaphor

The flame feeding on "self-substantial fuel" — beauty as a candle burning itself to nothing.

Imagery of the bud

The unopened flower that buries its "content" rather than blooming into an heir.

Key Concepts (2/2)

How does Sonnet 1 use paradox to describe the Young Man's self-destruction?

'Thyself thy foe, to thy sweet self too cruel' — he is both the victim and the perpetrator of the destruction of his own beauty through self-absorption.
