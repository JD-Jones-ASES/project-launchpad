Key Poetic Devices

Opening paradox

love too young to know conscience, yet conscience born of love — self-subverting logic

Soul/body division

the nobler part (soul) betrayed to the gross body's treason

Bawdy puns

"rising," "stand," "fall" — the body's sexual autonomy described with deliberate double meaning

Ironic self-defense

the couplet claims "no want of conscience" while obviously describing a failure of moral self-governance

Key Concepts (1/2)

What age-old paradox opens Sonnet CLI?

Love is too young to know what conscience is -- yet who knows not that conscience is born of love? Love and conscience are mutually dependent, both claiming ...

What does 'gentle cheater, urge not my amiss' mean?

The speaker tells the mistress not to press him with accusations of wrongdoing, since her own cheating created the situation.

What is the 'treason' the speaker describes in lines 5-6?

By betraying the mistress's trust, the speaker betrays his own nobler part (soul) to his gross body -- the body's desire overrides the soul's better judgment.

What does the body do at the mistress's name (lines 8-9)?

The body rises at her name, pointing her out as his triumphant prize -- a sexually explicit description of physical arousal.

What does 'Proud of this pride, / He is contented thy poor drudge to be' mean?

The body is proud of its own erection/desire ('this pride' = both pride and arousal) and is happy to be the mistress's poor servant.
