Shakespeare's Sonnet Form and How to Read a Sonnet

Study Slides

The Closing Couplet

Resolution

— a definitive statement capping the argument (Sonnet 18)

Reversal

— pulling the rug from under the preceding three quatrains (Sonnet 130)

Paradox

— a simultaneously true and self-contradicting statement (Sonnet 98)

Ironic undercutting

— the speaker says one thing and quietly means another

Key Rhetorical Devices

Device. Definition

Example in Sonnets

Anaphora. Repetition at the start of successive lines

"When I consider…" / "When I behold…" (Sonnet 15)

Personification. Giving abstract forces agency

Time with hands, a scythe, a voice of decay

Apostrophe. Direct address to absent person or abstraction

"Devouring Time" (Sonnet 19)

Antithesis. Contrasting ideas in parallel structures

fair/foul, eternal/mortal

Synecdoche. A part standing for the whole

"eyes" for the beloved; "pen" for writing

The Six Imagery Clusters (1/2)

Seasons and nature

— most pervasive; models the human life cycle

Time and its instruments

— clocks, scythes, hourglasses; Time personified as destroyer

Mirrors and reflection

— self-knowledge, the gap between self-image and appearance

Accounting and law

— love as contract, debt, legacy

Warfare and siege

— desire as army; the heart as besieged city

The Six Imagery Clusters (2/2)

Agriculture

— especially in procreation sonnets (1–17); cultivation and harvest

Placing contrasting ideas in parallel grammatical structures. In the sonnets it enacts the tensions at the heart of love: fair/foul, eternal/mortal, true/false.
