Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 10, Section 3

Sonnet CII — Study Guide

Study Slides

Literary Devices

Paradox

Love is stronger though seemingly weaker — the central surprise the poem unpacks.

Allusion and simile

The Philomel comparison draws on classical myth (Ovid's Metamorphoses) and sustains the analogy across four lines.

Commercial metaphor

"Merchandiz'd" love debases affection by treating it like a product advertised for profit.

Aphorism

"Sweets grown common lose their dear delight" functions as a proverbial observation about value and scarcity.

Key Concepts (1/2)

What paradox opens Sonnet CII?

The speaker's love has grown stronger even though it appears weaker -- he writes less, yet loves more deeply.

What does the speaker say about love that is publicized?

Love that is constantly displayed and advertised ('merchandiz'd') cheapens its own value.

Who is Philomel and how is she used in Sonnet CII?

Philomel (the nightingale) sings in spring but stops in summer; similarly, the speaker was vocal when love was new but has grown silent now that it has deepe...

Why does the nightingale stop singing in summer?

Not because summer is less pleasant, but because 'wild music burthens every bough' -- birdsong becomes common and loses its special delight.

What is the logic of the couplet?

The speaker sometimes holds his tongue not from lesser love but to avoid dulling the beloved with repetitive songs.

What does 'sweets grown common lose their dear delight' mean?

Pleasures that become too frequent cease to be treasured -- familiarity breeds indifference.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the form of the extended comparison in lines 7-11?

An extended simile comparing the speaker's silence to the nightingale's silence after spring.

What theme does Sonnet CII share with sonnets about the Rival Poet?

Both address why the speaker writes fewer poems, defending silence or restraint as a deeper form of love.
