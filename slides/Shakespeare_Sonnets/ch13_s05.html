Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 13, Section 5

Sonnet CXLVI

Study Slides

Key Poetic Devices

Apostrophe

the entire sonnet addresses the speaker's own soul

Economic metaphor

"buy," "sell," "lease," "revenues," "cost" — spiritual investment framed as financial management

"Fading mansion"

the body as a temporary, decaying house for the soul

Chiasmus / paradox in the couplet

feeding on Death ultimately kills Death — a triumphant logical reversal

Textual crux

line 2's repeated "My sinful earth" is almost certainly a compositor's error; editors supply various emendations

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the 'poor soul' addressed in Sonnet CXLVI?

The speaker's own soul, described as the centre of his 'sinful earth' -- the immortal, inner self trapped within and neglected by the body.

What does the soul do instead of attending to itself?

It spends its resources adorning the body's 'outward walls' (physical appearance), while the soul within pines with dearth (starves from neglect).

What is the meaning of 'why dost thou pine within and suffer dearth, / Painting thy outward walls so costly gay'?

Why does the soul neglect itself while lavishing resources on beautifying the body, which is only a temporary, decaying mansion?

What does 'short lease' (line 5) refer to?

The body's limited lifespan -- it is only rented for a short time and does not merit the enormous investment the soul makes in its upkeep.

What are 'worms, inheritors of this excess' (line 7)?

After death, worms will inherit the body and all its costly adornment -- the investment in the body is ultimately made for the benefit of worms.

What instruction does the speaker give the soul in lines 9-12?

Live on the body's loss -- let the body starve so the soul grows rich; buy divine time by selling off the dross (worthless material) of physical life.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the triumphant logic of the couplet?

'So shall thou feed on Death, that feeds on men, / And Death once dead, there's no more dying then' -- the soul that grows spiritually rich literally consume...

How does Sonnet CXLVI contrast with the rest of the Dark Lady sequence?

It is entirely inward and spiritual -- instead of erotic obsession, it advocates for the soul's cultivation over the body's temporary pleasures.
