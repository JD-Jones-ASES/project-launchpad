What does 'sick of welfare, found a kind of meetness / To be diseas'd, ere that there was true needing' mean?

The speaker made himself ill deliberately -- he was so full of the beloved's goodness that he artificially induced need for something else.

What is 'policy in love' (line 9)?

Calculated strategy in love -- the speaker devised a self-protective plan to prevent future excess by pre-emptively seeking out lesser pleasures.

What is the outcome of this 'policy' according to lines 10-12?

It backfired: anticipating ills that did not exist, he actually produced real faults and brought medicine (inferior love) to a perfectly healthy state.

What does the couplet conclude?

The speaker learns from this: drugs poison the man who falls sick of the beloved -- seeking a cure for excess love is itself the disease.

What is the irony of 'rank of goodness, would by ill be cur'd' (line 12)?

Key Concepts (2/2)

How does Sonnet CXVIII relate to the medical imagery in Sonnet CXI?

Both use medicine/disease metaphors for love -- CXI seeks cure from the beloved; CXVIII ironically treats the beloved's love as the disease.

What does this sonnet reveal about the speaker's psychological state?

A kind of restless perversity -- unable to simply accept happiness, he engineers his own misery through anticipatory self-medication.
