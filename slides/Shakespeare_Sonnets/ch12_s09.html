Sonnet CXXXV — The Pun on Will

Key Concepts (1/2)

What is the pun that drives Sonnet CXXXV?

The word 'Will' -- simultaneously meaning desire/lust, the speaker's own name (Will Shakespeare), and a sexual pun -- is repeated obsessively throughout the ...

What does 'Whoever hath her wish, thou hast thy Will' mean?

The mistress already possesses desire itself ('Will') -- she has plenty of will, and the speaker adds his own 'Will' on top of an already overflowing stock.

What is the sea/rain metaphor in lines 9-10?

The sea receives rain despite being full of water -- so the mistress, already rich in 'Will,' should be able to add one more will (the speaker's) to her store.

What is the speaker's request in line 6?

That the mistress vouchsafe (deign) to hide his will in hers -- to accept him despite having more than enough already.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What is the tone of Sonnet CXXXV?

Witty, punning, and sexually charged -- but underneath the wordplay lies genuine anguish at being just one of many who desire the mistress.

What does 'thy will is large and spacious' (line 5) suggest about the mistress?

She has many desires and many lovers -- the speaker is pointing to her promiscuity, but doing so with wordplay rather than direct accusation.
