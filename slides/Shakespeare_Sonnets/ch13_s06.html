Shakespeare's Sonnets: A Reading Companion

Chapter 13, Section 6

Sonnet CXLVII

Study Slides

Key Poetic Devices

Extended medical metaphor

fever, physician, prescriptions, cure — love as pathology sustained throughout

Personification

Reason as a physician who abandons the incorrigible patient

Double simile / anaphora

"as black as hell, as dark as night" — two stacked similes for maximum condemnatory force

"Past cure / past care" pun

chiasmus and wordplay emphasizing total abandonment by reason

Humoral theory context

"physic," "prescriptions," and "cure" invoke Elizabethan medicine's framework of rebalancing disordered humors

Key Concepts (1/2)

What metaphor governs Sonnet CXLVII?

Love as a fever -- the speaker's desire for the mistress is a disease that feeds on what nourishes it, making it worse rather than curing it.

What role does Reason play in lines 5-8?

Reason is the physician to the love-fever -- but it has abandoned the speaker in anger because its prescriptions (rational behaviour) were not followed.

What does 'Desire is death, which physic did except' mean?

What does 'Past cure I am, now Reason is past care' mean?

Beyond healing, since Reason no longer cares for him -- once the physician abandons the patient, the patient is incurable.

What does 'frantic-mad with evermore unrest' suggest about the speaker's mental state?

Total loss of rational control -- his thoughts and speech are like a madman's, random and disconnected from truth.

What is the devastating revelation of the couplet?
