Chapter 6, Section 3: The Tradition They Started

Key Concepts (1/2)

The Short Story as a Living Tradition

The modern short story is not static; it is a dynamic form continuously shaped by successive generations of writers who learn from and respond to their predecessors.

Forms of Influence

Writers are influenced in various ways, including direct imitation, engaging in "argument" with previous styles, or creating something entirely new as a response to what came before.

Legacy of the Masters

Chekhov, Joyce, and Hemingway established benchmarks that subsequent writers, such as O'Connor, Carver, Munro, Gallant, Frank O'Connor, and Lahiri, used as starting points for their own distinct li...

Theological Edge (O'Connor)

The use of spiritual or religious themes to sharpen the exploration of human flaws and self-deception, leading to moments of violent or revelatory truth.

Iceberg Method Extension (Carver)

Expanding on Hemingway's minimalist technique by focusing on working-class characters who suppress their problems, where silence and unstated emotions carry significant weight.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Novelistic Scope in Short Stories (Munro)

Crafting short stories that possess the emotional depth, temporal span, and thematic complexity typically found in novels, often utilizing non-linear, memory-like narratives.

Displacement and Cultural Identity

A recurring theme in many successor writers, exploring the experiences of individuals living far from home, navigating multiple cultures, or grappling with their national and cultural heritage.

Important Terms (1/2)

Living Tradition

A continuous and evolving practice or art form, like the modern short story, that is passed down and reinterpreted by new practitioners.

Theological Edge

A narrative approach that incorporates spiritual or religious insights to deepen the psychological or moral dimensions of characters and events.

Iceberg Method

A minimalist writing technique (associated with Hemingway) where much of the story's meaning is implied rather than explicitly stated, similar to how only a small portion of an iceberg is visible a...

Displacement

The state of being moved or forced to move from one's home or country, often leading to feelings of alienation or being caught between cultures.

Cultural Between-ness

The experience of living between two or more cultures, often involving conflicting expectations, languages, and identities.

Important Terms (2/2)

Domestic Ceremony

The rituals, routines, and significant events within family life that often reveal underlying character dynamics and cultural values.

Flannery O'Connor (1925–1964)

Connection to Chekhov

Shares Chekhov's acute perception of human self-deception.

Distinctive Style

Relocates this insight to the rural American South, injecting it with a unique "theological edge."

Characters

Often proud, self-righteous, and certain of their worldview until a sudden, violent, or revelatory moment shatters their illusions.

Tone

Stories are characterized by a blend of humor and terror.

Recommended Readings

"A Good Man Is Hard to Find," "Everything That Rises Must Converge."

Raymond Carver (1938–1988)

Connection to Hemingway

Pushed Hemingway's "iceberg method" to new extremes.

Themes and Setting

Focused on the lives of working-class Americans, often struggling financially and emotionally.

Style

Early stories are extremely stripped-down, almost skeletal, yet achieve profound emotional impact. Characters frequently drink excessively, speak minimally, and inhabit spaces where unspoken silenc...

Recommended Readings

"Cathedral," "What We Talk About When We Talk About Love."

Alice Munro (1931–2024)

Connection to Chekhov

Frequently called the "Chekhov of the English language" due to her interest in the cumulative effect of ordinary experiences leading to wisdom.

Scope and Complexity

Her stories often possess the emotional and thematic complexity typically associated with novels.

Setting and Narrative

Primarily set in small-town Ontario, her narratives span decades and often circle through time like memory, jumping and doubling back to reframe past events through new understanding.

Recommended Readings

"The Bear Came Over the Mountain," the collection *Runaway*.

Mavis Gallant (1922–2014)

Connection to Joyce

Explores themes similar to Joyce, focusing on characters whose national and cultural identities are sources of both pride and confusion.

Background

A Canadian writer who lived most of her life in Paris.

Themes

Her stories, many published in *The New Yorker*, frequently explore displacement, exile, and the subtle cruelties among people living far from their homelands.

Prose

Known for its precision, wit, and quietly devastating emotional impact.

Recommended Readings

The collection *The Selected Stories of Mavis Gallant*, "The Ice Wagon Going Down the Street."

Frank O'Connor (1903–1966)

Connection to Joyce

An Irish successor to Joyce, writing with a warmer tone and less irony, but maintaining comparable precision.

Themes and Setting

Rooted in the everyday life of 20th-century Ireland, his stories often examine the lives of priests, schoolchildren, and families navigating the tension between religious doctrine and personal feel...

Critical Contribution

His essay "The Lonely Voice" is considered a foundational critical study of the short story form.

Recommended Readings

"Guests of the Nation," "My Oedipus Complex."

Jhumpa Lahiri (b. 1967)

Connections to Joyce and Chekhov

Carries forward Joyce's meticulous attention to domestic ceremony and Chekhov's sensitivity to the subtle failures of human understanding.

Themes

Explores displacement and "cultural between-ness"—the experience of existing between two countries, languages, and sets of cultural expectations.

Focus

Her stories primarily depict the lives of Indian-American families.

Recommended Readings

The Pulitzer Prize-winning collection *Interpreter of Maladies*, the novella-length title story of *The Namesake*.
