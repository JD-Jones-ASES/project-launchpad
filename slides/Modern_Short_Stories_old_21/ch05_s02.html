The Conversation Nobody Is Having

Unspoken Subject

The story revolves around a couple discussing whether the girl should have an abortion, though the word "abortion" is never used.

Hemingway's Intent

This omission is not accidental; it's a deliberate choice by the author to reflect how people genuinely behave in high-stakes, public situations when honesty is too difficult.

Indirect Language

The man refers to the procedure as "an awfully simple operation" or "not really an operation at all," minimizing its significance. The girl remains largely silent on the specific topic.

Setting as Symbolism

Characters' Reactions

The couple sits between these two landscapes. The girl looks at both, reflecting her uncertainty. The man looks only at the table or his drink, indicating he has already decided what he wants.

Test of Perception

The girl's comment that the hills "look like white elephants" and the man's inability to see it highlights a fundamental difference in their perception and willingness to acknowledge difficult truths.

"White Elephants"

Double Meaning

The phrase "white elephant" literally describes the appearance of the hills, but it also carries the idiomatic meaning of something costly, burdensome, and unwanted—a gift you cannot refuse.

Girl's Revelation

By using this phrase, the girl subtly reveals her feelings about the pregnancy, suggesting she views it as a "white elephant" for their transient lifestyle.

Man's Blindness

The man's reply, "I've never seen one," suggests his refusal to acknowledge the deeper implications of their situation or the burden the pregnancy represents to her.

Contrast in Revelation
