Heroes of Old: Myths and Legends Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Heroism and Valor

Explores what it means to be a hero, showcasing individuals who display extraordinary bravery, strength, and a willingness to face danger for the good of others (e.g., Theseus, Arthur).

Bravery and Cleverness

Demonstrates that true heroism often involves not just physical courage but also intelligence, strategic thinking, and the ability to solve problems (e.g., Theseus's use of Ariadne's thread).

Consequences of Unchecked Desire

Illustrates the dangers of greed and avarice, showing how wishing for too much can lead to unforeseen and tragic outcomes (e.g., King Midas's Golden Touch).

Key Concepts (2/2)

Timeless Stories

Highlights how ancient myths and legends continue to resonate and teach important lessons across generations, influencing language and cultural understanding.

Important Terms (1/4)

avarice

An extreme greed for wealth or possessions. (King Midas)

labyrinth

A complicated maze or network of passages from which it is difficult to find one's way. (Minotaur's dwelling)

Minotaur

In Greek mythology, a monstrous creature with the body of a man and the head of a bull, who fed on human flesh.

mortal

Subject to death; a human being. (Theseus dealt a mortal blow to the Minotaur)

quest

A long search or journey in pursuit of something important, often involving adventure or challenge. (King Arthur's quest for justice and valor)

Important Terms (2/4)

symmetry

A balanced arrangement of parts on either side of a line or center. (The fearful symmetry of the Tyger)

valor

Great courage, especially in battle. (A quality sought by King Arthur)

Theseus

The young prince of Athens, known for his bravery and cleverness, who slayed the Minotaur.

Minos

The King of Crete who compelled Athens to send a dreadful tribute to the Minotaur.

Daedalus

The cunning craftsman who built the labyrinth for King Minos.

Important Terms (3/4)

Ariadne

King Minos's daughter who helped Theseus escape the labyrinth.

Midas

A very rich king who wished for the Golden Touch.

Marygold

King Midas's beloved daughter, tragically turned to gold.

Arthur

A young boy raised by Sir Ector, who unexpectedly became the true King of England by drawing the sword from the stone.

Uther Pendragon

The old king of England whose death plunged the land into trouble.

Important Terms (4/4)

Sir Ector

A noble knight who raised Arthur from a babe.

Kay

Sir Ector's son and Arthur's older brother.

How Arthur Got the Sword

Source

Retold by Claude from Howard Pyle's *The Story of King Arthur and His Knights* (1903).

Context

Legends of King Arthur have been told for over a thousand years, highlighting themes of chivalry, justice, and leadership. Pyle brought these stories to life for young American readers.

Characters

Plot Summary

Themes

True leadership, humility, destiny, justice, valor.

The Eagle by Alfred, Lord Tennyson (1851)

Source

One of Tennyson's shortest and most powerful poems.

Context

Tennyson was a famous Victorian poet. He wrote "The Eagle" after observing eagles.

Summary

A six-line poem describing an eagle perched high on a crag, observing the sea below, before descending "like a thunderbolt."

Imagery

Crooked hands (talons), lonely lands, azure world, wrinkled sea, mountain walls, thunderbolt.

Themes

Majesty of nature, power, solitude, predatory grace, swiftness.

The Tyger by William Blake (1794) (1/2)

Source

From Blake's collection *Songs of Experience*.

Context

Blake was an English poet and artist. "The Tyger" is part of a pair with his poem "The Lamb," asking profound questions about creation.

Summary

A six-stanza poem questioning the terrifying beauty and fearful symmetry of the tiger, pondering the nature of its Creator and whether the same Creator made both the gentle lamb and the fierce tiger.

Imagery

Burning bright, forests of the night, immortal hand/eye, fire of thine eyes, wings, hammer, chain, furnace, anvil, dread grasp.

Themes

Creation, divine power, good and evil, beauty and terror, the complexity of the Creator.

The Tyger by William Blake (1794) (2/2)

"Did You Know"

Blake illustrated his own books, coloring each page by hand. The poem raises a theological question about the coexistence of innocence ("The Lamb") and experience/fierceness ("The Tyger") in creation.
