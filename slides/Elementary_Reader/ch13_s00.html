Key Concepts

Adventure and Intrigue

The thrill of the unknown, the pursuit of treasure, and the arrival of mysterious strangers that set epic journeys in motion.

Bravery and Loyalty

The courage to face dangerous foes, the protective instincts for one's adopted family, and the unwavering commitment to a cause.

Character and Resilience

The development of personal integrity, the ability to maintain composure under pressure, and the strength to rebuild after setbacks.

Authorial Craft

How writers like Robert Louis Stevenson and Rudyard Kipling use vivid descriptions, suspense, and evocative language to engage readers and convey deeper meanings.

Coming of Age

The subtle lessons embedded in stories and poems that guide young readers towards understanding what it means to be a responsible, honorable individual.

Important Terms (1/5)

Sea-chest

A sailor's trunk for personal belongings, often used to carry valuables.

Hand-barrow

A frame with handles, carried by hand, used for transporting loads.

Tarry pigtail

A traditional sailor's hairstyle, often braided and stiffened with tar, suggesting a life at sea.

Sabre

A heavy cavalry sword with a curved blade and a single cutting edge.

Cove

A small, sheltered bay or inlet on a coast.

Important Terms (2/5)

Grog-shop

An old-fashioned term for a tavern or bar where alcoholic drinks are served.

Berth

A place to sleep or dock; in this context, a suitable lodging place.

Weather-eye

An idiom meaning to be watchful or alert, especially for danger.

Spanish Main

The coastal region of mainland America colonized by Spain, known historically for pirate activity.

Buccaneers

Pirates, especially those who preyed on Spanish shipping in the West Indies during the 17th and 18th centuries.

A small, carnivorous mammal native to Asia and Africa, known for its ability to hunt and kill snakes.

Burrow

Bungalow

A low house, usually with a single story, from India.

Cantonment

A military station or permanent military quarters in India.

Tailorbird

A small Old World bird that stitches leaves together to make its nest.

Sluice

Rikki-Tikki-Tavi (Part 1 & 2) (Adapted from Rudyard Kipling, *The Jungle Book*) (2/2)

Author's Background: Rudyard Kipling

If— (Rudyard Kipling, 1910)

Purpose

Written as advice from Rudyard Kipling to his son, John, on how to develop into a good, honorable, and mature person.

Structure

The poem is a single, long conditional sentence, presenting a series of virtues and qualities. The "reward" for embodying these traits is revealed only in the final two lines.

Key Virtues and Qualities

The Reward

If a person can embody all these difficult virtues, they will achieve true maturity and purpose: "Yours is the Earth and everything that's in it, / And—which is more—you'll be a Man, my son!"
