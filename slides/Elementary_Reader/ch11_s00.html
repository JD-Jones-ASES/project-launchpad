Important Terms (2/3)

Elegy

A poem or song of serious reflection, typically a lament for the dead. Walt Whitman's "O Captain! My Captain!" is a famous example.

"Four score and seven years ago"

The opening phrase of the Gettysburg Address, referring to 87 years prior to 1863, specifically 1776, the year the Declaration of Independence was signed.

Omnipotence

The quality of having unlimited or very great power, often attributed to God. Sullivan Ballou uses it to describe the power of God.

Vintagethe grape harvest

In the "Battle Hymn," it refers metaphorically to God's judgment, where the "grapes of wrath are stored."

Contemners

Those who show contempt or disdain. Used in the "Battle Hymn" in the context of divine judgment.

Important Terms (3/3)

Rack

In "O Captain! My Captain!" it refers to the aftermath of a storm, symbolizing the trials and tribulations of the Civil War.

Keel

The bottom beam of a ship, representing the foundation or strength of the vessel. In Whitman's poem, it symbolizes the enduring nation.

Key Themes

A Soldier's Letter: Sullivan Ballou (July 14, 1861)

Patriotism vs. Personal Love

Ballou expresses deep love for his wife, Sarah, describing it as "deathless," yet acknowledges that his "love of Country comes over me like a strong wind" compelling him to the battlefield.

Commitment to the Cause

He has "no misgivings" about the Union cause, believing in the importance of maintaining the government and paying a "debt" to those who fought in the Revolution.

Anticipation of Death

He writes, "Lest I should not be able to write you again, I feel impelled to write a few lines that may fall under your eye when I shall be no more."

Promise of Continued Presence

He beautifully reassures Sarah that if the dead can return, he will "always be near you," his spirit manifesting in soft breezes.

Hope for Reunion

Asks Sarah not to mourn but to "think I am gone and wait for thee, for we shall meet again."

Battle Hymn of the Republic: Julia Ward Howe (1862)

Divine Judgment and Righteous War

Portrays the Union cause as a holy war, with God actively involved, "trampling out the vintage where the grapes of wrath are stored" and "sifting out the hearts of men."

Moral Imperative for Freedom

Connects Christ's sacrifice ("As He died to make men holy") to the soldiers' sacrifice to "make men free."

God on the Union Side

Asserts that "His truth is marching on" and "Our God is marching on," giving the Union soldiers a sense of divine purpose and invincibility.

Call to Action

Urges the soul to "be swift... to answer Him! be jubilant, my feet!" in the march for justice.

O Captain! My Captain!: Walt Whitman (1865)

Triumph and Tragedy

National Mourning

Expresses deep personal and national grief over Lincoln's death, emphasizing the shock and disbelief that the "father" of the nation is gone just as peace is achieved.

Enduring Legacy

While the Captain is gone, the "vessel grim and daring" (the nation) has endured, suggesting that Lincoln's work enabled the nation's survival.

Elegy

As an elegy, it serves as a lament for the dead, a poetic expression of grief and remembrance.
