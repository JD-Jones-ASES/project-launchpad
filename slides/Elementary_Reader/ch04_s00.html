Chapter 4: Pilgrims and Explorers: Voyages to a New World Study Guide

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Exploration and "Discovery"

Christopher Columbus's voyage in 1492 led to the European "discovery" of lands previously unknown to them, initiating centuries of transatlantic exchange and colonization.

Motivations for Voyage

Explorers like Columbus sought new trade routes and wealth (gold, spices), while groups like the Pilgrims sought religious freedom and a place to worship as they chose.

Perilous Sea Journeys

Voyages across the Atlantic were long, dangerous, and unpredictable, fraught with violent storms, ship damage, and seasickness, as vividly described by William Bradford.

First Impressions of the New World

Early European accounts often highlighted the immense natural beauty, fertility, and abundant resources of the lands they encountered, as well as their initial perceptions of the indigenous peoples.

Colonization and Settlement Challenges

The establishment of permanent settlements like Jamestown faced severe difficulties, including disease, starvation, conflict with native populations, and the need for strong leadership to survive.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Primary Source Documentation

The letters and historical accounts from figures like Columbus, Bradford, and Smith provide invaluable primary sources for understanding this pivotal period in history.

Important Terms (1/4)

unclothed

Described by Columbus as the state of the indigenous people he encountered, reflecting European cultural norms and perceptions.

caravel

A small, highly maneuverable sailing ship used by the Portuguese and Spanish for exploration in the 15th and 16th centuries, like Columbus's ships.

Pilgrims

A group of English Separatists who sailed to America on the Mayflower in 1620, seeking freedom to practice their religion.

Mayflower

The ship that carried the Pilgrims from England to Plymouth Colony in 1620.

shroudly

Severely or grievously; used by William Bradford to describe how the Mayflower was shaken by storms.

Important Terms (2/4)

hull

To drift with sails furled, often done by a ship during a storm to maintain stability.

seel

A sudden roll or lurch of a ship, which in the Mayflower's case, caused a man to be thrown overboard.

halyards

Ropes or tackle used for raising and lowering sails or flags on a ship.

Cape Cod

The peninsula in Massachusetts where the Pilgrims first landed in 1620.

Jamestown

The first permanent English settlement in America, founded in Virginia in 1607.

Important Terms (3/4)

Powhatan people

A powerful confederation of Algonquian-speaking Native American tribes in present-day Virginia, with whom the Jamestown colonists interacted.

harbor

A sheltered body of water where ships can anchor safely.

perilous

Full of danger or risk; extremely risky.

provision

Supplies of food and other necessities, especially for a journey.

tempest

A violent storm with strong winds, often over the sea.

Important Terms (4/4)

voyage

A long journey, especially one by sea or in space.

Christopher Columbus's Voyage and Letter (1493) (1/2)

Motivation and Journey

In 1492, Columbus sailed west from Spain, intending to reach Asia. His voyage took 33 days, during which he commanded a fleet given by the King and Queen of Spain.

The "Discovery" and Claims

Instead of Asia, he encountered islands previously unknown to Europeans. He immediately took possession of these islands for the Spanish monarchs, naming the first one "San Salvador."

Christopher Columbus's Voyage and Letter (1493) (2/2)

Historical Impact

Columbus's letter was quickly printed and widely distributed across Europe, making him famous and sparking further European exploration and colonization of the Americas.
