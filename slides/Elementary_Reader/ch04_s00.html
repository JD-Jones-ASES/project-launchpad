Key Concepts (1/2)

The Age of Exploration

A period from the 15th to 17th centuries when European nations embarked on extensive overseas exploration, driven by economic, political, and religious motivations.

Motivations for Exploration

European powers sought new trade routes to Asia, valuable resources (gold, silver, furs), expanded empires, and opportunities to spread Christianity.

Pilgrim Migration

A specific movement of religious Separatists from England who sought freedom from persecution and the ability to practice their faith without interference, leading them to establish the Plymouth Co...

The Concept of a "New World"

The European perception of the Americas as newly discovered lands, largely ignoring the ancient civilizations and established societies of Indigenous peoples already living there.

Perilous Journeys

The immense challenges faced by voyagers, including dangerous sea travel, disease, limited supplies, and the psychological toll of uncertainty.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Colonial Foundation

The establishment of permanent European settlements in the Americas, marking the beginning of significant cultural exchange, resource extraction, and the reshaping of the global landscape.

Important Terms (1/2)

Explorer

An individual who travels to discover new lands, routes, or information, often driven by a quest for knowledge, wealth, or imperial expansion.

Pilgrim

A person who journeys to a sacred place for religious reasons. In the context of American history, it specifically refers to the English Separatists who founded Plymouth Colony in 1620.

Separatists

A group of English Protestants in the 16th and 17th centuries who wished to separate from the Church of England and form independent local churches. The Pilgrims were Separatists.

New World

A term historically used by Europeans to describe the Americas, signifying its "discovery" by Europeans and distinguishing it from the "Old World" of Europe, Asia, and Africa.

Colonialism

The policy or practice of acquiring full or partial political control over another country, occupying it with settlers, and exploiting it economically.

Important Terms (2/2)

Mayflower Compact

The first governing document of Plymouth Colony, signed by 41 male passengers aboard the Mayflower in 1620. It established a framework for self-governance and rules for the colony.

Indigenous Peoples

The original inhabitants of a particular region or country. In the context of the Americas, this refers to the numerous Native American tribes and nations.

Navigation

The science of planning and directing the route of a ship, aircraft, or other form of transport. Essential for early ocean voyages.

Caravel

A small, highly maneuverable sailing ship developed by the Portuguese in the 15th century, crucial for long-distance exploration.

Northwest Passage

A sea route through the Arctic Ocean, along the northern coast of North America, connecting the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans. Early explorers sought this passage as a quicker trade route to Asia.
