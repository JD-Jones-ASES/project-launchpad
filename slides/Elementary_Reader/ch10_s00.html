Chapter 10: Slavery and Freedom: Voices of Conscience

Study Slides

Key Concepts (1/2)

Slave Narratives

Autobiographical accounts written or told by formerly enslaved people, serving as vital tools for documenting the brutality of slavery, asserting humanity, and advocating for abolition.

Abolitionist Rhetoric

The art of persuasive speaking and writing used by abolitionists to argue against slavery, appeal to moral conscience, and mobilize public opinion.

Protest Literature

A broad category encompassing various literary forms (poetry, fiction, songs) that challenge social injustices, articulate resistance, and inspire change.

The Quest for Freedom and Literacy

The deep connection between attaining literacy and achieving intellectual and personal freedom, often highlighted in slave narratives as a path to agency and self-expression.

Resilience and Resistance

The various ways enslaved people maintained their dignity, culture, and hope, often through overt acts of defiance, covert resistance, spiritual practices, and the preservation of identity.

Key Concepts (2/2)

Conscience and Morality

The central role of moral conviction and ethical arguments in the abolitionist movement, challenging the societal norms and laws that upheld slavery.

Important Terms (1/2)

Abolitionism

The movement to end slavery, particularly in the 18th and 19th centuries in the United States and Great Britain.

Autobiography

A non-fiction account of a person's life written by that person, often crucial for first-hand testimonies of slavery.

Emancipation

The process of being set free from legal, social, or political restrictions; specifically, the freeing of enslaved people.

Fugitive Slave Acts

A series of laws passed by the U.S. Congress in 1793 and 1850, mandating the return of runaway enslaved people to their enslavers, even if they had reached free states.

Rhetoric

The art of effective or persuasive speaking or writing, especially the use of figures of speech and other compositional techniques.

Important Terms (2/2)

Spirituals

A genre of religious folk songs created and sung by enslaved African Americans, often containing coded messages about escape or expressing hope for freedom and justice.

Underground Railroad

Overarching Themes

The Dehumanization and Reassertion of Humanity

How slavery attempted to strip individuals of their identity and dignity, and how voices of conscience fought to reclaim it.

The Pursuit of Freedom

Exploring freedom not just as physical liberation, but also intellectual, spiritual, and moral emancipation.

Resistance and Agency

Highlighting the many ways enslaved people and their allies resisted the system, from overt rebellion to subtle acts of cultural preservation and self-empowerment.

The Role of Conscience

Examining the personal and collective moral awakening that fueled the abolitionist movement and challenged a nation to live up to its stated ideals.
