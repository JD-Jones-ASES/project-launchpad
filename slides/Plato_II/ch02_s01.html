Important Terms (1/2)

Phlius

A small city in the northeastern Peloponnese, home to a community of Pythagorean philosophers, where the frame narrative of the *Phaedo* begins.

Echecrates

A Pythagorean philosopher in Phlius who requests Phaedo to recount Socrates' final day.

Phaedo

A young Athenian disciple of Socrates who was present at his death and serves as the narrator of the dialogue.

Pythagoreans

Followers of Pythagoras, who believed in the immortality of the soul and reincarnation. Their ideas influence many of Socrates' arguments in the *Phaedo*.

melete thanatou

A Greek phrase meaning "the practice of death" or "the rehearsal of dying," used by Socrates to describe the philosopher's lifelong pursuit.

katharsis

auto to ison

Anamnesis

The Greek word for "recollection," central to Socrates' argument that learning is the process of remembering knowledge our souls possessed before birth.

The Frame Narrative

Setting

The *Phaedo* does not start in Socrates' prison cell but in Phlius, a city known for its Pythagorean community, some time *after* Socrates' death.

Characters

Phaedo, a young disciple, recounts the events of Socrates' final day to Echecrates, a Pythagorean philosopher.

Plato's Purpose for the Frame

Pleasure and Pain

Socrates' Observation

Philosophical Significance

This seemingly casual remark immediately introduces the central theme of the dialogue: the relationship between the body and the soul.

Philosopher's Task

To separate the soul from the body's demands as much as possible, enabling the soul to think clearly.

Socrates' Demeanor

His calm, cheerful tone while facing execution is a practical demonstration of his philosophy: a soul-focused philosopher has nothing to fear from the final separation.

Why the Philosopher Welcomes Death (1/2)

Cebes' Question

Socrates' friends are puzzled by his willingness to die, asking why a wise man wouldn't want to live.

"Practicing Dying" (melete thanatou)

Socrates' core claim is that the true philosopher spends life "practicing dying."

The Body as an Obstacle

True Knowledge

Comes not through the deceptive senses, but through the soul "thinking by itself"—withdrawing from bodily sensations (sight, hearing, pleasure, pain).

Definition of Death

Death is precisely "the separation of the soul from the body." Therefore, the philosopher's discipline of separating soul from body daily is a rehearsal for death.
