Key Concepts (2/2)

Describe Socrates' sharp distinction between the body and the soul.

The body belongs to the visible, changing, perishable world and is a source of confusion and desire, while the soul belongs to the invisible, unchanging, ete...

What analytical question does the text suggest readers consider regarding Socrates' sharp division between body and soul?

Whether this division is too sharp, if the body can truly be neatly separated from the mind, and what would be lost if Socrates' view were accepted completely.

What is the "Theory of Forms" as it becomes central to the argument in the Phaedo?

The idea that behind the imperfect things we experience, there exist perfect, eternal realities known as the Forms.

How did earlier dialogues, like the Euthyphro, hint at the concept of the Forms?

In the Euthyphro, Socrates' question about what makes all pious acts pious pointed toward a single Form of Piety.

How does Socrates connect the Theory of Forms to the arguments for the soul's immortality?

He argues that if the soul can know eternal things (the Forms) through direct acquaintance before birth, then perhaps the soul itself is eternal.

Besides philosophical arguments, what other narrative device does Socrates employ in the Phaedo?

He uses myths—stories about the afterlife, the geography of the underworld, and the fate of different kinds of souls.
