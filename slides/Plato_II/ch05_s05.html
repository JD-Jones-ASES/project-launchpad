Key Concepts (1/2)

Plato's Academy

Founded by Plato around 387 BC, it was a prominent intellectual center in Athens, lasting nearly nine centuries. It served as the training ground for many thinkers, including Aristotle.

Aristotle's Intellectual Journey

Arrived at the Academy at 17, stayed for 20 years as a student, teacher, and researcher, demonstrating exceptional brilliance and eventually developing philosophies that challenged Plato's core ideas.

Important Terms (1/3)

Plato's Academy

The school of philosophy founded by Plato in Athens around 387 BC, where Aristotle studied and taught.

Aristotle (384–322 BC)

Greek philosopher, student of Plato, tutor of Alexander the Great, and founder of the Lyceum. Known for his empirical approach and vast contributions across many fields of study.

Stagira

A small Greek colony in northern Greece, Aristotle's birthplace.

Nicomachus

Aristotle's father, who was the personal physician to King Amyntas III of Macedon.

King Philip II of Macedon

The king who invited Aristotle to tutor his son, Alexander, in the Macedonian court.

Important Terms (2/3)

Alexander the Great (356–323 BC)

King of Macedon, Aristotle's most famous pupil, who went on to conquer a vast empire and spread Greek culture.

Lyceum

The school founded by Aristotle in Athens around 335 BC, an intellectual rival to Plato's Academy.

Peripatetics

The name given to Aristotle's students at the Lyceum, derived from their practice of discussing philosophy while walking in the *peripatoi* (covered walkways).

*The School of Athens

*: A fresco painted by Raphael (1509–1511) that famously depicts Plato pointing upwards and Aristotle gesturing outwards, symbolizing their differing philosophies.

*Timaeus

*: A dialogue by Plato, held by him in Raphael's fresco, which discusses cosmology, the natural world, and the Forms.

Important Terms (3/3)

*Nicomachean Ethics

*: A treatise by Aristotle on ethics, held by him in Raphael's fresco, focusing on character, virtue, and the good life.

*Amicus Plato, sed magis amica veritas

*: A Latin phrase meaning "Plato is dear to me, but dearer still is truth," attributed to Aristotle, reflecting his intellectual independence.
