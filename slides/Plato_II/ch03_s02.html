Key Concepts

Mind-Body Problem

The fundamental philosophical question concerning the relationship between mental phenomena (consciousness, thoughts, emotions) and physical phenomena (the brain, body).

Hard Problem of Consciousness

Tripartite Soul

Plato's theory, primarily presented in the Republic, that the human soul is composed of three distinct parts: Reason, Spirit (Thumos), and Appetite. These parts often engage in internal conflict.

Principle of Non-Contradiction (Platonic Application)

The idea that the same thing cannot simultaneously desire and refuse the same object in the same respect, which Plato uses to argue for distinct parts within the soul when internal conflict occurs.

Functionalism

The philosophical view that mental states are defined by their functional roles within a system, rather than by the physical material that realizes them. Developed by Hilary Putnam.

Key Concepts (2/2)

What distinction does Chalmers draw between explaining brain function and solving the hard problem?

A complete functional description explains behavior but doesn't explain the subjective experience or 'inner life' associated with consciousness.

How did Socrates' argument against Simmias' harmony objection foreshadow the 'hard problem of consciousness'?

How does Plato's view of the soul in the Republic differ from that in the Phaedo?

In the Phaedo, the soul is presented as a single entity, whereas in the Republic, Plato develops a richer picture of the soul having three distinct parts.

What is Plato's primary reason for arguing the soul has multiple parts in the Republic?

The experience of internal conflict, where a person simultaneously desires and refuses the same object, indicates different parts of the soul are at play.

Name the three parts of the soul according to Plato's Republic.

Reason (logistikon), Spirit (thumos), and Appetite (epithymia).

Describe the function of 'Appetite' (epithymia) in Plato's tripartite soul.

Appetite is the part of the soul responsible for bodily desires and impulses, such as hunger, thirst, and sexual urges.
