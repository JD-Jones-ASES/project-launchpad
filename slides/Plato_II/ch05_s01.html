Important Terms (1/4)

Socrates (c. 470–399 BC)

Ancient Greek philosopher whose method of questioning (Socratic method) and inquiries into ethics and the soul profoundly influenced later philosophy.

Plato (c. 428–348 BC)

Student of Socrates, major Greek philosopher who proposed the theory of Forms, argued for the immortality of the soul, and a transcendent Good.

Aristotle (384–322 BC)

Student of Plato, major Greek philosopher whose work covered logic, metaphysics, ethics, politics, physics, and biology, emphasizing observation and empirical reasoning.

Phaedo

A dialogue by Plato featuring Socrates' arguments for the immortality of the soul.

Logos

A Greek philosophical term meaning "word," "reason," or "rational principle." In John's Gospel, it refers to Christ as the divine Word that orders all things.

Important Terms (2/4)

Justin Martyr (c. 100–165 AD)

Early Christian apologist who argued that Greek philosophy contained "seeds of truth" (*logoi spermatikoi*) planted by the divine Logos, thus belonging to Christians.

Augustine of Hippo (354–430 AD)

The most influential theologian in Western Christianity, whose conversion was influenced by Neoplatonism, and whose works (`Confessions`, `City of God`) fused Platonic philosophy with Christian faith.

Neoplatonism

A philosophical system developed in the 3rd century AD, based on Plato's ideas, particularly emphasizing a hierarchy of being emanating from the One, and the soul's ascent towards it. Influential o...

Boethius (c. 477–524 AD)

Roman statesman and philosopher whose `Consolation of Philosophy` (written in prison) transmitted Platonic themes through the early Middle Ages.

Al-Farabi (c. 872–950)

Influential Central Asian Muslim philosopher who sought to harmonize Plato and Aristotle.

Important Terms (3/4)

Avicenna (Ibn Sina, c. 980–1037)

Persian Muslim philosopher and physician whose synthesis of Aristotelian and Neoplatonic thought deeply influenced both Islamic and Christian philosophy.

Averroes (Ibn Rushd, 1126–1198)

Andalusian Muslim philosopher known as "the Commentator" for his extensive and influential commentaries on Aristotle's works.

Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274)

Dominican friar and the greatest systematic theologian of the Middle Ages, known for his synthesis of Aristotelian philosophy with Christian faith in works like the `Summa Theologica`.

Summa Theologica

Aquinas's monumental, encyclopedic work that systematically addresses Christian theology using Aristotelian methods and concepts.

The Five Ways

Thomas Aquinas's five arguments for the existence of God, derived from observable features of the natural world, heavily influenced by Aristotle's metaphysics.

Important Terms (4/4)

Thomism

The philosophical and theological system developed by Thomas Aquinas and his followers, based on Aristotelian principles integrated with Christian doctrine.

Augustinianism

A theological and philosophical tradition emphasizing the ideas of Augustine of Hippo, often characterized by a focus on divine grace, original sin, and a more Platonic worldview.
