Study Guide: Chapter 5 - Footnotes to Plato

Overview

Chapter 5 examines the lasting legacy of Plato's philosophy by tracing how his ideas were debated, transformed, and absorbed across nearly two and a half millennia of Western thought. From his founding of the Academy and his student Aristotle, through the Neoplatonism of Plotinus, the Christian theology of Justin Martyr, Augustine, Boethius, and Thomas Aquinas, to the Renaissance rediscovery of his dialogues, Plato's questions about justice, the soul, and ultimate reality have never stopped demanding answers. The chapter closes with an invitation to continue the philosophical journey by reading more Plato and engaging the examined life as a personal practice.

Key Concepts

  • Plato's Legacy as Living Questions: Whitehead's famous claim that Western philosophy "consists of a series of footnotes to Plato" means not that everyone agreed with Plato, but that every major thinker has been forced to respond to the questions Plato first posed with full rigor — about goodness, justice, knowledge, the soul, and reality.
  • The Theory of Forms: Plato's central philosophical claim that behind every beautiful thing, just action, or mathematical truth there exists a perfect, eternal, unchanging reality (a Form or Idea) that physical things merely imitate; this launched the entire history of Western metaphysics.
  • Neoplatonism: The philosophical system developed by Plotinus (c. 204–270 AD) that built on Plato's Forms, positing a single supreme principle called "the One" from which all reality flows in a hierarchy: the One → Nous (divine Mind) → Soul → the material world.
  • Praeparatio Evangelica (Preparation for the Gospel): The idea, developed by early Christian thinkers, that the best insights of Greek philosophy were partial glimpses of divine truth and that Plato in particular had, by reason alone, anticipated truths that Christianity would fully reveal.
  • Divine Simplicity: Augustine's answer to the Euthyphro dilemma — that the dilemma is a false choice because God's will and God's goodness are not two separate things; God does not choose to be good, God simply is Goodness itself.
  • Natural Law: Thomas Aquinas' doctrine that human laws are just only when they participate in God's eternal rational plan for the universe, and that human reason can discern the moral law because it participates in divine reason — connecting directly back to Socrates' arguments in the Crito.
  • The Examined Life as Practice: Socrates' conviction that philosophy is not a passive activity but a daily practice of questioning, honesty, courage, and humility — and the chapter's closing challenge to students to live that practice themselves.

Important Terms

  • Eide (Forms or Ideas): Plato's term for the eternal, perfect realities behind physical things — for example, Beauty itself behind all beautiful objects, or Justice itself behind all just actions.
  • The One: Plotinus' name for the single, supreme, utterly simple source of all being in his Neoplatonic system; it is beyond all description and is the ultimate origin from which Nous, Soul, and the material world all flow.
  • Nous: In Plotinus' system, the divine Mind or Intellect that proceeds from the One and contains the Platonic Forms; the second level in the hierarchy of reality.
  • Logos Spermatikos: Greek phrase meaning "seeds of the Word" or "seminal reason"; Justin Martyr's concept that God scattered seeds of divine truth throughout the world, some of which the Greek philosophers — including Socrates — cultivated through reason.
  • Praeparatio Evangelica: Latin for "preparation for the Gospel"; the theological idea that Greek philosophy, especially Platonism, providentially prepared the Greco-Roman world to receive Christian revelation.
  • Rationes Aeternae: Latin for "eternal ideas" or "eternal reasons"; Augustine's term for Plato's Forms re-located in the mind of God — the divine patterns according to which God created the world.
  • Natural Law: Aquinas' concept of the moral law that human reason can discover in nature because human reason participates in God's eternal rational governance of the universe; an unjust human law that contradicts the natural law is, in a sense, not truly a law at all.
  • Divine Simplicity: The theological doctrine, developed by Augustine and Aquinas, that God is not composed of separate parts (will, goodness, power, knowledge) but that all of these are ultimately identical in God's single, undivided nature.
  • Scholasticism: The dominant intellectual movement of the medieval universities (roughly the eleventh through fourteenth centuries) that sought to reconcile Greek philosophy with Christian theology using rigorous logical methods; Thomas Aquinas was its greatest representative.
  • Platonic Corpus: The complete collection of dialogues attributed to Plato — about thirty-six works in all, though the authenticity of some is debated; the full corpus was not available in Latin in the West until Marsilio Ficino's translations in 1484.
  • Academy: The school Plato founded in Athens in 387 BC in a grove sacred to the hero Akademos; it operated for nearly nine hundred years — the longest-running institution of higher learning in the ancient world — until closed by the Emperor Justinian in AD 529.
  • Lyceum: The school founded by Aristotle in Athens after he left Plato's Academy; it served as the intellectual home for his enormous range of philosophical and scientific investigations.
  • Enneads: The collected philosophical writings of Plotinus, organized by his student Porphyry into six groups of nine treatises each; the central text of Neoplatonism.

Detailed Notes

Whitehead's Claim and What It Means

  • In 1929, the British mathematician and philosopher Alfred North Whitehead published Process and Reality, which contained the famous line: "The safest general characterization of the European philosophical tradition is that it consists of a series of footnotes to Plato."
  • Whitehead was NOT saying every philosopher simply repeated Plato:
    • Aristotle disagreed with Plato on fundamental questions.
    • Thomas Aquinas reworked Platonic ideas through the lens of Christianity.
    • Immanuel Kant rejected much of ancient metaphysics entirely.
  • What Whitehead DID mean: the questions Plato asked are still the questions philosophers argue about today — every generation has had to respond to Plato, whether by agreeing, refining, or pushing back.
  • The three dialogues studied in this course each contain a living question still debated today:
    • Euthyphro: Is something good because God commands it, or does God command it because it is already good? (the Euthyphro dilemma)
    • Apology: Is the pursuit of wisdom more important than wealth, reputation, or even survival?
    • Crito: Must we obey just laws even at great personal cost, and may we never return injustice for injustice? (Martin Luther King Jr. invoked this principle two thousand years later.)

Plato's Academy

  • In 387 BC, Plato founded his school on the outskirts of Athens in a grove sacred to the hero Akademos — this location gave the English language the word "academy."
  • The Academy was not a modern school with classrooms and exams; it was a community of thinkers discussing philosophy, mathematics, and science.
  • Plato led the Academy until his death in 348 BC; it continued under successors for nearly nine hundred years.
  • It was finally closed in AD 529 by the Emperor Justinian, who ordered all pagan philosophical schools shut down.
  • The curriculum included geometry, astronomy, and harmonics (the mathematical study of musical ratios).
    • Plato believed mathematical reasoning trained the mind to think abstractly — to look past the particular and grasp the universal.
    • A (possibly legendary) inscription over the entrance read: "Let no one ignorant of geometry enter here."

Aristotle: Plato's Greatest Student and Critic

  • Aristotle (384–322 BC) arrived at the Academy at age seventeen from the northern kingdom of Macedon and stayed for twenty years, until Plato's death.
  • He was Plato's most brilliant student and his most independent-minded critic.
  • The key philosophical disagreement:
    • Plato: universals (Forms) exist separately from physical things, in an eternal realm above the material world.
    • Aristotle: universals exist in particular things, not apart from them; we should look outward at the natural world, not upward to abstract Forms.
    • Raphael captured this perfectly in The School of Athens: Plato points to the heavens; Aristotle extends his hand over the earth.
  • After leaving Athens, Aristotle tutored the young Alexander the Great, then returned to found his own school, the Lyceum.
  • His writings span logic, physics, biology, ethics, politics, rhetoric, and poetics; medieval scholars called him simply "The Philosopher."
  • Aristotle famously wrote: "Plato is dear to me, but dearer still is truth" (amicus Plato, sed magis amica veritas).

Plato's Four Most Influential Ideas

  • Theory of Forms: Behind every beautiful thing is Beauty itself — a perfect, unchanging reality that physical things only imitate; behind every just action is Justice itself. These eternal realities (eide) are more real than physical objects. The entire history of Western metaphysics can be read as an argument about whether Plato was right.
  • Immortality of the Soul: In the Phaedo, Plato has Socrates argue that the soul existed before birth and survives death; the soul belongs to the realm of eternal Forms, not to the changing physical world. This shaped virtually every later Western discussion of life after death.
  • The Philosopher-King: In the Republic, Plato argued the ideal city would be governed by philosophers — those who have seen the Form of the Good and can guide society toward true justice. This launched centuries of debate about wisdom and political power.
  • Knowledge as Recollection: Learning is really remembering — the soul once knew the Forms directly, and education draws out what it already knows. In the Meno, Socrates demonstrates this by leading an uneducated slave boy through a geometric proof using only questions.

Neoplatonism: Plotinus and the Great Chain of Being

  • Six centuries after Plato's death, Plotinus (c. 204–270 AD) transformed Platonic thought into a grand philosophical system that would influence pagan and Christian thought for a thousand years.
  • Plotinus was born in Roman Egypt, studied in Alexandria, and settled in Rome where he taught for over twenty-five years.
  • His student Porphyry collected his writings into the Enneads (six groups of nine treatises each).
  • Plotinus' System — a hierarchy of reality flowing from a single source:
    • The One: Supreme, utterly simple, perfect, beyond all description — the ultimate source of all being.
    • Nous (divine Mind or Intellect): Proceeds from the One; contains the Platonic Forms.
    • Soul: Proceeds from Nous.
    • The Material World: Proceeds from Soul.
    • Everything that exists is a kind of "overflow" from the One, like light radiating from a sun.
  • The goal of philosophy and human life, for Plotinus, was to ascend back through these levels and achieve mystical union with the One — an experience Plotinus reportedly had several times in his life.
  • Neoplatonism gave later thinkers a powerful framework for understanding the relationship between God and the world, the spiritual and the material, the eternal and the changing.

Plato and Christianity: The Preparation for the Gospel

  • Early Christians faced a challenge: the Gospel was rooted in Hebrew Scripture, but educated Romans thought in terms of Greek philosophy. How could Christianity speak to minds formed by Plato?
  • Many early Christians found Plato not an enemy but an ally — a pagan thinker who had, by reason alone, glimpsed truths the Gospel would fully reveal.
  • This idea — praeparatio evangelica ("preparation for the Gospel") — became one of the most important themes in Christian intellectual history.
    • The phrase comes from Eusebius of Caesarea (c. 260–340 AD), but Justin Martyr, Clement of Alexandria, and Origen developed versions of it earlier.

Justin Martyr: Seeds of the Word

  • Justin Martyr (c. 100–165 AD) was a pagan philosopher who converted to Christianity and became one of its first great intellectual defenders; he was later martyred in Rome under the Emperor Marcus Aurelius.
  • Before his conversion, Justin had studied Stoic, Aristotelian, Pythagorean, and Platonic philosophy.
  • His key concept: the logos spermatikos ("seeds of the Word") — God had scattered seeds of truth throughout the world, and the Greek philosophers had cultivated some of them.
  • Justin argued that Socrates had lived according to the divine Logos (the Word or Reason of God) even without knowing Christ — making Socrates a kind of "Christian before Christ."
  • Justin wrote: "Those who lived according to the Logos are Christians, even though they were counted as atheists — such as, among the Greeks, Socrates and Heraclitus." (First Apology, ch. 46)
  • The implication: philosophy is not the enemy of faith but its forerunner.

Augustine of Hippo: The Baptism of Plato

  • Augustine (354–430 AD) did more than anyone else to fuse Platonic philosophy with Christian theology.
  • His life: born in Roman North Africa to a pagan father and a devout Christian mother (Monica); brilliant and restless as a young man, he pursued rhetoric, philosophy, and pleasure; drifted through Manichaeism (a dualistic religion teaching an eternal conflict between good/spirit and evil/matter) and academic skepticism before encountering the Neoplatonists in Milan.
  • Reading Plotinus and Porphyry transformed his understanding of God — the Neoplatonists taught him to think of God as pure, immaterial Being, not as a physical substance or an unknowable abstraction.
  • In 386 AD, in a garden in Milan, he underwent a dramatic conversion he recounts in his Confessions.
  • He became Bishop of Hippo in North Africa and produced a massive body of work:
    • Confessions (c. 397–400): The first great autobiography in Western literature.
    • City of God (c. 413–426): A monumental work of philosophy, theology, and history.
  • Augustine's transformation of Plato's Forms:
    • Augustine placed the Forms where he believed they belonged: in the mind of God.
    • The Forms are not free-floating abstractions in a separate realm — they are the eternal ideas (rationes aeternae) in the divine mind according to which God created the world.
    • When we grasp a mathematical truth or recognize beauty, we are dimly seeing into the mind of God.
    • For Plato, the Form of the Good was the highest reality. For Augustine, the Form of the Good is God.
    • The Platonic hierarchy (physical world → soul → eternal Forms) becomes, in Augustine's hands, a map of creation leading back to its Creator.
  • Augustine's answer to the Euthyphro dilemma:
    • The dilemma — "Is something good because God wills it, or does God will it because it is good?" — is, Augustine argued, a false choice.
    • God's will and God's nature are not two separate things. God does not choose to be good; God is Goodness itself.
    • This is the doctrine of divine simplicity: God's goodness, will, knowledge, and being are ultimately one and the same.
    • What we call "the good" is participation in the nature of God; the source of morality is not a divine command imposed from outside but the divine nature from which all goodness flows.

Boethius: Philosophy as Consolation

  • Anicius Manlius Severinus Boethius (c. 477–524 AD) has been called "the last of the Romans and the first of the Scholastics."
  • He was a Roman senator and philosopher serving the Ostrogothic King Theodoric — until he was accused of treason, imprisoned, and executed.
  • While awaiting death in prison, he wrote the Consolation of Philosophy — one of the most widely read books in the Western world for over a thousand years.
  • In the Consolation, the personified Lady Philosophy appears to Boethius in his cell (echoing the personified Laws in Plato's Crito) and leads him through arguments about fortune, happiness, and God.
    • This literary technique of giving voice to an abstract idea (prosopopoeia) makes philosophy vivid and personal.
  • Boethius' core argument: True happiness cannot depend on fortune, wealth, or power — all of these can be taken away. True happiness is found only in the Good itself, which is God.
  • His calm in the face of death echoes Socrates' calm in the Apology and Crito.
  • Boethius also translated and commented on Aristotelian logical works, preserving them for the Latin West during the centuries when knowledge of Greek was largely lost in Western Europe; his translations were the primary source of logical training until the twelfth century.

The Medieval Synthesis: Faith and Reason

  • The medieval period (roughly the fifth through fifteenth centuries) constructed a grand synthesis of Greek philosophy and Christian revelation.
  • Early medieval period: Augustine's Christianized Platonism dominated. Anselm of Canterbury (1033–1109) used Platonic reasoning to construct proofs for the existence of God.
  • Rediscovery of Aristotle (twelfth–thirteenth centuries): Aristotle's works, lost to Western Europe, were rediscovered through Arabic translations and commentaries. Key Islamic philosophers:
    • Avicenna (Ibn Sina, 980–1037)
    • Averroes (Ibn Rushd, 1126–1198)
    • Their commentaries profoundly influenced Christian Scholastic thought.
  • Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274): Achieved the greatest synthesis of faith and reason.
    • Faith and reason are not enemies but allies — two paths to the same truth.
    • Reason (guided by Aristotle) can establish many truths about God, morality, and nature.
    • Faith (guided by revelation) confirms these truths and adds others reason alone cannot reach (e.g., the Trinity, the Incarnation).
  • Aquinas' natural law doctrine (directly connected to the Crito):
    • Human laws are just only when they participate in God's eternal rational law.
    • An unjust law — one that violates the natural law — is not truly a law and does not bind the conscience.
    • Aquinas defined natural law as "the rational creature's participation in the eternal law" (Summa Theologiae I-II, Q. 91, Art. 2).
    • This principle (traced back through Aquinas to Augustine — "an unjust law is no law at all") was invoked by Martin Luther King Jr. in his "Letter from Birmingham Jail" (1963).
  • Aquinas' refinement of Augustine on the Euthyphro dilemma:
    • Like Augustine, Aquinas held that God's will and goodness are one.
    • Aquinas added: God does not arbitrarily decree what is good; his commands reflect his rational nature.
    • Human reason can discern the moral law because it participates in divine reason — this is why natural law ethics is called "natural" and can be known by any rational person, even without Scripture.

The Renaissance: Plato Returns

  • By the fourteenth century, Aristotle's philosophy (as synthesized by Aquinas) dominated European universities; Plato's dialogues themselves were largely unread — known mainly through the filter of Augustine.
  • In 1438–1439, a church council met in Florence to discuss reuniting the Eastern and Western churches; Greek scholars brought manuscripts of Plato's complete works.
  • Cosimo de' Medici, ruler of Florence, sponsored young scholar Marsilio Ficino (1433–1499) to translate the entire Platonic corpus into Latin.
  • Ficino's translations, completed by 1484, made Plato's actual words available to Western readers for the first time in over a thousand years.
  • Ficino also founded the Platonic Academy of Florence — not a formal school but a circle of humanist scholars meeting to discuss Plato, Plotinus, and the relationship between ancient philosophy and Christian faith.
    • Members included Giovanni Pico della Mirandola (1463–1494), whose Oration on the Dignity of Man is sometimes called the manifesto of the Renaissance.
  • Ficino, like Augustine, believed Platonic philosophy and Christian theology were ultimately compatible.
  • The Florentine Platonic revival helped spark the broader Renaissance. Raphael's School of Athens (1509–1511) — which hangs in the Vatican itself — is a direct product of this revival.

Where Next? Continuing the Philosophical Journey

  • More Plato:
    • Meno: Can virtue be taught? Introduces the idea that learning is recollection; the famous slave-boy geometry scene.
    • Phaedo: Socrates' final hours before drinking hemlock; arguments for the immortality of the soul; philosophically demanding but deeply moving; completes the story of the Apology and Crito.
    • Republic: Plato's masterpiece; ten books exploring justice, the ideal city, the Theory of Forms, the Allegory of the Cave, and the philosopher-king.
  • Beyond Plato:
    • Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics: What is the good life? Happiness (eudaimonia) as an activity of the soul in accordance with virtue.
    • Marcus Aurelius, Meditations: A Roman emperor's private journal of Stoic philosophy; carries forward the Socratic spirit of self-examination.
    • Augustine, Confessions: Part autobiography, part philosophy, part prayer; traces the journey from restless ambition to Christian peace.
    • Boethius, Consolation of Philosophy: A powerful companion to the Crito — another philosopher facing death with calm resolve.
  • Philosophy and other disciplines:
    • Literature: Great novels, plays, and poems wrestle with philosophical questions; Hamlet's "To be or not to be" echoes Socrates; Dostoevsky's debates revisit the Euthyphro dilemma.
    • History: Ideas shape civilizations; the American founders read Plato and Aristotle; the civil rights movement drew on natural law.
    • Theology: The great theologians (Augustine, Aquinas, Calvin, Barth) were serious philosophers; understanding Plato makes you a better reader of the Church Fathers.
    • Science: Modern science grew from the philosophical tradition — from the pre-Socratics' rational explanations to Plato's emphasis on mathematical structure to Aristotle's systematic observation.

The Examined Life, Revisited

  • The chapter closes by returning to the Apology's most famous claim: "The unexamined life is not worth living."
  • For Socrates, the examined life was not a solitary activity — it was lived in conversation, by asking questions, testing assumptions, and listening to others.
  • The examined life requires three things:
    • Honesty: The willingness to admit when you are wrong.
    • Courage: The willingness to ask hard questions even when the answers are uncomfortable.
    • Humility: The recognition that wisdom begins with knowing how much you do not know.
  • The examined life is not a destination but a daily practice — a habit of questioning, reflecting, and seeking truth.
  • The chapter's final question, echoing Socrates' lifelong inquiry: "How will you live?"

Review Questions

  1. Alfred North Whitehead claimed that Western philosophy "consists of a series of footnotes to Plato." What did he mean by this? Use at least two specific examples from the chapter to explain how later thinkers responded to Plato's questions, even when they disagreed with his answers.
  2. Describe Plotinus' Neoplatonic hierarchy of reality. What are its three main levels below "the One," and how does the Neoplatonic system both extend and transform Plato's original Theory of Forms?
  3. How did Augustine resolve the Euthyphro dilemma, and what role did his encounter with Neoplatonism play in preparing him for that resolution? In your answer, explain what "divine simplicity" means and why Augustine believed the dilemma was a false choice.
  4. Explain Thomas Aquinas' doctrine of natural law and trace its connection back to Socrates' argument in the Crito. Why did Martin Luther King Jr. appeal to this same tradition in his "Letter from Birmingham Jail" more than seven centuries after Aquinas?
  5. According to the chapter's closing section, what three qualities does living an "examined life" require, and why did Socrates insist that this practice must be carried out in conversation rather than in isolation? Do you think these qualities are realistic goals for a person your age today? Explain your reasoning.
