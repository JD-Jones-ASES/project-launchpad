Study Guide: Euthyphro – The Setting and the Search for Piety

Overview

This section introduces the dramatic setting and initial premise of Plato’s Euthyphro. It details the ironic meeting between Socrates, who is facing charges of impiety, and Euthyphro, a self-proclaimed religious expert prosecuting his own father for a seemingly impious act. The core of the dialogue is established as Socrates' persistent quest for a universal definition of piety, initiating a philosophical exploration that challenges unreflective assumptions about religious and moral concepts.

Key Concepts

  • Socratic Elenchus (or Socratic Method): Socrates' characteristic method of philosophical inquiry, involving cross-examination to reveal contradictions or inadequacies in a stated thesis. Its goal is to test beliefs and identify ignorance, not merely to win an argument.
  • Socratic Demand for Definitions: Socrates insists on finding a universal definition for concepts like piety, justice, or virtue. He seeks the essential nature that all instances of a concept share, rather than just a list of examples. This demand is foundational for rigorous philosophical thought.
  • Distinction Between Example and Definition: A crucial logical difference where an example identifies a particular instance (e.g., "prosecuting wrongdoers is pious"), while a definition identifies the essential quality shared by all instances (e.g., "piety is that quality which..."). Socrates argues that one cannot reliably identify examples without first knowing the definition.
  • The Euthyphro Dilemma: A pivotal question posed by Socrates: "Is the pious loved by the gods because it is pious, or is it pious because it is loved by the gods?" This dilemma explores the relationship between divine approval and the inherent nature of goodness, with profound implications for ethics and theology.

Important Terms

  • King Archon (archon basileus): The Athenian magistrate responsible for cases involving religion and homicide.
  • Meletus: The young poet who served as the primary prosecutor in the case against Socrates, accusing him of impiety and corrupting the youth.
  • Impiety (asebeia): A serious charge in democratic Athens, meaning a failure to honor the gods of Athens, disrespect for divine law, or sacrilege. It could carry the death penalty.
  • Naxos: A large island in the Aegean Sea where Euthyphro's family owned an estate, the setting for the incident involving his father.
  • Diviner (mantis): A person who claims special knowledge of the gods' will, often interpreting omens, dreams, or inspired speech. Euthyphro identifies as a mantis.
  • to hosion: The Greek term for "the pious" or "the holy," referring to what is sanctioned by divine law or religious custom.
  • theophiles: A Greek term meaning "dear to the gods," used in Euthyphro's second definition of piety.
  • Homer and Hesiod: Two great poets of early Greek religion whose epic poems (e.g., Iliad, Odyssey, Theogony) portrayed the traditional myths and often conflicting actions of the Greek gods, forming a kind of religious scripture for Athenians.

Detailed Notes

The Setting

The dialogue Euthyphro opens outside the Porch of the King Archon in Athens, a court dealing with religious and homicide cases. This location immediately signals the dialogue's central themes.

  • Socrates' Situation: He has been summoned to court by Meletus on charges of impiety (asebeia) and corrupting the minds of young people. These were grave accusations in democratic Athens, potentially punishable by death, as evidenced by earlier prosecutions of intellectuals like Anaxagoras and Protagoras.
  • Euthyphro's Situation: Surprisingly, Euthyphro is at the same court to prosecute his own father for murder. The incident involves a hired laborer on their estate in Naxos who killed a slave, was bound by Euthyphro's father, and subsequently died from exposure and neglect before religious authorities could advise on the matter. Euthyphro believes his father's actions were impious, despite his relatives’ outrage.
  • The Irony of the Meeting: The dramatic irony is profound. Socrates, accused of lacking piety, meets Euthyphro, who not only claims to be an expert on piety but is willing to take extreme measures (prosecuting his father) in its name. Socrates expresses a hopeful desire to learn from Euthyphro, allowing him to defend himself against Meletus.
  • Dramatic Date: This conversation takes place before Socrates' trial, which is recounted in the Apology. Together with the Crito (which occurs after the trial, in prison), these three dialogues form a continuous narrative about Socrates' final days.

The Characters

  • Euthyphro: Portrayed as a confident, even self-important, diviner (mantis) – a professional religious expert. He claims special knowledge of the gods' wishes and speaks about piety with unwavering assurance, yet his name, meaning "straight-thinker," might be ironic given his unexamined assumptions.
  • Socrates: The famous philosopher, known for his relentless questioning. He approaches Euthyphro with seeming admiration, but his underlying purpose is to engage in the Socratic elenchus (cross-examination). He aims to test Euthyphro's beliefs, not to insult him, but to uncover whether his understanding of piety can withstand rational scrutiny.
  • Understanding Socrates and Euthyphro: It's important to recognize that Socrates is not simply trying to be difficult or dismissive. His goal is to seek a universal definition that captures the essence of a concept. Euthyphro, while not unintelligent, represents a common, uncritical approach to moral and religious ideas; his failure lies in his unreflectiveness, not a lack of intelligence.

The Central Question: What Is Piety?

Socrates' inquiry revolves around the question: "What is piety?" (in Greek, to hosion). He's not looking for examples of pious acts or behaviors sanctioned by the gods. Instead, he demands a definition of the essence of piety – the single, universal quality that makes all pious actions pious.

  • The Socratic Demand for Definitions: This is a cornerstone of Socrates' philosophy. He believes that one cannot reliably identify individual instances of a concept (like knowing if an act is truly pious) unless one first understands the universal definition of that concept. Without a definition, judgment is arbitrary or inconsistent.

Following the Argument: Euthyphro's Definitions (First Three Attempts)

The core of the Euthyphro is a series of attempts by Euthyphro to define piety, each meticulously examined and found wanting by Socrates.

  1. Definition 1: Piety Is What I Am Doing Now (Prosecuting Wrongdoers)

    • Euthyphro's Claim: He argues that piety is prosecuting wrongdoers, even one's own father, just as Zeus punished his father Kronos.
    • Socrates' Objection: This is an example of piety, not a definition. While prosecuting wrongdoers might be pious, it doesn't tell us what piety is in all its forms. Many pious acts (e.g., praying, offering sacrifices) have nothing to do with prosecution. Socrates isn't judging the moral rightness of Euthyphro's action; he's pointing out its logical inadequacy as a definition.

  2. Definition 2: Piety Is What Is Dear to the Gods (theophiles)

    • Euthyphro's Claim: Piety is whatever the gods love, and impiety is whatever they hate. This is an improvement as it offers a general principle.
    • Socrates' Objection: Drawing on the traditional Greek myths portrayed by poets like Homer and Hesiod, Socrates points out that the gods notoriously disagree. They quarrel, have conflicting desires, and love different things. If piety is simply what the gods love, then the same action could be simultaneously loved by one god (and therefore pious) and hated by another god (and therefore impious). This contradiction makes the definition unworkable.

  3. Definition 3: Piety Is What All the Gods Love

    • Euthyphro's Claim: To address the contradiction, Euthyphro modifies his definition: piety is what all the gods unanimously love, and impiety is what all the gods hate. This requires divine consensus.
    • Socrates' Follow-up: The Euthyphro Dilemma: This refined definition leads Socrates to ask his most famous question in the dialogue: "Is the pious loved by the gods because it is pious, or is it pious because it is loved by the gods?"
      • These two options present a fundamental philosophical problem. They are not the same:
        • Option 1: The pious is loved by the gods because it is pious. This implies that piety has an inherent quality or existence independent of the gods' love. The gods love it because it is good; its goodness is prior to and a reason for their love.
        • Option 2: It is pious because it is loved by the gods. This implies that the gods' love makes something pious. Its goodness is contingent upon divine approval; without their love, it would not be pious.
      • This dilemma forces a choice about the ultimate source of moral or religious value – is it something inherent that even the gods recognize, or is it merely dictated by divine will?

(The provided text concludes at the introduction of the dilemma, implying further discussion of its implications and Euthyphro's subsequent definitions would follow in later sections.)

Review Questions

  1. What are the specific, contrasting reasons Socrates and Euthyphro find themselves at the King Archon's porch? How does this dramatic irony set the stage for their philosophical discussion?
  2. Explain, using examples, the crucial distinction Socrates draws between an "example" of piety and a "definition" of piety. Why does Socrates insist that a definition is necessary for true understanding?
  3. Describe Euthyphro's first two attempts to define piety. What specific objections does Socrates raise against each of these definitions, and why does he find them inadequate?
  4. State the "Euthyphro Dilemma" in your own words. Why are the two options presented by Socrates not interchangeable, and what is the fundamental difference in what they imply about the nature of piety?
  5. Based on the characters and setting described, what do you think Plato intends to criticize or question about traditional Athenian religious beliefs and public understanding of morality through the figure of Euthyphro?
