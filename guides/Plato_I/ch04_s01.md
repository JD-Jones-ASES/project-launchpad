# Study Guide: Plato’s Crito – Reason, Justice, and the Social Contract

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the dialogue between Socrates and his friend Crito. It explores the philosophical arguments regarding the nature of justice, the obligations of a citizen to the state, and the authority of reason over emotion.

---

## Overview of the Dialogue

The *Crito* takes place in an Athenian prison cell in the early morning, approximately one month after Socrates’ trial. Socrates has been sentenced to death for impiety and corrupting the youth, but his execution was delayed due to the annual pilgrimage of a sacred ship to Delos. During this thirty-day period, the city of Athens remains ritually pure, and no executions are permitted.

The dialogue begins when the ship is sighted at Sunium, signaling its imminent arrival and Socrates' impending execution. Crito, a wealthy and devoted friend, visits Socrates to persuade him to escape into exile.

### Key Characters

| Character | Background | Primary Motivation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Socrates** | Philosopher recently condemned to death. | Driven by reason and the pursuit of a just life; remains calm in the face of death. |
| **Crito** | A wealthy, practical friend of Socrates whose name means "judge" (*krites*). | Driven by loyalty, fear for his friend, and concern for his own public reputation. |

## The Arguments for Escape

Crito presents several passionate arguments for why Socrates should flee Athens and avoid his execution:

1.  **Public Opinion:** Crito fears that "the many" will blame him for being too cheap or cowardly to save his friend. He worries about the damage to his own reputation if people believe he refused to provide the necessary funds for an escape.
2.  **Parental Duty:** Socrates has three sons (Lamprocles, Sophroniscus, and Menexenus). Crito argues that by choosing death, Socrates is abandoning his children and failing his duty to oversee their upbringing.
3.  **Feasibility:** Crito emphasizes that the escape is easy to arrange. He and others (such as Simmias and Cebes) have the money required to bribe the jailers, and safe houses are prepared for Socrates in Thessaly.
4.  **The Injustice of the Trial:** Crito argues that because the trial was handled corruptly and based on false charges, Socrates is not morally obligated to respect the verdict.

---

## Socrates’ Philosophical Response

Socrates systematically deconstructs Crito’s emotional appeals through a series of logical principles and analogies.

### 1. The Expert vs. The Many
Socrates challenges the importance of public opinion using the **Analogy of the Trainer**. Just as an athlete should follow the advice of a specialized trainer rather than the general public to avoid ruining their body, a person should prioritize the opinion of a moral expert over "the many." Listening to the crowd regarding right and wrong ruins the "soul" (*psyche*), which Socrates defines as the part of a person that reasons and is capable of moral goodness.

### 2. The Principle of Non-Retaliation
Socrates establishes a central ethical principle: **Never return injustice for injustice.** He argues that it is never right to do wrong, even if one has been wronged first. Because doing wrong corrupts the soul—which is more important than the body—Socrates concludes that an unjust escape would be more harmful to him than his own death.

### 3. The Allegory of the Laws (Prosopopoeia)
Socrates uses **prosopopoeia** to give a voice to the Laws of Athens. The Laws present four main arguments against his escape:
*   **Laws as Parents and Teachers:** The Laws provided the framework for Socrates’ birth, education, and life. To disobey them now would be as shameful as striking a parent.
*   **The Social Contract:** By living in Athens for seventy years, raising his children there, and rarely leaving the city, Socrates entered into an implicit agreement. He enjoyed the benefits of the city and therefore agreed to abide by its legal judgments.
*   **Destruction of the State:** If individuals can choose which court verdicts to obey and which to ignore, the entire legal system and the city itself would collapse.
*   **Persuade or Obey:** The Laws argue that a citizen has two choices: persuade the state that a law or verdict is wrong, or obey it. Socrates had the opportunity to persuade the jury during his trial, and having failed, he must now obey the outcome.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **Why was Socrates’ execution delayed for thirty days?**
    *   *Answer:* It was delayed because of the annual pilgrimage to Delos; executions were forbidden in Athens until the sacred ship returned.
2.  **What is the meaning of Crito’s name, and why is it ironic?**
    *   *Answer:* It means "judge" or "one who decides." It is ironic because while Crito wants Socrates to let him decide the course of action, Socrates is the one who performs the actual moral judging.
3.  **According to Socrates, what is the consequence of making bad moral choices?**
    *   *Answer:* Bad moral choices destroy the "soul" (*psyche*), the part of the person responsible for reason and character.
4.  **What is "prosopopoeia"?**
    *   *Answer:* It is a rhetorical device where an abstract idea or absent person is given a voice to speak directly.
5.  **What does the "Social Contract" theory in the *Crito* suggest about a citizen’s obligations?**
    *   *Answer:* It suggests that by staying in a city and accepting its benefits, a citizen agrees to obey its laws and judgments.
6.  **What was the margin of the vote in Socrates' trial?**
    *   *Answer:* He was convicted by a narrow margin of 280 to 221; a shift of only 30 votes would have acquitted him.
7.  **What alternative did the Laws offer Socrates if he disagreed with the verdict?**
    *   *Answer:* The Laws offered the choice to either persuade them that they were wrong or to obey their commands.
8.  **How does Socrates view the act of returning harm for harm?**
    *   *Answer:* He rejects it entirely, stating that one must never return injustice for injustice, as doing wrong is always harmful to the actor's soul.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Weight of Public Opinion:** Compare Crito’s concern for reputation with Socrates’ analogy of the athletic trainer. Is Socrates’ dismissal of "the many" realistic in a democratic society, or is Crito’s practical concern more valid?
2.  **The Parental Analogy:** Analyze the Laws' claim that they are like parents to the citizens. To what extent should a citizen feel a familial-style debt to their government? Does this debt ever expire?
3.  **The Social Contract and Dissent:** Socrates argues that the proper response to an unjust law is persuasion, not disobedience. Explore the limits of this argument. Is there a point where the "Social Contract" is broken by the state, justifying a citizen's disobedience?
4.  **Integrity and the Soul:** Socrates asserts that it is better to die with an uncorrupted soul than to live with a guilty one. Evaluate this stance. Does this philosophy represent the ultimate form of integrity, or is it a form of stubbornness that ignores practical responsibilities (like his children)?

## Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Analogy:** A comparison between two things used to explain or clarify a point (e.g., the athlete and the trainer).
*   **Hemlock:** A poisonous plant extract used as the standard method of execution in ancient Athens.
*   **Krites:** The Greek word for “judge” or “one who decides”; the root of the name Crito.
*   **Prosopopoeia:** A rhetorical device in which an abstract concept (like the Laws) is personified and given a voice.
*   **Psyche:** The soul; for Socrates, this refers to the inner self, the seat of reason and moral character.
*   **Social Contract:** The implicit agreement between citizens and their government, where the citizen obeys the law in exchange for the protections and benefits of the state.
*   **Sunium:** The southern tip of Attica where the sacred ship was sighted before arriving at Athens.
*   **Thessaly:** A region in Greece where Crito’s friends offered to provide a safe house for Socrates if he escaped.
