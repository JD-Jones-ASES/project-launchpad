Study Guide: Chapter 4 - Crito

Overview

In the Crito, Socrates sits in his Athenian prison cell on the morning before his execution, when his wealthy old friend Crito arrives with a practical plan for escape. Socrates refuses to flee, arguing that even an unjust verdict must be obeyed once the legal process has run its course, because doing wrong — even in response to wrong — corrupts the soul and destroys the rule of law. The dialogue then connects to broader questions about the social contract, the nature of justice, and the long tradition of thinking about when (if ever) citizens may disobey the laws of their city.

Key Concepts

  • Never Return Injustice for Injustice: Socrates' foundational moral principle: it is never right to do wrong, even in response to wrong done to us. Harming anyone — even an enemy who harmed you first — is always wrong because it damages the soul of the one who acts unjustly.
  • The Opinion of the Expert: Socrates argues that we should follow the judgment of a moral expert (someone who truly understands justice) rather than the opinions of the crowd. Just as an athlete trusts his trainer rather than random bystanders, we should trust reason rather than popular opinion when it comes to the health of our souls.
  • The Allegory of the Laws (Prosopopoeia): The most dramatic passage in the Crito, in which Socrates imagines the Laws of Athens as speaking characters who argue against his escape. This rhetorical device — giving a voice to an abstraction — is called prosopopoeia and became one of the most influential literary innovations in Western philosophy.
  • The Social Contract: The idea, introduced by the Laws in the Crito, that citizens and their government are bound by an implicit agreement: the government provides order and a framework for life; in return, citizens consent to obey its laws. If a citizen believes a law is unjust, the proper remedy is persuasion through argument — not disobedience.
  • Thrasymachus' Challenge: A direct philosophical counterpoint to Socrates from Book I of Plato's Republic: the sophist Thrasymachus argues that "justice is nothing but the interest of the stronger" — that laws are tools of power, not noble ideals. If he is right, Socrates in the Crito is a fool who dies in service of a fiction.
  • Just and Unjust Laws: The tradition of thought — running from Augustine and Aquinas to Martin Luther King Jr. — that argues some laws are so deeply unjust that they carry no moral authority and may (or must) be disobeyed. This contrasts sharply with Socrates' position that even unjust verdicts must be accepted once the legal process has concluded.

Important Terms

  • Prosopopoeia: A rhetorical device in which an absent or imaginary person — or an abstract idea such as the Laws of Athens — is given a voice and speaks directly. From the Greek prosopon (face, person) and poiein (to make). Socrates "makes a person" out of the Laws in the Crito.
  • Social Contract: The implicit agreement between citizens and their government: citizens consent to obey the laws in exchange for protection, order, and the conditions for a good life. Socrates introduces this concept in the Crito; it was later developed by Hobbes, Locke, and Rousseau.
  • Civil Disobedience: The deliberate, nonviolent refusal to obey a law considered unjust. The concept traces back to debates sparked by the Crito and was developed by thinkers including Henry David Thoreau, Mahatma Gandhi, and Martin Luther King Jr.
  • Sophist: An itinerant professional teacher in ancient Greece who charged fees for instruction in rhetoric and argumentation. Thrasymachus, who challenges Socrates in the Republic, is a sophist. Socrates distinguished himself from sophists by claiming to teach nothing and charging no fees.
  • Natural Law: The theory, developed by Aquinas from Aristotle and the Stoics, that there is a moral law built into the nature of things and discoverable by reason. An unjust human law that contradicts natural law is, for Aquinas, not truly law at all.
  • Psyche (Soul): When Socrates speaks of the "soul," he means the part of a person that reasons, chooses, and is capable of moral goodness or corruption — roughly what we might call character or inner self. Doing wrong damages the soul, regardless of what was done to the wrongdoer first.
  • General Will: Jean-Jacques Rousseau's concept that when citizens form a community they create a collective will aimed at the common good. Laws that express the general will are legitimate; laws that serve only private interests are not.
  • Analogy: A comparison between two things that are alike in some important way, used to explain or clarify a point. Socrates uses analogy throughout the Crito, most notably comparing care of the soul to the work of an athlete's trainer.
  • Hemlock: A poisonous plant (koneion) whose extract was used in Athens as the standard method of execution. The condemned person drank a cup of hemlock juice, which caused gradual paralysis and death.
  • State of Nature: Thomas Hobbes' concept of human life without any government — a condition of constant war of all against all, where life is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short."

Detailed Notes

The Setting and Context

The Crito opens in a Athenian prison cell at dawn, approximately one month after Socrates' trial and conviction.

  • Socrates was found guilty of impiety and corrupting the youth, as described in the Apology.
  • The only reason he is still alive is a matter of religious timing: Athens had sent its sacred ship on its annual pilgrimage to the island of Delos to honor Apollo. No executions could be carried out while the ship was at sea.
  • The ship has now been sighted at Sunium (the southern tip of Attica, about forty miles from Athens). It will arrive the next day. Socrates has one day left to live.
  • Crito, one of Socrates' oldest and wealthiest friends, has bribed the prison guard to enter before dawn. He finds Socrates sleeping peacefully — a striking image, since most condemned men would be in a panic.
  • The emotional contrast between the two men drives the entire dialogue: Crito is governed by loyalty, fear, and grief; Socrates is governed by reason.

The Characters

  • Crito is not a philosopher. He is a practical, generous, devoted friend who is terrified of losing Socrates. His arguments for escape are exactly what any caring friend would make.
    • His name comes from the Greek krites, meaning "judge" — but it is Socrates who does the real judging in this dialogue.
  • Socrates applies his philosophical method not to an opponent or a hostile jury, but to a friend who loves him. Unlike the Euthyphro or the Apology, this dialogue is a conversation between two people who genuinely care for each other, which makes Socrates' refusal to escape all the more striking — and, for many readers, all the more frustrating.

Crito's Four Arguments for Escape

Crito makes a passionate and reasonable case. His arguments fall into four categories:

  1. Public opinion will blame Crito. People will think he was too cheap or too cowardly to save his friend. "The many will not be persuaded that I wanted you to escape, and that you refused." His reputation is at stake.
  2. Socrates' children will suffer. Socrates has three sons: Lamprocles, Sophroniscus, and Menexenus. A man who brings children into the world has a duty to raise them. Choosing death when escape is possible is abandoning them.
  3. Escape is easy and safe. Friends have arranged everything. Money is no obstacle — Crito is wealthy, and allies in Thebes (Simmias and Cebes) have contributed funds. Safe houses in Thessaly are ready. The guards can be bribed. There is nothing practically stopping Socrates from walking out tonight.
  4. The trial was unjust. Socrates was convicted on false charges by a narrow margin (the vote was 280 to 221 — a shift of only 30 votes would have acquitted him). If the system itself was corrupt, why should Socrates respect its verdict?

Socrates' First Response: The Opinion of the Expert

Socrates does not dismiss Crito's concerns, but he immediately challenges the argument from public opinion.

  • The trainer analogy: Suppose an athlete is training for a competition. Whose advice should he follow — the general public's, or his trainer's, the one person who actually understands fitness? Obviously the trainer's. Listening to everyone's opinion about diet and exercise will ruin the body.
  • Socrates extends the analogy: if we would not trust the crowd with our physical health, why would we trust them with something far more important — the health of the soul?
    • Just as bad food destroys the body, bad moral choices destroy the soul.
    • The opinions of the many about right and wrong are no more reliable than their opinions about nutrition.
  • This argument cuts the ground from under Crito's first point entirely: if the crowd's opinion does not matter, then Crito's fear of blame is irrelevant. What matters is whether escaping would be right.

Socrates' Central Principle: Never Return Injustice for Injustice

With public opinion set aside, Socrates moves to the moral core of his position.

  • The absolute principle: It is never right to do wrong — not even in response to wrong done to us. If someone harms you unjustly, you are not thereby justified in harming them back.
  • This separates Socrates from the common Greek view, which held that it was noble to help your friends and harm your enemies. Socrates rejects this: harming anyone, even an enemy, is always wrong.
  • Why? Because doing wrong corrupts the soul, and the soul is more important than the body.
    • If Socrates escapes, he saves his body but damages his soul by acting unjustly — breaking the agreement he has implicitly made with Athens.
    • He would rather die with an uncorrupted soul than live with a guilty one.
  • This is a radical claim. Most people — in Socrates' Athens and today — believe that returning harm for harm is sometimes justified (self-defense, getting even, responding to an unjust attack). Socrates says no. The principle is absolute.

The Allegory of the Laws

The most famous and dramatic passage in the Crito comes when Socrates asks Crito to imagine that the Laws of Athens themselves could stand up and speak.

  • The rhetorical device is called prosopopoeia — giving a voice to an abstraction or an absent person. Socrates does not merely describe what the Laws would say; he stages a courtroom drama in which the Laws interrogate him.
    • The reversal is significant: in the Apology, Socrates was the defendant and Athens was the judge; in the Crito, Socrates becomes the judge of his own case, and the Laws are the accusers.
  • The Laws' four arguments:
    1. The Laws as parents and teachers. The Laws remind Socrates that they made his life possible. Under the Laws, his parents married; he was born, raised, and educated. The Laws provided the framework for everything good in his life. To destroy them through disobedience would be like striking a parent — an act especially serious in ancient Athens, where disrespect toward one's father was prosecutable as a crime.
    2. The social contract. Socrates was never a prisoner of Athens. As a free adult citizen, he could have emigrated at any time to Sparta, Thebes, Crete, or elsewhere. Instead, he chose to stay for seventy years. He raised his children in Athens. He enjoyed the city's festivals, courts, and public life. By staying, the Laws argue, he entered into an agreement: he would live under their authority and accept their judgments, even when those judgments went against him.
    3. Disobedience would destroy the city. What happens to a city whose legal judgments have no force? If any citizen can ignore a court verdict when it goes against him, the entire legal system collapses. Socrates' escape would not merely be an act of self-preservation — it would be an attack on the rule of law itself.
    4. Persuade or obey. The Laws offer one qualification: if a citizen believes a law is unjust, he has the right to try to change it through argument and persuasion. What he may not do is simply disobey. Socrates had his chance to persuade at the trial. He made his case. The jury disagreed. To flee now would be to say that persuasion only counts when it produces the outcome you want.
  • A key caution: A common misreading of the Crito is that Socrates believes all laws are just or that citizens must blindly obey every law. This is not his position. He acknowledges that the verdict against him was wrong. His argument is that even an unjust verdict must be accepted once the legal process has run its course, because the system of law — imperfect as it is — is more important than any single case.

The Laws' Final Appeal and Socrates' Decision

  • The Laws close with a warning that reaches beyond this life: if Socrates escapes to Thessaly, he will live as a fugitive. His philosophical conversations will become a joke. His children will be no better off. And when he dies and faces judgment in the underworld, the Laws below will not welcome a man who tried to destroy their brothers, the Laws of Athens.
  • When the Laws finish speaking, Socrates delivers one of the quietest and most powerful lines in ancient literature: "This, dear Crito, is the voice which I seem to hear murmuring in my ears, like the sound of the flute in the ears of the mystic."
  • Crito has nothing left to say. Socrates stays. He will drink the hemlock the following day, surrounded by his friends, still talking philosophy to the very end.

Thrasymachus and the Challenge to Justice

The Crito's assumption — that justice is worth more than life itself — is directly challenged in Book I of Plato's Republic by the sophist Thrasymachus.

  • Thrasymachus' argument: "Justice is nothing but the interest of the stronger." In every city, the ruling group makes the laws to benefit themselves, then calls obedience to these self-serving laws "justice." Justice is not a noble ideal; it is a tool of domination.
    • He argues that the perfectly unjust man — the successful tyrant — is happier than the just man, because he takes what he wants without punishment.
    • If Thrasymachus is right, Socrates in the Crito is a fool: he is obeying laws designed by his enemies to serve their own interests, and dying for a principle that is nothing more than a convenient fiction.
  • Plato's response: Plato clearly felt the force of Thrasymachus' challenge, because the Republic spends nine more books answering it. His ultimate answer is that justice is a harmony of the soul, that the just person is genuinely happier than the unjust, and that Socrates' willingness to die rather than act unjustly is the living proof that justice is real and worth more than life.

Social Contract Theory: Hobbes, Locke, and Rousseau

The Crito's social contract idea reappeared roughly two thousand years later in the work of three major European philosophers.

  • Thomas Hobbes (1588–1679): Wrote Leviathan (1651) after living through the chaos of the English Civil War. He argued that without a strong government, human life would be "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short" (the state of nature). Citizens surrender their natural freedom to a sovereign in exchange for security. Once you make this agreement, you are bound by it; disobedience is not an option, because the state of nature is worse than any tyranny.
    • Hobbes and Socrates: Hobbes would likely have approved of Socrates' decision. Even an unjust verdict must be obeyed, because undermining the legal system would be worse than any single injustice.
  • John Locke (1632–1704): Wrote Two Treatises of Government (1689). He agreed that government rests on consent, but argued that every person possesses natural rights — to life, liberty, and property — that no government may violate. If a government tramples on these rights, citizens not only may resist; they have a duty to do so.
    • Locke and Socrates: Locke might have challenged Socrates. If the trial violated Socrates' natural right to life, the Laws' claim on his obedience is void. A government that executes an innocent man has broken the contract first.
    • Locke's ideas deeply influenced the American founding fathers. Thomas Jefferson adapted "life, liberty, and property" into "life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness" in the Declaration of Independence.
  • Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778): Wrote The Social Contract (1762), famous for opening: "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains." He introduced the concept of the general will — the collective will of citizens aimed at the common good. Laws expressing the general will are legitimate; laws serving only private interests are not.
    • Rousseau and Socrates: Rousseau might have been torn. Socrates' obedience reflects the civic devotion Rousseau admired — but if the conviction was the product of prejudice and manipulation rather than the genuine general will of Athens, it lacks legitimacy.
  • Summary of the three positions: All three agreed government rests on consent. Hobbes said: obey anyway. Locke said: resist. Rousseau said: it depends on whether the law reflects the general will.

Just and Unjust Laws: Augustine, Aquinas, and Martin Luther King Jr.

The Crito's "persuade or obey" formula was challenged and developed by later thinkers who argued that some laws are so unjust they carry no moral authority at all.

  • Augustine of Hippo (354–430 AD): In De Libero Arbitrio (On Free Will), Augustine wrote the sentence that would echo through the centuries: "An unjust law is no law at all" (lex iniusta non est lex). A law that contradicts the eternal law of God — commanding what is evil or forbidding what is good — has no binding moral authority, regardless of who enacted it.
  • Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274): The great Dominican friar built on Augustine's insight and gave it a systematic framework in his Summa Theologiae. He distinguished four kinds of law:
    1. Eternal law: God's rational governance of the entire universe.
    2. Natural law: The participation of rational creatures (humans) in the eternal law — basic moral principles that reason can discover without special revelation.
    3. Human law: The specific laws enacted by governments, which should be applications of natural law to particular circumstances.
    4. Divine law: God's revealed law in Scripture, which guides us toward eternal life.
    • A human law that contradicts natural law is unjust and "no law at all." But Aquinas added a practical caution: even when a law is unjust, disobedience may sometimes cause more harm than obedience (by provoking violence or disorder). The decision to disobey requires prudence — practical wisdom about what will actually serve the good.
  • Martin Luther King Jr. (1929–1968): In his "Letter from Birmingham Jail" (1963), written while imprisoned for participating in nonviolent civil rights protests in Birmingham, Alabama, King brought this tradition into the modern world. He directly invoked Augustine and Aquinas:
    • "One has not only a legal but a moral responsibility to obey just laws. Conversely, one has a moral responsibility to disobey unjust laws."
    • King also invoked Socrates as an early practitioner of civil disobedience — but noted a crucial difference: Socrates argued that one must always obey the law or leave the city, while King argued that when a law is unjust, disobedience is not only permitted but required, provided it is nonviolent and the person is willing to accept the legal punishment.
  • The New Testament precedent: When the apostles Peter and John were ordered by the Sanhedrin to stop preaching about Jesus, they replied: "We ought to obey God rather than men" (Acts 5:29). This is the foundation of the Christian tradition of conscientious objection: when human law directly contradicts divine law, the Christian must obey God even at the cost of punishment.

The Literary Legacy of Prosopopoeia

The Crito's most original feature — giving speech to the Laws of Athens — had a long and remarkable afterlife.

  • Boethius (c. 477–524 AD): A Roman senator and philosopher who was unjustly imprisoned and sentenced to death in a situation remarkably like Socrates' own. In his Consolation of Philosophy (c. 524 AD) — one of the most widely read books in medieval Europe — Boethius is visited in his prison cell by a figure called Lady Philosophy, who argues with him about fortune, happiness, and the nature of God. Just as the Laws spoke to Socrates, Lady Philosophy speaks to Boethius. The echo is deliberate; Boethius knew his Plato.
  • Medieval allegory: The device of giving abstract ideas human voices appears throughout medieval literature, including the Romance of the Rose, where abstract qualities such as Reason, Love, and Jealousy appear as speaking characters.
  • Dante's Divine Comedy: The poet Virgil serves as guide through the afterlife — another figure who speaks with an authority beyond his literal person.
  • The simple act of giving a voice to the Laws of Athens opened a literary tradition that lasted for over a thousand years.

Review Questions

  1. Crito offers four arguments for why Socrates should escape. Which argument do you find most compelling, and how does Socrates respond to it? Do you think Socrates' response is adequate?
  2. Explain Socrates' "trainer analogy." What point is he trying to make about whose opinions we should follow when making moral decisions? What are the strengths and weaknesses of this analogy?
  3. The Laws of Athens argue that Socrates made an implicit "social contract" by choosing to live in Athens for seventy years. What does this contract require of him, and why? How would John Locke respond to this argument?
  4. Thrasymachus claims that "justice is nothing but the interest of the stronger." If this view is correct, what would it mean for Socrates' decision to stay in prison? Do you think Thrasymachus is right? Why or why not?
  5. Compare Socrates' position in the Crito with Martin Luther King Jr.'s position in the "Letter from Birmingham Jail." Both men faced unjust legal systems. How did each respond, and what principle drove each man's decision? Whose position do you find more persuasive?
