Study Guide: Chapter 2 - Euthyphro

Overview

In the Euthyphro, Socrates encounters a man who claims to be an expert on piety — and subjects that claim to rigorous philosophical cross-examination. Through five failed definitions, the dialogue exposes the difficulty of defining abstract moral concepts and introduces the Euthyphro Dilemma, one of the most consequential questions in the history of philosophy. The chapter also traces how this dilemma gave rise to Plato's Theory of Forms and has continued to shape debates in theology and ethics for over 2,400 years.

Key Concepts

  • The Socratic Demand for Definitions: Socrates insists on a universal definition — a statement of the essential nature shared by all instances of a concept — not mere examples. This distinction between an example and a definition is the engine of the entire dialogue and one of Socrates' greatest contributions to philosophy.
  • The Euthyphro Dilemma: The pivotal question Socrates poses: Is something pious because the gods love it, or do the gods love it because it is pious? This dilemma forces a choice between two troubling positions: either there is a moral standard above the gods (Horn 1), or morality is whatever the gods arbitrarily happen to approve (Horn 2).
  • Aporia as Productive Puzzlement: The dialogue ends without a definition of piety. Rather than a failure, this state of honest uncertainty (aporia) is, for Socrates, the true beginning of philosophical thinking — more valuable than the false confidence Euthyphro had at the start.
  • The Theory of Forms: Plato's response to the problem that defeated Euthyphro. Behind every general concept (piety, justice, beauty) there exists a perfect, eternal, unchanging Form. Particular acts are pious because they participate in the Form of Piety; the gods love pious things because those things participate in the Form of the Good.
  • The Divided Line: An image from the Republic describing four ascending levels of human understanding — from shadows and reflections, through physical objects and mathematical reasoning, up to philosophical knowledge of the Forms. Euthyphro, stuck in unreflective opinion, never makes it past the lowest levels.
  • Divine Command Theory vs. Natural Law: Two theological responses to the Euthyphro Dilemma that have shaped centuries of Christian thought. Divine Command Theory (Horn 2) grounds morality entirely in God's will; Natural Law Theory (Horn 1, as developed by Aquinas) holds that God's commands reflect an objective moral order rooted in God's own nature.

Important Terms

  • Piety (to hosion): "The holy" or "the pious" in Greek; acting in accordance with what the divine law requires. Socrates' central question in the dialogue is what makes all pious acts pious.
  • Impiety (asebeia): A failure to honor the gods properly; a criminal offense in Athens that could carry the death penalty. The formal charge brought against Socrates by Meletus.
  • Elenchus (elenchos): Socrates' method of philosophical cross-examination. He draws out his interlocutor's beliefs, then uses questioning to reveal contradictions or inadequacies. The goal is not to win an argument but to move closer to truth.
  • Aporia: A Greek term meaning "no passage" or "puzzlement." The state of genuine intellectual impasse reached when a question you thought you understood proves more complex than expected. Many Socratic dialogues end in aporia.
  • Theophiles: "Dear to the gods" — the quality Euthyphro invokes in Definitions 2 and 3 of piety. Socrates argues this describes a feature pious things happen to have, not the essential nature of piety.
  • Euthyphro Dilemma: The question: Is something pious (or good) because the gods love (or command) it, or do the gods love (or command) it because it is pious (or good)? A central problem in the philosophy of religion and ethics.
  • Forms / Eide (eidos): Plato's term for the perfect, eternal, unchanging realities behind general concepts. Particular things in the world are imperfect copies of these Forms. The Form of the Good is the supreme Form, the source of all reality and knowledge.
  • Theory of Forms: Plato's metaphysical theory that abstract, eternal Forms (Justice, Beauty, Goodness, Piety) are more real than physical objects. True knowledge is knowledge of the Forms; perception of physical things yields only opinion.
  • Doxa: Greek for "opinion." In Plato's epistemology, the kind of awareness we have of the physical world — changeable, unreliable, and inferior to true knowledge.
  • Episteme: Greek for "knowledge." In Plato, the certain grasp of eternal truths, especially the Forms, achieved through reason and dialectic.
  • Divine Command Theory (DCT): The ethical theory that moral obligations are grounded entirely in God's commands. An action is right if and only if God commands it. Associated with William of Ockham.
  • Natural Law: The moral law discoverable by human reason, which Aquinas defined as "the rational creature's participation in the eternal law." God commands the good because it is good, and that goodness is built into the nature of creation.
  • Moral Realism: The view that moral facts (e.g., "murder is wrong") exist objectively, independently of what anyone thinks or commands.
  • Voluntarism: The view that moral truths are grounded in will — either God's will (theological voluntarism) or human will.
  • King Archon (archon basileus): The Athenian magistrate responsible for cases involving religion and homicide, outside whose court the dialogue takes place.
  • Mantis (diviner): A person who claims to interpret the will of the gods through signs, dreams, or inspired speech. Euthyphro's profession.
  • Petitio principii ("begging the question"): A circular argument that assumes the very thing it is supposed to prove. Euthyphro's fifth definition commits this error.

Detailed Notes

The Setting and Dramatic Context

  • The dialogue takes place outside the Porch of the King Archon in Athens — the court where religious and homicide cases were heard.
  • Socrates has been summoned to answer a formal indictment by a young poet named Meletus, charging him with:
    • Impiety (asebeia) — failure to honor the gods of Athens
    • Corrupting the minds of young people
    • Both charges could carry the death penalty; Athens had prosecuted intellectuals before (Anaxagoras was exiled for teaching the sun was a hot stone; Protagoras had his books burned).
  • Euthyphro has come to the same court for a startling reason: he is prosecuting his own father for murder.
    • A hired laborer on the family's estate in Naxos killed a household slave in a drunken fight.
    • Euthyphro's father bound the killer, threw him in a ditch, and sent to Athens for religious guidance. The man died before the messenger returned.
    • Euthyphro's own relatives think the prosecution is outrageous; Euthyphro insists it is pious.
  • The irony is deliberate: Socrates, charged with impiety, meets a man who claims expert knowledge of piety — and who is willing to prosecute his own father in its name.
  • Dramatic arc: This dialogue takes place before Socrates' trial; the Apology covers the trial itself; the Crito takes place in prison afterward. Together they form a continuous narrative.

The Characters

  • Euthyphro: A professional religious expert and diviner (mantis) who interprets omens and claims to understand the gods' wishes.
    • His name in Greek (euthys + phron) means "straight-thinker" or "right-minded one" — likely an ironic choice by Plato.
    • He is confident to the point of self-importance, holding views many educated Athenians shared.
    • His failure is not stupidity but a lack of self-examination — he has never questioned his own assumptions.
  • Socrates: The questioner, approaching Euthyphro with apparent admiration. "Teach me what piety is," he says — using Socratic irony to draw Euthyphro into cross-examination.
    • Beneath his polite surface lies the elenchus: the method of posing a thesis and then asking questions to reveal its contradictions.

The Central Question: What Is Piety?

  • Socrates asks for a definition of piety — not examples of pious acts, but the essential quality that makes all pious things pious.
  • The key distinction — Example vs. Definition:
    • An example identifies a particular instance: "Prosecuting wrongdoers is pious."
    • A definition identifies the essential nature shared by all instances: "Piety is that quality which..."
    • Only a definition can serve as a reliable standard for judging new cases.
    • Socrates' demand for universal definitions is one of his greatest philosophical contributions and eventually leads Plato to develop the Theory of Forms.

The Five Definitions of Piety

  • Definition 1: Piety is what I am doing now (prosecuting wrongdoers)

    • Euthyphro points to his own action as the example of piety, appealing to the myth of Zeus punishing his father Kronos as justification.
    • Socrates' objection: This is an example, not a definition. There are many pious acts unrelated to prosecution. What do they all have in common?
    • Note: Socrates is not saying Euthyphro's prosecution is wrong — only that this particular act cannot define piety for all cases.

  • Definition 2: Piety is what is dear to the gods (theophiles)

    • An improvement — it offers a general principle, not a single example.
    • Socrates' objection: The Greek gods notoriously disagree (Ares loves war, Aphrodite loves beauty, Athena loves wisdom). If piety is what the gods love, and gods love different things, then the same action could be both pious and impious simultaneously. This is a contradiction.

  • Definition 3: Piety is what all the gods love unanimously

    • Euthyphro adjusts: piety is what every god agrees in approving; impiety is what every god agrees in disapproving.
    • This avoids the contradiction of Definition 2 by requiring divine consensus.
    • Socrates' objection: The Euthyphro Dilemma. He asks: "Is the pious loved by the gods because it is pious, or is it pious because it is loved by the gods?"
      • Horn 1: The gods love pious things because they are pious. Piety has an independent nature; the gods recognize it but do not create it. This implies a standard of goodness above the gods.
      • Horn 2: Pious things are pious because the gods love them. The gods' approval makes something pious. If they changed their minds, what is pious would change too — morality becomes arbitrary.
    • Socrates argues that "being loved by the gods" is a quality pious things happen to have (an effect), not the essence of piety (the cause). The gods' love must be a response to something already in pious things — and that "something" is the real definition still undiscovered.

  • Definition 4: Piety is the part of justice that involves service to the gods

    • Euthyphro tries a new approach: piety is a subset of justice — specifically, the care we owe to the gods (as opposed to the care we owe to other humans).
    • Socrates presses: What does pious service to the gods accomplish? Every service has a goal (doctors aim at health, shipbuilders at ships). What great work do the gods accomplish with the help of human piety?
    • Euthyphro cannot answer clearly — he says vaguely that piety involves things "gratifying" to the gods, but cannot name the gods' larger project. The definition stalls.

  • Definition 5: Piety is a kind of sacred transaction with the gods

    • Euthyphro's final attempt: humans give the gods honor, reverence, and sacrifice; the gods give blessings and protection in return — a sacred exchange.
    • Socrates asks: what do the gods actually receive from our sacrifices? They do not need our food or drink. If the answer is simply that our gifts "please" or are "dear to" them, then this definition has collapsed back into Definition 3 — piety is what is dear to the gods.
    • The argument has traveled in a complete circle (petitio principii — begging the question).

The Aporetic Ending and What It Means

  • At this point, Euthyphro excuses himself and leaves. Socrates remains outside the court, still without a definition of piety, still facing the charge of impiety.
  • Why this is not a failure:
    • At the start, Euthyphro was certain he knew what piety was. By the end, five definitions have been tested and found wanting.
    • The dialogue has revealed that piety is far more complex than it seems, and that everyday confidence in moral concepts is less secure than we assume.
    • Aporia — the recognition that you do not know something you thought you understood — is the beginning of real thinking, not its end.
    • Euthyphro walks away unchanged; Socrates stays, puzzled and still searching. Philosophy asks us to be more like Socrates.
  • Three takeaways from the dialogue:
    1. The difference between examples and definitions matters — a universal definition is needed to judge new cases reliably.
    2. The Euthyphro Dilemma is real and enduring — it has shaped 2,400 years of moral philosophy and theology.
    3. Aporia is a gift, not a defeat — honest uncertainty is more valuable than false confidence.

Plato's Theory of Forms: The Answer the Dialogue Implies

  • The question Euthyphro could not answer haunted Plato for the rest of his life, and his answer became one of the most influential ideas in Western thought.
  • The logic of Forms:
    • Socrates needed a universal — a single quality shared by every pious act in every time and place.
    • This quality cannot be any particular pious act (acts come and go) or any opinion (opinions change).
    • It must be stable, unchanging, and independent of any particular example.
    • Plato concluded that such universals really exist. He called them eide (Forms or Ideas): perfect, eternal realities of which particular things are imperfect copies.
  • The Forms:
    • There is a Form of Piety, a Form of Justice, a Form of Beauty, and above them all, the Form of the Good.
    • Particular pious acts are pious because they participate in or imitate the Form of Piety.
    • The Form is the original; particular acts are copies.
    • True knowledge (episteme) is knowledge of the Forms. The physical world yields only opinion (doxa).
  • How Forms resolve the Euthyphro Dilemma:
    • The gods love pious things because those things participate in the Form of the Good — not the other way around.
    • The first horn of the dilemma is correct: there is a standard of goodness above any will, human or divine.
    • But the standard is not mysterious or arbitrary — it is the Form of the Good, knowable by reason.

The Sun Analogy and the Divided Line (from the Republic)

  • In the Republic, Plato develops the Theory of Forms far beyond what the Euthyphro hints at.
  • The Sun Analogy (Republic, Book VI):
    • The Form of the Good is to the intelligible world what the sun is to the visible world.
    • The sun provides light (making seeing possible) and life (making growing possible).
    • The Form of the Good provides truth (making knowing possible) and being (making existing possible).
    • The Good is not just another Form among many — it is the source and ground of all reality, "far exceeding essence in dignity and power."
  • The Divided Line (Republic, Book VI):
    • Plato asks us to imagine a line divided into four segments, each representing a level of awareness, from lowest to highest:
      1. Shadows and reflections (eikasia) — The lowest level: mistaking images for reality, taking appearances at face value.
      2. Physical objects (pistis) — Ordinary experience of visible, changeable things; better than shadows, but still limited.
      3. Mathematical reasoning (dianoia) — The mind works with abstract concepts (numbers, geometric forms) but still relies on hypotheses and visible diagrams.
      4. Philosophical understanding (noesis) — The highest level: the mind grasps the Forms directly through dialectic, ascending to the Form of the Good.
    • Euthyphro, stuck in unreflective opinion, was at the lowest levels. Socrates was trying to pull him upward — but Euthyphro left before the ascent could begin.

The Euthyphro Dilemma in Christian Theology

  • When Christianity inherited both the Jewish tradition of one God and Greek philosophy, the Euthyphro Dilemma arrived with new force.
  • For polytheistic Greeks, the dilemma was partly about divine disagreement. For Christians, divine disagreement disappears — but the deeper problem remains: Is an action good because God commands it, or does God command it because it is good?
  • Horn 2 — Divine Command Theory:
    • Most famously defended by William of Ockham (c. 1287–1347), English Franciscan philosopher.
    • Morality just is God's will. God does not conform to an external standard; He establishes the standard by commanding it.
    • Ockham argued this does not make morality arbitrary, because God's nature is perfectly good.
    • Critics: if "goodness" just means "whatever God commands," then saying "God is good" reduces to the empty tautology "God does what God commands."
  • Horn 1 — Natural Law Theory:
    • Most famously defended by Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274), Italian Dominican theologian and philosopher.
    • God commands the good because it is good — but God is not subject to an external law. Rather, God's own nature is the standard of goodness.
    • The natural law is a reflection of God's eternal reason, discoverable by human reason.
    • Aquinas' elegant solution: the dilemma is false, because God's will and God's nature are not two separate things. He is the standard. His commands flow from what He is — and what He is, is Goodness itself (the doctrine of divine simplicity).

The Euthyphro Dilemma in Modern Philosophy

  • Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646–1716): Restated the dilemma in his Discourse on Metaphysics (1686). If goodness is simply what God wills, then praising God as "good" is meaningless. Sided with Horn 1: God wills the good because it is good, and His perfect intellect recognizes it infallibly.
  • Soren Kierkegaard (1813–1855): Examined the biblical story of Abraham and Isaac in Fear and Trembling (1843). If morality is independent of God's will (Horn 1), God's command to sacrifice Isaac was immoral — yet Abraham obeyed and was praised. Kierkegaard proposed a "teleological suspension of the ethical": faith may sometimes require acting against the universal moral law. This is Horn 2 taken with terrifying seriousness.
  • Contemporary philosophy: The dilemma continues to shape debates between moral realists (who hold that moral truths exist independently of any will) and voluntarists (who ground morality in some form of will or command). The debate remains unresolved — but the terms are clearer and the arguments more refined, thanks to this dialogue written twenty-four centuries ago.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the difference between an example and a definition as Socrates uses those terms. Why does Socrates insist on a definition rather than accepting Euthyphro's first answer, and why does this matter for all philosophical and scientific thinking?
  2. State the Euthyphro Dilemma in your own words, and explain why both horns create serious problems. Which horn do you find more troubling, and why?
  3. How does Plato's Theory of Forms attempt to answer the problem raised by the Euthyphro? In what sense does it resolve the Euthyphro Dilemma, and what new questions does it raise?
  4. Compare the responses of Thomas Aquinas (Natural Law) and William of Ockham (Divine Command Theory) to the Euthyphro Dilemma. How does Aquinas claim to escape both horns of the dilemma, and do you find his solution convincing?
  5. The dialogue ends in aporia — Socrates has no definition of piety and still faces a charge of impiety. Is the conversation a failure? Using specific evidence from the chapter, explain why Socrates (and Plato) would say it is not.
