Study Guide: The Philosophical Journey and the Examined Life

This study guide is designed to synthesize the key concepts, further readings, and interdisciplinary connections presented in the conclusion of the study of Plato’s early dialogues (Euthyphro, Apology, and Crito). It provides a framework for understanding how these foundational ideas extend into later Platonic works, broader philosophical traditions, and various academic disciplines.

Part 1: Key Concepts and Further Reading

The Works of Plato

Following the initial study of Socrates’ trials and final days, the following works represent the "next steps" in understanding Platonic thought:

  • Meno: Explores whether virtue can be taught. It introduces the concept of recollection, suggesting the soul already possesses truth and requires questioning to draw it out. A key scene involves Socrates guiding a slave boy through a geometric proof.
  • Phaedo: Details the final hours of Socrates' life. It focuses on the immortality of the soul and arguments for its survival after death, serving as a philosophical conclusion to the narrative started in the Apology and Crito.
  • Republic: Plato’s most ambitious work, consisting of ten books. It seeks to define justice, outlines an ideal city, introduces the Theory of Forms, presents the Allegory of the Cave, and argues for the necessity of philosopher-kings.

Philosophical Traditions Beyond Plato

The Socratic influence extends through various philosophers who adapted or challenged his ideas:

Author Work Key Themes
Aristotle Nicomachean Ethics Focuses on eudaimonia (happiness) as an activity of the soul in accordance with virtue; more practical than Plato.
Marcus Aurelius Meditations Stoic reflections on duty, death, and self-control; written as a private journal in the Socratic spirit.
Augustine Confessions A synthesis of Greek philosophy and Christian theology; explores the journey from restlessness to truth.
Boethius The Consolation of Philosophy A dialogue with "Lady Philosophy" written in prison; explores fortune and God's goodness, mirroring Socrates' calm in the Crito.

Part 2: Interdisciplinary Connections

Philosophy is not an isolated subject but a foundational element of other disciplines:

  • Literature: Philosophical questions regarding justice and truth appear in the works of Shakespeare (Hamlet) and Dostoevsky (the Euthyphro dilemma).
  • History: Philosophical ideas fueled the American Revolution (Plato, Aristotle, Cicero), the French Revolution (Enlightenment philosophy), and the civil rights movement (natural law).
  • Theology: The dialogue between philosophy and theology has lasted two millennia, influencing thinkers like Aquinas, Calvin, and the early Church Fathers.
  • Science: Modern science relies on philosophical assumptions about the reliability of reason and the intelligibility of nature, originating from the pre-Socratics and Plato’s mathematical focus.

Part 3: The Examined Life

Socrates famously stated, "The unexamined life is not worth living." Based on his example, the "examined life" is characterized by:

  1. Conversation: Engaging with others to test assumptions.
  2. Honesty: A willingness to admit when one is wrong.
  3. Courage: The strength to ask uncomfortable questions.
  4. Humility: The recognition that wisdom begins with acknowledging one's own ignorance.

Part 4: Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What is the central question explored in Plato's Meno?
  2. How does the Phaedo complete the story arc of the Apology and Crito?
  3. Define eudaimonia as used by Aristotle.
  4. Why is Marcus Aurelius' Meditations considered to be in the "Socratic spirit"?
  5. In what way does Boethius’ The Consolation of Philosophy mirror the Crito?
  6. According to the text, what are the three philosophical assumptions that science relies upon but cannot prove?
  7. What does the "recollection" theory in the Meno suggest about the nature of learning?
  8. Identify two historical movements mentioned that were fueled by philosophical ideas.

Part 5: Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Evolution of the Soul: Compare the presentation of the soul in the Apology (where Socrates faces death) with the arguments for the immortality of the soul presented in the Phaedo. How does the deeper philosophical development in the Phaedo change the reader's perspective on Socrates' fate?
  2. The Practice of Justice: Using the Republic and the Nicomachean Ethics as references, discuss the shift from Plato’s idealist search for justice to Aristotle’s practical search for the "good life." Which approach is more applicable to the challenges of modern living?
  3. Philosophy as Solace: Analyze the role of philosophy in times of suffering, using the Meditations of Marcus Aurelius and Boethius’ The Consolation of Philosophy. How does the Socratic tradition provide a framework for individuals facing death or imprisonment?
  4. The Interdisciplinary Thread: Choose one of the four disciplines mentioned (Literature, History, Theology, or Science) and argue why a grounding in Platonic philosophy is essential for a complete understanding of that field. Use specific examples from the source text.

Part 6: Glossary of Important Terms

  • Allegory of the Cave: A famous narrative in Plato's Republic used to illustrate his philosophical ideas (related to the Theory of Forms).
  • Eudaimonia: A Greek term for happiness, defined by Aristotle as an activity of the soul in accordance with virtue.
  • Examined Life: A practice of questioning, reflecting, and seeking truth through conversation, honesty, courage, and humility.
  • Natural Law Theory: A philosophical concept that influenced the civil rights movement.
  • Philosopher-Kings: The ideal rulers proposed by Plato in the Republic.
  • Recollection: The theory in the Meno that learning is actually the soul remembering truths it already knows.
  • Socratic Method: The practice of questioning and testing assumptions to pursue truth, as demonstrated by Socrates throughout the dialogues.
  • Stoic Philosophy: A school of thought focused on duty, self-control, and impermanence, exemplified by Marcus Aurelius.
  • Theory of Forms: A central Platonic doctrine developed in the Republic concerning the nature of reality.
