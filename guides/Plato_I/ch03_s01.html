Study Guide: The Trial of Socrates and Plato’s Apology

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the trial of Socrates in 399 BC, as documented in Plato’s Apology. It explores the historical context of the Athenian legal system, the nature of the accusations against Socrates, his philosophical defense, and the enduring themes of his final speeches.

I. Key Concepts and Themes

The Athenian Legal System

The trial took place in a dikasterion, a law court composed of 501 citizen jurors. The system lacked professional judges or lawyers. Accusers and defendants spoke for themselves, with time strictly managed by a klepsydra (water clock). Decisions were made via an immediate vote using bronze tokens, with no deliberation among jurors. If a defendant was convicted, a second phase of the trial determined the penalty, where the jury chose between a penalty proposed by the accuser and a counter-penalty proposed by the defendant.

Socratic Ignorance

Socrates’ wisdom was rooted in a paradox. Following the Oracle at Delphi’s claim that no one was wiser than him, Socrates discovered that while others (politicians, poets, and craftsmen) claimed to have deep knowledge they did not possess, he alone recognized his own lack of wisdom. His famous realization—"I know that I know nothing"—serves as the starting point for genuine inquiry rather than a conclusion of despair.

The "Gadfly" Metaphor

Socrates described himself as a gadfly (myops) sent by the god to sting a "great but sluggish horse" (Athens) into wakefulness. He viewed his role not as a teacher providing answers, but as an irritant who provoked citizens into examining their own beliefs to prevent the city from falling into a state of self-satisfied moral sleep.

The Examined Life

The central tenet of Socratic philosophy is that "the unexamined life is not worth living for a human being." Socrates argued that a life without philosophical self-scrutiny and the testing of one’s values is not a fully human existence. For Socrates, philosophy is a necessary activity of honesty and courage, rather than a mere intellectual hobby.

Philosophy vs. Rhetoric

Socrates drew a sharp distinction between his plain speech (parrhesia) and the polished rhetoric of the Sophists and orators. While orators sought to manipulate the jury’s emotions through flattery and appeals to pity, Socrates insisted on using straightforward language to seek the truth. He believed a juror’s duty was to judge based on facts and justice, not to grant favors based on emotional performance.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. Who were the "old accusers," and why did Socrates consider them more dangerous than the "new accusers"? The old accusers were years of gossip, suspicion, and comic ridicule, most notably the playwright Aristophanes. They were dangerous because their caricatures of Socrates had been absorbed by the jurors since childhood, and a rumor cannot be cross-examined.

2. What were the two formal charges brought against Socrates by the "new accusers"? He was charged with corrupting the young and with impiety (asebeia)—specifically, not believing in the city's gods and introducing new divinities.

3. How did the Oracle at Delphi set Socrates on his philosophical mission? The Oracle told Socrates’ friend Chaerephon that no one was wiser than Socrates. Puzzled because he knew he lacked knowledge, Socrates began questioning those with reputations for wisdom to test the Oracle’s meaning, eventually realizing his wisdom lay in acknowledging his own ignorance.

4. What was the daimonion, and what role did it play in Socrates’ life? The daimonion was an inner divine voice or sign that warned Socrates when he was about to do something wrong. During his trial, its silence was interpreted by Socrates as a sign that his actions and the resulting death sentence were not bad for him.

5. How did Socrates use the example of horse training to refute Meletus' charge of corrupting the youth? Socrates pointed out the absurdity of Meletus’ claim that all Athenians improve the young while only Socrates corrupts them. He argued that in every other field, like horse training, only a few specialists do good, while the majority are likely to do harm.

6. What was Socrates' counter-proposal for his penalty after being found guilty? He initially proposed that he should receive free meals at the Prytaneum, an honor typically reserved for Olympic victors and war heroes, because his service to the city was more valuable than a sports victory.

7. Why did Socrates refuse to bring his family into court to plead for mercy? He believed it was a violation of justice to use emotional manipulation to sway the jury. He argued that a defendant should convince the jury through truth and argument, not by arousing pity.

8. What are the two possibilities Socrates envisioned for death? Socrates posited that death is either annihilation (a dreamless, restful sleep) or a migration of the soul to another place where one can question the great heroes and thinkers of the past.

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Conflict of Inquiry and Stability: Socrates viewed himself as a necessary "gadfly" to the state, yet his questioning of traditional values led to his execution. Discuss the tension between the philosopher's commitment to relentless inquiry and the city's need for social and political stability. Was the jury's decision a result of mere prejudice, or did they have legitimate concerns about the impact of Socrates’ methods on Athenian society?

2. The Logic of Socratic Ignorance: Analyze the concept of Socratic Ignorance. How does the admission of knowing nothing serve as a foundation for his cross-examination (elenchus) of others? Explain how this stance distinguishes Socrates from both the "Natural Philosophers" and the "Sophists" mentioned in the text.

3. Integrity in the Face of Death: Compare Socrates' behavior during the trial with the expected behavior of an Athenian defendant. Why did Socrates choose a path of defiance (such as the Prytaneum proposal) rather than a path of compromise? To what extent was his death a deliberate "philosophical statement" rather than an unavoidable tragedy?

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Asebeia Impiety; one of the formal charges against Socrates involving the rejection of city gods.
Daimonion An inner divine voice or sign that warned Socrates against making specific mistakes.
Dikasterion An Athenian law court composed of citizen jurors; it operated without a judge or professional lawyers.
Elenchus The Socratic method of cross-examination used to uncover contradictions in an opponent’s beliefs.
Klepsydra A water clock used to time speeches in the Athenian courtroom.
Myops The Greek word for "gadfly"; used by Socrates to describe his provocative role in Athens.
Natural Philosophy Speculation about the physical world and heavenly bodies; a field Socrates explicitly distanced himself from.
Parrhesia "Frank speech"; the claim to speak openly, plainly, and without rhetorical artifice.
Prytaneum Athens' state dining hall where war heroes and Olympic victors were honored with free meals.
Sophistry The practice of professional teachers who charged fees to teach rhetoric and the art of winning arguments regardless of truth.
The Clouds A comedy by Aristophanes that caricatured Socrates as a charlatan, contributing to his "old accusations."
