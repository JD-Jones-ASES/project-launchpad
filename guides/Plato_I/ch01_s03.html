Study Guide: Socrates and Plato

Overview

This section introduces Socrates and Plato, two foundational figures who revolutionized ancient Greek philosophy. Socrates, who wrote nothing, transformed philosophical inquiry from speculation about the natural world to a deep investigation into human ethics and how to live. His student, Plato, documented Socrates' methods and developed his own complex philosophical systems through the unique literary form of dialogues, shaping the course of Western thought for centuries.

Key Concepts

  • Transformation of Philosophy: Socrates and Plato shifted philosophical focus from the pre-Socratics' speculation about nature to an investigation of human life, ethics, and how one ought to live.
  • The Socratic Problem: The historical challenge of distinguishing between the actual beliefs and teachings of Socrates and the philosophical ideas Plato attributed to him in his dialogues. Scholars often agree that early dialogues are closer to the historical Socrates.
  • The Socratic Method (Elenchus): Socrates' unique method of philosophical inquiry, involving persistent questioning, testing definitions, and seeking contradictions or counterexamples to expose false beliefs and lead interlocutors to admit their ignorance, thus moving closer to truth.
  • Maieutike (Midwifery): Socrates' metaphor for his philosophical role, where he saw himself as helping others "give birth" to their own ideas rather than producing ideas himself.
  • Plato's Dialogues: Plato's chosen literary form for presenting his philosophy, which captures philosophy as a living activity of questioning and discovery rather than a fixed set of doctrines.
  • Aporia: A state of intellectual puzzlement or perplexity, often the inconclusive ending of Plato's early Socratic dialogues. Socrates and Plato considered aporia philosophically valuable as the first step toward genuine understanding by recognizing one's own ignorance.
  • Periods of Plato's Dialogues: Scholars divide Plato's works into early (aporetic, close to historical Socrates), middle (Socrates advances Plato's own theories like the Theory of Forms and the philosopher-king), and late (more technical and self-critical).
  • Disillusionment with Athenian Democracy: Plato's profound disappointment with the Athenian democratic system, particularly after it condemned his mentor Socrates to death, which heavily influenced his political philosophy.
  • The Academy: The institution of higher learning founded by Plato in Athens, one of the first in the Western world, which operated for nearly 900 years.

Important Terms

  • Socrates (c. 470–399 BC): Athenian philosopher, father of Western philosophy, known for his Socratic method, famously wrote nothing, and was executed by the Athenian state.
  • Plato (c. 428–348 BC): Athenian philosopher, student of Socrates, founder of the Academy, and author of influential philosophical dialogues featuring Socrates.
  • Socratic Problem: The scholarly difficulty in discerning the true historical teachings of Socrates from Plato's literary portrayal.
  • Elenchus: The Greek term for Socrates' method of "cross-examination" or "refutation," involving questioning to expose contradictions in beliefs.
  • Interlocutor: A person who takes part in a dialogue or conversation; in Plato's dialogues, these are the individuals Socrates engages with.
  • Maieutike: "The art of midwifery," Socrates' self-description of his philosophical method of helping others develop their own ideas.
  • Peloponnesian War (431–404 BC): A major conflict between Athens and Sparta, which provides a significant historical context for Socrates' life and trial.
  • Agora: The central public space in ancient Greek city-states, serving as a marketplace and civic hub, where Socrates conducted many of his philosophical discussions.
  • Xenophon, Aristophanes, Aristotle: Other ancient authors whose writings provide some insight into the life and philosophy of Socrates, alongside Plato.
  • Aporia: Literally "without a path"; a state of perplexity or impasse that often concludes Plato's early dialogues, intended to stimulate further thought.
  • The Academy: The school founded by Plato in Athens around 387 BC, dedicated to philosophical and scientific inquiry.
  • King Archon: An Athenian magistrate responsible for religious affairs and cases involving impiety, before whom Socrates' charges were formally brought.
  • Meletus, Anytus, Lycon: The three primary accusers who brought charges of impiety and corrupting the youth against Socrates.
  • Euthyphro: One of Plato's early dialogues, depicting Socrates discussing the nature of piety with a self-proclaimed religious expert.
  • Apology: Plato's account of Socrates' defense speech during his trial before an Athenian jury.
  • Crito: A Platonic dialogue where Socrates discusses whether he should escape from prison with his friend Crito.
  • Phaedo: A Platonic dialogue that describes Socrates' final hours, his arguments for the immortality of the soul, and his death by hemlock.
  • Hemlock: A poisonous plant used as the method of capital punishment in Athens, which Socrates drank.

Detailed Notes

Socrates: The Man Who Knew Nothing

  • Biography:
    • Born in Athens around 470 BC.
    • Son of Sophroniscus (stonemason) and Phaenarete (midwife); the latter inspired his "maieutike" metaphor for philosophy.
    • Served with distinction as a soldier in the Peloponnesian War, noted for physical endurance.
    • Spent his civilian life in the agora and gymnasia, engaging in philosophical conversations.
    • Famously ugly, poor, married to Xanthippe with three sons.
    • Executed by the Athenian state in 399 BC at about age 70.
  • The Socratic Problem:
    • Socrates never wrote anything; all knowledge of him comes from others (Plato primarily, but also Xenophon, Aristophanes, and Aristotle).
    • This poses a challenge: distinguishing the historical Socrates from the character Plato created in his dialogues.
    • Scholarly consensus suggests early Platonic dialogues are closer to the historical Socrates, while later ones increasingly reflect Plato's own ideas.
    • For readers, the focus should be on the soundness of the arguments presented by the character of Socrates, rather than strict historical accuracy.

The Socratic Method (Elenchus)

  • Nature of the Method:
    • Unlike pre-Socratics who made grand claims, Socrates primarily asked questions.
    • The process begins when an interlocutor claims to know a concept (e.g., courage, justice, piety).
    • Socrates asks for a definition, then rigorously tests it with further questions, seeking contradictions or counterexamples.
    • Definitions almost invariably fail, leading to revision and further testing.
    • The conversation often ends with the interlocutor admitting their original ignorance.
  • Purpose of Elenchus:
    • To "cross-examine" or "refute" false beliefs.
    • Goal is not humiliation, but to strip away mistaken certainties and move closer to genuine truth.
    • It is a method of intellectual purification, making individuals aware of their lack of knowledge.
  • Examples in Key Dialogues:
    • Euthyphro: Examines definitions of piety.
    • Apology: Socrates explains and defends his practice of the method.
    • Crito: Applies the method to the question of whether he should escape prison.

Plato: The Student Who Became the Teacher

  • Biography:
    • Born into an aristocratic Athenian family around 428 BC.
    • Became a devoted follower of Socrates.
    • Socrates' execution (399 BC) was a defining event, leading to Plato's disillusionment with Athenian democracy.
    • Traveled widely, then founded The Academy in Athens (c. 387 BC), a pioneering institution of higher learning.
    • Spent his life teaching and writing over thirty dialogues, covering a vast range of philosophical topics.
    • Died around 348 BC at approximately eighty years of age.
  • Plato's Literary Form: The Dialogue:
    • Chose dialogues over treatises to convey philosophy.
    • Partly a tribute to Socrates; also a philosophical choice reflecting that philosophy is a "living activity" of questioning and discovery, not static doctrine.
  • Periods of Plato's Dialogues:
    • Early Dialogues: Short, dramatic, often ending in aporia (puzzlement) where no satisfactory answer is reached. Examples: Euthyphro, Apology, Crito. The value of aporia is intellectual humility – recognizing ignorance is the first step to true understanding.
    • Middle Dialogues: Longer, more ambitious, Socrates begins to articulate positive theories like the Theory of Forms, the immortality of the soul, and the concept of the philosopher-king. Examples: Republic, Phaedo, Symposium.
    • Late Dialogues: More technical, self-critical, Plato revisits and refines earlier theories, and Socrates may play a less central role. Examples: Sophist, Statesman, Laws.

The Dramatic Setting: Three Dialogues, One Story

  • Narrative Arc: The Euthyphro, Apology, and Crito form a connected narrative taking place over a few weeks in 399 BC, telling the story of Socrates' trial and death.
  • The Indictment: Charges brought against Socrates by Meletus, Anytus, and Lycon for impiety (not believing in the city's gods, introducing new divine beings) and corrupting the youth.
  • The Euthyphro: Socrates, on his way to his preliminary hearing, meets Euthyphro and discusses the nature of piety. This dialogue is dramatically ironic given Socrates' own indictment for impiety.
  • The Trial (Apology): Socrates defends himself before a jury of 501 Athenian citizens, is found guilty (280-221 vote), and sentenced to death.
  • The Imprisonment (Crito): While awaiting execution, Socrates refuses his wealthy friend Crito's offer to help him escape, arguing that breaking the law—even an unjust one—is morally wrong.
  • The Execution: Socrates drinks hemlock and dies, a scene described in the Phaedo.
  • Urgency of the Questions: The impending death of Socrates imbues the philosophical questions about piety, justice, and obedience to law with intense, real-world urgency, making them far from abstract exercises.

Review Questions

  1. What is the "Socratic problem," and why is it challenging for scholars trying to understand Socrates' actual philosophy? How does the textbook suggest readers approach this problem?
  2. Describe the core process of the Socratic method (elenchus). What is its primary goal, and how does it differ from simply giving answers or lectures?
  3. How did the execution of Socrates in 399 BC profoundly impact Plato's personal philosophy and his views on Athenian democracy?
  4. Explain the concept of aporia as it appears in Plato's early dialogues. Why did Socrates and Plato consider ending a philosophical discussion in aporia to be valuable rather than a failure?
  5. Outline the dramatic sequence of events concerning Socrates' trial and death as depicted across the Euthyphro, Apology, and Crito. How does this real-life drama elevate the importance and urgency of the philosophical questions being discussed?
