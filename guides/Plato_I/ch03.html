Study Guide: Chapter 3 - Apology

Overview

In 399 BC, Socrates stood trial before 501 Athenian citizens on charges of impiety and corrupting the young, delivering a remarkable defense speech that became one of the most celebrated texts in Western philosophy. Rather than flattering the jury or begging for mercy, Socrates argued that his relentless questioning of his fellow citizens was a divine mission given to him by the god Apollo, and that a life without philosophical self-examination is not worth living. The chapter also explores the Allegory of the Cave from Plato's Republic, the parallel between the trials of Socrates and Jesus, the Stoic philosophers' debt to Socrates, and the influence of the Apology on thinkers like Thoreau and Martin Luther King Jr.

Key Concepts

  • Socratic Ignorance: Socrates' claim that he was wiser than others not because he knew more, but because he alone recognized and admitted his own ignorance. He did not pretend to have answers he lacked, which he saw as the essential starting point of genuine philosophical inquiry.
  • The Gadfly Metaphor: Socrates compared himself to a gadfly (Greek: myops) that stings a large but sluggish horse — Athens — into wakefulness. His philosophical mission was not to provide answers but to provoke citizens into examining their own beliefs. The city did not enjoy being stung, but Socrates believed it needed the sting.
  • The Examined Life: The principle, stated in the Apology, that "the unexamined life is not worth living for a human being." For Socrates, a life without regularly questioning one's beliefs, testing one's values, and asking whether one is living rightly is not a fully human life. This makes philosophy not a luxury but a necessity.
  • Philosophy versus Rhetoric: Socrates drew a sharp contrast between his own plain, truth-seeking speech and the techniques of professional orators, who used emotional appeals, flattery, and weeping children to sway juries. He refused to manipulate the jury and insisted that truth is discovered by argument, not emotional pressure.
  • The Allegory of the Cave: Plato's famous image from the Republic (Book VII) in which prisoners chained in a cave mistake shadows on a wall for reality. When one prisoner escapes and sees the real world and the sun, he represents the philosopher's ascent from ignorance to true knowledge — and the hostility he faces when he returns to enlighten the others mirrors Socrates' fate.
  • The Philosopher-King: Plato's argument, developed in the Republic in response to Socrates' death, that the ideal city should be governed by philosophers — the only people trained to distinguish reality from appearance and wisdom from mere opinion.

Important Terms

  • Dikasterion: An Athenian law court, staffed by citizen jurors rather than professional judges or lawyers. Cases were decided entirely by a majority vote with no deliberation.
  • Klepsydra: Literally "water-thief"; a water clock used in Athenian courts to measure each speaker's allotted time.
  • Asebeia: The Greek word for impiety — disrespect toward the gods recognized by the city. One of the two formal charges against Socrates.
  • Daimonion: Socrates' inner divine voice or sign, which warned him when he was about to do something wrong. Its existence was cited in the indictment as evidence that Socrates was introducing new divinities.
  • Parrhesia: "Frank speech" — the valued quality of speaking openly and without artifice. Socrates turned this into a radical refusal to use any tricks of persuasion whatsoever.
  • Sophia: The Greek word for deep wisdom or genuine knowledge; the kind of understanding Socrates claimed he did not possess, and which he believed the politicians, poets, and craftsmen falsely claimed to have.
  • Elenchus: The method of cross-examination (also called Socratic refutation) that Socrates used throughout his philosophical questioning. The word anexetastos ("unexamined") in Socrates' famous line comes from the same root.
  • Prytaneum: Athens' state dining hall, where Olympic victors, war heroes, and distinguished public servants were honored with free meals at public expense. Socrates provocatively proposed this as his fitting "penalty" rather than a fine or exile.
  • Myops: The Greek word for a gadfly or horsefly; the creature Socrates used as a metaphor for his role in Athenian public life.
  • Stoicism: A philosophical movement founded about a century after Socrates' death by Zeno of Citium, which made calm acceptance of death, indifference to external circumstances, and focus on virtue central doctrines — principles directly inspired by Socrates' example.

Detailed Notes

The Setting: Athens in 399 BC

  • Socrates was 70 years old at the time of his trial — he had never held major public office, never written a book, and never charged a fee for his teaching.
  • The Athenian legal system was radically different from modern courts:
    • No professional lawyers, no judge, no rules of evidence in the modern sense.
    • Any citizen could file a sworn complaint against another citizen.
    • The accuser spoke first; the accused replied; the jury voted immediately by dropping bronze tokens into urns (solid token = acquittal; token with hole = conviction).
    • Time was measured by a water clock (klepsydra).
    • If convicted, both sides proposed a penalty and the jury chose between them — no compromise was possible.
  • The jury consisted of 501 Athenian citizens.

The Old and New Accusers

  • Socrates identified two distinct groups of accusers and considered the older, informal ones far more dangerous.
  • The old accusers were not people in the courtroom but years of accumulated gossip and comic ridicule:
    • The playwright Aristophanes, in his comedy The Clouds (performed 423 BC, over twenty years before the trial), portrayed "Socrates" as a charlatan who ran a "Thinkery" where students learned to make dishonest arguments.
    • This caricature led ordinary Athenians to associate Socrates with two things he actually opposed: natural philosophy (speculation about the heavens and physical world) and sophistry (winning arguments regardless of truth).
    • These old accusations were dangerous because the jurors had absorbed them since childhood — and you cannot cross-examine a rumor.
  • The new accusers were three specific men:
    • Meletus — a young poet who filed the formal complaint; motivated partly by personal resentment and partly by religious concern.
    • Anytus — a wealthy tanner and political leader who had helped overthrow the Thirty Tyrants in 403 BC; he likely saw Socrates as a political threat because several of Socrates' former associates (including Critias and Alcibiades) had been involved in anti-democratic activities.
    • Lycon — an orator about whom little is known, likely representing the interests of professional speakers whom Socrates had embarrassed.
  • Socrates himself said Meletus spoke for the poets, Anytus for the craftsmen and politicians, and Lycon for the orators.
  • The formal charges were twofold:
    1. Corrupting the young — turning Athenian youth against the values of their parents and city.
    2. Impiety (asebeia) — not believing in the city's gods and introducing new divinities (his daimonion).
  • Both charges carried the death penalty.

The Defense Speech: Section by Section

  • The Opening — Plain Speech:

    • Socrates warned the jury not to expect polished rhetoric. He would speak plainly, like a man in the marketplace.
    • Scholars call this parrhesia ("frank speech"). By refusing oratorical tricks, Socrates distinguished himself from the Sophists — but also risked seeming arrogant, since Athenian juries expected eloquence.

  • The Oracle at Delphi:

    • Socrates' friend Chaerephon once asked the Delphic oracle — the most sacred in Greece, where the priestess called the Pythia spoke for the god Apollo — whether anyone was wiser than Socrates. The oracle answered: no one.
    • Socrates claimed to be puzzled. He had no special knowledge of nature, politics, or the crafts. He set out to test the oracle by questioning those with reputations for wisdom:
      • Politicians — thought they were wise but could not explain or defend their views when pressed.
      • Poets — could produce beautiful work but could not explain what it meant; they composed "not by wisdom, but by a kind of genius and inspiration," like prophets who do not understand their own prophecies.
      • Craftsmen — genuinely knew their crafts, but their expertise led them to assume they were wise about everything else too.
    • Socrates concluded: he was wiser than others not because he knew more, but because he knew that he did not know. The others claimed knowledge they did not possess.
    • Crucial point: Socrates presented his entire philosophical career as an act of obedience to Apollo — making the impiety charge deeply ironic.

  • Cross-Examination of Meletus:

    • Socrates turned the Socratic method on his accuser, asking three devastating questions:
      1. Who improves the young? Meletus answered that all Athenians improve them except Socrates. Socrates pointed out this was absurd — in every other field of expertise (e.g., horse training), only a few specialists can do the job. Why would education be the one area where everyone is an expert and only one man causes damage?
      2. Does Socrates corrupt the young intentionally? If intentionally, no sane person harms himself on purpose (corrupted associates would harm him back). If unintentionally, then instruction — not prosecution — is the right remedy. Either way, the charge is incoherent.
      3. Does Socrates believe in no gods at all? Meletus blundered into claiming Socrates was a complete atheist who believed in nothing divine. But the indictment said Socrates introduced new divinities. These claims contradict each other — you cannot introduce new divine things while believing in nothing divine. Socrates compared this to claiming someone believes in horsemanship but not horses.
    • The cross-examination was intellectually devastating, but destroying arguments is not the same as winning hearts.

  • The Gadfly Speech:

    • Socrates explained why he continued his mission despite poverty, enemies, and danger:
      • Athens was like a great but sluggish horse — comfortable and self-satisfied, in danger of falling asleep.
      • Socrates was the gadfly (myops) that kept stinging the horse awake.
      • If the jury killed him, they would sleep undisturbed — unless the god sent another gadfly.
    • Socrates' poverty was evidence of his sincerity: he had neglected his own affairs entirely because he was carrying out a divine mission. If he were after money or fame, he would have charged fees like the Sophists.

The Verdict and the Penalty Phase

  • The jury voted 280 for conviction, 221 for acquittal. Socrates noted that a shift of only thirty votes would have resulted in a tie — and under Athenian law, a tie meant acquittal.
  • Meletus proposed death as the penalty. Socrates was required to propose an alternative:
    • In one of the most audacious moments in Western literature, Socrates proposed free meals at the Prytaneum — the honor given to Olympic victors and war heroes — as his fitting reward, claiming his philosophical service to Athens was worth more than winning a chariot race.
    • He eventually offered a fine of thirty minae, guaranteed by friends including Plato and Crito, but the damage was done.
  • The jury voted for death by a larger margin than the conviction vote — some jurors who had acquitted him now voted for death, apparently angered by his defiant counter-proposal. Ancient sources suggest the penalty vote was roughly 360 to 141.

Socrates' Final Words

  • To those who convicted him: His death would not silence philosophical questioning — younger, harsher questioners would come. The way to escape criticism is not to silence the critic but to make yourself genuinely good.
  • To those who acquitted him: His daimonion had been completely silent throughout the trial, which Socrates interpreted as a good omen — a sign that what was happening was not bad for him.
  • On death: Socrates offered a calm philosophical argument that death is either:
    1. Annihilation — a dreamless sleep; an eternity of deep, restful sleep would be a gain, not a loss.
    2. Migration of the soul — a journey to where the souls of the dead dwell; an even greater gain, since Socrates could spend eternity questioning the great heroes and thinkers of the past (Homer, Odysseus, Ajax).
  • He closed with his famous words: "The hour of departure has arrived, and we go our ways — I to die, and you to live. Which is better, only the god knows."
  • Note on historical accuracy: Plato was present at the trial and wrote the Apology some years afterward. It is not a transcript — Plato shaped the speech for philosophical and literary purposes. Xenophon's alternative account of the defense speech portrays a simpler Socrates more ready to die.

Key Themes of the Apology

  • The Examined Life:

    • "The unexamined life is not worth living for a human being" (ho de anexetastos bios ou biotos anthropo).
    • Socrates meant something radical: a life without philosophical self-scrutiny — without questioning your beliefs, testing your values, asking whether you are living as you ought — is not a fully human life. It is the life of a sleepwalker.
    • The examined life is not about intelligence; it is about honesty and courage.
    • Consequence: philosophy is not a hobby or a luxury — it is a necessity. A prosperous society that never questions its own values is worse off than a poor one that does.

  • Philosophy versus Rhetoric:

    • Professional orators brought weeping children to court, flattered juries, and told moving stories to arouse pity — this was standard Athenian courtroom practice.
    • Socrates refused. He would not bring his three sons to court, would not weep, would not beg.
    • His reason: the jury's job is to judge what is true, not to distribute favors to whoever makes the most moving appeal. A juror who acquits because of tears is being manipulated, not doing justice.
    • He would rather die justly than live by manipulation.

  • Death and the Philosopher:

    • Socrates' calm before death was not bravado — he treated death as a philosophical problem to reason about.
    • What mattered was not whether he would die but how he faced it. A life pursuing truth and refusing to compromise principles is a good life regardless of how long it lasts.
    • Socrates' courage before death became one of the founding images of Western philosophy. For centuries, philosophers were judged in part by how they faced their own deaths.

Ideas and Influence: The Allegory of the Cave

  • Plato never forgot the lesson of 399 BC. His teacher's death convinced him that no existing government could be trusted with justice.
  • In the Republic (written perhaps twenty years after the trial), Plato argued that the ideal city should be ruled by philosophers — those trained to perceive what is truly real and truly good.
  • The Allegory of the Cave (Republic, Book VII):
    • Prisoners have been chained in an underground cave since childhood, able only to see the wall in front of them. A fire behind them casts shadows of objects onto the wall, and the prisoners take these shadows for reality.
    • If a prisoner is freed and dragged toward the sunlight, he is first blinded and disoriented — the real world is too bright. Gradually his eyes adjust; he sees real objects, then the sun itself, source of all light and life.
    • If he returns to the cave to tell the other prisoners what he saw, they will think him mad and may try to kill him.
    • The allegory maps directly onto Plato's philosophy: the cave = the world of everyday appearances and opinion; the sun = the Form of the Good, the ultimate reality; the freed prisoner = the philosopher.
    • The parallel with Socrates is unmistakable: he was the freed prisoner who returned to his fellow Athenians and tried to awaken them — and received a death sentence for it.
  • The argument for the philosopher-king grows directly from the Apology: if the wisest man in Athens was condemned by democratic vote, democracy is inherently flawed, since the many are trapped in the Cave. This tension between philosophy and democracy remains a fundamental problem of political thought.

Socrates and Jesus: Two Trials

  • Early Christian writers, notably Justin Martyr (c. 100–165 AD), were struck by the parallels between the two trials.
  • Similarities:
    • Both were accused by their communities' religious authorities (Socrates: impiety toward Athenian gods; Jesus: blasphemy against the God of Israel).
    • Both chose death rather than compromise their message.
    • Both claimed a divine mission.
    • Both were condemned by legally technical but politically motivated processes.
    • The deaths of both became the defining events of the movements they inspired.
  • Differences:
    • Socrates argued at length; Jesus was largely silent before Pilate.
    • Socrates sought wisdom and admitted he lacked it; Jesus claimed to be the truth (John 14:6).
    • Socrates' death was a philosophical demonstration that the examined life is worth more than survival. Jesus' death, in Christian theology, was a redemptive event — an atonement for sin.
    • Socrates stayed dead. The central Christian claim is that Jesus rose from the dead, which changes the meaning of the death entirely.
  • Church Fathers developed the concept of praeparatio evangelica ("preparation for the Gospel") — the idea that Greek philosophy prepared the pagan world to receive Christian revelation.

The Stoic Inheritance

  • Socrates did not found a philosophical school, but his personal example profoundly shaped Stoicism, founded about a century after his death by Zeno of Citium (c. 334–262 BC).
  • Stoicism's central doctrines read like a working-out of principles Socrates demonstrated at his trial:
    • Epictetus (c. 50–135 AD), a former slave who became one of the greatest Stoic teachers, opened his Handbook (Enchiridion) with the distinction: "Some things are in our power, and some are not." What is in our power: our judgments, desires, and choices. What is not: our bodies, reputations, external circumstances — and whether we live or die. Socrates demonstrated exactly this division at his trial.
    • Seneca (c. 4 BC–65 AD), the Roman statesman and philosopher, returned repeatedly to Socrates' example. Forced to commit suicide by the emperor Nero, Seneca reportedly asked for water to pour a libation to Jupiter — deliberately echoing Socrates' final act in the Phaedo.
    • Marcus Aurelius (121–180 AD), Roman emperor and Stoic philosopher, wrote in his Meditations about the worthlessness of reputation and the importance of living rightly, clearly echoing Socratic themes. For the Stoics, Socrates was the prototype of the philosopher — the man who proved, by dying, that wisdom matters more than life itself.

Civil Disobedience and the Examined Life

  • The Apology raises a question every society must confront: what happens when individual conscience collides with the collective will?
  • Henry David Thoreau (1817–1862) read the Apology before writing "Civil Disobedience" (1849). After spending a night in jail for refusing to pay a tax that supported the Mexican-American War and the expansion of slavery, Thoreau argued that when a government commits injustice, the individual has not merely the right but the duty to refuse compliance — even at the cost of imprisonment.
  • Martin Luther King Jr. (1929–1968) invoked Socrates directly in his "Letter from Birmingham Jail" (1963), placing Socrates in a lineage of "creative extremists" who refused to accept injustice. For King, Socrates was a living example of the principle that unjust laws must be confronted openly and lovingly, with a willingness to accept the legal consequences.
  • An important distinction: Socrates accepted the authority of Athenian law and submitted to its verdict. King argued that unjust laws should be openly disobeyed — while accepting the legal penalty to demonstrate respect for the rule of law itself.
  • The connecting thread: Socrates called it the examined life; Thoreau called it conscience; King called it the moral law. In each case: a life spent obeying unjust authority without question is not a life worth living.

Review Questions

  1. Socrates distinguishes between his "old accusers" and his "new accusers," calling the old ones far more dangerous. Who were the old accusers, and why did Socrates believe they posed a greater threat to his defense than the three men standing in court?
  2. Explain the logic of Socrates' argument from the Delphic oracle. How did his investigation of politicians, poets, and craftsmen lead him to the conclusion that the oracle was correct — and what did his "wisdom" actually consist of?
  3. Describe the Allegory of the Cave in your own words and explain how it connects to the events of Socrates' trial. What does the sun represent? What does the freed prisoner's return to the cave represent?
  4. What was the "gadfly speech," and why is it central to understanding Socrates' self-image? Do you think Socrates was genuinely trying to save his life during his trial, or had he already decided to accept death? Use evidence from the text to support your answer.
  5. Compare Socrates' claim that "the unexamined life is not worth living" with Martin Luther King Jr.'s argument in the "Letter from Birmingham Jail." In what specific ways did King draw on Socratic ideas, and where does his argument go beyond what Socrates claimed in the Apology?
