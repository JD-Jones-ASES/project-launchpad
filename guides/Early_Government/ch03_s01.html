Who Rules? Aristotle's Question and the Cycle of Governments

Overview

This section explores fundamental questions about government by examining the foundational ideas of two ancient Greek thinkers: Aristotle and Polybius. Aristotle's systematic approach classified governments based on the number of rulers and whether they served the common good or their own interests. Building upon this, Polybius observed a recurring "cycle" of governmental change, proposing the concept of a "mixed constitution" as a way to achieve stability by blending different forms of rule.

Key Concepts

  • Aristotle's Two Classifying Questions: Aristotle proposed that governments could be understood by asking two questions: 1) How many people hold power (one, few, or many)? and 2) For whose benefit do they rule (the common good or themselves)?
  • Good vs. Corrupt Government: Aristotle's crucial insight was that the same number of rulers could lead to either a good or a corrupt government, depending on whether they govern for the entire community or for their own selfish gain.
  • Aristotle's Six Forms of Government: This classification includes:
    • One Ruler: Monarchy (good) vs. Tyranny (corrupt)
    • Few Rulers: Aristocracy (good) vs. Oligarchy (corrupt)
    • Many Rulers: Polity/Democracy (good) vs. Mob Rule/Ochlocracy (corrupt)
  • Anacyclosis (Polybius's Cycle of Governments): Polybius theorized that governments naturally decay and transform in a repeating cycle: monarchy to tyranny, aristocracy to oligarchy, and democracy to mob rule, eventually returning to monarchy.
  • Mixed Constitution: Polybius's solution to break the cycle of anacyclosis. It involves combining elements of monarchy, aristocracy, and democracy in one government, allowing each part to "check" and balance the others, preventing any single group from seizing total power. This idea heavily influenced the design of the American government.

Important Terms

  • Classify: To sort things into groups based on their similarities. (e.g., Aristotle classified governments).
  • Aristocracy: Government by the "best and most qualified" citizens, ruling for the common good. (From Greek: aristos "best" + kratos "rule").
  • Oligarchy: Government by a small group of wealthy or powerful people, ruling for their own benefit. (From Greek: oligos "few" + arkhein "to rule").
  • Tyranny: Rule by one person for their own benefit, typically using fear and force. A tyrant holds power unjustly.
  • Monarchy: A form of government with a single head of state, usually a king or queen, ruling for the common good.
  • Polity: Aristotle's term for a good form of government where many people rule for the common good, often blending elements of aristocracy and democracy.
  • Ochlocracy: Mob rule, a corrupt form of democracy where the unthinking crowd governs for its own immediate desires, often at the expense of minorities or justice.
  • Anacyclosis: Polybius's theory of a "rotating cycle" where governments naturally transition from one form to another, often decaying from good to corrupt forms. (From Greek: ana- "again" + kyklos "circle").
  • Mixed Constitution: A governmental system that combines elements of monarchy, aristocracy, and democracy to prevent the concentration of power and stabilize the state, as advocated by Polybius.

Detailed Notes

Aristotle's Fundamental Question: What Makes a Good Government?

  • About 2,400 years ago, the Greek philosopher Aristotle embarked on a comprehensive study of political systems to understand what constitutes good governance.
  • He meticulously studied many Greek city-states, interviewing people, reading records, and analyzing their governmental structures.

Aristotle's Two Big Questions for Classification

Aristotle developed a systematic approach based on two key questions:

  1. How many people hold power? This could be one person, a few people, or many people.
  2. For whose benefit do they rule? Do they rule for the common good of all citizens, or purely for their own selfish benefit?

Aristotle's Six Forms of Government (Good vs. Corrupt)

Aristotle's answers to these two questions yielded six distinct forms of government, categorized by whether they served the common good (good forms) or only the rulers' self-interest (corrupt forms):

  • Rule by One Person:
    • Good Form: Monarchy (A king or queen who governs wisely and cares for their people).
    • Corrupt Form: Tyranny (A ruler who holds power unjustly and uses fear/force for personal gain).
  • Rule by a Few People:
    • Good Form: Aristocracy (A council of experienced, qualified citizens who govern wisely for the community).
    • Corrupt Form: Oligarchy (A small group of wealthy or powerful individuals who grab power and resources for themselves).
  • Rule by Many People:
    • Good Form: Polity / Democracy (A system where the majority governs for the common good, respecting the minority).
    • Corrupt Form: Mob Rule / Ochlocracy (When the majority abuses its power to crush the minority or make decisions based on impulse, not reason).

Aristotle's Key Insight

Aristotle's lasting contribution was the idea that any form of government can be good or corrupt. The defining factor is the intent and purpose of the rulers: do they serve the common good or only themselves? This framework remains relevant for political analysis today.

Aristotle's Background

  • Born in northern Greece (384–322 BC).
  • Studied at Plato's Academy in Athens for 20 years.
  • Later became the tutor of Alexander the Great.
  • Wrote extensively on many subjects, including politics. His book Politics is a foundational text in political science.
  • He famously classified 158 constitutions from various Greek cities to develop his theories.

Polybius and the Cycle of Governments (Anacyclosis)

  • About 150 years after Aristotle, the Greek historian Polybius (c. 200–118 BC) studied the Roman Republic firsthand.
  • He asked a new question: Why do governments constantly change? Is there a predictable pattern?
  • Polybius's answer was the theory of anacyclosis, a "rotating cycle" of governmental forms. He observed that governments don't stay static but naturally decay and transition.

The Stages of Anacyclosis

Polybius described a seven-stage cycle of governmental change:

  1. Monarchy: A strong, wise ruler governs justly for the people.
  2. Tyranny: The monarch's heirs become selfish, ruling by fear and for their own benefit.
  3. Aristocracy: The "best citizens" overthrow the tyrant and govern wisely for the common good.
  4. Oligarchy: The noble aristocratic families become greedy, seizing power and wealth for themselves.
  5. Democracy (Polity): The common people rise up and rule through assemblies, seeking the common good.
  6. Ochlocracy (Mob Rule): The general populace loses discipline, becoming a chaotic crowd that demands only what pleases itself, often oppressing minorities.
  7. Monarchy (New Cycle): The chaos of mob rule becomes so unbearable that people beg for a strong leader to restore order, leading to the rise of a new monarch and restarting the cycle.

The Mixed Constitution: Breaking the Cycle

  • Polybius believed he found a solution to anacyclosis in the Roman Republic: the mixed constitution.
  • This system intentionally blends elements of monarchy, aristocracy, and democracy to create stability.
  • The Roman Republic as an Example:
    • Monarchical Element: The two consuls acted like kings, but their power was limited (one-year terms, two consuls checking each other).
    • Aristocratic Element: The Senate comprised experienced leaders who guided long-term policy and finances.
    • Democratic Element: The popular assemblies allowed citizens to vote on laws and elect leaders.
  • How it Works: By combining these elements, each part of the government could "check" the others. No single person or group could seize total power, effectively slowing down or even breaking the cycle of corruption.

Polybius's Key Contribution

Polybius's idea of the mixed constitution, with its emphasis on checks and balances, was profoundly influential. It provided a theoretical basis for limiting power and promoting governmental stability, directly inspiring the American Founders nearly two millennia later when they designed the U.S. system of three branches of government.

Polybius's Background

  • Greek historian (c. 200–118 BC) who spent years living in Rome.
  • He gained firsthand insight into the Roman Republic and its rise to power.
  • His work profoundly influenced James Madison and other American Founders, particularly his theories on anacyclosis and the mixed constitution.

Review Questions

  1. According to Aristotle, what two fundamental questions must be asked to classify any government, and why are both questions necessary for a complete understanding?
  2. Explain Aristotle's key insight regarding good versus corrupt governments. Provide examples for each of the three levels of rule (one, few, many) to illustrate this point.
  3. Describe Polybius's theory of anacyclosis. What are the key stages of this cycle, and what causes the transition from one stage to the next?
  4. How did Polybius propose to break the cycle of anacyclosis? Explain the concept of a "mixed constitution" and use the Roman Republic as an example to illustrate its components.
  5. Both Aristotle and Polybius were ancient Greek thinkers. Discuss the lasting relevance of their ideas to modern political thought and governance, particularly in the context of the American system of government.
