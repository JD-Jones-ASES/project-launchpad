Study Guide: The Texas Constitution

Overview

This section explores the fundamental role of state constitutions, contrasting them with the U.S. Constitution, and detailing the specific history and features of the Texas Constitution. It explains how a state constitution establishes government, protects citizens' rights, and limits power, while also outlining the unique evolution of Texas's many constitutions, including the current 1876 document, its robust Bill of Rights, and the process for its amendment.

Key Concepts

  • Role of a State Constitution: Each of the fifty U.S. states has its own constitution, which acts as a foundational "rulebook" for its state government, similar to how the U.S. Constitution guides the federal government. These state documents cannot contradict federal law but provide specific regulations for state operations.
  • Three Main Functions of a State Constitution:
    1. Creates State Government: Establishes the three branches (legislative, executive, judicial) and defines their powers and responsibilities.
    2. Lists Citizens' Rights: Includes a bill of rights that outlines fundamental freedoms and protections for the state's residents.
    3. Limits Government Power: Imposes restrictions on what the state government can do, preventing any single branch from becoming too dominant.
  • Historical Evolution of the Texas Constitution: Texas has adopted seven different constitutions throughout its history, with each new document reflecting significant political and historical shifts, such as independence from Mexico, joining the U.S., secession, and Reconstruction.
  • The 1876 Texas Constitution: This is Texas's current constitution. It was drafted after the Civil War and Reconstruction, by citizens determined to limit governmental power that they felt had become excessive. This desire for limited government resulted in one of the longest and most detailed state constitutions in the nation.
  • The Texas Bill of Rights (Article I): Found in Article I of the Texas Constitution, this section guarantees many of the same rights as the U.S. Bill of Rights (e.g., freedom of speech, press, religion, right to jury trial, protection against unreasonable searches). It also includes additional protections, such as equal rights regardless of sex or race, and the right to vote for eligible citizens.
  • Amendment Process: Changing the Texas Constitution is a two-step process. First, an amendment must be approved by a two-thirds vote in both the Texas House of Representatives and the Texas Senate. Second, it must be approved by a majority of Texas voters in a special statewide election, giving citizens the final say. Over 500 amendments have been added to the 1876 constitution.

Important Terms

  • state constitution: A written plan that describes the structure, powers, and limits of a state government.
  • amendment: A formal change or addition to a constitution.
  • ratify: To officially approve a law or constitution so it takes effect.
  • bill of rights: The section of a constitution that lists the rights that government must protect for its citizens.

Detailed Notes

The Role of a State Constitution

Just as the United States operates under one national Constitution, each of the fifty states has its own state constitution. These state-specific "rulebooks" work in conjunction with the U.S. Constitution. They define the framework for state government, much like the U.S. Constitution defines the federal government. Neither the state nor federal constitution can diminish the rights protected by the other. A helpful analogy is to think of the U.S. Constitution as the rulebook for an entire school, while a state constitution is like the specific rules for one classroom within that school.

What a State Constitution Does

Every state constitution serves three primary purposes:

  1. Creates the State Government: It establishes the three distinct branches of state government—the legislative (makes laws), executive (carries out laws), and judicial (interprets laws)—and outlines their powers and responsibilities.
  2. Lists the Rights of Citizens: Most state constitutions include their own bill of rights, similar to the federal Bill of Rights, which enumerates the fundamental freedoms and protections the government must guarantee for its citizens.
  3. Limits Government Power: A constitution places restrictions on the state government, specifying what it cannot do. This design prevents any one branch from becoming too powerful and ensures a balance of authority.

Texas's Many Constitutions

Texas has a particularly rich constitutional history, having operated under seven different constitutions. Each new constitution marked a significant turning point in Texas history:

  • 1827: Under Mexican rule (written in Spanish).
  • 1836: For the Republic of Texas (the first Texas constitution in English).
  • 1845: When Texas joined the United States.
  • 1861: Upon joining the Confederacy at the start of the Civil War.
  • 1866: Adopted immediately after the Civil War.
  • 1869: During the Reconstruction era, imposed by federal authorities.
  • 1876: The current Texas Constitution, written after Reconstruction ended.

The current Texas Constitution, adopted in 1876, was a direct response to the powerful state governments Texans experienced during and after the Civil War. The framers were determined to limit government authority, which led to a document that is notably one of the longest and most detailed state constitutions in the United States.

The Texas Bill of Rights

Article I of the Texas Constitution is dedicated to the Texas Bill of Rights. This section protects many of the same fundamental liberties found in the federal Bill of Rights, and in some cases, provides even broader protections. Key rights guaranteed include:

  • Freedom of speech, press, and religion.
  • The right to a jury trial.
  • Protection against unreasonable searches and seizures.
  • Equal rights for all people, explicitly regardless of sex or race.
  • The right to vote for eligible citizens.

Amending the Texas Constitution

Changing the Texas Constitution is a deliberate process designed to ensure careful consideration and public input. An amendment must pass through two main steps:

  1. Legislative Approval: The proposed amendment must first be approved by a two-thirds majority vote in both chambers of the Texas Legislature (the House of Representatives and the Senate).
  2. Voter Ratification: After legislative approval, the proposed amendment is put before Texas voters in a special statewide election. Voters then have the final say; the amendment only takes effect if a majority of those who vote approve it.

This two-step process ensures that the people of Texas, not just politicians, have the ultimate authority over changes to their foundational document. Because the 1876 constitution is so detailed, and because it is relatively easy for the legislature to propose amendments, Texas voters have approved more than 500 amendments to it over the years, contributing significantly to its considerable length.

Word Origins

The word "constitution" originates from the Latin word "constituere," which means "to set up" or "to establish." This etymology perfectly reflects the purpose of a constitution: to establish the framework, rules, and fundamental principles for a government from its very beginning.
