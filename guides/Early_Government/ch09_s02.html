Study Guide: How Citizens Participate

Overview

This section explains that a self-governing republic thrives only with active and informed citizens. It outlines various crucial ways individuals participate, ranging from fundamental duties like voting, obeying laws, and paying taxes, to direct civic service such as jury duty, staying informed, and engaging in community volunteering. The underlying message is that the strength of a democracy depends on citizens actively contributing to their community and government.

Key Concepts

  • Citizen Participation: Taking an active role in the community or government, rather than passively observing. A republic cannot function without active citizen involvement.
  • Informed Citizenry: The necessity for citizens to stay updated on government actions, community issues, and political candidates to make sound decisions, especially when voting.
  • Voting as a Right and Responsibility: Voting is a constitutional right, but also a fundamental responsibility citizens owe to their community to choose leaders and shape the future. Low voter turnout can lead to decisions made by a minority.
  • Jury Duty as Civic Service: Serving on a jury is a direct and vital form of civic participation, upholding the justice system and ensuring fair trials, which is a key protection in America.
  • Obeying Laws and Paying Taxes: These are fundamental duties that ensure a safe, fair, and functional society by funding public services (schools, roads, defense) and protecting everyone's rights and property.
  • Peaceful Legal Change: While citizens have a duty to obey laws, they also have the right and means to work peacefully and legally (through voting, petitioning, advocacy) to change laws they believe are unjust.
  • Volunteering and Community Building: Civic participation extends beyond government to non-paid efforts that strengthen communities from the ground up, such as helping neighbors, cleaning parks, or assisting those in need.

Important Terms

  • participation: Taking an active role in the community or government, not just watching from the sidelines.
  • informed citizen: A citizen who stays up to date on what government is doing and what issues the community faces.
  • volunteer: A person who gives time and energy to help others without being paid.
  • jury duty: The responsibility of an eligible citizen to serve on a jury when called by the court.

Detailed Notes

The Foundation of a Republic

  • A republic, unlike a monarchy, relies entirely on its citizens to be active and engaged.
  • Benjamin Franklin's famous quote, "A republic — if you can keep it," emphasizes that the survival and strength of a self-governing nation depend on its citizens' continuous support and participation.
  • This participation goes beyond understanding the three branches of government; it's about individual citizens doing their part.

Core Duties of Citizenship

1. The Most Basic Duty: Voting

  • In a democracy, leaders are chosen by the people. If citizens don't vote, a minority of eligible voters can end up making the choices for everyone.
  • Voter turnout in the United States is often lower compared to other democracies.
  • Voting is both a right (protected by the Constitution) and a responsibility (something citizens owe to their community).
  • To vote wisely, citizens must be informed – knowing the candidates, their platforms, and the issues at stake.

2. Serving on a Jury

  • The right to a jury trial is a fundamental protection in the American justice system.
  • This right is only effective if ordinary citizens are willing to serve as jurors when called for jury duty.
  • Serving on a jury is one of the most direct forms of civic service, directly helping to decide justice and whether laws have been broken.

3. Obeying the Law

  • Following the law is a simple yet crucial act of citizenship that benefits everyone.
  • Laws protect individuals and make communities safer and fairer (e.g., speed limits, respecting property).
  • If citizens disagree with a law, they should work to change it through legal and peaceful means (voting, petitioning, supporting candidates), rather than simply ignoring it.

4. Paying Taxes

  • Taxes are essential for funding public services that everyone relies on, such as schools, roads, fire departments, police, and national defense.
  • They represent how a community pools its resources to pay for collective needs that no individual could afford alone.
  • Paying taxes honestly is a fundamental duty of citizenship.

5. Staying Informed

  • An uninformed citizen cannot make good decisions at the ballot box or effectively engage with their government.
  • Ways to stay informed include:
    • Reading or watching news from reliable sources.
    • Paying attention to local issues (school board, city council, state legislation).
    • Researching candidates thoroughly before elections.
    • Understanding how government decisions impact daily life.

Beyond Government: Volunteering and Community Service

  • Civic participation is not limited to government-related activities.
  • Volunteers contribute to building strong communities from the ground up.
  • Examples include volunteering at food banks, coaching youth sports, participating in park clean-ups, or visiting elderly neighbors.
  • Communities are strengthened by citizens who invest their time and energy in them without expecting payment.

Example: Clara Barton (1821–1912)

  • Clara Barton demonstrated the power of individual civic courage and determination.
  • She was a teacher and one of the first women to work in a U.S. government office.
  • During the Civil War, she organized medical supply distribution and worked in field hospitals, earning the nickname "Angel of the Battlefield."
  • After the war, she helped identify missing soldiers.
  • Inspired by the International Red Cross, she campaigned for years to establish the American Red Cross in 1881 and served as its first president.
  • Her life showed that a single citizen, without holding elected office, can profoundly impact a nation.

Comprehensive List of Ways Citizens Participate in Self-Government

  • Vote in elections and encourage others to vote.
  • Stay informed about current issues and candidates.
  • Serve on a jury when called for duty.
  • Obey the law and work peacefully to change unfair laws.
  • Pay taxes honestly and on time.
  • Volunteer time and energy to serve the community.
  • Contact elected officials to share opinions and concerns.
  • Run for office or actively support those who do.
  • Teach children about their civic responsibilities and the importance of participation.

Review Questions

  1. Benjamin Franklin said, "A republic — if you can keep it." What did he mean by this statement, and how do the various forms of citizen participation discussed in this section directly relate to "keeping" the republic?
  2. The textbook notes that U.S. voter turnout is often lower than in other democracies. Explain why this is a concern for a self-governing republic and what potential consequences could arise from sustained low voter participation.
  3. Beyond voting, identify and explain two other essential duties of citizens outlined in this section (e.g., jury duty, obeying laws, paying taxes). How does fulfilling each of these duties contribute to the overall well-being and functionality of the community?
  4. Why is being an "informed citizen" crucial for effective participation in a republic? Provide examples of how staying informed impacts different civic actions, such as voting, advocating for change, or engaging in community service.
  5. The section highlights both participation in government processes and non-governmental community service. Using the example of Clara Barton, explain how an individual's civic engagement outside of elected office can still have a profound and lasting impact on a nation.
