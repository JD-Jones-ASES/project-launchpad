Study Guide: Congress: The Legislative Branch

Overview

This section introduces Congress as the legislative branch of the U.S. federal government, responsible for making laws. It explains that Congress is bicameral, meaning it has two chambers: the House of Representatives and the Senate, a structure established by the Great Compromise. The guide details the composition, terms, and distinct characteristics of each chamber, along with the key powers and responsibilities of Congress, such as lawmaking, controlling spending, declaring war, and oversight functions.

Key Concepts

  • Bicameral Legislature: Congress is divided into two distinct chambers – the House of Representatives and the Senate – to balance the power between states with large populations and those with smaller populations, a resolution stemming from the Great Compromise.
  • House of Representatives: This chamber has 435 members, with each state's representation based on its population. Representatives serve two-year terms, requiring them to stay closely attuned to their constituents' immediate needs and desires.
  • The Senate: Comprised of 100 members, with two senators from each state regardless of population size. Senators serve six-year terms, providing them with greater independence and allowing for a longer-term perspective on policy.
  • Lawmaking Process: A proposed law (bill) must successfully pass through both the House of Representatives and the Senate before being sent to the President for approval to become federal law.
  • Key Powers of Congress: Congress holds significant authority, including the exclusive power to make federal laws, approve the federal budget (control spending), declare war, and oversee other branches through actions like confirming appointments (Senate) and impeachment (House charges, Senate tries).

Important Terms

  • Congress: The legislative branch of the U.S. federal government, responsible for creating laws. It consists of two chambers: the Senate and the House of Representatives.
  • Senate: The upper chamber of Congress. Each state is represented by two senators, totaling 100 members.
  • House of Representatives: The lower chamber of Congress. The number of representatives from each state is determined by its population, totaling 435 members.
  • Bill: A proposal for a new law. To become law, a bill must pass both the House and the Senate and then be signed by the President.
  • Bicameral: A legislative body composed of two separate chambers or houses. The U.S. Congress is bicameral.
  • Constituent: A person residing in a particular electoral district and represented by an elected official. Representatives must stay closely connected to the wants of their constituents.
  • Impeach: To formally charge a federal official with misconduct. The House of Representatives has the power to impeach, while the Senate conducts the trial.

Detailed Notes

The Legislative Branch: Congress

  • The U.S. federal government's legislative branch is called Congress.
  • Its primary function is to create and pass laws that govern the United States.
  • All federal laws originate from an idea, are drafted into a bill, debated in committees, voted on by elected representatives, and then follow a rigorous process to become law.
  • The legislative work of Congress takes place in the U.S. Capitol building in Washington, D.C.

A Bicameral Legislature

  • Meaning of Bicameral: The term "bicameral" means "two chambers."
  • Structure of Congress: Congress is divided into two chambers:
    1. The House of Representatives
    2. The Senate
  • Reason for Bicameralism (The Great Compromise):
    • This two-chamber structure was a direct result of the Great Compromise during the Constitutional Convention (as discussed in Unit 4).
    • Larger states, with bigger populations, advocated for more representation and power based on their numbers.
    • Smaller states desired equal representation to prevent being overshadowed by larger states.
    • The compromise established one chamber (the House) where representation is based on population, and another (the Senate) where all states have equal representation.

The House of Representatives

  • Number of Members: There are 435 members in the House of Representatives.
  • Basis of Representation: Each state's number of representatives is determined by its population.
    • States with larger populations, like California, have more representatives (e.g., 52).
    • States with smaller populations, like Wyoming, have fewer representatives (e.g., 1).
  • Term Length: Representatives serve two-year terms.
  • Responsiveness to Constituents: Because they face re-election every two years, representatives are typically very responsive to the immediate concerns and desires of their constituents (the people they represent).

The Senate

  • Number of Members: There are 100 members in the Senate.
  • Basis of Representation: Each state, regardless of its size or population, sends exactly two senators.
    • This ensures that smaller states have an equal voice with larger states on many national decisions.
  • Term Length: Senators serve six-year terms.
  • Independence and Long-Term Thinking: The longer term length provides senators with greater independence from short-term public opinion, allowing them to focus on long-term policy goals and national interests.
  • Staggered Elections: Roughly one-third of the Senate faces election every two years, ensuring continuity and preventing a complete turnover of the chamber in a single election cycle.

What Congress Does (Powers and Responsibilities)

Congress possesses several critical powers that define its role as the legislative branch:

  • Makes Laws:
    • Any proposed law, known as a bill, must pass a majority vote in both the House of Representatives and the Senate.
    • After passing both chambers, the bill is sent to the President. If the President signs it, it becomes a federal law.
  • Controls Spending (Power of the Purse):
    • Congress has exclusive authority over the federal budget.
    • No money can be spent by the federal government without the approval of Congress. This gives Congress significant influence over government operations and priorities.
  • Declares War:
    • Only Congress has the constitutional power to formally declare war. This is a crucial check on the President's power as Commander-in-Chief.
  • Confirms Appointments (Senate Exclusive Power):
    • The Senate holds the power to approve or reject presidential nominees for various high-level positions, including:
      • Federal judges (including Supreme Court justices)
      • Cabinet members
      • Ambassadors
    • This power ensures a check on the executive branch's appointments.
  • Impeaches Officials:
    • House of Representatives: The House has the power to impeach (formally charge) federal officials, including the President, Vice President, and other civil officers, for "Treason, Bribery, or other high Crimes and Misdemeanors."
    • Senate: If the House votes to impeach, the Senate then conducts the trial to determine guilt or innocence and, if guilty, to remove the official from office.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the concept of a "bicameral" legislature and why the U.S. Congress was designed this way, referencing the historical context of the Great Compromise.
  2. Compare and contrast the House of Representatives and the Senate in terms of their membership numbers, how representation is determined, and the length of terms for their members.
  3. How do the different term lengths for representatives and senators influence their connection to their constituents and their approach to policymaking?
  4. Describe the basic process by which a bill becomes a law, highlighting the roles of both chambers of Congress and the President.
  5. Beyond making laws, identify and explain at least three other significant powers or responsibilities of Congress, providing a brief example for each.
