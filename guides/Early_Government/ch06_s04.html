Study Guide: Local Government: Counties, Cities, and Schools

Overview

This section explores the crucial role of local government in Texas, focusing on its three main types: counties, cities, and school districts. It details their structures, responsibilities, how they provide essential services directly impacting citizens' daily lives, and how they fit into the broader system of federal and state governance.

Key Concepts

  • Three Types of Local Government: Texas has counties, cities, and independent school districts (ISDs), each with distinct roles and responsibilities. They are the level of government closest to citizens.
  • Counties: Texas is divided into 254 counties, each covering specific geographic areas and providing services to both rural and urban residents. They are governed by a Commissioners Court and are responsible for roads, courts, records, elections, and some social services.
  • Cities: Formed when enough people reside in one place, city governments pass local laws (ordinances) and provide services like police, fire, utilities, parks, zoning, and local roads. They typically operate under either a mayor-council or council-manager system.
  • School Districts: Public education in Texas is managed by independent school districts (ISDs), overseen by elected school boards. These boards make decisions about hiring, budgets, curriculum, and school facilities.
  • Property Tax Funding: A significant portion of local government services, especially public schools, is funded through property taxes collected from landowners and businesses within the local area. This can lead to differences in resources between districts.
  • Multiple Levels of Government: The United States operates with three interconnected levels of government—federal, state, and local—each with specific powers and responsibilities, all working together to serve the public.
  • Accessibility of Local Government: Local government is the most accessible level for citizens to engage with, offering direct opportunities for participation and influence.

Important Terms

  • County: A division of a state used to organize local government; Texas has more counties (254) than any other state.
  • Ordinance: A law made by a city or county government, applying only within that local jurisdiction.
  • Property Tax: A tax paid by individuals or businesses who own land or buildings, primarily used to fund local services like public schools and other municipal operations.
  • School Board: An elected group of citizens who volunteer to oversee and govern a public school district, making key decisions for its operation.
  • Mayor: The elected leader of a city government, often responsible for leading the city council or managing daily operations depending on the city's governmental structure.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Local Government

Local government is the level of government closest to the daily lives of citizens. It impacts many aspects, from school bus routes and local parks to libraries and sidewalks. In Texas, there are three primary forms of local government: counties, cities, and school districts.

Counties

Texas is unique for having 254 counties, more than any other state, with every part of the state falling within a county. Counties serve residents in both urban and rural areas.

  • Structure: The Commissioners Court
    • Each county is governed by a Commissioners Court.
    • This court is composed of:
      • A County Judge: Chairs the court.
      • Four County Commissioners: Elected from different geographic parts of the county.
  • County Responsibilities
    • Maintaining county roads and bridges.
    • Operating the county courthouse and jail facilities.
    • Recording vital information such as births, deaths, and property ownership.
    • Running elections within the county.
    • Providing certain health and social services to residents.
  • Other Elected County Officials
    • Sheriff: Enforces laws, particularly in rural areas not covered by city police.
    • Tax Assessor-Collector: Responsible for collecting taxes, including property taxes.
    • District Attorney: Prosecutes criminal cases within the county.

Cities

When a concentration of people forms a community, they can establish a city government to provide specific services and pass local laws.

  • City Services
    • Police and fire protection services.
    • Establishing and maintaining water and sewer systems.
    • Creating and managing city parks and recreation centers.
    • Issuing building permits and enforcing zoning laws (rules about land use).
    • Maintaining local roads and traffic signals.
  • City Government Systems
    • Mayor-Council System: Voters elect a mayor (who leads the city) and a city council (which makes ordinances). Power is shared between the mayor and council. (e.g., Houston)
    • Council-Manager System: Voters elect a city council, which then hires a professional city manager to handle the daily operations of the city. The mayor's role in this system may be more ceremonial. (e.g., many mid-size Texas cities)

School Districts

Public education in Texas is overseen by independent school districts (ISDs), which are separate governmental entities.

  • Governance by School Boards
    • Each ISD is governed by a school board, composed of citizens elected by local voters.
    • School Boards are responsible for making key decisions, including:
      • Hiring and evaluating the district's chief executive, the superintendent.
      • Approving the district's annual budget.
      • Setting policies related to curriculum, school hours, student conduct, and other operational matters.
      • Overseeing the construction, maintenance, and facility needs of school buildings.
  • School Funding
    • Most school funding in Texas comes from property taxes collected within the local school district.
    • Homeowners and businesses pay a percentage of their property's value annually to support local schools.
    • This reliance on local property values can lead to disparities in school quality and resources between different districts.

The Importance of Local Government: A Historical Perspective

Before widespread local governments, communities relied heavily on individuals and neighbors for services and security. For instance, the frontier setting of Old Yeller illustrates a time in Texas (1860s) where families had no nearby sheriff, fire department, or formal school structure. This highlights why communities eventually created local governments to establish shared rules, services, and a support system beyond individual households.

How Local Government Affects You Every Day

Local government is deeply intertwined with your daily life.

  • School District: Dictates school start times and curriculum.
  • City Government: Builds and maintains roads for buses, provides police/fire protection.
  • County Government: Keeps official records and manages local elections.
  • Alongside state and federal governments, local governments ensure the essential services that make communities functional.

Three Levels of Government

The United States government is structured into three distinct yet interconnected levels:

  1. Federal Government:
    • Covers the entire country.
    • Makes national laws.
    • Led by the President and Congress.
    • Provides some education grants and protects civil rights.
  2. State Government:
    • Covers one specific state.
    • Makes state-level laws.
    • Led by the Governor and the state legislature.
    • Sets minimum education standards and provides some school funding.
  3. Local Government:
    • Covers specific counties, cities, or school districts.
    • Makes local laws (ordinances).
    • Led by officials like commissioners, mayors, and school boards.
    • The most accessible level for citizens, offering direct engagement opportunities.

  4. Beyond the classroom, identify three specific ways local government impacts your daily life, providing examples for each.
