Chapter 4, Section 1 Study Guide: The Colonies and the King

Overview

This section explores how the American colonies, due to their distance from England, developed strong traditions of self-government over 150 years. This autonomy was challenged when Britain, after the expensive French and Indian War, began imposing taxes to pay for colonial defense. The colonists, lacking representation in Parliament, viewed these taxes as tyranny, leading to a growing crisis that would ultimately spark the American Revolution.

Key Concepts

  • Colonial Self-Government: Due to thousands of miles separating them from England, the colonists developed their own methods of governance, fostering a strong tradition of making local political decisions.
  • Early Forms of Representative Government: Examples like the Virginia House of Burgesses (the first elected legislative body in the Americas) and New England town meetings demonstrated colonists' early and active participation in governing themselves.
  • Salutary Neglect: For much of the colonial period, Britain practiced a policy of "salutary neglect," largely leaving the colonies to manage their own affairs as long as they remained profitable trading partners.
  • Shift in British Policy (Post-1763): After the costly French and Indian War, Britain ended salutary neglect and sought to raise revenue from the colonies to help pay for their defense and imperial expenses.
  • "No Taxation Without Representation": This became the colonists' rallying cry against new British taxes (like the Stamp Act and Townshend Acts). They argued that Parliament had no right to tax them without granting them elected representatives to voice their interests in that body.
  • Escalation of Tensions: A series of events, including the Boston Massacre, the Boston Tea Party, and the British response with the Intolerable Acts, highlighted the growing animosity and moved the colonies closer to rebellion.

Important Terms

  • colony (KOL-uh-nee): A territory settled and governed by people from a distant country. The thirteen American colonies were settled by people from England and other European nations.
  • self-government: When a community makes its own political decisions rather than being governed by an outside power.
  • representative: A person chosen to speak and act on behalf of others. Colonial assemblies were early forms of representative government.
  • taxation without representation: Being required to pay taxes to a government in which you have no elected voice. This was a major colonial grievance against Britain and a central cause of the American Revolution.

Detailed Notes

The Foundations of Colonial Governance

  • Early Settlement (1607): English settlers arrived in Virginia, facing the challenge of building a new life and establishing governance thousands of miles from the English king.
  • Development of Self-Reliance: The vast distance from England encouraged colonists to develop their own systems for making political decisions.
  • Formation of Thirteen Colonies: Over the next century, thirteen English colonies emerged along the Atlantic coast, from Massachusetts to Georgia.
    • Reasons for Immigration: People came for various reasons, including religious freedom, economic opportunity, and the chance for a new life.
    • Shared Experience: Despite developing unique characteristics, all colonies shared the experience of making many decisions independently due to their distance from England.

Colonial Self-Government in Practice

  • Virginia House of Burgesses (1619):
    • Established just twelve years after the first settlement, it was the first elected legislative body in the Americas.
    • Colonists elected representatives to meet and create laws for the colony.
  • New England Town Meetings:
    • These were direct democracies where colonists gathered to debate and vote on local issues.
  • Experience Over Time: For over 150 years, each colony developed its own legislature, giving Americans significant practical experience in self-governance by the time of the American Revolution.

Britain's Changing Relationship with the Colonies

  • Salutary Neglect: For most of the colonial period, Britain maintained a policy of "salutary neglect," meaning they largely left the colonies to govern themselves. Britain benefited from the colonies as profitable trading partners and was preoccupied with European conflicts and politics.
  • The End of Salutary Neglect (1763):
    • French and Indian War: Britain had just concluded the expensive French and Indian War (known as the Seven Years' War in Europe).
    • Financial Need: The British government was in significant debt and needed money.
    • New Policy: Parliament looked to the prosperous American colonies, deciding they should contribute financially to their own defense and the cost of the empire.

The Grievance of "No Taxation Without Representation"

  • New British Taxes:
    • Stamp Act of 1765: Required colonists to pay a tax on paper goods, including newspapers, legal documents, and playing cards.
    • Townshend Acts: Imposed taxes on imported goods.
  • Colonial Outrage: Colonists were furious because:
    • They were accustomed to their own legislatures taxing them.
    • They had no elected representatives in the British Parliament, which was now imposing taxes on them.
  • The Rallying Cry: "No taxation without representation!" became the central argument. Colonists believed that if Britain wanted to tax them, they needed elected representatives in Parliament; otherwise, it was considered an act of tyranny and a violation of their rights as English citizens.

Escalation to Crisis

  • Boston Massacre (1770): British soldiers fired into a crowd of colonists in Boston, killing five people and further intensifying anti-British sentiment.
  • Boston Tea Party (1773): In protest of the Tea Act (which gave the British East India Company a monopoly on tea sales and included a tax), colonists disguised as Native Americans dumped an entire shipment of British tea into Boston Harbor.
  • Intolerable Acts (1774): Britain's harsh response to the Boston Tea Party:
    • Closed Boston Harbor.
    • Severely curtailed Massachusetts's self-government.
    • Aimed to punish Massachusetts and reassert British authority.
  • First Continental Congress (1774): In response to the Intolerable Acts, colonial leaders met to coordinate a unified response, marking a significant step toward colonial unity and resistance.
  • Path to Independence: By 1774, the crisis was well underway, setting the stage for the Declaration of Independence and the American Revolution.

Review Questions

  1. How did the geographical distance between England and the American colonies contribute to the development of self-government? Provide specific examples of early colonial self-governance.
  2. Explain the British policy of "salutary neglect." What caused Britain to abandon this policy, and how did this change impact the relationship with the colonies?
  3. What was the core principle behind the colonists' rallying cry, "No taxation without representation"? Why was this specific grievance so significant to their identity and rights?
  4. Describe three specific events that marked the growing crisis between Britain and the colonies between 1765 and 1774. How did each event contribute to escalating tensions?
  5. Imagine you are a colonist in 1774. How would you explain to a new arrival from England why you believe Parliament's actions are tyrannical, even though Parliament claims to be acting in the best interest of the empire?
