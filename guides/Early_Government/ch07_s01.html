Chapter 7, Section 1 Study Guide: What Makes a Law?

Overview

This section defines what a "law" is, distinguishing it from mere rules or customs, and explains why this distinction is crucial for understanding justice. It explores the different levels of government responsible for creating laws and introduces the hierarchy of legal authority in the United States. Finally, it outlines the key characteristics that define a "good" or just law according to the Founders' principles.

Key Concepts

  • Law vs. Rules vs. Customs: It's essential to understand that not all expectations or guidelines are laws.
    • Custom: A social tradition or habit, not enforced by government (e.g., saying "please").
    • Rule: A specific expectation in a particular setting, with consequences from that setting's authority (e.g., school rules).
    • Law: A formal rule made by a government, enforced with legal punishments (fines, jail, community service).
  • Sources of Law: Laws are made by different levels of government, each with its own jurisdiction.
    • Federal Laws (Statutes): Passed by Congress and signed by the President, apply nationwide (e.g., income tax, immigration).
    • State Laws (Statutes): Passed by state legislatures, apply within a specific state (e.g., state speed limits, driver's license rules).
    • Local Ordinances: Passed by city councils or county commissioners, apply within a specific city or county (e.g., noise ordinances, leash laws).
  • Hierarchy of Law: This principle establishes the order of authority for legal documents, meaning a lower law cannot contradict a higher one. The U.S. Constitution is the supreme law of the land.
    1. U.S. Constitution
    2. Federal statutes and treaties
    3. State constitutions
    4. State statutes
    5. Local ordinances
  • Characteristics of a Good Law: For laws to be considered just and fair, they should meet specific standards:
    • Apply equally to everyone.
    • Serve the common good.
    • Be publicly known.
    • Be enforced consistently.

Important Terms

  • Law: A rule made by a government that everyone in its territory must follow; breaking a law can result in punishment.
  • Custom: A tradition or habit that many people follow, but which is not enforced by the government.
  • Statute: A law written down and officially passed by a legislature (can be federal or state).
  • Ordinance: A law made by a local government (city or county).
  • Justice: The quality of being fair and righteous; the administration of law in a fair way.

Detailed Notes

The Difference Between Rules, Customs, and Laws

Understanding the foundation of a society's legal framework begins with distinguishing between various forms of societal expectations.

  • Customs: These are informal traditions or habits that are widely accepted and practiced within a community. They dictate social etiquette and common courtesies, such as saying "thank you." While following customs can lead to social approval and breaking them might lead to social disapproval or awkwardness, there are no formal, governmental punishments associated with them.
  • Rules: Rules are more formal expectations set within specific environments or activities. Examples include classroom rules, school policies, or rules in a game. Breaking a rule typically results in consequences enforced by the authority within that specific setting (e.g., detention for breaking a school rule, being benched in a game). These consequences are distinct from legal punishments.
  • Laws: Laws are the most formal and authoritative type of rule. They are created by governments and apply to everyone within the government's jurisdiction. What sets laws apart is their enforceability: governments use their authority, through police and courts, to ensure compliance. Consequences for breaking a law are legally binding and can include fines, community service, or imprisonment.

Who Makes the Laws?

In the United States, lawmaking power is distributed among different levels of government, reflecting the principle of federalism. No level of government can pass a law that contradicts the U.S. Constitution.

  • Federal Laws (Statutes): These laws are created by the U.S. Congress, a legislative body composed of the Senate and House of Representatives. Once passed by both chambers, a bill becomes law after being signed by the President. Federal laws apply to all 50 states and U.S. territories. Examples include laws related to federal income tax, immigration policy, protection of intellectual property (like copyright), and national defense.
  • State Laws (Statutes): Each of the 50 states has its own legislature (like the Texas Legislature mentioned in the text) responsible for creating laws that apply only within that specific state's borders. These laws cover a wide range of issues not exclusively handled by the federal government, such as state highway speed limits, requirements for obtaining a driver's license, and most criminal offenses.
  • Local Ordinances: These are laws passed by local governmental bodies, such as city councils for cities or commissioners courts for counties. Ordinances apply only within the boundaries of that specific city or county. They often address community-specific issues like noise regulations, zoning (how land can be used), animal control (e.g., leash laws), and local sanitation rules.

Hierarchy of Law

The "Hierarchy of Law" is a fundamental concept that ensures order and consistency within the legal system. It establishes a clear chain of authority, meaning that any law at a lower level cannot conflict with a law at a higher level. If such a conflict arises, the higher-level law takes precedence.

  1. U.S. Constitution: This is the supreme law of the land, establishing the framework of the federal government and guaranteeing fundamental rights. No other law, federal, state, or local, can violate its provisions.
  2. Federal Statutes and Treaties: Laws passed by Congress and international agreements ratified by the U.S. government are next in line.
  3. State Constitutions: Each state has its own constitution, which sets up its state government and defines rights within the state. State laws must adhere to both the U.S. Constitution and their respective state constitutions.
  4. State Statutes: Laws passed by state legislatures must comply with both the federal and state constitutions, as well as federal statutes and treaties.
  5. Local Ordinances: At the bottom of the hierarchy, local laws must be consistent with all higher forms of law.

What Makes a Good Law?

The concept of "justice" is central to understanding what constitutes a "good" or fair law. Drawing on ancient philosophical traditions, the Founders of the United States emphasized several key characteristics:

  • Apply Equally to Everyone: A just law does not favor one group or individual over another. It should be impartial, meaning the same rules and consequences apply to all citizens, regardless of their background, wealth, or status.
  • Serve the Common Good: Good laws are designed to benefit the entire community or society, rather than just a select few. They aim to promote safety, order, welfare, and general prosperity for all.
  • Be Publicly Known: For a law to be fair, citizens must have access to its text and be aware of its existence. It is unjust to punish someone for breaking a law they could not possibly have known about. This principle underlies the importance of published legal codes and public education about laws.
  • Be Enforced Consistently: The application and enforcement of laws should be predictable and uniform. Similar actions should consistently receive similar responses from law enforcement and the judiciary, preventing arbitrary or biased application of justice.

When laws fail to meet these standards, citizens have the right and responsibility to advocate for change through various democratic means, including speaking out, voting for representatives who support reform, and organizing peaceful movements to challenge unjust laws.

Word Origins

  • The word "Law" originates from the Old Norse word lag, meaning "something laid down" or "fixed," highlighting the idea of a permanent, established rule.
  • The word "Justice" comes from the Latin justitia, derived from justus meaning "righteous" or "fair." This Latin root also gives us related legal terms such as judge, jury, and judicial.

Review Questions

  1. Describe the key differences between a custom, a rule, and a law, providing an example for each category to illustrate your points.
  2. Identify the three main levels of government responsible for making laws in the United States. For each level, name the legislative body that creates the laws and provide an example of a type of law they would typically make.
  3. Explain the concept of the "Hierarchy of Law." Why is this hierarchy important for the functioning of the legal system, and what happens when a lower law conflicts with a higher one?
  4. According to the text, what four characteristics define a "good" or just law? Choose one characteristic and explain why it is particularly important for ensuring fairness in a society.
  5. Imagine you believe a local ordinance in your town is unfair or does not serve the common good. Based on the information in this section, what are some actions citizens can take to try and change such a law?
