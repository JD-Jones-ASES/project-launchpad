Study Guide: Six Flags Over Texas

Overview

This section explores the rich and complex history of Texas by examining the six different national flags that have flown over its land. From its early days as a Spanish colony to its current status as a U.S. state, Texas has undergone significant transformations, with each period leaving a unique cultural, political, and social legacy on the region and its people.

Key Concepts

  • Six Flags Over Texas: This phrase represents the six distinct governments that have claimed sovereignty over Texas throughout its history: Spain, France, Mexico, the Republic of Texas, the Confederate States of America, and the United States of America.
  • Spanish Colonization and Influence (1519–1821): Spain was the first major European power to claim and slowly colonize Texas, establishing missions and presidios. Its legacy profoundly shaped Texas's language, religion (Catholicism), and legal traditions.
  • French Challenge (1685–1690): France briefly challenged Spanish control with La Salle's expedition and Fort Saint Louis, but its presence in Texas was short-lived.
  • Mexican Rule and American Settlement (1821–1836): After Mexico gained independence from Spain, Texas became a part of the new Mexican nation. Mexico encouraged American immigration through empresarios like Stephen F. Austin, but cultural and political tensions between settlers and the Mexican government ultimately led to a push for independence.
  • The Republic of Texas (1836–1845): For nine years, Texas existed as an independent country following its victory over Mexico. This period was marked by challenges in finance, defense, and international relations, eventually leading to annexation by the United States.
  • Texas in the Confederacy (1861–1865): Texas seceded from the United States to join the Confederate States of America during the Civil War, contributing soldiers and resources to the Southern cause.
  • Texas as a U.S. State (1845–present): Texas first joined the U.S. in 1845 and, after the Civil War, was fully readmitted in 1870, remaining one of the largest and most populous states today.

Important Terms

territory : Land that belongs to or is controlled by a government. colony : A territory settled by people from a distant country, who still follow that country's rules. sovereignty : The power of a government to rule its own land without being controlled by another government. republic : A government in which the people choose leaders to represent them. missions : Churches and communities built by Spanish missionaries to teach the Catholic faith to native peoples. presidios : Forts built by Spanish soldiers to protect settlements. New Spain : The vast Spanish colony that included Mexico, Texas, and stretched to California for about three centuries. empresarios : Land agents hired by the Mexican government to recruit families to settle in Texas. Lone Star : The nickname for the flag and symbol of the independent Republic of Texas. Union : Refers to the United States of America, especially during the Civil War era. Confederate States of America : The group of Southern states that seceded from the United States in 1861, including Texas.

Detailed Notes

Introduction: Six Flags Over Texas

The phrase "Six Flags Over Texas" symbolizes the six distinct governments that have ruled Texas throughout its history. Each of these periods left an enduring legacy on the land, culture, and people of Texas, shaping it into the state it is today.

Flag 1: Spain (1519–1821)

  • Initial Claim: In 1519, Spanish explorer Hernán Cortés claimed Mexico for Spain, beginning a centuries-long Spanish presence in North America.
  • Expansion into Texas: Over the next 300 years, Spain gradually expanded its control northward into the region known today as Texas.
  • Establishment of Settlements:
    • Missions: Spanish missionaries established churches and communities to convert and educate Native American populations in the Catholic faith.
    • Presidios: Spanish soldiers built forts (presidios) to protect these missions and other Spanish settlements from hostile groups or rival European powers.
  • New Spain: For roughly three centuries, Texas was an integral part of New Spain, a vast Spanish colony stretching from Mexico to California.
  • Lasting Impact: Spanish rule deeply influenced Texas culture, leaving a legacy of the Spanish language, the Catholic faith, and Spanish legal traditions.

Flag 2: France (1685–1690)

  • French Exploration: French explorer René-Robert Cavelier, known as La Salle, aimed to establish a French presence in Texas.
  • Fort Saint Louis: In 1685, La Salle sailed to the Gulf of Mexico and built a small fort, Fort Saint Louis, on the Texas coast, claiming the land for France.
  • Short-Lived Colony: The French colony faced numerous difficulties and ultimately failed within a few years. Spain's response to the French intrusion helped solidify its own claims in Texas.
  • Word Origins: Texas: The word "Texas" (or "Tejas") comes from a Caddo word meaning "friends" or "allies," which Spanish explorers adopted to name the region.

Flag 3: Mexico (1821–1836)

  • Independence from Spain: In 1821, Mexico successfully gained its independence from Spain, and Texas became part of the new Mexican nation.
  • Encouraging Settlement: The Mexican government sought to populate its northern territories, including Texas, and offered large grants of land to attract settlers.
  • Empresarios: Land agents, known as empresarios, were hired to recruit families to move to Texas.
  • Stephen F. Austin:
    • The most famous empresario, Austin brought hundreds of American families from the United States to Texas between 1821 and 1827.
    • He is known as the "Father of Texas" and initially worked peacefully with the Mexican government before supporting the Texas Revolution.
    • His father, Moses Austin, had originally received the land grant.
  • Growing Tensions: American settlers cultivated cotton, raised cattle, and established towns. However, significant tensions developed between these settlers and the Mexican government over issues such as taxes, the practice of slavery (which Mexico had outlawed), and local self-governance rights. These conflicts eventually pushed many Texans toward seeking independence.

Flag 4: The Republic of Texas (1836–1845)

  • Declaration of Independence: In 1836, Texas declared its independence from Mexico.
  • Key Battles:
    • The Alamo: A small force of Texans bravely defended the Alamo mission against a much larger Mexican army, though they were ultimately defeated. This became a rallying cry for Texan independence.
    • Battle of San Jacinto: On April 21, 1836, General Sam Houston led the Texan army to a decisive victory over Mexican President Santa Anna's forces, securing Texas's independence.
  • Independent Nation: For nine years, Texas operated as an independent country, known as the Republic of Texas.
    • It had its own president (Sam Houston served as the first and third), army, laws, and its distinct "Lone Star" flag.
  • Challenges and Annexation: The young republic faced considerable financial difficulties, ongoing threats from Mexico, and conflicts with Native American nations on the frontier. Many Texans desired to join the more stable and powerful United States.

Flag 5: The Confederate States of America (1861–1865)

  • Joining the U.S.: In 1845, Texas was annexed by the United States and became the twenty-eighth state.
  • Secession and Civil War: With the outbreak of the American Civil War in 1861, Texas voted to secede from the Union and joined the Confederate States of America.
  • Contribution to the Confederacy: Texas provided thousands of soldiers and resources to the Confederate war effort.
  • Reunion: The Civil War concluded in 1865 with a Union victory, and Texas was subsequently reunited with the United States.

Flag 6: The United States of America (1845–present)

  • Current Status: Today, Texas remains one of the fifty states in the United States.
  • Readmission: After the Civil War, Texas met the requirements set by Congress and was fully readmitted to the Union in 1870.
  • Prominent State: Since then, Texas has grown to become one of the largest and most populous states in the country, maintaining its unique cultural identity shaped by its diverse history.

Review Questions

  1. How did the "Six Flags Over Texas" concept help you understand the major shifts in power and culture throughout Texas history?
  2. What were the most significant lasting impacts of Spanish rule on Texas, and how do we see evidence of these influences today?
  3. Compare and contrast the reasons for American settlement in Texas under Mexican rule with the reasons for Texans seeking independence from Mexico.
  4. What challenges did the Republic of Texas face as an independent nation, and how did these challenges influence its decision to join the United States?
  5. Beyond just changing flags, how did each successive government fundamentally change the daily lives, laws, and cultural identity of the people living in Texas?
