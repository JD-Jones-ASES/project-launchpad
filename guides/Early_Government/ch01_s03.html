Chapter 1, Section 3: What Is Government? Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concept of government, defining it as the system responsible for making and enforcing rules within a community or country. It explores the essential functions of government, from providing public services to ensuring justice and protection. The section also delves into why people accept government, drawing on the ideas of philosopher John Locke, and highlights the reciprocal relationship between citizens and their government, including rights and responsibilities.

Key Concepts

  • Definition and Purpose of Government: Government is the organized system of people, laws, and offices that creates and enforces rules for a community or country. Its primary purpose is to establish order, provide services, and ensure the safety and well-being of its members.
  • Essential Functions of Government: Governments perform several critical roles: they make and enforce laws, provide necessary public services (like roads, schools, and safety), settle disagreements fairly, and protect the community from both internal and external threats.
  • Authority: Authority is the legitimate right or power a government possesses to make decisions and enforce its rules over the people living within its jurisdiction. This power is essential for a government to effectively carry out its functions.
  • John Locke's Philosophy on Why People Accept Government: The philosopher John Locke argued that people accept government because life without it ("a state of nature") would be dangerous, chaotic, and unfair. Individuals willingly give up some personal freedom to a government in exchange for protection, order, and fair resolution of disputes, thereby ensuring their safety and broader freedom.
  • Citizenship, Rights, and Responsibilities: A citizen is a recognized member of a community or country. Citizens have rights that the government must protect (e.g., freedom, fair treatment) and responsibilities they must fulfill to help the community function effectively (e.g., following laws, participating in community life).

Important Terms

  • Government: The system of people, laws, and offices that makes and enforces rules for a community or country.
  • Authority: The right or power to make decisions and enforce rules. A government has authority over those who live under it.
  • Citizen: A person who is a member of a community or country, with both rights and responsibilities.
  • Justice: Treating people fairly. A just government protects everyone equally under the law.
  • Common Good: The well-being or benefit of the entire community or society, rather than just individual interests. Governments aim to provide for the common good through their services, laws, and protections.

Detailed Notes

What Is Government?

Government is the fundamental framework that organizes a community or country. It consists of the people, laws, and offices necessary to create and enforce rules. This system provides structure and order, much like a principal manages a school to ensure it runs smoothly and safely. Without government, essential functions like maintaining infrastructure, ensuring safety, and resolving conflicts would be absent, leading to chaos.

What Government Does

Governments perform a variety of crucial tasks to maintain order and provide for their communities:

  1. Makes Laws: Governments create rules that everyone within the community must follow. These laws provide a framework for acceptable behavior and societal expectations.
  2. Enforces Laws: Beyond making rules, governments ensure that laws are followed. When laws are broken, the government, through its legal and judicial systems, determines the consequences.
  3. Provides Services: Governments offer essential services that benefit the entire community. These can include building and maintaining roads, operating schools, providing public safety (police, fire departments), and running courts.
  4. Settles Disagreements: When conflicts arise between individuals or groups, the government provides mechanisms, such as courts, to resolve these disputes fairly and peacefully.
  5. Protects the Community: Governments are responsible for defending their community from both internal threats (like crime) and external threats (from other countries or entities).

Even small communities, like a classroom, demonstrate the basic principles of government, where a teacher sets and enforces rules to create an orderly learning environment.

Why Do People Accept Government?

The question of why people accept being governed has been pondered by philosophers for centuries. John Locke, an English philosopher from the 1600s, offered a compelling argument:

  • Life Without Government is Dangerous: Locke believed that without government, people would live in a "state of nature" where there are no established rules, no courts to settle disputes, and no authority to protect individuals from stronger or more aggressive people. In such a state, the powerful would dominate, and the weak would suffer.
  • Agreement for Protection and Order: Locke argued that people, recognizing the dangers and unfairness of life without rules, implicitly agree to form governments. This "social contract" involves individuals giving up some personal freedom—the freedom to do anything they want—in exchange for the government's protection of their safety and essential freedoms, as well as the establishment of order and justice.
  • Influence on Founding Documents: Locke's ideas were highly influential on the framers of America's founding documents, particularly the Declaration of Independence, underscoring the government's role in protecting the natural rights of its citizens.

Citizens and Government

The relationship between citizens and their government is reciprocal:

  • Citizenship: A citizen is a member of a community or country who is both protected by the government and has responsibilities towards it.
  • Rights: Citizens possess certain rights that the government is obligated to protect. These might include rights to free speech, fair trial, or the pursuit of happiness.
  • Responsibilities: Alongside rights, citizens also have responsibilities. These are actions they must take to help their community function well, such as obeying laws, paying taxes, participating in civic life, and treating others fairly. Practicing good citizenship, even in small ways like following school rules or being fair to classmates, contributes to the overall well-being and justice of the community.

Review Questions

  1. Based on the definition of government provided, can a family be considered a form of government? What are the similarities and differences?
  2. Imagine a community that suddenly had no government or rules. According to John Locke's ideas, what would likely happen, and what challenges would its members face?
  3. John Locke suggested people "agree" to have governments. If you were born into your country and didn't explicitly choose its government, do you still have a responsibility to accept its authority? Why or why not?
  4. Think about your school's rules. Name one rule that you believe serves the "common good" of all students and staff. Can you identify any school rule that you think is unfair, and how would you propose changing it to better serve justice?
  5. What is the essential difference between a "rule" and a "law"? Why does it matter who makes a rule or law in terms of its authority and enforcement?
