What Is a Good Citizen? Understanding Civic Virtue

Overview

This section defines what it means to be a "good citizen" by introducing the concept of civic virtue. It explains that civic virtue involves character qualities like honesty, responsibility, and courage that are essential for the common good and the success of a self-governing republic. Through historical examples like Cincinnatus and George Washington, the text illustrates how putting community interests above personal power is fundamental to a free society and applies to everyday actions of all citizens.

Key Concepts

  • Civic Virtue: The essential character qualities citizens need to contribute positively to the common good and sustain a republic. It emphasizes placing the community's welfare over individual selfish interests.
  • Importance of Civic Virtue for Republics: Self-government, such as that in a republic, cannot long survive without citizens who are honest, responsible, informed, and willing to work for the common good. Without these virtues, republics risk devolving into dictatorships.
  • Key Civic Virtues: Specific virtues highlighted include honesty, responsibility, courage, respect for law, concern for the common good, and fairness. Each plays a vital role in maintaining social trust and order.
  • Historical Models of Civic Virtue: Figures like Lucius Quinctius Cincinnatus and George Washington exemplify civic virtue by voluntarily relinquishing immense power once their duty was done, prioritizing the stability and integrity of their country over personal ambition.
  • Peaceful Transfer of Power: George Washington's decision to resign after two terms established a crucial tradition in America: the peaceful, voluntary transfer of presidential power, which is fundamental to a functioning democracy.
  • Civic Virtue in Everyday Life: Civic virtue is not exclusive to leaders; it is practiced daily through small acts of truth-telling, standing up for others, community care, respectful listening, and active learning about community needs.

Important Terms

  • Civic Virtue: The qualities of character that help a citizen contribute to the common good; examples include honesty, courage, responsibility, and justice.
  • Patriotism: Love of and loyalty to one's country, expressed through both pride and a commitment to making it better.
  • Common Good: What is best for the whole community, not just for one person or group.
  • Public Service: Work done for the benefit of the community, whether paid (as a government official) or unpaid (as a volunteer).

Detailed Notes

The Story of Cincinnatus: An Example of Civic Virtue

The section opens with the ancient Roman farmer, Lucius Quinctius Cincinnatus. In 458 BC, when Rome faced a severe military crisis, the Senate called upon Cincinnatus to become dictator—a temporary leader with absolute power—to save the city.

  • Acceptance and Action: Cincinnatus, found plowing his field, immediately accepted the call to duty.
  • Swift Resolution: Within 15 days, he successfully organized the army, rescued Roman forces, defeated the enemy, and returned in triumph.
  • Remarkable Resignation: Crucially, Cincinnatus then did something extraordinary: he resigned his dictatorial power and returned to his farm. He believed that a good man should not hold onto power beyond what is necessary.
  • Illustrating Civic Virtue: This act epitomizes civic virtue, demonstrating selflessness and a commitment to the common good over personal gain or continued authority.

What Is Civic Virtue?

Civic virtue refers to the character traits that enable a republic to function effectively.

  • Foundation of Self-Government: A republic, where citizens govern themselves, relies on its people being honest, responsible, informed, and willing to prioritize the common good over individual selfish interests, at least some of the time.
  • Historical Consensus: Ancient Romans, Greek philosophers, and America's Founders all agreed that self-government is impossible without civic virtue. Without it, a selfish, dishonest, or ignorant populace will eventually surrender its freedom to powerful figures, leading to dictatorships.

Key Civic Virtues Explained

The text highlights several specific virtues crucial for citizens:

  • Honesty: Telling the truth, even when difficult. This builds the trust necessary for society to operate effectively, as dishonesty from citizens or officials damages this trust.
  • Responsibility: Fulfilling one's duties to family, community, and country without needing to be forced. It's about proactive engagement and accountability.
  • Courage: Standing up for what is right, even when there's a personal cost or risk. Examples include Ruby Bridges during the Civil Rights Movement and the suffragists advocating for women's voting rights.
  • Respect for Law: Adhering to laws, even those one dislikes. If a law is considered unjust, citizens should work through legal means to change it, rather than disregard it.
  • Concern for the Common Good: Thinking beyond personal benefit to consider what is advantageous for the entire community and nation.
  • Fairness: Treating all individuals as one would wish to be treated, without prejudice or bias based on who they are.

George Washington: A Model of Civic Virtue

George Washington serves as another powerful historical example of civic virtue.

  • Revolutionary War Leader: He commanded the Continental Army for eight arduous years during the Revolutionary War, leading them to victory.
  • Refusal of Kingship: After the war ended in 1783, despite calls from soldiers and citizens to become king, Washington famously refused, choosing instead to return to his farm at Mount Vernon.
  • First President and Second Resignation: Six years later, he became the first President of the United States. After serving two terms, he voluntarily resigned again, setting a precedent.
  • Establishing Peaceful Transfer of Power: Washington's repeated willingness to give up power was unprecedented among leaders throughout history. His actions established the critical tradition of the peaceful transfer of power through elections in America, demonstrating that leaders choose to follow rules over grasping for indefinite power.

Civic Virtue in Everyday Life

Civic virtue is not limited to historical figures or grand gestures; it is evident in daily small actions:

  • Acknowledging Mistakes: Telling the truth after making an error.
  • Advocacy for Others: Standing up for a classmate who is being treated unfairly.
  • Community Care: Picking up litter in a park, even if one didn't cause it.
  • Respectful Dialogue: Listening respectfully to individuals who hold different opinions.
  • Active Citizenship: Learning about one's community and understanding its needs. A republic is a dual construction: it is built from the top down by its laws and institutions, and from the bottom up by the countless daily choices and actions of its ordinary citizens.

Review Questions

  1. How did the actions of Cincinnatus and George Washington exemplify civic virtue, particularly regarding their use and relinquishment of power? Why were these actions considered so remarkable?
  2. According to the text, why is civic virtue considered essential for the survival and success of a republic? What might happen if citizens lack these virtues?
  3. Choose three of the key civic virtues listed (Honesty, Responsibility, Courage, Respect for law, Concern for the common good, Fairness) and explain how each contributes to a healthy community.
  4. Beyond the examples given in the text, can you think of a real-life scenario from current events or your own experience where an individual demonstrated civic virtue? Describe the situation and which virtue(s) were displayed.
  5. The text states that a republic is built "from the top down by its laws and institutions—and from the bottom up by the daily choices of millions of ordinary citizens." What does this statement mean, and how do individual acts of civic virtue contribute to the "bottom-up" building of a republic?
