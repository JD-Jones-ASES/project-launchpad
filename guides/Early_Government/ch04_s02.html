Independence: The Big Idea Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the pivotal moment of American independence, focusing on the creation and core ideas of the Declaration of Independence. It delves into the dangerous decision by the colonists to break from Great Britain, the influential philosophical principles that underpinned their claim to freedom, and the key figures who shaped this revolutionary document. At its heart, the Declaration champions natural rights, self-governance, and the idea that governments derive their power from the consent of the governed.

Key Concepts

  • Independence: The fundamental concept of freedom from control by another government, allowing a people to govern themselves. The American colonies sought this freedom from British rule.
  • Natural Rights: Rights inherent to all people simply because they are human. These rights, identified by John Locke as life, liberty, and property (adapted by Jefferson to "the pursuit of Happiness"), exist prior to and are not granted by governments.
  • Unalienable Rights: A specific category of natural rights that cannot be taken away or given away, even by governments, reinforcing their inherent and inviolable nature.
  • Consent of the Governed: The foundational principle that governments derive their legitimate authority from the agreement of the people they rule. If a government fails to protect the people's rights or acts against their consent, the people have the right to alter or abolish it.
  • Thomas Paine's Common Sense: A highly influential pamphlet published in January 1776 that used plain, direct language to argue for American independence from monarchy, swaying public opinion significantly toward revolution.
  • Declaration of Independence: The formal document, primarily authored by Thomas Jefferson, that declared the 13 American colonies independent from Great Britain and articulated the philosophical principles of self-governance and individual rights.

Important Terms

  • Independence: Freedom from control by another government. To be independent means to govern yourself.
  • Natural Rights: Rights that belong to all people simply because they are human. They are not granted by governments; they exist before governments do. Thinker John Locke identified life, liberty, and property as natural rights.
  • Unalienable: Cannot be taken away or given away. The Declaration calls certain rights "unalienable" — even governments cannot remove them.
  • Consent of the Governed: The idea that governments get their power from the agreement of the people they rule. If a government loses that consent, the people have the right to change it.
  • Treason: The crime of betraying one's country, especially by attempting to overthrow its government. Signing the Declaration of Independence was considered treason by the British.
  • Revolutionary War: The armed conflict (1775-1783) between Great Britain and its 13 American colonies, which resulted in the independence of the United States.

Detailed Notes

I. The Perilous Path to Independence

  • A Declaration of Rebellion: On July 4, 1776, 56 men signed a document that explicitly declared their rebellion against the powerful British Empire. This act was considered treason under British law, punishable by death, highlighting the immense risk taken by the signers.
  • The Outbreak of War: By 1775, hostilities had already begun with the Battles of Lexington and Concord, marking the official start of the Revolutionary War.
  • Governing in Crisis: The Second Continental Congress, meeting in Philadelphia, began to function as the de facto government for the colonies, coordinating the war effort and deliberating on the future.
  • Public Opinion Shifts:
    • In January 1776, Thomas Paine's influential pamphlet, Common Sense, was published.
    • Paine argued forcefully and simply that monarchy was an absurd system and that complete American independence was necessary.
    • The pamphlet's widespread circulation (hundreds of thousands of copies) galvanized colonial support for separation from Britain.
  • Drafting the Formal Declaration: By June 1776, the momentum for independence was undeniable, prompting Congress to form a committee to draft a formal declaration.
    • Thomas Jefferson, a 33-year-old lawyer and planter from Virginia, was chosen as the primary author. He meticulously worked for 17 days to produce the initial draft.

II. The Declaration's Foundational Principles

  • Beyond a List of Grievances: The Declaration of Independence is far more than just a catalog of complaints against King George III. Its most profound section articulates universal principles that Jefferson described as "self-evident truths."
  • Influence of John Locke: These revolutionary ideas were deeply rooted in the philosophy of John Locke, an Enlightenment thinker.
    • Natural Rights: Locke identified fundamental human rights as life, liberty, and property. Jefferson adopted this concept, famously rephrasing it in the Declaration to life, liberty, and the pursuit of Happiness. This emphasized that these rights are inherent to humanity and not bestowed by governments.
    • Purpose of Government: The Declaration asserts that governments are instituted to protect these unalienable natural rights.
  • Consent of the Governed: A cornerstone of the Declaration, this principle states that governments derive their just powers from the agreement (consent) of the people they govern.
    • If a government fails in its duty to protect the people's rights, or actively infringes upon them, the people possess the right to alter or abolish that government and establish a new one.
  • Unalienable Rights: The Declaration stresses that certain rights are "unalienable," meaning they cannot be taken away or surrendered, underscoring their inherent and sacred nature.

III. The Vote and Official Signing

  • Approving Independence: On July 2, 1776, the Continental Congress formally voted to approve Richard Henry Lee's resolution for independence, making the separation from Britain official. John Adams believed this day should be the most celebrated.
  • Approving the Text: Two days later, on July 4, 1776, Congress approved Jefferson's final text of the Declaration of Independence. This is the date historically celebrated as America's Independence Day.
  • Official Signing: The delegates signed the official parchment copy of the Declaration on August 2, 1776.

IV. Key Figures Behind the Declaration

  • Thomas Jefferson (1743–1826):
    • A Virginia planter, lawyer, and an avid scholar with extensive knowledge of philosophy, science, and history, including John Locke's works.
    • His exceptional talent with words led to his selection as the main author of the Declaration.
    • He later served in various high-ranking government roles, including ambassador to France, Secretary of State, Vice President, and the third President of the United States.
    • Complex Legacy: Jefferson is remembered as a complex figure who penned the enduring words "all men are created equal" while personally enslaving over 600 people during his lifetime. This contradiction between his ideals and actions remains a subject of ongoing debate in American history.
  • Benjamin Franklin (1706–1790):
    • At 70 years old, Franklin was the oldest delegate to sign the Declaration, already possessing worldwide fame as a scientist (known for his kite and lightning experiment), inventor, and writer.
    • He played a crucial diplomatic role as ambassador to France, where he successfully secured vital French military and financial support for the American Revolution, which was instrumental to American victory.
    • He continued his service to the nascent nation, attending the Constitutional Convention at age 81, where he advocated for the new Constitution despite acknowledging its imperfections.

Review Questions

  1. Why was signing the Declaration of Independence considered an act of treason by the British, and what were the potential consequences for the signers?
  2. How did Thomas Paine's pamphlet, Common Sense, influence public opinion in the colonies and contribute to the push for independence?
  3. Explain the core philosophical ideas from John Locke that Thomas Jefferson incorporated into the Declaration of Independence, particularly regarding natural rights and the purpose of government.
  4. What does the phrase "consent of the governed" mean, and why is this concept fundamental to the Declaration's argument for legitimate government?
  5. Discuss the duality of Thomas Jefferson's legacy, considering his powerful articulation of equality in the Declaration alongside his personal practice of enslaving people. How has this contradiction been debated throughout American history?
