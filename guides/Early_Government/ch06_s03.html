Study Guide: Three Branches in Austin

Overview

This section explains that the Texas government operates with a structure mirroring the U.S. federal government, featuring three distinct branches: Executive, Legislative, and Judicial. While the fundamental design of separated powers and checks and balances is the same, Texas has its own unique officials, specific powers, and procedural differences, such as a biennial legislative session and two supreme courts.

Key Concepts

  • Three Branches of Texas Government: Texas's state government, like the U.S. federal system, is divided into Executive, Legislative, and Judicial branches to ensure separation of powers and checks and balances.
  • Executive Branch (The Governor): Headed by the Governor, this branch is responsible for carrying out state laws. Unlike the federal system, many top state executive officials (like the Attorney General and Comptroller) are directly elected by voters, not appointed.
  • Legislative Branch (The Texas Legislature): This branch is responsible for making state laws and consists of two chambers: the Texas Senate and the Texas House of Representatives. A key difference from the U.S. Congress is its biennial session, meeting only once every two years.
  • Judicial Branch (Texas Courts): This branch interprets laws and settles disputes. Uniquely, Texas has two highest courts: the Texas Supreme Court for civil cases and the Texas Court of Criminal Appeals for criminal cases. Texas judges are elected by voters, unlike federally appointed judges.
  • Powerful Lieutenant Governor: The Lieutenant Governor of Texas is unusually influential, presiding over the Texas Senate and controlling the legislative agenda, making them one of the most powerful figures in state government.
  • Comparison to Federal Government: While similar in structure, the Texas government differs in specifics such as the frequency of legislative sessions, the number of top courts, and the method of selecting many executive officials and all judges.
  • The Texas Pledge of Allegiance: Texas has its own state pledge, often recited alongside the U.S. Pledge, emphasizing the state's unique identity.

Important Terms

  • governor (GUV-er-ner): The head of a state's executive branch; like the “president” of the state.
  • lieutenant governor (loo-TEN-ant): The second-highest official in the state executive branch; also presides over the Texas Senate.
  • biennial session (by-EE-nee-ul): A legislative meeting that happens every two years rather than every year.
  • judiciary (joo-DISH-ee-air-ee): The court system; the branch of government that interprets laws and settles disputes.
  • Attorney General: The state's top lawyer.
  • Comptroller: The state official who manages the state's money.
  • Texas Senate: The upper chamber of the Texas Legislature, with 31 senators serving four-year terms.
  • Texas House of Representatives: The lower chamber of the Texas Legislature, with 150 members serving two-year terms.
  • civil cases: Legal disputes between people or organizations over money, contracts, or rights.
  • criminal cases: Legal disputes involving crimes against the law.

Detailed Notes

The Three Branches of Texas Government

The Texas government is designed with the same fundamental structure as the U.S. federal government: three distinct branches with separate powers and checks and balances on each other. These branches operate from Austin, the state capital, and are responsible for governing the state of Texas.

The Executive Branch: The Governor

  • Role: The Governor of Texas is the chief executive, heading the state's executive branch. Their primary job is to faithfully carry out Texas laws and lead the state government.
  • Term: Governors serve four-year terms and can be re-elected multiple times without term limits.
  • Key Powers:
    • Signing or Vetoing Bills: The governor has the power to approve legislation by signing bills into law or to reject them by issuing a veto.
    • Commanding the Texas National Guard: As commander-in-chief of the state's military forces.
    • Appointing Officials: The governor appoints judges, agency heads, and members of various state boards.
    • Calling Special Sessions: If urgent issues arise, the governor can call the Legislature into a special session outside of its regular schedule.
    • Representing Texas: The governor acts as the chief representative of Texas to other states and to the federal government.
  • Other Key Executive Officials: In addition to the Governor, other important executive officials include the Lieutenant Governor, the Attorney General (the state's top lawyer), and the Comptroller (who manages the state's finances).
  • Difference from Federal System: Unlike the U.S. President, who appoints a Cabinet, most of Texas's top executive officials are elected directly by voters, leading to a "plural executive" system where power is distributed among several independent elected officials.
  • Historical Note (Sam Houston): Sam Houston was a pivotal figure in Texas history. He commanded the Texas Army at the Battle of San Jacinto, securing Texas's independence. He served as the first and third President of the Republic of Texas, a U.S. Senator, and Governor of the state. He is remembered for his courage, especially for refusing to swear allegiance to the Confederacy in 1861, which led to his removal from office.

The Legislative Branch: The Texas Legislature

  • Role: The Texas Legislature is responsible for creating and passing state laws.
  • Structure (Bicameral): Like the U.S. Congress, it is bicameral, meaning it has two chambers:
    • Texas Senate: Composed of 31 senators, each serving four-year terms.
    • Texas House of Representatives: Consists of 150 members, each serving two-year terms.
  • Biennial Session: A significant difference from the U.S. Congress is that the Texas Legislature meets in a biennial session, meaning it convenes only once every two years. These regular sessions occur in odd-numbered years (e.g., 2025, 2027) for approximately 140 days. The governor retains the power to call special sessions for urgent matters during even-numbered years. This biennial schedule reflects a historical distrust of government and a desire to keep legislators as citizen-lawmakers rather than full-time politicians.
  • Key Jobs of the Legislature:
    • Writing and Passing Laws: Crafting and enacting legislation that governs the state.
    • Approving the State Budget: Determining how Texas will spend its money, a critical financial responsibility.
    • Overriding Governor's Veto: With a two-thirds vote in both chambers, the Legislature can override a governor's veto, turning a bill into law despite the governor's opposition.
    • Proposing Constitutional Amendments: Proposing changes to the Texas Constitution, which then must be approved by voters.
  • Power of the Lieutenant Governor: The Lieutenant Governor of Texas holds an exceptionally powerful position. Unlike the U.S. Vice President, who has limited powers in the Senate, the Texas Lieutenant Governor presides over the Texas Senate and has significant control over which bills are debated and voted on. This makes the Lieutenant Governor arguably the most powerful single official in Texas state government.

The Judicial Branch: Texas Courts

  • Role: The judiciary is the court system responsible for interpreting Texas laws and settling legal disputes.
  • Unique Structure (Two Top Courts): Texas's court system is unique because it has two highest courts, rather than one:
    • Texas Supreme Court: This court consists of nine justices and handles all civil cases (disputes between individuals or organizations over matters like money, contracts, or rights).
    • Texas Court of Criminal Appeals: This court also has nine judges and exclusively handles criminal cases (cases involving alleged violations of the law).
  • Court Hierarchy: Below these two top courts are a series of lower courts, including Courts of Appeals, District Courts, County Courts, and Justice of the Peace Courts.
  • Elected Judges: Another major difference from the federal system is that Texas judges are elected by voters, from the local level all the way up to the highest courts. Federal judges, in contrast, are appointed by the President.

Memory Work: The Texas Pledge of Allegiance

Texas has its own pledge, often recited in schools alongside the U.S. Pledge. It states: "Honor the Texas flag; I pledge allegiance to thee, Texas, one state under God, one and indivisible." The use of the word "indivisible," similar to the U.S. Pledge, suggests a strong sense of unity and permanence within the state.
