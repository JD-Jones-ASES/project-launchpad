The Courts: The Judicial Branch

Overview

This section explores the judicial branch of the United States government, focusing on the federal court system and the Supreme Court. It explains the structure and function of District Courts, Courts of Appeals, and the Supreme Court, highlighting the crucial power of judicial review. The section emphasizes the historical significance of the Marbury v. Madison case and the enduring legacy of Chief Justice John Marshall in shaping the role and influence of the judiciary.

Key Concepts

  • The Federal Court System: The U.S. judicial branch is structured in three layers: District Courts (trial courts), Courts of Appeals (review lower court decisions), and the Supreme Court (the highest court whose decisions are final). This tiered system allows for appeals and ensures legal scrutiny.
  • The Supreme Court: Composed of a Chief Justice and eight Associate Justices, the Supreme Court is the nation's highest judicial body. Justices are nominated by the President, confirmed by the Senate, and serve for life, which is intended to insulate them from political pressures. The Court primarily hears cases involving federal law and the Constitution, accepting only a small fraction of requested cases annually.
  • Judicial Review: This is the foundational power of courts to examine laws and government actions and declare them unconstitutional if they violate the Constitution. An unconstitutional law is rendered invalid and unenforceable. This power makes the judicial branch a critical check on the legislative and executive branches.
  • Marbury v. Madison (1803): This landmark Supreme Court case, led by Chief Justice John Marshall, formally established the power of judicial review. Although not explicitly stated in the Constitution, Marshall argued it was implied by the Constitution's supremacy as the law of the land, making the courts the ultimate interpreters of its validity.
  • Chief Justice John Marshall's Legacy: Serving for 34 years, Marshall significantly strengthened the federal government's power, established judicial review, and profoundly shaped the interpretation of the Constitution through numerous landmark decisions, making him one of the most influential figures in American legal history.

Important Terms

  • Judicial review: The power of courts to examine laws and government actions and declare them unconstitutional if they violate the Constitution.
  • Unconstitutional: Going against the rules of the Constitution. An unconstitutional law is invalid and cannot be enforced.
  • Supreme Court: The highest court in the United States. Its decisions are final on questions of federal law and the Constitution.
  • Justice: A judge on the Supreme Court.
  • Chief Justice: The head judge of the Supreme Court.
  • District Courts: The trial courts where most federal cases begin. There are 94 district courts across the country.
  • Courts of Appeals: Federal courts that hear appeals from district courts. There are 13 courts of appeals; they review whether the law was applied correctly, rather than holding new trials.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to the Judicial Branch

The judicial branch, primarily composed of the federal courts, plays a critical role in interpreting laws and the Constitution. A pivotal moment in its history occurred in 1803 with the case of Marbury v. Madison, which addressed the fundamental question of whether a regular law or the Constitution should prevail when in conflict. Chief Justice John Marshall's decision in this case would fundamentally define the power of the judicial branch.

The Federal Court System

The U.S. federal court system is structured in a three-tiered hierarchy, designed to ensure due process and thorough review:

  1. District Courts:

    • These are the trial courts of the federal system.
    • This is where most federal cases, both civil and criminal, begin.
    • There are 94 district courts located across the country, serving specific geographic areas.
    • In these courts, evidence is presented, witnesses testify, and juries or judges determine facts and apply the law.

  2. Courts of Appeals (Circuit Courts):

    • These courts hear appeals from the decisions made in the district courts.
    • There are 13 Courts of Appeals (12 regional circuits and one federal circuit).
    • They do not conduct new trials or hear new evidence. Their role is to review the legal proceedings of the lower court to determine if the law was applied correctly and if legal procedures were followed fairly.
    • Their decisions can affirm (uphold) the lower court's ruling, reverse it, or remand (send back) the case for further proceedings.

  3. The Supreme Court:

    • This is the highest court in the United States.
    • Its decisions are final and binding on all other federal and state courts regarding questions of federal law and the Constitution.
    • It typically hears cases that have broad national significance or resolve conflicting interpretations of law among the Courts of Appeals.

The Supreme Court: Composition and Role

The Supreme Court holds a unique and powerful position within the American government:

  • Composition: It consists of nine members: one Chief Justice (the head judge) and eight Associate Justices.
  • Appointment Process:
    • Justices are nominated by the President of the United States.
    • The nominations must then be confirmed by a majority vote in the Senate.
  • Tenure: Justices serve for life (or until they retire, resign, or are removed through impeachment). This lifetime tenure is designed to protect them from political pressures from the executive or legislative branches, allowing them to make decisions based solely on legal principles and the Constitution, rather than public opinion or political expediency.
  • Jurisdiction and Caseload:
    • The Supreme Court primarily hears cases involving federal law and the Constitution.
    • It does not conduct trials for crimes.
    • It has significant discretion in choosing which cases to hear. Out of thousands of requests (petitions for certiorari) each year, the Court accepts only about 100–150 cases for oral argument. These are typically cases that raise significant constitutional questions or conflicting interpretations of federal law.

Judicial Review: Marbury v. Madison (1803)

The most significant power of the judicial branch, judicial review, was established in a landmark case:

  • The Case: In Marbury v. Madison, Chief Justice John Marshall issued a ruling that forever changed the balance of power in the U.S. government.
  • The Ruling: Marshall declared that the Supreme Court has the authority to declare an act of Congress (or any government action) unconstitutional.
  • Constitutional Basis: This power was not explicitly written in the Constitution. However, Marshall argued that it was implied by the nature of the Constitution itself. If the Constitution is the "supreme law of the land" (as stated in Article VI), then any law passed by Congress or action taken by the executive that contradicts it must be invalid. The courts, as interpreters of the law, must therefore have the power to identify and strike down such contradictory laws.
  • Impact: Judicial review makes the judicial branch a powerful check on both the legislative (Congress) and executive (President) branches.
    • If Congress passes a law that violates a constitutional right (e.g., the First Amendment), the Supreme Court can strike it down.
    • If the President issues an executive order that oversteps constitutional boundaries, the courts can declare it invalid.
    • This power ensures that all government actions remain within the confines of the Constitution, maintaining its supremacy.

Chief Justice John Marshall (1755–1835)

John Marshall's influence on American law is unparalleled:

  • Longest Tenure: He served as Chief Justice for 34 years, from 1801 to 1835, making him the longest-serving Chief Justice in U.S. history.
  • Background: Prior to his judicial career, Marshall was a Revolutionary War soldier, a lawyer, and served as Secretary of State.
  • Transformative Leadership: During his long tenure, Marshall seized the opportunity to:
    • Establish Judicial Review: His ruling in Marbury v. Madison created the precedent for the Court's authority to interpret the Constitution.
    • Strengthen Federal Power: Through dozens of landmark decisions, he consistently interpreted the Constitution in ways that strengthened the power of the federal government over the states.
    • Shape Constitutional Meaning: His interpretations laid the groundwork for how the Constitution would be understood and applied for generations.
  • Legacy: Many scholars consider him the most influential figure in the history of American law, whose impact on the judiciary and the federal government rivals, and in some ways surpasses, that of many presidents.

Review Questions

  1. Describe the three-tiered structure of the federal court system. How do the roles of District Courts, Courts of Appeals, and the Supreme Court differ, and how do they work together?
  2. Explain the process by which a person becomes a Supreme Court Justice. Why is the lifetime tenure of Supreme Court Justices considered an important aspect of their role in the American political system?
  3. What is judicial review? Discuss the significance of the Marbury v. Madison case in establishing this power and how Chief Justice John Marshall justified it.
  4. How does the power of judicial review allow the judicial branch to act as a "check" on the other two branches of government? Provide specific examples of how it limits the power of Congress and the President.
  5. Beyond establishing judicial review, what were some other significant contributions or impacts of Chief Justice John Marshall during his long tenure on the Supreme Court?
