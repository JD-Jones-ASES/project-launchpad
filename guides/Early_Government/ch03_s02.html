Monarchy: Old and New Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the fundamental differences between two forms of monarchy: absolute monarchy and constitutional monarchy. While both involve a king or queen, an absolute monarch holds unlimited power, whereas a constitutional monarch's power is legally restricted, with actual governance handled by an elected legislature. The section traces how some monarchies, like that of the United Kingdom, evolved from absolute to constitutional forms.

Key Concepts

  • Absolute Monarchy: A system where one ruler holds supreme, unlimited power, with no legal or constitutional constraints. The monarch's decisions are law, and their authority is unquestionable.
  • Constitutional Monarchy: A system where a monarch serves as the head of state but their powers are defined and limited by a constitution and laws. Real political power typically rests with an elected parliament or legislature and a prime minister.
  • Sources of Absolute Power: Absolute monarchs typically derive their authority from military might or the concept of "divine right," claiming God chose them to rule.
  • Evolution of Constitutional Monarchy: The transition from absolute to constitutional monarchy often involves historical events and documents that progressively limit the monarch's power. The Magna Carta in 1215 England was a pivotal moment in this development.
  • Symbolic vs. Governing Power: In a constitutional monarchy, the monarch often fulfills important ceremonial roles and serves as a symbol of national unity, while elected officials handle the day-to-day governance and lawmaking.

Important Terms

  • Absolute Monarchy: A government in which one ruler has complete power with no legal limits. The ruler's word is the law.
  • Constitutional Monarchy: A government in which a king or queen is the head of state but must follow the country's laws and constitution. A parliament or legislature makes the actual laws.
  • Heir: The person next in line to inherit a throne or title.
  • Parliament: A legislature, especially in countries with a British-style government. From French: parler ("to speak"), highlighting the role of discussion and debate.
  • Monarch (Word Origin): From Greek monos ("one") + arkhein ("to rule") — literally meaning "one ruler."
  • Magna Carta ("Great Charter"): A historic document signed by King John of England in 1215, which for the first time legally limited the power of the monarch, establishing that even kings must follow the law.
  • Divine Right: The idea that God chose the ruler, and therefore obedience to the monarch is a religious duty. This was a common justification for absolute power.

Detailed Notes

The Core Difference: Absolute vs. Constitutional

The term "monarchy" can describe vastly different governmental systems.

  • Ancient Egypt's Pharaohs: Were absolute monarchs. Their word was law, with no documents or councils to limit or overrule them.
  • The United Kingdom's King: Is a constitutional monarch. While he appears on stamps and lives in palaces, he does not make laws; elected members of Parliament do.

Absolute Monarchy

  • Definition: A system where the ruler has no legal limits on their power. They control the military, courts, and all major government decisions. Laws exist at their discretion and can be changed at will.
  • Historical Prevalence: This was the most common form of government for most of human history, seen in ancient Egypt, medieval Europe, and many parts of Asia and Africa.
  • Sources of Power:
    • Military Force: The ruler maintains power through control of the army.
    • Divine Right: The belief that the ruler was chosen by God, making obedience a religious obligation.

Constitutional Monarchy

  • Definition: A system where the monarch is a ceremonial head of state, but their power is restricted by the country's constitution and laws. Real governing power is exercised by an elected parliament and a prime minister.
  • The United Kingdom Example:
    • The monarch (King or Queen) reigns, symbolizing the nation, but Parliament rules, handling actual governance.
    • Monarch's Roles: Opens Parliament, signs bills into law (often a formality), and serves as a symbol of national unity.
    • Limitations: The monarch cannot make laws, raise taxes, or deploy the army without Parliament's approval.

How the United Kingdom Changed: A Shift from Absolute to Constitutional

  • The transition was gradual, not instantaneous.
  • Key Turning Point (1215): The Magna Carta. English nobles compelled King John to sign this document, which was the first written limitation on a king's power, establishing the principle that the monarch was also subject to the law.
  • Centuries of Development: Over the centuries following the Magna Carta, the English Parliament steadily gained more power.
  • By the 1700s: The balance had shifted significantly, with the monarch reigning primarily as a figurehead while the elected Parliament governed.
  • Modern Examples: Today, constitutional monarchies include countries like the United Kingdom, Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Japan, and Spain.

Word Origins

  • Monarch: Derived from Greek monos ("one") and arkhein ("to rule"), literally meaning "one ruler," highlighting the singular nature of the head of state.
  • Parliament: Comes from Old French parlement, related to parler ("to speak"). This origin emphasizes the foundational idea that government should involve discussion, debate, and argument, rather than just issuing commands.

Review Questions

  1. What is the fundamental difference between an absolute monarchy and a constitutional monarchy? Provide an example of a historical or contemporary country that fits each definition.
  2. Explain the significance of the Magna Carta in the historical development of constitutional monarchy. How did it change the relationship between the monarch and the law?
  3. Describe the two primary sources from which absolute monarchs typically derive their power. How do these sources reinforce their unlimited authority?
  4. In a constitutional monarchy like the United Kingdom, what are some of the symbolic roles the monarch performs? Conversely, what key powers do they expressly not possess?
  5. The text highlights that the same word, "monarchy," describes two very different systems. Why is it crucial to understand this distinction when studying various forms of government, and what misconceptions might arise if one fails to do so?
