Study Guide: The Origins and Rise of Christianity

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the historical origins, key figures, and pivotal events that shaped the early development of Christianity, as detailed in the provided text.

Core Concepts and Historical Narrative

The Life and Ministry of Jesus

The foundations of Christianity are centered on the life of a Jewish man named Yeshua (Jesus), born around 4 BCE in Bethlehem and raised in Nazareth. Born to a carpenter named Joseph and his wife Mary, Jesus was deeply rooted in the Jewish tradition, educated in Jewish scriptures, and an observer of Jewish law. He spoke Aramaic, the common language of his region.

At approximately age thirty, Jesus began a three-year public ministry characterized by:

  • Teachings: He utilized parables—vivid, short stories involving everyday objects like seeds and coins—to convey his message.
  • Core Message: He proclaimed the arrival of the "Kingdom of God," described as a new order of justice, mercy, and divine presence.
  • Followers: He commissioned a circle of twelve close disciples, known as apostles, to spread his message.
  • Death and Resurrection: Around 30 CE, Jesus was arrested by Jewish religious authorities, handed to the Roman governor Pontius Pilate, and executed by crucifixion. Three days later, his followers reported a bodily resurrection, which became the foundational belief of the Christian faith.

The Early Church and the Role of Paul

The birth of the early church is marked by Pentecost, an event occurring fifty days after the resurrection when the Holy Spirit was said to have descended upon the apostles.

While Jesus founded the movement, Paul of Tarsus is credited with its powerful expansion and theological shaping. Paul, a former persecutor of Christians, converted after a blinding vision of the risen Jesus on the road to Damascus. His contributions include:

  • Missionary Work: Traveling thousands of miles to found believer communities across the Roman Empire.
  • Theology of Universalism: Arguing that faith in Jesus was open to all, not just Jews.
  • Scripture: Writing letters that eventually formed a significant portion of the New Testament.

Transition to an Imperial Religion

Christianity spread for nearly three centuries within the Roman Empire despite intermittent persecution. A major turning point occurred in the 4th century:

  • 313 CE (Edict of Milan): Emperor Constantine issued this edict, granting official religious freedom to Christians.
  • 325 CE (Council of Nicaea): Constantine convened this first major gathering of bishops to establish official doctrine. The council declared Jesus to be fully divine and of the "same substance as the Father," effectively turning the movement into a formal institution.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. What is the literal meaning of the word "Gospel"? Answer: It comes from the Old English godspel, meaning "good news."

2. In what language did Jesus commonly speak? Answer: Aramaic.

3. What was the central theological claim made by early Christians when they referred to Jesus as "the Christ"? Answer: They were claiming he was the Messiah, or the "anointed one," the promised deliverer foretold in Jewish scriptures.

4. How did the Council of Nicaea define the nature of Jesus? Answer: The council declared that Jesus was not a lesser creation of God, but was fully divine and of the "same substance as the Father."

5. What event is considered the birth of the early church? Answer: Pentecost, the occasion fifty days after the resurrection when the Holy Spirit descended on the apostles.

6. Who was the Roman governor responsible for sentencing Jesus to crucifixion? Answer: Pontius Pilate.

7. How did Paul of Tarsus justify the global expansion of Christianity? Answer: He argued for universalism, stating that faith in Jesus was open to everyone, not just those of the Jewish faith.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Transformation of a Movement: Analyze the transition of Christianity from a localized Jewish sect to a Roman imperial religion. In your essay, discuss the specific roles played by the Edict of Milan and the Council of Nicaea in institutionalizing the faith.

2. The Influence of Paul of Tarsus: Evaluate the impact of Paul of Tarsus on the survival and spread of Christianity. How did his conversion and his specific theological arguments regarding universalism change the trajectory of the movement compared to its beginnings in Galilee?

3. The Historical and Theological Context of Jesus: Describe the cultural and religious environment into which Jesus was born. How did his identity as a "man of his time and place" contrast with the theological claims later made by his followers and the established church at Nicaea?

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Apostle Derived from the Greek for "one sent out"; refers to the twelve disciples personally commissioned by Jesus to spread his message.
Christ The Greek translation of the Hebrew word Messiah, meaning "the anointed one."
Council of Nicaea A 325 CE gathering of Christian bishops convened by Constantine to hammer out official doctrine regarding the divinity of Jesus.
Edict of Milan A 313 CE proclamation by Emperor Constantine that granted religious freedom to Christians throughout the Roman Empire.
Gospel Accounts of Jesus's life, death, and resurrection; the term literally means "good news."
Messiah The "anointed one"; a promised deliverer whom many Jews expected God to send to redeem his people.
Parable A short, vivid story (such as those about seeds or lost sheep) used by Jesus to illustrate a moral or spiritual lesson.
Pentecost From the Greek word for "fiftieth day"; the event where the Holy Spirit descended on the apostles, empowering them to preach.
Universalism The theological argument, championed by Paul, that faith in Jesus was open to all people regardless of their background or ethnicity.
Yeshua The original Hebrew name of Jesus, rendered as Iesous in Greek.
