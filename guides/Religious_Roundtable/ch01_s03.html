Study Guide: The Origins of Christianity

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the origins of Christianity, tracing its development from the life of Jesus in the 1st century to its establishment as an imperial religion in the 4th century. It is designed to facilitate a deep understanding of the key figures, events, and theological foundations that shaped the early Christian movement.

I. Historical Context and the Life of Jesus

The origins of Christianity are rooted in the life and ministry of Jesus (Yeshua), a Jewish man born around 4 BCE.

Early Life and Cultural Background

  • Birth and Upbringing: Born in Bethlehem and raised in Nazareth, Jesus was the son of Mary and Joseph, a carpenter.
  • Cultural Identity: Jesus was deeply embedded in the Jewish world of his time. He was educated in Jewish scriptures, observed Jewish law, and spoke Aramaic.
  • The Messianic Hope: His era was characterized by a widespread hope for a "Messiah" (Hebrew for "anointed one"), a promised deliverer expected to redeem the Jewish people.

Public Ministry

Jesus began his public ministry around the age of thirty. His work was defined by several key elements:

  • The Twelve Apostles: He gathered a circle of twelve close followers, known as "apostles" (meaning "ones sent out"), whom he commissioned to spread his message.
  • Core Message: His central teaching was the arrival of the "Kingdom of God," described as a new order of justice, mercy, and divine presence.
  • Teaching Methods: He frequently utilized parables—short, vivid stories involving everyday objects like seeds, coins, and sheep—to convey spiritual truths.
  • Controversy: His claims about himself and God were divisive, leading to significant controversy among his contemporaries.

II. The Foundation of the Early Church

The transition from a localized movement to a growing faith was marked by several pivotal events following the death of Jesus.

Execution and Resurrection

Around the year 30 CE, Jesus was arrested by Jewish religious authorities and handed over to the Roman governor, Pontius Pilate. He was executed via crucifixion. The cornerstone of the Christian faith, however, was the report by his followers three days later that his tomb was empty and that they had seen him alive. This claim of bodily resurrection provided the primary impetus for the movement's continuation.

Pentecost and the Holy Spirit

Fifty days after the resurrection, an event known as Pentecost (from the Greek word for "fiftieth day") occurred. According to historical accounts, the Holy Spirit descended upon the apostles, empowering them to preach and marking the birth of the early church.

The Influence of Paul of Tarsus

Paul of Tarsus is credited as the most influential figure in shaping the early movement.

  • Conversion: Originally a fierce persecutor of Christians, Paul underwent a dramatic conversion following a vision of the risen Jesus on the road to Damascus.
  • Missionary Work: He traveled extensively across the Roman Empire, founding new communities of believers.
  • Theology and Universalism: Paul’s writings, which form a significant portion of the New Testament, argued for "universalism"—the idea that faith in Jesus was open to all people, not just Jews. This perspective gave Christianity global momentum.

III. From Persecuted Minority to Imperial Religion

For nearly three centuries, Christianity spread throughout the Roman Empire despite facing intermittent persecution. This status changed dramatically in the 4th century.

The Constantinian Shift

  • Edict of Milan (313 CE): Emperor Constantine issued this edict, officially granting religious freedom to Christians and ending state-sanctioned persecution.
  • Imperial Endorsement: Constantine converted to or strongly endorsed the faith, causing Christianity to transition from a persecuted minority to the religion of the empire within a single generation.

The Council of Nicaea (325 CE)

To resolve internal disputes and standardize the faith, Constantine convened the Council of Nicaea. This was the first great gathering of Christian bishops.

  • Official Doctrine: The council established that Jesus was not a lesser creation of God but was fully divine, of the "same substance as the Father."
  • Institutionalization: This event marked the transition of Christianity from a movement into a formal institution.

IV. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. What does the word "Apostle" literally mean? Answer: It means "one sent out," referring to the twelve disciples commissioned by Jesus.

2. What was the "Kingdom of God" in the context of Jesus's teachings? Answer: It was his core message regarding a new order of justice, mercy, and divine presence breaking into the world.

3. Who was the Roman governor responsible for the crucifixion of Jesus? Answer: Pontius Pilate.

4. What event is considered the "birth of the early church"? Answer: Pentecost, the occasion when the Holy Spirit descended on the apostles.

5. How did Paul of Tarsus change the demographic reach of Christianity? Answer: He argued for universalism, claiming that faith in Jesus was open to everyone, not just Jews.

6. What was the significance of the Edict of Milan? Answer: Issued by Emperor Constantine in 313 CE, it officially granted Christians religious freedom throughout the Roman Empire.

7. What was the primary theological conclusion of the Council of Nicaea? Answer: The council declared that Jesus was fully divine and of the "same substance as the Father."

V. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Evolution of Identity: Analyze the transition of Jesus’s followers from a sect within Judaism to a distinct, universal religion. Discuss the roles of the Messianic hope and Paul’s missionary theology in this shift.
  2. Political and Religious Intersection: Examine the impact of Emperor Constantine on the development of Christianity. How did moving from a "persecuted minority" to an "imperial religion" change the nature of the faith and its institutional structure?
  3. The Role of Narrative and Doctrine: Discuss the importance of the Gospels and the Council of Nicaea in defining Christianity. How did the "good news" of the parables and the life of Jesus evolve into the formal doctrines established by the early church bishops?

VI. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Apostle From the Greek for "one sent out"; refers to the twelve disciples personally commissioned by Jesus to spread his message.
Christ The Greek translation of the Hebrew word Messiah, meaning "the anointed one."
Council of Nicaea A gathering of Christian bishops in 325 CE convened by Constantine to hammer out official doctrine.
Edict of Milan A 313 CE proclamation by Constantine granting religious freedom to Christians in the Roman Empire.
Gospel From the Old English godspel, meaning "good news"; the four New Testament accounts of Jesus’s life, death, and resurrection.
Kingdom of God Jesus's core message regarding a new order of justice, mercy, and divine presence.
Messiah A Hebrew term meaning "the anointed one"; the promised deliverer expected by the Jewish people.
Pentecost From the Greek for "fiftieth day"; the occasion when the Holy Spirit descended on the apostles, empowering them to preach.
Resurrection The claim of Jesus's bodily return to life three days after his death; the cornerstone of Christian belief.
Universalism The theological argument made by Paul that faith in Jesus is open to all people regardless of their background.
