Islam: Tawhid and the Unknowable God

Overview

This section explores the foundational Islamic doctrine of tawhid, emphasizing God's absolute oneness and uniqueness, which leads to a radical understanding of divine transcendence. Simultaneously, it delves into the equally strong insistence on God's immanence and nearness, as expressed through the Beautiful Names of Allah and Quranic verses. The mystical Sufi tradition further exemplifies the experiential pursuit of divine closeness, demonstrating how these seemingly opposing aspects of God's nature are held in profound balance within Islam.

Key Concepts

  • Tawhid (Absolute Oneness of God): The cornerstone of Islamic theology, asserting that Allah is not only numerically one but uniquely one, without partners, equals, or anything comparable in creation. It defines God as "Being itself," distinct from all created things.
  • Radical Transcendence: A direct implication of tawhid, this concept posits that God is categorically different from anything in creation and beyond human imagination or comprehension. God is not merely a greater version of a created being but is fundamentally other.
  • Immanence (God's Nearness): Despite God's transcendence, Islam equally stresses God's constant presence and closeness to creation. This is conveyed through God's Beautiful Names (Asma ul-Husna) and Quranic descriptions, highlighting God's loving, responsive, and ever-present nature.
  • Shirk (Associating Partners with God): The gravest sin in Islam, representing the opposite of tawhid. Shirk involves attributing divine qualities or status to anything other than Allah, thus fundamentally misunderstanding the nature of reality and God.
  • Sufism and Experiential Immanence: The mystical branch of Islam that focuses on spiritual disciplines aimed at achieving qurb (nearness to God) through direct experiential awareness of the divine presence. Sufis see tawhid not just as a doctrine but as a reality to be lived and experienced, fostering intimacy with God without claiming identity.

Important Terms

  • Tawhid: (Arabic: توحيد) The Islamic doctrine of the absolute unity and uniqueness of God. It asserts that Allah is one, unparalleled, and without partners. It is the first and most fundamental affirmation of Islamic belief, expressed in the shahada.
  • Shirk: (Arabic: شرك) The Arabic term for associating partners with God or attributing divine qualities to anything other than Allah. It is considered the gravest sin in Islamic theology, as it fundamentally contradicts tawhid.
  • Asma ul-Husna: (Arabic: أسماء الحسنى) Literally "The Beautiful Names" or "The Most Excellent Names." These are the ninety-nine attributes or names of Allah found in the Quran and Hadith, each describing a different aspect of God's nature (e.g., The Magnificent, The Loving, The Near).
  • Qurb: (Arabic: قرب) An Arabic term meaning "nearness" or "proximity." In Sufism, it refers to the spiritual goal of achieving experiential closeness and intimacy with God.
  • Ittihad: (Arabic: إتحاد) An Arabic term meaning "union" or "identity." In Sufi discourse, it specifically refers to the concept of the creature becoming identical with God, which is rejected by mainstream Sufism to maintain the distinction between Creator and created.
  • Misbaha: (Arabic: مسبحة) A string of prayer beads, typically with ninety-nine beads, used by Muslims to recite the ninety-nine Beautiful Names of Allah or other devotional phrases in meditation and remembrance (dhikr).

Detailed Notes

The Core of Islam: Tawhid and Radical Transcendence

Islamic theology begins and centers on the declaration: "La ilaha illa Allah" — "There is no god but God." This statement encapsulates tawhid, the absolute oneness and uniqueness of Allah. Tawhid is not merely a numerical statement about the number of gods; it is a profound declaration about God's very nature. Allah is understood as uniquely, categorically different from everything in creation. God is not simply the greatest being among other beings; rather, Allah is "Being itself" in a way that nothing else is.

This understanding implies radical transcendence: God is utterly beyond human comparison, imagination, or comprehension. God is not a magnified or more powerful version of anything we perceive in the created world. To associate anything with God, known as shirk, is considered the gravest error in Islam. It is not just a moral failing but a fundamental misunderstanding of reality, equating something created with the incomparable Creator.

The Paradox of God's Nearness: Immanence

Despite the forceful insistence on God's transcendence, Islamic theology maintains, with equal emphasis, God's profound nearness and presence, known as immanence. This tension between transcendence and immanence is central to the Islamic understanding of God.

The Ninety-Nine Beautiful Names of Allah (Asma ul-Husna) serve as a theological guide to this balance. Names like Al-Azim (the Magnificent) and Al-Qadir (the All-Powerful) highlight God's transcendent power and majesty. In contrast, names such as Al-Wadud (the Loving), Al-Qarib (the Near), and Al-Mujib (the Responsive, the One Who Answers) emphasize God's intimate presence, care, and accessibility. These names are not mere titles but are seen as windows into God's diverse attributes, showing how God is both beyond us and intimately present to us.

The Quran itself powerfully expresses this balance. Surah 57:3 describes Allah as "the First and the Last, the Outward and the Inward," encompassing both extremes of existence and presence. Further, Surah 2:115 declares, "Wherever you turn, there is the face of Allah," asserting God's ubiquitous presence and immanence in the entirety of creation.

Sufism: Experiencing the Immanent God

The mystical tradition within Islam, known as Sufism, particularly emphasizes and explores the immanent aspect of God. Sufis are practitioners of spiritual disciplines aimed at achieving qurb, or nearness to God, through direct experiential awareness of the divine presence.

Sufi poets, such as Rumi, eloquently express the soul's deep longing for intimacy with God, often using metaphors of reunion with a lost beloved. However, the Sufi tradition is careful to distinguish this nearness from ittihad (union in the sense of identity), stressing that the creature always remains distinct from the Creator. Instead, Sufi closeness is described as proximity, intimacy, and a profound knowing that transcends mere intellectual understanding.

For Sufis, tawhid is not just a theological doctrine to be recited but a lived reality to be experienced. If God is truly one, then God is truly everywhere, pervading all existence. Through purifying the heart and engaging in spiritual practices, the mystic believes they can directly encounter God's presence, moving beyond a purely theological distance to an intimate, personal awareness.

Practical Connection: Misbaha and the Names of Allah

The practice of reciting the Ninety-Nine Beautiful Names of Allah bridges the transcendent and immanent aspects of God in a tangible way. Tradition holds that whoever memorizes these names "will enter Paradise." Many Muslims utilize a misbaha (prayer beads) with ninety-nine beads, using them to recite the names in meditation or dhikr (remembrance). This practice allows believers to physically hold and verbally repeat names that point to God's incomprehensible greatness (transcendence) while simultaneously engaging in a personal, intimate act of remembrance and connection (immanence).

Review Questions

  1. Explain the concept of tawhid in Islam. How is it more than just a numerical statement, and what does it imply about God's relationship to creation?
  2. How does Islamic theology reconcile God's "radical transcendence" with His "nearness" or immanence? Provide specific examples from the text, such as the Beautiful Names or Quranic verses.
  3. What is shirk, and why is it considered the gravest error or sin in Islam? How does it fundamentally contradict the principle of tawhid?
  4. Describe the role of Sufism in exploring the immanent aspect of God. How do Sufis understand qurb, and how do they differentiate it from ittihad?
  5. Discuss how the misbaha and the recitation of the Ninety-Nine Beautiful Names of Allah serve as a practical example of holding together the transcendent and immanent aspects of God in Islamic practice.
