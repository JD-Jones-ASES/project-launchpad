Norse Paganism: Philosophical Depth and Cultural Legacy

This study guide explores the enduring impact of Norse Paganism on the English-speaking world, its pervasive influence on modern culture and literature, and its unique philosophical approach to existence, mortality, and the end of the world.

I. Key Concepts

Linguistic and Cultural Legacy

The influence of Norse religion is deeply embedded in the daily life of the English-speaking world, primarily through language and creative media:

  • The Calendar: While the months of the year are named after Roman gods, four days of the week are derived from Norse divine names. Our modern calendar represents a fusion of Roman, Germanic, and Christian religious cultures.
  • Modern Literature: J.R.R. Tolkien, a scholar of Old Norse and Old English, used Norse mythology as a primary foundation for The Lord of the Rings and The Hobbit. His contemporary, C.S. Lewis, was also heavily influenced by Norse literature.
  • The Fantasy Genre: Much of modern fantasy—including video games, film, and tabletop games like Dungeons & Dragons—utilizes Norse elements such as the Nine Worlds, the World Tree, and the heroic warrior code.

The Uniqueness of Norse Philosophy

Norse Paganism is distinguished from other major traditions (such as Buddhism, Taoism, or even Communism) by its lack of an "ultimate hope" like heaven or nirvana.

  • Ragnarök: The central tenet of the Norse worldview is the eventual death of the gods and the end of the world. While a renewal follows, the gods currently living will not survive to see it.
  • Living with Integrity: Because the protecting powers will eventually fail, the Norse tradition emphasizes living a life of value in the present. This involves maintaining faith with one's community and developing personal courage, skill, and generosity.
  • The Power of Memory: In a world where everything—including the gods—is temporary, the only thing that endures is the "fame" or reputation of a person's deeds.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to the text, which two authors were significantly shaped by their study of Norse literature and language?

J.R.R. Tolkien and C.S. Lewis.

2. What is the D’vergatal, and how did it influence J.R.R. Tolkien?

The D’vergatal is the "Catalogue of Dwarves" found in the Völuspá (part of the Poetic Edda). Tolkien used it to source names for his characters, including Thorin, Fili, Kili, and Gandalf.

3. How does the Norse view of the future differ from traditions like Buddhism or Taoism?

Unlike other traditions that offer a form of ultimate hope (like nirvana or the enduring Tao), Norse tradition centers on Ragnarök—the inevitable end of the gods and the world.

4. What are the three specific traits the Norse encouraged individuals to develop in the face of inevitable failure?

Courage, skill, and generosity.

5. According to the Hávamál, what is the one thing that never dies?

The fame of a dead man’s deeds.

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Ethics of the Finite: Analyze the Norse response to the inevitability of Ragnarök. How does the "death of the gods" function as a catalyst for ethical behavior rather than a source of despair? Contrast this with "easy answers" mentioned in the text.
  2. Cultural Homage vs. Appropriation: Using the example of J.R.R. Tolkien’s use of the Poetic Edda, discuss the distinction between "stealing" and "paying homage" to a tradition. How does a professional understanding of a culture’s cosmology affect the way its elements are used in modern fiction?
  3. The Practicality of the Hávamál: The text describes the vision of the Hávamál as "earthy, practical, and quietly devastating in its honesty." Explain how the quote regarding the death of cattle and kinsmen supports this description and what it suggests about the human condition.

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
D’vergatal The "Catalogue of Dwarves"; a specific section within the Völuspá that serves as a list of dwarf names.
Hávamál Translated as the "Sayings of the High One" (Odin); a text providing a practical and honest vision of life and conduct.
Poetic Edda A collection of Old Norse poems that serves as a primary source for Norse mythology and the D’vergatal.
Ragnarök The prophesied end of the world and the death of the gods in Norse mythology.
Völuspá A specific section of the Poetic Edda containing the catalogue of names used by later fantasy writers.
World Tree A central element of Norse cosmology that, along with the Nine Worlds, remains a staple of the modern fantasy genre.
