Study Guide: The Global Impact and Significance of Christianity

This study guide explores the historical and contemporary influence of Christianity as outlined in Chapter 1, Section 5, "Why It Matters." It examines how Christian theology has shaped Western civilization, ethics, the arts, and the sciences, and continues to influence modern secular discourse.

Key Concepts and Themes

Global Demographics and Influence

Christianity is the world’s largest religion, with approximately 2.4 billion adherents, representing nearly one-third of the global population. Beyond its numerical scale, the religion’s significance lies in its fundamental role in shaping the civilizations of Europe and the Americas, which have exerted immense global influence.

Foundations of Law and Ethics

The text identifies several ways in which Christian theology provides the "grammar" for modern legal and ethical frameworks:

  • Universal Human Dignity: The concept that every individual possesses inherent worth, regardless of status or ability, is rooted in the doctrine of imago Dei—the belief that humans are made in the image of God.
  • Natural Rights: Christian thinkers such as Thomas Aquinas and John Locke developed the tradition of natural rights, which serves as a cornerstone of modern democracy.
  • Social Reform: Major historical movements, including the abolitionist movement, the early labor movement, and the American civil rights movement, were driven by theological interpretations of the Gospels. These movements viewed slavery and oppression as incompatible with Christian teachings.

The Arts and Sciences

The source context highlights that Western cultural history is deeply intertwined with Christian thought:

  • The Arts: Much of Western art through the nineteenth century—including the works of Dante, Bach, Michelangelo, and Dostoevsky—is considered unintelligible without an understanding of the underlying theology.
  • The Sciences: Modern science was founded by individuals who viewed their work through a religious lens. Figures such as Copernicus, Mendel, and Newton were devout Christians who believed that by studying science, they were investigating the order God built into the creation.

Modern Secular Discourse

Christianity’s influence persists in contemporary debates, often appearing in "invisible" ways within secular societies. Discussions regarding bioethics, social justice, and human rights frequently utilize concepts—such as the "dignity of the person" or the obligation to care for the vulnerable—that were shaped by centuries of Christian intellectual history.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

Question Answer Summary based on Source Context
1. What is the approximate number of Christian adherents worldwide? Approximately 2.4 billion, or one in three people on earth.
2. What theological concept serves as the root for universal human dignity? The concept of imago Dei, or the teaching that every human being is made in the image of God.
3. Which two Christian thinkers are credited with developing the natural rights tradition? Thomas Aquinas and John Locke.
4. How did the founders of modern science view their work? They viewed scientific work as a means of studying the order that God had built into the creation.
5. Name three social movements described as "theological projects." The abolitionist movement, the early labor movement, and the American civil rights movement.
6. Why is Western art through the 19th century often described as "unintelligible" without theology? Because the overwhelming majority of these works (e.g., Dante, Bach, Michelangelo) were built upon Christian theological foundations.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The "Invisible Grammar" of Modernity: Analyze the claim that modern secular societies still utilize a "grammar" shaped by Christian thought. Provide examples from contemporary debates in bioethics or social justice to support your argument.
  2. The Intersection of Faith and Reason in Science: Discuss how the religious beliefs of figures like Copernicus, Mendel, and Newton influenced the development of modern science. How does the text challenge the idea of a necessary conflict between religion and scientific inquiry?
  3. Theology as a Tool for Social Change: Evaluate the role of the Gospels in historical social reform movements. How did Christian theology provide the intellectual and moral framework for movements like abolitionism and civil rights?

Glossary of Important Terms and Figures

  • Abolitionist Movement: A social and political movement aimed at ending slavery, described in the text as a theological project driven by readings of the Gospels.
  • Aquinas, Thomas: A Christian thinker who helped develop the natural rights tradition that underpins modern democracy.
  • Bioethics: The study of ethical issues in biology and medicine; the text cites debates over the beginning of life and care for the terminally ill as being influenced by theological concepts.
  • Copernicus, Mendel, and Newton: Founders of modern astronomy, genetics, and physics, respectively, who viewed their scientific pursuits as the study of God's created order.
  • Dante, Bach, Michelangelo, and Dostoevsky: Significant figures in Western literature, music, and art whose works are deeply rooted in Christian theology.
  • Imago Dei: A Latin term meaning "image of God"; the theological basis for the concept of universal human dignity.
  • Locke, John: An influential thinker who, along with Aquinas, contributed to the development of natural rights.
  • Natural Rights: A tradition in political philosophy regarding inherent human rights, developed by Christian thinkers and foundational to modern democratic systems.
  • Universal Human Dignity: The idea that every person possesses inherent worth regardless of status, rooted in Christian teaching.
