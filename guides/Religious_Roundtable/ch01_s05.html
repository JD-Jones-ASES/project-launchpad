Study Guide: The Global Influence and Legacy of Christianity

This study guide examines the historical and contemporary significance of Christianity, focusing on its role in shaping Western civilization, ethics, law, science, and the arts. Based on the provided text, it explores how Christian theology serves as a foundational "grammar" for modern intellectual and social discourse.

Key Concepts and Themes

1. Global Scale and Reach

Christianity is the world’s largest religion, with approximately 2.4 billion adherents, representing nearly one in every three people on Earth. Its significance extends beyond demographics, as it fundamentally shaped the civilizations of Europe and the Americas, which have exerted immense global influence.

2. Foundations of Law and Ethics

The modern understanding of human rights and democracy is rooted in Christian theological developments:

  • Universal Human Dignity: Derived from the concept of imago Dei (the belief that humans are made in the image of God), asserting that every person possesses inherent worth regardless of status.
  • Natural Rights: Developed by Christian thinkers such as Thomas Aquinas and John Locke, forming the basis for modern democratic governance.

3. Theology as a Catalyst for Social Change

Major social reform movements have been driven by theological interpretations of the Gospels. Proponents of these movements argued that slavery and oppression were fundamentally incompatible with Christian teachings. Key examples include:

  • The Abolitionist Movement
  • The Early Labor Movement
  • The American Civil Rights Movement

4. Intellectual and Cultural Contributions

  • The Arts: Western art through the nineteenth century—including literature (Dante, Dostoevsky), music (Bach), and visual art (Michelangelo)—is considered largely unintelligible without an understanding of Christian theology.
  • Science: Many founders of modern science (Copernicus, Mendel, Newton) viewed their research as a means of studying the divine order built into creation.

5. The "Invisibly Theological" Modern World

Even in secular societies, modern debates regarding bioethics, social justice, and human rights utilize a moral language shaped by centuries of Christian thought. Terms like "human dignity" and "justice for the vulnerable" carry a theological heritage that persists in contemporary political and social activism.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What is the estimated number of Christian adherents worldwide, and what proportion of the global population does this represent?
  2. What theological concept serves as the root for the idea of universal human dignity?
  3. Which two Christian thinkers are specifically credited with developing the natural rights tradition?
  4. According to the text, how did founders of modern science like Isaac Newton and Gregor Mendel view their scientific work?
  5. List three major social movements that were described as "theological projects."
  6. In what way does the text suggest Western art is dependent on Christian theology?
  7. How do modern secular debates about bioethics and social justice remain connected to Christianity?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Theological Roots of Modern Democracy

Analyze the transition of Christian theological concepts into secular legal frameworks. How did the concept of imago Dei evolve into the modern understanding of universal human rights and the natural rights tradition? Use the contributions of Aquinas and Locke to support your argument.

2. Christianity and the Scientific Revolution

Contradicting the common perception of a conflict between religion and science, the text suggests that Christian belief was a primary motivator for early scientists. Discuss how the belief in a "divine order" influenced the work of figures like Copernicus and Newton.

3. The "Grammar" of Secular Discourse

The text claims that even those skeptical of religion are "in conversation" with Christian belief. Evaluate the argument that modern concepts of "justice" and "dignity" are invisibly theological. Can these concepts be fully separated from their Christian origins?

Glossary of Key Terms and Figures

Term/Figure Definition
Abolitionist Movement A social and political effort to end slavery, driven significantly by the belief that oppression is incompatible with the Gospels.
Aquinas, Thomas A Christian thinker who contributed to the development of the natural rights tradition.
Bioethics The study of ethical issues in biology and medicine, such as the beginning of human life and the treatment of the terminally ill.
Copernicus A devout Christian and a founder of modern astronomy who viewed science as studying God's order.
Gospels The records of the life and teachings of Jesus; cited as the primary inspiration for various social justice movements.
Imago Dei A Latin term meaning "Image of God"; the theological basis for the belief in inherent human dignity.
Locke, John An influential thinker who, alongside Aquinas, helped develop the natural rights tradition undergirding modern democracy.
Mendel, Gregor A Christian figure recognized as a founder of modern genetics.
Natural Rights A tradition of rights inherent to individuals, developed by Christian theologians and central to modern democratic thought.
Newton, Isaac A founder of modern physics who understood his scientific endeavors as a study of the order God established in creation.
Universal Human Dignity The idea that every human being possesses inherent worth, regardless of their status or ability.
