Study Guide: Confucianism: The Great Unity

Overview

This section explores Confucianism's unique perspective on societal ideals and the future, which differs significantly from typical eschatological visions. Rather than foreseeing divine intervention, Confucius looked to the past for models of harmonious society (the "Great Harmony" or Da Tong) achieved through human virtue and ritual. The achievability of this ideal hinges on the Confucian understanding of human nature, particularly Mencius's belief in its fundamental goodness, a vision that has profoundly influenced Chinese thought through the ages.

Key Concepts

  • Confucian Eschatology as Recovery: Unlike many traditions that predict a future divine intervention, Confucianism's vision of an ideal society is rooted in the recovery of a legendary past era of harmony. It is framed as a human challenge to cultivate virtue rather than a divine promise.
  • Da Tong (Great Harmony): The central Confucian utopian vision, described in the Book of Rites. It depicts a society where virtuous leaders govern, people care for all members as family, the able contribute, the weak are supported, and security prevails due to universal benevolence and propriety. It serves as both a historical ideal and a future aspiration.
  • Human Nature (Xing Shan): The debate about human nature is crucial to the achievability of Da Tong. Mencius argued for xing shan, the inherent goodness of human nature, believing that moral virtues are innate "sprouts" that can be cultivated. This makes the Great Harmony a realistic possibility.
  • Cultivation of Virtue: The path to achieving Da Tong relies on the human cultivation of virtues such as ren (benevolence), yi (righteousness), and li (ritual propriety), facilitated by education, ritual, and good examples.
  • Influence on Modern Chinese Thought: The ideals of Da Tong and a harmonious society have significantly impacted modern Chinese political thinkers, including reformists like Kang Youwei, nationalist leaders like Sun Yat-sen, and even the Communist Party of China.

Important Terms

  • Confucius: (551–479 BCE) The foundational figure of Confucianism, lived during a time of political fragmentation and societal collapse in China.
  • Spring and Autumn period: The historical era in which Confucius lived, characterized by warfare between states and a breakdown of traditional order.
  • Zhou dynasty: An ancient Chinese dynasty whose early period was considered by Confucius a time of ritual order and harmonious society.
  • Da Tong (Great Harmony / Great Commonwealth): The Confucian vision of an ideal, perfectly harmonious society, where people live together in peace, virtue, and mutual care.
  • Book of Rites (Li Ji): One of the Five Classics of Confucianism, a text compiled by Confucian scholars that contains descriptions of rites and the famous passage on Da Tong.
  • ren (benevolence): A fundamental Confucian virtue encompassing humanity, compassion, and altruism; central to a harmonious society.
  • yi (righteousness): A Confucian virtue representing moral uprightness, justice, and doing what is appropriate.
  • li (ritual propriety / ritual): The code of behavior, etiquette, and ritual practices that Confucius believed were essential for maintaining social order and cultivating virtue.
  • xing shan (goodness of human nature): Mencius's central teaching that human beings are born with an innate capacity for goodness, like seeds of virtue.
  • Mencius: (372–289 BCE) The most influential inheritor of Confucius's tradition, known for his teaching on the goodness of human nature.
  • Xunzi: An alternative Confucian thinker who held that human nature tends toward selfishness and requires external shaping through ritual and education.
  • Neo-Confucian tradition: A resurgence and reinterpretation of Confucianism during the Song dynasty (960–1279 CE), which integrated Buddhist and Taoist metaphysical ideas with classical Confucian ethics.
  • Kang Youwei: (1858–1927) A reformist Confucian thinker who reimagined Da Tong in his utopian work, Book of the Great Unity, advocating for global governance and equality.
  • Sun Yat-sen: The founder of modern China, who frequently quoted the Da Tong passage in his political speeches, highlighting its enduring influence.
  • hexie shehui (harmonious society): A modern political slogan used by the Communist Party of China, reflecting the deep cultural resonance of the Confucian vision of harmony.

Detailed Notes

I. Confucius's Vision in a Time of Chaos

  • Historical Context: Confucius lived during the Spring and Autumn period (551–479 BCE), a time of widespread political fragmentation, incessant warfare among states, and the perceived breakdown of the ritual order that had underpinned the earlier Zhou dynasty.
  • Confucius's Response: Instead of seeking divine intervention for future salvation, Confucius looked to the past. He idealized the sage-kings of antiquity (like Yao, Shun, and the Duke of Zhou) who, in his view, had created genuinely harmonious societies through the cultivation of virtue and faithful practice of li (ritual propriety).
  • Confucian "Eschatology": This approach leads to a unique "eschatology" – not a prophecy of a divine future, but a call for the recovery of a past ideal through human effort and challenge, rather than a divine promise.

II. The Ideal of Da Tong (The Great Harmony)

  • Source: The closest Confucianism comes to a comprehensive ideal future is the description of Da Tong (Great Harmony or Great Commonwealth) found in the Book of Rites (Li Ji). This passage is highly celebrated in the Confucian canon.
  • Characteristics of Da Tong:
    • Virtuous Leadership: Leaders are chosen based on their virtue and ability, not heredity.
    • Universal Care: People extend care beyond their own families, looking after all children and elders as if they were their own.
    • Shared Prosperity: The strong work for the benefit of all, not just themselves; the weak and vulnerable are supported and not abandoned.
    • Security and Trust: Society is so harmonious and trustworthy that people can leave their doors unlocked without fear.
    • Ethical Foundation: This utopian state is not supernaturally given but is a human achievement, made possible by the widespread cultivation of core Confucian virtues: ren (benevolence), yi (righteousness), and li (ritual propriety).
  • Dual Nature of Da Tong: It functions both as a description of a legendary, idealized past age and a hopeful vision for a possible future society that humanity can strive to build.

III. The Achievability of Da Tong: The Debate on Human Nature

  • Central Question: The possibility of achieving the Great Harmony hinges on the fundamental nature of human beings.
  • Mencius's View (Xing Shan):
    • Mencius (372–289 BCE), Confucius's most significant successor, argued that human nature is fundamentally good (xing shan).
    • He believed that every person is born with "four sprouts" or innate beginnings of benevolence, righteousness, propriety, and wisdom.
    • These sprouts, if cultivated through education, ritual, and exposure to good examples (especially sage-rulers), can grow into full virtues.
    • Eschatological Implication: This belief is profoundly eschatological in the Confucian sense: if humans are inherently good, then a truly good and harmonious society is not a mere fantasy but a realistic and achievable possibility. History can progress because human beings are capable of moral improvement.
  • Alternative View (Xunzi): The text briefly notes that Xunzi, another prominent Confucian thinker, held a contrasting view, believing human nature tended toward selfishness and required external shaping through ritual and education. However, Mencius's xing shan is presented as key to the achievability of Da Tong.
  • Neo-Confucian Developments: During the Song dynasty (960–1279 CE), the Neo-Confucian tradition further developed Mencius's insights. They integrated these ideas with concepts from Buddhism and Taoism, expanding the ideal of the sage from a purely moral and political figure to also a spiritual one, exploring the ultimate ground of reality.

IV. Enduring Influence of Da Tong in Modern China

  • Kang Youwei: A reformist Confucian thinker (1858–1927), who wrote Book of the Great Unity (Datong Shu), reimagining Da Tong in terms of global governance, social equality, and the elimination of national borders.
  • Sun Yat-sen: The founder of modern China, frequently quoted the Da Tong passage in his political speeches, demonstrating its inspirational power.
  • Communist Party of China: Despite officially rejecting Confucianism, the Communist Party has periodically employed the language of "harmonious society" (hexie shehui), underscoring how deeply the Confucian vision of harmony is embedded in Chinese culture and thought.

Review Questions

  1. How does the Confucian vision of an ideal society, particularly the concept of Da Tong, differ from traditional religious eschatological prophecies that involve divine intervention?
  2. Describe the key characteristics of Da Tong as outlined in the Book of Rites. What roles do ren, yi, and li play in achieving this ideal?
  3. Explain Mencius's concept of xing shan (goodness of human nature). How does this philosophical stance make the Great Harmony a realistic possibility in Confucian thought?
  4. Why did Confucius look to the sage-kings of antiquity and the Zhou dynasty rather than envisioning a new divine future to address the societal problems of his time?
  5. Discuss the lasting influence of the Da Tong concept on modern Chinese political thought, citing examples such as Kang Youwei, Sun Yat-sen, and the "harmonious society" idea.
