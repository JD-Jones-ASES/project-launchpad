Judaism: The Written and Oral Torah

Overview

This section introduces the foundational concept of Torah in Judaism, emphasizing its role not just as a holy book but as the comprehensive divine instruction and living heart of Jewish life. It explores the Torah's multi-faceted nature as history, law, poetry, and a profound relationship with God, and highlights the crucial doctrines of the Oral Torah and the communal practice of continuous reading.

Key Concepts

  • Torah as Comprehensive Divine Instruction: The Torah (Hebrew for "instruction" or "teaching") is far more than a legal code or scripture; it serves as Judaism's constitution, history, ethical guide, and the narrative of Israel's intimate relationship with God. It reveals God's nature, humanity's place, and the workings of the universe.
  • Devotional and Relational Aspect of Torah: The Jewish relationship with the Torah is deeply emotional, devotional, and even romantic, often compared to a marriage. It is seen as a living medium through which God personally communicates, fostering a profound spiritual connection rather than merely intellectual study.
  • The Oral Torah as Part of Revelation: According to rabbinic tradition, the Oral Torah—an interpretive tradition—was given to Moses alongside the Written Torah at Sinai. This tradition, later compiled as the Mishnah and expanded into the Talmud, underscores that interpretation is not a separate human endeavor but an integral part of God's ongoing revelation, making the divine word applicable to all generations.
  • Lectio Continua and Communal Life: The synagogue practice of lectio continua (continuous reading) involves publicly reading the entire Written Torah from beginning to end over the course of a year. This practice ensures the Torah remains the central, living element of Jewish communal life, symbolizing an unending story that continually renews and engages the community.

Important Terms

  • Torah (tor-AH): Hebrew for "instruction" or "teaching." In its narrowest sense, it refers to the Five Books of Moses (Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, Deuteronomy). In a broader sense, it encompasses the entire body of divine teaching given to Israel, including the Oral Torah.
  • Oral Torah: The interpretive tradition that, according to rabbinic teaching, was given to Moses alongside the Written Torah at Mount Sinai. It was transmitted orally for centuries before being written down in the Mishnah and later expanded into the Talmud.
  • Mishnah (MISH-nah): The first major written collection of Jewish oral traditions, forming the core of the Oral Torah, compiled around 200 CE.
  • Talmud (TAL-mud): A vast body of Jewish civil and ceremonial law and legend, comprising the Mishnah and rabbinic commentaries on it (Gemara). It is a central text of rabbinic Judaism.
  • Lectio Continua (LEK-tee-oh con-TIN-yoo-ah): Latin for "continuous reading." The synagogue custom of publicly reading the entire Written Torah scroll from beginning to end over an annual cycle.
  • Simchat Torah (sim-KHAHT tor-AH): Hebrew for "Rejoicing in the Torah." A Jewish holiday that marks the completion of the annual cycle of public Torah readings and the immediate beginning of the new cycle.

Detailed Notes

The Multi-faceted Nature of Torah

The Torah is the bedrock of Judaism, far exceeding the typical understanding of a "holy book."

  • Meaning: "Torah" in Hebrew translates to "instruction" or "teaching," not solely "law" in a restrictive sense.
  • Comprehensive Role: It functions as Judaism's constitution, detailing its foundational principles; its history, recounting the origins of Israel; its law code, providing ethical and ritual guidelines; its poetry, expressing divine beauty; and fundamentally, it is the narrative of God's profound love story with Israel.
  • Guidance: It doesn't just issue commands; it reveals the nature of God, defines human identity, and explains the fundamental order of the universe. For a devout Jew, engaging with the Torah is like listening to a parent share the deepest family story.

The Devotional and Relational Connection to Torah

The Jewish relationship with the Torah is deeply personal and emotional, extending beyond intellectual study.

  • Prevalence: The word "Torah" appears over 200 times in the Hebrew Bible, making it the most common and cherished term for scripture.
  • Psalm 119: This psalm, the longest chapter in the entire Bible (176 verses), is exclusively devoted to praising the goodness and beauty of Torah. It describes the Torah as a lamp, a light, a treasure, a delight, a counselor, and a source of comfort in affliction.
    • Example verses from Psalm 119: "O how I love thy law! it is my meditation all the day." "How sweet are thy words unto my taste! yea, sweeter than honey to my mouth!" "Thy word is a lamp unto my feet, and a light unto my path."
  • A "Marriage" with God: Rabbis metaphorically compare the relationship between Israel and the Torah to a marriage, emphasizing its intimate and committed nature.
  • A Living Medium: The Jewish tradition posits that the Torah is not merely information about God, but a direct medium through which God speaks personally and continually to each reader. It facilitates an ongoing, dynamic relationship.
  • Blessing: The Torah reading in the synagogue concludes with a blessing acknowledging God "who has given us a Torah of truth and planted eternal life within us," highlighting its life-giving and eternal significance.

The Doctrine of the Oral Torah

One of Judaism's most unique theological contributions is the concept of the Oral Torah.

  • Simultaneous Revelation: The rabbis teach that when God gave Moses the Written Torah at Sinai, an Oral Torah—an accompanying interpretive tradition—was also revealed.
  • Purpose: The Oral Torah's function is to make the timeless words of the Written Torah applicable and relevant to every generation and every new situation that arises. It provides the necessary framework for understanding and implementing the written text.
  • Transmission and Codification: This interpretive tradition was passed down orally from teacher to student for many centuries.
    • It was first systematically written down around 200 CE as the Mishnah.
    • Later, extensive commentaries, debates, and expansions on the Mishnah developed into the vast body of literature known as the Talmud.
  • Significance: This doctrine implies a profound truth: the sacred text is not self-interpreting. Rather, interpretation is not a human imposition on revelation but an intrinsic and vital part of the revelation itself, ensuring its perpetual relevance and dynamism.

The Practice of Lectio Continua

The continuous public reading of the Torah is a central pillar of Jewish communal life.

  • Meaning: Lectio continua means "continuous reading" in Latin.
  • Function: This synagogue practice ensures that the Written Torah remains at the very heart of Jewish communal worship and identity, having done so for over two millennia.
  • Annual Cycle: The entire Torah is read publicly, from beginning to end, over the course of each year.
  • Simchat Torah: The annual cycle culminates in the festival of Simchat Torah ("Rejoicing in the Torah"). On this day, the final words of Deuteronomy are read, and immediately afterward, the scroll is rolled back to the beginning to read the first words of Genesis.
  • Symbolism: This continuous cycle symbolizes that the story of God and Israel never truly ends; it is an ongoing narrative. Every year, the community approaches the ancient words with new perspectives, new questions, and new life experiences, allowing the Torah to speak anew in every generation.

Review Questions

  1. Beyond merely "law," how does the Jewish understanding of "Torah" encompass a broader range of meanings and functions in the life of the community and the individual?
  2. Explain how Psalm 119 illustrates the deeply devotional, emotional, and relational aspect of the Jewish people's connection to the Torah. What metaphors are used to describe this relationship?
  3. What is the "Oral Torah," and what is its historical development from Sinai to the Mishnah and Talmud? How does its existence challenge the idea of a self-interpreting scripture?
  4. Describe the synagogue practice of lectio continua and its significance. How does the festival of Simchat Torah embody the spirit of this practice?
  5. In what ways does the Jewish tradition suggest that the Torah functions not just as a source of information about God, but as a direct medium through which God speaks personally to each reader?
