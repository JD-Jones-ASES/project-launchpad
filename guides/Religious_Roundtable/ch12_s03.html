Communism: The Social Self — A Study Guide

Overview

This section explores Karl Marx's understanding of human nature, focusing on labor as the defining characteristic of our "species-being." It explains how industrial capitalism, according to Marx, alienates individuals from their work, products, fellow humans, and their true human potential. Finally, it presents communism as Marx's proposed solution to restore human dignity and flourishing by abolishing the conditions that create alienation.

Key Concepts

  • Centrality of Labor: For Marx, the most fundamental aspect of human existence is labor—the act of engaging with the material world to meet needs and express creativity. It is through labor that humans develop and define themselves, not primarily through beliefs or social relationships.
  • Species-Being (Gattungswesen): This term refers to the essential nature of the human species. Marx argued that what distinguishes humans from other animals is our capacity for free, conscious, and purposeful production. Unlike animals driven purely by instinct, humans can imagine, plan, and creatively transform the world.
  • Alienation: Under industrial capitalism, Marx contended that labor, which should be a source of self-realization, is turned against the worker. This alienation manifests in four key ways:
    • From the Product: Workers create goods that immediately become the property of the capitalist, confronting them as something foreign.
    • From the Process: The act of working is not free or self-directed but compelled by survival, leading to exhaustion rather than fulfillment.
    • From Species-Being: Workers are cut off from their inherent capacity for free, creative labor and reduced to working like animals simply to survive.
    • From Other Human Beings: Capitalism fosters competition, hindering natural human solidarity and cooperation.
  • Communist Revolution as Restoration of Humanity: Marx viewed the transfer of the means of production from private capitalists to collective worker control not just as economic reform, but as the essential cure for alienation. The goal is to restore full human dignity and allow individuals to realize their true "species-being."
  • Historical Materialism: Influenced by Hegel but "turned right-side up," Marx's approach argues that history is driven by the unfolding of material conditions, specifically the struggle over who controls the means of production, rather than the unfolding of ideas. This forms the foundation of Marxist thought.

Important Terms

  • Labor: In Marx's philosophy, this is the defining characteristic of human beings – the conscious, free, and creative activity through which humans interact with the material world, meet their needs, and express their essential nature.
  • Species-Being (Gattungswesen): Marx's term for the distinctive, essential nature of humanity, characterized by the capacity for free, conscious, and purposeful creative production, as opposed to instinctive activity.
  • Proletarians: The modern working class, laborers who must sell their labor power to survive, increasing capital for the bourgeoisie.
  • Bourgeoisie: The capitalist class who own the means of production and profit from the labor of the proletarians (implied by the contrast with "proletarians" and "capitalists").
  • Alienation: The condition, central to Marx's critique of capitalism, where individuals are separated from the product of their labor, the process of labor, their own essential human nature (species-being), and from other human beings.
  • Historical Materialism: Marx's theory, derived from Hegel, which posits that historical development is driven by changes in the material conditions of society, particularly the economic structure and the struggle over the means of production.

Detailed Notes

Marx's Starting Point: What We Do

  • Beyond Beliefs and Feelings: Karl Marx began his analysis of human nature not with internal states like belief or feeling, but with external action: "what we do," specifically, "what we produce."
  • Labor as Identity: He posited that "as individuals express their life, 'so they are'." For Marx, human identity is fundamentally tied to our labor—how we engage with the material world to meet our needs and express our creativity.
  • Not Reductive, but Revealing: Marx argued this focus on labor was not reductive but actually revealed the unique richness and distinctiveness of human life.

Species-Being: The Essence of Humanity

  • Defining Human Nature: The concept of "species-being" (Gattungswesen) is Marx's term for the characteristic activity that defines the human species.
  • Conscious, Free Production: What distinguishes humans from other animals is our capacity for conscious, free, and purposeful production.
    • Example: Architect vs. Bee: A bee builds a hive instinctively. A human architect first conceives the structure in imagination and then intentionally realizes it in the world. This imaginative, conscious, and free creation is Gattungswesen.
  • Source of Dignity and Self-Realization: For Marx, work should be a dignified, creative, and self-realizing activity through which we express ourselves and transform the world.

The Problem: Alienation Under Capitalism

  • The "Theft of Humanity": Marx believed that industrial capitalism devastated human nature by turning labor—the very activity that defines our species-being—against the worker.

  • The Proletariat's Plight (from The Communist Manifesto):

    • The modern working class (proletarians) grows with the bourgeoisie (capitalist class).
    • These laborers must sell themselves as a commodity to survive.
    • Extensive machinery and division of labor strip work of individual character and "charm."
    • The worker becomes an "appendage of the machine," reduced to simple, monotonous tasks.

  • Four Forms of Alienation: Capitalism severs the worker from:

    1. Alienation from the Product: The goods produced by the worker become property of the capitalist. The product confronts the worker as something "foreign and hostile" rather than an extension of their creative self.
    2. Alienation from the Process: The act of working itself is not free or self-directed. It is compelled by economic necessity, leading to exhaustion and misery rather than fulfillment.
    3. Alienation from Species-Being: The worker is denied the opportunity for free, creative, conscious labor, which is humanity's defining characteristic. Their labor is reduced to a means of mere survival, like an animal.
    4. Alienation from Other Human Beings: Capitalism fosters competition among workers, hindering natural human tendencies toward solidarity and cooperation. Relationships become transactional rather than communal.

The Solution: Communism and the Restoration of Humanity

  • Cure for Alienation: If alienation is the disease, then the abolition of its underlying conditions is the cure.
  • Beyond Economic Reform: Marx's vision of revolution—transferring the means of production from private capitalists to collective worker control—was not just economic but fundamentally about restoring full human potential and dignity.
  • Utopian Vision of Communist Society: In works like the Paris Manuscripts, Marx described a communist society as one where:
    • Labor is truly free and self-directed.
    • No one is confined to a single function or alienated role.
    • Individuals can engage in diverse activities: "hunt in the morning, fish in the afternoon, rear cattle in the evening, 'criticize after dinner'."
  • Humanistic Concern: While the realism of Marx's utopian vision is debated, its grounding in a profound concern for human dignity and flourishing is undeniable.

DID YOU KNOW? Historical Materialism

  • Influence of Hegel: Marx was deeply influenced by philosopher G.W.F. Hegel, who saw history as a process of conflict and resolution, an unfolding toward an ultimate destination.
  • "Turned Right-Side Up": Marx adopted Hegel's idea but inverted it. Where Hegel focused on the unfolding of ideas, Marx saw history as the unfolding of material conditions—specifically, the struggle over who controls the means of production.
  • Foundation of Marxist Thought: This approach, known as historical materialism, became the bedrock of Marxist philosophy.

Review Questions

  1. How does Karl Marx's starting point for understanding human nature differ from approaches that focus on beliefs or feelings? Why did he consider labor to be more fundamental?
  2. Explain Marx's concept of "species-being" (Gattungswesen). How does the analogy of the architect and the bee illustrate this concept?
  3. Describe the four distinct ways in which Marx argued industrial capitalism alienates workers. Provide a brief explanation for each form of alienation.
  4. According to Marx, what is the ultimate consequence of alienation on human dignity and potential? How does he propose that a communist society would "cure" this alienation?
  5. What is historical materialism, and how did Marx's perspective on history differ from that of his philosophical predecessor, Hegel?
