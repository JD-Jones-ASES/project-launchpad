Christian Theology: Core Beliefs Study Guide

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the fundamental doctrines of Christianity as outlined in the provided text. It covers the nature of God, the person of Jesus Christ, the human condition, and the mechanism of salvation.

I. Key Conceptual Pillars

The Trinity

The Christian understanding of God is distinct because it does not define the divine as a solitary being. Instead, it posits the Trinity: one divine being existing as three distinct persons—Father, Son, and Holy Spirit.

  • The Father: The creator and sustainer of all existence.
  • The Son: Jesus Christ, the person who entered human history.
  • The Holy Spirit: The continuing divine presence that empowers and guides believers.
  • Nature of the Union: These are not three separate gods, nor are they merely "masks" worn by one actor. They represent relationship, communication, and love within a single divine reality. While analogies like a triangle (three sides, one shape) are used, the Trinity is ultimately considered a mystery that exceeds human comprehension.

The Incarnation

The Incarnation is the belief that the eternal Son of God took on a fully human nature in the person of Jesus Christ.

  • Fully Human and Fully Divine: Christianity maintains that Jesus was not a god in disguise or a temporary observer. He was born of the Virgin Mary and experienced the full range of finite human life, including fatigue, grief, and death.
  • Purpose: The doctrine answers how far God would go to reach humanity, suggesting that the divine entered "all the way" into the human experience.

Sin and the Human Condition

Christianity identifies sin as the fundamental problem facing humanity.

  • Definition: Sin is any thought, word, or deed that turns a person away from God and toward the self, resulting in a separation from the source of life.
  • Original Sin: Drawing from the book of Genesis, the text explains that the first humans chose to disobey God, creating a "rupture" in human nature.
  • The "Sick Doctor" Analogy: Because this inclination toward selfishness and injustice is internal to human nature, humans cannot fix the problem through their own efforts. Just as a sick doctor cannot heal himself, humanity requires an outside intervention.

The Resurrection

The Resurrection is the belief that Jesus rose bodily from the dead on the third day following his crucifixion.

  • Nature of the Event: It was not a vision or a ghost, but a transformation into a physical body.
  • Significance: For Christians, this is the central event of history. It serves as evidence that death and the separation caused by sin have been defeated. It provides the basis for the hope of human restoration.

Grace and Salvation

Salvation refers to being made right with God and participating in eternal life.

  • The Role of Grace: Grace is defined as God's freely given, unearned love and favor. Salvation is a gift that cannot be achieved through religious effort or good behavior.
  • The Human Response: Humans respond to this gift through faith—a trust in what God has done through Christ.
  • Behavior and Change: While good behavior does not earn salvation, genuine faith is expected to produce a genuine change in a person's life. The change is the result of the gift, not the cause of it.

II. Glossary of Terms

Term Definition
Trinity The doctrine that God is three distinct persons (Father, Son, and Holy Spirit) who constitute one divine being.
Incarnation The belief that the eternal Son of God became fully human in the person of Jesus Christ.
Sin An act of separation from God; turning toward the self and away from the source of goodness.
Original Sin The foundational rupture in human nature caused by the first humans' disobedience.
Resurrection The bodily rising of Jesus Christ from the dead on the third day after his burial.
Grace The unearned, freely given love and favor of God toward humanity.
Salvation The process of being reconciled to God and granted eternal life.
Faith The human response of trust and reliance on God's actions in Christ.

III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does the Christian view of God differ from a "solitary" view of the divine?
    • Answer: Christianity views God as a Trinity of three distinct persons (Father, Son, and Holy Spirit) who exist in relationship and love within one divine being, rather than a single, self-contained entity.
  2. According to the text, what are the two natures of Jesus Christ?
    • Answer: Jesus is believed to be fully human and fully divine at the same time.
  3. Why is the Resurrection considered essential to the Christian faith?
    • Answer: It is viewed as the central event of history and evidence that death and sin have been defeated. As Paul wrote, without the resurrection, the faith is "empty."
  4. In the context of Christian theology, what is the relationship between "good works" and "salvation"?
    • Answer: Salvation is a gift of grace that cannot be earned by good works. However, genuine faith is believed to naturally produce a change in behavior.
  5. What does the "sick doctor" analogy illustrate about the human condition?
    • Answer: It illustrates that because sin has ruptured human nature, humans are incapable of saving or fixing themselves; they require an outside intervention from God.

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Ethics of Intervention: If God is described as all-powerful and all-loving, analyze the Christian argument for why a "rescue" via the Incarnation and the suffering of the cross was necessary. What does this suggest about the theological relationship between love, justice, and the exercise of divine power?
  2. The Paradox of the Incarnation: Explore the philosophical challenges presented by the claim that the "infinite God entered finite human life." How does the text distinguish the Incarnation from a god simply "disguising himself" or acting as an "observer"?
  3. The Internalization of Sin: Discuss the concept of sin as a "rupture into human nature" rather than just a series of mistakes. How does this specific definition of the human problem dictate the specific Christian solution of "Grace"?
