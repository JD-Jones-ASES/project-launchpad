Islam: Submission, Faith, and the Oneness of God

This study guide explores the fundamental principles of Islamic faith as presented in the "Religious Roundtable" series. It covers the linguistic and theological definitions of core concepts, the nature of revelation, and the central importance of divine unity.

Key Themes and Concepts

1. The Meaning of Islam and Iman

The word Islam is derived from the Arabic word for "surrender." This is defined not as a defeated capitulation, but as a willing and active alignment with a reality greater than oneself. A Muslim is "one who surrenders" in this manner.

Iman refers to the interior state of faith, trust, and conviction. It is not merely an intellectual agreement but a state involving the heart, mind, and actions. According to classical scholars, faith is dynamic: it increases through obedience and decreases through sin.

2. The Six Articles of Faith (Iman)

Iman involves belief in six specific pillars:

  • God (Allah): The central focus of faith.
  • Angels: Divine intermediaries.
  • Divine Books: Revelations sent to humanity.
  • Prophets: Messengers who deliver God's word.
  • The Day of Judgment: The ultimate accountability of the soul.
  • Divine Decree (Qadar): God's ultimate will and control.

3. The Shahada: The Declaration of Faith

The Shahada is the first of Islam’s Five Pillars and serves as the doorway to the faith. It consists of two parts:

  • Negation: "There is no god but God." This clears the field of false idols, such as wealth, power, or ideology.
  • Affirmation: "Muhammad is the messenger of God." This affirms that there is a genuine path and revelation to follow.

4. Tawhid vs. Shirk

Tawhid is the absolute unity and uniqueness of God. It asserts that God has no partners, children, equals, or rivals, and does not share divine sovereignty.

The opposite of Tawhid is Shirk, which is the act of associating anything with God that does not belong there. Islamic theology views Shirk as the most serious error because it distorts the fundamental truth of reality.

5. Revelation and the Quran

Islam honors all biblical prophets, including Adam, Abraham, Moses, and Jesus. However, it holds a distinct view on scripture:

  • The Quran: Understood as the direct, verbatim speech of God transmitted to Muhammad via the angel Jibril (Gabriel). It is considered final, perfect, and uncorrupted.
  • Previous Scriptures: The Torah and the Gospel are recognized as original revelations but are believed to have been partially corrupted over time through alteration or careless transmission.
  • Recitation: The word "Quran" means "recitation." Because it is the direct word of God, reciting it in Arabic is considered speaking God's own words.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. What is the difference between the "surrender" of Islam and the surrender of defeat? Answer: The surrender of Islam is a willing, active choice to align oneself with a greater reality, whereas the surrender of defeat is helpless.

2. List the six items of belief included in Iman. Answer: God (Allah), angels, divine books, prophets, the Day of Judgment, and divine decree (qadar).

3. How do classical Islamic scholars describe the fluctuation of faith? Answer: They state that faith increases with obedience and decreases with sin.

4. What are the two distinct functions of the Shahada? Answer: The first part negates false gods and rivals to the divine; the second part affirms the reality of revelation and the messenger.

5. Define "Tawhid" beyond simple arithmetic unity. Answer: Tawhid means God is utterly unique, unlike anything in creation, and without comparison, partners, or equals.

6. Why is "Shirk" considered the most serious error in Islamic theology? Answer: Because it distorts the most fundamental truth about reality: the absolute oneness of God.

7. How does the Islamic view of the Bible differ from its view of the Quran? Answer: The Bible is viewed as a collection of human writings about divine matters that has been partially corrupted. The Quran is viewed as the direct, verbatim speech of God, preserved perfectly.

8. Who is the "Seal of the Prophets" (Khatam al-Anbiya')? Answer: Muhammad.

9. What does the word "Quran" mean in Arabic? Answer: Recitation.

10. What is a "hafiz"? Answer: A person who has memorized the entire Quran by heart.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Relationship Between Faith and Action: Using the definition of iman, discuss how Islam bridges the gap between internal belief and external behavior. How does the concept of faith "increasing with obedience" affect the daily life of a believer?
  2. The Concept of Divine Unity: Analyze the significance of Tawhid and Shirk. Why is the negation of "false gods" (such as wealth or power) in the Shahada considered a necessary precursor to the affirmation of God?
  3. The Nature of Scripture: Contrast the Islamic understanding of the Quran with the Islamic perspective on the Torah and the Gospel. How does the concept of the Quran as "direct speech" rather than "human writing" influence how the text is handled and recited by Muslims?
  4. Communal and Personal Responsibility: Based on the verses from Surah Al-Baqarah (2:285-286), explain how Islam balances the idea that "each soul answers for itself" with the communal identity of the "faithful."

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Hafiz A person who has committed the entire Quran to memory.
Iman Faith, trust, and conviction; an interior state involving heart, mind, and action.
Islam "Surrender" or "submission"; the active alignment with the reality of God.
Jibril The angel (Gabriel) who transmitted the Quran verbatim to Muhammad.
Khatam al-Anbiya' "Seal of the Prophets"; the title for Muhammad as the final prophet.
Muslim "One who surrenders"; a practitioner of Islam.
Qadar Divine decree; one of the six articles of faith.
Quran "Recitation"; the final, uncorrupted, direct speech of God in Islam.
Shahada The declaration of faith: "There is no god but God, and Muhammad is the messenger of God."
Shirk The act of associating partners with God; regarded as the most serious theological error.
Tawhid The absolute unity, oneness, and uniqueness of God.
