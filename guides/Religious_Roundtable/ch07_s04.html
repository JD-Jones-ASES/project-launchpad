Study Guide: Confucian Sacred Texts—The Four Books and Five Classics

Confucianism is fundamentally rooted in the reverence for the written record of human wisdom. Central to this tradition is the belief that Confucius was not an innovator of new ideas, but a "transmitter" of ancient wisdom. As recorded in the Analects (7.1), Confucius stated, “I transmit but do not create; I believe in and love the ancients.” This study guide explores the two foundational collections of Confucianism: the Four Books and the Five Classics.

I. The Four Books

The Four Books were compiled and commented on by the Neo-Confucian philosopher Zhu Xi (1130–1200 CE). These texts eventually served as the core curriculum for the imperial examination system.

II. The Five Classics

The Five Classics are older texts that Confucius is said to have edited and transmitted. He regarded these as the essential records of ancient wisdom, covering a vast range of human experience from poetry to divination.

  • Book of Songs (Shijing): An anthology of ancient Chinese poetry. Confucius believed these poems were essential for cultivating moral imagination, sensitivity, and proper emotional expression.
  • Book of History (Shujing): A collection of records regarding ancient rulers. These serve as models for good governance and provide cautionary tales regarding the consequences of bad leadership.
  • Book of Rites (Liji): A detailed account of ritual practices, ceremonial forms, and social customs that define proper conduct.
  • Book of Changes (Yijing or I Ching): A cosmological text originally used for divination. Confucian thinkers interpreted it as a guide to the underlying patterns governing change in both nature and human affairs.
  • Spring and Autumn Annals (Chunqiu): A historical chronicle of the state of Lu. Though appearing to be a neutral narrative, it is studied for the moral judgments embedded within the history.

III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How did Confucius view his relationship to the texts he studied? Confucius did not view himself as an author or inventor; rather, he saw himself as a transmitter of ancient wisdom who believed in and loved the works of the ancients.
  2. Which philosopher was responsible for compiling the Four Books into the core curriculum for the imperial examination system? The Neo-Confucian philosopher Zhu Xi (1130–1200 CE).
  3. What is the "concentric vision of ethics" described in The Great Learning? It is the idea that the cultivation of virtue begins with the individual and expands outward to the family, the state, and ultimately the entire world.
  4. According to the Mencius, what is the inherent state of human nature? Human nature is fundamentally good, possessing innate "seeds" of virtue, such as compassion and a sense of right and wrong, which must be cultivated.
  5. What is the significance of the Book of Songs in Confucian education? Confucius considered the poetry in this anthology essential for developing a person’s moral imagination and proper feelings.
  6. How do the Spring and Autumn Annals convey Confucian values? While the text is a historical chronicle of the state of Lu, it contains moral judgments embedded within the seemingly neutral narrative.

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Goal of the Confucian Canon: Based on the variety of genres found in the Five Classics (poetry, history, ritual, and divination), analyze how Confucianism seeks to shape the "whole person." Discuss how these different areas of study address the emotional, social, and political dimensions of human life.
  2. Transmitter vs. Creator: Discuss the implications of Confucius’s statement, "I transmit but do not create." How does this perspective influence the authority of the Four Books and the Five Classics within the tradition?
  3. Metaphysics and Ethics: Compare the ethical approach of The Great Learning with the metaphysical approach of The Doctrine of the Mean. How does the Confucian tradition bridge the gap between individual behavior and the order of the cosmos?

V. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Analects (Lunyu): Meaning "selected conversations," this is the primary collection of Confucius's sayings and the central source for his thought.
  • Eight Steps: The process outlined in The Great Learning for moving from the investigation of things to bringing peace to the world.
  • Mencius (Meng Ke): Confucius’s most important disciple (372–289 BCE), known for the argument that human nature is fundamentally good.
  • Neo-Confucianism: The movement associated with Zhu Xi that revitalized Confucian thought and established the Four Books as the standard for the imperial examination system.
  • Zhongyong: The Chinese title for the Doctrine of the Mean, focusing on moderation, balance, and the connection between humanity and the cosmos.
