# Study Guide: The Traditions and Diversity of Christianity

This study guide examines the major branches of Christianity—Roman Catholicism, Eastern Orthodoxy, and Protestantism—exploring their historical origins, unique theological emphases, and shared foundational beliefs.

---

## I. Key Concepts and Historical Context

### The Great Schism (1054)
The first major division in Christianity occurred in 1054, splitting the faith into the Western Roman Catholic Church and the Eastern Orthodox Church. 

*   **Primary Causes:** 
    *   Disputes over the authority of the Pope (the bishop of Rome).
    *   Theological differences.
    *   Centuries of cultural drift between the East and West.
*   **Eastern Orthodoxy:** 
    *   **Geographic Centers:** Russia, Greece, and Eastern Europe.
    *   **Distinctive Practices:** Use of the ancient Greek liturgical tradition and deep reverence for icons (sacred images).
    *   **Key Theology:** *Theosis*—the belief that human beings can be transformed to share in the divine nature through prayer, sacraments, and holy living.

### The Protestant Reformation (1517)
The second major rupture began in the 16th century when Martin Luther challenged the practices of the Roman Catholic Church.

*   **Catalyst:** Martin Luther’s "Ninety-Five Theses," which objected to Church practices, specifically the sale of indulgences (payments to reduce time in purgatory).
*   **Theological Pillars:**
    *   *Sola Scriptura:* Scripture alone serves as the final authority for the faith.
    *   *Sola Fide:* Faith alone, rather than works or sacraments, is the means of salvation.
*   **Legacy:** The Reformation led to a proliferation of denominations, including Lutheran, Reformed (Calvinist), Anglican, and Anabaptist movements, eventually leading to modern groups such as Baptists, Methodists, and Pentecostals.

### Roman Catholicism
Roman Catholicism remains the largest single Christian body, with over one billion members.

*   **Authority:** Emphasizes the Pope and bishops as the direct successors of the apostles.
*   **Sacraments:** Recognizes seven sacraments. A central element is the Eucharist, in which the bread and wine are believed to truly become the body and blood of Christ.
*   **Tradition:** Scripture is interpreted through the lens of Church tradition, and there is a strong emphasis on the veneration of Mary and the saints.

### The Common Core
Despite deep institutional and theological divisions, the three branches share a "common core" based on ancient creeds.

| Feature | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **The Trinity** | Belief in the triune nature of God. |
| **The Incarnation** | The belief in God becoming human in Jesus Christ. |
| **The Resurrection** | The foundational belief in the rising of Christ from the dead. |
| **Scripture** | Recognition of the authority of the Bible. |
| **Eternal Life** | The hope of life beyond death. |

## III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Evolution of Authority:** Compare and contrast how authority is defined and exercised in Roman Catholicism, Eastern Orthodoxy, and Protestantism. How does the role of the Pope differ from the Protestant principle of *sola scriptura*?
2.  **Unity vs. Diversity:** Discuss the "common core" of Christianity in relation to the estimated 30,000 to 45,000 different denominations. Does the lack of a central authority to suppress diverse interpretations of "original sources" strengthen or weaken the tradition?
3.  **The Impact of the Great Schism:** Analyze the factors that led to the excommunication between the East and the West in 1054. How do the liturgical and theological differences established then continue to define Eastern Orthodoxy today?

## IV. Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Apostolic Succession:** The belief in Roman Catholicism that the Pope and bishops are the direct successors of the original apostles.
*   **Eucharist:** A central Christian sacrament; in Catholicism, it is the belief that bread and wine become the actual body and blood of Christ.
*   **Great Schism:** The formal split in 1054 between the Western (Roman Catholic) and Eastern (Orthodox) churches.
*   **Icons:** Sacred images used in worship, particularly revered within the Eastern Orthodox tradition.
*   **Indulgences:** Historically, payments made to the Church that were intended to reduce a soul's time in purgatory; a major point of contention for Martin Luther.
*   **Sola Fide:** "Faith alone"; the Protestant belief that salvation is achieved through faith rather than through works or sacraments.
*   **Sola Scriptura:** "Scripture alone"; the Protestant belief that the Bible is the final and sole authority for Christian faith and practice.
*   **Theosis:** The Eastern Orthodox theological concept of being gradually transformed to share in the divine nature.
*   **Trinity:** The core Christian doctrine regarding the nature of God, shared by all three major branches.
