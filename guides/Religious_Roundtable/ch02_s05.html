Study Guide: The Global Influence and Resilience of Judaism

This study guide explores the historical significance, intellectual contributions, and enduring resilience of Judaism. Despite representing a small fraction of the global population, Jewish thought has profoundly shaped both religious and secular history.

I. Key Concepts and Themes

1. Disproportionate Influence

Though there are only approximately 15 million Jews worldwide—less than two-tenths of one percent of the global population—Judaism is considered one of the most consequential forces in human history. Its influence extends through:

  • Religious Lineage: Judaism provided the roots for Christianity and Islam, which inherited monotheism, covenant theology, and the concept of a God who intervenes in history.
  • Secular Western Culture: Key Jewish concepts include the idea of linear history moving toward a purpose, the insistence on non-negotiable morality, and the prophetic tradition of speaking truth to power.

2. Intellectual and Artistic Contributions

Jewish thinkers have reshaped various fields of human inquiry, reconciling ancient traditions with modern thought:

  • Philosophy and Theology: Moses Maimonides bridged Greek philosophy and monotheism; Baruch Spinoza laid the groundwork for modern biblical criticism.
  • Science and Psychology: Sigmund Freud transformed the understanding of the mind, while Albert Einstein revolutionized the concepts of space and time.
  • Literature: Writers like Franz Kafka and Primo Levi articulated universal human experiences of alienation and survival.

3. The Shoah (The Holocaust)

The Holocaust remains a central event in Jewish history and modern philosophy.

  • Scope: Between 1941 and 1945, the Nazi regime systematically murdered six million Jews, representing one-third of the global Jewish population.
  • Theological Impact: The event forced a deep re-examination of theodicy—the question of why God permits evil—and has led to some of the most rigorous theological and philosophical reflections in any tradition.

4. Historical Resilience

The survival of Judaism through 2,000 years of diaspora, expulsion, and attempted annihilation is a subject of ongoing debate. Whether attributed to divine covenant or cultural resilience, the tradition has remained durable, maintaining its core practices, such as reading the Torah and celebrating Passover, across millennia and through diverse geographical contexts like Babylon and Rome.

II. Short-Answer Practice Quiz

Question Answer
What percentage of the global population is Jewish? Less than two-tenths of one percent (approximately 15 million people).
Which two major world religions grew directly from Jewish roots? Christianity and Islam.
What secular concept of time is attributed to Jewish influence? The concept of linear history moving toward a purpose.
Who was the medieval philosopher who reconciled Greek philosophy with monotheism? Moses Maimonides.
Which 17th-century thinker laid foundations for biblical criticism? Baruch Spinoza.
How many Jews were murdered during the Holocaust? Six million (roughly one-third of the global Jewish population at the time).
What specific religious practices are cited as evidence of Jewish continuity? Reading the Torah and celebrating Passover.

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Roots of Western Thought: Analyze how Jewish religious concepts—such as the prophetic tradition and the concept of a God who acts in history—have transitioned into the framework of secular Western culture.
  2. Intellectual Transformation: Choose two of the thinkers mentioned in the text (e.g., Einstein, Freud, Spinoza) and discuss how their work "transformed human inquiry" according to the source context.
  3. Theodicy and the Holocaust: Explain the impact of the Shoah on post-1945 Jewish philosophy. How does the text describe the nature of the questions raised by this tragedy?
  4. The Mystery of Survival: The text notes that historians and believers find the survival of Judaism "difficult to explain away." Compare the different perspectives offered regarding why the tradition has endured for over two thousand years.

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Covenant Theology: The religious framework involving a formal agreement or bond between God and the people, inherited by Christianity and Islam from Jewish roots.
  • Diaspora: The dispersion of the Jewish people away from their ancestral homeland, lasting for two thousand years.
  • Linear History: The concept that history moves forward toward a specific purpose or destination, rather than being cyclical.
  • Monotheism: The belief in a single, all-powerful God; a core tenet of Judaism passed to other major faiths.
  • Prophetic Tradition: The practice of "speaking truth to power," rooted in the historical Jewish prophets.
  • Shoah: The Hebrew term for the Holocaust; the systematic murder of six million Jews by the Nazi regime between 1941 and 1945.
  • Theodicy: The philosophical and theological study of why an all-powerful, good God permits the existence of evil.
  • Torah: The central scripture of Judaism, which has been read and studied consistently by the Jewish people throughout their history.
