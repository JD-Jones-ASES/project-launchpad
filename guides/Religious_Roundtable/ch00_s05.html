Why Study Religion: A Comprehensive Study Guide

This study guide provides an overview of the foundational reasons for the academic study of religion, exploring its impact on culture, philosophy, and global citizenship. It is designed to facilitate a deeper understanding of why religious literacy is essential for an educated person in the modern world.

Core Themes and Key Concepts

1. Historical and Cultural Literacy

Religion is inextricably linked to human civilization. Understanding the world’s great religious traditions is necessary to comprehend human achievement across various fields:

  • Architecture and Art: From the great cathedrals of Europe to the intricate art of India.
  • Intellectual History: The philosophy of ancient Greece and the legal codes of the Islamic world are rooted in religious context.
  • Literature: Major literary figures—including Shakespeare, Dante, Tolstoy, and Toni Morrison—wrote from perspectives steeped in religious thought.
  • Sociopolitical Movements: Significant historical events and movements, such as the American founding, the abolition movement, and the Civil Rights Movement, are deeply influenced by religious motivations and frameworks.

2. Philosophical Necessity

Religion addresses "hard questions" that fall outside the scope of scientific inquiry. While science explains the physical world, religion (and philosophy) wrestles with existential concerns:

  • Does anything exist beyond the physical world?
  • Is there a specific purpose to human life?
  • What are our moral obligations to one another?
  • Does ultimate justice exist beyond human legal systems?

3. Civic Necessity

In a global population of eight billion, understanding the convictions of others is a requirement for effective citizenship and professional life.

  • Empathy over Agreement: Understanding a religion does not require adopting its beliefs or even agreeing with them.
  • True Understanding: This goes beyond "weak" tolerance (merely putting up with others). It is the ability to see the world through another person's perspective and take their commitments seriously.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why is religion considered a "key" to understanding human achievement?

    • Answer: Religion provides the necessary context for interpreting history, art, law, and literature. Without it, major cultural landmarks like European cathedrals or the music of the African American church remain unintelligible.

  2. How does the scope of religious questioning differ from the scope of scientific questioning?

    • Answer: Science is designed to explain the physical world, whereas religion asks non-trivial, existential questions regarding purpose, the metaphysical, and ultimate justice—areas science is not designed to answer.

  3. Explain the difference between "tolerance" and "understanding" as described in the text.

    • Answer: Tolerance is described in a "weak sense" as merely putting up with people. Understanding is a "stronger" act: it is the ability to see the world as another person sees it and to treat their serious commitments with gravity.

  4. According to the Pew Research Center, what percentage of the world is religiously affiliated?

    • Answer: Approximately 84% (roughly 6.7 billion people).

  5. Name three American historical movements mentioned that were influenced by religion.

    • Answer: The American founding, the abolition movement, and the Civil Rights Movement.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Secular Benefit of Religious Study: Can an atheist or someone who does not believe in a deity still benefit from studying religion? Construct an argument based on the concepts of historical literacy and civic necessity. What might a non-believer gain, and conversely, what might they lack compared to a believer's perspective?

  2. The Distinction Between Study and Practice: Explore the difference between studying a religion and living one. How does the academic pursuit of "understanding" differ from the personal experience of religious "commitment"? Use the text’s definition of understanding to support your analysis.

  3. Religion and the Modern Citizen: In a world of eight billion people with radically different convictions, evaluate the claim that religious literacy is a "civic necessity." How does understanding a neighbor’s religious commitments affect professional and social interactions?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Civic Necessity: The requirement for citizens to understand the diverse beliefs of others to navigate the world effectively as neighbors, professionals, and human beings.
  • Historical and Cultural Literacy: The ability to understand and interpret the history, art, literature, and legal systems of human civilization through the lens of the religious contexts that shaped them.
  • Metaphysical Questions: Questions concerning existence beyond the physical world, such as the purpose of life or the existence of a higher justice.
  • Philosophical Necessity: The drive to confront hard questions about human existence that science is not designed to answer.
  • Religiously Unaffiliated: A demographic category (comprising about 16% of the global population) that does not identify with a specific religious group, though they may still hold spiritual beliefs.
  • Understanding: The ability to see the world from another person's perspective and take their most serious commitments seriously; distinct from mere agreement or weak tolerance.
