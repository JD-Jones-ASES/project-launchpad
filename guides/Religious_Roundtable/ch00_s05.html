Communism as a Religious Tradition: A Study Guide

This study guide examines Communism through the lens of religious studies, exploring why scholars classify it as a tradition alongside major world religions despite its founder’s explicit hostility toward religious institutions.

Overview: The Scholarly Classification of Communism

While Karl Marx famously described religion as the "opiate of the masses"—a tool that numbs people to their suffering and prevents them from taking action—modern scholars of religion include Communism in academic discourse. This inclusion is not a political endorsement but an analytical recognition that Communism functions structurally and intellectually like a religion.

Theoretical Frameworks

Scholars use two primary definitions to bridge the gap between traditional religion and secular movements like Communism:

  • Paul Tillich (Ultimate Concern): The twentieth-century theologian defined faith not as a specific belief in a deity, but as "ultimate concern." This represents whatever a person treats as the most important thing in their life, for which they would sacrifice everything else.
  • Mircea Eliade (The Sacred): This historian of religion argued that the "sacred" is not necessarily the supernatural. Rather, it is any reality that humans treat as set apart, charged with special significance, and requiring a response beyond ordinary behavior.

The Four Structural Features of Communism

Using the frameworks provided by Tillich and Eliade, scholars identify four specific religious structures within Communism:

Feature Description in Communism
Cosmic Story The theory of historical materialism explains all human history as a movement through specific stages: primitive communism, slavery, feudalism, and capitalism, culminating in a final classless society.
Moral Demands Communism calls for solidarity with the oppressed, resistance to exploitation, and the subordination of personal interest to the revolutionary cause.
Sacred Texts The Communist Manifesto (1848), Das Kapital (1867), and the Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844 serve as authoritative scriptures that are interpreted and debated by authorities.
Eschatological Hope The vision of a classless society represents ultimate redemption—the end of alienation, the restoration of human wholeness, and the final fulfillment of history.

Historical Foundations: Karl Marx

Karl Marx (1818–1883) was born into a Jewish family that converted to Protestant Christianity. His intellectual development was heavily influenced by G.W.F. Hegel at the University of Berlin.

  • Hegelian Influence: Hegel proposed that history moves toward a final fulfillment driven by ideas.
  • Marx’s Transformation: Marx adapted Hegel’s framework but argued that history is driven by material conditions—specifically, who owns the tools of production and who performs the labor. This shift from ideas to material reality is the basis of historical materialism.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why did Karl Marx refer to religion as the "opiate of the masses"?
  2. How does Paul Tillich’s definition of faith allow for Communism to be studied as a religion?
  3. According to Mircea Eliade, what characterizes "the sacred"?
  4. What are the five stages of human history identified in the "cosmic story" of historical materialism?
  5. List the three primary texts that function as "scripture" within the Communist tradition.
  6. In the context of Communism, what does the "Party" represent in relation to the "community of the faithful"?
  7. What was the fundamental difference between G.W.F. Hegel’s view of history and Karl Marx’s view of history?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Functional Definition of Religion: Analyze the argument for classifying Communism as a religion. Do you find the frameworks provided by Tillich and Eliade to be sufficient for categorizing a self-described atheist movement as a religious tradition? Use the "four structural features" to support your analysis.
  2. Historical Materialism as Mythology: Compare the "cosmic story" of historical materialism to traditional creation myths or religious narratives. How does the progression from primitive communism to a final classless society mirror religious themes of origin and eventual salvation?
  3. Eschatology and Human Nature: Explore the concept of the "classless society" as eschatological hope. Discuss how this vision addresses the questions of suffering, justice, and human alienation that are central to traditional world religions.

Glossary of Key Terms

  • Classless Society: The final stage of history in Marxist theory; a vision of ultimate redemption characterized by the end of alienation and the restoration of human wholeness.
  • Cosmic Story: A comprehensive narrative of reality; in Communism, this is fulfilled by the theory of historical materialism.
  • Eschatological Hope: A vision concerning the ultimate destiny of humanity or the end of history.
  • Faith (Tillich’s Definition): The structure of human commitment itself; whatever a person treats as their "ultimate concern."
  • Historical Materialism: The theory that history is driven by material conditions (production and labor) rather than ideas.
  • Opiate of the Masses: A metaphor used by Marx to describe religion as a substance that numbs people to the pain of their real-world suffering.
  • The Sacred (Eliade’s Definition): Any reality that is set apart from ordinary life and treated with special significance.
  • Solidarity: A moral demand in Communism requiring unity with the oppressed and the subordination of the self to the revolutionary cause.
