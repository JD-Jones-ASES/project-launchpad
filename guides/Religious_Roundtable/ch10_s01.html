Study Guide: Christianity — The Word Made Flesh

This study guide examines the foundational Christian concepts of faith, revelation, and the Incarnation as presented in the "Religious Roundtable — Cycle 1: Faith & Revelation" series.

I. Core Concepts and Theological Foundations

The Nature of Faith (Pistis)

In the Christian tradition, faith is not established through proofs or propositional lists. Instead, it is rooted in an act of trust and a "total orientation of the self toward God." This orientation involves the mind, the will, and the affections.

The Greek term used in the New Testament is pistis, which carries three specific connotations:

  1. Trust: A willingness to stake one’s life on something not fully visible.
  2. Conviction: Deep-seated belief.
  3. Loyalty: Commitment to a reality rather than just intellectual agreement.

Faith vs. Reason

The Christian tradition distinguishes faith from both "credulity" (thoughtless acceptance) and "certainty" (evidence-based confidence).

  • Thomas Aquinas: The medieval theologian argued that while human reason can conclude that God exists (via the cosmological argument), specific Christian doctrines—such as the Trinity and the Resurrection—cannot be reached by reason alone. These require the intellect to assent to divine truth based on God's authority.
  • Anselm of Canterbury: Known for the phrase fides quaerens intellectum ("faith seeking understanding"), Anselm posited that one must commit to faith first. Understanding is not a prerequisite for belief; rather, understanding unfolds from within the state of trust.

The Incarnation and the Logos

The most distinctive claim of Christian theology is the Incarnation. Unlike other forms of revelation where God might send a message or a prophet, Christianity maintains that God entered human history personally.

  • Jesus as the Word (Logos): The Gospel of John describes Jesus as the logos—the rationality and meaning of the universe—made flesh.
  • Supreme Revelation: Jesus is considered the final revelation because he represents a union of the "information" and the "informer." In this context, faith is defined as trust in a person (Jesus Christ) rather than a system of principles or a text.

II. Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Pistis The Greek New Testament word for faith, meaning trust, conviction, and loyalty.
Substance As used in Hebrews 11:1, it describes faith as something real that grounds ordinary visible reality.
Cosmological Argument The philosophical observation that the existence of the universe requires a cause; used by Aquinas to show that reason can identify God's existence.
Incarnation The theological doctrine that God became a human being in the person of Jesus Christ.
Logos A Greek term meaning "Word," used to describe Jesus as the embodied rationality and meaning of the universe.
Credo Latin for "I believe"; the linguistic root of the English word "creed."
Creed A formal, public statement of communal beliefs and loyalty (e.g., the Apostles' or Nicene Creeds).
Fides Quaerens Intellectum "Faith seeking understanding"; the principle that commitment precedes full comprehension of divine reality.

III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does the Letter to the Hebrews define faith in its eleventh chapter? It defines faith as "the substance of things hoped for, the evidence of things not seen," suggesting that faith has a reality that precedes visible existence.

  2. According to the text, what is the difference between pistis and certainty? Certainty is the evidence-based confidence similar to a scientist measuring a known quantity, whereas pistis is trust extended toward something real but not fully visible.

  3. What role did the Council of Nicea play in Christian history? The Council of Nicea in 325 CE formulated the Nicene Creed, one of the two most widely recited formal statements of belief in Christianity.

  4. How does Thomas Aquinas distinguish between the natural human intellect and Christian claims? Aquinas argued the natural intellect can conclude God exists through the cosmological argument, but specific claims like the Trinity or Incarnation must be received through faith as the intellect assents to divine revelation.

  5. Why is reciting a creed considered more than just an "intellectual exercise"? It is described as an act of communal loyalty and a public declaration of where an individual stands within the community of faith.

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Personhood of Revelation: Analyze the distinction between revelation as "information" and revelation as a "person." How does the concept of the Logos (the Word made flesh) fundamentally change the nature of faith from an intellectual agreement to a personal relationship, according to Christian theology?

  2. Anselm vs. Modernity: Anselm of Canterbury argued for "faith seeking understanding," suggesting that one must "step inside" and trust before understanding can unfold. Compare and contrast this approach with the modern tendency to demand empirical evidence or full comprehension before committing to a belief.

  3. The Philosophical Sophistication of Hebrews: The text describes the Letter to the Hebrews as one of the most philosophically sophisticated in the New Testament. Examine how the author of Hebrews uses the concept of "substance" to argue that faith is a position of strength rather than intellectual weakness or "credulity."
