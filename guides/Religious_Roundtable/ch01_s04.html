Study Guide: Sacred Texts in Christianity – The Bible

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the Christian Bible, exploring its structure, historical development, and the varying ways it is interpreted across different Christian traditions.

I. Overview of the Bible as a Library

Rather than a single book, the Bible is best understood as a "library" of diverse writings. Its unity is found in its central subject: the evolving relationship between God and humanity, spanning from creation and the fall to redemption and restoration.

  • Composition Timeline: Written over approximately one thousand years.
  • Original Languages: Hebrew, Aramaic, and Greek.
  • Authorship: Dozens of authors from various social backgrounds, ranging from kings to fishermen.
  • Literary Genres: The Bible contains law, history, poetry, prophecy, biography, letter-writing, and apocalyptic visions.

II. The Two Testaments

The Bible is divided into two primary sections, though the specific number of books varies by tradition.

The Old Testament

Known in Judaism as the Tanakh or Hebrew Bible, this section records God’s relationship with the people of Israel.

  • Key Figures and Themes: Includes the creation story, the lives of Abraham and Moses, the prophets, the psalms of David, and the history of the "chosen people."
  • Canon Variations:
    • Protestant Canon: 39 books.
    • Catholic Canon: 46 books, including additional works known as the Deuterocanon or Apocrypha.

The New Testament

Comprised of 27 books written in the first century by followers of Jesus.

  • The Gospels: Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John. These four accounts narrate the life, teachings, death, and resurrection of Jesus. They offer different perspectives tailored to different audiences.
  • Acts of the Apostles: A historical account of the early Christian church.
  • The Epistles: Formal letters written by Paul and other early leaders to address theological questions and provide guidance to young Christian communities.
  • The Book of Revelation: A symbolic, apocalyptic vision of cosmic history.

III. Canonization and Translation

The process of determining which texts were "sacred scripture" was gradual.

  • Formation: Early Christians circulated many texts, but not all were universally accepted.
  • The Council of Carthage (397): A pivotal meeting of bishops that ratified the 27-book New Testament canon recognized by most Christians today.
  • Historical Manuscripts: The Codex Sinaiticus (c. 330–360) is the oldest complete Greek manuscript of the New Testament.
  • Global Reach: The Bible is the most translated book in history. It has been translated into more than 700 languages, with portions available in over 3,500 languages.

IV. Interpretive Traditions

Different Christian denominations approach the authority and interpretation of the Bible in distinct ways:

Tradition Approach to Interpretation
Roman Catholicism The Bible is read through the lens of Church tradition and the Magisterium (the teaching authority of the Church hierarchy).
Protestantism Follows the principle of sola scriptura (Scripture alone), viewing the Bible as the ultimate authority. This has led to high interpretive diversity.
Eastern Orthodoxy Views Scripture and ancient Church tradition as inseparable; the Bible cannot be understood apart from the community that produced it.

  2. What are the three original languages in which the Bible was written?

VI. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Bible as a "Library": Discuss the implications of viewing the Bible as a collection of diverse genres and authors rather than a single, uniform book. How does this diversity affect its narrative of the relationship between God and humanity?
  2. Comparative Authority: Compare and contrast how Roman Catholics, Protestants, and Eastern Orthodox Christians define the relationship between the Bible and Church tradition. How do these differences impact the way the text is interpreted by believers?
  3. The Evolution of the Canon: Describe the process of how the New Testament came to be. Why was the Council of Carthage a significant milestone, and what does the history of the canon suggest about early Christian diversity?

VII. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Apocrypha / Deuterocanon: Additional books included in the Catholic Old Testament canon that are not found in the Protestant canon.
  • Canon: The authoritative collection of texts recognized by a religious community as sacred scripture.
  • Epistle: A formal letter; the New Testament contains many epistles written by Paul and other leaders to early Christian communities.
  • Gospel: One of the four New Testament accounts (Matthew, Mark, Luke, John) detailing the life and teachings of Jesus.
  • Magisterium: The teaching authority of the Roman Catholic Church hierarchy used to interpret Scripture.
  • Sola Scriptura: The Protestant principle that Scripture alone serves as the ultimate authority for Christian belief and practice.
  • Tanakh: The Hebrew Bible, referred to by Christians as the Old Testament.
