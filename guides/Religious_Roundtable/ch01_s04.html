Study Guide: Sacred Texts of Christianity: The Bible

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the Christian Bible, exploring its structure, history, and the various interpretive frameworks used by major Christian traditions.

Core Concepts and Structure

The Bible is the central sacred text of Christianity. While often referred to as a single book, it is more accurately described as a library of diverse works.

Composition and Unity

  • Diverse Authorship: The Bible was written over approximately one thousand years by dozens of authors with varying backgrounds, including kings, fishermen, and scholars.
  • Multilingual Origins: The texts were originally composed in three languages: Hebrew, Aramaic, and Greek.
  • Literary Genres: The collection contains various literary forms, including law, history, poetry, prophecy, biography, formal letters (epistles), and apocalyptic visions.
  • Central Theme: Despite its diversity, Christians view the Bible as having a unified subject: the relationship between God and humanity. This narrative arc moves from creation through the fall and redemption toward restoration.

The Two Testaments

The Bible is divided into two primary sections that record different eras of God's relationship with humanity.

The New Testament Components

  1. The Gospels: Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John. These four accounts narrate the life, teachings, death, and resurrection of Jesus Christ. They offer different perspectives and were written for different audiences.
  2. Acts of the Apostles: A historical account of the early Christian church.
  3. The Epistles: Formal letters written by Paul and other early leaders to address theological questions and provide guidance to young Christian communities.
  4. The Book of Revelation: A symbolic vision of cosmic history and the final restoration.

Canonization and History

The formation of the Bible was a gradual process. While many texts circulated among early Christians, not all were universally accepted.

  • Canonization: The "canon" refers to the authoritative collection of texts recognized as sacred scripture.
  • Council of Carthage (397): A pivotal moment where a council of bishops ratified the twenty-seven-book New Testament recognized by most Christians today.
  • Historical Manuscripts: The Codex Sinaiticus (dating to 330–360) is the oldest complete manuscript of the New Testament in Greek.
  • Translation: The Bible is the most translated book in history, with the full text available in over 700 languages and portions available in over 3,500.

Interpretive Traditions

Different Christian traditions employ distinct lenses when reading and applying biblical texts:

  • Roman Catholicism: Scripture is read through the lens of Church tradition and the magisterium (the teaching authority of the Church hierarchy).
  • Protestantism: Following the Reformation of the sixteenth century, Protestants emphasize sola scriptura (Scripture alone) as the ultimate authority for belief and practice.
  • Eastern Orthodoxy: Scripture and the ancient tradition of the Church are viewed as inseparable; the Bible is understood specifically within the context of the community that produced it.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why is it considered misleading to call the Bible a "single book"?
  2. What are the three original languages in which the Bible was written?
  3. According to the text, what is the central theme that provides unity to the various books of the Bible?
  4. What is the "Deuterocanon" (or Apocrypha), and which Christian tradition includes it in its canon?
  5. How do the four Gospels differ from a standard modern biography?
  6. What occurred at the Council of Carthage in 397?
  7. Define the Protestant principle of sola scriptura.
  8. What is the significance of the Codex Sinaiticus?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Evolution of the Canon: Discuss the process by which the New Testament was formed. Why was this process gradual, and what role did church councils play in finalizing the collection?
  2. Comparative Interpretations: Contrast the Roman Catholic, Protestant, and Eastern Orthodox approaches to biblical interpretation. How does each tradition's view of "Church tradition" affect how they read the text?
  3. Unity in Diversity: The Bible contains dozens of authors and numerous literary genres. Analyze how the overarching narrative of "creation, fall, redemption, and restoration" serves to unify these diverse elements.
  4. The Bible as a Global Text: Reflect on the historical impact of the Bible’s translation into thousands of languages. How does the democratization of the text through translation and the Protestant Reformation relate to the diversity of modern Christian conclusions?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Apocrypha / Deuterocanon: Additional books included in the Catholic Old Testament canon that are not found in the Protestant canon.
  • Canon: The authoritative collection of texts recognized by a religious community as sacred scripture.
  • Epistle: A formal letter; specifically, the letters written by Paul and other early leaders that make up a large portion of the New Testament.
  • Gospel: One of the four New Testament accounts (Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John) narrating the life and teachings of Jesus.
  • Magisterium: The teaching authority of the Roman Catholic Church hierarchy.
  • Sola Scriptura: The Protestant principle identifying "Scripture alone" as the ultimate authority for Christian faith and practice.
  • Tanakh: The Hebrew Bible, which Christians refer to as the Old Testament.
