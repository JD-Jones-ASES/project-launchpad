Study Guide: The Origins and Foundations of Judaism

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the foundational history, key figures, and central theological concepts of Judaism as presented in the historical record and religious texts.

I. Historical Narrative and Key Concepts

The Patriarchal Foundations

The origins of Judaism are traced back to approximately 1800 BCE with a man named Abram (later Abraham) in the Mesopotamian city of Ur. The tradition begins with a divine call for Abraham to leave his home for a new land, accompanied by a promise that he would become a "great nation." This established the Covenant, a formal, sacred agreement featuring mutual obligations: God would be the God of the people, and they would be His people.

Liberation and Law

Centuries after Abraham, his descendants were enslaved in Egypt. The figure of Moses is central to this era. Following a divine encounter at a burning bush, Moses led the Israelites out of Egypt—an event known as the Exodus. This liberation is considered the defining moment of the tradition, commemorated annually during the Passover Seder.

Following the Exodus, the Israelites received the Torah at Mount Sinai. This revelation included 613 commandments (mitzvot), including the Ten Commandments. These laws were not merely rules but the specific terms of the covenant, governing agriculture, family, and social conduct.

The Monarchy and the Temple

After forty years of wandering in the wilderness, the Israelites entered Canaan. Around 1000 BCE, King David unified the tribes and established Jerusalem as the capital. His son, Solomon, built the First Temple around 950 BCE. The Temple served as the theological center of the nation, believed to be the dwelling place of God’s presence.

Exile and Transformation

The history of early Judaism is marked by two major architectural and theological catastrophes:

  1. The Babylonian Exile (586 BCE): King Nebuchadnezzar destroyed the First Temple and exiled the population. This forced a theological shift; prophets like Isaiah and Ezekiel argued that God had not abandoned the people, but was using the exile as a consequence of unfaithfulness.
  2. The Roman Destruction (70 CE): After a period of return under the Persian King Cyrus and the building of the Second Temple, the Romans eventually destroyed the Second Temple following a Jewish revolt. Only the Western Wall (Kotel) remained.

These events initiated the Diaspora, the scattering of the Jewish people. With the Temple gone, the focus of the faith shifted from priestly sacrifice to the study of texts and the leadership of rabbis.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. Who is considered the founding patriarch of Judaism, and what was his original name?

  • Answer: Abraham is the founding patriarch; his original name was Abram.

2. What is the literal meaning of the name "Abraham"?

  • Answer: It means "father of many nations."

3. Define the "Covenant" as it relates to the relationship between God and the Israelites.

  • Answer: A covenant is a formal, sacred agreement with mutual obligations on both sides. In this context, God promised to be their God, and the Israelites promised to be His people.

4. What is the "Exodus," and why is it significant?

  • Answer: The Exodus is the liberation of the Israelites from slavery in Egypt. It is significant as the foundational act of God’s rescue and faithfulness and the defining moment of the Jewish tradition.

5. Where did Moses receive the Torah, according to Jewish tradition?

  • Answer: At the foot of Mount Sinai.

6. How many commandments (mitzvot) are contained within the Torah?

  • Answer: 613 commandments.

7. Which two kings are credited with reaching the political peak of Israel, and what were their primary achievements?

  • Answer: King David (unified the tribes and established Jerusalem as the capital) and King Solomon (built the First Temple).

8. What happened in 586 BCE?

  • Answer: The Babylonian empire under King Nebuchadnezzar invaded Jerusalem, destroyed the First Temple, and carried the population into exile.

9. How did the destruction of the Second Temple in 70 CE change the practice of Judaism?

  • Answer: The center of Jewish life shifted from priests and sacrifice to rabbis and study.

10. What is the translation and significance of the name "Israel" (Yisrael)?

  • Answer: It translates to "one who wrestles with God." It signifies a tradition that includes challenging, debating, and questioning God.

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Evolution of the Covenant: Discuss how the terms and understanding of the Covenant evolved from the time of Abraham’s initial call to the revelation of the 613 mitzvot at Mount Sinai. How did the Torah provide the "specific shape" of the relationship with God?
  2. Crisis and Continuity: Analyze the impact of the Babylonian Exile and the subsequent destruction of the Second Temple by the Romans. How did these catastrophes lead to a "profound rethinking" of the faith rather than its dissolution?
  3. The Role of Memory: The text states that Jews are commanded to consider themselves as though they "personally came out of Egypt." Explore the role of historical memory and ritual (such as the Passover Seder) in maintaining Jewish identity during the Diaspora.

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Covenant A formal, sacred agreement between God and a people, involving mutual obligations.
Diaspora The scattering of Jewish people away from their ancestral homeland to other parts of the world.
Exodus The liberation of the Israelites from slavery in Egypt; the foundational act of God's rescue.
Israel (Yisrael) A name meaning "one who wrestles with God," given to the patriarch Jacob.
Mitzvot The 613 commandments found in the Torah governing all aspects of life.
Passover Seder A ritual meal and retelling of the Exodus story.
Torah The first five books of the Hebrew Bible; also refers more broadly to all Jewish teaching and law.
Western Wall (Kotel) The outer western retaining wall of the Second Temple; the only part remaining after the Roman destruction in 70 CE.
