Study Guide: Sacred Texts and Traditions of Judaism

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the foundational texts, intellectual traditions, and communal practices of Judaism as outlined in the provided source material. It explores the structure of the Hebrew Bible, the development of the Talmudic tradition, and the role of collective prayer.

I. The Sacred Library: The Tanakh

The center of the Jewish tradition is a vast collection of writings known as the Tanakh, which serves as the Hebrew Bible. The name is an acronym derived from its three primary sections: Torah, Nevi'im, and Ketuvim.

Components of the Tanakh

Section Name Translation Description Key Contents
Torah The Law The innermost core of the faith, believed to be revealed by God to Moses at Sinai. The five books of Moses: Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy.
Nevi'im The Prophets Historical accounts and the writings of prophets who interpreted history and called for faithfulness. Isaiah, Jeremiah, Amos, Micah, and various historical books.
Ketuvim The Writings A collection of diverse literature including poetry, wisdom, and narrative. Psalms, Job, and the Song of Songs.

Note: While the Tanakh is roughly equivalent to the Christian "Old Testament," Jewish tradition regards it as a complete and authoritative work in its own right and does not use the Christian terminology.

II. The Oral Torah and the Talmudic Tradition

Jewish tradition holds that Moses received two forms of revelation at Sinai: the Written Torah and the Oral Torah. The Oral Torah consists of interpretations and applications of the law that were originally passed down through speech.

The Evolution of the Talmud

Following the destruction of the Second Temple in 70 CE, the Jewish community was scattered. To prevent the loss of the Oral Torah, the rabbis began the process of codifying these oral traditions into writing.

  1. The Mishnah (c. 200 CE): Compiled by Rabbi Judah the Prince, this is the first written collection of the Oral Torah, organizing rabbinic rulings by topic.
  2. The Gemara (c. 200–500 CE): A collection of rabbinic commentaries and discussions regarding the Mishnah, compiled in both Babylon and Palestine.
  3. The Talmud: The combination of the Mishnah + Gemara. It is the central text of Rabbinic Judaism, containing law, ethics, history, and theology.

The Nature of Rabbinic Argument

Unlike many other sacred texts that only record final conclusions, the Talmud preserves the arguments themselves. It records the disputes of various schools, most notably those of Rabbi Shammai and Rabbi Hillel.

  • Living Tradition: The principle "These and these are the words of the living God" illustrates that even minority or unadopted opinions are preserved as sacred.
  • Timelessness: In Jewish academies (yeshivas), students engage with these ancient debates as if the rabbis—some of whom lived hundreds of years apart—are in the same room.

III. Worship and the Siddur

Jewish religious life transitioned from a system of sacrifice led by priests to a system of interpretation and prayer led by rabbis.

Communal Prayer

  • The Siddur: The prayer book used to organize the three daily services (morning, afternoon, evening) as well as liturgies for Shabbat and holy days.
  • The Minyan: A quorum of ten adults required for communal prayer.
  • Collective Orientation: Jewish prayers are focused on the collective rather than the individual. They use "our God" rather than "my God" and focus on the restoration of Israel and humanity rather than personal salvation.

IV. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What does the acronym "Tanakh" stand for?
  2. How do the Jewish and Christian views of the Hebrew Bible's naming and authority differ?
  3. Why did the rabbis decide to write down the Oral Torah around 200 CE?
  4. Who was responsible for compiling the Mishnah?
  5. What is the mathematical "formula" that defines the Talmud?

V. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Evolution of Authority: Analyze the transition from a priestly tradition centered on sacrifice to a rabbinic tradition centered on text and interpretation. How did the destruction of the Second Temple necessitate this shift?
  2. The Theology of Argument: Discuss the significance of the phrase "These and these are the words of the living God." How does the Talmud’s inclusion of conflicting arguments reflect the Jewish approach to truth and tradition?
  3. Collectivism in Worship: Examine the structure and language of the Siddur. How does the focus on communal identity ("our God") distinguish Jewish prayer from individual-centric religious practices?

VI. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Gemara: The rabbinic commentary on the Mishnah; part of the Talmud.
  • Ketuvim: "The Writings"; the third section of the Tanakh.
  • Minyan: A quorum of ten adults required for certain communal prayers.
  • Mishnah: The first written collection of the Oral Torah, compiled around 200 CE.
  • Nevi'im: "The Prophets"; the second section of the Tanakh.
  • Oral Torah: The interpretations and applications of the law believed to have been given to Moses alongside the Written Torah.
  • Rabbi: A teacher and interpreter of the Torah; the central figure of Jewish life following the destruction of the Temple. The word means "my master" or "my teacher."
  • Siddur: The Jewish prayer book.
  • Talmud: The multi-volume compilation of Jewish law, ethics, and theology comprising the Mishnah and the Gemara.
  • Tanakh: The Hebrew Bible, consisting of the Torah, Nevi'im, and Ketuvim.
  • Torah: "The Law" or the Five Books of Moses; the innermost core of the Tanakh.
  • Yeshiva: A Jewish academy of study where students engage with sacred texts and rabbinic debates.
