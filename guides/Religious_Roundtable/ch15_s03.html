Study Guide: Buddhism and the Sangha

Overview

This section explores the central role of the Sangha, the Buddhist community, as one of the Three Jewels alongside the Buddha and the Dharma. It delves into the historical origins of the Sangha, the disciplined life of monastic practitioners (monks and nuns) governed by the Vinaya, and the expanded understanding of the Sangha in Mahayana Buddhism to include all practitioners committed to collective liberation. The text emphasizes that community is not merely a backdrop but an essential medium for spiritual practice and the realization of core Buddhist teachings.

Key Concepts

  • The Three Jewels (Triple Gem / Three Refuges): The foundational elements of Buddhist practice: the Buddha (the awakened teacher), the Dharma (the teaching), and the Sangha (the community). Taking refuge in all three is the defining act of a Buddhist.
  • The Sangha's Essential Role: The community is not an optional or secondary aspect of Buddhist life but an inseparable and indispensable component, as vital as the Buddha and the Dharma themselves.
  • Monastic Discipline (Vinaya): A detailed code of conduct governing the communal life of Buddhist monks (bhikkhus) and nuns (bhikkhunis) in monasteries, designed to create an environment conducive to awakening by reducing ego and distraction.
  • Community as a Tool for Awakening: Living in community, with its inherent interdependence, friction, and compromise, helps practitioners realize the teaching of anatta (non-self) by challenging the illusion of a fixed, permanent self.
  • The Bodhisattva Ideal (Mahayana Buddhism): A core Mahayana concept where an enlightened being postpones their own Nirvana to help all sentient beings achieve liberation. This ideal makes the path to liberation inherently communal and expands the definition of Sangha to include all practitioners.
  • Self-Mastery in Community: As illustrated by the Dhammapada, the disciplined endurance of challenges and the cultivation of inner strength in a communal setting are vital practices that lead to true freedom and spiritual advancement.

Important Terms

  • Sangha (SANG-hah): The community of Buddhist practitioners. In Theravada tradition, it often refers to the monastic community; in Mahayana, it includes all practitioners, monastic and lay. It is one of the Three Jewels.
  • Three Jewels / Three Refuges / Triple Gem: The fundamental objects of Buddhist veneration and commitment: the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Sangha. Taking "refuge" in these three signifies one's commitment to the Buddhist path.
  • Vinaya (vi-NAH-yah): The comprehensive code of monastic discipline that governs the daily life, conduct, and interactions of Buddhist monks and nuns.
  • Bhikkhu (BIK-koo): A fully ordained Buddhist monk. The feminine equivalent is Bhikkhuni. Monks and nuns live according to the Vinaya and form the core of the traditional monastic Sangha.
  • Bodhisattva (boh-dee-SAT-tvah): In Mahayana Buddhism, a being who has made a vow to attain enlightenment not for their own sake alone, but for the liberation and benefit of all sentient beings. This ideal underpins the communal aspect of Mahayana practice.
  • Dharma: The teachings of the Buddha; the path to liberation. One of the Three Jewels.
  • Buddha: The awakened teacher, Siddhartha Gautama, whose teachings form the basis of Buddhism. One of the Three Jewels.
  • Anatta: The Buddhist doctrine of "non-self" or "no-soul," teaching that there is no permanent, unchanging self or essence to an individual.

Detailed Notes

The Foundation: The Three Jewels and Taking Refuge

The commitment to Buddhism begins with "taking refuge" in the Three Jewels: the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Sangha. This act is recited daily by millions, underscoring the profound importance of community. The text explicitly states that the Sangha is not a secondary element but is as essential and inseparable from Buddhist life as the Buddha and Dharma themselves, likening them to the light and heat of a flame.

The Origins of the Sangha

According to tradition, after achieving enlightenment, the Buddha initially hesitated to teach. It was the god Brahma who persuaded him, arguing that some beings were ready to understand the teachings. The Buddha's subsequent decision to teach marked the very founding act of the Buddhist community, demonstrating that the path to awakening was intended to be shared, not solitary.

The Traditional Monastic Sangha

The original Sangha was comprised of monks (bhikkhus) and later nuns (bhikkhunis) who dedicated their lives fully to the Buddha's path.

  • Simple Life: Monastics live a life devoid of possessions, focused on simplicity.
  • The Vinaya: Their communal life is meticulously governed by the Vinaya, a detailed code of monastic discipline. This code covers every aspect of their existence, from eating and speaking to resolving conflicts.
  • The Monastery as a Training Ground: The monastery is not just a dwelling but a carefully constructed environment designed to foster awakening. Its routines, relationships, and rules aim to:
    • Reduce Ego and Distraction: The communal structure constantly challenges the illusion of a fixed, permanent self (anatta) by requiring compromise, interdependence, and exposure to others who know one intimately.
    • Increase Clarity and Compassion: By minimizing external distractions and internal self-centeredness, the environment promotes clear understanding and empathy.
  • Community and Anatta: The friction, compromise, and interdependence inherent in communal living serve as powerful practical lessons in the doctrine of anatta (non-self), helping practitioners directly experience the impermanent and interconnected nature of existence.

The Expanded Sangha in Mahayana Buddhism

In Mahayana Buddhism, the concept of the Sangha significantly broadens:

  • The Bodhisattva Ideal: This central Mahayana teaching emphasizes the vow to achieve enlightenment for the benefit of all sentient beings, not just oneself. This inherently communal goal extends the reach of liberation beyond the individual.
  • Inclusive Community: The Mahayana Sangha encompasses not only monks and nuns but also lay practitioners who support the monasteries and integrate Buddhist practice into their daily lives. The definition includes all beings who are, in any capacity, progressing toward awakening.
  • Collective Liberation: The bodhisattva vow exemplifies the radical connection between individual freedom and the liberation of the collective; true freedom is seen as intertwined with the suffering of others.

The Dhammapada: Endurance and Self-Mastery in Community

The Dhammapada, a revered Buddhist text, reinforces the importance of disciplined practice within a community context. Chapter 21, "The Elephant," uses the metaphor of a tamed elephant enduring arrows in battle to describe the strength of a monk who endures abuse and insult without reaction.

  • Strength Through Non-Reaction: This is not passive submission but a demonstration of profound inner freedom and mastery, where one does not need to prove power.
  • Self-Taming: The verses highlight that taming oneself is superior to taming animals, leading to the "untrodden country" (Nirvana).
  • Community as the Medium: The passage implicitly suggests that meditating and living with others who share a similar commitment is not merely convenient but is itself a vital practice. The Sangha provides the "medium" or context in which the spiritual life truly unfolds and deepens.

Review Questions

  1. What are the "Three Jewels" of Buddhism, and why is the Sangha considered an indispensable component, rather than just a "bonus feature," according to the text?
  2. Describe how the original monastic Sangha (bhikkhus and bhikkhunis) lived. How does adherence to the Vinaya and the structured environment of the monastery facilitate the path to awakening?
  3. Explain the key differences in how the "Sangha" is understood in Theravada Buddhism versus Mahayana Buddhism. How does the Mahayana concept of the Bodhisattva ideal relate to this expanded understanding of community?
  4. The text argues that living in community helps practitioners realize the teaching of anatta (non-self). Discuss specific ways in which the "friction, compromise, and interdependence" of communal life might challenge the illusion of a fixed, permanent self.
  5. Based on the excerpt from the Dhammapada, Chapter 21 ("The Elephant"), how does the text illustrate the concept of disciplined self-mastery? What does it suggest about the nature of true strength and its connection to enduring challenges in a communal context?
