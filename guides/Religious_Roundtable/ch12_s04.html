Chapter 12, Section 4: At the Roundtable: Comparing the Human Person

Overview

This section explores how three distinct traditions—Confucianism, Norse Paganism, and Communism—define the human person, diagnose fundamental problems, and propose solutions. It highlights a central contrast between traditions that see difficulties as cosmic and immutable versus those that view them as human-made and changeable, with Confucianism occupying a unique middle ground focused on internal cultivation and relational ethics.

Key Concepts

  • Confucian Definition of Humanity: Humans are constituted by their relationships, and their humanity (ren) is a practice realized through how they treat others.
    • Problem: Failure of cultivation, meaning a lack of effort in developing virtues.
    • Solution: Education, self-reflection, ritual practice, and guidance from wise teachers to foster virtue.
  • Norse Pagan Definition of Humanity: Humans are material creatures defined by their origin and fate, embedded in a cosmic web of destiny.
    • Problem: The inescapable difficulty of existence in a universe that offers no promise of safety, even for the gods.
    • Solution: Facing one's fated life with courage and character, striving to leave behind deeds worth remembering.
  • Communist Definition of Humanity: Humans are distinctively defined by their capacity for free, conscious, creative labor and production.
    • Problem: The capitalist economic system, which alienates workers from their labor, its products, their species-being, and each other.
    • Solution: Outward revolution to transform the economic structures that cause alienation.
  • Contrasting Loci of Problem: Norse Paganism locates the problem in the inherent cosmic order (fate, mortality), while Communism locates it in specific, changeable human arrangements (the capitalist system).
  • Opposing Solutions: Due to their differing diagnoses, Norse tradition calls for courage in the face of the unchangeable, while Communism calls for revolution to change what seems fixed.
  • Confucianism's Middle Ground: It focuses on internal cultivation and relational virtue, distinct from cosmic fate or economic structures. Its solution involves disciplined personal growth within daily relationships.

Important Terms

  • Ren (Confucianism): The core concept of humanity or benevolence; not a private possession but a practice realized through ethical conduct in relationships.
  • Norns (Norse Paganism): Deities who weave the web of fate, determining the destinies of gods and humans alike.
  • Alienation (Communism): A state experienced by workers under capitalism, where they are separated from the products of their labor, the act of laboring itself, their fundamental human nature ("species-being"), and other people.
  • Cultivation (Confucianism): The disciplined effort to develop virtues and improve oneself ethically, particularly in relation to others.
  • Capitalism: An economic system, seen by Communism as the root cause of human alienation due to its structure of private ownership and wage labor.

Detailed Notes

The Human Person: Three Perspectives

This section introduces three distinct ways of understanding the human person, each leading to a unique diagnosis of what is wrong with humanity and a corresponding proposed solution.

  1. Confucianism: The Relational Person

    • Definition: For Confucianism, human beings are fundamentally defined by their relationships. We are not isolated individuals who then choose to enter society; rather, our identity and humanity are constituted by our connections to others from birth.
    • Humanity (Ren): The essence of humanity, ren, is not an innate private quality but a dynamic practice. It is realized or failed based on how we actively treat the people around us—our family, friends, community members.
    • The Problem: The core issue is not a divine punishment or an oppressive economic system, but a "failure of cultivation." Humans have not diligently worked to develop the virtues (like kindness, respect, sincerity) that enable genuinely harmonious and ethical life.
    • The Solution: The path to rectifying this lies in:
      • Education: Learning proper conduct and wisdom.
      • Self-reflection: Regularly examining one's actions and intentions.
      • Ritual Practice: Engaging in structured social and cultural practices that reinforce respect and order.
      • Guidance of Wise Teachers: Learning from those who have successfully cultivated virtue.

  2. Norse Paganism: The Fated Person

    • Definition: Norse tradition views humans as material creatures whose identity is defined by their origin and inescapable fate. We are described as "trees given breath, sense, and warmth by three gods," signifying our earthly, physical nature. From birth, we are embedded in a cosmic "web of fate woven by the Norns."
    • Nature of Existence: Humans are not primarily spiritual beings seeking transcendence, but earthly beings called to confront their fated lives with courage and strong character. The cosmos itself, even for the gods, does not promise safety or eternal survival.
    • The Problem: The fundamental difficulty is not a personal failing or a societal flaw, but the "inescapable difficulty of existence" within a harsh, predetermined cosmos. Life is inherently challenging, and mortality is universal.
    • The Solution: Given the unchangeable nature of fate, the solution is not escape or revolution. Instead, it is about living courageously, accepting one's destiny, and acting in ways that leave behind "deeds worth remembering." This emphasizes honor, bravery, and lasting reputation.

  3. Communism: The Laboring Person

    • Definition: Communism defines the human person through their capacity for labor. What makes us uniquely human is our ability for "free, conscious, creative production"—to actively shape our world and express ourselves through work.
    • The Problem: The problem is explicitly not an internal spiritual failing or a lack of virtue. It is external and systemic: the "economic system" itself, specifically capitalism. Capitalism fundamentally distorts human nature by alienating workers.
    • Alienation: Capitalism causes alienation in several ways:
      • From products: Workers do not own or control what they produce.
      • From the act of laboring: Work becomes a means to an end, not a fulfilling expression of self.
      • From species-being: It separates humans from their essential creative and productive nature.
      • From each other: Competition and division replace cooperation.
    • The Solution: The remedy is not inward transformation but "outward revolution." The fundamental transformation of the economic structures that produce alienation is required to restore true human flourishing.

Contrasting the Diagnoses and Solutions

The three traditions offer compelling points of comparison:

  • Norse Paganism vs. Communism: These two traditions both acknowledge the profound difficulty of human existence. However, they locate the source of the problem in drastically different places:

    • Norse Paganism: The problem is cosmic, embedded in fate, mortality, and the inherent nature of the universe. It is largely unchangeable.
    • Communism: The problem is systemic and human-made, residing in the capitalist economic system. It is entirely changeable.
    • Opposing Responses: Their solutions directly reflect these diagnoses. Norse tradition advocates for courage and acceptance in the face of what cannot be changed, while Communism calls for active revolution to fundamentally alter what appears fixed.

  • Confucianism's Middle Position: Confucianism occupies a distinctive space between these two extremes.

    • It doesn't attribute the problem to inescapable cosmic fate (like Norse Paganism) nor to a specific, oppressive economic structure (like Communism).
    • Instead, its focus is on relationships and the "failure to cultivate virtue in the relational fabric of daily life."
    • Solution: Consequently, the Confucian solution is neither stoic courage in the face of doom nor political revolution. It is the "patient, disciplined work of becoming a better person in relation to others"—an ongoing process of self-improvement and ethical engagement within one's social sphere.

A Question for Reflection

The section concludes with a powerful question: What is most human about you? Is it your relationships, your courage in the face of fate, your work, or something else entirely—your consciousness, your freedom, your capacity to ask such questions?

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast how Confucianism, Norse Paganism, and Communism define the "human person." What are the core elements each tradition emphasizes?
  2. Explain the primary "problem" that each tradition identifies for humanity. How does each diagnosis directly influence the proposed "solution"?
  3. Discuss the fundamental difference between Norse Paganism and Communism regarding where they locate the difficulty of human existence. How does this lead to their entirely opposite solutions?
  4. In what ways does Confucianism occupy an "interesting middle position" when compared to Norse Paganism and Communism?
  5. Reflecting on the perspectives presented, which definition of the human person (relational, fated, or laboring) resonates most with you, and why? Are there elements from each that you find compelling?
