CHRISTIANITY: THE KINGDOM OF GOD – Study Guide

Overview

This section explores Christian eschatology, the study of "last things," focusing on the Christian belief in a future that is both entirely new and the fulfillment of all that has come before. It highlights the resurrection of Jesus as the central foundation for Christian hope and discusses various interpretations of the "millennium" found in the Book of Revelation, emphasizing that despite different views, all agree on God's ultimate victory and the renewal of creation. The section also underscores the present-day implications of eschatological hope and the symbolic nature of apocalyptic literature.

Key Concepts

  • Paradox of Christian Eschatology: The future promised by God is simultaneously entirely new and the culmination of history, not an escape from it. It represents the renewal and completion of creation, where suffering and death are overcome.
  • Hope Grounded in God's Character: Christian eschatology is fundamentally a doctrine of hope, stemming from the belief that God's final word is renewal, overriding the present realities of suffering and injustice.
  • The Centrality of Jesus' Resurrection: The bodily resurrection of Jesus is not a minor belief but the core of Christian eschatological hope. It is seen as concrete evidence that death is not final and as the "first fruits" of a broader renewal of all creation, making future hope an inference based on a past event.
  • The New Heaven and New Earth: This vision from the Book of Revelation (Chapter 21) describes a transformed reality where God dwells with humanity, and there is no more death, mourning, crying, or pain, signifying complete renewal.
  • Diversity in Millennial Interpretations: Christian thought on the "end times" includes three main positions regarding the "thousand-year reign" (millennium) described in Revelation 20:
    • Amillennialism: Views the millennium symbolically as Christ's present reign through the church.
    • Premillennialism: Believes Christ will return before a literal thousand-year reign on Earth.
    • Postmillennialism: Expects Christ to return after the church's faithful work has gradually transformed the world into a "millennial" period of peace and justice.
  • Eschatology's Present Claim: Regardless of specific timing, all millennial views agree that history is moving towards a good, God-directed destination. This belief claims that every act of love, justice, and faithfulness performed now participates in that future renewal.
  • Apocalyptic Literature as Genre: The Book of Revelation belongs to the apocalyptic genre, characterized by vivid, symbolic imagery used to convey theological truths about the conflict between good and evil and God's ultimate victory, rather than a literal blueprint of future events.

Important Terms

  • Eschatology: (From Greek eschatos "last" and logos "study") The branch of theology concerned with death, judgment, and the final destiny of the soul and the world. In Christianity, it includes beliefs about the resurrection of the dead, the return of Christ, the final judgment, and the renewal of all creation.
  • Resurrection: In Christian theology, the bodily raising of Jesus from death on the third day after his crucifixion. It is understood as the transformation and restoration of the whole person—body and soul—by God's power, and Christian eschatology expects a similar resurrection for all the dead at the end of time. This differs from the survival of a soul (immortality).
  • Millennium: Refers to the "thousand-year reign" mentioned in Revelation 20. Its interpretation (literal vs. symbolic, timing relative to Christ's return) is a key point of divergence among Christian eschatological views.
  • Amillennialism: An eschatological position holding that the thousand-year reign described in Revelation 20 is symbolic of the present age, during which Christ reigns spiritually through the church. It posits that history will conclude with the final judgment.
  • Premillennialism: An eschatological position asserting that Christ will return to earth before the millennium, establishing a literal thousand-year reign of peace and justice, which will then be followed by the final judgment.
  • Postmillennialism: An eschatological position believing that the kingdom of God will grow gradually on Earth through the church's faithful work, leading to a golden age of peace and righteousness (the millennium). Christ is then expected to return after this period, to a world already substantially transformed.
  • Apocalypse: (From Greek for "unveiling" or "revelation") A literary genre common in ancient Judaism and early Christianity that uses vivid, symbolic imagery (e.g., beasts, dragons, celestial battles) to communicate theological truths about the conflict between good and evil and God's ultimate victory.

Detailed Notes

1. The Nature of Christian Eschatology

  • A Paradoxical Hope: Christian eschatology presents a future that is both entirely new and a fulfillment of past promises. It's not an escape from history but its completion and ultimate renewal.
  • Overcoming Suffering: The present world, marked by suffering, injustice, and death, is not the final reality. God's word of renewal is the ultimate conclusion.
  • Grounded in God's Character: This hope is deeply rooted in the unchanging, good character of God.

2. The Foundation: The Resurrection of Jesus

  • Central Belief: The resurrection of Jesus is the absolute core of Christian eschatological hope, not a secondary doctrine. As Paul stated, without it, faith is futile (1 Corinthians 15:17).
  • Evidence of God's Power: Jesus' resurrection serves as the undeniable proof that death does not have the final say and that God possesses the power to reverse even the seemingly irreversible.
  • "First Fruits" of Creation's Renewal: The New Testament describes Jesus' resurrection as the "first fruits" of a coming harvest, signifying the beginning of a renewal that will encompass all of creation. The "groaning" of the present age (Romans 8:22) is seen as birth pangs of this new world, not a sign of God's absence.
  • Hope as Inference: Christian hope is not wishful thinking but an inference: "because this [Jesus' resurrection] has already happened, that [the ultimate renewal] will follow."
  • Bodily Transformation: The Christian understanding of resurrection emphasizes the transformation and restoration of the whole person (body and soul), differing from Greek concepts of the soul's immortality. A similar bodily resurrection is expected for all at the end of time.

3. Diverse Interpretations of the End Times (Millennial Views)

  • Variety and Debate: Christian eschatology is not a monolithic teaching but a field with significant historical variety and ongoing debate. The interpretation of the "thousand-year reign" (millennium) in Revelation 20 is a primary point of divergence.
  • Amillennialism:
    • Meaning of Millennium: Views the "thousand years" as symbolic, representing the entire period between Christ's first and second comings.
    • Christ's Reign: Christ is currently reigning spiritually through the church.
    • End of History: History will conclude with Christ's second coming, the general resurrection, and the final judgment.
  • Premillennialism:
    • Meaning of Millennium: Interprets the "thousand years" as a literal future period.
    • Christ's Return: Christ will return to Earth before this literal millennium to establish his physical reign of peace and justice.
    • End of History: After the literal millennium, there will be a final judgment.
  • Postmillennialism:
    • Meaning of Millennium: Believes the "thousand years" will be a future golden age, though often not literally a thousand years, preceding Christ's return.
    • Christ's Reign: The kingdom of God will gradually expand and flourish on Earth through the faithful work and spiritual influence of the church.
    • Christ's Return: Christ will return after this period of widespread righteousness and peace, to a world that has been largely transformed.
  • Common Ground: Despite their differences in timing and mechanism, these views agree on fundamental principles:
    • History is moving towards a specific, divinely intended destination.
    • God is actively involved in directing history.
    • The ultimate destination of history is good and redemptive.

4. Eschatology's Claim on the Present

  • More Than Prediction: Christian eschatology is not just about predicting the future; it makes a profound claim about the present.
  • Participating in the Future: Because the world has a good ending through God's renewal, every act of love, justice, and faithfulness performed now actively participates in that coming reality, even if its full impact is not immediately visible. This inspires ethical action and hope in the face of current challenges.

5. The Vision of Renewal: Revelation 21:1-5

  • New Creation: John's vision describes a "new heaven and a new earth," where the old has passed away.
  • New Jerusalem: The "holy city" descends from heaven, symbolizing God's perfect dwelling with humanity.
  • God's Presence: A central theme is God dwelling directly with people, being their God, and them being His people.
  • End of Suffering: Crucially, this new reality is free from death, sorrow, crying, and pain—the "former things" have passed away.
  • God's Promise: God declares, "Behold, I make all things new," emphasizing the comprehensive nature of this renewal.

6. Understanding Apocalyptic Literature

  • Genre of Revelation: The Book of Revelation belongs to the "apocalypse" genre (meaning "unveiling" or "revelation").
  • Symbolic Language: Apocalyptic writing frequently employs vivid, symbolic imagery (e.g., beasts, dragons, numbers, celestial battles).
  • Theological Truths: Its primary purpose is to communicate theological truths about the ongoing conflict between good and evil, God's sovereignty, and His ultimate victory.
  • Not a Literal Blueprint: Reading Revelation as a literal, chronological news report for future events misunderstands its genre. It is more akin to "political poetry," using symbolism to convey deeper spiritual and historical meaning.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the paradox at the heart of Christian eschatology regarding the future God promises. How does this differ from the idea of "escaping" history?
  2. Why is the resurrection of Jesus considered the foundational belief for Christian eschatological hope? How does it demonstrate God's power over death and serve as "first fruits" for broader creation?
  3. Describe the three major millennial positions (Amillennialism, Premillennialism, and Postmillennialism). What are their key differences regarding the "thousand-year reign," and what common ground do they share?
  4. The Book of Revelation is described as "apocalyptic literature." What does this mean, and why is it important to understand this genre when interpreting Christian beliefs about the end times?
  5. How does Christian eschatology, with its focus on God's ultimate renewal, influence a believer's actions, perspectives, and sense of purpose in the present world?
