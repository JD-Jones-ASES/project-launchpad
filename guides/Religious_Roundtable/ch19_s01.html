A Study Guide to The Questions That Remain: Comparative Traditions and Topics

This study guide examines the persistent inquiries that define human spiritual, philosophical, and social thought across nine distinct "cycles." It synthesizes the responses offered by various traditions—including Christianity, Judaism, Islam, Hinduism, Buddhism, Taoism, Confucianism, Norse Paganism, and Communism—to the fundamental questions of existence.

Part I: Key Concepts and Cycle Overviews

Cycle One: Faith and Belief

The Question: What do we believe, and how do we know it?

  • Christianity: Faith is centered on trust in a person—the incarnate God who lived and died in human history.
  • Judaism: Faith is a covenant relationship; it is a promise bound across generations rather than just a belief.
  • Islam: Faith begins with the shahada (declaration that there is no god but God) and influences all aspects of life.

Cycle Two: Creation and Origins

The Question: Where did the world come from, and what is it made of?

  • Hinduism: Emerges from a primordial unity called Brahman. The Nasadiya Sukta suggests that the ultimate origin may be a mystery even to the gods.
  • Buddhism: Rejects the search for a first cause. Existence is defined by "dependent origination," making the universe like a circle without an edge.
  • Taoism: Focuses on the "unnamed." The Tao that can be described or spoken is not the eternal, true Tao.

Cycle Three: The Human Person

The Question: What is the nature of a human being?

  • Confucianism: Humans are social creatures defined by relationships and the capacity for ren (benevolence) and li (proper conduct).
  • Norse Paganism: Humans are material creatures, described metaphorically as trees given breath and sense. They are bound by a web of fate woven by the Norns.
  • Communism: Humans are productive beings. Under systems where production is exploitative, humans become "estranged" from their work, others, and themselves.

Cycle Four: Sacred Texts

The Question: How is ultimate truth communicated?

  • Judaism: Truth is found in a living tradition of interpretation; the Torah serves as the "seed" and the Talmud as the "growth."
  • Hinduism: Utilizes a layered archive consisting of shruti (revelation that is "heard," like the Vedas) and smriti (tradition that is "remembered," like the epics).
  • Norse Paganism: Presents a paradox where texts were preserved by those who no longer believed them. Wisdom is tied to sacrifice, as seen in the god of wisdom's suffering on Yggdrasil to receive the runes.

Cycle Five: Morality and Ethics

The Question: How should we live, and what is the source of obligation?

  • Christianity: Ethics are grounded in the double commandment to love God and neighbor.
  • Taoism: Advocates for wu wei, or non-forcing, acting in harmony with the natural "grain" of things.
  • Communism: Morality is inseparable from material and economic conditions; personal justice cannot exist within a structurally unjust economy.

Cycle Six: Community and Worship

The Question: How do we practice together?

  • Islam: The ummah is a community defined by shared submission to God, unified by daily prayer facing a single direction.
  • Buddhism: The Sangha (community of practitioners) is one of the "Three Jewels."
  • Confucianism: Li (ritual) is the architecture of community, making trust and order visible.

Cycle Seven: Suffering

The Question: Why do we suffer, and can we be freed?

  • Buddhism: Suffering is rooted in tanha (craving or relentless wanting).
  • Norse Paganism: Suffering is an inevitable condition. Liberation is not promised, but courage is emphasized. As the Hávamál suggests, while life and kin die, the "fame of a dead man's deeds" lives on.
  • Communism: Suffering is structural, the result of a system built on exploitation.

Cycle Eight: Transcendence and Immanence

The Question: Is the divine beyond us or within us?

  • Islam: Primarily emphasizes the absolute transcendence of Allah, though the Sufi tradition explores intimate closeness.
  • Hinduism: Holds both simultaneously through Brahman nirguna (featureless/beyond) and Brahman saguna (accessible/with qualities).
  • Taoism: The Tao is a paradox, described as being everywhere and nowhere at once.

Cycle Nine: History and Hope

The Question: Does time have a direction?

  • Christianity: History is linear, moving toward a Kingdom, judgment, and consummation.
  • Judaism: Hope is messianic but focused on this world through tikkun olam (repairing the world).
  • Confucianism: Hope is found in the civilizational ideal of the junzi (exemplary person) through self-discipline.

Part II: Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does the Jewish conception of faith differ from a simple "belief in God"?
  2. What is the Buddhist term for the root cause of suffering, and what does it mean?
  3. In Hinduism, what is the difference between shruti and smriti?
  4. How does Norse Paganism describe the origin of human beings?
  5. According to the Confucian tradition, what are the two qualities humans are capable of at their best?
  6. What does the term wu wei represent in Taoist ethics?
  7. How does Communism explain the cause of human "estrangement"?
  8. Identify the "Three Jewels" mentioned in the Buddhist tradition of community.
  9. What is the significance of the Hávamál in the context of Norse suffering?
  10. Define Brahman saguna and Brahman nirguna.
  11. What is the Islamic ummah, and how is it defined?
  12. What is the goal of tikkun olam in Jewish history and hope?

Part III: Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Nature of Suffering: Compare and contrast the Buddhist diagnosis of suffering as interior (tanha) with the Communist diagnosis of suffering as structural. How does the Norse Pagan view of suffering as an inescapable condition offer a third alternative?
  2. Ritual vs. Relation: Analyze the role of li in Confucianism as the "architecture of community." Compare this to the Islamic ummah and the Buddhist Sangha. Does ritual accomplish something that words cannot?
  3. The Shape of Time: Discuss the different views of history presented in the text. Contrast the Christian view of linear consummation with the Confucian civilizational ideal and the Jewish focus on earthly repair.
  4. The Paradox of the Divine: Explore the tension between transcendence and immanence. Use the examples of the Tao, the Islamic Sufi tradition, and the Hindu concepts of Brahman to argue whether the divine can be both "beyond" and "here."

Part IV: Glossary of Important Terms

Term Tradition Definition
Brahman Hinduism The primordial unity from which all differentiation flows.
Brahman nirguna Hinduism The divine as featureless and beyond all description.
Brahman saguna Hinduism The divine as full of qualities and accessible (e.g., as Krishna).
Dependent Origination Buddhism The concept that the cosmos arises without a first cause; a circle with no edge.
Junzi Confucianism The "exemplary person" who serves as a model for human society.
Li Confucianism Proper conduct or ritual; the architecture that makes trust and order visible.
Nasadiya Sukta Hinduism An ancient text acknowledging that even the gods may not know the world's origin.
Norns Norse Paganism Beings who weave the web of fate before a person's birth.
Ren Confucianism Benevolence; one of the core capacities of the human person.
Sangha Buddhism The community of practitioners; one of the Three Jewels.
Shruti Hinduism "Heard" revelation, such as the Vedas.
Smriti Hinduism "Remembered" tradition, such as the epics and the Gita.
Tanha Buddhism Craving or relentless wanting; the root of suffering.
Tikkun Olam Judaism The "repair of the world"; a central aspect of messianic hope.
Ummah Islam A community defined by shared submission to God rather than ethnicity or nation.
Wu wei Taoism The principle of "non-forcing" or acting in harmony with the natural grain of things.
Yggdrasil Norse Paganism The world tree where the god of wisdom suffered for nine days to receive the runes.
