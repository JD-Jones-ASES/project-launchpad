Christianity: Law, Grace, and Virtue Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the foundations of Christian ethics, revealing it not as a mere set of rules, but as a way of life centered on unconditional love. It delves into Jesus's core teachings, particularly the radical moral vision presented in the Sermon on the Mount and the Beatitudes, and explains the crucial distinction between God's declaration of righteousness (justification) and the believer's ongoing growth in holiness (sanctification).

Key Concepts

  • Love as the Foundation of Christian Ethics: The entire Christian moral framework is built upon two great commandments: love God completely and love your neighbor as yourself. All other laws and moral obligations flow from this double source.
  • Agape Love: This is the distinctive, unconditional, self-giving love central to Christian ethics. It represents a complete moral program that prioritizes the well-being of others, even enemies.
  • The Sermon on the Mount: Considered the most concentrated summary of Christian moral vision, Jesus's teachings in Matthew 5-7 are challenging and push beyond conventional wisdom, advocating for virtues like meekness, mercy, and peacemaking.
  • The Beatitudes: The opening declarations of the Sermon on the Mount, they describe the character and conditions of those who are "blessed" (or "fully alive") in the Kingdom of God, often in ways that contradict worldly values. They describe a state of being, not just a goal.
  • The "Already and Not Yet" of the Kingdom of God: This concept highlights that the Kingdom of God has begun with Jesus's arrival but has not yet fully arrived. Christian ethics operates within this tension, demanding a radical ethic that reflects a future reality already breaking into the present.
  • Justification vs. Sanctification: A critical theological distinction, especially in Protestant traditions. Justification is God's act of declaring a believer righteous through faith, independent of moral works. Sanctification is the ongoing process of moral and spiritual growth, where a Christian becomes more holy and Christ-like through the Holy Spirit's work.

Important Terms

  • Agape (ah-GAH-pay): The Greek word for the unconditional, self-giving love that is the cornerstone of Christian ethics. It is distinct from friendship love (philia) or romantic love (eros).
  • Beatitudes (bee-AT-ih-tyoodz): The opening declarations of Jesus's Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5:3–12), each beginning "Blessed are..." They describe the character and condition of those who belong to the Kingdom of God. The Greek word makarios, translated as "blessed," means closer to "fully alive" or "living well in the deepest sense."
  • Justification: The theological concept, particularly important in Protestantism, referring to God declaring a person righteous in His sight through faith in Christ, not through their own moral achievements or works.
  • Sanctification: The process by which a Christian grows in holiness after conversion—the ongoing work of the Holy Spirit to conform the believer to the character of Christ.
  • Sermon on the Mount: Jesus's extended ethical teaching recorded in Matthew 5–7, widely considered the most concentrated summary of Christian moral vision in the entire New Testament.
  • "Already and Not Yet": A theological concept describing the Kingdom of God, which has already begun with Jesus's ministry and resurrection, but has not yet fully arrived or been consummated. This tension informs much of Christian ethical living.

Detailed Notes

The Foundation of Christian Ethics: Love, Not Rules

Christian ethics is fundamentally rooted in a call to love, not merely in adherence to a set of regulations. Jesus summarized all law and prophetic teaching into two "great commandments":

  1. Love God with all your heart, soul, and mind.
  2. Love your neighbor as yourself. These two commandments serve as the source from which all other moral obligations, including the Ten Commandments and calls for justice, flow. The Christian moral life is therefore less about strict compliance with a code and more about the ongoing transformation into a person capable of genuine, self-giving love.

Agape: The Core of Christian Love

The Greek word agape signifies the unconditional, self-giving love that lies at the heart of Christian ethics. This form of love is distinct from philia (friendship love) or eros (romantic love). Agape appears over 250 times in the Greek New Testament, demonstrating its centrality. The Apostle Paul's famous description of love in 1 Corinthians 13 ("Love is patient, love is kind. It does not envy, it does not boast, it is not proud, it keeps no record of wrongs.") is not a sentimental idea but a comprehensive moral program that guides Christian conduct.

The Radical Ethics of the Sermon on the Mount

The Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5–7) is Jesus's most concentrated ethical teaching, presenting a moral vision that is profoundly counter-cultural and "refuses to be merely reasonable." Commands like "Blessed are the meek," "Love your enemies," "Give to anyone who asks," and "Do not worry about tomorrow" push beyond conventional human prudence. These teachings reflect the ethics of the "Kingdom of God," which operates under a unique principle: it has "already begun but has not yet fully arrived." This "already and not yet" dynamic means Christians are called to live by the standards of a future, perfected Kingdom even in the present, imperfect world.

The Beatitudes: A Description of True Flourishing

The Beatitudes (Matthew 5:3–12) open the Sermon on the Mount and offer a striking redefinition of what it means to be "blessed" or "fully alive" (makarios). Unlike the world's values, which often celebrate strength, self-sufficiency, and power, Jesus proclaims blessing upon those who are:

  • Poor in spirit: Recognizing their spiritual need and dependence on God.
  • Those who mourn: Experiencing sorrow and compassion.
  • The meek: Gentle, humble, and not seeking power over others.
  • Those who hunger and thirst for righteousness: Desiring justice and moral goodness above all else.
  • The merciful: Showing compassion and forgiveness.
  • The pure in heart: Possessing integrity and single-minded devotion to God.
  • The peacemakers: Working to reconcile others and foster harmony.
  • Those who are persecuted for righteousness' sake: Enduring suffering for their commitment to God's ways.

The Beatitudes do not command people to try to be these things; rather, they declare that those who are these things are already blessed. They describe a different kind of life and a different path to true flourishing, turning ordinary moral calculations upside down.

Justification and Sanctification: God's Work and Our Growth

Understanding the distinction between justification and sanctification is crucial, especially in the Protestant tradition.

  • Justification: This refers to what God does. Through faith in Christ, a believer is declared righteous in God's eyes. This righteousness is not earned through good works but is a gift of grace ("not by works, lest anyone should boast," Ephesians 2:9). It addresses a person's legal standing before God.
  • Sanctification: This is the ongoing process that follows justification. Once a person is declared righteous, they are called to grow in holiness. Sanctification is the Holy Spirit's continuous work in a Christian's life, conforming their character and conduct to that of Christ. This means that while a good life is not earned by following rules, it naturally grows from a healthy, justified spiritual state, much like fruit grows from a healthy tree. Morality, in this sense, is the natural outflow of a transformed heart.

Review Questions

  1. According to the text, what are the two foundational commandments upon which all Christian ethics "hang," and how do they redefine the nature of Christian moral obligation?
  2. Explain the concept of agape love. How is it described as a "complete moral program," and why is it distinguished from other forms of love?
  3. Discuss why Jesus's Sermon on the Mount is considered "radical" and "refuses to be merely reasonable." Provide examples from the text to support your answer.
  4. What does it mean to say that the Beatitudes "turn ordinary moral calculations upside down"? How do they describe "blessedness" (makarios) differently from typical worldly views?
  5. Differentiate clearly between justification and sanctification. Why is understanding this distinction essential for grasping the Christian perspective on moral growth and the pursuit of a "good life"?
