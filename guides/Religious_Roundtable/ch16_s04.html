Comparing Suffering: Buddhism, Norse Paganism, and Communism

Overview

This section provides a comparative analysis of three distinct perspectives on human suffering: Buddhism, Norse Paganism, and Communism. It highlights their differing diagnoses of suffering's root cause—whether it originates within the individual, in the cosmic order, or in societal structures—and consequently, their proposed pathways to liberation or dignified existence. The section emphasizes that the answer to this fundamental question profoundly shapes one's approach to alleviating suffering.

Key Concepts

  • Buddhism: Internal Root of Suffering
    • Locates the origin of suffering within the individual's mind, specifically in "craving, grasping, and clinging" to impermanent things.
    • The prescription is an inward transformation through practices like the Eightfold Path, leading to individual nirvana (liberation).
  • Norse Paganism: Cosmic Root of Suffering
    • Identifies suffering as inherent in the very structure of the cosmos, a matter of predetermined wyrd (fate) that includes the death of gods (Ragnarök) and universal mortality.
    • There is no "liberation" in the Buddhist sense; instead, the counsel is to face inevitable suffering with courage and dignity, living a life worth remembering.
  • Communism (Marxist View): Structural Root of Suffering
    • Asserts that suffering is primarily a product of economic arrangements and social structures, particularly the exploitation of labor and resulting alienation.
    • Critiques spiritual or inward approaches as "opium of the people," distractions from real-world problems.
    • The prescription is structural change through revolution, abolishing private ownership of the means of production to create an equitable society.
  • The Core Disagreement: The central question posed is whether the primary source of human suffering is inside us, outside us (social structures), or built into the fabric of reality itself.
  • Implications of Diagnosis: Your answer to the root cause of suffering directly dictates what you believe must be done to address it, whether it's changing the mind, the economic system, or one's attitude towards fate.
  • Mutual Exclusivity: The text explores whether these perspectives are necessarily at odds, suggesting that elements of each might coexist or complement one another in a broader understanding of suffering.

Important Terms

  • Craving, Grasping, Clinging (Buddhism): The internal psychological processes, rooted in attachment and desire, that Buddhism identifies as the primary cause of suffering.
  • Eightfold Path (Buddhism): The systematic pathway of ethical conduct, mental discipline, and wisdom prescribed in Buddhism for achieving liberation from suffering (nirvana).
  • Nirvana (Buddhism): The ultimate goal of Buddhist practice; a state of liberation from suffering, desire, and the cycle of rebirth, characterized by profound peace.
  • Wyrd (Norse Paganism): A concept of fate or destiny in Norse tradition, understood as a powerful, immutable force woven into the fabric of the cosmos before birth, affecting all beings, including gods.
  • Ragnarök (Norse Paganism): The prophesied end of the world in Norse mythology, involving the death of many gods, natural disasters, and the submergence of the world in water, followed by its re-emergence. Illustrates the cosmic impermanence.
  • Opium of the People (Marxism): A phrase coined by Karl Marx to describe religion's function as a comfort or anesthetic that dulls the pain of suffering caused by economic exploitation and social injustice, preventing people from addressing the true causes of their misery.
  • Alienation (Marxism): A state of estrangement or detachment experienced by individuals, particularly workers under capitalism, from their labor, the products of their labor, their fellow humans, and their own species-being. A key component of suffering in the Marxist view.
  • Means of Production (Marxism): The non-human physical inputs used in the production of economic value, such as factories, machinery, tools, and land. Their private ownership is seen as the root of exploitation and suffering in Communism.

Detailed Notes

The Great Divide: Where Does Suffering Come From?

This section serves as a crucial comparative analysis, laying out three vastly different frameworks for understanding the origin of human suffering and, consequently, what ought to be done about it. The stark contrasts between Buddhism, Norse Paganism, and Communism provide a rich tapestry for examining core philosophical and practical questions about life's challenges.

Buddhism: The Inward Gaze

  • Root Cause: For Buddhism, suffering is not externally imposed but internally generated. It stems from the mind's inherent tendency to crave, grasp, and cling to things, mistakenly believing that impermanent experiences or possessions can offer lasting satisfaction. This attachment to the fleeting nature of existence is the fundamental error.
  • Prescription: The solution is therefore equally internal: a transformation of the self. The Eightfold Path provides a comprehensive guide for ethical conduct, mental discipline, and wisdom, systematically reshaping how an individual thinks, speaks, acts, and perceives reality.
  • Goal: The ultimate aim is nirvana, a state of liberation achieved through sustained individual practice, free from the cycle of suffering and rebirth.
  • Social Context: While the social environment is acknowledged, it is not considered the primary source of suffering. Personal spiritual development is paramount.

Norse Paganism: The Cosmic Reality

  • Root Cause: In contrast to Buddhism, Norse Paganism posits that suffering is woven into the very fabric of the cosmos. It's not a human failing but an inherent aspect of existence, dictated by wyrd (fate) that is predetermined even before birth. The inevitable demise of the gods at Ragnarök and the certainty of death for all beings underscore this cosmic impermanence and the futility of escaping it.
  • Prescription: There is no "path to liberation" from this cosmic reality. Instead, the Norse tradition offers a counsel of dignity and courage. The focus shifts from altering reality to altering one's response to it. One must face their wyrd with bravery, live a life filled with deeds worthy of remembrance, and accept that the world does not owe anyone an escape from its inherent harshness.
  • Outcome: This acceptance, though potentially uncomfortable, is presented as a form of freedom. By not resisting the unchangeable nature of reality, energy that would be spent in resentment can be redirected towards living a meaningful, courageous life.

Communism (Marxist Perspective): The Structural Imperative

  • Root Cause: Communism diametrically opposes both inward and cosmic explanations. It asserts that suffering is not internal or fated, but entirely structural, a direct product of specific economic arrangements. Specifically, the exploitation of workers under capitalism, where their labor is alienated and undervalued, is identified as the core problem.
  • Critique of Other Views: From a Marxist standpoint, telling a suffering worker to meditate or be courageous in the face of fate is a "cruel distraction." Such spiritual comforts are seen as "opium of the people," numbing the pain without addressing the underlying disease of economic injustice.
  • Prescription: The solution must therefore be structural as well: revolution. This entails the abolition of private ownership of the means of production and the creation of a classless society where exploitation is impossible, thereby eliminating the conditions that produce alienation and suffering.

The Deepest Disagreement: Inside, Outside, or Inherent?

The core of this comparative exercise boils down to a fundamental philosophical question: Is the primary source of human suffering inside us (psychological/spiritual), outside us (social/economic), or built into the structure of reality itself (cosmic/fated)?

The text emphasizes that this is not a mere academic debate. The answer one adopts directly dictates the proposed course of action to alleviate suffering:

  • If internal craving is the root, transforming the mind is paramount.
  • If economic exploitation is the root, changing the economic system is essential.
  • If suffering is intrinsic to the cosmos, then living with honesty, courage, and acceptance becomes the most important response.

Are These Diagnoses Mutually Exclusive?

Finally, the section thoughtfully explores the possibility that these perspectives might not be entirely contradictory but could, in some ways, coexist or offer complementary insights.

  • A Buddhist could acknowledge that unjust economic systems make cultivating the Eightfold Path significantly harder. Material hardship can be a barrier to spiritual practice.
  • A Marxist, even in a perfectly just economic system, would likely concede that personal grief, loss, or the fear of death would persist, as these are not solely products of economic structures.
  • A follower of Norse tradition might argue that courage doesn't demand passivity. One can accept ultimate fate while still engaging in both inward cultivation and outward resistance to injustice. Accepting one's wyrd doesn't mean passively accepting all forms of human-caused harm.

This nuanced understanding suggests that a truly comprehensive approach to suffering might draw insights from all three frameworks, recognizing the multifaceted nature of human experience.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast the primary root causes of suffering identified by Buddhism, Norse Paganism, and Communism. How does each tradition fundamentally differ in its diagnosis?
  2. For each tradition discussed, explain the specific "prescription" or solution offered to address suffering. How do these proposed solutions directly align with their respective diagnoses?
  3. Discuss Karl Marx's concept of "opium of the people" in the context of this comparison. What is his central critique of approaches that focus on internal or cosmic sources of suffering, and why does he advocate for structural change instead?
  4. The text poses the central question: "Is the deepest source of human suffering inside us, outside us, or simply built into the nature of reality?" Reflect on this question and explain how your personal answer would determine what you believe is the most important thing to do to alleviate suffering.
  5. The section suggests that these diagnoses might not be mutually exclusive. Provide specific examples of how elements from two or more of these traditions could potentially coexist or complement each other in a holistic understanding and response to suffering.
