Study Guide: Islamic Core Beliefs and the Five Pillars

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the foundational theological principles and practical requirements of Islam as outlined in the provided text. It covers the central concept of monotheism, the history of revelation, the articles of faith, and the Five Pillars.

I. Core Theological Principles

Tawhid (The Oneness of God)

The fundamental concept of Islam is tawhid, the absolute unity and oneness of God. This principle holds that God is unique, undivided, and without any rivals or partners.

  • Negation as Affirmation: Tawhid is a sweeping negation of anything that is not God. It rejects all idols, ideologies, rulers, or desires that claim ultimate loyalty.
  • Liberation Theology: By asserting that only God has absolute claim over a human being, tawhid functions as a liberation theology, freeing individuals from the absolute authority of any earthly power.
  • Shirk: The Arabic term for associating partners with God is shirk. Described as the only unforgivable offense in the Quran, it represents the ultimate rejection of tawhid.

Prophethood and Revelation

Islamic theology recognizes a long line of prophets chosen to deliver divine guidance.

  • Prophetic Lineage: The line begins with Adam and includes Noah, Abraham, Moses, and Jesus.
  • The Seal of the Prophets: Muhammad is recognized as the Khatam al-Anbiya', or the Seal of the Prophets. He is the final messenger whose revelation, the Quran, is considered the incorruptible word of God.
  • Correction of Previous Texts: While Islam honors the Hebrew Bible and the Gospels in principle, it teaches that these previous revelations were corrupted or distorted by human error over time. The Quran is viewed as the final authority that supersedes and corrects these earlier texts.

Arkan al-Iman (Articles of Faith)

Islamic belief is structured around six core articles of faith:

  1. Belief in one God.
  2. Belief in the angels.
  3. Belief in the revealed scriptures.
  4. Belief in the prophets.
  5. Belief in the Day of Judgment (Yawm al-Qiyama): A central motivator for ethical behavior, where every human will be resurrected and held accountable for their actions.
  6. Belief in divine providence (qadar): The recognition of God’s will and decree.

II. The Five Pillars (Arkan al-Islam)

The Five Pillars are the non-negotiable acts of worship that serve as the practical expression of Islamic faith.

Pillar Description Purpose and Significance
Shahada (Declaration) The witness that "there is no god but God, and Muhammad is the messenger of God." Making this declaration sincerely is what makes an individual a Muslim; it marks the beginning and end of life.
Salat (Prayer) Five daily prayers performed at specific times (dawn, midday, afternoon, sunset, and night) facing the Ka'ba in Mecca. A structured sequence of standing, bowing, and prostration that sanctifies the day.
Zakat (Almsgiving) An annual requirement to give 2.5% of savings to those in need, provided wealth meets a minimum threshold. Not viewed as optional charity, but as a mandatory purification of wealth and a religious obligation.
Sawm (Fasting) Total abstinence from food, drink, and physical pleasures from dawn to sunset during the month of Ramadan. A physical discipline that fosters gratitude and solidarity with the hungry; commemorates the first Quranic revelation.
Hajj (Pilgrimage) A journey to Mecca required once in a lifetime for those physically and financially able. A powerful experience of global Muslim unity and equality; rituals include circling the Ka'ba and praying at the plain of Arafat.

III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Define tawhid and explain its significance in Islamic theology.
  2. What is the specific Arabic term for idolatry or associating partners with God, and how is it viewed in the Quran?
  3. Why is Muhammad referred to as the "Seal of the Prophets"?
  4. How does the Islamic view of the Hebrew Bible and the Gospels differ from its view of the Quran?
  5. What are the three dimensions of Iman (faith)?
  6. Describe the financial requirement and the underlying purpose of Zakat.
  7. What does the Sawm (fasting) during Ramadan commemorate?
  8. Explain the significance of the clothing worn during the Hajj.

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Concept of Liberation: Analyze the argument that tawhid is a "liberation theology." How does the absolute rejection of earthly authority in favor of divine authority change an individual's relationship with modern power structures, ideologies, and material desires?
  2. The Interconnection of Belief and Practice: Explore the relationship between Iman (faith) and the Five Pillars. How do the physical acts of the Pillars (such as Salat and Sawm) reflect the internal dimensions of faith? Is it possible, within Islamic theology, to have one without the other?
  3. Accountability and Ethics: Discuss the role of Yawm al-Qiyama (the Day of Judgment) in Islamic life. How does the belief in resurrection and divine accountability serve as a "motivating force for every ethical choice"? Compare this to secular motivations for ethical behavior.

V. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Arkan al-iman: The articles of faith; the basic content of Islamic belief.
  • Hajj: The annual pilgrimage to Mecca; one of the Five Pillars.
  • Iman: Faith; specifically the three-fold orientation of belief in the heart, affirmation by the tongue, and enactment through deeds.
  • Khatam al-Anbiya': "Seal of the Prophets"; a title for Muhammad as the final messenger of God.
  • Prophets: Messengers chosen by God to deliver guidance; includes figures like Adam, Abraham, Moses, and Jesus.
  • Qadar: Divine providence; the decree or will of God.
  • Salat: Ritual prayer performed five times daily; one of the Five Pillars.
  • Sawm: Fasting during the month of Ramadan; one of the Five Pillars.
  • Shahada: The declaration of faith; the first of the Five Pillars.
  • Shirk: The act of associating partners with God; idolatry.
  • Tawhid: The absolute oneness and unity of God.
  • Yawm al-Qiyama: The Day of Judgment; the day of resurrection and accountability.
  • Zakat: Mandatory almsgiving (2.5% of wealth); one of the Five Pillars.
