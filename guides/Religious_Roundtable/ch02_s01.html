Study Guide: Core Beliefs of Judaism

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the foundational theological and ethical principles of Judaism as outlined in "Core Beliefs: One God, One People, One Law." It explores the nature of God, the concept of the covenant, the importance of interpretation, and the mandate for social repair.

Key Conceptual Overview

The Nature of God and Monotheism

The central pillar of Judaism is absolute monotheism, encapsulated in the Shema: "Hear, O Israel, the Lord is our God, the Lord is One." This declaration signifies more than a numerical value; it asserts that God is the sole creator, the undivided source of reality, and the ultimate ground of morality. Unlike other ancient traditions, Judaism recognizes no rival divine powers or equal forces of evil.

While God is viewed as the "distant architect" of the universe, Jewish tradition simultaneously defines God as intensely personal. God is depicted as an entity that speaks, hears prayer, and experiences emotions such as grief over suffering or anger at injustice. However, there is a strict prohibition against portraying God in human terms or creating physical images (idolatry), as such acts are seen as attempts to reduce the divine to something manageable or controllable.

The Covenant (Brit) and "Chosenness"

The relationship between God and the Jewish people is defined by the Brit, or covenant. This is a mutual, binding relationship involving genuine obligations:

  • God’s Commitment: To care for, guide, and redeem Israel.
  • Israel’s Commitment: To follow commandments, practice justice, and serve as a "holy nation."

The concept of being the "chosen people" is frequently misunderstood. Within Jewish theology, this is not a claim of superiority but a responsibility. To be chosen is to be called to a specific way of life and to model holiness and justice for the rest of the world.

The Sacredness of Inquiry

A distinctive feature of Judaism is its "intense interpretive energy." The Torah is not viewed as a static text but as an inexhaustibly rich source that must be debated and re-examined. This tradition of questioning is an expression of faith. The Talmud exemplifies this by recording centuries of scholarly debates where multiple, even conflicting, views are preserved as valid. In this context, the conversation itself is considered sacred.

Tikkun Olam: Repairing the World

The ethical expression of Jewish faith is found in Tikkun Olam, or the "repair of the world." This concept posits that:

  1. The world is currently broken or incomplete.
  2. Human beings are partners with God in the work of restoration.
  3. Acts of justice, compassion, and generosity are the tools of this repair.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. What is the Shema and what is its significance in a Jewish person's life?

  • Answer: The Shema is a six-word Hebrew declaration ("Hear, O Israel, the Lord is our God, the Lord is One"). It is the first prayer taught to children and often the last words spoken before death, serving as the core declaration of Jewish monotheism.

2. How does Jewish theology view the use of images or statues of God?

  • Answer: It strictly forbids them. This prohibition against idolatry is meant to prevent humans from reducing God to something manageable or controllable.

3. Define the "Brit" and the roles of the participants involved.

  • Answer: The Brit is a covenant or binding relationship. God, the "senior partner," commits to protecting and guiding Israel, while Israel commits to faithfulness, obedience to commandments, and the practice of justice.

4. What does the term "chosenness" signify in a theological context?

  • Answer: It signifies a responsibility and a calling to a specific way of life, rather than a claim of racial or spiritual superiority.

5. How does the Talmud handle unresolved scholarly debates?

  • Answer: The Talmud records and preserves the debates even when no resolution is reached, treating different positions as valid and viewing the ongoing engagement with the questions as sacred.

6. Provide three examples of specific Jewish laws that manifest the pursuit of justice.

  • Answer: 1) Leaving a corner of one's field for the poor; 2) The prohibition against charging interest to the vulnerable; 3) The obligation to welcome the stranger.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Paradox of the Divine: Analyze the tension in Jewish theology between God as an "intense personal" entity who hears prayer and God as an "utterly unlike anything human" entity that resists all reduction. How does the prohibition of idolatry serve to maintain this tension?
  2. Responsibility vs. Privilege: Critique the concept of the "Chosen People." Based on the source text, argue why viewing this status as a "responsibility" rather than a "privilege" changes the ethical requirements placed upon the community.
  3. Humanity as a Divine Partner: Explore the implications of Tikkun Olam. If humans are considered "God's partners in completing and healing creation," what does this suggest about the Jewish view of human agency and the nature of the world?
  4. The Sanctity of Debate: Discuss the role of interpretation in Judaism. Why might a religious tradition prioritize "ongoing engagement with questions" over "fixed answers," and how does this shape the community's relationship with its sacred texts?

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Brit The Hebrew word for "covenant"; a binding, mutual relationship between God and the Jewish people.
Idolatry The practice of making images of God or reducing the divine to something manageable; strictly forbidden in Judaism.
Monotheism The belief in exactly one God who is the creator and sustainer of all reality, with no rival divine powers.
Shema The central declaration of Jewish faith: "Hear, O Israel, the Lord is our God, the Lord is One."
Talmud A central text of Rabbinic Judaism that preserves centuries of interpretive debates and scholarly discussions.
Tikkun Olam Hebrew for "repair of the world"; the mandate to pursue justice and participate in restoring a broken creation.
Torah The primary sacred text of Judaism, which is continually studied, questioned, and re-examined.
