Study Guide: Core Beliefs of Judaism

This study guide examines the fundamental tenets of Jewish faith as outlined in the core principles of monotheism, the covenantal relationship between God and the people, and the ethical imperative to repair the world.

Key Concepts and Theological Foundations

The Shema and Absolute Monotheism

The central declaration of the Jewish faith is the Shema: Shema Yisrael, Adonai Eloheinu, Adonai Echad ("Hear, O Israel, the Lord is our God, the Lord is One"). This statement establishes a rigorous monotheism.

  • Numerical and Philosophical Unity: Monotheism in Judaism is not merely the belief in one chief god, but the assertion that there is exactly one God who is the sole creator and sustainer of reality.
  • The Rejection of Duality: Jewish theology posits that God is undivided and uncomplicated. There are no rival divine powers or forces of evil equal to the force of good; one God serves as the ultimate source of both reality and morality.

The Nature of the Divine

The Jewish conception of God balances two seemingly contradictory ideas:

  • A Personal God: God is viewed as intensely personal—capable of speaking, hearing prayer, grieving for human suffering, and expressing anger at injustice.
  • An Ineffable God: Conversely, God is utterly unlike anything human. Jewish tradition is cautious about using human terms to describe the divine and strictly forbids the creation of images of God (idolatry). This prohibition is intended to prevent humans from reducing the divine to something manageable or controllable.

Brit: The Covenantal Relationship

The relationship between God and the Jewish people is defined by the Brit, or covenant.

  • Mutual Obligations: While not a contract between equals, the covenant involves genuine obligations for both parties. God commits to guiding, caring for, and redeeming Israel. In return, Israel commits to faithfulness, practicing justice, and following the commandments.
  • The Concept of the "Chosen People": Rather than a claim of racial or spiritual superiority, "chosenness" is defined as a responsibility. It is a calling to a specific way of life and a mandate to model holiness and justice for the rest of the world.

The Sacredness of Inquiry

A distinctive feature of Judaism is its "interpretive energy." The Torah is viewed as a document of inexhaustible richness, meaning no single reading is ever final.

  • The Role of Debate: In Jewish tradition, the act of questioning and debating is considered an expression of faith.
  • The Talmud: This tradition of inquiry is preserved in the Talmud, which records centuries of scholarly debates. Notably, the Talmud often preserves conflicting arguments without resolution, treating the conversation itself as sacred.

Tikkun Olam: Ethical Action

The ethical expression of Jewish faith is found in Tikkun Olam, the "repair of the world."

  • Human Partnership: Human beings are viewed as partners with God in the ongoing work of restoring a broken creation.
  • Justice as Divine Order: Poverty, cruelty, and oppression are viewed as violations of the divine order.
  • Legal Manifestations: The obligation to repair the world is codified in Jewish law through specific commandments, such as leaving corners of fields for the poor, prohibiting interest on loans to the vulnerable, and the requirement to welcome strangers.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. What is the significance of the Shema in the life of a Jewish person? Answer: It is the first prayer learned in childhood and often the last words spoken before death. It serves as the definitive declaration of God’s absolute unity and status as the sole creator.

2. How does Jewish theology define the "prohibition on idolatry" beyond the rejection of statues? Answer: It is a rejection of the human tendency to reduce God to something manageable or controllable. It emphasizes that God resists all human attempts at reduction or representation.

3. Explain the "senior partner" dynamic in the concept of Brit. Answer: The covenant is not a contract between equals; God is the senior partner who offers protection and redemption, while the Jewish people occupy the role of the partner who offers obedience and faithfulness.

4. According to Jewish theologians, what is the true meaning of being a "chosen people"? Answer: It is a responsibility and a calling to a specific way of life, requiring the people to model justice and holiness for the world. It is not a claim of superiority.

5. Why are unresolved debates recorded in the Talmud? Answer: Because Judaism treats the ongoing engagement with questions and the conversation itself as sacred. Both sides of a fierce debate can be recorded as valid expressions of faith.

6. Provide three examples of how Tikkun Olam is expressed through specific Jewish laws. Answer: (1) Leaving a corner of one's field for the poor; (2) The prohibition against charging interest to the vulnerable; (3) The obligation to welcome the stranger.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Tension of the Divine: Analyze the tension between the "intensely personal" God who hears prayers and the "utterly unlike" God who resists human description. How does the prohibition of idolatry serve to maintain this balance in Jewish theology?
  2. The Sanctity of Argument: Discuss the role of interpretation and debate in Judaism. How does the belief that "no single reading can exhaust" the Torah influence the way Jewish thinkers approach the Talmud and the concept of sacred conversation?
  3. Restoration through Action: Explore the concept of Tikkun Olam. How does the idea of humans being "partners in completing and healing creation" change the relationship between the religious practitioner and social issues like poverty and injustice?

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Brit The Hebrew word for "covenant"; a binding, mutual relationship between God and the Jewish people involving genuine obligations on both sides.
Monotheism The belief in exactly one God who is the sole creator and sustainer of all reality, with no rival divine powers.
Shema The central declaration of Jewish faith: "Hear, O Israel, the Lord is our God, the Lord is One."
Talmud A central text of Jewish tradition that preserves centuries of interpretive debates and scholarly questions regarding the Torah.
Tikkun Olam Hebrew for "repair of the world"; the vocation to pursue justice and heal suffering as partners in restoring a broken creation.
Torah The foundational sacred text of Judaism, which is studied, debated, and re-examined in every generation.
