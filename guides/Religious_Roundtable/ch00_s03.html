Thinking Religiously: A Study Guide

This study guide explores the intellectual framework of "thinking religiously" as presented in the introductory material of Chapter 0, Section 3. It provides a structured overview of key concepts, philosophical examples, and critical terminology necessary for understanding religious traditions as serious objects of study.

I. Key Concepts: The Nature of Religious Thinking

Thinking religiously is defined not as an act of devotion or faith, but as a specific intellectual orientation. It involves taking "big questions" seriously and approaching them with a particular mindset.

Core Principles of Religious Thinking

  • Engagement with the "Big Questions": Addressing fundamental inquiries that cannot be resolved through laboratory experiments or mathematical calculations.
  • Genuine Curiosity: Approaching unfamiliar traditions and difficult questions with an open desire to understand.
  • Intellectual Persistence: A willingness to "sit with difficulty and uncertainty" rather than settling for easy or superficial answers.
  • Objective Understanding: The goal is not to determine which tradition is "correct," but to understand:
    • What each tradition is saying.
    • Why that message might be compelling to followers.
    • What those beliefs imply about how one should live.

II. Philosophical and Secular Parallels

The text argues that the structure of religious thought is so pervasive that it influences even secular philosophers and political theorists.

Immanuel Kant

The philosopher Immanuel Kant identified three central questions that define the human condition. While asked by a philosopher, these are religious in their fundamental structure:

  1. What can I know?
  2. What should I do?
  3. What may I hope for?

Albert Camus

The playwright and novelist Albert Camus explored the concept of the absurd. This is defined as the painful gap between the human longing for inherent meaning and a universe that appears to offer none. This "secular" inquiry is categorized as a religious question because of its focus on ultimate meaning.

Karl Marx

Despite his critique of religion as the "opiate of the masses," Karl Marx’s theory of history follows an unmistakably religious narrative structure:

  • The Fall: Primitive harmony.
  • Suffering: The era of capitalism.
  • Redemption: The act of revolution.
  • Paradise: The achievement of a classless society.

III. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Immanence The presence of the divine or ultimate within the world and human experience. It emphasizes that the sacred is close, accessible, and present in nature or the human heart.
Sacred That which is set apart from the ordinary; treated with special reverence, awe, or holiness. It is that which deserves a response beyond normal everyday behavior.
Secular Belonging to the ordinary world of everyday life; that which lacks special religious significance.
Transcendence The quality of being utterly beyond or outside the physical world. It stands in direct tension with immanence.

IV. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to the text, does thinking religiously require a person to be devout or adopt a specific faith? Answer: No. It involves an intellectual approach to big questions and a willingness to sit with uncertainty, regardless of one's personal beliefs.

2. What is the primary difference between the "sacred" and the "secular"? Answer: The sacred is set apart from the ordinary and treated with reverence/awe, while the secular belongs to the ordinary, everyday world without special religious significance.

3. What is the central tension found in almost every religious tradition mentioned in the text? Answer: The tension between transcendence (a God/ultimate beyond the world) and immanence (a God/ultimate present within the world).

4. Why does the text describe the questions asked by religion as having "gravity"? Answer: Because they possess a weight and seriousness that attracts serious thinkers—including philosophers and novelists—regardless of their personal religious convictions.

V. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Secular-Religious Divide: Analyze the text’s argument that thinkers like Karl Marx and Albert Camus are engaging in religious thinking despite their secular or anti-religious stances. How does the "structure" of their theories mirror religious narratives or concerns?

2. Immanence vs. Transcendence: Explain the tension between immanence and transcendence. How might a tradition’s emphasis on one over the other change how a person interacts with the "ordinary" or "secular" world?

3. The Methodology of Inquiry: The text suggests that religious thinking requires sitting with "difficulty and uncertainty." Compare this approach to the way one might approach a "textbook" question or a laboratory experiment. Why are "big questions" resistant to traditional calculation?
