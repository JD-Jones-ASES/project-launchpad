Study Guide: The Origins of Islam—The Prophet, the Revelation, and the Community

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the foundational period of Islam, focusing on the life of the Prophet Muhammad, the nature of divine revelation, the establishment of the early Muslim community, and the historical milestones that shaped the faith.

I. Historical Overview of the Prophet Muhammad

Early Life and Character

  • Birth: Muhammad ibn Abdullah was born around 570 CE in Mecca, located in the Arabian Peninsula.
  • Lineage: He belonged to the Quraysh tribe, who served as the guardians of the Ka’ba. Though his family was respected, they were not wealthy.
  • Upbringing: Orphaned at a young age—his father died before his birth and his mother when he was six—he was raised by his uncle, Abu Talib.
  • Professional Life: As a young merchant, he earned the nickname Al-Amin ("the trustworthy") due to his reputation for honesty.
  • Marriage: He married Khadijah, a prosperous widow fifteen years his senior. She was the first person to support his prophetic mission.

The First Revelation (610 CE)

At age 40, during a meditative retreat in a cave on Mount Hira, Muhammad experienced the beginning of divine revelation.

  • The Messenger: The angel Gabriel (Jibril) appeared and commanded Muhammad to "recite" (iqra’).
  • The Quran: The revelations continued for twenty-three years until his death in 632 CE. Muslims believe these revelations are the literal speech of God.

Preaching in Mecca and Persecution

Beginning public ministry around 613 CE, Muhammad’s message challenged the Meccan establishment on several fronts:

  1. Monotheism: Asserting there is only one God (Allah).
  2. Social Justice: Demanding that the rich fulfill obligations to the poor.
  3. Equality: Proclaiming that all humans are equal before God.
  4. Eschatology: Warning of a coming Day of Judgment.

Consequences: The wealthy polytheistic merchants of Mecca responded with boycotts, beatings, and killings. In 615 CE, some Muslims fled to Christian Ethiopia seeking protection from the Negus (king), marking the first Muslim diaspora.

II. The Hijra and the Medina Period

The Migration (622 CE)

In 622 CE, Muhammad and his followers migrated from Mecca to Yathrib (later renamed Medina). This event, known as the Hijra, is the founding moment of the Muslim community and serves as the starting point for the Islamic calendar.

Leadership and Governance

In Medina, Muhammad’s role expanded from preacher to statesman and lawgiver:

  • The Ummah: He established the ummah, a spiritual family of believers that transcended traditional ties of tribe, race, and nationality.
  • Constitution of Medina: He negotiated this agreement to organize the city’s Muslim, Jewish, and pagan tribes under a single common framework.

Military Conflict and the Return to Mecca

The tension between Medina and Mecca led to several formative battles:

  • Battle of Badr (624 CE)
  • Battle of Uhud (625 CE)
  • Battle of the Trench (627 CE)

In 630 CE, Muhammad returned to Mecca with a large army. The city surrendered with almost no bloodshed. Muhammad cleansed the Ka’ba of its idols, rededicating it to the one God.

III. Key Concepts and Themes

Concept Description
Meaning of Islam Derived from the Arabic root s-l-m, meaning peace, safety, and wholeness. It signifies "submission" or "surrender" to God.
The Ka’ba A cube-shaped shrine in Mecca believed to have been built by Abraham and Ishmael. It is the geographical center of Islamic worship.
The Prophet’s Death Muhammad died in 632 CE in Medina. He left no designated successor, leading to significant future questions regarding community leadership.
Rapid Expansion Within 100 years of Muhammad's death, Islam spread across Persia, Egypt, North Africa, Spain, and Central Asia.

IV. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What was Muhammad’s reputation in Mecca prior to his revelations?
    • Answer: He was known as "Al-Amin" (the trustworthy) due to his honesty and reliability as a merchant.
  2. Who was the first person to believe in Muhammad’s prophetic message?
    • Answer: His wife, Khadijah.
  3. What does the word "Quran" mean in relation to the first revelation?
    • Answer: It comes from the same root as iqra’, meaning to "recite" or "read aloud."
  4. Why did the Meccan elite persecute the early Muslim community?
    • Answer: They found his message of monotheism, social equality, and the obligation of the rich to the poor to be a threat to their polytheistic social and economic order.
  5. What is the significance of the year 622 CE in Islam?
    • Answer: It marks the Hijra (migration to Medina), the founding of the ummah, and the start of the Islamic calendar.
  6. How did Muhammad change the status of the Ka’ba in 630 CE?
    • Answer: He cleansed it of idols and rededicated it to the worship of the one God, restoring what Muslims believe was its original purpose under Abraham.

V. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Evolution of Leadership: Analyze the transition of Muhammad’s role from a persecuted preacher in Mecca to a statesman and lawgiver in Medina. How did the Constitution of Medina reflect this shift?
  2. The Concept of Ummah: Explain how the concept of the ummah challenged the existing social structures of 7th-century Arabia. In what ways did it prioritize spiritual identity over tribal or racial identity?
  3. Historical Significance of the Hijra: Discuss why the Hijra, rather than the birth of Muhammad or the first revelation, was chosen as the starting point for the Islamic calendar.

VI. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Al-Amin: "The trustworthy"; a nickname given to Muhammad for his honesty.
  • Allah: The Arabic word for God, used by Muslims, Arab Christians, and Jews.
  • Hijra: The migration of Muhammad and his followers from Mecca to Medina in 622 CE.
  • Iqra’: "Recite"; the first command given to Muhammad by the angel Gabriel.
  • Islam: "Submission" or "surrender" to God; related to the root for "peace."
  • Jibril: The Arabic name for the angel Gabriel, who transmitted the divine revelations.
  • Ka’ba: The sacred, cube-shaped shrine in Mecca; the focal point of Islamic prayer.
  • Muslim: One who makes "islam" (submission) to God.
  • Quraysh: The dominant tribe in Mecca and the guardians of the Ka’ba.
  • Ummah: The worldwide community of Muslim believers, viewed as a single spiritual family.
