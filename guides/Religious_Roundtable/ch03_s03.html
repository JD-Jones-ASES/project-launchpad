Study Guide: Sacred Texts and the Foundation of Islamic Law

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the primary scriptures and legal foundations of Islam, specifically focusing on the Quran, the Hadith, the Sunnah, and the development of Sharia.

I. Key Concepts and Overview

The Nature of the Quran

The Quran is the sacred scripture of Islam, considered by Muslims to be the direct, verbatim word of God (Allah). Unlike other religious texts that may be viewed as divinely inspired compositions by human authors, the Quran is viewed as God’s speech, with the Prophet Muhammad serving as a vessel for the revelation.

  • Etymology: The word "Quran" means "recitation."
  • Revelation: The text was revealed to Muhammad by God through the angel Gabriel between the years 610 and 632.
  • Language and Translation: The Quran is uniquely tied to the Arabic language. Muslims believe the Arabic text itself is miraculous in its precision and beauty. Consequently, translations into other languages are considered "interpretations" or "meanings" rather than the Quran itself.

Preservation and Compilation

The Quran began as an oral tradition and was gradually codified into a standardized written text.

  • Oral Tradition: From its inception, the community memorized the revelations. A person who has committed the entire Quran to memory is honored with the title hafiz ("one who preserves").
  • Early Records: During Muhammad’s life, fragments were recorded on various materials, including flat stones, bones, and palm leaves.
  • Codification:
    • Abu Bakr (First Caliph): Ordered the first compilation of fragments into a single document following Muhammad's death.
    • Uthman ibn Affan (Third Caliph): Produced the authoritative written version around 650. He sent standardized copies to major cities and ordered the destruction of all other versions to ensure consistency.

Structural Organization

The Quran is composed of 114 chapters, known as suras.

  • Ordering: Rather than being chronological or thematic, the suras are arranged roughly from longest to shortest.
  • Exception: The first chapter, Al-Fatiha, is a short opening chapter.
  • Scale: The text contains approximately 77,000 words and covers topics ranging from theology and ethics to law, history, and the nature of the afterlife.

II. Supplementary Guidance and Islamic Law

Because the Quran does not provide specific instructions for every practical scenario in life, Muslims look to secondary sources for guidance.

The Hadith and Sunnah

  • Hadith: These are recorded reports of Muhammad’s sayings, actions, and tacit approvals. They were compiled in the centuries following his death. The most authoritative collections were produced in the 9th century by al-Bukhari and Muslim.
  • Sunnah: This refers to the "practice" or the way of life of the Prophet Muhammad. It serves as the model for Muslim behavior and is preserved through the Hadith.

Sharia (Islamic Law)

Sharia is the collective body of Islamic law derived from the Quran and the Sunnah.

  • Literal Meaning: The term translates to "the path to the watering hole."
  • Purpose: It is intended not as a restrictive system, but as a "road to life" providing guidance in all areas of human conduct.

III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. What does the term "Quran" literally mean? Answer: It means "recitation."

2. Over what time period was the Quran revealed to Muhammad? Answer: Between 610 and 632.

3. Why is the Quran considered "untranslatable" in a strict sense? Answer: Because the Quran is defined as the specific Arabic text revealed to Muhammad; translations are viewed only as interpretations of its meaning, as the Arabic itself is considered miraculous and precise.

4. Who is a "hafiz"? Answer: A Muslim who has memorized the entire Quran (all 114 chapters).

5. Which caliph is responsible for the authoritative version of the Quran used today? Answer: The third caliph, Uthman ibn Affan, who standardized the text around 650.

6. How are the chapters (suras) of the Quran generally organized? Answer: They are arranged roughly from longest to shortest, with the exception of the opening chapter, Al-Fatiha.

7. What is "Al-Fatiha," and how often is it recited? Answer: Known as "The Opening" or "the mother of the Quran," it is a seven-verse chapter recited in every unit of the five daily prayers, totaling 17 times a day or over 6,000 times a year for a practicing Muslim.

8. What is the difference between the Quran and the Hadith? Answer: The Quran is believed to be the verbatim word of God, while the Hadith are reports of the sayings and actions of Muhammad (the Prophet).

9. What two sources form the foundation for Sharia? Answer: The Quran and the Sunnah (the example of the Prophet as recorded in the Hadith).

10. What are the names of the two most authoritative collectors of Hadith? Answer: Al-Bukhari and Muslim.

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Vessel vs. The Author: Analyze the Islamic perspective on Muhammad’s role in the creation of the Quran. How does the concept of Muhammad as a "vessel" rather than an "author" influence the way the text is treated, translated, and studied compared to other religious scriptures?
  2. The Transition from Oral to Written: Discuss the process by which the Quran was preserved and eventually codified. Why was the standardization of the text under Uthman ibn Affan a significant turning point in Islamic history, and how did the oral tradition of the hafiz complement the written record?
  3. The Interdependence of Sacred Sources: Explain the relationship between the Quran, the Sunnah, and Sharia. Why is the Quran alone insufficient for the practical application of Islamic law, and how does the Sunnah provide the necessary framework for a "path to the watering hole"?

V. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Al-Fatiha "The Opening"; the seven-verse first chapter of the Quran, recited repeatedly in daily prayers and referred to as the "mother of the Quran."
Gabriel The angel through whom God revealed the Quran to Muhammad.
Hadith Recorded reports of the Prophet Muhammad's sayings, actions, and approvals, compiled after his death.
Hafiz "One who preserves"; a title given to a Muslim who has memorized the entire Quran.
Quran The sacred scripture of Islam, believed to be the verbatim word of God revealed to Muhammad.
Sharia Islamic law, meaning "the path to the watering hole," derived from the Quran and the Sunnah.
Sunnah The practice and way of life of the Prophet Muhammad, serving as a model for Muslim behavior.
Sura A chapter of the Quran; there are 114 suras in total.
Uthman ibn Affan The third caliph who produced the authoritative written version of the Quran around 650.
