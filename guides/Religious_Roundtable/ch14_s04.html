Chapter 14, Section 4: At the Roundtable: Comparing Morality

Overview

This section explores three distinct traditions—Christianity, Taoism, and Communism—and their fundamentally different answers to the question of where moral obligation originates. It delves into each tradition's specific grounding for morality, the nature of its ethical demands, and then examines the critical questions they pose to each other. Finally, it addresses a common problem faced by all three: the disconnect between moral ideals and worldly power.

Key Concepts

  • Relational Morality (Christianity): Morality is grounded in love, specifically God's command and example as revealed through Jesus. Ethical obligation is relational, stemming from the experience of being loved, and aims for internal transformation rather than mere external compliance. The Beatitudes exemplify the character formed by this love.
  • Ontological Morality (Taoism): Morality originates from the Tao, the deep, natural pattern of reality. The good life involves removing obstacles to naturalness and aligning with this intrinsic order. Moral obligation is ontological because it arises from what things truly are when unforced, exemplified by "wu wei" (effortless action).
  • Historical and Material Morality (Communism): Morality is rooted in historical and material conditions, particularly the objective interests of the working class, which produces value but suffers exploitation. Moral obligations are not eternal but emerge from the struggle for liberation, demanding solidarity and collective action.
  • Inter-Traditional Critiques: Each tradition challenges the others' foundations: Christianity questions Taoism's definition of "nature"; Taoism questions Christianity's concept of "commanded love"; and Communism critiques both for offering "eternal" standards that may mask the interests of the powerful.
  • The Problem of Power and Moral Ideals: All three traditions acknowledge that individuals embodying their moral ideals (the meek, the sage, the class-conscious worker) often lack worldly power. Each offers a unique perspective on this predicament: Christianity (the last shall be first), Taoism (the soft overcomes the hard), and Communism (history is on our side).

Important Terms

  • Beatitudes: A series of blessings recounted by Jesus in the Sermon on the Mount, describing the characteristics of those blessed by God and embodying Christian love (e.g., the meek, the peacemakers). They describe a transformed state of being.
  • Tao (Dao): In Taoism, the ultimate source, pattern, and principle of everything that exists. It represents the natural, unforced way of the universe, underlying all human convention.
  • Wu Wei: A core Taoist concept meaning "non-action" or "effortless action." It refers to acting in harmony with the Tao, spontaneously and without artificial striving, leading to natural and effective results. It embodies being what one is without forcing.
  • Ontological: Relating to the nature of being or existence. In Taoism, moral obligation is described as ontological because it stems from the fundamental reality and inherent nature of the Tao and existence itself.
  • Historical and Material: A perspective that roots explanations in the concrete conditions of history and the physical, economic, and social structures of the material world. In Communism, moral obligations are understood to arise from these realities.
  • Exploitation: In a Marxist context, the unjust appropriation of surplus value (profit) by the owning class from the labor of the working class, leading to an unequal distribution of wealth and power.
  • Solidarity: Unity or agreement of feeling or action, especially among individuals with a common interest; mutual support within a group. In Communist thought, it is a crucial moral obligation for the working class to achieve collective liberation.
  • Class-conscious worker: A worker who understands their position within the class structure, recognizes the inherent conflict between their class and the owning class, and is aware of the need for collective action to achieve societal change.

Detailed Notes

The Core Question: Where Does Moral Obligation Come From?

This section addresses a fundamental philosophical question by presenting three distinct answers from major worldviews. Each tradition offers a unique grounding for what makes something morally right or obligatory.

Christianity: Grounding Morality in Love

  • Source of Obligation: God's command and example, specifically embodied in the life, death, and resurrection of Jesus. It's a relational grounding: one owes love because they have first been loved by God.
  • Nature of Morality: Not primarily about following external rules, but about internal transformation. The Beatitudes illustrate the character and state of being of a person formed by love for God and neighbor.
  • Ethical Demand: To be transformed by love, reflecting God's nature. This goes beyond mere compliance to a deep internal change.

Taoism: Grounding Morality in Nature

  • Source of Obligation: The Tao, the fundamental, deep pattern of reality that precedes and underlies all human constructs and conventions.
  • Nature of Morality: The good life is achieved not through more effort or rules, but by removing obstacles to one's natural state and aligning with the Tao.
  • Ethical Demand: To achieve "naturalness," to "be what it is" without force. The concept of "wu wei" (effortless action) describes the sage who acts in perfect harmony with the Tao, not by conscious compliance but by inherent reality. This is an ontological grounding.

Communism: Grounding Morality in History

  • Source of Obligation: The objective interests of the working class, who produce all value in society but are exploited. Morality is a product of actual historical and material conditions, not eternal truths.
  • Nature of Morality: Arises from conditions of exploitation and the possibility of liberation.
  • Ethical Demand: Solidarity with the working class and collective action to achieve the good life that capitalism has denied. Moral obligation is historical and material.

Hard Questions Posed by Each Tradition

  • Christianity to Taoism: If "nature" is the moral standard, which aspect of nature should be followed? Human nature also encompasses traits like competitiveness, violence, and tribalism.
  • Taoism to Christianity: If love is a divine command, does its commanded nature diminish its authenticity as "love"? What makes a divine command inherently binding?
  • Communism to Christianity and Taoism: Both "divine love" and "natural harmony" are questioned as potentially serving the interests of those not experiencing the harsh realities of factory work or economic exploitation. Communism asks where these "eternal" moral standards truly stand in the face of material inequality.

The Shared Deep Problem: Moral Ideals vs. Worldly Power

All three traditions recognize that the person who most embodies their moral ideal (the meek Christian, the Taoist sage, the class-conscious worker) often does not hold power in the world.

  • Christianity's Response: This lack of power is central to its message; "the last shall be first" implies a future reversal or a different kind of power.
  • Taoism's Response: The "soft overcomes the hard" suggests that the gentle, natural way of the Tao ultimately triumphs over rigid force.
  • Communism's Response: "It will not always be this way—history is on our side" asserts an inevitable historical progression towards the liberation of the working class and the triumph of their ideals.

Concluding Reflection

The section concludes by prompting the reader to reflect on their own sources of moral obligation and to consider whether the source of a moral action truly matters as long as the action itself is good.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast the Christian, Taoist, and Communist understandings of where moral obligation originates. How does each tradition define the "source" of morality?
  2. Explain the concept of "internal transformation" in Christianity and "naturalness" in Taoism. How do these concepts shape their respective ethical demands compared to a system based purely on rules?
  3. How does Communism argue that moral obligations are "historical and material" rather than "eternal"? What specific conditions does it identify as the source of moral duty?
  4. Discuss the critical questions each tradition raises for the others (Christianity to Taoism, Taoism to Christianity, Communism to both). What fundamental assumptions are being challenged in these critiques?
  5. All three traditions acknowledge that those who embody their moral ideals often lack worldly power. Explain each tradition's response to this "deep problem." Which response do you find most compelling, and why?
