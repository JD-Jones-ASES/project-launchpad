Study Guide: Sacred Texts and Foundations of Islamic Law

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the primary texts and foundational concepts of Islam, focusing on the Quran, the Hadith, and the development of Islamic law (Sharia).

Key Concepts and Thematic Overview

The Nature of the Quran

The Quran is the sacred scripture of Islam, considered by Muslims to be the direct, verbatim word of God. It was revealed to the Prophet Muhammad through the angel Gabriel over a twenty-two-year period, between 610 and 632 CE.

Unlike other religious texts, the Quran is not viewed as a book written or inspired by a human author. Instead, Muhammad is seen as a "vessel" for God’s speech. Because the text is considered the literal word of God in Arabic, it is held to be untranslatable in a strict sense. Any version of the Quran in a language other than Arabic is regarded as an interpretation or a translation of its meaning, rather than the Quran itself. The Arabic text is celebrated for its miraculous beauty, precision, and oral rhythms.

Compilation and Organization

  • Oral Tradition: The name "Quran" means "recitation." From its inception, the text has been memorized. A individual who memorizes all 114 chapters (approximately 77,000 words) is honored with the title of hafiz.
  • Written Record: During Muhammad’s life, revelations were recorded on various materials, including bones, stones, and palm leaves.
  • Standardization: Following Muhammad’s death, the first caliph, Abu Bakr, ordered the compilation of these fragments. The third caliph, Uthman ibn Affan, established the authoritative written text around 650 CE, distributing standardized copies and ordering the destruction of variant versions.
  • Structure: The Quran consists of 114 chapters called suras. These are generally arranged from the longest to the shortest, with the exception of the opening chapter, Al-Fatiha.

Sources of Guidance Beyond the Quran

While the Quran is the primary source of authority, it does not address every specific practical matter. Consequently, Muslims look to secondary sources:

  • Hadith: Recorded reports of the sayings, actions, and approvals of the Prophet Muhammad. These were compiled in the centuries following his death, with the most authoritative collections produced by al-Bukhari and Muslim in the 9th century.
  • Sunnah: The "practice and way of life" of Muhammad. Derived from the Hadith, the Sunnah serves as the model for Muslim behavior and ethics.
  • Sharia: Often translated as "Islamic law," the term literally means "the path to the watering hole." It represents a road to life rather than a restrictive set of rules. Sharia is founded upon the combination of the Quran and the Sunnah.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. What does the word "Quran" literally mean? Answer: The name means "recitation."

2. How does the Muslim view of the Quran’s authorship differ from other scriptures? Answer: Muslims do not believe Muhammad wrote or composed the Quran under divine inspiration; rather, they believe God spoke it directly, and Muhammad served as a vessel to recite God's verbatim words.

3. What is a "hafiz"? Answer: A hafiz is a person who has memorized the entire Quran—all 114 chapters and roughly 77,000 words.

4. Why is the Quran considered "untranslatable"? Answer: Because the Quran is viewed as the direct word of God in Arabic, the beauty and precision of the original language are considered miraculous. Other languages can only provide interpretations of the meaning, not the Quran itself.

5. Which caliph is responsible for producing the authoritative written text of the Quran used today? Answer: The third caliph, Uthman ibn Affan, produced the standardized text around 650 CE.

6. How are the suras (chapters) of the Quran generally organized? Answer: They are arranged roughly from longest to shortest, with the exception of the short opening chapter.

7. What is the difference between the Quran and the Hadith? Answer: The Quran is believed to be the verbatim word of God, while the Hadith are recorded reports of the sayings and actions of the Prophet Muhammad, compiled by scholars in later centuries.

8. What is the literal meaning of the word "Sharia"? Answer: It literally means "the path to the watering hole."

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Role of Orality and Memorization: Discuss the significance of the Quran as a text "meant to be heard as much as read." How has the tradition of the hafiz and the oral recitation of the Quran influenced its preservation and its role in the daily life of a Muslim?
  2. The Development of the Written Text: Trace the transition of the Quran from fragments on bones and palm leaves to the standardized Uthmanic text. Why was the standardization of the text a critical moment in Islamic history?
  3. The Interplay of Quran, Sunnah, and Sharia: Explain how the Quran and the Sunnah work together to form the basis of Sharia. Why is the example of the Prophet Muhammad (the Sunnah) necessary for practical guidance in the Muslim community?
  4. Analysis of Al-Fatiha: Known as the "Mother of the Quran," Al-Fatiha is recited at least 17 times daily by practicing Muslims. Based on its role in daily prayer, analyze why this specific chapter is considered central to the Islamic faith.

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Al-Fatiha "The Opening"; the seven-verse first chapter of the Quran, recited in every unit of daily prayer. Known as the "Mother of the Quran."
Hadith Recorded reports of the Prophet Muhammad's sayings, actions, and approvals, compiled in the centuries after his death.
Hafiz "One who preserves"; a person who has memorized the entire Quran.
Quran The sacred scripture of Islam; the verbatim word of God as revealed to Muhammad between 610 and 632 CE.
Sharia "The path to the watering hole"; the body of Islamic law derived from the Quran and the Sunnah.
Sunnah The practice and way of life of the Prophet Muhammad, serving as a model for Muslim behavior.
Sura A chapter of the Quran. There are 114 suras in total.
Uthman ibn Affan The third caliph who established the authoritative written version of the Quran around 650 CE.
