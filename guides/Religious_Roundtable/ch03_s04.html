Sacred Texts and the Living Law of Islam: A Comprehensive Study Guide

This study guide provides an overview of the foundational texts and legal framework of Islam, focusing on the nature, history, and structure of the Quran, as well as the supplementary roles of the Hadith, the Sunnah, and Sharia.

Key Concepts and Overview

The Nature of the Quran

The Quran is the sacred scripture of Islam, considered by Muslims to be the direct, verbatim word of God (Allah). Unlike other scriptures, it is not viewed as a book written by a human under divine inspiration. Instead, it is seen as God’s speech delivered through the Prophet Muhammad as a vessel.

  • Revelation: The text was revealed to Muhammad by the angel Gabriel between 610 and 632 CE.
  • Language: The Quran is strictly the Arabic text. Because the Arabic is held to be miraculous in its precision and beauty, any version in another language is considered an "interpretation" or "meaning" rather than the Quran itself.
  • Recitation: The word "Quran" means "recitation." It is intended to be heard as much as read, with its rhythms and sound patterns forming a core part of its impact.

Historical Compilation and Standardization

The preservation of the Quran moved from oral tradition and fragmented records to a standardized volume shortly after the Prophet’s death.

  1. Original Records: During Muhammad’s life, revelations were memorized and recorded on various materials like flat stones, palm leaves, and bones.
  2. Initial Compilation: The first caliph, Abu Bakr, ordered these fragments compiled into a single document.
  3. Standardization: The third caliph, Uthman ibn Affan, produced the authoritative written text around 650 CE. He sent standardized copies to major cities and ordered all other versions destroyed to ensure consistency.

Structure and Content

  • Suras: The Quran consists of 114 chapters called suras.
  • Organization: The chapters are generally arranged from longest to shortest. The primary exception is the Al-Fatiha, the short opening chapter.
  • Themes: The text covers a wide array of subjects, including theology, ethics, law, history, stories of earlier prophets, the nature of God, the duties of believers, and descriptions of Paradise and Hell.

Guidance Beyond the Quran

Because the Quran does not address every specific practical question, other sources of guidance are used to form the "Living Law."

  • Hadith: Recorded reports of the Prophet Muhammad's sayings, actions, and tacit approvals. These were compiled in the centuries following his death.
  • Sunnah: The "practice" or "way of life" of the Prophet. It serves as the model for Muslim behavior and is preserved within the Hadith.
  • Sharia: Derived from the Quran and the Sunnah, Sharia is the path of Islamic law. The term literally translates to "the path to the watering hole."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. What does the word "Quran" literally mean? Answer: It means "recitation."

2. Over what period of time was the Quran revealed to Muhammad? Answer: Between the years 610 and 632 CE.

3. Why do Muslims consider the Quran to be untranslatable in a strict sense? Answer: Because the Quran is specifically the Arabic text, which is viewed as miraculous in beauty and precision. Translations are considered interpretations of the meaning rather than the Quran itself.

4. What is a "hafiz"? Answer: A Muslim who has memorized the entire Quran (all 114 chapters and approximately 77,000 words).

5. Which caliph is responsible for the authoritative written text used today? Answer: The third caliph, Uthman ibn Affan, established the authoritative text around 650 CE.

6. How is the Al-Fatiha used in daily life? Answer: It is the opening chapter of the Quran and is recited in every unit of the five daily prayers. A practicing Muslim recites it at least 17 times per day.

7. Who authored the most authoritative collections of the Hadith? Answer: Al-Bukhari and Muslim (9th century).

8. What is the literal meaning of the word "Sharia"? Answer: It literally means "the path to the watering hole."

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Divine Vessel: The Islamic View of Prophetic Revelation

Analyze the distinction between the Muslim view of the Quran and the concept of "divine inspiration" found in other religious traditions. How does the belief that Muhammad was a "vessel" for God’s verbatim speech—rather than an author—impact the way the text is handled, memorized, and translated?

2. The Relationship Between Quran, Sunnah, and Sharia

Explain how the Quran and the Sunnah function together to create Sharia. Why is the Quran alone insufficient for addressing all practical questions of life, and how does the example of the Prophet (the Sunnah), as recorded in the Hadith, provide a "path to life" for the believer?

3. Preservation and Standardization

Discuss the historical process of the Quran’s compilation. Contrast the roles of Abu Bakr and Uthman ibn Affan in preserving the text. What were the motivations for creating a standardized version, and what was the significance of ordering the destruction of variant versions?

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Al-Fatiha "The Opening"; the first chapter of the Quran, recited 17 times daily in prayer and referred to as the "mother of the Quran."
Gabriel The angel through whom God revealed the Quran to Muhammad.
Hadith Recorded reports of the sayings, actions, and approvals of the Prophet Muhammad; compiled by scholars like al-Bukhari and Muslim.
Hafiz "One who preserves"; a title given to a person who has memorized the entire Quran.
Quran The sacred scripture of Islam, believed to be the verbatim word of God revealed to Muhammad.
Sharia Islamic law, meaning "the path to the watering hole"; the foundational code of life derived from the Quran and Sunnah.
Sunnah The "practice" and way of life of the Prophet Muhammad, serving as a model for Muslim behavior.
Sura A chapter of the Quran; there are 114 suras in total.
Uthman ibn Affan The third caliph who standardized the Quranic text around 650 CE.
