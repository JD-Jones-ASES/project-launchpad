Study Guide: Branches of Islam

This study guide examines the historical divisions and spiritual traditions within Islam, focusing on the emergence of Sunni, Shia, and Sufi streams following the death of the Prophet Muhammad.

Overview: The Crisis of Succession

In 632, the death of Muhammad created a leadership vacuum. Because he left no written instructions regarding a successor, the Muslim community (ummah) faced a fundamental question: Who has the legitimate authority to lead? This dispute became the primary driver of the major divisions within the faith.

Major Traditions and Streams

1. Sunni Islam

Sunni Islam is the largest branch, comprising approximately 85–90% of the global Muslim population.

  • Concept of Leadership: Leadership is vested in a caliph (meaning "successor" or "deputy"). Sunnis believe the leader should be a qualified individual chosen by community consensus to govern affairs and protect the faith.
  • The Rightly Guided Caliphs: This term refers to the first four leaders after Muhammad: Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, and Ali. Their era is considered the "golden age" of Islamic governance.
  • Internal Diversity: Sunni Islam includes four major schools of legal interpretation known as madhabs. While they agree on core tenets, they differ on specific details of practice.

2. Shia Islam

Shia Islam originated from the Shi'at Ali ("the party of Ali").

  • Concept of Leadership: Shias believe leadership should have remained within Muhammad’s immediate family, specifically his cousin and son-in-law, Ali ibn Abi Talib.
  • The Imams: Shias are led by Imams—descendants of Ali who possess divinely guided authority to interpret revelation. While they do not receive new prophecy, they are seen as spiritual guides.
  • The Battle of Karbala (680): A pivotal tragedy where Ali’s son, Husayn, was murdered. This event is commemorated annually during Ashura, a period of intense mourning and reflection.
  • Demographics: Shias form majorities in Iran, Iraq, Bahrain, and Lebanon.

3. Sufi Islam

Sufism is not a separate sect but a mystical spiritual current found within both Sunni and Shia traditions.

  • Core Objective: Sufism focuses on inward practice, devotion, and contemplation to achieve a direct experience of God’s presence, often referred to as qurb (nearness).
  • Key Practices:
    • Dhikr: The rhythmic chanting of God’s names.
    • Poetry: Devotional works, most notably by the 13th-century Persian poet Jalal al-Din Rumi.
    • Whirling Dervishes: A form of moving meditation where practitioners spin in circles. The right hand faces up to receive divine grace, while the left faces down to pass it to the earth, symbolizing the soul revolving around God and turning away from the self.
  • Historical Impact: Sufism was a primary vehicle for the peaceful spread of Islam into Africa, Central Asia, and South Asia.

Commonalities and Unity

Despite historical and theological differences, the three streams are united by core practices and beliefs:

  • Recitation of the same Quran.
  • Performing the same daily prayers.
  • Fasting during the month of Ramadan.
  • Facing the Ka'ba during prayer.
  • Adherence to the theological principle of the unity of the ummah.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What does the word "caliph" literally mean?
  2. What percentage of the world's Muslims identify as Sunni?
  3. According to Shia belief, who was the first rightful successor to Muhammad?
  4. What is the significance of the Battle of Karbala in Shia tradition?
  5. What is the primary goal of Sufi religious life?
  6. Name the 13th-century Sufi poet whose works on divine love are world-renowned.
  7. In the dance of the Sufi dervishes, what do the positions of the right and left hands symbolize?
  8. List three practices that Sunni, Shia, and Sufi Muslims all share.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Crisis of Authority: Analyze how the lack of written instructions for succession after Muhammad’s death in 632 led to the permanent division between Sunni and Shia Islam. Contrast their differing definitions of legitimate leadership.
  2. Sufism as a Bridge: Discuss the role of Sufism within the broader Islamic world. How does it function as a "spiritual current" rather than a denomination, and how has its focus on mysticism aided the spread of the faith geographically?
  3. Unity Amidst Diversity: Evaluate the statement: "The unity of the ummah is a theological principle that runs deeper than any division within it." Use examples of shared rituals and beliefs to support your argument.

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Ashura A day of profound mourning for Shia Muslims, commemorating the murder of Husayn at the Battle of Karbala.
Caliph Literally "successor" or "deputy"; the title for leaders of the Muslim community in the Sunni tradition.
Dhikr A Sufi practice involving the chanting of God’s names as a form of devotion.
Imam In Shia Islam, a descendant of Ali who possesses divine guidance to interpret revelation.
Madhabs The four major schools of legal interpretation within Sunni Islam.
Qurb A term meaning "nearness" or "closeness" to God; the ultimate goal of Sufi practice.
Shi'at Ali "The party of Ali"; the root of the term "Shia," representing those who believed Ali should succeed Muhammad.
Sufism The mystical strand of Islam emphasizing inward practice and the direct experience of God.
Ummah The collective worldwide community of Muslim believers.
