Study Guide: Core Beliefs of Christianity

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the fundamental doctrines that define the Christian faith, as outlined in the provided text. It covers the nature of God, the person of Jesus Christ, the human condition, and the mechanism of salvation.

Core Thematic Overview

1. The Trinity

Christianity presents a unique monotheism where God is not a solitary being but a Trinity. This doctrine asserts that there is one divine being consisting of three distinct persons:

  • The Father: The creator and sustainer of all things.
  • The Son: Jesus Christ, who entered human history.
  • The Holy Spirit: The continuing divine presence that guides and empowers believers.

The Trinity emphasizes that within the single divine reality, there is relationship, communication, and love. Christians acknowledge that while analogies (like a triangle with three sides) may be used, the Trinity remains a mystery that exceeds full human comprehension.

2. The Incarnation

The Incarnation is the belief that the eternal Son of God became fully human in the person of Jesus Christ. Key aspects include:

  • Nature: Jesus was not a god in disguise or a mere observer; he was fully human and fully divine simultaneously.
  • Experience: As a human, he experienced birth, growth, fatigue, grief, and death.
  • Purpose: It represents the extent to which God would go to reach humanity—entering finite human life entirely.

3. Sin and the Human Condition

Christianity identifies sin as the fundamental problem facing humanity.

  • Definition: Any thought, word, or deed that turns a person away from God and toward self, resulting in separation from the source of life.
  • Original Sin: Derived from the book of Genesis, this doctrine suggests that the first humans' disobedience introduced a rupture into human nature.
  • Impact: Humans are viewed as inherently inclined toward selfishness, pride, and injustice. Because this "sickness" is internal to human nature, no human effort alone can repair it; outside intervention is required.

4. The Resurrection

The Resurrection is considered the central event of history and the foundation of the Christian faith.

  • The Event: Christians believe Jesus rose bodily from the dead on the third day following his crucifixion.
  • Significance: It serves as evidence that death has been defeated and the separation between God and humanity caused by sin has been overcome.
  • Hope: It provides the basis for the hope that human beings can be transformed and restored.

5. Grace and Salvation

The process of being made right with God (salvation) is centered on the concept of grace.

  • The Gift: Salvation is a gift of unearned love and favor from God; it cannot be achieved through religious effort or good behavior.
  • The Response: While salvation is a gift, humans respond through faith—a trust and reliance on God's work in Christ.
  • Behavior: Genuine faith is expected to produce genuine change in a person's life, but this change is the result of the gift, not the means of earning it.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

Question Answer Based on Source Context
How does the Christian view of God differ from a "solitary" being? Christianity teaches that God is a Trinity—three distinct persons (Father, Son, and Holy Spirit) in one divine being.
What is the specific role of the Holy Spirit? The Holy Spirit is the continuing divine presence that guides and empowers believers.
Why is the Incarnation considered a "startling" claim? Because it asserts that the infinite, eternal God became a finite human who felt fatigue, grief, and death.
What is the definition of "sin" provided in the text? Any thought, word, or deed that turns a person away from God and toward the self, causing separation from goodness.
According to the text, why can't humans "fix" themselves? Human nature is ruptured and inclined toward selfishness; like a sick doctor, humanity cannot heal itself without outside intervention.
What did the Apostle Paul say regarding the Resurrection? Paul wrote that if Christ has not been raised, then the Christian faith is empty.
What is the relationship between good behavior and salvation? Salvation is a gift of grace that cannot be earned by behavior; however, genuine faith will naturally produce a change in behavior.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Paradox of the Incarnation: Analyze the theological implications of Jesus being "fully human and fully divine" at the same time. How does this claim differentiate Christianity from a belief system where a god merely "disguises" himself as a human?
  2. The Necessity of Intervention: Using the text's analogy of the "sick doctor," discuss why Christian doctrine insists that human effort is insufficient for salvation. How does the concept of "Original Sin" necessitate the Resurrection?
  3. Grace vs. Effort: Explore the tension between grace as an "unearned gift" and the expectation that "genuine faith produces genuine change." If behavior does not earn salvation, what is its role in the life of a believer according to Christian teaching?
  4. Love, Justice, and Power: Based on the "Big Question" in the text, evaluate the Christian answer to why God chose to become human and suffer rather than simply offering a decree of forgiveness. What does this suggest about the Christian understanding of God's character?

Glossary of Key Terms

  • Grace: God's freely given, unearned love and favor, which enables human beings to be forgiven and restored to relationship with God.
  • Incarnation: The belief that the eternal Son of God took on a fully human nature in the person of Jesus Christ, being born of the Virgin Mary.
  • Resurrection: The belief that Jesus rose bodily from the dead on the third day after his burial, in a transformed physical body.
  • Sin: Any thought, word, or deed that turns a person away from God and toward self; an act of separation from the source of life and goodness.
  • Trinity: The Christian doctrine that God is three distinct persons—Father, Son, and Holy Spirit—who together constitute one divine being.
