Study Guide: The Major Traditions of Christianity

This study guide explores the historical development, distinct characteristics, and shared core beliefs of the three primary branches of Christianity: Roman Catholicism, Eastern Orthodoxy, and Protestantism. It provides an overview of the key schisms that shaped the faith and the diverse expressions of Christian practice today.

Core Concepts Overview

The Three Major Branches

The Christian tradition, while sharing a common ancestry, is divided into three primary categories. Despite their differences, they remain united by a "common core" of theological beliefs.

Branch Primary Leadership/Authority Key Characteristics
Roman Catholicism The Pope (Bishop of Rome) and bishops Emphasis on seven sacraments, apostolic succession, and the veneration of Mary and the saints.
Eastern Orthodoxy Patriarchs (historically Constantinople) Focus on the ancient Greek liturgy, use of icons, and the theological concept of theosis.
Protestantism Varies by denomination (no central head) Emphasis on sola scriptura (Scripture alone) and sola fide (faith alone).

The Common Core

Despite historical ruptures, all three branches generally agree on the following foundational elements found in ancient creeds:

  • The Trinity: The nature of God as three persons.
  • The Incarnation: The belief in God becoming human in Jesus Christ.
  • The Resurrection: The belief in Christ rising from the dead.
  • Scriptural Authority: The fundamental importance of the Bible.
  • Eternal Life: The hope for life beyond death.

Historical Ruptures

The Great Schism (1054)

The first major division occurred between the Western (Roman) and Eastern (Orthodox) churches. The split was the result of several factors:

  • Authority: Tensions over the Pope’s claim of authority over all Christians.
  • Theology and Culture: Differing theological perspectives and centuries of cultural drift.
  • Excommunication: The formal break occurred when the Western and Eastern leadership excommunicated one another.

The Protestant Reformation (1517)

The second major split began when Martin Luther challenged the practices of the Roman Catholic Church.

  • The 95 Theses: Luther’s objections focused initially on the sale of indulgences—payments intended to reduce a soul's time in purgatory.
  • Institutional Critique: Luther argued that institutional power and layers of tradition had obscured the "simple Gospel truth."
  • Denominational Explosion: The Reformation led to the rise of Lutheran, Reformed (Calvinist), Anglican, and Anabaptist movements, eventually resulting in the 30,000 to 45,000 denominations that exist today.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Who is considered the head of the Roman Catholic Church?
  2. What does the term theosis represent in the Eastern Orthodox tradition?
  3. What were the two primary "rallying cries" of Martin Luther’s Reformation, and what do they mean?
  4. In which geographic regions is Eastern Orthodoxy currently the dominant Christian tradition?
  5. What is the Roman Catholic belief regarding the Eucharist?
  6. According to scholars, approximately how many distinct Christian denominations exist worldwide?
  7. Which 16th-century practice first prompted Martin Luther to post his ninety-five objections?
  8. What role do icons play in the Eastern Orthodox tradition?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Evolution of Authority: Analyze how the concept of authority differs between the three major branches of Christianity. Compare the Roman Catholic emphasis on the Papacy and tradition with the Protestant focus on sola scriptura.
  2. Unity Amidst Diversity: Despite the "Great Schism" and the "Great Rupture" of the Reformation, Christians claim a "common core." Discuss the specific theological points that unite these diverse traditions and evaluate whether these commonalities outweigh their historical disagreements.
  3. The Impact of the Reformation: Explore the long-term consequences of Martin Luther’s actions in 1517. How did the move away from a central authority lead to the modern landscape of 30,000+ denominations, and what does this diversity suggest about the Christian impulse to interpret original sources?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Apostolic Succession: The Catholic belief in the authority of bishops as the direct successors of the original apostles.
  • Great Schism: The formal split in 1054 between the Western Roman Catholic Church and the Eastern Orthodox Church.
  • Icons: Sacred images used in Eastern Orthodox worship, held in deep reverence.
  • Indulgences: Payments made to the Church that were historically believed to reduce the time a soul spent in purgatory.
  • Patriarch of Constantinople: The leader of the Eastern Church who historically rivaled the authority of the Pope.
  • Purgatory: A state or place where souls are believed to be purified before entering heaven.
  • Seven Sacraments: Central holy rituals in Roman Catholicism, including the Eucharist.
  • Sola Fide: "Faith alone"; the Protestant belief that salvation is achieved through faith rather than works or sacraments.
  • Sola Scriptura: "Scripture alone"; the Protestant belief that the Bible is the final and sole authority on matters of faith.
  • Theosis: The Eastern Orthodox theological concept that human beings can be transformed to share in the divine nature through prayer and holy living.
  • Trinity: The central Christian doctrine of God existing as three persons in one essence.
