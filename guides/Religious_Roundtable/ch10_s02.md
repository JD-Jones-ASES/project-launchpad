# Judaism: Torah and Living Tradition Study Guide

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the fundamental principles of the Jewish faith as outlined in the text "Judaism: Torah and Living Tradition." It focuses on the relational nature of the faith, the significance of the covenant, and the distinction between practice and belief.

---

## Key Concepts and Core Themes

### The Relational Foundation of Faith
Jewish faith is characterized not by a set of definitions or propositions, but by a relationship and a name. When God reveals Himself to Moses in the Book of Exodus, the name provided—*Ehyeh asher ehyeh*—is translated as "I am that I am" or "I will be what I will be." This suggests that God is a presence to be encountered and a partner in an unfolding covenant, rather than a mere theological concept to be believed.

### The Covenant (*Brit*)
The heart of Jewish faith is the *brit*, or covenant. This binding agreement establishes mutual obligations between God and the people of Israel (*am Yisrael*).
*   **Covenant vs. Contract:** Unlike a transactional contract, which ends once services are rendered and paid for, a covenant is relational. It creates a bond that persists through time, failure, and repair. 
*   **The Sinai Agreement:** At Sinai, the covenant transformed a group of individuals into a people. The terms of the relationship are:
    *   **God’s Role:** To be Israel’s God, to guide, and to protect them.
    *   **Israel’s Role:** To follow the Torah, the divine instruction.

### The Shema and Embodied Faith
The passage from Deuteronomy 6:4-9, known as the *Shema* (meaning "Hear"), is the most important sentence in Judaism. It is a declaration of relationship rather than a creed of doctrines.
*   **Recitation:** Observant Jews recite it twice daily and traditionally before death.
*   **The Commandment to Love:** It commands followers to love God with all their heart, soul, and might.
*   **Daily Life:** The *Shema* provides instructions for daily embodiment: teaching children, discussing the words at home and on the road, and physically binding the words to the hand and doorposts.

### Orthopraxy vs. Orthodoxy
Judaism places a higher emphasis on **orthopraxy** (correct practice) than on **orthodoxy** (correct belief). 
*   **The Fundamental Question:** While Christianity often asks, "Do you believe?", Judaism asks, "What do you do?"
*   **Action as Enacted Belief:** Belief is performed through the *mitzvot* (commandments) and the treatment of the vulnerable, such as the poor, the stranger, the widow, and the orphan. 
*   **Doubt vs. Observation:** A Jew who struggles with doubt but observes the commandments is considered observant.

### Ongoing Revelation
In the Jewish tradition, revelation did not end with Moses at Sinai. It is an ongoing process:
*   **Oral Torah:** The Talmud, a compendium of rabbinic discussion, is considered part of God’s revelation.
*   **Wrestling with Text:** Each generation continues the revelation by "wrestling" with the text, a tradition linked to the ancestor Jacob, who wrestled with God to receive a blessing.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. What is the literal meaning of the Hebrew word *brit*?**
> **Answer:** It means a binding agreement with mutual obligations.

**2. How does the text distinguish between a "contract" and a "covenant"?**
> **Answer:** A contract is transactional and ends when the transaction is complete. A covenant is relational, creating a bond between persons that persists through time and failure.

**3. What are the two different translations provided for the name God gives Moses (*Ehyeh asher ehyeh*)?**
> **Answer:** "I am that I am" or "I will be what I will be."

**4. When do observant Jews traditionally recite the *Shema*?**
> **Answer:** Twice daily—upon waking and before sleeping—and as the last prayer spoken before death.

**5. Define "orthopraxy" in the context of Jewish religious thought.**
> **Answer:** Orthopraxy refers to "correct practice." It is the emphasis on what one does (observing commandments/mitzvot) rather than solely what one believes.

**6. What is the *am Yisrael*?**
> **Answer:** It means "the people of Israel," the collective identity formed through the covenant at Sinai.

**7. According to the text, how is the Oral Torah (Talmud) viewed in relation to the revelation at Sinai?**
> **Answer:** The Oral Torah is understood as being part of the same revelation at Sinai, signifying that revelation is ongoing rather than a finished event.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Architecture of Relationship:** Analyze how the concept of the *brit* (covenant) shapes the identity of *am Yisrael*. In your essay, contrast the relational nature of a covenant with the transactional nature of a contract, and explain how this relationship influences the Jewish approach to "failure and repair."

2.  **Belief through Action:** Compare and contrast the concepts of "orthodoxy" and "orthopraxy" as presented in the text. Discuss why Judaism considers a Jew who struggles with doubt but follows the *mitzvot* to be observant, and explore the implications of "enacting belief" through the treatment of the poor, the stranger, the widow, and the orphan.

3.  **The Evolution of Revelation:** Explore the Jewish perspective that revelation did not end with Moses. Discuss the role of the Talmud and the act of "wrestling with the text" in the continuation of divine instruction. How does this view of ongoing revelation affect the way the faith is practiced in every generation?

## Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Am Yisrael** | "The people of Israel"; the identity of the Jewish people as a collective bound by covenant. |
| **Brit** | The Hebrew word for covenant; a binding agreement involving mutual obligations and a persistent relationship. |
| **Ehyeh asher ehyeh** | The name of God revealed to Moses; translated as "I am that I am" or "I will be what I will be." |
| **Mitzvot** | The commandments or divine instructions that observant Jews follow. |
| **Orthodoxy** | A focus on "correct belief" or adherence to specific creeds and doctrines. |
| **Orthopraxy** | A focus on "correct practice"; the embodiment of faith through daily actions and rituals. |
| **Shema** | The central declaration of Jewish faith from Deuteronomy 6:4-9, beginning with "Hear, O Israel." |
| **Talmud** | The Oral Torah; a vast compendium of rabbinic discussion and interpretation that is considered part of ongoing revelation. |
| **Torah** | Divine instruction; the law and guidance provided by God as part of the covenant. |
