Study Guide: Descartes' Meditation VI: Mind and Body

Overview

In the sixth and final Meditation, Descartes systematically reconstructs knowledge, focusing on the existence of material things and the intricate relationship between the mind and body. He establishes that material bodies exist, distinguishes the mind as a separate substance from the body, and critically acknowledges the profound, intimate union of soul and body that defines human experience, ultimately moving from abstract reasoning to the concrete reality of the embodied human being.

Key Concepts

  • Existence of Material Things: Descartes argues for the possibility and then the probability of material things existing based on God's power to create anything clearly and distinctly perceived, and the nature of imagination which seems to require a body.
  • Imagination vs. Pure Intellection: A crucial distinction where imagination involves a "particular effort of mind" and seems to apply the mind to a body, while pure intellection (conception) allows understanding without needing a mental image, demonstrating that imagination is not essential to the mind's essence.
  • Mind-Body Distinction: The faculty of imagination is not a necessary part of the mind's essence, suggesting it depends on something external, likely a body, thus implying the mind can exist independently of it.
  • Probabilistic Argument for Body: Initially, Descartes uses the existence and nature of imagination as a strong probability for the existence of bodies, seeing it as the most convenient explanation for this faculty.
  • Role of Senses and "Nature's Teachings": Descartes recounts his initial, pre-philosophical belief in the senses, noting their vividness, the involuntary reception of ideas, and the immediate, seemingly natural judgments formed about the world and his own body (e.g., hunger leads to a desire to eat).
  • Grounds for Doubting the Senses: Descartes recalls various reasons for doubting sensory information, including optical illusions (towers appearing different from afar/near), phantom limb pain, the indistinguishability of dreams from waking life, and the general possibility of a deceiving creator or a deceptive "nature."

Important Terms

  • Pure Intellection (or Conception): The faculty of understanding or conceiving ideas clearly and distinctly using the mind alone, without relying on sensory input or mental imagery.
  • Imagination: The faculty of forming mental images; Descartes describes it as the application of the faculty of knowledge to a body immediately present to it, requiring a "particular effort of mind."
  • Chiliagon: A figure with a thousand sides. Descartes uses it as an example to differentiate imagination from pure intellection, as one can conceive of a chiliagon but cannot distinctly imagine all its sides.
  • Corporeal Things: Pertaining to the body or physical matter; material things.
  • Phantom Limb Pain: The sensation of pain in a limb that has been amputated. Descartes uses this phenomenon to illustrate how even internal sensations can be deceptive and disconnect from the actual physical body.
  • "Nature's Teachings": Descartes' term for the innate, often pre-rational, inclinations and judgments formed through sensory experience, such as the automatic desire to eat when hungry. He later questions the reliability of these teachings.

Detailed Notes

I. Reconstructing Knowledge: The Existence of Material Things and Mind-Body Distinction

Descartes embarks on the final stage of his philosophical journey, aiming to prove the existence of material things and establish the real distinction between the mind (or soul) and the body. He acknowledges that human experience is not merely abstract thought but profoundly embodied.

A. Initial Argument for the Existence of Material Things: The Role of Imagination

  1. Possibility of Material Things:

    • Descartes begins by stating he knows material things may exist because he can perceive them clearly and distinctly as objects of pure mathematics (e.g., extension, figure, motion).
    • Since God is omnipotent and capable of producing anything clearly and distinctly perceived (unless it involves a contradiction), God can create material things.

  2. Imagination as an Indicator:

    • Descartes possesses a faculty of imagination, which he uses when considering material things.
    • He defines imagination as an "application of the faculty of knowledge to the body which is immediately present to it." This definition itself suggests a connection between imagination and a body.

B. Distinguishing Imagination from Pure Intellection (Conception)

  1. The Triangle Example:

    • When imagining a triangle, Descartes not only conceives it as a three-sided figure but also apprehends these three lines as present with an "inward vision of my mind."

  2. The Chiliagon Example:

    • He can conceive a chiliagon (a thousand-sided figure) as easily as a triangle, understanding it as a figure with a thousand sides.
    • However, he cannot imagine all thousand sides distinctly as present. While he might form a confused general image, this image doesn't differentiate a chiliagon from other many-sided polygons like a myriagon.
    • This highlights that conceiving is an act of pure intellect, independent of detailed imagery.

  3. The Pentagon Example:

    • For a pentagon, he can both conceive it (intellection) and imagine it (by applying attention to each of its five sides and the enclosed space).
    • Crucially, the act of imagination requires a "particular effort of mind" that is not needed for pure intellection.

  4. Conclusion on Imagination's Nature:

    • This "particular effort" clearly demonstrates the difference between imagination and pure intellection.
    • Imagination is not a necessary element of his nature or the essence of his mind. He could still be himself without it.
    • This suggests that imagination "depends on something which differs from me" (from his mind's pure essence).
    • Descartes conjectures that if a body exists to which his mind is conjoined, then by turning towards this body, the mind could imagine corporeal objects.
    • This provides a probable, convenient explanation for imagination, but not yet a necessary proof of body's existence.

II. The Nature of Sense Perception: Past Beliefs and Grounds for Doubt

Descartes now turns to the senses, acknowledging that they present ideas (colors, sounds, pains) less distinctly than mathematical objects but often more vividly. He plans to review his past beliefs based on senses, the reasons for doubting them, and finally, what he can now believe.

A. Past Beliefs Based on Sensory Experience

  1. Awareness of One's Own Body:

    • He perceived he had a body (head, hands, feet, etc.), which he considered part or even the whole of himself.
    • He felt this body interact with others, experiencing pleasure from beneficial effects and pain from harmful ones.
    • He experienced internal appetites (hunger, thirst) and corporeal inclinations (joy, sadness, anger).

  2. Perception of External World:

    • Beyond his own body, he perceived qualities like extension, figure, motion, hardness, heat, light, color, scents, and sounds, which allowed him to distinguish objects like sky, earth, and sea.

  3. Reasons for Believing Sensory Input:

    • Involuntary Reception: Sensory ideas presented themselves without his consent; he couldn't choose what to perceive or not perceive if an object was present.
    • Vividness and Distinctness: These ideas were "much more lively, more clear, and even, in their own way, more distinct" than ideas he formed in meditation or recalled from memory, suggesting they originated from external sources.
    • Assumed Similarity: He believed the external objects were similar to the ideas they produced in his mind.
    • Body as "My Own": His body felt uniquely his because he was inseparable from it, it was the locus of his appetites and affections, and he experienced pleasure and pain in its parts, not others.
    • "Nature's Teachings" (Pre-rational Judgments): He found himself forming immediate judgments (e.g., hunger leading to desire to eat, pain leading to sadness) without conscious reasoning, as if "nature taught him so." There seemed to be no intelligible affinity between, for instance, a stomach craving and the desire to eat, yet the connection was felt.

B. Grounds for Doubting the Senses

Despite the compelling nature of sensory experience, many past experiences gradually eroded his faith in them.

  1. Deception of External Senses:

    • Optical Illusions: Towers appearing round from afar but square up close; colossal statues appearing tiny from below. These were "an infinitude of other cases" of error.

  2. Deception of Internal Senses:

    • Phantom Limb Pain: He learned that amputees sometimes felt pain in a missing limb. This proved that pain sensations were not infallibly tied to the physical presence of a specific body part, casting doubt on the certainty of even his most intimate sensations.

  3. General Grounds for Doubt (Recalled from Earlier Meditations):

    • The Dream Argument: He could not reliably distinguish waking experiences from dreams, and since dream perceptions do not come from external objects, he couldn't be certain waking perceptions did either.
    • The Deceiving God/Evil Demon Argument: Being ignorant (or supposing himself ignorant) of the author of his being, he couldn't rule out having been created in such a way that he is deceived even in matters that seem most certain.

  4. Rejection of "Nature's Teachings":

    • Given that reason often contradicted what nature seemed to teach (e.g., in the case of illusions or phantom pain), Descartes concluded that he "should not trust much to the teachings of nature." The vividness of sensory ideas alone did not guarantee their origin from, or similarity to, external objects.

Review Questions

  1. Explain Descartes' initial argument for the existence of material things, detailing how he moves from the concept of God to the faculty of imagination.
  2. How does Descartes distinguish between the mental faculties of "imagination" and "pure intellection" (conception)? Use his examples of geometrical figures like the triangle, chiliagon, and pentagon to illustrate your answer.
  3. What does Descartes conclude about the essential nature of the mind based on his analysis of imagination? How does this lead to a probabilistic argument for the existence of the body?
  4. Before introducing his philosophical doubts, what were the primary reasons Descartes (and ordinary people) intuitively believed in the reality of their own body and the external world as presented by the senses? Include examples of both internal and external sensations.
  5. What specific examples and arguments does Descartes present in Meditation VI to demonstrate the unreliability of both external and internal senses? How do these experiences, combined with his earlier doubts, lead him to question "nature's teachings"?
