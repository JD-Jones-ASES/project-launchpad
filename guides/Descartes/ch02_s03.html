Study Guide: Descartes’ Meditation I – Of Doubt

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the foundational arguments presented in the first of René Descartes' Meditations on First Philosophy. It explores the methodological transition from total doubt to the search for an unshakable foundation for knowledge.

Key Concepts and Philosophical Arguments

1. The Methodological Doubt

Descartes begins his inquiry with the realization that many of the beliefs he held from his youth were false. To establish a "firm and permanent structure in the sciences," he determines that he must rid himself of all former opinions and "commence to build anew from the foundation."

  • The Criterion for Rejection: It is not necessary to prove every opinion false. Instead, if a belief possesses any reason for doubt, it is sufficient to justify its rejection.
  • The Edifice Metaphor: Rather than examining every individual opinion, Descartes attacks the "principles" or foundations upon which all former opinions rested. If the foundation is destroyed, the entire structure of belief will collapse.

2. The Unreliability of the Senses

Descartes notes that most of what he previously accepted as true was learned through the senses. However, he observes that the senses are sometimes deceptive (e.g., concerning objects that are far away or very small). He concludes that it is "wiser not to trust entirely to anything by which we have once been deceived."

3. The Dream Argument

While one might trust senses regarding immediate, local experiences—such as sitting by a fire in a dressing gown—Descartes argues that there are no "certain indications" to distinguish wakefulness from sleep.

  • The Illusion of Presence: In dreams, one often experiences the same sensations (being dressed, sitting by a fire) that one experiences while awake, despite actually being undressed in bed.
  • The Conclusion: This "astonishment" leads Descartes to conclude that even the existence of his own body and the immediate environment can be doubted.

4. The Reality of "Simples"

Even if specific objects (hands, eyes, etc.) are imaginary or dream-like, Descartes posits that they are like paintings—composed of real "colors" or simpler elements.

  • Composite vs. Simple Sciences: Sciences like Physics, Astronomy, and Medicine, which consider "composite things," are dubious.
  • Mathematical Certainty: Sciences like Arithmetic and Geometry treat "simple and very general" things (e.g., 2+3=5; a square has four sides). These appear indubitable because they remain the same whether one is awake or asleep.

5. The Deceiver God and the Evil Genius

To push doubt to its absolute limit, Descartes considers the nature of his origins.

  • The All-Powerful God: If an all-powerful God created him, that God could have brought it to pass that there is no earth or sky, yet Descartes still perceives them. He also questions if he is being deceived every time he adds two and three.
  • The Evil Genius: To prevent himself from sliding back into old habits of belief, Descartes hypothetically imagines a "supremely powerful and clever" evil genius who employs all his energies to deceive him. Under this hypothesis, the heavens, the earth, and even Descartes’ own body (hands, flesh, blood) are considered "illusions and dreams" designed to trap his credulity.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. Why does Descartes wait until he is at a "mature" age to begin this project? He believed the enterprise was so great that he needed to reach an age where he could not hope to be better fitted for the task later in life.

2. Does Descartes feel it is necessary to prove all his former opinions are false? No. He believes that withholding assent from matters that are not "entirely certain and indubitable" is sufficient. If he finds any reason to doubt a principle, he can reject the entire foundation.

3. What is the "painting" analogy used to explain? It explains that even if our dreams represent fictitious things (like sirens or satyrs), they are formed from "real and true" elements (simples), just as a painter uses real colors to create an imaginary creature.

4. According to the text, which sciences are more certain: Physics or Geometry? Why? Geometry is more certain because it deals with very simple and general things (like shapes and numbers) without concern for whether they exist in reality, whereas Physics deals with "composite things" that are more easily doubted.

5. What is the purpose of the "Evil Genius" hypothesis? It is a mental tool used to maintain a state of doubt. It prevents Descartes from falling back into his "ancient and commonly held opinions" by pretending that all external things are illusions.

6. How does Descartes describe the difficulty of maintaining this level of doubt? He describes it as a "laborious" task and compares himself to a "captive" who enjoys an imaginary liberty in sleep and fears awakening to a difficult reality.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Architecture of Knowledge: Analyze Descartes’ use of the "foundation" and "edifice" metaphor. Why does he believe that attacking the principles of knowledge is more effective than examining individual beliefs? How does this reflect his view of the "sciences"?
  2. The Limits of Human Reason: Descartes admits that he "cannot doubt" that God permits him to be deceived sometimes. Explore the tension between the concept of a "supremely good" God and the existence of human error as presented in Meditation I.
  3. The Dream Argument vs. The Evil Genius: Compare and contrast the levels of doubt reached by the Dream Argument and the Evil Genius hypothesis. What specific certainties does the Evil Genius call into question that the Dream Argument does not?
  4. Habit and Belief: Descartes notes that "long and familiar custom" makes his old opinions return against his inclination. Discuss the role of "set purpose" and "pretending" in Descartes' methodology. Is his doubt a purely intellectual exercise, or is it a psychological struggle?

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Assent The act of agreeing to or accepting something as true; Descartes resolves to withhold this from anything uncertain.
Composite Things Complex subjects of study, such as those found in Physics or Medicine, which are considered dubious because they rely on the existence of external objects.
Corporeal Nature The nature of physical bodies; characterized by extension, quantity, and existence in time and space.
Evil Genius A hypothetical, supremely powerful, and deceitful being used by Descartes to test the limits of what can be known for certain.
Extension The property of taking up space; a primary characteristic of physical objects that Descartes considers a "simple" and "universal" truth.
Indubitable That which cannot be doubted; the standard of certainty Descartes seeks for his new foundation of knowledge.
Simples The most basic, universal elements of reality (e.g., figure, number, magnitude) that form the basis of mathematical truths.
Universal Upheaval Descartes' term for the total rejection and reassessment of his former opinions and the foundations of his knowledge.
