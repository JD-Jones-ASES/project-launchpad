Study Guide: Descartes' Meditation V: God Again, and the Nature of Matter

Overview

In Meditation V, René Descartes addresses two core philosophical questions: the inherent nature of material things and a second, distinct proof for God's existence. He argues that the answers to both inquiries are interconnected, fundamentally relying on the human mind's unique capacity to know the unchanging "essences" of things through reason alone, independent of sensory experience.

Key Concepts

  • Essences of Material Things: Descartes contends that the mind can clearly and distinctly grasp the fundamental properties of material objects, such as extension (length, breadth, depth), quantity, figure, and movement. These are not learned from sensory experience but are innate truths discoverable by reason.
  • True and Immutable Natures: These are ideas that possess eternal, unchanging properties inherent to their concept, like the properties of a triangle (e.g., its angles sum to two right angles). Descartes argues these are neither invented by the mind nor derived from the senses but are discovered as objective, necessary truths present within thought.
  • The Ontological Argument for God's Existence: Descartes presents a second proof for God based on the idea of God as a "Supremely Perfect Being." Since existence is a perfection, it is as inseparable from the essence of a Supremely Perfect Being as the sum of its angles is from the essence of a triangle. Therefore, God must necessarily exist.
  • Inseparability of Essence and Existence in God: Unlike other concepts where existence can be separated from essence (e.g., a mountain without a valley), Descartes argues that God's unique nature as a supremely perfect being necessitates existence. This necessity is inherent to God Himself, not imposed by the meditator's thought.
  • Clarity and Distinctness as the Criterion for Truth: Throughout his argument, Descartes reinforces that any idea or truth perceived "clearly and distinctly" by the mind must be true. He places mathematical and geometric truths, and especially God's existence, in this category, asserting they are known with the highest degree of certainty.

Important Terms

  • Continuous Quantity / Extension: A fundamental, primary attribute of material things, referring to their spatial properties of length, breadth, and depth. Descartes sees this as distinctly knowable by the mind.
  • Essence: The fundamental, intrinsic nature or "whatness" of a thing, which defines its identity and properties. Descartes argues these essences are knowable by reason.
  • True and Immutable Natures: Ideas or concepts (especially mathematical and geometric ones) whose properties are eternal, unchanging, and necessarily true, independent of whether they exist physically or are invented by the mind.
  • Supremely Perfect Being: Descartes' conception of God, understood as possessing all possible perfections, including existence.
  • Ontological Argument: A type of philosophical argument for the existence of God that proceeds from the definition or concept of God alone, typically by arguing that existence is a necessary attribute of a perfect being.
  • Clarity and Distinctness: Descartes' epistemological criterion for certainty. A clear idea is present and manifest to the attentive mind, while a distinct idea is clear and also precisely separated from all other ideas.
  • Sophism: A subtly false or misleading argument, especially one used to deceive. Descartes uses this term to refer to potential objections that might seem logical but are flawed upon closer examination.
  • Factitious: Something artificial or made up; not natural or genuine. Descartes distinguishes the idea of God as a true and immutable nature from "factitious" ideas.

Detailed Notes

I. Re-entering the Doubt and the Task Ahead

  • Descartes begins Meditation V seeking to emerge from doubt, focusing on two primary investigations:
    1. What certain knowledge can be had about material things?
    2. Can God's existence be proven again, by a different route?
  • He notes that these questions are linked, both resting on the mind's capacity to grasp essences through reason.
  • Before examining whether objects exist outside him, he first considers the ideas of them within his thought, distinguishing between distinct and confused ideas.

II. The Nature of Material Things and True & Immutable Natures

  • Distinct Ideas of Quantity: Descartes finds he can distinctly imagine "continuous quantity," which philosophers call "extension" – length, breadth, and depth.
  • He can further attribute to this quantity many properties: different parts, size, figure, situation, local movement, and degrees of duration.
  • These properties are known with profound distinctness, both generally and in countless particulars (numbers, figures, movements).
  • Knowledge as Recollection: When he discovers these truths, it feels not like learning something new, but like recollecting something already present in his mind, though he hadn't consciously attended to it before.
  • Ideas Not Invented or Sensory:
    • Descartes emphasizes that he discovers "an infinitude of ideas of certain things which cannot be esteemed as pure negations."
    • These ideas are not framed by him (not invented), though he can choose to think of them or not.
    • They possess "natures which are true and immutable."
    • The Triangle Example: When he imagines a triangle, even if no physical triangle exists, it has a determinate, immutable, and eternal nature or essence. He hasn't invented its properties (e.g., its three angles equal two right angles; the greatest side is subtended by the greatest angle). He simply discovers these inherent truths.
    • Refutation of Sensory Origin: The idea of a triangle (and other figures) cannot come from the senses, because he can form in his mind an "infinitude of other figures" that have never been objects of sense, yet he can still demonstrate their properties clearly and distinctly.
    • Truth and Clarity: All that is conceived clearly and distinctly is true. This principle holds for mathematical truths, which even when he was strongly attached to sensory objects, he considered most certain.

III. The Ontological Argument for God's Existence (Second Proof)

  • Transition from Figures to God: If clearly and distinctly perceived properties truly belong to an object's idea, then this principle should apply to the idea of God.
  • The Idea of God: Descartes finds the idea of God – a "supremely perfect Being" – within himself as clearly and distinctly as the idea of any figure or number.
  • Existence as a Perfection: He knows no less clearly and distinctly that "actual and eternal existence pertains to this nature" than he knows properties belong to a figure.
  • Initial Apparent Sophism: At first, it might seem like a "sophism" because we usually distinguish existence from essence in other things. This might lead one to believe God's existence can be separated from His essence.
  • Descartes' Refutation of the Sophism:
    • Inseparability: Upon closer attention, Descartes clearly sees that existence is no more separable from God's essence than having three angles equal to two right angles is from a triangle's essence, or a valley from a mountain.
    • Repugnance: It is "repugnant" (contradictory) to conceive of a Supremely Perfect Being to whom a perfection (existence) is lacking.
    • Addressing the Mountain/Valley Analogy: The objection that conceiving a mountain with a valley doesn't mean it exists is flawed. For the mountain/valley, it only means they are inseparable if they exist. For God, the case is different.
    • Necessity in the Thing Itself: His thought does not impose necessity on things. Instead, the "necessity which lies in the thing itself," i.e., the necessity of God's existence, determines him to think this way. He can imagine a horse with or without wings, but he cannot conceive of a God (a supremely perfect Being) without existence (devoid of a supreme perfection).
  • Refutation of Conditional Necessity Objection:
    • Objection: It's only necessary to admit God exists if one presupposes He has all perfections (including existence), but the initial supposition isn't necessary. (Analogy: assuming all quadrilaterals can be inscribed in a circle, then a rhombus must be, which is false).
    • Descartes' Reply: While it's not necessary to always think of God, whenever he does consider a "first and sovereign Being," he must attribute every perfection to Him, including existence. This necessity is inherent to the idea of God itself, unlike the false supposition about quadrilaterals, which cannot be clearly and distinctly conceived.
    • The idea of God is "not something factitious," but an "image of a true and immutable nature."

IV. Further Confirmation of God's Nature

  • Descartes identifies several other ways in which the idea of God is unique and inherently true:
    1. Only God's essence necessarily includes existence.
    2. It's impossible to conceive of two or more such supremely perfect Gods.
    3. Such a God must have existed eternally and must exist eternally.
    4. He knows an "infinitude of other properties in God," none of which he can diminish or change.

V. The Enduring Primacy of Clarity and Distinctness

  • Descartes reiterates that only things conceived "clearly and distinctly" have the power to persuade him entirely.
  • Some truths are obvious, others require closer attention (e.g., the Pythagorean theorem), but once discovered, they are equally certain.
  • He concludes that if his mind were not clouded by prejudices and sensory distractions, there would be nothing he could know "more immediately and more easily" than God's existence, as it is the most manifest truth that a Supreme Being necessarily possesses existence.

Review Questions

  1. How does Descartes connect his investigation into the nature of material things with his second proof for God's existence in Meditation V? What common principle underlies both inquiries?
  2. Explain what Descartes means by "true and immutable natures," using the example of a triangle. How does he argue that these natures are not derived from the senses or invented by the mind?
  3. Outline Descartes' Ontological Argument for God's existence as presented in this section. What are its main premises and conclusion?
  4. Descartes addresses several "sophisms" or objections to his ontological argument. Choose one such objection and explain how Descartes refutes it, particularly focusing on the difference between God's essence and that of other things.
  5. Why does Descartes emphasize the concept of "clarity and distinctness" repeatedly in this Meditation? How does it serve as his ultimate criterion for truth, especially concerning God's existence, and why does he suggest God's existence should be the most manifest truth?
