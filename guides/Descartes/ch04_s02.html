Chapter 4, Section 2: Meditation III: The Existence of God

Overview

This section introduces the philosophical tradition of natural theology, which seeks to prove God's existence through human reason rather than revelation. It primarily focuses on the contributions of Thomas Aquinas, whose a posteriori arguments (known as the Five Ways) serve as a foundational background for understanding later philosophers like Descartes. The text delves into Aquinas's specific arguments, including his reasoning on why God's existence, while self-evident in itself, is not self-evident to human beings, and then details his First and Second Ways to demonstrate God's existence.

Key Concepts

  • Natural Theology: The philosophical enterprise of proving God's existence and attributes using human reason and observation of the natural world, without relying on religious revelation or scripture.
  • A Posteriori Proofs: Arguments for God's existence that derive knowledge from experience, observation, and empirical evidence about the world. Thomas Aquinas's Five Ways are prime examples.
  • A Priori Proofs: Arguments for God's existence that derive knowledge from reason alone, independent of sensory experience, typically starting from a concept or definition of God. Anselm's ontological argument (which Descartes further develops) is an example.
  • Aquinas's Five Ways: Thomas Aquinas's five distinct arguments for the existence of God, all of which are a posteriori and begin from an observable feature of the natural world, arguing that God must be the ultimate explanation for that feature. These include arguments from motion, efficient causation, contingency, degrees of perfection, and design.
  • Self-Evidence (in Aquinas): Aquinas distinguishes between something being "self-evident in itself" (where the predicate is contained in the essence of the subject, as "God is His own existence") and "self-evident to us" (where we fully understand the essence of both subject and predicate). God's existence is self-evident in itself but not to us, because we do not fully grasp God's essence.

Important Terms

  • A posteriori: (Latin for "from what comes after") Knowledge that depends on experience, derived from observation of the world.
  • A priori: (Latin for "from what comes before") Knowledge independent of experience, derived from reason alone.
  • Natural Theology: The attempt to prove God's existence and characteristics through reason and observation of nature.
  • Efficient Causation: The agent or force that brings something into being or causes a change. In Aquinas's Second Way, it refers to the sequence of causes and effects in the world.
  • Contingency: The quality of being possible but not necessary; something that could either exist or not exist. Aquinas's Third Way argues from the contingency of things to a necessary being.
  • Substance: (Descartes' definition) That which exists in such a way as to depend on nothing else for its existence.
  • The Philosopher: Medieval term for Aristotle, frequently used by Aquinas in the Summa Theologica.
  • The fool: A reference from Psalm 14:1, "The fool says in his heart, 'There is no God'," used by Anselm and Aquinas to represent someone who denies God's existence.

Detailed Notes

I. Introduction to Natural Theology and Descartes's Background

  • Tradition of Reasoned Proof for God: Descartes, in Meditation III, is part of a long tradition of philosophers who argue for God's existence through human reason, independent of scripture or revelation. This tradition is known as natural theology.
  • Historical Precursors:
    • Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274): A Dominican friar and philosopher, most famous for his Summa Theologica. He developed systematic a posteriori proofs for God's existence, based on observable facts about the world.
    • Anselm of Canterbury: Pioneered a purely rational, a priori proof from the concept of God, which Descartes would later develop.
  • Descartes's Focus: Descartes argues that the very idea of an infinite, perfect being in our minds demands a divine cause.

II. Aquinas's Five Ways: An A Posteriori Approach

  • Structure: All five of Aquinas's arguments for God's existence share a common structure:
    1. They begin from an observable feature of the natural world.
    2. They argue that an infinite regress of explanations for this feature is impossible.
    3. They conclude that God must exist as the ultimate, non-regressing explanation.
  • The Five Ways:
    1. From Motion: Everything that moves is moved by something else; an infinite regress of movers is impossible; therefore, there must be an unmoved first mover, which is God.
    2. From Efficient Causation: Nothing can be the efficient cause of itself; a chain of causes cannot regress to infinity; therefore, there must be a first uncaused cause, which is God.
    3. From Contingency: All observable things could possibly not exist; if everything could fail to exist, there would be a moment when nothing existed (which contradicts present existence); therefore, something must exist necessarily, and this is God.
    4. From Degrees of Perfection: We observe things that are more or less good, true, or noble; things that admit of degrees require a maximum standard; this maximum standard is God.
    5. From Design: Natural things lacking intelligence (e.g., plants) behave for ends (e.g., growth), as if directed by knowledge; this intelligent direction requires an intelligent being, which is God.

III. Aquinas: Whether the Existence of God Is Self-Evident? (Summa Theologica I, Q. 2, Art. 1)

  • Objections that God's Existence is Self-Evident:
    1. Natural Implantation: Knowledge of God is naturally implanted in all (Damascene).
    2. Anselm's Ontological Argument: The definition of "God" (that than which nothing greater can be conceived) implies actual existence, because existing actually is greater than existing only mentally.
    3. Truth: The existence of truth is self-evident, and God is truth itself.
  • On the Contrary: The "fool" (from Psalm 14:1) can mentally admit the opposite ("There is no God"), implying it's not self-evident.
  • Aquinas's Answer:
    • Two Ways for Self-Evidence: A proposition can be self-evident "in itself" or "to us."
      • Self-evident in itself: The predicate is included in the essence of the subject (e.g., "Man is an animal"). This is true for God's existence because "God is His own existence."
      • Self-evident to us: We know the essence of both the predicate and the subject.
    • God's Existence Not Self-Evident to Us: Because we do not know the essence of God directly, the proposition "God exists" is not self-evident to us. It therefore needs to be demonstrated from things more known to us (i.e., God's effects in the world).
  • Replies to Objections:
    1. Reply to Obj. 1 (Natural Implantation): We have a general and confused natural knowledge that God exists as our beatitude (happiness), as we naturally desire happiness. But this is not knowing absolutely that God exists, just as knowing someone is approaching isn't knowing Peter is approaching. People mistake happiness for riches, pleasures, etc., not necessarily God.
    2. Reply to Obj. 2 (Anselm's Argument):
      • Not everyone understands "God" as "that than which nothing greater can be thought" (some believe God is a body).
      • Even if one understands the definition, it only implies mental existence, not actual existence, unless one already admits that something than which nothing greater can be thought actually exists (which is precisely what the denier denies).
    3. Reply to Obj. 3 (Truth): The existence of truth in general is self-evident, but the existence of a Primal Truth (which is God) is not self-evident to us.

IV. Aquinas: Whether God Exists? (Summa Theologica I, Q. 2, Art. 3)

  • Objections that God Does Not Exist:
    1. Problem of Evil: If God is infinite goodness, evil would not exist. But there is evil in the world, therefore God does not exist.
    2. Sufficiency of Natural Explanations: Everything can be accounted for by other principles without God: natural things by nature, voluntary things by human reason/will. Therefore, God's existence is superfluous.
  • On the Contrary: Scriptural reference: "I am Who am" (Exodus 3:14), implying God's self-existence.
  • Aquinas's Answer (Introduction to the Five Ways): The existence of God can be proved in five ways, all arguing from effects (observable phenomena) to their cause (God).
    • The First Way: From Motion:
      • Observation: We perceive things in motion.
      • Principle: Whatever is in motion is put in motion by another. (A thing moves only when reduced from potentiality to actuality by something already in actuality. Nothing can be simultaneously in potentiality and actuality in the same respect, so nothing moves itself.)
      • No Infinite Regress: This chain of movers cannot go on to infinity, because if there were no first mover, there would be no subsequent movers (e.g., a staff moves only if moved by a hand).
      • Conclusion: Therefore, it is necessary to arrive at a first mover, put in motion by no other, and this is God.
    • The Second Way: From the Nature of the Efficient Cause:
      • Observation: We find an order of efficient causes in the world of sense.
      • Principle: Nothing is the efficient cause of itself (it would have to be prior to itself, which is impossible).
      • No Infinite Regress: In efficient causes, it is not possible to go on to infinity. If there were no first efficient cause, there would be no intermediate or ultimate causes (to remove the cause is to remove the effect).
      • Conclusion: Therefore, it is necessary to posit a first efficient cause, which everyone calls God.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast the approaches of Thomas Aquinas and Anselm of Canterbury in demonstrating God's existence. What is the fundamental difference between a posteriori and a priori arguments, and how do these philosophers exemplify each?
  2. Summarize the common structural elements shared by all five of Aquinas's Ways to prove God's existence. Why are these arguments classified as a posteriori?
  3. Explain Aquinas's distinction between something being "self-evident in itself" versus "self-evident to us." How does this distinction apply to the proposition "God exists," and why does Aquinas conclude that God's existence needs to be demonstrated rather than merely affirmed?
  4. How does Aquinas address the objection (from Anselm's ontological argument) that simply understanding the definition of "God" as "that than which nothing greater can be conceived" implies God's actual existence? What crucial point does Aquinas make about mental versus actual existence?
  5. Detail Aquinas's First and Second Ways for proving God's existence, as presented in Summa Theologica I, Q. 2, Art. 3. What observable phenomena do they start from, what principles do they rely on, and how do they lead to the conclusion of God's existence?
