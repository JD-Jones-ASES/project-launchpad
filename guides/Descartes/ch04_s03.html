Study Guide: Descartes' Meditation III – The Existence of God (Part 1)

Overview

In Meditation III, René Descartes embarks on the crucial task of proving God's existence. Having established his own existence as a thinking being (the cogito), he recognizes that the certainty of this truth, and indeed any truth, rests on the principle that whatever he perceives "clearly and distinctly" is true. To validate this foundational principle and eliminate the radical doubt posed by a potential "deceiver God," Descartes must demonstrate that God exists and is not a deceiver. This section explores his initial steps in this proof, examining the nature and origins of his own thoughts to identify potential sources of error.

Key Concepts

  • The Problem of Certainty Beyond the Cogito: While Descartes is certain he exists as a thinking thing, the reason for this certainty (clear and distinct perception) itself needs a guarantee to extend to other truths, especially against the possibility of a powerful deceiver.
  • Clear and Distinct Perception as a Criterion for Truth: Descartes' provisional rule states that all things he perceives very clearly and very distinctly are true. This rule, however, is vulnerable if a God or powerful entity could deceive him even in such matters.
  • God as the Guarantor of Truth: For Descartes, only a benevolent God, one who is not a deceiver, can ultimately assure the reliability of his clear and distinct perceptions and thus validate objective knowledge.
  • Classification of Thoughts: Descartes categorizes his mental states into three kinds: ideas (images), volitions/affections (acts of will), and judgments (affirmations/denials).
  • Error Resides in Judgments: He argues that ideas and volitions themselves cannot be false, as it is always true that one has them. Error primarily enters when one judges that an idea in the mind corresponds to or resembles an external reality.
  • Types of Ideas: Ideas can be innate (from within one's own nature), adventitious (seemingly from outside via the senses), or fabricated/invented (created by the mind).
  • Critique of Common Beliefs about External Objects: Descartes systematically challenges the conventional reasons for believing that adventitious ideas are caused by, and resemble, external objects, finding them based on unreliable impulses rather than rational proof.

Important Terms

  • Cogito (Cogito Ergo Sum): Latin for "I think, therefore I am." Descartes' first undeniable truth, establishing his existence as a thinking substance.
  • Clear and Distinct Perception: The standard of truth for Descartes; a perception is clear when it is present and manifest to the attentive mind, and distinct when it is so precisely and sharply separated from all other perceptions that it contains nothing but what is clear.
  • Deceiver God (or Evil Demon): A hypothetical, omnipotent being capable of deceiving Descartes about everything, even seemingly obvious truths like mathematical axioms or his own existence. The possibility of such a being drives Descartes' search for God.
  • Idea (Cartesian sense): A thought, image, or representation in the mind. Descartes considers them as modes of thought, not necessarily having external referents.
  • Volitions/Affections: Acts of the will, such as willing, desiring, fearing, or affirming/denying. These are modes of thought that add something to a simple idea.
  • Judgments: Mental acts of affirming or denying something about an idea. According to Descartes, this is the primary source of human error when the mind mistakenly asserts a correspondence between an idea and an external reality.
  • Innate Ideas: Ideas that are inherent to the mind itself, not derived from experience or fabrication (e.g., the ideas of "thing," "truth," "God").
  • Adventitious Ideas: Ideas that appear to come from outside the mind through the senses (e.g., perceptions of light, sound, heat).
  • Fabricated/Invented Ideas: Ideas constructed by the mind from other ideas (e.g., chimeras, hippogriffs).
  • Natural Light (Lumen Naturale): An infallible faculty of reason that allows one to recognize certain truths immediately and without doubt (e.g., that one cannot be thinking without existing).
  • Natural Impulse/Inclination: A spontaneous, uncritical tendency to believe something, often based on sensory experience or habit. Descartes argues this is an unreliable guide to truth and can lead to error.

Detailed Notes

The Need for God's Existence

  • Starting Point: The Cogito's Limitation: Descartes has proven he exists as a thinking thing. This certainty relies on the principle that "what I perceive clearly and distinctly is true."
  • The Deceiver God Problem: However, this general rule itself is under threat from the possibility of a powerful, deceptive God (or evil demon) who could make him believe even clear and distinct perceptions are false.
  • The Goal of Meditation III: To fully remove this metaphysical doubt, Descartes must inquire whether God exists and, if so, whether this God is a deceiver. Only a non-deceiving God can guarantee the truth of clear and distinct perceptions.

Re-examining the Nature of Self and Knowledge

  • What I Know: Descartes reiterates his nature as a thinking thing – one that doubts, affirms, denies, understands, ignores, wills, desires, imagines, and perceives. He is certain these "modes of thought" reside within him.
  • Establishing the General Rule: From the certainty of the cogito, he deduces that the criterion for truth must be clear and distinct perception. If something conceived so clearly and distinctly could be false, then even his own existence would be uncertain.
  • Previous Errors: He recalls previously believing sensory perceptions (earth, sky, etc.) were certain. He now realizes his error wasn't in having the ideas, but in judging that these ideas were similar to external objects from which they proceeded.
  • Mathematical Truths and the Deceiver: Even simple arithmetic (e.g., 2+3=5) can be doubted if a God might have created him such that he errs even in the most manifest things. This is a "metaphysical" doubt that needs resolution.
  • Provisional Trust in Clear and Distinct Perceptions: When directly attending to clear and distinct perceptions, he feels compelled to believe them true. But the root of doubt (the potential deceiver) lingers unless God's existence and benevolence are proven.

Classifying Thoughts and Identifying Sources of Error

Descartes divides his thoughts into different kinds to methodically search for truth or error.

  1. Ideas:

    • Definition: "Images of things" (e.g., man, chimera, heaven, angel, God).
    • Truth/Falsity: Ideas in themselves, considered merely as modes of thought, cannot be false. It is always true that he has an idea, regardless of whether it represents something real.

  2. Volitions or Affections:

    • Definition: Thoughts involving willing, fearing, approving, denying. These add an act of the mind to an idea.
    • Truth/Falsity: Like ideas, volitions cannot be false in themselves. It is true that he desires, fears, etc., even if the object of the desire/fear doesn't exist or is evil.

  3. Judgments:

    • Definition: Affirming or denying something about an idea (e.g., "the idea of a man corresponds to an external man").
    • Primary Source of Error: Judgments are the only class of thoughts where error can properly reside. The most common error is judging that ideas in the mind are "similar or conformable to things which are outside me." If he considered ideas only as modes of thought, without relating them to anything external, he would have little material for error.

The Origin of Ideas

Descartes then considers the possible origins of his ideas:

  1. Innate Ideas: Ideas that seem to be born with him, derived from his own nature (e.g., the power of understanding what a "thing" or "truth" is).
  2. Adventitious Ideas: Ideas that seem to come from outside, through his senses (e.g., hearing a sound, seeing the sun, feeling heat).
  3. Fabricated/Invented Ideas: Ideas formed or constructed by himself (e.g., sirens, hippogriffs).
  • Uncertainty of Origin: He acknowledges that he has not yet "clearly discovered their true origin," meaning he cannot be certain if an idea he perceives as adventitious isn't actually innate or fabricated.

Critiquing Reasons for Belief in External Objects

Descartes examines the common reasons for believing that adventitious ideas come from external objects and finds them insufficient:

  1. "Taught by Nature":

    • The Claim: A "spontaneous inclination" impels him to believe that ideas (like heat) come from external things (like fire).
    • Descartes' Rejection: He distinguishes between "natural light" (reason, which is infallible) and "natural impulse/inclination" (a spontaneous tendency, which is often unreliable). Natural impulses have led him to poor choices in matters of virtue and vice, so they cannot be trusted as guides to truth.

  2. Ideas Do Not Depend on My Will:

    • The Claim: Ideas often present themselves to his mind against his will (e.g., he feels heat whether he wants to or not), suggesting an external cause.
    • Descartes' Rejection: Just as natural impulses exist independently of his will, it's possible that an unknown faculty within him could produce these ideas without external assistance (much like ideas in dreams are produced internally). This argument does not definitively prove an external cause.

  3. Ideas Resemble External Objects:

    • The Claim: It's intuitive to believe that objects imprint their likeness upon the mind.
    • Descartes' Rejection: Even if ideas proceeded from external objects, it doesn't necessarily mean they resemble them. He points to the example of the sun:
      • Sensory Idea: The sun appears extremely small (an adventitious idea).
      • Astronomical Idea: Reason (derived from innate notions or formed by him) dictates the sun is several times larger than the Earth.
      • The Conflict: These two ideas cannot both resemble the same sun. Reason suggests the sensory idea, despite seeming direct, is less similar to the actual object.
    • Conclusion: This shows that his belief in external objects and their resemblance to his ideas has been based on "blind impulse," not certain judgment.

Review Questions

  1. Why is proving the existence of God, who is not a deceiver, an indispensable step for Descartes after he has already established his own existence through the cogito?
  2. Descartes distinguishes between "natural light" and "natural impulse." Explain the difference between these two and why he considers one a reliable guide to truth while the other is not.
  3. What are the three main kinds of thoughts Descartes identifies (ideas, volitions/affections, judgments), and which of these is the primary source of error? Provide an example of how error arises from this specific kind of thought.
  4. Describe Descartes' classification of ideas into innate, adventitious, and fabricated. Why does he conclude, at this stage of Meditation III, that he cannot yet be certain of the true origin of his ideas?
  5. Descartes critically examines two common reasons for believing that adventitious ideas come from external objects (i.e., being "taught by nature" and ideas not depending on one's will). Summarize his arguments against these reasons and explain why he finds them unconvincing proofs for external reality.
