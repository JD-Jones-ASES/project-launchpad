Study Guide: Meditation VI: Mind and Body

Overview

Meditation VI marks Descartes' return to the physical world after his methodical doubt, using his established proofs of God's existence and non-deception to affirm the reality of external objects. This section also introduces his famous argument for the "real distinction" between mind and body, positing them as two fundamentally different substances. While affirming this distinction, Descartes also grapples with how these two distinct substances can be intimately united in a human being, leading to the profound "interaction problem."

Key Concepts

  • Recovery of the External World: Descartes uses God's non-deceiving nature to prove the existence of an external world. Our strong natural inclination to believe that sensory ideas come from external objects, combined with God's goodness, ensures that such a world truly exists, even if our senses don't perfectly represent its exact nature.
  • The Real Distinction: This is Descartes' argument that mind and body are two distinct substances. He reasons that because he can clearly and distinctly conceive of his mind without a body, and a body without a mind, God could make them exist independently. Therefore, they are "really distinct" substances.
  • Substance Dualism (Cartesian Dualism): The philosophical view that reality contains two fundamentally different kinds of substances: mental substance (mind or res cogitans) and material substance (body or res extensa). The mind is a thinking, non-extended substance, while the body is an extended, non-thinking substance.
  • Mind (Res Cogitans): Defined as a thinking substance. It encompasses thought, doubt, will, imagination, feeling, and perception. Crucially, it has no spatial properties (no location, size, or shape) and is indivisible.
  • Body (Res Extensa): Defined as an extended substance. It occupies space, has shape and size, is divisible, and is incapable of thought.
  • Union of Mind and Body: Despite their real distinction, Descartes insists that mind and body are intimately united in a human being. This union is evident in sensory experiences like pain, hunger, and pleasure, which are felt by the mind due to bodily states, demonstrating a profound connection beyond a mere tool-user relationship.
  • The Interaction Problem: The central difficulty arising from Cartesian dualism: how can an immaterial, non-extended mind causally interact with a material, extended body? Descartes proposed the pineal gland as the site of interaction, but this merely re-locates the mystery without fully explaining the mechanism.
  • Correction of the Senses: Descartes concludes that while the senses are not reliable for philosophical precision or uncovering the deep nature of reality, they are trustworthy for practical purposes such as survival, navigating the world, and avoiding harm. He distinguishes waking from dreaming by the coherence and intelligibility of waking experience.

Important Terms

  • Real Distinction: Descartes' argument that mind and body are two fundamentally different kinds of substance that can exist independently of each other.
  • Substance Dualism: The philosophical position that reality consists of two distinct kinds of substance: mental (mind, soul) and material (body). Often called "Cartesian Dualism."
  • Cartesian Dualism: Refers specifically to Descartes' two-substance view of mind (res cogitans) and body (res extensa), characterized by their radical difference and real distinction.
  • Res Cogitans: Latin for "thinking thing"; Descartes' term for the mind or mental substance, which thinks, doubts, wills, and perceives, but has no spatial extension.
  • Res Extensa: Latin for "extended thing"; Descartes' term for the body or material substance, which occupies space, has shape, and is divisible, but does not think.
  • Cartesian: Of or relating to René Descartes or his philosophy.
  • Pineal Gland: A small structure in the brain that Descartes identified as the point of interaction between the immaterial mind (soul) and the material body.
  • Interaction Problem: The persistent philosophical challenge of explaining how an immaterial, non-extended mind can causally influence, and be influenced by, a material, extended body.
  • Gnosticism: A family of ancient religious movements often characterized by the belief that the material world is evil or an illusion, and the soul must escape the body. Descartes' dualism is distinct from Gnosticism because he emphasizes the intimate union and practical utility of the body.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Meditation VI: Mind and Body

  • Meditation VI is presented as a return to the world of bodies, senses, and the self, after the previous meditations established the existence of God and the self as a thinking thing.
  • Descartes' conclusions here are highly influential and often controversial in philosophy.

Recovering the External World

  • The Core Proof:
    1. Descartes has already proved God exists and is not a deceiver.
    2. He has a strong, natural, and irresistible inclination to believe that his sensory ideas are caused by external material objects.
    3. If this inclination were a systematic deception (i.e., no external objects exist), then God would have given him an inclination leading to systematic error without a means to correct it.
    4. But God is not a deceiver.
    5. Therefore, external material objects must exist and cause his sensory ideas.
  • Qualification on Sensory Representation:
    • Descartes clarifies that the senses provide practical information, not philosophically precise knowledge.
    • Colors, sounds, and smells might not exist in objects exactly as perceived.
    • What is certain is the existence of extended, material things that cause sensory experiences. Their exact nature requires scientific investigation, not just sensory input.
  • Dependence on God: This proof entirely relies on Descartes' prior proofs of God's existence and non-deception. Without a non-deceiving God, there's no guarantee that our natural inclinations align with reality. This highlights the crucial order of the Meditations.

The Real Distinction Between Mind and Body

  • This is the most significant philosophical claim in Meditation VI, leading to substance dualism.
  • Descartes' Argument:
    1. He can clearly and distinctly conceive of his mind (a thinking, doubting, willing, perceiving self) without conceiving of any body.
    2. He can clearly and distinctly conceive of bodies (extended, shaped, moving things) without conceiving of any thinking substance.
    3. Whatever he can clearly and distinctly conceive, God can bring about.
    4. Therefore, God could make his mind exist independently of his body, and vice versa.
    5. If two things can exist independently of each other, they are "really distinct" substances.
    6. Therefore, mind and body are really distinct substances.
  • Principle: This argument rests on the idea that conceivability implies possibility, especially for an omnipotent God.

Substance Dualism (Cartesian Dualism)

  • Definition: The philosophical view that reality consists of two fundamentally different kinds of substance.
  • Mind (Res Cogitans):
    • A thinking substance (doubts, wills, imagines, feels, perceives).
    • Has no spatial location, size, or shape.
    • Is indivisible.
  • Body (Res Extensa):
    • An extended substance (occupies space, has shape and size).
    • Is divisible.
    • Does not think.
  • The Human Being: On Descartes' account, a human is a union of these two radically different substances.

The Union of Mind and Body

  • Despite their real distinction, Descartes is not an "extreme" dualist; he emphasizes their intimate union.
  • Evidence of Union:
    • The mind doesn't just "use" the body as a sailor uses a ship; the body's distress (pain, hunger, thirst) directly causes discomfort in the mind.
    • These are not purely intellectual reports but "felt, urgent, embodied experiences."
  • Mechanism of Interaction (Proposed): Descartes proposed the pineal gland, a small, unpaired structure at the center of the brain, as the point of contact where the immaterial soul and material body interact.

The Interaction Problem

  • The Deepest Difficulty: How can two radically different substances – an immaterial, non-extended mind and a material, extended body – causally interact?
    • How can a mind with no spatial location move a physical body?
    • How can physical events in the brain cause non-physical sensations or thoughts in the mind?
  • Descartes' Acknowledgment: He recognized the problem but his proposal of the pineal gland only relocated the mystery without fully resolving the "how." An non-extended mind cannot be "at" a spatial location in the ordinary sense.

The Correction of the Senses

  • Practical Reliability: Descartes concludes that while senses are unreliable for philosophical precision, they are perfectly reliable for practical purposes:
    • Survival.
    • Navigation.
    • Avoiding immediate harm (e.g., pain warns away from danger).
  • Distinguishing Waking from Dreaming:
    • Descartes appeals to the coherence of experience.
    • Dreams are typically disjointed, illogical, and fail to cohere with memory and reason.
    • Waking experience forms a connected, intelligible whole that fits with memories and rational understanding. This coherence is the practical proof of being awake.

Clarification: Descartes Not Gnostic

  • Important Distinction: Descartes does not view the body as unimportant, evil, or a prison for the soul.
  • He affirms the intimate union of mind and body as crucial to human experience.
  • Sensations and emotions, arising from this union, are real features of the human composite, not illusions to be transcended.

Review Questions

  1. How does Descartes use his prior proofs of God's existence and non-deception to establish the existence of the external world? What are the limitations he places on the reliability of the senses in this context?
  2. Explain Descartes' argument for the "real distinction" between mind and body. What principle does this argument rely upon regarding conceivability and possibility?
  3. Define "Cartesian Dualism" by describing the characteristics of res cogitans (mind) and res extensa (body). Provide examples of properties unique to each.
  4. Descartes argues for both the "real distinction" of mind and body and their "intimate union." How does he reconcile these two seemingly contradictory claims, and what evidence does he offer for the intimate union?
  5. What is the "interaction problem," and why is it considered the deepest difficulty for Cartesian dualism? How did Descartes attempt to address it, and why did his solution largely fail to satisfy critics?
