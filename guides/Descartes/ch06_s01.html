CH06_APPROACH: Meditation V Study Guide

Overview

Meditation V is a pivotal and challenging section where Descartes tackles two major philosophical tasks: defining the fundamental nature of matter as pure extension and presenting a second, independent proof for God's existence. Both accomplishments are rooted in Descartes' core methodology – the belief that the mind can grasp essential truths through pure reason, rather than relying on sensory experience.

Key Concepts

  • Matter as Extension: For Descartes, the defining characteristic of all physical things is extension—the property of occupying space in three dimensions (length, breadth, and depth). Physical objects are essentially quantities of extension and can therefore be described in purely mathematical terms (shape, size, position, motion).
  • Mathematical Essences: This concept posits that the true nature of material things consists of measurable properties like shape, size, position, and motion, which are all expressible mathematically. This view laid the groundwork for modern physics, emphasizing mathematics as the language for understanding the physical world.
  • Primary vs. Secondary Qualities (Implied): While not explicitly named in this section, Descartes distinguishes between qualities inherent to matter itself (primary qualities like extension, shape, motion) and qualities that are subjective sensations produced in our minds by matter (secondary qualities like color, smell, taste, warmth, sound).
  • The Ontological Argument: This is an argument for God's existence that proceeds purely from the concept of God as a supremely perfect being, without appealing to any observation or experience. Descartes argues that existence is a perfection, and therefore a supremely perfect being must necessarily possess existence.
  • Pure Reason over Senses: A foundational methodological principle in Descartes' philosophy, highlighted in Meditation V, is that essential truths about reality (like the nature of matter or God's existence) are discovered through clear and distinct ideas grasped by the intellect, not through potentially deceptive sensory perceptions.

Important Terms

  • Extension: From the Latin extensio. For Descartes, this is the defining characteristic of all physical things—the property of occupying space, of having length, breadth, and depth. A body is extension, not merely something that has extension.
  • Mathematical Essences: The idea that the fundamental properties of material things (shape, size, position, motion) are inherently mathematical and can be precisely described and understood through mathematical principles. This makes mathematics the proper language for physics.
  • Ontological Argument: An argument that attempts to prove God's existence solely from the concept or definition of God, typically by asserting that existence is a necessary attribute of a perfect being. (From Greek ontos, meaning "being").
  • Cartesian Circle: An influential objection to Descartes' method, suggesting a circularity in his reasoning: he uses clear and distinct perception to prove God's existence, and then uses God's existence to validate the reliability of clear and distinct perception.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Meditation V: God Again, and the Nature of Matter

Meditation V is characterized by its conciseness and dense argumentation. Descartes uses this section to achieve two significant, yet seemingly distinct, goals:

  1. Defining Matter: He outlines what matter fundamentally is, even before fully proving its external existence.
  2. Second Proof of God: He offers a new, independent argument for God's existence, known as the ontological argument.

Both these tasks are unified by Descartes' underlying methodology: the conviction that the human mind can grasp essential truths directly through pure reason, bypassing the potentially deceptive input of the senses.

The Nature of Matter

Before proving the existence of an external world, Descartes addresses a prior question: what would the external world be like if it existed? What constitutes matter?

Matter as Pure Extension

  • Definition: Descartes' radical answer is that matter is extension. This means it is the property of occupying space in three dimensions: length, breadth, and depth.
  • Essential Properties: To be a material thing is to be extended. This implies having a determinate shape, size, and position. Material things can move, be divided, and possess measurable, mathematical properties.
  • What Matter Is Not: Importantly, Descartes argues that qualities like color, smell, taste, warmth, or sound are not inherent properties of matter itself. Instead, these are subjective sensations produced in our minds when our sense organs interact with extended objects. For example, the redness of an apple is a sensation in the mind, not strictly "in" the apple.

Consequences for Science

  • Mathematical Physics: This view has profound implications for scientific inquiry. If matter is pure extension, then the study of matter is fundamentally mathematics. The essential properties of material things (shape, size, position, motion) are all mathematical and measurable.
  • Language of Physics: Mathematics becomes the proper language for physics, describing the true nature of things rather than mere approximations. This vision of a mathematical physics of matter in motion, although Descartes' specific physics was later superseded, was immensely influential in the development of Newtonian mechanics and modern science.

Method of Discovery

Descartes arrives at this understanding of matter not through sensory observation, but through intellectual analysis. He finds the idea of extension as a "clear and distinct idea" within his own mind, whose content can be analyzed purely by reason. This reinforces his belief that the mind, not the senses, reveals essential truths.

The Ontological Argument: Descartes' Version

Following his discussion of matter, Descartes introduces a second, purely intellectual proof for God's existence.

Definition and Basis

  • Ontological Argument: This argument aims to prove God's existence purely from the concept or definition of God, without relying on sensory experience or external causes. It is called "ontological" because it concerns the nature of being (ontos).

Descartes' Argument in Steps

  1. I possess a clear and distinct idea of God as a supremely perfect being—a being that holds all perfections to an unlimited degree.
  2. Existence is a perfection. A being that exists is, in a relevant sense, "greater" or "more perfect" than a being that is merely possible.
  3. If a supremely perfect being lacked existence, it would be missing a perfection.
  4. Therefore, such a being would not be "supremely perfect," which contradicts our very definition and clear idea of God.
  5. Thus, a supremely perfect being must necessarily exist. Therefore, God exists.

Analogy to Geometry

Descartes draws a comparison to mathematical truths. Just as one cannot conceive of a triangle without understanding that its internal angles sum to two right angles (180 degrees)—a truth essential to triangularity, whether or not physical triangles exist—one cannot clearly and distinctly conceive of God without realizing that existence is part of God's essence. Existence is as integral to God as three angles are to a triangle.

How This Differs from Meditation III

It is crucial to differentiate the ontological argument of Meditation V from the causal argument presented in Meditation III.

  • Meditation III (Causal Argument): This argument reasons from the existence of the idea of an infinitely perfect being in Descartes' mind. Since Descartes, a finite being, could not be the cause of such an idea, an infinitely perfect being (God) must exist as its cause. This argument appeals to the principle that causes must be adequate to their effects.
  • Meditation V (Ontological Argument): This argument proceeds from an analysis of the concept of God itself. It argues that existence is an essential property of a supremely perfect being, making God's existence logically necessary. It does not appeal to any external cause for the idea.

These two independent proofs provide "philosophical redundancy," meaning if one proof faces objections, the other might still stand.

The Cartesian Circle Resolved?

With God's existence established (through either argument), Descartes can now confirm a crucial point: if God is not a deceiver, then anything he perceives clearly and distinctly must be true, not just in the moment of perception but durably. This allows him to trust his memory—if he recalls clearly proving a geometric theorem, that proof remains valid even when he isn't actively thinking about it.

The Objection

The Cartesian Circle is a famous objection raised by critics: Descartes uses clear and distinct perception to prove God's existence (in Meditations III and V), but then relies on God's existence to guarantee the reliability of clear and distinct perception. This appears to be a circular argument.

Discussion Points (Think Deeper)

  • Nature of Perception: Does Descartes use clear and distinct perception to prove God, or merely report what he immediately perceives clearly and distinctly? Is there a subtle but significant difference?
  • Burden of Proof: Who bears the responsibility for demonstrating circularity or its absence?
  • The Cogito: Does Descartes' most basic certainty, "I think, therefore I am" (cogito, ergo sum), require God's guarantee? Most scholars agree the cogito is self-validating and thus stands outside the circle.

Distinguishing from Anselm's Argument

It's important to note that while related, Descartes' ontological argument is not identical to Anselm's 11th-century version. Anselm's argument centers on the concept of "that than which nothing greater can be conceived," while Descartes focuses on a "supremely perfect being" and the idea of existence as a perfection. Both face similar objections, but their precise logical steps differ.

Previewing Kant's Objection

A significant future objection to the ontological argument, particularly impactful, comes from Immanuel Kant. Kant argues that existence is not a "real predicate" or a property that adds content to a concept. Saying "God exists" doesn't describe God more fully (like saying "God is powerful" does); rather, it asserts that the concept of God has an instance in reality. This raises the question: Is existence a property a thing has, or something fundamentally different?

Review Questions

  1. What does Descartes mean when he says "matter is extension"? How does this definition fundamentally change our understanding of physical objects compared to common sensory experience?
  2. Descartes argues that qualities like color and sound are not properties of matter itself. Where, then, do these qualities reside according to his philosophy, and what causes them?
  3. Explain Descartes' ontological argument for God's existence, outlining its key steps and comparing his reasoning to a geometric truth.
  4. How does the ontological argument of Meditation V differ from the causal argument for God's existence presented in Meditation III? What is the philosophical benefit of having two independent proofs?
  5. What is the "Cartesian Circle" objection, and why is it considered a significant challenge to Descartes' philosophical system? How might Descartes attempt to defend against this charge, particularly in light of his discussion of clear and distinct perception and memory?
