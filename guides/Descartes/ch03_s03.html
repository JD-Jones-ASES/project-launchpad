Study Guide: Descartes' Meditation II: The Cogito

Overview

In Meditation II, Descartes, still reeling from the radical doubt of the first meditation, embarks on a quest for an absolutely certain, unshakeable truth—an "Archimedean point" from which to rebuild all knowledge. He systematically doubts all previous beliefs, including the existence of his body and the external world, eventually arriving at the profound realization that the very act of doubting or thinking proves his own existence. From this foundational truth, "I think, therefore I am," he then meticulously examines the nature of this "I," concluding that it is essentially a "thinking thing," whose primary attributes are various modes of thought, independent of any physical or sensory attributes.

Key Concepts

  • Radical Doubt: Descartes continues the systematic and extreme skepticism introduced in Meditation I, rejecting anything that can be doubted, no matter how slightly. This includes all sensory experiences, the external world, and even the existence of his own body.
  • Archimedean Point: A metaphor for a single, fixed point of absolute certainty. Descartes seeks one indubitable truth that can serve as a foundation for all subsequent knowledge, just as Archimedes needed one fixed point to move the Earth.
  • The Cogito (I think, therefore I am): This is Descartes' famous first certainty. He realizes that even if a powerful deceiver is misleading him about everything else, he must exist in order to be deceived or to think. The very act of thinking (or doubting) confirms his existence each time he performs it.
  • "I" as a Thinking Thing (Res Cogitans): Once Descartes establishes his existence, he then investigates what kind of thing this "I" is. By eliminating all attributes that could still be doubted (like having a body, senses, or physical location), he concludes that his essential nature is that of a "thing which thinks"—a mind, soul, understanding, or reason.
  • Attributes of a Thinking Thing: Descartes identifies various activities that constitute thought and are inseparable from his being. These include doubting, understanding, affirming, denying, willing, refusing, imagining, and feeling. Importantly, "feeling" here refers to the subjective experience of sensation, rather than the objective reality of what is being sensed.
  • Imagination vs. Pure Intellection: Descartes distinguishes between understanding something through pure thought (intellection) and understanding it through imagination. He argues that imagination relies on forming images of corporeal things, which are still under doubt, and thus cannot provide certain knowledge about the nature of the "I." True self-knowledge comes from the mind's direct grasp of its own thought processes.

Important Terms

  • Archimedean Point: A hypothetical leverage point from which one could move the Earth; used by Descartes to signify a singular, absolutely certain truth upon which all other knowledge could be built.
  • Cogito, Ergo Sum: Latin for "I think, therefore I am." Descartes' most famous philosophical statement, representing his first indubitable certainty.
  • Radical Doubt: The method of systematically questioning and rejecting any belief that admits even the slightest possibility of error, employed by Descartes to uncover foundational truths.
  • Res Cogitans: Latin for "thinking thing." This is Descartes' conclusion about the essential nature of the self, distinct from a body.
  • Imagination: The mental faculty responsible for forming images of corporeal (bodily) things. Descartes argues it is unreliable for gaining certain self-knowledge because it presupposes the existence of bodies, which is still doubted.
  • Intellection (Pure Understanding): The mind's faculty for grasping concepts and truths directly through thought, without relying on sensory input or imaginative images.
  • Malicious Demon (or Deceiver): A hypothetical, all-powerful, and cunning being introduced in Meditation I to push doubt to its extreme, suggesting that all sensory experiences and even mathematical truths could be illusions.

Detailed Notes

The Crisis of Doubt and the Search for Certainty

Descartes begins Meditation II overwhelmed by the comprehensive doubt he cultivated in Meditation I. Everything he once believed has been called into question, leaving him feeling as if he's fallen into deep water, unable to find stable ground. His primary goal is to find one absolutely certain truth—an "Archimedean point"—from which he can begin to reconstruct his knowledge. He resolves to continue rejecting anything that admits the slightest doubt, treating it as if it were entirely false, until he finds something that is undeniably true, or until he concludes that nothing at all is certain.

The Discovery of the Cogito: "I Am, I Exist"

To find his fixed point, Descartes systematically reviews what he has doubted:

  • He supposes that all he sees is false.
  • He believes his memory is fallacious and nothing he remembers ever existed.
  • He considers himself to have no senses, and that body, figure, extension, movement, and place are mere fictions of his mind.
  • He even entertains the idea that there is no heaven, no earth, no minds, and no bodies at all.

However, a crucial realization emerges when he considers the possibility of a powerful deceiver. If such a deceiver exists and is constantly working to mislead him, then for him to be deceived, he must exist. The act of being persuaded, or simply the act of thinking (doubting, understanding, willing), requires a subject that thinks. Thus, the proposition "I am, I exist" must be true every time he thinks it or mentally conceives it. This is the first indubitable truth, the Cogito, ergo sum.

Defining the "I": What Am I?

Having established his existence, Descartes next endeavors to understand what this "I" is. He explicitly warns against prematurely assuming anything that might still be doubtful.

He first considers his former belief about himself: a man, defined as a "reasonable animal," or as a being with a body (face, hands, etc.) and a soul (something subtle like wind or flame, responsible for nourishment, walking, feeling, and thinking). He immediately rejects the "reasonable animal" definition as it would lead to an endless chain of definitions.

He then systematically re-examines these attributes under the lens of radical doubt (and the malicious demon hypothesis):

  • Physical Attributes (Body): Can he affirm he possesses a body, face, hands, nourishment, or the ability to walk? No, because the existence of a body and the external world is still doubted. If he has no body, he cannot walk or take nourishment.
  • Sensory Attributes (Sensation): Sensation is problematic because it seems to require a body, and he has often perceived things in dreams that were not real.
  • Thinking: This is the only attribute that remains. Even if he doubts everything, he is still thinking. Even if he is deceived about what he sees or feels, the fact that he seems to see or seems to feel is itself a thought or perception occurring within him. This act of thinking cannot be separated from him.

The "I" as a Pure Thinking Thing (Res Cogitans)

Descartes concludes that he is "not more than a thing which thinks." He equates this "thinking thing" with a mind, soul, understanding, or reason. This "I" is a real thing, existing as a substance whose essence is thought. He emphasizes that his knowledge of his own existence does not depend on things whose existence is not yet known (like the body or external world).

The Role and Limits of Imagination

Descartes then explores the role of imagination. He questions if he is anything more than a thinking thing, perhaps a collection of bodily members or a subtle air distributed through them. He argues against using imagination to define himself because:

  1. Imagination relies on corporeal images: To imagine something is to contemplate the figure or image of a physical thing.
  2. Corporeal things are still doubted: The existence of bodies and corporeal things is precisely what he has set aside as doubtful.
  3. It's counterproductive: Relying on imagination for self-knowledge would be like going to sleep to gain clearer perceptions, when dreams are the source of much doubt.

Therefore, knowledge of the self must come from pure intellection, from the mind reflecting on its own nature, rather than from forming mental images.

Attributes of the Thinking Thing Re-evaluated

Finally, Descartes enumerates the specific attributes that belong to a thinking thing:

  • Doubting: The very act he is performing.
  • Understanding (conceiving): The ability to grasp ideas.
  • Affirming: Assenting to truths.
  • Denying: Rejecting falsehoods.
  • Willing: Having desires or intentions.
  • Refusing: Opposing desires or intentions.
  • Imagining: Even if the things imagined are false, the act of imagining itself is a mental activity.
  • Feeling (perceiving): Even if the external causes of sensations are false (e.g., in a dream), the subjective experience of seeming to see light, hear noise, or feel heat is an undeniable internal perception, and thus a form of thought.

All these activities are indubitably part of his nature as a thinking thing. They cannot be separated from his thought, and therefore from himself. He has found clear and certain knowledge of what he is: a thinking thing.

Review Questions

  1. What is the significance of Descartes' search for an "Archimedean point" in Meditation II, and how does it relate to the radical doubt he experienced previously?
  2. Explain the reasoning behind Descartes' conclusion, "I am, I exist," commonly known as the Cogito. How does the hypothesis of a malicious deceiver actually strengthen this conclusion rather than undermine it?
  3. After establishing his existence, Descartes questions "What am I?" Describe how he systematically eliminates physical and sensory attributes from his definition of the self.
  4. Why does Descartes emphasize that he is a "thinking thing" rather than a "body" or a "man"? What specific attributes does he identify as belonging to this "thinking thing," and why are they considered indubitable?
  5. Descartes argues against using imagination to understand the nature of the "I." Explain his reasoning and how he distinguishes imagination from a more reliable mode of knowing himself.
