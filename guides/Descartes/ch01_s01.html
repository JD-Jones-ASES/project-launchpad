Study Guide: Descartes’ Meditations on First Philosophy (Dedicatory Letter and Synopsis)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the introductory materials and structural summary of René Descartes’ Meditations on First Philosophy, first published in 1641. It covers the historical context of the work, Descartes' stated motives for writing to the theological authorities of his time, and a meditation-by-meditation breakdown of his philosophical project.

Historical Context and Objectives

The Dedicatory Letter to the Sorbonne

Descartes addressed his work to the Faculty of Sacred Theology at the University of Paris (the Sorbonne). His primary aim was to secure the Church's protection and approval. This was motivated by the recent condemnation of Galileo in 1633, which had led Descartes to suppress his own work on physics, Le Monde.

Core Objectives of the Letter:

  • Demonstration via Natural Reason: Descartes argues that while faith is sufficient for the "faithful," philosophical arguments based on "natural reason" are required to persuade "infidels" of God’s existence and the soul's immortality.
  • Refuting Atheism: He claims that the "principal reason" for impiety is the belief that these two facts—God's existence and the distinction between soul and body—have never been demonstrated.
  • The Circular Argument Concern: He acknowledges that while we believe in God because of Scripture and in Scripture because of God, this "circular reasoning" cannot be presented to non-believers. Thus, independent philosophical proofs are necessary.
  • Authority of the Lateran Council: Descartes cites the Lateran Council under Leo X, which tasked Christian philosophers with refuting arguments that the soul perishes with the body.

The Preface to the Reader

In this section, Descartes clarifies his intended audience and responds to early criticisms of his "Method," which he had previously introduced in his 1637 work, Discourse on Method.

  • Intended Audience: Descartes advises that only those willing to "meditate seriously" and detach their minds from the senses and prejudices should read the book.
  • Response to Objections:
    • He clarifies that when he calls himself a "thinking thing," he is not initially excluding the possibility of other attributes, but rather identifying the only thing he knows with certainty to belong to his essence at that stage of the argument.
    • He distinguishes between the "material" sense of an idea (an act of the understanding) and the "objective" sense (the thing represented by that act), which allows for an idea to be more "perfect" than the thinker.

Synopsis of the Six Meditations

The following table summarizes the progression of Descartes' argument as outlined in his own synopsis:

Meditation Primary Theme Key Developments
I Universal Doubt Sets out reasons to doubt all things, especially material objects, to deliver the mind from prejudice and senses.
II The Nature of Mind The mind uses its liberty to doubt everything, concluding that it is impossible for the mind itself not to exist. Establishes the mind as a "thinking thing."
III Existence of God Provides a principal argument for God’s existence based on the idea of a "supremely perfect being" within the human mind.
IV Truth and Error Defines the nature of truth and error in speculative matters. Explains that clear and distinct perceptions are always true.
V Corporeal Nature Explains the nature of bodies generally and offers a second, "new" proof of God's existence. Notes that geometrical certainty depends on knowing God.
VI The External World Distinguishes understanding from imagination. Proves the real distinction between mind and body, their close union, and the existence of material things.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why did Descartes write the Meditations in Latin rather than French? Descartes wrote in Latin to ensure the work would be read by a scholarly audience. He feared that "feebler minds" might follow the "little trodden" path of his radical doubt and lose their way if the text were available to everyone in the vernacular.

  2. What comparison does Descartes use to describe the certainty of his philosophical proofs? He compares his demonstrations to those of Geometry (citing Archimedes and Apollonius), noting that while they are certain and evident, they are lengthy and require a mind free from prejudice to be understood.

  3. According to the synopsis of Meditation II, why is the mind considered "immortal"? Descartes argues that the mind is a "pure substance" that is not composed of "accidents" or parts. Unlike the body, which perishes when its configuration of members changes, the mind remains the same substance even if its thoughts or desires change.

  4. How does Descartes distinguish between the "material" and "objective" reality of an idea? The "material" reality refers to the idea as an act of the mind's understanding. The "objective" reality refers to the thing the idea represents. This distinction allows him to argue that the idea of God has more objective reality than the human mind itself.

  5. What is the "utility" of the general doubt introduced in Meditation I? The utility is threefold: it delivers the mind from all prejudice, provides a simple way for the mind to detach from the senses, and ensures that once truths are discovered, they can never again be doubted.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Intersection of Faith and Reason: Analyze Descartes’ argument in the Dedicatory Letter regarding why natural reason is a more effective tool than theology when addressing "infidels." How does he navigate the tension between wanting Church approval and asserting the independence of philosophical inquiry?

  2. The Geometric Method in Metaphysics: Descartes claims his proofs "surpass in certainty" the demonstrations of geometry. Discuss the requirements he sets for a reader to achieve this level of certainty. Why does he believe so few people are "fitted for metaphysical speculations" compared to those fitted for geometry?

  3. The Mind-Body Problem and Substance: Based on the Synopsis of Meditation VI, explain how Descartes reconciles the "real distinction" between mind and body with the fact that they are "so closely joined together that they form... a single thing." Use his definition of substance and accidents to support your analysis.

Glossary of Key Terms

  • Le Monde (The World): Descartes' unpublished treatise on physics and cosmology that defended a heliocentric view; it was suppressed following Galileo's condemnation.
  • Light of Nature (Natural Reason): The innate human capacity to perceive truth clearly and distinctly, independent of divine revelation or sensory input.
  • Objective Reality: The degree of reality or perfection represented by an idea. For example, the idea of an infinite being (God) has more objective reality than the idea of a finite substance.
  • Sorbonne: The Faculty of Theology at the University of Paris; a powerful body with the authority to approve or condemn philosophical works in 17th-century France.
  • Substance: According to Descartes, that which exists in such a way as to depend on nothing else for its existence.
  • Thinking Thing (Res Cogitans): The essence of the mind or soul, characterized by the faculty of thinking, willing, and perceiving, distinct from the physical extension of the body.
