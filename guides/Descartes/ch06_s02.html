The Ontological Argument: Anselm, Gaunilo, and Aquinas

Overview

This section delves into the ontological argument, one of the most debated philosophical arguments for the existence of God. It explores its original formulation by Anselm of Canterbury, the famous "perfect island" objection raised by Gaunilo, and the incisive rejection of the argument by Thomas Aquinas, focusing on the distinction between essence and existence. The discussion highlights the profound relationship between our concepts and reality.

Key Concepts

  • The Ontological Argument: An a priori argument for God's existence that attempts to prove God's existence based solely on the concept or definition of God, without appealing to sensory experience.
  • Anselm's Formulation: Defines God as "that than which nothing greater can be conceived." Argues that if such a being exists only in the mind, a greater being (existing in reality) could be conceived, leading to a contradiction. Therefore, God must exist in reality.
  • Gaunilo's Objection (The Perfect Island): A critique arguing that if Anselm's logic were valid, it could be used to prove the existence of any "greatest conceivable" thing, such as a perfect island, which is clearly absurd.
  • Anselm's Reply to Gaunilo: Contends that the argument applies only to a being whose non-existence is inconceivable, like God (the absolute maximum of greatness), and not to contingent, limited things like islands, for which no true "greatest" concept exists.
  • Aquinas's Rejection: Argues that human minds do not have direct, intuitive access to God's essence. Our concept of God is merely a "nominal definition" or an approximation, insufficient to move from concept to existence through pure analysis.
  • Essence and Existence:
    • In Creatures: Essence (what a thing is) and existence (that a thing is) are distinct. A concept can have an essence without existing in reality (e.g., a unicorn).
    • In God: Essence and existence are identical; God is existence itself, not merely a being that exists.
  • A Priori vs. A Posteriori Arguments: The ontological argument is a priori (based on reason alone), while Aquinas's "Five Ways" are a posteriori (based on observable effects in the world). Aquinas believed God's existence must be proven a posteriori.

Important Terms

  • Ontological Argument: A philosophical argument for the existence of God based on the concept of God's being or existence.
  • Anselm of Canterbury (1033–1109): Italian-born Benedictine monk, Archbishop of Canterbury, theologian, and philosopher. Formulated the ontological argument in his Proslogium. Coined fides quaerens intellectum.
  • Proslogium (1077–78): Anselm's work containing his original formulation of the ontological argument.
  • Fides quaerens intellectum: Latin for "faith seeking understanding," Anselm's conviction that reason can illuminate what faith already holds.
  • "That than which nothing greater can be conceived": Anselm's definition of God, central to his ontological argument.
  • Gaunilo of Marmoutiers (fl. 1080): Benedictine monk who authored On Behalf of the Fool, offering the famous "perfect island" objection to Anselm's argument.
  • Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274): Influential Dominican friar, philosopher, and theologian. A major critic of the ontological argument, known for his Summa Theologica and the "Five Ways."
  • Summa Theologica: Aquinas's monumental work of systematic philosophy and theology.
  • Nominal Definition: A definition that describes a thing by its name or common understanding, without providing a full or intrinsic grasp of its essence.
  • Essence: What a thing intrinsically is; its fundamental nature or "whatness."
  • Existence: The fact or state of being real or actual; "thatness."
  • Five Ways: Thomas Aquinas's five a posteriori arguments for God's existence, derived from observable features of the world: from motion, efficient causation, contingency, degrees of perfection, and the order of nature.
  • A Priori Argument: An argument whose premises are knowable independently of experience.
  • A Posteriori Argument: An argument whose premises are based on empirical observation or experience.

Detailed Notes

The Ontological Argument: A Lingering Debate

  • The ontological argument is considered the most discussed philosophical argument in the Western tradition.
  • It originated with Anselm of Canterbury in the 11th century, was adapted by Descartes in the 17th century, and faced strong opposition from figures like Aquinas and Kant, yet it has been continually revived.
  • The debate surrounding it explores fundamental questions about the relationship between human thought and objective reality.
  • Thomas Aquinas, a leading systematic philosopher and theologian, rejected the ontological argument, insisting that God's existence must be proved from observable effects, not from the concept of God alone.

Anselm's Original Argument and Gaunilo's Objection

  • Anselm's Formulation:
    • Anselm defined God as "that than which nothing greater can be conceived."
    • His argument states: If this greatest possible being existed only in the mind (as a concept) and not in reality, then one could conceive of something greater (the same being, but also existing in reality).
    • This leads to a contradiction, as it would mean the greatest conceivable being is not, in fact, the greatest.
    • Therefore, God must exist both in the mind and in reality.
  • Anselm's Proslogium Excerpt (Chapter IV):
    • Anselm distinguishes two ways of conceiving something: by conceiving the word or by truly understanding the entity itself.
    • "The Fool" (a skeptic) can say "God does not exist" in words, but cannot truly understand God not to exist.
    • Anyone who fully understands the definition of God ("that than which a greater cannot be conceived") must simultaneously understand that such a being must exist and cannot be conceived as non-existent.
  • Gaunilo's Objection ("On Behalf of the Fool"):
    • Gaunilo, a contemporary of Anselm, challenged the argument with the "greatest conceivable island" analogy.
    • He argued that if Anselm's logic were sound, one could imagine an island more perfect, beautiful, and fertile than any other, and by Anselm's reasoning, this "greatest conceivable island" would then have to exist in reality.
    • Since it's clear that merely thinking of a perfect island doesn't make it real, Gaunilo concluded that the ontological argument must be flawed.
  • Anselm's Reply to Gaunilo:
    • Anselm countered that his argument applies only to "that than which nothing greater can be conceived" – a unique description that fits God alone.
    • An island, no matter how perfect, is a contingent and limited thing. There's no inherent upper limit to how good an island could be, making the concept of a "greatest island" ultimately incoherent or lacking a true maximum.
    • God, however, is defined as the absolute maximum of greatness, a concept fundamentally different in kind. The debate over the success of this reply continues.

Aquinas's Rejection of the Ontological Argument

  • The Problem of Our Knowledge of God's Essence:
    • Aquinas, despite his admiration for Anselm's theology, rejected the ontological argument.
    • His core reason: human beings do not possess a direct or intuitive knowledge of God's essence.
    • While we use the phrase "that than which nothing greater can be conceived" as a description (a nominal definition) to point towards God, it doesn't mean we have a genuine intellectual grasp of God's infinite, intrinsic nature. Such a grasp is beyond our finite intellectual capacities.
    • Therefore, we cannot move from a mere concept (which is an approximation) to God's actual existence through pure logical analysis alone.
  • Essence and Existence in God vs. Creatures:
    • Aquinas's deeper point lies in the distinction between essence (what a thing is) and existence (that a thing is).
    • In all created beings: Essence and existence are distinct. For example, the essence of a unicorn (horse-like, single horn) can be conceived, but it lacks existence in reality. A human's essence (rational animal) is distinct from the fact of their existence.
    • In God alone: Essence and existence are identical. God is not merely a being that has existence; God is existence itself, or "Being itself."
  • Aquinas's Summa Theologica Excerpt (I, Q. 3, Art. 4):
    • Aquinas argues that if existence were distinct from God's essence, God's existence would have to be caused either by His essence or by an external agent.
    • Nothing can be the sufficient cause of its own existence if its existence is caused. An external cause for God is impossible, as God is the "first efficient cause."
    • Therefore, in God, essence and existence must be identical. God is pure actuality with no potentiality.
    • However, Aquinas clarifies that we can only understand the proposition "God is" to be true based on His effects in the world (e.g., the Five Ways), not from a direct intellectual grasp of His essence. We cannot understand God's essence directly, but we can infer His existence from what He has created.
  • The Five Ways and A Posteriori Proof:
    • Aquinas insists that establishing God's existence, and subsequently the identity of His essence and existence, requires a posteriori arguments.
    • His "Five Ways" (arguments from motion, causation, contingency, degrees of perfection, and the order of nature) are examples of such arguments, beginning from observable features of the world and reasoning back to God as their ultimate explanation.
    • He argues that one cannot "short-circuit" this process by simply defining God in a way that includes existence, because our limited human intellect lacks the necessary access to the divine essence to make such a definition genuinely insightful of reality.

Review Questions

  1. What is the central premise of Anselm's ontological argument, and how does his definition of God as "that than which nothing greater can be conceived" lead to the conclusion of God's existence?
  2. Explain Gaunilo's "perfect island" objection in detail. How does Anselm attempt to refute this objection, and what are the strengths and weaknesses of his response?
  3. Why does Thomas Aquinas reject the ontological argument? Discuss his reasoning concerning the human intellect's capacity to grasp God's essence.
  4. Elaborate on Aquinas's distinction between essence and existence as it applies to created beings versus God. How is this distinction fundamental to his critique of Anselm?
  5. Compare and contrast the a priori nature of the ontological argument with the a posteriori nature of Aquinas's "Five Ways." Why did Aquinas believe that God's existence must be demonstrated through a posteriori proofs rather than a priori ones?
