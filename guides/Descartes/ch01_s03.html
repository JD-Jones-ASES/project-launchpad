The Intellectual Context of René Descartes: A Study Guide

This study guide examines the historical and intellectual landscape of the seventeenth century that shaped René Descartes' philosophical motivations. It explores the "triple crisis" of authority—religious, scientific, and philosophical—that led Descartes to seek a new, indubitable foundation for human knowledge.

I. Key Concepts and Historical Context

The Triple Crisis of Authority

By the early seventeenth century, European confidence in inherited knowledge was destabilized by three interlocking upheavals:

  1. The Reformation: Initiated in 1517 by Martin Luther, this movement fractured European Christianity. It replaced theological certainty with a century of doctrinal conflict and devastating warfare, notably the Thirty Years' War (1618–1648), which Descartes witnessed during his most productive years.
  2. The New Science: A revolution in natural philosophy dismantled the two-thousand-year-old Aristotelian worldview. Discoveries by Copernicus, Galileo, and Kepler proved that traditional authorities (Aristotle, Ptolemy, and medieval commentators) were fundamentally incorrect about the structure of the universe.
  3. The Skeptical Revival: The rediscovery and publication of ancient Greek skeptical texts, particularly those of Sextus Empiricus in 1562, introduced a "skeptical crisis." This movement argued that human reason was too feeble and inconsistent to achieve certain knowledge.

Scholasticism

Descartes was educated at the Jesuit college of La Flèche in the tradition of Scholasticism. This system sought to harmonize Aristotelian philosophy with Christian doctrine. While Descartes admired the logical precision of the tradition, he eventually found it unreliable. He noted that Scholasticism could produce sophisticated arguments for any side of a question, leading to contradictions between different schools of thought rather than the establishment of objective truth.

The Mathematical Model

Descartes' response to the intellectual instability of his era was to "start over." He looked to Euclidean geometry as a model for his new system. Just as Euclid derived complex truths from a handful of self-evident axioms, Descartes sought a starting point for philosophy that was so certain it would be impossible to doubt.

II. Significant Figures in the Intellectual Revolution

Figure Role Key Contribution/Impact
Nicolaus Copernicus Astronomer Proposed the heliocentric model, suggesting the earth moves around the sun.
Galileo Galilei Scientist Known as the "father of modern science." Used the telescope to observe moons orbiting Jupiter and craters on the Moon; forced by the Inquisition to recant his support for Copernicanism in 1633.
Johannes Kepler Astronomer Demonstrated that planetary orbits are ellipses rather than the perfect circles required by Aristotelian physics.
Sextus Empiricus Philosopher An ancient Greek whose preserved accounts of Pyrrhonism were printed in Latin in 1562, sparking the Renaissance "skeptical crisis."
Michel de Montaigne Essayist Inventor of the essay; used Pyrrhonian skepticism to argue that human knowledge is conditioned by custom, habit, and accident. His motto was "Que sais-je?" (What do I know?).

III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What specific event is cited as the beginning of the Reformation, and in what year did it occur?
  2. How did the Thirty Years' War impact the population of German-speaking lands during Descartes' lifetime?
  3. What were two specific astronomical observations made by Galileo that challenged the Aristotelian view of the universe?
  4. According to the school of Pyrrhonism, what is the proper response when presented with equally strong arguments on both sides of a question?
  5. What was the primary goal of Scholasticism as taught at institutions like La Flèche?
  6. Why did Descartes believe that mathematics, specifically geometry, provided a better model for philosophy than the Scholastic method?
  7. What was the significance of the year 1562 regarding the history of skepticism?

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Collapse of Aristotelianism: Analyze how the "New Science" did more than just correct minor errors in astronomy. Explain why the discoveries of Copernicus, Galileo, and Kepler created a foundational crisis for European intellectuals who relied on ancient authorities.
  2. The Role of Skepticism: Compare and contrast the ancient Pyrrhonism of Sextus Empiricus with the Renaissance skepticism of Michel de Montaigne. Discuss how these ideas challenged the notion that human reason is a reliable tool for discovering truth.
  3. Descartes' Radical Method: Discuss Descartes’ decision to "tear down" the existing foundations of knowledge rather than patching the old system. How did the religious and philosophical climate of the early seventeenth century make this radical approach seem necessary?

V. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Epoche: A Greek term meaning "suspension of belief." This was the motto of the Pyrrhonists, who argued one should suspend judgment on all questions.
  • Heliocentrism: The scientific theory (supported by Copernicus and Galileo) that the earth and planets revolve around the sun, contradicting the Aristotelian/Ptolemaic model.
  • Pyrrhonism: An ancient school of radical skepticism named after Pyrrho of Elis. It holds that because there are equally strong arguments for any claim, the only wise course is to suspend judgment entirely.
  • Que sais-je?: French for "What do I know?" The famous motto of Michel de Montaigne, expressing the core of his skeptical outlook.
  • Scholasticism: The dominant philosophical and theological tradition of medieval universities. It used formal disputations to harmonize the works of Aristotle with Christian theology.
  • Skeptical Crisis: A period in the Renaissance sparked by the printing of Sextus Empiricus's works, leading to a widespread questioning of human reason's ability to achieve certainty.
  • Thirty Years' War (1618–1648): A massive conflict in Europe, largely driven by the religious fractures of the Reformation, occurring during Descartes' most productive years.
