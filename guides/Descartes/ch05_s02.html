Descartes' Meditation IV: Tracing Truth and Error

Overview

This section explores the philosophical problems of truth, error, intellect, and will, as addressed by Descartes in Meditation IV, by tracing their historical roots and subsequent developments. It contrasts Descartes' "inward" criterion for truth with Aquinas's correspondence theory and examines how the problem of intellectual error parallels the theological problem of evil, drawing on figures like Augustine and Leibniz. The discussion concludes by highlighting Descartes' expansive view of human free will compared to Hobbes's more deterministic stance.

Key Concepts

  • Correspondence Theory of Truth (Aquinas): The view that truth is a relation of conformity between the intellect and reality. Aquinas's formula, adaequatio intellectus et rei, means the matching of the intellect to the thing. For Aquinas, truth resides primarily in the intellect, but things are called "true" secondarily in their relation to the Divine intellect.
  • Descartes' Criterion of Truth: Descartes defines truth not by correspondence with external reality, but by the internal mental states of clarity and distinctness. An idea is true if it is perceived with full transparency and certainty within the mind itself. This represents a significant "shift inward" in philosophy.
  • Problem of Error as Privation: Descartes treats intellectual error similarly to how Augustine of Hippo addressed moral evil. Error is not a positive faculty or creation of God, but rather a privation – an absence of the complete knowledge or proper restraint that should be present. God, being perfect, is not the author of error.
  • Augustine's Privation Theory of Evil: Evil is understood not as a substance created by God, but as an absence or lack of good. Just as darkness is the absence of light, evil is the absence of goodness, arising from the free will of creatures turning away from the good.
  • Leibniz's Theodicy: Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz argued that God, being perfectly rational and good, created "the best of all possible worlds." In this framework, the existence of error and evil does not contradict God's perfection but is a necessary component of a world that allows for free creatures and genuine virtue, which is ultimately better than a world of mere automatons.
  • Descartes' Infinite Will: Descartes posits that the human will is virtually unlimited in its scope, capable of affirming or denying any proposition without compulsion. He views this expansive freedom as a participation in God's own infinite will, bestowing a profound dignity upon human beings.
  • Hobbes's Materialist View of Will: In contrast to Descartes, Thomas Hobbes argued that the will is not genuinely free but is simply the last desire or aversion that precedes an action. On this view, the will is entirely determined by prior physical and psychological causes, reflecting a mechanistic understanding of human behavior.

Important Terms

  • Correspondence Theory of Truth: The philosophical view that a proposition or idea is true if and only if it accurately represents or "corresponds to" the facts or reality it describes.
  • Adaequatio intellectus et rei: A Latin phrase meaning "the adequation (matching) of the intellect to the thing." This is Thomas Aquinas's core definition of truth, emphasizing the conformity between a mental concept and external reality.
  • Aquinas, Thomas (1225–1274): An influential Italian Dominican friar, philosopher, and theologian. He synthesized Aristotelian philosophy with Christian doctrine and developed a comprehensive theory of truth, among many other contributions.
  • Augustine of Hippo (354–430): A North African bishop, theologian, and philosopher. His concept of evil as a "privation of good" profoundly influenced Christian thought and subsequent philosophical discussions on the problem of evil.
  • Privation: In philosophy and theology, the absence or lack of something that ought to be present. It is used to explain evil (absence of good) and, by Descartes, error (absence of knowledge or proper restraint).
  • Leibniz, Gottfried Wilhelm (1646–1716): A German polymath, philosopher, mathematician, and logician. He is known for his rationalist philosophy, co-discovery of calculus, and his Theodicy, which sought to justify God's goodness in the face of evil in the world.
  • Theodicy: A theological and philosophical attempt to reconcile the existence of evil, suffering, and imperfection in the world with the belief in an omnipotent, omnibenevolent, and omniscient God. Leibniz's Theodicy famously argued that this is "the best of all possible worlds."
  • Hobbes, Thomas (1588–1679): An English philosopher, best known for his work Leviathan. He was a materialist who argued for a deterministic view of human will, seeing it as the result of prior causes rather than a free choice.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Meditation IV: Truth and Error

Descartes' Meditation IV delves into fundamental philosophical questions: the nature of truth, the source of error, and the interplay between the human intellect and will. These inquiries were not novel to Descartes, nor did they conclude with him; instead, this section traces a rich intellectual lineage, examining how these concepts evolved from earlier thinkers like Aquinas and influenced later ones such as Augustine, Leibniz, and Hobbes. The Dürer engraving Melencolia I, with its brooding figure surrounded by instruments of learning, serves as a poignant visual metaphor for the intellectual struggle to attain truth, a struggle central to Descartes' quest for certain knowledge.

Aquinas on Truth

Approximately four centuries before Descartes, Thomas Aquinas grappled with similar questions about truth and the intellect, albeit with distinct conclusions. For Aquinas, truth is fundamentally a correspondence between the intellect and reality, encapsulated in his formula: adaequatio intellectus et rei—the "adequation" or matching of the intellect to the thing. An idea is true if it accurately represents the external object.

In his Summa Theologica (I, Q. 16, Art. 1), Aquinas systematically investigates where truth resides:

  • Objections: He first considers arguments that truth resides in things themselves (e.g., Augustine's "That is true which is") or that it is relative to human knowledge, leading to the inadmissible conclusion that contradictory statements could both be true. The objections also suggest that the truth of our thought is caused by things, implying truth is primarily in things.
  • Aquinas's Response: Aquinas argues that truth, like "good," denotes that towards which an faculty tends. Just as "good" is in the object desired, "true" is in the intellect, because "knowledge is according as the thing known is in the knower."
    • He distinguishes between a thing's relation to an intellect essentially (e.g., a house to an architect's mind, or natural things to the Divine mind) and accidentally (e.g., a thing to our human intellect, which doesn't determine its essence).
    • Therefore, things are said to be "true" absolutely not by their relation to our intellect, but primarily by their relation to the Divine Intellect, from which their essence depends. An artificial thing is true if it conforms to the artisan's mind; a natural thing is true if it conforms to the species in God's mind.
    • Thus, truth resides primarily in the intellect (first in the Divine intellect, then in the human intellect conforming to reality), and secondarily in things as they are related to the intellect as their principle.
  • Reconciling Definitions: Aquinas explains that various definitions of truth (e.g., "truth is that whereby is made manifest that which is") capture different aspects, but the definition "adequation of thought and thing" applies to truth in both the intellect and in things (as they relate to an intellect).
  • Replies to Objections: Aquinas clarifies that Augustine speaks of the truth of things, excluding accidental relation to our intellect. The errors of ancient philosophers arose from not positing a Divine intellect, forcing them to relate truth to our fallible intellect. Finally, while the truth of our intellect is caused by the thing's being (not its truth), this doesn't mean truth resides primarily in the thing, just as health is not primarily in medicine but in the animal.

Aquinas's position insists that truth is in the intellect, yet it is not idealism because it still anchors the truth of things in their conformity to God's perfect knowledge. Human intellectual truth is a participation in this divine truth.

Descartes vs. Aquinas on Truth

Both Aquinas and Descartes agree that truth involves the intellect. However, their criteria diverge sharply. For Aquinas, truth is an outward-looking correspondence between the intellect and the external world. For Descartes, truth is an inward-looking property: clarity and distinctness within the mind itself. Descartes' standard does not require verification against external reality, but rather a transparent and evident perception of the idea. This "shift inward"—moving the standard of truth from the world to the mind—is a hallmark of modern philosophy.

The Problem of Evil and the Problem of Error

Descartes' approach to intellectual error in Meditation IV mirrors a long-standing philosophical and theological response to the problem of evil. Just as philosophers question how a perfect God could permit moral evil, Descartes asks how a perfect God could allow intellectual error. His solution borrows from the same conceptual framework.

The pivotal figure here is Augustine of Hippo, who famously argued that evil is not a positive substance created by God, but a privation of good. Evil is the absence of something good that ought to be present. For instance, darkness is not a thing in itself but the absence of light. God, being perfectly good, creates only good things; evil arises from the free will of creatures turning away from the good.

Descartes applies this exact structure to intellectual error:

  • Error is not a positive faculty or capacity that God created.
  • Instead, error is a privation—an absence of the knowledge, clarity, or proper restraint that should guide our judgment.
  • Therefore, God is not responsible for our intellectual mistakes, just as he is not responsible for our moral failings.

This argument was further developed by Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz in his Theodicy (1710). Leibniz contended that a perfectly rational and good God would create "the best of all possible worlds." In this optimal world, the existence of free creatures capable of error and moral evil is preferable to a world populated by flawless automatons, as it allows for genuine virtue and freedom. From this perspective, error is the "price of freedom."

Free Will and Judgment

Descartes' concept of the human will is remarkably expansive, bordering on the divine. He claims that the will is effectively infinite in its scope. Humans possess the capacity to affirm or deny any proposition, and no judgment is ever compelled. This freedom, while differing in extent, is essentially the same kind of freedom that God himself exercises. For Descartes, this unlimited freedom of judgment confers a profound dignity upon human beings, allowing them to participate in God's own unconstrained liberty.

This generous depiction of free will, however, was not universally accepted in the 17th century. Thomas Hobbes, a contemporary of Descartes, presented a starkly contrasting view. For Hobbes, the will is not free in any robust philosophical sense. Instead, he saw the will as merely the "last appetite" or aversion that occurs before an action. This final desire is entirely determined by prior causes—physical, psychological, and environmental. Hobbes's materialist philosophy thus offered a deterministic account of human action, fundamentally at odds with Descartes' vision of infinite human freedom.
