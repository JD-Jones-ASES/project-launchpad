Descartes and Christianity: A Philosophical and Theological Encounter

Overview

This section explores René Descartes' complex relationship with Christianity, highlighting his sincere Catholic faith and his intention to provide philosophical support for theological truths. Despite his aims, Descartes' revolutionary method of radical doubt and reliance on autonomous reason inadvertently created a tension with traditional Christian philosophy and led to unforeseen consequences for the role of faith in intellectual inquiry. The section contrasts his approach with that of Thomas Aquinas and discusses the lasting implications of Descartes' work for Christian thought.

Key Concepts

  • Descartes' Intention: René Descartes, a devout Catholic, believed his new philosophy could provide robust, rational foundations for proving God's existence and the soul's distinctness from the body. He aimed to replace the perceived failings of Aristotelian philosophy, which he felt was no longer adequate to defend Christian theology.
  • The Irony of the Index: Despite his explicit goal to support the Church, Descartes' Meditations was placed on the Roman Catholic Church's Index of Forbidden Books. The Church's concern stemmed from his method of beginning with radical doubt and rebuilding knowledge solely through human reason, which seemed to bypass the authority of tradition and revelation.
  • A Priori vs. A Posteriori Proofs:
    • A Priori: Knowledge or arguments that depend on reason alone, independent of experience or observation. Descartes' proofs for God's existence, such as his ontological argument, fall into this category as they proceed from the analysis of the concept of God.
    • A Posteriori: Knowledge or arguments that depend on experience and observation. Thomas Aquinas's Five Ways are classic examples, as they begin with observable facts about the world (e.g., motion, causation) and reason from those effects to God as their ultimate cause.
  • Autonomous Reason: Descartes' method of commencing from universal doubt and attempting to reconstruct all knowledge through individual, unaided reason effectively made reason "self-legislating"—independent of external authorities, including theological or revealed truth. This marked a significant shift from previous philosophical traditions.
  • Faith Seeking Understanding (Fides Quaerens Intellectum): This Latin phrase, coined by Anselm and embraced by Aquinas, describes the classical Christian view that faith and reason are complementary. Reason operates within the framework of faith, seeking to deepen, defend, and explore the implications of what faith already holds, rather than beginning from a position of doubt.
  • Challenges of Cartesian Dualism for Christian Anthropology: Descartes' sharp conceptual separation of mind (thinking substance) from body (extended substance), portraying the human person as a mind "accidentally united" to a body, created difficulties for Christian doctrines. This view often sits uneasily with Christian teachings on the goodness of creation, the Incarnation of Christ, and the resurrection of the body, which typically emphasize a more integrated unity of body and soul.

Important Terms

  • Index of Forbidden Books (Index Librorum Prohibitorum): The official list of publications compiled by the Roman Catholic Church, designating books deemed dangerous to faith or morals, which Catholics were forbidden to read without special permission. Several of Descartes' works were added to the Index in 1663.
  • A Posteriori: Pertaining to knowledge or arguments derived from sensory experience or empirical observation. Aquinas's proofs for God's existence are a posteriori because they start from observable features of the world.
  • A Priori: Pertaining to knowledge or arguments that can be known independently of experience, through reason alone. Descartes' ontological argument for God's existence is an a priori argument, relying on the analysis of the concept of God.
  • Essence (from Latin essentia): The intrinsic nature or "whatness" of a thing, distinct from its existence. In Thomistic thought, essence and existence are distinct for created beings but identical in God. Aquinas argued against inferring God's existence directly from His essence alone because humans do not have direct access to it.
  • Fides quaerens intellectum: Latin for "Faith seeking understanding." A phrase used by Anselm to characterize the classical Christian philosophical approach where reason serves to deepen and articulate the understanding of what is already accepted by faith.
  • Anthropology (philosophical/theological): The philosophical or theological study of human nature, addressing fundamental questions about human identity, the relationship between soul and body, and the human person's place in creation or in relation to God.

Detailed Notes

Descartes the Catholic Philosopher

  • Sincere Faith: René Descartes was a committed Catholic, educated in the faith, and maintained lifelong connections with Jesuit scholars. He genuinely believed that his philosophical project would strengthen, rather than undermine, religious truths.
  • Rebuilding Foundations: He saw his new philosophy as a necessary replacement for the Aristotelian framework, which he believed was collapsing under the weight of new scientific discoveries (e.g., Galileo and Kepler). Descartes aimed to provide theology with firmer philosophical ground, built on the certainty of his cogito (the "I think") and his proofs for God's existence.
  • Dedicatory Letter: The Meditations explicitly addresses the Faculty of Theology at the University of Paris, presenting his proofs of God's existence and the soul's immortality as valuable contributions to the Church's intellectual mission.

The Irony: Condemnation by the Church

  • Index of Forbidden Books: Despite Descartes' intentions, his works, including the Meditations, were placed on the Roman Catholic Church's Index of Forbidden Books in 1663, thirteen years after his death.
  • Concern for Method: The Church's objection was not primarily to Descartes' conclusions (he affirmed God and the soul's distinctness), but to his method. Starting from radical doubt and attempting to rebuild knowledge ab initio (from scratch), without appealing to the authority of tradition or revelation, was seen as dangerous. It appeared to place individual human reason above the established teachings and authority of the Church.

Descartes and Aquinas: Two Routes to God

  • Shared Goal, Divergent Paths: Both Thomas Aquinas and Descartes sought to demonstrate God's existence and the soul's distinctness from the body, but their philosophical approaches to these truths differed profoundly.

  • Aquinas's Approach: A Posteriori

    • Observation-Based: For Aquinas, knowledge of God comes primarily from observing the created world. His famous "Five Ways" prove God's existence a posteriori—from effects to cause.
    • Observable Facts: Each of the Five Ways begins with an observable feature of the world (motion, efficient causation, contingency, degrees of perfection, or the ordered nature of things) and reasons backward to God as the ultimate, uncaused source or explanation.
    • World as a Sign: This approach firmly anchors the knowledge of God in visible creation, seeing the world as a sign pointing to its maker.
    • Rejection of Ontological Argument: Aquinas explicitly rejected Anselm's ontological argument (an a priori proof) and the Thomistic tradition generally remained skeptical of Descartes' similar a priori arguments.
    • Essence vs. Existence: The Thomistic objection is that finite human minds cannot directly grasp God's essence, thus we cannot deduce His existence simply from analyzing His concept. In creatures, essence and existence are distinct; only in God are they identical, but this identity can only be known after proving God's existence, not by conceptual analysis alone.

  • Descartes' Approach: A Priori

    • Inward Turn: For Descartes, knowledge of God primarily comes from turning inward and analyzing the ideas present in the mind.
    • Proofs from Ideas: His arguments for God's existence in Meditation III (from the idea of an infinite, perfect being) and Meditation V (the ontological argument, where existence is necessarily contained in the concept of a supremely perfect being) are both a priori. They proceed from the analysis of ideas rather than from observation of the external world.

  • Shared Ground: Natural Knowability of God: Despite their methodological disagreement, both Aquinas and Descartes agreed on one crucial point: God's existence can be known by natural reason, without recourse to faith or divine revelation. This aligns with a long-standing tradition in Catholic thought, drawing support from Paul's letter to the Romans (1:20).

The Deeper Tension: Making Reason Autonomous

  • Radical Shift: Descartes' most significant, and unintended, legacy for Christian philosophy was the autonomy he granted to individual reason. By beginning with radical doubt and rebuilding knowledge solely through "unaided reason," he implicitly made reason self-legislating and independent of theological oversight.
  • Separation of Domains: This independence was highly productive for the natural sciences, freeing mathematicians and physicists from needing Church approval for their inquiries. However, when applied to foundational questions of God, the soul, and morality, this autonomy tended to separate philosophical inquiry from the community of faith.
  • "What Can I Prove by Reason Alone?": Descartes focused exclusively on this question, consciously "bracketing" (setting aside) questions of revelation. His successors often followed suit, finding this first question sufficiently challenging, and the second ("What has God revealed?") became increasingly optional, then superfluous.
  • Unintended Skepticism towards Faith: A method powerful enough to doubt sensory experience and memory was also powerful enough to doubt the testimony of tradition and the claims of revelation. Once the individual reasoning mind was established as the ultimate arbiter of truth, it was only a matter of time before the content of faith itself was subjected to the same rigorous, and often critical, scrutiny.

A Christian Reading of Descartes Today

  • 1. Affirmation of Rational Inquiry: Descartes' proofs, regardless of their specific philosophical strengths or weaknesses, affirm a vital principle: God's existence is a legitimate philosophical question, not merely a matter of blind faith. He underscores the classical Christian view that faith and reason are not inherently in conflict and that God's existence can be rationally defended.
  • 2. Value of Clarity and Rigor: Descartes' insistence on intellectual virtues like clarity, distinctness, careful argumentation, and withholding judgment when evidence is insufficient are invaluable habits of mind. These virtues serve both good philosophy and good theology, strengthening a faith that welcomes careful rational inquiry.
  • 3. Challenges of Cartesian Dualism for Christian Anthropology: Descartes' sharp separation of mind from body, viewing a human as a "thinking substance accidentally united to a body," presents significant challenges for Christian anthropology. This picture can conflict with core Christian doctrines regarding the inherent goodness of the created body, the Incarnation of Christ (God taking on a human body), and the resurrection of the body. The Thomistic understanding of the soul as the "form of the body" is often considered more harmonious with these theological tenets. It's important to note that this theological difficulty does not necessarily invalidate Descartes' philosophical arguments, but it highlights that his account cannot be uncritically adopted as the Christian view of the human person.
  • Ongoing Dialogue: Descartes does not offer final answers but presents sharply posed questions, rigorously pursued. The continued engagement with these questions, spanning centuries, is what keeps the Meditations a living, vital text in philosophical and theological discourse.

Review Questions

  1. What was René Descartes' stated intention for his philosophical project concerning religion, and why is this intention often considered ironic in retrospect by both the Church and later philosophers?
  2. Compare and contrast the primary methods Thomas Aquinas and René Descartes used to prove God's existence. In your answer, clearly explain the difference between a priori and a posteriori arguments, giving an example from each philosopher.
  3. Explain why the Roman Catholic Church, despite Descartes being a devout Catholic who affirmed God's existence and the soul's immortality, placed his works on the Index of Forbidden Books. What specific aspect of his methodology was deemed dangerous?
  4. How did Descartes' method of "making reason autonomous" unintentionally create a deeper tension with traditional Christian philosophy regarding the relationship between faith and reason? Discuss the implications for subsequent philosophical and theological inquiry.
  5. What are some of the lasting contributions and significant challenges that Descartes' philosophy, particularly his mind-body dualism, presents for a contemporary Christian understanding of human nature and the relationship between philosophical reason and theological faith?
