Study Guide: Meditation IV: Truth and Error – Descartes on Why We Make Mistakes

Overview

This section from Descartes' Meditations tackles a fundamental theological and philosophical problem: if an all-perfect God created us, why do human beings constantly fall into error? Descartes resolves this by distinguishing between the finite nature of human understanding and the infinite scope of human free will. He concludes that error arises not from a defect in God's creation, but from our misuse of our faculties, specifically when our boundless will makes judgments on matters not fully comprehended by our limited intellect.

Key Concepts

  • God as Non-Deceiver: Descartes reaffirms that God, being a supremely perfect being, cannot possess any imperfection, including the desire or ability to deceive. Deception implies malice or feebleness, which are contrary to God's nature.
  • Humanity's Intermediate Nature: Humans exist as something "intermediate" between God (supreme perfection) and "nothing" (non-being or infinite imperfection). This inherent limitation is why we are susceptible to error.
  • Error as a Privation/Defect: Error is not a positive faculty given by God, nor is it a "real thing" created by Him. Instead, it is a lack or privation of knowledge that we should possess, akin to a defect, stemming from our finite nature.
  • Limits of Human Understanding (Intellect): Our intellectual faculty, responsible for apprehending ideas, is finite and limited in its scope. It is perfect of its kind, but it cannot grasp everything, especially the infinite reasons behind God's actions.
  • Infinity of Human Will (Free Will/Liberty): The human will, or faculty of choice, is uniquely boundless and extensive, so much so that it reflects the image and similitude of God. It is the capacity to affirm or deny, pursue or shun, without external constraint.
  • True Liberty vs. Indifference: Descartes argues that true liberty is not indifference (being equally inclined to choose between two options due to lack of knowledge). Instead, genuine freedom is enhanced when we clearly perceive the good and the true, leading us to embrace them without hesitation. Indifference is the lowest grade of liberty, indicative of a defect in knowledge.
  • The Origin of Error: Error occurs when our infinite will extends beyond the finite bounds of our understanding, affirming or denying things that the intellect does not clearly and distinctly perceive. It's the misalignment between these two faculties that leads to mistakes.

Important Terms

  • Deceiver: One who intentionally misleads or causes others to err. Descartes argues God cannot be a deceiver due to His perfect nature.
  • Error: For Descartes, a privation or lack of knowledge, specifically a wrong judgment, rather than an inherent faculty. It implies a deviation from truth.
  • Perfection: The state of being complete, without defect or flaw. God is defined as supremely perfect.
  • Non-being (Nought): The absolute absence of existence or perfection. Humans are seen as intermediate between God (supreme being) and non-being.
  • Understanding (Intellect): The mental faculty responsible for apprehending ideas, perceiving concepts, and forming clear and distinct perceptions. It does not affirm or deny on its own.
  • Will (Free Will/Liberty of Choice): The mental faculty responsible for making judgments, affirming or denying, choosing to act or not to act. Descartes considers it infinite in its scope.
  • Final Causes: The ultimate purpose or end for which something exists or happens. Descartes suggests that attempting to understand God's final causes is beyond human capacity and "temerity."
  • Indifference: In the context of free will, a state of being equally inclined towards either choice, often due to a lack of clear reasons. Descartes considers this the lowest form of liberty, indicating a deficiency in understanding.

Detailed Notes

I. Reaffirming God's Perfection and Non-Deceiving Nature

Descartes begins by reinforcing a prior conclusion: God is not a deceiver. This is based on the premise that any form of fraud or deception arises from imperfection, such as malice or feebleness. Since God is a supremely perfect being, these imperfections cannot be attributed to Him. Therefore, the faculties we possess, having been received from God, cannot inherently lead us into error if used correctly.

II. The Problem of Error and Human Imperfection

This premise immediately raises a new problem: if God is perfect and created us, why do we constantly make errors? Descartes acknowledges the paradox.

  • He observes that while directing his mind wholly to God, he finds no cause for error. Yet, human experience undeniably shows a "multitude of errors."
  • Descartes proposes that humans are "intermediate" beings, positioned between God (supreme perfection, pure being) and "nothing" (non-being, infinitely removed from perfection).
  • Error, in this context, is not a positive thing created by God, nor is it a special faculty given for the purpose of erring. Instead, error is a defect or a privation – a lack of knowledge that we seemingly ought to possess. Our susceptibility to error stems from our finite, imperfect nature, which partakes in "nothingness" to some degree.

III. Addressing God's Design and Human Limitations

Descartes then explores why God might have created beings capable of error if He is truly perfect and always wills what is best.

  • Incomprehensibility of God's Ways: He argues that it is audacious for humans, with their "feeble and limited" nature, to presume to understand the "immense, incomprehensible, and infinite" nature of God. Many of God's actions and creations will transcend our knowledge. He specifically warns against seeking "final causes" (God's purposes) in physical things, as this exceeds our understanding.
  • Perfection of the Whole Universe: Descartes suggests that we should not assess the perfection of God's works by considering a single creature (like a human) in isolation. What might seem imperfect on its own could be perfectly designed as part of a larger, interconnected universe. God's perfection is reflected in the totality of creation, not necessarily in every single component when viewed separately. It's possible that a universe containing finite, erring beings is, on the whole, more perfect than one where error is impossible.

IV. The Two Faculties: Understanding and Will

Descartes identifies the root of error in the interaction of two distinct human faculties: the understanding (intellect) and the will (free will or liberty of choice).

  • A. The Understanding (Intellect):
    • Function: The understanding's role is to apprehend the ideas of things – to perceive them, not to affirm or deny them. It is the source of our concepts and perceptions.
    • Nature: It is finite and limited. There are infinitely many things we do not comprehend. However, the understanding is perfect of its kind; it correctly apprehends what it does apprehend. There is no error within the understanding itself, provided it only presents ideas without judgment. God is not obliged to give us an infinite understanding.
  • B. The Will (Free Will/Liberty of Choice):
    • Function: The will's role is to make judgments – to affirm or deny, to pursue or shun, based on the ideas presented by the understanding.
    • Nature: Unlike the understanding, the will is extraordinarily vast, boundless, and practically infinite in its scope. Descartes finds that his will is so extensive that he cannot conceive of anything greater, and this resemblance to God's infinite will is a key aspect of our "image and similitude" to God.
    • True Liberty: Descartes clarifies that true liberty is not a state of indifference (where one has no clear reason to choose one option over another). Indifference is, in fact, the lowest grade of liberty, indicating a lack or defect in knowledge. Instead, true freedom is enhanced when our understanding clearly perceives what is true and good. The more clearly we understand the reasons for a choice, the more freely and spontaneously we embrace it, without feeling constrained. Divine grace and natural knowledge, far from diminishing liberty, actually increase and strengthen it.

V. The Mechanism of Error

Descartes concludes that neither the understanding nor the will is inherently the source of error when considered individually.

  • The understanding, when apprehending correctly, does not err.
  • The will, being ample and perfect of its kind, is simply the power of choice.
  • The problem arises from their interaction: The will is much wider in its range and scope than the understanding. When the will extends beyond the clear and distinct perceptions of the understanding and makes judgments (affirms or denies) about things that are not fully comprehended, it easily falls into error (or sin). This occurs when we choose to believe or disbelieve, or act upon, ideas that are obscure, confused, or not fully understood by our intellect.

In essence, human error is a consequence of the disproportion between our finite understanding and our infinite will, leading us to assent to propositions that our intellect has not adequately scrutinized.

Review Questions

  1. According to Descartes, why is it impossible for God to be a deceiver? How does this initial premise set up the problem of human error?
  2. Descartes describes human beings as "intermediate" between God and "nothing." Explain what he means by this and how this intermediate nature contributes to our capacity for error.
  3. How does Descartes reconcile God's perfection with the existence of human error? What two main arguments does he offer to explain why God might create erring beings?
  4. Distinguish between the roles and natures of the human understanding (intellect) and the will (free will) as described by Descartes. Which faculty is limited, and which is effectively infinite?
  5. Explain Descartes' final conclusion regarding the precise mechanism by which errors occur. Why is indifference considered the "lowest grade of liberty" in his view, and how does true liberty relate to clear knowledge?
