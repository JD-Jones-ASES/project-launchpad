Chapter 7, Section 2: Meditation VI: Mind and Body

Overview

This section delves into the enduring philosophical problem of the mind and body, initiated by Descartes, by first examining the contrasting perspective of Thomas Aquinas. It explores Aquinas's hylomorphic understanding of the soul as the "form of the body," arguing for a substantial unity between the intellectual principle and the body, a view that significantly differs from Cartesian dualism and sets the stage for early modern and contemporary debates.

Key Concepts

  • Mind-Body Problem: The philosophical inquiry into the relationship between the mind (or consciousness) and the body (or brain). This problem, as framed by Descartes, typically investigates how two distinct substances—a non-physical mind and a physical body—can interact.
  • Hylomorphism: An ancient philosophical theory, derived from Aristotle and adopted by Aquinas, stating that every physical substance is composed of two intrinsic principles: hyle (matter) and morphe (form). Form is the organizing principle that makes matter into a specific kind of thing.
  • Soul as the Form of the Body (Aquinas): In the context of living beings, Aquinas argues that the soul is not a separate entity housed within the body (as in Descartes's dualism) but rather the form of the body. It is the principle that organizes a body's matter, making it a living, sensing, and understanding human being, rather than a mere aggregate of parts.
  • Intellectual Principle: Aquinas's term for the faculty of understanding or the rational soul. A core part of his argument is demonstrating that this intellectual principle is integrally united with the body as its form, even while possessing operations (like universal understanding) that transcend purely material processes.
  • Substantial Unity of Man: Aquinas's position that a human being is not merely a soul inhabiting a body, but a single, unified substance composed of both matter and form, where the intellectual soul serves as the form. This unity explains how a person can both sense (a corporeal act) and understand (an intellectual act) as one and the same individual.

Important Terms

  • Mind-Body Problem: The philosophical question concerning the relationship between mental phenomena (consciousness, thoughts, feelings) and physical phenomena (the body, brain, nervous system).
  • Hylomorphism: A philosophical doctrine asserting that individual substances are compounds of matter and form.
  • Form (in Hylomorphism): The organizing principle or essence that structures matter, making it into a particular kind of thing. For living beings, the soul is considered the form.
  • Matter (in Hylomorphism): The indeterminate "stuff" or potentiality that receives form to become a definite substance.
  • Soul (Aquinas's view): The first principle of life and the substantial form of the body, responsible for all vital operations including nutrition, sensation, movement, and intellect. It is not a separate entity imprisoned in the body but its animating and organizing essence.
  • Intellectual Principle: The faculty of human reason and understanding, which Aquinas argues is the highest aspect of the human soul.
  • Descartes: René Descartes (1596-1650), a French philosopher and mathematician, known for his rigorous dualism, which posited mind and body as two distinct substances.
  • Aquinas: Thomas Aquinas (c. 1225-1274), an Italian Dominican friar, philosopher, and theologian, whose work synthesized Aristotelian philosophy with Christian doctrine, developing the most influential medieval account of the soul.
  • Aristotle: Ancient Greek philosopher (384-322 BCE) whose hylomorphic theory significantly influenced Aquinas's understanding of the soul.

Detailed Notes

The Mind-Body Problem: A Historical Context

The mind-body problem, centrally posed by Descartes, examines the fundamental nature of mental and physical realities and their interaction. However, to grasp Descartes' radical departure, it's crucial to understand the dominant framework that preceded him: the hylomorphism of Aquinas, rooted in Aristotle.

Aquinas on the Soul: A Hylomorphic Framework

Unlike Descartes's view of the soul as a separate, distinct substance from the body, Aquinas adhered to an Aristotelian hylomorphic understanding.

  • Hylomorphism Defined: Every physical substance is a composite of matter (the stuff it's made of) and form (the organizing principle that makes it the kind of thing it is).
  • The Soul as Form: For living beings, the soul is the form of the body. It's not a ghost in the machine but the principle that animates and organizes matter into a living being.
  • Not a Prisoner: Aquinas rejects the idea of the soul being a prisoner of the body. Instead, it is what makes the body what it is. Just as a knife's form (its shape and sharpness) is integral to it being a knife, the soul is integral to a human body being a living human body.

Aquinas: Summa Theologica I, Q. 76, Art. 1 - "Whether the Intellectual Principle Is United to the Body As Its Form?"

Aquinas presents a rigorous argument to establish that the intellectual principle (the rational soul) is indeed the form of the human body. He begins by addressing several objections.

Objections to the Intellectual Principle as the Body's Form:

  1. Intellect is Separate: Aristotle stated the intellect is "separate" and "not the act of any body."
  2. Intellect's Unlimited Knowledge: Forms are determined by specific matter. If the intellect were a form, it would have a determinate nature and couldn't know "all things" (universals and immaterial objects).
  3. Immaterial Reception: Intellectual reception is "immaterially and universally," unlike bodily forms which receive "materially and individually."
  4. Intellectual Action is Not Corporeal: Intellectual action isn't an action of a body; therefore, the intellectual faculty (and essence) isn't of the body.
  5. Per Se Existence: The intellectual principle has per se (by itself) existence and is subsistent, but a form's existence belongs to the composite, not the form by itself.
  6. Incorruptibility: Forms cannot exist without their proper matter. But the intellectual principle is incorruptible and remains after the body's dissolution, implying it's not a form of the body.

On the Contrary: Aristotle's Argument

Aquinas counters by citing Aristotle: the "rational" difference (which defines man) is derived from man's form. Thus, the intellectual principle must be man's form.

Aquinas's Answer: The Core Argument

Aquinas asserts that the intellect, the principle of intellectual operation, is the form of the human body.

  • Principle of Action: That by which a thing primarily acts is its form (e.g., health is the form by which a body heals; knowledge is the form by which the soul knows).
  • Soul as Primary Principle of Life: The soul is the primary principle by which the body lives and performs all vital actions (nourishment, sensation, local movement, and understanding).
  • Conclusion: Therefore, the intellectual principle (or intellectual soul) is the form of the body. This is Aristotle's demonstration.

The Problem of "This Particular Man Understands"

Aquinas emphasizes that "each one is conscious that it is himself who understands." This action of understanding must be attributed to the individual man, not accidentally.

  • Refutation of Plato: Plato suggested man is the intellectual soul. Aquinas rejects this because the same man senses (which requires a body) and understands. The body must be a part of man.
  • Intellect as a Part, United to the Body: It follows that the intellect is a part of Socrates, united to his body.

Refutation of Alternative Modes of Union:

Aquinas then systematically refutes other ways the intellect might be united to the body:

  1. Union through Intelligible Species (The Commentator): This view holds that the intellect is linked to the body through "intelligible species" (abstract representations) in corporeal organs (phantasms).
    • Aquinas's Rejection: This union is insufficient. Just as a wall doesn't "see" because colors are on it, the fact that phantasms are in a man doesn't mean he understands, but rather that he or his phantasms are understood. It doesn't explain how the act of understanding is the act of Socrates.
  2. Union as a Motor (Intellect Moves the Body): Some proposed the intellect is united to the body as its mover, making them a single whole.
    • Aquinas's Rejection (Multiple Reasons):
      • Circular Reasoning: Intellect moves the body through appetite, which presupposes intellectual operation. Socrates understands before he is moved by his intellect.
      • Action Remains in Agent: The action of understanding remains in the agent (the intellect), unlike the action of heating (which passes to something else). Therefore, understanding cannot be attributed to Socrates because he is moved by his intellect.
      • Instrumental Relation: If understanding is attributed to Socrates as the action of what moves him, it's instrumental (like a saw to a carpenter). But understanding doesn't happen through a corporeal instrument.
      • Lack of Absolute Unity: If the intellect is only a motor, Socrates is not "one absolutely," and therefore not a "being absolutely," as being implies unity.

Final Conclusion: The Intellectual Principle as Form

Aquinas concludes that the only explanation is Aristotle's: this particular man understands because the intellectual principle is his form.

  • Operation Reveals Nature: Man's proper operation is to understand, surpassing all other animals. This operation defines his species.
  • Species from Form: The species of anything is derived from its form.
  • Ultimate Conclusion: Therefore, the intellectual principle is the proper form of man, constituting his very nature and species.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental difference between Descartes's approach to the mind-body problem and Aquinas's hylomorphic view of the soul.
  2. What does Aquinas mean when he says the soul is the "form of the body"? Provide an analogy to illustrate this concept.
  3. Summarize two of the objections Aquinas raises against the idea that the intellectual principle is the form of the body. How does he generally respond to these types of objections?
  4. Aquinas argues that "each one is conscious that it is himself who understands." How does this premise support his conclusion that the intellectual principle must be united to the body as its form, and what alternative views does it help him refute?
  5. Why does Aquinas reject the idea that the intellect is united to the body merely as a "motor"? Provide at least two reasons from his argument.
