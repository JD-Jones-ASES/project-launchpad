Study Guide: Scatter Plots and Trend Lines

This study guide explores the relationship between variables in real-world data sets. While mathematical equations often represent perfect lines, real-world observations typically follow approximately linear patterns. Scatter plots and trend lines provide the tools necessary to visualize, describe, and make predictions based on these patterns.

I. Fundamental Concepts

Scatter Plots

A scatter plot is a coordinate plane graph consisting of data points $(x, y)$. Each point represents a single observation of two related variables. Unlike the graphs of linear equations, scatter plots show how data is distributed rather than a single, continuous line.

Types of Correlation

Correlation describes the general direction or relationship between the variables in a scatter plot. There are three primary types:

Correlation Type Description Visual Trend
Positive Correlation As $x$ increases, $y$ tends to increase. Points trend upward from left to right.
Negative Correlation As $x$ increases, $y$ tends to decrease. Points trend downward from left to right.
No Correlation There is no clear upward or downward trend. Points are scattered with no obvious pattern.

Trend Lines (Lines of Best Fit)

A trend line, or line of best fit, is a straight line drawn through the data points on a scatter plot. The goal of a trend line is to follow the general direction of the data. A well-placed trend line should have roughly the same number of data points above it as it has below it.

II. Working with Trend Lines

Writing the Equation of a Trend Line

To model a data set, you can write the equation of a trend line by selecting two points on or near the line and using the point-slope form.

Example: Study Hours vs. Test Scores Consider the following data representing student performance:

Hours Studied ($x$) Test Score ($y$)
1 55
2 62
3 70
4 78
5 80
6 85
  1. Identify Correlation: As study hours increase, scores increase, indicating a positive correlation.
  2. Calculate Slope ($m$): Using points $(1, 55)$ and $(5, 80)$: $m = \frac{80 - 55}{5 - 1} = \frac{25}{4} = 6.25$
  3. Write the Equation: Use point-slope form with $(1, 55)$: $y - 55 = 6.25(x - 1)$ $y = 6.25x + 48.75$

Making Predictions

Once a trend line equation is established, it can be used to predict values for the dependent variable ($y$) based on given values of the independent variable ($x$).

  • Prediction for 8 hours of study: $y = 6.25(8) + 48.75 = 50 + 48.75 = 98.75$ (approximately a score of 99).
  • Prediction for 85°F temperature (using $y = 8x - 200$): $y = 8(85) - 200 = 680 - 200 = 480$ visitors.

III. Interpolation vs. Extrapolation

When using a trend line for predictions, it is important to distinguish between interpolation and extrapolation:

  • Interpolation: Estimating a value between existing data points. This is generally considered reliable because the prediction remains within the range of observed data. (Example: Predicting a score for 3.5 hours of study when the data ranges from 1 to 6 hours).
  • Extrapolation: Estimating a value beyond the observed data range. This is considered risky because the linear trend may not continue indefinitely. (Example: Predicting a score for 12 hours of study results in 123.75, which is impossible for a test score).

IV. Critical Analysis: Correlation and Causation

A critical rule of data analysis is that correlation does not imply causation. A strong relationship between two variables does not necessarily mean that one variable causes the change in the other.

Illustrative Example: Ice cream sales and drowning incidents show a strong positive correlation. However, buying ice cream does not cause drowning. Instead, both variables are influenced by a third factor: hot weather. Analysts must always think critically about whether a true cause-and-effect relationship exists.

V. Short-Answer Practice Quiz

  1. Define a scatter plot.
  2. What determines if a scatter plot has a negative correlation?
  3. How do you determine where to draw a trend line on a scatter plot?
  4. What is the difference between interpolation and extrapolation?

  6. If a trend line equation is $y = 8x - 200$, what is the predicted $y$ value when $x = 85$?

VI. Essay Questions for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Limits of Linear Models: Discuss why the linear trend for test scores ($y = 6.25x + 48.75$) failed when predicting the score for 12 hours of study. What does this reveal about the limitations of using trend lines for extrapolation in real-world scenarios?
  2. Causation vs. Correlation: Using the example of ice cream sales and drowning incidents, explain the importance of identifying "hidden variables" or third factors. Why is it dangerous for a researcher to assume that a strong correlation indicates a direct cause-and-effect relationship?
  3. The Role of the Trend Line: Explain the criteria for a "good" trend line. Why is it important for the line to have roughly the same number of points above and below it, and how does this line help researchers move from raw data to functional predictions?

VII. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Correlation: The general direction or relationship followed by the points in a scatter plot (positive, negative, or none).
  • Correlation Coefficient ($r$): A precise numerical measure of how closely data follows a linear trend (introduced in advanced courses).
  • Extrapolation: The process of estimating a value outside the range of observed data points.
  • Interpolation: The process of estimating a value within the range of observed data points.
  • Line of Best Fit: Another name for a trend line; a straight line that represents the general direction of data on a scatter plot.
  • Negative Correlation: A relationship where $y$ decreases as $x$ increases, shown by points trending downward.
  • Positive Correlation: A relationship where $y$ increases as $x$ increases, shown by points trending upward.
  • Scatter Plot: A coordinate graph where each point $(x, y)$ represents an observation of two related variables.
  • Trend Line: A straight line drawn through data points on a scatter plot to model the linear pattern of the data.
