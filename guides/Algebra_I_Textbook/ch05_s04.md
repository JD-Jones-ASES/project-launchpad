# Study Guide: Applications of Systems of Equations

This study guide focuses on the practical application of systems of equations to solve real-world problems involving two unknown quantities. By translating word problems into mathematical conditions, complex scenarios can be solved systematically using established algebraic methods.

## Core Strategy for Solving Word Problems

When addressing real-world applications, a structured approach ensures accuracy and clarity. The following five-step strategy is used to set up and solve systems:

1.  **Identify Unknowns:** Read the problem carefully to determine the two unknown quantities and assign a unique variable to each.
2.  **Find Relationships:** Identify the two conditions or relationships that connect these unknowns.
3.  **Write Equations:** Translate each relationship into a mathematical equation.
4.  **Solve the System:** Use algebraic methods such as **substitution** or **elimination** to find the values of the variables.
5.  **Check the Answer:** Verify that the solution makes sense within the specific context of the original problem.

## Primary Application Types

The source context identifies three distinct types of problems that are commonly solved using systems of equations:

### 1. Mixture Problems (Coins)
These problems typically involve a total count of items and a total value.
*   **Condition 1:** The sum of the number of items equals the total quantity (e.g., $n + d = 20$).
*   **Condition 2:** The sum of the values of the items equals the total monetary value, often converted to cents to simplify calculations (e.g., $5n + 10d = 135$).

### 2. Distance-Rate-Time Problems
These problems involve objects moving in relation to one another.
*   **Opposite Directions:** When two objects move in opposite directions from the same point, their individual distances are added together to find the total distance between them. 
*   **Equation Structure:** $(Rate_1 \times Time) + (Rate_2 \times Time) = Total Distance$.

### 3. Cost and Break-Even Problems
These problems compare two different services or options to find the point where they cost the same.
*   **Equation Structure:** Total Cost ($T$) = Setup Fee + (Rate per unit $\times$ Number of units).
*   **The Break-Even Point:** The solution is found by setting the two cost equations equal to one another ($T_1 = T_2$) to solve for the number of units.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

**Question 1:** A student has 20 coins consisting of quarters and dimes, totaling $3.50. Write the two equations needed to find the number of quarters ($q$) and dimes ($d$).
*   **Answer:** $q + d = 20$ and $25q + 10d = 350$.

**Question 2:** If two cyclists start from the same point and ride in opposite directions—one at 12 mph and the other at 8 mph—how many hours ($t$) will it take for them to be 50 miles apart?
*   **Answer:** 2.5 hours (Calculated as $20t = 50$).

**Question 3:** Company A charges a $50 setup fee and $0.75 per copy. Company B charges $20 for setup and $1.25 per copy. At how many copies ($c$) is the cost identical for both companies?
*   **Answer:** 60 copies.

**Question 4:** In the coin mixture example involving 13 nickels and 7 dimes for a total of $1.35, what is the specific equation used to represent the total value in cents?
*   **Answer:** $5n + 10d = 135$.

## Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Break-Even Point** | The specific value (such as number of copies) at which two different cost structures result in the exact same total cost. |
| **Condition** | A specific relationship or constraint in a word problem that connects the unknown quantities. |
| **Elimination** | A method of solving a system of equations by adding or subtracting equations to remove one variable. |
| **Rate** | A constant value multiplied by a variable, such as cost per copy or miles per hour. |
| **Substitution** | A method of solving a system where one equation is solved for a variable, and that expression is then plugged into the second equation. |
| **System of Equations** | A set of two or more equations with the same variables that are solved simultaneously to find a common solution. |
| **Unknowns** | The specific quantities in a problem that are not yet determined, typically represented by variables like $x$, $y$, $n$, or $d$. |
