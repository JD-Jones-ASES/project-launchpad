Study Guide: Percent Applications and Simple Interest

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of percent applications, including the fundamental percent equation, calculations for percent increase and decrease, and the application of the simple interest formula. Use this document to master the mathematical principles required for solving practical problems involving taxes, discounts, and interest rates.

Core Concepts and Foundations

Understanding Percents

The term "percent" literally translates to "per hundred." Therefore, any percentage represents a ratio of a number compared to 100.

  • Example: $45%$ is equivalent to $\frac{45}{100}$ or $0.45$.

The Percent Equation

To utilize percents within mathematical equations, they must first be converted into decimals or fractions. The relationship between a part, a percent, and a whole is defined by the following equation:

$$\text{part} = \text{percent} \times \text{whole}$$

In algebraic terms, where $p$ represents the percent (as a decimal) and $w$ represents the whole: $$part = pw$$

Calculations and Procedures

Finding a Part

To find a specific portion of a whole number, multiply the whole by the decimal form of the percentage.

  • Procedure: To find $35%$ of $80$, convert $35%$ to $0.35$ and multiply: $0.35 \times 80 = 28$.

Finding a Percent

To determine what percentage one number is of another, divide the part by the whole and convert the resulting decimal back into a percentage.

  • Procedure: To find what percent $12$ is of $50$, divide $12$ by $50$ to get $0.24$. Convert $0.24$ to $24%$.

Percent Increase and Decrease

Calculating the percentage of change between an original value and a new value requires a two-step process:

  1. Find the amount of change: Subtract the smaller value from the larger value.
  2. Divide the change by the original amount: Always use the original value as the divisor.
Scenario Calculation Steps Example
Percent Decrease $\frac{\text{Original Price} - \text{Sale Price}}{\text{Original Price}}$ A $$60$ jacket on sale for $$45$: $\frac{15}{60} = 0.25$ or $25%$
Percent Increase $\frac{\text{New Price} - \text{Original Price}}{\text{Original Price}}$ A $$40$ shirt now costing $$52$: $\frac{12}{40} = 0.30$ or $30%$

Simple Interest

Simple interest is a fundamental application of the percent equation used to calculate earnings on an investment or costs of a loan.

The Simple Interest Formula

$$I = Prt$$

  • $I$ (Interest): The amount of interest earned or charged.
  • $P$ (Principal): The starting amount of money.
  • $r$ (Annual Interest Rate): The rate expressed as a decimal (e.g., $4%$ becomes $0.04$).
  • $t$ (Time): The duration of the investment or loan in years.

Essential Distinction: Simple vs. Compound Interest

This guide focuses exclusively on Simple Interest, which is modeled by linear functions. This differs from Compound Interest, which is studied in advanced algebra and modeled by exponential functions because it involves interest earning additional interest over time.

Short-Answer Practice Quiz

1. What is the decimal equivalent of $18%$?

  • Answer: $0.18$

2. Solve for the part: What is $18%$ of $250$?

  • Answer: $45$ ($0.18 \times 250$)

3. Solve for the percent: $12$ is what percent of $50$?

  • Answer: $24%$ ($12 \div 50 = 0.24$)

4. If an item originally priced at $$40$ is increased to $$52$, what is the percent increase?

  • Answer: $30%$ ($\text{Change of } 12 \div 40 = 0.30$)

5. In the simple interest formula $I = Prt$, what must you do to the interest rate ($r$) before substituting it into the equation?

  • Answer: Convert the percent to a decimal (e.g., $4%$ to $0.04$).

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Role of the "Whole" in Percent Change: Explain why it is critical to divide by the original amount rather than the new amount when calculating percent increase or decrease. Provide a hypothetical example where using the wrong divisor would lead to an incorrect business conclusion.
  2. Linear vs. Exponential Growth in Interest: Based on the distinction made between simple and compound interest, describe the fundamental difference in how money grows under these two systems. Why is simple interest considered a "linear" application?
  3. Practicality of Percentages: Discuss how the percent equation ($part = percent \times whole$) serves as a foundation for daily financial decisions, such as calculating tips, sales tax, or discounts.

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Interest ($I$) The amount of money earned or paid for the use of a principal amount.
Percent A ratio that means "per hundred."
Percent Equation The mathematical relationship where the part is equal to the percent (as a decimal) multiplied by the whole.
Principal ($P$) The initial or starting amount of money in an interest calculation.
Rate ($r$) The annual interest percentage, which must be converted to a decimal for use in formulas.
Simple Interest A method of calculating interest where interest is only earned on the original principal, modeled by a linear function.
Time ($t$) The period, measured in years, over which interest is calculated.
Whole The total amount or original value that a percentage is applied to.
