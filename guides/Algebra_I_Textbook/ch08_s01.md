# Study Guide: Solving Quadratic Equations by Factoring

This study guide focuses on the methodology of solving quadratic equations using factoring techniques. It covers the foundational definitions, the governing properties of real numbers used in this process, and the specific steps required to reach a solution.

---

## Key Concepts

### 1. The Quadratic Equation
A quadratic equation is any equation that can be written in the standard form:
\[ ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \]
Where:
*   $a, b,$ and $c$ are real numbers.
*   $a \neq 0$.
*   The highest power of the variable is $2$.

### 2. The Zero Product Property
The Zero Product Property is the theoretical basis for solving quadratics by factoring. It states that if $a$ and $b$ are real numbers and $ab = 0$, then $a = 0$ or $b = 0$ (or both). In practical terms, if the product of two factors equals zero, at least one of those factors must be zero.

### 3. Procedural Steps for Solving by Factoring
To solve a quadratic equation using this method, follow these five steps:
1.  **Standard Form:** Rewrite the equation so that one side equals zero ($ax^2 + bx + c = 0$).
2.  **Factor:** Factor the non-zero side of the equation completely.
3.  **Apply Zero Product Property:** Set each individual factor equal to zero.
4.  **Solve:** Solve the resulting linear equations for the variable.
5.  **Check:** Substitute the solutions back into the original equation to verify their validity.

### 4. Special Considerations
*   **Factoring out the GCF:** If all terms in the equation share a common factor, factor out the Greatest Common Factor (GCF) first. This simplifies the remaining quadratic expression and makes it easier to factor further.
*   **The Division Caution:** Never divide both sides of an equation by a variable expression. Doing so can result in the loss of a valid solution (typically the solution where the variable equals zero). Always use factoring to handle variables present in multiple terms.

## Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Quadratic Equation** | An equation of the degree 2, typically written in the form $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$. |
| **Standard Form** | The specific arrangement of a quadratic equation where all terms are on one side, set equal to zero. |
| **Zero Product Property** | A property stating that if the product of multiple factors is zero, then at least one factor must be zero. |
| **Greatest Common Factor (GCF)** | The highest factor that is common to all terms in a polynomial expression. |
| **Factor** | To break down an expression into a product of simpler expressions or numbers. |

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. Write the standard form of a quadratic equation.**
*(Answer: $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$, where $a \neq 0$.)*

**2. Solve the equation $x^2 - 7x + 12 = 0$ by factoring.**
*(Answer: $(x - 3)(x - 4) = 0$; therefore, $x = 3$ or $x = 4$.)*

**3. What is the first step in solving the equation $x^2 + 3x = 10$?**
*(Answer: Subtract 10 from both sides to write the equation in standard form: $x^2 + 3x - 10 = 0$.)*

**4. Solve for $x$ in the equation $2x^2 + 10x = 0$.**
*(Answer: Factor out the GCF: $2x(x + 5) = 0$. The solutions are $x = 0$ and $x = -5$.)*

**5. If you solve $x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0$ and find the factors $(x - 2)$ and $(x - 3)$, what are the final solutions?**
*(Answer: $x = 2$ and $x = 3$.)*

**6. Why is it incorrect to divide both sides of $3x^2 - 12x = 0$ by $x$?**
*(Answer: Dividing by the variable $x$ causes the loss of the solution $x = 0$.)*
