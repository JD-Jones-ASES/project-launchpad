Study Guide: Adding and Subtracting Polynomials

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the fundamental principles of polynomials as outlined in Chapter 6, Section 4. It covers definitions, classification systems, operational procedures for addition and subtraction, and the mathematical properties governing these expressions.

I. Core Mathematical Concepts

1. Definitions of Terms

  • Monomial: A monomial is a single mathematical unit that can be a number, a variable, or the product of a number and one or more variables. Crucially, these variables must be raised to whole-number exponents.
    • Examples: $7$, $-3x$, $4a^{2}b^{3}$.
    • Non-examples: $5x^{-1}$ (contains a negative exponent) or expressions with fractional exponents.
  • Polynomial: A polynomial is either a single monomial or the sum of multiple monomials.
  • Terms: Each individual monomial within a polynomial is referred to as a "term."
  • Like Terms: These are terms that share the exact same variable(s) raised to the exact same power(s). Only like terms can be combined during addition or subtraction.

2. Determining Degree

The "degree" is a measurement of the complexity of the variables within an expression:

  • Degree of a Monomial: Calculated by finding the sum of the exponents of all its variables.
    • A nonzero constant (e.g., the number $5$) has a degree of $0$.
    • The number $0$ is unique in that it has no degree.
  • Degree of a Polynomial: This is determined by the highest degree of any single term within the polynomial.

3. Standard Form

A polynomial is organized in standard form when its terms are placed in descending order based on their degree, moving from the greatest degree to the least degree.

  • Example: $5x^{3} - 2x^{2} + x - 7$ is in standard form because the exponents decrease ($3, 2, 1, 0$).

II. Classification of Polynomials

Polynomials are categorized based on two primary criteria: the number of terms they contain and their highest degree.

III. Operations: Addition and Subtraction

1. Adding Polynomials

To add polynomials, the primary objective is to identify and group like terms.

  • Process:
    1. Group terms with the same variables and exponents.
    2. Combine the coefficients of those terms.
  • Example: $(3x^{2} + 5x - 4) + (x^{2} - 2x + 7)$
    • Grouped: $(3x^{2} + x^{2}) + (5x - 2x) + (-4 + 7)$
    • Result: $4x^{2} + 3x + 3$

2. Subtracting Polynomials

Subtraction requires an extra initial step to ensure the signs are handled correctly.

  • Process:
    1. Distribute the negative sign (multiply by $-1$) to every term in the polynomial being subtracted.
    2. Combine the resulting like terms.
  • Example: $(5x^{2} + 3x - 2) - (2x^{2} - x + 6)$
    • Distribute: $5x^{2} + 3x - 2 - 2x^{2} + x - 6$
    • Grouped: $(5x^{2} - 2x^{2}) + (3x + x) + (-2 - 6)$
    • Result: $3x^{2} + 4x - 8$

3. The Property of Closure

Polynomials are closed under addition and subtraction. This mathematical property means that whenever you add or subtract two polynomials, the resulting expression is guaranteed to be another polynomial.

IV. Short-Answer Practice Quiz

  1. Define a monomial and provide an example of an expression that is not a monomial.
  2. Calculate the degree of the following monomial: $4a^{2}b^{3}$.
  3. Identify the classification of the polynomial $7n$ by both degree and number of terms.
  4. Rewrite the following in standard form: $x - 2x^{2} + x^{3} + 5$.
  5. Simplify the expression: $(4n^{3} - n + 5) - (n^{3} + 3n - 8)$.
  6. True or False: A nonzero constant has a degree of 1.
  7. What is the degree of a cubic binomial?
  8. Explain the rule for combining terms during the addition of polynomials.

V. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Concept of Closure: Explain the significance of polynomials being "closed" under addition and subtraction. Why is it important for mathematicians to know that the sum or difference of two polynomials will always result in another polynomial?
  2. Structural Analysis: Compare and contrast the methods for adding and subtracting polynomials. Why is the distribution of the negative sign critical in subtraction, and what common errors might occur if this step is bypassed?
  3. Classification Systems: Describe how the degree of a polynomial is determined differently than the degree of a single monomial. Provide an example of a multi-variable polynomial and explain how to identify its degree and its classification based on the number of terms.

VI. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Binomial: A polynomial consisting of exactly two terms.
  • Closure: A property stating that an operation on members of a set (like polynomials) always produces a member of that same set.
  • Cubic: A polynomial of degree 3.
  • Degree of a Monomial: The sum of the exponents of the variables in the monomial.
  • Degree of a Polynomial: The highest degree of any term within the polynomial.
  • Linear: A polynomial of degree 1.
  • Monomial: A number, variable, or product of numbers and variables with whole-number exponents.
  • Polynomial: A monomial or a sum of monomials.
  • Quadratic: A polynomial of degree 2.
  • Standard Form: A format where polynomial terms are ordered from the highest degree to the lowest degree.
  • Term: An individual monomial that forms part of a polynomial sum.
  • Trinomial: A polynomial consisting of exactly three terms.
