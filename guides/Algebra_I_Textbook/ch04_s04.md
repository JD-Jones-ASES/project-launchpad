# Study Guide: Writing Linear Equations

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of writing linear equations, as outlined in Chapter 4, Section 4. It covers the three primary forms of linear equations, methodologies for deriving equations from specific data points, and the practical utility of each form.

## Key Concepts and Equation Forms

There are three standard forms used to write linear equations. While they all describe the same line and represents the same mathematical relationship, each form is uniquely suited for different types of initial information or mathematical tasks.

### 1. Slope-Intercept Form
The slope-intercept form is the most commonly used version because its primary components are immediately visible.
*   **Equation:** $y = mx + b$
*   **Components:** 
    *   $m$ represents the **slope**.
    *   $b$ represents the **$y$-intercept** (the $y$-coordinate where the line crosses the $y$-axis).
*   **Utility:** Ideal for graphing and identifying the rate of change and starting point directly from the equation.

### 2. Point-Slope Form
This form is particularly useful when the slope and a specific point on the line are known, provided that point is not the $y$-intercept.
*   **Equation:** $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$
*   **Components:** 
    *   $m$ represents the **slope**.
    *   $(x_1, y_1)$ represents any specific **point** on the line.
*   **Utility:** Serves as an efficient starting point for deriving the slope-intercept form when given a point and a slope or two points.

### 3. Standard Form
The standard form is used for specific algebraic operations, such as finding intercepts quickly or solving systems of equations.
*   **Equation:** $Ax + By = C$
*   **Requirements:** 
    *   $A, B,$ and $C$ must be **integers**.
    *   $A \geq 0$ (the coefficient of $x$ must be non-negative).
    *   $A$ and $B$ cannot both be zero.
*   **Utility:** Useful for the study of systems of equations.

## Methodologies for Writing Equations

### Writing an Equation from Slope and y-Intercept
When provided with the slope ($m$) and the $y$-intercept ($b$), use the **Slope-Intercept Form**. 
*   **Example:** Given a slope of $3$ and a $y$-intercept of $-4$.
*   **Process:** Substitute the values directly into $y = mx + b$.
*   **Result:** $y = 3x - 4$.

### Writing an Equation from Slope and One Point
When provided with the slope ($m$) and a point $(x_1, y_1)$ that is not the $y$-intercept, use the **Point-Slope Form** and then convert to slope-intercept form if required.
*   **Example:** Given a slope of $-2$ and the point $(3, 1)$.
*   **Process:** 
    1.  Substitute values into $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1) \rightarrow y - 1 = -2(x - 3)$.
    2.  Distribute the slope: $y - 1 = -2x + 6$.
    3.  Isolate $y$: $y = -2x + 7$.

### Writing an Equation from Two Points
When only two points are provided, the slope must be calculated before an equation can be written.
*   **Example:** Given points $(2, 5)$ and $(6, -3)$.
*   **Process:**
    1.  **Find the Slope ($m$):** Use the slope formula: $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$. In this case: $m = \frac{-3 - 5}{6 - 2} = \frac{-8}{4} = -2$.
    2.  **Use Point-Slope Form:** Select either point (e.g., $(2, 5)$) and substitute: $y - 5 = -2(x - 2)$.
    3.  **Convert:** $y - 5 = -2x + 4 \rightarrow y = -2x + 9$.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **Direct Substitution:** Write the equation of a line in slope-intercept form where the slope is $5$ and the $y$-intercept is $2$.
2.  **Point-Slope Application:** Given a slope of $4$ and the point $(-1, 2)$, what is the resulting equation in slope-intercept form?
3.  **Two-Point Derivation:** Determine the slope-intercept equation for a line passing through the points $(-1, 4)$ and $(3, -8)$.
4.  **Standard Form Conversion:** What are the three specific requirements for an equation to be considered in proper **Standard Form** ($Ax + By = C$)?
5.  **Form Comparison:** Which linear equation form is considered the most common because the slope and $y$-intercept can be read directly?

## Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Slope-Intercept Form** | A linear equation written as $y = mx + b$, where $m$ is the slope and $b$ is the $y$-intercept. |
| **Point-Slope Form** | A linear equation written as $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$, where $m$ is the slope and $(x_1, y_1)$ is any point on the line. |
| **Standard Form** | A linear equation written as $Ax + By = C$, where $A, B,$ and $C$ are integers, $A \geq 0$, and $A$ and $B$ are not both zero. |
| **y-intercept** | The $y$-coordinate of the point where a line crosses the $y$-axis. |
| **Slope ($m$)** | The measure of the steepness of a line, calculated as the change in $y$ over the change in $x$. |
