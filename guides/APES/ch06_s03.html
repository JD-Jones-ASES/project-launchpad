AP Environmental Science — Topic 6.3: Nuclear Energy — Fission, Reactors, Waste, and Disasters

Core Question

How does nuclear fission convert a tiny mass of uranium fuel into enormous electrical output without direct CO2 emissions, and why do its long-lived radioactive waste and finite accident risk make it one of the most contested questions in contemporary energy policy?

Key Concepts

  • Nuclear Fission: Splitting of a heavy nucleus (typically U-235) upon thermal-neutron absorption. The reaction U-235 + n → U-236* → fission products + 2-3 n + gamma + ~200 MeV releases about 50 million times more energy per atom than chemical combustion. The released neutrons sustain a chain reaction in nearby U-235 nuclei.
  • Multiplication Factor (k): The average number of fission neutrons that go on to cause another fission. k = 1 (criticality) defines stable reactor operation. k > 1 (supercriticality) means power grows; prompt criticality is the bomb condition. k < 1 (subcriticality) means the chain reaction dies. Reactors maintain k = 1 by adjusting absorbing rods and moderators.
  • Uranium Enrichment: Natural uranium is 0.7% U-235 + 99.3% U-238. U-238 does not fission with thermal neutrons. Most commercial reactors require 3-5% U-235 enrichment; weapons-grade material requires >90%. The enrichment gap is one reason commercial fuel is not weapons-usable.
  • Reactor Types: PWR (~70% of global capacity) uses water at ~155 atm to suppress boiling and transfer heat to a secondary steam loop. BWR boils water directly in the vessel; simpler but contaminates the turbine circuit; Fukushima units are BWRs. CANDU uses heavy water (D2O) and runs on natural uranium. Fast breeder reactors convert U-238 into fissile Pu-239 using fast neutrons but are expensive and limited in deployment.
  • Spent Fuel Hazard: After 3-4 years in core, rods accumulate fission products (Cs-137, Sr-90) and transuranic actinides (Pu-239 t1/2 = 24,100 yr, Am-241 t1/2 = 432 yr). Full radiological hazard persists for 10^5 to 10^6 years.
  • Spent-Fuel Storage Pathway: Water-filled cooling pools absorb radiation and remove decay heat for the first 5-10 years. Once cooled enough, rods can transition to dry-cask storage (concrete + steel, passive air-convection cooling). As of 2024, essentially all US spent fuel sits in on-site pools or dry casks because no permanent repository operates. Yucca Mountain was effectively halted after 2010; Finland's Onkalo is the world's first permanent geological repository under construction (~2025 target).
  • Chernobyl (1986): RBMK-1000 reactor had a positive void coefficient (steam bubbles raised reactivity) and graphite-tipped control rods that briefly increased reactivity on insertion. During a safety test at low power, the combination produced a ~30,000x power surge, two steam explosions, and a 10-day graphite fire releasing radionuclides. ~30 immediate deaths; WHO estimates ~4,000 thyroid cancers from I-131.
  • Fukushima Daiichi (2011): Magnitude-9.0 Tohoku earthquake + tsunami exceeding the 5.7 m seawall flooded backup diesel generators. Loss of cooling caused core meltdowns in three BWR units; hydrogen from zirconium-cladding oxidation exploded. ~150,000 residents evacuated. Radiation deaths very low; evacuation-related excess deaths in the hundreds. 2023 IAEA-approved release of treated cooling water into the Pacific.
  • Nuclear's Trade-Off: Zero direct CO2, ~92% capacity factor (highest of any source), among the lowest lifecycle emissions per kWh — vs. multi-100,000-year waste isolation and low-probability/high-consequence accident risk. The question is normative, not purely technical.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • 200 MeV per fission ≈ 50 million times a chemical reaction (per atom).
  • k = 1 criticality is the operational target; k > 1 grows power, k < 1 dies.
  • 0.7% natural U-235 vs. 3-5% commercial fuel vs. >90% weapons-grade.
  • (1/2)^10 = 1/1024: after 10 half-lives, ~0.1% of original activity remains.
  • Pu-239 t1/2 = 24,100 yr; full radiological hazard persists 10^5 to 10^6 yr.
  • Chernobyl positive void coefficient + control-rod graphite tips → uncontrolled supercriticality.
  • Fukushima 5.7 m seawall vs. >14 m tsunami → flooded diesel backups → loss of cooling → core melts.

Connect to the Exam

  • Be ready to explain why a positive void coefficient is inherently destabilizing (steam → less neutron absorption → more reactivity → more steam).
  • Compare and contrast Chernobyl (design + procedural failure) and Fukushima (beyond-design natural hazard).
  • Apply (1/2)^n decay math to a given isotope half-life and time interval.
  • Identify the trade-off between zero-direct-CO2 operation and long-lived waste / accident risk.
  • Distinguish PWR, BWR, CANDU, and breeder reactors by moderator, coolant, and fuel requirements.
  • Explain why on-site dry-cask storage proliferates in the US given the political halt of Yucca Mountain.

Self-Check

  • Why is the multiplication factor k = 1 (and not k > 1) the design target for a commercial reactor?
  • A sample of strontium-90 (t1/2 = 29 yr) is buried in 2026. After 5 half-lives, what fraction of activity remains? After 10?
  • The RBMK reactor had a positive void coefficient. Explain the physical chain that connects steam-bubble formation to power increase, and contrast it with the negative coefficient in a PWR.
  • Why does the United States have ~80,000 metric tons of spent fuel sitting on reactor sites? Identify one technical reason and one political reason.
  • Compare Chernobyl and Fukushima in terms of root cause, immediate radiation deaths, and the long-term ecological consequences observed at each site.
