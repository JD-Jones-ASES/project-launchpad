AP Environmental Science — Topic 4.2: Soil Formation, Horizons, and Composition

Core Question

Why must soil be treated as a non-renewable resource on human planning horizons, and how do the five interacting CLORPT factors (climate, organisms, topography, parent material, and time) generate the diversity of soil profiles that determine which lands are agriculturally productive and which are not? Soil sits at the intersection of geology, biology, hydrology, and human land use; reading its profile is reading the history of all four.

Key Concepts

  • Soil: Structured medium of mineral particles, organic matter, water, and air; foundation of food production, primary filter for water entering aquifers, and a major reservoir of carbon and nutrients.
  • Five soil-forming factors (CLORPT): Climate, Living organisms, Orography, Regolith/parent material, Time — together determining what kind of soil forms in a given location.
  • Soil profile and horizons: Vertical cross-section through mature soil shows distinct layers — O (organic litter), A (humus-rich topsoil), E (leached), B (illuvial subsoil), C (weathered parent material), R (bedrock).
  • A horizon (topsoil): Biologically richest mineral layer; dark color from humus; high CEC; the layer most damaged by erosion.
  • B horizon (subsoil): Illuvial zone where clay, iron oxides, and dissolved minerals leached from above accumulate; harder, less organic, often reddish.
  • Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC): Soil's ability to retain positively charged nutrients on negatively charged clay/humus surfaces; high-CEC soils are more fertile and lose less to leaching.
  • Soil texture and the soil triangle: Relative proportions of clay (<0.002 mm), silt (0.002-0.05 mm), and sand (0.05-2 mm) determine drainage, retention, and workability; loam is the ideal balance.
  • Soil pH: Controls nutrient solubility; <5.5 mobilizes toxic Al/Mn; >7.5 makes P, Fe, Zn, B unavailable; ideal crop range 6.0-7.0.
  • Salinization: Accumulation of salts under irrigation in arid climates; renders formerly productive land unusable.
  • Weathering: Physical (frost wedging, root expansion) and chemical (hydrolysis, oxidation, carbonation) breakdown of rocks; releases soluble nutrient ions and creates mineral particles.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • The soil texture triangle: Standard ternary diagram classifying soils into 12 textural classes by sand/silt/clay percentages; foundational tool in soil science.
  • The chernozem of the world's breadbaskets: Centuries of grassland accumulation in the US Midwest, Ukrainian steppes, and Argentine pampas built up deep, organic-rich A horizons under climates that did not decompose litter quickly enough — explaining global agricultural geography.
  • The Dust Bowl (1930s American Plains): Industrial-scale plowing of native prairie chernozems exposed dry topsoil; drought + wind erosion stripped centimeters of topsoil; the disaster motivated the Soil Conservation Service and modern soil-conservation practices.
  • The Aral Sea salinization crisis: Soviet diversion of Amu Darya and Syr Darya for cotton irrigation in arid Central Asia drove salinization across millions of hectares while shrinking the lake itself by ~90%; canonical case study in irrigation salinity.

Connect to the Exam

AP Environmental Science emphasizes soil's role as a slowly formed, easily lost resource and a determinant of agricultural sustainability. Free-response items often (a) provide a description of an erosion or salinization event and ask students to calculate the time required to replace lost soil and evaluate sustainability, (b) ask students to predict how a CLORPT factor (typically climate or vegetation change) would affect a soil profile, or (c) connect soil texture and CEC to fertilizer requirements and water management. Be ready to do simple unit conversions on soil-formation rates (e.g., 1 cm per 500 years), explain why high-CEC clay-and-humus soils require less fertilizer than sandy ones, and interpret a Munsell-color or ribbon-test result from a profile.

Self-Check

  • A 5 mm dust storm strips topsoil from an Iowa field. At 1 cm of soil per 500 years, how many years of formation were lost? Why does this matter for sustainability?
  • Compare a tropical Oxisol with a temperate chernozem. Which has the deeper A horizon, and how do climate and time (two CLORPT factors) drive the difference?
  • Why does a sandy soil require more frequent fertilization than a clay-loam soil, even at the same crop pH?
  • Distinguish stratospheric ozone from soil pH effects on aluminum and phosphorus availability. Why are pH ranges 5.5 and 7.5 critical thresholds for cropland?
