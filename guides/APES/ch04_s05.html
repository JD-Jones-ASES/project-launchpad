AP Environmental Science — Topic 4.5: Ocean Currents, Upwelling, and the El Niño–Southern Oscillation

Core Question

How does the ocean redistribute heat and nutrients across Earth, and why does a 1–3 °C temperature change in the equatorial Pacific produce droughts, floods, fishery collapses, and hurricane-season shifts on every inhabited continent?

Key Concepts

  • Surface currents (gyres): Wind-driven circulation in five major subtropical gyres; rotate clockwise in the Northern Hemisphere, counterclockwise in the Southern (Coriolis). Each gyre has a warm western boundary current and a cool eastern boundary current.
  • Western boundary current: Fast, narrow, warm, deep current carrying tropical heat poleward (Gulf Stream, Kuroshio). Gulf Stream heat transport is ~1,300× total US electricity capacity; warms Western Europe by 5–10 °C above its latitude.
  • Eastern boundary current: Slow, broad, cool, shallow current flowing equatorward (California, Humboldt/Peru, Canary, Benguela). Drives coastal upwelling because equatorward winds + Coriolis push surface water offshore.
  • Thermohaline circulation (THC): Density-driven deep ocean circulation. Cold, salty water sinks in the North Atlantic and around Antarctica; flows along the seafloor; upwells in the Indo-Pacific. ~1,000-year circuit timescale; major poleward heat-transport mechanism.
  • AMOC and abrupt climate shifts: The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation is sensitive to freshwater input. Glacial meltwater triggered the Younger Dryas (~12,900 years ago), centuries of rapid European cooling. Greenland ice melt may weaken AMOC in coming centuries.
  • Upwelling: Wind-driven surface divergence draws cold, nutrient-rich water from depth. Occurs along eastern ocean boundaries and at the equator. Brings nitrate and phosphate from below the photic zone — where organic matter has been remineralized — to fuel surface productivity.
  • Why upwelling drives massive fisheries: Open ocean is nutrient-limited at the surface. Upwelling delivers nutrients to the photic zone; phytoplankton blooms support zooplankton, anchovies, and the largest single-species fishery on Earth (Peruvian anchovy).
  • Walker circulation (neutral state): SE trade winds push warm surface water west; warm pool accumulates near Indonesia (8–9 °C warmer, ~2 m thicker than eastern Pacific); eastern thermocline shoals; cold upwelling surfaces off Peru. Air rises over warm pool, sinks over eastern Pacific.
  • El Niño (warm phase): Trade winds weaken; warm pool migrates east; eastern thermocline deepens; Peruvian upwelling fails; convection shifts east. Result: floods in Peru/Ecuador/California, drought in Australia/Indonesia/southern Africa, coral bleaching, Borneo/Sumatra fires.
  • La Niña (cool phase): Trade winds intensify; warm pool pushed further west; upwelling intensifies; Australia/Indonesia flood, California/Peru drought; Atlantic hurricane activity often increases due to reduced upper-level wind shear.
  • Teleconnections: Tropical Pacific SST anomalies of 1–3 °C reshape the jet stream, producing statistically robust climate links thousands of km away. Why ENSO is the dominant year-to-year climate-variability mode globally.
  • ENSO + anthropogenic warming: Background ocean temperatures are rising; ENSO events now ride on warmer baselines, amplifying ecological/economic impacts (2023–24 record SST, coral bleaching, marine heatwaves) without requiring greater atmospheric amplitude.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • Five subtropical gyres: North Atlantic, South Atlantic, North Pacific, South Pacific, Indian Ocean. Each closed by a continental margin on the west and east.
  • Coastal upwelling sequence (Northern Hemisphere): Equatorward wind → surface water deflected to the right (offshore) → cold nutrient-rich water rises from depth → photic-zone enrichment → phytoplankton bloom → fishery.
  • Walker → ENSO transition: Trade-wind weakening initiates warm-pool eastward migration; weakened upwelling, deepened eastern thermocline, eastward convective shift, jet-stream reshape, global teleconnections.
  • Major ENSO events: 1982–83 (~$8 B), 1997–98 (~$35–45 B + 2 Gt C from peat fires), 2015–16 (worst GBR bleaching to date, southern African food crisis), 2023–24 (record ocean heat content overlaid on anthropogenic warming).

Connect to the Exam

  • ENSO mechanism questions are FRQ classics — be ready to trace neutral → El Niño step by step (winds → thermocline → upwelling → biology → teleconnection). The mechanism chain is more important than memorized impacts.
  • Coriolis-driven coastal upwelling links Topic 4.4 (winds) with Topic 4.5 (currents) and 5.X fisheries.
  • Western vs. eastern boundary current distinction shows up as a stimulus comparison (warm/fast/narrow vs. cool/slow/broad) — know examples and the climatic consequences for adjacent coasts.
  • Thermohaline circulation + freshwater input + abrupt climate change is a Unit 9 hook — practice explaining why fresh meltwater can shut down AMOC.
  • "Nutrient-limited surface" + "remineralization at depth" is the conceptual core of why upwelling drives productivity. Often paired with eutrophication in dead-zone questions.

Self-Check

  • Trace the full mechanism: trade-wind weakening → Peruvian anchovy collapse. Name each step.
  • Why is Western Europe warmer than its latitude alone would predict? Identify the current, the heat-transport mode, and the atmospheric link.
  • Distinguish neutral, El Niño, and La Niña tropical-Pacific states by trade-wind strength, thermocline tilt, and upwelling intensity. Predict one regional precipitation impact for each state.
  • A student claims that as climate warms, ENSO will eventually disappear because the equator-to-pole gradient is shrinking. Critique this argument using evidence from the 2023–24 event.
