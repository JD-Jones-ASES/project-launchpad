AP Environmental Science — Topic 7.3: Indoor Air Pollution

Core Question

Why is indoor air pollution often a greater health risk than outdoor air pollution, and how do its drivers — building materials, appliances, energy-efficiency retrofits, and biomass cookstoves — differ between wealthy and less-developed countries?

Key Concepts

  • Indoor exposure burden: EPA estimates indoor pollutant concentrations are typically 2–5× outdoor levels (occasionally >100×). Americans spend ~90% of their time indoors, making indoor air a dominant component of population-weighted exposure.
  • Radon: Naturally occurring colorless, odorless radioactive gas (²²²Rn) produced by the decay of radium (²²⁶Ra) in uranium-bearing granitic and phosphate-rich bedrock. Seeps upward through soil and foundation cracks.
  • Radon hazard mechanism: The gas itself is relatively benign; danger arises from short-lived radioactive decay products (Po-218, Pb-214, Po-214) that are charged solid particles depositing on airway surfaces and emitting alpha particles directly into lung tissue, causing DNA damage. Second-leading US cause of lung cancer (~21,000 deaths/yr).
  • Radon mitigation: EPA action level is 4 pCi/L. Sub-slab depressurization — a pipe and fan that draws air from beneath the foundation and exhausts it outside — reduces indoor radon by 80–99%. Energy-efficiency retrofits that tighten ventilation can ironically raise indoor radon by reducing the air-exchange rate.
  • Asbestos: A family of fibrous silicate minerals widely used 1930–1980 for fireproofing, insulation, floor and ceiling tiles, pipe wrap, and joint compound. Intact asbestos-containing materials (ACMs) in good condition pose little risk.
  • Three asbestos diseases: Asbestosis (progressive lung scarring), mesothelioma (pleural cancer, 20–40 year latency, median survival <18 months), and lung cancer.
  • Asbestos management approach: EPA recommends in-place management (encapsulation, regular inspection) for intact ACMs because disturbance during removal creates the airborne fiber hazard. Removal must be done by licensed contractors only.
  • Carbon monoxide indoors: Produced by incomplete combustion of any carbon fuel (gas furnaces, unvented heaters, gas stoves, fireplaces, idling vehicles in attached garages). CO is colorless, odorless, tasteless; binds hemoglobin ~200× more than O2, causing cellular hypoxia.
  • VOCs in modern buildings: Paints/varnishes (toluene, xylene), pressed-wood products (formaldehyde from urea-formaldehyde resins), cleaning products, new carpets and upholstery. Formaldehyde (HCHO) is the most concerning — Group 1 IARC carcinogen.
  • Sick building syndrome: Nonspecific symptoms (headache, irritation, fatigue) reported by occupants of a specific building at rates higher than expected, typically tied to inadequate ventilation combined with elevated VOCs or mold. Distinguished from "building-related illness," which has an identifiable specific cause.
  • Cookstove crisis: ~2.3 billion people (~30% of global population) cook with wood, dung, charcoal, agricultural residue, or coal in poorly ventilated dwellings. WHO estimates 3.2 million premature deaths/yr globally — primarily COPD, childhood pneumonia, and lung cancer.
  • Why cookstove burden falls on women and children: The stove is inside or immediately adjacent to the living space; ventilation is minimal; women and children spend the most time near the fire. PM2.5 concentrations reach 10–50× the WHO air-quality guideline.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • Radon decay chain: ²³⁸U → ²²⁶Ra → ²²²Rn (gas) → ²¹⁸Po (alpha emitter) → ²¹⁴Pb → ²¹⁴Po (alpha emitter). The alpha-emitting solid daughters deposit on lung tissue and irradiate cells locally.
  • Radon detection: Short-term (≤90 days) or long-term (1 year) charcoal-canister or alpha-track detectors give reliable measurements.
  • CO mechanism: CO + Hb → COHb, displacing O2 and reducing blood oxygen-carrying capacity. 150 ppm causes headache in 2 hours; 400 ppm life-threatening; 1,600 ppm fatal in 2 hours.
  • Energy-efficiency paradox: Tighter buildings reduce heating/cooling costs but reduce ventilation, allowing radon, VOCs, and CO to accumulate unless balanced by mechanical ventilation systems.

Connect to the Exam

  • Distinguish radon (geological hazard, granitic bedrock) from other indoor pollutants and explain the alpha-particle decay-product mechanism — radon gas itself is not the proximal cause.
  • Be ready to argue why a homeowner should NOT remove asbestos themselves — disturbance creates the fiber-release hazard. Frame the answer around in-place management vs. removal.
  • Understand the carbon monoxide silent-killer profile — no sensory warning + ~200× hemoglobin affinity.
  • Compare wealthy-country indoor air problems (building materials, retrofits, ventilation) with less-developed-country biomass cookstove pollution — same general category, very different drivers and orders-of-magnitude different mortality.
  • Be ready to do simple arithmetic on 3.2 million deaths/yr (≈8,800/day) vs. US traffic deaths (~42,000/yr) to put the scale in context.

Self-Check

  • Why is radon more dangerous in a tightly sealed, energy-efficient home than in a drafty older home, even when the underlying bedrock is unchanged?
  • A homeowner finds asbestos floor tiles in good condition. Should she remove them herself? Justify your answer using the concept of fiber release and the in-place vs. removal distinction.
  • Compare the daily death toll from household biomass air pollution globally to US annual traffic fatalities. What does this scaling tell you about where the largest air-pollution health burden falls?
  • Identify two indoor sources of formaldehyde and explain the mechanism by which it causes nasopharyngeal cancer at chronic high exposures.
