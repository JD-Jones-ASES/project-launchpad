AP Environmental Science — Topic 3.4: Total Fertility Rate and the Demographic Transition

Core Question

How and why do societies move from high-birth, high-death pre-industrial demographics to low-birth, low-death post-industrial demographics — and what does this transition mean for the environment? This section establishes the demographic transition model and identifies the social and economic drivers that have proven consistent across every industrializing country.

Key Concepts

  • Total fertility rate (TFR): Average children per woman over her reproductive lifetime if current age-specific rates persist. Standard cross-country measure; replacement is ~2.1 in high-income countries.
  • Crude birth rate vs. TFR: CBR is sensitive to age structure; TFR is not. A young population can have high CBR even with modest TFR.
  • Demographic transition: Shift from high birth + high death rates to low + low, passing through a transitional phase of high birth + falling death rates that drives rapid growth.
  • Stage 1 (Pre-industrial): Both rates high (30–50 per 1,000), small stable populations, periodic crises. No country today.
  • Stage 2 (Early industrial): Death rates fall sharply with public health improvements; birth rates remain high. Most rapid population growth phase. Niger, Chad, Mali.
  • Stage 3 (Late industrial): Birth rates begin to fall as urbanization, education, and contraceptive access reshape family-size decisions. India, Bangladesh, Brazil, Mexico.
  • Stage 4 (Post-industrial): Both rates low; growth slow or near zero. Western Europe, North America, East Asia.
  • Stage 5 (Below-replacement): TFR well below replacement; native population declines absent immigration. Japan (1.3), South Korea (0.78), Italy (1.2).
  • Drivers of fertility decline: Women's education (most consistent), urbanization, declining infant mortality, contraceptive access, child labor laws, compulsory schooling.
  • Why mortality falls first: Public health acts quickly; social norms shift slowly. The gap drives Stage 2 growth.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • Warren Thompson (1929) and Frank Notestein (1945): Developed the demographic transition model from European and North American historical data; framework now applied globally.
  • Women's education as the most reliable driver: Sometimes called the most reliably replicated finding in development demography. A girl who completes secondary school in a low-income country bears 2–3 fewer children than a girl who does not.
  • Sub-Saharan Africa as the contemporary Stage 2/3 case: Average TFR ~4.5; the pace of further decline through women's education will largely determine whether global population stabilizes near 9 billion or climbs toward 12 billion.
  • Stage 3 environmental pressure point: India, Nigeria, and Indonesia simultaneously have continued population growth and rapidly rising per-capita consumption — multiplying total resource demand.

Connect to the Exam

This topic supports Science Practice 1 (concepts and processes) and Science Practice 4 (data analysis on demographic figures). Common stimulus types include the four-stage demographic transition figure (with you identifying which region/country corresponds to each stage), TFR comparisons across countries, and FRQ scenarios asking you to predict 30- or 50-year population dynamics from current TFR plus social indicators (especially female education enrollment). Expect questions linking the demographic transition to environmental policy — particularly the dual-lever strategy of girls' education plus per-capita consumption reduction.

Self-Check

  • Sketch the four (or five) stages of the demographic transition on a graph of birth rate and death rate vs. time. Label the population growth pattern in each stage.
  • Why does death rate fall before birth rate? Identify three specific mechanisms behind each change.
  • A country has TFR = 4.0 and a rapidly improving female secondary school enrollment rate. Predict its likely 30-year trajectory and justify your reasoning.
  • Why are policies that promote girls' education considered environmental policies, not just development policies?
