AP Environmental Science — Topic 6.4: Biomass, Biofuels, and Hydroelectric Power

Core Question

Why do some of the oldest energy technologies — burning wood and damming rivers — still supply more renewable energy than solar and wind combined, and what hidden costs (indoor air pollution, deforestation, displacement, sediment loss) accompany the renewable label?

Key Concepts

  • Biomass Energy: Any fuel derived from recently living organic material (wood, agricultural residues, dung, charcoal, energy crops). Aggregate ~5% of global primary energy, but 60-80% of household energy in sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and parts of Latin America. Aggregate statistics undercount this informal use.
  • Indoor Air Pollution: WHO estimates approximately 3.8 million premature deaths annually from indoor air pollution caused by biomass combustion in open fires and poorly vented cookstoves. Burden falls primarily on women and children. A central hidden public-health cost of traditional biomass.
  • Deforestation Cycle: In many low-income rural areas, wood is harvested faster than forests can regenerate — especially near urban centers. Result: forest degradation, reduced carbon storage, continued dependence on the same depleting resource.
  • First-Generation Biofuels: Ethanol from food-crop sugars/starches (corn, sugarcane), biodiesel from oil crops (soybean, palm). US corn ethanol: ~40% of the US corn crop diverted to ethanol, large enough to raise global grain prices. Food-vs.-fuel debate centers on this diversion's effect on net-food-importing low-income countries.
  • Net Energy Ratio (NER): Energy content of biofuel divided by fossil energy consumed in producing it. US corn ethanol NER ~1.3 (marginal). Brazilian sugarcane ethanol NER ~8.0 (substantial). NER < 1 means the fuel is an energy sink, not a source.
  • Why Sugarcane Outperforms Corn: (1) Higher fermentable sugar yield per hectare than corn, requiring less land/fertilizer/diesel per gallon. (2) Brazilian mills burn the fibrous bagasse residue for process heat instead of natural gas, dramatically reducing fossil-energy inputs.
  • Second-Generation Biofuels: Cellulosic feedstocks (corn stover, wheat straw, switchgrass) avoid the food-vs.-fuel conflict but require costly enzyme cocktails or acid pretreatment to break down lignin. Few commercial plants operate despite decades of research.
  • Third-Generation Biofuels: Algal biodiesel — 10-100x higher per-hectare yields than soybeans, can use non-arable land with saline or wastewater, no food-crop competition. Cost and scaling have prevented commercial deployment as of 2024.
  • Hydroelectric Generation: Global capacity ~1,400 GW supplies ~7% of primary energy and ~15% of global electricity. Run-of-river plants extract energy from natural flow with minimal storage and ecological impact. Large reservoir dams provide dispatchable power at the cost of upstream flooding, displacement, sediment trapping, and downstream delta erosion.
  • Three Gorges Dam (2012): 22,500 MW installed, largest power station on Earth. Concrete gravity dam 2,335 m long, 181 m high; reservoir 600 km long. Forced relocation of ~1.4 million people. Traps ~530 Mt/yr of sediment, accelerating Yangtze Delta erosion. Contributed to functional extinction of the Yangtze River dolphin (baiji).
  • Lifecycle Accounting: NER alone is insufficient. Clearing grassland or forest to grow biofuel crops releases stored carbon as CO2 that can take decades to repay under favorable NER. Policy must include land-use change emissions in evaluating biofuel mandates.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • 3.8 million premature deaths annually from biomass-fueled indoor air pollution.
  • US corn ethanol NER ~1.3 vs. Brazilian sugarcane ethanol NER ~8.0.
  • ~40% of US corn crop diverted to ethanol production.
  • 1,400 GW global hydro capacity → ~15% of global electricity.
  • Three Gorges: 22.5 GW capacity, 1.4 million people relocated, 530 Mt/yr sediment trapped, baiji extinction contribution.
  • Algal biodiesel yield: 10-100x higher per hectare than soybeans.

Connect to the Exam

  • Be ready to compare net energy ratios and explain the two structural reasons sugarcane ethanol outperforms corn ethanol.
  • Identify which generation of biofuel a given feedstock corresponds to (food crop = 1st; cellulosic residue = 2nd; algae = 3rd).
  • Frame the food-vs.-fuel debate around the ~40% US corn diversion and global grain prices.
  • Use Three Gorges as a case study for how 'clean and renewable' framing misses social and ecological costs.
  • Distinguish run-of-river from large-reservoir hydro and link each to specific environmental consequences.
  • Apply lifecycle accounting: explain why a high-NER biofuel can still have substantial lifecycle emissions if grassland or forest is cleared for crops.

Self-Check

  • A student claims that biomass energy is the cleanest source globally because it is renewable. Identify two facts about traditional biomass use that complicate this claim.
  • Compare the NERs of corn ethanol and sugarcane ethanol, and identify two specific reasons for the difference.
  • Identify two environmental costs of the Three Gorges Dam not captured by the phrase 'clean and renewable.'
  • A new biofuel has NER = 1.05. Why might a policy mandate for this fuel still impose significant environmental harm?
  • Why does the indoor-air-pollution death burden from biomass fall primarily on women and children?
