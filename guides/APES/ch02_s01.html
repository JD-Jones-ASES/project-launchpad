AP Environmental Science — Topic 2.1: Metrics of Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services

Core Question

What does it actually mean to "measure biodiversity," and why does the answer translate directly into the services that human economies and cultures depend upon?

Key Concepts

  • Biodiversity (three levels): The variety of life measured at the genetic, species, and habitat levels — each level can be gained or lost independently of the others.
  • Genetic diversity: Heritable variation within and among populations. It is the raw material on which natural selection acts; populations with low genetic diversity (e.g., cheetahs) are vulnerable to novel pathogens.
  • Species diversity: Combines species richness (count of species) with species evenness (how equitably individuals are distributed). Two communities with identical richness can differ dramatically in diversity due to evenness alone.
  • Habitat diversity: The variety of distinct ecosystem types or structural niches in a landscape; a heterogeneous watershed supports more total biodiversity than a monoculture of equal area.
  • Simpson's Diversity Index (D): D = 1 - sum(p_i^2). Ranges from 0 (single-species dominance) to nearly 1 (perfectly even distribution). The default AP exam calculation.
  • Shannon Diversity Index (H): H = -sum(p_i * ln(p_i)). Like D, it combines richness and evenness but is more sensitive to rare species because the logarithm grows very negative as p_i approaches zero.
  • Ecosystem services (four categories): Provisioning, regulating, cultural, and supporting — the framework adopted by the Millennium Ecosystem Assessment in 2005.
  • Provisioning services: Tangible goods such as food, fresh water, fiber, fuel, and genetic resources; pollinators alone support ~1/3 of global food production by value.
  • Regulating services: Climate regulation, water purification, pollination, flood control, disease regulation. Often invisible until they are lost (e.g., when wetlands are drained).
  • Cultural services: Non-material benefits — recreation, aesthetic value, spiritual significance, education. Ecotourism generates >$600 billion annually worldwide.
  • Supporting services: Soil formation, nutrient cycling, primary production. The substrate on which all other services rest.
  • Functional redundancy: Multiple species performing the same ecological role buffer the system against the loss of any single species; loss of a uniquely-functioning species (e.g., passenger pigeon) can collapse a service entirely.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • Millennium Ecosystem Assessment (2005): Codified the four-category framework (provisioning, regulating, cultural, supporting) used worldwide today.
  • Cheetah genome (1980s discovery): Skin grafts between unrelated individuals were accepted, evidence of extreme genetic uniformity from a population bottleneck and a foundational case for genetic-diversity conservation.
  • Yellowstone wolf reintroduction (1995): Trophic cascade demonstration — restoring a single predator triggered vegetation recovery that reduced stream-bank erosion, a measurable regulating service.
  • Wild tomato relative (Peru, 1970s): Genes incorporated into commercial cultivars conferred disease resistance worth hundreds of millions of dollars annually, illustrating the provisioning value of crop wild relatives.
  • Passenger pigeon (Martha, d. 1 September 1914): Iconic case of species loss that also removed mast-forest disturbance services no other species replaces.

Connect to the Exam

Topic 2.1 most often appears as a stimulus-based calculation question (Science Practice 6 — mathematical routines) where students must compute Simpson's D for a small dataset, interpret the value, and explain why two communities with the same richness can have different D values. FRQs frequently ask students to identify ecosystem services from each of the four categories provided by a specific habitat (wetland, forest, coral reef) and explain the mechanism by which each service is delivered (Science Practice 1 — concept explanation).

Self-Check

  • A 100-individual plot has 4 species with proportions 0.5, 0.3, 0.15, 0.05. Calculate Simpson's D and interpret the value relative to a plot with all four species at p_i = 0.25.
  • Distinguish species richness from species evenness, and explain why two communities with identical richness can have different Simpson's D values.
  • For a coastal mangrove forest, identify one ecosystem service from each of the four categories (provisioning, regulating, cultural, supporting) and explain the mechanism by which the mangrove delivers each.
  • Why does functional redundancy reduce the risk that a species extinction will collapse an ecosystem service? Use the passenger pigeon as a counterexample.
