AP Environmental Science — Topic 1.2: Terrestrial and Aquatic Biomes

Core Question

Why is the equator covered in rainforest while the high Arctic is covered in tundra, and why do coral reefs and estuaries support far more life per square meter than the open ocean? Section 1.2 maps the world's major biomes onto two pairs of master variables — temperature and precipitation on land, salinity and light penetration in water — and shows how these abiotic regimes architect biotic community structure. The convergence of biomes around the world (rainforests in Borneo and Congo share the same form despite different species) is the most powerful evidence that climate, not chance, organizes life on Earth.

Key Concepts

  • Biome: A large region defined by a characteristic climate and the distinctive plant and animal communities that have evolved there. Identified by dominant vegetation, which reflects temperature and precipitation regime.
  • Mean annual temperature and mean annual precipitation: The two master variables organizing terrestrial biomes. Together they determine water availability and growing-season length.
  • Ecotone: A transition zone between adjacent biomes that often supports higher species diversity than either adjacent community (the edge effect — species from both communities plus edge specialists).
  • Tropical rainforest: Equatorial, >2000 mm rain/yr, >18°C; rapid decomposition locks nutrients in biomass; highest NPP per unit area on land.
  • Temperate deciduous forest: 4 distinct seasons, 750-1500 mm/yr; deciduous leaves shed in autumn; nutrient-rich soils.
  • Boreal forest (taiga): Sub-Arctic, largest terrestrial biome by area; evergreen conifers; thin acidic nutrient-poor soils.
  • Grasslands (savanna, temperate prairie): Intermediate precipitation; fire is structural, preventing shrub encroachment.
  • Tundra: North of treeline; permafrost beneath the surface; growing season <60 days; lowest terrestrial NPP; warming at ~2x global rate.
  • Desert: Defined by aridity (<250 mm/yr); convergent water-conservation adaptations across hot and cold deserts.
  • Salinity: Dissolved-salt concentration; freshwater <0.5 ppt, ocean ~35 ppt, estuaries variable.
  • Photic zone: Upper layer where sunlight supports photosynthesis (~200 m in open ocean); aphotic zone below is dominated by decomposers and chemolithotrophs.

Key Figures / Cycles / Mechanisms

  • Temperature-precipitation biome chart: The canonical AP visualization. Plotting mean annual temperature against mean annual precipitation places terrestrial biomes into largely non-overlapping clusters. Boundaries are gradual ecotones, not sharp lines.
  • Tropical-soil paradox: Highest NPP biome has poorest soils. Mechanism: rapid decomposition keeps nutrients in living biomass, not soil. Cleared rainforest land is briefly productive, then collapses.
  • Coral-Symbiodinium mutualism: The engine of reef productivity in oligotrophic tropical waters. Algae photosynthesize sugars; corals provide protection, CO2, and nutrients. Heat stress disrupts the symbiosis (bleaching).
  • Open-ocean 'blue desert': Low NPP per m² but vast area gives a large total carbon fixation flux. Nutrients sink out of photic zone; only slow upwelling returns them.
  • Estuarine mixing: Salinity, temperature, and nutrient gradients in mixed water support nursery habitat. High productivity + coastal accessibility = high vulnerability.
  • Permafrost-thaw positive feedback: Arctic warming → permafrost thaws → stored carbon releases as CO2 and CH4 → more warming → more thaw. Examined in Unit 9.

Connect to the Exam

This section links Practice 5 (Data Analysis) with Practice 1 (Concept Explanation). Expect a stimulus showing an NPP table, a temperature-precipitation chart, or a biome map; you will be asked to identify a biome, justify it from the data, and explain the underlying mechanism (decomposition rates, growing-season length, water availability). Aquatic-biome questions often probe the productivity paradoxes — coral reefs in nutrient-poor seas, blue-desert open ocean — and test whether you can connect master variables (salinity, light) to community structure.

Self-Check

  • A student claims that tropical rainforest soil should be the most fertile on Earth because the biome has the highest NPP. Diagnose the conceptual error and write a corrected explanation in 2-3 sentences.
  • Identify the two master abiotic variables that best predict the type of terrestrial biome in a region, and explain why each variable matters for plant physiology.
  • Why is the open ocean called a 'blue desert' even though it contributes a large fraction of Earth's total annual carbon fixation? Distinguish per-unit-area NPP from total integrated flux.
  • Predict how Arctic tundra NPP would change if mean annual temperature increased by 3°C, and identify two mechanisms (one positive, one possibly stabilizing) that could drive that change.
