AP Environmental Science — Topic 9.1: Stratospheric Ozone Depletion and the Montreal Protocol

Core Question

Why did the international community successfully address stratospheric ozone depletion within roughly a decade of recognizing the problem, and what conditions of atmospheric chemistry, industry structure, and treaty design made that success possible? Understanding the Montreal Protocol matters not only as the most successful international environmental treaty in history but also as the template against which every subsequent global environmental negotiation — especially the climate negotiations of Topic 9.6 — has been measured.

Key Concepts

  • Stratospheric ozone (O3): The layer of ozone concentrated between roughly 15 and 35 km altitude that absorbs 95-99% of incoming solar UV-B and UV-C radiation, shielding surface life from DNA damage, skin cancer, cataracts, and immune suppression. Column abundance is measured in Dobson units (DU); global average is approximately 300 DU.
  • Tropospheric vs. stratospheric ozone: Both are the same O3 molecule, but their biological effect depends on altitude. Ground-level ozone is a respiratory pollutant; stratospheric ozone is a protective shield. The phrase "good up high, bad nearby" captures the dual role.
  • Chlorofluorocarbon (CFC): Synthetic carbon-fluorine-chlorine compound used as refrigerant, aerosol propellant, and foam-blowing agent. CFC-11 (CCl3F) and CFC-12 (CCl2F2) are the most common. Their chemical inertness in the troposphere allowed them to accumulate for decades and drift into the stratosphere.
  • Catalytic destruction cycle: A two-step cycle (Cl + O3 -> ClO + O2; ClO + O -> Cl + O2) in which chlorine acts as a catalyst, allowing one Cl atom to destroy roughly 100,000 ozone molecules before being sequestered by a sink reaction. The catalytic amplification is why parts-per-trillion CFC concentrations cause measurable ozone loss.
  • Antarctic ozone hole: A seasonal collapse of stratospheric ozone above Antarctica during austral spring (September-October), discovered by Farman, Gardiner, and Shanklin in 1985. The hole reached a maximum extent of 29.9 million km^2 in September 2006.
  • Polar stratospheric clouds (PSCs): Ice crystal clouds that form below approximately -78 degrees Celsius in polar winters. Their surfaces catalyze conversion of stable chlorine reservoirs (HCl, ClONO2) into reactive Cl2 that photodissociates when sunlight returns, producing the explosive springtime ozone destruction characteristic of the Antarctic hole.
  • Montreal Protocol (1987): International treaty under the Vienna Convention (1985) that phases out production and consumption of ozone-depleting substances. Universal ratification, stepped phase-out architecture, Multilateral Fund for technology transition, and the 2016 Kigali Amendment for HFCs.
  • UV-B radiation (280-320 nm): The biologically active ultraviolet band absorbed by stratospheric ozone. Surface UV-B causes skin cancer, cataracts, immune suppression, and DNA damage in plants and animals. The protocol is estimated to prevent 2 million annual US skin-cancer deaths by 2030.
  • Dobson unit (DU): Standard measure of ozone column abundance, equivalent to a layer of pure ozone 0.01 mm thick at standard pressure and temperature. Global average is roughly 300 DU; the Antarctic hole drops below 100 DU.
  • Kigali Amendment (2016): Amendment phasing down hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs), the CFC substitutes that do not deplete ozone but have GWP up to 14,800. Projected to prevent 0.3-0.5 degrees C of warming by 2100.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • Mario Molina and F. Sherwood Rowland (1974): Published the foundational hypothesis linking CFCs to stratospheric ozone destruction. Awarded the 1995 Nobel Prize in Chemistry. The catalytic-cycle insight reframed ozone depletion from a hypothetical concern into a chemically defined, quantifiable threat.
  • Joseph Farman, Brian Gardiner, and Jonathan Shanklin (1985): British Antarctic Survey scientists who measured a more than 50% drop in October stratospheric ozone above Halley Bay since the late 1970s. NASA's Total Ozone Mapping Spectrometer confirmed the global picture.
  • Sydney Chapman (1930): Described the natural ozone production-destruction cycle (O2 + UV-C -> 2O; O + O2 -> O3; O3 + UV-B -> O + O2). The Chapman cycle defines the natural steady state that anthropogenic chlorine perturbs.
  • Catalytic cycle two-step: Cl + O3 -> ClO + O2 destroys ozone; ClO + O -> Cl + O2 regenerates the catalyst. Sink: Cl + CH4 -> HCl + CH3 sequesters chlorine into the reservoir species, ending the cycle.

Connect to the Exam

AP Environmental Science tests this material through both content multiple choice and stimulus-based items involving treaty excerpts, atmospheric data, and reaction equations. Practice 1 (Concept Explanation) shows up directly: students must explain why CFCs survive in the troposphere but break down in the stratosphere (chemical inertness vs. UV-C exposure), and why one chlorine atom can destroy thousands of ozone molecules (catalysis). Practice 2 (Visual Representations) often includes the iconic NASA TOMS purple-blue Antarctic hole image — students should identify Dobson units, the seasonal nature, and the role of PSCs. Practice 5 (Data Analysis) appears in time-series questions tracking stratospheric chlorine loading (peaked around 1997, declining since) and ozone hole recovery (mid-latitudes by ~2066, Antarctic by ~2090). Practice 7 (Environmental Solutions) uses the Montreal Protocol as the canonical case for evaluating treaty design: stepped phase-out, Multilateral Fund, trade restrictions, and concentrated industry as enabling conditions.

Sample FRQ prompt: A scientist measures stratospheric chlorine loading at 1.5 ppb in 1980, 3.5 ppb in 1997, and 2.7 ppb in 2023. (A) Identify the international treaty most directly responsible for the post-1997 decline and describe ONE specific provision of that treaty. (B) Explain why the chlorine loading continued to rise from 1980 to 1997 even after scientists understood that CFCs were destroying ozone. (C) Predict whether the Antarctic ozone hole will fully recover by 2050, and justify your prediction using both atmospheric chemistry and treaty implementation considerations.

Self-Check

  • Write out the two-step catalytic chlorine cycle for ozone destruction. Label which step destroys ozone and which step regenerates the catalyst. Explain how the cycle terminates.
  • Why did the Montreal Protocol achieve universal ratification (every country on Earth) when the climate-change conventions have not? List at least three structural conditions of the ozone problem that made it more tractable than CO2.
  • A student claims the Antarctic ozone hole is permanent because CFCs have already been emitted. Evaluate this claim using both the atmospheric chemistry of CFCs and the projected recovery timeline under continued protocol compliance.
  • Explain how PSCs convert relatively unreactive chlorine reservoir species into the reactive Cl2 that drives the springtime ozone collapse. Why does this mechanism produce a hole only over the poles, not over mid-latitudes?
