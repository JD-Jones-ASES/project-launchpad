AP Environmental Science — Topic 5.4: Water Use, Irrigation Methods, and Salinization

Core Question

Agriculture accounts for ~70% of global freshwater withdrawals — yet much of that water never reaches crop roots. As aquifers decline, surface-water allocations are cut, and the lower Mesopotamian collapse echoes through history, improving irrigation efficiency is one of the few paths to feeding a growing population without further depleting freshwater stocks. This section addresses three interlinked problems: how much water current irrigation methods waste, how aquifer mining converts a renewable-seeming input into a non-renewable one, and how salt accumulation in poorly drained soils permanently degrades agricultural land.

Key Concepts

  • Irrigation efficiency: Fraction of withdrawn water reaching crop roots. Flood ~50%, furrow ~60%, sprinkler ~75%, drip ~90%. Inefficient water is lost to evaporation, runoff, deep percolation, or canopy interception.
  • Drip irrigation: Highest-efficiency method (~90%). Low-pressure emitters deliver water directly to root zone. Pioneered in Israel (1960s); now widely adopted in water-scarce regions for high-value crops.
  • Aquifer: Permeable subsurface formation that stores and transmits groundwater. Unconfined (recharged from above), confined (recharged via distant zone), or fossil-water (essentially non-renewable).
  • Fossil water: Groundwater that accumulated during wetter past climates and is not meaningfully recharged today. Extraction is mining of a non-renewable resource. Examples: Ogallala (US), deep Arabian and North African aquifers.
  • Ogallala Aquifer: Fossil-water reservoir under ~450,000 km² of US Great Plains; supports ~30% of US groundwater-irrigated cropland. Natural recharge ~0.5 cm/yr; withdrawals up to 30 cm/yr (60:1 mining). Saturated thickness down ~30% on average, >60% in parts of Kansas/Texas.
  • Land subsidence: Permanent loss of aquifer storage capacity caused by compaction of fine-grained sediments when groundwater is removed. Central Valley: up to 9 m subsidence over a century. Cannot be reversed.
  • Salinization: Accumulation of soluble salts in soil from inadequately drained irrigation. Even fresh water has dissolved minerals; evaporation/transpiration concentrate them in the root zone. Salt-tolerant crops (barley, sugar beet, cotton) outlast sensitive ones (strawberries, soybeans, onions) but no crop persists indefinitely. Effectively irreversible.
  • Mesopotamian collapse: Earliest documented case — ~4,000 years ago salinization began reducing barley yields; by 1700 BCE productivity had so declined it contributed to Sumerian collapse. Murray-Darling basin (Australia) now faces an analogous crisis.
  • Aral Sea desiccation: Soviet diversion of Amu Darya and Syr Darya rivers for cotton irrigation shrunk the lake from 68,000 km² to ~10% by 2007. Cascade: salinity tenfold rise → fish kill → exposed pesticide-laden dust → respiratory disease → continentalized climate.
  • Non-substitutability of water: No chemical or technological substitute exists for plant photosynthesis. Aquifer depletion is qualitatively different from most resource constraints because it cannot be engineered around — only reduced or accepted as a hard ceiling.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • The four irrigation methods (efficiency ranking): Flood (50%) < Furrow (60%) < Sprinkler (75%) < Drip (90%). The choice trades capital cost (drip highest) against water savings (drip largest). For high-value crops in water-scarce regions, the drip system pays for itself.
  • Ogallala mining math: Recharge ~0.5 cm/yr against withdrawals up to 30 cm/yr → mining ratio ~60:1 → time to deplete 300 cm of saturated thickness = 300 / (30 - 0.5) ≈ 10.2 years per pumping intensity unit.
  • Salinization mechanism: irrigation water contains some dissolved salts → water evaporates and transpires → salts left in root zone → without drainage, salts accumulate over decades → osmotic potential rises → roots cannot absorb water even when soil is moist → yield decline → field abandonment.
  • California Central Valley subsidence: Up to 9 m in a century. Fine clays compact under groundwater removal and cannot re-expand. Subsidence destroys aquifer storage permanently — a one-way ratchet.
  • Aral Sea cascade (textbook case study): Lake shrinkage → salinity 10x ocean → fishery collapse and tens of thousands of livelihoods → exposed lake bed = Aralkum Desert → respiratory disease, throat cancer rates among world's highest → continentalization. Kazakhstan's 2005 Kok-Aral Dam partially restored North Aral; fish returned within years; South Aral remains effectively dead.

Connect to the Exam

Topic 5.4 generates highly quantitative FRQs and stimulus questions. Expect: irrigation efficiency calculations (given a crop water requirement, compute total withdrawals across methods), aquifer mining ratio problems (recharge vs. withdrawal, time-to-depletion), and salinization mechanism explanations (why does a poorly drained field accumulate salt? why is the process effectively irreversible?). The Ogallala case is testable as both a quantitative and qualitative item: students should know the magnitude of mining (60:1), the implied timescale to depletion, and the policy implications (no large-scale alternative water source for affected crops).

The Aral Sea case study is exam-canonical: students should be able to identify the primary mechanism (river diversion for cotton irrigation, NOT direct climate change), trace the secondary cascade (salinity, fishery collapse, dust exposure, climate continentalization), and contrast the partial recovery (North Aral via Kok-Aral Dam, 2005) with the unrecovered (South Aral). The non-substitutability of water is a frequent exam concept: water has no chemical substitute, making aquifer depletion qualitatively different from depletion of any synthetically replaceable input.

Self-Check

  • Rank the four irrigation methods (flood, furrow, sprinkler, drip) from lowest to highest water-use efficiency. For each method, identify the primary pathway by which water is lost before reaching crop roots, and explain why drip irrigation pays for its higher capital cost in water-scarce regions.
  • The Ogallala Aquifer recharges at approximately 0.5 cm/yr in the areas it supplies. A farm currently pumps at a rate equivalent to removing 30 cm/yr from the aquifer depth. Calculate how many years it would take to deplete 300 cm (approximately 10 feet) of saturated aquifer thickness at this NET extraction rate. What does this imply about the definition of "sustainable" irrigation in this region?
  • Explain why salinization is described as effectively irreversible. What two physical interventions would in principle reverse it, and why are they not deployed at large scale?
  • Apply Hardin's tragedy framework (from Section 5.1) to the Aral Sea case study. Identify the rational individual decision-makers, the externalized costs, and ONE governance mechanism that — if in place in the 1960s — could have prevented the collapse.
