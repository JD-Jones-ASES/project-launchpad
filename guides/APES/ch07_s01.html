AP Environmental Science — Topic 7.1: Primary versus Secondary Pollutants and the Six Criteria Pollutants

Core Question

How does the distinction between pollutants emitted directly and pollutants formed through atmospheric chemistry organize the regulatory architecture of the US Clean Air Act, and why does this distinction matter for designing effective air-quality policy?

Key Concepts

  • Primary pollutant: A substance released directly from an identifiable source in the form that causes harm. Major examples include SO2 (coal combustion, smelting), NOx (high-temperature combustion in vehicles and boilers), CO (incomplete combustion), particulate matter (combustion exhaust, dust), lead (formerly leaded gasoline, now smelters and aviation gasoline), and VOCs (vehicle exhaust, solvents, vegetation).
  • Secondary pollutant: A substance not emitted directly but formed in the atmosphere via chemical reactions between primary pollutants or with natural constituents (water vapor, oxygen, hydroxyl radicals, UV light). Key examples: tropospheric ozone, sulfate particles, nitrate particles, peroxyacetyl nitrate (PAN).
  • Six criteria pollutants: SO2, NO2, CO, tropospheric O3, particulate matter (PM10 and PM2.5), and lead — pollutants for which the EPA establishes National Ambient Air Quality Standards under Clean Air Act §108.
  • National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS): Legally enforceable concentration limits with specified averaging times (1-hour, 8-hour, 24-hour, annual).
  • Primary vs. secondary NAAQS: Primary NAAQS protect public health (with margin of safety for sensitive populations — children, elderly, asthmatics). Secondary NAAQS protect public welfare — visibility, crops, materials, aquatic ecosystems, forests.
  • CO toxicity mechanism: CO binds hemoglobin with ~200× the affinity of O2, forming carboxyhemoglobin and displacing oxygen — causing cellular hypoxia.
  • PM2.5 health risk: Fine particles ≤2.5 µm bypass nasal filtration and reach alveoli (and bloodstream), driving cardiovascular and pulmonary disease and premature mortality.
  • Photochemistry of ozone: NOx + VOCs + sunlight → tropospheric O3 + PAN. The reaction requires all three; removing any one stops smog formation.
  • Attainment vs. nonattainment: Areas meeting the NAAQS are "attainment"; those failing must produce State Implementation Plans (SIPs). Failure can trigger Federal Implementation Plans and withholding of federal highway funds.
  • Why lead joined the list in 1978: Not in the original 1970 list. EPA added it under court order after advocacy showed automotive lead met all §108 triggers — illustrating the framework's responsiveness to evolving science and litigation.
  • Lead–catalytic converter linkage: Lead poisons platinum catalysts; the 1975 mandate for catalytic converters forced unleaded gasoline. Combined with the lead NAAQS, US childhood blood-lead levels fell >90% between 1976 and 1999.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • Sulfur pathway to acid rain (precursor to §7.4): SO2 (primary) → atmospheric oxidation in cloud droplets → H2SO4 → sulfate particles (secondary, PM2.5 component).
  • Nitrogen pathway: NOx (primary) reacts with hydroxyl radicals (·OH) → HNO3 → nitrate particles (secondary).
  • Photochemical chain: NO2 + UV → NO + O; O + O2 → O3. VOCs sustain accumulation by reoxidizing NO back to NO2 without consuming O3.
  • Statutory triggers (Clean Air Act §108): A pollutant qualifies for criteria status when (A) emissions endanger public health or welfare, (B) it comes from numerous or diverse sources, and (C) air quality criteria had not been issued before 1970.
  • Five-year NAAQS review: 1990 amendments require periodic reassessment; this rolling review tightened PM2.5 and ozone standards as epidemiology revealed harm at lower concentrations.

Connect to the Exam

  • AP exam questions typically ask you to classify specific pollutants as primary or secondary and identify the principal source or formation pathway.
  • Be ready to explain why secondary-pollutant regulation is harder than primary: precursor controls across multiple source categories, not a single smokestack fix.
  • Know the six criteria pollutants verbatim; do not confuse them with greenhouse gases (CO2, CH4, N2O — separately regulated) or hazardous air pollutants (mercury, benzene — under §112).
  • Understand the primary vs. secondary NAAQS distinction (health vs. welfare) — a frequent multiple-choice trap.
  • The lead story combines several themes: regulatory responsiveness, technology-forcing standards (catalytic converters), and one of the most successful US public-health outcomes of the 20th century.

Self-Check

  • Classify each as primary or secondary and state its principal source or formation pathway: (a) NO2 from a diesel truck, (b) sulfate particles in PM2.5, (c) lead from a battery-recycling smelter, (d) tropospheric O3, (e) PAN, (f) CO from a gas furnace.
  • Why does Section 108(a)(1)(C) restrict the criteria-pollutant list to substances for which criteria had not been issued before 1970? What does this tell you about the legislative intent?
  • Give one welfare impact (covered by a secondary NAAQS) that the primary NAAQS alone would not address, and explain why a separate standard is necessary.
  • Explain how the catalytic-converter mandate of 1975 indirectly accelerated the phase-out of leaded gasoline, and quantify the public-health outcome between 1976 and 1999.
