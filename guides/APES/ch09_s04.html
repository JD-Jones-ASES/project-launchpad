AP Environmental Science — Topic 9.4: Climate Consequences II: Ocean Warming and Acidification

Core Question

How does the ocean absorb the dominant share of anthropogenic heat and CO2, and what physical and chemical changes inside the ocean represent the cost of that buffering? Understanding ocean warming (and its discrete expression as marine heatwaves) and ocean acidification (and its threat to calcifying organisms) is essential because the ocean's role as planetary buffer has masked the full severity of climate forcing — but those internal changes are accelerating and largely irreversible on human timescales.

Core Question

Key Concepts

  • Ocean as heat reservoir: The ocean has absorbed ~91% of excess heat trapped by anthropogenic greenhouse gases since 1970. Ocean heat content in the upper 2,000 m reached records each year 2020-2023. Without ocean buffering, surface temperatures would have risen by tens of degrees.
  • Ocean as carbon sink: The ocean has absorbed ~30% of anthropogenic CO2 since the Industrial Revolution. Slows atmospheric accumulation but the chemistry comes with a cost: acidification.
  • Ocean acidification: Decrease in seawater pH from absorbed CO2 forming carbonic acid (H2CO3) and releasing hydrogen ions. Pre-industrial pH ~8.2; current pH ~8.1; projected pH ~7.8 by 2100 under high-emission scenarios. A 26% increase in [H+] today, projected to 151% by 2100.
  • Carbonic acid chemistry: CO2 + H2O <-> H2CO3 <-> H+ + HCO3-. The H+ ions lower pH and react with carbonate: H+ + CO3^2- -> HCO3-, consuming carbonate ions essential for calcification.
  • Saturation state (Omega): Ratio of ion product to solubility product for CaCO3 minerals. Omega > 1 = shells can form; Omega < 1 = corrosive water, shells dissolve. Aragonite saturation is the threshold for coral and pteropod skeletons.
  • Marine heatwave (MHW): Period of sea-surface temperatures exceeding the 90th percentile threshold for at least 5 consecutive days. Classified from moderate to extreme. Cause mass coral bleaching, kelp and seagrass mortality, toxic algal blooms, fish displacement.
  • Coral bleaching: Thermal stress causes corals to expel zooxanthellae (symbiotic algae providing 70-90% of energy). Bleached coral is not dead but starves if stress persists for months. Bleaching events have shifted from years apart to nearly annual on the Great Barrier Reef.
  • Calcifying organisms: Corals (aragonite skeletons), pteropods (thin aragonite shells), oysters/mussels/clams (calcite or aragonite), coccolithophores and foraminifera (microscopic calcite). All threatened by lower aragonite/calcite saturation.
  • Pteropods (sea butterflies): Pelagic snails with thin aragonite shells; primary consumers in polar food webs. Already documented shell dissolution in the Southern Ocean. Indicator species for acidification.
  • Pacific NW oyster hatchery collapse: 2000s-2010s; upwelled cold water with Omega-aragonite < 1 dissolved larval oyster shells. Early documented economic impact of acidification.
  • Polar acidification first: Cold water dissolves more CO2 (higher solubility), so polar oceans reach low aragonite saturation first. Southern Ocean is the most advanced globally.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • pH calculation worked example: Pre-industrial pH 8.2 -> current pH 8.1: ratio of [H+] = 10^(8.2-8.1) = 10^0.1 = 1.26 (26% increase). Projection to pH 7.8: 10^(8.2-7.8) = 10^0.4 = 2.51 (151% increase). Small pH numbers, large [H+] consequences because pH is logarithmic.
  • Northeast Pacific Blob (2014-2016): A major MHW that raised SSTs by up to 3 C for over two years. Triggered toxic algal blooms, seabird die-offs, and species displacement from California to Alaska.
  • Great Barrier Reef bleaching record: 1998, 2002, 2016, 2017, 2020, 2022, 2024 — interval collapsing from years to nearly annual. Synergy: bleaching kills colonies, acidification slows recovery, reef cannot rebuild before next event.
  • Mediterranean MHW 2003 and 2022: Record-breaking heatwaves causing mass mortality of sea fans, sponges, and posidonia seagrass meadows. Demonstrates that MHWs are a global, not just tropical, phenomenon.
  • CO2 + H2O -> H2CO3 -> H+ + HCO3- -> consumes CO3^2-: Reaction sequence that explains why acidification reduces aragonite saturation and threatens calcifiers.

Connect to the Exam

AP Environmental Science tests this material through Practice 6 (Mathematical Routines) — the pH calculation showing logarithmic increase in [H+] from a small pH change is the canonical example. Practice 1 (Concept Explanation) addresses the carbonic acid chemistry mechanism. Practice 2 (Visual Representations) often includes coral bleaching photos and SST anomaly maps. Practice 5 (Data Analysis) appears in pH-vs-time, sea-surface temperature anomaly graphs, or saturation-state-vs-latitude curves. Practice 7 (Environmental Solutions) requires evaluating mitigation (CO2 reduction) versus adaptation (assisted coral evolution, marine protected areas, hatchery interventions) responses. The forcing-feedback distinction from Topic 9.2 is reinforced here because warming and acidification act independently but synergistically on calcifying ecosystems.

Sample FRQ prompt: Pre-industrial ocean surface pH was 8.2; the 2024 value is 8.1, projected to fall to 7.8 by 2100 under high-emission scenarios. (A) Calculate the percent increase in [H+] from pre-industrial to 2024 and from pre-industrial to 2100, showing your work. (B) Explain the chemical mechanism by which CO2 absorption lowers ocean pH AND reduces carbonate ion availability for shell formation. (C) Describe one synergistic interaction between ocean warming and ocean acidification that threatens coral reefs more severely than either factor alone.

Self-Check

  • The pre-industrial ocean pH was 8.2; the current pH is 8.1. Using pH = -log10[H+], calculate the percentage increase in hydrogen-ion concentration. If pH falls to 7.9 by 2060, what would [H+] be relative to pre-industrial?
  • Explain why aragonite undersaturation is a critical threshold for coral reefs, and describe two ways in which ocean warming and acidification interact synergistically to threaten coral ecosystems.
  • The Pacific Northwest oyster hatchery collapses of the 2000s-2010s were an early signal of ocean acidification impacts. Identify the specific chemical mechanism that caused larval oyster shell failure, and explain why cold, upwelling water is particularly vulnerable to acidification.
  • Why are pteropods considered indicator species for ocean acidification? Identify two factors that make polar oceans the first to experience aragonite undersaturation.
