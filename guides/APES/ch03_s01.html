AP Environmental Science — Topic 3.1: Generalists, Specialists, and Survivorship Curves

Core Question

Why do some species, like raccoons and crows, expand into nearly every habitat humans modify, while others — pandas, koalas, condors — slide toward extinction? This section gives you the vocabulary (niche breadth, life-history strategy, survivorship type) to characterize these patterns precisely and connect them to conservation policy.

Key Concepts

  • Generalist species: Broad ecological niche; tolerates wide environmental conditions and consumes diverse foods (raccoons, crows, Norway rats). Resilient to habitat fragmentation and human land-use change; often net beneficiaries of disturbed edge habitats.
  • Specialist species: Narrow ecological niche; requires specific food, habitat, or abiotic conditions (koalas, black-footed ferrets, California condors). Outperforms generalists in stable environments but is highly vulnerable to disruption — disproportionately represented on endangered species lists.
  • Specialization as a trade-off: The physiology and behavior needed for one resource often reduce performance on others. Most species fall on a continuum, specializing on some dimensions and generalizing on others.
  • Survivorship curve: Graph of cohort survival vs. age, plotted on log scale so a straight line indicates constant mortality.
  • Type I (late-loss): Low mortality through life, steep at old age. Humans, elephants, whales. Adults are reproductive capital; elevated adult mortality is catastrophic.
  • Type II (constant): Linear on log scale; equal mortality at every age. Many birds and small mammals. Less sensitive to age-specific mortality sources.
  • Type III (early-loss): Steep drop early, then flat. Fish, insects, most plants. Resilient to juvenile mortality (huge offspring surplus) but vulnerable to adult mortality.
  • r-selected species: Many offspring, little parental investment, rapid population growth, typically Type III. Thrive in unstable, disturbed environments.
  • K-selected species: Few offspring with heavy parental investment, populations slow to recover, typically Type I. Vulnerable to overexploitation, habitat loss, introduced predators.
  • Conservation implication: K-selected species require large protected habitats and tight adult-mortality limits; r-selected species can often sustain higher harvest rates.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • The r-K framework: Endpoints of a reproductive-strategy continuum named after parameters in the logistic growth equation — r (intrinsic rate of natural increase) and K (carrying capacity).
  • Atlantic cod (Type III example): A female may release several million eggs per spawning event; the overwhelming majority of larvae die within weeks. Survivors face much better odds — illustrating why fisheries management for cod focuses on adult spawning biomass.
  • DDT and bald eagles (Type I/K-selected case): A pesticide that bioaccumulates and kills adults attacks the most demographically vulnerable life stage of a Type I species, illustrating how life-history theory predicts pollutant impact.
  • Reindeer of Saint Matthew Island: Foreshadowed in this section as a case where r-selected boom-and-bust dynamics can devastate slow-growing food resources (developed fully in 3.2).

Connect to the Exam

This topic supports Science Practice 1 (concepts) and Science Practice 2 (visual representations) — particularly reading and interpreting survivorship curves on log axes. Common stimulus types include life-history data tables (e.g., comparing offspring number, lifespan, and parental care across species), survivorship curve graphs requiring you to identify Type and explain demographic implications, and FRQ scenarios asking you to recommend management strategies based on r/K classification. Expect synthesis questions that connect this section to fisheries management, endangered species policy, or pesticide impact assessment.

Self-Check

  • Compare a generalist and a specialist species, naming one example of each, and explain why specialists are disproportionately threatened by habitat change.
  • Sketch the three survivorship curves on the same log axes and identify a representative species for each.
  • A bird species shows 35% mortality in year one, 32% in year two, and 30% in year three. Which survivorship type does this match, and what does that mean for management?
  • Why does the same percentage harvest rate threaten the long-term viability of a grouper population but not a herring population? Frame your answer using r- and K-selection.
