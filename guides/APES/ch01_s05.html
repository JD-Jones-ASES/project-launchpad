AP Environmental Science — Topic 1.5: Energy Flow and Trophic Dynamics

Core Question

If matter cycles indefinitely, why doesn't energy too? And why does this asymmetry — matter recycled, energy dissipated — explain ecosystem structure from the size of producer biomass to the rarity of apex predators to the energy bottleneck on human food supply? Section 1.5 establishes the thermodynamic principle (the Second Law applied to ecology) and uses it to derive the 10% rule and the architecture of food webs. Understanding trophic dynamics means understanding where energy comes from, how efficiently it is transferred, and why that efficiency is always less than 100%.

Key Concepts

  • Matter cycles, energy flows: The fundamental asymmetry. Atoms recycle indefinitely; energy degrades to heat at every transfer (Second Law).
  • Autotrophs (producers): Manufacture food from inorganic compounds. Photoautotrophs use sunlight; chemoautotrophs use chemical-bond energy.
  • Gross primary productivity (GPP): Total rate of photosynthetic energy fixation before plant respiration.
  • Net primary productivity (NPP): GPP - autotrophic respiration. The energy surplus available to consumers and what ecologists measure in the field.
  • Trophic level: Position in a food chain (producer = level 1; primary consumer = level 2; etc.).
  • Food chain vs. food web: Linear sequence vs. realistic network of overlapping food chains.
  • 10% rule (Lindeman's law): ~10% of energy at one trophic level transfers to the next; ~90% is lost as heat, feces/urine, and to non-biomass activity.
  • Energy loss pathways: (1) heat from cellular respiration; (2) feces/urine to decomposers; (3) movement, thermoregulation, etc.
  • Food-chain length constraint: Cumulative losses limit productive chains to ~4-5 trophic levels.
  • Decomposers (detritivores): Bacteria and fungi that break down detritus, releasing energy and mineralizing nutrients.
  • Detrital pathway: In most ecosystems, more biomass enters via litter/death than via herbivory; decomposers process most of an ecosystem's energy budget.

Key Figures / Cycles / Mechanisms

  • NPP equation: NPP = GPP - R_autotrophic. The plant respiration term (R_autotrophic) is substantial; ignoring it leads to a major overestimate of energy available to the food web.
  • The 10% rule applied (forward): 10,000 kcal grass → 1,000 grasshoppers → 100 frogs → 10 snakes → 1 kcal hawk per m² per year. Each transfer keeps ~10%, loses ~90%.
  • The 10% rule applied (reverse): A hawk needing 1,000 kcal/yr requires 10,000 of snakes → 100,000 of frogs → 1,000,000 of grasshoppers → 10,000,000 kcal of grass. Reverse calculation = multiply by 10 per trophic level.
  • Why apex predators are rare: After 4 trophic transfers, the apex receives 10^-4 of the producer flux. To sustain viable populations, apex predators need vast territories (a savanna supports thousands of wildebeest but only a handful of lions).
  • Detrital pathway dominance: Forest soils and marine sediments process more energy through decomposers than through herbivory. Leaf litter, dead roots, feces, and uneaten prey all feed decomposers, who release the bulk of an ecosystem's heat.
  • Decomposers couple energy and nutrients: While dissipating energy as heat, decomposers also mineralize nutrients (N, P, C) back to inorganic forms. The biogeochemical cycles and the energy budget cannot be separated.
  • Light-Bottle / Dark-Bottle field method: NPP = Final DO_light - Initial DO; R = Initial DO - Final DO_dark; GPP = NPP + R. Convert 1 mg O2/L ≈ 0.375 mg C/L. The standard AP lab investigation.

Connect to the Exam

The 10% rule is one of the most heavily-tested calculations on the AP exam — expect both forward problems (given producer NPP, calculate energy at trophic level N) and reverse problems (given apex consumer requirement, calculate required producer biomass). Practice 6 (Mathematical Routines) dominates here. NPP and GPP definitions appear in many multiple-choice questions; the field-lab dissolved-oxygen method is a likely Practice 5 (Data Analysis) stimulus. Free-response prompts often ask students to explain why apex predators are rare or to predict the consequence of removing decomposers, which test conceptual understanding (Practice 1) of the matter-cycles / energy-flows asymmetry. The food-system efficiency argument (animal vs. plant agriculture) is a common cross-unit synthesis question.

Self-Check

  • A food chain reads phytoplankton → zooplankton → herring → tuna. If phytoplankton NPP is 8,000 kcal/m²/yr, apply the 10% rule to estimate energy available to tuna per square meter per year. What does this imply about tuna population density relative to phytoplankton?
  • Explain how removing decomposers from an ecosystem would affect both energy flow and nutrient cycling. Why can neither function independently of the other?
  • A field investigator runs the Light-Bottle / Dark-Bottle method and finds Initial DO = 8 mg/L, Final DO_light = 11 mg/L, Final DO_dark = 6 mg/L. Calculate NPP, R, and GPP, and explain what each value means.
  • Why does the 10% rule constrain food-chain length to ~4-5 trophic levels? What real-world ecosystem patterns (apex-predator rarity, large lion territories) follow from this constraint?
