AP Environmental Science — Topic 9.2: The Greenhouse Effect and Anthropogenic Climate Change

Core Question

How does the natural greenhouse effect make Earth habitable, and how have human activities since 1750 amplified it beyond anything in the 800,000-year ice-core record? Distinguishing forcings (CO2 emissions) from feedbacks (water vapor, ice albedo) is essential for understanding why CO2 — despite having the lowest GWP per molecule — is the dominant driver of observed warming. The greenhouse effect is the foundation for every climate consequence examined in Topics 9.3-9.6.

Key Concepts

  • Natural greenhouse effect: The 33 degrees C of warming above the bare-rock baseline produced by atmospheric absorption and re-radiation of outgoing infrared radiation. Without the natural greenhouse effect, Earth's average surface temperature would equilibrate at approximately -18 degrees C, making liquid water and complex life impossible.
  • Radiative forcing: The change in net energy flux at the top of the troposphere caused by a perturbation, expressed in W/m^2. Positive forcing = warming influence; negative forcing = cooling. CO2 alone produces about +2.1 W/m^2 of forcing relative to pre-industrial; total anthropogenic forcing is approximately +3.0 W/m^2.
  • Carbon dioxide (CO2): The most abundant anthropogenic greenhouse gas by cumulative forcing. Pre-industrial 280 ppm; 2024 value 422 ppm (51% increase, unprecedented in 800,000 years of ice-core data). GWP_100 = 1 (reference). Atmospheric lifetime: decades to centuries.
  • Methane (CH4): GWP_100 of 27-30 times CO2. Sources: fossil-fuel leakage, livestock, rice paddies, landfills, thawing permafrost. Concentration has risen from 700 to 1930 ppb. Shorter lifetime (~12 years) than CO2 but more potent per molecule.
  • Nitrous oxide (N2O): GWP_100 of 273. Sources: synthetic nitrogen fertilizers, livestock manure, fossil and biomass combustion. Now the dominant ozone-depleting substance as CFCs decline.
  • Fluorinated gases (F-gases): HFCs, PFCs, and SF6 (GWP ~23,500). Synthetic only. Tiny concentrations but powerful per molecule. Phased down under the 2016 Kigali Amendment.
  • GWP_100 (Global Warming Potential): Standardized measure comparing 100-year radiative effect of one unit mass of a gas to one unit mass of CO2. Combines absorption efficiency and atmospheric lifetime on a common scale.
  • Water-vapor feedback (positive): Warmer air holds about 7% more water vapor per degree C; additional vapor amplifies infrared trapping. Roughly doubles the direct CO2-only warming and is the largest single amplifying feedback.
  • Ice-albedo feedback (positive): Melting ice exposes darker surfaces (snow ~0.8 to ocean ~0.06), absorbing more solar radiation. Drives Arctic amplification — the Arctic warms about three times faster than the global mean.
  • Planck response (negative): Stefan-Boltzmann law (flux ~ T^4) ensures that a warmer Earth radiates more energy to space, ultimately returning the system to balance at a higher temperature. The primary negative feedback preventing runaway warming.
  • Forcing vs. feedback distinction: A forcing is an external driver (CO2, solar, volcanic). A feedback responds to temperature (water vapor, ice). The skeptic's "water vapor is the dominant greenhouse gas" argument fails because water vapor is a feedback, not a driver.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • The 33-degree-C gift of the atmosphere: Bare rock at Earth's distance from the Sun would equilibrate at -18 C. Actual global average surface temperature is +15 C. The 33 C difference is provided by the absorption-re-radiation of triatomic and larger atmospheric molecules.
  • Stefan-Boltzmann law: Radiation flux ~ T^4. Underlies the Planck response and explains why a warmer Earth radiates more energy to space, eventually closing the greenhouse-induced energy imbalance.
  • NASA GISS surface temperature record: Documents approximately 1.1-1.2 C of warming above the 1850-1900 baseline since 1880. Half of the increase has occurred since 1980. The 2014-2024 decade contains the ten warmest years; 2023 was the warmest single year.
  • IPCC AR6 (2021) attribution conclusion: "It is unequivocal that human influence has warmed the atmosphere, ocean and land." This unprecedented language reflects convergence of multiple independent lines of evidence — model-observation comparisons, paleoclimate reconstructions, and direct emissions accounting.

Connect to the Exam

AP Environmental Science tests this material extensively, often through quantitative reasoning. Practice 1 (Concept Explanation) requires students to explain why Earth is 33 C warmer than a bare rock and why CO2 is the dominant driver despite its GWP of 1 (concentration and lifetime overcome low GWP per molecule). Practice 5 (Data Analysis) shows up in keystone calculations: percent change in CO2 (51% from 280 to 422 ppm); pH change in ocean; and ranking gases by GWP_100. Practice 7 (Environmental Solutions) requires evaluating the relative effectiveness of mitigating each gas: short-term cuts to CH4 and HFCs are powerful because of high GWP and short lifetimes; CO2 cuts are essential because of its long lifetime. The forcing-feedback distinction is a perennial favorite stimulus question, often involving water vapor.

Sample FRQ prompt: Atmospheric CO2 concentrations have risen from a pre-industrial value of 280 ppm to 422 ppm in 2024. (A) Calculate the percent increase and explain how it compares to the natural glacial-interglacial range of 180-280 ppm visible in the 800,000-year ice-core record. (B) Identify one positive feedback that amplifies the direct CO2 warming and explain its mechanism. (C) A skeptic argues that since methane has a GWP of 27-30 times CO2, methane reductions are more important than CO2 reductions. Evaluate this argument considering both atmospheric lifetime and total radiative forcing.

Self-Check

  • Explain why Earth's surface temperature is 33 C warmer than a bare rock at our distance from the Sun would be. Identify two molecular properties of greenhouse gases that make this warming possible.
  • Rank the following gases in order of decreasing GWP_100: SF6, N2O, CH4, CO2. Explain why CO2 — despite having the lowest GWP per molecule — is the most important driver of observed anthropogenic warming.
  • A skeptic argues that water vapor, not CO2, is the dominant greenhouse gas, so human CO2 emissions are climatically unimportant. Evaluate this argument using the distinction between forcings and feedbacks.
  • Mars has more CO2 percentage in its atmosphere than Earth but a smaller greenhouse effect. Identify the variable that explains this paradox.
