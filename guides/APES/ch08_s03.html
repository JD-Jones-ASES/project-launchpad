AP Environmental Science — Topic 8.3: Wetlands, Mangroves, and Coral Reef Bleaching

Core Question

Why are wetlands, mangrove forests, and coral reefs simultaneously the most ecologically productive ecosystems on Earth and among the most threatened, and what regulatory frameworks have been built — or rolled back — to protect them? The cumulative-stress framework reveals that each of these ecosystems faces multiple, interacting stressors that together produce decline rates no single stressor would explain alone, and that local management cannot substitute for global climate action when the dominant driver is sea-surface temperature anomalies driven by anthropogenic warming.

Key Concepts

  • Wetland: Area where soil is saturated permanently or seasonally, creating conditions that favor hydrophytic vegetation. Includes marshes, swamps, bogs, fens, and seasonally flooded bottomlands. The US has lost ~50% of its original wetland extent since European settlement.
  • Wetland ecosystem services: Water filtration (acre removes hundreds of pounds of N annually), flood attenuation (reduces downstream peaks by 20–60%), groundwater recharge, wildlife habitat, carbon storage. Engineered alternatives replicate functions piecemeal at higher cost.
  • CWA Section 404: Prohibits dredge/fill discharges into US waters without an Army Corps of Engineers permit. The structural mechanism for federal wetland protection.
  • Sackett v. EPA (2023): Supreme Court narrowed jurisdiction to wetlands with a "continuous surface connection" to navigable waters; removed federal protection from tens of millions of acres of ephemeral/isolated wetlands.
  • Mangrove: Salt-tolerant tree or shrub in tropical/subtropical intertidal zones, with prop roots/pneumatophores. Covers ~138,000 km² globally; ~35% lost since the 1950s, primarily to shrimp aquaculture, rice agriculture, urban development.
  • Mangrove storm-surge protection: 100-meter belt reduces wave height 50–70%; 2004 tsunami fatality rates 4–10x lower behind intact mangroves.
  • Blue carbon: Mangrove soils accumulate organic carbon 3–5x higher per hectare than tropical rainforest soils. Clearing releases stored carbon as CO₂.
  • Coral bleaching: Loss of color and productivity when thermal stress causes coral polyps to expel symbiotic zooxanthellae. SST 1°C above long-term summer maximum for 4+ weeks triggers expulsion via ROS.
  • Zooxanthellae: Photosynthetic dinoflagellates living in coral tissue, providing ~90% of polyp energy.
  • Coral reef stressors beyond thermal: ocean acidification, nutrient pollution, sedimentation, overfishing of herbivorous fish.
  • Cumulative-stress framework: Multiple stressors weaken corals so that thermal events push them past survivable thresholds. Local management cannot substitute for global climate action but buys time.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • Bleaching cellular mechanism: SST exceeds threshold → zooxanthellae metabolic rate increases → ROS production toxic to polyp → polyp expels algae as stress response → polyp loses ~90% of energy supply → if heat persists, polyp starves and dies.
  • GBR (2016–2024): 5 mass bleaching events in 9 years; ~50% coral cover loss since 2000; recovery interval compressed from 25–30 years to under 10.
  • IPCC projections:
    • 1.5°C warming: 70–90% loss of tropical coral reefs
    • 2°C warming: more than 99% experience annual/biannual bleaching, incompatible with recovery
  • Wetland ecosystem-service economics: A single wetland acre removes hundreds of pounds of nitrogen per year at far lower cost than engineered treatment.

Connect to the Exam

AP Environmental Science tests these ecosystems through case-based stimulus questions and FRQs. The Concept Explanation (CE) skill requires explaining the cellular bleaching mechanism, the cumulative-stress framework, and the wetland → engineered-infrastructure cost comparison. The Environmental Solutions (ES) skill applies to local stressor management as a complement (not substitute) for global climate action. The Source Analysis (SA) skill connects to Sackett v. EPA: students should understand how a Court decision can dramatically alter a regulatory framework's reach. The Concepts and Processes (CP) skill applies to the trade-off in mangrove conversion: short-term aquaculture revenue vs. long-term storm-surge protection and fishery production.

Sample FRQ prompt: A coastal community in Indonesia clears 200 acres of mangroves to build shrimp aquaculture ponds. Five years later, a typhoon makes landfall. (A) Identify TWO ecosystem services lost to the community by clearing the mangroves. (B) Explain the cellular-level mechanism by which mangrove forests dissipate storm-surge energy. (C) Calculate the carbon released if mangrove soils contained 1,000 metric tons of carbon per hectare. (D) Propose ONE alternative coastal land-use that would preserve mangrove storm-surge protection while supporting community economic needs.

Self-Check

  • Why do wetlands act as "the kidneys of the landscape," and what specific biogeochemical process(es) underlie their water-filtration function?
  • How did Sackett v. EPA (2023) change CWA jurisdiction over wetlands, and what category of wetlands lost federal protection?
  • Trace the cellular-level mechanism of coral bleaching from thermal stress to polyp death. Where in the cascade is recovery still possible?
  • Apply the cumulative-stress framework to the Great Barrier Reef: name TWO local stressors and ONE global stressor, and explain why managing local stressors alone is insufficient.
