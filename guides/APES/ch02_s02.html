AP Environmental Science — Topic 2.2: Island Biogeography and Habitat Fragmentation

Core Question

Why do larger and less isolated habitat patches support more species, and how does that quantitative pattern guide modern conservation reserve design as natural landscapes are fragmented by human land use?

Key Concepts

  • Island biogeography: The study of species richness on islands (or island-like patches) as a function of area and isolation; the MacArthur-Wilson 1967 framework remains the conceptual backbone of reserve design.
  • MacArthur-Wilson equilibrium: Species richness on an island reflects a dynamic balance between immigration (declines as the species list grows) and local extinction (rises with crowding). Composition turns over even when total richness is stable.
  • Species-area relationship: S = c*A^z; z typically 0.15-0.35 for oceanic islands. Rule of thumb: reducing area by 90% halves species richness over the long term.
  • Isolation effect: Islands close to a mainland receive more immigrants per unit time than remote islands, raising the equilibrium species count.
  • SLOSS (Single Large or Several Small): Modern synthesis favors large cores linked by corridors over either pure SL or pure SS designs.
  • Habitat fragmentation: Continuous habitat is divided into smaller, more isolated patches by roads, agriculture, and urban development. Reduces area, increases edge, reduces connectivity, and isolates populations simultaneously.
  • Edge effect: Ecological changes near patch boundaries — greater light, wind, temperature; lower humidity; more invasives, generalists, nest predation, brood parasitism. Interior specialists suffer disproportionately.
  • Edge-to-interior ratio: Geometric measure that grows as fragments shrink or elongate; reduces functional interior area beyond what geometric area suggests.
  • Connectivity: The ease with which organisms move between patches. Conservation resource as tangible as area itself.
  • Biological corridor: A strip of natural habitat linking fragments. Restores connectivity, gene flow, demographic rescue, and supports climate-driven range shifts. Trade-off: can also spread disease and invasives.
  • Metapopulation framework: Levins 1969, Hanski extension. Models populations as patch networks where local extinctions are balanced by recolonization through dispersal.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • Robert MacArthur and Edward O. Wilson (1967): Quantitative theory of island biogeography that turned out to apply to any isolated habitat patch.
  • Richard Levins (1969): Metapopulation framework — populations as networks of patches connected by dispersal, with local extinction balanced by recolonization.
  • Ilkka Hanski: Extended the metapopulation framework through the late twentieth century into the predictive form used in modern conservation.
  • Yellowstone-to-Yukon (Y2Y) Initiative: Most ambitious North American corridor project, linking protected areas from Wyoming to the Yukon. Justified by MacArthur-Wilson theory extended to terrestrial landscapes.
  • Banff National Park wildlife overpasses: Empirical demonstration that corridors increase movement, genetic exchange, and population persistence for large carnivores.

Connect to the Exam

Topic 2.2 commonly appears as a quantitative species-area calculation (Science Practice 6 — mathematical routines): given area reduction, calculate the predicted species loss using S = c*A^z. FRQ stimulus questions often present a fragmented landscape map and ask students to identify which species are most at risk (Science Practice 4 — model analysis), explain the mechanisms by which fragmentation harms them (edge effects, connectivity loss, inbreeding), and propose a corridor or buffer design that addresses one of those mechanisms.

Self-Check

  • Calculate the expected species loss when a 1,000 ha reserve is reduced to 100 ha, using z = 0.25.
  • A new highway bisects a forest reserve, producing two equal-area fragments. List three distinct mechanisms (beyond raw area loss) by which the species count in each fragment is expected to decline over time.
  • Why would a long, thin corridor have a higher edge-to-interior ratio than a square reserve of equal area, and why does that matter for an interior-specialist bird?
  • Under what conditions does the Several Small strategy outperform Single Large, and vice versa?
