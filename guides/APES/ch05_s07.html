AP Environmental Science — Topic 5.7: Mining, Urban Sprawl, and Ecological Footprints

Core Question

The extraction of metals, minerals, and fossil fuels and the expansion of human settlement across agricultural, forest, and wetland landscapes are two of the most physically transformative processes in contemporary land use. Together they illustrate how the cumulative aggregate of individually rational decisions — each mine, each subdivision, each interchange — restructures whole landscapes and permanently alters ecosystem function. The unit closes by placing these processes in the context of the ecological footprint, a single metric that attempts to express humanity's total demand on the biosphere in units comparable to the biosphere's actual productive capacity. Easter Island serves as the canonical parable.

Key Concepts

  • Surface mining: Removes overburden to access shallow deposits. Three forms: strip mining, open-pit mining, mountaintop removal. ~60% of US mineral/coal production. Higher land disturbance than subsurface mining but lower worker risk.
  • Strip mining: Sequential strip-and-restore for shallow coal seams. Regulated under SMCRA (1977).
  • Open-pit mining: Large pit advancing downward; copper (Bingham Canyon, Utah, largest man-made excavation), gold, iron, phosphate. Waste rock dumps can be unstable and produce AMD if sulfide-bearing.
  • Mountaintop removal (MTR): Up to 300 m of summit blasted away; spoil deposited as valley fill burying headwater streams. >3,200 km of Appalachian streams buried; >500 summits affected. Permanent.
  • Acid mine drainage (AMD): Pyrite + O2 + H2O → sulfuric acid + ferric hydroxide → dissolves Fe, Al, Mn, As, Pb, Cd. pH 2-3. >23,000 km of US streams affected. Treatment indefinite (lime/limestone neutralization).
  • Yellow boy: Iron hydroxide precipitate; orange-red coating on streambeds, eliminates benthic invertebrate habitat.
  • SMCRA (1977): Federal regulation of surface coal mining; requires restoration to approximate original contour, performance bond, suitable post-mining land use.
  • Subsurface mining: Room-and-pillar (grid of tunnels with support pillars) and longwall (mechanized panel with controlled roof collapse). Predictable subsidence over longwall panels can damage surface infrastructure.
  • Urban sprawl: Low-density, auto-dependent expansion outward from city centers. US developed land grew 25M → 100M acres 1945-2000 (4x) while population only ~doubled.
  • Smart growth: Mixed-use zoning, transit-oriented development, urban growth boundaries (Portland's 1979 UGB), brownfield reclamation.
  • Easter Island deforestation: Polynesian settlement ~800-1200 CE; deforestation by ~1500 CE; population crash from 15,000 to 2,000-3,000 by 1722. Parable of overshoot.
  • Ecological footprint: Global average 2.7 gha/person; biocapacity 1.6 gha/person. Overshoot factor ~1.7. US 8.1 gha vs. India 1.2 gha. ~5 Earths needed if everyone lived as the average American.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • AMD chemistry: FeS2 (pyrite) + O2 + H2O → Fe(OH)3 + 2 H2SO4 (simplified). The sulfuric acid (pH 2-3) dissolves additional metals from surrounding rock — iron, aluminum, manganese, arsenic, lead, cadmium. As long as pyrite remains in contact with oxygen and water, the reaction continues. Treatment (limestone or lime neutralization) must continue indefinitely; abandoned mines produce AMD for centuries.
  • MTR scale: Up to 300 m of summit removed; >3,200 km of Appalachian headwater streams buried by valley fill; >500 summits affected. Physical alteration is permanent — buried stream channels do not recover.
  • Sprawl arithmetic: US developed land 25M acres (1945) → 100M acres (2000), a 4x increase, while population grew only ~2x. The implication: per-person developed-area roughly doubled, reflecting declining residential density. This generates the externalities listed: farmland loss, vehicle miles, ecosystem-service erosion, heat islands, spatial inequity.
  • Easter Island cascade: Polynesian colonization ~800-1200 CE → forest clearance for canoes, moai construction, agriculture, fuel → by ~1500 CE largely deforested → loss of timber for ocean-going canoes → end of deep-sea fishing → soil erosion → declining agricultural productivity → intertribal conflict (toppled moai) → population from peak ~15,000 to ~2,000-3,000 by 1722 European contact. Revisions emphasize rats, disease, slave trade — but the deforestation/erosion sequence is well-documented.
  • Footprint inequality: US 8.1 gha vs. India 1.2 gha — a ~7x per-person difference. The 5-Earths figure for universalized US lifestyle illustrates that current developed-country consumption patterns are mathematically incompatible with global universalization at current population levels.

Connect to the Exam

Topic 5.7 generates AMD chemistry items (write the simplified equation, identify reactants and products, predict pH and metal mobilization), MTR impact items (quantify summit removal, valley fill, stream burial), and sprawl-vs-smart-growth comparison FRQs (identify externalities of sprawl; name and explain at least three smart-growth tools). Easter Island items typically ask students to identify the specific features that made overshoot irreversible (non-replaceable resources, social institutions failing) and draw an analogy to global overshoot.

The ecological footprint vocabulary recurs from Section 5.1: students should be able to define footprint, identify the global average and biocapacity values, calculate the overshoot factor (~1.7), and articulate why per-capita consumption matters as much as population. The 5-Earths-if-American-lifestyle figure is exam-canonical.

Self-Check

  • Explain the chemistry of acid mine drainage using the simplified reaction. Identify TWO specific ecological effects of AMD on a mountain stream (e.g., pH-driven invertebrate kill, yellow-boy coating eliminating benthic habitat), and describe ONE remediation technique and its limitation (e.g., limestone neutralization is effective but must continue indefinitely as long as pyrite, oxygen, and water remain in contact).
  • Easter Island is used as a "parable of overshoot." What specific features of Easter Island's situation made overshoot particularly irreversible once it began? Identify ONE factor that might make the global situation either more or less analogous to Easter Island's situation.
  • The ecological footprint of an American is approximately 8.1 gha; the ecological footprint of an Indian is approximately 1.2 gha. Calculate the per-capita footprint ratio and explain what would need to change about US consumption patterns for global universalization to be sustainable at current population.
  • Compare the ecological costs of mountaintop removal (MTR) and conventional underground (longwall) coal mining. Identify ONE permanent environmental cost of MTR that longwall mining avoids.
