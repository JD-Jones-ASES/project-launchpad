AP Environmental Science — Topic 8.6: Solid Waste, E-Waste, and Wastewater Treatment

Core Question

How does a modern industrial society manage — or fail to manage — the solid waste, hazardous waste, and wastewater that its production and consumption generate, and why is nearly every major US framework in this space the direct legislative response to a well-documented disaster? The Cuyahoga River fire of 1969 catalyzed the EPA and the Clean Water Act of 1972; Love Canal's contamination drove CERCLA in 1980; the 1976 Resource Conservation and Recovery Act emerged from the long failure of voluntary, end-of-pipe waste regulation; and modern wastewater-treatment standards reflect the cumulative scientific understanding documented across this entire unit. Reading the laws backward through the events that created them is the AP-tested key to remembering what each statute does and why.

Key Concepts

  • Solid Waste Hierarchy: EPA priority ranking from most to least preferred — (1) source reduction, (2) reuse, (3) recycling, (4) composting, (5) combustion with energy recovery, (6) landfilling. The earlier a waste is intercepted, the more embodied energy and materials are conserved. Source reduction outranks recycling because preventing waste avoids the energy and pollution of every downstream stage.
  • Sanitary Landfill: Modern engineered disposal cell with composite liner (clay + HDPE membrane), perforated leachate collection pipes, daily soil cover, and methane gas capture. Required under RCRA Subtitle D. Distinct from the open dumps that preceded the 1970s.
  • Leachate: Contaminated liquid from precipitation or groundwater percolating through waste; carries heavy metals, volatile organic compounds, chlorinated solvents, and decomposition byproducts. Captured and treated under RCRA.
  • Landfill Methane: Greenhouse gas from anaerobic decomposition; captured for electricity generation, but US landfills remain the third-largest anthropogenic methane source.
  • Incineration / Waste-to-Energy (WTE): Reduces volume 70-90% and generates electricity. Flue-gas controls (scrubbers, ESPs, activated carbon, fabric filters) remove mercury, dioxins, furans, HCl, and particulates. Environmental justice concerns frequently surround facility siting.
  • E-Waste: Discarded electronics; ~50 million metric tonnes/year globally; fastest-growing solid waste stream; only ~20% formally recycled. Contains valuable metals (gold, silver, copper, palladium) and hazardous substances (lead, cadmium, mercury, brominated flame retardants).
  • Basel Convention (1989, amended 2019): Restricts hazardous waste exports from industrialized to developing countries; "second-hand goods" loophole was widely exploited until 2019 amendments closed some gaps.
  • Wastewater Treatment Stages:
    • Primary (physical): bar screens + settling tanks (clarifiers) → 30-40% TSS, 30% BOD removal
    • Secondary (biological): activated sludge process — aeration tanks + secondary clarifier → 85-95% BOD and TSS reduction. Minimum legal standard for US POTWs.
    • Tertiary (advanced): removes N, P, pathogens, pharmaceuticals, trace organics. Required where TMDLs for nutrients apply.
  • Activated Sludge Process: The dominant secondary treatment design — aerobic bacteria suspended in aeration tanks decompose organic matter; secondary clarifier settles the biomass.
  • RCRA (1976): First comprehensive federal framework for hazardous and non-hazardous solid waste. Subtitle C cradle-to-grave manifest system; Subtitle D landfill standards; Subtitle I underground storage tanks. Forward-looking only.
  • CERCLA / Superfund (1980): Cleans up legacy contamination through National Priorities List (~1,300 sites) and strict, joint-and-several liability for PRPs (current owner, past owner, generator, transporter). Innocent landowner defense for buyers conducting "all appropriate inquiry."
  • Polluter-Pays Principle: Those who generate pollution internalize its full social cost; embedded in CERCLA, the Rio Declaration, and OECD guidelines.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • The cradle-to-grave manifest (RCRA Subtitle C): Every shipment of listed or characteristic hazardous waste must be tracked through generators, transporters, and treatment-storage-disposal facilities (TSDFs). All three categories of actor share legal responsibility, ending the pre-1976 regime in which generators could pay a hauler and forget the waste.
  • Strict, joint-and-several liability (CERCLA Section 107): Strict = fault is irrelevant; joint-and-several = a single PRP can be held responsible for the entire cleanup cost. Designed to ensure cleanups proceed even when major contributors are insolvent. Mitigated in practice through de minimis settlements and contribution litigation among PRPs.
  • Lois Gibbs (Love Canal Homeowners Association, 1978): Homeowner whose son attended the elementary school built on the contaminated former Hooker Chemical site. Her door-to-door canvassing, media outreach, and confrontations with state and federal officials forced the political response that culminated in CERCLA's passage.
  • The Cuyahoga River fire (June 22, 1969): Not the largest fire on the river — it was approximately the 13th since 1868, and the 1952 fire was worse — but the one that became a "focusing event" via Time magazine coverage that crystallized public outrage and produced the EPA (1970) and the CWA (1972).
  • Hooker Chemical / Occidental Petroleum (Love Canal generator, 1942-1953): Buried ~21,000 metric tons of dioxins and chlorinated solvents in an unfinished canal; sold the property for $1 to the Niagara Falls Board of Education with deeded notice and a liability disclaimer that a court later voided.
  • William Ruckelshaus (EPA Administrator, 1970-73): Banned DDT (1972) and oversaw early implementation of CWA and the Clean Air Act; reappears in §8.4's DDT story.

Connect to the Exam

Topic 8.6 is the legislation-and-disasters culmination of Unit 8. Expect MC questions that require you to (a) order the solid waste hierarchy from most to least preferred and explain why source reduction outranks recycling; (b) compare RCRA (forward-looking, cradle-to-grave for new waste) with CERCLA (retrospective, legacy contamination, strict joint-and-several liability); and (c) trace the causal chain from a specific event (Love Canal, Cuyahoga fire) to the legislative outcome (CERCLA, EPA founding + CWA), naming three or more actors. Wastewater questions test the three-stage progression — primary (physical, 30%), secondary (biological activated sludge, 85-95%), tertiary (nutrient and pathogen removal) — and the conditions under which tertiary becomes mandatory (downstream TMDLs for N and P, eutrophication-prone receiving waters). FRQ analysis prompts on the CERCLA primary source typically ask you to identify two ways strict liability encourages due diligence (rigorous Phase I environmental site assessments before purchase; corporate avoidance of relationships with poorly-run TSDFs) and to explain the innocent landowner defense's "all appropriate inquiry" requirement. Connect this section forward to environmental justice (incinerator siting, e-waste export to low-income countries) and backward to §8.5 (hazard-quotient calculations are how cleanup standards are set at NPL sites) and §8.2 (tertiary treatment for nutrient removal is the technical response to cultural eutrophication).

Self-Check

  1. List the six tiers of the solid waste hierarchy from most to least preferred. Explain why source reduction is ranked above recycling.
  2. Compare RCRA's regulatory approach to CERCLA's. Identify the specific gap in RCRA's coverage that CERCLA fills, and the legal mechanism CERCLA uses to ensure that polluters rather than taxpayers bear cleanup costs.
  3. Trace the causal chain connecting the Love Canal contamination to the passage of CERCLA, naming at least three actors (individuals, organizations, or government bodies) and their roles.
  4. A POTW discharges into a receiving water with a TMDL for nitrogen and phosphorus. The plant operates with primary and secondary treatment only. Identify the next required treatment stage and explain what processes it uses to reduce nutrient loading.
