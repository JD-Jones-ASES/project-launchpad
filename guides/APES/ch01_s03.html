AP Environmental Science — Topic 1.3: Biogeochemical Cycles I: Carbon and Hydrologic

Core Question

If Earth is a closed system for matter, what mechanisms physically move atoms between Earth's spheres? Section 1.3 introduces biogeochemical cycles as the answer, then examines the two cycles most central to climate and life: carbon (which determines atmospheric CO2 and therefore the planet's energy balance) and water (the universal solvent that drives the chemistry of every other cycle, weather, and ecosystem productivity). Both cycles will reappear in every subsequent unit; a firm grasp of their mechanics is non-negotiable for AP success.

Key Concepts

  • Biogeochemical cycle: The path of an element or compound through biotic and abiotic components of an ecosystem.
  • Reservoir: A storage compartment for a cycled substance (atmosphere, biosphere, hydrosphere, lithosphere).
  • Carbon cycle reservoirs: Atmosphere (CO2, CH4); biosphere (organic compounds); hydrosphere (dissolved CO2, carbonate ions); lithosphere (carbonate rocks, fossil fuels, soil organic matter).
  • Residence time: Average duration an atom stays in a reservoir = reservoir size / outflow rate. Atmosphere: years. Deep sediment: millions of years.
  • Fast loop / slow loop: Atmosphere-biosphere-ocean exchange operates over years to centuries; lithosphere exchange over millions of years. Fossil-fuel combustion injects slow-loop carbon into the fast loop in decades.
  • Flux: Rate of flow between reservoirs (Pg C/yr, where 1 Pg = 10^15 g).
  • Sink vs. source: Sink = inflows exceed outflows (carbon accumulating). Source = outflows exceed inflows (carbon releasing).
  • Sequestration: Long-term removal of CO2 from the atmosphere into a long-residence reservoir. Natural (forest growth, ocean uptake) vs. engineered (industrial capture and geological injection).
  • Hydrologic cycle: Movement of water among ocean, atmosphere, land surface, and groundwater. No significant geological slow loop.
  • Evapotranspiration (ET): Direct evaporation + plant transpiration. Tropical forests recycle enormous quantities, partly 'manufacturing' their own rainfall.
  • Runoff vs. infiltration: How precipitation reaching land partitions between surface flow and soil percolation. Impervious surfaces shift the balance toward runoff.
  • Aquifer / fossil water: Permeable rock storing groundwater. Slow recharge means depletion (Ogallala, North China Plain) is effectively permanent on human timescales.

Key Figures / Cycles / Mechanisms

  • Short-term carbon cycle: Photosynthesis (CO2 → biomass) ↔ respiration (biomass → CO2); ocean diffusion exchanges CO2 with the atmosphere. Decomposition releases CO2 and CH4. Operates on years-to-centuries.
  • Long-term carbon cycle: Marine organisms build CaCO3 shells → sediment → limestone (lithification). Subduction → mantle. Volcanic outgassing returns CO2. Organic matter buried under anoxic conditions slowly becomes coal/oil/gas over millions of years.
  • Anthropogenic acceleration: Combustion of fossil fuels returns slow-loop carbon to the atmosphere on decadal timescales — far faster than the natural sinks (ocean uptake, forest growth) can absorb. The result is the rising atmospheric CO2 trend.
  • Hydrologic processes: Evaporation/evapotranspiration → atmospheric water vapor → condensation → precipitation → runoff or infiltration → ocean or aquifer.
  • Urban hydrology: Impervious surfaces (roads, roofs, parking lots) increase runoff (raising flood peaks) and decrease infiltration (depleting groundwater supply) — a coupled hydrologic-engineering problem revisited in Units 4-5.
  • Carbon-water coupling: Soil moisture controls decomposition; precipitation pattern shifts alter terrestrial carbon storage; climate warming intensifies the water cycle (more evaporation, more intense precipitation events).

Connect to the Exam

Carbon-cycle questions appear in nearly every AP Environmental Science exam. Expect a stimulus showing a flux diagram (Pg C/yr arrows between reservoirs), a graph of atmospheric CO2 over time, or a paired table of natural vs. anthropogenic sources. You will be asked to (a) identify the source-sink direction of a perturbation, (b) explain the rate-mismatch mechanism behind anthropogenic CO2 rise, and (c) evaluate sequestration strategies. Hydrologic questions tend to focus on the urbanization scenario (paving over wetlands), 'fossil water' depletion, and ET as a regional climate driver. Practice 1 (Concept Explanation) dominates: name mechanism, then outcome.

Self-Check

  • Distinguish between a carbon source and a carbon sink. Give one natural example of each, and explain how the distinction is relevant to understanding current CO2 trends.
  • A city paves over a large wetland for development. Describe two hydrological consequences and explain the mechanism behind each.
  • Why is 'fossil water' depletion in the Ogallala and North China Plain effectively permanent on human timescales? Use the concept of recharge rate vs. withdrawal rate in your answer.
  • A volcano emits 0.1 Pg C/yr while global fossil-fuel combustion emits about 10 Pg C/yr. Use these flux values to explain why anthropogenic emissions, not volcanic emissions, drive contemporary atmospheric CO2 increase.
