AP Environmental Science — Topic 4.4: Global Wind, the Coriolis Effect, and Hadley Cells

Core Question

Why are Earth's biomes — rainforests, deserts, temperate grasslands, tundra — distributed in predictable latitudinal bands, and how do the three-cell atmospheric circulation model and the Coriolis effect produce this pattern? The locations of the world's great agricultural belts, deserts, and tropical forests follow directly from differential solar heating, the resulting pressure belts, and the lateral deflection imposed on every moving fluid by Earth's rotation.

Key Concepts

  • Differential solar heating: Equator receives nearly perpendicular solar rays (concentrated energy); poles receive oblique rays (energy spread). The resulting equator-to-pole temperature gradient drives all atmospheric circulation.
  • Intertropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ): Equatorial low-pressure belt with rising warm, moist air; produces the rainforest belt; surface "doldrums."
  • Hadley cell (0°-30°): Equatorial rising air → poleward flow at altitude → descent at ~30° → equatorward surface return; thermally direct and energetically dominant.
  • Ferrel cell (30°-60°): Mid-latitude cell, thermally indirect (mechanically driven by adjacent cells), weaker; produces the mid-latitude westerlies.
  • Polar cell (60°-90°): Cold, dense polar air sinks at the poles, flows equatorward at the surface, rises at ~60°; produces the polar easterlies.
  • Coriolis effect: Earth's rotation deflects moving objects to the right (Northern Hemisphere) or left (Southern Hemisphere). Magnitude proportional to speed × sin(latitude); zero at equator, maximum at poles.
  • Trade winds: Surface return flow of Hadley cells, deflected to produce northeast trades (NH) and southeast trades (SH); drove sailing-era trade routes.
  • Westerlies: Mid-latitude poleward Ferrel surface flow deflected to produce west-to-east winds; drive mid-latitude storms.
  • Subtropical high-pressure belt: Descending dry air at ~30° latitude, warming adiabatically, suppresses precipitation — origin of Earth's great deserts.
  • Adiabatic cooling/warming: Rising air cools by expansion (condenses moisture); descending air warms by compression (dries out).
  • Rain shadow: Dry leeward side of a mountain range produced by orographic lift on the windward side and adiabatic warming on descent (Atacama, eastern Cascades, Gobi).

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • George Hadley (1735): Proposed the original single-cell convection model for tropical circulation; modern Hadley cells are named for him. His model was extended by William Ferrel (mid-19th century) into the three-cell framework.
  • Gaspard-Gustave de Coriolis (1835): Derived the mathematical force law for moving objects in a rotating reference frame; the effect was applied to atmospheric circulation by Henrik Mohn and others later in the 19th century.
  • The Sahara dust-Amazon connection: Persistent northeast trade winds carry an estimated 0.1-0.4 billion tonnes of mineral dust each year from the Sahara to the Atlantic and Amazon basin, fertilizing ocean phytoplankton and tropical forests with iron and phosphorus — a transcontinental nutrient subsidy driven entirely by Hadley-cell circulation.
  • The Atacama Desert: A double rain-shadow desert blocked by the Andes (Atlantic moisture) and the cold Humboldt Current offshore (suppressed local evaporation); the driest non-polar place on Earth.

Connect to the Exam

AP Environmental Science treats global circulation as a unifying framework for biome distribution, hazard exposure, and pollutant transport. Free-response items often (a) ask students to explain why deserts cluster at 30° latitude using the three-cell model and adiabatic warming on Hadley descent, (b) require students to apply Coriolis deflection to predict trade-wind direction in either hemisphere, or (c) describe the mechanism by which orographic lift produces a wet windward and dry leeward climate (rain shadow). The Sahara → Atlantic dust pattern and the Atacama double rain shadow are high-utility connecting examples.

Self-Check

  • Why does the Sahara Desert lie at about 25°N rather than at the equator? Use the three-cell model and adiabatic warming in your answer.
  • Trace the Coriolis-effect path of equatorward surface return flow in the Northern Hemisphere Hadley cell. What wind belt does it become, and which direction does it blow toward?
  • Compare adiabatic cooling on the windward side of a mountain range with adiabatic warming on the leeward side. Why does the leeward side typically receive much less precipitation?
  • Why are eastbound transcontinental flights typically faster than westbound flights of the same route? Connect your answer to the Ferrel cell.
