AP Environmental Science — Topic 6.2: Coal, Oil, and Natural Gas — Extraction and Combustion

Core Question

What engineering methods are used to extract coal, oil, and natural gas from the Earth, what pollutants does their combustion release, and why does the market price of fossil fuels systematically understate their true social cost?

Key Concepts

  • Coal Extraction Methods: Surface methods (strip mining; mountaintop removal — MTR) work for shallow seams; underground methods (room-and-pillar at 40-60% recovery; longwall mining at 70-80% recovery with surface subsidence) work for deep seams. MTR has destroyed >500 Appalachian mountaintops, buried headwater streams in valley fill, and exposed sulfur-bearing rock to oxidation, generating acid mine drainage with pH sometimes below 3.
  • Hydraulic Fracturing (Fracking): Horizontal well drilled 1.5-3 km along shale; fluid mix (~90% water, 8-9% sand proppant, <1% chemical additives) injected at >70 MPa to fracture rock. Unlocked low-permeability shale and tight sandstone. Concerns include flowback water (chemicals + brine + sometimes radioactive material), methane leakage (1-4% of production at ~80x CO2 potency over 20 yr), water use (10-20 ML per well), and possible aquifer contamination.
  • Offshore Drilling and Tar Sands: One-third of global oil comes from offshore platforms with limited spill-containment ability. Canadian Athabasca oil sands hold viscous bitumen extracted by open-pit strip mining (depths <70 m) or steam-assisted gravity drainage (SAGD) for deeper deposits. Oil sands operations emit ~2x the CO2 per barrel of conventional oil due to natural-gas energy inputs.
  • Combustion Chemistry: Generic equation C_xH_y + O2 → CO2 + H2O + heat. Pure carbon releases ~32.8 MJ/kg. Real fuels contain sulfur, nitrogen, trace metals, and ash; combustion in air (~79% N2) generates thermal NOx.
  • Major Combustion Pollutants: CO2 (greenhouse gas; coal > oil > natural gas per MJ because coal has lowest H:C ratio); SO2 (forms H2SO4, drives acid deposition; coal is largest source); NOx (smog and ground-level ozone); PM2.5 (penetrates deep lung tissue; cardiovascular and respiratory mortality); mercury (volatilizes from coal, biomagnifies as neurotoxic methylmercury).
  • Math Routine — Coal Combustion: A 500 MW plant at 33% efficiency requires 1.80x10^6 MJ output → 5.45x10^6 MJ heat input → 227 tonnes/hr of bituminous coal at 24 MJ/kg → ~1.82 million tonnes/yr at 8,000 operating hours. Each kg of bituminous coal releases ~2.94 kg CO2 (carbon fraction 0.80 x 44/12 = 2.94) before efficiency losses.
  • Deepwater Horizon (2010): Misinterpreted negative-pressure test triggered blowout in the Macondo Prospect; BOP shear rams failed; rig sank; 11 workers killed. ~4.9 million barrels released over 87 days, oiling >1,100 km of Gulf shoreline. BP paid >$20 B; MMS reorganized into BSEE with strengthened well-control regulations.
  • Negative Externalities: Market prices fail to capture healthcare costs, ecosystem damage, oil-spill cleanup, and climate damages. The result is systematic over-consumption. Carbon taxes and cap-and-trade systems internalize these costs by making polluters pay a portion of the external damage.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • MTR mechanism: blasting → valley fill in stream hollows → buried headwaters → acid mine drainage from oxidized sulfur (pH sometimes <3) carrying dissolved heavy metals downstream.
  • Fracking sequence: horizontal drilling along shale → high-pressure fluid injection → rock fractures with sand-propped flow paths → flowback management.
  • Coal combustion math: output (MWh × 3.6 = MJ) → heat input (output / efficiency) → coal mass (heat input / energy density). Apply across mass-balance steps with units attached.
  • CO2-per-MJ ranking (coal > oil > natural gas) follows hydrogen-to-carbon ratio. Hydrogen oxidation produces water; only carbon oxidation produces CO2.
  • Oil-sands CO2 doubling: bitumen extraction + steam generation + upgrading to synthetic crude requires substantial natural-gas energy not needed for conventional crude.

Connect to the Exam

  • Be ready to compare extraction methods (surface vs. underground; conventional vs. tight-oil; conventional crude vs. bitumen) and identify their environmental signatures.
  • Apply the combustion-energy math routine to a different plant size or efficiency on the FRQ.
  • Evaluate claims that natural gas is 'clean' by raising methane leakage rates (1-4%) and full-fuel-cycle CO2 accounting.
  • Identify pollutants (SO2, NOx, PM2.5, Hg, CO2) and link each to a specific environmental or health pathway.
  • Use Deepwater Horizon as a case study for ultra-deepwater drilling risk and the limits of blowout containment.
  • Frame the externalities argument economically: market price < true social cost → over-consumption → policy levers (carbon taxes, cap-and-trade).

Self-Check

  • A 250 MW coal plant operates at 35% efficiency burning bituminous coal (24 MJ/kg). Estimate its hourly coal consumption.
  • A natural gas company claims its product is 'clean.' Identify two facts about extraction or the full fuel cycle that complicate this claim.
  • Compare longwall and room-and-pillar mining in terms of recovery fraction, surface impact, and worker safety.
  • Explain why mercury from coal combustion is more dangerous to predator fish than to phytoplankton, even though both are exposed.
  • Why does the market price of gasoline at the pump understate its true social cost? Name two specific externalities and one policy that addresses them.
