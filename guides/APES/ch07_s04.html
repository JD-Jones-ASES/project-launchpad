AP Environmental Science — Topic 7.4: Acid Deposition: Mechanics and Ecosystem Impacts

Core Question

How do SO2 and NOx emitted from one region produce acid deposition in another, why do some watersheds resist acidification while others fail catastrophically, and how did the US Title IV cap-and-trade program become the most successful market-based environmental policy in US history?

Key Concepts

  • Acid deposition: All pathways by which atmospheric acids (H2SO4, HNO3) or their precursors reach Earth's surface — both wet (precipitation) and dry (particles and gases absorbing onto surfaces).
  • Two dominant acidifying agents: Sulfuric acid (H2SO4) from SO2 (mainly coal combustion) and nitric acid (HNO3) from NOx (mainly mobile sources and high-temperature combustion). Historically, sulfur dominated US acid deposition because coal produced far larger SO2 volumes than vehicles produced NOx.
  • Sulfur pathway: SO2(g) + H2O → H2SO3(aq) → H2SO4 in cloud water. Alternatively, gas-phase SO2 + ·OH → H2SO4 → fine sulfate particles depositing dry.
  • Nitrogen pathway: NO2 + ·OH → HNO3(g), which dissolves into cloud water or absorbs onto surfaces.
  • Normal vs. acid rain pH: Normal rain is slightly acidic (pH ~5.6) because atmospheric CO2 dissolves to form carbonic acid. Acid rain has pH below 5.0; the northeastern US and parts of Europe routinely saw pH 4.2–4.4 at the peak of the problem in the 1970s–80s.
  • Wet vs. dry deposition: Wet deposition is acid carried in rain, snow, fog, or cloud water. Dry deposition is acid particles and gases absorbing directly onto surfaces. Dry deposition continues between rain events and is often underestimated.
  • Spring acid pulse: In regions with seasonal snowpack, winter acid input accumulates in the snow and releases as a concentrated burst during spring melt — coinciding with amphibian breeding and fish egg-hatching, the most vulnerable window for sensitive aquatic life.
  • Lake-pH thresholds: Below pH 6.0, many invertebrates disappear. Below pH 5.0, most fish cannot survive — salmonids (brook trout, Atlantic salmon) are especially sensitive because gill protein denaturation impairs ion regulation. By the early 1980s, hundreds of Adirondack lakes were fishless.
  • Three forest-damage mechanisms: (1) Direct foliar damage from acid mist leaching protective waxes and nutrients from conifer needles; (2) soil acidification displacing base cations (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, K⁺) by exchanging them for H⁺ and Al³⁺; (3) aluminum toxicity from soluble Al³⁺ at low pH attacking fine root tips. The Black Forest, Czech Sudeten mountains, and southern Appalachian spruce-fir forests all showed dieback.
  • Building/statue erosion: H2SO4 attacks carbonate stone (limestone, marble): CaCO3 + H2SO4 → CaSO4 + H2O + CO2. Calcite converts to soft, water-soluble gypsum. The Parthenon, Taj Mahal, and Gothic cathedrals lost more surface detail in the 20th century than in the preceding twenty.
  • Buffer capacity: A watershed's ability to neutralize incoming acidity without significant pH change. Carbonate bedrock dissolves in acid water — CaCO3 + 2H⁺ → Ca²⁺ + H2O + CO2 — providing high buffering. Granitic and quartzite watersheds have negligible buffering.
  • Why the Northeast acidified, Midwest did not: The Adirondacks, Catskills, and Canadian Shield rest on ancient granites and gneisses with no carbonate buffering. The limestone-underlain Midwest received the same or higher acid loading but neutralized the acid before it reached surface water.
  • Title IV cap-and-trade: 1990 Clean Air Act Amendments established a national cap-and-trade program for SO2 from electric utilities — the first large-scale emissions-trading program in US environmental law. Phase I (1995–1999) cap of 8.9 million tons; Phase II (2000+) tightened to 4.5 million tons. By 2020, US power-plant SO2 fell ~88% from 1990 levels.
  • Why cap-and-trade works: The cap guarantees aggregate reductions; trading concentrates abatement at the lowest-cost sources. Plants that can reduce cheaply sell surplus allowances; plants facing high abatement costs buy allowances. Aggregate cost is minimized while the cap is met with legal certainty.
  • Why SO2 worked but CO2 has been harder: Acid-rain victims (NE lakes, Adirondack forests) were a politically legible domestic constituency, the sources (Midwestern coal plants) were locatable, and the externality stayed within US jurisdiction. CO2 is a global commons problem with diffuse stakeholders and entrenched fossil-fuel political economy.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • Sulfur formation chemistry: SO2(g) + H2O → H2SO3(aq); H2SO3 + ½O2 → H2SO4(aq).
  • Limestone buffering: CaCO3 + 2H⁺ → Ca²⁺ + H2O + CO2.
  • Limestone erosion by acid: CaCO3 + H2SO4 → CaSO4 + H2O + CO2 (calcite to soft gypsum).
  • Title IV outcomes: ~88% emissions reduction by 2020; Adirondack lake recovery documented; soil base-cation depletion still being worked off.

Connect to the Exam

  • Be able to write the two-step chemical equation showing how SO2 becomes H2SO4 in cloud water.
  • Use buffer capacity to explain why two lakes receiving the same acid loading can have very different pH outcomes — granite vs. limestone bedrock is the classic comparison.
  • Articulate why cap-and-trade achieves the same environmental target at lower cost than command-and-control: trading concentrates abatement at the lowest-cost reduction options nationwide.
  • Be ready to discuss the political economy contrast between SO2 success and CO2 stalemate — identifiable victims, locatable sources, US jurisdiction.

Self-Check

  • Write the two-step equation showing how SO2 becomes sulfuric acid in cloud water, then write the equation showing how H2SO4 reacts with limestone to neutralize acidity.
  • Two lakes of similar size receive identical acid loading. One sits on Adirondack granite at pH 4.8; the other sits on Midwest limestone at pH 7.0. Explain the difference using the buffer-capacity concept.
  • Why does Title IV cap-and-trade achieve the SO2 reduction target at lower aggregate cost than a uniform percentage-reduction mandate would?
  • Identify three mechanisms by which acid deposition damages forests, distinguishing direct from indirect effects.
