AP Environmental Science — Topic 6.6: Energy Conservation, Efficiency, and CAFE Standards

Core Question

Why is the cheapest unit of energy the one never produced, and which thermodynamic and policy tools can be combined to deliver more useful work from less primary energy input?

Key Concepts

  • First Law of Thermodynamics: Energy is conserved across all conversions: E_input = E_useful + E_losses. A 33%-efficient coal plant converts 33% of heat to electricity and discards 67% as waste heat. The First Law is bookkeeping — it does not limit efficiency on its own.
  • Second Law of Thermodynamics: In any heat-to-work conversion, some waste heat is unavoidable; entropy always increases. The Second Law sets a fundamental ceiling — no engineering improvement can reach 100% efficiency.
  • Carnot efficiency: η_max = 1 − T_cold/T_hot (with both in Kelvin). A steam turbine between 600 K and 300 K has a Carnot ceiling of 50%; real turbines achieve 35–45%. Combined-cycle plants approach 60% by cascading a high-temperature gas turbine (~1,400°C) with a steam turbine that harvests its waste heat.
  • Real-world efficiencies: Gasoline engine 20–30%; coal plant ~33%; combined-cycle gas 55–60%; electric motor ~90%; LED 45–55%; incandescent ~5%. Replacing a 60 W incandescent with a 10 W LED produces the same light using one-sixth the electricity.
  • CAFE standards: Corporate Average Fuel Economy program — established 1975 in response to the 1973 oil embargo. Requires automakers to meet a minimum sales-weighted average mpg across passenger cars (and separately, light trucks). Has ratcheted from ~18 mpg (1978) to a 2026 target of ~49 mpg.
  • CAFE CO2 math: Combustion of 1 gallon of gasoline releases ~8.89 kg CO2. A vehicle driven 12,000 mi/yr at 30 mpg uses 400 gal and emits 3,556 kg CO2; the same miles at 50 mpg use 240 gal and emit 2,134 kg — a 40% reduction.
  • Energy Star: Voluntary EPA labeling identifying appliances, electronics, and buildings that exceed federal minimum efficiency standards. Energy Star products typically use 10–50% less energy than minimum-compliant alternatives.
  • LEED certification: Voluntary green-building rating system from the US Green Building Council. Buildings earn points for energy efficiency, water conservation, materials, indoor air quality, and site design. Levels: Certified, Silver, Gold, Platinum. LEED buildings use 25–30% less energy than code-minimum buildings.
  • Heat pumps: Transfer (not generate) heat from outdoor air or ground to indoors. Coefficient of performance (COP) routinely exceeds 3.0 — meaning 3 units of heat are delivered per unit of electricity. Cuts heating energy 50–70% vs. resistance electric or gas furnace. Does not violate the First Law because heat is moved, not created.
  • Smart thermostats and demand-side management: Devices like Nest and Ecobee deliver 10–15% annual heating/cooling savings via occupancy learning. Buildings account for ~40% of US energy use — a high-leverage target for conservation policy.
  • Unit conversions: 1 kWh = 3.6 MJ = 3,412 BTU; 1 therm = 100,000 BTU. The grid reports kWh; US building standards use BTU; international science uses MJ. Watts and kW are rates; Wh and kWh are energy quantities.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • Carnot equation: η_max = 1 − T_cold/T_hot. Higher T_hot or lower T_cold raises the ceiling.
  • Diminishing returns: Improving 33% → 40% saves 11% of fuel input; improving 50% → 57% saves only 9%. Carnot ceiling is unavoidable.
  • CAFE timeline: 18 mpg (1978) → 27.5 mpg (1990) → 54.5 mpg target (2025) → ~49 mpg (2026).
  • Light bulb math: 60 W incandescent at 5 h/day × 365 d = ~110 kWh/yr; 10 W LED ≈ 18 kWh/yr; savings ~91 kWh/bulb/yr.
  • CO2 emission factor: ~0.4 kg CO2 per kWh of US electricity (national grid average).
  • Buildings energy share: ~40% of US energy consumption.

Connect to the Exam

  • AP Environmental Science Unit 6 expects fluency with Carnot efficiency, CO2 calculations from gasoline/electricity, and unit conversions among kWh, MJ, and BTU.
  • FRQs commonly ask students to calculate annual CO2 from a vehicle at given mpg, evaluate why a heat-pump COP can exceed 1, or identify the policy mechanism (CAFE, Energy Star, LEED) that targets a particular sector.
  • Watch for the lab investigation on household electricity audits — recurring as a stimulus for both unit conversion and end-of-pipe behavior questions.

Self-Check

  • Calculate: a 1,500 W space heater runs 8 h/day for 30 days. What is monthly energy in kWh and MJ?
  • Why can a heat pump have COP > 1 without violating the First Law?
  • A coal plant operates at T_hot = 800 K and T_cold = 300 K. What is its Carnot efficiency ceiling, and why does the real plant achieve only ~33%?
  • Explain how raising CAFE from 30 to 50 mpg yields a ~40% CO2 reduction per vehicle, given that miles driven and emission per gallon are unchanged.
