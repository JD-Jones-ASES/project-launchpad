AP Environmental Science — Topic 6.1: Global Energy Trends and Fossil Fuel Formation

Core Question

Why does the modern global economy still depend on fossil fuels for roughly 80% of its primary energy, and what geological processes — operating over hundreds of millions of years — created the coal, oil, and natural gas we are now burning through in a few centuries?

Key Concepts

  • Primary vs. Secondary Energy: Primary energy is energy in its raw, unprocessed form (crude oil, coal, natural gas, sunlight, wind). Secondary energy is converted from primary energy, most commonly electricity. Roughly two-thirds of primary fuel energy is lost as waste heat in conversion.
  • Global Energy Mix (2024): Oil ~31%, coal ~25%, natural gas ~24%, hydroelectric ~7%, biomass ~5%, nuclear ~4%, wind+solar ~5%. Fossil fuels collectively supply ~80% of global primary energy, down only modestly from 86% in 2000 because total demand has grown faster than renewable buildout.
  • Energy Intensity: Energy consumed per unit of GDP (typically megajoules per dollar). Declines as economies shift from heavy manufacturing to services and as efficiency improves; rises in early industrialization. Global intensity is roughly half of 1970 levels, yet absolute consumption has nearly tripled because the economy grew much faster than efficiency gains.
  • Coal Formation: Solidified terrestrial biomass from Carboniferous wetland forests (~300-360 Myr ago). Lignin-rich plant material accumulated faster than it could decompose because lignin-digesting fungi had not yet proliferated. Burial drove off water, oxygen, and hydrogen, raising carbon content from peat (~50% C) through lignite, sub-bituminous, and bituminous to anthracite (>90% C).
  • Coal Rank and Energy Density: Higher rank means higher carbon, higher energy density (lignite ~15 MJ/kg, anthracite ~32 MJ/kg), and cleaner combustion. Major deposits cluster in regions where Carboniferous swamps once existed (Appalachian, Powder River, Ruhr, Donbass, Bowen Basin).
  • Oil and Natural Gas Formation: Microscopic marine plankton settled to the seafloor and accumulated in anoxic sediments, forming kerogen-rich source rock (typically black shale). Catagenesis at 60-120 deg C transformed kerogen into crude oil (the oil window); above ~150 deg C, oil cracked further into natural gas (the gas window). Buoyant hydrocarbons migrated upward through reservoir rock until trapped under impermeable cap rock.
  • Peak Oil: M. King Hubbert (1956) modeled production from a finite reservoir as a bell-shaped curve peaking when about half the recoverable resource has been extracted. He correctly predicted US conventional oil would peak around 1970 at ~9.6 Mb/d.
  • Tight-Oil Revolution: Hydraulic fracturing in the 2000s-2010s unlocked low-permeability shale and tight sandstone formations, raising US production from ~5 Mb/d (2008) to ~13 Mb/d (2023) and reversing the post-1970 decline. The principle of finite reserves still holds; tight oil simply expanded the size of the recoverable pool.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • The 80% fossil-fuel share of global primary energy has barely budged because energy demand is growing faster than renewables can be built.
  • Coal rank progression (peat → lignite → sub-bituminous → bituminous → anthracite) tracks rising temperature and pressure, falling water/oxygen/hydrogen content, and rising energy density (~15 to ~32 MJ/kg).
  • The oil window (60-120 deg C) and gas window (>150 deg C) define the depth/temperature regimes where each fuel forms in marine sedimentary basins.
  • Hubbert curves show production rising, peaking near 50% depletion, and declining symmetrically; they apply to any finite reservoir.
  • Timescale mismatch: 50-100 Myr to form vs. decades to consume — meaning no technical fix can replace fossil fuels by accelerating geology.

Connect to the Exam

  • Be ready to identify the geological setting of each fossil fuel: coal = Carboniferous terrestrial swamps; oil and gas = marine plankton in anoxic basins.
  • Know energy-mix percentages and recognize that fossil fuels still dominate (~80%) despite renewable growth.
  • Distinguish primary and secondary energy and explain conversion losses (~2/3 of primary becomes waste heat).
  • Use Hubbert's logic to argue why finite resources eventually peak, and why fracking expanded but did not invalidate the principle.
  • Connect declining energy intensity to structural shifts (manufacturing → services) and efficiency gains, while noting absolute consumption can still rise if GDP grows faster.

Self-Check

  • A country has 60% of its household energy supplied by traditional biomass yet only 5% biomass in its national energy statistics. Where is this country most likely located, and why is the discrepancy so large?
  • Explain why anthracite coal has higher energy density than lignite, even though both came from the same swamp biomass.
  • A new oil field is discovered in 200-Myr-old marine shale. Explain what each piece (age, environment, rock type) tells you about the formation pathway.
  • Energy intensity in a country has fallen 40% over 30 years while absolute consumption has doubled. What does this imply about GDP growth and structural change?
