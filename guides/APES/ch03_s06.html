AP Environmental Science — Topic 3.6: Case Studies in Demographic Change

Core Question

How do demographic models translate into real outcomes when applied at national scale? This section presents three detailed country-level case studies (China, Japan, sub-Saharan Africa), introduces climate migration, and walks you through a yeast-population lab that lets you observe logistic and exponential growth in real time.

Key Concepts

  • China's one-child policy (1979–2015): Coercive demographic policy averted ~400 million births and pushed TFR from 3.0 to ~1.6. Severe unintended consequences include sex-ratio distortion (~118:100 by 2010), accelerated aging, and erosion of civil liberties.
  • Policy reversibility lesson: Once fertility norms shift, they are difficult to reverse by decree. China's 2016 and 2021 reversals to allow more children have not raised TFR significantly.
  • Japan as Stage 5 archetype: TFR ~1.3, deaths exceeding births since 2007, population peak in 2010, projected decline below 100 million by 2060s. Despite extensive policy responses (retirement age extension, automation, selective immigration), fertility has not reversed.
  • Demographic dividend: Economic growth that can follow declining fertility when the working-age cohort dominates dependents — but only realized through productive employment, education, and institutions.
  • Sub-Saharan Africa's youth bulge: TFR ~4.5, working-age population projected to roughly double 2020–2050. Could fuel a demographic dividend or generate political instability depending on job creation and investment.
  • Sub-Saharan success cases: Ethiopia, Rwanda, Ghana, Senegal demonstrate that rapid growth and dividend capture are achievable in the region with the right institutional foundations.
  • Climate migrant: Person displaced by environmental change rather than political persecution. IPCC (2018) projects up to 200 million by 2050 without major emission reductions.
  • Population–climate intersection: High-fertility regions are also among the most climate-vulnerable, compounding migration pressure.
  • Twenty-first-century challenge: Neither population growth alone nor consumption growth alone — both together define global resource demand. Effective solutions act on both levers.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • China population trajectory: 540 million (1949) → 970 million (1979) → near 1.4 billion today; one-child policy enforced 1980–2015 with regional and temporal variation including coerced sterilizations and abortions in some periods.
  • Japan's Society 5.0: Official technology-policy framework relying on robotics and automation to compensate for declining labor supply.
  • ILO sub-Saharan jobs estimate: ~18 million new jobs per year through 2035 needed to absorb labor market entrants.
  • IPCC 2018 climate migration projection: Up to 200 million displaced by 2050 in scenarios without major adaptation investment, with sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and low-lying coastal areas most exposed.
  • Yeast lab investigation: Saccharomyces cerevisiae in 1% glucose broth shows exponential growth in early hours, transitioning to logistic deceleration as glucose depletes and ethanol accumulates. Plotting on semi-log scale reveals the exponential phase as a linear segment whose slope = r. K is set by nutrient availability.

Connect to the Exam

This topic supports Science Practice 1 (concepts), Science Practice 4 (data analysis on case-study and lab graphs), and Science Practice 7 (environmental solutions). Common stimulus types include case-study scenarios where you must identify the demographic stage and predict consequences (one-child policy aftermath, Japan aging, sub-Saharan youth bulge), graphs of TFR or population vs. time for specific countries, and lab-based questions on the yeast investigation requiring you to estimate r from semi-log slopes and identify the limiting factor that produces logistic deceleration. Expect FRQ-style questions linking demographic change to climate vulnerability and migration.

Self-Check

  • Identify two unintended consequences of China's one-child policy and explain the causal mechanism behind each.
  • Why is Japan's experience considered a warning for other Stage 4 countries? Cite at least two policy responses Japan has tried.
  • Explain why sub-Saharan Africa's youth bulge could be either an economic opportunity or a political risk. What policies determine which outcome occurs?
  • In the yeast lab, what is the most likely limiting factor that produces the logistic braking effect, and how does it operate at the cellular level?
