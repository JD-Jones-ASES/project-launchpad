AP Environmental Science — Topic 7.5: Mitigation Technologies and the Clean Air Act

Core Question

How do source controls and end-of-pipe technologies — wet scrubbers, electrostatic precipitators, selective catalytic reduction, and catalytic converters — work together with the Clean Air Act's statutory framework to deliver the most measurable public-health gains in US environmental law?

Key Concepts

  • Source control vs. end-of-pipe control: Source controls change the process itself — switching from high-sulfur to low-sulfur coal, replacing combustion with wind or solar, redesigning engines to produce less NOx. End-of-pipe controls add equipment at the output of a process to capture or convert pollutants that have already formed. Source controls eliminate; end-of-pipe controls capture. A complete air-quality strategy uses both.
  • Wet scrubber / flue gas desulfurization (FGD): Sprays an alkaline limestone slurry (CaCO3 + water) into hot coal-boiler flue gas. The reaction SO2 + CaCO3 + ½H2O → CaSO3·½H2O (with forced oxidation forming CaSO4·2H2O, gypsum) removes 95–99% of SO2. Gypsum is a saleable wallboard byproduct.
  • Electrostatic precipitator (ESP): Uses a 20,000–100,000 V electric field to charge fine particles, which are then collected on grounded plates and rapped off as a cake. Achieves >99% efficiency on PM10 and PM2.5; standard on virtually all large coal boilers, cement kilns, and steel mills.
  • Selective catalytic reduction (SCR): Injects ammonia or urea upstream of a vanadium pentoxide or zeolite catalyst bed. The two main reactions — 4NO + 4NH3 + O2 → 4N2 + 6H2O and 6NO2 + 8NH3 → 7N2 + 12H2O — convert NOx to harmless N2 and water at 80–95% efficiency. Widely installed under NSPS and MATS rules.
  • Three-way catalytic converter: Vehicle exhaust device with platinum, palladium, and rhodium on a ceramic honeycomb substrate. Simultaneously oxidizes CO to CO2, oxidizes unburned hydrocarbons to CO2 and H2O, and reduces NOx to N2. Operates within a narrow window around the stoichiometric air-fuel ratio; modern engine computers continuously adjust fuel injection.
  • Catalyst poisoning by lead: Lead compounds in gasoline adsorb on platinum and rhodium active sites and deactivate the catalyst. The 1975 federal mandate for catalytic converters therefore forced unleaded gasoline; the phase-out completed in 1996; US childhood blood-lead levels fell >90% between 1976 and 2000 — one of the largest US public-health gains of the 20th century.
  • Stage II vapor recovery: Rubber-boot/vacuum system at gasoline pumps that captures vapors displaced from the vehicle tank during refueling, cutting refueling VOC emissions by ~95% and reducing photochemical-smog precursors.
  • Diesel SCR + DPF: Heavy-duty diesel trucks and buses use aqueous urea (diesel exhaust fluid) injected upstream of a vanadium or zeolite catalyst, plus a diesel particulate filter. Modern diesels emit ~95% less NOx and PM than pre-regulation 1990s engines.
  • Clean Air Act framework (1970, amended 1977 and 1990): Mandate to "protect and enhance the quality of the nation's air resources." Key provisions: NAAQS for criteria pollutants (Title I); New Source Performance Standards (NSPS, §111) — technology-based limits reflecting best demonstrated technology; New Source Review (NSR) and BACT for nonattainment-area new sources; mobile-source standards (Title II); Title IV SO2 cap-and-trade; Title VI ozone-depleting substance phase-out aligned with the Montreal Protocol.
  • Enforcement: EPA is the primary federal regulator; states co-implement through SIPs. EPA may impose Federal Implementation Plans, withhold federal highway funds, and bring civil penalties up to $25,000 per day per violation. Citizen-suit provisions enable non-governmental enforcement.
  • Significance: EPA's 1990–2020 retrospective estimated the 1990 Amendments alone prevented ~230,000 premature deaths per year by 2020, with benefits exceeding costs by ~30:1.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • FGD chemistry: SO2 + CaCO3 + ½H2O → CaSO3·½H2O. With forced oxidation: CaSO3 + ½O2 + 2H2O → CaSO4·2H2O.
  • SCR chemistry: 4NO + 4NH3 + O2 → 4N2 + 6H2O.
  • Three-way converter reactions: 2CO + O2 → 2CO2; CxHy + O2 → CO2 + H2O; 2NO + 2CO → N2 + 2CO2.
  • Lichen bioindicator transect: Crustose lichens are the most pollution-tolerant; foliose and fruticose forms are progressively more sensitive. Plotting species richness vs. distance from a pollution source typically shows increasing diversity outward — an inexpensive integrative measure of air quality.

Connect to the Exam

  • Be ready to match a control technology to its target pollutant: ESP → fine PM; FGD/wet scrubber → SO2 (gypsum byproduct); SCR → NOx (N2 + H2O); catalytic converter → CO + HC + NOx in vehicle exhaust.
  • Explain the catalyst-poisoning argument that linked the 1975 catalytic-converter mandate to the lead phase-out, and why this regulatory linkage produced one of the largest 20th-century US public-health victories.
  • Distinguish NSPS (technology-based) from NAAQS (concentration-based) and from NSR/BACT (pre-construction permitting in nonattainment areas).
  • Be able to identify the major Clean Air Act titles: Title I (NAAQS, SIPs), Title II (mobile sources), Title IV (SO2 cap-and-trade), Title VI (ODS phase-out).
  • Use the lichen transect lab framework to analyze how integrative bioindicators reveal pollution gradients — and identify confounding variables (substrate type, sunlight, moisture).

Self-Check

  • A coal-fired power plant installs both a wet scrubber and an electrostatic precipitator. List the primary pollutant each device controls and describe the chemical product the FGD system generates as a saleable by-product.
  • Explain why the 1975 federal tailpipe emission standards (90% reduction in CO and HC from 1970 levels) required a technological transition to catalytic converters rather than simply better engine tuning, and why that transition required eliminating leaded gasoline.
  • Distinguish NSPS from NAAQS in their legal structure and scope. Why does NSPS apply regardless of attainment status?
  • In a lichen transect lab, crustose lichens are present at Station A near a highway, but foliose and fruticose lichens are absent until Station D. Explain in terms of pollutant concentrations and lichen growth-form sensitivity.
