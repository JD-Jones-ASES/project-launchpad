AP Environmental Science — Topic 3.5: Population Growth Math: Rule of 70 and Doubling Time

Core Question

How fast is a population growing right now, and how long until it doubles? This section equips you with the small set of formulas — population growth rate, crude rates, and the Rule of 70 — that you will use repeatedly on the AP exam, in lab work, and in any serious discussion of resource demand.

Key Concepts

  • Population growth rate equation: r = ((B - D) + (I - E)) / N × 100%. Combines natural increase (B - D) with net migration (I - E), divided by current population N.
  • Crude birth rate (CBR): Births per 1,000 population per year. Sensitive to age structure.
  • Crude death rate (CDR): Deaths per 1,000 population per year. Sensitive to age structure.
  • Natural rate of increase: CBR − CDR (per 1,000). Convert to percent by dividing by 10. Example: CBR 35, CDR 12 → 23 per 1,000 = 2.3% per year.
  • Rule of 70: T_double = 70 / r, where r is the growth rate in percent per year. Accurate to within ~1% for rates between 0.5% and 10%.
  • Doubling time: Years required for a quantity to double at constant growth rate. Inversely proportional to r.
  • Decimal-to-percent conversion: Always convert decimals to percent before applying Rule of 70. r = 0.025 → 2.5% → T = 70/2.5 = 28 years. Plugging 0.025 directly gives 2,800 years (wrong by 100×).
  • Compounding: Small rate differences produce large long-run differences. 1% doubles in 70 years (factor 2 over 70 years); 3% doubles every ~23 years (factor 8 over 70 years).
  • Rate vs. absolute increment: Different metrics. Global rate peaked ~1968 at 2.1% per year; absolute annual increment peaked ~1989 (still ~80 million per year through early 2020s).

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • Worked example 1 — Country r calculation: Population 50 million, 1,200,000 births, 600,000 deaths, +100,000 net migration. ΔN = 700,000. r = 1.4%. T_double = 50 years.
  • Worked example 2 — Two countries doubling time: Alpha at 4% per year doubles in 70/4 = 17.5 years. Beta at 1% per year doubles in 70 years. Four-fold difference in rate produces four-fold difference in doubling time.
  • World population trajectory: ~1 billion (1800), ~2 billion (1930), ~5 billion (1987), ~8 billion (2022). Linear growth before 1950 became accelerating after public-health-driven mortality declines spread through the developing world. Rate has slowed since 1968 as fertility decline spreads, but absolute increment remained near 80 million per year through the early 2020s.
  • Whether global population stabilizes near 9–10 billion or rises toward 11–12 billion depends primarily on the pace of TFR decline in high-fertility sub-Saharan African countries — which Section 3.4 established depends heavily on girls' educational attainment.

Connect to the Exam

This topic supports Science Practice 5 (mathematical routines) more directly than any other section in Unit 3. Expect FRQ-style problems requiring you to (1) calculate r from raw birth/death/migration data, (2) compute doubling time using Rule of 70, (3) interpret the difference between growth rate and absolute increment, and (4) catch the decimal-vs-percent conversion error. Common stimuli include population data tables and time-series graphs of world or country population since 1800.

Self-Check

  • A population of 10 million has 250,000 births, 100,000 deaths, and zero migration. Calculate r and T_double. Show all steps.
  • Convert these growth rates to doubling times: 0.5%, 2%, 4%, 7%, 10%.
  • A colleague claims that because Niger's TFR is much higher than the Netherlands's, Niger must add more people per year in absolute terms. Evaluate this claim, considering both rate and population base.
  • Why did the global population growth rate peak in 1968 but the annual absolute increment not peak until 1989? Explain in one or two sentences.
