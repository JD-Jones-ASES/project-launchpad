AP Environmental Science — Topic 7.2: Photochemical Smog and Thermal Inversions

Core Question

How do photochemical reactions in the atmosphere and meteorological caps on vertical mixing combine to produce the urban air-pollution episodes that have shaped 20th-century air-quality regulation?

Key Concepts

  • Photochemical smog: A mixture of secondary pollutants — primarily ground-level ozone (O3), peroxyacetyl nitrate (PAN), and fine secondary particulates — formed when VOCs and NOx react under UV sunlight. Removing any one of the three ingredients (VOCs, NOx, sunlight) halts smog formation.
  • NOx photolysis cycle: NO2 + UV → NO + O; the atomic oxygen reacts with O2 to form O3. This cycle alone would plateau, but VOC chemistry amplifies it.
  • VOC amplification: VOCs react with hydroxyl radicals (·OH) to form peroxy radicals (RO2·). These oxidize NO back to NO2 without consuming O3, allowing ground-level ozone to accumulate well above the simple photolysis equilibrium.
  • PAN (peroxyacetyl nitrate): A secondary product formed when acetaldehyde (a VOC oxidation product) reacts with NO2 in UV light. Powerful eye irritant and phytotoxin; stable at low temperatures, so it transports NOx far downwind and releases it on warming, acting as a regional reservoir.
  • Why peak O3 lags rush-hour emissions: The chain reaction requires hours of UV plus the amplification cycle; the air mass also moves downwind during this time, so peak O3 occurs in the early afternoon, often several miles downwind of the urban core.
  • Geographic vulnerability: Cities prone to photochemical smog share (1) high vehicle density, (2) strong UV (low-latitude or high-altitude clear skies), (3) low average wind speeds, and (4) basin/valley topography. LA, Mexico City, Beijing, and Delhi are textbook cases.
  • Environmental lapse rate: Temperature normally drops ~6.5 °C per 1,000 m of altitude. Surface air rises, mixes upward, and disperses pollutants — the atmosphere's primary self-cleaning mechanism.
  • Thermal inversion: A reversal of the normal lapse rate — a layer of warm air sits above cooler surface air. Cooler, denser surface air cannot rise; vertical mixing is suppressed; pollutants accumulate beneath the inversion like water under a sealed lid.
  • Inversion as a meteorological cap: Inversions do not create pollution. They eliminate the dilution pathway, allowing modest emission rates to produce dangerous concentrations when the inversion persists for several days.
  • Radiation inversion: Forms on clear calm nights via surface radiative cooling; shallow, dissipates after sunrise.
  • Subsidence inversion: Forms in descending high-pressure air that warms adiabatically; persistent for days to weeks; the dominant mechanism in chronic LA smog and the 1952 London Smog.
  • Valley inversion: Cold dense air drains into low-lying terrain at night, trapping any pollution sources within (Donora 1948).

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • Photolysis sequence: NO2 + hν → NO + O; O + O2 + M → O3 + M, where M is a third-body molecule (typically N2 or O2) that carries away excess energy.
  • VOC amplification reaction: RO2· + NO → RO· + NO2 — short-circuits the natural NO–O3 sink.
  • Donora 1948 case study: Five-day inversion in the Monongahela River valley trapped zinc-smelter and steel-mill SO2 and zinc oxide fumes. ~20 deaths within four days, ~7,000 ill (half the town's population). Catalyzed the 1955 Air Pollution Control Act, the first federal air-pollution legislation.
  • London Great Smog 1952: Five-day subsidence inversion trapped SO2 and smoke from ~1.5 million coal-burning domestic hearths and diesel buses. Death toll later reconstructed to ~12,000 excess deaths — the most lethal single air-pollution episode on record for a Western city. Triggered the UK Clean Air Act of 1956 (smoke-control zones, conversion from coal to smokeless fuels).

Connect to the Exam

  • AP exam questions frequently ask you to diagram how VOCs, NOx, and sunlight interact to produce ground-level ozone, labeling primary vs. secondary species.
  • Be ready to explain spatial and temporal lag in photochemical smog — why peak O3 is downwind and afternoon-shifted relative to rush-hour emissions.
  • Distinguish between the three inversion types (radiation, subsidence, valley) and which case studies match each.
  • Compare and contrast Donora vs. London — both involved inversions, but the chemistries (industrial SO2 + metals vs. domestic coal SO2 + diesel soot) differed and motivated different regulatory responses.
  • Understand the Key Concept that thermal inversions concentrate rather than create pollution. The same emissions become dangerous only because dilution pathways are blocked.

Self-Check

  • Sketch the smog-formation diagram: identify VOCs, NOx, sunlight, peroxy radicals, O3, and PAN. Mark which species are primary and which are secondary.
  • Why does peak ozone in LA occur in the afternoon and downwind of the urban core, even though VOC and NOx emissions peak in the morning rush hour near downtown?
  • Explain how Donora's narrow river valley and a multi-day temperature inversion combined with industrial emissions to produce the 1948 disaster.
  • Why is PAN regionally significant beyond local urban smog? What property allows it to transport NOx into rural areas?
