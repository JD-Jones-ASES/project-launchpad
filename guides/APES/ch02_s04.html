AP Environmental Science — Topic 2.4: Adaptation: Microevolution and Natural Selection

Core Question

How do populations of organisms change across generations in response to environmental pressure, and why does the slow, irreversible nature of evolutionary divergence make biodiversity loss categorically different from other forms of environmental degradation?

Key Concepts

  • Natural selection: The process by which heritable traits that increase reproductive success become more common over generations, while disadvantageous traits become rarer. Operates on existing variation; does not create it.
  • Three conditions for natural selection: Variation (heritable), heritability (parents pass traits to offspring), and differential reproduction (consistent fitness differences among variants). All three must hold simultaneously.
  • Sources of variation: Mutation, genetic recombination during sexual reproduction, and gene flow from neighboring populations. Without ongoing input, selection exhausts variation and adaptation halts.
  • Fitness: Relative contribution to the next generation's gene pool, measured as viable fertile offspring surviving to reproductive age. Always relative to the current environment.
  • Microevolution: Measurable changes in allele frequencies within a population over time.
  • Macroevolution: Larger-scale patterns including origin of new species and higher taxa. Continuous with micro, not categorically different.
  • Allopatric speciation: New species arise after a geographic barrier (mountain, ocean, glacier) splits a population; the isolated halves diverge until interbreeding is impossible.
  • Sympatric speciation: New species arise within the same geographic area through ecological specialization, polyploidy (chiefly in plants), or sexual selection.
  • Adaptive radiation: One ancestral lineage diversifies rapidly into many species exploiting different ecological niches; Galapagos finches, Hawaiian honeycreepers, and African cichlid fishes are textbook examples.
  • Heritability (h^2): Proportion of phenotypic variation due to genetic variation. Geospiza fortis beak depth has h^2 ~ 0.65-0.70, sufficient for selection to drive measurable change in offspring of survivors.
  • Irreversibility of extinction: Speciation is slow; an extinct species represents millions of years of evolutionary history not recoverable on human-relevant timescales.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • Charles Darwin (1809-1882): Co-discovered evolution by natural selection. HMS Beagle voyage (1831-1836) included Galapagos observations of finch beaks and tortoise shells. Co-presented with Wallace at Linnean Society on 1 July 1858; published On the Origin of Species in 1859.
  • Alfred Russel Wallace: Independently arrived at the theory of evolution by natural selection; his letter to Darwin prompted the joint Linnean Society presentation.
  • Peter and Rosemary Grant: From 1973 onward, banded and measured every medium ground finch on Daphne Major (~40 ha). Provided the most rigorous quantitative record of natural selection in a wild population.
  • 1976-77 ENSO drought (Galapagos): Eliminated small seeds; only hard caltrop seeds remained. Population fell 1,200 to 180. Survivors had larger beaks; offspring mean beak depth increased ~0.5 mm. Reversed in subsequent wet years.
  • Adaptive radiations: ~14 Darwin's finch species; 50+ Hawaiian honeycreepers from one finch ancestor; hundreds of African cichlid species in geologically recent lakes; Hawaiian Drosophila. The same recipe — colonist + isolation + diverse niches + time.

Connect to the Exam

Topic 2.4 most often appears as a Science Practice 1 (concept explanation) FRQ in which students must identify and verify each of Darwin's three conditions for natural selection in a given scenario (industrial melanism, antibiotic resistance, pesticide resistance, or finch beak shifts). Stimulus questions may present a graph of trait change across generations and ask students to interpret it as evolution, plasticity, or drift. Allopatric vs sympatric speciation often appears in island-biogeography or conservation FRQs (Science Practice 4 — model analysis).

Self-Check

  • A mouse population on a gray substrate shifts toward darker mean coat color over twelve generations. State each of Darwin's three conditions and identify the evidence for or against it in the scenario.
  • Why was the Grants' Daphne Major finch result conclusively natural selection rather than phenotypic plasticity?
  • Distinguish allopatric from sympatric speciation, and explain what feature of the Galapagos made it especially favorable for allopatric speciation.
  • Explain why the irreversibility of speciation is one of the strongest arguments for treating extinction as categorically different from other environmental degradation.
