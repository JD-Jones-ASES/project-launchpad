AP Environmental Science — Topic 4.6: Watersheds, Solar Radiation, and Earth's Seasons

Core Question

How do drainage geometry, the solar radiation budget, and Earth's axial tilt set the physical baseline against which every land-use, water-resource, and climate question is measured?

Key Concepts

  • Watershed (drainage basin): The area of land draining to a common outlet, bounded by topographic divides (ridgelines), independent of political boundaries. The Mississippi watershed (~3.2 million km², 31 states + 2 provinces) is the canonical example showing why upstream land use affects downstream water quality.
  • Surface runoff vs. groundwater: Two pathways for water leaving a watershed. Runoff moves quickly (hours–days) over the land surface to streams and the ocean; groundwater infiltrates and moves slowly (years–millennia) through aquifers, providing base flow between rains.
  • Aquifer: A permeable formation (sand, gravel, fractured rock, karst) that stores groundwater. Unconfined aquifers are open to direct recharge from above. Confined aquifers are bounded by an impermeable aquitard, recharge only at distant outcrops, and can produce artesian flow.
  • Aquifer recharge: Rate at which precipitation or surface water infiltrates to the saturated zone. Depends on precipitation, soil permeability, land cover, water-table depth. Shallow alluvial aquifers recharge near-instantaneously; deep arid-region aquifers (Ogallala) at only 1–3 cm/yr.
  • Ogallala / fossil water: The High Plains Aquifer was filled mostly during the wetter Pleistocene. Withdrawal rates of 30–60 cm/yr in heavy-irrigation zones exceed recharge by an order of magnitude. Water tables have dropped 30+ m since the 1950s. Canonical example of fossil-water depletion: thousands of years of accumulated water mined in decades.
  • Solar radiation budget: Of ~342 W/m² at the top of the atmosphere, ~30% is reflected to space, ~20% absorbed by the atmosphere, ~50% absorbed at the surface (driving evaporation, photosynthesis, and the thermal emissions trapped by greenhouse gases). Earth's effective albedo is ~0.30.
  • Albedo: Fraction of incident solar radiation reflected, 0 (absorber) to 1 (reflector). Fresh snow ~0.85, sea ice ~0.60, grassland ~0.20, forests ~0.10–0.15, ocean ~0.06–0.07, asphalt ~0.05. Land-cover changes shift albedo and force regional and global climate.
  • Ice-albedo feedback: Positive (amplifying) feedback. Warming melts ice (albedo ~0.60) → exposes ocean (albedo ~0.07) → ~9× more solar energy absorbed → more warming → more melting. Primary reason Arctic warming is 2–4× faster than global average.
  • Axial tilt and seasons: Earth's axis is tilted 23.5° relative to its orbital plane. As Earth orbits, hemispheres alternate between tilting toward and away from the Sun. Tilt — not orbital distance — drives seasons. Earth is actually closest to the Sun in early January (perihelion).
  • Why two effects matter for summer: When a hemisphere tilts toward the Sun, (1) solar rays strike at a higher angle → energy is concentrated per area, and (2) days are longer → more total daylight. Both raise temperatures. Both reverse in the other hemisphere, producing simultaneous opposite seasons.
  • Solstice: Sun at maximum/minimum declination. June solstice — perpendicular at Tropic of Cancer (23.5° N), longest NH day. December solstice — perpendicular at Tropic of Capricorn (23.5° S), shortest NH day. Above 66.5° latitude: midnight Sun in summer, polar night in winter.
  • Equinox: Sun crosses celestial equator. ~March 21 (vernal NH) and ~September 22 (autumnal NH). Day and night ~12 hours each at every latitude. Sun rises due east, sets due west.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • Watershed boundary delineation: On a topographic map, follow the ridgelines surrounding a stream valley. Every point inside contributes runoff to the same outlet.
  • Solar budget partition (mnemonic): 30 reflected / 20 atmosphere / 50 surface. Surface absorption is what drives the climate system; atmospheric absorption preheats the troposphere; reflection sets albedo.
  • Land-use → hydrology link: Forest → suburb conversion replaces high-infiltration surfaces with impervious ones, raising peak storm runoff (flashier floods) and lowering groundwater recharge (less base flow).
  • Latitudinal precipitation pattern: Rising air at 0° (ITCZ) and ~60° (Ferrel-Polar) → wet. Descending air at ~30° and the poles → dry. Connects directly to Topic 4.4 (three-cell model).

Connect to the Exam

  • Watershed FRQs: a typical setup names a drainage basin, asks you to identify upstream land uses contributing pollutants to a downstream point, and demands explicit mechanism (runoff, infiltration, base flow).
  • Albedo math: be ready to compute energy-budget changes for surface conversions (e.g., ice → water albedo shift × surface area × insolation = additional energy absorbed). Watch units (W/m² vs. W).
  • Axial-tilt vs. distance is a perennial misconception trap. Practice the explicit refutation: orbit is nearly circular, perihelion is in January, opposite hemispheres simultaneously have opposite seasons.
  • Ogallala depletion is the canonical case study for "non-renewable on human timescales" — links Topic 4.6 to Unit 5 (water-resource sustainability).
  • Ice-albedo feedback connects watersheds/seasons to Unit 9 climate change. Know the polarity (positive/amplifying) and magnitude argument (sea ice ~0.60 → water ~0.07).

Self-Check

  • Delineate the watershed for a stream on a topographic map. Why do political boundaries not match drainage geometry?
  • A boreal forest is clearcut and converts to grassland. Identify the albedo change, the radiative change in W/m² for a given insolation, and the local climate effect.
  • Explain to a peer why summer in the Northern Hemisphere occurs in June, even though Earth is closer to the Sun in January.
  • Why is the Ogallala called a "fossil" aquifer? Calculate roughly how many years of recharge would be lost in a single year of overdraft at 30 cm/yr withdrawal vs. 2 cm/yr recharge.
  • Connect Topic 4.4 (Hadley cells) to Topic 4.6: in what latitudinal belt does most of the world's freshwater recharge occur, and why?
