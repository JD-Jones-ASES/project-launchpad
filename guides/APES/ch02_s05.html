AP Environmental Science — Topic 2.5: Disturbance, Resilience, and Ecological Succession

Core Question

After a disturbance — fire, glacial retreat, eruption, agricultural abandonment — how does life reestablish, and what determines whether the recovered community resembles, exceeds, or falls short of the pre-disturbance state?

Key Concepts

  • Ecological succession: Directional, predictable change in community species composition and structure following disturbance.
  • Primary succession: Begins on substrate with no prior soil (bare rock, lava, fresh dunes). Requires pioneer species (lichens, mosses) to build initial soil through chemical weathering and organic accumulation. Slow — Glacier Bay sequence spans 200+ years.
  • Secondary succession: Begins where disturbance has removed the community but left soil intact (post-fire, abandoned agriculture). Far faster because seed bank and root systems persist; eastern-US fields can reach mixed forest in 20-50 years.
  • Pioneer species: First colonizers — lichens, mosses, stress-tolerant annuals. Their critical contribution is soil-building (organic-matter accumulation, chemical weathering, sometimes nitrogen fixation).
  • Facilitation: Early successional species modify the environment to enable later species (e.g., alders fixing nitrogen at Glacier Bay, enabling Sitka spruce establishment).
  • Climax community: Theoretical late-successional end state in dynamic equilibrium with regional climate; modern ecology recognizes most landscapes as disturbance-maintained mosaics rather than uniform climax states.
  • Resistance: Ability of a community to avoid displacement from its current state when disturbed.
  • Resilience: Ability of a community to recover original structure and function after displacement. Trade-off: high resistance often pairs with slow recovery; low resistance can pair with rapid recovery.
  • Determinants of resilience: Functional diversity, redundancy in critical roles, landscape connectivity, history of disturbances that maintain adaptive capacity. Stresses like pollution, fragmentation, and invasives erode resilience before major disturbance arrives.
  • Disturbance regime: Frequency, intensity, and spatial pattern of recurring disturbances that shape community composition.
  • Biological legacies: Surviving structures (logs, gopher-disturbed protected soil, root systems) that provide habitat and propagules during recovery — explain why succession is not a simple replay of pre-disturbance conditions.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • Henry Cowles (1899): Lake Michigan sand-dune study established the concept of ecological succession.
  • Frederic Clements (1916): Proposed monoclimax theory — all succession in a climate zone converges to the same end state. Modern ecology has revised this view.
  • Henry Gleason: Individualistic view — species respond independently to gradients, and observed communities reflect chance arrival, survival, and growth. Modern ecology has largely vindicated this view.
  • Aldo Leopold (1949, A Sand County Almanac): Land ethic — expand community to include soils, waters, plants, and animals. Foundational to modern conservation biology because it grounds biodiversity protection in moral obligation.
  • Mount St. Helens (1980): Largest terrestrial landslide in recorded history; primary succession on 600 km^2 of bare tephra. Sequence: chemoautotrophic microbes - lupine (nitrogen fixer) - fireweed - alder/willow - conifers. Salmon returned to the Toutle River within ~20 years.
  • Glacier Bay, Alaska: Classic primary-succession laboratory using glacial recession as a chronosequence — bare gravel near ice; alder thickets; Sitka spruce; western hemlock as the system ages.

Connect to the Exam

Topic 2.5 commonly appears as a Science Practice 1 (concept explanation) FRQ asking students to predict the successional stages following a specific disturbance, identify pioneer species, and explain what each stage contributes to the substrate. Stimulus questions often present a chronosequence or recovery dataset and ask students to interpret it (Science Practice 5 — data analysis). The lab-investigation segment supports Science Practice 6 (mathematical routines) — Simpson's D calculations and quadrat-sampling design. Resilience-vs-resistance contrasts also appear as conservation-management FRQs.

Self-Check

  • A volcanic island emerges from the ocean as bare rock. List the successional stages over the next 1,000 years, naming the pioneer species type at each stage and the substrate contribution it makes.
  • Distinguish resistance from resilience using a specific ecosystem example for each, and identify one management action that increases resilience but decreases resistance (or vice versa).
  • Why is succession on Mount St. Helens classified as primary in much of the blast zone but might be secondary in surrounding burned-but-not-stripped areas?
  • Two metrics — Simpson's D and species richness — are calculated for an old field and a managed lawn. Why might the two metrics give different relative rankings, and which two lawn-management practices most directly reduce D?
