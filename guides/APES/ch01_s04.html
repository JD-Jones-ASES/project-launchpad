AP Environmental Science — Topic 1.4: Biogeochemical Cycles II: Nitrogen and Phosphorus

Core Question

Why does an atmosphere that is 78% nitrogen still leave most ecosystems nitrogen-limited? Why does phosphorus, locked in rock, frequently determine whether a freshwater lake becomes a green soup of algae? Section 1.4 maps the two nutrient cycles whose disruption produces the most common environmental water-quality problems in the modern world: nitrate-contaminated drinking water, hypoxic coastal dead zones, and freshwater eutrophication. The section is also the first place students confront a non-renewable nutrient (phosphorus) — a long-term constraint with no industrial workaround.

Key Concepts

  • Nitrogen cycle reservoirs: Atmosphere (N2, 78% of air), organisms, soil (NH4+, NO2-, NO3-), water.
  • Five microbial transformations: Fixation, nitrification, assimilation, ammonification, denitrification.
  • Nitrogen fixation: N2 → NH4+/NH3. Performed by Azotobacter, cyanobacteria, Rhizobium (root-nodule mutualist), plus lightning. Industrially: Haber-Bosch.
  • Nitrification: NH4+ → NO2- (Nitrosomonas) → NO3- (Nitrobacter). Two-step oxidation.
  • Assimilation: Inorganic N (NH4+ or NO3-) taken up and built into organic molecules (proteins, nucleic acids, chlorophyll).
  • Ammonification: Decomposition returns organic N to NH4+. Closes the inorganic-organic loop.
  • Denitrification: NO3- → N2 (and N2O) under anaerobic conditions. The only path back to the atmospheric reservoir.
  • Phosphorus cycle: No significant atmospheric form. Lithosphere (apatite) → weathering → soil/water → ocean sediment → tectonic uplift (millions of years).
  • Phosphate ion (PO4^3-): The plant-available form. Built into ATP, DNA, RNA, phospholipids.
  • Limiting nutrient: The nutrient whose availability most constrains primary production. N typically limits marine systems; P typically limits freshwater.
  • Eutrophication: Nutrient enrichment driving algal blooms; decay drives bacterial respiration that depletes oxygen → hypoxic dead zone.
  • Cultural eutrophication: Anthropogenically accelerated eutrophication (fertilizer runoff, sewage).

Key Figures / Cycles / Mechanisms

  • Five-step nitrogen cycle: The classic AP diagram with fixation (N2 → NH4+), nitrification (NH4+ → NO2- → NO3-), assimilation (inorganic → organic), ammonification (organic → NH4+), and denitrification (NO3- → N2). Each step has a named microbial actor.
  • Haber-Bosch revolution: Pre-industrial natural fixation 100-140 Tg N/yr. Industrial fixation now ~120 Tg N/yr — roughly doubling reactive nitrogen entering the biosphere. The most profound human alteration of any biogeochemical cycle.
  • Reactive nitrogen cascade: Excess fixed N causes nitrate leaching (groundwater contamination), forest soil acidification (nitrogen deposition), N2O emissions (greenhouse gas and ozone-depleting), and coastal hypoxia.
  • Phosphorus weathering and burial: Rock → soil → water → marine sediment. No atmospheric reset; cycle dominated by slow lithospheric processes.
  • Eutrophication-to-dead-zone mechanism: Fertilizer N + P → algal bloom → bloom death → bacterial decomposition consumes O2 → hypoxia. Gulf of Mexico dead zone is the iconic example.
  • N vs. P limitation: Marine systems N-limited; freshwater systems P-limited. Management implications: cut N loading for coastal hypoxia, cut P loading for lake eutrophication.
  • Phosphorus finiteness: Mined as phosphate rock (Morocco, China, Florida); reserves measured in decades-to-centuries; no industrial substitute. Long-term agricultural sustainability concern.

Connect to the Exam

The nitrogen cycle is one of the most heavily-tested topics on the AP exam. Expect a free-response prompt that asks you to trace a nitrogen atom from N2 through a legume-rabbit-decomposer chain back to N2 — naming the microbial process at each step (Practice 1, Concept Explanation). Eutrophication is the gateway to Unit 8 (water pollution), so quiz-style questions that link fertilizer runoff → algal bloom → hypoxia recur. The N-vs-P limitation distinction is a frequent multiple-choice trap; remember the marine = N, freshwater = P pairing. Phosphorus's finite supply also appears in sustainability questions (Practice 7).

Self-Check

  • Trace a nitrogen atom from atmospheric N2 through a legume plant, a rabbit that eats the plant, and back to the atmosphere. Name the microbial process at each key transformation.
  • Explain why phosphorus is the limiting nutrient in most freshwater lakes while nitrogen is the limiting nutrient in most marine ecosystems. What does this imply for controlling eutrophication in each type of water body?
  • Describe the four-step mechanism by which agricultural fertilizer runoff produces a hypoxic dead zone. Identify the dominant cause of mortality in the affected aquatic animals.
  • Explain why phosphorus is described as a 'genuinely finite' nutrient. Compare it with nitrogen, whose atmospheric pool is effectively inexhaustible.
