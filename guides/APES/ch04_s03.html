AP Environmental Science — Topic 4.3: Earth's Atmosphere: Composition, Structure, and Function

Core Question

How does a thin shell of gas — only about 1% of Earth's radius and structured into four distinct layers — make the planet habitable? Understanding the atmosphere's composition (the trace-gas role of CO₂ and CH₄), its layered structure (defined by temperature gradients, not gas mixing), and its functions (greenhouse warming, UV shielding, pressure and temperature buffering) is the prerequisite to engaging with Unit 7 air pollution and Unit 9 climate change.

Key Concepts

  • Atmospheric composition: Dry air at sea level is 78.09% N₂, 20.95% O₂, 0.93% Ar (~99.9% combined), with CO₂ at 0.042% (420 ppm) and water vapor highly variable (0-4%). Trace gases include CH₄, N₂O, O₃, CFCs.
  • Troposphere (0-12 km): 80% of atmospheric mass, virtually all water vapor and weather. Temperature decreases at ~6.5°C/km. Deeper at equator (16 km) than poles (8 km).
  • Stratosphere (12-50 km): Temperature inversion driven by ozone absorption of UV. Highly stable against vertical mixing, so volcanic aerosols and CFCs persist for years.
  • Mesosphere (50-80 km): Temperature decreases with altitude, reaching coldest atmospheric temperatures (-90°C) at the mesopause; meteors burn up here.
  • Thermosphere (>80 km): Temperature rises to over 2,000°C due to molecular UV/X-ray absorption; aurora and ISS orbit here.
  • Greenhouse effect: Atmosphere transparent to incoming visible solar radiation but absorbs outgoing infrared via greenhouse gases — produces 33°C of natural warming, the difference between a frozen Earth and a habitable one.
  • Greenhouse gases: Water vapor (most abundant; feedback gas), CO₂ (key long-lived driver), CH₄ (~80× CO₂'s 20-yr warming potential per molecule), N₂O, CFCs.
  • Ozone layer: Stratospheric O₃ between 15-35 km absorbs >97% of incoming UV-B; only ~3 mm thick if compressed to sea-level pressure but irreplaceable as a UV shield.
  • Stratospheric vs. tropospheric ozone: Same molecule, opposite human-welfare effects — stratospheric protects, tropospheric is a smog pollutant and respiratory irritant.
  • Atmospheric functions: UV shielding (ozone), greenhouse warming, pressure regulation (allowing liquid water and gas exchange), temperature moderation (heat capacity buffers diurnal extremes).

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • The Mauna Loa CO₂ record (Charles David Keeling, 1958-present): The "Keeling Curve" tracks atmospheric CO₂ rising from ~315 ppm in 1958 to over 420 ppm today, with annual sawteeth from Northern Hemisphere photosynthesis. Foundational empirical evidence of anthropogenic atmospheric change.
  • The Antarctic ozone hole discovery (Joe Farman, 1985; Susan Solomon's heterogeneous-chemistry mechanism): Annual stratospheric ozone depletion over Antarctica catalyzed by CFC-derived chlorine on polar stratospheric cloud surfaces; led to the Montreal Protocol.
  • 800,000-year ice core record (Vostok, EPICA, Dome C): CO₂ has fluctuated between roughly 180 ppm (glacial) and 280 ppm (interglacial) for at least 800,000 years; current ~420 ppm is unprecedented in this record.
  • Mount Pinatubo eruption (1991): Injected ~17 Mt of SO₂ into the stratosphere, forming sulfate aerosols that cooled global average temperature by about 0.5°C for 1-2 years; demonstrated the long residence time of stratospheric aerosols.

Connect to the Exam

AP Environmental Science routinely tests the structure-temperature-function chain. Free-response items often (a) provide a temperature-altitude profile and ask students to identify layers and explain inversions, (b) ask why CO₂ at 0.042% drives climate change while N₂ at 78% does not (infrared absorption mechanism), (c) require simple ppm-percentage calculations on greenhouse gas changes, or (d) distinguish between stratospheric and tropospheric ozone in the context of public health policy. Be ready to explain the natural greenhouse effect quantitatively (33°C warming, the difference between -18°C and +15°C) and connect it to the anthropogenic enhancement explored in Unit 9.

Self-Check

  • Order the four atmospheric layers from lowest to highest, and state whether temperature increases or decreases with altitude in each. Explain the mechanism for the stratospheric inversion.
  • Distinguish stratospheric and tropospheric ozone in terms of origin, altitude, and effect on human health. Why is "ozone depletion" framed as harmful while "tropospheric ozone reduction" is framed as beneficial?
  • Explain why a 50% increase in CO₂ (from 280 to 420 ppm) — still less than 0.05% of the atmosphere — has a disproportionate effect on Earth's energy budget.
  • Why is water vapor described as a feedback gas rather than a primary driver of anthropogenic climate change?
