AP Environmental Science — Topic 3.2: Carrying Capacity, Logistic and Exponential Growth

Core Question

How do populations grow when resources are abundant, and what happens when they run up against the limits of their environment? This section gives you the two foundational mathematical models — exponential and logistic growth — and shows why exceeding carrying capacity (overshoot) can permanently degrade the resource base that sustained the population.

Key Concepts

  • Exponential growth: dN/dt = rN. Per-capita rate of increase r stays constant; the population accelerates continuously, tracing a J-shaped curve. Holds briefly for invasive species, recovering populations, or new colonizers.
  • Logistic growth: dN/dt = rN((K-N)/K). Per-capita rate decelerates as N approaches carrying capacity K. The (K-N)/K "braking factor" approaches 1 when N is small (near-exponential growth) and approaches 0 as N approaches K. Produces S-shaped (sigmoid) curve. Growth is fastest at N = K/2.
  • Carrying capacity (K): Maximum population an environment can sustain indefinitely without resource degradation. Dynamic, not fixed — shifts with drought, disease, predation, climate, human modification.
  • Overshoot: Population temporarily exceeds K, depleting resources faster than they regenerate. May reduce K below its previous level, producing dieback (crash) and boom-and-bust oscillation.
  • Density-dependent limiting factors: Strength rises with density — competition for food/space, disease transmission, intraspecific aggression, waste accumulation. The biological basis of the logistic braking term.
  • Density-independent limiting factors: Affect the same fraction of population regardless of density — hurricanes, drought, frost, fire, volcanic eruption.
  • Combined regulation: Most populations are regulated by both. Density-independent climate variability sets the envelope; density-dependent factors fine-tune within it.
  • Compounding: Even small differences in r produce vastly different long-run population sizes because the absolute increment scales with current N.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • Thomas Malthus (1766–1834): His 1798 Essay on the Principle of Population argued population grows geometrically while food supply grows arithmetically — leaving "misery and vice" (famine, disease, war) as the ultimate checks. Influenced Darwin (1838) and remains the conceptual foundation of population ecology and evolutionary biology.
  • Saint Matthew Island reindeer (1944–1969): 29 reindeer released onto a predator-free Alaskan island grew exponentially to ~6,000 by 1963, then crashed to 42 by 1969 after overgrazing slow-regenerating lichen — the textbook overshoot case.
  • Atlantic cod fishery collapse (1990s): Decades of harvest exceeding replacement rates pushed cod into a still-incomplete recovery — overshoot dynamics in a managed fishery.
  • Eutrophic algal blooms: Nutrient overshoot and oxygen depletion in lakes (Unit 8) — overshoot at the microbial scale.

Connect to the Exam

This topic supports Science Practice 5 (mathematical routines) and Science Practice 1 (ecological reasoning). Expect FRQ-style problems requiring you to plug values into the logistic equation and compute dN/dt, identify the inflection point, or explain why overshoot can permanently reduce K. Common stimulus types include population-size-vs-time graphs requiring identification of exponential, logistic, or overshoot dynamics; data tables of birth/death rates across density gradients; and case-study scenarios (Saint Matthew Island, Atlantic cod, algal blooms) where you must classify limiting factors and predict trajectories.

Self-Check

  • Calculate dN/dt for a population of N = 200, r = 0.4 yr⁻¹, K = 1,000. Show your work. (Answer key: dN/dt = 64.)
  • A barnacle population grows from 50 to 5,000 in three years on a freshly cleared rock face, then levels off at about 6,000. Sketch the trajectory and label the regions of exponential and logistic dynamics.
  • Distinguish density-dependent and density-independent regulation, give one example of each, and explain the mechanism.
  • Why did the carrying capacity of Saint Matthew Island never recover to its 1944 level? Explain in terms of overshoot dynamics and resource regeneration time.
