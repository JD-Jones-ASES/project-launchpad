AP Environmental Science — Topic 2.3: Ecological Tolerance and the Niche Concept

Core Question

What determines where a species can actually live, and how do abiotic tolerances and biotic interactions together shape the realized distribution of a species across landscapes?

Key Concepts

  • Range of tolerance: The complete span of values for a single abiotic variable (temperature, pH, salinity, etc.) within which a species can survive. Includes optimal range, two zones of physiological stress, and lethal limits.
  • Optimal range: Central portion of the tolerance curve where growth, reproduction, and survival are maximized; performance falls off symmetrically or asymmetrically toward the limits.
  • Zone of physiological stress: Region where the organism survives but at reduced performance. Climate change shifts brook trout from optimum (12-17 degrees C) into stress (>21 degrees C).
  • Liebig's law of the minimum: A species' performance is limited by the single most scarce resource relative to its needs, not by the average of conditions. Nitrogen-poor soil limits tree growth even when other factors are ideal.
  • Ecological niche: The complete set of biotic and abiotic conditions a species can survive and reproduce in; Hutchinson (1957) formalized it as a multidimensional hypervolume.
  • Fundamental niche: The full niche space a species could theoretically occupy without biotic interactions — defined by physiological tolerances alone.
  • Realized niche: The narrower subset of the fundamental niche actually occupied because of competitors, predators, and parasites.
  • Competitive exclusion principle (Gause's law): Two species with identical resource requirements cannot coexist indefinitely; the superior competitor displaces the inferior one.
  • Resource partitioning: Slight differences in resource use that reduce competition enough for coexistence; documented in MacArthur's 1958 warbler study.
  • Character displacement: Evolutionary divergence between coexisting competitors that reduces resource overlap.
  • Generalists vs. specialists: Generalists (raccoon) use broad resource and condition ranges and are resilient to change; specialists (giant panda) depend on a narrow set and are vulnerable to perturbation.
  • Keystone predator effect: Pisaster ochraceus removal allows mussels to expand from realized niche toward fundamental niche, then competitively exclude other intertidal species.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • Justus von Liebig (1840): Proposed the law of the minimum in agricultural chemistry — performance limited by the most scarce resource.
  • George Hutchinson (1957): Formalized the niche as a multidimensional hypervolume; introduced the fundamental-realized niche distinction.
  • G. F. Gause (1930s): Russian biologist whose Paramecium experiments demonstrated competitive exclusion empirically.
  • Robert MacArthur (1958): Classic study of five warbler species in Maine boreal spruce forests showing resource partitioning of canopy zones, foraging times, and search behaviors.
  • Pisaster ochraceus (Robert Paine's keystone-predator work): Removal experiments showed how a single predator regulates intertidal community composition by suppressing the dominant competitor.

Connect to the Exam

Niche questions on the AP exam usually test Science Practice 1 (concept explanation) — students must trace mechanism from a specific abiotic or biotic change through to its niche consequences. A complete answer names the variable that changed, classifies whether the issue is tolerance-range or competitive-exclusion, and describes the population-size outcome. Stimulus questions often present an experimental removal (Chthamalus-Balanus, Pisaster, P. aurelia-caudatum) and ask students to explain the result in terms of fundamental vs. realized niches. Generalist-specialist contrasts also recur as conservation FRQs.

Self-Check

  • Distinguish between a species' fundamental niche and its realized niche, and give one abiotic and one biotic factor that could reduce the realized below the fundamental.
  • A trout population declines as a stream warms. Write a complete-credit AP answer connecting tolerance range to the population outcome.
  • Two warbler species coexist on the same canopy tree. Explain the mechanism that allows them to avoid competitive exclusion.
  • Why is the giant panda more vulnerable to extinction than the raccoon? Frame your answer using niche-breadth vocabulary.
