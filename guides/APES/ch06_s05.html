AP Environmental Science — Topic 6.5: Solar, Wind, Geothermal, and Hydrogen Fuel Cells

Core Question

Why have solar and wind become the fastest-growing energy sources in the global mix, and what physical and grid-integration constraints determine how far they — alongside geothermal and hydrogen — can ultimately scale?

Key Concepts

  • Photovoltaic (PV) effect: Photons absorbed by a semiconductor (usually silicon) knock electrons loose, creating an electric current. Commercial silicon panels achieve 20–23% efficiency; multi-junction lab cells exceed 40%. DC output is converted to AC by inverters before grid injection.
  • Solar PV alternatives: Cadmium telluride (CdTe) thin film offers lower cost at slightly lower efficiency; perovskite cells show rising lab efficiency but durability remains a challenge. Solar module costs fell over 90% between 2010 and 2024, driving the 2019–2024 doubling of solar+wind generation share.
  • Concentrated Solar Power (CSP): Mirrors focus sunlight to drive a steam turbine. Molten-salt thermal storage (6–18 hours) enables dispatchable generation after sunset — a key advantage over PV. Best in regions with very high direct normal irradiance.
  • Passive solar design: Building-scale strategy using south-facing windows, roof overhangs, and thermal mass to harvest winter sun and shed summer sun. Can cut heating demand by 50% or more without moving parts.
  • Wind power cubic law: Power is proportional to the cube of wind speed (P ∝ v³). Doubling wind speed produces 8× more power, explaining why offshore wind (1–2 m/s faster average) is so much more productive per unit installed.
  • Onshore vs. offshore wind: Onshore has capacity factor 30–40% and is the lowest-LCOE generation in many regions. Offshore reaches 40–55% but costs 2–3× more to install and maintain. The European North Sea hosts the world's most developed offshore industry.
  • Wind environmental concerns: Bird/bat mortality from rotor strikes (especially on migratory flyways), visual and noise impacts, habitat disruption during construction, and variable output requiring grid integration measures.
  • Geothermal energy: Taps Earth's internal heat (primordial + radioactive decay of U, Th, K). Hydrothermal systems extract natural steam or hot water; capacity factors ~75% make geothermal a true baseload renewable. Iceland produces ~100% of its electricity from geothermal; The Geysers (~725 MW) is the largest US facility.
  • Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS): Inject water into hot dry rock at 3–10 km depth where temperatures exceed 150°C. Could expand geothermal globally but raises induced-seismicity concerns similar to fracking. First utility-scale US EGS entered operation in Nevada in 2024.
  • Hydrogen as energy carrier: Hydrogen does not exist in usable quantities in nature; it must be produced by consuming other energy. Green hydrogen (electrolysis from renewables) has near-zero lifecycle CO2; grey hydrogen (steam methane reforming) emits substantial CO2. Most commercial hydrogen today is grey.
  • Hydrogen fuel cells: Electrochemical reaction 2H2 + O2 → 2H2O + electricity. ~60% efficiency in combined heat-and-power applications. Promising for heavy transport (trucks, ships, aircraft) where battery limits become prohibitive.
  • Capacity factor: Ratio of actual annual output to the theoretical maximum if a plant ran at 100% capacity for all 8,760 hours. Nuclear ~92%, geothermal ~75%, combined-cycle gas ~55%, offshore wind 45–55%, hydro ~40%, onshore wind ~35%, utility solar PV ~25%. Lower CF reflects resource variability, not technology quality.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • PV efficiency: 20–23% commercial silicon; >40% lab multi-junction.
  • Solar capacity factors: 20–25% temperate; 25–30% deserts.
  • Wind cubic law: P ∝ v³ — doubling speed gives 8× power.
  • Hydroelectric global share: ~1,400 GW capacity, ~15% of global electricity.
  • Iceland: ~100% of electricity from geothermal due to volcanic geology.
  • Capacity-factor calculation: Annual MWh = rated MW × 8,760 h × CF. (Worked example: 400 MW × 8,760 × 0.50 = 1.752 million MWh for offshore wind.)

Connect to the Exam

  • AP Environmental Science Unit 6 expects students to compare capacity factors across sources, calculate annual energy output from rated capacity and CF, and evaluate intermittency trade-offs.
  • FRQs commonly require explanation of green vs. grey hydrogen lifecycle differences, justification of why nuclear has the highest CF, or calculation of relative output between two technologies.
  • Watch stimuli on grid-integration solutions (storage, transmission, demand response) — these are the policy lever recurring across the curriculum.

Self-Check

  • Calculate: a 200 MW utility solar PV plant has CF 25%. What is its annual MWh output? (Answer: 200 × 8,760 × 0.25 ≈ 438,000 MWh.)
  • Why does wind power scale with v³ rather than v² or v?
  • A friend claims green and grey hydrogen are interchangeable since both produce only water at the fuel cell. What is wrong with this claim?
  • List three grid-integration measures that allow high penetrations of variable renewables and explain one drawback of each.
