AP Environmental Science — Topic 3.3: Human Demographics and Age Structure

Core Question

Why does a country's age structure today predict its population dynamics for the next half-century? This section teaches you to read population pyramids, understand population momentum, and connect age structure to economic, social, and environmental outcomes.

Key Concepts

  • Age structure: Distribution of individuals across age classes at a given moment, visualized as a population pyramid (youngest at base, males left, females right). Encodes past fertility and mortality and predicts future labor supply and dependency.
  • Three iconic pyramid shapes:
    • Broad-base triangle (Niger): rapid growth, high fertility, strong population momentum.
    • Urn shape with wide middle and narrow base (Japan): below-replacement fertility, aging population, labor shortage.
    • Near-rectangular (United States): fertility near replacement plus moderate immigration, slow growth or stability.
  • Population momentum: Continued population growth for 40–70 years after fertility reaches replacement level. Driven by large existing young cohorts moving through reproductive years.
  • Replacement-level fertility: Approximately 2.1 in high-income countries — the slight buffer above 2.0 compensates for female children who do not survive to reproductive age. Higher (2.3–2.5) where child mortality remains elevated.
  • Crude birth rate vs. TFR: CBR (births per 1,000) is sensitive to age structure; TFR (children per woman) controls for age structure and is the standard comparative metric.
  • Dependency ratio: Non-working-age (under 15 + over 64) divided by working-age (15–64). High youth dependency strains schools/families; high elderly dependency strains pensions/elder care.
  • Demographic dividend: Window when falling fertility produces a large working-age cohort relative to dependents. Realized only when institutions, education, and employment match the demographic opportunity.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • Niger as Stage 2 archetype: TFR ~6.5, broad-base pyramid, dramatic population momentum. Each younger cohort dramatically larger than the one above; the children of today will fuel growth for decades regardless of near-term fertility decline.
  • Japan as Stage 5 archetype: TFR ~1.3, urn-shaped pyramid, deaths exceeding births since 2007. Population peaked at ~128 million in 2010, projected to fall below 100 million by the 2060s.
  • South Korea and Taiwan, 1965–1995: Captured the demographic dividend through declining fertility plus institutional investment, producing rapid economic growth as the working-age cohort expanded relative to dependents.
  • Sub-Saharan Africa's projected trajectory: Even rapid fertility decline still produces substantial growth through 2100 due to population momentum — a key driver of 21st-century environmental pressure.

Connect to the Exam

This topic supports Science Practice 4 (data analysis on graphical representations) and Science Practice 6 (mathematical routines like calculating dependency ratios). Common stimulus types include population pyramid figures requiring identification of stage, projected trajectory, and policy implications; tables comparing TFR, CBR, and dependency ratios across countries; and FRQ scenarios asking you to predict 30- or 50-year population dynamics from a given pyramid. Expect questions that distinguish CBR from TFR and that test understanding of population momentum.

Self-Check

  • Sketch and label the three iconic pyramid shapes (broad-base, near-rectangular, urn). Identify a representative country for each.
  • Explain in your own words why a country with TFR exactly 2.1 today can still grow for the next 40 years.
  • A country has CBR = 32 per 1,000 and CDR = 8 per 1,000. Calculate the natural rate of increase (without migration) as a percentage per year.
  • Why is the demographic dividend an opportunity rather than an automatic outcome? What institutional conditions are needed to realize it?
