AP Environmental Science — Topic 5.5: Pesticides, IPM, and Sustainable Agriculture

Core Question

When DDT was introduced after World War II it appeared to be a near-perfect solution to insect pests: cheap, effective, environmentally stable. Within a decade the first DDT-resistant mosquitoes had been documented. The same evolutionary dynamic that makes insects among the most adaptable organisms on Earth — rapid reproduction, large populations, genetic variation that natural selection acts upon — ensures that any chemical powerful enough to kill 99.9 percent of a pest population will face a population descended from the 0.1 percent survivors within a few generations. This section traces the pesticide treadmill, the IPM framework designed to slow it, and the broader landscape of sustainable agriculture (organic, agroforestry, permaculture, polyculture) that emerged in response.

Key Concepts

  • Pesticide treadmill: Each generation of pesticides selects for resistant pests, requiring more potent chemicals, which select for greater resistance.
  • Resistance evolution: Natural selection — the 0.1% survivors of a 99.9%-effective spray are disproportionately resistance carriers; their offspring inherit and proliferate resistance.
  • Integrated Pest Management (IPM): A decision framework combining ecological monitoring, biological control, cultural practices, and targeted chemical use; aims to keep pest populations below the economic threshold rather than eliminate them.
  • Economic threshold: The pest density at which the cost of crop damage exceeds the cost of control. The decision rule for whether to spray.
  • Calendar-based spraying: Fixed-schedule spraying ('every two weeks regardless'); the principal driver of resistance evolution because it consistently eliminates susceptibles.
  • Biological control: Use of living organisms (predators, parasitoids, pathogens) to suppress pests. Classical = deliberate introduction from origin; augmentative = mass-rearing and supplemental release.
  • Cultural controls: Modifications to farming practice that reduce pest pressure — crop rotation, intercropping, trap crops.
  • Bt refuge strategy: Non-Bt areas planted alongside Bt crops to maintain susceptible pest populations that dilute resistance alleles. Inadequate refuges allowed resistant rootworm populations to spread.
  • Persistent Organic Pollutant (POP): Synthetic organic chemical that resists biodegradation, persists for years to decades, is lipophilic, bioaccumulates and biomagnifies. DDT is the canonical example. Regulated under the Stockholm Convention (2001).
  • Organic agriculture: USDA-regulated production system prohibiting synthetic pesticides and nitrogen fertilizers; requires a three-year transition period before certification.
  • Agroforestry: Deliberate integration of trees with crops/livestock in one management unit. Examples: shade-grown coffee, cocoa under canopy, silvopasture.
  • Permaculture: Ecosystem-inspired agricultural design — diversity, redundancy, perennials, multi-layer production, closed-loop nutrient cycling.
  • Polyculture: Simultaneous cultivation of multiple crop species in the same field. Reduces monoculture vulnerability; harder to mechanize.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • DDT resistance in malaria mosquitoes: Anopheles gambiae developed resistance within fifteen years of DDT introduction across sub-Saharan Africa. Iconic case of the pesticide treadmill.
  • Pyrethroid resistance in bed bugs: Re-emerged globally in the 1990s after pyrethroid-based control was adopted as the front-line treatment.
  • Bt corn rootworm resistance: Diabrotica virgifera evolved resistance to Bt maize because large-scale planting did not maintain adequate non-Bt refuges. Even biological pesticides are not exempt from the treadmill.
  • IPM hierarchy of tactics: (1) scouting/economic thresholds; (2) biological control; (3) cultural controls; (4) physical/mechanical controls; (5) targeted chemical control as LAST RESORT, with narrow-spectrum, short-residue pesticides at lowest effective dose.
  • Organic certification (USDA): Three-year transition with no prohibited substances, then USDA Organic seal eligibility. Builds soil through cover crops (rye, vetch, hairy clover, oats), composted manure, and green manure.
  • Sustainable systems contrast: Agroforestry (trees + crops/livestock), permaculture (ecosystem design principles), polyculture (multiple species per field) — all reduce monoculture vulnerability and synthetic input dependence at the cost of higher labor requirements and lower per-hectare yields.

Connect to the Exam

Topic 5.5 generates two characteristic FRQs. First, the resistance-evolution mechanism: students should be able to explain why an insecticide that kills 99.9% of a pest population becomes ineffective within a few generations (natural selection on the surviving 0.1% with resistance alleles), identify the role of generation time and population size in accelerating selection, and propose ONE management change that would slow resistance development (rotate modes of action, add refuges, switch to IPM). Second, the IPM-vs-calendar contrast: students should articulate why calendar-based spraying is the principal driver of resistance, explain the economic threshold concept, and rank the IPM hierarchy (chemicals as last resort).

The Bt refuge case is exam-canonical: it illustrates that even biological pesticides face the treadmill, that resistance management requires DELIBERATE structural design (refuges to maintain susceptible populations), and that scaling without adequate refuges defeats the strategy. The POP/Stockholm Convention vocabulary is testable across Unit 8 as well; students should know POPs are lipophilic, biomagnify, persist for years to decades, and are regulated by an international treaty (not just FIFRA).

Self-Check

  • A farmer finds that the insecticide she has been using for five years is becoming less effective against the same pest, requiring higher doses for the same kill rate. Using the concept of natural selection, explain the biological mechanism responsible. Describe ONE change to her pest management approach that would slow further resistance development.
  • Explain why an IPM practitioner might choose NOT to spray a pesticide even when pests are present in a field. What information would she need to make this decision rationally?
  • Distinguish classical biological control from augmentative biological control. For each, give a textbook example and identify the practical limitations.
  • Compare organic agriculture, agroforestry, and polyculture as alternatives to industrial monoculture. Identify ONE shared principle and ONE specific feature of each that distinguishes it from the others.
