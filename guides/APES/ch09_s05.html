AP Environmental Science — Topic 9.5: Biodiversity Loss and the Sixth Extinction

Core Question

Is the contemporary biodiversity crisis a true mass extinction comparable to the five previous mass extinctions in Earth history, and what national and international frameworks have been designed to slow it? The answer requires understanding both the empirical scale of current species loss (rate, taxonomic breadth, and trajectory) and the political-legal architecture humans have constructed to address it. Unlike the five previous mass extinctions — caused by asteroid impact, massive volcanism, and ocean anoxia — the sixth has a single, identifiable cause: the cumulative impact of one species. That makes the crisis uniquely tractable in principle and uniquely contested in practice.

Key Concepts

  • Background extinction rate: ~1 E/MSY (one extinction per million species per year), inferred from the fossil record in the absence of unusual perturbations.
  • Sixth Extinction: The contemporary event, with observed rates of 100-10,000 E/MSY — at least 100 times background, often 1,000 times or higher. Popularized by Elizabeth Kolbert (2014).
  • Mass extinction (definition): Geologically rapid loss of more than 75% of species. Five recognized in fossil record: Ordovician-Silurian, Late Devonian, Permian-Triassic (largest at ~96%), Triassic-Jurassic, Cretaceous-Paleogene.
  • HIPPO drivers (E.O. Wilson): Habitat destruction (largest), Invasive species, Pollution, Population pressure, Overharvesting. Climate change increasingly recognized as a sixth driver amplifying all five.
  • Habitat destruction and fragmentation: ~75% of land surface and 66% of ocean significantly altered by human activity (IPBES 2019). Even without complete destruction, fragmentation isolates populations and creates edge effects favoring generalists.
  • Keystone species: Species whose ecological role is disproportionately large relative to abundance. Removal triggers cascading restructuring of entire communities. Examples: wolves, sea otters, African elephants.
  • Trophic cascade: Multi-level community change initiated by predator-prey interactions. Yellowstone wolf reintroduction (1995) is the canonical case.
  • Pollinator dependence: ~35% of global food crops and ~80% of flowering plant species depend on insect pollinators — making pollinator decline a foundational threat to food production.
  • Endangered Species Act (1973): US federal statute. Protects species AND ecosystems on which they depend. Section 9 prohibits 'take' (extends to private landowners). Section 7 requires federal agency consultation. Listing must be based on best science alone.
  • CITES (1973): International treaty regulating wildlife trade through three-tier appendix system. 184 parties; ~38,000 species listed across appendices. Notable: 1989 ivory trade ban.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • Famous modern extinctions: Passenger pigeon (Martha, 1914); Great auk (1844); Thylacine/Tasmanian tiger (1936); Western black rhinoceros (declared extinct by IUCN 2011); Vaquita porpoise (~6-10 individuals as of 2024 — almost certainly the next large-animal extinction).
  • Yellowstone wolf reintroduction (1995): Wolf predation altered elk grazing behavior → streamside vegetation recovered → bank erosion reduced → beaver habitat restored → stream hydrology changed. Demonstrates how a keystone predator's behavioral effects ripple through ecosystem structure.
  • Elizabeth Kolbert (Pulitzer 2014): The Sixth Extinction; popularized the framing of contemporary biodiversity loss as a mass-extinction event.
  • E.O. Wilson: Coined HIPPO acronym; foundational figure in conservation biology and biodiversity policy.
  • Living Planet Report (WWF) / IPBES Global Assessment (2019): Documents ~60% decline in monitored vertebrate populations since 1970; population-level erosion is functionally significant even where species are not yet extinct.

Connect to the Exam

The College Board APES exam tests biodiversity loss through stimulus-based multiple choice and FRQ items. Science Practice 7 (Environmental Solutions) is the dominant lens for this topic: students must evaluate the strengths and limitations of conservation policies (ESA, CITES, Convention on Biological Diversity, Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework). Science Practice 5 (Data Analysis) appears in extinction-rate comparisons (E/MSY background vs. observed) and in IPBES-style population-trend graphs. The HIPPO framework is the standard mnemonic the College Board uses to anchor causal questions.

Sample FRQ prompt: A regional planning agency proposes converting 40% of a forested watershed to suburban housing development. (a) Identify TWO HIPPO drivers of biodiversity loss that this conversion will activate, and explain the mechanism by which each operates. (b) Identify ONE keystone species likely to be present in the watershed, and explain the cascading effects of its decline if habitat conversion proceeds. (c) Explain how Section 7 consultation under the Endangered Species Act could legally constrain the federal permitting of this development. (d) Propose ONE habitat-corridor or fragmentation-mitigation design that could reduce the biodiversity impact of the proposed development.

Self-Check

  1. Why does Wilson rank habitat destruction first in HIPPO? Cite the IPBES (2019) finding that justifies this ranking and explain the mechanism by which fragmentation alone (without total destruction) reduces biodiversity.
  2. Compare the ESA and CITES on three dimensions: (a) which is legally binding on private landowners and which operates through trade permits; (b) how listing decisions are made; (c) one strength and one limitation of each. In a sentence, identify which would be more relevant to protecting a North American freshwater mussel and which to protecting an African elephant.
  3. Apply the keystone-species concept to a scenario in which sea otters disappear from Pacific coastal kelp forests. Trace the cascade through at least three trophic levels, ending with the effect on kelp biomass.
  4. The vaquita porpoise has approximately 6-10 individuals remaining as of 2024. Identify the immediate driver of its decline (a HIPPO category), explain why CITES Appendix I listing has been insufficient to prevent further loss, and propose ONE additional intervention that could change the trajectory.
