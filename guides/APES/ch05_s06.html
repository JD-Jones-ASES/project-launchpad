AP Environmental Science — Topic 5.6: CAFOs, Overfishing, and Aquaculture

Core Question

Human demand for animal protein has reshaped two of the planet's most productive ecosystems. On land, the intensive confinement of livestock in CAFOs has separated meat, egg, and dairy production from the land base that historically absorbed their waste — creating nutrient concentrations, pathogen reservoirs, and GHG sources increasingly difficult to manage. In the ocean, industrial fishing technology has allowed humanity to harvest marine populations faster than they can reproduce, collapsing stocks like Atlantic cod. The aquaculture industry that has grown to fill the gap brings its own complications. Each system is a version of Hardin's tragedy: individually rational actors extracting maximum short-term value from shared biological resources.

Key Concepts

  • CAFO (Concentrated Animal Feeding Operation): EPA-defined facility confining animals more than 45 days/year above size thresholds (1,000+ cattle, 2,500+ pigs, 125,000+ broilers, 82,000+ layers). ~80,000 in the US.
  • Manure lagoon: Open-air storage of CAFO waste; subject to breach during floods (Hurricane Floyd 1999), routine ammonia volatilization, and land-application contamination.
  • Antibiotic resistance: ~70% of medically important antibiotics in the US go to food-animal production as growth promoters; selective pressure breeds resistant strains that spread to humans.
  • Methane and N2O emissions: Cattle enteric fermentation (28x CO2) plus manure decomposition (N2O ~300x CO2). Global livestock = ~14.5% of anthropogenic GHGs.
  • Feed footprint: Indirect land use for animal feed; 100,000 cattle = ~50,000 ha cropland. Drives expansion in Brazil, Argentina, Southeast Asia.
  • Maximum Sustainable Yield (MSY): Largest average catch sustainable indefinitely; biologically near half unexploited carrying capacity. Quotas often exceed MSY due to political pressure.
  • Fishing down the food web: Daniel Pauly 1998 — mean trophic level of global catch declining since 1950s. Apex predators collapse first; effort shifts to lower trophic levels.
  • Atlantic cod collapse (1992): Grand Banks stock fell to ~1% of historical biomass; has not recovered in 30+ years. Canonical fisheries management failure.
  • Bottom trawling: Heavy nets and rollers dragged across seafloor; analogous to clearcutting; destroys coral, sponge beds, rock outcrops; recovery decades to centuries.
  • Bycatch: Incidental capture of non-target species. Shrimp trawl: 3-10 kg non-target per kg shrimp. Global: 7-25 million tonnes/year.
  • Open-net salmon aquaculture: Sea cages in coastal fjords; sea lice spread to wild smolts; seafloor smothering; high antibiotic use.
  • Shrimp aquaculture: Pond construction has destroyed ~30% of world mangrove area since 1980.
  • Recirculating Aquaculture Systems (RAS) and IMTA: Closed-water systems and integrated multi-trophic combinations (salmon + mussels + seaweed) — more sustainable models with higher capital cost.

Key Figures / Mechanisms

  • CAFO scale: A 10,000-animal hog CAFO produces more raw waste than a city of 40,000 people, but without secondary sewage treatment. Lagoon storage, ammonia volatilization, and land application are the three pathways for waste to reach the environment.
  • Fishing down the food web cascade: Cod, halibut, bluefin tuna, swordfish, sharks (apex predators) collapse first because of long generation times → fleet shifts to smaller fish (anchovies, planktivores) → mean trophic level of catch drops.
  • Atlantic cod collapse mechanism: Five centuries of productive fishing → 1980s scientific warnings → quotas held high under political pressure → 1992 collapse to 1% of historical biomass → moratorium → no recovery in 30+ years. Demonstrates that ecosystem state shifts can be slow to reverse.
  • Aquaculture spectrum: Open-net salmon (high impact, low capital) ↔ shrimp ponds (mangrove destruction) ↔ RAS/IMTA (low effluent impact, higher capital and energy use). Filter-feeding bivalves (oysters, mussels) are the lowest-impact category.

Connect to the Exam

Topic 5.6 generates two FRQ patterns. First, applying Hardin's tragedy framework (Section 5.1) to a fishery or grazing commons: students should identify the rivalry/non-excludability properties, name the specific governance failure (e.g., quotas exceeding MSY due to political pressure), and propose a governance mechanism to align incentives (catch shares/individual transferable quotas, marine protected areas, science-independent quota-setting bodies, community co-management à la Ostrom). Second, comparing the externalities of different food production systems: CAFOs (manure lagoons, antibiotic resistance, GHGs, feed footprint) vs. wild fishery (overfishing, bycatch, habitat destruction by trawling) vs. aquaculture types (open-net salmon vs. RAS vs. mangrove-destroying shrimp).

The Atlantic cod collapse is canonical: students must know the magnitude (1% of historical biomass), the institutional failure (quotas above MSY due to political pressure), and the slow-recovery lesson (30+ years and counting). Quantitative items: feed conversion ratios (chicken ~1.8:1 feed:gain vs. beef higher); MSY calculations; mangrove loss percentage (30%); antibiotic share (70%).

Self-Check

  • Apply Hardin's "Tragedy of the Commons" framework to the 1992 Atlantic cod collapse. Identify the specific institutional failure that prevented sustainable management, and propose ONE governance mechanism that could have reduced the risk of collapse without a complete moratorium.
  • Compare the environmental externalities of open-net salmon aquaculture with those of a land-based recirculating aquaculture system (RAS) for the same species. Which system more fully internalizes its environmental costs, and why?
  • A 100,000-cattle feedlot requires ~50,000 hectares of cropland for feed. Calculate the area per animal and explain why the "feed footprint" is rarely included in land-use accounting for meat production. Discuss ONE downstream environmental cost of this indirect land use.
  • Pauly's "fishing down the food web" describes a sequential depletion of higher trophic levels. Explain why apex predators are typically the first to collapse, citing TWO biological characteristics that make them vulnerable.
