AP Human Geography — Topic 7.3: The Geography of the Modern Economy

Core Question

Why did the regions that industrialized first lose manufacturing fastest, and where do the new economic geographies of the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries concentrate?

Key Concepts

  • Deindustrialization: The decline of manufacturing employment as a share of total economic activity in a previously industrial region, driven by three overlapping forces: (1) technological change — automation and robotics permanently reduce labor content (a car body requiring 40 welders in 1970 required 4 in 2000); (2) global labor-cost arbitrage — as container shipping costs fell, labor-intensive assembly migrated from the US Midwest to the US South, then to Mexico, then to East Asia; (3) demand composition shifts — rising incomes in core countries shifted spending toward non-tradable services (health, education, finance), reducing the manufactured-goods share. Deindustrialization produces spatially concentrated effects: communities most dependent on a single industry suffer disproportionately even as national output continues to grow.

  • The American Rust Belt: The manufacturing corridor stretching from the Great Lakes through the Ohio Valley — Detroit (automobiles), Pittsburgh (steel), Cleveland (metals and chemicals), Gary (steel), Buffalo, Youngstown. Pittsburgh lost approximately 150,000 manufacturing jobs in the 1970s–80s; Detroit's population fell from 1.85 million in 1950 to under 700,000 by 2020. Parallel geographies emerged in the English Midlands (Birmingham, Coventry), the German Ruhr, and Scottish Clydeside. Britain's manufacturing employment fell from ~38% of the workforce in 1960 to under 9% by 2010.

  • Maquiladoras and the Sun Belt shift: Firms seeking lower labor costs and weaker union environments migrated first to the US South and Southwest (Sun Belt), then across the border into Mexico's maquiladora zones. Mexico's Border Industrialization Program (BIP, 1965) allowed US-owned factories to import components duty-free, assemble using Mexican labor, and re-export paying tariff only on value added in Mexico. NAFTA (1994) accelerated this: maquiladora employment grew from ~550,000 in 1993 to over 1.3 million by 2000. Cities like Tijuana, Ciudad Juárez, and Monterrey built explosive export-manufacturing sectors.

  • Offshoring vs. outsourcing: Offshoring means relocating a business function to another country (whether within the same company or by contracting a foreign firm). Outsourcing means contracting a function to an external firm rather than performing it in-house — and may be domestic or foreign. The combination of outsourced offshore manufacturing defines the global apparel and electronics supply chains. A US firm closing its in-house IT department and hiring a US firm has outsourced but not offshored; closing its factory and contracting to a Bangladeshi supplier has both outsourced and offshored.

  • Special Economic Zones (SEZs) and Export-Processing Zones (EPZs): EPZs are fenced, customs-controlled zones offering tax holidays, duty-free inputs, and streamlined regulation in exchange for exporting most output — primarily designed to attract labor-intensive assembly, existing in over 130 countries. SEZs are broader: they can permit domestic sales, offer financial sector liberalization, apply special labor regulations, and — in China's case — serve as testing grounds for market reforms the government is unwilling to apply nationally. China's SEZs were the most consequential in history.

  • Shenzhen (1980–2024): In 1979, Shenzhen was a fishing village of ~30,000 people. Designated China's first SEZ in 1980, it became the fastest large-scale urbanization in recorded history: ~12 million residents by 2024, GDP over $450 billion in 2023 (larger than many mid-sized European countries). The formula: Hong Kong capital and management expertise + cheap migrant labor from inland provinces + state-built infrastructure + a flexible regulatory environment. Foxconn operates its largest facility here assembling iPhones; Huawei, Tencent, and BYD were incubated here, moving Shenzhen up the value chain from assembly toward genuine R&D.

Models / Theories / Geographers

Manuel Castells & Peter Hall — Technopoles of the World (1994)

Castells (sociologist) and Hall (urban planner), both at UC Berkeley, coined the term "technopole" to describe the planned or spontaneous concentration of high-technology industries near major research universities.

Central claim: Late twentieth-century capitalism increasingly generates value through information processing and innovation rather than physical manufacturing. The spatial expression is the technopole — a high-tech industrial cluster whose competitive advantage rests on proximity to research universities, venture capital, and networks of highly skilled workers. Self-reinforcing knowledge spillovers constitute a new form of agglomeration economy that physical infrastructure or raw materials alone cannot create.

Key examples:

  • Silicon Valley: Stanford and UC Berkeley supply research and graduates; Sand Hill Road venture capital firms provide financing; engineer density generates idea spillovers so powerful that even extreme land and labor costs do not cause firms to disperse.
  • Boston / Route 128: MIT and Harvard anchor a biotech and software cluster; Kendall Square is the world's most productive biotech corridor by patent output per square mile.
  • Bangalore: IITs supply software engineering talent; English-language proficiency and time-zone advantage for US clients built Infosys and Wipro's global IT empires.
  • Tel Aviv / Haifa: Military R&D (IDF Unit 8200) as a de facto technology incubator; Technion anchors hardware and materials science.
  • Cambridge (UK): "Silicon Fen" cluster in biosciences, software, and semiconductors (ARM Holdings) around Cambridge University.

Evidence: Patent applications, venture capital investment, and high-tech employment are all highly geographically concentrated — the San Jose–Sunnyvale–Santa Clara metro receives more venture capital than most countries.

Critique: Technopoles exacerbate housing inequality and concentrate knowledge-economy rewards among the already-advantaged. Deindustrialized regions rarely become technopoles simply by building a science park; agglomeration cannot be manufactured top-down (many planned "Silicon Steppes" in Russia and science cities in Korea have failed).

Connect to the Exam

SR (Spatial Relationships): The Rust Belt–Sun Belt–Maquiladora triangle is the canonical spatial relationships case. The same causal forces — wage differentials, transport cost changes, union strength — produced three spatially distinct outcomes simultaneously: job loss in the industrial Midwest, job gain in the US South, export-manufacturing growth in northern Mexico. A strong AP response identifies the common cause producing geographically dispersed effects rather than treating each location separately.

CP (Concepts and Processes): Weber's labor-deviation logic (Topic 7.1) is the theoretical engine behind deindustrialization. Apply it explicitly: lower container costs + lower Mexican wages = critical isodapane crossed → firms relocate.

DA (Data Analysis): Employment data showing manufacturing share falling in core regions while rising in Mexico/East Asia illustrates spatial division of labor. Shenzhen's GDP growth curve is a data interpretation question waiting to happen.

SOA (Source Analysis): A photograph of a derelict factory (like Michigan Central Station) or a maquiladora assembly floor is a visual source asking you to identify the geographic processes at work — deindustrialization, labor-cost arbitrage, spatial division of labor.

Sample FRQ prompt: Explain how deindustrialization in the American Rust Belt and industrialization in Mexico's maquiladora zones were products of the same underlying geographic and economic forces, and identify one social consequence of deindustrialization in communities like Detroit or Pittsburgh.

Cross-Section Connections

  • Topic 7.1 (Weber / Agglomeration): Technopoles are the knowledge-economy evolution of Weber's agglomeration economies — knowledge spillovers replace shared physical infrastructure as the primary clustering force. Silicon Valley and Emilia-Romagna are both agglomeration stories, one built on innovation, one on craft manufacturing.
  • Topic 7.2 (Wallerstein / Rostow): Shenzhen's trajectory illustrates both frameworks simultaneously — Wallerstein would identify it as a semi-peripheral zone climbing toward core functions; Rostow would call it the world's most compressed take-off. The Rust Belt's decline illustrates Wallerstein's point that core status is not permanent.
  • Topic 7.4 (Globalization / Trade): NAFTA accelerated the maquiladora boom; the "China shock" research (Autor, Dorn, Hanson) is the empirical complement to the Rust Belt narrative — showing that geographically concentrated trade losses produce intense political backlash even when aggregate national gains are positive.
  • Unit 5 (Agriculture): Maquiladora zones drew heavily on internal Mexican migration from rural agricultural regions — the same rural-to-urban shift that fueled early British industrialization and contemporary Chinese manufacturing.
  • Unit 6 (Cities): Shenzhen is the most extreme case of rapid urbanization in the history of the world system; Rust Belt cities like Detroit and Pittsburgh illustrate shrinking-city dynamics (population decline, fiscal crisis, infrastructure abandonment).
