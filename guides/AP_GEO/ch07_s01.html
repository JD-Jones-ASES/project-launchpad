AP Human Geography — Topic 7.1: The Industrial Revolution and Location Theory

Core Question

Why did industrialization begin in Britain, how did it diffuse across the globe, and where will a profit-maximizing firm choose to build its factory?

Key Concepts

  • Britain's industrial hearth: The Industrial Revolution originated in Britain because of overlapping geological, institutional, and geographic advantages — shallow, accessible coalfields in the English Midlands, South Wales, Yorkshire, and Northumberland; proximity of coal and iron ore deposits (especially in the Black Country and Sheffield region); and canal networks linking interior production zones to port cities. Manchester's "Cottonopolis" growth — from 25,000 to 180,000 people between 1771 and 1831 — illustrates how steam power, moist air, rural labor migration, and break-of-bulk position on the Liverpool canal system combined to create the archetypal industrial city.

  • Break-of-bulk point: Any location where cargo must transfer between transport modes (ocean ship to river barge; rail to truck) concentrates warehousing, processing, and commercial activity. Rotterdam, Singapore, Hong Kong, and New Orleans all owe significant economic importance to this function. Manchester's canal link to Liverpool is the canonical early example; Singapore's position on the Strait of Malacca (through which one-quarter of global maritime trade passes) is the contemporary one.

  • Second Industrial Revolution and the assembly line: The 1870–1914 period added steel, electricity, and industrial chemistry. Ford's 1913 Highland Park assembly line reduced Model T assembly from 12.5 hours to 93 minutes by decomposing production into standardized sequential tasks — separating mental labor (design, engineering) from physical labor (assembly floor).

  • Diffusion pathways: Belgium industrialized first on the continent (Liège and Charleroi coalfields); Germany accelerated after 1871 unification, with the Ruhr Valley surpassing Britain in steel output by 1900. US industrialization drew on Appalachian coal and Mesabi Range iron ore; Carnegie's Homestead Works integrated both via the Erie Canal. Japan's Meiji-era industrialization was state-directed and compressive (three to four decades vs. Britain's century), with private zaibatsu conglomerates (Mitsui, Mitsubishi) taking over government model factories. The East Asian Tigers (South Korea, Taiwan, Hong Kong, Singapore) followed export-led strategies in the 1960s–80s, with South Korean chaebol (Samsung, Hyundai, LG) replicating aspects of the zaibatsu model.

  • Shipping container revolution (1956): Malcom McLean's standardized intermodal container reduced ocean freight rates by roughly 90 percent in real terms between the 1950s and 1970s. This collapsed transport costs globally and made the labor-deviation pull in Weber's model far stronger — it became cheaper to manufacture goods halfway around the world and ship them back than to produce domestically. Entire industries (electronics, apparel, shoes) relocated from the industrial core to lower-wage regions.

  • Agglomeration economies: Industries cluster for three reasons beyond raw-material pull: (1) localization economies — shared specialized labor pools, suppliers, and information spillovers within an industry cluster (Silicon Valley engineers, Emilia-Romagna leatherworkers); (2) urbanization economies — general benefits of large cities (transport, finance, legal services, cultural amenities); (3) Marshallian industrial districts — Alfred Marshall's observation of informal knowledge transfer "in the air" of specialized districts that no single firm could internalize alone.

Models / Theories / Geographers

Alfred Weber — Least Cost Theory (1909)

Weber asked where a rational profit-maximizing firm will locate its factory, structuring the answer around three factors:

  1. Transportation costs: The "location triangle" places the optimal factory within the triangle formed by two raw-material sources and one market, pulled toward whichever vertex has the greatest transport weight. Weight-losing (bulk-reducing) industries — steel smelting, copper refining — locate near raw materials because ore and coal weigh far more than finished product. Weight-gaining (bulk-increasing) industries — soft-drink bottling — locate near markets because water is added and the finished product is heavier than ingredients.

  2. Labor cost deviation: A firm will move away from the transport-minimum location if a lower-wage site reduces total costs enough to offset extra transport expense. Weber formalized this using isotims (lines of equal transport cost) and isodapanes (lines of equal total transport-cost increase). If a cheaper-labor site lies inside the critical isodapane, the firm relocates.

  3. Agglomeration economies: Multiple firms sharing infrastructure, specialized labor, and suppliers can reduce per-firm costs, forming industrial districts.

Evidence: Appalachian and Ruhr steel mills near coal and iron ore (weight-losing), bottling plants near every major city (weight-gaining), and the textile shift from New England → US South → Bangladesh (labor-deviation) all track Weber's logic precisely. Critique: The model assumes perfect information, a flat plain, and ignores government policy (tax incentives, EPZs), land rent, and consumer-proximity agglomeration.

Connect to the Exam

CP (Concepts and Processes): Weber's location triangle is a direct CP tool — predict factory location from input/output weight ratios before consulting a map. Always name the model, state weight-losing vs. weight-gaining logic, and apply it to the specific case.

SR (Spatial Relationships): The diffusion of industrialization from Britain → Belgium → Germany → US → Japan → East Asian Tigers illustrates hierarchical and contagious diffusion of a technology and economic system simultaneously.

DA (Data Analysis): When a prompt provides a table of industrial inputs and outputs by weight, use Weber's weight ratio to determine whether the plant should locate near materials or market.

SOA (Source Analysis): The Ford assembly line image illustrates agglomeration — all production stages concentrated in one facility near Detroit's steel and transport networks. Ask what spatial logic the source reveals.

SC (Scale Analysis): The container revolution looks like a logistics innovation at the firm scale, a trade-cost collapse at the national scale, and a wholesale reorganization of the global economic geography at the world scale.

Sample FRQ prompt: Explain why Pennsylvania and the German Ruhr Valley developed as major steel-producing centers using Weber's Least Cost Theory, and then explain how the shipping container revolution of the 1950s–1970s altered the relative importance of Weber's three location factors for labor-intensive manufacturing.
