AP Human Geography — Topic 7.2: Measuring Global Development — Models in Tension

Core Question

How do geographers measure development, and do poor countries lag behind rich ones on a shared road — or are they held back by the structure of the global economy itself?

Key Concepts

  • GDP vs. GNI vs. GNI per capita (PPP): Gross Domestic Product measures total market value of goods and services produced within a country's borders. Gross National Income adds income earned abroad by residents and subtracts income earned domestically by foreigners — a crucial distinction for remittance-dependent countries like the Philippines and Mexico. Dividing by population gives GNI per capita. Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) adjustment corrects for price-level differences across countries (a haircut costs $30 in New York, $3 in Dhaka; the real output is identical), roughly tripling India's per-capita income figure compared to the nominal exchange-rate figure.

  • Human Development Index (HDI): Introduced in 1990 by Mahbub ul Haq and Amartya Sen as an explicit challenge to the GDP-only paradigm. Combines three pillars using a geometric mean: (1) health — life expectancy at birth; (2) education — mean years of schooling (adults) and expected years of schooling (children); (3) standard of living — GNI per capita (PPP). The geometric mean prevents a very high score in one dimension from compensating for a very low score in another. Norway's HDI approaches 1; Niger's approaches 0.

  • Gender Inequality Index (GII): Measures gender-based disadvantage across reproductive health (maternal mortality, adolescent birth rate), empowerment (parliamentary seats, secondary education), and labor market participation. Introduced in 2010 to replace earlier measures because it explicitly frames gender inequality as a loss of human development capacity — countries forfeit HDI points they could have earned under equal opportunity.

  • Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI): Identifies the poor by simultaneous deprivations across ten indicators in health (nutrition, child mortality), education (years of schooling, attendance), and living standards (cooking fuel, sanitation, water, electricity, housing, assets). A household is "multidimensionally poor" if deprived in at least one-third of weighted indicators. Consistently reveals that income poverty alone misses substantial real deprivation.

  • Extreme poverty trends (1990–2024): Global extreme poverty (under $2.15/day in 2017 PPP) fell from ~38% of humanity in 1990 to ~9% in 2019. The reduction was geographically concentrated in East and South Asia; China alone accounted for over 800 million people escaping poverty between 1981 and 2015. Sub-Saharan Africa made percentage-point progress but saw the absolute number of extremely poor people rise as population growth outpaced gains. COVID-19 reversed gains for approximately 97 million people in 2020, with losses concentrated in South and South-East Asia and Sub-Saharan Africa.

Models / Theories / Geographers

Walt Rostow — Stages of Economic Growth (1960)

Rostow proposed that all societies move through five sequential stages, roughly replicating Western European and North American historical experience:

  1. Traditional Society: subsistence agriculture, limited technology, hierarchical social structure.
  2. Preconditions for Take-Off: entrepreneurial elite emerges; infrastructure investment begins; new scientific ideas penetrate agriculture.
  3. Take-Off: sustained investment and industrial output growth; a leading sector (textiles, railroads, steel) drives broad expansion; urban industrial regions emerge.
  4. Drive to Maturity: economy diversifies beyond the leading sector; modern technology spreads; workforce majority enters industrial and service sectors.
  5. Age of High Mass Consumption: leading sector shifts to consumer durables and services; welfare-state programs distribute growth broadly.

Context: Rostow explicitly subtitled his book "A Non-Communist Manifesto" and wrote it as Cold War policy — the U.S. government used the Stages to justify foreign aid. Evidence: South Korea's GDP per capita grew from ~$155 in 1960 to over $33,000 today; China's post-1978 growth is a plausible "world's largest take-off." Critique: Linear, teleological, and Eurocentric — treats Western industrial capitalism as universal destination; ignores colonialism's role in shaping (and distorting) preconditions; cannot explain why some countries have received prescribed capital and aid for generations without taking off.

Immanuel Wallerstein — World-Systems Theory (1974)

Wallerstein argued that the modern world economy is a single capitalist system that reproduces inequality through unequal exchange — poverty is a produced condition, not an original one.

Tripartite structure:

  • Core countries: advanced technology, capital-intensive industries, skilled labor, strong state institutions; command favorable terms of trade. (US, Germany, Japan, South Korea.)
  • Semi-periphery countries: exploit peripheral zones while being exploited by core zones; serve as political buffer preventing periphery unity. (Brazil, Mexico, China, India, Russia — the BRICS broadly.)
  • Periphery countries: supply raw materials and low-wage labor; weaker states, narrowly specialized economies, historically disadvantageous terms of trade. (Sub-Saharan Africa, Haiti, Central Asian republics.)

Unequal exchange: A Congolese miner extracts cobalt for $1/day; that cobalt ends up in a battery sold in Frankfurt for $80,000. Trade at world prices appears equal; embedded labor values are not. Individual countries can move between zones (South Korea: semi-periphery → core; Mexico: periphery → semi-periphery), but the system's overall inequality is self-reproducing — someone must always occupy the periphery.

Evidence: Persistent terms-of-trade deterioration for primary-commodity exporters (the Prebisch-Singer hypothesis); geographic concentration of R&D, patents, and high-value manufacturing in a handful of countries while assembly and extraction remain in the global South. Critique: Structural determinism — if the system always reproduces itself, how do the East Asian Tigers rise? Also underweights domestic institutions, culture, and policy choices.

Connect to the Exam

DA (Data Analysis): When a prompt provides a table ranking countries by HDI, GNI per capita, or GII, ask: (1) Are there outliers — countries ranking much higher on HDI than GNI (Cuba, Sri Lanka — strong public investment in health and education)? (2) Which development dimension is lagging — high life expectancy but low education scores tells a different policy story than the reverse.

CP (Concepts and Processes): Rostow vs. Wallerstein is the central conceptual debate. Know that Rostow is a modernization/stage model (optimistic, linear, policy-prescriptive) and Wallerstein is a structural/dependency model (the system produces inequality deliberately).

SR (Spatial Relationships): The geographic concentration of poverty reduction — East and South Asia gaining while Sub-Saharan Africa's absolute numbers rose — illustrates how global-scale processes produce spatially uneven outcomes at the regional scale.

SOA (Source Analysis): A table of HDI vs. GNI rankings is a classic AP source. Identify the dimension where a country under- or over-performs relative to income, then connect to policy or historical context.

Sample FRQ prompt: Using Wallerstein's World-Systems Theory, explain why some countries have remained primary-commodity exporters despite decades of development assistance, and identify one piece of evidence from global trade patterns that supports this argument.

Cross-Section Connections

  • Topic 7.1 (Weber / Industrial Revolution): Rostow's take-off stage maps onto the industrialization hearths described in Section 7.1 — Britain's take-off (1783–1802), US take-off (1843–1860). Wallerstein's core-periphery structure explains why Britain's industrialization required peripheral zones supplying raw cotton (American South, India).
  • Topic 7.3 (Modern Economic Landscape): The East Asian Tigers' rise from periphery/semi-periphery toward core is the key empirical challenge to Wallerstein's determinism — and the key evidence Rostow's supporters cite. Shenzhen is the most compressed take-off example.
  • Topic 7.4 (Globalization and Trade): IMF structural-adjustment conditionality in the 1980s–90s cut public health and education spending in many African and Latin American countries — directly undermining the human-capital investment that Rostow's model requires for take-off, and illustrating Wallerstein's argument that the system reproduces peripheral status.
  • Unit 2 (Population): GII and MPI connect to demographic transition — countries with high gender inequality and multidimensional poverty tend to have higher fertility rates, connecting development theory to population dynamics.
  • Unit 6 (Cities): HDI outliers like Cuba and Sri Lanka — high health/education scores relative to income — illustrate how urbanization and public service provision can decouple human welfare from raw economic output.
