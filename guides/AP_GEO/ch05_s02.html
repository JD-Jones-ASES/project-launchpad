AP Human Geography — Topic 5.2: Spatial Patterns of Global Agriculture

Core Question

What forces — physical geography, transport costs, cultural history, and political economy — determine which agricultural systems exist where, and how does von Thünen's model explain the spatial logic of land use around markets?

Key Concepts

  • Subsistence vs. commercial agriculture: Subsistence agriculture produces food primarily for the farming household, with little or no surplus sold in markets. Commercial agriculture produces for external markets, relies on purchased inputs, and is integrated into global supply chains. The distinction is about the relationship between production and market exchange, not technological sophistication.

  • Major subsistence systems:

    • Shifting cultivation (swidden): Oldest form of tropical farming; fields are cleared, burned, cultivated 2–5 years, then left fallow 10–25 years. Sustainable at low population densities; practiced in Amazon Basin, Congo Basin, and mainland Southeast Asia.
    • Intensive subsistence agriculture: Characteristic of densely populated river deltas and plains in East and South Asia; wet-rice paddy cultivation requires extraordinary labor (10–20x dryland grain farming) but yields 4–6 tonnes/hectare; double- and triple-cropping routine.
    • Pastoral nomadism: Herding livestock across seasonally variable grazing ranges in areas too arid for reliable crops (Sahel, East African savanna, Central Asian steppe, Arabian Peninsula).

  • Major commercial systems:

    • Mixed crop-livestock farming: American Corn Belt and European heartland; livestock convert feed grains into higher-value meat/dairy.
    • Dairy farming: Temperate, humid climates with cool summers, clustered near urban markets due to milk perishability (US Dairy Belt: Vermont → Wisconsin).
    • Grain farming: Extensive systems on world's great interior grasslands — Argentine pampas, Russian steppe, American/Canadian Great Plains.
    • Plantation agriculture: Single export crop on large tropical estates; historically inseparable from colonialism; legacy visible in export-crop dependence of former colonies.
    • Market gardening (truck farming): High-value fruits/vegetables near urban markets; California Central Valley provides ~50% of US fruits and vegetables.
    • Commercial ranching: Extensive livestock grazing for beef; Argentine pampas, Brazilian cerrado, Australian outback, American Mountain West.

  • Extensive vs. intensive agriculture: Extensive agriculture applies low inputs per unit of land and relies on scale (American Great Plains). Intensive agriculture applies large inputs of labor, capital, or technology per unit of land (East Asian rice farming, California vegetables).

Models / Theories / Geographers

Johann Heinrich von Thünen's Concentric Rings Model (1826): In Der isolierte Staat ("The Isolated State"), von Thünen posited that in a featureless plain with a single central market, agricultural activities sort themselves into concentric rings based on the interaction of land rent (highest near the city) and transport costs. The most perishable, high-value goods with high transport costs per unit occupy the innermost ring; the most extensive, least perishable goods occupy the outermost ring.

The four classic zones:

  1. Market gardening and dairying — perishable products (milk, vegetables, flowers) produced intensively very close to the city.
  2. Timber and firewood — heavy, bulky pre-industrial fuel; obsolete in post-industrial economies.
  3. Extensive crop farming — storable grains at moderate distance.
  4. Ranching and extensive livestock — live animals can "walk to market," minimizing transport cost at the periphery.

Globalization of the rings: Von Thünen's model scales to the planetary level when airfreight replaces the cart horse. Kenyan roses and green beans airfreighted to London in 24 hours occupy the inner ring by the logic of high perishability and high value — despite being geographically distant. Chilean blueberries in British supermarkets in January, Mexican avocados in Wisconsin in two days — the rings have gone global.

Von Thünen's critique: The model assumes a featureless homogeneous plain, a single market, and pre-global transport. Rivers, roads, and topography deform the rings; global supply chains and airfreight compress distance. The core insight — transport cost and perishability determine spatial sorting — retains explanatory power even as the assumptions are relaxed.

Connect to the Exam

Concepts and Processes (CP): Know the agricultural type vocabulary precisely — an FRQ that asks you to "describe" intensive subsistence vs. extensive commercial farming requires distinguishing inputs, labor, market orientation, and geographic context.

Spatial Relationships (SR): Von Thünen's model is a classic spatial-relationship analysis tool. On an FRQ: (1) identify the "market" (city, global node, export terminal); (2) identify the relevant "transport cost" variable (road, rail, airfreight, container ship); (3) assess perishability. Then predict where the commodity should be produced.

Data Analysis (DA): Map interpretation questions may show agricultural land-use patterns around a city or region — apply von Thünen's logic to explain the spatial pattern and identify deviations caused by real-world factors (rivers, highways).

Scale of Analysis (SOA): Local application (dairy near a city) vs. global application (airfreighted Kenyan flowers to London) demonstrates the model's multi-scale utility.

Source Comparison (SC): Von Thünen derived his model from empirical cost-accounting data on his own Mecklenburg estate — a useful example of the interplay between direct observation and theoretical abstraction in geography.

Sample FRQ Prompt: Using von Thünen's concentric rings model, explain why market gardening and dairy farming locate closest to cities while ranching occupies the periphery. Then describe one real-world modification — physical, technological, or economic — that distorts the idealized ring pattern and explain the geographic mechanism behind the distortion.

Cross-Section Connections

  • Topic 5.1 (Agricultural Revolutions): The agricultural types visible today are direct legacies of where the three revolutions took hold — intensive subsistence rice in Asia reflects the First Revolution's Yangtze/Yellow River hearths; extensive grain farming on the Great Plains reflects Second Revolution mechanization diffusion.
  • Topic 5.3 (Agribusiness): Commercial agricultural systems (plantation, grain, market gardening) feed directly into the agribusiness commodity chains discussed in section 5.3; von Thünen's globalized rings explain why Honduran bananas reach Wisconsin.
  • Topic 5.4 (Environmental Consequences): The spatial concentration of commercial grain and ranching in extensive systems drives the deforestation and runoff problems examined in section 5.4.
  • Unit 6 (Urbanization): Von Thünen's model links directly to urban bid-rent theory; the same logic that sorts agricultural uses into rings around a market also sorts urban land uses into concentric rings around a CBD.
  • Unit 7 (Economic Geography / Development): Plantation agriculture's colonial legacy and pastoral nomadism's decline under sedentarization pressure connect to development geography and world-systems theory (core–periphery).
