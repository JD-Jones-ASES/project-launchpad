AP Human Geography — Topic 2.2: Modeling Demographic Change: DTM and Population Pyramids

Core Question

How do geographers model the relationship between birth rates, death rates, and population change across stages of economic development — and what does a population's age-sex structure reveal about its demographic past and future?

Key Concepts

  • The Demographic Transition Model (DTM): A five-stage framework linking birth rates (CBR), death rates (CDR), and natural increase to levels of economic development. The model's core insight is that death rates decline before birth rates — creating an interval of explosive population growth before fertility eventually falls. Derived from Western European industrialization data and formalized by Frank Notestein in 1945 (building on Warren Thompson's 1929 observation).

  • DTM Stage 1 — High Stationary: Both CBR and CDR hover at 35–50 per 1,000; population grows slowly because high mortality offsets high fertility. No country currently occupies Stage 1; it describes most of human history before the 18th century.

  • DTM Stage 2 — Early Expanding: Death rates fall sharply (sanitation, vaccines, clean water) while birth rates remain high due to persistent cultural, economic, and religious norms favoring large families. Result: explosive natural increase. Sub-Saharan Africa (Niger, Mali, Chad) remains here in the 2020s. Niger's total fertility rate (TFR) of approximately 7 is among the world's highest.

  • DTM Stage 3 — Late Expanding: Birth rates begin to fall as urbanization reduces the economic value of children, women gain education and employment access, and contraceptives become available. Population continues to grow but at a decelerating rate. Much of South and Southeast Asia occupies Stage 3.

  • DTM Stage 4 — Low Stationary: Both CBR and CDR are low; population is large but essentially stable near replacement-level fertility (TFR ≈ 2.1). The United States, France, Australia, and China broadly occupy Stage 4.

  • DTM Stage 5 — Declining: Birth rates fall below replacement. Without immigration, population shrinks in absolute terms. Germany, Italy, Japan, South Korea, and most of Eastern Europe are in Stage 5. Japan's population has declined since 2009; South Korea's TFR fell to approximately 0.7 in 2023 — the world's lowest for a large nation.

  • Population pyramids: Paired horizontal bar charts showing a population's age-sex structure. Three canonical shapes: (1) expansive/triangular (Stage 2, wide base — Niger); (2) stationary/rectangular (Stage 4 — United States); (3) constrictive/inverted (Stage 5, narrow base — Japan). A pyramid is a compressed spatial argument: it encodes a population's demographic history and forecasts future labor supply, pension pressure, and immigration needs.

  • Dependency ratios: The ratio of economically dependent people (under 15 + over 64) to working-age population (15–64). A high youth dependency ratio (Stage-2 countries) strains current resources but contains a future "demographic dividend." A high elderly dependency ratio (Stage-4/5 countries) creates immediate fiscal pressure on pensions and healthcare systems. Niger's dependency ratio exceeds 100; Japan's is rising past 70 and climbing.

  • Epidemiological Transition Model (ETM): Proposed by Abdel Omran (1971), the ETM describes how causes of death shift as countries develop — from infectious/parasitic diseases (Stage 1–2) to chronic degenerative diseases like cardiovascular disease and cancer (Stage 4–5). The ETM complements the DTM by explaining the "why" behind falling death rates. Its reversibility is challenged by emerging infectious diseases (HIV/AIDS, COVID-19, drug-resistant tuberculosis).

  • Stage-5 aging crisis: When fertility falls persistently below replacement, the age structure inverts. Japan's worker-to-retiree ratio fell from roughly 9:1 in 1970 to below 2:1 by the 2020s, with pension solvency and healthcare demand as cascading consequences. The fiscal remedy — immigration — is politically contested.

Models / Theories / Geographers

Warren Thompson (1929) and Frank Notestein (1945): Co-originators of the DTM. Thompson observed the empirical pattern in industrialized-country data; Notestein formalized it into the five-stage model. The DTM is derived from Western European experience and may not be a universal law — sub-Saharan Africa has resisted the expected fertility decline despite falling death rates; Eastern European countries moved to Stage 5 without the extended Stage 3 Western Europe experienced. The model also omits migration, which can significantly alter a country's actual growth rate independently of birth and death rates.

Abdel Omran (1971): Proposed the Epidemiological Transition Model. His three-phase framework (Age of Pestilence and Famine → Age of Receding Pandemics → Age of Degenerative and Human-Made Diseases) explains the cause-of-death dimension of demographic transition. Emerging infectious diseases demonstrate that the transition is not irreversible.

Japan as Stage-5 case study: With a TFR of approximately 1.2 and a population declining since 2009, Japan is the world's clearest Stage-5 laboratory. By 2040, the Japanese government projects 35 percent of the population will be over 65. The government's pronatalist responses ("womenomics" policies, extended retirement ages, guest-worker visa expansion) have not reversed the fertility decline. South Korea's TFR of 0.7 in 2023 represents an even more extreme Stage-5 trajectory.

Connect to the Exam

CP — Concepts and Processes: The DTM is among the highest-frequency models on the AP Human Geography exam. Be able to: (1) place a country in the correct DTM stage given CBR/CDR/TFR data; (2) identify the shape of a population pyramid and link it to a DTM stage; (3) explain the mechanism driving population growth or decline at each stage.

SR — Spatial Relationships: Population pyramids and DTM stages map onto identifiable geographic patterns. Stage-2 countries with expansive pyramids concentrate in sub-Saharan Africa and parts of South Asia. Stage-5 constrictive pyramids cluster in East Asia and Europe. When analyzing FRQ stimuli, connect pyramid shape to: the relevant DTM stage, the likely geographic region, and economic/policy implications (labor supply, pension strain, immigration pressure).

DA — Data Analysis: The dependency ratio formula is directly calculable from pyramid data. Practice: if 30% under 15, 10% over 64, 60% working-age → dependency ratio = (0.30 + 0.10)/0.60 × 100 = 66.7. Both youth and elderly dependency ratios are test-ready.

SOA — Source Analysis: Primary source stimuli featuring demographic data (census tables, vital statistics) should be analyzed for: the historical period they represent, the country or region they describe, and what inference about DTM stage is warranted — and what is not (e.g., a single-year snapshot may not capture a trend).

SC — Scales of Analysis: A national-level TFR can mask internal variation — China's TFR differs between urban and rural populations; the U.S. TFR varies significantly by state and demographic group.

Sample FRQ Stimulus Prompt: A population pyramid for Country X shows a very wide base (0–14 cohort) and very narrow upper age groups. Country Y shows equal bar widths through most age groups with slight narrowing at the top.

Part A: Identify the DTM stage most likely represented by Country X and explain one demographic process that produces the pyramid shape shown.

Part B: Describe one economic challenge Country X is likely to face over the next 30 years given the pyramid shown, and explain why.

Part C: Explain how Country Y's demographic profile differs from Country X's and identify one policy response a Stage-4 or Stage-5 government might adopt.

Cross-Section Connections

  • Topic 2.1 (Distribution and Density): DTM stage predicts whether a country's population is growing (Stage 2–3) or stabilizing/declining (Stage 4–5), directly affecting population density trajectories. Sub-Saharan African countries in Stage 2 face rising physiological density pressure.

  • Topic 2.3 (Population Theories): The DTM provides the empirical framework within which Malthus's pessimism and Boserup's counter-thesis operate. Stage-2 countries are the test cases for whether population pressure triggers crisis or innovation.

  • Topic 2.4 (Migration): Stage-5 aging creates immigration pressure — young workers from Stage-2 and Stage-3 countries fill the labor-force gap in Stage-4/5 economies. The Zelinsky Migration Transition Model explicitly ties migration type to DTM stage.

  • Unit 4 (Political Geography): The aging-population crisis generates internal political debates about immigration, pension reform, and the welfare state — all of which have spatial dimensions (immigrant concentration in cities, regional aging patterns).

  • Unit 6 (Urbanization): Urbanization is both a cause and effect of demographic transition. The DTM's Stage-3 mechanism (urbanization reduces economic value of children) directly links population change to city growth.
