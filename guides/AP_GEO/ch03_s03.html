AP Human Geography — Topic 3.3: The Geography of Religion

Core Question

How do different religious traditions spread across space, leave material imprints on the cultural landscape, and generate political conflict when competing sacred claims overlap on the same territory?

Key Concepts

  • Universalizing vs. ethnic religions: A universalizing religion actively seeks converts and claims its message is appropriate for all humanity; its global reach comes through missionary diffusion. The three largest are Christianity (~2.4B), Islam (~1.9B), and Buddhism (~500M). An ethnic religion defines membership by descent, ethnicity, or cultural belonging — it does not recruit and produces spatially concentrated distributions. Hinduism, Judaism, and Shinto are the principal examples.

  • Hearths and diffusion paths:

    • Hinduism: Rooted in the Indus Valley (~2000 BCE); no single founder; spread within South Asia and through Hindu kingdoms and trade networks into Southeast Asia.
    • Buddhism: Founded in Lumbini (present-day Nepal, 6th century BCE); spread under Mauryan emperor Ashoka; diffused along trade routes into Central Asia, East Asia, and Southeast Asia; largely displaced in South Asia by resurgent Hinduism.
    • Christianity: Emerged in first-century Palestine; spread through the Roman Empire by contagious diffusion along trade routes and hierarchical diffusion through urban centers and imperial patronage (Constantine's conversion, 312 CE); extended globally through European colonialism.
    • Islam: Founded in Mecca (7th century CE); spread rapidly out of Arabia after Muhammad's death through military expansion and trade. Indonesia — the world's most populous Muslim-majority country — was reached through maritime trade and Sufi missionaries, not conquest. Diffusion pathway shapes landscape outcome.

  • Sacred spaces and built religious forms: Each major tradition produces a characteristic landscape:

    • Mosques: Oriented toward Mecca (the qibla); minarets for the call to prayer; interior free of representational imagery; organized around the mihrab (prayer-direction niche) and minbar (pulpit). A mosque in Jakarta faces northwest; one in Madrid faces southeast.
    • Buddhist stupas: Hemispherical mound structures housing relics; adapted into the pagoda (East Asia) and chedi (Southeast Asia).
    • Christian churches: Often cruciform; altar traditionally at the east end (toward Jerusalem); Gothic cathedrals achieve height through pointed arches and flying buttresses.
    • Hindu temples: Sacred geometry prescribes the layout; the central tower (shikhara) rises above the innermost sanctuary (garbhagriha); the whole structure represents Mount Meru.

  • Religious conflict and sacred space: Where different communities claim the same territory as sacred, conflict becomes intractable:

    • Jerusalem: Simultaneously sacred to Judaism (Temple Mount / Western Wall), Islam (Dome of the Rock / al-Aqsa Mosque), and Christianity (Church of the Holy Sepulchre). The Old City covers ~1 km²; any sovereignty decision has implications for hundreds of millions worldwide.
    • Northern Ireland (the Troubles, 1968–1998): Unionist (Protestant/British) vs. Nationalist (Catholic/Irish) identity. Religion served as an ethnic marker; the landscape of Belfast remains divided by peace walls, murals, and flags.
    • Kashmir (1947–present): Muslim-majority territory that acceded to predominantly Hindu India at Partition; two full-scale wars and ongoing insurgency; a heavily militarized Line of Control.

Models / Theories / Geographers

  • Mircea Eliade: Historian of religion who developed the concept of sacred space and the axis mundi. In religious experience, space is not uniform: sacred places are qualitatively different from profane ones, oriented toward a cosmic center. His framework underlies AP Human Geography's analysis of sacred space.

  • Carl Sauer (connection): Religious built forms — mosques, stupas, cathedrals, temples — are the most architecturally concentrated expression of Sauer's claim that culture groups imprint their values and cosmologies on the natural environment.

Connect to the Exam

CP: Universalizing vs. ethnic religion is one of the exam's most frequently tested distinctions. Be precise: universalizing religions seek converts; ethnic religions define membership by descent. Know which major faiths fall in which category, and why each type produces a different spatial distribution.

SR: Use the mosque orientation example as a model for spatial reasoning: the qibla creates a predictable directional pattern in mosque placement worldwide — testable with a map.

DA: Questions about religious distribution maps ask students to identify patterns, name the diffusion process that produced them, and explain exceptions (Indonesia's Muslim majority reached through maritime trade, not conquest).

SOA: Paired mosque/cathedral images are model source-analysis exercises: identify two architectural features per structure, explain what each reveals about the faith tradition, and consider how historical context of construction affects interpretation.

SC: Jerusalem is the exam's canonical case for how past sacred claims persist into present territorial conflict. Connect to Topic 3.1's palimpsest principle: multiple historical claims layered on the same square kilometer.

Sample FRQ prompt: Using a specific example, explain how the concept of sacred space contributes to the intractability of a religious territorial conflict. Identify the competing claims of at least two groups and explain why their spatial overlap makes resolution difficult.

Cross-Section Connections

  • 3.1 (Cultural Landscape): Sacred buildings are Sauer's cultural landscape at its most intentional — forms whose every dimension is prescribed by cosmological belief.
  • 3.2 (Language): Arabic spread as a lingua franca along the same routes as Islam; Hebrew's revival as a modern spoken language is inseparable from the Zionist religious-nationalist project.
  • 3.4 (Ethnicity): The Northern Ireland and Myanmar cases both illustrate how religious identity fuses with ethnic and national identity, blurring the line between religious and ethnic conflict.
  • Unit 4 (Political Geography): Sacred-space claims become geopolitical disputes; Jerusalem is the prime case for how religious geography drives sovereignty conflict.
  • Unit 6 (Urban Geography): The medieval Christian city's layout — organized around the parish church or cathedral — is an architectural imprint of religious hierarchy on urban form.
