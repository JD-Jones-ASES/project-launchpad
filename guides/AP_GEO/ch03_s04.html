AP Human Geography — Topic 3.4: Ethnicity, Race, and the Organization of Gendered Space

Core Question

How do social categories — ethnicity, race, and gender — determine who lives where, who controls which spaces, and whose knowledge and labor get inscribed into the cultural landscape?

Key Concepts

  • Ethnicity vs. race: Ethnicity is a self-defined social identity based on shared cultural heritage — ancestry, language, religion, customs, and homeland; it can be maintained across migration and evolve over generations. Race is a system of classifying humans based on perceived physical characteristics. Geographers regard race as a social construct — not biologically fixed (genetic variation within racial groups exceeds variation between them) but socially enforced and capable of producing profound geographic consequences: where people live, what schools they attend, what wealth they accumulate.

  • Ethnic enclaves: Neighborhoods where a substantial proportion of residents share a common ethnic identity, expressed in the cultural landscape through ethnic-language signage, cuisine, religious institutions, and community organizations. San Francisco's Chinatown, Miami's Little Havana, and Dearborn's Arab-American community are canonical examples. Enclaves reflect both voluntary community formation and, historically, forced clustering produced by legal discrimination.

  • Ethnoburbs: Geographer Wei Li (1998) coined this term for suburban ethnic enclaves — immigrant communities dispersing to suburbs rather than concentrating in inner-city neighborhoods. Monterey Park and San Gabriel in the Los Angeles metro area are the canonical Chinese-American examples: suburban settings with ethnic commercial strips, Chinese-language banks, and high proportions of foreign-born residents. Ethnoburbs challenge the assumption that ethnic geography is confined to the inner-city "Chinatown" model.

  • Spatial assimilation and heterolocalism: Spatial assimilation (Chicago School) describes immigrant groups gradually moving out of ethnic enclaves as they achieve economic mobility. Heterolocalism describes the different pattern observed among many post-1965 immigrants: communities disperse geographically at or near arrival but maintain ethnic ties through digital communication, shared institutions, and periodic gathering. A Hmong family in Minneapolis may have no Hmong neighbors within a mile yet participate in a dense community network across the Twin Cities.

  • Ethnic cleansing and genocide: The forced removal or destruction of a group to produce ethnic homogeneity; genocide occurs when the intent is to destroy the group "in whole or in part." Three AP-essential cases: Rwanda (1994) — ~800,000 Tutsi killed in 100 days, rooted in colonial-era Belgian racial classification; Srebrenica, Bosnia (1995) — ~8,000 Bosniak men and boys killed by Bosnian Serb forces in a UN "safe area," ruled genocide by the ICTY; Rohingya, Myanmar (2017–present) — ~700,000–1,000,000 Rohingya Muslims fled to Bangladesh (Cox's Bazar camp, world's largest), with the UN concluding genocidal intent.

  • Organization of gendered space: Gender has a geography. Most societies historically structured built space around a public/private divide — public space (street, market, political arena) associated with men; domestic space (home, reproduction) associated with women. These distinctions took cultural-specific forms (the South Asian zenana; 19th-century "separate spheres" ideology). The Gender Inequality Index (GII), developed by UNDP, measures reproductive health, empowerment, and labor-market participation on a 0 (equality) to 1 (maximum inequality) scale.

Models / Theories / Geographers

  • Judith Carney (b. 1955): Cultural and historical geographer at UCLA. Her 2001 Black Rice argued that South Carolina's rice economy depended on the specific agricultural expertise of enslaved Gambian women — sophisticated tidal floodplain cultivation and sluice-gate water management developed along West Africa's "Rice Coast." Planters specifically sought enslaved women for this knowledge. Carney recovered a "hidden geography of expertise": the antebellum rice landscape was built on knowledge extracted from enslaved African women under coercion, yet rendered invisible in the historical record.

  • Wei Li: Coined ethnoburb in 1998 to capture the suburban dispersal of late-20th-century immigrant communities — a spatial form the Chicago School model did not predict.

  • Yi-Fu Tuan (connection): Tuan's topophilia — the emotional bonds people form with place — explains why ethnic enclaves carry strong community attachment; exclusion from the broader city made the enclave a refuge and a site of identity investment.

Connect to the Exam

CP: Distinguish ethnic enclave from ethnoburb, and spatial assimilation from heterolocalism. These pairings test whether students understand how contemporary immigrant geography departs from the classic Chicago School model.

SR: Use the San Francisco Chinatown image exercise as a model: at the neighborhood scale, the enclave reflects exclusion and self-organization; at the metropolitan scale, it coexists with the dispersal that moved many Chinese Americans to the broader Bay Area suburbs. Both scales must be addressed.

DA: GII data questions ask what a high score indicates (severe structural disadvantage for women in reproductive health, political empowerment, and labor-market access) and which conditions — civil conflict, poverty, legal restriction — drive extreme scores.

SOA: Carney's research models the critical landscape-reading move the AP exam rewards: ask not just what the landscape looks like but whose choices, constraints, and coerced knowledge produced it.

SC: All three genocide cases involve deliberate reorganization of ethnic geography through violence — post-war Bosnia is more ethnically segregated than pre-war Bosnia. Contrast with the gradual, voluntary dispersal of spatial assimilation.

Sample FRQ prompt: Using Judith Carney's research on the South Carolina rice landscape as a specific example, explain how gender is embedded in the cultural landscape in ways that are not immediately visible. Identify the knowledge system involved, explain how it shaped the landscape, and discuss why it was rendered invisible in the historical record.

Cross-Section Connections

  • 3.1 (Cultural Landscape): The critical extension of Sauer's framework — "reading the landscape requires asking not just what it looks like, but whose choices and constraints produced it" — is the analytical move Carney's Black Rice performs and that Topic 3.4 teaches as method.
  • 3.2 (Language): Language is the most prominent marker of ethnic identity. Forced suppression of indigenous languages is one of the primary mechanisms through which dominant groups dismantle ethnic cultural landscapes.
  • 3.3 (Religion): Rohingya and Northern Ireland cases illustrate that ethnicity and religion are often inseparable — religious difference functions as an ethnic marker, and the landscape consequences (displacement camps, peace walls) reflect both simultaneously.
  • Unit 2 (Population and Migration): Ethnic enclaves, ethnoburbs, and heterolocal communities are spatial outcomes of migration. Post-1965 immigration produced the ethnoburb pattern; earlier waves produced inner-city neighborhoods.
  • Unit 4 (Political Geography): Ethnic cleansing and genocide are extreme instruments of political-geographic reorganization, producing homogeneous territories by destroying or displacing previously heterogeneous populations. Rwanda and Srebrenica connect to international law and the failure of multilateral intervention.
