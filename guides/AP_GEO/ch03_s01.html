AP Human Geography — Topic 3.1: Culture and the Physical Landscape

Core Question

How do human cultures transform the natural environment into a cultural landscape, and why can the same physical setting produce fundamentally different human geographies?

Key Concepts

  • Cultural landscape (Sauer's definition): The visible, material imprint that a culture group leaves on the natural environment over time. Sauer's formula: culture is the agent, the natural area is the medium, the cultural landscape is the result. He redirected geography from environmental determinism toward human agency as the transforming force.

  • Palimpsest principle: Real landscapes are layered records of successive occupance — each culture group inscribes its mark without fully erasing what came before. The American South, where antebellum plantation houses survive alongside sharecropper cabins surrounded by later railroad towns and highway interchanges, is the canonical example.

  • Folk culture vs. popular culture: Folk cultures are small, homogeneous, and place-specific; their practices evolved in relative isolation and change slowly. Popular culture is globally distributed through mass media and commercial networks, producing landscape uniformity — the franchise strip visible from suburb to suburb.

  • Placelessness: Edward Relph's term (1976) for the erosion of geographic distinctiveness that popular culture produces. When a highway corridor in Ohio is visually identical to one in Texas, the specificity of "place" has been displaced by mass-distribution standardization.

  • Diffusion types: Cultural traits spread from their hearth through relocation diffusion (the trait moves with migrating people — Italian immigrants transplanting pizza to American cities) or expansion diffusion (outward spread without source-population migration). Expansion has three subtypes: hierarchical (high fashion filtering from Paris to mass-market retail), contagious (rapid spread through direct contact, like the westward spread of wheat agriculture), and stimulus diffusion (the underlying idea spreads but is transformed locally — Buddhism adapting to Chinese Confucian values while retaining core doctrine).

  • Environmental determinism vs. possibilism: Environmental determinism (Ellsworth Huntington) argued that climate determines cultural achievement — entangled with colonial racial ideology and today thoroughly discredited. Possibilism (Vidal de la Blache) holds that the environment sets a range of possibilities; human agency, technology, and history determine which are realized. Jared Diamond's Guns, Germs, and Steel represents neo-determinism: it avoids racial essentialism but still grounds historical outcomes in biogeographic variables, a move critics argue naturalizes global inequality.

Models / Theories / Geographers

  • Carl Sauer (1889–1975): Founded the Berkeley School of cultural geography. His 1925 "Morphology of Landscape" established the cultural landscape as the discipline's organizing concept and proposed Southeast Asia (not the Fertile Crescent) as the earliest plant-domestication hearth.

  • Vidal de la Blache (1845–1918): French geographer and possibilism's founding theorist. Studied the genres de vie of French regional communities to show that each adapted creatively to its environment rather than being determined by it.

  • Edward Relph: Coined placelessness in 1976 to describe how popular culture erodes the geographic distinctiveness of local landscapes.

Connect to the Exam

CP: Know Sauer's definition precisely and apply it to any built-landscape example the exam presents.

SR: Practice reading a landscape image for cultural-value evidence. Angkor Wat — a geometric sacred space on an alluvial rice-growing hinterland — is the source text's model exercise.

DA: Diffusion-type classification is a frequent short-answer target. Be able to distinguish the four subtypes with one concrete example each.

SOA: Sauer's 1925 essay is an AP-style primary source. Identify: (1) the theoretical opponent (environmental determinism), (2) the core claim (human agency over environmental determination), (3) the rhetorical strategy (morphological vocabulary to anchor geography in observable artifacts).

SC: Palimpsest logic applies to any question about why landscapes look the way they do — layers of cultural imprint from successive occupants.

Sample FRQ prompt: Explain how the concept of stimulus diffusion accounts for why a cultural practice in a receiving region resembles but differs from that same practice in its hearth. Use a specific example in your response.

Cross-Section Connections
