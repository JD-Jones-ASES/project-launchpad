AP Human Geography — Topic 4.2: Boundaries and Geopolitics

Core Question

How do the timing and circumstances of boundary-making shape the stability of states — and what geographic theories have driven great-power competition over territory?

Key Concepts

  • Hartshorne's four boundary types: Political geographer Richard Hartshorne classified boundaries by their relationship to pre-existing human settlement. An antecedent boundary predates significant settlement (e.g., the 49th Parallel west of the Great Lakes, surveyed before major migration). A subsequent boundary is drawn after settlement and attempts to reflect cultural or linguistic patterns already on the ground (post-WWI and post-WWII European borders redrawn along ethnic lines). A superimposed boundary is imposed by an outside power with no regard for existing communities (African colonial borders from the Berlin Conference, 1884–85). A relict boundary has ceased to function as a political border but remains visible in the cultural or physical landscape (Hadrian's Wall; the former Berlin Wall's trace in Berlin's street patterns and nightlife geography).

  • The Berlin Conference and superimposed Africa: At the 1884–85 Berlin Conference, fourteen European powers divided Africa by invoking "effective occupation" — a Eurocentric legal standard that required European-style administrative presence and ignored indigenous political structures. Roughly one-third of Africa's colonial boundary segments follow geometric straight lines with no correspondence to physical or cultural features. These borders became the postcolonial state system through the legal principle of uti possidetis (inherited boundary presumed valid), locking in the colonial cartographic legacy.

  • UNCLOS maritime zones (1982): The United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea created a layered framework of maritime sovereignty. The territorial sea (0–12 nautical miles) gives full sovereignty including navigation regulation. The contiguous zone (12–24 nm) allows enforcement of customs, fiscal, and immigration laws. The Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ, 0–200 nm) grants exclusive resource rights (fish, oil, gas, deep-sea minerals) while preserving foreign navigation. The continental shelf extension (up to 350 nm where physical shelf extends further) covers seabed resource rights. EEZ overlaps are now among the world's most contentious territorial disputes.

  • Mackinder's Heartland Theory (1904): British geographer Halford Mackinder argued in "The Geographical Pivot of History" that whoever controlled the landlocked interior of Eurasia (the Heartland) could dominate the World-Island (Eurasia + Africa) and, through it, the world. His formula: "Who rules East Europe commands the Heartland; who rules the Heartland commands the World-Island; who rules the World-Island commands the World." Railways were the key — they gave land-based powers the mobility that sea power had previously denied them. The theory shaped British imperial strategy and informed U.S. Cold War containment doctrine, despite the critique that the USSR controlled the Heartland for 70 years and was outcompeted by maritime states.

  • Spykman's Rimland Theory (1944): Dutch-American scholar Nicholas Spykman inverted Mackinder, arguing in The Geography of the Peace that the coastal periphery of Eurasia — the Rimland, encompassing Western Europe, the Middle East, South Asia, and East Asia — held more strategic value because it contained most of the world's population, agriculture, and industry. "Who controls the Rimland rules Eurasia." Spykman's framework directly shaped U.S. Cold War containment policy: NATO, the Japan alliance, and interventions in Korea and Vietnam all reflect Rimland logic.

  • Ratzel's Organic Theory of the State (1897): German geographer Friedrich Ratzel argued that the state should be understood as a biological organism that needs Lebensraum ("living space") to grow and survive. His framework was intended as scholarly description, not prescription. However, Nazi geopoliticians — especially Karl Haushofer — stripped the theory of its nuance and used Lebensraum to justify German eastward conquest in the 1930s–40s. Ratzel's case is a canonical example of how academic frameworks can be weaponized when stripped of their scholarly caution and married to racial ideology.

  • East China Sea case (Senkaku/Diaoyu): Eight uninhabited islands totaling 7 sq km have generated one of the world's sharpest geopolitical disputes because overlapping EEZ claims give whoever controls the islands exclusive resource rights over potentially oil- and gas-rich waters. Japan administers the islands; China and Taiwan assert historical sovereignty. Japan's 2012 government purchase of three islands triggered China's 2013 Air Defense Identification Zone (ADIZ) declaration. The U.S.–Japan Mutual Security Treaty's Article 5 creates escalation risk disproportionate to the territory's size — illustrating how the EEZ framework can intensify rather than reduce maritime conflict.

Models / Theories / Geographers

  • Richard Hartshorne — four boundary types (antecedent, subsequent, superimposed, relict); the most widely used typology for AP boundary classification questions.
  • Halford Mackinder (1861–1947) — Heartland Theory; geographic pivot argument; "Who rules East Europe commands the Heartland" formula; influenced Cold War containment doctrine.
  • Nicholas Spykman (1893–1943) — Rimland Theory; inverted Mackinder; "Who controls the Rimland rules Eurasia"; direct theoretical basis for U.S. containment strategy.
  • Friedrich Ratzel (1844–1904) — Organic Theory; Lebensraum concept; spatial laws of state growth; misuse by Nazi Geopolitik is a required critical caveat.

Connect to the Exam

CP (Concepts and Processes): Apply Hartshorne's typology to classify boundaries described in stimulus prompts. The most common error is confusing superimposed (imposed by outside power) with subsequent (drawn after settlement to reflect it).

SR (Source Analysis): The Berlin Conference General Act and Mackinder's 1904 paper are both FRQ-ready primary sources. Berlin tests sourcing around "effective occupation" language and its Eurocentric framing; Mackinder tests the rhetorical purpose of geographic argument in British imperial strategy.

DA (Data Analysis): UNCLOS maritime zone diagrams and maps of African colonial borders appear frequently as data stimuli.

SOA (Spatial Organization): The global pattern of superimposed borders (Africa, South Asia, Middle East) vs. subsequent borders (post-WWI Europe) reflects the spatial legacy of colonial and imperial power.

SC (Scale Analysis): Maritime boundary disputes operate simultaneously at the local (fishing rights), national (resource sovereignty), and global (great-power military competition) scales — the Senkaku/Diaoyu case exemplifies this multi-scale dynamic.

Sample FRQ prompt: "In 1885, the Berlin Conference divided the African continent among European colonial powers using the principle of 'effective occupation.'" (A) Using Hartshorne's typology, classify the borders created by the Berlin Conference and justify your classification. (B) Explain ONE long-term consequence of these borders for political stability in postcolonial Africa. (C) Explain how the principle of uti possidetis has perpetuated this legacy.

Cross-Section Connections

  • Topic 4.1 (States, Nations, Sovereignty): Superimposed borders are the direct spatial expression of the Westphalian system's application under colonialism. Stateless nations (Kurds, Rohingya) often trace their statelessness to boundary decisions made at imperial conferences and post-war treaties.
  • Topic 4.3 (Internal Organization): Superimposed borders that divide ethnic groups create the multinational states and centrifugal tensions explored in 4.3–4.4. Nigeria's hundreds of ethnolinguistic groups inside one colonial border is a direct cause of internal political fragmentation.
  • Topic 4.4 (Devolution and Supranationalism): UNCLOS and NATO represent supranational frameworks for managing the maritime and military consequences of the boundary and geopolitical dynamics introduced here.
  • Topic 7 (Industrialization and Development): Ratzel's Organic Theory connects to economic geography — states' need for resources was the material driver behind the territorial competition these theories described.
