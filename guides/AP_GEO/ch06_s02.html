AP Human Geography — Topic 6.2: Urban Systems: Hierarchies and Central Place Theory

Core Question

Why do cities come in so many different sizes, and what geometric and mathematical patterns govern how settlements distribute themselves across a landscape to serve a dispersed population?

Key Concepts

  • The Rank-Size Rule (Zipf's Law): In a mature urban system, the population of the nth-largest city equals the population of the largest city divided by n. A country whose second city is roughly half the size of the first, third city one-third, and so on approximates rank-size regularity. The United States is the classic large-country example. When city populations are plotted against rank on a log-log scale, the result is a straight line.

  • Primate City: When one city is so dominant that no mathematical rule captures its size advantage, it is called a primate city. Primacy results from colonial history (disproportionate investment in the port capital), political centralization (government services concentrated in the capital), and self-reinforcing agglomeration economies (firms cluster where other firms are). Bangkok (10–12 million) versus Chiang Mai (300,000) represents an extreme case, with a ratio exceeding 30:1.

  • Central Place Theory — Threshold and Range: Christaller's theory explains regional settlement hierarchies through two key concepts. Threshold is the minimum demand needed to make offering a service economically viable — high-threshold services (organ transplant centers, opera companies) appear only in large cities. Range is the maximum distance a consumer will travel for a service — high-order goods have large ranges; low-order goods (bread, gasoline) have small ranges.

  • The Hexagonal Lattice: On an idealized uniform plain, market areas that cover every point without overlap and with maximum efficiency take hexagonal form. Hexagons tile perfectly and minimize average consumer travel distance. The result is a nested hierarchy: tiny hexagons for low-order goods nested within larger hexagons for higher-order goods.

  • K-Values: Christaller identified three organizing principles — K=3 (Marketing Principle: maximizes number of market centers), K=4 (Transportation Principle: places lower-order cities on straight roads between higher-order cities), and K=7 (Administrative Principle: each higher-order city entirely encompasses six lower-order places for unambiguous jurisdiction).

  • The Gravity Model: Interaction between two places is proportional to the product of their populations and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them (I = P₁P₂/d²). Used to predict commuter flows, migration streams, and retail catchment areas. New York and Chicago interact more than New York and Denver because of combined population mass and relative proximity.

Models / Theories / Geographers

  • Mark Jefferson (1863–1949): American geographer who named the primate city pattern in his 1939 paper "The Law of the Primate City." Observed that many national systems produce one city that is qualitatively dominant — not merely the largest, but supereminent in political, economic, and cultural terms. His "law" is more an observed tendency than a predictive rule.

  • Walter Christaller (1893–1969): German geographer who developed Central Place Theory in his 1933 work Die zentralen Orte in Süddeutschland (Central Places in Southern Germany). Derived threshold and range concepts and the hexagonal lattice from idealized assumptions about a uniform plain and perfect competition. His theory gained international recognition only in the 1950s–60s during geography's quantitative revolution. Note: Christaller's wartime involvement with Nazi planning of occupied territories is a cautionary case study in the political misuse of spatial models.

Connect to the Exam

CP (Concepts and Processes): Threshold and range are high-frequency multiple-choice targets. Know which direction each concept points — high-order services have high thresholds AND large ranges; low-order services have low thresholds AND small ranges.

SR (Spatial Relationships): Christaller's hexagonal lattice describes how settlements spatially relate to one another across a region. Be ready to explain why the pattern breaks down in mountainous terrain or near coastlines.

DA (Data Analysis): A table of city populations asking you to identify rank-size versus primate patterns is a standard data-analysis prompt. Calculate the ratio of largest to second-largest city; a ratio near 2:1 suggests rank-size; 5:1 or higher signals primacy.

SOA (Spatial Organization and Arrangement): The nested hierarchy of central places — hamlets inside village areas inside town areas inside city areas — is a spatial organization pattern directly applicable to flat agricultural regions like Iowa or the North China Plain.

SC (Scale Analysis): The gravity model operates at multiple scales simultaneously — it can predict neighborhood-level retail flows and national-scale migration streams using the same formula.

Sample FRQ Prompt: A country has cities with populations of 12 million, 1.8 million, 900,000, 700,000, and 600,000. (A) Identify whether this urban system more closely resembles a rank-size or primate distribution and explain your reasoning. (B) Explain TWO factors that could cause a country to develop a primate city rather than a rank-size distribution. (C) Describe one consequence of urban primacy for regional economic development.

Cross-Section Connections

  • Topic 6.1 (Origins of Urban Settlements): Borchert's Sail-Wagon port cities correspond closely to the highest-order central places in a national hierarchy — their break-of-bulk situation advantages translated into primate or near-primate status.
  • Topic 6.3 (Internal City Structure): Central Place Theory explains where cities fall in a regional hierarchy; Burgess, Hoyt, and Harris-Ullman explain what happens inside those cities — complementary spatial scales.
  • Topic 6.4 (Urban Challenges): Primate cities concentrate services and investment in the capital, draining peripheral regions and intensifying urban challenges like housing shortages and informal settlements — topics covered in the final section.
  • Topic 4.x (Political Geography): Christaller's K=7 Administrative Principle connects directly to how states organize territorial jurisdiction — a spatial logic that reappears in political geography discussions of gerrymandering and administrative hierarchies.
