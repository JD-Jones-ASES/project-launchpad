AP Human Geography — Topic 6.1: The Origins and Evolution of Urban Settlements

Core Question

Why do cities form where they do, and how has the relationship between transportation technology and urban form changed across time?

Key Concepts

  • Site vs. Situation: Site refers to the physical, intrinsic characteristics of a location — access to fresh water, defensible terrain, fertile hinterland, natural harbors. Situation describes a location's relative position within a broader network of trade routes, transportation corridors, and surrounding settlements. Site factors are place-bound; situation factors are relational and can shift dramatically as technology evolves.

  • Break-of-Bulk Points: Locations where goods must be transferred between modes of transport — where a navigable river meets a road, where ocean shipping meets a continental interior — generate enormous economic power from position alone. Chicago's Chicago Portage exemplifies this principle: a short overland link connecting two drainage systems became the hinge of a continental trade network.

  • The Sjoberg Pre-Industrial City: Before mechanized transport, all cities shared a common spatial logic driven by walking-speed constraints. The elite clustered at the center near seats of power and major religious institutions; the poor occupied the exposed periphery — the inverse of modern North American cities. Urban radius was capped at roughly 1.5 miles (a 30–45 minute walk), keeping even the largest pre-industrial capitals extremely dense.

  • Borchert's Epochs of Urban Transportation: American metropolitan evolution followed five distinct technological eras: (1) Sail-Wagon (pre-1830) — coastal ports and river-mouth cities; (2) Iron Horse (1830–1870) — railroad junction cities like Chicago; (3) Steel Rail (1870–1920) — heavy-industry manufacturing belt metropolises; (4) Auto-Air (1920–1970) — Sunbelt cities and suburban sprawl enabled by the gasoline engine; (5) High-Tech (1970–present) — knowledge clusters and edge cities organized around digital infrastructure.

  • Friction of Distance and Urban Form: Each successive transport technology reduced the cost of overcoming distance, reshaping city form accordingly. The walking city was dense because distance was expensive; the streetcar city spread along corridors; the automobile city sprawled in all directions; the digital city allows knowledge workers to locate by quality-of-life preference.

  • Chicago as a Case Study: Chicago's history nearly perfectly illustrates both site-situation tension and Borchert's epochs. Its site (flat, swampy lakeshore) was uninspiring; its situation (the Chicago Portage linking the Great Lakes drainage to the Mississippi watershed) was exceptional. The canal (1848) and railroad made it the hub of the continental interior, and successive economic eras — from grain and livestock to financial derivatives — trace Borchert's model exactly.

Models / Theories / Geographers

  • Gideon Sjoberg (1925–2010): Proposed the Pre-Industrial City model (1960), arguing that all pre-industrial cities regardless of culture share a common spatial structure with elite at the center and poor at the periphery. Criticized for overgeneralizing and for being a static cross-section rather than a dynamic explanation.

  • John Borchert (1918–2001): University of Minnesota geographer whose 1967 paper "American Metropolitan Evolution" identified five epochs of urban growth tied to dominant energy and transportation technology. Focused on American cities; critics note he underemphasized social forces like racial segregation and federal housing policy.

Connect to the Exam

CP (Concepts and Processes): Site vs. situation is a near-certain multiple-choice target — know specific examples for each (harbors = site; portage location = situation). Borchert's epoch names and their defining technologies are frequently tested.

SR (Spatial Relationships): Be ready to explain why Chicago's flat hinterland, initially seeming a disadvantage, became an asset in the Steel Rail epoch (ideal for grid agriculture and rail distribution).

DA (Data Analysis): A map showing city locations along river confluences, portage points, or coastal harbors tests whether you can read site and situation factors from spatial data.

SOA (Spatial Organization and Arrangement): Sjoberg's pre-industrial model (elite center / poor periphery) contrasts directly with modern North American models — a common comparison prompt.

SC (Scale Analysis): Urban decisions operate simultaneously at local (site drainage), regional (situation within trade networks), national (federal infrastructure investment), and global (commodities pricing) scales.

Sample FRQ Prompt: Explain how the concepts of site and situation account for Chicago's growth from a small portage settlement to a major industrial metropolis. In your response, describe one site characteristic and one situation characteristic, and explain how a change in transportation technology altered the relative importance of each.

Cross-Section Connections

  • Topic 6.2 (Urban Hierarchies): Borchert's Sail-Wagon coastal ports are the same cities that develop into dominant primate or rank-size leaders — situation advantages in the first epoch compounded into urban hierarchy dominance.
  • Topic 6.3 (Internal City Structure): The Sjoberg pre-industrial model provides the baseline spatial logic that the Burgess, Hoyt, and Harris-Ullman models depart from as industrialization and automobiles transform urban form.
  • Topic 5.x (Agriculture and Rural Land Use): Chicago's Steel Rail growth was inseparable from the agricultural Corn Belt its rail network served — urban and agricultural geography are linked systems.
  • Topic 1.x (Geography: Its Nature and Perspectives): Site and situation are foundational spatial concepts that reappear in virtually every unit of the AP Human Geography course.
