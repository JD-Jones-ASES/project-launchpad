AP Human Geography — Topic 1.2: Geospatial Technologies and Spatial Data

Core Question

How have remote sensing, GPS, GIS, and volunteered geographic information transformed who can produce geographic knowledge — and what new questions of data quality, spatial bias, and ethical use arise from that transformation? The section examines both the architecture of modern geospatial technology and the democratic and distorting effects of opening geographic data production to non-professionals.

Key Concepts

  • Remote sensing: The acquisition of data about the Earth's surface from a distance, using sensors on satellites, aircraft, or drones; passive sensors detect reflected or emitted radiation, while active sensors (like LiDAR and radar) emit their own energy pulses and measure the return signal.
  • LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging): An active remote sensing technology that emits rapid laser pulses to build precise three-dimensional point clouds of terrain, capable of resolving elevation differences of centimeters and "seeing through" forest canopy to reveal structures below.
  • GPS (Global Positioning System): A U.S. Department of Defense satellite constellation of at least 24 satellites that enables receivers to determine position through trilateration — measuring signal travel times from four or more satellites to calculate three-dimensional location.
  • Trilateration: The GPS positioning technique of measuring travel times from multiple satellites and solving for the single point in space simultaneously at the calculated distance from each, requiring at least four satellites to correct for clock drift.
  • GIS (Geographic Information System): An integrated software environment for storing, manipulating, analyzing, and displaying spatially referenced information organized as layers — each layer a distinct category of geographic data — that can be overlaid and queried simultaneously.
  • Vector data: GIS data representing geographic features as discrete points, lines, or polygons (e.g., wells, roads, county boundaries), well suited to network analysis and representing bounded objects.
  • Raster data: GIS data dividing the landscape into a regular grid of cells each storing a value, suited to representing continuous spatial variation such as elevation, temperature, or land-cover classification.
  • Quantitative spatial data: Measurable numeric values attached to locations (population counts, precipitation totals, housing values), essential for identifying patterns across large numbers of places but silent about meaning and experience.
  • Qualitative spatial data: Descriptive, interpretive, or narrative geographic information (ethnographic field notes, oral histories, mental maps), capturing how people experience and construct their spatial worlds.
  • Volunteered Geographic Information (VGI): Spatial data created and shared by private individuals using internet-connected devices without formal training, ranging from deliberate map contributions to incidental geotagged social media posts; coined by geographer Michael Goodchild in 2007.

Models / Theories / Geographers

  • Landsat program (NASA/USGS, launched 1972): The primary U.S. satellite Earth-observation initiative, carrying multispectral sensors that record reflectance across visible, near-infrared, and thermal bands; the near-infrared band is foundational for the Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) used to monitor drought, deforestation, and vegetation change.
  • MODIS (Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer, 1999): NASA's Terra satellite instrument capturing daily global imagery at 250 m–1 km resolution, central to tracking land-cover change, wildfire, and sea surface temperature for over two decades.
  • Michael Goodchild (2007): Geographer who coined the term "Volunteered Geographic Information" (VGI) to describe the phenomenon of private individuals contributing spatial data via internet-connected devices, raising questions about coverage bias and data quality.
  • OpenStreetMap (Steve Coast, 2004): A free, editable, openly licensed global map built collaboratively by millions of volunteers; as of the mid-2020s it contains over 8 billion geographic nodes and is often the most detailed geographic reference in regions where commercial mapping coverage is incomplete, including much of sub-Saharan Africa and refugee settlements.
  • Ushahidi (Kenya, 2007–2008): A crisis-mapping VGI platform built after Kenya's disputed presidential election to geocode mobile-phone reports of violence in near-real time; subsequently used for earthquake damage mapping in Haiti (2010) and election monitoring across Africa, demonstrating both the power and the coverage gaps of community-sourced spatial data.
  • Yi-Fu Tuan / topophilia (introduced here): Referenced in connection with qualitative data; Tuan's work on the deep emotional bond between people and their environments drew primarily on qualitative evidence such as literary texts and personal narratives rather than quantitative datasets. Connect to the Exam

The Data Analysis (DA) skill is the primary assessment lens for this section. AP exam stimuli frequently present maps derived from remote sensing or GIS analysis, and full credit on free-response items requires identifying not just what pattern is visible but also what the data type's limitations are for answering the geographic question posed. A choropleth built from GIS-modeled flood risk is only as accurate as the underlying elevation model; LiDAR-derived models are far more precise than older surveying data. When a stimulus uses VGI-derived data, students should ask: who contributed these data, and which communities are likely under-represented? The Spatial Perspectives (CP) skill applies when evaluating why Ushahidi's map was most detailed near universities and urban areas — the spatial bias in data collection is itself a geographic phenomenon. The Source Analysis (SOA) skill is tested when students must evaluate the reliability, coverage, and perspective of a spatial dataset.

Sample FRQ prompt: A humanitarian organization uses OpenStreetMap to plan aid distribution after a major earthquake in a densely forested, rural region. (A) Explain ONE advantage of using VGI data such as OpenStreetMap compared to official government maps for this purpose. (B) Explain ONE limitation of VGI data that could affect the accuracy of the organization's distribution plans. (C) Describe how LiDAR remote sensing could supplement the VGI data to improve planning, and identify ONE geographic question LiDAR could answer that OpenStreetMap data cannot.
