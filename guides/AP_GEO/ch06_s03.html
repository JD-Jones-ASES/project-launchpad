AP Human Geography — Topic 6.3: Theoretical Models of Internal City Structure

Core Question

How do geographers and sociologists explain the spatial organization of land use, income, and social class within cities, and why do models developed for North American industrial cities fail to describe cities on other continents?

Key Concepts

  • The Chicago School: A cluster of sociologists and geographers at the University of Chicago in the 1920s–1940s who used their own city as a living laboratory to map land use, social patterns, and neighborhood change. Their models — Burgess, Hoyt, Harris-Ullman — became the canonical framework for urban geography worldwide, though all were derived from a specific American industrial city under specific historical conditions.

  • Invasion and Succession: Burgess's term for the continuous outward expansion of each urban zone into the next, driven by population growth and economic pressure. Each new wave of immigrants begins in the Zone of Transition and over generations moves outward to more stable residential zones, while new arrivals take their place at the urban core.

  • Filtering: In Hoyt's sector model, new high-quality housing is built at the outer edge of the high-income sector; previously newest housing becomes available to the next income tier, and so on down the chain. In theory, filtering delivers decent used housing to lower-income groups; in practice, racial segregation and disinvestment often arrested the process.

  • Agglomeration Economies and Repulsion: The Harris-Ullman model explains why multiple nuclei form: some activities require specialized facilities available only at particular locations (ports, runways); others benefit from clustering with similar activities (jewelry wholesalers, financial firms); and others are mutually repellent (heavy industry drives out high-end residential).

  • Edge Cities and the Galactic City: By the 1980s, large suburban office-retail complexes — edge cities — had become secondary downtowns, often larger in employment than the historic CBD. The Galactic City model describes a metropolitan form organized around a galaxy of roughly equivalent nodes rather than a single dominant center.

  • Global Models Challenge North American Universality: The Chicago School models assumed industrial capitalism, automobile ownership, racial segregation, a weak welfare state, and federal suburban subsidies — conditions that do not apply universally. McGee's Southeast Asian model, Griffin-Ford's Latin American model, and the Sub-Saharan African model each describe fundamentally different spatial organizations shaped by colonial history, informal housing, and different city-building processes.

Models / Theories / Geographers

  • Ernest Burgess (1886–1966): Concentric Zone Model (1923) — five rings: CBD, Zone of Transition, Zone of Working-Class Housing, Zone of Better Residences, Commuter Zone. Elite near the periphery; poorest at the inner ring (inverse of Sjoberg). Criticized for being static, assuming a single CBD, and ignoring racial segregation.

  • Homer Hoyt (1895–1984): Sector Model (1939) — land uses form wedge-shaped sectors radiating outward from the CBD along transportation arteries. High-income residential areas begin near the CBD along an attractive corridor and expand outward along the same sector. More realistic than Burgess for cities with dominant transportation axes.

  • Chauncy Harris (1914–2003) and Edward Ullman (1912–1976): Multiple Nuclei Model (1945) — the modern city develops around several distinct nuclei, not one CBD. Best fits the post-1945 American metropolis (Los Angeles is the canonical example). Descriptive rather than predictive.

  • Gideon Sjoberg (1925–2010): Pre-Industrial City Model (1960) — elite at the center, poor at the periphery, walking-scale density. Provides the historical baseline from which all modern models diverge. Still visible today in the medinas of North African and Middle Eastern cities.

  • T. G. McGee (b. 1936): Southeast Asian City Model (1967) — colonial cities contain at least three distinct commercial cores: a Western-style port district, a Chinese commercial zone, and a traditional indigenous market area. High-density squatter settlements appear along transport corridors rather than at a middle-class suburban fringe.

  • Ernst Griffin and Larry Ford: Latin American City Model (1980) — a vibrant CBD with an adjacent colonial historic center; a high-income spine extending outward in one direction; surrounding zones of decreasing prosperity; and a disamenity sector of desperate poverty on topographically marginal land (favelas, callampas, colonias populares). The most affluent residents live near the CBD, not the outermost suburbs — the key contrast with Burgess.

Connect to the Exam

CP (Concepts and Processes): All six models are testable. Know at minimum: what each model proposes, who proposed it, what evidence supports it, and its key criticism. The Burgess-to-Hoyt-to-Harris-Ullman progression as a historical sequence is frequently asked.

SR (Spatial Relationships): A common question asks you to apply a model to a described city and explain which model best fits. Los Angeles → Multiple Nuclei; a city with a strong radial transit system → Hoyt Sector; a Latin American capital with a prestigious boulevard → Griffin-Ford.

DA (Data Analysis): A map showing concentric rings, sectors, or multiple nodes may ask you to identify the applicable model and explain one strength and one limitation.

SOA (Spatial Organization and Arrangement): The contrast between Burgess (elite at periphery) and Sjoberg (elite at center) and Griffin-Ford (elite near CBD spine) is a high-value comparison item for multiple-choice and short-answer.

SC (Scale Analysis): Edge cities represent a shift in the scale at which economic activity organizes — from the metropolitan CBD to a dispersed suburban galaxy — with consequences for infrastructure, inequality, and transit planning.

Sample FRQ Prompt: A geographer studying a rapidly growing city in Southeast Asia observes three distinct commercial zones: a modern high-rise district near the port, a dense market area run primarily by ethnic Chinese merchants, and a traditional indigenous bazaar. (A) Identify the urban model that best describes this structure and name its author. (B) Explain TWO ways this structure differs from the Burgess Concentric Zone Model. (C) Explain ONE historical factor that accounts for the differences you identified in Part B.

Cross-Section Connections

  • Topic 6.2 (Urban Hierarchies): Central Place Theory explains inter-city hierarchies; internal structure models explain intra-city organization — two complementary spatial scales.
  • Topic 6.4 (Urban Challenges): Burgess's Zone of Transition is where gentrification and displacement dynamics play out; the disamenity sector of the Griffin-Ford model is where informal housing and infrastructure deficits are most severe.
