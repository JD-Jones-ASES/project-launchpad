AP Human Geography — Topic 4.4: Centripetal vs. Centrifugal Forces: Devolution and Supranationalism

Core Question

What holds states together and what pulls them apart — and how do devolution (power shifting downward) and supranationalism (power shifting upward) represent opposite responses to the same underlying pressures?

Key Concepts

  • Centripetal forces: Conditions, institutions, and ideas that bind a state's population together and reinforce national identity. A shared national language is among the most consistently cited — France used public schools and the military to promote French at the expense of regional languages (Breton, Occitan, Basque). Shared religion can function centripetally when it distinguishes a population from its neighbors (Polish Catholicism sustained national identity through 123 years of partition). Shared history, civic mythology, and infrastructure (roads, railways, telecommunications) all create the "imagined community" (Benedict Anderson) of the nation. Key caveat: none of these forces is sufficient alone — religiously homogeneous societies (Northern Ireland) can experience severe centrifugal conflict.

  • Centrifugal forces: Conditions that drive populations away from unity toward fragmentation, autonomy, or secession. Ethnic and religious conflict, economic disparities between regions, geographic barriers, and internet-enabled parallel information ecosystems that sustain grievances all operate as centrifugal forces. The critical insight is that economic inequality between regions — not just cultural difference — consistently appears in devolutionary movements (Catalonia as net fiscal contributor to Spain; Scottish oil revenues in the Brexit debate).

  • Devolution: The transfer of authority from a central government to sub-national units without abolishing central sovereignty. Devolution is political geography's most common institutional response to centrifugal pressure. Four case studies are canonical. Catalonia (2017): wealthy, Catalan-speaking autonomous community held an independence referendum the Spanish Constitutional Court declared unconstitutional; Spanish government invoked Article 155 to impose direct rule, imprisoning or exiling senior Catalan officials. Scotland (2014 and Brexit): voted 55.3%–44.7% to remain in the UK in 2014, then voted 62% to remain in the EU in 2016 while England voted for Brexit — re-energizing independence demands; the UK Supreme Court ruled in 2022 that a binding second referendum requires Westminster consent. South Sudan (2011): seceded from Sudan after a 98.8% independence referendum following decades of north-south civil war; became independent July 9, 2011, but descended into its own civil war by 2013. Quebec (1980 and 1995): two independence referenda; the 1995 vote failed by less than 1% (50.58% to 49.42%); Canada's federal Clarity Act (2000) formalized the conditions under which secession could be negotiated.

  • Supranationalism: Voluntary pooling of aspects of sovereignty into multinational organizations. If devolution shifts power downward from the national to the regional scale, supranationalism shifts power upward from the national to the global scale. Key examples: the United Nations (1945, 193 members, limited by the P5 veto); NATO (1949, 32 members as of 2024, Article 5 collective defense, eastward expansion as a factor in Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine); ASEAN (1967, 10 members, non-interference norm); African Union (2002, 55 members); Mercosur (1991, South American trade bloc).

  • The EU as the deepest supranational experiment: Evolution in stages: European Coal and Steel Community (1951) — pooled heavy industry to make French-German war economically self-defeating; European Economic Community (1957, Treaty of Rome) — common market for goods, services, capital, labor; Maastricht Treaty (1993) — common citizenship, common currency plans, shared foreign policy; Eurozone (1999, 20 members by the 2020s) — transferred monetary sovereignty (interest rates, money supply) to the European Central Bank. The 2008–2012 European debt crisis exposed the eurozone's internal tension: member states could not devalue their currencies to adjust to external shocks. Brexit (2020) demonstrated that supranational integration is not irreversible — the 52–48% UK referendum in 2016 and the January 2020 departure showed that democratic electorates can withdraw the voluntary consent that sustains pooled sovereignty.

  • Responsibility to Protect (R2P, 2005): The UN General Assembly's 2005 doctrine holds that sovereignty is not a shield — when a state fails to protect its population from genocide, war crimes, or ethnic cleansing, the international community has a responsibility to act. R2P emerged from the failures of Rwanda (800,000 killed in 100 days, 1994) and Srebrenica (8,000 Bosniak Muslims executed, 1995). It was invoked for NATO airstrikes in Libya (2011); but Russia and China blocked application to Syria, arguing the Libya intervention exceeded its mandate. R2P is simultaneously a meaningful normative advance and a doctrine whose application is entirely dependent on great-power politics.

Models / Theories / Geographers

  • Benedict Anderson — "imagined community": nations are socially constructed through shared narrative, print media, and collective memory. The theoretical foundation for analyzing centripetal forces built from shared history and civic mythology.
  • Centripetal / Centrifugal framework: The central organizing model for 4.4; borrowed from physics to describe political cohesion vs. fragmentation. Every devolution and supranationalism case study is an application of this framework.
  • EU integration stages (ECSC → EEC → Maastricht → Eurozone → Brexit): The canonical timeline for illustrating progressive sovereignty pooling — and its limits.
  • Responsibility to Protect (R2P): An emerging international norm that directly challenges the Westphalian principle of absolute internal sovereignty, connecting 4.4 back to 4.1.

Connect to the Exam

CP (Concepts and Processes): Be precise about the direction of power transfer: devolution = downward (national to regional); supranationalism = upward (national to global). Exam prompts frequently test whether students can correctly classify a given institution or event as an example of one or the other.

SR (Source Analysis): The EU flag, NATO charter Article 5, and the Clarity Act are all potential source stimuli. R2P documents test sourcing around the gap between humanitarian language and geopolitical constraints.

DA (Data Analysis): Referendum result data (Scottish independence, Quebec, South Sudan, Brexit) and EU membership/eurozone timelines appear as data stimuli requiring explanation of trends.

SOA (Spatial Organization of the World): The global pattern of devolution (European autonomous regions, post-colonial secessionist movements) and supranational organizations (EU, AU, ASEAN, NATO) reflects both the spatial legacy of state formation and the scale at which different political problems are best addressed.

SC (Scale Analysis): This section is the primary test of SC. A single policy domain (e.g., environmental regulation) can simultaneously involve municipal, national, EU-level, and Paris Accord-level governance. Questions may ask students to identify which scale is most relevant for explaining a specific political outcome.

Sample FRQ prompt: "Devolution and supranationalism represent opposite responses to the same underlying pressures on the modern state." (A) Define devolution and give ONE specific example from a named country after 1990. (B) Define supranationalism and explain ONE way in which the European Union has required member states to pool their sovereignty. (C) Using the concepts of centripetal and centrifugal forces, explain why both devolution and supranationalism are occurring simultaneously in the modern world.

Cross-Section Connections

  • Topic 4.1 (States, Nations, Sovereignty): R2P and the EU directly challenge the Westphalian model of absolute sovereignty introduced in 4.1. Brexit and the Catalan case test the limits of sovereignty-pooling and sovereignty-assertion respectively.
  • Topic 4.2 (Boundaries and Geopolitics): NATO's eastward expansion — a strategic application of Spykman's Rimland logic — is a direct centrifugal force on Russian foreign policy and a cause of the 2022 Ukraine invasion. UNCLOS is a supranational framework for managing the maritime boundary disputes introduced in 4.2.
  • Topic 4.3 (Internal Organization): Devolution represents the dynamic process that moves states along the unitary–federal spectrum introduced in 4.3. Scotland's Parliament, Catalonia's autonomous government, and Quebec's provincial powers are all examples of unitary states partially federalizing under centrifugal pressure.
  • Topic 3 (Cultural Patterns): Language, religion, and ethnicity are the specific cultural forces that function as centripetal or centrifugal depending on context. Belgium's French-Flemish linguistic divide and Catalonia's Catalan language are cultural geography concepts operating as political geography drivers.
  • Topic 6 (Industrialization and Development): The EU's eurozone and Mercosur are both economic integration projects that link development strategy to political geography — connecting Unit 4's political analysis to Unit 6's economic patterns.
