AP Human Geography — Topic 4.3: Internal Political Organization and Electoral Geography

Core Question

How does the distribution of political authority within a state — between central and sub-national governments, and through the drawing of electoral districts — shape democratic representation and political power?

Key Concepts

  • Unitary state: The central (national) government holds supreme authority; sub-national units exercise only those powers the center delegates. France is the canonical example — 101 departments are administrative arms of Paris, not autonomous sovereignties. China, Japan, and the UK (despite devolution to Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland) are also primarily unitary. Unitary structures offer policy coherence but can be inflexible in diverse societies.

  • Federal state: The constitution divides sovereignty between a central government and sub-national units (states, provinces, Länder), and neither can unilaterally abolish the other. The United States was the first modern federal republic; Germany's Basic Law (1949) establishes 16 Länder with distinct competences; India constitutionally lists Union List (central), State List (state), and Concurrent List (shared) powers. Federal systems accommodate diversity but generate persistent fiscal and regulatory tensions between levels.

  • Confederation: An association of sovereign states that delegates limited, specific powers (typically defense, trade, foreign policy) to a common body while members retain sovereignty. The U.S. under the Articles of Confederation (1781–1788), the Confederate States, and aspects of the EU exemplify this form. Confederations are inherently unstable because members can veto or withdraw.

  • Reapportionment: Following each decennial census, seats in a legislative body are redistributed among states or provinces to reflect population change. After the 2020 U.S. Census, Texas gained two House seats while California, New York, and Illinois each lost one. Reapportionment is about how many seats each unit gets, not where district lines are drawn within units.

  • Redistricting: After reapportionment determines seat totals, redistricting redraws the geographic boundaries of individual legislative districts within each state. The one-person-one-vote principle (Reynolds v. Sims, 1964) requires districts to be as equal in population as possible. In most U.S. states, redistricting is controlled by the state legislature — the party in power draws the maps for its own elections.

  • Gerrymandering: The manipulation of district boundaries to give one party or group an unfair electoral advantage. Two techniques are routinely combined. Cracking disperses opposition voters across multiple districts so they form a minority in each. Packing concentrates opposition voters into as few districts as possible so the party-in-power wins more districts by narrower but sustainable margins while the opposition "wastes" votes winning a few by enormous margins. Computer modeling has made modern gerrymandering far more precise than the original 1812 salamander-shaped district that gave the practice its name.

  • Rucho v. Common Cause (2019): The Supreme Court ruled 5–4 that federal courts have no "judicially manageable standard" to review partisan gerrymandering claims, leaving them as political questions under federal law. State courts, however, remain free to apply state constitutional provisions guaranteeing free elections — producing a patchwork outcome in which North Carolina and Pennsylvania courts later struck down gerrymanders under state law. The ruling illustrates the limits of judicial remedies and the ongoing role of political geography in determining electoral outcomes.

Models / Theories / Geographers

  • Unitary / Federal / Confederal spectrum: The primary organizational framework for comparing internal state structures on the AP exam. Know canonical examples for each type and be able to explain one advantage and one disadvantage of unitary vs. federal structures.
  • Reynolds v. Sims (1964): Supreme Court established the one-person-one-vote principle for legislative redistricting — the legal foundation for all modern redistricting analysis.
  • Rucho v. Common Cause (2019): The controlling precedent on partisan gerrymandering at the federal level; the ruling's patchwork outcome demonstrates how political geography intersects with constitutional law.
  • Elbridge Gerry (1812): Governor of Massachusetts who signed the bill creating a salamander-shaped district; the term "gerrymandering" derives from this episode and is the required origin story for the concept on the AP exam.

Connect to the Exam

CP (Concepts and Processes): Distinguish unitary, federal, and confederal clearly — know at least two examples of each and be able to explain one advantage and one disadvantage of unitary vs. federal organization. On gerrymandering, be precise about cracking vs. packing and their combined effect.

SR (Source Analysis): District maps and shape descriptions (Maryland's "broken-winged pterodactyl," North Carolina's highway-width 12th District) function as visual primary sources. Questions may ask whether a district's shape suggests cracking, packing, or racial gerrymandering (Shaw v. Reno, 1993).

DA (Data Analysis): Census data and seat-allocation tables are standard data stimuli for reapportionment questions. Maps showing before-and-after redistricting are used to test cracking/packing identification.

SOA (Spatial Organization): The spatial distribution of federal vs. unitary states reflects colonial history, cultural diversity, and geographic scale. Large, diverse countries (U.S., India, Brazil, Nigeria) tend toward federalism; smaller, homogeneous ones (France, Japan) toward unitary organization. This pattern is a testable SOA generalization.

SC (Scale Analysis): Reapportionment operates at the national scale; redistricting at the state scale; individual district lines at the local scale. Understanding which level a given question targets is essential for accurate analysis.

Sample FRQ prompt: "The process of redistricting in the United States is controlled in most states by the party holding power in the state legislature." (A) Explain the difference between reapportionment and redistricting. (B) Describe how cracking and packing work together as gerrymandering techniques. (C) Explain ONE limitation on federal courts' ability to remedy partisan gerrymandering, with reference to a specific Supreme Court ruling.

Cross-Section Connections

  • Topic 4.1 (States, Nations, Sovereignty): The federal/unitary distinction directly maps onto the sovereignty question — federalism constitutionally divides sovereignty between levels of government, while unitary systems locate sovereignty exclusively at the national level.
  • Topic 4.4 (Devolution and Supranationalism): Devolution (e.g., Scotland's Parliament, Catalonia's autonomy) represents a middle path between the unitary and federal poles introduced here. Understanding the unitary/federal spectrum provides the baseline against which devolutionary change is measured.
  • Topic 3 (Cultural Patterns): Federal structures often emerge in culturally diverse countries — India's linguistic states, Nigeria's ethnolinguistic federalism, Switzerland's multilingual cantons — connecting internal political organization to the cultural geography of Unit 3.
  • Topic 2 (Population and Migration): Reapportionment is driven by population change recorded in the census. Internal migration patterns (Sunbelt growth, Rust Belt decline) directly determine which states gain and lose seats — a link between demographic geography and political geography.
