AP Human Geography — Topic 2.4: The Geography of Migration

Core Question

Why do people move from one place to another, what spatial patterns does that movement produce, and how have geographers modeled the relationship between migration and development?

Key Concepts

  • Push and pull factors: The foundational analytical framework for migration. Push factors are conditions at the origin that drive out-migration: poverty, unemployment, conflict, political persecution, environmental hazard. Pull factors are conditions at the destination that attract in-migration: higher wages, political freedom, family networks, educational opportunity. Both operate simultaneously; a person rarely migrates without some combination of origin dissatisfaction and destination opportunity. The same condition can function as both — drought pushes from a drying region and pulls toward irrigated agricultural zones.

  • Four categories of push/pull factors: (1) Economic — wage differentials between countries are the most powerful force in modern international labor migration; (2) Political — persecution, civil war, and authoritarian governance generate forced migration; (3) Social — chain migration creates self-reinforcing networks that lower cost and risk for subsequent migrants from the same community; (4) Environmental — climate change (sea-level rise, desertification, intensifying storms) is accelerating environmentally driven displacement.

  • Voluntary vs. forced migration: Voluntary migration occurs when individuals freely choose to relocate for economic, social, or environmental improvement. Forced migration occurs when individuals are compelled by violence, persecution, natural disaster, or legal coercion with no realistic safe alternative. The legal and moral distinction is significant, but in practice the boundary is blurry: extreme poverty in a conflict-affected region can make "choosing to stay" a life-threatening non-option.

  • International vs. internal migration: International migration crosses a national border; internal migration occurs within a country. Most migration is internal. China's rural-to-urban movement of an estimated 250–300 million workers since the 1980s is entirely internal and is one of the largest human population movements in history. Globally, roughly 3.5 times as many internal migrants exist as international migrants.

  • Legal categories of forced migrants: A refugee is a person outside their country of nationality who cannot return because of a well-founded fear of persecution (1951 Refugee Convention definition). An asylee (asylum seeker) has applied for refugee status but awaits adjudication. An internally displaced person (IDP) is forced from their home by conflict or disaster but remains within their own country's borders — IDPs are not covered by the 1951 Refugee Convention and depend on their own government for protection. A stateless person is recognized by no country as a national; the Rohingya of Myanmar are the world's largest stateless group.

  • Intervening obstacles and opportunities: Between any origin and destination lie obstacles — physical (oceans, mountains, deserts), political (border controls, visas), economic (transit costs), and cultural (language barriers, xenophobia). Samuel Stouffer's theory of intervening opportunities (1940) adds that migrants stop at the nearest place offering an equivalent opportunity — explaining step-migration and why internal migration typically precedes international migration in a migrant's trajectory.

  • Distance decay and distance-decay exceptions: Migration volume generally decreases with increasing distance (Ravenstein's first law). However, long-distance air travel, diaspora networks, and refugee crisis conditions can override distance-decay effects — a point that limits the applicability of Ravenstein's 19th-century model to contemporary forced migration.

  • Remittances and brain waste: Remittances — money sent by migrants back to their origin country — are a critical economic link in global migration systems. The Philippines' overseas workers (approximately 10 million) generate remittances equal to 9–10 percent of Philippine GDP. Brain waste (deskilling) complicates this: highly educated professionals (nurses, engineers) take lower-skilled positions abroad because the wage gap makes even deskilled foreign work more remunerative than professional domestic work. The remittance economy encodes a structural dependency.

  • Chain migration: The process by which early migrants establish networks — housing information, employment contacts, cultural familiarity — that lower the cost and risk for subsequent migrants from the same community. Chain migration explains ethnic clustering in destination cities and the persistence of migration corridors between specific origin and destination regions over generations.

Models / Theories / Geographers

Ernst Georg Ravenstein (1834–1913): German-British cartographer who analyzed the 1871 and 1881 British censuses to derive seven empirical laws of migration, published in the Journal of the Statistical Society of London (1885, 1889). The laws: (1) Most migrants travel short distances; (2) migration proceeds in steps; (3) longer-distance migrants gravitate to large urban centers; (4) every migration stream generates a counterstream; (5) women dominate short-distance migration, men dominate long-distance migration (now significantly modified — women constitute approximately 48% of international migrants globally); (6) migration volume increases with technological development (transport, communication); (7) the primary motive for migration is economic. Ravenstein's laws have proven remarkably durable: distance decay, step-migration, and economic motivation are confirmed across different periods and countries. Their limits: derived from rapidly industrializing Victorian Britain; do not predict forced migration flows; long-distance air travel has weakened distance-decay effects; and the 19th-century gender pattern no longer holds.

Wilbur Zelinsky (1921–2013): American geographer who proposed the Migration Transition Model (1971), linking dominant migration types to stages of the Demographic Transition Model. Five stages: (1) Pre-modern traditional (DTM Stage 1) — limited circulation, people rarely move beyond their birth village; (2) Early transitional (DTM Stage 2) — massive rural-to-urban migration; significant emigration to "new lands" (19th-century European emigration to the Americas, Australia, New Zealand); (3) Late transitional (DTM Stage 3) — rural-to-urban continues but moderates; international migration becomes the dominant flow type; (4) Advanced (DTM Stage 4) — rural-to-urban migration largely complete; inter-city, suburban, and amenity/retirement migration dominate; (5) Future super-advanced (speculative Stage 5) — physical movement may decline as telecommunications reduce face-to-face needs. Evidence: European emigration history broadly fits. Critique: derived from Western European trajectory; China's massive internal migration at a Stage-3 level of development challenges a simple linear reading.

Everett Lee (1966): Extended Ravenstein's framework with a formal push-pull model that added the individual decision calculus — personal factors, awareness, and the role of intervening obstacles — that Ravenstein's census-level data could not capture.

Case study — Colombia and Syria (IDPs): Colombia (~7–8 million IDPs at peak, driven by 60+ years of civil conflict) and Syria (~6 million IDPs + ~6 million cross-border refugees from the 2011 civil war) represent the two largest IDP populations as of the mid-2020s. Syria illustrates the distinction between IDPs and refugees: the same underlying conflict produces both categories depending on whether people are able to cross an international border.

Case study — Philippines (remittances and brain waste): Approximately 10 million overseas Filipino workers (OFWs) in 160+ countries. Remittances = 9–10% of GDP. Brain waste: Filipino nurses earning professional salaries at home may earn five times as much as domestic workers in Hong Kong. The individual rational decision is economically optimal but represents a collective loss to Philippine public services.

Connect to the Exam

CP — Concepts and Processes: Know the push-pull framework cold. Be able to categorize a migration scenario as voluntary vs. forced, internal vs. international, and identify the relevant legal status (refugee, IDP, asylee, stateless). Know Ravenstein's laws and their limits. Know Zelinsky's stages and their DTM links.

SR — Spatial Relationships: When analyzing a migration flow map: (1) identify direction and volume of dominant flows; (2) look for distance-decay effects; (3) identify intervening obstacles (flows cluster by corridor rather than diffusing uniformly); (4) connect flows to the push-pull framework. Scale matters: a global remittance-corridor map looks very different from a sub-national IDP displacement map, even though both depict migration.

DA — Data Analysis: Remittance-to-GDP ratios, IDP/refugee population counts, and TFR data for sending vs. receiving countries are common FRQ data stimuli. Be comfortable reading flow maps, choropleth maps of refugee concentration, and tabular migration statistics.

SOA — Source Analysis: Ravenstein's "laws" paper is explicitly framed in the register of Victorian natural science — he uses "law" deliberately to invoke Newtonian regularity and claim that human migration behavior is as predictable as planetary motion. Analyzing this source requires identifying: his empirical method (census data, cartographic analysis), his rhetorical strategy (claiming scientific authority), and the limits of his categories (gender distinctions encoded Victorian social assumptions).

SC — Scales of Analysis: Zelinsky's model operates at the national/developmental scale. Ravenstein's laws operate at the regional/local scale. Chain migration operates at the community/neighborhood scale. An FRQ may require analysis at multiple scales simultaneously — e.g., explaining why a specific neighborhood in Minneapolis has a large Somali community requires scale-switching from global forced migration patterns to local chain-migration networks.

Sample FRQ Stimulus Prompt: A table shows that Country X sends the largest number of migrants to Country Y. Country X has a TFR of 4.5, a GDP per capita of $3,200, and a Stage-2 DTM classification. Country Y has a TFR of 1.4, a GDP per capita of $42,000, and a Stage-5 DTM classification. A second flow (smaller) runs from Country Y back to Country X.

Part A: Using Ravenstein's laws, explain two spatial or demographic patterns illustrated by the migration data shown.

Part B: Explain how the demographic conditions in Country Y create a pull factor for migrants from Country X. Use the Zelinsky Migration Transition Model in your answer.

Part C: The smaller return flow from Y to X illustrates one of Ravenstein's laws. Identify the law and explain one reason why some migrants return to their country of origin.

Cross-Section Connections

  • Topic 2.1 (Distribution and Density): Migration is the third component of population change (alongside natural increase and emigration) that shapes population distribution. The Northeast U.S. megalopolis and the Gulf States' rapid population growth are both products of massive in-migration, not natural increase alone.

  • Topic 2.2 (DTM): Zelinsky explicitly links migration type to DTM stage. Stage-5 countries' aging crisis creates immigration demand from Stage-2/3 countries — the demographic complement driving global labor migration corridors. The DTM omits migration, which can significantly alter a country's actual growth rate independently of birth and death rates.

  • Topic 2.3 (Population Theories): Boserupian intensification failures are environmental push factors for migration. Climate-driven displacement is the 21st-century frontier of Neo-Malthusian concern made manifest in migration flows — desertification in the Sahel, rising sea levels in Bangladesh and Pacific Islands.

  • Unit 4 (Political Geography): The 1951 Refugee Convention and the principle of non-refoulement are products of post-World War II political geography. Contemporary debates about border enforcement, "safe third country" agreements, and immigration quotas are political geography questions with direct demographic roots.

  • Unit 6 (Urbanization): Rural-to-urban migration is the primary engine of urbanization in Stage-2 and Stage-3 countries. Squatter settlements (informal housing zones) at the urban periphery in Lagos, Dhaka, and Mexico City are the landscape expression of step-migration arriving faster than formal housing markets can absorb it.

  • Unit 3 (Cultural Geography): Chain migration produces ethnic enclaves — Chinatowns, Little Italys, Somali neighborhoods — that are among the most visible cultural-geographic features of immigrant-receiving cities. Cultural distance between origin and destination is itself an intervening obstacle in migration decision-making.
