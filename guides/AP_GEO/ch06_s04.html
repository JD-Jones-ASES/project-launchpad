AP Human Geography — Topic 6.4: Urban Challenges and Sustainable Design

Core Question

How did deliberate policy choices create the racialized geography of American cities, what forces are reshaping those patterns today, and how are cities around the world adapting to automobile-dependent sprawl and accelerating climate risk?

Key Concepts

  • Redlining: The practice, formalized by the Federal Housing Administration (FHA) and the Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC) from the 1930s onward, of rating neighborhoods by mortgage risk and color-coding maps — designating neighborhoods with Black residents as "hazardous" (red) regardless of actual financial conditions. Federal mortgage insurance was denied in redlined areas, barring Black families from the low-interest loans simultaneously building White middle-class suburbs. The resulting wealth gap persists in today's census data.

  • Restrictive Covenants and Blockbusting: Restrictive covenants — legally enforceable deed clauses barring sale to racial or religious minorities — structured suburban demographics before being declared unenforceable in Shelley v. Kraemer (1948). Blockbusting exploited racial transition fears: agents frightened White homeowners into selling below market, then resold to Black families above market on contract-for-deed arrangements that built no equity, stripping wealth from both groups while profiting brokers.

  • White Flight and Urban Renewal: The postwar departure of White middle-class residents from American cities was accelerated by blockbusting, by federal highway investment making suburban commuting practical, and by urban renewal programs that demolished "blighted" (overwhelmingly Black and immigrant) neighborhoods to build highways, stadiums, and universities. The tax base collapse that followed deepened the deterioration of urban public services.

  • Gentrification: The in-movement of higher-income households into disinvested urban neighborhoods, driving up property values and rents and displacing long-term lower-income residents. Gentrification moves spatially along corridors of amenity — good transit, walkable commercial streets, proximity to universities, historic housing stock. Neil Smith's rent gap theory argues that gentrification is driven not by residential taste but by the gap between current low rents in disinvested neighborhoods and potential rents if redeveloped; when the gap is large enough, capital moves in.

  • Smart Growth and Transit-Oriented Development: A planning philosophy concentrating new development in existing urban cores near transit infrastructure rather than allowing continued low-density sprawl. Principles include mixed land uses, compact design, walkable streets, and a range of housing types. Portland, Oregon's Urban Growth Boundary (1979) legally prevents development from spreading beyond a defined perimeter. Jeff Speck's walkability framework identifies four necessary conditions: walking must be useful, safe, comfortable, and interesting.

  • Climate-Adaptive Urbanism: Coastal cities — the engines of two centuries of global economic growth — face sea-level rise, storm surge intensification, and extreme heat. Rotterdam's "Room for the River" approach designs public infrastructure to serve double duty: the Waterplein (water square) at Benthemplein functions as a skateboard park in dry weather and a stormwater reservoir during heavy rainfall. Singapore's vertical greenery program reduces urban heat island effects; Phoenix deploys cool pavements and mandatory shade structures; Bangladesh's coastal districts experiment with salt-tolerant rice varieties and raised homestead platforms as sea-level rise and saline intrusion displace rural residents toward Dhaka and Chittagong.

Models / Theories / Geographers

  • Neil Smith: Critical geographer who argued that gentrification is driven by the rent gap — the difference between current low rents in disinvested neighborhoods and potential rents under redevelopment — rather than by middle-class taste for urban living. Smith's framework connects displacement to the structural logic of capital investment rather than to individual household preferences.

  • Jeff Speck: Urban planner and author of Walkable City (2012). Synthesized research on pedestrian cities into a four-condition framework: walking must be useful (destinations reachable on foot), safe (traffic-calmed streets), comfortable (human-scale buildings), and interesting (active ground-floor uses). Conventional American zoning law that separates residential from commercial uses structurally prevents condition one.

  • Rotterdam as Case Study: The Dutch engineering shift from "fight the water" to "make room for the water" under the national Ruimte voor de Rivier (Room for the River) program. Rotterdam's multi-function public spaces (water squares, rooftop parks, floating neighborhoods) are now studied by climate-adaptation planners worldwide.

Connect to the Exam

CP (Concepts and Processes): Redlining, blockbusting, white flight, gentrification, smart growth, and urban heat island are all standard vocabulary items. Know the historical sequence: redlining (1930s) → restrictive covenants → white flight (1950s–60s) → urban disinvestment → gentrification (1990s–present).

SR (Spatial Relationships): Gentrification moves along corridors of amenity from already-gentrified neighborhoods — a spatial diffusion pattern. Redlining created spatially clustered zones of disinvestment that still correlate with income and racial geography today.

DA (Data Analysis): A HOLC map or census data showing racial wealth gaps may ask you to connect historical policy to present spatial outcomes. Practice reading color-coded risk maps and connecting them to mortgage access and homeownership rates.

SOA (Spatial Organization and Arrangement): Urban renewal's spatial logic — demolishing inner-city neighborhoods to build highways and stadiums — reorganized the Zone of Transition (Burgess) and pushed lower-income residents to new peripheral locations.

SC (Scale Analysis): Rotterdam's Waterplein operates at the local scale as a public plaza, city scale as stormwater infrastructure, national scale as part of Room for the River policy, and global scale as an exported climate-adaptation model. Bangladesh's shrimp-farming case shows the reverse: global carbon emissions → regional sea-level rise → local agricultural displacement → national urban migration stream.

Sample FRQ Prompt: Explain how federal housing policies enacted in the 1930s–1950s contributed to the racial segregation of American metropolitan areas that persists today. In your response, identify TWO specific policies or practices and explain the geographic mechanism through which each produced or reinforced residential segregation. Then explain ONE contemporary urban process that intersects with these legacies and describe its spatial pattern.

Cross-Section Connections

  • Topic 6.1 (Origins of Urban Settlements): The federal interstate highway system — a Borchert Auto-Air epoch investment — was also the instrument of urban renewal demolitions; the same infrastructure that built Sunbelt suburbs bisected communities of color in older industrial cities.
  • Topic 6.2 (Urban Hierarchies): Primate city concentration of services mirrors the domestic redlining dynamic at a global scale — investment flows to dominant nodes, leaving peripheral areas — whether peripheral urban neighborhoods or peripheral regions — starved of capital.
  • Topic 6.3 (Internal City Structure): Gentrification operates primarily in Burgess's Zone of Transition and Zone of Working-Class Housing — neighborhoods that experienced white flight and disinvestment now attract reinvestment as the rent gap widens.
  • Topic 5.x (Agriculture and Rural Land Use): The Bangladesh case study directly connects Topic 5 agricultural land-use change (rice-to-shrimp conversion) with Topic 6 urban migration pressure — a cross-unit chain that operates across multiple geographic scales and is a prime target for integrative FRQ questions.
