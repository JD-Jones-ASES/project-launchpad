AP Human Geography — Topic 1.1: The Cartographic Tradition: Maps, Projections, Scale

Core Question

How do the choices cartographers make — which projection to use, what scale to adopt, and what type of map to draw — shape what spatial knowledge is visible and what remains hidden? Maps are never neutral documents; every map encodes assumptions about which geographic properties deserve faithful representation and which can be sacrificed. Understanding these choices is the foundation of geographic literacy, because it reveals that spatial representations are arguments, not mirrors.

Key Concepts

  • Choropleth map: A thematic map that shades predefined geographic units with a graduated color scale proportional to a statistical variable, useful for showing regional patterns but prone to visual bias when large low-density units dominate the map.
  • Isoline map: A map that connects points of equal value with continuous lines (e.g., topographic contours, isotherms, isobars), best suited for representing continuous gradients across space.
  • Dot density map: A map that places a symbol at the location of each occurrence of a phenomenon to convey absolute distribution and the texture of spatial clustering.
  • Proportional symbol map: A map using symbols whose size scales to the magnitude of a variable at each location, not constrained by political unit boundaries.
  • Map projection: A mathematical transformation that converts the three-dimensional Earth onto a two-dimensional plane, requiring trade-offs among shape, area, distance, and direction — making projection selection an inherently political as well as technical act.
  • Cartographic scale: The ratio between a distance on the map and the corresponding distance on the ground; large-scale maps (small denominator) show small areas in great detail, while small-scale maps (large denominator) show large areas with little detail.
  • Analytical scale (scale of analysis): The spatial extent at which a geographic phenomenon is studied — local, regional, national, or global — with the insight that the same phenomenon can look entirely different depending on which level is applied.
  • Absolute location: The exact, mathematically fixed position of a place on Earth expressed in a coordinate system (latitude and longitude) that is universal and context-independent.
  • Relative location: A description of where a place is in relation to other places, transportation routes, or natural features; context-dependent and subject to change as surrounding geography changes.
  • Distance decay: The principle that interaction between two places tends to decrease as the distance between them increases, reflecting the economic, temporal, and psychological costs of crossing space.
  • Gravity model: A formula borrowed from Newtonian physics predicting that spatial interaction between two places is proportional to the product of their populations and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

Models / Theories / Geographers

  • Gerardus Mercator (1569): Flemish cartographer whose conformal projection preserved compass bearings (rhumb lines) for maritime navigation at the cost of severe area distortion that inflates high-latitude landmasses — Greenland appears roughly the same size as Africa, which is in reality about fourteen times larger.
  • Arno Peters / Gall-Peters projection (1973): An equal-area projection that preserves relative land area — restoring Africa and South America to their true continental scale — while severely distorting shape; promoted by Peters as an anti-colonial corrective to Mercator's Eurocentrism, generating sharp debate among professional cartographers.
  • Arthur Robinson projection (1963): A compromise projection developed on commission from Rand McNally that minimizes all distortions simultaneously without being fully conformal or equal-area; adopted by the National Geographic Society as its standard world map in 1988.
  • J. Paul Goode / Goode's Homolosine projection (1923): An interrupted projection that splits the ocean surfaces to flatten continental land masses with near-equal-area accuracy, shaped like a flattened orange peel and commonly used for global thematic maps of vegetation, climate, and biomes.
  • Gravity model (applied in geography): Adapted from Newton's law of gravitation, the model predicts interaction between cities as I = P₁P₂/d², and has been applied to migration flows, retail trade areas, telephone traffic, and airline passenger volumes.

Connect to the Exam

AP Human Geography tests cartographic literacy directly through stimulus-based questions. The Scale Analysis (SC) skill requires students to distinguish cartographic scale (the map ratio) from analytical scale (local/regional/national/global), a confusion that appears frequently on both multiple-choice and free-response items. The Data Analysis (DA) skill requires evaluating the limitations of a data type for a given geographic question — a choropleth of GIS-modeled flood risk is only as accurate as its underlying elevation model, and VGI-derived data may underrepresent rural or low-income communities. The Spatial Relationships (SR) skill applies when analyzing how map type choices affect which patterns are visible. The map-as-argument principle connects to Concepts and Processes (CP): students should approach every map stimulus by asking not just "what does this map show?" but also "what has this map been designed to make visible, and what has it obscured?"

Sample FRQ prompt: A school district in a large metropolitan area uses a choropleth map shaded by average test scores for each school district. (A) Describe ONE limitation of choropleth maps for representing this type of educational data. (B) Identify a different map type that would more accurately represent where high- and low-scoring students are concentrated, and explain why it is more appropriate. (C) Explain how changing the scale of analysis from the district level to the individual school level might change the geographic story the data tell.
