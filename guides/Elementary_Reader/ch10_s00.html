Chapter 10: Slavery and Freedom: Voices of Conscience

Overview

This section introduces the powerful voices of individuals who experienced slavery and dedicated their lives to fighting against it. Through autobiographical accounts, speeches, poetry, and fiction, readers gain insight into the profound injustices of slavery, the resilience of the human spirit, and the tireless struggle for freedom and equality. The readings highlight the diverse strategies used to resist oppression, from self-education to eloquent oratory and literary activism.

Key Concepts

  • Literacy as a Tool for Freedom: Frederick Douglass's narrative powerfully illustrates how gaining the ability to read and write was a crucial step towards mental and eventual physical emancipation, enabling him to understand his condition and articulate the injustice of slavery.
  • Challenging Stereotypes through Personal Experience: Sojourner Truth's "Ain't I a Woman?" speech masterfully dismantles prevailing gender and racial stereotypes by using her own life experiences as an enslaved woman to assert her strength, capabilities, and right to equal treatment.
  • The Emotional Toll of Slavery: John Greenleaf Whittier's poem, "The Farewell of a Virginia Slave Mother," uses a poignant literary device (refrain) to convey the unbearable grief and dehumanization inherent in the forced separation of families under slavery, aiming to evoke empathy in readers.
  • The Power of Empathy and Interracial Connection: Harriet Beecher Stowe's Uncle Tom's Cabin demonstrates how fictional narratives can humanize enslaved individuals and foster empathy, using the bond between Uncle Tom and Eva to highlight the moral bankruptcy of slavery and the potential for human connection beyond artificial boundaries.
  • Abolitionism and Activism: The lives and works of Douglass, Truth, Whittier, and Stowe exemplify the broad-based abolitionist movement, showcasing how different individuals used their unique talents—whether speaking, writing, or organizing—to advocate for the end of slavery and broader human rights.

Important Terms

  • Stratagems: (from Douglass) Clever tricks or plans, especially those used to deceive an enemy or achieve a goal. Douglass used these to teach himself.
  • Urchins: (from Douglass) Young children, often mischievous or poorly dressed. Douglass exchanged bread with these children for reading lessons.
  • Incompatible: (from Douglass) So different in nature as to be incapable of coexisting; unable to live or work together. Education and slavery were seen as incompatible by slaveholders.
  • Out of kilter: (from Truth) Out of order; not right or in proper balance. Sojourner Truth used this phrase to describe the societal state concerning rights.
  • Intellect: (from Truth) The ability to think and reason. Men at the convention argued women lacked sufficient intellect for equal rights.
  • Refuge: (from Whittier) A safe place; shelter. The slave mother hoped God would prove a refuge for her children.
  • Abolitionist: A person who supported or worked for the immediate termination of slavery. Douglass, Truth, Whittier, and Stowe were all significant figures in the abolitionist movement.
  • Refrain: (from Whittier) A literary device where a line or set of lines is repeated several times over the course of a poem or song. Whittier uses this to emphasize the mother's grief.
  • Sympathy: (from Stowe) Shared feeling or understanding with what is grand and noble. Eva possessed this quality, enhancing her reading of the Bible.
  • Arbor: (from Stowe) A shady garden shelter, typically made of a trellis or open framework covered with climbing plants. Tom and Eva sat in an arbor.

R01_DOUGLASS - Learning to Read (Adapted from Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass, 1845)

  • Context: In most Southern states, teaching enslaved people to read or write was illegal, as slaveholders feared literacy would make them harder to control.
  • Frederick Douglass's Background: Born into slavery in Maryland around 1818. Sent to live with the Auld family in Baltimore as a boy.
  • Learning to Read:
    • Lived with Master Hugh's family for about seven years.
    • Succeeded in learning to read and write through "various stratagems" without a regular teacher.
    • His mistress initially began to instruct him kindly but ceased upon her husband's advice, believing education and slavery were "incompatible."
    • Nothing made his mistress angrier than seeing him with a newspaper.
    • Method for Reading: Befriended little white boys in the street, converting many into teachers. He would carry bread to "bestow upon the hungry little urchins," who in return gave him the "more valuable bread of knowledge."
    • Desire for Freedom: Would tell the boys he wished to be as free as they would be at 21, troubling them and eliciting sympathy and hope for his freedom.
  • Learning to Write:
    • The idea came from observing carpenters at Durgin and Bailey's ship-yard, who wrote letters on timber.
    • Method for Writing: When he met boys who could write, he would claim he could write as well, prompting them to challenge him ("Let me see you try it"), thereby receiving lessons.
    • Tools: His "copy-book" was fences, brick walls, and pavements; his "pen and ink" was a lump of chalk.
  • Frederick Douglass (c.1818–1895) - Reading Context:
    • Escaped slavery in 1838.
    • Became one of America's most powerful speakers and writers.
    • His Narrative (1845) exposed the horrors of slavery to thousands.
    • Advised President Abraham Lincoln during the Civil War.
    • Spent his life fighting for the rights of all people.
  • Did You Know:
    • The quality of Douglass's Narrative led some to doubt it was written by a formerly enslaved man.
    • To prove its authenticity, Douglass included real names and places, putting him at risk of recapture.
    • Friends raised money to legally purchase his freedom, allowing him to continue his activism without fear.

R02_SOJOURNER_TRUTH - Ain't I a Woman? (Adapted from Speech at the Women's Convention, Akron, Ohio, 1851)

  • Before You Read Context: At the 1851 women's rights convention, men argued women were too delicate for equal rights. Sojourner Truth, a tall, strong formerly enslaved woman, responded.
  • Sojourner Truth's Background:
    • Born into slavery in New York around 1797 as Isabella Baumfree.
    • Gained freedom in 1826.
    • Became a powerful speaker for the rights of both enslaved people and women.
  • Speech Highlights:
    • Acknowledges the "racket" about rights between "negroes of the South and the women at the North," predicting trouble for "white men."
    • Challenges Gender Stereotypes: Directly contradicts the idea that women need special help ("helped into carriages," "lifted over ditches," "best place").
    • Uses Personal Experience: Declares, "Nobody ever helps me into carriages, or over mud-puddles, or gives me any best place! And ain't I a woman?"
    • Asserts Strength: Highlights her physical labor: "I have ploughed and planted, and gathered into barns, and no man could head me! And ain't I a woman?"
    • Endurance of Slavery: Notes her ability to "work as much and eat as much as a man—when I could get it—and bear the lash as well! And ain't I a woman?"
    • Motherhood and Grief: Recalls bearing "thirteen children" and seeing "most all sold off to slavery," with "none but Jesus heard me!" in her grief.
    • Addresses "Intellect" Argument: Uses a simple analogy: "If my cup won't hold but a pint, and yours holds a quart, wouldn't you be mean not to let me have my little half-measure full?" to argue for rights regardless of perceived intellectual capacity.
    • Addresses Religious Argument: Counters the claim that women can't have rights because "Christ wasn't a woman" by stating, "Where did your Christ come from? From God and a woman! Man had nothing to do with Him."
    • Call to Action: References Eve, the "first woman God ever made," being "strong enough to turn the world upside down all alone," suggesting that women together "ought to be able to turn it back, and get it right side up again!"
  • Sojourner Truth (c.1797–1883) - Reading Context:
    • Chose the name Sojourner Truth (traveler who speaks the truth).
    • Never learned to read or write, yet became one of the 19th century's most famous orators.
    • Spoke for both abolition and women's rights.
  • Did You Know:
    • The most widely known version of her speech was recorded from memory by Frances Gage 12 years after it was given.
    • Scholars debate the exact wording, but her extraordinary power and courage in silencing a hostile crowd with direct language are certain.

R03_WHITTIER - The Farewell of a Virginia Slave Mother (1838)

  • Before You Read Context: John Greenleaf Whittier, a Quaker and abolitionist, used poetry to change hearts and minds against slavery, particularly focusing on the anguish of families separated.
  • John Greenleaf Whittier's Background: Quaker poet and abolitionist who used his writing to fight against slavery.
  • Poem's Theme: Gives voice to a mother's grief as her children are sold away from her. Subtitled "To the Memory of Margaret Mercer."
  • Stanza 1:
    • Describes the children being sold to a "rice-swamp dank and lone"—a harsh, deadly place.
    • Details the suffering awaiting them: "slave-whip ceaseless swings," "noisome insect stings," "fever-demon strews Poison with the falling dews," "sickly sunbeams glare."
    • Refrain: "Gone, gone,—sold and gone, / To the rice-swamp dank and lone, / From Virginia's hills and waters;— / Woe is me, my stolen daughters!"
  • Stanza 2:
    • Emphasizes the mother's inability to comfort her children: "no mother's eye is near them," "no mother's ear can hear them."
    • Highlights the permanent pain: "Never, when the torturing lash / Seizes on their flesh, shall dash / Of their tears be wiped away / By a mother, night or day."
    • Repeats the refrain, reinforcing the growing grief.
  • Stanza 3:
    • Appeals to God for the children's well-being: "Oh, may He, to whom alone / All their cruel wrongs are known, / Still their hope and refuge prove, / With a more than mother's love."
    • Invokes God's holy love and mercy ("By the bruised reed He spareth").
    • Concludes with the refrain.
  • John Greenleaf Whittier (1807–1892) - Reading Context:
    • Quaker poet from Massachusetts.
    • Devoted much of his life to the abolitionist cause.
    • Wrote poems, pamphlets, and newspaper articles against slavery.
    • Helped found the American Anti-Slavery Society.
    • His Quaker faith taught him that all people are equal before God.
  • Did You Know:
    • The poem uses a refrain ("Gone, gone,—sold and gone") to create a mournful, echoing cry, making the reader feel the mother's growing grief.
    • Refrains are common in hymns, ballads, and spirituals.

R04_UNCLE_TOMS_CABIN - Eva and Uncle Tom (Adapted from Harriet Beecher Stowe, Uncle Tom's Cabin, 1852)

  • Before You Read Context: Harriet Beecher Stowe's 1852 novel Uncle Tom's Cabin profoundly impacted public opinion, turning millions against slavery through its compassionate portrayal of enslaved people.
  • Setting the Scene:
    • Uncle Tom, a faithful and deeply Christian man, has been sold from his Kentucky family to New Orleans.
    • He forms a tender friendship with his new master's young daughter, Eva.
    • The scene takes place on a Sunday evening in a garden by a lake.
  • The Friendship of Tom and Eva:
    • Their bond grew with Eva's growth.
    • Tom loved her as "something frail and earthly," yet "almost worshipped her as something heavenly and divine."
    • Tom frequently brought her gifts: rare bouquets from flower-stalls and choicest fruits.
    • Eva was a beautiful reader, possessing a "fine musical ear, a quick poetic fancy, and a sympathy with what is grand and noble," making her Bible readings particularly moving to Tom.
  • Garden Scene:
    • They are seated on a mossy seat in an arbor at the foot of the garden.
    • Eva's Bible is open on her knee.
    • She reads from the Bible: "And I saw a sea of glass, mingled with fire."
    • Eva connects this passage to the glassy water of the lake before them.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast the methods Frederick Douglass used to learn to read and write. What do these "stratagems" reveal about his determination and the obstacles he faced?
  2. How does Sojourner Truth effectively challenge the arguments against women's rights in her "Ain't I a Woman?" speech? Discuss her rhetorical strategies, particularly her use of personal experience and direct questions.
  3. Analyze the impact of the refrain in John Greenleaf Whittier's poem, "The Farewell of a Virginia Slave Mother." How does this literary device contribute to the poem's emotional power and its message against slavery?
  4. In the excerpt from Uncle Tom's Cabin, what does the friendship between Uncle Tom and Eva reveal about human connection and the artificial barriers created by slavery? How does Stowe use their relationship to evoke empathy?
  5. Across these four texts, what common themes or "voices of conscience" emerge regarding the fight against slavery and for freedom? How do these diverse forms (autobiography, speech, poetry, fiction) each contribute uniquely to understanding the historical period and the human experience of slavery?
