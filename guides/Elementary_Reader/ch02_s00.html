Wisdom and Fable: Lessons for the Young Study Guide

Overview

This section explores ancient wisdom traditions through two primary forms: fables and proverbs. Fables, often featuring animals, teach moral lessons through storytelling, while proverbs offer short, memorable sayings packed with practical advice. Together, these timeless literary forms provide insights into human behavior and guide individuals toward wise living, demonstrating their enduring relevance across cultures and generations.

Key Concepts

  • Fables: Short stories, often featuring anthropomorphic animals, that illustrate a moral lesson. They typically present clear examples of wise and foolish behavior, allowing readers to learn from characters' mistakes and successes.
  • Proverbs: Concise, wise sayings that offer advice, express a general truth, or provide practical guidance for daily life. They are often memorable due to their brevity and poetic structure.
  • Moral of the Story: The underlying lesson or principle that a fable or proverb aims to convey. This can be explicitly stated (as in many Aesop's fables) or implied.
  • Diligence and Preparation: The importance of hard work, planning, and preparing for future needs, contrasting with laziness and its consequences. Exemplified by "The Ants and the Grasshopper" and "Go to the Ant."
  • Humility vs. Pride: The dangers of arrogance and the wisdom of accepting limitations or treating others with respect. Illustrated by "The Fox and the Grapes" and "Pride goeth before destruction."
  • The Power of Kindness and Persuasion: The idea that gentle influence, empathy, and even small acts of goodness can be more effective and powerful than force or outward strength. Shown in "The North Wind and the Sun" and "The Lion and the Mouse."
  • Timeless Wisdom: The enduring nature of these ancient lessons, which continue to resonate and offer valuable guidance to people in diverse cultures throughout history, from Aesop and King Solomon to the Panchatantra and Anansi stories.

Important Terms

  • diligent: (adj.) Showing careful and persistent effort.
  • prudent: (adj.) Showing good judgment and careful thought.
  • quarry: (n.) An animal that is hunted; prey.
  • rebuke: (v., n.) To criticize sharply; a sharp criticism.
  • sluggard: (n.) A lazy, idle person.
  • vain: (adj.) Having too much pride in one's appearance or abilities; also, useless or fruitless.

Detailed Notes

Introduction: Wisdom and Fable

  • This unit focuses on learning wisdom from the experiences and mistakes of others.
  • It introduces two literary forms used for teaching lessons: fables (stories with animal characters) and proverbs (short, memorable sayings).
  • These ancient forms of wisdom are presented as being as relevant today as they were thousands of years ago.

Fables by Aesop (Part 1)

The Ants and the Grasshopper

  • Characters: Diligent Ants and a lazy Grasshopper.
  • Plot:
    • During summer, the Ants diligently gather and store grain.
    • The Grasshopper spends the summer singing, neglecting to prepare for winter.
    • In winter, the Grasshopper is starving and begs the Ants for food.
    • The Ants rebuke him for his idleness, telling him he must face the consequences of his lack of preparation.
  • Moral: "It is best to prepare for the days of necessity." This fable highlights the importance of hard work, foresight, and planning for the future.

The Fox and the Grapes

  • Characters: A famished Fox.
  • Plot:
    • The Fox sees ripe black grapes hanging from a vine and tries various tricks to reach them.
    • After much effort, she fails to reach the grapes.
    • To hide her disappointment, she dismisses the grapes as "sour" and "not ripe."
  • Moral: "It is easy to despise what you cannot get." This fable illustrates how people often rationalize their failures or dismiss things they desire but cannot obtain, rather than admitting defeat.

Did You Know? - Global Fables

  • Fables are found in almost every culture worldwide.
  • India: The ancient collection Panchatantra uses animal stories to teach wisdom, similar to Aesop.
  • West Africa: Anansi the Spider is a famous trickster figure who outsmarts larger animals in traditional tales.
  • These examples demonstrate the universal human practice of using animal stories to convey life lessons.

Proverbs from the King James Version

What is a Proverb?

  • A proverb is defined as a short, wise saying that offers advice for daily living.
  • The Book of Proverbs in the Bible contains hundreds of such sayings.

"Go to the Ant" (Proverbs 6:6–11)

  • Audience: Addressed to the "sluggard" (lazy person).
  • Advice: Observe the ant's behavior and learn wisdom from it.
  • Ant's Example: Ants diligently gather food in summer and harvest without needing a "guide, overseer, or ruler." They are self-motivated and self-sufficient.
  • Warning: Continued laziness (sleep, slumber, folding of hands) will lead to poverty and want, described vividly as an approaching traveler or armed man.
  • Lesson: Emphasizes diligence, self-reliance, and avoiding laziness to prevent hardship.

Wise Sayings (Selected Proverbs)

  • Proverbs 15:1: "A soft answer turneth away wrath: but grievous words stir up anger." Teaches the power of gentle communication to de-escalate conflict.
  • Proverbs 16:18: "Pride goeth before destruction, and an haughty spirit before a fall." Warns against arrogance and excessive pride, predicting a downfall for those who possess it.
  • Proverbs 17:17: "A friend loveth at all times, and a brother is born for adversity." Highlights the loyalty and enduring nature of true friendship, especially in difficult times.
  • Proverbs 22:1: "A good name is rather to be chosen than great riches, and loving favour rather than silver and gold." Emphasizes the supreme value of reputation and goodwill over material wealth.
  • Proverbs 27:17: "Iron sharpeneth iron; so a man sharpeneth the countenance of his friend." Suggests that friends can positively influence and improve each other through interaction and constructive feedback.

About the Book of Proverbs

  • Attribution: Traditionally attributed to King Solomon, renowned for his wisdom (1 Kings 4:32 mentions him speaking thousands of proverbs).
  • Purpose: Written primarily to educate young people on how to live wisely.
  • Relevance: Its advice on topics like hard work, honesty, and friendship remains valuable and applicable today.

Fables by Aesop (Part 2)

The North Wind and the Sun

  • Characters: The North Wind and the Sun.
  • Conflict: They dispute who is more powerful, agreeing to test their strength by trying to remove a traveler's cloak.
  • Wind's Attempt: The North Wind blows with all its might, but the traveler only wraps his cloak tighter. The Wind fails.
  • Sun's Attempt: The Sun shines gently with warmth. The traveler, feeling the heat, voluntarily takes off his garments and even bathes.
  • Moral: "Persuasion is better than force." This fable teaches that gentle influence and warmth are often more effective in achieving desired outcomes than aggression or brute strength.

The Lion and the Mouse

  • Characters: A powerful Lion and a small Mouse.
  • Plot:
    • A Mouse accidentally wakes a sleeping Lion. The Lion, angered, catches the Mouse and is about to kill him.
    • The Mouse piteously begs for his life, promising to repay the kindness. The Lion laughs at the idea but lets him go.
    • Later, the Lion is trapped by hunters.
    • The Mouse, recognizing the Lion's roar, comes and gnaws through the ropes, freeing the Lion.
  • Moral: "No act of kindness, no matter how small, is ever wasted." This fable illustrates that even the smallest beings can offer significant help, and kindness, even when seemingly insignificant, can yield unexpected and valuable rewards. It also highlights not underestimating others.

Did You Know? - Fables' Enduring Legacy

  • Jean de La Fontaine: A 17th-century French poet who retold many of Aesop's fables in verse, still memorized by French schoolchildren.
  • Longevity: Aesop's stories have been retold for over 2,500 years, proving their lasting appeal and power as "good stories."

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast fables and proverbs as tools for teaching wisdom. What are the unique strengths of each form?
  2. Analyze the theme of diligence and the consequences of laziness as presented in "The Ants and the Grasshopper" and the proverb "Go to the Ant." How do these texts reinforce similar messages?
  3. Discuss how "The Fox and the Grapes" and the proverb "Pride goeth before destruction" reflect different aspects of human pride or self-deception.
  4. Explain the different types of "strength" or "power" demonstrated in "The North Wind and the Sun" and "The Lion and the Mouse." What common lesson do these fables teach about effective interaction with others?
  5. Given that many of these fables and proverbs are thousands of years old and originate from diverse cultures, what makes their lessons so universally understood and continuously relevant today?
