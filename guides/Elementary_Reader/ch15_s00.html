Study Guide: Character and Courage: Moral Tales

Overview

This section explores the idea that stories can guide us on how to live a good and virtuous life. Through an adaptation of Louisa May Alcott's Little Women, an excerpt from John Bunyan's The Pilgrim's Progress, and the wisdom of Christina Rossetti's poems alongside Jesus' Beatitudes, we examine themes of moral development, spiritual journeys, and the pursuit of goodness amidst life's challenges.

Key Concepts

  • Life as a Pilgrimage: The overarching metaphor in this unit, suggesting that life is a journey with spiritual goals, challenges ("burdens"), and a destination ("Celestial City" or "peace") that represents goodness and happiness. This idea is central to both Little Women and The Pilgrim's Progress.
  • Inner Character and Self-Conquest: The importance of developing virtues and overcoming personal faults. Father's letter in Little Women explicitly calls for fighting "bosom enemies" and "conquering themselves," aligning with the pursuit of righteousness.
  • Poverty and Spiritual Riches: The texts explore different dimensions of "poverty." In Little Women, the sisters lament their material poverty but learn to find joy in family and generosity. The Beatitudes bless the "poor in spirit," suggesting that humility and spiritual need are paths to the "kingdom of heaven."
  • Allegory as a Teaching Tool: The Pilgrim's Progress is presented as an allegory, a story where characters, events, and settings symbolize deeper moral or spiritual truths. This literary device allows for profound lessons to be conveyed through narrative.
  • Perceiving the Unseen: Rossetti's "Who Has Seen the Wind?" highlights how invisible forces can be known through their effects. Similarly, the spiritual truths in the Beatitudes and the moral growth in Little Women are often felt and observed through actions rather than seen directly.
  • Virtues and Blessings: The Beatitudes outline specific virtues—humility ("poor in spirit"), empathy ("they that mourn"), gentleness ("meek"), spiritual hunger ("hunger and thirst after righteousness"), compassion ("merciful"), sincerity ("pure in heart"), peacemaking, and resilience in the face of persecution—and promise profound blessings for those who embody them.
  • Grace and Redemption: In The Pilgrim's Progress, Christian's admission through the Wicket Gate by Good-will, despite his "undeserving" past, illustrates the concept of grace and the opportunity for new beginnings on the spiritual path.

Important Terms

  • Allegory: A story, poem, or picture that can be interpreted to reveal a hidden meaning, typically a moral or political one. In this unit, The Pilgrim's Progress is a prime example, where characters and events represent spiritual truths.
  • Blessed: Favored by God; happy in a deep, spiritual way. This term is central to the Beatitudes, describing the profound well-being granted to those who live virtuously.
  • Burden: A heavy load, either physical or spiritual; something difficult to bear. In the context of Little Women and The Pilgrim's Progress, it represents life's challenges, sins, or spiritual struggles.
  • Meek: Gentle, humble, and patient; not easily angered or provoked. One of the virtues praised in the Beatitudes, associated with inheriting the earth.
  • Pilgrim: A person who journeys to a sacred place for religious reasons; metaphorically, a person on a spiritual journey. The concept frames the moral development of the March sisters and Christian's quest.
  • Righteousness: The quality of being morally right and just. The Beatitudes emphasize hungering and thirsting for righteousness as a path to spiritual fulfillment.

Detailed Notes

Chapter 15: Character and Courage: Moral Tales

This unit uses classic literature and scripture to explore how stories teach us to live meaningful lives, focusing on character development, moral choices, and the spiritual journey.

I. From Little Women: "Playing Pilgrims" (Louisa May Alcott, 1868)

  • Historical Context: Written shortly after the American Civil War, reflecting a time of hardship and absence for many families. Alcott based the story on her own family experiences.
  • The March Sisters' Personalities and Initial Complaints:
    • Jo: Grumbles about the lack of presents, expressing frustration with poverty. (Tomboyish writer, based on Alcott herself).
    • Meg: Sighs over her old dress, feeling the sting of poverty compared to others. (Older sister, based on Anna).
    • Amy: Complains about the unfairness of some having "plenty of pretty things" while others have "nothing at all," showing a childlike sense of injustice. (Youngest, becomes a painter, based on May).
    • Beth: Contentedly reminds them of their blessings (Father, Mother, each other), showing her gentle and compassionate nature. (Shy, dies young, based on Elizabeth).
  • Theme of Sacrifice and Generosity: Meg explains their mother's reason for no presents – to save money during a hard winter when "our men are suffering so in the army." Beth proposes they each buy a gift for their mother instead of themselves, demonstrating selflessness.
  • Father's Letter and Moral Guidance: The letter from their father in the army is a powerful message of love, faith, and encouragement. He asks them to be "loving children," "do their duty faithfully," "fight their bosom enemies bravely," and "conquer themselves beautifully."
    • "Bosom enemies" refer to inner faults, vices, or negative tendencies (e.g., Jo's temper, Meg's vanity, Amy's selfishness).
    • "Conquer themselves" emphasizes self-improvement and moral growth.
  • The Pilgrim's Metaphor: Mrs. March uses the childhood game of "Pilgrim's Progress" to explain life's moral journey.
    • Their "burdens" (challenges, faults) are real.
    • Their "road is before us" (life's path).
    • Their "longing for goodness and happiness" is their guide.
    • The goal is "peace which is a true Celestial City" (a state of spiritual fulfillment and contentment, not just a physical place).
  • Author Spotlight: Louisa May Alcott (1832–1888):
    • Grew up in Concord, Massachusetts, amidst prominent literary figures.
    • Her father, Bronson Alcott, was a philosopher but struggled financially.
    • Louisa worked various jobs before finding success with Little Women.
    • The novel is semi-autobiographical, with the sisters mirroring her own family.

II. From The Pilgrim's Progress: "The Wicket Gate" (John Bunyan, 1678)

  • Allegory as a Literary Form: This text is a classic allegory, a story where every character, event, and object has a symbolic meaning representing deeper moral or spiritual truths.
  • Christian's Journey:
    • The Character: Christian, burdened by sin (represented by a heavy load on his back).
    • Starting Point: The "City of Destruction" (symbolizes the sinful, worldly life).
    • Destination: "Mount Zion" or the "Celestial City" (symbolizes salvation, heaven, or spiritual perfection).
    • Guidance: Evangelist points him toward a distant gate.
  • Encounter at the Wicket Gate:
    • Christian reaches the gate, over which is written, "Knock, and it shall be opened unto you."
    • He knocks repeatedly, expressing his sorrow and undeserving nature as a "rebel."
    • A "grave person" named Good-will (representing God's grace or willingness to receive sinners) asks his identity and purpose.
    • Christian identifies himself as a "poor burdened sinner" from the City of Destruction, seeking deliverance from "wrath to come."
  • The Welcome and the Danger:
    • Good-will willingly opens the gate, pulling Christian in.
    • The pull is explained: "Beelzebub is the captain" of a "strong castle" nearby, from which arrows are shot at those approaching the gate, hoping to prevent their entry.
    • Christian experiences mixed emotions: "I rejoice and tremble," recognizing both the safety of entry and the peril overcome.
    • Good-will assures him: "An open door is set before thee, and no man can shut it."
  • Spiritual Significance: This passage symbolizes the initial step in the Christian faith journey—repentance, seeking salvation, and being received by divine grace despite past sins, while acknowledging the constant spiritual warfare against evil forces.
  • Author Spotlight: John Bunyan (1628–1688):
    • An English "tinker" (mender of pots and kettles) who became a powerful preacher.
    • Imprisoned for 12 years for preaching without a government license.
    • Wrote The Pilgrim's Progress during his imprisonment.
    • The book became one of the most widely read in English after the Bible, translated into over 200 languages.

III. Poems and The Beatitudes: Christina Rossetti and Jesus

  • Context: This section brings together a Victorian poet's reflections on the unseen and offerings, with Jesus' foundational teachings on blessedness.

A. Christina Rossetti's Poetry

  • "Who Has Seen the Wind?":
    • A short, two-stanza poem that addresses the invisible nature of the wind.
    • It poses the question, then answers that while no one sees it directly, its presence is known by its effects: leaves trembling, trees bowing.
    • This reflects how many important forces or spiritual realities might not be tangible but are evident through their impact.
  • "In the Bleak Midwinter":
    • A beloved Christmas carol, originally a poem.
    • The first stanza paints a stark, cold picture of the winter setting of Christ's birth ("Earth stood hard as iron, Water like a stone").
    • The second stanza shifts to a personal reflection: what gift can "poor as I am" give to the Christ child?
    • It considers and rejects material gifts ("a lamb," "do my part" as a wise man) in favor of the ultimate offering: "Give my heart." This emphasizes the value of sincere devotion over material wealth.
  • Author Spotlight: Christina Rossetti (1830–1894):
    • Born in London to an artistic and literary family.
    • Devoted much of her life to her Anglican faith, which heavily influenced her poetry.
    • Her poems often explore themes of devotion, sacrifice, and the natural world.
    • "In the Bleak Midwinter" was set to music by Gustav Holst and became a famous Christmas carol.

B. The Beatitudes (Matthew 5:3–12, King James Version)

  • Context: These are the opening statements of Jesus' Sermon on the Mount, outlining the qualities of truly blessed people. They are counter-cultural, valuing humility and spiritual focus over worldly success.
  • Key Beatitudes and Their Meanings:
    • "Blessed are the poor in spirit: for theirs is the kingdom of heaven." — Not literal poverty, but spiritual humility, recognizing one's need for God.
    • "Blessed are they that mourn: for they shall be comforted." — Mourning for sin, injustice, or suffering, which leads to spiritual comfort and healing.
    • "Blessed are the meek: for they shall inherit the earth." — Gentle, humble, and patient individuals, who are not aggressive but trust in God's providence.
    • "Blessed are they which do hunger and thirst after righteousness: for they shall be filled." — A deep longing for moral rightness and justice, which will be satisfied spiritually.
    • "Blessed are the merciful: for they shall obtain mercy." — Showing compassion and forgiveness to others, which will be returned.
    • "Blessed are the pure in heart: for they shall see God." — Those with sincerity and integrity, whose motives are untainted, will have a clear spiritual vision.
    • "Blessed are the peacemakers: for they shall be called the children of God." — Those who actively work to resolve conflict and foster harmony.
    • "Blessed are they which are persecuted for righteousness' sake: for theirs is the kingdom of heaven." — Enduring suffering or ill-treatment for upholding moral or spiritual principles, receiving ultimate reward.
  • Overall Message: The Beatitudes describe a radical ethic focused on internal character, spiritual values, and selfless actions, promising profound spiritual rewards and a unique form of happiness.

Review Questions

  1. In "Playing Pilgrims," how do the personalities of Jo, Meg, Amy, and Beth reveal themselves through their initial reactions to not having presents? How does Beth's suggestion change the dynamic of the conversation?
  2. Mrs. March compares life to the game of "Pilgrim's Progress." Explain what she means by "burdens," "road," and "Celestial City" in the context of their family's experiences and moral development.
  3. The Pilgrim's Progress is described as an allegory. What does this mean, and what do Christian's "heavy burden" and the character "Good-will" at the Wicket Gate symbolize in a spiritual context?
  4. Compare and contrast the themes in Christina Rossetti's "Who Has Seen the Wind?" and "In the Bleak Midwinter." How do both poems explore ideas of perception, presence, and offering?
  5. Choose three Beatitudes from Matthew 5:3–12. Explain what each one means in practical terms for living a good life, and discuss how these teachings connect with the moral lessons found in Little Women or The Pilgrim's Progress.
