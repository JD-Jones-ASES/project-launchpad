Exploring the American West: Frontier and Expansion

Overview

This section delves into the early American westward movement, highlighting the spirit of exploration, the arduous journey of pioneers, and the cultural expressions that emerged from life on the frontier. It examines the landmark Lewis and Clark expedition's discoveries, the challenging experiences of settlers on the Oregon Trail, and the resilience and humor captured in traditional frontier folk songs.

Key Concepts

  • Westward Expansion: The historical movement of Americans beyond the established eastern settlements, particularly after the nation's formation, driven by the lure of vast unexplored territories beyond the Appalachian Mountains.
  • Exploration and Discovery: The vital role of expeditions, such as that of Lewis and Clark, in mapping, documenting, and providing the first detailed descriptions of the landscapes, flora, fauna, and indigenous peoples of the newly acquired Western lands.
  • The American Frontier: The concept of the "edge of settled land" or "known world" that continuously shifted westward, representing both opportunity and immense challenge for those seeking new lives.
  • Pioneer Experience: The demanding and often perilous journey undertaken by emigrants along routes like the Oregon Trail, characterized by physical hardship, determination, and the collective effort of wagon trains.
  • Cultural Adaptation and Expression: How pioneers used traditional folk songs to cope with the isolation and difficulties of the journey, providing entertainment, expressing hopes and fears, and preserving stories of their experiences, often with humor or longing.

Important Terms

  • Emigrant: A person who leaves one country or region to live in another.
  • Expedition: A journey undertaken for a specific purpose, especially exploration.
  • Frontier: The border of settled land; the edge of the known world.
  • Prairie: A large, open area of grassland.
  • Wilderness: A wild, uncultivated region with no human inhabitants.
  • Lewis and Clark Expedition: A historic journey (1804-1806) led by Meriwether Lewis and William Clark, commissioned by President Thomas Jefferson, to explore the vast territory west of the Mississippi River.
  • Rocky Mountains: A major mountain range in western North America, representing a significant natural barrier and landmark encountered by early explorers and pioneers.
  • Great Falls of the Missouri River: A series of five major waterfalls on the Missouri River in present-day Montana, a significant natural wonder documented by Lewis and Clark.
  • Oregon Trail: A 2,000-mile historic route used by hundreds of thousands of American pioneers in the 1840s to travel from Missouri to the Pacific Northwest.
  • St. Louis: A major city in Missouri, serving as a significant starting point and hub for westward bound emigrants and traders in the 19th century.
  • Independence, Missouri: A town located on the extreme frontier of Missouri, serving as a common rendezvous point for emigrant parties preparing to embark on the Oregon Trail.
  • Sacagawea: A young Shoshone woman who served as an invaluable interpreter and guide for the Lewis and Clark Expedition, her presence signaling peaceful intentions to Native American tribes.
  • Folk Songs: Traditional songs that are passed down orally from person to person, often changing over time, reflecting the experiences and culture of a community.
  • Wagon Trains: Groups of covered wagons traveling together across the plains and mountains, providing safety and companionship for emigrants heading West.

Detailed Notes

Introduction: The Call of the West

After the United States was established, Americans began to look westward beyond the Appalachian Mountains. This vast territory, with its prairies, mountains, and seemingly endless frontier, captivated the national imagination. This period saw explorers venturing into unknown lands, pioneers undertaking arduous journeys to settle new territories, and the creation of cultural expressions like folk songs that captured the spirit of the age.

The Lewis and Clark Expedition (1804-1806)

  • Mission and Scope: President Thomas Jefferson commissioned Meriwether Lewis and William Clark in 1804 to explore the newly acquired Louisiana Purchase territory west of the Mississippi River. Their expedition lasted over two years and covered more than eight thousand miles, charting the region and establishing relations with Native American tribes.
  • Meriwether Lewis's Journals: Lewis meticulously documented the expedition's findings, providing detailed accounts of the landscapes, newly discovered animals, and the various indigenous peoples they encountered.
    • First Sight of the Rocky Mountains (May 26, 1805): Lewis recounted his "secret pleasure" upon first seeing the distant, snow-capped peaks. This joy was quickly tempered by the realization of the immense "difficulties" and "sufferings and hardships" that crossing this "snowy barrier" would entail, anticipating the challenges on their path to the Pacific.
    • The Great Falls of the Missouri River (June 13, 1805): Lewis described his awe at this "sublimely grand spectacle," hearing the falls before seeing the spray rise "like a column of smoke." He vividly depicted the "perfect white foam" that formed "a thousand forms" and the "beautiful rainbow" produced by the sun's reflection on the spray, adding to the "majestically grand scenery."
  • Key Individuals and Discoveries:
    • Meriwether Lewis: A Virginia soldier and President Jefferson's personal secretary, chosen for his courage, wilderness expertise, and naturalist skills. His journals were crucial for understanding the American West.
    • Expedition Composition: Included about 45 people and Lewis's Newfoundland dog, Seaman.
    • Scientific Contributions: Discovered over 170 plants and 120 animals new to Western science, including the grizzly bear, prairie dog, and pronghorn antelope.
    • Sacagawea: A young Shoshone woman who served as an essential interpreter and guide. Her presence, along with her infant son, was vital in signaling peaceful intentions to the Native American communities they met along the way.

The Oregon Trail (1840s)

  • The Great Migration: In the 1840s, thousands of Americans embarked on the Oregon Trail, a challenging two-thousand-mile journey from Missouri to the Pacific Northwest, seeking new opportunities and land.
  • Francis Parkman's Account: In 1846, a young Harvard student named Francis Parkman traveled part of the trail, documenting his experiences in his influential book, The Oregon Trail.
    • St. Louis as a Departure Hub: Parkman described St. Louis in the spring of 1846 as a bustling center. It was crowded with emigrants heading to Oregon and California, as well as traders bound for Santa Fe. Hotels, gunsmiths, and saddlers were busy, and steamboats frequently departed up the Missouri River, laden with passengers and supplies.
    • The Journey Begins: Parkman and a friend departed St. Louis on April 28. Their steamboat was heavily loaded with emigrant provisions, animals, and supplies, struggling against the rapid, "turbid" current of the Missouri River, often getting stuck on snags and sand-bars for days.
    • Rendezvous at Independence: Along the Missouri, they began seeing signs of the westward movement, with emigrant parties camped along the banks, all making their way to Independence, Missouri. Independence served as the "common rendezvous" and the "extreme frontier" before the open prairies.
    • First Obstacles on the Trail: Parkman's initial experience on the trail was immediately challenging. As they prepared to leave, a shaft mule bucked and nearly overturned their cart. Soon after leaving Westport (where they acquired a new mule from a descendant of Daniel Boone), their cart became stuck in a deep, muddy gully, providing an immediate "foretaste of prairie experience."
  • Francis Parkman's Legacy: Though suffering from poor health, Parkman's determination led him to experience the frontier firsthand, producing one of America's great travel books. He later became a celebrated historian, documenting the colonial struggles for North America.

Frontier Songs

  • Purpose and Origin: As pioneers traveled westward, folk songs provided entertainment, boosted morale, and helped pass the time during long journeys. These songs were primarily oral traditions, learned by listening, with words often changing from singer to singer.
  • "Home on the Range":
    • Theme: This song celebrates the serene beauty and freedom of the open Western landscape.
    • Imagery: It evokes images of buffalo, deer, and antelope roaming freely, pure air, gentle breezes, and picturesque streams.
    • Sentiment: The lyrics express a deep longing for a simple, natural life on the range, valuing it above "all of the cities so bright." It became an iconic anthem for the American West.
  • "Sweet Betsy from Pike":
    • Theme: This humorous folk song recounts the hardships and comical misadventures of a pioneer couple, Betsy and Ike, on their journey west.
    • Narrative: It tells of their departure from "Pike County" with their animals, their struggle through the desert where Betsy "gave out," and the gradual loss of their animals and provisions (the Shanghai rooster ran off, cattle died, bacon ran out).
    • Tone: Despite the challenges, the song maintains a lighthearted and resilient tone, typical of frontier humor which helped travelers cope with difficulties.
  • The Importance of Trail Songs:
    • Entertainment and Morale: With wagon trains traveling only about fifteen miles a day (making the trip 4-6 months), singing was a crucial form of entertainment and helped keep spirits up during the long, arduous journey.
    • Community Building: Families gathered around campfires at night to share meals, stories, and songs, fostering a sense of community.
    • Reflection of Experience: Songs like "Home on the Range" conveyed the aspiration for a new life in the wide-open spaces, while humorous songs like "Sweet Betsy from Pike" allowed pioneers to laugh at their own struggles and hardships.

Review Questions

  1. What was the primary goal of the Lewis and Clark expedition, and how did Meriwether Lewis's journal entries help fulfill that goal?
  2. Upon first seeing the Rocky Mountains, Lewis experienced both "secret pleasure" and reflections on "difficulties." What do these contrasting feelings tell us about the nature of westward expansion?
  3. Describe the atmosphere and activities in St. Louis in the spring of 1846 as observed by Francis Parkman. How did this setting reflect the broader movement of westward migration?
  4. Compare and contrast the portrayal of frontier life in "Home on the Range" with that in "Sweet Betsy from Pike." What different aspects of the pioneer experience does each song emphasize?
  5. Why do you think folk songs were such an important part of pioneer life on the trails, and how did they contribute to the travelers' mental and emotional well-being?
