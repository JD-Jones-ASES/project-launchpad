Tales of Adventure: Stories from Far and Wide Study Guide

Overview

This section embarks on a journey through classic tales of daring and self-discovery. Students will delve into the thrilling world of pirates with an excerpt from Treasure Island, witness the heroic bravery of a mongoose in Rikki-Tikki-Tavi from The Jungle Book, and reflect on the virtues of a well-lived life through Rudyard Kipling's timeless poem, "If—". Together, these selections explore themes of courage, resilience, and the essence of character in the face of extraordinary circumstances.

Key Concepts

  • Adventure and Intrigue: The thrill of the unknown, the pursuit of treasure, and the arrival of mysterious strangers that set epic journeys in motion.
  • Bravery and Loyalty: The courage to face dangerous foes, the protective instincts for one's adopted family, and the unwavering commitment to a cause.
  • Character and Resilience: The development of personal integrity, the ability to maintain composure under pressure, and the strength to rebuild after setbacks.
  • Authorial Craft: How writers like Robert Louis Stevenson and Rudyard Kipling use vivid descriptions, suspense, and evocative language to engage readers and convey deeper meanings.
  • Coming of Age: The subtle lessons embedded in stories and poems that guide young readers towards understanding what it means to be a responsible, honorable individual.

Important Terms

  • Sea-chest: A sailor's trunk for personal belongings, often used to carry valuables.
  • Hand-barrow: A frame with handles, carried by hand, used for transporting loads.
  • Tarry pigtail: A traditional sailor's hairstyle, often braided and stiffened with tar, suggesting a life at sea.
  • Sabre: A heavy cavalry sword with a curved blade and a single cutting edge.
  • Cove: A small, sheltered bay or inlet on a coast.
  • Grog-shop: An old-fashioned term for a tavern or bar where alcoholic drinks are served.
  • Berth: A place to sleep or dock; in this context, a suitable lodging place.
  • Weather-eye: An idiom meaning to be watchful or alert, especially for danger.
  • Spanish Main: The coastal region of mainland America colonized by Spain, known historically for pirate activity.
  • Buccaneers: Pirates, especially those who preyed on Spanish shipping in the West Indies during the 17th and 18th centuries.
  • Mongoose: A small, carnivorous mammal native to Asia and Africa, known for its ability to hunt and kill snakes.
  • Burrow: A hole or tunnel dug by a small animal, such as a rabbit, for shelter.
  • Bungalow: A low house, usually with a single story, from India.
  • Cantonment: A military station or permanent military quarters in India.
  • Tailorbird: A small Old World bird that stitches leaves together to make its nest.
  • Muskrat: A large, semi-aquatic rodent.
  • Cobra: A highly venomous snake native to Africa and Asia, known for its hood.
  • Fledglings: Young birds that have just left the nest.
  • Valiant: Possessing or showing courage or determination.
  • Sluice: A sliding gate or other device for controlling the flow of water.
  • Veranda: A roofed, open-air gallery or porch attached to the exterior of a building.
  • Brood: A family of young animals, especially birds, produced at one hatching or birth.
  • Impostors: People who pretend to be someone else to deceive others; in the poem, it refers to success and failure being deceptive in their true value.
  • Knaves: Dishonest or unscrupulous men.
  • Pitch-and-toss: A simple gambling game played with coins, involving pitching them towards a mark or tossing them in the air.

Detailed Notes

The Old Sea-Dog (Adapted from Robert Louis Stevenson, Treasure Island)

  • Introduction: The story begins with young Jim Hawkins, the narrator, at his family's inn. The arrival of a mysterious stranger sets the scene for adventure.
  • Captain Bill Bones's Arrival:
    • He is described as a "tall, strong, heavy, nut-brown man" with a "tarry pigtail," scarred hands, and a "sabre cut" on his cheek. His appearance immediately suggests a rough, seafaring past.
    • He arrives with a sea-chest and requests rum, finding the inn a "handy cove" and "pleasant grog-shop" with little company, which suits his secretive nature.
    • He instructs Jim's father to call him "captain" and settles in, demanding rum, bacon, and eggs.
  • Captain's Habits and Secrets:
    • He is generally silent but spends his days scouting the cove with a brass telescope.
    • He bribes Jim with a silver fourpenny to keep a "weather-eye open for a seafaring man with one leg," indicating he is in hiding or fears someone from his past.
    • On some nights, he sings "wicked, old, wild sea-songs" or forces company to listen to his dreadful stories.
    • His stories are about grim topics like hanging, walking the plank, storms at sea, and "wild deeds" on the Spanish Main, revealing his past among "wickedest buccaneers."
  • Author's Background: Robert Louis Stevenson:
    • Scottish writer (1850–1894) with a love for adventure, despite lifelong poor health.
    • Treasure Island (1883) was inspired by a treasure map he drew with his stepson.
    • Also known for A Child's Garden of Verses and Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde.
    • Did You Know: Treasure Island initially began as a game and was serialized in a children's magazine as "The Sea Cook" before becoming the novel.

Rikki-Tikki-Tavi (Part 1 & 2) (Adapted from Rudyard Kipling, The Jungle Book)

  • Introduction to Mongooses: Small, fierce animals from Asia and Africa, famous for their bravery against deadly snakes like cobras.
  • Rikki-Tikki-Tavi's Character:
    • A young mongoose, described as cat-like in fur and tail but weasel-like in head and habits.
    • Known for his pink eyes, restless nose, ability to fluff his tail like a bottle brush, and his distinctive "Rikk-tikk-tikki-tikki-tchk!" war cry.
    • Eaten up with curiosity, his family motto is "Run and find out," making him fearless.
  • Origin and New Home:
    • A high summer flood washes him out of his burrow.
    • He is discovered by a human family (the "big man," his wife, and their son Teddy) and nursed back to health, becoming their protector.
    • The setting is a large bungalow garden in Segowlee cantonment, India.
  • Encountering the Enemy:
    • Rikki-tikki explores the garden and hears the sorrowful Darzee the Tailorbird and his wife.
    • They reveal that Nag, a large black cobra (five feet long), ate one of their babies.
    • Nag appears, arrogant and menacing, warning Rikki-tikki to "look, and be afraid!"
    • Rikki-tikki, despite never having met a cobra, instinctively knows his life's purpose is to fight snakes, and his tail fluffs up in defiance.
  • The War Begins (Part 2):
    • The Cobras' Plan: Rikki overhears Nag and his mate, Nagaina, planning to kill the human family to drive the mongoose away and reclaim the garden.
    • Nag's Attack: Nag sneaks into the bathroom through a sluice and coils himself around a water jar, waiting to strike the big man at dawn.
    • Rikki's Counterattack: Rikki-tikki patiently waits, then jumps, aiming for Nag's head. He holds on fiercely despite being violently shaken.
    • Crucial Aid: The "big man" awakes and fires both barrels of a shotgun, killing Nag and saving Rikki-tikki's life.
    • Nagaina's Retribution and Rikki's Strategy:
      • Rikki-tikki is stiff but resolute: "Now I have Nagaina to settle with."
      • He finds Nagaina's twenty-five eggs in the melon bed and crushes all but one.
      • Darzee's wife warns that Nagaina is attacking Teddy on the veranda.
      • Nagaina, coiled by Teddy's chair, threatens to strike.
      • Rikki-tikki confronts her, holding up her last egg as leverage.
      • Nagaina seizes the egg and flees for her hole, with Rikki-tikki in hot pursuit—a very risky move for a mongoose.
    • Triumph: Rikki-tikki emerges from the hole, dirty but victorious, announcing, "It is all over. The widow will never come out again."
  • Author's Background: Rudyard Kipling:
    • Born in Bombay, India (1865), and spent his early childhood immersed in Indian culture and wildlife.
    • The Jungle Book (1894) reflects his deep love for India.
    • Did You Know: Kipling's descriptions of mongoose-cobra fights are remarkably accurate; mongooses are fast, their fur provides some protection, and they aim for the back of the cobra's head.
    • The Jungle Book is a collection of seven stories, not just Mowgli's tale, with "Rikki-Tikki-Tavi" being a standalone story.

If— (Rudyard Kipling, 1910)

  • Purpose: Written as advice from Rudyard Kipling to his son, John, on how to develop into a good, honorable, and mature person.
  • Structure: The poem is a single, long conditional sentence, presenting a series of virtues and qualities. The "reward" for embodying these traits is revealed only in the final two lines.
  • Key Virtues and Qualities:
    • Composure and Self-Trust: Keeping calm when others are panicking and blaming you; trusting yourself despite others' doubts, but also understanding their skepticism.
    • Patience and Integrity: The ability to wait without tiring; maintaining honesty even when lied about; refraining from hate even when hated; and practicing humility (not looking too good or talking too wise).
    • Vision and Rationality: Dreaming without letting dreams control you; thinking deeply but not becoming solely focused on thoughts as an end.
    • Equanimity in Success and Failure: Treating both "Triumph" (victory) and "Disaster" (defeat) as "impostors"—meaning they are temporary and should not overly inflate or deflate one's spirit.
    • Resilience and Rebuilding: Bearing betrayal (truth twisted by "knaves"); courageously rebuilding cherished endeavors ("things you gave your life to") even with limited resources ("worn-out tools").
    • Risk-Taking and Humility in Loss: The willingness to risk all gains on a single chance ("pitch-and-toss"); and if losing, to quietly start over without complaining ("never breathe a word about your loss").
  • The Reward: If a person can embody all these difficult virtues, they will achieve true maturity and purpose: "Yours is the Earth and everything that's in it, / And—which is more—you'll be a Man, my son!"

Review Questions

  1. How does the arrival of Captain Bill Bones at the Admiral Benbow Inn immediately establish a sense of adventure and mystery for Jim Hawkins? What details contribute to this feeling?
  2. Compare and contrast the motivations and methods of Rikki-tikki-tavi and Nagaina. How do their differing approaches to survival and territory manifest in their actions?
  3. In "If—," Kipling describes "Triumph and Disaster" as "two impostors." What does he mean by this, and how might this perspective help someone navigate the ups and downs of life?
  4. Discuss how the authors (Stevenson and Kipling) use descriptions of physical appearance, habits, and dialogue to develop distinct and memorable characters (Captain Bill Bones, Rikki-tikki, Nag).
  5. Which piece of advice from Kipling's "If—" do you find most challenging to follow in modern life, and why? How might a character like Rikki-tikki-tavi embody some of these virtues in his own "great war"?
