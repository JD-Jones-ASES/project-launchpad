A New Century: Immigration, Industry, and Hope – Study Guide

Overview

This section explores pivotal aspects of American and global history at the turn of the 20th century, examining the influx of immigrants seeking a new life, the harsh realities of urban poverty they often faced, and significant historical events that shaped the era. Through poetry and prose, we encounter symbols of hope like the Statue of Liberty, grim depictions of tenement life, the wild adventure of the Klondike Gold Rush, a heroic yet tragic military charge, and the burgeoning artistic expression of the Harlem Renaissance.

Key Concepts

  • Immigration and the American Dream: Millions arrived in America seeking opportunity, symbolized by the Statue of Liberty and Emma Lazarus's poem, "The New Colossus," which articulates America's welcome to the "huddled masses."
  • Urban Poverty and Social Reform: The rapid growth of cities led to overcrowded, unsanitary tenement housing. Jacob Riis's work, "How the Other Half Lives," exposed these harsh conditions and played a crucial role in advocating for housing reform.
  • The Call of the Wild and Frontier Spirit: The Klondike Gold Rush fueled a demand for strong working dogs, highlighting the brutal forces of nature and the abrupt changes individuals (and animals) faced when drawn into the pursuit of wealth in unforgiving environments.
  • Courage, Duty, and the Costs of War: Alfred, Lord Tennyson's "The Charge of the Light Brigade" commemorates a real historical event during the Crimean War where soldiers bravely obeyed a mistaken order, illustrating themes of military discipline, sacrifice, and the devastating consequences of war.
  • Cultural Identity and Artistic Expression: The Harlem Renaissance marked a flourishing of African American art and literature. Langston Hughes's poetry, such as "The Negro Speaks of Rivers," aimed to celebrate Black identity and heritage, connecting past experiences with present pride and future hope.

Important Terms

  • The New Colossus: A sonnet written by Emma Lazarus in 1883 for the pedestal of the Statue of Liberty, famously welcoming immigrants to America.
  • Mother of Exiles: The symbolic name given to the Statue of Liberty in Lazarus's poem, representing America's role as a refuge for those fleeing oppression or seeking a new start.
  • Tenements: Overcrowded, often dilapidated apartment buildings in cities, especially common in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, where many poor immigrants lived in harsh conditions.
  • Sanitarian: A public health worker; someone concerned with improving public health and hygiene, particularly in urban environments.
  • Klondike Gold Rush: A migration by an estimated 100,000 prospectors to the Klondike region of Yukon, in northwestern Canada, between 1896 and 1899, after gold was discovered there.
  • Demesne: An estate or domain; the land attached to a manor, providing revenue for the owner. Used in "The Call of the Wild" to describe Judge Miller's property.
  • Crimean War (1853–1856): A military conflict fought between Russia and an alliance of Britain, France, and the Ottoman Empire, notable for early battlefield reporting.
  • Light Brigade: A cavalry unit of the British Army involved in a disastrous charge during the Battle of Balaclava in the Crimean War, famously immortalized by Tennyson.
  • Poet Laureate: A poet officially appointed by a monarch or government, typically expected to compose poems for state occasions. Alfred, Lord Tennyson held this position for Britain.
  • Harlem Renaissance: An intellectual and cultural revival of African American music, dance, art, fashion, literature, theater, and politics centered in Harlem, New York City, during the 1920s and 1930s.

Detailed Notes

The New Colossus and How the Other Half Lives (Emma Lazarus and Jacob Riis)

  • Historical Context (1880-1920): This period saw massive immigration to America, with over 20 million newcomers, many arriving through New York Harbor and passing by the Statue of Liberty.
  • "The New Colossus" (Emma Lazarus):
    • Author: Emma Lazarus (1849–1887), a Jewish American poet from New York City.
    • Purpose: Written in 1883 to raise funds for the Statue of Liberty's pedestal. It was engraved on a bronze plaque inside the pedestal in 1903.
    • Symbolism of the Statue: Not a "brazen giant" of war like Greek mythological figures, but a "mighty woman with a torch" – the "Mother of Exiles."
    • America's Welcome: The poem offers a "world-wide welcome," specifically calling for the "tired," "poor," "huddled masses yearning to breathe free," and "wretched refuse" of other lands. It portrays America as a beacon of hope and opportunity ("golden door").
  • "From How the Other Half Lives" (Jacob Riis):
    • Author: Jacob Riis (1849–1914), a Danish immigrant, journalist, and social reformer.
    • Purpose: Published in 1890, Riis used words and photographs (a revolutionary technique at the time) to expose the harsh living conditions in New York City's tenements.
    • Urban Crowding: Riis describes how three-fourths of New York's population lived in tenements, which had swelled from 15,000 in the previous generation to 37,000, housing over 1.2 million people.
    • Social Commentary: Riis highlights the ignorance and apathy of the wealthy ("one half of the world does not know how the other half lives") and aims to shock readers into action, noting the "dark enough" story of the tenements.
    • Contrast: The section emphasizes the stark difference between the promise of "The New Colossus" and the grim reality depicted by Riis for many immigrants.

The Call of the Wild (Adapted from Jack London)

  • Author: Jack London (1876–1916), an adventurous American writer who drew heavily on his own experiences, including the Klondike Gold Rush.
  • Historical Context: The Klondike Gold Rush (starting in 1897) led thousands of men to rush to the frozen Northland in search of gold, creating a massive demand for strong, furry sled dogs.
  • Buck's Life Before: Buck is introduced as a powerful, comfortable dog living an idyllic life at Judge Miller's large estate in the "sun-kissed Santa Clara Valley," enjoying freedom and family life. He is neither a mere house-dog nor a kennel-dog, but rules the entire "demesne."
  • The Change: Buck is unaware of the Gold Rush, but a gardener's helper, Manuel, kidnaps him to sell him to meet the demand for sled dogs.
  • Kidnapping and Journey: Buck is tricked, a rope is tightened around his neck, and he is violently subdued. His strength ebbs, and he is thrown into a baggage car, marking an abrupt end to his comfortable life and the beginning of his struggle for survival in the wild.
  • Theme: This excerpt immediately establishes a central theme of the story: the sudden, brutal transition from civilization to the primal, unforgiving "wild."

The Charge of the Light Brigade (Alfred, Lord Tennyson)

  • Author: Alfred, Lord Tennyson (1809–1892), one of the most popular poets of the Victorian era and Poet Laureate of Great Britain for over 40 years.
  • Historical Event: On October 25, 1854, during the Crimean War, approximately 670 British cavalrymen of the Light Brigade charged into a valley surrounded by Russian cannons due to a mistaken order. Nearly half were killed or wounded.
  • Tennyson's Inspiration: He wrote the poem almost immediately after reading about the disaster in a newspaper, driven by admiration for the soldiers' courage and obedience.
  • Poetic Devices:
    • Rhythm: The poem uses a galloping rhythm ("Half a league, half a league, / Half a league onward") to imitate the sound of charging horses, immersing the reader in the action.
    • Repetition: Phrases like "Into the valley of Death / Rode the six hundred" emphasize the fatal certainty and the unit's collective fate.
    • Imagery: Vivid descriptions of cannons, sabers flashing, battery-smoke, and the "jaws of Death," "mouth of Hell" convey the horror and chaos of battle.
  • Themes:
    • Obedience and Duty: "Theirs not to make reply, / Theirs not to reason why, / Theirs but to do and die." The soldiers' unwavering obedience despite knowing a blunder had occurred.
    • Courage and Sacrifice: The poem honors the soldiers' immense bravery in facing almost certain death.
    • Tragedy of War: It highlights the devastating human cost of military mistakes and conflict.
  • Public Reaction: The Crimean War was one of the first to be widely covered by newspaper reporters. The public was outraged by the officers' mistake, but Tennyson's poem focused on honoring the soldiers.

Two Poems: The Negro Speaks of Rivers (Langston Hughes)

  • Author: Langston Hughes (1902–1967), a leading voice of the Harlem Renaissance.
  • Historical Context: The Harlem Renaissance (1920s) was a period of extraordinary artistic, musical, and literary flourishing for African Americans in New York City.
  • Hughes's Poetic Philosophy: Hughes believed in celebrating the everyday lives and authentic voices of Black Americans, affirming their identity and heritage in great literature.
  • "The Negro Speaks of Rivers": (Partial content provided)
    • Theme: The poem connects the speaker's soul and the collective Black experience to ancient, historical rivers across the world.
    • Symbolism of Rivers: Rivers symbolize longevity, depth, journey, history, and the continuous flow of human experience and identity.
    • Imagery: "I've known rivers ancient as the world and older than the / flow of human blood in human veins." This line immediately establishes a profound, ancient connection to human history.
    • Personal and Collective Identity: "My soul has grown deep like the rivers" suggests a personal connection that is also deeply rooted in a shared, ancient heritage. The mention of the "Euphrates" links this heritage to the dawn of civilization.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast the vision of America presented in Emma Lazarus's "The New Colossus" with the reality depicted in Jacob Riis's "How the Other Half Lives." What do these two pieces reveal about the immigrant experience at the turn of the century?
  2. How does Jack London's "The Call of the Wild" immediately establish the stark contrast between Buck's former life and his impending struggle? What literary techniques does London use to convey this rapid change?
  3. Alfred, Lord Tennyson's "The Charge of the Light Brigade" memorializes a military disaster. How does Tennyson manage to honor the soldiers' courage and obedience while also implicitly criticizing the mistaken order? Discuss the poem's use of rhythm and repetition to achieve its effect.
  4. Based on the introduction to Langston Hughes and the opening lines of "The Negro Speaks of Rivers," what was Hughes's primary goal as a poet during the Harlem Renaissance? How do the initial images of rivers help achieve this goal?
  5. Across these varied texts – poems about immigration and war, and prose about urban life and the wilderness – what common themes emerge regarding challenges and resilience faced by individuals (or animals) in the face of monumental historical forces and societal changes?
