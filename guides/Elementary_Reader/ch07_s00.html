A New Nation: Constitution and Early Republic Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the crucial period after the American Revolution when the new nation grappled with establishing its government, defining its identity, and addressing the diverse needs of its people. We delve into foundational documents like the U.S. Constitution, hear the early calls for women's rights, understand the birth of a national anthem during conflict, and glimpse the societal transformations through a classic piece of American literature.

Key Concepts

  • Founding Principles of Government: The U.S. Constitution, particularly its Preamble, established the fundamental purposes and democratic nature of the American government, emphasizing that power originates from "We the People."
  • Early Presidential Leadership: George Washington's first presidency set vital precedents for leadership, emphasizing humility, dependence on citizens, divine guidance, and a peaceful transfer of power.
  • Advocacy for Women's Rights: Abigail Adams's powerful letter to her husband, John Adams, highlights early calls for women's inclusion and rights in the new legal framework, foreshadowing future struggles for equality.
  • National Identity and Symbols: "The Star-Spangled Banner," born from the War of 1812, became a potent symbol of American resilience, patriotism, and the enduring spirit of the nation.
  • Impact of the American Revolution on Society: Washington Irving's "Rip Van Winkle" uses a fictional narrative to humorously illustrate the profound societal and political changes brought about by the Revolution, reflecting on how individuals experienced this transformation.
  • Emergence of American Literature: Washington Irving is recognized as a pioneering figure in American literature, crafting tales that capture the spirit and landscape of the young nation.

Important Terms

Constitution: The supreme law of the United States, outlining the structure and function of the government. Preamble: The introductory statement of the Constitution, explaining its purposes and guiding principles. Inaugural Address: A speech delivered by a newly elected public official at the beginning of their term. Ordain: To officially establish or enact. Domestic Tranquility: Peace and order within the country. Common Defence: Protection of the nation from external threats. General Welfare: The health, happiness, and prosperity of all citizens. Posterity: All future generations. Providential Aid: Guidance or help believed to come from God. Tyrants: Rulers who use power cruelly or oppressively. Foment a rebellion: To stir up or incite a revolt. Tyrannical: Using power in a cruel or unjust way. Perilous: Full of danger or risk. Ramparts: Defensive walls or embankments of a fort or city. Gallantly: In a brave or heroic manner. Star-Spangled Banner: The national flag of the United States, famously referenced in the national anthem. Insuperable aversion: An unconquerable dislike or opposition to something. Henpecked: (Of a man) constantly nagged or intimidated by his wife. Assiduity: Constant and careful attention to a task; diligence. Perseverance: Steadfastness in doing something despite difficulty or delay in achieving success. Fowling-piece: A light gun for shooting wild fowl. Latitude: (In John Adams's reply) full extent or freedom.

Detailed Notes

1. Building the Government: The Constitution and Washington's Leadership

The American Revolution achieved independence, but creating a stable government was the next monumental task.

  • The United States Constitution (1787):
    • Purpose: Delegates from twelve states convened in Philadelphia to draft a plan for how the new nation's government would operate.
    • The Preamble: This concise, 52-word introduction explains the core intentions of the government.
      • "We the People": This phrase was carefully chosen to signify that the government's authority derives directly from ordinary citizens, not from a monarch or a select elite.
      • Six Key Goals: To "form a more perfect Union," "establish Justice," "insure domestic Tranquility" (peace at home), "provide for the common defence" (protection), "promote the general Welfare," and "secure the Blessings of Liberty to ourselves and our Posterity" (future generations).
  • George Washington's First Inaugural Address (1789):
    • Context: Two years after the Constitution was written, Washington was elected the first president.
    • Reflections: He expressed significant "anxiety" about the immense responsibility but also deep "reverence and love" for his country's call.
    • Dependence and Divine Guidance: Washington emphasized his reliance on the support of "every citizen" and the "providential aid" of Almighty God, noting that the nation's progress seemed marked by divine care.
    • Legacy of Leadership: Washington's decision to refuse the title "Your Majesty," preferring the simple "Mr. President," established a democratic precedent. He further amazed the world by voluntarily stepping down after two terms, setting an example of peaceful and limited leadership.

2. Voices for Change: Abigail Adams and Women's Rights

Even as the new government was forming, vital questions about who would be included in "We the People" were being raised.

  • Abigail Adams's Letter to John Adams (March 31, 1776):
    • Context: Written while John Adams was participating in the Continental Congress, as the Founders shaped the new nation's laws. Abigail managed their farm in Massachusetts.
    • The Plea: "Remember the Ladies": Abigail urged her husband to consider the rights of women in the "new code of laws," advocating for them to be treated "more generous and favorable" than in past traditions.
    • Critique of Male Power: She warned against putting "unlimited power into the hands of the husbands," asserting that "all men would be tyrants if they could."
    • Threat of Rebellion: Adams declared that if "particular care and attention is not paid to the ladies," women would "foment a rebellion" and refuse to be bound by "laws in which we have no voice or representation."
    • Hope for a "Friend": She encouraged men to give up the "harsh title of master for the more tender and endearing one of friend."
  • John Adams's Reply (April 14, 1776):
    • Playful Dismissal: John Adams playfully laughed off his wife's "extraordinary code of laws."
    • Societal Unrest: He acknowledged that the struggle for independence had "loosened the bands of Government everywhere," leading to disobedient children, apprentices, and even slaves. He joked that women were "another tribe, more numerous and powerful" now discontented.
    • "Masculine Systems": He insisted that men "know better than to repeal our masculine systems," though he conceded that in practice, these systems were "little more than theory" and men "dare not exert our power in its full latitude."
  • Abigail Adams's Significance: Despite never attending formal school, Abigail Adams became a well-read and influential figure. Her letters are invaluable historical documents, showcasing her intelligence, her role as a trusted adviser to her husband, and her pioneering voice in advocating for women's place in the nascent republic.

3. Forging National Symbols: The Star-Spangled Banner

National identity is often forged in moments of crisis and embodied in enduring symbols.

  • Context: The War of 1812 (September 1814):
    • The British navy heavily bombarded Fort McHenry in Baltimore Harbor for 25 hours.
  • Francis Scott Key's Inspiration:
    • Key, an American lawyer, watched the battle from a ship nearby.
    • At dawn, after a night of "rocket's red glare" and "bomb bursting in air," he saw the large American flag (30 by 42 feet, sewn by Mary Pickersgill) still flying over the fort. This sight profoundly moved him.
  • The Poem ("Defence of Fort M'Henry"):
    • Stanza 1: Captures the intense "perilous fight" and the enduring hope as the "star-spangled banner" continued to wave, proving the nation's resilience.
    • Stanza 4: Reflects on future victories and peace, affirming that "when freemen shall stand / Between their lov'd home and the war's desolation," they will prevail. It invokes the motto "In God is our trust" and reiterates the triumph of the flag "O'er the land of the free and the home of the brave."
  • Historical Significance: The poem was quickly set to music and gained widespread popularity. It was officially designated the national anthem in 1931. The original flag that inspired Key is preserved at the Smithsonian Institution.

4. The Revolution's Echoes in Literature: Rip Van Winkle

Literature can provide unique insights into historical periods and the human experience of change.

  • Washington Irving (1783–1859):
    • Often called the "father of American literature," Irving's works helped establish a distinctive American literary voice.
    • His collection The Sketch-Book (1819) included "Rip Van Winkle" and "The Legend of Sleepy Hollow."
  • "Rip Van Winkle" Story:
    • Setting: The tale is set in a Dutch village at the foot of the Catskill Mountains in New York.
    • Plot: Rip, a "lazy but good-hearted" villager, escapes his farm duties and "henpecked" wife by wandering into the mountains. He encounters mysterious figures, drinks a potent brew, and falls asleep for twenty years.
    • The Awakening: When he wakes, he finds that the American Revolution has occurred, and the world he knew has dramatically changed – politically, socially, and personally.
    • Character of Rip: He possessed an "insuperable aversion to all kinds of profitable labour," preferring to fish or hunt squirrels all day. However, he was kind, a good neighbor, and always willing to help others with tasks outside of his own farm work, often accompanied by his dog, Wolf. His struggle to cope with family responsibilities contrasted with his generous spirit toward others.
    • Literary Significance: Irving uses humor, vivid descriptions, and the fantastical element of Rip's long slumber to explore the profound impact of the American Revolution on ordinary lives and the transformation of a colonial society into a new nation. It's a tale that reflects on identity, change, and the passage of time.

Review Questions

  1. What were the main purposes of government outlined in the Preamble to the United States Constitution? How does the phrase "We the People" reflect the source of the government's power?
  2. Discuss the significance of George Washington's actions and words during his first inaugural address and presidency. How did he set precedents for future American leaders?
  3. Analyze Abigail Adams's arguments for women's rights in her letter to John Adams. What specific changes did she advocate for, and what warning did she issue? How did John Adams respond, and what does his reply reveal about gender attitudes of the time?
  4. Describe the historical context and events that inspired Francis Scott Key to write "The Star-Spangled Banner." What emotions and ideas are conveyed in the poem, particularly in the first and fourth stanzas, regarding national identity and resilience?
  5. How does the character of Rip Van Winkle, and his twenty-year slumber through the American Revolution, serve as a literary device to reflect the changes and impact of the new nation's founding? What does Rip's personal experience tell us about the societal transformation of the era?
