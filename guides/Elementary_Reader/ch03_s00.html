Heroes of Old: Myths and Legends Study Guide

Overview

This section explores timeless tales of courage, wisdom, and the human condition from ancient Greece and medieval England. Through myths like "The Minotaur" and "The Golden Touch," and legends such as "How Arthur Got the Sword," readers learn about heroes facing challenges and the consequences of their actions. The section also introduces two powerful animal poems, "The Eagle" and "The Tyger," which showcase the majesty and mystery of the natural world.

Key Concepts

  • Heroism and Valor: Explores what it means to be a hero, showcasing individuals who display extraordinary bravery, strength, and a willingness to face danger for the good of others (e.g., Theseus, Arthur).
  • Bravery and Cleverness: Demonstrates that true heroism often involves not just physical courage but also intelligence, strategic thinking, and the ability to solve problems (e.g., Theseus's use of Ariadne's thread).
  • Consequences of Unchecked Desire: Illustrates the dangers of greed and avarice, showing how wishing for too much can lead to unforeseen and tragic outcomes (e.g., King Midas's Golden Touch).
  • True Leadership: Examines how true leaders are identified not by birthright or force, but by inherent character, humility, and the ability to prove their worth through unexpected means (e.g., Arthur drawing the sword).
  • The Majesty and Mystery of Nature: Uses vivid poetry to portray the power, beauty, and awe-inspiring nature of wild animals, prompting reflection on their creation and place in the world (e.g., The Eagle, The Tyger).
  • Timeless Stories: Highlights how ancient myths and legends continue to resonate and teach important lessons across generations, influencing language and cultural understanding.

Important Terms

  • avarice: An extreme greed for wealth or possessions. (King Midas)
  • labyrinth: A complicated maze or network of passages from which it is difficult to find one's way. (Minotaur's dwelling)
  • Minotaur: In Greek mythology, a monstrous creature with the body of a man and the head of a bull, who fed on human flesh.
  • mortal: Subject to death; a human being. (Theseus dealt a mortal blow to the Minotaur)
  • quest: A long search or journey in pursuit of something important, often involving adventure or challenge. (King Arthur's quest for justice and valor)
  • symmetry: A balanced arrangement of parts on either side of a line or center. (The fearful symmetry of the Tyger)
  • valor: Great courage, especially in battle. (A quality sought by King Arthur)
  • Theseus: The young prince of Athens, known for his bravery and cleverness, who slayed the Minotaur.
  • Minos: The King of Crete who compelled Athens to send a dreadful tribute to the Minotaur.
  • Daedalus: The cunning craftsman who built the labyrinth for King Minos.
  • Ariadne: King Minos's daughter who helped Theseus escape the labyrinth.
  • Midas: A very rich king who wished for the Golden Touch.
  • Marygold: King Midas's beloved daughter, tragically turned to gold.
  • Arthur: A young boy raised by Sir Ector, who unexpectedly became the true King of England by drawing the sword from the stone.
  • Uther Pendragon: The old king of England whose death plunged the land into trouble.
  • Sir Ector: A noble knight who raised Arthur from a babe.
  • Kay: Sir Ector's son and Arthur's older brother.

Detailed Notes

The Minotaur

  • Source: Retold by Claude from Nathaniel Hawthorne's Tanglewood Tales for Girls and Boys (1853).
  • Context: Ancient Greek myths explained the world and taught courage. Hawthorne retold these myths for young readers, believing in their lasting power.
  • Characters:
    • Theseus: Brave young prince of Athens.
    • King Minos: King of Crete, who demanded human tribute for the Minotaur.
    • Daedalus: The cunning craftsman who built the Labyrinth.
    • Minotaur: A terrible creature with a man's body and a bull's head, living in the Labyrinth and feeding on humans.
    • Ariadne: King Minos's daughter, who helped Theseus.
  • Plot Summary:
    1. Every year, King Minos of Crete demanded a tribute of seven young men and seven young maidens from Athens to be devoured by the Minotaur in the Labyrinth.
    2. Prince Theseus volunteers to go, determined to slay the monster.
    3. In Crete, Ariadne provides Theseus with a sword and a spool of thread, advising him to unwind it to find his way back.
    4. Theseus enters the dark Labyrinth, finds the bellowing Minotaur, and, after a fierce struggle, kills it.
    5. Using Ariadne's thread, Theseus safely navigates back out of the maze.
  • Themes: Bravery, cleverness, sacrifice, overcoming evil.

The Golden Touch

  • Source: Retold by Claude from Nathaniel Hawthorne's A Wonder-Book for Girls and Boys (1851).
  • Context: Cautionary tale about wishes and the consequences of greed.
  • Characters:
    • King Midas: A very rich king, fond of gold above all else except his daughter.
    • Marygold: King Midas's beloved young daughter.
    • Stranger (divine figure): Grants Midas his wish.
  • Plot Summary:
    1. King Midas is incredibly wealthy but constantly desires more gold.
    2. A stranger appears and offers Midas one wish. Midas wishes for everything he touches to turn to gold.
    3. At sunrise, his wish comes true: bedclothes, furniture, books, and garden roses all turn to gold.
    4. Tragically, when Midas kisses his daughter, Marygold, she too turns into a golden statue.
    5. Overwhelmed with despair, Midas realizes gold is worthless without his daughter and begs the stranger to reverse the gift.
    6. The stranger instructs Midas to bathe in a river, which washes away the Golden Touch and restores Marygold to life.
  • Themes: Greed (avarice), consequences of desires, the true value of love over material wealth.
  • "Did You Know": The phrase "the Midas touch" is still used today to describe someone who is very successful at making money, directly referencing this myth.

How Arthur Got the Sword

  • Source: Retold by Claude from Howard Pyle's The Story of King Arthur and His Knights (1903).
  • Context: Legends of King Arthur have been told for over a thousand years, highlighting themes of chivalry, justice, and leadership. Pyle brought these stories to life for young American readers.
  • Characters:
    • Uther Pendragon: The previous king of England, whose death caused a crisis of succession.
    • Archbishop of Canterbury: Prays for a sign to determine the rightful king.
    • Sir Ector: A noble knight who raised Arthur.
    • Kay: Sir Ector's son, Arthur's older brother, whom Arthur served as squire.
    • Arthur: A young, unassuming boy who proves to be the true king.
  • Plot Summary:
    1. After King Uther Pendragon dies, England is in chaos because no one knows who the rightful heir is.
    2. On Christmas morning, a miraculous sword appears, thrust into an anvil and stone, with an inscription: "Whoso pulleth out this sword... is the true-born King of England."
    3. Many proud lords and knights attempt to pull out the sword but fail.
    4. Arthur, a young boy raised by Sir Ector and serving as his brother Kay's squire, rides to London for a tournament.
    5. When Kay forgets his sword, Arthur tries to fetch it but finds their lodging locked.
    6. Passing the churchyard, Arthur sees the sword in the stone and, needing a sword for Kay, pulls it out effortlessly.
    7. The lords marvel, and Arthur is crowned king, beginning his quest for justice and valor.
  • Themes: True leadership, humility, destiny, justice, valor.

Two Poems about Powerful Creatures

The Eagle by Alfred, Lord Tennyson (1851)

  • Source: One of Tennyson's shortest and most powerful poems.
  • Context: Tennyson was a famous Victorian poet. He wrote "The Eagle" after observing eagles.
  • Summary: A six-line poem describing an eagle perched high on a crag, observing the sea below, before descending "like a thunderbolt."
  • Imagery: Crooked hands (talons), lonely lands, azure world, wrinkled sea, mountain walls, thunderbolt.
  • Themes: Majesty of nature, power, solitude, predatory grace, swiftness.

The Tyger by William Blake (1794)

  • Source: From Blake's collection Songs of Experience.
  • Context: Blake was an English poet and artist. "The Tyger" is part of a pair with his poem "The Lamb," asking profound questions about creation.
  • Summary: A six-stanza poem questioning the terrifying beauty and fearful symmetry of the tiger, pondering the nature of its Creator and whether the same Creator made both the gentle lamb and the fierce tiger.
  • Imagery: Burning bright, forests of the night, immortal hand/eye, fire of thine eyes, wings, hammer, chain, furnace, anvil, dread grasp.
  • Themes: Creation, divine power, good and evil, beauty and terror, the complexity of the Creator.
  • "Did You Know": Blake illustrated his own books, coloring each page by hand. The poem raises a theological question about the coexistence of innocence ("The Lamb") and experience/fierceness ("The Tyger") in creation.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast Theseus and King Midas as heroes. What different qualities do they demonstrate, and what lessons do their stories teach about human nature and desire?
  2. How does Arthur's ascent to kingship in "How Arthur Got the Sword" differ from what the proud lords and knights expected? What does this story suggest about the nature of true leadership?
  3. Both "The Minotaur" and "The Golden Touch" were retold by Nathaniel Hawthorne. What common elements or themes do you notice in his approach to retelling ancient myths for young readers?
  4. "The Eagle" and "The Tyger" both describe powerful animals. How do the poets use imagery and questions to evoke a sense of awe and mystery about these creatures? What different aspects of power do they focus on?
  5. Many of the stories in this section are ancient or medieval in origin but are still read today. Why do you think these "heroes of old" and their tales continue to resonate with readers centuries later?
