Colonial Voices: Life in Early America Study Guide

Overview

This section offers a glimpse into the diverse experiences and enduring values of early American colonists through the works of a prominent Puritan poet, a practical Enlightenment thinker, and a foundational children's schoolbook. We explore themes of faith, love, practical wisdom, and the importance of education, revealing the spiritual depth, shrewd common sense, and instructional priorities that shaped colonial life.

Key Concepts

  • Puritanism and Faith: Reflected in Anne Bradstreet's devotional poetry and The New England Primer's integration of religious instruction with basic literacy. Puritan beliefs emphasized God's sovereignty, human sinfulness, and the importance of spiritual introspection and a moral life.
  • Early American Literature: Anne Bradstreet's pioneering poetry as the first published poet in the colonies, showcasing personal expression within a devout framework. Benjamin Franklin's practical and witty prose, influential through his Almanack and autobiographical anecdotes.
  • Practical Wisdom and Self-Improvement: Central to Benjamin Franklin's philosophy, emphasizing diligence, thrift, hard work, and prudence through memorable proverbs and cautionary tales like "The Whistle." These values were crucial for survival and prosperity in the New World.
  • Colonial Education: The New England Primer exemplifies the dual purpose of colonial schooling: teaching basic literacy (ABCs) alongside moral and religious lessons. Education was highly valued, especially for the ability to read the Bible.
  • Extended Metaphor: A literary device where a comparison between two unlike things is extended throughout a poem or a section of text. Bradstreet uses this effectively in "The Author to Her Book," comparing her book to a flawed child.
  • The Role of Women in Colonial Society: Anne Bradstreet's achievements as a published poet were extraordinary for a woman in her time, highlighting the challenges and rare opportunities for female intellectual and creative expression in a male-dominated society.

Important Terms

  • Almanac: A yearly publication containing useful information, calendars, weather predictions, and wise sayings. Benjamin Franklin's Poor Richard's Almanack was a famous example.
  • Diligence: Careful and persistent work or effort. A key virtue promoted by Franklin's proverbs.
  • Extended Metaphor: A comparison between two unlike things that continues throughout a series of sentences in a paragraph or lines in a poem. Anne Bradstreet uses this in "The Author to Her Book."
  • Hearth: The floor of a fireplace; by extension, a symbol for the home and its comforts.
  • Hornbook: A single page containing the alphabet, numbers, and often the Lord's Prayer, protected by a thin sheet of transparent horn, used as a primer for colonial children.
  • Primer: A basic reading textbook for young children. The New England Primer was the most popular such book in colonial America.
  • Proverb/Aphorism: A short, well-known saying stating a general truth or piece of advice. Benjamin Franklin's Almanack was full of proverbs.
  • Thrift: The wise and careful use of money and resources, avoiding waste. A core principle advocated by Benjamin Franklin.
  • Vanity: Excessive pride in one's appearance, achievements, or possessions; also, the quality of being worthless or futile.
  • Virtue: Behavior showing high moral standards; a good quality or habit. Colonial education and literature often aimed to instill virtue.

Detailed Notes

I. Anne Bradstreet: Puritan Poetry

  • Background: Anne Bradstreet (c. 1612–1672) was the first published poet in the American colonies. She was a Puritan wife who immigrated to Massachusetts and wrote poems by candlelight amidst the hardships of colonial life. Her brother-in-law secretly took her poems to London, where they were published without her initial knowledge, a remarkable feat for a woman of her era.

  • "To My Dear and Loving Husband" (c. 1650–1678):

    • Theme: This poem is a profound expression of marital love, deep affection, and spiritual devotion between a husband and wife. It highlights the Puritan ideal of a loving, enduring partnership that reflects divine love.
    • Content:
      • The opening lines declare a unique and unparalleled unity ("If ever two were one, then surely we.").
      • Bradstreet values her husband's love more than earthly riches ("mines of gold" or "riches that the East doth hold").
      • Her love is insatiable and only her husband's love can reciprocate it ("rivers cannot quench, Nor ought but love from thee, give recompense").
      • She prays for divine blessings upon her husband for his love.
      • The poem concludes with a wish for their love to persevere through life, so that their union may continue eternally after death.

  • "The Author to Her Book" (c. 1650–1678):

    • Theme: This poem explores the struggles of authorship, self-criticism, modesty, and the unexpected public reception of one's work.
    • Extended Metaphor: Bradstreet famously uses an extended metaphor, comparing her book of poems to an "ill-formed offspring" or a flawed child.
      • "Thou ill-formed offspring of my feeble brain": The book is a product of her own mind, but she views it as imperfect.
      • "snatched from thence by friends, less wise than true, Who thee abroad exposed to public view": Her poems were published without her consent, much like a child being sent out into the world unprepared.
      • "I cast thee by as one unfit for light": She initially rejected her book, finding its appearance displeasing.
      • "I washed thy face... rubbing off a spot, still made a flaw": Her attempts to revise her work (the "child's face") only revealed more imperfections, indicating the difficulty of revision.
      • "I stretched thy joints to make thee even feet": "Feet" refers to metrical feet in poetry; she tried to improve the poem's rhythm and structure.
      • "nought save homespun cloth i' the house I find": The book (child) is dressed in simple, humble "homespun cloth," reflecting her modest status and the unrefined nature of her work.
      • Advice to the "child": It should roam among ordinary people ("vulgars") but beware of critics and claim no father and a poor mother, further emphasizing her humility and the circumstances of its publication.
    • Context: This poem offers insight into the challenges of female writers in a period when publication was rare and often met with skepticism, especially for women.

II. Benjamin Franklin: Practical Wisdom

  • Background: Benjamin Franklin (1706–1790) was an extraordinary figure in colonial America—a printer, scientist, inventor (lightning rod, bifocal glasses, Franklin stove), writer, and statesman. He was a key author of the Declaration of Independence and the Constitution. He published Poor Richard's Almanack under the pen name "Richard Saunders" for 25 years.

  • From Poor Richard's Almanack (1732–1758):

    • Purpose: The almanac was an annual publication offering weather forecasts, recipes, and, most famously, a collection of witty and practical proverbs.
    • Themes: Franklin's proverbs emphasize virtues like hard work, diligence, thrift, self-reliance, prudence, the value of education, and good character. They reflect the common-sense philosophy essential for success in colonial America.
    • Examples of Proverbs and Their Meanings:
      • "Early to bed and early to rise, makes a man healthy, wealthy, and wise." (Promotes discipline and a productive lifestyle.)
      • "A penny saved is a penny earned." (Encourages thrift and frugality.)
      • "God helps them that help themselves." (Advocates self-reliance and initiative.)
      • "An investment in knowledge pays the best interest." (Highlights the long-term value of education.)
      • "Well done is better than well said." (Emphasizes action and results over mere talk.)
      • "Little strokes fell great oaks." (Illustrates the power of persistent effort.)
      • "An ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure." (Stresses foresight and proactive measures.)
      • "By failing to prepare, you are preparing to fail." (Underlines the importance of planning.)
      • "Fish and visitors stink in three days." (A humorous take on overstaying one's welcome.)

  • From "The Whistle" (1779):

    • Narrative: This is an autobiographical anecdote from Franklin's childhood. At seven years old, he impulsively spent all his "coppers" (money) on a whistle, charmed by its sound.
    • The Lesson Learned: His family ridiculed him for paying four times its worth, making him regret the good things he could have bought. This experience taught him a valuable lesson about the true cost and value of things.
    • Enduring Phrase: "Don't give too much for the whistle" became a personal mantra and a famous aphorism, meaning one should not overpay for trivial pleasures or desires, or sacrifice too much for something that isn't truly worth it. It promotes thoughtful decision-making and avoiding impulsive, costly mistakes.

III. The New England Primer: Education and Faith

  • Background and Significance: The New England Primer was the most popular schoolbook in colonial America, first printed around 1690 and used for nearly 150 years. Millions of children learned to read from its pages, making it a foundational text for colonial literacy and moral instruction.

  • The Alphabet:

    • Structure: Each letter of the alphabet was accompanied by a short rhyme and often an illustration, designed to teach both literacy and moral/religious lessons simultaneously.
    • Integration of Faith: Unlike modern schoolbooks, almost every lesson included a message about God, sin, or salvation. This reflects the colonial belief that learning to read and learning about faith were inseparable.
    • Examples:
      • "In Adam's Fall, We sinned all." (Introduces the concept of original sin with the letter 'A'.)
      • "Thy Life to mend, This Book attend." (Connects reading the Bible or religious texts with moral improvement.)
      • "The idle Fool, Is whipt at School." (Teaches a lesson about diligence and punishment.)

  • "An Evening Prayer":

    • Content: The primer included simple, memorable prayers like "Now I lay me down to sleep, I pray the Lord my soul to keep; If I should die before I wake, I pray the Lord my soul to take."
    • Reflects: This prayer underscores the pervasive Puritan piety, the acceptance of mortality, and the reliance on divine protection and salvation in daily life.

  • Colonial Schools:

    • Environment: Typically one-room schoolhouses where children of all ages sat together on hard wooden benches.
    • Curriculum: Students learned letters, numbers, and Bible verses.
    • Materials: Paper was expensive, so children often used hornbooks—small boards covered with a thin sheet of horn for practicing writing.
    • Discipline: Schoolmasters were strict, and corporal punishment (like being rapped on the knuckles) or public shaming (wearing a pointed cap) was common for misbehaving students.
    • Values: Despite the hardships, colonists highly valued education, believing that every child should be able to read the Bible for their spiritual well-being.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast how Anne Bradstreet and Benjamin Franklin reflect the values of colonial America in their writings. What distinct aspects of colonial life or thought does each author emphasize?
  2. In "The Author to Her Book," Anne Bradstreet uses an extended metaphor to describe her poetry. Explain this metaphor and analyze what it reveals about her feelings regarding her literary work and its unexpected publication.
  3. Choose three proverbs from Benjamin Franklin's Poor Richard's Almanack and explain what practical advice each offers. How might these proverbs have been particularly relevant to people living in the American colonies?
  4. Discuss the dual purpose of The New England Primer. How does its content, particularly the alphabet rhymes and prayers, demonstrate the intertwined nature of education and religious faith in colonial society?
  5. Based on the selections from Bradstreet, Franklin, and The New England Primer, what conclusions can you draw about the daily challenges, moral priorities, and intellectual landscape of early American colonists?
