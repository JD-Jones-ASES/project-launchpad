The Natural World: Poets and Naturalists Study Guide

Overview

This chapter explores how the natural world has profoundly inspired diverse writers across different eras. Through the writings of naturalists like John Muir and Henry David Thoreau, and poets such as Emily Dickinson, William Wordsworth, and Robert Frost, we examine themes of deep connection to nature, the power of observation, the search for meaning, and the impact of personal choices on one's life.

Key Concepts

  • Nature as a Source of Spiritual and Emotional Connection: Many writers, particularly Muir and Wordsworth, describe nature as a powerful, almost divine entity that evokes profound emotional responses, joy, and a sense of being an inseparable part of the natural world.
  • The Value of Deliberate Living and Simplicity: Henry David Thoreau's experiment at Walden Pond highlights the importance of stripping life down to its essentials to understand what truly matters, fostering a conscious and intentional existence.
  • Observing the Ordinary with Wonder: Emily Dickinson demonstrates how keen observation of everyday natural phenomena—a bird, a daffodil, the concept of hope—can reveal extraordinary insights and profound meaning, even from a quiet, solitary life.
  • The Impact of Choice and Reflection: Robert Frost's "The Road Not Taken" uses a natural setting to explore the universal human experience of making choices and reflecting on their long-term consequences, often with a sense of ambiguity or retrospection.
  • Nature's Enduring Beauty and Wealth: From Muir's descriptions of the "divine, enduring, unwastable wealth" of the Sierra Nevada to Wordsworth's daffodils flashing upon his "inward eye," the texts emphasize the lasting beauty and restorative power of natural experiences.
  • Advocacy for Nature's Preservation: John Muir's writings and his direct influence on figures like President Theodore Roosevelt underscore the critical role literature and personal experience can play in inspiring conservation efforts and protecting wild places.

Important Terms

  • Naturalist: A person who studies and is an expert on natural history, especially one who observes nature firsthand. (e.g., John Muir, Henry David Thoreau)
  • Deliberately (Living): To live with conscious intention and purpose; to make choices with careful thought rather than by chance or habit, as advocated by Thoreau.
  • Romantic Poetry: A literary movement (late 18th to mid-19th century) emphasizing emotion, individualism, the glorification of nature, and the past. William Wordsworth is a key figure.
  • Fascicles: Small handmade booklets of poems, often sewn together, like those Emily Dickinson created.
  • Auroral: Resembling the dawn; characterized by a golden, fresh, and luminous quality. (Used by Thoreau to describe his cabin.)
  • Tarn: A small mountain lake, typically formed in a cirque excavated by a glacier. (Used by Thoreau to describe Walden Pond.)
  • Jocund: Cheerful; merry; full of high spirits and gladness. (Used by Wordsworth to describe the company of daffodils.)
  • Abash: To make someone feel ashamed, embarrassed, or discomfited. (Used by Dickinson in "Hope Is the Thing with Feathers" to describe what a storm cannot do to hope.)
  • Extremity: The most desperate need or severe difficulty. (Used by Dickinson to note that hope never asks for a crumb even in such circumstances.)
  • Undergrowth: A dense growth of shrubs and other plants, especially under trees in woodland. (Used by Frost to describe where the road bent.)

Detailed Notes

R01_MUIR: "My First Summer in the Sierra" (Adapted from John Muir)

  • Muir's Motivation: John Muir, passionate about the Sierra Nevada mountains, took a job with a sheep-owner, Mr. Delaney, to go with his flock to the headwaters of the Merced and Tuolumne rivers in 1869, fulfilling his desire to explore the high country.
  • Immersion in Nature: Muir describes a profound, almost spiritual union with the mountains. He feels they are "in us, kindling enthusiasm," making him feel transparent and "an inseparable part of it, thrilling with the air and trees... immortal."
  • Vivid Descriptions:
    • Mountains: He sees snowy peaks near Yosemite as clear and "saturated" with blue air, extending a "consuming strong invitation."
    • Sundown: A "glory of color transfiguring" everything, with trees standing "hushed and thoughtful," as if receiving a solemn "good-night" from the sun. The forest "breathes free" under the stars.
    • Dawn/Sunrise: Rose light turning to daffodil yellow, level beams "awakening and warming all the mighty host" of pines for their "shining day's work."
    • Midday: "Sun-gold noons" and "alabaster cloud-mountains," with the landscape "beaming with consciousness like the face of a god," representing "Divine, enduring, unwastable wealth."
  • Legacy: Muir (1838–1914) was born in Scotland, lived in Wisconsin, then California where he fell in love with the Sierra Nevada. His writings were instrumental in convincing President Theodore Roosevelt to protect Yosemite as a national park. He is known as the "Father of the National Parks."
  • Presidential Influence: In 1903, Muir camped with Roosevelt in Yosemite, advocating for federal protection. Roosevelt later doubled the number of national parks, attributing it to those three days.

R02_DICKINSON: "Three Poems" (Emily Dickinson)

  • Dickinson's Life and Style: Emily Dickinson (1830–1886) lived a reclusive life in Amherst, Massachusetts, yet observed the world with extraordinary sharpness from her home. She wrote nearly 1,800 poems, most published posthumously. Her unique punctuation, especially dashes, creates a distinctive, breathless rhythm.
  • "A Bird Came Down the Walk":
    • Keen Observation: The poem details a bird's actions: eating a worm "raw," drinking "Dew," hopping "sidewise" to let a beetle pass.
    • Personification/Interaction: The bird's "rapid eyes" are "frightened Beads." The speaker offers a crumb, prompting the bird to "unroll his feathers / And rowed him softer home."
    • Figurative Language: The bird's flight is compared to "Oars divide the Ocean, / Too silver for a seam" and "Butterflies... Leap, splashless, as they swim," emphasizing its effortless grace.
  • "I'm Nobody! Who Are You?":
    • Theme of Anonymity: The speaker embraces being "Nobody," seeing it as a shared, secret identity.
    • Critique of Public Life: She views being "Somebody" as "dreary" and "public," comparing it to a frog loudly announcing itself to an "admiring Bog." This suggests a preference for privacy and an disdain for self-promotion.
  • "Hope Is the Thing with Feathers":
    • Extended Metaphor: Hope is personified as a resilient "thing with feathers" (a bird) that "perches in the soul."
    • Persistence of Hope: It "sings the tune without the words— / And never stops—at all—."
    • Strength in Adversity: Hope is heard "sweetest—in the Gale—" and is so strong that even a severe storm ("sore must be the storm") cannot "abash" (discourage) it.
    • Selflessness: The bird of hope keeps "many warm" and, even in "Extremity" (the most desperate need), "It asked a crumb—of me," implying hope is freely given and never demanding.
  • Posthumous Discovery: Dickinson wrote on scraps and sewed her poems into "fascicles." Her sister Lavinia discovered 40 such booklets (about 1,800 poems) after her death.

R03_WORDSWORTH_FROST: "Two Poems about Nature and Choice"

  • Shared Inspiration: William Wordsworth (England) and Robert Frost (New England), though living a century apart, both found deep inspiration in the natural world and used nature to explore larger life ideas.
  • "I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud" (William Wordsworth):
    • Initial Experience: The poet, feeling solitary ("lonely as a cloud"), suddenly encounters a vast "host, of golden daffodils" by a lake, "Fluttering and dancing in the breeze."
    • Immense Scale: They are described as "Continuous as the stars" and in a "never-ending line," with "Ten thousand" seen at a glance, out-dancing the waves in "glee."
    • Lasting Impact: The poet gazes, initially unaware of the "wealth" this sight brings. Later, in solitude or pensive mood, the memory of the daffodils "flash upon that inward eye," filling his heart with pleasure and making it "dance with the daffodils." This highlights the enduring power of memory and nature's restorative quality.
  • "The Road Not Taken" (Robert Frost):
    • A Moment of Choice: The speaker encounters "Two roads diverged in a yellow wood" and must choose one, regretting that he "could not travel both."
    • Ambiguity of Choice: He initially describes taking the "other, as just as fair," perceiving it as "grassy and wanted wear" (less traveled). However, he immediately acknowledges that "as for that the passing there / Had worn them really about the same," and both paths "equally lay / In leaves no step had trodden black." This suggests the paths were more similar than initially claimed.
    • Reflection and Rationalization: The speaker acknowledges the finality of choices ("how way leads on to way, / I doubted whether I should ever come back").
    • Retrospective Framing: The famous lines, "I shall be telling this with a sigh / Somewhere ages and ages hence: / Two roads diverged in a wood, and I— / I took the one less traveled by, / And that has made all the difference," suggest a future narrative where the choice is given a grand, defining significance, even if the actual difference at the time was minimal or uncertain.
  • Poets' Backgrounds:
    • Wordsworth (1770–1850): A key English Romantic poet, advocated simple language and drawing on personal nature experiences.
    • Frost (1874–1963): Beloved American poet, known for seemingly simple poems with underlying depth.
  • Wordsworth's Inspiration: The daffodil poem was inspired by a real sighting with his sister Dorothy on April 15, 1802, along Ullswater in England's Lake District.

R04_THOREAU: "Where I Lived and What I Lived For" (Adapted from Henry David Thoreau, Walden)

  • Walden Experiment: In 1845, Henry David Thoreau moved to a small, unfinished cabin beside Walden Pond near Concord, Massachusetts, living alone for two years. His book Walden chronicles this experience and his philosophy.
  • The Setting: He describes his "auroral" (golden and fresh) cabin, initially a simple "defence against the rain," and the serene beauty of Walden Pond, which he compares to a "tarn" (mountain lake), with mist "stealthily withdrawing."
  • Core Motivation: Thoreau explicitly states his reason for going to the woods: "I went to the woods because I wished to live deliberately, to front only the essential facts of life, and see if I could not learn what it had to teach, and not, when I came to die, discover that I had not lived."
  • Rejection of Non-Life: He did not want to live "what was not life," or "practise resignation."
  • Embrace of Essential Experience: His goal was to "live deep and suck out all the marrow of life," emphasizing a profound, essential engagement with existence.
  • Call for Simplicity: The excerpt ends with the powerful declaration: "Simplicity, simplicity, simplici," underscoring his central philosophical tenet.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast John Muir's and William Wordsworth's experiences with nature. How do their descriptions and emotional responses to the natural world differ or align?
  2. What does Henry David Thoreau mean by "living deliberately," and how does his experience at Walden Pond serve as an example of this philosophy?
  3. Emily Dickinson's poems often focus on small, ordinary details. How do "A Bird Came Down the Walk" and "Hope Is the Thing with Feathers" illustrate her unique way of finding profound meaning in the mundane?
  4. Analyze the theme of choice in Robert Frost's "The Road Not Taken." Does the speaker truly take the "less traveled by" path, or is the poem more about the human tendency to retroactively assign significance to decisions?
  5. Across all these selections, what common messages or themes emerge about humanity's relationship with the natural world? How do these authors collectively argue for the importance of connecting with nature?
