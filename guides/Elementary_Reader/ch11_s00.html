The Civil War: Voices of a Nation Divided

Overview

This section delves into the profound impact and multifaceted experiences of the Civil War through the words of key historical figures. It presents powerful texts from President Abraham Lincoln, soldier Sullivan Ballou, hymn writer Julia Ward Howe, and poet Walt Whitman, offering diverse perspectives on the conflict's purpose, the human cost, the moral imperative of the Union, and the national grief following its conclusion.

Key Concepts

  • The Enduring Nation and Equality: Abraham Lincoln's speeches underscore the Civil War as a test of whether a nation founded on liberty and the principle that "all men are created equal" could survive. He calls for a "new birth of freedom" and the preservation of democratic government.
  • Sacrifice and Devotion: The texts highlight the immense personal sacrifice made by soldiers and citizens. Lincoln honors those who "gave the last full measure of devotion" at Gettysburg, while Sullivan Ballou's letter profoundly expresses a soldier's willingness to die for his country despite his deep love for his family.
  • Divine Providence and Moral Cause: Julia Ward Howe's "Battle Hymn of the Republic" frames the Union cause as a righteous, divinely ordained struggle against injustice, emphasizing God's judgment and the fight to make "men free."
  • Reconciliation and Lasting Peace: Lincoln's Second Inaugural Address articulates a vision for healing and unity after the war, urging the nation to act "with malice toward none; with charity for all" to achieve a "just, and a lasting peace."
  • Grief and Remembrance: The nation's sorrow for its fallen is a central theme. Lincoln dedicates a cemetery for the dead, while Whitman's "O Captain! My Captain!" poignantly captures the collective mourning for President Lincoln, using the metaphor of a fallen ship's captain.
  • The Human Cost of War: Ballou's letter and the sheer number of casualties mentioned at Gettysburg reveal the devastating personal and societal impact of the conflict, demonstrating that behind grand ideals lay profound individual suffering.

Important Terms

  • Gettysburg Address: A two-minute speech delivered by Abraham Lincoln in November 1863 at the dedication of the Soldiers' National Cemetery in Gettysburg, Pennsylvania, four months after the pivotal battle. It redefined the purpose of the Civil War, linking it to the Declaration of Independence and the ideal of human equality.
  • Second Inaugural Address: Abraham Lincoln's speech delivered in March 1865, as the Civil War was nearing its end. It reflected on the causes and meaning of the war and called for reconciliation and healing with "malice toward none; with charity for all."
  • First Battle of Bull Run: The first major land battle of the Civil War, fought on July 21, 1861, near Manassas, Virginia. It resulted in a Confederate victory and shocked both sides into realizing the war would be much longer and bloodier than initially expected.
  • Emancipation Proclamation: An executive order issued by Abraham Lincoln on January 1, 1863, declaring all enslaved people in Confederate-held territory to be free. It shifted the war's purpose to include the abolition of slavery.
  • Battle Hymn of the Republic: A patriotic anthem of the Union during the Civil War, written by Julia Ward Howe in 1862. It connected the Union cause to a divine mission for justice and freedom.
  • Elegy: A poem or song of serious reflection, typically a lament for the dead. Walt Whitman's "O Captain! My Captain!" is a famous example.
  • "Four score and seven years ago": The opening phrase of the Gettysburg Address, referring to 87 years prior to 1863, specifically 1776, the year the Declaration of Independence was signed.
  • Omnipotence: The quality of having unlimited or very great power, often attributed to God. Sullivan Ballou uses it to describe the power of God.
  • Vintagethe grape harvest: In the "Battle Hymn," it refers metaphorically to God's judgment, where the "grapes of wrath are stored."
  • Contemners: Those who show contempt or disdain. Used in the "Battle Hymn" in the context of divine judgment.
  • Rack: In "O Captain! My Captain!" it refers to the aftermath of a storm, symbolizing the trials and tribulations of the Civil War.
  • Keel: The bottom beam of a ship, representing the foundation or strength of the vessel. In Whitman's poem, it symbolizes the enduring nation.

Detailed Notes

CHAPTER 11: WAR AND REMEMBRANCE: THE CIVIL WAR

This chapter explores the profound impact of the Civil War through primary source documents, focusing on how individuals experienced, interpreted, and remembered the conflict.

Words of a President: Abraham Lincoln (1863, 1865)

Context: Abraham Lincoln served as the 16th President of the United States during its greatest crisis. A self-taught lawyer, he guided the Union through the Civil War and issued the Emancipation Proclamation. He was assassinated shortly after the war's end in April 1865.

The Gettysburg Address (November 19, 1863):

  • Occasion: Dedication of the Soldiers' National Cemetery at Gettysburg, Pennsylvania, four months after the exceptionally bloody Battle of Gettysburg (July 1-3, 1863), which resulted in over 50,000 casualties.
  • Length & Impact: Although only 272 words and lasting about two minutes, it became one of the most famous speeches in history, distilling the essence of American ideals and the war's purpose. Edward Everett, the main orator that day, spoke for two hours but recognized Lincoln's profound message.
  • Key Themes:
    • Founding Principles: Re-establishes the nation's founding on "Liberty" and the proposition that "all men are created equal," tying the war back to the Declaration of Independence.
    • Test of the Nation: Describes the Civil War as a test of whether a nation "so conceived and so dedicated, can long endure."
    • Sacrifice and Dedication: States that the living cannot truly dedicate the ground, as the soldiers who fought there have already consecrated it with their lives. It's the soldiers' actions, not the speakers' words, that truly hallow the ground.
    • "Unfinished Work": Calls upon the living to be dedicated to the "great task remaining before us" – ensuring that the fallen "shall not have died in vain."
    • "New Birth of Freedom": Envisions a post-war America with greater freedom, implicitly addressing slavery's end and the expansion of liberty.
    • Preservation of Democracy: Concludes with the enduring vision of "government of the people, by the people, for the people, shall not perish from the earth."

From the Second Inaugural Address (March 4, 1865):

  • Occasion: Delivered shortly before the war concluded, anticipating Union victory.
  • Key Themes:
    • Reconciliation: Urges a spirit of healing and unity for the post-war nation: "With malice toward none; with charity for all."
    • Duty to Care: Emphasizes the national responsibility to "bind up the nation's wounds" and care for veterans, widows, and orphans.
    • Just and Lasting Peace: Expresses a desire to "achieve and cherish a just, and a lasting peace" both domestically and internationally.
    • Divine Will: Reflects on the role of God in the conflict, suggesting both sides may have misjudged His will.

A Soldier's Letter: Sullivan Ballou (July 14, 1861)

Context: Written by Major Sullivan Ballou, a lawyer and father of two, one week before the First Battle of Bull Run (July 21, 1861). He had a strong premonition of his own death, which tragically came true in that battle. The letter was found among his belongings.

Key Themes:

  • Patriotism vs. Personal Love: Ballou expresses deep love for his wife, Sarah, describing it as "deathless," yet acknowledges that his "love of Country comes over me like a strong wind" compelling him to the battlefield.
  • Commitment to the Cause: He has "no misgivings" about the Union cause, believing in the importance of maintaining the government and paying a "debt" to those who fought in the Revolution.
  • Anticipation of Death: He writes, "Lest I should not be able to write you again, I feel impelled to write a few lines that may fall under your eye when I shall be no more."
  • Promise of Continued Presence: He beautifully reassures Sarah that if the dead can return, he will "always be near you," his spirit manifesting in soft breezes.
  • Hope for Reunion: Asks Sarah not to mourn but to "think I am gone and wait for thee, for we shall meet again."

Significance: This letter provides a deeply personal and poignant insight into the immense emotional cost of the war, showcasing the internal struggle of a soldier torn between family and duty, and facing mortality head-on. It gained widespread recognition after being featured in Ken Burns's documentary, "The Civil War."

The First Battle of Bull Run (July 21, 1861):

  • The first major battle of the war, expected to be an easy Union victory.
  • Instead, it was chaotic, fierce, and resulted in a shocking Union defeat.
  • This battle shattered illusions of a short war and demonstrated its brutal reality.

Battle Hymn of the Republic: Julia Ward Howe (1862)

Context: Julia Ward Howe, a poet and reformer, visited Union army camps near Washington in November 1861. Inspired by soldiers singing "John Brown's Body," she wrote new, more profound lyrics for the stirring tune in February 1862. It was published in The Atlantic Monthly.

Key Themes:

  • Divine Judgment and Righteous War: Portrays the Union cause as a holy war, with God actively involved, "trampling out the vintage where the grapes of wrath are stored" and "sifting out the hearts of men."
  • Moral Imperative for Freedom: Connects Christ's sacrifice ("As He died to make men holy") to the soldiers' sacrifice to "make men free."
  • God on the Union Side: Asserts that "His truth is marching on" and "Our God is marching on," giving the Union soldiers a sense of divine purpose and invincibility.
  • Call to Action: Urges the soul to "be swift... to answer Him! be jubilant, my feet!" in the march for justice.

Significance: The "Battle Hymn" became a powerful anthem for the Union cause, imbuing the conflict with religious and moral fervor. It rallied soldiers and civilians alike by framing the struggle as a crusade for God-given freedom. Julia Ward Howe later became a prominent advocate for women's rights.

O Captain! My Captain!: Walt Whitman (1865)

Context: Walt Whitman, a poet and volunteer nurse during the Civil War, wrote this elegy in April 1865, shortly after President Lincoln's assassination on April 14, just five days after the war ended. The nation was plunged into mourning.

Key Metaphor:

  • The poem uses an extended metaphor:
    • "Captain": Represents Abraham Lincoln.
    • "Ship": Represents the United States.
    • "Fearful trip" / "terrible voyage": Symbolizes the Civil War.
    • "Port" / "prize": Represents the successful conclusion of the war and preservation of the Union.

Key Themes:

  • Triumph and Tragedy: The poem contrasts the joy of victory ("the prize we sought is won," "bells I hear," "people all exulting") with the profound sorrow of losing the leader who guided the nation through its crisis ("O the bleeding drops of red," "Fallen cold and dead").
  • National Mourning: Expresses deep personal and national grief over Lincoln's death, emphasizing the shock and disbelief that the "father" of the nation is gone just as peace is achieved.
  • Enduring Legacy: While the Captain is gone, the "vessel grim and daring" (the nation) has endured, suggesting that Lincoln's work enabled the nation's survival.
  • Elegy: As an elegy, it serves as a lament for the dead, a poetic expression of grief and remembrance.

Significance: "O Captain! My Captain!" is one of the most famous elegies in American literature, powerfully capturing the nation's collective shock and sorrow at Lincoln's assassination. It reflects the deep respect and affection many felt for Lincoln, and the sense that the triumph of peace was tragically overshadowed by his loss.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast Abraham Lincoln's primary message in the Gettysburg Address with that of his Second Inaugural Address. How do his concerns and visions for the nation evolve or remain consistent across these two crucial speeches?
  2. How does Sullivan Ballou's personal letter to his wife provide a humanizing perspective on the sacrifices made during the Civil War, complementing the broader historical narratives?
  3. Analyze how Julia Ward Howe's "Battle Hymn of the Republic" uses religious imagery and themes to rally support for the Union cause. What moral or spiritual arguments does it put forth?
  4. Walt Whitman's "O Captain! My Captain!" uses an extended metaphor to express national grief. Explain this metaphor and discuss how the poem captures both the triumph of the Union victory and the tragedy of Lincoln's assassination.
  5. Considering all four documents (Lincoln's speeches, Ballou's letter, Howe's hymn, and Whitman's poem), discuss how they collectively portray the emotional, ideological, and human cost of the Civil War, from its early days to its immediate aftermath.
