Study Guide: Creation and the Oldest Stories

Overview

This section explores ancient stories that explain the world and teach moral lessons. It presents the biblical account of creation from Genesis, two classic fables by Aesop that feature animal characters and clear morals, and a poetic reflection on creation from Psalm 19. Together, these selections highlight humanity's long-standing tradition of using narrative to understand existence, impart wisdom, and express wonder.

Key Concepts

  • Ancient Storytelling Traditions: Humans have told stories for millennia to explain the world, teach lessons, and express beliefs, often through oral traditions before writing was common.
  • Creation Narratives: Stories that explain how the world, life, and humanity began. The Genesis account details a seven-day process initiated by God.
  • Fables and Morals: Short fictional stories, often featuring anthropomorphic animals, designed to illustrate a universal truth or teach a practical lesson (a "moral").
  • Poetic Expression of Nature: Literature that uses vivid imagery and figurative language to describe the natural world and reflect on its spiritual or philosophical significance, as seen in the Psalms.
  • Literary and Cultural Significance: Certain ancient texts, like the King James Bible and Aesop's Fables, have profoundly influenced language, literature, and thought across centuries and cultures.

Important Terms

  • creation: The act of bringing something into existence; refers specifically to the world and everything in it as made by God in the biblical account.
  • dominion: Authority or rule over something; sovereignty. In Genesis, humanity is given dominion over the earth and its creatures.
  • fable: A short story, often with animal characters, that teaches a moral lesson.
  • firmament: The sky or heavens, especially as seen from the earth. In Genesis, it refers to the expanse God created between the waters.
  • hare: A fast-running animal similar to a rabbit, but typically larger. A character in Aesop's "The Hare and the Tortoise."
  • moral: The lesson or message of a story or fable.
  • King James Bible: An English translation of the Christian Bible completed in 1611, known for its elegant language and significant influence on English literature.
  • Psalm: A sacred song or poem, especially one from the Book of Psalms in the Bible.

Detailed Notes

I. In the Beginning: Stories and Origins

  • The Nature of Early Stories: Humans have always used stories to explain phenomena (like creation), characterize animals, and understand their relationship with the divine.
  • Focus of the Section: This unit presents foundational stories from ancient times, including a biblical creation narrative, fables, and a psalm.

II. The Creation (Genesis 1:1–2:3, King James Version abridged)

  • Context: This passage is from Genesis, the first book of the Bible. It describes the origin of the universe and life.
  • Before You Read - Noticing Patterns: The reader is encouraged to observe the rhythmic language and the repeating pattern of God's creative acts across seven days.
  • The Narrative of Creation:
    • Initial State: "Without form, and void; and darkness was upon the face of the deep." The Spirit of God was present over the waters.
    • Day 1: God speaks light into existence, separating it from darkness. He names them Day and Night.
    • Day 2: God creates the "firmament" (sky/expanse) to separate the waters above from the waters below, calling it Heaven.
    • Day 3: God gathers the waters to reveal dry land, calling them Earth and Seas. The land is made to produce vegetation (grass, herbs, fruit trees).
    • Day 4: God creates the "two great lights" (the sun to rule the day, the moon to rule the night) and the stars to provide light and mark seasons.
    • Day 5: God creates living creatures in the water (great whales and other moving creatures) and birds in the air.
    • Day 6: God creates humanity ("man and female") in "our image, after our likeness," giving them "dominion" (authority) over all other creatures on land, sea, and air. God declares all creation "very good."
    • Day 7: God rests from all His work, blessing and sanctifying the seventh day.
  • About the King James Bible:
    • Translation: Translated into English in 1611 for King James I of England by a team of scholars.
    • Language: Its language is over 400 years old, which means some words (like "firmament" and "dominion") may seem unfamiliar to modern readers but are explained in the text.
    • Significance: It is considered one of the most important and influential books in the English language due to its literary quality and historical impact.

III. Two Fables by Aesop

  • Context: Aesop's Fables are ancient Greek stories known for their moral lessons.
  • Before You Read - What is a Fable?: A fable is defined as a short story that teaches a lesson (a moral), often using animal characters that act and talk like people.
  • "The Hare and the Tortoise":
    • Plot: A boastful Hare ridicules a slow Tortoise. The Tortoise challenges the Hare to a race. The Hare, overconfident, takes a nap during the race. The steady Tortoise continues without stopping and reaches the goal first.
    • Moral: "Slow but steady wins the race." This emphasizes the value of perseverance and consistency over speed and overconfidence.
  • "The Shepherd's Boy and the Wolf":
    • Plot: A Shepherd-boy repeatedly tricks villagers by crying "Wolf!" when there is no danger, then laughs at them. When a wolf genuinely attacks his flock, his cries for help are ignored because he has lost the villagers' trust.
    • Moral: "There is no believing a liar, even when he speaks the truth." This highlights the consequence of dishonesty and the destruction of credibility.
  • About Aesop:
    • Origin: A Greek storyteller, believed to have lived around 600 BC (over 2,500 years ago).
    • Tradition: Legend says he was a slave who earned his freedom through his clever stories.
    • Historical Debate: It is uncertain whether Aesop was a real person or a legendary figure, but the fables attributed to him have been universally loved and translated into almost every language.
  • "Did You Know" - Cultural Impact:
    • Idioms: Aesop's fables have deeply influenced English language and common phrases, such as "sour grapes" (from "The Fox and the Grapes") and "a wolf in sheep's clothing" (from another fable).
    • Literary Influence: Writers across centuries and cultures have retold these fables, including French poet Jean de La Fontaine in the 1600s, and they share patterns with American "Brer Rabbit" tales.

IV. Psalm 19:1–6 (King James Version)

  • Context: Psalm 19 is part of the Book of Psalms, a collection of 150 poems and songs from the Bible, originally intended to be sung.
  • Before You Read - Poetic Devices: The reader is directed to notice how the poet personifies (compares to a person) the sun.
  • The Psalm's Message:
    • Nature Declares God's Glory: The heavens ("firmament") visually demonstrate God's "handywork" and glory without spoken words ("no speech nor language").
    • Constant Communication: Day and night continually convey knowledge of God's power and existence to the whole world, their "voice" (message) reaching "to the end of the world."
    • The Sun as a Metaphor: God has created a "tabernacle" (dwelling place) for the sun. The sun is then personified:
      • It is like a "bridegroom coming out of his chamber," joyful and splendid.
      • It "rejoiceth as a strong man to run a race," eager and powerful in its daily course.
      • Its "circuit" (path) extends across the entire heaven, and nothing can escape its heat.
  • Themes: This psalm celebrates God's glory revealed through the vastness and order of the natural world, particularly the heavens and the sun, seeing them as constant witnesses to divine power and wisdom.

Review Questions

  1. How do the stories from Genesis, Aesop, and Psalm 19 each attempt to explain or interpret aspects of the world or human behavior?
  2. Describe the seven-day pattern of creation presented in Genesis. What is significant about the sixth day and the seventh day?
  3. What is the main characteristic of a fable, and how do "The Hare and the Tortoise" and "The Shepherd's Boy and the Wolf" exemplify this? What moral lesson does each fable teach?
  4. How does Psalm 19 use the imagery of the heavens and the sun to convey a message about God? What specific human qualities are attributed to the sun?
  5. Based on the "About the King James Bible" and "About Aesop" sections, discuss why these ancient texts continue to be important in literature and culture today.
