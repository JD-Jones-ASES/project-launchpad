Chapter 12: Rebuilding: Reconstruction and New Voices

Overview

This section explores the challenging period of Reconstruction after the Civil War through the lens of influential "new voices." It features the autobiography of Booker T. Washington, who recounts his journey from slavery to leadership and the founding of the Tuskegee Institute; two powerful poems by Paul Laurence Dunbar that express hidden suffering and the longing for freedom; and the beginning of O. Henry's short story "The Last Leaf," which hints at resilience, sacrifice, and the power of art amidst hardship.

Key Concepts

  • The Aftermath of Slavery: The realities faced by four million formerly enslaved people after 1865, including the struggle to build new lives and the enduring hardships.
  • The Power of Education and Self-Help: Booker T. Washington's belief in vocational training and practical skills as a path to economic independence and self-sufficiency for African Americans.
  • Hidden Suffering and Resilience: Paul Laurence Dunbar's use of metaphors (the "mask" and the "caged bird") to articulate the internal pain and longing for freedom experienced by African Americans, even when outwardly presenting a different face.
  • Art as Expression and Act of Love: The role of poetry in giving voice to unspoken emotions, and the potential for artistic sacrifice and human connection, as hinted in O. Henry's story.
  • The Will to Live: The psychological battle against despair, as seen in Johnsy's struggle with pneumonia and her belief that her fate is tied to nature.

Important Terms

  • Plantation: A large farm, especially in the Southern United States, where crops like cotton or tobacco were grown, primarily by the labor of enslaved people.
  • Emancipation Proclamation: An executive order issued by President Abraham Lincoln on January 1, 1863, declaring all enslaved people in Confederate states to be free.
  • Tuskegee Institute: A historically black college founded by Booker T. Washington in Alabama, focused on vocational training, practical skills, and agricultural education.
  • Metaphor: A figure of speech in which a word or phrase is applied to an object or action to which it is not literally applicable, for example, "a mask that grins and lies" or "a caged bird."
  • Pneumonia: A serious infection of the lungs, common and often deadly in the era described in "The Last Leaf."
  • "We Wear the Mask": A poem by Paul Laurence Dunbar that uses the metaphor of a mask to represent the forced facade of cheerfulness and contentment African Americans often presented to the world, hiding their true sorrow and pain.
  • "Caged Bird": A central metaphor in Dunbar's poem "Sympathy," symbolizing the oppression and longing for freedom experienced by African Americans.
  • Greenwich Village: A neighborhood in New York City historically known as a hub for artists and bohemians, serving as the setting for O. Henry's "The Last Leaf."
  • O. Henry: The pen name of William Sydney Porter, an American short story writer famous for his witty, often ironic, and surprise endings.
  • Surprise Ending: A narrative technique in which the conclusion of a story features an unexpected twist or revelation that changes the reader's understanding of events.

Detailed Notes

Booker T. Washington: From Slavery to Freedom and Education

  • Early Life in Slavery:
    • Born enslaved on a plantation in Franklin County, Virginia, around 1856.
    • Described his early environment as "miserable, desolate, and discouraging."
    • Lived in a small, rough log cabin (14x16 feet) with his mother, brother, and sister. The cabin also served as the plantation kitchen.
    • Conditions were harsh: no glass windows (only openings), a poorly hung door with large cracks, making the room uncomfortable.
    • His mother was the plantation cook.
    • As a young child, he performed various tasks: cleaning yards, carrying water to field workers, and making a dreaded three-mile weekly trip to the mill to grind corn.
    • Had no formal schooling as a slave, but a vivid memory of seeing children in a schoolhouse instilled in him a deep desire for education, which he viewed as "paradise."
    • Recalled the "torture" of wearing a new flax shirt, made from the roughest, cheapest part of the plant, which felt like "a dozen or more chestnut burrs" against his skin.
  • The Day of Emancipation:
    • Experienced the reading of the Emancipation Proclamation by a United States officer.
    • This moment declared him and all other enslaved people free to go "when and where we pleased."
    • Witnessed his mother's profound joy and tears as she kissed her children, explaining that this was the day she had long prayed to see.
    • Recognized that freedom brought a new challenge: how to "build a life in freedom."
  • Legacy and the Tuskegee Institute:
    • After emancipation, Washington pursued education with immense perseverance, sometimes walking hundreds of miles to reach a classroom.
    • He founded the Tuskegee Institute in Alabama, a pioneering school dedicated to training African Americans in practical skills such as trades, teaching, and agriculture.
    • His philosophy emphasized self-help, vocational training, and economic independence as crucial for the advancement of African Americans.
    • His autobiography, Up From Slavery, became widely read and influenced generations.
    • By 1915, Tuskegee had grown from a single shanty with 30 students to over a hundred buildings and nearly 1,500 students, many of the buildings designed and constructed by the students themselves.

Paul Laurence Dunbar: Poetic Voices of Hidden Suffering

  • Background:
    • Son of formerly enslaved parents, born in 1872, grew up in Dayton, Ohio.
    • Began writing poetry as a teenager and became one of the first nationally recognized African American poets.
    • Explored the complexities of Black life in America, including both joys and sorrows.
    • Wrote in both standard English and African American dialect, capturing authentic voices.
  • "We Wear the Mask":
    • Uses the central metaphor of a "mask that grins and lies" to represent the facade African Americans were often compelled to present to the world.
    • This mask conceals "torn and bleeding hearts" and "tortured souls" beneath a smiling exterior.
    • The poem expresses the immense emotional cost of hiding one's true suffering ("This debt we pay to human guile").
    • It highlights the desire to prevent the "world" from understanding the depth of their pain ("Why should the world be over-wise, / In counting all our tears and sighs?").
    • Ultimately, the mask is a means of survival, a way to navigate a prejudiced society while inwardly crying out to a higher power.
  • "Sympathy" (The Caged Bird):
    • Employs the powerful metaphor of a "caged bird" to symbolize the experience of oppression and the yearning for freedom.
    • Contrasts the bird's confined existence with the beauty and freedom of the natural world outside its cage.
    • The bird's actions—beating its wings against "cruel bars" until "blood is red"—vividly convey the pain and desperation of being trapped.
    • The "old, old scars" symbolize historical and ongoing suffering.
    • The bird's "song" is not one of "joy or glee," but rather "a prayer" and "a plea" for freedom, emanating from "its heart's deep core."
    • This poem later inspired the title of Maya Angelou's famous autobiography, I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings.
  • Impact and Recognition:
    • Published his first book of poems, Oak and Ivy, at age twenty, selling copies to passengers while working as an elevator operator.
    • Gained national recognition after meeting Frederick Douglass, who called him "the most promising young colored man in America."
    • Despite dying young at 33, he left a significant legacy of poetry, novels, and short stories.

O. Henry: "The Last Leaf" – Art, Hope, and Sacrifice

  • Author's Style:
    • O. Henry (William Sydney Porter) was a renowned master of the short story, particularly famous for his "surprise endings."
    • His stories often contained witty dialogue, irony, and unexpected twists that recontextualized the entire narrative.
  • Setting and Characters:
    • The story is set in Greenwich Village, New York City, a hub for struggling artists.
    • Sue and Johnsy (Joanna): Two young female artists who share a studio apartment. Johnsy is from California, Sue from Maine, and they bonded over shared artistic tastes.
    • Mr. Pneumonia: Personified as a "cold, unseen stranger" stalking the artists' colony, highlighting the severe and often fatal nature of the illness in that era.
    • Old Behrman: A failed painter in his sixties who lives on the ground floor, described with a "Michael Angelo's Moses beard."
  • The Plot Setup and Johnsy's Despair:
    • Johnsy falls gravely ill with pneumonia, and her doctors give her a slim chance of recovery (one in ten), emphasizing the critical importance of her "will to live."
    • As autumn progresses, Johnsy becomes fixated on an old ivy vine outside her window, which is rapidly losing its leaves.
    • She develops a severe delusion: she believes she will die when the last leaf falls from the vine. This belief saps her will to fight the illness.
    • Sue tries desperately to encourage Johnsy and maintain her spirits, while Johnsy grows weary of waiting and wants to "turn loose my hold on everything."
  • Hint of Sacrifice:
    • The introductory text explicitly states that "an unlikely sacrifice saves her life," foreshadowing a significant act of selflessness, likely involving Old Behrman, the failed painter. This act would embody the story's theme of art as an act of love and hope.

Review Questions

  1. What challenges did formerly enslaved people like Booker T. Washington face after the Civil War, and how did Washington propose to overcome them?
  2. Compare and contrast the metaphors of the "mask" and the "caged bird" used by Paul Laurence Dunbar. What do these metaphors reveal about the experience of African Americans in post-Reconstruction America?
  3. How did Booker T. Washington's early experiences as a slave, particularly his observations about schooling, shape his later life's mission and the founding of the Tuskegee Institute?
  4. In "The Last Leaf," Johnsy ties her will to live to the falling leaves of an ivy vine. What does this suggest about the role of hope and mental state in physical recovery, and how might art (or an act of love) play a role in restoring that hope?
  5. All three readings introduce figures who, in different ways, faced immense hardship or social constraints. Discuss how each individual (Washington, Dunbar, and the implied sacrifice in O. Henry's story) demonstrates resilience, perseverance, or an act of love in response to their circumstances.
