Chapter 17: The Open Page: Reading, Writing, and the World Ahead

Overview

This final unit offers a reflective journey through classic American literature and wisdom. It explores themes of childhood ingenuity with Mark Twain, the power of ambition and nature's grandeur on the Mississippi River, and profound meditations on endings, the passing of time, and humanity's place in the natural world through poetry by Robert Frost and Sara Teasdale, culminating in the timeless wisdom of Ecclesiastes.

Key Concepts

  • Childhood and Ingenuity: Explores the cleverness and perspective of youth, particularly in turning work into play.
  • American Humor and Realism: Introduces Mark Twain's characteristic wit, sharp observation, and groundbreaking use of authentic American dialects to capture the sounds of real speech.
  • Ambition and Dreams: Illustrates the powerful, lasting dreams of boyhood and how the Mississippi River shaped a writer's identity and pen name.
  • Nature and the Passage of Time: Contemplates the beauty and transience of nature, the cyclical rhythms of seasons, and nature's indifferent continuity.
  • Reflection on Life and Endings: Offers meditations on duty, the inevitability of change, and the idea that all aspects of human experience—joy, sorrow, creation, destruction—have their appointed time.
  • Literary Craft: Highlights different literary forms (novel, memoir, poetry, wisdom literature) and techniques such as dialect, imagery, and unique rhyme schemes.

Important Terms

  • Humorist: A writer or performer whose aim is to make people laugh. Mark Twain is considered a master American humorist.
  • Dialect: A particular form of a language that is peculiar to a specific region or social group. Twain was a pioneer in using dialect to create realistic characters.
  • Memoir: A historical account or biography written from personal knowledge or special sources; a personal narrative of one's own life experiences.
  • Pen Name: An assumed name used by a writer instead of their real name; also known as a pseudonym. Samuel Langhorne Clemens used "Mark Twain."
  • Fathom: A unit of length equal to six feet, used in measuring the depth of water. "Mark twain" was a river term meaning two fathoms (twelve feet), the minimum safe depth for steamboats.
  • Stanza: A group of lines forming the basic recurring metrical unit in a poem; a verse.
  • Rhyme Pattern: The ordered pattern of rhymes at the ends of the lines of a poem or verse. Frost's "Stopping by Woods" uses a unique linked rhyme scheme.
  • Wisdom Literature: A genre of literature, common in the ancient Near East, characterized by sayings and teachings that offer guidance on how to live a good and meaningful life. Ecclesiastes is an example.

Detailed Notes

The Glorious Whitewasher (Adapted from Mark Twain, The Adventures of Tom Sawyer, 1876)

  • Author Background: Mark Twain (Samuel Langhorne Clemens) grew up in Hannibal, Missouri, which served as the inspiration for the fictional St. Petersburg. He is renowned as the greatest American humorist.
  • Plot Summary: On a bright Saturday morning, Tom Sawyer is faced with the dreaded chore of whitewashing a long fence. Initially despairing, he sees his friend Jim and attempts to bribe him to do the work. Jim, however, has been warned by "Ole Missis" (Aunt Polly) not to help Tom and risks punishment if he does. Tom, undeterred, thinks of a "magnificent inspiration" to turn the tedious work into an enviable activity. (The excerpt ends before the trick is fully revealed but sets up the premise of Tom's cleverness).
  • Key Themes: Childhood innocence and cunning, the burden of work, the power of persuasion, humor derived from everyday situations.
  • Literary Significance: This chapter showcases Twain's ability to capture the spirit of boyhood and use humor to critique adult expectations.
  • Twain's Use of Dialect: Twain was revolutionary in his authentic depiction of American speech. In this passage, Jim's dialogue reflects the dialect of enslaved people in Missouri, while Tom's reflects the everyday English of a white boy. This was a deliberate choice by Twain to record actual sounds of American speech rather than formal English, as noted in Huckleberry Finn.

The Boys' Ambition (Adapted from Mark Twain, Life on the Mississippi, 1883)

  • Genre: This is a memoir, meaning Twain is recounting his own true boyhood experiences and ambitions.
  • Author Background: Before becoming a writer, Twain's deepest desire was to be a steamboatman on the Mississippi River.
  • Central Idea: Twain describes the overwhelming, permanent ambition shared by all boys in his village: to become a steamboatman. Other ambitions (clowns, pirates) were fleeting, but the allure of the steamboat remained.
  • Impact of the Steamboat: The arrival of a steamboat transformed the sleepy, "drowsing" town into a bustling, excited place. The steamboat captain was an object of envy and admiration, representing a heroic figure. The town came alive with expectancy before its arrival and became "dead and empty" after its departure.
  • Twain's Pen Name: The name "Mark Twain" is a river term used by steamboat crews. It means "two fathoms" (twelve feet), indicating a safe depth for navigation. Samuel Clemens chose this pen name to honor his deep connection and love for the Mississippi River.

Three Poems of Reflection (Robert Frost, 1923; Sara Teasdale, 1920)

This section features three poems that reflect on endings, seasons, and the passage of time.

Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening (Robert Frost)

  • Setting: A quiet winter evening in snowy woods, away from human habitation.
  • Speaker's Dilemma: The speaker pauses to admire the beautiful, dark, and deep woods, feeling a pull towards rest and contemplation ("The darkest evening of the year"). However, he is reminded of his responsibilities and commitments ("promises to keep," "miles to go before I sleep").
  • Themes: The allure of solitude and nature, the tension between personal desire and duty, the contemplation of life's journey and ultimate rest (sleep often a metaphor for death).
  • Rhyme Scheme: Frost uses a unique, linked rhyme pattern. The third line of each stanza sets the rhyme for the next stanza (AABA BBCB CCDC). The final stanza breaks this pattern with all four lines rhyming (DDDD), bringing a sense of closure.

Nothing Gold Can Stay (Robert Frost)

  • Central Metaphor: "Nature's first green is gold" refers to the delicate, golden-green hue of new spring leaves and blossoms.
  • Theme: The poem reflects on the transient nature of beauty, innocence, and life itself. The most precious and beautiful things ("gold") are the most difficult to hold onto.
  • Allusions: "So Eden sank to grief" references the biblical story of the Garden of Eden, where an initial state of perfection and innocence was lost. "So dawn goes down to day" illustrates how even the beautiful start of a day gives way to ordinary daylight.
  • Message: Everything beautiful and pure is fleeting; it passes quickly, emphasizing the ephemeral quality of existence.

There Will Come Soft Rains (Sara Teasdale)

  • Historical Context: Written during World War I, the poem carries a powerful message about human conflict and nature's enduring presence.
  • Theme: The poem imagines a world without humanity, where nature continues its peaceful, beautiful cycles utterly indifferent to human existence or absence.
  • Imagery: Vivid sensory details of nature: soft rains, smell of the ground, swallows, frogs, plum trees, robins.
  • Message: Nature operates on its own timeline and according to its own laws. Human events, even catastrophic ones like war and extinction, are insignificant in the grand scheme of the natural world. "Not one would mind, neither bird nor tree / If mankind perished utterly."

To Every Thing There Is a Season (Ecclesiastes 3:1–8, King James Version)

  • Source: From the book of Ecclesiastes, one of the wisdom books of the Bible.
  • Author (Traditional): King Solomon.
  • Central Theme: This passage asserts that there is an appointed time and purpose for everything in life. It emphasizes the cyclical nature of human experience and the world.
  • Examples: It lists numerous contrasting pairs of experiences, such as birth and death, planting and plucking, weeping and laughing, mourning and dancing, getting and losing.
  • Message: Life is a series of seasons, and every event, whether joyful or sorrowful, has its rightful place and time. This offers a reflective and accepting perspective on the totality of human experience.

Review Questions

  1. How does Mark Twain use humor and dialect in "The Glorious Whitewasher" to develop Tom Sawyer's character and create a vivid sense of the setting?
  2. In "The Boys' Ambition," what does the steamboat symbolize for the boys and the town, and how does Twain's personal experience enhance the memoir's impact?
  3. Compare and contrast the views on the relationship between humanity and nature presented in Robert Frost's "Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening" and Sara Teasdale's "There Will Come Soft Rains."
  4. Both Frost's "Nothing Gold Can Stay" and the passage from Ecclesiastes deal with the passage of time and the transient nature of things. How do their messages differ or overlap, and what effect does each have on the reader?
  5. Across these selections, what overarching messages about life, duty, and the natural world emerge, and how do they connect to the idea of "reading, writing, and the world ahead"?
