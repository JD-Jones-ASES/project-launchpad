Chapter 4: Pilgrims and Explorers: Voyages to a New World Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the initial European encounters with the Americas through the firsthand accounts of pivotal figures. It details Christopher Columbus's "discovery" and his impressions of the land and its people, the Pilgrims' perilous journey across the Atlantic seeking religious freedom, and Captain John Smith's descriptions of the fertile Virginia landscape and the early struggles of the Jamestown settlement. These texts offer a glimpse into the motivations, challenges, and initial perceptions of those who embarked on voyages to a new world.

Key Concepts

  • Exploration and "Discovery": Christopher Columbus's voyage in 1492 led to the European "discovery" of lands previously unknown to them, initiating centuries of transatlantic exchange and colonization.
  • Motivations for Voyage: Explorers like Columbus sought new trade routes and wealth (gold, spices), while groups like the Pilgrims sought religious freedom and a place to worship as they chose.
  • Perilous Sea Journeys: Voyages across the Atlantic were long, dangerous, and unpredictable, fraught with violent storms, ship damage, and seasickness, as vividly described by William Bradford.
  • First Impressions of the New World: Early European accounts often highlighted the immense natural beauty, fertility, and abundant resources of the lands they encountered, as well as their initial perceptions of the indigenous peoples.
  • Colonization and Settlement Challenges: The establishment of permanent settlements like Jamestown faced severe difficulties, including disease, starvation, conflict with native populations, and the need for strong leadership to survive.
  • Primary Source Documentation: The letters and historical accounts from figures like Columbus, Bradford, and Smith provide invaluable primary sources for understanding this pivotal period in history.

Important Terms

  • unclothed: Described by Columbus as the state of the indigenous people he encountered, reflecting European cultural norms and perceptions.
  • caravel: A small, highly maneuverable sailing ship used by the Portuguese and Spanish for exploration in the 15th and 16th centuries, like Columbus's ships.
  • Pilgrims: A group of English Separatists who sailed to America on the Mayflower in 1620, seeking freedom to practice their religion.
  • Mayflower: The ship that carried the Pilgrims from England to Plymouth Colony in 1620.
  • shroudly: Severely or grievously; used by William Bradford to describe how the Mayflower was shaken by storms.
  • hull: To drift with sails furled, often done by a ship during a storm to maintain stability.
  • seel: A sudden roll or lurch of a ship, which in the Mayflower's case, caused a man to be thrown overboard.
  • halyards: Ropes or tackle used for raising and lowering sails or flags on a ship.
  • Cape Cod: The peninsula in Massachusetts where the Pilgrims first landed in 1620.
  • Jamestown: The first permanent English settlement in America, founded in Virginia in 1607.
  • Powhatan people: A powerful confederation of Algonquian-speaking Native American tribes in present-day Virginia, with whom the Jamestown colonists interacted.
  • harbor: A sheltered body of water where ships can anchor safely.
  • perilous: Full of danger or risk; extremely risky.
  • provision: Supplies of food and other necessities, especially for a journey.
  • tempest: A violent storm with strong winds, often over the sea.
  • voyage: A long journey, especially one by sea or in space.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Voyages to a New World

This chapter invites readers to imagine the bravery and uncertainty of embarking on a sea voyage to an unknown land. It introduces the experiences of both explorers seeking new routes and wealth, and pilgrims seeking religious freedom, highlighting what they found and how they documented their journeys.

Christopher Columbus's Voyage and Letter (1493)

  • Motivation and Journey: In 1492, Columbus sailed west from Spain, intending to reach Asia. His voyage took 33 days, during which he commanded a fleet given by the King and Queen of Spain.
  • The "Discovery" and Claims: Instead of Asia, he encountered islands previously unknown to Europeans. He immediately took possession of these islands for the Spanish monarchs, naming the first one "San Salvador."
  • Impressions of Native People: Columbus described the indigenous people as "unclothed," "remarkably timid," and "generous." He noted they lacked iron, steel, or weapons. Intriguingly, he observed their belief that he and his men "came from the heavens." He mistakenly called them "Indians" because he believed he had reached the Indies (Asia).
  • Impressions of the Land: Columbus was profoundly impressed by the natural beauty and fertility of the islands, particularly "Juananow" (Cuba). He noted "exceedingly fertile" land, beautiful harbors, wide rivers, high land, and "lofty mountains." He described lush forests with "a thousand kinds" of trees, some in bloom, some bearing fruit, and vibrant birdlife even in November. He also mentioned "mines of metals" and a large population.
  • Promises to the Crown: Columbus pledged to provide the King and Queen with "as much gold as they need," along with spices, cotton, and "a thousand other things of value," emphasizing the potential riches of these new lands.
  • Historical Impact: Columbus's letter was quickly printed and widely distributed across Europe, making him famous and sparking further European exploration and colonization of the Americas.

The Pilgrims' Voyage and William Bradford's Account (c. 1650)

  • Motivation: The Pilgrims were English families who sailed to America in 1620 aboard the Mayflower to escape religious persecution and worship God according to their own beliefs.
  • The Perilous Journey (September 6 onwards): The voyage began with favorable winds but soon encountered "fierce storms." The ship was "shroudly shaken," its upper works became leaky, and a main beam in the midships cracked, leading to fears that the ship might not complete the journey.
  • Overcoming Challenges: Despite the damage, the crew and passengers used a large "iron screw" brought from Holland to raise the cracked beam back into place. They "committed themselves to the will of God" and resolved to continue.
  • Dangers at Sea: During intense storms, they were forced to "hull" (drift with sails furled). A young man named John Howland was dramatically swept overboard by a "seel" (roll) of the ship but miraculously caught hold of the topsail halyards and was pulled back to safety.
  • Arrival and Gratitude: After a long and difficult journey, they finally reached land at "Cape Cod." Upon arrival in a good harbor, they "fell upon their knees and blessed the God of heaven" for their safe deliverance across the "vast and furious ocean."
  • William Bradford's Legacy: Bradford later became governor of Plymouth Colony and wrote Of Plymouth Plantation, a vital historical record of the Pilgrims' journey and early settlement for future generations.

Sailors, Settlers, and the Virginia Colony

  • Psalm 107:23-31: This biblical passage reflects the experience of seafarers. It describes those who "go down to the sea in ships" witnessing God's "wonders in the deep" but also facing "stormy wind" and high waves that make their "soul... melted because of trouble." It speaks of crying out to the Lord, who brings calm, stillness, and safe arrival to a "desired haven," prompting praise for God's goodness. This psalm would have resonated deeply with the religiously motivated voyagers.

  • Captain John Smith's Arrival in Virginia (1607):

    • First Impressions of the Land: Upon discovering the first river in Virginia, Smith described the land as neither "desert nor barren." He was "almost ravished at the first sight" of its beauty, noting plentiful trees, "sweet-smelling blossoms," "fresh waters," "fair meadows," and "goodly tall trees." He concluded that "Heaven and earth never agreed better to frame a place for man's habitation."
    • Founding Jamestown: Smith was instrumental in founding Jamestown in 1607, the first permanent English settlement in America.
    • Early Struggles of Jamestown: The colony faced immense hardship. Of the initial 104 colonists, only 38 survived the first year due to widespread disease, starvation, and conflicts with the Powhatan people.
    • Smith's Leadership: Captain John Smith's strict leadership was crucial for the colony's survival. He organized work, ensuring everyone contributed, and established trade with the Powhatan people for food. His famous rule was: "He that will not work shall not eat."
    • Historical Impact: Smith's vivid writings encouraged more English settlers to venture to the New World, and Jamestown eventually grew into the significant colony of Virginia.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast Christopher Columbus's first impressions of the New World with Captain John Smith's first impressions of Virginia. What natural features or qualities did they both emphasize, and what were some differences in their observations or immediate concerns?
  2. What were the primary motivations for Christopher Columbus's voyage in 1492 and the Pilgrims' voyage in 1620? How did these different motivations influence their actions and the ways they documented their experiences?
  3. Describe at least three specific challenges or dangers the Pilgrims faced during their voyage on the Mayflower, as recounted by William Bradford. How did they demonstrate resilience or seek solutions to these difficulties?
  4. Based on the descriptions provided, how did the early European explorers and settlers generally perceive the native populations they encountered? What significant misconception did Columbus hold, and how did it influence his naming conventions?
  5. How do the various texts in this section (Columbus, Bradford, Smith, and Psalm 107) collectively illustrate both the physical perils of transatlantic travel and the spiritual or emotional responses of the voyagers to their experiences?
