AP US History — Topic 7.3: World War I and Civil Liberties

Key Concepts

  • The road to war — neutrality under pressure: The United States maintained neutrality for nearly three years after the European war began in 1914. Geographic distance, Washington's tradition against permanent alliances, and the memory of 1812 made neutrality the default. But unrestricted submarine warfare and the Zimmermann Telegram made it politically untenable. Wilson's transformation from "He kept us out of war" to war message in one month illustrates a recurring American pattern.

  • Wilson's idealistic framing — the Fourteen Points: American participation was framed not as a conventional great-power war but as a crusade to restructure the international order around self-determination, open diplomacy, and collective security. The Fourteen Points were idealistic by design — a deliberate contrast to the nationalist and imperialist war aims European governments had pursued. The vision was ultimately unrealized at Paris and in the Senate.

  • Total mobilization and the dark corollary: The Selective Service, War Industries Board, Food Administration, and Committee on Public Information demonstrated that modern war required mobilizing industry, agriculture, and public opinion as fully as military forces. The mobilization of public opinion had a dark corollary: systematic suppression of dissent through the Espionage and Sedition Acts.

  • Schenck v. United States and the limits of the First Amendment: The Supreme Court unanimously upheld antiwar speech prosecutions under the "clear and present danger" standard. Holmes's opinion introduced the first constitutional test for speech limits — but Holmes himself would begin revising the standard in his Abrams dissent later the same year. The standard was eventually replaced by Brandenburg v. Ohio's (1969) more protective two-part test.

  • The First Red Scare and the Palmer Raids: Postwar fear of domestic communism — triggered by the Russian Revolution and 3,000+ labor strikes in 1919 — produced mass arrests that violated the constitutional protections the government claimed to defend. The raids were eventually discredited, but they established a precedent for using immigration and sedition law to purge political dissidents.

  • The recurring emergency-suppression pattern: The section establishes the pattern that recurs throughout American history: in moments of perceived national emergency, civil liberties are suppressed in ways that subsequent historical verdicts find disproportionate to the actual threat, and the suppression disproportionately targets groups already marginal to the mainstream political community.

Vocabulary Review

  • Lusitania: British ocean liner sunk by a German U-boat on May 7, 1915, killing 1,198 including 128 Americans. Major provocation in the path to American entry; Germany's later resumption of unrestricted submarine warfare after the Sussex Pledge ultimately triggered Wilson's war message.

  • Zimmermann Telegram: January 1917 secret German diplomatic cable to Mexico proposing a wartime alliance against the United States in exchange for assistance recovering Texas, New Mexico, and Arizona. British intelligence intercepted and decoded it; its publication in March 1917 shifted public opinion decisively toward intervention.

  • Sussex Pledge (1916): Germany's temporary agreement to restrict submarine warfare, preserving American neutrality — until Germany rescinded it in February 1917 and resumed unrestricted attacks on all shipping.

  • Fourteen Points: Wilson's January 1918 peace framework: self-determination, freedom of the seas, open diplomacy, arms reduction, League of Nations. Idealistic by design; ultimately unrealized at the Paris Peace Conference and blocked in the Senate.

  • Selective Service Act (1917): Authorized the military draft; registered 24 million men, inducted 3 million. The legal basis for mass military mobilization.

  • Committee on Public Information (CPI): George Creel's federal propaganda agency producing industrial-scale support for American war aims. Demonstrated government's capacity to manage mass opinion in modern warfare.

  • Espionage Act (1917): Criminalized obstruction of military recruitment, causing insubordination, or mailing material violating those provisions. Over 2,000 prosecutions, including Eugene V. Debs (ten-year sentence for antiwar speech).

  • Sedition Act (1918): Extended Espionage Act to criminalize any 'disloyal, profane, scurrilous, or abusive language' about the government, Constitution, flag, or military. Broader speech restriction — the Sedition Act, not the Espionage Act, is the closer parallel to direct censorship of political opinion.

  • Schenck v. United States (1919): Supreme Court upheld Espionage Act prosecution; Holmes established the "clear and present danger" standard. First constitutional test for First Amendment speech limits.

  • 'Clear and present danger': Holmes's Schenck test — speech is unprotected if it poses a clear and present danger of producing illegal action Congress has the right to prevent. Eventually replaced by Brandenburg (1969).

  • Brandenburg v. Ohio (1969): Replaced Schenck's standard with a more speech-protective two-part test: speech must be both directed to inciting imminent lawless action AND likely to produce such action.

  • First Red Scare (1919–20): Postwar fear of domestic Bolshevik infiltration triggered by Russian Revolution, labor strikes, and mail bombs. Led to Palmer Raids.

  • Palmer Raids: Attorney General Palmer's mass arrests (1919–20) targeting suspected communists, anarchists, and radical immigrants. ~6,000 arrests in January 1920 alone; ~500 deportations including Emma Goldman. Discredited when Palmer's revolutionary uprising prediction failed to materialize.

Key Figures and Events

  • Woodrow Wilson (1856–1924): Won re-election 1916 on neutrality; entered war April 1917; framed war as crusade for democracy; proposed Fourteen Points; suffered Senate defeat of League of Nations; stroke 1919 while campaigning. His career captures the era's defining tension: idealistic rhetoric and suppressive domestic policy.

  • Arthur Zimmermann: German Foreign Secretary whose telegram to Mexico provided the final trigger for American intervention. Classic example of intelligence shaping foreign-policy decisions.

  • Charles Schenck: General secretary of the Socialist Party of Philadelphia who mailed 15,000 leaflets to draft registrants arguing the draft violated the Thirteenth Amendment. His prosecution produced the most important WWI-era civil liberties ruling.

  • Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes: Author of the unanimous Schenck opinion establishing "clear and present danger." His subsequent dissent in Abrams v. United States (1919) began revising the standard toward greater speech protection — one of the most intellectually significant shifts in Supreme Court history.

  • Eugene V. Debs: Socialist Party leader; ten-year sentence under Espionage Act for antiwar speech. His prosecution demonstrated that political dissent — not just concrete obstruction — was the target of wartime suppression.

  • A. Mitchell Palmer: Attorney General who orchestrated the Red Scare raids. His predicted May 1, 1920, Bolshevik uprising never materialized; political credibility collapsed, demonstrating that government overreach can be self-defeating.

  • Emma Goldman: Anarchist activist deported during Palmer Raids. Represented the use of immigration law to expel political dissidents — a pattern connecting to subsequent deportation-as-political-control episodes.

  • Louis Post: Assistant Secretary of Labor whose review of Palmer Raids deportation cases found most detainees had no radical connections. A rare instance of internal executive accountability checking civil liberties excess.

  • Herbert Hoover: Headed the Food Administration during WWI; managed agricultural output and consumption as part of total war mobilization. Pre-cursor to his Depression-era role as president.

Themes

World War I and Civil Liberties operates at the intersection of the Politics (POL), National Identity (NAT), and World Relations (WOR) themes. The war itself is a WOR story: how a republic that tradition made allergic to entangling alliances ended up deploying 2 million soldiers to Europe. The Fourteen Points represent Wilson's attempt to use WOR (international organization) to serve NAT (making the world safe for American democratic values).

But the section's most enduring analytical substance is the POL + NAT intersection: the wartime suppression of civil liberties reveals how "national security" can be used to enforce political and social conformity as much as to neutralize genuine threats. The targets of the Espionage and Sedition Acts — socialists, immigrants, labor organizers — were disproportionately people already considered marginal to mainstream American identity. The Palmer Raids extended this targeting pattern into peacetime, using immigration law to deport people whose views were deemed incompatible with American values.

The Schenck decision is one of the most important AP-testable Supreme Court cases in American history. Students should be able to: (1) describe the facts of the case; (2) explain Holmes's "clear and present danger" standard and the "fire in a theatre" analogy; (3) explain how Holmes began revising his own standard in Abrams; (4) describe how Brandenburg v. Ohio (1969) eventually replaced Schenck with a more speech-protective standard; and (5) explain the significance of this evolution for understanding the First Amendment.

Compare and Contrast

Espionage Act vs. Sedition Act: The Espionage Act (1917) targeted specific acts — obstructing recruitment, causing insubordination — that required some connection between speech and concrete military harm. The Sedition Act (1918) went further, criminalizing any disloyal or abusive language about the government, Constitution, or flag, regardless of whether the speech had any connection to military harm. The Sedition Act represents the fuller form of wartime censorship; the Espionage Act, while still broad, at least required nominal connection to military conduct.

First Red Scare (1919–20) vs. Second Red Scare (McCarthyism, 1950s): Both involved government using real Communist activity (Russian Revolution; Soviet atomic bomb, Korean War) as the trigger for suppression disproportionate to actual domestic threat; both targeted political dissidents (socialists, labor organizers; Hollywood writers, suspected party members) as much as genuine spies; both were eventually discredited (Palmer's failed prediction; Army-McCarthy hearings); both used loyalty as the criterion for inclusion in the political community.

AP Exam Approach

HAPP Analysis — Schenck v. United States (1919):

  • Historical context: Wartime, Espionage Act, Socialist antiwar activism, mass draft registration.
  • Audience: Legal — addressed to lower courts; but functionally to the public, establishing what speech the government could suppress.
  • Purpose: Establish constitutional limits on First Amendment protection during wartime emergency; uphold the Espionage Act conviction.
  • Point of view: Holmes writing at the height of wartime, with broad public support for suppression of antiwar dissent; his own evolution in the Abrams dissent later the same year suggests he recognized the standard's limitations.

Common AP prompts for this section:

  • Explain the constitutional significance of Schenck v. United States. How did Holmes's "clear and present danger" standard differ from the Brandenburg standard that eventually replaced it?
  • Compare the Espionage Act and the Sedition Act. Which represented a greater restriction on First Amendment rights, and why?
  • Evaluate the Palmer Raids as a civil-liberties episode. What mechanisms of democratic accountability eventually discredited them?

Short-Answer Question Practice:

"In the years following America's entry into World War I, the federal government restricted civil liberties in the name of national security."

  • Part A: Describe ONE way the federal government restricted civil liberties during or immediately after WWI.
  • Part B: Explain ONE way in which the targets of wartime civil liberties restrictions reflected existing social and political marginalizations.
  • Part C: Explain how the Schenck decision both established and eventually contributed to the revision of First Amendment doctrine.
