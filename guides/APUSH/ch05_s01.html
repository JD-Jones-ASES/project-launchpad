AP US History — Topic 5.1: Manifest Destiny and the Mexican-American War

Key Concepts

  • Manifest Destiny as ideology, not description: John O'Sullivan's 1845 phrase crystallized a cluster of interlocking beliefs — Anglo-American racial superiority, Protestant providentialism, and the claim that continental expansion was divinely ordained. Historians are careful to distinguish between the fact of westward migration and the ideology that justified it. Manifest Destiny was an argument for acquisition by "any means necessary — purchase, negotiation, or war," not a neutral observation about population movement.

  • Texas annexation and the slavery question: American settlers in Mexican Texas were largely slaveholders who revolted in 1836 partly to protect slavery, which Mexico had banned. The Republic of Texas's nine-year delay before annexation reflected the inseparability of Texas from the slave-state question: admitting Texas meant admitting a huge slaveholding state and disrupting the Senate's sectional balance. Congressional action via joint resolution in 1845 bypassed the two-thirds treaty requirement.

  • Polk's manufactured casus belli: James K. Polk ordered General Taylor to advance to the Rio Grande — territory Mexico claimed — and used the resulting skirmish to declare that "American blood has been shed on American soil." Critics, including first-term Illinois congressman Abraham Lincoln, challenged whether the spot was genuinely American territory. This controversy established a recurring American pattern: presidential construction of incidents to justify wars Congress was being asked to authorize.

  • The Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo and its broken promises: Mexico ceded roughly half its territory (present-day California, Nevada, Utah, Arizona, New Mexico, and portions of Colorado and Wyoming) for $15 million. The Treaty promised citizenship and property rights to Mexican and indigenous residents of the ceded territories. In practice, these promises were violated systematically: land grants were challenged in American courts, owners were dispossessed through litigation, and former Mexican citizens existed in legal and social limbo.

  • The Wilmot Proviso and sectional arithmetic: Representative David Wilmot's 1846 proposal to ban slavery from any territory acquired from Mexico never became law — but its divergent fate in House (passed, reflecting free-state population majorities) and Senate (failed, reflecting slave-state near-parity) dramatized the structural tension of American politics. The Proviso defined the political fault line that would produce the Free Soil Party (1848) and ultimately the Republican Party (1854).

  • Territorial expansion as crisis accelerant: The war's most consequential legacy was internal rather than territorial. Every acre acquired from Mexico intensified rather than resolved the slavery question by forcing politicians to confront irreconcilable positions on slavery's expansion into the territories. The Missouri Compromise formula of 1820 could not absorb the new territory. The search for a new organizing principle — popular sovereignty, judicial doctrine, violence — would consume American politics for the next fifteen years.

  • Multi-perspectival costs of expansion: For Mexico, the war was a catastrophic loss of more than half its national territory and a generational wound. For indigenous peoples in the ceded territories, Manifest Destiny was dispossession by another sovereign rather than preservation. For the Mexican residents promised citizenship, it was legal limbo. The nationalist narrative of triumphant continental consolidation effaces these perspectives.

Vocabulary Review

  • Manifest Destiny: The doctrine, popularized from 1845, holding that U.S. continental expansion was inevitable and divinely ordained; fused nationalism, Protestant providentialism, and racial hierarchy.
  • Empresario grants: Land grants from the Mexican government to American contractors (empresarios) who brought settlers to Mexican Texas in the 1820s–30s, creating the demographic imbalance that preceded the Texas Revolution.
  • Texas Revolution (1836): Armed revolt by American settlers in Mexican Texas (many protecting slavery, which Mexico had banned) that produced the independent Republic of Texas.
  • Joint resolution: A legislative mechanism requiring only simple majorities in both chambers of Congress, used to annex Texas in 1845, bypassing the two-thirds Senate majority required for treaty ratification.
  • Casus belli: Latin for "cause of war"; the incident or provocation used to justify going to war. Lincoln challenged Polk's casus belli — the skirmish between American and Mexican forces — by questioning whether the territory involved was genuinely American soil.
  • Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo (1848): The peace settlement ending the Mexican-American War; Mexico ceded roughly half its territory to the United States in exchange for $15 million.
  • Mexican Cession: The territory ceded by Mexico under the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo — present-day California, Nevada, Utah, Arizona, New Mexico, and parts of Colorado and Wyoming.
  • Wilmot Proviso: Proposed 1846 amendment that would have banned slavery from territory acquired from Mexico; passed the House but failed in the Senate; never became law.
  • Popular sovereignty: The antebellum doctrine that territorial settlers, not Congress, should decide the slavery question; first associated with Senator Lewis Cass (1848).
  • Free Soil movement: Political position (and after 1848, party) holding that western territories must be reserved for free white labor; opposed slavery's expansion but not its abolition where it existed.
  • Missouri Compromise line (36°30′): The 1820 formula dividing future territory into slaveholding (south of the line) and free (north of the line) zones; politically unsustainable after the Mexican Cession.
  • Sectional arithmetic: The structural feature of American politics by which free-state population majorities dominated the House while the Senate's equal-state representation preserved slave-state influence — determining the fate of the Wilmot Proviso and subsequent slavery legislation.

Key Figures and Events

  • John O'Sullivan: New York journalist who coined "manifest destiny" in 1845, giving the expansionist ideology its enduring label and crystallizing its racial, nationalist, and providential components.
  • Sam Houston / Santa Anna: Central figures of the Texas Revolution (1836) — Houston commanded the Texian forces; Santa Anna, the Mexican forces. The Texas Republic's subsequent relationship to the United States was partly defined by the question of slavery Santa Anna had sought to suppress.
  • James K. Polk (1795–1849): Expansionist president who sought California and New Mexico, engineered the Mexican-American War, and presided over the largest territorial acquisition in U.S. history. His ordering of Taylor to the Rio Grande and subsequent war message were the central political controversies of his presidency.
  • Abraham Lincoln's "spot resolutions" (1847): Lincoln, as a first-term Illinois congressman, introduced resolutions demanding that Polk identify the exact "spot" where American blood was shed, challenging the war's legal and moral justification. Though politically unsuccessful, the resolutions established Lincoln's skepticism of executive war-making and his willingness to challenge presidential authority.
  • General Zachary Taylor: Commander of U.S. forces in northern Mexico; his advance to the Rio Grande triggered the war. Later elected president in 1848 on his military fame.
  • Winfield Scott: Commanded the most strategically decisive campaign of the war — the amphibious landing at Veracruz and the march to Mexico City, which fell in September 1847. Scott's campaign was a model for later military planning.
  • Representative David Wilmot (1814–1868): Pennsylvania congressman whose 1846 proviso defining the slavery question for the acquired territories made him one of the most consequential minor figures of the antebellum period; his measure, though never enacted, structured the subsequent decade of political conflict.
  • Gold discovery at Sutter's Mill (January 24, 1848): Occurred just eight days before the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo was signed; triggered the California Gold Rush and the rapid growth of California's non-indigenous population to over 100,000 by 1850, forcing the California statehood question onto the political agenda.
  • John Gast's "American Progress" (1872): Painted retrospectively, the allegorical image of Columbia floating westward trailing telegraph wire while indigenous peoples retreat is the most reproduced icon of Manifest Destiny's ideology — and the most useful primary source for analyzing how that ideology constructed its narrative of providential progress.

Themes and Analytical Frames

National Identity (NAT) and Geography (GEO): Manifest Destiny's geographic vision was inseparable from its identity claims. To define the nation as continental was simultaneously to define it as white and Anglo-American. The territorial shape Americans imagined for their republic was not driven by geographic necessity but by racial and providential ideology. Mexico had governed the ceded territories for decades; indigenous nations had inhabited them for centuries. The United States did not fill an empty space — it imposed a new national identity on peoples who already had their own.

Politics and Power (POL): The Mexican-American War produced a direct constitutional crisis through the Wilmot Proviso debates. The divergent outcome in House vs. Senate dramatized how the Constitution's apportionment formulas encoded sectional power. Polk's use of the joint resolution to annex Texas, and his engineering of a military incident to request a war declaration, exemplify presidential power-expansion using executive initiative and legislative acquiescence.

Causation and Contingency: The war was not structurally inevitable — it resulted from specific decisions by Polk and Mexican leaders within a context of competing territorial claims. But its consequences — the Wilmot Proviso debates, the California statehood crisis, the Compromise of 1850's instability, Bleeding Kansas — formed a chain of causation leading to the Civil War. The war did not cause the Civil War, but it removed the territorial buffer that had allowed politicians to avoid the irreconcilable confrontation.

Multi-perspectival analysis: The standard nationalist narrative of Manifest Destiny as heroic continental consolidation must be read against the experiences of those dispossessed: Mexican citizens whose property rights were systematically violated; indigenous nations for whom one colonial sovereign was replaced by another; and enslaved people whose status in the new territories became the central question of American politics.

Compare and Contrast

Manifest Destiny vs. earlier expansionism: The Louisiana Purchase (1803) was negotiated diplomatically (from France, with no significant armed resistance) and involved territory largely outside the existing republic. Texas annexation and the Mexican Cession involved active military conflict against a neighboring sovereign nation and the incorporation of large populations of Mexican and indigenous peoples — raising questions about race, citizenship, and sovereignty that Jefferson's purchase had not.

Wilmot Proviso vs. Missouri Compromise: The Missouri Compromise (1820) had managed the sectional question by dividing territory along a geographic line, giving each section a zone. The Wilmot Proviso sought to exclude slavery entirely from all acquired territory — reflecting the hardening of Northern free-soil opinion after 1846. The shift from compromise-by-geography to exclusion-by-principle measured the radicalization of sectional positions in the intervening twenty-six years.

AP Exam Approach

Stimulus-based question focus: For the Gast painting ("American Progress"), the AP exam will likely ask you to identify the point of view, purpose, and historical situation of the image. Key points: (1) painted in 1872 but depicting the ideology of the 1840s–60s; (2) Columbia's allegorical figure embodies providential progress — light advancing west; (3) indigenous peoples in darkness represents dispossession naturalized as fate; (4) telegraph wire and railroads signal technological "civilization"; (5) the image deliberately effaces violence and conquest.

SAQ Practice

The Mexican-American War (1846–48) produced both territorial gains and political crises for the United States.

Part A: Describe ONE way the Mexican-American War intensified sectional tensions over slavery.

Part B: Explain ONE way the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo's promises to Mexican residents of the ceded territories differed from the actual experience of those residents.

Part C: Explain how the Wilmot Proviso revealed a structural feature of American politics that would shape sectional conflict through 1860.

DBQ/LEQ framing: When asked to evaluate the causes or consequences of Manifest Destiny, avoid triumphalist framing. The AP exam rewards analysis that acknowledges multiple perspectives: territorial gain for the United States was dispossession for Mexico, indigenous nations, and Mexican residents of the ceded territories. The ideological argument (providential destiny) masked specific political and racial choices — use this tension as the analytical core.
