AP US History — Topic 2.3: Puritan New England

Key Concepts

  • New England as a religious project: Unlike the Chesapeake's profit-driven origins, Massachusetts Bay was founded by Puritans who intended to build a model Christian commonwealth — a "city upon a hill" that would illuminate the path for a corrupt England to follow. This religious purpose shaped the colony's political culture, social organization, and intellectual life in ways that persist, in transformed versions, in American political rhetoric to the present.

  • Covenant theology as the framework of community: Puritans understood themselves as collectively covenanted with God. This corporate theology had direct political implications: because God's displeasure at individual sin would fall on the entire community, the community was justified in — indeed obligated to — monitor and correct its members' behavior. Religious deviance was not a private matter but a public danger.

  • Congregationalism as democratic training: The self-governing structure of Puritan churches — in which congregations chose their ministers, managed finances, and admitted members — trained ordinary men in democratic deliberation before they exercised it politically. The same habits of collective decision-making practiced in the church meeting were exercised in the town meeting. This is the cultural foundation of New England's democratic political tradition.

  • The limits of Puritan orthodoxy — dissent and expulsion: The commitment to communal orthodoxy generated its own opposition from within. Roger Williams challenged both church-state integration and the colonists' title to indigenous land. Anne Hutchinson's antinomian theology undermined ministerial authority. Both were banished. The treatment of dissenters reveals the coercive underside of the Puritan communal ideal.

  • The Puritan Paradox — democracy and coercion inseparable: New England bequeathed to American political culture both democratic self-governance and coercive orthodoxy — not as contradictions but as two expressions of the same conviction that the community as a whole bore responsibility for building a godly society. The congregationalist tradition that trained men in participatory democracy also expelled dissenters and hanged alleged witches.

  • Violence and crisis in the late seventeenth century: King Philip's War (1675–78) was the bloodiest per-capita conflict in American history, breaking the military power of New England's indigenous nations. The Salem witch trials (1692) erupted in a community traumatized by war, economic stress, and anxiety about declining piety — illustrating how external violence and internal social stress combined to produce collective crisis.

Vocabulary Review

  • Puritan: Reformed Protestant who believed the Church of England remained corrupt and sought to purify it; theologically Calvinist, committed to covenantal community.
  • Separatist (Pilgrim): Calvinist who believed complete separation from the Church of England was necessary; founded Plymouth Colony (1620) via the Mayflower.
  • Covenant theology: The framework in which God and the Puritan community entered a binding agreement; the community's collective responsibility for members' behavior follows directly from this theology.
  • Congregationalism: Church governance model placing authority in the self-governing gathered congregation rather than bishops or external hierarchy; trained men in democratic deliberation.
  • Antinomianism: The belief that saved Christians are not bound by the moral law as evidence of salvation; Anne Hutchinson's theology undermined ministerial authority by claiming direct revelation.
  • Half-Way Covenant (1662): Pragmatic compromise allowing children of partially-converted members to have their own children baptized; marked the beginning of dilution of Puritan theological standards.
  • City upon a Hill: Phrase from Winthrop's 1630 Arbella sermon; framed Massachusetts Bay as an exemplary community; later selectively invoked by U.S. leaders stripped of its communal demands.
  • King Philip's War (1675–78): The deadliest per-capita war in American history; led by Metacom (Wampanoag sachem); broke indigenous military power in New England.
  • Metacom (King Philip): Wampanoag sachem who led the 1675–78 indigenous alliance against English expansion; killed August 1676; his defeat opened vast territories to colonial settlement.
  • Salem witch trials (1692): Crisis in which over 200 were accused, 19 hanged, in a community under multiple social stresses; ended when prominent people were accused; Judge Sewall publicly apologized in 1697.
  • Mayflower Compact (1620): Voluntary political framework created at Cape Cod by Pilgrim men who had landed outside their charter area; established governance from consent of governed.
  • General Court (Massachusetts): Elected representative assembly of Massachusetts Bay Colony; embodied both representative governance and the power to enforce religious orthodoxy.

Key Figures and Events

  • John Winthrop (1588–1649): Governor elected twelve times; delivered the "city upon a hill" sermon; balanced authoritarian discipline with reluctant representative governance; central figure in defining Puritan New England's political character.
  • Roger Williams (c. 1603–1683): Minister banished 1636; founded Rhode Island on principles of religious tolerance and church-state separation; purchased Narragansett land rather than seizing it by royal claim.
  • Anne Hutchinson (1591–1643): Midwife and spiritual counselor whose antinomian theology drew crowds and undermined ministerial authority; tried by the General Court in 1637; banished; killed in conflict with the Wappinger in 1643.
  • Metacom / King Philip (d. 1676): Wampanoag sachem who organized the most significant indigenous military resistance in New England history; his death in August 1676 ended the war that bears his English name.
  • Judge Samuel Sewall: One of the Salem witch trial judges who publicly apologized in 1697 — one of colonial America's rare moments of institutional self-criticism.
  • The Arbella crossing (1630): The Winthrop Fleet that carried approximately 700 Puritans to Massachusetts Bay; the founding migration of the Puritan project; setting for Winthrop's seminal sermon.
  • Mayflower at Cape Cod (1620): Blown off course from Virginia destination; Pilgrim men signed the Mayflower Compact to create a self-governing political framework in the absence of any external charter authority.

Themes

Religion and Politics (Cultural / ARC): New England Puritanism is the most concentrated example in colonial American history of a society in which religion and politics were inseparable. The covenant theology that framed collective spiritual responsibility also framed collective political responsibility. The Congregationalist church structure that trained democratic deliberation also empowered the community to expel dissenters. Understanding Puritanism as simultaneously democratic and coercive — rather than as either/or — is essential to AP analysis.

Politics and Power (PCE): Puritan New England developed representative governance through church meetings (congregationalism) and political meetings (town meetings, General Court). These institutions trained an entire colonial political class in habits of self-governance that would animate resistance to Parliament after 1765. But this representative tradition coexisted with — and was structurally connected to — the power to banish, hang, and dispossess those who challenged the covenant community's orthodoxy.

Social Structures (SOC): The Salem witch trials reveal the gendered dimensions of Puritan social order. Most of the accusers were young women and girls; many of the accused were women whose social independence or spiritual influence challenged patriarchal norms. Anne Hutchinson's earlier story — a woman whose spiritual authority drew crowds of Boston's most prominent men — establishes the precedent of female spiritual authority being suppressed by the male-governed Puritan establishment.

Interaction with Environment (ENV) / Indigenous Peoples: King Philip's War was the military culmination of decades of indigenous land dispossession driven by the expansion of Puritan settlements. The source frames the war as the consequence of a structural pattern — "expanding colonial settlements had displaced and encircled the region's indigenous nations" — not simply as indigenous aggression. Roger Williams' practice of purchasing rather than seizing Narragansett land stands as the exceptional alternative model within colonial New England.

Connections to Earlier/Later Periods

Connection backward: Puritan theology drew from the Reformed Protestant tradition of John Calvin and the English Reformation. The covenant concept had roots in Reformed theological tradition. The Mayflower Compact drew on English common-law traditions of social contract and representative governance that predated colonization. The Narragansett and Wampanoag peoples had established societies in New England for thousands of years before English contact; the colonial period disrupted but did not erase their presence.

Connection forward: The Congregationalist tradition of self-governance directly shaped colonial resistance to the Stamp Act (1765) and the logic of the Declaration of Independence (1776) — "the consent of the governed" has deep Puritan roots. Roger Williams' Rhode Island provided an early model of church-state separation that influenced the First Amendment's Establishment Clause. The Salem witch trials' eventual acknowledgment of error by participants (including Judge Sewall's 1697 apology) provided a cultural precedent for institutional self-examination that would recur in later American life.

The "city upon a hill" phrase has been selectively invoked in nearly every era of American political culture — from Puritan covenant theology to Cold War exceptionalism to Reagan's rhetorical patriotism — demonstrating how founding texts are continuously redeployed, translated, and transformed across time.

AP Exam Approach

Common essay prompt types for this topic:

  • SAQ: Describe ONE way Congregationalism shaped colonial political culture, and ONE way the Puritan commitment to orthodoxy limited democratic participation.
  • DBQ: The Mayflower Compact is a common primary source for document analysis; practice sourcing (audience, purpose, historical situation, point of view) and contextualizing.
  • LEQ / Comparison: Puritan New England vs. Chesapeake on origins, social structure, and political culture; Puritan New England vs. Middle Colonies on religious pluralism.

Key analytical moves:

  1. Avoid hagiography: Puritan New England is often discussed in reverential terms in popular culture. AP analysis requires acknowledging that the same community that established democratic deliberation also banished dissenters, hanged alleged witches, and dispossessed indigenous peoples — and that these were not accidents but expressions of the same covenant logic.

  2. Source the Mayflower Compact carefully: The document was written by the signatories themselves, all male, in a crisis of legal legitimacy. Its emphasis on loyalty to the king while asserting self-legislative authority reflects the political tightrope of colonial autonomy claims.

  3. Use the Puritan Paradox as your organizing concept: For essay prompts asking about the sources of American democratic tradition OR about the limits of colonial democracy, the Puritan Paradox (democratic training AND coercive orthodoxy as twin expressions of covenant theology) gives you a sophisticated analytical framework.

  4. Multi-perspectival framing: The founding of New England looks radically different from the perspectives of (a) Puritan founders (a godly commonwealth); (b) Roger Williams and Anne Hutchinson (an intolerant theocracy); (c) indigenous nations like the Wampanoag and Narragansett (an expanding dispossessing power); (d) later American political culture (a founding myth, selectively edited).

  5. Connect the Half-Way Covenant to long-term change: The 1662 compromise signals the beginning of New England's secularization — a process that would accelerate with the Great Awakening (1740s) as new religious movements competed with established Congregationalism.
