AP US History — Topic 5.3: Dred Scott, John Brown, and the Election of 1860

Key Concepts

  • Dred Scott as constitutional rupture: The 1857 decision was the most explosive the Supreme Court had ever issued. By declaring that Congress had never had constitutional authority to ban slavery from territories, it invalidated the Republican Party's central platform. By stripping Black Americans — enslaved or free — of any citizenship claim, it removed the possibility of a legal pathway to Black rights. By treating enslaved people as property protected by the Fifth Amendment, it made anti-slavery legislation a constitutional violation. The decision attempted to settle the slavery question permanently in slavery's favor; its actual effect was to make the question more explosive.

  • The decision's multiple consequences: Dred Scott simultaneously invalidated the Missouri Compromise (already repealed), threatened popular sovereignty (if Congress can't ban slavery from territories, can territorial residents?), and gave the Republican Party a cause — the court's capture by the "slave power" — that was more powerful politically than the original free-soil platform. Justice Curtis's dissent proved Taney's historical argument factually wrong, delegitimizing the decision in Northern eyes.

  • John Brown and the failure of legal remedies: Brown's Harpers Ferry raid (October 1859) represented the logic of those who believed that the Dred Scott decision, the Fugitive Slave Act, and Bleeding Kansas had demonstrated that slavery could not be ended through legal or political means. Brown intended to seize weapons and ignite a general slave insurrection. The raid collapsed within 36 hours; the enslaved population did not rise. But the contrasting Northern and Southern reactions to Brown's execution revealed incompatible moral frameworks — demonstrating that the two sections were now living in separate moral universes.

  • The election of 1860 as structural revelation: The election was effectively two simultaneous elections in different regions. The Democratic Party had fractured at its Charleston convention between Northern Democrats (Douglas, popular sovereignty) and Southern Democrats (Breckinridge, federal slave-code). Lincoln won 180 electoral votes entirely from free states, with his name not even on the ballot in ten Southern states, receiving only 39.8% of the popular vote. A candidate acceptable to one section had become intolerable to the other — demonstrating that the political system had become structurally incapable of producing a result both sections would accept.

  • Secession and Confederate national identity: The Deep South moved before Lincoln was inaugurated. South Carolina's secession declaration cited the election of a president "hostile to slavery." Confederate Vice President Alexander Stephens's "Cornerstone Speech" declared slavery the Confederacy's "cornerstone" — primary source evidence contradicting the post-war "Lost Cause" narrative that secession was about constitutional principle rather than slavery's preservation.

  • The compact theory of secession vs. Lincoln's constitutional counter: Southern secessionists argued that the Constitution was a compact among sovereign states that could withdraw when the federal government violated the compact's terms. Lincoln's counter-argument — that the Constitution was a permanent instrument of the people, not a revocable contract among governments — was the constitutional position the Civil War settled by force, not judicial resolution.

Vocabulary Review

  • Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857): Supreme Court decision holding that Black Americans had no citizenship or standing to sue; that Congress had no authority to ban slavery from territories; and that enslaved people were Fifth Amendment-protected property. Widely considered the worst Supreme Court decision in American history.
  • Compact theory: The constitutional theory that the Constitution was a contract among sovereign states, each of which retained the right to withdraw if the federal government violated the compact's terms. The basis of the Southern secession argument.
  • Freeport Doctrine: Douglas's 1858 debate argument that territorial residents could exclude slavery by refusing to pass slave codes, even after Dred Scott. Satisfied Northern Democrats but alienated Southern Democrats, fracturing the party and destroying Douglas's 1860 presidential prospects.
  • Harpers Ferry raid (1859): John Brown's failed attempt to seize the federal arsenal at Harpers Ferry, Virginia, and ignite a general slave insurrection. Recaptured by Colonel Robert E. Lee within 36 hours. Brown was hanged December 2, 1859.
  • Constitutional Union Party: Fourth-party 1860 candidate John Bell's party, composed of former Whigs who refused to take a position on slavery; won Virginia, Kentucky, Tennessee.
  • Confederate States of America: Government organized at Montgomery, Alabama, in February 1861, with Jefferson Davis as president. Explicitly grounded in the preservation of racial slavery per Alexander Stephens's Cornerstone Speech.
  • Fort Sumter: Federal garrison in Charleston Harbor, South Carolina; Confederate artillery bombardment on April 12, 1861, began the Civil War. Lincoln had decided to resupply the fort, forcing the Confederacy to choose between accepting Union presence or firing first.
  • "Lost Cause" mythology: Post-war Southern narrative portraying Confederate secession as motivated by constitutional principle and states' rights rather than slavery. Directly contradicted by primary sources like Alexander Stephens's 1861 Cornerstone Speech.
  • Slave power: Northern term for the perceived political conspiracy of slaveholders to control the federal government — the Supreme Court, the presidency, and Congress — in the interests of slavery's expansion. Dred Scott intensified belief in the "slave power" conspiracy.

Key Figures and Events

  • Dred Scott (c. 1799–1858): Enslaved man who sued for his freedom, arguing that residence in the free state of Illinois and free territory of Wisconsin (under the Missouri Compromise) had made him free. The Supreme Court ruled he had no standing to sue. He was freed by his owner shortly after the decision and died of tuberculosis in September 1858 — eighteen months after his case became the center of national controversy.
  • Chief Justice Roger Taney (1777–1864): Seventh Chief Justice; author of the Dred Scott majority opinion. His decision attempted to resolve the slavery question constitutionally but instead intensified the crisis. He died in October 1864 and was replaced by Salmon Chase — a former anti-slavery politician — in one of the war's many ironic historical footnotes.
  • Justice Benjamin Curtis (1809–1874): Associate Justice who wrote the powerful Dred Scott dissent, proving through historical evidence that free Black men had been citizens at ratification. He resigned from the Court after the decision, unable to continue serving alongside colleagues who had, in his view, abandoned constitutional principle for political results.
  • John Brown (1800–1859): Radical abolitionist whose career traced the logic of political radicalization from legal failure to violence: from the free-soil settler movement in Kansas (Pottawatomie 1856) to armed insurrection (Harpers Ferry 1859). His final note — "I, John Brown, am now quite certain that the crimes of this guilty land will never be purged away but with blood" — became an epitaph for the limits of peaceful political resolution.
  • Abraham Lincoln (1809–1865): Republican nominee who won the 1860 election without a single Southern electoral vote, triggering secession before his inauguration. His first inaugural address attempted to persuade the South that he posed no threat to slavery where it existed; his constitutional argument that secession was void and illegal was the position the Civil War would enforce.
  • Jefferson Davis (1808–1889): Mississippi senator and former Secretary of War who became president of the Confederate States of America in February 1861. His government's legitimacy rested on the compact theory of secession; he spent his post-war years defending that theory in his memoir The Rise and Fall of the Confederate Government.
  • Alexander Stephens (1812–1883): Confederate Vice President whose "Cornerstone Speech" (March 21, 1861) is the most direct primary source statement of the Confederacy's founding purpose — the preservation and extension of racial slavery.

Themes and Analytical Frames

Politics and Power (POL): The election of 1860 represents POL at its most foundational. The question was not which slavery policy would prevail but whether the constitutional framework that contained political competition could survive a result one section refused to accept. The sectional party system produced a structural impasse: Lincoln could win the Electoral College without Southern votes, but Southern states would not accept his governance. The Confederacy's formation was not merely a political act — it was a claim that the constitutional framework itself had broken down.

National Identity (NAT): Two competing national identities crystallized in 1860–61. Lincoln's America (articulated more fully in the Gettysburg Address and Second Inaugural) would be defined by the Declaration's promise that "all men are created equal." The Confederate national identity, as Stephens explicitly stated, was grounded in the permanence of racial hierarchy — "the great truth that the negro is not equal to the white man." The Civil War was not only a military conflict but a competition between two visions of what America was.

Continuity/Change: The period 1857–61 represents a decisive change from the earlier pattern of sectional compromise. The Dred Scott decision closed off legislative options; Brown's raid foreclosed the possibility of racial moderation; the 1860 election produced a result one section would not accept. The constitutional framework that had managed sectional conflict since 1787 had ceased to function as a conflict-resolution mechanism.

HAPP Analysis: Alexander Stephens, "Cornerstone Speech" (1861)

This is a high-value HAPP (Historical context, Audience, Purpose, Point of view) primary source.

  • Historical context: March 21, 1861 — the Confederate government had been organized one month earlier; Lincoln's inauguration was two weeks past; Fort Sumter would be attacked three weeks later. Stephens was speaking to a Savannah, Georgia audience as the newly installed Confederate Vice President.
  • Audience: White Southern Confederate supporters; the speech was also reprinted in newspapers nationally and internationally, reaching audiences in the North and in Britain and France (whose recognition the Confederacy sought).
  • Purpose: To articulate the ideological foundation of the new Confederate government — to explain to supporters and potential allies why the Confederacy existed and what it stood for.
  • Point of view: Stephens spoke as a former Unionist who had opposed secession but now accepted it; his direct, unapologetic statement that slavery was the Confederacy's cornerstone contrasts with later "Lost Cause" attempts to reframe the Confederacy's purpose in constitutional rather than racial terms.

AP exam significance: Stephens's speech is direct primary source evidence that the Confederacy's leaders understood themselves to be fighting to preserve and extend racial slavery — directly contradicting the "Lost Cause" narrative. AP essay prompts that ask whether slavery was the primary cause of secession should always include this speech.

AP Exam Approach

SAQ Practice

The Dred Scott v. Sandford decision (1857) and John Brown's raid at Harpers Ferry (1859) each intensified the sectional crisis in the years before the Civil War.

Part A: Explain ONE specific way the Dred Scott decision undermined the platform of the Republican Party.

Part B: Explain ONE way the reactions to John Brown's raid revealed that the two sections were operating in incompatible moral frameworks.

Part C: Explain how the outcome of the 1860 presidential election demonstrated that the American political system had become fundamentally sectional by that year.

DBQ/LEQ framing: When evaluating causation for the Civil War, the 1857–61 period offers essential evidence that the crisis was structural rather than merely the product of individual decisions. Dred Scott removed constitutional options; Harpers Ferry made emotional compromise impossible; the 1860 election demonstrated that the political framework could not produce an acceptable result. Use these three events together to argue that the constitutional crisis, not merely sectionalism or individual political failures, made the Civil War unavoidable by 1861.
