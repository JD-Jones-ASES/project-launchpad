AP US History — Topic 6.3: Labor, Strikes, and Class Conflict

Key Concepts

  • The permanent industrial working class as capitalism's necessary complement: By 1880 more Americans worked for wages in factories, mines, and railroads than farmed their own land. This structural shift was of civilizational magnitude — conditions were harsh (12–14 hour days, child labor, wage cuts, blacklists), and the labor history of the Gilded Age is the history of workers' attempts to resist these conditions and the extraordinary violence deployed to suppress that resistance.

  • Knights of Labor vs. AFL — two models of labor organization: The Knights represented the inclusive, transformative model: organizing across craft, racial, and gender lines, seeking not just better wages but an alternative cooperative economic order. The AFL represented the exclusive, pragmatic model: skilled craft workers only, higher wages and shorter hours through collective bargaining, no political radicalism. The Knights collapsed after Haymarket; the AFL survived and grew. Their comparison illuminates the trade-offs between solidarity and effectiveness in an inhospitable environment.

  • The four great strikes and the federal government as capital's enforcer: The Great Railroad Strike (1877), Haymarket (1886), Homestead (1892), and Pullman (1894) constitute a consistent pattern: employers used blacklists, Pinkerton agents, and public opinion; the state used city police, state militia, and federal troops; the courts used conspiracy doctrine, the Sherman Act against unions, and injunctions. Every major Gilded Age strike ended with the state on capital's side.

  • Government by injunction: The Pullman Strike produced the crucial legal tool that employers would use for three more decades: the federal court injunction. By obtaining an order against the strike and jailing Debs for contempt, the Cleveland administration established that the government could suppress labor action without criminal trial, bypassing the jury system. In re Debs (1895) constitutionalized this doctrine.

  • The Gospel of Wealth as ideological weapon and contradiction: Carnegie's 1889 essay argued that the wealthy were stewards of surplus capital obligated to administer it for public benefit. Written three years before Homestead, the essay reveals both the ideological purpose of philanthropic rhetoric (defend concentration, forestall redistribution) and the gap between stated values and industrial practice. Carnegie was in Scotland while Frick hired Pinkerton mercenaries and deployed the Pennsylvania National Guard.

  • Race, ethnicity, and class as mutually reinforcing divisions: Industrial capitalism's class conflict was simultaneously a racial and ethnic conflict. The AFL's exclusion of unskilled, immigrant, and Black workers fragmented labor's potential solidarity. Employers deliberately used ethnic and racial strikebreakers to prevent cross-racial organizing. The racial hierarchy of the Gilded Age labor market (native-born white Protestants in management; Irish/German in semi-skilled trades; new immigrants in the most dangerous, lowest-paid work) was actively maintained, not merely inherited.

  • The paradox of Gilded Age labor: The United States had the most active labor movement in the industrial world in the 1880s yet developed the weakest union infrastructure by the early twentieth century. The explanation is structural: the combined weight of employer blacklisting, private mercenary forces, state military intervention, judicial hostility (liberty of contract, Sherman Act misapplication), and racially-organized labor supply suppressed even the most determined organizing efforts.

Vocabulary Review

  • Knights of Labor: Founded 1869; public organization from 1879 under Powderly; 700,000 members (1886 peak); inclusive across craft, race, and gender; sought cooperative transformation of capitalism; collapsed after Haymarket.

  • American Federation of Labor (AFL): Founded 1886 by Gompers; skilled craft workers only; "pure and simple" trade unionism — wages, hours, conditions through collective bargaining; excluded unskilled, immigrant, and Black workers.

  • Pure and Simple Trade Unionism: Gompers's philosophy: achieve higher wages, shorter hours, and better conditions through collective bargaining without political radicalism or utopian programs.

  • Great Railroad Strike (1877): First national labor uprising; first federal troop deployment against labor; established federal government as capital's enforcer.

  • Haymarket Riot (1886): Chicago rally for eight-hour day; bomb killed 7 officers; eight anarchists convicted on insufficient evidence; four executed; permanently associated labor with radical violence in public mind.

  • Homestead Strike (1892): Carnegie Steel; Frick locked out Amalgamated union; 300 Pinkertons fought 12-hour battle with steelworkers; 8,500 National Guard ended siege; union destroyed; Carnegie Steel non-union 4 decades.

  • Pullman Strike (1894): Pullman Palace Car Company wage cuts without rent reductions; Debs's ARU sympathy boycott paralyzed Midwest rail; Cleveland obtained federal injunction; troops sent over Altgeld's objection; Debs jailed.

  • Government by Injunction: Doctrine produced by Pullman Strike: federal courts could order strikes ceased and jail union leaders for contempt without jury trial whenever interstate commerce was affected; used by employers for three decades.

  • In re Debs (1895): Supreme Court case upholding the federal injunction against the Pullman boycott and Debs's imprisonment for contempt; constitutional foundation of government by injunction.

  • Liberty of Contract: Judicial doctrine that workers and employers were equal parties to voluntary wage negotiations; made labor protective legislation constitutionally suspect; ignored actual power imbalances.

  • Blacklist: Employer-circulated list of strike participants and union organizers; made individual workers unemployable in their trade as punishment for labor activity.

  • Pinkerton Agents: Private mercenary guards hired to protect strikebreakers and intimidate workers; used at Homestead (1892); eventually restricted by Anti-Pinkerton Act (1893), weakly enforced.

  • Gospel of Wealth (1889): Carnegie's essay; stewardship of surplus capital for public benefit; "the man who dies thus rich dies disgraced"; revealed as ideological by Homestead (1892) three years later.

  • Deskilling: The process by which machinery — the Bessemer process, mechanized rolling mills — replaced craft workers' skilled knowledge with machinery-tended unskilled labor, eliminating workers' bargaining power.

Key Figures and Events

  • Terence V. Powderly: Knights of Labor leader; pursued cooperative and political transformation agenda; ultimately unable to protect the Knights from the Haymarket backlash.

  • Samuel Gompers: AFL founder; pragmatic "pure and simple" trade unionist; maintained political non-partisanship; AFL's survival and growth during the Gilded Age is both an achievement and a reflection of what had to be sacrificed — solidarity — to survive.

  • Andrew Carnegie: Absent in Scotland during Homestead; approved wage cuts that precipitated the confrontation; donated ~$350 million to public institutions after selling Carnegie Steel in 1901. The gap between the Gospel of Wealth and Homestead is the defining contradiction of his biography.

  • Henry Clay Frick: Carnegie's deputy at Homestead; hired Pinkertons; survived assassination attempt by Alexander Berkman; symbol of the brutal enforcement arm of industrial capitalism.

  • Eugene V. Debs: ARU founder; jailed for contempt during Pullman Strike; radicalized in prison; Socialist Party presidential candidate (1900–1920); one of the most significant political figures produced by Gilded Age labor conflict.

  • President Grover Cleveland: Democrat; sent federal troops to break Pullman Strike over Illinois Governor Altgeld's objection; obtained the injunction against Debs; illustrates that the Democratic Party's relationship with labor was as ambivalent as the Republican Party's.

  • John Altgeld: Illinois governor who objected to federal intervention in Pullman; had previously pardoned Haymarket defendants; sacrificed his political career for principled opposition to government-by-injunction.

  • Great Railroad Strike, 1877: Historical significance: first national labor uprising; first federal military deployment against labor; revealed the depth of class anger in industrial America and established the federal government's role.

Themes

Work, Exchange, and Technology (WXT): The Bessemer process and mechanized production that made Carnegie's steel cheap also systematically destroyed the craft knowledge that gave skilled ironworkers bargaining power. Deskilling was not a byproduct of industrial efficiency — it was a mechanism of employer power. When skilled workers' knowledge became obsolete, their leverage disappeared. The WXT theme in labor history is always also a power-relations story.

Social Stratification (SOC): The racial and ethnic structure of the Gilded Age labor market was not simply prejudice inherited from an earlier era — it was actively maintained through AFL exclusions, employer strikebreaker strategy, and immigration patterns. Native-born white Protestants in management; Irish and German "old immigrants" in semi-skilled trades; southern and eastern European "new immigrants" in the most dangerous and lowest-paid work; Chinese workers in specific racialized niches; Black workers systematically excluded from the most organized sectors. This hierarchy served employers by preventing the cross-racial solidarity that would have made labor far more powerful.

Politics and Power (POL): The Gilded Age state was not neutral in labor disputes. The 1877 precedent — federal troops breaking a strike — was repeated consistently through 1894. The courts' application of conspiracy doctrine, liberty of contract, and the Sherman Act against unions constructed a legal framework that treated labor organizing as inherently criminal while treating business monopoly as essentially legitimate. The state's role in labor conflict was not occasional or reluctant — it was systematic.

Connections

Connection to Section 6.2 (Industrial Capitalism): The strikes described here are the direct response to the industrial capitalism described in Section 6.2. Carnegie's vertical integration and cost-cutting created the wage-cut-and-speedup conditions that produced Homestead. Rockefeller's monopoly pricing and railroad rate discrimination created the structural economic grievances that drove workers into unions and farmers into the Populist movement. Industrial capitalism's efficiency gains and its labor conflicts are two aspects of the same process.

Connection to Section 6.4 (Immigration): Employers deliberately used immigrant workers as strikebreakers, exploiting ethnic tensions to prevent working-class solidarity. The AFL's exclusion of recent immigrants — justified by Gompers as protecting skilled workers' wages — deepened the ethnic fragmentation of the labor market. Immigration and labor conflict were structurally linked: the same immigration that supplied industrial capitalism's labor force also provided the ethnic divisions employers weaponized against organizing.

Connection to Progressive Era: The Gilded Age labor defeats created the conditions for Progressive Era labor reform. The "government by injunction" doctrine was eventually restricted by Norris-LaGuardia (1932). The liberty of contract doctrine was repudiated by West Coast Hotel v. Parrish (1937). The right to organize was guaranteed by the Wagner Act (1935). The Gilded Age defeats are comprehensible only in light of the Progressive and New Deal reversals they eventually generated.

AP Exam Approach

HAPP Analysis — Gospel of Wealth (1889):

  • Historical Context: Written during a period of intensifying labor conflict and public anxiety about industrial concentration; published three years before Homestead.
  • Audience: Literate middle- and upper-class readers of North American Review — the opinion-making elite whose framing of inequality mattered.
  • Purpose: Defend industrial concentration; recast wealthy as stewards rather than exploiters; forestall labor militancy and government redistribution.
  • Point of View: Carnegie was the era's second-richest man; his conduct at Homestead (1892) directly contradicted his published values — the "stewardship" framing served his class interest whether sincerely held or not.

Continuity and Change Over Time (CCOT): Federal government's relationship with labor conflict, 1877–1935: CONTINUITY through 1932 — consistent deployment of troops, courts, and injunctions against labor. CHANGE beginning with Norris-LaGuardia (1932) and Wagner Act (1935) — federal government shifted toward protecting workers' right to organize.

Short Answer Question (SAQ): Part A: Compare the Knights of Labor and the AFL in terms of membership and goals. Part B: Explain how the Pullman Strike (1894) produced the "government by injunction" doctrine. Part C: Explain ONE way in which the racial and ethnic divisions within the Gilded Age labor movement weakened workers' bargaining power.

Long Essay Question (LEQ): Evaluate the extent to which the federal government served as capital's instrument against labor during the Gilded Age (1865–1900). Build the argument around the 1877, 1892, and 1894 cases, the Sherman Act's labor application, the liberty of contract doctrine, and the courts' consistent alignment with employers. Acknowledge the first halting regulatory responses (ICC 1887, Sherman Act 1890) as evidence against the strongest version of the claim, while demonstrating that their actual enforcement reinforced rather than challenged capital's dominance.
