AP US History — Topic 7.4: The Cultural Clashes of the 1920s

Key Concepts

  • Two visions of America — not a caricature contest: The 1920s central conflict was between urban modernism (pluralist, comfortable with rapid change) and rural traditionalism (Protestant, defending a cultural order under threat). Neither side was simply enlightened or simply bigoted — both were responding to real disruptions. The analytical key is understanding both sides' internal logic.

  • Mass consumer culture as disruptor: The automobile, radio, motion pictures, and national advertising created a shared mass culture that challenged traditional authority structures. The flapper was not an isolated anomaly but the visible expression of a structural transformation in how Americans lived, consumed, and related to gender conventions.

  • The Harlem Renaissance — cultural assertion amid political oppression: African American artists, writers, and musicians in Harlem created the most significant flowering of Black cultural expression the nation had yet produced, simultaneously asserting dignity and demonstrating the gap between cultural achievement and political equality. Jim Crow did not pause for the Jazz Age.

  • The traditionalist backlash — Klan, Prohibition, immigration restriction: The second Klan (4 million members at peak), Prohibition's nativist dimensions, and the National Origins Act's racial hierarchy of immigrants were not isolated phenomena but expressions of a coherent traditionalist politics organized around Protestant moral order and Anglo-Saxon cultural dominance.

  • Immigration restriction as racial policy: The National Origins Act (1924) used the 1890 census baseline deliberately to minimize representation of Southern and Eastern European immigrants — virtually shutting immigration from Catholic and Jewish countries while maintaining openness to Northern and Western Europeans. Asian immigration was nearly eliminated. The law encoded racial hierarchy into federal immigration policy for four decades.

  • The fragility of democratic consensus: The decade's historical significance is not that either side won but that the conflict revealed how fragile consensus is in a rapidly changing, heterogeneous democracy — and that both competing visions had a plausible claim to democratic legitimacy.

Vocabulary Review

  • Flapper: Young women of the 1920s whose visible rejection of Victorian gender conventions (bobbed hair, short skirts, public smoking, dancing) embodied the challenge that mass consumer culture posed to traditional authority structures.

  • Jazz: Music born in African American traditions of New Orleans and Chicago; became the decade's defining cultural sound and a flashpoint for debates about racial mixing, modernity, and moral order.

  • Harlem Renaissance: The most significant flowering of African American artistic and intellectual culture the nation had yet produced, emerging from the Great Migration's concentration of Black artists and intellectuals in northern cities, c. 1920–1935.

  • Great Migration: The mass movement of Black Southerners to northern cities (primarily 1910s–1920s), creating the demographic conditions for the Harlem Renaissance while not escaping northern racism.

  • Lost Generation: American writers (Hemingway, Fitzgerald) who experienced WWI's trauma and critiqued 1920s commercial optimism as shallow and meaningless substitutes for genuine values.

  • Second Ku Klux Klan: Revived 1915, peaked at 4 million members c. 1924; expanded beyond anti-Black violence to target Catholics, Jews, immigrants, and moral deviants; collapsed after leader's rape-and-murder conviction in 1925.

  • Prohibition (Eighteenth Amendment): Ban on manufacture, sale, and transportation of intoxicating liquors (1920–1933). Produced organized crime, corrupted law enforcement, and disrespect for law; repealed by Twenty-First Amendment.

  • Emergency Quota Act (1921): First significant immigration restriction by national origin; set quota at 3% of 1910 census.

  • National Origins Act (1924): Reduced quota to 2% based on 1890 census, virtually eliminating Southern and Eastern European immigration and nearly eliminating Asian immigration. Encoded racial hierarchy into immigration law.

  • Scopes Trial (1925): Trial of Tennessee teacher for teaching evolution; became national cultural spectacle of the urban-rural cultural divide, with Darrow cross-examining Bryan on biblical literalism.

  • Sacco and Vanzetti case: Italian immigrant anarchists convicted of murder on disputed evidence; executed 1927; symbol of nativist injustice in the decade's anti-radical climate.

  • Birth of a Nation (1915): D.W. Griffith film celebrating Klan Reconstruction-era terrorism; associated with the second Klan's refounding. Demonstrates how popular culture provides ideological sanction for hate movements.

Key Figures and Events

  • Langston Hughes (1902–1967): Harlem Renaissance poet who wrote in vernacular Black speech and insisted Black life was worthy of serious literary attention. His poem 'I, Too, Sing America' directly asserted Black belonging in the American national narrative.

  • Zora Neale Hurston (1891–1960): Anthropologist and fiction writer who documented Southern Black folk culture. Their Eyes Were Watching God (1937) is her masterwork — rooted in the Harlem Renaissance's assertion of Black cultural richness.

  • Louis Armstrong (1901–1971): Jazz musician who transformed New Orleans collective improvisation into a virtuoso global art form. His influence on American music extended through the twentieth century.

  • F. Scott Fitzgerald (1896–1940): The Great Gatsby (1925) critiqued the 1920s dream of self-reinvention through wealth as hollow and morally corrupted. Jay Gatsby's trajectory — obsession, self-invention, destruction — was Fitzgerald's comment on American consumer culture's substitution of materialism for meaning.

  • William Jennings Bryan (1860–1925): Three-time Democratic presidential candidate; Populist hero; anti-imperialist; biblical literalist. Prosecuted the Scopes Trial; died five days after the verdict. His career's final chapter illustrates the complexity of Progressive-era political figures whose later positions confound easy categorization.

  • Clarence Darrow (1857–1938): Nation's most celebrated defense attorney; agnostic. Cross-examined Bryan at Scopes on biblical literalism, exposing the contradictions in a literal reading of Genesis to a national press audience.

  • David Stephenson: National Klan leader convicted of rape and murder of a young woman in Indiana in 1925. His conviction collapsed the Klan's political influence — not external resistance but internal moral corruption.

  • Nicola Sacco and Bartolomeo Vanzetti: Italian immigrant anarchists convicted of murder in Massachusetts 1921, on evidence later historians consider thin. Executed 1927 after a seven-year legal ordeal. Their case attracted worldwide attention and crystallized the decade's nativist anxieties.

  • Al Capone (1899–1947): Chicago organized crime boss who became a celebrity gangster through Prohibition's black market. His rise illustrated how legislating cultural behavior against embedded social practices creates criminal enterprises and corrupts law enforcement.

Themes

The 1920s section illustrates the Social (SOC), Cultural (CUL), and National Identity (NAT) themes in particularly rich ways. The decade produced a genuine cultural revolution in gender norms, popular music, and consumer culture — and produced equally genuine resistance to that revolution organized around Protestant moral order, Anglo-Saxon nativity, and rural community values.

The Harlem Renaissance is central to AP analysis because it demonstrates the disconnect between cultural achievement and political equality. The Great Migration created the demographic conditions for the Renaissance; Jim Crow and northern racism created the oppressive context it responded to. Langston Hughes's assertion that Black life was worthy of serious literary attention was simultaneously an artistic claim and a political demand — in a decade when that claim was contested by Klan violence, immigration restriction's implicit racial hierarchy, and continuing disenfranchisement.

The NAT theme runs through every element of the section: who is authentically American? The traditionalists' answer — Protestant, Anglo-Saxon, rural — was challenged by the pluralists' answer — heterogeneous, urban, ethnically diverse. The National Origins Act's four-decade impact shows which answer the federal government adopted for immigration policy. The Harlem Renaissance shows that Black artists did not wait for that answer before asserting their own claim to full American cultural citizenship.

Compare and Contrast

Harlem Renaissance and Lost Generation: Both were cultural responses to WWI's devastation and 1920s commercial culture — but from opposite starting points. The Lost Generation writers (Hemingway, Fitzgerald) were white, often expatriate, and critiqued the American dream from within the dominant culture. The Harlem Renaissance writers were Black, urban, and asserted the worth of a culture the dominant society had attempted to suppress. Both rejected the complacency of 1920s commercial optimism; neither was simply celebratory.

Immigration restriction (National Origins Act) and nativist cultural politics (Klan, Prohibition): Both expressed the same underlying anxiety about a heterogeneous America in which Protestant Anglo-Saxon cultural dominance was no longer automatic. The National Origins Act addressed the anxiety through federal law; the Klan addressed it through political organization and private terror; Prohibition addressed it through legislated moral conformity. All three operated simultaneously during the decade, reinforcing each other's cultural politics.

AP Exam Approach

Common AP prompts for this section:

  • Explain how the Harlem Renaissance was both a response to racial oppression and an assertion of cultural independence. Use at least two specific artists or works.
  • Analyze the connection between the immigration restriction acts of 1921 and 1924 and the nativist anxieties of the 1920s. What assumptions about American national identity do these laws encode?
  • Evaluate Prohibition as a reform. Was it a failure? What evidence supports and qualifies that conclusion?

Key analytical move: The exam will often ask you to explain both sides of the 1920s cultural conflict without reducing either to a caricature. The modernist side included genuine artistic innovation (Harlem Renaissance, jazz, Lost Generation) and genuine democratic expansion (women's mass consumer culture). The traditionalist side included genuine community anxiety about real disruptions (urbanization, immigration, moral change), not just irrational bigotry — though bigotry was also real and consequential.

Short-Answer Question Practice:

"The 1920s was a decade in which American cultural life was defined by fundamental conflicts over American identity."

  • Part A: Describe ONE way in which urban modernist culture challenged traditional American values during the 1920s.
  • Part B: Describe ONE way in which traditionalist movements responded to those challenges.
  • Part C: Explain how the Harlem Renaissance both reflected and challenged the racial hierarchy of 1920s American society.
