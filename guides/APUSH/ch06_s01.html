AP US History — Topic 6.1: The Conquest of the West

Key Concepts

  • Western settlement as coordinated dispossession: The post-Civil War incorporation of the trans-Mississippi West was not an empty-land migration but the systematic displacement of indigenous nations through military force, treaty violation, and federal legislation. The transcontinental railroad, the commercial buffalo hunt, the mining rushes, the Homestead Act, and the Dawes Severalty Act together constituted a coherent policy — sometimes explicit, often informal — of converting communally held indigenous land into privatized American property. Historian Patricia Limerick's term "conquest" more accurately names this process than the celebratory frontier mythology.

  • The railroad as political project, not engineering feat: The Pacific Railway Acts of 1862 and 1864 reveal that the transcontinental railroad was born of congressional action — made possible by federal land grants and bond loans — not private enterprise alone. The Crédit Mobilier scandal illustrated the Gilded Age's defining feature: the federal government as capitalism's enabling partner, not its regulator. The railroad's Chinese and Irish workers were systematically erased from the commemorative record, a visual testament to racial hierarchy in the conquest narrative.

  • The 100th meridian and the limits of the homestead ideal: The Homestead Act's promise of free land partially succeeded in the humid Midwest but was routinely undermined west of the 100th meridian, where 160 acres was too small for viable dry-land farming, drought broke families, and speculators pre-empted the best land. Of 500 million acres distributed under federal land programs between 1862 and 1900, much ended up in corporate and large-landholder hands rather than with family farmers.

  • Mining frontiers and the myth of the lone prospector: Successive mineral rushes across the Rockies and Great Plains followed a predictable arc: individual prospectors followed by capital-intensive industrial operations, boom followed by bust. The "frontier" was not a space of rugged individual independence but an investment frontier where Eastern and European capital rapidly supplanted the lone miner. Virginia City, Leadville, and Deadwood were symbols of western capitalism, not frontier individualism.

  • The Plains Indian Wars as policy, not defense: Federal policy oscillated between treaty-making and military conquest, but in both phases the underlying goal was subjugation. The Fort Laramie Treaty of 1868 was violated within six years when gold was discovered in the Black Hills. Little Bighorn (1876) demonstrated that Native nations could win battles but could not reverse the structural imbalance of power. Wounded Knee (1890) ended armed resistance; the Dawes Act (1887) completed land dispossession through legislation what the military had begun through force.

  • The Dawes Act's reformist packaging, catastrophic outcome: Supported by Massachusetts reformers who genuinely believed allotment would help Native peoples, the Dawes Act stripped ~90 million of 138 million reservation acres by 1934. The "surplus" mechanism — selling all land beyond individual allotment needs — made dispossession structurally inevitable. This is a critical case study in the gap between stated reform intent and policy outcome.

  • The frontier myth and its ideological function: Turner's 1893 Frontier Thesis treated the West as the formative space of American democratic character. Its erasure of indigenous peoples served ideological purposes — naturalizing conquest as civilization, presenting dispossession as progress — and remained dominant in American culture for decades. The "New Western History" pioneered by Limerick and others in the 1980s–90s challenged this mythology directly.

Vocabulary Review

  • Transcontinental Railroad: The first rail link spanning the continental United States, completed May 10, 1869, at Promontory Summit, Utah Territory. Built by Union Pacific (west from Council Bluffs) and Central Pacific (east from Sacramento) with federal land grants and bond loans; reduced cross-continental travel from months to six days.

  • Pacific Railway Acts (1862, 1864): Federal legislation providing ~10 million acres in alternating land-grant sections and $16,000–$48,000/mile in bond loans to the transcontinental railroad companies.

  • Crédit Mobilier: Construction subsidiary of the Union Pacific that charged inflated costs and distributed discounted stock to members of Congress; the 1872 scandal revealed the corrupt entanglement of government and railroad capital.

  • Homestead Act (1862): Offered 160 acres of public land free to any head of household who settled and improved it for five years. Partially successful in the humid Midwest; undermined by semiarid conditions, drought, and speculation on the Great Plains.

  • 100th Meridian: The approximate boundary between sufficient and insufficient rainfall for conventional agriculture; a key geographical limit on Homestead Act viability.

  • Open Range: Unfenced federal and state land used for grazing by the Texas longhorn cattle industry, 1860s–1890; ended by the brutal winter of 1886–87 and arrival of homesteader barbed-wire fencing.

  • Fort Laramie Treaty (1868): Acknowledged Sioux ownership of the Black Hills; violated by the federal government six years later when gold was discovered there.

  • Battle of Little Bighorn (1876): Largest Native military victory of the Plains Wars; Lakota, Cheyenne, and Arapaho forces annihilated Custer's Seventh Cavalry. Led to intensified military retaliation and most Lakota returning to reservations within a year.

  • Ghost Dance Movement: Messianic religious revival among Plains peoples in the late 1880s; misinterpreted by the army as armed insurrection, leading to the Wounded Knee Massacre.

  • Wounded Knee Massacre (1890): Soldiers killed 150–300 Lakota men, women, and children during a disarmament operation; marked the violent end of armed resistance on the Plains.

  • Dawes Severalty Act (1887): Divided reservation land into individual allotments; "surplus" land sold to white settlers. By 1934, approximately 90 million of 138 million reservation acres had passed to non-Indian ownership.

  • Indian Reorganization Act (1934): Reversed the Dawes allotment policy; allowed tribes to reconstitute communal landholding.

  • Frederick Jackson Turner: Historian who delivered the "Frontier Thesis" in 1893, arguing that successive frontiers shaped American democratic character. Critiqued for treating the West as empty land and erasing indigenous peoples.

  • Patricia Limerick: "New Western History" scholar who reframed the frontier as "conquest," centering dispossession, racial hierarchy, and environmental limits.

Key Figures and Events

  • Sitting Bull (Hunkpapa Lakota): Spiritual and military leader of the Plains resistance; organized the coalition that defeated Custer at Little Bighorn; fled to Canada, surrendered 1881, killed 1890 during Ghost Dance crisis.

  • Crazy Horse (Oglala Lakota): War leader at Little Bighorn and the Rosebud; surrendered May 1877; killed in custody at Fort Robinson, September 1877.

  • Lt. Col. George Armstrong Custer: Commander of five Seventh Cavalry companies annihilated at Little Bighorn; became a martyred symbol in white American mythology, though his attack was militarily reckless and followed federal treaty violations.

  • Henry Dawes (Massachusetts senator): Sponsor of the 1887 Severalty Act; reformist intent does not mitigate the catastrophic land-loss outcome of the legislation he championed.

  • General Philip Sheridan: Commander of post-Civil War Plains campaigns; reportedly acknowledged that commercial buffalo hunters were doing more to subjugate Plains peoples than decades of soldiers — a candid admission of deliberate ecological warfare.

  • Promontory Summit, May 10, 1869: Symbolic completion of the transcontinental railroad; the ceremony's exclusion of Chinese and Irish workers from the commemorative photograph encapsulates the racial politics of the conquest narrative.

  • Black Hills Gold Rush (1874–1876): Custer's 1874 expedition confirmed gold; the Grant administration declined to enforce the Fort Laramie Treaty; prospectors poured in; Sioux and Cheyenne resistance culminated at Little Bighorn.

  • Granger Laws (1870s): State-level railroad rate regulation backed by the Patrons of Husbandry (the Grange); initially upheld in Munn v. Illinois (1877), later gutted by Wabash (1886), leading to the Interstate Commerce Act (1887).

Themes

Geography and Environment (GEO): The 100th meridian is one of the most consequential geographical lines in American history. Its ecological reality — insufficient rainfall for conventional agriculture — made the Homestead Act's 160-acre unit structurally inadequate on the Plains, produced repeated drought failures, and ultimately forced farmers into the railroad-and-bank economy that the free-labor vision was supposed to liberate them from. The commercial buffalo hunt's near-eradication of 30 million bison by 1889 was simultaneously an ecological catastrophe and a deliberate instrument of Indian policy.

Migration (MIG): The post-Civil War West absorbed the largest internal migration in American history — hundreds of thousands of families from the East and from Europe, drawn by Homestead Act promises and railroad advertising. The migrants who came were themselves instruments of dispossession, even when their own experiences on the Plains were often brutal: drought, debt, crop-price crashes, and isolation.

Politics and Power (POL): Federal policy toward the West operated on multiple tracks simultaneously — land distribution (Homestead Act), railroad subsidization (Pacific Railway Acts), treaty-making and violation (Fort Laramie 1868), military conquest (1876–1890), and legislative dispossession (Dawes Act 1887). What looks like oscillating policy was in practice a consistent drive to convert indigenous land into private American property.

American and National Identity: The frontier myth — from dime novels' erasure of Black and Mexican cowboys to Turner's Frontier Thesis — constructed a white masculine narrative of western settlement that naturalized conquest. The "New Western History" deconstructs this mythology by centering the perspectives of indigenous peoples, immigrants, and non-white workers whose labor and land were consumed by the process.

Connections

Connection to Section 6.2 (Industrial Capitalism): The railroad that made western settlement possible was also the primary mechanism of industrial capitalism's expansion. The same Crédit Mobilier structure that defrauded the federal government also modeled the corporate fraud and government capture that Carnegie and Rockefeller would practice in steel and oil. Western natural resources — Mesabi iron ore, Pennsylvania coal, western silver and copper — fed the industrial furnaces of the Gilded Age.

Connection to Section 6.5 (Populism): The railroad that "conquered" the West also trapped western farmers in structural dependency. Wheat farmers in Kansas who could not negotiate freight rates with monopoly railroads, or who carried mortgage debt at 8–12 percent against falling crop prices, were the social base of the Populist movement. The conquest of the West created the agrarian grievance that fueled the most significant mass political movement of the nineteenth century.

Connection to Reconstruction and Section 6.6 (Segregation): The Dawes Act's citizenship offer — extended to Native Americans who accepted allotments and "adopted the habits of civilized life" — used the same paternalistic language as Reconstruction-era freedmen's policy, and produced similarly catastrophic outcomes through federal abandonment. The post-1877 withdrawal of federal protection for Black Southerners and the simultaneous escalation of military conquest against Plains Indians reflect a consistent federal retreat from the post-Civil War expansion of rights.

Long-term Connection — Indian Reorganization Act (1934): The reversal of the Dawes Act by the 1934 IRA illustrates how policy disasters can be acknowledged and partially corrected, but cannot undo irreversible land losses. The ~90 million acres lost between 1887 and 1934 were not returned.

AP Exam Approach

Document-Based Question (DBQ) strategy: If a DBQ involves western expansion, Native American policy, or the "frontier," practice distinguishing between the rhetoric of "civilization," "progress," and "improvement" in primary sources and the material reality of dispossession those documents authorized. A source from Henry Dawes justifying allotment policy should be analyzed for historical context (post-military conquest; reformer audience), purpose (defend policy to eastern liberals), and point of view (paternalistic assumption of indigenous cultural inferiority), then compared to the policy's actual outcomes.

Long Essay Question (LEQ): Common prompts compare the federal government's role in western expansion to its role in other economic or social developments. Practice arguing that the federal government was not a passive facilitator but an active agent — through land grants, bond loans, military deployments, treaty violations, and allotment legislation — in creating both the industrial railroad economy and the systematic dispossession of indigenous peoples.

Short Answer Question (SAQ):

Part A: Describe ONE way in which the transcontinental railroad accelerated the dispossession of Plains Indian nations beyond simply enabling military campaigns against them.

Part B: Explain ONE reason why the Homestead Act failed to fulfill its promise of turning landless workers into independent farmers on the Great Plains.

Part C: Explain how the Dawes Severalty Act (1887) illustrates the gap between reformers' stated intentions and the actual consequences of federal Indian policy.

Continuity and Change Over Time (CCOT): Federal Indian policy, 1850s–1934: From treaty-making (Fort Laramie 1851, 1868) to military conquest (1876–1890) to legislative allotment (Dawes 1887) to reversal (IRA 1934). The consistent thread across all phases: the conversion of indigenous communal land tenure into private American property.

Comparison prompt: Compare the experiences of homesteaders on the Great Plains with the experiences of Plains Indian nations displaced by the same federal policies. Both groups were subject to the structural power of railroads, federal land policy, and commodity markets — but from radically different positions of power and with radically different outcomes.
