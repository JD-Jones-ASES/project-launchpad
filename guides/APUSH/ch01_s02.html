AP US History — Topic 1.2: European Expansion and Spanish Conquest

Key Concepts

  • European expansion was driven by specific pressures, not accidents of curiosity: Maritime expansion in the fifteenth century emerged from religious, economic, and geopolitical pressures that pushed Iberian powers into the Atlantic at the precise moment when improved ship technology made the venture survivable. The Reconquista, economic competition with Portugal, and the search for a western route to Asia's trade goods converged in 1492.

  • The Reconquista as ideological foundation for conquest: For nearly eight centuries, Castile and Aragon fought to reclaim the Iberian Peninsula from Muslim rulers. This experience produced a distinctly militant Catholic culture that celebrated holy war, rewarded soldiers with land and tribute, and developed legal frameworks (the requerimiento) for subordinating non-Christians. These elements transferred almost seamlessly into the framework for American conquest.

  • Three simultaneous events, January 1492: The fall of Granada (completing the Reconquista), the expulsion of the Jews, and the commission of Columbus all occurred within months of each other in 1492. The source argues they were not coincidental — all reflected the same ideology of religious exclusion and holy war that would define Spanish behavior in the Americas.

  • Portuguese technological innovation made transoceanic voyaging possible: Beginning under Prince Henry the Navigator, Portugal systematically developed the lateen-rigged caravel and accumulated astronomical and navigational data. Dias rounded the Cape of Good Hope in 1488; da Gama reached India in 1498. Columbus's westward voyage in 1492 was in strategic response to Portugal's African monopoly.

  • The conquest was contingent, not inevitable: The Spanish conquest of the Aztec and Inca empires was enabled by a specific convergence of three factors: (1) epidemic disease decimated indigenous populations before decisive battles; (2) indigenous political divisions created alliance partners who outnumbered Spanish forces; (3) Reconquista ideology gave Spaniards a flexible legal and religious framework for subordinating non-Christians. The Noche Triste — which killed nearly half of Cortés's Spanish force in June 1520 — demonstrates how close to failure the conquest came.

  • The conquest was as much a civil war as a foreign invasion: The Tlaxcalans, subject to Aztec tribute extraction, supplied tens of thousands of indigenous warriors to the Spanish cause. Spanish forces were a minority within their own army. Understanding this dimension is essential for explaining why the conquest succeeded so rapidly.

  • Epidemic disease preceded Spanish soldiers: Smallpox killed Aztec emperor Cuitláhuac in 1520 before Tenochtitlan fell in 1521. In the Andes, smallpox transmitted overland from the Caribbean — before Pizarro arrived — killed Emperor Huayna Capac and triggered the civil war that Pizarro exploited at Cajamarca. Pathogens were the vanguard of conquest.

Vocabulary Review

  • Reconquista: The centuries-long series of Christian military campaigns (c. 718–1492) to reclaim the Iberian Peninsula from Muslim political control. Its legacy — holy war ideology, land-and-tribute rewards for soldiers, legal frameworks for non-Christian subordination — structured Spanish behavior in the Americas.

  • Hidalgo: A member of the Spanish minor nobility; the social class from which most conquistadores were drawn. Hidalgos sought honor, wealth, and social advancement through military service, creating the social engine of conquest.

  • Requerimiento: A legal document read aloud to indigenous peoples offering them submission before military attack — described by the source as a 'legal fiction' because it created a formal pretext for conquest regardless of whether indigenous peoples could understand, respond, or consent.

  • Conquistador: The Spanish term for the soldiers and adventurers who led military campaigns subjugating indigenous civilizations. They operated under capitulaciones — royal licenses granting the right to conquer territories in exchange for a crown share of the spoils.

  • Capitulación: A royal license authorizing a specific conquest expedition; a contract between the crown and the conquistador that distributed risk, authority, and profit while extending imperial reach at minimal direct crown cost.

  • Lateen-rigged caravel: The Portuguese ship design capable of sailing against the wind and in shallow coastal waters; the technology that made systematic Atlantic exploration and eventually transoceanic voyaging feasible.

  • Treaty of Tordesillas (1494): The papal-brokered agreement dividing the non-European world between Spain and Portugal along a line 370 leagues west of the Cape Verde Islands; gave Portugal Brazil and African/Asian routes, Spain the vast remainder of the Americas.

  • Tlaxcalans: An indigenous people of central Mexico who joined Cortés as alliance partners due to their subordination under Aztec tribute extraction; they supplied tens of thousands of warriors, making the conquest as much a civil war as a foreign invasion.

  • Noche Triste: The military disaster of June 1520 in which nearly half of Cortés's Spanish force was killed during a retreat from Tenochtitlan — evidence of the conquest's contingency and the real possibility of Spanish defeat.

  • Cuauhtémoc: The last Aztec emperor, captured when Tenochtitlan fell in August 1521; tortured, and executed in 1525. His capture completed the military destruction of the Aztec Triple Alliance.

  • Atahualpa: The Inca emperor captured by Pizarro at Cajamarca in November 1532 after a surprise attack that killed thousands of unarmed attendants. Paid the largest ransom in history (a room of gold and silver) but was executed in August 1533, collapsing the Inca imperial structure.

  • Cartas de Relación (Letters of Relation): Cortés's letters to the Spanish crown — both a primary source for the conquest and, as the source emphasizes, 'a masterclass in self-promotion.' Essential HAPP document for AP analysis.

Key Figures and Events

  • Isabella and Ferdinand (Los Reyes Católicos): The monarchs of Castile and Aragon who completed the Reconquista with the fall of Granada (January 1492), expelled Spain's Jews, and commissioned Columbus's westward voyage — three acts reflecting the same militant Catholic ideology.

  • Prince Henry the Navigator (c. 1394–1460): Portuguese royal patron who organized systematic Atlantic and African coast exploration, fostering the caravel's development and the accumulation of navigational data that made transoceanic voyaging feasible. Portugal's African monopoly directly provoked Spain's search for an alternative western route.

  • Bartolomeu Dias (1488): Portuguese navigator who rounded the Cape of Good Hope, demonstrating that Africa could be circumnavigated and opening a sea route to the Indian Ocean and Asia.

  • Vasco da Gama (1498): Portuguese navigator who reached India, completing the sea route to Asia's trade goods and demonstrating the practical success of Portugal's Atlantic investment.

  • Christopher Columbus (1492–1506): Genoese navigator commissioned by Castile who led four voyages between 1492 and 1504; his first landfall was in the Bahamas, with subsequent voyages to Cuba, Hispaniola, and the Caribbean and Central American coasts. He permanently breached the Americas' isolation from Afro-Eurasian networks, though he died (1506) believing he had reached the outskirts of Asia.

  • Hernán Cortés (1519–1521): Led the conquest of the Aztec Empire with roughly 500 soldiers; his success rested on political maneuvering — forging alliances with Aztec subject peoples, exploiting epidemic disease, and adapting to defeat at the Noche Triste. Appointed Governor of New Spain but died (1547) without the political recognition he sought.

  • Francisco Pizarro (1532–1535): Led the conquest of the Inca Empire with 168 soldiers; arrived when the Inca state was already destabilized by a succession war triggered by smallpox. Captured Atahualpa at Cajamarca, extracted a massive ransom, then executed him.

  • Cuitláhuac: The Aztec emperor who succeeded Moctezuma II during the siege of Tenochtitlan; killed by smallpox in 1520 before the city fell in 1521 — a direct example of epidemic disease shaping the political and military course of conquest.

  • Huayna Capac: The Inca emperor whose death (c. 1527) from smallpox transmitted overland from the Caribbean — before any Spaniard reached Peru — triggered the succession war that Pizarro exploited. His death is one of the clearest examples of disease preceding and enabling conquest.

Themes

Religion and Political Ideology (CUL + POL)

The Reconquista's legacy dominates this section's Political and Cultural themes. The concept of holy war against non-Christians — authorized by the Church and rewarded with land and tribute — was not merely rhetoric; it was institutionalized in the capitulación system and the requerimiento. The three January 1492 events (fall of Granada, expulsion of Jews, commission of Columbus) are the clearest evidence of a unified militant Catholic ideology organizing Spanish state action. For AP essays, this theme supports arguments about how cultural and religious frameworks shaped political and economic behavior.

Contingency and Multi-causality (Historical Thinking)

The conquest's success resulted from a convergence, not a single cause. The AP course emphasizes this explicitly: the Structural Logic of Conquest rests on three distinct factors — disease, political division, military technology — any one of which, removed, might have changed the outcome. Cortés survived the Noche Triste and rebuilt; without the Tlaxcalan alliance, his force was insufficient. This multi-causal analysis is exactly what AP essay rubrics reward.

Technology and the Environment (GEO + WXT)

Portuguese caravel technology directly enabled sustained Atlantic exploration and eventually transoceanic colonization. This is a Technology-Environment connection: better ship design plus accumulated astronomical knowledge changed what was geographically possible. Columbus's westward route was not feasible before the caravel and the navigational data that supported it. This parallels the role of Song dynasty navigational technology (compass) in the AP World curriculum — and can be used for cross-period comparison.

Connections to Earlier/Later Periods

  • Connections to Section 1.1 (Indigenous America Before Contact): The pre-contact complexity of the Aztec and Inca empires — established in Section 1.1 — is essential context for understanding why their conquests required alliance-building with subject peoples and why epidemics were so devastating. The Aztec tribute system that created Tlaxcalan resentment is the same tribute system introduced in Section 1.1.

  • Connections forward to Section 1.3 (The Columbian Exchange): The epidemic disease that prefigured and enabled the conquests is the same biological phenomenon analyzed in depth in Section 1.3 as the Columbian Exchange's most destructive dimension.

  • Connections forward to Section 1.4 (Encomienda and Casta): The capitulación system's land-and-tribute rewards for conquistadores directly generated the encomienda system: the same logic (military service rewarded with indigenous labor and tribute) was institutionalized into the colonial economy.

  • Connections to Unit 2 (English and French colonization): The Spanish conquest model — capitulación, requerimiento, encomienda — explicitly did NOT transfer to English colonization, which developed different legal and economic frameworks. English colonial promoters often defined themselves against Spanish "Black Legend" atrocities described by Las Casas, even as they replicated many structural patterns of indigenous dispossession.

  • Connections to AP World comparison: The Portuguese caravel and the Song dynasty magnetic compass represent parallel technological enablers of transoceanic exchange. Portuguese navigational technology diffused from Islamic and Iberian traditions, just as the compass diffused from Song China through Islamic navigators to European mariners.

AP Exam Approach

Document Analysis: Cortés's Letters of Relation

The source describes the Letters of Relation as 'a primary source for the conquest and a masterclass in self-promotion.' Apply the HAPP framework:

  • Historical Context: Written during and after an unauthorized expedition; Cortés needed royal legitimation.
  • Audience: King Charles I of Spain and the royal court — the one institution that could legitimize or punish Cortés.
  • Purpose: Political lobbying — to transform an illegal expedition into a celebrated royal service and maximize his own rewards.
  • Point of View: Cortés wrote as someone who needed to appear both loyal and irreplaceable. His accounts of indigenous rulers, Spanish heroism, and tactical decisions are all filtered through this self-promotional lens.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Attributing conquest solely to military technology: The source explicitly rejects this. Always name all three factors: disease, political division, technology.
  2. Treating the conquest as inevitable: The Noche Triste is your evidence against inevitability. Use it.
  3. Ignoring the indigenous alliance dimension: Tlaxcalans supplied tens of thousands of warriors. The 'Spanish' army was majority indigenous.
  4. Confusing the Reconquista's timeline with Columbus: The Reconquista was complete January 1492; Columbus sailed August 1492. They are connected, not simultaneous.

Sample Short-Answer Prompt

In the early sixteenth century, Spanish forces conquered both the Aztec Empire (1519–21) and the Inca Empire (1532–35) with relatively small military forces.

Part A: Identify ONE factor beyond Spanish military technology that explains the speed of the Spanish conquest of either the Aztec or Inca Empire.

Part B: Explain how the ideology and practices developed during the Reconquista shaped Spanish approaches to conquest in the Americas.

Part C: Explain ONE way in which indigenous peoples actively shaped the course of the Spanish conquest — as allies, resisters, or both.
