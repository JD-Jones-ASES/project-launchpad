AP US History — Topic 8.5: Vietnam, the Great Society, and the Counterculture

Key Concepts

  • Vietnam as a strategic and moral failure: The war in Vietnam failed on its own strategic terms — attrition strategy could not inflict casualties faster than North Vietnam could absorb them — and simultaneously constituted a moral catastrophe: the use of napalm, Agent Orange, search-and-destroy operations, and the massive civilian toll generated both international condemnation and domestic anti-war sentiment that eroded the war's legitimacy. The Tet Offensive's psychological impact was not merely a public relations problem; it exposed the fundamental dishonesty of the administration's optimistic public claims.

  • Vietnam and the Great Society are inseparable: Johnson's presidency is often discussed in two separate compartments — domestic triumph (Great Society) and foreign policy disaster (Vietnam). Historically these are inseparable: Vietnam consumed the federal budget and political capital that domestic reform required; the urban uprisings generated by conditions the Great Society promised to address were intensified by the sense that federal resources were being spent in Southeast Asia rather than in American cities. The two trajectories reinforced each other's failures.

  • The Gulf of Tonkin and war powers: The Gulf of Tonkin Resolution was passed based on reports of attacks that may not have occurred (the second incident was disputed at best). Johnson used the resolution as the functional equivalent of a declaration of war — committing 500,000 troops without Congress ever formally declaring war. The constitutional war-powers question raised by Vietnam was addressed by the War Powers Resolution (1973), which critics argue inadequately constrained executive war-making.

  • The Great Society's institutional legacy vs. its unfulfilled promise: The Great Society created Medicare, Medicaid, federal education aid (ESEA), Head Start, and VISTA — institutional changes that outlasted their political moment and represent the most significant expansion of the federal government's domestic role since the New Deal. But its anti-poverty ambitions were curtailed by Vietnam's costs, urban backlash, and the rights movements' demands for substantive economic equality beyond what the liberal coalition would fund. The gap between the Great Society's institutional achievements and its unfulfilled economic justice promise is historically essential.

  • The rights revolution as a chain reaction: The civil rights movement's rights language and organizing tactics were adopted with remarkable speed by women, Chicano farmworkers, Native Americans, and gay Americans. Each movement drew on civil rights precedents while developing its own distinct analysis of subordination. The cumulative effect was a permanent transformation of the social contract: the federal government's obligations expanded beyond protection from discrimination to active promotion of inclusion.

  • Immigration Act of 1965 — unintended transformation: The abolition of the National Origins Formula produced demographic consequences its architects did not anticipate. The shift in immigration sources from northern and western Europe to Asia, Latin America, and Africa was not the architects' goal (they expected the system to produce only modest changes); it was an unintended structural effect of replacing national origin criteria with family reunification and skills-based preferences.

Vocabulary Review

  • Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (1964): Congressional authorization for the president to use "all necessary measures" in Southeast Asia; passed 416-0/88-2; Johnson used it as a war declaration for a conflict that eventually involved 500,000 troops.
  • Vietnamization: Nixon's strategy of withdrawing American troops while transferring combat responsibility to South Vietnamese forces; extended the war by four years.
  • Attrition strategy: Westmoreland's Vietnam approach of inflicting unsustainable casualties; failed because body-count metrics masked enemy replenishment capacity.
  • Tet Offensive (1968): North Vietnamese coordinated offensive against 100+ South Vietnamese cities; militarily defeated but psychologically decisive — exposed the administration's false optimism about the war's progress.
  • Credibility gap: The widening gulf between official government claims about the war's progress and the reality visible on television; destroyed public trust in Johnson's Vietnam policy.
  • Great Society: LBJ's domestic reform program (1964–68): Medicare, Medicaid, Head Start, War on Poverty, ESEA, Immigration Act, Voting Rights Act.
  • Medicare: Federal health insurance for Americans over 65, established 1965; one of the Great Society's most enduring institutional legacies.
  • Medicaid: Federal-state health coverage program for low-income Americans of any age, established 1965.
  • Head Start: Federally funded preschool for low-income children; one of the most enduring War on Poverty programs.
  • Immigration and Nationality Act (1965): Abolished the National Origins Formula; replaced with family reunification and skills-based criteria; dramatically shifted immigration sources toward Asia, Latin America, and Africa.
  • The Feminine Mystique (1963): Friedan's book naming the "problem that has no name" — educated women's frustration in domestic confinement; crystallized second-wave feminism.
  • NOW (National Organization for Women): Co-founded by Friedan (1966); pursued gender equality through law and politics.
  • ERA (Equal Rights Amendment): Constitutional amendment prohibiting sex discrimination; passed Congress 1972; failed ratification by 1982 deadline.
  • Roe v. Wade (1973): Supreme Court ruling recognizing constitutional right to abortion in the first trimester; most controversial ruling of the era; overturned in Dobbs v. Jackson (2022).
  • United Farm Workers (UFW): Founded by Chavez and Huerta (1962); led successful grape boycott (1965–70); part of the Chicano rights movement.
  • AIM (American Indian Movement): Founded 1968; combined direct action (Wounded Knee occupation, 1973) with legal advocacy for treaty rights and self-determination.
  • Stonewall uprising (1969): Resistance to police raid on a Greenwich Village bar; launched the modern gay rights movement.
  • War Powers Resolution (1973): Congressional attempt to constrain executive war-making by requiring notification and limiting troop deployments without congressional authorization; critics argue it was inadequate.

Key Figures and Events

  • Lyndon B. Johnson (1908–1973): The most legislatively skilled president since FDR; his Great Society program was the apex of postwar American liberalism; his Vietnam escalation destroyed his presidency and shattered the liberal consensus that had made the Great Society possible. Johnson declined to seek reelection in March 1968 following the Tet Offensive's political impact.

  • Ngo Dinh Diem: South Vietnamese leader whose corrupt and unpopular government was the recipient of American military aid from Eisenhower through Kennedy; assassinated in November 1963 in a US-backed coup, three weeks before Kennedy's own assassination.

  • General William Westmoreland: Commander of US forces in Vietnam who developed the attrition strategy; his body-count metrics were manipulated to show progress; his request for 206,000 additional troops following Tet was leaked to the press, revealing that the administration's optimistic claims were hollow.

  • Walter Cronkite: CBS evening news anchor whose February 1968 editorial declaring Vietnam a "stalemate" influenced mass public opinion and reportedly influenced Johnson's own political calculus.

  • Betty Friedan (1921–2006): Author of The Feminine Mystique (1963) and co-founder of NOW (1966); the most prominent figure of second-wave feminism's liberal wing.

  • Cesar Chavez (1927–1993) and Dolores Huerta (b. 1930): Co-founders of the United Farm Workers; leaders of the Chicano labor and civil rights movement; the grape boycott demonstrated that consumer and labor organizing could be combined with the civil rights movement's tactics.

Themes

America in the World (WOR): Vietnam was a test of the containment doctrine's limits — could the United States sustain a limited war against a revolutionary nationalist movement that had fought colonialism for decades? The answer was no. The war demonstrated that Cold War strategic logic (preventing a domino effect of communist expansion) could not override the political and human costs of fighting a war without clear victory conditions, public support, or moral clarity.

Politics and Power (POL): The Gulf of Tonkin Resolution concentrated war-making power in the executive at the expense of Congress — a constitutional imbalance that the War Powers Resolution (1973) attempted to address. The fracturing of the Democratic Party between antiwar and pro-war factions at the 1968 Chicago convention illustrated how Vietnam split the New Deal coalition along cultural and generational lines that proved more durable than the economic coalition FDR had assembled.

Social Structures (SOC): The rights revolution demonstrated that the civil rights movement's structural insight — formal legal equality is insufficient without substantive economic and social power — was generalizable across different marginalized groups. Women, Chicanos, Native Americans, and gay Americans each developed distinct analyses of their community's subordination while sharing the rights language the civil rights movement had placed at the center of American political culture.

Connections

To ch08_s03 and ch08_s04 (Civil Rights): The rights language that made second-wave feminism, the Chicano movement, AIM, and Stonewall possible was developed in the civil rights movement. The Immigration Act of 1965 was also a product of the civil rights era's rights consciousness — the National Origins Formula's racial hierarchy was inconsistent with the equal protection principles the movement had centered.

To ch08_s06 (Nixon and the Crisis of Confidence): Vietnam's strategic failures and the credibility gap that Tet exposed set the stage for Nixon's "Silent Majority" appeal. Nixon's promise to end the war (while extending it through Vietnamization) was the central foreign policy theme of 1968–72. The war's eventual conclusion (Paris Peace Accords, 1973) came after four more years of fighting, additional casualties, and the expansion into Cambodia and Laos.

To the Reagan Revolution: The Great Society's institutional legacy (Medicare, Medicaid) was the target of Reagan-era reduction proposals; the ERA's failure and Roe v. Wade's passage were the mobilizing grievances of the New Right coalition. The rights revolution's expansion of the social contract became the fiscal and cultural target of conservative politics from 1980 forward.

AP Exam Approach

Likely essay contexts: Vietnam appears in prompts about the limits of American power, the relationship between foreign policy and domestic politics, and the causes of political change in the late 1960s. The Great Society appears in prompts about federal power expansion, the New Deal liberal tradition, and comparisons to earlier reform eras (Progressive Era, New Deal). The rights revolution appears in comparisons between different social movements and in change-over-time prompts about civil rights.

HAPP source practice: The Feminine Mystique (Friedan, 1963) is a frequently assigned document. Key analytical moves: (1) The historical context is the postwar suburban boom AND the civil rights movement's provision of rights vocabulary; (2) The audience is the book's crucial limitation — educated white middle-class women; (3) The purpose is to name the problem to mobilize around it; (4) The point of view is insider critique — Friedan speaking from within the domesticity she criticized. Critics (bell hooks) argued the audience limitation reflected a class- and race-specific perspective that excluded most women.

Common errors to avoid:

  • Do not describe the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution as based on fabricated evidence that Congress knew was false; Congress believed the attacks had occurred; the second incident's disputed status emerged later.
  • Do not describe the Great Society as a failure; its institutional legacies (Medicare, Medicaid, ESEA, Head Start) were durable successes; what failed was its more ambitious anti-poverty agenda.
  • Do not describe the Immigration Act of 1965 as intentionally designed to produce demographic transformation; the architects did not anticipate its long-term demographic consequences.

Short-Answer Practice:

Part A: Explain the strategic logic behind the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution. How did Johnson use it, and why did its underlying strategy ultimately fail?

Part B: Describe ONE Great Society program and explain its significance as an expansion of the federal government's domestic role.

Part C: Explain how the civil rights movement's rights language was adopted by ONE other social movement of the late 1960s or early 1970s.
