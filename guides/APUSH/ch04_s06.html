AP US History — Topic 4.6: Manifest Destiny in Foreign Affairs

Key Concepts

  • Manifest Destiny as empire in liberty's clothing: The ideology named by O'Sullivan in 1845 combined three elements: Protestant providentialism (God ordained American expansion), republican exceptionalism (the U.S. as the world's model of self-government), and racial hierarchy (Anglo-Saxon civilization as the continent's natural master). The source calls it "a doctrine of empire dressed in the language of liberty" — because from the perspective of the indigenous peoples, Mexicans, and others displaced, it was conquest justified by providence. The belief predated the phrase; O'Sullivan named an existing ideology that had been operating since at least the Louisiana Purchase.

  • The Monroe Doctrine's power and its limits: Monroe's 1823 declaration was the formal expression of American hemispheric ideology: the Americas were closed to future European colonization; any extension of European political systems would be treated as a threat to U.S. safety. Its ideological significance was immediate and lasting. Its operational significance was modest: enforced by British naval power (not American), largely ignored by European powers when they chose to intervene, and asserted a protectorate over Latin American republics who had not been consulted. The gap between ideological assertion and operational capacity is itself historically significant.

  • The Monroe Doctrine's silences: The doctrine said nothing about the rights of the hemisphere's indigenous peoples, enslaved Black Americans, or the Latin American republics in whose name it purported to speak. These silences are as revealing as the doctrine's assertions. It claimed hemispheric guardianship for a republic that held one-seventh of its people in bondage and was systematically dispossessing indigenous nations from the continent it claimed Providence had allotted to it.

  • Douglass's Fourth of July speech as strategic rhetoric: Douglass chose July 5 deliberately. His strategy was to accept the founders' principles as genuine moral truths — human equality, natural rights, government by consent — and then demonstrate that those principles made American slavery inescapable hypocrisy. He did not attack the founding; he used it. This strategy — meeting the republic on its own ideological ground and demonstrating the contradiction — was more devastating than a frontal assault on the founding, because it forced the audience to choose between their founding principles and their tolerance of slavery.

  • Two documents, two faces of one contradiction: The Monroe Doctrine and Douglass's Fourth of July speech both used the language of American liberty, but to opposite ends. Monroe projected liberty outward as the justification for American hemispheric power. Douglass exposed liberty's betrayal inward — demonstrating that a republic claiming to champion liberty while enslaving four million people was structurally dishonest about what it valued. Read together, they define the central moral tension of the period 1800–1848.

  • The gap between principle and practice as a structural choice: The period's central insight, articulated most clearly by Douglass: the gap between the republic's professed principles (all men are created equal) and its actual practice (slavery, Indian removal, women's legal subordination) was not an oversight or a temporary delay. It was a choice — repeated, maintained, and defended by those with the power to make it. Every major assertion of American liberty in this period was accompanied by a systematic exclusion of most of humanity from that liberty.

Vocabulary Review

  • Manifest Destiny: The ideology that U.S. continental expansion was divinely ordained, historically inevitable, and racially justified. Named by O'Sullivan (1845); operated since the Louisiana Purchase.

  • Monroe Doctrine (1823): Monroe's declaration closing the Western Hemisphere to future European colonization and warning against extension of European political systems; primarily authored by Secretary of State Adams.

  • John Quincy Adams: Secretary of State under Monroe; primary architect of the Monroe Doctrine; insisted on a unilateral American declaration rather than the joint Anglo-American statement Canning proposed.

  • Holy Alliance: Post-Napoleonic alliance of Austria, Prussia, and Russia; feared to be contemplating intervention to restore Spanish authority in Latin America; one trigger for the Monroe Doctrine.

  • John L. O'Sullivan: Democratic journalist who coined "Manifest Destiny" in 1845; named an existing ideology rather than inventing a new one.

  • "What to the Slave is the Fourth of July?" (1852): Douglass's July 5 speech to the Rochester Ladies' Anti-Slavery Society; called the greatest antislavery oration in American history; used the republic's own principles against its practice.

  • Fugitive Slave Act (1850): Required Northern citizens to assist in capturing escaped enslaved people; made slavery a national institution requiring Northern complicity; cited by Douglass as evidence of the hypocrisy of Northern liberty claims.

  • Protectorate relationship: The implied relationship between the United States and Latin American republics in the Monroe Doctrine — the U.S. claimed to protect them from European intervention without consulting them about this role.

  • The gap between principle and practice: The source's central interpretive framework for the period 1800–1848: every major assertion of American liberty was accompanied by systematic exclusion of most of humanity from that liberty — not through oversight but through deliberate choice.

Key Figures and Events

James Monroe (1758–1831): Fifth president; delivered the doctrine bearing his name but did not write it. Representative of the Virginia dynasty of slave-owning presidents who simultaneously articulated American liberty and maintained its contradictions.

John Quincy Adams (1767–1848): Monroe's Secretary of State; primary author of the Monroe Doctrine. Later, as a Congressman (1831–48), he led the successful fight to repeal the gag rule (1844). His career illustrates the contradictions of the era: he authored the hemispheric liberty doctrine and later fought for the right to debate slavery in Congress.

George Canning: British Foreign Secretary who proposed a joint Anglo-American declaration against European intervention in Latin America. Adams insisted on a unilateral American statement. Canning was privately amused: the Americans had asserted what Britain's navy would actually enforce.

Frederick Douglass (1818–1895): His Fourth of July speech (1852) is examined in this section as the era's most powerful single articulation of the gap between American principle and practice. The most photographed American of the 19th century, conscious of his visual representation as a political statement. His entire career — from self-taught reading to international lecturer and Lincoln advisor — embodied the republic's contradiction.

Monroe Doctrine and its 19th-century applications: The doctrine was invoked in the 1840s against European designs on Texas and California; the Roosevelt Corollary (1904) extended it to claim U.S. intervention rights across Latin America. The doctrine's legacy includes U.S. interventions in Cuba (1898, 1961), Nicaragua, Guatemala, and the Dominican Republic.

Mexican-American War (1846–48): The territorial expansion Manifest Destiny ideology justified. Taken together with the Texas annexation (1845) and the Oregon settlement (1846), the Mexican-American War completed the continental expansion the Monroe Doctrine had ideologically framed. Opposed by abolitionists (including Lincoln in Congress) as a war to extend slavery; Mexico lost half its territory.

Themes

Liberty and Empire as the Same Claim: Manifest Destiny and the Monroe Doctrine illustrate how the language of liberty was deployed to justify the expansion of American power over others. The doctrine claimed to protect liberty by excluding European influence; Manifest Destiny claimed to spread republican government by displacing indigenous peoples and Mexicans. From the perspective of those displaced, "liberty" and "conquest" were the same event described differently. The ideology's brilliance — and its dishonesty — was in making the conquest appear to serve universal values rather than American imperial interests.

What Silences Reveal: The Monroe Doctrine's silences — about indigenous peoples, enslaved Black Americans, Latin American republics — are as historically significant as its explicit claims. A doctrine that claims to protect the hemisphere's liberty while being silent about the majority of the hemisphere's people's rights reveals the actual scope of the liberty being protected: the interests of the white male American republic, not hemispheric liberation. Douglass's speech was significant partly because it explicitly named what Monroe's doctrine had silenced.

Rhetoric and Reality in Foreign Policy: The Monroe Doctrine was primarily a rhetorical act in 1823 — American power was insufficient to enforce it, and European powers ignored it when convenient. Yet its ideological framing shaped American foreign policy for a century. This illustrates an important historical lesson: ideological frameworks can be consequential even when initially unenforceable, because they establish the terms within which future actors think and act.

Douglass as the Period's Most Important Voice: The source presents Douglass's Fourth of July speech as the era's most penetrating analysis of its own contradictions. By accepting the founding principles, honoring the founders, and then demonstrating that accepting those principles required condemning slavery, Douglass made an argument that was more demanding of his white audience than any frontal attack on the founding could have been. His speech remains relevant because it models a form of moral argument that takes seriously the gap between stated values and actual practice.

Connections to Earlier and Later Periods

Earlier: The Monroe Doctrine builds directly on the Louisiana Purchase's implicit logic: the continent belongs to the United States. Jackson's Indian removal was the domestic expression of the same ideological claim that the Monroe Doctrine made hemispheric. The doctrine's assertion of a protectorate over Latin American republics anticipated the imperial foreign policy that would emerge more fully after 1898.

Later: The Monroe Doctrine became the foundation of American hemispheric policy through the 20th century. Roosevelt's Corollary (1904) converted Monroe's defensive anti-colonization claim into an affirmative American right to intervene in Latin American nations. Cold War containment policy drew on the same ideology — the United States as liberty's guardian in its sphere of influence. Douglass's Fourth of July speech became canonical not just for abolitionism but for all subsequent American civil rights discourse — from Frederick Douglass himself through the civil rights movement's invocation of the Declaration's principles.

The period as a whole (1800–1848): The section draws together the chapter's central argument: the period's simultaneous generation of the most expansive articulations of American liberty and the clearest evidence of that liberty's limits. The Louisiana Purchase, the market revolution, Jacksonian democracy, the Monroe Doctrine, and Manifest Destiny were all accompanied by — and in many cases required — the dispossession of indigenous peoples, the expansion of slavery, and the legal subordination of women and Black Americans. This was not contradiction; it was structure.

AP Exam Approach

Stimulus-based questions for this section will typically feature:

  • The Monroe Doctrine text — analyze its two central assertions, its silences, and its historical context
  • Douglass's "What to the Slave is the Fourth of July?" excerpts — analyze the rhetorical strategy and historical significance
  • Maps of territorial expansion — analyze the connection between expansion ideology and dispossession

HAPP for the Monroe Doctrine (1823):

  • Historical context: Independent Latin American republics; Holy Alliance threat; Russian Pacific expansion
  • Audience: Congress formally; European governments (especially Holy Alliance) practically
  • Author's purpose: Diplomatic deterrence; assert hemispheric primacy; close the Americas to future colonization
  • Point of view: Adams's nationalist vision of American continental destiny; protecting American interests while framing them as universal liberty; silent about whose liberty was actually protected

HAPP for Douglass's Fourth of July speech (1852):

  • Historical context: Fugitive Slave Act (1850); 4 million enslaved; antislavery movement under pressure
  • Audience: Northern white antislavery sympathizers; the broader American public indirectly
  • Author's purpose: Moral provocation; force comfortable Northerners to confront their complicity; expose hypocrisy of liberty rhetoric in a slave republic
  • Point of view: Speaking from exclusion — the perspective of those for whom the republic's celebrated liberty was "gross injustice"

Short-answer questions (SAQ) will often ask you to:

  • Explain the Monroe Doctrine's two central assertions and their historical context
  • Explain Douglass's rhetorical strategy: why did he begin by praising the founders?
  • Explain ONE connection between the Monroe Doctrine/Manifest Destiny and domestic politics of slavery or Indian removal

Key arguments to be ready to make:

  1. Manifest Destiny was empire in liberty's clothing — a claim of providential right that justified dispossession of indigenous peoples, Mexicans, and others from the perspective of American imperial expansion.
  2. The Monroe Doctrine's ideological significance was real and lasting despite its initial operational weakness — a rhetorical framework can shape foreign policy for a century without being immediately enforceable.
  3. The gap between the republic's professed principles and its actual practice (slavery, Indian removal, women's subordination) was not an oversight but a structural choice — maintained by those with the power to make it.
  4. Douglass's rhetorical strategy — accepting the founding principles and using them against the republic's practice — was more powerful than rejecting the founding, because it forced the audience to choose between their stated values and their tolerance of slavery.
