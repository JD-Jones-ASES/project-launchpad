AP US History — Topic 5.6: Presidential versus Radical Reconstruction

Key Concepts

  • The central constitutional question: Was Reconstruction an executive function (the president's war powers) or a legislative function (Congress's authority over admission of states)? Lincoln's assassination in April 1865, just as Reconstruction was beginning, left this question unresolved — and placed the executive in the hands of Andrew Johnson, a Tennessee Democrat with deeply racist views who would use presidential prerogatives to block the transformative Reconstruction that Radical Republicans sought.

  • Johnson's Presidential Reconstruction as counter-revolution: Johnson's Reconstruction (1865–67) was, as the source states, "indistinguishable from the restoration of the antebellum social order." He pardoned former Confederates by the thousands, allowing the planter class to resume political power. The Black Codes enacted under his watch — requiring Black people to present annual proof of employment or be arrested as vagrants, restricting occupational choices — were designed to recreate slavery's labor control under a different legal name.

  • Congress's constitutional counter-offensive: Congress responded to Johnson's obstructionism with escalating interventions: overriding his veto of the Civil Rights Act (1866), enacting the Reconstruction Acts of 1867 (military districts, Black male suffrage requirements), and attempting to constrain his executive power through the Tenure of Office Act. His impeachment (the first in American history) came within one Senate vote of removal.

  • The constitutional revolution of the Reconstruction Amendments: The Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments are the most consequential revisions to the Constitution since the Bill of Rights. The Thirteenth abolished slavery (overcoming the Emancipation Proclamation's limits). The Fourteenth established birthright citizenship (overturning Dred Scott), subjected state governments to federal constitutional constraints through the due process and equal protection clauses, and created the constitutional basis for most twentieth-century civil rights law. The Fifteenth prohibited racial denial of voting rights.

  • Black political agency during Radical Reconstruction: Approximately 2,000 Black men held elected office in the South between 1867 and 1877. Black voters, voting in overwhelming numbers, elected legislators, sheriffs, judges, and US senators. Reconstruction state governments established public school systems, built infrastructure, and enacted civil rights protections. These achievements directly contradict the Dunning School portrayal of Reconstruction as corrupt "Negro rule" — they were evidence of sophisticated political organization, not incapacity.

  • The gap between constitutional promise and enforcement: The Reconstruction Amendments' long-term significance — as the constitutional basis for twentieth-century civil rights law — exceeded what their framers anticipated. But the gap between ratification and effective enforcement spans nearly a century. The Thirteenth Amendment's exception clause enabled convict leasing; the Civil Rights Cases (1883) and Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) would narrow the Fourteenth Amendment's scope. Constitutional change without sustained political will is insufficient to enforce itself.

Vocabulary Review

  • 10% Plan: Lincoln's December 1863 Reconstruction plan requiring 10% of a state's 1860 voters to take a loyalty oath before the state could form a new government; prioritized quick reunification without guaranteeing Black rights.
  • Wade-Davis Bill (1864): Congressional Reconstruction bill requiring 50% voter loyalty oaths and stronger readmission guarantees; pocket-vetoed by Lincoln; reflected Radical Republicans' claim that Reconstruction was a legislative function.
  • Presidential Reconstruction (1865–67): Johnson's Reconstruction policy; lenient pardons for ex-Confederates, permitted Black Codes, opposed Black civil and political rights; essentially restored antebellum social order.
  • Black Codes: Post-emancipation laws in former Confederate states regulating Black labor, movement, and civil rights to recreate slavery's labor control under a different legal form.
  • Civil Rights Act of 1866: First civil rights legislation; granted citizenship to all U.S.-born persons; vetoed by Johnson; first congressional override of a major presidential veto in American history.
  • Reconstruction Acts of 1867: Divided former Confederate states into five military districts; required Black male suffrage and Fourteenth Amendment ratification for readmission; vetoed (repeatedly) by Johnson and overridden by Congress.
  • Tenure of Office Act (1867): Prohibited presidential removal of Senate-confirmed officials without Senate approval; Johnson's violation triggered his 1868 impeachment (one vote short of removal).
  • Thirteenth Amendment (1865): Abolished slavery throughout the United States; exception clause: "except as a punishment for crime."
  • Fourteenth Amendment (1868): Birthright citizenship; due process clause; equal protection clause; overturned Dred Scott; constitutional basis for twentieth-century civil rights law.
  • Fifteenth Amendment (1870): Prohibited denial of voting rights based on race, color, or previous condition of servitude; did not prohibit poll taxes, literacy tests, or other non-racial disfranchisement mechanisms.
  • Freedmen's Bureau (1865–1872): First federal social-welfare agency; distributed food, established schools and hospitals, supervised labor contracts; chronically underfunded; first recognition of federal obligation to address slavery's consequences.
  • Black officeholding (Reconstruction): ~2,000 Black officeholders in the South, 1867–77; 16 in Congress including Hiram Revels and Blanche Bruce (U.S. senators from Mississippi); Reconstruction state governments established the South's first public schools.
  • Dunning School: Early twentieth-century historical interpretation portraying Reconstruction as corrupt "Negro rule" imposed by vindictive Northerners — the intellectual foundation for Jim Crow legitimation. Definitively refuted by W.E.B. Du Bois (1935) and Eric Foner (1988).

Key Figures and Events

  • Thaddeus Stevens (1792–1868): House Radical Republican leader; advocated "conquered province" theory of Reconstruction (rebel states had forfeited constitutional rights), land redistribution to freed people ("forty acres"), and stringent Reconstruction requirements. His death in August 1868 removed the most uncompromising voice for Black equality from Congress.
  • Charles Sumner (1811–1874): Senate Radical Republican leader; Massachusetts senator who had been beaten by Preston Brooks in 1856. Advocated for Black civil and political rights; introduced multiple civil rights bills. The Civil Rights Act of 1875 (forbidding racial discrimination in public accommodations), enacted after his death, was partly his legislative legacy — and was struck down by the Supreme Court in 1883.
  • Andrew Johnson (1808–1875): The central obstacle to Reconstruction. His racial views, states'-rights ideology, and political identification with poor white Southerners made him constitutionally and psychologically unable to implement Reconstruction fairly. His systematic obstruction — pardons, Black Code tolerance, vetoes — required Congress to override him, constrain him (Tenure of Office Act), and ultimately impeach him.
  • Hiram Revels (1827–1901): First Black U.S. senator; elected from Mississippi in 1870 to fill the seat Jefferson Davis had vacated to become Confederate president. A minister and educator; symbolically occupied the seat of the Confederacy's president.
  • Blanche Bruce (1841–1898): Second Black U.S. senator; served a full six-year Senate term (1875–1881) representing Mississippi; chaired a Senate committee — the first Black senator to preside over the Senate. His post-Senate career included appointed federal positions.
  • Thaddeus Stevens's "forty acres" proposal: Stevens argued for land redistribution — dividing confiscated Confederate plantations into 40-acre plots for formerly enslaved people — as the economic foundation for Black freedom. The proposal was rejected by Congress, and without it, freed people had to negotiate labor contracts with the same planters who had enslaved them. The failure of land redistribution is often cited as the structural reason Reconstruction could not fully transform the South's racial economy.

Themes and Analytical Frames

Politics and Power (POL): The clash between Presidential and Radical Reconstruction is one of the most consequential constitutional confrontations in American history. It asked: who had the authority to define citizenship, and who would enforce it? The Reconstruction Acts' military district system, the Tenure of Office Act, and Johnson's impeachment all represent Congress using its constitutional powers to override executive obstruction of a democratic program. The confrontation also established important precedents about the limits of impeachment as a political weapon (the one-vote margin).

Society and Culture (SOC): Reconstruction state governments — biracial, elected by Black and white voters — enacted the South's first public school systems and built infrastructure. These were genuine social transformations: Reconstruction created communities of interracial democratic governance that the Redeemer governments would destroy through violence and fraud. The Dunning School's characterization of Reconstruction as misrule has been definitively refuted — the failure was not in Black political incapacity but in the organized violence and political abandonment that overthrew what the freedpeople built.

Continuity/Change: The Reconstruction Amendments represent the most significant constitutional change since the original Constitution. But the gap between their ratification and their effective enforcement reveals that constitutional change is not self-executing. The Fourteenth Amendment was ratified in 1868; it became the constitutional basis for Brown v. Board in 1954 — eighty-six years later. The text changed; enforcement required sustained political will that the 1870s did not provide.

AP Exam Approach

SAQ Practice

The constitutional achievements of Reconstruction — the Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments — have been described as the most consequential revision of the American constitutional order since the original Constitution.

Part A: Explain how the Fourteenth Amendment overturned the holding of Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857).

Part B: Explain ONE limitation of the Thirteenth Amendment that allowed Southern states to recreate aspects of enslaved labor after its ratification.

Part C: Explain why Congress enacted the Reconstruction Amendments rather than relying on ordinary legislation to protect Black civil rights.

DBQ/LEQ framing: When analyzing Reconstruction, the AP exam rewards awareness of the historiographical debate: the Dunning School (Reconstruction as corrupt misrule) vs. the revisionist interpretation (Foner, Du Bois). For AP purposes: Reconstruction was a genuine democratic revolution that created the South's first public schools, established biracial government, and amended the Constitution. It was not undone by its own failures but overthrown by organized violence and political abandonment. This distinction — failure vs. overthrow — is the analytical core.
