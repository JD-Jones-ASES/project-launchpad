AP US History — Topic 4.4: The Second Great Awakening and the Reform Impulse

Key Concepts

  • Perfectionism as the theological engine: The Second Great Awakening's most politically consequential innovation was the doctrine of perfectionism — the belief that human beings, aided by divine grace, could achieve moral perfection in this life. This stood in direct opposition to Calvinist predestination, which held that humans were inherently sinful and that salvation was entirely God's prior decision. Perfectionism was "politically explosive" because it authorized human agency in moral transformation: if individuals could be saved from sin, then society could be reformed from vice.

  • Charles Finney and the systematization of revival: Finney brought "forensic precision" to revival mechanics, rejecting Calvinist passivity for a systematic theology of human choice. His "new measures" — protracted meetings, the anxious bench, deliberate emotional intensity — made conversion a producible outcome rather than a mysterious gift. His Rochester revivals (1830–31) produced mass conversions and measurable declines in social disorder, demonstrating that religious enthusiasm and social reform were "not separate projects but aspects of the same transformation."

  • Temperance as the reform movement's training ground: The most broadly and rapidly successful reform movement of the era, the American Temperance Society enrolled over a million members by 1833. Its breadth rested on multiple support bases: evangelical ministers, factory owners seeking disciplined workers, and women finding a legitimate public moral platform. Temperance pioneered the mass-petition, tract-distribution, and women's auxiliary techniques that all subsequent reform movements adopted wholesale.

  • Education reform and its racial limits: Horace Mann's common school program aimed to create publicly funded, universally accessible elementary education as the "great equalizer." He doubled the Massachusetts school year, established the first normal schools, and modeled American schools on Prussian institutions. But Mann did not challenge racial exclusion: antebellum common schools were typically segregated, and Black children were excluded from or confined to inferior schools. Mann's "universal vision had a racial limit built in."

  • Utopian communities as perfectionism in practice: Some reformers concluded that incremental improvement of a corrupted society was insufficient and sought to build new societies from scratch. Brook Farm (1841–47) combined manual labor and intellectual life but was financially unviable. Oneida (1848–1881) was more radical and more durable, practicing complex marriage, communal child-rearing, and eventually eugenics — the perfectionist premise extended to deliberate biological engineering of virtue.

  • Dorothea Dix and the limits of women's reform politics: Dix secured new mental hospitals in more than 30 states without the right to vote or hold office, working through personal persuasion and documented moral argument. Her career illustrates both possibilities and limits: she achieved enormous institutional change by working within the domesticity ideology (directing female care toward the suffering), but her power rested on gender conventions she did not challenge rather than on formal political rights she had helped herself to.

  • The reform impulse's chain logic: The Second Great Awakening → perfectionism → reform movements, each drawing on the same theological premise and organizational techniques as the last. This chain eventually produced the most radical reform demand of the era (immediate abolitionism) and women's political consciousness — the discovery, through reform organizing, that women were excluded from the formal political system that governed what they sought to change.

Vocabulary Review

  • Second Great Awakening: Wave of Protestant revivalism (c. 1800–1840) whose core doctrine of perfectionism provided the theological engine for antebellum reform movements.

  • Perfectionism: The doctrine that human beings can achieve moral perfection through divine grace and sustained effort. Rejected Calvinist predestination; authorized the reform impulse by asserting that social conditions could be changed.

  • Charles Finney: The era's most influential Eastern evangelist; former lawyer; developed "new measures" for systematic revival; Rochester revivals (1830–31) became the model.

  • New measures: Finney's revival techniques: protracted meetings, anxious bench, deliberate emotional intensity through music and testimonials.

  • Cane Ridge Revival (1801): Kentucky camp meeting drawing 10,000–20,000 attendees; the western face of the Second Great Awakening; intense physical and emotional manifestations.

  • Temperance movement: Reform campaign targeting alcohol, evolving from moderation to total abstinence to prohibition; American Temperance Society (1826) enrolled 1+ million members by 1833; pioneered organizational techniques adopted by later reform movements.

  • Horace Mann: Massachusetts education reformer; secretary of the State Board of Education (1837); champion of the common school as "the great equalizer"; doubled the school year, established normal schools; did not challenge racial school segregation.

  • Normal schools: Teacher training colleges established under Mann's program; the first in the United States; raised teaching to a professional occupation.

  • Brook Farm (1841–47): Transcendentalist utopian community near Boston; combined intellectual and manual labor; financially unviable; George Ripley founder.

  • Oneida Community (1848–1881): Radical utopian community in upstate New York; John Humphrey Noyes founder; practiced complex marriage, communal child-rearing, and eugenics; survived 33 years; reorganized as silverware company.

  • Dorothea Dix: Massachusetts reformer who surveyed conditions in Massachusetts jails and almshouses (1841–43); her Memorial (1843) argued mental illness was a medical condition; secured state mental hospitals in 30+ states without the right to vote or hold office.

  • Voluntary associations: Non-governmental organizations formed by citizens around shared moral goals; the primary organizational vehicle of the antebellum reform movements; created a new kind of organized civil society.

  • Common school: Horace Mann's vision of publicly funded, universally accessible elementary education that would produce moral, informed citizens and equalize opportunity across class lines.

Key Figures and Events

Charles Grandison Finney (1792–1875): Converted from law to revivalism; developed systematic techniques for producing conversion; his Rochester revivals demonstrated that religious enthusiasm and social reform were the same project. Went on to become president of Oberlin College and continued as an influential abolitionist.

Cane Ridge Revival (1801): Massive Kentucky camp meeting; exemplified the western style of the Second Great Awakening — outdoor, mass gathering, convulsive physical responses. Signaled the movement's popular, democratic energy.

American Temperance Society (1826): The largest voluntary organization in the country by 1833 with 1+ million members. Pioneered the organizational techniques of the reform era. Its evolution from moderation to total abstinence prefigured the legal prohibition movement that eventually produced the 18th Amendment (1919).

Horace Mann's Prussian tour and 12 annual reports: Mann traveled to Prussia, studied its state school system, and returned convinced of the need for longer school years, professional teacher training, and standardized curriculum. His 12 annual reports to the Massachusetts Board became the blueprint for American educational reform for generations.

Brook Farm (1841–47): Founded by George Ripley; attracted Nathaniel Hawthorne; visited but declined by Emerson; financially collapsed after fire; illustrated the limits of utopian community as a reform strategy.

Oneida Community (1848–1881): Founded by John Humphrey Noyes; more radical, more durable; practiced complex marriage, communal child-rearing, eugenics; survived as a community for 33 years before reorganizing as Oneida Ltd. silverware company.

Dorothea Dix's Memorial (1843): Presented clinical evidence of brutal conditions for the mentally ill in Massachusetts jails and almshouses; argued for state-funded humane treatment. Secured mental hospitals in 30+ states by working through the ideology of female moral superiority and documented moral argument rather than formal political power.

Themes

Ideology and Social Structure: The Second Great Awakening illustrates how theological ideas (perfectionism's rejection of predestination) shaped social and political action. Finney's assertion of human agency in salvation was not politically neutral — it implicitly endorsed the idea that social conditions were changeable, not divinely ordained. This theological shift authorized reformers to challenge the status quo. The reform movements also reveal the social structure of reform itself: middle-class, Northern, heavily female, organized through voluntary associations — all products of the market revolution's structural changes.

Gender and Reform: Women were disproportionately represented in reform movements as members and often as leaders, yet they were excluded from formal political participation. The temperance movement gave respectable women a legitimate public moral platform. Dix achieved major institutional reform by working within domesticity ideology rather than challenging it. The abolitionist movement eventually forced a direct confrontation between women's reform activism and women's political exclusion — producing the women's rights movement. The reform era was central to the emergence of women's political consciousness.

The Racialized Limits of Reform: The antebellum reform movements consistently reproduced racial exclusion even as they challenged other forms of oppression. Common schools were segregated; reform organizations maintained racial hierarchies; and even abolitionists often distinguished between ending slavery and providing full equality for Black Americans. Horace Mann's "universal vision had a racial limit built in" — a phrase that applies broadly to the reform impulse as a whole.

The Chain from Revivalism to Rights: The Second Great Awakening created a chain of reform: perfectionism → temperance → education → prison/asylum → abolitionism → women's rights. Each link built on the previous one ideologically (the same perfectionist logic) and organizationally (the same mass-petition and tract-distribution techniques pioneered by temperance). The chain illustrates how religious movements produce political consequences that their originators did not necessarily intend.

Connections to Earlier and Later Periods

Earlier: The Second Great Awakening was a direct response to the rationalist religious tendencies of the Enlightenment era (Deism, Unitarianism). The market revolution's disruptions — displacement from self-sufficient farms, new urban poverty, anonymous social relations — created spiritual anxieties that revivalism addressed. The democratic spirit of the Jacksonian era's white male politics found its religious equivalent in perfectionism's assertion of individual human agency.

Later: Temperance eventually produced Prohibition (18th Amendment, 1919) and its repeal (21st Amendment, 1933). The prison reform movement Dix launched evolved into modern mental health policy debates. Most importantly, the abolitionist and women's rights movements that grew from the reform impulse required the Civil War and the 19th Amendment to achieve their central demands. The organizational forms pioneered in the antebellum reform era — mass petition, voluntary association, moral suasion campaign — became the template for Progressive Era reform (settlement houses, labor movement, suffrage) and civil rights organizing of the 20th century.

AP Exam Approach

Stimulus-based questions for this section often feature:

  • Excerpts from Finney's revivalist writings or contemporary descriptions of camp meetings
  • Documents from temperance organizations or the American Anti-Slavery Society
  • Dorothea Dix's Memorial to the Legislature of Massachusetts

Short-answer questions (SAQ) will often ask you to:

  • Explain how Finney's theology of perfectionism differed from Calvinist predestination and why this had political consequences
  • Explain Dorothea Dix's strategies for reform without the vote
  • Compare Brook Farm and Oneida as different expressions of the perfectionist impulse

Long essay questions (LEQ) for this period often ask you to evaluate the extent to which the antebellum reform movements challenged or reinforced existing social hierarchies — requiring you to discuss both what they challenged (drunkenness, ignorance, prison conditions, slavery) and what they did not challenge (racial segregation in common schools, women's political exclusion in most organizations, class hierarchies in reform leadership).

Key arguments to be ready to make:

  1. The Second Great Awakening's perfectionism was the ideological engine of the antebellum reform impulse — all the major reform movements drew from its theological premises.
  2. The reform impulse was both a theological and a social formation — made possible by the market revolution's creation of a salaried middle class with surplus resources for civic engagement.
  3. Women's political consciousness emerged through reform organizing: women who organized to change social conditions discovered they were excluded from the political system that governed those conditions.
  4. The antebellum reform movements had consistent racial limits: even "universal" reform visions like Mann's common school excluded Black Americans, reflecting the broader constraints of Northern reformism.
