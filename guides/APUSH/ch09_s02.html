AP US History — Topic 9.2: The End of the Cold War and the Unipolar Moment

Key Concepts

  • Why the Cold War ended — and the debate over causes: The Soviet collapse had multiple causes, and historians debate their relative weight. Internal Soviet dysfunction (the command economy's failure, the burden of Afghanistan, the unintended consequences of glasnost and perestroika) drove the collapse from within. American pressure — military buildup, SDI, Reagan Doctrine — added stress from outside. Which factor mattered more is genuinely contested. The most defensible position: neither alone was sufficient; the combination of internal structural failure and external pressure created conditions neither side fully controlled.

  • Glasnost and perestroika as unintentional catalysts: Gorbachev's reforms were designed to save the Soviet system, not destroy it. Glasnost allowed the expression of grievances that had been suppressed for decades; once expressed, they proved uncontainable. Perestroika's partial marketization disrupted the existing economy without yet delivering the efficiency gains that might have compensated. The reforms that were meant to modernize Soviet communism instead exposed its fundamental illegitimacy.

  • The fall of the Berlin Wall as an unscripted event: The Wall did not fall because of a planned government decision. The East German government announced — at a confused press conference — that travel restrictions were being lifted; border guards lacking orders stood aside; crowds gathered. The unplanned character of the Wall's fall is historically significant: it illustrates that the revolutions of 1989 were driven by popular will acting faster than governmental authority, not scripted from above.

  • The unipolar moment and its paradoxes: After 1991 the United States was the world's only superpower — unprecedented in modern history. But dominance did not equal direction. The decade's crises (Somalia, Bosnia, Kosovo, Gulf War) produced mixed results and intense debates about when, how, and under whose authority the United States should act. The decade's unresolved debates were rehearsals for the choices September 11 would force.

  • The Gulf War as the model and its limits: The 1991 Gulf War demonstrated American military power under optimal political conditions: UN authorization, genuine coalition, limited and achievable objective, defined exit. George H.W. Bush's decision not to advance on Baghdad and remove Saddam was deliberate — and, in retrospect, showed strategic restraint that his son's administration lacked twelve years later. The contrast between 1991 and 2003 is one of the most productive comparisons on the AP exam.

  • Clinton's economic foreign policy: Clinton's signature foreign-policy initiatives were trade agreements — NAFTA (1994) and WTO (1995). In the post-Cold War world, economic integration was itself a form of power projection. Both agreements generated fierce debates about their effects on American manufacturing workers that prefigured the political conflicts of the 2010s. NAFTA and WTO were not simply commercial agreements; they embedded the United States in multilateral economic frameworks with significant domestic consequences.

  • The interventions of the 1990s — mixed results: Somalia ended in withdrawal and emboldened adversaries. Bosnia and Kosovo demonstrated that air power could halt ethnic cleansing without ground combat, but that military success without durable political settlement merely paused rather than resolved conflicts. The decade's interventions were a laboratory for the limits of American power in ambiguous situations without clear strategic interest.

Key Figures and Events

Mikhail Gorbachev (1931–2022): The central figure of the Cold War's end. His decisions — glasnost, perestroika, non-intervention in Eastern Europe (1989), acceptance of German reunification — were of historic consequence. Whether he intended to preserve communism or understood he was ending it remains debated. Western audiences awarded him the Nobel Peace Prize; many Russians blamed him for economic collapse and national humiliation. The gap between those two assessments captures the historical complexity of his legacy.

George H.W. Bush (1989–93): A foreign-policy president who managed the Cold War's end with skill (Gulf War, German reunification) but struggled to articulate a domestic vision. His decision not to march on Baghdad in 1991 demonstrated strategic restraint. He lost the 1992 election to Bill Clinton largely on domestic economic grounds — "It's the economy, stupid" — illustrating that foreign-policy success does not guarantee electoral success.

Bill Clinton (1993–2001): Defined his foreign policy through economic integration (NAFTA, WTO) and reluctant humanitarian intervention (Somalia, Bosnia, Kosovo). His administration faced the hardest cases of the post-Cold War world — conflicts where no clear American strategic interest existed and where intervention produced mixed results. The Clinton years established the patterns of controversy about American intervention that the Bush administration would inherit.

The Velvet Revolution (Czechoslovakia, November 1989): The nearly bloodless transition from communist rule in Czechoslovakia, led by playwright Václav Havel. Named for its nonviolent character. Illustrates how Gorbachev's signal that Soviet forces would not intervene changed the political calculus for dissidents throughout Eastern Europe.

Romanian Revolution (December 1989): The exception to the pattern of peaceful revolutions. Dictator Nicolae Ceausescu was arrested and executed on Christmas Day 1989 after attempting to use security forces against demonstrators. Illustrates that the revolutions of 1989, while mostly peaceful, carried the possibility of violence.

The August 1991 Coup: Hardline Communist officials attempted to remove Gorbachev from power to halt his reforms. The coup collapsed within three days — Boris Yeltsin's defiance in front of the Russian parliament became the defining image. The coup's failure discredited the Communist Party and accelerated the independence declarations of Soviet republics. Its failure was as consequential as its launch.

Themes

World Relations (WOR): The Cold War's end restructured the international order more rapidly than any transition since 1945. The United States found that dominance was not control — that managing a unipolar world required constant coalition-building without the organizing threat that had made Cold War coalitions automatic. The Gulf War showed what was possible; Somalia, Bosnia, and Kosovo showed the limits.

Politics and Power (POL): The Cold War's end deprived both parties of their organizing foreign-policy assumption. Both had to reconstruct their identities — a process still in progress when September 11 intervened. The 1990s debates about intervention, multilateralism, and American purpose were not resolved; they were suspended by the new emergency.

Work, Exchange, and Technology (WXT): Clinton's economic foreign policy — NAFTA and WTO — illustrates how trade agreements function simultaneously as economic and foreign-policy instruments. The consequences for American manufacturing workers were profound and politically consequential, generating resentments that erupted in the 2010s.

Compare and Contrast

Gulf War (1991) vs. Iraq War (2003): This comparison appears frequently on the AP exam. The Gulf War had UN authorization, a genuine coalition, a limited and achievable objective, and a defined exit — it succeeded. The Iraq War had no UN authorization, a smaller coalition, a regime-change objective without a defined exit — it produced military success followed by catastrophic occupation. The contrast illustrates how political conditions shape military outcomes even when military power is overwhelming.

Gorbachev's Approach vs. Brezhnev Doctrine: The Brezhnev Doctrine (1968) held that the Soviet Union would intervene militarily to preserve communist governments in Eastern Europe — as it had in Hungary (1956) and Czechoslovakia (1968). Gorbachev's decision to abandon the doctrine — implicitly in 1989 when he did not intervene — was the pivotal change that made the revolutions of 1989 possible. The contrast between these two Soviet approaches is the single most important factor in explaining when the Eastern European revolutions succeeded.

AP Exam Approach

Long Essay: Questions about the Cold War's end typically ask students to evaluate the relative importance of internal Soviet dysfunction vs. American pressure. Key moves:

  • Acknowledge both factors with specific evidence
  • Argue that neither alone was sufficient
  • Use the INF Treaty to show how American pressure and Soviet willingness to negotiate interacted
  • Distinguish between what ended the Cold War (structural Soviet failure plus American pressure) and what determined its timing (Gorbachev's specific decisions)

Short Answer: When asked to explain the 'unipolar moment,' use the A/B/C structure:

  • A: The end of the Cold War created a period of unprecedented American dominance in which no rival power existed.
  • B: The United States was the world's largest economy with an unmatched military, and the USSR had dissolved into fifteen successor states.
  • C: Yet this dominance produced uncertainty rather than direction — the 1990s debates about Somalia, Bosnia, and Kosovo showed that power without strategic clarity does not guarantee coherent policy.

Primary Source HAPP Application: Images of Germans tearing down the Berlin Wall:

  • Historical Context: November 1989; Cold War at low-level equilibrium; Soviet military had not intervened in Eastern European revolutions; Gorbachev had signaled non-intervention.
  • Audience: International media — the images circled the globe within hours.
  • Purpose: The destruction was spontaneous; no central authority planned it.
  • Point of View: Ordinary citizens acting before governmental authorization — the images captured a genuinely unscripted historical moment.
