AP US History — Topic 3.4: Articles of Confederation and the Constitutional Convention

Key Concepts

  • The Articles as a principled but flawed design: The Articles of Confederation were not a failure of imagination — they were a deliberate response to the colonial experience of distant British authority that taxed without consent and governed without accountability. The architects built a government incapable of those abuses. The problem was that they also built a government incapable of governing.

  • The Confederation's specific structural failures: No taxation power (could only request funds from states, which ignored the requests); no commerce regulation (states erected tariff barriers against each other); no executive branch to enforce decisions; supermajority requirements for legislation; unanimous consent for amendments. These were not peripheral weaknesses but fundamental incapacities.

  • Shays' Rebellion as a catalyst: The 1786–87 farmer uprising in Massachusetts did more than demonstrate the Confederation's inability to suppress domestic resistance — it terrified property owners who feared that democratic majorities in state legislatures would enact inflationary monetary policies and debtor relief that would destroy their wealth. This fear directly accelerated the Philadelphia Convention.

  • The Constitution's three slavery compromises: The Three-Fifths Compromise (giving slaveholders extra representation), the Slave Trade Compromise (protecting the trade until 1808), and the Fugitive Slave Clause (requiring return of escaped enslaved people) embedded slavery's protection into the Constitution's structure. The same document that created a viable nation simultaneously protected an institution incompatible with the equal liberty it proclaimed.

  • Sovereignty transferred: The Constitution's central achievement was transferring sovereignty from states to "the People" — giving the federal government the ability to act directly on individual citizens rather than going through state intermediaries. The taxing power, commerce power, and supremacy clause gave the federal government tools the Confederation entirely lacked.

  • The Northwest Ordinance as the Confederation's lasting achievement: The ban on slavery in the Northwest Territory was the first federal restriction on slavery's geographic expansion and established the template for orderly westward expansion that lasted until the Civil War — the Confederation's most durable legacy.

Vocabulary Review

  • Articles of Confederation: First American national government (1777/1781–1789); created a league of sovereign states, not a national government; no taxation, commerce, or enforcement powers.
  • Salutary Neglect (comparative): The British policy whose end triggered the Revolution; the Articles were designed as a permanent guarantee against the return of centralized authority that salutary neglect had temporarily allowed colonists to avoid.
  • Northwest Ordinance (1787): Confederation Congress act organizing the territory north of the Ohio River into future states; banned slavery from the Northwest Territory — first federal antislavery restriction.
  • Shays' Rebellion (1786–87): Armed uprising of debt-burdened Massachusetts farmers; demonstrated Confederation's inability to maintain domestic order; accelerated the Philadelphia Convention.
  • Virginia Plan: Madison's proposal at the Philadelphia Convention for a genuinely national government with independent taxing power, population-based bicameral legislature, executive, and judiciary.
  • Great Compromise (Connecticut Compromise): Roger Sherman's proposal creating a bicameral Congress — House apportioned by population, Senate with two seats per state; resolved the large-state vs. small-state conflict.
  • Three-Fifths Compromise: Counted each enslaved person as three-fifths of a free person for congressional apportionment and direct taxation; gave Southern states extra representation for seventy years.
  • Slave Trade Compromise: Prohibited Congress from banning the importation of enslaved people before 1808; a twenty-year protection of the slave trade.
  • Necessary and Proper Clause: Authorized Congress to make all laws "necessary and proper" for executing its enumerated powers; basis for implied powers doctrine; Anti-Federalists called it a blank check.
  • Supremacy Clause: Established federal law as the supreme law of the land, binding on state judges; a fundamental departure from the Articles' structure.
  • Enumerated Powers: Congress's specifically listed powers in Article I, Section 8; limited federal authority to what was explicitly granted, plus implied powers under necessary and proper.
  • Sovereignty: The ultimate source of political authority; under Articles = states; under Constitution = "the People" (acting through the federal government which could reach individual citizens directly).

Key Figures and Events

  • James Madison (1751–1836): The Convention's most important intellectual force; primary drafter of the Virginia Plan; co-author of the Federalist Papers; his vision of faction-competing republics and separated powers defined the Constitution's theoretical framework. Later called the "Father of the Constitution" — though he modestly declined the title.

  • Roger Sherman (1721–1793): Connecticut delegate who proposed the Great Compromise (bicameral Congress with population-based House and equal-state Senate); his compromise rescued the Convention from deadlock and is arguably as structurally important as any provision in the Constitution.

  • George Washington (presiding): Presided over the Philadelphia Convention; his participation legitimized the Convention in the eyes of the public and his signature on the final document made ratification significantly more likely. Washington's willingness to serve as president under the new document was widely understood as a condition for the Constitution's success.

  • Benjamin Franklin (1706–1790): The Convention's elder statesman (age 81 in 1787); his final speech urging delegates to sign despite their individual objections — expressing that he was astonished to find the document so nearly perfect — helped achieve the near-unanimous signature that gave the Constitution its public credibility.

  • Daniel Shays: Former Continental Army captain whose debt-protest movement (1786–87) became the symbol of governmental weakness under the Articles; his rebellion was suppressed by Massachusetts state militia, but its aftershocks reshaped the political will for constitutional reform.

Timeline:

  • 1777: Articles of Confederation adopted by Continental Congress
  • 1781: Articles ratified (Maryland's final ratification)
  • 1785–86: States' commercial conflicts intensify; Annapolis Convention calls for Philadelphia meeting
  • 1786–87: Shays' Rebellion
  • 1787: Northwest Ordinance (July); Philadelphia Convention (May–September)
  • 1787–88: Ratification debates; Constitution ratified by required 9 states (June 1788)

Themes

Politics and Power (POL): The transition from Articles to Constitution represents one of the most significant realignments of political authority in American history. The Articles deliberately dispersed power to the states; the Constitution deliberately concentrated enough federal authority to govern while distributing it across three branches to prevent tyranny. The Convention's debates over representation, taxation, slavery, and executive power reveal the range of political interests that had to be accommodated in a workable constitutional design.

Work, Exchange, and Technology (WXT): The Articles' lack of a commerce power allowed states to erect tariff barriers against each other, threatening the integrated national economy that merchants and manufacturers needed. The Constitution's commerce clause — giving Congress power to regulate interstate commerce — addressed this directly. Hamilton's later financial program built on the Constitution's taxing and commerce powers to construct an economic architecture for national development.

Social Structures (SOC): The Constitution's three slavery compromises reveal that property in human beings was as politically embedded in the founding as property in land or goods. The Three-Fifths Compromise was not peripheral — it shaped Southern congressional representation for seventy years, amplifying slaveholder political power in every Congress, every presidential election (through the Electoral College), and every Supreme Court appointment until the Civil War.

American National Identity (NAT): The Constitution's preamble — "We the People of the United States" — proclaimed a national identity that had not fully existed before. But "the People" was a contested category: the same document that created national political identity protected slavery and made no provision for indigenous peoples' rights or sovereignty.

Connections to Earlier/Later Periods

Earlier: Shays' Rebellion echoed earlier debt-resistance movements and the agrarian populism that had periodically challenged elite economic power in the colonial period. The constitutional debates about representation repeated (in inverted form) the debates about parliamentary taxation during the imperial crisis: how to tax legitimately, who counted for representation, and what limits constrained governmental power.

Later: The necessary and proper clause became the basis for implied powers doctrine — first exercised in Hamilton's Bank of the United States (1791), then established as constitutional precedent in McCulloch v. Maryland (1819). The Three-Fifths Compromise shaped American politics until the Civil War, when the Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments finally abolished slavery, defined citizenship, and guaranteed voting rights. The Northwest Ordinance's anti-expansion model was revived in the Missouri Compromise (1820) and contested in the Kansas-Nebraska Act (1854).

AP Exam Approach

Primary source analysis — Articles of Confederation: When analyzing the Articles' text, identify what the specific article reveals about the founders' design philosophy (fear of central power, state sovereignty as default) and then explain why that philosophy produced structural problems in practice. The gap between the Articles' design intent and its functional outcomes is the key analytical insight.

Common SAQ formats:

  • Explain how Shays' Rebellion demonstrated the Articles' weaknesses and why it accelerated the Philadelphia Convention.
  • Describe the Three-Fifths Compromise and explain TWO long-term political consequences.
  • Using the Articles' text, identify ONE specific power that Confederation Congress lacked that proved fatal to its effectiveness.

Essential comparison: Articles vs. Constitution is a perennial AP comparison question. Organize your comparison around the three key structural differences: sovereignty (states vs. People), powers (no taxation/commerce vs. enumerated + necessary and proper), and enforcement (no executive vs. president + courts + supremacy clause).

Anti-triumphalist framing: The Constitutional Convention was not a triumph of idealism over self-interest — it was a complex set of compromises that protected slavery, over-represented slaveholders, and excluded women, non-property-owners, and indigenous peoples from "We the People." Strong AP essays acknowledge both the constitutional achievement (creating a viable federal government) and its compromises with inequality.
