AP US History — Topic 3.2: Common Sense and Independence

Key Concepts

  • The path from protest to independence was not inevitable: As late as mid-1775, most Continental Congress delegates were fighting for restored rights within the British Empire, not independence. The transition required the closing of reconciliation options, a radical ideological reframing, and a philosophical justification — all of which came together between July 1775 and July 1776.

  • Common Sense as an ideological revolution: Previous colonial arguments targeted Parliament's specific acts; Paine attacked the institution of monarchy and hereditary rule itself. By making the king — not Parliament — the problem, Paine aligned with the constitutional logic the Declaration would require: since colonial allegiance to Britain ran through the Crown, independence demanded repudiating the king.

  • The Continental Congress's structural weakness: The Congress raised and directed the Continental Army, issued currency, and negotiated alliances — but had no formal taxation power, depending on voluntary state contributions. This structural weakness (no taxing authority) would prove fatal to the Confederation's finances and drive the Constitutional Convention.

  • The Declaration's dual character: The Declaration was simultaneously a legal act (justifying independence to the world) and a philosophical manifesto (advancing universal claims about natural rights and popular sovereignty that went far beyond the immediate colonial dispute). Its universal language — "all men are created equal" — created claims that enslaved people, women, and later civil rights advocates would use to demand inclusion in the republic.

  • The Declaration's foundational contradiction: The most prominent signatories of a document proclaiming all men equal were slaveholders. Congress deleted Jefferson's condemnation of the slave trade. This gap between the Declaration's rhetoric and American reality became the central tension of the next two centuries of American history.

  • Print culture and democratic argument: Paine's deliberate use of plain English — reaching an estimated 100,000–500,000 readers in a population of 2.5 million — democratized the independence argument. Common Sense demonstrated that colonial print culture could shape mass political opinion, anticipating the role of media in American democracy.

Vocabulary Review

  • Second Continental Congress: The de facto governing body of the revolutionary United States (1775–1781), which raised the Continental Army, appointed Washington, issued currency, negotiated the French alliance, and adopted both the Declaration and the Articles of Confederation.
  • Olive Branch Petition (1775): Congress's final appeal for reconciliation with George III; rejected without being read and answered with a declaration of colonial rebellion.
  • Hessian Mercenaries: German soldiers hired by George III to fight the colonists; their use confirmed for many colonists that the king was an active aggressor, not a misguided monarch.
  • Common Sense (1776): Thomas Paine's pamphlet that shifted the debate from parliamentary rights to the legitimacy of monarchy itself; argued reconciliation was "literally childish."
  • Popular Sovereignty: The principle that government derives legitimate authority from the consent of the governed; central to both Common Sense and the Declaration.
  • Unalienable Rights: Rights held to be inherent to all persons and inalienable — "life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness" in the Declaration's formulation, adapting Locke's "life, liberty, and property."
  • Social Contract: Locke's theory that governments are created by consent to protect natural rights; when they become destructive of those ends, the people may alter or abolish them; directly incorporated into the Declaration's preamble.
  • Committee of Five: The congressional committee (including Adams, Franklin, Jefferson) tasked with drafting the Declaration of Independence; Jefferson did most of the writing.
  • Continentals: Paper currency issued by the Continental Congress that depreciated rapidly because Congress lacked taxation power; "not worth a Continental" became synonymous with worthlessness.
  • Natural Law: The philosophical tradition (drawing on Locke and others) holding that certain rights exist independent of human law — the Declaration grounds American rights claims in natural law rather than English statute.
  • HAPP Analysis: Historical context, Audience, Purpose, Point of View — the AP framework for analyzing primary sources like Common Sense and the Declaration.

Key Figures and Events

  • Thomas Paine (1737–1809): English-born radical immigrant who arrived in America in 1774 with no inherited loyalty to the Crown. His outsider perspective allowed him to attack monarchy with a directness that colonial-born writers hesitated to employ. Common Sense's mass readership transformed colonial opinion.

  • George Washington: Appointed commander of the Continental Army by the Second Continental Congress in June 1775; directed the war effort for eight years. His appointment was partly political — a Virginian commanding a New England-based army broadened the coalition.

  • Thomas Jefferson (1743–1826): Virginia planter-lawyer who drafted the Declaration in seventeen days, drawing on Locke, colonial petition rhetoric, and his own genius. The contradictions of his life — slaveholder who wrote "all men are created equal" — mirror the contradictions of the founding era.

  • John Adams (1735–1826): Massachusetts lawyer, key member of the Committee of Five, and fierce advocate for independence in Congress. His account of the Declaration as "an expression of the American mind" captures its representative rather than purely Jeffersonian character.

  • Richard Henry Lee: Virginia delegate who introduced the independence resolution on June 7, 1776 — the legal act that preceded the Declaration's philosophical justification.

  • Benjamin Franklin (1706–1790): Elder statesman and Committee of Five member; his presence lent the Declaration credibility with European audiences, particularly in France where he was already celebrated.

Timeline of Transition to Independence:

  • April 19, 1775: Lexington and Concord — armed conflict begins
  • May 1775: Second Continental Congress convenes
  • July 1775: Olive Branch Petition sent to George III
  • August 1775: George III declares colonies in rebellion; hires Hessians
  • January 1776: Common Sense published
  • June 7, 1776: Lee's independence resolution introduced
  • July 4, 1776: Declaration of Independence adopted

Themes

Ideas, Beliefs, and Culture (CUL): Common Sense demonstrates how print culture could transform political opinion at mass scale. Paine's deliberate use of plain English rather than learned legal prose democratized the independence argument, making it accessible to artisans and farmers alongside lawyers and merchants. The pamphlet's extraordinary circulation (100,000–500,000 copies) established the model of political argument by mass publication that would characterize American democracy.

Politics and Power (POL): The Declaration of Independence established popular sovereignty as the only legitimate foundation of republican government — a radical claim by the standards of 1776, when hereditary monarchy governed virtually every major state in the world. The Declaration's philosophical framework also defined the problem of the new republic: how to build a government capable of securing natural rights while preventing tyranny.

American National Identity (NAT): The Declaration simultaneously created and complicated American national identity. Its universal language proclaimed principles that claimed to speak for all of humanity — yet its signatories excluded enslaved people, women, and indigenous nations from their vision of the republic. The tension between the Declaration's universalism and its exclusions defined the ongoing struggle over who would be included in "We the People."

Migration and Settlement (MIG) / Social Structures (SOC): Paine's biography — an English immigrant whose outsider perspective gave him freedom to attack the Crown — illustrates how immigration could infuse American political culture with perspectives unavailable to colonial-born writers. The Continental Congress's diversity (twelve colonies, varied economic interests, from New England merchants to Southern planters) shows how coalition-building required compromises like the deletion of the slave trade condemnation.

Connections to Earlier/Later Periods

Earlier: The First Continental Congress (1774) established the institutional framework — intercolonial coordination, boycott organization — that the Second Continental Congress inherited. The constitutional arguments developed during the imperial crisis (1765–74) provided the grievances listed in the Declaration's indictment, but Paine and Jefferson went beyond those arguments to establish a philosophical foundation for independence.

Later: The Declaration's universal language directly inspired future rights movements. Abolitionists from Frederick Douglass to Abraham Lincoln argued from the Declaration's premises. The Seneca Falls Declaration of Sentiments (1848) deliberately echoed the Declaration's structure: "We hold these truths to be self-evident: that all men and women are created equal." Civil Rights leaders, including Martin Luther King Jr., invoked the Declaration as an unfulfilled promise. The Declaration's global impact was immediate: it inspired the French Revolution (1789), the Haitian Revolution (1791–1804), and Latin American independence movements.

AP Exam Approach

HAPP Analysis for Common Sense:

  • Historical context: Written six months after Lexington and Concord, just before Congress took up independence; George III had declared the colonies in rebellion (August 1775) and hired Hessian mercenaries.
  • Audience: Ordinary colonists — Paine deliberately wrote in plain English, avoiding legal rhetoric, to reach artisans, farmers, and the general literate public.
  • Purpose: To recast reconciliation as childishness and independence as adult maturity; to shift the debate from parliamentary rights to the legitimacy of monarchy itself.
  • Point of View: An English-born immigrant with no inherited loyalty to the Crown — able to attack monarchy with directness unavailable to colonial-born writers.

HAPP Analysis for the Declaration of Independence:

  • Historical context: Adopted after George III's rejection of the Olive Branch Petition; written amid an ongoing war that had begun over a year earlier.
  • Audience: Multiple — the American public, foreign powers (especially France), and posterity; Adams called it "an expression of the American mind."
  • Purpose: To justify independence to the world by demonstrating that the colonists were not rebels but a people exercising a universal natural right to alter destructive government.
  • Point of View: Jefferson, a Virginia slaveholder writing "all men are created equal" — the contradiction between his stated principles and his personal life is as historically significant as the document itself.

Common DBQ/SAQ Prompts:

  • Explain how Paine's Common Sense was more effective than earlier colonial constitutional arguments in shifting opinion toward independence.
  • Identify TWO Enlightenment ideas in the Declaration of Independence and explain how each justified independence.
  • Explain the contradiction in the Declaration and describe ONE way later Americans used the Declaration's language to challenge that contradiction.

Common Errors:

  • Don't say the Declaration immediately granted equality to all Americans — it proclaimed a principle that was immediately contradicted by the existence of slavery.
  • Don't treat Paine's argument as simply a more passionate version of earlier constitutional arguments — his strategic innovation was changing the target from Parliament to monarchy itself.
  • Don't forget that the Continental Congress's lack of taxation power was a structural weakness that created problems throughout the war and led directly to the Constitutional Convention.
