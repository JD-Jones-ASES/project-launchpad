AP US History — Topic 6.4: New Immigration and Urbanization

Key Concepts

  • The racial ideology embedded in the old/new immigrant distinction: The shift from northwestern to southern and eastern European immigration after 1880 was not experienced simply as a demographic change but as a racial threat by nativists. Their arguments — that new immigrants were racially inferior, culturally contaminated by Catholicism or Judaism, and incapable of democratic self-government — were pseudoscientific and morally bankrupt, but politically effective, eventually producing the National Origins Act of 1924. Understanding this distinction requires recognizing that "race" in the Gilded Age was applied to European ethnic groups as well as to non-white peoples, and that the Chinese Exclusion Act (1882) represented the most categorical expression of the racial hierarchy embedded in American immigration law.

  • Angel Island as institutional racism made concrete: While Ellis Island processed European immigrants under a general presumption of eventual admissibility, Angel Island was explicitly designed to discourage entry — prolonged detention (weeks to months), elaborate "paper son" examinations, physically inferior conditions. The difference was not administrative efficiency but legal and racial intent: the Chinese Exclusion Act's racial exclusion was built into the institutional design of Angel Island. The poems carved into the barracks walls are the immigrants' direct testimony against this system.

  • Immigrant agency vs. middle-class framing: The immigrant working class were not passive victims of industrial capitalism. They built churches, mutual aid societies, synagogues, union halls, foreign-language presses, and cooperative economic networks that sustained community life under extraordinary pressure. Both Riis's journalism and the settlement house movement, despite their genuine contributions, tended to cast immigrants as objects of reform rather than agents — a paternalism that the actual density of immigrant institution-building directly refutes.

  • The political machine as a dual institution: Tammany Hall and its counterparts were simultaneously vehicles of real and extensive corruption (Boss Tweed's $30–200 million; systematic contract graft) and the immigrant working class's most reliable intermediary with state power. In an era of minimal government social provision, machines delivered what the welfare state would not — jobs, legal help, food, coal. Progressive reformers who attacked machines as purely corrupt without understanding what they replaced were missing the structural reality of Gilded Age governance.

  • The ethnic enclave and the limits of the melting pot: The 25 million immigrants of 1880–1920 did not simply melt into undifferentiated American identity. They built parallel communities — ethnic enclaves, mutual aid societies, foreign-language press, ethnic churches — that maintained cultural distinctiveness across generations while enabling economic participation. Partial assimilation, persistent ethnic identity, and structural racism that slowed assimilation for groups racialized as non-white describe what actually happened, not the melting pot ideal.

  • The racial hierarchy of the industrial labor market: The Gilded Age industrial economy was organized as a racial and ethnic hierarchy — native-born white Protestants in management; old immigrants in semi-skilled trades; new immigrants in the most dangerous and lowest-paid work; Chinese workers in legally constrained niches. This stratification was not simply inherited prejudice; it was actively maintained by employers using ethnic divisions to prevent cross-racial working-class solidarity.

Vocabulary Review

  • New Immigrants: Post-1880 arrivals, predominantly from southern and eastern Europe (Italy, Poland, Russia, Austria-Hungary, Greece), as well as Asia and Mexico. Often Catholic, Eastern Orthodox, or Jewish; often without capital or professional skills; concentrated in urban industrial neighborhoods.

  • Nativism: Ideology privileging white, Protestant, native-born interests over immigrants; combined economic anxiety, racial pseudoscience, and religious antagonism. Produced Chinese Exclusion Act (1882) and National Origins Act (1924).

  • Chinese Exclusion Act (1882): Only American law explicitly barring immigration by nationality and race; ended most legal Chinese immigration; reflected and reinforced the racial hierarchy of American immigration policy.

  • Ellis Island: Gateway for ~12 million European immigrants, 1892–1954. Class differential: first/second class processed on ship; steerage examined and detained; ~2% deported.

  • Angel Island: San Francisco Bay entry point for Chinese and Asian immigrants; deliberately designed to discourage entry; weeks/months detention; "paper son" examinations; poems carved in barrack walls.

  • Paper Son System: Strategy allowing some Chinese immigrants to claim exempt status by falsely claiming U.S.-born parentage; Angel Island's elaborate interrogations were designed to detect such claims.

  • Tenement: Multi-story urban housing maximizing occupant density at minimum cost; dumbbell design complied technically with 1879 air-shaft regulation while providing inadequate ventilation, sanitation, and light. Dominant housing for the Gilded Age urban poor.

  • Ethnic Enclave: Immigrant neighborhood cluster providing social capital: job information, familiar language and institutions, kin networks, co-ethnic credit networks. Examples: Little Italy, Jewish Lower East Side, Chinatown.

  • Mutual Aid Society: Immigrant self-help organization providing sick benefits, death insurance, small loans, and social community. The informal welfare state in the absence of government social provision. Italian società di mutuo soccorso, Jewish landsmanshaftn.

  • Padrone System: Ethnic labor brokerage in which an Italian-American broker recruited and supervised immigrant workers for employers, extracting commissions from both sides.

  • Political Machine: Urban political organization maintaining power through services-for-votes exchange. Ward captains delivered jobs, legal help, food, coal, and entertainment in exchange for reliable voter support.

  • Tammany Hall: New York's Democratic machine; Boss Tweed stole $30–200 million; Richard Croker continued the tradition; simultaneously corrupt and the immigrant working class's most reliable political intermediary.

  • Settlement House: Institution in which educated middle-class reformers lived in poor urban neighborhoods to provide services and study conditions. Hull House (Addams, 1889) is the defining example; contributed important social research alongside genuine but paternalistic care.

  • Melting Pot: Metaphor for cultural fusion into undifferentiated American identity; describes a desired process, not an observed one; partial assimilation, persistent ethnic identity, and structural racism were the actual outcomes.

  • National Origins Act (1924): Immigration restriction law encoding nativist racial ideology; quotas designed to preserve existing ethnic composition; effectively ended mass immigration from southern and eastern Europe.

Key Figures and Events

  • Jacob Riis (1849–1914): Danish-born journalist; How the Other Half Lives (1890) shocked middle-class readers with flash-photography documentation of tenement conditions; contributed to reform legislation; framing problem: immigrants as objects rather than agents.

  • Jane Addams (1860–1935): Co-founded Hull House (1889); pioneered urban social work and sociological research; Hull-House Maps and Papers (1895); Nobel Peace Prize 1931. Represents the settlement house movement's most important figures.

  • Boss William Tweed: Tammany Hall leader; stole $30–200 million; brought down by Thomas Nast's Harper's Weekly cartoons (1869–71); the paradigmatic machine-corruption figure.

  • Israel Zangwill: Author of The Melting Pot (1908 play); popularized the metaphor that described assimilation as a desired process — not the complex, partial reality that historians observe.

  • Madison Grant: Author of The Passing of the Great Race (1916); pseudoscientific racial nativism at its most explicit; influenced the National Origins Act (1924).

  • Ellis Island (opened 1892): 12 million European immigrants processed; symbol of American immigration, hope, and bureaucratic power.

  • Angel Island (1910–1940): Chinese and Asian immigration processing; designed to discourage entry; poems in the barrack walls; symbol of exclusionary racial immigration policy.

Themes

Migration (MIG): The 1880–1920 immigration wave was the most dramatic demographic event in American history — 25 million people, predominantly from southern and eastern Europe, transforming the ethnic and religious composition of the country. The wave's source region shift (from northwestern to southern/eastern Europe) produced the nativist reaction that eventually closed the immigration door. Simultaneously, the Chinese Exclusion Act demonstrated that the racial hierarchy of American immigration policy was not limited to European categories — Asian immigration was categorically excluded on explicitly racial grounds.

Social Stratification (SOC): The new immigration was embedded in, and reinforced, the racial hierarchy of Gilded Age industrial capitalism. New immigrants concentrated in the most dangerous and lowest-paid industrial work; their ethnic enclaves and mutual aid societies provided resilience against that structural position; political machines provided political representation and social services in the absence of government provision. The story of immigration is simultaneously a story about ethnic community-building and about structural economic subordination.

Politics and Power (POL): Urban machine politics, nativism, and settlement house reform were all responses to the political challenge posed by 25 million new residents. Machines traded services for votes; reformers advocated professionalized government and scientific social administration; nativists pushed for exclusion. The eventual victory of the restriction position (National Origins Act 1924) reflects the political power of the coalition linking nativism to Progressive Era bureaucratic reform — both opposed the machine and both were willing to limit immigration.

Connections

Connection to Section 6.3 (Labor): The racial and ethnic stratification of the industrial labor market directly structured the labor movement's failures. Employers used ethnic divisions and immigrant strikebreakers to prevent cross-racial solidarity. The AFL's exclusion of new immigrants reflected both nativist ideology within the labor movement and a strategic calculation about leverage — skilled workers' labor was hard to replace; unskilled immigrant labor was not. The labor movement's weakness and the immigration wave are structurally linked.

Connection to Section 6.6 (Gilded Age Politics): Political machines were a response to the same minimal-government ideology that governed tariff politics and civil service reform. The machine filled the social welfare vacuum that Gilded Age laissez-faire created. Progressive reformers' attack on machines was simultaneously a critique of corruption and an attempt to replace ethnic/working-class political power with professional/middle-class administration.

Long-term Connection — Progressive Era and New Deal: The settlement house movement's urban social research fed directly into Progressive Era labor legislation (child labor laws, maximum hour laws, factory inspection). The New Deal's welfare state permanently altered the landscape that the mutual aid society and political machine had occupied, providing government social insurance as a universal right rather than a machine favor. The 1924 National Origins Act's restrictions remained in place until the Immigration and Nationality Act of 1965.

AP Exam Approach

Comparison prompt: Compare the experience of European immigrants processed at Ellis Island with the experience of Chinese immigrants processed at Angel Island. What does this comparison reveal about the racial hierarchy embedded in American immigration policy? Key points: Ellis Island — general presumption of admissibility, class-differential processing, ~2% deported. Angel Island — deliberately designed to discourage entry, weeks/months detention, elaborate "paper son" exams. The difference reflects the Chinese Exclusion Act's explicit racial exclusion translated into institutional design.

Short Answer Question (SAQ): Part A: Explain ONE social function that ethnic enclaves served for new immigrants in Gilded Age American cities. Part B: Explain ONE way in which political machines like Tammany Hall served the immigrant working class, and ONE limitation of this service. Part C: Explain ONE way in which Gilded Age nativism was embedded in federal law and ONE way it was embedded in immigration processing institutions.

Long Essay Question (LEQ): Evaluate the extent to which new immigrants to the United States between 1880 and 1920 experienced social mobility or structural subordination. Build the argument around: (1) structural economic subordination in the labor market; (2) self-organized community resilience (mutual aid, enclaves); (3) racial hierarchy embedded in law (Exclusion Act, Angel Island, eventual restriction); (4) partial assimilation patterns by the second generation. Avoid both the triumphalist "rags to riches" narrative and the purely victimizing narrative — acknowledge both subordination and agency.

CCOT: Immigration to the United States, 1820–1965: Three major periods — old immigration (northwestern European, 1820–1880); new immigration (southern/eastern European + Chinese exclusion, 1880–1924); restricted immigration (National Origins Act regime, 1924–1965); Hart-Celler Act (1965) abolished national origins quotas, reopening mass immigration from Latin America and Asia.
