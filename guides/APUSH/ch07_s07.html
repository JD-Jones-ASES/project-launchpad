AP US History — Topic 7.7: WWII at Home: Internment, Bracero, Women in Industry, Double V

Key Concepts

  • The central contradiction: The United States fought against fascism and racial hierarchy abroad while practicing racial hierarchy at home — in segregated armed forces, in Japanese American internment, in the exclusion of Black workers from full New Deal benefits, and in the temporary nature of women's wartime industrial gains. Understanding this contradiction is not a footnote to the "greatest generation" narrative; it is the narrative's central tension.

  • Japanese American internment as constitutional failure: Executive Order 9066 resulted in the imprisonment of 120,000 people based solely on racial ancestry, despite FBI and naval intelligence reports of no organized security threat. Korematsu v. United States (1944) upheld this racial policy; Justice Murphy's dissent correctly identified it as "a legalization of racism." The Civil Liberties Act (1988) apologized; Trump v. Hawaii (2018) explicitly overruled Korematsu — 74 years too late for the internees.

  • The Bracero Program — labor under duress: The same labor shortage that the internment helped create was addressed through the Bracero Program, which imported Mexican agricultural workers under contracts that theoretically guaranteed protections but in practice were unenforced. Workers' deportability gave employers structural leverage to maintain substandard conditions, establishing a pattern of guest-worker exploitation that persisted long after the program ended.

  • Women's wartime industrial employment — temporary by design: Women's proportion in the paid workforce rose from 27% to 37%, with real skills, higher wages, and factory presence. The transformation proved largely reversible: the same government-media apparatus that recruited women then ran a postwar campaign to displace them. The gap between wartime experience and postwar ideology became a structural tension that produced the second wave of feminism.

  • The Double V Campaign and African American military service: The Pittsburgh Courier's Double V slogan — victory over fascism abroad and victory over racism at home — was the clearest articulation of the WWII paradox. The Tuskegee Airmen and 761st Tank Battalion systematically disproved the military's racial ideology through their combat records. Their service strengthened the postwar desegregation case that Truman's Executive Order 9981 (1948) partly fulfilled.

  • The civil rights movement's WWII roots: Understanding the postwar civil rights movement requires understanding the WWII home front. The Double V campaign, the military records of Black units, the returning veterans' heightened expectations and deepened grievances, and A. Philip Randolph's strategic use of military leverage all connect WWII directly to the Brown v. Board of Education era.

Vocabulary Review

  • Executive Order 9066 (February 19, 1942): FDR's order authorizing military exclusion of persons from designated areas; applied specifically to Japanese Americans on the West Coast. Based on racial ancestry, not individual evidence of disloyalty.

  • Nisei: Second-generation Japanese Americans — U.S.-born citizens; 62% of those interned. The 442nd Regimental Combat Team was composed entirely of Nisei soldiers.

  • Korematsu v. United States (1944): Supreme Court decision upholding EO 9066 exclusion orders; Justice Black majority; Justice Murphy dissent ('legalization of racism'). Explicitly overruled by Trump v. Hawaii (2018).

  • Civil Liberties Act (1988): Congressional apology for Japanese American internment; $20,000 reparations to each surviving internee.

  • Bracero Program (1942–64): Bilateral U.S.-Mexico labor agreement bringing ~4.6 million agricultural workers to the United States under temporary contracts. Contracts specified protections; enforcement was minimal; deportability gave employers leverage over workers.

  • Rosie the Riveter: J. Howard Miller's iconic 'We Can Do It!' propaganda poster; symbol of women's wartime industrial labor. Women's workforce proportion: 27% (1940) → 37% (1945).

  • Double V Campaign: Pittsburgh Courier's February 1942 slogan: victory over fascism abroad AND victory over racism at home. The clearest contemporary articulation of the WWII home-front contradiction.

  • Tuskegee Airmen (332nd Fighter Group): Black pilots who flew 15,000+ sorties; credited with never losing a bomber to enemy fighters on escort duty. Systematically disproved military's racial incapacity ideology.

  • 761st Tank Battalion: 'Black Panthers'; Black armored unit that fought across France, Belgium, and Germany. Requested by Patton. Military record refuted segregation's racial ideology.

  • Executive Order 9981 (1948): Truman's order desegregating the armed forces; taken partly in response to A. Philip Randolph's threatened draft resistance campaign.

  • A. Philip Randolph: Labor and civil rights leader whose threatened 1941 March on Washington produced FDR's EO 8802 (defense industry anti-discrimination); whose 1948 draft resistance threat produced Truman's EO 9981.

  • 442nd Regimental Combat Team: Most decorated unit of its size in Army history; composed entirely of Nisei soldiers; families interned while they served.

Key Figures and Events

  • Franklin D. Roosevelt: Signed EO 9066; also the wartime president who oversaw the military service of the Tuskegee Airmen, 761st, and 442nd. His signature on the internment order stands alongside his WWII leadership as simultaneously the era's greatest democratic achievement and its most significant constitutional failure.

  • General John DeWitt: Commander of the Western Defense Command whose internal report — 'the racial strains are undiluted' — revealed the actual reasoning for internment. His report was the smoking gun that revealed military necessity as racial pretext.

  • Justice Frank Murphy (1890–1949): Supreme Court Justice who dissented in Korematsu, calling the decision 'a legalization of racism.' His dissent proved historically correct; Trump v. Hawaii (2018) adopted its logic.

  • Justice Hugo Black (1886–1971): Author of the Korematsu majority; wrote 'pressing public necessity may sometimes justify the existence of such restrictions.' His decision is now explicitly overruled. Black was also the author of the incorporation doctrine that expanded civil liberties protections — his Korematsu opinion stands as the most contradicted decision of his career.

  • Tuskegee Airmen: Trained at Tuskegee, Alabama; flew in European and Mediterranean theaters; escort record was unmatched. Their collective achievement was an argument, not just a military record.

  • 761st Tank Battalion: 'Black Panthers'; trained at Camp Hood; fought from November 1944 through Germany's defeat. General Patton's request for the unit, and their subsequent record, were essential to the postwar desegregation case.

  • A. Philip Randolph: Most important civil rights and labor leader of the WWII era. His strategic use of threatened direct action — March on Washington (1941), draft resistance (1948) — illustrates how political leverage, not only moral argument, moved the federal government on civil rights.

  • Florence Owens Thompson: The subject of Dorothea Lange's Migrant Mother (1936); a California farmworker. Her image connects the Depression's rural poverty to the wartime labor demands addressed by the Bracero Program — a thread of agricultural worker exploitation running through the 1930s and 1940s.

  • Pittsburgh Courier: The leading Black newspaper that launched the Double V campaign in February 1942. The Black press played a crucial role in articulating the WWII contradiction and building the intellectual and political case for postwar civil rights demands.

Themes

This section is the most thematically concentrated in the unit, operating at the intersection of Social History (SOC), World Relations (WOR), and National Identity (NAT) simultaneously. The AP's emphasis on the WOR + SOC + NAT intersection makes this section's analytical framework essential for high-scoring exam responses.

The WOR mission (defeat fascism and racial hierarchy) created a specific kind of moral leverage for domestic civil rights demands: if racial hierarchy was what made fascism evil, then racial hierarchy at home was self-contradicting. The Double V campaign used this logic explicitly. The Tuskegee Airmen and 761st Tank Battalion's records used it empirically. A. Philip Randolph used it strategically.

The SOC theme reveals the gap between wartime rhetoric and practice across multiple groups simultaneously: Japanese Americans (imprisoned as presumptive traitors); African Americans (fighting in segregated forces for a democracy that denied them equality); Mexican braceros (laboring under unenforceable contracts with deportation as the employer's leverage); women (recruited into industrial work as a temporary wartime expedient, then displaced).

The NAT theme surfaces in the question of who constitutes 'the people' the democracy serves. EO 9066's answer: Japanese Americans — even citizens — could be excluded based on race. The Double V's answer: African American soldiers were full members of the national community, and the nation owed them the democratic principles it proclaimed abroad. These two answers — the government's and the civil rights movement's — are in direct tension, and understanding that tension is essential to understanding what the WWII era meant for American democracy.

Compare and Contrast

Japanese American internment and German/Italian American treatment: The United States was simultaneously at war with Germany and Italy but did not apply mass race-based internment to German or Italian Americans. Individual German and Italian nationals were detained; no mass exclusion orders applied by ethnicity or national origin. The contrast makes the racial basis of EO 9066 explicit — the determination of security threat was made by race, not by evidence of individual conduct, and was applied only to the non-white enemy-origin group.

Double V Campaign and WWI African American military service: In WWI, African Americans served in segregated units (including the 92nd and 93rd Divisions); their wartime service was followed by intensified racial violence — the Red Summer of 1919. The WWII generation had this pattern as historical memory. The Double V campaign represented a more organized, more explicit demand that wartime service translate into postwar equality — a demand shaped by knowledge of the WWI betrayal.

Rosie the Riveter and postwar Second Wave Feminism: The wartime expansion of women's industrial employment was a temporary disruption of the "separate spheres" ideology, not its permanent defeat. The postwar reimposition of domesticity — driven by the same government-media apparatus that had recruited women — created the specific frustration that Betty Friedan documented in The Feminine Mystique (1963). Second-wave feminism's origins lie partly in this structural reversal.

AP Exam Approach

HAPP Analysis — Korematsu v. United States (1944):

  • Historical context: Ten weeks after Pearl Harbor; EO 9066; FBI and naval intelligence had found no organized security threat; General DeWitt's racial reasoning internal report.
  • Audience: Legal community; American public; Japanese American internees.
  • Purpose: Majority (Black) — uphold EO 9066 as constitutional under emergency necessity. Dissent (Murphy) — identify the decision as 'a legalization of racism.'
  • Point of view: Black wrote from a deference-to-executive position under wartime emergency; Murphy wrote from a civil liberties absolutist position. Murphy's point of view proved correct by both the historical record and the Court's own subsequent judgment.

Common AP prompts for this section:

  • Explain why the Supreme Court upheld Japanese American internment in Korematsu v. United States, and evaluate the strength of that reasoning.
  • How did the Double V campaign connect the foreign and domestic dimensions of WWII for African Americans? What specific wartime experiences strengthened the postwar civil rights movement?
  • To what extent was the wartime transformation of women's industrial roles permanent? Use specific evidence.

Short-Answer Question Practice:

"The home front of World War II revealed fundamental contradictions between American democratic rhetoric and American democratic practice."

  • Part B: Explain how African American military service during WWII strengthened the postwar case for civil rights reform.
