AP US History — Topic 9.1: The Reagan Revolution

Key Concepts

  • The crisis context of 1980: Reagan's victory was not accidental — it arose from a genuine collapse of public confidence. Stagflation (simultaneous inflation and economic stagnation), the Iran hostage crisis, a botched rescue mission, and Carter's "malaise" framing combined to create a political opening. Reagan offered not just different policies but a different emotional register: optimism rather than diagnosis.

  • The three-stream coalition: Reagan's 1980 coalition was structurally novel. It fused (1) economic conservatives who wanted lower taxes and deregulation, (2) the religious right mobilized by Roe v. Wade and cultural change, and (3) neoconservatives who had broken from the Democratic Party over Cold War hawkishness. No previous candidate had successfully combined all three. The coalition was durable: it defined the Republican Party's electoral base for the next four decades.

  • Supply-side economics and the contested legacy of Reaganomics: Supply-side theory held that tax cuts on upper-income earners would stimulate investment and ultimately generate more revenue than the cuts cost (the Laffer Curve). Supporters credit it with ending stagflation and producing the 1983–90 economic expansion. Critics argue it tripled the national debt, accelerated income inequality, and concentrated gains at the top while cutting programs for the poor. Historians genuinely disagree about whether the expansion was caused by the tax cuts or by Federal Reserve monetary policy. This is a real interpretive debate, not a settled question.

  • Deregulation and its consequences: Reagan pursued deregulation across industries — abolishing the Civil Aeronautics Board, rescinding the Fairness Doctrine, deregulating savings and loans. The S&L deregulation stands as a cautionary case: freed from regulatory constraints, savings and loan banks made risky investments that failed, requiring a taxpayer bailout of over $130 billion. Deregulation produced both genuine efficiencies and serious systemic risks.

  • The PATCO firing as symbolic politics: Reagan's decision to fire 11,345 striking air traffic controllers signaled a new relationship between the federal government and organized labor. Whether the action was legally justified (PATCO members were federal employees prohibited from striking) or a political assault on the labor movement is itself debated. The outcome was clear: union membership's long decline accelerated through the 1980s.

  • Reagan's Cold War posture — confrontation, rollback, and arms control: Reagan rejected détente and committed to what he called rollback — arming anti-communist insurgencies (Reagan Doctrine) and massively increasing defense spending. SDI (Strategic Defense Initiative, 1983) alarmed Soviet planners even while critics questioned its feasibility. Yet Reagan also negotiated the INF Treaty (1987), the first pact to eliminate an entire weapons class — demonstrating that confrontational posturing and genuine diplomacy were not mutually exclusive in his foreign policy.

  • The "Evil Empire" speech as primary source: The March 1983 address to the National Association of Evangelicals recast the Cold War as a moral struggle. HAPP analysis reveals its purpose (galvanize evangelical base, make conciliation appear morally cowardly), its audience (religious voters, not the general public), and its point of view (sincerely held but strategically calculated — Reagan made conciliatory arms-control remarks to a different audience the same week). The speech is a model for how political communication is tailored to specific audiences.

  • Iran-Contra and the limits of executive power: The Iran-Contra affair — selling weapons to Iran and diverting profits to the Nicaraguan Contras in defiance of the Boland Amendment — was the most serious constitutional crisis of the Reagan years. It raised questions about covert operations, congressional oversight, and executive accountability that remained relevant in subsequent administrations. The episode's resolution — limited criminal consequences, Reagan's approval recovery — illustrated the asymmetry between executive will and congressional oversight capacity.

Key Figures and Events

Ronald Wilson Reagan (1911–2004): The defining political figure of the late twentieth century. Reagan's trajectory — from New Deal Democrat to Hollywood actor to Goldwater supporter (1964 "Time for Choosing" speech) to California governor to president — traced the arc of the conservative movement itself. His accomplishments included ending stagflation, winning the Cold War (debated), and producing sustained economic growth. His failures included the Iran-Contra scandal, tripling the national debt, and the S&L crisis. His legacy is contested: conservatives credit him with reviving American confidence and defeating Soviet communism; critics argue he launched the era of rising inequality and weakened the regulatory and labor-relations infrastructure that had protected working-class Americans.

Jerry Falwell and the Moral Majority (1979): Founded one year before Reagan's election, the Moral Majority registered previously disengaged evangelical voters and delivered them to the Republican column. It illustrated the political mobilization of a constituency that had largely avoided partisan engagement — and demonstrated that religious institutions could function as political organizations.

Arthur Laffer and supply-side theory: The economist whose "Laffer Curve" became the intellectual justification for Reagan's tax cuts. The curve itself is uncontroversial in principle (at 100% taxation, revenues are zero; at 0%, revenues are also zero — so some rate maximizes revenue). The debate is over where the curve peaks — and whether the U.S. in 1981 was above or below that point.

The PATCO Strike (August 1981): The Professional Air Traffic Controllers Organization — one of the few unions that had endorsed Reagan in 1980 — went on strike for higher wages and better conditions. Reagan gave them 48 hours, then fired them. The action was legally defensible (federal employees cannot strike) but its political signal was unambiguous.

The INF Treaty (December 1987): Signed in Washington by Reagan and Gorbachev. Eliminated all ground-launched ballistic and cruise missiles with ranges of 500–5,500 km. Required on-site verification inspections — a dramatic departure from earlier arms-control agreements that relied on national technical means. Destroyed 2,692 missiles.

Iran-Contra Revelations (November 1986): The Lebanese magazine Ash-Shiraa first reported the weapons sales to Iran; the Contra diversion was discovered shortly after. Congressional hearings (1987) featured Oliver North's dramatic testimony. Fourteen officials were indicted; eleven convicted (several convictions later overturned on appeal).

Themes

Politics and Power (POL): The Reagan Revolution was a political realignment as consequential as FDR's 1932 coalition. Reagan welded together constituencies that had not previously coalesced — economic libertarians, the religious right, and neoconservatives — into a durable majority that made the Republican Party the vehicle for anti-government, socially conservative, and hawkish foreign policy sentiment. The South's transformation from solidly Democratic to solidly Republican (a process that began with Goldwater in 1964 and Nixonian "Southern Strategy") was completed during the Reagan years. The structural consequence — that no Democratic candidate could win by proposing large government expansion — outlasted Reagan himself.

Work, Exchange, and Technology (WXT): Reaganomics represented a fundamental shift in the government's role in the economy. The New Deal consensus that the federal government should actively manage economic outcomes — through regulation, labor protections, and social insurance — was challenged and partially dismantled. Tax cuts, deregulation, and anti-union policy shifted the distribution of power between capital and labor in ways that contributed to the income inequality trends of subsequent decades.

American and Regional Culture (ARC): Reagan's cultural legacy was as significant as his economic one. By weaving religious identity into Cold War ideology (Evil Empire speech), he made cultural conservatism and political conservatism nearly inseparable — a fusion that defined American cultural politics through at least the 2000s. His "Morning in America" optimism restored a national narrative of confidence after the self-doubt of the 1970s, but critics argued it papered over genuine problems that would re-emerge.

World Relations (WOR): Reagan's Cold War strategy combined confrontation (Reagan Doctrine, military buildup, SDI) with eventual negotiation (INF Treaty). Historians debate which element mattered more in ending the Cold War — did American pressure push the Soviets toward reform, or did internal Soviet dysfunction make the collapse inevitable regardless of American policy? The question illustrates the difficulty of counterfactual reasoning in history.

Compare and Contrast

Reagan vs. FDR as coalition builders: Both presidents achieved electoral realignments that defined their parties for decades. FDR built a coalition of Northern liberals, organized labor, Southern whites, and urban ethnic voters that dominated presidential elections for thirty years. Reagan built a coalition of economic conservatives, evangelical Christians, and neoconservative intellectuals that dominated for a similar period. Both transformed what their parties stood for; both made the opposing party accommodate itself to the new framework.

Reagan's Cold War posture vs. détente (Nixon-Kissinger): Nixon and Kissinger pursued realpolitik — accepting the Soviet Union as a permanent rival to be managed through negotiation and mutual recognition. Reagan rejected détente as appeasement: he supported anti-communist insurgencies, massively increased defense spending, and deployed SDI. Yet Reagan also negotiated arms-control agreements — suggesting the contrast with détente was less absolute in practice than in rhetoric.

AP Exam Approach

Document-Based and Long Essay Questions on the Reagan era frequently ask students to evaluate the causes of the conservative movement's rise, assess the impact of Reaganomics, or analyze the Cold War endgame. Key moves:

  1. Contextualization: Place Reagan in the context of the post-1973 economic crisis and the cultural upheavals of the 1960s–70s. The conservative movement responded to real failures in the liberal policy consensus (stagflation, Vietnam, urban unrest) — acknowledge what drove it without endorsing its claims.

  2. Thesis construction: Avoid simple "Reagan good / Reagan bad" theses. A defensible thesis must explain why certain outcomes occurred, not merely assert they happened. Example: "Reagan's electoral coalition succeeded because it translated three distinct strands of conservative discontent — economic, cultural, and Cold War — into a governing majority that redefined the terms of American political debate for the next three decades."

  3. Complexity: The Evil Empire speech and the INF Treaty in the same presidency are both historically significant. Use them together to show that Reagan's foreign policy combined confrontational rhetoric with genuine pragmatism when the evidence warranted it.

  4. Primary source analysis (HAPP): When presented with an excerpt from the Evil Empire speech:

    • Historical Context: 1983 — Soviet-American tensions unusually high; nuclear freeze movement at its peak; INF deployments ongoing.
    • Audience: National Association of Evangelicals — a religious, politically engaged constituency central to Reagan's coalition.
    • Purpose: Reframe anti-communism as moral witness; make conciliation appear as moral cowardice; reward evangelical support.
    • Point of View: Sincerely held beliefs combined with strategic calculation — Reagan made conciliatory remarks to arms-control audiences the same week.

Short-Answer Questions: Use the A/B/C structure — claim, evidence, explanation. Example: "Reagan's 1980 coalition represented a new alignment in American politics. [A] One new element was the mobilization of evangelical Protestants, previously politically disengaged, through organizations like Jerry Falwell's Moral Majority. [B] The Moral Majority registered voters and raised money specifically to elect Reagan. [C] This brought a previously low-participation demographic into Republican electoral politics and made social conservatism a defining commitment of the party."
