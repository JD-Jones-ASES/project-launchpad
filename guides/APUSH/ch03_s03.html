AP US History — Topic 3.3: The Revolutionary War and Its Disparate Impacts

Key Concepts

  • The war's survival required external intervention: The Continental Army was chronically undersupplied, underpaid, and undermined by the Continental Congress's lack of taxation power. American survival was not inevitable — it required French naval and military intervention (made possible by Saratoga), British strategic mistakes, and Washington's genius for maintaining an army when everything conspired to dissolve it.

  • The Battle of Saratoga as a diplomatic turning point: Saratoga's primary significance was not military (the British continued fighting) but diplomatic: it convinced France that the American cause was viable. The resulting 1778 alliance brought French naval power capable of challenging British Atlantic control, French troops, and loans — transforming a colonial rebellion into a global war.

  • Women's service and republican motherhood: Women were active participants in the Revolution — as Daughters of Liberty organizing boycotts, as camp followers providing essential services, and occasionally as combatants in disguise. After independence, the ideology of republican motherhood acknowledged this participation while redirecting demands for formal rights into the sphere of maternal influence, leaving women's legal subordination intact.

  • Enslaved people as active agents: Approximately 450,000 enslaved people in 1775 — one-fifth of the total population — watched a revolution fought in liberty's name and drew obvious conclusions. Lord Dunmore's Proclamation created an opportunity that 800–2,000 took directly, while tens of thousands fled during British occupations. The Revolution's outcomes varied dramatically by region: Northern gradual emancipation vs. Southern entrenchment of slaveholder autonomy.

  • Indigenous nations: the Revolution as disaster: The removal of British authority dissolved the Proclamation Line that had offered some protection for indigenous land. The Iroquois Confederacy fractured along British/American alignment lines, producing civil war within a three-century-old alliance. The Treaty of Paris granted the United States sovereignty over indigenous territories without any consultation — setting the stage for accelerated dispossession.

  • The Revolution's uneven liberation: The same vocabulary — popular sovereignty, unalienable rights, consent of the governed — meant radically different things to different populations. White male property owners gained the fullest expansion of rights; women gained ideological elevation without legal equality; enslaved people in the North gained (gradual) freedom while those in the South faced strengthened slaveholder power; indigenous nations lost territorial sovereignty without compensation.

Vocabulary Review

  • Continental Army: The regular American military force raised by the Second Continental Congress under Washington's command; chronically undersupplied and underpaid due to Congress's inability to tax.
  • Continental dollars: Paper currency issued by Congress that depreciated rapidly; "not worth a Continental" became a synonym for worthlessness.
  • Battle of Saratoga (1777): American victory in upstate New York in which General Gates compelled British General Burgoyne's surrender of 5,700 men; primary significance was diplomatic, convincing France to formally ally with the United States.
  • French Alliance (1778): Formal military alliance signed after Saratoga; France brought naval power, troops, and loans; drew Spain and the Netherlands into the conflict against Britain.
  • Valley Forge (1777–78): Continental Army winter encampment where conditions killed perhaps a quarter of the men; Baron von Steuben drilled the survivors into a more professional fighting force.
  • Battle of Yorktown (1781): Final major battle; Washington and Rochambeau trapped Cornwallis while de Grasse's French fleet blocked naval relief; Cornwallis's surrender effectively ended the war.
  • Treaty of Paris (1783): Recognized American independence and granted territory to the Mississippi River; made no provision for indigenous peoples.
  • Lord Dunmore's Proclamation (1775): Virginia royal governor's offer of freedom to enslaved men who escaped patriot masters to join British forces; demonstrated enslaved people's active agency in seeking liberation.
  • Daughters of Liberty: Women's network that organized boycotts and domestic production alternatives during the imperial crisis.
  • Republican Motherhood: Post-Revolutionary ideology elevating women's domestic/educational labor as patriotically essential to republican survival, while deflecting demands for formal political rights.
  • Coverture: Legal doctrine subsuming a married woman's legal identity into her husband's; republican motherhood did not alter coverture.
  • Camp Followers: Women accompanying army encampments who provided cooking, laundry, nursing, and supply services essential to military operations.
  • Sullivan-Clinton Expedition (1779): Scorched-earth campaign destroying Haudenosaunee villages, food stores, and orchards — designed to starve communities, not defeat warriors.
  • Iroquois Confederacy / Haudenosaunee: Alliance of six nations that fractured during the Revolution when Mohawks aligned with Britain and Oneida/Tuscarora with Americans; never recovered.
  • Joseph Brant (Thayendanegea): Mohawk leader and Loyalist commander; his British alignment reflected the calculation — ultimately proved wrong by the Treaty of Paris — that Britain would better protect Haudenosaunee land.

Key Figures and Events

  • George Washington: Commander of the Continental Army for eight years; his genius for maintaining cohesion through defeats (New York, Philadelphia), desperate winters (Valley Forge), and financial crisis made him indispensable. His coordination with French forces at Yorktown demonstrated strategic vision beyond mere battlefield command.

  • Horatio Gates: American general who commanded at Saratoga — though historians debate how much credit belongs to subordinates like Benedict Arnold. Saratoga's success set the stage for the French alliance regardless of the precise attribution of command credit.

  • Baron Friedrich von Steuben: Prussian military officer who drilled the Continental Army at Valley Forge, transforming it from an ad hoc militia into a professional force capable of standing against British regulars.

  • Admiral de Grasse (François Joseph Paul): French naval commander whose fleet blocked British naval relief at Yorktown, making Cornwallis's surrender inevitable — illustrating that the war was ultimately decided by sea power as much as land battles.

  • Lord Dunmore: Royal governor of Virginia whose 1775 proclamation offered freedom to enslaved men joining British forces; intended to destabilize the Patriot economy, it also created an opportunity that thousands of enslaved people seized.

  • Deborah Sampson: Massachusetts woman who disguised herself as a man to serve in combat — an exceptional case illustrating both the disruption of gender norms during wartime and the formal barriers to women's official participation.

  • Joseph Brant (Thayendanegea, 1743–1807): Mohawk leader who fought alongside the British; brilliant commander whose British alignment reflected strategic calculation about indigenous land protection — undermined by the Treaty of Paris, which ignored all indigenous allies.

Timeline:

  • 1775: Lord Dunmore's Proclamation; Lexington and Concord; Valley Forge winter begins (1777–78)
  • 1777: Battle of Saratoga (October)
  • 1778: French Alliance (February); British shift to Southern strategy
  • 1779: Sullivan-Clinton Expedition against Haudenosaunee
  • 1781: Battle of Yorktown (October 19) — Cornwallis surrenders
  • 1783: Treaty of Paris

Themes

Social Structures (SOC): The Revolutionary War's social dimension reveals multiple competing struggles within the broader conflict. Women organized boycotts, managed households, provided military support services, and occasionally fought — but the post-war ideology of republican motherhood channeled their participation into a domestically defined role. Enslaved people demonstrated active agency in seizing opportunities created by the conflict; the war's outcomes for them depended entirely on their geographic location. Indigenous nations were treated as obstacles to post-war expansion regardless of which side they had supported.

American National Identity (NAT): The construction of American national identity after the Revolution required defining who was and was not included in "the People." The exclusion of women from voting, the entrenchment of slavery in the South, and the dispossession of indigenous peoples were all constitutive acts — national identity was built partly by defining its limits. These exclusions would define the central contests of the next two centuries.

America in the World (WOR): The French alliance transformed the Revolutionary War into a global conflict — the same dynamic that had produced the Seven Years' War now worked in reverse. France's strategic calculation at Saratoga and naval intervention at Yorktown demonstrate that American independence was not achieved in isolation but as part of European imperial rivalry. The Treaty of Paris itself reflected British and French diplomatic interests as much as American military achievement.

Work, Exchange, and Technology (WXT): The Continental Army's financial crisis — rooted in Congress's lack of taxation power — illustrated the economic prerequisites of military capacity. The depreciation of Continental dollars demonstrated that paper currency without tax backing was unsustainable. These lessons about public finance drove the movement toward constitutional reform.

Connections to Earlier/Later Periods

Earlier: The Seven Years' War established patterns that recurred during the Revolution: global European conflict shaping North American outcomes; indigenous nations forced to choose between European powers; the use of indigenous allies by both sides. Lord Dunmore's Proclamation echoed earlier British wartime offers to enslaved people and earlier indigenous experiences of European powers exploiting division.

Later: Republican motherhood established the ideological framework for expanded female education in the early republic, contributing long-term to the arguments for women's suffrage. The Seneca Falls Convention (1848) explicitly invoked the Declaration of Independence's language to argue for women's political rights. The Northern emancipation laws created free Black communities and abolitionist movements that challenged Southern slavery in the antebellum period. The Sullivan-Clinton Expedition established the template for the U.S. Army's treatment of indigenous peoples in the nineteenth-century West — scorched-earth campaigns against civilian infrastructure rather than military engagement.

AP Exam Approach

Multi-perspectival framing is essential: AP APUSH exam questions frequently require analysis of how the same event or process affected different groups differently. For the Revolutionary War, always be prepared to address: white male colonists, women, enslaved people (with the North/South distinction), and indigenous peoples. A strong essay acknowledges all four groups and explains why their experiences diverged.

Common SAQ format:

  • Explain how the Battle of Saratoga changed the Revolutionary War.
  • Describe the concept of republican motherhood and explain TWO ways it both expanded and limited women's position.
  • Compare the Revolution's impact on enslaved people in the North and South and explain why outcomes differed.

Common essay pitfall: Don't describe the Revolution as a unified American liberation story — the APUSH course explicitly requires acknowledging that the Revolution's liberating effects were "radically uneven" and that it was experienced as disaster by many of its participants. Essays that ignore women, enslaved people, and indigenous nations are incomplete by design.

Causation analysis: When explaining American victory, avoid single-cause explanations. Strong answers combine: French intervention (necessary condition after Saratoga), British strategic errors (southern strategy overextension, Cornwallis's Virginia Peninsula vulnerability), Washington's leadership (maintaining army cohesion through Valley Forge), and the decentralized guerrilla nature of the conflict (making it unwinnable at sustainable cost for Britain).

Primary source connections: If asked to analyze Lord Dunmore's Proclamation, note that it was both a military measure (depriving Patriots of labor) and a moment that revealed enslaved people's active agency — the source explicitly frames enslaved people as actors, not just acted-upon. This is the multi-perspectival framing the AP exam rewards.
