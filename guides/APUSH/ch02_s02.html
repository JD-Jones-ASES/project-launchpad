AP US History — Topic 2.2: From Indenture to Chattel Slavery

Key Concepts

  • Slavery as deliberate legal construction, not natural evolution: Race-based chattel slavery in Virginia was not the inevitable result of pre-existing racial prejudice. It was built through a specific legislative sequence between 1662 and 1705, in deliberate response to the class crisis that freed white indentured servants had created. Each statute closed a pathway to freedom that had existed in the earlier, more ambiguous period.

  • The 1662 statute as a pivotal turning point: By establishing that enslaved status followed the mother (partus sequitur ventrem), Virginia reversed English common law and created a system in which enslaved women's children were automatically enslaved regardless of paternity. This single statute made sexual violence against enslaved women economically profitable for slaveholders, since assault now also produced enslaved property.

  • The Middle Passage as organized atrocity: The transatlantic crossing that transported enslaved captives from West Africa to the Americas killed roughly 15 percent of captives per voyage. The brutality was not incidental — it was the consequence of economic calculations by slavers who maximized cargo per voyage. Olaudah Equiano's 1789 memoir transformed these statistics into testimony, making the human cost legible to a British public debating abolition.

  • African expertise and the Carolina economy: The Carolina plantation economy was built not simply on enslaved African labor but on enslaved African technical knowledge. Planters specifically sought workers from West African rice-growing regions because those workers possessed cultivation expertise that English planters lacked. This fact challenges the assumption that the slave trade treated Africans as undifferentiated physical labor.

  • Racial solidarity as a tool of class management: The shift to race-based slavery created a racial hierarchy that elevated all white colonists — including the landless poor — above all African Americans. This cross-class racial solidarity was not an accident but a political achievement: even the poorest white Virginian had a stake in a system guaranteeing his racial status, defusing the class tensions that Bacon's Rebellion had exposed.

  • Chattel slavery's distinguishing features: American chattel slavery differed from Roman slavery and many forms of African bondage in being permanently hereditary (through the mother), racially defined, and backed by a comprehensive legal apparatus prohibiting literacy, assembly, court testimony, and self-defense for enslaved people.

Vocabulary Review

  • Chattel: Personal movable property; in chattel slavery, human beings were legally defined as chattels that could be bought, sold, inherited, and mortgaged.
  • Partus sequitur ventrem: Latin legal principle (Virginia, 1662) meaning the offspring follows the status of the mother; made enslaved status automatically heritable through the maternal line.
  • Middle Passage: The second leg of the triangular trade — the transatlantic crossing from West Africa to the Americas; named for its position in the middle of the trade triangle.
  • Slave codes: Colonial and later state statutes systematically defining the legal status, rights, and treatment of enslaved people; Virginia's codes (1662–1705) became a model for other colonies.
  • Virginia Slave Code of 1705: Consolidated earlier Virginia statutes into a comprehensive legal framework defining enslaved people as property, stripping them of legal rights, and authorizing extreme punishment.
  • Triangular trade: The Atlantic commercial circuit linking England (manufactured goods), West Africa (enslaved captives), and the Americas (colonial commodities), operating in multiple overlapping routes.
  • Olaudah Equiano: Author of the most widely read abolitionist memoir by an enslaved African; his 1789 Interesting Narrative described the Middle Passage in testimony used to advance British abolition.
  • Seasoning: A euphemism for the period of forced acclimation imposed on enslaved people after arrival in the Americas; killed a significant fraction in the first year beyond Middle Passage mortality.
  • Rice Coast: West African coastal region (present-day Sierra Leone, Guinea-Bissau, Senegal) from which Carolina planters specifically recruited enslaved workers for their technical rice-cultivation knowledge.
  • Stono Rebellion (1739): South Carolina slave uprising; roughly twenty enslaved people marched toward Spanish Florida; triggered more repressive legislation despite (or because of) the colony's continued economic dependence on enslaved labor.
  • Anthony Johnson: Free Black Virginia landowner of the 1630s–40s whose legal trajectory demonstrates the pre-code ambiguity of racial status and proves that race-based slavery was constructed, not predetermined.

Key Figures and Events

  • Olaudah Equiano (c. 1745–1797): Kidnapped from Nigeria as a child; survived the Middle Passage; eventually purchased his own freedom; published his Interesting Narrative in 1789 as both personal testimony and abolitionist political instrument during the British slave trade debate.
  • Anthony Johnson: Early African settler who gained freedom and property in Virginia — the key evidence that race-based chattel slavery was not predetermined but was constructed through deliberate legislative choices made after his time.
  • Virginia legislature (1662–1705): The collective agent of slavery's legal construction; each successive assembly tightened the legal architecture, closing ambiguities and pathways to freedom: 1662 (partus), 1667 (baptism), 1670 (free Blacks lose right to own white servants), 1691 (interracial marriage ban), 1705 (comprehensive code).
  • Stono Rebellion (1739): Twenty enslaved South Carolinians who seized weapons, killed approximately twenty whites, and marched toward Spanish Florida; confirmed planter fears about a system of control stretched over a Black majority colony; triggered more repressive legislation.
  • The Carolina colony (established 1663): Developed a rice and indigo plantation economy in the Low Country, drawing specifically on the technical expertise of enslaved West Africans; by the early eighteenth century, the only mainland colony with a Black majority.
  • Middle Passage — scale and mortality: Virginia's African population grew from fewer than 300 (1650) to roughly 13,000 (1700) to approximately 150,000 (1750). This scale of forced migration required accepting average 15 percent per-voyage mortality as an economic cost of doing business.

Themes

Social Structures (SOC): Race-based chattel slavery was the defining social transformation of the late seventeenth-century Chesapeake. By legally defining all persons of African descent as property rather than persons, the slave codes eliminated a juridical middle ground. The racial hierarchy this created served dual purposes: it provided planters with a permanent labor force and provided poor white colonists with a racial identity that compensated for their economic powerlessness. The construction was deliberate, not natural.

Work, Exchange, and Technology (WXT): Enslaved African labor was the productive foundation of the Chesapeake tobacco economy and the Carolina rice economy. In the Carolina case, this labor included specific technical knowledge (rice cultivation) that European planters did not possess — making African expertise, not just African muscle, the engine of colonial prosperity. The triangular trade integrated the Chesapeake and Carolina into an Atlantic commercial system in which British merchants captured the profits while enslaved workers captured none of the value they created.

Politics and Power (PCE): The legislative construction of race-based slavery was a political project: planters and assemblies chose, statute by statute, to define slavery as hereditary, racial, and permanent in direct response to the class threat revealed by Bacon's Rebellion. This was governance — the deliberate use of law to organize society in ways that protected elite interests. Understanding slavery as a political construction is essential to understanding how it could eventually be challenged by political means.

American and Regional Culture (ARC): The development of chattel slavery created the foundational cultural divide between a free-labor North and an enslaved-labor South that would organize American political conflict for the next two centuries. The legal, economic, and demographic patterns established in the Chesapeake and Carolina in the late seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries were the structural preconditions for the antebellum plantation South.

Connections to Earlier/Later Periods

Connection backward: The Middle Passage was the American extension of a Portuguese-initiated Atlantic slave trade that had been operating since the mid-fifteenth century. West African political entities (kingdoms like Dahomey and the Ashanti) participated as sellers in the trade, often enslaving captives from rival polities. The triangular trade built on earlier Portuguese and Spanish models of plantation slavery in the Atlantic islands and Caribbean. The Virginia slave codes drew on Barbadian law (1661), which provided a model for defining enslaved people as property.

Connection forward: The legal architecture constructed in Virginia and the Carolinas between 1662 and 1705 became the template for slave legislation across the American colonies and the early Republic. The Constitutional Convention's compromises on slavery (Three-Fifths Clause, Fugitive Slave Clause, twenty-year moratorium on restricting the slave trade) all reflect the power of interests whose foundation lay in this colonial legal construction. The abolition movement of the nineteenth century was, in part, a campaign to reverse a specific legislative construction — which is why its opponents argued so fiercely that slavery was natural rather than constructed. The Thirteenth (1865), Fourteenth (1868), and Fifteenth (1870) Amendments were the legislative deconstruction of the edifice built in the late seventeenth century.

AP Exam Approach

Common essay prompt types for this topic:

  • SAQ / DBQ: Analyze how and why the legal definition of slavery changed in the seventeenth-century Chesapeake; use Equiano's Narrative as a document requiring sourcing (audience, purpose, historical situation, point of view).
  • LEQ: Evaluate the extent to which Bacon's Rebellion transformed the labor system of the colonial South.
  • Comparison: Virginia chattel slavery vs. Caribbean sugar slavery; colonial chattel slavery vs. Roman slavery.

Key analytical moves:

  1. Distinguish cause from acceleration: Bacon's Rebellion did not cause the shift to chattel slavery — the process had begun decades earlier — but it dramatically accelerated it by clarifying the political dangers of the indenture system.

  2. Source Equiano's narrative carefully: AP Document-Based Questions reward students who analyze documents' purpose, audience, and historical situation. Equiano wrote in Britain in 1789 for a reading public debating abolition — his rhetorical choices (visceral description, moral appeal) were calibrated to that audience and that debate.

  3. Distinguish chattel slavery from other forms of bondage: The AP exam rewards precise vocabulary. Chattel slavery was distinguished by its heritable, racial, permanent character — not just by the fact of coerced labor.

  4. Multi-perspectival analysis: The construction of chattel slavery looked radically different from the perspectives of (a) tidewater planters (political necessity after Bacon's threat); (b) enslaved Africans (systematic legal destruction of personhood); (c) poor white colonists (racial identity as compensation for economic powerlessness); (d) free Black Virginians like Anthony Johnson's descendants (progressive exclusion from legal protections they had enjoyed earlier).

  5. Causation precision: Avoid the framing that racial prejudice caused slavery. The source's argument — and strong APUSH analytical framing — reverses the causation: the legal construction of race-based slavery created and deepened racial prejudice as a justification for an economic and political system already in place.
