AP US History — Topic 7.5: The Great Depression and the New Deal

Key Concepts

  • The crash as trigger, not cause: Black Tuesday (October 29, 1929) triggered the Depression by making simultaneously visible four pre-existing structural weaknesses: fragile banking with inadequate reserves, a farm sector depressed since 1921, a consumer economy dependent on installment credit, and a global economy destabilized by unresolved WWI war debts and reparations.

  • Hoover's constrained response: Hoover was not the passive president of caricature — the RFC and Federal Home Loan Bank Act were genuine interventions. But his ideological commitment against direct individual relief, combined with his balanced-budget orthodoxy, meant his response was insufficient to the scale of the catastrophe. By 1932, private charity was overwhelmed and local governments were bankrupt.

  • FDR's First Hundred Days — relief, recovery, reform: Fifteen major pieces of legislation in 104 days, all passed. The Emergency Banking Act restored confidence; the FDIC eliminated bank run incentives; the CCC, TVA, and FERA provided relief; the NRA and AAA attempted recovery. The First New Deal was ad hoc and experimental — FDR himself said he would try something, and if it didn't work, try something else.

  • The Second New Deal — structural transformation: Responding to left-wing pressure (Huey Long, Townsend, labor movement) and to the Supreme Court's destruction of the NRA and AAA, FDR shifted toward permanent structural reform: Social Security (retirement and unemployment insurance), the Wagner Act (collective bargaining rights), the WPA (8.5 million employed), and the Fair Labor Standards Act (minimum wage, maximum hours, child labor prohibition).

  • New Deal exclusions — the racial bargain: Social Security, the Wagner Act, and the Fair Labor Standards Act all excluded domestic servants and agricultural workers — categories disproportionately African American and Latino — because Southern Democrats whose votes were needed for passage demanded those exclusions. The New Deal's racial architecture was not incidental; it was structural.

  • The court-packing fight: FDR's 1937 proposal to add justices for each justice over 70 was constitutionally legal but politically catastrophic. Congress refused to pass it. But Justice Roberts's "switch in time" upheld the New Deal without a single new appointment — the threat accomplished what the act could not, while the fight damaged FDR's political coalition.

  • Federal power transformed: The New Deal permanently transformed the federal government from primarily regulatory and promotional to insurer, employer, labor arbiter, wage-setter, and direct service provider — a transformation so significant it has been politically contested in every subsequent decade.

Vocabulary Review

  • Black Tuesday: October 29, 1929 — worst single-day stock market collapse; trigger of the Great Depression.

  • Dust Bowl: Environmental catastrophe in the Great Plains from drought and overcultivation; displaced ~2.5 million; the Okie migration; documented by Lange and Steinbeck.

  • Hoovervilles: Makeshift shantytowns named after President Hoover, appearing in every American city as unemployment reached 25%.

  • Bonus Army: 20,000 WWI veterans marching on Washington for early bonus payment (1932); dispersed by MacArthur with tanks and tear gas; catastrophic optics for Hoover.

  • Reconstruction Finance Corporation (RFC): Hoover-era agency providing emergency loans to banks and railroads — largest peacetime federal relief program to that point, demonstrating Hoover's institutional rather than individual relief approach.

  • Emergency Banking Act (1933): Authorized Treasury to examine and reopen sound banks under federal supervision, ending the acute banking panic of FDR's inauguration week.

  • FDIC: Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation; insured individual bank deposits; eliminated bank run incentive; "the single most important banking reform of the New Deal."

  • Agricultural Adjustment Act (AAA): Paid farmers to reduce production to raise prices; struck down by Supreme Court (1936). Displaced Black sharecroppers as landowners pocketed payments and evicted tenants.

  • Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA): Dams and hydroelectric plants across seven Southern states; brought electricity to areas private utilities deemed unprofitable.

  • Civilian Conservation Corps (CCC): Put 300,000 young unemployed men to work in national forests and parks; racially segregated but inclusive.

  • National Recovery Administration (NRA): Industry fair-competition codes plus labor organizing rights; struck down by Supreme Court (1935).

  • Social Security Act (1935): Federal old-age insurance and unemployment insurance; financed by payroll taxes; excluded domestic servants and agricultural workers (disproportionately Black and Latino) to secure Southern Democratic votes.

  • Wagner Act (1935): Guaranteed right to organize and bargain collectively; created NLRB. Excluded agricultural and domestic workers.

  • Works Progress Administration (WPA): Employed 8.5 million in construction and Federal Arts Projects.

  • Fair Labor Standards Act (1938): First federal minimum wage (25 cents/hr), 44-hour maximum workweek, child labor prohibition in interstate commerce. Excluded domestic and agricultural workers.

  • Court-packing plan (1937): FDR's proposal to add justices for each justice over 70; constitutionally legal, politically catastrophic; defeated in Congress.

  • 'Switch in time that saved nine': Justice Roberts's vote shift to uphold New Deal after the court-packing fight; New Deal's constitutional survival without new appointments.

Key Figures and Events

  • Franklin D. Roosevelt (1882–1945): Four-term president; inaugurated March 4, 1933; First Hundred Days; Fireside Chats; court-packing fight; WWII leadership; died April 12, 1945. The most transformative domestic president of the twentieth century — and also the president who signed Executive Order 9066 (Japanese internment).

  • Herbert Hoover (1874–1964): President 1929–33; not passive but ideologically constrained; RFC and Federal Home Loan Bank Act; opposed direct individual relief; Bonus Army dispersal. Later revised by historians as more activist than contemporary reputation suggested — but still insufficient to the crisis.

  • Dorothea Lange (1895–1965): Farm Security Administration photographer whose Migrant Mother (1936) became the defining image of Depression-era suffering. Documentary photography as political argument for federal relief.

  • John Steinbeck (1902–1968): The Grapes of Wrath (1939) — novel documenting the Okie migration from Dust Bowl to California; Nobel Prize for Literature 1962. Provided literary documentation of what Lange's photography captured visually.

  • Huey Long (1893–1935): Louisiana Senator; "Share Our Wealth" movement; pushed FDR leftward; assassinated 1935. Represented the political pressure from the left that shaped the Second New Deal.

  • Justice Owen Roberts: His "switch in time" vote shift — upholding minimum wage laws and the Wagner Act — ended the Supreme Court's hostility to the New Deal after the court-packing fight, without any new appointments.

  • Black Tuesday (October 29, 1929): The Depression's trigger; made visible structural weaknesses in banking, agriculture, consumer credit, and global finance.

  • Schechter Poultry Corp. v. United States (1935): Supreme Court struck down the NRA as an unconstitutional delegation of legislative power. "Sick chicken case." Accelerated FDR's move toward Second New Deal structural reforms.

  • United States v. Butler (1936): Supreme Court struck down the AAA. Combined with Schechter, created the constitutional crisis that prompted the court-packing proposal.

Themes

The Great Depression and New Deal section is the richest in the course for the Politics (POL) and Economy (ECO) themes. The POL theme runs in two directions simultaneously: FDR expanded federal power dramatically, while the court-packing fight revealed the structural limits on that expansion — independent judiciary, congressional veto, and democratic norms constraining even a president who had won 46 of 48 states.

The ECO theme is central: the Depression demonstrated the systemic vulnerability of an industrial capitalist economy to financial crisis, and the New Deal's response permanently transformed the federal government's role from bystander to active manager of economic outcomes. The shift from Hoover's balanced-budget orthodoxy to FDR's deficit spending is one of the most important economic policy transformations in American history.

The Social (SOC) theme appears in the New Deal's racial architecture. Social Security, the Wagner Act, and FLSA all excluded domestic servants and agricultural workers — categories disproportionately African American and Latino — to secure Southern Democratic votes. This was not an oversight; it was a deliberate political bargain. The New Deal's social insurance system built inequality into its foundations, creating a two-tier welfare state in which Black and Latino workers received less protection than white workers from the outset.

Compare and Contrast

First New Deal vs. Second New Deal: The First New Deal (1933) was primarily emergency relief and experimental recovery — FDIC, CCC, TVA, NRA, AAA. It focused on immediate crisis management and was largely ad hoc. The Second New Deal (1935–38) was more structurally ambitious: Social Security (permanent social insurance), Wagner Act (permanent labor rights), WPA (ongoing employment), FLSA (permanent wage floor). The Second New Deal built the institutional architecture of the modern American welfare state; the First New Deal stabilized the emergency.

Hoover's response vs. FDR's response: Both used federal institutional tools (Hoover: RFC, FLHBA; FDR: RFC continuation, banking acts, TVA). The key differences: (1) FDR embraced deficit spending; Hoover insisted on balanced budgets. (2) FDR implemented direct relief (FERA, CCC, WPA); Hoover opposed direct individual relief on ideological grounds. (3) FDR used radio and personal communication to build political legitimacy; Hoover's communication was formal and distant. (4) FDR was willing to experiment; Hoover was ideologically committed to specific approaches.

AP Exam Approach

HAPP Analysis — FDR's First Inaugural (March 4, 1933):

  • Historical context: Banking system in acute crisis; 38 states had declared bank holidays; 25% unemployment; massive deflation. The inaugural occurred at the literal bottom of the Depression.
  • Audience: The American public — 100,000 in person, millions listening on radio. The inauguration was Roosevelt's first use of mass media to establish the relationship between president and public that the Fireside Chats would sustain.
  • Purpose: Restore confidence ('the only thing we have to fear is fear itself'); signal decisive action; establish FDR's authority to govern; define the crisis in terms that justified federal intervention.
  • Point of view: FDR as a patrician Democrat who genuinely believed in both democratic participation and activist government — his 'bold, persistent experimentation' reflected a pragmatic rather than ideological approach to economic crisis.

Common AP prompts for this section:

  • Explain the difference between the First New Deal and the Second New Deal in terms of goals and primary programs.
  • Why did Social Security exclude domestic servants and agricultural workers? What does this reveal about the political constraints on New Deal reform?
  • Evaluate FDR's court-packing plan. Was it a legitimate response to judicial obstruction or an unacceptable attack on judicial independence?
