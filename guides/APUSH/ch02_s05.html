AP US History — Topic 2.5: Mercantilism, Salutary Neglect, and the Great Awakening

Key Concepts

  • Mercantilism as imperial logic: Mercantilism was not a unified theory but a set of widely shared assumptions grounded in the belief that wealth was finite and zero-sum. Colonies existed to serve the mother country: supplying raw materials, absorbing manufactured goods, and generating customs revenue. The Navigation Acts attempted to enforce this logic on a transatlantic scale, requiring that enumerated colonial goods flow exclusively through English channels.

  • The gap between regulation and enforcement: The Navigation Acts existed on paper; their enforcement was another matter. Under the informal policy historians call salutary neglect, customs officials were corrupt or understaffed, enforcement mechanisms were weak, and London was perpetually distracted by European conflicts. New England merchants ran their own triangular trade routes in open technical violation of the Molasses Act of 1733. Colonial commercial life operated largely outside the regulatory framework.

  • Salutary neglect as political training: The long-term effect of salutary neglect was to train an entire colonial political class in the habits and expectations of self-governance. Colonial assemblies accumulated effective legislative authority — controlling governors' salaries, passing their own tax laws — simply because no one in London challenged them. When Parliament attempted to reassert authority after 1763, it confronted not passive subjects but experienced legislators with deeply held constitutional expectations.

  • The Great Awakening as cultural revolution: The revival of the 1730s–40s challenged established clerical authority by demonstrating that emotional appeal and personal conviction — not ordination, education, or institutional position — were the bases of legitimate spiritual authority. Itinerant preachers like George Whitefield reached servants, women, and enslaved people as well as the propertied, bypassing established churches. This democratization of spiritual authority cultivated the habit of popular democratic judgment.

  • The Awakening's political implications: The Awakening did not cause the Revolution, but it prepared colonists culturally for revolutionary rhetoric. By training ordinary people to evaluate and choose their own spiritual authority, it made them more likely to evaluate and potentially reject civil authority as well. The revivalist model of earned authority (through persuasion) versus inherited authority (through institution) mapped directly onto the colonial constitutional argument against Parliament's claim to unlimited taxation authority.

  • By 1754 — a prepared collision: The Navigation Acts had integrated the colonies into a British imperial economy that generated real resentment. Salutary neglect had given colonial assemblies a century of practice in self-governance, producing entrenched expectations of autonomy. The Great Awakening had created a shared evangelical culture that crossed colonial boundaries and trained colonists in popular democratic judgment. The empire held together because it had not yet tried to assert itself. When it did, after 1763, it found the colonies far better prepared than it expected.

Vocabulary Review

  • Mercantilism: The economic philosophy that national wealth is finite and zero-sum; colonies exist to enrich the mother country by supplying raw materials and providing captive markets.
  • Navigation Acts: English (later British) parliamentary statutes beginning 1651 regulating colonial trade; enumerated goods must go to England; all trade in English ships; European goods through English middlemen.
  • Enumerated goods: Specific colonial commodities required by the Navigation Acts to be exported only to England: tobacco, sugar, rice, indigo, naval stores.
  • Molasses Act (1733): Duty on non-British Caribbean molasses, largely evaded under salutary neglect; its replacement (Sugar Act, 1764) with actual enforcement was a colonial grievance.
  • Salutary neglect: The informal British policy of allowing colonies wide latitude in self-governance and commercial affairs; associated with Walpole's ministry (1721–42); cultivated colonial legislative experience and autonomy expectations.
  • Robert Walpole: British prime minister 1721–42; most associated with salutary neglect; his long ministry gave colonial assemblies decades of uncontested legislative practice.
  • First Great Awakening: Wave of religious revivals in the 1730s–40s; key figures: Jonathan Edwards (Northampton), George Whitefield (itinerant); created New Light / Old Light split; democratized spiritual authority; first mass communications event in American history.
  • Jonathan Edwards: Calvinist minister; 'Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God' (1741); preached emotionally intense theology; key New England Awakening figure.
  • George Whitefield: English itinerant minister; drew thousands to outdoor meetings across all thirteen colonies 1739–1770; bypassed established clergy; owned enslaved people despite preaching equal spiritual dignity.
  • New Light / Old Light: Great Awakening divide: New Lights embraced revival, itinerancy, emotional preaching; Old Lights defended established ecclesiastical authority and educated ordained clergy.
  • Itinerant preacher: A minister who traveled from community to community rather than serving a fixed congregation; Whitefield's itinerancy was the mechanism of the Awakening's intercolonial reach.
  • Naval stores: Ship-supply forest products (tar, pitch, timber) enumerated under the Navigation Acts; key export of New England and the Carolinas.

Key Figures and Events

  • Robert Walpole (1676–1745): Britain's first effective prime minister; his ministry (1721–42) systematized the practical pattern of salutary neglect; his approach cultivated the colonial legislative autonomy that would make post-1763 parliamentary assertion so explosive.
  • Jonathan Edwards (1703–1758): New England's greatest colonial theologian; his 'Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God' (1741) represents the emotional intensity of Awakening preaching; later became president of Princeton; combined intellectual rigor with revivalist urgency.
  • George Whitefield (1714–1770): The Awakening's greatest popular preacher; his extraordinary theatrical voice drew thousands to outdoor meetings from Georgia to New England; his tours were the first genuinely intercolonial cultural event; he modeled the earned-authority relationship between speaker and audience that influenced colonial political culture.
  • Virginia House of Burgesses / Massachusetts General Court / Pennsylvania Assembly: The colonial assemblies that accumulated effective legislative authority under salutary neglect, controlling governors' salaries and understanding themselves as local equivalents of Parliament — the politically experienced legislators who would resist Parliament's post-1763 assertions.
  • Navigation Acts (1651, 1660, 1663, onward): The successive parliamentary statutes that attempted to enforce mercantilist logic on the Atlantic scale; their irregular enforcement defined the colonial commercial experience more than their regulatory intent did.
  • Molasses Act (1733): The statute New England merchants violated with impunity — the clearest example of salutary neglect allowing colonial commercial autonomy to develop outside the regulatory framework.
  • New Light colleges (Princeton 1746, Brown 1764, Dartmouth 1769, Rutgers 1766): Institutions founded by revivalist denominations to train a new kind of minister; the institutional legacy of the Great Awakening demonstrating that the revival had lasting organizational effects beyond emotional experience.

Themes

Economics and Commerce (WXT): Mercantilism and the Navigation Acts represent the imperial economic framework that connected the thirteen colonies to a British Atlantic system. The triangular trade — in all its actual complexity, far messier than the geometric metaphor — was the operational mechanism of this integration. Colonial resentment of the Navigation Acts' costs (denied access to higher-paying European buyers) was a real economic grievance that would be mobilized as a political argument after 1763. Salutary neglect meant that this resentment built for a century without producing crisis — until enforcement arrived.

Politics and Power (PCE): Salutary neglect is one of the most important unintended-consequences stories in American history. A policy of inaction produced the single most consequential long-term outcome: a colonial political class trained in self-governance, constitutionally confident, and impossible to subordinate once challenged. The colonial assemblies' accumulation of power under neglect is the direct institutional precondition for the Revolutionary-era assertion of "no taxation without representation."

Religion and Culture (ARC): The Great Awakening created a shared evangelical culture that crossed colonial, denominational, and in some measure social boundaries — servants and women heard Whitefield alongside propertied men. By democratizing spiritual authority (earned through persuasion rather than inherited through institution), the Awakening cultivated the habit of popular democratic judgment. This cultural transformation was not a political program, but it prepared ordinary colonists to engage with revolutionary rhetoric in ways that earlier generations, trained to defer to established authority, might not have.

Interaction/Environment and Atlantic Context (ENV + Global): The triangular trade integrated the thirteen colonies into an Atlantic system that stretched from English manufacturing centers through West African slave-trading ports to Caribbean sugar islands to New England rum distilleries. This integration was not merely commercial — it connected colonial prosperity to the slave trade in ways that the colonies' economies could not easily escape. The Navigation Acts and triangular trade were the economic infrastructure of the Atlantic slave system as much as of the colonial commercial system.

Connections to Earlier/Later Periods

Connection backward: The Navigation Acts built on earlier English commercial regulation (especially against the Dutch) and drew directly on the mercantilist tradition developed by French finance minister Jean-Baptiste Colbert and English theorists like Thomas Mun. The triangular trade extended the Portuguese-initiated Atlantic slave trade and the Caribbean plantation system already operating before the mainland colonies were established. The Great Awakening drew on the tradition of Reformed Protestantism that included Puritanism, Presbyterianism, and continental Pietism — all of which emphasized personal spiritual experience over clerical mediation.

Connection forward: Salutary neglect's end is the proximate cause of the Revolutionary crisis: Parliament's Stamp Act (1765), Townshend Acts (1767), Intolerable Acts (1774), and other post-1763 measures were experienced as violations of a century of established colonial constitutional practice. The colonial assemblies' accumulated power under neglect became the institutional base for the Continental Congress and state legislatures. The Great Awakening's institutional legacy (new colleges) contributed to the print culture and educated leadership of the Revolutionary generation. The Awakening's internal contradictions about slavery — Whitefield's mental gymnastics vs. Quaker abolitionism — foreshadowed the central tension of the new nation's founding documents.

AP Exam Approach

Common essay prompt types for this topic:

  • SAQ: Explain how salutary neglect contributed to the development of representative self-governance in the colonies; explain ONE way the Great Awakening challenged established social hierarchies.
  • LEQ: Evaluate the extent to which the Great Awakening transformed colonial political culture in the period 1730–1763.
  • DBQ context: Salutary neglect and colonial assemblies are frequent contextual elements in DBQs about colonial resistance to parliamentary authority; mercantilism appears as context for documents about colonial trade regulation.

Key analytical moves:

  1. The unintended consequences argument: Salutary neglect is the clearest APUSH example of an unintended-consequences pattern — a policy intended to avoid conflict produced the constitutional expectations that made later conflict inevitable. This is a sophisticated analytical frame for any essay on colonial-Revolutionary continuity.

  2. Distinguish mercantilism as theory from the Navigation Acts as practice: The theory (wealth is zero-sum, colonies serve the mother country) justified the policy (Navigation Acts). The gap between policy and enforcement (salutary neglect) is what actually shaped colonial development. Students who conflate theory, policy, and practice will miss the nuance the AP rewards.

  3. The Awakening's mechanism, not just its effects: Don't just state that the Awakening 'contributed to the Revolution.' Explain the mechanism: itinerant preachers modeled earned authority vs. inherited authority; ordinary colonists practiced evaluating and choosing spiritual leaders; this cultural training prepared them to evaluate and challenge civil leaders. The mechanism is what AP graders reward.

  4. Whitefield's contradiction on slavery: The AP rewards students who complicate simple narratives. Whitefield's simultaneous preaching of equal spiritual dignity and ownership of enslaved people is a productive example of historical contradiction — the Awakening planted antislavery seeds but did not deliver the fruit, illustrating the gap between theological implication and historical practice.

  5. Connect 1607–1754 as a coherent period: The chapter covers nearly 150 years, but all three forces in this section — Navigation Acts, salutary neglect, Great Awakening — help explain why 1754 (the eve of the Seven Years' War and the colonial crisis) looks the way it does. Show the examiner you understand how these forces accumulated over time to produce a specific historical situation.
