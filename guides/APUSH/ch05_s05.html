AP US History — Topic 5.5: Lincoln, Emancipation, and Federal Power

Key Concepts

  • Lincoln as strategic emancipationist: Lincoln entered the war committed to Union preservation, not emancipation. His movement toward the Emancipation Proclamation was driven by three strategic calculations — military necessity (depriving the Confederacy of enslaved labor), diplomatic necessity (foreclosing British and French recognition), and political necessity (maintaining Radical Republican support). Understanding emancipation as a product of these strategic pressures, rather than purely moral conviction, is essential for AP analysis of Lincoln's decision-making.

  • The Proclamation's deliberate limits: The Emancipation Proclamation applied only to states "in rebellion," explicitly exempting the border states and Confederate areas under Union control. This left approximately 750,000–800,000 enslaved people immediately unaffected. Lincoln justified this through his commander-in-chief powers: emancipation in rebel states was a military measure; emancipation in loyal states would require legislation or a constitutional amendment. Critics who note he "freed the enslaved where he had no power" are correct as a factual matter — the constitutional logic is the response.

  • The Proclamation's real achievements: Despite its limits, the Proclamation transformed the war's character. Approximately 180,000 enslaved people fled to Union lines (many enlisted as USCT soldiers); European recognition of the Confederacy became politically impossible; the war became an explicitly anti-slavery struggle; and the political logic was set in motion that culminated in the Thirteenth Amendment.

  • The Gettysburg Address as rhetorical revolution: Lincoln's 272-word address performed a constitutional reinterpretation through historical dating: "four score and seven years ago" (1776, not 1787) made the Declaration of Independence — not the Constitution — the nation's founding document. If the founding promise was equality, the Constitution that permitted slavery was not its fulfillment but its betrayal. This move redefined what the war was for without a single constitutional amendment.

  • The Second Inaugural's moral reckoning: Unlike a triumphalist victory speech, Lincoln's Second Inaugural (March 4, 1865) assigned moral culpability to the entire nation — North and South — for the crime of slavery. "Both sides prayed to the same God; neither prayer was answered as asked" was a refusal of the comfortable narrative that the North was righteous and the South was guilty. The closing "with malice toward none, with charity for all" set a conciliatory tone for Reconstruction that Lincoln's assassination left Andrew Johnson to implement without Lincoln's moral seriousness.

  • Lincoln's rhetorical legacy: The three documents together — Emancipation Proclamation, Gettysburg Address, Second Inaugural — created the interpretive framework through which Americans have argued about their history ever since: freedom as the war's purpose, equality as the founding promise, and national moral responsibility for slavery as the war's deepest meaning.

Vocabulary Review

  • Emancipation Proclamation (January 1, 1863): Executive order issued under Lincoln's commander-in-chief powers, freeing enslaved people in states "in rebellion." Exempted border states and Confederate areas under Union control. Transformed the war's character from constitutional dispute to anti-slavery struggle.
  • Commander-in-chief powers: The constitutional authority of the president as the military's supreme commander. Lincoln's constitutional justification for the Emancipation Proclamation: depriving the Confederacy of enslaved labor was a military measure, not legislation, and therefore within executive authority.
  • Preliminary Emancipation Proclamation (September 22, 1862): Issued after Antietam; warned that unless rebel states returned to the Union by January 1, 1863, their enslaved people would be freed. None returned; the final Proclamation followed.
  • Thirteenth Amendment (1865): Abolished slavery throughout the United States, including in areas the Emancipation Proclamation had exempted. Overcoming the Proclamation's geographic limits, it was the constitutional completion of what the Proclamation had begun.
  • Gettysburg Address (November 19, 1863): Lincoln's 272-word dedication speech at the Soldiers' National Cemetery; redefined the war's meaning by grounding it in the Declaration of Independence's equality promise rather than the Constitution.
  • "Four score and seven years ago": The Gettysburg Address's opening; dates the American founding to 1776 (Declaration), not 1787 (Constitution) — the rhetorical move that made equality, not constitutional self-government, the founding's central promise.
  • "New birth of freedom": Lincoln's phrase for what the war was creating — implying that the existing nation had failed to fulfill its founding promise and that freedom needed to be reborn through the war's sacrifice.
  • Second Inaugural Address (March 4, 1865): Lincoln's second inaugural, delivered knowing Confederate defeat was imminent; offered a theological reckoning with the war's meaning, assigning national moral culpability for slavery to North and South alike; closed "with malice toward none."
  • "With malice toward none": The closing sentiment of the Second Inaugural; conciliatory spirit that defined Lincoln's intended approach to Reconstruction and was then applied very differently by Andrew Johnson after Lincoln's assassination.
  • HAPP analysis: Historical context, Audience, Purpose, Point of view — the AP framework for analyzing primary sources. The Gettysburg Address and Second Inaugural are both high-frequency HAPP targets.

Key Figures and Events

  • Abraham Lincoln (1809–1865): The central figure of the war presidency. His evolution from constitutional unionist to author of emancipation to framer of national moral reckoning illustrates the transformative logic of war on political leadership. His rhetorical genius produced three documents that have defined American democratic aspiration; his assassination left their application to a far less capable successor.
  • William H. Seward (1801–1872): Secretary of State who advised Lincoln to wait for a military victory before issuing the Emancipation Proclamation, so it would not appear an act of desperation. His advice was taken; Antietam provided the occasion. Seward was also the diplomatic architect of the Union's strategy to prevent European recognition of the Confederacy.
  • Antietam (September 17, 1862): The bloodiest single day in American military history. General George McClellan failed to pursue the retreating Confederate Army after the battle, a failure that led Lincoln eventually to replace him. But the battle was enough of a Union success to provide the occasion Lincoln needed for the preliminary Emancipation Proclamation.
  • Battle of Gettysburg (July 1–3, 1863): The turning point of the Eastern war; Lee's second Northern invasion was repulsed with 51,000 total casualties. The Confederate Army of Northern Virginia never recovered its offensive capacity. The cemetery dedication four months later provided the occasion for Lincoln's most enduring rhetorical achievement.
  • Edward Everett: The main speaker at the Gettysburg cemetery dedication; his two-hour oration is now forgotten. He later wrote Lincoln: "I wish that I could flatter myself that I had come as near to the central idea of the occasion, in two hours, as you did in two minutes." Lincoln's rhetorical economy had permanently altered how Americans understood the event.

Themes and Analytical Frames

Politics and Power (POL): Lincoln's exercise of presidential power during the war was transformative — the Emancipation Proclamation was the most consequential executive order in American history, issued without congressional authorization under a broad interpretation of commander-in-chief powers. This expansion of executive authority in wartime has defined American constitutional practice ever since.

National Identity (NAT): Lincoln's Gettysburg Address performed the most significant revision of American national identity in the nineteenth century. By dating the founding to the Declaration of Independence and defining the founding promise as equality rather than constitutional structure, he created a national narrative that could encompass the emancipation of enslaved people — and, ultimately, the Fourteenth Amendment's citizenship guarantee.

Causation and contingency: Emancipation was not inevitable from the war's beginning — it was the product of specific military, diplomatic, and political pressures converging at a specific moment. Seward's advice about timing, Antietam's outcome, European diplomatic calculations, and Radical Republican political pressure all shaped the Proclamation's content and timing. Understanding emancipation as strategic rather than purely moral does not diminish it — it explains how the impossible became politically achievable.

HAPP Analysis: Lincoln's Three Key Documents

Emancipation Proclamation (January 1, 1863)

  • Historical context: After Antietam (September 1862), preliminary proclamation (September 22, 1862), ten months into the war with no Confederate return to the Union.
  • Audience: Rebel state governments, enslaved people, European governments, the Union's Northern civilian public.
  • Purpose: Military (deprive Confederate labor), diplomatic (foreclose European recognition), political (maintain Radical Republican coalition).
  • Point of view: Lincoln as commander-in-chief exercising military authority, not as legislator; the commander-in-chief framing determined the Proclamation's geographic scope.

Gettysburg Address (November 19, 1863)

  • Historical context: Four months after the Union victories at Gettysburg and Vicksburg (the war's turning points), ten months after the Emancipation Proclamation.
  • Audience: 15,000 at the cemetery dedication; Northern public via newspaper reproduction.
  • Purpose: Redefine the war's meaning as a struggle for equality and "a new birth of freedom," drawing on the Declaration of Independence.
  • Point of view: Lincoln as president and as the author of an interpretation, not as military commander; the rhetorical choice to cite the Declaration, not the Constitution, was strategic.

Second Inaugural Address (March 4, 1865)

  • Historical context: Four weeks before Appomattox; the war was nearly won; Lincoln could have been triumphalist.
  • Audience: The assembled crowd in Washington plus the national public; the speech was clearly intended for the historical record as much as the immediate moment.
  • Purpose: Offer a moral framework for reconstruction that assigned national culpability while urging reconciliation.
  • Point of view: Lincoln as a leader who refused comfortable narratives; his assignment of shared national culpability for slavery's two and a half centuries was politically generous to the South while morally rigorous.

AP Exam Approach

SAQ Practice

Abraham Lincoln's three major wartime documents — the Emancipation Proclamation, the Gettysburg Address, and the Second Inaugural Address — each contributed to a fundamental revision of what the Civil War was about.

Part A: Explain the specific constitutional basis Lincoln used to justify the Emancipation Proclamation and describe ONE limitation that resulted from that constitutional basis.

Part B: Identify the specific rhetorical move Lincoln makes in the Gettysburg Address's opening sentence that reframes the meaning of the American founding, and explain its constitutional significance.

Part C: Explain how the Second Inaugural Address's treatment of moral responsibility for slavery differed from a triumphalist interpretation of the Union's impending victory.

DBQ/LEQ framing: When analyzing Lincoln's leadership, the AP exam rewards analysis of his strategic pragmatism over hagiographic tributes. Lincoln moved toward emancipation because of strategic necessity — this does not diminish the Proclamation's moral significance, but it explains its timing, scope, and constitutional form. The HAPP framework applied to Lincoln's three documents will yield the strongest primary source analysis on any AP essay.
