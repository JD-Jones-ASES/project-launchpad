AP US History — Topic 3.7: Washington's Farewell and the Election of 1800

Key Concepts

  • Washington's voluntary retirement as a constitutional act: By choosing to leave after two terms, Washington proved the republic did not depend on any single man's continued presence — and established the two-term tradition that held for 144 years. A republic that required its founding hero's permanent presence was not yet a self-governing republic.

  • The Farewell Address as a document of principled frustration: Washington's warnings against parties and permanent alliances were both principled and personal. He had watched Hamilton and Jefferson destroy cabinet unity; foreign powers (France and Britain) had manipulated American domestic politics throughout his presidency. The Address was as much a reflection of lived experience as of abstract republican theory.

  • The Election of 1800 as a constitutional crisis: The original Constitution's lack of separate presidential/vice-presidential ballots — designed for a world without organized parties — produced the Jefferson-Burr tie when all Democratic-Republican electors voted for both men. The Federalist-controlled House had to decide between two candidates from the party that had defeated them.

  • Hamilton's intervention as institutional over partisan loyalty: Hamilton's decision to support Jefferson — despite despising him — over Burr, whom he regarded as genuinely dangerous to the republic, is a model of placing institutional integrity above personal and partisan advantage. It also contributed to the personal enmity with Burr that ended in Hamilton's death.

  • The Revolution of 1800 — genuinely and partially revolutionary: The peaceful transfer of executive power between opposing parties was genuinely revolutionary by 1800 world standards. What was NOT revolutionary: Hamilton's financial architecture survived, governing required pragmatism, and the Louisiana Purchase demonstrated that even strict constructionists found implied powers necessary when governing.

  • The 1790s' three lasting institutional foundations: The two-party system (despite the Founders' anti-party theory), judicial review (articulated in Federalist No. 78, established in Marbury v. Madison 1803), and peaceful power transfer (established by the election of 1800) structured American democracy for the centuries that followed.

Vocabulary Review

  • Farewell Address (1796): Washington's published letter warning against the spirit of party and permanent foreign alliances; a foundational document of American foreign policy and anti-party tradition.
  • Spirit of Party: Washington's term for organized factional competition; he believed it elevated faction loyalty above national interest and invited foreign manipulation.
  • Permanent Alliances: The foreign entanglements Washington warned against — political and military commitments to European powers; distinguished from commercial relations (which he encouraged).
  • Two-Term Tradition: Washington's voluntary establishment (not a constitutional requirement until the Twenty-Second Amendment, 1951) that presidents serve no more than two terms.
  • Election of 1800: The presidential contest between Adams (Federalist) and Jefferson/Burr (Democratic-Republicans) that produced the electoral tie and the House resolution.
  • Electoral College Tie: When all Democratic-Republican electors voted for both Jefferson and Burr, producing 73 votes each — triggering the constitutional provision sending the decision to the House.
  • Revolution of 1800: Jefferson's phrase for his election as a restoration of republican principles after Federalist overreach; also the first peaceful partisan transfer of executive power in republican history.
  • Twelfth Amendment (1804): Constitutional correction requiring separate ballots for president and vice president; prevented future ties like 1800's.
  • Louisiana Purchase (1803): Jefferson's purchase of the Louisiana Territory from France — the largest federal power expansion in the early republic; authorized by a president who had argued for strict construction, illustrating the demands of pragmatic governance.
  • Judicial Review: Courts' authority to invalidate unconstitutional legislation; theorized in Federalist No. 78; established in Marbury v. Madison (1803).
  • Marbury v. Madison (1803): Chief Justice Marshall's decision establishing judicial review in practice — fulfilling Federalist No. 78's theoretical framework under a Democratic-Republican administration.
  • Monroe Doctrine (1823): President Monroe's declaration closing the Western Hemisphere to European colonization; drew on Washington's Farewell Address tradition of distinguishing commercial from political-military foreign entanglements.
  • Twenty-Second Amendment (1951): Made Washington's two-term tradition a constitutional requirement after FDR's four-term presidency.

Key Figures and Events

  • George Washington (1732–1799): The "indispensable man" whose prestige held the constitutional experiment together while the new government established legitimacy. His voluntary retirement established the two-term precedent; his Farewell Address defined the standards against which Americans would measure partisan excess and foreign entanglement for generations.

  • John Adams (1735–1826): Second president (1797–1801); navigated the XYZ Affair; signed the Alien and Sedition Acts; accepted electoral defeat and transferred power peacefully — his dignified departure was as constitutionally significant as Jefferson's victory. His statesmanlike Convention of 1800 (ending the Quasi-War) cost him Federalist support and contributed to his electoral defeat.

  • Thomas Jefferson (1743–1826): Author of the Declaration of Independence, Secretary of State under Washington, Vice President under Adams, elected third president in 1800. His "Revolution of 1800" framing captured both what was genuinely new (peaceful partisan power transfer) and what his governance would reveal — that ideological purity yields to pragmatic necessity.

  • Aaron Burr (1756–1836): Vice presidential candidate who tied Jefferson in the 1800 electoral vote; served as VP (1801–05) but was politically destroyed by Hamilton's opposition and the 1804 duel in which he killed Hamilton. Burr later attempted to establish a western empire (the "Burr Conspiracy") and was tried for treason (acquitted, 1807).

  • Alexander Hamilton (1755 or 1757–1804): His intervention in the 1800 House vote — choosing Jefferson over Burr on principled grounds — was an act of institutional courage that contributed to his death in the Burr-Hamilton duel (July 1804). His financial architecture (debt, Bank, credit) survived Jefferson's presidency, vindicating the durability of his work despite Federalist political defeat.

  • Chief Justice John Marshall (1755–1835): Appointed by Adams as a "midnight judge" in 1801; as Chief Justice until 1835, established judicial review (Marbury v. Madison, 1803), national supremacy (McCulloch v. Maryland, 1819), and federal commerce power (Gibbons v. Ogden, 1824) — translating Federalist constitutional theory into judicial precedent under Democratic-Republican administrations.

Timeline:

  • September 19, 1796: Washington's Farewell Address published
  • November–December 1796: Adams elected president (71 electoral votes to Jefferson's 68)
  • 1797–98: XYZ Affair
  • 1798: Alien and Sedition Acts; Kentucky and Virginia Resolutions
  • 1798–1800: Quasi-War
  • November–December 1800: Election of 1800 — Jefferson-Burr tie at 73 votes each
  • February 17, 1801: House chooses Jefferson after 36 ballots
  • March 4, 1801: Jefferson's inauguration — peaceful power transfer
  • February 24, 1803: Marbury v. Madison establishes judicial review
  • 1804: Twelfth Amendment ratified; Burr-Hamilton duel (July)

Themes

Politics and Power (POL): The 1790s decade culminated in the election of 1800, which tested whether the constitutional framework could channel partisan competition without producing civil war or coup. The peaceful transfer of power between Adams and Jefferson established the foundational precedent of American democratic legitimacy: elections, not armies or courts, determine who governs. The three institutional foundations — two-party system, judicial review, peaceful transfer — established in this decade have structured American democracy ever since.

Ideas, Beliefs, and Culture (CUL): Washington's Farewell Address became canonical text — invoked whenever Americans worried about partisan excess or foreign entanglement. The Address illustrates how founding documents gain authority beyond their original political context: Washington's warnings were "immediately overtaken by events" but remained influential precisely because the events he warned against continued to occur.

America in the World (WOR): Washington's anti-alliance doctrine, institutionalized through the Monroe Doctrine, defined American foreign policy for the nineteenth century. The Farewell Address distinguished commercial engagement (desirable) from political-military entanglement (dangerous) — a distinction that shaped American responses to European conflicts from the Napoleonic Wars through World War I.

Work, Exchange, and Technology (WXT): Jefferson's Louisiana Purchase, which expanded federal authority dramatically beyond what his strict construction doctrine permitted, illustrates how governing pressures can override philosophical commitments. The practical logic of territorial expansion — and Napoleon's irresistible offer — overrode theoretical consistency. Hamilton's financial architecture's survival similarly demonstrates how institutional structures can outlast the political coalitions that created them.

Connections to Earlier/Later Periods

Earlier: Washington's two-term retirement created the norm whose violation (FDR, 1940) prompted the Twenty-Second Amendment. The election of 1800's peaceful resolution was the fulfillment of the Constitution's promise that the 1787 Convention had made — but nearly failed to keep. The nullification theory articulated in the Kentucky and Virginia Resolutions (1798) directly connected to the next generation's states' rights conflicts.

Later: The Farewell Address tradition directly influenced: Monroe Doctrine (1823), American neutrality in European conflicts throughout the nineteenth century, Senate rejection of the League of Nations (1919–20), and the "America First" tradition in twentieth-century politics. Marbury v. Madison (1803) established judicial review as the organizing principle of American constitutional law — every Supreme Court decision invoking judicial authority traces to this precedent. The election of 1800's peaceful transfer precedent remained the gold standard for measuring democratic legitimacy and has been tested in every subsequent contested election.

AP Exam Approach

HAPP Analysis for Washington's Farewell Address:

  • Historical context: Eight years of presidency marked by Hamilton-Jefferson cabinet conflict; foreign policy crises (Jay's Treaty, XYZ Affair); France and Britain actively manipulating American domestic politics; Quasi-War imminent.
  • Audience: American public and political leaders of both parties; aimed at posterity — published, not delivered, for maximum circulation.
  • Purpose: Warn against parties and permanent alliances as the twin dangers to republican survival; establish Washington's legacy as the founding figure who identified these dangers.
  • Point of view: Personal experience of watching Hamilton and Jefferson destroy cabinet unity; Virginian agricultural background less commercially dependent on Atlantic entanglement than New England.

Common SAQ/Essay formats:

  • Use the Farewell Address excerpt to identify Washington's TWO main warnings and explain which has been more consistently followed.
  • Explain the constitutional defect that produced the 1800 Electoral College crisis and how the Twelfth Amendment resolved it.
  • Evaluate Jefferson's characterization of his election as the "Revolution of 1800" — identify ONE genuinely revolutionary element and ONE element of continuity.

Key synthesis prompt: The decade of the 1790s established three institutional foundations (two-party system, judicial review, peaceful transfer of power) that structured American democracy. Strong essays explain not just WHAT these foundations were but WHY they emerged from the specific conflicts of the 1790s — how bank debates, foreign policy crises, and electoral mechanics produced lasting institutional patterns.

Common errors:

  • Don't describe Washington as politically neutral — he was a Federalist in everything but formal affiliation; his Farewell Address's anti-party warning targeted Democratic-Republican partisanship as much as Federalist partisanship.
  • Don't describe the Revolution of 1800 as a sudden or complete transformation — Jefferson's Louisiana Purchase shows that the continuities were as important as the changes.
  • Don't forget that Hamilton's death (1804 duel) was a direct consequence of the 1800 election's political dynamics — the story of the early republic's constitutional politics was also intensely personal.
