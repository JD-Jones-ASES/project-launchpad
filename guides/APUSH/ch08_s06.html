AP US History — Topic 8.6: Stagflation, Watergate, and the Crisis of Confidence

Key Concepts

  • Watergate as constitutional crisis, not mere political scandal: The Watergate scandal is often described as "a political scandal." It was more than that — a systematic pattern of illegal surveillance, obstruction of justice, and abuse of government power for partisan purposes, organized from the White House. The constitutional confrontation over the White House tapes raised fundamental questions about executive privilege, the rule of law, and whether the president was above it. United States v. Nixon (1974) established that executive privilege has limits — a precedent with lasting constitutional significance.

  • Nixon's foreign policy paradox: Nixon was simultaneously a genuine foreign policy innovator (China opening, détente, SALT I) and the president who committed the most serious constitutional abuses in American history since Reconstruction. The paradox illuminates a core historical question: do foreign policy achievements legitimate a presidency, or are they separable from domestic conduct? The historical consensus is that they are separable — the China opening was genuinely significant; the obstruction of justice was genuinely impeachable.

  • Stagflation and the Keynesian breakdown: The 1970s economic crisis exposed the limits of the Keynesian consensus that had guided American economic policy since the New Deal. The simultaneous occurrence of high inflation and high unemployment — stagflation — violated the theoretical trade-off (the Phillips Curve) that Keynesian prescriptions depended on. Addressing one problem worsened the other. This intellectual and policy failure created the opening for supply-side economics and Reagan's 1981 tax cuts — an alternative framework that claimed to resolve the dilemma.

  • The convergence of crises and the conservative opening: Vietnam, Watergate, stagflation, and the Iran hostage crisis were not merely four separate problems. Together, they shattered the governing consensus that had sustained postwar American politics — the belief that the federal government could manage the economy, contain communism, and expand social provision. Public trust in government fell from 75% to below 25% between the mid-1960s and 1980. The political reaction was not a demand for new progressive solutions but for less government — exactly the program Ronald Reagan offered.

  • Carter's tragedy: Carter was diagnostically accurate about the crisis of confidence he diagnosed; his policy achievements were real (Camp David Accords, Panama Canal Treaties, human rights emphasis). But he could not translate accurate diagnosis into effective political leadership, and the Iranian hostage crisis — 444 days, failed rescue operation — made his failure concrete and public. Carter illustrates the distinction between policy competence and political leadership: they are not the same.

  • The domestic costs of Cold War foreign policy: The Iranian Revolution (1979) demonstrated a specific cost of Cold War strategy: the United States had supported the Shah's authoritarian government purely on anti-communist grounds, suppressing the democratic and reformist movements that the Shah persecuted. The revolution that overthrew him — and the hostage crisis that followed — was partly a product of the anti-Americanism those policies had created. Carter's Camp David Accords were a genuine achievement; the Iran hostage crisis was partly a consequence of the Cold War logic that had preceded him.

Vocabulary Review

  • Vietnamization: Nixon's strategy of withdrawing American troops while transferring combat responsibility to South Vietnamese forces; extended the war by four years.
  • Silent Majority: Nixon's November 1969 framing of patriotic, traditional Americans as a silent majority supporting the war against a vocal antiwar minority; pioneered culture-war political framing.
  • Kent State (May 4, 1970): National Guard killed four students during protests against Cambodia bombing; produced the largest campus strike in American history.
  • Détente: Nixon-Kissinger policy of managing US-Soviet relations through arms control (SALT I) and normalized trade; tacit acceptance that the US could not roll back Soviet power.
  • China Opening (February 1972): Nixon's diplomatic revolution ending 23 years of US non-recognition of the PRC; exploited the Sino-Soviet split.
  • SALT I (1972): Strategic Arms Limitation Talks; first major US-Soviet arms control agreement; limited strategic ballistic missile launchers.
  • Watergate: The scandal that forced Nixon's resignation; began with the 1972 DNC break-in; revealed systematic abuse of power and obstruction of justice.
  • Executive privilege: President's claimed right to confidential communications; ruled not absolute in United States v. Nixon (1974).
  • United States v. Nixon (1974): Unanimous Supreme Court ruling requiring Nixon to surrender White House tapes; established executive privilege has limits.
  • 'Smoking gun' tape: June 23, 1972 recording proving Nixon ordered CIA obstruction of the FBI investigation; ended his ability to resist impeachment.
  • Stagflation: Simultaneous high inflation, high unemployment, and slow growth; violated the Keynesian Phillips Curve trade-off; dominated the 1970s economy.
  • OPEC oil embargo (1973): OPEC cut off oil exports to countries supporting Israel in the Yom Kippur War; quadrupled oil prices; accelerated inflation.
  • Nixon Shock (1971): Nixon ended dollar-gold convertibility, abandoning the Bretton Woods system; removed constraints on money creation, contributing to inflation.
  • Bretton Woods: The post-WWII international monetary system pegging currencies to the dollar (convertible to gold); ended by Nixon in 1971.
  • Camp David Accords (1978): Carter brokered the first Egyptian-Israeli peace treaty; most significant Carter foreign policy achievement.
  • Iranian Revolution (1979): Overthrew the Shah, brought Khomeini to power; produced second oil shock and the hostage crisis.
  • Iran Hostage Crisis: 66 American diplomats held for 444 days; failed rescue (Operation Eagle Claw); defined Carter's presidency and contributed to Reagan's 1980 victory.
  • War Powers Resolution (1973): Passed over Nixon's veto; required congressional notification within 48 hours of combat deployment; limited deployment to 60 days without authorization.
  • Supply-side economics: The alternative to Keynesian consensus that emerged in the late 1970s; argued that tax cuts and deregulation would stimulate growth; became Reagan's economic framework.
  • Phillips Curve: Keynesian economic model positing a trade-off between inflation and unemployment; stagflation violated this trade-off, undermining Keynesian policy prescriptions.

Key Figures and Events

  • Richard Nixon (1913–1994): 37th president; foreign policy achievements (China, détente, SALT I) coexisted with domestic abuses (Watergate, illegal surveillance, obstruction of justice); resigned August 8, 1974 — the first and only presidential resignation in American history.

  • Henry Kissinger: National Security Advisor (1969–75) and Secretary of State (1973–77); the intellectual architect of détente and the China opening; Nobel Peace Prize (1973, controversially) for Vietnam ceasefire.

  • Special Prosecutors Archibald Cox and Leon Jaworski: Cox was fired by Nixon in the "Saturday Night Massacre" (October 1973) for refusing to accept a compromise on the tapes; Jaworski continued the investigation and obtained the Supreme Court ruling requiring tape surrender.

  • Bob Woodward and Carl Bernstein: Washington Post reporters whose investigation, guided by the anonymous source "Deep Throat" (later revealed as FBI Deputy Director Mark Felt), kept the Watergate story alive when other media were letting it drop.

  • Gerald Ford (1913–2006): Appointed Vice President after Spiro Agnew resigned in a separate corruption scandal; became president upon Nixon's resignation; his pardon of Nixon almost certainly cost him the 1976 election.

  • Jimmy Carter (1924–2024): 39th president; Camp David Accords were his greatest achievement; his presidency was undone by stagflation, the energy crisis, and the Iran hostage crisis. His post-presidential human rights and diplomacy work eventually won him a Nobel Peace Prize (2002).

Themes

Politics and Power (POL): Watergate raised the fundamental constitutional question of whether the president is above the law. United States v. Nixon (1974) answered: no. The ruling established that executive privilege, while real, has limits — it cannot be used to obstruct a criminal investigation. The precedent has been invoked in subsequent presidential controversies. The Watergate crisis also permanently altered public trust in government institutions, a political legacy that shaped subsequent decades.

America in the World (WOR): The 1970s illustrated the feedback between domestic politics and foreign policy. Watergate paralyzed American foreign policy at a critical juncture. Détente collapsed after the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan (December 1979). The Iranian Revolution demonstrated the costs of a Cold War policy that supported authoritarian governments on purely anti-communist grounds — the Shah's suppression of reformist movements generated the anti-Americanism that produced the hostage crisis. Nixon's China opening and Carter's Camp David Accords both outlasted their domestic crises, demonstrating that foreign policy achievements can be durable even when presidencies collapse.

Economic Systems: Stagflation's challenge to the Keynesian consensus is a pivotal economic policy moment. The breakdown of the Phillips Curve trade-off in the 1970s created the intellectual space for the supply-side alternative — lower taxes, deregulation, reduced government spending — that Reagan implemented from 1981. Understanding stagflation is essential to understanding why Reagan's election represented not merely a political shift but an economic policy revolution.

Connections

To ch08_s05 (Vietnam and the Great Society): This section picks up where the fracture of liberal consensus (1968) left off. Nixon inherited the Vietnam War and extended it by four years; the costs — military, moral, and political — continued to accumulate. The credibility gap that Tet had opened became a permanent feature of public attitudes toward government. The Great Society's anti-poverty programs survived the Nixon presidency in reduced form but were increasingly starved of the political and fiscal resources Vietnam consumed.

To ch08_s01 (Containment and the Early Cold War): Détente was the middle phase of the arc: Kennan's political-economic containment (1946) → NSC-68's military containment (1950) → Korean War precedent of limited war → Vietnam's demonstration of limited war's limits → détente's acceptance that roll-back was impossible → Reagan's reassertion of confrontation. Détente was not a betrayal of containment but its realistic modification in light of four decades of experience with its limits.

Forward to Chapter 9 (Reagan Revolution): The 1970s crisis is the necessary precondition for understanding Reagan's 1980 election. The public trust collapse from 75% to below 25% explains why voters were receptive to a message of less government, lower taxes, and renewed national confidence. Stagflation's discrediting of Keynesian economics provided the intellectual framework Reagan's supply-side program filled. The Iranian hostage crisis's humiliation provided the emotional energy.

AP Exam Approach

Likely essay contexts: Watergate appears in prompts about constitutional crises, executive power, and political accountability. Détente appears in change-over-time prompts about American Cold War strategy. Stagflation appears in prompts about economic policy, the limits of government intervention, and the causes of the conservative shift. The 1970s crisis as a whole appears in prompts asking why the New Deal liberal order collapsed and why conservatism rose.

Constitutional analysis: Watergate is a valuable case study for AP students in constitutional structure and the separation of powers. The key constitutional principle is executive privilege's limits — the president has no absolute right to confidentiality when it conflicts with a criminal investigation. United States v. Nixon is a landmark Supreme Court case that students should be able to describe and analyze.

Common errors to avoid:

  • Do not describe Watergate solely as "the break-in"; the break-in was the triggering incident, but the scandal was about the cover-up and the pattern of presidential abuse of power.
  • Do not describe détente as abandoning the Cold War or "losing" to the Soviet Union; it was a strategic adjustment that produced real arms control achievements.
  • Do not treat stagflation as solely the product of OPEC; the OPEC embargo was one cause among several (Nixon Shock, Vietnam-era deficits, loose monetary policy).

Short-Answer Practice:

Part A: Explain the constitutional significance of the Watergate crisis. What specific constitutional principles were at stake in the confrontation over the White House tapes?

Part B: Describe how stagflation challenged the Keynesian economic consensus that had guided American policy since the New Deal.

Part C: Explain how the convergence of Vietnam, Watergate, stagflation, and the Iran hostage crisis created the political conditions for Ronald Reagan's 1980 election.
