AP US History — Topic 9.3: Globalization, the Digital Economy, and Wealth Inequality

Key Concepts

  • Two simultaneous revolutions: Globalization and the digital revolution reshaped the American economy simultaneously and interactively. Globalization integrated national economies into a single world market, accelerating the movement of manufacturing to lower-wage locations. The digital revolution created entirely new industries and destroyed others. Together they produced the most consequential economic transformation since the Industrial Revolution — and the most consequential widening of inequality since the Gilded Age.

  • The ecosystem of innovation: The PC revolution did not emerge from government planning or large corporate research. It arose from a specific American ecosystem: research universities (Stanford, MIT), venture capital, and a countercultural ethic of individual empowerment through technology. This framing is important for the AP exam because it resists both the "Great Man" narrative (individual genius) and the purely governmental narrative (ARPANET → Internet), emphasizing instead the social and institutional conditions that enable innovation.

  • The Internet's trajectory: ARPANET (1969, Defense Department) → civilian infrastructure → World Wide Web (Berners-Lee, 1991) → commercial Internet (Netscape 1994, Amazon 1994, eBay 1995, Google 1998) → dot-com bubble (1995–2000) → smartphone era (iPhone, 2007) → social media dominance (Facebook 2004, YouTube 2005, Twitter 2006, Instagram 2010). Each stage expanded access and transformed commerce, communication, and eventually politics.

  • Financialization and its consequences: The growing dominance of the financial sector at the expense of manufacturing was driven by Reagan-era deregulation, shareholder-value ideology, and the proliferation of complex financial instruments. By 2010, the financial sector earned nearly 28% of corporate profits (up from 16% in 1979), while manufacturing employment fell by 8 million jobs. This structural shift concentrated profit and power in the financial sector while leaving manufacturing communities devastated.

  • The 2008 financial crisis — immediate trigger vs. structural causes: The immediate trigger was the housing bubble and subprime mortgage collapse. The structural causes were decades of financial deregulation, the proliferation of complex securities that concealed and concentrated risk, and the ideology of shareholder value that directed corporate behavior toward short-term financial gains rather than long-term investment. The crisis was the largest peacetime economic intervention since the New Deal; its resolution remains contested from both left (no prosecutions) and right (regulatory burden).

  • Two political responses, one underlying anger: Tea Party (2009) and Occupy Wall Street (2011) expressed the same underlying anger — that the economic system rewarded the powerful at the expense of everyone else — from opposite ideological directions. Tea Party: government spending and bailouts are the problem; reduce the state. Occupy: wealth concentration is the problem; redistribute through the state. Neither resolved the distributional grievances that generated them. Both shifted American political vocabulary in ways that shaped the 2016 election.

  • The distributional facts — what the numbers actually say: Top 1% income share: 10% (1980) → 20%+ (2019). Top 1% wealth share: 27% → 35%+. Manufacturing jobs: 19.6 million (1979) → 11.5 million (2010). Median household income barely grew in real terms for most Americans below the top quintile over thirty years. The "productivity-pay gap" — workers produced more but wages stagnated — is the central empirical fact of the era.

Key Figures and Events

Bill Gates and Paul Allen (Microsoft): Founded in 1975 in Albuquerque after writing an operating system for the Altair 8800. Microsoft's rise to dominance came through the MS-DOS deal with IBM (1981) and the Windows operating system. By the 1990s, Microsoft's near-monopoly on PC operating systems made it the subject of landmark antitrust proceedings (United States v. Microsoft, 1998–2001).

Steve Jobs (1955–2011) and Apple Computer: Co-founded Apple with Steve Wozniak in 1976 (the Apple I); the Macintosh (1984) pioneered the graphical user interface for mass-market computers. Jobs was ousted from Apple in 1985, returned in 1997, and led the company's second revolutionary phase: the iPod (2001), iTunes (2003), iPhone (2007), iPad (2010). The iPhone is perhaps the most consequential consumer product of the early twenty-first century.

Tim Berners-Lee and the World Wide Web (1991): A British computer scientist working at CERN who invented HTTP, HTML, and the URL — the protocols and markup language that created the Web. By making the Internet accessible through browsers, Berners-Lee transformed a specialized research network into a mass communication and commerce platform. He released the Web royalty-free, a decision that enabled its explosive growth.

Lehman Brothers (collapse September 15, 2008): The bankruptcy of a 158-year-old investment bank was the pivot point that transformed a financial crisis into a systemic collapse. Unlike Bear Stearns (which the government brokered a sale of in March 2008), Lehman was allowed to fail — a decision the Bush administration and Federal Reserve later described as driven by legal constraints on bailout authority. Whether allowing Lehman to fail was a mistake remains debated among economists.

Anne Case and Angus Deaton: Princeton economists who documented the 'deaths of despair' phenomenon — rising mortality among middle-aged white Americans without college degrees, driven by overdose, suicide, and alcohol-related disease. Their research, published in 2015 and expanded in Deaths of Despair and the Future of Capitalism (2020), provided the empirical foundation for arguments about the human costs of deindustrialization and inequality.

The Tea Party and 2010 Midterms: Republicans gained 63 House seats in November 2010 — their largest midterm gain since 1938. The Tea Party wave replaced moderate Republicans with more ideologically conservative members, shifted the House Republican caucus to the right, and introduced a confrontational governing style that shaped the decade's politics. The wave was powered by opposition to TARP, the ACA, and the Obama stimulus.

Themes

Work, Exchange, and Technology (WXT): This section is the WXT theme's richest illustration in Unit 9. Technological revolutions create enormous wealth but distribute it unevenly — this is the recurring pattern from the Industrial Revolution through the digital era. The digital economy created billionaires and high-wage jobs in coastal cities while deindustrialization destroyed the economic foundation of communities built around manufacturing. The question the CED WXT theme asks is: who captures the gains of technological change, and who bears the costs? The answer in this era is stark: financial sector and tech, on one side; manufacturing workers and their communities, on the other.

Social Structures (SOC): Inequality is not merely an economic phenomenon — it has social consequences. Educational attainment tracks income, which tracks life expectancy, health outcomes, community stability, and political participation. Geographic sorting reinforced economic inequality: prosperous Americans cluster in high-opportunity regions while economically struggling Americans are left in deindustrializing regions with declining public services and institutions.

Politics and Power (POL): The political consequences of economic inequality became visible in the 2010s. Both the Tea Party and Occupy Wall Street drew their energy from the same underlying perception — that the economic system was rigged for the already-powerful. The 2016 election expressed that perception in electoral form, with Donald Trump's protectionist, anti-establishment campaign drawing working-class voters who had previously voted Democratic and Bernie Sanders's democratic socialist campaign drawing young voters frustrated with economic inequality.

Compare and Contrast

Industrial Revolution vs. Digital Revolution — distributional consequences: The pattern is striking: both eras produced enormous aggregate wealth and devastating inequality. Gilded Age: Carnegie, Rockefeller, Vanderbilt at the top; Haymarket, Homestead, Triangle Fire among workers. Digital era: Amazon, Apple, Google at the top; deaths of despair, opioid crisis, community devastation among deindustrialized workers. In both cases, the distributional outcomes were not inevitable: the Progressive Era reforms of the early twentieth century redistributed some of the Gilded Age's gains. Whether a comparable redistribution would follow the digital revolution remained unresolved.

Tea Party vs. Occupy — two populisms: Both are populist movements responding to perceived elite capture of the economy. Tea Party populism: government is the mechanism of elite capture; reduce government and markets will correct. Occupy populism: markets are the mechanism of elite capture; expand government to redistribute. Both produced political energy without solving the underlying distributional problem. Their coexistence illustrates that economic grievances can be channeled in opposite ideological directions.

AP Exam Approach

Long Essay: Questions frequently ask students to evaluate the extent of change in the American economy during a given period. For 1980–2020:

  • Contextualization: Place in context of Gilded Age/Progressive Era precedent (earlier technological revolution and inequality)
  • Thesis: Argue that the period produced dramatic economic transformation but concentrated its gains in ways that produced political instability by the 2010s
  • Evidence: Use specific data (top 1% income share, manufacturing job losses, specific crisis events)
  • Complexity: Distinguish between the digital economy's genuine wealth creation and the distributional failures; note that the same period produced different outcomes for different Americans

Short Answer: When comparing Tea Party and Occupy:

  • A: Both movements expressed anger at a system perceived as rigged, but proposed opposite solutions.
  • B: The Tea Party opposed government bailouts and spending (TARP, ACA); Occupy demanded redistribution from the top 1% to the 99%.
  • C: The shared grievance (elite capture of the economy) generated opposite political responses because the two movements identified different mechanisms of that capture — government intervention vs. market outcomes.

Document Analysis: The TARP debate (2008–09) is a productive document-analysis scenario. Key analytical moves:

  • Identify the source's position and institutional perspective
  • Note that both left-wing critics (accountability, prosecution) and right-wing critics (moral hazard, regulatory burden) opposed the government response — from opposite directions
  • Ask what the critique reveals about each group's underlying theory of economic justice
