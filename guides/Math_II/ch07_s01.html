Triangle Angle Sum and Exterior Angles Study Guide

Overview

This section reviews how to classify triangles based on their sides and angles, providing a foundation for understanding their properties. It then introduces two fundamental principles of triangle geometry: the Triangle Angle-Sum Property, which states that the sum of a triangle's interior angles is always 180 degrees, and the Exterior Angle Theorem, which relates an exterior angle to its remote interior angles. These properties are crucial for solving for unknown angles in various geometric problems.

Key Concepts

  • Triangle Classification: Triangles can be categorized by the lengths of their sides (scalene, isosceles, equilateral) and by the measures of their interior angles (acute, right, obtuse).
  • Triangle Angle-Sum Property: The sum of the measures of the three interior angles of any triangle is always 180 degrees ($m A + m B + m C = 180^\circ$). This is a foundational concept used to find missing angles.
  • Exterior Angle: An angle formed outside a triangle by extending one of its sides. It forms a linear pair (adds up to 180 degrees) with the adjacent interior angle.
  • Remote Interior Angles: For any given exterior angle, the two interior angles of the triangle that are not adjacent to it are called the remote interior angles.
  • Exterior Angle Theorem: The measure of an exterior angle of a triangle is equal to the sum of the measures of its two remote interior angles ($m \text{Exterior Angle} = m \text{Remote Interior Angle}_1 + m \text{Remote Interior Angle}_2$).

Important Terms

  • Scalene Triangle: A triangle with no congruent (equal length) sides.
  • Isosceles Triangle: A triangle with at least two congruent sides. The angles opposite the congruent sides (base angles) are also congruent.
  • Equilateral Triangle: A triangle with three congruent sides. All three angles are also congruent, each measuring $60^\circ$.
  • Acute Triangle: A triangle in which all three interior angles are acute (each less than $90^\circ$).
  • Right Triangle: A triangle that has exactly one right angle (an angle measuring exactly $90^\circ$).
  • Obtuse Triangle: A triangle that has exactly one obtuse angle (an angle measuring greater than $90^\circ$).
  • Triangle Angle-Sum Property: The rule stating that the sum of the measures of the interior angles of any triangle is $180^\circ$.
  • Exterior Angle: An angle formed by one side of a triangle and the extension of an adjacent side.
  • Remote Interior Angles: The two interior angles of a triangle that are not adjacent to a given exterior angle.
  • Exterior Angle Theorem: A theorem stating that the measure of an exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the measures of its two remote interior angles.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Triangle Angle Properties

In geometry, triangles are fundamental shapes, and understanding their angle properties is essential. This section revisits how we classify triangles and introduces two key theorems that will be used extensively in future geometric reasoning.

I. Classifying Triangles

Triangles can be classified in two main ways: by the lengths of their sides and by the measures of their angles.

A. Classification by Sides

  • Scalene Triangle: All three sides have different lengths. Consequently, all three angles also have different measures.
  • Isosceles Triangle: At least two sides are congruent (equal in length). The angles opposite these two congruent sides, called the base angles, are also congruent. The angle formed by the two congruent sides is called the vertex angle.
  • Equilateral Triangle: All three sides are congruent. An equilateral triangle is a special type of isosceles triangle. Because all sides are equal, all three angles are also congruent, and since their sum must be $180^\circ$, each angle in an equilateral triangle measures $60^\circ$.

B. Classification by Angles

  • Acute Triangle: All three interior angles are acute, meaning each angle measures less than $90^\circ$.
  • Right Triangle: Exactly one interior angle is a right angle, measuring exactly $90^\circ$. The side opposite the right angle is called the hypotenuse, and the other two sides are called legs.
  • Obtuse Triangle: Exactly one interior angle is an obtuse angle, meaning it measures greater than $90^\circ$.

II. The Triangle Angle-Sum Property

This property is a cornerstone of triangle geometry.

  • Property Statement: The sum of the measures of the interior angles of any triangle is always $180^\circ$.

    • If a triangle has interior angles $A$, $B$, and $C$, then $m A + m B + m C = 180^\circ$.

  • Intuitive Understanding: Imagine tearing off the three corners of any paper triangle and placing them side by side. You will find that they always form a straight line, which measures $180^\circ$.

  • Applications:

    • Finding a Missing Angle: If you know the measures of two angles in a triangle, you can easily find the third angle by subtracting the sum of the known angles from $180^\circ$.
      • Example: If two angles are $47^\circ$ and $68^\circ$: $m C = 180^\circ - (47^\circ + 68^\circ) = 180^\circ - 115^\circ = 65^\circ$.
    • Working with Isosceles Triangles: Since the base angles of an isosceles triangle are equal, if you know the vertex angle, you can find the base angles. If the vertex angle is $40^\circ$, and each base angle is $x$, then $x + x + 40^\circ = 180^\circ \implies 2x = 140^\circ \implies x = 70^\circ$.
    • Real-World Problems: This property is used in various fields like architecture and engineering to ensure structural stability and accurate designs. For example, a roof truss with two $55^\circ$ base angles would have a peak angle of $180^\circ - (55^\circ + 55^\circ) = 180^\circ - 110^\circ = 70^\circ$.

III. Exterior Angles of a Triangle

When a side of a triangle is extended, a special angle is formed outside the triangle.

  • Definition:

    • An exterior angle of a triangle is formed by one side of the triangle and the extension of an adjacent side.
    • The two interior angles that are not adjacent to the exterior angle are called the remote interior angles.

  • The Exterior Angle Theorem:

    • Property Statement: The measure of an exterior angle of a triangle is equal to the sum of the measures of its two remote interior angles.
    • If angle $D$ is an exterior angle and angles $A$ and $B$ are its remote interior angles, then $m D = m A + m B$.

  • Relationship to the Triangle Angle-Sum Property: The Exterior Angle Theorem is a direct consequence of the Triangle Angle-Sum Property and the concept of linear pairs.

    1. We know that $m A + m B + m C = 180^\circ$ (Triangle Angle-Sum Property).
    2. The exterior angle $D$ and its adjacent interior angle $C$ form a linear pair, so $m C + m D = 180^\circ$.
    3. From (1), $m C = 180^\circ - (m A + m B)$.
    4. Substitute this into (2): $(180^\circ - (m A + m B)) + m D = 180^\circ$.
    5. Subtract $180^\circ$ from both sides: $-(m A + m B) + m D = 0$.
    6. Rearrange: $m D = m A + m B$.

  • Applications:

    • Finding an Exterior Angle: If $m P = 50^\circ$ and $m Q = 65^\circ$, the exterior angle at $R$ (say, $m PRS$) would be $m PRS = m P + m Q = 50^\circ + 65^\circ = 115^\circ$.
    • Finding a Remote Interior Angle: If an exterior angle is $130^\circ$ and one remote interior angle is $75^\circ$, the other remote interior angle is $130^\circ - 75^\circ = 55^\circ$.

  • Important Caution: An exterior angle is not one of the interior angles. It is supplementary (adds to $180^\circ$) to its adjacent interior angle. Always extend one side to clearly identify the exterior angle.

Review Questions

  1. Describe the characteristics of an isosceles right triangle. How would you classify its angles and sides?
  2. If the angles of a triangle are in the ratio $2:3:5$, find the measure of each angle and classify the triangle by its angles.
  3. In a triangle, an exterior angle measures $110^\circ$. If one of the remote interior angles is $45^\circ$, what are the measures of the other two interior angles of the triangle?
  4. Explain how the Triangle Angle-Sum Property and the concept of a linear pair logically lead to the Exterior Angle Theorem.
  5. A triangular sign is being designed. The designer wants one of the base angles to be $70^\circ$ and the vertex angle to be $40^\circ$. Is this design possible for an isosceles triangle? If not, what would the measures of the base angles be if the vertex angle is $40^\circ$?
