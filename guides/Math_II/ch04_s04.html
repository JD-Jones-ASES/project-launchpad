Solving Equations with the Distributive Property

Overview

This section focuses on a fundamental algebraic skill: solving equations that contain parentheses. It introduces a clear, step-by-step strategy that combines the distributive property with combining like terms and inverse operations to isolate the variable. Additionally, the section demonstrates how to apply these equation-solving skills to real-world scenarios, such as problems involving consecutive integers and geometric perimeters.

Key Concepts

  • The Distributive Property: This property is crucial for removing parentheses in equations. It states that to multiply a sum or difference by a number, you multiply each term inside the parentheses by that number: $a(b+c) = ab + ac$.
  • Strategy for Solving Equations with Parentheses: A systematic approach involving four steps:
    1. Distribute to remove parentheses.
    2. Combine like terms on each side of the equation.
    3. Use inverse operations to isolate the variable.
    4. Check the solution in the original equation.
  • Combining Like Terms: After distribution, terms with the same variable and exponent (or constants) on the same side of the equation can be added or subtracted to simplify the expression.
  • Inverse Operations: These are operations that undo each other (e.g., addition and subtraction; multiplication and division). They are used to move terms across the equals sign and ultimately isolate the variable.
  • Translating Word Problems into Equations: Many real-world problems can be represented by algebraic equations requiring the distributive property. Identifying variables and setting up the correct equation is a key skill.
  • Checking Solutions: Always substitute the numerical value you found for the variable back into the original equation to verify that it makes the equation true.
  • Distributing Negatives: A common pitfall to avoid is forgetting to change the sign of every term inside the parentheses when distributing a negative number or a subtraction sign. For example, $-2(x - 5) = -2x + 10$.

Important Terms

  • Distribute: To multiply a factor by each term inside a set of parentheses. For example, in $3(x+2)$, you distribute the $3$ to both $x$ and $2$.
  • Like Terms: Terms in an algebraic expression that have the same variable(s) raised to the same power(s). Constants are also like terms with other constants. For example, $5x$ and $-2x$ are like terms.
  • Inverse Operations: Operations that reverse the effect of another operation. For example, addition is the inverse of subtraction, and multiplication is the inverse of division.
  • Consecutive Integers: Integers that follow each other in order, differing by 1. Examples include $n$, $n+1$, $n+2$.
  • Perimeter: The total distance around the outside edge of a two-dimensional shape. For a rectangle, the perimeter ($P$) is $2 \times \text{length} + 2 \times \text{width}$.
  • Dimensions: The measurements of length, width, and height (or side lengths) of a geometric figure.

Detailed Notes

Solving equations that include parentheses requires a structured approach. The primary tool for removing these parentheses is the Distributive Property. Once parentheses are removed, the equation can be solved using familiar techniques of combining like terms and applying inverse operations.

The Four-Step Strategy for Equations with Parentheses

To effectively solve equations involving the distributive property, follow these sequential steps:

  1. Distribute to remove parentheses.

    • This is always the first step. Multiply the term (or number) immediately outside the parentheses by every single term inside the parentheses.
    • Example: For $4(x - 3)$, you would multiply $4 \times x$ and $4 \times (-3)$, resulting in $4x - 12$.
    • Caution: When distributing a negative number, remember to change the sign of all terms inside the parentheses. For instance, $-2(x - 5)$ becomes $-2x + 10$ (not $-2x - 10$).

  2. Combine like terms on each side of the equation.

    • After distributing, look at each side of the equals sign independently. Group and combine any terms that have the same variable and exponent (e.g., $3x$ and $5x$) or any constant terms. This simplifies the equation before you start isolating the variable.
    • Example: In $6x - 2 + 4x = 18$, combine $6x$ and $4x$ to get $10x$, simplifying the left side to $10x - 2 = 18$.

  3. Use inverse operations to isolate the variable.

    • Once the equation is simplified (no parentheses, no uncombined like terms), use standard algebraic operations to get the variable by itself on one side of the equation.
    • Typically, this involves adding or subtracting constants to both sides, then multiplying or dividing both sides by the variable's coefficient.
    • Remember: Whatever operation you perform on one side of the equation, you must perform on the other side to maintain equality.

  4. Check the solution in the original equation.

    • The final and crucial step is to substitute the numerical value you found for the variable back into the very first form of the equation. Perform the calculations to ensure that both sides of the equation are equal. If they are, your solution is correct!

Illustrative Examples

Example 1: Distribute Then Solve

Problem: Solve $3(x + 2) = 15$. Solution:

  1. Distribute: $3x + 6 = 15$.
  2. Combine Like Terms: (None on either side to combine.)
  3. Inverse Operations:
    • Subtract $6$ from both sides: $3x = 9$.
    • Divide by $3$: $x = 3$.
  4. Check: $3(3 + 2) = 3(5) = 15$. (Correct)

Example 2: Distribute and Combine on the Same Side

Problem: Solve $2(3x - 1) + 4x = 18$. Solution:

  1. Distribute: $6x - 2 + 4x = 18$.
  2. Combine Like Terms: Combine $6x$ and $4x$ on the left side: $10x - 2 = 18$.
  3. Inverse Operations:
    • Add $2$ to both sides: $10x = 20$.
    • Divide by $10$: $x = 2$.
  4. Check: $2(3 \times 2 - 1) + 4(2) = 2(6 - 1) + 8 = 2(5) + 8 = 10 + 8 = 18$. (Correct)

Solving Word Problems

The ability to translate a word problem into an algebraic equation is a powerful application of these skills.

Example 3: Consecutive Integers

Problem: The sum of three consecutive integers is $72$. Find the integers. Solution:

  1. Define Variables: Let the first integer be $n$.
    • The second consecutive integer is $n+1$.
    • The third consecutive integer is $n+2$.
  2. Form the Equation: The sum is $72$: $n + (n+1) + (n+2) = 72$.
  3. Distribute: (No distributive property needed here, as the parentheses just group terms, implying a multiplication by 1).
  4. Combine Like Terms: Combine the $n$ terms ($n+n+n=3n$) and the constants ($1+2=3$): $3n + 3 = 72$.
  5. Inverse Operations:
    • Subtract $3$ from both sides: $3n = 69$.
    • Divide by $3$: $n = 23$.
  6. State the Answer: The integers are $23$, $23+1=24$, and $23+2=25$.
  7. Check: $23 + 24 + 25 = 72$. (Correct)

Example 4: Perimeter Problem

Problem: The length of a rectangle is $5$ more than twice the width. The perimeter is $52$ cm. Find the dimensions. Solution:

  1. Define Variables:
    • Let $w$ be the width.
    • The length ($l$) is $5$ more than twice the width: $l = 2w + 5$.
  2. Form the Equation (Perimeter formula): $P = 2l + 2w$.
    • Substitute known values: $52 = 2(2w + 5) + 2w$.
  3. Distribute: $52 = 4w + 10 + 2w$.
  4. Combine Like Terms: Combine $4w$ and $2w$ on the right side: $52 = 6w + 10$.
  5. Inverse Operations:
    • Subtract $10$ from both sides: $42 = 6w$.
    • Divide by $6$: $w = 7$.
  6. State the Answer:
    • Width ($w$) = $7$ cm.
    • Length ($l$) = $2(7) + 5 = 14 + 5 = 19$ cm.
  7. Check: $2(7) + 2(19) = 14 + 38 = 52$. (Correct)

Checkpoint Problem

Problem: Solve $4(x - 3) = 20$. Solution Steps:

  1. Distribute: $4x - 12 = 20$.
  2. Add 12 to both sides: $4x = 32$.
  3. Divide by 4: $x = 8$.
  4. Check: $4(8 - 3) = 4(5) = 20$. This confirms the solution $x=8$ is correct.
