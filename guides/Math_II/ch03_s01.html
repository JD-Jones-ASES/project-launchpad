Chapter 3, Section 1: Ratios, Rates, and Proportions Study Guide

Overview

This section reviews and expands upon the fundamental concepts of ratios, rates, and proportions, skills you previously encountered in Mathematics I. It demonstrates how to solve various types of proportions, including those requiring multiple algebraic steps, primarily using the cross products property. Furthermore, it establishes a crucial connection between ratios and percents, showing how percent problems can be effectively solved by setting them up as proportions, and applies these concepts to real-world scenarios involving rates.

Key Concepts

  • Ratios: A comparison of two quantities using division. Ratios can be expressed in several ways (e.g., $a/b$, $a:b$, or "$a$ to $b$") and are fundamental for comparing amounts, whether they have the same or different units.
  • Rates and Unit Rates: A rate is a specific type of ratio where the two quantities being compared are measured in different units (e.g., miles per hour, cost per item). A unit rate is a rate simplified so that its second quantity (the denominator) is 1, making it easy to understand the amount per single unit (e.g., $60 \text{ mi/h}$).
  • Proportions: An equation that asserts the equality of two ratios. Proportions are crucial for solving problems where one ratio is known and an equivalent ratio with an unknown value needs to be found (e.g., $a/b = c/d$).
  • Cross Products Property (Cross Multiplication): This property states that if two ratios form a proportion ($a/b = c/d$), then the product of the means equals the product of the extremes ($ad = bc$). This is the primary algebraic tool used to solve for an unknown variable within a proportion.
  • Solving Proportions: The general strategy involves using the cross products property to transform the proportion into a linear equation, which can then be solved using standard algebraic techniques (e.g., distribution, combining like terms, isolating the variable).
  • Connecting Ratios to Percents: A percent is a special ratio where the second quantity is always 100 (e.g., 40% means $40/100$). Any percent problem can be structured as a proportion using the formula: $part/whole = \text{percent}/100$. This allows for a systematic approach to solving various percent-related questions.
  • Real-World Applications: Ratios, rates, and proportions are widely used in everyday situations, from calculating fuel efficiency and map scales to understanding recipes and unit prices, and are foundational in scientific calculations.

Important Terms

  • Ratio: A comparison of two quantities by division, written as $a/b$, $a:b$, or "$a$ to $b$," where $b \neq 0$.
  • Rate: A ratio that compares two quantities measured in different units (e.g., miles per gallon, dollars per pound).
  • Unit Rate: A rate whose second quantity (denominator) is 1. It expresses how much of the first quantity corresponds to one unit of the second quantity (e.g., $70 \text{ miles/hour}$).
  • Proportion: An equation that states that two ratios are equal, typically written as $a/b = c/d$, where $b \neq 0$ and $d \neq 0$.
  • Cross Products (or Cross Multiplication): A property of proportions that states if $a/b = c/d$, then $ad = bc$. This algebraic step is vital for solving proportions by converting them into linear equations.

Detailed Notes

1. Understanding Ratios

A ratio is a way to compare two quantities. For example, if a basketball team wins 3 games and loses 2, the ratio of wins to losses is $3/2$, $3:2$, or "3 to 2."

  • Notation: Ratios can be written as a fraction ($a/b$), using a colon ($a:b$), or with the word "to" ($a$ to $b$).
  • Denominator restriction: The second quantity, $b$, can never be zero because division by zero is undefined.
  • Units: Ratios can compare quantities with the same units (like wins to losses) or different units, which leads us to rates.

2. Rates and Unit Rates

When a ratio compares two quantities with different units, it's called a rate.

  • Examples of Rates: $120$ miles in $2$ hours, $5$ dollars for $10$ apples, $15$ liters of water per $3$ minutes.
  • Unit Rate: To make rates easier to compare and understand, we often express them as unit rates. A unit rate has a denominator of $1$.
    • To find a unit rate, divide the first quantity by the second quantity.
    • For example, if you travel $120$ miles in $2$ hours, the rate is $120 \text{ mi} / 2 \text{ h}$. To find the unit rate, divide: $120/2 = 60$. So, the unit rate is $60 \text{ mi/h}$ (60 miles per hour).

3. What is a Proportion?

A proportion is an equation that states two ratios are equal. It's written in the form $a/b = c/d$.

  • Conditions: For a proportion to be valid, the denominators ($b$ and $d$) cannot be zero.
  • Purpose: Proportions are used to find an unknown value when you know three out of the four values in two equivalent ratios.

4. The Cross Products Property (Cross Multiplication)

This property is the key to solving proportions.

  • Statement: If you have a proportion $a/b = c/d$, then you can "cross-multiply" to get the equivalent equation $ad = bc$.
  • How it works: You multiply the numerator of the first ratio by the denominator of the second ratio, and set it equal to the product of the denominator of the first ratio and the numerator of the second ratio.

5. Solving Proportions

To solve a proportion, you typically follow these steps:

  1. Set up the proportion: Ensure corresponding units or quantities are in the same position in both ratios (e.g., $gallons/miles = gallons/miles$).
  2. Cross-multiply: Apply the cross products property to convert the proportion into a linear equation.
  3. Solve the equation: Use algebraic techniques to isolate the variable.

  • Example 1: Solving a One-Step Proportion Solve $x/6 = 5/9$.

    1. Cross-multiply: $9 \times x = 6 \times 5$ $9x = 30$
    2. Divide both sides by 9: $x = 30/9$
    3. Simplify (if possible): $x = 10/3$

  • Example 2: A Multi-Step Proportion Solve $(2x + 1)/5 = 7/4$.

    1. Cross-multiply: $4 \times (2x + 1) = 5 \times 7$ $4(2x + 1) = 35$
    2. Distribute the 4 on the left side: $8x + 4 = 35$
    3. Subtract 4 from both sides: $8x = 31$
    4. Divide by 8: $x = 31/8$
    5. Decimal form (if preferred): $x = 3.875$

6. Connecting Ratios to Percents

A percent is simply a special type of ratio where the "whole" or second quantity is always $100$. For instance, $40%$ means $40$ out of $100$, or the ratio $40/100$. This connection allows us to solve percent problems using proportions: $part / whole = \text{percent} / 100$

  • Example 3: Using a Proportion to Solve a Percent Problem What number is $35%$ of $80$?
    1. Identify the knowns: The percent ($p$) is $35$. The whole is $80$. The part is unknown (let it be $x$).
    2. Set up the proportion: $x/80 = 35/100$
    3. Cross-multiply: $100x = 80 \times 35$ $100x = 2800$
    4. Divide by 100: $x = 28$ So, $28$ is $35%$ of $80$.

7. Real-World Applications with Rates

Proportions are excellent tools for solving real-world problems involving rates, as long as the rate remains constant.

  • Example 4: A Word Problem with Rates A car uses $3.5$ gallons of gasoline to travel $91$ miles. At the same rate, how many gallons are needed to travel $260$ miles?
    1. Identify the relationship: Gallons per mile.
    2. Set up the proportion: Make sure the units are consistent across the ratios. $gallons / miles = gallons / miles$ $3.5 / 91 = x / 260$
    3. Cross-multiply: $91 \times x = 3.5 \times 260$ $91x = 910$
    4. Divide by 91: $x = 10$ Therefore, the car needs $10$ gallons of gasoline to travel $260$ miles.

Mastering ratios, rates, and proportions is a foundational skill in mathematics, appearing frequently in science, engineering, and daily life scenarios like recipes, map scales, and calculating unit prices.
