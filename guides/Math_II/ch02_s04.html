Chapter 2, Section 4: Repeating Decimals as Fractions Study Guide

Overview

This section focuses on the crucial skill of converting decimals back into their fractional form. You'll learn two primary methods: using place value for terminating decimals and an algebraic method involving powers of 10 for repeating decimals. Mastering these conversions reinforces the understanding that all rational numbers can be expressed as either a terminating or repeating decimal, and vice versa.

Key Concepts

  • Rational Numbers and Decimals: Every rational number can be written as a decimal that either terminates (ends) or repeats (has a block of digits that endlessly repeats). This section teaches the reverse process.
  • Terminating Decimals to Fractions: These are converted by writing the digits after the decimal point as the numerator and a power of 10 corresponding to the last decimal place as the denominator. The resulting fraction must then be simplified.
  • Algebraic Method for Repeating Decimals: This method uses algebra to isolate and eliminate the repeating part of a decimal. It involves setting the decimal equal to a variable, multiplying by a power of 10 to shift the decimal, subtracting the original equation, and solving for the variable.
  • Powers of 10 in Repeating Decimals: The power of 10 used for multiplication depends on the number of digits in the repeating block. If 'n' digits repeat, you multiply by $10^n$.
  • Decimals with Non-Repeating and Repeating Parts: For decimals like $0.1\overline{6}$, a two-step algebraic approach is needed. First, multiply to move the non-repeating part past the decimal, then multiply again to move a full repeating block past the decimal, and finally subtract the two equations that isolate the repeating part.
  • The Special Case of $0.\overline{9}$: The repeating decimal $0.\overline{9}$ is mathematically equivalent to $1$. This is a direct consequence of the algebraic conversion method.

Important Terms

  • Terminating Decimal: A decimal that has a finite number of digits (it ends).
    • Example: $0.75$, $2.6$
  • Repeating Decimal: A decimal that has a digit or a block of digits that repeats infinitely. A bar is often placed over the repeating block.
    • Example: $0.\overline{3}$, $0.\overline{27}$, $0.1\overline{6}$
  • Rational Number: Any number that can be expressed as a fraction $\frac{a}{b}$, where $a$ and $b$ are integers and $b \neq 0$.
  • Place Value: The value of a digit in a number based on its position (e.g., in $0.75$, the $7$ is in the tenths place, and the $5$ is in the hundredths place).
  • Repeating Block: The sequence of digits that repeats indefinitely in a repeating decimal.
    • Example: In $0.\overline{27}$, the repeating block is $27$.

Detailed Notes

Introduction

Recall from Section 2-1 that every rational number can be written as a decimal that either terminates or repeats. This section teaches you how to perform the reverse operation: converting a given decimal back into its fractional form.

Terminating Decimals to Fractions

Converting terminating decimals is straightforward and relies on understanding place value.

Property: Terminating Decimal to Fraction

  1. Numerator: Write the digits after the decimal point as the numerator.
  2. Denominator: The denominator is a power of $10$ corresponding to the place value of the last decimal digit.
    • If the last digit is in the tenths place, the denominator is $10$.
    • If the last digit is in the hundredths place, the denominator is $100$.
    • If the last digit is in the thousandths place, the denominator is $1000$, and so on.
  3. Simplify: Always reduce the resulting fraction to its simplest form.

Examples:

  • $0.75$: The last digit ($5$) is in the hundredths place.
    • Fraction: $\frac{75}{100}$
    • Simplest Form: $\frac{75 \div 25}{100 \div 25} = \frac{3}{4}$
  • $0.125$: The last digit ($5$) is in the thousandths place.
    • Fraction: $\frac{125}{1000}$
    • Simplest Form: $\frac{125 \div 125}{1000 \div 125} = \frac{1}{8}$
  • $2.6$: This is a mixed number. The whole number part ($2$) remains as is. The decimal part ($0.6$) is converted. The last digit ($6$) is in the tenths place.
    • Decimal part: $\frac{6}{10}$
    • Simplest Form (decimal part): $\frac{3}{5}$
    • Combined: $2\frac{3}{5}$
    • As an improper fraction: $\frac{(2 \times 5) + 3}{5} = \frac{10 + 3}{5} = \frac{13}{5}$

Repeating Decimals to Fractions: The Algebraic Method

Repeating decimals require an algebraic approach to eliminate the repeating part.

Property: Algebraic Method for Repeating Decimals

  1. Let $x$ equal the decimal: Set $x$ equal to the repeating decimal. Write out a few repetitions to clearly see the pattern.
  2. Multiply by a power of $10$: Multiply both sides of the equation by a power of $10$ that shifts the decimal point past one full repeating block.
    • If one digit repeats (e.g., $0.\overline{3}$), multiply by $10^1 = 10$.
    • If two digits repeat (e.g., $0.\overline{27}$), multiply by $10^2 = 100$.
    • If 'n' digits repeat, multiply by $10^n$.
  3. Subtract the original equation: Subtract the original equation ($x = \text{decimal}$) from the new equation created in step 2. This step is crucial because it aligns the repeating parts so they cancel out.
  4. Solve for $x$: Solve the resulting equation for $x$ and simplify the fraction.

Examples:

  • Single-Digit Repeat: Convert $0.\overline{3}$ to a fraction.

    1. Let $x = 0.333...$
    2. Multiply by $10$ (since one digit repeats): $10x = 3.333...$

    3. Subtract: $10x = 3.333...$ $- x = 0.333...$

      $9x = 3$
    4. Solve for $x$: $x = \frac{3}{9} = \frac{1}{3}$

  • Two-Digit Repeat: Convert $0.\overline{27}$ to a fraction.

    1. Let $x = 0.272727...$
    2. Multiply by $100$ (since two digits repeat): $100x = 27.272727...$

    3. Subtract: $100x = 27.272727...$ $- x = 0.272727...$

      $99x = 27$
    4. Solve for $x$: $x = \frac{27}{99} = \frac{3 \times 9}{11 \times 9} = \frac{3}{11}$

Decimals with a Non-Repeating Part Before the Repeat

Some repeating decimals have digits that do not repeat before the repeating block starts (e.g., $0.1\overline{6}$). The algebraic method needs a slight modification using two multiplications.

Strategy:

  1. Let $x$ equal the decimal.
  2. First Multiplication: Multiply by a power of $10$ that shifts the decimal point just past the non-repeating digits. Call this Equation 1.
  3. Second Multiplication: Multiply by a power of $10$ that shifts the decimal point past one full repeating block (from the original starting point). Call this Equation 2.
  4. Subtract the two modified equations: Subtract Equation 1 from Equation 2. This will align and eliminate the repeating parts and the initial non-repeating parts.
  5. Solve for $x$.

Example: Convert $0.1\overline{6}$ to a fraction.

  1. Let $x = 0.1666...$
  2. Multiply by $10$ (to move the non-repeating $1$ past the decimal): $10x = 1.666...$ (Equation 1)
  3. Multiply by $100$ (to move the first repeating block $16$ past the decimal from original $x$): $100x = 16.666...$ (Equation 2)

  4. Subtract Equation 1 from Equation 2: $100x = 16.666...$ $- 10x = 1.666...$

    $90x = 15$
  5. Solve for $x$: $x = \frac{15}{90} = \frac{15 \div 15}{90 \div 15} = \frac{1}{6}$

Longer Repeating Block (Pure Repeat)

The method for pure repeating decimals extends to any length of repeating block.

Example: Convert $0.\overline{123}$ to a fraction.

  1. Let $x = 0.123123123...$
  2. Multiply by $1000$ (since three digits repeat): $1000x = 123.123123...$

  3. Subtract: $1000x = 123.123123...$ $- x = 0.123123...$

    $999x = 123$
  4. Solve for $x$: $x = \frac{123}{999} = \frac{123 \div 3}{999 \div 3} = \frac{41}{333}$

Special Note: $0.\overline{9} = 1$

Using the algebraic method, we find that $0.\overline{9}$ is equivalent to $1$.

  1. Let $x = 0.999...$
  2. $10x = 9.999...$
  3. $10x - x = 9.999... - 0.999... \implies 9x = 9$
  4. $x = \frac{9}{9} = 1$ This is a mathematically correct and important identity.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the key difference in the method used to convert a terminating decimal versus a repeating decimal into a fraction.
  2. Convert the terminating decimal $0.048$ into a fraction in its simplest form, showing your steps.
  3. Use the algebraic method to convert the repeating decimal $0.\overline{5}$ into a fraction in its simplest form.
  4. Convert the repeating decimal $0.\overline{18}$ into a fraction in its simplest form, explaining why you chose to multiply by $100$.
  5. Describe the steps required to convert a decimal like $0.2\overline{45}$ into a fraction. Why is it necessary to perform two different multiplications by powers of 10 before subtracting?
