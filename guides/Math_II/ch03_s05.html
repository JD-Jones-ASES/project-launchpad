Study Guide: Units of Measurement and Conversion

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concept of measurement, highlighting its importance across various fields. It focuses on the two primary measurement systems used in the United States: the customary system and the metric system. The core objective is to review the units within each system and provide practice in converting measurements from one unit to another, equipping you with essential proportional reasoning skills.

Key Concepts

  • Measurement Systems: The two main systems for quantifying properties like length, weight/mass, and capacity are the Customary System (used primarily in the U.S.) and the Metric System (used globally and in science).
  • Customary Units: A system with various units that often have less intuitive conversion factors. Key conversions must be memorized or referenced for length (inches, feet, yards, miles), weight (ounces, pounds, tons), and capacity (fluid ounces, cups, pints, quarts, gallons).
  • Metric Units: A decimal-based system where conversions are simplified because units are related by powers of 10. This makes multiplying or dividing by 10, 100, or 1,000 common.
  • Metric Prefixes: Standard prefixes (e.g., kilo-, centi-, milli-) indicate the power of 10 relationship to the base unit (meter, gram, liter). Understanding these prefixes is key to metric conversions.
  • Conversion Factor: A ratio that relates two different units of measurement (e.g., 12 inches / 1 foot). When converting, you multiply by the conversion factor in a way that cancels the original unit and leaves the desired unit.
  • Converting Within a System:
    • To convert from a larger unit to a smaller unit: Multiply by the appropriate conversion factor (the number will get larger).
    • To convert from a smaller unit to a larger unit: Divide by the appropriate conversion factor (the number will get smaller).
  • Multi-Step Conversions: Some conversions, especially in the customary system, require multiple steps to reach the desired unit (e.g., gallons to cups involves converting gallons to quarts, then quarts to pints, then pints to cups).
  • Problem Solving with Conversions: Real-world problems often require converting units before performing calculations like addition, subtraction, or comparison. Ensure all quantities are in the same unit before operating on them.

Important Terms

  • Customary System: A system of measurement used predominantly in the United States, including units such as feet, pounds, and gallons.
  • Metric System: A decimal-based system of measurement used worldwide and in scientific contexts, including units such as meters, grams, and liters. It is based on powers of 10.
  • Unit of Measurement: A standard quantity used to express a physical property (e.g., inch, kilogram, liter).
  • Conversion: The process of changing a measurement from one unit to another while maintaining the same value.
  • Length: A measure of distance. Customary units include inch (in), foot (ft), yard (yd), mile (mi). Metric units include millimeter (mm), centimeter (cm), meter (m), kilometer (km).
  • Weight/Mass: Weight is a measure of the force of gravity on an object; mass is a measure of the amount of matter in an object. Customary units for weight include ounce (oz), pound (lb), ton. Metric units for mass include milligram (mg), gram (g), kilogram (kg).
  • Capacity: A measure of the amount a container can hold, typically for liquids. Customary units include fluid ounce (fl oz), cup (cup), pint (pt), quart (qt), gallon (gal). Metric units include milliliter (mL), liter (L).
  • Prefix (Metric): A modifier added to a base unit (like meter, gram, liter) to indicate a multiple or submultiple of 10. Examples:
    • kilo-: 1,000 times the base unit (e.g., 1 kilometer = 1,000 meters).
    • centi-: 1/100 of the base unit (e.g., 1 centimeter = 0.01 meters).
    • milli-: 1/1,000 of the base unit (e.g., 1 milliliter = 0.001 liters).

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Units of Measurement

Measurement is a critical skill across many disciplines, from everyday cooking to advanced scientific research. In the United States, we commonly encounter two systems: the Customary System and the Metric System. Understanding both and being able to convert between units within each system is an essential skill.

The Customary System

The customary system (sometimes called the imperial system) uses various units that can seem less straightforward to convert due to non-decimal relationships between units.

Common Customary Conversions:

  • Length:
    • 1 foot (ft) = 12 inches (in)
    • 1 yard (yd) = 3 feet = 36 inches
    • 1 mile (mi) = 5,280 feet
  • Weight:
    • 1 pound (lb) = 16 ounces (oz)
    • 1 ton = 2,000 pounds
  • Capacity:
    • 1 cup = 8 fluid ounces (fl oz)
    • 1 pint (pt) = 2 cups
    • 1 quart (qt) = 2 pints
    • 1 gallon (gal) = 4 quarts

Converting Customary Units - Examples:

  1. Converting a larger unit to a smaller unit (Multiplication): To convert 5.25 feet to inches:

    • Identify the conversion factor: 1 ft = 12 in.
    • Since you're going from feet (larger) to inches (smaller), you multiply.
    • Calculation: $5.25 \text{ ft} \times 12 \text{ in/ft} = 63 \text{ in}$.
    • Answer: 63 inches.

  2. Mixed Customary Conversion (Multi-step): To find out how many cups are in 3.5 gallons:

    • This requires multiple steps, converting one unit at a time until you reach cups.
    • Step 1: Gallons to Quarts
      • Conversion: 1 gal = 4 qt
      • Calculation: $3.5 \text{ gal} \times 4 \text{ qt/gal} = 14 \text{ qt}$
    • Step 2: Quarts to Pints
      • Conversion: 1 qt = 2 pt
      • Calculation: $14 \text{ qt} \times 2 \text{ pt/qt} = 28 \text{ pt}$
    • Step 3: Pints to Cups
      • Conversion: 1 pt = 2 cups
      • Calculation: $28 \text{ pt} \times 2 \text{ cups/pt} = 56 \text{ cups}$
    • Answer: 56 cups.

The Metric System

The metric system is widely favored in science and internationally because its units are based on powers of 10. This characteristic makes conversions much simpler, often only requiring the movement of a decimal point.

Common Metric Conversions:

  • Length:
    • 1 centimeter (cm) = 10 millimeters (mm)
    • 1 meter (m) = 100 centimeters = 1,000 millimeters
    • 1 kilometer (km) = 1,000 meters
  • Mass:
    • 1 gram (g) = 1,000 milligrams (mg)
    • 1 kilogram (kg) = 1,000 grams
  • Capacity:
    • 1 liter (L) = 1,000 milliliters (mL)

Metric Prefixes and Conversion Rules: The metric system uses prefixes to denote multiples or submultiples of the base unit. This creates a chain of units where each step is typically a factor of 10.

  • To move to a larger unit (e.g., from grams to kilograms): You divide by the appropriate power of 10. The numerical value will get smaller, reflecting that you need fewer of the larger units. This involves moving the decimal point to the left.
  • To move to a smaller unit (e.g., from meters to centimeters): You multiply by the appropriate power of 10. The numerical value will get larger, reflecting that you need more of the smaller units. This involves moving the decimal point to the right.

Consider the length chain: km -> m -> cm -> mm

  • km to m: Multiply by 1,000 (move decimal 3 places right)
  • m to cm: Multiply by 100 (move decimal 2 places right)
  • cm to mm: Multiply by 10 (move decimal 1 place right)

The reverse applies when converting to larger units:

  • mm to cm: Divide by 10 (move decimal 1 place left)
  • cm to m: Divide by 100 (move decimal 2 places left)
  • m to km: Divide by 1,000 (move decimal 3 places left)

Converting Metric Units - Examples:

  1. Converting a smaller unit to a larger unit (Division): To convert 3,450 grams to kilograms:

    • Identify the conversion factor: 1 kg = 1,000 g.
    • Since you're going from grams (smaller) to kilograms (larger), you divide.
    • Calculation: $3450 \text{ g} \div 1000 \text{ g/kg} = 3.45 \text{ kg}$.
    • Answer: 3.45 kg.

  2. Word Problem with Metric Conversion (Common Units First): A recipe calls for 750 mL of broth. You have 2 liters. How many milliliters of broth will remain?

    • Step 1: Convert to a common unit. Before subtracting, both quantities must be in the same unit. Convert liters to milliliters.
      • Conversion: 1 L = 1,000 mL.
      • Calculation: $2 \text{ L} \times 1,000 \text{ mL/L} = 2,000 \text{ mL}$.
    • Step 2: Perform the calculation.
      • Subtract the required amount from the total amount: $2,000 \text{ mL} - 750 \text{ mL} = 1,250 \text{ mL}$.
    • Answer: 1,250 mL will remain.

  3. Checkpoint Example: Convert 2.6 kilometers to meters.

    • Identify the conversion: 1 km = 1,000 m.
    • Moving from kilometers (larger) to meters (smaller), so multiply.
    • Calculation: $2.6 \text{ km} \times 1,000 \text{ m/km} = 2,600 \text{ m}$.
    • Answer: 2,600 m.

Importance of Metric Units in Science

It's important to note that in scientific courses and many professional fields globally, the metric system is the standard. Developing a strong familiarity with metric units and the ability to convert within them is an essential skill for future studies and careers.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast the customary and metric measurement systems. What are the main advantages and disadvantages of each system for general use and for scientific applications?
  2. Explain why conversions within the metric system are generally considered simpler than conversions within the customary system. Provide an example for each system to illustrate your point.
  3. Describe the general rule for determining whether to multiply or divide when converting between units within the same measurement system. Give an example for each case (multiplication and division).
  4. A construction worker needs 3.5 yards of electrical wire. If the wire is sold in 10-foot spools, how many spools should the worker buy, and how much extra wire (in feet) will they have? (Assume they can only buy full spools.)
  5. A chef is following a recipe that calls for 400 grams of sugar. If the chef has a bag of sugar that weighs 1.5 kilograms, how much sugar (in grams) will be left after making the recipe?
