Study Guide: Surface Area of Prisms and Cylinders

Overview

This section introduces the concept of surface area, which is the total area covering the exterior of a three-dimensional solid. It provides methods and formulas for calculating the surface area of various prisms, including rectangular and triangular prisms, as well as cylinders. Understanding these calculations is essential for practical applications like wrapping gifts or painting objects.

Key Concepts

  • Surface Area (SA): The total area of all the faces, or curved surfaces, of a three-dimensional solid. It represents the amount of material needed to cover the object entirely.
  • Prism: A three-dimensional solid characterized by two parallel and congruent polygonal bases, connected by rectangular lateral faces. Prisms are named according to the shape of their bases (e.g., rectangular prism, triangular prism).
  • Net: A two-dimensional pattern that can be folded to form a three-dimensional solid. Nets are valuable tools for visualizing all the individual faces of a solid and understanding how their areas combine to form the total surface area.
  • Rectangular Prism: A prism whose bases are rectangles. Its surface area is found by summing the areas of its six rectangular faces.
  • Triangular Prism: A prism whose bases are triangles. Its surface area is the sum of the areas of its two triangular bases and its three rectangular lateral faces.
  • Cylinder: A three-dimensional solid with two parallel and congruent circular bases connected by a curved lateral surface. When "unrolled," the curved lateral surface forms a rectangle.
  • Formulas for Surface Area:
    • Rectangular Prism: $SA = 2lw + 2lh + 2wh$, where $l$ is length, $w$ is width, and $h$ is height.
    • Cylinder: $SA = 2\pi r^2 + 2\pi rh$, where $r$ is the radius of the base and $h$ is the height of the cylinder.

Important Terms

  • Surface Area (SA): The sum of the areas of all the outer surfaces of a three-dimensional object.
  • Prism: A polyhedron with two identical, parallel faces (bases) and lateral faces that are rectangles.
  • Net: A flat pattern that can be folded along its edges to form a three-dimensional shape.
  • Bases: The two congruent, parallel polygonal faces of a prism or circular faces of a cylinder that define its shape.
  • Lateral Faces: The faces of a prism that are not its bases (usually rectangles). For a cylinder, it refers to the single curved surface between the two circular bases.
  • Length ($l$): One dimension of a rectangular prism, typically describing how long it is.
  • Width ($w$): One dimension of a rectangular prism, typically describing how wide it is.
  • Height ($h$): The perpendicular distance between the two bases of a prism or cylinder.
  • Radius ($r$): The distance from the center of a circular base to any point on its circumference.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Surface Area

Surface area is a measurement that tells us how much material it would take to completely cover the outside of a 3D object. Think of it like wrapping paper for a gift, paint for a wall, or fabric for a tent. It is always measured in square units (e.g., cm², in.²).

Prisms

A prism is a solid that has two identical (congruent) and parallel faces called bases. The other faces, which connect the bases, are called lateral faces and are always rectangles. Prisms are named by the shape of their bases. For example, a prism with triangular bases is a triangular prism, and one with rectangular bases is a rectangular prism.

Nets as a Visualization Tool: A net is a crucial concept for understanding surface area. It's like unfolding a 3D shape into a flat, 2D pattern. By laying out all the faces, a net makes it easy to see each individual face and calculate its area before summing them up for the total surface area.

Surface Area of a Rectangular Prism

A rectangular prism has six rectangular faces. If you imagine opening up a box, you'd see the top, bottom, front, back, and two side faces.

  • The top and bottom faces have dimensions length ($l$) and width ($w$), so their combined area is $2 \times (l \times w)$.
  • The front and back faces have dimensions length ($l$) and height ($h$), so their combined area is $2 \times (l \times h)$.
  • The two side faces have dimensions width ($w$) and height ($h$), so their combined area is $2 \times (w \times h)$.

By adding these together, we get the formula for the surface area of a rectangular prism: $SA = 2lw + 2lh + 2wh$

Example: Find the surface area of a rectangular prism with $l = 8$ cm, $w = 5$ cm, and $h = 3$ cm.

  1. Identify dimensions: $l=8$ cm, $w=5$ cm, $h=3$ cm.
  2. Apply the formula: $SA = 2(8)(5) + 2(8)(3) + 2(5)(3)$
  3. Calculate each pair of faces:
    • $2(8)(5) = 2(40) = 80$ cm² (top and bottom)
    • $2(8)(3) = 2(24) = 48$ cm² (front and back)
    • $2(5)(3) = 2(15) = 30$ cm² (two sides)
  4. Sum the areas: $SA = 80 + 48 + 30 = 158$ cm²

Surface Area of a Triangular Prism

A triangular prism has two congruent triangular bases and three rectangular lateral faces. To find its surface area, you calculate the area of each of these five faces and add them up.

Example: A triangular prism has a triangular base with base $6$ in., height $4$ in., and hypotenuse $7.2$ in. The prism is $10$ in. long (which is its height). Find its surface area.

  1. Calculate the area of the two triangular bases:
    • Area of one triangle $= \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} = \frac{1}{2} (6)(4) = 12$ in.²
    • Area of two triangles $= 2 \times 12 = 24$ in.²
  2. Calculate the area of the three rectangular lateral faces:
    • The "length" of the prism (distance between the triangular bases) is $10$ in. This acts as the width for all three rectangular faces.
    • One rectangular face will have dimensions $6$ in. (base of triangle) by $10$ in. (prism length). Area $= (6)(10) = 60$ in.²
    • Another rectangular face will have dimensions $4$ in. (height of triangle) by $10$ in. (prism length). Area $= (4)(10) = 40$ in.²
    • The third rectangular face will have dimensions $7.2$ in. (hypotenuse of triangle) by $10$ in. (prism length). Area $= (7.2)(10) = 72$ in.²
    • Total area of three rectangular faces $= 60 + 40 + 72 = 172$ in.²
  3. Sum the areas of the bases and lateral faces: $SA = 24 \text{ in.}^2 (\text{bases}) + 172 \text{ in.}^2 (\text{lateral faces}) = 196$ in.²

Cylinders

A cylinder is a solid with two parallel and congruent circular bases connected by a curved lateral surface. Imagine "unrolling" the curved lateral surface of a cylinder. It would flatten out into a rectangle.

  • The height of this rectangle is the same as the height ($h$) of the cylinder.
  • The length of this rectangle is the same as the circumference of the circular base. The circumference of a circle is given by $2\pi r$, where $r$ is the radius. So, the area of the lateral surface (the unrolled rectangle) is $2\pi r \times h$, or $2\pi rh$.

The cylinder also has two circular bases. The area of one circle is $\pi r^2$. Since there are two bases, their combined area is $2\pi r^2$.

Adding the areas of the two bases and the lateral surface gives the formula for the surface area of a cylinder: $SA = 2\pi r^2 + 2\pi rh$

Example (Application): A cylindrical canister has radius $3.5$ in. and height $8$ in. How much material is needed to cover the entire canister? Use $3.14$ for $\pi$.

  1. Identify dimensions: $r = 3.5$ in., $h = 8$ in.

  2. Apply the formula: $SA = 2\pi r^2 + 2\pi rh$ Substitute $3.14$ for $\pi$: $SA = 2(3.14)(3.5)^2 + 2(3.14)(3.5)(8)$

  3. Calculate the area of the bases ($2\pi r^2$): $2(3.14)(3.5)^2 = 2(3.14)(12.25) = 76.93$ in.²

  4. Calculate the area of the lateral surface ($2\pi rh$): $2(3.14)(3.5)(8) = 2(3.14)(28) = 175.84$ in.²

  5. Sum the areas: $SA = 76.93 + 175.84 = 252.77$ in.²

    (Note: If calculated in terms of $\pi$, $SA = 2(3.5)^2\pi + 2(3.5)(8)\pi = 2(12.25)\pi + 56\pi = 24.5\pi + 56\pi = 80.5\pi$ in.²)

General Approach to Surface Area

For any prism or cylinder, the general strategy for finding surface area involves:

  1. Identifying all the individual faces (or curved surfaces) that make up the solid's exterior.
  2. Calculating the area of each individual face/surface.
  3. Summing up all these individual areas to get the total surface area.

Review Questions

  1. What is surface area, and why is the concept of a "net" a helpful tool when calculating it for a three-dimensional solid?
  2. Explain how a prism is named and how its naming convention reflects its structural characteristics.
  3. A rectangular storage container has a length of $6$ meters, a width of $2.5$ meters, and a height of $4$ meters. Calculate the total surface area of the container that would need to be painted.
  4. Describe, in your own words, the steps you would take to find the surface area of a triangular prism. What distinct types of shapes do you need to calculate areas for?
  5. How is the formula for the lateral surface area of a cylinder ($2\pi rh$) derived? Explain the connection between the cylinder's dimensions and the dimensions of the rectangle formed when its curved surface is unrolled.
