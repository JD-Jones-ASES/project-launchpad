Study Guide: Probability of Simple and Compound Events

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concepts of probability, which quantifies the likelihood of an event occurring. It covers how to calculate the probability of simple events, the concept of complementary events, and how to determine probabilities for independent compound events using the multiplication rule. Understanding these concepts is essential for analyzing outcomes in various real-world scenarios.

Key Concepts

  • Probability of an Event ($P(E)$): A measure of how likely an event is to occur. It is calculated as the ratio of the number of favorable outcomes to the total number of equally likely outcomes.
    • Formula: $P(E) = \frac{\text{number of favorable outcomes}}{\text{total number of outcomes}}$
  • Probability Range: The probability of any event $E$ is always between 0 and 1, inclusive ($0 \le P(E) \le 1$).
    • $P(E) = 0$ means the event is impossible.
    • $P(E) = 1$ means the event is certain.
  • Complementary Events: Two events are complementary if one event occurring means the other does not occur. The sum of their probabilities is 1.
    • Complement Rule: $P(\text{not } E) = 1 - P(E)$.
  • Independent Events: Two events are independent if the outcome of one event does not influence or affect the outcome of the other event.
  • Multiplication Rule for Independent Events: If events $A$ and $B$ are independent, the probability that both $A$ and $B$ occur is the product of their individual probabilities.
    • Formula: $P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \times P(B)$. This rule only applies to independent events.

Important Terms

  • Probability of an Event: The numerical measure of the likelihood that a specific event will occur, expressed as a fraction, decimal, or percentage between 0 and 1.
  • Simple Event: An event consisting of a single outcome. For example, rolling a 3 on a die.
  • Compound Event: An event that combines two or more simple events. For example, rolling a 3 and flipping a coin to get tails.
  • Favorable Outcome: An outcome that satisfies the conditions of the specific event being considered.
  • Total Outcomes: The complete set of all possible results of a probability experiment.
  • Impossible Event: An event with a probability of 0, meaning it can never happen.
  • Certain Event: An event with a probability of 1, meaning it is guaranteed to happen.
  • Complement of an Event (not E or E'): All outcomes in the sample space that are not part of event $E$.
  • Independent Events: Events where the occurrence of one does not change the probability of the other occurring.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Probability

Probability is a branch of mathematics that quantifies uncertainty. It tells us how likely something is to happen. For instance, a weather forecast stating a 35% chance of rain uses probability.

Calculating Probability of a Simple Event

The most basic way to calculate probability assumes all possible outcomes are equally likely. The formula is: $P(E) = \frac{\text{Number of favorable outcomes}}{\text{Total number of outcomes}}$

  • Example 1: Rolling a Die

    • Problem: Find the probability of rolling a number greater than 4 on a standard six-sided die.
    • Favorable outcomes (numbers greater than 4): {5, 6}. There are 2 favorable outcomes.
    • Total outcomes (all possible rolls): {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}. There are 6 total outcomes.
    • Calculation: $P(\text{greater than 4}) = \frac{2}{6} = \frac{1}{3}$.

  • Example 2: Drawing a Card

    • Problem: What is the probability of drawing a heart from a standard 52-card deck?
    • Favorable outcomes (hearts): There are 13 hearts (Ace, 2, ..., 10, Jack, Queen, King of hearts).
    • Total outcomes (all cards in the deck): 52.
    • Calculation: $P(\text{heart}) = \frac{13}{52} = \frac{1}{4}$.

  • Checkpoint Example: Marbles in a Bag

    • A bag contains 3 red, 5 blue, and 2 green marbles. What is the probability of drawing a blue marble?
    • Total marbles = $3 + 5 + 2 = 10$.
    • Favorable outcomes (blue marbles) = 5.
    • Calculation: $P(\text{blue}) = \frac{5}{10} = \frac{1}{2}$.

Complementary Events

Sometimes it's easier to calculate the probability that an event doesn't happen and use that information to find the probability that it does happen.

  • Complement Rule: The probability that an event $E$ does not occur is $P(\text{not } E) = 1 - P(E)$.

    • This means that an event either happens or it doesn't, and the sum of these probabilities is 1 (or 100%).

  • Example: Probability of No Rain

    • Problem: The probability of rain tomorrow is 0.35. What is the probability that it does not rain?
    • Using the complement rule: $P(\text{no rain}) = 1 - P(\text{rain})$.
    • Calculation: $P(\text{no rain}) = 1 - 0.35 = 0.65$.

Independent Compound Events

Compound events involve two or more simple events. If the outcome of one event does not affect the outcome of another, they are called independent events.

  • Definition of Independent Events: Events $A$ and $B$ are independent if the occurrence of $A$ does not change the probability of $B$, and vice versa. For example, flipping a coin and then rolling a die are independent events because the coin flip's result has no bearing on the die roll's result.

  • Multiplication Rule for Independent Events: To find the probability that two independent events $A$ and $B$ both occur, you multiply their individual probabilities: $P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \times P(B)$.

  • Example 1: Flipping Two Coins

    • Problem: A fair coin is flipped twice. What is the probability of getting heads both times?
    • Let $A$ be the event of getting heads on the first flip. $P(A) = \frac{1}{2}$.
    • Let $B$ be the event of getting heads on the second flip. $P(B) = \frac{1}{2}$.
    • Since the flips are independent: $P(\text{heads and heads}) = P(A) \times P(B) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4}$.

  • Example 2: Spinner and Coin

    • A spinner has 3 equal sections (red, blue, green). A coin has two sides (H, T). You spin the spinner and flip the coin.
    • Total possible outcomes (using a tree diagram): RH, RT, BH, BT, GH, GT. There are $3 \times 2 = 6$ equally likely outcomes.
    • (a) Probability of (red and heads)
      • $P(\text{red}) = \frac{1}{3}$ (1 red section out of 3 total).
      • $P(\text{heads}) = \frac{1}{2}$ (1 head out of 2 total).
      • $P(\text{red and heads}) = P(\text{red}) \times P(\text{heads}) = \frac{1}{3} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{6}$.
    • (b) Probability of (blue or green, and tails)
      • First, find $P(\text{blue or green})$. There are 2 favorable outcomes (blue, green) out of 3 total. So, $P(\text{blue or green}) = \frac{2}{3}$.
      • $P(\text{tails}) = \frac{1}{2}$.
      • $P(\text{blue or green, and tails}) = P(\text{blue or green}) \times P(\text{tails}) = \frac{2}{3} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{2}{6} = \frac{1}{3}$.

  • Checkpoint Example: Die Roll and Coin Flip

    • A die is rolled and a coin is flipped. What is the probability of rolling a 3 and getting tails?
    • $P(\text{rolling a 3}) = \frac{1}{6}$ (1 favorable outcome out of 6 total).
    • $P(\text{getting tails}) = \frac{1}{2}$ (1 favorable outcome out of 2 total).
    • Since these are independent events: $P(\text{rolling a 3 and getting tails}) = \frac{1}{6} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{12}$.

Important Caution

Remember that the multiplication rule, $P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \times P(B)$, is only for independent events. If the outcome of one event affects the probability of the other (e.g., drawing two cards from a deck without replacement), then the events are dependent, and a different method (involving conditional probability) is required.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words what probability measures and why it's always a value between 0 and 1. Provide an example of an event with a probability of 0 and an event with a probability of 1.
  2. A bag contains 4 red, 6 blue, and 10 yellow marbles.
    • a) What is the probability of drawing a red marble?
    • b) What is the probability of drawing a marble that is not yellow? Explain how you used the complement rule.
  3. Define "independent events." Give two examples of real-world independent events and two examples of events that would likely be dependent.
  4. You roll a standard six-sided die and then flip a fair coin.
    • a) What is the probability of rolling an even number and flipping a tail?
    • b) What is the probability of rolling a number less than 3 and flipping a head?
    • c) Draw a simple tree diagram to show all possible outcomes for this compound event.
  5. Why is it crucial to identify whether events are independent or dependent before calculating their combined probability? What could happen if you incorrectly assume independence?
