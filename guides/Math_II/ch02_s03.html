Study Guide: Operations with Rational Numbers

Overview

This section reviews and deepens your understanding of the four basic arithmetic operations—addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division—when applied to rational numbers, including fractions, mixed numbers, and decimals. The focus is on developing proficiency in multi-step problems that often combine these different forms of rational numbers, requiring strategic conversion and application of the order of operations.

Key Concepts

  • Fraction Operations:
    • Addition/Subtraction: To add or subtract fractions, a common denominator (preferably the Least Common Denominator, LCD) is required. Once found, convert fractions, then add/subtract numerators.
    • Multiplication: Multiply numerators together and denominators together. Simplify the resulting fraction.
    • Division: To divide by a fraction, multiply by its reciprocal (flip the second fraction).
  • Operations with Mixed Numbers: Mixed numbers (a whole number and a fraction) can be converted into improper fractions (numerator greater than or equal to the denominator) before performing operations, or the whole and fractional parts can be handled separately (though conversion often simplifies complex operations, especially subtraction).
  • Decimal Operations: Standard arithmetic operations apply. For multi-step problems, the order of operations (Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication and Division (from left to right), Addition and Subtraction (from left to right)) is crucial.
  • Combining Fractions and Decimals: When an expression contains both fractions and decimals, it is necessary to convert all numbers to a single consistent form (either all fractions or all decimals) before performing calculations. Converting to fractions is often more precise, while converting to decimals can sometimes be faster depending on the numbers involved.
  • Real-World Application: Rational number operations are fundamental for solving practical problems, such as those found in recipes, measurements, and financial calculations.

Important Terms

  • Rational Number: Any number that can be expressed as a fraction $\frac{a}{b}$, where $a$ and $b$ are integers and $b \neq 0$. This includes integers, fractions, mixed numbers, and terminating or repeating decimals.
  • Least Common Denominator (LCD): The smallest common multiple of the denominators of two or more fractions. It is essential for adding and subtracting fractions.
  • Improper Fraction: A fraction where the absolute value of the numerator is greater than or equal to the absolute value of the denominator (e.g., $\frac{5}{2}$).
  • Mixed Number: A number consisting of an integer and a proper fraction (e.g., $2\frac{1}{2}$).
  • Reciprocal: The multiplicative inverse of a number. For a fraction $\frac{a}{b}$, its reciprocal is $\frac{b}{a}$. Multiplying a number by its reciprocal always equals 1.
  • Order of Operations: A set of rules that dictate the sequence in which mathematical operations should be performed to ensure a consistent result (Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication and Division (from left to right), Addition and Subtraction (from left to right)).

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Operations with Rational Numbers

This section serves as a review and expansion of basic arithmetic skills with fractions and decimals, emphasizing how to approach problems that involve multiple operations and different forms of rational numbers. Mastery of these operations is vital for success in higher-level mathematics and practical problem-solving.

Review of Fraction Operations

Let's revisit the fundamental rules for working with fractions:

  • Addition and Subtraction:

    • To add or subtract $\frac{a}{b}$ and $\frac{c}{d}$, you must first find a common denominator. The formula $\frac{a}{b} \pm \frac{c}{d} = \frac{ad \pm bc}{bd}$ provides a general method, but it's often more efficient to use the Least Common Denominator (LCD).
    • Example: To compute $5/6 - 3/8$:
      • Find the LCD of 6 and 8, which is 24.
      • Convert both fractions: $\frac{5}{6} = \frac{5 \times 4}{6 \times 4} = \frac{20}{24}$ and $\frac{3}{8} = \frac{3 \times 3}{8 \times 3} = \frac{9}{24}$.
      • Subtract: $\frac{20}{24} - \frac{9}{24} = \frac{11}{24}$.
    • Checkpoint Example: $2/3 + 5/12$.
      • LCD of 3 and 12 is 12.
      • $\frac{2}{3} = \frac{2 \times 4}{3 \times 4} = \frac{8}{12}$.
      • $\frac{8}{12} + \frac{5}{12} = \frac{13}{12}$.
      • Convert to a mixed number: $1\frac{1}{12}$.

  • Multiplication:

    • To multiply $\frac{a}{b}$ by $\frac{c}{d}$, simply multiply the numerators together and the denominators together: $\frac{a}{b} \times \frac{c}{d} = \frac{ac}{bd}$. Always simplify your final answer.
    • Example (a): $3/4 \times 10/9$.
      • Multiply directly: $\frac{3 \times 10}{4 \times 9} = \frac{30}{36}$.
      • Simplify by dividing numerator and denominator by their greatest common divisor (6): $\frac{30 \div 6}{36 \div 6} = \frac{5}{6}$.
      • Alternative (pre-simplification): Cross-cancel common factors before multiplying. $3$ and $9$ share a factor of $3$; $4$ and $10$ share a factor of $2$. $\frac{\cancel{3}^1}{_2\cancel{4}} \times \frac{\cancel{10}^5}{\cancel{9}_3} = \frac{1 \times 5}{2 \times 3} = \frac{5}{6}$.

  • Division:

    • To divide $\frac{a}{b}$ by $\frac{c}{d}$ (where $c \neq 0$), you multiply the first fraction by the reciprocal of the second fraction: $\frac{a}{b} \div \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a}{b} \times \frac{d}{c}$.
    • Example (b): $5/6 \div 15/8$.
      • Multiply by the reciprocal of $15/8$, which is $8/15$: $\frac{5}{6} \times \frac{8}{15}$.
      • Multiply directly: $\frac{5 \times 8}{6 \times 15} = \frac{40}{90}$.
      • Simplify: $\frac{40 \div 10}{90 \div 10} = \frac{4}{9}$.
      • Alternative (pre-simplification): Cross-cancel $5$ and $15$ by $5$; $6$ and $8$ by $2$. $\frac{\cancel{5}^1}{_3\cancel{6}} \times \frac{\cancel{8}^4}{\cancel{15}_3} = \frac{1 \times 4}{3 \times 3} = \frac{4}{9}$.

Operations with Mixed Numbers

When computing with mixed numbers, the most straightforward approach is often to convert them into improper fractions first. This eliminates the need to handle whole number and fractional parts separately, especially simplifying subtraction.

  • Converting Mixed to Improper Fractions: Multiply the whole number by the denominator, add the numerator, and place the result over the original denominator. For example, $5\frac{1}{3} = \frac{(5 \times 3) + 1}{3} = \frac{16}{3}$.
  • Example: Subtracting Mixed Numbers: Compute $5\frac{1}{3} - 2\frac{3}{4}$.
    • Convert to improper fractions: $5\frac{1}{3} = \frac{16}{3}$ and $2\frac{3}{4} = \frac{11}{4}$.
    • Find the LCD of 3 and 4, which is 12.
    • Convert fractions: $\frac{16}{3} = \frac{16 \times 4}{3 \times 4} = \frac{64}{12}$ and $\frac{11}{4} = \frac{11 \times 3}{4 \times 3} = \frac{33}{12}$.
    • Subtract: $\frac{64}{12} - \frac{33}{12} = \frac{31}{12}$.
    • Convert back to a mixed number: $\frac{31}{12} = 2\frac{7}{12}$.
  • Caution: When subtracting mixed numbers without converting to improper fractions, if the fractional part of the first number is smaller than the fractional part of the second, you must "borrow" from the whole number part. Converting to improper fractions completely bypasses this potential point of error.

Operations with Decimals

Decimal operations follow standard arithmetic rules, with careful attention to place value. Multi-step problems require strict adherence to the order of operations.

  • Example: Multi-Step Decimal Computation: Evaluate $3.6 \div 0.25 + 1.8 \times 0.4$.
    • Follow the order of operations: Perform multiplication and division first, from left to right, then addition.
    • Division: $3.6 \div 0.25 = 14.4$. (You can think of this as $360 \div 25$ by moving the decimal two places to the right in both numbers).
    • Multiplication: $1.8 \times 0.4 = 0.72$.
    • Addition: $14.4 + 0.72 = 15.12$. (Align decimal points for addition/subtraction).
  • Checkpoint Example: Compute $4.2 - 1.75$.

Combining Fractions and Decimals

When an expression involves both fractions and decimals, you must choose a consistent format for all numbers before performing calculations.

  • Conversion Strategy:
    • To Fractions: This is often preferred for precision, especially with repeating decimals or when the problem asks for a fractional answer. To convert a terminating decimal to a fraction, write the decimal part over a power of 10 and simplify (e.g., $0.6 = \frac{6}{10} = \frac{3}{5}$).
    • To Decimals: This can be faster if the fractions convert easily to terminating decimals (e.g., $3/4 = 0.75$) and the problem allows for a decimal answer.
  • Example: Combining Fractions and Decimals: Evaluate $3/4 + 0.6 - 1/5$.
    • Convert all terms to fractions.
      • $3/4$ (already a fraction).
      • $0.6 = 6/10 = 3/5$.
      • $1/5$ (already a fraction).
    • Now the expression is $\frac{3}{4} + \frac{3}{5} - \frac{1}{5}$.
    • Find the LCD for 4 and 5, which is 20.
    • Convert fractions:
      • $\frac{3}{4} = \frac{3 \times 5}{4 \times 5} = \frac{15}{20}$.
      • $\frac{3}{5} = \frac{3 \times 4}{5 \times 4} = \frac{12}{20}$.
      • $\frac{1}{5} = \frac{1 \times 4}{5 \times 4} = \frac{4}{20}$.
    • Perform operations: $\frac{15}{20} + \frac{12}{20} - \frac{4}{20} = \frac{27}{20} - \frac{4}{20} = \frac{23}{20}$.
    • Convert to a mixed number: $1\frac{3}{20}$.
  • Example: Word Problem with Mixed Operations: A recipe calls for $2\frac{1}{2}$ cups of flour. Maya wants to make $3/4$ of the recipe. How much flour does she need?
    • This is a multiplication problem: "3/4 of" implies multiplication.
    • Convert $2\frac{1}{2}$ to an improper fraction: $2\frac{1}{2} = \frac{(2 \times 2) + 1}{2} = \frac{5}{2}$.
    • Multiply: $\frac{3}{4} \times \frac{5}{2} = \frac{3 \times 5}{4 \times 2} = \frac{15}{8}$.
    • Convert back to a mixed number for context: $\frac{15}{8} = 1\frac{7}{8}$ cups.
  • Checkpoint Example: Evaluate $2/3 \times 1.5$.
    • Convert $1.5$ to a fraction: $1.5 = 1\frac{5}{10} = 1\frac{1}{2} = \frac{3}{2}$.
    • Multiply: $\frac{2}{3} \times \frac{3}{2}$.
    • Cross-cancel the 2s and 3s: $\frac{\cancel{2}^1}{\cancel{3}_1} \times \frac{\cancel{3}^1}{\cancel{2}_1} = \frac{1 \times 1}{1 \times 1} = 1$.

Review Questions

  1. Explain why finding a Least Common Denominator (LCD) is crucial for adding and subtracting fractions, but not for multiplying or dividing them.
  2. Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of converting mixed numbers to improper fractions versus working with their whole and fractional parts separately during calculations. In what scenarios might one method be preferred over the other?
  3. When faced with an expression containing both fractions and decimals, how do you decide whether to convert all numbers to fractions or all to decimals? Provide examples of types of numbers or problem contexts where each conversion strategy would be more advantageous.
  4. Describe the steps involved in evaluating a multi-step expression that combines different operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division) and different forms of rational numbers (fractions, mixed numbers, decimals). Why is following a specific order important?
  5. Create a word problem that requires at least three different arithmetic operations (e.g., multiplication, then addition, then subtraction) involving at least one fraction, one mixed number, and one decimal. Show the step-by-step solution to your problem.
