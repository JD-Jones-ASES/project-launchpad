Study Guide: Similar Triangles

Overview

This section introduces the concept of similar figures in geometry, focusing specifically on triangles. Two figures are similar if they have the same shape but not necessarily the same size. The core idea is that similar triangles have congruent corresponding angles and proportional corresponding sides. A key shortcut for proving triangle similarity, the Angle-Angle (AA) Similarity Postulate, is also presented, stating that only two pairs of congruent corresponding angles are sufficient to establish similarity.

Key Concepts

  • Similar Figures: Geometric figures that have the exact same shape but can differ in size. This means their corresponding angles are congruent, and their corresponding sides are proportional.
  • Corresponding Parts: In similar figures, the angles and sides that are in the same relative position are called corresponding parts. Matching angles are corresponding angles, and matching sides are corresponding sides.
  • Proportional Sides: When two figures are similar, the ratio of the lengths of any pair of corresponding sides is constant. This constant ratio is known as the scale factor.
  • Angle-Angle (AA) Similarity Postulate: A fundamental theorem that allows you to determine if two triangles are similar by only comparing two pairs of angles. If two angles of one triangle are congruent to two angles of another triangle, then the triangles are similar. This is because the third pair of angles must also be congruent since the sum of angles in any triangle is 180 degrees.

Important Terms

  • Similar Figures: Figures that have the same shape but not necessarily the same size. Denoted by the symbol $\sim$.
  • Corresponding Angles: Angles in the same relative position in two different figures (especially similar figures).
  • Corresponding Sides: Sides in the same relative position in two different figures (especially similar figures), which are opposite corresponding angles.
  • Proportional: Two quantities are proportional if they have a constant ratio. In similar figures, corresponding sides are proportional.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Similar Triangles

In geometry, "similar" means figures that share the same shape, even if they are different sizes. Think of enlarging or shrinking a photo; the image changes size, but its overall form remains the same. This section helps us understand when triangles are similar and how to prove it.

Definition of Similar Figures

For any two figures to be similar, they must satisfy two conditions:

  1. Their corresponding angles are congruent. This means that each angle in one figure has a matching angle in the other figure that has the same measure.
  2. Their corresponding sides are proportional. This means that the ratio of the length of any side in the first figure to the length of its corresponding side in the second figure is constant.

Identifying Corresponding Parts

When triangles are named to indicate similarity, the order of the letters is crucial. For example, if we state that $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF$:

  • The first letters correspond: Angle A corresponds to Angle D ($A \cong D$).
  • The second letters correspond: Angle B corresponds to Angle E ($B \cong E$).
  • The third letters correspond: Angle C corresponds to Angle F ($C \cong F$).

Once you know which angles correspond, you can identify the corresponding sides. A side is corresponding to the side opposite its corresponding angle.

  • Side BC is opposite angle A, and side EF is opposite angle D. So, $BC \sim EF$.
  • Side AC is opposite angle B, and side DF is opposite angle E. So, $AC \sim DF$.
  • Side AB is opposite angle C, and side DE is opposite angle F. So, $AB \sim DE$.

Example: Identifying Corresponding Parts If $\triangle RST \sim \triangle XYZ$:

  • Corresponding angles: $R \cong X$, $S \cong Y$, $T \cong Z$.
  • Corresponding sides: $RS \sim XY$, $ST \sim YZ$, $RT \sim XZ$.

The Angle-Angle (AA) Similarity Postulate

One of the most powerful tools for proving triangle similarity is the AA Similarity Postulate. Statement: If two angles of one triangle are congruent to two angles of another triangle, then the triangles are similar.

Why does this work? Remember that the sum of the interior angles in any triangle is always $180^\circ$. If you know two pairs of corresponding angles are congruent, then the third pair must also be congruent. For instance, if $\angle A \cong \angle D$ and $\angle B \cong \angle E$, then: $m\angle C = 180^\circ - m\angle A - m\angle B$ $m\angle F = 180^\circ - m\angle D - m\angle E$ Since $m\angle A = m\angle D$ and $m\angle B = m\angle E$, it follows that $m\angle C = m\angle F$. Therefore, all three pairs of corresponding angles are congruent, fulfilling one part of the similarity definition.

Applying AA Similarity

Example 1: Using AA Similarity with given angles Given: $\triangle ABC$ has $m\angle A = 50^\circ$ and $m\angle B = 60^\circ$. Given: $\triangle DEF$ has $m\angle D = 50^\circ$ and $m\angle F = 70^\circ$. Are the triangles similar?

Solution:

  1. Find missing angles:
    • In $\triangle ABC$: $m\angle C = 180^\circ - 50^\circ - 60^\circ = 70^\circ$.
    • In $\triangle DEF$: $m\angle E = 180^\circ - 50^\circ - 70^\circ = 60^\circ$.
  2. Compare angles:
    • $m\angle A = 50^\circ$ and $m\angle D = 50^\circ$ (Congruent).
    • $m\angle B = 60^\circ$ and $m\angle E = 60^\circ$ (Congruent).
    • $m\angle C = 70^\circ$ and $m\angle F = 70^\circ$ (Congruent, as expected). Since two pairs of angles ($A \cong D$ and $B \cong E$) are congruent, by AA Similarity, $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF$.

Checkpoint: Triangle $GHI$ has angles $35^\circ$, $90^\circ$, $55^\circ$. Triangle $JKL$ has angles $55^\circ$, $90^\circ$, $35^\circ$. Are they similar? Answer: Yes. Both triangles have angles of $35^\circ$, $55^\circ$, and $90^\circ$. Since two pairs of corresponding angles are congruent (e.g., $35^\circ$ and $90^\circ$ from one triangle match $35^\circ$ and $90^\circ$ from the other), the triangles are similar by AA Similarity.

Example 2: Similarity in the Real World (Surveyor Problem) A surveyor sights the top of a building ($B$) from point $P$, noting $\angle BPC = 40^\circ$. An assistant at closer point $Q$ measures $\angle BQC = 40^\circ$. Point $C$ is the base of the building, and the building is vertical (meaning $\angle C$ in both $\triangle BPC$ and $\triangle BQC$ is a right angle). Explain why $\triangle BPC \sim \triangle BQC$.

Solution:

  1. Identify congruent angles:
    • Both triangles share a common angle, $\angle B$. So, $\angle B \cong \angle B$.
    • Both triangles have a right angle at $C$ (building is vertical). So, $\angle BCP \cong \angle BCQ$ ($= 90^\circ$).
  2. Apply AA Similarity: Since two pairs of angles are congruent (the shared $\angle B$ and the right angles at $C$), $\triangle BPC \sim \triangle BQC$ by AA Similarity. (Note: The textbook example implies B is the vertex and C is the base, so the problem setup as BPC = 40 and BQC = 40 implies C is the common vertex for these angles, not B. The provided solution refers to "shared B" and "right angle at C", which means we need to reinterpret. Assuming B is the angle at the top of the building, and C is the common base. Let's stick to the textbook's solution logic: "Both triangles have a right angle at C" and "Both triangles contain $B$ (shared angle)". This means the surveyor problem might be poorly phrased in the textbook, but the logic of AA similarity from a shared angle and a right angle remains valid.)

Example 3: Determining if Triangles Are Similar (Non-similar case) $\triangle MNO$ has angles $45^\circ$, $65^\circ$, and $70^\circ$. $\triangle PQR$ has angles $45^\circ$, $55^\circ$, and $80^\circ$. Are the triangles similar?

Solution:

  1. Compare angles:
    • One pair matches: $45^\circ \cong 45^\circ$.
    • The remaining angles are $65^\circ, 70^\circ$ for $\triangle MNO$ and $55^\circ, 80^\circ$ for $\triangle PQR$. These do not match. Since only one pair of angles is congruent, and not two, the triangles are not similar.

Caution!

It's tempting to assume triangles are similar just because they "look alike" or seem to have similar proportions. However, geometric similarity must be proven. Always verify that at least two pairs of corresponding angles are congruent (using the AA criterion) or that the full definition of similarity (congruent angles AND proportional sides) is met.
