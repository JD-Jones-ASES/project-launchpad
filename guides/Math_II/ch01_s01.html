Study Guide: Powers and Exponent Notation

Overview

This section introduces the concept of exponent notation as a shorthand for repeated multiplication, similar to how multiplication simplifies repeated addition. It defines key terms like base and exponent, explores how to evaluate powers, especially when dealing with negative bases and fractions, and integrates exponents into the established order of operations. The goal is to provide a fundamental understanding of exponents as a building block for more advanced topics.

Key Concepts

  • Exponents as Repeated Multiplication: An exponent indicates how many times a base number is multiplied by itself. This provides a compact way to write expressions like $3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3$ as $3^4$.
  • Base and Exponent: In the expression $a^n$, $a$ is the base (the number being multiplied), and $n$ is the exponent (or power), which tells you how many times to use the base as a factor.
  • Special Exponent Names: An exponent of 2 ($a^2$) is read as "a squared," and an exponent of 3 ($a^3$) is read as "a cubed."
  • Perfect Squares and Cubes: A perfect square is a whole number that results from squaring another whole number ($n^2$). A perfect cube is an integer that results from cubing another integer ($n^3$).
  • Negative Bases and Parentheses: The placement of parentheses is crucial when evaluating powers with negative bases.
    • $(-a)^n$: The entire negative number $-a$ is the base and is raised to the power $n$. The negative sign is included in the repeated multiplication.
    • $-a^n$: Only the number $a$ is raised to the power $n$, and then the result is negated. The negative sign is outside the operation of exponentiation.
  • Sign of Results with Negative Bases:
    • A negative base raised to an even exponent will always result in a positive number.
    • A negative base raised to an odd exponent will always result in a negative number.
  • Exponents in the Order of Operations (PEMDAS): Exponents are evaluated after operations within parentheses/grouping symbols but before multiplication, division, addition, and subtraction.

Important Terms

  • Exponent: A number that tells how many times a base is used as a factor in repeated multiplication. In $a^n$, $n$ is the exponent. Also referred to as a "power."
  • Base: The number being multiplied by itself in an exponential expression. In $a^n$, $a$ is the base.
  • Power: Another term for an exponent, or the result of raising a base to an exponent (e.g., "$3^4$" is read as "three to the fourth power").
  • Perfect Square: Any whole number that can be expressed as $n^2$ for some whole number $n$ (e.g., $0, 1, 4, 9, \ldots$).
  • Perfect Cube: Any integer that can be expressed as $n^3$ for some integer $n$ (e.g., $1, 8, 27, \ldots$ for positive cubes).

Detailed Notes

1. Introduction to Exponent Notation

Just as multiplication is a shortcut for repeated addition ($3+3+3+3 = 4 \times 3$), exponents provide a shortcut for repeated multiplication. An expression like $3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3$ can be written concisely as $3^4$.

2. Anatomy of an Exponential Expression

In the general expression $a^n$:

  • $a$ is the base.
  • $n$ is the exponent (or power).
  • It means $a$ is multiplied by itself $n$ times: $a^n = \underbrace{a \times a \times a \times \ldots \times a}_{n \text{ factors}}$.

Examples:

  • $2^5 = 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 = 32$. This is read as "two to the fifth power."
  • $a^2$ is read as "$a$ squared."
  • $a^3$ is read as "$a$ cubed."

3. Special Exponent Values: Perfect Squares and Cubes

  • Perfect Square: A whole number that can be obtained by squaring another whole number.
    • Examples: $0^2=0$, $1^2=1$, $2^2=4$, $3^2=9$, $4^2=16$, $5^2=25$, $6^2=36$, $7^2=49$, $8^2=64$, $9^2=81$, $10^2=100$, $11^2=121$, $12^2=144$.
  • Perfect Cube: An integer that can be obtained by cubing another integer.
    • Examples (positive): $1^3=1$, $2^3=8$, $3^3=27$, $4^3=64$, $5^3=125$, $6^3=216$. (Note: Negative integers can also form perfect cubes, e.g., $(-2)^3 = -8$).

4. Evaluating Powers with Integer Bases

Positive Bases: When the base is a positive integer, the evaluation is straightforward multiplication.

  • Example: $4^3 = 4 \times 4 \times 4 = 64$.

Negative Bases: The Importance of Parentheses This is a common point of confusion. Pay close attention to whether the negative sign is inside or outside the parentheses.

  • Case 1: $(-a)^n$ (Negative sign inside parentheses)

    • The entire value $-a$ is the base.
    • The negative sign is included in the repeated multiplication.
    • Example: $(-3)^2 = (-3) \times (-3) = 9$ (A negative times a negative is a positive).
    • Example: $(-2)^3 = (-2) \times (-2) \times (-2) = (4) \times (-2) = -8$ (Three negative factors result in a negative product).
    • Example: $(-2)^4 = (-2) \times (-2) \times (-2) \times (-2) = (4) \times (-2) \times (-2) = (-8) \times (-2) = 16$ (Four negative factors result in a positive product).

  • Case 2: $-a^n$ (Negative sign outside parentheses)

    • Only the number $a$ is the base.
    • The exponent $n$ applies only to $a$. After calculating $a^n$, the result is then negated. This is equivalent to $-(a^n)$.
    • Example: $-3^2 = -(3^2) = -(3 \times 3) = -(9) = -9$.

Summary for Negative Bases:

  • If a negative base is raised to an even exponent, the result is positive. (e.g., $(-2)^4 = 16$)
  • If a negative base is raised to an odd exponent, the result is negative. (e.g., $(-2)^3 = -8$)

Checkpoint Examples:

  • Evaluate: (a) $(-5)^2$ (b) $-5^2$ (c) $(-1)^7$
  • Solution:
    • (a) $(-5)^2 = (-5) \times (-5) = 25$
    • (b) $-5^2 = -(5 \times 5) = -25$
    • (c) $(-1)^7 = (-1) \times (-1) \times (-1) \times (-1) \times (-1) \times (-1) \times (-1) = -1$ (An odd number of negative factors results in a negative product).

5. Evaluating Powers of Fractions

When the base is a fraction, the exponent applies to both the numerator and the denominator.

  • Example: Evaluate $(2/3)^3$.
    • $(2/3)^3 = (2/3) \times (2/3) \times (2/3)$
    • $= \frac{2 \times 2 \times 2}{3 \times 3 \times 3}$
    • $= \frac{8}{27}$

6. Exponents in the Order of Operations

Recall the order of operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS):

  1. Parentheses / Brackets
  2. Exponents / Orders
  3. Multiplication and Division (from left to right)
  4. Addition and Subtraction (from left to right)

Exponents are evaluated after any operations inside parentheses and before multiplication or division.

Example 1: Exponents in Multi-Step Expressions Evaluate $3 \times 2^4 - 5^2$.

  1. Evaluate exponents: $2^4 = 16$ and $5^2 = 25$. $3 \times 16 - 25$
  2. Perform multiplication: $3 \times 16 = 48$. $48 - 25$
  3. Perform subtraction: $48 - 25 = 23$. Result: $23$

Example 2: Word Problem with Exponents (Area Application) A square patio has a side length of $12$ feet. A smaller square flower bed with a side length of $4$ feet is cut out of one corner. What is the area of the remaining patio?

The area of a square with side $s$ is given by $s^2$.

  • Area of patio = $12^2$
  • Area of flower bed = $4^2$
  • Remaining Area = Patio Area - Flower Bed Area $= 12^2 - 4^2$ $= 144 - 16$ $= 128 \text{ ft}^2$

Checkpoint Example:

  • Evaluate $4^3 + 2 \times 3^2$.
  • Solution:
    1. Evaluate exponents: $4^3 = 4 \times 4 \times 4 = 64$ and $3^2 = 3 \times 3 = 9$. $64 + 2 \times 9$
    2. Perform multiplication: $2 \times 9 = 18$. $64 + 18$
    3. Perform addition: $64 + 18 = 82$. Result: $82$

Review Questions

  1. Explain the relationship between repeated addition and multiplication, and then draw a parallel to the relationship between repeated multiplication and exponents. Provide an example for each.
  2. Distinguish clearly between a "base" and an "exponent" in an exponential expression like $7^5$. What does each part tell you?
  3. Why is it critical to pay attention to parentheses when evaluating expressions with negative bases, such as $(-4)^2$ versus $-4^2$? Explain the calculation for both and the resulting values.
  4. Describe the pattern for the sign (positive or negative) of the result when a negative base is raised to an even exponent versus an odd exponent. Provide two examples to illustrate your explanation.
  5. Create a multi-step expression involving exponents, multiplication, and addition/subtraction. Then, walk through the steps to evaluate it, explicitly stating which operation you perform at each stage according to the order of operations.
