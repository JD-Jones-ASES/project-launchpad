Study Guide: Solving Equations with Fractions and Decimals

Overview

This section introduces an efficient method for solving algebraic equations that contain fractions or decimals. The core strategy involves transforming these equations into simpler forms with integer coefficients by multiplying every term by a carefully chosen number. This "clearing" process makes the arithmetic much easier, allowing students to solve the resulting integer equations using familiar algebraic techniques.

Key Concepts

  • The Clearing Strategy: The primary method for solving equations with fractions or decimals is to eliminate them. This is achieved by multiplying every term on both sides of the equation by a specific number (the LCD for fractions, or a power of 10 for decimals). The transformed equation is equivalent to the original but has only integer coefficients, simplifying the solving process.
  • Clearing Fractions: To remove fractions from an equation, identify the Least Common Denominator (LCD) of all fractions present. Multiply every single term in the equation by this LCD. This action cancels out all denominators, converting the equation into one with integer coefficients.
  • Clearing Decimals: To remove decimals from an equation, identify the term with the greatest number of decimal places. Then, multiply every single term in the equation by the smallest power of 10 (e.g., 10, 100, 1000) required to shift all decimal points to the right, making every decimal coefficient an integer.
  • Equivalence Principle: Multiplying both sides of an equation by the same non-zero number (such as the LCD or a power of 10) results in an equivalent equation. This means the new equation has the exact same solution(s) as the original equation.
  • Simplification and Solving: After clearing fractions or decimals, the resulting equation with integer coefficients can be solved using standard algebraic steps: combining like terms, isolating the variable, and performing inverse operations.

Important Terms

  • Least Common Denominator (LCD): For a set of fractions, this is the smallest positive integer that is a multiple of all the denominators. It is the key to efficiently clearing fractions from an equation.
  • Power of 10: Numbers like 10, 100, 1000, and so on. These are used in decimal clearing to shift decimal points to the right, converting decimal numbers into integers.
  • Coefficient: A numerical factor that multiplies a variable (e.g., in $5x$, 5 is the coefficient). Clearing fractions or decimals aims to make these coefficients integers.
  • Term: A single number, a variable, or a product of numbers and variables within an expression or equation. Terms are typically separated by addition or subtraction signs. The clearing strategy requires multiplying every single term.
  • Equivalent Equation: An equation that has the exact same solution set as another equation, even if they look different. Clearing fractions or decimals creates equivalent equations.

Detailed Notes

The Core Strategy: Simplifying Complex Equations

Equations containing fractions or decimals can often appear intimidating. However, this section introduces a fundamental trick: you can eliminate these fractions or decimals by multiplying every single term in the equation by a carefully chosen number. This transforms the original equation into an equivalent one that only contains integer coefficients, which are significantly easier to manipulate and solve.

Clearing Fractions

To clear fractions from an equation, the goal is to find a number that, when multiplied by each fractional term, will cancel out its denominator.

Property: Clearing Fractions with the LCD To clear fractions from an equation, multiply every term on both sides of the equation by the Least Common Denominator (LCD) of all the fractions.

Step-by-Step Process for Clearing Fractions:

  1. Find the LCD: Identify all denominators in the equation and determine their LCD.
  2. Multiply Every Term: Carefully multiply each and every term on both sides of the equation by the LCD. It's crucial not to miss any terms, including those that aren't fractions or are on the opposite side of the equals sign.
  3. Simplify: Perform the multiplication. All denominators should now cancel out, leaving you with an equation composed entirely of integers.
  4. Solve: Use standard algebraic methods (combining like terms, isolating the variable) to solve the resulting integer equation.
  5. Check: Substitute your solution back into the original equation to verify it is correct.

Example 1: Basic Fraction Clearing Solve $x/3 + x/4 = 7$.

  • Denominators: 3 and 4.
  • LCD: The LCD of 3 and 4 is 12.
  • Multiply every term by 12: $12(x/3) + 12(x/4) = 12(7)$
  • Simplify: $4x + 3x = 84$
  • Combine like terms: $7x = 84$
  • Divide to solve for $x$: $x = 84 / 7$ $x = 12$
  • Check: $12/3 + 12/4 = 4 + 3 = 7$. The solution is correct.

Example 2: Fractions on Both Sides (and implied structure) Solve $(2x - 1)/3 = (x + 4)/6$. (Note: The original textbook's formatting 2x - 13 = x + 46 implies these terms are fractions like (2x - 1)/3 and (x + 4)/6 based on the provided solution steps.)

  • Denominators: 3 and 6.
  • LCD: The LCD of 3 and 6 is 6.
  • Multiply every term by 6: $6 * (2x - 1)/3 = 6 * (x + 4)/6$
  • Simplify: $2(2x - 1) = x + 4$ (Here, the 6 and 3 on the left simplify to 2, and the 6s on the right cancel.)
  • Distribute on the left side: $4x - 2 = x + 4$
  • Subtract $x$ from both sides: $3x - 2 = 4$
  • Add 2 to both sides: $3x = 6$
  • Divide by 3 to solve for $x$: $x = 6 / 3$ $x = 2$
  • Check: Left side: $(2(2) - 1)/3 = (4 - 1)/3 = 3/3 = 1$ Right side: $(2 + 4)/6 = 6/6 = 1$ Since $1 = 1$, the solution is correct.

Clearing Decimals

Similar to fractions, decimals can be cleared by multiplying by an appropriate power of 10. The goal is to shift all decimal points to the right until all coefficients are integers.

Property: Clearing Decimals To clear decimals from an equation, multiply every term on both sides by the smallest power of 10 that makes all coefficients integers.

Step-by-Step Process for Clearing Decimals:

  1. Identify Most Decimal Places: Look at all the decimal numbers in the equation and find the term with the greatest number of decimal places.
  2. Determine Power of 10:
    • If the greatest number of decimal places is one, multiply by $10^1 = 10$.
    • If it's two, multiply by $10^2 = 100$.
    • If it's three, multiply by $10^3 = 1000$, and so on.
  3. Multiply Every Term: Multiply each and every term on both sides of the equation by this determined power of 10.
  4. Simplify: Perform the multiplication. All decimals should now be gone, leaving you with an equation with integer coefficients.
  5. Solve: Solve the resulting integer equation using standard algebraic methods.
  6. Check: Substitute your solution back into the original equation to verify it is correct.

Example 3: Basic Decimal Clearing Solve $0.3x + 1.2 = 4.5$.

  • Greatest number of decimal places: All terms have one decimal place.
  • Power of 10: Multiply by 10.
  • Multiply every term by 10: $10(0.3x) + 10(1.2) = 10(4.5)$
  • Simplify: $3x + 12 = 45$
  • Subtract 12 from both sides: $3x = 33$
  • Divide by 3 to solve for $x$: $x = 33 / 3$ $x = 11$
  • Check: $0.3(11) + 1.2 = 3.3 + 1.2 = 4.5$. The solution is correct.

Example 4: A Word Problem with Decimals Admission to a fair is $4.50 for adults and $2.50 for children. A group of 20 people pays a total of $70. How many adults and how many children are in the group?

  • Define variables: Let $a$ be the number of adults. The number of children is $20 - a$ (since there are 20 people in total).
  • Set up the equation: Cost from adults + Cost from children = Total cost $4.50a + 2.50(20 - a) = 70$
  • Clear decimals: The greatest number of decimal places (ignoring trailing zeros) is one (e.g., $4.5$, $2.5$).
    • Power of 10: Multiply by 10.
  • Multiply every term by 10: $10(4.50a) + 10(2.50(20 - a)) = 10(70)$ $45a + 25(20 - a) = 700$
  • Distribute: $45a + 500 - 25a = 700$
  • Combine like terms: $20a + 500 = 700$
  • Subtract 500 from both sides: $20a = 200$
  • Divide by 20 to solve for $a$: $a = 200 / 20$ $a = 10$
  • Find the number of children: Children $= 20 - a = 20 - 10 = 10$.
  • Answer: There are 10 adults and 10 children.
  • Check: $4.50(10) + 2.50(10) = 45 + 25 = 70$. The total cost matches.

Checkpoint Practice: Solve $x/2 + x/5 = 14$.

  • LCD: The LCD of 2 and 5 is 10.
  • Multiply by LCD: $10(x/2) + 10(x/5) = 10(14)$
  • Simplify: $5x + 2x = 140$
  • Combine and Solve: $7x = 140 \implies x = 20$.

Important Note: While clearing fractions or decimals is a highly effective and recommended strategy for simplifying equations, it is not always strictly necessary. You can solve equations with fractions or decimals directly by performing operations on them (e.g., finding common denominators for addition/subtraction of fractions, or working directly with decimal arithmetic). However, clearing them first typically reduces the complexity of calculations and minimizes the chances of arithmetic errors.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental principle behind clearing fractions or decimals from an equation. Why does multiplying every term by the LCD or a power of 10 maintain the equivalence of the equation, thus preserving its solution?
  2. Consider an equation that contains both fractions and decimals, such as $1/2x + 0.75 = 3/4x - 1.5$. Describe two different approaches you could take to clear this equation (one focusing on fractions first, one on decimals first). Which approach do you find more intuitive or efficient, and why?
  3. Walk through the process of solving the equation $y/5 - 2 = (y + 1)/10$ by clearing the fractions. Clearly state each step: identifying the LCD, multiplying all terms, simplifying, and solving for $y$.
  4. A student is attempting to solve the equation $0.05x + 0.2 = 1.15$. They decide to multiply every term by 10. What effect will this have on the equation, and what mistake might they be making? What is the correct power of 10 to use, and why?
  5. Why is it crucial to multiply every single term on both sides of the equation when clearing fractions or decimals? What would happen if you mistakenly missed multiplying one of the terms? Provide an example to illustrate your answer.
