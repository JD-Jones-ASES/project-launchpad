Study Guide: Volume of Prisms and Cylinders

Overview

This section introduces the concept of volume, which quantifies the amount of space inside three-dimensional figures. It provides fundamental formulas for calculating the volume of prisms and cylinders, emphasizing the use of cubic units. Key applications include determining the capacity of containers and real-world objects like pools and beakers.

Key Concepts

  • Volume Definition: Volume measures the total space occupied by a three-dimensional object, expressed in cubic units (e.g., cm$^3$, in.$^3$, ft$^3$). It represents the number of unit cubes that can fit inside the object.
  • Volume of Any Prism: The volume ($V$) of any prism is calculated by multiplying its base area ($B$) by its height ($h$). The formula is $V = Bh$. The base area $B$ will vary depending on the shape of the prism's base (e.g., rectangle, triangle).
  • Volume of a Rectangular Prism: A specific application of the prism formula, where the base is a rectangle. If the length ($l$), width ($w$), and height ($h$) are known, the volume is $V = lwh$.
  • Volume of a Cylinder: For a cylinder, the base is a circle. The base area ($B$) is $\pi r^2$ (where $r$ is the radius). Therefore, the volume formula for a cylinder is $V = \pi r^2 h$.
  • Units of Volume: Volume is always measured in cubic units, reflecting its three-dimensional nature.
  • Volume and Liquid Capacity Conversion: In the metric system, there's a direct relationship between volume and liquid capacity: $1 \text{ cm}^3 = 1 \text{ mL}$. This allows for easy conversion between solid volume and liquid capacity measurements.

Important Terms

  • Volume: The amount of three-dimensional space enclosed by a solid object. It is measured in cubic units.
  • Cubic Units: Standard units for measuring volume, such as cubic centimeters (cm$^3$), cubic inches (in.$^3$), or cubic feet (ft$^3$).
  • Prism: A three-dimensional solid with two identical, parallel bases (polygons) and rectangular lateral faces connecting them.
  • Cylinder: A three-dimensional solid with two identical, parallel circular bases and a curved lateral surface.
  • Base Area ($B$): The area of the base of a prism or cylinder. This is a key component in the volume formulas.
  • Height ($h$): The perpendicular distance between the two bases of a prism or cylinder.
  • Length ($l$): One dimension of a rectangular base.
  • Width ($w$): Another dimension of a rectangular base, perpendicular to the length.
  • Radius ($r$): The distance from the center of a circular base to any point on its circumference.
  • Capacity: The maximum amount that something can contain, often referring to liquid volume (e.g., in milliliters or liters).

Detailed Notes

Understanding Volume

  • What is Volume? Volume is a measure of the space occupied by a 3D object. Imagine filling an object with tiny, identical cubes; the number of cubes needed is its volume.
  • Units: Because volume involves three dimensions (length, width, height), its units are cubic. For example, if dimensions are in centimeters, volume is in cubic centimeters (cm$^3$).

Volume of Prisms

  • General Formula for Prisms: The fundamental principle for finding the volume of any prism is to multiply the area of its base ($B$) by its height ($h$).
    • $V = Bh$
  • Rectangular Prisms:
    • A rectangular prism has a rectangular base. The area of a rectangular base is $B = \text{length} \times \text{width} = lw$.
    • Substituting this into the general prism formula, we get: $V = lwh$.
    • Example: For a rectangular prism with $l = 10$ cm, $w = 4$ cm, and $h = 6.5$ cm:
      • $V = (10 \text{ cm})(4 \text{ cm})(6.5 \text{ cm}) = 260 \text{ cm}^3$.
  • Triangular Prisms:
    • A triangular prism has a triangular base. The area of a triangular base is $B = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base of triangle} \times \text{height of triangle}$.
    • Once you find $B$, use the general prism formula: $V = Bh$, where $h$ is the height (or length) of the prism.
    • Example: A triangular prism with a base triangle of base $8$ in. and height $3$ in., and a prism length (height) of $12$ in.
      • First, find the area of the triangular base: $B = \frac{1}{2}(8 \text{ in.})(3 \text{ in.}) = 12 \text{ in.}^2$.
      • Then, calculate the volume: $V = (12 \text{ in.}^2)(12 \text{ in.}) = 144 \text{ in.}^3$.

Volume of Cylinders

  • Formula for Cylinders: A cylinder can be thought of as a prism with a circular base.
    • The base area ($B$) of a circle is $\pi r^2$, where $r$ is the radius.
    • Using the general prism formula $V = Bh$, substitute $B = \pi r^2$: $V = \pi r^2 h$.
    • Example (Swimming Pool): A circular wading pool has a diameter of $8$ ft and a depth (height) of $1.5$ ft.
      • First, find the radius: $r = \text{diameter} / 2 = 8 \text{ ft} / 2 = 4 \text{ ft}$.
      • Calculate the volume in terms of $\pi$: $V = \pi (4 \text{ ft})^2 (1.5 \text{ ft}) = \pi (16)(1.5) = 24\pi \text{ ft}^3$.
      • Calculate the approximate volume using $\pi \approx 3.14$: $V \approx 24(3.14) = 75.36 \text{ ft}^3$.

Science Connection: Volume and Capacity

  • Conversion: In science, there's a direct and useful conversion:
    • $1 \text{ cm}^3 = 1 \text{ mL}$ (milliliter)
  • This relationship allows you to easily determine the liquid capacity of a container if you know its volume in cubic centimeters.
  • Example (Cylindrical Beaker): A cylindrical beaker has a radius of $3$ cm and a height of $10$ cm.
    • Calculate the volume: $V = \pi r^2 h = \pi (3 \text{ cm})^2 (10 \text{ cm}) = \pi (9)(10) = 90\pi \text{ cm}^3$.
    • Using $\pi \approx 3.14$: $V \approx 90(3.14) = 282.6 \text{ cm}^3$.
    • Since $1 \text{ cm}^3 = 1 \text{ mL}$, the capacity of the beaker is approximately $282.6 \text{ mL}$.

Self-Assessment Checkpoint

  • Problem: A rectangular aquarium is $24$ in. long, $12$ in. wide, and $16$ in. tall. Find its volume in cubic inches.
  • Solution: Use the formula for a rectangular prism, $V = lwh$.
    • $V = (24 \text{ in.})(12 \text{ in.})(16 \text{ in.}) = 4,608 \text{ in.}^3$.

Review Questions

  1. Explain why volume is measured in cubic units, rather than square units or linear units. Provide an example of a situation where you would use cubic units.
  2. Describe the key difference between calculating the volume of a rectangular prism and a triangular prism. How is the general formula $V = Bh$ still applicable to both?
  3. A storage container is shaped like a cylinder. If you double its radius while keeping its height the same, how does its volume change? Justify your answer using the volume formula.
  4. Imagine you have two containers: one is a rectangular prism measuring 10 cm x 5 cm x 4 cm, and the other is a cylinder with a radius of 4 cm and a height of 5 cm. Which container has a greater volume, and by approximately how much (using $\pi \approx 3.14$)?
  5. Discuss the practical application of the conversion $1 \text{ cm}^3 = 1 \text{ mL}$. How might a chef or a scientist use this knowledge in their work?
