Study Guide: Square Roots and Cube Roots

Overview

This section introduces square roots and cube roots as inverse operations to squaring and cubing numbers. It defines these roots, explains their properties (including principal roots and domain restrictions), and provides methods for evaluating, estimating, and simplifying radical expressions.

Key Concepts

  • Inverse Operations: Exponents (squaring, cubing) and roots (square roots, cube roots) are inverse operations, meaning one "undoes" the other.
  • Square Root: For a nonnegative number $a$, its square root is a nonnegative number $b$ such that $b^2 = a$. It is written as $\sqrt{a}$.
  • Principal Square Root: Every positive number has two square roots (one positive, one negative). The symbol $\sqrt{a}$ specifically denotes the principal (nonnegative) square root. The negative square root is written as $-\sqrt{a}$.
  • Domain of Square Roots: Square roots of negative numbers are not defined within the set of real numbers, because no real number multiplied by itself results in a negative number. Thus, the radicand $a$ in $\sqrt{a}$ must be nonnegative ($a \ge 0$).
  • Cube Root: For any number $a$, its cube root is a number $b$ such that $b^3 = a$. It is written as $\sqrt[3]{a}$.
  • Domain of Cube Roots: Unlike square roots, cube roots are defined for all real numbers, including negative numbers.
  • Estimating Non-Perfect Roots: For numbers that are not perfect squares or cubes, their roots can be estimated by finding the two consecutive whole numbers between which the root lies.
  • Product Property of Square Roots: For nonnegative numbers $a$ and $b$, the square root of their product can be split into the product of their square roots: $\sqrt{ab} = \sqrt{a} \cdot \sqrt{b}$.
  • Simplifying Square Roots: To simplify a square root, identify and factor out the largest perfect square from the radicand, then apply the product property.

Important Terms

  • Square Root: A number that, when multiplied by itself, gives a specific nonnegative number. For a number $a$, the square root is $b$ such that $b^2 = a$.
  • Radical Sign: The symbol $\sqrt{\quad}$, used to indicate a root. For square roots, the small "2" (index) is usually omitted.
  • Radicand: The number or expression written under the radical sign.
  • Principal Square Root: The unique nonnegative square root of a nonnegative number. For example, $\sqrt{25} = 5$.
  • Cube Root: A number that, when multiplied by itself three times, gives a specific number. For a number $a$, the cube root is $b$ such that $b^3 = a$. It is written as $\sqrt[3]{a}$.
  • Perfect Square: An integer that is the square of another integer (e.g., $1, 4, 9, 16, 25, \dots$).
  • Perfect Cube: An integer that is the cube of another integer (e.g., $1, 8, 27, 64, 125, \dots$, and also negative cubes like $-1, -8, -27, \dots$).

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Roots as Inverse Operations

Just as multiplication and division are inverse operations (e.g., $5 \times 3 = 15$, and $15 \div 3 = 5$), exponents and roots are also inverses.

  • Squaring a number means multiplying it by itself (e.g., $7^2 = 7 \times 7 = 49$).
  • Finding a square root asks, "What number, when multiplied by itself, gives this result?" (e.g., $\sqrt{49}$ asks "What number squared equals 49?").

Square Roots

Definition and Notation

The square root of a nonnegative number $a$ is the nonnegative number $b$ such that $b^2 = a$.

  • We write this as $\sqrt{a} = b$.
  • The symbol $\sqrt{\quad}$ is called the radical sign.
  • The number under the radical sign, $a$, is called the radicand.

Principal Square Root

Every positive number actually has two square roots: one positive and one negative.

  • For example, both $5$ and $-5$ are square roots of $25$ because $5^2 = 25$ and $(-5)^2 = 25$.
  • The symbol $\sqrt{a}$ always denotes the principal square root, which is the nonnegative root. So, $\sqrt{25} = 5$.
  • To indicate the negative square root, a negative sign is placed outside the radical, like $-\sqrt{25} = -5$.

Evaluating Square Roots

To evaluate a square root, find the nonnegative number that, when squared, equals the radicand.

  • $\sqrt{49} = 7$ because $7^2 = 49$.
  • $-\sqrt{81} = -9$ because $\sqrt{81} = 9$.
  • $\sqrt{0} = 0$ because $0^2 = 0$.
  • $\sqrt{144} = 12$ because $12^2 = 144$.

Domain Restriction for Real Numbers

An important caution about square roots is that $\sqrt{a}$ is defined only when $a \ge 0$ (meaning $a$ is nonnegative).

  • There is no real number whose square is negative. For instance, $\sqrt{-4}$ has no real value, because no real number multiplied by itself gives $-4$.

Cube Roots

Definition and Notation

The cube root of a number $a$ is the number $b$ such that $b^3 = a$.

  • We write this as $\sqrt[3]{a} = b$. The small "3" is called the index of the root.

Key Difference: Negative Numbers

Unlike square roots, cube roots are defined for negative numbers as well.

  • For example, $\sqrt[3]{-8} = -2$ because $(-2)^3 = (-2) \times (-2) \times (-2) = 4 \times (-2) = -8$.
  • This is possible because an odd number of negative factors results in a negative product.

Evaluating Cube Roots

To evaluate a cube root, find the number that, when cubed, equals the radicand.

  • $\sqrt[3]{27} = 3$ because $3^3 = 27$.
  • $\sqrt[3]{-64} = -4$ because $(-4)^3 = -64$.
  • $\sqrt[3]{125} = 5$ because $5^3 = 125$.

Estimating Non-Perfect Roots

Not all numbers are perfect squares or perfect cubes. When the radicand is not a perfect square or cube, its root is not a whole number.

  • We can estimate the value by finding which two consecutive whole numbers the root lies between.
  • Example: Estimating $\sqrt{50}$
    • We know $7^2 = 49$ and $8^2 = 64$.
    • Since $49 < 50 < 64$, it follows that $\sqrt{49} < \sqrt{50} < \sqrt{64}$.
    • Therefore, $7 < \sqrt{50} < 8$. The square root of 50 lies between 7 and 8.
  • For more precision, you can test decimals: $3.1^2 = 9.61$ and $3.2^2 = 10.24$, so $\sqrt{10}$ is between 3.1 and 3.2.

Simplifying Square Roots

We can often simplify square roots by factoring out perfect squares from the radicand. This relies on the Product Property of Square Roots.

Product Property of Square Roots

For nonnegative numbers $a$ and $b$: $$ \sqrt{ab} = \sqrt{a} \cdot \sqrt{b} $$ This property allows us to separate the square root of a product into the product of individual square roots.

Method for Simplifying

  1. Find the largest perfect square factor of the radicand.
  2. Rewrite the radicand as a product of this perfect square and the remaining factor.
  3. Apply the product property: $\sqrt{\text{perfect square} \times \text{other factor}} = \sqrt{\text{perfect square}} \times \sqrt{\text{other factor}}$.
  4. Evaluate the square root of the perfect square.

Examples of Simplifying Square Roots

  • Simplify $\sqrt{50}$:
    • The largest perfect square factor of $50$ is $25$ ($25 \times 2 = 50$).
    • $\sqrt{50} = \sqrt{25 \cdot 2} = \sqrt{25} \cdot \sqrt{2} = 5\sqrt{2}$.
  • Simplify $\sqrt{72}$:
    • The largest perfect square factor of $72$ is $36$ ($36 \times 2 = 72$).
    • $\sqrt{72} = \sqrt{36 \cdot 2} = \sqrt{36} \cdot \sqrt{2} = 6\sqrt{2}$.
  • Simplify $\sqrt{200}$:
    • The largest perfect square factor of $200$ is $100$ ($100 \times 2 = 200$).
    • $\sqrt{200} = \sqrt{100 \cdot 2} = \sqrt{100} \cdot \sqrt{2} = 10\sqrt{2}$.

Important Caution: The Product Property does NOT apply to addition!

Remember that $\sqrt{a + b} \ne \sqrt{a} + \sqrt{b}$.

  • For instance, $\sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5$.
  • But, $\sqrt{9} + \sqrt{16} = 3 + 4 = 7$.
  • Since $5 \ne 7$, the sum property does not hold. Only the product property works for multiplication.

Word Problem Application

Roots are used in various real-world problems, especially those involving geometry.

  • Example: Square Garden Area
    • A square garden has an area of $128$ square feet. To find the length of one side ($s$), we use the area formula $A = s^2$, so $s = \sqrt{A}$.
    • $s = \sqrt{128}$. To simplify:
      • Find the largest perfect square factor of $128$. $64 \times 2 = 128$.
      • $s = \sqrt{64 \cdot 2} = \sqrt{64} \cdot \sqrt{2} = 8\sqrt{2}$.
    • The side length of the garden is $8\sqrt{2}$ feet.

Review Questions

  2. What is the difference between $\sqrt{49}$ and $-\sqrt{49}$? Why is $\sqrt{-49}$ not a real number, while $\sqrt[3]{-27}$ is?
  3. Describe the process for estimating the value of a non-perfect square root. For example, between which two consecutive whole numbers does $\sqrt{75}$ lie?
  4. State the Product Property of Square Roots. Explain how this property is used to simplify a radical expression like $\sqrt{108}$.
  5. Why is it incorrect to say that $\sqrt{a + b} = \sqrt{a} + \sqrt{b}$? Provide a specific numerical example to support your answer.
