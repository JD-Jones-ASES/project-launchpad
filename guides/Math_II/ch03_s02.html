Percent Increase and Decrease Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the concept of percent change, a fundamental tool for understanding how quantities like prices, populations, or scores change over time. You will learn two primary methods for calculating percent changes and finding new amounts: using the direct percent change formula and employing the "multiplier" method. A crucial aspect covered is how to correctly handle successive percent changes, which often involves a common misconception.

Key Concepts

  • Percent Change Formula: This formula helps determine the percentage difference between an original amount and a new amount. percent change = (new amount - original amount) / original amount * 100% A positive result indicates a percent increase, while a negative result signifies a percent decrease.
  • Multiplier for Percent Change: A convenient factor used to directly calculate a new amount after a percent change.
    • Percent Increase of r%: To find the new amount, multiply the original amount by (1 + r), where r is the rate expressed as a decimal.
    • Percent Decrease of r%: To find the new amount, multiply the original amount by (1 - r), where r is the rate expressed as a decimal.
    • The factor (1 + r) or (1 - r) is known as the multiplier.
  • Successive Percent Changes: When multiple percent changes occur one after another (e.g., a discount followed by another discount), they do not simply add together. Instead, each change applies to the current amount, meaning their multipliers are multiplied. This often leads to a different overall percent change than a simple sum.

Important Terms

  • Percent Change: The relative change in a quantity, expressed as a percentage of the original amount. It quantifies how much a value has increased or decreased.
  • Original Amount: The starting value of a quantity before any change occurs.
  • New Amount: The final value of a quantity after a percent change has occurred.
  • Rate (r): The percentage of change, written as a decimal in formulas (e.g., 12% is 0.12).
  • Multiplier: The factor (1 + r) or (1 - r) that, when multiplied by the original amount, directly yields the new amount after a percent change.
  • Successive Percent Changes: Multiple percent increases or decreases that happen sequentially, with each subsequent change applied to the result of the previous change, not the original amount.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Percent Change

Percent change is a widely used concept in everyday life, from economic reports to personal finance. It allows us to express how much a value has changed in proportion to its initial size. Whether prices go up, populations decline, or performance metrics improve, percent change provides a standardized way to measure these shifts.

Calculating Percent Change

To find the percent change between an original amount and a new amount, use the following formula:

percent change = (new amount - original amount) / original amount * 100%

  • Interpreting the Result:
    • If the calculated percent change is positive, it signifies a percent increase.
    • If the calculated percent change is negative, it signifies a percent decrease.

Example: Calculating the Percent Change A town's population fell from 8,500 to 7,650. What is the percent change?

  1. Identify Original and New Amounts:
    • Original amount = 8,500
    • New amount = 7,650
  2. Apply the Formula: percent change = (7650 - 8500) / 8500 * 100% percent change = -850 / 8500 * 100% percent change = -0.10 * 100% percent change = -10% The negative sign indicates a 10% decrease in population.

Using Multipliers for Percent Change

The multiplier method offers a quicker way to find the new amount after a known percent change. It's especially useful when you need to apply multiple changes or work backward.

  • For a Percent Increase of r%: To find the new amount, you add r% of the original amount to the original amount. This can be expressed as original * (1 + r). Here, r must be the decimal equivalent of the percentage (e.g., for 12%, r = 0.12).

    • Formula: new amount = original * (1 + r)
    • The (1 + r) part is the multiplier.

    Example: Finding a Percent Increase A shirt costs $25. The price increases by 12%. Find the new price.

    1. Convert Percent to Decimal: 12% = 0.12
    2. Calculate the Multiplier: 1 + 0.12 = 1.12
    3. Multiply Original Amount by Multiplier: $25 * 1.12 = $28 The new price is $28.00.

  • For a Percent Decrease of r%: To find the new amount, you subtract r% of the original amount from the original amount. This can be expressed as original * (1 - r). Again, r must be the decimal equivalent of the percentage (e.g., for 15%, r = 0.15).

    • Formula: new amount = original * (1 - r)
    • The (1 - r) part is the multiplier.

    Example: Finding a Percent Decrease A laptop originally costs $640. It goes on sale for 15% off. What is the sale price?

    1. Convert Percent to Decimal: 15% = 0.15
    2. Calculate the Multiplier: 1 - 0.15 = 0.85
    3. Multiply Original Amount by Multiplier: $640 * 0.85 = $544 The sale price is $544.00.

  • Working Backward with Multipliers: The multiplier method is also powerful for finding the original amount when you know the new amount and the percent change. If new amount = original * multiplier, then original = new amount / multiplier.

    Example: If the new price after a 25% increase is $150, we can write original * 1.25 = 150. Solving for the original: original = 150 / 1.25 = 120. So, the original price was $120.

Successive Percent Changes

A common and significant error is to add successive percent changes. For instance, if an item is 20% off, and then an additional 10% is taken off, the total discount is not 30%. This is because each discount (or increase) is applied to a different current amount.

Key Rule: Successive percent changes apply their multipliers sequentially; they do not add.

Example: Successive Discounts A jacket with an original price of $80 is marked 20% off. A store coupon gives an additional 10% off the sale price. Find the final price and the overall percent decrease.

  1. First Discount (20% off):

    • Decimal rate: r1 = 0.20
    • Multiplier: 1 - 0.20 = 0.80
    • Price after first discount: $80 * 0.80 = $64

  2. Second Discount (10% off the sale price):

    • The second discount applies to the new price of $64.
    • Decimal rate: r2 = 0.10
    • Multiplier: 1 - 0.10 = 0.90
    • Final price: $64 * 0.90 = $57.60

  3. Calculate Overall Percent Decrease:

    • Original amount = $80
    • New amount = $57.60
    • percent change = (57.60 - 80) / 80 * 100%
    • percent change = -22.40 / 80 * 100%
    • percent change = -0.28 * 100%
    • percent change = -28%

The final price is $57.60, and the overall discount is 28%, not 30%.

Important Caution: Even if you have an increase followed by an equal decrease, you won't necessarily return to the original amount. For example, a 20% increase followed by a 20% decrease:

  • Start with $100.
  • 20% increase: $100 * (1 + 0.20) = 100 * 1.20 = $120.
  • 20% decrease (on new amount): $120 * (1 - 0.20) = 120 * 0.80 = $96. The result is $96, which is a 4% decrease from the original $100.

Review Questions

  1. A car's value depreciated from $25,000 to $20,750 in one year. What was the percent change in its value? Was it an increase or a decrease?
  2. An artist increases the price of her paintings by 15%. If a painting originally cost $400, what is its new price?
  3. A store is having a clearance sale, offering 30% off all items. If a bicycle originally costs $350, what is its sale price?
  4. Explain why an 8% salary raise followed by a 5% salary cut does not result in a net 3% raise.
  5. A smartphone originally priced at $800 is first discounted by 25%. Later, for a special event, an additional 10% is taken off the discounted price. What is the final price of the smartphone, and what is the overall percent decrease from the original price?
