Understanding Irrational Numbers and The Real Number System

Overview

This section introduces irrational numbers, which are numbers whose decimal representations never terminate or repeat. Together, irrational numbers and rational numbers form the set of real numbers, which correspond to every single point on the number line. We will explore how to identify and classify these numbers, understand the structure of the real number system, and learn methods for approximating irrational values.

Key Concepts

  • Irrational Numbers: Numbers that cannot be expressed as a simple fraction $\frac{a}{b}$ (where $a$ and $b$ are integers and $b \neq 0$). Their decimal expansions are non-terminating and non-repeating.
  • Real Numbers ($\mathbb{R}$): The comprehensive set of all rational and irrational numbers. Every point on the number line corresponds to exactly one real number, and every real number corresponds to exactly one point on the number line.
  • Hierarchy of Number Systems: The real numbers encompass several smaller sets: Natural Numbers ($\mathbb{N}$), Whole Numbers ($\mathbb{W}$), Integers ($\mathbb{Z}$), and Rational Numbers ($\mathbb{Q}$). These sets are nested, meaning each is a subset of the next larger one, culminating in the real numbers.
  • Proof by Contradiction: A logical method used to prove a statement by assuming its opposite is true, and then demonstrating that this assumption leads to a contradiction, thereby proving the original statement. This method is demonstrated for the irrationality of $\sqrt{2}$.
  • Approximating Irrational Numbers: While irrational numbers cannot be written exactly as fractions or terminating/repeating decimals, they can be approximated to any desired level of precision using decimals, often by finding values between perfect squares.

Important Terms

  • Irrational Number: A number that cannot be written as a fraction $\frac{a}{b}$ where $a$ and $b$ are integers and $b \neq 0$. Its decimal expansion is non-terminating and non-repeating. Examples include $\sqrt{2}$, $\sqrt{3}$, and $\pi$.
  • Rational Number ($\mathbb{Q}$): A number that can be written as a fraction $\frac{a}{b}$ where $a$ and $b$ are integers and $b \neq 0$. Its decimal expansion either terminates or repeats.
  • Real Numbers ($\mathbb{R}$): The set of all rational and irrational numbers. The entire number line is composed of real numbers.
  • Natural Numbers ($\mathbb{N}$): The counting numbers: ${1, 2, 3, \ldots}$.
  • Whole Numbers ($\mathbb{W}$): The natural numbers including zero: ${0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots}$.
  • Integers ($\mathbb{Z}$): All whole numbers and their negatives: ${\ldots, -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots}$.
  • Perfect Square: An integer that is the square of an integer (e.g., $1, 4, 9, 16, \ldots$). The square root of a perfect square is always a rational number.
  • Proof by Contradiction: A logical argument where one assumes the opposite of what is to be proven, and if this assumption leads to a logical inconsistency (a contradiction), then the original statement must be true.

Detailed Notes

What are Irrational Numbers?

Until now, you might have primarily worked with rational numbers. However, not every number you encounter on the number line can be expressed as a simple fraction. An irrational number is defined as a number that cannot be written in the form $\frac{a}{b}$, where $a$ and $b$ are integers and $b \neq 0$.

The key characteristic of an irrational number's decimal representation is that it neither terminates nor repeats. This means the digits after the decimal point go on infinitely without any discernible pattern of repetition. Common examples of irrational numbers include:

  • $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.41421356\ldots$
  • $\sqrt{3} \approx 1.73205080\ldots$
  • $\pi \approx 3.14159265\ldots$

It's important to note that not all numbers under a radical sign are irrational. For instance, $\sqrt{4} = 2$, which can be written as $\frac{2}{1}$, making it a rational number. Only the square roots of non-perfect square integers are irrational.

Classifying Numbers as Rational or Irrational: To classify a number, consider its definition:

  • $\sqrt{9}$: This simplifies to $3$, which can be written as $\frac{3}{1}$. Therefore, it is rational.
  • $\sqrt{5}$: Since $5$ is not a perfect square, $\sqrt{5}$ is an irrational number.
  • $0.121212\ldots$: This is a repeating decimal, which means it can be expressed as a fraction (in this case, $\frac{12}{99}$ or $\frac{4}{33}$). Therefore, it is rational.
  • $\frac{\pi}{2}$: Since $\pi$ is irrational, any non-zero rational multiple or quotient of $\pi$ (like $\frac{\pi}{2}$) will also be irrational.
  • $-\frac{7}{3}$: This is already in the form $\frac{a}{b}$ where $a=-7$ and $b=3$ are integers. Therefore, it is rational.

Why is $\sqrt{2}$ Irrational? (Informal Proof)

One of the most famous proofs in mathematics demonstrates why $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational using a technique called proof by contradiction. Here's a simplified version:

  1. Assume the opposite: We assume that $\sqrt{2}$ is rational. This means we can write $\sqrt{2} = \frac{a}{b}$, where $a$ and $b$ are integers, $b \neq 0$, and the fraction is in its simplest form (meaning $a$ and $b$ share no common factors other than 1).
  2. Square both sides: Squaring the equation gives $2 = \frac{a^2}{b^2}$, which can be rearranged to $a^2 = 2b^2$.
  3. Deduce properties of $a$: Since $a^2$ equals $2$ times some integer ($b^2$), $a^2$ must be an even number. If $a^2$ is even, then $a$ itself must also be an even number (because an odd number squared is always odd).
  4. Substitute and deduce properties of $b$: Since $a$ is even, we can write it as $a = 2k$ for some integer $k$. Substituting this back into $a^2 = 2b^2$: $(2k)^2 = 2b^2$ $4k^2 = 2b^2$ $2k^2 = b^2$
  5. Contradiction: This last equation shows that $b^2$ is also an even number (since it equals $2$ times an integer $k^2$). If $b^2$ is even, then $b$ must also be an even number.
  6. Conclusion: We've reached a contradiction! We initially assumed that $\frac{a}{b}$ was in simplest form, meaning $a$ and $b$ had no common factors. But our proof shows that both $a$ and $b$ must be even, which means they both have a common factor of $2$. This contradicts our initial assumption. Therefore, our initial assumption that $\sqrt{2}$ is rational must be false. Hence, $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.

This type of proof is specific to $\sqrt{2}$, but similar logic can be applied to prove the irrationality of other non-perfect square roots like $\sqrt{3}$ or $\sqrt{5}$.

The Real Numbers

The real numbers ($\mathbb{R}$) represent the entire collection of all rational numbers and all irrational numbers combined. Essentially, every single point that exists on the continuous number line corresponds to exactly one real number, and every real number corresponds to exactly one point on that line. This makes the real number line complete, with no "gaps."

The real number system has a hierarchical structure, meaning sets of numbers are contained within larger sets:

  • Natural Numbers ($\mathbb{N}$): ${1, 2, 3, \ldots}$ (counting numbers).
  • Whole Numbers ($\mathbb{W}$): ${0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots}$ (natural numbers plus zero).
  • Integers ($\mathbb{Z}$): ${\ldots, -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots}$ (whole numbers and their opposites).
  • Rational Numbers ($\mathbb{Q}$): All numbers that can be written as a fraction $\frac{a}{b}$. This includes all integers, as any integer $n$ can be written as $\frac{n}{1}$.
  • Irrational Numbers: Numbers that cannot be written as a fraction. These are separate from rational numbers but are also real numbers.
  • Real Numbers ($\mathbb{R}$): The union of all rational and irrational numbers.

This hierarchy can be visualized as nested sets: $\mathbb{N} \subset \mathbb{W} \subset \mathbb{Z} \subset \mathbb{Q} \subset \mathbb{R}$. The irrational numbers exist alongside the rational numbers within the set of real numbers.

Identifying Number Sets: Let's classify some numbers into the sets they belong to:

  • $-7$: This is an integer, and since integers are rational, it is also rational and real. (Integer, Rational, Real)
  • $\frac{5}{8}$: This is a fraction of integers, so it's rational. All rational numbers are real. (Rational, Real)
  • $\sqrt{16}$: This simplifies to $4$. $4$ is a natural number, a whole number, an integer, a rational number, and a real number. (Natural, Whole, Integer, Rational, Real)
  • $\sqrt{11}$: Since $11$ is not a perfect square, $\sqrt{11}$ is irrational. All irrational numbers are real. (Irrational, Real)

Approximating Irrational Numbers

Since irrational numbers have non-terminating, non-repeating decimals, we often need to approximate them for practical purposes. We can do this by finding increasingly precise decimal values.

Example: Approximating and Plotting $\sqrt{7}$ To approximate $\sqrt{7}$ to the nearest hundredth and plot it:

  1. Estimate between integers: We know $2^2 = 4$ and $3^2 = 9$. So, $2 < \sqrt{7} < 3$.
  2. Refine to tenths:
    • $2.6^2 = 6.76$
    • $2.7^2 = 7.29$ Therefore, $2.6 < \sqrt{7} < 2.7$.
  3. Refine to hundredths:
    • $2.64^2 = 6.9696$
    • $2.65^2 = 7.0225$ Since $7$ is closer to $7.0225$ than $6.9696$, we can say $\sqrt{7} \approx 2.65$ to the nearest hundredth.
  4. Plotting: On the number line, $\sqrt{7}$ would be placed approximately two-thirds of the way between $2$ and $3$, specifically just after $2.6$ and slightly before $2.7$, very close to $2.65$.

Similarly, to approximate $\sqrt{20}$ to the nearest tenth:

  • $4^2 = 16$ and $5^2 = 25$, so $4 < \sqrt{20} < 5$.
  • $4.4^2 = 19.36$
  • $4.5^2 = 20.25$ Since $20$ is closer to $20.25$ than $19.36$, $\sqrt{20} \approx 4.5$ to the nearest tenth.

The distinction between rational and irrational numbers is fundamental and will continue to be important as you advance in your mathematical studies.

  1. Define an irrational number in your own words. How does its decimal representation differ from that of a rational number? Provide three distinct examples of irrational numbers.
  2. Explain the relationship between rational numbers, irrational numbers, and real numbers. How are these sets organized, and what does it mean for "every point on the number line to correspond to exactly one real number"?
  3. Describe how you would determine if a number expressed with a radical sign, such as $\sqrt{X}$, is rational or irrational. Give an example of a rational number in radical form and an example of an irrational number in radical form, explaining your reasoning.
