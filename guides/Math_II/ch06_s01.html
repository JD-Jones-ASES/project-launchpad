Study Guide: Review of Perimeter and Area

Overview

This section provides a thorough review of finding the perimeter and area of fundamental geometric shapes, building upon concepts learned in previous mathematics courses. It expands on these skills by applying formulas to problems involving fractional and decimal measurements, and introduces techniques for calculating the area of composite, or L-shaped, regions by breaking them down into simpler components.

Key Concepts

  • Perimeter: The total distance around the boundary of a two-dimensional shape. It is a linear measurement.
  • Area: The amount of surface enclosed within the boundaries of a two-dimensional shape. It is measured in square units.
  • Perimeter and Area Formulas: Specific mathematical equations used to calculate the perimeter (or circumference for circles) and area for various basic geometric shapes (e.g., triangles, trapezoids, circles).
  • Fractional and Decimal Measurements: The application of perimeter and area formulas to problems where the dimensions are given as fractions or decimals, requiring careful calculation with these number types.
  • Composite Figures (2-D): Irregular shapes that can be divided into (or thought of as being composed of) two or more basic geometric shapes. Their total area is found by calculating the areas of the individual components and then adding or subtracting them as appropriate.

Important Terms

  • Perimeter: The length of the boundary of a closed planar figure.
  • Area: The measure of the extent of a two-dimensional surface or region.
  • Base (of a triangle/trapezoid): A side of a polygon, often used as a reference for calculating height or area. Triangles have one base; trapezoids have two parallel bases ($b_1$ and $b_2$).
  • Height (of a triangle/trapezoid): The perpendicular distance from a base to the opposite vertex (for a triangle) or to the opposite parallel base (for a trapezoid).
  • Radius ($r$): The distance from the center of a circle to any point on its circumference.
  • Diameter ($d$): The distance across a circle through its center; it is twice the radius ($d = 2r$).
  • Circumference ($C$): The perimeter of a circle, calculated as $C = \pi d$ or $C = 2\pi r$.
  • Trapezoid: A quadrilateral with at least one pair of parallel sides.
  • Composite Figure: A geometric figure made up of two or more basic geometric shapes.
  • Fraction: A numerical quantity that is not a whole number (e.g., $1/2, 3/4$).
  • Decimal: A number that contains a decimal point, representing a fractional part (e.g., $7.2, 5.5$).
  • $\pi$ (Pi): A mathematical constant approximately equal to $3.14159$, representing the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter. For many calculations, $3.14$ is used as an approximation.

Detailed Notes

This section serves as a refresher on calculating perimeter and area for common shapes, extending these skills to more complex scenarios involving non-integer dimensions and irregular figures.

General Concepts of Perimeter and Area

  • Perimeter is a measure of length, so its units are linear (e.g., cm, ft, m).
  • Area is a measure of surface, so its units are square units (e.g., cm$^2$, ft$^2$, m$^2$).

Area Formulas with Fractional and Decimal Measurements

The fundamental area formulas remain the same, regardless of whether the dimensions are whole numbers, decimals, or fractions. The key is to correctly apply arithmetic operations for these number types.

1. Area of a Triangle

The area of a triangle is half the product of its base and height.

  • Formula: $A = \frac{1}{2}bh$

  • Example: Area of a Triangle with Decimal Dimensions Find the area of a triangle with base $7.2$ cm and height $5.5$ cm.

    Solution. Using the formula $A = \frac{1}{2}bh$: $A = \frac{1}{2}(7.2)(5.5)$ First, multiply the base and height: $7.2 \times 5.5 = 39.6$ Then, multiply by $\frac{1}{2}$ (or divide by 2): $A = \frac{1}{2}(39.6) = 19.8$ The area is $19.8$ cm$^2$.

2. Area of a Trapezoid

The area of a trapezoid is half the product of the sum of its two parallel bases and its height.

  • Formula: $A = \frac{1}{2}(b_1 + b_2)h$

  • Example: Area of a Trapezoid with Given Dimensions Find the area of a trapezoid with bases $34$ ft and $54$ ft and height $23$ ft.

    Solution. Using the formula $A = \frac{1}{2}(b_1 + b_2)h$: $A = \frac{1}{2}(34 + 54)(23)$ First, sum the bases: $34 + 54 = 88$ Then, substitute into the formula: $A = \frac{1}{2}(88)(23)$ $A = 44 \times 23$ $A = 1012$ The area is $1012$ ft$^2$. (Note: The original textbook content provides an illustrative calculation that leads to $23$ ft$^2$ with different intermediate steps: $1/2(84)(23) = 1/2(2)(23) = 23 ft^2$. For the given dimensions, the mathematically correct calculation is $1012$ ft$^2$ as shown above.)

3. Area of a Circle

The area of a circle is $\pi$ times the square of its radius.

  • Formula: $A = \pi r^2$

  • Circumference Formula: $C = 2\pi r$ or $C = \pi d$ (where $d$ is diameter)

  • Example: Area of a Circle Find the area of a circle with radius $6$ in. Give the answer (a) in terms of $\pi$ and (b) using $3.14$.

    Solution. (a) In terms of $\pi$: $A = \pi r^2 = \pi (6)^2 = 36\pi$ in.$^2$. (b) Using $3.14$ for $\pi$: $A = 36 \times 3.14 = 113.04$ in.$^2$.

  • Checkpoint: Circumference and Area of a Circle Find the circumference and area of a circle with diameter $10$ m. Use $3.14$. Solution. First, find the radius. Since diameter $d = 10$ m, the radius $r = d/2 = 10/2 = 5$ m. Circumference: $C = 2\pi r$ $C = 2(3.14)(5)$ $C = 10(3.14)$ $C = 31.4$ m. Area: $A = \pi r^2$ $A = 3.14(5)^2$ $A = 3.14(25)$ $A = 78.5$ m$^2$.

Composite Figures (2-D)

Composite figures are shapes made up of two or more basic geometric shapes. To find their area:

  1. Decomposition: Divide the irregular shape into simpler, recognizable shapes (rectangles, triangles, circles, etc.).
  2. Individual Area Calculation: Find the area of each of these simpler shapes.
  3. Combination: Add the areas of all the component shapes, or subtract the area of any "missing" parts.

  • Example: L-Shaped Floor Plan (Application) A room has an L-shaped floor. The outer dimensions are $12$ ft by $10$ ft, and a $4$ ft by $5$ ft rectangular piece is missing from one corner. Find the floor area.

    Solution. This L-shaped region can be viewed as a large rectangle with a smaller rectangular piece removed from it. Area of the large outer rectangle: $A_{large} = \text{length} \times \text{width} = 12 \text{ ft} \times 10 \text{ ft} = 120 \text{ ft}^2$. Area of the missing rectangular piece: $A_{missing} = \text{length} \times \text{width} = 4 \text{ ft} \times 5 \text{ ft} = 20 \text{ ft}^2$. To find the floor area, subtract the missing area from the large area: $A_{floor} = A_{large} - A_{missing}$ $A_{floor} = 120 \text{ ft}^2 - 20 \text{ ft}^2$ $A_{floor} = 100 \text{ ft}^2$. The floor area is $100$ ft$^2$.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the difference between perimeter and area, including the types of units used for each. Why is it important to use the correct units?
  2. Describe the steps involved in finding the area of a triangle. If a triangle has a base of $8.5$ inches and a height of $4.2$ inches, what is its area?
  3. How do you calculate the area of a trapezoid? Explain how the formula accounts for the two different base lengths.
  4. A circular garden has a radius of $7$ meters. Calculate both its circumference and its area, first in terms of $\pi$ and then using $\pi \approx 3.14$. Explain when one form of the answer might be preferred over the other.
  5. What is a composite figure, and what strategies can be used to find its area? Provide an example of how you would break down an irregular shape (e.g., a cross-shape or a shape that looks like a house with a triangular roof on a rectangular base) to calculate its total area.
