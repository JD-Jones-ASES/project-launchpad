Study Guide: Surface Area and Volume of Spheres

Overview

This section introduces the sphere, a fundamental three-dimensional shape characterized by its perfect roundness and lack of flat faces or edges. It defines a sphere based on its center and radius, and critically, provides the essential formulas for calculating its surface area and volume. The content emphasizes the importance of using the radius in these calculations and illustrates their application through various examples, including real-world scenarios.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of a Sphere: A sphere is a set of all points in three-dimensional space that are equidistant from a central point. This fixed distance is known as the radius.
  • Surface Area of a Sphere: The total area of the outer surface of a sphere. The formula for the surface area ($SA$) of a sphere with radius $r$ is $SA = 4\pi r^2$.
  • Volume of a Sphere: The amount of space occupied by a sphere. The formula for the volume ($V$) of a sphere with radius $r$ is $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$.
  • Radius vs. Diameter: The radius ($r$) is the distance from the center of the sphere to any point on its surface. The diameter ($d$) is the distance across the sphere passing through its center, meaning $d = 2r$. It is crucial to always use the radius in the surface area and volume formulas; if given the diameter, remember to divide it by 2 to find the radius first.
  • Expressing Answers: Calculations can be expressed in terms of $\pi$ (exact answer) or as a decimal approximation (using $\pi \approx 3.14$ or a calculator's $\pi$ value).

Important Terms

  • Sphere: A perfectly round three-dimensional geometric object, like a ball, where every point on its surface is the same distance from its center.
  • Center: The central point within a sphere from which all points on the surface are equidistant.
  • Radius ($r$): The distance from the center of a sphere to any point on its surface. It is a key measurement used in all sphere formulas.
  • Diameter ($d$): The distance across a sphere passing through its center. It is twice the radius ($d = 2r$).
  • Surface Area ($SA$): The total area of the "skin" or outer boundary of a three-dimensional object. For a sphere, it's the area of its curved surface.
  • Volume ($V$): The amount of three-dimensional space occupied by an object. For a sphere, it's the capacity or space enclosed within its surface.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Spheres

A sphere is a unique 3D shape because it lacks flat faces, edges, or vertices, making it perfectly smooth and round. Everyday examples include sports balls (basketballs, soccer balls), globes, and marbles.

  • Definition: Formally, a sphere is defined as the collection of all points in space that are a fixed distance (the radius) from a central point (the center).

Key Formulas for Spheres

  1. Surface Area of a Sphere

    • The formula to calculate the surface area ($SA$) of a sphere is given by: $$SA = 4 \pi r^2$$
    • Where:
      • $SA$ represents the surface area.
      • $\pi$ (pi) is a mathematical constant, approximately $3.14159$.
      • $r$ is the radius of the sphere.
    • Units for surface area are always square units (e.g., cm$^2$, in.$^2$, km$^2$).

  2. Volume of a Sphere

    • The formula to calculate the volume ($V$) of a sphere is given by: $$V = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3$$
    • Where:
      • $V$ represents the volume.
      • $\pi$ (pi) is the mathematical constant.
      • $r$ is the radius of the sphere.
    • Units for volume are always cubic units (e.g., cm$^3$, in.$^3$, km$^3$).

Important Calculation Tip

  • Always use the radius ($r$): Both formulas for surface area and volume require the radius. If a problem provides the diameter ($d$), you must first divide the diameter by 2 to find the radius ($r = d/2$) before substituting into the formulas.

Examples and Applications

Let's walk through the examples provided in the textbook section to solidify understanding:

  1. Surface Area of a Sphere (Given Radius)

    • Problem: Find the surface area of a sphere with radius $7$ cm. Give the answer in terms of $\pi$ and as a decimal.
    • Solution:
      • Given $r = 7$ cm.
      • Using the formula $SA = 4 \pi r^2$: $SA = 4 \pi (7)^2$ $SA = 4 \pi (49)$ $SA = 196 \pi \text{ cm}^2$ (in terms of $\pi$)
      • To get the decimal approximation, use $\pi \approx 3.14$: $SA \approx 196 \times 3.14$ $SA \approx 615.44 \text{ cm}^2$ (as a decimal)

  2. Volume of a Sphere (Given Diameter)

    • Problem: Find the volume of a sphere with diameter $12$ in. Give the answer in terms of $\pi$ and as a decimal.
    • Solution:
      • First, find the radius: $r = d/2 = 12 \text{ in.} / 2 = 6 \text{ in.}$
      • Using the formula $V = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3$: $V = \frac{4}{3} \pi (6)^3$ $V = \frac{4}{3} \pi (216)$ $V = 4 \pi (216/3)$ $V = 4 \pi (72)$ $V = 288 \pi \text{ in.}^3$ (in terms of $\pi$)
      • To get the decimal approximation, use $\pi \approx 3.14$: $V \approx 288 \times 3.14$ $V \approx 904.32 \text{ in.}^3$ (as a decimal)

  3. Basketball (Application for Surface Area)

    • Problem: A regulation basketball has a diameter of about $9.4$ in. Find its surface area. Use $\pi \approx 3.14$.
    • Solution:
      • First, find the radius: $r = d/2 = 9.4 \text{ in.} / 2 = 4.7 \text{ in.}$
      • Using the formula $SA = 4 \pi r^2$: $SA \approx 4 (3.14) (4.7)^2$ $SA \approx 4 (3.14) (22.09)$ $SA \approx 277.45 \text{ in.}^2$

  4. Comparing Planets (Application for Volume)

    • Problem: Mars has a radius of approximately $3,390$ km. Find the volume of Mars in terms of $\pi$.
    • Solution:
      • Given $r = 3,390$ km.
      • Using the formula $V = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3$: $V = \frac{4}{3} \pi (3,390)^3$ $V = \frac{4}{3} \pi (38,958,219,000)$ $V \approx 51,944,292,000 \pi \text{ km}^3$ (in terms of $\pi$)
      • This can also be expressed in scientific notation: $V \approx 5.19 \times 10^{10} \pi \text{ km}^3$.

Checkpoint Review

  • Problem: Find the volume of a sphere with radius $3$ ft. Give the answer in terms of $\pi$.
  • Solution:
    • Given $r = 3$ ft.
    • Using $V = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3$: $V = \frac{4}{3} \pi (3)^3$ $V = \frac{4}{3} \pi (27)$ $V = 4 \pi (27/3)$ $V = 4 \pi (9)$ $V = 36 \pi \text{ ft}^3$

Review Questions

  1. What are the defining characteristics of a sphere that differentiate it from other three-dimensional geometric shapes like cubes or cones? How do these characteristics simplify or complicate the calculation of its surface area and volume?
  2. Explain why it is essential to use the radius ($r$) and not the diameter ($d$) when calculating the surface area and volume of a sphere. If a problem provides the diameter, what is the first step you should take?
  3. A spherical globe has a diameter of $16$ inches. Calculate its surface area and volume. Provide your answers both in terms of $\pi$ and as decimal approximations rounded to two decimal places (use $\pi \approx 3.14$).
  4. If the surface area of a beach ball is $400\pi$ square centimeters, what is its radius? What would its volume be?
  5. Consider two spheres: Sphere A has a radius of $5$ cm, and Sphere B has a radius of $10$ cm. Without performing exact calculations, describe how the volume of Sphere B compares to the volume of Sphere A. Justify your reasoning using the volume formula.
