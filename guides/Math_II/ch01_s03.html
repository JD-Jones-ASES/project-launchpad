Study Guide: Zero and Negative Exponents

Overview

This section extends the fundamental rules of exponents to include cases involving zero and negative integer exponents, which allows us to simplify expressions where the exponent in the denominator is greater than or equal to the exponent in the numerator. It also introduces the "Power of a Quotient" rule for handling fractions raised to a power, providing a comprehensive set of rules for simplifying a wider range of exponential expressions.

Key Concepts

  • Zero Exponent Rule: Any nonzero base raised to the power of zero equals 1. This rule arises from simplifying a quantity divided by itself using the quotient rule (e.g., $a^m / a^m = a^{m-m} = a^0$, and $a^m / a^m = 1$).
  • Negative Exponent Rule: A nonzero base raised to a negative exponent is equivalent to its reciprocal raised to the corresponding positive exponent. This means $a^{-n} = 1/a^n$ and $1/a^{-n} = a^n$. A negative exponent indicates "take the reciprocal" of the base.
  • Power of a Quotient Rule: When a fraction (quotient) is raised to a power, both the numerator and the denominator are raised to that power: $(a/b)^n = a^n/b^n$.
  • Negative Exponent with Fractions (Shortcut): A negative exponent applied to a fraction means "flip" the fraction (take its reciprocal) and then raise it to the positive exponent: $(a/b)^{-n} = (b/a)^n$.
  • Combined Exponent Rules: All exponent rules (product, quotient, power to a power, and now zero/negative exponents and power of a quotient) can be applied together to simplify complex expressions.

Important Terms

  • Zero Exponent: An exponent of 0. For any nonzero number $a$, $a^0 = 1$.
  • Negative Exponent: An exponent that is a negative integer. For any nonzero number $a$ and positive integer $n$, $a^{-n} = 1/a^n$.
  • Base: The number or variable being multiplied by itself in an exponential expression. In $a^n$, $a$ is the base.
  • Reciprocal: For a number $x$, its reciprocal is $1/x$. Negative exponents are directly related to finding the reciprocal of the base.
  • Quotient Rule (revisited): A previously learned rule, $a^m / a^n = a^{m-n}$, which is now expanded to include cases where $m \le n$ due to the introduction of zero and negative exponents.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Zero and Negative Exponents

Previously, the quotient rule for exponents ($a^m/a^n = a^{m-n}$) required the exponent in the numerator ($m$) to be greater than the exponent in the denominator ($n$) to ensure a positive resulting exponent. This section extends our understanding to cover situations where $m = n$ or $m < n$, leading to zero or negative exponents.

The Zero Exponent

The concept of a zero exponent can be understood by considering the division of any nonzero quantity by itself.

  • Derivation: We know that any nonzero quantity divided by itself equals 1. For example, $a^4/a^4 = 1$.
  • Using the Quotient Rule: If we apply the quotient rule to $a^4/a^4$, we get $a^{4-4} = a^0$.
  • Conclusion: Since both results must be equal, we conclude that $a^0 = 1$.

Property: Zero Exponent For any nonzero number $a$, $a^0 = 1$.

Important Caution: The expression $0^0$ is not defined in this context. When using the zero exponent rule, the base must always be nonzero.

Examples of Evaluating Zero Exponents:

  • $7^0 = 1$
  • $(-4)^0 = 1$ (The entire base, $-4$, is nonzero.)
  • $-4^0 = -(4^0) = -(1) = -1$ (Here, only $4$ is the base, not $-4$. The negative sign is applied after evaluating $4^0$.)
  • $(3x)^0 = 1$ (Provided $x \neq 0$, so the base $3x$ is nonzero.)

Checkpoint Practice:

  • Evaluate: (a) $12^0$ (b) $-12^0$
    • Answer: (a) $1$ (b) $-1$

Negative Exponents

Negative exponents are introduced to handle cases where the exponent in the denominator is larger than the exponent in the numerator when using the quotient rule.

  • Derivation: Consider $a^2/a^5$.
    • By expanding: $a^2/a^5 = (a \cdot a) / (a \cdot a \cdot a \cdot a \cdot a) = 1/(a \cdot a \cdot a) = 1/a^3$.
    • By quotient rule: $a^2/a^5 = a^{2-5} = a^{-3}$.
  • Conclusion: Combining these, we see that $a^{-3} = 1/a^3$.

Property: Negative Exponent For any nonzero number $a$ and positive integer $n$:

  • $a^{-n} = 1/a^n$
  • $1/a^{-n} = a^n$

Key Idea: A negative exponent means "take the reciprocal" of the base and then apply the positive exponent.

Examples of Rewriting with Positive Exponents:

  • $5^{-2} = 1/5^2 = 1/25$
  • $x^{-4} = 1/x^4$
  • $3y^{-2} = 3 \cdot (1/y^2) = 3/y^2$ (Note: only $y$ is raised to the negative exponent, not $3$.)

Checkpoint Practice:

  • Write $7^{-3}$ using a positive exponent, then evaluate.
    • Answer: $1/7^3 = 1/343$

Power of a Quotient

Similar to raising a product to a power, we can raise a quotient (fraction) to a power.

Property: Power of a Quotient For any nonzero numbers $a$ and $b$ and any integer $n$:

  • $(a/b)^n = a^n/b^n$

Examples of Power of a Quotient:

  • $(3/4)^3 = 3^3/4^3 = 27/64$
  • $(x^2/y)^4 = (x^2)^4/y^4 = x^8/y^4$
  • $(2/a)^{-3}$: This can be simplified by applying the negative exponent rule directly to the fraction: $(2/a)^{-3} = 1/(2/a)^3 = 1/(2^3/a^3) = 1/(8/a^3) = a^3/8$.

Important Note: A shortcut for a negative exponent on a fraction is to "flip" the fraction and then apply the positive exponent: $(a/b)^{-n} = (b/a)^n$.

  • Using this shortcut for $(2/a)^{-3}$: $(2/a)^{-3} = (a/2)^3 = a^3/2^3 = a^3/8$.

Simplifying with Multiple Rules

Exponent rules are often used in combination to simplify more complex expressions.

Example: Simplifying an Expression Simplify $6x^3 y^{-2} / (2x^{-1}y^4)$. Write the answer with positive exponents only.

Solution Steps:

  1. Group like terms: $(6/2) \cdot (x^3/x^{-1}) \cdot (y^{-2}/y^4)$
  2. Apply quotient rule for coefficients: $6/2 = 3$
  3. Apply quotient rule for $x$ terms: $x^{3 - (-1)} = x^{3+1} = x^4$
  4. Apply quotient rule for $y$ terms: $y^{-2 - 4} = y^{-6}$
  5. Combine terms: $3x^4y^{-6}$
  6. Convert negative exponent to positive: $3x^4 \cdot (1/y^6) = 3x^4/y^6$

Final Answer: $3x^4/y^6$

Checkpoint Practice:

  • Simplify $a^5b^{-3} / (a^2b^{-1})$. Write the answer with positive exponents only.
    • Answer: $a^{5-2}b^{-3-(-1)} = a^3b^{-3+1} = a^3b^{-2} = a^3/b^2$

Complete Exponent Rules: This section, along with previous ones, lays out a complete set of rules for working with integer exponents, allowing for simplification of a wide variety of algebraic expressions involving powers. It's crucial to understand how to apply each rule individually and in combination.

Review Questions

  1. Explain, using the quotient rule for exponents, why $a^0$ must be equal to $1$ for any nonzero number $a$.
  2. What is the fundamental meaning of a negative exponent? How does it differ from a positive exponent, and how does it relate to the concept of a reciprocal? Provide an example.
  3. Evaluate the following expressions and explain the reasoning for each result:
    • a) $(-6)^0$
    • b) $-6^0$
    • c) $(5xy)^0$, where $x \neq 0, y \neq 0$
  4. Simplify the expression $(m/n)^{-4}$ using two different methods: first by applying the power of a quotient rule and then the negative exponent rule, and second by using the "flipping" shortcut. Show that both methods yield the same result.
  5. Simplify the expression $\frac{10a^{-2}b^5}{2a^3b^{-1}}$ completely, ensuring your final answer contains only positive exponents. Describe each step you take.
