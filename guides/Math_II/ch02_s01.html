Chapter 2, Section 1: Rational Numbers on the Number Line Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces rational numbers, defining them as numbers that can be expressed as a fraction of two integers. It explores the connection between rational numbers and their decimal representations, noting that they always result in either terminating or repeating decimals. Finally, the section discusses how to plot rational numbers on a number line and highlights the "density property," which states that there are infinitely many rational numbers between any two distinct rational numbers.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of a Rational Number: A number that can be written in the form $a/b$, where $a$ and $b$ are integers and $b$ is not equal to zero.
  • Integers as Rational Numbers: Every integer $n$ is a rational number because it can be written as $n/1$.
  • Terminating Decimals: A decimal representation of a rational number that has a finite number of decimal places (it ends).
  • Repeating Decimals: A decimal representation of a rational number that has a block of one or more digits that repeats forever, often indicated by a bar over the repeating block.
  • Decimal Expansion Property: A number is rational if and only if its decimal expansion either terminates or repeats.
  • Plotting Rational Numbers: Every rational number corresponds to a unique point on the number line, and fractions can be plotted by dividing unit intervals into equal parts.
  • Density Property of Rational Numbers: Between any two distinct rational numbers, there is always another rational number, and in fact, infinitely many rational numbers.

Important Terms

  • Rational Number: Any number that can be expressed as a fraction $a/b$, where $a$ and $b$ are integers and $b \neq 0$. The set of rational numbers is denoted by $Q$.
  • Terminating Decimal: A decimal number that has a finite number of digits after the decimal point; it eventually comes to an end. Examples include $0.25$ or $0.375$.
  • Repeating Decimal: A decimal number in which a digit or a block of digits repeats infinitely after the decimal point. This repeating block is typically indicated by a bar placed over it, e.g., $0.\overline{3}$ or $0.\overline{18}$.
  • Density Property: A characteristic of the rational numbers (and real numbers) stating that between any two distinct numbers in the set, there exists at least one other number from the same set. For rational numbers, this means there are infinitely many rational numbers between any two given rational numbers.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Rational Numbers

In previous math courses, you worked with whole numbers, integers, and fractions. This chapter delves deeper into the set of rational numbers, which encompasses all these types of numbers and more. Understanding rational numbers is fundamental to higher mathematics.

Defining Rational Numbers

A rational number is formally defined as any number that can be written in the form $a/b$, where $a$ and $b$ are integers and $b \neq 0$. The set of rational numbers is symbolized by $Q$.

  • Why Integers are Rational: This definition is quite broad. Any integer $n$ can be written as a fraction with a denominator of $1$. For example, $5 = 5/1$, $-3 = -3/1$, and $0 = 0/1$. Therefore, all integers are rational numbers.

Decimals and Rational Numbers

There's a crucial relationship between fractions and decimals. When you divide the numerator of a fraction by its denominator, the resulting decimal will always fall into one of two categories:

  1. Terminating Decimal: A decimal that has a finite number of decimal places; it ends.

    • Examples:
      • $1/4 = 0.25$
      • $3/8 = 0.375$
      • $7/20 = 0.35$

  2. Repeating Decimal: A decimal that has a block of one or more digits that repeats forever. We denote the repeating block by placing a bar over it.

    • Examples:
      • $1/3 = 0.\overline{3}$
      • $2/11 = 0.\overline{18}$
      • $5/6 = 0.8\overline{3}$

Key Property: Decimals and Rational Numbers A number is rational if and only if its decimal expansion either terminates or repeats. This means:

  • If a number is rational, its decimal form will terminate or repeat.
  • If a number's decimal form terminates or repeats, then the number is rational.

Classifying Decimals Example: Let's look at some examples to classify numbers as rational or not:

  • $0.75$: This is a terminating decimal. Since it ends, it is rational. It can be written as $3/4$.
  • $0.\overline{142857}$: This is a repeating decimal (the block "142857" repeats). Since it repeats, it is rational. It can be written as $1/7$.
  • $0.010010001...$ (the number of zeros increases each time): This decimal neither terminates nor repeats (the pattern of zeros changes each time). Therefore, it is not a rational number.

Checkpoint: Is $0.\overline{6}$ a rational number? If so, write it as a fraction.

  • Answer: Yes, $0.\overline{6}$ is a repeating decimal, so it is rational. It can be written as $2/3$.

Plotting Rational Numbers on the Number Line

Every rational number corresponds to a unique point on the number line. To plot a fraction, you can convert it to a decimal or consider the unit intervals.

  • Method: Divide the appropriate unit interval (the space between two consecutive integers) into equal parts corresponding to the denominator of the fraction.
  • Example: Plot $-3/2$, $2/5$, $1.75$ on the number line.
    • $-3/2 = -1.5$: This is halfway between $-2$ and $-1$.
    • $2/5 = 0.4$: This is two-fifths of the way from $0$ to $1$.
    • $1.75 = 1 \text{ and } 3/4$: This is three-fourths of the way from $1$ to $2$.

Density of Rational Numbers

The Density Property of rational numbers is a fascinating characteristic:

  • Between any two distinct rational numbers, there is always another rational number.
  • In fact, there are infinitely many rational numbers between any two rational numbers, no matter how close they are.

This property implies that the rational numbers are "packed tightly" on the number line; there are no "gaps" that can't be filled by another rational number.

Finding a Rational Number Between Two Others One simple way to find a rational number between two given rational numbers is to calculate their average.

  • Example: Find a rational number between $1/3$ and $1/2$.
    • Average: $(1/3 + 1/2) / 2$
    • Find a common denominator for the sum: $(2/6 + 3/6) / 2$
    • Sum the fractions: $(5/6) / 2$
    • Divide by 2 (multiply by $1/2$): $5/12$
    • To check: $1/3 = 4/12$ and $1/2 = 6/12$. Since $4/12 < 5/12 < 6/12$, $5/12$ is indeed between $1/3$ and $1/2$.

Checkpoint: Find a rational number between $3/4$ and $4/5$.

  • Answer: Average $= (3/4 + 4/5) / 2 = (15/20 + 16/20) / 2 = (31/20) / 2 = 31/40$.

Review Questions

  1. Define what a rational number is using the specified mathematical form. Explain why all integers, including zero, fit this definition.
  2. Describe the two possible types of decimal expansions that all rational numbers must have. Provide an original example for each type.
  3. How can you determine if a number, given in its decimal form, is rational or not? Illustrate with an example of a rational decimal and an example of a non-rational decimal.
  4. Explain the process of plotting a rational number like $5/3$ or $-0.75$ on a number line. What is the significance of the unit intervals in this process?
  5. What does the "Density Property" of rational numbers mean in practical terms when thinking about the number line? If you are given two very close rational numbers, say $0.12$ and $0.13$, how can you easily find another rational number between them?
