Scientific Notation Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces scientific notation as a compact and efficient method for writing very large or very small numbers, which are common in scientific contexts. It covers the process of converting numbers between standard form and scientific notation, performing multiplication and division using this notation, and understanding the concept of orders of magnitude for comparing quantities.

Key Concepts

  • Scientific Notation Definition: A way to express numbers as a product of two parts: a coefficient (a number between 1 and 10, including 1) and a power of 10. The format is $a \times 10^n$, where $1 \le a < 10$ and $n$ is an integer.
  • Converting to Scientific Notation: Involves moving the decimal point to create the coefficient $a$ (with exactly one nonzero digit to its left) and counting the number of places moved to determine the exponent $n$. The sign of $n$ depends on whether the original number was very large (positive $n$) or very small (negative $n$).
  • Converting to Standard Form: The reverse process, where the decimal point is moved to the right for positive $n$ or to the left for negative $n$, adding zeros as necessary.
  • Multiplying in Scientific Notation: Multiply the coefficients ($a$ and $b$) and add the exponents ($m$ and $n$). The product $(ab) \times 10^{m+n}$ might need adjustment if $ab$ is not between 1 and 10.
  • Dividing in Scientific Notation: Divide the coefficients ($a$ by $b$) and subtract the exponents ($m$ minus $n$). The quotient $(a/b) \times 10^{m-n}$ might also need adjustment if $a/b$ is not between 1 and 10.
  • Orders of Magnitude: Represents the power of 10 closest to a given number. It's used to compare quantities; numbers that differ by roughly a factor of 10 differ by one order of magnitude.

Important Terms

  • Scientific Notation: A method of writing very large or very small numbers compactly as $a \times 10^n$, where $1 \le a < 10$ and $n$ is an integer.
  • Order of Magnitude: The power of 10 that is closest to a given number. It provides a quick way to estimate and compare the relative sizes of numbers.

Detailed Notes

Why Scientific Notation?

Very large numbers (like the distance from Earth to the Sun: 150,000,000 km) and very small numbers (like the diameter of a hydrogen atom: 0.000000000106 m) are common in science. Writing these numbers out with all their zeros is cumbersome and can lead to errors. Scientific notation offers a concise and standardized way to express them.

Defining Scientific Notation

A number is in scientific notation if it is written in the form $a \times 10^n$, where:

  • $a$ (the coefficient): Must be a number such that $1 \le a < 10$. This means $a$ will have exactly one non-zero digit to the left of the decimal point.
  • $n$ (the exponent): Must be an integer (positive, negative, or zero).

Converting from Standard Form to Scientific Notation

To convert a number from its standard form to scientific notation:

  1. Locate the decimal point: Even if not explicitly written (e.g., in 93,000,000, the decimal is understood to be at the end).
  2. Move the decimal point: Shift it until there is only one nonzero digit to its left. The resulting number is your coefficient $a$.
  3. Count the places moved: The number of places you moved the decimal point determines the absolute value of your exponent $n$.
  4. Determine the sign of $n$:
    • If the original number was very large (greater than or equal to 10), the exponent $n$ is positive. This indicates you moved the decimal point to the left.
    • If the original number was very small (between 0 and 1), the exponent $n$ is negative. This indicates you moved the decimal point to the right.

Examples:

  • $93,000,000$: Move the decimal 7 places to the left to get $9.3$. Since $93,000,000$ is a large number, $n$ is positive. So, $9.3 \times 10^7$.
  • $0.000,47$: Move the decimal 4 places to the right to get $4.7$. Since $0.000,47$ is a small number, $n$ is negative. So, $4.7 \times 10^{-4}$.
  • $602,000,000,000,000,000,000,000$: Move the decimal 23 places to the left to get $6.02$. Since it's a large number, $n$ is positive. So, $6.02 \times 10^{23}$.

Converting from Scientific Notation to Standard Form

To convert a number from scientific notation ($a \times 10^n$) back to standard form:

  1. Examine the exponent $n$:
    • If $n$ is positive, move the decimal point in $a$ to the right $n$ times. Add zeros as placeholders if needed.
    • If $n$ is negative, move the decimal point in $a$ to the left $n$ times (treating $n$ as its absolute value). Add zeros as placeholders if needed.

Examples:

  • $3.6 \times 10^5$: The exponent is $5$ (positive). Move the decimal in $3.6$ five places to the right. $3.6 \rightarrow 360,000$.
  • $7.1 \times 10^{-3}$: The exponent is $-3$ (negative). Move the decimal in $7.1$ three places to the left. $7.1 \rightarrow 0.0071$.

Multiplying and Dividing in Scientific Notation

Arithmetic operations are simplified by using exponent rules.

Multiplication

To multiply two numbers in scientific notation, $(a \times 10^m)(b \times 10^n)$:

  1. Multiply the coefficients: Multiply $a$ by $b$.
  2. Add the exponents: Add $m$ and $n$ to get the new power of 10.
  3. Combine: The result is $(ab) \times 10^{m+n}$.
  4. Adjust if necessary: If the product $(ab)$ is not between $1$ and $10$, adjust it by moving the decimal and changing the exponent $m+n$ accordingly.
    • If $ab \ge 10$, move the decimal one place left and add 1 to the exponent.
    • If $ab < 1$, move the decimal one place right and subtract 1 from the exponent.

Example:

  • Compute $(3 \times 10^4)(2.5 \times 10^6)$:
    • Multiply coefficients: $3 \times 2.5 = 7.5$.
    • Add exponents: $4 + 6 = 10$.
    • Result: $7.5 \times 10^{10}$. (No adjustment needed as $7.5$ is between 1 and 10).
  • Adjustment Example (from Checkpoint): Compute $(4 \times 10^5)(3 \times 10^{-2})$:
    • Multiply coefficients: $4 \times 3 = 12$.
    • Add exponents: $5 + (-2) = 3$.
    • Initial result: $12 \times 10^3$.
    • Adjust: Since $12$ is not between $1$ and $10$, we rewrite $12$ as $1.2 \times 10^1$.
    • Final result: $(1.2 \times 10^1) \times 10^3 = 1.2 \times 10^{1+3} = 1.2 \times 10^4$.

Division

To divide two numbers in scientific notation, $(a \times 10^m) / (b \times 10^n)$:

  1. Divide the coefficients: Divide $a$ by $b$.
  2. Subtract the exponents: Subtract $n$ from $m$.
  3. Combine: The result is $(a/b) \times 10^{m-n}$.
  4. Adjust if necessary: Similar to multiplication, if the quotient $(a/b)$ is not between $1$ and $10$, adjust it and the exponent.

Example:

  • Compute $(8.4 \times 10^9) / (2.1 \times 10^3)$:
    • Divide coefficients: $8.4 / 2.1 = 4$.
    • Subtract exponents: $9 - 3 = 6$.
    • Result: $4 \times 10^6$. (No adjustment needed as $4$ is between 1 and 10).

Orders of Magnitude

The order of magnitude of a number is simply the power of $10$ closest to that number. It provides a quick way to compare the approximate sizes of quantities. If two quantities differ by one order of magnitude, it means one is roughly 10 times larger (or smaller) than the other.

Example:

  • Earth to Sun distance: $1.5 \times 10^8$ km. Jupiter to Sun distance: $7.8 \times 10^8$ km.
  • To compare, divide Jupiter's distance by Earth's distance: $(7.8 \times 10^8) / (1.5 \times 10^8) = (7.8 / 1.5) \times 10^{8-8} = 5.2 \times 10^0 = 5.2$.
  • Jupiter is about $5.2$ times farther from the Sun than Earth. Since $5.2$ is less than $10$, their distances are considered to be the same order of magnitude. Both are closest to $10^8$.

Scientific Calculators and Notation

Many scientific calculators display scientific notation using an "E" (or "EE") symbol. For example, $3.6 \times 10^5$ might be shown as 3.6E5. The "E" stands for "exponent" (of 10).
