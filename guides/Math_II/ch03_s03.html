Study Guide: Markup, Discount, and Commission

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental business concepts of markup, discount, and commission, all of which are calculated using percentages. You will learn how to determine selling prices, sale prices, and earned commissions, and practice solving multi-step problems that combine these ideas with sales tax, understanding the correct order of operations for calculations.

Key Concepts

  • Markup: The amount added to a product's cost by a business to determine its selling price. It's usually expressed as a percentage (markup rate) of the cost.
  • Discount: A reduction in the original price of an item, resulting in a lower sale price. It's typically expressed as a percentage (discount rate) of the original price.
  • Commission: An amount of money earned by an individual (often a salesperson) based on a percentage (commission rate) of their total sales.
  • Multi-Step Problems: Many real-world scenarios involve multiple percentage calculations, such as an item being discounted and then having sales tax applied. It's crucial to perform these calculations in the correct order.
  • Order of Operations for Discount and Tax: When an item is discounted and then subject to sales tax, the discount is always applied first to find the sale price, and then the sales tax is calculated on that reduced sale price.

Important Terms

  • Markup: The amount added to the cost of an item to determine its selling price.
  • Markup Rate: The percentage of the cost that is added as markup.
    • Formula: markup = markup rate × cost
  • Selling Price: The price at which a business sells an item to a customer.
    • Formula: selling price = cost + markup
  • Discount: The amount subtracted from the original price of an item to arrive at the sale price.
  • Discount Rate: The percentage of the original price that is subtracted as a discount.
    • Formula: discount = discount rate × original price
  • Original Price: The regular, non-discounted price of an item.
  • Sale Price: The price of an item after a discount has been applied.
    • Formula: sale price = original price - discount
  • Commission: An amount of money earned by an individual, typically based on a percentage of their total sales.
  • Commission Rate: The percentage of total sales that an individual earns as commission.
    • Formula: commission = commission rate × total sales
  • Cost: The price a business pays for an item before any markup.
  • Sales Tax: A percentage charged by a government on the sale of goods and services, typically added to the final sale price.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Markup

Businesses need to make a profit to operate. They achieve this by buying products at one price (the cost) and selling them at a higher price (the selling price). The difference between these two prices is the markup.

  • Calculation:

    • selling price = cost + markup
    • The markup rate is a percentage of the cost.
    • markup = markup rate × cost

  • Example: Finding Selling Price with Markup

    • A store buys shoes for $$36$ and applies a $75%$ markup.
    • Step 1: Calculate the markup amount.
      • markup = 0.75 × $36 = $27
    • Step 2: Calculate the selling price.
      • selling price = $36 (cost) + $27 (markup) = $63
    • Alternative Formula: selling price = cost × (1 + markup rate)
      • selling price = $36 × (1 + 0.75) = $36 × 1.75 = $63

Understanding Discount

When items go on sale, their price is reduced. This reduction is called a discount.

  • Calculation:

    • sale price = original price - discount
    • The discount rate is a percentage of the original price.
    • discount = discount rate × original price

  • Example: Finding Sale Price with Discount

    • A television has a regular price of $$480$ and is on sale for $30%$ off.
    • Step 1: Calculate the discount amount.
      • discount = 0.30 × $480 = $144
    • Step 2: Calculate the sale price.
      • sale price = $480 (original price) - $144 (discount) = $336
    • Alternative Method (using a multiplier): If an item is $30%$ off, you are paying $100% - 30% = 70%$ of the original price.
      • sale price = original price × (1 - discount rate)
      • sale price = $480 × (1 - 0.30) = $480 × 0.70 = $336 This is often a quicker way to calculate the sale price directly.

Understanding Commission

Many salespeople earn a commission, which is a payment based on the amount of sales they generate.

  • Calculation:

    • commission = commission rate × total sales

  • Example: Commission Problem

    • A real estate agent earns a $3.5%$ commission. The agent sells a home for $$260,000$.
    • Step 1: Calculate the commission earned.
      • commission = 0.035 × $260,000 = $9,100

Multi-Step Problems: Discount and Tax

In real-world scenarios, you often encounter situations where both a discount and sales tax apply. It is critical to perform these calculations in the correct order.

  • Rule: Sales tax is always calculated on the final, discounted price, not the original price. The customer pays tax on the amount they actually pay.

  • Example: Discount Then Tax

    • A coat has a regular price of $$120$. It is $25%$ off, and the sales tax rate is $7%$. Find the total cost.
    • Step 1: Find the sale price (after discount).
      • The coat is $25%$ off, so the customer pays $100% - 25% = 75%$ of the original price.
      • sale price = $120 × 0.75 = $90
    • Step 2: Calculate the sales tax on the sale price.
      • sales tax = $90 × 0.07 = $6.30
    • Step 3: Add the tax to the sale price to find the total cost.
      • total cost = $90 (sale price) + $6.30 (tax) = $96.30

Key Formulas Summary:

  • Selling Price = Cost × (1 + Markup Rate)
  • Sale Price = Original Price × (1 - Discount Rate)
  • Commission = Commission Rate × Total Sales
  • Tax is applied after any discount.

Practice Checkpoint

  • A bookstore buys a textbook for $$18$ and applies a $60%$ markup. What is the selling price?
    • Solution:
      • Markup amount = $0.60 \times $18 = $10.80$
      • Selling price = $$18 + $10.80 = $28.80$

Important Caution

Never calculate sales tax on the original price if an item is discounted. The tax should only apply to the actual amount the customer pays after all discounts.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the difference between markup and discount. How do their respective rates relate to the "cost" or "original price" in their calculations?
  2. A bicycle store buys a bike for $$150$ and marks it up by $80%$. What is the selling price of the bike? If the store later offers a $15%$ discount on this selling price, what would be the new sale price?
  3. A salesperson earns a $6%$ commission on all sales. If they sold $$15,000$ worth of merchandise last month, how much commission did they earn?
  4. Describe the steps involved in calculating the total cost of an item that is on sale and subject to sales tax. Why is the order of these steps important?
  5. A jacket originally priced at $$95$ is on sale for $30%$ off. The sales tax rate is $8.5%$. Calculate the final total cost a customer would pay for the jacket. Show your work step-by-step.
