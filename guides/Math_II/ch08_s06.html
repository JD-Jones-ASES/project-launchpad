Study Guide: Slope-Intercept Form

Overview

This section introduces the slope-intercept form of a linear equation, $y = mx + b$, which is a highly versatile way to represent straight lines. It explains how to identify the slope ($m$) and $y$-intercept ($b$) from an equation, how to use these values to quickly graph a line, and how to write the equation of a line given a graph, two points, or a real-world scenario.

Key Concepts

  • Slope-Intercept Form: A linear equation written as $y = mx + b$, where $m$ represents the slope and $b$ represents the $y$-coordinate of the $y$-intercept.
  • Identifying Slope ($m$) and $y$-intercept ($b$): When an equation is in $y = mx + b$ form, the coefficient of $x$ is the slope ($m$), and the constant term is the $y$-intercept ($b$). The $y$-intercept is always an ordered pair $(0, b)$.
  • Graphing from Slope-Intercept Form: To graph a line, first plot the $y$-intercept $(0, b)$. Then, use the slope $m$ (expressed as a fraction $\frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}}$) to find a second point by moving "rise" units vertically and "run" units horizontally from the $y$-intercept.
  • Writing an Equation from a Graph: Locate the $y$-intercept $(0, b)$ on the graph to find $b$. Then, choose two clear points on the line and calculate the slope $m$ using the formula $m = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}}$ or $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$. Substitute $m$ and $b$ into $y = mx + b$.
  • Linear Models (Applications): Real-world situations with a fixed initial value (base fee, starting amount) and a constant rate of change (cost per item, speed) can be modeled using slope-intercept form. The fixed value is typically the $y$-intercept ($b$), and the rate of change is the slope ($m$).
  • Writing an Equation from Two Points: First, calculate the slope $m$ using the two given points. Then, substitute the calculated slope $m$ and the coordinates of one of the points $(x, y)$ into $y = mx + b$ to solve for $b$. Finally, write the equation using the found $m$ and $b$.

Important Terms

  • Slope-Intercept Form: The format of a linear equation, $y = mx + b$, where the slope and $y$-intercept are directly visible.
  • Slope ($m$): A measure of the steepness and direction of a line, calculated as the ratio of vertical change (rise) to horizontal change (run). It represents the rate of change.
  • $y$-intercept ($b$): The point where a line crosses the $y$-axis. Its coordinates are always $(0, b)$. The value $b$ is the $y$-coordinate of this point.
  • Linear Equation: An equation whose graph is a straight line.
  • Rate of Change: How one quantity changes in relation to another; represented by the slope ($m$) in linear equations.
  • Ordered Pair: A pair of numbers $(x, y)$ that describes the location of a point on the coordinate plane.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Slope-Intercept Form

The slope-intercept form, $y = mx + b$, is a fundamental way to express linear equations. It's incredibly useful because it directly reveals two key characteristics of a line: its slope and where it crosses the $y$-axis. This makes graphing lines and creating linear models much more straightforward.

The Slope-Intercept Form Property

A linear equation written as $y = mx + b$ is in slope-intercept form.

  • $m$ represents the slope of the line.
  • $b$ represents the $y$-intercept (specifically, the $y$-coordinate of the point $(0, b)$ where the line crosses the $y$-axis).

Identifying Slope and $y$-Intercept

To find $m$ and $b$, simply look at the equation once it's arranged in the form $y = mx + b$.

Examples:

  1. $y = 4x - 7$
    • Here, $m = 4$.
    • The constant term is $-7$, so $b = -7$.
    • The $y$-intercept is the point $(0, -7)$.
  2. $y = -23x + 5$
    • Here, $m = -23$.
    • The constant term is $5$, so $b = 5$.
    • The $y$-intercept is the point $(0, 5)$.
  3. $y = x$
    • This can be written as $y = 1x + 0$.
    • Here, $m = 1$.
    • The constant term is $0$, so $b = 0$.
    • The $y$-intercept is the point $(0, 0)$, which is the origin.

Graphing Using Slope and $y$-Intercept

Graphing a line using its slope and $y$-intercept is a two-step process:

  1. Plot the $y$-intercept: Locate the point $(0, b)$ on the $y$-axis and mark it.
  2. Use the slope to find a second point: Remember that slope $m = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}}$.
    • From the $y$-intercept, count "rise" units vertically (up for positive rise, down for negative rise).
    • Then, count "run" units horizontally (right for positive run, left for negative run).
    • Mark this new point.
  3. Draw the line: Connect the two points with a straight line and extend it in both directions.

Example: Graph $y = \frac{3}{2}x - 1$

  1. The $y$-intercept is $b = -1$, so plot the point $(0, -1)$.
  2. The slope is $m = \frac{3}{2}$. This means "rise 3, run 2".
    • From $(0, -1)$, move up 3 units (to $y=2$).
    • Then, move right 2 units (to $x=2$).
    • This brings you to the point $(2, 2)$.
  3. Draw a line through $(0, -1)$ and $(2, 2)$.

Checkpoint Example: Graph $y = -2x + 4$

  1. The $y$-intercept is $b = 4$, so plot $(0, 4)$.
  2. The slope is $m = -2$. This can be written as $\frac{-2}{1}$. This means "rise -2 (go down 2), run 1".
    • From $(0, 4)$, move down 2 units (to $y=2$).
    • Then, move right 1 unit (to $x=1$).
    • This brings you to the point $(1, 2)$.
  3. Draw a line through $(0, 4)$ and $(1, 2)$.

Writing an Equation from a Graph

If you have a graph of a line, you can determine its equation in slope-intercept form by identifying its $y$-intercept and calculating its slope.

  1. Find $b$: Look for where the line crosses the $y$-axis. This will be the point $(0, b)$.
  2. Find $m$: Choose two distinct points on the line. Use the slope formula: $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$. Alternatively, count the rise and run between the two points.
  3. Substitute into $y = mx + b$: Once you have $m$ and $b$, plug them into the general form.

Example: A line crosses the $y$-axis at $(0, 2)$ and also passes through $(3, 8)$. Write its equation.

  1. The $y$-intercept is given as $(0, 2)$, so $b = 2$.
  2. To find the slope, use the points $(0, 2)$ and $(3, 8)$: $m = \frac{8 - 2}{3 - 0} = \frac{6}{3} = 2$.
  3. Substitute $m = 2$ and $b = 2$ into $y = mx + b$: The equation is $y = 2x + 2$.

Linear Models: Cost Equation

Many real-world situations can be modeled with linear equations. Often, there's a fixed starting value (the $y$-intercept) and a constant rate of change (the slope).

Example: A streaming service charges a $5 monthly base fee plus $2 per movie rented. Write a cost equation $C$ in terms of movies $m$.

  • Base fee: The $5 is a fixed cost, regardless of how many movies are rented. This is the initial value, so it represents the $C$-intercept (or $y$-intercept), $b = 5$.
  • Cost per movie: The $2 per movie is the rate of change. This is the slope, $m = 2$.
  • Equation: $C = 2m + 5$.
    • The slope is $2$ (cost per movie).
    • The $C$-intercept is $(0, 5)$ (the base fee for 0 movies).

Writing an Equation from Two Points

When you're given two points on a line, you can find its equation in slope-intercept form using a two-step process:

  1. Calculate the slope ($m$): Use the slope formula with the two given points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$: $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$.
  2. Find the $y$-intercept ($b$): Substitute the calculated slope ($m$) and the coordinates of one of the given points $(x, y)$ into the slope-intercept form $y = mx + b$. Then, solve the equation for $b$.
  3. Write the equation: Once you have both $m$ and $b$, write the final equation in the form $y = mx + b$.

Example: A line passes through $(1, 4)$ and $(5, 12)$. Write its equation.

  1. Find the slope: $m = \frac{12 - 4}{5 - 1} = \frac{8}{4} = 2$.
  2. Find the $y$-intercept ($b$): Use $m = 2$ and one of the points, for example, $(1, 4)$: $y = mx + b$ $4 = 2(1) + b$ $4 = 2 + b$ $b = 2$
  3. Write the equation: The equation is $y = 2x + 2$.

Checkpoint Example: Write the equation of the line through $(-2, 1)$ and $(4, 7)$.

  1. Find the slope: $m = \frac{7 - 1}{4 - (-2)} = \frac{6}{6} = 1$.
  2. Find the $y$-intercept ($b$): Use $m = 1$ and the point $(-2, 1)$: $y = mx + b$ $1 = 1(-2) + b$ $1 = -2 + b$ $b = 3$
  3. Write the equation: The equation is $y = x + 3$.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words what the slope ($m$) and $y$-intercept ($b$) represent in the equation $y = mx + b$, both mathematically and conceptually.
  2. Given the equation $y = -\frac{1}{3}x + 2$: a. Identify the slope and the $y$-intercept. b. Describe the steps you would take to graph this line.
  3. A line passes through the point $(0, -3)$ and has a slope of $4$. What is its equation in slope-intercept form? How would you use this information to find a second point on the line?
  4. A company charges a fixed setup fee of $100 for a service, plus an hourly rate of $50. a. Write a linear equation in slope-intercept form to model the total cost $C$ in terms of hours worked $h$. b. What do the slope and $y$-intercept represent in this specific context?
  5. A line passes through the points $(-1, 5)$ and $(3, -3)$. Write the equation of this line in slope-intercept form, showing all your steps.
