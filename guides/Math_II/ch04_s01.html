Simplifying Algebraic Expressions: A Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concepts of algebraic expressions, focusing on how to simplify them by combining like terms. You will learn key vocabulary such as terms, coefficients, constants, and like terms, and then practice applying the rules for combining these terms in various algebraic expressions. Mastering this skill is crucial for solving equations and further algebraic work.

Key Concepts

  • Algebraic Expressions: These are mathematical phrases built from numbers, variables (letters representing unknown values), and mathematical operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, exponents). They do not contain an equals sign.
  • Terms and Their Components: An algebraic expression is made up of one or more terms. A term is a single number, a single variable, or a product of numbers and variables. The coefficient is the numerical factor in a term, and a constant term is a term that does not contain any variables.
  • Like Terms: Terms are considered "like terms" if they have exactly the same variable parts, meaning the same variables raised to the same powers. For example, $3x$ and $-7x$ are like terms, but $3x$ and $3x^2$ are not.
  • Combining Like Terms: The core principle of simplifying expressions is that only like terms can be combined. To combine them, you add or subtract their coefficients while keeping the variable part exactly the same.
  • Simplification Process: Simplifying an expression involves identifying like terms, grouping them together, and then performing the indicated operations (addition or subtraction) on their coefficients.

Important Terms

  • Algebraic Expression: A mathematical phrase that combines numbers, variables, and operations (like addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division). Examples include $3x + 5$ or $y^2 - 4y + 7$.
  • Term: A single number, a single variable, or a product of numbers and variables within an algebraic expression. Terms are separated by addition or subtraction signs.
  • Coefficient: The numerical factor that multiplies the variable(s) in a term. For example, in the term $5x^2$, the coefficient is $5$. In the term $-3y$, the coefficient is $-3$. If a variable has no number written in front of it (like $x$), its coefficient is $1$.
  • Constant Term: A term in an algebraic expression that does not contain any variables. It is simply a number. For example, in $2x + 7$, the term $7$ is a constant term.
  • Like Terms: Terms that have exactly the same variable parts, including the same variables raised to the same powers. For instance, $4xy$ and $-2xy$ are like terms, but $4xy$ and $4x$ are not.

Detailed Notes

What is an Algebraic Expression?

An algebraic expression is essentially a mathematical statement built using:

  • Numbers: Such as $1, -5, 1/2$.
  • Variables: Letters (like $x, y, a, n$) that represent unknown values.
  • Operations: Addition (+), subtraction (-), multiplication (often implied, e.g., $3x$), division (/), and exponents (e.g., $x^2$).

The main goal of simplifying expressions is to make them easier to work with, often by reducing the number of terms.

Understanding the Components of Terms

Every term in an expression has a specific structure:

  • Term: A product of a number and one or more variables. For example, in $5x^2 - 3x + 7$:
    • $5x^2$ is a term.
    • $-3x$ is a term.
    • $7$ is a term.
  • Coefficient: This is the numerical part of a term that multiplies the variable(s).
    • In $5x^2$, the coefficient is $5$.
    • In $-3x$, the coefficient is $-3$.
    • In $7$, the coefficient is $7$ (as it's a constant term).
  • Constant Term: This is a term that has no variable part; it's just a number.
    • In the example $5x^2 - 3x + 7$, the term $7$ is the constant term.

Identifying Like Terms: The Key to Simplification

The concept of "like terms" is fundamental because only like terms can be combined.

  • Definition: Like terms must have exactly the same variable part. This means:

    1. They must contain the same variables.
    2. Each variable must be raised to the same power.

  • Examples of Like Terms:

    • $2x$ and $-5x$ (same variable $x$ to the power of 1)
    • $7y^2$ and $y^2$ (same variable $y$ to the power of 2)
    • $3ab$ and $1/2 ab$ (same variables $a$ and $b$ to the power of 1)
    • $4$ and $-10$ (both are constant terms, so they have no variable part, making them "like")

  • Examples of Unlike Terms (and why):

    • $3x$ and $3x^2$: Not like terms because the exponents of $x$ are different ($1$ vs. $2$).
    • $4xy$ and $4x$: Not like terms because one has variable $y$ and the other does not.
    • $5p^2q$ and $5pq^2$: Not like terms because the powers of $p$ and $q$ are different in each term.

The Process of Combining Like Terms

Once you can identify like terms, combining them is straightforward:

  1. Identify: Scan the expression and identify all the terms.
  2. Group: Mentally or physically (by rewriting) group the like terms together. It's often helpful to keep the signs associated with each term.
  3. Combine Coefficients: Add or subtract the coefficients of the like terms.
  4. Keep Variable Part: The variable part of the combined term remains unchanged.

General Rule: $ax^n + bx^n = (a + b)x^n$

Let's look at the examples:

Example 1: Basic Combining Simplify $4x + 7 - 2x + 3$.

  • Identify like terms: $(4x, -2x)$ and $(7, 3)$.
  • Group: $(4x - 2x) + (7 + 3)$
  • Combine coefficients:
    • For $x$ terms: $(4 - 2)x = 2x$
    • For constant terms: $(7 + 3) = 10$
  • Simplified expression: $2x + 10$

Example 2: Multiple Variables Simplify $3a + 5b - a + 2b - 4$.

  • Identify like terms: $(3a, -a)$, $(5b, 2b)$, and $(-4)$.
  • Group: $(3a - a) + (5b + 2b) + (-4)$
  • Combine coefficients:
    • For $a$ terms: $(3 - 1)a = 2a$ (Remember, $-a$ has a coefficient of $-1$)
    • For $b$ terms: $(5 + 2)b = 7b$
    • For constant term: $-4$ (no other constant terms to combine with)
  • Simplified expression: $2a + 7b - 4$

Example 3: Terms with Exponents Simplify $6x^2 + 3x - 2x^2 + x - 5$.

  • Identify like terms: $(6x^2, -2x^2)$, $(3x, x)$, and $(-5)$.
  • Group: $(6x^2 - 2x^2) + (3x + x) + (-5)$
  • Combine coefficients:
    • For $x^2$ terms: $(6 - 2)x^2 = 4x^2$
    • For $x$ terms: $(3 + 1)x = 4x$ (Remember, $x$ has a coefficient of $1$)
    • For constant term: $-5$
  • Simplified expression: $4x^2 + 4x - 5$

Example 4: Simplifying with Fractional Coefficients Simplify $1/2n + 2/3n - 4$.

  • Identify like terms: $(1/2n, 2/3n)$ and $(-4)$.
  • Group: $(1/2n + 2/3n) + (-4)$
  • Combine coefficients (requires a common denominator):
    • For $n$ terms: $1/2n + 2/3n = 3/6n + 4/6n = (3+4)/6n = 7/6n$
    • For constant term: $-4$
  • Simplified expression: $7/6n - 4$

Checkpoint Practice: Simplify $5y - 3 + 2y + 8$.

  • Solution: Group $(5y + 2y)$ and $(-3 + 8)$.
    • $(5+2)y = 7y$
    • $(-3+8) = 5$
  • Answer: $7y + 5$

Why is this skill important?

Simplifying expressions is not just an exercise; it's a foundational skill. In upcoming sections and chapters, you will use this ability to manipulate and solve equations, work with polynomials, and tackle more complex algebraic problems. A well-simplified expression is often much easier to evaluate or integrate into further calculations.
