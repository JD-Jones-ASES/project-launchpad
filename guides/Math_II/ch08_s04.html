Graphing Linear Equations from Tables

Overview

This section introduces how to graph linear equations in two variables by creating a table of values. It explains the standard form of a linear equation, how to calculate corresponding y-values for chosen x-values, and how to plot these points to draw a straight line. The section also covers identifying the y-intercept and graphing special cases like horizontal and vertical lines.

Key Concepts

  • Linear Equation in Two Variables: An equation that can be written in the form $y = mx + b$, where $m$ and $b$ are real numbers. Its graph is always a straight line on the coordinate plane.
  • Making a Table of Values: A fundamental method for graphing linear equations. It involves selecting several x-values, calculating their corresponding y-values using the equation, and then listing these (x, y) coordinate pairs in a table.
  • Graphing from a Table: Once a table of values is created, the coordinate pairs are plotted as points on the coordinate plane. These points will lie on a straight line, which is then drawn through them.
  • The $y$-Intercept: The specific point where a line crosses the $y$-axis. At this point, the $x$-coordinate is always 0. In the standard form $y = mx + b$, the $y$-intercept is the point $(0, b)$.
  • Horizontal Lines: Equations of the form $y = c$ (where $c$ is a constant) graph as a horizontal line. Every point on this line has the same $y$-coordinate, $c$.
  • Vertical Lines: Equations of the form $x = c$ (where $c$ is a constant) graph as a vertical line. Every point on this line has the same $x$-coordinate, $c$. Vertical lines are not functions because they fail the vertical line test.

Important Terms

  • Linear Equation in Two Variables: An algebraic equation that, when graphed, forms a straight line. It typically involves two variables (often $x$ and $y$) and can be expressed in the form $y = mx + b$.
  • $y$-Intercept: The point on the coordinate plane where a graph intersects the $y$-axis. For any point on the $y$-axis, its $x$-coordinate is 0. In the equation $y = mx + b$, the $y$-intercept is $(0, b)$.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Linear Equations and Their Graphs

A linear equation in two variables is an equation that produces a straight line when graphed on the coordinate plane. These equations are fundamental in algebra and are commonly written in the slope-intercept form, $y = mx + b$. In this form, $m$ and $b$ represent real numbers that determine the characteristics of the line.

Making a Table of Values to Graph a Line

To graph a linear equation using a table of values, follow these steps:

  1. Choose $x$-values: Select several (typically 3-5) different values for $x$. It's often helpful to choose a mix of negative numbers, zero, and positive numbers to see the line's behavior across different quadrants.
  2. Compute corresponding $y$-values: Substitute each chosen $x$-value into the linear equation and solve for $y$. This will give you a coordinate pair $(x, y)$.
  3. Organize in a table: List your chosen $x$-values and their calculated $y$-values in a table.
  4. Plot the points: Locate each $(x, y)$ coordinate pair on the coordinate plane and mark it with a dot.
  5. Draw the line: Once all points are plotted, use a ruler to draw a straight line that passes through all of them. Extend the line beyond the plotted points and add arrows on both ends to indicate that the line continues infinitely.

Example: Graphing $y = 2x + 1$

Let's choose $x$-values like $-2, -1, 0, 1, 2$.

$x$ $y = 2x + 1$ $y$ Point $(x, y)$
$-2$ $2(-2) + 1$ $-3$ $(-2, -3)$
$-1$ $2(-1) + 1$ $-1$ $(-1, -1)$
$0$ $2(0) + 1$ $1$ $(0, 1)$
$1$ $2(1) + 1$ $3$ $(1, 3)$
$2$ $2(2) + 1$ $5$ $(2, 5)$

After computing the $y$-values, you would plot the points $(-2, -3)$, $(-1, -1)$, $(0, 1)$, $(1, 3)$, and $(2, 5)$ and draw a straight line through them.

The $y$-Intercept

The $y$-intercept is a very important feature of a line. It's the point where the line crosses the $y$-axis. At this point, the $x$-coordinate is always 0. For an equation in the form $y = mx + b$, the $y$-intercept is always the point $(0, b)$. This makes it very easy to identify directly from the equation.

Example: Identifying the $y$-Intercept

  • For $y = 3x - 4$: Here, $b = -4$, so the $y$-intercept is $(0, -4)$.
  • For $y = -12x + 7$: Here, $b = 7$, so the $y$-intercept is $(0, 7)$.
  • For $y = 5x$: This can be written as $y = 5x + 0$, so $b = 0$. The $y$-intercept is $(0, 0)$, which is the origin.

Special Cases: Horizontal and Vertical Lines

Horizontal Lines

An equation of the form $y = c$, where $c$ is any constant number, will always graph as a horizontal line. This means that no matter what value $x$ takes, the $y$-coordinate will always be $c$.

  • The line will pass through the point $(0, c)$ on the $y$-axis.
  • Every point on this line has a $y$-coordinate of $c$. For example, for $y = 3$, points like $(-2, 3)$, $(0, 3)$, and $(4, 3)$ are all on the line.

Vertical Lines

An equation of the form $x = c$, where $c$ is any constant number, will always graph as a vertical line. This means that no matter what value $y$ takes, the $x$-coordinate will always be $c$.

  • The line will pass through the point $(c, 0)$ on the $x$-axis.
  • Every point on this line has an $x$-coordinate of $c$. For example, for $x = -2$, points like $(-2, -1)$, $(-2, 0)$, and $(-2, 4)$ are all on the line.

Important Note: A vertical line is not a function. This is because it fails the vertical line test, meaning a single $x$-value corresponds to multiple (in fact, infinitely many) $y$-values. However, it is still a valid equation whose graph is a straight line.

Example: Graphing Horizontal and Vertical Lines

  • Graph $y = 3$: This is a horizontal line where all $y$-coordinates are 3. It passes through $(0, 3)$.
  • Graph $x = -2$: This is a vertical line where all $x$-coordinates are -2. It passes through $(-2, 0)$.

Application of Graphing from Tables

Linear equations are used to model real-world situations. Creating a table of values and graphing can help visualize these relationships.

Example: Taxi Cost A taxi charges a flat fee of $3.00 plus $1.50 per mile. We can write the cost $C$ as a function of miles $m$: $C = 1.50m + 3$. To make a table for $m = 0, 2, 4, 6$:

$m$ $C = 1.50m + 3$ $C$ Point $(m, C)$
$0$ $1.50(0) + 3$ $3$ $(0, 3)$
$2$ $1.50(2) + 3$ $6$ $(2, 6)$
$4$ $1.50(4) + 3$ $9$ $(4, 9)$
$6$ $1.50(6) + 3$ $12$ $(6, 12)$

The $C$-intercept (which is like the $y$-intercept) is $(0, 3)$. This means that even before traveling any miles (when $m=0$), the cost is $3.00, representing the flat fee.

Review Questions

  2. Describe the step-by-step process for graphing a linear equation using a table of values. Why is it important to choose several points rather than just one or two?
  3. What is the $y$-intercept of a line, and what special property does its coordinate pair always have? Given the equation $y = -5x + 9$, identify its $y$-intercept.
