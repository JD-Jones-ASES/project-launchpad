Writing and Solving Equations from Word Problems: A Study Guide

Overview

This section focuses on the crucial skill of translating real-world scenarios, presented as word problems, into solvable mathematical equations. It outlines a systematic, step-by-step strategy for identifying unknown quantities, defining variables, constructing appropriate equations, solving them, and critically checking the solutions against the original problem context to ensure they make practical sense. This approach equips you to apply algebraic skills to solve practical problems across various domains.

Key Concepts

  • Systematic Problem-Solving: A five-step strategy is essential for tackling word problems effectively:
    1. Read and Understand: Carefully analyze the problem to identify given information and what needs to be found.
    2. Define a Variable: Assign a letter (e.g., x, n, t) to represent the unknown quantity you need to find. If there are multiple unknowns, express them in terms of this single variable.
    3. Write an Equation: Translate the words and relationships in the problem into a mathematical equation that models the situation.
    4. Solve the Equation: Use algebraic techniques to find the value of the variable.
    5. Check and State Answer: Verify that your solution satisfies the original problem's conditions and makes logical sense. Always include appropriate units in your final answer.
  • Translating Words to Algebra: Understanding common phrases and their algebraic equivalents (e.g., "is" means equals, "times" means multiplication, "less than" implies subtraction).
  • Modeling Real-World Scenarios: Equations are powerful tools for representing relationships in various contexts, including:
    • Number Problems: Direct translation of numerical relationships.
    • Age Problems: Relating the ages of different individuals.
    • Geometry Problems: Using formulas for perimeter, area, etc., to find unknown dimensions. (This section specifically covers perimeter.)
    • Rate Problems: Involving distance, rate (speed), and time using the formula d = rt.
    • Conversion Problems: Using formulas to convert between different units or scales, such as temperature.
  • Importance of Checking Solutions: After solving, always re-read the original word problem and plug your answer back in. This step helps catch algebraic errors and ensures the solution is realistic (e.g., a negative age or distance is usually incorrect).

Important Terms

  • Variable: A symbol, typically a letter (like x, n, b, t, s), used to represent an unknown numerical quantity in an equation.
  • Equation: A mathematical statement that asserts the equality of two expressions. It always contains an equals sign (=).
  • Perimeter: The total distance around the boundary or outside edge of a two-dimensional shape. For a triangle, it's the sum of the lengths of its three sides.
  • Rate: A measure of how quickly one quantity changes in relation to another. Often expressed as "per," such as miles per hour (mph), which is a unit of speed.
  • Distance-Rate-Time Formula: The fundamental formula d = rt, where d represents distance, r represents rate (speed), and t represents time.
  • Fahrenheit (F): A temperature scale where water freezes at 32 degrees and boils at 212 degrees.
  • Celsius (C): A temperature scale where water freezes at 0 degrees and boils at 100 degrees.

Detailed Notes

The primary goal of this section is to equip you with the skills to solve real-world problems by translating them into algebraic equations. This process isn't just about finding an answer; it's about developing a structured approach to problem-solving.

The Problem-Solving Strategy

Every word problem, regardless of its specific context, can be approached systematically using the following five steps:

  1. Read the Problem Carefully: This is the most critical first step. Read the problem multiple times if necessary. Identify precisely what information is given (the "knowns") and what you are asked to find (the "unknowns"). Circle keywords or numbers.
  2. Define a Variable: Choose a letter to represent the unknown quantity you want to find. If there are multiple unknowns, try to express all of them in terms of this single variable. For example, if "Anna is 5 years older than twice Ben's age," you might let b be Ben's age, and then Anna's age would be 2b + 5.
  3. Write an Equation: This step involves translating the words of the problem into mathematical symbols and relationships. Look for phrases like "is," "equals," "sum," "difference," "product," "quotient," "more than," "less than," "times," etc., to help build your equation.
  4. Solve the Equation: Use the algebraic techniques you've learned (combining like terms, inverse operations, distribution, etc.) to isolate the variable and find its numerical value.
  5. Check the Solution and Include Units: Once you have a solution, don't just assume it's correct. Plug your answer back into the original word problem (not just your equation) to see if it makes sense. For instance, if you found a person's age to be -5 years, you'd know there was an error. Always include the appropriate units (e.g., cm, hours, degrees Celsius) in your final answer.

Types of Word Problems and Examples

Let's explore common types of word problems and how to apply the strategy:

1. Number Problems

These problems often involve direct translation of numerical relationships.

  • Example: "Four less than three times a number is 23. Find the number."
    • Step 1 (Read): We know a relationship between a number and 23. We need to find the number.
    • Step 2 (Variable): Let n be the number.
    • Step 3 (Equation): "Three times a number" is 3n. "Four less than" means subtract 4: 3n - 4. "Is 23" means = 23. So, 3n - 4 = 23.
    • Step 4 (Solve):
      • 3n - 4 = 23
      • Add 4 to both sides: 3n = 27
      • Divide by 3: n = 9
    • Step 5 (Check): Three times 9 is 27. Four less than 27 is 27 - 4 = 23. This matches the problem. The number is 9.

2. Age Problems

Age problems often involve comparing the ages of two or more people.

  • Example: "Anna is 5 years older than twice Ben's age. The sum of their ages is 38. Find each person's age."
    • Step 1 (Read): We know the relationship between Anna's and Ben's ages, and their total age. We need to find both ages.
    • Step 2 (Variable): Let b be Ben's age. Then Anna's age is 2b + 5.
    • Step 3 (Equation): The sum of their ages is 38: b + (2b + 5) = 38.
    • Step 4 (Solve):
      • b + 2b + 5 = 38 (Combine like terms)
      • 3b + 5 = 38
      • Subtract 5 from both sides: 3b = 33
      • Divide by 3: b = 11
      • Ben's age is 11. Anna's age is 2(11) + 5 = 22 + 5 = 27.
    • Step 5 (Check): Ben is 11, Anna is 27. Their sum is 11 + 27 = 38. This is correct. Ben is 11 years old; Anna is 27 years old.

3. Geometry Problems (Perimeter)

These problems use geometric formulas, often for perimeter or area.

  • Example: "The second side of a triangle is 3 cm longer than the first side. The third side is twice as long as the first side. The perimeter is 43 cm. Find the length of each side."
    • Step 1 (Read): We know side relationships and the total perimeter. We need individual side lengths.
    • Step 2 (Variable): Let s be the length of the first side.
      • Second side: s + 3
      • Third side: 2s
    • Step 3 (Equation): The perimeter is the sum of the sides: s + (s + 3) + 2s = 43.
    • Step 4 (Solve):
      • s + s + 3 + 2s = 43 (Combine like terms)
      • 4s + 3 = 43
      • Subtract 3 from both sides: 4s = 40
      • Divide by 4: s = 10
      • First side is 10 cm.
      • Second side is 10 + 3 = 13 cm.
      • Third side is 2(10) = 20 cm.
    • Step 5 (Check): The sides are 10 cm, 13 cm, and 20 cm. Their sum is 10 + 13 + 20 = 43 cm. This matches the given perimeter. The sides are 10 cm, 13 cm, and 20 cm.

4. Rate Problems (Distance-Rate-Time)

These problems involve motion and use the formula d = rt.

  • Distance-Rate-Time Formula: d = r * t (distance = rate × time)
  • Example: "Two hikers start from the same trailhead at the same time and walk in opposite directions. One hikes at 3.5 mph and the other at 4 mph. After how many hours are they 22.5 miles apart?"
    • Step 1 (Read): We know two rates, their direction, and the total distance apart. We need to find the time.
    • Step 2 (Variable): Let t be the time in hours.
      • Distance of hiker 1: d1 = 3.5t
      • Distance of hiker 2: d2 = 4t
    • Step 3 (Equation): Since they are walking in opposite directions, their individual distances add up to the total distance apart: 3.5t + 4t = 22.5.
    • Step 4 (Solve):
      • 3.5t + 4t = 22.5 (Combine like terms)
      • 7.5t = 22.5
      • Divide by 7.5: t = 3
    • Step 5 (Check): After 3 hours, hiker 1 travels 3.5 * 3 = 10.5 miles. Hiker 2 travels 4 * 3 = 12 miles. Together, they are 10.5 + 12 = 22.5 miles apart. This matches the problem. They are 22.5 miles apart after 3 hours.

5. Temperature Conversion

These problems use specific formulas to convert between units.

  • Formulas:
    • Fahrenheit to Celsius: C = (5/9)(F - 32)
    • Celsius to Fahrenheit: F = (9/5)C + 32
  • Example: "The temperature is 68°F. Convert to Celsius."
    • Step 1 (Read): We are given a temperature in Fahrenheit and need to convert it to Celsius.
    • Step 2 (Variable): We are solving for C. We are given F = 68.
    • Step 3 (Equation): Use the Fahrenheit to Celsius formula: C = (5/9)(F - 32).
    • Step 4 (Solve):
      • C = (5/9)(68 - 32)
      • C = (5/9)(36)
      • C = 5 * 4 (since 36/9 = 4)
      • C = 20
    • Step 5 (Check): A quick mental check: 68°F is a warm room temperature, and 20°C is also a comfortable room temperature. The calculation seems reasonable. The temperature is 20°C.

Important Note: Always remember that checking your answer in the context of the original word problem is just as crucial as performing the algebra correctly. An answer that doesn't make logical sense (like a negative age or distance, or a boiling point of water that's not 100°C) is a strong indicator that you need to re-examine your equation or your calculations.

Self-Assessment Checkpoint: "Five more than twice a number is 31. Find the number."

  • Answer: Let n be the number. The equation is 2n + 5 = 31. Subtract 5: 2n = 26. Divide by 2: n = 13. Check: 2(13) + 5 = 26 + 5 = 31. The number is 13.

Review Questions

  1. Explain, in your own words, why it is important to follow a structured problem-solving strategy when dealing with word problems. What specific step do you think is most commonly overlooked by students, and why is it vital?
  2. Imagine you are trying to solve a word problem where the length of a rectangle is described in terms of its width. If you let w represent the width, how would you express the length if it's "4 feet less than twice the width"?
  3. A car travels at a certain speed for 2 hours, and then increases its speed by 10 mph for the next 3 hours. If the total distance traveled is 270 miles, how would you set up an equation to find the initial speed of the car? (You do not need to solve it.)
  4. Why is the final step of checking your solution against the original problem (not just your derived equation) so important? Provide an example of a situation where your algebraic solution might be numerically correct but physically impossible or illogical in the context of the problem.
  5. Consider a problem involving two quantities, A and B. If A is "three times B minus 7," and their sum is 53, describe the process of defining variables and writing an equation. What algebraic steps would you take to solve for B?
