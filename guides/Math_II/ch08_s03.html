Chapter 8, Section 3: The Midpoint Formula

Overview

This section introduces the Midpoint Formula, a fundamental tool for finding the exact center of a line segment in the coordinate plane. It explains how to calculate the midpoint given two endpoints by averaging their respective coordinates. Additionally, it covers how to use the formula in reverse to find a missing endpoint when the midpoint and one endpoint are known.

Key Concepts

  • Midpoint Definition: The midpoint of a line segment is the point located precisely halfway between its two endpoints. It divides the segment into two equal parts.
  • The Midpoint Formula: For a segment with endpoints $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$, the midpoint $M$ is given by: $M = \left(\frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right)$
  • Averaging Coordinates: The formula works by independently averaging the x-coordinates and averaging the y-coordinates. This is a direct extension of finding the average of two numbers on a number line.
  • Finding a Missing Endpoint: If you know one endpoint and the midpoint, you can set up two separate equations (one for x, one for y) using the Midpoint Formula and solve for the coordinates of the unknown endpoint.
  • Applications: Finding midpoints is useful in various real-world scenarios, such as determining the center of a physical object, a geographical location, or a route.

Important Terms

  • Midpoint: The central point of a line segment, equidistant from both endpoints.
  • Endpoint: One of the two points that define the boundaries of a line segment.
  • Coordinate Plane: A two-dimensional plane defined by a horizontal x-axis and a vertical y-axis, used to locate points using ordered pairs $(x, y)$.
  • x-coordinate: The first value in an ordered pair $(x, y)$, representing the horizontal position of a point.
  • y-coordinate: The second value in an ordered pair $(x, y)$, representing the vertical position of a point.
  • Average: The sum of a set of numbers divided by the count of the numbers. In the Midpoint Formula, we average the x-coordinates and the y-coordinates separately.

Detailed Notes

Understanding the Midpoint

The midpoint is a crucial concept in geometry, representing the exact center of a line segment. Imagine a straight path on a map; the midpoint is the halfway point. This concept is applicable in many fields, from engineering (e.g., finding the center of a bridge span) to navigation (e.g., locating the halfway point of a hiking trail).

The Midpoint Formula

The core of this section is the Midpoint Formula. It provides a simple and direct way to calculate the coordinates of the midpoint ($M$) given the coordinates of its two endpoints. Let the two endpoints be $P_1 = (x_1, y_1)$ and $P_2 = (x_2, y_2)$. The formula for the midpoint $M$ is: $M = \left(\frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right)$

In simpler terms: To find the x-coordinate of the midpoint, you add the two x-coordinates together and divide by 2. To find the y-coordinate of the midpoint, you add the two y-coordinates together and divide by 2.

Examples of Finding a Midpoint

  1. Finding a Midpoint with Positive Coordinates:

    • Problem: Find the midpoint of the segment from $A(2, 8)$ to $B(10, 4)$.
    • Solution:
      • Identify the coordinates: $x_1 = 2, y_1 = 8$ and $x_2 = 10, y_2 = 4$.
      • Apply the formula: $M = \left(\frac{2 + 10}{2}, \frac{8 + 4}{2}\right)$ $M = \left(\frac{12}{2}, \frac{12}{2}\right)$ $M = (6, 6)$
      • The midpoint is $(6, 6)$.

  2. Midpoint with Negative Coordinates:

    • Problem: Find the midpoint of the segment from $(-5, 3)$ to $(7, -9)$.
    • Solution:
      • Identify the coordinates: $x_1 = -5, y_1 = 3$ and $x_2 = 7, y_2 = -9$.
      • Apply the formula, being careful with signs: $M = \left(\frac{-5 + 7}{2}, \frac{3 + (-9)}{2}\right)$ $M = \left(\frac{2}{2}, \frac{-6}{2}\right)$ $M = (1, -3)$
      • The midpoint is $(1, -3)$.

  3. Practice Problem (from checkpoint):

    • Problem: Find the midpoint of the segment from $(4, -2)$ to $(-6, 10)$.
    • Solution:
      • $M = \left(\frac{4 + (-6)}{2}, \frac{-2 + 10}{2}\right)$
      • $M = \left(\frac{-2}{2}, \frac{8}{2}\right)$
      • $M = (-1, 4)$

Finding an Endpoint Given the Midpoint

Sometimes, you're given one endpoint and the midpoint, and you need to find the other endpoint. This requires using the Midpoint Formula "in reverse." You set up equations based on the formula and solve for the unknown coordinates.

How to do it: Let the known endpoint be $(x_1, y_1)$, the midpoint be $M(x_M, y_M)$, and the unknown endpoint be $(x_2, y_2)$. You'll set up two separate equations:

  1. For the x-coordinate: $x_M = \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}$
  2. For the y-coordinate: $y_M = \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}$ Then, solve each equation for $x_2$ and $y_2$.

Example: Finding a Missing Endpoint

  • Problem: The midpoint of $AB$ is $M(3, 5)$. If $A = (1, 2)$, find $B$.
  • Solution:
    • Let $A = (x_1, y_1) = (1, 2)$.
    • Let $M = (x_M, y_M) = (3, 5)$.
    • Let the unknown endpoint $B = (x_2, y_2)$.
    • Set up the x-coordinate equation: $\frac{1 + x_2}{2} = 3$ $1 + x_2 = 3 \times 2$ $1 + x_2 = 6$ $x_2 = 6 - 1$ $x_2 = 5$
    • Set up the y-coordinate equation: $\frac{2 + y_2}{2} = 5$ $2 + y_2 = 5 \times 2$ $2 + y_2 = 10$ $y_2 = 10 - 2$ $y_2 = 8$
    • So, the missing endpoint $B$ is $(5, 8)$.

Application Example: Center of a Segment

  • Problem: A straight bridge runs from point $P(-2, 7)$ to point $Q(14, 3)$ on a city map. Where should the city place a sign at the exact center of the bridge?
  • Solution:
    • This is a direct midpoint problem, where the endpoints are $P(-2, 7)$ and $Q(14, 3)$.
    • $M = \left(\frac{-2 + 14}{2}, \frac{7 + 3}{2}\right)$
    • $M = \left(\frac{12}{2}, \frac{10}{2}\right)$
    • $M = (6, 5)$
    • The sign should be placed at the point $(6, 5)$ on the map.

Practice Problem (from checkpoint):

  • Problem: $M(4, -1)$ is the midpoint of $CD$. If $C = (7, 3)$, find $D$.
  • Solution:
    • Let $C = (x_1, y_1) = (7, 3)$.
    • Let $M = (x_M, y_M) = (4, -1)$.
    • Let the unknown endpoint $D = (x_2, y_2)$.
    • For the x-coordinate: $\frac{7 + x_2}{2} = 4$ $7 + x_2 = 8$ $x_2 = 1$
    • For the y-coordinate: $\frac{3 + y_2}{2} = -1$ $3 + y_2 = -2$ $y_2 = -5$
    • Therefore, $D = (1, -5)$.

Connection to Averaging on a Number Line

It's helpful to remember that the Midpoint Formula is essentially a two-dimensional application of finding the average of two numbers. For example, on a simple number line, the midpoint between 3 and 7 is $\frac{3+7}{2} = \frac{10}{2} = 5$. The coordinate plane formula just applies this concept separately to the x-coordinates and the y-coordinates.
