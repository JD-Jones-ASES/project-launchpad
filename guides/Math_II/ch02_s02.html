Study Guide: Comparing and Ordering Rational Numbers

Overview

This section provides essential strategies for determining which of two rational numbers is greater or for arranging a list of rational numbers in ascending or descending order. You will learn two primary methods: rewriting numbers with a common denominator and converting them to decimal form. Mastering these techniques is crucial for working with rational numbers in various mathematical and real-world contexts.

Key Concepts

  • Comparing Fractions with Common Denominators: If two fractions share the same positive denominator, the fraction with the larger numerator is the greater fraction.
  • Least Common Denominator (LCD): To compare fractions with different denominators, you must first rewrite them as equivalent fractions using their least common denominator (LCD). Once they have the same denominator, compare their numerators.
  • Converting to Decimals for Comparison: Converting rational numbers (especially fractions) to their decimal forms can often be a quicker and more straightforward method for comparison, particularly when dealing with mixed sets of fractions and decimals.
  • Ordering Mixed Sets: When presented with a mix of fractions, decimals, and integers, the most reliable approach is to convert all numbers to a single, consistent form—typically decimals—before ordering them from least to greatest or vice versa.
  • Comparing Negative Numbers: A critical rule for negative numbers is that the number with the larger absolute value is actually the smaller number. For instance, -5 is less than -2 because |-5| (which is 5) is greater than |-2| (which is 2).
  • General Strategies: The main strategies involve using a common denominator, converting to decimals, or visualizing their positions on a number line (numbers to the right are greater).

Important Terms

  • Rational Number: Any number that can be expressed as a fraction $\frac{p}{q}$, where $p$ and $q$ are integers and $q \neq 0$. This includes integers, fractions, and terminating or repeating decimals.
  • Least Common Denominator (LCD): The smallest positive common multiple of the denominators of two or more fractions. It is used to rewrite fractions with a common base for comparison or addition/subtraction.
  • Numerator: The top number in a fraction, indicating how many parts of the whole are being considered.
  • Denominator: The bottom number in a fraction, indicating the total number of equal parts into which the whole is divided.
  • Decimal: A way of representing non-integer numbers, typically based on powers of 10.
  • Absolute Value: The distance of a number from zero on the number line, always a non-negative value. It is denoted by vertical bars around the number (e.g., $|-5|=5$).

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Comparing and Ordering

When faced with a set of rational numbers, we often need to determine their relative sizes—which is larger, which is smaller, or how to arrange them in a specific order. This section outlines two robust methods to accomplish this: using common denominators and converting to decimals.

Method 1: Comparing with Common Denominators

This method is particularly effective when comparing two or more fractions.

  • Property: Fractions with a Common Denominator If two fractions already have the same positive denominator, the comparison is straightforward: the fraction with the larger numerator is the greater fraction.

    • Example: If $d > 0$, then $\frac{a}{d} < \frac{b}{d}$ if and only if $a < b$.
    • So, $\frac{3}{7} < \frac{5}{7}$ because $3 < 5$.

  • Steps for Fractions with Different Denominators:

    1. Find the LCD: Determine the least common denominator (LCD) of all the fractions you need to compare. The LCD is the smallest positive number that is a multiple of all the denominators.
    2. Rewrite Fractions: Convert each fraction into an equivalent fraction with the LCD as its new denominator. To do this, multiply both the numerator and the denominator by the same factor that makes the denominator equal to the LCD.
    3. Compare Numerators: Once all fractions have the same denominator, simply compare their numerators. The fraction with the largest numerator is the greatest.

  • Example: Comparing $\frac{5}{8}$ and $\frac{7}{12}$

    1. The denominators are 8 and 12. The least common multiple of 8 and 12 is 24. So, the LCD is 24.
    2. Rewrite the fractions:
      • For $\frac{5}{8}$: To get a denominator of 24, we multiply 8 by 3. So, multiply the numerator by 3 as well: $\frac{5 \times 3}{8 \times 3} = \frac{15}{24}$.
      • For $\frac{7}{12}$: To get a denominator of 24, we multiply 12 by 2. So, multiply the numerator by 2 as well: $\frac{7 \times 2}{12 \times 2} = \frac{14}{24}$.
    3. Compare numerators: We now compare $\frac{15}{24}$ and $\frac{14}{24}$. Since $15 > 14$, we conclude that $\frac{15}{24} > \frac{14}{24}$.
    4. Therefore, $\frac{5}{8} > \frac{7}{12}$.

Method 2: Comparing with Decimals

This method is often preferred for its speed, especially when some numbers are already in decimal form or when dealing with mixed types of rational numbers.

  • Steps for Comparing using Decimals:

    1. Convert to Decimals: Convert all rational numbers (fractions, mixed numbers) to their decimal equivalents. If a fraction results in a repeating decimal, it's often helpful to round to a consistent number of decimal places (e.g., three decimal places) for comparison, especially when an exact representation isn't required.
    2. Align Decimal Points: Line up the numbers by their decimal points.
    3. Compare Digit by Digit: Compare the numbers from left to right, starting with the largest place value. If the digits are the same, move to the next digit to the right.

Ordering Mixed Sets of Rational Numbers

In real-world scenarios, you'll often encounter data presented in a mix of fractions, decimals, and sometimes integers. The most effective strategy is to convert all numbers to a single, consistent format, usually decimals, before attempting to order them.

  • Caution: Comparing Negative Numbers When comparing negative numbers, it's crucial to remember that the number with the larger absolute value is actually the smaller number. Think of a number line: numbers further to the left are smaller.

    • Example: $-5 < -2$ because $-5$ is further to the left on the number line than $-2$, even though $|-5|=5$ and $|-2|=2$, and $5 > 2$.

  • Example: Order from least to greatest: $-\frac{2}{3}$, $-0.5$, $\frac{1}{4}$, $-\frac{3}{4}$, $0.6$.

    1. Convert all to decimals (approximating where necessary, typically to three decimal places for repeating decimals):
      • $-\frac{2}{3} \approx -0.667$
      • $-0.5 = -0.500$
      • $\frac{1}{4} = 0.250$
      • $-\frac{3}{4} = -0.750$
      • $0.6 = 0.600$
    2. Now, compare the decimals:
      • Start with the negatives: $-0.750$, $-0.667$, $-0.500$. Remember, the larger the absolute value, the smaller the negative number. So, $-0.750$ is the smallest, then $-0.667$, then $-0.500$.
      • Then the positives: $0.250$, $0.600$.
    3. Combining and ordering: $-0.750 < -0.667 < -0.500 < 0.250 < 0.600$.
    4. Original order: $-\frac{3}{4}$, $-\frac{2}{3}$, $-0.5$, $\frac{1}{4}$, $0.6$.

  • Word Problem Example: Comparing Measurements Ali: $12\frac{3}{8}$ m, Bea: $12.4$ m, Carlos: $12\frac{1}{3}$ m. Order shortest to longest.

    1. Convert to decimals:
      • $12\frac{3}{8} = 12 + \frac{3}{8} = 12 + 0.375 = 12.375$ m
      • $12.4 = 12.400$ m
      • $12\frac{1}{3} = 12 + \frac{1}{3} \approx 12 + 0.333 = 12.333$ m
    2. Compare the decimals: $12.333 < 12.375 < 12.400$.
    3. Order shortest to longest: $12\frac{1}{3}$ m (Carlos), $12\frac{3}{8}$ m (Ali), $12.4$ m (Bea).

Summary of Strategies for Comparing Rational Numbers

  • Common Denominator: Rewrite fractions with the Least Common Denominator (LCD) and then compare their numerators. This is ideal for comparing fractions directly.
  • Decimals: Convert all numbers to their decimal form and compare them digit by digit from left to right. This is highly versatile for mixed sets of numbers.
  • Number Line: Visualize the numbers on a number line. Numbers positioned further to the right are greater, and numbers further to the left are smaller. This provides a good conceptual understanding.
