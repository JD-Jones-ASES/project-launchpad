Study Guide: Composite Figures

Overview

Composite figures are real-world objects or shapes formed by combining two or more basic geometric shapes. To calculate properties like area, perimeter, volume, or surface area for these complex figures, the key strategy is to break them down into their simpler components, calculate the desired measurement for each part, and then combine those results through addition or subtraction.

Key Concepts

  • What are Composite Figures? These are figures that are not simple geometric shapes (like a single rectangle or cylinder) but are instead composed of multiple basic shapes joined together or with parts removed.
  • General Strategy for Composite Figures:
    1. Identify Basic Shapes: Break down the complex figure into its fundamental geometric components (e.g., rectangles, circles, triangles, prisms, cylinders, spheres, cones).
    2. Decide on Addition or Subtraction: Determine whether the figure is formed by adding the measures of its parts (if it's built from pieces) or by subtracting the measure of a removed part from a larger whole.
    3. Calculate Each Part Separately: Use the appropriate formulas to find the area, perimeter, volume, or surface area for each identified basic shape.
    4. Combine Results: Add or subtract the individual measurements as determined in step 2 to find the total measurement for the composite figure.
  • 2-D Composite Figures: These involve flat shapes, where you typically calculate total area or perimeter.
  • 3-D Composite Figures: These involve solid objects, where you typically calculate total volume or surface area.
  • Surface Area Caution: When calculating the surface area of a 3-D composite figure, be careful not to include the interior faces where two pieces are joined. These faces are hidden and do not contribute to the external surface area.

Important Terms

  • Composite Figure: A geometric figure (either 2-D or 3-D) that is made up of two or more basic geometric shapes. Examples include a house (rectangular prism + triangular prism), a silo (cylinder + hemisphere), or a window (rectangle + semicircle).
  • Perimeter: The total distance around the outside edge of a 2-D shape.
  • Area: The amount of space a 2-D shape covers, measured in square units.
  • Volume: The amount of space a 3-D object occupies, measured in cubic units.
  • Surface Area: The total area of all the surfaces of a 3-D object.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Composite Figures

Many objects in the real world are not simple, perfect geometric shapes. Think of a swimming pool with a rectangular main section and a rounded end, or a grain silo that's cylindrical with a dome on top. These are all examples of composite figures. To work with them mathematically – for instance, to find out how much paint is needed for a wall (area), how much water a pool holds (volume), or how much fencing is needed for a garden (perimeter) – we need a systematic approach.

The Strategy for Composite Figures

The core idea is to break down the complex into the simple:

  1. Identify the basic shapes: Look at the composite figure and determine which fundamental geometric shapes (e.g., rectangles, squares, circles, triangles, semicircles, rectangular prisms, cylinders, cones, spheres, hemispheres) make up the whole.
  2. Decide whether to add or subtract:
    • If the composite figure is built by joining shapes together (like a house made of a prism and a pyramid), you will generally add their individual measurements.
    • If the composite figure is formed by removing a part from a larger shape (like a hole cut out of a piece of metal), you will generally subtract the measurement of the removed part from the measurement of the original whole.
  3. Calculate each part separately: Use the appropriate formulas for each basic shape you identified. Make sure to use the correct dimensions (radius, height, length, width) for each component.
  4. Combine the results: Perform the final addition or subtraction to get the total measurement for the composite figure.

2-D Composite Figures (Area and Perimeter)

Example 1: Area of a Window (Rectangle Plus Semicircle)

A window is shaped like a rectangle topped with a semicircle. The rectangle is 4 ft wide and 6 ft tall. Find the total area. (Use $\pi \approx 3.14$)

Solution:

  1. Identify shapes: A rectangle and a semicircle.
  2. Add or Subtract: We add their areas because the window is made of both parts.
  3. Calculate each part:
    • Rectangle:
      • Dimensions: Length = 6 ft, Width = 4 ft
      • Area ($A_{rect}$) = Length $\times$ Width = $6 \text{ ft} \times 4 \text{ ft} = 24 \text{ ft}^2$
    • Semicircle:
      • The semicircle's diameter is the width of the rectangle, so diameter = 4 ft.
      • Radius ($r$) = diameter / 2 = 4 ft / 2 = 2 ft.
      • Area of a full circle = $\pi r^2$. Area of a semicircle ($A_{semi}$) = $\frac{1}{2}\pi r^2$.
      • $A_{semi} = \frac{1}{2} (3.14) (2 \text{ ft})^2 = \frac{1}{2} (3.14) (4 \text{ ft}^2) = 6.28 \text{ ft}^2$
  4. Combine results:
    • Total Area ($A_{total}$) = $A_{rect} + A_{semi} = 24 \text{ ft}^2 + 6.28 \text{ ft}^2 = 30.28 \text{ ft}^2$

Example 2: Area of Remaining Metal (Subtracting a Region)

A square piece of sheet metal has sides of 10 in. A circle with diameter 6 in. is cut from the center. Find the area of the remaining metal. (Use $\pi \approx 3.14$)

Solution:

  1. Identify shapes: A square and a circle.
  2. Add or Subtract: We subtract the circle's area from the square's area because the circle is removed.
  3. Calculate each part:
    • Square:
      • Side length = 10 in.
      • Area ($A_{square}$) = Side $\times$ Side = $(10 \text{ in.})^2 = 100 \text{ in.}^2$
    • Circle:
      • Diameter = 6 in.
      • Radius ($r$) = diameter / 2 = 6 in. / 2 = 3 in.
      • Area ($A_{circle}$) = $\pi r^2 = (3.14) (3 \text{ in.})^2 = (3.14) (9 \text{ in.}^2) = 28.26 \text{ in.}^2$
  4. Combine results:
    • Remaining Area ($A_{remaining}$) = $A_{square} - A_{circle} = 100 \text{ in.}^2 - 28.26 \text{ in.}^2 = 71.74 \text{ in.}^2$

Example 3: Perimeter and Area of a Garden (Application)

A garden has the shape of a rectangle (20 ft by 10 ft) with a semicircular extension on one short end. Find (a) the perimeter (for fencing) and (b) the area. (Use $\pi \approx 3.14$)

Solution:

  1. Identify shapes: A rectangle ($20 \text{ ft} \times 10 \text{ ft}$) and a semicircle (diameter 10 ft, so $r = 5 \text{ ft}$).

  2. Part (a) Perimeter:

    • The perimeter consists of the two long sides of the rectangle, one short side of the rectangle, and the arc of the semicircle. The internal 10 ft side of the rectangle (where the semicircle attaches) is not part of the perimeter.
    • Perimeter ($P$) = $20 \text{ ft} + 20 \text{ ft} + 10 \text{ ft} + (\text{circumference of semicircle})$
    • Circumference of a full circle = $2\pi r$. Circumference of a semicircle arc = $\frac{1}{2}(2\pi r) = \pi r$.
    • $P = 20 + 20 + 10 + (3.14)(5 \text{ ft}) = 50 \text{ ft} + 15.70 \text{ ft} = 65.70 \text{ ft}$

  3. Part (b) Area:

    • The total area is the sum of the area of the rectangle and the area of the semicircle.
    • Area of rectangle ($A_{rect}$) = $20 \text{ ft} \times 10 \text{ ft} = 200 \text{ ft}^2$
    • Area of semicircle ($A_{semi}$) = $\frac{1}{2}\pi r^2 = \frac{1}{2}(3.14)(5 \text{ ft})^2 = \frac{1}{2}(3.14)(25 \text{ ft}^2) = 39.25 \text{ ft}^2$
    • Total Area ($A_{total}$) = $A_{rect} + A_{semi} = 200 \text{ ft}^2 + 39.25 \text{ ft}^2 = 239.25 \text{ ft}^2$

3-D Composite Figures (Volume and Surface Area)

Example 4: Volume of a Silo (Cylinder Plus Hemisphere)

A grain silo consists of a cylinder with radius 5 m and height 12 m, topped with a hemisphere (half of a sphere) of the same radius. Find the total volume. (Give the answer in terms of $\pi$ and as a decimal using $\pi \approx 3.14$)

Solution:

  1. Identify shapes: A cylinder and a hemisphere.
  2. Add or Subtract: We add their volumes as the silo is made of these two parts.
  3. Calculate each part:
    • Cylinder:
      • Radius ($r$) = 5 m, Height ($h$) = 12 m
      • Volume ($V_{cyl}$) = $\pi r^2 h = \pi (5 \text{ m})^2 (12 \text{ m}) = \pi (25 \text{ m}^2) (12 \text{ m}) = 300\pi \text{ m}^3$
    • Hemisphere:
      • Radius ($r$) = 5 m
      • Volume of a full sphere = $\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$. Volume of a hemisphere ($V_{hemi}$) = $\frac{1}{2} (\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3) = \frac{2}{3}\pi r^3$.
      • $V_{hemi} = \frac{2}{3}\pi (5 \text{ m})^3 = \frac{2}{3}\pi (125 \text{ m}^3) = \frac{250}{3}\pi \text{ m}^3$
  4. Combine results:
    • Total Volume ($V_{total}$) = $V_{cyl} + V_{hemi} = 300\pi \text{ m}^3 + \frac{250}{3}\pi \text{ m}^3$
    • To add, find a common denominator: $300\pi = \frac{900}{3}\pi$.
    • $V_{total} = \frac{900}{3}\pi \text{ m}^3 + \frac{250}{3}\pi \text{ m}^3 = \frac{1150}{3}\pi \text{ m}^3$
    • As a decimal: $V_{total} \approx \frac{1150}{3}(3.14) \text{ m}^3 \approx 1203.33 \text{ m}^3$

Example 5: Volume of a Cone on a Cylinder

A solid is made by placing a cone (radius 4 cm, height 6 cm) on top of a cylinder (radius 4 cm, height 10 cm). Find the total volume. (Use $\pi \approx 3.14$)

Solution:

  1. Identify shapes: A cylinder and a cone.
  2. Add or Subtract: We add their volumes.
  3. Calculate each part:
    • Cylinder:
      • Radius ($r$) = 4 cm, Height ($h$) = 10 cm
      • Volume ($V_{cyl}$) = $\pi r^2 h = (3.14) (4 \text{ cm})^2 (10 \text{ cm}) = (3.14) (16 \text{ cm}^2) (10 \text{ cm}) = 502.4 \text{ cm}^3$
    • Cone:
      • Radius ($r$) = 4 cm, Height ($h$) = 6 cm
      • Volume ($V_{cone}$) = $\frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h = \frac{1}{3}(3.14) (4 \text{ cm})^2 (6 \text{ cm}) = \frac{1}{3}(3.14) (16 \text{ cm}^2) (6 \text{ cm}) = (3.14) (16 \text{ cm}^2) (2 \text{ cm}) = 100.48 \text{ cm}^3$
  4. Combine results:
    • Total Volume ($V_{total}$) = $V_{cyl} + V_{cone} = 502.4 \text{ cm}^3 + 100.48 \text{ cm}^3 = 602.88 \text{ cm}^3$

Important Note on Surface Area of Composite Solids

When calculating the surface area of a composite solid, it is crucial to remember that any faces or surfaces where two pieces are joined together are internal to the new composite figure. These internal faces are hidden from view and therefore should not be included in the total surface area calculation. You only count the external surfaces.
