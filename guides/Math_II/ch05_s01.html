Study Guide: Writing and Graphing Inequalities

Overview

This section introduces inequalities as mathematical statements that compare two expressions using symbols like less than, greater than, less than or equal to, or greater than or equal to. It covers how to translate real-world phrases into mathematical inequalities, graph these inequalities on a number line, and interpret a number line graph back into an inequality. Understanding inequalities is crucial for describing situations where quantities are not strictly equal.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of an Inequality: An inequality is a mathematical statement that shows a relationship between two expressions where one is not necessarily equal to the other. Instead, it might be greater than, less than, greater than or equal to, or less than or equal to.
  • Inequality Symbols:
    • < (is less than)
    • > (is greater than)
    • ≤ (is less than or equal to)
    • ≥ (is greater than or equal to)
  • Translating Verbal Phrases: Many common phrases in everyday language correspond directly to one of the inequality symbols, enabling us to model real-world scenarios mathematically.
  • Graphing Inequalities on a Number Line: Solutions to inequalities in one variable can be visualized on a number line.
    • Open Circle (): Used for strict inequalities (< or >) to indicate that the boundary value is not included in the solution set.
    • Closed Circle (): Used for inclusive inequalities (≤ or ≥) to indicate that the boundary value is included in the solution set.
    • Shading: The number line is shaded to the left for "less than" solutions and to the right for "greater than" solutions, representing all possible values that satisfy the inequality.
  • Writing Inequalities from Graphs: By observing the type of circle (open or closed) and the direction of shading on a number line, one can determine the corresponding inequality.

Important Terms

  • Inequality: A mathematical statement that compares two expressions using one of the symbols <, >, ≤, or ≥. Unlike equations, inequalities represent situations where quantities are not necessarily equal.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Inequalities

While equations state that two expressions are equal, many real-world situations involve quantities that are not equal. For example, a speed limit sets a maximum speed, not an exact speed. A height requirement for a ride sets a minimum height. These situations are described using inequalities.

An inequality is a mathematical statement that compares two expressions using one of the following symbols:

  • a < b: "$a$ is less than $b$"
  • a > b: "$a$ is greater than $b$"
  • a ≤ b: "$a$ is less than or equal to $b$"
  • a ≥ b: "$a$ is greater than or equal to $b$"

Translating Words into Inequalities

To solve real-world problems using inequalities, it's essential to translate common phrases into their corresponding mathematical symbols. Here are some key phrases and their meanings:

Phrase Inequality Symbol Example (Let $x$ be the number)
is less than < $x < 5$ (A number is less than 5)
is fewer than <
is greater than > $x > 10$ (A number is greater than 10)
is more than >
is less than or equal to ≤ $x \le 7$ (A number is less than or equal to 7)
is at most ≤
is no more than ≤
is greater than or equal to ≥ $x \ge 2$ (A number is greater than or equal to 2)
is at least ≥
is no less than ≥

Example: Translating Words to Inequalities

Let $x$ represent the unknown number.

  1. A number is greater than $-3$.
    • Translation: $x > -3$
  2. The temperature is at most $100$ degrees.
    • "At most" means less than or equal to.
    • Translation: $x \le 100$
  3. You must be at least $16$ years old to drive.
    • "At least" means greater than or equal to.
    • Translation: $x \ge 16$
  4. No more than $30$ students may enroll.
    • "No more than" means less than or equal to.
    • Translation: $x \le 30$

Graphing Inequalities on a Number Line

An inequality in one variable can be visually represented on a number line. The graph shows all the possible values that satisfy the inequality. The two key components of graphing are determining the type of circle at the boundary point and the direction of shading.

Open and Closed Circles

  • Open Circle (): Use an open circle for inequalities using < (less than) or > (greater than). This signifies that the boundary value itself is not part of the solution set.
  • Closed Circle (): Use a closed circle for inequalities using ≤ (less than or equal to) or ≥ (greater than or equal to). This signifies that the boundary value is part of the solution set.

Shading Direction

  • Shade Left: For "less than" (< or ≤) inequalities, shade the number line to the left of the boundary point.
  • Shade Right: For "greater than" (> or ≥) inequalities, shade the number line to the right of the boundary point.

Example: Graphing an Inequality ($x \ge -2$)

Graph $x \ge -2$ on a number line.

  • Symbol: The symbol is ≥, which means "greater than or equal to."
  • Circle: Because it includes "equal to," we use a closed circle at $-2$.
  • Shading: Because it's "greater than," we shade to the right.

Example: Graphing a Strict Inequality ($x < 1.5$)

Graph $x < 1.5$ on a number line.

  • Symbol: The symbol is <, which means "less than."
  • Circle: Because it's strictly "less than" (not equal to), we use an open circle at $1.5$.
  • Shading: Because it's "less than," we shade to the left.

Checkpoint: Graph $x > 0$ on a number line. What kind of circle do you use at $0$?

  • Answer: An open circle at $0$, shaded to the right. (Because > means strictly greater than, and the solutions are numbers larger than 0).

Writing an Inequality from a Graph or Application

You can also reverse the process: observe a number line graph or read a real-world scenario and write the corresponding inequality.

Example: Writing an Inequality from a Graph (Application)

A theme park ride requires riders to be at least $48$ inches tall. Write an inequality for the height $h$ (in inches) of an eligible rider, and describe its graph.

  • Translation: "At least $48$ inches" means the height must be greater than or equal to $48$.
  • Inequality: $h \ge 48$
  • Graph Description: The graph would have a closed circle at $48$ and be shaded to the right.

Checkpoint: The speed limit on a road is $55$ mph. Write an inequality for legal speeds $s$.

  • Answer: $s \le 55$. (Also, implicitly, $s \ge 0$ since speed cannot be negative). The phrase "speed limit" implies the maximum allowed speed.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental difference between an equation and an inequality. Provide a real-world example for each.
  2. List the four main inequality symbols and explain what each one means in words.
  3. Describe the rules for choosing between an open circle () and a closed circle () when graphing an inequality on a number line. Why is this distinction important?
  4. Translate the following verbal statements into inequalities. Let $y$ represent the unknown number:
    • a) Your test score must be no less than $70$ to pass.
    • b) The car's fuel tank holds a maximum of $15$ gallons.
    • c) To qualify for the discount, you must spend more than $25$.
    • d) A child's age for a certain toy is $10$ years or younger.
  5. Graph the inequality $x < -1$ on a number line. Then, explain in words how you would write an inequality from a number line graph that shows a closed circle at $3$ and is shaded to the left.
