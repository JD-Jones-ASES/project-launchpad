Study Guide: Simple and Compound Interest

Overview

This section introduces the concepts of simple and compound interest, which are fundamental ways that money grows or costs over time. We will learn how interest is calculated on an initial amount (principal) and compare the different financial outcomes of simple interest, which is calculated only on the principal, versus compound interest, which also calculates interest on accumulated interest.

Key Concepts

  • Interest: The money paid for the use of borrowed money, or the income earned from deposited money.
  • Principal (P): The initial amount of money deposited or borrowed.
  • Annual Interest Rate (r): The percentage charged or earned per year, expressed as a decimal in formulas.
  • Time (t): The duration, in years, for which the money is deposited or borrowed.
  • Simple Interest: Interest calculated only on the original principal amount. The principal remains constant throughout the investment/loan period for calculation purposes.
    • Formula: $I = Prt$ (where $I$ is the interest earned)
  • Compound Interest (Annual): Interest calculated on the original principal and on all accumulated interest from previous periods. This leads to "interest on interest" and faster growth.
    • Formula: $A = P(1 + r)^t$ (where $A$ is the final total amount)
  • Exponential Growth: Compound interest is an example of exponential growth, where the growth rate is applied to an ever-increasing base.
  • Units Consistency: It is critical that the annual interest rate ($r$) is expressed as a decimal and the time ($t$) is in years for both formulas.

Important Terms

  • Interest: The fee charged by a lender to a borrower for the use of money, or the fee paid by a bank to a depositor for keeping money in an account.
  • Principal (P): The original amount of money invested or borrowed.
  • Annual Interest Rate (r): The rate at which interest is charged or earned, expressed per year. In formulas, this rate must be converted from a percentage to a decimal (e.g., 4% becomes 0.04).
  • Time (t): The length of the investment or loan period, always expressed in years for these formulas.
  • Simple Interest (I): A method of calculating interest where the interest earned or paid is based solely on the initial principal amount.
  • Compound Interest (A): A method of calculating interest where the interest earned or paid is based on the initial principal and all accumulated interest from previous periods.
  • Compounded Annually: Means that the interest is calculated and added to the principal once per year.
  • Final Amount (A): The total sum of money at the end of the investment or loan period, including both the principal and the accumulated interest.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Interest

Interest is essentially the cost of borrowing money or the reward for lending money. When you deposit money into a savings account, the bank uses your money and pays you interest as a thank you. When you take out a loan, you pay interest to the lender for the privilege of using their money. This section explores two main ways interest is calculated: simple and compound.

Simple Interest

Simple interest is the most straightforward way to calculate interest. It is always calculated based only on the initial amount of money, known as the principal (P). This means the principal amount used for interest calculations never changes.

The formula for simple interest is: $I = Prt$ Where:

  • $I$ = the total interest earned or paid
  • $P$ = the principal (the original amount of money)
  • $r$ = the annual interest rate (expressed as a decimal)
  • $t$ = the time (in years)

Example: If you deposit $1,200 ($P$) in an account that earns 4% ($r = 0.04$) simple interest per year for 3 years ($t=3$), the interest earned is: $I = 1200 \times 0.04 \times 3 = 144$ You would earn $144 in interest. The total amount in the account after 3 years would be $1200 (principal) + $144 (interest) = $1,344.

Compound Interest (Annual)

Compound interest is a more powerful way to calculate interest, as it takes into account not only the original principal but also any interest that has accumulated over time. This means you earn "interest on your interest." When interest is compounded annually, it means the interest is calculated and added to the principal once every year.

The formula for the total amount ($A$) when interest is compounded annually is: $A = P(1 + r)^t$ Where:

  • $A$ = the final amount (principal + accumulated interest)
  • $P$ = the principal (the original amount of money)
  • $r$ = the annual interest rate (expressed as a decimal)
  • $t$ = the number of years

Example: You deposit $1,200 ($P$) in an account that earns 4% ($r = 0.04$) interest compounded annually for 3 years ($t=3$). $A = 1200(1 + 0.04)^3$ $A = 1200(1.04)^3$ First, calculate $(1.04)^3$: $1.04 \times 1.04 \times 1.04 = 1.124864$ Then, calculate $A$: $A = 1200 \times 1.124864 = 1349.8368$ Rounded to the nearest cent, the final amount in the account is $1,349.84. The interest earned is the final amount minus the principal: $1349.84 - 1200 = $149.84.

Notice that with compound interest, you earned $149.84, which is more than the $144 earned with simple interest over the same period and rate. This difference becomes more significant over longer periods.

Comparing Simple and Compound Interest

The key difference between simple and compound interest is how the principal amount is treated. With simple interest, the principal always stays the same for interest calculations. With compound interest, the principal effectively grows each year as the earned interest is added to it, becoming part of the new principal for the next period.

Side-by-Side Comparison Example: Let's compare investing $2,000 at 5% for 4 years using both methods:

(a) Simple Interest: $I = Prt = 2000 \times 0.05 \times 4 = 400$ Total amount = Principal + Interest = $2000 + $400 = $2,400

(b) Compound Interest (Annually): $A = P(1 + r)^t = 2000(1 + 0.05)^4 = 2000(1.05)^4$ First, $(1.05)^4 = 1.21550625$ Then, $A = 2000 \times 1.21550625 = 2431.0125$ Rounded to the nearest cent, $A = $2,431.01. Interest earned = Final Amount - Principal = $2431.01 - $2000 = $431.01.

In this comparison, compound interest earned $431.01 - $400 = $31.01 more than simple interest over 4 years. This demonstrates the power of compound interest, especially over longer time frames. The compound interest formula is a fundamental example of exponential growth, a concept you will explore further in mathematics.

Important Considerations for Calculations

  • Rate as a Decimal: Always convert the annual interest rate from a percentage to a decimal before using it in the formulas. For example, 6% becomes 0.06.
  • Time in Years: Ensure that the time period ($t$) is expressed in years. If a time period is given in months (e.g., 6 months), convert it to years (e.g., 6 months / 12 months/year = 0.5 years).
  • Loan Applications: These formulas apply to both money you earn (like in a savings account) and money you owe (like a loan). For loans, the total amount calculated ($A$ or $P+I$) represents the total repayment.

Example: Loan Application Maria borrows $800 at 6% simple interest for 2.5 years. $I = Prt = 800 \times 0.06 \times 2.5 = 120$ The total amount she repays is the principal plus the interest: $800 + $120 = $920.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental difference between simple interest and compound interest. In what scenarios might one be preferred over the other (e.g., for investors vs. borrowers)?
  2. You want to invest $5,000 for 5 years. Bank A offers 6% simple interest annually, while Bank B offers 5.8% interest compounded annually. Which bank would you choose and why? Show your calculations.
  3. Describe the role of each variable (P, r, t) in both the simple interest formula ($I=Prt$) and the compound interest formula ($A=P(1+r)^t$). How does the final amount 'A' in the compound interest formula differ from the interest 'I' in the simple interest formula?
  4. Why is it crucial for the annual interest rate (r) to be converted to a decimal and for time (t) to be expressed in years when using these formulas? Provide an example of a common mistake related to these units and its consequence.
  5. If you were taking out a loan for a significant purchase, would you prefer the interest to be calculated with simple interest or compound interest? Explain your reasoning and how it impacts the total amount you would repay.
