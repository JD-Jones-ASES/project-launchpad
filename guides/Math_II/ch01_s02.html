Product, Power, and Quotient Rules: A Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces fundamental rules for working with exponents, providing shortcuts for simplifying expressions involving multiplication, division, and raising powers to other powers or products to powers. By understanding these rules—the Product Rule, Power Rule, Power of a Product Rule, and Quotient Rule—you can efficiently simplify complex exponential expressions without needing to expand them fully.

Key Concepts

  • Product Rule for Exponents: When multiplying powers with the same base, keep the base and add the exponents. ($a^m a^n = a^{m+n}$)
  • Power Rule for Exponents: When raising a power to another power, keep the base and multiply the exponents. ($(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$)
  • Power of a Product: When raising a product of factors to a power, distribute the exponent to each individual factor within the product. ($(ab)^n = a^n b^n$)
  • Quotient Rule for Exponents: When dividing powers with the same base, keep the base and subtract the exponent of the denominator from the exponent of the numerator. ($a^m/a^n = a^{m-n}$, where $m > n$)

Important Terms

  • Exponent: A number that indicates how many times the base is to be used as a factor. For example, in $a^n$, $n$ is the exponent.
  • Base: The number or variable that is being multiplied by itself, as indicated by the exponent. For example, in $a^n$, $a$ is the base.
  • Power: An expression written with a base and an exponent, such as $2^3$ or $x^5$.
  • Product Rule: The rule $a^m a^n = a^{m+n}$, used for multiplying powers with the same base.
  • Power Rule: The rule $(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$, used for raising a power to another power.
  • Power of a Product Rule: The rule $(ab)^n = a^n b^n$, used for raising a product to a power.
  • Quotient Rule: The rule $a^m/a^n = a^{m-n}$, used for dividing powers with the same base.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Exponent Rules

Working with exponents can often involve long expansions of products. Exponent rules provide shortcuts that simplify expressions much faster. This section covers three fundamental rules and two related properties. Throughout these rules, it's assumed that bases are nonzero and exponents are positive integers.

1. Multiplying Powers with the Same Base (Product Rule)

  • Concept: When you multiply two powers that have the exact same base, you can combine them by adding their exponents.
  • Explanation: Consider $2^3 \cdot 2^4$. Expanding this, we get $(2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2) \cdot (2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2)$. This is a total of $3 + 4 = 7$ factors of 2. So, $2^3 \cdot 2^4 = 2^7$.
  • Rule: For any nonzero base $a$ and positive integers $m$ and $n$: $a^m a^n = a^{m+n}$ Key takeaway: Keep the base, add the exponents.
  • Important Caution: This rule only applies when the bases are the same. You cannot use the product rule to simplify $2^3 \cdot 3^4$ because the bases (2 and 3) are different.
  • Examples:
    • $x^5 x^3 = x^{5+3} = x^8$
    • $4^2 4^6 = 4^{2+6} = 4^8$
    • $a^2 a a^4 = a^2 a^1 a^4 = a^{2+1+4} = a^7$ (Remember $a$ by itself implies $a^1$)

2. Raising a Power to a Power (Power Rule)

  • Concept: When an exponential expression (a power) is raised to another exponent, you can combine them by multiplying the exponents.
  • Explanation: Consider $(2^3)^4$. This means $2^3$ is used as a factor four times: $(2^3)^4 = 2^3 \cdot 2^3 \cdot 2^3 \cdot 2^3$. Using the Product Rule, we add the exponents: $3+3+3+3 = 12$. Alternatively, $3$ repeated $4$ times is $3 \times 4 = 12$. So, $(2^3)^4 = 2^{12}$.
  • Rule: For any nonzero base $a$ and positive integers $m$ and $n$: $(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$ Key takeaway: Keep the base, multiply the exponents.
  • Examples:
    • $(x^4)^3 = x^{4 \cdot 3} = x^{12}$
    • $(5^2)^5 = 5^{2 \cdot 5} = 5^{10}$

3. Power of a Product

  • Concept: When a product of two or more factors is raised to an exponent, you apply that exponent to each factor individually.
  • Explanation: Consider $(ab)^3$. This means $(ab)$ is used as a factor three times: $(ab)^3 = (ab)(ab)(ab)$. Rearranging the factors, we get $a \cdot a \cdot a \cdot b \cdot b \cdot b = a^3 b^3$.
  • Rule: For any nonzero bases $a$ and $b$ and positive integer $n$: $(ab)^n = a^n b^n$ Key takeaway: Distribute the exponent to each factor within the parentheses.
  • Examples:
    • $(3x)^4 = 3^4 x^4 = 81x^4$
    • $(2a^3)^2 = 2^2 (a^3)^2 = 4a^{3 \cdot 2} = 4a^6$ (This combines the Power of a Product rule with the Power Rule)
    • $(x^2 y^3)^4 = (x^2)^4 (y^3)^4 = x^{2 \cdot 4} y^{3 \cdot 4} = x^8 y^{12}$

4. Dividing Powers with the Same Base (Quotient Rule)

  • Concept: When you divide two powers that have the exact same base, you can combine them by subtracting the exponent of the denominator from the exponent of the numerator.
  • Explanation: Consider $2^7/2^3$. Expanding this: $\frac{2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2}{2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2}$. You can cancel three factors of 2 from the numerator and denominator, leaving $2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 = 2^4$. This is equivalent to subtracting the exponents: $7 - 3 = 4$.
  • Rule: For any nonzero base $a$ and positive integers $m$ and $n$ with $m > n$: $a^m/a^n = a^{m-n}$ Key takeaway: Keep the base, subtract the exponents (numerator's exponent minus denominator's exponent).
  • Important Note: For now, this rule is introduced with the condition that the numerator's exponent ($m$) is greater than the denominator's exponent ($n$). This ensures the result has a positive exponent. Future sections will expand on this to include cases where $m \le n$.
  • Examples:
    • $x^9/x^4 = x^{9-4} = x^5$
    • $12a^7/3a^2 = (12/3) \cdot (a^7/a^2) = 4a^{7-2} = 4a^5$ (Separate the coefficients and variables)

Summary of Exponent Rules (So Far)

  • Product Rule: $a^m a^n = a^{m+n}$
  • Power Rule: $(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$
  • Power of a Product: $(ab)^n = a^n b^n$
  • Quotient Rule: $a^m/a^n = a^{m-n}$ (for $m > n$)

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words why the Product Rule ($a^m a^n = a^{m+n}$) works. Provide an example using numerical bases to illustrate your explanation.
  2. What is the key condition that must be met to apply the Product Rule or the Quotient Rule? Why is this condition important?
  3. Simplify the expression $(3x^4 y^2)^3$. Clearly show each step and indicate which exponent rule(s) you are using at each step.
  4. Compare and contrast the Power Rule ($(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$) and the Power of a Product Rule ($(ab)^n = a^n b^n$). How are they similar, and how are they different in their application?
  5. A student simplifies $10b^{12} / 5b^4$ as $2b^3$. Identify the mistake made by the student and provide the correct simplified expression, explaining your reasoning.
