Study Guide: The Pythagorean Theorem

Overview

The Pythagorean Theorem is a fundamental principle in geometry that establishes a clear mathematical relationship between the three sides of a right triangle. This theorem is crucial for calculating unknown side lengths, verifying if a triangle contains a right angle, and solving a wide range of problems involving distances and geometry.

Key Concepts

  • Right Triangle Components: Understanding the specific names for the sides of a right triangle is essential. The hypotenuse is always the longest side and is located opposite the right (90-degree) angle. The other two sides are called legs, and they form the right angle.
  • The Pythagorean Theorem ($a^2 + b^2 = c^2$): This foundational formula states that in any right triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse (c) is exactly equal to the sum of the squares of the lengths of the two legs (a and b).
  • Finding Missing Sides: The theorem is a powerful tool for determining an unknown side length. If you know the lengths of the two legs, you can find the hypotenuse. If you know the hypotenuse and one leg, you can find the other leg.
  • Simplified Radical Form: When the calculated side length is not a whole number, it's often expressed as a square root. Simplified radical form means simplifying this square root by factoring out any perfect-square factors (other than 1) from under the radical sign.
  • Pythagorean Triples: These are special sets of three positive integers ($a$, $b$, $c$) that perfectly satisfy the Pythagorean Theorem ($a^2 + b^2 = c^2$). Recognizing common triples (like 3, 4, 5) can simplify calculations, and multiples of these triples also form valid triples.
  • Converse of the Pythagorean Theorem: This converse provides a method to test if a given triangle is a right triangle. If the square of the longest side equals the sum of the squares of the two shorter sides, then the triangle must be a right triangle.

Important Terms

  • Hypotenuse: In a right triangle, this is the side directly opposite the right angle. It is always the longest side of the three.
  • Legs: The two shorter sides of a right triangle that form (meet at) the right angle.
  • Pythagorean Theorem: A fundamental theorem in geometry stating that in a right triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse ($c$) is equal to the sum of the squares of the lengths of the two legs ($a$ and $b$), expressed as $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$.
  • Simplified Radical Form: The form of a square root where no perfect-square factor (other than 1) remains under the radical symbol. For example, $\sqrt{50}$ simplifies to $5\sqrt{2}$.
  • Pythagorean Triple: A set of three positive integers ($a$, $b$, $c$) that precisely satisfy the Pythagorean Theorem, meaning $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$. Examples include (3, 4, 5) and (5, 12, 13).

Detailed Notes

The Pythagorean Theorem is one of the most widely recognized and applied results in all of mathematics. It provides a simple yet profound connection between the three sides of any right triangle, making it invaluable for geometry, architecture, engineering, and many other fields.

Parts of a Right Triangle

To correctly apply the Pythagorean Theorem, you must first be able to identify the specific parts of a right triangle:

  • Right Angle: This is the 90-degree angle within the triangle, typically marked with a square symbol.
  • Hypotenuse: This is the side opposite the right angle. Crucially, it is always the longest side of a right triangle. In the Pythagorean Theorem formula, the hypotenuse is represented by the variable $c$.
  • Legs: These are the two sides that form the right angle. They are the shorter sides of the right triangle. In the Pythagorean Theorem formula, the legs are represented by the variables $a$ and $b$.

The Pythagorean Theorem

The theorem itself is stated as follows: In a right triangle with legs $a$ and $b$ and hypotenuse $c$, the relationship between their lengths is: [ a^2 + b^2 = c^2 ] This equation mathematically means that if you imagine building a square on each side of a right triangle, the area of the square built on the hypotenuse will exactly equal the sum of the areas of the squares built on the two legs. For instance, a triangle with legs of $3$ and $4$ will have a hypotenuse of $5$ because $3^2 + 4^2 = 9 + 16 = 25$, and $5^2 = 25$.

Important Caution: It is absolutely vital that the variable $c$ in the formula $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$ always represents the hypotenuse (the longest side, opposite the right angle). If you mistakenly assign one of the legs to $c$, your calculations will lead to an incorrect result.

Using the Theorem to Find Missing Sides

The Pythagorean Theorem is primarily used to find the length of an unknown side in a right triangle when the other two side lengths are known.

  1. Finding the Hypotenuse (given both legs):

    • Example: A right triangle has legs of length $6$ and $8$. Find the hypotenuse.
    • Solution: [ \begin{align*} a^2 + b^2 &= c^2 \ 6^2 + 8^2 &= c^2 \ 36 + 64 &= c^2 \ 100 &= c^2 \ c &= \sqrt{100} \ c &= 10 \end{align*} ] The hypotenuse is $10$.

  2. Finding a Leg (given the hypotenuse and one leg):

    • Example: A right triangle has a hypotenuse of $13$ and one leg of $5$. Find the other leg.
    • Solution: [ \begin{align*} a^2 + b^2 &= c^2 \ 5^2 + b^2 &= 13^2 \ 25 + b^2 &= 169 \ b^2 &= 169 - 25 \ b^2 &= 144 \ b &= \sqrt{144} \ b &= 12 \end{align*} ] The other leg is $12$.

Simplified Radical Form

Often, when calculating a side length using the Pythagorean Theorem, the result for $c^2$ or $b^2$ will not be a perfect square. In these situations, the answer should be expressed in simplified radical form.

  • Definition: A square root $\sqrt{n}$ is in simplified radical form when no perfect-square factor (other than $1$) remains under the radical sign. For example, $\sqrt{50}$ is simplified to $5\sqrt{2}$.

  • Example: A right triangle has legs of length $4$ and $7$. Find the hypotenuse in simplified radical form.

    • Solution: [ \begin{align*} c^2 &= 4^2 + 7^2 \ c^2 &= 16 + 49 \ c^2 &= 65 \ c &= \sqrt{65} \end{align*} ] Since $65 = 5 \times 13$ has no perfect-square factors (other than 1), $\sqrt{65}$ is already in simplified radical form. So, $c = \sqrt{65}$.

  • Example: A right triangle has legs of length $2$ and $6$. Find the hypotenuse in simplified radical form.

    • Solution: [ \begin{align*} c^2 &= 2^2 + 6^2 \ c^2 &= 4 + 36 \ c^2 &= 40 \ c &= \sqrt{40} \end{align*} ] To simplify $\sqrt{40}$, we look for perfect square factors of $40$. We know that $40 = 4 \times 10$. Since $4$ is a perfect square ($2^2=4$): $c = \sqrt{4 \times 10} = \sqrt{4} \times \sqrt{10} = 2\sqrt{10}$. So, $c = 2\sqrt{10}$.

Pythagorean Triples

A Pythagorean triple is a special set of three positive integers ($a$, $b$, $c$) that satisfy the equation $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$. Recognizing these sets can often speed up problem-solving.

  • Common Pythagorean Triples:
    • (3, 4, 5) because $3^2 + 4^2 = 9 + 16 = 25 = 5^2$
    • (5, 12, 13) because $5^2 + 12^2 = 25 + 144 = 169 = 13^2$
    • (8, 15, 17) because $8^2 + 15^2 = 64 + 225 = 289 = 17^2$
  • Multiples of Triples: Any positive integer multiple of a Pythagorean triple is also a Pythagorean triple. For example, multiplying (3, 4, 5) by 2 gives (6, 8, 10), which is also a triple: $6^2 + 8^2 = 36 + 64 = 100 = 10^2$.
  • Testing for a Triple: To check if a set of numbers (like 7, 24, 25) is a Pythagorean triple, substitute them into the formula: $7^2 + 24^2 = 49 + 576 = 625$. Since $25^2 = 625$, then (7, 24, 25) is indeed a Pythagorean triple.

Testing Whether a Triangle Is a Right Triangle (Converse of the Pythagorean Theorem)

The Converse of the Pythagorean Theorem provides a way to determine if a given triangle is a right triangle if you know all three of its side lengths.

  • Property: If $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$ for the three sides of a triangle (where $c$ is designated as the longest side), then the triangle is a right triangle.

  • Example: A triangle has sides of length $9$, $40$, and $41$. Is it a right triangle?

    • Solution: First, identify the longest side, which will be $c$. In this case, $c = 41$. Next, check if $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$: [ 9^2 + 40^2 = 81 + 1600 = 1681 ] Now, calculate $c^2$: [ 41^2 = 1681 ] Since $9^2 + 40^2 = 41^2$, the triangle is indeed a right triangle.

Important Note: When using the converse to test if a triangle is a right triangle, always ensure that the largest value among the three side lengths is substituted for $c$ in the equation $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$. If you don't use the longest side for $c$, your conclusion might be incorrect.

Working with Decimal Side Lengths

The Pythagorean Theorem applies equally well when the side lengths involve decimals.

  • Example: A right triangle has legs of length $1.5$ and $2$. Find the hypotenuse.
    • Solution: [ \begin{align*} c^2 &= 1.5^2 + 2^2 \ c^2 &= 2.25 + 4 \ c^2 &= 6.25 \ c &= \sqrt{6.25} \ c &= 2.5 \end{align*} ] The hypotenuse is $2.5$.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words what the Pythagorean Theorem states and why it is only applicable to right triangles. What defines a "right" triangle?
  2. Describe the difference between the hypotenuse and the legs of a right triangle. Why is it absolutely crucial to correctly identify the hypotenuse before applying the Pythagorean Theorem?
  3. A builder is constructing a rectangular frame. He measures the diagonal to be 25 feet, and one side to be 7 feet. What should the length of the other side be to ensure the frame is perfectly rectangular (has right angles)? Show your calculation.
  4. A right triangle has legs measuring $5$ cm and $8$ cm. Calculate the length of the hypotenuse and express your answer in simplified radical form. What steps did you take to simplify the radical?
  5. What is a Pythagorean triple? Give two examples of Pythagorean triples that are not explicitly listed in the textbook content (you may use multiples of listed ones) and explain how you can generate more triples from existing ones.
