The Distance Formula: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the Distance Formula, a powerful tool derived from the Pythagorean Theorem that allows you to calculate the straight-line distance between any two points in the coordinate plane. It explains the formula's derivation, demonstrates its application with various coordinate types, including negative numbers and real-world scenarios, and highlights common pitfalls to avoid.

Key Concepts

  • The Coordinate Plane: The two-dimensional space defined by an x-axis and a y-axis, where points are located using ordered pairs $(x, y)$.
  • Pythagorean Theorem: The fundamental theorem that states in a right-angled triangle, the square of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle) is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides ($a^2 + b^2 = c^2$). This theorem forms the basis for the Distance Formula.
  • Derivation of the Distance Formula: By drawing horizontal and vertical lines between two points, a right triangle is formed. The lengths of the horizontal and vertical legs are the absolute differences in the x-coordinates and y-coordinates, respectively. Applying the Pythagorean Theorem to these legs and the distance as the hypotenuse leads to the formula.
  • The Distance Formula: For any two points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$, the distance $d$ between them is given by: $d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$
  • Order of Points: When using the formula, it does not matter which point is designated $(x_1, y_1)$ and which is $(x_2, y_2)$. This is because squaring the differences $(x_2 - x_1)$ and $(y_2 - y_1)$ will always result in a positive value, regardless of the sign of the difference.
  • Simplified Radical Form: Distances that are not perfect squares (like $\sqrt{34}$) are often left in simplified radical form for an exact answer, especially when further calculations might be needed or when an approximation isn't appropriate.
  • Real-World Applications: The Distance Formula can be used in practical situations, such as finding distances on a map or determining the length of a diagonal path, often requiring unit conversions based on a given scale.

Important Terms

  • Coordinate Plane: A plane formed by the intersection of a horizontal number line (x-axis) and a vertical number line (y-axis).
  • Coordinates: An ordered pair of numbers $(x, y)$ that specifies the location of a point on a coordinate plane.
  • Pythagorean Theorem: A fundamental relation in Euclidean geometry among the three sides of a right triangle: $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$.
  • Horizontal Leg: The segment of a right triangle parallel to the x-axis, representing the difference in x-coordinates. Its length is $|x_2 - x_1|$.
  • Vertical Leg: The segment of a right triangle parallel to the y-axis, representing the difference in y-coordinates. Its length is $|y_2 - y_1|$.
  • Hypotenuse: The longest side of a right triangle, opposite the right angle. In the context of the Distance Formula, it represents the distance between the two points.
  • Distance Formula: A formula used to calculate the distance between two points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ in a coordinate plane: $d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$.
  • Simplified Radical Form: An expression with a square root where the radicand (the number under the radical sign) has no perfect square factors other than 1. For example, $\sqrt{50}$ simplifies to $5\sqrt{2}$.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to the Distance Formula

In previous lessons, you might have found the distance between two points by plotting them on a grid, forming a right triangle, and then using the Pythagorean Theorem to find the length of the hypotenuse. This section takes that concept a step further by generalizing it into a universal formula that works for any two points in the coordinate plane, even when a grid isn't visible or practical to draw.

Deriving the Formula

  1. Start with Two Points: Let's consider two arbitrary points in the coordinate plane: $P_1(x_1, y_1)$ and $P_2(x_2, y_2)$.
  2. Form a Right Triangle: Imagine drawing a horizontal line segment from $P_1$ to an intermediate point $(x_2, y_1)$ and a vertical line segment from $(x_2, y_1)$ to $P_2$. These two segments, along with the straight-line segment connecting $P_1$ and $P_2$, form a right triangle.
  3. Determine Leg Lengths:
    • The length of the horizontal leg is the absolute difference between the x-coordinates: $|x_2 - x_1|$.
    • The length of the vertical leg is the absolute difference between the y-coordinates: $|y_2 - y_1|$.
  4. Apply Pythagorean Theorem: Let $d$ be the distance between $P_1$ and $P_2$ (which is the hypotenuse of our right triangle). According to the Pythagorean Theorem ($a^2 + b^2 = c^2$): $d^2 = (|x_2 - x_1|)^2 + (|y_2 - y_1|)^2$ Since squaring any number makes it positive, the absolute value signs are not strictly necessary inside the square: $d^2 = (x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2$
  5. Solve for d: To find $d$, take the square root of both sides: $d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$

The Distance Formula

The distance $d$ between points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ is: $d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$

Important Note on Point Order

A helpful aspect of the Distance Formula is that it doesn't matter which point you label as $(x_1, y_1)$ and which as $(x_2, y_2)$. This is because the differences $(x_2 - x_1)$ and $(y_2 - y_1)$ are squared. For example, $(7 - 1)^2 = 6^2 = 36$, and $(1 - 7)^2 = (-6)^2 = 36$. Squaring always results in a positive value, eliminating any concerns about the order of subtraction.

Examples of Using the Distance Formula

  1. Basic Example: Find the distance between $A(1, 2)$ and $B(7, 10)$.

    • Let $(x_1, y_1) = (1, 2)$ and $(x_2, y_2) = (7, 10)$.
    • $d = \sqrt{(7 - 1)^2 + (10 - 2)^2}$
    • $d = \sqrt{(6)^2 + (8)^2}$
    • $d = \sqrt{36 + 64}$
    • $d = \sqrt{100}$
    • $d = 10$

  2. Distance with Negative Coordinates: Find the distance between $C(-3, 4)$ and $D(5, -2)$.

    • Let $(x_1, y_1) = (-3, 4)$ and $(x_2, y_2) = (5, -2)$.
    • $d = \sqrt{(5 - (-3))^2 + (-2 - 4)^2}$
    • $d = \sqrt{(5 + 3)^2 + (-6)^2}$
    • $d = \sqrt{(8)^2 + (-6)^2}$
    • $d = \sqrt{64 + 36}$
    • $d = \sqrt{100}$
    • $d = 10$

  3. Distance in Simplified Radical Form: Find the distance between $(1, 3)$ and $(4, 8)$. Write your answer in simplified radical form.

    • Let $(x_1, y_1) = (1, 3)$ and $(x_2, y_2) = (4, 8)$.
    • $d = \sqrt{(4 - 1)^2 + (8 - 3)^2}$
    • $d = \sqrt{(3)^2 + (5)^2}$
    • $d = \sqrt{9 + 25}$
    • $d = \sqrt{34}$
    • Since 34 has no perfect-square factors (its factors are 1, 2, 17, 34), $\sqrt{34}$ is already in simplified radical form.

  4. Coordinate Map Application: On a coordinate map where each unit represents 0.5 mile, a school is at $(2, 3)$ and a store is at $(8, 11)$. What is the straight-line distance between them?

    • Step 1: Find the distance in map units.
      • Let $(x_1, y_1) = (2, 3)$ and $(x_2, y_2) = (8, 11)$.
      • $d = \sqrt{(8 - 2)^2 + (11 - 3)^2}$
      • $d = \sqrt{(6)^2 + (8)^2}$
      • $d = \sqrt{36 + 64}$
      • $d = \sqrt{100}$
      • $d = 10$ units
    • Step 2: Convert map units to miles.
      • Each unit represents 0.5 mile.
      • Total distance $= 10 \text{ units} \times 0.5 \text{ miles/unit} = 5 \text{ miles}$.

Caution: A Common Mistake

Be extremely careful not to forget to square the differences before adding them. A frequent error is to incorrectly compute $d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1) + (y_2 - y_1)}$ instead of the correct $d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$. Remember the Pythagorean Theorem involves squares of the leg lengths.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words how the Distance Formula is directly derived from the Pythagorean Theorem, identifying which parts of the formula correspond to the legs and hypotenuse of a right triangle.
  2. When calculating the distance between $(x_A, y_A)$ and $(x_B, y_B)$, does it matter if you use $(x_A, y_A)$ as $(x_1, y_1)$ or as $(x_2, y_2)$? Justify your answer mathematically.
  3. Describe a real-world scenario where you would need to use the Distance Formula. How would you apply it in that situation, and what kind of result would you expect?
  4. After calculating a distance using the formula, you arrive at $\sqrt{72}$. How would you express this distance in simplified radical form, and why is this form often preferred in mathematics?
  5. A student calculated the distance between $P(2, 5)$ and $Q(8, 7)$ as $d = \sqrt{(8-2) + (7-5)} = \sqrt{6 + 2} = \sqrt{8}$. Identify the specific error the student made and explain the correct first step in applying the Distance Formula.
