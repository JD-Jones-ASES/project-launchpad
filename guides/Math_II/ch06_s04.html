Study Guide: Volume of Pyramids and Cones

Overview

This section introduces pyramids and cones as "pointed" three-dimensional solids that taper from a base to a single apex (or vertex). It reveals a crucial relationship: the volume of both pyramids and cones is exactly one-third the volume of a corresponding prism or cylinder that shares the same base area and height. The section provides formulas and examples for calculating the volume of various types of pyramids and cones.

Key Concepts

  • "Pointed" Solids: Pyramids and cones are characterized by tapering from a base to a single point called an apex or vertex.
  • The 1/3 Factor: A fundamental principle in geometry states that the volume of a pyramid or a cone is one-third the volume of a prism or cylinder, respectively, that has the same base area and the same height.
  • Volume of a Pyramid: The formula for the volume of any pyramid is $V = \frac{1}{3}Bh$, where $B$ represents the area of its polygonal base and $h$ is its height.
  • Volume of a Cone: The formula for the volume of a cone is $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$, where $r$ is the radius of its circular base and $h$ is its height. This is derived from the pyramid formula, as the base area $B$ for a cone is $\pi r^2$.
  • Base Area Calculation: For pyramids, the base area $B$ must be calculated first based on the shape of the base (e.g., square, rectangle, triangle).

Important Terms

  • Pyramid: A solid with one polygonal base and triangular lateral faces that meet at a common vertex (apex). Pyramids are named by the shape of their base (e.g., square pyramid, triangular pyramid).
  • Cone: A solid with one circular base and a curved lateral surface that tapers to a single point (the apex).
  • Apex (or Vertex): The single point at the top of a pyramid or cone where all the lateral faces or the curved lateral surface meet.
  • Base Area ($B$): The area of the bottom face of the pyramid or cone. For a pyramid, this will be the area of its polygonal base. For a cone, it's the area of its circular base ($\pi r^2$).
  • Height ($h$): The perpendicular distance from the apex to the base of the pyramid or cone.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Pyramids and Cones

Pyramids and cones are unique three-dimensional shapes because they "point" to a single apex (or vertex). Unlike prisms and cylinders which have two parallel, congruent bases, pyramids and cones only have one base. The lateral faces of a pyramid are triangles, while a cone has a single curved lateral surface.

The "1/3 Factor" Relationship

A surprising and important relationship exists between the volume of a "pointed" solid (pyramid or cone) and its "flat-topped" counterpart (prism or cylinder). If a pyramid and a prism have exactly the same base area ($B$) and the same height ($h$), the pyramid's volume is precisely one-third of the prism's volume. The same principle applies to cones and cylinders: a cone's volume is one-third the volume of a cylinder with the same circular base and height. This means:

  • Volume of Pyramid = $\frac{1}{3}$ * (Volume of corresponding Prism)
  • Volume of Cone = $\frac{1}{3}$ * (Volume of corresponding Cylinder)

Volume of a Pyramid

The general formula for the volume of any pyramid is: $$V = \frac{1}{3}Bh$$ Where:

  • $V$ is the volume of the pyramid.
  • $B$ is the area of the base of the pyramid. This is critical: you must first calculate the area of the specific polygonal base (e.g., square, rectangle, triangle) before using it in the volume formula.
  • $h$ is the height of the pyramid, which is the perpendicular distance from the apex to the plane containing the base.

Example: Volume of a Square Pyramid To find the volume of a square pyramid with a base side length of $9$ cm and a height of $14$ cm:

  1. Calculate the base area ($B$): Since the base is a square with side length $9$ cm, $B = \text{side} \times \text{side} = 9^2 = 81 \text{ cm}^2$.
  2. Apply the volume formula: $V = \frac{1}{3}Bh = \frac{1}{3}(81)(14)$
  3. Calculate the volume: $V = \frac{1}{3}(1134) = 378 \text{ cm}^3$.

Example: Volume of a Rectangular Pyramid To find the volume of a rectangular pyramid with a base measuring $6$ in. by $4.5$ in. and a height of $10$ in.:

  1. Calculate the base area ($B$): Since the base is a rectangle, $B = \text{length} \times \text{width} = (6)(4.5) = 27 \text{ in.}^2$.
  2. Apply the volume formula: $V = \frac{1}{3}Bh = \frac{1}{3}(27)(10)$
  3. Calculate the volume: $V = \frac{1}{3}(270) = 90 \text{ in.}^3$.

Volume of a Cone

The same $\frac{1}{3}$ relationship applies to cones and cylinders. A cylinder's volume is $V = \text{Base Area} \times \text{height} = \pi r^2 h$. Therefore, the volume of a cone is: $$V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$$ Where:

  • $V$ is the volume of the cone.
  • $\pi$ (pi) is a mathematical constant, approximately $3.14159$.
  • $r$ is the radius of the circular base.
  • $h$ is the height of the cone, the perpendicular distance from the apex to the center of the base.

Example: Volume of a Cone To find the volume of a cone with a radius of $5$ cm and a height of $12$ cm, expressing the answer in terms of $\pi$ and as a decimal:

  1. Apply the volume formula: $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h = \frac{1}{3}\pi (5)^2 (12)$
  2. Simplify: $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi (25)(12) = \frac{1}{3}\pi (300)$
  3. Calculate in terms of $\pi$: $V = 100\pi \text{ cm}^3$.
  4. Calculate as a decimal (using $\pi \approx 3.14$ as often done in textbooks, though more precise is better for actual calculations): $V \approx 100 \times 3.14 = 314 \text{ cm}^3$.

Example: Ice-Cream Cone (Application) To find how much ice cream an ice-cream cone can hold (ignoring the scoop above the rim) if it has a diameter of $3$ in. and a height of $6$ in. (Use $\pi \approx 3.14$):

  1. Determine the radius ($r$): The diameter is $3$ in., so the radius is half of that: $r = \frac{3}{2} = 1.5 \text{ in.}$
  2. Apply the volume formula: $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h = \frac{1}{3}(3.14)(1.5)^2(6)$
  3. Calculate:
    • $V = \frac{1}{3}(3.14)(2.25)(6)$
    • $V = \frac{1}{3}(42.39)$
    • $V = 14.13 \text{ in.}^3$.

Checkpoint: Volume of a Triangular Pyramid Find the volume of a triangular pyramid whose base is a triangle with base $10$ m and height $6$ m, and whose pyramid height is $9$ m.

  1. Calculate the base area ($B$): The base is a triangle. The area of a triangle is $\frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}$.
    • $B = \frac{1}{2}(10 \text{ m})(6 \text{ m}) = 30 \text{ m}^2$.
  2. Apply the pyramid volume formula: $V = \frac{1}{3}Bh = \frac{1}{3}(30 \text{ m}^2)(9 \text{ m})$.
  3. Calculate the volume: $V = \frac{1}{3}(270) = 90 \text{ m}^3$.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words the key difference between a pyramid and a prism, and how this difference affects their respective volume formulas.
  2. Why is the base area ($B$) specifically mentioned in the formula $V = \frac{1}{3}Bh$ for pyramids, rather than directly using side lengths? Provide an example of how $B$ would be calculated for a hexagonal pyramid.
  3. Describe the "1/3 factor" relationship in geometry. Can you think of a real-world scenario where understanding this relationship would be useful?
  4. A cone and a cylinder both have a radius of $4$ cm and a height of $9$ cm. Calculate the volume of both solids and discuss how their volumes are related.
  5. A builder is designing a new building with a pyramidal roof. The roof's base is a $20$ ft by $20$ ft square, and the height of the roof is $15$ ft. If the builder needs to order insulation that costs $0.50 per cubic foot to fill the entire volume of the roof, what will be the total cost of the insulation?
