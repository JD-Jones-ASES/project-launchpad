Making Polite Requests in Spanish: Pedir Favores

Overview

This section explores how to soften direct commands into polite requests in Spanish, a crucial skill for navigating social interactions with grace. It introduces specific structures like ¿Puedes?, ¿Podrías?, and using Por favor with commands, guiding learners on when to use each based on the level of formality and relationship with the listener. The section also reinforces important grammar points like stem-changing verbs in commands and introduces new practical vocabulary.

Key Concepts

  • Commands vs. Polite Requests: Commands are direct and powerful, suitable for close relationships, younger individuals, or urgent situations. Polite requests are gentler and more appropriate for formal contexts, unfamiliar individuals, or when asking for a favor.
  • Three Main Structures for Polite Requests:
    • ¿Puedes + infinitive...? (Can you...?): Used for everyday requests with classmates, friends, and family members. It's a common and generally informal way to ask politely.
    • ¿Podrías + infinitive...? (Could you...?): This is a more formal and very polite way to make a request, ideal for teachers, adults you don't know well, or professional settings.
    • Por favor + tú command (or tú command + por favor): Adding "Por favor" (Please) directly to a tú command softens it significantly, making it polite for many situations, from family requests to general polite interactions.
  • Context Dictates Formality: The choice between ¿Puedes? and ¿Podrías? (or a direct command) depends heavily on the relationship with the person being addressed and the social context.
  • Stem-Changing Verbs and Commands: A key reminder that if a verb stem-changes in the él/ella present tense form, its tú command will also stem-change. This rule simplifies remembering command forms for these verbs.
  • Useful Verbs for Requests: Familiarization with verbs like abrir (to open), cerrar (to close), and venir (to come) in their command and request forms is emphasized for common household and classroom interactions.

Important Terms

  • Pedir favores: To ask for favors; to make requests.
  • Peticiones corteses: Polite requests.
  • Mandatos: Commands.
  • Por favor: Please.
  • ¿Puedes...?: Can you...? (informal polite request).
  • ¿Podrías...?: Could you...? (more formal polite request).
  • Las reglas: The rules.
  • El trabajo: The (school) work; the job.
  • Antes de empezar: Before starting.
  • Respetuoso: Respectful.
  • Todos los días: Every day.
  • Los colores: The colored pencils / markers.
  • El proyecto: The project.
  • La instrucción / Las instrucciones: Instruction(s).
  • El silencio: Silence / quiet.
  • Tranquilo: Calm, quiet.
  • El párrafo: The paragraph. Tiene sentido: Makes sense.

Detailed Notes

1. The Importance of Softening Commands

While direct commands (mandatos) are essential for giving instructions, especially to people you know very well or in urgent situations, they can sound abrupt or demanding in other contexts. "Pedir favores" (making polite requests) is about choosing a gentler tone that shows respect, particularly when addressing teachers, strangers, or in professional settings. This section provides the tools to achieve that elegant balance in Spanish communication.

2. Key Structures for Making Polite Requests

Spanish offers clear structures to transform a command into a polite request:

  • ¿Puedes + infinitive...? (Can you...?)

    • Structure: This literally translates to "Can you...?" followed by the infinitive form of the verb.
    • Usage: This is a very common and versatile way to make an everyday polite request. It's suitable for classmates, family members, or people you know reasonably well. It's informal but respectful.
    • Examples:
      • ¿Puedes pasar la sal? (Can you pass the salt?)
      • ¿Puedes ayudarme con mi tarea? (Can you help me with my homework?)
      • ¿Puedes cerrar la ventana? (Can you close the window?)

  • ¿Podrías + infinitive...? (Could you...?)

    • Structure: This uses the conditional form of poder (to be able to), followed by the infinitive.
    • Usage: This structure is notably more formal and deferential than ¿Puedes...?. It's the go-to choice for teachers, adults you don't know well, people in positions of authority, or in professional environments. It conveys a higher level of politeness and respect.
    • Examples:
      • ¿Podría explicar la lección otra vez, por favor? (Could you explain the lesson again, please?)
      • ¿Podrían indicarme el camino al museo? (Could you show me the way to the museum?) (using formal ustedes form)
      • ¿Podrías enviarme el informe mañana? (Could you send me the report tomorrow?) (if addressing a colleague you don't know well)

  • Por favor + tú command (or tú command + por favor)

    • Structure: Simply adding "Por favor" (Please) to a regular tú command makes it polite. It can be placed at the beginning or end of the command.
    • Usage: This is a straightforward way to soften any direct command without changing the verb form. It's widely applicable in many situations where a general level of politeness is desired, even with family or friends, or when you want to be polite but still direct.
    • Examples:
      • Por favor, escucha cuando hablo. (Please, listen when I speak.)
      • ¡Cierra la puerta, por favor! (Close the door, please!)
      • Por favor, haz tu tarea. (Please, do your homework.)

3. More Useful Verbs for Commands and Requests

This section introduces three common verbs useful in daily interactions:

Verb (Infinitive) tú Command tú Polite Request (¿Puedes...?) tú More Formal Request (¿Podrías...?) Meaning
Abrir (to open) ¡Abre! ¿Puedes abrir? ¿Podrías abrir? Open!
Cerrar (to close) ¡Cierra! ¿Puedes cerrar? ¿Podrías cerrar? Close!
Venir (to come) ¡Ven! ¿Puedes venir? ¿Podrías venir? Come!

4. Important Grammar Reminder: Stem-Changing Verbs in Commands

Remember that the tú command form often mirrors the stem-change seen in the él/ella form of the verb in the present tense. This is a crucial shortcut:

  • If a verb stem-changes in él/ella present tense, it will also stem-change in the tú command.
  • Example: Cerrar (e -> ie stem-changer)
    • él/ella cierra (present tense)
    • ¡Cierra! (tú command)
  • This means if you know the él/ella present tense, you almost always know the tú command (except for the 8 irregular tú commands like venir which become ¡Ven!, ser which becomes ¡Sé!, hacer which becomes ¡Haz! etc. that you learned in the previous section).

5. Practical Application: Classroom Rules

The "Las reglas de la clase" section vividly demonstrates the difference in tone between commands and polite requests, even when conveying the same message.

Commands (Mandatos) Polite Requests (Peticiones Corteses)
¡Escucha cuando el maestro habla! Por favor, escucha cuando el maestro habla.
¡Escribe tu nombre en todos los trabajos! ¿Puedes escribir tu nombre en todos los trabajos?
¡Lee las instrucciones antes de empezar! Por favor, lee las instrucciones antes de empezar.
¡Sé respetuoso con todos! ¿Podrías ser respetuoso con todos?
¡Haz tu tarea todos los días! Por favor, haz tu tarea todos los días.

Notice how the polite requests soften the directive, making it sound more like a cooperative request than a direct order. The choice of ¿Podrías? for "ser respetuoso" highlights its more formal nature, often used for broader behavioral expectations.

6. Conversation Analysis: ¿Me ayudas, por favor?

The dialogue between Camila, Tomás, and Rodrigo provides a real-world example of these structures in action:

  • "Tomás, ¿puedes pasar los colores?": Camila uses ¿Puedes...? for a casual request to a classmate.
  • "¿Me ayudas con las instrucciones?": Tomás also uses ¿Puedes...? (implied ¿Puedes ayudarme...?) for a peer-to-peer request.
  • "Rodrigo, ¡cierra la puerta, por favor!": Camila uses a direct tú command (¡cierra!) for her younger brother, but softens it with "por favor." This shows she's still being polite, but given the sibling relationship, a direct command is acceptable.
  • "Sí, pero sé tranquilo. ¡Y no toques nuestro proyecto!": Camila uses direct commands (¡sé!, ¡no toques!) for Rodrigo, emphasizing the urgency of his behavior around their project.
  • "Rodrigo, ven aquí — ¿puedes leer este párrafo...?": Tomás uses a direct command (¡Ven!) to get Rodrigo's attention, followed by a ¿Puedes...? for a specific task, indicating a slightly more formal (or persuasive) approach than a direct command, even for a younger child.

This conversation perfectly illustrates how speakers adapt their language depending on their relationship with the listener and the specific context of the request.
