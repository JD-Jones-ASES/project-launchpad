Study Guide: Los Mandatos Irregulares (Irregular Tú Commands)

Overview

This section introduces the eight irregular tú commands in Spanish, which do not follow the standard rule of matching the él/ella present-tense form. It emphasizes the importance of memorizing these common commands and provides practical strategies for doing so. The section also explores the nuanced use of commands in poetic and cultural contexts, illustrating how they can convey affection or responsibility within family dynamics.

Key Concepts

  • Eight Irregular Tú Commands: A specific, finite group of eight verbs (venir, tener, salir, hacer, decir, ser, poner, ir/ver) that have unique, non-standard tú command forms.
  • Deviation from Regular Pattern: Unlike most affirmative tú commands, these irregular forms do not match the él/ella present-tense conjugation of their infinitives (e.g., venir → ven, not viene).
  • Importance of Memorization: Due to their high frequency in everyday Spanish, these eight commands must be learned by heart.
  • Context for ve: The command form ve can come from either ir (to go) or ver (to see). Contextual cues, such as following a + a place for ir or an object for ver, help differentiate their meanings.
  • Memorization Aids: Effective techniques like rhythmic repetition ("The Command Song") and the "story method" (linking commands to a vivid narrative) are presented to facilitate learning.
  • Commands Beyond Orders: The section highlights how commands can express emotions like love and friendship, as exemplified by Gabriela Mistral's poem "Dame la mano," where dame acts as an invitation.
  • Cultural Significance of Commands: In cultures like Venezuela, commands within the family are integral to teaching responsibility, fostering contribution, and strengthening intergenerational bonds, particularly around shared activities like meals.

Important Terms

  • Mandatos irregulares: Irregular commands; specifically referring to the eight tú commands that do not follow the standard conjugation pattern.
  • Tú commands: Informal affirmative commands directed at "you" (singular, informal).
  • Infinitivo: The base form of a verb (e.g., venir, hacer).
  • Presente de indicativo (él/ella): The third-person singular (he/she/it) form of a verb in the present indicative tense. This is the form that regular tú commands are based on.
  • Reflexive pronoun: A pronoun that reflects the action of the verb back to the subject (e.g., se).
  • Imperative mood: The grammatical mood used to express commands, requests, or prohibitions.
  • Poeta: Poet.
  • Familia multigeneracional: A family structure where multiple generations (e.g., grandparents, parents, children) live together or in close proximity.
  • El almuerzo: The midday meal, often the main meal of the day in Spanish-speaking cultures, and a significant family gathering time.
  • Objeto directo: The noun or pronoun that receives the action of the verb directly.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Irregular Tú Commands

While most affirmative tú commands are formed by using the él/ella present-tense form of the verb, there are eight crucial verbs that defy this rule. These are known as the irregular tú commands, and due to their common usage, memorizing them is essential for fluency.

The Eight Irregular Tú Commands

These verbs have unique, fixed command forms that must be learned as they are. They are:

  1. VEN (from venir - to come)
    • Él viene vs. ¡Ven! (Come!)
  2. TEN (from tener - to have)
  3. SAL (from salir - to leave/go out)
  4. HAZ (from hacer - to do/make)
    • Él hace vs. ¡Haz! (Do!/Make!)
  5. DI (from decir - to say/tell)
  6. SÉ (from ser - to be)
  7. PON (from poner - to put/place)
  8. VE (from ir - to go, or ver - to see/watch)

Accent Marks

  • SÉ (be!): Has an accent mark to distinguish it from the reflexive pronoun se.
  • DI (say!/tell!): While historically it sometimes had an accent mark, in modern Spanish, it is generally written without one.

Distinguishing "Ve" from "Ir" and "Ver"

The command form ve is unique because it can originate from two different infinitives: ir (to go) and ver (to see/watch). Context is key to understanding which verb is intended:

  • From ir (to go): Often followed by "al/a la" and a place.
    • ¡Ve al supermercado! (Go to the supermarket!)
  • From ver (to see/watch): Typically followed by an object that is being seen or watched.
    • ¡Ve la película! (Watch the movie!)

Memorization Strategies

To help commit these eight irregular commands to memory, two methods are suggested:

  1. The Command Song (Rhythm and Rhyme):

    • Repeat the commands rhythmically: VEN - TEN - SAL - HAZ - DI - SÉ - PON - VE.
    • Practicing this sequence multiple times can help create a memorable auditory pattern.

  2. The Story Method (Visual Narrative):

    • Create a silly, vivid story where each command is linked to an action or image. The more absurd or striking the story, the better it will stick in your mind.
    • Example Story: "COME (ven) to the store. HAVE (ten) a coin in your hand. LEAVE (sal) early before the rain. MAKE (haz) a big cake for everyone. SAY (di) hello to your neighbor. BE (sé) kind and generous. PUT (pon) the cake on the table. GO (ve) home before dark!"
    • Visualize each scene clearly as you say the command to reinforce the memory.

Cultural Context: Commands in Poetry and Family Life

"Dame la mano" by Gabriela Mistral

  • About the Poet: Gabriela Mistral (1889–1957) was a Chilean educator, diplomat, and the first Latin American woman to win the Nobel Prize in Literature (1945). Known for her warm, simple, and deeply human poetry, especially for children.
  • About the Poem: "Dame la mano" ("Give Me Your Hand") is from her collection Ternura (Tenderness, 1924). It's an invitation to companionship and solidarity, using the imperative mood to express love and friendship rather than a strict order.
  • Analysis of "Dame": The word dame is a command form combining da (the tú command from dar - to give) and the object pronoun me (to me). So, dame means "give me." In the poem, this command is gentle and loving, inviting connection rather than demanding obedience.
  • Commands for Affection: Mistral's use of commands like dame transforms a typically authoritative grammatical mood into an expression of warmth, invitation, and shared experience, showing the versatility of commands in language. Her background as a schoolteacher likely influenced her ability to craft poems for children with such clarity and emotional depth.

La familia venezolana (The Venezuelan Family)

  • Close-Knit Structure: Venezuelan families are typically multigenerational and close-knit, with grandparents often living nearby or in the same home, playing an active role in raising grandchildren.
  • Children's Responsibility: Children are expected to contribute to household chores from an early age, viewing it as a way to learn responsibility and contribute to the family's well-being, not just a burden.
    • Examples of commands used: ¡Pon la mesa, por favor! (Set the table, please!) and ¡Ayuda a tu abuela con la cocina! (Help your grandmother with the cooking!). These are direct, yet imply participation and care.
  • The Midday Meal (El almuerzo): This is a central daily event where the entire family gathers. Eating together strengthens family bonds, fosters social skills, and reinforces polite language usage. Commands given during this time are part of teaching social norms and interaction within the family unit. Commands in this context reinforce a sense of community and shared duties rather than being simply authoritative.

Review Questions

  1. Why are the eight irregular tú commands particularly important to memorize, even though there are only a few of them? How do they differ from regular tú commands?
  2. Explain how you would determine whether the command ve means "go" (from ir) or "see/watch" (from ver). Provide an example sentence for each scenario.
  3. Describe the "story method" for memorizing the irregular tú commands. How does creating a vivid narrative help with retention?
  4. In Gabriela Mistral's poem "Dame la mano," the command dame is used. Analyze how this command conveys a feeling of friendship and invitation rather than a forceful order.
  5. Based on the "La familia venezolana" section, discuss how commands like ¡Pon la mesa! or ¡Ayuda a tu abuela! are used within the family context to teach children responsibility and reinforce family values.
