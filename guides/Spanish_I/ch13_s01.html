Chapter 13, Section 1: En la Tienda (At the Store) Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces essential Spanish vocabulary for discussing clothing and navigating a shopping experience, whether in a modern store or a vibrant open-air market. It also reviews and expands on numbers from 100 to 1000, focusing on crucial gender agreement rules, and offers cultural insights into the unique experience of shopping and bargaining in Latin American markets, particularly in Bolivia.

Key Concepts

  • Essential Shopping Vocabulary: Learning words for various items of clothing (la ropa) and terms related to the shopping environment (la tienda, el precio, el dinero) is fundamental for real-world interactions.
  • Using llevar for Wearing: The versatile verb llevar is key for describing what someone is wearing, directly translating to "to wear" in this context.
  • Numbers 100-1000 and Gender Agreement: Expanding numerical knowledge beyond 100, including the critical rule that numbers 200-900 must agree in gender with the noun they describe. Understanding the distinction between cien and ciento is also vital.
  • Bargaining as a Cultural Practice (regatear): In many Latin American markets, bargaining is not only accepted but expected and considered a social ritual, offering a unique cultural experience beyond a simple transaction.
  • Cultural Connections through Rhymes: Traditional children's rhymes, like "Aserrín, aserrán," provide a glimpse into everyday community life, traditional crafts, and the use of onomatopoeia, enriching language learning with cultural context and a playful approach.

Important Terms

  • la ropa: clothing, clothes (always used in singular)
  • el zapato / los zapatos: shoe / shoes
  • la camisa: shirt (button-up or collared)
  • el vestido: dress
  • el pantalón / los pantalones: pants, trousers
  • la falda: skirt
  • el calcetín / los calcetines: sock / socks
  • el sombrero: hat (wide-brimmed)
  • la gorra: baseball cap
  • el bolso: bag, purse, handbag
  • la joya / las joyas: jewel / jewels, piece of jewelry
  • la talla: size (clothing size)
  • llevar: to wear, to carry
  • la tienda: store, shop
  • el mercado: market (open-air or indoor)
  • el precio: price
  • el regalo: gift, present
  • el dinero: money
  • caro/cara: expensive
  • barato/barata: cheap, inexpensive
  • la oferta: sale, special offer (en oferta = on sale)
  • el descuento: discount
  • el recibo: receipt
  • la tarjeta: card (e.g., la tarjeta de crédito/débito = credit/debit card)
  • el efectivo: cash (pagar en efectivo = to pay in cash)
  • el cambio: change (money returned); also: change (general)
  • regatear: to bargain, to haggle
  • onomatopeya: onomatopoeia (a word that imitates the sound it represents)

Detailed Notes

I. Shopping Vocabulary

Learning the right words is the first step to confidently shop in a Spanish-speaking country.

  • A. La ropa (Clothing)

    • la ropa is always singular, even though it refers to multiple items of clothing.
    • Many clothing items are often used in the plural, even when referring to a single pair: los zapatos (shoes), los pantalones (pants), los calcetines (socks).
    • Distinguish between el sombrero (wide-brimmed hat) and la gorra (baseball cap).
    • la talla is specifically for clothing size.
    • las joyas refers to jewelry.

  • B. ¿Qué llevas hoy? (What are you wearing today?)

    • The verb llevar is extremely useful. It means both "to wear" and "to carry."
    • Example: Hoy llevo una camisa azul y pantalones negros. (Today I am wearing a blue shirt and black pants.)

  • C. En la tienda (At the Store)

    • la tienda is a general term for a store, while el mercado refers to a market, often open-air and vibrant.
    • Key terms for transactions include el precio (price), el dinero (money), el recibo (receipt), and el cambio (the money you get back).
    • Adjectives caro/cara (expensive) and barato/barata (cheap) are essential for discussing prices. Remember they agree in gender with the noun they describe.
    • Look out for la oferta (sale/special offer) or el descuento (discount) to save money! En oferta means "on sale."
    • You can pagar en efectivo (pay in cash) or use la tarjeta de crédito/débito (credit/debit card).

II. Numbers 100–1000 (A Quick Review and Expansion)

Reviewing numbers is crucial for understanding prices.

  • A. Core Numbers:

    • 100: cien
    • 200: doscientos/doscientas
    • 300: trescientos/trescientas
    • 400: cuatrocientos/cuatrocientas
    • 500: quinientos/quinientas
    • 600: seiscientos/seiscientas
    • 700: setecientos/setecientas
    • 800: ochocientos/ochocientas
    • 900: novecientos/novecientas
    • 1000: mil

  • B. Combining Numbers:

    • To form numbers in between, combine the hundreds with tens and units, typically using "y" (and) for numbers between 31 and 99.
    • Example: doscientos treinta y cinco (235)

  • C. ¡Ojo! Los números concuerdan en género (Numbers Agree in Gender)

    • This is a common point of confusion. Numbers 200 through 900 must agree in gender with the noun they describe.
      • doscientos libros (200 books - masculine)
      • doscientas camisas (200 shirts - feminine)
    • Important exception: cien (100) and mil (1000) do not change for gender.

  • D. Cien vs. Ciento

    • Use cien when referring to exactly 100. (e.g., cien pesos, cien personas)
    • Use ciento when the number is 101 or more. (e.g., ciento uno, ciento veinte, ciento noventa y nueve)
    • Think of cien as the "round hundred" and ciento as "the hundred plus more."

III. Memory Tip: La simulación de compras (Shopping Simulation)

  • This technique encourages active learning. By mentally walking through a shopping scenario in a Latin American market and naming items and actions in Spanish, you strengthen vocabulary recall.
  • Make your imaginary trip as vivid as possible to enhance memory retention.

IV. Cultural Insights

  • A. Los mercados en Bolivia (Markets in Bolivia)

    • Bolivia is renowned for its diverse open-air markets, which are central to daily life and culture.
    • Examples include:
      • Mercado de las Brujas (La Paz): Sells traditional herbal remedies, charms, and spiritual items, reflecting indigenous Aymara and Quechua healing practices.
      • Artisan markets (Sucre): Known for hand-woven textiles, silver jewelry, and pottery with indigenous patterns.
      • Potosí markets: Offer everything from produce to handmade crafts, often displayed in colorful aguayos (traditional woven cloths).
    • Regatear (Bargaining): In Bolivian markets, bargaining is not just common; it's a cherished social ritual. It's expected and enjoyed, often involving a friendly back-and-forth until a fair price is reached. Bargain with a smile and a friendly tone to enhance the cultural experience. Paying the first price can sometimes be seen as unusual by vendors.

  • B. Aserrín, aserrán (Traditional Children's Rhyme)

    • This traditional Latin American children's rhyme imitates the sound of a saw cutting wood (aserrín, aserrán is an example of onomatopoeia).
    • It celebrates the work of carpenters and craftspeople, linking to the artisan culture seen in markets.
    • Children often perform physical movements while reciting it, demonstrating how physical engagement can aid in memory and rhythm. This connection between movement, sound, and language is a powerful learning tool.

  3. Explain the rule for gender agreement when using numbers like "two hundred" or "five hundred" in Spanish. Provide an example using both masculine and feminine nouns. Why do "one hundred" and "one thousand" not follow this rule?
