Estudio Guiado: Números, Hora, Días y Meses

Overview

This section expands your Spanish vocabulary to include numbers up to 100, how to tell time, and essential calendar terms like the days of the week and months of the year. By understanding the patterns in numbers and specific grammatical rules for expressing time and dates, you'll gain crucial conversational skills and insight into Spanish-speaking calendar conventions.

Key Concepts

  • Number Patterns 0-100: Spanish numbers follow specific patterns. Numbers 16-19 and 21-29 are written as single words (e.g., dieciséis, veintiuno), while numbers 31 and above combine the tens and ones with "y" (e.g., treinta y uno).
  • Telling Time (¿Qué hora es?): The verb phrase used to tell time depends on the hour. Use "Es la" only for 1:00, and "Son las" for all other hours (2:00 and up). Fractions of the hour like "y cuarto" (quarter past) and "y media" (half past) are common.
  • Time of Day: Specify morning, afternoon, or night using "de la mañana," "de la tarde," and "de la noche" to clarify the time.
  • Spanish Calendar Conventions: In Spanish-speaking countries, the calendar week typically starts on Monday. Both days of the week and months of the year are not capitalized in Spanish (e.g., lunes, enero).
  • Expressing Dates: Dates are formatted as day + "de" + month, with the optional addition of "de" + year (e.g., el quince de octubre).
  • Memory Techniques: Engaging with cultural resources like counting songs and utilizing memory aids such as "Number Pegs" can significantly enhance vocabulary retention.

Detailed Notes

I. Los Números (Numbers)

  • Review 0-10: You should already be familiar with cero, uno, dos, tres, cuatro, cinco, seis, siete, ocho, nueve, diez.
  • Numbers 11-15: These are unique: once, doce, trece, catorce, quince.
  • Numbers 16-19 (One Word): These are formed by combining "diez" (ten) and the single digit, often with a slight spelling change. They are written as one word:
    • dieciséis (16)
    • diecisiete (17)
    • dieciocho (18)
    • diecinueve (19)
  • Numbers 20-29 (One Word): Similar to 16-19, these are formed from "veinte" (twenty) and the single digit, also written as one word:
    • veinte (20)
    • veintiuno (21)
    • veintidós (22)
    • veintitrés (23)
    • ... up to veintinueve (29)
  • Numbers 30 and Above (Tens + y + Ones): For numbers 31 through 99, you use the tens digit, followed by y (and), then the ones digit. This is a crucial pattern.
    • treinta (30)
    • treinta y uno (31)
    • cuarenta y cinco (45)
    • cincuenta y dos (52)
    • sesenta y ocho (68)
    • setenta y tres (73)
    • ochenta y siete (87)
    • noventa y nueve (99)
  • Cien (100): The number one hundred is cien.

II. ¿Qué hora es? (What Time Is It?)

  • Asking the Time: Always use ¿Qué hora es?
  • Basic Rules for Responding:
    • Es la una: Use Es la only for 1:00 (since una is singular).
      • Es la una en punto. (It's 1:00 sharp.)
    • Son las [number]: Use Son las for all other hours (2:00 and up, as these are plural).
      • Son las dos. (It's 2:00.)
      • Son las diez. (It's 10:00.)
  • Fractions of the Hour:
    • y cuarto: "quarter past" (add 15 minutes)
      • Son las cinco y cuarto. (It's 5:15.)
    • y media: "half past" (add 30 minutes)
      • Es la una y media. (It's 1:30.)
    • menos cuarto: "quarter to" (subtract 15 minutes from the next hour)
      • Son las tres menos cuarto. (It's a quarter to three, i.e., 2:45.)
  • Morning, Afternoon, or Night: Add these phrases to clarify the time of day:
    • de la mañana: in the morning (roughly midnight to noon)
    • de la tarde: in the afternoon/early evening (roughly noon to sunset)
    • de la noche: at night (roughly sunset to midnight)
    • de la madrugada: in the early morning/predawn hours (often used for times like 2 AM - 5 AM)
    • Son las ocho de la mañana. (It's 8:00 AM.)
    • Son las seis de la tarde. (It's 6:00 PM.)
    • Es la una de la noche. (It's 1:00 AM.)
  • Useful Extra:
    • en punto: "on the dot" or "sharp"
      • La clase empieza a las tres en punto. (Class starts at 3:00 sharp.)
  • Example Conversation Breakdown:
    • Sofía asks: ¿Qué hora es? (What time is it?)
    • Marco replies: Son las dos y media. (It's 2:30.) - Uses Son las for 2:00 and y media for 30 minutes past.
    • Sofía asks: ¿A qué hora empieza la clase de español? (At what time does Spanish class start?) - Note the "A" before qué hora when asking when something happens.
    • Marco replies: A las tres de la tarde. (At 3:00 in the afternoon.) - Uses A las when stating the time an event occurs, and de la tarde for clarification.

III. Los Días de la Semana (The Days of the Week)

  • List of Days:
    • lunes (Monday)
    • martes (Tuesday)
    • miércoles (Wednesday)
    • jueves (Thursday)
    • viernes (Friday)
    • sábado (Saturday)
    • domingo (Sunday)
  • Important Cultural Notes:
    • Calendar Starts on Monday: In Spanish-speaking countries, the calendar week typically begins with Monday, not Sunday.
    • No Capitalization: Days of the week in Spanish are not capitalized unless they are at the beginning of a sentence.
  • Useful Expressions with Days:
    • el lunes: on Monday (singular, specific Monday)
    • los martes: on Tuesdays (plural, every Tuesday)
    • todos los días: every day
    • entre semana: during the week, weekdays
    • el fin de semana: the weekend

IV. Los Meses del Año (The Months of the Year)

  • List of Months:
    • enero (January)
    • febrero (February)
    • marzo (March)
    • abril (April)
    • mayo (May)
    • junio (June)
    • julio (July)
    • agosto (August)
    • septiembre (September)
    • octubre (October)
    • noviembre (November)
    • diciembre (December)
  • No Capitalization: Like days of the week, months in Spanish are not capitalized.
  • Expressing Dates: The standard format is day + de + month (+ de + year).
    • el primero de enero (January 1st) - Use el primero for the first day of the month.
    • el quince de octubre (October 15th)
    • el dos de marzo de dos mil veinticuatro (March 2nd, 2024)

V. Recursos Adicionales

  • «Un elefante se balanceaba» Counting Song: This traditional children's song is an excellent way to practice numbers sequentially from one upwards. Listening and counting along reinforces number recognition and pronunciation in a fun, cultural context.
  • Técnica: Clavijas numéricas (Number Pegs): This memory technique helps associate numbers with vivid, rhyming mental images. Creating a "peg" for each number (e.g., uno sounds like "moon," imagine the moon) and then linking these pegs in a story can be very effective for remembering sequences, like the months of the year in order. The sillier and more detailed the image, the better it works!

Review Questions

  1. Explain the key differences in forming numbers between 16-29 and 31-99 in Spanish, providing examples for each category.
  2. Imagine it's 1:30 PM, 7:15 AM, and 11:45 PM. How would you express each of these times in Spanish, including the time of day?
  3. What are two important cultural conventions regarding days and months in Spanish-speaking countries that differ from English? How do these conventions affect capitalization and calendar layout?
  4. If a class starts a las nueve y cuarto de la mañana and ends a la una menos cuarto de la tarde, what are the start and end times in English (using AM/PM)?
  5. Describe how the "Number Pegs" memory technique could be applied to learn the months of the year in order, providing an example for the first few months.
