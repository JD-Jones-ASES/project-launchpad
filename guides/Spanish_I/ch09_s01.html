El Verbo IR: Going Places

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental Spanish verb ir, meaning "to go," which is essential for discussing movement and destinations. You'll learn its irregular conjugations, a vital set of vocabulary words for common places, and the mandatory contractions al and del that combine prepositions with the masculine definite article. Mastering these elements will enable you to ask and answer questions about where people are going.

Key Concepts

  • The Verb "Ir" (To Go): Ir is a highly irregular verb used to express movement towards a destination. Its forms must be memorized.
  • Common Places: A core vocabulary list of places is provided to use with the verb ir.
  • Mandatory Contractions "al" and "del": In Spanish, the preposition a (to) followed by el (the masculine singular article) must contract to al. Similarly, de (from/of) followed by el must contract to del. These contractions are required and cannot be separated.
  • No Contraction with Feminine/Plural Articles: Contractions al and del only occur with el. When a or de precede la, los, or las, no contraction takes place.
  • Multiple Meanings of "¡Vamos!": The nosotros/as form, vamos, can mean "we go" or "we are going," but when used with an exclamation mark and lively tone, it often translates to "Let's go!"

Detailed Notes

The Verb "Ir" (To Go)

The verb ir is one of the most common and essential verbs in Spanish, used to talk about going to places. It is completely irregular, meaning its conjugations do not follow standard patterns, so they must be memorized.

Conjugation of Ir

Subject Pronoun Form of Ir English Translation
yo voy I go, I am going
tú vas you (informal) go, you are going
él/ella/Ud. va he/she/you (formal) goes/is going
nosotros/as vamos we go, we are going
vosotros/as vais you all (informal, Spain) go/are going
ellos/ellas/Uds. van they/you all (formal) go/are going

Examples:

  • Yo voy al parque. (I go to the park.)
  • ¿A dónde vas? (Where are you going?)
  • Ella va a la biblioteca. (She goes to the library.)
  • Nosotros vamos al cine. (We go to the movie theater.)

Notice that some places use la (feminine) and others use el (masculine). This distinction is crucial for applying the contraction rules.

Mandatory Contractions: "al" and "del"

Spanish has two essential contractions that occur automatically:

  1. a + el = al
    • This contraction happens whenever the preposition a (to) is followed by the masculine singular definite article el (the).
    • Example: You cannot say Voy a el parque. You must say Voy al parque. (I go to the park.)
  2. de + el = del
    • This contraction happens whenever the preposition de (from/of) is followed by the masculine singular definite article el (the).
    • Example: You cannot say Soy de el museo. You must say Soy del museo. (I am from the museum.)

Important Note: No Contraction with Other Articles These contractions only happen with el. When a or de are followed by feminine singular (la) or plural (los, las) articles, no contraction occurs. The words remain separate.

  • a la: Voy a la biblioteca. (I go to the library.)
  • a los: Vamos a los estadios. (We go to the stadiums.)
  • a las: Ellas van a las iglesias. (They go to the churches.)
  • de la: El libro es de la escuela. (The book is from the school.)
  • de los: Los estudiantes son de los Estados Unidos. (The students are from the United States.)
  • de las: La música es de las fiestas. (The music is from the parties.)

The Special Case of "¡Vamos!"

The nosotros/as form of ir is vamos.

  • When used in a statement, it means "we go" or "we are going."
    • Nosotros vamos al restaurante. (We are going to the restaurant.)
  • However, ¡Vamos! can also be an exclamation meaning "Let's go!" or "Come on!"
    • ¡Vamos! La película empieza pronto. (Let's go! The movie starts soon.)
    • Context, an exclamation mark, and the speaker's tone of voice usually indicate this meaning.

Practical Application: Asking and Answering Where You're Going

To ask "Where are you going?" you use ¿Adónde vas? The answer will use a form of ir + a (or al) + the place.

Example Conversation Structure: A: ¿Adónde vas después de la escuela? (Where are you going after school?) B: Voy al banco. Tengo que ir. (I'm going to the bank. I have to go.) A: ¡Qué lástima! Pensaba que íbamos al cine esta tarde. (What a shame! I thought we were going to the movie theater this afternoon.) B: Lo siento. Mañana vamos. (I'm sorry. We're going tomorrow.)

Practice replacing the destinations in conversations with different places from the vocabulary list to solidify your understanding and fluency.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the primary function of the verb "ir" and why it's considered irregular. How does its irregularity affect how you learn its forms?
  2. What are the two mandatory contractions introduced in this section, and when do they occur? Provide one example sentence for each contraction and one example where no contraction is needed.
  3. How does the phrase "¡Vamos!" demonstrate the importance of context in understanding Spanish? Give two different examples showing its dual meaning.
  4. Conjugate the verb "ir" for all six subject pronouns (yo, tú, él/ella/Ud., nosotros/as, vosotros/as, ellos/ellas/Uds.). Then, use three different forms in sentences where you state you are going to different places from the vocabulary list.
  5. Imagine you are planning your day. Using the verb "ir" and at least three different places from the vocabulary, describe where you are going at different times of the day (e.g., after school, this afternoon, on the weekend).
