Study Guide: Los mandatos negativos (Negative Commands)

Overview

This section introduces negative tú commands in Spanish, which are used to tell someone what not to do. It explains a simple formula for forming these commands using "no" followed by a specific verb form, differing from affirmative commands. The context provided is giving advice and warnings while traveling.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of Negative Commands: Negative commands are instructions that tell someone not to perform an action. In English, they typically start with "Don't." In Spanish, they use "no" combined with a special verb form.
  • Basic Formation Rule (Tú form): For negative tú commands, the formula is no + present subjunctive (tú form). While the full subjunctive mood is studied later, this section provides a simplified conjugation pattern for immediate use.
  • Conjugation Patterns for -ar, -er, -ir Verbs:
    • For -ar verbs: Drop the -ar ending and add -es. (e.g., hablar -> no hables)
    • For -er and -ir verbs: Drop the -er or -ir ending and add -as. (e.g., comer -> no comas, escribir -> no escribas)
  • Difference from Affirmative Commands: It's crucial to note that negative commands often look different from their affirmative counterparts (e.g., Ve vs. No vayas). This difference is due to the use of the subjunctive form in negative commands.
  • Context of Travel: The section illustrates negative commands with practical examples related to travel, such as warnings about passports, luggage, or interactions in public places.
  • Memorization for Now: While the subjunctive is a complex topic, for this chapter, the advice is to memorize the negative command forms as whole phrases, especially the common irregular ones.

Important Terms

  • Los mandatos negativos: Negative commands; instructions telling someone what not to do.
  • Present subjunctive (tú form): A specific verb mood used for expressing wishes, emotions, doubts, and, in this context, negative commands. For now, it's presented as a specific set of endings to be applied.
  • Tú form: The informal singular "you" form in Spanish, used when addressing a friend, family member, or someone you know well.
  • El aeropuerto: The airport. (Example travel vocabulary)
  • El avión: The airplane. (Example travel vocabulary)
  • El pasaporte: The passport. (Example travel vocabulary)

Detailed Notes

What Are Negative Commands?

Negative commands are essential for giving warnings, advice, or telling someone to refrain from an action. Imagine you're traveling, and someone wants to tell you: "Don't touch that!" or "Don't forget your bags!" These are negative commands. In English, we use "Don't" at the beginning of the sentence. In Spanish, we use "no" followed by a specific verb form.

Let's look at some examples from the textbook:

  • No hables con extraños en el aeropuerto. (Don't talk to strangers in the airport.)
  • No comas en el avión si tienes alergia. (Don't eat on the plane if you have an allergy.)
  • No escribas tu número de teléfono en el pasaporte. (Don't write your phone number in the passport.)
  • No salgas sin tu maleta. (Don't leave without your suitcase.)

Notice how all these sentences start with "No" and then use a verb form that might look a little different from what you're used to for the tú form.

Forming Negative Tú Commands

The basic formula for forming a negative tú command is straightforward:

no + present subjunctive (tú form)

For this chapter, you don't need to understand all the complexities of the subjunctive mood. Just focus on these simple rules for changing the verb endings:

  1. For -AR verbs:

    • Drop the -ar ending.
    • Add -es.
    • Example:
      • hablar (to speak)
      • Stem: habl-
      • Negative command: No hables (Don't speak)

  2. For -ER and -IR verbs:

    • Drop the -er or -ir ending.
    • Add -as.
    • Examples:
      • comer (to eat)
      • Stem: com-
      • Negative command: No comas (Don't eat)
      • escribir (to write)
      • Stem: escrib-
      • Negative command: No escribas (Don't write)

Important Distinction: Affirmative vs. Negative Commands

It's critical to understand that the negative command form is often different from the affirmative (positive) command form for the same verb. This is because negative commands utilize the subjunctive mood, which you'll explore in depth in a later chapter. For now, simply be aware of these differences and commit them to memory as phrases.

Here are some common examples from the text that highlight this difference:

Affirmative Tú Command Meaning Negative Tú Command Meaning
Ve al mercado. Go to the market. No vayas al mercado. Don't go to the market.
Sé amable. Be nice. No seas antipático. Don't be unfriendly.
Ten cuidado. Be careful. No tengas miedo. Don't be afraid.

Notice how Ve, Sé, and Ten (which are irregular affirmative commands) change significantly to No vayas, No seas, and No tengas for their negative forms. This reinforces the idea that you shouldn't just put "no" in front of an affirmative command. You need to use the specific negative command form.

More Travel-Related Negative Commands

Here are additional examples that you might encounter while traveling, demonstrating the application of these rules:

  • No pierdas el pasaporte. (Don't lose your passport.)
    • (perder is an -er verb, so it becomes pierdas)
  • No llegues tarde al aeropuerto. (Don't arrive late to the airport.)
    • (llegar is an -ar verb, so it becomes llegues. Note: llegar is a g to gu stem change verb in the subjunctive, which results in llegues instead of lleges to maintain the hard 'g' sound.)
  • No pongas líquidos en tu maleta de mano. (Don't put liquids in your carry-on bag.)
    • (poner is irregular, similar to tener and salir, but follows the subjunctive pattern.)
  • No digas tu número de habitación en voz alta. (Don't say your room number out loud.)
    • (decir is irregular, similar to tener and salir, but follows the subjunctive pattern.)

Travel Word Connections

The textbook provides a helpful memory tip: many Spanish travel words share roots with English words (e.g., avión for aviation, pasaporte for passport, aeropuerto for airport). Making these connections can significantly aid vocabulary acquisition. Always look for cognates or similar-sounding words to build your vocabulary more efficiently.

  3. Why do many negative commands look different from their affirmative counterparts (e.g., Ve vs. No vayas)? What grammatical concept is hinted at here?
