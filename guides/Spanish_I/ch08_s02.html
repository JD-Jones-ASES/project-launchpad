Study Guide: Expresiones con Tener

Overview

This section introduces a crucial difference between English and Spanish: how certain feelings and physical states are expressed. While English often uses the verb "to be" (e.g., "I am hungry"), Spanish predominantly uses the verb tener (to have) followed by a noun (e.g., "I have hunger"). Mastering these tener expressions is key to sounding more natural in Spanish.

Key Concepts

  • Tener + Noun for Feelings/States: In Spanish, many physical sensations and emotional states are expressed by combining the verb tener (to have) with a noun, rather than estar (to be) with an adjective, as is common in English. This is often conceptualized as "having" a sensation.
  • Conjugation of Tener: The verb tener must be conjugated to agree with the subject of the sentence (e.g., yo tengo, ella tiene). However, the noun that follows tener in these expressions remains in its singular form and does not change.
  • Expressing Age: When stating someone's age in Spanish, tener is always used, and the word años (years) must always be included after the number. For example, "I am twelve years old" is Tengo doce años.
  • Intensifying Expressions with Mucho/Mucha: To say "very" with tener expressions, you must use mucho or mucha, not muy. Mucho is used before masculine nouns, and mucha before feminine nouns, agreeing in gender with the noun in the expression.
  • "The Body Method" Memory Aid: A technique for remembering tener expressions by associating each feeling or state with a specific body part, making the connection more physical and memorable.

Important Terms

  • tener: To have. The core verb used in these expressions.
  • hambre: Hunger. Used in tener hambre (to be hungry / to have hunger).
  • sueño: Sleepiness, sleep. Used in tener sueño (to be sleepy / to have sleepiness).
  • frío: Cold. Used in tener frío (to be cold / to have cold).
  • calor: Heat. Used in tener calor (to be hot / to have heat).
  • sed: Thirst. Used in tener sed (to be thirsty / to have thirst).
  • miedo: Fear. Used in tener miedo (to be afraid / to have fear).
  • razón: Reason, right. Used in tener razón (to be right / to have reason).
  • años: Years. Always included when expressing age with tener.
  • mucho/a: Much, a lot of, very. Used to intensify tener expressions, agreeing in gender with the noun.
  • muy: Very. Not used to intensify tener expressions; instead, it modifies adjectives or adverbs.

Detailed Notes

The Core Concept: Tener vs. "To Be"

One of the biggest differences between Spanish and English is how certain physical sensations and emotional states are described. In English, we often use "to be" + adjective (e.g., "I am hungry," "He is cold"). In Spanish, for these specific expressions, you must use tener (to have) + noun. Think of it as "having" the sensation or state, like "I have hunger," "He has cold."

Conjugating Tener

Remember that tener is an irregular verb, and it must be conjugated to match the subject of the sentence. The noun in the expression, however, remains unchanged.

Subject Tener Conjugation Example Meaning
yo (I) tengo Yo tengo hambre. I am hungry.
tú (you, informal) tienes Tú tienes frío. You are cold.
él/ella/usted (he/she/you, formal) tiene Ella tiene sueño. She is sleepy.
nosotros/as (we) tenemos Nosotros tenemos sed. We are thirsty.
vosotros/as (you all, informal) tenéis Vosotros tenéis calor. You all are hot.
ellos/as/ustedes (they/you all, formal) tienen Ellos tienen miedo. They are afraid.

Common Tener Expressions

Here are some of the most common feelings and states expressed with tener:

  • tener hambre (to be hungry)
  • tener sed (to be thirsty)
  • tener frío (to be cold)
  • tener calor (to be hot)
  • tener sueño (to be sleepy)
  • tener miedo (to be afraid)
  • tener razón (to be right)
  • no tener razón (to be wrong / not to be right)
  • tener prisa (to be in a hurry)
  • tener ganas de (+ infinitive) (to feel like doing something)
  • tener éxito (to be successful)
  • tener vergüenza (to be ashamed/embarrassed)
  • tener suerte (to be lucky)

Expressing Age

When you talk about someone's age, you always use tener followed by the number and the word años (years).

  • Tengo doce años. (I am twelve years old.) NOT Tengo doce.
  • Mi hermana tiene veinte años. (My sister is twenty years old.)

Intensifying Tener Expressions: Mucho/Mucha vs. Muy

This is a common point of confusion for English speakers. To say "very" with tener expressions, you do not use muy. Instead, you use mucho or mucha, which act like "a lot of" or "much." These words must agree in gender with the noun in the expression.

  • Tengo mucha hambre. (I am very hungry.) — hambre is a feminine noun, so mucha is used.
  • Ella tiene mucho sueño. (She is very sleepy.) — sueño is a masculine noun, so mucho is used.
  • Tenemos mucho frío. (We are very cold.) — frío is a masculine noun, so mucho is used.

Remember, muy is used to modify adjectives or adverbs (e.g., muy bonito - very pretty, muy bien - very well). Since hambre, sueño, frío, etc., are nouns in these expressions, mucho/a is the correct intensifier.

Memory Tip: El Método del Cuerpo (The Body Method)

This technique leverages your knowledge of body parts to help you memorize tener expressions.

  1. Associate: Assign each tener expression to a specific body part where that feeling might "live." For example, you might associate tener hambre with your stomach, tener frío with your whole body shivering, tener sueño with your head or eyes.
  2. Visualize and Scan: Close your eyes and slowly "scan" your body from head to toe. As you mentally (or physically) touch each assigned body part, say the corresponding tener expression out loud. This physical and mental connection can significantly aid long-term memory.

Tener in Action: Conversation Analysis

Let's look at the example conversation between Ana and Marcos:

Ana: ¡Hola, Marcos! ¿Cómo estás hoy? ¡Tengo mucha hambre y mucho sueño! Marcos: ¡Hola, Ana! Yo también. Tengo mucho frío. Es lunes, ¿verdad? ¡Uf! ¡Vámonos!

  1. What two tener expressions does Ana use?
    • Ana uses tener hambre (specifically mucha hambre - very hungry) and tener sueño (specifically mucho sueño - very sleepy).
  2. What day of the week is it? How do you know?
