Study Guide: El Verbo Gustar — "To Like" (Reversed!)

Overview

This section introduces the Spanish verb gustar, which means "to like" but translates more literally as "to be pleasing to." Unlike English, where the person who likes something is the subject, in Spanish, the thing or activity that is pleasing is the subject, and the person is the indirect object. This unique "reversed" structure is key to understanding and correctly using gustar.

Key Concepts

  • Reversed Structure: In Spanish, gustar literally means "to be pleasing to." Instead of "I like music," the structure is "Music pleases me." The thing or action that is liked is the grammatical subject of gustar, not the person doing the liking.
  • Indirect Object Pronouns: These pronouns (me, te, le, nos, les) indicate who is pleased by something. They always precede the verb gustar.
  • Conjugation of Gustar: The verb gustar primarily uses two forms:
    • gusta (singular): Used when the thing being liked is singular (e.g., el libro) or when an action (expressed as an infinitive) is being liked (e.g., leer).
    • gustan (plural): Used when the things being liked are plural (e.g., los deportes).
  • Subject-Verb Agreement: The form of gustar (gusta or gustan) must agree with the subject of the sentence, which is the thing or action that is pleasing, and not with the indirect object pronoun.
  • Clarifying le and les: Since le and les can refer to several different people, an a + [person/name/pronoun] phrase can be added at the beginning of the sentence for clarity or emphasis (e.g., A ella le gusta).
  • Forming Negatives: To make a gustar sentence negative, simply place no before the indirect object pronoun (e.g., No me gusta).
  • Cultural Context: Understanding verbs like gustar helps connect to cultural expressions, such as traditional songs and games that highlight community and shared enjoyment.

Important Terms

  • Gustar: To like; literally, "to be pleasing to."
  • Pronombre indirecto: (Indirect object pronoun) The pronoun that indicates to whom something is pleasing (e.g., me, te, le, nos, les).
  • Sujeto: (Subject) The noun or infinitive after gustar that "does the pleasing" and determines if gusta or gustan is used.
  • Infinitive: The basic, unconjugated form of a verb (e.g., leer - to read, bailar - to dance). When an action is liked, the infinitive is used as the subject of gustar.
  • Víbora: (Snake) Referenced in the traditional children's game song "A la víbora de la mar."
  • La mar: (The sea) An older or poetic term for el mar (the sea), also featured in the song.
  • Manzana: (Apple) One of the choices players make in the "A la víbora de la mar" game.
  • Limón: (Lemon) The other choice players make in the "A la víbora de la mar" game.
  • Los fines de semana: (Weekends) Common phrase used in conversational practice.
  • ¡Qué divertido!: (How fun!) An exclamation indicating enjoyment.
  • Prefiero: (I prefer) A verb for expressing preference, often used in conjunction with discussing likes.

Detailed Notes

The "Reversed" Nature of Gustar

The verb gustar is often confusing for English speakers because its structure is "reversed" compared to how we express "to like."

  • English: Subject (person) + verb ("like") + object (thing/action)
    • I like soccer.
  • Spanish: Indirect Object Pronoun (person) + gustar (pleases) + Subject (thing/action)
    • Me gusta el fútbol. (Literally: Soccer pleases me.)

The key is to remember that the thing or action is the grammatical subject, and the person is the indirect object.

The Basic Structure of Gustar Sentences

The general formula for gustar sentences is: [Indirect Object Pronoun] + gusta/gustan + [Subject (noun or infinitive)]

Indirect Object Pronouns

These small pronouns tell us who is doing the liking (or, more accurately, to whom something is pleasing). They always come before the form of gustar.

Pronoun English Equivalent Example
me to me Me gusta (It pleases me / I like)
te to you (informal singular) Te gusta (It pleases you / You like)
le to him/her/you (formal singular) Le gusta (It pleases him/her/you / He/She/You like)
nos to us Nos gusta (It pleases us / We like)
les to them/you (plural) Les gusta (It pleases them/you / They/You like)

Using gusta (Singular Form)

Use the singular form gusta when the subject (the thing/action that is pleasing) is:

  1. A single noun:
    • Me gusta el libro. (I like the book. - el libro is singular)
    • Te gusta la música. (You like music. - la música is singular)
  2. An infinitive (an action): When you like to do something, the infinitive is used as the subject, and it's always considered singular.
    • Nos gusta leer. (We like to read. - leer is a single action)
    • Le gusta bailar. (He/She/You like to dance. - bailar is a single action)

Using gustan (Plural Form)

Use the plural form gustan when the subject (the things that are pleasing) is:

  1. Multiple nouns (plural):
    • Me gustan los deportes. (I like sports. - los deportes is plural)
    • Les gustan las canciones de ese artista. (They like that artist's songs. - las canciones is plural)

Important Note: Do not be fooled by the indirect object pronouns (me, te, le, nos, les). They tell you who is pleased, but the noun after the verb is the true subject. It's the subject that dictates whether you use gusta or gustan.

Clarifying le and les

Since le can mean "to him," "to her," or "to you (formal singular)," and les can mean "to them" or "to you (plural)," Spanish often uses a clarifying phrase at the beginning of the sentence: a + [person/name/pronoun]. This adds clarity and can also be used for emphasis.

  • A él le gusta el fútbol. (He likes soccer.)
  • A ella le gusta la música. (She likes music.)
  • A usted le gusta leer. (You [formal] like to read.)
  • A Juan le gustan los libros. (Juan likes books.)
  • A mis amigos les gusta la fiesta. (My friends like the party.)
  • A nosotros nos gusta la pizza. (We like pizza. - This is for emphasis, as 'nos' is already clear.)

Making Gustar Sentences Negative

To express that someone doesn't like something, simply place no before the indirect object pronoun.

  • No me gusta el café. (I don't like coffee.)
  • No les gustan las verduras. (They don't like vegetables.)
  • No nos gusta trabajar los fines de semana. (We don't like to work on weekends.)

Cultural Insight: "A la víbora de la mar"

This section provides a cultural context for how enjoyment and preferences are expressed in Latin American communities.

  • Traditional Game Song: "A la víbora de la mar" is a well-known Mexican children's game song, played at celebrations.
  • Game Description: Two children form an arch, and others pass underneath, like a snake (la víbora) through water (la mar). When trapped, a player chooses a side (manzana or limón) for a tug-of-war.
  • Themes: The game embodies suspense, laughter, and community – things that "les gustan a todos" (everyone likes). This connects back to the idea of shared enjoyment using the verb gustar.
  • Cultural Significance: Such games highlight the importance of music, movement, and group participation in community celebrations in Latin America. It's a vivid example of how culture uses shared experiences to build bonds and create joy.
