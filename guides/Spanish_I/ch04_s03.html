Study Guide: Describing People and Things (Describir Personas y Cosas)

Overview

This section focuses on expanding your Spanish vocabulary to describe people and objects in detail, building upon your understanding of adjective agreement. It introduces a new set of descriptive adjectives and reinforces the rules for making them match the nouns they modify in both gender and number. Additionally, it provides an important cultural note on the usage of certain descriptive terms like "gordo/a" and "viejo/a."

Key Concepts

  • Adjective Agreement: The fundamental rule in Spanish stating that adjectives must always match the gender (masculine/feminine) and number (singular/plural) of the noun they describe. This means adjusting the adjective's ending (e.g., -o/-a, adding -s/-es).
  • Expanded Descriptive Vocabulary: Learning new adjectives to express a wider range of characteristics, including appearance (pretty, ugly, thin, fat), personality (fun, boring, friendly, unfriendly), and state (new, old).
  • Connecting Multiple Adjectives: When two or more adjectives are used to describe the same noun and are connected by "y" (and), all adjectives must agree with the noun in gender and number.
  • Cultural Nuance of "Gordo/a" and "Viejo/a": Understanding that words like "gordo/a" (fat/chubby) and "viejo/a" (old) are often considered neutral descriptive terms in Spanish, particularly in everyday language, stories, and when describing non-human subjects. While generally benign, context and tone are still important when using them for people.
  • Using "Ser" for Descriptions: Adjectives of description are typically used with the verb "ser" (to be) to describe inherent or permanent qualities of people and things.

Detailed Notes

Mastering Descriptive Adjectives

This section introduces you to a crucial set of adjectives that will greatly enhance your ability to describe the world around you. Remember that these adjectives will always work in conjunction with the verb ser (to be) when describing inherent qualities.

New Descriptive Adjectives

Here's a breakdown of the new vocabulary, often presented in pairs of opposites, to help you remember them:

  • Appearance:
    • bonito/a (pretty, nice-looking) vs. feo/a (ugly)
    • gordo/a (fat, chubby) vs. delgado/a (thin, slender)
  • Personality/Temperament:
    • divertido/a (fun, entertaining) vs. aburrido/a (boring)
    • simpático/a (friendly, nice) vs. antipático/a (unfriendly, unpleasant)
  • Condition/Age:
    • nuevo/a (new) vs. viejo/a (old)

Recalling Previously Learned Adjectives

Don't forget the adjectives you've already learned! These are equally important for description:

  • grande (big)
  • pequeño/a (small)
  • alto/a (tall)
  • bajo/a (short)
  • inteligente (intelligent)

Remember that adjectives like grande and inteligente are often invariable for gender (they don't change from -o to -a), but they still need to agree in number (add -s for plural). For example: un hombre inteligente, unas mujeres inteligentes.

Adjective Agreement: The Golden Rule

The most critical rule when using adjectives in Spanish is agreement. An adjective must always match the noun it describes in:

  1. Gender: If the noun is masculine, the adjective is masculine. If the noun is feminine, the adjective is feminine.
    • El chico alto. (The tall boy.)
    • La chica alta. (The tall girl.)
  2. Number: If the noun is singular, the adjective is singular. If the noun is plural, the adjective is plural.
    • El perro bonito. (The pretty dog.)
    • Los perros bonitos. (The pretty dogs.)
    • La casa vieja. (The old house.)
    • Las casas viejas. (The old houses.)

Combining Multiple Adjectives: When you want to use more than one adjective to describe a single noun, and you connect them with y (and), every single adjective must agree with the noun.

  • Mi padre es alto y delgado. (My father is tall and thin.)
  • Mis tías son simpáticas y divertidas. (My aunts are friendly and fun.)
  • Los libros son grandes y aburridos. (The books are big and boring.)

Cultural Note: Using "Gordo/a" and "Viejo/a"

It's important to understand the cultural context of certain descriptive adjectives in Spanish. Unlike in English, where "fat" or "old" can sometimes carry negative connotations or be used as insults, gordo/a (fat/chubby) and viejo/a (old) are often considered neutral descriptive words in Spanish.

  • They appear frequently in everyday conversation, children's songs, and stories without any negative intent.
  • For example, describing a cartoon character, a pet, or even a cherished elderly relative (like your great-grandmother) as "gordo" or "vieja" is perfectly normal and acceptable.
  • However, just as in English, context and tone still matter. When describing real people, always use these words carefully and respectfully. If you are unsure, it's often best to use softer alternatives or focus on other positive attributes.

Applying Adjectives in Context: "La familia Rodríguez"

The reading "La familia Rodríguez" serves as an excellent example of how to use these new adjectives naturally within descriptions. Notice how:

  • Lucía is described as alta y delgada (tall and thin) and muy inteligente (very intelligent).
  • Roberto is bajo (short) but muy divertido (very fun).
  • Miguel is alto (tall) and muy serio (very serious). His books are grandes y aburridos (big and boring).
  • Isabella is pequeña (small) and muy simpática (very friendly). She makes cosas bonitas (pretty things).
  • Carlos (the baby) is gordo y muy divertido (fat/chubby and very fun).
  • Their casa is vieja pero bonita (old but pretty), and the jardín (garden) is pequeño pero verde (small but green).

Pay attention to how the adjectives change endings based on the gender and number of the noun they describe (e.g., madre alta, hijos altos, casa vieja, cosas bonitas).

Practice: ¡Personaliza!

The "Personalize It!" activity encourages you to apply what you've learned to your own life. When describing people or pets, remember to:

  1. Use the verb ser.
  2. Choose appropriate descriptive adjectives.
  3. Ensure your adjectives agree in gender and number with the noun you are describing.
  4. If adding a description of something owned, like a backpack or shirt, remember to make the color adjective agree as well!

Example: Mi abuela es baja y simpática. (My grandmother is short and friendly.) Su mochila es roja. (Her backpack is red.)
