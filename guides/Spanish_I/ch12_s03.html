Capítulo 12, Sección 3: ¿Qué te gusta hacer? - Expressing Likes with Infinitives

Overview

This section expands on the use of gustar and similar verbs, showing how to combine them with infinitives to express what you like to do. It also introduces new action verbs, focusing on the e to i stem-changing pattern seen in verbs like pedir and servir, and clarifies the difference between preguntar and pedir. The section concludes with a reading passage about Sofía's hobbies, applying the new grammar and vocabulary.

Key Concepts

  • Gustar with Infinitives: To express liking an action, place an infinitive (the unconjugated verb, e.g., hablar, comer, vivir) directly after gustar. In this construction, always use the singular form gusta (or encanta, interesa, etc.) because the action itself is the "subject" that is pleasing.
  • Versatility of Gustar-Family Verbs: The gustar construction can be combined with any infinitive you know, allowing you to form complex ideas easily, such as Me gusta bailar (I like to dance) or Nos interesa aprender (We are interested in learning).
  • e → i Stem-Changing Verbs: Some verbs, like pedir (to ask for) and servir (to serve), undergo a stem change where the e in their stem changes to an i when conjugated. This change occurs in all forms except the nosotros form.
  • Distinguishing Preguntar and Pedir: Both verbs relate to "asking," but preguntar is used when asking a question to get information, while pedir is used when asking for something to be received (e.g., an object, a favor, or to order food).
  • Everyday Action Vocabulary: Introduction of new verbs to describe common daily activities, enhancing conversational ability.

Important Terms

  • Infinitive: The basic, unconjugated form of a verb in Spanish (e.g., hablar, comer, vivir), equivalent to "to [verb]" in English.
  • Gustar (verb): "To be pleasing to"; used to express likes and dislikes.
  • Encantar (verb): "To enchant," "to love" (as in really like something); a gustar-family verb.
  • Interesar (verb): "To interest"; a gustar-family verb.
  • Pedir (verb): To ask for (something to be received); an e → i stem-changing verb.
  • Servir (verb): To serve; an e → i stem-changing verb.
  • Preguntar (verb): To ask (a question, to get information).
  • Especialmente (adverb): Especially.
  • Tocar la guitarra (verb phrase): To play the guitar.
  • Sacar fotos (verb phrase): To take photos.
  • El barrio (noun): Neighborhood.
  • En cambio (phrase): On the other hand.
  • Reunirnos (verb, reflexive): To get together, to meet up (from reunir).
  • La cumbia (noun): A traditional Colombian dance and music style.

Detailed Notes

1. Gustar and Infinitives: Expressing What You Like to Do

You've learned that gustar (and verbs like encantar, interesar, doler, etc.) works differently from English "to like." Instead of saying "I like X," you say "X is pleasing to me."

  • Me gusta la música. (Music is pleasing to me / I like music.)

This section introduces a powerful new application: using gustar with an infinitive to express what you like to do.

  • The Rule: When the "subject" of your liking is an action (an infinitive), you always use the singular form of gustar, which is gusta.

    • Me gusta escuchar música. (To listen to music is pleasing to me / I like to listen to music.)
    • ¿Te gusta bailar? (Is to dance pleasing to you? / Do you like to dance?)
    • A nosotros nos encanta viajar. (To travel is enchanting to us / We love to travel.)
    • A ella no le interesa estudiar. (To study doesn't interest her / She isn't interested in studying.)

  • Why gusta? Even if you like many actions, each action is considered a singular concept. For example, escuchar música is one action, and that action is pleasing.

  • Flexibility: This construction is incredibly versatile. You can combine gustar-family verbs with any infinitive you know to express a wide range of activities and preferences.

2. New Everyday Action Verbs (Verbos Nuevos)

The textbook introduces eight new useful verbs for everyday actions. While six follow regular conjugation patterns, two are special stem-changing verbs that require careful attention: pedir and servir.

Stem-Changing Verbs: e → i

This is a new type of stem change. Unlike e → ie (like querer) that you might have learned before, these verbs change e to i in their stem.

  • Pedir (to ask for, to order)

    • Stem Change: e changes to i in all forms except nosotros.
    • Conjugation of pedir:
      • Yo: pido
      • Tú: pides
      • Él/Ella/Ud.: pide
      • Nosotros/Nosotras: pedimos (no stem change)
      • Ellos/Ellas/Uds.: piden
    • Example: Yo pido una pizza. (I order a pizza.) Ella pide ayuda. (She asks for help.)

  • Servir (to serve)

    • Stem Change: Follows the same e → i pattern as pedir.
    • Conjugation of servir:
      • Yo: sirvo
      • Tú: sirves
      • Él/Ella/Ud.: sirve
      • Nosotros/Nosotras: servimos (no stem change)
      • Ellos/Ellas/Uds.: sirven
    • Example: Él sirve la cena. (He serves dinner.) ¿Sirves café? (Do you serve coffee?)

3. Preguntar vs. Pedir: A Key Distinction

While both preguntar and pedir can translate to "to ask" in some contexts, they are not interchangeable. Understanding their specific uses is crucial:

  • Preguntar: Use preguntar when you are asking a question to get information.

    • Quiero preguntar algo. (I want to ask something.)
    • El profesor siempre nos pregunta: "¿Saben de dónde viene la cumbia?" (The professor always asks us: "Do you know where cumbia comes from?")

  • Pedir: Use pedir when you are asking for something to be received. This could be an object, a favor, or ordering food/drink.

    • Yo pido un refresco. (I ask for / order a soda.)
    • Ella pide ayuda a su hermano. (She asks her brother for help.)
    • Pedimos el postre. (We order dessert.)

Think of it this way: preguntar is for questions you want answered; pedir is for things you want received.

4. Reading: "Los pasatiempos de Sofía"

This passage introduces Sofía, a 12-year-old student from Bogotá, Colombia, and her favorite hobbies. It provides excellent examples of the gustar + infinitive construction in a natural context.

  • Sofía's Hobbies:
    • She loves listening to music (Me encanta escuchar música) — especially salsa and reggaetón.
    • She is very interested in playing the guitar (Me interesa mucho tocar la guitarra). She takes classes and feels happy after practice.
    • Her other favorite hobby is photography (Me gusta sacar fotos)—she takes pictures of her neighborhood, markets, and people.
  • Family Interactions:
    • Her mom loves looking at her photos (A mi mamá le encanta mirar mis fotos).
    • Her younger brother, however, isn't interested in photography (no le interesa nada la fotografía); he only likes video games (a él le gustan solamente los videojuegos).
    • On Sundays, her family loves to get together (nos gusta mucho reunirnos) to listen to music, eat, and dance.
    • Her dad loves Colombian music history and often asks them about the origin of cumbia (Él siempre nos pregunta: "¿Saben de dónde viene la cumbia?").

New Vocabulary from the Reading:

  • especialmente = especially
  • tocar la guitarra = to play the guitar
  • sacar fotos = to take photos
  • el barrio = neighborhood
  • en cambio = on the other hand
  • reunirnos = to get together
  • la cumbia = a traditional Colombian dance and music style

This reading demonstrates how to express personal preferences, hobbies, and family activities using the gustar + infinitive structure and other previously learned grammar.
