Mastering Irregular Verbs: The "-go" Pattern and Weather Expressions

Overview

This section introduces four crucial Spanish verbs—hacer, decir, poner, and salir—that share a unique irregularity: their "yo" (I) form ends in "-go," while all other conjugations follow regular patterns. It also covers the versatile uses of these verbs, particularly hacer for expressing weather conditions, and introduces additional high-frequency regular -ar verbs.

Key Concepts

  • The "-go" Irregularity: Four important verbs (hacer, decir, poner, salir) are irregular only in their yo form, which consistently ends in "-go." All other forms conjugate regularly for -er or -ir verbs.
  • Stem Change in Decir: While decir follows the "-go" pattern in the yo form (digo), it also exhibits an e to i stem change in all other forms except nosotros.
  • Multiple Uses of Hacer: This verb is highly versatile, used for "to do," "to make," asking questions, and describing weather.
  • Hacer for Weather Expressions: In Spanish, hacer (specifically the third person singular form, hace) is used for most weather expressions (e.g., hace frío - "it's cold"), unlike English which uses "to be."
  • Memorization Strategy: The Story Method: A personalized, vivid story incorporating the yo forms (hago, digo, pongo, salgo) can be an effective way to remember these irregular conjugations.
  • Additional Regular -AR Verbs: A set of common -ar verbs (pasar, llevar, esperar, entrar, buscar) are introduced, reinforcing regular -ar conjugation patterns and highlighting their diverse meanings.

Important Terms

  • hacer: (to do, to make) - A versatile verb used for actions, creation, and weather.
  • decir: (to say, to tell) - Used for communicating speech or information.
  • poner: (to put, to place, to set) - Implies placing an object somewhere or setting something up.
  • salir: (to leave, to go out) - Describes departing from a place or engaging in social activities outside.
  • -go pattern: The unique characteristic of hacer, decir, poner, and salir where their yo (I) form ends with -go.
  • Stem change (e → i): A grammatical phenomenon where the vowel in the stem of a verb changes during conjugation; specifically seen in decir (e.g., dice from decir).
  • pasar: (to pass, to happen) - A regular -ar verb with two common meanings.
  • llevar: (to carry, to wear) - A regular -ar verb meaning to transport something or to have clothing on.
  • esperar: (to wait, to hope) - A regular -ar verb expressing anticipation or desire.
  • entrar: (to enter) - A regular -ar verb meaning to go inside.
  • buscar: (to look for, to search) - A regular -ar verb meaning to seek out.

Detailed Notes

Irregular Verbs: The "-go" Pattern

Spanish has several irregular verbs, and this section introduces a common and important group: hacer (to do, to make), decir (to say, to tell), poner (to put, to place, to set), and salir (to leave, to go out). The key to remembering these verbs is a special pattern they share:

  • The yo form is irregular and ends in -go:
    • hacer → hago
    • decir → digo
    • poner → pongo
    • salir → salgo
  • All other forms are regular: Once you know the yo form, the rest of the conjugations for these verbs follow the standard patterns for -er or -ir verbs.

Let's look at each verb in detail:

1. Hacer (to do, to make)

Subject Conjugation
yo hago
tú haces
él/ella/Ud. hace
nosotros/as hacemos
vosotros/as hacéis
ellos/ellas/Uds. hacen

Hacer is a very versatile verb with many uses:

  • Doing homework: hacer la tarea
  • Making food: hacer la cena (to make dinner)
  • Asking questions: hacer preguntas (to ask questions)
  • Describing weather: hace frío (it's cold), hace calor (it's hot)

2. Decir (to say, to tell)

Subject Conjugation
yo digo
tú dices
él/ella/Ud. dice
nosotros/as decimos
vosotros/as decís
ellos/ellas/Uds. dicen

Decir is unique because it combines the "-go" irregularity with a stem change:

  • yo form: digo (ends in -go, but does not have the stem change).
  • Other forms (except nosotros): The e in the stem changes to i (e.g., dices, dice, dicen). The nosotros form (decimos) does not have the stem change. Common phrases:
  • ¿Qué dices? (What do you say?)
  • Dice que sí (He/she says yes)

3. Poner (to put, to place, to set)

Subject Conjugation
yo pongo
tú pones
él/ella/Ud. pone
nosotros/as ponemos
vosotros/as ponéis
ellos/ellas/Uds. ponen

Poner is irregular only in the yo form (pongo). All other forms are completely regular for an -er verb. Common phrases:

  • poner la mesa (to set the table)
  • poner música (to put on music)
  • poner atención (to pay attention)

4. Salir (to leave, to go out)

Subject Conjugation
yo salgo
tú sales
él/ella/Ud. sale
nosotros/as salimos
vosotros/as salís
ellos/ellas/Uds. salen

Like poner, salir is irregular only in the yo form (salgo). All other forms are regular for an -ir verb. Common phrases:

  • salir de casa (to leave the house)
  • salir con amigos (to go out with friends)
  • ¿A qué hora sales? (What time do you leave?)

Memory Tip: The Story Method

To help remember the irregular yo forms (hago, digo, pongo, salgo), try creating a silly story that incorporates them. For example: "Every morning I MAKE (hago) a pizza and SAY (digo) it's delicious. Then I PUT (pongo) it on the table and LEAVE (salgo) before anyone can eat it!" The more outlandish and personal your story, the better it will stick in your memory. Try acting it out for an even stronger recall!

Special Use of Hacer for Weather

One of the most important uses of hacer is for describing weather conditions. This is often confusing for English speakers because we use the verb "to be" ("It is cold"). In Spanish, you use hace (the third-person singular form of hacer):

  • Hace frío. (It's cold. – Literally: "It makes cold.")
  • Hace calor. (It's hot. – Literally: "It makes hot.")
  • Hace sol. (It's sunny. – Literally: "It makes sun.")
  • Hace viento. (It's windy. – Literally: "It makes wind.")

Important Note: Always use hace for these expressions. Never use es or está with these types of weather phrases. However, estar is used for está nublado (it is cloudy). If you're unsure, always check your vocabulary list!

More Regular -AR Verbs

This section also introduces five high-frequency regular -ar verbs that follow the standard conjugation patterns:

  • entrar (to enter)
  • buscar (to look for, to search)
  • pasar (to pass, to happen)
    • pasar can mean "to pass" (physically) or "to happen" (e.g., ¿Qué pasa? - "What's happening?").
  • llevar (to carry, to wear)
    • llevar can mean "to carry" something or "to wear" clothing.
  • esperar (to wait, to hope)
    • esperar can mean "to wait" or "to hope." Think of the related Spanish word esperanza (hope).

These verbs are essential for everyday conversation and reinforce your understanding of regular -ar verb conjugations.
