Study Guide: Capítulo 16, Sección 2 - Los Verbos Reflexivos

Overview

This section introduces reflexive verbs (verbos reflexivos) in Spanish, which describe actions the subject performs on themselves. You will learn about reflexive pronouns, how to conjugate these verbs, and special rules for using them, particularly when discussing daily routines and body parts.

Key Concepts

  • Reflexive Verbs (Verbos Reflexivos): These verbs indicate that the subject of the sentence is both performing the action and receiving the action. The action "reflects" back on the subject.
  • Reflexive Pronouns: Essential components of reflexive verbs, these pronouns (me, te, se, nos, os, se) always precede the conjugated verb and must match the subject in person and number.
  • Conjugation of Reflexive Verbs: When conjugating, remove the "-se" from the infinitive, place the correct reflexive pronoun before the verb, and conjugate the verb normally according to its tense and subject.
  • Stem-Changing Reflexive Verbs: Some reflexive verbs follow stem-changing patterns (e.g., e>ie, o>ue, e>i) in all forms except the nosotros and vosotros forms, just like non-reflexive stem-changing verbs.
  • Body Parts with Definite Articles: When using reflexive verbs to talk about actions done to body parts (e.g., brushing hair/teeth), Spanish typically uses a definite article (el/la/los/las) instead of a possessive adjective (mi/tu/su).
  • Daily Routine Practice: Actively narrating your own daily routine using reflexive verbs is highlighted as an effective memorization strategy.

Important Terms

  • Verbos reflexivos: Reflexive verbs; verbs where the subject performs the action upon itself (e.g., levantarse - to get oneself up).
  • Reflexive pronouns (Pronombres reflexivos): Small words that accompany reflexive verbs, indicating that the action is reflexive. They are me (myself), te (yourself, informal), se (himself, herself, itself, yourself formal, themselves, yourselves), nos (ourselves), os (yourselves, informal plural).
  • Infinitive: The basic, unconjugated form of a verb (e.g., hablar, comer, vivir). Reflexive infinitives end in "-se" (e.g., levantarse).
  • Conjugated verb: A verb that has been changed to match its subject and tense (e.g., yo levanto, tú comes).
  • Stem-changing verbs (Verbos de cambio de raíz): Verbs whose stem (the part before the -ar, -er, or -ir ending) changes its vowel sound in most conjugations (e.g., dormir > duermo).
  • Rutina diaria: Daily routine; the sequence of activities one does every day.
  • Doña Blanca: A character from a traditional Latin American children's game and song featured in this section.
  • Jicotillo: A little wasp or beetle, a character in the "Doña Blanca" song.
  • Pilar: Pillar; an architectural support, mentioned in the "Doña Blanca" song as being made of gold and silver.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Reflexive Verbs

In Spanish, verbos reflexivos (reflexive verbs) are used when the subject of the sentence performs an action upon themselves. Think about getting ready for your day: "I wake myself up," "You dress yourself," "She brushes her own hair." These are all reflexive actions.

Examples from the textbook:

  • Me levanto a las seis. (I get up at six o'clock. I get myself up.)
  • ¿Te bañas por la mañana? (Do you bathe in the morning? Do you bathe yourself?)
  • Ella se viste rápido. (She gets dressed quickly. She dresses herself.)
  • Nos cepillamos los dientes después de comer. (We brush our teeth after eating. We brush our own teeth.)
  • Los niños se acuestan a las nueve. (The children go to bed at nine. The children put themselves to bed.)

Notice the small pronouns (me, te, se, nos) directly before the conjugated verbs. These are the reflexive pronouns, and they are crucial for reflexive verbs.

Reflexive Pronouns and Their Placement

A reflexive verb indicates that the subject is both performing and receiving the action. The reflexive pronoun must always match the subject of the sentence in person and number.

Reflexive Pronoun Chart:

Subject Pronoun Reflexive Pronoun Meaning
yo me myself
tú te yourself (informal)
él/ella/usted se himself/herself/itself/yourself (formal)
nosotros/as nos ourselves
vosotros/as os yourselves (informal plural)
ellos/ellas/ustedes se themselves/yourselves (formal plural)

Placement: In most simple sentences, the reflexive pronoun is placed directly before the conjugated verb.

Conjugating Reflexive Verbs

Reflexive verbs are listed in dictionaries with "-se" attached to the infinitive (e.g., levantarse, bañarse, vestirse). When you conjugate them, follow these steps:

  1. Remove the "-se" from the infinitive.
  2. Identify the correct reflexive pronoun for the subject.
  3. Place the reflexive pronoun directly before the verb.
  4. Conjugate the verb normally according to its ending (-ar, -er, -ir) and the subject.

Example: Conjugating levantarse (to get up) — a regular -ar reflexive verb

  • (yo) me levanto
  • (tú) te levantas
  • (él/ella/usted) se levanta
  • (nosotros/as) nos levantamos
  • (vosotros/as) os levantáis
  • (ellos/ellas/ustedes) se levantan

Daily Routine Reflexive Verbs

This chapter features several reflexive verbs common in daily routines. It's important to study the provided vocabulary list to learn these verbs, paying special attention to those that are also stem-changing verbs. Common examples include verbs for waking up, bathing, getting dressed, and going to bed.

Reflexive Verbs with Stem Changes

Some reflexive verbs are also stem-changing verbs. They follow the same stem-change rules you learned in Chapter 10:

  • The stem vowel changes in all forms except for nosotros and vosotros.

Common Stem-Changing Reflexive Verbs from this chapter:

  • despertarse (e > ie): to wake up (oneself)
  • vestirse (e > i): to get dressed (oneself)
  • acostarse (o > ue): to go to bed (oneself)

Examples:

  • Me despierto temprano cuando tengo un vuelo. (I wake up early when I have a flight.)
  • Mi hermana se viste en cinco minutos. (My sister gets dressed in five minutes.)
  • Nos acostamos tarde los viernes. (We go to bed late on Fridays.)
    • Notice that "nos acostamos" does not have a stem change because it's the nosotros form.

"¡Cepillarse!" - Special Rule for Body Parts

When using reflexive verbs to describe actions performed on body parts (like brushing hair or teeth), Spanish uses a definite article (el, la, los, las) instead of a possessive adjective (mi, tu, su). This is because the reflexive pronoun already indicates whose body part is being acted upon.

Correct Usage:

  • Me cepillo los dientes. (I brush my teeth. Literally: I brush the teeth to myself.)
  • Se cepilla el pelo. (She brushes her hair. Literally: She brushes the hair to herself.)

Incorrect Usage:

  • Me cepillo mis dientes. (This is grammatically redundant in Spanish.)
  • Se cepilla su pelo.

This pattern applies to many verbs involving body parts.

Memory Tip: La historia de la mañana (The Morning Routine Story)

The most effective way to learn reflexive verbs is to integrate them into your daily life. Narrate your own morning routine in Spanish, either out loud or in your head. Start with a few verbs and gradually add more. This consistent, real-world practice will help you internalize the verbs and pronouns naturally.

Example Routine:

  • Me despierto. (I wake myself up.)
  • Me levanto. (I get myself up.)
  • Me baño. (I bathe myself.)
  • Me visto. (I dress myself.)
  • Me cepillo los dientes. (I brush my teeth.)
  • ¡Estoy listo/lista! (I'm ready!)

Cultural Spotlight: "Doña Blanca"

This section also introduces "Doña Blanca," a traditional and beloved children's game song from Latin America. The song describes Doña Blanca, protected by "pillars of silver and gold," and a jicotillo (a little wasp or beetle) trying to reach her. Listening to the song can help you recognize familiar vocabulary and observe descriptive language in a cultural context. After listening, practice acting out the game with a partner, using new commands like "¡No entres!" (Don't enter!). This connects language learning with cultural immersion and interactive practice.

Review Questions

  1. What is the fundamental characteristic that defines a "reflexive verb" in Spanish? Provide an example.
  2. List the six reflexive pronouns and explain their general placement in a sentence relative to the conjugated verb.
  3. How does conjugating a stem-changing reflexive verb (like acostarse or despertarse) differ from conjugating a regular reflexive verb? Give an example of this difference.
  4. Explain the special rule regarding the use of definite articles (el/la/los/las) instead of possessive adjectives (mi/tu/su) when discussing body parts with reflexive verbs. Why is this rule in place?
  5. The textbook suggests practicing your own daily routine in Spanish to learn reflexive verbs. Describe how you could implement this strategy and why you think it would be effective for memorization.
