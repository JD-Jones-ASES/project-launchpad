Study Guide: El pretérito de los verbos -ar

Overview

This section introduces the preterite tense for regular -ar verbs in Spanish, which is used to describe completed actions in the past. It explains how to form these verbs, highlights essential accent marks, clarifies the role of time expressions, and points out a key difference between the present and preterite forms for "nosotros." Mastering the preterite is fundamental for narrating past events and sharing experiences in Spanish.

Key Concepts

  • Purpose of the Preterite Tense: The preterite tense is used to talk about actions that were completed at a specific point in the past. It's similar to adding "-ed" to verbs in English (e.g., "I walked," "they talked").
  • Regular -AR Verb Conjugation: To form the preterite of regular -ar verbs, remove the -ar ending from the infinitive and add the specific preterite endings: -é, -aste, -ó, -amos, -asteis, -aron.
  • Crucial Accent Marks: The yo form (-é) and the él/ella/Ud. form (-ó) must have accent marks. Forgetting them changes the verb to a different tense or mood, altering the meaning significantly.
  • Nosotros Form Ambiguity: The nosotros (we) form of regular -ar verbs in the preterite (-amos) is identical to its present tense form. Context, especially time expressions, is essential to distinguish between "we walk" (present) and "we walked" (preterite).
  • Time Expressions (Expresiones de tiempo): Words and phrases like ayer (yesterday), anoche (last night), la semana pasada (last week), or hace tres días (three days ago) are frequently used with the preterite to specify when an action occurred.
  • Actions on a Timeline: Conceptually, the preterite places completed actions firmly on the "past" side of a timeline, signifying that they have a clear beginning and end.

Important Terms

  • Pretérito (Preterite Tense): A past tense in Spanish used to express actions completed in the past.
  • Verbos -ar (-AR Verbs): Verbs in Spanish whose infinitive form ends in -ar (e.g., hablar, caminar, estudiar).
  • Stem: The base part of a verb that remains after removing the infinitive ending (-ar, -er, -ir). For example, the stem of hablar is habl-.
  • Expresiones de tiempo (Time Expressions): Adverbs or phrases that indicate when an action takes place, commonly used with the preterite to specify past timing.
  • Acento (Accent Mark): A diacritical mark (´) placed over vowels in Spanish to indicate stress or distinguish between words, crucial for the yo and él/ella/Ud. preterite forms.
  • Corrido: A traditional Mexican narrative ballad, often telling stories of history, revolution, or daily life.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to the Preterite Tense

The preterite tense (el pretérito) is one of the most important past tenses in Spanish. It is used to talk about actions that were completed in the past, often at a specific point in time. Just like in English you say "I walked" instead of "I walk" when referring to a past action, Spanish uses the preterite to convey this sense of completion. Learning the preterite will allow you to tell stories, describe events that have happened, and share your memories in Spanish.

Forming the Preterite of Regular -AR Verbs

To conjugate a regular -AR verb in the preterite tense, follow these two simple steps:

  1. Remove the -ar ending from the infinitive verb to find its stem. (This is the same stem you use for present tense conjugation).
    • Example: For hablar (to speak), the stem is habl-.
    • Example: For caminar (to walk), the stem is camin-.
  2. Add the appropriate preterite endings to the stem.

Here are the preterite endings for regular -ar verbs:

Subject Pronoun Ending Example: hablar
yo (I) -é hablé
tú (you, informal) -aste hablaste
él/ella/Ud. (he/she/you formal) -ó habló
nosotros/nosotras (we) -amos hablamos
vosotros/vosotras (you all, informal, Spain) -asteis hablasteis
ellos/ellas/Uds. (they/you all formal) -aron hablaron

Conjugation Example: hablar (to speak) in the Preterite

  • Yo hablé (I spoke)
  • Tú hablaste (You spoke)
  • Él/Ella/Ud. habló (He/She/You formal spoke)
  • Nosotros/Nosotras hablamos (We spoke)
  • Vosotros/Vosotras hablasteis (You all spoke)
  • Ellos/Ellas/Uds. hablaron (They/You all formal spoke)

Crucial Points for Conjugation

  1. Mandatory Accent Marks: The accent marks on the yo form (-é) and the él/ella/Ud. form (-ó) are absolutely essential. They are not optional and directly affect the meaning and tense of the verb.

    • Compare: hablé (I spoke, preterite) vs. hable (a subjunctive form, to be learned later).
    • Compare: habló (he/she/Ud. spoke, preterite) vs. hablo (I speak, present tense). Forgetting these accents means you are writing a different verb form or even a different tense altogether. Always double-check them!

  2. The Nosotros Form: Be aware that the nosotros form (-amos) for regular -ar verbs is identical in both the present tense and the preterite tense.

    • Present: Hoy caminamos a la escuela. (Today we walk to school.)
    • Preterite: Ayer caminamos a la escuela. (Yesterday we walked to school.) The context, particularly the use of time expressions, is what clarifies whether you are referring to a present or past action.

Time Expressions (Expresiones de tiempo)

Time expressions are crucial for setting the scene and making your preterite sentences clear and natural. They tell your listener or reader when an action happened. Here are some common ones:

  • ayer: yesterday
  • anteayer: the day before yesterday
  • anoche: last night
  • la semana pasada: last week
  • el mes pasado: last month
  • el año pasado: last year
  • hace (time period) + que: (time period) ago (e.g., hace tres días = three days ago)
  • de repente: suddenly
  • una vez: once, one time

Examples:

  • Anoche estudié por dos horas. (Last night I studied for two hours.)
  • La semana pasada visitamos el museo. (Last week we visited the museum.)
  • Hace tres días, ella llamó a su abuela. (Three days ago, she called her grandmother.)
  • De repente, el niño lloró. (Suddenly, the boy cried.)

Cultural Context: "La Cucaracha"

"La cucaracha" (The Cockroach) is a hugely popular traditional Mexican song, dating back to the Mexican Revolution (1910–1920). It is a classic example of a corrido, a type of narrative ballad common in Mexican folk music that often tells stories, sometimes humorous, sometimes poignant, about daily life, struggle, and historical events. Over time, different versions and adaptations of the song have emerged across Latin America, and it remains one of the most recognized Spanish-language songs globally.

Reflect: The song's popularity during the Mexican Revolution invites reflection. Why might a song about a cockroach, which can symbolize resilience, persistence, or even an underdog, resonate with people experiencing revolution? What could the "cockroach" represent or symbolize in such a tumultuous historical period? Consider discussing this with others or writing down your thoughts.

Memory Tip for Preterite Endings

To help memorize the regular -AR preterite endings, try practicing them in a rhythmic pattern:

-é \ \ -aste \ \ -ó \ \ -amos \ \ -aron

Notice the accents on yo (-é) and él/ella/Ud. (-ó). The tú form, -aste, is the longest. Practice saying them out loud with a steady rhythm, like: "AY, AH-stay, OH, AH-mohs, AH-rohn." Writing them out multiple times from memory can also solidify them.
