Study Guide: Los Adjetivos (Adjectives)

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concept of adjectives in Spanish, focusing on how they change to agree with the nouns they describe. You will learn about gender and number agreement, different patterns for adjective endings, where to place adjectives in a sentence, and how to use them to describe people and things.

Key Concepts

  • Adjective Agreement (Concordancia): In Spanish, adjectives must always match the noun they describe in both gender (masculine/feminine) and number (singular/plural). This is a core rule for correct Spanish grammar.
  • Gender and Number: Nouns have a grammatical gender (masculine or feminine) and can be singular or plural. Adjectives change their endings to reflect the gender and number of the noun they modify.
  • Adjectives Ending in -o/-a: Most descriptive adjectives follow this pattern. Their endings change to -o (masculine singular), -a (feminine singular), -os (masculine plural), or -as (feminine plural).
  • Adjectives Ending in -e or a Consonant: These adjectives generally do not change for gender. They have the same form for masculine and feminine singular. To make them plural, you add an -s (for -e endings) or -es (for consonant endings).
  • Adjective Placement: Unlike in English, descriptive adjectives in Spanish almost always come after the noun they describe.
  • Using Adjectives with "Ser": The verb "ser" (to be) is commonly used with adjectives to describe inherent qualities or characteristics of people and things.

Important Terms

  • Los adjetivos: Adjectives; words that describe nouns.
  • Concordancia: Agreement; the grammatical rule that adjectives must match nouns in gender and number.
  • Género: Gender; in Spanish grammar, nouns are either masculine (el/un) or feminine (la/una).
  • Número: Number; refers to whether a noun is singular (one) or plural (more than one).
  • Masculino: Masculine (e.g., el libro, un chico).
  • Femenino: Feminine (e.g., la mochila, una chica).
  • Singular: Singular (e.g., el perro, la casa).
  • Plural: Plural (e.g., los perros, las casas).
  • Adjective Placement: The position of an adjective relative to the noun it describes (in Spanish, usually after the noun).
  • Ser: The verb "to be," used for describing permanent characteristics.
  • ¿Cómo es...? What is... like? (Used to ask for a description).
  • Joven: Young.
  • Serio/a: Serious.
  • Un poco: A little bit.
  • Muy: Very.
  • Bueno/a: Good.
  • Favorito/a: Favorite.

Detailed Notes

The Core Rule: Adjective Agreement (La concordancia de los adjetivos)

Adjectives are words that describe nouns. In Spanish, a crucial rule is that adjectives must agree with the nouns they describe in both gender (masculine or feminine) and number (singular or plural). This is known as concordancia.

Let's look at how this agreement works based on the adjective's ending:

1. Adjectives ending in -o/-a (Most Descriptive Adjectives)

Many common adjectives end in -o in their masculine singular form. These adjectives change their ending to match the noun they describe:

  • Masculine Singular: -o (e.g., el chico alto - the tall boy)
  • Feminine Singular: -a (e.g., la chica alta - the tall girl)
  • Masculine Plural: -os (e.g., los chicos altos - the tall boys)
  • Feminine Plural: -as (e.g., las chicas altas - the tall girls)

Examples:

Noun Gender/Number Adjective (Masculine Singular) Agreed Adjective Translation
el libro Masculine Singular blanco blanco the white book
la mochila Feminine Singular blanco blanca the white backpack
los libros Masculine Plural blanco blancos the white books
las mochilas Feminine Plural blanco blancas the white backpacks

2. Adjectives ending in -e or a Consonant

These adjectives behave differently regarding gender:

  • No Gender Change: Adjectives ending in -e or a consonant generally have the same form for both masculine and feminine nouns in the singular.
    • Masculine Singular: -e or consonant (e.g., el chico inteligente - the intelligent boy)
    • Feminine Singular: -e or consonant (e.g., la chica inteligente - the intelligent girl)
  • Number Change (Plural):
    • If the adjective ends in -e, add -s for the plural.
      • e.g., el estudiante inteligente -> los estudiantes inteligentes
      • e.g., la mujer grande -> las mujeres grandes
    • If the adjective ends in a consonant, add -es for the plural.
      • e.g., el carro azul -> los carros azules
      • e.g., la falda gris -> las faldas grises

Important Note (¡Ojo!): The adjective grande (big/large) is an example of an adjective ending in -e. It does not change for gender (el libro grande, la mochila grande). For plural, it becomes grandes (los libros grandes, las mochilas grandes).

Colors that follow this pattern: azul (blue), verde (green), and gris (gray). They are the same for masculine and feminine; just add -es for the plural.

  • el carro azul / la casa azul -> los carros azules / las casas azules
  • el pantalón verde / la camisa verde -> los pantalones verdes / las camisas verdes
  • el gato gris / la gata gris -> los gatos grises / las gatas grises

Adjective Placement

In Spanish, descriptive adjectives almost always come after the noun they describe. This is a significant difference from English.

  • English: a red car
  • Spanish: un carro rojo (car red)

Examples:

  • el libro interesante (the interesting book)
  • la mochila negra (the black backpack)
  • los estudiantes inteligentes (the intelligent students)
  • las clases difíciles (the difficult classes)

Summary of the Process for Adjective Agreement

  1. Find the noun: Identify the noun the adjective is describing.
  2. Determine gender and number: Check the article (el/un = masculine; la/una = feminine) and see if it's singular or plural.
  3. Match the adjective: Adjust the adjective's ending to agree with the noun's gender and number.
  4. Place the adjective: Position the adjective after the noun.

Examples with "Ser"

The verb "ser" (to be) is frequently used with adjectives to describe characteristics.

  • Ella es alta. (She is tall.)
  • Los libros son viejos. (The books are old.)
  • Él es inteligente. (He is intelligent.)
  • Las clases son interesantes. (The classes are interesting.)

Describing Someone (Describir a alguien) - Conversation Example

The dialogue illustrates how adjectives are used in natural conversation to describe people and their traits. Notice how the adjectives change based on whether the teacher is masculine ("maestro") or feminine ("maestra").

  • ¿Cómo es tu maestro de matemáticas? (What is your math teacher like?)
    • Es alto y simpático. Y es muy inteligente! (He is tall and friendly. And he is very intelligent!)
  • ¿Es joven o viejo? (Is he young or old?)
    • No es muy viejo. Es joven. (He's not very old. He's young.)
  • ¿Y tu maestra de ciencias? (And your science teacher?)
    • Es muy alta y delgada. Es un poco seria, pero es muy buena maestra. (She is very tall and thin. She is a bit serious, but she is a very good teacher.)
  • Mi clase favorita es la de arte! La maestra es divertida y muy creativa. (My favorite class is art! The teacher is fun and very creative.)

Notice the agreement:

  • maestro (masculine singular) -> alto, simpático, inteligente, joven
  • maestra (feminine singular) -> alta, delgada, seria, buena, divertida, creativa
  • clase (feminine singular) -> favorita
