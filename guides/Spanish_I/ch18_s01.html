Study Guide: The Preterite Tense of -er and -ir Verbs

Overview

This section expands your ability to discuss past events in Spanish by introducing the preterite tense for regular -er and -ir verbs. You will learn that both verb types share the same set of preterite endings, simplifying conjugation. Additionally, the section covers important distinctions for the nosotros form, a key spelling rule for verbs like leer and creer, and the cultural use of diminutives like -ito/-ita.

Key Concepts

  • Preterite Tense for Completed Actions: The preterite tense is used to describe actions that were completed in the past, often at a specific point in time.
  • Shared Endings for -er and -ir Verbs: Unlike the present tense, regular -er and -ir verbs use the exact same set of endings in the preterite tense: -í, -iste, -ió, -imos, -ieron.
  • Conjugation Process: To conjugate a regular -er or -ir verb in the preterite, drop the -er or -ir ending from the infinitive and add the appropriate preterite ending.
  • Distinguishing Nosotros Forms: For -ir verbs, the nosotros form in the preterite (e.g., vivimos) is identical to its present tense counterpart. Context, especially time words (like ayer or anoche), is crucial for understanding which tense is being used. For -er verbs, the nosotros preterite (comimos) has an -i-, while the present (comemos) has an -e-.
  • Spelling Changes (i > y): Verbs whose stem ends in a vowel, such as leer and creer, undergo a spelling change in the él/ella/usted and ellos/ellas/ustedes preterite forms. The -i- of the ending changes to a -y- when it appears between two vowels (e.g., leyó, leyeron). This is a spelling rule for pronunciation, not an irregularity in conjugation.
  • Diminutives (-ito/-ita): Suffixes like -ito and -ita are added to words to convey smallness, affection, or endearment. They are common in Latin American Spanish and are used to express warmth rather than just physical size.

Important Terms

  • Pretérito: The simple past tense in Spanish, used for actions completed in the past.
  • Verbos -er/-ir: Spanish verbs whose infinitives end in -er (e.g., comer, aprender) or -ir (e.g., vivir, escribir).
  • Conjugación: The process of changing a verb's ending to match the subject and tense.
  • Infinitivo: The basic, unconjugated form of a verb (e.g., comer, vivir).
  • Stem: The part of the verb that remains after removing the -ar, -er, or -ir ending (e.g., com- from comer).
  • Diminutivo: A suffix (like -ito, -ita, -cito, -cita) added to a word to indicate smallness, tenderness, or affection (e.g., chiquitito, hijito).
  • Contexto: The surrounding words, sentences, and situation that help clarify the meaning of a word or phrase, especially useful for distinguishing verb tenses.

Detailed Notes

The Preterite Tense of -er and -ir Verbs

Building upon your knowledge of the preterite tense with -ar verbs, this section introduces how to talk about past actions using -er and -ir verbs. The good news is that these two verb categories share the exact same set of preterite endings!

1. Identifying the Pattern Consider these examples:

  • Comí una pizza deliciosa anoche. (I ate a delicious pizza last night.)
  • ¿Bebiste el jugo de naranja? (Did you drink the orange juice?)
  • Ella vivió en México por dos años. (She lived in Mexico for two years.)
  • Escribieron una carta muy larga. (They wrote a very long letter.)
  • Aprendimos mucho en esa clase. (We learned a lot in that class.)

Notice the endings: -í, -iste, -ió, -imos, -ieron. These are the preterite endings you will use for both -er and -ir verbs.

2. Conjugating Regular -er and -ir Verbs To form the preterite, simply drop the -er or -ir ending from the infinitive and add the appropriate preterite ending:

Subject Pronoun -er and -ir Verb Endings Example: comer (to eat) Example: vivir (to live)
yo -í comí viví
tú -iste comiste viviste
él/ella/usted -ió comió vivió
nosotros/as -imos comimos vivimos
vosotros/as -isteis (not covered in text) (not covered in text)
ellos/ellas/ustedes -ieron comieron vivieron

3. The Nosotros Form: A Special Case Pay close attention to the nosotros (we) form:

  • For -ir verbs: The nosotros form in the preterite (e.g., vivimos) is identical to its present tense form (e.g., vivimos). You'll need to rely on context or time-specific words (like ayer for yesterday, anoche for last night) to determine if the action happened in the past or is happening now.
  • For -er verbs: The nosotros form in the preterite (e.g., comimos) has an -i-, while the present tense form (e.g., comemos) has an -e-. This slight difference (comimos vs. comemos) helps distinguish the tenses.

4. Spelling Changes (i > y) for Vowel-Stem Verbs Some verbs, particularly those whose stems end in a vowel, exhibit a spelling change in the preterite for the third-person singular (él/ella/usted) and third-person plural (ellos/ellas/ustedes) forms. When the -i- of the preterite ending (-ió, -ieron) would appear between two vowels, it changes to a -y- to facilitate pronunciation.

  • Leer (to read):
    • leí
    • leíste
    • leyó (not leió)
    • leímos
    • leyeron (not leieron)
  • Creer (to believe):
    • creí
    • creíste
    • creyó (not creió)
    • creímos
    • creyeron (not creieron)
  • This rule also applies to other similar verbs like oír (to hear): oyó, oyeron. This is a regular spelling adjustment, not an irregular verb conjugation.

Cultural Highlight: "El barquito chiquitito"

"El barquito chiquitito" is a beloved children's counting song throughout the Spanish-speaking world. It tells the humorous and repetitive story of a tiny boat that initially struggles to sail. The song emphasizes patience, persistence, and making the best of a difficult situation, as the crew keeps waiting and eating. It's an example of a long tradition of counting songs for children in Spanish, similar to English nursery rhymes. The lyrics you saw include the repetitive lines that build anticipation before the boat finally sails (or doesn't, depending on the version!).

Grammar Point: Diminutives (-ito/-ita)

Did you notice chiquitito in the song title? This is an example of a diminutive.

  • Formation: Adding suffixes like -ito or -ita (or -cito, -cita, etc., depending on the word) to nouns or adjectives.
  • Meaning: Diminutives are used to:
    • Express smallness (e.g., chiquito - small, chiquitito - very small/tiny).
    • Convey affection, tenderness, or endearment. For example, a grandmother calling her grandchild hijito (little child) or her loved one corazoncito (little heart) is expressing warmth and affection, not necessarily physical size.
  • Common Use: You will frequently hear and read diminutives in Latin American Spanish, often adding a touch of warmth or familiarity to conversations.
