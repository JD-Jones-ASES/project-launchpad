Mis Metas (My Goals) - Study Guide

Overview

This section focuses on equipping you with the language tools to discuss your personal aspirations, future plans, and possibilities in Spanish. You'll learn essential vocabulary related to goals and achievements, delve into the different ways to express future actions, and explore the cultural context of youth making a difference in Latin America, all while reinforcing previously learned grammatical structures like the subjunctive mood.

Key Concepts

  • Expressing Personal Goals and Aspirations: Learn verbs and phrases essential for articulating what you dream of becoming, hope to achieve, and intend to do in the future.
  • Three Ways to Talk About the Future: Understand the nuances and common usage of ir + a + infinitivo (most common in daily speech), the simple future tense (formal/written), and quiero que + subjuntivo (expressing desires for others).
  • The Importance of the Subjunctive: Reinforce your understanding of the subjunctive mood for expressing wishes, hopes, and necessities, particularly when discussing desires or expectations for others.
  • Avoiding False Cognates: Recognize and correctly use the word éxito (success) and differentiate it from the English word "exit," which in Spanish is la salida.
  • Youth Contributions in Latin America: Gain cultural insight into how young people in various Latin American countries are actively shaping their future through environmentalism, entrepreneurship, journalism, and cultural preservation.

Important Terms

  • Mis metas: My goals
  • El futuro: The future
  • Vocabulario: Vocabulary
  • Éxito: Success (NOT "exit")
  • La salida: The exit
  • Soñar con ser (médica/artista): To dream of being (a doctor/an artist)
  • Lograr: To achieve, to attain
  • Intentar: To try, to attempt
  • Continuar aprendiendo: To continue learning
  • Ir + a + infinitivo: Going to do [verb] (e.g., Voy a estudiar - I'm going to study)
  • El futuro tense: The future tense (e.g., Estudiaré - I will study)
  • Quiero que + subjuntivo: I want that [you/he/she] [verb in subjunctive] (e.g., Quiero que estudies - I want you to study)
  • Jóvenes: Young people, youth
  • Biodiversidad: Biodiversity
  • Emprendedores: Entrepreneurs
  • Periodistas: Journalists
  • Preservar: To preserve
  • Apoyar: To support
  • Seguir (tu talento): To follow (your talent) / To continue
  • Mayor (desafío): Greatest, biggest (challenge)
  • Hacer un esfuerzo: To make an effort
  • Ojalá que: Hopefully that, I hope that (triggers subjunctive)
  • La esperanza: Hope

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Your Future Goals

This section builds on your ability to express wishes for others by focusing on your own aspirations and future plans. It introduces vocabulary and grammar necessary for articulating personal goals, achievements, and possibilities in Spanish, preparing you to talk about "El futuro" (The Future).

Key Vocabulary for Goals and Future

To talk about your dreams and what you hope to achieve, here are some essential verbs and phrases:

  • Soñar con ser [profesión]: "To dream of being [profession]." For example, Sueño con ser médica (I dream of being a doctor).
  • Esperar lograr [meta]: "To hope to achieve [goal]." For example, Espero lograr mis metas (I hope to achieve my goals).
  • Ir a intentar [acción]: "To be going to try to [action]." For example, Voy a intentar hablar más español (I'm going to try to speak more Spanish).
  • Debemos continuar [acción]: "We must continue [action]." For example, ¡Debemos continuar aprendiendo! (We must continue learning!).

Avoiding False Friends: Éxito vs. Salida

One of the most crucial vocabulary distinctions is understanding the false cognate éxito.

  • Éxito: Means success.
    • Correct example: El estudiante tuvo mucho éxito. (The student had great success.)
  • Salida: Means exit.
    • Correct example: When you see a sign that says "Salida" on a building, it means the way out. Remember: Éxito does NOT mean "exit." Confusing these can lead to humorous but significant misunderstandings!

Talking About the Future (Hablar del futuro)

Spanish offers three primary ways to express future actions or events:

  1. Option 1 (Most Common in Everyday Speech): ir + a + infinitivo

    • This is the equivalent of "to be going to do something" in English. You first learned this in Chapter 9!
    • Structure: Conjugated form of ir (to go) + a + infinitive verb.
    • Examples:
      • Voy a estudiar medicina. (I'm going to study medicine.)
      • Ella va a ser ingeniera. (She's going to be an engineer.)
      • Vamos a continuar aprendiendo. (We're going to continue learning.)
    • Usage: This is the most frequently used way to talk about the future in casual, spoken Spanish. Use it with confidence!

  2. Option 2 (Formal/Written Spanish - Preview): The Future Tense Ending

    • This is the equivalent of "will do something" in English. While not fully taught in this textbook, it's good to recognize.
    • Examples:
      • Estudiaré medicina. (I will study medicine.)
      • Ella será ingeniera. (She will be an engineer.)
    • Usage: More common in formal writing or speeches, and less so in everyday conversations. You'll learn more about this in future courses.

  3. Option 3 (Expressing Desires for Someone Else's Future): quiero que + subjuntivo

    • You recently learned this! It's used when you want to express a wish, hope, or desire that someone else does something in the future.
    • Structure: A verb of desire/hope (like querer or esperar) + que + verb in the subjunctive mood.
    • Examples:
      • Quiero que estudies medicina. (I want you to study medicine.)
      • Espero que ella sea ingeniera. (I hope she will be an engineer.)
    • Usage: Crucial for expressing influence or expectations over others' actions. Other common subjunctive triggers include Es importante que... (It's important that...) and Es necesario que... (It's necessary that...).

Youth in Latin America: Shaping the Future (Los jóvenes de Latinoamérica)

This cultural section highlights the significant contributions of young people across Latin America. It showcases how:

  • In Costa Rica, teenage environmental activists protect biodiversity.
  • In Chile, young entrepreneurs contribute to "Silicon Andes" tech startups.
  • In Mexico, student journalists document local stories.
  • In Ecuador and Bolivia, indigenous youth work to preserve ancestral languages. These examples demonstrate that "el futuro belongs to those who work toward it — one meta at a time," emphasizing the importance of actively pursuing one's goals.

Dialogue Analysis: Planes para el futuro

The conversation between Valentina and Mateo provides practical examples of the grammar and vocabulary learned:

  • Mateo's plans: Voy a estudiar ciencias. Quiero ser científico. (Uses ir + a + infinitivo and Quiero ser...)
  • Valentina's dreams: Yo sueño con ser artista. Espero que mi familia apoye mi decisión. (Uses Sueño con ser... and Espero que + subjuntivo).
  • Mateo's advice: Es importante que tú sigas tu talento. (Uses Es importante que + subjuntivo).
  • Mateo's challenge: Para mí, es necesario que yo aprenda más matemáticas. ¡Pero voy a intentarlo! (Uses Es necesario que + subjuntivo and ir + a + infinitivo). The "Palabras clave" like oye, después de, apoye, seguir, mayor, and ¡intentarlo! are key to understanding the dialogue.

Reading Comprehension: Mis planes para el futuro

Sofía's text reinforces the themes of personal goals, future plans, and global awareness.

  • She uses espero que mi familia tenga (I hope my family has) and es importante que todos los jóvenes logren (it's important that all young people achieve) — examples of the subjunctive for hopes/necessities regarding others.
  • Quiero que mis amigos y yo hagamos un esfuerzo (I want my friends and I to make an effort) is another example of querer que + subjuntivo.
  • Ojalá que el mundo del futuro sea más justo y más verde (Hopefully the world of the future will be more just and greener) uses Ojalá que which always triggers the subjunctive.
  • Her own plans are expressed using ir + a + infinitivo: Voy a estudiar mucho, voy a practicar español e inglés, y voy a intentar ser una buena líder. The "Después de leer" exercises are designed to help you actively identify and understand these grammatical structures in context.

Personalizing Your Future: Memory Tip

This section encourages a powerful visualization technique:

  1. Close your eyes and imagine yourself five years in the future, using Spanish fluently.
  2. Ask yourself: ¿Dónde estoy? (Where am I?), ¿Con quién hablo? (Who am I speaking with?), ¿Qué hago con mi español? (What am I doing with my Spanish?).
  3. Write three sentences in Spanish about this vision. This helps personalize your learning journey and connect your present studies to future successes.

Review Questions

  1. What are the three main ways to talk about the future in Spanish, as described in this section? Provide one example sentence for each, explaining when you would use each option.
  2. Explain the meaning of éxito and why it is a common "false cognate" for English speakers. What is the correct Spanish word for "exit"?
  3. How does the subjunctive mood play a role when discussing goals and future plans, particularly when your desires involve other people? Give an example from the section or create your own.
  4. Based on the "Los jóvenes de Latinoamérica" section, describe at least three ways young people are positively impacting their communities or the world. How do their actions embody the idea of working towards "mis metas"?
  5. Imagine your own biggest challenge (mayor desafío) for learning Spanish. How would you express your intention to overcome it using the ir + a + infinitivo structure, and what is one thing you hope your teacher (maestro/a) does to support you, using the espero que + subjuntivo structure?
