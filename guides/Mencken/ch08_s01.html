H.L. Mencken’s Liberty and Democratic Man: A Study Guide

This study guide provides a comprehensive analysis of Section I.7 of H.L. Mencken’s "Liberty and Democratic Man." It explores Mencken’s paradoxical argument regarding democracy and freedom, his use of specific rhetorical strategies, and the historical context of 1920s America that informed his perspective.

I. Core Concepts and Themes

The Central Paradox of Democracy

Mencken’s argument hinges on a fundamental inversion of traditional political thought: democracy, often championed as the guardian of liberty, is actually its greatest threat. While the "common man" may speak of liberty, Mencken argues he does not truly desire it. Instead, the common man seeks security and is driven by envy.

The Nature of the Free Man vs. The Common Man

Mencken establishes a sharp dichotomy between the rare individual capable of liberty and the democratic masses.

Attribute The Free Man The Common Man ("Homo boobiens")
Primary Driver Self-reliance and resolution Envy and the desire for security
Status A "sort of god" in his own world An "ox" or "anthropoid" subspecies
Capacity Capable of "doing without" and facing "dreadful loneliness" No talent for self-reliance; comparable to an animal
Relationship to Power Independent and defiant Submissive, "licking the butcher behind the ear"

Historical Context: 1920s America

Mencken’s skepticism was rooted in contemporary events where democratic majorities or governance restricted personal freedoms:

  • The Eighteenth Amendment (Prohibition): Personal freedom restricted by majority morality.
  • The Palmer Raids (1919–1920): Targeted political dissidents.
  • The Scopes Trial (1925): Scientific inquiry pitted against popular religious sentiment.

II. Rhetorical and Stylistic Analysis

Voice and Tone

Mencken shifts his tone to emphasize his arguments:

  1. Clinical Diagnostician: Opens with a casual observation of democratic life.
  2. Reverent/Nietzschean: Elevates the concept of the free man to a god-like status.
  3. Contemptuous: Plunges into "barnyard" imagery to describe the common man.
  4. Scholarly: Uses a measured, historical tone to catalogue human servitude.

Diction and Imagery

Mencken uses contrasting linguistic registers to create a hierarchy of value.

  • Elevated Diction: Words like "magnificent," "resolution," and "enterprise" are reserved for liberty.
  • Animal Imagery: The proletariat is described as "anthropoid" or an "ox." This suggests the relationship between the ruler (the butcher) and the ruled (the ox) is biological rather than political.

Syntax and Rhythm

  • The Long-Short Combo: Mencken often uses long, parallel clauses to build a complex requirement for liberty (the "pretension"), followed by a brutally short sentence to deliver the "reality" (e.g., "That is to say, he has no talent whatsoever").
  • The Dash: Used as a rhetorical "ambush" to insert sardonic qualifications or parenthetical verdicts that redirect the reader’s expectations.

III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to Mencken, what is the "common man" primarily driven by?
    • Answer: The common man is intellectually limited, politically manipulable, and primarily driven by envy.
  2. How does Mencken describe the "talent" of the common man for self-reliance?
    • Answer: He compares it to the talent for writing symphonies in the style of Beethoven, concluding the common man has "no talent whatsoever."
  3. What is the significance of Frederick the Great in Mencken’s argument?
    • Answer: Frederick the Great is used as an example of the central paradox: an absolute monarch who supported freedom of speech and religious tolerance, proving that liberty depends on the quality of the ruler rather than the form of government.
  4. Define the "Ox and Butcher" metaphor.
    • Answer: The common man is an ox (strong but stupid, destined for slaughter) who licks the butcher (the political master) behind the ear. This represents abject submission disguised as affection or pride.
  5. What role does alliteration play in Mencken’s prose?
    • Answer: It binds phrases together, creates rhythm, and makes ideas more memorable and physically pleasurable to read, such as in the phrases "ham and cabbage" or "butcher behind."

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Inversion of Liberty: Analyze Mencken’s argument that democracy is the enemy of liberty. How does he use historical examples and the concept of "majority morality" to support this claim?
  2. The Nietzschean Influence: Discuss the "Nietzschean exaltation" found in Mencken’s description of the free man. How does the contrast between the "god-like" individual and the "animalistic" mob serve Mencken’s rhetorical purpose?
  3. Diction as Argument: Examine how Mencken’s choice of vocabulary (specifically the contrast between "liturgical" and "barnyard" registers) functions as a systematic denial of the common man's capacity for rational self-governance.
  4. The Aesthetic of Contempt: Perform a close reading of Mencken’s "ox and butcher" metaphor. How do the sound, syntax, and irony of this specific passage distill his entire critique of the democratic electorate?

V. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Anthropoid: Resembling a human being but not quite one; used by Mencken to reduce the proletariat to a subspecies.
  • Antithesis: The pairing of opposites for rhetorical effect (e.g., liberty vs. security).
  • Grandiloquently: Speaking in a lofty, pompous style; used to describe the mob’s empty talk of liberty.
  • Homo boobiens: Mencken’s cynical term for the common man or the "boob."
  • Mountebanks: Charlatans or frauds; Mencken’s term for democratic politicians.
  • Poltroonery: Utter cowardice; applied to the democratic electorate to contrast them with the chivalric tradition.
  • Votaries: Devoted followers, often of a cult; Mencken uses this to describe the few genuine lovers of liberty.
