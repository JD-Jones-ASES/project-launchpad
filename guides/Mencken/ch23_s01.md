# Study Guide: Corruption Under Democracy (Analysis of H.L. Mencken)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of H.L. Mencken’s analysis of democratic corruption as presented in Chapter 23, Section 1. It examines the structural arguments, rhetorical strategies, and linguistic choices Mencken employs to argue that corruption is the fundamental operating principle of democratic governance.

---

## Key Concepts and Themes

### 1. Democracy as "Wholesale Bribery"
The central thesis of the text is that corruption is not a tangential "disease" of the democratic system but its core operating principle. Mencken argues that any government answerable to "the mob" must inevitably sustain itself through a continuous cycle of bribery. This includes:
*   **Official Bribery:** Buying labor leaders with offices.
*   **Legislative Bribery:** Buying the followers of those leaders with favorable legislation.
*   **Special Interest Trading:** Providing specific benefits to manufacturers, religious groups (Methodists and Catholics), and farmers to maintain power.

### 2. The Complicity of the "Mob"
Mencken adopts a Nietzschean perspective, suggesting that the "herd" is not an innocent victim of corruption but is actively complicit in its own exploitation. From the perspective of the mob, democratic corruption is viewed as entirely rational. The citizenry is depicted as having "prehensile" greed—an animalistic urge to grasp and clutch at benefits.

### 3. The Industrialization of Public Opinion
One of the most significant arguments in the text is that public opinion is not a spontaneous or reasoned force. Instead, it is treated as a raw material:
*   **The Source:** It originates as the "mob's fears."
*   **The Process:** It is "piped" to central factories where it is "flavored and colored."
*   **The Result:** It is "put into cans," representing a standardized, manufactured product designed to ensure compliance rather than reflect genuine self-government.

### 4. Plutocracy and Revolution
Mencken explores the relationship between capitalism and democracy, asserting that the "plutocracy" has mastered the manipulation of democratic institutions so thoroughly that revolution has become impossible. The system is designed to absorb and redirect dissent through the aforementioned cycles of bargaining and haggling.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. According to Mencken, why is corruption "essential" rather than "accidental" in a democracy?**
*Answer:* Because democracy is a government answerable to the mob, and Mencken argues that such a system is itself a form of wholesale bribery. It requires constant bargaining and the trading of favors to function.

**2. What are the three distinct "registers" or tones Mencken uses to build his argument?**
*Answer:* 
1. The **Clinical register** of the political scientist (stating blunt theses).
2. The **Historian’s register** (marshaling evidence from figures like Tocqueville and Henry Adams).
3. The **Satirist’s register** (using vivid, often mocking imagery).

**3. What does the "whiskey anecdote" symbolize in the context of the democratic experience?**
*Answer:* The anecdote represents the fraud of the democratic promise. The citizen (the man sneaking home the jug) expects self-government (the "whiskey") but receives a fraudulent, harmful substitute (the compound of pepper, prune juice, and wood alcohol).

**4. How does Mencken use the word "prehensile" to describe democratic minorities?**
*Answer:* He uses it to suggest an "animal greed," comparing the grasping nature of special interest groups to a monkey’s tail that reaches and clutches for benefits.

**5. What is the significance of the phrase "government within the general government"?**
*Answer:* It refers to labor leaders who must be bought with offices, highlighting how democracy consists of competing power centers that all require bribery to satisfy.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

### I. The Industrial Metaphor of Public Opinion
Analyze Mencken's concluding metaphor of public opinion being "piped to central factories" and "put into cans." How do his choices of passive verbs and industrial terminology strip the democratic citizen of agency? Discuss the implications of his "canned" pun regarding the authenticity of public sentiment.

### II. The Lexicon of Criminality in Political Analysis
Mencken deliberately avoids idealistic political language, replacing it with the vocabulary of the marketplace and the "back room." Examine his use of terms like *rooked*, *swindled*, *looted*, and *bargaining*. How does this linguistic shift reframe the reader’s perception of the social contract?

### III. Rhetorical Momentum: The Role of Anaphora and Syntax
Discuss how Mencken uses anaphora (the repetition of phrases like "one day... the next day... the day after") and long, accumulative sentences to create a sense of inevitability. How does the rhythm of his prose mirror the "endless, exhausting parade" of democratic deal-making he describes?

## Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Anaphora:** The repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses or sentences; used by Mencken to create a drumbeat effect of accumulation.
*   **Conies:** An archaic term for rabbits; used by extension to mean dupes or "gulls" who are easily cheated.
*   **Coup de grâce:** A final, decisive blow that ends suffering; used to describe Mencken’s final metaphor of the "canning" of public opinion.
*   **Friedrich von Gentz:** A German diplomat and secretary to Prince Metternich; cited by Mencken as a historical example of someone who became disillusioned with democratic governance.
*   **Immemorial:** Existing beyond the reach of memory; ancient. Mencken uses this to describe the mob's fears as a permanent feature of human nature.
*   **Prehensile:** Capable of grasping or wrapping around; used to describe the greedy nature of minority groups in a democracy.
*   **Rooked:** A colloquial term meaning cheated or swindled.
*   **Tergiversations:** Evasions or shifts of allegiance; a Latinate term used to describe political dishonesty in a scholarly tone.
