Analysis of Mencken’s "The Maker of Laws": A Comprehensive Study Guide

This study guide provides a detailed analysis of H.L. Mencken’s critique of the American legislator as presented in Chapter 17, Section 1 of The Maker of Laws. It explores the structural arguments, rhetorical strategies, and linguistic choices Mencken employs to dismantle the character and competence of the U.S. Congressman.

Key Concepts and Core Themes

The Residency Requirement and Structural Mediocrity

Mencken’s central argument is that the mediocrity of American legislators is not accidental but structurally guaranteed by the U.S. Constitution.

  • Constitutional Basis: Article I, Sections 2 and 3 require members of the House and Senate to be inhabitants of the state they represent. By custom, House members must also reside within their specific districts.
  • The "Talent Pool" Argument: Mencken argues that these requirements confine each district to the limited talent available within its own borders. He contrasts this with the British system (at least as it existed prior to the 1920s), which allowed constituencies to choose distinguished figures from anywhere in the nation.
  • Resultant Parochialism: The American rule ensures that representatives are often "resident imbeciles" or "puerile local politicians" rather than national statesmen.

The Rhetorical Shift: From Analysis to Invective

Mencken employs a calculated shift in tone to establish credibility before launching into satire.

  • The Mask of Objectivity: He begins with a measured, constitutional tone, citing specific articles and sections of the Constitution to earn intellectual authority.
  • The Shift to Satire: Once the structural thesis is established, Mencken abandons objectivity for a "vocabulary of contempt." This shift signals his belief that the evidence of legislative incompetence is so overwhelming that dispassionate argument is no longer sufficient.

The Use of Internal Indictment

To solidify his critique, Mencken utilizes an anecdote involving Senator Reed of Missouri. By quoting a sitting Senator who describes his own colleagues as "poltroon souls aquiver with apprehension," Mencken transforms his external criticism into an internal confession, suggesting that even those within the system recognize its flaws.

Rhetorical Devices and Diction

The Two Registers of Language

Mencken’s comedy and impact derive from the collision of two distinct linguistic registers:

Register Examples Purpose
Legalistic/Constitutional "Article I, Sections 2 and 3," "electoral unit," "legislative bodies" Mimics the dignity of civics textbooks to create a mock-serious atmosphere.
Abusive/Invective "Puerile," "imbeciles," "jackasses," "noodles," "vermin" Strips away the "costume" of government to reveal the perceived reality of the individuals involved.

The Catalogue as a Weapon

Mencken frequently uses enumeration or cataloguing—the deliberate listing of items or examples—to overwhelm the reader.

  • The Career Path Catalogue: He describes the typical Southern Congressman’s ascent from a "hedge school" to "native swamps" and finally to the "dignity of county superintendent."
  • The Effect: This relentless accumulation of mediocre milestones creates the impression of exhaustive knowledge. It suggests that all congressional biographies are identical in their insignificance, making the argument feel unanswerable.

Animal and Fraternal Metaphors

Mencken reduces the status of legislators through specific imagery:

  • Zoological Metaphors: Terms like "jackasses" and "vermin" suggest legislators are either brainless beasts of burden or pestilential creatures.
  • The "Knights of Zoroaster": Mencken invents this farcical fraternal order to pair with the real-world "Kleagle of the Ku Klux." By placing the Congressman between a sinister recruiter and a ridiculous "grand worthy bow-wow," Mencken suggests that democratic politics is a mix of mob bigotry and absurd nonsense.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to Mencken, how does Article I of the Constitution contribute to the "shallowness" of the talent pool in Congress?
    • Answer: It requires representatives to be inhabitants of the states (and by custom, the districts) they represent, preventing districts from seeking higher-quality candidates from the rest of the nation.
  2. What is the specific rhetorical purpose of Mencken’s shift from a "measured" tone to the "vocabulary of contempt"?
    • Answer: It establishes initial intellectual credibility through structural analysis before signaling that the evidence of mediocrity is so overwhelming that only satire and invective are appropriate responses.
  3. Explain the significance of the term "below the salt" in the context of Mencken’s writing.
    • Answer: It is a medieval reference to social hierarchy; Mencken uses it to apply a feudal class distinction to the democratic officials in Washington, suggesting their inferior rank/character.
  4. How does Mencken use the Latin abbreviation "i.e." for satirical effect?
    • Answer: He uses it to perform a "mock translation," pretending to clarify a technical term (congressional junket) while actually delivering an insult (a drunken holiday at government expense).
  5. What syntactic technique does Mencken use to deliver the "verdict" after a long, descriptive passage?
    • Answer: He follows long, elaborate cataloguing sentences with short, terse, and often monosyllabic sentences to provide a "hammering force."

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Architecture of Contempt: Analyze how Mencken balances structural political science arguments with personal satire. Does his focus on the "residency requirement" make his insults more persuasive, or does the vitriol undermine his constitutional analysis?
  2. The Power of the Catalogue: Discuss Mencken’s use of enumeration as a rhetorical strategy. Using the example of the Southern Congressman’s career path, explain how the rhythm and accumulation of detail function to "bury" the reader under the weight of the author's evidence.
  3. The Collision of Registers: Examine the effect of mixing legalistic terminology with the language of abuse. How does this contrast reinforce Mencken’s theme that the "dignified language of constitutional government is a costume worn by clowns"?
  4. Internal vs. External Critique: Evaluate Mencken's use of Senator Reed’s quotation. How does the inclusion of a "sitting Senator" change the rhetorical situation of the text, and why is this an effective tool for a satirist?

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Below the Salt A phrase referring to those of inferior rank (historically, those seated further from the salt cellar at a medieval table).
Catalogue (Enumeration) A rhetorical device involving the deliberate listing of items, qualities, or examples in a series to create an impression of abundance or absurdity.
Coup de Grâce French for "stroke of mercy"; the final blow that ends a contest or settles an argument.
Jackasses An animal metaphor used by Mencken to suggest legislators are stubborn and brainless beasts of burden.
Kleagle A recruiter for the Ku Klux Klan; Mencken uses the term to link democratic politics to mob bigotry.
Knights of Zoroaster An invented, farcical fraternal order created by Mencken to mock the grandiose and absurd titles of political figures.
Noodles Archaic slang for simpletons; used for its comic sound to reinforce contempt.
Puerile Childish or immature; suggesting an inability to think with adult complexity.
Residency Requirement The constitutional mandate (Article I) that a representative live in the state they represent; viewed by Mencken as a guarantee of parochialism.
Vermin Small animals/insects that are harmful or pestilential; Mencken’s strongest term for legislators, suggesting they are a pestilence on the state.
