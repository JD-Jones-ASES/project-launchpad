Study Guide: H.L. Mencken’s "Notes on Democracy" – Section IV.2: Last Words

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the final section of H.L. Mencken’s Notes on Democracy. It examines the text’s central arguments regarding the psychological appeal of democracy, its inherent paradoxes, and the author’s perspective on the democratic system as a form of public entertainment.

Key Concepts and Themes

The Appeal of Falsehood

Mencken posits that democracy is "charming" because it is built upon propositions that are "palpably not true." He argues that humanity possesses an "incurable romanticism" that prefers comforting illusions over the harsh finality of truth. The central "ancient promise" of democracy is the idea that the lowly shall inherit the earth—a concept Mencken identifies as being shared by Christianity, though democracy gives it higher authority and an appearance of objective truth.

The Psychology of the "Mob Man"

Democracy provides the common citizen, or "mob man," with several psychological benefits:

  • A Sense of Power: It makes the citizen feel as though they are "genuinely running things," a feeling Mencken compares to the satisfaction felt by archbishops or police sergeants.
  • The Illusion of Wisdom: It convinces the citizen that their views are taken seriously by their "betters."
  • Consciousness of Duty: It provides a sense of "high duty triumphantly done."

The Spectator vs. The Democrat

Mencken distinguishes between the participant in democracy and the objective observer:

  • The Democrat: Suffers from the "naïve delusion" that happiness is achieved by taking it from others. This quest for happiness inevitably ends in disaster and unhappiness.
  • The Spectator: Finds democracy to be a "show of the first cut and calibre." To the observer, the "fraud" and "obvious imbecilities" of democracy are a source of high entertainment and "exhilarating" spectacle.

The Self-Devouring Nature of Government

Mencken characterizes democracy as a "self-limiting" and "self-devouring" disease. He points to several contradictions in its practice:

  • Abandonment of Philosophy: Under national strain, democratic leaders (such as Lincoln, Roosevelt, and Wilson) frequently convert themselves into "despots" to protect national safety.
  • War on Liberty: While liberty is the theoretical cornerstone of democracy, the system often jails citizens for exercising it, such as reading the Bill of Rights.
  • Institutional Paradoxes: The system claims a "government of laws," yet sets men on benches to decide what those laws mean; it exalts the citizen's service to the state while simultaneously assuming the citizen is dishonorable.

Rascality and Civilization

Mencken suggests that the inherent "rascality," wastefulness, and dishonesty found in democracy are common to all forms of government. He concludes that civilization itself may be a "colossal swindle," and that "rascality" might be a necessary component of human governance.

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition in Context
Archangel Bryan A reference to William Jennings Bryan, cited as a typical "democratic prophet."
Bill of Rights The foundational legal document of the U.S.; Mencken notes the irony of citizens being jailed for reading it.
Dunderhead A slow-witted person; one of the types of individuals Mencken claims democracy exalts.
Lowly shall inherit the earth The "ancient promise" Mencken identifies as the core psychological foundation of both Christianity and democracy.
Mob man The average citizen functioning within a democratic system, characterized by "maudlin herding" and a false sense of importance.
Mountebank A person who deceives others; used to describe the rogues followed by the democratic mob.
Psychologist The role Mencken adopts when arguing that the "fraud" of democracy is inherently amusing.
Self-devouring A term used to describe democracy’s tendency to abandon its own philosophy and "kill the thing it theoretically loves."
Trimmer A person who adapts their views for political or personal gain; one of the classes of people Mencken observes rising to power in a democracy.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why does Mencken argue that democracy is "charming"?

    • Answer: He believes it is charming because it is based on false propositions that are more fascinating and satisfying to the majority of people than the harshness of truth.

  2. According to the text, what is the "ancient promise" at the bottom of both Christianity and democracy?

    • Answer: The promise that "the lowly shall inherit the earth."

  3. How does Mencken describe the behavior of democratic leaders during times of national menace?

    • Answer: He states they convert themselves into "despots of an almost fabulous ferocity," abandoning their democratic philosophy.

  4. Which three U.S. Presidents does Mencken explicitly name as examples of democratic leaders who became despots under strain?

    • Answer: Lincoln, Roosevelt, and Wilson.

  5. What irony does Mencken find in the democratic "quest for happiness"?

    • Answer: He argues that the quest for happiness, as pursued through the delusion of taking from others, ultimately brings only unhappiness.

  6. What does Mencken claim is the "corner-stone" of democracy's political metaphysic?

    • Answer: Liberty.

  7. Why does Mencken say he "enjoys" democracy?

    • Answer: Because he finds it "incomparably idiotic" and "incomparably amusing" as a spectator watching a "welter of fraud."

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Paradox of Liberty: Mencken describes the spectacle of Americans jailed for reading the Bill of Rights as "gaudily humorous." Analyze the text’s argument regarding the contradictions between democratic theory (liberty) and democratic practice (policing and law enforcement).

  2. The Spectator’s Malice: Mencken admits to being a "somewhat malicious man" who finds the "debauching" of the "suckers" (citizens) exhilarating. Discuss how Mencken’s perspective as an outside observer shapes his critique of democratic morality.

  3. Civilization as a Swindle: At the conclusion of the text, Mencken suggests that "rascality is necessary to human government" and that civilization may be a "colossal swindle." Explore how this cynical view justifies his tolerance for democratic corruption and waste.

  4. Democracy vs. Religion: Compare and contrast Mencken’s treatment of democracy and Christianity. How does he link their psychological appeals and their "transparently false" promises?
