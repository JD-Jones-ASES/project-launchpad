Study Guide: H.L. Mencken and the American Mercury

This study guide examines the rise and fall of The American Mercury, its editor H.L. Mencken, and the magazine's role as a cultural touchstone during the American Jazz Age.

I. Key Concepts and Historical Context

The Evolution of Mencken’s Editorial Influence

Before the launch of The American Mercury, H.L. Mencken and George Jean Nathan co-edited The Smart Set (1914–1923). While The Smart Set began as a society magazine, Mencken and Nathan transformed it into a platform for iconoclastic literary criticism and new American fiction. Seeking a bolder, more sophisticated vehicle for his worldview, Mencken eventually partnered with publisher Alfred A. Knopf to launch The American Mercury in 1924.

The Ethos of The American Mercury

The American Mercury was characterized by its distinctive green cover and its appeal to a specific demographic: the "educated skeptic."

  • Target Audience: College students, young professionals, and those who wished to distance themselves from small-town values and traditional "pieties."
  • Editorial Stance: The magazine provided readers with "permission to feel superior" to the masses. Mencken used his monthly editorials to attack a consistent set of targets, including:
    • Puritanism and evangelical religion.
    • Prohibition and censorship.
    • "Democratic mediocrity" and the common man.
    • Academic pomposity.

The Jazz Age Backdrop

The 1920s were a period of intense volatility and culture war. Mencken’s work was fueled by the following historical tensions:

  • Legislative Shifts: The 18th Amendment (Prohibition) and the 19th Amendment (Women’s Suffrage).
  • Social Conflict: The massive revival of the Ku Klux Klan and the 1925 Scopes Trial, which highlighted the clash between science and fundamentalism.
  • Technological Change: The rapid adoption of radio, cinema, and the automobile.

The Impact of the Great Depression

The stock market crash of 1929 and the subsequent Great Depression rendered Mencken’s "aristocratic scorn" for the common man obsolete. As Americans faced bread lines and home foreclosures, mocking the "mediocrity" of the public was viewed as obscene rather than entertaining. By 1933, Mencken resigned as editor, and the libertarian skepticism of the Mercury was largely replaced by the New Deal liberalism of Franklin Roosevelt.

II. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Alfred A. Knopf The publisher who launched The American Mercury in January 1924.
George Jean Nathan An American drama critic and co-editor with Mencken at The Smart Set and The American Mercury; known for his taste for European culture and irreverence.
Iconoclastic Characterized by the attack on settled beliefs or institutions; a primary trait of Mencken’s literary criticism.
New Deal Liberalism The political and intellectual movement associated with Franklin Roosevelt that displaced Mencken’s influence in the 1930s.
Prohibition The era established by the 18th Amendment banning the sale and manufacture of alcohol, which Mencken frequently attacked.
The American Language A philological work that Mencken focused on in his later years after resigning from The American Mercury.
The Smart Set A literary magazine (1900–1930) that served as the precursor to The American Mercury.

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Rhetoric of Superiority

Analyze how H.L. Mencken used The American Mercury to create a sense of intellectual hierarchy in the 1920s. How did his attacks on "Sunday school, Rotary Club meetings, and the pieties of the Saturday Evening Post" help define the identity of the "educated skeptic"?

2. Literature in a Culture War

The source text states that Mencken was "writing in a culture war." Discuss how the social upheavals of the Jazz Age—specifically Prohibition, the revival of the KKK, and the Scopes Trial—provided the necessary "volatile landscape" for The American Mercury to become a sensation.

3. The Limits of Iconoclasm

Compare the cultural reception of Mencken's "aristocratic scorn" during the mid-1920s to its reception during the early years of the Great Depression. Why did the same editorial tone that made him the "most famous journalist in America" eventually come to be seen as "obscene" and "cruel"?
