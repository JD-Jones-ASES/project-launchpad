# Politics Under Democracy: A Comprehensive Study Guide

This study guide examines H.L. Mencken’s critique of democratic political machinery, focusing on the psychological drivers of the electorate and the tactics employed by democratic leaders. Based on the provided text, it explores the themes of fear-based governance, the role of the demagogue, and the historical application of these principles in the United States.

---

## Key Concepts and Themes

### 1. Government by Fear
Mencken posits that democracy is essentially "government by fear." Rather than being driven by reasoned policy or constructive goals, the political process is an "endless parade of invented monsters" pursued by statesmen. The primary function of politics in a democratic state is the "discovery, chase and scotching of bugaboos"—imaginary objects of fear used to alarm the public.

### 2. The Role of the Demagogue
Demagogues are described as "professors of mob psychology." Their power is derived from their ability to identify and exploit the fears and prejudices of the "mob" rather than through rational argument. In Mencken’s view, the demagogue makes fear the "corner-stone" of their political science.

### 3. The Statesman as "Witch-Hunter"
In a democratic framework, the statesman's role is reduced to that of a "mere witch-hunter" or a "glorified smeller and snooper." Their primary activity is to maintain a state of alarm, perpetually searching for new threats to keep the electorate in a state of agitation.

### 4. Negative Voting and the Preference for Mediocrity
A central observation in the text is that the democratic electorate does not vote *for* candidates or programs, but always *against* perceived threats. This leads to two specific outcomes:
*   **Rejection of Imagination:** Leaders with genuine imagination and ability are "shining marks" and easily targeted by demagogues.
*   **Election of Mediocrities:** "Colourless mediocrities" are preferred by the mob because they do not inspire fear.

### 5. The Failure of Constructive Programs
The text argues that constructive, technical programs are "highly technical" and "unintelligible" to the majority. Because the mob cannot understand these programs, they view them with suspicion. Mencken cites William Jennings Bryan as an example: Bryan was successful when chasing the "bugaboo of plutocracy" but failed when he attempted to advocate for a constructive political economy.

### 6. War and Mob Psychology
War represents democratic processes in their most "naked" form. The text details how the American public was moved from isolationism to war-readiness through the strategic manipulation of fear:
*   **Substitution of Fear:** To enter World War I, leaders had to replace the fear of danger with a more powerful fear: the threat of a victorious, vengeful Germany.
*   **Official Propaganda:** Government agencies and newspapers were used to spread "dreadful surmises," such as attributing industrial accidents to foreign agents and emphasizing the danger to American women and infants.
*   **The Heroic Mood:** The implementation of the draft allowed those not serving (the "John Does") to develop an "altruistic eagerness" for war, as they could appear bold and heroic without personal risk.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **According to Mencken, what is the "corner-stone" of the science practiced by demagogues?**
    *   *Answer:* Fear is the cornerstone of their science, as they use mob psychology to appeal to popular desires and prejudices.

2.  **Why does the text claim that "plain people" prefer "colourless mediocrities" for office?**
    *   *Answer:* Mediocrities are preferred because it is impossible for the public to fear them, whereas imaginative leaders are easy targets for demagogues to bring down.

3.  **What was the "incredible folly" committed by William Jennings Bryan in 1896?**
    *   *Answer:* He threw his energies into advocating for a constructive, highly technical program that was unintelligible to the mob, causing them to view him with suspicion and fear.

4.  **How did the U.S. government shift public opinion regarding entry into World War I?**
    *   *Answer:* By substituting the fear of entering the war with a greater fear of a victorious Germany seeking revenge for American violations of neutrality.

5.  **What role did the Department of Justice play during the lead-up to World War I?**
    *   *Answer:* The Department of Justice ascribed various domestic fires, floods, and industrial accidents to the work of German agents to stoke public alarm.

6.  **According to the text, why did the draft make the general public more "heroic" regarding the war?**
    *   *Answer:* The draft relieved the majority of the obligation to fight, allowing those safe at home to adopt a bold, "truculent front" and support frontal attacks without personal consequence.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Mechanics of Fear:** Analyze Mencken’s assertion that democracy is "government by fear." Using examples from the text, discuss how the "discovery, chase and scotching of bugaboos" serves as a substitute for constructive governance.

2.  **The Psychology of the Electorate:** Explore the concept that democratic citizens vote "against something" rather than "for anything." How does this psychological tendency shape the type of leaders who are successful in a democratic system, and what are the consequences for national progress?

3.  **The Evolution of the Bugaboo:** The text provides a long list of historical "monsters" from American history. Select three examples from the text and explain how they illustrate the transition from one national fear to the next, as described in the "complete chronicle of the Republic."

4.  **The Demagogue vs. The Statesman:** Contrast Mencken’s definition of a "demagogue" with his description of a "statesman of genuine imagination." Why does the democratic environment inherently favor the former and punish the latter?

## Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Bolshevism:** A radical revolutionary ideology mentioned as one of the "imaginary monsters" pursued in American history.
*   **Bugaboo:** An imaginary object of fear; a source of needless alarm used by politicians to distract or motivate the public.
*   **Demagogue:** A political leader who gains power by appealing to popular desires and prejudices rather than by reasoned argument; a "professor of mob psychology."
*   **Hyphenates:** A term used in the text to describe a group (likely Americans of dual nationality) targeted as a "bugaboo" during times of national tension.
*   **Mob Psychology:** The study or exploitation of the collective emotions and prejudices of the general public, used by demagogues to maintain control.
*   **Plutocracy:** Government by the wealthy; rule by a rich elite.
*   **Scotching:** To put an end to something; used in the text to describe the act of "defeating" a manufactured political threat.
*   **Venal:** Susceptible to bribery; corrupt.
*   **Witch-hunter:** A metaphor for a democratic statesman who maintains power by identifying and pursuing perceived internal or external enemies.
