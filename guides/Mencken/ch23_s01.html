Study Guide: Corruption Under Democracy

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the analysis of H.L. Mencken’s "Corruption Under Democracy" (Chapter 23, Section 1). It explores Mencken’s central thesis that corruption is the fundamental operating principle of democratic governance rather than a peripheral defect.

Key Concepts and Core Arguments

1. Democracy as Wholesale Bribery

The central argument of this section is that democracy is not a system of high ideals but a structural form of bribery. Mencken asserts that any government answerable to "the mob" must inevitably secure support through various forms of payoffs. These bribes manifest in several ways:

  • Political Appointments: Buying labor leaders with offices.
  • Targeted Legislation: Buying the "dupes" of labor leaders or specific interest groups (Methodists, Catholics, farmers, manufacturers) with laws that tilt the scales of justice in their favor.

2. The Relationship Between Capitalism and Democracy

Mencken offers a "Nietzschean" analysis of power, suggesting that the plutocracy has mastered democratic institutions so thoroughly that they have rendered revolution impossible. In this view:

  • The "herd" (the common people) is not merely a victim of exploitation but is actively complicit in it.
  • Corruption is rational from the perspective of the mob, as they participate in the bargaining and haggling of the political marketplace.

3. The Industrialization of Public Opinion

Mencken concludes his analysis with a devastating industrial metaphor regarding how public opinion is formed.

  • Raw Material: Public opinion begins as the "raw" and "immemorial" fears of the mob.
  • Processing: These fears are "piped" to "central factories" where they are "flavored and colored."
  • Standardization: The final product is "put into cans," suggesting that democratic thought is mass-produced, artificial, and stripped of individual agency.

4. Rhetorical Strategy and Tone

Mencken utilizes a "worldly-wise" persona, shifting between three distinct registers to persuade his audience:

  • The Clinical Register: Uses the blunt, simple logic of a political scientist to state his thesis.
  • The Historian Register: Marshals evidence from figures such as Friedrich von Gentz, Tocqueville, and Henry Adams to provide intellectual authority.
  • The Satirist Register: Uses vivid, often low-brow imagery to make his arguments memorable and felt by the reader.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to the text, why does Mencken believe democracy will never rise against bribery?

Answer: Because democracy itself is a form of wholesale bribery; it is the essential operating principle of the system, not a disease within it.

2. What historical examples does Mencken use to build his case that democratic corruption is universal?

Answer: He cites Friedrich von Gentz, Tocqueville, Henry Adams, and the American administrations of Grant and Wilson.

3. Explain Mencken’s use of the term "prehensile" in describing the mob.

Answer: He uses it to suggest an animal-like greed, comparing the various minorities within the mob to creatures with tails capable of reaching and clutching for their own interests.

4. What is the significance of the "Whiskey Anecdote"?

Answer: It serves as a parable for the democratic experiment. The citizen anticipates the "whiskey" (self-government) but finds it is a fraudulent compound of "pepper, prune juice and wood alcohol," representing the reality of political corruption.

5. How does Mencken use the dash (—) as a syntactic tool?

Answer: He uses dashes to insert "stage whispers" or devastating asides that interrupt the main sentence to deliver a sharp judgment that might otherwise be too blunt for the main clause.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Lexicon of Criminality and Commerce

Mencken intentionally avoids idealistic political language, opting instead for words like rooked, looted, swindled, and bargaining. Analyze how this choice of diction strips the "idealistic vocabulary" from democracy. How does framing the citizen as a "customer" or a "mark" change the reader's understanding of the social contract?

2. The Mechanics of Anaphora

Mencken frequently uses anaphora—the repetition of phrases at the beginning of successive clauses—to build rhetorical momentum. Compare Mencken’s use of this device with traditional oratory (such as Martin Luther King Jr.). In your essay, argue whether Mencken’s repetition serves to inspire the reader or to overwhelm them with an "endless, exhausting parade" of evidence.

3. The Industrial Metaphor of the Mob

Examine the closing image of public opinion being "flavored, colored, and put into cans." How does this metaphor interact with Mencken's view of human nature? Discuss the implications of describing public opinion as something "manufactured" rather than "reasoned," and how this reflects his stance on the possibility of true self-government.

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition Context in Text
Anaphora The repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses. Used to create a "drumbeat effect" to hammer home the indictment of democratic deal-making.
Conies Rabbits; by extension, dupes or gulls. Used to describe the victims of political confidence tricks, echoing Elizabethan literature.
Coup de grâce A final, decisive blow that ends suffering. Refers to Mencken’s final industrial metaphor that seals his argument against democracy.
Friedrich von Gentz (1764–1832) A German diplomat and secretary to Prince Metternich. A historical source used by Mencken to lend respectability to his disillusionment with democracy.
Prehensile Capable of grasping (literally, like a monkey's tail). Used to describe the animalistic greed of the minorities that make up the mob.
Rooked Cheated or swindled. A colloquial term used to juxtapose the "low conduct" of politicians with their "high office."
Specialists in Rectitude An ironic term for those who exposed the Harding scandals. Used to frame honesty as a "social disability" in a corrupt democratic environment.
Tergiversations Evasions or shifts of allegiance; turning one's back on a position. A Latinate term used to elevate the accusation of political dishonesty to a scholarly level.
