Study Guide: Analysis of Mencken’s "Where Puritanism Fails"

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the themes, legal critiques, and social observations presented in the provided text, which examines the intersection of Puritanical values, democratic governance, and the American legal system.

Key Concepts and Thematic Analysis

1. The Weaponization of Law and Police Power

In a democratic society influenced by "fanaticism," the law is no longer a neutral arbiter but a tool used against unpopular citizens. The text argues that the police power in some American states (specifically California and Pennsylvania) has expanded to surpass the authority of ancient oriental monarchies. This expansion is built upon the foundational assumption that every citizen is a potential "traitor, libertine, and scoundrel."

2. The Persecution of Minority Thought

The legal system focuses its "awful powers" selectively. While influential politicians and hypocrites remain unmolested, those in the "unpopular minority"—particularly those who advocate for the Bill of Rights—are targeted for persecution. The process of legal attack often follows a specific pattern:

  • Targeting: An unpopular individual is singled out.
  • Fabrication: Police, prosecutors, and courts collaborate to find a statute the individual might have broken, aided by a multiplicity of "sumptuary and inquisitorial" laws.
  • Escalation: A case may begin with a vague charge like "disturbing the peace" and end with crimes carrying "staggering penalties."

3. The Erosion of Constitutional Protections

The text highlights a systematic abandonment of legal safeguards, including:

  • Common Law Rules: Protections against double jeopardy and hearsay are frequently suspended to ensure the conviction of "heretics."
  • Specific Legislation: The Volstead Act is cited as a prime example of legislation that destroys the right to a jury trial and violates prohibitions against unreasonable searches and seizures.
  • Inquisitorial Statutes: Certain states, notably California, possess laws that essentially criminalize "unpopular thoughts."

4. Judicial and Prosecutorial Bias

A significant portion of the American judiciary consists of former prosecuting attorneys who bring a "prosecutorial mind" to the bench. They often operate under the assumption that an accused person is ipso facto guilty. In this environment, a prosecutor’s success—and their eventual promotion to the bench—is determined by their "ferocity" and "extravagance of attack."

5. The Conflict Between "Common Decency" and Democracy

"Common decency" is defined as the habit of viewing others with tolerance and charity. The text asserts that the "democrat" (viewed as a "Puritan doubly damned") is incapable of this decency. The democratic impulse to force others into a "dull and docile way of thinking" leads to:

  • Cultural Collapse: Self-respecting, well-informed individuals flee regions (such as New England or the Tennessee hinterland) where democratic Puritanism is most dominant to avoid harassment and "lynching" by the "moron" majority.
  • Loss of Magnanimity: The Puritan prioritizes the "letter of the law" over kindness or forgiveness.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to the text, how does the democratic law view the nature of the average citizen?

    • Answer: It views every citizen as naturally being a "traitor, a libertine, and a scoundrel."

  2. Which two states are specifically mentioned as places where citizens have "no rights that the police are bound to respect"?

    • Answer: California and Pennsylvania.

  3. What role does the "mob" play in the prosecution of legal cases?

    • Answer: The mob applauds the "show" of the prosecution, which discourages judges and attorneys from enforcing constitutional guarantees.

  4. What was Bishop Luther B. Wilson’s stance on atheism and free speech?

    • Answer: He argued that atheism is treasonous to the state and should not be defended by "specious" claims of immunity under constitutional guarantees of free speech.

  5. How did Attorney General Stewart respond to the defense's protest during the Scopes trial?

    • Answer: He stated candidly that an atheist had no right to a fair trial in Tennessee.

  6. Why does the text claim Americans are "probably the least happy in Christendom" despite material wealth?

    • Answer: Because of a lack of mutual trust. Citizens are constantly watched and judged by neighbors and religious figures, preventing the "common decency" and social ease necessary for happiness.

  7. What historical legal figure is used as an example of the "harsh Puritan compulsion" to prioritize the letter of the law over magnanimity?

    • Answer: "The late Dr. Wilson," regarding the imprisonment of "poor old silly Debs."

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Evolution of the Prosecutor: Analyze the text’s claim that the American legal system encourages "ferocity" in prosecutors. Discuss how the transition from prosecutor to judge affects the impartiality of the court and the presumption of innocence.
  2. The Paradox of Prosperity and Happiness: Explore the argument that material success (e.g., "money in the bank and a Ford in the garage") is insufficient for a charming life. How does the "Puritanical" surveillance of one's neighbors, as described in the text, destroy the social fabric required for genuine happiness?
  3. Institutionalized Heresy: Examine the examples of Bishop Wilson and the Scopes trial. How does the text use these instances to argue that certain classes of people—atheists, Catholics, and others—are effectively "proscribed" and denied their constitutional rights in various American regions?

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition from Context
Common Decency The habit of viewing the acts and ideas of others with tolerance and charity; the basis of being a reliable friend and good citizen.
Inquisitorial Statutes Laws designed for intensive questioning or investigation into a citizen's private thoughts or behaviors.
Ipso Facto "By the fact itself"; used here to describe the judicial assumption that an accused person is automatically guilty.
Magnanimity The impulse to be kindly, generous, and forgiving toward others; an attribute the text claims a Puritan is "constitutionally unable to grasp."
Polizei A term used to refer to the police force or the exercise of police power, particularly in a repressive or overreaching manner.
Proscribed Forbidden or denounced by the law; used to describe classes of people (like atheists or specific minorities) who are denied legal standing.
Specious Superficially plausible but actually wrong; misleading in appearance.
Sumptuary Laws Statutes that attempt to regulate consumption or behavior based on moral or religious grounds.
Volstead Act A specific law cited as habitually violating constitutional prohibitions against unreasonable searches and the right to a jury trial.
