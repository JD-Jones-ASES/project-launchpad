Analysis of Puritanism and Democracy in the American Legal System: A Study Guide

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the critique regarding the intersection of Puritanism, democratic governance, and the American judicial system. It explores the erosion of constitutional rights, the weaponization of the law against unpopular minorities, and the resulting cultural and psychological impacts on society.

Core Concepts and Themes

1. The Weaponization of Democratic Law

Under the influence of fanaticism, democratic law operates on the assumption that the average citizen is inherently prone to evil. This justifies an expansion of police power that, in some states like California and Pennsylvania, exceeds the authority found in ancient oriental monarchies. This power is not applied universally but is used selectively against "unpopular minorities," particularly those who advocate for the Bill of Rights.

2. The Mechanics of Legal Persecution

The legal system utilizes a "multiplicity of sumptuary and inquisitorial statutes" to target specific individuals. The process typically follows a specific pattern:

  • Targeting: An unpopular individual is singled out.
  • Discovery: Authorities search for a law the individual might have broken.
  • Escalation: The accused begins with vague charges (e.g., disorderly conduct) and ends with crimes carrying heavy penalties.
  • Suspension of Rights: Common Law safeguards—such as protections against hearsay and double jeopardy—are often formally or habitually suspended in cases of "heresy."

3. Judicial Bias and the "Prosecutorial Mindset"

A significant portion of American judges are former prosecuting attorneys. This professional background often results in a bench that views the accused as ipso facto guilty. In this environment, the mention of constitutional rights by a political or religious "heretic" is viewed by the court as a form of blasphemy.

4. The Cultural Exodus

The text argues that the "Puritan ethic" and "democratic epistemology" create an environment hostile to well-informed and self-respecting individuals. In regions where these values dominate—such as the Tennessee hinterland—intellectuals and men of "sound sense" feel unsafe and lonesome. This leads to a "cultural collapse" as these individuals migrate to escape harassment, leaving behind a "cultural backwardness" in parts of New England, the South, and the Middle West.

5. The Erosion of Common Decency

"Common decency" is defined as the habit of viewing others with tolerance and charity. The text posits that the democrat/Puritan is unable to practice this, as they feel a paramount duty to save others from "sin" rather than allowing them to be happy in their own way. This lack of magnanimity and mutual trust results in a society that is materially prosperous but psychologically unhappy.

Key Legal and Historical References

Reference Description
The Volstead Act Cited as a statute that destroys the right to a jury trial and leads to habitual violations of constitutional protections against unreasonable search and seizure.
The Scopes Trial A trial in Dayton, Tennessee, where the court and prosecution (Attorney General Stewart and Judge Raulston) explicitly stated that an atheist had no right to a fair trial.
The Bill of Rights Described as a document that, if defended or mentioned in certain states like Pennsylvania, can lead to the advocate being clubbed or jailed.
Eugene Debs A "misguided and harmless old man" whose imprisonment was maintained by Woodrow Wilson, illustrating the Puritan preference for the "letter of the law" over magnanimity.
Bishop Luther B. Wilson A high-ranking Methodist Episcopal leader who publicly argued that atheism is treason and should not be protected by constitutional guarantees of free speech.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does the text describe the relationship between a prosecuting attorney’s "ferocity" and their career advancement?

    • Answer: A prosecuting attorney’s success and promotion to higher office, including the judiciary, are directly tied to their level of passion and extravagance in attacking the accused.

  2. According to the text, why does the "mob" generally support the prosecution in trials of unpopular individuals?

    • Answer: The mob views the prosecution as "giving the show" and applauds the spectacle of the proceedings.

  3. What is the "democratic epistemology" mentioned in the text?

    • Answer: It refers to a way of thinking characterized by a dull, docile conformity and an eagerness to force that conformity onto others through oppression and unfairness.

  4. Why does the text claim that a Puritan is "constitutionally unable" to be magnanimous?

    • Answer: The Puritan believes their duty to save others from sin is more important than their duty to let others be happy; they value being "steadfast" or "just" over being decent or kind.

  5. What specific classes of people were cited as facing legal disabilities in different parts of the United States?

    • Answer: Atheists (Tennessee), Catholics and Negroes (The South), anti-Catholics (Massachusetts), and anti-Semites (New York).

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Selective Enforcement of Liberty: Analyze the text’s argument that "the man of influence" and the "adept hypocrite" are exempt from the police powers that crush unpopular minorities. How does this selective enforcement undermine the pretense of impartial democratic law?

  2. The Psychological Cost of Puritanism: Explore the assertion that Americans are "the least happy in Christendom" despite their material wealth. Focus on the role of mutual trust, "common decency," and the "Puritan compulsion" to monitor and judge neighbors.

  3. Judicial Continuity: Discuss the implications of the text’s claim that half of American judges carry "habits of mind acquired on the other side of the bar" into their judicial roles. How does this transition from prosecutor to judge affect the application of the Bill of Rights and Common Law?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Common Decency: The individual habit of viewing the acts and ideas of others with tolerance and charity; being a reliable friend and generous opponent.
  • Inquisitorial Statutes: Laws designed to explore a citizen's thoughts or private conduct for the purpose of uncovering "heresy" or unpopular ideas.
  • Ipso Facto: By the very fact or act; the text uses it to describe how judges assume an accused person is automatically guilty.
  • Magnanimity: The quality of being generous, forgiving, and kind, especially toward a rival or someone less powerful; a quality the text claims Puritans lack.
  • Polizei: A term used to refer to the police power or the police force, particularly in the context of its extension into an authoritative or oppressive role.
  • Specious: Something that is superficially plausible but actually wrong or misleading; used to describe arguments against granting atheists constitutional immunity.
  • Sumptuary Laws: Statutes that attempt to regulate personal behavior or consumption, used in the text as tools to simplify the legal persecution of individuals.
  • The Fathers: A reference to the founders of the United States, whose writings are described as being read "uncritically" by "romantic fanatics" who still believe in constitutional rights.
