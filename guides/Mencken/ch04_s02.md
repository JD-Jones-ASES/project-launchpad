# Study Guide: Mencken’s "The New Psychology" and the Democratic Mind

This study guide explores the psychological and sociological arguments presented in the provided text regarding the nature of the "common man," the limitations of democracy, and the behavioral foundations of human emotion and intellect.

---

## Key Concepts and Summary

### 1. The Theory of Arrested Development
The text argues that human beings are not mentally or qualitatively equal. Instead of a uniform capacity for growth, most individuals experience "arrested development." While "superior men" continue to develop independence and intelligence, the majority of the population reaches a mental plateau around the age of puberty. Consequently, the "mob" or the "common man" operates with the ideas, emotions, and logic of an adolescent.

### 2. The Metaphor of the Greased Pole
Mencken uses the image of "greased poles" to describe human achievement and social hierarchy:
*   **The Ascent:** Every soul attempts to climb toward "gaudy and seductive flags" representing success or enlightenment.
*   **The Majority:** Most people find the climb too agonizing and give up early, settling for easier comforts like "Golf," "Rotary," or "Fundamentalism."
*   **Governance:** In an aristocratic society, those at the top of the poles govern. In a democratic society, government is the function of those who have remained near the ground.

### 3. Public Opinion in a Democracy
Public opinion is defined not as rational thought, but as a "complex of prejudices." This complex is formed by the interaction of two primary emotions felt by the lower orders toward those above them:
*   **Bitterness:** Directed toward genuine superiority.
*   **Admiration:** Directed toward fraud or superficial success.

### 4. The Primacy of Fear
Drawing from behaviorist psychology, the text identifies fear as the earliest, deepest, and most profound human emotion. 
*   **Phylogenic Origin:** Fear is an inherited race experience. Dr. John B. Watson suggests it may stem from "arboreal man’s" ancestral hazards, such as falling from trees.
*   **Evolution of Fear:** While infants fear loud noises or loss of support, adults develop fears of "strange men," "strange ideas," and "powers and principalities of the air."

### 5. The Limits of Education
The central aim of a rational education system is to help individuals shed these "immemorial fears" and become serene and self-assured. However, the text asserts that this is impossible for the "lower orders" because:
*   They are "congenitally incapable" of intellectual progress.
*   They cannot grasp abstractions or bald facts.
*   They are imitative like "trained animals," appearing to learn while remaining entirely unresponsive to actual ideas.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **How does the text define the "superior man"?**
    The superior man is a rare individual characterized by genuine intelligence, taste, and independence—the type of person that democracy inevitably suppresses.

2.  **According to the text, what is the difference between an aristocratic and a democratic government?**
    In an aristocratic society, government is the function of those who have climbed high up the "greased poles" through prowess or inheritance. In a democracy, government is the function of the majority who have remained near the ground.

3.  **What does Dr. John B. Watson identify as the two primary fears of an infant?**
    An infant fears loud noises (which cause trembling) and the loss of physical support (which creates the sensation of falling).

4.  **What is the "disastrous fallacy" of old-time introspective psychology mentioned in the text?**
    The fallacy was viewing a child as simply a "little man" and focusing exclusively on the reflective mental processes of superior adults, rather than understanding the unique autonomy of the child mind.

5.  **What did Dr. Eleanor R. Wembridge discover regarding the "lower orders" of men?**
    She discovered that they are unable to grasp even the simplest abstractions and that words, beyond the most elemental, convey nothing to them. Their thinking is limited to primitive appetites and emotions.

6.  **Why does the text claim that "public opinion" maintains its main outlines despite "fresh delusions"?**
    Because it is a stable complex of prejudices rooted in the adolescent mental age of the mob, driven by a cycle of bitterness toward the superior and admiration for the fraudulent.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Psychology of the Mob:** Analyze Mencken’s use of behaviorism to critique democratic systems. How does the shift from "introspective psychology" to "the study of the child mind" provide him with a framework to dismiss the intellectual capacity of the voting public?

2.  **Education as Fear Management:** The text claims the ideal educated man is one who has "put away as foolish the immemorial fears of the race." Discuss the tension the author identifies between this goal and the "congenital" limitations he attributes to the majority of citizens.

3.  **The Greased Pole and Social Mobility:** Examine the metaphor of the "greased pole." According to the text, what forces determine how far a person climbs, and why does the author believe democracy empowers those who fail the climb?

## Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Arrested Development** | The psychological theory that mental growth in most humans stops at an adolescent stage, preventing them from reaching adult levels of reason. |
| **Arboreal Man** | A theoretical "ape-man" ancestor who lived in trees; used by Dr. Watson to explain the instinctive human fear of falling. |
| **Behaviorism** | A school of psychology (associated here with John B. Watson) that focuses on observable behaviors and physiological responses, such as fear, rather than introspection. |
| **Fundamentalism** | Used in the text as an example of an "easy" intellectual or social refuge for those who lack the stamina for genuine intellectual achievement. |
| **Phylogenic** | Relating to the evolutionary development and diversification of a species; used to describe fears that are inherited as "race experience." |
| **Public Opinion** | Under democracy, a stable complex of prejudices formed by the bitterness and admiration of the "mob" toward those above them. |
| **Superior Man** | Mencken’s Nietzschean term for a rare individual of genuine intelligence and independence who is distinct from the "lower orders." |
| **Yokel** | A derogatory term used to describe the uneducated common man, specifically in the context of their primitive reactions to pain or fear. |
