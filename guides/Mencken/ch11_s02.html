Study Guide: The Two Kinds of Democracy

This study guide provides a comprehensive analysis of the provided text, which examines the nuances and alleged distinctions between direct and representative democracy. Based on the critique in "The Two Kinds of Democracy," this document outlines the core arguments regarding the nature of the democratic citizen, the influence of political minorities, and the perceived futility of democratic reforms.

Key Concepts and Core Themes

The Illusion of Distinction

The text argues that the debate between representative government and direct democracy is largely academic and irrelevant. Whether a state is a constitutional monarchy, a representative republic, or a pure democracy, the fundamental power structure remains the same. In all these forms, sovereignty resides in the "mob," which acts as the ultimate judge of ideas and the source of power.

The Nature of the Democratic Citizen

The democratic citizen is characterized as having "credulity and imbecility" that often outweigh their simple desires. While theoretically the "boss" of the state, the average citizen is easily misled by "mountebanks" and "scoundrels" because they gravitate toward ideas they cherish and words they understand—often the rhetoric of "thieves."

The Role of Minorities and Rogues

Politics is described as a "battle of rival rogues." These "camorra of self-seeking minorities" aim to inflame, delude, and victimize the masses. Even when a minority is relatively honest or enlightened, their success is viewed as a "miracle" rather than a standard outcome of the political process.

The Failure of "Purer" Democracy

Liberal reformers often argue that the "cure for the evils of democracy is more democracy," suggesting tools like the recall, initiative, and referendum to turn representatives into mere "clerks." However, the text asserts that these measures fail because:

  • Mass Formulation is Impossible: Large groups cannot formulate complex measures en masse.
  • Historical Precedent: Even lauded institutions like the New England town-meeting were dominated by a few men of initiative—often demagogues and fanatics—rather than the collective wisdom of the folk.
  • Inherent Limitations: The general public remains "congenitally unable" to comprehend complex problems or consider them in an unbiased manner.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to the text, why is the specific form of government (monarchy vs. republic) of "small importance"?

    • Answer: Because in all forms, the "mob" is both actually and legally the sovereign power and the ultimate judge of ideas, and the process is always a battle between self-seeking minorities trying to influence that mob.

  2. What is the "Liberal" remedy for the perceived diseases of representative government?

    • Answer: The remedy is a resort to "purer democracy," involving the initiative, the referendum, and the recall, which aim to take power out of the hands of representatives and place it directly in the hands of the voters.

  3. How does the text use the analogy of "popular ballads" to explain the New England town-meeting?

    • Answer: It argues that just as popular ballads were written by concrete poets and only selected by the folk, the measures of the town-meeting were formulated by a few leaders, while the citizens merely acted as referees to decide which would survive.

  4. What two factors usually prevail over "reason" in the minds of the citizens during public discussion?

    • Answer: Prejudice and superstition (often driven by "fear of hell" or "blood" rather than logic or the Beatitudes).

  5. Why do agents or politicians gain more power than the average citizen?

    • Answer: Their position gives them the power of influencing electors ex officio. They often use this influence for their own ends and sell whatever influence remains.

  6. According to the text, how were the American people brought into the "late war"?

    • Answer: Despite being in an overwhelming majority against entering the war, they were "shoved into" it by professional politicians who were influenced by small, determined minorities with special knowledge and interests.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Myth of the Sagacious Citizen: Analyze the text’s argument that the "plain people" are congenitally unable to comprehend national policy. To what extent does the author believe that the failures of democracy are rooted in the biological or intellectual nature of the citizenry rather than the structure of the government?

  2. The "Rival Rogues" Framework: Discuss the text's portrayal of the political process as a "battle of rival rogues." How does this perspective challenge the traditional view of representative government as a system of checks and balances or a pursuit of the common good?

  3. Referees vs. Creators: The author posits that the "folk" never formulate ideas but only act as referees between ideas presented by others. Evaluate this claim using the example of the New England town-meeting provided in the text. Does this distinction effectively negate the value of direct democracy?

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Contextual Definition
Beatitudes References to supreme blessedness or religious teachings, noted as being less influential on the mob than "blood."
Camorra A group or "camorra of self-seeking minorities" that works to delude and victimize the public for its own interests.
Direct Democracy A system where the final determination of public questions is in the hands of the voters themselves via tools like the referendum.
Ex officio By virtue of one's office or status; used to describe the inherent power politicians gain through their positions.
Imbecility Stupidity or extreme foolishness; cited as a primary trait of the "lowly Christian" or democratic citizen.
Initiative/Referendum Political mechanisms that allow voters to propose or strike down laws, intended by Liberals to create a "purer" democracy.
Monomaniacs Individuals obsessed with a single idea; the text describes the leaders of New England town-meetings as such.
Mountebanks Deceptive people or swindlers who mislead the "credulous" public for political gain.
Philologues Students of language or literature; criticized in the text for wrongly believing that "folk" composed popular ballads.
Stall-fed Rousseaus A derogatory term for political theorists or "university pundits" who advocate for idealistic, Rousseau-style democratic reforms.
