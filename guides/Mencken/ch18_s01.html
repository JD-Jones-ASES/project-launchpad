Study Guide: The Rewards of Virtue (An Analysis of H.L. Mencken)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of H.L. Mencken’s rhetorical strategies and central arguments in "The Rewards of Virtue." It explores the structural obstacles facing the honest public servant, Mencken's transition from satirist to personal witness, and the specific linguistic tools used to critique democratic institutions.

I. Key Concepts and Central Arguments

1. The Paradox of Virtue in Democracy

The central thesis of the text is that under a democratic system, virtue is not merely ignored but actively punished. Mencken argues that the "mob" is incapable of comprehending disinterested public service because it cannot imagine any individual acting without self-interest. Consequently, the public assumes all officials are corrupt. This environment favors the "crook" over the honest man; while the crook understands the "game" of corruption, the honest man suffers under false imputations of sordid motives.

2. The Case of Senator James A. Reed

Senator James A. Reed (1861–1944) serves as the primary example of the "rare exception"—the honest, intelligent man in public life.

  • Defiance of the Mob: Reed was known for his fierce independence, opposing the League of Nations, Prohibition, and Progressive-era legislation.
  • Survival through Talent: Mencken notes that Reed survived in office despite defying his party and the mob because of his "amazing talents as a popular orator."
  • The Political Price: Despite his talents, Reed represents the "political price" paid by those who refuse to flatter the public.

3. Rhetorical Shift: From Satirist to Witness

Mencken employs a calculated rhetorical move by stepping out from behind his mask as a third-person satirist to offer first-person testimony.

  • Autobiographical Turn: He recounts personal experiences as a journalist, such as being accused of taking money from the "Medical Trust," the Vatican, brewers, and distillers.
  • Purpose: This shift transforms abstract political theory into "personal witness," using controlled indignation to highlight the impossibility of a critic being viewed as disinterested by the public.
  • The "Persona": Readers are cautioned that this self-portrait of an "honest critic persecuted by the mob" is itself a carefully crafted rhetorical construction.

4. Critique of American Journalism

Mencken characterizes the typical American editor as a figure of servility and intellectual hostility. He uses the editor as a symbol of aristocratic disdain, famously describing one as writing "a million words in favour of Coolidge and half a million against Darwin." This highlights a preference for mediocre political flattery over revolutionary scientific thought.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to Mencken, why is the "honest man" at a disadvantage compared to the "crook" in public life?

Answer: The mob assumes all public men are corrupt. The "actual crook" knows how to play the game of corruption, whereas the honest man suffers because his disinterested motives are misunderstood and treated as hidden self-interest.

2. What role does "Katzenjammer" play in Mencken's description of the post-World War era?

Answer: Mencken uses the German word (meaning "cat’s wailing" or a hangover) to describe the national mood as a collective "hangover from patriotic excess." Using a German word in the 1920s was also an act of defiance, as German culture was still suspect in American public life.

3. How does Mencken define the "eternal tragedy of democracy"?

Answer: It is the sincere yet performatively stated belief that democracy inherently punishes the good and rewards the expedient, driven by the mob's inability to imagine virtue.

4. What are the two stages of the legislative process described by Mencken, and who is responsible for each?

Answer: The two stages are invention and enactment. Laws are invented by "frauds and fanatics" and put upon the statute books (enacted) by "poltroons and scoundrels."

5. What is the significance of the "periodic sentence" in Mencken’s writing?

Answer: It is a sentence that withholds its main point or clause until the end, building suspense through subordinate clauses. Mencken uses it to deliver "devastating conclusions" after a rhythmic build-up.

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Architecture of Contempt: Analyze Mencken’s use of alliteration and parallel structure in his final verdict on the legislative process ("frauds and fanatics," "poltroons and scoundrels"). How do these linguistic choices contribute to the "judicial" tone of the passage?
  2. The Mask of the Witness: Discuss the shift from third-person satire to first-person autobiography in this section. Does this shift make Mencken's argument more persuasive, or does it merely replace one "rhetorical construction" with another? Use his accusations regarding the "Medical Trust" and the "Vatican" as evidence.
  3. The Epigram as Argument: Mencken claims that "a good epigram does the work of a paragraph." Evaluate his epigram regarding the American editor’s word counts for Coolidge versus Darwin. How does this single sentence encapsulate a broader critique of American intellectual life?
  4. The Character of the Mob: Based on the text, explain why Mencken believes the democratic "mob" is pathologically unable to recognize "rectitude." How does this perception of the public shape his view of the "rare exception" in politics?

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Comstocks Named after Anthony Comstock; used as a common noun to denote any moralistic censor.
Epigram A brief, witty, and memorable statement that compresses a complex idea into a single sentence.
Katzenjammer Literally "cat's wailing"; refers to a hangover or the miserable aftermath of excess (used for the post-war mood).
Periodic Sentence A sentence that withholds its main clause until the end to build suspense or emphasis.
Poltroon A spiritless coward; one of the types Mencken identifies as enacting laws.
Prehensile Capable of grasping; used by Mencken to describe the "grabbing" nature of corrupt public servants.
Rectitude Private moral uprightness; the quality Mencken argues is at odds with "political necessity."
Sordid Morally ignoble or base; the type of motives the mob attributes to honest men.
Timorousness Fearfulness or timidity; Mencken uses this to describe the entirety of American political culture.
