Study Guide: Disproportional Representation and the Democratic Will

This study guide examines the analysis of democratic machinery and the nature of representation as presented in the provided text. It explores the contradictions between the "one-man-one-vote" theory and the practical application of political power in the United States.

Core Concepts and Analysis

1. The Nature of Disproportional Representation

The text argues that disproportional representation—where votes in one community carry more weight than those in another—is not a failure of democracy but a deliberate mechanism to ensure its stability.

  • The Federal Model: The United States Senate is the primary example, where small states (e.g., Delaware, Nevada) possess the same voting power as large states (e.g., Pennsylvania, New York).
  • State-Level Disparity: This model is replicated in state legislatures, where upper houses are often organized by geography rather than population, leading to the under-representation of congested urban centers.
  • The "Make-Weight" Theory: Disproportionality serves as a counter-balance. Because urban voters act as a "compact mob" with shared, quickly formed opinions, rural voters (often termed "yokels" in the text) are given heavier representation to withstand "city stampedes."

2. The Myth of Constitutional Checks

The text challenges the idea that the Supreme Court and the Constitution act as rigid barriers against the popular will.

  • Judicial Responsiveness: Supreme Court justices are described as "practical politicians" who keep their "ears close to the grass-roots." Their decisions often reflect the "popular rages of the hour" rather than immutable law.
  • The Malleability of the Constitution: The text asserts that a determined majority can achieve any outcome—from expropriating all private property to abolishing the jury system—without departing from the constitutional framework.

3. The Democratic Transaction

Politics under democracy is characterized as a "master transaction" between two groups:

  • The Electorate: Driven by a desire for safety, security, and "rough entertainment," the typical voter is willing to trade theoretical freedoms for tangible advantages like jobs or business gains.
  • The Politicians: Described as "jackals" who "hoodwink and swindle" the public, politicians provide the appearance of what the people want in exchange for power and the fulfillment of their own interests.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to the text, why does reapportionment in the lower houses of state legislatures often lag behind population growth?

Answer: Because cities grow in population much faster than country districts, and the legislative process of reapportionment cannot keep pace with this rapid urban expansion.

2. How does the text describe the typical democrat's attitude toward "natural rights" during economic "boom times"?

Answer: The typical democrat becomes impatient with natural rights and is willing to exchange the theoretical boons of freedom for something usable, such as a job or business advantage.

3. What is the primary reason given for why the American public generally rejects "direct action" or revolution?

Answer: The public believes that revolution is unnecessary because any reform advocated by a respectable majority can be achieved peacefully and legally through constitutional amendments and the existing political framework.

4. In what way does the text suggest the Supreme Court is influenced by the President and the Senate?

Answer: When filling vacancies, the President and Senators act as practical politicians, selecting justices who adhere to doctrines that are currently fashionable among the general public (the "cross-roads grocery-stores and barber-shops").

5. What does the text identify as the three things the populace wants most from its leaders?

Answer: Safety and security, to be soothed with "mellifluous words," and heroes to worship.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

I. The Paradox of "Pure Democracy"

The text claims that disproportional representation is "not a device to nullify democracy, but simply a device to make it more workable." Write an essay analyzing this argument. Consider whether the "voluntary sacrifice" of the one-man-one-vote principle is a betrayal of democratic theory or, as the author suggests, a necessary pragmatic adjustment to prevent "mob" rule by urban centers.

II. The Limitless Power of the Majority

The author lists a series of extreme actions the American people could legally take under the Constitution, including the abolition of private property, the enfranchisement of "aliens" or "idiots," and the legalization of assassination for public officers. Discuss the author's view of the Constitution as a "hoary scripture" that offers no real protection against a determined majority. Is the Constitution a safeguard of rights, or merely a flexible tool for the "sovereign people"?

III. The "Master Transaction" of Politics

Critique the author's characterization of democratic politics as a "combat between jackals and jackasses." How does this view of the relationship between the "swindling" politician and the "wanton" voter challenge traditional idealistic views of civic engagement and representative government? Use examples from the text regarding taxation, Prohibition, or property rights to support your analysis.

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition per Source Context
Bucolic Imbecilities A derogatory term used to describe laws or policies (such as Prohibition) favored by rural populations ("yokels") that impact the entire nation.
Direct Action Revolutionary or non-constitutional means of forcing political change, which the text argues is unnecessary in the United States.
Fuglemen Leaders or advocates who promote specific, often idealistic or "romantic" theories of pure democracy.
Make-Weight A compensatory factor; in this context, giving rural voters extra influence to balance the natural organizational advantages of urban populations.
Mellifluous Sweet or musical; used to describe the deceptive and soothing rhetoric politicians use to placate the masses.
Moiety A portion or share of something; specifically, the portion of democratic theory sacrificed for practical efficiency.
One-Man-One-Vote The democratic principle that every citizen's vote should have equal weight, which the text argues is frequently subverted in practice.
Sophistication In this context, the subtle distortion or complicated alteration of a simple formula (like the one-man-one-vote rule).
Yokels A term used in the text to describe rural or country-dwelling voters who are characterized as "dispersed and largely inarticulate."
