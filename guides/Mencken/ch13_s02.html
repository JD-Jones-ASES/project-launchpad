Study Guide: Mencken on Disproportional Representation and Democratic Will

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the analysis found in "Disproportional Representation," an excerpt from H.L. Mencken’s Notes on Democracy. It explores the mechanics of American representation, the tension between urban and rural populations, and the nature of popular sovereignty.

Key Concepts and Thematic Analysis

1. The Function of Disproportional Representation

The text argues that the subversion of the "one-man-one-vote" principle is not an accident or a failure of democracy, but a deliberate tool to make it more "workable." Mencken identifies two primary levels where this occurs:

  • Federal Level: The United States Senate gives equal weight to every state regardless of population, meaning voters in small states like Delaware or Nevada have the same representation as those in Pennsylvania or New York.
  • State Level: Upper houses of state legislatures are often organized by geographical boundaries rather than population, leading to the gross under-representation of "congested urban centres."

2. The Urban-Rural Dynamic

Mencken posits that disproportional representation acts as a "make-weight" to protect rural populations (referred to as "yokels") from the political dominance of cities.

  • Urban Voters: Characterized as a "compact mob" that acts quickly and effectively due to physical and mental proximity.
  • Rural Voters: Described as "dispersed and largely inarticulate," unable to compete with the "city stampede" without the structural advantage of disproportional representation.

3. The Responsiveness of Constitutional Checks

Contrary to the idea that the Constitution and the Supreme Court act as static barriers to the public will, the text argues they are highly sensitive to "popular rages of the hour."

  • The Judiciary: Supreme Court justices are described as "practical politicians" who keep their "ears close to the grass-roots" and consider "election returns" when making celebrated decisions.
  • Legislative Fluidity: The American majority can effect any change—from abolishing juries to expropriating property—as long as the desire is strong enough. The Constitution is viewed as a flexible "hoary scripture" that survives even when its fundamental protections are ignored or amended by popular acclamation.

4. The "Master Transaction" of Politics

In Mencken’s view, the essence of democratic politics is a sordid trade.

  • The Public's Desires: The masses seek safety, security, heroes to worship, and "rough entertainment."
  • The Politician's Role: Politicians "hoodwink and swindle" the public, trading these simple desires for the power and advantages the politicians and their backing minorities crave.

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition according to the Source Context
Bucolic Imbecilities A pejorative term used to describe rural-driven policies, specifically referencing Prohibition.
Compact Mob A description of urban voters who, due to living in close physical and mental contact, form opinions quickly and act with a "solid front."
Disproportional Representation A system where voting power is assigned by geographical boundaries rather than population, resulting in some citizens' votes counting more than others.
Fuglemen Leaders or spokesmen for a particular cause, specifically those advocating for "pure democracy" through complicated remedies.
Make-weight A structural advantage given to a weaker party (rural voters) to balance the natural effectiveness of a stronger party (urban centers).
Moiety A portion or half; specifically, the part of democratic theory sacrificed by the sovereign people for "safer and more efficient practice."
Practical Politicians An appellation for public officials, including judges, who prioritize fashionable doctrines and private profit over abstract justice.
Yokels A term used to describe rural inhabitants who receive disproportionately heavy representation to withstand urban political pressure.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to the text, why do cities often remain under-represented in state legislatures even when representation is ostensibly proportional?

    • Answer: Cities grow in population much faster than rural districts, and the process of reapportionment consistently lags behind that growth.

  2. What is the "paradox" Mencken identifies regarding the subversion of the one-man-one-vote principle?

    • Answer: The subversion is itself an expression of the democratic will; the sovereign people voluntarily sacrifice theoretical equality for what they perceive as a safer and more efficient practice.

  3. How does Mencken characterize the primary motivations of the "typical democrat"?

    • Answer: The typical democrat is willing to trade theoretical boons of freedom for something usable, such as a job, a business advantage, or a sense of security and safety.

  4. How does the text describe the Supreme Court’s relationship with popular opinion?

    • Answer: The court is highly responsive to the "shifts of popular opinion and passion," often reflecting fashionable doctrines discussed in "cross-roads grocery-stores" rather than acting as a static curb on legislation.

  5. List three extreme actions the text claims the American people could legally take within the framework of the Constitution.

    • Answer: (Any three of): Expropriate all private property, enfranchise aliens or children, abolish Federal and State Legislatures, extend the President's term to life, or abolish the jury system.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Utility of Inequality: Mencken argues that disproportional representation is "not a device to nullify democracy, but simply a device to make it more workable." Analyze this argument. How does the text justify the intentional imbalance of power between urban and rural citizens, and what does this suggest about the author's view of "pure democracy"?

  2. The Malleability of Law: The text suggests that the Constitution is a "hoary scripture" that can be used to justify almost any action, including the destruction of property and the removal of rights. Discuss the relationship between "popular rages" and constitutional stability as presented in the text. Does Mencken believe the law is a protection for the minority, or a tool for the majority?

  3. The Science of Politics as "Swindling": Mencken describes the political process as a "combat between jackals and jackasses." Deconstruct this metaphor using the data points provided in the text. How do the concepts of "affecting rhetoric" and "sordid bargaining" define the interaction between the sovereign people and their elected representatives?
