Study Guide: The Phenomenon of the Defeated Politician in Democracy

This study guide examines the "lame duck" phenomenon as described in the provided analysis of American democracy. It explores the psychological and political consequences of electoral defeat, the historical figures who embody these traits, and the systemic differences between democratic and despotic treatments of unsuccessful leaders.

Key Concepts

The Nature of the "Lame Duck" In the context of this analysis, a "lame duck" is a professional politician who has suffered a crushing electoral defeat. Because democratic politics is viewed as a struggle for the "job" and its rewards rather than for principles, defeat leaves the candidate "stripped bare." This often results in a state of rage, bitterness, and a desire for revenge that can poison public life.

Political Retribution and "Running Amok" When a defeated politician cannot be pacified with an appointive office (such as a federal commission or a seat on the bench), they may use their remaining influence with the "mob" as a weapon against the nation. This behavior is characterized as a "running amok," where personal misanthropy is vented through anti-social acts or the destabilization of the country.

The Dynastic Nature of Democracy The text argues that all democratic politics eventually resolves into "dynastic questions." The primary objective is the acquisition of office. When this objective is thwarted, the resulting psychological trauma to the candidate often manifests as pathological behavior rather than political discourse.

Historical Case Studies: The "Warlocks" of American Politics

The following table outlines the specific historical figures cited as examples of defeated politicians who turned their bitterness into destructive public action.

Figure Political Context Consequences of Defeat
Aaron Burr Failed to defeat Jefferson in 1800; failed to succeed Adams. Became a misanthrope; engaged in a duel with Hamilton, a conspiracy with Blennerhassett, and a trial for treason.
Henry Clay Three-time presidential candidate (1824, 1832, 1840). Turned toward "the Interests" (Mellons, Morgans); advocated for centralization and protective tariffs.
John C. Calhoun Failed to reach the White House/succeed Jackson. Became a primary architect of Nullification; worked to break up the Union and prepare the way for the Civil War.
James G. Blaine Defeated in 1876 and 1884. Perpetuated Civil War-era hatreds ("waving the bloody shirt") and engaged in a succession of anti-social acts.
Theodore Roosevelt Defeated in 1912. Became a "political killer"; reversed his convictions on Germany to spite Woodrow Wilson; helped force the U.S. into WWI to seek personal military command.
William Jennings Bryan Three-time presidential candidate (last defeat in 1908). Attacked the "intelligent minority" via a campaign against evolution; prioritized hurting his opponents over public education or tranquility.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to the text, what is the primary objective of politics under a democracy? The primary objective is "the job" (office and its rewards) rather than principle.

  2. How do fellow professional politicians typically try to save the "livelihood and face" of a defeated colleague? They often place them in "gaudy appointive office," such as Federal commissions or the Federal bench.

  3. What was the "bloody shirt," and which politician is associated with waving it "recklessly and obscenely"? The "bloody shirt" refers to the practice of keeping alive the hatreds of the Civil War; it is associated with James G. Blaine.

  4. According to the author, what was William Jennings Bryan’s true motivation for his campaign against evolution? It was not religious fanaticism, but a "colossal lust for revenge" against the intelligent minority who had consistently opposed his presidential bids.

  5. What is the "lamentable exhibition" that countries under despotism manage to escape? They escape the public venting and destructive "bellowing" of defeated aspirants, as these individuals are usually killed, exiled, or forced by etiquette to remain silent.

  6. In what way did Theodore Roosevelt’s behavior change after the outbreak of World War I in 1914? Initially an admirer of the "German scheme of things," he switched sides to annoy his successful rival, Woodrow Wilson, eventually forcing the U.S. into the war.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Pathology of Ambition: Analyze the author’s argument that the behavior of defeated politicians is "pathological" rather than "political." Use the examples of Aaron Burr and Theodore Roosevelt to discuss how personal ego can override national interest in a democratic system.

  2. Democracy vs. Despotism: Contrast the author’s view of how democracies and despotisms handle "unsuccessful aspirants for the crown." Is the author’s sardonic praise for the "efficiency" of despotism a genuine critique of democratic stability?

  3. The Impact of the "Interests": Examine the case of Henry Clay. How does his transition from a democratic candidate to an "attorney for the Interests" illustrate the text's claim that defeated politicians turn their backs on the public?

  4. The "Intelligent Minority" and the Mob: Discuss William Jennings Bryan’s use of the "mob" as a weapon. How does the text characterize the relationship between a populist leader’s personal resentment and the attack on "intellectual decency"?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Bloody Shirt: A political tactic of arousing post-Civil War animosity for partisan gain.
  • Centralization: The concentration of administrative power in a central government, a policy favored by Henry Clay.
  • Lame Duck: A defeated politician whose loss has left them bitter, vengeful, and potentially dangerous to the state.
  • Maison de Santé: A private asylum or health establishment; mentioned as a place a Crown Prince might wish to confine a King.
  • Misanthrope: A person who dislikes humankind and avoids human society; used to describe Aaron Burr after his political failures.
  • Nullification: The legal theory that a state has the right to invalidate any federal law it deems unconstitutional; a doctrine championed by John C. Calhoun.
  • Protective Tariff: A duty imposed on imports to protect domestic industries; viewed by the author as a lingering "blessing" of Henry Clay’s influence.
  • The Interests: Powerful financial and industrial figures (e.g., Mellon, Morgan, Schwab) for whom defeated politicians may become agents.
  • Warlock: A term used by the author to describe destructive, trouble-making politicians on both national and local levels.
