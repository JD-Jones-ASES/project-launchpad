# Study Guide: Envy as a Philosophy

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the arguments presented in H.L. Mencken’s analysis of democracy, specifically focusing on the role of envy, the distinction between rural and urban populations, and the mechanisms used by the plutocracy to maintain social order.

---

### Key Concepts and Themes

#### 1. Envy as the Engine of Democracy
Mencken posits that democracy is not fueled by idealism, liberty, or justice, but by **envy**. He argues that the "inferior man" does not desire abstract rights; rather, he desires the possessions and experiences of those he considers his betters and harbors resentment toward them for possessing those things.

#### 2. The Rural "Yokel" and Puritanism
The rural dweller (referred to as the "peasant," "yokel," or "husbandman") is characterized by a life of isolation and drudgery.
*   **Source of Envy:** The rural man views the "city man" with hatred because the city man enjoys pleasures—cabarets, romantic society, and varied stimulants—that are unavailable in the village.
*   **Moralism as a Weapon:** Unable to access urban delights, the rural man seeks to ban them through law. Mencken argues that **Prohibition** was not born of a desire for sobriety, but of the rural man's envy of the "gay society" and "music of harps" found in city cabarets.
*   **Sexual Resentment:** Living in "unmitigated monogamy" with "strictly Christian" wives, the rural man resents the perceived "antinomianism" and "carnalities" of city women. This resentment fuels support for moral legislation like the **Mann Act**.

#### 3. The Urban Proletarian and "Panem et Circenses"
The city worker's natural envy is mitigated by what Mencken calls *panem et circenses* (bread and circuses).
*   **The Role of the Plutocracy:** The wealthy elite (plutocracy) have learned that the urban masses are not dangerous as long as their "belly is filled and [their] eyes kept a-pop."
*   **Social Services:** The city worker is "artfully relieved" of elemental anxieties through extensive public charity, including free medical care, maternity services, and comprehensive public schooling.
*   **Entertainment and Sublimation:** Through tabloids and "gaudy entertainment," the city worker’s envy is converted into "civic pride" or "boozy contentment." They enjoy vicarious participation in the debaucheries of the wealthy rather than resenting them.

### Comparison of Social Classes

| Feature | The Rural "Yokel" | The Urban Proletarian |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Driver** | Raw, unmitigated envy and resentment. | Sublimated envy and "boozy contentment." |
| **View of the Wealthy** | Moralistic hatred; desires to suppress their "Babylonish revels." | Vicarious enjoyment; takes pride in urban "levantine joys." |
| **Social Control** | Driven by "Puritanism" and the theology of local pastors. | Managed by the plutocracy through charity and entertainment. |
| **Domestic Life** | Isolated, "swinish," and strictly monogamous. | Socialized; relieved of parental burdens by public machinery. |
| **Response to Media** | Views tales of city life as "brimstone" and immorality. | Views tales of city life as thrilling "shows" and "frou-frou." |

### Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **According to Mencken, what is the true origin of Prohibition?**
    *   *Answer:* It is the rural man’s envy of the cabaret. Because he is forced to drink "unpalatable stimulants" in "solitary swinishness," he seeks to use the law to abolish the "delicate and romantic" drinking culture of the city man.

2.  **How does the American plutocracy prevent the urban proletariat from becoming "dangerous"?**
    *   *Answer:* By ensuring they have "enough surplus wealth" to fund public charity and entertainment. This keeps the worker’s "belly filled and his eyes kept a-pop," distracting him from "sober thinking" or "brooding" over his grievances.

3.  **What role does "yellow-back" literature like "Night Life in Chicago" play in rural politics?**
    *   *Answer:* It propagates "idealism" by convincing the "yokel" that urban life is a series of "Babylonish revels" that must be put down by law to ensure the survival of Christianity.

4.  **Describe the "machinery" available to the urban mother as outlined in the text.**
    *   *Answer:* She has access to obstetricians, gynecologists, and visiting nurses who provide diet advice and free layettes. She can park her child at a crèche, and the public school system provides free books, lunches, dental care, and spectacles.

5.  **How does Mencken differentiate the "disdain" of the truly superior from the "envy" of the inferior?**
    *   *Answer:* He notes that genuine superiority carries a disdain that is "quite different" from envy. Envy is "an inferiority that admits itself" (*une infériorité qui s’avoue*), where the inferior man hates his betters specifically because he lacks what they have.

### Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Sublimation of Resentment:** Analyze Mencken’s argument that the urban environment "deftly draws the fangs" of the proletariat. Compare the "harmless tabloids" of the city to the "yellow-backs" of the country. How does the medium of delivery change the political outcome of envy?

2.  **The Theology of Envy:** Mencken suggests that "Puritanism" is merely a manifestation of democratic envy. Explore the relationship between rural theology and the "melancholy heaving of manure." To what extent does Mencken believe moral "idealism" is a psychological coping mechanism for geographic and economic isolation?

3.  **The Paternalistic State:** Examine the description of the "immense development of public charity" in American cities. Does Mencken view these benefits—such as free medical care and education—as genuine progress, or as a calculated tool of the plutocracy to reduce the worker to the "biological function of a boar in a barnyard"?

### Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Antinomianism:** A rejection of socially established morality; in the text, it refers to the perceived moral or sexual freedom of urban women.
*   **Glabella:** The smooth part of the forehead between the eyebrows; the rural man "feels along his glabella" for the onset of a headache after drinking crude stimulants.
*   **Hind / Yokel / Husbandman:** Terms used by Mencken to describe the rural or agricultural worker, often characterized as inferior or driven by resentment.
*   **Mann Act:** A federal statute used as an example of democratic moralism fueled by the rural man's resentment of urban sexual freedom.
*   **Panem et Circenses:** "Bread and circuses"; a metaphor for the superficial means of gratification used by a government to appease the masses.
*   **Plutocracy:** The wealthy class that controls a state; Mencken describes them as experts in "dealing with the proletarian."
*   **Prohibition:** The 18th Amendment banning alcohol; Mencken cites it as the ultimate example of "democratic moralism" and rural envy.
*   **Puritanism:** Defined by Mencken as the "twin" of democracy; the impulse to regulate the private behavior of others through legislation.
*   **Sackbuts:** Ancient wind instruments; used here to illustrate the romantic, musical atmosphere of the city cabaret that the rural man envies.
*   **Yellow-backs:** Cheap, sensationalist novels or pamphlets that described urban vice, fueling rural indignation.
