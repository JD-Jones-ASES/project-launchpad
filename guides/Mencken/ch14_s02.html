Analysis of the Democratic Politician: A Study Guide

This study guide examines the themes and arguments presented in "The Politician Under Democracy," an analysis of the psychological and professional nature of the democratic political class. The text explores the politician not as an individual, but as a specific species characterized by deceit, flattery, and a singular drive for power.

Key Concepts and Themes

The Courtier of Democracy

The politician is defined as a "courtier of democracy." Just as a traditional courtier flattered a king to exploit him, the democratic politician flatters the "mob" or the public to rule them. Their professional life is described as a "sustained exercise in deceit." While ostensibly serving as altruists devoted to the public good, their true aim is self-interest—"to butter his parsnips."

The Nature of the Political Class

The text argues that the political class is composed of individuals who have traded their honor for a place at the "public trough." Key characteristics include:

  • Adaptability of Belief: A politician will lie to hold a job, and if lying fails, they will "embrace new truths" to suit the popular mood.
  • Sensory Acuteness: Success depends on having an "ear close to the ground" to detect popular clamor or delusions before they are fully formed by the public.
  • Lack of Principle: There is no shadow of honor or principle in the professional aspect of the politician. Any action—changing convictions overnight or getting into office through false pretenses—is considered "moral" if it secures their position of power and public honor.

The "Subterranean" Boss and the Mob

The text distinguishes between the "statesman" (the politician in their most noble aspect) and the "boss" who operates on lower, subterranean levels.

  • The Power of the Boss: The boss derives power from the weaknesses of the common people (Homo vulgaris), specifically their inability to understand complex issues, their tendency to fly into preposterous alarms, and their envy of their superiors.
  • The Herd Instinct: The common man is described as having a "congenital fear of liberty" and an incapacity for independent action. He prefers the safety of the herd and requires a "bell-wether" to lead him.
  • The Boss as King: To the mob, the boss is the "fount of honor" and a source of romanticism, filling the void left by traditional kings.

The Two Branches of Politics

The art of democratic politics is divided into two complementary, yet degrading, roles:

  1. The Demagogue: One who preaches doctrines they know to be false to men they know to be "idiots."
  2. The Demaslave: One who listens to the "idiots" and pretends to believe their opinions. Most successful politicians must perform both roles simultaneously.

The Influence of Organized Minorities

The text highlights the power of highly efficient minority groups (such as the Anti-Saloon League) that use intimidation to control politicians. These minorities awaken the mob’s envy and hatred of "betters," forcing politicians to submit cravenly to their dictation to remain in office.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to the text, why is the politician compared to a courtier?
  2. What is the "principal and often sole aim" of the democratic politician?
  3. Define the term "demaslave" as used in the text.
  4. Why does the text claim that an educated man stating "elementary notions" could likely never be elected?
  5. What role does "public honor" play in the life of a politician?
  6. How does the text describe the relationship between the "boss" and the common man's fear of liberty?
  7. What is the "reductio ad absurdum" of the democratic process?
  8. According to the text, what happens to a man of "sounder self-respect" who enters politics?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Survival of the Unfit in Democratic Governance

Analyze the text’s argument that the democratic system naturally selects for "cowards and trimmers" rather than men of honor. How does the requirement to "woo and flatter inferiors in sense" lead to what the author describes as the "muck of swinishness" in law-making bodies?

2. The Psychology of Homo Vulgaris

Explore the depiction of the common citizen in the text. To what extent does the author believe the failures of the democratic politician are actually the failures of the public? Discuss the concept of the "herd" and the "congenital incapacity" for civilized citizenship.

3. The Role of Organized Intimidation

Discuss the influence of minority organizations as described in the text. How do these groups use the "gross weaknesses" of the public to intimidate politicians, and what are the resulting effects on judicial and administrative posts?

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Bell-wether A leader that the "herd" of the common people follows; one who rings the "clarion bell" of popular appeal.
Boss A political leader on the "subterranean" level who owns the mob through a shrewd understanding of their sentimentalities and basic needs (jobs, coal).
Courtier of Democracy A metaphor for the politician who flatters the public (the employer) in order to rule and victimize them.
Demagogue A politician who gains power by preaching doctrines they know to be untrue to a public they believe to be unintelligent.
Demaslave A politician who abjectly follows and pretends to believe the opinions of the "idiots" who make up the voting public.
Homo vulgaris The author’s term for the "common or vulgar species of human being" characterized by a preference for the herd and a fear of liberty.
Public Trough A metaphor for public office and the associated power and "public honor" that politicians strive to maintain for their own satisfaction.
Trimmer ex officio A person who shifts their professional opinions for the sake of expediency; a coward by virtue of their office.
