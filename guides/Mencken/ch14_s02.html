Study Guide: The Politician Under Democracy

This study guide explores H.L. Mencken’s analytical critique of the democratic politician as presented in his work Notes on Democracy. Mencken examines the politician not as an individual, but as a species—a "courtier of democracy" whose professional existence is defined by deceit, flattery, and the pursuit of power at the expense of principle.

I. Key Concepts and Themes

1. The Politician as "Courtier"

Mencken adopts Professor Robert Michels’ definition of the politician as the "courtier of democracy." Just as historical courtiers flattered monarchs to victimize and rule them, the democratic politician flatters the "mob." While ostensibly an altruist devoted to public service, the politician is characterized as a "sturdy rogue" whose primary objective is to maintain his position at the "public trough."

2. The Nature of the "Mob" (Homo Vulgaris)

The power of the politician is rooted in the perceived weaknesses of the common people, or Homo vulgaris. According to the text, these weaknesses include:

  • Cognitive Limitations: An inability to grasp complex issues, responding only to the simple and banal.
  • Emotional Volatility: A tendency to succumb to "preposterous alarms," envy, and hatred of superiors.
  • Fear of Liberty: A "congenital incapacity" for independent action and a preference for the "warmth and safety of the herd."
  • Venality: A willingness to be bought with simple material rewards, such as a job or a load of coal.

3. The Two Branches of Democratic Politics

Mencken divides the art of democratic politics into two complementary, yet degrading, roles:

  • The Demagogue: One who preaches doctrines he knows to be false to an audience he considers idiots.
  • The Demaslave: One who listens to the "idiots" and pretends to believe their delusions. Most successful politicians must master both roles to secure and hold elective office.

4. The Role of "The Boss"

The political boss represents the "eternal reductio ad absurdum" of the democratic process. He fulfills the elemental wants of the inferior man and provides a sense of order. To the common man, the boss is the personification of the state, inheriting the "romance that used to hang about a king." Mencken argues that when one boss is overthrown, he is inevitably replaced by another authority figure, such as a "prehensile Methodist parson" or a "grand goblin."

5. The Influence of Organized Minorities

Politicians are not only subject to the whims of the mob but also to the intimidation of highly efficient minority groups. The text cites the Anti-Saloon League as a primary example. These minorities use techniques of intimidation to force politicians into "craven submission," filling legislative, administrative, and judicial posts with those who prioritize the minority's agenda over their own convictions.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to Mencken, what is the "technical equipment" of a politician?

Answer: It consists simply of an "armamentarium of deceits." His skill lies in lying to hold his job, embracing new truths if lying fails, and detecting popular clamor before the people themselves are even aware of it.

2. How does Mencken describe the moral code of the democratic politician?

Answer: The politician’s code deems anything moral if it furthers his main concern: keeping his place at the public trough. This includes getting into office under false pretenses or changing convictions overnight to follow popular trends (e.g., Prohibition).

3. Why does Mencken argue that an educated man is unlikely to be elected to office?

Answer: An educated man stating "elementary notions" plainly would arouse fears in the mob. The politician’s business is not to be frank, but to arouse specific fears that will run in his favor.

4. What comparison does Mencken use to describe the "old birds" (experienced politicians) who have compromised their honor?

Answer: He compares them to "chorus girls of long experience" who have resignedly accepted that they must compromise themselves (in the politician's case, by swallowing convictions) as the price of keeping their jobs.

5. What is the distinction between "vanity" and "pride" in the surviving politician?

Answer: By the time a politician has successfully navigated the democratic system, his dignity and pride have vanished, leaving only a "vacuum" in his subconscious; however, his vanity remains.

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Courtier Metaphor: Analyze Mencken’s use of the "courtier" metaphor. How does the shift from serving a king to serving a "mob" change the nature of political flattery and deceit? Use examples from the text regarding the "boss" and the "statesman" to support your analysis.

  2. The Psychology of the Herd: Mencken suggests that Homo vulgaris suffers from a "congenital fear of liberty." Discuss how this psychological trait, according to the text, creates a vacuum that is filled by "interlocking despotisms" such as the boss, the priest, or the "drill-sergeant."

  3. The Monopoly of the "Typical" Law-maker: Evaluate Mencken’s claim that men of "decenter habit" cannot compete with those willing to "crawl" and "dissemble." What are the long-term implications for a democratic government if public office is monopolized by those who have "sacrificed any principle" for votes?

  4. Minority Rule in a Democracy: Using the example of the Anti-Saloon League provided in the text, discuss the mechanics of how a minority group can intimidate the "whole class of place-seeking politicians." How does this challenge the traditional definition of democracy as "majority rule"?

IV. Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Boss The ultimate expression of the democratic process who provides simple needs (jobs, coal) to the "mob" in exchange for power.
Courtier of Democracy A term for the politician who flatters the voting public (the employer) in order to victimize and rule them.
Demagogue A political actor who intentionally spreads false doctrines to those he considers "idiots" to gain power.
Demaslave A political actor who pretends to believe the delusions and opinions of the "mob" to maintain their favor.
Homo Vulgaris Mencken’s term for the "common or vulgar species of human being," characterized by a lack of independent thought and a preference for the herd.
Public Trough A metaphor for public office and the access to power and "public honor" it provides to the politician.
Reductio ad Absurdum A Latin phrase meaning "reduction to absurdity"; used by Mencken to describe how the political boss represents the most absurd extreme of democratic logic.
Trimmer A person who adapts their views for the sake of expediency; a coward "ex officio" who lacks fixed principles.
