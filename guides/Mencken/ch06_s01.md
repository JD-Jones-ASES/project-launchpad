# Study Guide: Mencken’s “The Role of the Hormones”

This study guide provides a comprehensive analysis of the rhetorical strategies, thematic depth, and linguistic techniques employed by H.L. Mencken in Chapter 6, Section 1, "The Role of the Hormones." It explores how Mencken shifts the discussion of democratic politics from a philosophical framework to a biological and endocrinological one to critique the "inferior man."

---

## I. Key Concepts and Themes

### The Biological Reduction of Politics
Mencken’s central strategy is the displacement of rational agency. By framing political behavior as the result of glandular secretions rather than intellectual conviction, he denies the democratic citizen the dignity of being a rational actor. This biological reductionism serves as a rhetorical weapon to deflate the pretensions of democratic self-governance.

### The Mock-Clinician Persona
Mencken adopts the persona of a detached, "mock-clinician." He examines the "body politic" with a sense of amused professionalism, using scientific neutrality to discuss shocking subjects like violence and religious fanaticism. This persona allows him to treat citizens as laboratory specimens rather than participants in a shared civic life.

### The Psychology of the "Inferior Man"
Mencken utilizes contemporary psychological frameworks—specifically Freudian theory and behaviorism—to categorize the "inferior man." He argues that this individual is governed by infantile drives:
*   **Primary Drives:** Rage, fear, and a craving for physical pleasure.
*   **Incapacity for Empathy:** An inability to imagine the good of others, which Mencken argues inevitably leads to a spirit of persecution.
*   **The Peasant Archetype:** Mencken identifies the peasant farmer as the purest specimen of the inferior man, whose theology and politics are driven by unmitigated self-interest and a desire for violence against dissenters.

### Taxonomy of the "Political Animal"
A recurring theme is the systematic dehumanization of the citizen through animal analogies. Mencken creates a "barnyard taxonomy," comparing the "inferior man" to:
*   **A Tom cat or rooster:** Representing crude sexuality and barnyard morality.
*   **A wolf:** Representing inherent cruelty.
*   **A boar:** Representing base biological functions.

## II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. What is Mencken’s primary goal in using biological and endocrinological language to describe politics?**
*Answer:* Mencken uses this language as a rhetorical strategy to strip democratic man of his rational agency. By attributing political behavior to hormones and glands, he suggests that humans are governed by biological instincts rather than philosophical or historical reasoning.

**2. How does Mencken utilize the theories of Sigmund Freud and John B. Watson?**
*Answer:* He borrows their vocabulary—such as "libido" and "stimulus and response"—to provide a "vocabulary of reduction." He treats humans as laboratory specimens to be diagnosed and classified, though he does not necessarily accept their scientific systems in full.

**3. Describe the significance of the phrase "fresh from God's hands" in the context of this section.**
*Answer:* It is a piece of mock-pious irony. Mencken uses the religious phrase to describe a creature that he immediately proceeds to define by its erogenous zones and glandular functions, creating a comic shock between conventional piety and clinical reality.

**4. What does the term "Homo stultus" signify in Mencken’s work?**
*Answer:* It is a mock-scientific coinage meaning "foolish man." It mimics the biological classification *Homo sapiens* to label the democratic citizen as a specimen of stupidity.

**5. How does Mencken’s use of the colon function rhetorically?**
*Answer:* The colon acts as a hinge between a formal, general statement and a concrete, deflating specification. It creates a sense of "logical inevitability," making the transition from an abstract concept (like "natural cowardice") to a brutal conclusion (like "cruelty") seem natural.

## III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

### 1. The Rhetoric of Scientific Detachment
Analyze Mencken’s adoption of the "mock-clinician" persona. How does the use of clinical and technical diction (such as *tumescence* and *anthropoid*) affect the reader’s perception of the subjects being described? Discuss how this "scientific neutrality" actually serves to heighten the contemptuous tone of the passage.

### 2. Register Mixing and Satire
Mencken frequently mixes high-register medical vocabulary with low-register "barnyard" language. Discuss how this collision of registers functions as a tool for satire. In your analysis, explain how the use of words like *sage-femme* or *gloriously* alongside descriptions of "slatternly" wives and "Tom cats" reinforces his central argument about human nature.

### 3. The Psychology of the Peasantry
According to the text, Mencken views the peasant farmer as the "purest specimen" of the inferior man. Examine Mencken's claim that the peasant's theology and politics are merely expressions of self-interest and fear. How does Mencken use hyperbole and staccato syntax to deliver a "verdict" on this specific social class?

### 4. Dehumanization and Animal Analogy
Mencken presents a "complete barnyard taxonomy of the democratic citizen." Evaluate the effectiveness of his animal analogies (the wolf, the boar, the rooster). To what extent does this systematic dehumanization support his broader argument that democracy is a failed or irrational system?

## IV. Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition in Context |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Animism** | The attribution of a soul or consciousness to natural objects; used by Mencken to equate peasant theology with "savage" religion. |
| **Antinomianism** | The theological doctrine that moral law is not binding; used loosely by Mencken to describe the rejection of conventional sexual morality. |
| **Anthropoid** | Ape-like; used to describe the base, primate-like appetites that Mencken argues underlie human "sentiment." |
| **Auto-erotic** | Relating to sexual gratification obtained solely through one’s own body; applied provocatively by Mencken to infant behavior. |
| **Erogenous zones** | Areas of the body sensitive to stimulation; a Freudian term used to reduce human development to biological sensitivity. |
| **Homo stultus** | Mencken’s satirical coinage meaning "foolish man," modeled on the scientific name for humans. |
| **Libido** | Psychic and emotional energy associated with biological drives; used to provide a vocabulary for the "reduction" of human behavior. |
| **Litotes** | A figure of speech using understatement via the negation of the contrary (e.g., "of course"); used by Mencken to dismiss arguments as settled facts. |
| **Onanism** | Mentioned in relation to the peasant's existence; implies a sterile or self-absorbed state of being. |
| **Sage-femme** | French for "midwife"; used to lend mock-elegance to descriptions of rural sexual urgency. |
| **Tumescence** | The swelling of body tissue; used clinically to describe infant sexuality and create reader discomfort. |
