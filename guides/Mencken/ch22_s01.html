Comprehensive Study Guide: H.L. Mencken’s Analysis of Democratic Moralism

This study guide provides an in-depth examination of H.L. Mencken’s rhetorical and philosophical critique of Puritanism within a democratic framework, as presented in "Where Puritanism Fails." It explores the shift from the psychological motives of moralism to its practical, often destructive, consequences in American society.

Key Concepts and Central Arguments

1. The Perversion of Law and Justice

Mencken argues that democratic moralism transforms the legal system from a protective shield into a tool for persecution. He posits that under the influence of Puritanism:

  • Virtue is replaced by hypocrisy: Legislation intended to enforce morality does not make people better; it merely forces them to hide their true behaviors.
  • Degradation of Rights: Mencken suggests that the democratic citizen has "no rights that the police are bound to respect." This is a deliberate echo of Chief Justice Roger B. Taney’s language in the Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857) decision, comparing the status of political dissidents to that of enslaved persons.
  • The "Mill of Justice": Mencken uses this metaphor to describe a legal system that "grinds" rather than judges, escalating vague accusations like "disturbing the peace" into crimes with "staggering penalties."

2. Historical Examples of Democratic Anti-Intellectualism

Mencken identifies three specific historical markers to illustrate the machinery of persecution:

  • The Scopes Trial (1925): The prosecution of John T. Scopes for teaching evolution. Mencken views this as a primary example of democratic anti-intellectualism.
  • The Volstead Act (1919): Named for Representative Andrew Volstead, this act provided the enforcement mechanism for Prohibition, representing a "sumptuary" law that regulates personal behavior.
  • Bishop Wilson’s Sermon: A case study in the clerical demand for the suppression of free speech, where the bishop argued that "Atheism does not deserve a place" and should not be protected by constitutional guarantees.

3. The Destruction of Civic Trust

The final movement of Mencken’s argument focuses on the loss of "common decency." He contends that:

  • Surveillance replaces community: Mutual trust is destroyed when neighbors watch one another through "knotholes."
  • Social Isolation: The "decent citizen" is eventually driven from the community by the hostility and suspicion of Puritanical neighbors.
  • Negation of Prosperity: Material success (symbolized by "money in the bank and a Ford in the garage") is rendered meaningless if a citizen lives in constant fear of jail and clerical persecution.

Rhetorical Strategies and Stylistic Analysis

Strategy Function in the Text
Invective A sustained verbal attack that combines evidence with contempt. It is "anger organized by intelligence."
Ironic Prosecution Presenting an opponent (like Bishop Wilson) with "mock ceremony" and "puissance" to allow their own "bloodthirsty" words to convict them before the reader.
Periodic Construction Long sentences that withhold the main point until the end, forcing the reader to navigate a "thicket" of clauses, mirroring the complexity of the legal system.
Syntactic Parallelism Using similar grammatical structures to equate different ideas—such as equating a neighbor's spying with a pastor's plan to jail a citizen.
Diction Collision Juxtaposing formal legal vocabulary (habeas corpus, due process) with words of violence (lynching, clubbed, railroaded) to show the gap between legal promise and practice.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What is the significance of Mencken’s reference to the Dred Scott decision?

    • Answer: It is used to illustrate that under democratic moralism, the political dissident has no rights that the authorities are required to respect, mirroring the lack of rights afforded to Black Americans in the 1857 ruling.

  2. How does Mencken define the "mill of justice"?

    • Answer: He describes it as a machine that escalates minor or vague accusations into severe penalties, emphasizing that the system "grinds" rather than provides genuine judgment.

  3. What does the word "knotholes" imply in the context of Mencken’s close-reading passage?

    • Answer: It suggests furtive, shameful spying by neighbors, reducing them from moral guardians to "peeping toms" and highlighting the destruction of mutual trust.

  4. According to the text, what is the "paradox of Mencken’s invective"?

    • Answer: The paradox is that while the rage is genuine and "furious," the prose remains "architecturally sound" and "controlled."

  5. What specific items does Mencken use to represent 1920s middle-class American prosperity?

    • Answer: Money in the bank and a Ford in the garage.

  6. How did Bishop Wilson view the relationship between Atheism and free speech?

    • Answer: He believed Atheism did not deserve a place in society and should not be defended by any "specious claim" to constitutional protection of free speech.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Architecture of Anger: Analyze how Mencken uses "disciplined style" to prevent his indignation from becoming a "mere rant." Discuss the relationship between his emotional tone and his logical organization.
  2. Legal Language as Indictment: Explore Mencken’s use of judicial vocabulary (habeas corpus, statutes, double jeopardy). How does he use the language of the law to convict the practitioners of the law?
  3. The Death of Common Decency: Mencken shifts from a "prosecutorial" voice to an "elegiac" one at the end of the text. Discuss how this shift in tone affects the reader’s perception of the consequences of Puritanism.
  4. The Individual vs. The Community: Using Mencken's description of neighbors and pastors, evaluate his argument regarding how surveillance and "moralism" fundamentally alter the nature of the American community.

Glossary of Key Terms

  • Avails: (Archaic) To be of use or value. Mencken uses this to mimic the oratorical register of the Puritan sermons he critiques.
  • Caveat: A warning or caution; from the Latin for "let him beware." Mencken uses it to describe the "feeble protests" of those defending liberty.
  • Invective: A form of verbal attack that uses abusive or critical language. Effective invective is documented and organized by intelligence rather than just anger.
  • Proscribed: Forbidden or outlawed. The term carries historical associations with Roman political death lists, where those named could be killed with impunity.
  • Puissance: Great power, influence, or prowess. Used ironically by Mencken to describe Bishop Wilson's status.
  • Specious: Superficially plausible, but actually wrong. Mencken quotes Bishop Wilson using this word to dismiss constitutional claims.
  • Sumptuary: Relating to laws that limit private expenditure or regulate personal behavior, such as the consumption of food and drink.
  • Volstead Act (1919): The National Prohibition Act, which provided the enforcement mechanism for the Eighteenth Amendment; named for Representative Andrew Volstead.
