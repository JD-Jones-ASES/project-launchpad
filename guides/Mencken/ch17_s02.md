# Study Guide: The Maker of Laws (Mencken’s Critique of the American Legislator)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the analysis of the American legislative system as presented in H.L. Mencken’s *Notes on Democracy*. It examines the constitutional constraints on representation, the character of the House of Representatives versus the Senate, and the influence of local politics on national law-making.

---

## Key Concepts and Analysis

### The Residency Requirement and the Cult of Mediocrity
The central argument regarding the failure of American legislation rests on Article I, Sections 2 and 3 of the U.S. Constitution. These sections require that a legislator be an actual resident of the district they represent. 
*   **The Intent:** To ensure every electoral unit has a direct voice in government.
*   **The Reality:** Mencken argues this rule fills legislative bodies with "puerile local politicians" who lack the intelligence to grasp complex governmental problems. 
*   **The Comparison:** In England, a district can choose a representative from anywhere (e.g., John Morley), allowing for high-caliber representation. In the U.S., a district is limited to its own borders, which may lack "civilized" or competent inhabitants.

### The Character of the House of Representatives
The House is depicted as a collection of "scrub stock" and "knavish" nonentities. According to the text, the average member follows a specific, uninspiring trajectory:
1.  Education in a "hedge school" or "preposterous" sectarian college.
2.  A career as a rural schoolteacher or county superintendent.
3.  Transitioning to law and local politics (district attorney or county judge).
4.  Multiple attempts to run for Congress until successful.

Once in Washington, these representatives are characterized by their "unfitness," their preference for "nonsense" over sense, and a daily toil focused on securing jobs for relatives ("patronage") or engaging in "junkets" (drunken holidays at government expense).

### The Senate: Independence vs. Political Necessity
The Senate is described as "measurably better" than the House, primarily due to the six-year term.
*   **The Two-Year House Term:** Forces Congressmen into a state of "continuous bootlicking" of local bosses to ensure re-election.
*   **The Six-Year Senate Term:** Allows a Senator to potentially forget local bosses for a few years and exercise genuine statesmanship or intellectual dignity.
*   **The "Eternal Tragedy":** Despite the potential for independence, Mencken notes that the average Senator remains a "party hack" who will abandon private rectitude for political necessity when pressured.

### The Influence of Corrupt Agencies and Fanatics
Because American legislators are often "ignoramuses," they become susceptible to the influence of "corrupt and sinister agencies" and "demagogues." 
*   **The Eighteenth Amendment:** Cited as a primary example of legislation forced through by "ignoble fellows" and "degraded noodles" from backward states.
*   **The Anti-Saloon League:** A specific agency mentioned as having immense power over incompetent legislators like the fictional "Congressman John J. Balderdash."

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

| Question | Answer Summary |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Why does Mencken believe the residency rule is harmful?** | It limits the talent pool to a single district, often resulting in the election of "mediocrities" because competent men are either unavailable or unpopular with the "local mob." |
| **How does the text describe the intellectual level of the House?** | It is compared to a "gang of bootleggers," deficient in information, integrity, and competent leadership. |
| **What is a "congressional junket" according to the source?** | A "drunken holiday" taken at government expense. |
| **What distinction does the text make between the House and the Senate?** | Senators are slightly more independent because their longer terms allow them to briefly ignore local bosses, whereas Congressmen must constantly campaign. |
| **Who was Senator Reed of Missouri and what was his contribution?** | An "uncompromisingly independent" Senator who openly called his colleagues "cowards" and "poltroons" for prioritizing their offices over the Constitution. |
| **Why might a man like Roscoe Pound fail to be elected in his own district?** | Because his independence and intelligence are "familiar and hence offensive" to his neighbors. |

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Constitutional Critique:** Analyze Mencken’s argument that Article I, Sections 2 and 3 of the Constitution are responsible for the "evil" influence of backward states on national legislation. To what extent does the residency requirement democratize or degrade the quality of the legislature?
2.  **The Anatomy of a Politician:** Using the description of the "average Southern member" of Congress, discuss how the text portrays the relationship between rural education, local law, and national unfitness. How does this career path supposedly lead to a preference for "nonsense"?
3.  **The Influence of the "Mob":** Explore the role of the "rustic mob" and "Fundamentalist clergy" in shaping the American legislative body. How does the text suggest these groups suppress "civilized inhabitants" and empower "pliant and unconscionable jackasses"?
4.  **The Tragedy of Democracy:** Mencken describes the surrender of private rectitude to political necessity as "the eternal tragedy of democracy." Examine this statement in the context of Senator Reed’s 1924 speech. Is the "poltroon soul" an inevitable byproduct of a democratic system?

## Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Bootlicking:** Characterized in the text as the submissive behavior of a Congressman toward local bosses to secure re-election.
*   **Hedge School:** A term used to describe the rudimentary or inferior early education of many Southern legislators.
*   **Junket:** A trip taken by a politician at the taxpayers' expense, described by Mencken as a "drunken holiday."
*   **Kleagle:** A rank within the Ku Klux Klan; used here to categorize the low character of certain legislators.
*   **Noodle:** A derogatory term for a person lacking intelligence; specifically applied to those in State Legislatures who ratified the 18th Amendment.
*   **Poltroon:** A spiritless coward. Senator Reed used this term to describe colleagues who fear losing votes more than they value their duty.
*   **Puerile:** Childishly silly or trivial; used to describe the nature of local politicians.
*   **Rectitude:** Morally correct behavior or thinking; righteousness.
*   **Scrub Stock:** A term for inferior livestock, applied metaphorically to the low-quality individuals found in the House of Representatives.
*   **Venality:** The quality of being open to bribery or corruption.
