Study Guide: H.L. Mencken’s Analysis of the "Lame Duck"

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of H.L. Mencken’s rhetorical and historical analysis regarding defeated politicians, as presented in the "Footnote on Lame Ducks." It explores the psychological profile of the "defeated aspirant," the use of bathos as a rhetorical weapon, and the specific historical figures Mencken utilizes to illustrate his critique of democratic politics.

I. Key Concepts and Historical Context

The Lame Duck Problem

Historically, a "lame duck" is a politician who continues to serve in office after losing an election or choosing not to run again. In the 1920s, this was a significant issue because defeated Congressmen remained in power until March 4 of the following year. While the Twentieth Amendment (ratified in 1933) eventually shortened this period by moving the inauguration to January 20, Mencken focuses less on constitutional mechanics and more on the psychological type of the defeated politician.

The Defeated Aspirant

Mencken’s analysis centers on the "defeated aspirant"—men who sought the presidency and, upon failing, turned their remaining energy toward sabotage and destructive rage. He suggests that for these individuals, ambition "curdles" into a bitterness that poisons American public life.

Rhetorical Framing: The "Footnote"

Mencken frames this section as a "footnote" or a "coda." This is a deliberate rhetorical device:

  • False Modesty: By presenting the material as an afterthought, he suggests the examples are so obvious they hardly require mention.
  • Closing Argument: It serves as a postscript to his broader case against democratic politics.
  • Structural Disguise: The "footnote" label masks what is actually a sustained and detailed historical narrative.

II. Profiles in Political Bitterness

Mencken provides a "historical gallery" of figures whose careers exemplify the destructive nature of the defeated politician.

Figure Mencken’s Description/Role
Henry Clay Brief portrait; treated as a defeated aspirant whose ambition turned to sabotage.
John C. Calhoun Brief portrait; included in the list of those whose failures influenced their subsequent actions.
Aaron Burr A slightly more detailed portrait of a failed political aspirant.
Theodore Roosevelt A longer portrait than the preceding figures, leading toward the climax of the section.
William Jennings Bryan The "emotional peak" of the section. Described with "venomous" contempt as an "ambulent boil" and a walking embodiment of democratic pathology.

III. Rhetorical Strategies and Diction

Bathos: The Art of the Calculated Anticlimax

Mencken is a master of bathos—the deliberate descent from elevated, serious language to the trivial or absurd.

  • Purpose: To deflate pomposity, evoke laughter, and demonstrate a command of tonal range.
  • Primary Example: The description of William Jennings Bryan’s death. Mencken moves from the clinical (a temperature of 110 degrees) to the gothic (eyes rolling horribly) to the bureaucratically precise (1600 Pennsylvania Avenue, N.W.) and finally to the bathetic (the "leaky copper roof" of the White House).

Diction and Register Mixing

Mencken frequently moves between elevated/poetic language and vulgar/clinical terms within a single sentence to create comedy and irony.

  • Religious Irony: He describes Bryan as having "consecrated himself to reprisals." Using "consecrated" (a word of religious devotion) to describe political revenge mocks Bryan’s evangelical background.
  • Medical Precision: He uses terms like "tertiary lues" (syphilis) and "endocarditis" (heart inflammation) with deadpan clinical precision to deliver jokes about political figures.

Hyperbole and Metaphor

  • Hyperbole: Mencken claims Bryan’s attack on evolution was the "most gross attack upon human dignity... in modern times" and asserts the damage Bryan caused was "greater than that done by Sherman's army."
  • Metaphor: Bryan is described as an "ambulent boil," transforming a politician into a literal disease or infection.

IV. Glossary of Terms

  • Ambulent: Walking or ambulatory. Mencken uses this to describe Bryan as a "walking infection."
  • Amok: To be in a frenzy of violence; used to describe the wild nature of a defeated politician’s revenge.
  • Bathos: A deliberate descent from the elevated or serious to the trivial or absurd for comic effect.
  • Endocarditis: Inflammation of the heart's inner lining. Used in a joke regarding a Crown Prince's secret wishes for the King's death.
  • Maison de santé: A French euphemism for a lunatic asylum (literally "house of health").
  • Surcease: Relief or cessation, especially from suffering; used ironically to describe a politician's quest for revenge.
  • Tertiary lues: The advanced, final stage of syphilis characterized by neurological damage.
  • Warlocks: Male witches or sorcerers; Mencken applies this to minor politicians who exert a "dark magic" influence.

V. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does Mencken define the "lame duck" in a psychological sense?
    • Answer: He defines them as defeated aspirants whose thwarted ambitions curdle into a "colossal lust for revenge" and destructive rage.
  2. What is the purpose of the "Crown Prince" fantasy at the end of the section?
    • Answer: It serves as a bathetic contrast to democracy. While a prince must keep his murderous wishes for the King's death silent due to etiquette, democracy allows these grievances to be "bellowed from every stump."
  3. Why does Mencken include the specific postal address of the White House in Bryan’s death scene?
    • Answer: To achieve bathos. The bureaucratic precision of "1600 Pennsylvania Avenue, N.W." undercuts the "gothic horror" of the scene, reducing a symbol of power to a mere mailing address.
  4. How does Mencken use the word "consecrated" in relation to William Jennings Bryan?
    • Answer: He uses it ironically to describe Bryan’s devotion to political "reprisals," mockingly applying the language of religious faith to the act of seeking revenge.
  5. What historical event does Mencken compare to the damage done by Bryan’s anti-evolution campaign?
    • Answer: Sherman’s March to the Sea ("Sherman's army").

VI. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Weaponization of Bathos: Analyze Mencken’s use of bathos in the "Footnote on Lame Ducks." How does the "deliberate descent" from the serious to the trivial function as a more effective tool for political critique than sustained, serious invective?
  2. The Anatomy of the Bryan Portrait: Examine the linguistic layers Mencken uses to describe William Jennings Bryan. How do clinical reportage, religious terminology, and gothic horror combine to transform Bryan from a historical figure into a "walking embodiment of democratic pathology"?
  3. Monarchy vs. Democracy: Based on the "Crown Prince" fantasy, discuss Mencken’s final antithesis regarding the expression of political ambition. How does he use the concept of "discretion" to compare the secret yearnings of royalty with the public "bellowing" of democratic politicians?
  4. The Historian’s Mask: Discuss Mencken’s "mock-historical" tone. How does he balance the posture of a detached historian surveying "wreckage" with the "character assassination" and anecdotal style of a dinner party raconteur?
