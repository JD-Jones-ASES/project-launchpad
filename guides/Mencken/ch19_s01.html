Study Guide: Mencken’s “Footnote on Lame Ducks” (Chapter 19, Section 1)

This study guide provides a comprehensive analysis of H.L. Mencken’s rhetorical and historical treatment of defeated politicians. It explores the structural framing of his "footnote," his psychological profile of the "lame duck," and the specific literary devices—most notably bathos—used to critique democratic politics.

I. Key Concepts and Historical Context

The Rhetorical Framing of the "Footnote"

Mencken titles this section a "footnote" to Part II of his work, serving as a coda or closing argument. This framing is a deliberate rhetorical device:

  • False Modesty: By presenting the material as an afterthought, Mencken implies the examples are so obvious that any educated reader could identify them.
  • Structural Transition: It shifts from the structural analysis of preceding chapters into a sustained historical narrative and a "gallery" of political figures.
  • Tone Shift: The section moves from clinical historical detachment to character assassination and, finally, to absurdist comedy.

The "Lame Duck" Defined

The text distinguishes between two definitions of a "lame duck":

  1. Technical/Constitutional: Historically, defeated Congressmen served until March 4 of the following year. This period was shortened by the Twentieth Amendment (ratified in 1933), which moved the inauguration to January 20.
  2. Mencken’s Psychological Definition: Mencken focuses on the "defeated aspirant"—the politician whose failed ambition for the presidency curdles into a "destructive rage" and a "colossal lust for revenge." His list includes figures who do not fit the technical definition (such as Clay, Roosevelt, and Bryan) but who embody the spirit of the "thwarted ego."

The Portraits of Defeat

Mencken presents a series of historical portraits that grow in length and intensity:

  • Brief Sketches: Henry Clay and John C. Calhoun.
  • Moderate Detail: Aaron Burr and Theodore Roosevelt.
  • The Emotional Peak: William Jennings Bryan. Bryan is the primary target, described as an "ambulent boil" whose anti-evolution campaign is characterized as a gross attack on human dignity.

II. Rhetorical Strategies and Stylistic Devices

1. Bathos (The Art of Sinking)

Bathos is the central rhetorical strategy of this section. It is defined as a deliberate descent from the elevated or serious to the trivial or absurd for comic effect.

Example of Bathos Context Effect
The Leaky Copper Roof Describing Bryan’s death-bed gaze toward the White House (1600 Pennsylvania Ave, N.W.). Reduces a symbol of ultimate power to a building with a minor maintenance problem.
The Crown Prince Fantasy Shifting from a serious historical survey to a sketch of a prince wishing his father would die of endocarditis. Deflates the gravity of the preceding historical analysis through a "calculated anticlimax."
Clinical Specificity Using precise medical terms (temperature of 110 degrees) alongside gothic descriptions (eyes rolling horribly). Creates a jarring collision between the scientific and the grotesque.

2. Hyperbole and Metaphor

Mencken utilizes extreme exaggeration and vivid metaphors to deconstruct his subjects:

  • Hyperbole: Mencken claims the damage done by Bryan was "greater than that done by Sherman’s army." This is not a literal historical judgment but a "performance of outrage."
  • Metaphor: Bryan’s theology is framed not as faith, but as a weapon. He is described as "consecrated" to the act of "reprisals," using religious language to describe destructive political revenge.

3. Diction and Register Mixing

The comedy in this section often derives from the collision of elevated, poetic language with vulgar or clinical terms.

  • Religious/Sin Diction: Words like "consecrated" and "lust" are applied to political maneuvers.
  • Medical Diction: Terms like "tertiary lues" and "endocarditis" are dropped into political anecdotes with deadpan precision.

III. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition and Contextual Use
Ambulent Walking or ambulatory. Mencken describes Bryan as an "ambulent boil," suggesting he is a walking infection.
Amok In a frenzy of violence. Used to add a note of wildness to the portrait of the defeated politician.
Bathos A deliberate descent from the serious to the absurd for comic effect. Derived from the Greek for "depth."
Endocarditis Inflammation of the heart's inner lining. Used in the Crown Prince fantasy to add clinical specificity to a patricidal wish.
Maison de santé French for "house of health"; a polite term for a lunatic asylum. Used as a diplomatic euphemism for locking up a monarch.
Surcease Relief or cessation. Mencken uses this archaic word to mock the base motives of politicians seeking revenge.
Tertiary lues The advanced/final stage of syphilis involving neurological damage. Mencken uses this as a mock diagnosis for exiled aspirants in Paris.
Warlocks Male witches or sorcerers. Applied to minor political figures to suggest their influence is a form of "dark magic."

IV. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why does Mencken refer to this section as a "footnote"?
    • Answer: It serves as a rhetorical device to suggest the examples provided are obvious and supplementary to his main argument, while actually providing a sustained historical narrative.
  2. How did the Twentieth Amendment change the "lame duck" period?
    • Answer: It shortened the time between the election and the inauguration by moving the date from March 4 to January 20.
  3. What does Mencken mean by describing William Jennings Bryan as an "ambulent boil"?
    • Answer: He uses it as a metaphor to frame Bryan as a walking disease or a human embodiment of infection and democratic pathology.
  4. How does the address "1600 Pennsylvania avenue, N.W." contribute to the tone of Bryan's death scene?
    • Answer: The bureaucratic precision of the full postal address, including the compass abbreviation, creates a bathetic contrast with the gothic horror of Bryan’s rolling eyes and high fever.
  5. What is the "final antithesis" presented in the Crown Prince fantasy?
    • Answer: The contrast between monarchy, where murderous ambitions must be kept secret and silent, and democracy, where such grievances are "bellowed from every stump."

V. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Weaponization of Bathos: Analyze how Mencken uses bathos not merely for humor, but as a tool for political and character deconstruction. Use the examples of the "leaky copper roof" and the "Crown Prince" to support your argument.
  2. Mencken’s Psychological Profile of Power: Contrast the technical definition of a "lame duck" with Mencken’s psychological interpretation. How does his focus on "curdled ambition" and "sabotage" change the reader's understanding of political history?
  3. The Intersection of Registers: Discuss how Mencken’s use of medical, religious, and diplomatic diction affects the authority of his narrative. How does the "collision between registers" serve his critique of William Jennings Bryan?
  4. Historical Narrative vs. Performance: Evaluate Mencken’s use of hyperbole (e.g., the comparison to Sherman’s army). To what extent is this section intended to be a factual historical survey, and to what extent is it a "performance of outrage"?
