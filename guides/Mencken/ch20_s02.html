Study Guide: The Will to Peace and the Democratic Illusion

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of H.L. Mencken’s critique of democratic principles as presented in Notes on Democracy, III.1: The Will to Peace. It analyzes the fundamental psychological drivers of the "common man," the inherent conflict between democracy and the superior individual, and the degradation of human dignity within democratic systems.

Core Themes and Key Concepts

1. The Paradox of Liberty

Mencken argues that liberty is a "fraud" when applied to the common man (Homo vulgaris). Contrary to democratic theory, the average person does not possess a genuine love for liberty, sense, justice, or truth. Instead, the experience of true liberty leaves the common man feeling:

  • Uncomfortable: The lack of structure is distressing.
  • Alarmed: Freedom brings a sense of danger.
  • Lonely: The individual is isolated from the safety of the collective.

Ultimately, the common man prefers the "warm, reassuring smell of the herd" and the presence of a "herdsman" over the burden of self-governance.

2. The Will to Peace vs. The Will to Power

Mencken critiques Friedrich Nietzsche’s concept of the "will-to-power" (or "will-to-free-function"). He asserts that while Nietzsche’s vision applies to a "small and disreputable minority," it is inaccurate for the masses.

  • The Lower Level: For the common man, the driving force is a will-to-peace.
  • The Penitentiary Ideal: This peace is described as the "ignominious sort of peace" found by a prisoner in a well-managed penitentiary. To achieve this safety, the common man is willing to sacrifice dignity, pride, and liberty.

3. The Role of the Policeman and the Law

In a democracy, law and its enforcers are viewed with "mysterious sanctity." Mencken defines the policeman as a "charlatan" who offers protection in exchange for obedience. This protection is threefold:

  1. Protection from superiors.
  2. Protection from equals.
  3. Protection from oneself (the most valued service, intended to prevent the common man from ruining himself through vice or excess).

4. Suppression of the Superior Individual

Democracy is characterized by its "dread of heresy" and its effort to force minorities to be "untrue to their nature."

  • The Target: The superior individual (the "free spirit") is the primary victim of democracy.
  • The Method: The system seeks to "iron them out," "pump them dry of self-respect," and break them to the "common harness."
  • The Goal: To reduce the superior individual to the level of a "docile John Doe."

5. The Degradation of Dignity

While democracy provides the "have-nots" with the delusion of equality—which may offer a superficial sense of dignity—it ultimately destroys true dignity. In a democratic society, leaders are stripped of their rational dignity:

  • Kings and Presidents become common figures "jabbering of normalcy."
  • Religious leaders become "Methodist bishops in natty business-suits."
  • Military leaders become back-slapping "Elks" haranguing social clubs.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to Mencken, what is the "exclusive possession" of a small minority?
    • Answer: Liberty, knowledge, courage, and honor.
  2. How does Mencken describe the common man's view of liberty in others?
    • Answer: He thinks of it only as something that should be taken away from them.
  3. What does the "will-to-peace" prioritize above all else?
    • Answer: Safety and the absence of alarm, even at the cost of dignity and liberty.
  4. According to the text, what is the "only sort of liberty that is real under democracy"?
    • Answer: The liberty of the "have-nots" to destroy the liberty of the "haves."
  5. What historical Roman law does Mencken cite as a tool to protect the state against aristocrats and free spirits?
    • Answer: The lex de majestate.
  6. How does the text compare the democratic "dread of heresy" to a specific social figure?
    • Answer: It is compared to a Sunday-school superintendent’s dread of "scarlet women, light wines and beer, and the unreadable works of Charles Darwin."
  7. What is the "measure of civilization" according to Mencken?
    • Answer: The extent to which superior individuals resist being brought down to the common level and survive.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Psychology of the Herd: Analyze Mencken’s claim that the common man is "intolerably lonely" when free. How does this psychological profile justify the common man’s "veneration for policemen" and the surrender of individual rights for collective safety?
  2. Democracy as a Leveling Mechanism: Mencken asserts that "democracy stands in greater peril of the free spirit than any sort of despotism." Explore this argument, focusing on how democratic societies use "the common harness" to neutralize those who hold themselves answerable only to their own notions.
  3. The Delusion of Equality: Discuss the trade-off Mencken describes between the "have-nots" gaining a sense of dignity through the "delusion" of equality and the subsequent loss of rational dignity in leadership. Use his examples of political and religious figures to support your analysis.

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Droit de Seigneur: Historically the "right of the lord"; used here to describe the superior individual's right to follow their own nature.
  • Free Spirit: The superior individual who resists the "common harness" of democracy and remains answerable only to their own intellect and standards.
  • Homo Vulgaris: The common or average man; the "have-nots" who prioritize safety over liberty.
  • Lex de Majestate: A Roman law regarding treason or crimes against the state; Mencken identifies its true purpose as a democratic tool to break aristocrats.
  • The Herd: The mass of common men who seek comfort in numbers and the direction of a "herdsman."
  • Will-to-Peace: The fundamental drive of the lower classes to seek a secure, controlled environment (similar to a well-managed prison) rather than the challenges of freedom.
  • Will-to-Power: Nietzsche’s concept of the drive toward "free-function" and self-assertion, which Mencken argues is only applicable to a small minority.
