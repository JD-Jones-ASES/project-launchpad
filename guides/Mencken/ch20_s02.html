Study Guide: The Will to Peace (Notes on Democracy, III.1)

This study guide provides a comprehensive analysis of H.L. Mencken’s critique of democratic principles as presented in "The Will to Peace." It explores the psychological motivations of the "common man," the structural functions of democratic society, and the resulting impact on individual liberty and human dignity.

Key Concepts and Analysis

1. The Paradox of Liberty

The text posits that while democracy claims to embody liberty, the "common man" (Homo vulgaris) does not actually desire it. To the average individual, liberty is a source of discomfort, alarm, and loneliness. Rather than seeking the freedom to act independently, the common man seeks the "warm, reassuring smell of the herd."

  • The Burden of Freedom: Liberty is described as "too cold to be borne" or "too hot for [the common man's] hands." It is a quality that requires a "special sort of man"—a minority often viewed as outlaws in a democratic society.
  • Safety over Liberty: The fundamental drive of the common man is not the "will-to-power" or "will-to-free-function," but a "will-to-peace." This is defined as a desire for the ignominious safety found in a well-managed penitentiary.

2. The Role of the "Policeman"

In a democracy, the veneration of law and the policeman is central to the common man's sense of security. The policeman is characterized as a "charlatan" who offers a trade: protection in exchange for obedience. This protection is three-fold:

  1. From Superiors: Protecting the common man from those with more power or talent.
  2. From Equals: Protecting the common man from his peers.
  3. From Himself: Protecting the common man from his own perceived vices (e.g., smoking opium, frequenting night clubs).

3. Democracy vs. The Superior Individual

The text argues that democracy is inherently hostile to the "free spirit" or the "superior individual." Because the "mob" fears heresy and dissent, democratic systems work to reduce subversive minorities to impotence.

  • Suppression of Nature: Democracy is described as a history of efforts to force minorities to be "untrue to their nature."
  • The Lex de Majestate: This Roman law is cited as a democratic invention intended to protect the state against aristocrats and free spirits who hold themselves answerable only to their own notions.
  • Leveling: The goal of democracy is to "iron out" free spirits, pump them dry of self-respect, and make them "docile John Does."

4. The Degradation of Dignity

While democracy offers a "delusion" of equality that may temporarily augment the dignity of the "have-nots," it ultimately kills the very dignity it claims to love. By bringing the superior down to the level of the common, the roles of leadership—the King, the Pope, and the Generalissimo—are stripped of their rational dignity and transformed into common, mundane figures (e.g., a "Methodist bishop in a natty business-suit").

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to the text, why does the common man react with "so little murmuring" when his liberties are invaded? Answer: Because his love of liberty is imaginary; he is actually uncomfortable and alarmed when free and prefers the safety and companionship of the "herd."

2. How does Mencken redefine Nietzsche’s "will-to-power" regarding the "lower levels" of humanity? Answer: He argues it should be called a "will-to-peace"—a longing for the simplest, most ignominious sort of security, comparable to that of a prisoner in a well-managed penitentiary.

3. What are the three things a policeman allegedly protects the common man from? Answer: His superiors, his equals, and himself.

4. What is the "only sort of liberty that is real under democracy," according to the document? Answer: The liberty of the "have-nots" to destroy the liberty of the "haves."

5. How does the text describe the democratic reaction to "heresy"? Answer: Democracy is in "vast dread" of heresy, comparing its fear to that of a Sunday-school superintendent fearing "scarlet women, light wines and beer."

6. What is the "measure of civilization" mentioned in the text? Answer: The extent to which superior individuals resist being brought down to the common level and manage to survive.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Will-to-Peace vs. The Will-to-Power: Analyze the text’s critique of Nietzsche’s philosophy. How does the shift from a "will-to-power" to a "will-to-peace" change the understanding of democratic stability and the behavior of the "common man"?
  2. The Function of the "Policeman" in Social Control: Evaluate the argument that the "policeman" is a democratic invention designed to protect the individual from himself. What does this suggest about the document's view on the "ice-wagon drivers" and "Y.M.C.A. secretaries" of society?
  3. The Victimization of the Superior Individual: Discuss the claim that the superior individual is the "chief victim of the democratic process." Use the document's references to the lex de majestate and the "common harness" to support your analysis.
  4. The Illusion of Equality and the Loss of Dignity: Explore the paradox presented at the end of the text: that democracy "kills the thing it loves" (human dignity) by attempting to make all men equal. How does the transformation of historical figures (the King, the Pope, the Generalissimo) into "common" men illustrate this point?

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Homo vulgaris The "common man"; characterized by a preference for safety and herd-belonging over true liberty.
Will-to-Peace The primary drive of the common man; a desire for safety and the absence of conflict, even at the cost of dignity and freedom.
The Herd The collective mass of common men; provides a "warm, reassuring smell" of safety and conformity.
Free Spirit The superior individual or "man of superior kidney" who values independence and is often viewed as an outlaw or heretic in a democracy.
Lex de majestate A Roman law cited as a tool used by the people (via Saturninus) to protect the state against aristocrats and independent thinkers.
Droit de seigneur A term used here to imply a right of the superior; the text suggests democracy attempts to prevent individuals from following their own nature.
Heresy Any thought or act that dissents from the common democratic consensus; something democracies "dread" and seek to put down.
Charlatan A term used to describe the policeman, who offers a false or exaggerated sense of protection in exchange for total obedience.
Tabakparlement A reference to a common or informal gathering (specifically regarding President Coolidge), used to illustrate the loss of "rational dignity" in democratic leaders.
