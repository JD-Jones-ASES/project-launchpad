# Study Guide: The Origins and Dogma of Democracy

This study guide examines the historical and philosophical analysis of democracy as presented in the text "His Appearance in the World" from *Notes on Democracy*. It explores the transition of the "common man" from a worker to a political ideal and the subsequent development of democratic dogma.

## Key Concepts and Themes

### The Intellectual Origin of Democracy
Contrary to the belief that democracy arose from a "bawling from below," the text argues it was a fashion imposed by upper-level intellectuals and "metaphysicians." These individuals—described as "sentimentalists" and the ancestors of modern Liberals—created an idealized version of the common man. This ideal was rooted in Jean-Jacques Rousseau’s concept of the "noble savage," portraying the working class as beings of "ineffable virtues" and "mystical merit."

### The "Mob" vs. The Ideal
The text draws a sharp distinction between the "democratic man" imagined by intellectuals and the actual members of the "mob" (the electorate):
*   **The Intellectual View:** The people possess a "seraphic intuition" and a "reservoir of righteousness" capable of solving any problem.
*   **The Reality of the Mob:** Historically, the common man was not interested in democratic ideals or ruling. Instead, he sought "concrete and highly materialistic" improvements: more food, higher wages, lower taxes, and less work.
*   **Historical Precedent:** During the French Revolution, the Paris proletariat killed their King only to eventually kill the leaders who misled them, ultimately welcoming a new King and aristocracy back into power by 1796.

### The Evolution of Democratic Rights
The text outlines a progression of the status and self-perception of the lower classes:
1.  **Initial Status:** Slaves or serfs with no political voice.
2.  **The Rise of Rhetoric:** Through oratory (particularly in England and America), democratic theory seeped into the masses.
3.  **Self-Veneration:** As the common man gained the right to criticize his masters, he began to believe his own wishes were "natural rights" and that he possessed a "peculiar rectitude of thought."
4.  **The Doctrine of Superiority:** By the mid-19th century (1828 in America, 1848 in Europe), the belief emerged that all "moral excellence" resided in the "inferior four-fifths of mankind."
5.  **Extreme Proletarianism:** A "philosopher out of the gutter" eventually pushed this to the extreme, suggesting the superior minority had no rights at all, leading to the "dictatorship of the proletariat."

### The Modern Democratic Dogma
The text identifies two fundamental axioms that govern modern Christian nations:
1.  The masses have an inalienable right to govern themselves.
2.  The masses are competent to do so.

When the masses commit "gross and lamentable imbecilities," the dogma maintains that they are merely misinformed by exploiters; therefore, the suggested remedy is always "more education" or "more democracy."

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **According to the text, who were the primary architects of the democratic idea?**
2.  **How does the text describe the actual desires of the "early democratic man" compared to the theories of the intellectuals?**
3.  **What historical event in 1793 is used to illustrate the mob’s true preference for authority over democratic rule?**
4.  **What shifted in the self-perception of the common man as he gained the freedom to criticize his masters?**
5.  **By 1828 in America, what specific doctrine regarding "moral excellence" had arisen?**
6.  **According to the "philosopher out of the gutter," to whom does the world exclusively belong?**
7.  **What are the two fundamental axioms of democracy that the text claims are accepted "everywhere in Christendom"?**
8.  **What is the standard democratic remedy for "swinish" or "naughty" behavior by the masses?**

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

### 1. The "Noble Savage" in Modern Governance
Analyze the text's critique of Rousseau’s "noble savage" concept. How does the author argue that this poetic idealization of the working class has shaped modern political structures, and what are the "important consequences" mentioned in the text?

### 2. Rhetoric as a "Stealthy Plague"
The text suggests that democratic theory did not take hold through logic but through "rhetoric" and "oratory," comparing it to a plague. Discuss how the text describes the spread of these ideas in America and England versus Continental Europe, and evaluate the role of "veneration" in the common man’s eventual acceptance of democracy.

### 3. The Paradox of the Proletariat
The author claims that the "dictatorship of the proletariat" proved "impracticable," yet the "underlying democratic dogma" remains stronger than ever. Construct an argument based on the text explaining why democratic failure (referred to as "gross and lamentable imbecilities") frequently results in a call for "more democracy" rather than a rejection of the system.

## Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Acroamatic** | Pertaining to oral instruction; esoteric or secret. |
| **Dulcet** | Sweet and pleasant, especially to the ear. |
| **Ineffable** | Too great or extreme to be expressed or described in words. |
| **Lugubrious** | Looking or sounding sad and dismal. |
| **Metaphysician** | One who studies the fundamental nature of reality and being; here used to describe intellectuals who create political theories. |
| **Modernism** | A movement toward modifying traditional beliefs in accordance with modern ideas. |
| **Noble Savage** | A literary and philosophical stock character who embodies the concept of an idealized indigene, untainted by civilization. |
| **Omnipotence** | Unlimited power; the quality of being all-powerful. |
| **Omniscience** | The quality of knowing everything. |
| **Proletariat** | Workers or working-class people, regarded collectively. |
| **Rectitude** | Morally correct behavior or thinking; righteousness. |
| **Sagacity** | The quality of being wise or having good judgment. |
| **Seraphic** | Characteristic of or resembling an angel or seraph. |
| **The Mob** | The author’s term for the democratic electorate or the mass of ordinary voters deemed unfit to govern. |
| **Twittering** | Making a succession of light, high-pitched sounds; chattering. |
