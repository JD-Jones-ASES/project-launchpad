# Study Guide: Analysis of "The Eternal Mob," Section I.9

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the rhetorical analysis of Section I.9 from H.L. Mencken’s work. It explores the themes of democratic man, the structural function of the text, and the specific linguistic strategies employed to deliver a final verdict on the nature of the "mob."

---

## Key Concepts and Themes

### The Mob as a Permanent Feature
The central thesis of Section I.9 is that the "mob"—the common man on the "nether levels"—is not a transient social problem but a permanent, unchangeable feature of human civilization. Mencken argues that no amount of education, reform, or revolution can alter the fundamental nature of the democratic citizen. This section serves as the **peroration** for Part I of the book, gathering the themes of intellectual failing, political manipulability, and hostility to progress into a single, dark conclusion.

### Rhetorical Structure and Purpose
Section I.9 functions in two primary ways:
1.  **As a Summation:** It compresses the arguments of the previous eight sections into a final judgment.
2.  **As a Bridge:** By establishing an uncompromising verdict on the nature of "democratic man," it prepares the reader for Part II, which focuses on the "Democratic State"—the institutions built by and for the mob.

### The Syntax of Abundance: The Cumulative Sentence
Mencken relies heavily on the **cumulative sentence** (also known as a "loose sentence") to overwhelm the reader. Unlike a periodic sentence, which withholds the main idea until the end to create suspense, a cumulative sentence begins with the main clause and then piles on details, modifiers, and amplifications. This creates an effect of "saturation," where the initial claim feels increasingly inevitable as layers of evidence are added.

### Antithesis as a Structural Principle
Mencken uses **antithesis**—the pairing of opposites—to illustrate the perverse judgment of the mob. By contrasting figures the mob admires with those it persecutes, Mencken suggests a consistent pattern of hostility toward excellence and truth.

| The Mob's Heroes (Tyranny/Fraud) | The Mob's Victims (Truth/Reform) |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Nero:** Tyrannical Roman emperor. | **Galileo:** Astronomer persecuted for his scientific findings. |
| **Torquemada:** Grand Inquisitor of the Spanish Inquisition. | **Savonarola:** Friar executed for challenging Church corruption. |
| **Cagliostro:** Adventurer and confidence man. | **Danton:** Revolutionary leader with courage. |
| **Barnum:** Showman and huckster. | **Bryan:** (A subtler pairing) Populist leader/demagogue. |

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **What is the significance of Mencken's shift in tone in Section I.9 compared to previous sections?**
    *   *Answer:* While earlier sections mixed contempt with wit, Section I.9 is predominantly bleak and final. The humor shifts from playful to "gallows humor," and the voice takes on the quality of a secular prophet delivering a final judgment.

2.  **How does Mencken use "litotes" to describe human progress?**
    *   *Answer:* He uses understatement through negation, claiming that in two thousand years, the common man has "moved an inch." This "inch" is ironically revealed to be a move from one form of barbarism (Roman arena sports) to another (lynching parties).

3.  **Define a "tricolon" and explain how Mencken uses it in his description of the common man.**
    *   *Answer:* A tricolon is a rhetorical structure consisting of three parallel elements. Mencken uses it in the phrase "He is lazy, improvident and unclean," where each adjective increases in severity, moving from a mundane fault to a biblical-style condemnation.

4.  **What is the rhetorical effect of Mencken's "vocabulary of finality"?**
    *   *Answer:* By using absolute terms like "eternally," "infallibly," "always," and "never," Mencken closes down the possibility of debate or hope for reform, framing his conclusions as inescapable truths.

5.  **Why does Mencken refer to the Apostle Paul as a "Stammvater"?**
    *   *Answer:* He uses the German term for "ancestor" or "patriarch" to link his critique to the tradition of German cultural criticism (specifically Nietzsche) and to distance himself from the English-speaking "mob."

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Iconoclast's Perspective on Religion:** Analyze Mencken’s attack on the Apostle Paul and modern "mob-masters." How does Mencken use culinary and carnival metaphors to degrade the theological subjects he discusses? In what way does he frame religion as a tool for "enchanting" the mob?

2.  **Syntax as Argument:** Compare and contrast the rhetorical effects of the **periodic sentence** and the **cumulative sentence**. Using examples from the text, argue how Mencken’s choice of sentence structure reinforces his portrayal of the common man as an unchanging, burdensome weight on civilization.

3.  **The History of Hostility:** Examine Mencken’s use of historical pairings (e.g., Nero vs. Galileo). How does this use of proper names serve as "shorthand" for his argument? Does this strategy effectively sort the "educated reader" from the "mob," as the text suggests?

## Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Blackguard:** A villain or scoundrel. Mencken uses this archaic term to describe the Apostle Peter.
*   **Cumulative Sentence:** A sentence that starts with a main clause followed by successive phrases or clauses that elaborate on the initial point.
*   **Hosannas:** Shouts of praise; used ironically by Mencken to describe the mob’s celebration of tyrants.
*   **Improvident:** Lacking foresight; failing to provide for the future.
*   **Litotes:** A figure of speech featuring understatement through the use of negation (e.g., "moved an inch" to describe stagnation).
*   **Nether:** Lower or situated below; often carries overtones of the underworld or hell.
*   **Peroration:** The concluding part of a speech or argument, intended to inspire emotion and summarize key themes.
*   **Stammvater:** A German term for ancestor, progenitor, or patriarch.
*   **Tricolon:** A rhetorical device involving a series of three parallel words, phrases, or clauses.
