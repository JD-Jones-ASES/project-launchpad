Study Guide: Mencken’s Analysis of the Democratic Politician

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of H.L. Mencken’s rhetorical and clinical examination of the politician within a democratic framework, as analyzed in "The Politician Under Democracy." It covers key thematic concepts, linguistic strategies, and structural devices used to strip away the pretensions of political figures.

Key Concepts and Thematic Analysis

The Politician as a Clinical Specimen

Mencken shifts the focus from democratic systems to the individual "human product" of those systems. Rather than treating the politician as a figure of authority, Mencken adopts the perspective of a naturalist examining a biological specimen. This rhetorical stance invites the reader to observe the subject from a distance of intellectual superiority, effectively dehumanizing the politician into a creature driven by instinct rather than choice.

The "Boss System" and Historical Context

The analysis references the American urban political "bosses" active from the 1870s through the 1930s (e.g., William Tweed, Tom Pendergast, and Frank Hague). Mencken views this system—which traded patronage and services for votes—not merely as a vehicle for corruption, but as a reflection of the electorate that sustains it.

The Duality of the "Demaslave"

A central theme is the inherent contradiction of the democratic leader. Mencken uses the term demaslave to describe a politician who, while appearing to lead (demagogue), actually follows the whims and weaknesses of the mob. This "shot-gun marriage" of roles forces the politician into a state of perpetual self-degradation.

Extended Analogies: Courtiers and Chorus Girls

Mencken utilizes two primary analogies to define the politician's character:

  • The Courtier: Democratic politics is framed as "feudalism in reverse," where the politician acts as a servile nobleman flattering the "king" (the mob) to maintain power.
  • The Chorus Girl: This analogy compares political compromise to sexual exploitation. Just as a chorus girl might sacrifice her person for a job, the politician sacrifices honor for office. Both figures eventually view their degradation with complacency, retaining vanity (pleasure in the regard of others) while losing pride (independent self-respect).

Linguistic and Stylistic Strategies

The Three Registers of Diction

Mencken moves between three distinct vocabularies to expose the reality of political life:

Register Examples Purpose
Legal/Political "Altruist," "convictions," "honour" Representing the politician’s false self-image.
Vernacular/Physical "Butter his parsnips," "tarred with the same stick" Dragging the politician from the platform to the "mud."
Zoological/Clinical "Specimen," "breed," "congenital" Treating political behavior as predictable biological instinct.

Syntactic Architecture

Mencken employs specific sentence structures to deliver his critiques:

  • The Long-Short Combo: He builds long, periodic sentences that accumulate evidence or parallel clauses (often using semicolons) to create an oppressive catalogue of flaws, followed by a short, blunt sentence that acts as a "verdict."
  • Periodic Sentences: By delaying the main clause until the end, Mencken builds suspense and ensures the reader absorbs the full weight of the argument before reaching the final, often devastating, word.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Who is Robert Michels, and what concept does Mencken borrow from him? Robert Michels was a German-Italian sociologist who advanced the “iron law of oligarchy,” which posits that all organizations, regardless of how democratic they intend to be, inevitably develop into oligarchies.

  2. Explain the significance of the term "armamentarium" in the context of Mencken’s writing. Borrowed from medical terminology (a doctor’s collection of treatments), Mencken applies it to the politician's "toolkit of deceptions," giving political fraud an air of professional, clinical competence.

  3. What does Mencken mean by the phrase "his ear is ever close to the ground"? This vernacular phrase suggests that the politician is constantly monitoring the whims and "preposterous alarms" of the common people rather than leading based on principle.

  4. How does Mencken use the device of "apparent concession"? He claims to describe the politician not at his "inordinate worst" but in the "sunshine of normalcy." This intensifies the critique by suggesting that the described depravity is actually the standard behavior.

  5. According to the text, what is the difference between "vanity" and " pride"? Vanity is the pleasure taken in the regard and applause of others (which politicians retain), whereas pride is an internal self-respect independent of others (which politicians lose through compromise).

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Ethics of the Clinical Gaze: Analyze Mencken’s strategy of treating the politician as a "zoological specimen." How does this shift in perspective affect the reader's perception of political authority, and what are the moral implications of viewing human behavior as "congenital" rather than choice-driven?
  2. The Vaudeville Verdict: Discuss the impact of Mencken’s tonal instability. How does his movement between the "judge’s bench" (formal indictment) and the "barroom" (vernacular humor) serve to strip the politician of dignity? Use the "chorus girl" analogy as a primary example.
  3. The Architecture of Conviction: Examine Mencken’s use of syntax—specifically the periodic sentence and the semicolon—as tools of persuasion. How do these structures force the reader to accept his conclusions as "axioms" rather than mere opinions?

Glossary of Key Terms

  • Armamentarium: A collection of equipment or methods; used by Mencken to describe the deceptive tools available to a politician.
  • Clapper-clawing: A term meaning to scratch or claw noisily; used to describe the vulgar, animalistic applause of a crowd.
  • Demaslave: Mencken’s coinage for a politician who pretends to follow the people while appearing to lead them; a hybrid of "demagogue" and "slave."
  • Iron Law of Oligarchy: The theory that all organizations eventually become controlled by a small elite group.
  • Periodic Sentence: A sentence that delays its main point or completion until the very end to build suspense and emphasis.
  • The Boss System: A historical American political structure where "city bosses" controlled patronage networks, trading jobs and favors for votes.
  • Zeugma: A figure of speech where one word (usually a verb) governs two or more words in different ways (e.g., one literal and one figurative). Mencken achieves a similar effect by yoking abstract concepts like "dissembled" with physical ones like "boot-polish."
