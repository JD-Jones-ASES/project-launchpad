# Study Guide: H.L. Mencken’s "The Future of Democracy"

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the themes, arguments, and terminology found in Section 2, Chapter 24 of "The Future of Democracy" (Notes on Democracy, IV.1). It is designed to facilitate a deep understanding of H.L. Mencken’s critique of the democratic system.

---

## I. Key Concepts and Themes

### 1. Democracy as Pathology
Mencken positions himself not as a "therapeutic" agent (one who provides a cure) but as a "pathologist" (one who diagnoses a disease). He argues that democracy is a "self-limiting disease" and that his role is to examine its defects realistically rather than proposing a "cure-all" system.

### 2. The "Cure-All" Mentality
A central tenet of the text is that democracy functions on the premise that every human problem is solvable through political means. Mencken suggests that democracy attempts to "repeal the laws of nature" and re-enact them with "moral amendments," treating voting and law-making as magical solutions for immutable human realities like stupidity, cowardice, and poverty.

### 3. The Secular Religion
The text posits that democracy has replaced Medieval Christianity as the dominant system of faith. Like a religion, it relies on "hocus-pocus" and "mysticism" rather than logic. Mencken argues that democratic doctrines are "not vulnerable to logical attack" because they lie outside the range of rational ideas, much like religious dogmas.

### 4. Plutocracy vs. Aristocracy
Mencken distinguishes between the "missing aristocracy" and the "plutocracy" that replaces it in democratic states:
*   **True Aristocracy:** Characterized by culture, public spirit, honesty, honor, and courage.
*   **Plutocracy:** Driven by the "striving for money," it lacks tradition and goal. Mencken describes the plutocracy as "incurably timorous," living in fear of revolution and demagogues, and bringing the "ignobilities" of the mob from which it recently emerged.

### 5. The Paradox of Equality
The text highlights a "suicidal" contradiction in democratic philosophy. It offers the "inferior man" (John Doe) a way to rise above his peer (Richard Roe), but then nullifies the benefit of that rise by insisting that Roe is Doe's equal. This leads to the invention of "brummagem" (counterfeit) class distinctions and titles to satisfy the human need for hierarchy.

### 6. The Failure of the Intelligentsia
Mencken critiques the "pedagogues" and "Liberals" who should theoretically challenge the system. He argues they are hindered by a "haunting fear of losing their jobs," causing their revolts to "peter out in metaphysics." Consequently, politics becomes a choice between the plutocracy and "preposterous impossibilists."

## II. Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition according to the Source Context |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Pathology** | The study of the nature of a disease; Mencken’s chosen approach to analyzing democracy. |
| **Therapeutics** | The branch of medicine concerned with the treatment of disease; Mencken rejects this role, claiming he has no "cure" for democracy. |
| **Bona fides** | Good faith; sincerity. Mencken notes that his refusal to offer a remedy leads others to doubt his sincerity. |
| **Hocus-pocus** | Meaningless talk or activities used to deceive; used to describe democratic arguments like the Gettysburg Address. |
| **Ratiocination** | The process of exact thinking or reasoning; Mencken argues the majority of men find this "unbearably painful." |
| **Plutocracy** | A governing class defined by wealth rather than merit or tradition; described as lacking the courage and honor of a true aristocracy. |
| **Brummagem** | Cheap, showy, or counterfeit; used to describe the titles and "serene highnesses" of democratic leaders. |
| **Vox populi, vox Dei** | "The voice of the people is the voice of God." Mencken disputes this, suggesting the "voice of the people" is often a raucous defiance of divine will. |
| **Honnête homme** | A man of honor and integrity; Mencken places the plutocracy far below this standard. |
| **Impossibilists** | A term used for radicals or fanatics (like the "Bolsheviki") who offer "impossible alternatives" in the democratic political landscape. |

## III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **How does Mencken describe the difference between his "business" and the expectations of democratic society?**
    *   *Answer:* Mencken describes his business as "diagnosis" and "pathology" (identifying the disease) rather than "prognosis" or "therapeutics" (predicting the future or providing a cure). Democratic society, however, views the lack of a remedy as a lack of license to discuss the disease.

2.  **According to the text, what was the Medieval Christian view of human suffering compared to the modern democratic view?**
    *   *Answer:* Medieval Christianity viewed the evils of the world (sin, poverty, stupidity) as "incurable" and "immutable realities," deferring the quest for a perfect order until heaven. Democracy views these as solvable through practical politics and legislation.

3.  **What example does Mencken use to illustrate the "appalling gyrations" of democratic exponents?**
    *   *Answer:* He points to James Bryce’s "Modern Democracies," noting that Bryce amasses evidence that democracy does not work but concludes with a declaration that it does.

4.  **Why does Mencken characterize the American Liberal’s support for the League of Nations as "clinical material"?**
    *   *Answer:* Because the Liberal initially argued the League would save the world, but when confronted with proof of its "fraudulence," switched to the doctrine that simply *believing* in it would save the world—illustrating "democratic psychopathology."

5.  **What is the "last joke upon man" according to the author?**
    *   *Answer:* That man may never learn how to govern himself in a rational and competent manner.

6.  **Why does Mencken believe the plutocracy is "comprehensible to the mob"?**
    *   *Answer:* Because the plutocracy’s aspirations are essentially those of "inferior men"—the striving for money—which is an ideal the mob understands and shares.

7.  **What is the primary reason "pedagogues" (teachers/intellectuals) fail to create a "party of liberty"?**
    *   *Answer:* They are "marked by their haunting fear of losing their jobs," and their families' needs make the "costs of heresy" prohibitive, leading them to conform rather than act heroically.

## IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Secularization of Faith:** Analyze Mencken’s argument that democracy is not a political system but a form of "mysticism" and a substitute for religion. How does his comparison to the "dogma of the Trinity" and "Transubstantiation" support his critique of democratic reasoning?

2.  **The Anatomy of the Plutocracy:** Compare and contrast Mencken’s definitions of "aristocracy" and "plutocracy." Why does he believe the "timorousness" of the wealthy class is particularly damaging to a democratic state, and why does he argue they can never truly replace a "genuine aristocracy"?

3.  **The Burden of the Insoluble:** Explore Mencken’s claim that "it is one of the peculiar intellectual accompaniments of democracy that the concept of the insoluble becomes unfashionable." How does this refusal to accept "immutable realities" shape the way democratic governments approach legislation and social problems, according to the text?

4.  **The Requirements for Liberty:** Mencken concludes that democracy will never have a "party of liberty" until it "invents and installs a genuine aristocracy." Evaluate his reasoning for this claim, focusing on his views of the mob, the "pedagogue," and the necessity of "aristocratic security" for free opinion.
