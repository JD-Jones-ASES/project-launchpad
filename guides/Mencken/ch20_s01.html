Study Guide: The Will to Peace (Chapter 20, Section 1)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of H.L. Mencken’s "The Will to Peace," a pivotal section in Notes on Democracy. It explores the central arguments, rhetorical strategies, philosophical influences, and linguistic techniques used to critique the democratic citizen’s relationship with liberty.

I. Core Argument and Themes

The Primacy of Safety over Liberty

The central thesis of this section is that the common man does not actually desire liberty; instead, he craves safety and security. Mencken argues that liberty is a "cold and lonely" state that requires courage and self-reliance—traits he believes the average citizen lacks. The common man finds comfort in the "warm, reassuring smell of the herd" and prefers a "policeman" to protect him from his own impulses rather than the responsibility of true freedom.

Democracy as a Destructive Force

Mencken asserts that democracy is the most efficient destroyer of liberty because the "mob" cannot tolerate a free spirit. The democratic system is described as a force that moves to crush the individual. By the end of the section, Mencken reduces democratic dignity to a farce, illustrating how once-great offices of civilization have been occupied by "buffoons."

The "Elect" Reader

Mencken’s rhetorical strategy targets an "educated skeptic" who views himself as one of the "superior" men. By engaging with Mencken’s critique, the reader is flattered into believing they are part of an intellectual "elect," separate from the "herd" being described.

II. Philosophical Context

Mencken draws on German idealist philosophy to frame his critique of the common man’s desires.

Philosopher Key Concept Influence on Mencken
Friedrich Nietzsche Will to Power; Herd Morality Influenced Mencken’s view of the common man as part of a "herd" that rejects individual excellence.
Arthur Schopenhauer Will to Live; Pessimism Provided the foundation for the "will to peace"—the fundamental drive for security over struggle.

Mencken contrasts these high-level philosophical concepts with the "ignominious" reality of modern democratic life.

III. Rhetorical Analysis

Voice and Tone

Mencken adopts the persona of a "weary diagnostician" or a physician naming symptoms of a terminal disease.

  • Initial Tone: Sober and philosophical, engaging with theory.
  • Transition: As the argument moves from theory to practice, the tone darkens into contempt.
  • Final Tone: Grotesque comedy and savage amusement, using "comic deflation" to mock democratic institutions.

Syntax and Rhythm

Mencken’s writing utilizes a "forensic" rhythm. He often constructs long, complex sentences that build through qualification and evidence, only to deliver a "verdict" in a short, blunt, declarative sentence.

  • The Use of the Dash:
    • Interruption/Commentary: Introduces unexpected consequences or converts observations into indictments.
    • Enumeration: Used to mimic legal or contractual language, reinforcing metaphors of liberty as a transaction.

Diction and Register

Mencken employs two simultaneous registers:

  1. Philosophical: Using terms like "will-to-power," "dignity," and "liberty."
  2. Institutional/Vernacular: Using images from the barnyard, the prison, and the circus. The "signature move" of Mencken’s style is the collision of these two registers; the gap between dignified language and undignified reality forms the core of his argument.

IV. Dominant Figures of Speech

The Penitentiary Analogy

Mencken compares the common man’s ideal existence to that of a "trusty in a well-managed penitentiary."

  • The Trusty: Represents a person who has forfeited liberty for privileges.
  • The Penitentiary: Suggests that democratic citizenship is actually a form of comfortable, obedient confinement.
  • Well-managed: Implies the common man values a government that runs smoothly over one that is just.

Apostrophe

While subtle, Mencken uses apostrophe by treating "Liberty" as a character in a drama or a patient on an examination table. He addresses liberty with clinical detachment rather than reverence, treating an abstract ideal as a physical object that can be "possessed, mishandled, or taken away."

Comic Deflation

In the closing paragraphs, Mencken uses rapid analogies to demolish democratic dignity:

  • The King is reduced to Harding.
  • The Pope is reduced to a Methodist bishop.
  • The Generalissimo becomes Pershing slapping the backs of Elks at a Rotary meeting.

V. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to Mencken, what does the common man want more than liberty?
    • Answer: The common man wants safety and the "warm, reassuring smell of the herd."
  2. How does Mencken describe the nature of liberty?
    • Answer: He describes it as "cold and lonely," demanding courage and self-reliance.
  3. What does Mencken mean by the "forensic" rhythm of his sentences?
    • Answer: It is a structure where several pieces of evidence are presented in long, qualifying sentences, followed by a short, blunt sentence that acts as a verdict.
  4. In the penitentiary analogy, why is the term "well-managed" significant?
    • Answer: It implies that the common man’s ideal is a government that functions efficiently, regardless of whether it is just or allows for freedom.
  5. How does Mencken’s use of the word "simply" in the phrase "simply wants to be safe" function rhetorically?
    • Answer: It suggests that the desire is both "merely" safety and that the desire is intellectually "primitive" or uncomplicated.
  6. Why does Mencken borrow the theological term "heresy"?
    • Answer: To imply that democracy has become a religion that punishes political dissent as if it were a religious crime.

VI. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Architecture of Contempt: Analyze how Mencken uses the collision of "philosophical" and "barnyard/institutional" registers to degrade the concepts of democracy and the common man. How does this diction support his role as a "diagnostician"?
  2. Safety vs. Agency: Mencken argues that liberty is a burden the average person cannot bear. Evaluate his argument that democracy is the "most efficient destroyer" of liberty. Does his "penitentiary analogy" successfully redefine the role of the citizen?
  3. The Rhetoric of the Elect: Discuss how Mencken’s prose creates an "in-group" between the author and the reader. How does his tone of "resigned authority" and "savage amusement" serve to flatter the reader’s intellect while condemning the "mob"?

VII. Glossary of Key Terms

  • Apostrophe: A figure of speech in which a speaker addresses an absent person, an abstract concept (like Liberty), or a thing as if it were present and capable of responding.
  • Charlatan: A person falsely claiming to have special knowledge or skill; a fraud. Mencken applies this to the policeman, framing law enforcement as a "protection racket."
  • Heresy: Belief or opinion contrary to orthodox doctrine. Mencken uses this to describe political dissent in a democratic society.
  • Ignominious: Deserving or causing public disgrace or shame. Mencken uses this to describe the "peace" craved by the common man at the cost of his liberty.
  • Prehensile: Capable of grasping. Mencken uses this metaphorically to describe the "mob's" greedy seizure of power over the individual.
  • Will-to-Peace: Mencken’s adaptation of Schopenhauer’s "will to live"; the primitive drive for security and the absence of conflict.
  • Will-to-Power: A Nietzschean concept describing the fundamental drive for achievement and ambition, which Mencken contrasts with the democratic desire for safety.
