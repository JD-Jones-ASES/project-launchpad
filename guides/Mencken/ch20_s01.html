Study Guide: H.L. Mencken’s “The Will to Peace” (Notes on Democracy)

This study guide provides a comprehensive analysis of Chapter 20, Section 1, titled “The Will to Peace,” from H.L. Mencken’s Notes on Democracy. This section represents a critical shift in Mencken's work, moving from a diagnosis of democratic institutions to a trial of its core principles.

Key Concepts and Themes

1. The Structural Pivot of Part III

In earlier sections of Notes on Democracy, Mencken focuses on the "anatomizing" of the democratic citizen and the state. Part III serves as a rhetorical and structural pivot where he begins to evaluate the relationship between democracy and its stated ideals: liberty, peace, morality, and decency.

2. Liberty vs. Safety

The central thesis of "The Will to Peace" is that the common man does not actually desire liberty. Mencken argues that liberty is "cold and lonely," requiring courage and self-reliance—traits he believes the average citizen lacks. Instead, the citizen seeks:

  • Safety: The primary desire of the common man.
  • The Herd: The "warm, reassuring smell" of collective security.
  • Protection: The presence of a "policeman" to guard him from his own impulses and others.

3. Democracy as a Destroyer of Liberty

Mencken posits that democracy is the most efficient destroyer of liberty because the "mob" cannot tolerate the "free spirit." The democratic system is described as a mechanism that moves to crush the individual in favor of herd conformity.

4. The Philosophical Foundation

Mencken draws on German idealist philosophy to frame his arguments, specifically:

  • Friedrich Nietzsche: Influenced Mencken with concepts of the “will to power” and “herd morality.”
  • Arthur Schopenhauer: Influenced Mencken with his pessimism and the concept of the “will to live” as the fundamental human drive.

5. Rhetorical Strategy: The "Weary Diagnostician"

Mencken adopts the persona of a weary physician who has seen the same disease too many times to be surprised. His tone is characterized by:

  • Resigned Authority: He treats the "case" of democracy as closed.
  • Savage Amusement: Moments of contempt expressed through grotesque comedy.
  • Flattery of the Reader: He addresses the "educated skeptic," making the reader feel part of an intellectual "elect."

Analysis of Rhetorical Techniques

Diction and Collision of Registers

Mencken frequently juxtaposes elevated philosophical vocabulary (will-to-power, dignity) with "barnyard, prison, and circus" imagery. This creates a "collision of registers" where the gap between dignified language and undignified reality forms the core of his argument.

The Penitentiary Analogy

Mencken compares the common man’s ideal peace to that of a "trusty in a well-managed penitentiary." This analogy implies:

  • Forfeiture of Liberty: Like a convict, the citizen has traded freedom for lack of conflict.
  • Obedience over Merit: Privileges are earned through compliance, not character.
  • Efficiency over Justice: A "well-managed" government is one that runs smoothly, regardless of whether it is just.

Syntactic Signature

Mencken’s writing often follows a "forensic" rhythm:

  1. The Build-up: Long sentences utilizing qualification and subordination to provide evidence.
  2. The Verdict: A short, blunt, declarative sentence that delivers the final judgment.
  3. The Dash: Used to introduce unexpected consequences or to create a "quasi-legal" enumeration of points.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What is the primary desire of the common man according to Mencken, and why does he prefer it over liberty?

    • Answer: The common man desires safety and the "warm, reassuring smell of the herd." He prefers this because liberty is "cold and lonely" and requires courage and self-reliance, which Mencken argues the average citizen lacks.

  2. How does Mencken utilize the character of the "policeman" in his argument?

    • Answer: The policeman represents a "charlatan" who offers a protection racket, promising to protect the citizen from his superiors, his equals, and even himself.

  3. Describe the tone shift that occurs at the end of "The Will to Peace."

    • Answer: The tone shifts from a relatively sober, philosophical engagement with Nietzsche and Schopenhauer to grotesque comedy and "comic deflation," where dignified roles (King, Pope, Generalissimo) are replaced by "buffoons" (Harding, a Methodist bishop, Pershing).

  4. In Mencken's "Penitentiary Analogy," what does the term "trusty" signify regarding democratic citizenship?

    • Answer: It signifies a citizen who is a "compliant inmate"—someone who has forfeited liberty for the sake of safety and is grateful for small comforts provided by a well-managed authority.

  5. Explain Mencken's use of the term "heresy" in a political context.

    • Answer: Mencken uses "heresy" to describe political dissent, implying that democracy functions like a religion that punishes those who do not subscribe to its orthodoxies.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Architecture of Contempt: Analyze how Mencken uses the "collision of registers"—mixing high-level philosophical terms with low-brow imagery of prisons and circuses—to dismantle the dignity of democratic ideals. How does this technique reinforce his persona as a "weary diagnostician"?

  2. The Paradox of Democratic Liberty: Mencken argues that democracy is the "most efficient destroyer" of liberty rather than its safeguard. Using the source text, evaluate his claim that the "mob" inherently moves to crush the free spirit and discuss the implications of his "penitentiary analogy."

  3. Syntactic Authority: Examine Mencken’s "forensic" prose style. How do his specific choices in sentence length, parallelism, and punctuation (specifically the dash) contribute to the sense that his conclusions are "inescapable" and "final"?

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition in Mencken’s Context
Apostrophe A figure of speech addressing an abstraction (like Liberty) as if it were a physical entity or character in a drama.
Charlatan A fraud or person claiming false knowledge; Mencken applies this to the policeman/law enforcement.
Heresy Belief contrary to orthodox doctrine; used by Mencken to describe political dissent under the "religion" of democracy.
Ignominious Deserving or causing public disgrace; used to describe the "peace" craved by those who trade liberty for safety.
Prehensile Capable of grasping; used metaphorically to describe the mob’s greedy seizing of power over the individual.
Will-to-Power A Nietzschean concept describing the fundamental drive of the "superior" man, contrasted with the common man's "will-to-peace."
Will-to-Peace Mencken’s term for the common man’s desire for safety and the absence of conflict within the "herd."
Trusty A convict given special privileges for good behavior; Mencken’s metaphor for the compliant democratic citizen.
