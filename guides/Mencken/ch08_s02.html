Study Guide: Liberty and Democratic Man

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the analysis of democracy and liberty as presented in the provided text. It explores the psychological and historical arguments regarding the common man's capacity for freedom, the origins of democratic ideals, and the nature of political power.

Key Concepts and Themes

The Illusion of the Democratic Love for Liberty

The text posits that while democracy claims liberty as its highest achievement, the "common man" or "proletarian" does not actually desire it. Historical revolutions, often attributed to a love for liberty, are characterized as being driven by "hungry city mobs" seeking "ham and cabbage" rather than rational freedom. When these mobs succeed, their first instinct is often to destroy existing freedoms and those who advocate for them.

The Definition of "Genuine Liberty"

Genuine liberty is defined not as a political right, but as a demanding state of being that requires:

  • Courage: The willingness to fight and potentially shed blood for freedom.
  • Self-reliance: The capacity to stand alone and be "precarious" in one's own territory.
  • Resolution and Enterprise: The strength to endure the "dreadful loneliness" and responsibilities associated with being the master of one's own world.
  • The Capacity for Doing Without: The ability to prioritize independence over comfort.

The Aristocratic Origins of Freedom

The document asserts that ideas of freedom were not created by the masses, but by a small minority:

  • Country Gentlemen: Figures like Thomas Jefferson who formulated the underlying philosophies of modern states.
  • Intellectuals: Poets and philosophers who aided in articulating these concepts.
  • Absolute Monarchs: Paradoxically, the text notes that figures like Frederick the Great provided the first legal support for concepts such as free speech.

Biological and Historical Determinism

A central argument is that the capacity for liberty must be "bred in." Because the vast majority of humans are descended from populations that were enslaved or lived in near-savagery (as recently as the late 18th and mid-19th centuries), they possess a natural tendency to shrink from the responsibility of acting alone. To these "lower orders," liberty is an uncomfortable and alarming concept that they are psychologically incapable of bearing.

The Political Cycle of Fear

In theoretically free states, the masses remain "supine" and submit to exploitation because they lack the resolution to revolt. Politics, therefore, becomes a trade of playing upon the "natural poltroonery" (cowardice) of the citizenry. Politicians succeed by scaring the public and then offering to save them, leading the masses to follow leaders slavishly, even when those leaders are discovered to be dishonest.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to the text, what is the primary motivation for "city mobs" during a revolution?
    • Answer: They fight for "ham and cabbage" (basic material needs) rather than a rational sense of liberty.
  2. How does the text describe the common man’s reaction to genuine liberty?
    • Answer: He fears it, finds it unfathomable, and is unable to stand the "dreadful loneliness" and responsibility it requires.
  3. What specific quality does the text claim the "inferior man" lacks that is essential for liberty?
    • Answer: Courage.
  4. Who does the author cite as the first legal supporter of free speech?
    • Answer: Frederick the Great, described as the most absolute monarch of modern times.
  5. According to Sir Francis Galton, what is the "natural tendency" of the majority of the human race?
    • Answer: To shrink from the responsibility of standing and acting alone.
  6. What does the text claim the common man mistakes for liberty?
    • Answer: The "banal right to empty hallelujahs upon his oppressors" or the right to choose between two political "mountebanks."
  7. What is the "heritage of freedom" according to the author's contention?
    • Answer: It belongs to a small minority descended from the old lords of the soil or patricians, as the idea of liberty must be bred into a lineage.
  8. What is the "species" name the author uses to describe the gullible, credulous citizen?
    • Answer: Homo boobiens.
  9. Why do the masses follow leaders even after they are revealed to be scoundrels?
    • Answer: Because the majority lacks the resolution to take risks and looks for leaders with courage to protect them.
  10. What does the text identify as the "corner-stone of modern free states" that was only a dream until 1867?
    • Answer: Universal manhood suffrage.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Psychological Burden of Freedom: Analyze the text’s assertion that liberty is a "dreadful loneliness." Compare this to the common man's supposed preference for "security" and "protection." How does the author use the metaphor of the ox and the butcher to illustrate this relationship?
  2. Historical Heritage vs. Political Theory: The author argues that liberty cannot be "implanted in the mind of man at will, but must be bred in." Discuss how the author uses the history of slavery in Europe and America (specifically mentioning the 18th and 19th centuries) to justify the claim that the majority of people are still "incapable of bearing the pangs of liberty."
  3. The Role of the Elite in Democratic Foundations: Evaluate the author’s claim that liberty was formulated by "country gentlemen" and "eccentric kings" rather than the "city mob." How does the author suggest that figures like Thomas Jefferson or Thomas Paine would have fared under the rule of the very "proletarians" they theoretically sought to liberate?
  4. The Mechanics of Democratic Politics: Examine the author’s critique of the "practical politician." How does the text explain the "supine" submission of the masses to "democratic plutocracy"? Discuss the cycle of "scaring" and "saving" that the author claims defines modern political trade.

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Booboisie / Homo boobiens Terms coined by the author to describe the "bourgeoisie of boobs" or the gullible, credulous common citizen.
Cant Hypocritical and sanctimonious talk, particularly concerning morals or religion.
Fellahin Peasant laborers, specifically compared in the text to those who built the pyramids.
Mountebank A person who deceives others; a political charlatan or dishonest official.
Panem et circenses "Bread and circuses"; basic food and entertainment used to keep the masses content and distracted.
Poltroonery Utter cowardice; a characteristic the author attributes to the majority of men in the face of responsibility.
Proletarian A member of the "lower orders" or the working class, described as being motivated by security rather than liberty.
Supine Lying face upward; failing to act or resist through passivity or mental inertia.
Votary A devoted follower or advocate of a particular cause or belief (e.g., a "votary of liberty").
