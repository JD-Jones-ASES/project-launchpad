# Analysis of H.L. Mencken’s "The Politician Under Democracy": Study Guide

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of H.L. Mencken’s rhetorical and clinical examination of the democratic politician. It explores the transition from institutional analysis to the study of individual character, the historical context of the American boss system, and the specific linguistic tools Mencken employs to deconstruct his subject.

---

## Key Concepts and Themes

### 1. From Institution to Individual
Mencken shifts his focus from the "machinery" of democracy—its theories and electoral processes—to the "human product" of that machinery: the politician. He treats the politician not as a figure of authority, but as a biological specimen to be examined by a naturalist. This perspective invites the educated reader to observe the subject with clinical detachment.

### 2. The Boss System and the Electorate
The text references the "boss system" prevalent in American urban politics from the 1870s through the 1930s (e.g., Tammany Hall’s William Tweed). While these systems were corrupt, they were functional, providing services to the poor and immigrants. Mencken’s interest lies in what this system reveals about the electorate that sustains it, rather than the corruption itself.

### 3. Sociological Foundations: The Iron Law of Oligarchy
Mencken utilizes the authority of sociologist Robert Michels to ground his polemic. Michels’ "iron law of oligarchy" posits that all organizations, regardless of how democratic their intentions, inevitably develop into hierarchies controlled by a few.

### 4. Vocabularies of Exposure
Mencken employs three distinct linguistic registers to strip away the politician’s pretenses:
*   **Legal/Political:** Using terms like "altruist" and "honour" with "invisible quotation marks" to highlight the politician's false self-image.
*   **Vernacular/Physical:** Using earthy, "gutter" language (e.g., "butter his parsnips," "ear to the ground") to drag the politician down to reality.
*   **Zoological/Clinical:** Categorizing the politician as a "species" or "specimen" driven by "congenital" instincts rather than conscious choice.

### 5. Rhetorical Devices and Syntax
*   **Extended Analogy:** The politician is compared to a courtier who flatters to victimize and a chorus girl who trades her person for a job.
*   **Periodic Sentences:** Mencken builds suspense by delaying the main clause or "verdict" of a sentence until the very end.
*   **The Semicolon:** Used to chain parallel clauses together, creating an "oppressive catalogue" of evidence against the subject.
*   **Zeugma:** A figure of speech that yokes incompatible ideas (the literal/physical and the figurative/abstract) to force the reader to experience the politician's debasement.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. Who was Robert Michels, and why does Mencken cite him?**
Mencken cites Robert Michels, a German-Italian sociologist known for the "iron law of oligarchy," to lend academic respectability to his argument that democratic organizations inevitably become oligarchies.

**2. Define Mencken’s original coinage, "demaslave."**
A "demaslave" is a hybrid of Latin and Greek terms. Unlike a demagogue (a leader of the people), a demaslave is one who pretends to follow the people, highlighting the incompatible roles democracy forces upon a man.

**3. What is the significance of the "chorus girl" analogy?**
The analogy equates political compromise with sexual exploitation. It suggests that the politician, like the chorus girl, sacrifices something intimate (honour or pride) for something trivial (a job), eventually viewing the degradation with complacency.

**4. How does Mencken use the term "armamentarium"?**
Borrowing from medical terminology, Mencken uses "armamentarium" to describe the collection of deceptions and methods a politician uses, giving political fraud the appearance of professional competence.

**5. Describe the structural relationship between Mencken's long and short sentences.**
Mencken often uses a long, complex sentence to pile up evidence or clauses of an argument, followed by a short, blunt sentence that acts as a "gavel" or final verdict on the matter.

**6. What distinction does Mencken make between "vanity" and "pride"?**
Through the chorus girl analogy, Mencken suggests that politicians retain "vanity" (pleasure in how others see them) but lose "pride" (internal self-respect that is independent of others).

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

### 1. The Naturalist's Perspective
Analyze Mencken’s rhetorical strategy of transforming the politician into a "zoological specimen." How does this "clinical" approach affect the reader’s perception of political authority? Does it succeed in "stripping away pretension," or does it merely replace one bias with another?

### 2. The Feudalism of Democracy
Mencken compares the democratic politician to a courtier, suggesting that democracy is a form of "feudalism in reverse." Explore this analogy. In Mencken’s view, if the politician is the servile courtier, what role does the "mob" or the electorate play, and how does this dynamic challenge traditional definitions of democratic leadership?

### 3. Tonal Instability as Argument
Mencken frequently shifts between formal, academic language and vulgar, barroom vernacular. Discuss how this "tonal instability" serves as a rhetorical argument in itself. How does the movement between the judge’s bench and the "gutter" reflect Mencken’s ultimate conclusion about the dignity of the political profession?

## Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Armamentarium** | A collection of equipment or methods available for a purpose; Mencken uses it to describe a politician's tools of deception. |
| **Clapper-clawing** | An old English word meaning to scratch or claw noisily; Mencken uses it to describe the vulgar applause of a crowd. |
| **Demaslave** | A politician who pretends to follow the people rather than lead them (a "shot-gun marriage" of Latin and Greek). |
| **Iron Law of Oligarchy** | The theory by Robert Michels that all organizations, regardless of democratic intent, eventually become oligarchies. |
| **Periodic Sentence** | A sentence where the main clause is delayed until the end to build suspense and deliver a final "verdict." |
| **The Boss System** | A historical American political structure where "city bosses" controlled patronage and favors in exchange for votes. |
| **Vocabularies of Exposure** | Word choices designed to reveal the "ugly machinery" beneath a subject's pretension, ranging from clinical to vernacular. |
| **Zeugma** | A figure of speech where one word (usually a verb) governs two or more words in different ways (e.g., one literal and one figurative). |
