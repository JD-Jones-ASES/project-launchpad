Study Guide: The Democrat as Moralist and the Critique of Puritanism

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the analysis regarding the intersection of democracy, Puritanism, and morality as presented in the source context. It explores the psychological underpinnings of democratic legislation and the societal impacts of moralistic crusades.

I. Core Themes and Philosophical Arguments

1. The Identity of Democracy and Puritanism

The text argues that democracy and Puritanism are essentially the same phenomenon appearing in different forms. Both are rooted in the "psyche" of the "inferior man" and are driven by two primary emotions:

  • Fear: A dread of those who are stronger, more courageous, and more capable.
  • Envy: A resentment of "superior" individuals who navigate the world with more success and enjoyment.

Under autocracies, the "green fury of the mob" was historically held in check, which allowed the spirit of superior men to flourish, leading to milestones like the Renaissance. Democracy, conversely, is defined as a political mechanism that releases this envy and grants it the force of law.

2. The Mob’s Hostility Toward Superiority

The text posits that when the mob is liberated—such as during the French or Russian Revolutions—it immediately begins a "war to the death" against superiority of all kinds.

  • Historical Examples: The execution of the scientist Lavoisier during the French Revolution and Wat Tyler’s practice of hanging anyone who could read or write are cited as evidence that the mob seeks to destroy intelligence and achievement.
  • Democratic Law: In a democracy, figures like Wat Tyler are transformed from criminals into the "ideal Good Man," as their resentment of excellence is codified into public policy.

3. The Psychology of the Puritan Moralist

The Puritan moralist is characterized not by true virtue, but by a lack of "civilized" capacity. The text suggests that the Puritan’s desire to legislate morality is based on:

  • Incapacity: The Puritan is unable to enjoy life (sex, art, or alcohol) on a "civilized" or "romantic" plane. For the Puritan, sex is reduced to the "barnyard" and art to mere "natural functions."
  • Punitive Logic: Because the Puritan cannot enjoy these things safely or elegantly, they seek to punish others who can.
  • Virtuoso of Virtue: The Puritan mistakes their own docility and lack of enterprise for spiritual eminence, viewing their cowardice as a moral merit.

II. The Mechanics of Democratic Law-Making

1. The Legislative Process

The text describes democratic law-making as a "process of law-making by orgy." The machinery functions as follows:

  • Fanatics: Supply the motive power by inflaming the mob with "brutal envies and quaking fears."
  • Scoundrels: Politicians and lawmakers who, fearing the mob, precipitately enact dubious laws to satisfy the majority.
  • Haste: Laws are passed with "ferocious haste" to prevent the mob from having "sober second thoughts."

2. The Failure of the "Upper Chambers"

The "Fathers" (the founders of the American system) originally intended for upper chambers (like the Senate) to be removed from the "mob’s hot rages." However, the text notes that these chambers have lost their "prophylactic remoteness" and are now directly exposed to the same pressures as the lower houses, rendering them ineffective as a buffer against fanaticism.

3. The Role of the Legal System

The criminal law system in a democracy is described as having a "flavour of fraud."

  • District Attorneys: These figures often use high-profile prosecutions of "superior" or wealthy men (e.g., Thaw or Fatty Arbuckle) to gain political favor with the masses. The text argues that a rich man actually faces a heavier burden in court because the mob delights in seeing their "betters manhandled."
  • Judges: Many judges prioritize their jobs over professional dignity, enforcing laws they know to be loathsome or unjust to avoid offending the democratic majority.

III. Case Studies in Moral Legislation

Legislation/Event Declared Altruistic Motive Alleged Actual Motive (per Source) Result/Outcome
Prohibition To abate drunkenness, crime, poverty, and disease. To punish others for having a better time; "sadism." Increased drunkenness, crime, and the corruption of the courts.
Comstockery To suppress vice and protect public morals. An inability to see anything in art/literature beyond "natural functions." Suppression of free expression and the fine arts.
Mann Act Moral regulation of sexual conduct. Inability to manage sex as a "romantic adventure." Regulation of private behavior to the level of the "barnyard."
Anti-Evolution Laws Defense of theological "truth." Envy and hatred of those with "unusual intelligence." Persecution of men who defend constitutional liberties.

IV. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to the text, what is the "primal essence" shared by both democracy and Puritanism?
    • Answer: The inferior man’s fear and hatred (envy) of his betters.
  2. How does the text characterize the historical role of autocracies regarding civilization?
    • Answer: They held the "green fury of the mob" in check, thereby setting free the spirit of the superior man and making eras like the Renaissance possible.
  3. Why does the text claim that Prohibitionists continue their "frenzy" even though the policy failed?
    • Answer: Because their true motive is sadism—the lust to inflict discomfort and disgrace upon those they hate—and they can do so without personal risk behind a line of police.
  4. What is the author's view of John Milton?
    • Answer: Milton was not a Puritan, but a libertarian, which the text considers the "exact opposite" of a Puritan.
  5. Who is the "Forgotten Man" mentioned in the text?
    • Answer: The normal, well-behaved, decent citizen who is beset by the envy of inferiors and the blood lust of fanatics.
  6. What was the "one genuinely valuable contribution to moral legislation" mentioned, and who created it?
    • Answer: The Code Napoléon, created by a committee of "violent anti-Puritans" during a reaction against democracy.

V. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Psychology of Envy: Analyze the text’s argument that envy is the primary engine of democratic legislation. How does this perspective challenge traditional views of democracy as a system based on equality and justice?
  2. The Corruption of the Judiciary: Discuss the text's portrayal of District Attorneys and Judges. How does the pursuit of "higher office" and the need to appease the "virtuous masses" affect the application of law to "superior" or "rich" individuals?
  3. Puritanism vs. Libertarianism: Using the text’s definitions, contrast the Puritan moralist with the "libertarian" (as exemplified by the author's view of Milton). Focus on their differing attitudes toward art, sex, and personal liberty.
  4. The Failure of Prohibition: The source context claims that Prohibition increased the very evils it sought to cure. Examine the reasons provided for this failure and how it led to what the text calls "law-making by orgy."

VI. Glossary of Key Terms

  • Brummagem: Something that looks great but is actually cheap, showy, or counterfeit; used to describe the "cure-all" promises of Prohibitionists.
  • Casuistry: The use of clever but unsound reasoning, especially in relation to moral or legal issues.
  • Comstockery: A term for moralistic censorship and the suppression of free expression (named after Anthony Comstock).
  • Libertarian: Defined in the text as the "exact opposite" of a Puritan; one who values liberty over moral legislation.
  • Mann Act: A specific piece of moral legislation cited as an example of the Puritan impulse to regulate sexual experience.
  • Puritanism: In the text, a psychological state of the "natural democrat" characterized by an impulse to punish others for enjoying life and an inability to experience life on a "civilized plane."
  • Sadism: As used in the text, the psychological aberration that moves moralists to inflict inconvenience and disgrace upon those who are free from their superstitions.
  • Theocracy: A system of government in which priests or religious authorities rule in the name of God; used to describe early New England.
  • Virtuoso of Virtue: A sarcastic term for the Puritan who venerates themselves for their supposed moral excellence, which the author argues is actually just docility and cowardice.
