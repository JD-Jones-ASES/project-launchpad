# Study Guide: Where Puritanism Fails – An Analysis of Mencken’s Critique

This study guide examines H.L. Mencken’s analysis of democratic moralism and its practical consequences as presented in Chapter 22, Section 1 of his work. The text transitions from a psychological evaluation of Puritan motives to a prosecutorial indictment of how these motives manifest in the American legal and social landscape.

---

## Key Concepts and Themes

### 1. The Perversion of Law and Justice
Mencken argues that under the influence of democratic moralism, the legal system ceases to protect the citizen and instead becomes an instrument of persecution.
*   **The "Mill of Justice":** Mencken uses this metaphor to describe a system that does not judge but "grinds." He asserts that legal processes are used to escalate vague accusations (like "disorderly conduct") into crimes with "staggering penalties."
*   **Erosion of Rights:** The text notes a deliberate echo of the *Dred Scott v. Sandford* (1857) decision. Just as Chief Justice Taney ruled that Black Americans had no rights white men were bound to respect, Mencken contends that the political dissident under democratic moralism has no rights the police are bound to respect.
*   **Case Studies:** Mencken cites the **Scopes Trial** (anti-intellectualism), **the Volstead Act** (Prohibition enforcement), and **Bishop Wilson’s sermon** as primary examples of the machinery of justice being perverted to serve moralistic persecution.

### 2. Rhetorical Strategy: Passion vs. Discipline
The text is characterized by a "paradox of invective." While Mencken’s tone is one of genuine fury and indignation, his prose remains cold, architecturally sound, and logically organized.
*   **The Prosecutorial Voice:** Mencken adopts the persona of a defense attorney arguing before the "court of educated opinion."
*   **Periodic Construction:** Mencken utilizes long sentences that withhold the main point until the end. This structure mirrors the "labyrinthine processes" of the legal system, forcing the reader to navigate complex clauses just as a citizen must navigate a thicket of regulations.
*   **The Shift to Elegiac Tone:** At the conclusion, the tone shifts from combative anger to a melancholy reflection on the loss of "common decency" and mutual trust.

### 3. The Destruction of Common Decency
Mencken concludes that the ultimate result of Puritan surveillance is the destruction of community life. He posits that:
*   Mutual trust is impossible when neighbors spy on one another ("watching through knotholes").
*   The "decent citizen" is eventually driven from the community by the hostility of those around them.
*   Prosperity (symbolized by a "Ford in the garage") is meaningless if a citizen is constantly under threat of jail for personal behavior.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. What is the significance of Mencken’s reference to the *Dred Scott* decision?**
*Answer:* Mencken echoes the language of the *Dred Scott* decision to argue that political dissidents under democratic moralism have been stripped of their rights, effectively comparing their legal status to that of enslaved persons before the Civil War.

**2. How does Mencken use the term "mill of justice"?**
*Answer:* He uses it as a metaphor for a legal system that does not seek truth or justice but instead mechanically "grinds" citizens through a process of escalating accusations and penalties.

**3. Describe the "paradox of Mencken’s invective" as mentioned in the text.**
*Answer:* The paradox lies in the combination of genuine rage (passion in content) and highly controlled, architecturally sound prose (discipline in form). The reader feels the heat of his anger but admires the "coolness of the execution."

**4. What does Mencken mean by the "vocabulary of violence" in his writing?**
*Answer:* Alongside formal legal terms, Mencken uses words like "clubbed," "lynching," and "railroaded" to highlight the gap between the Constitution's promises of rights and the brutal reality of their enforcement.

**5. How does Mencken characterize Bishop Wilson in his analysis?**
*Answer:* Mencken uses "mock ceremony" and irony, describing the bishop’s "dignity and puissance" to make his actual argument—which dismisses the right to free speech—appear more contemptible and "bloodthirsty."

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

### 1. The Architecture of Invective
Analyze Mencken’s use of **invective**. How does he distinguish his writing from "mere ranting"? In your answer, discuss the relationship between his use of documented evidence (such as the Volstead Act or the Scopes trial) and his tone of contempt. Use the concept of "anger organized by intelligence" to support your argument.

### 2. The Duality of Language: Jurisprudence vs. Brutality
Mencken frequently juxtaposes a "legal register" (e.g., *habeas corpus*, *due process*) with a "vocabulary of violence." Examine how this collision of diction serves his central argument regarding the betrayal of the American state’s founding principles. How does this linguistic strategy expose the discrepancy between legal theory and practice?

### 3. The Death of Mutual Trust
In his closing paragraphs, Mencken shifts from a critique of law to a critique of social life. Explore his argument that "Puritan surveillance" destroys the possibility of living among neighbors. Use his imagery of the "Ford in the garage" and "knotholes" to explain how he defines the loss of "common decency" in a democratic society.

## Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition in Context |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Caveat** | A warning or caution; used by Mencken to describe the weak protests of those defending liberty. |
| **Invective** | The art of verbal attack; abusive or critical language that combines evidence with contempt to build a case against an institution or group. |
| **Periodic Construction** | A syntactic strategy where the main point of a sentence is withheld until the final clause, mirroring the complexity of the legal system. |
| **Proscribed** | Forbidden or outlawed by authority; carries associations with Roman "proscription lists" where citizens could be killed with impunity. |
| **Specious** | Superficially plausible but actually wrong; Mencken uses this to describe claims that dismiss constitutional protections. |
| **Sumptuary** | Relating to laws that regulate personal behavior or consumption (such as food or drink); used to link Prohibition to historical government overreach. |
| **Syntactic Parallelism** | The repetition of similar grammatical structures; used by Mencken to equate neighborly surveillance with institutional persecution. |
