H.L. Mencken’s Critique of Democratic Reform: A Study Guide

This study guide examines H.L. Mencken’s analysis of the American democratic system, specifically focusing on the inherent difficulties faced by "gentlemen" or people of integrity who attempt to enter the political sphere. It explores the mechanisms of political corruption, the influence of the "mob," and the comparative differences between the American and English systems of governance and law.

Key Concepts and Thematic Analysis

1. The Reformer’s Dilemma

Mencken posits that it is a "psychic impossibility" for a person of honor to hold federal office without compromising their integrity. He uses the analogy of placing virgins in a "bawdy-house" to describe the Liberal ideal of sending good men into politics: either the individuals flee the environment immediately, or they are corrupted by it.

2. Adaptation to the "Mob"

To remain in power, a political aspirant must adapt to the tastes and prejudices of the "mob." This process involves:

  • Mountebankery: Using tricks and "balderdash" to soothe the masses.
  • Moral Anesthesia: Prioritizing political advantage over points of honor.
  • Submission to Mob-Masters: Making terms with the agents of "prehensile minorities" who manipulate public opinion.

3. The "Tired Reformer" and the Apostate

Mencken identifies "tired reformers" as the worst enemies of political decency. These are individuals who enter politics with high ideals but eventually become "party hacks."

  • Theodore Roosevelt: Cited as a "violent and shameless demagogue" who initially entered politics to fight demagoguery.
  • Henry Cabot Lodge: Described as a "reductio ad absurdum" of the gentleman turned messiah; a man whose private convictions became indistinguishable from his political dodges.

4. Intellectual Isolation in American Leadership

Unlike European systems where there is "interpenetration" between the political class and the intellectual aristocracy (e.g., Oxford and Cambridge influencing Westminster), American politics is isolated. Mencken notes that American officials, such as Warren G. Harding, preferred the company of "bootleggers" over "metaphysicians," reflecting a broader trend where the political class separates itself from civilized thought.

5. The Decay of the Judiciary

Mencken argues the American judiciary has "sunk low" because:

  • Political Appointments: Judges are often chosen for political reasons or through popular election, favoring "shysters" over reputable lawyers.
  • Constitutional Neglect: Most judges have acquiesced to the destruction of various Constitutional Amendments (1st, 2nd, 4th, 5th, 6th, 14th, and 15th).
  • Lack of Theoretical Contribution: Very few American judges contribute to legal theory, instead enforcing laws provided by "ignorant and corrupt legislators."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to Mencken, what is the "grim dilemma" faced by an office-holder regarding the Constitutional oath? An office-holder must swear to uphold the Constitution (including Prohibition laws). If they provide wine to a dying relative, they become a "liar" (violating their oath); if they refuse, they become a "pig" (lacking human decency).

2. Why does Mencken believe the English judiciary is superior to the American one? The English system is protected by a "governing oligarchy" that maintains high standards for the bench, preventing "ignoramuses" and "shysters" from attaining office, whereas the American system relies on popular elections and political patronage.

3. What happens to a "haughty amateur" who enters politics with rectitude but refuses to use the "cologne spirits of the trade"? They are "swiftly heaved over the fence" and their career in statecraft comes to an end after their first battle.

4. How did Theodore Roosevelt differ from other American Presidents in his social habits, according to the text? Roosevelt was the only President since Washington to attempt to break down barriers between politics and other classes, inviting people who "actually knew something" (intellectuals and artists) to his table.

5. What is the "remedy" advocated by professional Liberals that Mencken finds absurd? The idea that it is the "duty of a gentleman to go into politics" as a way to cure the "quagmire" of the system.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Evolution of the Political Apostate: Using Mencken's descriptions of Theodore Roosevelt and Henry Cabot Lodge, analyze the psychological and professional transition from a "sword drawn against demagogy" to a "party hack." What specific pressures does Mencken suggest cause this transformation?

  2. Democracy vs. Aristocratic Tradition: Compare Mencken’s view of American democracy with his description of the English "aristocratic tradition." Does Mencken argue for a return to aristocracy, or is he simply using it as a tool to highlight the perceived failures of the American system? Support your argument with his observations on the judiciary and the "intellectual class."

  3. The Judicial Breach of Faith: Examine Mencken’s claim that the American judiciary has "supinely connived" at the destruction of the Bill of Rights. Based on the text, what are the systemic causes of this perceived failure, and how does it relate to the quality of individuals who pursue the "ermine" (judicial office) in America?

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition per Source Context
Apostate A person who renounces a former belief or allegiance; specifically, a reformer who becomes a corrupt politician.
Balderdash Senseless talk or writing; nonsense used by politicians to soothe the "mob."
Demagogue A political leader who seeks support by appealing to the desires and prejudices of the ordinary people rather than by using rational argument.
Gelehrte Scholars or learned people; those with whom the average American Governor rarely interacts.
Mountebank A flamboyant charlatan; a person who deceives others, especially in a political context, to maintain power.
Oligarchy Government by a small group of powerful people; in England, this group is credited with protecting the judiciary.
Prehensile Greedy or grasping; used to describe "prehensile minorities" who control the "mob-masters."
Rectitude Morally correct behavior or thinking; righteousness that Mencken claims is often lost in the political process.
Shyster A person, especially a lawyer, who uses unscrupulous, fraudulent, or deceptive methods.
Sempiternal Everlasting or eternal; used to describe the enduring prejudices of the "mob."
