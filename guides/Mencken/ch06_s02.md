# Study Guide: The Biological Basis of Democratic Man

This study guide provides a comprehensive analysis of the psychological and physiological critiques of "democratic man" as presented in the text "The Role of the Hormones." It explores the intersection of biology, psychology, and political theory, focusing on the assertion that civic behavior is driven by glandular secretions rather than noble ideals.

---

## I. Key Concepts and Themes

### The Hormonal Basis of Emotion
The text argues that the emotions governing "democratic man" are not products of the soul or political theory, but are instead "glandular secretions." Civic virtue is reduced to animal instinct, specifically the hormones that dominate the human organism.

### Persistent Infantilism
A central thesis is that the "inferior man" suffers from "incurable infantilism." His adult emotions are merely exaggerated versions of the sensations experienced in the cradle, such as the physical delight of being stroked or tickled. The text asserts that the brain of the inferior man ceases to develop in the early teens, while his glands "unfold gloriously," leading to a life dominated by physical urges.

### The Physicality of Love
Refencing Freudian psychology, the text describes "love" in the inferior man as an "earthly" and physical matter rooted in erogenous zones. It rejects the "poetry" of love as a facade for "anthropoid appetite." The inferior man is said to lack the capacity for sublimation or symbolism, preferring gross sexual provocation and physical gratification over refined sentiment.

### Deficiency of Imagination and Empathy
The "inferior man" is characterized by an inability to project himself into the place of another. This deficiency results in:
*   **Inability to Conceptualize Universal Good:** He cannot formulate a concept of "good" that is not his own private advantage.
*   **Cruelty and Cowardice:** His congenital fear is easily translated into cruelty, manifesting as a desire to suppress heretics and radicals.
*   **Hostility Toward Superiors:** While charitable to his own kind, he is described as "wolfish" when dealing with those he perceives as "betters."

### The Rural "Yokel" vs. The Urban Proletariat
The text establishes a hierarchy within the lower classes:
*   **The Urban Proletariat:** Regarded as slightly superior to the peasant because they had the "wit to escape" rural life. They may be "run amok by ideas," even if those ideas are senseless.
*   **The Yokel (Peasant):** Described as the "lowest caste among civilized men" and the "bulwark of all democratic states." His mind is characterized by primitive animism, superstition, and an absolute intolerance for dissent (as seen in anti-evolution laws).

## II. Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition in Context |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Brummagem** | Counterfeit, cheap, or showy; used to describe "progressivism" that lacks genuine substance. |
| **Homo stultus** | Literally "stupid man"; used to describe the "bucolic variety" of inferior human beings. |
| **Mountebank** | A charlatan or deceiver; specifically, politicians who wrap self-seeking in the "trappings of Service." |
| **Sage-femme** | A midwife; mentioned in the context of the consequences of rural animalistic urges. |
| **Tumescence** | A physiological state of swelling; cited as a "satirical fact" occurring even at birth to illustrate the biological nature of human urges. |
| **Yokel** | A derogatory term for a rustic or peasant, characterized by the text as the "glory and bulwark" of democracy. |

## III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **According to the text, what is the "seat" of the delights experienced by the "raw human being"?**
    *   *Answer:* The seats are chiefly in erogenous zones and are related to hormones rather than the soul.
2.  **How does the text describe the development of the "inferior man" in his second decade?**
    *   *Answer:* His brain ceases to develop, but his glands begin to unfold, making him a "vast geyser of hormones" by his middle teens.
3.  **What does the text claim is the "corner-stone" of the tabloid newspapers' metaphysical system?**
    *   *Answer:* The fact that love for the inferior man remains a physical matter, requiring no sublimation or symbolism, only gross provocation.
4.  **Why does the "inferior man" exhibit heat against heretics throughout history?**
    *   *Answer:* Because he cannot imagine a good that is not his own, and his congenital fear is easily translated into cruelty toward those who are different.
5.  **What is the "alpha and omega" of the progressivism that emanates from the farm?**
    *   *Answer:* The belief that the city man should be taxed to make up for the peasant's losses; it is characterized as "bald self-seeking."
6.  **What role did the "American Legion" play in the text's description of the inferior man's history?**
    *   *Answer:* They are cited as an example of the inferior man marching against radicals—those who sought to end his exploitation.

## IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Biological Critique of Democracy:** Analyze Mencken’s argument that democracy is not a system of ideas but a manifestation of "animal instinct." How does his focus on "glandular secretions" undermine traditional views of "civic virtue"?
2.  **Infantilism and the Adult Organism:** Explore the text’s assertion that the "inferior man" never truly outgrows the "auto-erotic exercises in the cradle." How does this "incurable infantilism" shape his interactions with society and his taste in popular culture?
3.  **The Psychology of the "Yokel":** Compare the text’s depiction of the rural peasant with the urban proletarian. Why does the author identify the "yokel" as the ultimate "democratic man," and what role does "primitive animism" play in his political and theological views?
4.  **Empathy and Justice:** The text claims that the concepts of honor, liberty, and justice are "eternally beyond" the inferior man due to a deficiency in imagination. Discuss the implications of this claim on the possibility of a fair and just democratic society.
