H.L. Mencken: Journalist, Critic, and the Sage of Baltimore

This study guide explores the career and intellectual contributions of H.L. Mencken, a figure who defined himself through his work in daily journalism and his relentless critique of American culture. It covers his literary alliances, his stylistic innovations, and his fundamental opposition to the "genteel tradition."

Core Themes and Philosophical Stance

The Supremacy of Journalism

Mencken identified primarily as a newspaper man. He viewed daily journalism as the most honest form of writing because it required:

  • Clarity: Direct communication without academic obfuscation.
  • Speed: The ability to process and report information quickly.
  • Real-world contact: A grounding in the actual world rather than theoretical abstractions.

Conversely, Mencken harbored a deep distrust of academia, particularly "professors of English." He viewed most literary criticism as "a species of fraud," preferring a style of criticism that pronounced judgment with absolute confidence and entertaining prose.

Literary Values

Mencken prioritized three specific qualities in literature over established norms:

  1. Vitality over propriety: He preferred raw energy to "polite" literature.
  2. Honesty over elegance: He valued truth-telling above stylistic polish.
  3. Genuine talent over approved mediocrity: He sought out unique voices rather than those sanctioned by the cultural establishment.

Heroes and Enemies

Mencken’s worldview was defined by a clear set of values and antagonisms:

The Mencken Style: Key Stylistic Techniques

Mencken’s prose is characterized by several deliberate techniques designed to provoke and engage the reader:

  • Register Mixing: The juxtaposition of formal, scholarly diction with slang and colloquialisms to deflate pretension.
  • Hyperbole: The use of extreme exaggeration (stating a case "by a factor of ten") to challenge the reader to find the underlying argument.
  • Mock-Scientific Diction: Borrowing vocabulary from biology, medicine, and psychology to treat human behavior and politics as clinical specimens.
  • Devastating Short Sentences: The use of a brief, lethal sentence to deliver a final verdict after a long, complex build-up.

Major Works and Influences

Landmark Publications

  • The American Language (1919): A monumental scholarly study (expanded through 1948) that tracked how American English diverged from British English in vocabulary, idiom, and pronunciation. It demonstrated Mencken’s capacity for meticulous detail.
  • Prejudices (1919–1927): A six-volume collection of essays. The title reflected Mencken’s belief that objectivity was a pretension and that open bias was more honest.

Key Literary Alliances

  • Theodore Dreiser (1871–1945): Mencken championed Dreiser’s "raw naturalism" in works like Sister Carrie, defending him against critics who labeled the work vulgar or immoral.
  • Sinclair Lewis (1885–1951): An ally in the war against the "booboisie." Mencken promoted Lewis’s satirical attacks on middle-class conformity in novels such as Main Street and Babbitt.
  • European Influences: Mencken introduced American readers to Joseph Conrad and Friedrich Nietzsche to highlight the provincialism of the American literary establishment.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why did Mencken prefer daily journalism over academic writing? Answer: He believed it was more honest because it demanded clarity, speed, and direct contact with the actual world, whereas he viewed academic criticism as a form of fraud.

  2. What was the "genteel tradition," and who coined the term? Answer: Coined by George Santayana in 1911, it referred to the polite, moralistic, and lifeless literary culture that dominated America in the late 19th century.

  3. What was the purpose of Mencken’s use of "mock-scientific diction"? Answer: He used the vocabulary of fields like biology and medicine to discuss politics and culture, effectively treating human folly as a clinical specimen to be analyzed.

  4. In what major work did Mencken explore the evolution of American English? Answer: The American Language (1919).

  5. What did the title of the series Prejudices signify about Mencken’s approach to criticism? Answer: It was a provocative admission of his biases; he considered "objectivity" to be a pretension of mediocre minds.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The War on the Genteel: Analyze how Mencken’s support for Theodore Dreiser and Sinclair Lewis served as a direct assault on the "genteel tradition." Use examples of how their literary styles (naturalism and satire) contrasted with the "polite" culture of the era.

  2. The Anatomy of Style: Examine the four key components of the "Mencken Style" (Register mixing, hyperbole, mock-scientific diction, and short sentences). Discuss how these techniques collectively worked to "deflate pretension" and "keep the reader off balance."

  3. Mencken’s "Natural Aristocracy": Discuss Mencken’s social and cultural hierarchy. Contrast his "heroes" (Nietzsche, the independent mind) with his "enemies" (the "uplift," democratic moralism). How did his scholarly work, like The American Language, coexist with his provocative persona?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • The "Booboisie": A term used by Mencken and Sinclair Lewis to describe the uncultured, middle-class American public.
  • Democratic Moralism: A subset of what Mencken called "the uplift," referring to the societal pressure to conform to moralistic and sentimental standards in a democracy.
  • The Genteel Tradition: The polite, moralistic literary culture of the late 19th century that Mencken considered his personal enemy.
  • Naturalism: A literary movement (pioneered in the U.S. by Theodore Dreiser) characterized by raw, unvarnished depictions of reality, often rejected by "respectable" critics as vulgar.
  • The Uplift: Mencken’s collective label for the various forces he despised, including Puritanism, Prohibition, and democratic moralism, which sought to "improve" society through regulation and sentimentality.
  • Provincialism: A lack of sophistication or narrowness of mind; Mencken used European literature to show that the American establishment suffered from this quality.
