Study Guide: H.L. Mencken’s "The Future of Democracy"

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the analytical and polemical themes found in H.L. Mencken’s Notes on Democracy, specifically Part IV, Section 1: "The Future of Democracy." It is designed to assist in the deep analysis of Mencken’s critique of democratic systems, his diagnostic approach to political science, and his observations on the psychological foundations of the "mob."

Key Concepts and Analytical Themes

1. Diagnosis vs. Therapeutics

Mencken distinguishes his work from that of political reformers. He defines his role not as a doctor seeking a "cure" (therapeutics) but as a "pathologist" delivering a "diagnosis." He argues that democracy is characterized by an inability to accept the "insoluble." In a democratic framework, lacking a remedy for a social ill is seen as a disqualification from discussing the disease itself.

2. Democracy as a "Cure-All"

The text asserts that democracy functions as a secular religion or a "mystical emanation from the mob." It operates on the premise that any "boil upon the body politic" can be healed by a vote or a law. Mencken suggests that democracy attempts to "repeal the laws of nature" and re-enact them with moral amendments, treating war as a device to end war and politics as a means to abolish immutable realities like sin, poverty, and stupidity.

3. The Shift from Medieval Immutability

Mencken contrasts modern democracy with Medieval Christianity.

  • Medieval View: Evils were seen as incurable in the mortal world; a perfect moral order was only achievable post-mortem. This created a satisfactory system of checks and balances because it was never put to a practical test.
  • Democratic View: Every human problem is brought into the range of "practical politics." The belief in "illimitable progress" replaces the faith in divine immutability.

4. Logic and the "Hocus-Pocus" of Democracy

Mencken argues that democracy is not vulnerable to logical attack because it lies outside the range of logical ideas. He points to James Bryce’s Modern Democracies and Lincoln’s Gettysburg Address as examples of "appalling gyrations" where evidence of democracy's failure is ignored in favor of "heroic gulps" of faith. He likens this to religious dogmas like the Trinity or Transubstantiation—concepts that are believed specifically because they cannot be understood.

5. The Paradox of Equality

The text identifies a "suicidal" paradox within democratic philosophy:

  • It provides a means for "John Doe" to rise above "Richard Roe."
  • Simultaneously, by declaring Roe equal to Doe, it strips away the value of Doe's achievement.
  • As a result, democratic societies constantly invent new, "brummagem" (cheap/showy) class distinctions and titles (e.g., "Grand Goblin," "Supreme Worthy Archon") to satisfy the innate human need for hierarchy.

6. Plutocracy vs. Aristocracy

In the absence of a true aristocracy, a democratic state elevates the "plutocracy" (the wealthy). Mencken distinguishes the two sharply:

  • True Aristocracy: Characterized by a clean tradition, culture, public spirit, honesty, honor, and courage.
  • Plutocracy: Characterized by "incurable timorousness," a lack of public duty, and a preoccupation with money. The plutocrat is an "inferior man" who has risen from the mob and retained its ignobilities and fears.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. How does Mencken describe the "aim of government" under a democratic system? The aim is to repeal the laws of nature and re-enact them with "moral amendments," treating the state as a transcendental power capable of fixing even the most "inscrutable" problems.

2. Why does Mencken believe that democracy is the antithesis of the "old religion"? Because democracy brings all problems into the realm of "practical politics" and assumes they can be solved on earth, whereas the old religion (Medieval Christianity) accepted that earthly evils were incurable and postponed the quest for perfection until heaven.

3. What is the "sweet democratic axiom" that Mencken identifies as a primary issue in the United States? The notion that the man who "hopes absurdly" (the optimist) is a better citizen than the man who "detects and exposes the truth."

4. According to the text, what is the primary difference between a "pedagogue" and an "aristocrat"? The pedagogue is marked by a "haunting fear of losing their job" and is policed by both the plutocracy and the mob. They lack the "aristocratic disinterestedness" and security necessary for free opinion.

5. What does Mencken suggest is the "last joke" upon man? The last joke may be that man "never learned how to govern himself in a rational and competent manner."

6. Why does the author claim that the plutocracy is "comprehensible to the mob"? Because the aspirations of the plutocracy are essentially the same as those of "inferior men"—the pursuit of money and material wealth—which is why "mob religions" like Christianity represent heaven as being paved with gold.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Pathology of Optimism: Analyze Mencken’s argument that democracy treats optimism as a virtue and realism as a vice. How does this "psychopathology" affect the democratic approach to international relations, specifically citing his examples of the League of Nations and the Washington Disarmament Conference?

  2. The Evolution of Class in a Classless Society: Explore the paradox Mencken describes regarding equality. How does the "natural yearning" for superiority manifest in a system that theoretically abhors class distinctions? Discuss the role of "brummagem" titles and the rise of the plutocracy in this context.

  3. The Necessity of a "Party of Liberty": At the conclusion of the text, Mencken argues that democracy needs a "party of liberty" backed by a "genuine aristocracy." Based on his descriptions of the mob, the plutocracy, and the "pedagogues," why does he believe such a party is currently impossible to sustain?

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition in Context
Brummagem Showy but cheap and of inferior quality; used to describe democratic titles of honor.
Coda The closing movement of a work; used here to describe the final section of Mencken’s critique.
Hocus-pocus Meaningless talk or activities used to deceive; refers to the "mysticism" required to believe democratic dogmas.
Honnête homme A person of integrity and social grace; the standard against which the "timorous" plutocrat is measured.
Immutability The state of being unchanging; the medieval belief that human suffering and sin were permanent features of life.
Pathology The study of the nature and causes of diseases; Mencken’s preferred method of analyzing democracy.
Plutocracy Rule by the wealthy; a group that lacks the culture and courage of a true aristocracy.
Puissant Having great power, influence, or potency.
Ratiocination The process of exact thinking or logical reasoning; Mencken claims most men find this "unbearably painful."
Therapeutics The branch of medicine concerned with the treatment of disease and the action of remedial agents.
Vox populi, vox Dei "The voice of the people is the voice of God"; a maxim Mencken disputes, arguing the voice of the people is often a raucous defiance of divine order.
