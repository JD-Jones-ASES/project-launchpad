Study Guide: The Rewards of Virtue in Democratic Politics

This study guide examines the analysis provided in "Notes on Democracy, II.8: The Rewards of Virtue." It explores the systemic challenges facing honest individuals in democratic political systems, the role of public perception, and the influence of the media on governance.

Key Concepts and Themes

The Paradox of the Honest Politician

The text posits that in a democratic system, the rare honest man who enters politics is frequently punished for his virtues, while fraudulent individuals are rewarded for their vices. Any success an honorable man achieves is often described as a "suspension of natural laws" and is typically transient.

Geographical Barriers to Integrity

Political integrity varies by region. According to the analysis:

  • Newer and Border States: Honest men have a marginal chance at success due to local preferences for "vigorous, rough-and-ready fighters" or unique historical circumstances (e.g., the post-war "Katzenjammer").
  • Established States (e.g., Georgia and Vermont): Where party fidelity is deeply entrenched, men of genuine dignity and ability are effectively barred from public life. These areas are instead served by "party hacks" who engage in "ignoble contortions" and "trimming."

The Assumption of Venality

A central tenet of the text is that the "mob" (the general public) is unable to comprehend the concept of honor. Consequently, the public assumes that every politician is motivated by "sordid and degrading motives" or "private advantage." This creates a "technique of terrorization" where:

  • Honest acts are ascribed to corrupt motives.
  • Public figures are accused of being "in the pay" of various interest groups.
  • Decent men are driven out of office by the weight of these baseless assumptions.

The Role of the Press

The text offers a scathing critique of newspaper editors, describing them as:

  • Influencers of the Mob: They lack sound knowledge and intelligence, which makes them fit for influencing the masses.
  • Trimmers and Flatterers: They prioritize their jobs over self-respect, often praising fraudulent ideas and denouncing honorable men.
  • Agents of Elimination: By voicing the ideas of the mob, they help eliminate sense and decency from public life.

The Cult of Incompetence

Citing French critic Emile Faguet, the text argues that democracy leads to a "Cult of Incompetence." In this environment:

  • Law-making becomes a series of panics ("government by orgy and orgasm").
  • Public service becomes a refuge for "prehensile morons" focused solely on personal gain.
  • Competence is viewed with suspicion and creates enemies among less capable peers.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to the text, what specific factors allowed the Senator from Missouri to overcome the "tremendous odds" against him?

Answer: His success was attributed to his "amazing talents as a popular orator," the "post-war Katzenjammer," and a local cultural preference for "vigorous, rough-and-ready fighters."

2. Why does the author believe that party fidelity prevents men of dignity from entering office in states like Georgia and Vermont?

Answer: In states with long-standing party fidelity, the system favors "party hacks" who are willing to trim and fawn, whereas men of dignity are barred because they would require a frank and competent discussion of issues that these systems forbid.

3. What "technique of terrorization" did Prohibitionist leaders use against their opponents?

Answer: They accused any politician who spoke against them of "grossest baseness," whispering that they were "secret drunkards" or in the pay of the "Whiskey Ring" or "Beer Trust."

4. How did the "Medical Trust" accusation manifest in the author's own experience?

Answer: When the author exposed the "quackeries" of osteopaths and chiropractors, he was accused of taking a retainer from the Medical Trust, which purportedly included the Mayo brothers and the faculty of Johns Hopkins.

5. How does the author describe the "typical American editor"?

Answer: As an "adept trimmer and flatterer" who has written millions of words in favor of conventional figures (like Coolidge) and against scientific figures (like Darwin), prioritizing their job over their self-respect.

6. What is the public reaction to a newspaper editor who shows courage and honesty?

Answer: They encounter "incredulous hostility," are often dismissed as a "Bolshevik," or are assumed to be engaged in a "nefarious scheme of personal aggrandizement."

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Psychology of the Mob: Analyze the author's argument that the public is "unable to grasp the concept of honour." How does this psychological barrier function as a tool for political corruption and the exclusion of "decent men" from public life?
  2. Media as a Tool of Democracy: The text suggests that the press, rather than being a watchdog, is a primary driver of the "democratic process of eliminating all sense and decency." Discuss the specific characteristics of editors and the "concrete men" behind newspapers that lead to this outcome according to the text.
  3. Institutionalized Incompetence: Using the concept of the "Cult of Incompetence," evaluate how the democratic process, as described in the source, actively discourages mastery of one's job in the public service. What are the stated consequences for a public servant who actually "masters his job"?

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition per Source Context
Bolshevik A label used to dismiss someone as harboring an "occult and unintelligible yearning" to destroy the Republic and religion.
Comstocks A group or individuals involved in what the author describes as "sinister and dishonest censorship of literature."
Katzenjammer A term used to describe the post-war state of confusion or distress that influenced voter behavior in Missouri.
Party Hack A politician who is a loyal follower of a party, characterized by "depressing imbecilities" and "ignoble contortions."
Prehensile Morons A derogatory term for individuals in public service who are solely interested in personal acquisition ("get yours, and run").
Trimming The act of adjusting one's opinions or policies for the sake of expediency or to please the mob.
Venality The quality of being open to bribery or corruption; the author notes the mob assumes this of all public figures.
Whiskey Ring / Beer Trust "Bugaboos" or mythical interest groups used by Prohibitionists to accuse opponents of being corruptly funded.
