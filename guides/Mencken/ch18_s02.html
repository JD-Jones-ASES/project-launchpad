Study Guide: The Rewards of Virtue in Democratic Politics

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the analysis provided in "The Rewards of Virtue," an excerpt from H.L. Mencken’s Notes on Democracy. It explores the systemic challenges facing honorable individuals in democratic systems, the psychological nature of the "mob," and the roles of the press and institutional corruption.

Key Concepts and Themes

1. The Survival of the Unfit in Politics

The text posits that democracy naturally selects against men of integrity. An intelligent and honorable man can only succeed through a "suspension of natural laws," and such success is typically transient. In contrast, those who thrive are often "party hacks" who have "trimmed and fawned," avoiding frank discussions on principal national issues to maintain their positions.

2. Regional Differences in Political Integrity

The document identifies specific geographical trends in American politics:

  • Established States: In states where party fidelity is historically strong, such as Georgia or Vermont, men of genuine dignity and ability are effectively barred from public life.
  • Border and Newer States: Honest men have a slightly better chance in these regions, though they still face "incredible bitterness" and opposition from all political factions.

3. The Mob’s Incapacity for Honor

A central argument is that the "mob" (the general public) and the majority of politicians are unable to grasp the concept of honor. Consequently, when a person of rectitude acts, their motives are inevitably ascribed to "sordid and degrading" incentives, such as private advantage or bribery. This assumption of venality is cited as the primary factor keeping decent men out of public life.

4. The Technique of Terrorization

Using the Eighteenth Amendment (Prohibition) as a case study, the text illustrates how interest groups use "terrorization" to silence opposition. Politicians who spoke against Prohibition were accused of being secret drunkards or being in the pay of the "Whiskey Ring." Paradoxically, many leaders of the Prohibition movement were themselves on the payroll of the Anti-Saloon League or involved in criminal activities.

5. The Role of the Press

The typical American editor is described as an "adept trimmer and flatterer" who voices the ideas of the mob. The press often:

  • Praises fraudulent men and ideas while denouncing those respected by "their betters."
  • Treats honest political or social combat as a "sham battle" for publicity.
  • Views any editor who displays courage or honesty with "incredulous hostility."

6. The "Cult of Incompetence"

Citing French critic Emile Faguet, the text argues that democracy is "furiously inimical" to competence and honor. Under this system:

  • Law-making becomes a series of "panics" or "orgies."
  • Public service is a "refuge for prehensile morons" focused on personal gain.
  • Mastering a job makes a public servant an enemy to their less capable peers.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

Question Answer
Why is the success of an honest man in democracy usually transient? Because he faces "incredible bitterness" and a combination of all political factions that eventually overcome him; his first term is commonly his last.
According to the text, what happened to the Senator from Missouri? Despite his ability and integrity, he was opposed by all parties and factions. He only won due to his oratorical talents and a local preference for "rough-and-ready fighters."
What is the primary motive the "plain people" ascribe to any public man? A "yearning for private advantage" or sordid motives similar to those of the "orthodox colleagues."
How did Prohibitionist leaders silence their political opponents? They used a "technique of terrorization," accusing opponents of being secret drunkards or being in the pay of the "Whiskey Ring" or "Beer Trust."
What was the irony regarding the accusations made by the Anti-Saloon League? While they accused others of corruption, their own agents were on the payroll of the League, and some were later imprisoned for grave crimes.
How does the text describe the intellectual leanings of the typical American editor? As someone who has written a million words in favor of Coolidge and half a million against Darwin; they lack sound knowledge and genuine intelligence.
What two specific editors are cited as examples of honest men facing hostility? Fremont Older of San Francisco and Julian Harris of Columbus, Georgia.
What is meant by the term "Bolshevik" in the context of the text? A label used to dismiss those with "occult and unintelligible" motives to overthrow the Republic or religious order.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Psychology of Projection: Analyze the author's claim that the public is "unable to imagine any other motive save a yearning for private advantage." How does this psychological projection create a barrier to entry for honorable individuals in public service? Use examples from the text regarding Prohibition and the author's personal experiences.

  2. The Media as a Mirror of the Mob: The document suggests that the press does not lead public opinion but rather "voices the ideas of the mob." Discuss the implications of this relationship on the "democratic process of eliminating all sense and decency from public life."

  3. Institutionalized Incompetence: Compare the author’s observations of American politics with Emile Faguet’s "The Cult of Incompetence." To what extent does the text argue that democracy is naturally allergic to mastery and merit in the public sector?

  4. The Price of Dissent: Drawing on the author’s personal experiences with "the comstocks," the "Medical Trust," and religious groups, discuss the consequences of challenging established social and political theories. How does the "assumption of cowardice and venality" affect those who are not seeking office but are engaged in public discussion?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Anti-Saloon League: A powerful Prohibitionist organization that used its payroll to influence judges, attorneys-general, and other high officers.
  • Comstocks: A group or movement described as engaging in "sinister and dishonest censorship of literature."
  • Katzenjammer: A term used to describe the post-war state of confusion or distress (specifically the "post-war Katzenjammer" in Missouri).
  • Medical Trust: A mythical entity (including the Mayo brothers and Johns Hopkins) that the author was accused of being paid by when he exposed "quackeries" such as osteopathy and chiropractic.
  • Prehensile Morons: A derogatory term for those in public service who are focused solely on "getting theirs" (personal gain).
  • Rectitude: Morally correct behavior or thinking; righteousness.
  • The Cult of Incompetence: A concept by Emile Faguet describing a system that favors the unqualified and treats law-making as a series of panics.
  • Trimmer: A person who adapts their views to the prevailing political climate for personal or professional survival; a flatterer.
  • Venality: The state of being open to bribery or corruption.
  • Whiskey Ring / Beer Trust: "Bugaboos" or labels used by Prohibitionists to accuse their opponents of being in the pay of the alcohol industry.
