Study Guide: The Rewards of Virtue in Democratic Politics

This study guide examines the analysis provided in "The Rewards of Virtue," an excerpt from Notes on Democracy. The text explores the inherent difficulties faced by honorable individuals in democratic systems, the psychological gap between the "mob" and the man of integrity, and the systemic corruption within politics and the press.

Core Concepts and Themes

1. The Paradox of the Honest Candidate

In a democratic system, the rare honest man who enters politics is typically punished for his virtues, while the "fraud" is rewarded for his vices. Success for an honorable man is often regarded as a "suspension of natural laws" and is usually transient.

  • Barriers to Entry: In states with long-standing party fidelity (e.g., Georgia and Vermont), men of dignity are effectively barred from public life. These positions are instead filled by "party hacks" who engage in "ignoble contortions" and "trimming."
  • The Missouri Exception: The text cites a specific Senator from Missouri who retained his seat despite opposition from all factions. His success is attributed to "amazing talents" as an orator and a local preference for "rough-and-ready fighters," rather than a systemic appreciation for his integrity.

2. The Mob’s Incapacity for Honor

A central argument of the text is that the "mob" (the general public) and the majority of politicians are unable to grasp the concept of honor.

  • Assumption of Sordid Motives: When a man of rectitude performs a public act, the public ascribes his actions to "sordid and degrading motives." The public believes that every man is motivated solely by "private advantage."
  • The "Technique of Terrorization": Using the Eighteenth Amendment (Prohibition) as an example, the text illustrates how leaders play upon mob prejudices. Anyone opposing Prohibition was accused of being a "secret drunkard" or in the pay of the "Whiskey Ring," even when the accusers themselves were the ones receiving corrupt payments.

3. The Role of the Press

Newspaper editors are identified as a powerful class that reinforces the worst instincts of the democratic process.

  • The Editor as "Trimmer": Editors are described as flatterers who lack sound knowledge and honor. Their daily work involves praising fraudulent men and denouncing those respected by "their betters."
  • Mob Alignment: Editors come from the mob and voice its ideas: that a fraud is a "regular fellow" and that an honest man is either dangerous or non-existent.
  • Treatment of Dissent: Editors who show courage or honesty (e.g., Fremont Older or Julian Harris) are met with hostility and accused of "personal aggrandizement" or being "Bolsheviks."

4. The Cult of Incompetence

Citing French critic Emile Faguet, the text argues that democracy is "furiously inimical" to competence and honor.

  • Hostility toward Mastery: A public servant who masters his job earns the enmity of his colleagues and the suspicion of the masses.
  • Government by Orgy: Law-making is characterized as a series of panics, and public service becomes a "refuge for prehensile morons."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to the text, why is the success of an honest man in politics usually "transient"?
  2. What specific "technique of terrorization" did Prohibitionist leaders use against their opponents?
  3. Why did the brewers offer to put the author on their payroll, and what was their reaction when he declined?
  4. How does the text define a "typical American editor" in smaller towns?
  5. What happened to the author when he protested the "censorship of literature" by the Comstocks?
  6. According to Emile Faguet, what does the business of law-making become under a democracy?
  7. Identify the two editors mentioned as examples of honest men who faced hostility from their colleagues.
  8. What is the "first idea" of the mob regarding frauds and honest men?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Psychology of Accusation: Analyze the text’s argument that the public ascribes "sordid and degrading motives" to honorable men. How does this psychological barrier prevent "decent men" from entering public life, and what does it suggest about the moral mirror the "mob" holds up to itself?
  2. The Press as a Democratic Filter: Examine the role of the newspaper editor as described in the text. How does the editor’s "happy obtuseness to notions of honour" serve to eliminate "sense and decency" from public life?
  3. The Institutionalization of Incompetence: Using the references to "party hacks" and Emile Faguet’s "The Cult of Incompetence," discuss the systemic factors that the text claims prioritize "prehensile morons" over competent public servants.

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition per the Source Context
Anti-Saloon League An organization whose supporters and officers (including judges and attorneys-general) were revealed to be on its payroll while advocating for Prohibition.
Bolshevik A derogatory label used to dismiss someone as one who harbours an "unintelligible yearning to put down the Republic."
Comstocks A group responsible for what the text describes as "sinister and dishonest censorship of literature."
Cult of Incompetence A concept by Emile Faguet describing a system where mastery of a job is viewed with suspicion and law-making is driven by "panics."
Katzenjammer A state of post-war distress or confusion that, in the case of Missouri, helped an honest Senator overcome political odds.
Medical Trust A mythical entity that critics accused the author of being paid by when he exposed "quackeries" like osteopathy and chiropractic.
Prehensile Morons A term used to describe those who treat public service as a mere refuge for personal gain ("get yours, and run").
Trimmer A person (usually a politician or editor) who adapts their views and fawns over others for the sake of professional security or "private advantage."
Whiskey Ring / Beer Trust "Bugaboos" or fabricated threats used by Prohibitionists to discredit their opponents through accusations of corruption.
