# Study Guide: Mencken’s Critique of Democracy and Progress

This study guide examines the arguments presented in Chapter 9, Section 2 of H.L. Mencken’s *Notes on Democracy*. The text provides a scathing analysis of the relationship between democratic civilization and human progress, arguing that the masses inherently oppose the very innovations that benefit them.

---

### Key Concepts and Themes

#### The Conflict Between the Minority and the Masses
Mencken posits that progress is exclusively the product of a "rare and gifted few." In contrast, the "inferior man"—the democratic citizen—is depicted as a "natural slave" who is incapable of understanding or desiring genuine liberty. This creates a fundamental tension where the majority seeks to "put down" the superior minority out of envy and a lack of comprehension.

#### The Paradox of Progress
A central theme is that the masses consistently oppose social and scientific reforms, even when those reforms are designed for their own benefit. Mencken argues that "social justice" in a democracy is often a false construct used by the laborer to gain more than his "fair hire," while true justice remains unpopular and persecuted.

#### The Psychology of Resistance
The text attributes the hostility of the masses (referred to as *Homo neandertalensis*) to a fear of complexity. Because scientific knowledge and fine arts are complex and "occult" to the uneducated mind, the masses turn to "short cuts" and "simple formulæ." This explains the popularity of:
*   **Superstitions:** Fundamentalism and spiritualism.
*   **Quackery:** Chiropractic and osteopathy.
*   **Political Platitudes:** Shibboleths that make the unintelligible seem simple.

#### The Protection of Learning through Obscurity
Mencken suggests that higher learning and the fine arts survive only because the "mob" is largely unaware of them. If the masses fully understood the nature of scientific inquiry or high art, they would likely use the law to suppress them, as evidenced by historical and contemporary censorship.

### Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. According to the text, why is the "inferior man" unable to understand the desire for liberty in his superiors?**
The inferior man is a "natural slave" who cannot apprehend liberty except as an appetite for a good he is incapable of possessing. This leads to envy and a desire to suppress those who harbor a genuine desire for liberty.

**2. What historical examples does Mencken provide to show that the American public opposed beneficial government bureaus?**
Mencken notes that the American Department of Agriculture was almost unanimously opposed by farmers when it began in 1830. Additionally, he mentions that John Adams was denounced as a "scoundrel" for proposing a Weather Bureau.

**3. How did the German proletariat react to Otto von Bismarck’s social legislation?**
The legislation—which included workingmen’s insurance and child labor laws—was fought "with the utmost ferocity" by its intended beneficiaries. Bismarck had to carry out the government unconstitutionally because the masses and their "mob-masters" in the Reichstag opposed him.

**4. What did Sir Henry Maine argue would have happened if universal suffrage had been established during the eras of scientific invention?**
Maine argued that universal suffrage would have prohibited the spinning-jenny, the power loom, and the threshing-machine. It would have prevented the adoption of the Gregorian Calendar and led to the proscription of Roman Catholics and Dissenters.

**5. Why does the text claim that "Fundamentalism" is popular among the "peasantry"?**
Fundamentalism is popular because it offers a simple cosmogony (such as the first chapter of Genesis) that a "yokel" can grasp instantly, whereas the cosmogonies of educated men are inordinately complex and require a special habit of thought.

**6. What role did the American Legion play in the "pogrom against Reds" and the suppression of the arts?**
The American Legion provided "heroic services" in destroying the rights of fellow proletarians during the post-war crusade against "Reds" and used "clubs against fiddlers" who played the music of Beethoven and Bach in the Middle West.

**7. How is the censorship of literature in Boston described?**
Censorship in Boston is led by "Methodist anthropoids" and the "Chandala of the Latin rite," who carry out a violent crusade against books of sound quality, forcing citizens to procure literature by stealth.

### Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Architecture of Inequality:** Analyze Mencken’s argument that "it takes quite as long to breed a libertarian as it takes to breed a race-horse." How does this biological metaphor support his view of democratic civilization? Discuss the implications of his claim that the "superior minority" is an inherited rather than a manufactured class.
2.  **Complexity and the "Egyptian Night":** Explore the relationship between intellectual complexity and mass hostility. Using examples from the text—such as the opposition to vaccination in Los Angeles or the rejection of the "new physics"—evaluate Mencken’s thesis that the average human being fears what they cannot understand.
3.  **The Role of the "Mob-Master":** Mencken mentions that public policies are often determined by "small minorities playing upon the fears and imbecilities of the mob." Discuss how these minorities (both honest men and "rogues") navigate the democratic system to implement changes that the majority might otherwise reject.
4.  **Censorship as a Defense Mechanism:** Examine the text's assertion that the fine arts and sciences are safe only when they remain outside the "cognizance of the mob." To what extent does Mencken view the "ignorance" of the masses as a necessary condition for the survival of high culture?

### Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Common Man** | A political abstraction representing the ordinary citizen; treated by Mencken with sustained contempt. |
| **Homo neandertalensis** | A derogatory term used to describe the masses, suggesting they are intellectually primitive. |
| **Proletarian** | The working-class citizen who, according to Mencken, is often willing to destroy the rights of others and values comforts (like beer) over liberty. |
| **Libertarian** | In this context, a rare individual who possesses a genuine understanding of and desire for liberty, often described as the "grandson of similar heretics." |
| **Fundamentalism** | A popular religious movement described as a "farrago of absurdities" that appeals to the masses due to its simplicity. |
| **Chiropractic/Osteopathy** | Mentioned as "quackeries" or "short cuts" that the masses prefer over complex medical sciences like pathology. |
| **The Hookworm Belt** | A derogatory regional reference to areas (likely the American South) where anti-evolution acts were vociferously supported by the "lower orders." |
| **Chandala** | A term used to describe the lower social or intellectual orders involved in the censorship of literature in Boston. |
| **Feinschmecker** | Literally a "gourmet" or "connoisseur"; used sarcastically to describe a person with a refined interest in pornography but an ignorance of intellectual figures like Freud. |
| **Vox Populi** | "The voice of the people"; Mencken argues that as this voice becomes more powerful in the state, the rights of enlightened minorities decrease. |
