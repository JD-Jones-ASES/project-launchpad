# Study Guide: The Democrat as Moralist

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the analysis of democracy and Puritanism as presented in the text "The Democrat as Moralist" from *Notes on Democracy*. It explores the psychological underpinnings of democratic law, the historical transition from autocracy to the "rule of the mob," and the specific moral crusades that characterize the democratic state.

---

## Key Concepts and Core Themes

### The Identity of Democracy and Puritanism
The text asserts that democracy and Puritanism are essentially the same phenomenon ("different facets of the same gem"). Both originate from a psychological state of "fear and hatred" felt by the "inferior man" toward his "betters." This hatred is rooted in envy—specifically the observation that superior individuals possess more courage and enjoy life more fully.

### The Function of Autocracy vs. Democracy
*   **Autocracies:** Historically, autocracies served civilization by holding the "green fury of the mob" in check. This allowed the "spirit of superior man" to be set free, facilitating eras such as the Renaissance and the modern age.
*   **Democracy:** Defined as a political scheme that releases the mob's envy and gives it the "force and dignity of law." When the mob is liberated (as seen in the French and Russian Revolutions), it wage a "war to the death upon superiority of every kind."

### The "Anthropoid Good Man" and Puritan Legislation
The democratic state tends toward being a Puritan state because the political and theological ideas of the "natural democrat" are indistinguishable. 
*   **Stated Motive:** Puritan legislation (Prohibition, Comstockery, vice crusading) is often defended as altruistic or evangelical—an effort to benefit others against their will.
*   **Actual Motive:** The text argues the true motives are punitive: to punish others for having a better time and to reduce them to a lower, unhappier level.

### The Mechanics of Democratic Law-Making
Mencken describes the process of law-making in a democracy as "law-making by orgy."
1.  **Fanatics:** Inflame the mob with "brutal envies and quaking fears."
2.  **Scoundrels (Politicians):** Fearing the mob, they precipitately enact laws to satisfy the fanatics.
3.  **Haste:** Laws are passed quickly to prevent "sober second thought" and to cut off debate via the appeal to the majority.

### The Judicial System and the "Virtuoso of Virtue"
The legal system under democracy becomes an instrument of "psychopathological persecution."
*   **District Attorneys:** Use high-profile prosecutions of "men above the general" (such as rich or intelligent individuals) to gain favor with the mob and advance their political careers.
*   **The Mob:** Delights in seeing superior men "manhandled and humiliated."
*   **The Result:** The criminal law acquires a "flavour of fraud," and judges often prioritize their jobs over professional dignity.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **According to the text, what is the "primal essence" shared by democracy and Puritanism?**
    *   *Answer:* The "inferior man’s fear and hatred of his betters," driven by envy of their courage and their ability to enjoy life.

2.  **How did the collapse of autocracies under Flavius Honorius affect Europe?**
    *   *Answer:* It left Europe in chaos for four hundred years until the revival under Charlemagne.

3.  **What historical example is used to show the mob’s hatred of scientists?**
    *   *Answer:* The murder of Lavoisier during the French Revolution, where the Revolutionary Tribunal stated, "We need no scientists in France."

4.  **Why does the text claim the Puritan is against fine arts?**
    *   *Answer:* Because the Puritan misses the "exaltations of the spirit" and sees only the physical or "natural functions" (e.g., seeing only a model’s pudenda in a picture).

5.  **How does the text define the "Forgotten Man"?**
    *   *Answer:* The normal, well-behaved, decent citizen who is beset by laws designed to satisfy the envy of their inferiors and the "blood lust" of fanatics.

6.  **What was the "one genuinely valuable contribution to moral legislation" mentioned, and who created it?**
    *   *Answer:* The Code Napoléon, which was created by a committee of anti-Puritans reacting against democracy.

7.  **Why is haste necessary in democratic law-making?**
    *   *Answer:* To ensure the mob is not "shaken by sober second thought" before the legislation is enacted.

8.  **What psychological aberration is attributed to Prohibitionists who demand stricter enforcement despite the failure of the law?**
    *   *Answer:* Sadism—the "lust to inflict inconvenience, discomfort, and... disgrace" upon those they hate.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Evolution of Superiority:** Compare and contrast the text's view of how "superior men" fared under autocracies versus democratic systems. Use the examples of Lavoisier, Wat Tyler, and the "modern age" to support your analysis.
2.  **The Illusion of Altruism:** Critically analyze the argument that Puritan legislation is grounded in "punitive and remedial purposes" rather than evangelical ones. How does the text use Prohibition and the Mann Act to illustrate this point?
3.  **The Corruption of the Judiciary:** Discuss the text's claim that the American criminal law is a "flavour of fraud." Focus on the role of the District Attorney as a political climber and the mob’s influence on the treatment of "rich" or "conspicuous" defendants.
4.  **Milton vs. The Puritan:** The text claims John Milton was not a Puritan but a "libertarian." Explore the distinction the author makes between these two identities and why the author believes no true Puritan has ever produced great art.

## Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition from Source Context |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Casuistry** | Clever but unsound reasoning used to justify the jailing of inoffensive citizens for violating unjust laws. |
| **Comstockery** | Moralistic censorship and the suppression of free expression (named after Anthony Comstock). |
| **Democratic Law** | An instrument born of the mob's envy, used to release hatred and give it the force of law. |
| **Forgotten Man** | The decent, normal citizen who becomes a victim of laws designed to satisfy the "blood lust" of fanatics. |
| **Libertarian** | Defined in the text as the "exact opposite" of a Puritan; an individual who values liberty over moral legislation. |
| **Mann Act** | An example of Puritan legislation used to reduce romantic or "civilized" sexual adventure to the "terms of the barnyard." |
| **Puritan** | A "natural democrat" characterized by docility, lack of originality, and a desire to bring others down to their own unhappy level. |
| **Sadism** | The underlying motive of moral crusaders who enjoy inflicting discomfort and disgrace on those free from their superstitions. |
| **Theocracy** | Government ruled by religious authority; specifically referenced regarding the New England theocracy’s failure to restrain "libidinousness." |
| **Virtuoso of Virtue** | A Puritan who venerates themselves as having unusual moral talent, mistaking their own cowed docility for spiritual eminence. |
