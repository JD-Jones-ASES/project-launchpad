Study Guide: Mencken’s Analysis of Disproportional Representation

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of H.L. Mencken’s rhetorical analysis of democratic machinery, specifically focusing on the concepts of representation, popular sovereignty, and the use of satirical language to critique political institutions.

Key Concepts and Thematic Analysis

1. The Paradox of Disproportional Representation

Mencken addresses the common complaint that the United States political system is inherently "unfair"—noting examples such as the equal representation of Wyoming and New York in the Senate and the underrepresentation of cities in state legislatures. While reformers view these as flaws to be corrected, Mencken argues they are a "feature" of democracy: a voluntary sacrifice of pure democratic theory in exchange for a "workable practice."

2. The Absurdity of Popular Sovereignty

The text explores the concept of the "limitless power of the sovereign people." Mencken argues that while the people have the power to do almost anything, that power is often exercised in absurd or contradictory ways. Through a series of hypothetical scenarios, he demonstrates that if the democratic will is truly absolute, it is legally capable of enacting ridiculous mandates—from making it a crime to drink coffee to legalizing the assassination of public officers.

3. Satire of Judicial and Legislative Figures

Mencken strips the "civics textbook" dignity away from government officials. He characterizes Supreme Court justices not as impartial sages, but as "practical politicians" who have spent years struggling for "exalted honours." He uses medical and ceremonial language to suggest that their primary skill is not the pursuit of justice, but the ability to "genuflect" or bend to political and private interests.

Rhetorical Techniques and Stylistic Devices

Diction and Vocabulary

Mencken’s writing is characterized by a "virtuosity" in oscillating between different levels of language:

  • Technical vs. Colloquial: He mixes constitutional terminology (e.g., "proportional representation") with "barnyard contempt" (e.g., "yokels," "bucolic imbecilities").
  • Medical Metaphor: He applies clinical, anatomical language to political figures. By describing the "limberness" of the hamstrings (biceps femoris, semitendinosus, and semimembranosus), he uses medical precision to deliver a hidden insult: that judges are practiced at bowing to power.

Syntactic Structures

  • Parallelism: Mencken uses a "rhetorical avalanche" of "They could..." clauses. This repetition gives equal weight to both plausible constitutional amendments and surreal mandates, suggesting that under democracy, the reasonable and the insane have equal standing.
  • Periodic Sentences: He frequently employs long, qualifying structures that withhold the main point until the final clause, forcing the reader to absorb his full characterization of a subject before reaching the conclusion.

Argumentative Strategies

  • Reductio ad absurdum: Mencken extends the logic of popular sovereignty to its most ridiculous conclusions to demonstrate its inherent flaws.
  • Irony and Paradox: The central irony of the text is Mencken's attempt to prove the people are sovereign only to demonstrate that their sovereignty is ultimately worthless or contemptible.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does Mencken redefine "disproportional representation" in contrast to the views of reformers?

    • Answer: Mencken views it not as a flaw or an injustice, but as a "feature" and a "voluntary sacrifice" of pure theory for the sake of a workable practice.

  2. What is the significance of the word "fuglemen" in Mencken’s critique?

    • Answer: Originally referring to soldiers who demonstrate drill movements, Mencken uses it to reduce democratic reformers to "parade-ground performers" who offer show without substance.

  3. Explain the joke behind Mencken’s use of the terms biceps femoris, semitendinosus and semimembranosus.

    • Answer: These are the Latin names for the hamstring muscles used for bending the knee. Mencken uses them to mock the Supreme Court justices, implying they are physically practiced at "genuflecting" or bowing to political interests.

  4. What does Mencken mean by the phrase "elevated to the ermine"?

    • Answer: "Ermine" refers to the white fur worn by judges/monarchs. Mencken uses this ceremonial phrase ironically to treat a Supreme Court appointment as a "coronation" of individuals who were actually careerist politicians.

  5. What is the purpose of the "They could..." catalogue in the second half of the section?

    • Answer: The anaphoric repetition hammers home the "limitless scope" of popular sovereignty, escalating from plausible actions to absurd ones to show that the democratic will is capable of anything, including farce.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Anatomy of Satire: Analyze Mencken’s use of medical and anatomical diction. How does his clinical description of the "senile deterioration" of the Senate or the "limberness" of judges serve to dehumanize political institutions? Discuss how this "scientific" tone contributes to his sardonic authority.

  2. Theory vs. Practice in Democracy: Mencken argues that "pure theory" must be sacrificed for "workable practice." Using the text as a guide, evaluate Mencken’s skepticism regarding "pure democracy." Does he believe a truly proportional system is possible, or even desirable?

  3. The Mask of the Provocateur: Trace the shift in Mencken’s tone from "sardonic patience" to "reckless" provocation. How does his persona evolve as he moves from discussing the Constitution to proposing the legalization of assassination? What does this shift suggest about his relationship with his audience?

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition in Context
Biceps femoris, semitendinosus, semimembranosus The three muscles of the posterior thigh (hamstrings); used by Mencken to mock the "limberness" or genuflection of judges.
Ermine White fur traditionally worn by judges as a symbol of authority; used to mock the "coronation" of Supreme Court justices.
Fuglemen Originally drill-demonstration soldiers; used by Mencken to describe advocates of "pure democracy" as empty performers.
Kine in the byres Archaic phrase for cattle in their stalls; used to mock the idea of extending the vote to anyone or anything (universalism).
Parallelism The use of similar grammatical structures in successive clauses to create rhythm and a sense of logical inevitability.
Periodic Sentence A sentence that delays its main point or predicate until the very end to build suspense or emphasize a portrait.
Puissant Powerful or mighty; used with a "medieval echo" to describe Supreme Court justices as feudal lords.
Reductio ad absurdum A mode of argument that proves a proposition is false or ridiculous by following its logic to a preposterous conclusion.
Sovereignty The supreme power or authority of a state; in this context, the unlimited (and often absurd) power of the voting public.
