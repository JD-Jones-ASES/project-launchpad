Study Guide: Analysis of Mencken’s "Disproportional Representation"

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of H.L. Mencken’s rhetorical strategies and thematic arguments in his analysis of democratic machinery. It focuses on his critique of representation, the Supreme Court, and the nature of popular sovereignty.

Key Concepts and Thematic Overview

1. The Practicality of Disproportional Representation

Mencken addresses the common complaint that the United States political system is inherently unfair due to unequal representation (e.g., the equal Senate representation of Wyoming and New York). While reformers view this as a flaw, Mencken argues it is a voluntary sacrifice of pure theory in favor of workable practice. He contends that such "injustice" is actually an expression of the democratic will itself.

2. The Nature of the Supreme Court

Mencken demystifies the Supreme Court, stripping away its ceremonial dignity. He characterizes the justices not as impartial sages, but as "practical politicians" who have spent their careers struggling for "exalted honours." He suggests they possess a "beautiful talent" for aligning justice with private profit.

3. Unlimited Popular Sovereignty

A central theme is the limitless, though rarely exercised, power of the "sovereign people." Mencken argues that under a democracy, the people have the legal right to enact any absurdity they choose. This sovereignty is not necessarily a tool for progress but a demonstration of the potential for farcical and reckless collective action.

4. Critique of Reformers

Mencken treats democratic reformers—whom he calls "romantic fuglemen"—with sardonic patience. He views their complicated remedies for "pure democracy" as failures that have already been tried and found wanting. To Mencken, these reformers are more interested in "show" and performance than in the messy reality of political machinery.

Rhetorical and Stylistic Devices

Diction and Vocabulary

Mencken’s style is defined by a "virtuosity" that oscillates between two extremes:

  • Technical/Constitutional: Phrases like "one-man-one-vote formula" or "proportional representation."
  • Colloquial/Barnyard: Derisive terms such as "yokels," "bucolic imbecilities," and "kine in the byres."
  • Medical/Anatomical: The use of Latin anatomical terms to describe political behavior (e.g., the hamstrings).

Syntactic Strategies

  • Parallelism and Anaphora: Mencken uses the repetitive "They could..." structure to create a "rhetorical avalanche." This highlights the scope of popular power by giving equal weight to both reasonable and insane propositions.
  • Periodic Sentences: Mencken often delays the main point of a sentence until the final clause, forcing the reader to absorb his descriptive characterizations before reaching the ultimate conclusion.

Argumentative Modes

  • Reductio ad absurdum: Mencken extends the logic of popular sovereignty to its most ridiculous extremes (e.g., making it a crime to drink coffee) to demonstrate the inherent instability or absurdity of the concept.
  • Structural Irony: The entire argument is built on the paradox that the more power Mencken attributes to the people, the more he highlights their failure to use it wisely.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What does Mencken mean by the term "fuglemen" in the context of democratic reform?
  2. How does Mencken justify the existence of disproportional representation in the United States?
  3. Explain the irony behind Mencken’s use of the term "elevated to the ermine."
  4. In the section discussing Supreme Court justices, what is Mencken actually saying when he refers to the "limberness" of their biceps femoris and semitendinosus?
  5. What is the purpose of the long catalogue of "They could" clauses in the second half of the text?
  6. How does Mencken use "barnyard" imagery to describe democratic universalism?
  7. Why does Mencken characterize Supreme Court justices as "practical politicians"?
  8. According to the text, what is the relationship between "pure theory" and "workable practice" in democracy?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Anatomy of Political Satire

Analyze Mencken’s use of medical and anatomical vocabulary. How does his clinical description of politicians' bodies serve to dehumanize them or strip away their professional dignity? Discuss how this "scientific" tone contributes to his overall sardonic persona.

2. Sovereignty and Absurdity

Mencken argues that the democratic will is capable of anything, "including absurdity." Explore his use of reductio ad absurdum in his list of potential laws (such as legalizing assassination or requiring the President to shave his head). How does this strategy undermine the "romantic" view of popular sovereignty?

3. The Conflict Between Reform and Reality

Contrast Mencken’s "practical political observer" persona with the "romantic fuglemen" he critiques. Based on the text, why does Mencken believe that reformers’ attempts to achieve "pure democracy" are doomed to fail? Use his views on disproportional representation to support your argument.

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Anaphora The repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses (e.g., "They could...").
Biceps femoris, semitendinosus, semimembranosus Latin names for the hamstring muscles. Mencken uses these to mock the "limberness" (subservience) of judges.
Ermine White fur worn by judges/monarchs; used by Mencken to mock the ceremonial dignity of the Supreme Court.
Fuglemen Originally soldiers who demonstrated drill movements; Mencken uses it to describe reformers who are "all show" and expect mindless imitation.
Kine in the byres An archaic phrase meaning cattle in their stalls; used to make democratic enfranchisement sound like a "barnyard roll call."
Parallelism The use of similar grammatical structures in successive clauses to create rhythm and logical inevitability.
Periodic Sentence A sentence that withholds its main point or predicate until the very end for rhetorical effect.
Puissant Powerful or mighty; used with a "medieval echo" to describe Supreme Court justices.
Reductio ad absurdum An argumentative strategy that proves a point by showing that the opposite or the extension of the logic leads to ridiculous results.
Sovereignty The supreme power or authority of a state or the people.
