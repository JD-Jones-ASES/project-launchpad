H.L. Mencken’s Legacy: A Study Guide

This study guide examines the life, stylistic influence, and enduring controversy surrounding H.L. Mencken, as detailed in "Mencken's Legacy: The Prose That Outlived the Polemic." It explores how Mencken’s craft allowed his work to survive the decline of his social and political influence.

Key Concepts

The Decline of Influence

While Mencken reached the peak of his fame in 1926 with the publication of Notes on Democracy, his popularity waned significantly by 1935. Two primary historical factors contributed to this shift:

  • The Great Depression: Mencken’s "aristocratic contempt" for the common man was viewed as cruel rather than clever during a period of 25% unemployment and widespread economic suffering.
  • The Rise of Fascism: As authoritarian regimes rose in Europe, Mencken’s habitual attacks on democracy were no longer seen as intellectual sport, but as potential collaboration with tyranny.

The Craft of Prose

The guide emphasizes that Mencken’s work survived not because of his specific political stances—many of which concerned dead issues like Prohibition or the Scopes Trial—but because of his mastery of language. His "craft" is defined by:

  • An ear for rhythm.
  • An instinct for "devastating" word choices.
  • The ability to transform contempt and outrage into entertainment and beauty.

Separating Craft from Content

A central theme in the study of Mencken is the distinction between how something is said and what is being said. Analyzing Mencken’s rhetoric serves as a form of "intellectual self-defense," allowing readers to identify:

  • Hyperbole: The use of exaggeration as a diagnostic tool.
  • Irony: Conveying meanings through the opposite of literal statements.
  • Loaded Diction: Words chosen to manipulate the reader's emotions.

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Booboisie A term used by Mencken to describe the American public; Tom Wolfe’s satirical portraits of status-seeking owe a debt to this concept.
Gonzo Journalism A style invented by Hunter S. Thompson where the reporter becomes a central figure in the story, blending fact with personal commentary and satire.
New Journalism A movement in the 1960s and 70s, pioneered by Tom Wolfe, applying fiction techniques (dialogue, scene-by-scene construction) to factual reporting.
Polemicist A writer who engages in aggressive verbal or written attacks on the opinions or principles of others; exemplified by Christopher Hitchens.
The Great Depression The worldwide economic downturn starting in 1929; in the U.S., it led to 25% unemployment and the loss of homes and savings for millions.
Hyperbole Deliberate and obvious exaggeration used for effect; Mencken used this as a "diagnostic tool" in his essays.
Literary Journalism A tradition of factual writing using literary styles, dating back to 18th-century essayists Addison and Steele.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What were the titles of Mencken’s three memoirs written in the 1940s, and how did their tone differ from his earlier work? The memoirs were Happy Days (1940), Newspaper Days (1941), and Heathen Days (1943). They were characterized as warmer, more nostalgic, and less combative than his earlier polemics.

  2. How did Hunter S. Thompson’s "Gonzo journalism" differ from Mencken’s approach to reporting? While both treated American politics as a "spectacle of human folly," Thompson placed himself inside the spectacle as a central figure, whereas Mencken observed it from an "aristocratic" distance above.

  3. What did Christopher Hitchens consider to be a "form of moral action"? Hitchens believed that a "well-turned sentence" constituted a form of moral action.

  4. Who were Joseph Addison and Richard Steele? They were 18th-century English essayists who co-founded The Tatler and The Spectator, establishing the model for literary journalism that Mencken later "Americanized."

  5. What event in 1948 ended Mencken’s career as a writer? A devastating stroke in 1948 took away his ability to read and write, leaving him in silence for the final eight years of his life.

  6. What was the specific controversy surrounding the 1989 publication of Mencken’s diary? The diary, edited by Charles A. Fecher, revealed private passages containing "unambiguously bigoted" language regarding African Americans and Jews, which contradicted Mencken’s public record of mocking the KKK and defending Jewish friends.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Paradox of Reputation

Mencken is frequently described as a "paradoxical" figure. Based on the provided text, evaluate the tension between Mencken's public record of opposing organized bigotry (such as his attacks on the Ku Klux Klan) and the private sentiments revealed in his posthumous diaries. Does the brilliance of a writer’s craft "negate" the ugliness of their private views, or should the two be weighed equally?

2. Mencken’s Modern Relevance

The text argues that while the specific targets of Mencken's 1920s essays (such as Warren Harding and the Scopes Trial) are no longer current, his rhetorical techniques remain relevant. Discuss how Mencken’s observations on the "power of demagogues" and the "tendency of democratic majorities to suppress dissent" apply to the permanent features of democratic life in the twenty-first century.

3. The Lineage of American Prose

Compare and contrast the influence of Mencken on Tom Wolfe and Hunter S. Thompson. How did Mencken’s "cruder, funnier, and more willing to offend" version of literary journalism manifest in the "New Journalism" of the 1960s and the "Gonzo journalism" of the 1970s? Use specific examples from the text regarding their fascination with manners, status, and the blurring of fact and performance.
