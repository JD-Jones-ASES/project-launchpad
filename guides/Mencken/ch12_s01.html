Study Guide: H.L. Mencken on "The Popular Will"

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the analysis of H.L. Mencken’s critique of democratic sovereignty as presented in Chapter 12, Section 1: "The Popular Will." It explores the rhetorical strategies, linguistic choices, and philosophical arguments used to challenge the notion that "the people" possess a coherent or competent political will.

Key Concepts and Rhetorical Analysis

1. The Paradox of Sovereignty vs. Incompetence

Mencken’s argument rests on a central tension: the theoretical total power of the American people versus their practical total incompetence. While the "fundamental compact" grants citizens the right to do anything—from enfranchising "idiots" to abolishing legislatures—Mencken argues this power is meaningless because voters lack the knowledge and will to use it. They are portrayed as being easily manipulated by "prehensile soothsayers" and "enterprising minorities."

2. Enumeratio: The Catalogue as Rhetorical Weapon

A primary syntactic feature of this section is the use of enumeratio, or the extended catalogue. Mencken provides a page-long list of absurd actions the public could legally take, such as:

  • Enfranchising "the kine in the byres" (cattle in the barns).
  • Extending the President’s term to life.
  • Requiring the President to shave his head or sleep in his underclothes.

The purpose of this list is twofold: it proves the technical reality of unlimited sovereignty while simultaneously making that sovereignty appear ridiculous through the accumulation of increasingly absurd examples.

3. The "Science of Politics"

Mencken redefines political science under democracy not as a study of governance, but as a "master transaction" or a trade. He identifies a three-way exchange:

  1. The People: Yield up power in exchange for what they want (or the mere appearance of it).
  2. The Politician: Provides "affecting rhetoric" to "hoodwink" the voters.
  3. Enterprising Minorities: The forces behind the politician who receive the actual benefits of the transaction.

4. Animal Imagery and Metaphor

Mencken uses a descending hierarchy of animal metaphors to describe the electorate and the political process:

  • The Electorate: Described as "sheep" (docile), "donkeys" (stubborn/stupid), and "goats" (gullible victims).
  • The Conflict: Defined as a "combat between jackals and jackasses." The politicians are the predators (jackals) and the voters are the beasts of burden (jackasses).

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition in Context
Antinomian Traditionally one who rejects moral law; used by Mencken to mock voters who applied religious categories to a candidate's secular life (e.g., divorce).
Blackleg A swindler or cheat, specifically applied by Mencken to Prohibition agents to equate law enforcement with criminal enterprise.
Enumeratio A figure of speech involving the listing or cataloging of details to overwhelm the reader or emphasize the absurdity of a point.
Mellifluous Sweet-sounding or honeyed; describes the "affecting rhetoric" used to mask sordid political bargaining.
Prehensile Soothsayers A phrase describing politicians as grasping (prehensile) like primates while pretending to be prophets (soothsayers).
Rhetorical Question A question asked for emphasis rather than an answer; Mencken uses these to make his conclusions feel self-evident to the reader.
Sovereignty The supreme power or authority of a state; Mencken concedes the people have it in theory but denies they have the capacity to exercise it.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does Mencken justify calling the American people "goats"? He suggests they are more than just docile (sheep) or stupid (donkeys); they are "willing victims" who actively collaborate in their own exploitation.

  2. What is the significance of Mencken's shift in tone throughout the section? Mencken moves from a dispassionate, "mock-scientific" analyst to a "prophet of doom." This instability is intended to keep the reader off balance and complicates the reader's understanding of Mencken's perspective.

  3. According to the "Close Reading" section, why is the comparison of politics to the "sale of a mule" significant? It strips away the "affecting rhetoric" of democracy to reveal a "simple and sordid" transaction. The choice of a mule—sterile and associated with poverty—emphasizes the lack of nobility in the democratic process.

  4. How does Mencken use the term "hoary scripture" to describe the Constitution? He uses elevated, religious diction to highlight the gap between the perceived grandeur of political theory and the "meanness" of political reality.

  5. What is the role of "enterprising minorities" in Mencken's political model? They are the entities standing behind the politician who receive what they want from the transaction, while the voters receive only the "mere appearance" of what they want.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Rhetoric of Contempt

Analyze Mencken’s use of animal metaphors and "dialect" to establish a sense of class contempt. How does his linguistic strategy (e.g., shifting from "clinical detachment" to "savage" prose) serve to isolate the "educated skeptic" from the general public? Use specific examples like the sheep/donkey/goat hierarchy and the "jackal/jackass" conclusion.

2. The Illusion of Choice and the "Science of Politics"

Explore Mencken’s argument that democracy is a "performance" or a "swindle." How does he use the imagery of "sweet, soft music" and "the choir" to contrast with the reality of the "simple and sordid" transaction? Discuss the three-way relationship between the voters, the politicians, and the "enterprising minorities."

3. The Power of the Catalogue

Examine the role of the "Catalogue as Rhetorical Weapon." Why does Mencken choose to list increasingly absurd legal possibilities (such as the President shaving his head) rather than focusing on serious political issues? Discuss how the use of enumeratio functions to make the concept of total sovereignty appear both "comic and terrifying."
