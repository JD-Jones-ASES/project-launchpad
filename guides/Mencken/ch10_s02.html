Study Guide: The Eternal Mob (Notes on Democracy)

This study guide examines the themes and arguments presented in H.L. Mencken’s critique of the common man, or "the mob," within democratic society. The text focuses on the psychological, historical, and religious characteristics that Mencken attributes to the masses, particularly their resistance to progress and their preference for irrationality.

Key Concepts and Themes

The Stagnation of the Mob

Mencken argues that the mob is "eternal" and largely immune to the forces of human progress. While society may advance technologically or intellectually, the individual at the "nether levels" remains unchanged. According to the text, the mob has moved only slightly in two thousand years: shifting from the violence of the Roman arena to lynching parties, and from the "obscenities of the Saturnalia" to the "obscenities of the Methodist revival."

The Pathological Choice of Ideas

A central theme is the mob’s inability to grasp truth or beauty. Mencken posits that the common man suffers from a "pathological compulsion" to choose unsound ideas over sound ones. This instinct leads the mob to support tyrants and "butchers" like Nero and Torquemada while opposing those who seek to liberate the human spirit, such as Galileo and Savonarola.

Brute Labor vs. Durable Values

The text identifies the mob’s only contribution to the world as "brute labor." However, it claims that even this labor is given begrudgingly. Mencken asserts that all "durable values"—the significant achievements of civilization—have been created not because of the mob, but in spite of its opposition.

The Theological Divide: Christ vs. Paul

Mencken draws a sharp distinction between the "simple and magnificent" teachings of Christ and the "bogus Christianity" embraced by the masses.

  • The System of Christ: Described as logical, plausible, and ethically austere, focusing on the "duties of a gentleman." Mencken argues that the mob turned against Christ because his ideas were too rigorous for them to handle.
  • The System of Paul: Attributed to the Apostle Paul, described as a "travelling labour leader" and the "Stammvater" (forefather) of modern mob-masters. Paul is credited with inventing a "discordant and nonsensical" code of doctrines that appealed to the mob’s love for the unintelligible and "comfortable balderdash."

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition in Context
Balderdash Senseless talk or writing; nonsense offered to the mob to soothe them.
Cagliostro / Barnum Figures representing "mountebanks" or showmen whom the mob treats as heroes.
Evangelical Relating to the Christian gospel; used by Mencken to describe anti-intellectual, revivalist Christianity.
Hosannas Shouts of praise; used to describe the mob's enthusiastic support for tyrants.
Improvident Lacking foresight; failing to plan for the future. Mencken uses this to characterize the mob's nature.
Little Bethel A reference to small, often narrow-minded or austere nonconformist chapels; used to contrast Peter with Paul.
Mountebank A person who deceives others; a charlatan. Mencken argues the mob’s beliefs are the inventions of such people.
Stammvater The founding father or ancestor of a lineage; here, Paul is the Stammvater of Christian "mob-masters."
Turgid Excessively embellished or pompous; used to describe Paul’s "witless metaphysics."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does Mencken describe the mob’s relationship with human progress?

    • Answer: He claims human progress passes the mob by; its aims are unintelligible to them and they only receive the "fruits" that fall from the tree, shared with "four-footed brothers."

  2. According to the text, what is the mob’s "pathological compulsion"?

    • Answer: When confronted with a choice between a sound idea and an unsound one, the mob almost infallibly chooses the unsound idea.

  3. Contrast the mob’s reaction to Galileo versus their reaction to Nero.

    • Answer: The mob was against Galileo by instinct (as a liberator of the spirit) but marched with "loud hosannas" behind Nero (a tyrant/butcher).

  4. What does Mencken claim is the world’s debt to the mob?

    • Answer: The world owes the mob nothing of "solid dignity or worth," not even democracy; it only receives their "brute labor."

  5. Why does Mencken refer to Paul as the "prophet of the sewers"?

    • Answer: Because he provided a debased, nonsensical version of Christianity that appealed to the lower instincts and limited intellect of the mob.

  6. What is the "fundamental difference" between the Roman Church and Pauline Christianity according to the excerpt?

    • Answer: The Roman Church, during the aristocratic feudal age, promoted Peter (who was free from "Little Bethel" taint), whereas Paul remained the figurehead for revivalists like Luther, Calvin, and Wesley.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Concept of the "Eternal" Mob: Analyze Mencken’s argument that the nature of the masses does not change over millennia. Evaluate his use of historical parallels (e.g., the arena vs. the lynching-party) to support his claim that the "change is for the worse quite as often as it is for the better."

  2. The Religion of the Nether Levels: Discuss the distinction Mencken makes between the "system of Christ" and the "system of Paul." How does Mencken use this distinction to critique modern evangelical Christianity and its relationship with the "democratic state"?

  3. Intellectualism vs. Instinct: Explore the text’s assertion that the mob is governed by instinct rather than logic. How does this "pathological compulsion" toward the irrational influence the political and social structures described in the document?

  4. The Aristocratic Perspective on Ethics: Mencken describes Christ’s ethics as the "duties of a gentleman." Examine how this phrasing, contrasted with the "turgid metaphysics" of Paul, reflects the author's underlying views on class, intellect, and moral dignity.
