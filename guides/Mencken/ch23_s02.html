Study Guide: Corruption Under Democracy

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the arguments presented in "Corruption Under Democracy," an excerpt from H.L. Mencken’s Notes on Democracy. It explores the thesis that corruption is the fundamental characteristic of democratic government, driven by the nature of the "mob" and the transition from aristocracy to plutocracy.

Key Concepts and Themes

The Inherent Corruption of Democracy

The text argues that corruption is not a peripheral defect but the defining feature of democratic rule. The "mob"—the general populace—tolerates corrupt officials because they recognize a kindred spirit in them. Democratic man is described as naturally corrupt, seeking to "beat the government" whenever possible, viewing such actions as devoid of moral turpitude.

The Myth of "Service" and Puritanism

The "Ur-Puritan" of New England is characterized not as a moral paragon but as a "virtuoso of sharp practices." This legacy continues in modern democratic society through the "cult of Service."

  • The Cult of Service: Described as a "sop to conscience" and a "bait to catch conies" (victims). It is viewed as a form of imposture that enlists advertising talents to swindle the public.
  • The Cost of Governance: Approximately one-sixth of an American’s income is taken by "rogues" in the name of government, while much of the remainder is lost to private "sharpers."

Democracy as Wholesale Bribery

Rather than a government with a fixed goal, democracy is presented as a system that maintains security through constant bargaining with the "mob's vagaries."

  • The Process of Trading: Successive groups—labor leaders, manufacturers, religious groups, and farmers—are "bought" with offices or favorable legislation.
  • The Executive as Haggler: Presidents are forced to enter office with lists of promises for private rewards from the public store. The text notes that the democratic ethic does not find making these promises immoral; it only reproaches the failure to keep them.

The Judicial System and the Bill of Rights

The text presents a cynical view of the American Department of Justice, labeling it a source of oppression and corruption.

  • Sharp Practices: The department is accused of using perjury, spies, and agents provocateurs to undo its enemies.
  • Nullification of Rights: It is argued that the government often labors to nullify the Bill of Rights, treating its favorites as immune to criminal process while exposing enemies to intense persecution.
  • Government of Men: Democracy is described as a "government of men, not of laws," where the majority’s tyranny allows for the suspension of just laws.

Capitalism and the Plutocracy

Capitalism is deemed safer under democracy than under any other system because the "mob" understands and shares the aspirations of the capitalist.

  • Diversion of Anger: When the public is exploited, the government and capitalists protect themselves by diverting the "mob’s" bile toward "internal enemies" (e.g., draft-dodgers, radicals, or "Reds").
  • The Rise of Plutocracy: As old aristocracies decline, they are replaced by a plutocracy. Business leaders (like J.P. Morgan or Rockefeller) are viewed by the public as more competent and admirable than statesmen or artists because their achievements are more easily comprehended by the "inferior man."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to the text, why does the "mob" tolerate thieves in government?

    • Answer: The mob recognizes a kindred spirit in them; democratic man is corrupt himself and assumes his leaders are of the "same kidney."

  2. What was Friedrich von Gentz’s 1809 argument against the parliamentary system?

    • Answer: He contended that the introduction of democracy would bring a reign of bribery and destroy the integrity and authority of the state.

  3. How does the text define the "cult of Service" in the United States?

    • Answer: It is described as half a "sop to conscience" and half a "bait to catch conies," representing a peak of "imposture as a fine art."

  4. What specific examples of "democratic masterpieces" are cited as "virtuous pretences" and "unmitigated swindles"?

    • Answer: Bryanism, Ku Kluxery, and "the war to end war."

  5. What happened in the German Republic in 1919 regarding a Cabinet minister?

    • Answer: For the first time since Henry the Fowler, a Cabinet minister crossed the border "between days" with his loot under his arm.

  6. Why does the text claim capitalism is more secure under democracy than under absolutism?

    • Answer: Because democratic man can understand and magnify the aims of capitalism as his own, making him friendly to the system and suspicious of those who wish to overthrow it.

  7. How was the 1926 general strike in England "wrecked" according to the text?

    • Answer: It was defeated by pitting one half of the proletariat against the other half.

  8. What is the "Puritan habit" described in the context of legal persecutions?

    • Answer: The habit of inflicting as much mental suffering as possible upon victims by defaming them in addition to legal action.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Ethics of Democratic Bribery: Analyze the assertion that democracy is "itself a form of wholesale bribery." How does the text differentiate between the "democratic ethic" regarding political promises and traditional morality?
  2. The Psychology of Envy: The text suggests that public indignation against corruption is rarely rooted in virtue, but rather in envy. Explore this argument using the examples of the Wilson administration "Treasury raids" and the American soldiers' reaction to war profiteers.
  3. The Transition from Aristocracy to Plutocracy: Discuss the text's claim that a "rich manufacturer or banker" is placed higher in democratic society than a soldier or a man of learning. What does this suggest about the "public opinion" of the "inferior man"?
  4. The Role of Institutional Oppression: Examine the critique of the Department of Justice and the use of agents provocateurs. How does the text support the claim that democratic governments are "governments of men, not of laws"?

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Agents Provocateurs Government agents who incite suspected persons to commit illegal acts so they can be prosecuted.
Anadyomene A reference to rising from the sea (often associated with Venus/Aphrodite); used to describe the emergence of capitalists in Germany.
Bryanism A reference to the political movement of William Jennings Bryan, characterized by the text as demagoguery and "religious anti-intellectualism."
Conies A term for victims of a swindle or "dupes."
Fidei Defensor "Defender of the Faith"; used to describe a government’s efforts to silence critics to protect its own perceived infallibility.
Homo boobiens A derogatory term used in the text to describe the "average" or "inferior" democratic man.
Katzenjammer A hangover or state of confusion and distress; used to describe the post-war disillusionment of conscripts.
Peculations The embezzlement or theft of public funds.
Plutocracy Government by the wealthy; the class that the text argues inherits power as aristocracies decline.
Shibboleths A custom, principle, or belief distinguishing a particular class or group of people, often regarded as outmoded or empty.
Tergiversations Evasion of straightforward action or clear-cut statement; equivocation.
Turpitude Depravity or wickedness; the text argues "beating the government" is seen as lacking this by democratic men.
Ur-Puritan The original, "carnal" New England Puritan who was a "virtuoso of sharp practices."
Usurpation The wrongful or illegal seizure of power or authority.
