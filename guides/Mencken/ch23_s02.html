Study Guide: Corruption Under Democracy

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the arguments presented in the analysis of democratic corruption. It explores the premise that corruption is not an accidental byproduct of democratic governance but its fundamental characteristic, driven by the psychological nature of the "mob" and the structural requirements of maintaining power in a majoritarian system.

Key Concepts and Core Arguments

1. The Essential Nature of Democratic Corruption

The text argues that corruption is the defining feature of democracy. Rather than being a defect, it is the mechanism by which the system functions.

  • Kindred Spirits: The "mob" (the democratic populace) tolerates corrupt officials because they recognize a shared nature. The common man is viewed as inherently corruptible, seeking whatever he can safely obtain regardless of the law.
  • Wholesale Bribery: Democracy is described as a system of "wholesale bribery." Because the government has no fixed goal other than maintaining power, it must constantly bargain with the "vagaries" of the mob and various prehensile minorities (e.g., labor leaders, farmers, religious groups).
  • The Cult of Service: Professional groups and politicians often use the rhetoric of "Service" as a "sop to conscience" or a "bait to catch conies" (victims). This "imposture" is viewed as a fine art in the United States, particularly within advertising and political campaigning.

2. The Psychology of the "Mob"

The democratic citizen’s relationship with the state is characterized by mutual exploitation and deep-seated psychological drivers.

  • Moral Turpitude and the State: The common man views the government as an external group of exploiters with antagonistic interests. Consequently, "beating the government" is not seen as an act of moral depravity but as a natural counter-squeeze.
  • Envy vs. Virtue: Public indignation toward large-scale corruption is rarely rooted in virtue. Instead, it is rooted in envy. The masses ignore or condone massive thefts (such as those during the Wilson or Harding administrations) but feel anger toward those whose smaller-scale "steals" are within their own reach and imagination.
  • The "Homo Boobiens" Perspective: The democratic man assumes his leaders are "knaves and mountebanks." If a leader shows genuine rectitude or refuses a bribe, the populace views them as either a liar or an idiot rather than a hero.

3. Democracy, Capitalism, and Plutocracy

The text suggests that capitalism finds its greatest security within democratic structures rather than absolute ones.

  • Shared Aspirations: The aims of capitalism are simply the magnified aspirations of the common man, making him a natural ally to the system.
  • Strategic Division: Capitalists maintain control by pitting different segments of the proletariat against each other. For example, after World War I, the grievances of returning soldiers were diverted away from war profiteers and toward "Reds" and draft-dodgers.
  • The Rise of Plutocracy: As old aristocracies decline, they are replaced by a plutocracy. In a democracy, the successful banker or manufacturer is held in higher esteem than the scholar or artist because their achievements are more easily understood by the "inferior man."

4. The Role of Government Institutions

The text highlights specific ways the democratic state maintains power through extra-legal and oppressive means.

  • The Department of Justice: Characterized as a source of "oppression and corruption," this institution is accused of using perjury, spies, and agents provocateurs to nullify the Bill of Rights and silence critics.
  • Successive Usurpations: Democratic rulers, limited by terms, feel no "sense of power" in merely executing laws. True power is found in evading or augmenting laws. Consequently, the history of democratic government is a history of the wrongful seizure of authority (usurpation).
  • Manufactured Public Opinion: Public opinion is described as the "mob's fears" which are "piped to central factories," flavored, colored, and canned by those in power.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to the text, why does the "democratic man" feel no moral turpitude when attempting to "squeeze" the government?
  2. How does the author define the "Cult of Service" in the context of American private enterprise and politics?
  3. What was Friedrich von Gentz’s 1809 argument regarding the introduction of democracy on the European continent?
  4. How does the text explain the "philosophic acceptance" of the massive corruption during the Grant and Wilson administrations?
  5. In what way does the democratic state act as a "fidei defensor" (defender of the faith)?
  6. Why does capitalism have a "further advantage" in a democracy when its interests are attacked?
  7. What is the "Puritan habit" described by Brooks Adams that democratic governments tend to adopt when dealing with their enemies?
  8. According to the text, why did the American Legionaries hunt "Reds" after World War I?
  9. Why is a successful financial manipulator like J. Pierpont Morgan ranked higher in democratic public opinion than a poet or soldier?
  10. How does the text characterize the difference between the "sense of power" in executing laws versus evading them?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Evolution of Hegemony: Analyze the transition from aristocracy to plutocracy as described in the text. How does the "comprehensibility" of wealth dictate the social hierarchy of a democratic society compared to an aristocratic one?
  2. Corruption as a Structural Necessity: Critique the argument that democracy is "itself a form of wholesale bribery." To what extent does the text suggest that the "popular will" forces leaders to become "shady men"?
  3. The Psychology of the Proletariat: Explore the role of envy and shared nature in Mencken’s critique of the "mob." How does the author use the example of the returning WWI soldiers to illustrate the internal divisions that protect the ruling class?
  4. Institutionalized Oppression: Discuss the portrayal of the Department of Justice and the concept of "government of men, not of laws." How does the text argue that constitutional checks are rendered ineffective by the very nature of democratic power?

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Agents Provocateurs Government agents who incite suspected persons to commit illegal acts so they can be prosecuted.
Bryanism Political movements or ideologies associated with William Jennings Bryan, characterized in the text as democratic demagoguery.
Conies A term for victims or "marks" who are easily swindled or tricked.
Fidei Defensor "Defender of the Faith"; used here to describe the government's aggressive defense of its own infallibility.
Homo Boobiens A satirical term used to describe the "inferior" or "stupid" democratic man.
Imposture The act of practicing deception under a false character or name; described as a "fine art" in American democracy.
Katzenjammer A hangover or state of confusion and distress; used to describe the post-war disillusionment of soldiers.
Plutocracy Government by the wealthy; the class that the text argues inherits power as aristocracies decline.
Prehensile Minorities Small, grasping groups within the populace that demand specific bribes or legislation from the government.
Tergiversations Evasions or the act of being deliberately ambiguous; used to describe the United States' foreign relations.
Turpitude Depravity or wickedness; the text argues "beating the government" is seen as lacking this quality.
Ur-Puritan The original or primitive version of the New England Puritan, described as carnal and given to "sharp practices."
Usurpation The wrongful or illegal seizure of power or authority, which the text claims is the history of democratic government.
