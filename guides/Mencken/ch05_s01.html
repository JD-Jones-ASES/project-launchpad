Study Guide: H.L. Mencken’s Analysis of Politics Under Democracy

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the rhetorical analysis of H.L. Mencken’s Politics Under Democracy. It explores Mencken's portrayal of the democratic system, his unique linguistic techniques, and his cynical perspective on the relationship between leadership and the "inferior man."

Core Concepts and Rhetorical Themes

1. The Nature of Democratic Politics: "The Bugaboo"

Mencken defines politics under a democracy not as a rational administrative process, but as the "discovery, chase and scotching of bugaboos." He argues that the democratic system is sustained by manufacturing existential threats to manipulate a fearful and credulous public.

  • The Witch-Hunt Metaphor: Mencken casts the statesman as a "witch-hunter" or a "glorified smeller and snooper." By using nursery-rhyme allusions like "Fe, Fi, Fo, Fum!", he reduces high-level governance to a childish game of monster-hunting.
  • The Catalogue of Fears: Mencken utilizes a long list of historical "terrors"—from the British "red-coats" and "Wall Street" to "German spies" and "Bolshevism"—to demonstrate a repeating pattern of manufactured panic in American history.

2. Register Mixing

A signature element of Mencken’s style is register mixing, which is the deliberate juxtaposition of different levels of linguistic formality.

  • High Register: Uses Latinate vocabulary, academic terminology, and complex syntax (e.g., "professors of mob psychology," "ignominiously").
  • Low Register: Uses colloquialisms, slang, and "street-corner" language (e.g., "mob," "bugaboo," "barnyard economics").
  • Rhetorical Effect: This technique creates irony and a deflating effect. By placing formal words next to crude ones, Mencken argues that the "dignified surface" of politics is merely a mask for something low and contemptible.

3. Syntactic Patterns and Rhythm

Mencken employs specific sentence structures to reinforce his arguments:

  • The Cumulative Catalogue: He often uses a long, rolling sentence to pile up evidence (such as the list of American bugaboos), followed by a "short, sharp verdict." This creates a rhythmic punchline that suggests the evidence is overwhelming.
  • The Semicolon Hinge: Mencken uses parallel clauses separated by semicolons to create sharp contrasts, such as his description of William Jennings Bryan’s "cross-of-gold speech" versus his "cow State political economy."
  • The Dash for Escalation: In more complex passages, such as those regarding World War I, Mencken uses dashes to create parenthetical escalations, mimicking the way propaganda adds layers of alarm to a narrative.

4. Government by "Orgy and Orgasm"

In his analysis of the First World War, Mencken’s tone shifts from sardonic amusement to a darker clinical observation. He describes democratic governance as "government by orgy, almost by orgasm," suggesting that politics is a form of collective physical excitement and mass arousal rather than a rational endeavor. He posits that war is the "most naked" and "beautifully displayed" expression of this emotional climax.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. How does Mencken define the primary function of a statesman in a democracy?

  • Answer: The statesman is defined as a "witch-hunter" and a "glorified smeller and snooper" whose job is to discover and defeat "bugaboos" (imaginary monsters) to keep the public in a state of panic.

2. What is "register mixing," and why does Mencken use it?

  • Answer: Register mixing is the juxtaposition of formal, elevated language with informal, colloquial speech. Mencken uses it to create irony and to deflate the perceived dignity of democratic institutions, suggesting they are crude at their core.

3. What does Mencken mean by the phrase "government by orgy"?

  • Answer: He suggests that democratic politics is driven by collective emotional and physical excitement—similar to mass arousal—rather than reason or abstract thought.

4. How does Mencken use the "catalogue" (enumeratio) as a rhetorical weapon?

  • Answer: He lists numerous items (such as historical fears or "bugaboos") in rapid succession to create a sense of overwhelming evidence, making his argument about the repetitive nature of democratic panic seem irrefutable.

5. According to the text, what is the significance of Mencken’s transition from using the word "bugaboos" to "frenzies" and "terror"?

  • Answer: This shift in diction marks a tonal darkening. It moves the reader from a comic view of political absurdity to a serious indictment of how manufactured fear is used to send men to their deaths in war.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Rhetoric of Fear: Analyze Mencken’s list of American "bugaboos." How does the "sheer accumulation" of these items serve his prosecutorial brief against democracy? Discuss how he uses historical context to portray the democratic citizen as "pre-modern" and superstitious.
  2. Linguistic Contrast as Argument: Explore the concept of "register mixing" in Mencken’s description of demagogues as "professors of mob psychology." How does the collision between academic and "street" language function as a standalone argument about the nature of political science?
  3. The Evolution of Tone: Examine the transition in Mencken’s writing from the "sardonic amusement" of the early sections to the "darker edge" of the World War I analysis. How does his use of syntax and word choice (e.g., "altruistic eagerness") reflect this shift in perspective?
  4. The Aesthetic of the Obscene: Closely read Mencken’s claim that democratic processes are "most beautifully displayed at times when they stand most naked." Discuss how his use of aesthetic language ("beautifully") to describe a "catastrophe" (war) establishes his persona as a "world-weary chronicler" and observer of the species.

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Bugaboo A nursery word for an imaginary monster; used by Mencken to describe objects of national political fear.
Catalogue (Enumeratio) A rhetorical device involving the listing of multiple items in rapid succession to create an effect of overwhelming accumulation.
Demagogue Referred to by Mencken as "professors of mob psychology"; leaders who capitalize on the fears and emotions of the public.
Ignominiously In a humiliating or disgraceful manner; a high-register word used to emphasize the weight of a defeat.
Puissant Powerful or mighty; used ironically by Mencken when attached to the "science" of mob psychology.
Register The level of formality in language, ranging from formal (academic, legal) to informal (slang, colloquial).
Register Mixing The deliberate juxtaposition of different registers within a single passage to create irony or a deflating effect.
Scotching To put an end to or decisively defeat; used to lend a faintly archaic dignity to the undignified process of "bugaboo" hunting.
Venal Susceptible to bribery; corrupt. A formal Latinate word often contrasted with Mencken’s more colloquial descriptions.
Witch-hunter Mencken’s central metaphor for a democratic statesman, implying that political threats are as imaginary as witches.
