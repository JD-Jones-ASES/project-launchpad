Study Guide: H.L. Mencken’s Critique of Democratic Moralism

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of H.L. Mencken’s analysis of "The Democrat as Moralist," specifically focusing on Section III.2 of his work, Notes on Democracy. It examines the historical context of Prohibition, Mencken’s rhetorical strategies, and the psychological framework he uses to critique the intersection of democracy and Puritanism.

Core Concepts and Arguments

The Definition of Democratic Moralism

Mencken identifies "Puritanism" as the driving force behind democratic moralism. He defines this not as a genuine pursuit of virtue, but as a "psychopathological" impulse to regulate the private behavior of others through the force of law. In Mencken’s view, democracy provides the machinery—the votes of the "mob"—to translate private resentment into public policy.

The Psychology of the Puritan

Mencken argues that the moralist’s crusade is rooted in two primary emotions:

  1. Envy: The desire to punish others for enjoying pleasures the Puritan cannot or does not experience.
  2. Sadism: The derivation of pleasure from inflicting pain, inconvenience, or disgrace on others under the guise of virtue.

The Failure of Prohibition

Mencken uses the Eighteenth Amendment and the Volstead Act as the primary evidence for his claims. While Prohibition was intended to eliminate alcohol consumption, Mencken highlights its actual consequences:

  • The explosion of organized crime.
  • The erosion of respect for the law.
  • The creation of an "army of bravos" (enforcement officers) who Mencken characterizes as professional criminals.

Diction and the "Three-Register Collision"

Mencken’s rhetorical power stems from his use of three distinct linguistic registers to strip the Puritan of dignity:

  • Theological: (e.g., altruistic, evangelical, sin) Used to expose the Puritan's pretensions.
  • Clinical: (e.g., psychopathological, sadism) Used to reduce moral motives to psychological disorders.
  • Colloquial: (e.g., buncombe, brummagem, fraud) Used to dismiss the entire movement as a swindle.

Historical Context: Prohibition

Key Term Definition and Impact
Eighteenth Amendment Ratified in 1919; banned the manufacture, sale, and transportation of intoxicating liquors.
Volstead Act (1919) The enforcement mechanism for the Eighteenth Amendment.
Prohibition Era 1920–1933; a period marked by underground alcohol consumption and the rise of organized crime.
Twenty-first Amendment Ratified in 1933; repealed the Eighteenth Amendment, ending Prohibition.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to Mencken, what is the "climax" of his argument in Notes on Democracy?
  2. What does Mencken claim are the two actual motives of the Puritan?
  3. How does Mencken describe the personnel of the Prohibition enforcement agencies?
  4. Define the term "accumulation" (congeries) as used in Mencken’s prose.
  5. What is the significance of the word "safely" in Mencken’s characterization of the Puritan?
  6. Distinguish between Mencken’s attack on "moralism" and an attack on "morality."
  7. What three linguistic registers does Mencken collide to create his rhetorical effect?
  8. What does Mencken mean when he calls Prohibition a "brummagem cure-all"?
  9. Describe the shift in Mencken's "voice" throughout the section.
  10. According to the text, how does the "mob" contribute to the rise of democratic moralism?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Architecture of Rage

Analyze the sentence: "They lust to inflict inconvenience, discomfort, and, whenever possible, disgrace upon the persons they hate..." Explain how Mencken uses specific rhetorical devices—such as climax (gradatio), parentheticals, and the verb "lust"—to transform an analytical observation into a "controlled fury."

2. The Puritan as a Clinical Case Study

Mencken shifts from viewing the Puritan as a religious figure to viewing him as a psychological "aberration." Discuss how Mencken uses clinical language (e.g., psychopathological, sadism) to strip the moralist of spiritual authority. Does this reduction make his argument more or less persuasive?

3. The Relationship Between Democracy and Persecution

Mencken argues that the unfitness of the common man leads to the "active persecution of the competent, the free, and the pleasure-loving." Explore Mencken’s premise that democracy is the essential "machinery" for this persecution. In his view, why is the "inferior man" naturally hostile to the "free spirit"?

4. Rhetorical Strategy: The Managed Persona

Discuss Mencken’s transition from a "cool diagnostician" to an "angry polemicist." How does establishing himself as a dispassionate analyst in the beginning of his text help him "earn" the outrage he expresses later?

Glossary of Key Terms

  • Accumulation (Congeries): A rhetorical figure where the writer heaps up words, phrases, or examples to create an overwhelming effect, making denial of the argument difficult.
  • Bravos: Hired thugs or assassins; Mencken uses this to describe Prohibition enforcement officers.
  • Brummagem: Cheap, showy, and counterfeit. Derived from Birmingham, England, once known for fake coins.
  • Buncombe: Insincere or foolish talk, particularly by politicians.
  • Casuistry: The use of clever but unsound reasoning, specifically regarding moral questions.
  • Comstockery: The overzealous censorship of literature and art on the grounds of perceived immorality.
  • Gradatio (Climax): A rhetorical figure in which a series of items are arranged in an order of increasing importance or severity.
  • Mann Act: A federal law (used by Mencken as an example of moralistic overreach) involving the transport of individuals across state lines for "immoral" purposes.
  • Puritanism: Defined by Mencken as the impulse to regulate the private behavior of others through law.
  • Sadism: The derivation of pleasure from inflicting pain or humiliation on others; applied by Mencken to the motives of moral crusaders.
