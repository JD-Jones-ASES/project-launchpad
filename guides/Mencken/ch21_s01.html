H.L. Mencken’s Critique of Democratic Moralism: A Study Guide

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of H.L. Mencken’s analysis of "Puritanism" and democratic moralism as presented in Notes on Democracy. It explores the rhetorical strategies, historical context, and psychological arguments Mencken uses to critique the intersection of private resentment and public law.

Key Concepts and Themes

1. The Definition of Puritanism

Mencken defines Puritanism not as a religious movement, but as the impulse to regulate the private behavior of others through the force of law. He argues that this impulse is the climax of democratic moralism, where the "inferior man’s envy" and the "mob’s hostility to the free spirit" converge to create a system of "psychopathological persecution" dressed in the language of virtue.

2. The Motives of Democratic Moralism

According to the text, the drive behind movements like Prohibition and vice crusading is not a genuine concern for the common good. Instead, it is fueled by:

  • Envy: The desire to punish others for enjoying pleasures the Puritan cannot experience.
  • Sadism: The derivation of pleasure from inflicting pain, inconvenience, or disgrace on those who are perceived as free or "having a better time in the world."
  • Cowardice: Mencken suggests the Puritan forbids what he fears or is incapable of doing himself, describing Puritan morality as being "against every human act that he is incapable of himself — safely."

3. Historical Context: Prohibition

The Eighteenth Amendment (1919) and the Volstead Act (1920) serve as Mencken's primary evidence for the failure of democratic moralism. By 1926, Mencken viewed Prohibition as a "brummagem cure-all" that had:

  • Driven alcohol consumption underground.
  • Fueled the explosion of organized crime.
  • Eroded respect for the law.
  • Empowered an "army of bravos" (enforcement officers) whom Mencken characterized as professional criminals.

4. Rhetorical Strategies

Mencken employs specific techniques to dismantle the dignity of his targets:

  • The Three-Register Collision: The simultaneous use of theological (e.g., grace, ascetic), clinical (e.g., psychopathological, sadism), and colloquial (e.g., buncombe, fraud) language to reduce moral crusading to a psychiatric disorder or a swindle.
  • Accumulation (Congeries): Piling up lists of examples—such as the Mann Act, Prohibition, and anti-evolution laws—to create an overwhelming indictment that makes denial impossible.
  • Voice Oscillation: Shifting between the "cool diagnostician" (the detached theorist) and the "angry polemicist" (the disgusted observer) to lead the reader from a calm analysis to a state of justified outrage.
  • Syntactic Rhythms: Using complex, labyrinthine sentences to mirror the "machinery of persecution," followed by short, blunt sentences (e.g., "It is a democratic invention") to deliver a forensic verdict.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. What does Mencken identify as the "actual motives" of the Puritan? Answer: To punish others for having a better time in the world and to bring others down to their own "unhappy level."

2. Explain the significance of the word "safely" in Mencken's description of the Puritan. Answer: It implies that the Puritan’s morality is rooted in cowardice rather than conviction; he only opposes acts he is incapable of performing or fears to perform himself.

3. Describe the "three-register collision" in Mencken’s diction. Answer: It is the clashing of theological language (to mock pretensions), clinical language (to pathologize motives), and colloquial language (to dismiss the movement as a fraud).

4. How does Mencken characterize the Prohibition enforcement officers? Answer: He describes them as an "army of bravos" and "professional criminals" who are sworn to do the bidding of moralists.

5. What is the rhetorical purpose of "accumulation" or "congeries"? Answer: To heap up words, phrases, and examples until the sheer volume of evidence makes the author's case seem indisputable.

6. Why does Mencken use the word "lust" when describing the actions of moralists? Answer: To accuse them of hypocrisy by suggesting that their crusade against pleasure is, in itself, a form of perverted sexualized pleasure.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Psychology of the Persecutor

Analyze Mencken’s claim that democratic moralism is a "form of genuine evil" rooted in envy and sadism. In your essay, discuss how Mencken uses clinical and theological vocabulary to strip the "Puritan" of moral authority. Is his psychological reduction of the moralist effective as a rhetorical strategy?

2. The Architecture of Rage

Examine the "Close Reading" passage regarding the sentence: "They lust to inflict inconvenience, discomfort, and, whenever possible, disgrace upon the persons they hate..." Detail how Mencken uses syntactic structures—such as the three-part climax (gradatio), parentheticals, and the final redefinition—to build a case against moralists. How does the architecture of this sentence support his "controlled fury"?

3. The Failure of Legislated Morality

Using the historical context of Prohibition provided in the text, discuss Mencken’s argument that democracy provides the "machinery" to translate private resentment into public law. How does Mencken use the specific failure of the Eighteenth Amendment to argue that democratic moralism produces tyranny rather than virtue?

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Accumulation (Congeries) A rhetorical figure involving the piling up of words, phrases, or examples to create an effect of overwhelming abundance and make a case undeniable.
Bravos Hired thugs or assassins; men of violence employed for intimidation. Used by Mencken to describe Prohibition enforcement officers.
Brummagem Cheap, showy, and counterfeit. Mencken uses this to describe Prohibition as a fraudulent "cure-all."
Buncombe Insincere or foolish talk, particularly from politicians. Derived from Buncombe County, NC.
Casuistry The use of clever but unsound reasoning, particularly regarding moral questions and legal defenses.
Comstockery The overzealous censorship of literature and art on the grounds of "morality" or "indecency."
Gradatio (Climax) A rhetorical figure where ideas are arranged in an ascending order of importance or severity.
Puritanism As defined by Mencken, the impulse to use the law to regulate the private behavior and pleasures of others.
Sadism The derivation of pleasure from inflicting pain or humiliation on others; used by Mencken to describe the psychological motive of moral crusaders.
Volstead Act The 1919 legislation that provided the enforcement mechanism for Prohibition in the United States.
