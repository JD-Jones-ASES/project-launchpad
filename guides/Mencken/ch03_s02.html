Study Guide: Varieties of Homo Sapiens (Notes on Democracy)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the analysis of human intelligence and its implications for democratic governance as presented in the text "Varieties of Homo Sapiens." It explores the biological basis of the mind, the validity of intelligence testing, and the critique of democracy as a theological rather than a rational construct.

Key Concepts and Core Themes

The Biological Basis of Intelligence

The text rejects the notion that the human mind is governed by "congenital intuitions" or "inherent benevolences." Instead, it posits that the mind is a function of physical and chemical phenomena. Mental development is subject to the same natural laws that govern physical organs, such as the lungs or kidneys. Consequently, individuals are born with varying levels of "equipment," leading to a natural hierarchy of intellectual capacity.

The Intellectual Divide: Superior vs. Inferior

A central theme is the inherent inequality among men. The text categorizes humanity into two broad groups based on their capacity for ratiocination (reasoning):

  • Superior Minds: Possess an extraordinary capacity for development and function. With proper training, these individuals can acquire vast knowledge and reach the highest levels of human skill.
  • Inferior Minds: Characterized by sharp limitations. These individuals reach a "mental age" of ten or twelve and then cease to develop further, regardless of the training provided. While they mature physically and "sprout beards," they remain mentally on the level of schoolboys.

The Validity of Intelligence Testing

While initially skeptical of "pedagogues," the author argues that intelligence tests (specifically those by Simon and Binet) have been proven effective through empirical corroboration. These tests serve two critical functions:

  1. Scientific Demonstration: They provide a means to prove the natural differences in intelligence between individuals.
  2. Rational Measurement: They offer a scale to define intelligence with precision, establishing the concept of the "unteachable"—those whose progress is arrested before reaching the natural limits of the species.

Democracy as Theology

The text argues that democracy is not a rational political system but a form of "theology" or "metaphysics." It identifies several "stigmata" of this belief system:

  • Reliance on Phantasms: Democrats depend on the unproven theory that the "inferior man" possesses inherent wisdom and virtue.
  • Rejection of Facts: When confronted with uncomfortable scientific evidence regarding intelligence, democrats appeal to "highest sentiments" and the "dignity of man."
  • Moral Judgment: Critics of democracy are not viewed as merely being in error, but as "wicked."
  • Fundamentalist Roots: Democratic belief is likened to "Fundamentalism," relying on "gratuitous, transcendental assumptions" similar to those found in the Book of Genesis.

The Nature of Homo Stultus

The text introduces the term Homo stultus to describe man on the lower levels of intelligence. This category of human is defined by:

  • Susceptibility to Delusion: While they struggle to grasp truth, they absorb "palpable nonsense" and "impostures" with little resistance.
  • Victimization: Historically, they are the perpetual victims of "quacks," including priests and politicians.
  • Hatred of Progress: They instinctively hate those who work for the progress of the race, viewing honest intellectual labor as "unsound, immoral and of the devil."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to the text, what are the primary drivers of the development and operation of the human mind?

The mind is primarily a function of physical and chemical phenomena, governed by the same natural laws that regulate the human lungs or kidneys.

2. How does the author characterize the "inferior man"?

The inferior man is the average citizen whose intellectual limitations make self-government impossible. He is described as having no more inherent wisdom or virtue than the superstitious notion that Friday is an unlucky day.

3. Why do "Liberals" and democrats oppose intelligence tests?

They recognize that these tests offer a scientific means of demonstrating the natural inequality of intelligence, which poses a direct threat to the democratic theory that all men possess the same capacity for wisdom.

4. What is the author's definition of an "intelligent man" versus a "stupid man"?

An intelligent man is capable of taking in knowledge until he reaches the natural limits of the species. A stupid man is "unteachable"; his mental progress is arrested at a specific point (often a mental age of ten or twelve) even as his body continues to age.

5. In what way does democracy resemble "Fundamentalism" according to the text?

Democracy relies on "sentiments of the human heart" and "gratuitous, transcendental assumptions" rather than evidence. It treats political theory as a form of theology and views dissenters as morally wicked.

6. What is the historical relationship between the "inferior man" and those who labor for human progress?

The inferior man has historically hated those laboring for the progress of the race because their teachings are beyond his grasp, leading him to believe such progress is immoral or "of the devil."

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Biological Argument Against Democracy: Analyze the author’s claim that human inequality is a matter of "natural law." How does the comparison between the mind and physical organs (lungs, kidneys, noses) serve to undermine the "theory" of democratic equality?
  2. The Concept of the "Unteachable": Discuss the implications of the "unteachable" mind in the context of political participation. If, as the text suggests, a significant portion of the population remains at a mental age of ten or twelve, what does this imply for the "raw material" of a democratic government?
  3. The Psychology of Delusion: Explore the text’s description of Homo stultus and their "dreadful capacity for embracing and cherishing impostures." Why does the author believe that truth "daunts" the lower-level man while delusions find "lodgment in his cranium with so little resistance"?

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition from Context
Homo sapiens Latin for "wise man"; the species name for humans, used ironically by the author to highlight the lack of wisdom in the average man.
Homo stultus A term used by Charles Richet (cited by Mencken) to describe the "stupid man" who thinks in terms of palpable nonsense.
Inferior Man Mencken’s term for the average citizen whose intellectual limitations make them unfit for self-government.
Pedagogue A teacher, typically used contemptuously to describe those who are pedantic or strict.
Ratiocination The process of exact thinking or reasoning.
The Unteachable Individuals whose mental development stops in childhood or infancy, regardless of physical maturation or training.
Phantasms Illusory ideas or "rubbish" with no basis in fact; used to describe the democratic belief in the wisdom of the common man.
Little Bethel A reference to a type of narrow-minded, provincial religious chapel; used to describe where one might hear arguments based on faith rather than reason.
Simon and Binet The creators of early intelligence tests, which the author cites as providing a scientific basis for measuring natural intelligence.
Fundamentalist One who adheres strictly to ancient, often literal interpretations of scripture; here, used to describe democrats who stick to their theories despite contradictory evidence.
