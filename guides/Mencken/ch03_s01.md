# Study Guide: Analysis of Mencken’s "Varieties of Homo Sapiens"

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the rhetorical analysis of Chapter 3, Section 1 of H.L. Mencken’s *Notes on Democracy*. It explores Mencken’s shift in persona, his use of pseudo-scientific language, and his underlying philosophical influences.

---

## Key Concepts and Rhetorical Analysis

### The Central Argument
In "Varieties of Homo Sapiens," Mencken transitions from attacking the theory of democracy to attacking the "raw material" of the system: the people. His premise is that human intelligence varies as obviously as physical height, leading to the conclusion that the vast majority of human beings are intellectually incapable of self-governance.

### Rhetorical Context and Strategy
Mencken employs a deliberate strategy to frame his prejudices as objective data:
*   **The Mock-Scientist Persona:** He adopts the tone of a naturalist or laboratory observer. By categorizing humans as "specimens," he strips them of their status as citizens with rights and treats them as objects of biological study.
*   **Targeting the "Democrat":** Mencken defines a "democrat" not by political affiliation, but as anyone who maintains a sentimental faith in the fundamental competence and equality of the common man.
*   **Scientific Veneer:** By using the language and methods of natural science, Mencken implies his conclusions are "findings" rather than mere opinions.

### Voice and Tone
The text identifies a dual nature in Mencken's voice:
1.  **Clinical Detachment:** A calm, faintly amused, and objective tone that mimics a scientific report.
2.  **Calculated Contempt:** Periodic "eruptions of contempt" that the text compares to "insensate sweating, like that of a kidney." These breaks in the clinical mask are intended to make his scorn seem like the inevitable result of objective observation.

### Philosophical Influence: Friedrich Nietzsche
Mencken was a primary popularizer of Nietzsche in America. His division of humanity into "superior" and "inferior" types is a direct echo of Nietzsche’s "master" and "slave" moralities. Mencken views democratic egalitarianism as a manifestation of the "resentment of the weak" disguised as moral principle.

## Linguistic and Stylistic Features

### Diction and Vocabulary
Mencken utilizes "register mixing" by yoking high-level scientific Latin with aggressive editorial judgment.

| Term | Context/Meaning in Text |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Homo stultus** | Latin for "foolish man"; a term Mencken attributes to Charles Richet to give contempt a scientific veneer. |
| **Ratiocination** | The process of logical, methodical reasoning; Mencken reserves this term for the "superior" mind. |
| **Pedagogue** | A teacher; used contemptuously to represent the "delusion" that everyone is educable. |
| **Transcendental** | Used mockingly to describe democratic faith as a form of theology or superstition. |
| **Insensate** | Lacking feeling or consciousness; used to reduce human thought to a mindless biological process. |

### Syntax and Rhythm
*   **Cumulative Style:** Mencken uses long, cataloguing sentences filled with subordinate clauses to mimic the gathering of scientific data.
*   **Parallelism and Antithesis:** He uses balanced structures (e.g., "Of one... Of another...") to create an illusion of judicial fairness, even when the underlying argument is asymmetrical.
*   **The "Punch" Sentence:** A signature move involving a long, elaborate build-up followed by a devastatingly short sentence (e.g., "In other words, they actually work") to deliver a final verdict.

### Figures of Speech
*   **Extended Metaphor:** The dominant figure is the treatment of humanity as a "zoological collection." This includes Linnaean naming conventions and the classification of the electorate as specimens to be sorted.
*   **The Kidney Simile:** Mencken compares the mental activity of the "inferior" mind to the "insensate sweating" of a kidney. This reduces thought to a mindless bodily secretion/excretion, removing consciousness and dignity from the subject.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **How does Mencken’s persona change in Chapter 3 compared to Chapter 2?**
    *   *Answer:* He shifts from a "mock-historian" to a "mock-scientist" or naturalist.
2.  **What is Mencken’s "simple and brutal" argument regarding human intelligence?**
    *   *Answer:* That humans differ in intelligence as obviously as they do in height, and most are too stupid to govern themselves.
3.  **According to the text, who are the "secondary targets" of Mencken’s critique in this section?**
    *   *Answer:* Critics of the intelligence testing movement, whom he identifies as Liberals clinging to a sentimental faith in equality.
4.  **What is the purpose of Mencken’s use of "pseudo-scientific vocabulary"?**
    *   *Answer:* To lend his prejudices the weight of taxonomy and the appearance of objective, biological fact.
5.  **How does the "Kidney Simile" function as a rhetorical weapon?**
    *   *Answer:* It reduces human thought to a mindless, biological excretion (processing waste), implying the "inferior" man has no true consciousness.
6.  **Who is the French physiologist Mencken cites for the term *Homo stultus*?**
    *   *Answer:* Charles Richet.

## Glossary of Key Terms

*   **Antithesis:** A rhetorical device where two opposite ideas are put together in a sentence to achieve a contrasting effect (e.g., comparing "superior equipment" to "insensate sweating").
*   **Extended Metaphor:** A comparison between two unlike things that continues throughout a series of sentences in a paragraph or even an entire work.
*   **Homo stultus:** Mencken's mock-Latin classification for the "foolish man."
*   **Insensate:** Lacking physical sensation, power of mind, or feeling; used by Mencken to describe the "thinking" of the masses.
*   **Linnaean System:** The biological system of naming and classifying organisms; Mencken borrows this structure to categorize political "specimens."
*   **Mock-Scientist:** A rhetorical persona that adopts the language and detached tone of a researcher to present subjective or satirical views as objective findings.
*   **Pedagogue:** A teacher; in Mencken’s usage, a symbol of the democratic belief in universal education, which he views with contempt.
*   **Ratiocination:** Methodical, logical reasoning; considered by Mencken to be a rare ability limited to the "superior" mind.
*   **Register Mixing:** The practice of using different levels of formality or specialized language (e.g., scientific Latin vs. crude similes) within the same context to create a specific rhetorical effect.
