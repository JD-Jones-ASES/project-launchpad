Study Guide: Analysis of Mencken’s "The New Psychology" (Chapter 4, Section 1)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the rhetorical strategies, thematic concerns, and linguistic techniques used by H.L. Mencken in Chapter 4, Section 1 of his analysis. It is designed to assist in understanding how Mencken utilizes behaviorist psychology and specific literary devices to argue that human behavior is driven by fear rather than reason.

Key Concepts and Rhetorical Analysis

1. The Rhetorical Context: Behaviorism as Authority

Mencken adopts a "mock-scientific" approach by drawing on behaviourism, the dominant school of American psychology in the 1920s. Founded by John B. Watson, behaviorism rejected the study of internal mental states (introspection) in favor of observable stimulus-response patterns.

  • Selective Application: Mencken uses the authority of Dr. John B. Watson and Dr. Eleanor R. Wembridge to lend his polemic the appearance of a literature review. However, he is selective; he embraces behaviorism's focus on the fear reflex but ignores its emphasis on environmentalism (the idea that any infant can be trained into any profession).
  • Target Audience: The text is directed at an educated reader who likely shares Mencken’s suspicion that most people are incapable of independent thought.

2. Voice and Tone: The Scientist-Entertainer

Mencken’s persona oscillates between a clinical investigator and a vaudeville entertainer. This serves two purposes: keeping the reader engaged and demonstrating his claim that even educated people are governed by emotion (humor) rather than pure logic.

  • Comic Set Pieces: He punctuates his clinical tone with absurd imagery, such as an infant shaking like a "Sunday-school superintendent taken in adultery" or "yokels" reacting to a "travelling tooth-puller."
  • Tactical Hedging: Mencken occasionally qualifies his statements or "hedges" his positions—suggesting he might not follow Watson's theories entirely—only to immediately reassert his original point. This creates a facade of fair-mindedness.

3. Diction: The Collision of Registers

A hallmark of Mencken’s style is the crashing together of high-level scientific vocabulary and low-level colloquialisms.

  • Weaponized Connotation: Mencken uses the emotional associations of words to perform his argumentative work. For example, calling the common man's emotions those of "tabby-cats" suggests softness, dependence, and triviality without needing to state those qualities explicitly.
  • Metaphorical Conversion: He uses imagery like "greased poles" and "gaudy flags" to describe intellectual life. By using words like "gaudy" (showy/tasteless) and "greased poles" (a fairground game), he reduces high-minded intellectual aspiration to a cheap county-fair competition.

4. Syntax and Rhythm

Mencken uses sentence structure to mirror the physical or intellectual effort he describes.

  • Inversion: In the "greased pole" passage, he inverts word order ("Up each a human soul goes shinning") to create a sense of physical struggle.
  • The Descending List: Mencken utilizes a specific syntactic device where a list of items is treated as equivalent through parallel structure, but the items themselves descend in seriousness (e.g., "Golf... Rotary... Fundamentalism... osteopathy... Americanism"). This implies that political ideologies and religious movements are as mindless as leisure activities.

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition and Contextual Usage
Abysm An archaic or poetic form of abyss. Mencken uses it to suggest depths of the species' deep past that are beyond ordinary measurement.
Arboreal Relating to trees or living in trees. Mencken dismisses the theory that human fear responses are inherited from ancient ancestors living in treetops.
Behaviourism A psychological school founded by John B. Watson that rejects introspection in favor of observing external, stimulus-response behaviors.
Connotation The emotional association or suggestive meaning of a word beyond its literal definition. Mencken uses "weaponized connotation" to influence the reader's judgment.
Denotation The literal, dictionary definition of a word.
Phylogenic Relating to the evolutionary development of a species. Mencken uses this to argue that fear is "wired" into humans at an evolutionary level.
Shinning The act of climbing a pole by gripping it with the arms and legs. Mencken uses this colloquial term to describe the "climbing" of the human soul toward intellectual goals.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Which two psychologists does Mencken cite by name to lend his work scientific authority?
    • Answer: Dr. John B. Watson and Dr. Eleanor R. Wembridge.
  2. What is Mencken's primary argument regarding the "common man's mind" in this section?
    • Answer: He argues that the common man's mind is governed by fear rather than reason.
  3. Explain the significance of the "tabby-cats" comparison.
    • Answer: It uses connotation to characterize the common man as pampered, dependent, and incapable of independence.
  4. What is the purpose of the "descending list" (ending in "Americanism")?
    • Answer: The syntactic parallelism treats all items—from sports to political ideology—as equally mindless substitutes for genuine thinking.
  5. What does the "greased pole" represent in Mencken’s central metaphor?
    • Answer: It represents the difficulty of intellectual aspiration and the struggle for genuine thought.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Selective Use of Science: Analyze how Mencken utilizes behaviorist psychology as a rhetorical tool. How does he choose which elements of Watson’s theories to include and which to discard to support his polemical agenda?
  2. The Greased Pole Metaphor: Evaluate the effectiveness of Mencken’s "greased pole" metaphor as a commentary on social and intellectual hierarchy. How does his choice of imagery (flags, shinning, slipping) influence the reader's perception of "human souls"?
  3. Collision of Registers: Discuss the impact of Mencken’s "collision of registers." How does the mixing of clinical, scientific language with colloquial, "vaudeville" imagery serve his larger argument about the nature of human intelligence?
  4. The Role of Emotion in Persuasion: Mencken claims that most people are governed by emotion, yet he uses humor and "weaponized connotation" to persuade his own "educated" readers. To what extent does Mencken's prose prove his own point about the relationship between reason and emotion?
