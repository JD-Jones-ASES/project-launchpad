Study Guide: Analysis of H.L. Mencken’s "The Occasional Exception"

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the rhetorical strategies, historical context, and central arguments found in the analysis of Chapter 16, Section 1 of H.L. Mencken’s Notes on Democracy.

Key Concepts and Themes

The Tactical Concession

Mencken employs a specific rhetorical move known as a tactical concession. He acknowledges the "strongest objection" to his argument—that men of integrity and ability do occasionally hold high office. However, he argues that these exceptions "prove the rule" in a logical sense: they test the rule and ultimately confirm its validity.

Historical and Comparative Context

Mencken contrasts the American democratic system with three distinct European models:

  • English Oligarchy: He invokes an idealized vision of English governance (Oxford, Cambridge, Westminster) where scholarship and "breeding" temper popular opinion.
  • Feudalism: He uses the medieval hierarchy to illustrate how modern democracy has merely redirected servitude from a lord to "the mob."
  • Prussian Aristocracy: Through anecdotes of Frederick the Great and Wilhelm I, Mencken highlights a code of honor, dignity, and rectitude that he believes is absent in democratic systems.

The Rhetorical Persona

A central element of Mencken’s writing is his persona—a constructed voice or "mask" used for rhetorical effect.

  • The Persona: An aristocratic outsider who is amused, contemptuous, unbribable, and detached.
  • The Reality: A working journalist in Baltimore who was deeply engaged in the political process and maintained diverse friendships.
  • The Purpose: This gap allows Mencken to make provocative statements that a more literal or "openly positioned" writer could not.

The Vocabulary of Degradation

Mencken utilizes a sustained antithesis in his diction, pairing the language of honor with the language of shame to describe the evolution (or devolution) of the politician:

  • Honor: Integrity, rectitude, dignity, self-respect.
  • Degradation: Compromised, crawled, broken to the goose-step, "male Magdalens."

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Amour Propre: (French) Self-love or self-regard. Mencken uses this term to imply vanity, suggesting politicians defend a cheap self-image rather than true honor.
  • Charlatanry: The practice of a charlatan; pretending to possess knowledge or skill one does not have. Mencken applies this to veteran politicians who have practiced deception so long it has become second nature.
  • Exemplum: A brief narrative or anecdote drawn from history used to illustrate a general moral or principle (e.g., the Frederick the Great anecdote).
  • Male Magdalens: A metaphor applying the language of sexual shame and prostitution to political compromise. It suggests politicians have sold their "most intimate" possessions and live in a state of perpetual, yet repeated, sin.
  • Persona: The constructed character or voice through which an author speaks, distinct from the author's real-life personality.
  • Suzerain: A feudal lord to whom a vassal owes allegiance.
  • Tactical Concession: A rhetorical maneuver where an arguer acknowledges a valid point from the opposing side to strengthen their own overall case.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What is Mencken’s "essential objection" to democracy compared to feudalism?

    • Answer: Feudalism imposed degrading acts upon the powerless (the vassal), whereas democracy imposes degrading acts upon the powerful (those responsible for the welfare and dignity of the state).

  2. How does Mencken use the phrase "male Magdalens" to characterize politicians?

    • Answer: He uses it to equate political compromise with sexual shame, suggesting the politician has "sold" his integrity and exists in a state of grotesque dependence and repentance.

  3. According to the text, why did Wilhelm I of Prussia refuse the imperial crown?

    • Answer: He refused it on the grounds of honor, stating he could only take the crown from his equals, not from those he considered inferiors.

  4. What syntactic device does Mencken use to create a "rhythmic drumbeat" in his indictment of politicians?

    • Answer: He uses anaphora, specifically the repetition of "They have" at the beginning of successive clauses (e.g., "They have been broken... They have learned... They have kept silent...").

  5. Why does the text argue that Mencken’s use of the English comparison was "effective"?

    • Answer: It was effective because his American audience had no direct experience with the English system to test his idealized vision of it against reality.

  6. What does the anecdote of Frederick the Great illustrate about democratic theory?

    • Answer: It illustrates that democrats view uncorruptible men (like the Junker) as "half-wits" or "scoundrels" because they cannot be bought, revealing the corrupt values of the democratic system itself.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Architecture of Antithesis

Analyze Mencken's use of structural antithesis in the final paragraph of the section. How does the mathematical symmetry of his sentences reinforce his argument regarding the "degradation" of leadership? Discuss how his parallel construction leads the reader to a specific conclusion without stating it directly.

2. The Satirist’s Mask vs. Philosophical Resignation

Explore the tonal shifts in this section. How does Mencken transition from a "prosecutorial mode" to one of "philosophical resignation"? Use the example of the Wilhelm I anecdote to discuss the moments where Mencken "drops the satirist's mask" and identify what values he is actually defending in those moments.

3. The Function of the Historical Exemplum

Examine how Mencken uses historical figures (Frederick the Great, Wilhelm I, and references to Tocqueville) to anchor his critique of 1920s American democracy. Does his reliance on an "idealized" past strengthen or weaken his critique of the present? Support your answer with evidence from the text’s discussion of his rhetorical effectiveness.

4. Persona as a Rhetorical Instrument

Discuss the relationship between "Mencken the man" and "Mencken the persona." How does his identity as an "aristocratic outsider" serve his rhetorical goals, and why might it be necessary for him to maintain this detachment while being an "engaged journalist" in reality?
