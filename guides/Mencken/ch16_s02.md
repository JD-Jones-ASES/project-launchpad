# Study Guide: The Occasional Exception in Democratic Politics

This study guide examines the analysis of democratic leadership and the challenges of integrity within a democratic framework, as presented in the text "The Occasional Exception" from *Notes on Democracy*.

---

## Key Concepts and Analysis

### The Rarity of Integrity (The "Failure of the Rule")
The text argues that while men of unquestionable integrity and ability occasionally hold high office, their presence is not a feature of democracy but rather a "failure of the rule." The democratic system is structurally biased against the self-respecting candidate. To succeed, an individual must either possess an intellectual cynicism that views the deception of the populace as an "intellectual exercise" or resign themselves to "false pretences" as a necessary cost of doing business.

### The "Male Magdalen" and the Goose-Step
The author characterizes members of the national House of Representatives as "male Magdalens"—individuals who have compromised their honor for professional survival. These politicians are described as being "broken to the goose-step," forced to follow the whims of "job-mongers" and "blackmailers" while remaining silent on good causes and advocating for evil ones. 

### Government as a Conspiracy
A central argument is that all government, regardless of form, is essentially a conspiracy of a small group against the "masses of diligent and useful men." The primary objective of this group is to maintain positions that are more comfortable than those they could obtain through free competition.

### Comparison: The Courtier vs. The Democratic Politician
The text draws a sharp distinction between the degradations of monarchy and those of democracy:
*   **The Courtier:** While a courtier must perform "genuflections," he does so before a sovereign who is theoretically his superior or equal. He is not required to pretend to be worse than he is and can often maintain his honor because his sovereign may also be a man of honor.
*   **The Democratic Politician:** The politician must abase himself before the "mob," whom the author describes in disparaging terms. This process is viewed as more humiliating because the politician must pretend to respect the wisdom of a group the author deems "ignorant" and "stupid."

### The Myth of Mob Wisdom
The doctrine that the "mob" is full of rectitude and always makes the right decision in the long run is dismissed as an invention of "mob-masters" to save their own faces. The text asserts that genuine statesmanship often proceeds on the assumption that the mob cannot be relied upon and that seeking its support is inherently degrading.

### The Constitutional Intent of the Founders
The author notes that the "Fathers" of the U.S. Constitution (referencing Article II, Sections 1 and 2) originally intended to protect high officers from the "degrading necessity" of seeking mob support by making certain offices appointive rather than elective. This is presented as a device to safeguard the honor of state officials by removing them from the direct gift of the populace.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **According to the text, why is it difficult for a man of integrity to count on winning a democratic election?**
    *   *Answer:* The odds are heavily against him from the start, and any effort to diminish those odds requires a compromise with "complete candour."

2.  **How does the author describe the difference between an "old Congressman" of influence and an "occasional maverick"?**
    *   *Answer:* The maverick is thrown in "by miracle" and usually lasts only one session. The veteran congressman is either so stupid that he genuinely shares the ideas of the mob or is so "far gone in charlatanry" that he is unconscious of his shame.

3.  **What was Frederick the Great’s reasoning for giving army commissions only to Junker?**
    *   *Answer:* He believed they would not lie and could not be bought.

4.  **How does the democratic politician typically attempt to save his *amour propre* (self-respect) when faced with his own degradation?**
    *   *Answer:* He denies the facts, converting his "self-seeking into public spirit" and his "swinishness into heroism," while convincing himself that the mob is full of wisdom and rectitude.

5.  **What is the "essential objection" to democracy compared to the "essential objection" to feudalism?**
    *   *Answer:* The objection to feudalism was that it imposed degrading acts upon the vassal (to a suzerain); the objection to democracy is that it imposes degrading acts upon the men responsible for the state (to their constituents).

6.  **Why does the author suggest that appointive offices are superior to elective ones?**
    *   *Answer:* Seeking an office from a President or Governor—even a flawed one—is considered less humiliating than seeking it from the "mob," as a singular executive can at least understand "plain English" and does not require the "arts of the circus clown."

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Price of Power:** Analyze the author’s claim that "the higher they rise, the further they fall." Discuss how the text defines the relationship between political advancement and personal integrity in a democratic system.
2.  **The Nature of Political Abasement:** Compare and contrast the author’s descriptions of the "courtier" and the "democratic politician." In what ways does the author argue that democracy is more damaging to a leader’s honor than a monarchy or despotism?
3.  **Sovereignty and Honor:** The text cites Wilhelm I’s refusal of the imperial crown from a parliament and Frederick the Great’s reliance on the Junker as examples of honor-based leadership. Critique the author’s argument that "any attitude based upon a concept of honour... seems contemptible and offensive" to a democrat.
4.  **Institutional Safeguards against the "Mob":** Explore the author’s interpretation of the U.S. Constitution as a tool designed to shield state officials from the influence of the populace. Does the text suggest that democracy is most successful when it is "leavened" by men who do not believe in it?

## Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition per Source Context |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Amour propre** | Self-esteem or self-respect; specifically how a politician justifies his dishonorable actions to himself. |
| **Bosh-mongers** | Those who deal in or promote nonsense or empty talk (analogous to "flatterers" in a monarchy). |
| **Buncombe** | Insincere or foolish talk; specifically used to describe the history of anti-slavery agitation. |
| **Charlatanry** | The practice of a fraudulent claim to knowledge or skill. |
| **Cozening** | Deceiving or tricking; used to describe the politician's interaction with the populace. |
| **Genuflections** | Acts of bending the knee or showing servile respect; the "homage" a courtier pays to a king. |
| **Junker** | Members of the landed nobility in Prussia, cited by Frederick the Great as men of integrity who "will not lie." |
| **Magdalens** | A reference to reformed or "broken" individuals; used here to describe Congressmen who have compromised their honor. |
| **Maverick** | An independent-minded politician who occasionally enters office "by miracle" but rarely lasts more than one session. |
| **Shyster** | A person who acts in a disreputable or unethical way, particularly in politics or law. |
| **Suzerain** | A feudal overlord to whom a vassal owes homage. |
