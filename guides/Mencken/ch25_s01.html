Study Guide: H.L. Mencken’s "Last Words" (Chapter 25, Section 1)

This study guide provides a comprehensive analysis of the final section of H.L. Mencken’s Notes on Democracy. It explores the rhetorical strategies, tonal shifts, and linguistic choices Mencken employs to conclude his sustained polemic against democratic governance.

Key Concepts

1. The Spectator’s Confession

In a surprising departure from the previous twenty-three chapters of sustained attack, Mencken concludes his work not with a final denunciation, but with a confession: he enjoys democracy. He clarifies that this enjoyment does not stem from the system's efficacy—which he has spent the book debunking—but from its "incomparable" idiocy. This move transforms Mencken from a political prosecutor into a "connoisseur of the downfall" or a theater critic watching a "democratic circus" for entertainment.

2. Understatement After Hyperbole (Litotes)

Throughout the book, Mencken utilizes extravagant metaphors and thundering denunciations. However, in the final section, he shifts to litotes (understatement). By using quieter, more modest language and self-deprecating claims (e.g., "I report only..."), Mencken suggests that his case is so thoroughly proven that further hyperbole is unnecessary. The sudden arrival of understatement after pages of fireworks carries a devastating rhetorical force.

3. The Aesthetic Reduction

Mencken shifts his vocabulary from Latinate medical and scientific terminology (like "psychopathology") to simpler, seemingly positive words like "charming." However, calling a government "charming" is a calculated insult; it reduces a serious political system to something merely decorative or aesthetic, rather than just or effective.

4. Catechistic Rhythm

Mencken utilizes a syntactic pattern of rhetorical questions followed by blunt, declarative answers. This creates a "catechistic rhythm," mimicking a religious instruction format where the questioner leads the pupil to a predetermined conclusion. This structure allows Mencken to concede the worst charges against democracy while simultaneously dismissing them with a shrug.

5. The Final Paradox

The book ends with an unresolvable puzzle: "How can any man be a democrat who is sincerely a democrat?" This question employs the word "democrat" in two different senses, creating a loop that suggests democracy requires a level of self-deception that makes "sincere" adherence impossible.

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Contumacy A legal term referring to a stubborn refusal to obey or comply; Mencken uses it to describe intellectual independence in a democracy.
Dunderheads A blunt, Anglo-Saxon term for stupid people; used to punctuate more elaborate vocabulary.
Litotes A figure of speech in which the speaker deliberately says less than they mean, often expressing an affirmative by negating its opposite.
Maudlin Weakly and effusively sentimental; applied to the way democratic citizens follow "rogues and mountebanks."
Palpably In a way that is readily perceived or obvious; used to dismiss democratic propositions as self-evidently false.
Trimmers Those who deviate from their principles for reasons of expediency; included in Mencken’s list of democratic "exalted" figures.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What is the "unexpected solution" Mencken finds for ending his sustained polemic?

    • Answer: Instead of delivering a final denunciation, he confesses that he enjoys democracy immensely because it is "incomparably idiotic" and provides a form of entertainment.

  2. How does the word "confess" change the relationship between Mencken and his reader?

    • Answer: It creates a sense of intimacy and a "guilty pleasure," drawing the reader into a private, conspiratorial understanding that enjoying democratic folly is a slightly shameful but delightful act.

  3. According to the text, why is the word "charming" actually a "devastating" critique of democracy?

    • Answer: It reduces democracy from the realm of the serious (justice and wisdom) to the realm of the decorative, implying its appeal is purely aesthetic rather than rational.

  4. What is the significance of the list "dunderheads, cowards, trimmers, frauds, cads"?

    • Answer: The list escalates from the stupid to the morally weak to the actively dishonest. It is comprehensive enough to suggest these categories cover all of democratic humanity while remaining short enough to land with the force of a "punch."

  5. What does Mencken mean by the phrase "the joy of seeing them come down"?

    • Answer: He views the inevitable failure or fall of corrupt or stupid leaders as a form of "joy" that balances the pain of seeing them rise to power in the first place.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Evolution of the Critic: Compare the persona Mencken adopts in the early chapters (the mock-historian or diagnostician) with the persona of the "spectator" in Chapter 25. How does this shift in stance from prosecution to connoisseurship affect the overall persuasiveness of his argument?

  2. Rhetorical Mastery through Restraint: Analyze Mencken’s use of litotes and understatement in the final section. Why might a writer who is famous for hyperbole choose to end a 200-page attack with "studied casualness"? Discuss how this technique forces the reader to "supply the missing intensity."

  3. The Ethics of the Spectator: Mencken claims that the "spectacle of democratic folly" is "infinitely exhilarating." Explore the "darker side" of Mencken's tone. Is his "good humor" actually a form of cruelty? Discuss the implications of viewing political and social collapse as a form of "amusement."

  4. The Unresolvable Conclusion: Deconstruct the final sentence of the book: "How can any man be a democrat who is sincerely a democrat?" Evaluate whether Mencken is suggesting that democracy is an inherent contradiction or if he is critiquing the intellectual honesty of its adherents.
