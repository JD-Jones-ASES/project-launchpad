Study Guide: Mencken’s "Last Words" on Democracy

This study guide provides a comprehensive analysis of the final section of H.L. Mencken’s Notes on Democracy. It explores the rhetorical shifts, linguistic choices, and philosophical paradoxes Mencken employs to conclude his sustained polemic against democratic governance.

I. Core Analytical Themes

The Rhetorical Shift: From Prosecutor to Spectator

The final section of Notes on Democracy serves a unique rhetorical purpose: resolving a long-form attack. Rather than concluding with a final denunciation, Mencken adopts a "spectator" persona. He reveals that while he finds democracy ineffective and corrupt, he enjoys it as a form of entertainment. This shift transforms the book from a political indictment into a performance of connoisseurship, where the author savors the "democratic circus" for its "incomparable idiocy."

Tonal Transition

After twenty-three chapters of aggressive attack, Mencken’s tone becomes warmer and more conversational. He moves away from the "lectern" to confide in the reader, sharing what he describes as a "guilty pleasure." However, this geniality is underpinned by a ruthless detachment; his enjoyment is likened to watching a building collapse or a cat playing with a mouse.

The Power of Understatement

Throughout the work, Mencken utilizes hyperbole—extravagant metaphors and thundering denunciations. In this final section, he reverses course, utilizing litotes (understatement). By speaking with studied casualness ("I do not know; I report only..."), he implies that his case against democracy is so thoroughly proven that further high-volume argument is unnecessary.

II. Rhetorical and Stylistic Techniques

Syntax and Rhythm

  • Catechistic Rhythm: Mencken employs a pattern of rhetorical questions followed by declarative answers (e.g., "Is rascality at the very heart of it? Well, we have borne that rascality since 1776..."). This mimics religious instruction, suggesting the answers are self-evident and predetermined.
  • The Unresolved Paradox: The book concludes with a circular question: "How can any man be a democrat who is sincerely a democrat?" This creates a loop with no exit, leaving the reader in a state of intellectual suspension.

Diction and Vocabulary

Mencken shifts from "Latinate medical and scientific terminology" (like psychopathology) to simpler, direct language (like charm and delight). This shift is deceptive:

  • "Charming": By calling democracy "charming," Mencken reduces it from a serious political system to a mere aesthetic decoration.
  • "Confess": This word implies intimacy and a shared secret between the author and the reader, suggesting that enjoying the folly of democracy is a slightly shameful act.

III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to the text, why does Mencken claim to "enjoy" democracy?

    • Answer: He enjoys it not because it is effective, but because it is "incomparably idiotic" and "incomparably amusing." He views it as a spectacle or a circus rather than a functional government.

  2. How does the word "charming" function as a critique in the opening of this section?

    • Answer: It reduces democracy from the realm of the rational, just, or effective to the realm of the decorative. It implies the system's appeal is merely aesthetic and not based on truth or wisdom.

  3. What is "litotes," and how does Mencken use it in the final section?

    • Answer: Litotes is a form of understatement that expresses an affirmative by negating its opposite. Mencken uses it to transition away from hyperbole, making his claims seem more devastating by presenting them with modest, quiet language.

  4. Describe the "catechistic rhythm" found in the final paragraph.

    • Answer: It is a sequence of questions and answers where Mencken concedes a charge against democracy (e.g., it is wasteful) and then dismisses it with a shrug, creating a rhythm of acceptance.

  5. How does Mencken's role change from the beginning of the book to the end?

    • Answer: He moves from being a "prosecutor" or "diagnostician" delivering a methodical indictment to being a "spectator" or "theater critic" watching a performance for amusement.

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Ethics of the Spectator: Mencken’s confession of "delight" in democracy’s failures is described as a form of "cruelty" and "malice." Analyze the relationship between Mencken and his reader in this final section. Does the reader, by laughing at the "mob" along with Mencken, become an accomplice in his contempt?

  2. The Paradox of Sincerity: Evaluate the final sentence of the book: "How can any man be a democrat who is sincerely a democrat?" Explore the different meanings of "democrat" in this context. Is Mencken suggesting that honesty and democratic belief are fundamentally incompatible?

  3. Rhetorical Evolution: Compare the "mock-historian" tone of the book's early chapters (as mentioned in the teacher notes) with the "mock-confessor" tone of Chapter 25. How does this shift in persona affect the persuasiveness of Mencken’s overall argument?

V. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition Contextual Usage
Maudlin Weakly and effusively sentimental. Used to describe the "herding" of citizens after "rogues and mountebanks."
Palpably Obviously; in a way that is readily perceived. Used to dismiss democratic propositions as "palpably not true."
Dunderheads Stupid people. A blunt, Anglo-Saxon word used to describe those "exalted" by democracy.
Contumacy Stubborn refusal to obey or comply (especially a legal order). Applied to the intellectual independence of a dissenting "pedagogue."
Litotes A figure of speech using understatement for emphasis, often through negation. The dominant rhetorical strategy of Mencken’s "Last Words."
Catechism A set of questions and answers used for instruction. Refers to the rhythmic syntax Mencken uses to dismiss charges against democracy.
Polemic A strong verbal or written attack on someone or something. The overarching genre of Notes on Democracy.
Trimmers People who adapt their views for the sake of policy or to stay in favor. Included in Mencken’s list of those exalted by the democratic system.
