Study Guide: The Popular Will and the Nature of Democracy

This study guide examines the analysis of popular sovereignty and the democratic process as presented in the text. It explores the relationship between the "popular will," the political class, and the historical context of voting rights.

I. Key Concepts and Core Themes

1. The Paradox of Popular Sovereignty

The text argues that while the "popular will" possesses immense power, it lacks the requisite knowledge to exercise that power effectively. Democracy is characterized as a transaction between "jackals" (predatory politicians) and "jackasses" (the easily led public).

  • Theoretical vs. Practical Democracy: Theoretical democracy—where the popular will is completely energized—is deemed impossible due to "insuperable impediments" in the common mind.
  • Democracy "In Being": Democracy exists not when the masses are constantly involved in every decision, but when the threat of their sudden, devastating intervention makes politicians "walk a bit warily."

2. The Absolute Sovereignty of the "Mob"

The text posits that the common people in modern democracies (specifically the U.S. and Western Europe) are more sovereign than ancient despots.

  • Historical Comparisons: Ancient monarchs, such as the Merovingian kings or Japanese emperors, were limited by mayors of the palace, shoguns, or the Church.
  • Modern Power: In a democracy, there are no legal bars to the public's will once it emerges into consciousness. The public has the power to "throw off their chains" and "chop off the heads of their seducers" (politicians) through devices like the jury system or by intimidating Congress.

3. The Nature of Disfranchisement and Indifference

The text challenges the idea that democracy is "incomplete" because certain groups are barred from voting.

  • Presumptive Incompetence: If a group is disfranchised, it is often because they are not "actively eager" for the ballot. Lack of interest is presented as evidence of political incompetence.
  • The Origins of Democracy: Democracy was not won by the masses but was "forced upon its beneficiaries" by small groups of visionaries from outside the benefited class.

4. The Impact of Franchise Extension

The text argues that expanding the right to vote (to women or lower classes) has historically failed to improve government.

  • The Prussian Example: After the abolition of the three-class voting system in Prussia, government honesty and efficiency allegedly declined.
  • Women’s Suffrage: The text claims women's suffrage brought none of the promised reforms, stating that most women vote "unwillingly and without clear purpose," often succumbing to the same sentimentalities as men.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. How does the text define the "lowest" and "highest" rewards for those who master the art of victimizing the public? Answer: The lowest reward is a seat in Congress or a job as a Prohibition agent ("licensed blackleg"); the highest reward is immortality.

2. According to the text, why did the three-class system of voting survive for so long in Belgium and Prussia? Answer: It survived because the masses were too "inept at politics" and "indifferent to the rights involved" to make a genuine effort to end it.

3. What specific reason is given for why many farm-wives voted against the Democratic candidate Cox in 1920? Answer: They voted against him because he was a divorcee (an "antinomian") and because the party leader, Dr. Wilson, had remarried too soon after the death of his first wife.

4. How does the text describe the relationship between "Nordic" politicians and Henry Lincoln Johnson at the 1920 Republican National Convention? Answer: Despite potential racial loathing, the politicians were "fawning" and "polite" to Johnson because he held political power and "voted them as if they had been stuffed chemises."

5. What comparison is made between a New York Caucasian avoiding jury duty and a Southern Negro avoiding the polls? Answer: The text argues their motives are identical: both value their "peace and security" (avoiding boredom/jury duty vs. avoiding violence/broken heads) more than the "boon" of the franchise.

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Threat of the Executioner: Analyze the text’s argument that the "jury system" and "mass action" are the ultimate expressions of popular sovereignty. How does the author use the example of "executioners" in the South to illustrate the power of the mob?
  2. The Illusion of Reform: Critique the author’s perspective on the extension of the franchise. Does the text provide a purely political argument, or is its critique of women’s suffrage and class-based voting rooted in biological and social essentialism? Use examples from the Prussian and 1920 U.S. election cases.
  3. Sovereignty and Responsibility: The text suggests that the right to vote is only valid if one is "willing to fight for it, even at the cost of his last drop of gore." Discuss the implications of this "democratic dogma" on the author’s view of marginalized groups and their "unfitness for citizenship."

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Antinomian One who rejects socially established morality; used in the text to describe a divorcee.
Aframerican A term used in the text to refer to Americans of African descent.
Blackleg A person who acts dishonestly; a swindler or a scab.
Capital Cases Matters of great importance or "prodigies of nature" where the popular will becomes coherent.
Divertissement A minor entertainment or recreation; used to describe the change in political conventions after suffrage.
Mountebank A person who deceives others, especially in order to trick them out of their money; a charlatan.
Prehensile Literally "capable of grasping"; used metaphorically by the author to describe the greed of political "soothsayers."
Popular Sovereignty The doctrine that the people have the power and the right to govern themselves.
Runnymede The site where King John was forced to sign the Magna Carta, used as a historical reference for limiting royal power.
Von Gottes Gnaden German for "by the grace of God"; referring to the divine right of kings.
