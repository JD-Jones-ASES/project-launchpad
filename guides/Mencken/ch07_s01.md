# Study Guide: Envy as a Philosophy — An Analysis of Mencken’s Critique of Democracy

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of H.L. Mencken’s philosophical and rhetorical critique of democracy, as presented in "Envy as a Philosophy." It explores the Nietzschean foundations of his work, his use of vivid caricature, and the specific linguistic techniques employed to argue that democracy is rooted in the psychological state of envy.

---

## Key Concepts and Central Arguments

### 1. Democracy as a Philosophy of Envy
Mencken argues that democracy is not merely a political system but a philosophy driven by envy. He posits that the "inferior man" does not actually desire justice or liberty; rather, he desires the possessions and status of those he considers his "betters" and harbors hatred toward them for possessing those things.

### 2. Nietzschean Foundations: Ressentiment
Mencken draws heavily from Friedrich Nietzsche, specifically the concept of **ressentiment**. This is a disguised form of envy felt by the powerless.
*   **The Transformation of Values:** In *ressentiment*, the envious person declares the thing they cannot have as "evil" and their own deprivation as "virtue."
*   **The Sour Grapes Metaphor:** Similar to the fox who declares unreachable grapes sour, the democratic moralist declares unattainable pleasures sinful to justify their own lack of access to them.
*   **Master vs. Slave Morality:** Nietzsche distinguished between "master morality" (courage, independence) and "slave morality" (pity, humility). Mencken views democracy and Christianity as expressions of slave morality designed to restrain the strong.

### 3. The American Context: Rural vs. Urban Envy
Mencken translates Nietzsche’s European abstractions into concrete American figures:
*   **The Iowa Farmer:** Represents raw, violent envy. He views the "sinful" pleasures of the city from his desolate farm and uses the government (via Prohibition or blue laws) to suppress those pleasures.
*   **The City Proletarian:** Represents managed and sublimated envy. This figure is kept docile through the "welfare state"—a system of social services Mencken likens to *panem et circenses* (bread and circuses).

### 4. Rhetorical Strategy: Hyperbole and Caricature
Mencken utilizes **hyperbole** (deliberate exaggeration) to reveal underlying truths. His portrait of the farmer is not intended as objective journalism but as a **caricature**—an exaggeration designed to illuminate the psychological reality of envy.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. According to Mencken, what is the "philosophical core" of democracy?**
*Answer:* Democracy is a philosophy of envy where the inferior man hates his betters and desires their possessions rather than actual liberty or self-governance.

**2. How does Mencken adapt Nietzsche’s "slave morality" for an American audience?**
*Answer:* He substitutes the European "slave" with the American "Iowa farmer" and replaces "Christian asceticism" with "Prohibition."

**3. Define "ressentiment" in the context of Mencken’s argument.**
*Answer:* It is the envy felt by those powerless to act on their desires, leading them to declare what they cannot have as evil and their own lack as a virtue.

**4. What is the significance of the "barn-door scene"?**
*Answer:* It serves as an extended metaphor for *ressentiment*. The farmer peers through a crack at a world of music and glamour he cannot access, ultimately retreating into the "virtue" of Prohibition to cope with his envy.

**5. What does the term "register collision" refer to in Mencken’s writing?**
*Answer:* It is the sudden shift between high-level, academic, or foreign language (e.g., French literary quotes) and low-level, "barnyard Anglo-Saxon" (e.g., "heaving of manure").

**6. How does Mencken describe the urban proletarian’s relationship to luxury?**
*Answer:* The city worker is described as a "hog in a comfortable sty," managed by social services. Unlike the farmer, he is close enough to luxury to hear it (the "frou-frou" of silk), but he is still not free.

**7. What syntactic device does Mencken use to describe the social services provided to the city worker?**
*Answer:* He uses the **cumulative sentence**, piling clause upon clause of benefits (dentists, oculists, free lunches) to create a comic effect through sheer enumeration.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

### 1. The Rhetoric of Caricature
Mencken’s portrait of the Iowa farmer is described as a caricature rather than journalism. Analyze how Mencken uses specific details (the "slatternly wife," "unappetizing dram," "heaving of manure") to build this caricature. Does this exaggeration illuminate a deeper truth about human psychology, or does it merely distort the reality of rural life?

### 2. Irony and the Welfare State
In the section regarding the city proletarian, Mencken uses a "mock-celebratory" tone to describe social services. Explore how his use of irony and "boosterism" diction (e.g., "obstetricians of the highest eminence," "brilliant, aseptic") serves to criticize the welfare state. How does he use the concept of *panem et circenses* to argue that the urban worker has been reduced to a passive, managed entity?

### 3. The Anatomy of a Sentence
Perform a close reading of the following sentence: *“So he gets down his unappetizing dram, feels along his glabella for the beginning headache, and resumes his melancholy heaving of manure — a Prohibitionist by conscience, doubly-riveted and immovable.”* Discuss how the shifting linguistic registers, the tricolon structure, and the final metaphor of "metalwork" support Mencken’s thesis on *ressentiment*.

## Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Antinomianism** | The rejection of moral law; used by Mencken to describe the perceived sexual liberation of urban women. |
| **Frou-frou** | A rustling sound, specifically of a woman’s silk dress; represents the proximity of luxury to the urban worker. |
| **Glabella** | The smooth area of the forehead between the eyebrows; used by Mencken to provide clinical, medical precision to a comic scene. |
| **Hyperbole** | Deliberate exaggeration for rhetorical effect, intended to reveal an underlying truth. |
| **Panem et circenses** | Latin for "bread and circuses"; the Roman formula for keeping the public docile through food and entertainment. |
| **Ressentiment** | Nietzsche’s term for envy felt by the powerless, involving a transformation of values where the unattainable is labeled "evil." |
| **Sackbuts** | Renaissance brass instruments resembling trombones; used by Mencken to create a mock-poetic atmosphere. |
| **Slatternly** | Slovenly or untidy; used as a word of contempt for the farmer's wife. |
| **Tricolon** | A rhetorical figure consisting of three parallel clauses or phrases, often building to a climax or comic turn. |
