Study Guide: Shakespeare's Sonnets 105-108 (Partial Content)

Overview

This section explores Shakespeare's Sonnets 105 through the beginning of 108, focusing on themes of constancy, poetic immortality, and the beloved's unmatched beauty. The speaker defends his consistent praise as a celebration of genuine truth, argues that all past poetry foreshadowed his beloved, and triumphantly asserts love's endurance over historical uncertainty and death.

Key Concepts

  • Constancy in Love: Sonnet 105 directly addresses and defends the speaker's unchanging praise, asserting that the beloved truly embodies "fair, kind, and true" with unwavering constancy, making repetitive praise a celebration of truth rather than idolatry.
  • Prophetic Praise: In Sonnet 106, the speaker posits that descriptions of beauty in ancient poetry were not merely reflections of past figures but unconscious prophecies or prefigurations of his beloved, who embodies the ultimate form of beauty towards which all earlier art was tending.
  • Love's Triumph Over Time and Public Doom: Sonnet 107 moves from personal fears and societal anxieties to a triumphant declaration that love has outlasted predicted catastrophes. This is often interpreted as an allusion to significant historical events, such as the peaceful succession after Queen Elizabeth I's death.
  • Poetry as an Immortal Monument: A recurring theme, particularly prominent in Sonnet 107, is the idea that poetry grants lasting fame and remembrance to the beloved, outliving physical monuments and the ravages of time and conflict.
  • The Blazon Tradition and its Critique: Sonnet 106 engages with the conventional Renaissance poetic form of the "blazon" (a detailed description of the beloved's features). Shakespeare both uses and transcends this tradition by having past blazons prophesy the current beloved, suggesting conventional praise is insufficient to capture their unique reality.
  • Political Allusion in Sonnets: Sonnet 107 stands out for its strong suggestion of topical historical events, possibly referring to the anxieties surrounding the succession of the English crown and the subsequent era of peace.

Important Terms

  • Idolatry: The worship of idols; excessive devotion to something that does not deserve it. The charge addressed in Sonnet 105, implying that repetitive praise of one person might be seen as a false religion.
  • Wondrous: Wonderful; astonishing; beyond ordinary understanding. Emphasizes genuine admiration rather than mere flattery in Sonnet 105.
  • Argument: Subject; theme. Used in a rhetorical sense, referring to the core subject matter of the poem.
  • Invention: Creative power; poetic imagination. The speaker's "invention" is "spent" on the singular theme of "fair, kind, and true" in Sonnet 105.
  • Chronicle of wasted time: The historical record of the past; "wasted time" refers to time that has passed and been used up, not squandered. Appears in Sonnet 106.
  • Wights: Persons; creatures. An archaic word for human beings used in medieval and Renaissance poetry, found in Sonnet 106.
  • Blazon: A detailed, itemized description of beauty in poetry, often enumerating features like hand, foot, lip, eye, brow. Derived from heraldry. Discussed in Sonnet 106.
  • Prefiguring: Foreshadowing; representing in advance. Used in Sonnet 106 to describe how past poets unknowingly sketched the beloved.
  • Divining eyes: Prophetic eyes; eyes with the gift of foreseeing the future. Used in Sonnet 106 to characterize the predictive vision of past poets.
  • Prophetic soul: A soul capable of foreseeing the future; the world's capacity for dread prophecy, as mentioned in Sonnet 107.
  • Lease: A limited period; a contract of limited duration. In Sonnet 107, love's "lease" was supposed to be "confined" to a short, doomed term.
  • Mortal moon: Most probably an allusion to Queen Elizabeth I, whose symbol was the moon. In Sonnet 107, her "eclipse" could refer to her death in March 1603.
  • Sad augurs: Gloomy prophets; those who predicted disaster. Refers to pessimists whose prophecies of strife were disproven in Sonnet 107.
  • Balmy time: A mild, healing, pleasant time. Suggests both physical gentleness and medicinal properties, indicating a time of recovery or peace in Sonnet 107.
  • Subscribes: Yields; submits; acknowledges defeat. In Sonnet 107, Death "subscribes" to the speaker, conceding that poetry will have the final word.
  • Character: A mark made by writing; a written letter or figure. Found in the partial Sonnet 108, referring to what the brain may capture in ink.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet 105: Constancy and the Muse

Sonnet 105 begins with the speaker defending his consistent praise of the beloved against the accusation of "idolatry." He argues that his unwavering adoration is not for an "idol" but for a living truth: the beloved truly is always "Fair, kind, and true." This three-word refrain becomes the poem's structural and thematic core, embodying the "wondrous" constancy that is the subject of his verse. The speaker's "invention" or creative power is entirely "spent" on expressing these three virtues, which he claims have never before converged in a single person. This unique embodiment of "Fair, kind, and true" elevates the beloved above all others, making the speaker's repetitive praise an honest reflection of unparalleled excellence.

The theological language of idolatry connects to Renaissance debates about whether human beauty could reflect divine beauty or lead to blasphemy, a tension also explored by Petrarch. Within Shakespeare's sequence, the theme of unchanging love resonates with Sonnet 116, and the beloved's unique "substance" echoes Sonnet 53 and Sonnet 20, which celebrate one person as embodying all beauty.

Sonnet 106: Prophecy of Beauty

In Sonnet 106, the speaker examines historical records and old poetry, specifically "chronicles" and "blazons" (detailed descriptions of beauty), from "wasted time" (the past). He observes that earlier writers, when praising "fairest wights" and "lovely knights," were in fact providing imperfect sketches or "prophecies" of his beloved. He asserts that their "antique pen" could only "prefigure" the beauty that the current beloved "master[s] now." These past poets, despite their "divining eyes," lacked the skill to truly "sing" the worth of the beloved because they had not seen the original. The sonnet concludes with a volta where the speaker, living in the present and seeing the actual beloved, admits that even he, despite his "eyes to wonder," still "lack[s] tongues to praise." This places the beloved's beauty beyond the reach of any poetry, past or present, as the unique culmination of all aesthetic history.

This sonnet directly engages with the "Blazon Tradition," a conventional form in Renaissance love poetry where features of the beloved were systematically catalogued. While many poets, including Shakespeare elsewhere (e.g., Sonnet 130), parodied this convention, here it is used seriously but with a critical distance. The past poets' blazons are honored as prophetic, yet insufficient to capture the beloved's reality. The idea of past poetry as a prophecy of the beloved mirrors the Christian typological argument where Old Testament prophets foresaw Christ, suggesting an educated Elizabethan audience would appreciate this comparison. Connections are drawn to Sonnets 53 and 21, which similarly declare the beloved's superiority to all prior artistic representations, and Sonnet 103, which also addresses the speaker's inadequacy in praise.

Sonnet 107: Triumph Over Doom

Sonnet 107 begins with a defiant dismissal of personal "fears" and the "prophetic soul / Of the wide world dreaming on things to come." The speaker proclaims that these gloomy predictions have failed to limit or destroy the "lease" of his true love, which was "supposed as forfeit to a confined doom." A significant, transformative event is indicated: "The mortal moon hath her eclipse endured," "sad augurs mock their own presage," and "uncertainties now crown themselves assured." This points to a new era of stability and "peace [that] proclaims olives of endless age." In this "balmy time" of healing and mildness, the speaker declares that his love looks fresh, and even Death "subscribes" (yields) to him. Through his "poor rime," the beloved will find their "monument" and live on, while "tyrants' crests and tombs of brass are spent."

This is one of the most historically charged sonnets in the sequence. The "mortal moon" is widely interpreted as an allusion to Queen Elizabeth I (whose symbol was the moon). Her "eclipse" would signify her death in March 1603, after which many feared political chaos. The subsequent "peace" and "olives of endless age" likely refer to the peaceful succession of James I and his peace treaties (e.g., with Spain in 1604). This sonnet thus becomes a remarkable political document, celebrating the triumph of stability over feared catastrophe and using this public relief as a metaphor for love's own endurance over mortality. The theme of poetry as an enduring monument connects to other "monument sonnets" like 55, 65, and 81, and the assertion that love is not "Time's fool" recalls Sonnet 116.

Sonnet 108 (Partial): Enduring Love's Freshness

The provided text for Sonnet 108 is incomplete, ending abruptly. However, the initial lines present a rhetorical question from the speaker: "What’s in the brain, that ink may character, / Which hath not figured to thee my true spirit? / What’s new to speak, what now to register, / That may express my love, or thy dear merit?" These lines suggest a continuation of the theme from Sonnet 105—the challenge of finding new words for an unchanging, profound love. The speaker implies that everything he could write or think has already been used to express his spirit and the beloved's merit. The word "character" here means a mark made by writing, or a letter, reinforcing the idea of written expression.

Review Questions

  1. How does Sonnet 105 defend the speaker's seemingly repetitive praise of the beloved against the charge of "idolatry"? What is the significance of the phrase "Fair, kind, and true"?
  2. In Sonnet 106, how does the speaker reinterpret ancient poetry and its descriptions of beauty? What is the paradox of the speaker's own ability to praise the beloved, as revealed in the couplet?
  3. Discuss the potential historical allusions in Sonnet 107, particularly regarding the "mortal moon" and the "balmy time." How do these political events serve as a metaphor for the endurance of the speaker's love?
  4. Compare and contrast the theme of poetic immortality in Sonnets 105, 106, and 107. How does each sonnet present poetry as a means of defying time and decay?
  5. What common threads or "connections" can be drawn between these sonnets and others in Shakespeare's sequence, as highlighted in the provided text? Discuss how these sonnets build upon or relate to broader themes found elsewhere.
