Sonnet CII — Study Guide

Overview

Sonnet CII is the speaker's defense of poetic silence. After two sonnets scolding the Muse for neglect, he now acknowledges that restraint can itself be a form of faithfulness. The poem pivots from accusation to self-justification, arguing that constant outpouring of praise cheapens love, just as common things lose their rarity.

Meaning

The opening paradox — love strengthened but less displayed — sets the tone. The speaker criticizes love that is "merchandiz'd," publicly advertised like goods for sale. He compares his early verses to Philomel (the nightingale) singing at the start of summer, and his current silence to her stopping when riper summer days arrive. The reason is not that summer is less pleasant, but that when song is everywhere, it loses its singular beauty. The couplet confirms: he sometimes holds his tongue precisely because he does not want to dull the beloved with overuse.

Literary Devices

  • Paradox: Love is stronger though seemingly weaker — the central surprise the poem unpacks.
  • Allusion and simile: The Philomel comparison draws on classical myth (Ovid's Metamorphoses) and sustains the analogy across four lines.
  • Commercial metaphor: "Merchandiz'd" love debases affection by treating it like a product advertised for profit.
  • Aphorism: "Sweets grown common lose their dear delight" functions as a proverbial observation about value and scarcity.

Themes of Constancy and Faithfulness

This sonnet redefines what constancy looks like. Faithful love does not necessarily announce itself loudly; it may express itself through measured silence that preserves the beloved's dignity and the verse's power. Quality over quantity is the poet's rule of faithfulness here.
