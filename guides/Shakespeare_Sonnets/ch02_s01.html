What Is a Sonnet? A Comprehensive Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the sonnet, a compact fourteen-line poetic form enduring in Western literature due to its balance of constraint and flexibility. It outlines the three core architectural features shared by all sonnets—fourteen lines, a fixed rhyme scheme, and iambic pentameter—and then details the two primary forms: the Petrarchan (Italian) and the Shakespearean (English) sonnet, along with the Spenserian variation. A key concept explored is the "volta," or turn, which marks a pivotal shift in a sonnet's argument or perspective.

Key Concepts

  • Sonnet Definition: A fourteen-line poem characterized by a fixed rhyme scheme and a meter built on iambic pentameter. It is a self-contained, durable short form.
  • Basic Architecture: All sonnets share three defining features:
    • Fourteen Lines: Long enough to develop an idea, short enough to grasp as a whole.
    • Fixed Rhyme Scheme: Provides an audible structure.
    • Iambic Pentameter: A ten-syllable line (five iambs) that mimics natural English speech while elevating the tone.
  • Iambic Pentameter: The fundamental rhythmic pattern of many sonnets. An iamb is an unstressed syllable followed by a stressed syllable (da-DUM). Pentameter means five such iambs per line, totaling ten syllables. Poets vary this for emphasis and naturalness.
  • Petrarchan (Italian) Sonnet:
    • Structure: Divided into an octave (eight lines) and a sestet (six lines).
    • Rhyme Scheme: Octave typically abbaabba; sestet variations include cdecde or cdccdc.
    • Function: Octave presents a problem, situation, or feeling; the sestet responds, resolves, or complicates it.
  • Shakespearean (English) Sonnet:
    • Structure: Composed of three quatrains (four lines each) followed by a concluding couplet (two lines).
    • Rhyme Scheme: abab cdcd efef gg.
    • Function: Each quatrain develops a facet of the idea; the couplet provides a decisive, often paradoxical, resolution or twist.
  • Spenserian Sonnet:
    • Structure: Similar to Shakespearean, with three quatrains and a couplet.
    • Rhyme Scheme: abab bcbc cdcd ee, featuring interlocking rhymes between quatrains for a seamless flow.
  • Volta: Italian for 'turn,' this is the structural and thematic heart of a sonnet. It marks a shift in argument, perspective, or tone, transforming the poem's direction. Its placement varies by sonnet type but is crucial for understanding the poem's meaning.

Important Terms

  • Sonnet: A fourteen-line poem, usually in iambic pentameter, with a specific rhyme scheme.
  • Iamb: The smallest rhythmic unit in poetry, consisting of an unstressed syllable followed by a stressed syllable (da-DUM).
  • Pentameter: A line of verse containing five metrical feet (in this context, five iambs).
  • Iambic Pentameter: A line of poetry composed of five iambs, totaling ten syllables, with an alternating unstressed/stressed rhythm.
  • Octave: The first eight lines of a Petrarchan sonnet, typically presenting a problem or situation.
  • Sestet: The final six lines of a Petrarchan sonnet, typically responding to or resolving the octave.
  • Quatrain: A stanza of four lines, as found in the Shakespearean and Spenserian sonnet forms.
  • Couplet: A pair of rhyming lines, especially the final two lines of a Shakespearean or Spenserian sonnet, often providing a conclusive statement or twist.
  • Volta: Italian for 'turn,' referring to the shift in thought, argument, or emotion that occurs between the octave and sestet in a Petrarchan sonnet, or often before the final couplet in a Shakespearean sonnet.
  • Petrarchan Sonnet: Also known as the Italian sonnet, structured as an octave (abbaabba) and a sestet (cdecde or cdccdc).
  • Shakespearean Sonnet: Also known as the English sonnet, structured as three quatrains (abab cdcd efef) and a concluding couplet (gg).
  • Spenserian Sonnet: A variation of the English sonnet, with an interlocking rhyme scheme (abab bcbc cdcd ee) and a final couplet.

Detailed Notes

The Enduring Form of the Sonnet

The sonnet is celebrated for its longevity and adaptability, serving as a robust framework for poets to explore complex ideas with precision. Its inherent constraints—fourteen lines, a fixed rhyme scheme, and iambic pentameter—are not arbitrary but are carefully interwoven to guide both composition and interpretation, allowing poets to think, argue, and conclude forcefully.

The Basic Architecture of a Sonnet

All sonnets, regardless of their origin or historical period, adhere to three fundamental structural principles:

  1. Fourteen Lines: This length is deemed ideal for developing a single, cohesive idea or argument without becoming overly expansive or too brief.
  2. Fixed Rhyme Scheme: The deliberate pattern of rhyming words provides an "audible architecture," helping readers to intuitively grasp the poem's structure and the relationships between its parts.
  3. Iambic Pentameter: This specific meter defines the rhythm of the lines. An iamb is a two-syllable unit where the first syllable is unstressed and the second is stressed (da-DUM). Pentameter means there are five such iambs in a line, creating a ten-syllable rhythm. This meter naturally aligns with the cadence of English speech while also giving the poem a formal, elevated quality. Poets often introduce slight variations to this pattern for emphasis or to achieve a more natural conversational flow, but the underlying "da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM" pulse remains evident. A classic example is the opening of Shakespeare's Sonnet XVIII: "Shall I com-PARE thee TO a SUM-mer's DAY?"

The Italian Form: Petrarch's Inheritance

The sonnet originated in Italy, and the Petrarchan (or Italian) sonnet reflects the linguistic characteristics of Italian.

  • Structure: It is distinctly divided into two main parts: an octave (the first eight lines) and a sestet (the final six lines).
  • Rhyme Scheme: The octave consistently rhymes abbaabba. The sestet, however, offers more flexibility in its rhyme scheme, typically following cdecde, cdccdc, or similar variants.
  • Thematic Movement: The octave usually serves to introduce a problem, present a situation, or express a profound feeling. The sestet then traditionally offers a response, resolution, or a further complication of the initial theme.
  • The Volta: The crucial pivot point in a Petrarchan sonnet is the volta (Italian for 'turn'), which typically occurs at line 9, marking the transition from the octave to the sestet. This "turn" is often signaled by conjunctions like "but," "yet," or "however," or simply by a shift in the poem's tone or perspective. It transforms the poem into a "balanced debate"—a thesis and response, a challenge and a reply. The strict abbaabba rhyme scheme of the octave, while fluid in Italian, proved challenging for English poets due to the relative scarcity of rhymes in English.

The English Form: Shakespeare's Innovation

When the sonnet arrived in England during the sixteenth century, English poets, notably William Shakespeare, adapted the form to better suit the English language.

  • Structure: The Shakespearean (or English) sonnet consists of three quatrains (four-line stanzas) followed by a concluding couplet (two-line stanza).
  • Rhyme Scheme: Its distinct rhyme scheme is abab cdcd efef gg.
  • Advantages: This structure requires only seven distinct rhyme sounds, making it far more manageable than the Petrarchan form for English poets. Beyond practicality, this structure also established a new logical rhythm: each quatrain could incrementally develop a different aspect of the central idea, building an argument through accumulation.
  • The Closing Couplet: A signature feature of the Shakespearean sonnet, the final couplet often delivers a powerful, epigrammatic, and decisive conclusion. It can crystallize the preceding ideas into a memorable statement or, daringly, introduce a sudden twist or reversal that recontextualizes everything that came before. Readers learn to anticipate this "turn" at line 13, adding to the reading experience.

The Spenserian Variation

Edmund Spenser, a contemporary of Shakespeare, introduced his own variation, famously used in his sonnet sequence Amoretti.

  • Structure: It maintains the three quatrains and a concluding couplet structure of the English sonnet.
  • Rhyme Scheme: The Spenserian sonnet employs an interlocking rhyme scheme: abab bcbc cdcd ee.
  • Effect: The shared rhyme sounds between quatrains create a sense of continuous flow, lending a more unified and seamless quality to the poem, contrasting with the more distinct block-like development of the Shakespearean form. While its demanding rhymes made it less common, in Spenser's hands, it produced verse of remarkable fluidity.

The Volta: The Heart of the Turn

The volta is arguably the most critical structural and interpretive element in any sonnet. It is the moment when the poem's argument shifts, its perspective changes, or its direction pivots.

  • Petrarchan Volta: Typically falls at line 9, marking the transition from the problem presented in the octave to the resolution or response in the sestet.
  • Shakespearean Volta: More versatile, the volta in an English sonnet can occur at line 9 (often a minor shift) or, more decisively, at line 13, just before the final couplet. Shakespeare himself was masterful at varying its placement, sometimes turning earlier, sometimes delaying the major shift until the very end, or even leaving a productive ambiguity.
  • Active Reading: Recognizing the volta transforms reading from a passive activity to an active engagement. By identifying where and how the poem "turns," readers can delve deeper into its meaning, asking critical questions: "When does it change direction? What exactly changes? Why is this change significant?" The volta is where the sonnet's deepest intellectual and emotional work often happens.

Review Questions

  1. What are the three defining features shared by all sonnets, and why are they considered "interlocking structural elements" rather than arbitrary rules?
  2. Compare and contrast the structural divisions, rhyme schemes, and typical thematic functions of the Petrarchan (Italian) and Shakespearean (English) sonnet forms.
  3. Explain the concept of "iambic pentameter" using an example. How do poets typically use variations in this meter to achieve specific effects?
  4. What is the "volta," and why is it considered the "structural heart of any sonnet"? How does its typical placement and impact differ between Petrarchan and Shakespearean sonnets?
  5. Discuss the challenges English poets faced when adapting the sonnet from Italian, and explain how both the Shakespearean and Spenserian forms addressed these challenges in their unique ways.
