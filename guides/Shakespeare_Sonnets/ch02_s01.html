Study Guide: What Is a Sonnet?

Overview

A sonnet is a fourteen-line poem written in iambic pentameter with a fixed rhyme scheme. These three features — line count, meter, and rhyme — are universal across all sonnet traditions and centuries. The form is compact enough to hold in mind at once, yet long enough to develop a genuine argument. Its durability comes from this balance between constraint and expressive freedom.

Key Structural Terms

Iambic pentameter — the metrical backbone of the sonnet. An iamb is one unstressed syllable followed by one stressed syllable (da-DUM). Pentameter means five iambs per line, producing ten syllables. Poets vary the pattern for emphasis, but the underlying pulse is always present.

Octave — the opening eight lines of a Petrarchan sonnet (rhyming ABBAABBA). It poses a problem, situation, or feeling.

Sestet — the closing six lines of a Petrarchan sonnet (variable rhyme). It responds to, resolves, or complicates the octave.

Quatrain — a four-line unit. The Shakespearean sonnet has three quatrains (ABAB CDCD EFEF) followed by a couplet.

Couplet — a two-line closing unit (GG) in the Shakespearean form. At its best, it crystallizes the argument, reverses it, or lands a paradox.

Volta — Italian for "turn." The moment when the poem pivots: the argument shifts direction, the perspective changes, or the emotional register transforms. In Petrarchan sonnets, the volta falls at line 9; in Shakespearean sonnets, at line 9, line 13, or both.

The Three Sonnet Forms

Form Structure Rhyme Scheme
Petrarchan (Italian) Octave + Sestet ABBAABBA + variable sestet
Shakespearean (English) 3 Quatrains + Couplet ABAB CDCD EFEF GG
Spenserian 3 Interlocked Quatrains + Couplet ABAB BCBC CDCD EE

Why the Volta Matters

The volta is the single most important structural feature of any sonnet. Finding it transforms passive reading into active interpretation. Ask: When does the poem change direction? What changes — the tone, the subject, the imagery, the tense? Why does the change happen here? These questions unlock the sonnet's meaning.

Review Questions

  1. What are the three universal features shared by every sonnet?
  2. How does the logical structure of the Petrarchan form differ from the Shakespearean form?
  3. Why did English poets abandon the Petrarchan octave's interlocked rhyme scheme?
  4. What are the four functions a Shakespearean closing couplet can perform?
  5. Where should a reader first look for the volta in a Shakespearean sonnet?
