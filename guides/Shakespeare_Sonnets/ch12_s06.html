Study Guide: Sonnet CXXXII — Mourning Eyes and the Merciful Heart

Overview

Sonnet CXXXII builds on the dark-beauty argument of CXXVII and CXXXI, but introduces a new dimension: the gap between the mistress's eyes and her heart. The speaker interprets her dark eyes as mourners dressed in black, grieving sympathetically for his pain — even as her heart continues to torment him with disdain. The sonnet becomes a petition: if her eyes can mourn, let her heart join them.

Key Themes

Eyes vs. Heart. The central tension of the sonnet is between outward appearance and inward intention. Her dark eyes look upon the speaker with "pretty ruth" (gentle pity), suggesting sympathy. But this sympathetic appearance is all the more painful because her heart remains unmoved. The speaker pleads for interior alignment with exterior appearance.

Dark Beauty as Celestial. Shakespeare elevates the mistress's dark eyes through two magnificent celestial similes: the morning sun on the grey eastern horizon, and the evening star lighting the sober west. These comparisons do what CXXX refused to do — they reach for cosmic analogies — but they apply them to dark, not fair, beauty.

Conditional Oath. The couplet offers a conditional: if she mourns for him as her eyes suggest, he will swear that beauty itself is black and all lacking her complexion are foul. This is the sequence's strongest statement of the dark-beauty argument, but it is bargained for rather than freely given.

Literary Devices

  • Personification: eyes as mourners, heart as separate agent of cruelty
  • Celestial simile: morning sun/grey east, evening star/sober west
  • Petitionary structure: conditional request building to a promised oath
  • Paradox: mourning as beautifying; darkness as glory

Significance

The sonnet captures a recurring Dark Lady dynamic: the speaker reads comfort into her physical appearance even as he acknowledges the cruelty beneath it. His interpretive generosity is both touching and self-deceiving.
