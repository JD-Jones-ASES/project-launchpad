Study Guide: Sonnet CXXXIX — Wound Me Directly

Overview

Sonnet CXXXIX is a striking appeal to the mistress to be cruel honestly rather than subtly. The speaker asks her not to torture him with sidelong glances at other men but to tell him plainly that she loves elsewhere. The indirect wound of the wandering eye is worse than direct speech. In a remarkable turn, he even offers a generous excuse for her behavior: her eyes are his enemies, and she aims them elsewhere to protect him. The couplet intensifies the paradox: kill me outright rather than let me linger.

Key Themes

Direct vs. Indirect Cruelty. The sonnet distinguishes between two modes of wounding: the tongue (direct, honest) and the eye (indirect, cunning). The speaker explicitly prefers the former. He can endure a plain declaration of infidelity; he cannot endure watching her eyes stray to others while he is present. Honest cruelty is somehow more tolerable than artful cruelty.

The Loving Excuse. Lines 11-14 are psychologically revealing: the speaker invents a protective interpretation for her behavior. Her eyes wander elsewhere to spare him their injurious force. This may be delusion, but it is tender, self-aware delusion — the act of a man still fully in love despite his suffering.

The Death Wish. The couplet's paradox — "kill me outright with looks, and rid my pain" — reveals the logic of prolonged torment: it may be worse than a clean end. The intensity of the wish for a killing blow shows how far the speaker is from relief.

Literary Devices

  • Antithesis: eye vs. tongue; cunning vs. might; art vs. power
  • Paradox: asking for more complete wounding rather than less
  • Generous rationalization: turning her cruelty into concern (lines 11-14)
  • Hyperbole: kill me outright — end the slow torture

Significance

CXXXIX shows the speaker's emotional intelligence and self-awareness: he knows how she tortures him, he can name the mechanism precisely, and he still finds a way to love her enough to excuse it.
