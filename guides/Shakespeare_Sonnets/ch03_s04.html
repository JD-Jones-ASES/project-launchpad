Study Guide: Sonnets 11-15 from The Case for Posterity

Overview

This section delves into five of Shakespeare's procreation sonnets (11-15), exploring the speaker's multifaceted arguments for the Fair Youth to have children. The poems move from social duty and the inevitability of time's decay to philosophical appeals about stewardship of beauty and the dire consequences of refusing to reproduce, often employing vivid metaphors and rhetorical traditions to impress upon the Youth the urgency of continuing his line.

Key Concepts

  • Social Obligation of Procreation: Sonnet XI broadens the argument from personal duty to a societal one, asserting that universal refusal to reproduce would lead to the end of humanity, and that those "best endow'd" have a greater responsibility to procreate.
  • Time's Destructive Power (Ubi Sunt Tradition): Sonnet XII powerfully illustrates the relentless march of time and decay through a catalogue of natural images. It highlights the fleeting nature of beauty and life, positioning procreation ("breed") as the sole defense against Time's inevitable destruction, echoing the ancient ubi sunt poetic tradition.
  • Stewardship of Beauty and Life: Sonnet XIII argues that the Youth doesn't own his beauty but holds it "in lease," implying a temporary guardianship. Failing to pass on this beauty through offspring is presented as a neglectful act, akin to letting a "fair house fall to decay."
  • Astrology of Procreation: Sonnet XIV employs an extended astronomical conceit. The speaker claims a unique form of "astronomy" that reads the Youth's eyes, predicting that "truth and beauty shall together thrive" only if he reproduces, otherwise facing "doom and date."
  • The Printer's Seal Metaphor: Introduced in Sonnet XI, this metaphor vividly portrays Nature as having "carv'd" the Youth as a "seal" with the explicit purpose of "print[ing] more" copies of himself, not letting his unique impression die unused.
  • Filial Gratitude: Sonnet XIII makes a direct emotional appeal by reminding the Youth, "You had a father: let your son say so," suggesting that just as he benefited from his father's procreation, he should extend the same gift to the next generation.

Important Terms

  • Store: A stock or supply; also, the means of reproduction. To be "made for store" implies an intention by nature for breeding and increasing the world's beauty. (Sonnet XI, XIV)
  • Seal: A carved device used to impress a wax seal on documents, authenticating them. Metaphorically, the Youth is a seal meant to print copies of himself. (Sonnet XI)
  • Endow'd: Given gifts by nature; equipped with qualities. Implies an endowment, a perpetual fund whose interest should be spent, not hoarded. (Sonnet XI)
  • Brave: Splendid; magnificent (older meaning). Used in Sonnet XII to describe a "brave day sunk in hideous night," setting a tone of grandeur destroyed.
  • Sable: Black; dark (from heraldry). Refers to dark hair that turns white, adding a formal weight to the image of aging. (Sonnet XII)
  • Bier: A frame or carriage for conveying a coffin. Used in Sonnet XII to describe harvested grain, making the harvest explicitly funerary.
  • Scythe: The traditional implement of the Grim Reaper, symbolizing Time's weapon. A powerful and recurrent motif. (Sonnet XII)
  • Breed: Offspring; also, the act of reproduction. A plain, biological term used to underscore the essential simplicity of the solution to time's decay. (Sonnet XII)
  • Semblance: Appearance; likeness; outward form. Refers to the Youth's visible beauty that should be passed on. (Sonnet XIII)
  • Lease: A legal term for the right to occupy and use property for a fixed period. Implies that beauty is not owned outright but held temporarily. (Sonnet XIII)
  • Determination: The legal expiry of a term, or the end of a lease. A child would prevent beauty's "determination" or termination. (Sonnet XIII)
  • Husbandry: Careful management; stewardship of an estate. Also implicitly refers to the duties of a husband, elegantly doubling the poem's demands. (Sonnet XIII)
  • Astronomy: Here used loosely for astrology—the practice of reading stars to predict future events. (Sonnet XIV)
  • Prognosticate: To foretell; to predict. Used in the context of astrology, but here applied to the dire consequences of the Youth's choice. (Sonnet XIV)
  • Doom and date: A formulaic phrase meaning the final, irreversible conclusion or appointed ending. (Sonnet XIV)

Detailed Notes

Sonnet XI: The Social Argument for Posterity

  • Broadened Scope of Duty: Unlike earlier sonnets focusing on the Youth's personal obligation, Sonnet XI extends the argument to a social imperative. It posits that if everyone were to refuse reproduction like the Youth, "the times should cease / And threescore year would make the world away," emphasizing the world's need for continuity through procreation.
  • Proportional Obligation: The poem distinguishes between individuals, stating that "those whom nature hath not made for store, / Harsh, featureless, and rude, barrenly perish" without significant loss. However, the Youth, being "best endow'd," bears a greater, proportional obligation to reproduce.
  • The Printer's Seal Metaphor: This is one of the most memorable images in the sequence. Nature is depicted as having "carv'd thee for her seal," intending the Youth to "print more" impressions of himself. The metaphor stresses that the Youth's purpose, like a seal's, is to multiply his image, not to let the original "copy die" by remaining unused.

Sonnet XII: Time's Inevitability and Procreation as Defense

  • Catalogue of Decay: Sonnet XII is a profound meditation on time, constructed through a detailed catalogue of natural decay: the ticking clock, day turning to night, a violet past its prime, black hair turning white, barren trees, and harvested grain laid on a "bier." This builds an overwhelming sense of the inevitability of destruction.
  • The Youth's Certain Destruction: The catalogue leads inexorably to the "question" of the Youth's beauty, making his eventual destruction feel as certain as the cyclical changes in nature.
  • "Save Breed" as the Sole Weapon: The couplet delivers a stark truth: "And nothing ’gainst Time’s scythe can make defence / Save breed." Procreation is presented as the only effective "weapon" against Time, portrayed as an armed enemy with a "scythe" (the Grim Reaper's tool).
  • Ubi Sunt Tradition: The poem draws on the ancient ubi sunt ("where are they?") poetic tradition, which laments the passing of beauty and power. Shakespeare ingeniously adapts this by displacing the question into the future, asking "where will your beauty be in forty years?"
  • Connections to Other Sonnets: This sonnet's themes and imagery (Time's scythe, catalogue of decay, the violet) anticipate and resonate with later "time sonnets" in the Fair Youth sequence, particularly Sonnets 60, 64, 65, and 99.

Sonnet XIII: Stewardship and the Legacy of Fatherhood

  • Temporary Stewardship of Beauty: Sonnet XIII opens with a philosophical dilemma: the Youth is "No longer yours, than you yourself here live." This suggests that the Youth is not the permanent owner of his beauty but a temporary steward, holding it "in lease" from Nature.
  • Preventing "Determination": The poem argues that generating a child is the only way to prevent this "lease" on beauty from reaching its "determination" (legal expiry or termination) at death, effectively extending beauty beyond one lifetime.
  • The House Metaphor: The third quatrain uses a vivid metaphor of a "fair house" falling to decay. It questions who would let such a house ruin when careful "husbandry" (management and, implicitly, the duties of a husband) could preserve it against the "stormy gusts of winter’s day / And barren rage of death’s eternal cold."
  • Emotional Appeal to Filial Gratitude: The couplet makes a poignant personal plea: "You had a father: let your son say so." This appeals to the Youth's own experience of being fathered, urging him to reciprocate this gift to the next generation, making the argument both logical and deeply emotional.

Sonnet XIV: The Astrologer of Procreation

  • Unique Astronomical Conceit: Sonnet XIV constructs an elaborate astronomical metaphor. The speaker disclaims the conventional astrologer's art—predicting plagues, famines, or the fortunes of princes from the stars above.
  • Reading the Youth's "Constant Stars": Instead, the speaker claims to derive his "knowledge" and read "constant stars" in the Youth's own eyes. These "stars" reveal a unique astrological truth.
  • The Procreative Prophecy: The speaker's prophecy is specific: "Truth and beauty shall together thrive, / If from thyself, to store thou wouldst convert." This means that the Youth's inherent truth and beauty will endure only if he transforms from self-absorption ("thyself") to procreation ("to store").
  • Dire Prognostication: The couplet presents the stark alternative: if the Youth does not reproduce, his death will signify "truth’s and beauty’s doom and date"—the final and irreversible end of these qualities in his line. The word "prognosticate" links back to the astrological theme, but for a much more personal and urgent prediction.

Sonnet XV: The Fleeting Moment of Perfection (Partial Content)

  • Please note: The provided text for Sonnet XV cuts off mid-sentence. Based on the introductory line and the themes of the preceding sonnets, it is highly probable that Sonnet XV would continue to explore the destructive power of time and the fleeting nature of human life and beauty.
  • Considering Growth and Decay: The opening lines "When I consider everything that grows / Holds in perfection but a little moment" immediately establish a familiar theme of natural cycles, emphasizing the brief duration of perfection in the natural world, including human life.
  • Life as a Stage: The phrase "That this huge stage presenteth nought but shows / Whereon the stars in secret influence comment" suggests a theatrical view of life, where human existence is a performance influenced by unseen forces, further highlighting human transience.
  • Humans as Plants: "When I perceive that men as plants increase, / Cheered and checked even by the self-same sky, / Vaunt in their youthful sap" explicitly likens human growth and decline to that of plants. This reinforces the idea that humans, like all living things, are subject to the same natural forces of encouragement and decay, and that youthful vigor ("sap") is temporary.
  • Likely Continuation: Given the pattern of the procreation sonnets, Sonnet XV would almost certainly pivot from this observation of fleeting beauty to an urgent plea for the Youth to "convert" this temporary perfection into a lasting legacy through procreation, defying time's ultimate victory.

Review Questions

  1. How does Sonnet XI broaden the argument for procreation beyond merely the Youth's personal duty, incorporating a social or societal obligation?
  2. Analyze how the "catalogue of decay" in Sonnet XII functions to create a sense of inevitability. What is the speaker's proposed solution, and how is it metaphorically presented?
  3. Discuss the significance of the "lease" and "husbandry" metaphors in Sonnet XIII. How do they reframe the Youth's relationship with his own beauty and his potential role as a father?
  4. In Sonnet XIV, the speaker claims a unique form of "astronomy." How does this "astrology" differ from conventional star-reading, and what specific "prognostication" does it offer the Youth?
  5. Across Sonnets 11-15, what are the most vivid or impactful metaphors (e.g., printer's seal, Time's scythe, the fair house) used to persuade the Youth, and how do they contribute to the overarching theme of procreation?
