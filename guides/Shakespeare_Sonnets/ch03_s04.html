Study Guide: Sonnet IV

Summary

Sonnet IV is the most financially dense poem of the procreation sequence. Shakespeare presents Nature as a lender who extends beauty as a loan — not an outright gift — to the fair youth. The terms of the loan require that beauty be passed on generously; hoarding it is a breach of contract. The youth is called a "beauteous niggard" and a "profitless usurer" — contradictions that expose the absurdity of his self-dealing. The couplet presents the final ledger: unused beauty goes to the tomb; used beauty (a child who inherits it) becomes the executor of the parent's estate, carrying the legacy forward beyond death.

Key Literary Devices

  • Extended financial metaphor: Legacy, lend, spend, niggard, usurer, traffic, audit, executor — beauty is a loan from Nature that demands repayment through reproduction.
  • Oxymoron: "Beauteous niggard" and "profitless usurer" — admiring and condemning the youth simultaneously.
  • Personification: Nature as a generous but contract-bound lender; the grave as the repository of wasted beauty.
  • Paradox: The profitless usurer — a money-lender who makes no profit because he only lends to himself.

Thematic Significance

Sonnet IV is the philosophical center of the financial strand running through Sonnets 1–17. By framing beauty as a loan from Nature rather than private property, Shakespeare makes selfishness not merely sad but legally and morally wrong. The youth does not own his beauty; he holds it in trust. Wasting it is fraud against Nature's terms. The couplet's image of beauty as "executor" is particularly rich: if beauty is invested in a child, the child serves the same function as a will's executor — carrying out the deceased's intentions and preserving the estate beyond death.
