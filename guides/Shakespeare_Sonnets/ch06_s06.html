Study Guide: Sonnet XLVIII

Overview

Sonnet XLVIII is a meditation on possessive anxiety. The speaker sets out on a journey and carefully locks away all his valuables — but the one thing most precious to him, the beloved, cannot be secured. The poem moves through the conceit of locks, chests, bars, and thieves to express the jealous fear that absence leaves the beloved vulnerable to being "stol'n."

Meaning and Argument

The speaker's careful housekeeping before departure (lines 1–4) serves as an ironic contrast: he was scrupulous with trifles but careless with his greatest treasure. The paradox of line 5 is central — the beloved makes all jewels look like trifles, yet the beloved alone is left unprotected. The speaker's only "chest" is his own heart, but the heart has a "gentle closure," not a lock, and the beloved moves in and out at pleasure. The couplet delivers a final, bleak conceit: even truth proves "thievish" for so dear a prize — the beloved's own irresistible worth makes him a target no lock can guard.

Key Literary Devices

  • Extended conceit: Locks, keys, chests, bars, thieves, wards — all sustain the metaphor of the beloved as a jewel that cannot be secured.
  • Antithesis: "most worthy comfort, now my greatest grief" — presence is comfort, absence is grief.
  • Paradox: The heart as an open rather than locked container — the very place of love is not secure.
  • Irony: The speaker who guarded trifles carefully left his greatest treasure unguarded.

Themes

The sonnet explores jealous love and the limits of possession: love cannot own the beloved, no matter how fervently it wishes to. The heart, unlike a chest, cannot be locked. The poem anticipates later sonnets on absence and the fear of infidelity.
