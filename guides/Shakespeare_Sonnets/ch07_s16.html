Study Guide: Sonnet LXXIV

Summary

Sonnet LXXIV offers consolation after the grief of the preceding sonnets by drawing a distinction between the body and the spirit. Death is figured as a "fell arrest / Without all bail" — a final, inescapable legal seizure. But the speaker consoles the beloved: my life has interest (a stake, a presence) in this verse, which will remain as a memorial. When you review this poem, you review what was consecrated to you. The earth takes only earth — the body is merely the dregs of life, the "coward conquest of a wretch's knife," too base to deserve the beloved's grief. The spirit — the better part — is the beloved's to keep. The couplet closes with elegant self-referentiality: "The worth of that is that which it contains, / And that is this, and this with thee remains." The poem points to itself as the spirit's dwelling place.

Key Themes

  • Body versus spirit: The body is "dregs," worthless; the spirit (preserved in verse) is what the beloved truly possesses.
  • Consolation through verse: Unlike LXXI and LXXII's requests for forgetting, LXXIV offers positive consolation.
  • Legal metaphor: Death is an arrest without bail; verse is the spirit's legal representative that remains.

Key Literary Devices

  • Legal metaphor: "Fell arrest," "bail," "interest," "consecrate" — death framed in legal/financial terms.
  • Body/spirit dualism: The contemptible body vs. the precious spirit is the sonnet's central contrast.
  • Self-referential couplet: "This" points to the poem itself — a moment of meta-poetic self-awareness.

Vocabulary

  • Fell: Cruel, deadly
  • Bail: Legal release through payment; here, impossible payment
  • Interest: A legal stake or claim; a portion belonging to someone
  • Consecrate: Dedicated, set apart as holy or specially belonging
  • Dregs: The worthless sediment left after the valuable liquid is drawn off
