Study Guide: Sonnet LXXII

Summary

Sonnet LXXII continues the self-abnegating sequence by extending the request for forgetting into the realm of the speaker's posthumous reputation. After death, the speaker asks the beloved to forget him entirely, because the beloved can prove nothing worthy in him. Unless the beloved were to invent a "virtuous lie" — false praise beyond what the speaker's merit deserves — "niggard truth" will have little to offer. The speaker then turns the concern to the beloved's reputation: the beloved's true love might seem false to the world if he praises someone as unworthy as the speaker. Better that the speaker's name be buried with his body. The couplet delivers a blunt self-condemnation: the speaker is shamed by everything he brings forth, and the beloved, loving "things nothing worth," should likewise be ashamed.

Key Themes

  • Self-deprecation as love: The speaker erases himself out of concern for the beloved's reputation and to spare him the embarrassment of loving the unworthy.
  • Truth versus virtuous falsehood: The tension between honest assessment of worth and love's desire to praise.
  • Memory and burial: The speaker asks that his name, like his body, be simply buried and forgotten.

Key Literary Devices

  • Ironic self-deprecation: The sonnet is itself evidence of the speaker's talent — making the self-condemnation paradoxically moving.
  • Legal language: "Task you to recite" — the world as a court demanding justification.
  • Litotes: "Things nothing worth" — understatement that in its baldness achieves devastating force.

Vocabulary

  • Task: To require, to demand of someone
  • Merit: Worth, deserving quality
  • Virtuous lie: A well-intentioned falsehood designed to do more good than truth can
  • Niggard: Miserly, stingy
  • Desert: Merit, what one deserves
