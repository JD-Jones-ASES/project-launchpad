Study Guide: Sonnet CLIII — The Anacreontic Coda (Part 1)

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet CLIII opens the sequence's two-sonnet mythological coda. The poem retells a classical myth (drawn from the Greek Anthology, Anacreontic tradition): Cupid falls asleep, a maid of Diana seizes the opportunity to steep his love-kindling torch in a cold fountain, which absorbs the "holy fire" and becomes a perpetually seething medicinal bath. The twist is personal: Love's brand is re-fired at the speaker's mistress's eye, he falls sick with love, seeks the bath for cure — and finds none. The cure lies where the fire was re-kindled: in his mistress's eyes. The paradox is perfect and closing: the source of the disease is identical to the only possible cure.

Key Poetic Devices

  • Classical myth: the Anacreontic legend of Cupid's sleeping, the chaste nymph, the fire quenched — a graceful, elegant register after the intensity of CLII
  • Paradox of cure and cause: the bath (cure) was made by love's fire; love's fire now lives in the mistress's eye; therefore the cure is the cause
  • "Dateless lively heat": the fire's immortal, perpetual quality — "dateless" meaning without end
  • "Sad distemper'd guest": the speaker arrives at the spa as a melancholy, disordered patient seeking help

The Anacreontic Coda: Themes

Love's incurability: No external remedy exists; the disease's source is the only help, and seeking help from the source only worsens the condition. Moral struggle and sin: By recasting the Dark Lady's power in ancient myth, Shakespeare elevates the speaker's entrapment to a universal human condition. Shame transformed: The personal shame of the preceding sonnets gives way to mythological inevitability — not absolution, but a different frame for the same inescapable desire. Together, CLIII and CLIV function as a bittersweet close: the sequence's anguish is real, but it is as old as Eros himself.
