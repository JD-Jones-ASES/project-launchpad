Sonnet CXII — Study Guide

Overview

Sonnet CXII follows directly from CXI: having confessed his moral infection and asked for pity, the speaker now declares that the beloved's love has healed the scandal's mark. The poem then pushes further — with the beloved's approval secured, all other voices in the world, critics and flatterers alike, become completely irrelevant.

Meaning

The beloved's love and pity fill the mark stamped by "vulgar scandal" on the speaker's brow. Since the beloved covers over his faults and approves his good, the speaker no longer cares what anyone else thinks. He identifies the beloved as his "all-the-world," the sole source of moral reckoning. In a striking image, he throws all care of others' voices into a "profound abysm" and becomes like an adder — deaf to charmer's music — stopped to both critic and flatterer. The closing lines are absolute: the beloved is so thoroughly embedded in the speaker's purpose that all the world beyond him seems dead.

Literary Devices

  • Metaphor: Scandal's "impression" stamped on the brow like a brand or seal.
  • Horticultural metaphor: "O'er-green" — love growing over faults like living vegetation over barren ground.
  • Allusion: The "adder's sense" alludes to the proverbial deafness of adders, as noted in Psalm 58.
  • Absolutism: "All the world besides methinks are dead" collapses the entire world into one person.

Themes of Constancy and Faithfulness

The poem explores faithfulness as an exclusive orientation. By making the beloved the speaker's entire world, it suggests that perfect constancy requires a kind of radical indifference to all competing voices — a narrowing of the self toward one absolute center.
