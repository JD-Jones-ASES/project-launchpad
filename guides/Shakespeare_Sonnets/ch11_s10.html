Study Guide: Sonnet CXXVI

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet CXXVI is the formal close of the Fair Youth sequence, and it is unique in the entire collection: only twelve lines, six rhymed couplets, with parenthetical blanks printed in early editions where the final couplet should appear — a deliberate or suggestive incompleteness. The speaker addresses the youth one last time as "my lovely boy," noting the paradox that he "hast by waning grown" — that his growth seems to accompany others' withering. Time's hourglass and Time's hours are in the youth's power, and Nature, "sovereign mistress over wrack," has been holding the youth back from decay, using his beauty to disgrace Time and kill its wretched minutes.

But the warning is clear and tender: Nature may delay but cannot keep her treasure forever. Her audit must eventually be answered; her quietus — her final reckoning — is to deliver the youth to Time. The blank parentheses where lines 13-14 would appear seem to perform the very silence of that surrender.

Literary Devices

  • Unique structure: Six couplets with blank lines in place of the conventional closing couplet — the form enacts the content of incompletion and surrender.
  • Personification: Nature as a sovereign mistress who hoards the youth but must pay her debt to Time.
  • Echo of Hamlet: "Quietus" (a final reckoning or release through death) resonates deeply with Shakespeare's later tragedy.

Themes of Farewell and Apology

CXXVI is the sequence's true farewell — a leave-taking from the youth as a living ideal held outside Time's reach. Nature must render what she has withheld; the audit falls due. The Fair Youth sequence ends not in triumph but in elegiac acceptance that even the most beautiful thing must be surrendered to Time's claim.
