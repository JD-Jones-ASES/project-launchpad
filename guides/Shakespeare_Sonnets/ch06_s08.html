Study Guide: Sonnet L

Overview

Sonnet L captures the physical and emotional burden of traveling away from the beloved. The speaker's horse plods dully forward, seeming by instinct to share its rider's grief; even the spur cannot provoke speed. The closing couplet delivers the poem's sharpest insight: grief lies forward, joy lies behind.

Meaning and Argument

The poem opens with the question of why travel is so heavy when the journey's end offers ease — the answer is that ease only measures how far the speaker is from the beloved. The horse's sluggishness is attributed not to laziness but to instinctive sympathy; it too has been "made from thee" (moved by departure from the beloved). Angry spurring produces only a groan, which reminds the speaker that his grief accumulates ahead of him while everything joyful has been left behind.

Key Literary Devices

  • Personification: The horse is given instinct, sympathy, and an expressive groan — it becomes a mirror for the speaker's inner state.
  • Metaphor: "Heavy" runs through the poem as both literal and emotional weight; "my joy behind" gives sorrow a spatial direction.
  • Extended conceit: The entire journey becomes a figure for love and separation.
  • Pathetic fallacy: The external world (the horse, the road) reflects the speaker's emotion.

Themes

The sonnet explores the geography of loss: the beloved defines where joy is located, so any journey away from him is a journey into grief. It pairs with Sonnet LI, which imagines the return journey with reversed emotional valence. Together they form a diptych on the directionality of love.
