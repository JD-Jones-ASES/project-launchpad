Study Guide: Sonnets 59-63 (From Devouring Time)

Overview

This section explores several of Shakespeare's sonnets (specifically 59-62, with 62 being partial), delving into profound themes of time's destructive power, the potential immortality of verse, the nature of love, longing, and self-perception. The sonnets question whether anything is truly new, meditate on the relentless march of time, express the torment of sleepless love and jealousy, and confront the illusion of self-love against the harsh reality of aging.

Key Concepts

  • The Cyclical Nature of Time and Lack of Novelty (Sonnet 59): This concept explores the philosophical idea, drawn from Ecclesiastes, that all events and human experiences have happened before. The poet questions if his praise of the youth is merely a repetition of ancient admirations.
  • The Destructive Power of Time (Sonnets 60, 62): Time is personified as a relentless force that governs all life, leading everything from birth to decay. It is depicted as a "confounder" of gifts, a "transfixer" of youth, and a "mower" of all that stands, ultimately bringing wrinkles and physical decline.
  • The Immortality of Verse (Sonnets 59, 60): Despite time's universal destruction, the poet defiantly asserts that his verse will endure, preserving the beloved's beauty and worth against oblivion. This theme posits poetry as a lasting monument that transcends temporal decay.
  • Unrequited Love, Sleepless Longing, and Jealousy (Sonnet 61): The poet vividly portrays the agony of insomnia caused by an absent beloved, initially attributing it to the beloved's will, then correcting himself to acknowledge it as his own overwhelming love. The sonnet concludes with a bitter note of jealousy, suggesting the beloved is unfaithful.
  • Self-Perception vs. Reality (Sonnet 62): This sonnet confronts the "sin of self-love," where the poet's vanity leads him to believe he is incomparably beautiful. However, the mirror ("glass") shatters this illusion, revealing the true, aged appearance.

Important Terms

  • Beguil'd (Sonnet 59): Deceived; led astray. Refers to the brain's false belief in its own originality.
  • Burthen (Sonnet 59): Burden; also, the refrain of a song. Conveys both the weight of creation and the repetition of old themes.
  • Record (Sonnet 59): (stressed on second syllable) A written account; a chronicle. Implies a sense of official permanence.
  • Character (Sonnet 59): Writing; inscription. Refers to thought being set down in written form.
  • Revolution (Sonnet 59): A turning around; the orbital period of a planet. Signifies the cyclical nature of time.
  • Wh'r (Sonnet 59): A contraction of "whether," used for rapid enumeration of possibilities.
  • Sequent toil (Sonnet 60): Successive effort; the relentless, orderly pushing forward, like waves.
  • Nativity (Sonnet 60): Birth; the moment of being born. In Elizabethan astrology, it also referred to one's horoscope.
  • Confound (Sonnet 60): To destroy utterly; to bring to nothing. Describes Time's annihilation of its own gifts.
  • Transfix (Sonnet 60): To pierce through; to impale. Time's violent action against youth's flourishing.
  • Parallels (Sonnet 60): Lines of latitude; also, wrinkles on a face. Time "delves" these lines into beauty's brow.
  • Scythe (Sonnet 60): The curved blade of Father Time or the Grim Reaper, used for mowing. Symbolizes time's ultimate destructive power.
  • Heavy eyelids (Sonnet 61): Eyelids weighted by exhaustion, struggling to stay open.
  • Tenure (Sonnet 61): A holding; the terms on which something is held; duration of office. "Scope and tenure of thy jealousy" refers to the full reach and terms of jealous surveillance.
  • Watchman (Sonnet 61): One who keeps guard through the night. The poet transforms his insomnia into an active duty for love.
  • Sin of self-love (Sonnet 62): Excessive love of oneself; narcissism.
  • Remedy (Sonnet 62): A cure; to set right.
  • Grounded (Sonnet 62): Deeply rooted; firmly established. Describes how self-love is ingrained in the heart.
  • Surmount (Sonnet 62): To overcome; to surpass.
  • Glass (Sonnet 62): A mirror.
  • Beated (Sonnet 62): Worn; beaten (the face made old).

Detailed Notes

Sonnet 59: The Question of Novelty

Sonnet 59 opens with a philosophical reflection on the idea that "there is nothing new under the sun," a concept drawn from Ecclesiastes. The poet questions whether all human endeavors, particularly the act of praising beauty, are merely repetitions of what has already occurred.

  • Philosophical Opening: The sonnet directly addresses the cyclical nature of existence, asking if our efforts for "invention" (original ideas) are just replaying past events. The poet's "brains beguil'd" suggests a humorous self-awareness of this delusion.
  • Desire for Historical Proof: The speaker expresses a wish to look back "five hundred courses of the sun" (years) to see if the beloved's image was recorded in "some antique book" since "mind at first in character was done" (since writing began). This desire is to understand if humanity has "mended" (improved) or if time's "revolution" (cycle) means all things are constant.
  • Defiant Conclusion: The couplet offers a wry but confident judgment. The poet asserts that even if past "wits" (intelligent people) praised similar beauty, they would have been working with "subjects worse." This "backhanded compliment" declares the youth's beauty to be incomparable across all of recorded history.

Sonnet 60: Time's Relentless Advance

Sonnet 60 is a profound meditation on the relentless, destructive march of time and the poet's defiant claim to immortality through verse.

  • Wave Metaphor: The sonnet famously begins with the simile of waves advancing on a "pebbled shore," each displacing the last, representing minutes hastening to their end. This image encapsulates both the orderly sequence and the exhausting, mechanical quality of time.
  • Life's Progression: The second quatrain traces a life from "nativity" (birth) to "maturity," where "crooked eclipses" (adversities, misfortunes) fight against one's glory. Time is presented first as a giver of life, then as a destroyer who "doth now his gift confound."
  • Time as Destroyer: The third quatrain details Time's specific instruments of destruction: it "transfixes" (pierces through) the "flourish" (showy display) of youth, "delves the parallels" (ploughs wrinkles) into beauty's brow, and "feeds on the rarities of nature's truth." The ultimate image is Time's "scythe" mowing down everything, leaving "nothing" standing.
  • The Defiant Couplet: Despite the preceding twelve lines of overwhelming evidence for time's universal destruction, the sonnet pivots dramatically in the couplet. The poet asserts, "And yet to times in hope, my verse shall stand," praising the beloved's worth "despite his cruel hand." This is a bold claim for the enduring power of poetry.
  • Literary Context (Ovid): The sonnet's imagery and themes, particularly the comparison of time to flowing water and the assertion of poetic immortality, draw heavily from Ovid's Metamorphoses, specifically Pythagoras's speech on constant change and Ovid's own claim of lasting fame for his work. Shakespeare personalizes Ovid's philosophical observations into a more direct, defiant statement.

Sonnet 61: Sleepless Longing and Jealousy

Sonnet 61 is an intense exploration of insomnia, obsessive love, and a bitter undercurrent of jealousy.

  • Poet's Insomnia: The sonnet opens with the poet lying awake, his "heavy eyelids" resisting sleep, tormented by the "image" of the beloved and "shadows like to thee." He questions whether this sleeplessness is the beloved's will, a form of surveillance to "pry" into his "deeds" and find "shames and idle hours."
  • Reversal of Agency: The sestet dramatically reverses the initial questioning. The poet realizes, "O, no! thy love, though much, is not so great." Instead, it is "Mine own true love" that keeps him awake, forcing him to "play the watchman ever for thy sake." This shift highlights the depth of the poet's devotion, which is far greater than any solicitude from the beloved.
  • The Sting of Jealousy: The couplet delivers a harsh, melancholic punch. While the poet selflessly "watch[es]" for the beloved, the beloved is "awake elsewhere, / From me far off, with others all too near." This final line reveals a painful jealousy and a sense of betrayal, suggesting the beloved's infidelity.

Sonnet 62: The Deception of Self-Love (Partial)

Sonnet 62 begins a candid confession of narcissism and the subsequent disillusionment brought by reality.

  • The "Sin" of Narcissism: The sonnet opens with the powerful declaration that "Sin of self-love possesseth all mine eye / And all my soul, and all my every part." The poet admits that this excessive self-admiration is "grounded inward in my heart" and for it, "there is no remedy."
  • Idealized Self-Image: Driven by this self-love, the poet believes "no face so gracious is as mine," and that he "all other in all worths surmount." He defines his own worth as incomparable.
  • Mirror's Harsh Truth: The turning point comes with the line, "But when my glass shows me myself indeed / Beated and chopp'd with tan." The mirror, a symbol of objective reality, exposes the poet's self-deception, revealing an aged and weathered face, contradicting his narcissistic fantasy. Review Questions

  1. Philosophical Foundations: Sonnet 59 opens with the idea that "there is nothing new under the sun." How does Shakespeare develop this philosophical concept, and what is the poet's final attitude towards it in the couplet?
  2. Imagery of Time: Sonnet 60 uses the powerful image of waves on a shore to represent the passage of time. Discuss how this metaphor is extended and developed throughout the sonnet to describe the stages of life and time's destructive actions.
  3. Defiance Against Time: Both Sonnets 59 and 60 assert the lasting power of the poet's verse. Compare and contrast the specific claims made in the couplets of these two sonnets regarding the immortality of poetry and the beloved's worth.
  4. Emotional Arc of Longing: Trace the emotional progression of Sonnet 61. How does the poet's understanding of his sleeplessness evolve from the opening quatrains to the final couplet, and what does the ending reveal about his relationship with the beloved?
  5. Self-Perception and Reality: Based on the beginning of Sonnet 62, how does the poet initially perceive himself, and what role does the "glass" (mirror) play in challenging this perception? What does this reveal about the theme of vanity?
