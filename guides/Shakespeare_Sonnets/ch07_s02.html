Study Guide: Sonnet LX

Summary

Sonnet LX is one of Shakespeare's most celebrated meditations on time's destructive power. It opens with the famous simile of waves advancing on a pebbled shore — each one replacing the last in a relentless sequential march toward the end. The second quatrain traces the full arc of a human life: birth ("Nativity, once in the main of light"), the rise to maturity, then the assault of "Crooked eclipses" against glory, and finally Time's gift given and then confounded. The third quatrain catalogs Time's specific acts of destruction: transfixing youth's flourish, delving wrinkles into beauty's brow, feeding on nature's finest rarities, and mowing everything down with a scythe. The couplet delivers a defiant counter-promise: despite all this, the speaker's verse "shall stand," praising the beloved's worth in times to come, regardless of Time's cruel hand.

Key Themes

  • Time as relentless destroyer: Every moment advances toward death as inevitably as waves toward shore.
  • The life cycle: Birth, maturity, and decline are compressed into a single inexorable sequence.
  • Verse as immortality: The couplet's promise — that verse will stand — is the primary consolation against time's power.

Key Literary Devices

  • Simile: Waves/minutes comparison (lines 1–2) establishes the sonnet's central argument visually.
  • Personification: Time is given a scythe, a hand, and the capacity to "transfix," "delve," and "feed."
  • Anaphora: Repeated "And" in lines 11–14 builds the catalog of Time's destructions to a climax before the couplet's turn.

Vocabulary

  • Sequent: Sequential, following in order
  • Nativity: Birth
  • Transfix: To pierce through and destroy
  • Parallels: The parallel lines of wrinkles on a face
  • Delves: Digs, carves
