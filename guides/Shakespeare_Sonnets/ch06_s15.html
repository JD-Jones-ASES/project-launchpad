Study Guide: Sonnet LVII

Overview

Sonnet LVII is one of the most psychologically complex in the separation sequence. The speaker casts himself as the beloved's "slave" — waiting endlessly, daring not to complain, not to find absence bitter, not to ask where the beloved is. On the surface this is devoted submission; beneath it runs a vein of bitter irony.

Meaning and Argument

The opening declaration — "Being your slave what should I do but tend / Upon the hours, and times of your desire?" — establishes total subordination. The speaker has no time of his own, no services to offer until called, no right to "chide the world-without-end hour" while watching the clock for his sovereign. He dare not think absence is sour, nor even form jealous thoughts about what the beloved is doing. He is reduced to thinking only of "where you are, how happy you make those" — a painful image of the beloved elsewhere bringing joy to others. The couplet seals the irony: love is such a fool that it thinks no ill of anything the beloved does.

Key Literary Devices

  • Extended metaphor: Master/slave throughout, reinforced by "sovereign," "servant," "sad slave," "vassal" (in companion Sonnet LVIII).
  • Hyperbole: "world-without-end hour" — a liturgical phrase applied to a single waiting hour.
  • Irony: The permitted thought — imagining the beloved making others happy — is itself a source of pain.
  • Personification: Love as a "true fool" in the couplet, excusing everything.

Themes

The sonnet explores the humiliation and self-erasure of absolute devotion. The speaker's love has consumed all his agency; he cannot even permit himself to feel grief properly. Whether this is read as sublime dedication or self-betrayal is the poem's central ambivalence.
