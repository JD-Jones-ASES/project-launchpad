Sonnet CVI — Study Guide

Overview

Sonnet CVI takes an unusual argument from history: the beloved is not merely beautiful, but is the culmination of all beauty that past poets ever attempted to describe. Reading old chronicles of "ladies dead and lovely knights," the speaker finds that their blazons were actually unconscious prophecies about his beloved.

Meaning

When the speaker reads ancient poems cataloguing physical beauty — hand, foot, lip, eye, brow — he sees that the poets were "divining" something they could only partially grasp: the beauty of his contemporary friend. Their praises were prophecies. Yet this creates a paradox: ancient poets lacked the skill to capture what they sensed, while present-day observers who can see the beloved directly find they "lack tongues to praise." Beauty at its peak overwhelms all language — past and present.

Literary Devices

  • Allusion: References to chronicles and ancient verse locate the beloved within a long literary tradition.
  • Blazon: The catalogue "hand, of foot, of lip, of eye, of brow" mimics the Renaissance blazon convention.
  • Conceit: The reinterpretation of ancient praise as prophecy is an extended, witty argument.
  • Paradox (couplet): Eyes wonder but tongues fail — the paradox of inexpressible beauty.

Themes of Constancy and Faithfulness

The sonnet extends constancy across historical time: the ideal that all past poets have been faithful to — even without knowing it — is the very beloved celebrated here. This makes the speaker's devotion part of a vast, continuous human tradition of faithfulness to an ideal of beauty that has finally been made real.
