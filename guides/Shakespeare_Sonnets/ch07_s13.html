Study Guide: Sonnet LXXI

Summary

Sonnet LXXI is a tender, self-abnegating farewell in which the speaker asks the beloved to mourn him no longer than the duration of a funeral bell. The octave elaborates this request with extraordinary humility: the speaker does not want the beloved to dwell on his memory ("the hand that writ it"), because if remembering him causes the beloved sorrow, the speaker would rather be forgotten entirely. The sestet deepens this: even if the beloved reads these very lines after the speaker is "compounded with clay," he should not rehearse the speaker's "poor name." The couplet reveals the social dimension of this request — the speaker fears that the "wise world" will observe the beloved's mourning and mock them both. Love's final act is to liberate the beloved from grief and its social consequences.

Key Themes

  • Self-abnegating love: The speaker's love is so selfless that he wishes for his own erasure rather than the beloved's pain.
  • Death and decomposition: The "vile world with vilest worms" creates a grim landscape of mortality.
  • Social shame: The couplet introduces a practical reason for forgetting — the world's mockery of grief.

Key Literary Devices

  • Apostrophe: The entire sonnet is addressed directly to the beloved — a dramatic monologue of farewell.
  • Paradox: Writing a poem to request being forgotten — the very verse that might immortalize also records the wish for oblivion.
  • Alliteration: "Surly sullen bell" — the consonance of s mimics the heavy tolling.

Vocabulary

  • Surly: Gruff, forbidding in tone
  • Sullen: Heavy, dull, and mournful
  • Compounded: Mixed or blended; dissolved into
  • Rehearse: To repeat, to recite aloud
  • Moan: An expression of grief; public mourning
