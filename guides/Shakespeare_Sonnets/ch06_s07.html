Study Guide: Sonnet XLIX

Overview

Sonnet XLIX is an exercise in preemptive self-abnegation. The speaker contemplates a future he dreads: the day when the beloved will no longer love him, will coldly pass him by, and will have justified his departure through rational "advised respects." Rather than fight against this possibility, the speaker takes up a position of utter submission — raising his own hand in legal testimony against himself.

Meaning and Argument

The anaphoric repetition of "against that time" (lines 1, 7, 11) accumulates dread, each iteration adding a new feature of the feared future: a frowning face, a cold greeting, love converted into reasoned gravity. The speaker's response is not to plead innocence but to shelter within his own acknowledged unworthiness. In the sharpest self-deflation in the sequence, the couplet states that the beloved has "the strength of laws" to leave — and the speaker cannot even "allege" a reason why he should be loved.

Key Literary Devices

  • Anaphora: "Against that time" repeated three times to build anticipatory dread.
  • Extended legal conceit: "audit," "sum," "reasons," "strength of laws," "allege," "cause" — all drawn from legal discourse.
  • Metaphor: "that sun, thine eye" — the beloved's warm gaze is a sun whose withdrawal signals emotional cold.
  • Hyperbole of self-deprecation: Raising his hand in testimony against himself is a dramatically humble gesture.

Themes

The sonnet explores the fragility of love and the lover's inability to command affection. If love is subject to rational audit, the speaker — who can "allege no cause" — loses entirely. It is one of the darkest meditations on the asymmetry of love in the sequence.
