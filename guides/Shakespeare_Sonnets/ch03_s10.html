Study Guide: Sonnet X

Summary

Sonnet X is the angriest poem of the procreation sequence. It opens with the exclamation "For shame!" and immediately levels the accusation that the youth, beloved by many, loves no one. This incapacity for love has turned into "murderous hate" directed at his own legacy: he conspires against himself by refusing to reproduce and preserve the "beauteous roof" of his family line. The sestet shifts register, appealing to the youth's better nature with flattery ("Be, as thy presence is, gracious and kind") and then to personal affection: "Make thee another self for love of me." The couplet holds out two possibilities for beauty's survival — in the youth's children ("thine") or in the youth himself if he changes.

Key Literary Devices

  • Exclamatory rebuke: "For shame!" — the most direct and confrontational opening in the sequence so far.
  • House metaphor: "That beauteous roof to ruinate" — family lineage as a beautiful house that must be maintained by an heir.
  • Paradox: Loved by many, loving none — the contradiction at the heart of the youth's character.
  • Volta: The shift from accusation (quatrains) to personal appeal ("O! change thy thought, that I may change my mind") marks the emotional turning point.

Thematic Significance

Sonnet X marks a turning point in the sequence: for the first time, the poet introduces his own love ("for love of me") as a motive for the youth to reproduce. The procreation argument, which had been civic, financial, and natural, becomes personal. This shift signals that the relationship between poet and youth is deepening — and anticipates the later sonnets in which the poet's verse, not just biological children, is offered as a form of immortality. The accusation of "murderous hate" is the sequence's most severe moral charge, elevating the youth's selfishness from mere waste to something approaching a crime against love itself.
