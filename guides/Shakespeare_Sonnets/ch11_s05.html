Study Guide: Sonnet CXXI

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet CXXI breaks sharply from the penitential mode of the preceding sonnets. Rather than apologizing further, the speaker erupts in defiant self-assertion against unnamed critics whose morally corrupt eyes judge him. The opening paradox — that it is better to actually be vile than to be falsely accused of vileness when innocent — launches a furious rejection of external moral judgment. Why should "adulterate eyes" presume to read his nature? Why should "frailer spies" whose own wills call good things bad have any authority over him?

The pivot in line 11 — "No, I am that I am" — borrows God's self-identification from Exodus and asserts absolute moral self-determination. The speaker argues that those who level accusations at him are only exposing their own crooked character: he may be straight while they are bevel. The couplet reduces their position to absurdity: if everyone is bad, the accusers are included in that condemnation.

Literary Devices

  • Biblical allusion: "I am that I am" (Exodus 3:14) — divine language applied to human self-definition, a gesture of extraordinary pride and defiance.
  • Geometric metaphor: "Straight" vs. "bevel" (upright vs. crooked) renders moral character in spatial/mathematical terms.
  • Tu quoque: The accusation is turned back on the accusers — their judgment reveals their own corruption.

Themes of Farewell and Apology

CXXl is a farewell to submission. After four sonnets of confession and apology, the speaker refuses to grovel further before corrupt censurers. The theme of farewell here is a farewell to self-abasement — a reassertion of inner integrity as the only valid court of judgment.
