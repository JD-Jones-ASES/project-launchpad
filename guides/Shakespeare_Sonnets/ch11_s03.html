Study Guide: Sonnet CXIX

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet CXIX is the most emotionally vivid of the apology group, describing the speaker's experience during the affair with imagery of poison, madness, and sensory disorder. He consumed "Siren tears" — beautiful, treacherous appeals — distilled from vessels foul at their core, and lost himself in the oscillation of "applying fears to hopes, and hopes to fears." His eyes were flung from their natural orbits by "this madding fever," and he committed "wretched errors" at the very moments he believed himself most blessed. The first eight lines are a controlled portrait of self-deception at its most thorough.

The volta at line 11 ("O benefit of ill!") pivots sharply: from the rubble of this experience, the speaker discovers a genuine truth — that "better is, by evil still made better." Love ruined and then rebuilt does not merely recover; it grows "fairer than at first, more strong, far greater." The couplet completes the arc: the speaker returns chastened to the youth and finds he has gained "thrice more" than he spent in the ordeal.

Literary Devices

  • Classical allusion: Sirens (mythology) introduce the deceptive beauty of the affair.
  • Alchemical imagery: "Limbecks" and "potions" situate the affair in the language of distillation and transformation.
  • Climactic amplification: "Fairer than at first, more strong, far greater" builds to an ascending climax.

Themes of Farewell and Apology

The sonnet is the most hopeful of the group: it discovers redemption in disaster. The affair was genuinely harmful, but the speaker's return to the youth is not merely a restoration of what was — it is a tested, deepened love. The farewell to error becomes a homecoming to something better.
