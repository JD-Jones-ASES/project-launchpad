Sonnets 122-125: Farewell to the Fair Youth Study Guide

Overview

This section explores Sonnets 122 through 125, part of the "Fair Youth" sequence, focusing on the speaker's evolving relationship with the youth and his understanding of love, memory, and time. These sonnets demonstrate a shift from earlier themes, asserting the supremacy of internal memory over physical records, defiantly challenging Time's power, defining true love as independent of circumstance, and contrasting genuine devotion with superficial display.

Key Concepts

  • Memory's Superiority (Sonnet 122): The speaker argues that the memory of the beloved is so deeply ingrained in his mind and heart that physical tokens or written records are redundant and even imply a lack of perfect remembrance. His internal "tables" are far more reliable and enduring than any external object.
  • Defiance of Time (Sonnet 123): Unlike earlier sonnets that sought to immortalize through verse, this poem directly challenges Time's power. The speaker asserts that Time's apparent novelties and monuments are merely old things disguised, and he refuses to be deceived by its changes, vowing unwavering constancy.
  • True Love's Independence (Sonnet 124): This sonnet posits that genuine love is not a product of external circumstances, political fortune, or societal pressures ("child of state"). Instead, it is intrinsically built, immune to the fluctuations of prosperity or adversity, and transcends worldly cunning or temporary advantage.
  • Authentic Devotion vs. Outward Show (Sonnet 125): The speaker distinguishes between an honest, unadulterated offering of self and superficial, ceremonial displays of honor. He rejects the "outward honouring" and expensive but ultimately fragile monuments, valuing instead a "poor but free" and mutual exchange of hearts.

Important Terms

  • tables (Sonnet 122): Writing tablets; flat surfaces coated in wax or chalk for making temporary notes, common as personal gifts.
  • full character'd (Sonnet 122): Fully inscribed; written through and through. "Character" meant both a written letter and the act of engraving.
  • idle rank (Sonnet 122): Useless grade or category; mere external position among objects.
  • tallies (Sonnet 122): Notched sticks used for keeping numerical records before widespread literacy; a tally was split lengthwise and one half kept by each party as proof of a transaction.
  • adjunct (Sonnet 122): Something attached as a supplement; an accessory.
  • pyramids (Sonnet 123): Ancient monuments; spectacular structures representing human attempts at permanence, not limited to Egyptian structures but any towering monument.
  • dressings of a former sight (Sonnet 123): New costumes on old sights; the familiar disguised as the new, suggesting theatrical costume.
  • foist (Sonnet 123): To thrust or pass off (something spurious) as genuine; to deceive someone into accepting an inferior or false article as real.
  • registers (Sonnet 123): Official records; historical documents that claim to record truth.
  • scythe (Sonnet 123): The curved blade traditionally carried by Father Time or the figure of Death, symbolizing the cutting down of lives.
  • child of state (Sonnet 124): A product of circumstances; something contingent on political fortune rather than intrinsic worth. "State" here means both political condition and personal fortune.
  • Fortune's bastard (Sonnet 124): An illegitimate offspring of chance; something without stable parentage, implying no legal standing and easy disownment.
  • thralled discontent (Sonnet 124): Enslaved resentment; grievance that has become a kind of bondage. "Thralled" means held in thrall, captive.
  • policy, that heretic (Sonnet 124): Worldly cunning or shrewd self-interest, labeled a heretic because it operates only by short-term calculation, without faith in anything beyond immediate advantage.
  • hugely politic (Sonnet 124): Supremely wise in strategy; powerfully calculating. Ironically, love achieves this by refusing to play politics.
  • fools of time (Sonnet 124): Possibly: those who are fooled by time's reversals, or those who are time's subjects; also possibly those who repent of a misspent life only at the moment of death.
  • bore the canopy (Sonnet 125): Carried the ceremonial cloth held over a king, noble, or dignitary in procession, a formal honor but also a service role.
  • extern (Sonnet 125): External appearance; outward show, used as a noun.
  • obsequious (Sonnet 125): Devoutly attentive; devoted. In Elizabethan English, it did not necessarily carry the modern connotation of fawning servility but could mean "dutiful in service."
  • oblation (Sonnet 125): An offering; specifically a religious offering or sacrifice, elevating the speaker's devotion to a quasi-sacramental act.
  • not mix'd with seconds (Sonnet 125): Unadulterated; not blended with inferior material. "Seconds" were lower grades of goods mixed in to bulk out a product.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet 122: The Mind's Superior Record

Sonnet 122 begins by addressing a curious incident: the speaker has apparently given away or discarded writing tablets that were a gift from the youth. He then offers a vigorous defense of his actions. His central argument is that the memory of the youth is etched so perfectly and permanently within his own "brain" and "heart" that no external record could ever compare. These internal "tables" are "full character'd with lasting memory," meaning they are fully inscribed and eternally present.

The speaker dismisses physical records—the gift tablets, or even crude "tallies" (notched sticks for record-keeping)—as "idle rank" or unnecessary aids. To keep such an "adjunct" (an addition or supplement) to memory would imply that his natural memory might fail. This, he argues, would be an insult to the depth and permanence of his love for the youth, suggesting a potential for forgetfulness that he vehemently denies.

This sonnet presents a significant reversal of themes found in earlier sonnets (like 18, 55, 65), where the power of poetry itself was often presented as the immortalizing medium for the beloved. Here, the speaker paradoxically distrusts writing and champions the living brain as the ultimate archive. This creates an intentional tension within the sequence: if internal memory is sufficient, why bother writing sonnets at all? This discomfort may highlight a shift in the speaker's perspective or an exploration of different forms of remembrance.

Sonnet 123: A Challenge to Time

Sonnet 123 is a bold and proud challenge directed at Time itself. Unlike many earlier "time sonnets" that acknowledged mutability's power but sought to defeat it through the immortality of verse, this sonnet takes a different, more defiant approach. The speaker simply refuses to be impressed by Time's apparent innovations. He declares that Time's "pyramids"—its grand monuments, its supposedly novel wonders—are "nothing novel, nothing strange" to him. They are merely "dressings of a former sight," old things adorned in new costumes, suggesting Time is a deceiver or a stage manager.

The poem explains that human life is "brief," and because of this brevity, people are easily fooled into admiring what Time "foist[s] upon us that is old," mistaking recycled creations for genuine novelties. The speaker, however, vows not to be tricked. He openly defies both Time's "registers" (official historical records, which he claims "doth lie") and Time itself, asserting his constancy and fidelity are beyond the reach of Time's destructive "scythe." He concludes with a direct and unwavering vow: "I will be true despite thy scythe and thee."

This sonnet echoes the defiance seen in Sonnet 55 and 107, but instead of using verse or prophecy to defeat Time, it achieves victory through sheer refusal to be moved—a stoic posture reminiscent of Sonnet 116. The idea that all "new" things are merely old things re-presented anticipates the philosophical skepticism explored in Sonnet 59.

Sonnet 124: Love Beyond Accident

Sonnet 124 makes a profound philosophical claim about the true nature of love. The speaker asserts that his "dear love" is not the "child of state," meaning it is not born of external circumstances, political fortunes, or societal whims. If it were, it would be vulnerable to being disowned by "Fortune’s bastard," an illegitimate offspring of chance without stable parentage or legal standing. Love that depends on outward conditions—"smiling pomp" (ostentatious display), "thralled discontent" (enslaved resentment), or shifting political favor—is depicted as fragile and temporary.

In contrast, the poet's love was "builded far from accident," implying an intrinsic, foundational quality that is independent of chance. It "suffers not in smiling pomp, nor falls / Under the blow of thralled discontent," meaning it is indifferent to both prosperity and adversity. It also "fears not policy, that heretic"—worldly cunning or strategic calculation, which is condemned as a false belief that operates only on short-term advantage. Instead, this true love "all alone stands hugely politic," paradoxically achieving the most strategic and enduring position by refusing to engage in worldly politics at all. It is unaffected by "heat" (favour) or "showers" (adversity).

The enigmatic couplet calls upon the "fools of time" as witnesses—perhaps those who are easily deceived by Time's reversals, or, more darkly, those who, after a life of crime, only repent "for goodness" at the point of death. Even these individuals, the speaker suggests, can testify to love's independence from earthly calculation. This sonnet forms a philosophical pairing with Sonnet 116, defining constancy through political and legal imagery rather than meteorological metaphors. The critique of "policy" also echoes its negative portrayal in Sonnet 118.

Sonnet 125: The True Offering

Sonnet 125 draws a sharp distinction between two kinds of devotion: the outward, ceremonial, and superficial, and the genuine, inner, and heartfelt. The speaker questions the value of outward acts of honor, asking if it would mean anything to him to "bore the canopy" (carried the ceremonial cloth over a dignitary) or to build "great bases for eternity," which he observes often prove "more short than waste or ruining." He has witnessed people who pursue "form and favour" lose everything by "paying too much rent" for superficial pleasures ("compound sweet"), ultimately becoming "pitiful thrivers" spent in "gazing."

The speaker wants none of this outward show. Instead, he desires to be "obsequious in thy heart" (devoutly attentive in the youth's inner being) and asks the youth to accept his "oblation" (offering). This offering is described as "poor but free"—modest but unconstrained, uncontaminated, and "not mix'd with seconds" (not adulterated with inferior material). It "knows no art" (no deceit or elaborate display) but is simply a "mutual render, only me for thee"—a pure, reciprocal exchange of selves.

The couplet introduces a sudden, almost violent dismissal of a "suborned informer" (a bribed spy or false witness). This abrupt address suggests a specific external threat or accusation in the background, possibly from the Rival Poet or some intermediary attempting to undermine the speaker's standing with the youth. The speaker asserts that "a true soul / When most impeach'd, stands least in thy control," implying that false accusations only prove the true soul's unwavering integrity and independence.

Review Questions

  1. In Sonnet 122, the speaker argues that his internal memory is superior to physical tokens. How does this argument reverse or challenge themes presented in earlier sonnets about the immortalizing power of poetry? What tension does this create?
  2. Sonnet 123 presents a defiant stance against Time. How does the speaker use the imagery of "pyramids" and "dressings of a former sight" to argue that Time's apparent power is a deception?
  3. Discuss the philosophical claim about the nature of true love in Sonnet 124. What does it mean for love to be "builded far from accident," and how does this contrast with being the "child of state"?
  4. Sonnet 125 distinguishes between two forms of devotion. What is the difference between "bearing the canopy" and offering a "mutual render, only me for thee"? Why does the speaker reject the former in favor of the latter?
  5. Across these four sonnets (122-125), identify a common thread or evolving idea about the speaker's understanding of his love for the Fair Youth. How do these sonnets collectively define or redefine the nature of this love in relation to memory, time, and external pressures?
