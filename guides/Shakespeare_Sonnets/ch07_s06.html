Study Guide: Sonnet LXIV

Summary

Sonnet LXIV is a sustained meditation on witnessed ruin that leads the speaker to a devastating personal conclusion. The first twelve lines are organized by anaphoric "When I have seen" constructions, cataloging instances of time's destruction: the defaced monuments of buried ages, lofty towers razed, brass enslaved to mortal rage, the ocean encroaching on the shore, and the firm soil winning back from the sea — a ceaseless interchange of loss and gain in which "state itself" is confounded to decay. The observation of all this ruin teaches the speaker to "ruminate": Time will come and take his love away. The couplet delivers the emotional weight: this thought is "as a death which cannot choose / But weep to have, that which it fears to lose." The act of loving creates the very vulnerability that makes loss unbearable.

Key Themes

  • Ruin as meditation: Observing historical and natural decay is the path to the speaker's personal terror.
  • The paradox of possession: Having what one loves makes the prospect of its loss into a kind of pre-emptive death.
  • Absence of consolation: Unlike Sonnets LXIII and LXV, Sonnet LXIV offers no verse-immortality promise — only grief.

Key Literary Devices

  • Anaphora: "When I have seen" / "When sometime" structures the first 12 lines as a catalog of ruined witnesses.
  • The exchange image: "Increasing store with loss, and loss with store" — the chiasmic balance of sea and shore.
  • Simile (couplet): The thought of loss is compared to "a death" that can only weep.

Vocabulary

  • Fell: Cruel, savage
  • Defac'd: Defaced, obliterated
  • Down-raz'd: Razed to the ground
  • Confounded: Destroyed, thrown into ruin
  • Ruminate: To meditate deeply, to chew over in the mind
