Study Guide: Sonnet LXXXVIII

Overview

Sonnet LXXXVIII extends the self-abnegating logic of LXXXVII into active performance: the speaker does not merely accept separation but offers to fight against himself on the beloved's behalf. If the beloved decides to hold the speaker in contempt, the speaker will supply the evidence for his own condemnation — confessing hidden faults, arguing the beloved's case, even proving the beloved virtuous despite oath-breaking. The gesture is extreme, paradoxical, and deeply revealing about the nature of the speaker's attachment.

Key Themes

The dominant theme is self-sacrifice as love's ultimate proof. The speaker's logic is paradoxical: by harming himself, he gains. Because all his thoughts are focused on the beloved, any act that benefits the beloved — including self-condemnation — rebounds as benefit to the speaker through the doubled logic of love ("double-vantage me"). The couplet crystallizes this: "Such is my love, to thee I so belong, / That for thy right, myself will bear all wrong." Love so complete that it abolishes the distinction between the self's interests and the beloved's.

Poetic Devices

The oxymoron "upon thy side, against myself I'll fight" is the sonnet's rhetorical pivot, capturing the self-defeating nature of radical devotion. "Attainted" carries its legal sense of disgrace and conviction, continuing the legal register from LXXXVII. "Double-vantage" employs the commercial metaphor of profit, showing how love's arithmetic works differently from ordinary calculation.

Estrangement Dimension

Where LXXXVII accepted separation as legally inevitable, LXXXVIII shows the speaker actively colluding in the process of estrangement. The beloved need not even do the work of justifying abandonment; the speaker will provide all the material. This masochistic self-erasure is simultaneously an expression of love and a kind of surrender of selfhood entirely.
