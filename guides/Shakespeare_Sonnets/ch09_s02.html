Study Guide: Sonnets 87-91 - Estrangement and Return

Overview

This section explores a series of Shakespeare's sonnets (87-91) that delve into themes of estrangement, self-abnegation, and the speaker's acceptance of a beloved's potential withdrawal of affection. The sonnets portray a speaker who, far from resisting, actively offers to justify the beloved's rejection, ultimately finding a paradoxical strength or unique form of love in absolute submission and a desire to face the worst of sorrow immediately.

Key Concepts

  • Legal and Commercial Language in Love (Sonnet 87): The speaker frames the relationship using terms like "charter," "patent," "bonds," and "estimate" to describe the beloved's worth and the speaker's claim to their love. This highlights the formal, almost contractual nature of the perceived break, where the speaker concludes he never had a legal or moral right to the beloved's affection.
  • Self-Effacement and Paradoxical Gain (Sonnet 88): The speaker offers to actively argue against himself, using his own intimate knowledge of his faults to justify the beloved's decision to "set him light." This extreme self-sacrifice is presented as a form of "vantage" or benefit to the speaker, as it aligns with his absolute love for the youth.
  • Total Self-Erasure (Sonnet 89): This sonnet extends the self-abnegation into practical, daily life. The speaker promises to play along with any invented fault, feign physical lameness, suppress the beloved's name, and actively avoid their presence, effectively erasing himself from the beloved's world. This culminates in the chilling logic of hating himself because the beloved hates him.
  • Stoic Acceptance of Immediate Loss (Sonnet 90): Shifting from submissive acceptance, the speaker here pleads for immediate, decisive abandonment rather than a prolonged, piecemeal withdrawal. He argues that facing the "worst of fortune's might" at once is preferable, as it allows other sorrows to diminish in comparison to the central loss of the beloved.
  • The Beloved as the Measure of Suffering: Across these sonnets, especially in 90, the beloved's importance is underscored as the ultimate standard by which the speaker calibrates all other joy and suffering. Their loss eclipses all other griefs.
  • Estrangement and the Dream of Love: The relationship is often depicted as something transient or illusory, as seen in Sonnet 87's "dream doth flatter" comparison, where the speaker wakes to a harsh reality after a period of imagined possession.

Important Terms

  • dear (S. 87): Precious, costly, but also beloved. The pun suggests the youth is too expensive both in price and affection for the poet to maintain.
  • estimate (S. 87): The assessed value or worth of something. Refers to the youth's self-knowledge of their own value.
  • charter (S. 87): A formal legal document granting rights or privileges. The youth's inherent worth grants them freedom from the speaker's claims.
  • bonds (S. 87): Legal obligations or ties. The speaker's claims on the relationship are "determinate" (expired).
  • patent (S. 87): An exclusive right granted by authority. The speaker's "patent" to the youth's love is revoked due to lack of merit.
  • misprision (S. 87): A legal term for error, misunderstanding, or failure to report a crime. Here, the gift of love was founded on a mistake in judgment.
  • set me light (S. 88): To treat with contempt; to regard as worthless or insignificant.
  • forsworn (S. 88): Having broken a sworn oath; perjured.
  • attainted (S. 88): Convicted; stained with dishonor; found guilty. A legal term implying formal condemnation.
  • vantage (S. 88): Advantage; benefit; gain.
  • lameness (S. 89): Literally, a physical disability; here, a figurative weakness or defect of character.
  • halt (S. 89): To limp; to walk with difficulty.
  • profane (S. 89): To treat something sacred with disrespect; to violate. The speaker fears his mention of the youth's name would dishonor it.
  • haply (S. 89): By chance; perhaps.
  • bent to cross (S. 90): Inclined to obstruct; determined to work against.
  • rearward (S. 90): The rear guard of an army; that which follows behind. To "come in the rearward of a conquer'd woe" is to attack after the main battle is already lost.
  • purpos'd overthrow (S. 90): A deliberate defeat; a planned destruction.
  • onset (S. 90): The first attack; the beginning of a battle or assault.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet 87: "Farewell! thou art too dear for my possessing,"

This sonnet is a formal and controlled expression of grief, built meticulously on the language of Elizabethan law and commerce. The speaker issues a "farewell" not out of anger, but from a calculated conclusion that he never deserved the beloved's affection.

  • Legal Framework: The sonnet is a sustained conceit using legal terms. The beloved's inherent worth is their "charter," entitling them to freedom. The speaker's claim to their love was a "patent" that has now "determinate" (expired) because he lacks "deserving." He essentially audits his own claim and finds it invalid.
  • The Beloved's Value: The opening line, "thou art too dear for my possessing," uses "dear" with a double meaning: precious/beloved and costly/expensive. The youth's "estimate" (assessed value) is too high for the speaker to afford, suggesting a disparity in worth.
  • Misprision and Revocation: The speaker concludes that the beloved must have given themselves originally out of "misprision" – a legal error or misunderstanding – either not knowing their own value or the speaker's unworthiness. With "better judgement making," the "great gift" naturally "Comes home again."
  • Dream vs. Reality: The devastating couplet ("Thus have I had thee, as a dream doth flatter, / In sleep a king, but waking no such matter") frames the entire relationship as an illusion. The speaker's "kingship" over the beloved was merely a pleasant fantasy that dissolves upon awakening to reality. This evokes a poignant sense of loss without self-pity.
  • Context of Elizabethan Law: Shakespeare's use of legal vocabulary was common in the era to describe relationships and obligations, reflecting the actual structure of patronage. The sonnet's coolness in argumentation, despite the emotional weight, gives it a unique "ache."

Sonnet 88: "When thou shalt be dispos'd to set me light,"

Following the logic of unworthiness from Sonnet 87, this sonnet takes self-abnegation to an extreme. The speaker not only accepts the loss but actively offers to assist in his own rejection.

  • Arguing Against Himself: If the beloved decides to "set me light" (treat him with contempt), the speaker pledges to "fight / Upon thy side, against myself." He will become his own accuser, using his intimate knowledge of his "concealed faults" to "prove thee virtuous" even if the beloved is "forsworn" (perjured or broken an oath).
  • Paradoxical Gain: The remarkable twist is the speaker's claim that this self-destruction is a form of "gainer." By directing all his "loving thoughts" towards the beloved's benefit, even the "injuries that to myself I do" ultimately "double-vantage" him. His selfless love is its own reward, finding benefit even in his own harm if it serves the beloved.
  • Absolute Submission: The couplet crystallizes this paradox: "Such is my love, to thee I so belong, / That for thy right, myself will bear all wrong." This declares an absolute surrender of self for the beloved's sake, raising questions about the nature of such extreme devotion.
  • Connections: This self-effacement echoes earlier sonnets like 57 and 58, where the speaker portrays himself as a slave. It forms part of a cluster depicting psychological subjection within the sonnet sequence.

Sonnet 89: "Say that thou didst forsake me for some fault,"

This sonnet extends the speaker's self-erasure from legal argument into everyday life and social interaction, demonstrating a willingness to invent and perform faults to justify the beloved's abandonment.

  • Inventing Faults: The speaker promises to cooperate with any reason the youth might invent for their separation. If accused of "lameness" (a defect), he will "straight will halt" (limp), providing physical evidence for a false accusation. He will offer no defense against the beloved's "reasons."
  • Social Disgrace and Self-Imposed Exile: The speaker's self-disgrace is deeper than anything the beloved could inflict. He vows to "strangle" their "acquaintance" (knowledge of/association with), choosing to "look strange" to the beloved. He will avoid the beloved's "walks" and prevent their "sweet beloved name" from dwelling on his tongue, fearing that his own "profane" association would "do it wrong."
  • Chilling Logic of Submission: The couplet provides a stark conclusion: "For thee, against my self I'll vow debate, / For I must ne'er love him whom thou dost hate." If the beloved now hates the speaker, the speaker logically must hate himself, completing the cycle of total submission and self-annihilation.
  • Connections: The themes of enforced silence and self-imposed exile connect this sonnet to 83 and 85, where silence is debated as a form of honor. Here, however, it is a penance. The anguish of self-imposed separation also anticipates sonnets 97 and 98.

Sonnet 90: "Then hate me when thou wilt; if ever, now;"

This sonnet marks a shift in the speaker's appeal, not begging to be kept, but rather pleading for the timing of his abandonment: decisively and immediately, not piecemeal.

  • Timing of Loss: The speaker urges the beloved to "hate me when thou wilt; if ever, now." He wants the worst blow to fall at once, while "the world is bent my deeds to cross" and he is already braced for misfortune. This prevents an "after-loss" that would come after his heart has "’scap’d this sorrow."
  • Cumulative Grief: The vivid image, "Give not a windy night a rainy morrow," captures the exhaustion of prolonged suffering. A single storm is bearable, but continuous, attritional grief is more devastating than an initial, severe blow. He seeks to avoid a "purpos'd overthrow" that "lingers out."
  • Stoicism vs. Sentiment: The logic here is more stoic. It's easier to endure the "very worst of fortune’s might" in "the onset" (the first attack) rather than suffering a series of "petty griefs" only to face the greatest loss last.
  • The Beloved as the Ultimate Measure: The powerful couplet reveals the sonnet's core message: "And other strains of woe, which now seem woe, / Compar’d with loss of thee, will not seem so." All other sorrows are rendered insignificant and trivial when measured against the overwhelming loss of the beloved, who is thus established as the ultimate calibrator of the speaker's emotional landscape.
  • Connections: The military imagery ("rearward," "onset," "conquered woe") links this sonnet to other instances of battlefield metaphors in Shakespeare's work, emphasizing the speaker's sense of being under siege.

Review Questions

  1. In Sonnet 87, the speaker uses legal and commercial language to describe the end of his relationship. What effect does this formal, detached vocabulary have on the emotional impact of the sonnet, and why might Shakespeare have chosen this approach?
  2. Sonnet 88 presents a speaker willing to argue against himself and find "vantage" in his own disgrace. Do you view this as an act of genuine, selfless love, or as a problematic surrender of self-worth? Discuss the ethical implications of such extreme self-abnegation.
  3. Sonnet 89 depicts the speaker's readiness to actively disgrace himself and erase his presence from the beloved's life. How does this compare to modern notions of processing heartbreak, and what does it suggest about the power dynamics within this relationship?
  4. In Sonnet 90, the speaker begs for immediate and complete abandonment rather than a prolonged "purpos'd overthrow." What psychological insight does this reveal about coping with profound loss, and how does the image of "a windy night a rainy morrow" contribute to this understanding?
  5. Across these four sonnets (87-90), the speaker demonstrates varying responses to impending or actual estrangement. Trace the evolution of his emotional and psychological state, identifying both continuities and shifts in his perspective.
