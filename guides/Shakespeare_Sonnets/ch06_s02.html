Chapter 6, Section 2: Sonnets 43-47 - Study Guide

Overview

This section explores Shakespeare's Sonnets 43 through 47, delving into the speaker's experience of separation and absence from the beloved. Across these five sonnets, the speaker grapples with the physical and emotional challenges of distance, using vivid metaphors drawn from dream visions, classical elements, and legal disputes to portray the intense longing, internal conflict, and imaginative solutions employed to sustain love in separation.

Key Concepts

  • The Paradox of Vision: Sonnet 43 introduces the idea that true sight is often found in darkness or sleep, where the beloved's image appears more vividly than in waking hours. This challenges conventional understanding of perception.
  • Body vs. Thought (The Four Elements): Sonnets 44 and 45 establish a central conflict between the heavy, immobile physical body (made of "earth and water") and the swift, unbounded mind/spirit (made of "air and fire"). This framework from classical philosophy explains the speaker's inability to traverse distance and the emotional impact of absence.
  • The Eye-Heart Conflict: Sonnet 46 dramatizes an internal "mortal war" between the eye (seeking external sight) and the heart (claiming internal possession of the beloved). This common poetic trope explores the different ways love is perceived and held.
  • The Power of Imagination and Inner Vision: Throughout these sonnets, especially in 43 and 47, the speaker relies heavily on dreams, thought, and mental imagery to keep the beloved's presence alive. Imagination becomes a vital tool for emotional sustenance in absence.
  • Cyclical Nature of Grief and Consolation: Sonnet 45 vividly illustrates a continuous cycle of fleeting joy upon receiving news of the beloved, immediately followed by renewed sadness as thought and desire are sent back, perpetuating the longing.
  • Love as Legal Dispute and Truce: Sonnets 46 and 47 frame the internal conflict over the beloved's image in legal terms, ultimately arriving at a "truce" where both external sight and internal feeling find a way to coexist and nourish each other.

Important Terms

  • attend time's leisure: To wait patiently for time's slow unfolding; implies a feeling of being trapped by the indifferent passage of time.
  • badges of either's woe: A poignant image in Sonnet 44; refers to tears as symbolic representations of the sorrow shared by both the heavy elements of earth and water, which constitute the speaker's body.
  • bar: (In Sonnet 46) Both a barrier and a legal term for a court or the legal profession; signifies the legal dispute between the eye and the heart.
  • closet never pierced with crystal eyes: (In Sonnet 46) Refers to the heart's claim of being a private, unbreachable sanctuary where the beloved resides, hidden from the external gaze of the eye.
  • darkly bright: A Shakespearean paradox (Sonnet 43) describing the luminous clarity of vision experienced in the darkness of dreams; seeing brilliantly in a sightless state.
  • doth commerce: (In Sonnet 47) Engages in trade or exchange; here, refers to the reciprocal sharing of the beloved's image between the eye and the heart.
  • due to thee: (In Sonnet 46) What is owed or rightfully belongs to the beloved, specifically their image.
  • dull substance: (In Sonnet 44) Refers to the physical body, emphasizing its heavy, inert, and slow nature, in contrast to agile thought.
  • earth and water: (In Sonnet 44) Two of the four classical elements, associated with coldness, wetness, heaviness, and slowness; represents the physical body's limitations.
  • imperfect shade: (In Sonnet 43) The dream-image of the beloved; it is only a copy or reflection, yet powerful enough to transform the night.
  • injurious distance: (In Sonnet 44) Distance personified as a harmful agent actively causing suffering by separating the lovers.
  • life's composition: (In Sonnet 45) The ideal balance of the four classical elements that constitutes a healthy, living human being; disruption of this balance leads to illness or melancholy.
  • love and beauty: (In Sonnet 46) Refers to the more abstract, inner qualities of the beloved that the heart claims to possess.
  • mortal war: (In Sonnet 46) An intense, life-or-death conflict, used metaphorically to describe the dispute between the eye and the heart.
  • Neoplatonic idea: A philosophical concept (mentioned in context for 43) that the lover carries the beloved's ideal image imprinted on the soul, which can be perceived in certain states like sleep or intense thought.
  • nimble thought: (In Sonnet 44) Thought conceived as swift and agile, capable of instantly traversing great distances, unlike the slow body.
  • painted banquet: (In Sonnet 47) A metaphor for a mental or imaginative feast; the beloved's image serves as a visual and emotional sustenance for the eye and heart, even in absence.
  • present-absent: (In Sonnet 45) A Shakespearean paradox; refers to the elements (and thus thought/desire) being simultaneously with the beloved and absent from the speaker.
  • purging fire: (In Sonnet 45) Cleansing, refining fire; associated with the burning energy of desire and the vital spirit.
  • recur'd: (In Sonnet 45) Restored to its proper, healthy state; cured or brought back to wholeness.
  • shadow: (In Sonnet 43) Can mean an image, a reflection, or literal darkness/shade; Shakespeare plays on all meanings simultaneously, as the beloved's image shines even in darkness.
  • slight air: (In Sonnet 45) Light, insubstantial air; emphasizes the element's lack of weight and swiftness, associated with intellect and thought.
  • store: (In Sonnet 47) A supply or reserve; the imagination provides a continuous supply of the beloved's image.
  • tender embassy: (In Sonnet 45) A gentle or affectionate diplomatic mission; the light elements (thought and desire) act as messengers of love to the beloved.
  • unrespected: (In Sonnet 43) Unnoticed, disregarded; refers to ordinary things seen during the day that lack true significance compared to the beloved's image.
  • wink: (In Sonnet 43) In Elizabethan usage, commonly meant to close one's eyes in sleep, not necessarily to blink one eye.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet XLIII (43)

  • Summary: The speaker finds that his eyes see the beloved most clearly not when awake, but when he sleeps and dreams. The waking world offers only "unrespected" sights, while the darkness of night, through dreams, illuminates the beloved with a "darkly bright" glow. He ponders how much more glorious it would be to see the beloved in daylight if a mere dream-image is so radiant. The sonnet concludes by redefining day and night: all days are "nights" when the beloved is absent, and only nights that bring dreams of the beloved are truly "bright days."
  • Key Imagery/Metaphors: Paradox of seeing in darkness ("darkly bright"), inversion of day and night, the beloved's "shadow" as a source of light.
  • Context: Dreaming and Vision in the Renaissance: This sonnet taps into a rich tradition where dreams were considered gateways to truth, allowing perception of deeper realities. It also draws on the Neoplatonic idea of the beloved's image being imprinted on the soul, becoming visible when daily distractions are stripped away.
  • Connections: This sonnet's paradox of seeing and blindness anticipates Sonnet 47, which also explores inward vision, and later "absence" sonnets (97, 98) that invert the seasons to reflect the beloved's absence.

Sonnet XLIV (44)

  • Summary: The speaker urgently wishes his physical body were composed of "thought" rather than heavy "flesh." If so, distance ("injurious distance") would pose no barrier, as "nimble thought" can instantly traverse "sea and land." However, he laments that he is made of "earth and water" (two slow, heavy classical elements) and must endure time's slow pace, expressing his grief only through "heavy tears."
  • Key Imagery/Metaphors: Contrast between "dull substance" (body) and "nimble thought," personification of distance, the body's composition as "earth and water," tears as "badges of woe."
  • Context: The Four Elements and the Humoral Body: Elizabethan natural philosophy viewed the body as a microcosm composed of four elements. Earth and water were considered heavy and slow, explaining the body's immobility, while mind and spirit were lighter elements. This framework explains the speaker's physical suffering due to absence.
  • Connections: Sonnet 44 forms a direct philosophical pair with Sonnet 45, both exploring the four elements. The "heavy tears" at the end of 44 set the emotional stage for the beginning of 45. The contrast between swift thought and slow flesh also foreshadows Sonnets 50 and 51, where a slow horse symbolizes reluctance to travel away from the beloved.

Sonnet XLV (45)

  • Summary: Continuing the elemental theme, the speaker explains that the "other two" elements – "slight air" (thought) and "purging fire" (desire) – are perpetually with the beloved, acting as "swift messengers." When these lighter elements depart from him, his "life's composition" (the balance of elements) is disrupted, leaving him diminished to only earth and water, causing profound melancholy. He experiences brief joy when these messengers return with news of the beloved's health, but this very joy causes them to race back, plunging him immediately into sadness again.
  • Key Imagery/Metaphors: The four elements personified as parts of the self and as "tender embassy" (ambassadors), "present-absent" paradox, "life's composition" as a delicate balance.
  • Context: The Soul's Elements in Elizabethan Thought: This sonnet identifies air with intellect/thought and fire with passion/desire, giving these internal states a tangible, elemental reality. This allows Shakespeare to dramatize the emotional cycle of separation through a philosophical framework.
  • Connections: As the second part of a pair with 44, it fully develops the elemental conceit. The idea of elements as ambassadors echoes the theme of devoted service in later sonnets (57, 58). The rapid cycle of gladness and sadness anticipates the structure of Sonnet 52, which also deals with intermittent pleasure in absence.

Sonnet XLVI (46)

  • Summary: The speaker describes a "mortal war" between his eye and his heart over possession of the beloved's image. The eye claims the right to see the beloved's physical form ("thy picture's sight"), while the heart claims the beloved resides exclusively within it, in a "closet never pierced." The sonnet uses extensive legal terminology to describe this internal dispute, culminating in a "truce" and a division: the eye is granted the external "form" (the beloved's body), and the heart is given the internal "love and beauty."
  • Key Imagery/Metaphors: Legal dispute metaphor (mortal war, plead, bar, verdict, inquest, deed, moiety, tenure), the eye vs. heart convention, the heart as a secret "closet."
  • Context: The "Eye vs. Heart" Convention: This sonnet engages with a common Renaissance poetic trope that explores the tension between physical sight and internal affection. The legal framing adds a layer of dramatic and intellectual complexity to this internal conflict.
  • Connections: This sonnet builds on the theme of sight and inner vision introduced in 43, providing a "legal" justification for the internal presence of the beloved. It directly sets up the "truce" that is elaborated and resolved in Sonnet 47. The Neoplatonic idea of an imprinted image (from 43) resonates with the heart's claim here.

Sonnet XLVII (47)

  • Summary: Following the "truce" from Sonnet 46, the eye and heart resolve their conflict by entering a pact. When either feels starved of the beloved's presence, the other provides sustenance: the eye sends its visual memories to the heart, or the heart sends its inner, imagined image to the eye. The beloved's image is thus always present in thought, creating a "painted banquet" that feeds both eye and heart, sustaining them even in physical absence. This imaginative communion keeps the beloved perpetually near.
  • Key Imagery/Metaphors: The "truce" as an ongoing agreement, the beloved's image as a "painted banquet" (mental feast), the "commerce" between eye and heart.
  • Context: Imagination as Sustenance: This sonnet offers a practical, imaginative resolution to the earlier conflict. It highlights the Renaissance understanding of the mind's power to create and sustain images, thereby nourishing the emotional life in the beloved's absence.
  • Connections: This sonnet directly follows and resolves the conflict presented in 46. The "painted banquet" metaphor echoes the dream vision of 43, reinforcing the idea that imagined or internal presence can be as powerful as, if not more powerful than, physical sight. It emphasizes the enduring theme of inner sustenance during separation.

Review Questions

  1. How do Sonnets 43, 46, and 47 explore the concept of "seeing" the beloved, particularly in absence? Discuss the paradoxes and internal resolutions presented.
  2. Explain the significance of the four classical elements (earth, water, air, fire) in Sonnets 44 and 45. How do they help Shakespeare convey the speaker's physical and emotional suffering due to separation?
  3. Analyze the "mortal war" between the eye and the heart in Sonnet 46. What specific claims does each make, and how does the legalistic language contribute to the sonnet's effect?
  4. In Sonnet 45, the speaker experiences a cyclical pattern of joy and sadness. How does this cycle function, and what does it reveal about the nature of love and longing in absence?
  5. Discuss the role of imagination and inner vision as a means of coping with separation across these sonnets (43-47). Provide specific examples from at least two different sonnets.
