Study Guide: Sonnet VI

Summary

Sonnet VI opens as a direct sequel to Sonnet V, immediately urging the youth to distill himself before winter's "ragged hand" defaces him. The vial of perfume from Sonnet V becomes here a container the youth must fill with "beauty's treasure" — that is, he must have children before age destroys him. Shakespeare playfully extends the financial metaphor: one child is good, ten children better, each one a tenfold return on beauty's investment. The rhetorical climax comes with the question, "What could death do if thou shouldst depart, / Leaving thee living in posterity?" The answer is clear — nothing. The couplet then pivots to the repulsive alternative: be "self-willed" and die without children, and your only heirs are worms.

Key Literary Devices

  • Continuation of distillation metaphor: "Make sweet some vial" — the youth must fill a perfume vial (child) with his essence before winter destroys the flower.
  • Personification: "Winter's ragged hand" — winter given a physical, destructive hand that defaces beauty.
  • Paradox on usury: Reproduction is a "use" (interest) that is not forbidden usury because it benefits all parties willingly.
  • Carrot and stick: The ten-children fantasy (reward) and the worm-heir threat (punishment) bracket the argument.

Thematic Significance

Sonnets V and VI form a miniature triptych with the distillation image at their center: Sonnet V introduces it, Sonnet VI applies it urgently. Together they represent the most poetic and sensory version of the procreation argument — not a legal or financial obligation but a beautiful natural process, like making perfume from summer roses. The couplet's "make worms thine heir" is one of the sequence's most memorably grotesque images, making the alternative to reproduction viscerally repellent. Death is conquered not by poetry (that comes later) but by the living warmth of children.
