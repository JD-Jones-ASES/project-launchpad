The Case for Posterity: Sonnets 6-10 Study Guide

Overview

This section delves into Sonnets 6 through 10 of Shakespeare's sequence, intensifying the speaker's arguments for the Youth to procreate and preserve his beauty for posterity. Using a rich tapestry of metaphors—from financial transactions and the sun's daily journey to musical harmony and the concept of waste—these sonnets explore the moral imperative of reproduction, warning against self-will and the destructive nature of celibacy. The arguments progress from logical appeals to increasingly personal and accusatory tones, culminating in a direct plea for the Youth to act out of love.

Key Concepts

  • Immortality Through Procreation: The central theme, reiterated across these sonnets, is that having children is the only way for the Youth's beauty and essence to survive beyond his death. This is presented as a duty and a vital act against the ravages of time.
  • The Consequences of Celibacy: The sonnets consistently portray the Youth's refusal to have children as a form of self-destruction, stubbornness ("self-will'd"), or even "murderous hate." It is depicted as a waste of beauty that only benefits "worms."
  • Metaphors of Time and Decay: Sonnet VI uses "winter's ragged hand" to symbolize aging, while Sonnet VII employs the sun's daily journey from "orient" dawn to "weary car" dusk to illustrate the inevitable decline of beauty and attention over time.
  • Financial and Economic Imagery: Sonnet VI reintroduces financial terms like "usury" to argue that procreation is a beneficial loan of beauty. Sonnet IX contrasts the circulating nature of monetary wealth with the permanent destruction of wasted beauty, emphasizing the unique, non-transferable nature of the Youth's gift.
  • Musical Harmony and Discord: Sonnet VIII cleverly uses the metaphor of music, specifically "concord" and the harmonious union of musical "parts," to represent the ideal family unit. The Youth's "singleness" is presented as a destructive discord.
  • The Speaker's Evolving Tone: The arguments shift from logical persuasion (Sonnet VI's "distillation" argument, Sonnet VII's analogy) to more direct reproach (Sonnet VIII's "sweetly chide," Sonnet IX's "murd'rous shame") and finally to a deeply personal plea in Sonnet X, where the speaker asks the Youth to reproduce "for love of me."

Important Terms

  • Vial: (Sonnet VI) A small container; metaphorically used here for the womb, holding the "distill'd" essence of beauty for new life.
  • Usury: (Sonnet VI) Lending money at excessive interest, generally condemned in Elizabethan England. Shakespeare redefines it here as a beneficial "lending" of beauty to produce more beauty, arguing it is not just permitted but required.
  • Self-will'd: (Sonnet VI) Stubbornly determined, refusing advice; implies wilfulness and pride, cardinal sins of prioritizing self over duty.
  • Orient: (Sonnet VII) The East, where the sun rises; evokes splendor and newness, reflecting the Youth's prime.
  • Pilgrimage: (Sonnet VII) A sacred journey. The sun's daily arc is likened to a sacred journey, elevating the Youth's beauty to a quasi-religious significance.
  • Reeleth: (Sonnet VII) Staggers or sways, like someone overcome by age or drink; vividly captures the sun's apparent loss of control as it declines, mirroring human aging.
  • Concord: (Sonnet VIII) Harmony or agreement, especially in music; represents the ideal social and familial harmony, contrasting with the Youth's "singleness."
  • Singleness: (Sonnet VIII) The state of being unmarried; also, a single musical note. The pun suggests the Youth is "ruining" his potential by remaining alone.
  • Parts: (Sonnet VIII) Individual musical lines that combine in harmony; also, personal roles or qualities. The double meaning highlights the Youth's failure to fulfill both musical and social roles.
  • Issueless: (Sonnet IX) Without children or offspring; a legal term that makes childlessness sound like a failure to execute a proper will for one's legacy.
  • Makeless: (Sonnet IX) Without a mate; also implies "peerless" or "matchless." The world mourning the childless Youth is like a widow who has lost someone irreplaceable.
  • Unthrift: (Sonnet IX) A spendthrift; one who wastes money. Used ironically to show that wasted beauty is far more destructive than wasted money, as beauty cannot simply circulate.
  • Unprovident: (Sonnet X) Lacking forethought; failing to provide for the future. Carries financial and legal connotations, suggesting a failure of duty.
  • Beauteous Roof: (Sonnet X) A metaphor for the Youth's body, his lineage, or the house that shelters his beauty.
  • Ruinate: (Sonnet X) To ruin or destroy.
  • Repair: (Sonnet X) To restore or renovate; presented as the Youth's duty to his "beauteous roof."

Detailed Notes

Sonnet VI: The Imperative to Distill and Reproduce

  • Direct Instruction: Opens with a direct command to the Youth: "Then let not winter’s ragged hand deface, / In thee thy summer, ere thou be distill’d." This reinforces the "distillation" argument from Sonnet V, urging immediate action before beauty fades.
  • Financial Metaphors: The concept of "usury" is cleverly reinterpreted. While usually condemned, Shakespeare argues that lending beauty (through procreation) is a beneficial transaction ("not forbidden usury") that "happies those that pay the willing loan." This implies a reciprocal joy between parent and child.
  • Arithmetic of Posterity: The poem playfully escalates the argument for more children, suggesting "ten times happier" to have ten children than none, or for one's image to be "ten times refigur'd" in descendants.
  • Victory Over Death: The ultimate stake is confronting death. If the Youth reproduces, he can depart "Leaving thee living in posterity," thereby escaping death's total conquest.
  • The Stark Alternative: The couplet delivers a blunt warning: "Be not self-will’d, for thou art much too fair / To be death’s conquest and make worms thine heir." Refusal to reproduce means beauty is wasted, and only decay benefits. The term "self-will'd" highlights stubborn pride as a vice.

Sonnet VII: The Sun's Daily Journey as a Metaphor for Life

  • Extended Metaphor: The entire sonnet is built on the analogy of the sun's journey through the sky.
    • Dawn (Youth): "In the orient when the gracious light / Lifts up his burning head," the world ("each under eye") pays homage, adoring its new appearance.
    • Noon (Middle Age): The sun is still admired, having "climb’d the steep-up heavenly hill," resembling "strong youth in his middle age." "Mortal looks adore his beauty still."
    • Dusk (Decline): When the sun is past its "highmost pitch," with "weary car," it "reeleth from the day" like "feeble age." Attention shifts; "The eyes, ’fore duteous, now converted are / From his low tract, and look another way."
  • Blunt Application: The couplet directly applies the metaphor to the Youth: "So thou, thyself outgoing in thy noon: / Unlook’d, on diest unless thou get a son." Just as the world loses interest in the setting sun, it will cease to admire the Youth once his beauty wanes, unless a son serves as a "new rising sun."
  • Connections: This motif of the sun's rise and fall, and the world's changing gaze, connects to other sonnets, particularly Sonnet 33 (on the sun obscured by clouds, applied to the Youth's behavior) and Sonnet 73 (where the speaker contemplates his own aging).

Sonnet VIII: Music, Harmony, and Singleness

  • Musical Argument: This sonnet presents the most unique argument for procreation, using music as its central metaphor.
  • The Puzzle: The speaker opens by questioning why the Youth, who is himself "music to hear," listens to actual music sadly. The implied answer is that music's harmony highlights his own lack of harmony.
  • Concord as a Rebuke: The "true concord of well-tuned sounds" and "unions married" offend the Youth's ear because "They do but sweetly chide thee, who confounds / In singleness the parts that thou shouldst bear." The harmonious blending of notes symbolizes the ideal union of marriage and family that the Youth rejects.
  • Domestic Harmony: The metaphor extends to a "domestic scene" in the third quatrain: "Mark how one string, sweet husband to another, / Strikes each in each by mutual ordering; / Resembling sire and child and happy mother, / Who, all in one, one pleasing note do sing." This paints a picture of the family as a harmonious chord, where individual parts contribute to a beautiful whole greater than themselves.
  • The Music's Message: The couplet delivers the "speechless song's" message directly: "Sings this to thee: ‘Thou single wilt prove none.’" Remaining "single" (unmarried, a single note) amounts to nothing; he destroys the potential for harmony and legacy.

Sonnet IX: Waste, Widowhood, and "Murd'rous Shame"

  • Refutation of an Excuse: The sonnet begins by imagining a sympathetic reason for the Youth's celibacy—fear of causing a widow's grief. The speaker immediately dismisses this.
  • The World as Widow: If the Youth dies childless ("issueless"), the entire world will be his "makeless widow" and "still weep." Unlike a private widow who can see her husband's "shape in mind" through her children's eyes, the world will have no such consolation for the irreplaceable Youth.
  • Contrast of Wealth and Beauty: A key economic argument is introduced: "Look! what an unthrift in the world doth spend / Shifts but his place, for still the world enjoys it." Ordinary wealth merely changes hands. However, "beauty’s waste hath in the world an end, / And kept unused the user so destroys it." Beauty, unlike money, is a non-renewable resource that perishes if not reproduced, making its waste far more destructive.
  • Harsh Condemnation: The couplet offers a severe judgment: "No love toward others in that bosom sits / That on himself such murd’rous shame commits." Refusing to have children is portrayed not as restraint but as a violent act against oneself and others, a profound moral failing.
  • Connections: This contrast between circulating wealth and finite beauty refines financial arguments seen in Sonnets 4 and 6. The "murd'rous shame" foreshadows similar self-destructive themes.

Sonnet X: Accusation and Personal Appeal

  • Direct Accusation: This sonnet is the most confrontational, opening with "For shame! deny that thou bear’st love to any," directly accusing the Youth of loving no one.
  • "Murderous Hate": While granting that many love the Youth, the speaker argues that the Youth's "unprovident" refusal to reproduce demonstrates he loves none of them, because he is "possess’d with murderous hate" against himself. His celibacy is an act of self-destruction.
  • The "Beauteous Roof": The Youth is accused of conspiring "’gainst thyself... / Seeking that beauteous roof to ruinate / Which to repair should be thy chief desire." The "beauteous roof" likely refers to his own body, his lineage, or the legacy of his beauty that he is allowing to collapse.
  • A Shift to Personal Appeal: The sonnet takes a pivotal turn in line 9: "O! change thy thought, that I may change my mind." This marks a rare moment where the speaker explicitly personalizes the argument, presenting the Youth's change of heart as a gift to the speaker.
  • Love for the Speaker: The couplet seals this personal plea: "Make thee another self for love of me, / That beauty still may live in thine or thee." Procreation is no longer just a duty or a fight against time, but an act of affection requested by the speaker himself. This introduces a new layer of emotional complexity to the relationship.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast the speaker's use of financial metaphors in Sonnets VI and IX. How do they each contribute to the overall argument for procreation, and how does Sonnet IX refine this argument?
  2. How does the extended metaphor of the sun's journey in Sonnet VII function to persuade the Youth? What emotional impact might this imagery have on the reader and the Youth?
  3. Analyze the role of puns and double meanings (e.g., "singleness," "parts," "makeless") in Sonnets VIII and IX. How do these linguistic devices enhance the sonnets' persuasive power?
  4. Trace the progression of the speaker's tone and level of accusation from Sonnet VI to Sonnet X. What is the significance of the speaker's direct, personal appeal in the couplet of Sonnet X ("for love of me")?
  5. Sonnet IX describes the Youth's refusal to procreate as "murd'rous shame," and Sonnet X calls it "murderous hate" against himself. Discuss the implications of this strong language. How does it elevate the stakes of the procreation argument beyond mere vanity or duty?
