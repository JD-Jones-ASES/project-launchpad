Study Guide: Sonnet III

Summary

Sonnet III opens with a mirror — the youth is told to look at his own face and recognize it as a face that must be reproduced. Shakespeare argues that no woman fair enough to deserve the youth would reject him, so there is no excuse for childlessness. The poem's most tender moment is the image of the youth's mother, who sees her own "lovely April" (the spring of her prime) reflected in her son. Just as he is his mother's mirror, so he should give a child the gift of seeing his own golden time in future eyes. The couplet delivers the starkest threat of the sequence so far: die single, and your image — your face, your beauty, your very self — dies with you.

Key Literary Devices

  • Mirror metaphor: The glass is both literal prompt and symbol — the face seen in it must be reproduced or lost.
  • Agricultural metaphor: "Unear'd womb" and "tillage of thy husbandry" — reproduction as plowing and cultivation.
  • Window metaphor: "Through windows of thine age" — eyes as windows through which old age can glimpse lost youth in a child.
  • Rhetorical questions: Lines 5–8 challenge the youth to name any woman who would refuse him, making childlessness inexcusable.

Thematic Significance

Sonnet III personalizes the procreation argument by introducing the mother as a living example of its benefits. The logic is cyclical and deeply human: the youth already knows the joy of being someone's mirror (his mother's); the poem urges him to give that same gift forward. The mirror device subtly anticipates later sonnets in which the poet's verse will serve as a different kind of mirror. Here, however, the only true mirror is a living child, and the couplet leaves no ambiguity: choosing singleness is choosing oblivion.
