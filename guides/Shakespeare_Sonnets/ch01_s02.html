The Works

Key Concepts

  • Shakespeare wrote or co-wrote 37 plays, two long narrative poems, and 154 sonnets
  • His plays are traditionally grouped into four categories: comedies, histories, tragedies, and late romances
  • The comedies share a grammar of confused identities and festive resolution, but contain shadows of melancholy
  • The histories form a loose cycle examining English kingship, power, and rebellion, with Falstaff as a towering comic creation
  • The four great tragedies — Hamlet, Othello, King Lear, Macbeth — explore jealousy, revenge, madness, and corrupted ambition
  • The late romances are stranger and more archaic, steeped in wisdom about loss and forgiveness
  • Venus and Adonis (1593) and The Rape of Lucrece (1594) established Shakespeare's literary reputation; both were dedicated to Southampton
  • The sonnets were circulating among private friends by 1598 and published in full by Thorpe in 1609
  • The bulk of the sonnets are dated to the 1590s, though some may have been composed or revised as late as 1609

Summary

Shakespeare's output spans 37 plays, two narrative poems, and 154 sonnets — a range that no other English writer has matched for breadth and command of register. His plays are conventionally divided into comedies, histories, tragedies, and late romances, though he observed no such boundaries himself. The comedies move from comic confusion to festive resolution but are never wholly sunny; the histories probe the human cost of political ambition across two centuries of English kingship. The four great tragedies of the early 1600s — Hamlet, Othello, King Lear, and Macbeth — are philosophically devastating explorations of jealousy, revenge, and corrupted power. The late romances close the career on a note of hard-won wisdom about loss and reconciliation. Before theatre made him famous, his reputation rested on his narrative poems: Venus and Adonis went through ten editions in his lifetime. The sonnets themselves circulated privately from at least 1598 and share with the plays a set of obsessions — the cruelty of time, the tyranny of desire, the consoling power of art.

Key Terms

  • comedies: Plays characterised by confused identities, crossed lovers, witty banter, and festive resolution — though Shakespeare's comedies retain notes of melancholy and exclusion
  • histories: Plays refashioning English historical chronicles into an examination of power, legitimacy, and rebellion, covering the reigns from Richard II to Henry VIII
  • tragedies: Plays of extreme psychological and moral intensity; the four great middle-period tragedies are Hamlet, Othello, King Lear, and Macbeth
  • late romances: The final phase of Shakespeare's drama — Pericles, Cymbeline, The Winter's Tale, The Tempest — featuring archaic plots of separation, reunion, and supernatural intervention
  • Venus and Adonis: Shakespeare's first published work (1593), dedicated to Southampton; a bestseller that established his literary reputation
  • The Rape of Lucrece: The second narrative poem (1594), also dedicated to Southampton; a grave poem about honour, shame, and political power
  • Palladis Tamia: Francis Meres' 1598 literary survey, which referred to Shakespeare's "sugared sonnets among his private friends" — the earliest evidence of the sonnets' circulation
  • The Passionate Pilgrim: A pirated anthology (1599) that included two of the sonnets, placing them in existence by that date
