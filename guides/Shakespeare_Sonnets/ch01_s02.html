The Works of William Shakespeare: A Study Guide

Overview

This section provides a comprehensive look at the remarkable scope of William Shakespeare's literary output. It details his prolific career, encompassing 37 plays categorized into comedies, histories, tragedies, and late romances, as well as two significant narrative poems and 154 sonnets. The text highlights how Shakespeare, despite lacking a university education and writing for the commercial stage, achieved an astonishing range and ambition, with recurring themes and stylistic elements that bridge his dramatic and poetic works.

Key Concepts

  • Prolific and Diverse Output: Shakespeare's career produced a staggering volume and variety of work, including 37 plays, two long narrative poems, and 154 sonnets, demonstrating immense range without formal university training.
  • Categorization of Plays: Scholars group Shakespeare's plays into four traditional categories—comedies, histories, tragedies, and late romances—each with distinct characteristics, though Shakespeare himself did not rigidly observe these boundaries.
  • Comedies with Depth: Shakespeare's comedies, while featuring mistaken identities, witty banter, and happy endings, often include elements of melancholy, excluded figures, or moments of cruelty, making them "comedies with their eyes open."
  • Histories as Political Sagas: The history plays form a cycle examining English kingship, power, legitimacy, and the human cost of ambition, often drawing on historical sources like Holinshed's Chronicles.
  • Tragedies of Intense Exploration: The great tragedies (e.g., Hamlet, Othello, King Lear, Macbeth) delve into profound philosophical and emotional territories such as revenge, jealousy, madness, and corruption.
  • Late Romances as Reconciliation: The final phase of plays, the late romances, are characterized by archaic plots, long separations, and supernatural elements, ultimately leading to themes of loss, forgiveness, and hard-won wisdom.
  • Narrative Poems and Literary Reputation: Shakespeare's two long narrative poems, Venus and Adonis and The Rape of Lucrece, were crucial in establishing his literary fame among contemporaries, predating his dominance on the stage.
  • Porous Boundary Between Plays and Sonnets: There is significant thematic and stylistic overlap between Shakespeare's plays and sonnets, sharing obsessions like the cruelty of time, the power of art, and the complexities of love and desire.
  • Dating the Sonnets: The precise composition date of the sonnets is debated but largely placed in the 1590s, with some later revisions, supported by references from contemporaries and early publications.
  • Sonnets as a Crafted Sequence: The sonnets are understood not as spontaneous outpourings but as a carefully crafted sequence by a master poet, whether based on real experiences, literary personae, or a combination.

Important Terms

  • Commercial Stage: The public theaters for which Shakespeare primarily wrote, contrasting with writing solely for literary reputation or aristocratic patronage.
  • Holinshed's Chronicles: A multi-volume historical work that served as a major source of historical information and plot inspiration for many of Shakespeare's history plays.
  • Tetralogies: A series of four plays, specifically referring to Shakespeare's two groups of history plays that chronicle sequential periods of English monarchy.
  • Late Romances: The category for Shakespeare's final plays (Pericles, Cymbeline, The Winter's Tale, The Tempest), characterized by a blend of tragic and comic elements, magical interventions, and themes of redemption.
  • Valediction: A farewell statement or gesture, often used to describe The Tempest as Shakespeare's metaphorical good-bye to his dramatic career.
  • Narrative Poems: Long poems that tell a story, like Shakespeare's Venus and Adonis and The Rape of Lucrece, which were significant for his early literary renown.
  • Ovid's Metamorphoses and Fasti: Ancient Roman poetic works by Ovid that served as key sources for the myths and stories retold in Shakespeare's narrative poems.
  • Pirated Anthology: An unauthorized collection of literary works, such as The Passionate Pilgrim, which published some of Shakespeare's sonnets without his permission.
  • Terminus: A final or concluding point; the date of the first authorized publication of the full sonnet sequence (1609) acts as a terminus for their composition.
  • Literary Personae: A character or identity created by an author to speak in a poem or story, distinct from the author's own voice or experiences.

Detailed Notes

The Works: An Introduction

  • Shakespeare's Productivity: Over approximately 25 years, he produced:
    • 37 plays (solo-authored or co-written)
    • 2 long narrative poems
    • 154 sonnets
  • Remarkable Achievement: This output is notable because Shakespeare:
    • Never attended university.
    • Wrote primarily for the commercial stage, not for high literary reputation.
  • Range and Ambition: His work spans diverse genres, from light comedies to devastating tragedies, history plays, and unique late romances, showcasing unparalleled command of language and register.

The Plays: An Overview

  • Traditional Grouping: Scholars categorize the plays into four groups, although Shakespeare himself did not rigidly define these.

The Comedies

  • Early Cluster: Often among his earliest works.
  • Examples: The Comedy of Errors, The Taming of the Shrew, A Midsummer Night's Dream, Much Ado About Nothing, As You Like It, Twelfth Night.
  • Common Grammar/Elements:
    • Confused identities
    • Crossed lovers
    • Witty banter and wordplay
    • Final resolution, often in marriage and reconciliation
  • Depth and Nuance: Despite festive endings, they frequently contain:
    • A "shadow at the edge": notes of melancholy, excluded figures, or moments of cruelty.
    • Examples: Duke's misery in As You Like It, Malvolio's humiliation in Twelfth Night, Antonio's isolation in The Merchant of Venice.
    • Often described as "comedies with their eyes open."

The Histories

  • Loose Cycle: Cover nearly two centuries of English kingship, from Richard II to Henry VIII.
  • Tetralogies:
    • First Tetralogy (Wars of the Roses): Henry VI, Parts I, II, and III; Richard III.
    • Second Tetralogy: Richard II, Henry IV, Parts I and II; Henry V.
  • Major Literary Project: A sustained examination of:
    • Power and legitimacy
    • Rebellion and succession
    • The human cost of political ambition
  • Source Material: Often drew on Holinshed's Chronicles.
  • Notable Character: Falstaff (from the Henry IV plays) is considered one of Shakespeare's greatest comic creations.

The Tragedies

  • Philosophical and Emotional Depth: Shakespeare at his most searching and devastating.
  • Middle Period (around 1600): Four great tragedies written within a half-dozen years:
    • Hamlet (revenge, delay, mortality)
    • Othello (jealousy, manipulation)
    • King Lear (filial ingratitude, madness)
    • Macbeth (ambition, corruption)
  • Roman Tragedies:
    • Julius Caesar, Antony and Cleopatra, Coriolanus.
    • Characterized by a "cooler, more political intelligence" while still exploring similar themes.

The Late Romances

  • Final Phase: Pericles, Cymbeline, The Winter's Tale, The Tempest.
  • Characteristics:
    • "Stranger and more difficult" than earlier works.
    • Deliberately archaic plots: long separations, improbable reunions, death-and-resurrection patterns, supernatural interventions.
  • Themes: Steeped in "hard-won wisdom about loss and forgiveness."
  • The Tempest as Valediction:
    • Almost certainly Shakespeare's last sole-authored play.
    • Prospero's renunciation of magic and drowning of his books is often interpreted as Shakespeare himself laying down his pen.

The Narrative Poems

  • Early Literary Reputation: Shakespeare first established his literary fame through these poems.

Venus and Adonis (1593)

  • Context: Published when London theaters were closed due to plague, turning Shakespeare's energy to print.
  • Dedication: To the Earl of Southampton, whom Shakespeare called "the first heir of my invention" (suggesting he initially valued poems over plays).
  • Content: Retells Ovid's myth of the goddess Venus's ardent pursuit of the mortal Adonis, who scorns her and is killed by a boar.
  • Style: Lush, witty, and at times "bracingly comic" (e.g., Venus's absurdity, Adonis's horse).
  • Success: A runaway bestseller, with ten editions in Shakespeare's lifetime, securing his fame as an author.

The Rape of Lucrece (1594)

  • Dedication: Also to Southampton, with a warmer tone indicating a deepening relationship.
  • Contrast with Venus and Adonis: Grave and tragic (vs. comic and erotic).
  • Content: Retells the Roman story of Tarquin's violation of the chaste Lucrecia, and the political consequences (expulsion of Tarquin kings, founding of the Roman Republic).
  • Themes: Honor, shame, political power.
  • Technique: Long rhetorical set-pieces (Tarquin's monologue, Lucrece's lament) serve as "soliloquy-exercises," honing dramatic techniques for the stage.
  • Sources: Both poems drew heavily on Ovid's Metamorphoses and Fasti.
  • Anticipated Themes for Sonnets: Beauty inviting destruction, tyranny of desire, the consolation of art.

The Sonnets and the Plays: A Shared World

  • Porous Boundary: Plays and sonnets share many obsessions and thematic connections.
  • Shared Obsessions:
    • The cruelty of time
    • The inadequacy of language to capture beauty
    • Love shading into obsession, self-deception, and shame
  • Echoes in Plays:
    • Immortalizing Power of Verse: Explored in plays like Love's Labour's Lost (where lords compose verses).
    • Embedded Sonnets: Romeo and Juliet opens with a shared sonnet between the lovers, performing their miraculous meeting in language.
    • Darker Emotional Terrain:
      • Othello's jealousy ("green-eyed monster") relates to the tortured possessiveness in some Dark Lady sonnets.
      • Hamlet's meditations on mortality, the ideal vs. actual, and difficulty of action echo sonnets like 66 and 73.
      • Macbeth's despair ("tale told by an idiot, full of sound and fury, signifying nothing") resonates with similar struggles in the sonnets.

Dating the Sonnets

  • Uncertainty and Debate: The precise date of composition is unknown and has been debated since the 18th century.
  • Broad Consensus: Most scholars place the bulk of the sequence in the 1590s, with potential revisions or new compositions as late as 1603-1609.
  • Evidence Informing this View:
    • Francis Meres' Palladis Tamia (1598): Mentions Shakespeare's "sugared sonnets among his private friends," proving some were in circulation.
    • The Passionate Pilgrim (1599): A pirated anthology that included Sonnets 138 and 144, confirming their existence by this date.
    • Thomas Thorpe's Publication (1609): The full sequence was published, serving as a terminus for the collection, though its authorization is unclear.
    • Imagery and Versification: Consistent with Shakespeare's lyric intensity in plays from the mid-to-late 1590s through the early 1600s.
    • Later Sonnets' Tone: Some Dark Lady poems have a "rawness and impatience" akin to the great tragedies (1600-1608), suggesting later composition or revision.
  • Conclusion: The sonnets are a "crafted sequence," not spontaneous, shaped by a master at the height of his powers. They may record real experiences, be literary personae, or a combination of both, standing among the most accomplished lyric poems in English.

Review Questions

  1. How did Shakespeare's background (never attending university, writing for the commercial stage) influence the remarkable range and ambition of his output?
  2. Compare and contrast Shakespeare's comedies with his late romances, discussing their characteristic plots, themes, and how they approach resolution or wisdom.
  3. Discuss the significance of Shakespeare's two long narrative poems, Venus and Adonis and The Rape of Lucrece. How did they establish his literary reputation, and what thematic connections do they share with his plays and sonnets?
  4. Explain how scholars use historical documents and internal evidence (like imagery and tone) to estimate the composition dates of Shakespeare's sonnets, acknowledging the challenges in determining a precise timeline.
  5. Provide specific examples from the text to illustrate how the "boundary" between Shakespeare's plays and sonnets is "porous," highlighting shared themes, emotional territories, or structural elements.
