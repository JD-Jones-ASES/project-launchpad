Study Guide: Sonnet CLII

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet CLII is the chapter's — and arguably the sequence's — climactic confession of perjury. The speaker begins by accusing the Dark Lady of being "twice forsworn" (breaking her marriage vow and then vowing hate after love), but the argument rapidly inverts. He breaks twenty oaths to her two; all his vows were made to misuse her; he swore deep oaths of her kindness and truth while "giving eyes to blindness" to make her seem bright. The couplet is the most devastating self-indictment: "I have sworn thee fair; more perjured I" — calling her fair when she is foul is his greatest lie, the perjury that contains all the others. The sonnet collapses the entire preceding praise of the Dark Lady into self-confessed falsehood.

Key Poetic Devices

  • Forensic rhetoric: the accumulation of counts (her two oaths, his twenty) gives the sonnet a quasi-legal structure of indictment
  • Self-reversing argument: the initial accusation against her is turned against himself with greater force
  • "Gave eyes to blindness": oxymoron — using the capacity to see in order to cultivate blindness, a willful self-deception
  • Oath/perjury language: "forsworn," "oaths," "faith," "sworn" — the poem is saturated with the vocabulary of broken sacred vows

Central Themes

Sin and shame: Perjury in Renaissance culture was a grave moral and spiritual offense — to be forsworn before God. The sonnet catalogs multiple instances of oath-breaking with escalating seriousness. Moral struggle: The speaker does not rationalize here as he does in CLI; CLII is a moment of stark self-accusation, the clearest-eyed moral reckoning in the chapter. Truth and falsehood: The couplet exposes all previous praise as lie — retroactively reframing every compliment in the sequence as sworn false testimony. This sonnet functions as the Dark Lady sequence's confession before the mythological coda.
