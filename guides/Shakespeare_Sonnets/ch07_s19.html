Study Guide: Sonnet LXXVII

Summary

Sonnet LXXVII concludes Chapter 7 on a note of practical wisdom. The speaker addresses the beloved with three objects that together form an instrument of self-knowledge: a glass (mirror), a dial (sundial), and a book with blank pages. The mirror will show the beloved how his beauties wear — and the wrinkles it reveals will give memory of "mouthed graves." The sundial's shadow, moving with "shady stealth," will show "Time's thievish progress to eternity." The blank book is for writing: whatever memory cannot contain should be committed to the "waste blanks," and those thoughts — "children nurs'd, deliver'd from thy brain" — will reward the writer with a new acquaintance with his own mind when re-read. The couplet is warmly practical: these "offices" will profit the beloved and enrich his book.

Key Themes

  • Practical response to time: Instead of lamenting time's destruction, the speaker offers instruments for awareness and engagement.
  • Writing as self-knowledge: The blank book is a tool for preserving memory and thought against time's theft.
  • The chapter's conclusion: The chapter moves from fearful meditation on time to purposeful, active engagement with it.

Key Literary Devices

  • Triple conceit: Three objects (glass, dial, book) are each extended into metaphor.
  • Metaphor of childbirth: Written thoughts are "children nurs'd, deliver'd from thy brain" — the mind gives birth to written ideas.
  • Personification: Time is "thievish," its progress a stealthy theft toward eternity.

Vocabulary

  • Dial: A sundial
  • Waste blanks: Empty, blank pages
  • Shady stealth: The imperceptible, creeping movement of the sundial's shadow
  • Offices: Duties, habitual practices, useful tasks
  • Enrich: To make richer; to increase in value or depth
