Study Guide: Sonnet LXVIII

Summary

Sonnet LXVIII continues the theme of Sonnet LXVII, presenting the beloved's face as a "map" of an outworn, authentic past. The sonnet contrasts two historical eras: a former time when beauty lived and died naturally, before artificial cosmetics ("bastard signs of fair") existed, and before the golden hair of corpses was cut from graves to make wigs for the living. In that earlier, purer era, beauty was genuine — it did not borrow from others, did not adorn itself with another's vitality. The beloved embodies that earlier era's values: he is "Without all ornament, itself and true," making no summer of another's green, robbing no old to dress his beauty new. The couplet states Nature's strategy: she stores the beloved as a living map to show "false Art what beauty was of yore."

Key Themes

  • Authentic versus artificial beauty: The beloved represents natural, unadorned beauty against a cosmetically falsified age.
  • The beloved as living museum: Nature preserves the beloved as a display of what genuine beauty once looked like.
  • Moral condemnation of cosmetic artifice: Borrowing beauty from the dead is framed as a moral desecration.

Key Literary Devices

  • Map metaphor: The beloved's face is a map or faithful record of a past era.
  • Temporal structure: "Before...before...ere..." creates a sequence distinguishing genuine past from corrupt present.
  • Contrast: Natural beauty (the beloved) versus "bastard signs," "false painting," and borrowed "golden tresses."

Vocabulary

  • Bastard signs: Illegitimate, false marks of beauty
  • Sepulchres: Tombs, burial vaults
  • Shorn: Cut away
  • Fleece: Hair, here used of the dead's hair made into wigs
  • Yore: Former times, the past
