Study Guide: Sonnet XXXVII

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet XXXVII introduces the figure of the "decrepit father" who vicariously delights in his active child's youthful deeds — the speaker's self-portrait as someone disabled by Fortune ("made lame by Fortune's dearest spite") who draws all comfort from the beloved's abundant worth. The beloved is endowed with beauty, birth, wealth, and wit, any of which — or all combined — sits "crowned" in their person.

The speaker's response to his own poverty is not despair but a strategy of "engrafting": attaching his love like a graft onto the beloved's vigorous stock so that he draws life and sufficiency from the beloved's abundance. The paradox of the "shadow" line (12) — "this shadow doth such substance give" — crystallizes the chapter's title: the speaker IS the shadow, yet through love he finds real substance.

The couplet achieves genuine joy: wishing the best for the beloved, and knowing that wish is one he already has, produces a happiness "ten times" magnified by love's arithmetic of mutual possession.

Key Literary Devices

  • Simile: The decrepit father and active child open the poem with a domestically vivid image of vicarious participation.
  • Paradox: A shadow providing substance — insubstantial reflected glory that nevertheless nourishes and satisfies.
  • Horticultural metaphor: "Engrafted" — grafting joins a weak cutting to a strong host; the speaker's love is such a graft.
  • Hyperbole: "Ten times happy me" — the arithmetic of love multiplies ordinary happiness.

Themes

Shadow and substance: The speaker embraces his shadowed condition but discovers that being a shadow attached to such abundant light provides real, sufficing substance.

Vicarious joy as genuine joy: The father-child simile suggests that loving participation in another's excellence is not secondhand or inferior — it is a full and legitimate form of happiness.

Fortune versus love: Fortune has lamed and impoverished the speaker; love for the beloved restores everything Fortune took, through the logic of spiritual union and shared identity.
