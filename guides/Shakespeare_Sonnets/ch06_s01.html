Study Guide: Sonnet XLIII

Overview

Sonnet XLIII opens Chapter 6's meditation on love from afar with a striking visual paradox: the speaker sees most clearly when his eyes are shut in sleep. During the day, his waking gaze finds nothing worth looking at, yet in dreams the beloved appears "darkly bright" — a luminous figure who turns darkness into light.

Meaning and Argument

The sonnet moves through three logical steps. First, the speaker observes that sleep gives better vision than waking (lines 1–4). Second, he reasons that if the beloved's mere shadow (dream-image) can illuminate the night, how dazzling his actual presence in daylight would be (lines 5–12). Third, the closing couplet inverts the entire natural order: days are like nights without the beloved, and nights become bright days when dreams reveal him.

Key Literary Devices

  • Paradox / Oxymoron: "When most I wink, then do mine eyes best see"; "darkly bright"; "sightless eyes" — the poem is built on deliberate contradictions that capture the disorienting power of longing.
  • Antanaclasis: "thy shadow's form form happy show" repeats "form" as both noun and verb.
  • Light imagery: "bright," "clearer light," "shade shines" — the beloved is consistently associated with radiance that no earthly light source can match.

Themes

The sonnet explores absence and imaginative compensation: physical separation cannot silence desire, which finds expression through the mind's eye. It also touches on the supremacy of inner vision, suggesting that love creates a sight more reliable than the senses. The couplet's reversal — days as nights, nights as days — is a hallmark of Petrarchan conceits applied here to celebrate the compensating power of dreams.
