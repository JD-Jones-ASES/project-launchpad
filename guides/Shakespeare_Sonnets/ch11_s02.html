Farewell to the Fair Youth: Sonnets 117-120

Overview

This section explores Sonnets 117 through 120, a cluster of poems often termed the "accusation group" where the speaker offers apologies and self-justifications to the Fair Youth. These sonnets utilize legal and medical metaphors to examine themes of neglect, perceived betrayal, and eventual reconciliation, culminating in a nuanced understanding of mutual trespass and forgiveness.

Key Concepts

  • The Rhetoric of Apology and Self-Justification: Sonnets 117-120 showcase the speaker's attempts to explain his actions, particularly his perceived distance or infidelity, using sophisticated rhetorical strategies. This includes inviting accusation (prolepsis) before offering a reinterpretation of events.
  • Legal Metaphors in Love: Throughout these sonnets, the language of courts, contracts, and obligations (e.g., "bonds," "proof," "appeal," "trespass," "ransom") is used to describe the emotional and moral dynamics of the relationship, framing guilt and pardon as legal transactions.
  • Perverse Logic and Self-Inflicted Illness (Sonnet 118): The speaker employs a specious medical analogy, claiming to have sought "bitter sauces" or "sickness" when sated with the youth's "sweetness," as a misguided preventive measure. This highlights a self-destructive tendency and a warped understanding of "policy" in love.
  • The Benefit of Ill (Sonnet 119): This paradoxical concept suggests that suffering, error, and destructive experiences (the "madding fever" and "Siren tears") can ultimately lead to a deeper understanding, purification, and strengthening of true love. Ruined love, when rebuilt, becomes "fairer... more strong, far greater."
  • Mutual Ransom and Reconciliation (Sonnet 120): The most psychologically subtle of the group, this sonnet proposes a profound concept of mutual forgiveness. It suggests that each party's past "trespass" or unkindness serves as a "fee" or "ransom" for the other's, creating an equilibrium of shared guilt and pardon that restores the relationship.

Important Terms

  • scanted: Stinted; given too little of. Implies a measured shortfall rather than outright neglect.
  • bonds: Obligations and legal ties. Carries both emotional weight and a precise legal sense.
  • dear-purchas'd right: A right dearly bought, at great cost; something earned and owed.
  • level of your frown: Within range, as of a weapon aimed level. To be taken aim at.
  • surmise, accumulate: To add conjecture upon conjecture, building a flimsy case from unproven assumptions.
  • appeal: A legal term for a formal plea or counter-argument; a formal defense.
  • prolepsis: A classical rhetorical strategy of anticipating and answering objections or accusations before they are raised by an opponent.
  • eager compounds: Sharp, bitter medicinal preparations, often herbal mixtures. "Eager" means keen-edged or tart.
  • purge: To take a purgative; to cleanse the body by forced evacuation, a standard Galenic remedy.
  • meetness: Fitness; appropriateness; suitability.
  • policy: Worldly cunning; calculated strategy. Often carried a slightly sinister connotation in Shakespeare's time, suggesting manipulation.
  • rank of goodness: Surfeited or sickened by goodness; "rank" meaning swollen, overfull, or corrupt from excess.
  • fell sick of you: Became ill; but also, fell ill because of you, highlighting the paradox of the youth's overwhelming goodness.
  • Siren tears: The deceptive, enchanting weeping of an enchantress. Refers to the Sirens of classical myth who lured sailors to their destruction.
  • limbecks: Alembics; alchemical distillation vessels used to purify or concentrate substances. Here, "foul" implies something vile being refined into its deceptive essence.
  • spheres (eyes out of their spheres): In Ptolemaic cosmology, planets moved in crystalline spheres. Eyes "out of their spheres" implies being jolted from their proper orbits due to astonishment or madness.
  • madding fever: A feverish frenzy; a maddening heat or derangement.
  • benefit of ill: Profit or advantage gained through suffering; a paradox where adversity leads to greater good.
  • befriends me now: Proves useful to me now; serves as a benefit in the present.
  • nerves: Sinews; tendons.
  • ransom: A sum paid for release; to free from captivity or obligation by payment.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet 117: Invitation to Accusation and Reinterpretation

  • Confession and Self-Accusation: The speaker invites the youth to list his faults: "scanted" (failed to give enough), "forgot" calls, spent time with "unknown minds," gave away the youth's "dear-purchas'd right," and "hoisted sail" away from the youth. He asks the youth to "Book both my wilfulness and errors down" and accumulate "surmise" (conjecture) to bring him within range of a "frown."
  • The Volta and Defense: The turn (volta) in the final lines offers a surprising defense. The speaker claims his actions were not betrayal, but a deliberate "appeal" (formal plea/counter-argument) to "strive to prove / The constancy and virtue of your love." The recklessness is reframed as an experiment.
  • Rhetorical Ingenuity vs. Genuine Self-Knowledge: The poem deliberately leaves open whether this defense is sincere self-knowledge or a clever rhetorical maneuver.
  • Legal Metaphors: The sonnet is steeped in legal language: "deserts repay," "bonds," "dear-purchas'd right," "book," "errors," "proof," "surmise, accumulate," "level of your frown," "appeal." This elevates a personal dispute to a courtroom argument.
  • Context: The Rhetoric of Apology: Sonnet 117 is part of the "accusation group" (117-120), characterized by apologies and self-justifications using legal vocabulary. Shakespeare uses prolepsis—inviting accusation before disarming it—a standard oratorical move, but imbued with emotional texture.
  • Connections: The legal framing anticipates Sonnet 120's "exchange-economy of reconciliation." The device of inviting accusation echoes Sonnet 49 (poet imagines youth's reasons for desertion) and Sonnet 88 (poet argues against himself). It contrasts with the defiant stance of Sonnet 121.

Sonnet 118: Medical Analogy and Perverse Policy

  • Medical Analogy: The speaker explains his straying through a twisted medical analogy. Just as one might take "eager compounds" (bitter medicines) to sharpen a dull appetite or "purge" to prevent unseen illnesses, he sought out "bitter sauces" (unpleasant company or experiences) when "full of your ne'er-cloying sweetness." He was "sick of welfare," finding "meetness" (appropriateness) in self-inflicted sickness.
  • Faulty Logic and "Policy": This "policy" (calculated strategy, here with negative connotations of cunning) was intended to "anticipate" non-existent "ills." However, it "grew to faults assur'd," bringing a "healthful state" to "medicine" (cure it) when it was already "rank of goodness" (overfull or corrupted by excess of good).
  • Bitter Reversal (Couplet): The couplet admits the self-defeating nature of this strategy. The "cure" proved more poisonous than the condition it was meant to treat: "Drugs poison him that so fell sick of you." The youth's goodness was overwhelming, and the attempted "prevention" caused the disease.
  • Connections: The medical conceit links to broader health and sickness imagery in the sonnet sequence. The idea that excess of good drives one to seek bad is a dark inversion of the temperance arguments in the procreation sonnets (1-17), where beauty hoarded was a sickness. The self-condemnation foreshadows the self-disgust in Sonnet 129 (Dark Lady sequence).

Sonnet 119: Self-Accusation, Madness, and the "Benefit of Ill"

  • Wild Self-Accusation: The speaker recounts a period of profound error and self-deception. He describes drinking "Siren tears" (deceptive, treacherous weeping) "Distill'd from limbecks foul" (derived from a corrupt, hellish source). This led to a state where he was "Applying fears to hopes, and hopes to fears," constantly losing even when he thought he was winning. His heart committed "wretched errors," and his "eyes out of their spheres been fitted" (jolted from their proper place) in the "distraction of this madding fever."
  • The Paradoxical "Benefit of Ill" (Volta): The turn begins with the exclamation "O benefit of ill!" The speaker realizes that suffering and destructive infatuation, by purging him of illusion, have ultimately strengthened his true love. The adversity has taught him a profound truth: "better is, by evil still made better; / And ruin'd love, when it is built anew, / Grows fairer than at first, more strong, far greater."
  • Reconciliation and Gain (Couplet): The poem concludes with an accounting image. The speaker returns "rebuk'd" but has gained "thrice more than I have spent" through this difficult experience. His folly, though costly, led to invaluable growth and a deeper appreciation of his true love.
  • Connections: The paradox of "ruin'd love" growing "fairer" echoes themes of estrangement and return (Chapters 8 and 9) and anticipates the mutual ransom of Sonnet 120. The alchemical imagery of distillation links to broader themes of transformation. The destructive female figure of the Siren recalls the "Dark Lady" sonnets (e.g., 133, 137).

Sonnet 120: Mutual Trespass and Reconciliation

  • The Utility of Past Hurt: The speaker reflects on the youth's past unkindness, acknowledging that "That you were once unkind befriends me now." This past suffering was a gift, teaching him what it felt like to be wounded—knowledge he wished he had applied sooner. He recognizes that he "Needs must I under my transgression bow" (submit to the weight of his own wrongdoing) because his "nerves" (sinews, strength) are not "brass or hammer'd steel" (invulnerable).
  • Empathy and Shared Suffering: He realizes that if the youth was "shaken" by his unkindness, just as he was by the youth's, then the youth "pass'd a hell of time." He admits to having been a "tyrant" who "no leisure taken / To weigh how once I suffer'd in your crime." There's a recognition of mutual pain and responsibility.
  • The Wish for Timely Comfort: The speaker laments that their "night of woe" didn't immediately remind him "how hard true sorrow hits," so he could have "tender'd / The humble salve" (offered comfort) to the youth, as the youth had once done for him.
  • Mutual Ransom (Couplet): The sonnet beautifully resolves the mutual accounting with a legal conceit: "But that your trespass now becomes a fee; / Mine ransoms yours, and yours must ransom me." Each party's past offense becomes the "ransom" (payment for release) that clears the other's debt. Guilt and pardon cancel each other out, restoring balance and allowing for mutual forgiveness and equilibrium in the relationship.

Review Questions

  1. In Sonnet 117, the speaker claims his "wilfulness and errors" were a deliberate attempt to "prove / The constancy and virtue of your love." Is this a convincing defense, or an elegant evasion of responsibility? What does it suggest about how the speaker understands the nature of love and fidelity?
  2. The "accusation group" (Sonnets 117-120) extensively uses legal vocabulary (bonds, proof, appeal, trespass, ransom) to describe an intimate relationship. What effect does this legal framing have on our understanding of the love between the speaker and the youth? Does it enhance or diminish the emotional depth of their bond?
  3. Sonnet 118 employs a medical analogy where the speaker seeks out "bitter sauces" when "sick of welfare." Evaluate the effectiveness and implications of this analogy. What does it reveal about the speaker's self-destructive tendencies and his "policy" in love?
  4. The phrase "O benefit of ill!" in Sonnet 119 introduces a paradox where suffering and error lead to a stronger love. Discuss how the speaker's experience of "Siren tears" and "madding fever" ultimately contributes to this "benefit." How does this idea challenge conventional notions of love and adversity?
  5. Sonnet 120 culminates in the idea of "mutual ransom," where each party's trespass becomes the fee for the other's. Explain this concept of reciprocal forgiveness and reconciliation. What psychological insights does it offer into overcoming conflict and restoring balance in a relationship?
