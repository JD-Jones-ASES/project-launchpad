Study Guide: Sonnet CXVIII

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet CXVIII continues the apology sequence by explaining the psychological logic behind the speaker's straying. Using extended culinary and medical analogies, he argues that just as people sharpen a dulled appetite with bitter compounds, or purge themselves to prevent future illness, he sought "bitter sauces" to counteract the youth's "ne'er-cloying sweetness" and preemptively treated a love-sickness he feared might develop. The irony the speaker confesses is devastating: his "policy in love" — a calculated strategy of prophylactic bitterness — turned what was genuinely healthy into something requiring a cure that did not exist. Worse, the very drugs he sought became poison.

The couplet delivers the lesson with bitter precision: "Drugs poison him that so fell sick of you." The one who was only metaphorically sick from the abundance of the youth's goodness found that the actual remedies (infidelity and straying) were lethal.

Literary Devices

  • Extended simile: The culinary and medical comparisons (lines 1-10) build an elaborate analogy that renders the speaker's logic comprehensible even as it indicts it.
  • Irony: The preventive medicine becomes the disease — a structural irony central to the poem's argument.
  • Personification of policy: "Policy in love" is treated as an agent that "grew to faults assur'd."

Themes of Farewell and Apology

This sonnet is the most intellectually elaborate of the apologies — the speaker does not merely confess but attempts to diagnose the reasoning behind his fault. The diagnosis is self-condemnatory: the strategy was clever but catastrophically wrong. The apology gains weight precisely because the speaker understands his own error clearly.
