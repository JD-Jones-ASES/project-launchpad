Sonnet C — Study Guide

Overview

Sonnet C opens Chapter 10 with a sharp rebuke of the poet's own Muse. After a period of creative silence, the speaker demands that his Muse return to work and dedicate itself to singing his beloved's praises. The apostrophe — direct address to the absent Muse — gives the poem an urgent, almost scolding energy.

Meaning

The speaker charges that the Muse has been wasting its talent on "worthless" subjects while neglecting the one person truly worthy of praise. He commands it to "return," "redeem" the lost time, and survey the beloved's face for any signs of age inflicted by Time. If wrinkles have appeared, the Muse must turn satirist and expose Time's cruelty. The couplet delivers the ultimate commission: give the beloved fame at a rate that outpaces Time's destruction.

Literary Devices

  • Apostrophe: The entire poem addresses the Muse as if she were a negligent servant.
  • Personification: Time appears as a harvester armed with "scythe and crooked knife."
  • Imagery: "Wrinkle graven" evokes Time as a stone-carver slowly defacing beauty.
  • Imperative mood: A series of commands — "Return," "Sing," "Rise," "survey," "Give" — builds urgency.

Themes of Constancy and Faithfulness

Even as the Muse has strayed, the speaker remains loyal to the beloved and insists that poetry must be loyal too. The sonnet frames verse as the most faithful weapon against Time's inevitable assault. This introduces Chapter 10's central tension: the struggle to maintain constancy in love and in artistic dedication despite distraction and decay.
