Study Guide: Sonnet XCVI

Overview

Sonnet XCVI considers the beloved's faults from a social rather than purely personal perspective. Where XCV described the speaker's own perception, XCVI notes that all of society — high and low — is divided between seeing the beloved's behaviors as faults and seeing them as graces. The beloved's charm is so transformative that he literally converts faults into virtues in the eyes of observers. The queen's ring and wolf-in-lamb analogies develop this idea before the couplet reveals the speaker's personal stake.

Key Themes

The central theme is the social consequences of irresistible charm. The beloved's capacity to make faults seem like graces is not merely personal; it operates at the level of public perception across all social ranks. The wolf analogy intensifies the warning: if the beloved deployed the full strength of his position and charm to lead people astray, he would be practically irresistible. The couplet then reveals why the speaker cares: because the beloved is "mine," his reputation is the speaker's reputation.

Poetic Devices

The anaphora of "some say... some say" mirrors XCVII's catalog technique but applies it to competing social interpretations. The queen's ring simile works as a transferred analogy: the base jewel elevated by the queen's status parallels the beloved's errors elevated by his personal magnificence. The wolf/lamb conditional — "how many lambs might the stern wolf betray" — gives the warning its sharpest edge, framing the beloved's potential behavior as predatory.

Estrangement Dimension

XCVI marks the end of the moral-warning sub-sequence (XCIV-XCVI) and reveals the speaker's continued investment in the beloved through self-interest. The chapter then pivots in XCVII to the entirely different register of absence poetry and seasonal elegy.
