Farewell to the Fair Youth: A Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the final ten poems (Sonnets 117-126) of Shakespeare's "Fair Youth" sequence. These sonnets do not present a dramatic climax but rather a gradual and regretful withdrawal, characterized by the poet's apologies, justifications, and quiet acknowledgments of past errors. The sequence concludes with a formal leave-taking, paving the way for the subsequent "Dark Lady" sonnets.

Key Concepts

  • Gradual Withdrawal and Ending: The close of the Fair Youth sequence is presented as a "gradual, rueful withdrawal" rather than an abrupt climax, marked by a series of reckonings and apologies.
  • Contrite but Justified Posture: The poet adopts a contrite stance, confessing neglect and "love-madness," but his apologies are often accompanied by arguments and defenses, framing guilt within the context of the bond's strength.
  • Legal and Commercial Metaphors: Guilt, reconciliation, and the nature of the relationship are frequently explored through metaphors of debt and repayment, using terms like "bonds," "ransoms," "adjuncts," and "audits."
  • Reciprocity of Pardon (Sonnet 120): This sonnet highlights a rare instance of mutual forgiveness, where the youth's past unkindness is used to cancel the poet's own debt, creating a shared currency of pardon.
  • Defiance Against Slander (Sonnet 121): One of the most defiant poems, it asserts the speaker's self-definition ("I am that I am") and rejects external judgment, turning accusations of vice back onto the accusers.
  • The Envoi and Formal Leave-Taking (Sonnet 126): The sequence concludes with a unique twelve-line poem, not a strict sonnet, serving as a formal farewell (envoi) to the Fair Youth, with a poignant warning about Time's inevitable claim.

Important Terms

  • Fair Youth Sequence: The collection of Shakespearean sonnets (traditionally 1-126) primarily addressed to a beautiful young man, exploring themes of love, beauty, time, and mortality.
  • Contrite: Feeling or expressing remorse or penitence; apologetic.
  • Abject: Completely without pride or dignity; self-abasing. The text notes the poet's apologies are not abject, implying they retain a degree of self-respect or argument.
  • Legal/Commercial Metaphors: Figurative language drawing from law or business (e.g., bonds, ransoms, audits) used to describe emotional or relational dynamics, such as guilt, obligation, and forgiveness.
  • Reciprocity: The practice of exchanging things with others for mutual benefit, especially privileges granted by one country or organization to another. In this context, mutual forgiveness or exchange of past wrongs.
  • Defiant: Openly resistant; challenging authority or convention.
  • Envoi: A short stanza concluding a poem, used either to address an important person or to summarize the preceding text. In Sonnet 126, it serves as a formal farewell.
  • Audit: An official inspection of accounts or records. Here, "Nature's audit" refers to the inevitable reckoning or accounting that Time demands from all living things.
  • Dark Lady Sequence: The subsequent section of Shakespeare's sonnets (traditionally 127-152), addressed to a mysterious and often scornful woman.

Detailed Notes

The Closing of the Fair Youth Sequence

The final ten sonnets (117-126) mark the definitive end of the poet's intense relationship with the Fair Youth. This conclusion is not abrupt or climactic but rather a "gradual, rueful withdrawal." The poems function as a series of apologies, justifications, and quiet "reckonings," designed to clear the emotional air before the youth disappears from the poetic landscape.

The Poet's Contrition and Defense

Throughout this concluding group, the poet's primary "posture" is one of contrition. He makes several explicit confessions:

  • Neglect (Sonnet 117): Acknowledges periods of absence or lack of attention.
  • Seeking "bitter sauces" (Sonnet 118): Suggests he pursued other, perhaps less wholesome, experiences or affections as a counterpoint to the sweetness of his love for the youth.
  • Love-Madness (Sonnet 119): Admits to having been lost in an obsessive or unbalanced love before returning to a more "chastened" devotion.

Importantly, these apologies are rarely simple or "abject." Shakespeare characteristically intertwines confession with a defense. The poet often constructs an argument to justify his actions, reframing them or mitigating his guilt.

Legal and Commercial Framing of Relationships

A significant feature of these sonnets is the pervasive use of legal and commercial metaphors. The language of debt and repayment, guilt and reconciliation, is frequently employed:

  • Bonds: Obligations or agreements.
  • Ransoms: Payments made to secure release from captivity or obligation.
  • Adjuncts: Something added or connected as a subordinate part.
  • Audits: An official examination of accounts. These metaphors frame the poet's transgressions and subsequent attempts at reconciliation as a balancing of accounts, a negotiation of contractual obligations, or a repayment of a debt.

Standout Sonnets in the Group

Three sonnets are specifically highlighted for their unique contributions:

  1. Sonnet 117: This poem boldly opens with "Accuse me thus," inviting accusations of wrongdoing. However, it quickly pivots, reinterpreting the poet's "straying" or neglect not as a betrayal, but as a test of the strength and resilience of his bond with the youth, ultimately serving as proof of his profound love.

  2. Sonnet 120: This sonnet achieves a rare "genuine reciprocity." Instead of simply forgiving the youth's past unkindness, the poet leverages it. The youth's previous wrongs become an "entry" in a "ledger" that effectively cancels out the poet's own debt. Their "shared wounds" are transformed into a "currency of mutual pardon," suggesting a more balanced and mature understanding of their complex relationship.

  3. Sonnet 121: Described as "arguably the most defiant poem in the entire sequence," this sonnet features the powerful declaration, "I am that I am." Here, the speaker vehemently refuses to allow "slanderous observers" to define his inner life or morality. He turns the accusations of vice back upon his accusers, suggesting that those who judge him are themselves corrupt or hypocritical. It's a profound assertion of personal autonomy and self-truth.

The Envoi: Sonnet 126 and the Farewell

The sequence famously closes with Sonnet 126, which is distinct from the other sonnets in its form:

  • Not a Strict Sonnet: It consists of only twelve lines, structured as six rhyming couplets, lacking the characteristic fourteen lines and the final "turn" or volta of a traditional Shakespearean sonnet.
  • An Envoi: This twelve-line structure functions as an "envoi," a formal leave-taking or concluding address.
  • Address to Nature: The poem addresses Nature itself as the "sovereign mistress" of the youth. Nature is depicted as having protected her "lovely boy" from the ravages of time.
  • The Quiet Warning: The poem ends with a "single, quiet warning": Nature's "audit must eventually be answered." This implies that even Nature's power to preserve beauty is temporary, and the youth will ultimately succumb to time's inevitable demands.
  • Transition: With this poignant and formal farewell, the Fair Youth "vanishes" from the sequence, signaling the transition to the "darker world of the Dark Lady" in the subsequent sonnets.

Review Questions

  1. How do Sonnets 117-126, as a group, contribute to the sense of an "ending" for the Fair Youth sequence? What specific elements or themes create this feeling of withdrawal rather than a dramatic conclusion?
  2. Describe the poet's typical approach to apologizing in these closing sonnets. How do his apologies differ from a purely "abject" confession, and what does this reveal about his character or his relationship with the youth?
  3. Explain the significance of "legal and commercial metaphors" (e.g., bonds, ransoms, audits) in this group of sonnets. How do these metaphors shape our understanding of guilt, reconciliation, and the nature of the relationship between the poet and the youth?
  4. Choose two of the "stand out" sonnets (117, 120, or 121) and explain what makes each poem particularly unique or important within the context of the closing sequence. How does each sonnet contribute to the overall themes of apology, justification, or self-assertion?
  5. Discuss the unusual form and content of Sonnet 126. Why might Shakespeare have chosen to conclude the Fair Youth sequence with a twelve-line "envoi" rather than a traditional fourteen-line sonnet? What is the impact of its final "quiet warning" and its role in transitioning to the next part of the collection?
