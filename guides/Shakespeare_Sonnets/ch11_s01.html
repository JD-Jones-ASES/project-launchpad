Study Guide: Sonnet CXVII

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet CXVII opens Chapter 11's sequence of farewell and apology with a bold rhetorical move: the speaker preemptively invites the Fair Youth to catalog every grievance against him. He confesses to "scanting" what he owed the youth — failing to call upon his love, associating with "unknown minds," giving to others the time that rightfully belonged to the youth, and deliberately sailing as far from the youth's sight as possible. Rather than waiting for accusation, the speaker accepts it wholesale, even asking the youth to accumulate further surmise beyond what is proven ("on just proof surmise, accumulate").

The sestet makes a crucial turn: the speaker accepts the frown of justified displeasure but begs the youth not to unleash "waken'd hate." The final couplet offers the speaker's defense — his "appeal" — that the straying was itself a test of the constancy and virtue of the youth's love.

Literary Devices

  • Legal metaphor: The vocabulary of indictment, proof, and appeal structures the entire sonnet; the speaker acts as both prosecutor and defendant.
  • Nautical metaphor: "Hoisted sail to all the winds" renders the straying as deliberate flight from the youth.
  • Paradox: Inviting accusation as a form of defense is rhetorically audacious — control is maintained by preempting the charge.

Themes of Farewell and Apology

This sonnet inaugurates the chapter's confessional mode. The apology is strategic: by naming every fault himself, the speaker occupies the moral high ground while still asking for mercy. The test-of-love defense in the couplet will remain ethically ambiguous — whether it genuinely redeems the straying or merely rationalizes it is a question the sequence leaves deliberately open.
