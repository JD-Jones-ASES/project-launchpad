Study Guide: Sonnet LXXIII

Summary

Sonnet LXXIII is widely regarded as one of Shakespeare's supreme achievements. It presents the speaker's aging in three progressively compressed metaphors, each one bringing death closer. The first quatrain shows "that time of year" — late autumn — with yellow or bare boughs, ruined choir lofts where birds once sang. The second quatrain compresses to a single day: twilight after sunset fading in the west, swallowed by black night, "Death's second self." The third quatrain compresses further still, to a single moment: the glowing of dying embers on the ashes of consumed youth, the fire expiring on its own death-bed, "consum'd with that which it was nourish'd by." Each metaphor is more intimate and urgent than the last. The couplet completes the argument: perceiving this, the beloved loves more strongly — because awareness of approaching loss intensifies love for what must soon be left.

Key Themes

  • Three metaphors of mortality: Autumn, twilight, and dying fire represent the speaker's aging in escalating urgency.
  • Intensification through compression: Each metaphor narrows the timeframe, creating a mounting sense of imminence.
  • Love strengthened by loss: The couplet's paradox — perceiving decline strengthens love — is the sonnet's emotional climax.

Key Literary Devices

  • Triple conceit: Three extended metaphors in three quatrains — the sonnet's structural masterstroke.
  • Paradox: The fire is consumed by what nourished it; approaching loss intensifies love.
  • Allusion: "Bare ruin'd choirs" evokes the dissolution of the monasteries under Henry VIII.

Vocabulary

  • Choirs: The choir loft sections of a church; arched wooden structures
  • Fadeth: Fades, diminishes
  • Seals up: Closes completely, as in sealing a tomb
  • Expire: To breathe one's last; to die
  • Consum'd: Consumed, destroyed by its own fuel
