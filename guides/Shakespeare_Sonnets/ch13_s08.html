Study Guide: Sonnet CXLIX

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet CXLIX is a catalog of self-abasements presented as evidence of love. The speaker challenges the Dark Lady's claim that he does not love her by enumerating the ways he has betrayed himself on her behalf: siding against himself, refusing friendship with her enemies, fawning on those she favors, punishing himself when she scowls. The third quatrain's admission — "all my best doth worship thy defect" — is the sequence's most extreme statement of voluntary self-degradation: his finest qualities are enslaved to adoring her flaws. The couplet delivers a bitter insight rather than a resolution: having told her to "hate on," he acknowledges that her love goes to those who see clearly, while he is blind — the usual Petrarchan reward system perfectly inverted.

Key Poetic Devices

  • Rhetorical question cascade: the octave is built of interrogatives, accumulating evidence of self-betrayal
  • Antithesis and inversion: Petrarchan conventions (the beloved merits worship) are inverted — he worships her "defect"
  • "Commanded by the motion of thine eyes": her nonverbal power over his will, reducing him to an automaton
  • Ironic command "hate on": resigned permission for her to continue as she is — a defeated acceptance

Central Themes

Sin and shame: The speaker's shame is not only at sin but at the completeness of his surrender to someone he knows to be unworthy. Self-abasement and moral struggle: He actively abandons his own best interests, friendships, and self-respect — a form of moral self-destruction driven by desire. Blindness and knowledge: The couplet's insight is that she loves the clear-sighted; his blindness disqualifies him from her affection, creating the cruelest irony of the sequence — love's blindness prevents the love it seeks.
