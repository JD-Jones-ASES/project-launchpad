The Life and Times of William Shakespeare

Overview

This section provides a concise overview of William Shakespeare's life, from his birth in Stratford-upon-Avon and rigorous classical education to his emergence as a prominent playwright and actor in London's bustling theatre scene. It details his family life, the mysterious "lost years," his unique role as a shareholder in the Lord Chamberlain's Men, and his eventual prosperous retirement and death, emphasizing the influences that shaped his unparalleled literary output.

Key Concepts

  • Classical Education as Foundation: Shakespeare's grammar school curriculum, focused on Latin authors like Ovid and Virgil, provided an "inexhaustible" literary foundation that profoundly influenced his themes, rhetoric, and dramatic structures. This intense classical training deeply embedded the cadences and narratives that would reappear in his later works.
  • The "Lost Years" and Ambition: The period between 1585 and 1592, for which little biographical evidence exists, likely represents Shakespeare's strategic move to London, driven by ambition to participate in the burgeoning professional theatre world. This transition from a market town to the capital was crucial for his career development.
  • Shareholder-Actor-Playwright Model: Unlike many contemporaries, Shakespeare's unique position as an actor and shareholder in his company (the Lord Chamberlain's Men/King's Men) gave him a direct financial stake in its success and shaped his writing for immediate performance rather than solely for the printed page. This dual role fostered a practical understanding of theatrical production.
  • The Popularity and Diversity of Elizabethan Theatre: The theatre was a truly popular art form, drawing audiences from all social strata, from apprentices in the yard to nobles in the lords' rooms. This diverse audience contributed to the extraordinary tonal breadth and universality of Shakespeare's plays, enabling him to blend comedy and tragedy and address complex ideas accessibly.
  • Patronage System: While commercially successful, Shakespeare also participated in the traditional patronage system, dedicating early narrative poems to noble patrons like the Earl of Southampton. This highlights the dual economic realities for artists of the era, who sought both commercial success and aristocratic support.
  • Evolution of Career and Themes: His career trajectory, from early narrative poems and historical plays to intensely dramatic tragedies and later "romances," shows a conscious artistic development. The late plays often return to themes of reconciliation, loss, and recovery with a more reflective and mellow tone, rounding off his extraordinary career.

Important Terms

  • Stratford-upon-Avon: The market town in Warwickshire, England, where William Shakespeare was born, raised, and eventually retired and died, serving as a constant anchor throughout his life.
  • King's New School: The rigorous grammar school in Stratford-upon-Avon where Shakespeare received a classical education, studying Latin authors and rhetoric, which formed the bedrock of his literary knowledge.
  • The "Lost Years": The undocumented period between 1585 (baptism of his twins) and 1592 (first evidence of his presence in London) in Shakespeare's life, filled with biographical speculation about his activities before he became known as a playwright.
  • Lord Chamberlain's Men (later King's Men): The premier acting company in London that Shakespeare joined around 1594, in which he was both an actor and a significant shareholder. The company was renamed King's Men in 1603 after receiving royal patronage from King James I.
  • Bankside: The area in Southwark, just south of the Thames in London, where many of the professional theatres, including the Globe and the Rose, were located. Its location outside city limits allowed theatres to operate with fewer restrictions from civic authorities.
  • Patronage System: A traditional method of financial and social support for artists, writers, and musicians by wealthy individuals or institutions. Shakespeare engaged with this system by dedicating his early narrative poems to the Earl of Southampton.
  • New Place: A grand house in Stratford-upon-Avon that Shakespeare purchased in 1597. This acquisition symbolized his financial success and his intention to maintain roots in his hometown, eventually returning there for retirement.
  • Late Romances: A group of Shakespeare's final plays (e.g., Pericles, Cymbeline, The Winter's Tale, The Tempest) characterized by their blend of tragic and comedic elements, themes of reconciliation, loss, and recovery, and often a more magical or fantastical tone.

Detailed Notes

Early Life and Family Background (1564-1582)

William Shakespeare was born in April 1564 in the market town of Stratford-upon-Avon, Warwickshire. While his baptism was recorded on April 26th, tradition commonly fixes his birth date as April 23rd, St. George's Day. He was the third child of John Shakespeare, a successful glover who also held significant civic offices including high bailiff, and Mary Arden, who came from a prosperous local farming family. The Shakespeare family was comfortable, though not wealthy, and their house on Henley Street where William was raised is preserved today.

Education and Intellectual Foundation

Shakespeare attended Stratford's King's New School, a rigorous grammar school that sons of burgesses, like John Shakespeare, could attend without fee. His education was intensely classical, focusing heavily on Latin authors such as Ovid, Virgil, and Cicero, alongside Latin grammarians. Students spent six days a week developing skills in rhetoric, declamation, debate, and moral philosophy through the Stoics, absorbing the rhythms and cadences of Roman poetry. Although there's no evidence Shakespeare attended university (then primarily for the clergy and gentry), his grammar school training provided an "inexhaustible" literary foundation, particularly evident in the pervasive influence of Ovid's Metamorphoses throughout his plays and sonnets.

Marriage and Family Life

In November 1582, at the age of eighteen, Shakespeare married Anne Hathaway of Shottery, a village a mile outside Stratford. Anne was eight years his senior, at twenty-six, and already three months pregnant, necessitating a special license for their wedding to proceed before the usual calling of banns. Their first child, Susanna, was baptized in May 1583, followed by twins, a son named Hamnet and a daughter named Judith, in February 1585. For much of his adult working life, Shakespeare would reside in London while Anne and their children remained in Stratford. His will's famous bequest of his "second-best bed" to his wife has led to centuries of biographical speculation about their relationship, with interpretations ranging from dismissive to deeply personal, acknowledging that the best bed was often reserved for guests.

The "Lost Years" (1585-1592)

A significant biographical gap, known as the "lost years," exists between the baptism of Shakespeare's twins in 1585 and the first solid evidence of his presence in London in 1592. Speculation has filled this void with various theories, from him working as a schoolmaster or soldier to traveling on the Continent. However, the most likely explanation is that a young man of Shakespeare's ambition and ability made his way to London, the only city in England where professional theatre was a commercial enterprise capable of supporting professional writers. By 1592, he was already successful enough to be perceived as a threat by established playwrights, as evidenced by Robert Greene's warning about an "upstart crow, beautified with our feathers."

Arrival in London and the Theatre World

The London Shakespeare entered was a vibrant metropolis of perhaps 200,000 people, the largest in northern Europe, bustling with commerce, politics, plague, and intellectual energy. Professional theatres were strategically located outside the city walls, primarily on Bankside in Southwark (home to The Theatre, the Curtain, the Rose, and later the Globe), to circumvent the strict regulations and suspicion of the Lord Mayor and city aldermen who often linked playhouses with less reputable entertainments like bear-baiting and brothels.

Around 1594, Shakespeare joined the Lord Chamberlain's Men, a company that quickly rose to become the premier playing company in England. His role was unique: he was both a working actor and a shareholder in the company. This financial stake in the company's success profoundly shaped his career, as he wrote plays specifically for his colleagues and their audiences, intended for performance rather than just the printed page. Performances typically took place in the afternoon in large, open-air amphitheatres like the Globe, built in 1599 from the timbers of the earlier Theatre, which could accommodate around 3,000 spectators. Later, after being renamed the King's Men in 1603 under royal patronage from James I, the company also utilized smaller, roofed indoor theatres such such as Blackfriars from 1609 onwards.

Audiences and the Patronage System

Elizabethan theatre was a genuinely popular art form, attracting a cross-section of society. Apprentices stood in the yard for a penny, while merchants and lawyers occupied the galleries, and nobles and gentry might purchase seats in the expensive lords' rooms. This broad audience significantly shaped Shakespeare's dramaturgy, giving his work its extraordinary tonal breadth—the ability to hold comedy and tragedy in the same breath and to address profound philosophical questions through the mouths of diverse characters, including clowns. By writing for everyone, he ensured his work resonated with all who came after.

Alongside the commercial theatre, the patronage system was a vital aspect of an artist's career. Playwrights and poets dedicated their works to wealthy nobles, hoping for financial reward, commissions, or social protection. Shakespeare followed this convention early in his career, dedicating his narrative poems Venus and Adonis (1593) and The Rape of Lucrece (1594) to Henry Wriothesley, the Earl of Southampton, with effusive compliments suggesting a relationship of genuine respect. Whether Southampton was also the "Fair Youth" to whom the first 126 sonnets are addressed remains one of the enduring puzzles of English literary history.

Later Years and Retirement

By the early 17th century, Shakespeare was prosperous and highly celebrated. He had purchased New Place, one of the grandest houses in Stratford, in 1597, and continued to invest in property in both London and his hometown. Around 1610 or 1611, he began a gradual withdrawal from London life, spending more time in Stratford while still collaborating on plays with younger colleagues, most notably John Fletcher, with whom he co-wrote The Two Noble Kinsmen, Henry VIII, and the lost Cardenio. His late romances—Pericles, Cymbeline, The Winter's Tale, and The Tempest—bear the marks of a playwright consciously rounding off a career, returning to themes of reconciliation, loss, and recovery with a mellowness quite different from the fiery intensity of his great tragedies.

Shakespeare died on April 23, 1616, in Stratford-upon-Avon, aged fifty-two, perhaps fittingly on his own birthday. The cause of his death is unknown, though his will, drawn up in January and hastily revised in March of that year, suggests he was in declining health. He was buried in the chancel of Holy Trinity Church, Stratford, beneath a gravestone bearing a famous curse:

friend for Jesus sake forbeare, To dig the dust encloased heare; Blest be the man that spares thes stones, And curst be he that moves my bones.

This verse successfully kept his bones undisturbed, but his plays and poems, needing no such protection, had already begun their journey into the world.

Review Questions

  1. Describe Shakespeare's early life and education in Stratford-upon-Avon. How did his grammar school training prepare him for his future career, even without a university education?
  2. What are the "Lost Years" (1585-1592), and why is this period significant for biographers? What is the most widely accepted theory for what Shakespeare was doing during this time, and why is it plausible?
  3. Explain Shakespeare's unique role within the Lord Chamberlain's Men (and later the King's Men). How did being both an actor and a shareholder influence his approach to playwriting and his financial success?
  4. Discuss the characteristics of Elizabethan theatre audiences. How did the diverse social strata of spectators impact Shakespeare's dramaturgy and the enduring appeal of his works to a wide range of people?
  5. Summarize Shakespeare's later years, including his withdrawal from London, his collaborations, and the distinctive themes and tone prevalent in his "late romances." What do these final plays suggest about his artistic journey and perspective?
