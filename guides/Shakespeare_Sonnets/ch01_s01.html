Life and Times

Key Concepts

  • Shakespeare was born in April 1564 in Stratford-upon-Avon to a civic-minded family
  • His education at King's New School was intensely classical, grounding him in Ovid, Virgil, and Cicero
  • He married Anne Hathaway in 1582; they had three children — Susanna, Hamnet, and Judith
  • The "lost years" (1585–1592) are a biographical gap filled largely by speculation
  • By 1592 he was established enough in London to draw the jealousy of rival playwright Robert Greene
  • He joined the Lord Chamberlain's Men around 1594, uniquely serving as both shareholder and actor
  • The Globe theatre (built 1599) held approximately 3,000 spectators drawn from every social rank
  • He dedicated his two narrative poems to Henry Wriothesley, Earl of Southampton
  • He retired gradually from London around 1610–1611 and died in Stratford on 23 April 1616

Summary

William Shakespeare was born in Stratford-upon-Avon in April 1564, the son of a glover who rose to become the town's high bailiff. His education at the local King's New School was rigorous and classical, immersing him in Latin literature and rhetoric. He married Anne Hathaway in 1582 and had three children before the historical record goes largely silent until 1592, when a hostile comment from playwright Robert Greene confirms he was already a significant presence in London. He joined the Lord Chamberlain's Men around 1594 as both a shareholder and a working actor — an unusual dual role that gave him a financial stake in the company's success and shaped how he wrote. The theatre world he inhabited was a genuinely popular institution; the Globe could hold 3,000 spectators from apprentices to nobles. In the early 1600s he began withdrawing from London, and he died in Stratford on 23 April 1616, buried under a famous curse warning against disturbing his grave.

Key Terms

  • high bailiff: The chief civic officer of a town, equivalent to mayor — the role John Shakespeare attained in Stratford
  • Lord Chamberlain's Men: The playing company Shakespeare joined around 1594; renamed the King's Men under royal patronage in 1603
  • shareholder: A part-owner of the theatrical company, giving Shakespeare a financial stake in the enterprise beyond a playwright's fee
  • lost years: The biographical gap between 1585 (baptism of the twins) and 1592 (first firm evidence of Shakespeare in London)
  • Globe: The open-air amphitheatre built in 1599 from the timbers of an earlier playhouse, capable of holding approximately 3,000 spectators
  • patronage system: The practice of poets and playwrights dedicating works to wealthy nobles in hopes of financial reward or social protection
  • Blackfriars: The smaller indoor roofed theatre used by the King's Men from 1609 onwards
