Study Guide: Shakespeare's Sonnets 1-3 – The Case for Posterity

Overview

This section introduces Shakespeare's famous sonnet sequence, focusing on the opening three poems addressed to the "Fair Youth." These sonnets collectively urge a beautiful young man to marry and have children, arguing that his exceptional beauty carries an obligation to be reproduced for future generations. The speaker criticizes the Youth's self-absorption and celibacy as a destructive act, comparing it to hoarding treasure, inviting shame, and denying the world of renewed beauty.

Key Concepts

  • The Procreation Imperative: The central argument that beauty is not a personal possession but a gift that must be passed on through offspring. This is presented as a moral and biological duty.
  • Time as an Adversary: Time is personified as a destructive force that will inevitably diminish beauty and life. Procreation is presented as the primary defense against this decay.
  • Self-Love as a Vice: The Youth's refusal to procreate is portrayed as a form of narcissism and "self-substantial" consumption, which is detrimental to himself and the world.
  • Immortality through Offspring: Having children is depicted as a way to achieve a kind of immortality, allowing one's beauty and "blood" to continue living even after one's own death.
  • Economic and Agricultural Metaphors: The sonnets frequently use language of treasure, increase, husbandry, and accounting to frame beauty as a valuable resource that should be invested and multiplied rather than wasted.
  • Reversal of Petrarchan Convention: Unlike traditional love sonnets where the poet adores an unattainable beloved, Shakespeare begins with a "counsellor" rebuking a "self-indulgent youth," setting a unique tone for the sequence.
  • The "Rose" as an Emblem: The rose symbolizes transient beauty that needs to be preserved, implicitly through reproduction.

Important Terms

  • Increase: Offspring or progeny; also, the broader principle of natural multiplication and growth.
  • Contracted: Pledged or betrothed (as in marriage); also, narrowed or shrunk, turned inward.
  • Self-substantial: Self-consuming; made of the same material as itself, suggesting a closed, destructive circuit.
  • Niggarding: Miserly hoarding; failing to give generously. From "niggard," a miser.
  • Churl: A rude, miserly, or peasant-like person; one who is mean-spirited.
  • Livery: An outward garment or uniform; here, the Youth's youthful appearance.
  • Lusty: Full of vigor and vitality; energetic and healthy (not primarily sexual desire).
  • Thriftless: Wasteful, profitless; producing no gain.
  • Succession: The legal and biological passing of inheritance from parent to child.
  • Sum my count: To settle a financial account; to provide the final balance or profit.
  • Glass: A mirror; a looking glass.
  • Fresh repair: Renewal, restoration, or good condition.
  • Beguile: To charm or deceive; to while away time; to cheat.
  • Unear'd: Unplowed, uncultivated.
  • Tillage: The cultivation of land by plowing.
  • Husbandry: Farming; also, the management of a household or marriage.
  • Posterity: Future generations; descendants.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet I: The Indictment of Self-Love

Sonnet I immediately establishes the central theme of the entire sequence: the imperative for the beautiful Fair Youth to reproduce. The speaker begins by stating that "From fairest creatures we desire increase," framing procreation as a natural and universally desired outcome. Beauty, symbolized by "beauty’s rose," is presented as something that should not "die" but be passed on through an "heir" who bears the parent's "memory."

However, the Youth is indicted for his self-absorption, described as being "contracted to thine own bright eyes." This phrase carries a double meaning: he is both betrothed only to his own reflection (narcissism) and also "shrunk" or narrowed, turning inward. He "feed’st thy light’s flame with self-substantial fuel," an image of self-consumption, like a lamp burning its own material without external sustenance, leading to "famine where abundance lies."

The sonnet's volta (turn) occurs at line 9, where the accusation intensifies. The Youth, though "the world’s fresh ornament," is actively destructive. He "buriest thy content" within himself, and, in an oxymoronic pairing, acts as a "tender churl"—outwardly gentle but inwardly a "rude, miserly person" who makes "waste in niggarding," hoarding his beauty rather than sharing it. The speaker frames this as a "cruel" act, not only to himself but to the "world."

The concluding couplet delivers a harsh verdict, using the language of appetite: the Youth is a "glutton" who "eat[s] the world’s due," selfishly consuming what belongs to others and burying his potential legacy in the grave instead of entrusting it to a child. This sets a serious, almost admonishing tone for the sequence, moving away from conventional Petrarchan adoration. The procreation argument itself has classical precedents, like Erasmus's writings, but Shakespeare's use of a compressed, accusatory sonnet form makes it urgent and personal.

Sonnet II: Time's Assault and the Shame of Emptiness

Sonnet II continues the procreation argument but makes it more personal by projecting the Youth into old age. The speaker vividly describes the ravages of time: "When forty winters shall besiege thy brow," digging "deep trenches" (wrinkles) in his once-beautiful face. His "youth’s proud livery" (outward appearance) will become a "tatter’d weed" (a worn-out garment) of no value.

The central question posed is: what will the Youth say when asked "where all thy beauty lies" in old age? To point to his "own deep sunken eyes" would be an "all-eating shame, and thriftless praise"—an empty boast that yields no profit or legacy. This emphasizes that beauty is not truly possessed if it cannot be passed on.

The volta at line 9 shifts from this bleak future to a more desirable alternative. How much more "praise deserv’d thy beauty’s use" if he could answer, "This fair child of mine / Shall sum my count, and make my old excuse"? The child becomes his justification, the "profit" on the investment of his beauty, "proving his beauty by succession thine!" The language of "sum my count" ties into financial metaphors, portraying procreation as a responsible accounting for one's gifts.

The powerful closing couplet offers procreation as a form of resurrection: "This were to be new made when thou art old, / And see thy blood warm when thou feel’st it cold." This image of vicarious survival, seeing one's vitality continue in a child even as one's own life fades, is one of Shakespeare's most affecting. This sonnet transforms a common Renaissance argument about marriage into an emotionally vivid and urgent plea. It also foreshadows later sonnets that will develop the metaphor of time as a military attacker.

Sonnet III: The Mirror, Mother, and Finality

Sonnet III presses the procreation argument through the intimate device of the mirror, and the poignant image of the Youth's mother. The speaker directly instructs the Youth to "Look in thy glass" and recognize that "Now is the time that face should form another." His face, in its "fresh repair," should be renewed in offspring. By not doing so, he "dost beguile the world" (cheats it of beauty) and "unbless some mother"—denying a woman the joy of motherhood.

The poem then challenges the Youth's perceived uniqueness: "For where is she so fair whose unear’d womb / Disdains the tillage of thy husbandry?" This question implies that no woman would refuse to bear his beautiful child, reinforcing the idea of his unique attractiveness and the societal expectation of marriage and procreation. The agricultural metaphors of "unear’d womb" and "tillage of thy husbandry" link human reproduction to the natural fertility of the earth.

The most affecting argument arrives with the image of the Youth's mother: "Thou art thy mother’s glass and she in thee / Calls back the lovely April of her prime." Just as he reflects her youth, he should create a child to reflect his own, allowing him to "see, / Despite of wrinkles this thy golden time" through his offspring. The mirror, therefore, serves both literally (for self-reflection) and metaphorically (as a generational reflection).

The sonnet concludes with a stark, blunt couplet that leaves no room for ambiguity: "But if thou live, remember’d not to be, / Die single and thine image dies with thee." This final line underscores the absolute consequence of his refusal to procreate—not only the loss of his beauty but the complete erasure of his image and legacy from the world. This sonnet directly links self-love to the cessation of posterity, setting the stage for future arguments about various forms of immortality.

Review Questions

  1. Sonnet I frames the Youth's self-love as a "glutton" eating "the world's due." What assumptions does this make about beauty and its social role? Do you agree that beauty carries an obligation to be reproduced?
  2. In Sonnet II, the speaker argues that pointing to "deep sunken eyes" in old age would be "all-eating shame." How does the use of vivid, negative imagery contribute to the persuasive power of the argument for procreation?
  3. Sonnet III suggests that by staying single, the Youth "unbless[es] some mother." How does bringing the perspective of a potential mother into the argument change or enhance the appeal to the Youth?
  4. Shakespeare uses various metaphors across these sonnets, including economic (hoarding, summing counts) and agricultural (tillage, rose, increase). Choose one type of metaphor and discuss how it shapes the reader's understanding of the procreation argument.
  5. These sonnets challenge the Youth to consider his legacy. In what ways do these early sonnets lay the groundwork for the themes of immortality, time, and the preservation of beauty that will recur throughout Shakespeare's sonnet sequence?
