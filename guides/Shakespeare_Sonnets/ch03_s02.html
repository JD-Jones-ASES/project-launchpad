Study Guide: Sonnet II

Summary

Sonnet II projects the fair youth forty years into the future, imagining him as an old man confronted by the ruins of his former beauty. Shakespeare uses the vivid military metaphor of winter besieging the brow and digging "trenches" (wrinkles) to make the threat of age viscerally real. The central question is: what account will the youth give of himself when his beauty is gone? If he has no child to show, his answer — that beauty lives only in his own sunken eyes — will be a shameful admission. But if he has a child who inherits his beauty, he is "new made" even in old age, seeing his own warm blood in his heir.

Key Literary Devices

  • Military metaphor: "Forty winters shall besiege thy brow / And dig deep trenches" — age as an invading army attacking the face.
  • Financial metaphor: "Treasure," "sum my count," "beauty's use," "succession" — beauty treated as capital that must yield a return.
  • Clothing metaphor: "Youth's proud livery" becomes a "tatter'd weed" — beauty as a garment that wears out.
  • Personification: Beauty's "treasure" as something with an owner and an accountant who will demand a reckoning.

Thematic Significance

Sonnet II moves from the threat of Sonnet I to a positive incentive: reproduction as personal renewal. Where Sonnet I ends with punishment (the grave), Sonnet II ends with a reward — the pleasure of watching one's own blood run warm in a child. The financial vocabulary (treasure, sum, account, succession) frames beauty as a legacy to be managed wisely, deepening the economic logic of the procreation sequence. The couplet's promise that the youth can be "new made" through a child represents an early version of the immortality-through-offspring theme that runs through all seventeen sonnets.
