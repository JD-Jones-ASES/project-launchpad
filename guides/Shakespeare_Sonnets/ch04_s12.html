Study Guide: Sonnet XXIX — "When in disgrace with fortune and men's eyes"

Overview

Sonnet XXIX is one of the most emotionally powerful poems in the sequence. It opens with the speaker at his lowest: in social disgrace, friendless, cursing his fate, envying other men their looks, friends, talents, and opportunities. Yet at the poem's exact center — "Haply I think on thee" — the entire emotional landscape transforms. The thought of the beloved arrives unexpectedly and lifts the speaker from earth like a lark rising to sing hymns at heaven's gate. The closing couplet declares that this love brings such wealth that he scorns to trade his state with kings.

Key Themes

Despair and Transformation. The volta here is one of the most dramatic in the sequence. The catalogue of misery in the octave is so detailed and specific — wishing for another man's art, another's friends, another's scope — that when the thought of the beloved arrives, its transforming power is all the more striking.

Love as Transcendent Wealth. The closing couplet redefines wealth and power: the beloved's love is worth more than royal fortune. This is not mere sentiment but a philosophical claim — love provides something that external goods cannot, a contentment that makes all social comparison irrelevant.

The Lark Simile. The image of the lark rising from sullen earth to sing hymns at heaven's gate is one of the sonnet sequence's most celebrated moments — a swift, joyful ascent from gloom to ecstasy, perfectly capturing the emotional reversal.

Literary Devices

  • Catalogue: the list of envied qualities creates the emotional weight of the octave
  • Volta: the sharp turn at "Yet... Haply I think on thee" (lines 11–12)
  • Simile: the lark rising from sullen earth
  • Paradox: poverty made rich by love's memory
  • Archaism: "haply" (by chance or perhaps)

Significance

Sonnet XXIX stands among the most loved poems in English for its truthful depiction of despair and the sudden, life-altering force of remembered affection. Its emotional arc — from self-loathing to transcendence — is enacted in the sonnet's very structure.
