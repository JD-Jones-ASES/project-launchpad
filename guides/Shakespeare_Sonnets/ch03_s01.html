Study Guide: Shakespeare's Sonnets 1-17 – The Case for Posterity

Overview

This section introduces the unique opening of Shakespeare's sonnet sequence, specifically Sonnets 1-17. Unlike earlier traditions of unrequited love, these sonnets present a persuasive argument to a beautiful young man (the Fair Youth) urging him to marry and have children. The central theme is procreation as a moral duty to preserve beauty against the ravages of time, a theme later supplemented by the poet's offer of immortality through verse.

Key Concepts

  • Procreation as a Moral Imperative: The speaker insists that the Fair Youth's beauty is a gift held in trust for the world, not solely for his personal enjoyment. He has a moral obligation to pass on his likeness to a new generation through children.
  • Transience of Beauty and Mercilessness of Time: A recurring motif is the fleeting nature of beauty and the destructive power of time, which relentlessly leads to decay and oblivion.
  • Children as a Defense Against Oblivion: Having a child is presented as the primary means to combat time's destruction, allowing the father's beauty and likeness to live on.
  • The Poet's Verse as a Second Strategy for Immortality: Sonnet 15 introduces the poet's own work as another powerful method to defeat time, "grafting" the Youth's image into verse.
  • The Speaker's Evolving Role: The speaker begins as a gentle reproacher and counsellor, evolving into a friend, and finally, a poet who offers a unique form of immortality.
  • Double Immortality (Child and Poem): By Sonnet 17, the procreation argument is joined by the promise of poetic immortality, offering the Youth two ways to live beyond his natural lifespan: "in it [a child], and in my rhyme."

Important Terms

  • Sonnet Sequence: A series of sonnets linked by theme, subject, or narrative. Shakespeare's sonnets are famous for their interconnectedness.
  • Petrarchan Tradition: A style of sonnet popularised by the Italian poet Petrarch, typically focusing on the speaker's unrequited love for an idealised woman (e.g., Laura) and the suffering it causes.
  • Fair Youth: The unnamed, beautiful young man to whom the first 126 of Shakespeare's sonnets are addressed. In Sonnets 1-17, he is the target of the procreation argument.
  • Procreation: The act of producing offspring; having children. This is the central theme of Sonnets 1-17, presented as a duty.
  • Transience: The state or fact of lasting only for a short time; impermanence. This describes the fleeting nature of human beauty and life.
  • Oblivion: The state of being forgotten or unknown. The sonnets argue that children (and later, poetry) can save one from oblivion.
  • Volta: The "turn" or shift in thought or argument in a sonnet, typically occurring between the octave (first eight lines) and the sestet (last six lines) in a Petrarchan sonnet, or before the final couplet in a Shakespearean sonnet. In this context, Sonnet 15 is described as a "quiet volta" where the poet's verse is introduced as a new solution.
  • Immortality: The ability to live forever or to have endless life. The sonnets propose two avenues to immortality: through one's children and through enduring poetry.

Detailed Notes

The Uniqueness of Shakespeare's Opening Sonnets (1-17)

  • Departure from Tradition: Unlike earlier European sonnet traditions (e.g., Petrarch's adoration of Laura, Sidney's unrequited love for Astrophil), Shakespeare's initial sonnets (1-17) are not about longing for a distant beloved.
  • A "Legal Brief" for Procreation: Instead, the speaker presents a sustained, almost legalistic argument, urging a beautiful young man (the Fair Youth) to marry and have children.
  • Central Theme: Procreation: The binding theme across these seventeen sonnets is the speaker's insistent demand that the Youth propagate his beauty. This beauty is portrayed not as a personal possession, but as a gift held in trust for the world, carrying a moral obligation to be passed on.

The Argument for Procreation: Variety and Progression

  • Diverse Metaphors and Language: Shakespeare employs a rich tapestry of imagery and vocabulary to press the same core point from multiple angles.
    • Horticulture: References to "nature's seeds and harvests" emphasize natural cycles of growth, decay, and regeneration.
    • Finance and Law: Terms like "legacy, usury, audit, executor" frame beauty as an inheritance or capital that should be invested and passed on, rather than wasted.
    • Astronomy, Music, Classical Mythology: These broader allusions elevate the argument, connecting the Youth's duty to universal principles and timeless themes.
  • The Core Message: Regardless of the metaphor, the essential point remains consistent: beauty is fleeting, time is destructive, and the only enduring defense against being forgotten (oblivion) is to have a child who will inherit the father's likeness.
  • Progression of the Argument:
    • Sonnets 1: Begins with a "gentle reproach," condemning the Youth's self-absorption and implied refusal to procreate. He is accused of narcissism and of "devouring" his own beauty.
    • Sonnets 5 and 12: The imagery intensifies, showcasing increasingly vivid and destructive portrayals of time's impact on beauty and life.
    • Sonnet 15: The Philosophical Pivot: This sonnet marks a significant shift, a "quiet volta." Here, the poet introduces his own verse as a second, parallel strategy to combat time's decay. While still advocating for an heir, the poet now actively steps forward as a "combatant," promising to "graft" the Youth's image into his poetry.
    • Sonnet 17: The Culmination: The sequence concludes by merging both strategies for immortality. The final couplet explicitly states that the Youth can achieve a "double life" – "in it [a child], and in my rhyme."

Landmark Sonnets within the Group

  • Sonnet 1:
    • Crucial for setting the entire sequence in motion.
    • Contains a "double accusation": the Youth is both a narcissist (feeding on his own beauty) and a glutton (devouring what belongs to the world/future).
  • Sonnet 12:
    • Considered one of the most powerful meditations on time in the entire sequence.
    • Opens with a vivid "catalogue of decay": images like the violet past its prime, sable (dark) curls turning white, and tall trees stripped bare.
    • Culminates in the "iconic image of Time's scythe," personifying time as a reaper of life.
  • Sonnet 15:
    • Described as the "quiet volta" (turn) of this introductory chapter.
    • Signifies the poet's emergence as an active participant in the battle against time.
    • He offers his verse as a means to preserve the Youth's image, even as he continues to press for the Youth to produce an heir.

The Speaker's Role and the "Generous Supplement"

  • Beyond Admiration: The speaker is not merely a "besotted admirer." His roles expand throughout the sequence.
  • Counsellor, Friend, and Poet: He acts as an advisor, guiding the Youth towards what he believes is a moral imperative, and ultimately, as a poet who recognizes the power of words to bestow immortality.
  • Verse as a "Generous Supplement": By Sonnet 17, the argument for procreation has been so thoroughly made that the offer of poetic immortality feels not like a retreat or a lesser option, but an additional, expansive gift. It broadens the scope of survival, suggesting that life can be extended through both biological lineage ("child and poem") and artistic creation ("life and rhyme").

Review Questions

  1. How do Shakespeare's opening seventeen sonnets (1-17) diverge from the traditional European sonnet themes established by poets like Petrarch and Sidney?
  2. What is the primary argument presented by the speaker to the Fair Youth in Sonnets 1-17, and what are the various categories of imagery (e.g., horticulture, finance) Shakespeare uses to support this argument?
  3. Discuss the significance of Sonnet 15 as a "philosophical pivot" or "quiet volta." What new strategy for defeating time is introduced here, and how does it relate to the initial procreation argument?
  4. Identify and explain the key accusations or criticisms leveled against the Fair Youth in Sonnet 1. How do these set the stage for the rest of the procreation sequence?
  5. By the conclusion of Sonnet 17, the speaker offers the Fair Youth two distinct paths to immortality. Explain these two paths and how the second (poetic immortality) is presented as a "generous supplement" rather than a replacement.
