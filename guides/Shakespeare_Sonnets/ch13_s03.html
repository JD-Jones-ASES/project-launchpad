Study Guide: Sonnets 147-150 – The Dark Lady: Reckoning

Overview

This section delves into four sonnets (147-150) from Shakespeare's "Dark Lady" sequence, exploring the speaker's tormented and self-destructive obsession with a woman he simultaneously desires and condemns. The sonnets vividly portray a mind grappling with self-deception, perceived betrayal, and the overwhelming power of irrational love, using metaphors of illness, distorted vision, and total subjection to illuminate the speaker's profound internal conflict.

Key Concepts

  • Love as a Consuming Illness: Sonnet 147 presents love as a debilitating fever, a clinical disease that the speaker self-diagnoses. His reason, acting as a physician, has abandoned him for refusing the prescribed cure, leaving him "past cure" and "frantic-mad." This metaphor highlights the self-destructive nature of his obsession.
  • The Deception of Love's Vision: Sonnet 148 interrogates the reliability of the speaker's perception. His "love-struck" eyes either see falsely or his judgment misinterprets what they see truly. The sonnet concludes that "cunning Love" deliberately blinds him with his own tears, preventing him from seeing the Dark Lady's "foul faults."
  • Absolute Self-Abasement: Sonnet 149 details the speaker's complete submission to the Dark Lady, even at the cost of his self-respect and relationships. He actively works against his own interests, abandons friends, and punishes himself with misery, demonstrating the profound extent of his devotion despite her cruelty.
  • The Paradox of Unworthiness: Sonnet 150 marvels at the Dark Lady's "powerful might" despite her "insufficiency." The speaker finds himself loving her more the more he perceives cause for hate, suggesting that her very flaws and "worst deeds" somehow enhance her hold over him, challenging rational understanding of attraction.
  • Reason vs. Passion: Throughout these sonnets, the conflict between rational thought and overwhelming passion is central. The speaker is acutely aware of his irrationality but unable to overcome it, illustrating the Stoic and Elizabethan understanding of passion's capacity to undermine reason.
  • Evolution of the Dark Lady's Image: While earlier "Dark Lady" sonnets might have presented her "darkness" as an unconventional beauty, in this sequence, her attributes are increasingly framed as grounds for outright condemnation, deception, and moral failing.

Important Terms

  • approveto prove; to demonstrate: To confirm or establish something by experience or evidence. In Sonnet 147, the speaker "approves" that desire is death through his own suffering.
  • becoming of things ill: The quality of making bad or flawed things appear attractive or fitting. In Sonnet 150, the Dark Lady has this power to transform vice into apparent virtue.
  • censuresjudges; evaluates critically: To make an official or critical judgment. In Sonnet 148, the speaker's judgment "censures falsely" what his eyes see correctly.
  • doteto be excessively and foolishly fond: To love or admire someone or something with a lack of discernment or critical judgment. In Sonnet 148, his "false eyes dote" on what may not be truly fair.
  • frantic-mad: In a state of wild madness; beside oneself with frenzy. Used in Sonnet 147 to describe the speaker's uncontrolled mental state after reason has abandoned him.
  • give the lie: To contradict directly and bluntly; to accuse of lying. In Sonnet 150, the Dark Lady makes the speaker "give the lie to my true sight," denying what he plainly sees.
  • insufficiencyinadequacy; lack of ability: A state of lacking what is required or expected; a deficiency. In Sonnet 150, the speaker is baffled by the Dark Lady's power despite her "insufficiency."
  • longing still: Always craving; perpetually desirous. "Still" here means "continually." In Sonnet 147, his love is a fever "longing still" for what prolongs the disease.
  • lour'stfrowns; looks threateningly: To look sullen or displeased; to cast a dark or angry look. In Sonnet 149, if the Dark Lady "lour'st on me," he punishes himself.
  • partaketo take sides with; to join in the judgment against: To share in or participate, often by siding with someone. In Sonnet 149, he "against myself with thee partake," meaning he sides with her against his own interests.
  • past care: Beyond the point at which anyone will care for him; also, no longer caring about anything. In Sonnet 147, the physician (reason) has given up, and the speaker himself no longer cares for his own well-being.
  • physicmedicine; medical treatment: The art of healing or medical practice. In Sonnet 147, his reason, the physician, has "prescriptions" (advice) that are not kept.
  • revengeto take vengeance; retaliation: To inflict punishment or injury in return for an offense. In Sonnet 149, he spends "revenge upon myself" with grief.
  • vexed with watching: Troubled by sleepless vigil; exhausted by keeping watch. In Sonnet 148, the speaker's eyes are physically impaired by sleeplessness and tears.
  • warrantise of skill: A guarantee of proficiency; an assurance of competence or expertise. In Sonnet 150, even in her "refuse of thy deeds," there seems to be a "warrantise of skill."
  • worship thy defect: To reverence what is actually flawed or worthless; to treat the mistress's failings as virtues. In Sonnet 149, the speaker adores her imperfections.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet 147: Love as a Fever

  • Main Idea: The speaker's love is a debilitating illness, a self-destructive fever that craves what prolongs the disease. His reason, the "physician," has abandoned him for not following its counsel. Now "past cure" and "frantic-mad," his thoughts are irrational, culminating in a devastating realization that the Dark Lady, whom he once thought "fair" and "bright," is "as black as hell, as dark as night."
  • Medical Metaphor: The sonnet is built entirely on a clinical metaphor of love as a fever. The speaker acts as both patient and diagnostician, describing his longing, the refusal of "physic" (medicine/reason's counsel), and the abandonment by his "reason." This reflects Elizabethan medical understanding of "amor hereos" (lovesickness) as a genuine condition linked to the four humors.
  • Abandonment of Reason: "My reason, the physician to my love, / Hath left me." This line is crucial, signifying a complete surrender to passion. The Stoic philosophical concept of reason governing passions is inverted; here, reason has given up entirely.
  • Desire is Death: The speaker "approves" (proves by experience) that "Desire is death," acknowledging the self-destructive nature of his uncontrolled longing.
  • Climax and Reversal: The sonnet progresses from controlled medical diagnosis to a final, violent declaration. The couplet – "For I have sworn thee fair, and thought thee bright, / Who art as black as hell, as dark as night" – delivers one of the most brutal reversals in the sequence, condemning the Dark Lady and exposing the speaker's past declarations as delusional.
  • Connections: The fever metaphor anticipates Sonnets 153 and 154 (the Anacreontic coda), where the speaker seeks a cure for his "love's fever." The medical logic echoes Sonnet 118 (taking poison to prevent disease) and Sonnet 129 (desire's madness). The Dark Lady's "blackness" directly references Sonnet 127, but now transforms from unconventional beauty to outright condemnation.

Sonnet 148: The Deceit of Love's Vision

  • Main Idea: The speaker questions the reliability of his own eyes and judgment when it comes to the Dark Lady. He's caught in a paradox: either his eyes are faulty, or his judgment misinterprets what they see. The sonnet concludes that "cunning Love" intentionally blinds him with his own tears, preventing him from perceiving the Dark Lady's "foul faults."
  • Epistemological Puzzle: The sonnet presents a central question: "what eyes hath Love put in my head, / Which have no correspondence with true sight?" The speaker cannot reconcile his perception of the Dark Lady with what the world, or presumably his own deeper judgment, tells him.
  • Love's Distortion: The speaker attributes his distorted vision to being "vexed with watching and with tears"—the physical exhaustion and emotional outpouring of obsessive love. This physical impairment prevents clear sight.
  • "Cunning Love": The couplet resolves the puzzle with a dark twist: "O cunning Love! with tears thou keep'st me blind, / Lest eyes well-seeing thy foul faults should find." Love isn't just foolishly blind; it's strategically deceptive, using the speaker's own suffering (tears) to maintain his delusion and prevent him from seeing the truth about his beloved's imperfections.
  • Context of Love Philosophy: This sonnet subverts Neoplatonic ideas of love's "spiritual vision." Instead of love elevating perception to see inner beauty, Shakespeare portrays love as degrading perception, making the lover literally blind to flaws.

Sonnet 149: The Cruelty of Complete Subjection

  • Main Idea: The speaker exhaustively lists the ways he has sacrificed his own well-being, reputation, and friendships in complete submission to the Dark Lady. He asks rhetorically what merit he holds that would make him refuse her service. The bitter answer in the couplet is that his devotion proves his blindness, not her worthiness; she values those who can see, and he is incapable.
  • Self-Abasement: The sonnet is a catalogue of self-inflicted pain and submission: "I against myself with thee partake," forgetting himself, calling her enemies his friends, fawning on those she favors, punishing himself when she "lour'st" on him.
  • "Worship thy defect": This striking phrase reveals the extent of his delusion—he reveres her flaws as if they were virtues, commanded by "the motion of thine eyes."
  • Bitter Self-Knowledge: The couplet, "But, love, hate on, for now I know thy mind; / Those that can see thou lov'st, and I am blind," brings a moment of clear, painful self-awareness. He realizes his devotion is a symptom of his blindness, not an expression of her worth, and that she favors those who are not thus deluded. This self-knowledge, however, comes too late to restore his dignity, only confirming his surrender.

Sonnet 150: The Paradox of Power from Unworthiness

  • Main Idea: The speaker expresses profound bewilderment at the Dark Lady's inexplicable power over him. Despite her "insufficiency" and the "refuse of thy deeds" (her worst actions), she still sways his heart, making him deny his own senses and love her more the more reason he has to hate her. The couplet, born of wounded pride, argues that his capacity to love someone so unworthy makes him more worthy of her love.
  • Inexplicable Power: The speaker questions the source of her "powerful might" given her clear lack of merit ("insufficiency"). He wonders how she can make him "give the lie to my true sight" and deny obvious truths.
  • Paradoxical Attraction: The central paradox is that her failings seem to increase his love: "Who taught thee how to make me love thee more, / The more I hear and see just cause of hate?" Even her worst behavior ("refuse of thy deeds") possesses a "strength and warrantise of skill" that makes her "worst all best exceeds."
  • Twisted Logic: The couplet presents a desperate, circular argument: "If thy unworthiness rais'd love in me, / More worthy I to be belov'd of thee." This is an attempt to salvage dignity from utter subjection, asserting that his profound, irrational love for an unworthy person proves his own exceptional capacity for love, and thus he deserves to be loved by her.
  • Connections: This sonnet's paradox of loving more despite cause for hate connects to Sonnets 147, 148, and 149 in its exploration of obsessive, irrational love. It also echoes Sonnet 141, where the speaker's senses testify against the Dark Lady, yet his heart remains devoted.

Review Questions

  1. How does the extended medical metaphor in Sonnet 147 establish the speaker's psychological state and the self-destructive nature of his love? What does the abandonment by "reason" signify?
  2. Sonnet 148 explores the paradox of "Love's vision." Discuss how "cunning Love" is portrayed as actively blinding the speaker, rather than just being foolishly enamored. What role do his own tears play in this deception?
  3. Analyze the extent of the speaker's self-abasement in Sonnet 149. What are the specific ways he sacrifices his own interests and dignity? How does the couplet reveal his painful self-awareness, and what does this realization add to the sonnet's impact?
  4. In Sonnet 150, the speaker is baffled by the Dark Lady's power despite her "insufficiency." Explain the paradox that her very unworthiness seems to strengthen his love. How does the final couplet attempt to reconcile his profound subjection with a desire for personal dignity?
  5. Across these four sonnets (147-150), how does the speaker's understanding and depiction of the Dark Lady evolve or solidify? What overarching message do these sonnets convey about the nature of obsessive, unrequited, or destructive love?
