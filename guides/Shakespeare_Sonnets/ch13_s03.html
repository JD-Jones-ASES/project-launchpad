Study Guide: Sonnet CXLIV

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet CXLIV is one of the most explicitly allegorical in the sequence. The speaker identifies "two loves" — a fair man (the better angel) and a dark woman (the worser spirit) — who correspond to the Fair Youth and the Dark Lady. Drawing on the morality-play tradition of a good and bad angel contending for a soul, the speaker describes his fear that the female evil is corrupting his better angel, seducing the saint into becoming a devil. The octave stages this spiritual contest; the sestet moves to the speaker's anguished uncertainty — he cannot know directly whether the angel has been corrupted, since both figures have departed from him. The couplet resolves nothing, offering only grim irony: he will remain in doubt until his "bad angel" fires his good one out — an allusion to venereal disease as the ultimate proof of betrayal.

Key Poetic Devices

  • Morality play allegory: good angel / bad angel contending for a soul, drawn directly from theatrical tradition
  • Antithesis: fair/dark, comfort/despair, better/worser, saint/devil — the entire poem operates in binary opposition
  • Dark pun: "fire my good one out" alludes to venereal disease, darkening the couplet's wit
  • "Colour'd ill": physical description (dark complexion) merged with moral condemnation

Central Themes

Moral struggle: The speaker is literally a battleground between virtue and vice personified in the two people he loves. Sin and corruption: The Dark Lady corrupts what is good — turning the saint/angel to a devil. Jealousy and uncertainty: The emotional core is suspense about a betrayal the speaker suspects but cannot confirm. Spiritual damnation: "Hell" operates both theologically and sexually, layering sacred and profane registers in a single word. The sonnet captures the full complexity of the sequence's triangular drama.
