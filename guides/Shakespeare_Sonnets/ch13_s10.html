Study Guide: Sonnet CLI

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet CLI opens with a philosophical paradox about love and conscience, then uses it to mount an ironic defense of the speaker's behavior. Love, being "too young to know what conscience is," cannot be held accountable — yet conscience is itself born of love, making Love's ignorance of conscience structurally impossible. The speaker tells the Dark Lady not to press his moral failings, since doing so implicates her: her betrayal of him caused his soul to betray his nobler nature to the gross body. The second half of the sonnet turns deliberately bawdy: the body rises at her name, stands as her "triumphant prize," falls by her side — and the speaker defends this as a valid form of love. The couplet's wit is darkly comic: calling lust "love" is defended as no failure of conscience since love is love's own frame of reference.

Key Poetic Devices

  • Opening paradox: love too young to know conscience, yet conscience born of love — self-subverting logic
  • Soul/body division: the nobler part (soul) betrayed to the gross body's treason
  • Bawdy puns: "rising," "stand," "fall" — the body's sexual autonomy described with deliberate double meaning
  • Ironic self-defense: the couplet claims "no want of conscience" while obviously describing a failure of moral self-governance

Central Themes

Moral struggle: The war between soul and body is made explicit — the soul loses, the flesh triumphs. Sin and rationalization: The sonnet dramatizes the mind's capacity to rationalize sin through clever argument, even while clearly knowing what it is doing. Shame: The speaker's sophistication increases rather than relieves the shame — he knows he is sinning and constructs arguments around it rather than reforming. Love and lust: CLI is the sequence's most direct treatment of the distinction between love and lust, and the speaker's willingness to blur that line.
