Study Guide: Sonnet CXXXVII — Love's Corruption of Sight

Overview

Sonnet CXXXVII is one of the Dark Lady sequence's most penetrating self-analyses. The speaker addresses Love as a blind fool who has infected his own eyes with blindness — but the blindness described is not simple ignorance. His eyes know the truth and see it; the problem is that Love has forged hooks binding his judgment to his corrupted perception. He takes the worst for the best, the common for the private, the foul for the fair. Knowing the truth does not set him free.

Key Themes

The Split Between Seeing and Judging. The sonnet's central paradox is that the eyes "behold and see not what they see." The faculty of sight is intact; the faculty of interpretation is shattered. Love has inserted itself between perception and judgment, producing systematic delusion despite full knowledge.

Private vs. Common. The agricultural metaphor of a "several plot" (private land) vs. "the wide world's common place" (public commons) encodes a painful truth: he has treated as his own exclusive possession what is available to everyone. His delusion has been not only aesthetic but possessive.

The Plague of False Perception. The couplet's "false plague" echoes CXXIX's hell — both sonnets conclude that awareness of the trap is no escape from it. CXXXVII goes further: his heart and eyes have been "transferr'd" to the plague, suggesting permanent residence in delusion.

Literary Devices

  • Apostrophe: addressing Love as a personified blind fool
  • Paradox: see yet see not; hooks of falsehood tied to judgment
  • Property metaphor: several plot vs. common place
  • Disease metaphor: false plague to which eyes and heart are transferred

Significance

CXXXVII is the sequence's most explicit statement of self-aware delusion — the speaker sees clearly that he sees falsely, and sees that this meta-awareness changes nothing.
