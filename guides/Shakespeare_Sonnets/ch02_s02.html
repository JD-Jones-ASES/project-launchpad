Study Guide: A Brief History of the Sonnet

Overview

The sonnet is over 700 years old. It originated in thirteenth-century Sicily, traveled through Italy to become the dominant European love lyric, crossed to England in the sixteenth century, and has been continuously written — in dozens of languages — ever since. Understanding this history explains what Shakespeare inherited, what he transformed, and why his sonnets still matter.

The Sonnet's Origin (1230s–1300s)

The form first appears in the court of Holy Roman Emperor Frederick II in Sicily. Giacomo da Lentini, a notary in Frederick's court, is credited with the invention. He combined the Sicilian strambotto (an eight-line folk lyric) with the thematic conventions of Provencal troubadour poetry — the idealized beloved, the suffering lover, introspective devotion.

Petrarch and the Tradition (1304–1374)

Francesco Petrarch did not invent the sonnet but defined it for two centuries. His Canzoniere (366 poems addressed to Laura) established the conventions that every Renaissance poet would know:

  • Blazon — catalogue of the beloved's physical perfections
  • Oxymoron — the lover's contradictory states ("I freeze and burn")
  • The unattainable mistress — object of worship, source of torment
  • Elevated, sustained devotion — treating the beloved as nearly divine

Shakespeare knew these conventions intimately — and delighted in subverting them.

The Sonnet in England (1500s)

Sir Thomas Wyatt and Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey introduced the sonnet to England after diplomatic visits to Italy. Surrey adapted the form for English by using alternating rhymes (ABAB) and introduced iambic pentameter as the dominant English line length. Their work appeared in Tottel's Miscellany (1557), sparking wide English interest.

The Elizabethan Craze (1580s–1590s)

The posthumous publication of Sir Philip Sidney's sonnet sequence in 1591 launched a flood of imitations. Key sequences of the period:

Poet Sequence Notable Feature
Sidney Astrophil and Stella Psychological realism, ironic self-awareness
Spenser Amoretti (1595) Ends in happy marriage — breaks Petrarchan convention
Daniel Delia (1592) Time and poetic immortality
Drayton Idea (1594) Famous volta in Sonnet 61

Shakespeare published his sequence in 1609 — later than most contemporaries, but with greater mastery.

Shakespeare's Innovations

  1. First 126 sonnets addressed to the Fair Youth (a young man) — upending the heterosexual Petrarchan dynamic
  2. The Dark Lady (Sonnets 127–154) — the explicit anti-type of Petrarchan idealization
  3. The closing couplet transformed from a grace note into a structural weapon

After Shakespeare

  • Milton (17th c.) — expanded the sonnet into civic and devotional themes
  • The Romantics — rediscovered Shakespeare's sonnets; Barrett Browning brought feminine subjectivity
  • Modern and contemporary poets — Hopkins, Rilke, Millay, Heaney continue the tradition

Review Questions

  1. What two earlier traditions did Giacomo da Lentini draw on when assembling the sonnet?
  2. Name three "Petrarchan conventions" and explain what each means.
  3. What contribution did Surrey make beyond adapting the rhyme scheme?
  4. Why was Sidney's 1591 publication so significant for the Elizabethan sonnet?
  5. How did Shakespeare's treatment of the Dark Lady subvert the Petrarchan tradition?
