A Comprehensive Study Guide: A Brief History of the Sonnet

Overview

This section traces the remarkable seven-century journey of the sonnet, from its origins in 13th-century Sicily to its enduring presence in contemporary poetry. It highlights the pivotal roles of key figures like Giacomo da Lentini, Petrarch, Wyatt, Surrey, and Shakespeare, detailing how the form evolved in structure, theme, and emotional depth as it traveled across cultures and literary eras.

Key Concepts

  • Origin of the Sonnet: The sonnet was first clearly identifiable around the 1230s in the court of Holy Roman Emperor Frederick II in Sicily, largely credited to Giacomo da Lentini. It combined elements from the Sicilian strambotto and Provençal troubadour poetry.
  • Petrarchan Sonnet: Francesco Petrarch, through his Canzoniere, defined the sonnet's themes and conventions for centuries. These included an idealized, unattainable beloved, a suffering lover, and specific poetic devices like the blazon and oxymoron.
  • Sonnet's Arrival in England: Sir Thomas Wyatt and Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey, introduced the sonnet to England in the early 16th century. Surrey notably adapted the rhyme scheme to the English pattern (abab cdcd efef gg) and introduced iambic pentameter, making it more suitable for the English language.
  • Elizabethan Sonnet Craze: The late 16th century saw an explosion of sonnet writing in England, triggered by Sir Philip Sidney's Astrophil and Stella. This period refined the form, introducing psychological complexity and playing knowingly with Petrarchan conventions.
  • Shakespearean Innovations: William Shakespeare revolutionized the sonnet by addressing his first 126 sonnets to a young man (the Fair Youth) and later ones to a "Dark Lady," subverting traditional Petrarchan ideals. He also developed the closing couplet into a powerful structural tool, often reversing or intensifying the preceding argument.
  • Sonnet's Enduring Evolution: After Shakespeare, the sonnet was revived by John Milton, who expanded its subject matter beyond love into civic and personal themes. Romantics, Victorians, and modern poets continued to adapt and experiment with the form, ensuring its survival due to its inherent flexibility and capacity to explore deep feelings concisely.

Important Terms

  • Sonnet: A lyric poem of fourteen lines, typically written in iambic pentameter, with a defined rhyme scheme, focusing on a single theme or idea.
  • Giacomo da Lentini: A Sicilian poet from the 13th century, widely credited with the invention of the sonnet form.
  • Hendecasyllabic lines: Eleven-syllable lines, characteristic of early Italian sonnets.
  • Octave: The first eight lines of a sonnet, typically presenting a problem, question, or argument.
  • Sestet: The final six lines of a sonnet, typically offering a resolution, answer, or commentary on the octave.
  • Francesco Petrarch (1304–1374): An influential Italian Renaissance poet whose Canzoniere established the dominant themes and conventions of the sonnet for centuries.
  • Canzoniere: Petrarch's collection of 366 poems, mostly sonnets, largely addressed to an idealized woman named Laura.
  • Petrarchan conventions: A set of poetic tropes and themes popular in Renaissance love poetry, derived from Petrarch, including the idealized mistress, the suffering lover, blazon, and oxymoron.
  • Blazon: A poetic device in which a poet describes a beloved's physical features by cataloguing and comparing them to beautiful objects (e.g., eyes like stars, lips like rubies).
  • Oxymoron: A figure of speech that combines contradictory terms, often used in Petrarchan poetry to express the lover's conflicting emotions (e.g., "freezing fire," "living death").
  • Sir Thomas Wyatt (1503–1542): One of the first English poets to introduce the sonnet to England, primarily through translations and imitations of Petrarch.
  • Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey (c. 1517–1547): An English poet who further adapted the sonnet, introducing the English (or Shakespearean) rhyme scheme (abab cdcd efef gg) and helping to establish iambic pentameter as the dominant meter in English verse.
  • Tottel's Miscellany (Songes and Sonettes, 1557): A landmark anthology that published the works of Wyatt and Surrey, popularizing the sonnet in England.
  • Elizabethan Sonnet Craze: A period in the late 16th century (1580s-1590s) marked by a widespread enthusiasm for writing sonnet sequences in England.
  • Sir Philip Sidney (Astrophil and Stella, 1591): An influential Elizabethan poet whose sonnet sequence revitalized the form and introduced greater psychological complexity and self-awareness.
  • Fair Youth: The unnamed young man who is the primary addressee of Shakespeare's first 126 sonnets, a departure from the traditional female beloved.
  • Dark Lady: A mysterious, non-idealized woman who is the object of the speaker's complex and often degrading desire in Shakespeare's Sonnets 127–154.
  • Couplet: A pair of rhyming lines, especially the final two lines of an English (Shakespearean) sonnet, often used by Shakespeare to provide a summary, twist, or dramatic intensification.
  • Volta: The "turn" or shift in thought or argument within a sonnet, typically occurring between the octave and sestet in Petrarchan sonnets, or before the final couplet in English sonnets.
  • John Milton (1608–1674): An English poet who revived the sonnet in the 17th century, expanding its thematic scope beyond love to include civic and personal themes.
  • Iambic Pentameter: A common meter in English poetry consisting of five metrical feet, each comprising one unstressed syllable followed by one stressed syllable (e.g., "da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM").

Detailed Notes

The Enduring Appeal of the Sonnet

The sonnet, a poetic form dating back over 700 years, has shown remarkable resilience, surviving numerous historical upheavals and shifts in literary tastes. Its fourteen-line structure has proven flexible enough to accommodate diverse poetic demands across centuries and languages. Understanding its origins and transformations is key to appreciating its later masters, particularly Shakespeare.

Origins: Sicily and the Thirteenth Century

The sonnet form first emerged around the 1230s in the vibrant court of Holy Roman Emperor Frederick II in Sicily. The poet most often credited with its invention is Giacomo da Lentini, a notary and skillful poet in Frederick's court. Da Lentini's innovation was a synthesis of existing traditions:

  • Structural Basis: Likely derived from the Sicilian strambotto, an eight-line folk lyric.
  • Content: Drew heavily on the refined conventions of Provençal troubadour poetry, which featured an idealized, often unattainable beloved and an introspective, suffering lover.

Da Lentini's formal contribution was significant: he established the sonnet as fourteen hendecasyllabic lines (eleven syllables in Italian), structured into an octave (first eight lines) and a sestet (last six lines), all centered on a single topic with a clear, structured argument. Although the Sicilian school eventually dispersed, the form traveled north to Tuscany, where it found its greatest early exponent.

Petrarch and the Canzoniere

Francesco Petrarch (1304–1374) did not invent the sonnet, but he undeniably defined it for the next four centuries. His monumental work, the Canzoniere—a collection of 366 poems, predominantly sonnets, addressed to an idealized woman named Laura—established the emotional register, thematic scope, and poetic conventions that would influence Renaissance poets across Europe.

The Canzoniere explores the poet-speaker's tormented desire and self-divided nature through an idealized, unattainable Laura. The poems oscillate between hope and despair, spiritual aspiration and earthly longing. After Laura's death in the sequence, the tone deepens into elegy, transforming love into a meditation on mortality.

Petrarch's influence was so profound that scholars refer to a distinct set of "Petrarchan conventions," which became standard in love poetry for two centuries:

  • The blazon: A detailed catalogue of the beloved's physical perfections.
  • The oxymoron: The use of contradictory terms to express the lover's conflicting emotional states ("I freeze and burn").
  • The unattainable mistress: A beloved who is both the source of the poet's suffering and his poetic inspiration.
  • An elevated, sustained devotion: Treating the beloved as almost divine.

Shakespeare, intimately familiar with these conventions, would later delight in subverting them.

The Sonnet Arrives in England

The sonnet reached England in the early 16th century, brought by two poets who encountered it during diplomatic missions to Italy:

  • Sir Thomas Wyatt (1503–1542): Translated several of Petrarch's sonnets directly and penned original sonnets in the Petrarchan style.
  • Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey (c. 1517–1547): Significantly adapted the form for the English language. Surrey abandoned Petrarch's demanding interlocked octave rhyme scheme for a looser pattern of alternating rhymes (abab cdcd efef gg), which became the standard English (or Shakespearean) sonnet structure. Critically, Surrey also introduced iambic pentameter as the dominant line length in English verse, a contribution of immense and lasting importance.

The works of both poets circulated in manuscript during their lives and were later published posthumously in Richard Tottel's Songes and Sonettes (1557), commonly known as Tottel's Miscellany. This anthology introduced the sonnet to a broader English readership and ignited its subsequent popularity.

The Elizabethan Sonnet Craze

The 1580s and 1590s witnessed an explosion of sonnet writing in England, a genuine literary "craze." Sonnet sequences, typically addressed to idealized beloveds, proliferated. The catalyst for this surge was the posthumous publication in 1591 of Sir Philip Sidney's Astrophil and Stella. This sequence of 108 sonnets, addressed to a woman identified with Penelope Devereux, electrified the literary world.

Sidney's sequence was revolutionary: his sonnets felt psychologically real, imbued with irony and self-awareness, and utilized Petrarchan conventions knowingly rather than mechanically. Sidney's Astrophil, when told by his Muse to "look in thy heart and write," both invokes and gently mocks the literary tradition he inhabits.

Following Sidney, the sonnet sequence became a prestige form.

  • Edmund Spenser's Amoretti (1595) charted a courtship that, uniquely, ended in happy marriage, deviating from the Petrarchan norm of unrequited love.
  • Samuel Daniel's Delia (1592) brought a melancholy grandeur to the form, exploring themes of time and poetic immortality, which Shakespeare would later intensify.
  • Michael Drayton's Idea (1594, revised through 1619) included some of the era's finest individual sonnets, notably the famous sixty-first ("Since there's no help, come let us kiss and part") with its stunning volta.

It was into this sophisticated and crowded literary landscape that William Shakespeare published his own sonnet sequence in 1609.

Shakespeare's Place in the Tradition

Shakespeare arrived at the sonnet sequence later than many of his peers, demonstrating not belatedness but profound mastery. He had absorbed the Petrarchan tradition, observed the innovations of Sidney, Spenser, Daniel, and Drayton, and then proceeded to radically remake the form from within.

His innovations were manifold and groundbreaking:

  • Addressees: He famously addressed the first 126 sonnets not to a woman, but to a young man, the "Fair Youth," thus upending the conventional heterosexual dynamic of the Petrarchan sequence. His subsequent sonnets (127–154) are addressed to a "Dark Lady," an explicit anti-type of the Petrarchan ideal: she is dark, physically present, and the object of a desire the speaker himself describes as degrading, contrasting sharply with the elevating nature of Petrarchan love.
  • Use of the Closing Couplet: While earlier poets used the final couplet as a summary or grace note, Shakespeare transformed it into a "structural weapon." At its best, his couplet reverses, undercuts, or paradoxically intensifies everything established in the preceding three quatrains, lending his sonnets unparalleled complexity and ironic depth.

After Shakespeare: The Sonnet Through the Ages

Shakespeare's sonnets, though masterpieces today, were not immediately recognized as such. After their 1609 publication, they garnered little critical attention for most of the 17th century. It was John Milton who significantly revived the form for serious English poetry, expanding its subject matter far beyond love lyric into public and devotional realms, addressing themes like the death of a friend, his blindness, and the English Civil War. Milton's sonnets combined Petrarchan structure with Shakespearean ambition.

The Romantics of the 19th century enthusiastically rediscovered Shakespeare's sonnets. William Wordsworth, a prolific sonneteer himself, famously wrote that "with this key / Shakespeare unlocked his heart." John Keats deeply absorbed Shakespeare's sonnets, their compressed intensity influencing his celebrated odes. Elizabeth Barrett Browning's Sonnets from the Portuguese (1850) introduced a profound feminine subjectivity to the form, containing one of the most beloved poems in English, "How do I love thee? Let me count the ways."

The Victorians and moderns further stretched the sonnet's boundaries. Gerard Manley Hopkins applied his innovative "sprung rhythm" to the sonnet, producing poems of explosive physical energy. Rilke composed his Sonnets to Orpheus in German. In the 20th century and beyond, poets like Edna St. Vincent Millay, E.E. Cummings, Claude McKay, and Seamus Heaney continued to write sonnets, some adhering strictly to traditional forms, others experimenting with expansive interpretations that retained only the spirit of fourteen lines and a volta.

The sonnet's enduring survival lies in its capacity to address a perennial human challenge: how to carefully articulate deep feelings within a concise, structured space. Shakespeare, understanding this better than anyone, ensured that his sonnets continue to resonate, discussed and admired, over four hundred years later.

Review Questions

  1. How did Giacomo da Lentini synthesize existing poetic traditions (Sicilian strambotto and Provençal troubadour poetry) to invent the sonnet, and what were its key formal characteristics (structure, line count, syllable count)?
  2. Explain Petrarch's immense influence on the sonnet form. What are some specific "Petrarchan conventions" he established, and how did they shape European love poetry for centuries?
  3. Discuss the roles of Sir Thomas Wyatt and Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey, in bringing the sonnet to England and adapting it for the English language. What was Surrey's most significant contribution to the English sonnet's structure and meter?
  4. Describe the "Elizabethan Sonnet Craze" of the late 16th century. How did Sir Philip Sidney's Astrophil and Stella influence subsequent sonneteers, and how did poets like Spenser and Daniel build upon or diverge from the established Petrarchan tradition?
  5. What were Shakespeare's major innovations to the sonnet form, particularly concerning his choice of addressees (Fair Youth and Dark Lady) and his distinctive use of the closing couplet? How did these innovations differentiate his sonnets from those of his predecessors and contemporaries, and what impact did they have on the form's expressive power?
