Study Guide: Sonnet XCVII

Overview

Sonnet XCVII begins the chapter's second movement: from anxious self-deprecation and moral warning to lyrical absence-elegy. The speaker compares his time apart from the beloved to winter — despite the fact that the actual season was summer and autumn, the richest and most abundant time of year. The entire natural world, at its most fertile and beautiful, felt barren and cold because the one who gives everything its meaning was absent.

Key Themes

The central theme is subjective winter within objective abundance. The paradox of experiencing summer as winter is a powerful expression of how completely the beloved determines the speaker's experience of reality. Autumn's harvest, normally a symbol of joyful abundance, becomes "hope of orphans, and unfather'd fruit" — because summer and its pleasures belong to the beloved, not to the season. The natural world is robbed of its significance by a single absence.

Poetic Devices

The central conceit — absence equals winter — operates through sustained seasonal imagery that inverts the expected emotional associations: autumn richness should be joyful but is experienced as bereft. The pregnant autumn personification ("big with rich increase," "widow'd wombs") gives fertility a quality of lonely sorrow. The birds' muted song in the couplet employs pathetic fallacy — even nature shares and reflects the speaker's grief. Alliteration in "freezings... fleeting" contributes to the sonnet's musical texture.

Estrangement Dimension

XCVII represents the chapter's emotional pivot. After twelve lines of self-negation and warning, the sequence turns to something more purely lyrical and less rhetorical. Estrangement is no longer a legal or moral problem to be argued about but a felt, physical reality expressed through the transformation of the entire natural world into an index of absence.
