Sonnets 28-32: Explorations of Grief, Despair, and Redemptive Love

Overview

This section delves into three profound sonnets (XXVIII, XXIX, and XXX) that explore the speaker's intense emotional states, from persistent sorrow and abject despair to the sudden, transformative power of remembering the beloved. Across these poems, Shakespeare employs intricate metaphors, personification, and a masterful control of emotional arc to portray the speaker's inner world and the profound impact of love.

Key Concepts

  • The Relentless Nature of Grief: Sonnet XXVIII vividly portrays how external forces (day, night, toil) conspire with internal sorrow to create an inescapable cycle of suffering. Even attempts at flattery or distraction prove futile against the speaker's deep-seated unhappiness.
  • Emotional Transformation and the Volta: Sonnet XXIX showcases one of the most dramatic emotional shifts in the sonnet sequence. It moves from intense self-loathing and envy to exultant joy, illustrating how a single, chance thought of the beloved can completely reverse a state of utter despair. The strategic placement of the "volta" (turn) at line 10 enhances this surprise and grace.
  • Memory as a Legal Tribunal: Sonnet XXX uses an elaborate "legal conceit," framing memory as a court session where past losses, dead friends, and cancelled sorrows are summoned and recounted. This metaphor underscores the systematic and exhaustive nature of grief, yet also highlights the beloved's power to definitively "settle" these emotional "debts" and "restore all losses."
  • Personification and Extended Metaphor: Across these sonnets, abstract concepts like Day, Night, and Memory are personified or become part of extended metaphors (e.g., day/night as torturers, memory as a court, the lark as ascending joy). These devices give vividness and agency to the speaker's internal struggles and the forces that affect him.
  • The Redemptive Power of the Beloved: In Sonnets XXIX and XXX, the memory of the "dear friend" or "thee" acts as a singular, overwhelming force capable of dispelling deep sorrow, transforming despair into joy, and settling the accumulated "accounts" of grief. This central theme underscores the profound importance of the beloved in the speaker's emotional landscape.

Important Terms

  • debarre'd: Excluded or shut out. In Sonnet XXVIII, it carries a legal connotation, implying a formal, almost judicial deprivation of rest.
  • swart-complexion'd night: Dark-faced night. A vivid personification in Sonnet XXVIII, depicting night as a dark-skinned figure.
  • twire: To peep or glance furtively. In Sonnet XXVIII, it describes stars that are not peeping out due to clouds, adding an intimate touch to the night sky.
  • gild'st the even: To make golden or illuminate the evening. In Sonnet XXVIII, the beloved's beauty is said to provide light in the absence of stars.
  • bootless: Without profit; useless; in vain. In Sonnet XXIX, "bootless cries" are prayers or complaints to an unresponsive heaven.
  • scope: Range, freedom of action, or opportunity. In Sonnet XXIX, the speaker envies others' "scope," feeling limited and deprived of possibility.
  • haply: By chance; perhaps; fortunately. A crucial word in Sonnet XXIX, signifying that the memory of the beloved arrives accidentally, not by conscious will.
  • lark: A skylark, a songbird associated with dawn, joy, and ascending flight in English poetry. In Sonnet XXIX, it symbolizes the speaker's joyful ascent from despair.
  • sullen: Heavy, gloomy, or slow to respond. In Sonnet XXIX, "sullen earth" refers both to the literal ground and the poet's own heaviness of spirit.
  • sessions: Formal sittings of a law court. In Sonnet XXX, this word establishes the central legal metaphor, portraying memory as a court where past grievances are reviewed.
  • dateless: Endless; without a fixed term or expiry date. In Sonnet XXX, "death's dateless night" emphasizes the permanence of death.
  • cancell'd woe: Discharged grief; a sorrow that was supposedly settled or crossed out like a debt. In Sonnet XXX, such grief can be reopened and re-felt.
  • expense: Expenditure, outlay, or loss. In Sonnet XXX, it refers to the outpouring of tears and the loss of what caused them.
  • fore-bemoaned: Previously mourned or lamented. In Sonnet XXX, it highlights the repetitive and exhausting nature of reliving old griefs.
  • tell o'er: To count over or reckon up. In Sonnet XXX, it relates to the accounting metaphor, meticulously listing sorrows.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet XXVIII: The Conspiracy of Day and Night

Sonnet XXVIII continues directly from Sonnet XXVII's theme of sleeplessness and profound distress. The core idea is that the natural order has been inverted to specifically torment the speaker: Day and Night, normally opposing forces, have "conspired" and "shake hands to torture me."

During the day, the speaker is consumed by physical labor or "toil," which keeps him separated from the beloved, causing suffering. At night, instead of finding the expected relief of rest, he is tormented by "grief about that distance," which prevents sleep. This creates a relentless cycle of oppression, where he is "day by night and night by day oppress'd."

The speaker attempts a futile strategy in the second quatrain: flattery. He tries to appease Day by claiming the beloved's brightness "dost him grace" even when clouds obscure the sun. Similarly, he flatters the "swart-complexion'd night" by suggesting the beloved "gild'st the even" (brightens the evening) when stars are absent. However, this flattery yields no reprieve. The concluding couplet delivers a stark and undeniable truth: "But day doth daily draw my sorrows longer, / And night doth nightly make grief’s length seem stronger." There is no escape, only a wry acknowledgment of his trapped state.

The word "debarre'd" carries a legal weight, implying a formal exclusion from rest, almost as if his suffering is a judicial sentence. The personification of day and night as enemies who unite only to persecute the speaker is a striking example of prosopopoeia, giving these abstract forces a tangible, malicious agency that makes his grief feel inhabited. This sustained longing and sleeplessness anticipate later sonnets dealing with absence, such as Sonnets 43 and 44, which more explicitly address the nature of separation.

Sonnet XXIX: From Disgrace to Kingship

Sonnet XXIX presents one of the most dramatic and complete emotional arcs within the sonnet sequence, transitioning from profound despair to exultant joy within its fourteen lines, all catalyzed by the memory of a single person.

The opening octave is a powerful depiction of self-loathing and dejection. The poet describes himself as "in disgrace with fortune and men's eyes," feeling outcast and isolated. He "beweep[s] my outcast state" and "trouble[s] deaf heaven with my bootless cries," implying his prayers or complaints are unheard and useless. He curses his fate, envying others who are "more rich in hope," "featur'd like him," "with friends possess'd," and possess "this man’s art, and that man’s scope." He is "With what I most enjoy contented least," indicating a pervasive dissatisfaction with his own life, bordering on self-despising.

The emotional turning point, or "volta," is strategically placed not at the conventional line 9, but at line 10, mid-sentence: "Yet in these thoughts my self almost despising, / Haply I think on thee." The word "Haply" is crucial, suggesting that the memory of the beloved is not willed or summoned, but arrives accidentally, a moment of unexpected grace. This accidental nature makes the subsequent transformation all the more moving and powerful.

With this thought, his state instantly changes. The poem employs one of Shakespeare's most kinetic and joyful images: "Like to the lark arising / From sullen earth, sings hymns at heaven's gate." The lark, a symbol of dawn and joyful song, ascends from the "sullen earth" (representing both the literal ground and the poet's heavy, gloomy spirit) to sing, embodying pure ascent and release. The final couplet reaffirms this transformation, declaring that "thy sweet love remember’d such wealth brings / That then I scorn to change my state with kings." The wealth of remembered love far outweighs any worldly fortune or status, providing genuine contentment.

The unusual placement of the volta at line 10 allows the despair to accumulate for nine full lines, making the sudden arrival of grace feel even more impactful. The lark image doesn't just describe the change; it enacts it, allowing the reader to feel the ascent. This sonnet's movement from despair to joy through the beloved's memory is a theme echoed in other sonnets, notably Sonnet XXX. The speaker's self-deprecating envy also foreshadows the self-doubt seen in Sonnets 87 and 88. Its clear emotional journey has made it a favorite for musical settings.

Sonnet XXX: The Sessions of Sweet Silent Thought

Sonnet XXX is renowned for its sustained and intricate legal conceit, where memory is imagined as a formal court session. The speaker "summon[s] up remembrance of things past" as if bringing cases before a judge.

The first three quatrains meticulously detail the accumulated losses and griefs that memory brings forth. The speaker sighs over "the lack of many a thing I sought" and "with old woes new wail my dear time’s waste." He "drown[s] an eye, unused to flow," weeping for "precious friends hid in death’s dateless night" (emphasizing the endless, permanent nature of death). He "weep[s] afresh love’s long since cancell’d woe" – griefs that were supposedly settled but are now reopened. He mourns "the expense of tears of many a vanish’d sight," lamenting both the tears shed and the things lost.

The legal and financial vocabulary accumulates throughout these lines: "sessions," "summon up," "cancell'd woe," "expense," "tell o'er" (to count over or reckon up), and "sad account." The speaker effectively grieves "at grievances foregone," reliving old sorrows and "heavily from woe to woe tell o’er / The sad account of fore-bemoaned moan, / Which I new pay as if not paid before." This illustrates the exhausting, repetitive, and seemingly endless nature of grief, where old pains are re-experienced with fresh intensity, like an unpaid debt.

The sonnet's "volta" and resolution arrive in the couplet with striking abruptness and power: "But if the while I think on thee, dear friend, / All losses are restor’d and sorrows end." A single thought of the "dear friend" completely cancels every accumulated debt and sorrow. The legal metaphor is carried right to the end, with "all losses are restor’d" implying that the accounts are settled, the ledger cleared, and the judgment reversed. This sonnet’s structure and emotional architecture are widely considered masterful, providing a perfect illustration of the beloved's redemptive power. It closely parallels Sonnet XXIX in demonstrating how the memory of the friend can alleviate and even eliminate profound grief.

Review Questions

  1. In Sonnet XXVIII, how does the speaker use personification of Day and Night to convey his state of torment? What does his futile flattery reveal about his emotional desperation?
  2. Sonnet XXIX features a dramatic emotional turn. Identify the specific lines and words that mark this "volta." How does the accidental nature of remembering the beloved ("Haply") contribute to the poem's overall emotional impact?
  3. Discuss the significance of the "lark" image in Sonnet XXIX. How does it function beyond a simple simile to represent the speaker's transformation? What does it suggest about the nature of joy and despair?
  4. Explain the extended legal conceit in Sonnet XXX. How do words like "sessions," "summon up," "cancell'd woe," and "tell o'er" contribute to this metaphor? How does the couplet resolve this elaborate accumulation of grief within the legal framework?
  5. Compare and contrast the way the "dear friend" or "thee" provides relief in Sonnet XXIX versus Sonnet XXX. Are these acts of relief presented similarly or differently, and what does this tell us about the beloved's diverse impact on the speaker's emotional state?
