Study Guide: Sonnet XXI — "So is it not with me as with that Muse"

Overview

Sonnet XXI is a poem about poetic method — specifically, a defense of honest, restrained praise over the fashionable hyperbole of rival poets. The speaker distances himself from a "Muse stirr'd by a painted beauty" who piles up cosmic comparisons (sun, moon, sea, April flowers) in what the speaker calls a "couplement of proud compare." By contrast, the speaker claims to write simply and truly, making only the honest claim that his beloved is "as fair / As any mother's child" — a genuine compliment free of exaggeration.

Key Themes

Sincerity vs. Hyperbole in Praise. The rival poet's extravagance is implicitly criticized as sales rhetoric — praise designed to impress rather than to tell the truth. The speaker's restrained method is presented as morally superior: "true in love, but truly write."

The Anti-Petrarchan Stance. The Petrarchan tradition in sonnets relied heavily on exaggerated comparisons (beloved's eyes like suns, hair like gold wire, etc.). Sonnet XXI rejects this tradition in favor of sober, honest praise — though paradoxically, the poem's very rejection of hyperbole is itself a sophisticated poetic move.

The Relationship Between Praise and Truth. The closing line — "I will not praise that purpose not to sell" — introduces a marketplace metaphor that equates excessive praise with advertising. True praise, the speaker implies, does not need to sell anything.

Literary Devices

  • Irony: the speaker criticizes rivals' comparisons while using comparisons himself
  • Catalogue: the rival's cosmic comparisons (sun, moon, sea, April flowers)
  • Rhetorical question: implied in "So is it not with me"
  • Metaphor: praise as commercial selling
  • Allusion: the tradition of the Petrarchan blazon, which the speaker subverts

Significance

Sonnet XXI creates self-aware distance between Shakespeare's speaker and the conventions of his own tradition. Its meta-poetic argument about how to praise honestly is central to the chapter's meditation on beauty and its representation.
