Study Guide: Sonnet XV

Summary

Sonnet XV opens with a universal observation: everything that grows holds its peak "but a little moment." Life is a stage where the stars secretly govern human fate; men, like plants, vaunt in their youthful sap, peak, and then are worn "out of memory." Contemplating this universal pattern brings the youth vividly into the poet's sight — rich in youth but already under siege by Time and Decay, who conspire together to turn his bright day into "sullied night." The couplet delivers a dramatic shift: for the first time in the sequence, the poet himself enters the fight. "All in war with Time for love of you, / As he takes from you, I engraft you new." Poetry — not children — is offered as a means of preservation.

Key Literary Devices

  • World-as-stage: "This huge stage presenteth nought but shows" — the familiar Renaissance theatrum mundi metaphor.
  • Plant metaphor: Men and plants alike governed by the same sky, growing and declining.
  • Personification: "Wasteful Time debateth with Decay" — Time and Decay as co-conspirators actively plotting against the youth.
  • Grafting metaphor: "Engraft you new" — poetry as horticultural grafting, inserting the youth's living essence into the rootstock of the verse.

Thematic Significance

Sonnet XV is the sequence's pivot. For fourteen sonnets Shakespeare has argued that children are the only answer to Time's destruction. Now, in the couplet's two lines, he offers a second answer: his own verse. "I engraft you new" is the poet's declaration of war against Time, using poetry as the weapon. This introduces the immortality-through-verse theme that dominates Sonnets 18 onward. Within the procreation sequence, Sonnet XV is the moment when the poet's personal investment becomes not just emotional (as in Sonnet X's "for love of me") but artistic and active — he will fight Time himself, with his pen.
