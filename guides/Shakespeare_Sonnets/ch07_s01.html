Chapter 7, Section 1: Devouring Time

Overview

This section introduces Chapter 7 of Shakespeare's sonnet sequence, highlighting Time as the primary antagonist. It explores the sonnets' deep engagement with themes of mortality and decay, contrasting Time's destructive power with the fragile, yet enduring, hope that poetry offers as a lasting legacy. The chapter presents a range of moods and focuses, culminating in four particularly significant sonnets that encapsulate these central ideas.

Key Concepts

  • Time as the Antagonist: Throughout the sonnets in this chapter, Time is personified as a powerful, destructive force that erodes beauty, destroys structures, and brings about ruin and death.
  • Mortality and Decay: A central theme is the inevitability of death, aging, and the physical degradation that affects all living things and human creations. The sonnets grapple intensely with the transient nature of life and beauty.
  • The Power of Poetry: The sonnets ultimately present the act of writing and the resulting verse as humanity's only enduring defense against Time's destructive march, offering a path for beauty and memory to persist.
  • Varied Perspectives on Time: The sonnets approach the theme of time from different angles, sometimes depicting it as a mythological, external force, and other times reflecting on the speaker's personal aging and anxieties.
  • The Youth's Role: Occasionally, the sonnets momentarily divert to explore the moral character or inherent beauty of the youth addressed in the sequence, though these reflections remain shadowed by the overarching theme of time.
  • Pinnacle Sonnets: Sonnets 60, 64, 65, and 73 are identified as exceptionally profound and moving explorations of time, mortality, and the potential of poetry.

Important Terms

  • Antagonist: The principal opponent or adversary of the main character or speaker; in this context, Time is presented as the primary force working against beauty and life.
  • Mortality: The state of being subject to death; the inevitability of dying.
  • Decay: The process of rotting or decomposition, or the deterioration or decline of something over time.
  • Elegiac: Relating to or characteristic of an elegy (a mournful, melancholic, or plaintive poem, especially a lament for the dead); expressing sorrow or lamentation.
  • Diptych: A pair of pictures on two panels, typically hinged together; here used metaphorically to describe two sonnets (64 and 65) that are closely linked in theme and argument.
  • Sovereignty: Supreme power or authority; in this context, Time's absolute control over all things.
  • Couplet: The final two lines of a sonnet, typically rhyming and providing a summary, resolution, or twist to the preceding twelve lines.

Detailed Notes

Time's Unrelenting Destructive Force

The sonnets in Chapter 7 establish Time as a formidable enemy, responsible for the destruction of all things beautiful and enduring. It is depicted as a force that "devours beauty, crumbles towers, swallows kingdoms, and turns the freshest face into a map of ruin." This portrayal is not merely metaphorical but takes on an almost mythological quality, referred to as "Time's fell hand" (Sonnet 64) and "the wrackful siege of battering days" (Sonnet 65), against which even the most robust materials like brass and stone offer no lasting resistance. This powerful imagery underscores the hopelessness of resisting Time through physical means alone.

Inward Reflection on Aging and Personal Mortality

While some sonnets portray Time as an external, monumental force, others turn inward, exploring the speaker's personal experience of aging. These sonnets, often characterized by an "elegiac quietness," offer intimate contemplations on the passage of life. Sonnet 73 is highlighted as a prime example, using a series of poignant metaphors to describe the speaker's "late-autumn life":

  • Bare boughs: Suggesting the end of growth and the approach of winter.
  • Fading twilight: Evoking the diminishing light of day and the approach of night.
  • A fire consuming its own ashes: Illustrating life burning down and feeding on its own remnants, nearing extinction. This deeply personal exploration makes such sonnets among the most cherished in the English language.

Fleeting Diversions and Underlying Shadows

Chapter 7's sonnets occasionally diverge from the direct confrontation with time to consider other themes, though these digressions are always "shadowed by time's approach."

  • Youth's Moral Character: Sonnets like 69 and 70 might pause to examine the moral integrity of the young man addressed in the sequence.
  • Youth as Beauty's Treasury: Sonnets 67 and 68 position the youth as nature's last true repository of genuine beauty, a precious but ultimately transient gift.
  • Poet's Anxieties: Sonnets 72 and 76 reveal the poet's own self-deprecating concerns about the worth and longevity of his own verse. Despite these shifts in focus, the destructive power of time remains an implicit or explicit backdrop, reminding the reader of the fragility of beauty, virtue, and artistic endeavor.

The Pinnacle Sonnets: Deep Meditations

Four sonnets are singled out as masterpieces within this chapter and the entire sequence, profoundly exploring time and its human responses:

  • Sonnet 60: The March of Minutes to Death

    • Opens with the arresting image of "waves advancing on a shore, each replacing the last."
    • This image serves as a powerful metaphor for the "unstoppable march of our minutes toward death," illustrating the continuous, incremental, and inevitable progression of time that leads to mortality.

  • Sonnets 64 and 65: A Diptych of Despair and Defiance

    • These two sonnets form a connected pair, exploring time's destructive capabilities and searching for a solution.
    • Sonnet 64: Surveys the ruins of human civilization—crumbling monuments, overthrown kingdoms—as stark evidence of time's irresistible power. It concludes with a profound sense of grief that "cannot choose but weep," recognizing that everything beautiful must eventually succumb.
    • Sonnet 65: Follows up on this despair with a desperate question: how can beauty possibly survive when "nothing made of matter" can endure time's onslaught? The answer is a "defiant miracle": "black ink," signifying that poetry is the only means to preserve beauty and memory against decay.

  • Sonnet 73: Metaphors of Late Life and Enduring Love

    • Considered one of the most intimate of the time sonnets, it offers three exquisite metaphors for the speaker's own aging, which were detailed earlier (bare boughs, fading twilight, fire consuming ashes).
    • From these images of decline, the sonnet draws a couplet of "unsurpassed tenderness," suggesting that the awareness of impending loss only strengthens the love between the speaker and the youth, making it more precious and cherished.

Poetry as the Enduring Answer

A fundamental conviction underlies all these sonnets: the belief that the "act of writing" and the resulting "verse" are the only truly human response to time's absolute authority. In a world where all physical forms of beauty and achievement are destined to perish, poetry offers a unique kind of immortality. The very existence of the book containing these sonnets serves as a testament to this conviction—that words can outlast the devouring hand of time.

Review Questions

  1. How does the section describe Time as an "antagonist" in Shakespeare's sonnets? Provide specific examples of Time's destructive power mentioned in the text.
  2. What is the core argument presented in this chapter about the relationship between poetry and time? How does "black ink" become a "defiant miracle" in Sonnet 65?
  3. Compare and contrast the portrayal of Time as an "almost mythological force" (Sonnets 64, 65) with the more "elegiac quietness" of Sonnet 73. How do these different approaches contribute to the chapter's overall themes?
  4. Choose one of the "pinnacle sonnets" (60, 64, 65, or 73) and explain its central metaphor or image. How does this image contribute to the sonnet's exploration of time and mortality?
  5. Beyond the direct confrontation with time, what other "apparent departures" in theme do some sonnets in this chapter take (e.g., youth's character, poet's anxieties)? How do these seemingly unrelated themes remain "shadowed by time's approach"?
