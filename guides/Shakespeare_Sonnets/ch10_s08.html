Sonnet CVII — Study Guide

Overview

Sonnet CVII is one of the most historically debated poems in the sequence because it seems to reference a specific public event — commonly linked to Queen Elizabeth I's death (1603) or a crisis survived during her reign. Whatever the historical occasion, the poem uses it to declare that love and verse have outlasted all prophesied doom.

Meaning

The speaker opens by insisting that no personal fear, and no collective prophetic dread, could end his love — which had been "supposed as forfeit" to some confin'd doom. A great crisis (the mortal moon's eclipse) has passed; the gloomy prophets were proved wrong; peace now reigns. In this changed atmosphere, love looks fresh, Death yields ("subscribes"), and the speaker will live through his verse while Death triumphs only over the silent. The couplet offers the beloved a monument of verse that will outlast even the brass tombs of tyrants.

Literary Devices

  • Allusion: The "mortal moon" most likely alludes to Elizabeth I or a major historical crisis.
  • Symbolism: Olives represent peace; the scythe and "speechless tribes" represent Death's domain.
  • Antithesis: Those who are silenced by Death vs. the poet who lives in verse.
  • Conceit: Verse as monument, superior to "tombs of brass."

Themes of Constancy and Faithfulness

The poem argues that love's constancy survives not just personal doubt but historical upheaval. Despite cosmic fears and prophesied endings, the poet's faithfulness endures. The verse produced by that faithfulness becomes the beloved's permanent monument, proof that constancy outlasts even empires.
