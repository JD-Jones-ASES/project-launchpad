Study Guide: Sonnet LXIII

Summary

Sonnet LXIII anticipates the beloved's future aging with both grief and resolution. The speaker imagines the day when Time's "injurious hand" will crush and overwear his love, when hours will drain the beloved's blood and fill his brow with lines, when "youthful morn" has traveled on to "age's steepy night," and all the treasures of the beloved's spring are stolen away. Having imagined this future ruin in full, the speaker states his present purpose: he now fortifies himself against "confounding age's cruel knife," preparing a defense so that the beloved's beauty will never be cut from memory — even though the beloved's life will eventually end. The couplet delivers the triumphant resolution: the beloved's beauty shall be seen in "these black lines," and both the lines and the beloved shall live, the beloved remaining forever "green" (youthful) within the poem.

Key Themes

  • Verse as a fortress against time: The speaker uses military language ("fortify," "confound") to frame poetry as defense.
  • The distinction between life and beauty: Verse can preserve beauty in memory even after physical life ends.
  • Youth and spring: The beloved's beauty is figured as a "spring" treasure that time steals.

Key Literary Devices

  • Personification: Time is given an "injurious hand" and a "cruel knife."
  • Metaphor: "Youthful morn" / "age's steepy night" — the life journey as a day's arc.
  • Military metaphor: "Fortify" and "confound" cast the speaker's poetic project as preparation for battle.

Vocabulary

  • O'erworn: Worn out, used up by time
  • Steepy: Steep; a steep descent into darkness
  • Travell'd on: Journeyed, progressed
  • Fortify: To build defenses in preparation for attack
  • Confounding: Thwarting, ruining the plans of
