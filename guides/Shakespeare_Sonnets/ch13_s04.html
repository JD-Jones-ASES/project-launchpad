Study Guide: Sonnets 152-154 from "The Dark Lady: Reckoning"

Overview

This section concludes the intensely personal and morally fraught Dark Lady sequence with a stark self-reckoning in Sonnet 152, followed by two classical, Anacreontic epigrams (Sonnets 153-154). Sonnet 152 delves into the speaker's profound self-indictment for perjury and self-deception in his affair, exposing the "foul lie" he told about his mistress's fairness. Sonnets 153 and 154 then transition to a mythological narrative about Cupid's torch creating a healing hot spring, only to paradoxically demonstrate the incurable nature of the speaker's own love-sickness.

Key Concepts

  • Moral Reckoning and Self-Indictment: Sonnet 152 presents the speaker's deepest confession of guilt, acknowledging he is "more perjured" than his mistress, having willingly deceived himself and others about her true nature.
  • The Language of Oaths and Perjury: The sonnet heavily employs legal and religious vocabulary (forsworn, bed-vow, perjured, oaths) to frame the affair not as mere romantic carelessness, but as a grave moral and spiritual transgression.
  • Self-Deception and the "Foul Lie": The speaker admits to having "gave eyes to blindness" and "sworn thee fair" despite evidence to the contrary, revealing his profound self-deception and the ultimate "lie" that sustained his perception of the Dark Lady.
  • Anacreontic Epigrams as a Coda: Sonnets 153 and 154 are adaptations of a classical Greek epigram, providing a mythological framework to conclude the intensely emotional sequence. This shift is debated as either a formal artistic choice, an editorial decision, or a functional attempt to universalize the speaker's personal suffering.
  • Love's Incurable Fever: Both 153 and 154 narrate the myth of Cupid's brand creating a healing spring, but the speaker finds no cure for his own love-sickness, concluding that love's fire is intractable and untransformable by external remedies.
  • The Paradox of Love's Power: Sonnet 154's final couplet, "Love's fire heats water, water cools not love," encapsulates the central paradox: love has the power to transform its environment, yet nothing in the environment can transform or extinguish love itself.

Important Terms

  • forsworn: Having broken a solemn oath; perjured. Carries significant legal and religious weight, indicating a breach of faith before God.
  • bed-vow: The marriage vow; specifically, the oath of fidelity made in the context of the marriage bed. Breaking it signifies adultery.
  • perjured: Guilty of perjury; having given false testimony under oath. This word recurs to emphasize the speaker's profound self-indictment.
  • gave eyes to blindness: A metaphor meaning willingly blinded oneself; surrendered one's capacity for clear sight in order to maintain a desired (but false) perception.
  • enlighten thee: To illuminate her; to make her appear radiant or virtuous. The speaker tried to shed light on the "darkness" of the Dark Lady.
  • brand (Cupid's brand): A burning torch or firebrand, Cupid's traditional weapon used to ignite love in hearts.
  • Dian (Diana): The Roman goddess of the hunt, the moon, and chastity. Her "votaries" or followers were sworn to virginity.
  • dateless lively heat: A warmth that endures without end; perpetual heat, meaning without fixed term or expiration.
  • seething bath: A hot spring or thermal pool, "seething" meaning boiling or bubbling, often associated with healing properties.
  • distemper'd: Disordered; out of proper balance; sick. Relates to the humoral medical vocabulary of the time.
  • Love-god: Cupid, the Roman god of love (or Eros in Greek mythology), typically depicted as a winged boy with a torch and bow.
  • votary: A devoted follower; one who has taken a vow, often religious or quasi-religious.
  • general of hot desire: A military metaphor for Cupid, casting him as the commander-in-chief of erotic longing.
  • quenched: Extinguished; put out; cooled. Refers to the action of plunging Cupid's torch into water.
  • thrall: A slave; a person held in servitude; one entirely subject to another's will. The speaker describes himself as enslaved to his mistress.
  • Love's fire heats water, water cools not love: The final aphorism of the sequence, an elegant chiasmus that states love transforms its environment but is itself untransformable.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet 152: The Moral Nadir

  • Speaker's Confession: The sonnet opens with the speaker confessing he is "forsworn" (having broken an oath) by loving the Dark Lady.
  • Dark Lady's Transgressions: He accuses her of being "twice forsworn":
    1. Breaking her "bed-vow" (marriage oath) through adultery.
    2. "Vowing new hate after new love bearing" – suggesting betrayal or emotional inconstancy even within their affair.
  • Greater Guilt: The speaker immediately undercuts his accusation, stating he breaks "twenty" oaths compared to her "two." He admits, "I am perjur'd most."
  • Nature of His Perjury: His oaths were all lies, "to misuse thee," meaning to deceive himself and her. He swore to her "deep kindness," "love," "truth," and "constancy."
  • Self-Deception: To maintain these lies, he "gave eyes to blindness" or "made them swear against the thing they see," deliberately ignoring her faults and fabricating her virtues. He tried to "enlighten" her, to make her appear radiant despite her inherent "darkness."
  • The "Foul Lie": The couplet delivers a devastating self-indictment: he is "more perjured" because he swore her "fair" – a lie about her beauty and moral purity that was the foundation of their relationship (linking to Sonnets 127 and 130).
  • Moral Climax: This sonnet represents the lowest moral point in the Dark Lady sequence, a complete and bitter reckoning of mutual deceit, with the speaker taking the greater share of the blame for his self-delusion.

Context: The Language of Oaths and Perjury

  • Elizabethan Significance: In Shakespeare's era, oaths carried immense legal and spiritual weight. Breaking an oath was a sin against God and could be a crime punishable by law.
  • Framing Love's Failure: The speaker uses this intense vocabulary to frame his romantic failures as serious transgressions, not mere romantic follies.
  • Auditor's Report: The counting of oaths ("twenty" vs. "two") evokes an audit, where the speaker's moral debt is revealed to be far greater than he had imagined.

Connections

  • Culmination of Moral Reckoning: Extends themes of mutual accusation and guilt from Sonnet 142.
  • Contrast with Fair Youth: Unlike the strategic self-accusation in Sonnets 88 and 89 (to absolve the Fair Youth), here the self-accusation is a genuine, painful reckoning.
  • "Sworn Thee Fair": Directly connects to the premise of the Dark Lady sonnets (127, 130), where her unconventional beauty was "redeemed" by the speaker's choice to call it fair. This sonnet reveals that choice as systematic self-deception.

Sonnet 153: Cupid, Diana's Maid, and the Incurable Fever

  • Anacreontic Epigram: This sonnet is the first of two adaptations of a classical Greek epigram, marking a shift in tone and style.
  • The Myth: Cupid falls asleep, leaving his "brand" (love-torch) unattended. A virgin "maid of Dian" (Diana, goddess of chastity) finds it and plunges it into a cold fountain.
  • Transformation: The fountain absorbs love's fire, becoming a "seething bath" – a hot spring with "dateless lively heat," renowned for its healing properties against "strange maladies."
  • Speaker's Plight: The speaker, "sick withal" (sick with love's fever) and "a sad distemper'd guest," seeks this miraculous cure.
  • Failure of Cure: He finds no help. The bath's healing power stems from "Love's fire," and the source of that fire for him is his "mistress' eyes." Therefore, the very source of the cure is also the source of his illness, rendering him incurable.
  • Theme: Love's fever is intractable; external remedies derived from love's power cannot cure the love-sickness originating from its ultimate source.

Context: The Anacreontic Source

  • Greek Origin: Sonnets 153 and 154 are based on an epigram from the Anacreontea, a collection of poems in the style of the ancient Greek poet Anacreon.
  • Renaissance Knowledge: The story was known in Renaissance Europe through translations.
  • Debate over Significance: Scholars debate whether this coda is a graceful return to classical allusion, an editorial placement, or a functional attempt to mythologize the speaker's personal suffering.

Sonnet 154: The Retold Epigram and Love's Final Paradox

  • Retelling the Myth: This sonnet retells the same story as 153 with slight variations:
    • "The little Love-god" (Cupid).
    • "Many nymphs that vow'd chaste life to keep" (plural).
    • "The fairest votary" (one specific virgin) takes the "heart-inflaming brand."
    • "General of hot desire" is disarmed.
    • The brand is "quenched in a cool well," creating a "bath and healthful remedy."
  • Speaker's Continued Suffering: Again, the speaker, now explicitly his "mistress' thrall" (slave), comes to the bath for a cure and finds none.
  • The Final Aphorism: The couplet delivers the ultimate conclusion of the sequence: "Love's fire heats water, water cools not love."
  • Meaning of the Paradox: This elegant chiasmus (fire/water, water/love) states that love (like Cupid's brand) has the power to transform its environment (heat cold water into a healing spring), but nothing in the external world (water, or any remedy) can diminish or cool the intense, burning nature of love itself.
  • Conclusion of the Sequence: The sequence ends not with resolution or a cure, but with an irrefutable demonstration of love's inherent, untransforming power and the speaker's enduring, incurable fever.

Context: The Coda and Its Classical Precedents

  • Scholarly Debate: The placement of these two mythological sonnets after 152 intensely personal ones is a point of debate:
    1. Formal Bow: A return to impersonal classical myth as a graceful postlude.
    2. Editorial Choice: Suggests the publisher (Thomas Thorpe) assembled the sequence, and the placement might not be Shakespeare's original design.
    3. Functional Reading: The speaker attempts to translate his personal "incurable fever" into a universal myth, seeking understanding through allegory.

Review Questions

  1. In Sonnet 152, the speaker quantifies his moral failures, claiming to have broken "twenty" oaths to the Dark Lady's "two." Does this arithmetic truly matter, or is it a rhetorical device? What is the overall effect of framing moral failure in such quantifiable terms?
  2. Sonnet 152 begins by accusing the Dark Lady but ends with the speaker's profound self-condemnation ("more perjured I... so foul a lie"). What does this shift in focus suggest about the speaker's emotional and moral state at this late stage in the sequence?
  3. Shakespeare uses the language of law ("forsworn," "perjured," "oaths") in Sonnet 152 and earlier commercial language ("bonds," "revenues," "rents") in other sonnets (e.g., 134, 142) to describe love's failures. What does the poet gain by framing personal, romantic transgressions in the vocabulary of legal and financial accountability?
  4. Sonnets 153 and 154 retell essentially the same mythological narrative about Cupid's brand. Why might Shakespeare choose to present the story twice, with only minor variations? What is the cumulative effect of this repetition on the reader and the final message of the sequence?
  5. The Anacreontic coda (Sonnets 153-154) concludes the entire sonnet sequence. Discuss the different interpretations offered for this choice (e.g., formal bow, editorial placement, functional mythologizing). Which interpretation do you find most compelling, and how does it affect your understanding of the sonnets as a whole?
