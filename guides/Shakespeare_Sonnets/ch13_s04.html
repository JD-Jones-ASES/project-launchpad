Study Guide: Sonnet CXLV

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet CXLV narrates a small, emotionally precise episode: the Dark Lady says "I hate" to the languishing speaker, then, seeing his misery, has mercy and amends her words to "not you," saving his life. The movement from "I hate" through mercy to the amended phrase enacts a micro-drama of rejection, compassion, and relief. Unlike most Dark Lady sonnets, which are entangled in guilt, accusation, and paradox, CXLV is unusually tender — mercy actually arrives and acts, and the speaker is saved by a simple grammatical exception.

Key Poetic Devices

  • Iambic tetrameter: CXLV is metrically anomalous — the only sonnet in the sequence written in tetrameter (four feet per line) rather than pentameter, giving it a lighter, song-like rhythm
  • "Hate away" / Hathaway pun: many scholars read a pun on Anne Hathaway's surname, suggesting this may be an early poem repurposed for the sequence
  • Day/night simile: "as gentle day / Doth follow night" maps the amended phrase onto a cosmic renewal
  • "Gentle doom": Elizabethan "doom" meaning judgment, capturing her tongue's habitual kindness

Central Themes

Mercy and moral struggle: Unlike the harsher surrounding sonnets, this poem shows mercy actually functioning — a rare moment of relenting in the chapter. Words as life and death: The speaker languishes and is "saved" by a two-word adjustment; language bears ultimate power over existence. Shame and reprieve: The context of "hate" implies the speaker has done something reproachable; mercy does not deny the fault but suspends its consequences. The sonnet's probable early origin (pre-Dark Lady sequence) gives it an innocence and lightness that throws the surrounding darkness into relief.
