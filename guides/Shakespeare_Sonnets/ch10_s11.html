Sonnet CX — Study Guide

Overview

Sonnet CX is the most explicit confession of wandering in the chapter. Where Sonnet CIX deflected the charge of faithlessness, Sonnet CX opens with "Alas! 'tis true" — a direct and unambiguous admission. Yet the poem turns this admission into a deeper proof of the beloved's worth.

Meaning

The speaker confesses to going here and there, making a spectacle of himself ("motley"), wounding his own integrity, and making cheap what should have been most precious. He admits to having looked at truth "askance and strangely." Yet these "blenches" — his strayings and diversions — gave his heart a renewed youth, because comparing others to the beloved only proved the beloved was best. The final quatrain makes the vow: no more testing of newer attachments. The couplet asks for welcome to the beloved's breast — "next my heaven the best."

Literary Devices

  • Anaphora: "Made... made... Gor'd... sold" lists transgressions in rapid accumulation.
  • Metaphor: "Motley" — the jester's patchwork costume — represents self-degradation.
  • Paradox: Straying proved the beloved's supremacy rather than undermining it.
  • Superlative intensification: "Most most loving breast" pushes language beyond normal limits of praise.

Themes of Constancy and Faithfulness

This sonnet shows constancy tested and ultimately strengthened by failure. The speaker's infidelities become, paradoxically, evidence of his beloved's incomparable worth. Returning to the beloved after ranging is a richer form of faithfulness than one never tested by experience.
