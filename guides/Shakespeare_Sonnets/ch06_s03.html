Sonnets 48-52: Love from Afar and the Paradoxes of Possession

Overview

This section delves into five sonnets (48-52) that explore the complexities of love, absence, and possession. The speaker grapples with the inability to secure his beloved, pre-emptively anticipates rejection, and vividly describes the emotional weight of separation, contrasted with the exhilarating speed of desire upon return. The sequence culminates in a reflection on how scarcity and controlled access can enhance the value and pleasure of something precious.

Key Concepts

  • The Paradox of Possession: Sonnets 48 and 52 collectively explore the challenge of possessing and valuing the beloved. Sonnet 48 laments that the most precious "jewel" (the beloved) cannot be physically locked away and is vulnerable even to "truth," while Sonnet 52, conversely, argues that infrequent access preserves the intensity of pleasure and the beloved's special value, much like a treasure kept under lock and key for rare moments.
  • Pre-emptive Self-Condemnation: In Sonnet 49, the speaker prepares for a future where the beloved might abandon him by internalizing and arguing the case against himself. He acknowledges his own unworthiness and defects, essentially disarming potential criticism by agreeing with it in advance.
  • The Journey as Emotional Metaphor: Sonnets 50 and 51 use the physical act of travel as a powerful metaphor for the speaker's emotional state. The slow, heavy journey away from the beloved (Sonnet 50) embodies grief and reluctance, while the imagined swift, desire-driven journey back (Sonnet 51) highlights the overwhelming force of love and longing.
  • The Nature of Desire: Sonnet 51 specifically elevates desire above physical limitations. It suggests that true love and desire are so potent they can outrun any earthly means of travel, operating with a "winged speed" beyond the capabilities of even the fastest horse.
  • The Value of Rarity: Sonnet 52 proposes that restricting constant access to something beloved or treasured can enhance its perceived value and the pleasure derived from it. The "seldom pleasure" argument suggests that anticipation and scarcity keep an experience "fine" and prevent it from becoming mundane.

Important Terms

  • trifles: Something of little value. The speaker's worldly possessions are "trifles" compared to the beloved, making the beloved ironically impossible to secure by ordinary means.
  • truest bars: The most reliable or secure locks. "Truest" implies bars that cannot be deceived or corrupted.
  • vulgar thief: An ordinary, common thief. The term "vulgar" (from Latin vulgus, the common people) suggests that the world at large is a potential threat to the beloved's safety.
  • gentle closure: A soft or mild enclosure. The speaker imagines his breast (heart) as a gentle, open vault, contrasting with the secure chests for his possessions.
  • truth proves thievish: A paradox suggesting that fidelity, virtue, or honesty itself can become a kind of thief. The beloved's genuine worth is so overwhelming that even virtuous things are drawn irresistibly toward him.
  • audit: A formal accounting or balancing of books. The beloved is imagined as eventually calling for a reckoning on the relationship to assess its worth.
  • advis'd respects: Considered, deliberate judgments. "Advis'd" suggests careful, rational reasoning rather than impulse, implying the beloved's departure would be well-thought-out.
  • strangely pass: To walk by as a stranger. "Strangely" means "in the manner of a stranger," indicating the beloved treating the speaker as an unknown person.
  • settled gravity: A composed, serious demeanor. The beloved's love, once converted from passion to reasoned seriousness, will have logical grounds for its departure.
  • ensconce me here: To shelter or fortify oneself in this position. The speaker takes up a defensive stance within the knowledge of his own unworthiness.
  • mine own desert: What I deserve; the speaker's acknowledged merit or lack thereof. He knows he deserves to be left.
  • heavy: Both "sorrowful" and "physically weighted." The speaker's grief makes him a literal and emotional burden on his horse.
  • plods dully: Moves slowly and without energy. "Dully" captures both the horse's lack of spirit and the speaker's own numbness from grief.
  • instinct: Innate impulse; natural prompting. The horse is imagined as sensing its rider's unhappiness without being told, deepening the pathos.
  • the bloody spur: A pointed device to urge a horse forward, used with such force as to draw blood. It suggests the speaker's desperation and anger, yet it fails to move the horse meaningfully.
  • my grief lies onward, my joy behind: The sonnet's governing reversal. "Onward" is the direction of travel away from the beloved, while "behind" is where joy and the beloved have been left.
  • dull bearer: The slow-moving horse or carrier. "Bearer" suggests not just a riding animal but something that carries the burden of the speaker's grief and separation.
  • posting: Travelling at speed by relay horses ("post-horses"). A post-horse journey was the fastest available means of land travel in Shakespeare's time.
  • swift extremity: The utmost of swiftness. Even the greatest conceivable speed will feel too slow on the return journey to the beloved.
  • neigh no dull flesh: Desire will not summon or call for any slow-footed animal. The verb "neigh" is brilliantly unexpected, treating desire itself as an impatient, spirited horse.
  • jade: A worn-out, disreputable horse; a contemptible person. The speaker affectionately dismisses his horse as unnecessary for love's true speed.
  • wilful-slow: Deliberately slow. The horse went slowly on purpose because its rider willed it so, not wanting to leave the beloved.
  • blessed key: The key to open a treasure chest, symbolizing access to something precious.
  • sweet up-locked treasure: Something of great value kept securely locked away.
  • blunting the fine point of seldom pleasure: Making the sharp, intense joy of infrequent experience dull or common through constant access.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet XLVIII: The Unsecurable Treasure

  • Summary/Paraphrase: The speaker describes how meticulously he locked away all his material possessions before a journey, securing even the smallest "trifles." However, he laments that his beloved, who is far more precious than any jewel, cannot be similarly secured. The beloved resides only within the vulnerable "gentle closure" of the speaker's breast, a place that is paradoxically open to the world. The speaker fears that such a unique and valuable "prize" is at risk of being stolen, even by "truth" itself, highlighting the inherent vulnerability of profound love.
  • Key Imagery/Metaphors: The central conceit involves the imagery of security: "truest bars," "locks," "chests," and "wards of trust" contrasted with the beloved, who is a "jewel" but also "prey." The speaker's "breast" or heart is metaphorically a "gentle closure" that offers no real protection.
  • Language & Tone: The tone begins with a sense of carefulness ("How careful was I") but shifts to one of anxiety and paradox. The legalistic and economic language ("wards of trust," "prey," "prize") underscores the theme of possession and loss.
  • Analysis of the Couplet: "And even thence thou wilt be stol’n I fear, / For truth proves thievish for a prize so dear." This couplet delivers a dark, witty paradox. The beloved's very worth, beauty, and freedom make them irresistible, suggesting that even honesty or virtue ("truth") could be compelled to "steal" such a valuable prize. The beloved's openness to the world is the very threat no physical barrier can guard against.
  • Connections to Other Sonnets/Works: The "chest and lock imagery" connects directly to Sonnet 52, which also uses the conceit of a "blessed key" to treasure, but explores the opposite approach to value. It also relates to Sonnet 65, which questions how any "strong hand" can protect beauty from Time, ultimately suggesting the "ink of verse" as the only lasting security.

Sonnet XLIX: Anticipating Rejection

  • Summary/Paraphrase: The speaker psychologically prepares for an inevitable future where the beloved's affection will wane. He envisions a time when the beloved will frown upon his "defects," conduct an "audit" of their love, and pass by him as a stranger with "settled gravity." In anticipation of this painful rejection, the speaker fortifies himself by acknowledging his own unworthiness and defects. He promises not to dispute the beloved's right to leave, essentially arguing the case against himself.
  • Key Imagery/Metaphors: The sonnet employs extended legal and financial metaphors: "audit," "advis'd respects," "lawful reasons," and "allege no cause." The beloved's "sun, thine eye" suggests a warmth that will be withdrawn. The speaker "ensconces" himself, taking a defensive posture.
  • Language & Tone: The repeated phrase "Against that time" emphasizes the speaker's foreboding and pre-emptive self-condemnation. The tone is resigned, self-deprecating, and quietly devastating.
  • Analysis of the Couplet: "To leave poor me thou hast the strength of laws, / Since why to love I can allege no cause." The couplet reveals the ultimate self-defeating nature of the speaker's defense: he believes the beloved has every "lawful" right to leave because he can offer no justification for why he should be loved. He gives up the argument before it even begins.
  • Connections to Other Sonnets/Works: This sonnet’s "pre-emptive self-condemnation" and the speaker’s willingness to argue against himself anticipate similar themes in Sonnet 87 ("Farewell! thou art too dear for my possessing") and Sonnets 88 and 89, where the speaker offers to help the beloved construct a case against him. The legal language also connects to the extended legal conceit in Sonnet 46.

Sonnet L: The Burden of Separation

  • Summary/Paraphrase: The speaker is on a horseback journey away from his beloved, and every mile marks an increasing distance and deepens his sorrow. His horse, seemingly sensing its rider's grief and reluctance, plods "dully," lacking spirit. Even the desperate use of a "bloody spur" cannot hasten the horse; its pained "groan" cuts the speaker more deeply than the spur cuts the horse. This groan serves as a painful reminder of the sonnet's central truth: his joy, and his beloved, are "behind," while "grief lies onward."
  • Key Imagery/Metaphors: The primary metaphor is the journey itself, with the horse's actions mirroring the speaker's internal state. The "heavy" burden of grief is both emotional and physical. The "bloody spur" is an image of desperation, and the horse's "groan" becomes a poignant symbol of shared sorrow.
  • Language & Tone: The tone is one of profound sorrow, weariness, and resignation. Words like "heavy," "weary," "dully," and "groan" create a palpable sense of suffering. The gentle anthropomorphism of the horse ("as if by some instinct the wretch did know") deepens the pathos.
  • Analysis of the Couplet: "For that same groan doth put this in my mind, / My grief lies onward, and my joy behind." The couplet delivers a powerful reversal. The horse's groan, rather than being a complaint against the spur, is revealed to be a deep echo of the speaker's own grief. It serves as a stark reminder that the direction of his travel is the direction of sorrow, leaving all happiness in the past.
  • Connections to Other Sonnets/Works: Sonnet 50 forms a "paired diptych" with Sonnet 51, both exploring the horse conceit and the journey away from and back toward the beloved. This sequence is one of the most vivid travel descriptions in early modern poetry. The grieving horse also appears in Shakespeare's Venus and Adonis, though with a different emotional context (uncontrollable desire rather than sorrow).

Sonnet LI: The Speed of Return

  • Summary/Paraphrase: This sonnet directly contrasts with Sonnet 50, shifting from the sorrowful outward journey to the exhilarating imagined return. The speaker can readily "excuse" his horse's slowness when travelling away from the beloved—why rush from what you love? But on the return journey, even the "swift extremity" of a wind-mounted horse would seem too slow. His intense "desire," born of "perfect'st love," will outpace any physical conveyance, needing no "dull flesh" at all. Love itself will run back to the beloved, generously excusing the horse's deliberate slowness on the outward path.
  • Key Imagery/Metaphors: The sonnet focuses on speed, using metaphors of travel: "speed," "posting," "winged speed," and desire itself as a tireless, neighing horse. The "dull bearer" (the horse) is contrasted with the "fiery race" of desire.
  • Language & Tone: The tone is vibrant, energetic, and full of longing, a stark contrast to the preceding sonnet. Words like "haste," "swift extremity," and "fiery race" convey urgency and passion. The anthropomorphism of desire ("neigh no dull flesh") gives it a powerful, independent agency.
  • Analysis of the Couplet: "'Since from thee going, he went wilful-slow, / Towards thee I’ll run, and give him leave to go.'" The couplet provides love's final, witty justification. The horse's slowness on the outward journey is reframed as a "wilful-slow" act of loyalty to the beloved. This excuse allows the speaker's desire to run unhindered back, dismissing the horse as an unnecessary impediment to love's true speed.
  • Connections to Other Sonnets/Works: Read alongside 50, Sonnet 51 completes a "paired meditation on speed, desire, and direction." The "winged speed" of desire connects to the swift elements (thought and fire) in Sonnets 44 and 45, which can instantly traverse distance, unlike "flesh." The conceit of desire outrunning earthly vehicles also appears in A Midsummer Night's Dream (love as swift and ungovernable) and Romeo and Juliet (Romeo's eagerness compared to lightning).

Sonnet LII: The Value of Seldom Pleasure

  • Summary/Paraphrase: The speaker compares himself to a wealthy individual who possesses a valuable treasure but doesn't access it constantly. The purpose of this restraint is to maintain the intensity and specialness of the pleasure derived from the treasure, preventing it from becoming ordinary or "blunted" through too frequent exposure. This principle is akin to why significant events like "feasts" are "solemn and so rare"—their infrequent nature enhances their enjoyment and perceived value.
  • Key Imagery/Metaphors: The central metaphor is that of a rich man, a "blessed key," and "sweet up-locked treasure." This extends to the idea of feasts being "solemn and so rare," implying that scarcity enhances appreciation.
  • Language & Tone: The tone is contemplative and philosophical, exploring the psychology of desire and value. The language focuses on economic and experiential worth.
  • Analysis of the lines (Couplet not provided in text): The initial lines establish the central argument: the deliberate withholding of access to something precious prevents its "blunting" or dulling. The rarity makes the experience more potent and valued, suggesting that anticipation and controlled access deepen appreciation rather than diminish it.
  • Connections to Other Sonnets/Works: This sonnet directly links to Sonnet 48 through the shared conceit of a "treasure chest" and "lock imagery." However, Sonnet 52 offers an opposing perspective: while 48 laments the inability to secure the beloved and the vulnerability of constant presence, 52 suggests that controlled, infrequent access is precisely what preserves the beloved's value and the intensity of pleasure, rather than constant possession.

Review Questions

  1. How do Sonnets 48 and 52 explore different, even opposite, approaches to securing and valuing a precious beloved? Discuss the implications of each approach.
  2. Analyze the speaker's emotional state and his strategy for dealing with potential rejection in Sonnet 49. How effective or self-defeating is this strategy, and what does it reveal about the speaker's self-perception?
  3. Discuss the role of anthropomorphism and the horse conceit in Sonnets 50 and 51. How do these sonnets use the journey to represent the speaker's inner world and the nature of his longing?
  4. In Sonnet 51, the speaker differentiates between physical speed and the speed of desire. How does he articulate this difference, and what does it suggest about the power and independence of love in these sonnets?
  5. The speaker often employs paradox in these sonnets (e.g., "truth proves thievish," "my grief lies onward, and my joy behind"). Choose one such paradox from any of the five sonnets and explain its meaning and significance within its specific context.
