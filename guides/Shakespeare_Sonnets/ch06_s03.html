Study Guide: Sonnet XLV

Overview

Sonnet XLV completes the two-part meditation on the four classical elements begun in Sonnet XLIV. Where XLIV lamented that the speaker's body is made of heavy earth and water and cannot travel to the beloved, XLV addresses the lighter swift elements — air (thought) and fire (desire) — that can fly to the beloved as ambassadors of love.

Meaning and Argument

The poem opens mid-thought ("The other two"), linking it directly to its companion. Air and fire are constantly traveling to the beloved as thought and desire, acting as "tender embassy of love." But their departure leaves the speaker depleted — made of only the two heavy elements, he "sinks down to death, oppress'd with melancholy." Relief comes when the messengers return bearing news of the beloved's good health. The speaker rejoices briefly — then immediately sends them back and "straight grow sad" again. The couplet exposes the exhausting, cyclical quality of this consolation.

Key Literary Devices

  • Extended conceit: The four-element framework from classical philosophy organizes both XLIV and XLV as a philosophical diptych.
  • Oxymoron: "present-absent" captures the paradox of mediated contact — the elements are simultaneously here and there.
  • Personification: Air and fire become gentle diplomatic envoys carrying love across distance.
  • Cyclical structure: The couplet loops the emotional state back to sadness, denying any stable resolution.

Themes

The poem deepens the exploration of separation and mediated love: the only connection across distance is imaginative (thought) and emotional (desire). Even this consolation is temporary — the cycle of sending and waiting renews grief endlessly. It raises the question of whether incomplete presence is better than pure absence.
