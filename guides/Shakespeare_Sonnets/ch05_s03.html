Study Guide: Sonnet XXXV

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet XXXV performs a surprising reversal: the injured speaker becomes the beloved's consoler and defense counsel. Opening with "No more be griev'd," the speaker offers nature's universal imperfection (roses with thorns, silver fountains with mud, canker in the bud) as evidence that the beloved's fault is neither exceptional nor unforgivable. So far, this is generous consolation.

But the poem turns on itself. By line 8 the speaker realizes he has gone too far — excusing the sins "more than thy sins are." He has corrupted himself by authorizing the trespass with comparisons, and he names himself an "accessary" (legal term: accomplice) to the "sweet thief" who robs him. A "civil war" rages in his soul between love that compels forgiveness and moral sense that demands acknowledgment of wrong.

The couplet's ending note — "sourly robs from me" — ensures the reader feels the bitterness beneath all the generous excuse-making. Forgiveness is given, but it costs the speaker greatly.

Key Literary Devices

  • Nature imagery: Roses, fountains, eclipses, buds — a catalog of beautiful things corrupted by their inherent flaws, normalizing the beloved's transgression.
  • Legal vocabulary: "Authorizing trespass," "adverse party," "advocate," "lawful plea," "accessary" — the speaker frames his own moral confusion as a courtroom drama.
  • Oxymoron: "Sweet thief" — the beloved's charm and their wrongdoing coexist inseparably.
  • Civil war metaphor: The internal conflict between love and moral judgment is given the weight of a nation at war with itself.

Themes

Self-implicating forgiveness: The act of forgiving can itself become a form of moral corruption when taken too far — the speaker's excess of lenience makes him complicit in the wrong.

The paradox of love and justice: Love wants to excuse; justice demands accountability. Neither can fully win, producing the chapter's defining emotional state: a civil war within the self.

Betrayal from within: The final bitterness of "sourly robs from me" confirms that forgiveness granted does not mean pain erased.
