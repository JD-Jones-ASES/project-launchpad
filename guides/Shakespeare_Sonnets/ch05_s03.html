Study Guide: Sonnets 38-42 from Shadows and Forgiveness

Overview

This section explores a complex interplay of themes, beginning with lavish praise for the Youth as the speaker's ultimate poetic inspiration. It then delves into a philosophical paradox regarding identity and the necessity of separation for genuine admiration. The emotional core of the section shifts dramatically with the introduction of a triangular relationship, where the Youth's "lascivious grace" leads to an act of betrayal that tests the speaker's capacity for forgiveness and love.

Key Concepts

  • The Youth as Poetic Muse: Sonnet 38 elevates the Youth beyond the classical Muses, declaring him the sole and superior source of the speaker's poetic invention and the guarantee of his verse's immortality.
  • Paradox of Praise and Identity: Sonnet 39 grapples with the philosophical dilemma that if the Youth is "the better part of me," praising him feels like self-praise, which is improper. Deep identification blurs the lines between self and other.
  • Absence as a Condition for Love: In Sonnet 39, separation and absence are reframed not as pure torment, but as the necessary conditions that create distance, allowing for distinct appreciation and genuine praise of the beloved.
  • The Triangular Relationship: Sonnets 40-42 introduce a dramatic conflict where the Youth has taken "all my loves" (referring to a shared mistress). This establishes an emotional triangle between the speaker, the Youth, and a woman, exploring themes of jealousy, betrayal, and conditional forgiveness.
  • Forgiveness and Grievance: Sonnet 40 presents a speaker who attempts to forgive the Youth's "robbery" but simultaneously expresses profound hurt, noting that "it is a greater grief / To bear love's wrong, than hate's known injury." The act of forgiving is complicated by the lingering pain of betrayal.
  • "Lascivious Grace": This oxymoronic phrase applied to the Youth in Sonnet 40 encapsulates his paradoxical nature: his captivating beauty ("grace") is intertwined with sexual wantonness ("lascivious"), making even his transgressions seem alluring yet morally hazardous.
  • The Youth's Inevitable Temptation: Sonnet 41 suggests that the Youth's beauty and youth make him inherently susceptible to temptation, particularly from a wooing woman, mitigating but not excusing his "pretty wrongs."

Important Terms

**want subject to invent**
To lack material or inspiration for poetic creation. In Renaissance usage, "invent" meant to "find" or "discover" subject matter.
<dt>**vulgar paper**</dt>
<dd>An ordinary, common piece of writing or composition. The Youth's excellence is too profound for mere everyday verse.</dd>

<dt>**invention**</dt>
<dd>The faculty of creative imagination, particularly in finding or devising poetic material.</dd>

<dt>**tenth Muse**</dt>
<dd>A hyperbolic compliment where the beloved is elevated above the nine classical Muses of arts and sciences, implying a superior source of inspiration.</dd>

<dt>**eternal numbers**</dt>
<dd>Poetry or verses of immortal quality, destined to outlive time. "Numbers" refers to metrical compositions.</dd>

<dt>**with manners**</dt>
<dd>Decorously; with proper behavior or decorum. The speaker questions how he can praise the Youth appropriately without seeming to praise himself.</dd>

<dt>**the better part of me**</dt>
<dd>The nobler, superior aspect of a person. Drawing on Platonic ideas, it suggests the Youth is the speaker's higher, truer self.</dd>

<dt>**sour leisure**</dt>
<dd>The bitter idleness or painful time experienced during absence, made difficult by longing, yet paradoxically yielding "sweet leave" for contemplation.</dd>

<dt>**hence**</dt>
<dd>From here; from this place. Used in Sonnet 39 to highlight the paradox of the beloved being absent in place but present in thought.</dt>

<dt>**all my loves**</dt>
<dd>A deliberately ambiguous phrase in Sonnet 40, referring both to all of the speaker's affections and, more specifically, to the beloved woman with whom the speaker was involved.</dd>

<dt>**wilful taste**</dt>
<dd>A deliberate, chosen indulgence or act, implying agency and therefore culpability in the Youth's actions.</dt>

<dt>**steal thee all my poverty**</dt>
<dd>A poignant expression meaning to take even the little that the speaker possesses. It underscores the severity of the theft, as the speaker has so little to lose.</dd>

<dt>**lascivious grace**</dt>
<dd>A striking oxymoron in Sonnet 40, combining "lascivious" (sexually wanton or lustful) with "grace" (beauty, elegance, divine favor). It describes the Youth's captivating yet morally transgressive nature.</dt>

<dt>**pretty wrongs**</dt>
<dd>Lesser offenses or misdeeds, perhaps made more tolerable by the Youth's charm, but still morally culpable.</dd>

<dt>**liberty**</dt>
<dd>Freedom, especially to act without restraint; often implies a license that leads to transgression.</dd>

<dt>**physical attractiveness (beauty)**</dt>
<dd>The Youth's physical comeliness, which is presented as both a source of inspiration and a cause of temptation for himself and others.</dd>

<dt>**prevail**</dt>
<dd>To triumph; to be effective or successful, particularly in overcoming resistance or winning someone over.</dd>

<dt>**my seat**</dt>
<dd>Figuratively, the speaker's place, residence, or established position, particularly in a relationship. The Youth should have respected this "seat."</dd>

<dt>**straying youth**</dt>
<dd>The Youth's wandering or misguided behavior, implying his lack of moral discipline or fidelity, often attributed to the impulsiveness of youth.</dd>

<dt>**twofold truth**</dt>
<dd>A double betrayal: the Youth's infidelity to the speaker, and potentially to the woman involved, or to the ideal of truth itself.</dd>

Detailed Notes

Sonnet 38: The Youth as the Ultimate Muse

  • The Youth Replaces the Muse: The sonnet opens with a rhetorical question, asking how the speaker's "Muse" (poetic inspiration) could ever lack subject matter while the Youth exists. This immediately establishes the Youth not just as a patron, but as the supreme source of creative power.
  • Transcendence and Immortality: The Youth is described as "too excellent / For every vulgar paper to rehearse," implying that his qualities demand the highest form of verse. His inherent excellence is the "engine of inspiration," ensuring that any poet writing of him will produce "eternal numbers."
  • The "Tenth Muse" Conceit: This is a hyperbolic compliment, elevating the Youth above the nine classical Muses of Greek mythology. It's a striking contrast to the darker themes often found in this chapter, emphasizing the speaker's profound admiration. This compliment echoes historical praise given to figures like Sappho.
  • Reversal of Credit: The closing couplet (lines 13-14) offers a neat division of labor and credit: "If my slight Muse do please these curious days, / The pain be mine, but thine shall be the praise." The poet accepts the "pain" of composition, while all the "praise" for the poem's quality is attributed to the Youth's inspiring essence.
  • Connections: This sonnet links to other "praise-of-the-patron" themes, particularly Sonnet 18, which immortalizes the Youth's beauty in verse. It also anticipates later sonnets like 55 and 65, which further explore the theme of poetry granting immortality to the beloved. Its placement in Chapter 5, amid difficult introspection, serves as an affirmation of love's creative power.

Sonnet 39: The Paradox of Praise and Absence

  • Philosophical Paradox of Identification: The sonnet introduces a complex problem: if the Youth is "all the better part of me," how can the speaker praise him "with manners" without effectively praising himself? This reflects a deep spiritual and emotional identification between the two, blurring the lines of individual identity.
  • The Need for Separation: The speaker proposes a radical solution: "Even for this, let us divided live." Separation is presented not as a tragedy, but as a necessary condition to create enough distance for genuine, unselfish praise to flow from one to the other. This allows the Youth to receive "that due to thee which thou deserv'st alone."
  • Absence Reimagined: The volta (turn) of the sonnet reinterprets absence. Initially a "torment," absence is transformed into a "sour leisure gave sweet leave." The pain of separation allows for "thoughts of love" that "sweetly doth deceive" time, making the absence bearable and even productive.
  • "Making One Twain": The couplet concludes by stating that separation teaches "how to make one twain" (two distinct individuals). This occurs through the act of writing, where the speaker can praise the "him here who doth hence remain"—the beloved who is physically absent but present in the poet's thoughts and verse.
  • Connections: Sonnet 39 foreshadows later meditations on absence and thought's ability to transcend distance in Sonnets 44 and 45. It also builds upon the theme of unified love despite separate lives found in Sonnet 36, but here it finds a philosophical purpose for that separation.

Sonnet 40: Forgiveness and Betrayal

  • A Shift in Emotional Register: This sonnet marks a sharp turn in the sequence's emotional tone, introducing conflict and betrayal. The speaker addresses the Youth directly, granting him permission to "Take all my loves," implicitly referring to a romantic partner of the speaker, thus revealing a triangular relationship.
  • Dizzying Generosity and its Collapse: The speaker attempts a logically generous argument: since all his love already belonged to the Youth, nothing new has truly been taken. However, this logic is quickly undermined by the raw emotion that follows. The speaker blames the Youth if he "thyself deceivest / By wilful taste of what thyself refusest"—suggesting a self-inflicted harm or hypocrisy in the Youth's actions.
  • The Pain of Love's Wrong: Line 12 delivers the sonnet's emotional core: "it is a greater grief / To bear love's wrong, than hate's known injury." This powerful statement reveals that betrayal by a beloved inflicts a deeper wound than hostility from an enemy. The speaker's "forgiveness" (line 9) is conditional and tinged with profound hurt.
  • "Lascivious Grace": This striking oxymoron describes the Youth, highlighting his dangerous allure. His "grace" (beauty, charm) is inseparable from his "lascivious" (lustful, wanton) nature, making his transgressions seem captivating despite their moral impropriety. This captures the Youth's peculiar power to make even "ill well shows."
  • Helpless, Unconditional Love: The concluding line, "Kill me with spites yet we must not be foes," expresses a powerful, almost desperate commitment. Despite the pain and betrayal, the speaker cannot allow the relationship to end in enmity, underscoring a deep, perhaps unhealthy, bond.
  • Context of the Triangular Relationship: Sonnets 40-42 are widely interpreted as detailing a specific dramatic situation involving the speaker, the Youth, and a woman (possibly the "Dark Lady" of later sonnets, though debated). This scenario draws on classical precedents and Elizabethan ideals of male friendship, where a female "intrusion" could be seen as deeply disruptive.
  • Connections: Sonnet 40 forms a crucial part of the triangle narrative completed by Sonnets 41 and 42. It foreshadows the more intense and dark exploration of lust and betrayal in the Dark Lady sonnets (e.g., 129). The idea of beauty concealing corruption also echoes in Sonnet 93, where the Youth's face might hide "Eve's apple."

Sonnet 41: Excusing the Youth's "Pretty Wrongs"

  • The Youth's Temptability: Sonnet 41 continues the theme of the triangular relationship by exploring the reasons for the Youth's infidelity. The speaker acknowledges that the Youth's "beauty, and thy years full well befits" such "pretty wrongs" because "still temptation follows where thou art." The Youth's inherent attractiveness makes him a target for seduction.
  • The Aggressive Woman: The sonnet implies that the woman is the aggressor, noting that "Gentle thou art, and therefore to be won, / Beauteous thou art, therefore to be assail'd." The speaker suggests that when a woman actively "woos," few men would resist "till he have prevail'd." This attempts to shift some blame from the Youth to the circumstances and the woman's pursuit.
  • The "Twofold Truth": Despite the excuses, the speaker asserts that the Youth should have respected "my seat" (the speaker's place in the relationship). The Youth's actions are described as breaking "a twofold truth"—a betrayal not only of the speaker but possibly also of the woman, or of his own moral integrity.
  • Straying Youth and Beauty's Riot: The Youth's "straying youth" and his beauty are presented as forces that lead him into "riot," pushing him into transgressive behavior. This suggests an internal conflict within the Youth, where his admirable qualities become a source of moral hazard.
  • Sonnet 42 (Implicit): While the full text of Sonnet 42 is not provided, the "context" section indicates that Sonnets 40-42 together complete the narrative of the triangular relationship. Sonnet 42 typically concludes this sequence by suggesting a complex form of forgiveness or acceptance, where the speaker finds a way to unite his love for the Youth with the Youth's love for the woman, claiming a shared love that ultimately still points back to him.

Review Questions

  1. The speaker in Sonnet 40 claims to "forgive" the Youth, yet the poem meticulously details the injury received. Do you believe the forgiveness is genuine? What evidence within the sonnet supports or complicates your reading of the speaker's true feelings?
  2. In Sonnet 40, the line "Kill me with spites yet we must not be foes" is a powerful emotional statement. What does the phrase "must not" imply about the speaker's state of mind—is it a command to himself, an expression of a desperate wish, or a declaration of what he believes is an unavoidable truth of their bond?
  3. Analyze the oxymoron "lascivious grace" in Sonnet 40. How does the combination of these two words illuminate the speaker's perception of the Youth's character and the complex nature of his appeal? What does it suggest about the moral implications of the Youth's beauty?
  4. How does Sonnet 38 redefine the concept of a "Muse" for the speaker? What is the significance of the "tenth Muse" conceit, and how does this extreme compliment relate to the themes of inspiration and immortality in the sonnet?
  5. Explain the central paradox of praise and separation presented in Sonnet 39. How does the speaker argue that absence, initially a "torment," can become a "sweet leave" that actually facilitates a deeper, more genuine expression of love?
