Study Guide: Sonnet CXXIV

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet CXXIV makes the most philosophically ambitious claim of the chapter: the speaker's love is not "the child of state" — not born of Fortune, circumstance, or political calculation — but is founded on something absolute, beyond accident. If it were contingent, it could be Fortune's bastard, cast off in a change of weather. Instead, it was "builded far from accident," suffering neither in "smiling pomp" (favorable fortune) nor under "thralled discontent" (compelled political turbulence). It fears not "policy, that heretic" — cunning self-interest — which operates only on "leases of short-number'd hours." The love stands "hugely politic" on its own, neither growing with heat nor drowning with rain.

The couplet witnesses to this constancy by invoking "the fools of time" — those who lived for crime but died in theatrical goodness — whose last-minute conversion proves by contrast the speaker's lifelong, genuine constancy.

Literary Devices

  • Political and legal vocabulary: "Child of state," "Fortune's bastard," "unfather'd," "policy," "heretic," "thralled" — the sonnet situates love within political discourse to argue it transcends that discourse.
  • Philosophical terms: "Accident" (contingent quality, Aristotelian) vs. essence — the love is essential, not accidental.
  • Metaphor of weather: Heat and showers as Fortune's extremes leave this love unmoved.

Themes of Farewell and Apology

CXXIV is a farewell to contingency. The love described here is not dependent on the favorable circumstances that might justify it to the world; it needs no external validation. This is the strongest assertion in the sequence that the bond transcends worldly conditions.
