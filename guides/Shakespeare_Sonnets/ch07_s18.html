Study Guide: Sonnet LXXVI

Summary

Sonnet LXXVI defends the speaker's poetic constancy against an imagined charge of being "barren of new pride." Why, the speaker asks, does he not glance aside at new methods and strange compounds? Why does he write the same thing ever and always, his invention always in a "noted weed" — a recognizable, familiar garment — so that every word almost tells his name? The sestet answers with disarming simplicity: he always writes of the beloved, and love is always his argument. All his best work is "dressing old words new" — spending again what has already been spent. The couplet offers a natural analogy: the sun is both daily new and old; and so the speaker's love, always telling the same thing, is always new in its telling.

Key Themes

  • Constancy in love and verse: The speaker's thematic monotony is a virtue — the expression of devoted, singular love.
  • Novelty versus constancy: The age demands variation; the speaker offers the paradox of the sun — same yet perpetually fresh.
  • Verse as self-portrait: So distinctive is the speaker's style that his every word identifies him.

Key Literary Devices

  • Rhetorical self-questioning: Four "Why" questions (lines 1–10) set up the sestet's resolution.
  • Clothing metaphor: "Noted weed" — the speaker's style as a recognizable garment.
  • Solar simile (couplet): The sun, daily new and old, is the model for how constant love remains perpetually alive.

Vocabulary

  • Barren: Lacking, empty, unproductive
  • Variation: Change, novelty in style
  • Compounds strange: Unusual combinations of words or unusual compound words
  • Noted weed: A well-known garment; a recognizable style
  • Argument: Theme, subject matter
