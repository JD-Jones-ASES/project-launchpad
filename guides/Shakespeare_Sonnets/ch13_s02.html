Study Guide: Sonnet CXLIII

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet CXLIII develops an extended domestic simile: the Dark Lady is compared to a housewife who sets down her baby to chase a runaway bird. The speaker is the neglected baby, trailing after a mother whose attention is entirely consumed by what eludes her. The allegory maps cleanly onto the triangular situation: the speaker pursues the Dark Lady, who herself pursues a rival (possibly another "Will"). The sestet introduces a conditional bargain — if she catches her quarry, the speaker asks her to "turn back, kiss me, be kind." The couplet delivers one of the sequence's most celebrated puns: "Will," meaning both future-tense intention and the name William, encodes the speaker's identity directly into the bargain.

Key Poetic Devices

  • Extended simile: the entire octave is a sustained domestic allegory, unusually humble in its register for a love poem
  • "Will" pun: Shakespeare's signature self-referential wordplay, linking erotic desire, the speaker's name, and grammatical futurity
  • Bathos: comparing the speaker to a crying baby is deliberately self-deprecating and faintly comic
  • Conditional structure: the sestet's "if... then" logic frames desire as a negotiated exchange

Central Themes

Neglect and desire: The speaker is secondary to the Dark Lady's own obsession, reduced to an infant crying for attention. Triangular desire: Desire operates in a chain — the speaker wants the lady, who wants someone who flees her — a structure of perpetual frustration. Shame and self-abasement: Casting himself as a helpless baby is the speaker's most extreme act of self-diminishment in the sequence, underscoring how thoroughly desire has unmanned him. Conditional affection: His prayer for her success is explicitly conditional, revealing that even in abasement he retains a bargaining position.
