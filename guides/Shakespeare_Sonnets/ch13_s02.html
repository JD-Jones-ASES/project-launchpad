Study Guide: Shakespeare's Sonnets 142-146 - The Dark Lady: Reckoning

Overview

This section delves into a tumultuous segment of Shakespeare's Dark Lady sonnets (142-145, with references to 146), exploring the speaker's complex, often morally ambiguous relationship with the Dark Lady. It showcases themes of mutual sin, hypocrisy, emotional diminishment, and the dramatic introduction of the Fair Youth into this already fraught dynamic, culminating in an unusual, tender interlude. The sonnets here reveal a speaker grappling with judgment, desire, and uncertainty, often with a biting wit and self-awareness.

Key Concepts

  • Moral Equivalence and Hypocrisy (Tu Quoque): The speaker frequently turns the Dark Lady's accusations of sin back on her, arguing that her own moral failings (e.g., "profan'd their scarlet ornaments") disqualify her from judging him. This rhetorical strategy, known as tu quoque ("you also"), highlights a pervasive theme of shared transgression.
  • The Triangular Relationship: Sonnet 144 explicitly introduces the Fair Youth ("better angel") into the Dark Lady sequence, depicting a love triangle where the Dark Lady ("worser spirit") is suspected of seducing the Fair Youth. This bridges the two major subsequences of Shakespeare's sonnets.
  • Good and Bad Angel Tradition: The allegorical framework of a "good angel" and a "bad angel" contending for a human soul, familiar from morality plays, is used to describe the speaker's inner and external conflicts, elevating his personal drama to a cosmic struggle.
  • Self-Diminishment and Pathos: In Sonnet 143, the speaker humorously but touchingly casts himself as a neglected infant, crying for the Dark Lady's attention while she pursues another. This presents a deliberate deflation of romantic dignity.
  • Puns and Wordplay: Shakespeare masterfully employs puns, most notably with "Will" (referring to desire, the name William, or a rival) in Sonnet 143, and "Hathaway" (Shakespeare's wife's surname) in Sonnet 145, adding layers of meaning and personal connection.
  • Emotional Ambiguity and Doubt: Particularly in Sonnet 144, the speaker lives in a state of unresolved suspicion, unable to confirm whether his "better angel" has been corrupted. This suspension of certainty is a characteristic feature of the Dark Lady sequence.
  • Literary and Metrical Variation: Sonnet 145 stands out through its use of iambic tetrameter (eight syllables per line) instead of the standard iambic pentameter, giving it a unique, lighter, and more song-like quality that suggests an earlier composition.

Important Terms

  • Profan'd: Desecrated; treated as common what should be sacred. Used in the context of lips that have made false vows.
  • Scarlet ornaments: Refers to the Dark Lady's red lips; "scarlet" also carries connotations of sin and prostitution in Elizabethan usage.
  • Seal'd false bonds: Executed fraudulent contracts; a metaphor for making promises of love that were not intended to be kept.
  • Revenues of their rents: Income properly belonging to others; metaphorically, the conjugal rights or love owed to other partners.
  • Tu Quoque: Latin for "you also"; a rhetorical counter-charge that deflects an accusation by pointing out the accuser's equal guilt.
  • Careful housewife: A woman who manages her household diligently; used ironically in Sonnet 143 to describe the Dark Lady neglecting her "child" (the speaker).
  • Feather'd creatures broke away: A hen or other poultry that has escaped; a mundane image used to deflate the romantic register.
  • Will: A significant pun, potentially meaning desire/lust, the name William (Shakespeare), or a specific rival.
  • Still (verb): To quiet, calm, or make silent.
  • Suggest (verb): To prompt, tempt, or incite to evil, carrying a diabolical connotation.
  • Colour'd ill: Dark-complexioned; also morally tainted or evil in character.
  • Foul pride: Corrupt arrogance; here, the Dark Lady's sexual license and self-aggrandizement.
  • Fiend: A demon or devil, marking a complete inversion from an "angel."
  • Hell: At once the theological place of damnation and an Elizabethan slang term for the female body.
  • Doom: A judgment or sentence; also, in Elizabethan English, death or ultimate fate.
  • Hate away: To send hate away, or dismiss it. This phrase contains a pun on "Hathaway," Shakespeare's wife's surname.
  • Languish'd: Wasted away from longing; suffered from unrequited love.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet 142: "Love is my sin, and thy dear virtue hate"

This sonnet opens the "Dark Lady" sequence in this chapter with a direct confrontation. The speaker immediately concedes that his love for the Dark Lady is a "sin." However, he quickly turns the moral tables on her, using the rhetorical device of tu quoque. He argues that her "virtue hate" – her hatred of his sin – is itself grounded in her own "sinful loving."

  • The Argument of Hypocrisy: The speaker asserts that if he is to be reproved, it should not come from "those lips of thine." He accuses her of having "profan'd their scarlet ornaments" and "seal'd false bonds of love as oft as mine," thereby having "Robb'd others’ beds’ revenues of their rents." These powerful metaphors paint her as equally, if not more, morally compromised. "Scarlet ornaments" brilliantly evokes both her red lips and the Elizabethan association of "scarlet" with sin and prostitution.
  • Legal Metaphors: The language is infused with legal terms: "reproving," "seal'd false bonds," "revenues of their rents." Love vows are framed as contracts, broken by the Dark Lady as often as by him, undermining her right to judge.
  • A Plea for Pity, with a Twist: The speaker asks her to "Root pity in thy heart," suggesting that if she shows him pity, she herself might deserve to be pitied in the future. The couplet delivers a "sharp legal twist": "If thou dost seek to have what thou dost hide, / By self-example mayst thou be denied!" This means if she denies him the pity she herself might need, she sets a precedent that will deny her the very mercy she seeks from others.
  • Moral Equivalence: The sonnet establishes a foundation of moral equivalence, asserting that both parties are implicated in similar transgressions, thereby dismantling the Dark Lady's position of moral superiority.

Sonnet 143: "Lo, as a careful housewife runs to catch"

This sonnet employs an extended, deliberately comic conceit to depict the speaker's emotional state and the Dark Lady's inattention.

  • The Housewife Analogy: The speaker compares the Dark Lady to a "careful housewife" who, in her haste to retrieve an escaped "feather'd creature" (a runaway hen or poultry), sets down her "babe" (the speaker) and chases the fleeting object. The "housewife" is ironically "careful" only for the bird, neglecting her actual charge.
  • The Speaker as Neglected Infant: The speaker casts himself in the undignified role of the "neglected child" or "babe," crying for his mother's attention while she is absorbed in another pursuit. This self-mockery introduces a note of pathos amidst the humor.
  • The Pursuit of "Will": The Dark Lady is "runn'st... after that which flies from thee" (another man, or perhaps desire itself). The speaker offers a bargain in the couplet: if she catches her "hope" (her quarry), he will pray for her success ("So will I pray that thou mayst have thy 'Will'") on the condition that she turns back to him and "play the mother’s part, kiss me, be kind" and "still" his "loud crying."
  • The "Will" Pun: The capitalized "Will" in the quarto signals a multifaceted pun: it can refer to carnal desire or lust, the name William (Shakespeare's own first name), or a specific rival. This pun deepens the meaning, implying either the Dark Lady's own desires, a rival also named William, or even the speaker's acceptance of her choosing someone else, if only she returns to him.

Sonnet 144: "Two loves I have of comfort and despair"

This is a pivotal sonnet, explicitly bridging the Fair Youth and Dark Lady sequences and introducing the central conflict of a love triangle.

  • The Good and Bad Angel: The speaker frames his emotional life as a spiritual battle between two opposing forces: the "better angel" (the Fair Youth), who offers "comfort," and the "worser spirit" (the Dark Lady), who brings "despair." This imagery directly invokes the Elizabethan morality play tradition, where good and bad angels contend for a soul.
  • The Dark Lady's Seduction: The core drama is the speaker's growing suspicion that the Dark Lady, his "female evil," is actively tempting his "better angel" away from him. She "Tempteth my better angel from my side, / And would corrupt my saint to be a devil, / Wooing his purity with her foul pride."
  • The "Hell" Pun: The sonnet culminates in the famous line, "I guess one angel in another’s hell." "Hell" functions as a deliberate pun, simultaneously referring to the theological place of damnation and, in Elizabethan slang, the female sexual organ. This double meaning makes the accusation of seduction both spiritually grave and physically explicit.
  • Unresolved Suspicion: Crucially, the speaker admits he "may, yet not directly tell" whether his angel "be turn'd fiend." He lives in "doubt," stating, "Till my bad angel fire my good one out." This ending highlights the psychological acuity of the Dark Lady sequence, which often suspends resolution and leaves the speaker, and the reader, in a state of uncertainty.
  • Connections: This sonnet is the only place where both the Fair Youth and the Dark Lady appear together explicitly. It connects directly to earlier sonnets (40 and 41) where the speaker also suspects the Fair Youth of stealing his mistress. Its spiritual crisis anticipates Sonnet 146.

Sonnet 145: "Those lips that Love’s own hand did make"

Sonnet 145 stands out as a unique and tender anomaly within the Dark Lady sequence, lighter in tone and form.

  • A Miniature Drama of Reversal: The sonnet narrates a brief, poignant moment where the Dark Lady's lips first speak the hurtful words "I hate," causing the speaker to "languish'd." However, seeing his "woeful state," she experiences mercy.
  • The Altered Phrase: Her tongue, "Chiding that tongue that ever sweet / Was us'd in giving gentle doom," then alters the phrase. The "I hate" is completed with "not you," reversing the rejection and saving the speaker's "life." This transformation is likened to "gentle day" following "night, who like a fiend / From heaven to hell is flown away."
  • Iambic Tetrameter: Unlike nearly all other sonnets in the collection, Sonnet 145 is written in iambic tetrameter (eight syllables per line) rather than pentameter (ten syllables). This shorter line length gives it a distinct, song-like, and lighter feel, contributing to its unusual character within the sequence.
  • The Anne Hathaway Pun: Most scholars believe this sonnet is an earlier composition, possibly predating the main sequence. This theory is strongly supported by the pun in the final couplet: "‘I hate’, from hate away she threw, / And sav’d my life, saying ‘not you’." When spoken aloud, "hate away" sounds strikingly like "Hathaway," the surname of Shakespeare's wife, Anne. This pun provides a rare personal glimpse into Shakespeare's own domestic life.

Sonnet 146 is referenced in connections for 144 as dealing with a "spiritual crisis" and "language of angels and demons," suggesting it further explores themes of internal conflict and spiritual reckoning.)

Review Questions

  1. In Sonnet 142, how does the speaker use the tu quoque rhetorical strategy to undermine the Dark Lady's moral authority, and what does this reveal about their relationship dynamic?
  2. Analyze the extended conceit in Sonnet 143. How does casting the speaker as a neglected infant and the Dark Lady as a busy housewife affect the tone and emotional impact of the poem? Discuss the significance of the "Will" pun.
  3. Sonnet 144 is considered a "hinge" sonnet. Explain how it connects the Fair Youth and Dark Lady subsequences, and discuss the implications of framing the conflict as a struggle between a "good angel" and a "bad angel."
  4. Discuss the significance of the "hell" pun in Sonnet 144. How does Shakespeare use this double meaning to convey both spiritual and physical dimensions of the perceived corruption?
  5. Sonnet 145 stands out formally and thematically. How does its use of iambic tetrameter contribute to its unique tone, and what does the "Hathaway" pun suggest about its potential origin and connection to Shakespeare's personal life?
