Study Guide: Sonnet LII

Overview

Sonnet LII takes a philosophical turn on separation, arguing that rarity enhances value. Rather than lamenting absence, the speaker finds consolation in the idea that, like a rich man's locked treasure, a precious feast, or a jewel in a carcanet, the beloved's value is actually heightened by the scarcity of their meetings.

Meaning and Argument

The poem opens with a simile: the speaker is like a wealthy man who has a key to a treasure he visits only rarely — precisely because constant viewing would "blunt the fine point of seldom pleasure." Three analogies elaborate this: solemn feasts (rare and therefore precious), jewels sparsely set in a necklace (valuable because they do not crowd each other), and a prized robe kept hidden in a wardrobe (dramatic when unveiled). In each case, rarity creates value. The couplet resolves the argument: the beloved's worth means that having him brings triumph, and lacking him generates hope — neither state is simply loss.

Key Literary Devices

  • Simile and extended analogy: Rich man/key, feast days, carcanet jewels, hidden robe — four parallel illustrations of the rarity-value principle.
  • Metaphor: "captain jewels" — the chief gems as commanders, asserting primacy.
  • Wordplay: "special instant special-blest" — the repetition intensifies the moment.
  • Couplet resolution: The paradox of hope and triumph reframes absence as enriching rather than merely painful.

Themes

The central theme is the philosophy of scarcity and desire: what is rare is precious; what is precious increases in worth through waiting. Sonnet LII is one of the most consolatory poems in the separation sequence, converting the pain of absence into an argument for the beloved's supreme value.
