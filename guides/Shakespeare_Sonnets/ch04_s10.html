Study Guide: Sonnet XXVII — "Weary with toil, I haste me to my bed"

Overview

Sonnet XXVII describes the speaker's double exhaustion: his body is worn out from the day's labor and travel, yet when he seeks sleep, his mind refuses rest and begins an imaginary "pilgrimage" to the absent beloved. His eyes stay open in darkness, seeing nothing like the blind — yet his soul's inner sight conjures the beloved's image, which shines like a jewel in the ghastly night. The closing couplet sums up the condition: by day his limbs find no rest, by night his mind finds none — for the beloved's sake, quiet is impossible.

Key Themes

Absence and Sleeplessness. The sonnet is one of a pair (with Sonnet XXVIII) exploring how the beloved's absence denies the speaker rest at every hour. The body/mind division — body resting, mind working — is the sonnet's central structural opposition.

The Pilgrimage of Thought. The word "pilgrimage" elevates the speaker's mental journey to the absent beloved to the status of a sacred act. The beloved is implicitly the holy destination of the speaker's devotion.

Darkness Made Beautiful. The jewel simile in lines 11–12 is one of the sequence's most beautiful images: the beloved's shadow in the mind transforms the "ghastly night" into something beautiful, giving darkness a renewed face.

Literary Devices

  • Extended metaphor: night journey as pilgrimage
  • Simile: beloved's image as a jewel hung in dark night
  • Paradox: "looking on darkness which the blind do see" — open eyes seeing nothing
  • Personification: night with an "old face" made new
  • Juxtaposition: body's exhaustion vs. mind's wakeful activity

Significance

Sonnets XXVII and XXVIII function as companion poems meditating on the painful duality of the speaker's separation from the beloved. XXVII introduces the condition; XXVIII deepens it by showing even his attempted flattery of day and night fails to bring relief.
