Study Guide: Sonnet CXXII

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet CXXII is a defense of an apparently indefensible act: the speaker has given away writing tablets that the youth presented as a gift. Rather than treat this as ingratitude, the speaker argues it is the highest tribute to the youth's hold on his memory. The brain, "full character'd with lasting memory," is a more permanent and more capacious record of the youth than any physical tablet. Physical tablets have "poor retention" compared to the living mind; tallies and external records imply the very forgetfulness they try to prevent. To need reminders is to confess you might forget; to keep the tablets would be to "import forgetfulness" into the relationship.

The memory preserved in the brain will last "beyond all date, even to eternity" — or at least as long as the brain and heart have natural faculty to function. No physical relic is needed when the person is inscribed in the very substance of the mind.

Literary Devices

  • Paradox: Giving away the gift proves deeper devotion than keeping it.
  • Vocabulary of inscription: "Character'd," "tallies," "retention," "score" — the sonnet treats memory as a superior writing surface.
  • Eternity and nature imagery: "Beyond all date," "to eternity," "till each to raz'd oblivion yield" — temporal scope frames the argument.

Themes of Farewell and Apology

This sonnet offers a philosophical farewell to external memory — to relics and tokens as proofs of love. Genuine love requires no such props. The farewell is gentle but absolute: objects are shed because the interior record is inexhaustible.
