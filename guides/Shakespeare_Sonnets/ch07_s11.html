Study Guide: Sonnet LXIX

Summary

Sonnet LXIX begins as praise and ends as veiled criticism. The first quatrain establishes that the beloved's outward beauty is beyond reproach: the world's eye sees nothing that hearts could improve, and even enemies offer bare, unvarnished praise. The second quatrain introduces the complication: those same praising tongues, looking deeper than outward appearance, see the beloved's mind and measure it by his deeds. The third quatrain describes the effect: what began as kind thoughts turns "churlish" — the rank smell of weeds is added to the fair flower of the beloved's beauty. The couplet delivers a direct verdict: "The soil is this, that thou dost common grow." The beloved has become "common" — vulgar, morally loose — and his behavior undermines the beauty that the world so willingly praises.

Key Themes

  • Appearance versus inner character: Outward beauty is universal and undeniable; inward worth, judged by deeds, is another matter.
  • The gap between reputation and reality: The beloved's "odour" (inner character) does not match his "show" (external beauty).
  • Moral accountability: For Shakespeare, beauty carries a moral dimension — the beloved's actions tarnish his appearance.

Key Literary Devices

  • Nature metaphor: "Fair flower" contrasted with "rank smell of weeds" — beauty vs. moral corruption.
  • Structural movement: The sonnet moves from unreserved praise to qualified praise to criticism.
  • Synecdoche: "All tongues, the voice of souls" — tongues stand for the entire community of observers.

Vocabulary

  • Want: Lack, be deficient in
  • Churls: Rude, surly people; the thoughts turn churlish (uncharitable)
  • Rank: Foul-smelling, overgrown and offensive
  • Soil: The cause, the reason (as in "that is the soil of the problem")
  • Common: Ordinary; morally base, promiscuous
