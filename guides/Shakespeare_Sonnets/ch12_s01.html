The Dark Lady: Desire - A Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces Shakespeare's "Dark Lady" sonnet sequence (Sonnets 127-154), a stark departure from the idealized love of the Fair Youth sonnets. It explores themes of raw desire, authenticity versus artifice, and complex psychological states by challenging conventional Elizabethan beauty standards and depicting an unidealized, often troubled relationship. The identity of the Dark Lady remains a persistent scholarly mystery, adding to the enigmatic power of these intense poems.

Key Concepts

  • Subversion of Elizabethan Beauty Standards: Unlike the prevailing ideal of "fairness" (light complexion) as a mark of beauty, the Dark Lady is explicitly depicted with dark features. Shakespeare uses this to critique artificial cosmetics and champion a more "honest", natural beauty.
  • Authenticity vs. Artifice: The sequence opens with an argument that artificial "fairing" (cosmetics) has devalued traditional beauty. The Dark Lady's natural darkness is thus presented as more authentic than the painted faces around her, setting up a central tension.
  • The Irresolvable Identity: Despite extensive scholarly debate, the true identity of the Dark Lady is unknown. She may have been a real person, a composite of several individuals, or a purely fictional construct, allowing Shakespeare to explore universal aspects of desire.
  • Unidealized, Obsessive Desire: The relationship with the Dark Lady is far from the platonic or idealized love often found in sonnet traditions. It is marked by clear-sighted awareness of the beloved's faults (lies, inconstancy, moral darkness) alongside the speaker's overwhelming, often self-loathing, obsession and enslavement to his desire.
  • Psychological Acuity: These sonnets are celebrated for their profound psychological insight, dissecting complex emotions like lust, self-deception, and the conflict between intellect and carnal craving. They offer an unflinching look at the darker, more unsettling aspects of human relationships.

Important Terms

  • Fair Youth: The idealized male figure addressed in the first 126 of Shakespeare's sonnets, praised for his beauty and virtue. The Dark Lady sequence stands in stark contrast to these poems.
  • Dark Lady: The mysterious, unnamed woman addressed in the final 28 sonnets (127-154) of Shakespeare's collection. She is characterized by dark hair, eyes, and possibly skin, and her relationship with the speaker is intense, troubled, and driven by raw desire.
  • Elizabethan Equation of Beauty: The prevailing aesthetic standard in Elizabethan England, which associated beauty with "fairness," meaning a light complexion, often enhanced by cosmetics.
  • Fairing: The act of using cosmetics to lighten one's skin or enhance one's appearance, often to achieve the "fair" aesthetic ideal. Sonnet 127 critiques this practice as debasing natural beauty.
  • Blazon: A poetic device (common in Petrarchan poetry) that describes a beloved by cataloging her physical features, typically in highly complimentary and idealized terms (e.g., eyes like stars, lips like roses).
  • Anti-Blazon: A poetic technique, exemplified in Sonnet 130, that deliberately subverts or negates the traditional blazon. Instead of idealizing, it offers realistic or even unflattering comparisons, often with a twist that affirms a more genuine love.
  • Petrarchan Comparisons: Poetic comparisons inspired by the Italian poet Petrarch, typically involving highly stylized and often exaggerated descriptions of a beloved's beauty, comparing her to natural wonders (sun, stars, roses, pearls).
  • Sonnet 127: The opening sonnet of the Dark Lady sequence, which provocatively introduces her dark features and critiques artificial "fairing," declaring her natural darkness more honest.
  • Sonnet 129: A searing and clinical dissection of lust—its anticipation, brief pleasure, and subsequent shame and self-loathing. It highlights the mind's inability to control overwhelming physical urges.
  • Sonnet 130: The famous anti-blazon, which humorously negates traditional Petrarchan comparisons for the Dark Lady, only to declare a more authentic and heartfelt love in its concluding lines.
  • Sonnet 138: Explores the theme of mutual deception in a relationship, where both parties are aware of each other's lies but tacitly agree to sustain the fiction, as honesty would destroy their fragile comfort.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to the Dark Lady Sequence

  • Contrast with Fair Youth: The Dark Lady sonnets mark a dramatic shift from the preceding Fair Youth sequence. While the Fair Youth was praised as an ideal of beauty, the Dark Lady is introduced with an emphasis on "negation," "obsession," and "frank reckoning with desire." This suggests a less conventional, more challenging exploration of love and attraction.
  • Challenging Elizabethan Beauty Standards: Shakespeare deliberately "upends" the prevailing Elizabethan ideal that equated beauty with "fairness" or light complexion. The Dark Lady is characterized by her dark hair, dark eyes, and possibly darker skin.
  • Critique of Artifice (Sonnet 127): Rather than apologizing for her darkness or simply replacing one ideal with another, Shakespeare uses the Dark Lady's features to critique artificial beauty. Sonnet 127 argues that the widespread use of cosmetics ("fairing") to achieve a light complexion has cheapened conventional beauty. In this context, the Dark Lady's natural darkness is presented as more "honest" and authentic.
  • Emerging Themes: This provocative opening sets the stage for the sequence's key themes: authenticity, obsession, and the disturbing gap between intellectual understanding and overwhelming physical desire.

The Unresolved Identity of the Dark Lady

  • A Persistent Mystery: The identity of the Dark Lady has been a subject of intense scholarly debate for centuries, but remains "irresolvable." The sonnets themselves do not name her, and no definitive historical document links any specific woman to these poems with certainty.
  • Prominent Candidates:
    • Emilia Lanier (née Bassano): A strong contender. She was a musician of Italian origin, known to have dark coloring, connected to the court and patronage networks, and was herself a significant poet (the first professional woman poet in English). A.L. Rowse vigorously championed her case, though his evidence has been criticized as selective.
    • Mary Fitton: A Victorian favorite who served as a maid of honor to Queen Elizabeth I. However, portraits show her to be fair-skinned, and the chronology of her life doesn't align perfectly with the sonnets' likely composition.
    • Lucy Morgan (also known as Lucy Negro): A former royal servant who later became a brothel-keeper. She appears in contemporary satirical writings, and some scholars find the circumstantial evidence intriguing, though not conclusive.
    • Penelope Rich: The real-life inspiration for Philip Sidney's "Stella" in his sonnet sequence Astrophil and Stella.
    • Other Women: Various other women in Shakespeare's social or professional circle have also been proposed.
  • Scholarly Consensus: The generally accepted academic position is that the Dark Lady's identity cannot be definitively proven. She might have been a real individual, a composite figure drawing characteristics from several women, or a completely fictional creation through whom Shakespeare could explore a specific kind of desire and relationship.

Nature of the Relationship and Thematic Exploration

  • Departure from Idealized Love: The relationship depicted in the Dark Lady sonnets is markedly different from the idealized, often platonic, love described in many sonnet traditions, including Shakespeare's own Fair Youth sequence. It is gritty, raw, and often painful.
  • Speaker's Clear-Sightedness vs. Enslavement: The speaker of these sonnets sees the Dark Lady's flaws with "painful clarity." He catalogues her lies, her inconstancy, and even her "moral darkness." Yet, despite this intellectual awareness, he confesses himself "enslaved" by his desire for her, highlighting the conflict between mind and body.
  • Specific Sonnets as Case Studies:
    • Sonnet 129 ("Th'expense of spirit in a waste of shame"): This is described as "not a love poem at all but a clinical dissection of lust." It graphically details the stages of lust: the shameful anticipation ("lust in action, and till action, lust"), the brief moment of blissful gratification ("past reason hunted"), and the subsequent profound self-loathing and regret ("a bliss in proof, and proved, a very woe"). The concluding couplet acknowledges the tragic irony that understanding this destructive process does nothing to prevent it.
    • Sonnet 130 ("My mistress' eyes are nothing like the sun"): This famous poem is an "anti-blazon." It systematically deflates every traditional Petrarchan comparison that would idealize a beloved (e.g., her eyes are not like the sun, her lips are not like coral). However, it is not a rejection of love. Instead, the final two lines affirm a genuine, grounded love that values her despite, or perhaps because of, her realistic imperfections. The "joke is also a declaration" of true affection.
    • Sonnet 138 ("When my love swears that she is made of truth"): This sonnet delves into the complexities of mutual self-deception in a relationship. Both the speaker and the Dark Lady know that the other is lying, yet they tacitly agree to maintain the fiction. The poem suggests that this shared pretense is preferable because "honesty"—revealing the uncomfortable truths—would "destroy what little comfort remains." It reveals a profound psychological insight into the compromises and fictions people build to sustain difficult relationships.
  • Overall Character of the Sonnets: These are described as "not comfortable sonnets." They are among the "most psychologically acute poems in the English language" and demand "slow, attentive reading" to fully grasp their depth and unflinching portrayal of human desire and its consequences.

Review Questions

  1. How does the introduction of the Dark Lady sonnets (Sonnets 127-154) immediately distinguish itself in tone and theme from the preceding Fair Youth sonnets (1-126)?
  2. Explain how Shakespeare uses the Dark Lady's physical appearance, particularly in Sonnet 127, to challenge and critique traditional Elizabethan beauty standards and notions of "fairness."
  3. Discuss the ongoing scholarly debate surrounding the identity of the Dark Lady. Why do scholars generally conclude that her identity is "irresolvable," and what are the implications of this ambiguity for interpreting the sonnets?
  4. In what ways does the relationship described between the speaker and the Dark Lady differ from an idealized or romanticized love? Provide specific examples from Sonnets 129, 130, and 138 to illustrate this contrast.
  5. The text describes these sonnets as "uncomfortable" yet "psychologically acute." What does this mean in the context of the themes explored (authenticity, obsession, desire, deception), and why do these qualities make the poems rewarding for "slow, attentive reading"?
