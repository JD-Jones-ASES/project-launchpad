Study Guide: Sonnet CXXVII — The Dark Lady's Black Beauty

Overview

Sonnet CXXVII opens the Dark Lady sequence and immediately challenges Petrarchan convention. Where Renaissance love poetry typically prized fair skin, golden hair, and rosy cheeks, Shakespeare's speaker announces that black is now beauty's rightful heir. The sonnet does not merely celebrate his mistress; it mounts a social critique: because cosmetics allow anyone to manufacture a fair appearance, authentic natural beauty has been driven into disrepute.

Key Themes

The Corruption of Beauty by Artifice. The speaker argues that "Art's false borrowed face" has flooded the world with counterfeit fairness, so that true beauty "hath no name, no holy bower." Cosmetics are presented as a form of fraud — a moral failing that slandered nature itself.

Dark Beauty as Mourning. The mistress's raven-black eyes are described as mourners at a funeral, grieving for beauty's lost integrity. This metaphor elevates darkness from a deficiency to an emblem of authentic feeling and moral seriousness.

Redefinition of the Standard. The couplet performs a rhetorical reversal: because her dark eyes mourn so gracefully, "every tongue says beauty should look so." Dark beauty is not merely equal to fair beauty — it becomes the new universal standard.

Literary Devices

  • Antithesis: "Fairing the foul" vs. natural beauty
  • Personification: Beauty given a "holy bower" and dragged into "disgrace"
  • Metaphor: Eyes as mourners in black garments
  • Irony: Darkness, once excluded, becomes the definitive image of beauty

Significance

This sonnet introduces the Dark Lady as a figure who defies conventional praise and demands a new poetic vocabulary — one honest enough to find beauty in what society dismisses.
