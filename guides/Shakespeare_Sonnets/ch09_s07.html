Study Guide: Sonnet XCIII

Overview

Sonnet XCIII takes the unsettling hint of XCII's couplet and develops it into a full meditation: the speaker compares himself to a deceived husband who cannot detect his partner's changed heart because the beloved's face is constitutionally incapable of showing inner truth. Heaven decreed at the beloved's creation that his face would always show sweetness and love regardless of his actual feelings. This divine decree, which sounds like a gift, is revealed as a form of epistemological trap.

Key Themes

The central theme is the disconnection between beautiful appearance and inner moral reality. Most people's faces reveal their hearts; frowns and moods tell the truth about hidden feelings. But the beloved's face is supernaturally beautiful in a way that conceals rather than reveals. The speaker cannot know whether he is loved or deceived, because the beloved's expression will always show love regardless. This makes trust impossible to verify.

Poetic Devices

The deceived husband simile is central and psychologically acute — it locates the speaker's condition within a recognizable domestic tragedy. The biblical allusion in the couplet — "How like Eve's apple doth thy beauty grow" — transforms the beloved's beauty into a symbol of dangerous, deceptive sweetness. The conditional "if thy sweet virtue answer not thy show" frames the warning: beauty without matching virtue is a moral hazard. The poem ends on a warning rather than a resolution.

Estrangement Dimension

XCIII deepens the estrangement theme by making the return to trust genuinely impossible: even if the beloved returned, the speaker could never be certain the face before him was truthful. The beloved's beauty, which was a source of joy in earlier chapters, becomes here a source of epistemological anxiety.
