Study Guide: Sonnet XX — "A woman's face with nature's own hand painted"

Overview

Sonnet XX is one of the most discussed and debated poems in the sequence. It is addressed to a beloved with a woman's face and a woman's gentle heart, yet also possessing male qualities and, ultimately, a male body. The speaker uses the blazon tradition — cataloguing the beloved's features — to establish a figure who surpasses both men and women: more beautiful than women but less inconstant, captivating to both sexes. The poem ends with an account of Nature's error (or perhaps deliberate choice) in making the beloved male, and the speaker's graceful accommodation of this fact.

Key Themes

Gender and Beauty. Sonnet XX engages directly with questions of gender, beauty, and desire. The beloved combines the best qualities of both sexes without the worst of either: woman's face and heart, man's constancy and commanding presence. The blazon tradition is here applied to a male figure, making visible the sequence's complex engagement with the Fair Youth.

Nature's Agency. Nature is personified as a craftsperson who "fell a-doting" on her own creation. This personification explains the beloved's gender not as a cosmic law but as an accident of infatuation, treating the subject with humor and lightness.

The Feminine Rhyme Scheme. Every line ends with an extra unstressed syllable (feminine ending), giving the sonnet a distinctive sonic quality that reinforces its meditation on gender.

Literary Devices

  • Blazon: systematic catalogue of the beloved's attributes
  • Oxymoron: "master mistress of my passion"
  • Personification: Nature as infatuated craftsperson
  • Feminine rhyme: all 14 lines end with a soft extra syllable
  • Wordplay: "hues/use," the pun on "prick'd thee out"

Significance

Sonnet XX stands apart from its neighbors in tone and subject. Its playful wit and frank engagement with the beloved's dual-gendered appeal make it a touchstone for discussions of Shakespeare's sonnets and Elizabethan attitudes toward beauty and desire.
