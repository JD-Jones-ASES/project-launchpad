Study Guide: Sonnet CXXXIV — The Usurer of Hearts

Overview

Sonnet CXXXIV continues directly from CXXXIII, now replacing the imagery of prison and bail with the language of financial debt, mortgage, surety, and usury. The speaker attempts a desperate bargain — he will forfeit himself completely if the mistress will return his friend — but the attempt is doomed. She is too covetous to release anyone, the friend too loyal to leave while the speaker is captive, and the couplet closes with total loss: the friend is gone, she holds both men, and the speaker is still not free.

Key Themes

Financial Exploitation. The mistress is cast as a merciless usurer who puts all her assets "forth to use" — lending beauty at punishing interest, enforcing the full "statute" of what is owed to her, and refusing any mercy. The financial metaphor makes her cruelty systematic rather than personal: she does not hate the speaker; she simply extracts maximum value from all her debtors.

Loyal Friendship Ensnared. The friend's "kindness" — his generosity in going surety for the speaker — becomes the instrument of his own destruction. Virtue (loyalty, generosity) leads directly into the same trap. The world of the Dark Lady sonnets has no moral exit: goodness and badness alike are swallowed.

The Accounting of Loss. The couplet's final ledger is devastating in its precision: she has both men, the friend pays everything, the speaker pays nothing and yet remains enslaved. The arithmetic of the situation makes the injustice visible with painful clarity.

Literary Devices

  • Extended financial metaphor: mortgage, forfeit, surety, usurer, statute, bond, pays
  • Irony: the friend's kindness leads to his deepest captivity
  • Antithesis: "he pays the whole, and yet am I not free"
  • Legal vocabulary: statute, bond, surety, use (interest)

Significance

CXXXIV is the Dark Lady sequence's most explicit treatment of emotional exploitation as financial predation. The speaker is no longer merely heartbroken; he is morally indignant about the systematic nature of her conquest.
