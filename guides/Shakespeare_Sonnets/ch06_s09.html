Study Guide: Sonnet LI

Overview

Sonnet LI is the joyful companion to Sonnet L. Where L lamented the heaviness of journeying away from the beloved, LI turns to imagine the return — and finds that no horse, not even one mounted on the wind, could match the speed desire would demand. The poem moves from logical excuse to rapturous hyperbole.

Meaning and Argument

The speaker first excuses his horse's slow pace on the outward journey: since he is moving away from joy, why hurry? But when he turns to imagine returning, the argument flips dramatically. Desire, "of perfect'st love being made," outstrips all physical speed — even winged speed would feel like standing still. The horse becomes irrelevant: desire needs no animal, so the speaker can "give him leave to go." The couplet concludes by forgiving the horse's earlier slowness, since love will race ahead without it on the return.

Key Literary Devices

  • Hyperbole: "mounted on the wind," "winged speed no motion shall I know" — impossible exaggerations for desire's urgency.
  • Personification: Desire is given a body that races, neighs, and moves in a "fiery race."
  • Ironic pardon: The horse is comically forgiven for its slowness in the couplet — now that desire supersedes it.
  • Antithesis (structural): The poem's first half excuses slowness; the second half celebrates impossibly fast return.

Themes

The sonnet celebrates desire's transcendence of physical limitation. Where the body plods, the spirit races. Together with Sonnet L, it maps the emotional geography of the journey: slow outward, blazing fast inward. The poem also gently deflates its own metaphor — the horse can "go" at the end because it was never truly needed.
