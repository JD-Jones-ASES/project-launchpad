Study Guide: Sonnets 132-135 (The Dark Lady: Desire)

Overview

This section explores Sonnets 132 through 135 from Shakespeare's "Dark Lady" sequence, delving into the speaker's complex and often tormented relationship with his mistress. These sonnets showcase the Dark Lady's captivating yet cruel nature, depicting themes of unrequited love, jealousy arising from a triangular relationship involving a friend, and the speaker's profound psychological and emotional subjugation, all conveyed through intricate metaphors and audacious wordplay.

Key Concepts

  • The Conceit of Mourning Eyes (Sonnet 132): The transformation of the Dark Lady's dark eyes, a traditional anti-Petrarchan feature, into a source of unexpected beauty through the elaborate conceit that they are "in mourning" for the speaker's pain, thereby softening her disdain with "pretty ruth."
  • Triangular Relationship and Betrayal (Sonnets 133 & 134): The painful complication where the Dark Lady not only enthralls the speaker but also ensnares his closest friend (traditionally the Fair Youth), leading to themes of double dispossession, jealousy, and profound emotional suffering for the speaker.
  • Legal and Financial Metaphors (Sonnets 133 & 134): An extended and ruthless use of legal and financial terminology (slavery, ward, bail, engross'd, mortgag'd, usurer, surety, statute) to depict the exploitative nature of the Dark Lady's power and the speaker's and his friend's entrapment.
  • Audacious Wordplay and Pun on "Will" (Sonnet 135): The masterful and ribald use of a triple pun on the word "Will" – referring to the poet's own name (William Shakespeare), sexual desire or appetite, and a legal testament – to make a bold, yet desperate, plea for inclusion in the Dark Lady's affections.
  • Speaker's Helplessness and Subjugation: Across these sonnets, the speaker consistently portrays himself as utterly at the mercy of the Dark Lady's will, stripped of self, agency, and even his dearest friend, highlighting the consuming and oppressive nature of his desire.
  • Transformation of Beauty and Ugliness: Shakespeare continues to challenge conventional beauty standards, particularly in Sonnet 132, where dark features are re-framed as beautiful, and in the couplet where he declares "beauty herself is black."

Important Terms

  • bail: Security given for the release of a prisoner, allowing them to go free pending a hearing.
  • beshrew: A mild curse; to invoke evil upon.
  • covetous: Excessively desirous; greedy.
  • disdain: Contempt; scorn; to regard with contempt.
  • engross'd: Taken over entirely; absorbed; also a legal term meaning to write out a document in formal script (making it binding).
  • large: Generous; also: broad, coarse (in meaning).
  • mortgag'd to thy will: Pledged as security for a debt; also, entirely subjected to her desire. "Will" carries its triple meaning: desire, legal will, and the name Will.
  • mourn: To feel or express sorrow.
  • mourning / morning: A pun Shakespeare exploits; the eyes are in mourning (grief) and they become her face as the morning sun becomes the eastern sky.
  • pent: Confined; shut in; imprisoned.
  • perforce: Necessarily; of necessity; by force.
  • rigour: Severity; harshness; strict enforcement of a rule or penalty.
  • ruth: Compassion; pity; sorrow for the misfortune of another.
  • statute of thy beauty: The legal authority conferred by her beauty—the right she claims, as a creditor, to enforce the full terms of the bond.
  • suit thy pity like in every part: Let your heart dress itself in pity the same way your eyes have dressed themselves in mourning—match the expression of your heart to the expression of your eyes.
  • surety-like: In the manner of a surety—someone who assumes legal responsibility for another's debt.
  • the full star that ushers in the even: The evening star—the planet Venus, which appears in the west at dusk.
  • to boot: In addition; as a bonus.
  • torment: Severe pain; suffering; to cause suffering.
  • usurer: A moneylender who charges excessive interest, condemned by both law and morality in Shakespeare's England.
  • vouchsafe: To grant condescendingly; to give or permit.
  • ward: A prison cell; also, guardianship over a ward (a person under legal protection).
  • Will: Simultaneously: the name William (the poet's own name); sexual desire or appetite; a legal will or testament.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet CXXXII: Thine eyes I love...

This sonnet offers a unique perspective on the Dark Lady's characteristic dark features, particularly her eyes. The speaker cleverly transforms his suffering caused by her "disdain" into a compliment, crafting an elaborate conceit where her dark eyes are not just dark, but have "put on black" as if in mourning for his pain, looking at him with "pretty ruth" (pity).

Shakespeare develops this image through two astronomical comparisons. Just as the morning sun enhances the "grey cheeks of the east" and the "full star that ushers in the even" (the planet Venus) adorns the western sky at dusk, so too do her "mourning eyes" beautify her face. These comparisons, while echoing Petrarchan conventions, are used sincerely here, focusing on the specific beauty of her eyes rather than generalized comparisons. A subtle pun between "mourning" and "morning" enriches this conceit, linking the eyes' sorrowful appearance to the dawn's beauty.

The speaker's plea is gently logical: if mourning so becomes her eyes, then her heart should also "mourn" for him, matching her outward expression with inward pity. The sonnet's couplet pushes this idea to an extravagant conclusion: if her heart were to show pity, he would then declare "beauty herself is black," and anyone lacking her dark complexion would be deemed "foul." This demonstrates Shakespeare's capacity to use wit to transform conventional standards of beauty and suffering into a deeply personal, if hyperbolic, compliment.

Sonnet CXXXIII: Beshrew that heart...

Sonnet 133 introduces a crucial and painful triangular relationship that defines much of the Dark Lady sequence: the mistress has enslaved not only the speaker but also his closest friend (traditionally identified as the Fair Youth). The speaker bitterly curses the Dark Lady's heart for the "deep wound" it inflicts on both him and his friend, asking if it's not enough to torture him alone.

He laments that her "cruel eye" has taken him "from myself" and, even more harshly, has "engross'd" (taken complete possession of) his "next self," his dear friend. This leaves him "forsaken" of himself, his friend, and the Dark Lady, creating a "torment thrice three-fold."

The sonnet then shifts into a legal negotiation metaphor. The speaker begs her to "prison my heart in thy steel bosom's ward" (her body as a jail cell) but to "bail" his friend's heart, offering his own as surety for the friend's release. He attempts to argue that if she keeps him, she cannot enforce "rigour" (severity) on his friend's freedom. However, the couplet delivers a crushing blow: "And yet thou wilt; for I, being pent in thee, / Perforce am thine, and all that is in me." This means that because the speaker himself is completely imprisoned within her power, everything he possesses or could offer, including his heart as surety for his friend, is also hers to control. The poem powerfully captures the complete helplessness and self-dissolution experienced by someone whose will is entirely absorbed by another's.

Sonnet CXXXIV: So, now I have confess'd...

Directly continuing the legal and financial metaphors from Sonnet 133, Sonnet 134 deepens the despair of the triangular predicament. The speaker "confess'd that he is thine" (the friend belongs to her) and acknowledges that he himself is "mortgag'd to thy will," completely subjected to her desire, which also carries the punning sense of his own name, "Will."

He offers himself as a "forfeit," a sacrifice, hoping that in return, she will "restore" his friend (his "other mine") to be his comfort. However, the speaker immediately anticipates her refusal: "But thou wilt not, nor he will not be free, / For thou art covetous, and he is kind." The Dark Lady's greed and the friend's generous nature are contrasted. The friend, being "kind," acted "surety-like" and signed a "bond" for the speaker's debt to the Dark Lady, thereby binding himself equally fast.

The speaker characterizes the Dark Lady as a "usurer" – a moneylender charging excessive interest, a figure condemned in Shakespeare's time – who "putt'st forth all to use" (lends everything out for profit). She enforces the "statute of thy beauty," using her allure as a legal right to sue the friend, who became a "debtor for my sake." The tragic irony is that the speaker loses his friend "through my unkind abuse" (his involvement with the Dark Lady, which put the friend in this position).

The couplet presents the bleak and inescapable arithmetic of their situation: "Him have I lost; thou hast both him and me: / He pays the whole, and yet am I not free." The friend is lost, the Dark Lady has both men, the friend makes the ultimate sacrifice, yet the speaker remains trapped and unfree. This ruthless extension of the financial metaphor strips any romantic illusion from the relationship, portraying it as pure exploitation.

Sonnet CXXXV: Whoever hath her wish, thou hast thy 'Will'...

Sonnet 135 is a masterclass in linguistic extravagance and multi-layered wordplay, built almost entirely on the triple pun of "Will." This single word appears thirteen times, playing on:

  1. William: The poet's own name.
  2. Will (sexual desire/appetite): Her abundant lust or desire for men.
  3. Will (legal testament): A will or bequest, implying inheritance or possession.

The speaker begins by stating that the Dark Lady, who already has "her wish" (desire), also has "thy 'Will'" (his name), "and 'Will' to boot, and 'Will' in over-plus." This suggests she has an abundance of "will" in all its senses, including multiple lovers named Will, or excessive desire. The speaker, "more than enough am I that vex'd thee still," offers himself as "addition" to her "sweet will."

He then poses a rhetorical question: "Wilt thou, whose will is large and spacious, / Not once vouchsafe to hide my will in thine?" He argues that if "will" (desire/men) is accepted "gracious" in others, why should his "will" not find "fair acceptance"?

An analogy from nature illustrates his point: just as the sea, already full of water, "receives rain still / And in abundance addeth to his store," so too should she, being "rich in 'Will'," add one more "will" (his own) to her vast store, making her "large will more."

The couplet serves as a direct, albeit audacious and somewhat desperate, plea: "Let no unkind 'No' fair beseechers kill; / Think all but one, and me in that one 'Will.'" He asks her not to reject fair suitors and to consider all her desires/suitors as one collective "Will," within which he hopes to be included. The poem's ribald and playful tone masks an underlying vulnerability and intense desire for acceptance.

Review Questions

  1. How does Sonnet 132 use the imagery of mourning and astronomical comparisons to transform the Dark Lady's dark features into a source of beauty and to express the speaker's pain?
  2. Analyze the development of the triangular relationship in Sonnets 133 and 134. What specific metaphors are used to describe the speaker's and his friend's entrapment, and how do they reveal the Dark Lady's character?
  3. Discuss the significance of the legal and financial terminology (e.g., "engross'd," "bail," "mortgag'd," "usurer," "statute") in Sonnets 133 and 134. How do these metaphors strip the relationship of any romantic illusion?
  4. Explain the multi-layered pun on the word "Will" in Sonnet 135. How does this extensive wordplay function as both an audacious plea and a reflection of the speaker's emotional state?
  5. Across Sonnets 132-135, what different facets of the Dark Lady's character are revealed, and how do these portrayals contribute to the speaker's internal conflict and ultimate subjugation?
