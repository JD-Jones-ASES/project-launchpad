Study Guide: Sonnets 110-114 – Constancy and the Muse

Overview

This section delves into a sequence of Shakespeare's sonnets (110-114) that explore themes of confession, social stigma, absolute devotion, and the transformative power of love on perception. The speaker grapples with past infidelities and the public shame of his profession, ultimately reaffirming his exclusive devotion to the beloved. These sonnets form a profound psychological exploration of loyalty, public image, and the subjective reality of a lover's mind.

Key Concepts

  • Confession and Forgiveness: Sonnet 110 presents a candid confession of the speaker's "wanderings" and "worse essays," acknowledging past infidelities or follies. However, this admission serves to reinforce the beloved's unique and enduring value, leading to a plea for welcome and forgiveness.
  • Social Stigma and Public Image: Sonnet 111 directly addresses the social disgrace associated with the speaker's public profession (likely acting). He blames "Fortune" for his "public means" and the "brand" it places upon his name and character, likening it to a dyer's permanently stained hand.
  • Exclusive Devotion and Disregard for External Opinion: Sonnet 112 portrays the speaker's profound psychological reorientation, where the beloved's love and pity become the sole arbiters of his self-worth. He declares himself impervious to external criticism or flattery, throwing all "care of others' voices" into an "abysm."
  • Love's Transformative Power on Perception: Sonnet 113 vividly illustrates how the speaker's mind, "replete with" the beloved, actively reshapes all visual input. His physical eye registers images, but his inner mind transforms every object—be it bird, flower, or mountain—into the beloved's "feature."
  • The Nature of Love's Perception (Questioning Reality): Sonnet 114 continues the philosophical inquiry from Sonnet 113, questioning whether this transformation of perception is a form of "flattery" (self-deception or idealization) or a profound "alchemy" that reveals a deeper truth through the beloved's love.

Important Terms

  • motley: The multi-colored costume worn by a court jester; by extension, a fool. To make oneself "a motley to the view" means to play the fool publicly.
  • gor'd: Wounded; stabbed; pierced with a sharp point. Metaphorically, the speaker has "gor'd mine own thoughts" by straying.
  • blenches: Flichings; turnings aside; moments of wavering or infidelity.
  • essays: Attempts; trials; tests. "Worse essays" refers to inferior loves or diversions that proved the beloved's superiority by contrast.
  • grind: To wear down; to sharpen by friction. "Mine appetite I never more will grind / On newer proof" means no longer sharpening one's desire by testing it on new experiences.
  • brand: A mark burned into flesh as a sign of disgrace; a stigma. The speaker's "name receives a brand," indicating public infamy.
  • the dyer's hand: A vivid image referring to a dyer whose hands are permanently stained by dyes, symbolizing how one's profession can indelibly mark one's identity or character.
  • eisel: Vinegar; a bitter liquid used as a remedy or penance.
  • double penance: Penance beyond what is required. The speaker is willing to endure severe punishment for his faults.
  • vulgar: Common; of or belonging to the common people. "Vulgar scandal" refers to common gossip and public censure.
  • o'er-green: To cover over with green growth; to restore freshly. Love "o'er-greens" the speaker's bad reputation as grass covers a wound.
  • abysm: Abyss; a bottomless depth. Into this "profound abysm" the speaker casts all concern for others' opinions.
  • adder's sense: The hearing of a deaf adder. The adder was believed to stop its ears against charmers, symbolizing the speaker's deliberate shutting out of external voices (critics and flatterers).
  • latch: To catch; to perceive; also to close. The eye "latches" onto objects but doesn't hold them, as they are reshaped by the mind.
  • rud'st: Roughest; most crude or unrefined.
  • favour: Face; expression; appearance. A common Renaissance usage.
  • feature: Form; shape; appearance; the lineaments of a face. Retains its broader sense of the whole face and figure.
  • flattery: Excessive or insincere praise; here, potentially self-deception in how love perceives reality.
  • alchemy: A form of chemistry, especially in the medieval period, focused on transforming base metals into gold or finding a universal elixir. Here, it refers to a transformative process of perception through love.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet 110: Confession and Rediscovery

  • Confession and Apology: The speaker begins with an "alas" and a full confession, admitting to past wanderings ("gone here and there"), making himself "a motley to the view" (a public fool, possibly referencing his theatrical career), and cheapening his affections. He acknowledges looking at truth "askance and strangely."
  • Paradoxical Renewal: Despite these transgressions, the speaker argues that these "blenches" (waverings) and "worse essays" (inferior trials of affection) paradoxically "gave my heart another youth" and proved the beloved to be his "best of love." The experience of straying confirmed the original value of the beloved by contrast.
  • Resolution and Return: The volta at line 13 ("Now all is done") signals a genuine and absolute resolution. The speaker asserts he will no longer "grind" his appetite on "newer proof" to test his "older friend." He describes the beloved as "a god in love" and seeks unconditional welcome, not based on merit but on complete dependency.
  • Context: The Actor's Shame: The phrase "motley to the view" is strongly linked to Shakespeare's life as an actor and playwright, a profession that carried significant social stigma in Elizabethan England. This sonnet, alongside 111, may reflect the personal anxiety Shakespeare felt about his public life affecting his reputation as he sought to establish himself as a gentleman.
  • Connections: This sonnet forms a pair with 109, completing an apology (denial in 109, admission in 110). It echoes themes of infidelity seen in sonnets 40-42 and anticipates the absolute devotion of 116.

Sonnet 111: Blaming Fortune and Seeking Pity

  • Blaming Fortune: The speaker blames "Fortune" – the goddess of fate and social station – for assigning him a "public means which public manners breeds," implicitly referring to his theatrical profession. He sees this livelihood as having tainted his character and reputation.
  • The "Brand" and "Dyer's Hand" Metaphor: He laments that his "name receives a brand," a mark of disgrace, and that his nature is "subdu'd / To what it works in, like the dyer's hand." This powerful simile illustrates how his profession has indelibly stained and corrupted his identity.
  • Desire for Renewal and Penance: The speaker expresses a deep desire to be "renew'd" and is willing to undertake drastic measures, including drinking "potions of eisel" (vinegar, a bitter medicine) and enduring "double penance," to cleanse himself.
  • The Power of Pity: The couplet offers a transformative twist: the beloved's pity alone is enough to cure him. This shifts the focus from external blame and self-punishment to the redemptive power of the beloved's empathy.
  • Connections: This sonnet pairs with 110 in its self-reproach. The "dyer's hand" image became famously adapted by T. S. Eliot. The plea for pity sets up Sonnet 112, where the beloved's opinion becomes paramount.

Sonnet 112: Exclusive Devotion and Indifference to the World

  • Beloved's Opinion as Sole Standard: The sonnet opens by asserting that the beloved's "love and pity doth the impression fill" that "vulgar scandal stamp'd upon my brow." The beloved's approval effectively heals the speaker's wounded reputation. He declares he no longer cares "who calls me well or ill," as long as the beloved "o'er-green my bad, my good allow."
  • "All-the-World" Devotion: The speaker states, "You are my all-the-world," and his self-worth is entirely derived from the beloved's judgment. He must "know my shames and praises from your tongue."
  • Shutting Out the World: He profoundly rejects all other opinions, throwing "all care / Of others' voices" into a "profound abysm." He compares himself to an "adder's sense" (a deaf adder), effectively deafening himself to both "critic and to flatterer."
  • Extreme Psychological Reorientation: The sonnet concludes with an intense statement of exclusive devotion: "You are so strongly in my purpose bred, / That all the world besides methinks are dead." This signifies a total psychological focus on the beloved, rendering everyone and everything else insignificant.
  • Connections: This sonnet's theme of the beloved's judgment being the only one that matters connects to 111 and anticipates the philosophical absolutism of 116. The "all-the-world" sentiment echoes 109's couplet and highlights a deep psychological dependency, while the world-as-dead image links it to 29 and 30, where the beloved offers rescue from societal contempt.

Sonnet 113: Love's Distorted Vision

  • The Mind's Eye Overrides Physical Sight: The speaker describes a profound shift in his perception "Since I left you." His physical eye still functions, but "mine eye is in my mind," meaning his internal mental state dictates what he truly sees. His eye "seems seeing, but effectually is out" in terms of independent perception.
  • Transformation of All Images: The mind no longer registers the true form of objects. Instead, every sight – from "bird, of flower" to "rud'st or gentlest sight," "mountain or the sea," "crow or dove" – is transformed and "shapes them to your feature" (the beloved's face/form).
  • Paradox of Fidelity: The sonnet explores a beautiful paradox: the speaker's "most true mind" (faithful to the beloved) "thus maketh mine untrue" (his eye becomes a liar, incapable of seeing things as they really are). Absolute internal fidelity leads to external perceptual distortion.
  • Connections: This conceit of the mind's eye overriding physical vision connects to the literary tradition of love as a form of blindness or transformative perception. It sets up Sonnet 114, which directly continues this philosophical inquiry into whether love distorts or clarifies reality.

Sonnet 114: Questioning Love's Alchemy (Partial Content)

  • Continuation of Philosophical Inquiry: Sonnet 114 picks up directly from the theme of Sonnet 113, questioning the nature of the beloved's power over the speaker's perception.
  • Two Possibilities for Love's Influence: The speaker presents two options:
    1. Is his mind, "being crown'd with you," succumbing to "the monarch's plague, this flattery"? (i.e., is he idealizing the beloved to the point of self-deception, seeing only what he wants to see, a form of intellectual sycophancy common to monarchs?)
    2. Or does his "eye saith true," and the beloved's love has taught it a profound "alchemy"? (i.e., is love transforming his perception to reveal a deeper, more refined truth, much like alchemy transforms base metals into gold?)
  • The Nature of Reality and Perception: The sonnet, even in its partial form, frames a fundamental question about love: does it blind us with flattery and illusion, or does it grant us a unique, alchemical vision that transcends ordinary reality? This reflects a broader philosophical debate about subjective experience versus objective truth.
  • Connections: This sonnet forms a direct continuation and philosophical expansion of Sonnet 113's exploration of distorted vision. It probes whether the beloved's influence is a deceptive flattery or a profound, transformative "alchemy" that elevates perception.

Review Questions

  1. How do Sonnets 110 and 111 complement each other in their portrayal of the speaker's self-reproach and his relationship with the beloved? Discuss the different sources of his confessed "errors."
  2. Analyze the significance of the "actor's shame" in Sonnets 110 and 111. How does Shakespeare use vivid imagery (e.g., "motley to the view," "dyer's hand," "brand") to convey the social stigma associated with his profession?
  3. Sonnet 112 presents a powerful declaration of exclusive devotion. Discuss the implications of the speaker's statement, "all the world besides methinks are dead." Is this total psychological reorientation portrayed as healthy dependency or a potentially troubling isolation?
  4. Explain the paradox presented in Sonnet 113: "my most true mind thus maketh mine untrue." How does the sonnet illustrate the mechanism by which the speaker's fidelity to the beloved leads to a distortion of his physical perception?
  5. Based on the opening lines of Sonnet 114 and its connection to Sonnet 113, what overarching philosophical question about the nature of love and perception is being explored in this sequence (113-114)? Do you think love, in this context, is ultimately presented as a form of "flattery" or "alchemy"?
