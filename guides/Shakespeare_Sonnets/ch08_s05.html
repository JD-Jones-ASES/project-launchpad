Study Guide: Sonnet LXXXII

Overview

Sonnet LXXXII adopts a tone of magnanimous generosity. The speaker releases the youth from any exclusive obligation to his verse and grants him full freedom to seek out other poets' praise. This apparent concession is, however, a calculated rhetorical move: by granting the youth permission to read elaborate, strained verse, the speaker sets up the comparison that will favor his own plain sincerity.

Meaning and Argument

The opening concession — "I grant thou wert not married to my Muse" — is gracious but also pointed; it acknowledges that the youth was never bound to the speaker exclusively. The youth's worth surpasses the speaker's praise, so he must seek fresher expression from more current poets. But "do so, love" comes with a "yet": when those rivals have exhausted all the strained rhetorical touches they can muster, the youth was "truly sympathiz'd / In true plain words, by thy true-telling friend." The couplet delivers the verdict on the rivals' "gross painting": elaborate rhetorical cosmetics belong on those who need them — those with blood-deficient cheeks — not on one naturally beautiful. Applied to the youth, such painting is abused.

Poetic Devices

The cosmetic metaphor of "painting" versus plain truth runs through the sestet and couplet, linking artistic ornamentation to false cosmetics applied to faces that don't need them. "Strained touches" and "gross painting" are the speaker's disparaging labels for rhetorical excess. The anaphora of "true" in lines 13-14 ("truly fair," "truly sympathiz'd," "true plain words," "true-telling friend") creates a drumbeat of authenticity that implicitly indicts the rivals' artificiality.

Themes

The central themes are authenticity versus artifice in poetic praise, the ethics of flattery, and the speaker's self-identification as the honest mirror of the youth's true worth. The sonnet argues that plain truth is superior to elaborate rhetoric — a defense of simplicity as the highest poetic virtue when the subject is already perfect.
