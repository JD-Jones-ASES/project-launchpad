Study Guide: Sonnets 16-17 - The Case for Posterity

Overview

This section explores Sonnets 16 and 17, which continue to urge the young man (the Youth) to procreate as a means of defying time and achieving immortality. While Sonnet 16 champions biological continuation over the speaker's poetry, Sonnet 17 introduces a nuanced argument: the Youth's living child would serve to validate the truthfulness of the speaker's verse, making procreation and poetry complementary paths to lasting legacy.

Key Concepts

  • Procreation as a "Mightier Way" Against Time: Sonnet 16 presents having children as a more effective and "blessed" method to combat the destructive power of time compared to the speaker's "barren rhyme."
  • The Speaker's Self-Deprecating Poetry: The speaker deliberately diminishes his own poetic skill, calling his verse "barren rhyme" or a "tomb" in comparison to the vibrant life a child would embody. This serves as a rhetorical strategy to persuade the Youth.
  • "Lines of Life": This brilliant phrase in Sonnet 16 holds a double meaning, referring simultaneously to the lines of poetry (verse) and the genealogical line of descent (children), both of which "repair" or restore life.
  • Poetic Credibility in Sonnet 17: The speaker expresses anxiety that if he were to truly capture the Youth's exceptional beauty in verse, future generations would dismiss it as exaggeration, "a poet’s rage," or "stretched metre."
  • The Child as Validator of Poetic Truth: Sonnet 17 proposes a resolution: a living child of the Youth would provide tangible proof of the beauty described in the poem, ensuring that the verse is believed and thus the Youth lives "twice."
  • Poetry and Procreation as Partners: The culminating idea of Sonnet 17 is that these two strategies—biological continuation through a child and artistic immortality through verse—are not rivals but synergistic partners in ensuring the Youth's lasting presence.

Important Terms

  • maiden gardens : (Sonnet 16) A metaphor referring to the bodies of young women, conceptualized as fertile ground or gardens full of potential life waiting to be "planted" or propagated with children. This extends the agricultural imagery from earlier sonnets.
  • painted counterfeit : (Sonnet 16) A portrait or painted likeness that merely resembles the original without possessing its actual life or vitality. The term implies an imitation, contrasting with the "living flowers" (children) that are the "real thing."
  • lines of life : (Sonnet 16) A clever pun that simultaneously refers to the written lines of poetry (the speaker's verse) and the genetic or ancestral "line" of offspring, both of which are intended to "repair" or renew the Youth's life.
  • deserts : (Sonnet 17) Merits; the qualities or virtues that make someone worthy of praise or reward. "Your most high deserts" refers to the Youth's exceptional beauty, virtue, and excellence.
  • rage : (Sonnet 17) In this context, it refers to a poet's "poetic frenzy" or divine inspiration, often associated with exaggerated or passionate expression. Here, it is used dismissively, suggesting future readers might view the speaker's praise as mere enthusiasm rather than factual truth.
  • stretched metre : (Sonnet 17) Exaggerated or overwrought verse; poetry that has been unnaturally extended or distorted to accommodate excessive praise, implying a lack of genuine truth.
  • antique song : (Sonnet 17) Old-fashioned or outdated poetry; a poem from an earlier, perhaps less reliable or credible era. Future readers might dismiss the sonnets as outmoded and untruthful.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet 16: The "Mightier Way" of Procreation

Sonnet 16 opens with a direct and challenging question to the Youth: "But wherefore do not you a mightier way / Make war upon this bloody tyrant, Time?" The speaker immediately positions procreation as a superior method for battling time compared to his own poetic efforts. This is a deliberate rhetorical move, as the speaker calls his own poetry "barren rhyme," diminishing its power to emphasize the biological alternative.

The sonnet describes the Youth as being at the peak of his life, "on the top of happy hours," surrounded by "maiden gardens, yet unset" – a vivid horticultural metaphor for young women ready to bear children. These children would be "living flowers," far more authentic and vital than any "painted counterfeit" (a portrait) or, by extension, any poem. The speaker contends that neither his "pupil pen" nor "Time’s pencil" (referring to a painter or perhaps Time itself as an artist) can truly immortalize the Youth's "inward worth" or "outward fair" in the eyes of men.

A key phrase in this sonnet is "So should the lines of life that life repair," which beautifully encapsulates the central tension. "Lines of life" cleverly puns on two meanings: the lines of verse (poetry) and the line of descent (genealogy). Both, the speaker argues, have the capacity to "repair" or restore life, implying a perpetual renewal against time's decay.

The couplet of Sonnet 16 provides an elegant resolution to the argument for procreation: "To give away yourself, keeps yourself still, / And you must live, drawn by your own sweet skill." This is a paradox where spending oneself (in having children) does not diminish but rather perpetuates the self. The Youth’s "sweet skill" refers to his ability to create life, ensuring his continued existence. This reinforces the idea that biological legacy is the most potent form of immortality. The "maiden gardens" metaphor echoes the "unear'd womb" from Sonnet 3, connecting this sonnet to the broader procreation sequence and its consistent use of agricultural imagery.

Sonnet 17: The Crisis of Credibility and Complementary Immortality

Sonnet 17 continues the theme of immortality but shifts focus to a crisis of poetic credibility. The speaker poses a skeptical question: "Who will believe my verse in time to come, / If it were fill’d with your most high deserts?" He argues that even an accurate description of the Youth's extraordinary beauty and merits ("most high deserts") would be dismissed by future generations as mere poetic exaggeration.

The speaker's verse, he fears, would be seen as insufficient, described as "but as a tomb / Which hides your life, and shows not half your parts." He anticipates that future readers, upon reading his fervent praise, would scorn his "papers, yellow’d with their age," dismissing them "like old men of less truth than tongue." They would accuse him of a "poet’s rage" (over-the-top inspiration) and "stretched metre of an antique song" (exaggerated, old-fashioned verse). This concern reflects a genuine Renaissance anxiety about hyperbole in love poetry, where extravagant praise could actually undermine a poet's credibility. Shakespeare turns this commonplace on its head by using it as an argument for procreation.

This self-undermining strategy, where the speaker admits the limits of his own art, cleverly sets up the sonnet's resolution in the couplet. Here, the two paths to immortality—procreation and poetry—are finally presented not as rivals, but as partners:

"But were some child of yours alive that time, You should live twice,—in it, and in my rhyme."

The living beauty of the Youth's child would serve as tangible proof, a visible testament, to the truth of the speaker's seemingly incredible poetic descriptions. The child’s existence would validate the poem, allowing both the child and the verse to ensure the Youth’s enduring legacy. The Youth would "live twice"—once in the physical continuation through his offspring, and once in the artistic preservation through the speaker's poetry.

This couplet is a pivotal moment, resolving the tension that has run through the preceding sonnets. It anticipates Sonnet 18's famous declaration ("So long as men can breathe, or eyes can see, / So long lives this, and this gives life to thee") by establishing poetry as a powerful force for immortality, but with the added dimension that a living heir strengthens the poem's claim to truth. The phrase "antique song" also foreshadows Sonnet 106, where the speaker considers how earlier poets struggled to describe the Youth's beauty. Sonnet 17 thus acts as a hinge, moving from rivalry to partnership in the grand debate between biological and artistic immortality.

Review Questions

  1. Compare the arguments for immortality presented in Sonnet 16 and Sonnet 17. How do they differ in their initial approach, and how do they ultimately complement each other?
  2. Analyze the speaker's self-deprecating remarks about his own poetry (e.g., "barren rhyme," "tomb," "stretched metre") in both sonnets. What rhetorical purpose do these serve, and how effective are they?
  3. Explain the multi-layered meaning of the phrase "lines of life" in Sonnet 16. How does this phrase capture the central tension between genetic and poetic immortality that runs through the early sonnets?
  4. In Sonnet 17, the speaker expresses significant anxiety about future readers disbelieving his verse. What specific phrases and concerns does he use to convey this "poetic credibility problem"? How does this anxiety connect to Renaissance literary conventions?
  5. Discuss the significance of the couplet in Sonnet 17: "But were some child of yours alive that time, / You should live twice,—in it, and in my rhyme." How does this resolve the tension between procreation and poetry, and what does it foreshadow for later sonnets in the sequence?
