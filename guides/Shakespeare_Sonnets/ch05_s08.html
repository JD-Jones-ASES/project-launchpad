Study Guide: Sonnet XL

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet XL opens the triangular crisis cluster (XL-XLII) in which the beloved has become involved with a woman the speaker also loves. The poem's opening gesture is sardonic permission: "Take all my loves, my love, yea take them all." A cascade of wordplay on "love" in the first six lines entangles affections, persons, and concepts in a labyrinth from which neither speaker nor reader easily escapes.

The speaker's logical argument is that the beloved can gain nothing genuinely new by this theft — everything was already spiritually the beloved's. Yet the beloved is still blamed: knowingly taking what one's own conscience refuses is the particular sin of willful self-deception. The speaker then grants forgiveness ("I do forgive thy robbery, gentle thief") but immediately qualifies it: love's wrong causes greater grief than hate's known injury, because it comes from the trusted.

The couplet's oxymoron — "lascivious grace" — holds the whole poem's contradiction in two words: the beloved's charm makes their wrongdoing beautiful even as it makes the speaker ache. The final instruction "we must not be foes" is both a plea and a determination — the friendship must survive even this.

Key Literary Devices

  • Polyptoton / wordplay: The cycling of "love" through multiple meanings in lines 1-6.
  • Oxymoron: "Gentle thief," "lascivious grace" — beauty and wrongdoing inseparably joined.
  • Conceit: The moral argument that prior spiritual ownership means the beloved "steals" nothing truly new.
  • Volta: The couplet pivots from the painstaking argument about grief to a compressed, paradoxical final plea.

Themes

Betrayal by intimacy: An enemy's harm is expected; a friend's betrayal cuts deeper precisely because it comes from where trust lived.

Anguished forgiveness: Forgiveness is granted but not cheaply — it exists alongside acknowledged grief, not instead of it.

The paradox of attractive wrongdoing: "Lascivious grace, in whom all ill well shows" — the beloved's beauty makes their sins not ugly but aesthetically pleasing, which is itself a moral problem.
