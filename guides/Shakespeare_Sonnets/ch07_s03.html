Study Guide: Sonnet LXI

Summary

Sonnet LXI explores the torment of loving someone who is absent. The speaker lies awake at night, his "heavy eyelids" unable to close, haunted by shadows that seem to take the beloved's form. He imagines that perhaps the beloved's "spirit" is responsible — sent from far away to pry into the speaker's shames and idle hours out of jealousy. The volta at line 11 corrects this hypothesis: "O, no! thy love, though much, is not so great." It is not the beloved's controlling jealousy that keeps the speaker awake — it is his own love. He plays the watchman, ever vigilant for the beloved's sake. The couplet delivers the bitter irony: while the speaker keeps this loving vigil, the beloved is awake elsewhere, with others who are "all too near."

Key Themes

  • Absence and longing: Love without the beloved's presence becomes a form of sleepless torment.
  • Self-knowledge and correction: The volta is a self-correcting moment — the speaker dismisses his own imaginative hypothesis and finds the true cause within himself.
  • Jealousy and devotion: The speaker projects jealousy onto the beloved, then redirects it into a more painful self-awareness.

Key Literary Devices

  • Volta: The sharp "O, no!" at line 11 pivots from hypothetical jealousy to the truth of the speaker's own love.
  • Personification: Shadows given the power to "mock" the speaker's sight.
  • Dramatic irony: The watchman's vigil is rendered absurd and poignant by the couplet's revelation.

Vocabulary

  • Slumbers: Sleep
  • Pry: To spy or intrude
  • Scope and tenure: Range and purpose
  • Watchman: A guard who stays awake through the night to keep watch
  • Whilst: While
