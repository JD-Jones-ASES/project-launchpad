Study Guide: Devouring Time – Sonnets 64-68

Overview

This section explores Sonnets 64, 65, and 66, a powerful sequence that delves into the relentless destruction wrought by time. Sonnet 64 confronts the inevitable decay of all things, culminating in a personal fear for the beloved. Sonnet 65 builds on this despair, questioning how fragile beauty can withstand time's might, yet tentatively offers the "miracle" of poetry as a form of preservation. Finally, Sonnet 66 presents a comprehensive lament of the world's injustices and suffering, finding the sole reason to endure in the love for the beloved.

Key Concepts

  • Time's Universal Destruction (Sonnet 64): The sonnet emphasizes time's pervasive power to erode monuments, alter natural landscapes, and ultimately bring all things to decay. This universal process leads the poet to a devastating personal conclusion about the impermanence of his love.
  • Absence of Poetic Consolation (Sonnet 64): Unlike some other "time sonnets," Sonnet 64 offers no redemptive turn in its couplet, instead expressing pure grief and fear over anticipated loss, highlighting a moment of profound despair.
  • The Fragility of Beauty (Sonnet 65): Beauty is depicted as incredibly vulnerable, likened to a flower against the "wrackful siege" of time, which even conquers "brass, nor stone, nor earth, nor boundless sea."
  • The "Miracle" of Black Ink (Sonnet 65): The sonnet concludes with a fragile, almost miraculous hope that poetry—represented by "black ink"—can preserve the beloved's beauty, defying the natural laws of decay. This is a subtle, less triumphal assertion of poetic immortality compared to other sonnets.
  • World-Weariness and Social Critique (Sonnet 66): The poet catalogues a litany of social ills and injustices, from merit unrewarded to virtue corrupted and authority misused, expressing a profound desire for "restful death" as an escape from a corrupt world.
  • Love as the Sole Anchor (Sonnet 66): Despite the overwhelming despair and longing for death, the poet's love for the beloved serves as the single, powerful reason to endure, demonstrating love's capacity to transcend profound suffering and disappointment.

Important Terms

  • fell (Sonnet 64): Fierce; savage; destructive. Refers to the cruel, powerful action of time.
  • defac'd (Sonnet 64): Erased; disfigured; ruined. Time not only destroys but removes identity and legibility.
  • rich-proud cost (Sonnet 64): The expensive, ostentatious display of past civilizations' grand monuments. Conveys both vanity and magnificence.
  • down-raz'd (Sonnet 64): Completely demolished; razed to the ground. Emphasizes total destruction.
  • interchange (Sonnet 64): Exchange; alternation of state. Refers to the ceaseless, purposeless change between sea and land.
  • ruminate (Sonnet 64): To think deeply; to chew over. Describes the poet's slow, contemplative process of confronting a terrible discovery.
  • o'ersways (Sonnet 65): Overpowers; overcomes; sways by superior force. Mortality is the stronger force in all contests.
  • plea (Sonnet 65): A legal argument; a case presented in court. Beauty cannot win a lawsuit against time.
  • wrackful (Sonnet 65): Causing wreck and ruin; destructive. Describes the devastating "siege" of time.
  • stout (Sonnet 65): Strong; resistant; courageous in resistance. Even impregnable rocks are not stout enough against time.
  • spoil (Sonnet 65): Plunder; what is taken in a raid. Time is cast as a raider stripping beauty.
  • miracle (Sonnet 65): A wonder beyond natural explanation. The survival of beauty in ink is presented as something defying natural rules.
  • needy nothing (Sonnet 66): Worthless nothingness dressed up as something important or joyful.
  • forsworn (Sonnet 66): Renounced or given up on oath; betrayed. Refers to purest faith being unfortunately placed or broken.
  • rudely strumpeted (Sonnet 66): Prostituted; dishonored in a crude and violent manner. Refers to maiden virtue being corrupted.
  • limping sway disabled (Sonnet 66): Weakened or put at a disadvantage by imperfect, hobbling authority. Strength hobbled by incompetent power.
  • miscall'd simplicity (Sonnet 66): Simple truth wrongly called foolishness or lack of sophistication.
  • captive good attending captain ill (Sonnet 66): Goodness enslaved and serving under the command of evil.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet 64: When I have seen by Time’s fell hand defac’d

Sonnet 64 is often called the "ruins" sonnet, as it vividly depicts the evidence of time's relentless destruction. The poet observes magnificent human achievements and natural phenomena succumbing to decay: once-lofty towers are "down-raz’d," durable brass monuments are worn away, and even the "hungry ocean" and "firm soil" engage in a ceaseless, purposeless "interchange" of territory, where neither truly wins. This profound survey of universal decay leads to a single, devastating personal conclusion in the third quatrain: "That Time will come and take my love away."

Unlike many other "time sonnets" where the poet finds consolation in the enduring power of verse, Sonnet 64 offers no such rescue. The couplet, "This thought is as a death which cannot choose / But weep to have, that which it fears to lose," expresses pure, unadulterated grief and fear. It is a poem that confronts the universe's indifference and finds no solace, making it one of the darkest in the sequence.

Historical Context: Elizabethans were familiar with ruins from the recent dissolution of monasteries under Henry VIII, which left many grand structures "roofless across the countryside." This provided an immediate, local resonance to the sonnet's imagery. The poem also draws on the classical "ubi sunt" (where are they now?) lament tradition, but Shakespeare personalizes it, shifting the ultimate catastrophe from the fall of empires to the anticipated loss of his beloved.

Connections: This sonnet is the darkest point in a series of meditations on time, forming a bridge between Sonnet 63 (which imagines the youth's decay and responds with verse) and Sonnet 65 (which attempts to find an answer to the despair of 64). Its "interchange of state" recalls Ovid's Pythagoras on endless change. The couplet's emotional register echoes Sonnet 30's catalog of losses, but here, the beloved is the object of anticipated loss, not the consolation.

Sonnet 65: Since brass, nor stone, nor earth, nor boundless sea

Sonnet 65 acts as a direct sequel to Sonnet 64, picking up at the height of despair. If even the most robust materials and forces—brass, stone, earth, and the boundless sea—cannot withstand "sad mortality," the poet asks, "How with this rage shall beauty hold a plea, / Whose action is no stronger than a flower?" The sonnet then accumulates powerful images of time's overwhelming force: "the wrackful siege of battering days," "rocks impregnable," and "gates of steel." Against these, beauty is presented as incredibly fragile, likened to "summer’s honey breath."

The sestet escalates the fear into a series of urgent, almost panicked questions: where can beauty hide from time? Who can stop time's swift foot or forbid its "spoil"? The implied answer to these questions in the first twelve lines is "no one." However, the couplet then provides a tentative, whispered "O!"—a "miracle" that offers a glimmer of hope: "That in black ink my love may still shine bright." This is not a confident boast but a fragile possibility, presenting one of poetry's most beautiful and understated claims for immortality.

Historical Context: The paradox of "black ink" preserving beauty is striking because ink and paper are physically fragile. Yet, Shakespeare places his hope here, echoing classical boasts of poetic immortality (like Horace's Exegi monumentum and Ovid's Metamorphoses). However, Shakespeare's claim is tempered by "unless this miracle have might," which acknowledges the genuine terror of the preceding lines and the fragile nature of this hope.

Connections: Sonnets 64 and 65 form a diptych: 64 identifies the problem without a solution, while 65 poses it more urgently and finds a tentative answer. The "black ink" of 65 connects to Sonnet 63's "black lines" and anticipates Sonnet 74's idea of the spirit surviving in verse. It contrasts sharply with Sonnet 55's more confident declaration of poetic immortality ("Not marble, nor the gilded monuments of princes / Shall outlive this powerful rhyme"), showcasing a range of poetic confidence within the sequence.

Sonnet 66: Tired with all these, for restful death I cry

Sonnet 66 is a profound cry of world-weariness and disillusionment, often compared to Hamlet's soliloquies for its bitter survey of human folly and injustice. The poet expresses a deep longing for "restful death," listing a series of societal corruptions that make life unbearable. These grievances are delivered through a relentless anaphora of "And" clauses across twelve lines, cataloging:

  • Deserving individuals born into poverty ("desert a beggar born").
  • Worthless people receiving praise and happiness ("needy nothing trimm’d in jollity").
  • Pure faith being betrayed ("purest faith unhappily forsworn").
  • Honour being wrongly bestowed ("gilded honour shamefully misplac’d").
  • Innocence corrupted ("maiden virtue rudely strumpeted").
  • Goodness disgraced ("right perfection wrongfully disgrac’d").
  • Strength undermined by weak authority ("strength by limping sway disabled").
  • Art silenced by censorship ("art made tongue-tied by authority").
  • Folly controlling wisdom ("folly, doctor-like, controlling skill").
  • Truth dismissed as naivety ("simple truth miscall’d simplicity").
  • Good enslaved to evil ("captive good attending captain ill").

The cumulative effect of this list is a powerful statement of despair over the moral and social decay of the world. However, the sonnet pivots dramatically in the couplet: "Tir’d with all these, from these would I be gone, / Save that, to die, I leave my love alone." Love for the beloved is the only reason the poet does not embrace death, serving as the sole anchor and justification for enduring an otherwise intolerable existence.

Historical Context: While specific historical events are not detailed in the provided text, the list of grievances speaks to universal social problems that resonate across eras. The sentiment of preferring death over the world's injustices is a common theme in literature, making the sonnet's raw emotional honesty particularly potent.

Connections: This sonnet is a critical turning point in the sequence, emphasizing the profound value and power of the beloved's presence. It highlights that even amidst comprehensive disillusionment, love can provide a compelling reason for life. The intense "And" structure is a rhetorical device that builds emotional momentum and emphasizes the endless nature of the world's problems.

Review Questions

  1. Sonnet 64 is described as offering "no such rescue" and weeping instead of promising poetic triumph. What does this refusal of consolation say about the poem's emotional honesty, and how does it make the sonnet more or less powerful compared to those that do offer poetic immortality (like Sonnet 65)?
  2. In Sonnet 64, the "interchange of state" between the sea and land, where neither truly wins, can be read as a figure for the instability of all "states," including political ones. How does this potential political undercurrent deepen the poem's meditation on time and decay?
  3. Sonnet 65 uses powerful imagery of natural forces and human constructs (brass, stone, rocks, steel gates) to build a relentless case against survival before offering a "miracle" in the couplet. Does this structural buildup make the couplet's hope feel like a genuine answer, or more like wishful thinking in the face of overwhelming odds?
  4. Compare the closing claim of Sonnet 65 ("O! none, unless this miracle have might, / That in black ink my love may still shine bright") with the more confident assertion in Sonnet 55 ("Not marble, nor the gilded monuments of princes / Shall outlive this powerful rhyme"). What are the implications of Shakespeare's choice of language in each, and which do you find more convincing or moving as an argument for poetry's immortality?
  5. Sonnet 66 presents a comprehensive list of social and moral injustices that drive the poet to long for death. Analyze the rhetorical effect of the repeated "And" clauses throughout the first twelve lines. How does this extended list emphasize the poet's world-weariness, and how does the single exception in the couplet dramatically reframe the entire sonnet's argument?
