Study Guide: Shadows and Forgiveness — Shakespeare's Sonnets 33-37

Overview

This section delves into Sonnets 33, 34, and 35 of Shakespeare's Fair Youth sequence, exploring the speaker's complex emotional response to a transgression committed by the beloved Youth. Through a progression of metaphors—from a clouded sun to a storm-ridden journey and ultimately a legal battle—the sonnets trace the speaker's journey from melancholy forgiveness to direct accusation, and finally to a profound act of self-accusation and complicated love.

Key Concepts

  • The Solar Conceit Transformed: The traditional Elizabethan metaphor of the beloved as a "sun" is re-imagined. In Sonnet 33, the Youth-as-sun is not merely obscured by external forces but "permits" his own obscuration, introducing the idea of his agency and hinting at betrayal.
  • The Progression of Forgiveness: Across the three sonnets, the speaker's approach to forgiveness evolves. Sonnet 33 offers a cosmic pardon, 34 finds forgiveness in the Youth's tears, and 35 culminates in the speaker blaming himself, revealing the intense psychological struggle within the speaker.
  • Nature of Betrayal and Disappointment: The sonnets vividly portray the pain of being let down by a loved one. The initial beauty of the "glorious morning" (33) gives way to the "rotten smoke" (34) of disappointment, highlighting the contrast between expectation and reality.
  • Insufficient Remedies: Sonnet 34 explores the idea that remorse, while potentially genuine, cannot fully undo the harm caused. The "salve" for a wound doesn't remove the "disgrace" or "scar," suggesting that some hurts are irreversible.
  • The Legal Metaphor: Sonnet 35, in particular, employs extensive legal vocabulary to describe the speaker's internal conflict. He becomes both accuser and advocate, ultimately an "accessary" to the Youth's "theft," illustrating the self-destructive nature of his love and rationalization.
  • Internal Conflict and Self-Accusation: The speaker's struggle between love and grievance is central. Sonnet 35 highlights this "civil war" within, where the speaker's love is so powerful it compels him to defend the one who wronged him, even at his own expense.
  • Natural Imperfection as Justification: Sonnet 35 uses comparisons to natural phenomena (thorns on roses, mud in fountains, cankers in buds) to initially justify the Youth's "faults," revealing a human tendency to rationalize the imperfections of those we love.

Important Terms

  • Sovereign eye: The sun's gaze, or the Youth's, described as that of a supreme ruler, implying majesty, power, and the ability to bestow or withdraw favor.
  • Gilding: Covering with gold; adorning with beauty. Used of sunlight turning pale streams golden, but also suggesting the deceptive beauty of surface appearances.
  • Rack: A mass of wind-driven clouds; suggests clouds are seized and carried off, not just appearing.
  • Celestial: Of the heavens; divine. Describes the Youth's face, making his obscuration all the more devastating.
  • Stain: Both a mark of discoloration and a mark of moral shame. Shakespeare holds both senses simultaneously for the Youth's fault.
  • Bravery: Finery; splendor. Here, the Youth's radiant beauty, hidden by shameful conduct.
  • Salve: An ointment for a wound; a remedy. Acknowledges an attempt at healing, but the speaker rejects its sufficiency.
  • Physic: Medicine; a remedy. Used to argue that the Youth's shame, like a doctor's grief, doesn't cure the patient.
  • Repent: To feel regret and turn from wrongdoing. Shakespeare distinguishes between repentance and the undoing of consequences.
  • Ransom: Payment to free a captive; here, reparation sufficient to cancel a debt. Transforms the Youth's tears into something of sovereign value.
  • Loathsome canker: A caterpillar or grub that destroys rose buds from within; also a plant disease. An image combining outward beauty with inward corruption.
  • Trespass: A transgression; a wrong committed. Carries both legal and moral weight.
  • Amiss: A fault; a wrong. "Salving thy amiss" means applying a healing ointment to wrongdoing.
  • Sensual fault: A fault arising from physical appetite or desire. The closest the sonnet comes to naming the transgression.
  • Adverse party: The opposing side in a legal action; the accuser. The speaker acknowledges he should be this.
  • Advocate: A lawyer; one who pleads a cause in court. The speaker turns his role as wronged party inside out by defending the Youth.
  • Accessary: An accessory to a crime; one who assists a wrongdoer without directly committing the act. The speaker's devastating self-accusation.
  • Solar Conceit: A common poetic convention in Elizabethan verse where a beloved or patron is compared to the sun. Shakespeare transforms this by allowing his "sun" to misbehave.
  • Petrarchan tradition: A poetic tradition, derived from Petrarch, where the beloved is typically a fixed point of brightness and ideal beauty. Shakespeare deviates from this.
  • Fair Youth sequence: The sequence of Shakespeare's sonnets (1-126) primarily addressed to a young man.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Shadows and Forgiveness

Chapter 5, Section 2 introduces a pivotal moment in the Fair Youth sequence, moving from unconditional praise to a more complicated, shadowed love. The sonnets in this section (33-35) directly address a perceived transgression by the Youth, exploring the speaker's emotional fallout and his tortuous path towards understanding, blame, and ultimately, a unique form of forgiveness.

Sonnet 33: "Full many a glorious morning have I seen"

This sonnet opens with a breathtaking description of a sunrise, painting a vivid picture of natural beauty and majesty. The sun, with its "sovereign eye," flatters mountain tops and gilds streams, an image of powerful, benevolent radiance. However, this beauty is short-lived as "basest clouds" soon "ride" with "ugly rack" to obscure the sun's "celestial face," hiding its "visage" with "disgrace."

The turn (line 9) reveals the sonnet's true subject: "Even so my sun one early morn did shine," comparing the Youth to this glorious but fleeting sun. The Youth briefly illuminated the speaker's world with "triumphant splendour" but was "but one hour mine" before a "region cloud hath mask'd him from me now." This reveals a deep personal betrayal or withdrawal.

The couplet offers a melancholic pardon: "Yet him for this my love no whit disdaineth; / Suns of the world may stain when heaven’s sun staineth." The speaker forgives, rationalizing the Youth's fault by comparing it to the natural imperfections of the heavenly sun. However, the initial beauty, the brevity of the light, and the word "disgrace" in line 8 carry the weight of what was lost and the sorrow beneath the forgiveness.

Key points:

  • Delayed Revelation: The first eight lines establish a beautiful natural scene before the personal application. This heightens the impact of the Youth's "disgrace."
  • Transformed Solar Conceit: Unlike traditional Petrarchan idealization, the Youth-as-sun here actively "permits" his own obscuration, suggesting agency in his fault.
  • Ambiguous Forgiveness: Is it genuine forgiveness or a sorrowful rationalization? The speaker's love is unwavering, but the underlying pain is clear.
  • Connection: This sonnet establishes the central solar imagery that reappears throughout the chapter and sequence, often signifying the Youth's presence or absence.

Sonnet 34: "Why didst thou promise such a beauteous day"

Moving from the veiled complaint of Sonnet 33, Sonnet 34 confronts the Youth more directly, using the weather metaphor as an explicit accusation. The speaker questions why the Youth promised a "beauteous day" only to leave him vulnerable, traveling "without my cloak," exposed to "base clouds" that hide the Youth's "bravery" in their "rotten smoke."

The Youth has attempted to make amends; he has "broken through the cloud" and his tears are mentioned ("those tears are pearl"). However, the speaker rejects this as an insufficient remedy. He argues that a "salve" (ointment) might heal the wound but doesn't "cure the disgrace" or remove the scar. Similarly, the Youth's "shame" and "sorrow" cannot provide "physic" (medicine) for the speaker's grief, as the "offender's sorrow" offers "weak relief" to the one who bears the "strong offence's cross." The pain of the wronged party remains.

Yet, in a characteristic Shakespearian reversal, the couplet undermines the speaker's own strong rebuke: "Ah! but those tears are pearl which thy love sheds, / And they are rich and ransom all ill deeds." The Youth's tears are transformed from a sign of regret into precious gems, capable of paying for and canceling all his wrongdoings. The speaker's love triumphs over his grievance, leaving the sonnet suspended between justified anger and helpless tenderness.

Key points:

  • Direct Accusation: A shift from the indirect complaint of 33, directly questioning the Youth's actions and broken promises.
  • Insufficient Reparation: Explores the idea that sincere remorse doesn't necessarily undo the harm caused; some losses are permanent.
  • Love's Triumph: Despite the strong arguments against forgiveness, the speaker's profound love for the Youth ultimately prevails in the couplet.
  • Connection: This sonnet introduces "legal language" ("offender," "offence's cross," "ransom") which is further developed in Sonnet 35.

Sonnet 35: "No more be griev'd at that which thou hast done"

Sonnet 35 presents a striking rhetorical reversal where the speaker blames himself for the Youth's transgression. He begins by urging the Youth not to grieve, offering natural comparisons as justification: "Roses have thorns, and silver fountains mud," "Clouds and eclipses stain both moon and sun," and a "loathsome canker lives in sweetest bud." All things, including the Youth, have inherent imperfections, making his fault seem natural.

However, the speaker immediately catches himself in this act of justification. By "Authorizing thy trespass with compare" and "salving thy amiss" (excusing his fault), he recognizes that he is "Myself corrupting," making himself an accomplice. The sonnet then brilliantly employs a detailed legal metaphor to describe this internal conflict. The speaker, who should be the "adverse party" (the accuser), instead becomes the "advocate" (defender) for the Youth, commencing a "lawful plea" against himself.

This internal struggle is described as "civil war" in his "love and hate." The devastating conclusion in the couplet is that the speaker, by defending the Youth, becomes an "accessary" to the "sweet thief" who "sourly robs from me." He has been wronged, yet his overwhelming love has made him complicit in his own suffering. The "sensual fault" of the Youth, vaguely hinted at, is swallowed by the speaker's self-blame.

Key points:

  • Self-Accusation: The speaker, instead of blaming the Youth, blames himself for enabling and excusing the Youth's fault.
  • Extensive Legal Metaphor: This sonnet is a masterclass in using legal terms ("trespass," "amiss," "adverse party," "advocate," "plea," "accessary") to illustrate the speaker's psychological conflict.
  • "Civil War": A powerful phrase to describe the internal division between love and grievance.
  • Complicity in Suffering: The speaker becomes an "accessary" to the harm done to him, highlighting the self-destructive power of his love.
  • Context: Legal Language: The pervasive use of legal terms reflects the widespread understanding of legal processes and vocabulary in Elizabethan England, enriching the sonnet's resonance.

Connections Across the Sonnets (33-35)

This sequence of sonnets demonstrates a clear progression in the speaker's emotional response to the Youth's transgression. Sonnet 33 offers a veiled complaint and cosmic forgiveness, 34 presents a direct accusation softened by the Youth's tears, and 35 culminates in profound self-blame through a complex legal metaphor. Together, they move from a naturalistic framing of the problem to a more psychological and morally intricate exploration of love, betrayal, and forgiveness. The imagery of light and shadow, clouds and clear skies, runs through the entire chapter, signifying the shifting fortunes of the speaker's relationship with the Youth.

Review Questions

  1. The speaker in Sonnet 33 says that because the sun in heaven sometimes "stains," his "sun" (the Youth) cannot be blamed. Is this genuine forgiveness, or is it a rationalization to avoid confronting the Youth's actions? What does the word “disgrace” in line 8 suggest about the speaker's true feelings?
  2. Sonnet 33's first eight lines beautifully describe a natural phenomenon before introducing the personal betrayal. What effect does this "delayed revelation" have on the reader's reception of the sonnet's emotional impact, particularly on how we understand the couplet?
  3. In Sonnet 34, the speaker initially rejects the Youth's tears as a sufficient remedy ("no man well of such a salve can speak"). How does the couplet then dramatically reverse this argument, and what does this reversal suggest about the nature of the speaker's love for the Youth?
  4. Sonnet 35 uses an extensive legal metaphor, culminating in the speaker declaring himself an "accessary" to the "sweet thief" who robs him. Explain how the speaker justifies the Youth's actions and, in doing so, implicates himself. What does this "civil war" between his love and hate reveal about the complexities of his emotions?
  5. Across Sonnets 33, 34, and 35, trace the evolution of the speaker's response to the Youth's transgression. How does the speaker's perspective shift from veiled complaint to direct accusation, and finally to self-blame, and what do these shifts tell us about the nature of their relationship?
