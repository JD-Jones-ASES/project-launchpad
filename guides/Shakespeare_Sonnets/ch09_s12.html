Study Guide: Sonnet XCVIII

Overview

Sonnet XCVIII is a companion piece to XCVII, both centered on seasonal absence-elegy. Where XCVII describes absence during summer and autumn, XCVIII focuses on spring — the most vivid, most celebratory season, when even the melancholy planet Saturn was moved to laugh and leap. Yet the speaker could find no delight in spring's offerings because all natural beauty — the birds' songs, the flowers' colors and scents — was revealed to be merely derivative of the beloved. Nature copies the beloved's qualities; without him, spring is only shadow-play.

Key Themes

The central theme is the beloved as the original of all natural beauty. Where earlier sonnets in the sequence describe the beloved in terms of nature (roses, seasons), XCVIII reverses the relationship: nature is described in terms of the beloved. The lily's white comes from the beloved's hand; the roses' colors come from his complexion. The beloved is declared "you pattern of all those" — the archetype from which every flower draws its qualities. Natural beauty, experienced without him, is a shadow of the real thing.

Poetic Devices

"Proud-pied April, dress'd in all his trim" is a bold personification of spring as an elegantly attired proud figure. The reference to Saturn (planet of melancholy) laughing and leaping heightens the contrast with the speaker's unmoving grief — if even the most morose force in the cosmos was moved by spring, the speaker's numbness is all the more striking. The couplet's "shadow" metaphor — playing with shadows rather than the real thing — compresses the entire sonnet's theme.

Return Dimension

XCVIII, paired with XCVII, represents the chapter's "Return" beginning to emerge: the beloved is not simply absent but pervasively present in all natural things. This prepares the ground for XCIX's full imaginative reunion with the beloved through the world of flowers.
