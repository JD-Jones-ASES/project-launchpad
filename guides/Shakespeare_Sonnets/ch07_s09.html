Study Guide: Sonnet LXVII

Summary

Sonnet LXVII asks a sequence of indignant rhetorical questions: why should the beloved live among infection and grace impiety with his presence? Why should false cosmetics imitate his natural coloring? Why should artificial beauty seek borrowed roses when the beloved's rose is genuine? The implicit critique is of a debased age that exploits authentic beauty as cover for its own corruption. The third quatrain offers an economic metaphor: Nature herself is now bankrupt, emptied of her blood and vitality, and the beloved is her only remaining treasury. Nature lives "upon his gains" — exploiting the beloved as the sole source of beauty in a world that has exhausted all others. The couplet reveals Nature's ultimate purpose: she stores the beloved to display "what wealth she had / In days long since, before these last so bad."

Key Themes

  • The beloved as authentic beauty in a corrupt age: The beloved is genuine; the world around him is artificial and infected.
  • Nature's bankruptcy: The natural world has spent all its beauty resources on the beloved alone.
  • Temporal decline: The present age is explicitly inferior to a past age of genuine beauty.

Key Literary Devices

  • Rhetorical questions: The repeated "Why should he...?" creates a tone of indignant wonder.
  • Economic metaphor: "Bankrupt," "exchequer," "gains," "wealth" — Nature is figured as a depleted treasury.
  • Irony: The beloved's presence, meant to uplift, instead only legitimizes the corrupt age's pretensions.

Vocabulary

  • Infection: Moral corruption, contamination
  • Lace: To adorn, to embellish
  • Exchequer: A treasury or financial reserve
  • Bankrupt: Emptied of resources, insolvent
  • Gains: Profits, earnings
