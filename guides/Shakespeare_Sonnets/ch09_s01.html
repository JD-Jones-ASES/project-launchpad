Study Guide: Sonnet LXXXVII

Overview

Sonnet LXXXVII opens Chapter 9 with a formal farewell that is also an act of radical self-deprecation. The speaker concedes that the beloved is "too dear" for him to deserve, framing the entire relationship in the language of Elizabethan legal and commercial contracts: charters, patents, bonds, and grants. This vocabulary is not cold but devastatingly personal — by casting love as a legal transaction, the speaker reveals that he has no rightful claim to the beloved at all. The gift of the beloved's companionship was founded on error: either the beloved misjudged his own worth or misjudged the speaker as a worthy recipient. Now that better judgment prevails, the gift reverts.

Key Themes

The central theme is estrangement through unworthiness. Unlike sonnets that blame the beloved or outside forces for separation, LXXXVII places all responsibility on the speaker's inadequacy. This is not self-pity but a logical argument: no merit, no claim. The legal metaphor ("my bonds in thee are all determinate") transforms emotional loss into an expired contract, making grief feel inevitable and orderly rather than chaotic.

Poetic Devices

The sustained legal conceit — charter, bonds, patent, deserving, riches — gives the sonnet unusual structural coherence. The closing dream image disrupts this coldly logical register: "In sleep a king, but waking no such matter." The dream simile humanizes the legal argument, revealing that behind the precise legal language is a person who briefly experienced the beloved's love as majesty and dignity, now lost on waking. The volta arrives gently in the couplet rather than at line 9, which allows the emotional weight to accumulate across the full twelve lines.

Estrangement Dimension

This sonnet establishes the chapter's core posture: separation is presented as just, inevitable, and the speaker's own fault. The absence of accusation or bitterness — replaced by legal rationality and a dream's soft sorrow — makes the estrangement all the more poignant.
