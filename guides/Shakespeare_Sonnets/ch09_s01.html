Study Guide: Estrangement and Return (Sonnets 87-99)

Overview

This chapter delves into a relationship experiencing profound strain, focusing on a speaker who feels abandoned and blames himself excessively. The sonnets trace a journey from intense self-reproach and a willingness to accept any fault, through philosophical contemplation of power and corruption, to a poignant engagement with nature where the beloved's absence colors every season and flower. The overarching theme is estrangement, explored through psychological depth, intellectual paradox, and lyrical expression.

Key Concepts

  • Estrangement and Self-Blame: The core emotional state of the speaker, who feels cast aside and readily accepts, even amplifies, any fault attributed to him by the beloved. This willingness to take responsibility is a defining characteristic of this section.
  • Radical Self-Abnegation: In sonnets like 87-89, the speaker demonstrates an extreme form of self-sacrifice and self-denial. He offers to invent wrongs, take the beloved's side against himself, and even slander his own name, showcasing a profound loss of self-worth or, alternatively, an ultimate expression of selfless love.
  • The Ambiguity of Sonnet 94: This sonnet presents a complex philosophical puzzle. On the surface, it praises those with great power who choose to hold it in reserve. However, its final couplet, particularly the image of "festering lilies," introduces a sharp warning that transforms the apparent praise into something deeply ambivalent and open to conflicting interpretations regarding its judgment of the youth.
  • Absence and Transformed Nature: Sonnets 97 and 98 explore the speaker's physical separation from the beloved. This absence is so profound that it distorts his perception of time and nature, making vibrant seasons like summer and spring feel bleak and joyless.
  • Playful Accusation and Lyric Enchantment (Sonnet 99): This unique, fifteen-line sonnet takes the theme of absence and the beloved's omnipresence to a whimsical extreme. The speaker playfully accuses various flowers of "stealing" their beauty – color, scent, softness – from his absent beloved, showcasing a charming and imaginative form of poetic expression.

Important Terms

  • Estrangement: The state of being alienated or separated from someone or something, often leading to feelings of sadness or hostility. In this context, it describes the emotional distance between the speaker and the beloved.
  • Self-Abnegation: The act of denying one's own interests, desires, or needs in favor of others, or an extreme form of self-sacrifice. Here, it refers to the speaker's readiness to degrade himself for the beloved.
  • Adjudicate: To make a formal judgment or decision about a disputed matter. The text notes Shakespeare "does not adjudicate," meaning he presents the experience without explicitly judging the speaker's behavior.
  • Ambivalent: Having mixed feelings or contradictory ideas about something or someone. This term describes the dual nature of Sonnet 94, which seems to both praise and condemn.
  • Festering Lilies: A powerful and memorable image from the final couplet of Sonnet 94, symbolizing beauty that has become corrupt, dangerous, or putrid. It introduces a stark warning and dark undertone to the sonnet.
  • Lyric Enchantment: A quality in poetry that creates a sense of wonder, charm, or captivating beauty, often by transforming ordinary perceptions or extending a poetic conceit in an imaginative way. Sonnet 99 is cited as an example.
  • Philosophical Paradox: A seemingly contradictory statement or proposition that may nonetheless be true, or one that, when investigated, reveals a deeper truth or complexity. Sonnet 94 embodies this by presenting a surface meaning that is complicated by its underlying implications.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Estrangement and Return (Sonnets 87-99)

This group of sonnets, from 87 to 99, focuses on a period of intense emotional strain and separation within the relationship between the speaker and the beloved youth. The narrative thread is one of "estrangement," characterized not solely by the youth's perceived coldness but primarily by the speaker's profound internal struggle, marked by self-blame and an extraordinary willingness to accept fault. The chapter shows a progression from deep psychological introspection to broader philosophical reflection, culminating in a lyrical engagement with the natural world that is profoundly shaped by absence.

The Dynamics of Estrangement and Self-Blame

The speaker believes he has been "cast aside" by the beloved. His reaction is not to accuse or defend himself but to turn inward, blaming himself "with extraordinary thoroughness." He's not just accepting fault; he's willing to "amplify, whatever fault is laid at his door," demonstrating a deep-seated humility or, perhaps, a painful sense of unworthiness. Even when he observes the beauty of nature, his thoughts inevitably return to the "absent face of the one he loves."

Sonnets 87, 88, and 89: Radical Self-Abnegation

These opening sonnets are highlighted as being among the most "psychologically revealing" in the entire sequence. The speaker's behavior here is striking:

  • Refusal to Defend: He explicitly "does not argue for his innocence or plead for justice."
  • Extreme Self-Sacrifice: Instead, he performs an "act of radical self-abnegation." This means he offers to:
    • "Invent wrongs he never committed."
    • "Take the youth's side against himself."
    • "Speak ill of his own name if the beloved requires it."
  • Interpretations: This behavior has sparked significant debate among readers. Some view it as "the highest expression of selfless love," demonstrating an ultimate devotion. Others see it as a "deeply troubling portrait of a man who has lost all sense of his own worth." The text notes that Shakespeare, "characteristically," avoids making a judgment, instead rendering the experience "with unflinching precision," allowing for both interpretations to coexist.

Sonnet 94: The Most Debated Sonnet

Sonnet 94 is central to the chapter due to its complex and ambiguous nature.

  • Surface Praise: Ostensibly, the sonnet praises individuals who possess great power but choose to restrain it. It speaks of those who "can move others" while remaining "themselves as stone"—implying a controlled, perhaps stoic, nature.
  • The Curdling Warning: The critical shift occurs in the final couplet, which introduces a "warning so sharp" that it "curdles" the apparent praise. The vivid image of "festering lilies" is key here. Lilies, traditionally symbols of purity and beauty, are shown decaying from within, suggesting that even the most beautiful or powerful can become corrupt and dangerous if their inner nature is unsound or their power is misused.
  • Unresolvable Tension: Readers have never reached a consensus on whether this sonnet ultimately "admires or indicts the youth." This inherent ambiguity and "unresolvable tension" are precisely what make Sonnet 94 so compelling and enduringly debated. It encourages deep reflection on the nature of power, control, and hidden corruption.

Sonnets 97 and 98: Absence and Transformed Nature

These two sonnets are "remarkable pair of absence poems" that focus on the speaker's experience of physical separation from the beloved.

  • Seasonal Distortion: The beloved's absence has a profound impact on the speaker's perception of the natural world.
    • "Summer and harvest feel like winter" because the youth is gone, stripping the warmer seasons of their warmth and joy.
    • "Spring arrives splendidly dressed but brings no joy," indicating that even the most vibrant and rejuvenating season is dulled by the speaker's internal state.
  • Emotional Landscape: The physical absence creates an emotional landscape where the external world, regardless of its inherent beauty, is experienced through the lens of profound longing and sorrow.

Sonnet 99: Playful Accusation and Lyric Enchantment

Sonnet 99 stands out as "an oddity in the sequence" for two main reasons:

  • Fifteen Lines: Unlike the standard fourteen-line sonnet, Sonnet 99 contains fifteen lines, making it structurally unique.
  • Playful Hyperbole: It pushes the logic of absence to a "playful extreme." The speaker engages in a fanciful accusation: he charges "every flower in the garden of theft." He claims each bloom has "stealing its color, scent, or softness from his beloved."
  • Lyric Enchantment: This sonnet is described as an example of "lyric enchantment." The speaker's whimsical accusation, though hyperbolic, is presented with such imaginative charm that it creates a captivating and delightful effect, emphasizing the beloved's pervasive influence even in absence.

The Chapter's Emotional Arc

The chapter concludes by highlighting the emotional journey these sonnets collectively trace. It moves from:

  • Legal Self-Surrender (87-89): The speaker's profound willingness to take blame and offer himself up.
  • Philosophical Paradox (94): The complex and debated exploration of power, restraint, and hidden corruption.
  • Lyric Enchantment (97-99): The imaginative and poignant depiction of absence transforming the natural world.

This progression, from internal psychological depth to intellectual ambiguity and finally to expressive lyrical play, serves as a "miniature" representation of the "full arc of the sonnets' emotional world."

Review Questions

  1. What is the central emotional theme running through Sonnets 87-99, and how does the speaker primarily react to it?
  2. Explain the concept of "radical self-abnegation" as it appears in Sonnets 87-89. How have readers interpreted this behavior, and why does Shakespeare avoid definitive judgment?
  3. Discuss the ambiguity of Sonnet 94. What is its apparent message, and how does the final couplet, particularly the image of "festering lilies," complicate this interpretation?
  4. How do Sonnets 97 and 98 illustrate the speaker's experience of absence? How does the beloved's absence transform the speaker's perception of the natural world and the seasons?
  5. Sonnet 99 is described as "an oddity" and an example of "lyric enchantment." In what ways does it stand out, and how does it humorously extend the chapter's themes of absence and the beloved's pervasive influence?
