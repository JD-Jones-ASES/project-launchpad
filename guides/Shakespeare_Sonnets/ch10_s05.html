Study Guide: Shakespeare's Sonnets 115-116 — Constancy and the Muse

Overview

This section explores Sonnets 115 and 116, presenting two seemingly contrasting yet ultimately complementary views on the nature of love and its relationship with time. Sonnet 115 acknowledges love's capacity for growth and change, admitting that previous declarations of "fullest flame" were merely reflections of a moment. Sonnet 116, on the other hand, provides one of the most famous definitions of true love as an unyielding, constant force, impervious to the erosions of time and circumstance.

Key Concepts

  • Love's Dynamic Nature (Sonnet 115): The speaker revisits previous declarations of love, recognizing that even what felt like the "fullest flame" at one point could grow "clearer" later. This challenges the idea of a static, unchangeable love, seeing it instead as a "babe" that continuously develops.
  • Time's Tyranny: Sonnet 115 highlights Time's destructive power, which "creeps in 'twixt vows," "changes decrees of kings," "tans sacred beauty," and "blunts the sharp'st intents." This constant alteration necessitates a re-evaluation of love's previous expressions.
  • Love as a Growing Child: The concluding couplet of Sonnet 115 philosophically frames love as an entity that is "still doth grow," implying that to declare it "full growth" prematurely is to misunderstand its essential, evolving nature.
  • True Love's Immutability (Sonnet 116): This sonnet famously asserts that true love does not "alter when it alteration finds" or diminish when the beloved withdraws. It is defined by its steadfastness and resilience in the face of change.
  • Love as an "Ever-Fixed Mark": Through powerful navigational metaphors, love is depicted as a permanent landmark or a guiding star for "every wandering bark." It provides reliable direction and stability, measurable in its presence but invaluable in its worth.
  • Love's Independence from Time: Sonnet 116 directly confronts Time, declaring that "Love's not Time's fool." While physical beauty succumbs to Time's "bending sickle," true love endures "even to the edge of doom," transcending mortal limits.
  • The "Marriage of True Minds": The opening lines reference a legal marriage formula to establish the idea of a spiritual, intellectual union of souls that cannot be hindered by external "impediments," aligning with Platonic ideals but grounding them in practical terms.
  • Productive Tension: The two sonnets, 115 and 116, create a deliberate tension: love grows and changes (115), yet true love is absolute and unchanging (116). This invites readers to contemplate love's dual nature as both an evolving experience and an enduring ideal.

Important Terms

  • reckoning: Counting; calculation; an accounting. In Sonnet 115, "Reckoning Time" means taking Time fully into account—measuring it honestly.
  • million'd accidents: The countless, unpredictable events that time produces. These "creep in 'twixt vows," suggesting time's capacity to erode even the most solemn promises.
  • tyranny: Cruel, oppressive, arbitrary rule. Time's "tyranny" is its power to alter and destroy what was fixed.
  • incertainty: Uncertainty; the state of being unsure. The speaker was "certain o'er incertainty"—confident despite knowing that absolute certainty was impossible.
  • impediments: Obstacles; hindrances; legal bars to marriage. Drawn from the Anglican marriage service, Shakespeare's speaker refuses to admit any such barriers to the union of "true minds."
  • alteration: A change; a modification. The central test of love in Sonnet 116 is whether it "alters when it alteration finds"—changes when circumstances change around it.
  • ever-fixed mark: A permanent navigational landmark; a fixed point for sailors. Love is presented as a concrete, practical, and reliable reference point.
  • bark: A small sailing vessel; a ship. "Every wandering bark" refers to every lost ship looking for a star to steer by, guided by love.
  • bending sickle: The curved blade of the grim reaper; Time's harvesting scythe. The "bending" suggests both the curve of the blade and the arc of its destructive swing.
  • brief hours and weeks: The short measure of mortal time. This contrasts with "the edge of doom," emphasizing love's transcendence of temporal limits.
  • edge of doom: The last judgment; the end of the world. "Doom" retains its older meaning of a solemn decree—specifically God's final verdict at the last day.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet 115: Love's Growth and Time's Effect

  • The Speaker's Recantation: The sonnet opens with a startling admission: "Those lines that I before have writ do lie." These are lines where he previously stated he could not love the beloved "dearer."
  • The Paradox of Past Judgment: The speaker clarifies that his earlier judgment was not intentionally false; at the time, he genuinely believed his love was at its peak. He "knew no reason why / My most full flame should afterwards burn clearer." This establishes a theme of love's capacity to deepen and intensify beyond perceived limits.
  • Time as a Catalyst for Change: The central reason for this re-evaluation is "Time, whose million'd accidents." Time is personified as a powerful force that:
    • "Creep in 'twixt vows": It erodes promises.
    • "Change decrees of kings": It alters even the most powerful human laws.
    • "Tan sacred beauty": It diminishes physical allure.
    • "Blunt the sharp'st intents": It weakens resolve.
    • "Divert strong minds to the course of altering things": It shifts focus and purpose.
  • Prudence in Declarations: Fearing "Time's tyranny," the speaker questions why he might have been hesitant to declare his "best love" in the past. Perhaps it was a wise caution, "Crowning the present, doubting of the rest," acknowledging the uncertainty of the future.
  • Love as a Continuously Developing Entity: The concluding couplet offers a philosophical resolution: "Love is a babe, then might I not say so, / To give full growth to that which still doth grow?" This metaphor suggests that love is not a static state but a living, growing entity. To claim it had reached "full growth" would be to prematurely limit its potential, hindering its natural development.
  • Connections: This sonnet’s theme of growth connects to the "procreation sonnets" (like 1-17) and the idea of continuity, and prepares for the declaration of enduring love in Sonnet 116 by establishing the context of love's dynamic journey.

Sonnet 116: The Definition of Unchanging Love

  • A Legal and Philosophical Opening: The sonnet begins with a formal, almost legalistic declaration: "Let me not to the marriage of true minds / Admit impediments." This echoes the Anglican marriage service and immediately sets a tone of gravitas and commitment. It asserts that genuine spiritual and intellectual unions ("true minds") should not be hindered by external obstacles.
  • Defining Love by What It Is Not: The speaker defines true love by refuting what it is not:
    • It does not "alter when it alteration finds" (change when circumstances change).
    • It does not "bend with the remover to remove" (waver when the beloved withdraws or changes). This establishes love's unconditional and steadfast nature.
  • Navigational Metaphors for Constancy: Love is then powerfully defined through two central metaphors:
    • "An ever-fixed mark": A permanent navigational landmark (like a coastal feature) that withstands "tempests" (violent storms) and "is never shaken." Love provides unwavering stability.
    • "The star to every wandering bark": A guiding star for lost ships. Its "worth's unknown" (immeasurable), but its "height be taken" (its position can be used for navigation). Love offers guidance and certainty even when its full value is unfathomable.
  • Love's Triumph Over Time: The third quatrain directly confronts Time's destructive power, so prominent in Sonnet 115:
    • "Love's not Time's fool": Love is not subject to Time's capricious whims.
    • While "rosy lips and cheeks" (physical beauty) fall within "Time's bending sickle's compass," love itself "alters not with his brief hours and weeks."
    • Love "bears it out even to the edge of doom": It endures until the very end of the world, transcending mortality.
  • The Bold Couplet: The sonnet concludes with a daring challenge: "If this be error and upon me prov'd, / I never writ, nor no man ever lov'd." This rhetorical gamble stakes the entirety of the speaker's literary work and human experience on the truth of his definition, powerfully reinforcing the absolute nature of his claims.
  • Context: Platonic Love and Practicality: While referencing the Platonic tradition of love as a union of souls ("true minds"), Shakespeare grounds this ideal in practical, measurable terms (navigational landmarks, stars whose height can be taken). This makes love a real, usable, and reliable force, not just a mystical aspiration.
  • Connections: Sonnet 116 is considered a philosophical summit of the sequence. Its ideals are immediately complicated by the following sonnets, like 117 (speaker's inconsistency). It also contrasts with other profound statements on love and time, such as Sonnet 18 (love preserved in verse), 55 (love outlasting monuments), 65 (love's fragility), and 129 (love as destructive compulsion), the latter offering a dark counterpoint. The legal metaphor of "impediments" connects to the familiar Elizabethan marriage service.

Interplay Between Sonnets 115 and 116

  • Together, these sonnets create a "productive tension." Sonnet 115 presents love as growing and evolving, subject to the changing forces of time. Sonnet 116 presents true love as utterly constant and immune to time's alterations. This dynamic invites a deeper understanding of love as both an experiential journey and an enduring ideal, prompting the reader to reconcile how love can both grow (115) and be unaltering (116). One could interpret 115 as describing the experience of love over time, and 116 as defining the nature of true, ideal love itself.

Review Questions

  1. How does Sonnet 115 challenge the idea of a fixed or static love, and what role does "Time's tyranny" play in this re-evaluation?
  2. In Sonnet 116, Shakespeare uses navigational metaphors ("ever-fixed mark," "star to every wandering bark") to define love. How do these images contribute to the sonnet's argument about love's nature?
  3. The couplet of Sonnet 115 states, "Love is a babe, then might I not say so, / To give full growth to that which still doth grow?" Explain how this metaphor encapsulates the sonnet's central idea.
  4. Sonnet 116 concludes with the bold wager: "If this be error and upon me prov'd, / I never writ, nor no man ever lov'd." What is the rhetorical effect of this challenge, and does it strengthen the poem's argument or reveal a potential vulnerability?
  5. Consider the "productive tension" between Sonnets 115 (love grows) and 116 (love does not alter). How can these two seemingly contradictory ideas about love be reconciled, or what does their juxtaposition suggest about Shakespeare's complex understanding of love?
