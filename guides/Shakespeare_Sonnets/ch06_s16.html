Study Guide: Sonnet LVIII

Overview

Sonnet LVIII is the closing poem of Chapter 6 and the darkest in the separation sequence. It deepens Sonnet LVII's slave metaphor into feudal bondage ("vassal"), acknowledges that the beloved's freedom may amount to "self-doing crime," and ends with the speaker accepting that waiting is hell — but that he will not blame the beloved no matter what pleasure he takes.

Meaning and Argument

The opening invokes the god who ordained the speaker's slavery from the beginning — the condition is not chosen but fated. The speaker would not presume even in thought to control the beloved's pleasures. He must instead "suffer" the "imprison'd absence of the beloved's liberty" — a striking paradox in which the beloved's freedom is itself the speaker's prison. Lines 11–14 grant the beloved an absolute "charter": go where you please, privilege your time as you will, even pardon yourself for whatever crimes you commit in your freedom. The couplet strips away all consolation: waiting is hell, the beloved's pleasure may be "ill" — and still the speaker will not blame.

Key Literary Devices

  • Extended master/slave metaphor: "slave," "vassal," "servant," "beck" — feudal and chattel slavery combined.
  • Paradox: "imprison'd absence of your liberty" — freedom causes bondage.
  • Legal metaphor: "charter," "privilege," "pardon," "crime" — the beloved's rights as an absolute legal franchise.
  • Brutal couplet: "waiting so be hell" — the poem's most honest acknowledgment of the cost of submission.

Themes

The chapter closes on the themes of absolute submission and the limits of love's selflessness. The speaker has moved from consoling dreams and philosophical arguments to a stripped-down acceptance of helplessness. Whether this submission is devotion, co-dependence, or the degradation of the self is left for the reader to decide.
