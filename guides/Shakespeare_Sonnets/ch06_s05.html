Study Guide: Sonnet XLVII

Overview

Sonnet XLVII resolves the war between the speaker's eye and heart declared in Sonnet XLVI. Now the two faculties have struck a "league" — a peace treaty — through which they cooperate to sustain the speaker's love during the beloved's absence. The key tools of this alliance are a portrait of the beloved and the power of loving thought.

Meaning and Argument

The league works in two directions. When the eye hungers for the beloved, it feasts on his portrait and invites the heart to share the "painted banquet." When the heart is overwhelmed with loving thoughts, the eye becomes the heart's guest and participates in those thoughts. Through these mutual exchanges, the beloved is "present still" — he can never travel farther than the speaker's thoughts reach. The couplet adds a final assurance: even during sleep, the eye's sight of the portrait wakes the heart, ensuring no moment is truly empty of the beloved's presence.

Key Literary Devices

  • Food/feast metaphor: "famish'd for a look," "painted banquet" — visual longing is treated as hunger, the portrait as nourishment.
  • Legal/diplomatic language: "league" continues from the legal conceit of XLVI, but now describes alliance rather than conflict.
  • Paradox: "thyself away, art present still with me" — absence is denied through imaginative presence.
  • Closure: The couplet extends the comfort to sleep itself, suggesting the consolation is truly complete.

Themes

The poem explores the power of art and imagination to bridge absence. The portrait is a technology of love — it preserves the beloved's image for the eye. Thought is an interior technology — it preserves him for the heart. Together, they make separation bearable. The sonnet is among the most quietly optimistic in the separation sequence.
