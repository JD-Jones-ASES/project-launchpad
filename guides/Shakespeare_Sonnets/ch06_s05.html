Study Guide: Sonnet 58 – Love from Afar

Overview

Sonnet 58 continues and intensifies the themes of absolute devotion and profound submission introduced in Sonnet 57. The speaker, likening himself to a "vassal" or "slave," grants the beloved complete freedom and autonomy over their actions and time, even to the point of self-pardon for any misdeeds. Despite the deep suffering this waiting and power imbalance causes, the speaker vows unwavering patience and a complete absence of blame, solidifying a bleak and unconditional surrender.

Key Concepts

  • Unconditional Submission and Vassalage: The speaker describes himself as a "slave" and "vassal" bound by a divine decree, signifying a complete and irreversible subjugation to the beloved's will. This isn't mere service, but a formal, almost legal, bond of subservience.
  • Absolute Freedom and "Charter" of the Beloved: The beloved is portrayed as possessing a "charter"—a royal grant of absolute rights and privileges—allowing them to control their own time and actions without accountability. This extends to the shocking right to "pardon of self-doing crime," illustrating unchecked power.
  • The Speaker's "Imprison'd Absence": A central paradox where the beloved's liberty directly results in the speaker's confinement. The speaker is trapped by the beloved's freedom, enduring an enforced, suffering patience.
  • "Waiting as Hell": The couplet reveals the depth of the speaker's torment, explicitly stating that his imposed waiting is a form of "hell." Despite this suffering, he commits to enduring it without complaint or blame.
  • Refusal to Blame, Even for Misdeeds ("Ill or Well"): The speaker's vow not to blame the beloved "be it ill or well" signifies a terrifying level of surrender. It means the speaker must accept even the beloved's potentially harmful or immoral actions without judgment, stripping away any last vestige of personal agency or moral expectation.
  • Legal and Feudal Language: The sonnet is rich with terms like "slave," "vassal," "charter," and "privilege," drawing on Elizabethan legal and feudal systems to formalize and intensify the power dynamics between the speaker and the beloved.

Important Terms

  • Vassal: A person bound in feudal service to a lord; stronger than "servant," suggesting a formal legal bond of subjugation.
  • Beck: A nod or gesture of command; to be "at someone's beck" means to be ready to respond to the slightest signal.
  • Imprison'd absence of your liberty: A striking paradox where the speaker is confined or restricted (imprisoned) precisely because the beloved is free (absence of the beloved due to their liberty creates the speaker's confinement).
  • Patience, tame to sufferance: Patience that has been subdued or broken, becoming a passive, unresisting endurance of suffering. "Tame" suggests its natural resistance has been crushed.
  • Bide each check: To endure or withstand every reproof, setback, or rebuff without complaint or resistance.
  • Charter: A royal or legal grant of rights and privileges; here, it refers to the beloved's absolute, almost kingly, right to do as they please.
  • Self-doing crime: A wrong committed against oneself by oneself; the beloved is given the paradoxical right to pardon themselves for their own misdeeds, highlighting their extreme power and lack of accountability.
  • Diptych: A work of art or literature presented in two panels or parts; used here to describe Sonnets 57 and 58 as a pair.
  • Self-abnegation: The denial of one's own interests in favor of the interests of others; self-sacrifice.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Sonnet 58

Sonnet 58 serves as a direct companion to Sonnet 57, further exploring and deepening the theme of the speaker's abject submission to the beloved. While Sonnet 57 depicted the speaker as a patient, foolish servant, Sonnet 58 escalates the emotional stakes, revealing the true torment underlying this devotion. It uses language of divine decree and legal authority to formalize the beloved's absolute power and the speaker's unconditional surrender.

Quatrain 1 (Lines 1-4)

That god forbid, that made me first your slave,
I should in thought control your times of pleasure,
Or at your hand the account of hours to crave,
Being your vassal, bound to stay your leisure!

The sonnet opens with a powerful declaration, invoking "that god" who, the speaker claims, made him the beloved's "slave." This sets a tone of divinely ordained, inescapable servitude. The speaker immediately rejects any notion of controlling the beloved's "times of pleasure" or demanding an "account of hours." This reinforces the beloved's absolute freedom from scrutiny. The term "vassal" further solidifies the feudal relationship, emphasizing a formal, binding obligation to serve and "stay your leisure"—to wait patiently for the beloved to be ready. The speaker's submission is presented as so fundamental that even thinking of interfering is forbidden.

Quatrain 2 (Lines 5-8)

O! let me suffer, being at your beck,
The imprison’d absence of your liberty;
And patience, tame to sufferance, bide each check,
Without accusing you of injury.

Here, the speaker explicitly accepts suffering as his lot. To be "at your beck" means to be constantly ready to respond to the beloved's slightest command. The powerful phrase "The imprison'd absence of your liberty" is a central paradox: the speaker is trapped and confined precisely because the beloved is free to be absent or disregard him. The beloved's freedom is the direct cause of the speaker's suffering and lack of freedom. His "patience" is no longer a virtue but something "tame to sufferance"—broken and subdued into mere endurance. He vows to "bide each check" (endure every rebuff or setback) without ever accusing the beloved of causing him "injury." This demonstrates an extreme suppression of personal feelings and a complete denial of self-worth.

Quatrain 3 (Lines 9-12)

Be where you list, your charter is so strong
That you yourself may privilage your time
To what you will; to you it doth belong
Yourself to pardon of self-doing crime.

This stanza further elaborates on the beloved's immense power, likening it to a "charter." In Elizabethan England, royal charters granted absolute rights and privileges. Here, the beloved possesses a "charter" that is "so strong" that it grants them unconditional freedom to "privilege your time / To what you will." They are accountable to no one. The most shocking assertion is the beloved's right to "Yourself to pardon of self-doing crime." This means the beloved has the authority to forgive themselves for any wrongs they commit, even against themselves, effectively placing them beyond any moral or legal judgment. This highlights the absolute nature of the beloved's power and the speaker's utter powerlessness.

Couplet (Lines 13-14)

I am to wait, though waiting so be hell,
Not blame your pleasure be it ill or well.

The concluding couplet delivers a devastating blow, stripping away any last shred of consolation. The speaker explicitly admits that his fate—to wait for the beloved—is an experience of "hell." Yet, even in this profound suffering, he refuses to "blame your pleasure." The phrase "be it ill or well" is crucial; it's the "turn of the knife," as the "mainidea" section notes. It signifies that the speaker is not even allowed to wish for the beloved to behave well or morally. He must accept their actions unconditionally, whether they are good or bad, without complaint or accusation. This final line seals the speaker's fate in a state of torment and complete, unquestioning submission.

Themes and Imagery

The sonnet is rich in feudal and legal imagery, with words like "slave," "vassal," "charter," and "privilege" establishing a formal power hierarchy. The paradox of freedom and imprisonment ("imprison'd absence of your liberty") is a key intellectual and emotional conceit. The sonnet explores the psychological depths of suffering and self-abnegation, culminating in the stark admission that "waiting so be hell." The unconditional nature of love/submission is taken to an extreme, where even moral judgment is surrendered.

Connections to Other Sonnets

Sonnet 58 is best understood as a "diptych" with Sonnet 57, forming a pair that explores the darker aspects of subservience. Where Sonnet 57 ends with the speaker playfully admitting to being a "fool," Sonnet 58 escalates the emotional impact by concluding with "waiting so be hell," showing that the submission is far from peaceful. The legal language of "charter" and "privilege" connects to Elizabethan legal culture, highlighting an ironic grant of power for causing pain rather than economic gain. This sonnet's themes of self-condemnation and tortured self-abnegation resonate with other sonnets in the sequence, such as 49 (where the speaker pre-emptively argues for his own abandonment) and the more intensely self-sacrificing 88 and 89.

Review Questions

  1. How does the speaker's use of terms like "slave" and "vassal" in Sonnet 58 intensify the posture of submission compared to Sonnet 57?
  2. Analyze the paradox "The imprison'd absence of your liberty." What does this phrase reveal about the speaker's emotional state and his relationship with the beloved?
  3. Discuss the significance of the beloved's "charter" and their right to "pardon of self-doing crime." What does this convey about the extent of the beloved's power and accountability?
  4. Examine the impact of the final couplet, particularly the phrase "Not blame your pleasure be it ill or well." How does this line deepen our understanding of the speaker's suffering and the nature of his submission?
  5. In what ways does Sonnet 58 draw upon Elizabethan legal and feudal culture to explore the themes of power and subjugation? Provide specific examples from the text.
