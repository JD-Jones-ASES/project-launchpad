Sonnet CVIII — Study Guide

Overview

Sonnet CVIII asks whether there is anything new left to say in a long love. The speaker's answer is elegant: novelty is unnecessary because love, like daily prayer, renews itself through repetition. The poem is a meditation on how faithful repetition differs from stale redundancy.

Meaning

Having written so much, the speaker asks whether any new expression of his "true spirit" remains. His answer is "Nothing, sweet boy" — yet he must keep saying the same things daily, as one repeats prayers. Counting nothing old, treating the beloved's name as hallowed since the first encounter, eternal love clothes itself in a fresh present-tense case and ignores the "dust and injury of age." The closing paradox is powerful: love finds its original freshness precisely where time and outward signs would say it should be dead.

Literary Devices

  • Rhetorical question: The opening invites the reader to see the problem before the speaker resolves it.
  • Analogy: Divine prayer provides the model for constant, repeated love that never loses its meaning.
  • Paradox: Love's vitality is found where time indicates it should be extinct.
  • Religious diction: "Hallow'd," "prayers divine," and "eternal" give the love a devotional register.

Themes of Constancy and Faithfulness

This sonnet is perhaps the clearest statement that faithfulness is not about variety but about depth. Constancy means returning daily to the same devotion and finding it perpetually fresh — as inexhaustible as the love it expresses. The prayer analogy frames this as both natural and sacred.
