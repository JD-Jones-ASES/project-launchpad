Study Guide: Sonnet XLI

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet XLI continues the triangular crisis by focusing on the mechanics of how the betrayal happened rather than the speaker's moral response to it. The opening phrase — "pretty wrongs that liberty commits" — signals a strategy of minimization that the sonnet will gradually dismantle. The beloved's straying is first called "pretty" (trivial) and ascribed to the natural consequences of freedom, beauty, and youth.

The central argument of lines 7-10 is a cultural excuse: a gentle, beautiful person will inevitably be won when pursued; and what man could refuse a woman who persistently seeks him? This quasi-apology acknowledges human weakness as near-inevitable given the beloved's specific combination of qualities.

But the sestet introduces the word "yet" — the beloved might have restrained himself ("Ay me! but yet thou mightst my seat forbear"). The closing couplet then withdraws all the excuse-making and assigns precise double blame: the beloved's beauty tempted her to him (betraying her) and made him false to the speaker (betraying him). One act, two broken truths.

Key Literary Devices

  • Escalation: "Pretty wrongs" at the start versus "break a twofold truth" at the end — the sonnet enacts the dismantling of its own initial minimization.
  • Rhetorical question: "What woman's son / Will sourly leave her?" — a cultural appeal that simultaneously excuses and, in context, exposes the inadequacy of the excuse.
  • Chiasmus: The couplet's parallel structure ("Hers by thy beauty... / Thine by thy beauty...") assigns blame symmetrically across both betrayals.

Themes

Beauty as ethical agent: The beloved's beauty is the grammatical and causal subject of the couplet — it acts, tempts, and betrays. The poem implicates beauty itself as a dangerous force.

Excuse versus accountability: The sonnet enacts the journey from excuse to acknowledgment, showing how much rhetorical work goes into minimizing a serious wrong before the full weight of it is admitted.

Double betrayal: One action can break faith in two directions simultaneously — a structural insight about how triangular relationships multiply harm.
