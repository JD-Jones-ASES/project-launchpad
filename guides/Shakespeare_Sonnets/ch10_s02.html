Chapter 10, Section 2: Sonnets 100-104 - Constancy and the Muse

Overview

This section presents a cluster of five sonnets (100-104) that explore the speaker's relationship with his poetic inspiration (the Muse), the passage of time, and the beloved's enduring beauty. The sonnets oscillate between reproaching the Muse for her silence and offering various justifications for it, ultimately affirming the beloved's transcending beauty and the immortalizing power of verse.

Key Concepts

  • The Poet's Muse: The divine source of poetic inspiration, often personified as a fickle or delinquent figure who must be summoned, admonished, and guided by the speaker. Her actions, or lack thereof, reflect the poet's own creative struggles and intentions.
  • Time's Destructive Power: Time is depicted as an unrelenting enemy, personified with a "scythe and crooked knife," constantly threatening to diminish beauty and erase memory. The poems express an urgent desire to "prevent" its spoils.
  • Poetry's Immortalizing Power: A central theme is the belief that verse can defy time, preserving the beloved's beauty and fame beyond physical decay and outliving "gilded tombs." Poetry's purpose is seen not as mere flattery but as eternal preservation.
  • The Nature of Beauty and Truth: The beloved is presented as an embodiment of truth and beauty, so perfect that the speaker sometimes questions if poetry can add anything, or if it might even diminish his natural grace.
  • The Paradox of Poetic Silence: The speaker offers various reasons for his periods of silence—ranging from the Muse's neglect to the beloved being "beyond praise," and even to the idea that constant praise cheapens love, making silence a more profound compliment.
  • The Beloved's Unchanging Appearance (and the fear of deception): Despite the clear passage of time, the beloved appears to the speaker to remain constant, yet the speaker acknowledges the subtle, imperceptible decay of beauty, leading to a fear that his own eyes might be "deceiv'd."

Important Terms

  • Fury: Poetic frenzy; divine inspiration of the Muses in classical tradition.
  • Lays: Songs; lyric poems; short musical compositions.
  • Argument: The subject or theme of a poem; its rhetorical purpose.
  • Resty: Sluggish; lazy; reluctant to move or act.
  • Prevent'st: Outrun; forestall; arrive before, thereby stopping or hindering.
  • Truant: One who neglects duty; an absentee.
  • Truth in beauty dy'd: Truth dyed (colored) in beauty; truth embodied in a beautiful form.
  • Gilded tomb: An ornate, gold-covered monument, implying elaborate but ultimately perishable memorials.
  • Haply: Perhaps; by chance.
  • Merchandiz'd: Turned into merchandise; made commercial, implying cheapening or trivializing.
  • Philomel: The nightingale, from the mythological princess Philomela, often associated with mournful or beautiful song.
  • Burthens: Burdens; also, a musical term for the bass accompaniment or refrain of a song.
  • Poverty: Insufficiency; lack; poor quality (of verse or artistic output).
  • Scope: Range; extent; opportunity.
  • Over-goes: Surpasses; exceeds; goes beyond.
  • Blunt: Dull; not sharp; lacking refinement.
  • Pass: Purpose; aim; end.
  • Process: A course of events; a legal writ.
  • Steal: To take secretly; to pass imperceptibly.
  • Hue: Color; appearance; complexion.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet 100: "Where art thou Muse that thou forget'st so long"

  • Speaker's Reproach: The sonnet begins with a direct, urgent question to the Muse, scolding her for her prolonged absence and for wasting her "fury" (poetic inspiration) on "worthless" subjects instead of the beloved, who is her true source of power.
  • Call to Action: The speaker commands the "forgetful Muse" to return, redeem lost time, and "sing to the ear that doth thy lays esteem." The beloved is presented as the rightful "argument" (subject) for her pen.
  • Urgency of Time: The speaker emphasizes the passage of time, fearing that the beloved's face may already show signs of age. He wants the Muse to "survey" the beloved's face and, if wrinkles are present, to make them a "satire to decay."
  • Poetry as a Weapon against Time: The couplet sharpens the urgency into a military image: the Muse must "Give my love fame faster than Time wastes life," thereby "preventing his scythe and crooked knife" and immortalizing the beloved's beauty.
  • Context and Connections: This sonnet functions as both a resumption of the sequence after a silence and a rededication of the verse to its primary subject. It introduces the theme of the Muse's responsibility and connects to other sonnets that personify Time (60, 73) and emphasize the urgency of poetic immortality (107, 116).

Sonnet 101: "O truant Muse what shall be thy amends"

  • Muse's Proposed Excuse: The speaker imagines the "truant Muse" offering an excuse for her neglect: the beloved's beauty and truth are so perfectly "fix'd" that they need no poetic "colour" or "pencil" (paint) to display them. His beauty is inherently "best" and should not be "intermix'd" with poetic artifice.
  • Speaker's Rejection of the Excuse: The speaker rejects this, asserting that while the beloved may "need no praise" for his inherent perfection, the Muse still has a duty to him. Her purpose is not to decorate but to preserve.
  • Poetry's True Purpose: The verse's function is to make the beloved "outlive a gilded tomb" and to be "prais'd of ages yet to be." The speaker concludes by instructing the Muse on her "office": "To make him seem long hence as he shows now," emphasizing poetry's role in time's defiance.
  • Connections: This sonnet continues the dialogue with the Muse and elaborates on the idea of the beloved's beauty being beyond description (echoing 103). It also reinforces the idea of poetry's superiority to physical monuments (55).

Sonnet 102: "My love is strengthen'd, though more weak in seeming"

  • A New Explanation for Silence: The speaker offers a more nuanced, gentler reason for his poetic silence: excessive, constant praise can cheapen love, making it "merchandiz'd." True love, though less publicly displayed, remains strong.
  • The Nightingale Analogy (Philomel): He compares himself to the nightingale (Philomel), who sings profusely in spring but falls silent as summer matures. This is not because she loves summer less, but because "wild music burthens every bough," and "sweets grown common lose their dear delight."
  • Silence as a Compliment: Therefore, the speaker holds his tongue "Because I would not dull you with my song." His silence is a deliberate act to preserve the special, rare quality of his praise, making it more valued when it does occur.
  • Connections: This sonnet introduces a unique justification for poetic silence, contrasting with the earlier reproaches. It connects to a rich literary tradition surrounding the nightingale and resonates with other sonnets (23) that suggest silence can be more eloquent than speech, and that familiarity can diminish appreciation (118).

Sonnet 103: "Alack! what poverty my Muse brings forth"

  • Admission of Poetic Inadequacy: The speaker laments the "poverty" of his Muse's output, declaring that the beloved's inherent worth ("the argument, all bare") is "of more worth / Than when it hath my added praise beside." He feels his verse cannot do justice to the beloved.
  • Beloved Transcends Art: The beloved's face, when seen in a "glass" (mirror), "over-goes my blunt invention quite," meaning his actual appearance surpasses the poet's creative ability, "Dulling my lines, and doing me disgrace."
  • The Risk of "Marring": The speaker questions if it would be "sinful" to try and "mend" (improve) something already perfect, suggesting that to do so might "mar the subject that before was well." His verses have no other "pass" (purpose) than to describe the beloved's graces, but even that is insufficient.
  • Mirror as Ultimate Truth: The couplet reinforces this idea: "And more, much more, than in my verse can sit, / Your own glass shows you when you look in it." The beloved's own reflection provides a truer, more complete image than any poem can.
  • Connections: This sonnet prominently features the "mirror conceit" (3, 77) and reiterates the theme that the beloved's beauty transcends artistic expression (17, 106), making the poet's efforts seem futile, yet paradoxically, it remains a profound compliment.

Sonnet 104: "To me, fair friend, you never can be old"

  • Declaration of Constancy: The sonnet opens with a strong declaration that the beloved "never can be old," asserting that his beauty appears the same as "when first your eye I ey'd" three years prior. The speaker lists the cycle of seasons (three winters, summers, springs, Junes) to emphasize the elapsed time.
  • Acknowledged Decay, Feared Deception: Despite this declaration, the speaker immediately introduces doubt. He observes that beauty "like a dial-hand, / Steals from his figure, and no pace perceiv'd"—beauty imperceptibly decays. He admits that his own eyes "may be deceiv'd," suggesting a fear that he is blinded by love to the beloved's true aging.
  • Bold Claim of Eternal Beauty: The sonnet concludes with a profound and somewhat audacious claim addressed to "age unbred" (future generations): "Ere you were born was beauty's summer dead." This suggests that the beloved embodies such ultimate beauty that he effectively predates and defines the very concept of beauty, making him eternally youthful and a standard against which all other beauty is measured.

Review Questions

  1. How does the speaker's relationship with his Muse evolve across Sonnets 100 and 101, and what does this reveal about his view of poetry's purpose?
  2. Discuss the paradox of poetic silence as presented in Sonnets 102 and 103. In what ways does the speaker argue that not writing can be a greater compliment than writing?
  3. Analyze the various ways Time is personified and battled against in these sonnets. What strategies does the speaker propose for overcoming Time's destructive power?
  4. Explore the tension between the beloved's inherent beauty and the poet's capacity to describe it. How do Sonnets 101 and 103 particularly grapple with the idea that the beloved might be "beyond praise"?
  5. Sonnet 104 begins with a declaration of constancy but ends with an admission of potential deception, followed by a monumental claim. Discuss how this sonnet reflects both the speaker's enduring love and his anxieties about the passage of time and the limits of perception.
