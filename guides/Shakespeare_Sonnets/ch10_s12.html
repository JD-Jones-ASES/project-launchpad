Sonnet CXI — Study Guide

Overview

Sonnet CXI is widely read as autobiographical, with the speaker blaming his public theatrical career for corrupting his personal reputation and manners. He asks the beloved to chide Fortune for the circumstances that degraded him, and declares himself a willing patient ready to drink any bitter medicine — asking only for the beloved's pity as cure.

Meaning

The speaker opens by directing the beloved to blame Fortune, calling her "guilty goddess." Fortune provided him only with a public livelihood, which bred public manners and stained his name and nature — much as a dyer's hand takes on the color of the dye it constantly works in. He accepts the role of a patient willing to drink eisel (vinegar/bitter medicine) to cure his "strong infection," accepting any penance. The couplet is confident: the beloved's pity, by itself, is sufficient to cure all the moral staining.

Literary Devices

  • Personification: Fortune is a guilty goddess who provided poorly for the speaker.
  • Simile: The dyer's hand is one of Shakespeare's most famous images of professional contamination.
  • Medical metaphor: Infection, potions, patient, and cure build a sustained conceit of moral disease and healing.
  • Penitential rhetoric: Echoes of religious confession and penance give the poem a devotional weight.

Themes of Constancy and Faithfulness

The sonnet explores constancy under external pressure. The speaker's failures are attributed partly to circumstance (Fortune's provision of public life), yet he takes penitential responsibility. His faithfulness expresses itself through vulnerability and trust that the beloved's love is powerful enough to heal him.
