Study Guide: Sonnet XVI

Summary

Sonnet XVI is the direct sequel to Sonnet XV. Where Sonnet XV ended with the poet offering to fight Time through verse ("I engraft you new"), Sonnet XVI immediately challenges the youth: "But wherefore do not you a mightier way / Make war upon this bloody tyrant, Time?" The poet calls his own verse "barren rhyme" and argues that the youth has access to a more powerful weapon — biological reproduction. The "maiden gardens" (eligible women) stand ready to bear him "living flowers" (children) that are far more like him than any "painted counterfeit" (portrait). The couplet resolves with a paradox: to give yourself away (in a child) is the only way to keep yourself — living, drawn by your own sweet biological skill.

Key Literary Devices

  • Self-deprecation: "My barren rhyme" — the poet strategically diminishes his own verse to argue for reproduction's superiority.
  • Garden metaphor: "Maiden gardens, yet unset" — eligible women as unplanted gardens, ready for cultivation.
  • Pun: "Lines of life" — both the lines of genealogy (children) and the lines of verse; both can "repair" life, but living lines are more powerful.
  • Paradox in couplet: "To give away yourself keeps yourself still" — reproduction is paradoxically both a giving and a keeping.

Thematic Significance

Sonnet XVI represents the sequence's final argument for biological reproduction over verse. By calling his own poem "barren rhyme," Shakespeare performs a deliberate rhetorical sacrifice: he diminishes his art to make the case for life. The "drawn by your own sweet skill" of the couplet is a beautiful reversal of the portrait metaphor — instead of a painter's skill preserving the youth's face, it is the youth's own reproductive "skill" that draws his most accurate self-portrait in a child. Sonnets XV and XVI together form the hinge of the sequence: verse is introduced as a weapon (XV), then judged inferior to reproduction (XVI), before Sonnet XVIII will famously resolve this tension in verse's favor.
