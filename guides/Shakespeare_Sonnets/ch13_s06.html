Study Guide: Sonnet CXLVII

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet CXLVII constructs one of Shakespeare's most sustained medical metaphors. Love is a fever that craves what worsens it; Reason is the physician who, having had his prescriptions ignored, angrily abandons the patient. Left without rational counsel, the speaker descends into desperate madness: his thoughts are "at random from the truth," his discourse like a lunatic's. The couplet delivers a brutal self-revelation: all his sworn praise of the Dark Lady's fairness and brightness was delusion — she is "as black as hell, as dark as night." The poem is thus a retrospective diagnosis: the speaker has been critically ill for the entire sequence, and only now, in the terminal stage, can he name the disease for what it is.

Key Poetic Devices

  • Extended medical metaphor: fever, physician, prescriptions, cure — love as pathology sustained throughout
  • Personification: Reason as a physician who abandons the incorrigible patient
  • Double simile / anaphora: "as black as hell, as dark as night" — two stacked similes for maximum condemnatory force
  • "Past cure / past care" pun: chiasmus and wordplay emphasizing total abandonment by reason
  • Humoral theory context: "physic," "prescriptions," and "cure" invoke Elizabethan medicine's framework of rebalancing disordered humors

Central Themes

Love as disease and madness: The sonnet extends the Dark Lady sequence's medical imagery to its logical extreme — love is a fatal illness. Moral struggle and self-deception: The speaker has been lying to himself (and the reader) throughout; the couplet's revelation collapses all previous praise. Reason's defeat: The poem dramatizes reason's systematic failure against desire — relevant to both the psychological and the ethical dimensions of sin. Sin and shame: "Black as hell" carries both moral and physical meaning, conflating the lady's dark complexion with her moral darkness.
