Sonnet CXIV — Study Guide

Overview

Sonnet CXIV picks up where CXIII left off, asking why the speaker's eye transforms everything it sees into an image of the beloved. He poses two explanations: either his mind, "crown'd" with love, is drinking the monarch's plague of flattery; or his love has genuinely taught his eye an alchemy that transmutes base things into beauty. He resolves the debate with rueful self-awareness: it is flattery.

Meaning

The poem opens with an either/or question: is the speaker's distorted perception a symptom of the self-deception that afflicts kings surrounded by flatterers — or a genuine transformation taught by love? He imagines the alchemy case: love might truly have taught the eye to remake monsters and unformed things into cherubim resembling the beloved. But then he admits: it is the first option. His great mind most kingly drinks up flattery. The couplet accepts this with a kind of wry resignation: even if the perception is poisoned, the eye that loves the cup and drinks first commits only a lesser sin.

Literary Devices

  • Conceit: The eye as a court taster who prepares a flattering cup for a king.
  • Metaphor: Alchemy represents love's power to transmute perception; the monarch's plague represents self-serving delusion.
  • Irony: "My great mind most kingly" — celebrating a form of self-deception with mock-regal language.
  • Dilemma structure: The poem is built as a formal either/or argument before the speaker picks his answer.

Themes of Constancy and Faithfulness

The sonnet introduces an epistemological complication into the chapter's praise of constancy: faithfulness can produce a flattering, unreliable perception. The speaker acknowledges this honestly but accepts the lesser sin gladly — suggesting that love's distortions may be more humanly true than cold objectivity.
