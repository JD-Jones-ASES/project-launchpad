Study Guide: Sonnets 78-82 from The Rival Poet Sequence

Overview

This section introduces and develops the "Rival Poet" theme within Shakespeare's Sonnets, specifically focusing on Sonnets 78 through 81. The speaker grapples with the unsettling presence of other poets who are also drawing inspiration from the young man's beauty and influence. Across these sonnets, the speaker navigates feelings of inadequacy, defends the unique depth of his own connection to the youth, and ultimately reasserts the immortalizing power of his verse.

Key Concepts

  • The Rival Poet: The emergence of other writers ("alien pens") who also look to the young man as a Muse, challenging the speaker's previously exclusive poetic relationship.
  • The Patron-Poet Relationship: Reflects the Elizabethan social reality where poets sought patronage from wealthy individuals. The youth's approval is both practical and emotionally significant, leading to competition.
  • The Nature of Poetic Inspiration: The speaker initially feels that the youth's influence elevates even his rivals, but then argues that their praise merely reflects what already exists in the youth. For the speaker, the youth is the substance of his art, not just an embellishment.
  • Self-Deprecation as a Tactic: The speaker often describes his own verse as "rude ignorance" or a "saucy bark" (small boat), seemingly diminishing his own skill while simultaneously delivering superb poetry that undermines his rivals' claims.
  • The Immortalizing Power of Verse: Despite the rivalry, the speaker consistently asserts that his poetry is the ultimate vehicle for preserving the young man's memory, transcending time and even death.
  • Poetic Paradox: The speaker often uses clever paradoxes, such as the rival "robbing" the youth to "pay" him back with praise, to highlight the superficiality of the rivals' contributions compared to his own deep connection.

Important Terms

  • Invoked: Called upon; summoned. In poetry, to call upon the Muse for divine inspiration.
  • Alien pen: A foreign or rival writer's instrument, symbolizing an intruder or competitor in the speaker's poetic relationship with the youth.
  • Disperse: To scatter or distribute widely; in this context, rival poets spreading their work.
  • Compile: To compose; to gather and put together in writing, suggesting a more humble, crafted act than divine inspiration.
  • Mend the style: To improve the manner or surface of writing, implying a superficial enhancement rather than fundamental creation.
  • Rude ignorance: The speaker's self-deprecating description of his own lack of formal learning or poetic skill, often used ironically.
  • Gracious numbers: Pleasing verses; "numbers" is a Renaissance term for metrical poetry.
  • Sick Muse: The speaker's weakened or ailing poetic inspiration, metaphorically describing his creative vitality being drained by the rival's presence.
  • Lovely argument: A beautiful subject or theme for a poem; "argument" in Elizabethan usage refers to the subject matter.
  • Travail: Labor; toil; great effort, acknowledging the rival's diligent work.
  • Invent: To discover or find; to devise. In Renaissance rhetoric, inventio was the art of finding ideas and arguments.
  • He robs thee of, and pays it thee again: A paradox describing how the rival's praise is merely a reflection of the youth's inherent qualities, taken from him and returned.
  • Faint: To grow weak; to lose courage; to feel dizzy with awe, describing the speaker's intimidation and creative paralysis.
  • Better spirit: A greater poetic genius; "spirit" could refer to a person of exceptional creative power.
  • Saucy bark: An impertinent or bold small boat; a self-deprecating but defiant image the speaker uses for himself.
  • Broad main: The open ocean; the wide expanse of deep water, representing the youth's vast worth.
  • Soundless deep: Water too deep to be measured, symbolizing the profound depths of the youth's worth explored by the rival.
  • Tall building: A ship of imposing construction and height, representing the rival's superior poetic skill.
  • Cast away: Shipwrecked; destroyed; discarded, with a deliberate pun on poetic ruin or emotional rejection.
  • Epitaph: An inscription on a tombstone; a memorial tribute.
  • Rehearse: To speak of; to recite; to repeat.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet LXXVIII (78): The Alien Pen

This sonnet opens the "Rival Poet" sequence by directly addressing the young man, acknowledging a significant change in their relationship. The speaker observes that he is no longer the sole recipient of the youth's creative influence.

  • The Youth as Universal Muse: The speaker notes that he "invoked thee for my Muse," but now "every alien pen" has also gained inspiration, dispersing their "poesy" (poetry) under the youth's influence. This suggests the youth's beauty and grace are so profound they can inspire anyone.
  • Elevating the Unlearned: The youth's influence is so potent that it has "added feathers to the learned's wing" and even enabled the "dumb on high to sing" and "heavy ignorance aloft to fly." This is a grudging admission that the rival poets, even if less skilled, are genuinely elevated by the youth.
  • Speaker's Unique Connection: In the third quatrain, the speaker offers his consolation: while others might "mend the style" and add "sweet graces" to their existing "arts" by borrowing from the youth, the speaker's relationship is more fundamental.
  • Youth as Substance, Not Ornament: The speaker declares, "But thou art all my art, and dost advance / As high as learning, my rude ignorance." For the speaker, the youth is not an embellishment but the very source and essence of his poetic creation. The youth transforms his "rude ignorance" into something profound, whereas for others, the youth merely refines what they already possess.

Sonnet LXXIX (79): The Borrowed Praise

Building on the previous sonnet, the speaker fully concedes that a rival poet is at work, but cleverly subverts the rival's apparent advantage by critiquing the nature of his praise.

  • Loss of Exclusive Grace: The speaker laments, "Whilst I alone did call upon thy aid, / My verse alone had all thy gentle grace; / But now my gracious numbers are decay’d, / And my sick Muse doth give an other place." His creative inspiration is diminished by the rival's presence.
  • Concession and Counter-Argument: He grants that the youth's "lovely argument" (beautiful subject) "Deserves the travail of a worthier pen." However, this apparent humility immediately pivots into a pointed counter-argument: the rival cannot truly give the youth anything new.
  • The Paradox of Borrowed Virtue: The rival poet, for all his skill, can only "invent" (discover) what "he robs thee of, and pays it thee again." Every virtue and beauty the rival praises—like "virtue" from the youth's "behaviour" or "beauty" from his "cheek"—originates from the youth himself.
  • No Gratitude Owed: The couplet, "Then thank him not for that which he doth say, / Since what he owes thee, thou thyself dost pay," delivers the final blow. The youth owes no gratitude to the rival, as his own innate excellence is the true source of all the compliments. This sonnet makes a philosophical point about the limits of poetic praise and the self-sufficiency of the youth's excellence.

Sonnet LXXX (80): The Saucy Bark

This sonnet is a masterpiece of self-deprecation and defiant wit, using an extended sea voyage metaphor to illustrate the rivalry.

  • Paralysis by Superiority: The speaker confesses to feeling weak and "tongue-tied" ("I faint when I of you do write, / Knowing a better spirit doth use your name"). He acknowledges the rival's superior talent ("better spirit") directly.
  • The Sea Metaphor: The youth's "worth, wide as the ocean is," becomes the vast sea. The rival poet is a "tall building" of a ship, built for the "soundless deep," representing profound skill and ability to explore the youth's deepest qualities.
  • The "Saucy Bark": In contrast, the speaker describes himself as a "saucy bark," an "impertinent or bold small boat." He acknowledges his inferiority ("inferior far to his") but defiantly chooses to sail on the same "broad main" (open ocean) anyway.
  • Survival in the Shallows: The speaker admits he only needs the youth's "shallowest help" to stay "afloat," while the rival navigates the depths. This implies a more surface-level understanding or a less ambitious poetic project, yet it is still sustained by the youth.
  • Noble Ruin: The final couplet poses a potential tragic outcome: "Or, being wrack’d, I am a worthless boat, / He of tall building, and of goodly pride: / Then if he thrive and I be cast away, / The worst was this: my love was my decay." If his poetic endeavor fails (he is "cast away"), the cause will be his profound love, a fate that is presented as almost noble rather than shameful. The sonnet's brilliance lies in its ability to confess paralysis while actively demonstrating poetic mastery.

Sonnet LXXXI (81): The Immortal Epitaph

Stepping back from the direct rivalry, this sonnet reasserts the speaker's primary claim: the immortalizing power of his own poetry for the youth.

  • The Poet's Mortality, the Youth's Immortality: The speaker contrasts his own inevitable death and forgotten grave ("I in earth am rotten," "each part will be forgotten," "common grave") with the lasting memory he promises the youth. His verse will ensure that the youth's "memory death cannot take" and his "name from hence immortal life shall have."
  • Verse as Monument: The speaker explicitly states, "Your monument shall be my gentle verse." This poem itself, and others like it, will serve as a lasting memorial, more enduring than any physical structure.
  • Future Generations as Audience: This "gentle verse" will be read by "eyes not yet created" and its content "rehearse[d]" by "tongues to be, when all the breathers of this world are dead." This emphasizes the enduring nature of his art across centuries.
  • Living in the Mouths of Men: The couplet powerfully concludes, "You still shall live, such virtue hath my pen, / Where breath most breathes, even in the mouths of men." The youth will achieve immortality not in stone or legend, but in the living words spoken and read by people through time, all thanks to the speaker's poetic "virtue." This reasserts the speaker's unique and fundamental role in preserving the youth's legacy, overshadowing any rival's momentary praise.

Review Questions

  1. How does the speaker's initial acknowledgment of the "alien pen" in Sonnet 78 evolve into the more direct criticism of the rival in Sonnet 79? What specific rhetorical strategies does he use in each?
  2. Analyze the central metaphor of the sea voyage in Sonnet 80. How does the speaker use the images of the "saucy bark" and the "tall building" to simultaneously humble himself and subtly assert his own defiance and importance?
  3. In Sonnet 79, the speaker argues that the rival "robs thee of, and pays it thee again." Explain this paradox and discuss how it undermines the rival's praise while elevating the youth's inherent worth.
  4. Compare and contrast the speaker's self-perception in Sonnet 78 (where he claims "rude ignorance") and Sonnet 80 (where he is a "saucy bark"). Do these self-deprecating descriptions reflect genuine humility or are they a form of sophisticated poetic strategy? Justify your answer.
  5. Sonnet 81 shifts focus from the rivalry to the theme of immortality through verse. How does this sonnet serve as a powerful conclusion to this segment of the "Rival Poet" sequence, reinforcing the speaker's unique claim on the youth's legacy despite the presence of competitors?
