Study Guide: Sonnet LXXIX

Overview

Sonnet LXXIX deepens the rivalry by confronting directly what the rival poet offers the Fair Youth. The speaker opens with a nostalgic contrast between the time when he alone invoked the youth and the present moment when his "gracious numbers are decay'd" and his "sick Muse" has yielded place to another. Yet the sonnet's real work is not lamentation but logical deconstruction: the speaker systematically argues that the rival's praise adds nothing to the youth that was not already the youth's own.

Meaning and Argument

The octave concedes that the youth's "lovely argument" deserves a worthier pen, which seems like generous self-deprecation. But the sestet converts this concession into an attack: whatever the rival praises — virtue, beauty, worth — he has stolen from the youth's own behavior and appearance, and is only returning it. The rival "lends" virtue that he "stole" from the youth's conduct; he "gives" beauty that he "found" in the youth's cheek. He invents nothing. The couplet delivers the logical conclusion: the youth should not thank the rival for his praise, since the youth himself has paid for every word of it.

Poetic Devices

The key device is the chiasmus of theft and repayment: "He robs thee of, and pays it thee again." This figure captures the circular futility of the rival's praise — it goes nowhere because it only returns what was always already the youth's. The language of financial transaction (lends, stole, owes, pays) frames praise as an economic exchange that the rival has rigged in his own favor while offering nothing real.

Themes

The sonnet explores the authenticity of poetic praise, the ethics of compliment, and the self-sufficiency of the youth's virtues. Against the rival's elaborate but borrowed praise, the speaker positions honest recognition of what is genuinely the youth's own — a rivalry not of craft but of integrity.
