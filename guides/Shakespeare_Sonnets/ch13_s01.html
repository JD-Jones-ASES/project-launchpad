Study Guide: Sonnet CXLII

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet CXLII opens with the speaker's frank self-indictment — "Love is my sin" — but immediately pivots to a tu quoque defense: the Dark Lady has no moral authority to reproach him because her own conduct is equally sinful. Her "virtue" of hating his sin is exposed as a paradox: it is "grounded on sinful loving," meaning her very moral posture rests on the same foundation she condemns. The speaker catalogues her offenses — profaned lips, broken love-vows, adultery that "robb'd others' beds' revenues" — and demands she compare her state to his before judging him. The sestet shifts from accusation to a plea for pity, asking her to nurture compassion so that she herself may deserve pity in return. The couplet closes with a warning: if she withholds what she herself would hide (pity, mercy), she sets a precedent that will be turned against her own desires.

Key Poetic Devices

  • Tu quoque argument: the speaker deflects blame by establishing her equal guilt
  • Legal/economic metaphor: "bonds," "revenues," "rents" frame love and fidelity as contractual obligations both parties have breached
  • Paradox: her virtue is grounded in the very sin she condemns
  • Periphrasis: "scarlet ornaments" for her lips — a classicizing circumlocution that intensifies the accusation of profanation

Central Themes

Moral hypocrisy: Neither the speaker nor the lady stands on moral high ground; the sonnet dramatizes the impossibility of sincere reproach between two sinners. Sin and shame: Love is explicitly named as sin; the Dark Lady's own sins are catalogued without euphemism. Reciprocity: Pity, mercy, and even the right to reproach are presented as symmetrical — what you deny, you may yourself be denied. The sonnet functions as both confession and counterattack, positioning the Dark Lady sequence as an unflinching exploration of complicit transgression.
