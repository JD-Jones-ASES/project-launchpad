Chapter 13, Section 1: The Dark Lady Sequence - A Reckoning

Overview

This section introduces the final phase of the Dark Lady sonnets, characterizing it not as a resolution but as a "reckoning." It explores the profound impact of intense desire on the human soul, tracing a descent through mutual accusation, spiritual crisis, erotic entanglement, and moral collapse. The sequence culminates in a paradoxical demonstration of love's ungovernable nature, rather than offering comfort or clear answers.

Key Concepts

  • The Concept of Reckoning: The chapter's central theme, combining both an "accounting sense" (a final tallying of debts and consequences) and a "moral sense" (a confrontation with ethical fallout). This frames the entire conclusion of the Dark Lady sequence.
  • Desire's Impact on the Soul: The overarching inquiry of this chapter is how intense, often illicit, desire affects one's spiritual and moral well-being, leading to crisis and collapse.
  • Movement from Accusation to Collapse: The sonnets progress through distinct stages: beginning with mutual blame (142), moving to spiritual and moral distress (146, 152), and concluding with a stark, unresolved paradox.
  • The Triangular Drama: Sonnet 144 explicitly crystallizes the long-standing love triangle involving the speaker, the Fair Youth ("better angel"), and the Dark Lady ("worser spirit"), and the speaker's suspicion of their entanglement.
  • Spiritual Crisis and Renunciation: Sonnet 146 marks a pivotal moment where the soul turns away from worldly desires and the body's vanities, seeking immortality through spiritual renunciation, making it the most overtly religious poem in the collection.
  • Moral Degeneration and Failed Judgment: The later sonnets, particularly 147 and 152, depict the speaker's descent into a state where reason fails, judgment is clouded, and he mistakes beauty for virtue, acknowledging his own extensive perjury.
  • Paradoxical Conclusion: The final two sonnets (153, 154) offer no traditional resolution or cure for love's fever. Instead, they demonstrate love's enduring and ungovernable power, leaving the reader with a sense of its intractable nature.

Important Terms

  • Reckoning: A term with dual meaning in this context: 1) A final tallying of debts, consequences, or accounts. 2) A moral confrontation with the results of one's actions.
  • Iambic Tetrameter: A poetic meter consisting of four iambic feet per line (eight syllables, alternating unstressed and stressed). Sonnet 145 is notable for being the only sonnet in the sequence written in this meter, departing from the standard iambic pentameter.
  • Iambic Pentameter: The standard poetic meter for Shakespearean sonnets, consisting of five iambic feet per line (ten syllables, alternating unstressed and stressed).
  • Anacreontea: A collection of ancient Greek lyric poems, often attributed to the poet Anacreon, known for their celebration of love, wine, and revelry. Sonnets 153 and 154 are adaptations from an epigram attributed to this collection.
  • Moral Nadir: The lowest point or worst state of a person's morality or ethical conduct. This term is applied to Sonnet 152, where the speaker acknowledges his extensive broken vows.
  • Forsworn/Perjured: Having sworn a false oath or broken a solemn vow. The speaker admits to being "more perjured" than the Dark Lady.

Detailed Notes

The Nature of "Reckoning"

The final phase of the Dark Lady sonnets is framed by the concept of "reckoning." This isn't a neat resolution but a comprehensive confrontation with the outcomes of the speaker's (and the Dark Lady's) actions. The term encompasses two key aspects:

  • Accounting Sense: A final tallying of all the emotional, moral, and spiritual "debts" accumulated throughout the tumultuous relationship.
  • Moral Sense: A direct confrontation with the painful consequences and ethical failures stemming from desire and entanglement. The chapter's trajectory moves from initial accusations to deep spiritual and moral collapse, concluding with stark paradox rather than comfort. Its central question is what desire ultimately does to the soul.

Sonnet 142: Mutual Accusation

  • The sequence begins with mutual blame. The speaker challenges the Dark Lady's authority to judge him.
  • He highlights her hypocrisy, pointing out that she herself has committed the same transgressions and broken the same vows she now condemns in him. This sets an immediate tone of contentious engagement.

Sonnet 144: The Great Bridge - Triangular Drama

  • This sonnet serves as a critical link, articulating the core love triangle that has simmered throughout the sequence.
  • The Fair Youth is explicitly identified as the "better angel," representing purity, good influence, and perhaps ideal love.
  • The Dark Lady is cast as the "worser spirit," symbolizing corruption, temptation, and destructive passion.
  • The speaker expresses a profound suspicion that these two figures are together, believing his "good angel" has been tainted and drawn into the "hell" of his "bad angel." This crystallizes the agonizing internal conflict and external betrayal.

Sonnet 145: An Anomalous Lightness

  • Sonnet 145 stands out dramatically from the rest of the sequence due to its unique metrical form: it is written in iambic tetrameter (four feet per line) rather than the standard iambic pentameter (five feet per line).
  • This metrical change gives it a lighter, more lyrical, and less intense feel.
  • It is widely theorized to be an earlier, possibly genuine, love lyric addressed to Anne Hathaway (Shakespeare's wife), which may have been included in the published sequence either by accident or design. Its presence offers a brief, almost jarring, moment of innocence amidst the surrounding turmoil.

Sonnet 146: Spiritual Crisis and Renunciation

  • Immediately following the lightness of 145, Sonnet 146 strikes with immense spiritual weight.
  • This is the most overtly religious moment in the entire collection.
  • The speaker's soul actively turns away from the transient "vanities" of the body and worldly desires.
  • It expresses a yearning for immortality, not through the legacy of verse (as often promised to the Fair Youth), but through spiritual renunciation and a focus on the soul's inner life. This marks a profound shift towards an internal, spiritual struggle.

Sonnet 147: Madness and Moral Reversal

  • This sonnet presents one of the sequence's most chilling and "clinical" images of madness driven by love.
  • Love is depicted as a consuming "fever," while reason is personified as an "abandoned physician" who has given up on the speaker's case.
  • The final couplet delivers a brutal reversal, calling the Dark Lady "black as hell, as dark as night."
  • This sonnet sets a harsh tone for what follows, portraying a world where the speaker's judgment has utterly failed. He can no longer distinguish genuine virtue from superficial beauty, mistaking destructive desire for true love.

Sonnet 152: The Moral Nadir

  • Sonnet 152 represents the absolute moral nadir of the sequence.
  • The speaker undertakes a painful tallying of his own broken oaths—more than twenty in number—all made in a desperate attempt to convince himself (and perhaps others) that the Dark Lady was worthy of his unwavering devotion.
  • He admits that both he and the Dark Lady are "forsworn" (have broken their vows), but critically acknowledges that he himself is "the more perjured." This marks a devastating moment of self-recrimination and acceptance of his own profound moral failure.

Sonnets 153 & 154: The Paradoxical Conclusion

  • The sequence concludes with a matched pair of sonnets, 153 and 154, which are adaptations of a late Greek epigram from the Anacreontea.
  • The epigram tells a mythological tale: Cupid's torch, a symbol of love's fire, is quenched in a spring, which then causes the spring's water to become therapeutically warm.
  • The speaker visits this now-heated spring, hoping to find a cure or remedy for the "fever" of his own love.
  • However, he finds no relief. The central paradox is articulated in the final line of the entire collection: "Love's fire heats water, water cools not love."
  • This ending is not a resolution that brings comfort or closure. Instead, it serves as a "demonstration" of love's fundamental nature: ungovernable, intractable, and beyond human control or cure. The reckoning is complete, but love itself remains untamed.

Review Questions

  1. How does the chapter's title, "Reckoning," encapsulate the overall movement and themes of the Dark Lady sonnets discussed in this section? What dual meanings does the term carry?
  2. Explain the significance of Sonnet 144 as the "great bridge." How does it solidify the recurring triangular drama and what does it reveal about the speaker's state of mind?
  3. Compare and contrast Sonnet 145 and Sonnet 146. How does the unique form of 145 highlight the themes and tone of 146, and what is the "spiritual crisis" presented in 146?
  4. Describe the speaker's moral and psychological decline as depicted in Sonnets 147 and 152. How does his judgment fail, and what does he acknowledge about his own actions?
  5. Sonnets 153 and 154 conclude the entire collection. In what sense do these sonnets offer a "demonstration" rather than a "resolution"? What is the final, paradoxical message about love presented in the closing line?
