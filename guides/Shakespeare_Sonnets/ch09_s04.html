Study Guide: Sonnets 97-99 — Estrangement and Return

Overview

This section explores Sonnets 97, 98, and 99, a trio of poems that delve into the speaker's profound experience of absence from the beloved. Across different seasons—summer/autumn, spring, and a catalog of flowers—the sonnets paradoxically depict a world physically abundant but emotionally desolate, with nature's beauty registering only as "shadows" or "thefts" without the youth's presence. They showcase Shakespeare's masterful use of conceit to illustrate the beloved's central importance as the source and "pattern" of all delight.

Key Concepts

  • Absence as Emotional Winter: The central paradox of Sonnets 97 and 98 is that even during the vibrant seasons of summer, autumn, and spring, the speaker experiences an internal "winter" due to the beloved's absence, demonstrating how emotional state can fundamentally alter perception of reality.
  • The Beloved as the "Pattern" of Beauty: Sonnets 98 and 99 establish the beloved not merely as beautiful, but as the original source or "pattern" from which all natural beauty (flowers, scents, colors) is derived. Nature's delights are merely "figures of delight" or "shadows" of the beloved.
  • Nature's "Theft" or "Copy": In the beloved's absence, nature's beauty is perceived as either an inadequate copy or a "theft" of the beloved's qualities. This reverses the traditional praise sonnet, where the beloved is compared to nature; here, nature is compared to the beloved and found wanting.
  • Paradox of Abundance and Desolation: The physical world, rich with "teeming" autumn fruit or "proud-pied April," is contrasted with the speaker's emotional desolation and inability to find joy. This gap measures the depth of the speaker's love and longing.
  • Shakespeare's Seasonal Sonnet Tradition: These sonnets deepen the long-standing tradition of using seasons to reflect emotional states. Shakespeare's specific contribution is the "estrangement" of season from emotion, creating a "double vision" where the speaker feels disconnected from the world he inhabits.
  • The 15-Line Sonnet: Sonnet 99 is unique in Shakespeare's sequence for its extra line, transforming the first quatrain into five lines. This formal irregularity may mirror the poem's theme of nature's "excessive" stealing from the beloved, or the profusion of the flower catalogue itself.

Important Terms

  • Canker: A plant disease causing rot; also a caterpillar or grub that destroys flowers from within. Used as punishment for the rose's "theft."
  • Canzoniere: The title given to Petrarch's collection of poems, influential in establishing the use of passing seasons as a central structural device for mapping emotional states.
  • Conceit: An extended metaphor or elaborate comparison that forms the controlling idea of a poem or passage.
  • Fleeting year: The passing year; the year in its swift movement through seasons.
  • Figures of delight: Images or representations of pleasure; copies of something that exists elsewhere in a more perfect form.
  • Forward: Bold; presumptuous; premature. Used to describe the violet in Sonnet 99.
  • Grossly dy'd: Colored too deeply; stained crudely. Implies a clumsy or excessive theft of color.
  • Heavy Saturn: The planet Saturn was associated in Renaissance astrology with melancholy, old age, and heaviness of spirit. Used to highlight spring's extraordinary vivacity.
  • Lays: Songs; the musical calls of birds.
  • Marjoram: A fragrant herb with small, soft leaves and a delicate scent.
  • Proud-pied April: Brilliantly and variously colored April, strutting like someone in fine clothes. "Pied" means multicolored; "proud" suggests both beauty and self-display.
  • Purple pride: The violet's vivid purple color, described as arrogance or boasting.
  • Reverdie: A poetic tradition ("re-greening") in which spring's return was the occasion for celebrating love renewed.
  • Shadow: A reflection, image, or copy; something without the substance of the original.
  • Teeming: Full; pregnant; productive. Autumn is "teeming" with fruit.
  • Unfather'd: Without a father; fatherless. Describing the fruit of autumn without the "father" (the beloved/summer).
  • Vermilion: A bright red, specifically the red of the rose.
  • Volta: The "turn" or shift in thought or emotion in a sonnet, typically occurring between the octave and the sestet in an Italian sonnet, or before the final couplet in an English sonnet.
  • Wanton burden of the prime: The unrestrained, freely given offspring of spring.
  • Widow'd wombs: Wombs made barren by the death of the "lords" (the summer sun) who caused their fertility. Refers to harvest fruit as orphaned.

Detailed Notes

General Themes of Estrangement and Return

Sonnets 97-99 continue the sequence's exploration of the speaker's relationship with the young man, specifically focusing on the theme of absence. These poems explore how the beloved's absence can distort the speaker's perception of the natural world, transforming beauty into desolation and abundance into emptiness. This collection deepens the earlier praise sonnets by making the beloved the source of nature's beauty, rather than merely comparable to it.

Sonnet 97: Absence in Summer and Autumn

  • Main Idea: The speaker describes experiencing summer and harvest as a bleak winter due to the youth's absence, highlighting a profound paradox where physical abundance yields emotional desolation.
  • The Winter of Absence: The sonnet opens with a powerful simile, comparing the speaker's absence to "a winter." Despite the time physically being summer and autumn ("teeming autumn, big with rich increase"), the speaker feels "freezings" and sees "dark days" and "old December's bareness."
  • Orphaned Plenty: The paradox is sustained by separating the seasons from their usual emotional meanings. Autumn's "rich increase" is real, but without the youth, it is merely "orphaned plenty," "unfather'd fruit." The imagery of "widow'd wombs" suggests fertility without a sustaining "lord" (the summer sun, implicitly linked to the youth's presence). This elaborate image suggests that while the fruit exists, its meaning or joy is absent because the source of its vitality (the youth's presence) is gone.
  • Nature Reflects Despair: Even the birds' songs are "dull," or they are "mute," and the leaves themselves "look pale, dreading the winter's near." This transference of the speaker's dread onto the natural world intensifies the sense of universal desolation, showing how deeply the speaker's internal state colors external reality. It suggests that the world itself feels the lack of the beloved, not just the speaker.
  • Volta and Deepened Paradox: The volta occurs at the couplet, which doesn't resolve the paradox but deepens it. The birds might sing, but their song only emphasizes the approaching winter's chill, further cementing the idea that the world is physically abundant but emotionally desolate. This gap between physical reality and emotional experience is the true measure of the speaker's love.
  • Contextual Link: This sonnet inherits from the seasonal sonnet tradition (like Petrarch and the reverdie), but Shakespeare innovates by not simply mapping an internal state onto a season, but actively estranging the two, creating a "double vision" unique to the speaker's love.

Sonnet 98: Absence in Spring

  • Main Idea: Following Sonnet 97's focus on autumn, Sonnet 98 covers spring, describing how even the glorious vibrancy of "proud-pied April" fails to inspire joy in the speaker because the beloved is absent.
  • Spring's Empty Glory: April is personified as "proud-pied" and so full of "spirit of youth" that even "heavy Saturn" (associated with melancholy) is moved to laugh. The season is characterized by birdsong ("lays"), varied flower fragrances, and the specific beauty of the lily and the rose.
  • The Beloved as the "Pattern": Despite this overflowing beauty, the speaker cannot "any summer's story tell" or take delight. The crucial explanation is that all these natural wonders are "but sweet, but figures of delight, / Drawn after you, you pattern of all those." The youth is the original, the ultimate blueprint for beauty, from which all natural forms are merely copies.
  • Playing with "Shadows": Because the youth is absent, the speaker can only "play" with these "shadows" – the flowers and their beauty are insubstantial reflections without the original. This connects to earlier sonnets (e.g., 43) where the concept of "shadows" or images of the beloved is explored.
  • Repetition of Theme: The couplet reiterates the central theme of absence transforming reality: "Yet seem'd it winter still, and you away." This echoes the refrain of Sonnet 97, linking the two poems as a "natural pair" covering consecutive seasons of absence.
  • Connections: This idea of the youth as the "pattern" echoes earlier praise sonnets like Sonnet 18, but explicitly reverses the comparison. Here, nature doesn't illuminate the beloved; the beloved is the source of nature's beauty.

Sonnet 99: The Flowers as Thieves

  • Main Idea: Sonnet 99 playfully accuses various flowers of "stealing" their beauty, scent, and color from the beloved, solidifying the idea that the youth is the ultimate source of all natural delight.
  • The Fifteen-Line Sonnet: This sonnet is unique in the sequence for having fifteen lines, an extra line in the first quatrain. This irregularity might formally embody the poem's theme of "excess" – the proliferating flower catalogue and nature's "theft" from the youth.
  • Arraigning the Flowers: The speaker "chides" the "forward violet" for stealing its sweet scent from the beloved's breath and its "purple pride" from the beloved's veins, "grossly dy'd." The lily is condemned for its hand-like whiteness, and marjoram buds for resembling the beloved's hair.
  • The Three Roses: The sonnet details three roses: one blushing red with "shame" (for its theft), another white with "despair," and a third, neither red nor white, that "annex'd" both colors and the beloved's breath. This third rose is punished for its greed with a "vengeful canker," symbolizing the corrupting consequence of stealing from the original source of beauty.
  • Reversed Praise Logic: The tone is playful, but the underlying logic is profound. Instead of comparing the beloved to flowers, the flowers are accused of stealing their attributes from the beloved. This establishes the beloved as the supreme standard of beauty, the "original" from which all other beauty is merely a copy or a "theft."
  • Universal Conclusion: The final couplet draws a universal conclusion, reinforcing the main idea: "More flowers I noted, yet I none could see, / But sweet, or colour it had stol'n from thee." Every beautiful flower owes its existence to the youth.

Review Questions

  1. In Sonnet 97, the speaker experiences summer and autumn as winter. How does Shakespeare use specific imagery (e.g., "orphaned plenty," "widow'd wombs," "mute" birds) to create and sustain this paradox, and what does it suggest about the nature of the speaker's love?
  2. Sonnets 98 and 99 both assert that the beloved is the "pattern" or "original" for nature's beauty. Compare and contrast how each sonnet develops this idea—one through the concept of "shadows," the other through "theft."
  3. Discuss the significance of Sonnet 99 being a fifteen-line sonnet. How might this formal irregularity relate to the poem's content and its playful tone of accusing flowers of "theft"?
  4. How do Sonnets 97-99 contribute to or deepen the traditional "seasonal sonnet tradition" (like the reverdie or Petrarch's Canzoniere)? What is Shakespeare's unique contribution to this literary convention?
  5. Consider the role of personification in these three sonnets (e.g., "proud-pied April," "heavy Saturn laugh'd," "leaves look pale, dreading"). How does Shakespeare use personification to convey the speaker's emotional state and the impact of the beloved's absence on the natural world?
