Sonnet CV — Study Guide

Overview

Sonnet CV is the chapter's most explicit meditation on constancy as a virtue. The speaker defends his singular, unwavering devotion against the hypothetical charge that it resembles idolatry. His defense is both theological and philosophical: true love, like true worship, is constant rather than roving.

Meaning

The speaker preemptively denies that his love constitutes idolatry. His evidence is constancy: his poems all praise one person, expressing one consistent excellence. The triple refrain "Fair, kind, and true" — repeated and varied across the sonnet — both describes the beloved and enacts the poem's argument through form. The couplet makes the ultimate claim: fairness, kindness, and truth have historically existed separately in different people, but now for the first time all three reside in a single person.

Literary Devices

  • Anaphora / refrain: "Fair, kind, and true" recurs throughout, mirroring the constancy it describes.
  • Allusion: The idolatry charge evokes Protestant-era debates about religious devotion and proper worship.
  • Platonic triad: Fair (beauty), kind (goodness), true (truth) invoke the classical triad absorbed by Renaissance humanism.
  • Enactment: The poem's formal repetition enacts constancy — the verse is itself "confin'd" to one thing.

Themes of Constancy and Faithfulness

This is the chapter's keynote sonnet on constancy. The speaker's argument is that faithfulness to one perfect person is not a limiting vice but the highest possible form of devotion. The beloved's own threefold constancy (fair, kind, true) makes singular loyalty not just defensible but necessary.
