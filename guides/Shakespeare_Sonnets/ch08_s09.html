Study Guide: Sonnet LXXXVI

Overview

Sonnet LXXXVI is the climax and conclusion of the Rival Poet sequence, and one of Shakespeare's most dramatically structured sonnets. Through a series of rhetorical questions that raise and dismiss possible causes for the speaker's creative silence, the poem arrives at a couplet of quiet devastation: it was not the rival's verse, not supernatural spirits, but the youth's own countenance filling the rival's lines that stripped the speaker of his subject and enfeebled his pen.

Meaning and Argument

The first quatrain asks whether the rival's "proud full sail" of verse — echoing the tall-ship imagery of Sonnet LXXX — entombed the speaker's ripe thoughts before they could be born, using the extraordinary coinage "inhearse" to describe premature burial of ideas. The second quatrain raises a darker possibility: that the rival was taught by supernatural spirits to write above mortal pitch, striking the speaker dead. The third quatrain denies both: neither the rival's verse nor his "affable familiar ghost" — a supernatural helper that merely "gulls" (deceives) the rival with false intelligence — astonished the speaker's verse. The speaker feared none of this. The couplet then identifies the true cause: when the youth's countenance filled the rival's line, the speaker lacked matter — the youth himself, the very substance of his writing, had gone to inhabit another poet's work.

Poetic Devices

"Inhearse" is the sequence's most striking coinage — a verbal form of "hearse" that fuses the images of birth and death (tomb and womb) into a single word. The triple rhetorical question structure (Was it...? Was it...? No...) builds forensic tension before the devastating couplet redirect. The nautical "proud full sail" echoes Sonnet LXXX, giving the sequence architectural coherence.

Themes

The themes are creative paralysis, the destructive power of the beloved's approval of another, and the complicity of the youth in the speaker's displacement. LXXXVI reveals that the rival did not conquer the speaker; the youth, by giving himself to the rival's verse, removed the speaker's reason to write.
