How to Read a Sonnet: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Overview

This section provides practical, actionable advice for new readers approaching Shakespeare's sonnets, aiming to enrich the reading experience beyond a superficial glance. It emphasizes active engagement with the poem's sound, structure, and language, encouraging readers to slow down and appreciate the intricate artistry embedded within each sonnet and the sequence as a whole.

Key Concepts

  • Read Aloud: Engaging your voice transforms sonnet reading from a visual task into a physical, temporal experience, making the meter, rhymes, and compressed language more apparent and comprehensible.
  • Feel the Meter: While iambic pentameter forms the rhythmic backbone (da-dum five times per line), the expressive variations (like trochees, spondees, or pyrrhics) are crucial. Noticing where the natural speech resists the meter reveals Shakespeare's deliberate emphasis and meaning.
  • Identify the Rhyme Scheme: Recognizing the Shakespearean rhyme pattern (ABAB CDCD EFEF GG) helps reveal the poem's structural "architecture," marking the end of quatrains and the powerful arrival of the final couplet.
  • Find the Volta: The "turn" or shift in argument, usually occurring around line 9 or line 13, is a critical structural element. Identifying it clarifies the sonnet's development, separating its setup from its response.
  • Read the Couplet Last—and Again: The final two lines often carry significant weight, providing resolution, reversal, or complex nuance. Rereading them in isolation helps to fully grasp their impact and potential ambiguities.
  • Pause at Punctuation, Not Line Ends: For comprehension, follow grammatical punctuation (commas, periods, semicolons, colons) rather than automatically pausing at the end of each line, which is a rhythmic, not necessarily grammatical, unit.
  • Deal Patiently with Archaic Language: Understanding Elizabethan English requires using provided tools (footnotes, glossaries), recognizing old pronoun forms (thou, thee, thy/thine), noting verb endings (-est, -eth), and identifying the main subject and verb in complex sentences.
  • Let the Sequence Work on You: The 154 sonnets are not isolated pieces but a connected "sequence." Reading them with an awareness of recurring themes, images, and developing narratives enhances their collective meaning and impact.

Important Terms

  • Sonnet: A 14-line poem, typically written in iambic pentameter, with a specific rhyme scheme and often exploring a single theme or argument.
  • Iambic Pentameter: A poetic meter consisting of five iambs per line, totaling ten syllables, with an unstressed syllable followed by a stressed syllable (da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM).
  • Iamb: A metrical foot in poetry consisting of one unstressed syllable followed by one stressed syllable.
  • Trochee: A metrical foot in poetry consisting of one stressed syllable followed by one unstressed syllable, often used to create emphasis at the beginning of a line.
  • Spondee: A metrical foot in poetry consisting of two stressed syllables in a row, used to create weight or halt the forward movement of a line.
  • Pyrrhic: A metrical foot in poetry consisting of two unstressed syllables, often used to create lightness and speed.
  • Rhyme Scheme (Shakespearean Sonnet): A pattern of rhymes at the end of each line, specifically ABAB CDCD EFEF GG (three quatrains followed by a rhyming couplet).
  • Quatrain: A stanza or section of four lines, typically with an alternating rhyme scheme in Shakespearean sonnets.
  • Couplet: A pair of successive lines of verse, especially when they rhyme and have the same meter; in Shakespearean sonnets, the final two lines (GG).
  • Volta: The "turn" or shift in thought, argument, or emotion within a sonnet, often occurring at the beginning of the third quatrain (line 9) or the couplet (line 13).
  • Archaic Language: Words or phrases that are no longer in common use but were characteristic of an earlier historical period, such as Shakespeare's Elizabethan English.
  • Thou/Thee/Thy/Thine: Elizabethan second-person pronouns: thou (subject form, like "you"), thee (object form, like "you"), thy/thine (possessive forms, like "your").
  • -est/-eth: Archaic verb endings: -est for the second person singular ("thou dost"), and -eth for the third person singular ("he doth").

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Reading Sonnets

Before diving into Shakespeare's 154 sonnets, it's beneficial to adopt specific reading habits. These strategies are particularly useful for those new to sonnets and aim to make the experience richer and more profound.

1. Read Aloud

  • Why it's Crucial: Shakespeare wrote for performance; his language is inherently theatrical. Reading aloud allows you to experience the sonnets as they were meant to be heard.
  • Benefits:
    • Forces decisions on pace, emphasis, and pause, actively engaging you with the text.
    • Makes the iambic meter audible, allowing you to feel the rhythm physically.
    • Exposes rhymes and repetitions that might be overlooked in silent reading.
    • Slows you down, which is essential for processing Shakespeare's dense and compressed language.
  • Practical Tip: If self-conscious, try whispering. The goal is to engage your voice and perceive the poem as a physical, temporal event.

2. Feel the Meter—But Do Not Force It

  • Iambic Pentameter: The foundational rhythm of Shakespeare's verse: five iambs (unstressed, stressed) per line, for a total of ten syllables (da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM).
  • How to Approach: Do not count syllables. Instead, read naturally, focusing on the meaning, and notice where the natural rhythm of speech seems to "resist" the underlying iambic pulse.
  • Expressive Variations: Departures from regular iambic pentameter are deliberate and carry meaning:
    • Trochee (stressed-unstressed): Often at the start of a line, signaling emphasis (e.g., "TIRED with all these" in Sonnet 66, conveying exhaustion).
    • Spondee (two stressed syllables): Stops the line's forward movement, adding weight (e.g., "BARE RUIN'D CHOIRS" in Sonnet 73, impossible to rush).
    • Pyrrhic (two unstressed syllables): Creates lightness and speed, often for movement or transience.
  • Key Insight: Every significant metrical variation is interpretive. If you encounter a "bump" in the rhythm, pause and consider what effect Shakespeare is creating and how it relates to the poem's meaning.

3. Identify the Rhyme Scheme

  • The Shakespearean Pattern: Dedicate a moment to confirm the rhyme scheme: ABAB CDCD EFEF GG.
  • Audible Architecture: The rhyme scheme acts as a map, delineating the poem's structure into three four-line quatrains and a final two-line couplet.
  • Structural Awareness: Recognizing the pattern allows you to anticipate the end of a quatrain and feel the particular force of the concluding rhyming couplet, which often signals a resolution or definitive statement.

4. Find the Volta

  • The "Turn": The volta is the crucial shift in the sonnet's argument, thought, or emotion.
  • Where to Look:
    • Line 9 (beginning of the third quatrain): This is the primary place to look in a Shakespearean sonnet.
    • Line 13 (the couplet): The turn can also occur here, often intensifying or summarizing the preceding argument.
  • Signal Markers:
    • Explicit words: "but," "yet," "however," "thus," "then," "and yet."
    • Shifts in: tense (present to future), subject (speaking about vs. speaking to), emotional register (celebration to elegy), or imagery cluster (natural to legal).
  • Impact: Once identified, the volta clarifies the sonnet's structure: everything before is the setup, everything after is the response. The quality of the sonnet often hinges on the relationship between these two movements.

5. Read the Couplet Last—and Again

  • Weight of the Couplet: The final two lines of a sonnet carry the full weight of the preceding twelve.
  • Strategy: Read the couplet once within the natural flow of the poem, then pause and reread it in isolation.
  • Questions to Ask: Does it resolve, reverse, or suspend the argument? Is the speaker confident or ironic? Does the rhyme feel triumphant or muted?
  • Complexity: Shakespeare's couplets can seem simple but often open into deeper complexity upon rereading, as seen in the conditional "if" of Sonnet 116.

6. Pause at Punctuation, Not Line Ends

  • Common Mistake: New readers often pause at the end of every line.
  • Line vs. Sense: In metered poetry, a line is a rhythmic unit, but not necessarily a complete grammatical unit. The sense often flows across line breaks.
  • Guidance: Follow the punctuation: pause at commas, stop at periods and semicolons, and let colons invite you to continue. This ensures you grasp the grammatical sense and avoid an awkward, stuttering reading.

7. Deal Patiently with Archaic Language

  • A Familiar Stranger: Shakespeare's English is an older version of your own, requiring patience.
  • Practical Strategies:
    • Use Footnotes/Glossary: Don't struggle with unfamiliar words; utilize the provided resources immediately.
    • Slow Down for Pronouns: Master the Elizabethan second-person pronouns:
      • thou (subject: "thou art")
      • thee (object: "I see thee")
      • thy/thine (possessive: "thy beauty," "thine eyes")
    • Watch Verb Endings: Recognize -est for second-person singular ("thou dost") and -eth for third-person singular ("he doth," "beauty fadeth").
    • Read for the Main Verb: In complex sentences with inversions or subordinate clauses, identify the core subject and verb to unlock the fundamental meaning.
    • Read it Twice: The first reading is for overall sound and impression; the second is for detailed sense. Allow meaning to unfold gradually.

8. Let the Sequence Work on You

  • More Than a Collection: Shakespeare's sonnets were published as a sequence, and they are best appreciated when read as such.
  • Benefits of Sequential Reading:
    • Accumulated Meaning: Recurring images, themes, and motifs gain deeper significance across multiple sonnets.
    • Interconnected Arguments: Arguments initiated in one sonnet are often revisited or answered in others.
    • Emotional Narrative: The sequence builds a coherent (though perhaps not autobiographically literal) emotional journey.
  • Architecture of the Whole: Be attentive to cross-references and echoes between sonnets, even those from different thematic groups (e.g., Fair Youth and Dark Lady). These connections reveal the vast, carefully designed meditation on universal themes like love, time, beauty, and the power of language.
