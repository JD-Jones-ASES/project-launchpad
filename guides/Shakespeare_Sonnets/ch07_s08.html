Study Guide: Sonnet LXVI

Summary

Sonnet LXVI is one of Shakespeare's most famous complaints against the social and moral order. Weary of the world's injustices, the speaker cries for "restful death." What follows is a catalog of thirteen specific grievances, each introduced by "And": desert born to beggary, worthlessness celebrated, faith forsworn, honor misplaced, virtue prostituted, perfection disgraced, strength disabled by corrupt authority, art silenced, folly controlling skill, truth dismissed as simplicity, and goodness enslaved to evil. The cumulative weight of these anaphoric "And" lines creates an overwhelming sense of a world turned upside down. The sole reason the speaker does not seek the death he longs for appears in the couplet: "to die, I leave my love alone." Love is the last remaining tether to a world otherwise utterly worthless.

Key Themes

  • Social and moral corruption: Time's destruction is not only physical — it extends to the moral fabric of society.
  • Love as the only reason to live: The couplet focuses all the sonnet's weight onto a single point: the beloved.
  • Weariness and existential despair: The accumulation of injustice produces a tone of total exhaustion.

Key Literary Devices

  • Anaphora: Repeated "And" builds an overwhelming catalog of grievances.
  • Chiasmus (embedded): "Captive good attending captain ill" — goodness and evil are reversed in their proper social hierarchy.
  • Volta: The couplet's "Save that" creates the turn from death-wish to its single negation: love.

Vocabulary

  • Desert: Worthiness, merit
  • Forsworn: Broken by oath, perjured
  • Strumpeted: Treated like a prostitute; degraded
  • Niggard: Miserly, stingy
  • Simplicity: Used pejoratively here: mere plainness, stupidity
