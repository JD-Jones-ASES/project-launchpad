Study Guide: Sonnet VII

Summary

Sonnet VII departs from the financial and botanical metaphors of the earlier sonnets and builds a sustained allegory around the sun's daily arc. The three quatrains trace the sun's journey from glorious dawn (universal homage) through noble midday (still admired in "middle age") to feeble, abandoned sunset (eyes turning away). The couplet snaps the allegory into place with startling bluntness: past his noon, the youth will die "unlook'd on" — forgotten and unmourned — unless he gets a son. The pun on "sun/son" is almost certainly intentional, binding the solar metaphor to the procreation argument.

Key Literary Devices

  • Extended allegory: The sun's daily arc (dawn → noon → sunset) mirrors the human lifespan (youth → prime → old age).
  • Classical allusion: "Weary car" and "golden pilgrimage" allude to the myth of Phoebus Apollo driving the solar chariot.
  • Personification: The sun given courtly attributes — "sacred majesty," attended on its "golden pilgrimage."
  • Pun: "Get a son" — the word "son" resonates against the poem's solar imagery, making sun and son conceptually identical.

Thematic Significance

Sonnet VII is the most classically elevated poem of the early procreation sequence. By choosing the sun as its central figure, Shakespeare gives the argument cosmic scale: even the greatest thing in the sky is abandoned at its setting. The allegory is more emotionally affecting than the legal or financial arguments because it does not threaten or cajole — it simply shows what happens. The turning of admiring eyes away from the setting sun is a profoundly melancholy image, and it makes the couplet's warning feel less like a lecture and more like compassionate advice from someone who has seen how human attention works.
