Study Guide: Sonnet LXXXV

Overview

Sonnet LXXXV is the sequence's most nuanced meditation on the relationship between silence and love. The speaker's tongue-tied Muse has held still while the rivals compile golden, polished verse — yet the speaker argues that his silence is not absence of feeling but its deepest expression. Love that resides in thought is prior to and more real than love expressed in words.

Meaning and Argument

The octave paints the literary landscape: rivals write "comments of your praise richly compil'd" with "golden quill" and "precious phrase by all the Muses fil'd" — every resource of Renaissance poetic craft deployed in the youth's service. Against this, the speaker offers only a curious self-portrait: he thinks good thoughts while others write good words. He is like the "unlettered clerk" who can only say "Amen" to hymns he cannot himself compose — a humble participatory response rather than an originating creative act. The sestet complicates this: hearing the youth praised, the speaker enthusiastically agrees and even adds to the praise, but that addition exists only in thought, where his love — though words come last — holds its rank first.

Poetic Devices

The simile of the unlettered clerk crying "Amen" introduces a liturgical register: the rivals' verse becomes a kind of scripture, and the speaker its devout but inarticulate congregation. This is self-deprecating but also quietly subversive — authentic faith matters more than elaborate liturgy. The couplet's oxymoron "dumb thoughts, speaking in effect" is the poem's central paradox: muteness that communicates, silence that is eloquent.

Themes

The central themes are interiority versus expression, the priority of love over eloquence, and the spiritual quality of genuine devotion. The sonnet asks whether love that cannot speak is less real than love perfectly expressed — and answers firmly that silent love, holding its rank in thought, surpasses all the rivals' polished words.
