The Sonnets in Context

Key Concepts

  • The 1609 Quarto was registered and published by Thomas Thorpe; Shakespeare appears to have had no public involvement
  • The quarto contained 154 sonnets plus A Lover's Complaint; it was sold in St Paul's Churchyard
  • The cryptic dedication to "Mr. W. H." has generated centuries of debate; its key word, "begetter," is ambiguous
  • The two main candidates for Mr. W. H. are Henry Wriothesley (Earl of Southampton) and William Herbert (Earl of Pembroke)
  • The sonnets divide into three major groupings: Fair Youth (1–126), Dark Lady (127–152), and Rival Poet (roughly 78–86)
  • The Dark Lady sonnets are tormented and self-lacerating; the Fair Youth sonnets span a wider range of emotions
  • Christopher Marlowe is the most compelling candidate for the Rival Poet
  • The debate between reading the sonnets as autobiography versus literary performance is central to their interpretation
  • The sonnets rose to canonical status in the Romantic period through the advocacy of Wordsworth and Keats

Summary

The 1609 Quarto containing Shakespeare's sonnets was published by the stationer Thomas Thorpe and sold from booksellers' stalls at St Paul's Churchyard. Shakespeare himself appears to have taken no public part in its production, made no comment on it, and never supervised a revised edition. The volume's dedication — to "Mr. W. H." — is among the most debated passages in English literary history: the word "begetter" is ambiguous, pointing either to the inspirer of the poems or to whoever procured the manuscript for Thorpe. The two leading candidates are Henry Wriothesley, Earl of Southampton, and William Herbert, Earl of Pembroke. Reading the sonnets as a sequence, scholars discern three figures: the Fair Youth (Sonnets 1–126), addressed with intense admiration; the Dark Lady (Sonnets 127–152), desired with tormented self-hatred; and the Rival Poet (roughly 78–86), a more learned competitor for the Youth's favour. The identity of all three remains officially unresolved. The broader debate — whether the sonnets are autobiography or art — has no easy answer; the most honest reading treats them as rooted in genuine experience but shaped by a supremely skilled literary intelligence into something more enduring than a diary.

Key Terms

  • 1609 Quarto: The only collection of Shakespeare's sonnets published with any claim to authority, registered by Thomas Thorpe and printed by George Eld
  • A Lover's Complaint: The longer poem that followed the 154 sonnets in the 1609 Quarto
  • begetter: The crux of the dedication — ambiguous between "the person who inspired the sonnets" and "the person who procured the manuscript"
  • Fair Youth: The beautiful young man addressed in Sonnets 1–126; his identity is traditionally debated between Southampton and Pembroke
  • Dark Lady: The woman of dark complexion and dark hair desired in Sonnets 127–152; associated with tormented, self-lacerating emotional intensity
  • Rival Poet: A competitor for the Fair Youth's patronage, addressed in roughly Sonnets 78–86; Christopher Marlowe is the most compelling candidate
  • Petrarchan blazon: The conventional form of praise that catalogued the beloved's physical features by comparison to natural phenomena; Sonnet 130 famously parodies this convention
  • negative capability: Keats's term for the capacity to remain in uncertainty without irritable reaching after fact — a quality he found modelled in Shakespeare's sonnets
  • bowdlerised: Edited to remove material considered improper; the 1640 reprint of the sonnets changed pronouns to obscure the male-to-male address
