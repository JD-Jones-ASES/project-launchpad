The Sonnets in Context: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the historical and literary context surrounding the publication of Shakespeare's Sonnets in the 1609 Quarto. It delves into the mysteries of its unauthorized appearance, the cryptic dedication to "Mr. W.H.," and the identities of the three central figures—the Fair Youth, the Dark Lady, and the Rival Poet. The guide also examines the long-standing debate over whether the sonnets should be read as autobiography or as highly crafted art, ultimately highlighting their enduring power to address universal human experiences.

Key Concepts

  • The 1609 Quarto Publication: The first and only authorized collection of Shakespeare's sonnets, published by Thomas Thorpe, yet its origins and Shakespeare's involvement remain debated.
  • Controversy over Authorization: Scholars debate whether Shakespeare personally arranged and authorized the 1609 publication or if Thorpe acquired the manuscript without his blessing.
  • The "Mr. W.H." Dedication: A brief, cryptic dedication by the publisher T.T. (Thomas Thorpe) to "Mr. W.H.," sparking centuries of speculation about whether W.H. was the sonnets' inspirer (the Fair Youth) or their procurer.
  • The Fair Youth (Sonnets 1-126): A beautiful young man addressed with intense affection, urged to marry and procreate, whose beauty is celebrated and mourned against the ravages of time. Key candidates for his identity are Henry Wriothesley (Earl of Southampton) and William Herbert (Earl of Pembroke).
  • The Dark Lady (Sonnets 127-152): A woman desired with obsessive and tormented intensity, described with dark features and as unfaithful. These sonnets are characterized by self-laceration and bitterness. Emilia Lanier is a prominent candidate for her identity.
  • The Rival Poet (Sonnets 78-86): A competitor for the Fair Youth's patronage, described as technically superior and learned. Christopher Marlowe is considered the most compelling candidate.
  • Autobiography vs. Art: The central debate over how to interpret the sonnets—as a literal record of Shakespeare's life and emotions, or as sophisticated literary creations engaging with established poetic conventions (like the Petrarchan tradition).
  • Enduring Appeal: The sonnets' lasting significance stems from their direct address, in the first person, to fundamental human questions about love, beauty, time, betrayal, and the power of language.

Important Terms

  • Stationers' Company: The guild in London responsible for regulating the book trade, where new books were registered for publication.
  • Quarto: A book size resulting from printing on sheets folded twice, creating four leaves (eight pages). The 1609 edition of Shakespeare's Sonnets was a quarto.
  • London stationer: A printer, publisher, or bookseller in London during Shakespeare's time. Thomas Thorpe was a stationer.
  • Thomas Thorpe (T.T.): The London stationer who registered and published Shake-speares Sonnets in 1609.
  • Mr. W.H.: The mysterious individual to whom Thomas Thorpe dedicated the 1609 Quarto, whose identity and role remain highly debated.
  • Begetter: A key word in the dedication to "Mr. W.H."; its interpretation (either "inspirer" or "procurer") is central to identifying W.H.
  • Fair Youth: The beautiful young man, likely an aristocrat, who is the primary subject of Sonnets 1-126, celebrated by the poet.
  • Dark Lady: The mysterious woman with dark features who is the subject of Sonnets 127-152, depicted as an object of tormented desire.
  • Rival Poet: A competing poet mentioned in Sonnets 78-86, seen as vying for the Fair Youth's patronage and affection.
  • Petrarchan Blazon: A poetic convention, common in sonnets, where the poet details the physical attributes of the beloved, typically comparing them to ideal natural elements (e.g., eyes like the sun, lips like coral). Shakespeare famously parodies this in Sonnet 130.
  • Negative Capability: A term coined by Romantic poet John Keats, referring to the ability to be "in uncertainties, mysteries, doubts, without any irritable reaching after fact and reason." Keats admired Shakespeare's sonnets as an example of this.
  • Bowdlerised: Edited or censored by removing passages considered offensive or inappropriate, as happened to the 1640 reprint of the Sonnets to obscure the male-to-male address.

Detailed Notes

The 1609 Quarto and Its Publication

  • Registration and Content: In 1609, Thomas Thorpe, a London stationer, registered Shake-speares Sonnets. Never before Imprinted. The volume contained 154 sonnets followed by A Lover's Complaint.
  • Mystery of Origin: This was the only collection of Shakespeare's sonnets published with any claim to authority, yet Shakespeare appears to have had no public involvement and never supervised a second edition. The publication was not a "prestige" item for the time.
  • "Shake-speares Sonnets" Title: The possessive form "Shake-speares" (rather than "Shakespeare's") was not unusual but has been interpreted by some scholars as Thorpe's confident claim to the text.
  • Authorization Debate:
    • Argument for Authorization: Some scholars argue that the careful arrangement, thematic groupings, and evident design suggest Shakespeare's direct hand in preparing the manuscript.
    • Argument Against Authorization: Others point to the absence of a dedication from Shakespeare, the lack of a supervised revised edition, and the earlier unauthorized publication of two sonnets in an anthology. This suggests Thorpe acquired the manuscript surreptitiously, perhaps from a private circulation.
    • Implication: This debate is crucial because it influences how we read the sequence: as a deliberate literary monument or as a collection not intended for wide public consumption.

The Mysterious Dedication

  • Cryptic Nature: The dedication to the 1609 Quarto is brief, in unusual capitals, and "utterly cryptic." It is signed "T.T." (Thomas Thorpe).
  • "Mr. W.H.": The central puzzle of the dedication is the identity of "Mr. W.H."
  • The Crux of "Begetter": The meaning of "begetter" is debated:
    • Does it mean the inspirer of the sonnets (the Fair Youth)? If so, W.H. points to the poems' subject.
    • Does it mean the person who procured the manuscript for Thorpe (a literary middleman)? If so, W.H. is less central to the poetry itself.
  • Leading Candidates for Mr. W.H.:
    • Henry Wriothesley, Earl of Southampton: Patron to whom Shakespeare dedicated Venus and Adonis and The Rape of Lucrece. His initials (H.W.) require reversal to match W.H.
    • William Herbert, Earl of Pembroke: A prominent patron, to whom the First Folio was dedicated. His initials (W.H.) align perfectly.
  • Conclusion: The identity of Mr. W.H. remains unsolved. The text argues that while biographical speculation is "seductive," it can distract from the poetry's inherent greatness.

The Cast of Characters

Most readers discern three main figures in the sonnets, treated by scholars as real people who inspired the poems, though the extent of autobiographical fidelity is uncertain.

  • The Fair Youth (Sonnets 1-126, with exceptions):

    • Description: A beautiful young man addressed with intense admiration and affection. The poet urges him to marry and have children (Sonnets 1-17), celebrates his beauty, grieves separation, fears time's damage, experiences estrangement, and ultimately proclaims an enduring love.
    • Emotional Registers: Varied and contradictory: tender, despairing, angry, self-abasing, playful, desolate.
    • Leading Candidates:
      • Henry Wriothesley, Earl of Southampton (1573–1624): Patrons of Shakespeare, renowned for youthful beauty, resistant to marriage in the 1590s (aligning with "procreation sonnets"). His initials (H.W.) reversed match the dedication.
      • William Herbert, Earl of Pembroke (1580–1630): Also resistant to marriage in his youth, a notable literary patron (First Folio dedicated to him). His initials (W.H.) align directly with the dedication.
    • Conclusion: Arguments for both are elaborate and inconclusive; the identity remains "officially open."

  • The Dark Lady (Sonnets 127-152):

    • Description: A woman the poet desires obsessively and tormentedly. She has dark complexion, eyes, and hair, and exerts a "shameful" power over him. She is depicted as unfaithful to both her husband and the poet.
    • Emotional Temperature: Very different from the Fair Youth poems. These are self-lacerating, sometimes misogynistic, and filled with bitterness from a despised desire.
    • Leading Candidates:
      • Emilia Lanier (née Bassano, 1569–1645): A poet, of Italian descent (explaining "dark" colouring), moved in court/theatrical circles, mistress of Lord Hunsdon (patron of Lord Chamberlain's Men).
      • Mary Fitton: A maid of honor, proposed in the 19th century based on a disputed portrait.
      • Lucy Morgan: A gentlewoman turned brothel-keeper.
    • Conclusion: No conclusive identification. The power of these sonnets comes from their honest portrayal of despised desire, not from identifying the woman.

  • The Rival Poet (Sonnets 78-86):

    • Description: A competitor for the Fair Youth's patronage and affection, characterized as having "greater technical facility" and a "more learned manner," diminishing the speaker's confidence.
    • Evidence: This figure is largely constructed from inference, with thinner evidence than for the Youth or Lady.
    • Leading Candidates:
      • Christopher Marlowe: Shakespeare's great contemporary rival, a "university man," whose high-flown rhetoric matches descriptions in the sonnets (Sonnet 86 may allude to Marlowe's dealings).
      • George Chapman: Translator of Homer.
      • Ben Jonson.
    • Conclusion: Certainty is "out of reach."

Autobiography or Art? The Great Debate

  • The Autobiographical Temptation: The intensity of the sonnets leads many readers to interpret them as direct autobiography, a "versified diary" of Shakespeare's actual emotional life.
  • The Counterarguments (Sonnets as Art):
    • Conventional Form: The sonnet was a highly conventional literary form in Shakespeare's time, with established rules about emotions and expression, largely based on Petrarch's template (unattainable beloved, suffering poet, immortalizing verse).
    • Poetic Personae: English poets like Philip Sidney (Astrophil and Stella) and Edmund Spenser (Amoretti) worked within these conventions, often using elaborate personae, even when their poems had personal roots. Sidney explicitly called his sequence fiction.
    • Shakespeare's Subversion: Shakespeare both used and deliberately subverted these conventions. Sonnet 130 famously parodies the Petrarchan blazon ("My mistress' eyes are nothing like the sun") before asserting her rarity despite false comparisons. This demonstrates a "self-conscious literary performance."
  • Conclusion: The "most honest reading" is that the sonnets are "both": rooted in genuine emotional experience but shaped and transformed by "supremely skilled literary intelligence" into something "more crafted and more enduring than autobiography could be."

Why the Sonnets Endure

  • Historical Obscurity and Revival: The 1609 Quarto was not reprinted until 1640 (and then bowdlerised to obscure male-to-male address). The sonnets' ascent to canonical status began in the Romantic period, with Wordsworth and Keats praising their emotional depth and artistic merit ("negative capability").
  • Universal Human Questions: The sonnets endure because Shakespeare directly addresses fundamental questions that resonate with every human experience:
    • The nature of love and desire.
    • The inevitable fading of beauty and destructive power of time.
    • The capacity of language to preserve experience.
    • The possibility of redemption for betrayal, weakness, and shameful desires.
  • Dramatization of Feeling: Instead of providing answers, the sonnets "dramatise" and "enact feeling" with all its complexity—its contradictions, self-deceptions, moments of transcendence, and descents into bitterness—with remarkable precision.

Review Questions

  1. What were the circumstances surrounding the publication of Shake-speares Sonnets in 1609, and why is Shakespeare's personal involvement in its release considered controversial?
  2. Discuss the two main interpretations of the word "begetter" in the dedication to "Mr. W.H." Who are the leading candidates for "Mr. W.H." and why do their identities remain inconclusive?
  3. Compare and contrast the portrayal of the Fair Youth and the Dark Lady in the sonnets. What are the distinct emotional tones and thematic concerns associated with each, and why are their identities still debated?
  4. Explain the "Autobiography or Art?" debate concerning Shakespeare's Sonnets. What evidence from the text supports the idea of the sonnets as artistic convention, and what is the textbook's ultimate conclusion?
  5. Beyond their initial publication, how did the sonnets gain their current canonical status? What enduring human questions do they address that contribute to their lasting appeal?
