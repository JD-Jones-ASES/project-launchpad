Sonnets 83-86: The Rival Poet - A Study Guide

Overview

This section delves into four sonnets (83-86) from Shakespeare's "Rival Poet" sequence, forming a dramatic climax. The speaker navigates his artistic paralysis and defends his silence regarding the Fair Youth's beauty, initially arguing that the youth's perfection transcends all poetic praise. He contrasts his unspoken admiration with the elaborate verses of a rival, ultimately revealing that his creative block isn't due to the rival's skill, but rather the youth's shifting attention, which leaves the speaker devoid of inspiration.

Key Concepts

  • The Inadequacy of Poetic Praise: Sonnets 83 and 84 explore the idea that the Fair Youth's inherent beauty and unique being are so profound that any attempt to describe them in verse might either fall short or even diminish their true essence. The speaker suggests that the youth's mere existence is more eloquent than any poem.
  • Silence as a Defense and a Virtue: Initially, the speaker's failure to write verse for the youth is perceived as a "sin" (Sonnet 83). However, he gradually reframes this silence, arguing that it stems from reverent restraint, a commitment to truth, and a deeper, more genuine form of love than the superficial flattery offered by others (Sonnet 85).
  • The Nature of True Poetry: The speaker champions fidelity to the subject as the highest poetic achievement. He suggests that simply and accurately reflecting the youth's inherent perfection is superior to elaborate embellishment, which can distort or "kill" the beauty it attempts to honor (Sonnet 84).
  • The Rival Poet's Challenge: The presence of a "Rival Poet" is central to these sonnets, as the speaker grapples with his own creative struggles in comparison. While acknowledging the rival's magnificent verse, the speaker ultimately denies that the rival's skill or alleged supernatural inspiration is the direct cause of his own silence (Sonnet 86).
  • The Youth's Agency and Impact: The Fair Youth is not merely a passive object of poetic attention. His preferences—such as a "fondness on praise" (Sonnet 84)—and, critically, his apparent shift of allegiance or attention, are shown to have a profound and direct impact on the speaker's creative capacity.
  • Creative Paralysis: The speaker's struggle with writer's block is a pervasive theme, described through images like a "tongue-tied Muse" (Sonnet 85) and "ripe thoughts... inhearsed" (Sonnet 86). This creative paralysis is presented as a painful consequence of the complex triangular relationship between the speaker, the youth, and the rival.

Important Terms

  • painting need: Cosmetic enhancement; the need for artificial improvement. The speaker implies the youth's natural beauty requires no poetic "makeup."
  • barren tender of a poet's debt: A meager, unfruitful fulfillment of a poet's obligation. Refers to insufficient or worthless poetic tribute.
  • slept in your report: Remained silent about your qualities; failed to record your praises.
  • extant: Still existing; currently alive. Emphasizes the youth's living presence.
  • modern quill: A contemporary writer's pen. Often implies "ordinary" or "fashionable" rather than exceptional in Shakespeare's usage.
  • immured: Walled in; enclosed; confined within walls. Suggests the youth's unique qualities are intrinsically contained within him.
  • lean penury: Extreme poverty; want. Describes a poet lacking the intellectual or artistic resources to praise the youth adequately.
  • counterpart: An exact copy; a perfect likeness. Implies a poem that faithfully reproduces the youth's truth.
  • fond: Foolish; also: affectionate. Used to gently rebuke the youth's appetite for excessive flattery.
  • comments richly compiled: Elaborate, collected annotations or praises. Refers to the rival's detailed, ornate writing.
  • golden quill: A precious, excellent writer's pen. A metaphor for the rival's superior (or showy) poetic craft.
  • precious phrase by all the Muses filed: Language refined and polished by the nine classical goddesses of artistic inspiration. "Filed" means smoothed and perfected.
  • unlettered clerk: A church official or scribe who cannot read and must rely on rote responses. A self-diminishing image used by the speaker.
  • able spirit: A writer of genuine talent and power, acknowledging the rival's gifts.
  • holds his rank before: Maintains a position ahead of the spoken words; has priority. The speaker claims his unspoken love is more important.
  • proud full sail: The magnificent, fully rigged vessel of the rival's verse. A powerful nautical metaphor for the rival's grand poetry.
  • inhearse: To enclose as in a hearse or coffin; to bury alive. A vivid and unusual word describing the rival's verse silencing the speaker's thoughts.
  • compeers: Companions; equals; fellow poets. Referring to the rival poet's associates.
  • astonished: Struck with wonder and awe (stronger than modern usage).
  • affable familiar ghost: An easy-to-speak-with, courteous, or pleasant spirit who is intimately known (familiar), implying a supernatural muse or collaborator.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet LXXXIII (83)

Sonnet 83 opens this sequence by establishing the speaker's primary defense for his silence: the Fair Youth's extraordinary beauty requires no embellishment. The speaker claims he "never saw that you did painting need," implying that to add poetic "painting" would be an insult. He argues that the youth's inherent perfection "did exceed / That barren tender of a poet's debt," rendering conventional praise inadequate. His silence, therefore, was not negligence ("slept in your report") but a reverent restraint. The youth, simply "being extant" (currently alive), could "show / How far a modern quill doth come too short" in describing his worth.

The speaker acknowledges the youth imputes his silence as a "sin" but pivots to claim it as his "glory," asserting that he does "not impair beauty being mute." In a sharp critique of the rival, he suggests that while others might attempt to "give life" through elaborate praise, they in fact "bring a tomb," meaning false or excessive praise can paradoxically kill what it claims to honor. The concluding couplet confidently asserts that "There lives more life in one of your fair eyes / Than both your poets can in praise devise," emphasizing the unparalleled power of the youth's living presence over any poetic tribute. This sonnet connects to Sonnet 79 by reiterating the idea that the youth is his own best text, and his reality surpasses any literary representation. It also sets up the social anxiety and growing torment that will deepen in later sonnets, particularly Sonnet 86.

Sonnet LXXXIV (84)

Sonnet 84 continues the theme of appropriate praise by posing a rhetorical question: what is the highest praise a poet can offer? The speaker answers that it is simply acknowledging "that you alone are you," a unique original for whom "no equal grew." He argues that a poet who cannot find worthy praise for such a subject suffers from "lean penury" (extreme poverty of thought). Conversely, a poet who accurately "can tell / That you are you, so dignifies his story."

The speaker advocates for a poetry of fidelity: "Let him but copy what in you is writ, / Not making worse what nature made so clear." Such faithful representation, creating a "counterpart" (exact likeness), will truly "fame his wit" and earn admiration. However, the couplet delivers a stinging rebuke to the youth himself: "You to your beauteous blessings add a curse, / Being fond on praise, which makes your praises worse." The youth's "fondness" (foolish affection) for flattery encourages poets to provide insincere or excessive compliments, thereby degrading the quality of the tributes he receives. This sonnet connects to the Renaissance concept of mimesis (imitation of reality), arguing that for a perfect subject, pure imitation is the highest art. It also reinforces the idea from Sonnet 83 that the youth's existence outweighs any poetic description.

Sonnet LXXXV (85)

In Sonnet 85, the speaker explains his continued silence not as an inability, but as a kind of decorum or reserved dignity. His "tongue-tied Muse in manners holds her still," while other poets produce "comments richly compiled" with "golden quill" and "precious phrase by all the Muses fil’d." He positions himself humbly, like an "unlettered clerk" in a church, only able to cry "Amen" to the "hymn" offered by the "able spirit" (the rival poet).

Despite this self-diminishing comparison, the speaker asserts a deeper claim to affection. He insists that his "good thoughts" and unspoken love "hold his rank before" the rival's "good words." His silence is not emptiness but a fullness of intimate, wordless devotion. He concludes by claiming that while others are respected for "the breath of words," he should be respected "for my dumb thoughts, speaking in effect." This sonnet's image of the "tongue-tied Muse" echoes Sonnet 80 ("O how I faint when I of you do write") and anticipates the creative paralysis of Sonnet 86. It also reveals a subtle tonal modulation, moving from the speaker's earlier confidence to a more self-deprecating, yet ultimately profound, assertion of his unique love.

Sonnet LXXXVI (86)

Sonnet 86 marks the dramatic climax and conclusion of the Rival Poet sequence, directly confronting the cause of the speaker's silence. The speaker poses a series of intense rhetorical questions: Was his creative paralysis caused by the rival's "proud full sail of his great verse," metaphorically comparing the rival's poetry to a grand ship pursuing the "prize" of the youth? Was it the rival's alleged supernatural inspiration, his "spirit, by spirits taught to write, / Above a mortal pitch"?

The speaker emphatically dismisses each possibility: "No, neither he, nor his compeers by night / Giving him aid, my verse astonished." He rejects the rival's style, his fellow poets, and even the "affable familiar ghost" (a possible allusion to the rival's claim of a supernatural muse, perhaps Marlowe's "mighty line" or a reference to spiritual aid). None of these, he states, were powerful enough to defeat him. The true, delayed revelation comes in the final, impactful couplet: "But when your countenance fill’d up his line, / Then lacked I matter; that enfeebled mine." The speaker was silenced not by the rival's inherent skill, but because the youth himself—his face, his favor, his very presence—had begun to inspire the rival's poetry. The youth's shift of attention or affection deprived the speaker of his subject, leaving him without "matter" (content or inspiration) and thus "enfeebled" (weakened). This sonnet powerfully reuses the nautical metaphor from Sonnet 80 and brings the Rival Poet conflict to a poignant resolution, highlighting the youth's ultimate agency in the speaker's creative fate.

Review Questions

  1. How does the speaker initially defend his poetic silence in Sonnet 83, and how does this defense evolve into a subtle critique of the rival poet's approach to praise?
  2. Explain the central argument of Sonnet 84 regarding what constitutes the highest form of praise. What specific criticism does the speaker offer to the Fair Youth in the concluding couplet of this sonnet?
  3. In Sonnet 85, the speaker contrasts his "tongue-tied Muse" with the "richly compiled" praises of others. How does he argue that his apparent silence represents a deeper, more genuine form of devotion than the rival's eloquent verse?
  4. Sonnet 86 dramatically explores the reasons for the speaker's creative paralysis. What potential causes related to the rival poet does he consider and emphatically dismiss before revealing the true reason in the concluding couplet?
  5. Across Sonnets 83-86, trace the development of the speaker's understanding and justification of his "silence." How does its meaning shift from being a perceived "sin" to a claimed "glory" and finally to a state of lacking "matter" for poetic creation?
