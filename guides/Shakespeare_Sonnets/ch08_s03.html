Study Guide: Sonnet LXXX

Overview

Sonnet LXXX is one of the most personally vulnerable poems in the entire sequence. Here the speaker openly admits his inferiority to the rival while choosing, with stubborn will, to continue writing anyway. The extended nautical metaphor that organizes the poem — two ships sailing on the ocean of the youth's worth — gives the rivalry a vivid spatial and physical dimension, making the disparity in ability palpable.

Meaning and Argument

The octave opens with the speaker's confession of faintness upon writing about the youth, knowing the rival is also writing with superior skill. Yet the youth's worth, like a vast ocean, can sustain both the humble and the proud. The sestet elaborates the ship metaphor: the speaker's "saucy bark" needs only the shallowest help to float, while the rival's tall ship rides the soundless deep. Even if the speaker is wrecked, the rival thrives. The couplet names the worst outcome not as defeat but as something more poignant: "my love was my decay" — the love that drove the speaker to write in the first place is the very thing that will ruin him.

Poetic Devices

The sustained nautical metaphor is the poem's structural backbone: ocean = the youth's worth; the speaker = a saucy, inferior bark; the rival = a tall ship of goodly pride. "Soundless deep" exploits the double meaning of soundless (silent and unable to be sounded, i.e., fathomless). The couplet's paradox of love as decay — that devotion is simultaneously the motive and the cause of destruction — is among the most emotionally complex moments in the sequence.

Themes

The central themes are the courage of acknowledged inferiority, the self-destructive nature of love, and perseverance in the face of an unequal contest. LXXX's willingness to admit the rival's superiority while still writing distinguishes the speaker's love-driven persistence from mere pride.
