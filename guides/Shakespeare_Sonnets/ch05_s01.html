Study Guide: Sonnet XXXIII

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet XXXIII opens Chapter 5 with a breathtaking extended metaphor: the sun on a glorious morning flatters mountain tops, kisses meadows, and gilds pale streams with "heavenly alchemy," only to be hidden by base clouds that steal unseen to the west in disgrace. In the sestet, this natural spectacle becomes personal — "my sun" shone triumphantly on the speaker for just one early morning before a "region cloud" masked him away.

The poem captures a specific emotional experience: the joy of a relationship at its peak, followed by sudden withdrawal or cooling. The beloved's brief bright moment in the speaker's life is rendered all the more painful by the vividness of what it felt like at its height.

Key Literary Devices

  • Extended metaphor (conceit): The sun-and-cloud imagery sustained across all 14 lines is the poem's structural backbone. The beloved IS the sun; the speaker's world is the landscape illuminated and then darkened.
  • Personification: The sun has a "sovereign eye," a "celestial face," and moves with disgrace — giving it human pride and shame, preparing the reader for the beloved's emotional parallel.
  • Volta: Line 11 ("Even so my sun") marks the pivot from nature description to personal application.
  • Alliteration: "Gilding... green," "forlorn... face" and "sovereign... sun... stain... staineth" in the couplet reinforce the musical texture.

Themes

Shadow and light: The entire poem is built on the contrast between full sun and obscuring cloud — the chapter's governing metaphor. Shadows here are not merely absence of light but active forces of concealment.

Forgiveness and acceptance: The couplet refuses disdain. The speaker's move — "suns of the world may stain when heaven's sun staineth" — universalizes the beloved's lapse into a cosmic pattern, enabling forgiveness without excusing the harm.

Betrayal mitigated by grandeur: The beloved's earlier radiance is so magnificently described that its withdrawal, though painful, feels part of a majestic natural cycle rather than a petty personal slight.
