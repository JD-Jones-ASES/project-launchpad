Study Guide: Sonnet CXLVI

Summary and Meaning

Sonnet CXLVI stands apart from its neighbors as a pure spiritual meditation addressed not to the Dark Lady but to the speaker's own soul. Employing the ancient body/soul dualism, the speaker scolds his soul for investing too heavily in the "fading mansion" of the body — adorning outward walls while starving the inner life. The argument proceeds through a series of pointed economic metaphors: the soul should sell "hours of dross" (wasted time on the body) to purchase "terms divine." The couplet delivers one of the most celebrated paradoxes in the sequence: the soul, by feeding on Death (through spiritual nourishment at the body's expense), will ultimately outlast Death itself — "Death once dead, there's no more dying then."

Key Poetic Devices

  • Apostrophe: the entire sonnet addresses the speaker's own soul
  • Economic metaphor: "buy," "sell," "lease," "revenues," "cost" — spiritual investment framed as financial management
  • "Fading mansion": the body as a temporary, decaying house for the soul
  • Chiasmus / paradox in the couplet: feeding on Death ultimately kills Death — a triumphant logical reversal
  • Textual crux: line 2's repeated "My sinful earth" is almost certainly a compositor's error; editors supply various emendations

Central Themes

Soul vs. body: The poem advocates subordinating bodily care to spiritual cultivation, drawing on both Platonic and Christian traditions. Moral struggle: The soul is indicted for colluding with the body's vanity — a form of spiritual negligence. Death and immortality: The couplet promises that investing in the soul defeats death utterly, not merely surviving it. In the context of the Dark Lady sequence, this sonnet represents the speaker's only moment of genuine spiritual detachment — a turning of attention entirely away from erotic obsession toward eternal life.
