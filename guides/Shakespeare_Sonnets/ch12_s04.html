CHAPTER 12, SECTION 4: SONNETS 137-141 - The Dark Lady: Desire

Overview

This section delves into four sonnets (137-140) from Shakespeare's "Dark Lady" sequence, exploring the speaker's anguished and conflicted desire. These poems move beyond simple infatuation, portraying a love characterized by self-delusion, mutual deception, and profound suffering. The speaker grapples with the paradox of clearly seeing his mistress's flaws and infidelity yet remaining bound by an irresistible, corrupting desire.

Key Concepts

  • Love's Corrupted Vision and Epistemological Failure: The speaker's eyes and judgment are warped by desire, causing him to choose "the worst" (a promiscuous woman) as "the best." This isn't just traditional blindness of love, but a conscious moral and intellectual failing.
  • Mutual Deception as Relational Glue: In Sonnet 138, the speaker and the Dark Lady engage in a deliberate, unspoken agreement to believe each other's lies, creating a fragile equilibrium based on flattery and avoidance of "simple truth."
  • The Paradox of Cruelty: The speaker's suffering reaches a point where he prefers direct, honest cruelty from his mistress over her subtle, indirect manipulations. He desires an end to his torment, even if it means outright destruction.
  • Pragmatism and Reputation: Sonnet 140 reveals the speaker's surprising concern for public perception. He warns the Dark Lady against overt infidelity, not solely for his emotional well-being, but also to protect her reputation and prevent himself from descending into madness and slander.
  • The Dark Lady's Dominance and Promiscuity: These sonnets firmly establish the Dark Lady as a figure of irresistible, yet destructive, desire, characterized by infidelity and a manipulative nature that holds the speaker captive.

Important Terms

  • "Anchor'd in the bay where all men ride" (137): A striking metaphor implying the Dark Lady is a woman of easy virtue, visited by many men, and the speaker's gaze is fixed there despite this knowledge.
  • "A several plot" / "common place" (137): "A several plot" is privately owned land, contrasted with "the wide world's common place," a public space or common land, carrying the bawdy implication that the mistress is shared by many.
  • "False plague" (137): A deadly epidemic or affliction of falsehood and deception, indicating the speaker views his infatuation as a severe, contagious, and potentially mortal threat.
  • "Made of truth" (138): The Dark Lady's claim that she is entirely composed of faithfulness and absolute fidelity, which the speaker knows is a lie.
  • "Untutor'd youth" (138): A young man lacking worldly experience or knowledge of deception; the role the speaker pretends to play to flatter his mistress.
  • "Love's best habit is in seeming trust" (138): Love's most effective guise or habitual practice is the performance of trust, even if the trust is not genuine. "Habit" refers to both clothing (a disguise) and customary behavior.
  • "I lie with her, and she with me" (138): The powerful double entendre in the closing couplet, meaning both to tell falsehoods and to engage in sexual intimacy. Both meanings are fully intended and intertwined.
  • "Justify the wrong" (139): To provide a defense, excuse, or explanation for the injury or injustice she inflicts upon him. The speaker does not want to be forced to excuse her behavior.
  • "Slay me not by art" (139): Do not kill or torment me through cunning, skill, or artifice – specifically, through subtle cruelty like glancing at other men. The speaker prefers honest, direct assault.
  • "Tongue-tied patience" (140): The speaker's suffering patience, which has rendered him silent and unable to express his pain directly.
  • "Pity-wanting pain" (140): A pain so profound and overwhelming that it is devoid of any pity or compassion, either from the mistress or from anyone witnessing his suffering.
  • "Ill-wresting world" (140): A world that tends to misinterpret, distort, or twist things negatively, making it fertile ground for rumors and slander.
  • "Bear thine eyes straight, though thy proud heart go wide" (140): A pragmatic plea for the Dark Lady to maintain an outward appearance of fidelity ("bear thine eyes straight") even if her heart is unfaithful ("her proud heart go wide"), to prevent public scandal.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet CXXXVII: "Thou blind fool, Love"

This sonnet marks a shift to a darker, more tormented register, moving away from the playful tone of previous sonnets.

  • Accusation of Love: The speaker directly addresses Cupid, personified as "Thou blind fool, Love," accusing him of corrupting his eyesight. His eyes "behold, and see not what they see," indicating a deep epistemological failure – he knows what beauty is but is compelled to choose "the worst."
  • The Dark Lady's Promiscuity: The sonnet uses stark metaphors to describe the mistress's lack of fidelity. His eyes are "anchor'd in the bay where all men ride," a clear and barbed reference to her promiscuity. His heart, foolishly, thinks she is "a several plot" (his exclusive property) when he knows she is "the wide world's common place" (publicly shared).
  • Self-Betrayal and Delusion: The central theme is the speaker's clear-eyed recognition of his own delusion. He sees the truth of her character but is unable to escape his attraction. The final couplet confesses that both his "heart and eyes have err'd" in matters of "plain fact" and are now "transferr'd" to this "false plague," emphasizing the deceptive and destructive nature of his infatuation.
  • Connections: This sonnet links to the broader theme of love's traditional blindness, but elevates it to a moral failing where perception is "corrupt" rather than merely dazzled. It resonates with other sonnets (e.g., 131, 141, 148) that explore the gap between perception and judgment, and anticipates the tragic blindness of obsessive desire seen in Shakespeare's plays (e.g., Othello, Leontes).

Sonnet CXXXVIII: "When my love swears that she is made of truth"

This sonnet offers a devastatingly honest and unsentimental portrait of a relationship sustained by mutual deception.

  • The Core Deception: The mistress claims fidelity ("made of truth"), which the speaker knows is a lie. He, in turn, pretends to believe her, playing the part of an "untutor'd youth" to flatter her vanity.
  • Reciprocal Pretense: This deception is mutual. She also pretends not to notice his advancing age ("my days are past the best") because it flatters him. Both partners consciously suppress the "simple truth" – her infidelity and his age.
  • "Love's best habit": The speaker observes that "love's best habit is in seeming trust," suggesting that the performance or outward appearance of trust is crucial for maintaining their relationship, even when genuine trust is absent. "Habit" here cleverly puns on both clothing (a disguise) and customary practice.
  • The Double Meaning of "Lie": The climactic couplet, "Therefore I lie with her, and she with me, / And in our faults by lies we flatter'd be," masterfully uses the word "lie" with a dual meaning. It signifies both telling falsehoods and engaging in sexual intimacy. This demonstrates that their entire relationship – emotional and physical – is built upon a foundation of shared untruths and flattering deceptions.
  • Context of Revision: The existence of an earlier version in The Passionate Pilgrim (1599) with "I smiling credit her false-speaking tongue" versus the 1609 Quarto's "Simply I credit" highlights Shakespeare's careful revision process. The shift from "smiling" (self-consciously amused) to "simply" (complicit, resigned) changes the tone from wry amusement to a more poignant acceptance of their flawed reality.
  • Connections: This sonnet stands in stark contrast to the ideal, unchanging love depicted in Sonnet 116, presenting a more realistic, lived accommodation with imperfection. It also contributes to the overarching theme of truth and falsehood explored throughout the Dark Lady sequence. The dynamic of mutual knowing-and-pretending is echoed in plays like Much Ado About Nothing and, more darkly, in Othello.

Sonnet CXXXIX: "O! call not me to justify the wrong"

This sonnet is a plea for direct, honest cruelty rather than the subtle, torturous pain inflicted by the Dark Lady's wandering eye.

  • The Speaker's Plea: The speaker asks his mistress not to make him "justify the wrong" she does him. He wants her to own her cruelty rather than forcing him to excuse it.
  • Two Modes of Cruelty: He distinguishes between open, direct force ("use power with power") and subtle manipulation ("slay me not by art"). He prefers the former, as her glancing at other men ("glance thine eye aside") while in his sight is a more insidious wound. He has no "o'erpress'd defence" left against her might.
  • Love as Torture: The speaker implies that her indirect cruelty – wounding him with her eye, not her tongue – is a form of torture, a slow, agonizing death. He begs her to tell him directly that she loves someone else rather than making him witness her disloyalty.
  • Masochistic Reversal: The couplet presents a dramatic reversal. Since he is already "near slain" by her glances, he asks her to "Kill me outright with looks, and rid my pain." This expresses a desire for mercy through decisive destruction, preferring an end to the prolonged agony.
  • Connections: This poem draws on the Petrarchan tradition of the beloved as a cruel mistress, but inverts it: the Dark Lady's cruelty stems from her excess and promiscuity, not her chastity. The masochistic logic of desiring outright death as relief from pain prefigures the speaker's enslavement in Sonnet 141, where senses condemn but the heart remains tied.

Sonnet CXL: "Be wise as thou art cruel"

This sonnet presents an unusual, pragmatic warning from the speaker to his mistress, driven by concern for reputation and the threat of his own madness.

  • Pragmatic Advice: The speaker advises the Dark Lady to be "wise as thou art cruel." He warns her not to push his "tongue-tied patience" too far with "too much disdain," lest his sorrow find words to express his "pity-wanting pain."
  • The Threat of Slander: The core of his warning is that if driven to despair, he might go "mad" and "speak ill of thee." He notes that in "this ill-wresting world," "Mad slanderers by mad ears believed be," implying that gossip and negative reputation spread easily and can cause real damage.
  • Controlling Her Public Actions: He suggests that if he were to "teach thee wit" (wisdom), it would be "better...though not to love, yet love to tell me so." He compares this to "testy sick men" who want only to hear good news, even if false. The goal is to avoid the public spectacle of his despair.
  • Protecting Reputation: The speaker's primary concern here shifts from his own emotional torment to the potential public scandal. He wants to prevent himself from becoming a slanderer and, by extension, to protect her (and his own) reputation.
  • The Plea for Outward Decorum: The final couplet encapsulates his pragmatic request: "That I may not be so, nor thou belied, / Bear thine eyes straight, though thy proud heart go wide." He asks her to maintain an outward appearance of fidelity and directness ("Bear thine eyes straight"), even if her heart is unfaithful ("her proud heart go wide"), to avoid public disgrace and his own potential descent into madness and destructive speech.
  • Connections: This sonnet foregrounds the speaker's agency as a poet whose words can, in extremis, become a weapon. It also highlights the societal importance of reputation and the destructive potential of public scandal, a theme frequently explored in Shakespeare's plays. The speaker's concern for public perception marks a slight shift from purely personal anguish to a more calculated consideration of consequences.

Review Questions

  1. Sonnet CXXXVII describes the speaker's eyes as "corrupt by over-partial looks" and his heart/eyes "transferr'd" to a "false plague." How does this portrayal differ from traditional notions of "love's blindness," and what does it suggest about the speaker's agency and self-awareness?
  2. Sonnet CXXXVIII is famous for its depiction of mutual deception. Discuss the various ways both the speaker and the Dark Lady lie to each other, and explore whether their "flatter'd" arrangement can be considered a form of "love" or simply a pragmatic accommodation.
  3. In Sonnet CXXXIX, the speaker asks the Dark Lady to "slay me not by art" but instead "use power with power" or even "Kill me outright with looks." What does this preference for direct cruelty over subtle manipulation reveal about the nature of his suffering and his relationship with her?
  4. Sonnet CXL presents a pragmatic warning: the speaker advises the Dark Lady to "Be wise as thou art cruel." How does his concern for potential slander and public perception shape his advice to her, and what does this tell us about the societal context of their relationship?
  5. Across these four sonnets, the Dark Lady is depicted as promiscuous, manipulative, and cruel. Yet, the speaker remains deeply involved. Analyze the complex interplay of desire, self-delusion, and pragmatic self-interest that keeps the speaker tied to this "false plague."
