Shakespeare's Sonnets 69-73: A Study Guide on Praise, Blame, and Mortality

Overview

This section explores Sonnets 69-73, a sequence that delves into the complexities of love, reputation, aging, and mortality. The poems shift from qualified praise and criticism of the beloved's character to profound expressions of the poet's selflessness and contemplation of his own death, culminating in poignant metaphors for his physical and temporal decline.

Key Concepts

  • The Paradox of Outward vs. Inward Beauty (Sonnet 69): This sonnet highlights a sharp contrast between the beloved's universally admired physical perfection and his perceived "common" or morally base inner character, emphasizing that outward appearance can mask inner flaws.
  • Slander as a Mark of Distinction (Sonnet 70): The poet argues a paradoxical idea: being the target of slander can actually be a testament to one's true worth and beauty, as corrupt forces ("canker vice") naturally attack what is most valuable and "sweet."
  • Selfless Love and Self-Abnegation (Sonnets 71, 72): Driven by profound love, the poet makes an extraordinary request for the beloved to completely forget him after his death. This act of self-erasure aims to spare the beloved grief and protect him from the "wise world's" potential scorn.
  • The Fear of Shame and Worthlessness (Sonnet 72): The poet's intense self-deprecation suggests that his own perceived lack of merit would bring shame upon the beloved if his memory were preserved, leading him to advocate for absolute oblivion.
  • Metaphors of Aging and Mortality (Sonnet 73): Through vivid natural imagery like autumn leaves and fading twilight, the poet illustrates his advancing age and inevitable approach of death, inviting the beloved to witness and understand this decline.
  • Societal Judgment: A recurring concern about how the "wise world" (society) perceives and judges individuals, particularly how it might react to the beloved's mourning for a man deemed unworthy.

Important Terms

  • Mend (Sonnet 69): To improve or repair something deficient; implies that the beloved's outward parts need no improvement even in the most critical imagination.
  • Confound (Sonnet 69): To ruin, destroy, or mix in confusion; here, meaning to negate or demolish praise given in one context by criticism in another.
  • Churls (Sonnet 69): Rude, rough-mannered people; peasants. Shakespeare notes that even such individuals can accurately judge inner character.
  • Rank (Sonnet 69): Offensive in smell; overgrown; coarse. Used metaphorically to describe the unpleasant moral character of the beloved, contrasted with his "fair flower."
  • Common grow (Sonnet 69): To become common; to lower oneself through ordinary, base, or promiscuous behavior, losing what is special about oneself.
  • Suspect (Sonnet 70): Suspicion; a shadow of doubt. Paradoxically presented as a natural accessory or consequence of genuine beauty.
  • Canker vice (Sonnet 70): Corrosive, consuming evil; a disease that destroys plants or living tissue. Used metaphorically for corruption attacking valuable moral qualities.
  • Prime (Sonnet 70): The beginning; the best time; springtime; a period of greatest vigor. Refers to the youth's uncorrupted and excellent early life.
  • Ambush of young days (Sonnet 70): The dangers and temptations inherent in youth, conceived as hidden assailants.
  • Enlarg'd (Sonnet 70): Set free; released from constraint. Describes envy's persistent ability to escape and return.
  • Mourn (Sonnet 71): To grieve; to feel sorrow for.
  • Surly sullen bell (Sonnet 71): The death-knell; the bell tolled to announce a death, personified as having a bitter, brooding character.
  • Vile world (Sonnet 71): A debased, morally worthless, or despicable world, often seen as a source of injustice.
  • Compounded am with clay (Sonnet 71): Dead and decomposed; mixed with and become part of the earth.
  • Rehearse (Sonnet 71): To repeat; to go over; to recite. The poet asks for absolute silence regarding his name.
  • Virtuous lie (Sonnet 72): A falsehood told for a good moral purpose; a kind deception. The poet suggests the beloved invent such a lie to protect his reputation.
  • Niggard truth (Sonnet 72): Truth that is stingy or reluctant in giving praise; an honest verdict that finds nothing worthy.
  • Untrue (Sonnet 72): False; dishonest. Plays on both speaking inaccurately about someone and speaking about an untrue (dishonest/unworthy) person.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet 69: The Flaw Within

  • Outward Perfection: The sonnet opens by acknowledging the beloved's flawless outward appearance, which is admired by everyone, including his enemies. His physical beauty is beyond improvement.
  • Inner Critique: However, the "same tongues" that praise his looks also delve deeper, examining his mind and actions. Here, their judgment changes.
  • Moral Corruption: They add "the rank smell of weeds" to his "fair flower," symbolizing a moral corruption or base character that undermines his external beauty.
  • Direct Reproach: The concluding couplet reveals the poet's direct diagnosis: the beloved's inner qualities ("odour") do not match his outer show because he has "common grown," implying he has lowered himself through inappropriate or base behavior.
  • Shift in Tone: This sonnet marks a significant shift in the poet's address, moving from pure adoration to a gentle yet firm critique of the beloved's character.

Sonnet 70: Slander's True Mark

  • Defense of the Beloved: This sonnet serves as a defense of the youth against the criticism implied in Sonnet 69, arguing that being slandered is not a flaw but a mark of distinction.
  • Beauty Attracts Suspicion: The poet claims that slander always targets the beautiful and worthy. "The ornament of beauty is suspect," meaning suspicion is a natural accompaniment to genuine beauty.
  • Proof of Worth: If the youth remains morally "good," slander only "doth but approve / Thy worth the greater," because "canker vice" (corruption) naturally attacks the most valuable and "sweetest buds."
  • Overcoming Temptation: The beloved is praised for having survived "the ambush of young days," suggesting he has either avoided or triumphed over youthful temptations.
  • Inevitable Envy: The couplet concedes that complete praise cannot entirely suppress envy. Some "suspect of ill" will always linger; otherwise, his virtues would be so overwhelming as to capture "kingdoms of hearts."

Sonnet 71: A Selfless Request for Oblivion

  • Plea to Be Forgotten: The poet makes an astonishingly selfless request: the beloved should mourn him only briefly, as long as the "surly sullen bell" tolls, and then forget him entirely.
  • Love's Sacrifice: He asks the beloved to even forget the "hand that writ it" (his poems) if remembering him would cause grief. This is an ultimate act of love, prioritizing the beloved's emotional well-being over his own memory.
  • Protection from Scorn: The final reason is practical: the poet fears that if the beloved mourned excessively for a man of no perceived worth, the "wise world" would mock them both after his death.
  • Emotional Depth: This sonnet highlights a tension within the sequence: while other poems claim immortalization through verse, this one expresses a willingness to be erased from memory for the sake of the beloved's peace and reputation.

Sonnet 72: The "Virtuous Lie"

  • Extreme Self-Deprecation: Deepening the self-effacement of Sonnet 71, the poet urges the beloved to "forget me quite" after his death, denying that he ever possessed any merit worth loving.
  • Inventing Praise: If questioned about his love for the poet, the beloved should "devise some virtuous lie" to praise him. This is because "niggard truth" (reluctant, stingy truth) would find nothing worthy to say, leading to embarrassment.
  • Shared Shame: The poet insists his name should be "buried where my body is," to prevent shame from falling upon both himself and the beloved.
  • Worthlessness of the Poet: He states, "I am shamed by that which I bring forth," suggesting his work or his very being is unworthy, and implies the beloved should feel shame for loving "things nothing worth."
  • Rhetorical Ambiguity: This sonnet presents a complex rhetorical challenge: it could be read as extreme humility, or as a sophisticated way to provoke the beloved into a passionate reaffirmation of his love and the poet's worth.

Sonnet 73: The Signs of Decline

  • Metaphors of Aging: The poet uses two vivid natural metaphors to convey his advancing age and approach of death to the beloved:
    • Autumn: He compares himself to "that time of year" when "yellow leaves, or none, or few, do hang / Upon those boughs," which are like "Bare ruin’d choirs, where late the sweet birds sang." This evokes images of decay, silence, and the passing of vibrant life from a once flourishing state.
    • Twilight: He then likens himself to "the twilight of such day / As after sunset fadeth in the west; / Which by and by black night doth take away, / Death’s second self." This illustrates the gradual fading of light into darkness, with night explicitly personified as a form of death.
  • Beloved as Witness: The repeated phrases "thou mayst in me behold" and "In me thou see’st" directly address the beloved, inviting him to observe and acknowledge the poet's physical and temporal decline.

Review Questions

  1. How does Sonnet 69 establish a contrast between the beloved's external beauty and internal character? What specific imagery is used to highlight this disparity, and what does the phrase "thou dost common grow" imply about the beloved's actions?
  2. In Sonnet 70, the poet presents a counter-intuitive argument about blame and slander. Explain his reasoning for why "That thou art blam’d shall not be thy defect" and how he uses the "canker vice" metaphor to support this claim.
  3. Sonnets 71 and 72 explore the theme of the poet's death and memory. Compare and contrast the specific requests the poet makes of the beloved in these two sonnets. What emotional and practical reasons does he give for desiring to be forgotten?
  4. Sonnet 72 introduces the concept of a "virtuous lie." Explain what the poet means by this phrase and why he suggests the beloved might need to employ it. How does this suggestion reflect the poet's anxieties about his own worth and the beloved's reputation?
  5. Analyze the primary metaphors of aging and mortality presented in the provided lines of Sonnet 73 (autumn leaves and twilight). How do these images work together to convey the poet's sense of his approaching end, and what effect might this have on the beloved, who is asked to "behold" these changes?
