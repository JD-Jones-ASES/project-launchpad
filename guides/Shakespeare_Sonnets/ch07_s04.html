Study Guide: Sonnet LXII

Summary

Sonnet LXII confesses to the sin of self-love with brazen excess before deflating it entirely through the mirror. The octave is almost comically vain: the speaker declares that no face is as gracious as his, no shape as true, and that he surpasses all others in worth. This vanity is declared incurable, "grounded inward" in his very heart. Then the mirror intervenes. The glass shows the speaker "myself indeed" — the word "indeed" carrying full weight — and the face it reveals is "Beated and chopp'd with tanned antiquity." The sestet performs the correction: self-love thus expressed would be sheer iniquity. The couplet resolves the paradox elegantly: it is not himself but the beloved that the speaker praises when he thinks he is loving himself — he "paints his age" with the beloved's youthful beauty.

Key Themes

  • Self-love and its correction: Vanity is redeemed by being revealed as love for the beloved.
  • The mirror as truth: The glass is the instrument of honest self-knowledge against delusion.
  • Age and beauty: The speaker's aged face, revealed honestly, forces a reorientation of love.

Key Literary Devices

  • Anaphora: "And all my soul, and all my every part" builds the totality of self-love's possession.
  • Volta: "But when" at line 11 introduces the deflating reality of the mirror.
  • Metaphor: "Painting my age with beauty of thy days" — the beloved's youth is a paint applied over the speaker's aged self.

Vocabulary

  • Possesseth: Takes complete hold of, dominates
  • Gracious: Beautiful, pleasing in appearance
  • Beated and chopp'd: Beaten down and roughened, weather-worn
  • Antiquity: Old age; a former, ancient era
  • Iniquity: Wickedness, moral wrongdoing
