Sonnet CXV — Study Guide

Overview

Sonnet CXV is a sophisticated philosophical poem about the impossibility of declaring love's peak. The speaker confesses that his earlier lines lied when they said he could not love the beloved more — because love has continued to grow. But the poem then defends those earlier lines: he could not have known the love would deepen further, because Time's accidents are unforeseeable.

Meaning

Earlier declarations of maximum love were lies — not from insincerity, but because love has since burned even clearer. The explanation: Time's "million'd accidents" creep between vows, tan beauty, blunt intentions, and divert strong minds. At the moment of writing, the speaker was certain of his love but could only be "certain o'er incertainty" — he crowned the present, not knowing the future. The couplet resolves everything with a memorable metaphor: love is a babe, still growing. To claim it has reached its height is to deny its continuing growth.

Literary Devices

  • Paradox: Earlier declarations of maximum love are now retrospectively false because love has grown beyond them.
  • Metaphor: Time as a tanner, blunter, and diverter of beauty and vows.
  • Simile: Love as a growing infant — never at its final stature.
  • Philosophical argument: The sonnet is structured as a formal logical problem about temporal certainty and love's growth.

Themes of Constancy and Faithfulness

This sonnet redefines constancy as a dynamic rather than static quality. True faithfulness is not a fixed point but a continuing growth. Any declaration that love has reached its maximum is necessarily premature. Constancy here means endless deepening — a faithfulness that grows rather than merely holds steady.
