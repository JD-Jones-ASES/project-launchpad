Sonnets 18-22: Praise and Beauty

Overview

This section delves into three of Shakespeare's most iconic sonnets—18, 19, and 20—exploring themes of immortalizing beauty, defying time's destructive power, and the complex nature of admiration. Sonnet 18 establishes the power of poetry to grant eternal life to the beloved's beauty, while Sonnet 19 directly challenges Time to do its worst, only to declare the triumph of verse. Sonnet 20, the most debated, explores the beloved's unique blend of masculine and feminine qualities and the poet's distinct form of admiration.

Key Concepts

  • Immortality Through Verse: A central theme across these sonnets, asserting that the beloved's beauty and essence can escape the decay of nature and time by being preserved within the lines of poetry. The act of writing and reading the poem grants eternal life.
  • Defiance of Time: Shakespeare personifies Time as a destructive force (Sonnet 19), but ultimately positions the poet's art as capable of resisting or even triumphing over its ravages. This defiance is both a philosophical argument and a confident boast.
  • Nature vs. Art: The sonnets often contrast the fleeting, imperfect beauty of the natural world (e.g., summer's day) with the enduring perfection that art (poetry) can bestow. Nature decays by chance or course, but art provides an "eternal summer."
  • The Blazon and Its Undoing: Sonnet 18 playfully subverts the traditional Elizabethan poetic form of the "blazon" (cataloging physical features). Instead of praising specific attributes by comparing them to nature, Shakespeare praises the beloved by demonstrating nature's inferiority.
  • Complex Admiration and Desire: Sonnet 20 introduces the intricate and often debated nature of the poet's relationship with the Fair Youth. It explores an intense, non-sexual devotion, acknowledging the beloved's captivating qualities while simultaneously defining the boundaries of the poet's desire.

Important Terms

  • temperate: Moderate, evenly balanced; implies a philosophical ideal of temperance and proper balance of qualities.
  • lease: A contract granting temporary possession; used metaphorically for summer's limited duration.
  • complexion: In Elizabethan times, referred to both outward appearance and the blend of humors determining temperament.
  • eternal lines: The poem's enduring verses, which weave the beloved into permanence beyond the reach of time.
  • ow'st: Own'st; possessest.
  • brag: To boast.
  • devouring: Consuming; destroying greedily, especially referring to Time's destructive power.
  • phoenix: A mythical bird that burns and rises from its ashes, symbolizing immortality and renewal. By allowing Time to destroy even the phoenix, Shakespeare elevates Time's power, making the poetic claim to immortality even more audacious.
  • antique pen: A pun on "antique" (ancient, old) and "antic" (grotesque, clownish); refers to the wrinkles Time inscribes on a face, contrasting it with the poet's serious pen.
  • beauty's pattern: A model or exemplar of beauty; the beloved is the ideal by which future generations will understand beauty.
  • master mistress: A compound term in Sonnet 20 resisting easy categorization, indicating both male gender and the emotional role of the beloved as an object of devotion.
  • acquainted: Familiar; known.
  • hue: Color, appearance, complexion; also a pun on "Hugh" or "hew," possibly suggesting a proper name.
  • controlling: Dominating; having power over.
  • fell a-doting: To fall in doting love; to become besotted.
  • one thing to my purpose nothing: A biological feature (the penis) that makes sexual consummation between the male poet and the male youth impossible or not desired for the poet's specific form of love.
  • prick'd thee out: Chose or selected you; also contains a bawdy secondary meaning relating to the male sexual organ, exploited for comic effect in the couplet.

Detailed Notes

Sonnet XVIII: "Shall I compare thee to a summer’s day?"

Main Idea: This sonnet begins with a question that is immediately undermined by the poem's argument. Shakespeare systematically proves that a summer's day, while lovely, is ultimately flawed and fleeting, whereas the beloved's beauty is constant and eternal, especially when preserved in the poet's verse.

Analysis: The opening line's rhetorical question, "Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?", sets up a comparison that the poem then deconstructs. The initial quatrains list the imperfections of summer:

  • Its beauty is inconsistent ("Rough winds do shake the darling buds of May").
  • Its duration is brief ("summer's lease hath all too short a date").
  • Its beauty can be extreme ("Sometime too hot the eye of heaven shines").
  • Its beauty can be obscured ("And often is his gold complexion dimm'd").
  • All beauty in nature is subject to decay ("every fair from fair sometime declines, / By chance, or nature's changing course untrimm'd").

The volta (the turn in thought) occurs powerfully at line 9 with "But thy eternal summer shall not fade." This marks the poet's triumphant counter-claim. The beloved's "eternal summer" is not subject to nature's decay because it is preserved by the poem. Death cannot claim the beloved, as the "eternal lines" of the verse will keep them alive.

The couplet ("So long as men can breathe, or eyes can see, / So long lives this, and this gives life to thee") provides a confident conclusion, asserting the poem's own power to immortalize. The poem itself is the instrument of preservation, and its continued reading across centuries validates the poet's boast.

Context and Connections:

  • The Blazon: This sonnet is a subtle subversion of the traditional blazon, where poets would catalog physical features. Instead of comparing the beloved to nature, Shakespeare shows how the beloved surpasses nature, transforming a praise poem into a meditation on the enduring power of art.
  • Immortality Theme: Sonnet XVIII serves as a foundational statement for the recurring theme of verse immortalizing the beloved, echoed in sonnets like 55, 60, and 65.
  • Classical Precursors: Shakespeare draws inspiration from classical poets like Ovid, who also boasted of his work's survival, but Shakespeare makes his claim more intimate and addressed to a specific individual.

Sonnet XIX: "Devouring Time, blunt thou the lion’s paws"

Main Idea: Sonnet XIX adopts a more combative approach to time than Sonnet XVIII. The poet directly commands Time, granting it sweeping destructive powers over the natural world, only to then issue one crucial prohibition: Time must not mar the beloved's beauty. Ultimately, the poet declares that his verse will preserve the youth's eternal youth.

Analysis: The sonnet opens with a powerful invocation, "Devouring Time," setting a tone of confrontation. The first two quatrains catalog Time's destructive capabilities, presenting them in almost mythological terms:

  • Time can "blunt thou the lion's paws" and make "earth devour her own sweet brood."
  • It can "Pluck the keen teeth from the fierce tiger's jaws."
  • It can even destroy the mythical phoenix, a symbol of immortality, by burning it "in her blood."
  • Time can manipulate the seasons ("Make glad and sorry seasons as thou fleets").

The volta arrives with "But I forbid thee one most heinous crime." After granting Time such vast dominion, the poet draws a line, prohibiting Time from carving wrinkles ("lines") into the beloved's "fair brow" or causing them to fade. The beloved is to remain "untainted," serving as "beauty's pattern to succeeding men."

The couplet ("Yet do thy worst, old Time; despite thy wrong, / My love shall in my verse ever live young") offers a final, confident defiance. Despite Time's ultimate power, the poet asserts that his verse will circumvent its ravages, ensuring the beloved's eternal youth and beauty through art.

Context and Connections:

  • Dialogue with Sonnet XVIII: Sonnet XIX is a direct companion to Sonnet XVIII. While the first sonnet sidesteps Time's power by emphasizing the poem's existence, Sonnet XIX directly challenges and defies Time.
  • Mythological Allusions: The sonnet draws heavily on classical mythology, particularly Ovid's Metamorphoses, for images of "devouring time" and the phoenix.
  • Extended Meditations on Time: This sonnet's confrontational stance against Time anticipates later, more elaborate meditations on Time's destructive nature in sonnets like 60 and 65.

Sonnet XX: "A woman’s face with nature’s own hand painted"

Main Idea: Sonnet XX is a unique and often debated poem, addressing the Fair Youth as "the master mistress of my passion." It praises the youth's exquisite beauty, which combines feminine grace with masculine constancy, while simultaneously clarifying the non-sexual nature of the poet's admiration due to a specific biological "addition" made by Nature.

Analysis: The sonnet describes the beloved's extraordinary beauty, blending traditionally feminine and masculine attributes:

  • Feminine Beauty: "A woman's face with nature's own hand painted," "An eye more bright than theirs."
  • Masculine Constancy: "A woman's gentle heart, but not acquainted / With shifting change, as is false women's fashion." The beloved is praised for lacking the perceived inconstancy attributed to women.
  • Magnetic Charm: "A man in hue all hues in his controlling, / Which steals men's eyes and women's souls amazeth." The beloved's captivating appearance (hue/Hugh) mesmerizes both men and women.

The core of the sonnet's complexity lies in lines 9-12, where the poet explains the beloved's origin story:

  • "And for a woman wert thou first created": Implies the youth's initial design was feminine.
  • "Till Nature, as she wrought thee, fell a-doting, / And by addition me of thee defeated": Nature, falling in love with her own creation, added a "thing" (the penis) that frustrated the poet's sexual desire.
  • "By adding one thing to my purpose nothing": This line humorously and candidly states that this biological addition renders physical consummation with the poet impossible or undesired for his specific "purpose."

The couplet ("But since she prick'd thee out for women's pleasure, / Mine be thy love and thy love's use their treasure") delivers a witty and elegant resolution. Since Nature designated the youth for women's physical pleasure (a double entendre with "prick'd thee out"), the poet claims the youth's platonic love, leaving the physical "use" of that love to women.

Context and Connections:

  • The "Master Mistress" Debate: This sonnet is central to critical discussions about the nature of the poet's love for the Fair Youth. Some see it as evidence of homoerotic desire that is formally renounced, others as a sophisticated compliment within the conventions of intense male friendship, and still others as deliberately ambiguous.
  • Gender and Identity: The poem plays with gender roles and attributes, highlighting the beloved's unique qualities that transcend conventional categories.
  • Wit and Wordplay: Shakespeare uses extensive wordplay, including puns on "hue" and "prick'd," to convey a complex blend of admiration, humor, and a clear definition of the relationship's boundaries.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast the strategies Shakespeare employs in Sonnet 18 and Sonnet 19 to address the theme of time's destructive power. How do the poet's approaches to securing immortality differ in these two poems?
  2. How does Shakespeare subvert or play with the traditional Elizabethan "blazon" in Sonnet 18? What effect does this indirect method of praise have on our understanding of the beloved's beauty?
  3. Discuss the significance of the "volta" (turn in thought) in Sonnet 18 and Sonnet 19. How does the shift at line 9 or "But I forbid thee..." fundamentally change the direction and argument of each poem?
  4. Analyze the complexity of the beloved's portrayal in Sonnet 20. In what ways is the youth presented as embodying both "feminine" and "masculine" qualities, and how does this contribute to the concept of "the master mistress of my passion"?
  5. Sonnet 20 concludes by defining the nature of the poet's love for the Fair Youth. Explain the poet's distinction between "love" and "love's use" in the final couplet, and discuss how this shapes our understanding of the relationship presented in the sonnet.
