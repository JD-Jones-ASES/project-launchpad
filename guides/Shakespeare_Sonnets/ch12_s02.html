Study Guide: The Dark Lady: Desire - Sonnets 127-129

Overview

This section introduces Shakespeare's "Dark Lady" sequence, focusing on Sonnets 127, 128, and 129. It explores a profound redefinition of beauty, challenging Elizabethan ideals and the use of cosmetics in Sonnet 127. Sonnet 128 offers a playful look at jealousy and desire through a musical scene, while Sonnet 129 delivers a stark, unflinching analysis of lust's destructive and irresistible nature. Together, these sonnets delve into themes of authenticity, desire, and the complex realities of human passion.

Key Concepts

  • Reversal of Elizabethan Beauty Standards: Sonnet 127 dramatically challenges the conventional ideal of fair (pale) beauty, asserting that "black" (dark coloring) has now become the "successive heir" to beauty, largely due to the widespread use of artificial cosmetics that counterfeit natural fairness.
  • The Deception of Cosmetics: Shakespeare critiques the use of "Art's false borrowed face," arguing that cosmetics have "profaned" and discredited true natural beauty. This aligns with broader Elizabethan anxieties about artifice and authenticity.
  • Authenticity vs. Artifice: A pervasive theme, particularly in Sonnet 127, where natural dark beauty is presented as honest in contrast to the false fairness achieved through makeup. This concern extends to deeper anxieties about pretense in a world of performance.
  • Desire and Playful Jealousy: Sonnet 128 uses a lighthearted, witty tone to explore the speaker's desire for the Dark Lady, expressed through his humorous jealousy of the inanimate keys of a musical instrument. It highlights music as a catalyst for erotic yearning.
  • The Anatomy of Lust: Sonnet 129 offers a relentless, almost clinical, examination of lust, detailing its destructive stages from intense pursuit to immediate post-gratification disgust. It portrays lust as a "bliss in proof" that immediately becomes "a very woe."
  • The Psychological Paradox of Human Desire: Sonnet 129 culminates in the profound observation that humanity universally understands the destructive nature of lust ("All this the world well knows"), yet remains powerless to "shun the heaven that leads men to this hell," revealing a core conflict between knowledge and compulsion.

Important Terms

  • fair: Used with a double meaning throughout Sonnet 127: both beautiful and light-complexioned. Shakespeare highlights the collapse of the natural equation between light skin and loveliness.
  • successive heir: A legitimate heir who inherits by right of succession. Shakespeare uses this to present natural dark beauty as the rightful inheritor of beauty's throne, usurped by artificial fairness.
  • bower: A lady's private chamber or a shaded garden retreat, here symbolizing the sacred, sheltered place that true beauty should occupy but has been driven from.
  • fairing the foul with Art's false borrowed face: A phrase describing the use of cosmetics to make the ugly beautiful through artificial means. Art refers to skill or craft, and borrowed emphasizes the lack of genuine beauty.
  • raven black: Refers to the deep black color of a raven, a traditional symbol of darkness. Here, it describes the mistress's eyes, presented as authentically mourning the corruption of beauty.
  • jacks: The keys of a virginal or harpsichord. Shakespeare also uses the colloquial sense of impudent fellows in "saucy jacks," personifying the keys as presumptuous rivals.
  • wiry concord: The harmonious sound produced by the wire strings of a keyboard instrument. Concord means musical agreement; wiry evokes the physical strings.
  • inward of thy hand: The sensitive inner palm of the hand, which the speaker imagines the keys kissing intimately.
  • chips: Small pieces of wood, used dismissively to describe the keys of the instrument, ironically highlighting their perceived advantage over the speaker's "living lips."
  • tickled: Lightly touched, but also carrying an erotic suggestion of pleasurable sensation in Sonnet 128.
  • expense of spirit: The spending or exhausting of vital energy. In Elizabethan physiology, spirit referred to the animating vital force, and expense meant expenditure. Sexual activity was believed to diminish this force.
  • waste of shame: A deliberate ambiguity meaning both a squandering that results in shame and a shameful desert—a ruined landscape left by the expenditure of vital energy.
  • perjur'd: Having broken a solemn oath or guilty of false swearing. Lust is depicted as a moral criminal, guilty of multiple offenses before gratification.
  • past reason hunted: Pursued beyond the bounds of rational thought, illustrating how desire overrides deliberation and good judgment.
  • a bliss in proof, and proved, a very woe: In proof means while being tested or experienced; proved means once the experience is over. This chiastic structure (bliss becoming woe) dramatizes the reversal.
  • heaven / hell: The final words of Sonnet 129, enclosing the trap of lust: it presents itself as heaven but inevitably leads to hell, a path willingly chosen.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to the Dark Lady Sequence (Sonnets 127-131)

This section begins the "Dark Lady" sequence, which deviates sharply from the preceding "Fair Youth" sonnets. The Dark Lady is often portrayed as enigmatic, passionate, and challenging conventional beauty and morality. The sonnets in this sequence frequently explore themes of desire, betrayal, jealousy, and the complexities of love that go beyond idealized adoration.

Sonnet CXXVII: Reimagining Beauty

  • Opening a New Sequence: This sonnet marks the beginning of the Dark Lady sequence, immediately setting a transgressive tone by challenging established beauty norms.
  • Reversal of Elizabethan Beauty Standards: The sonnet argues that in "the old age," dark coloring ("black") was not considered beautiful. However, in the present, "black" is now "beauty's successive heir," inheriting the place formerly held by "fair" beauty.
  • Critique of Cosmetics: Shakespeare attributes this reversal to the widespread use of cosmetics. Artificial means ("Art's false borrowed face") allow anyone to "fair the foul," counterfeiting the conventionally prized pale complexion. This practice has "slandered" and "profaned" authentic beauty, reducing it to a "bastard shame."
  • The Dark Lady's Authentic Beauty: Into this corrupt world, the Dark Lady's "raven black" eyes appear. They are presented as mourners for the corruption of genuine beauty. Her natural darkness is implicitly a form of honesty in a world of artificiality.
  • Wry Conclusion: The couplet delivers a clever twist: the Lady's eyes mourn so "becomingly" that "every tongue says beauty should look so." This suggests that her natural, dark beauty is so compelling it redefines the standard, exposing the old one as a fraud.
  • Elizabethan Beauty Standards and Cosmetics Culture: In Shakespeare's time, the ideal female beauty was pale skin, light hair, and rosy cheeks, often codified in Petrarchan poetry. Darker complexions were associated with outdoor labor and lower class. The Dark Lady, being "non-fair," transgressed this standard. Cosmetics like ceruse (white lead) were widely used, sparking moralistic denunciations of their deceptive and toxic nature. Shakespeare's critique of "Art's false borrowed face" participates in this ongoing cultural debate, positioning the Dark Lady's natural darkness as a symbol of truth.
  • Connections: Sonnet 127's argument about authentic beauty displaced by artificial imitation is echoed and extended in Sonnet 130 (the famous anti-blazon, which affirms love despite the mistress's deviation from Petrarchan ideals). The theme of cosmetic deception also links to Sonnet 68 in the Fair Youth sequence, where Shakespeare laments "bastard signs of fair" stolen from the dead, reflecting a broader anxiety about authenticity.

Sonnet CXXVIII: Playful Jealousy and Musical Desire

  • A Shift in Tone: Following the weighty aesthetic arguments of Sonnet 127, Sonnet 128 offers a lighter, more playful, and erotic register.
  • The Scene: The speaker watches the Dark Lady play the virginal (a keyboard instrument), observing her "sweet fingers" moving over the keys, which produce "wiry concord."
  • Jealousy of Inanimate Objects: The speaker expresses humorous jealousy, not of a rival person, but of the "saucy jacks" (the keys) that "nimble leap, / To kiss the tender inward of thy hand." His own lips, which "should that harvest reap," are left "blushing" and at a distance.
  • Erotic Imagination: The speaker imagines the pleasure of being "tickled" by her touch, wishing his lips could "change their state / And situation with those dancing chips." He contrasts "dead wood" (the keys) with his "living lips," lamenting their disadvantage.
  • The Proposition: The couplet, characteristic of Shakespeare's sonnets, delivers a witty demand: "Since saucy jacks so happy are in this, / Give them thy fingers, me thy lips to kiss." This playful proposal underscores the intense, though lighthearted, erotic desire.
  • Music as a Vehicle for Desire: Here, music isn't a symbol of harmony or a moral lesson (as it sometimes is in other sonnets); instead, it provides the setting for a scene of sexual longing and playful rivalry.
  • Connections: The theme of music appears in other sonnets, such as Sonnet 8, where music teaches the Fair Youth about love and procreation. The topos of envying an inanimate object touching the beloved has precedents in classical love poetry, like Ovid's Amores. The light handling of desire here provides a stark contrast to the darkness of the very next sonnet, 129.

Sonnet CXXIX: The Anatomy of Lust

  • A Non-Love Poem: Sonnet 129 stands apart as a detached, almost clinical, diagnosis of lust, rather than a declaration of love. It is one of Shakespeare's most profound explorations of human appetite.
  • The Stages of Lust: The poem meticulously enumerates the destructive trajectory of lust:
    • Before gratification: Lust is "perjur'd, murderous, bloody, full of blame, / Savage, extreme, rude, cruel, not to trust." It is "past reason hunted."
    • During gratification: It is a brief moment of "bliss in proof."
    • After gratification: It is "despised straight," "past reason hated," and becomes "a very woe." It is "behind a dream"—something fleeting and regretted.
  • Lust as a Baited Trap: The central metaphor is of a "swallow'd bait, / On purpose laid to make the taker mad." Lust ensnares its victim, driving them to madness in both pursuit and possession.
  • The "Expense of Spirit in a Waste of Shame": This opening line encapsulates the sonnet's core idea: sexual desire ("lust in action") is an expenditure of vital energy ("spirit") that results in a profound loss and leaves behind a shameful, ruined state ("waste of shame").
  • The Psychological Paradox: The devastating final couplet reveals the core insight: "All this the world well knows; yet none knows well / To shun the heaven that leads men to this hell." Humanity is fully aware of lust's destructive cycle, yet remains compelled and unable to resist its deceptive allure. This highlights a fundamental and enduring human paradox.
  • Context of Lust, Sin, and Elizabethan Moral Tradition: The critique of lust as irrational and shameful has deep roots in classical philosophy (Aristotle) and Christian theology (Augustine, medieval Seven Deadly Sins). Elizabethan moralists frequently condemned lust. However, Shakespeare's treatment distinguishes itself by its complete absence of moral exhortation or proposed remedies. Instead, it offers an almost detached observation of an irresistible human mechanism, closer to existential honesty than a sermon. The sonnet's insights resonate with Shakespeare's dramatic explorations of passion and its consequences in his plays.

Review Questions

  1. How does Sonnet 127 challenge and redefine Elizabethan beauty standards? What role do cosmetics play in this redefinition, and what larger social anxieties does Shakespeare tap into?
  2. Compare and contrast the speaker's expression of desire in Sonnet 128 with the analysis of lust in Sonnet 129. How do their respective tones and uses of imagery contribute to their different approaches to human passion?
  3. Discuss the concept of "authenticity versus artifice" as presented in Sonnet 127. How does the Dark Lady's natural beauty stand in opposition to the "false borrowed face" created by cosmetics?
  4. In Sonnet 129, Shakespeare meticulously outlines the stages of lust. Explain what the poem suggests about the nature of desire before, during, and after gratification. What is the significance of the final couplet's "heaven that leads men to this hell"?
  5. Consider the introductory nature of Sonnet 127 for the "Dark Lady" sequence. Based on Sonnets 127-129, what themes or aspects of human desire and relationships do you anticipate will be central to this sequence?
