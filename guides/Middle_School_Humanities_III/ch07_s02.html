Chapter 7, Section 2: Maps - A Study Guide

Overview

This section explores how maps serve as crucial tools for understanding the First World War, from the pre-war alliance systems to the post-war reshaping of Europe. It delves into the geographical and technological reasons behind the devastating stalemate on the Western Front, the complex process of redrawing national borders after the collapse of empires, and the profound challenges that arose from the Treaty of Versailles, including Germany's humiliation and the structural weaknesses of the new League of Nations.

Key Concepts

  • Maps as Historical Narrators: Maps provide immediate visual insights into complex historical situations, such as pre-war alliances, the immobility of the Western Front, and the redrawn post-war European landscape.
  • Western Front Stalemate: The paradoxical lack of movement despite intense fighting for four years was primarily due to the mismatch between rapidly industrialized weaponry and outdated Napoleonic-era infantry tactics.
  • Dominance of Defense: New technologies like the machine gun, barbed wire, and heavy artillery made frontal assaults nearly suicidal, trapping armies in defensive trench warfare.
  • Limited Impact of New Weapons: Early tanks and airplanes, though introduced to break the stalemate, were initially too few and unreliable to significantly alter the static nature of the Western Front.
  • Self-Determination vs. Reality: President Wilson's principle of self-determination, while idealistic, proved incredibly difficult to implement in a Europe with intermixed populations and competing historical claims, leading to new ethnic tensions.
  • Post-Empire Europe: The collapse of the German, Austro-Hungarian, Russian, and Ottoman Empires necessitated the creation of numerous new nations, often with diverse and conflicting internal populations.
  • Germany's Humiliation: The terms of the Treaty of Versailles, including the war-guilt clause and reparations, combined with the "stabbed in the back" myth, fostered deep resentment and instability in the new Weimar Republic.
  • League of Nations' Flaws: Despite being a revolutionary idea for international peace, the League of Nations was fundamentally weakened by its lack of an enforcement mechanism (no army) and the absence of universal membership (notably, the United States never joined).
  • Lessons from Westphalia vs. Versailles: Unlike the Peace of Westphalia, which included defeated parties and avoided blame, the Treaty of Versailles' punitive approach created a lasting sense of injustice that destabilized Europe for decades.

Important Terms

  • Triple Alliance (1914): A military alliance between Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Italy on the eve of World War I.
  • Triple Entente (1914): A military alliance linking France, Russia, and Great Britain before World War I.
  • Western Front: The primary theater of war in Western Europe during World War I, characterized by trench warfare and minimal movement along a 700-kilometer line from the English Channel to Switzerland.
  • No Man's Land: The heavily contested, unowned ground between the trenches of two opposing armies.
  • Self-determination: The principle, championed by President Woodrow Wilson, that peoples of a common nationality should have the right to govern themselves.
  • Czechoslovakia: A new nation created after WWI from parts of the Austro-Hungarian Empire, combining Czech and Slovak populations but also including a significant German minority.
  • Yugoslavia: Meaning "Land of the South Slavs," a new nation formed after WWI that united various South Slavic ethnic groups with diverse religions, alphabets, and historical tensions.
  • Polish Corridor: A strip of land granted to newly independent Poland by the Treaty of Versailles, providing access to the Baltic Sea but separating the German province of East Prussia from the rest of Germany.
  • League of Nations mandates: Former territories of the Ottoman Empire (like Syria, Lebanon, Mesopotamia, Palestine) that were placed under the administration of Allied powers (Britain and France) by the League of Nations.
  • "Stabbed in the back" myth: A widely circulated, false belief in post-WWI Germany that the army was not defeated on the battlefield but rather betrayed by revolutionaries and Jews on the home front.
  • War-guilt clause (Article 231): A provision in the Treaty of Versailles that explicitly assigned blame for World War I solely to Germany and its allies.
  • Reparations: Payments demanded from Germany by the Allied powers after WWI to compensate for war damages.
  • Weimar Republic: The democratic German government established in 1919 after the abdication of Kaiser Wilhelm II, named for the city where its constitution was drafted. It governed Germany until 1933.
  • League of Nations: An international organization proposed by Woodrow Wilson and established in 1920, designed to resolve international disputes through negotiation and collective security, thereby preventing future wars.
  • Collective Security: The principle upon which the League of Nations was based, stating that an attack on one member nation would be considered an attack on all, prompting a collective response.
  • Peace of Westphalia (1648): A series of peace treaties that ended the Thirty Years' War and established a new framework for international relations in Europe, emphasizing state sovereignty.
  • Treaty of Versailles (1919): The most significant of the peace treaties that officially ended World War I between the Allied powers and Germany, signed in the Palace of Versailles.

Detailed Notes

Reading the Geography of the Great War

  • Maps as Essential Tools: Maps are indispensable for understanding WWI's complexities.
    • 1914 Alliance System: Visualizes the pre-war geopolitical "trap" for Germany, surrounded by the Triple Entente (France, Russia, Britain) and facing the Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy). Britain's naval dominance is also evident.
    • Western Front: Clearly illustrates the four-year stalemate, where intense fighting yielded almost no territorial movement.
    • Post-Versailles Europe: Shows a continent dramatically remade with new borders, but also reveals immediate sources of instability.

Why Couldn't They Move? The Geography of Stalemate

  • The Western Front:
    • Length: Approximately 700 kilometers, stretching from the English Channel to Switzerland.
    • Immobility: Armies barely moved for four years along most of this line.
  • Weapons vs. Tactics: The Industrial Revolution transformed weapons, but military tactics remained stuck in the Napoleonic era (infantry assaults in formation). This made 1914 tactics suicidal against modern firepower.
  • Key Technologies Dominating Defense:
    • Machine Gun: Fired 400-600 rounds per minute, capable of stopping an entire infantry company. Thousands deployed by armies like the British.
    • Barbed Wire: Halted attacking infantry, leaving them exposed to machine gun and artillery fire. Artillery barrages often failed to clear it and sometimes buried it deeper.
    • Heavy Artillery: Could destroy trenches but also turned battlefields into cratered, muddy wasteland, hindering attackers. Barrages served as warnings, allowing defenders to take positions before the assault.
  • Attempts to Break the Stalemate:
    • Tanks: First appeared at the Somme in 1916. Initially too few and unreliable, they only became effective in 1918 when combined with new infantry tactics.
    • Airplanes: Also introduced to warfare, though their specific role in breaking the stalemate isn't detailed here, they represent another attempt to overcome the static defenses.
  • Contrast with the Eastern Front: The Eastern Front was much longer, less densely held, and featured more open terrain and cavalry, leading to greater territorial movement, although it was equally deadly.

The New Map of Europe

  • Post-War Challenge: Peacemakers in Paris (1919) faced the monumental task of redrawing Europe after the collapse of four empires: German, Austro-Hungarian, Russian, and Ottoman.
  • Wilson's Principle of Self-determination: The idea that national groups should govern themselves sounded appealing but was impractical due to Europe's highly intermixed populations and competing historical claims.
  • New Nations from Austria-Hungary:
    • Czechoslovakia: Combined Czechs and Slovaks (different languages, some cultural ties), but crucially included three million ethnic Germans in the Sudetenland.
    • Yugoslavia: "Land of the South Slavs," merged Serbs, Croats, Slovenes, Bosnians, and Macedonians—peoples with distinct religions (Orthodox, Catholic, Muslim), alphabets, and centuries of mutual suspicion.
    • Poland: Re-established after 123 years of partition. The "Polish Corridor" granted Poland sea access but cut off East Prussia from the rest of Germany, creating new ethnic tensions between Germans and Poles.
    • Hungary: Separated from Austria and lost two-thirds of its pre-war territory, leaving millions of ethnic Hungarians outside its new borders.
  • Dismemberment of the Ottoman Empire:
    • Its Arab territories (Syria, Lebanon, Mesopotamia/Iraq, Palestine) were divided between Britain and France as "League of Nations mandates."
    • This betrayal angered Arab leaders who had been promised independence for their support during the war.

Germany Humiliated

  • The "Stabbed in the Back" Myth: Germany was not invaded, and its armies remained intact at the armistice. Many Germans found it impossible to accept defeat and embraced the false myth that they were betrayed by internal revolutionaries and Jews. This contradicted the reality of attrition and Allied blockade.
  • Impact of Treaty of Versailles:
    • The "war-guilt clause" and harsh reparations demands were perceived by many Germans as revenge, not justice.
    • The Weimar Republic, Germany's new democratic government, was born associated with national defeat and humiliation, making it vulnerable to extremist attacks.

The League of Nations: A New Idea, a Fatal Flaw

  • Wilson's Vision: The most hopeful element of Wilson's Fourteen Points (Point XIV) was the creation of an international organization for resolving disputes peacefully.
  • Innovations: The League, established 1920, created the first permanent international court, international labor organization, and systematic humanitarian efforts. It succeeded in smaller disputes.
  • Fatal Flaws:
    • No Enforcement Mechanism: The League lacked an army. Its only tools were economic sanctions and diplomatic pressure, which a determined aggressor could ignore. "There was no sheriff."
    • Lack of Universal Membership: The United States, the world's largest economy and most powerful neutral nation, never joined due to the Senate's refusal to ratify the Treaty of Versailles. Germany and the Soviet Union were also initially excluded, rendering "collective" security incomplete.
  • Wilson's Point I (Open Covenants): Advocated for transparent diplomacy, directly reacting to the secret alliances that fueled WWI. Wilson believed open diplomacy would prevent wars by making governments accountable to democratic publics, a notion that proved idealistic.
  • John Maynard Keynes's Prediction: The economist predicted that the treaty's combination of humiliation and economic burden would destabilize Europe, a prediction that proved accurate.

Westphalia and Versailles: Two Great Peace Conferences

  • Comparison: Both the Peace of Westphalia (1648) and the Treaty of Versailles (1919) ended catastrophic wars and attempted to create new international orders.
  • Westphalia's Successes:
    1. Included defeated parties in negotiations, giving them a stake.
    2. Did not assign blame or demand punishment, reducing incentives to repudiate.
    3. Recognized that lasting peace required all parties to accept the outcome as legitimate.
  • Versailles' Failures: Violated all three of Westphalia's principles by excluding Germany, assigning blame, and demanding punishment. This laid the groundwork for future instability.

The Unfinished Peace

  • The Great War and its settlement profoundly influenced the 20th century. New nations faced legitimacy crises, German humiliation fueled extremism, and the weakened League of Nations failed to enforce peace, setting the stage for future conflicts.

Review Questions

  1. Analyze how the map of Europe's alliance system in 1914 illustrated the "trap" that led to World War I. How did these alliances contribute to the rapid escalation of a regional conflict into a global war?
  2. Beyond the raw numbers of casualties, explain why the Western Front is considered a paradox in military history. What specific technologies and tactical shortcomings led to the four-year stalemate, and how did these fundamentally change warfare?
  3. Evaluate President Wilson's principle of self-determination in the context of post-WWI Europe. Provide specific examples from the newly formed nations of Czechoslovakia, Yugoslavia, and Poland to illustrate the practical difficulties and ethnic tensions that arose from its implementation.
  4. Discuss the significance of the "stabbed in the back" myth and the Treaty of Versailles's war-guilt clause and reparations demands on post-war Germany. How did these factors undermine the newly formed Weimar Republic and contribute to the seeds of future instability?
  5. Identify and explain the two major structural flaws that prevented the League of Nations from effectively maintaining international peace. Compare the League's approach to peace enforcement and inclusion of defeated parties with that of the Peace of Westphalia (1648), highlighting what Versailles "got wrong."
