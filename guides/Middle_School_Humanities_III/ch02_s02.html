Study Guide: Literature in the Spanish Empire

Overview

This section introduces two pivotal Spanish writers, Miguel de Cervantes and Bartolomé de las Casas, who offer indispensable perspectives on the Spanish Empire during its zenith and initial decline. Cervantes, through his epic novel Don Quixote, explores the tension between heroic ideals and harsh reality with both comedy and melancholy. Las Casas, a priest and historian, vividly documented the moral catastrophe of the Spanish conquest of the Americas in A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies, using religious authority to condemn exploitation. Together, they present a full, complex portrait of Spain's triumphs and profound ethical challenges.

Key Concepts

  • The Gap Between Ideals and Reality: Explored by Cervantes in Don Quixote, this concept highlights the contrast between cherished heroic or chivalric notions and the often-unheroic, painful truths of the world. Don Quixote's delusions serve as a powerful metaphor for this struggle.
  • Don Quixote as a Reflection of Spain: Many interpret Don Quixote's anachronistic chivalry as an allegory for Spain itself—a nation clinging to outdated ideals of crusade and conquest while the geopolitical and economic landscape shifts around it.
  • The "Tragic Sense of Life": Coined by philosopher Miguel de Unamuno, this concept describes the simultaneous laughter and grief evoked by Don Quixote, reflecting the beautiful but often unattainable nature of ideals in a flawed world.
  • The Imago Dei and Moral Duty: Central to Las Casas's arguments, imago Dei (image of God) asserts that all human beings, including indigenous peoples, are created in God's likeness. This theological principle underpinned Las Casas's claim that the Spanish Crown had a sacred duty to protect, not exploit, these populations.
  • Internal Critique of Empire: Both Cervantes and Las Casas were loyal Spaniards who, from within their society and faith, critically examined the empire's practices. Cervantes depicted the fading glory and disillusionment, while Las Casas directly challenged the moral legitimacy of colonial atrocities.
  • Loyalty and Protest: The enduring legacy of both authors lies in their ability to combine deep loyalty to Spain and its faith with a powerful critique of its failings, demonstrating that patriotism can coexist with profound moral questioning.

Important Terms

  • Miguel de Cervantes Saavedra (1547–1616): Renowned Spanish novelist, playwright, and poet. A veteran of the Battle of Lepanto and former slave, he is best known for Don Quixote.
  • Don Quixote (the novel): A two-part novel (1605, 1615) by Cervantes, widely considered the first modern novel and the greatest work in the Spanish language.
  • Alonso Quijano / Don Quixote de la Mancha: The protagonist of Cervantes's novel, an hidalgo who reads so many chivalric romances that he loses his sanity and sets out to revive knighthood.
  • Sancho Panza: Don Quixote's loyal squire, who often represents common sense and practicality, contrasting with Don Quixote's idealism.
  • Battle of Lepanto (1571): A decisive naval battle in which a Holy League fleet (including Spanish forces) defeated the Ottoman Empire. Cervantes was wounded in this battle.
  • Barbary Pirates: Mediterranean pirates who captured Cervantes on his voyage home from war and held him in Algiers as a slave for five years.
  • Tilting at Windmills: The most famous episode from Don Quixote, where the protagonist attacks windmills, believing them to be giants, a metaphor for attacking imaginary enemies or pursuing impossible ideals.
  • Bartolomé de las Casas (1484–1566): A Spanish Dominican friar, historian, and social reformer who became an early advocate for the rights of indigenous peoples in the Americas.
  • A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies (1542): Las Casas's groundbreaking historical work addressed to Prince Philip, detailing the atrocities committed by Spanish colonists against indigenous populations and arguing for their protection.
  • Imago Dei (Image of God): A theological concept that asserts that human beings are created in God's image, used by Las Casas to argue for the inherent dignity and rights of indigenous peoples.
  • Encomenderos: Spanish settlers who were granted a specific number of indigenous people in a particular area, with the right to extract labor and tribute from them, in exchange for protecting them and converting them to Christianity. Las Casas was initially an encomendero.

Detailed Notes

Introduction: Two Witnesses to an Empire

  • Miguel de Cervantes and Bartolomé de las Casas both lived through the peak and beginning of the decline of the Spanish Empire.
  • Their writings are essential for understanding the empire "from the inside."
  • Cervantes (1547–1616) experienced military heroism, capture, slavery, and poverty, eventually writing Don Quixote.
  • Las Casas (1484–1566) was a priest who championed indigenous rights, arguing against colonial exploitation.
  • Together, they offer a spectrum of the Spanish experience: comedy and tragedy, laughter and grief.

Miguel de Cervantes and Don Quixote

  • Cervantes's Eventful Life:
    • Fought at the Battle of Lepanto (1571), suffering a crippling wound to his left hand.
    • Captured by Barbary pirates and enslaved in Algiers for five years before being ransomed.
    • Returned to Spain a hero but faced poverty, worked as a tax collector, and was imprisoned twice.
    • Began writing Don Quixote while in prison.
  • Biography's Influence on Don Quixote:
    • His life reflected the tension between heroic ideals and painful reality, a core theme of the novel.
    • Cervantes knew both real combat and the indifference of society to a lifetime of service.
    • He imbued his protagonist, Alonso Quijano, with a love for heroic ideals and a world that ignores them.
  • Don Quixote (Part I, 1605; Part II, 1615):
    • Often hailed as the first modern novel and the greatest work in Spanish literature.
    • Plot: Alonso Quijano reads too many chivalric romances, believes he is a knight, renames himself Don Quixote, and embarks on adventures to revive knighthood.
    • Famous Episodes:
      • Windmill Episode (Part I, Ch. 8): Don Quixote mistakes windmills for giants and charges them, only to be knocked down. He blames an enchanter for transforming the "giants." This scene is both slapstick comedy and deeply sad.
        • Interpretations: It functions as comedy, an allegory for a man committed to ideals regardless of reality, and historically, a metaphor for Spain clinging to old rhetoric (crusade, chivalry) while the world changes (published 17 years after the Armada's failure). It prompts questions about whether one can be heroic and deluded simultaneously.
      • Cave of Montesinos (Part II, Ch. 23): Don Quixote describes an impossible, extended vision while underground for a short time. His confident delusion begins to crack, and both Sancho and Quixote himself question the reality of the vision. This signifies the collapse of his ideals.
  • Comedy or Tragedy?: Cervantes called it a "comic history." Readers often experience both laughter and grief. Philosopher Miguel de Unamuno noted Cervantes gave Spain its "tragic sense of life"—the awareness that ideals are beautiful but often clash with a harsh reality.

Bartolomé de las Casas and A Short Account

  • Purpose of A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies (1542):
    • Addressed directly to Prince Philip (later Philip II).
    • Intended to shock the Spanish court into reforming its colonial practices in the Americas.
    • Las Casas himself witnessed the conquest of Cuba and described unspeakable atrocities.
  • Description of Indigenous Peoples:
    • Initially found by Las Casas to be "open and innocent," "simplest people," "unassuming, long-suffering, unassertive, and submissive."
    • "Without malice or guile," "utterly faithful and obedient."
  • Description of Spanish Actions:
    • Spaniards "behaved like ravening wild beasts," committing acts of "killing, terrorizing, afflicting, torturing, and destroying" with "new methods of cruelty, never seen or heard of before."
  • Specific Atrocities and Their Impact:
    • Forced labor in mines (men) and fields (women), leading to starvation and exhaustion.
    • Women's milk dried up, causing infants to die.
    • Separation of men and women prevented new births.
    • Resulted in the extinction of entire indigenous populations ("this whole race of people was extinct").
    • Las Casas emphasized, "I speak of what I myself saw. I was there."
  • Theological Argument:
    • Las Casas argued from Christian theology: indigenous peoples are human beings made in the imago Dei (image of God).
    • Baptizing people who are enslaved and worked to death is a "mockery of the faith."
    • He asserted that the Spanish Crown had a divinely mandated duty to protect these peoples, not exploit them.
  • Internal Critique: Las Casas was not an outsider but a priest, a Spaniard, and even a former encomendero. His transformation from participant to critic is a significant moral example. He used Christian teaching to argue against the exploitation justified by earlier religious authority (e.g., Urban II's call for crusades), instead advocating for the defense of the conquered.

Reading These Texts Together

  • Cervantes and Las Casas were contemporaries (Cervantes: 1547–1616; Las Casas: 1484–1566).
  • They both observed Spain's rise to greatness and the undeniable costs of its empire.
  • Cervantes personally suffered for Spain's empire, while Las Casas fought its worst excesses from within the Church.
  • Neither abandoned Spain or its faith; instead, they used their love for their nation and religion to challenge its evolving nature.
  • Their combination of loyalty and protest, celebration and grief, makes them enduring figures in Spanish literature and the moral history of Western civilization.

Review Questions

  1. How did Cervantes's personal life experiences, such as fighting at Lepanto, being enslaved, and experiencing poverty, shape the central themes and the character of Don Quixote?
  2. Analyze the "tilting at windmills" scene in Don Quixote. What does it reveal about the protagonist's worldview, and how can it be interpreted allegorically in relation to Spain during its empire?
  3. The philosopher Miguel de Unamuno suggested that Cervantes gave Spain its "tragic sense of life." Explain what this means in the context of Don Quixote. Can a character be both heroic and deluded at the same time? Provide specific examples from the novel.
  4. What was Bartolomé de las Casas's primary objective in writing A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies? How did he use Christian theology and his own eyewitness accounts to construct his argument against Spanish colonial practices?
  5. Compare and contrast Cervantes and Las Casas as "witnesses" to the Spanish Empire. In what ways did both authors exemplify a combination of "loyalty and protest" toward their nation and its faith, challenging its trajectory from within?
