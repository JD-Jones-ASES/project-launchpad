Study Guide: World War I

Overview

In the summer of 1914, a single pistol shot in a Balkan city set in motion a chain of events that would kill seventeen million people and destroy four empires. The Great War grew from decades of colonial rivalry, arms races, secret alliances, and nationalist ambitions wound tight as a clock spring. When the spring released, Europe tore itself apart with industrial efficiency. This chapter examines the war from three angles: the military and political history; the literature of two German veterans (Junger and Remarque) who experienced the same war and drew opposite conclusions; and the peace settlement that ended the fighting but sowed the seeds of further catastrophe. The curriculum notes that "history rarely ends cleanly" — the guns stopped on November 11, 1918, but the consequences were only beginning.

Key Concepts

  • The Alliance System as Amplifier: Alliances were designed to deter war by making aggression too costly. They failed in 1914 because military timetables moved faster than diplomacy, mobilization could not be cancelled once begun, and leaders catastrophically underestimated industrial war's destructiveness. The assassination that triggered the war was a regional crisis; the alliance system made it a world war in six weeks.
  • Industrial Warfare and the Western Front Stalemate: The Industrial Revolution had created weapons of defense (machine gun, barbed wire, artillery) so powerful that attacking infantry could not cross open ground faster than defenders could kill them. This is why four years of costly offensives barely moved the front lines — technology had made defense almost impregnable and offense almost impossible.
  • The Two Witnesses (Junger and Remarque): The same facts produced two different honest accounts. Junger found in combat exhilaration and aliveness; Remarque found meaningless destruction. Neither is simply right or wrong — both are honest witnesses who saw the same reality and experienced it differently. This teaches a fundamental lesson about primary sources: they reflect the perspective of their author.
  • Wilson's Idealism vs. European Realpolitik: Wilson arrived at Paris with a vision of a new world order based on self-determination, open diplomacy, and a League of Nations. European powers devastated by the war wanted a peace that would prevent German revival, not a magnanimous settlement that would create stability. The compromise produced a treaty that was neither generous enough to reconcile Germany nor harsh enough to permanently disable it.
  • The Unfinished Peace: The Treaty of Versailles left four dangerous tensions: a humiliated Germany with a nationalist grievance; ethnic minorities trapped in new states; a toothless League without the United States; and a continent exhausted and grieving. These were not accidents but structural consequences of trying to impose a vindictive peace on the map's ethnic realities.

Important Terms

  • Alliance system: Network of military defense treaties dividing Europe into Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy) and Triple Entente (France, Russia, Britain); converted a regional assassination into a world war in six weeks.
  • Archduke Franz Ferdinand: Heir to the Austro-Hungarian throne; assassinated in Sarajevo on June 28, 1914 by Gavrilo Princip; triggering event of WWI.
  • Gavrilo Princip: Bosnian nationalist who assassinated Archduke Franz Ferdinand; 19 years old; his pistol shot set in motion the chain of events leading to WWI.
  • Schlieffen Plan: Germany's pre-war strategy: knock out France through Belgium in six weeks, then turn east against Russia; failed at the First Battle of the Marne (September 1914).
  • Trench warfare: Opposing armies dug into parallel trench systems; machine guns, barbed wire, and artillery made attacking across No Man's Land catastrophically costly; stalemated the Western Front for four years.
  • No Man's Land: The strip of cratered, barbed-wire ground between opposing trenches; soldiers crossed it in attacks at enormous cost.
  • Battle of the Somme (1916): British offensive in northern France; first day (July 1, 1916): 57,000 British casualties including 20,000 dead; bloodiest single day in British military history; over one million total casualties.
  • Battle of Verdun (1916): German attack on French fortress; intended to "bleed France white"; lasted 10 months; approximately 700,000 dead; symbol of the war's senseless consumption.
  • Gallipoli (1915-1916): Allied attempt to knock Ottoman Empire out of war by forcing the Dardanelles; championed by Winston Churchill; catastrophic failure; nearly 500,000 casualties.
  • Lusitania: British ocean liner sunk by German submarine in 1915, killing 1,198 including 128 Americans; contributed to US public opinion shifting toward entering the war.
  • Zimmermann Telegram: German diplomatic message (intercepted by Britain) proposing Mexico attack the US in exchange for recovering Texas, New Mexico, and Arizona; helped push Wilson toward asking Congress for war declaration.
  • Armistice: The formal agreement to stop fighting, signed November 11, 1918 at 11:00 AM; not yet a peace treaty but ended the killing; commemorated as Remembrance Day and Veterans Day.
  • Fourteen Points: Wilson's peace proposal (January 1918): open diplomacy, freedom of seas, arms reduction, national self-determination, League of Nations; mostly rejected or weakened at Paris.
  • Treaty of Versailles (1919): Peace treaty ending WWI; signed June 28, 1919; war guilt clause, 132 billion gold marks reparations, territorial losses, military limits; Germany not invited to negotiate.
  • War guilt clause: Article 231 forcing Germany to accept full responsibility for the war; basis for reparations; considered a lie by most Germans; rallying cry for nationalists.
  • Reparations: Financial payments demanded from Germany; set at 132 billion gold marks; contributed to German economic instability through the 1920s and early 1930s.
  • League of Nations: International peace organization established by the Treaty of Versailles; collective security principle; no army; US never joined; failed to stop aggressors in the 1930s.
  • Self-determination: Wilson's principle that peoples of a common nationality should govern themselves; in practice, Europe's intermixed populations made clean national boundaries impossible.

Detailed Notes

The Causes of the War

  • Long-Term Causes
    • Decades of colonial rivalry, arms races, and secret alliances
    • Nationalist ambitions wound tight as a clock spring
    • The alliance system: designed to deter war, it amplified the crisis
    • Military timetables that moved faster than diplomats
  • The Trigger: Sarajevo (June 28, 1914)
    • Archduke Franz Ferdinand rode through Sarajevo in an open car
    • Gavrilo Princip, 19-year-old Bosnian nationalist, fired two shots
    • Both Franz Ferdinand and his wife Sophie died within the hour
  • The Six-Week Chain
    • Austria-Hungary: impossible ultimatum to Serbia (designed to be refused)
    • Serbia refused one clause; Austria-Hungary declared war
    • Russia mobilized to protect its Slavic ally Serbia
    • Germany, bound by treaty to Austria-Hungary, declared war on Russia and France
    • Germany invaded neutral Belgium to attack France from the north
    • Britain, pledged to protect Belgian neutrality since 1839, declared war on Germany
    • By early August 1914: most of Europe at war

The Western Front

  • The Schlieffen Plan's Failure
    • Plan: massive wheeling movement through Belgium; encircle France in 6 weeks; then east against Russia
    • German armies swept through Belgium; pushed deep into France; came within 30 miles of Paris
    • First Battle of the Marne (September 1914): French and British halted the advance
    • Both sides tried to outflank each other northward (the "Race to the Sea")
    • By November 1914: trenches from English Channel to Swiss border — nearly 700 km
    • Germany stuck in the long war of attrition it had hoped to avoid
  • The Stalemate of Industrial Warfare
    • Magazine rifle, machine gun, heavy artillery, poison gas
    • Attacking infantry could not cross open ground faster than defenders could kill them
    • Technology strongly favored defense over offense
    • Four years of costly attacks barely moved the front lines
  • The Great Battles
    • Battle of the Somme (July-November 1916):
      • British commanders launched offensive to relieve French at Verdun
      • July 1, 1916: 57,000 British casualties, nearly 20,000 dead in ONE DAY
      • Bloodiest single day in British military history
      • By battle's end: over one million total casualties; Allied advance measured in miles
    • Battle of Verdun (February-December 1916):
      • German commander Falkenhayn chose Verdun deliberately for symbolic importance
      • Strategy: "bleed France white" - force France to fight for a point of pride
      • France refused to let Verdun fall
      • Ten months; approximately 700,000 dead (300,000 French, 400,000 German)
      • Verdun became the symbol of the war's senseless consumption of human life

Other Fronts

  • The Eastern Front
    • Germany and Austria-Hungary faced Russia across a longer, more fluid line
    • Russia: vast armies but poorly equipped and badly led
    • Unlike Western Front: never completely stalemated; large territorial movements
  • Gallipoli (1915-1916)
    • Championed by Winston Churchill (British First Lord of the Admiralty)
    • Goal: force the Dardanelles; capture Constantinople; knock Ottoman Empire out of war
    • Allied forces landed April 1915; immediately pinned on beaches
    • Eight months pinned; withdrew after approximately 500,000 combined casualties
    • One of the most complete Allied failures of the war

1917: The Decisive Year

  • The United States Enters
    • Wilson kept America neutral for nearly three years
    • German submarine warfare: sank merchant ships without warning
    • Lusitania (1915): 1,198 dead including 128 Americans
    • Zimmermann Telegram: Germany tried to recruit Mexico against US
    • April 1917: Wilson asked Congress for declaration of war
    • American troops arrived in France in large numbers in 1918
  • Russia Collapses
    • War had exhausted the Tsarist government
    • February 1917: revolution overthrew Tsar Nicholas II
    • Provisional government tried to keep Russia in the war
    • October 1917: Bolsheviks (Lenin) seized power
    • March 1918: Russia left the war; freed German forces for the Western Front

The End of the War

  • Germany's Last Effort
    • Spring 1918 offensive: dramatic early gains but exhausted reserves
    • Allied counteroffensives of summer and autumn 1918 shattered German armies
  • Collapse of the Central Powers
    • Bulgaria: armistice September 1918
    • Ottoman Empire: armistice October 1918
    • Austria-Hungary: armistice early November 1918
    • Kaiser Wilhelm II abdicated November 9, 1918
    • November 11, 1918, 11:00 AM: guns on the Western Front fell silent
  • The Cost
    • Approximately 17 million dead; 20 million wounded
    • Whole generations of young men consumed
    • French army mutinies in 1917 after yet another failed offensive
    • Ottoman massacres of Armenian Christians added another layer of atrocity

The Paris Peace Conference and the Treaty of Versailles

  • Wilson's Fourteen Points
    • Announced January 1918; idealistic vision for a new world order
    • Open diplomacy (no secret treaties); freedom of the seas
    • Arms reduction; national self-determination
    • League of Nations: the peace's crown jewel in Wilson's vision
  • European Realpolitik
    • France: lost nearly 1.4 million soldiers; industrial heartland devastated; wanted Germany permanently crippled
    • Britain: wanted to preserve empire and naval supremacy
    • Neither wanted Wilson's generous peace
  • The Treaty of Versailles (June 28, 1919)
    • Signed exactly five years after the assassination of Franz Ferdinand
    • War guilt clause (Article 231): Germany accepts full responsibility; justifies reparations
    • Reparations: 132 billion gold marks; economists said impossible to pay without destroying German economy
    • Territorial losses: Alsace-Lorraine to France; "Polish Corridor" cutting off East Prussia; other territories
    • Military limits: tiny army; no air force; no submarines
    • Germany not invited to negotiate; presented as a diktat
  • The League of Nations
    • Written into the treaty; Wilson's hope for permanent peace
    • US Senate refused to ratify treaty; America retreated into isolation
    • League limped forward without its most powerful potential member
    • No army; only tools were economic sanctions and moral pressure
  • Unresolved Tensions (the seeds of future catastrophe)
    • Humiliated Germany: war guilt clause was a rallying cry for nationalists
    • Reparations contributing to economic instability
    • Ethnic minorities trapped in new states: Poles, Germans, Hungarians, Czechs often under wrong flags
    • Toothless League without US: unable to enforce peace
    • Economist John Maynard Keynes (resigned in protest): treaty "sow[ed] the decay of the whole civilised life of Europe"

Literature: Three Witnesses

  • The Comparison Exercise
    • Both Junger and Remarque served on the Western Front; both published books in 1929
    • Two honest witnesses to the same catastrophe who drew opposite conclusions
    • Neither is simply right or wrong; both are telling the truth of their own experience
    • Lesson: primary sources reflect the perspective of their author — two witnesses can describe the same event truthfully in incompatible ways
  • Ernst Junger: The Storm of Steel (1929)
    • German soldier; enlisted at 19; served four years; wounded 14 times
    • Received the Pour le Merite (Germany's highest military honor)
    • Wrote from diaries kept in the trenches
    • Did not flinch from death, blood, or destruction — but also described exhilaration, pride, intense aliveness
    • "Cold clarity" in combat: "Fear was there, but it was clean — it sharpened rather than paralyzed"
    • Not a Nazi; declined to join the party; lived to age 102
  • Erich Maria Remarque: All Quiet on the Western Front (1929)
    • German veteran; same front, same year of publication
    • Described the war as meaningless destruction of youth
    • One of the most celebrated anti-war novels ever written
  • Wilfred Owen
    • Young British officer-poet; wrote the most famous poem of the war in 1917
    • "Dulce et Decorum Est" — title from Horace: "It is sweet and fitting to die for one's country"
    • Owen called this "the old lie"
    • Died one week before the Armistice

Connections

  • The alliance system that failed to prevent war in 1914 was a direct product of the competitive nation-state system established at the Peace of Westphalia (Chapter 3) — sovereignty and national interest, taken to their logical extreme, produced the catastrophe Westphalia's framers could not have foreseen.
  • Germany's unification and industrial power (Chapter 6, Bismarck) made it the dominant continental power whose rivalry with France and Britain was one of the war's long-term causes.
  • The Russian Revolution of 1917 brought Lenin's Bolsheviks to power, directly connecting to Marx's Communist theory (Chapter 6) — the first real-world test of the Communist Manifesto's prescription.
  • The Treaty of Versailles's failure to create a stable peace connects directly to Wilson's Fourteen Points and the Enlightenment's hope that reason and principle could organize international relations.
  • The unresolved tensions of Versailles — humiliated Germany, the League of Nations without teeth, dissatisfied minorities — are the direct context for the catastrophes of the 1930s and 1940s that this curriculum does not cover but implicitly points toward.
