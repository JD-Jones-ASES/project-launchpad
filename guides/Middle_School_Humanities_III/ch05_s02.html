The Age of Expansion: Literary Witnesses to Power and Conscience

Overview

This section explores the literature of the age of expansion through the eyes of three diverse witnesses: Olaudah Equiano, Bartolomé de las Casas, and William Shakespeare. Each writer, using distinct approaches rooted in their own experiences and cultural traditions, compels us to confront critical questions about power, conscience, the ethics of conquest, and the human cost of dispossession during this transformative historical period.

Key Concepts

  • Abolitionist Argumentation: The use of personal narrative, reason, and moral principles to advocate for the end of slavery, as exemplified by Equiano.
  • Enlightenment Natural Rights: The philosophical idea, championed by thinkers like John Locke, that all human beings are born with inherent rights to life, liberty, and property. Equiano applied this framework to condemn slavery as fundamentally contradictory.
  • Christian Human Dignity (Imago Dei): The theological concept that all humans are created in the "image of God" and therefore possess an inherent dignity that demands respect. Both Equiano and Las Casas drew upon this to argue against enslavement and brutalization.
  • Prophetic Witness: The act of speaking truth to power, often from within a corrupt system, to condemn injustice. Las Casas serves as a prime example, advocating against the encomienda system from his position as a Spanish priest.
  • The Middle Passage: The brutal and dehumanizing sea voyage forced upon enslaved Africans across the Atlantic, vividly described by Equiano as a scene of unimaginable horror.
  • The Encomienda System: A Spanish colonial labor system that granted colonists the right to demand labor and tribute from indigenous populations, often resulting in severe exploitation and mass deaths.
  • Colonial Dispossession: The act of forcibly removing indigenous peoples from their ancestral lands and stripping them of their autonomy and heritage, powerfully depicted through Caliban in The Tempest.
  • Rhetorical Strategy: The deliberate choices an author makes to persuade an audience. Examples include Equiano's ethnographic descriptions to humanize Africans and Las Casas's use of polemic and first-hand testimony to shock the Spanish crown.

Important Terms

  • Abolition: The movement to end slavery and the slave trade.
  • Enlightenment: An 18th-century intellectual and philosophical movement that emphasized reason, individualism, and human rights.
  • Natural Rights: Fundamental rights that are believed to be inherent to all human beings and not dependent on government or cultural norms.
  • Imago Dei: A Latin theological term meaning "image of God," referring to the belief that human beings are created in God's likeness and therefore possess inherent dignity.
  • Middle Passage: The sea journey undertaken by slave ships from West Africa to the West Indies, notorious for its horrific conditions and high mortality rates.
  • Encomienda System: A labor system implemented by the Spanish Crown during the colonization of the Americas, in which Spanish colonists were granted control over indigenous populations, demanding labor and tribute in exchange for protection and Christian instruction (which was often neglected).
  • Conquistadors: Spanish conquerors of Mexico and Peru in the 16th century.
  • Council of Valladolid (1550-51): A significant debate in Spanish history between Bartolomé de Las Casas and Juan Ginés de Sepúlveda regarding the treatment of indigenous peoples in the Americas.
  • Polemic: A strong verbal or written attack on someone or something; a controversial argument.
  • Dispossession: The action of depriving someone of land, property, or other possessions.
  • Ethnographic Mode: A style of writing or description that provides detailed accounts of the customs, social life, and culture of a particular people or group.

Detailed Notes

Introduction: Three Witnesses

The literature of the Age of Expansion offers diverse perspectives on a period marked by vast global change. This section focuses on three key figures whose writings provide unique "witnesses" to the era's complexities:

  • Olaudah Equiano: A survivor of the Middle Passage who used Enlightenment principles and literary skill to advocate for abolition.
  • Bartolomé de las Casas: A Spanish priest who condemned the conquest and enslavement of indigenous peoples in the Americas based on Christian theology.
  • William Shakespeare: Through his play The Tempest, he explored themes of dispossession and power dynamics, reflecting on early colonial encounters. These texts collectively challenge readers to reflect on power, conscience, and the importance of confronting uncomfortable truths.

Olaudah Equiano, The Interesting Narrative (1789)

Biography and Context:

  • Born around 1745 in the Igbo region of West Africa (modern-day Nigeria).
  • Kidnapped at age eleven with his sister, separated, and forced onto a slave ship bound for Barbados.
  • Survived the Middle Passage and was sold to a Royal Navy officer, serving in naval campaigns.
  • Managed to earn money through trade while enslaved and purchased his freedom in 1766 from a Quaker merchant.
  • Converted to Christianity and became a prominent figure in the British abolitionist movement.
  • Published his influential Narrative in 1789, coinciding with the French and Haitian Revolutions.

The Narrative as a Work:

  • A multifaceted text: part memoir, spiritual autobiography, and political argument.
  • Achieved wide readership, going through nine editions during Equiano's lifetime.
  • Educated members of Parliament, abolitionists, and ordinary citizens about the horrors of the slave trade from an insider's perspective.
  • Rhetorical Strategy (African Childhood): Equiano's detailed descriptions of Igbo customs, food, and social life served two crucial purposes:
    1. Humanization: It presented enslaved Africans as people with a rich civilization, history, and culture, challenging dehumanizing stereotypes.
    2. Counter-argument: It implicitly refuted the claim that Africans were "barbaric" and benefited from European "civilization," demonstrating his strategic writing for a British audience.

The Middle Passage (Chapter 2):

  • Equiano's account is a precise, controlled, and devastating depiction of the voyage.
  • His initial "astonishment" at the slave ship quickly turned into "terror."
  • He described being "handled and tossed up to see if I were sound," highlighting the dehumanization of enslaved people treated as commodities.
  • The conditions were horrific: extreme crowding, heat, "copious perspirations," "loathsome smells," "sickness," "shrieks of the women, and the groans of the dying," creating an "almost inconceivable" scene of horror.
  • His youth and sickness perhaps spared him some of the worst treatment, keeping him mostly on deck and unfettered.

Equiano and the Enlightenment:

  • Beyond a story of suffering, the Narrative is a moral argument.
  • Equiano skillfully used the language of natural rights, echoing John Locke and Thomas Jefferson, to argue that enslaved people are human beings with inherent rights equal to their captors.
  • This presented slavery as not just cruel, but philosophically incoherent, contradicting the very principles of liberty and justice that many European societies claimed to uphold.

Christian Identity and Imago Dei:

  • Equiano's abolitionist stance was also deeply rooted in his Christian faith, which he described as a genuine spiritual transformation.
  • His faith provided a framework where every human being is made in the image of God (imago Dei) and therefore possesses an inviolable dignity that no human institution can revoke.

Combination of Traditions:

  • Equiano's powerful argument against slavery uniquely combined two traditions: the Enlightenment philosophy of natural rights and the Christian understanding of human dignity (imago Dei). This dual approach was highly effective in his specific historical and cultural context.

Bartolomé de Las Casas, A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies (1542)

Revisiting Las Casas:

  • Previously encountered for his accounts of Spanish atrocities, this unit focuses on his condemnation of the encomienda system.

The Encomienda System:

  • Definition: A labor system in Spanish colonial America where the Crown granted a colonist (an encomendero) the right to demand labor and tribute from a specific group of indigenous people.
  • Theory vs. Practice: Theoretically, encomenderos were responsible for the welfare of those in their care. In practice, it was a brutal system of forced labor that led to the deaths of enormous numbers of indigenous people. Las Casas campaigned against it for decades.

From A Short Account: The Encomienda System:

  • Las Casas's language is deliberately shocking, making it a powerful polemic (impassioned advocacy).
  • He attributed the immense scale of murder and destruction to "purely and simply greed," driven by the desire for gold and rapid personal enrichment.
  • He powerfully described the Spanish treatment of indigenous people: "not as brute animals — indeed, I would to God they had done... so much as piles of dung in the middle of the road."
  • He emphasized his "first-hand experience," lending credibility and urgency to his confession and accusation.
  • Comparison with Equiano: Both Equiano and Las Casas appealed to conscience. Equiano used vivid description to evoke empathy from British readers; Las Casas used polemical condemnation to highlight the violation of Christian duty to the Spanish Crown.

Prophetic Witness From Within a System:

  • Las Casas was not an outsider; he was a Spanish priest and former beneficiary of the colonial system whose conscience was awakened.
  • He used his insider status to gain audiences with the King of Spain and famously debated Juan Ginés de Sepúlveda at the Council of Valladolid (1550–51).
  • Impact: Las Casas won the theological debate, leading to the official condemnation of the encomienda system. However, the conquest and exploitation largely continued.
  • Sobering Lesson: Moral argument can change the official record and legal precedents even when it fails to immediately change widespread behavior. This "changed record" became important for later human rights discourse.
  • Legacy: Las Casas's campaign significantly contributed to the development of international law and the concept of human rights. His arguments were foundational for the Dominican theologians of the School of Salamanca and later thinkers like Hugo Grotius, demonstrating that "prophetic witness" is rarely wasted in the long term.

William Shakespeare, The Tempest (c. 1611)

Historical Context:

  • One of Shakespeare's last plays, written around 1611, at the very beginning of England's colonial expansion into the Americas.
  • Likely inspired by the real-life shipwreck of the Sea Venture off Bermuda in 1609.

Characters and Setting:

  • Takes place on a mysterious, unnamed island.
  • Prospero: The rightful Duke of Milan, exiled by his treacherous brother, a powerful sorcerer.
  • Miranda: Prospero's daughter.
  • Ariel: A spirit-servant, indentured to Prospero.
  • Caliban: The island's original inhabitant, son of the witch Sycorax. Prospero has enslaved him and uses him for labor.

Caliban's Accusation:

  • In Act I, scene 2, Caliban directly confronts Prospero, expressing his deep sense of injustice and dispossession.
  • His poignant lines, "This island's mine, by Sycorax my mother, / Which thou tak'st from me," encapsulate the experience of indigenous peoples whose lands were seized by more powerful newcomers.
  • He highlights the initial deception: Prospero initially "strok'st me and made much of me" before enslaving him. The Tempest offers a haunting literary image of the costs of colonialism.

Review Questions

  1. How do Olaudah Equiano, Bartolomé de las Casas, and William Shakespeare each "witness" the age of expansion, and what common themes related to power and conscience emerge from their diverse testimonies?
  2. Explain how Olaudah Equiano's Interesting Narrative combines both Enlightenment philosophy (natural rights) and Christian theology (imago Dei) to construct a powerful argument against slavery.
  3. Describe the encomienda system in Spanish colonial America and analyze how Bartolomé de Las Casas's "prophetic witness" challenged it. What was the immediate and long-term impact of his efforts?
  4. Discuss Caliban's role in Shakespeare's The Tempest. How does his story serve as an allegorical representation of colonial dispossession and the complex power dynamics between colonizers and indigenous populations?
  5. Compare and contrast the rhetorical strategies used by Equiano and Las Casas to appeal to the consciences of their respective audiences. Which approach do you think was more effective in its historical context, and why?
