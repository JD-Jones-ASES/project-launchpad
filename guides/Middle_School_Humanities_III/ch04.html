Study Guide: The Enlightenment

Overview

The seventeenth century left Europe with telescopes revealing Jupiter's moons, experiments measuring air's weight, and mathematics predicting comet orbits. By 1700, educated Europeans asked whether reason that had unlocked nature's laws could also unlock the laws of society and morality. The Enlightenment was Europe's great experiment in applying reason to every corner of human life. Its achievements were genuine: natural rights, constitutional government, religious toleration. Its failures were equally real: a French Revolution that devoured its children, and a confidence in human reason that sometimes forgot the human heart. This chapter examines the thinkers who made the modern world and assesses their work honestly.

Key Concepts

  • Reason as the Universal Solvent: The Enlightenment's philosophes believed that just as Newton discovered the laws of physics, reason could discover the laws of justice and human happiness. This confidence in reason was both the movement's greatest strength and, when divorced from Christian humility about human sinfulness, its greatest danger.
  • Natural Rights and Their Christian Roots: Locke's concept of natural rights (life, liberty, property) drew on the Christian natural law tradition going back to Aquinas. Jefferson's crucial addition — that rights are "endowed by their Creator" — grounded them in God rather than human opinion, making them unremovable by any majority vote.
  • The Danger of Rousseau's General Will: Unlike Locke's individual natural rights, Rousseau's general will is collective and requires someone to decide what it is. That someone can then override individual rights in the name of the common good. This is the intellectual road from the philosophes to Robespierre and the guillotine.
  • American vs. French Revolution: The American Revolution was conservative in the philosophical sense: it appealed to existing rights of Englishmen and to God-given natural rights, preserving institutions including the church. The French Revolution replaced Christianity with abstract Reason and ended in the Terror. The difference illustrates what happens when Enlightenment reason is applied with and without Christian restraint.
  • Descartes's Unintended Consequences: Descartes was a sincere Catholic who intended the Method of Doubt to strengthen faith. Later thinkers used his tool to attack religious belief. This illustrates a recurring historical lesson: ideas travel beyond their authors' intentions.

Important Terms

  • Enlightenment: Intellectual movement (c. 1680-1800) applying human reason to improve government, society, religion, and morality; centered in France, England, Scotland, and German states.
  • Philosophes: French word for "philosophers"; nickname for Enlightenment thinkers across Europe who used reason to question tradition and challenge authority.
  • Method of Doubt: Descartes's technique of doubting everything until reaching the undoubtable; produced "I think, therefore I am" (cogito ergo sum).
  • Cogito ergo sum: Descartes's Latin phrase ("I think, therefore I am"); the foundation of his philosophy; shifted focus from external authority to the individual thinking mind.
  • Deism: The belief that God created the universe but does not intervene in it; God as clockmaker; held in various forms by Voltaire, Jefferson, and Franklin; the text identifies it as a "theological impoverishment."
  • Natural Rights: Rights belonging to every human by nature, not granted by governments; Locke's core rights: life, liberty, property; roots in Christian natural law tradition.
  • State of Nature: Locke's philosophical thought experiment imagining life before government; establishes that rights exist before and independent of any state.
  • Social Contract: The agreement by which people consent to be governed in exchange for protection of their rights; if government violates rights, the people may replace it; directly influenced the Declaration of Independence.
  • General Will: Rousseau's concept of what the community truly needs, not the sum of individual desires; used by Robespierre to justify the Terror.
  • Separation of Powers: Montesquieu's remedy for tyranny: dividing government into legislative, executive, and judicial branches that check each other; foundation of the US Constitution.
  • Encyclopedie: Diderot and d'Alembert's 35-volume, 70,000-article summary of all human knowledge (1751-1772); treated craftsmen with the same seriousness as theologians; a political challenge to Church and Crown as sole arbiters of truth.
  • L'infame: Voltaire's term for "the infamous thing" — religious intolerance, superstition, and persecution.

Detailed Notes

Foundations: The Scientific Revolution

  • Newton's Principia Mathematica (1687)
    • Showed a single mathematical law (universal gravitation) governed both falling objects and planetary orbits
    • Universe: orderly, rational, and knowable by human reason
    • Inspired the Enlightenment question: if reason can unlock nature's laws, can it unlock society's?
  • The Philosophes
    • Term for Enlightenment thinkers across Europe
    • Believed reason could discover laws of justice, liberty, and human happiness
    • Used reason to question tradition, challenge authority, propose reforms

Descartes: The Philosophical Foundation

  • Discourse on Method (1637)
    • Problem: many things taught may be wrong; senses deceive; others' opinions may be mistaken
    • Solution: systematically doubt everything until reaching the undoubtable
    • Result: cogito ergo sum - even if everything is illusion, the act of thinking proves existence
    • Shifted focus from God and tradition to the individual thinking mind as starting point
  • Unintended Consequences
    • Descartes was a sincere Catholic who intended to strengthen faith through reason
    • Later thinkers used Cartesian doubt to attack religious belief
    • Lesson: ideas travel beyond their authors' intentions

Isaac Newton and the Clockwork Universe

  • The Orderly Universe
    • Principia Mathematica (1687): the most important scientific book ever written
    • Same force makes apples fall and keeps planets in orbit
    • Universe: a vast machine running on precise mathematical laws
  • Theological Implications
    • Many Enlightenment thinkers moved toward Deism: God as clockmaker
    • The God of Deism: abstract First Cause, not the personal God of Scripture
    • Text calls this a "theological impoverishment"

Locke: Natural Rights and the Social Contract

  • Two Treatises of Government (1689)
    • First Treatise: demolished divine right theory (refuting Filmer's argument from Adam)
    • Second Treatise: positive account of what government is and ought to be
  • The State of Nature
    • Not historical but philosophical thought experiment
    • In this state: all humans naturally equal and free
    • Natural rights to life, liberty, and property precede government
  • The Social Contract
    • People give up some unlimited freedom for government protection of rights
    • Contract has conditions: government may be dissolved if it violates rights
    • Revolutionary: authority comes from consent of the governed, not from God directly
  • Connection to Christian Natural Law
    • Natural rights have deep roots in Aquinas and St. Paul (Romans)
    • Natural rights are God-given; Locke did not sever this connection completely

Voltaire: The Wit of the Enlightenment

  • Life and Target
    • Most famous and feared Enlightenment writer
    • Personal reasons to be angry: saw Protestant family destroyed by religious prejudice
    • Main target: l'infame - religious intolerance, superstition, persecution
    • Weapons: satire and ridicule (made bigotry look not only wrong but stupid)
  • Candide (1759)
    • Naive hero witnesses earthquakes, Inquisitions, slavery, and war
    • Philosopher Pangloss (parody of Leibniz) insists this is "the best of all possible worlds"
    • Candide's final wisdom: "We must cultivate our garden"
    • Voltaire's real philosophy: practical wisdom over utopian theories
  • What Voltaire Was Not
    • Not an atheist; a Deist who believed in God as rational author of the universe
    • Attacked the Church for cruelty and corruption, not Christianity itself
    • Famous quip: "If God did not exist, it would be necessary to invent him"

Rousseau: The Dark Side of the Enlightenment

  • The Social Contract (1762)
    • Opening: "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains"
    • Civilization corrupted naturally good humans through property, inequality, and vanity
    • Just government must express the general will
  • The General Will - and Its Danger
    • Not the same as majority vote: it is what is truly good for the community
    • Someone must decide what the general will is
    • That someone can then force people to be "free" whether they want to be or not
    • Robespierre and the Committee of Public Safety used it to justify mass executions
    • Key fault line of modern politics: Locke's individual rights vs. Rousseau's collective will

Montesquieu: Separation of Powers

  • Spirit of the Laws (1748)
    • Applied scientific method to government: studied governments comparatively
    • Key conclusion: tyranny results from concentrated power
    • Remedy: separation into three branches (legislative, executive, judicial)
    • Each branch checks the others
  • Influence
    • Drew on the English model from the Glorious Revolution
    • Madison cited Montesquieu more than any other thinker in The Federalist Papers
    • Became the structural principle of the US Constitution

The Encyclopedie: Knowledge for Everyone

  • The Project
    • Diderot and d'Alembert, 1751-1772: 35 volumes, 150 contributors, 70,000 articles
    • Goal: complete summary of all human knowledge
    • Church condemned it; government banned it twice; sold 20,000 copies
  • Political Significance Beyond Reference
    • Treated mechanical trades with same seriousness as theology
    • Implied: craftsman who builds a loom deserves the same respect as a bishop
    • No single authority - Church or Crown - owned the truth

The Enlightenment in Practice

  • The American Revolution (1776)
    • Declaration of Independence: most important political document the Enlightenment produced
    • Every phrase Lockean: natural rights, consent of the governed, right of revolution
    • Jefferson's addition: rights "endowed by their Creator" - unremovable by majority vote
    • Contradiction: Jefferson enslaved over 600 people; "all men created equal" vs. slavery
    • Took nearly a century and a civil war to begin resolving
  • The French Revolution (1789-1799)
    • Began with genuine Enlightenment ideals
    • Became the Terror: over 17,000 executed by guillotine
    • Based on Rousseau's general will; replaced Christianity with abstract Reason
    • Closed churches; renamed Notre-Dame "Temple of Reason"
    • Ended with Napoleon's dictatorship (1799)
    • Edmund Burke's critique: tradition more reliable than abstract theory; "he was right"

Connections

  • Locke's natural rights theory builds directly on the Christian natural law tradition of Aquinas (earlier curricula); the Enlightenment did not create natural rights but secularized them.
  • The difference between Locke (individual rights, consent, right of revolution) and Rousseau (general will, collective good, forced freedom) is one of the great fault lines of modern politics, still visible in debates between liberalism and collectivism.
  • The American founding's grounding of rights in the Creator connects forward to Catholic Social Teaching (Chapter 6): both ground human dignity in God rather than in political ideology.
  • The failure of the French Revolution connects to the warning about applying reason "without Christian restraint" - a theme the text treats as one of the Enlightenment's central lessons.
  • Montesquieu's separation of powers connects to the constitutional developments in England (Chapter 1) and directly to the structure of the US government that would face the challenges described in Chapter 5.
