Chapter 5, Section 1: Cartography - Reading the Map of Empire

Overview

This section explores how maps were used as tools of European colonial power, revealing the scale of empire while obscuring indigenous presence. It then delves into the economic engines of this expansion, particularly the brutal triangle trade and the plantation economy, highlighting their devastating human cost. Finally, it examines the complex role of Christian missionaries in the colonial era, comparing this expansion to earlier crusades and considering its lasting global legacy.

Key Concepts

  • Maps as Documents of Power: European colonial maps were not neutral representations but deliberate tools used to assert control and visually legitimize territorial claims, often depicting occupied lands as empty.
  • The Triangle Trade: An incredibly profitable, interconnected economic system linking Europe (manufactured goods), West Africa (enslaved people via the Middle Passage), and the Americas (colonial products like sugar, tobacco, cotton, rum). The enslaved were the fundamental commodity driving the system.
  • The Plantation Economy: The economic logic that fueled the Atlantic slave trade. It was a rational, albeit morally abhorrent, system driven by European demand for cash crops and the need for cheap, intensive labor, leading to exceptionally high death rates for enslaved people, particularly in the Caribbean.
  • Missionary Encounter Complexity: Christian missionaries, driven by genuine religious zeal, often served as pioneers in newly encountered regions. Their actions ranged from compassionate aid and cultural understanding (e.g., Jesuits in China, reductions in Paraguay) to unintentional facilitation of colonial control and cultural destruction.
  • Colonial Expansion vs. Crusades: While both involved outward movements of Western power, colonial expansion was distinct in its primary aim (trade/wealth vs. holy sites), permanence (settler societies), vastly greater scale (especially the slave trade), and the emergence of internal Christian critiques against its injustices.
  • Legacy of Colonial Expansion: The age of expansion fundamentally shaped the modern world, influencing global economic structures, racial categories, national borders, and the distribution of wealth between the Global North and South. By 1914, European nations controlled 84% of the world's land surface, a result of specific choices, technologies, and economic systems.
  • Extraction from Periphery: Similar to the Roman Empire, European colonial empires enriched their centers by extracting wealth and resources from conquered territories, using local elites as intermediaries and justifying their dominance with claims of civilizational superiority.

Important Terms

  • Triangle Trade: The system of Atlantic commerce (roughly 1500-1867) that connected Europe, West Africa, and the Americas in a circuit of exchange, primarily involving manufactured goods, enslaved people, and colonial products.
  • Middle Passage: The brutal transatlantic journey enslaved Africans were forced to endure, from West Africa to the Americas. Approximately 1.8 million people died during this passage.
  • Plantation Economy: An agricultural system, particularly prevalent in the Americas, that relied on large-scale production of cash crops (like sugar, tobacco, cotton) using coerced, often enslaved, labor. It was the driving economic force behind the Atlantic slave trade.
  • Missionary Encounter: The meetings and interactions between Christian missionaries and indigenous peoples during the age of expansion, characterized by a complex mix of religious conversion, cultural exchange, aid, and often, the paving of the way for colonial influence.
  • Francis Xavier (1506–1552): A prominent Jesuit priest and missionary who traveled extensively in Asia (India, Japan) during the 16th century, achieving significant conversions.
  • Matteo Ricci (1552–1610): An Italian Jesuit missionary who adapted Christian teachings to Chinese intellectual culture, learning the language and Confucian philosophy.
  • Reductions (reducciones): Self-governing communities established by Jesuits in Paraguay and surrounding regions for Guarani indigenous people, combining Catholic religious life with indigenous practices and operating outside Spanish colonial labor demands. They offered unique protection but were also controversial.
  • David Livingstone (1813–1873): A Scottish Congregationalist missionary and explorer in 19th-century central Africa who combined evangelism with anti-slavery campaigns, contributing to British interest and intervention in East Africa.
  • Tokugawa Shogunate: The last feudal government of Japan, which violently suppressed Christianity in the early 17th century due to fears of its connection to European power.
  • Las Casas, Equiano, Wilberforce: Figures (a Spanish priest, a former enslaved person, and a British politician, respectively) mentioned as prominent critics of colonial violence and slavery, demonstrating Christianity's internal resources for self-criticism.

Detailed Notes

Reading the Map of Empire

  • Maps are not neutral: They are created with specific purposes, by specific people, and from particular viewpoints.
  • European colonial maps:
    • Created by Europeans, for Europeans.
    • Showed the extent of European control (e.g., British pink, French blue).
    • Often depicted territories as "empty land," ignoring the indigenous populations already living there, treating them as part of the landscape.
  • Documents of Power and Evidence: Colonial maps explicitly demonstrate the power dynamics and the undeniable scale of European expansion and conquest.

The Triangle Trade

  • Economic Engine: The primary driver of Atlantic expansion and colonization.
  • A Continuous Circuit: Connected Europe, West Africa, and the Americas.
  • Legs of the Trade:
    1. Europe to West Africa: European ships carried manufactured goods (textiles, guns, metal goods).
    2. West Africa to the Americas (Middle Passage): Traded manufactured goods for enslaved people, who were then forcibly transported across the Atlantic.
    3. Americas to Europe: Sold enslaved people and loaded ships with colonial products (sugar, tobacco, cotton, rum) for return to Europe.
  • Profitability: Each leg of the triangle was profitable, with enslaved people being the central commodity.
  • Scale of the Slave Trade (1500-1867):
    • Approximately 12 million enslaved Africans were transported.
    • About 10.7 million arrived alive.
    • Approximately 1.8 million died on the Middle Passage.
    • Major Destinations: Brazil (4.9 million), Caribbean islands (4.7 million).
    • To what is now the U.S. About 400,000.

The Plantation Economy

  • Destination for Enslaved Africans: Most were sent to plantations in the Americas.
  • Driven by Cash Crops: Sugar was particularly significant, but tobacco and cotton were also key.
  • Intensive Labor: Sugar cultivation, especially in the Caribbean, required brutal, intensive labor in harsh tropical conditions.
  • High Death Rates: Enslaved workers died rapidly from overwork, disease, and mistreatment.
  • Economic Calculation: It was often considered cheaper by planters to work an enslaved person to death and purchase a new one than to maintain a healthy workforce.
  • Consequences:
    • Explains higher death rates and constant demand for new enslaved people in the Caribbean compared to North America, where the enslaved population grew through natural increase.
    • The plantation economy was the core economic logic of the entire Atlantic system, connecting European consumer demand for cash crops with coerced labor in the Americas. It was economically "rational" despite being morally monstrous.

Missionary Encounter

  • Motivations: European missionaries were often driven by genuine religious conviction to spread the Gospel.
  • Complexity:
    • Missionaries genuinely cared for the people they served.
    • They also represented European power and culture, sometimes paving the way for conquest.
    • Results varied widely: genuine conversion, mutual respect, cultural destruction, forced religious change.

Francis Xavier and the Jesuits in Asia

  • Francis Xavier (16th Century): A key Jesuit missionary, traveled to India, Japan, and attempted to enter China. Baptized tens of thousands.
  • Jesuit Sophistication: Jesuits developed nuanced approaches:
    • Matteo Ricci in China: Learned Chinese, studied Confucianism, and presented Christianity within Chinese intellectual frameworks.
    • Japan: Achieved significant conversions until the Tokugawa shogunate suppressed Christianity due to fears of European influence.

Reductions in Paraguay

  • Self-Governing Communities: Jesuits established reducciones for Guarani indigenous people in South America.
  • Protection: Aimed to operate outside Spanish colonial labor demands, protecting indigenous lives.
  • Combined Cultures: Integrated Catholic religious life with indigenous governance and agricultural practices.
  • Controversy: Resented by Spanish colonists for interfering with access to indigenous labor. Historians debate whether they were genuinely humane or paternalistic and culturally displacing. They collapsed after the Jesuits' expulsion in 1767.

Protestant Missions in Africa

  • Later Development: Accelerated in the 19th century.
  • David Livingstone: A Scottish missionary who explored Central Africa, combining evangelism with anti-slavery activism. His writings fueled British anti-slavery intervention in East Africa.
  • Complex Relationship with Colonialism: Many missionaries opposed exploitation, but their presence often preceded and facilitated British colonial control, even unintentionally.
  • Overall Verdict on Missions: Not monolithic. Figures like Las Casas and Wilberforce criticized colonial violence, while others brought genuine learning and faith. However, the missionary enterprise was deeply embedded in European power structures.

Comparing Expansions: Crusade and Colony

  • Similarities:
    • Outward movement of Western military, economic, and religious power.
    • Justified with religious arguments.
    • Produced cultural encounters and violence.
  • Differences:
    • Primary Aim: Crusades focused on holy sites/defending Christian territory; colonial expansion focused on trade and wealth.
    • Permanence: Crusades were finite expeditions; colonial expansion created permanent settler societies and economic systems.
    • Scale: The Atlantic slave trade alone affected far more people than the Crusades.
    • Nature of Opposition: The Christian tradition contained resources for self-criticism, allowing figures like Las Casas, Equiano, and the abolitionists to challenge colonial injustices on Christian principles, suggesting a capacity for moral growth.

Then and Now: The Legacy of Colonial Expansion

  • Shaping the Modern World: The age of expansion is foundational to:
    • The global economy.
    • Racial categories.
    • National borders.
    • Wealth distribution between the Global North and South.
  • Scale of European Control: By 1914, European nations controlled approximately 84% of the world's land surface.
  • Contributing Factors: Specific historical choices, technologies (gunpowder, steel, ships, railroads, telegraph), and economic systems (plantation economy, triangle trade, East India Company).
  • Comparison to Roman Empire:
    • Similarities: Extraction of wealth from conquered territories, use of local elites, justification through civilizational superiority claims.
    • Differences: Roman system was more integrated; colonial empires were more extractive.
    • Fundamental Dynamic: The center enriching itself from the periphery is a recognizable pattern.

Review Questions

  1. How did European colonial maps reflect and reinforce the imperial worldview, and what crucial aspects of reality did they often omit or distort?
  2. Describe the three main legs of the triangle trade, identifying the primary goods exchanged on each leg and explaining why enslaved people were considered the "commodity that made the whole system work."
  3. Explain the economic logic behind the plantation economy, particularly in the Caribbean. How did this "rational" economic calculation lead to vastly different demographic outcomes for enslaved populations compared to North America?
  4. Discuss the complex and often contradictory roles played by Christian missionaries during the age of expansion. Provide examples of both their benevolent actions and the ways their work could inadvertently facilitate colonial control.
  5. In what significant ways did the European colonial expansion differ from the earlier Crusades, and what does the emergence of figures like Las Casas and Equiano suggest about the Christian tradition during this period?
