Study Guide: Westphalia: The Birth of the Modern World

Overview

Between 1618 and 1648, Europe tore itself apart in the most devastating war it had ever known. The Thirty Years' War killed perhaps a third of Germany's population through battle, plague, and starvation. It began as a religious struggle and ended as something stranger and more modern: a war of states pursuing power for its own sake. The Peace of Westphalia that ended it did not simply stop the fighting — it invented a new way of organizing the world, establishing state sovereignty, legal equality of nations, and non-interference as the foundations of international relations still used today. This chapter also examines the rise of Louis XIV's absolute monarchy in France, the Dutch legal theorist Hugo Grotius who laid foundations for international law, and the literary witnesses Grotius and Schiller who grappled with what the war meant.

Key Concepts

  • The Last Religious War / The First Modern War: The Thirty Years' War began as a conflict over religion between Catholic and Protestant princes but ended as a power struggle in which Catholic France funded Protestant armies against the Catholic Habsburgs. This shift — raison d'etat replacing religious confession as the basis of foreign policy — marks the birth of modern international politics.
  • Raison d'etat: Cardinal Richelieu's calculated decision to support Protestant enemies of the Catholic Habsburgs to advance French national interest shocked contemporaries. It established the principle that states may act against their stated values when national interest demands it — a principle that remains both influential and controversial.
  • The Westphalian System: The three principles of the Peace of Westphalia (sovereignty, legal equality of states, non-interference) remain the foundational framework of international relations to this day. Every discussion of whether one country may intervene in another's affairs invokes Westphalian principles.
  • Grotius and the Christian Roots of International Law: Hugo Grotius drew on Roman law, Aquinas's natural law tradition, and Scripture to argue that nations are bound by moral law. International law is not a secular invention — it has deep roots in the Christian natural law tradition that runs from Aquinas through Grotius to modern institutions like the United Nations.
  • Absolute Monarchy vs. Constitutional Monarchy: Louis XIV's France and post-1689 England represent opposite answers to the question of how political authority should be organized. Louis controlled everything without institutional check; England's Parliament limited royal authority through law.

Important Terms

  • Defenestration of Prague (1618): The act of throwing three Catholic officials from a castle window in Prague by Protestant Bohemian nobles; triggered the Thirty Years' War.
  • Raison d'etat: French for "reason of state"; the idea that governments may take morally questionable actions if they serve national interest; associated with Machiavelli and Richelieu.
  • Edict of Restitution (1629): Emperor Ferdinand II's demand that Protestants return Catholic church property seized since 1552; appeared to signal total Catholic victory but provoked Swedish intervention.
  • Gustavus Adolphus: Swedish king called the "Lion of the North" who reformed the Swedish army and intervened decisively on the Protestant side; killed at the Battle of Lutzen (1632).
  • Peace of Westphalia (1648): The peace settlement ending the Thirty Years' War, negotiated in Osnabruck and Munster; established sovereignty, legal equality, and non-interference as the principles of international relations.
  • Sovereignty: The supreme authority of a government over its own territory and people, free from outside control; the central principle of international relations established at Westphalia.
  • International law: A body of rules governing relations between nations; founded by Grotius's Rights of War and Peace (1625), rooted in Christian natural law tradition.
  • Hugo Grotius (1583-1645): Dutch legal scholar; "father of international law"; argued nations are subject to natural law in The Rights of War and Peace (1625).
  • Absolute monarchy: A system in which the monarch holds supreme, unrestricted power; Louis XIV of France is the classic example.
  • Louis XIV (1638-1715): French king who embodied absolute monarchy; ruled 72 years; built Versailles; revoked the Edict of Nantes (1685).
  • Huguenots: French Protestants, mostly Calvinist; protected by the Edict of Nantes (1598) until Louis XIV revoked it in 1685; the Huguenot diaspora of 200,000-400,000 people damaged France and strengthened its rivals.
  • Edict of Nantes (1598): Henry IV's grant of religious freedom to French Protestants; revoked by Louis XIV in 1685.

Detailed Notes

The Thirty Years' War: Causes

  • The Holy Roman Empire
    • A vast patchwork of kingdoms, duchies, bishoprics, and free cities covering central Europe
    • Voltaire: "neither holy, nor Roman, nor much of an empire"
    • Deeply divided between Catholics and Protestants; uneasy truce breaking down
    • Each prince could choose his territory's religion; that arrangement was fracturing
  • The Trigger: Defenestration of Prague (1618)
    • Protestant Bohemian nobles threw three Catholic royal officials from a window
    • Officials survived (landed in a dung heap)
    • Act signaled open rebellion against Habsburg Catholic authority
    • Triggered the Bohemian revolt and the thirty-year war

The Four Phases

  • Bohemian Phase (1618-1625)
    • Protestant revolt against Emperor Ferdinand II, a devout Catholic
    • Protestant army crushed at the Battle of White Mountain (1620)
    • Bohemian nobility suppressed; Protestant worship banned; thousands fled
  • Danish Phase (1625-1629)
    • King Christian IV of Denmark entered for religious sympathy and strategic reasons
    • Quickly defeated by Imperial forces
    • Edict of Restitution (1629): Protestants must return church property seized since 1552
    • Looked like total Catholic victory
  • Swedish Phase (1630-1635)
    • Gustavus Adolphus: "Lion of the North"; reformed Swedish army with new tactics
    • Won major victories; drove deep into Germany
    • Killed at Battle of Lutzen (1632); general Oxenstierna kept Sweden fighting
  • French Phase (1635-1648)
    • Cardinal Richelieu had been secretly funding Protestant armies
    • France declared war openly on Habsburgs - not for religion but for raison d'etat
    • A Catholic cardinal funded Protestant armies to weaken a Catholic emperor
    • This ruthless calculation ended the age when religion alone explained European politics

The Human Cost

  • Germany as Battlefield
    • Primary battlefield for thirty years of marching armies, sieges, and deliberate destruction
    • Soldiers lived by looting countryside; famine followed stripped regions; plague spread
    • Lost between a quarter and a third of entire population
    • Most deaths from starvation and disease, not battle
    • Some regions lost more than half their people
  • The Sack of Magdeburg (1631)
    • Imperial troops stormed the Protestant city
    • Approximately 20,000 civilians killed; city burned to the ground
    • Historians call it the worst single atrocity of the war
    • Even General Tilly reportedly shed tears at the destruction

The Peace of Westphalia (1648)

  • The Negotiation
    • Ambassadors gathered in two Westphalian cities: Osnabruck and Munster
    • Negotiated for years; produced one of the most significant diplomatic documents in history
  • The Three Principles
    • State sovereignty: supreme authority within own territory; no external override
    • Equality of sovereign states: legal equality in international law regardless of size
    • Non-interference: no state may interfere in another's internal affairs
  • What Changed
    • Before 1648: wars often fought to impose universal religious/political order
    • After 1648: wars fought between sovereign states pursuing national interests
    • Pope and emperor lost authority to override national governance on religion

Hugo Grotius and International Law

  • The Rights of War and Peace (1625)
    • Written during the Thirty Years' War while it was still raging
    • Central argument: nations, like individuals, are subject to natural law they cannot repeal
    • Even in war: killing civilians without cause, breaking treaties, and treachery are forbidden
    • Law applies to all nations, Christian and non-Christian, because it flows from our rational nature
  • Intellectual Foundations
    • Drew on Roman law, Aquinas's natural law tradition, and Scripture
    • International law is not a secular invention; it has deep Christian roots
    • "Father of international law"; Peace of Westphalia partially implemented his ideas

Louis XIV and Absolute Monarchy

  • The Sun King
    • Became king at age four; ruled 72 years (longest major European reign)
    • Embodied absolute monarchy: controlled army, courts, church, economy
    • Brought nobility to Versailles to weaken them; governed through loyal ministers
    • "L'Etat, c'est moi": whether or not he said it, it captures his philosophy
  • The Huguenot Catastrophe
    • Revoked Edict of Nantes (1685): removed Protestant freedoms granted in 1598
    • 200,000-400,000 Huguenots fled France with their skills and capital
    • Strengthened France's rivals: England, Dutch Republic, Prussia, South Africa
    • A catastrophic self-inflicted wound driven by religious intolerance

Literature: Grotius and Schiller

  • Grotius: Is There Law in War?
    • Acknowledged the common view: "in war laws are silent"
    • Refuted it by appeal to "right reason and conscience"
    • Natural law binds nations: the same moral order that governs individuals governs states
  • Schiller: History as Moral Witness
    • History of the Thirty Years' War (1790s): based on archival research
    • Account of Magdeburg: preserved horror without propaganda
    • Even included Tilly's tears: commanders intended one thing, war produced another
    • Both Grotius and Schiller ask: how do humans build rules that limit their own destruction?

Connections

  • The Westphalian principles of sovereignty and non-interference remain the framework of the United Nations and modern international relations — and the source of ongoing controversy when humanitarian intervention is proposed.
  • Grotius's rooting of international law in Christian natural law connects back to Aquinas (earlier curricula) and forward to the development of international human rights law.
  • Louis XIV's revocation of the Edict of Nantes parallels Philip III's expulsion of the Moriscos from Spain (Chapter 2): both acts of religious intolerance caused serious self-inflicted economic harm.
  • The contrast between France's absolute monarchy and England's constitutional monarchy (Chapter 1) is the central political comparison of 17th-century Europe, with consequences extending to the American and French Revolutions (Chapter 4).
  • The Thirty Years' War's demonstration that religiously motivated politics produces catastrophic results connects to the Enlightenment's project of grounding politics in reason rather than religious confession (Chapter 4).
