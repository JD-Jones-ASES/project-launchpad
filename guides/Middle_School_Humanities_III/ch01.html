Study Guide: Queen Elizabeth I to the Glorious Revolution

Overview

From Elizabeth I's accession in 1558 to the English Bill of Rights in 1689, England transformed from a kingdom torn by religious rivalry into the world's first constitutional monarchy. Along the way the English fought a civil war, executed their king, and invited a foreign prince to take the throne — all in the name of law, liberty, and parliamentary government. This era also produced Shakespeare, Milton, Newton, and Francis Bacon, making it one of the most creative centuries in English history. The central constitutional achievement — parliamentary supremacy — would directly shape the American founding and the modern democratic tradition.

Key Concepts

  • The Elizabethan Settlement: Elizabeth I's brilliant religious compromise of 1559-1563 made England officially Protestant while retaining enough Catholic forms to satisfy moderates. By refusing to "open windows into men's souls," she prioritized outward conformity over private belief, avoiding the religious civil wars that devastated France and the Holy Roman Empire for most of her 45-year reign.
  • Divine Right of Kings: The Stuart doctrine that monarchs answer only to God, not to Parliament or subjects. James I and Charles I used this belief to justify arbitrary taxation, imprisonment without cause, and ultimately eleven years of personal rule — the direct cause of the Civil War.
  • Parliamentary Sovereignty: The hard-won principle that Parliament is the supreme law-making authority in England, permanently established by the Glorious Revolution and the Bill of Rights. Every constitutional struggle of this era — the Petition of Right, the Civil War, the Glorious Revolution — was a battle over this principle.
  • The Social Contract (John Locke): Published in 1689 in direct response to the Glorious Revolution, Locke argued that government exists by the consent of the governed and may be overthrown when it violates natural rights. This idea directly shaped American independence and constitutional government worldwide.
  • Freedom of the Press: Milton's Areopagitica (1644) made the first great English argument that truth will defeat falsehood in open debate without government help. Though Parliament ignored it initially, the argument won the intellectual debate and the Licensing Act lapsed in 1695 — influencing the First Amendment (1791).

Important Terms

  • Elizabethan Settlement: The religious compromise of 1559-1563 making England Protestant while retaining Catholic worship forms; aimed at peace through outward conformity rather than enforced belief.
  • Divine right of kings: The belief that a monarch's authority comes from God alone and cannot be limited by Parliament; used by James I and Charles I to justify arbitrary rule.
  • Petition of Right (1628): Parliament's declaration of specific royal abuses demanding Charles I acknowledge limits on his authority; a foundational constitutional document alongside Magna Carta.
  • Personal Rule (1629-1640): Charles I's eleven-year period of governing without Parliament, funded by controversial taxes like ship money; ended by the Scottish rebellion.
  • Cavaliers and Roundheads: The two sides of the English Civil War; Cavaliers supported the king, Roundheads supported Parliament (named for their short-cropped hair).
  • New Model Army: Parliament's reorganized army under Fairfax and Cromwell; its discipline proved decisive in the Civil War.
  • Commonwealth (1649-1653): The republican government after Charles I's execution; England governed by Parliament and a Council of State without a king.
  • Protectorate (1653-1659): Oliver Cromwell's government as Lord Protector; near-royal power without the crown; collapsed after his death.
  • Restoration (1660): The return of Charles II and the monarchy; also restored the Church of England and the theaters.
  • Glorious Revolution (1688): The nearly bloodless transfer of the crown from James II to William and Mary; permanently established Parliamentary supremacy.
  • English Bill of Rights (1689): The conditions Parliament imposed on William and Mary; prohibited royal suspension of laws, arbitrary taxation, and standing armies without Parliament; banned excessive bail and cruel punishments.
  • Parliamentary supremacy: The principle that Parliament is the supreme law-making authority and no monarch may override Acts of Parliament.
  • Habeas corpus: The legal protection requiring the government to charge a prisoner and bring them before a court; Latin for "you shall have the body"; strengthened by the 1679 Act.
  • Areopagitica (1644): Milton's argument against pre-publication censorship; first great defense of freedom of the press in English.

Detailed Notes

The Elizabethan Era (1558-1603)

  • The Religious Settlement
    • England had changed official religion four times in 25 years before Elizabeth
    • Settlement (1559) re-established Church of England; required clergy to accept royal supremacy
    • Book of Common Prayer designed to satisfy both moderate Protestants and traditionalists
    • Result: forty-five years of relative peace compared to France's religious civil wars
  • Mary Queen of Scots
    • Catholic cousin with plausible claim to English throne; fled Scotland 1568
    • Spent 19 years as Elizabeth's prisoner; focal point for Catholic plots
    • Executed February 1587 after endorsing assassination plot
    • Execution enraged Philip II; immediate context for the Armada
  • The Spanish Armada (1588)
    • Philip II's 130-ship fleet intended to escort a Spanish army from the Netherlands to conquer England
    • Defeated by combination: English tactics (smaller faster ships, fire vessels), Drake's leadership, Atlantic storms
    • Roughly half the fleet destroyed; England not invaded
    • Victory crystallized English Protestant national identity and emphasis on sea power

The Stuart Kings (1603-1642)

  • James I (r. 1603-1625)
    • Son of Mary Queen of Scots; united English and Scottish crowns
    • Intelligent but clumsy with Parliament; believed passionately in divine right
    • Major achievement: King James Bible (1611), completed by 54 scholars
  • Charles I and the Road to War
    • Extended ship money tax to the entire country without Parliamentary consent
    • Petition of Right (1628): Parliament demanded he acknowledge constitutional limits
    • Charles signed it to get taxes; then ignored it
    • Personal Rule (1629-1640): governed without Parliament
    • Scottish rebellion over new prayer book (1637) forced him to recall Parliament
    • "Short Parliament" (April-May 1640) dissolved when it refused to cooperate
    • "Long Parliament" (November 1640) refused to be dismissed

The Civil War and Interregnum (1642-1660)

  • The Civil War
    • Cavaliers (royalists) vs. Roundheads (Parliamentarians) from 1642-1651
    • New Model Army under Fairfax and Cromwell decisive
    • Charles I captured, tried, and executed January 30, 1649
    • First time in European history a reigning monarch was publicly tried and executed by his subjects
  • The Commonwealth and Protectorate
    • Commonwealth (1649-1653): republic governed by Parliament and Council of State
    • Oliver Cromwell: devout Puritan, brilliant general, increasingly authoritarian ruler
    • Protectorate (1653-1659): Cromwell as Lord Protector with near-royal powers
    • Cromwell refused the crown but dissolved Parliament when it displeased him
    • Died 1658; son Richard lacked his authority and resigned within months

The Restoration and Glorious Revolution (1660-1689)

  • The Restoration
    • Charles II returned from French exile in 1660; charming and politically shrewd
    • Restored monarchy, Church of England, and theaters
    • Avoided direct conflict with Parliament
  • James II's Provocations
    • Catholic king appointed Catholics to military and civil offices
    • Suspended laws keeping Catholics out of public life
    • Birth of Catholic male heir (June 1688): guaranteed Catholic succession
    • Leading Protestants invited William of Orange and his wife Mary (James's daughter)
  • The Glorious Revolution
    • William landed with army November 1688; James II fled to France
    • Parliament declared James had abdicated and offered crown to William and Mary
    • English Bill of Rights (1689): conditions under which crown was offered
    • Parliamentary supremacy permanently established as constitutional principle

Literature of the Era

  • Shakespeare's Henry V (c. 1599)
    • Written during tension over Elizabeth's succession
    • The Williams exchange: does the king bear moral responsibility for soldiers' souls?
    • Henry's answer: "Every subject's duty is the king's; but every subject's soul is his own."
    • St. Crispin's Day speech: shared sacrifice creates brotherhood transcending class
  • Milton's Areopagitica (1644)
    • Written against Parliament's Licensing Order requiring pre-publication censorship
    • Central argument: "Let her and Falsehood grapple; who ever knew Truth put to the worse?"
    • Most essential freedom: "the liberty to know, to utter, and to argue freely according to conscience"
    • Influenced Locke, American founders, First Amendment
  • The English Bill of Rights (1689)
    • A declaration of existing law, not a new grant of rights
    • Each provision addresses a specific Stuart abuse
    • "Cruel and unusual punishments" language directly influenced the American Eighth Amendment

Connections

  • The Elizabethan Settlement's pragmatism echoes throughout English constitutional history: the preference for workable compromise over theoretical purity.
  • The divine right doctrine challenged here connects directly to Locke's refutation of it in the Two Treatises (1689), which in turn shaped Jefferson's Declaration of Independence (1776).
  • Milton's Areopagitica is the direct ancestor of the First Amendment's press freedom guarantee.
  • The English Bill of Rights (1689) is the immediate ancestor of the American Bill of Rights (1791).
  • Habeas corpus remains a foundational protection in American, British, and Commonwealth law today.
  • The contrast between England's constitutional monarchy and France's absolute monarchy (Chapter 3) illuminates why these two nations developed such different political trajectories through the 18th century.
