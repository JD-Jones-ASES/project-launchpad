The Two Faces of War: Literature of World War I

Overview

This section explores the profoundly different literary responses to World War I by German veterans Ernst Jünger and Erich Maria Remarque, published in the same year, 1929. It also introduces British officer-poet Wilfred Owen. By contrasting Jünger's portrayal of war as a test of spirit and a source of exhilaration with Remarque's depiction of war as a relentless destroyer of a generation, the section highlights how individual perspective shapes the truth of lived experience and the complex nature of historical memory.

Key Concepts

  • Contrasting Perspectives on War: The section demonstrates that the same historical event (World War I) can be experienced and described truthfully in profoundly different, even incompatible, ways by individuals. Jünger found meaning and exhilaration, while Remarque found only destruction and despair.
  • The Nature of Primary Sources: Literary works, like other primary sources, are not objective accounts but reflections of their author's unique perspective, experiences, and conclusions. Understanding history requires analyzing these varied perspectives.
  • War as a Test (Jünger): Ernst Jünger viewed combat as a crucible that revealed intense aliveness, courage, and the bonds between soldiers. For him, "clean fear" sharpened the senses, and endurance was the highest form of respect for the fallen.
  • War as Destruction (Remarque): Erich Maria Remarque's All Quiet on the Western Front illustrates how the war systematically stripped young soldiers of their youth, idealism, and future, leaving them emotionally scarred and disconnected from civilian life.
  • The Ancient Tension: The differing views of Jünger and Remarque reflect a timeless human struggle to understand war – as both glorious and terrible – a tension also present in ancient works like Homer's Iliad.
  • Remembering War: Societies often choose to remember war through the lens of heroism, sacrifice, and endurance (Jünger's mode), while literature frequently preserves the other mode of loss and destruction (Remarque's mode). A complete historical understanding requires both.

Important Terms

  • Western Front: The main theater of war during World War I in Western Europe, characterized by trench warfare and immense casualties. Both Jünger and Remarque served here.
  • Pour le Mérite: Germany's highest military honor for bravery, awarded to Ernst Jünger.
  • Armistice: An agreement made by opposing sides in a war to stop fighting; a truce. Wilfred Owen died one week before the Armistice that ended World War I on November 11, 1918.
  • Primary Sources: Original materials from the time period under study, such as diaries, letters, photographs, and literary works like memoirs or novels, offering direct insights into historical events and perspectives.
  • Memoir: A historical account or biography written from personal knowledge or special sources, often based on diaries or recollections. The Storm of Steel is a memoir.
  • Conscripted: To be compulsorily enlisted for military service. Erich Maria Remarque was conscripted into the German Army.
  • Shell-shocked: A term used during World War I to describe the psychological trauma experienced by soldiers due to prolonged exposure to combat, intense bombardment, and horrific conditions. Wilfred Owen experienced this.
  • Endurance: The capacity of something to last or to withstand wear and tear. Jünger linked endurance directly to a soldier's duty and love for fallen comrades.
  • Idealism: The practice of forming or pursuing ideals, especially unrealistically. Remarque's novel portrays the destruction of the initial idealism of young recruits.

Detailed Notes

The Same War, Opposite Responses

  • The section introduces two German veterans, Ernst Jünger and Erich Maria Remarque, who both wrote about their Western Front experiences in books published in 1929.
  • Despite sharing the same trenches, artillery, mud, and death, they arrived at vastly different conclusions: Jünger saw war as a magnificent test, while Remarque saw it as meaningless anti-war devastation.
  • A third voice, British officer-poet Wilfred Owen, also wrote powerfully about the war before dying just one week before the Armistice.
  • Pedagogical Purpose: The comparison highlights that primary sources reflect the author's perspective. Two honest witnesses can describe the same event truthfully but incompatibly. This is not relativism but history, requiring students to analyze differences. This tension is ancient, akin to how Homer's Iliad portrays war as both glorious and terrible.

Ernst Jünger: The Soldier as Hunter

  • Biography:
    • Born 1895, died 1998.
    • Enlisted at 19, served four years on the Western Front.
    • Wounded 14 times, received the Pour le Mérite (Germany's highest military honor).
    • His memoir, The Storm of Steel, was written from his wartime diaries, published in German (1920) and English (1929).
    • Controversial figure post-war, celebrated war and nationalism but not a Nazi. Lived to 102.
  • Perspective on War:
    • Jünger acknowledges the war's terror (blood, death, destruction) with a "precise, clinical eye."
    • However, he also describes exhilaration, pride, intense aliveness in danger, and the bond between soldiers.
    • Night Raid Passage: Describes a night raid into enemy lines. Jünger felt "cold clarity," all "fog of daily life had burned away." Fear was "clean," sharpening rather than paralyzing. He felt "more fully alive" and "tested."
    • Aftermath of Loss Passage: After a long bombardment, he counts his dead and wounded. He explains that "mourning came later," but in the field, the dead demanded "endurance." He believed "love for the dead is best expressed not in tears but in endurance."
  • Teacher Note Insights: Jünger's response (intense aliveness, clean fear) is a documented human response to extreme danger, not pathological. His processing of loss through endurance should be compared to Remarque's characters who cease enduring. Jünger's mode often aligns with how war is remembered in memorials.

Erich Maria Remarque: The Generation That Was Destroyed

  • Biography:
    • Born 1898, died 1970.
    • Conscripted into German Army in 1916 at 18, served briefly, wounded by shrapnel.
    • Changed his name to honor his mother and use French spelling.
    • His novel, All Quiet on the Western Front, published in German (1928) and English (1929), sold 2.5 million copies in 18 months.
    • Nazis burned his books and revoked his citizenship; he emigrated.
  • Perspective on War:
    • Remarque's novel, narrated by Paul Bäumer, shows how the war destroys young men—not just physically, but by eroding their idealism, youth, and ability to feel at home in the world.
    • Counterattack Passage: Paul describes being overwhelmed by a wave of ferocity, turning soldiers into "thugs, into murderers," driven by fear and madness, seeking only "deliverance." They strike without hesitation, even at their own fathers. Afterward, "there is not much left of us."
    • Reflection on Generation Passage: Paul states, "We are not youth any longer." They are "fleeing from ourselves. From our life." The war "burst in our hearts," cutting them off from progress and purpose. They "believe in such things no longer. We believe in the war."
  • Teacher Note Insights: The line "We believe in the war" is devastating, meaning war has become the sole trusted reality, replacing all prior hopes and beliefs. Remarque's portrayal (war as destruction of self) stands in stark contrast to Jünger's (war as affirmation of self). Students should consider if either or both are "the truth."

Wilfred Owen: The Pity of War

  • Biography:
    • Born 1893, died 1918.
    • British officer on the Western Front.
    • Suffered shell-shock, met poet Siegfried Sassoon in hospital, who encouraged his writing.
    • Returned to the front and was killed by machine-gun fire on November 4, 1918, one week before the Armistice.
    • Known for powerfully expressing "the pity of war." (The section ends abruptly here).

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast Ernst Jünger's and Erich Maria Remarque's core experiences and conclusions about World War I. What specific elements from their provided passages best illustrate their differing views?
  2. The textbook states that Jünger and Remarque are "two honest witnesses to the same catastrophe" who drew "different conclusions." What does this tell us about the nature of truth in historical accounts, especially those based on personal experience?
  3. Jünger describes "clean fear" and "intense aliveness" during combat, while Remarque's Paul Bäumer feels overwhelmed by a wave of "ferocity" and becomes a "murderer." How do these descriptions reflect their overall perspectives on the impact of war on the individual soldier?
  4. Jünger writes that "love for the dead is best expressed not in tears but in endurance." How does this philosophy contrast with the despair and sense of being "cut off" from the future expressed by Remarque's Paul Bäumer?
  5. Considering the teacher's note that "monuments, memorials, and official histories tend toward Jünger's mode," what are the implications of choosing one mode of memory (heroism and endurance) over another (destruction and loss) when teaching or remembering war?
