Study Guide: Modern Problems

Overview

By 1848 the Industrial Revolution had transformed Europe beyond recognition. Factories replaced fields; cities swelled with workers laboring twelve hours a day for wages that barely kept them alive. Children hauled coal in mines. Karl Marx looked at this suffering and declared all history was a war between rich and poor, and that workers would overthrow their oppressors. Pope Leo XIII looked at the same suffering and declared workers had God-given rights to just wages — but that destroying property and inciting class war would only make things worse. Fyodor Dostoevsky asked whether human beings could really be saved by bread and ideology alone. This chapter examines the Industrial Revolution and the great argument it provoked: What do human beings owe each other, and who is responsible for the poor?

Key Concepts

  • The Industrial Revolution's Double Legacy: The Industrial Revolution produced enormous new wealth and enormous new misery simultaneously. Understanding both — the genuine economic progress and the systematic human suffering — is essential to understanding the political upheavals of the 19th century.
  • The Social Question: The central political debate of 1840s-1914 Europe: What does a just society owe to workers? Three answers competed: liberal capitalism (the market will solve it), Marxist socialism (only revolution can solve it), and Catholic Social Teaching (moral reform rooted in human dignity, without either market anarchy or revolutionary destruction).
  • Marx's Diagnosis vs. Prescription: The text insists students distinguish these. Marx's diagnosis was sharp and largely accurate: children were in coal mines, workers were earning starvation wages, industrial capitalism's brutality was real. His prescription failed catastrophically because his atheism rejected moral checks on power, his materialism reduced persons to economic units, and his utopianism believed perfection achievable by force.
  • Rerum Novarum as Third Way: Pope Leo XIII refused both laissez-faire ("the market will fix it") and Marxism ("only revolution can fix it"). His foundation was theological: workers are human beings made in the image of God (imago Dei), and any system treating them as mere economic instruments violates their inherent dignity. This provided a framework for reform without revolution.
  • The Dostoevsky Question: Beneath all the political arguments about bread and wages and property, Dostoevsky asks a deeper question: what does it mean to be truly free? His Grand Inquisitor suggests that most people fear their own freedom more than oppression. This challenges all utopian politics — socialist, liberal, or otherwise.

Important Terms

  • Industrial Revolution: Transformation from agriculture and hand-production to machine manufacturing and factory labor; began in Britain c. 1760-1780; spread across Europe through the 19th century.
  • Social question: The 19th-century debate over how industrial society should respond to poverty, labor exploitation, and inequality; generated competing answers from liberal capitalism, socialism, and Catholic Social Teaching.
  • Karl Marx (1818-1883): German philosopher and author of The Communist Manifesto (1848) and Das Kapital; diagnosed industrial capitalism's exploitation accurately; prescribed catastrophically wrong solution of violent revolution.
  • Communist Manifesto: Published February 1848 by Marx and Engels; argued all history is class struggle; predicted inevitable proletarian revolution; prescribed violent overthrow of capitalism and abolition of private property.
  • Bourgeoisie: In Marxist analysis, the class owning the means of production (factories, land, capital); the capitalist ruling class.
  • Proletariat: In Marxist analysis, the working class owning no property; must sell their labor to survive; Marx predicted they would overthrow the bourgeoisie.
  • Class struggle: Marx's central concept: the conflict between economic classes (owners vs. workers) is the engine of all historical change.
  • Revolutions of 1848: Wave of European revolutions in 1848; mostly led by liberal middle class, not factory workers; all suppressed within two years.
  • Rerum Novarum: Pope Leo XIII's 1891 encyclical "On the Condition of Labor"; founding document of Catholic Social Teaching; rejected both socialism and laissez-faire capitalism.
  • Imago Dei: Latin "image of God" (Genesis 1:27); every human being possesses inherent dignity that no economic system may violate; the theological foundation of Rerum Novarum.
  • Catholic Social Teaching: Body of official Church teaching on social, economic, and political questions beginning with Rerum Novarum; principles include human dignity, common good, private property, option for the poor, and subsidiarity.
  • Laissez-faire: "Let it do"; economic philosophy of government non-interference in markets; rejected by Rerum Novarum because workers are persons, not commodities.
  • Subsidiarity: The principle that decisions should be made at the most local level capable of handling them; prevents both state overcentralization and complete market anarchy.
  • Pope Pius X (1835-1914): Leo XIII's successor; focused on condemning theological "Modernism" within the Church; died days after WWI began.

Detailed Notes

The Industrial Revolution

  • Origins in Britain
    • Britain had coal, iron, rivers for power, stable property rights, commerce-friendly Parliament, and empire
    • Key inventions: spinning jenny (1764), steam engine (1769), power loom
    • By 1820: Britain was the factory floor of the world
    • Spread across Europe through the 19th century
  • The Human Cost
    • A new kind of poverty: systematic, year-round, bound to the machine's rhythms
    • Workers (women and children included): 12-16 hours/day, 6 days/week
    • Children as young as 5 in coal mines; crawling through tunnels too small for adults
    • No education; many never learned to read
    • Urban slums: no clean water, no sanitation, no fresh air
    • Cholera epidemics from sewage-contaminated water
  • Charles Dickens as Witness
    • Oliver Twist (1837): orphaned children sold to workhouses
    • Hard Times (1854): "Coketown" where workers were called "Hands"
    • Writing journalism in the form of fiction; documenting real conditions

Karl Marx and The Communist Manifesto

  • The Manifesto's Three Parts
    • Diagnosis: "The history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles"
    • History: ancient masters and slaves → medieval lords and serfs → industrial bourgeoisie and proletariat
    • The bourgeoisie "put an end to all feudal, patriarchal, idyllic relations... left remaining no other nexus between man and man than naked self-interest"
    • Prediction: Industrial Revolution concentrating wealth; proletariat will inevitably rise up
    • Prescription: "The Communists disdain to conceal... their ends can only be attained by the forcible overthrow of all existing social conditions"
    • Final line: "Workers of the world, unite!"
  • Marx's Real Insight
    • His description of suffering was accurate: children in mines, starvation wages, real
    • His portrait of capitalism reducing all human relationships to cash captures something true
    • The diagnosis was sharp
  • Why the Prescription Failed
    • Atheism: rejected the natural law tradition that provides a moral check on power
    • Materialism: reduced human beings to economic units, not persons made in God's image
    • Utopianism: believed a perfect society achievable on earth by political force
    • 20th-century record: Soviet Union (20 million killed under Stalin), Communist China (45 million in Great Leap Forward), Cambodia under Pol Pot (quarter of population)

The Revolutions of 1848

  • The Wave
    • Manifesto published February 1848; revolutions in France, Austria, Hungary, Italy, German states within weeks
    • Causes: bad harvest 1847, urban poverty, frustrated nationalism, middle class wanting constitutions
    • Marx hoped they would become the proletarian uprising he predicted
  • The Failure
    • Most led by liberal middle-class reformers, not factory workers
    • Within two years, conservative governments suppressed nearly all
    • France: Louis-Napoleon elected president → became Emperor Napoleon III
    • Austria: crushed Hungarian revolution with Russian help
    • German states: returned to authoritarian rule
  • The Lesson
    • Spontaneous popular uprisings not enough
    • Organized, disciplined revolutionary movements needed
    • Shaped development of socialist and Communist parties for the rest of the century

Rerum Novarum and Catholic Social Teaching

  • Leo XIII's Response (1891)
    • Agreed with Marx: industrial workers were being exploited; exploitation was unjust
    • Against socialism: private property is a natural right; abolishing it hands unlimited power to state
    • Against laissez-faire: employers must pay a living wage; workers are persons, not commodities
    • Workers' rights: just wages, safe conditions, freedom to form unions
    • Goal: not class war but harmony between capital and labor
  • The Foundation: Imago Dei
    • Every human being made in God's image (Genesis 1:27)
    • Inherent dignity that no economic system may violate
    • The entire argument for workers' rights rests on this theological foundation, not on economics
  • Catholic Social Teaching Principles
    • Human dignity (imago Dei)
    • The common good
    • The right to private property
    • The option for the poor
    • Subsidiarity: decisions at most local capable level
    • Offers a "third way" between unrestrained capitalism and socialism
  • The State's Role
    • The state has a positive duty to protect workers: limit hours, prohibit child labor, ensure living wages
    • Workers have the right to form unions to negotiate with employers
    • Balance: neither market anarchy nor state control

Pius X and Theological Modernism

  • The Contrast with Leo XIII
    • Leo XIII engaged economic and social modernity with a reasoned alternative
    • Pius X (1903-1914) focused on perceived theological threats within the Church itself
  • The Modernist Crisis
    • Some Catholic theologians applied methods of modern historical scholarship to Scripture and doctrine
    • Pius X condemned this as "the synthesis of all heresies" (1907)
    • Required clergy to swear anti-Modernist oath
    • Established internal surveillance boards in every diocese
    • Widely seen as overly harsh; created intellectual suspicion within Catholicism for decades
  • Pius X's Social Teaching
    • Continued in Leo XIII's tradition: supported Catholic trade unions and lay organizations
    • Died August 1914, days after WWI began; "grieved deeply" at the war

The European Political Landscape 1848-1914

  • Germany Under Bismarck
    • German unification 1871 under Prussian leadership after swift military victory over France
    • Bismarck: "Iron Chancellor"; introduced first national social insurance system
    • Motivation: reduce socialist appeal, not idealism
    • Model for the modern welfare state
  • Britain
    • Parliament gradually extended the vote
    • Factory acts regulated working hours and conditions
  • Russia
    • Most autocratic and backward of the great powers
    • 1905 revolution: Tsar suppressed but could not fully reverse
  • By 1914
    • Socialist parties major forces in nearly every European country
    • Pressures building: nationalism, imperial rivalry, social conflict
    • The social question would not be resolved — it would be interrupted by the most destructive war in history

Literature: Three Voices on One Crisis

  • Marx (1848): The Indictment
    • "The history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles"
    • Powerful writer; his portrait of capitalism reducing humanity to cash is devastating
    • But: his prescription required forcible overthrow of all existing social conditions
    • 20th-century record proved: when you build on the premise that all existing order must be destroyed, you produce violence at the scale of your ambitions
  • Leo XIII (1891): The Alternative
    • Workers' dignity is God-given; employers have moral obligations
    • Private property is a natural right; abolishing it creates tyranny
    • Reform, not revolution; harmony, not class war
  • Dostoevsky (1880): The Deeper Question
    • The Brothers Karamazov: "The Grand Inquisitor"
    • What does it mean to be truly free?
    • Could it be that humans most fear not oppression but the weight of their own freedom?
    • The Grand Inquisitor offers bread and belonging in exchange for freedom
    • Challenge to all utopian politics: socialist, Enlightenment, or otherwise

Connections

  • The imago Dei principle that grounds Rerum Novarum connects back through Las Casas (Chapter 2) and Equiano (Chapter 5) to the entire Christian tradition of grounding human dignity in God's creation of humanity in His image.
  • Marx's atheism and materialism connect to the Enlightenment's project of grounding everything in human reason alone (Chapter 4) — demonstrating what happens when that project is carried to its logical extreme without the Christian restraint the text discusses.
  • Bismarck's social insurance anticipates the modern welfare state; it also shows that even non-ideological conservative leaders recognized that industrial capitalism's social costs required political response.
  • Dostoevsky's question about freedom connects forward to the political catastrophes of the 20th century: the great totalitarian regimes of fascism and Communism both offered security and belonging in exchange for freedom.
  • The unresolved social question of this chapter becomes one of the pressures leading to World War I (Chapter 7) and remains unresolved even today.
