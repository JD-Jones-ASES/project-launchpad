Chapter 1, Section 2: Science - Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the revolutionary shift in the pursuit of knowledge that occurred in seventeenth-century England, paralleling the era's political upheavals. It highlights how figures like Francis Bacon challenged ancient authorities by advocating for observation and experimentation (the inductive method) as the foundation of knowledge. The establishment of the Royal Society fostered a collaborative scientific community, culminating in Isaac Newton's groundbreaking synthesis of the natural world, forever changing humanity's understanding of the universe and emphasizing a new demand for evidence and accountability in both science and governance.

Key Concepts

  • Challenge to Ancient Authority: For nearly two millennia, knowledge about the natural world was largely derived from the writings of Aristotle and his commentators. The 17th century saw a revolutionary rejection of this reliance on unquestioned tradition in favor of direct observation and empirical testing.
  • The Inductive Method (Baconian Science): Championed by Francis Bacon, this method involves moving from specific observations and experiments to general conclusions. It emphasizes gathering evidence, identifying patterns, and drawing conclusions only from what has been directly observed and tested, in stark contrast to starting with general principles (deduction).
  • "Nature to be commanded must be obeyed": This key Baconian principle asserts that to effectively utilize or control nature (e.g., build machines, cure diseases), one must first understand its actual workings through systematic observation and experiment, rather than imposing preconceived ideas.
  • The Royal Society: Founded in London in 1660, this organization formalized and promoted the new scientific approach. Its motto, Nullius in verba ("Take nobody's word for it"), epitomized its commitment to evidence and experimentation over authority, fostering a community where discoveries were shared and scrutinized.
  • Newtonian Synthesis: Isaac Newton's Principia Mathematica (1687) represented a monumental achievement, unifying seemingly disparate phenomena (like falling apples and planetary orbits) under a single mathematical law of universal gravitation. This demonstrated the universe as a rational, orderly system governed by consistent laws.
  • Science and Political Revolution: The intellectual shifts in science mirrored the political transformations of the 17th century (e.g., English Civil War, Glorious Revolution). Both movements fundamentally questioned established authority, demanding evidence and accountability—whether to nature, to law, or to the people.

Important Terms

  • Aristotle: An ancient Greek philosopher whose works were the primary authority on the natural world in medieval Europe for almost two thousand years.
  • Francis Bacon (1561–1626): English lawyer, philosopher, and statesman; articulated the inductive method and critiqued reliance on ancient authority.
  • Idols: Bacon's term for mental habits, prejudices, and biases that distort objective observation and prevent clear understanding of the natural world.
  • Inductive method: The foundational approach to modern science, advocated by Bacon, which moves from specific observations and experiments to general conclusions. Also known as the "scientific method."
  • Deduction (Aristotelian): The traditional method of reasoning that starts with accepted general principles and derives particular conclusions; contrasted by Bacon with the inductive method.
  • Galileo Galilei (1564–1642): Italian polymath who made significant contributions to astronomy (confirming Earth orbits the Sun) and the study of motion through observation and experimentation, often clashing with established authorities.
  • Royal Society (of London for Improving Natural Knowledge): An organization founded in 1660 and chartered in 1662 by Charles II to promote scientific inquiry through observation and experiment. The oldest continuously operating scientific society in the world.
  • Nullius in verba: The Latin motto of the Royal Society, meaning "Take nobody's word for it," expressing its commitment to evidence over unquestioned authority.
  • Philosophical Transactions: The world's first scientific journal, published by the Royal Society beginning in 1665, facilitating the sharing and verification of experimental results.
  • Robert Boyle (1627–1691): Early member of the Royal Society, often called the founder of modern chemistry; developed the air pump and formulated Boyle's Law.
  • Christopher Wren (1632–1723): Early member of the Royal Society, astronomer and England's greatest architect (e.g., St. Paul's Cathedral).
  • Isaac Newton (1643–1727): English physicist and mathematician, a towering figure in the Royal Society; discovered the laws of motion and universal gravitation, invented calculus, and published Principia Mathematica.
  • Principia Mathematica (1687): Isaac Newton's seminal work which presented his theory of universal gravitation and laws of motion, unifying terrestrial and celestial mechanics.
  • Natural philosophy: The term used in the 17th century for the study of the natural world through observation, experiment, and mathematics, which we now call "science." The word "scientist" was coined much later (1833).
  • Peer review: A modern scientific practice (descended from the Royal Society's Nullius in verba) where scientific findings are critically evaluated by independent experts before acceptance.

Detailed Notes

A New Way of Knowing: Challenging Authority

  • The Seventeenth-Century Intellectual Shift: The 17th century in England saw a profound shift in thinking, mirroring political struggles over governmental authority. Scholars began to question who held ultimate authority in knowledge itself.
  • Aristotle's Dominance: For almost 2,000 years, Aristotle's writings were the unquestioned source of knowledge about the natural world in European universities. Answers to scientific questions were derived from his texts through reasoning, rather than direct observation.
  • The Flaw in Deduction: Aristotle's method was largely deductive, starting from accepted principles. If the initial principle was incorrect, all subsequent conclusions would be flawed, and the method offered no built-in way to check these conclusions against reality. For example, Aristotle's claim that heavier objects fall faster went unchallenged by experiment for centuries.

Francis Bacon and the Inductive Method

  • Bacon's Role: Francis Bacon, a lawyer and philosopher, was a pivotal figure not as a primary scientist, but as a philosopher of science. He articulated why the old method was flawed and proposed a new, systematic approach.
  • Critique of the Mind ("Idols"): Bacon argued that the human mind, left to its own devices, is prone to "idols"—prejudices and mental habits that distort observation and lead to false conclusions.
  • The Inductive Solution: To overcome these idols, Bacon proposed the inductive method:
    1. Gather Evidence: Systematically collect data and observations from the natural world.
    2. Look for Patterns: Analyze the collected evidence to identify recurring patterns and relationships.
    3. Draw Conclusions: Formulate general principles or theories only from what has been directly observed and tested, avoiding preconceived notions.
  • Novum Organum (1620): Bacon's great work laid out this new methodology, emphasizing that human understanding and power are limited by what we observe and understand about nature.
  • "Nature to be commanded must be obeyed": This famous Baconian aphorism highlights the practical utility of the inductive method. To harness nature (e.g., for technology or medicine), one must first understand its true workings, rather than trying to impose human will without knowledge. This proved the inductive method superior not just philosophically, but practically.
  • Contrast with Deduction: Bacon directly contrasted his inductive method with the Aristotelian deductive approach. The inductive method includes a vital "correction" mechanism: nature itself provides the test of any conclusion.
  • Contemporary Empirical Inquiry: Bacon's ideas were part of a broader European trend. Galileo Galilei in Italy was already using observation (with his improved telescope) to study celestial bodies and falling objects, confirming the Earth's orbit around the Sun, which led to conflict with Catholic authorities.
  • Bacon's Core Insight: The revolutionary idea was simple: "look before you conclude." Trust observation and experiment over ancient authority. This democratized knowledge, as any educated person could, in principle, test and correct previous assumptions.

The Royal Society: Science as a Community

  • Founding and Charter: In 1660, a group of London scholars began meeting to share scientific observations. Two years later, Charles II granted them a royal charter, officially establishing the Royal Society of London for Improving Natural Knowledge (the Royal Society).
  • Nullius in verba: The Society's Latin motto, "Take nobody's word for it," explicitly rejected reliance on authority (ancient or contemporary). It demanded empirical evidence and verifiable experiments.
  • Philosophical Transactions: Starting in 1665, the Royal Society published the world's first scientific journal. This allowed experiments to be shared, repeated, and scrutinized by a wider community, reinforcing accountability and transparency in science.
  • Prominent Early Members:
    • Robert Boyle: A chemist who developed the air pump and formulated Boyle's Law, considered a founder of modern chemistry.
    • Christopher Wren: An astronomer who later became a renowned architect, designing St. Paul's Cathedral.
    • Isaac Newton: A towering genius whose work unified fundamental aspects of the natural world.

Isaac Newton's Synthesis

  • Principia Mathematica (1687): Newton's monumental work, published with Royal Society support, presented his theory of universal gravitation and the laws of motion.
  • Unifying the Cosmos: Newton demonstrated that the same mathematical law explained both celestial phenomena (planetary orbits, as observed by Kepler) and terrestrial phenomena (falling objects, studied by Galileo). Every object attracts every other object proportionally to their masses and inversely to the square of the distance between them.
  • Rational Universe: Newton's work showed the universe not as a collection of separate mysteries, but as a single, rational, and orderly system governed by comprehensible laws.
  • Science and Faith: For Newton, his scientific discoveries were not in conflict with faith. He saw the orderly laws of nature as evidence of a rational Creator, viewing the study of physics as "reading God's mind."
  • Natural Philosophy: The term "natural philosophy" was used in the 17th century to describe what we now call "science." The word "scientist" was coined much later.

Revolution in the Mind, Revolution in the State

  • Parallel Revolutions: The intellectual revolution in science occurred concurrently with major political upheavals in England (Civil War, Glorious Revolution). This was not a coincidence.
  • Questioning Authority: Both movements posed the same fundamental question: "By what authority do you claim the right to tell me what is true?"
    • Political: Parliament challenged the divine right of kings, demanding accountability to law and people.
    • Scientific: Bacon and the Royal Society challenged ancient authorities like Aristotle, demanding accountability to empirical evidence from nature.
  • Departure from Medieval Scholasticism: Unlike medieval philosophers like Aquinas, who worked within the authoritative framework of Scripture, Church Fathers, and Aristotle, 17th-century science removed Aristotle from his privileged position. While Scripture and theology retained authority in faith, the natural world became open to unfettered investigation, with experiment trumping ancient texts.
  • Science and Faith in England: Unlike Galileo's fraught relationship with the Catholic Church in Italy, many founders of the Royal Society in predominantly Protestant England were clergymen who saw no inherent conflict between careful observation of nature and Christian faith. The "science vs. religion" conflict model is a later historical construct.
  • "Then and Now" - Peer Review: The Royal Society's principle of Nullius in verba is a direct ancestor of modern scientific peer review, where experiments and findings are independently checked by other scientists before acceptance. This demand for accountability through evidence is a continuous thread from 17th-century science to modern scientific and legal principles.

Review Questions

  1. How did Francis Bacon's inductive method fundamentally challenge the traditional Aristotelian approach to knowledge, and what was the practical argument Bacon made for its superiority?
  2. Explain the significance of the Royal Society's motto, Nullius in verba, in the context of 17th-century scientific inquiry. How did the publication of Philosophical Transactions reinforce this principle?
  3. Describe Isaac Newton's major scientific contributions as presented in Principia Mathematica. How did his work unify previous observations and fundamentally change the understanding of the universe?
  4. The text suggests a parallel between the scientific and political revolutions of the 17th century. What common question did both movements pose, and how did they each demand a new form of accountability?
  5. Discuss the role of "authority" in the medieval university versus the emerging scientific institutions like the Royal Society. How did the treatment of figures like Aristotle differ, and what implications did this have for the pursuit of knowledge?
