Study Guide: The Year Everything Changed: 1492 and the Rise and Fall of Spain

Overview

This section details the pivotal year of 1492, marking the completion of the Reconquista, the expulsion of Jews, and Columbus's voyage, which transformed Spain into a global power. It covers Spain's rapid conquest of vast American empires, the exploitation of native populations through systems like encomienda, and the moral debates sparked by figures like Bartolomé de las Casas. The guide also explores Spain's zenith under Philip II as the champion of the Counter-Reformation and traces the multifaceted causes of its eventual decline.

Key Concepts

  • The Transformative Year 1492: This year marked the end of the nearly 800-year Reconquista, expelling Muslim rule from Spain, and simultaneously saw the expulsion of Spain's Jewish population. Most significantly, Christopher Columbus's voyage, commissioned in the same year, led to the "discovery" of the Americas, catapulting Spain from a regional power to a global empire.
  • Rapid Conquest of New World Empires: Spanish conquistadors like Hernán Cortés and Francisco Pizarro, with surprisingly small forces, swiftly overthrew advanced civilizations such as the Aztec and Inca Empires. This success was due to military superiority, strategic alliances with indigenous rivals, and devastating European diseases like smallpox, which decimated native populations lacking immunity.
  • Exploitation and the Encomienda System: To manage and profit from its vast new territories, Spain implemented the encomienda system, granting Spanish colonists control over native labor in exchange for promises of protection and Christianization. In practice, this system often resulted in brutal forced labor, exploitation, and the destruction of indigenous communities.
  • Moral Debate and Prophetic Witness: Bartolomé de las Casas, a Dominican friar, emerged as a fierce critic of the encomienda system and Spanish colonial abuses. His advocacy led to the New Laws of 1542, attempting to reform the system, and his arguments in the Valladolid Debate (1550-1551) raised profound questions about human rights and the moral legitimacy of conquest, unique among colonial powers.
  • Philip II and the Zenith of Spanish Power: During the reign of Philip II (1556–1598), Spain reached the peak of its global influence, controlling vast territories across two hemispheres. Philip was a fervent champion of the Catholic Counter-Reformation, leading victories like the Battle of Lepanto against the Ottomans, but also faced costly wars and challenges.
  • The Seeds of Decline: Despite its immense power, Spain began to face significant challenges. Costly wars, such as the ongoing Dutch Revolt and the defeat of the Spanish Armada in 1588, drained resources. The influx of New World silver fueled inflation and an unsustainable economy, masking underlying weaknesses.
  • The Long Retreat: Following Philip II, Spain entered a prolonged period of decline. Events like the expulsion of the Moriscos (skilled Muslim converts), the Thirty Years' War, and the War of Spanish Succession further diminished its power, leading to territorial losses and the end of Spanish hegemony in Europe by the early 18th century.

Important Terms

  • Reconquista: The centuries-long campaign (8th to 15th century) by Christian kingdoms to retake the Iberian Peninsula from Muslim rulers, ending in 1492 with the fall of Granada. It profoundly shaped Spanish identity and expansionist ideology.
  • Alhambra Decree: The royal edict issued by Ferdinand and Isabella in March 1492, ordering the expulsion of all Jews from the Kingdoms of Spain.
  • Treaty of Tordesillas (1494): An agreement between Spain and Portugal, sanctioned by the Pope, that divided newly discovered lands outside Europe along a meridian in the Atlantic Ocean. Spain claimed lands to the west, and Portugal to the east (including Brazil and the route to India).
  • Hernán Cortés: Spanish conquistador who led the expedition that conquered the Aztec Empire in Mexico between 1519 and 1521.
  • Francisco Pizarro: Spanish conquistador who conquered the Inca Empire in Peru between 1532 and 1536.
  • Viceroyalty: A large colonial territory governed by a viceroy, an official who ruled on behalf of the Spanish crown with almost royal authority. Examples include New Spain (Mexico) and Peru (South America).
  • Encomienda System: A Spanish labor system in its American colonies where a Spanish encomendero was granted the labor of a specific number of native people, in exchange for the obligation to Christianize and protect them. It frequently devolved into forced labor and abuse.
  • Bartolomé de las Casas: A Dominican friar and former encomendero who became a vocal critic of Spanish colonial abuses and a lifelong advocate for the rights and humanity of indigenous peoples.
  • New Laws of 1542: A set of laws issued by King Charles I of Spain (Emperor Charles V) that attempted to reform the encomienda system, curtailing its abuses and eventually abolishing native slavery. Their implementation faced strong resistance.
  • Philip II (reigned 1556–1598): King of Spain during its Golden Age, a staunch defender of Catholicism and leader of the Counter-Reformation. His reign saw Spain's greatest global power but also planted seeds of future decline.
  • Escorial: A vast royal palace, monastery, and mausoleum built by Philip II near Madrid, symbolizing the power and religious devotion of the Spanish Habsburg monarchy.
  • Battle of Lepanto (1571): A decisive naval battle in which a Spanish-led Holy League fleet crushed the Ottoman fleet off the coast of Greece, marking a major Christian victory against Ottoman expansion in the Mediterranean.
  • Counter-Reformation: The Catholic Church's internal reform movement and response to the Protestant Reformation in the 16th and 17th centuries, supported vigorously by Spain under Philip II.
  • Valladolid Debate (1550–1551): A formal legal and theological debate convened by King Charles I of Spain to discuss the moral legitimacy of Spain's conquest of the Americas and the treatment of its indigenous inhabitants.
  • Juan Ginés de Sepúlveda: A Spanish humanist scholar and theologian who, during the Valladolid Debate, argued that indigenous peoples were "natural slaves" and could be justly conquered and ruled by the Spanish.
  • Spanish Armada (1588): A large Spanish fleet sent by Philip II to invade England, which was defeated by the English navy and severe storms. This defeat was a major strategic and psychological blow to Spain.
  • Moriscos: Descendants of Muslims who converted to Christianity after the Reconquista but were later expelled from Spain in 1609. Their expulsion caused significant economic and demographic loss.
  • Peace of Westphalia (1648): A series of treaties that ended the Thirty Years' War, which significantly weakened Spain's power and marked the rise of France as the dominant European power.
  • War of Spanish Succession (1701–1714): A major European conflict fought over the succession to the Spanish throne after the death of the last Habsburg king of Spain without an heir.
  • Treaty of Utrecht (1713): The treaty that ended the War of Spanish Succession, installing a French Bourbon king on the Spanish throne but stripping Spain of its remaining European territories, confirming the end of its hegemony.

Detailed Notes

The Pivotal Year: 1492

  • End of the Reconquista: In January 1492, the Christian reconquest of the Iberian Peninsula culminated with the surrender of Granada, the last Muslim stronghold, to Ferdinand and Isabella. This nearly eight-century campaign instilled a strong sense of national identity and a conviction for expanding Christian civilization in Spain.
  • Expulsion of the Jews: In the same year, the Alhambra Decree mandated the expulsion of Spain's Jewish population, consolidating a religiously uniform kingdom but removing a significant portion of its skilled populace.
  • Columbus's Voyage: In August 1492, Cristoforo Colombo (Christopher Columbus) embarked from Palos, commissioned to find a western route to Asia. His arrival in the Americas, though not Asia, was "far more consequential," transforming Spain into a global power.
  • Global Transformation: This "discovery" changed Spain "almost overnight" from a medium-sized Iberian kingdom into a dominant world power.
  • Treaty of Tordesillas (1494): Spain and Portugal divided the newly "discovered" lands, with Spain claiming territories west of an Atlantic line and Portugal claiming lands to the east (including Brazil and the sea route to India). Indigenous peoples' opinions were not considered.

Age of Conquest and Empire Building

  • Cortés and the Aztec Empire: In 1519, Hernán Cortés landed in Mexico with roughly 500 soldiers. Within two years, he overthrew the vast Aztec Empire, centered in Tenochtitlán. The fall of Tenochtitlán in 1521 was facilitated by Spanish military technology, alliances with rival native groups, and, critically, the devastating impact of smallpox, to which native populations had no immunity.
  • Pizarro and the Inca Empire: A decade later, Francisco Pizarro conquered the Inca Empire in Peru with an even smaller force. The Inca ruler Atahualpa was captured, ransomed for immense quantities of gold and silver, but then executed.
  • Establishment of Viceroyalties: These conquests established Spain's claim to vast viceroyalties, large colonial territories governed by viceroys. The two primary ones were New Spain (Mexico and Central America) and Peru (South America).

Colonial Exploitation and Moral Dilemmas

  • The Encomienda System: Spain needed labor for its silver mines and agricultural lands. The encomienda system granted Spanish colonists (encomenderos) the labor of specific numbers of native people, theoretically in exchange for Christianization and protection. In practice, it became a brutal system of forced labor, leading to high mortality rates and the destruction of indigenous families and villages.
  • Bartolomé de las Casas's Protest: Bartolomé de las Casas, a Dominican friar, initially participated in the conquest but later dedicated his life to defending native peoples. He testified before the Spanish court, wrote extensively, and published A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies (1542), directly appealing to the king.
    • Las Casas's Argument: He argued that indigenous peoples were fully human, possessed rational souls, and their enslavement was a mortal sin.
    • Impact: His efforts led to the New Laws of 1542, which aimed to curb the worst abuses of the encomienda system, though with mixed success due to resistance from colonists.
    • "Prophetic Witness": Las Casas exemplified "prophetic witness from within a system," challenging injustice while remaining part of the Spanish and Christian framework.
  • The Valladolid Debate (1550-1551): King Charles I (also Holy Roman Emperor Charles V) temporarily halted conquests in the New World to convene a council to debate the moral justice of Spain's actions.
    • Juan Ginés de Sepúlveda: Argued that indigenous peoples were "natural slaves" in the Aristotelian sense—barbarous and incapable of self-governance—and thus could be legitimately conquered and Christianized by force.
    • Bartolomé de las Casas: Countered that all human beings possess reason and natural rights, and war could not justly be waged against people who had not harmed Spain.
    • Outcome: No definitive verdict was issued, and conquests resumed. However, the debate was remarkable as a unique self-inquiry by a colonial power into the moral legitimacy of its empire.

The Apex of Power: Philip II (1556–1598)

  • Global Empire: Under Philip II, Spain reached its zenith, ruling Spain, Portugal, the Netherlands, much of Italy, and a vast overseas empire.
  • Champion of Counter-Reformation: Philip was the leading defender of Catholic orthodoxy against Protestantism. He supported key institutions of the Counter-Reformation, including the Council of Trent, the Jesuits, and the renewed Inquisition.
  • The Escorial: He built the Escorial, a monumental palace, monastery, and mausoleum near Madrid, symbolizing his devout Catholic rule and the power of the Spanish monarchy.
  • Battle of Lepanto (1571): A Spanish-led coalition of Catholic navies decisively defeated the Ottoman fleet, shattering the myth of Ottoman naval invincibility and marking one of the greatest naval victories of the era. Miguel de Cervantes, author of Don Quixote, fought and was wounded at Lepanto.

Seeds of Decline and The Long Retreat

  • Costly Wars: Despite its power, Philip II's reign saw the seeds of decline. The revolt of the Dutch Netherlands (c. 1568) led to an eighty-year war. The defeat of the Spanish Armada in 1588 by England was a major strategic and psychological blow.
  • Economic Vulnerabilities: New World silver funded Spain's expansion and wars, but also fueled inflation and led to unsustainable spending, undermining the long-term health of the Spanish economy.
  • Philip III and the Expulsion of the Moriscos (1609): Philip III expelled the Moriscos (descendants of Muslim converts), leading to the loss of an estimated 300,000 skilled farmers and craftsmen, further damaging the Spanish economy.
  • Thirty Years' War (1618–1648): This devastating European conflict further drained Spain's resources. The Peace of Westphalia (1648) formally marked Spain's loss of dominance in Europe to France.
  • War of Spanish Succession (1701–1714): The death of the last Habsburg king of Spain without an heir led to a major European conflict. The Treaty of Utrecht (1713) installed a French Bourbon on the Spanish throne but stripped Spain of its remaining European territories (e.g., Spanish Netherlands, Italian possessions), formally ending its age of hegemony.
  • Recurring Pattern of Empires: Spain's trajectory illustrates a pattern of rapid expansion fueled by extracted wealth, followed by the immense political and economic costs of maintaining an overstretched empire, eventually leading to decline when resources dwindle and internal structures weaken.

Review Questions

  1. How did the completion of the Reconquista and the events surrounding Christopher Columbus's voyage in 1492 fundamentally reshape Spain's identity, ambitions, and global position?
  2. Analyze the factors that contributed to the rapid and successful Spanish conquest of the powerful Aztec and Inca Empires. What role did disease play in these conquests?
  3. Describe the encomienda system and its impact on the indigenous populations of the Americas. What were Bartolomé de las Casas's main arguments against this system, and what effect did his advocacy have?
  4. Explain Philip II's role as the "great champion of the Catholic Counter-Reformation." What were some of the significant achievements and major challenges of his reign?
  5. Discuss the various internal and external factors that contributed to Spain's long decline after the reign of Philip II, from economic issues to costly wars and political shifts in Europe. What lessons about imperial power can be drawn from Spain's experience?
