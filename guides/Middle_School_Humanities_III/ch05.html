Study Guide: Expansion and Conquest

Overview

Between 1492 and 1888, European nations built empires spanning every inhabited continent, transforming the world in ways both magnificent and terrible. They carried Christianity and literacy; they also carried chains. They opened trade routes connecting the globe for the first time; they also opened the wound of the Atlantic slave trade, the largest forced migration in human history. This chapter examines that contradictory legacy honestly — reading the words of Olaudah Equiano, who survived the Middle Passage; returning to Bartolome de las Casas; and examining Shakespeare's The Tempest. The central question is one that history forces on us: How do we account for a civilization that proclaimed the dignity of every human soul while buying and selling human beings?

Key Concepts

  • The Scale of the Atlantic Slave Trade: Approximately 12 million Africans were transported across the Atlantic over three and a half centuries. About 1.8 million — one in seven — died during the ocean crossing alone. This is not statistics but a moral reckoning: the largest crime against humanity in the pre-modern world, conducted by nations that claimed Christian civilization.
  • The Plantation Economy as the Driver: The demand for enslaved labor was created by the plantation cash-crop economy of the Americas. Sugar, tobacco, cotton, and rice required enormous physical labor in brutal heat. European colonists made the calculation that enslaved African labor was cheapest — a calculation that concealed a human catastrophe.
  • The Christian Conscience: Both Cause and Cure: The same Christian faith was used to justify slavery (grotesque distortions of the faith) and to produce its most effective critics (Las Casas, Equiano, Wilberforce, the Quakers). The difference lay in whether people allowed their faith to challenge their comfort. This is not a paradox that dissolves — it is the truth about human nature.
  • Equiano's Rhetorical Strategy: By using Locke's natural rights language, Equiano made the most powerful possible argument to a British audience: if you accept Locke's premises (which the Declaration of Independence endorsed), then slavery is philosophically incoherent — it contradicts the very principles its defenders claimed to believe.
  • The Haitian Revolution as History's Test: The Haitian Revolution (1791-1804) was the most radical real-world test of Enlightenment ideas about human equality. It proved that enslaved people were not content, not incapable of self-governance, and would fight for their freedom with extraordinary courage. It terrified slaveholders and inspired abolitionists — and it helped force the Louisiana Purchase.

Important Terms

  • Middle Passage: The sea journey enslaved Africans were forced to make from West Africa across the Atlantic; the middle leg of the triangle trade; approximately 1.8 million died of approximately 12 million transported.
  • Triangle trade: The three-leg commercial circuit: European goods to Africa, enslaved people to the Americas, American products back to Europe.
  • Plantation economy: Large estates producing a single cash crop for export using enslaved labor; made small numbers of landowners wealthy while reducing millions to property.
  • East India Company: Private English trading corporation founded 1600; evolved into a governing power with its own army that controlled most of India before the British crown took direct control in 1858.
  • Sepoy Mutiny: The 1857-1858 Indian uprising against East India Company rule; resulted in the British crown assuming direct imperial control of India.
  • Haitian Revolution (1791-1804): The uprising of enslaved people in French Saint-Domingue; led by Toussaint Louverture; founded Haiti as the first Black republic and the only successful slave revolt in history.
  • Toussaint Louverture: Formerly enslaved military commander who led the Haitian Revolution to victory against French, British, and Napoleonic forces.
  • Abolitionism: The movement to legally end the slave trade and slavery; grounded in both Christian teaching (imago Dei) and Enlightenment natural rights.
  • William Wilberforce: Evangelical Christian MP who led the British Parliamentary campaign against the slave trade for nearly twenty years; slave trade abolished 1807; Emancipation Act 1833.
  • Emancipation: Being set free from slavery; the British Emancipation Act (1833) freed approximately 800,000 people in British colonies.
  • Olaudah Equiano: Nigerian-born survivor of the Middle Passage who purchased his own freedom, converted to Christianity, and published The Interesting Narrative (1789), one of the most powerful weapons of the abolitionist movement.
  • Opium Wars: British wars against China (First: 1839-42) fought after China destroyed illegal opium shipments; China forced to open ports and cede Hong Kong; the text calls it honestly "a war fought to protect drug trafficking."

Detailed Notes

The Atlantic Slave Trade

  • Scale and Duration
    • c. 1500-1867: approximately 12 million Africans transported
    • About 1.8 million died on the Middle Passage alone
    • Many more died within a few years of arrival
    • Driven by plantation economy demand for cheap labor
  • The Middle Passage
    • West African coast to Americas; 4-8 weeks
    • Horrifying overcrowding; little food, water, or air
    • "The shrieks of the women, and the groans of the dying, rendered the whole a scene of horror almost inconceivable" (Equiano)
    • Millions more died shortly after arrival
  • African Participation
    • African kings and merchants participated in capturing and selling enslaved people
    • European demand created and sustained the market
    • History is more complex than simple perpetrator/victim story
    • Commercial incentives corrupted moral judgment across several cultures simultaneously

European Expansion in Asia

  • The East India Company
    • Founded 1600 by Queen Elizabeth I's charter
    • Started as monopoly trading company for spices and textiles
    • Pattern of expansion: offer protection to local rulers, create dependence, absorb territory
    • By 18th century: had its own army, fought wars, collected taxes, governed territories
    • 1857: Sepoy Mutiny against Company rule
    • 1858: British crown assumed direct governance of India
  • The Opium Wars
    • China had little interest in European goods; running British trade deficit
    • British East India Company sold Indian-grown opium illegally in China
    • By 1830s: opium addiction was a Chinese social crisis
    • 1839: China destroyed British opium shipments
    • First Opium War (1839-42): Britain at war to restore the opium trade
    • Result: China opened 5 ports, paid reparations, ceded Hong Kong
    • Honest assessment: a war fought to protect drug trafficking

Revolution and Its Complications

  • The American Contradiction
    • Declaration (1776): "all men are created equal" with unalienable rights
    • Jefferson enslaved over 600 people; Washington, Madison enslaved hundreds
    • Abigail Adams noted the contradiction to her husband
    • Samuel Johnson: "How is it that we hear the loudest yelps for liberty among the drivers of Negroes?"
    • Resolution required nearly a century and a civil war (1865)
  • The Haitian Revolution (1791-1804)
    • Most radical application of Enlightenment equality ideas
    • 1791: Enslaved people on French Saint-Domingue rose in revolt
    • Led by Toussaint Louverture, formerly enslaved
    • Fought French, British, then Napoleon's army
    • January 1, 1804: Haiti declared independence — first Black republic in the world
    • Only successful slave revolt to create a lasting independent state
    • Napoleon's failure led France to sell Louisiana Territory (1803)
    • Terrified slaveholders; inspired abolitionists worldwide
  • Latin American Independence
    • Inspired by Enlightenment ideas, American and Haitian revolutions
    • Began c. 1810; nearly all Spanish America independent by 1826
    • Leaders: Simon Bolivar (South America), Miguel Hidalgo (Mexico)
    • Brazil: separate path; independent 1822; abolished slavery 1888 (last in Western Hemisphere)

The Abolitionist Movement

  • Religious Origins
    • Quakers: first organized religious group to condemn slavery as incompatible with Christianity
    • By 1780s: broad coalition of Protestant Christians, Enlightenment rationalists, formerly enslaved people
    • Both foundations: imago Dei (Christian) and natural rights (Enlightenment)
  • William Wilberforce
    • Evangelical Christian Member of Parliament
    • Led Parliamentary campaign for nearly 20 years
    • 1807: Parliament abolished British slave trade
    • 1833: Emancipation Act freed approximately 800,000 enslaved people in British colonies
    • Compensation paid to slaveholders, not enslaved people
    • Wilberforce died three days after hearing the bill had passed
  • Olaudah Equiano
    • Born c. 1745 in Igbo region of Nigeria (now southern Nigeria)
    • Kidnapped at about age 11; separated from his sister immediately
    • Survived the Middle Passage; sold to a Royal Navy officer
    • 1766: purchased his own freedom from a Quaker merchant
    • Converted to Christianity; became active in British abolitionist movement
    • Published The Interesting Narrative (1789) — nine editions in his lifetime
    • Read by MPs, abolitionists, and ordinary readers
    • His rhetorical strategy: used Locke's natural rights to show slavery contradicts its defenders' own principles

The Balance Sheet of Empire

  • Positive Contributions
    • Spread of Christianity, literacy, eventually Western medicine
    • Abolition itself was the product of Christian conscience
    • Missionaries like Francis Xavier brought the Gospel to Asia and Africa
    • Trade created real wealth and connections between cultures
    • Contact, however violent, produced genuine intellectual and cultural exchange
  • Genuine Evils
    • Atlantic slave trade: "a crime of staggering proportions"
    • Indigenous civilizations shattered by conquest, disease, forced labor, cultural destruction
    • Plantation economy built European prosperity on human suffering
    • Legacy of racism, poverty, and broken communities persists to present day
  • The Central Tension
    • Same Christian conscience enabled colonialism AND produced its most effective critics
    • Las Casas, Wilberforce, and Equiano's faith were real
    • So was the slaveholder who sang hymns on Sunday morning
    • The difference: whether faith was allowed to challenge comfort

Connections

  • Las Casas's argument from imago Dei (Chapter 2) is the same theological foundation on which Equiano and Wilberforce build their abolitionist arguments — and the same foundation as Pope Leo XIII's Rerum Novarum (Chapter 6).
  • Equiano's use of Lockean natural rights (Chapter 4) to indict slavery demonstrates how Enlightenment ideas could be weaponized against the very contradictions of those who claimed to believe them.
  • The Haitian Revolution's influence on the Louisiana Purchase directly affected the territorial expansion of the United States, whose contradictions regarding slavery would eventually lead to the Civil War.
  • The East India Company's evolution from trade to governance illustrates the same pattern as Chapter 3's discussion of how commercial and political power tend to merge in imperial contexts.
  • The Opium Wars illustrate how the Enlightenment principle of "free trade" could be used to justify policies that were, by any honest assessment, morally indefensible.
