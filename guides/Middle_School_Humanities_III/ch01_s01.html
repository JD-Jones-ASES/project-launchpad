Literature: From Queen Elizabeth I to the Glorious Revolution

Overview

This section explores the fundamental question of what grants authority its right to rule, examining how this question evolved in England from the late 16th century to the late 17th century. Through analyses of Shakespeare's Henry V, Milton's Areopagitica, and the English Bill of Rights, we trace shifting perspectives on kingship, the control of ideas, and the ultimate supremacy of law over monarchy. These texts collectively demonstrate England's journey from a divinely ordained monarchy to one where parliamentary consent and individual liberties began to define governance.

Key Concepts

  • The Nature of Kingship and Royal Responsibility: Examined in Henry V, this concept questions a king's moral accountability for the lives lost in battle and explores the idea of shared sacrifice and honor across social classes.
  • Freedom of the Press and Open Debate: Advocated in Areopagitica, this principle asserts that truth will naturally prevail over falsehood in a free and open exchange of ideas, making government censorship unnecessary and detrimental.
  • Parliamentary Supremacy and the Rule of Law: Cemented by the English Bill of Rights, this establishes that the law, made by Parliament, stands above the monarch, severely limiting royal prerogative and safeguarding certain liberties.
  • Individual Liberty and Rights: A recurring theme across all three texts, highlighting that dignity and certain protections (like habeas corpus, freedom of conscience, and the right to petition) are not exclusive to the elite but extend to all subjects, paving the way for modern conceptions of rights.

Important Terms

  • Battle of Agincourt (1415): A pivotal English victory during the Hundred Years' War, where King Henry V's outnumbered army defeated a much larger French force. It serves as the historical backdrop for Shakespeare's Henry V.
  • St. Crispin's Day Speech: King Henry V's iconic speech in Shakespeare's play, delivered on the eve of the Battle of Agincourt, renowned for its themes of brotherhood, shared sacrifice, and the elevation of common soldiers.
  • Areopagus: The ancient Athenian hill where the city's supreme court met. John Milton referenced this location in the title of his treatise Areopagitica, drawing a parallel to a forum for open discussion and judgment.
  • Licensing Order (1643): A decree passed by the Long Parliament requiring all books to undergo pre-publication censorship, which prompted John Milton to write Areopagitica in protest.
  • Glorious Revolution (1688): A bloodless coup in which King James II was overthrown and replaced by William and Mary, leading directly to the drafting of the English Bill of Rights. It marked a decisive shift towards parliamentary power.
  • Habeas Corpus: A fundamental legal protection ensuring that a person cannot be imprisoned indefinitely without being charged with a specific crime and brought before a court. The Latin phrase means "you shall have the body."
  • Parliamentary Supremacy: The constitutional principle, largely established by the English Bill of Rights, that Parliament is the supreme legal authority in the United Kingdom, holding ultimate power over the monarch and government.

Detailed Notes

Introduction: Words That Changed England

This section explores the crucial question: "What gives authority its right to rule?" This question is addressed through three significant texts spanning a century of profound change in England: Shakespeare's Henry V (1599), Milton's Areopagitica (1644), and the English Bill of Rights (1689). Each text offers a different perspective on authority, ranging from monarchical power and intellectual freedom to parliamentary sovereignty.

Shakespeare: Henry V and the Nature of Kingship

  • Historical Context: Written around 1599, a period marked by war, religious tension, and anxieties over Queen Elizabeth I's succession. Shakespeare chose the historical Battle of Agincourt (1415) as his subject, celebrating an English victory.
  • King Henry's Responsibility: The play famously depicts King Henry V, disguised as a common soldier, walking among his troops on the eve of battle. His soldiers confront him directly about the justice of the king's cause and the king's responsibility for the souls of those who die.
    • Williams' Challenge: A soldier named Williams questions the king's moral accountability, suggesting the king bears a "heavy reckoning" for the "legs and arms and heads, chopped off in battle."
    • King Henry's Response: Henry asserts, "Every subject's duty is the king's; but every subject's soul is his own." This acknowledges a shared duty but places ultimate spiritual responsibility on the individual, delineating the boundaries of royal authority.
  • The St. Crispin's Day Speech: The following morning, facing an exhausted and heavily outnumbered army, Henry delivers one of English literature's most powerful speeches.
    • Themes of Sacrifice and Brotherhood: The speech inspires his men by appealing to shared sacrifice, camaraderie, and the lasting honor they will earn. He proclaims that anyone who sheds blood with him "shall be my brother," and this act will "gentle his condition," meaning raise him to noble status regardless of birth.
    • Radical Claim: This claim was revolutionary in a society defined by hereditary privilege. It implies that true nobility can be earned through shared experience and courage, not just inherited. The ambiguity of whether this is genuine belief or effective wartime rhetoric contributes to the play's enduring power. It foreshadows later ideas that rights and dignity are not solely for the wealthy or well-born.

Milton: Areopagitica and the Freedom of the Press

  • Background to Censorship: In 1643, the Long Parliament, ironically the body Milton was supporting, passed a Licensing Order requiring government approval for all publications. John Milton, a prominent poet and pamphleteer, saw this as an affront to liberty.
  • Milton's Argument in Areopagitica (1644): Addressed directly to Parliament, Milton's treatise argued against pre-publication censorship as ineffective, un-English, and philosophically unsound.
    • Truth vs. Falsehood: Milton's core belief was that "Truth is stronger than Falsehood" and will triumph in open debate without needing government protection. He famously stated, "Let her and Falsehood grapple; who ever knew Truth put to the worse, in a free and open encounter?"
    • Ineffectiveness of Censorship: He argued that suppressing ideas merely drives them underground, making them more dangerous. Furthermore, he questioned who possessed the infallible judgment to decide which ideas were worthy of public discourse.
    • Essential Liberty: Milton powerfully articulated what he considered the most vital freedom: "Give me the liberty to know, to utter, and to argue freely according to conscience, above all liberties."
  • Legacy and Impact: While Areopagitica did not immediately lead to the repeal of the Licensing Order (it remained until 1695), Milton's arguments proved profoundly influential. His ideas became foundational for later liberal thought, directly influencing figures like John Locke and, subsequently, the American Founders, whose First Amendment to the U.S. Constitution (1791) reflects Milton's principles of free speech and press.

The English Bill of Rights (1689)

  • Context: The Glorious Revolution: Following the Glorious Revolution, where James II was replaced by William and Mary, Parliament seized the opportunity to formally define the limits of monarchical power.
  • A Declaration of Law: The English Bill of Rights was not a request but a declaration of existing law, presented to William and Mary as the explicit conditions for their acceptance of the crown. It ensured that future monarchs would govern under the law, not above it.
  • Addressing Royal Abuses: The document directly countered the specific abuses of the Stuart kings, particularly James II, which included:
    • Suspending laws without Parliament's consent.
    • Maintaining a standing army in peacetime without parliamentary approval.
    • Interfering with free elections and the independence of courts.
    • Imposing excessive bail or fines, and inflicting cruel and unusual punishments.
  • Key Provisions and Principles:
    • No Suspension of Laws: The monarch cannot suspend laws or their execution without parliamentary consent.
    • No Standing Army in Peacetime: Raising or keeping an army in peacetime without Parliament's consent is illegal.
    • Free Elections: Elections for Parliament members must be free.
    • Protection against Excessive Punishments: Prohibits excessive bail, fines, and cruel and unusual punishments.
    • Right to Petition: Subjects have the right to petition the king, and prosecutions for doing so are illegal.
    • Habeas Corpus: While strengthened by a separate Act in 1679, the principle of habeas corpus (preventing unlawful detention) is a core element of the liberties affirmed.
  • Significance: Although it did not establish a full democracy (voting rights were still limited), the English Bill of Rights established the irreversible principle of Parliamentary Supremacy: the law is above the king, and Parliament is the law-making body within which the monarch must govern. This document, like Magna Carta (1215), framed rights as "ancient" rather than newly invented, lending historical weight to their legitimacy.

Review Questions

  1. How do Shakespeare's Henry V and the English Bill of Rights both address the fundamental question of a ruler's responsibility to their subjects, despite originating from different literary forms and historical contexts?
  2. Explain John Milton's core argument against pre-publication censorship in Areopagitica. According to Milton, what happens when truth and falsehood are allowed to "grapple" in a free and open encounter?
  3. The St. Crispin's Day speech in Henry V suggests that fighting alongside the king will "gentle" a common soldier's condition and make him the king's "brother." Why was this considered a "radical claim" in Elizabethan society, and what does it suggest about the emerging ideas of merit and social mobility?
  4. Identify and describe at least three specific abuses of power by the Stuart kings that the English Bill of Rights directly sought to prevent. How did the document's provisions solidify the principle of Parliament's authority over the monarch?
  5. The introduction states that all three texts ask: "What gives authority its right to rule?" Compare and contrast the different answers or perspectives offered by Henry V, Areopagitica, and the English Bill of Rights regarding the source and limits of authority.
