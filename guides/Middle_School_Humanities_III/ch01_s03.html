Study Guide: From Queen Elizabeth I to the Glorious Revolution

Overview

This section covers a pivotal period in English history, from Queen Elizabeth I's efforts to establish religious stability and secure England against foreign threats, through the tumultuous reigns of the Stuart kings which led to civil war and the execution of a monarch. It culminates in the Restoration of the monarchy and the Glorious Revolution, which fundamentally redefined the balance of power, firmly establishing Parliament's supremacy over the crown.

Key Concepts

  • Elizabethan Settlement: Queen Elizabeth I's religious compromise in the late 16th century that made England officially Protestant (Church of England) while retaining some Catholic forms of worship. Its goal was to create a broad church that most English people could belong to without violence, prioritizing outward conformity over private belief.
  • Defeat of the Spanish Armada (1588): A decisive victory for England against a massive invading Spanish fleet. This event was a major turning point, saving England from invasion, solidifying its Protestant identity, fostering a sense of national unity, and establishing England's growing sea power.
  • Divine Right of Kings: The political doctrine championed by James I and Charles I, asserting that a monarch's authority comes directly from God and is not subject to earthly institutions like Parliament. This belief was a major source of conflict between the kings and Parliament.
  • English Civil War (1642-1651): A conflict between the Royalist "Cavaliers" (supporters of King Charles I) and the Parliamentarian "Roundheads." It resulted from profound disagreements over royal power, religion, and taxation, culminating in the execution of Charles I and the establishment of a republican government.
  • Glorious Revolution (1688): A nearly bloodless transfer of the English crown from James II to William of Orange and Mary. It is called "glorious" because it avoided widespread conflict and permanently established the principle of parliamentary supremacy, ensuring that future monarchs would rule with the consent of Parliament.
  • English Bill of Rights (1689): A foundational constitutional document passed by Parliament after the Glorious Revolution. It significantly limited the monarch's power, guaranteeing parliamentary rights (like free elections and control over taxation and standing armies) and protecting individual liberties, thereby cementing parliamentary supremacy.

Important Terms

  • Elizabethan Settlement: The religious compromise established by Elizabeth I in 1559–1563, making England officially Protestant (Church of England) while keeping many Catholic forms of worship to achieve peace.
  • Divine Right of Kings: The belief that a monarch's authority comes directly from God and cannot be limited by any human institution, including Parliament.
  • Petition of Right: A declaration passed by Parliament in 1628, listing specific abuses of royal power and demanding Charles I acknowledge limits to his authority, particularly regarding imprisonment without cause, taxation without Parliament's consent, and billeting soldiers.
  • Commonwealth: The republican government established in England after the execution of Charles I in 1649, existing as a state without a king, governed by Parliament and a Council of State until 1653.
  • Protectorate: The government of England from 1653 to 1659, headed by Oliver Cromwell as "Lord Protector," where Cromwell held near-kingly powers but refused the crown.
  • Glorious Revolution: The 1688 transfer of the English crown from James II to William and Mary, notable for being achieved without battle on English soil and permanently establishing Parliament's supremacy.
  • Parliamentary Supremacy: The constitutional principle, firmly established by the Glorious Revolution and the Bill of Rights (1689), that Parliament is the supreme law-making authority in England, and no monarch may override or ignore its acts.

Detailed Notes

Queen Elizabeth I (1558-1603)

  • Religious Stability: Elizabeth inherited a nation exhausted by religious conflict, having switched official religions four times in 25 years under Henry VIII, Edward VI, and Mary I.
  • The Elizabethan Settlement (1559-1563):
    • Re-established the Church of England under royal authority.
    • Required all clergy to accept the queen's supremacy.
    • Produced a Book of Common Prayer designed to appeal to both moderate Protestants and traditionalists.
    • Elizabeth famously cared more about "outward conformity" than "private belief," ensuring peace for most of her 45-year reign, avoiding the religious civil wars seen elsewhere in Europe.
  • Mary Queen of Scots: Elizabeth's Catholic cousin and a rival claimant to the English throne, who became a focal point for Catholic plots against Elizabeth. After 19 years as Elizabeth's prisoner, Mary was executed in 1587 for endorsing an assassination plot.
  • The Spanish Armada (1588):
    • Philip II of Spain, a powerful Catholic monarch, was enraged by Mary's execution and planned to invade England to restore Catholicism.
    • A fleet of 130 ships, the Spanish Armada, sailed for England, intending to escort a Spanish army from the Netherlands.
    • Defeat: English naval tactics (smaller, faster ships, fire vessels), leadership (e.g., Sir Francis Drake), and catastrophic Atlantic storms destroyed roughly half the fleet. England was not invaded.
    • Significance: This victory was a turning point, solidifying English national identity around the Protestant queen, English liberty, and the country's growing sea power.

The Stuart Kings: James I and Charles I

  • James I (1603-1625):
    • Son of Mary Queen of Scots, he became James VI of Scotland and James I of England, uniting the two kingdoms.
    • Believed passionately in the divine right of kings, which meant he felt monarchs answered only to God, not to Parliament or his subjects. This caused tension with Parliament.
    • His reign's enduring achievement was the King James Bible (1611), a translation that profoundly shaped English language and worship.
  • Charles I (1625-1649):
    • Succeeded his father, James I, and was even less skilled at managing Parliament.
    • Levied controversial taxes, most notably "ship money," which he extended inland, traditionally a coastal tax for the navy.
    • Petition of Right (1628): Parliament demanded he acknowledge limits to his power, including not imprisoning subjects without cause, levying taxes without Parliament's consent, or billeting soldiers in private homes. Charles signed it but largely ignored it.
    • "Personal Rule" (1629-1640): Charles dismissed Parliament and ruled without it for eleven years.
    • Scottish Rebellion: His attempt to impose a new prayer book on Scotland forced him to recall Parliament to raise money for a war.
    • Short Parliament (April–May 1640) & Long Parliament (November 1640): Both refused to cooperate, with the Long Parliament proving even more resistant and refusing to be dismissed.

The English Civil War and the Commonwealth (1642-1660)

  • English Civil War (1642-1651):
    • England divided into Cavaliers (king's supporters) and Roundheads (Parliament's army, led by figures like Oliver Cromwell).
    • Parliament's New Model Army, highly disciplined, proved decisive.
    • Execution of Charles I (January 30, 1649): Charles was captured, tried, and publicly beheaded—a revolutionary event in European history.
  • The Commonwealth (1649-1653):
    • After Charles's execution, England became a republic, a "Commonwealth," governed by Parliament and a Council of State.
  • The Protectorate (1653-1659):
    • Oliver Cromwell became Lord Protector, holding near-kingly powers. He was a devout Puritan, a brilliant military commander, but also an increasingly authoritarian ruler who dissolved Parliament when it displeased him.
    • After Cromwell's death in 1658, his son Richard's brief rule quickly collapsed.

The Restoration and the Glorious Revolution (1660-1689)

  • The Restoration (1660):
    • Parliament invited Charles II, Charles I's son, to return from exile and restore the monarchy.
    • The monarchy, the Church of England, and previously banned theaters were all restored.
    • Charles II was a politically shrewd king who carefully avoided direct conflict with Parliament.
  • James II (1685-1688):
    • Charles II's Catholic brother, who openly appointed Catholics to positions of power and suspended laws that kept them out of public life, seeming to steer England back toward Rome.
    • The birth of his son in June 1688 guaranteed a Catholic succession, alarming leading Protestants.
    • Invitation to William of Orange: Protestant leaders secretly invited William of Orange, the Dutch ruler married to James's Protestant daughter Mary, to intervene.
  • The Glorious Revolution (1688):
    • William landed in England with an army in November 1688. James's support collapsed, and he fled to France.
    • Parliament declared James had abdicated and offered the crown jointly to William and Mary, with conditions.
    • This nearly bloodless transfer of power permanently established that Parliament, not the monarch, was supreme.
  • English Bill of Rights (1689):
    • Parliament passed this document, declaring that the monarch could not suspend laws, levy taxes, or keep a standing army without parliamentary consent.
    • It also ensured free elections for Parliament and prohibited "excessive bail," "excessive fines," and "cruel and unusual punishments."
    • This document firmly established the principle of parliamentary supremacy, which has governed England ever since.

Review Questions

  1. How did Queen Elizabeth I navigate the intense religious divisions she inherited, and what were the key elements of her "Elizabethan Settlement" that aimed to bring peace and stability to England?
  2. Explain the reasons behind the Spanish Armada's invasion of England in 1588 and detail the various factors that contributed to its defeat. What lasting impact did this victory have on English national identity and foreign policy?
  3. Compare and contrast the political philosophies and approaches to Parliament of James I and Charles I. How did their shared belief in the "divine right of kings" contribute to the escalating conflicts that led to the English Civil War?
  4. Describe the forms of government that existed in England during the Commonwealth and Protectorate periods following the execution of Charles I. What were the challenges and characteristics of Oliver Cromwell's rule as Lord Protector?
  5. What events led to the Glorious Revolution, and why is it considered "glorious"? How did the English Bill of Rights (1689) fundamentally redefine the relationship between the monarch and Parliament, establishing the principle of parliamentary supremacy?
