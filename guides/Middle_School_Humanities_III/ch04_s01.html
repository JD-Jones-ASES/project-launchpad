Chapter 4, Section 1: Literature of The Enlightenment Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces three foundational figures of the Enlightenment: John Locke, Voltaire, and René Descartes. It explores Locke's political philosophy, including natural rights, the social contract, and the right to revolution, as articulated in his Second Treatise of Government. It then delves into Voltaire's satirical critique of false optimism and institutional cruelty in Candide. Finally, it touches upon Descartes's revolutionary philosophical method of doubt from his Discourse on Method, which laid the groundwork for Enlightenment epistemology.

Key Concepts

  • Natural Rights: Rights inherent to human beings simply by virtue of being human, existing independently of any government or law. Locke believed these include life, liberty, and property.
  • State of Nature: A philosophical thought experiment conceived by Locke to imagine human existence and rights before the formation of governments. It posits perfect freedom and equality, governed only by the "law of nature."
  • Law of Nature: Universal moral principles, deeply rooted in ancient and Christian philosophical traditions, that dictate human behavior and are discoverable through reason. Locke translated this concept into political vocabulary.
  • Social Contract: An implicit agreement among individuals to unite into a community and establish a government, surrendering some individual liberties for mutual protection and the secure enjoyment of their natural rights.
  • Consent of the Governed: The principle that a government's legitimacy and moral right to use state power is justified and lawful only when consented to by the people or society over which that political power is exercised.
  • Right to Revolution: Locke's radical idea that if a government fails to protect the natural rights of its citizens, or actively attacks them, it breaks the social contract, and the people have not only the right but the duty to overthrow and replace it.
  • Leibnizian Optimism: The philosophical belief, caricatured by Voltaire in Candide, that this world, despite its flaws, is "the best of all possible worlds" because it was created by a benevolent and omnipotent God.
  • Satire: A literary genre that uses humor, irony, exaggeration, or ridicule to expose and criticize people's stupidity or vices, particularly in the context of contemporary politics and other topical issues. Voltaire used it effectively in Candide.
  • Method of Doubt: René Descartes's philosophical approach of systematically doubting everything that can be doubted, no matter how slightly, in order to arrive at fundamental truths that are absolutely certain and beyond question.
  • Property as a Natural Right: Locke's groundbreaking assertion that an individual's right to their possessions and labor is a natural right, preceding and independent of government, a stark contrast to feudal systems.

Important Terms

  • Enlightenment: An influential intellectual and philosophical movement in 18th-century Europe emphasizing reason, individualism, and skepticism of tradition and religious dogma.
  • Divine Right of Kings: A political doctrine asserting that monarchs derive their authority directly from God and are not accountable to any earthly authority, often used to justify absolute rule.
  • Glorious Revolution (1688): A bloodless revolution in England that established the supremacy of Parliament over the monarch and significantly influenced Locke's political writings.
  • Auto-da-Fé: A ritual of public penance of convicted heretics or apostates, especially in the Spanish Inquisition, often involving torture and execution by burning. Voltaire critiques this in Candide.
  • Pangloss: A character in Voltaire's Candide, who is a philosopher and Candide's tutor, famously characterized as an extreme optimist who believes "all is for the best in this best of all possible worlds," despite overwhelming evidence to the contrary.
  • Surinam: A Dutch colony in South America, depicted in Candide as a place where the horrors of slavery are vividly illustrated through the encounter with a mutilated enslaved man.
  • Cultivate Our Garden: The concluding, iconic phrase from Candide, representing a practical, humble wisdom: focusing on improving one's immediate circumstances through work rather than engaging in grand, abstract philosophical debates.
  • Deism: A belief in a God who created the universe but does not intervene in its affairs (like a clockmaker who winds a clock and lets it run). Voltaire held deistic views, though he ferociously attacked the institutional Church.
  • Discourse on Method: René Descartes's seminal philosophical work (1637) that introduced his method of systematic doubt and laid the foundation for modern Western philosophy.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to the Enlightenment Texts

The Enlightenment's literary and philosophical achievements are defined for English-speaking students by three pivotal writers:

  • John Locke: Author of Second Treatise of Government, a cornerstone of political philosophy.
  • Voltaire: Author of the satirical novel Candide, which critiques false optimism.
  • René Descartes: Author of Discourse on Method, establishing foundational philosophical principles. These works are essential for understanding the period and offer rich opportunities for comparison with earlier texts.

John Locke: Second Treatise of Government (1689)

  • Context: Published in 1689, coinciding with the Glorious Revolution in England, which significantly shifted power towards Parliament. Locke had been working on the treatises for years.
  • Two Treatises of Government:
    • The First Treatise dismantled the concept of the divine right of kings by refuting Robert Filmer's arguments.
    • The Second Treatise offers a positive explanation of the nature and purpose of legitimate government.
  • Chapter 2: The State of Nature
    • Philosophical Thought Experiment: Locke asks readers to imagine a world without government, not as a historical claim, but to determine inherent human rights.
    • Natural Freedom and Equality: In this state, all people are naturally free to order their actions and dispose of their possessions and persons, subject only to the "law of nature," and no one has natural authority over another.
    • Law of Nature: This concept is not new to Locke; it draws from Stoicism, St. Paul, and Aquinas. It outlines inherent moral boundaries and translates a Christian natural law tradition into a modern political framework.
  • Chapter 8: Consent and the Beginning of Political Society
    • Source of Government Authority: Locke argues that government gains its authority solely through the consent of the governed.
    • The Social Contract: Individuals willingly agree to leave the state of nature and form a civil society, establishing common rules for mutual protection, security, and the peaceful enjoyment of their property.
  • Chapter 19: The Dissolution of Government
    • Government's Core Purpose: To preserve and protect the natural rights (especially property, which for Locke encompasses life, liberty, and estate) of the people.
    • Breach of Trust: If the legislative power transgresses this fundamental rule, attempting to seize absolute power or attacking the people's rights, it forfeits its authority.
    • Right and Duty to Rebel: When the government dissolves its own legitimacy by betraying the trust placed in it, the people are at liberty to establish a new legislative power. This was a profoundly revolutionary idea.
  • Property and Feudalism (Discussion Point): Locke's concept of property as a natural right was revolutionary. Unlike the feudal system, where land ownership was hierarchical and granted by the king, Locke posited that individuals have an inherent right to their property that precedes government. This idea challenged traditional European political structures and had profound implications for concepts like slavery.

Voltaire: Candide, or Optimism (1759)

  • Origin: Reportedly written quickly after the devastating Lisbon earthquake of 1755 (which killed 30,000 people). Voltaire saw the disaster as a powerful refutation of Gottfried Leibniz's philosophical optimism – the idea that this is "the best of all possible worlds."
  • Plot: The naive protagonist, Candide, journeys through a series of horrific events (earthquakes, war, inquisition, slavery) while his tutor, Pangloss (a caricature of Leibniz), relentlessly insists that "all is for the best."
  • Chapter 6: The Auto-da-Fé
    • Critique of Religious Institutions: Following the Lisbon earthquake, the Portuguese Inquisition burns supposed heretics, believing this will prevent future disasters.
    • Target: Voltaire's critique is aimed not at God, but at the human institutions that commit atrocities in God's name, rationalizing cruelty and suffering.
  • Chapter 19: The Slave in Surinam
    • Moral Weight: Candide encounters a brutally mutilated enslaved man (missing a hand and a leg) in the Dutch colony of Surinam. The man's suffering is the "price" Europeans pay for cheap sugar.
    • Hypocrisy of Christianity: The enslaved man highlights the profound contradiction between Christian teachings of human equality ("all of us children of Adam") and the barbarity of the slave trade.
    • Abolitionist Influence: This powerful passage significantly influenced the burgeoning abolitionist movement, despite Voltaire not being a fully committed abolitionist himself.
  • Chapter 30: Cultivate Our Garden
    • Practical Wisdom: The novel concludes with Candide and other survivors settling on a farm in Turkey. Candide, weary of Pangloss's relentless philosophizing, declares, "we must cultivate our garden."
    • Meaning: This is not defeatism, but a nuanced philosophy:
      1. A rejection of elaborate, dishonest philosophical systems that attempt to rationalize all suffering.
      2. An embrace of practical effort and work to improve one's immediate world, rather than abstract theory.
      3. A call for humility and action on a local, human scale.
    • Voltaire's Religious Views (Discussion Point): Voltaire was a Deist who believed in God but not divine intervention. He was a fierce critic of the institutional Church's corruption and cruelty, a defender of toleration, and challenged orthodox Christian dogma. He defies easy categorization.

René Descartes: Discourse on Method (1637)

  • Background: Descartes was a polymath (mathematician, scientist, philosopher) who served as a soldier. He deliberately wrote Discourse on Method in French, not Latin, to make it accessible to a broader educated public, not just scholars.
  • Part II: The Method of Doubt
    • Rebuilding Knowledge: Descartes resolved to systematically doubt all his existing beliefs, particularly those he had absorbed from tradition, teachers, and books, without critical examination.
    • Criterion for Truth: He would accept nothing as certain unless it presented itself to his reason with such clarity and distinctness that no possibility of doubt remained. This radical skepticism was a cornerstone of Enlightenment thought, emphasizing individual reason over received wisdom.

Review Questions

  1. How does John Locke's concept of the "state of nature" function as a philosophical tool, and what fundamental rights does he argue exist within it, independent of government?
  2. Explain the relationship between Locke's idea of the "social contract" and the "dissolution of government." What conditions, according to Locke, justify a people's right to revolution?
  3. Voltaire uses the Lisbon earthquake and the enslaved man in Surinam in Candide to challenge the philosophy of optimism. How do these specific events serve as powerful critiques of both philosophical complacency and institutional hypocrisy?
  4. What is the significance of the concluding phrase "cultivate our garden" in Voltaire's Candide? Discuss whether this represents a cynical retreat from the world's problems or a more profound form of practical wisdom.
  5. What was René Descartes's "method of doubt" as described in Discourse on Method? Why did he believe it was necessary to employ such a rigorous approach to knowledge?
