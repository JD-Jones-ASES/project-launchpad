Study Guide: The Rise and Fall of Spain

Overview

In 1492 Spain completed the Reconquista, expelled its Jewish population, and sent Columbus west — all in a single year. Within a generation, Spain ruled the largest empire the world had ever seen, stretching from Peru to the Pacific. Yet the seeds of decline were planted almost immediately: the encomienda system generated moral protest from within Spain itself, the silver that funded empire also fueled ruinous inflation, and eighty years of Dutch revolt and constant warfare exhausted Spanish power. By 1648 France had replaced Spain as Europe's dominant power. The chapter also explores two literary witnesses — Cervantes and Las Casas — who used what they loved about Spain to challenge what Spain was becoming.

Key Concepts

  • The Reconquista's Legacy: Eight centuries of Christian reconquest ended in 1492, shaping a Spanish national identity built around the conviction that Christian civilization must be defended and expanded at all costs. This identity drove both the conquest of the Americas and the eventual overextension that contributed to Spain's decline.
  • Disease and Conquest: The fall of the Aztec and Inca Empires was not primarily achieved by military force. Smallpox, to which indigenous populations had no immunity, killed far more than Spanish swords. Understanding this changes the meaning of "conquest."
  • Prophetic Witness from Within: Las Casas represents what the text calls "prophetic witness from within a system" — he did not leave Spain or abandon his faith, but used both to indict injustice. His transformation from encomendero to defender of indigenous peoples is one of history's most remarkable moral conversions.
  • The Valladolid Debate: Spain was unique among colonial powers in conducting a formal theological and legal inquiry into whether its own empire was morally legitimate. That the inquiry produced no verdict and did not stop the conquests does not make it less remarkable; it shows that moral questions were being asked even by the perpetrators.
  • Silver and Decline: Spain's New World silver made it the envy of Europe but created an inflation spiral that undermined its economy. When silver declined and wars continued, there was nothing structural to fall back on — a pattern of extraction-based wealth that the text contrasts unfavorably with Rome's taxation-and-trade model.

Important Terms

  • Reconquista: The centuries-long Christian campaign to retake the Iberian Peninsula from Muslim rulers; completed 1492 with the fall of Granada; shaped Spanish national identity.
  • Alhambra Decree (1492): The expulsion of Spain's Jewish population, issued the same year as Columbus's voyage.
  • Treaty of Tordesillas (1494): The agreement dividing newly discovered lands between Spain and Portugal; Spain received everything west of a line through the Atlantic.
  • Viceroyalty: A large colonial territory governed by a viceroy on behalf of the Spanish crown; the two great viceroyalties were New Spain and Peru.
  • Encomienda: The Spanish colonial labor system granting colonists (encomenderos) the labor of indigenous people in exchange for Christianizing them; in practice, forced labor working people to death.
  • Bartolome de las Casas: Dominican friar and former encomendero who became the great defender of indigenous peoples; wrote A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies (1542); his arguments produced the New Laws of 1542.
  • New Laws of 1542: Laws attempting to reform the worst encomienda abuses, produced partly by Las Casas's advocacy; had mixed success.
  • Valladolid Debate (1550-1551): Formal theological inquiry into whether Spain's conquest of the Americas was morally just; Sepulveda argued natural slavery, Las Casas argued universal human dignity; no verdict issued.
  • Counter-Reformation: The Catholic Church's reform movement responding to Protestantism; key institutions: Council of Trent, Jesuits, renewed Inquisition; Spain under Philip II was its political champion.
  • Battle of Lepanto (1571): The Spanish-led Catholic naval coalition that destroyed the Ottoman fleet; Cervantes fought there and was wounded.
  • Moriscos: Descendants of Muslim converts to Christianity expelled by Philip III in 1609; roughly 300,000 skilled workers lost to Spain's economy.
  • Treaty of Utrecht (1713): Peace settlement ending the War of Spanish Succession; installed a French Bourbon as king of Spain but stripped Spain of its European territories; ended Spanish hegemony.

Detailed Notes

The Year 1492

  • Three World-Historical Events
    • January: Fall of Granada; Reconquista completed after nearly 800 years
    • Alhambra Decree: Jews expelled from Spain
    • August: Columbus departs for Asia; reaches the Americas instead
  • Immediate Consequences
    • Treaty of Tordesillas (1494) divided known world between Spain and Portugal
    • Neither treaty asked indigenous peoples' opinions
    • Spain positioned for rapid empire-building

Conquest of the Americas

  • Cortes and the Aztecs (1519-1521)
    • Roughly 500 soldiers and a handful of horses
    • Fall of Tenochtitlan: a civilization of millions with a capital larger than any European city
    • Smallpox played decisive role: killed far more than Spanish weapons
    • Destruction of Tenochtitlan in 1521 was the destruction of a world
  • Pizarro and the Incas (1530s)
    • Even smaller force; captured Inca ruler Atahualpa
    • Atahualpa filled a room with gold and silver as ransom; was executed anyway
    • Established Spain's claim to a vast viceroyalty across South America

The Colonial System

  • The Encomienda
    • Encomenderos granted labor of specific indigenous people
    • Official purpose: Christianization and protection
    • Actual practice: forced labor; men worked to death in silver mines; families destroyed
  • Las Casas's Protest
    • A Short Account (1542): addressed directly to Prince Philip
    • Eyewitness testimony: "I speak of what I myself saw. I was there."
    • Described indigenous peoples as fully human, made in God's image
    • Called Spanish behavior "ravening wild beasts, wolves, tigers, or lions"
    • New Laws (1542): partial reform with mixed enforcement
  • The Valladolid Debate
    • Sepulveda: Aristotelian natural slavery; indigenous peoples barbarous
    • Las Casas: all humans have reason; no just war against those who never harmed Spain
    • No verdict; conquests resumed; but Spain had asked the question

Spain at the Height of Power

  • Philip II (r. 1556-1598)
    • Ruled the largest empire on earth
    • Champion of Catholic Counter-Reformation
    • The Escorial: palace-monastery-mausoleum as monument to Spanish Catholic civilization
    • Battle of Lepanto (1571): largest Mediterranean naval battle since antiquity; destroyed Ottoman fleet
  • Miguel de Cervantes at Lepanto
    • Future author of Don Quixote fought there; wounded in the left hand
    • Called Lepanto "the greatest event witnessed by ages past, present, and to come"

The Long Decline

  • Seeds of Decline (Philip II's reign)
    • Dutch revolt (c. 1568): 80 years of costly warfare
    • Spanish Armada defeat (1588): psychological and strategic blow
    • Silver spent faster than mined; war costs exceeded revenue
  • After Philip II
    • Expulsion of Moriscos (1609): 300,000 skilled workers lost
    • Thirty Years' War (1618-1648): further drain on Spanish resources
    • Peace of Westphalia (1648): France replaced Spain as dominant European power
    • War of Spanish Succession (1701-1714): last Habsburg king died without heir
    • Treaty of Utrecht (1713): Bourbon king installed; Spanish European empire ended

Literature: Two Witnesses to an Empire

  • Cervantes and Don Quixote
    • Biography: Lepanto, five years enslaved in Algiers, poverty, prison
    • Don Quixote (Part I 1605, Part II 1615): first modern novel, greatest Spanish literary work
    • Windmill episode: Don Quixote sees giants; Sancho sees windmills
    • Simultaneously comic and tragic; Spain living in rhetoric of crusade while ground shifted
    • Part II's Cave of Montesinos: the ideal finally begins to crack from within
  • Las Casas's Short Account
    • Addresses indigenous peoples as innocent, faithful, and long-suffering
    • Describes Spanish colonizers as "ravening wild beasts"
    • "I speak of what I myself saw" — eyewitness moral authority
    • Grounds argument in imago Dei: baptizing people you exploit mocks Christian faith

Connections

  • Spain's pattern of extraction-based imperial wealth parallels and contrasts with Rome's taxation-and-trade model (covered in earlier curricula) — Rome's system proved more durable.
  • Las Casas's theological argument (imago Dei) anticipates the Catholic Social Teaching of Rerum Novarum (Chapter 6): both ground human dignity in the image of God rather than in political ideology.
  • The Valladolid Debate's questions about conquest and human dignity connect forward to the Enlightenment's natural rights philosophy (Chapter 4) and the abolitionist movement (Chapter 5).
  • Spain's expulsion of the Moriscos parallels Louis XIV's expulsion of the Huguenots (Chapter 3): both acts of religious intolerance caused significant self-inflicted economic damage.
  • Cervantes's portrait of Spain living in outdated heroic ideals connects to the broader theme of the tension between tradition and modernity that runs through the entire course.
