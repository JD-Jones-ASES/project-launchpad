Chapter 6, Section 1 Study Guide: Industry – The Machinery of a New World

Overview

This section explores the profound and rapid technological and political transformations of the 19th century, which dramatically reshaped human life, global connections, and the European balance of power. It highlights how advancements like the railroad and telegraph, alongside imperial projects like the Suez Canal and the "Scramble for Africa," created a fundamentally new world, setting the stage for both reform efforts and the catastrophic events of 1914.

Key Concepts

  • Technological Revolution as a Backdrop: The political and social debates of the 19th century occurred amidst unprecedented physical transformation, driven by technologies that drastically increased the speed of communication, travel, and trade.
  • The Railroad as a Global Integrator: No technology transformed daily life more than the railroad, which achieved for land travel what sailing ships did for sea—allowing goods, people, and armies to move across vast distances faster and cheaper. This integrated national economies, facilitated urban growth, and revolutionized military logistics.
  • Information's Emancipation: The electric telegraph was revolutionary because it was the first technology to transmit information faster than a human could travel, establishing the principle that information could be separated from physical transport. This had immediate impacts on finance, governance, and news dissemination.
  • Technology and Imperialism: Major engineering achievements like the Suez Canal, while beneficial for global trade, were often built in non-European countries using non-European labor, operated primarily for European benefit, and served as strategic assets for expanding European empires.
  • Drivers of the "Scramble for Africa": The rapid colonization of Africa was fueled by economic desires (raw materials, markets), strategic military interests, nationalist prestige, and the widespread European ideology of civilizational superiority, which justified brutal conquest.
  • German Unification and the Balance of Power: The creation of a unified German Empire in 1871 under Prussian leadership profoundly disrupted the established European balance of power, creating a new, powerful military and industrial giant that sought its own "place in the sun," ultimately leading to a division of Europe into armed camps.

Important Terms

  • Railroad: A system of iron (later steel) tracks on which steam-powered locomotives pulled carriages or freight cars. It was the defining technology of the mid-nineteenth century, slashing travel times, opening new markets, enabling mass troop movements, and knitting together national economies.
  • Telegraph: An electrical communication system that transmits coded signals (Morse code) over wires. Developed in the 1830s–1840s by Samuel Morse and others, it was the first technology in human history to transmit information faster than a human being could travel, connecting continents via submarine cables by 1866.
  • Berlin Conference: A conference held in Berlin from November 1884 to February 1885, convened by German Chancellor Bismarck, at which the European powers established rules for the colonial division of Africa. No African leader or representative was invited. It formalized the principle that European claims to African territory would be recognized if backed by effective occupation, accelerating the "Scramble for Africa."
  • German unification: The political process by which the German states were united into a single nation-state, the German Empire, in 1871, under the leadership of Prussian Chancellor Otto von Bismarck and King (later Kaiser) Wilhelm I. The new Germany was the most powerful military state on the European continent and immediately disrupted the balance of power established after Napoleon's defeat.

Detailed Notes

The Machinery of a New World

  • Rapid Transformation: The 19th century saw unprecedented physical changes.
    • Communication Speed: In 1800, a message from London to Edinburgh took days by horse; by 1870, it took minutes via electrical impulse.
    • Travel Speed: A four-day coach journey from London to Edinburgh in 1800 became a nine-hour train ride by 1850.
  • Fundamental Changes: These were not minor conveniences but transformations in the basic conditions of human life, impacting:
    • The speed of news dissemination.
    • The mobility of armies.
    • The ease of continent-spanning trade.
    • Global relationships.
  • Historical Significance: Understanding this industrial machinery is crucial for comprehending major historical events like Rerum Novarum (a papal encyclical on social conditions) and the outbreak of World War I in 1914.

The Railroad: Shrinking the World

  • Defining Technology: The railroad was the most transformative technology of the 19th century.
    • Origins: The first commercial steam railway, the Stockton and Darlington Railway, opened in northeastern England in 1825, using George Stephenson's locomotive to transport coal.
    • Rapid Spread: Within a generation, railroads spread across Britain, France, Belgium, Germany, and the United States.
  • Staggering Effects:
    • Reduced Travel Times: Cut journeys dramatically (e.g., London to Edinburgh from four days to nine hours).
    • Economic Integration:
      • Allowed perishable goods (fish, vegetables, dairy) to reach distant urban markets before spoiling.
      • Enabled cities to grow larger by drawing food from hundreds of miles away.
      • Facilitated inter-city trade (e.g., Birmingham manufacturers shipping to Liverpool in a morning).
  • Transformation of Warfare:
    • Armies could be assembled and transported in days, not months.
    • Led to faster wars, faster escalation, and increased need for pre-planning.
    • Military planners developed detailed mobilization schedules, which would be crucial in the lead-up to WWI.
  • Historical Parallel: The railroad performed for land what sailing ships did for sea, enabling faster and cheaper movement over vast distances. It can be compared to the Roman road network as a system of imperial communication and control, but on a much grander scale (50,000 Roman miles vs. 700,000 railroad miles by 1914).

The Electric Telegraph: Faster Than the Body

  • Breakthrough Communication: In 1844, Samuel Morse sent the first long-distance telegraph message.
  • Revolutionary Principle: For the first time, information traveled faster than a human could carry it, fundamentally changing how information was conceived and exchanged.
  • Global Connection: By 1866, a transatlantic cable linked Britain and North America, reducing communication time from weeks to minutes.
  • Immediate Impacts:
    • Finance: Financial markets in London and New York could react simultaneously to news.
    • Governance: Governments could direct distant ambassadors and military commanders in real time.
    • News: News from distant conflicts reached home audiences within hours.
  • Legacy: The telegraph is the direct ancestor of all modern communication technologies (telephone, radio, internet), establishing the revolutionary idea that information could be separated from physical transport.

The Suez Canal and the Compression of the Globe

  • Major Engineering Feat: Opened in 1869, connecting the Mediterranean and Red Seas through the Isthmus of Suez in Egypt.
    • Construction: Built over ten years by French engineer Ferdinand de Lesseps, utilizing Egyptian forced labor.
  • Dramatic Route Reduction: Cut the sea route from London to Bombay (India) by nearly half (from 11,000 to 6,200 miles), reducing journey time from six months to three weeks.
  • Strategic Importance: Of immense strategic and commercial value to the British Empire, which controlled India and parts of East Africa and Asia.
    • Britain effectively seized control in 1875 when Prime Minister Benjamin Disraeli purchased the Egyptian government's shares.
  • Pattern of Imperialism: The Canal exemplifies how 19th-century European expansion involved:
    • Technological achievements benefiting global trade.
    • Construction in non-European countries using non-European labor.
    • Primary operation for European benefit.
    • Conversion into military and strategic assets for European empires, demonstrating the non-neutral relationship between technology and empire.

The Berlin Conference and the Scramble for Africa

  • Opening Africa's Interior: Before 1880, Africa's interior was largely unknown to Europeans due to disease. The quinine treatment for malaria (developed in the 1840s) allowed European explorers, missionaries, and soldiers to penetrate the continent.
  • The Scramble: Between 1880 and 1895, European powers (Britain, France, Germany, Belgium, Portugal, Italy, Spain) rapidly and ruthlessly colonized almost the entire African continent.
  • The Berlin Conference (1884–85):
    • Purpose: Convened by German Chancellor Bismarck to regulate European competition for African territory.
    • Exclusion of Africans: Fourteen nations sent representatives; no African leaders were present.
    • Rules of Conquest: Established the principle of "effective occupation"—European claims to territory would only be recognized if backed by actual presence and control—which accelerated the rush to claim land.
  • Consequences:
    • By 1914, Europe controlled ~84% of the world's land surface.
    • Only Ethiopia (which defeated Italy in 1896) and Liberia remained independent.
    • Atrocities: The Belgian Congo, ruled as King Leopold II's personal property, became infamous for systematic atrocities, such as workers having their hands cut off for failing to meet rubber quotas.
    • Vast Empires: The British Empire spanned a quarter of the globe; France controlled vast territories in West Africa and Southeast Asia; Germany acquired territories in Africa and the Pacific.
  • Driving Forces and Justifications: The Scramble was driven by:
    • Desire for raw materials (rubber, copper, ivory, diamonds).
    • New markets for manufactured goods.
    • Strategic naval bases.
    • Nationalist prestige.
    • Ideology: The widespread belief in European civilizational superiority and a "civilizing mission" was used to justify brutal conquest, echoing the Spanish conquest of the Americas.

German Unification and the New Balance of Power

  • Political Transformation: German unification in 1871 under Prussian leadership was one of the most significant political events of the century.
    • Historical Context: "Germany" had been a patchwork of hundreds of independent states for centuries.
    • Prussian Leadership: Prussia, a powerful and militaristic state, led by Chancellor Otto von Bismarck, unified these states through force.
  • Wars of Unification:
    • Denmark (1864)
    • Austria (1866)
    • France (1870–71)
  • Proclamation of Empire: Following victory over France, the German Empire was proclaimed in the Hall of Mirrors at Versailles, a symbolic gesture of German ascendance. France was forced to cede Alsace-Lorraine and pay a huge indemnity.
  • Disruption of European Order: German unification shattered the balance of power established by the Congress of Vienna (1815).
    • New Giant: A unified German state emerged as Europe's largest industrial economy and most efficient military, determined to assert its influence ("place in the sun").
    • Readjustment: Other powers (Britain, France, Russia, Austria-Hungary) had to scramble to adjust to this new reality. This can be compared to the Peace of Westphalia's establishment of the balance of power principle.
  • Alliance System:
    • Bismarck's Strategy: Bismarck cautiously built a complex network of alliances to isolate France and preserve peace.
    • Wilhelm II's Dismissal: This system began to unravel after the impulsive Kaiser Wilhelm II dismissed Bismarck in 1890.
    • Two Armed Camps: By 1907, Europe had divided into the Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy) and the Triple Entente (France, Russia, Britain), setting the stage for the escalating tensions leading to WWI.

Review Questions

  1. How did the technological advancements of the 19th century, particularly the railroad and telegraph, fundamentally change the "basic conditions of human life" and global interactions? Provide specific examples for each technology.
  2. Analyze the complex relationship between European technological superiority and imperial expansion, using the Suez Canal and the "Scramble for Africa" as primary examples. How did technology enable and serve imperial ambitions?
  3. Discuss the motivations behind the "Scramble for Africa." Beyond economic factors, what ideological beliefs did Europeans use to justify their conquest and rule over African territories?
  4. Explain how the unification of Germany in 1871 drastically altered the European balance of power. What were the immediate consequences of this shift, and how did it contribute to the formation of rival alliance systems by 1907?
  5. Compare and contrast the impacts of the 19th-century railroad network with the Roman road network (as mentioned in the text). In what ways were they similar in their functions for integrating territories and exercising control, and what was the key difference in their scale and context?
