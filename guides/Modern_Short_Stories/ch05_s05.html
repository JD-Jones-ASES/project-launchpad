Study Guide: The Iceberg in Practice

Chapter 5 — Ernest Hemingway, "Hills Like White Elephants" (1927)

Overview

This section steps back from the story's specific moments to examine the technique behind them. The iceberg principle — introduced earlier in the course — is Hemingway's core idea: the power of a story comes from what the writer leaves out. "Hills Like White Elephants" is the purest demonstration of that principle. The story has no backstory, no interior monologue, no character names, and no resolution. What it has instead is the feeling of all those things, carried by two characters talking in a railway station bar. This section examines how that works and why it matters.

Key Concepts

  • The iceberg principle: a story's power comes from what is left out. The writer knows the full mass; the reader sees only the tip — but feels the rest.
  • "Hills Like White Elephants" deliberately omits backstory, character names, interior monologue, and resolution.
  • Hemingway's restraint is not coldness; it is respect — for the characters and for the reader.
  • The close reading exercise in this section examines the gap between the man's words (he says he'll defer to her) and his actions (he never actually drops the subject).
  • The story ends without resolution. The ending enacts the iceberg principle: we feel what has been decided without being told.

Important Terms

Iceberg principle — Hemingway's theory that the dignity of a story's movement comes from what the writer leaves out. What is omitted is felt even without being seen.

Restraint — The deliberate withholding of information, explanation, or emotional commentary. In Hemingway, restraint is a form of respect and a source of power.

Interior monologue — Direct presentation of a character's thoughts, feelings, and mental processes. "Hills Like White Elephants" contains none; the reader must infer interior life from external dialogue and action.

Resolution — The moment in a story when the conflict is settled or the decision revealed. This story provides no resolution — the ending is an enactment of the iceberg.

Detailed Notes

What the Story Leaves Out

The list of absences in this story is striking:

  • No backstory: we do not know how long they have been together, where they met, where they are coming from.
  • No names: the man is "the American." The girl is "the girl" or sometimes "Jig."
  • No interior monologue: we never enter either character's thoughts.
  • No resolution: the story ends with the man carrying bags and the girl saying she feels fine. We do not know what she has decided.

A less confident writer would have filled in those gaps with explanation and emotional commentary. Hemingway trusts that what is felt does not need to be said.

How You Know These People

By the end of the story, you know the man and the girl — not because Hemingway has described them, but because he has let you overhear them. The section compares this to sitting near a couple in a restaurant and gradually realizing that their conversation is far more serious than its tone suggests. That is the kind of knowledge "Hills Like White Elephants" provides: intimate, earned, and indirect.

Restraint as Respect

The section addresses a common misreading: that Hemingway's restraint is coldness or indifference. It is not. It is respect for the characters, who are allowed to be complicated without being explained, and for the reader, who is trusted to do the work of interpretation. This is a high form of literary courtesy — the writer refuses to condescend by spelling out what the reader can discover.

The Close Reading Exercise

The exercise asks you to count how many times the man says some version of "I don't want you to do it if you don't want to." Then count how many times, after saying this, he actually drops the subject. The gap between those two numbers is a precise measure of his pressure — a pressure more effective than open argument because it looks like deference while functioning as insistence.

The Ending and the Iceberg

The story ends with the man carrying the bags across the station, and the girl saying she feels fine. We do not know what she has decided. The ending is an iceberg ending: the resolution — whatever it is — is submerged. You feel it without seeing it. That feeling is Hemingway's point. The iceberg holds up everything visible, even when you cannot see the iceberg itself.
