"The Dead" by James Joyce: Part One Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the Morkan sisters' annual dance, a long-standing Dublin tradition, and the arrival of their favorite nephew, Gabriel Conroy, and his wife, Gretta. It establishes the lively yet somewhat old-fashioned setting, and immediately delves into Gabriel's internal anxieties and social discomfort through his interactions with the caretaker's daughter, Lily, and his ongoing preparations for a speech he is to deliver.

Key Concepts

  • Tradition and the Past: The Morkan sisters' annual dance is a cherished, decades-old event, symbolizing a fading way of life in Dublin. The sisters themselves embody an older generation.
  • Gabriel Conroy's Character: Gabriel is presented as an intellectual, self-conscious, and somewhat insecure man who often feels superior to others but is easily unsettled by criticism or perceived social missteps. He grapples with his identity and place within his family and society.
  • Social Class and Cultural Divide: The interaction between Gabriel and Lily highlights a subtle but palpable class distinction. Gabriel's internal monologue about his speech further emphasizes his anxiety about his audience's "grade of culture" versus his own "superior education."
  • Discomfort and Alienation: Gabriel frequently experiences moments of unease and discomposure, both from external interactions (Lily's remark) and his own self-critical thoughts. This foreshadows a deeper sense of alienation.
  • Appearance vs. Reality: Gabriel maintains a composed exterior, but internally, he is plagued by self-doubt and overthinking, particularly regarding his intellect and social standing.

Important Terms

  • Caretaker’s Daughter: Lily, a young working-class girl employed by the Morkan sisters to assist with the party. Her bitterness hints at deeper societal issues.
  • Usher’s Island: The location of the Morkan sisters' "dark gaunt house" in Dublin, a setting that emphasizes the old and perhaps fading nature of their lives.
  • Haddington Road / Antient Concert Rooms: Locations associated with Mary Jane's musical career, signifying her professional success and role as the "main prop" of the household.
  • Adam and Eve’s: The church where Aunt Julia is the leading soprano, reflecting her past achievements and connection to the community.
  • Diamond-bone sirloins, three-shilling tea, bottled stout: Specific items mentioned to illustrate the Morkan sisters' commitment to modest but good living, indicative of a particular historical period and lifestyle.
  • Freddy Malins: A perpetually late and potentially inebriated guest, whose anticipated arrival causes anxiety for the Morkan sisters, highlighting social anxieties about appearances.
  • Goloshes: Waterproof overshoes, worn by Gabriel and Gretta. They become a point of playful contention and symbolize Gabriel's attempts to bring "continental" (European) modernity into their Irish lives, as well as his overly protective nature.
  • Gresham: The name of a well-known Dublin hotel where Gabriel and Gretta have booked a room, indicating their desire for a private, more modern arrangement separate from the family home.
  • Robert Browning, Shakespeare, The Melodies: Literary and musical references that underscore Gabriel's intellectual interests and his struggle to connect with his audience through appropriate cultural references.

Detailed Notes

1. Setting the Scene: The Morkan Sisters' Annual Dance

  • A Lively Household: The story opens at the Morkan sisters' annual dance, a bustling event that has been a tradition for "thirty years." The house on Usher's Island, though "dark gaunt," comes alive with guests.
  • The Morkan Sisters (Miss Kate and Miss Julia): Elderly, fussy, but ultimately warm and hospitable. Kate is described as "more vivacious," while Julia is "quite grey" and seems more passive. They live with their niece, Mary Jane.
  • Mary Jane: Their niece, and the "main prop of the household." She is an accomplished organist and music teacher, whose pupils come from "better-class families," signifying her elevated social standing and financial contribution.
  • Lily, the Caretaker's Daughter: Overworked and "literally run off her feet" attending to guests. She is a slim, pale girl with "hay-coloured hair," providing a glimpse into the working-class life within the household.
  • Anticipation and Concern: The aunts are anxious about the late arrival of Gabriel and Gretta, and particularly worried that Freddy Malins might arrive "screwed" (drunk), which would be an embarrassment, especially to Mary Jane's pupils.

2. Gabriel and Gretta's Arrival

  • A Late Entrance: Gabriel and Gretta arrive late, attributing Gretta's prolonged dressing as the cause. Gabriel scrapes snow from his "goloshes," indicating the winter weather.
  • Gabriel's Interaction with Lily:
    • Lily greets Gabriel respectfully, but Gabriel's attempt at friendly banter ("we’ll be going to your wedding one of these fine days") backfires.
    • Lily's bitter retort, "The men that is now is only all palaver and what they can get out of you," deeply discomposes Gabriel.
    • Gabriel, embarrassed, quickly tries to give Lily a coin as a Christmas gesture, which she initially refuses but then accepts. This exchange highlights social awkwardness and a class divide.
  • Gabriel's Physical Description: He is a "stout tallish young man" with a "hairless face," polished glasses, and glossy black hair. His appearance suggests a refined, intellectual air.

3. Gabriel's Inner Turmoil and Social Anxieties

  • Discomposure and Self-Doubt: Lily's sharp remark leaves Gabriel feeling "discomposed" and casts a "gloom over him." He immediately feels he "had made a mistake."
  • The Impending Speech: Gabriel is preoccupied with a speech he is to give later. He reviews his notes, particularly questioning a quotation from Robert Browning.
  • Cultural Superiority and Insecurity: He fears his audience (who he perceives as having a lower "grade of culture") won't understand Browning and will think he is "airing his superior education." He worries he will "make himself ridiculous" and "fail with them just as he had failed with the girl in the pantry." This reveals his deep insecurity despite his intellectual pride.

4. Family Interactions and Social Commentary

  • Aunts' Affection for Gabriel: Kate and Julia warmly greet Gabriel, their "favourite nephew," son of their dead sister Ellen.
  • The "Goloshes" Conversation:
    • Gabriel insists Gretta wear goloshes for health, revealing his somewhat fastidious and protective nature.
    • Gretta playfully resists, joking about Gabriel's fussiness and his desire for her to wear a "diving suit." She finds the word "goloshes" funny because it reminds her of "Christy Minstrels."
    • Aunt Julia's ignorance about goloshes highlights a generational or perhaps cultural gap, as Gabriel mentions they are worn "on the continent." Gabriel expresses slight anger at the trivialization of what he considers a practical, modern habit.
  • Plans for the Night: Gabriel and Gretta have booked a room at the Gresham Hotel and arranged for a caretaker, Bessie, to look after their children. This indicates a degree of independence from the aunts' household.
  • Aunt Kate's Concern for Lily: Kate remarks that Lily "is not the girl she was at all," suggesting a recent change or problem with Lily, which Gabriel almost asks about before being interrupted.
  • Aunt Julia's Wandering: The section ends with Aunt Julia wandering off down the stairs, prompting Aunt Kate to call out to her. This suggests Julia's increasing frailty or mental disengagement.

Review Questions

  1. Describe the initial atmosphere of the Morkan sisters' annual dance. What specific details (e.g., character actions, descriptions of the house, references to the past) contribute to this atmosphere?
  2. How does Lily's interaction with Gabriel, particularly her bitter remark, immediately establish a sense of tension or discomfort in the story? What does this exchange reveal about Gabriel's character and his perceptions of himself?
  3. What are Gabriel's primary concerns and anxieties regarding his upcoming speech? How do these concerns connect to broader themes of social class, education, and his own self-image?
  4. Discuss the significance of the "goloshes" conversation. What does it reveal about Gabriel and Gretta's relationship, and what might the goloshes themselves symbolize in the context of the story's setting and characters?
  5. What impressions do we get of Aunt Kate and Aunt Julia Morkan from this section? How do their personalities and their role in the household contrast with or complement Gabriel and Gretta's presence?
