Study Guide: The Unresolved Ending

Chapter 2 | Anton Chekhov, "The Lady with the Dog"

Overview

This section addresses the most discussed and debated aspect of "The Lady with the Dog": its refusal to end. Chekhov does not punish his characters, reward them, or deliver a moral. He leaves Gurov and Anna in a hotel room, in love, with no solution in sight, and then stops. This is not laziness or incompleteness; it is one of the most deliberate artistic choices in the story, and understanding why Chekhov makes it — and what effect it has on the reader — is essential to understanding the story as a whole. The analysis connects the ending to the iceberg principle from Chapter 1 and suggests that the reader's frustration with the non-ending is itself part of what the story is doing.

Key Concepts

  • Open ending as artistic choice: The refusal to resolve is a conscious decision, not an incompletion
  • Resolution as falsification: Chekhov's position that neat endings would misrepresent the emotional reality of certain situations
  • The iceberg principle applied to endings: Withholding resolution because resolution would be false, not merely to create mystery
  • Reader as participant: The reader's frustration with the ending replicates the characters' frustration with their situation
  • Honest fiction: The idea that a story can honor its subject by refusing the comfort of closure

Important Terms

Open ending — An ending that deliberately withholds resolution, leaving characters and situations unresolved. Distinguished from an ambiguous ending, which may suggest multiple possible resolutions.

Conventional resolution — An ending that closes the story's central conflict through a recognizable narrative structure: characters are punished, rewarded, reconciled, separated, or changed in clearly marked ways.

Closure — The sense of completion and finality that conventional endings provide; one of the things Chekhov deliberately withholds.

The iceberg principle — Hemingway's concept (introduced in Chapter 1) that a story's strength comes from what the writer leaves out. Chekhov applies this to resolution itself: what is left out (how the story ends) is where the power lives.

Reader participation — The way some literary techniques make readers active co-creators of meaning rather than passive recipients. Chekhov's open ending requires readers to sit with unresolved experience rather than consuming a resolved one.

Honest fiction — Writing that prioritizes emotional and psychological truth over narrative convenience or reader comfort.

Detailed Notes

What the Ending Does (and Does Not) Do

The ending of "The Lady with the Dog" refuses nearly everything readers typically expect from a story's conclusion. It does not resolve the affair. It does not punish adultery or reward loyalty. It does not show the couple finding a way to be together or a way to part. It does not deliver a lesson about love, marriage, or human nature. Instead, it shows Gurov and Anna in a hotel room, aware that their situation is impossible, aware that it is also irreducible, and naming what faces them: "the most complicated and difficult part." Then Chekhov stops.

Why Resolution Would Be a Lie

Chekhov's reasoning, as the analysis presents it, is philosophical as much as aesthetic. Some situations do not resolve. Some problems are not solved; they are lived with. Gurov and Anna's love is one of those problems. It is too real to abandon — Gurov has demonstrated this at every stage of the story. It is too entangled to resolve — they are both married, they live in different cities, the social world they inhabit offers no framework for what they have. Any ending that pretended to resolve this would falsify the truth of the situation. The honest choice — the only honest choice, given what the story has established — is to leave it exactly where it is.

The Iceberg Principle and the Ending

The analysis explicitly connects Chekhov's ending to the iceberg principle from Chapter 1. The iceberg principle holds that a story's strength comes from what the writer leaves out. Applied to endings, this means that what Chekhov leaves below the waterline is resolution itself. He is not withholding resolution to be mysterious or to frustrate readers arbitrarily. He is withholding it because including it would falsify the experience the story is creating. The ending's power comes precisely from its refusal to provide what we expect — and what we expect would, in this case, be a lie.

The Reader's Frustration as the Story's Final Effect

One of the analysis's most striking claims is that the reader's frustration with the ending is not a problem to be overcome but the story's final effect. By refusing resolution, Chekhov makes the reader share the condition of the characters: unable to resolve the situation, unable to leave it, caught between what is and what cannot be. Gurov and Anna feel exactly what the reader feels on finishing the story — the sense of something unresolved that cannot comfortably be put down. That is a remarkable achievement: the ending does not describe the characters' predicament; it enacts it in the reader's experience.

Close Reading the Final Two Paragraphs

The close reading exercise asks readers to examine the story's last paragraphs carefully:

  • What do Gurov and Anna actually understand about their situation?
  • What do they refuse to face, or cannot yet name?
  • What does Chekhov mean by "the most complicated and difficult part" — what part is this?
  • Why does he choose to end here, at the beginning of something, rather than at the end?
  • What has he chosen to leave below the waterline?

These questions do not have single correct answers. They are designed to train readers to think about what stories choose to withhold and why.
