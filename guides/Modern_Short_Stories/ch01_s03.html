The Evolution of the Modern Short Story: A Study Guide

Overview

This section explores a significant transformation in short fiction that occurred in the late nineteenth century. It highlights the shift from traditional, plot-driven narratives, where every event leads to a clear resolution, to modern stories that prioritize the internal experiences, consciousness, and ordinary moments of characters, often leaving resolutions ambiguous to reflect the complexities of real life.

Key Concepts

  • The Modern Turn (circa 1890): This refers to the fundamental change in short fiction during the late 19th century. Writers moved away from focusing solely on external events and plot mechanics toward exploring the internal lives of characters.
  • Shift from "What Happens Next?" to "What Does It Feel Like?": Traditional stories emphasized plot progression and resolution. Modern stories, however, delve into the emotional and psychological experience of being a character in a particular moment, focusing on feelings, perceptions, and consciousness rather than just events.
  • Prioritizing Interior Life: A core conviction of the modern short story is that an honest portrayal of a character's inner thoughts, feelings, and perceptions is a sufficient and compelling basis for a narrative, even without a dramatic external plot.
  • Emphasis on Ordinary Moments: Instead of grand, dramatic occurrences (like murders or last-minute rescues), modern stories often find significance in the subtle, everyday experiences – a pause, a quiet observation, a slow realization – that shape a person's understanding of life.
  • The "Unfinished" Nature of Modern Stories: Unlike traditional stories that provide clear conclusions, modern short stories frequently end without definitive resolutions or moral lessons. This lack of closure is intentional, reflecting the ongoing, often unresolved nature of life itself and encouraging readers to contemplate the ambiguities.

Important Terms

  • Traditional Short Story: A type of short fiction characterized by a tightly wound plot, a clear climax, and a definitive resolution where mysteries are solved, conflicts are settled, and outcomes are clear. Edgar Allan Poe is cited as an exemplar.
  • Modern Short Story: A type of short fiction that emerged in the late 19th century, focusing less on external plot and more on the internal experiences, consciousness, and psychological states of characters. These stories often lack conventional resolution and highlight ordinary moments. Anton Chekhov, James Joyce, and Ernest Hemingway are key figures.
  • Plot Machinery: Refers to the conventional elements of traditional narratives that drive the plot forward, such as dramatic events (murders, revelations, rescues), clear conflicts, and resolutions.
  • Interior Life: The sum of a character's thoughts, feelings, perceptions, motivations, and consciousness. Modern short stories extensively explore this inner world.
  • Texture of Consciousness: A phrase describing the intricate, nuanced way in which individuals think, notice details, process information, and understand (or misunderstand) themselves and the world around them. It emphasizes the subjective and often fragmented nature of human experience.
  • Resolution: The conclusion of a story, where conflicts are typically resolved, questions are answered, and a sense of closure is achieved. Modern stories often subvert or omit traditional resolution.

Detailed Notes

The Old Way: Plot-Driven Narratives

Before the late 19th century, many short stories followed a predictable structure. They were often "tightly wound machines" where every sentence served to propel the plot towards a specific climax and a clear-cut ending.

  • Characteristics:
    • Event-focused: Something dramatic happens.
    • Clear outcomes: Mysteries are solved, villains are defeated, secrets are revealed.
    • Definitive resolution: The reader understands "exactly what happened and why."
  • Example: Edgar Allan Poe's stories are described as prime examples of this style, where the ending "snaps shut like a trap."

The New Way: The Emergence of the Modern Short Story

Around 1890, a significant shift, often called "The Modern Turn," began to transform short fiction. Writers started to lose interest in the mechanical aspects of plot and turned their attention to something more subtle and introspective.

  • Shift in Focus:
    • From External to Internal: The focus moved away from external "machinery of plot" (murders, revelations, rescues) and toward "the way people actually experience their own lives."
    • From Dramatic to Ordinary: The significance shifted from grand, dramatic events to the nuanced, everyday moments – "the pause before someone speaks," "the thing a person notices but cannot explain," "the slow realization."
    • From "What Happens Next?" to "What Does It Feel Like?": The central question guiding these new stories became less about the sequence of events and more about the internal, subjective experience of a character.

Characteristics of the Modern Short Story (e.g., Chekhov)

Anton Chekhov is presented as a seminal figure in this new approach, whose stories redefined the possibilities of fiction.

  • Lack of Traditional Resolution: Modern stories often conclude without clear-cut resolutions. Characters might realize something profound, but their external circumstances remain complicated or unresolved.
    • Example: The story of two lovers who realize they are in love, but the "most difficult part of their lives is just beginning."
  • No Explicit Lessons or Rewards: Characters are not necessarily punished or rewarded, nor do they always learn a clear moral lesson by the story's end. The story reflects life's ambiguities rather than imposing a definitive judgment.
  • Emphasis on Interior Life and Consciousness: The fundamental conviction of modern writers is that "the interior life of a single person, rendered honestly, is enough to build a story around." This involves exploring:
    • How people think: Their thought processes, internal monologues.
    • What they notice: Their subjective perceptions of the world.
    • What they fail to understand about themselves: Their blind spots, self-deceptions, or gradual realizations.
  • "Unfinished" Quality: Modern stories often "feel unfinished" because they mirror the continuous and often unresolved nature of life itself. This is not a flaw but "the point," inviting deeper reflection rather than providing easy answers.
  • Purpose: The modern short story "cares less about what happens and more about what it feels like to be alive while it is happening."

Key Pioneers

The textbook mentions Chekhov, Joyce, and Hemingway as early and influential examples of this new form. They each approached this "shift" in their unique ways but shared the core belief in the power of rendering an individual's interior life honestly.

Review Questions

  1. How do the stories of Edgar Allan Poe serve as a contrast to the stories of Anton Chekhov in terms of plot structure and resolution?
  2. What significant change occurred in short fiction around 1890, and what new aspect of human experience did writers begin to prioritize?
  3. Explain the statement: "The modern short story... cares less about what happens and more about what it feels like to be alive while it is happening." How does this shift affect the reader's experience?
  4. Why do modern short stories often conclude without definitive resolution, and how is this considered a strength rather than a weakness?
  5. The text suggests that "the interior life of a single person, rendered honestly, is enough to build a story around." Discuss what this means for both the writer (in terms of subject matter) and the reader (in terms of engagement).
