Study Guide: How to Read These Stories

Chapter 1: What Is a Modern Short Story?

Overview

This section provides practical guidance for reading Chekhov, Joyce, and Hemingway's stories. Because these texts are deliberately incomplete, ambiguous, and resistant to easy summary, readers need specific strategies to engage with them productively. The section reframes what might feel like failures of comprehension — confusion, uncertainty, a sense of being lost — as signs of active engagement. It offers five concrete practices: treat re-reading as essential, treat confusion as information, resist the temptation to treat the ending as a solution, pay careful attention to what is not said, and keep a reading journal. The section closes with a close reading exercise using the three opening sentences, which model different narrative methods.

Key Concepts

  • Re-reading as Essential: These stories are designed for multiple readings; a single reading sees only half
  • Productive Confusion: Moments of confusion are not failures — they mark where the story is doing important work
  • The Ending as Clue, Not Answer: Modern story endings reveal what the story is about, but do not explain everything
  • Attention to Absence: Silences, deflections, unsaid things, and subject changes carry as much meaning as what is stated
  • Recurring Images as Structure: Objects and images that repeat throughout a story are intentional — they build meaning without stating it
  • The Reading Journal: A two-stage reflective tool for tracking the development of understanding across readings

Important Terms

Close Reading — The practice of reading slowly and attentively, attending to specific word choices, sentence structures, silences, and images rather than skimming for plot summary.

Recurring Image — An object, setting detail, or image that appears more than once in a story. In these three writers' work, repetitions are intentional devices for building meaning. Examples: a dog, a song, a landscape, a piece of luggage.

Subtext — The unstated layer of meaning beneath dialogue and action. What characters do not say to each other — the silences, deflections, and topic changes — often carries the story's emotional and thematic core.

Reading Journal — A reflective writing practice the section recommends: write one sentence after the first reading and one after the second, then compare them to locate where understanding has developed.

Opening Sentence — The first sentence of each story, which the section identifies as doing more work than it appears to — signaling the writer's method and the kind of story that follows.

Detailed Notes

Why These Stories Feel Difficult

The section opens by refusing the comfort of a false excuse: these stories are not difficult because they are linguistically complicated or filled with obscure references. They are difficult because they trust the reader. This reframe is significant — difficulty becomes respect rather than obstacle. The stories "leave things out. They refuse to explain themselves. They end without resolution."

Recognizing this as intentional rather than accidental is the first step to reading them well.

Re-Reading Is Not Cheating

Modern short stories are built in layers. The first reading establishes what happens on the surface. The second reading — now that you know where the story goes — allows you to see the design that was always there. Elements that seemed random reveal themselves as deliberate. Moments that seemed unimportant prove to be central.

The section's formulation is precise: "If you read one of these stories only once, you are seeing half of it." Re-reading is not a remedial activity for slow readers — it is the activity the authors planned for.

Confusion as Information

When something in a story "confuses you — a sentence that seems to come from nowhere, a conversation that does not make sense, an ending that refuses to end" — the section's advice is to mark it and stay with it. The places of confusion are "usually the places where the story is doing its most important work."

This inverts the typical student instinct to skip past difficulty and look for clear, summarizable content. In modern short fiction, the clearly summarizable is often the least important part.

The Ending as Puzzle Piece

In detective fiction, the ending is the answer. In modern short fiction, the ending is still part of the puzzle. The section instructs: "Read the ending as one more piece of the puzzle, not the solution." It reveals what the story was really about — but "the answer is almost never simple."

This means the reader should not race toward the ending hoping it will clarify everything. The work of understanding is distributed throughout the entire story.

Watching for Absence and Repetition

The section identifies four specific things to watch:

  1. First and last sentences — always doing more work than they appear to
  2. What characters do not say — silences, deflections, subject changes
  3. Recurring objects and images — not accidental; they build unstated meaning
  4. The opening sentences as method signals — Chekhov's gossip, Joyce's person in motion, Hemingway's landscape without people

The Three Opening Sentences Analyzed

Chekhov: "It was said that a new person had appeared on the sea-front: a lady with a little dog." — Begins with reported gossip. Someone heard something about someone else. The narrative stance is social, indirect, observational.

Joyce: "Lily, the caretaker's daughter, was literally run off her feet." — A specific person in a specific role, already in motion. Immediate, concrete, socially placed.

Hemingway: "The hills across the valley of the Ebro were long and white." — No people. Landscape alone. The environment will carry the emotional weight the characters cannot carry in words.

Each sentence is a miniature demonstration of the writer's entire method.
