Study Guide: Noticing Gretta

Chapter 3 — James Joyce, "The Dead": Part One

Overview

This section examines the staircase scene near the end of the party, in which Gabriel catches sight of his wife Gretta listening to a song and experiences a sudden, unexpected rush of tenderness and desire. The moment is brief but pivotal: it is the first time Gabriel responds to another person without calculation, and it changes the emotional register of everything that follows. The section also identifies a subtle but important limitation in Gabriel's response — his tendency to turn the person he is seeing into an object of his own imagination — and establishes this as a tension that will matter enormously in Part Two.

Key Concepts

  • The first uncalculated response: After an evening of social performance and managed interactions, Gabriel's response to Gretta is the first moment of genuine, unexpected feeling
  • Seeing vs. composing: Gabriel does not go to Gretta or speak — he watches her from below and composes a mental image, turning her into "a symbol of something" rather than engaging with her as a person
  • Deliberate placement: Joyce positions this moment carefully to change the story's emotional register and set up Part Two
  • Two simultaneous truths: Gabriel's capacity for genuine feeling and his tendency to objectify others are both real and will both matter in Part Two
  • The image held at section's end: A man at the bottom of a staircase, looking up at his wife, feeling more than he understands

Important Terms

Gretta Conroy — Gabriel's wife. The staircase scene is our introduction to Gretta as a figure of significance in the story. She is still, unaware of being watched, and absorbed in distant music — qualities that will take on additional meaning in Part Two.

The staircase scene — The moment when Gabriel sees Gretta on the stairs listening to music, late in the party. It is brief but structurally pivotal: it shifts the story's emotional key before Part Two begins.

"A symbol of something" — The phrase Gabriel uses in his mind to describe what he sees in Gretta. The section uses this phrase to identify a limitation: composing a person as a symbol is not the same as seeing them.

Composing vs. seeing — The section's key distinction for understanding Gabriel's relationship with other people. To see a person is to engage with who they actually are; to compose a picture of them is to turn them into material for one's own imagination.

Emotional register — The section's term for the tone or mood of the narrative. The staircase scene shifts the register from social comedy and cultural critique to something more intimate and more dangerous.

Detailed Notes

The Staircase Moment

Late in the party, Gabriel looks up and sees Gretta standing on the stairs, still and absorbed, listening to a song being sung elsewhere in the house. She is not aware of being watched. Gabriel feels a sudden rush of tenderness and desire — not the comfortable familiarity of long marriage but something sharper and more urgent, as if he is seeing her for the first time.

The section identifies this as the first time all evening that Gabriel responds to another person without calculation. After hours of managing impressions, rehearsing the speech, navigating the Lily exchange, surviving the Miss Ivors challenge, and performing at supper, he is simply feeling something he did not expect to feel.

What Gabriel Does — and Does Not Do

Joyce is precise about Gabriel's response: he does not go to Gretta. He does not speak to her. He watches her from below. He composes an image: "a woman standing on the stairs in the shadow, listening to distant music, a symbol of something."

The section draws attention to this. There is a difference between seeing a person and composing a picture of them, and Joyce knows it. Gabriel's response is genuinely feeling and simultaneously a limitation. His capacity for feeling is real. His tendency to turn other people into objects of his own imagination is also real. Both truths coexist, and both will matter enormously in Part Two.

Why Joyce Places This Moment Here

The staircase scene comes late — after the speech, the dinner, the dancing. Joyce places it here deliberately. It changes the emotional register of the story. The social comedy and cultural diagnosis that have structured the party section give way to something more intimate and more unsettling. Joyce is setting something up, though at this point the reader does not yet know what.

The image the section ends with is the image Part One ends with: a man at the bottom of a staircase, looking up at his wife, feeling more than he understands. What he is feeling, what Gretta is feeling, and what the song means to her — all of this lies ahead in Part Two.

Reading Forward

The staircase scene is the hinge of "The Dead." Everything in Part One — the party's paralysis, Gabriel's anxiety, the Miss Ivors challenge, the planted detail of Julia's frailty — prepares for what the staircase moment sets in motion. The reader who has followed the section's analysis will arrive at Part Two with two things to watch for: Gabriel's capacity for genuine feeling, and his tendency to see his wife as a symbol of his own making rather than as the person she actually is.
