Study Guide: The Two Landscapes

Chapter 5 — Ernest Hemingway, "Hills Like White Elephants" (1927)

Overview

Hemingway opens "Hills Like White Elephants" not with dialogue but with description. He describes two very different landscapes on either side of the Ebro valley. This section explains why those two landscapes matter — not as background color, but as the story's primary symbolic architecture. In a story with almost no narrator, the setting must carry the weight that narration normally bears. The two sides of the valley are the two futures the couple is deciding between, and the way each character relates to those landscapes tells us everything about where they stand.

Key Concepts

  • The two landscapes are not decoration; they are the story's central symbolic image.
  • Barren versus fertile maps onto the decision the couple faces: end the pregnancy or continue it.
  • Who looks and who does not is one of the clearest signals of character in the story. The girl looks at the landscape because she is undecided. The man looks at the table because he has already decided.
  • The phrase "white elephant" carries a specific idiomatic meaning — something costly, burdensome, and unwanted. The girl applies this language to the landscape; the man cannot see what she means.
  • The exchange about white elephants is a test of perception: the girl passes, the man fails.

Important Terms

Symbolism — The use of objects, settings, or images to represent ideas or emotional states. The two landscapes in this story are symbolic: they stand for two possible futures.

White Elephant — An idiom for something costly, burdensome, and unwanted — a gift you cannot refuse and cannot use. In context, the girl may be describing the pregnancy in these terms.

Setting as narration — When the physical environment of a story carries the meaning that a narrator would normally provide. In a story nearly without narration, Hemingway makes the setting do this work.

Perception — The ability to see and understand what is present. In the story, characters differ dramatically in what they are willing and able to perceive.

Detailed Notes

The Two Sides of the Valley

Hemingway describes the setting in two distinct halves. On one side: hills "long and white," country "brown and dry." On the other: fields of grain and trees along the Ebro. These are not accidental details. In a story that operates almost entirely through dialogue, every sentence of description carries symbolic weight.

The barren side corresponds to the future without the pregnancy — the couple returning to their previous life of travel and surface pleasures. The fertile side corresponds to the future with the pregnancy — something growing, rooted, and full.

The couple sits between these two landscapes at the station bar. Their position is the story's argument in spatial form: they are between two futures, and they have not yet chosen.

Who Looks and What It Means

The section makes a precise observation about attention. The girl looks at both sides of the valley. The man looks at the table, his drinks, the girl. She is still deciding; her gaze moves outward because she has not foreclosed possibility. He has already decided; there is nothing in the landscape to interest him because his mind is made up.

This difference in attention is one of Hemingway's quietest character revelations. He never says "the man had already decided." He shows you by recording where each character's eyes go.

The White Elephant Exchange

The girl looks at the hills and says they look like white elephants. The man says he has never seen one. She replies: "No, you wouldn't have."

This exchange is brief, but the section calls it one of the sharpest lines in the story. On the surface, they are discussing the appearance of the hills. Beneath the surface:

  • "White elephant" means something costly, burdensome, and unwanted. If the hills look like white elephants to her, she may be saying the pregnancy looks that way too — or that it looks that way to him.
  • "I've never seen one" means more than literal ignorance of white elephants. It means he cannot see the hills as she sees them — cannot see the weight and strangeness she is perceiving.
  • "No, you wouldn't have" is the girl's clearest statement of the difference between them. He is blind to things that carry cost and beauty simultaneously. She sees them.

Landscape as Test

The section frames the landscape exchange as a test: can the man see what she sees? He cannot. The hills are just hills to him. To her, they are beautiful, strange, and heavy with meaning. The test is not about knowledge — it is about willingness and capacity to perceive what is genuinely difficult to see.
