Study Guide: What Isn't Said in "Hills Like White Elephants"

Overview

This section explores Ernest Hemingway's masterful use of omission and subtext in "Hills Like White Elephants," where the unsaid conversation about an abortion drives the entire narrative. It delves into how Hemingway reveals character, conflict, and meaning through seemingly simple dialogue, symbolic landscapes, and a profound power imbalance, all while adhering to his "iceberg principle."

Key Concepts

  • The Power of Omission: Hemingway deliberately leaves out explicit details, including the central subject of the conversation (abortion), character names, backstory, and resolution. This forces the reader to actively interpret meaning.
  • Subtext as Dialogue: In "Hills Like White Elephants," subtext isn't merely an undercurrent; it is the dialogue. Characters convey their true feelings, desires, and conflicts through indirect statements, silence, and what they avoid saying.
  • Symbolic Landscape: The setting, particularly the contrasting "barren and dry" versus "fertile" sides of the Ebro valley, serves as a powerful symbol for the two potential futures the couple faces, doing the work narration would typically do.
  • "White Elephant" Symbolism: The girl's observation about the hills looking like "white elephants" carries a specific cultural meaning (something costly, burdensome, and unwanted), subtly revealing her perspective on the pregnancy and their relationship.
  • Dialogue and Power Imbalance: The rhythm and content of the characters' speech expose a clear power dynamic. The man dominates the conversation with repetitive, persuasive arguments, while the girl's resistance is shown through deflection, questions, and desperate pleas.
  • The Iceberg Principle: Hemingway's literary theory that the true power of a story lies in what is left unsaid. Only a small part of the story (the dialogue and brief descriptions) is visible, while the vast, deeper meaning remains beneath the surface, felt rather than stated.

Important Terms

  • Subtext: The underlying message or meaning of a piece of dialogue or text, which is not explicitly stated but implied by the characters' words, actions, or the context.
  • White Elephant: An idiom referring to a possession that is troublesome to maintain or costly to keep but produces no benefit, often acquired as a "gift" one cannot refuse. In the story, it symbolizes the unwanted and burdensome nature of the pregnancy.
  • Iceberg Principle: Ernest Hemingway's theory of writing, which suggests that only a small portion of the story's meaning or content should be explicitly presented, while the larger, deeper truth is implied and left for the reader to infer.

Detailed Notes

The Conversation Nobody Is Having

  • Unspoken Subject: The story revolves around a couple discussing whether the girl should have an abortion, though the word "abortion" is never used.
  • Hemingway's Intent: This omission is not accidental; it's a deliberate choice by the author to reflect how people genuinely behave in high-stakes, public situations when honesty is too difficult.
  • Indirect Language: The man refers to the procedure as "an awfully simple operation" or "not really an operation at all," minimizing its significance. The girl remains largely silent on the specific topic.
  • Reader's Role: Unlike most fiction where a narrator explains what's important, Hemingway trusts the reader to infer meaning by listening to the rhythm of speech, silences, repetitions, and omissions, much like in a real-life conversation.

Subtext

  • Meaning Beyond Words: Subtext is what characters mean but don't say. In "Hills Like White Elephants," subtext isn't hidden; it is the core of the dialogue.
  • Transparency of Surface: Hemingway's skill makes the surface dialogue so transparent that the deeper, unstated meanings (what characters are arguing about, what they want, how they feel) become clear to the reader by the story's end.

The Two Landscapes

  • Setting as Symbolism: Hemingway begins with a detailed description of the Ebro valley, featuring two distinct landscapes:
    • Barren Side: "Long and white" hills, "brown and dry" country – symbolic of a future without the pregnancy, perhaps an empty or sterile existence for the relationship.
    • Fertile Side: "Fields of grain and trees along the banks of the Ebro" – symbolic of a future with the pregnancy, potentially representing life and growth.
  • Characters' Reactions: The couple sits between these two landscapes. The girl looks at both, reflecting her uncertainty. The man looks only at the table or his drink, indicating he has already decided what he wants.
  • Test of Perception: The girl's comment that the hills "look like white elephants" and the man's inability to see it highlights a fundamental difference in their perception and willingness to acknowledge difficult truths.

"White Elephants"

  • Double Meaning: The phrase "white elephant" literally describes the appearance of the hills, but it also carries the idiomatic meaning of something costly, burdensome, and unwanted—a gift you cannot refuse.
  • Girl's Revelation: By using this phrase, the girl subtly reveals her feelings about the pregnancy, suggesting she views it as a "white elephant" for their transient lifestyle.
  • Man's Blindness: The man's reply, "I've never seen one," suggests his refusal to acknowledge the deeper implications of their situation or the burden the pregnancy represents to her.

"He Said, She Said" — Who Has the Power?

  • Dialogue Patterns: An analysis of the dialogue reveals a significant power imbalance:
    • The Man: Talks more, initiates topics, repeatedly steers the conversation back to the operation, uses longer, more assured, and repetitive sentences (e.g., "awfully simple operation," "I know you wouldn't mind it"). His calm is born of certainty, not openness.
    • The Girl: Deflects, changes the subject, makes observations about the scenery, orders drinks, and uses short, questioning sentences when she does address the operation. Her power lies in indirection and resistance.
  • "Sales Pitch" Dynamic: The man's persistent assurances have the quality of a sales pitch, suggesting he is trying to manage her decision rather than truly listen.
  • Desperate Resistance: The girl's repetition of "please please please please please please please stop talking" is a poignant moment, showing her at the end of her emotional tether, using emphasis as her last tool of protest.

"That's All We Do"

  • Emotional Center: A pivotal moment occurs when the girl expresses her true feelings about their entire relationship, not just the operation. She says, "And we could have all this And we could have everything and every day we make it more impossible."
  • Surface-Level Existence: Her follow-up, "That's all we do, isn't it — look at things and try new drinks," describes a life of superficiality, travel, and pleasant scenery without any real depth or substance.
  • Core Relationship Problem: The operation is presented not as an isolated issue but as the latest symptom of a deeper problem: their relationship lacks substance.
  • Man's Dismissal: The man fails to engage with her profound insight, responding with a dismissive and likely untrue "I don't care about me." This illustrates their communication breakdown.
  • Unsaid Truth: While the man doesn't acknowledge it, the girl's honest assessment changes the conversation. A truth has been spoken, even if it remains unaddressed.

The Iceberg in Practice

  • Omission as Strength: "Hills Like White Elephants" is a prime example of Hemingway's iceberg principle, demonstrating the power of what is not said.
  • Absence of Details: The story lacks:
    • Backstory: No information about their past, duration of relationship, or destinations.
    • Names: The characters are simply "the American" and "the girl" (Jig).
    • Interior Monologue: Readers never access the characters' thoughts directly.
    • Resolution: The story ends ambiguously, with the reader unsure of the girl's decision.
  • Reader's Interpretation: Hemingway trusts the reader to infer character motivations, emotional states, and the relationship's dynamic through observation of dialogue and actions, much like overhearing a real conversation.
  • Respect for Characters and Reader: This restraint is not coldness but respect—allowing characters to be complex without explanation and empowering the reader to engage in interpretation. The "feeling" of deeper meaning beneath the words is the unseen part of the iceberg.

Comparing the Three Styles (Hemingway's Context)

  • Contrast in Revelation: While other authors like Chekhov and Joyce might grant readers direct access to characters' interior lives (thoughts, memories, consciousness), Hemingway intentionally withholds this.
  • Hemingway's Method: His method relies on revealing meaning through external actions, dialogue, and symbolism, trusting the reader to feel the unstated truths.

Review Questions

  1. How does Hemingway use the unspoken word "abortion" to reveal the dynamics of the couple's relationship and the inherent conflict?
  2. Explain the symbolism of the two landscapes (barren vs. fertile) in the Ebro valley. How do the characters' interactions with these landscapes reflect their perspectives on their situation?
  3. Discuss the significance of the girl's comment, "They look like white elephants." What does this phrase reveal about her understanding of the pregnancy and her relationship with the man?
  4. Analyze the power dynamics present in the dialogue between the man and the girl. Provide specific examples from their speech patterns and content to support your analysis.
  5. How does "Hills Like White Elephants" exemplify Hemingway's "iceberg principle"? What important elements does he omit, and how do these omissions contribute to the story's overall impact and meaning?
