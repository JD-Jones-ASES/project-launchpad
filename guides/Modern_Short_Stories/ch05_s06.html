Study Guide: Comparing the Three Styles

Chapter 5 — Ernest Hemingway, "Hills Like White Elephants" (1927)

Overview

This final section of Chapter 5 steps back from Hemingway alone and places "Hills Like White Elephants" in the context of the chapter's larger project: examining three writers who each solve the same fundamental problem in different ways. Chekhov, Joyce, and Hemingway all write about the distance between what people feel and what they say. But each writer develops a different technique for representing that gap — a different kind of trust between writer and reader. Comparing the three reveals something that no single writer, studied in isolation, could show: that there is more than one way to write honestly about human interiority, and that each way changes what fiction can do.

Key Concepts

  • All three writers address the same problem: representing people who cannot or will not speak honestly about their inner lives.
  • Chekhov: window into the mind — narration moves in and out of consciousness, producing intimacy.
  • Joyce: immersion in consciousness — accumulation of detail in a wide, anxious awareness.
  • Hemingway: closed door — no interiority; the reader must supply what the writer omits.
  • None of the three approaches is superior to the others; each is a different kind of trust.
  • Each technique, in its own way, changed what the short story could do.

Important Terms

Free indirect narration — A technique in which the narrator's voice blends with a character's consciousness, moving in and out without announcement. Associated with Chekhov in this section.

Stream of consciousness / Accumulation — Joyce's technique of layering perception, memory, anxiety, and observation into a continuous field of awareness.

Objective narration — Narration that reports only observable actions and dialogue, without access to any character's inner thoughts. Hemingway's technique in "Hills."

Comparative reading — Reading two or more texts side by side to understand how each makes different choices and what those choices produce.

Detailed Notes

The Shared Problem

All three stories are about people who cannot say what they mean or feel. Gurov (Chekhov) cannot say what Anna means to him until the very end, and even then only to himself. Gabriel (Joyce) cannot acknowledge his inadequacy until Gretta's revelation forces him to. The girl (Hemingway) cannot name what she knows about the hollowness of her life. Each writer asks: how do you represent the inner life of a person who cannot or will not speak it?

Chekhov's Window

Chekhov's narration in "The Lady with the Dog" slides in and out of Gurov's consciousness. You know what he thinks; you follow the development of his feelings over months and years. This produces intimacy — you understand Gurov from the inside, the way you understand a person when you can think alongside them. The narrator does not explain Gurov; it inhabits him and lets you inhabit him too.

Joyce's Immersion

Joyce's narration in "The Dead" is not a focused stream but a wide, anxious field. Gabriel is aware of everything at once — the room, the guests, his speech, his wife, his own inadequacy. This is consciousness as it actually feels: sprawling, recursive, alert to social pressure and private shame simultaneously. Joyce builds this through accumulation: detail after detail until the final revelation, when Gabriel's sense of himself collapses, lands with the weight of everything that has come before it.

Hemingway's Closed Door

Hemingway removes interiority from the page entirely. You never know what the American thinks. You never know what the girl thinks. You know only what they say and what they do. Everything else — the longing, the fear, the calculation, the grief — you must supply yourself. Hemingway closes the door and trusts you to figure out what is happening on the other side.

Three Kinds of Trust

The section ends with a comparative summary that is also a defense of literary diversity. Chekhov trusts you to think alongside the character. Joyce trusts you to follow layered, accumulating consciousness. Hemingway trusts you to supply what he leaves out. None of these is better than the others. Each makes a different demand on the reader, offers a different kind of access, and opens a different part of what fiction can represent. Together, these three writers defined the terms under which the modern short story still operates.
