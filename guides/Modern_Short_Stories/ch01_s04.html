Study Guide: Three Writers

Chapter 1: What Is a Modern Short Story?

Overview

This section provides biographical portraits of Chekhov, Joyce, and Hemingway — not comprehensive life histories, but focused accounts of the experiences and circumstances that shaped each writer's methods and themes. Chekhov's poverty, medical training, and tuberculosis gave him an intimate knowledge of suffering without self-pity. Joyce's Jesuit education, complicated relationship with the Catholic Church, and exile from a Dublin he could never stop writing about produced an obsessive attentiveness to a specific place and its people. Hemingway's war wound, Paris years, and iceberg principle grew from a conviction that honest prose must not lie — and that what is left out, when the writer knows enough, carries more power than what is stated. Each biography illuminates one writer's answer to the modern short story's central problem: how do you render the texture of consciousness honestly on the page?

Key Concepts

  • Chekhov's Quiet Realism: Born from poverty, medical practice, and illness — stories about people who are stuck and cannot be rescued
  • Joyce's Productive Exile: Left Dublin at 22 but wrote only about Dublin; his distance sharpened his attentiveness
  • Hemingway's War and Prose: WWI wound shaped a worldview that demanded honest, unlying prose
  • The Iceberg in Practice: Hemingway's theory is traceable to specific biographical and intellectual influences
  • Dubliners' Troubled Publication: Nine-year delay illustrates the resistance Joyce's honest realism encountered
  • "The Dead" as Outlier: Warmer and more generous than Chekhov's other Dubliners stories; written at age 25

Important Terms

Serf — A peasant legally bound to a landowner's estate under the Russian feudal system. Chekhov's grandfather was a serf, placing his family's rise from that condition within living memory.

Tuberculosis — The chronic and often fatal respiratory disease Chekhov contracted. As a physician, he diagnosed himself but minimized the illness to others, dying at forty-four in 1904.

Dubliners — Joyce's short story collection, finished in 1905 but not published until 1914. "The Dead" is its final and most celebrated story.

Iceberg Principle — Hemingway's own term for his method of strategic omission: a writer who knows enough can leave out much, and the reader will feel what is missing. Developed in Paris in the early 1920s.

Men Without Women — The 1927 Hemingway collection that includes "Hills Like White Elephants."

Paralysis — The condition Joyce identified as defining Dublin and Irish life — a kind of cultural, spiritual, and emotional stasis. The Dubliners stories are an anatomy of this paralysis.

Detailed Notes

Anton Chekhov (1860–1904)

Chekhov came from the very bottom of Russian social life. His grandfather was a serf; his father ran a grocery that failed. The family was poor and religiously oppressive. When the shop collapsed, the family fled to Moscow — leaving sixteen-year-old Chekhov alone in Taganrog to finish his schooling. This early experience of isolation and survival without rescue is legible in his fiction.

He studied medicine in Moscow and paid for it with his pen — writing comic sketches and stories at remarkable speed. The literary establishment dismissed him as a humor writer. He became one of Russia's most widely-read authors anyway.

Through the 1880s and 1890s his work changed fundamentally: it stopped having punch lines, stopped having plots in the conventional sense. Stories became about what it felt like to be alive, confused, and unable to fix anything. "A man goes on vacation and falls in love; nothing is resolved. A woman realizes her marriage is empty; nothing changes."

All of this was shaped by knowledge he never fully acknowledged: he had tuberculosis. He diagnosed it himself — being a doctor — and then "spent years pretending it was not as serious as he knew it was." He hid bloody handkerchiefs from friends. He wrote some of the greatest plays in theatrical history. He married the actress Olga Knipper in 1901. He was dead by 1904, at forty-four.

"The Lady with the Dog" (1899) is shaped by this knowledge — "not obviously, not sentimentally, but in its refusal to pretend that love solves anything."

James Joyce (1882–1941)

Joyce was born into a Dublin family in steady social decline. His father had charm and talent for losing money; the family moved constantly to worse addresses. His Jesuit education gave him rigorous intellectual discipline and a lifelong, complicated resentment of the Catholic Church.

He left Ireland in 1904 with Nora Barnacle — a woman he had met months before — and moved to Trieste, then Zurich, then Paris. He essentially never returned to Dublin. But he never wrote about anywhere else. "Every novel, every story, every poem is set in the city he left behind." His famous claim: he wanted to write Dublin so completely that if the city were destroyed, it could be reconstructed from his books.

Dubliners was finished by 1905 and published — after a nine-year battle — in 1914. Publishers feared real place names, real pubs, an unflattering portrait. A printer destroyed proofs rather than print it. Joyce fought for every sentence and changed nothing.

The irony: the book feared to offend Dublin is now considered one of the great literary acts of love for a city.

"The Dead" is the collection's final story, and it differs from the others in being generous and warm — it has a party, music, food, and people who care about each other. It also ends with "one of the most devastating endings in English literature." Joyce was twenty-five when he wrote it.

Ernest Hemingway (1899–1961)

Hemingway grew up in Oak Park, Illinois — a suburb he later called a place of "wide lawns and narrow minds." He was athletic, competitive, and eager to see the world. At eighteen he volunteered as a Red Cross ambulance driver on the Italian front in WWI. Less than a month in, a mortar shell exploded nearby, driving over 200 pieces of shrapnel into his legs. The man beside him was killed. Hemingway spent months in a Milan hospital, fell in love with a nurse who did not love him back, and went home with a limp "and a view of the world that would never quite recover."

He went to Paris in the early 1920s — part of the post-war American expatriate generation — determined to write prose that did not lie. He studied Chekhov. He studied Flaubert. He studied bullfighting, which he believed taught something about facing the worst possible thing with precision and control. From this came the iceberg principle: if a writer knows his material thoroughly enough, he can omit things and the reader will feel them anyway.

"Hills Like White Elephants" was written in a single afternoon in a Madrid café in 1926. It is roughly 1,500 words. Two people talk at a train station about a decision that will change their lives — a word that is never spoken. "The landscape — dry hills on one side of the tracks, a green valley on the other — does the emotional work that the characters cannot do in words."

The section ends with a précis of Hemingway's achievement: "He had seen something terrible, and instead of describing it, he left a space in the prose where the terrible thing should be, and trusted the reader to feel its absence."
