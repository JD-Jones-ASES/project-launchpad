Study Guide: That's All We Do

Chapter 5 — Ernest Hemingway, "Hills Like White Elephants" (1927)

Overview

There is a moment in "Hills Like White Elephants" when the girl stops arguing about the specific question at hand — the operation — and says something larger and truer about the whole of their relationship. "That's all we do, isn't it — look at things and try new drinks." This section identifies that moment as the emotional center of the story, and explains why. In a single sentence, the girl diagnoses their entire life together as a life of surfaces — pleasant, transient, and ultimately hollow. The operation is not a separate problem; it is the latest symptom of the same disease.

Key Concepts

  • The emotional center of the story is the girl's line about "looking at things and trying new drinks" — not the argument about the operation.
  • The girl is not talking about the operation when she says this line. She is talking about their entire relationship.
  • The relationship is a life of surfaces: travel, drinks, scenery, with no depth, commitment, or honest connection underneath.
  • The man's response — "I don't care about me" — is a deflection, not an engagement with what she has named.
  • After this line, the conversation changes. Something cannot be unsaid, even though neither character acknowledges it.
  • The girl is not confused at this moment. She sees clearly.

Important Terms

Emotional center — The moment in a story where the deepest feeling surfaces and the stakes become fully visible. In this story, that moment is the girl's observation about the couple's hollow life.

Surface living — A life organized around pleasures and appearances (travel, drinks, novelty) without depth, commitment, or honest engagement with what matters.

Deflection — The man's response strategy: saying something that sounds engaged but actually avoids the real point. "I don't care about me" is a deflection from her diagnosis of their relationship.

Irrevocable statement — Something said that cannot be taken back or ignored, even if both parties pretend it was not said. The girl's line functions this way.

Detailed Notes

The Setup: "We Could Have Everything"

Just before the most famous line, the girl says: "And we could have all this... And we could have everything and every day we make it more impossible." This is the first time she frames the decision not as a specific choice but as a pattern. Every day they make the real life — the life with depth, with something at stake, with a child — more impossible. The operation would be one more step in that direction.

The Diagnosis

"That's all we do, isn't it — look at things and try new drinks."

This is not a complaint about this particular trip or this particular drink. It is a description of the entire structure of their life together. They travel. They look at scenery. They order new drinks. They move on. There is no accumulation, no roots, no honest reckoning with anything. The girl has seen this clearly and named it for the first time.

The section calls this the emotional center because it reveals what has been underneath all the talk about the operation: the question of whether their life has any substance at all.

The Man Does Not Hear

"I don't care about me," the man says. The section notes this is "almost certainly untrue and certainly beside the point." What she has said is not about him. She is talking about them — about the quality of their shared life. His response redirects to himself, which is both self-serving and, in the context of her real point, a kind of failure of comprehension. He cannot meet her where she is.

This is the pattern of the entire conversation: she sees clearly, he reassures, and neither can reach the other.

After the Line

The section makes a careful observation about what happens after the girl's line. The conversation changes. "Something has been said that cannot be unsaid, even though neither of them acknowledges it." Neither character refers back to what she has named. But the quality of the dialogue shifts. A truth has been spoken. Both characters know it is there, even if neither will look at it directly.

This is another instance of Hemingway's technique: the most important moments are the ones that are not followed up, not explained, not acknowledged — and yet present in everything that comes after them.

The Girl's Clarity

The section emphasizes that the girl is not confused at this moment. She has looked at their life honestly and reported what she saw. She is not asking a question; she is making a statement. What she does not know — what Hemingway leaves unresolved — is whether knowing clearly will change anything. Clarity and action are different things, and the story does not assume they follow each other.
