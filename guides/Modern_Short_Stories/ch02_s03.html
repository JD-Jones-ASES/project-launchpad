Study Guide: Anton Chekhov's 'The Lady with the Dog' (Chapter 2, Section 3)

Overview

This section introduces Dmitri Dmitritch Gurov, a married, cynical man on holiday in Yalta, who seeks temporary diversions through affairs. He becomes interested in Anna Sergeyevna, a seemingly naive and solitary young woman, also married. Their casual acquaintance quickly escalates into an intimate encounter, after which Gurov maintains his detached cynicism, while Anna experiences profound guilt and remorse, revealing a deep dissatisfaction with her own life.

Key Concepts

  • The Pursuit of Diversion: Gurov's primary motivation in Yalta is to escape the monotony of his life and marriage through casual affairs, which he views as "light and charming adventures" despite knowing they often lead to "intricacy."
  • Contrasting Moralities: The immediate aftermath of the affair highlights a stark difference between Gurov's casual, almost clinical detachment and Anna Sergeyevna's deep-seated guilt, self-loathing, and moral distress.
  • Appearance vs. Reality: Gurov's wife is presented as "intellectual" and dignified, but Gurov secretly finds her "unintelligent." Similarly, Anna's initial reserved demeanor hides a profound dissatisfaction with her married life and a yearning for something more.
  • Societal Constraints and Female Agency: Anna's confession reveals the limited choices and emotional suffocation experienced by women in her era, leading her to seek escape and "live" through actions she later condemns herself for.
  • The Male Gaze and Objectification: Gurov categorizes women and views them as "the lower race," even while being drawn to them. His internal monologue reveals a pattern of objectifying women based on his past experiences, which influences his initial perception of Anna.

Important Terms

  • Dmitri Dmitritch Gurov: The protagonist of the story; a middle-aged, married man from Moscow who is a banker but trained in arts and opera. He is cynical about women and marriage but frequently engages in extramarital affairs.
  • Anna Sergeyevna von Dideritz: The "lady with the dog"; a young, married woman from Petersburg, living in S—, who is on holiday alone in Yalta. She is initially presented as shy and innocent.
  • Yalta: A popular resort town on the Black Sea coast of Crimea, known for its mild climate and as a destination for the Russian upper class. It also has a reputation for illicit romantic encounters.
  • Pomeranian: A breed of small, fluffy dog. Anna's white Pomeranian is a distinctive feature that gives her the moniker "the lady with the dog" and serves as Gurov's initial pretext for approaching her.
  • Béret: A soft, round, flat-crowned hat, often made of wool. Anna's béret is part of her distinctive attire.
  • Patronymic: A component of a personal name based on the first name of one's father, grandfather, or an earlier male ancestor. "Dmitritch" (son of Dmitri) and "Sergeyevna" (daughter of Sergei) are examples.
  • Lorgnette: A pair of spectacles or opera glasses with a handle, typically held up to the eyes. Anna uses one while watching the steamer, suggesting a certain elegance or a desire to observe.
  • Groyne: A low wall or sturdy timber barrier built out into the sea from a beach to prevent coastal erosion or to protect a harbor. Gurov and Anna walk on the groyne to watch the steamer.
  • "The lower race": Gurov's dismissive and contemptuous term for women, reflecting his misogynistic views and attempts to rationalize his infidelity.
  • "Flunkey": A derogatory term for a person who performs servile duties or acts as a sycophant; a lackey. Anna uses this term to describe her husband, expressing her profound disdain and dissatisfaction with him.

Detailed Notes

I. Gurov's Introduction and Character

  • Initial Impression: Dmitri Dmitritch Gurov, a man "under forty," has been in Yalta for two weeks and is actively observing new arrivals, particularly women.
  • Family Life and Marriage: He is married with a twelve-year-old daughter and two sons at school. He married young, and now his wife seems "half as old again as he." He deeply dislikes his wife, whom he considers "unintelligent, narrow, inelegant," despite her self-proclaimed intellectualism and dignified demeanor. He is afraid of her and avoids home.
  • History of Infidelity: Gurov admits to being unfaithful to his wife "long ago" and "often." This history informs his general cynicism towards women, whom he calls "the lower race," even though he cannot live "two days together" without their company.
  • Paradoxical Nature: He feels bored and "not himself" in the company of men, but "free" and at ease with women. He possesses an "attractive and elusive" quality that draws women to him, and he is aware of this power.
  • View of Affairs: His "bitter experience" has taught him that initial "light and charming adventures" inevitably become "regular problems of extreme intricacy." Yet, he repeatedly forgets this lesson with each "fresh meeting with an interesting woman."

II. Anna Sergeyevna's Introduction and the First Meeting

  • Appearance: Anna is a "fair-haired young lady of medium height, wearing a béret," always accompanied by a "white Pomeranian dog."
  • Solitude and Mystery: She is seen walking alone, and her identity is unknown, leading everyone to call her "the lady with the dog." Gurov immediately notes her apparent solitude.
  • Gurov's Calculation: Gurov observes her at dinner and deduces her situation: "a lady, that she was married, that she was in Yalta for the first time and alone, and that she was dull there." He remembers tales of "easy conquests" in Yalta and decides to act.
  • The Approach: Gurov uses the Pomeranian as an icebreaker, beckoning it and shaking his finger. This leads to Anna's first words, blushing, and a brief, courteous exchange.
  • First Conversation: They talk about the "dulness" of Yalta, finding common ground. Gurov shares details about his life (Moscow, banker, opera training, property), and Anna shares hers (Petersburg, married two years, lives in S--, husband might come, her name: Anna Sergeyevna von Dideritz).
  • Anna's Vulnerability: Gurov observes her "diffidence, the angularity" and recalls her being "a girl at school." He perceives "something pathetic about her."

III. The Affair and its Immediate Aftermath

  • Progression: A week passes, and Gurov and Anna spend considerable time together. The atmosphere is "sultry," reflecting a simmering tension.
  • The Kiss: While watching a steamer by the groyne, Gurov impulsively puts his arm around Anna and kisses her. He immediately feels anxious about being seen, indicating his continued concern for outward appearances rather than emotional depth.
  • Gurov's Internal Monologue on Women: In Anna's hotel room, Gurov reflects on his past relationships, categorizing women:
    • Careless, good-natured: Women who loved cheerfully and were grateful.
    • Like his wife: Women who loved without genuine feeling, affectedly, hysterically, seeing love as "something more significant."
    • Beautiful, cold, rapacious: Domineering, unintelligent women whose beauty turned to hatred when he grew cold.
  • Anna's Distinct Reaction: Anna does not fit Gurov's existing categories. Her reaction is marked by "diffidence, the angularity of inexperienced youth," and a deep sense of "consternation." She views the encounter as a "fall," expressing profound guilt and self-loathing.
  • Anna's Confession: She believes Gurov will "despise" her and calls herself a "bad, low woman," admitting she has "deceived myself for a long time." She describes her husband as a "flunkey" and confesses a powerful longing "to live!" driven by "curiosity" and an uncontrollable urge for "a different sort of life." She claims she "could not be restrained."
  • Gurov's Detachment: In stark contrast to Anna's emotional turmoil, Gurov feels "bored already, listening to her." He is "irritated by the naïve tone, by this remorse, so unexpected and inopportune," almost thinking she is "jesting or playing a part." He eats watermelon calmly, highlighting his disengagement from her distress.

Review Questions

  1. Describe Gurov's character and his views on women at the beginning of the story. How do his internal thoughts (e.g., about his wife, his past affairs) reveal contradictions in his personality?
  2. What clues does Chekhov provide about Anna Sergeyevna's character and her life before Gurov approaches her? How do these initial observations foreshadow her emotional reaction to their intimacy?
  3. Compare and contrast Gurov's and Anna's reactions immediately after their intimate encounter. What does this difference reveal about their respective moral frameworks, life experiences, and expectations of such an affair?
  4. Anna describes her husband as a "flunkey" and expresses a desperate desire "to live!" What does this tell us about her motivations for coming to Yalta and engaging in the affair, beyond simply seeking a temporary dalliance?
  5. The section ends with Gurov feeling "bored" and "irritated" by Anna's remorse. What does this suggest about his emotional depth, his capacity for empathy, and his understanding of the women he pursues?
