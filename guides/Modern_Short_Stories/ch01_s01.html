Study Guide: How to Read Modern Short Stories

Overview

This section introduces a unique approach to reading modern short stories, particularly those by authors like Chekhov, Joyce, and Hemingway. It explains that these stories are designed to be challenging, not through complex language, but by trusting the reader to engage actively with ambiguity, omissions, and unresolved endings. Rather than signs of failure, confusion and re-reading are presented as essential tools for uncovering the deeper meaning embedded within these literary works.

Key Concepts

  • Trust in the Reader & Intentional Omissions: Modern short stories, unlike conventional narratives, deliberately leave out explanations, refuse explicit resolutions, and trust the reader to interpret unspoken meanings.
  • Confusion as a Starting Point: Feeling confused while reading these stories is not a failure but an indicator that you are paying attention and identifying crucial points where the story is doing significant work, prompting closer examination.
  • Re-reading as a Core Strategy: These stories are designed for multiple readings. The first reading reveals surface details, while subsequent readings illuminate missed nuances and provide a deeper understanding as the reader knows the narrative's trajectory.
  • Endings as Puzzles, Not Solutions: Unlike mystery novels, the endings of these short stories do not provide simple answers or wrap everything up. Instead, they serve as another piece of the interpretive puzzle, revealing what the story was really about in a complex way.
  • Paying Attention to Absence and Repetition: Significant meaning is often conveyed through what is not explicitly stated (silences, deflections, unsaid words) and through recurring objects, images, or phrases (motifs), which build meaning implicitly.
  • Active Engagement Through Journaling: Keeping a reading journal encourages active reflection, allowing readers to track their evolving understanding of a story between readings and uncover the "distance" of their comprehension.

Important Terms

  • Modern Short Story: A genre of short fiction characterized by intentional ambiguity, lack of explicit resolution, reliance on implication, and often designed for multiple readings.
  • Ambiguity: The quality of being open to more than one interpretation; inexactness. In these stories, it's a deliberate technique to invite deeper reader engagement.
  • Resolution (absence of): Unlike traditional narratives where conflicts are typically resolved, modern short stories often conclude without a clear or simple resolution, leaving readers to ponder implications.
  • Implication: Meaning that is suggested or hinted at rather than stated directly. Modern short stories heavily rely on implication to convey their themes and character motivations.
  • Recurring Motifs/Images: Objects, phrases, ideas, or visual elements that appear repeatedly throughout a story, often carrying symbolic weight or contributing to its underlying themes.
  • First Reading: The initial encounter with a story, where a reader primarily focuses on plot, characters, and basic comprehension.
  • Second Reading: A subsequent reading intended to catch previously missed details, appreciate narrative structure, and deepen understanding, often leading to new insights.
  • Reading Journal: A personal log or notebook used by a reader to record thoughts, questions, observations, and evolving interpretations of a text, especially between multiple readings.

Detailed Notes

The Nature of Modern Short Stories

This section challenges conventional expectations of storytelling. The three stories presented in this book (by Chekhov, Joyce, and Hemingway) are not difficult because of complex vocabulary or obscure references. Their difficulty stems from their trust in the reader. They are designed to:

  • Leave things out: Important information or context may be omitted.
  • Refuse to explain themselves: The narrative doesn't explicitly state character motivations or thematic significance.
  • End without resolution: Stories may conclude ambiguously, without a clear solution to conflicts or a definitive understanding of what happened next.

Feeling confused after a first reading is explicitly stated as a sign of paying attention, not a failure. This perspective sets the stage for a different kind of reading experience, emphasizing active participation and interpretive effort from the reader.

Practical Advice for Reading These Stories

  1. Re-reading Is Not Cheating:

    • Designed for Multiplicity: These stories are intentionally crafted to be read more than once.
    • First vs. Second Reading:
      • First Reading: You will naturally notice some things but inevitably miss others.
      • Second Reading: You will catch what was missed before, and the story will feel different because you now have an awareness of its trajectory and conclusion. This transformation is part of the story's design.
    • Authorial Intent: Authors like Chekhov, Joyce, and Hemingway specifically wrote their stories with the second reading in mind as much as the first. Reading a story only once means experiencing only half of what it offers.

  2. Confusion Is a Starting Point:

    • Identify Ambiguity: When you encounter something confusing—an out-of-place sentence, an illogical conversation, or an unresolved ending—do not skip it.
    • Mark and Reflect: Actively mark these confusing passages, perhaps by writing them down.
    • Significance of Difficulty: The areas where you feel most lost are often the places where the story is performing its most crucial work.
    • Information, Not Problem: Your confusion is valuable information; it points to where the story demands closer attention and deeper thought, rather than something to be immediately "solved."

  3. The Ending Is Not the Answer:

    • Contrast with Mystery Novels: Unlike a mystery novel where the ending reveals "what happened," the endings in these modern short stories illuminate "what the story was really about."
    • Complexity of Meaning: This "answer" about the story's deeper meaning is almost never simple or straightforward.
    • Part of the Puzzle: Do not read solely to find an explanatory ending. Instead, approach the ending as one more piece of a larger puzzle, not the definitive solution.

  4. Watch for What Is Not There:

    • First and Last Sentences: These sentences are frequently charged with more meaning and purpose than they initially appear to have. Pay close attention to their wording and implications.
    • Unspoken Communication: Observe the silences between characters, their deflections, and sudden changes of subject. What is not said can be as significant, or more significant, than what is explicitly stated. These omissions reveal underlying tensions, unexpressed emotions, or unspoken truths.
    • Recurring Objects and Images (Motifs): Be alert for objects or images that appear repeatedly, such as a specific animal (a dog), a piece of music (a song), a particular setting (a landscape), or an item (luggage). These repetitions are intentional and serve to build meaning and thematic depth without direct explanation.

  5. Keep a Reading Journal:

    • Track Evolving Understanding: This is a practical tool for active engagement.
    • First Impression: After your initial reading, write a single sentence capturing your immediate thoughts or feelings about the story.
    • Refined Understanding: After your second reading, write another single sentence, reflecting your new perspective.
    • The "Distance": Comparing these two sentences allows you to observe how your understanding has changed and deepened. The "distance" between these initial and refined reactions is where genuine comprehension truly resides.

Applying the Advice: An Introductory Exercise

The "Try This" exercise provides a concrete example of how to begin applying these reading strategies. By examining the opening sentences of the three stories—Chekhov's "It was said that a new person had appeared on the sea-front: a lady with a little dog," Joyce's "Lily, the caretaker's daughter, was literally run off her feet," and Hemingway's "The hills across the valley of the Ebro were long and white"—the section encourages readers to:

  • Analyze what the sentence tells and does not tell.
  • Consider the questions it raises.
  • Anticipate the kind of story expected from such a beginning.

This analysis highlights how different opening styles (gossip, character-centric action, landscape description) can immediately signal the author's method and the tone of the story to follow. This pre-reading exercise reinforces the idea of active engagement with every part of the text, even before the main narrative unfolds.

Review Questions

  1. According to this section, what makes these modern short stories "difficult," and why is confusion considered a positive sign for the reader?
  2. Explain the concept of "re-reading is not cheating" in the context of these stories. How does a second reading enhance understanding compared to a first?
  3. How does the function of an ending in these modern short stories differ fundamentally from the ending in a typical mystery novel? What should a reader look for in their conclusions?
  4. Beyond explicit dialogue, what specific elements or absences should a reader pay close attention to in order to uncover deeper meaning in these stories? Provide at least three examples mentioned in the text.
  5. Describe the process of using a reading journal as suggested, and explain how comparing entries from different readings helps a reader develop a "real understanding."
