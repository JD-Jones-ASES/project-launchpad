Study Guide: Reading Anton Chekhov's "The Lady with the Dog"

Overview

This section delves into Anton Chekhov's masterful storytelling in "The Lady with the Dog," particularly focusing on his subtle character development, the symbolic use of setting, and innovative narrative techniques. It explores how Chekhov challenges reader expectations, revealing a profound transformation in the protagonist, Gurov, through indirect means and culminating in a powerfully unresolved ending that reflects the complexities of real life.

Key Concepts

  • Gurov's Transformation: Initially portrayed as a cynical womanizer, Gurov undergoes a profound, yet unannounced, change as his affair with Anna Sergeyevna evolves. Chekhov masterfully shows this shift through accumulating small details rather than explicit statements.
  • Chekhov's Restraint and the "Trap": Chekhov avoids direct editorial comment on Gurov's character, allowing the reader to initially believe Gurov's self-assessment. This restraint sets a "trap," as Gurov's assumptions about himself and love are systematically proven inadequate.
  • Thematic Role of Setting: Different locations in the story (Yalta, Moscow, Anna's provincial town) are not mere backdrops but active forces that reflect and influence the characters' emotional states and the narrative's progression. Yalta represents temporary freedom and possibility, while Moscow and Anna's town symbolize enclosure and societal constraints.
  • Isolation and the "Sturgeon Exchange": A pivotal scene where Gurov tries to confide in an acquaintance highlights his profound isolation. The mundane response about sturgeon underscores the unbridgeable gap between Gurov's intense inner experience and the superficiality of his social world.
  • Free Indirect Discourse: A sophisticated narrative technique where the narrator subtly blends with the character's thoughts and voice, offering insights into Gurov's consciousness without using direct quotes or explicit internal monologue. This creates intimacy while allowing the reader to maintain a critical perspective.
  • The Unresolved Ending: The story concludes without a conventional resolution to Gurov and Anna's affair. Chekhov intentionally leaves their future uncertain, suggesting that some of life's most complex problems are not solved but rather lived with, reflecting a realistic ambiguity.

Important Terms

  • The "Inferior Race": Gurov's initial private, dismissive term for women, revealing his cynical and misogynistic worldview at the beginning of the story.
  • Chekhov's Restraint: The author's deliberate choice to present events and character traits without explicit judgment, allowing readers to form their own conclusions.
  • The "Trap": The narrative device through which Chekhov initially portrays Gurov as he sees himself, only to reveal later that Gurov's self-perception is deeply flawed and inadequate for the profound experiences he will undergo.
  • Resort Town (in fiction): A setting that functions as a temporary escape from ordinary life, often enabling characters to shed conventional rules and engage in behaviors they wouldn't otherwise. In this story, Yalta serves this purpose.
  • The Sturgeon Exchange: A key scene in the story where Gurov attempts to express his profound feelings about Anna to a dining companion, only to be met with a trivial remark about fish. It symbolizes Gurov's extreme isolation and the inability of his social world to comprehend his inner transformation.
  • Free Indirect Discourse (FID): A narrative technique where the narrator's voice merges with a character's thoughts, feelings, and vocabulary without using quotation marks or explicit tags like "he thought." It provides intimate access to a character's consciousness while maintaining a third-person perspective.
  • Iceberg Principle: (Mentioned in connection to the ending) A literary concept, often associated with Ernest Hemingway, suggesting that the true power and meaning of a story lie in what is omitted or left unsaid, with only a small portion visible on the surface. Chekhov applies this by withholding a definitive resolution.

Detailed Notes

Gurov's Initial Character and Chekhov's Subtle Hand

In the opening of "The Lady with the Dog," Chekhov introduces Dmitri Gurov as a man of cynical habits and beliefs regarding women. He is a serial philanderer who privately labels women as "the inferior race," seeing his affairs as predictable patterns of pursuit, conquest, and mild regret. Crucially, Chekhov presents this character portrait with remarkable restraint, offering no explicit judgment or moral commentary. This authorial objectivity is a deliberate technique, creating a narrative "trap." By allowing readers to initially take Gurov at his word, Chekhov prepares them for the later revelation that Gurov's own self-understanding is deeply inadequate. This subtle approach ensures that Gurov's eventual transformation feels earned and genuine, as the reader experiences the accumulation of evidence before the character himself fully comprehends his change.

The Unfolding Transformation

The core of the story lies in Gurov's unexpected emotional awakening. What begins as another routine affair with Anna Sergeyevna in Yalta defies his expectations. Instead of fading, his feelings for Anna intensify, growing "stubbornly and inconveniently" into something he has no framework to process. Chekhov avoids any dramatic declaration of change; instead, Gurov's transformation is conveyed through small, specific details. These include his persistent thoughts of Anna, the profound restlessness that grips him back in Moscow, and his impulsive, desperate trip to her provincial town. This gap between the reader's growing awareness of Gurov's change and Gurov's own slow realization is where the story's emotional power resides, allowing us to witness a man shedding his hardened cynicism for genuine feeling.

The Significance of Setting: Yalta, Moscow, and the Provincial Town

Chekhov masterfully uses setting not just as a backdrop, but as a condition that shapes character and plot. Yalta, as a resort town, functions as a temporary space where conventional rules are suspended. It enables the affair between Gurov and Anna by fostering an atmosphere of freedom, warmth, and idleness. Both characters assume the affair will have a "built-in expiration date," a common expectation in such temporary places. However, Chekhov demonstrates that feelings generated in a temporary place can become permanent.

This idea is reinforced through contrastive settings. When Gurov returns to Moscow, he finds it cold, enclosed, and filled with obligations—a life that cannot accommodate the profound emotions kindled in Yalta. The stark difference highlights Gurov's internal struggle. Similarly, Anna's provincial town, described as gray, dull, and cage-like, underscores her own confinement and the societal barriers to their love. Each setting visually and emotionally communicates the characters' states of mind and the challenges they face.

The Climax of Isolation: The Sturgeon Exchange

A seemingly minor scene in Moscow becomes a crucial turning point, epitomizing Gurov's profound isolation. While dining with an acquaintance, Gurov, unable to contain his burgeoning feelings for Anna, makes a clumsy attempt to confide in him. The acquaintance's indifferent response—a remark about the sturgeon—is devastating. This moment illustrates the unbridgeable distance between Gurov's inner life and his superficial social world. His marriage and friendships offer no space for his authentic experience. This profound lack of an audience for his true self, this desperate need for connection, is what ultimately propels Gurov to seek Anna out in her distant town. His journey is born not just of romantic passion, but a desperate yearning to be truly seen and understood by someone.

Chekhov's Narrative Art: Free Indirect Discourse

Chekhov employs a sophisticated narrative technique called free indirect discourse (FID). The story is told in the third person, but the narrator frequently and subtly slips into Gurov's thoughts and perceptions without explicit markers like "he thought" or quotation marks. For instance, describing Gurov's wife as "half as long again as he really was" is not an objective description but Gurov's resentful perception. This blurring of the narrator's voice with the character's creates a remarkable intimacy without sentimentality. Readers experience Gurov's world from the inside—his assumptions, biases, and slow realization—but are also free to observe him with a critical distance. This efficient technique allows Chekhov to convey both what Gurov observes and how he sees it, revealing the character's inner life and blind spots simultaneously.

The Power of the Unresolved Ending

The story concludes without offering a conventional resolution. Gurov and Anna are deeply in love but no closer to a solution for their complicated situation, with the narrative explicitly stating that "the most complicated and difficult part" is only just beginning. Chekhov deliberately refuses to provide a neat ending, a moral lesson, or a clear fate for the characters. This is not authorial laziness but a profound artistic choice. Chekhov understood that some of life's most significant problems are not "solved" but rather "lived with."

This unresolved ending connects to the iceberg principle, where the strength of the story comes from what is left unsaid. By withholding closure, Chekhov makes a powerful statement about the messy, ongoing nature of human experience. The frustration a reader might feel at this lack of resolution is intentional, mirroring the characters' own predicament. It leaves the reader with the honest feeling of being inside a life that is still unfolding, reflecting the true complexities and ambiguities of love and existence.

Review Questions

  1. How does Chekhov introduce Gurov's character in the beginning of the story, and what specific techniques does he use to signal a potential "trap" for the reader regarding Gurov's self-perception?
  2. Discuss the contrasting roles of Yalta, Moscow, and Anna's provincial town in the story. How do these settings reflect the characters' emotional states and the narrative's progression?
  3. Analyze the "sturgeon exchange" scene in Moscow. What does Gurov want to communicate, and what does the acquaintance's response reveal about the nature of Gurov's isolation?
  4. Explain the concept of "free indirect discourse" and provide examples from the textbook content to illustrate how Chekhov uses this technique. What effect does it have on the reader's understanding of Gurov's inner life?
  5. Why does Chekhov choose to leave the story of Gurov and Anna unresolved? Discuss the significance of this ending in relation to the themes of the story and Chekhov's overall artistic philosophy.
