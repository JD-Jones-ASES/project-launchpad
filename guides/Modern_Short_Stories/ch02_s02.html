Study Guide: Yalta as a Place Out of Time

Chapter 2 | Anton Chekhov, "The Lady with the Dog"

Overview

This section examines how Chekhov uses Yalta — and setting more broadly — as a carrier of emotional and thematic meaning. Yalta is not merely a backdrop for the affair; it is a structural element that makes the affair possible and defines its apparent terms. The resort town creates a suspended space where ordinary rules are relaxed and behavior otherwise unthinkable becomes possible. Chekhov's deeper insight, which drives the entire middle section of the story, is that the feelings generated in a temporary place can turn out to be permanent — and this is the mismatch that undoes Gurov's confident assumption that the affair will end when the vacation does.

Key Concepts

  • Setting as emotional register: Each location in the story (Yalta, Moscow, Anna's provincial town) carries specific emotional meaning through sensory detail rather than authorial explanation
  • The resort as suspended space: Yalta enables behavior that would not occur in ordinary life by lifting consequences and social rules
  • The temporary/permanent mismatch: The central irony — a setting and arrangement designed to be temporary generates feelings that prove permanent
  • Show don't tell through landscape: Chekhov uses physical description to convey psychological states without narration
  • Built-in expiration date: The logic of the resort — both parties know they will leave — makes the affair feel safe, which is exactly what makes it dangerous

Important Terms

Resort town (as literary setting) — A space in fiction where ordinary social rules are suspended, creating the conditions for behavior and feeling that would not arise in everyday life.

Temporal setting — The use of time and place together to create meaning; Yalta's meaning comes partly from being "out of time" — a gap in ordinary life rather than part of it.

Setting as character — A technique in which the physical environment carries as much meaning as the people within it; Chekhov's settings do active narrative work.

Emotional geography — The mapping of emotional states onto physical locations; in this story, warmth/cold, open/enclosed, and freedom/cage are all rendered through geography.

Expiration date — The built-in sense that the Yalta arrangement will end when the vacation does; this makes it feel emotionally safe to both parties.

Detailed Notes

Why Resort Towns Are "Dangerous" in Fiction

Chekhov's analysis of resort towns is precise: they create a condition, not just a setting. People on vacation eat differently, sleep differently, talk to strangers they would ignore at home. The rules governing their behavior in their ordinary lives are suspended — not permanently, but long enough. This suspension is what makes Yalta dangerous: it creates a space where people can behave as if consequences belong to someone else. The warmth, the sea, the promenade, the idle hours all conspire to make the affair feel natural, even inevitable. And because both Gurov and Anna know they will leave, the arrangement carries a built-in endpoint that makes it feel safe. This is Gurov's error: he mistakes the temporary frame for a container that will hold the feelings inside it.

Yalta, Moscow, Anna's Town: A Landscape of Emotions

Chekhov structures the story's settings as an emotional triptych:

  • Yalta: Warmth, openness, the sea visible from every vantage point. It is possibility, the suspension of ordinary life, the space where feeling is generated.
  • Moscow: Cold, enclosed, full of obligations and social noise. It is the life that cannot accommodate what Yalta made possible. Gurov returns here carrying Anna in his thoughts, and Moscow has no room for her.
  • Anna's provincial town: Gray, dull, described with a fence topped with nails. It is a cage — the life Anna must return to and the visual embodiment of her imprisonment.

Chekhov does not explain any of this. The details do the work. The reader feels the contrast without being told to.

The Central Irony: Permanent Feelings from a Temporary Place

The section's core insight is the mismatch between the resort's temporal logic and the permanence of the feelings it generates. Gurov has extensive experience with temporary arrangements. He knows how they end. What he cannot account for is that this one will not follow his familiar pattern: when he returns to Moscow, Anna follows him — not physically, but in the texture of his daily restlessness, his dissatisfaction, his obsessive thought. The temporary place has produced a permanent result, and he has no framework for it.
