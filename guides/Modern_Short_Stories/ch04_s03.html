CH04_TEXT: Gabriel and Gretta's Departure: A Stirring of Memory and Desire

Overview

This section describes the end of the Morkan sisters' annual party, focusing on Gabriel Conroy's changing perceptions of his wife, Gretta. As they prepare to leave, a haunting song triggers a deep emotional response in Gretta, which in turn ignites a flood of memories and intense desire in Gabriel. The journey home in a cab becomes a crucible for Gabriel's internal world, culminating in a charged atmosphere as they arrive at their hotel room, poised on the brink of a potentially transformative moment.

Key Concepts

  • Gabriel's Evolving Gaze: Gabriel initially sees Gretta as a mysterious, symbolic figure on the stairs, an object of artistic contemplation. This detached observation gradually transforms into a deeply personal, physically charged desire as he recalls their shared past.
  • The Power of Music and Memory: Bartell D'Arcy's reluctant rendition of "The Lass of Aughrim" profoundly moves Gretta, drawing her into a private, sorrowful reflection. This external event directly triggers Gabriel's own internal cascade of tender and passionate memories of their life together.
  • Public Façade vs. Private Emotion: The polite, almost mundane goodbyes and small talk about the weather and snow starkly contrast with Gabriel's burgeoning internal world of intense desire, longing, and reawakened love for Gretta.
  • Symbolism of Snow: The snow, mentioned as "general all over Ireland," creates a setting of widespread coldness and perhaps isolation, but also carries a delicate, beautiful quality for some characters. For Gabriel, it might subtly underscore the "cold" and "dull" aspects of their recent life, while also providing a pristine backdrop for his reawakened passion.
  • Anticipation and Desire: The journey from the party to the hotel room is marked by Gabriel's escalating physical and emotional longing for Gretta, fueled by vivid recollections of their intimacy and youthful passion.
  • Unspoken Tension: Much of the section's drama unfolds within Gabriel's mind. The silence and the physical proximity in the cab and on the stairs create an intense, unspoken tension between him and Gretta.

Important Terms

  • Terracotta and Salmon-Pink Panels: The distinct colors of Gretta's skirt, which Gabriel notices, revealing his keen, almost painterly observation of her, especially when they appear altered by shadow.
  • Old Irish Tonality: Refers to the traditional, sometimes melancholic, style of Irish music, suggesting the cultural resonance and emotional depth of the song Bartell D'Arcy sings.
  • "The Lass of Aughrim": The specific folk song that Bartell D'Arcy sings. Its lyrics about grief and a cold babe profoundly affect Gretta, revealing a hidden sorrow and setting in motion the deeper emotional currents of the story.
  • Hoarse as a crow: A simile used to describe Bartell D'Arcy's voice, highlighting his rough, unpleasant tone and his unwillingness to sing, contrasted with the beauty of the song's "air."
  • Dusty fanlight: A window above a door, often semicircular, which allows light into a hallway. Gretta stands beneath it, illuminated, making her a focal point for Gabriel's gaze.
  • Four Courts: A prominent judicial building in Dublin, visible across the river in the murky morning light, contributing to the story's strong sense of place and atmosphere.
  • Heliotrope envelope: A specific, intimate detail from Gabriel's memory, likely a love letter, symbolizing a cherished moment of passion and secrecy in his and Gretta's past.
  • O'Connell Bridge: A major bridge in Dublin. The superstition about seeing a "white horse" when crossing it adds a touch of local lore and conversational filler, contrasting with Gabriel's internal intensity.
  • Guttering candle: A candle whose flame is flickering and nearly going out, held by the hotel porter. This detail subtly contributes to the hushed, almost somber atmosphere in the hotel and the growing tension as Gabriel and Gretta approach their room.

Detailed Notes

Gretta on the Stairs: A Vision of Grace and Mystery

The party is concluding, and Gabriel Conroy lingers in the darkened hall, observing his wife, Gretta, standing motionless near the top of the first flight of stairs. She is in shadow, but he notes the "terracotta and salmon-pink panels of her skirt" which appear "black and white." Gretta is leaning on the banisters, listening intently to a distant, plaintive song. Gabriel is struck by her "stillness" and sees "grace and mystery in her attitude," imagining her as a "symbol of something." He mentally frames her as a painting titled "Distant Music," highlighting her "blue felt hat" and "bronze of her hair." The faint music, described as being in an "old Irish tonality" and sung by a "hoarse" and "uncertain" voice, expresses profound grief: "O, the rain falls on my heavy locks / And the dew wets my skin, / My babe lies cold."

The Song's Interruption and Bartell D'Arcy's Reluctance

Aunt Kate, Aunt Julia, and Mary Jane descend, still laughing about Freddy Malins. Gabriel silences them, drawing their attention to the song. Mary Jane recognizes the singer as Bartell D'Arcy, surprised he is singing, as he has refused all evening. She attempts to coax him into singing more, but the music abruptly stops. Gretta then descends with D'Arcy and Miss O'Callaghan. D'Arcy, when confronted by Mary Jane and the aunts about his beautiful singing, rudely claims to have a "dreadful cold" and to be "as hoarse as a crow," retreating to the pantry.

The Conversation About Snow

The other characters, taken aback by D'Arcy's rudeness, awkwardly shift the conversation to the weather. Aunt Julia and Aunt Kate remark on the prevalence of colds due to "the weather." Mary Jane adds that the snow is "general all over Ireland," the heaviest in thirty years. Aunt Julia and Miss O'Callaghan express their love for snow, connecting it to the Christmas spirit, while Aunt Kate playfully teases D'Arcy about his dislike of it. D'Arcy eventually returns, having wrapped his neck, and offers a "repentant tone," recounting the history of his cold.

Gretta's Revelation and Gabriel's Joy

Gabriel continues to watch Gretta, who remains apart from the conversation, "unaware of the talk." He notes the gaslight illuminating her "rich bronze of her hair," which he had seen her drying days before. He observes a "colour on her cheeks" and "shining" eyes. A "sudden tide of joy" surges through Gabriel when Gretta finally speaks, asking D'Arcy the name of the song. D'Arcy identifies it as "The Lass of Aughrim," admitting he couldn't remember it properly. Gretta repeats the title thoughtfully, unable to recall it herself.

Farewells and the Journey Home

After final polite exchanges and good-nights, Gabriel, Gretta, Bartell D'Arcy, and Miss O'Callaghan depart into the dark, slushy morning. The streetlights burn "redly," and the "palace of the Four Courts" stands "menacingly." Gretta walks ahead with D'Arcy, holding up her skirt from the slush, her earlier "grace of attitude" gone.

Gabriel's Flood of Memories and Desire

Despite Gretta's changed demeanor, Gabriel's internal world is ablaze with "happiness," "proud, joyful, tender, valorous" thoughts. He longs to grab her, to defend her, and to be alone with her. Vivid "moments of their secret life together burst like stars upon his memory": a "heliotrope envelope," birds twittering, a shimmering curtain, buying a ticket for her, watching a man make bottles in a "roaring furnace." The furnace image, in particular, elicits a question ("Is the fire hot, sir?") that symbolically reflects his own burning desire.

He recalls a letter he once wrote to her, lamenting the inadequacy of words to express his tenderness. He believes their "souls' tender fire" has not been quenched by the years of "dull existence," children, or household cares. He anticipates their solitude in the hotel room, longing to rekindle their shared "ecstasy."

The Cab Ride Continues

In the cab, Gabriel is grateful for the rattling noise that spares him from conversation. Gretta appears tired. As they cross O'Connell Bridge, Miss O'Callaghan mentions the superstition of seeing a white horse. Gabriel points to a snow-covered statue, identifying it as a "white man" (Daniel O'Connell) and gaily salutes it ("Good-night, Dan"). Upon arrival at the hotel, Gabriel pays the cab driver generously, exchanging "A prosperous New Year" greetings.

Arrival at the Hotel: Heightened Tension

As Gretta alights, she leans on Gabriel's arm "lightly as when she had danced." This physical contact, combined with his flood of memories, sends a "keen pang of lust" through him. He feels they have "escaped from their lives and duties" for a "new adventure." They follow a dozing old porter upstairs, their footsteps muffled by the thick carpet. Gabriel observes Gretta's "bowed head," "frail shoulders curved as with a burden," and "skirt girt tightly." His own "arms were trembling with desire to seize her," barely held in check. In the silence on the stairs, he hears the "falling of the molten wax" from the porter's "guttering candle" and the "thumping of his own heart."

Inside the hotel room, the porter places the candle on a toilet-table. Gabriel quickly dismisses the need for light, stating, "We have light enough from the street," and asks the porter to remove the candle. The surprised porter complies, and Gabriel locks the door, leaving them with a "ghostly light from the street lamp."

Review Questions

  1. How does Gabriel's perception and description of Gretta change from the beginning of the section (when he sees her on the stairs) to their arrival at the hotel room? What do these changes reveal about his internal state?
  2. What is the significance of "The Lass of Aughrim" in this section? How does Gretta's reaction to the song impact Gabriel?
  3. Discuss the contrasting atmosphere between the party guests' small talk (e.g., about snow, D'Arcy's cold) and Gabriel's internal thoughts and memories during the journey home. What does this contrast highlight?
  4. Analyze the role of memory and desire in Gabriel's experience during the cab ride. Which specific memories does he recall, and how do they contribute to his "keen pang of lust"?
  5. Examine the final moments in the hotel, from the leaning on Gabriel's arm to the dismissal of the porter and the candle. How does Joyce use sensory details, physical sensations, and symbolic actions to build a sense of heightened intimacy and tension?
