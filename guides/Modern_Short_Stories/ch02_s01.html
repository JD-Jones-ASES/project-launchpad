Study Guide: Gurov and What He Thinks of Women

Chapter 2 | Anton Chekhov, "The Lady with the Dog"

Overview

This section introduces the reader to Dmitri Gurov, the story's central figure, through the lens of his attitudes toward women. Chekhov's approach is deliberately restrained: he presents Gurov's cynical worldview without editorial commentary, allowing readers to accept it before the story systematically dismantles it. The key to understanding this section lies in recognizing it as the setup for a transformation — and understanding why Chekhov withholds judgment at the opening in order to make the story's eventual revelation more powerful.

Key Concepts

  • Chekhov's authorial restraint: Presenting a character's flawed worldview without commenting on it, trusting the reader to draw conclusions
  • The narrative trap: Drawing readers to accept a character's self-portrait so that its collapse has maximum dramatic impact
  • Self-deception as characterization: Gurov's cynicism is not simply a flaw but a blind spot that structures his entire self-understanding
  • Transformation through small detail: Chekhov signals character change through accumulating specifics, never through announcement
  • The gap between character knowledge and reader knowledge: One of Chekhov's primary tools for generating emotional power

Important Terms

Cynicism — An attitude of distrust and contempt toward human motives or sincerity. Gurov's cynicism about women is the defining feature of his initial self-presentation.

Self-deception — The act of denying or rationalizing something true about oneself. Gurov's belief that he is detached and in control of his emotional life is the central self-deception the story exposes.

Narrative restraint — A technique in which the author withholds judgment, explanation, or direct commentary, letting facts and details speak for themselves.

Transformation arc — The structural pattern of a character changing in fundamental ways across the course of a story. Gurov's arc is from cynical detachment to genuine, disorienting love.

"The inferior race" — Gurov's private, contemptuous phrase for women; a key signal of his initial attitudes and self-contradiction.

Detailed Notes

Chekhov's Ruthless Opening

Chekhov's opening strategy is described as "quietly ruthless": he lets Gurov expose himself through his own attitudes. Gurov has had many affairs, considers women tiresome yet irresistible, and privately calls them "the inferior race" — yet spends most of his energy pursuing them. Chekhov records this without comment. He does not editorialize; he does not tell the reader that Gurov is wrong or shallow. He simply presents the facts. This restraint is not neutrality. It is strategic: the trap only works if readers believe Gurov at his word.

The Trap and Why It Works

The trap Chekhov sets is structural. By presenting Gurov's worldview convincingly and without critique, he creates a character whose entire framework for experience will be invalidated by the story. Gurov approaches Anna Sergeyevna as he has approached women before: pursuit, conquest, mild regret, return to routine. He knows the pattern. He has no reason to think this will be different. What neither he nor the reader yet knows is that this affair will refuse to end when Yalta ends — will grow, instead, into something he has no vocabulary for.

The Evidence of Transformation

Chekhov never announces Gurov's transformation. There is no scene where Gurov declares himself changed. Instead, evidence accumulates in specific, concrete details:

  • The persistence of his thoughts about Anna once he returns to Moscow
  • The restlessness that unsettles his daily life in ways he cannot explain
  • The "absurd and desperate" trip to Anna's provincial town

By the time Gurov begins to understand what has happened to him, the reader has understood it for several pages. This lag — between character recognition and reader recognition — is central to the story's effect.

The Armor That Was a Costume

The cynicism Gurov wears throughout the story's opening is ultimately revealed as costume rather than armor. The word choice matters: armor implies genuine protection; a costume implies surface performance. Beneath Gurov's contempt for women and his practiced emotional detachment is a man capable of the very depth of feeling he has spent his adult life evading. Chekhov's achievement is to make this revelation feel both surprising and completely inevitable.
