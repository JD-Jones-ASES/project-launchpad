Study Guide: Analyzing Ernest Hemingway's "Hills Like White Elephants" - Questions and Discussion

Overview

This section serves as a deep dive into Ernest Hemingway's short story, "Hills Like White Elephants," guiding readers to analyze its complex themes, character dynamics, and distinctive literary style. Through a series of thought-provoking questions and creative writing prompts, it encourages a close reading of the text, emphasizing the importance of subtext, implied meaning, and the relationship between spoken words and unspoken emotions. The aim is to foster a comprehensive understanding of Hemingway's craft and the subtle power of his dialogue-driven narrative.

Key Concepts

  • Subtext and Implied Meaning: Hemingway's "iceberg theory" of writing is central, where much of the story's meaning lies beneath the surface of the dialogue. Readers must infer characters' true feelings, intentions, and the underlying conflict from what is not explicitly said.
  • Character Motivation and Conflict: The core tension between the American man and the girl, Jig, stems from a disagreement about a significant decision (implied to be an abortion). Understanding each character's desires, fears, and methods of persuasion is crucial.
  • Symbolism: Objects or phrases in the story, like the "white elephants," carry deeper meanings, reflecting characters' perspectives, their relationship, and the story's themes.
  • Hemingway's Literary Style: The story exemplifies Hemingway's minimalist approach, characterized by sparse narration, heavy reliance on dialogue, and an objective tone that requires the reader to actively interpret emotions and events.
  • Honesty in Dialogue vs. Explicit Meaning: The section explores the difference between characters saying exactly what they mean and the often more realistic way people communicate, where true feelings are hidden or hinted at, making dialogue both honest in its realism and deceptive in its directness.
  • Narrative Endings and Resolution: By comparing the ending of "Hills Like White Elephants" to other stories, the study guide prompts consideration of what constitutes a hopeful or resolved ending, and the impact of ambiguity.

Important Terms

  • Subtext: The underlying and often unspoken meaning, themes, or intentions conveyed in a conversation or piece of writing. In Hemingway, it's paramount for understanding characters.
  • Dialogue: The conversation between two or more characters in a story. Hemingway heavily relies on dialogue to reveal character and advance plot.
  • Narration: The act of telling a story; the perspective from which a story is told. Hemingway often uses minimal, objective narration.
  • Interior Monologue: A literary device that presents a character's thoughts and feelings directly, as if overheard in the mind. Hemingway typically avoids this to emphasize external action and dialogue.
  • Symbolism: The use of objects, people, or ideas to represent something else. For example, "white elephants" serve as a key symbol in the story.
  • Irony: A literary device where there is a contrast between what is said and what is actually meant, or between what appears to be and what actually is. The girl's final line, "I feel fine," can be seen as ironic.
  • Literary Style: The particular way an author writes, encompassing their word choice, sentence structure, use of imagery, and overall tone. Hemingway's style is famously minimalist and direct.
  • Ambiguity: The quality of being open to more than one interpretation; inexactness. Many aspects of "Hills Like White Elephants," especially its ending, are intentionally ambiguous.

Detailed Notes

Understanding the Central Conflict and Characters

The initial questions immediately plunge into the story's core:

  • The Couple's Argument: The story revolves around the couple's discussion (and argument) regarding an "operation," heavily implied to be an abortion. The man desires the girl to have it, presenting it as a simple, easy solution that will allow them to return to their carefree lifestyle. The girl, while not directly refusing, expresses deep reservations, fear, and a sense of loss associated with the procedure, hinting at a desire for a different, more stable life or to keep the pregnancy. Look for lines where the man repeatedly assures her it's "perfectly simple" and "not really an operation at all," contrasted with her questions about its irreversible nature and impact on their relationship.
  • Character Change: This question encourages analyzing character development, or lack thereof. The man largely remains static, consistently pushing his agenda. The girl, however, shows a subtle but significant shift. Initially passive, she becomes more assertive, eventually shutting down the conversation. Her final line, "I feel fine. There's nothing wrong with me," is crucial. It likely does not mean what it appears to mean; instead, it could be an act of resignation, emotional exhaustion, or a desperate attempt to reclaim some agency and internal peace, even if external circumstances haven't changed. It suggests a profound internal shift, possibly a decision made in silence.

Analyzing Dialogue and Relationship Dynamics

  • The Man's Sincerity: The man's statement, "I don't want you to do it if you don't want to," is explored for its truthfulness. By contrasting his words with his subsequent persuasive actions, readers can discern his true intent. He continually pressures her, reiterates his desire for her to have it, and dismisses her concerns, revealing his statement to be disingenuous. This highlights a manipulative dynamic where his "choice" is heavily conditional.
  • The "White Elephants" Symbolism: This exchange is highly symbolic. "White elephants" are often seen as something unwanted or a burdensome gift. The girl's perception of the hills as white elephants suggests her view of the pregnancy—or perhaps their entire relationship—as a beautiful yet burdensome thing. The man's inability or refusal to see it highlights a profound disconnect in their perspectives. He sees only a landscape, while she sees a metaphor for their predicament, revealing a fundamental distance in how they experience and interpret their shared world.

Broader Literary Comparisons and Craft

  • Honesty in Dialogue: This question asks readers to consider Hemingway's particular realism. His dialogue is often praised for capturing the way people actually talk, full of pauses, evasions, and unspoken implications. Characters rarely state their true feelings directly. The "honesty" lies not in explicit truth-telling but in the realistic portrayal of how human beings, especially in conflict, communicate indirectly. This contrasts with more explicit forms of dialogue and challenges readers to think about the relationship between what is said and what is truly meant.
  • Comparing Endings: The study guide prompts a comparison of different narrative resolutions. "Hills Like White Elephants" ends with ambiguity and a sense of unresolved tension, despite the girl's declaration. Chekhov's and Gabriel's (from James Joyce's "The Dead") endings, though not provided, likely offer different degrees of hope or resolution.
    • "The most complicated and difficult part" (Chekhov): Suggests an ongoing struggle, perhaps little hope for easy resolution, but an acknowledgment of complexity.
    • "Snow falls" (Gabriel/Joyce): Often implies a sense of coldness, isolation, or a quiet, somber reflection on mortality and the past, typically not hopeful in a traditional sense.
    • "I feel fine" (Jig/Hemingway): Highly ambiguous. It could be seen as resignation, a suppressed scream, or even a nascent act of defiance.
    • Hope vs. Resolution: A hopeful ending implies a positive outlook for the future, while a resolved ending means the main conflict is settled, which might not always be positive. Hemingway's ending is certainly not resolved, and its hopefulness is deeply debatable.

Creative Writing Prompts: Exploring Style and Subtext

These prompts are designed to deepen understanding of Hemingway's style and the power of narrative choices.

  • "The Next Stop" (Continuing the Story):
    • Purpose: To practice writing in Hemingway's minimalist style, focusing on dialogue to convey character emotion and plot without explicit narration or interior monologue. This forces the writer to rely on subtext, gestures (implied), and subtle shifts in conversation.
    • Focus: What is decided (or not decided)? Has the argument truly ended, or merely paused? What does their continued conversation (or lack thereof) imply about their future?
  • "The Conversation They Are Not Having" (Explicit Dialogue):
    • Purpose: To highlight the contrast between implied meaning and direct communication. By having characters say exactly what they mean, the prompt illuminates what Hemingway's style omits and why.
    • Focus: How does raw honesty change the tone, tension, and impact? Is it truly "more honest," or simply "more explicit"? This distinction is critical for understanding the artistry of indirect communication.
  • "Pure Narration" (Rewriting a Scene):
    • Purpose: To understand the impact of narrative voice and interiority. By stripping away dialogue and focusing on internal thoughts and detailed descriptions, the reader can see what is gained (direct access to thoughts, rich sensory details) and what is lost (the raw, immediate drama of conversation, the challenge of interpretation).
    • Focus: Comparing versions helps in understanding how style shapes reality in a story. Which version "feels more real" is subjective but sheds light on the reader's preference for different literary approaches.

Review Questions

  1. Describe the central conflict between the man and the girl in "Hills Like White Elephants." What specific desires or fears does each character reveal through their words and actions?
  2. Analyze the girl's statement, "I feel fine. There's nothing wrong with me," at the end of the story. How does this line contribute to the story's ambiguity, and what different interpretations could it hold regarding her emotional state or decision?
  3. How does the symbolic meaning of "white elephants" illuminate the characters' differing perspectives on their situation and their relationship? Provide examples from their conversation to support your answer.
  4. Discuss Hemingway's "iceberg theory" as it applies to the dialogue in "Hills Like White Elephants." How does the story's reliance on subtext and implied meaning create a sense of realism or tension that might be absent in a more explicit narrative?
  5. Consider the concept of "honesty in dialogue." Do you think the characters in Hemingway's story are being honest with each other, even when they are not saying exactly what they mean? Explain your reasoning, focusing on the difference between explicit truth and authentic human interaction.
