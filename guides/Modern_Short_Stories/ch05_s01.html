Study Guide: The Conversation Nobody Is Having

Chapter 5 — Ernest Hemingway, "Hills Like White Elephants" (1927)

Overview

"Hills Like White Elephants" is built around an absence. The couple at the center of the story is negotiating whether the girl should have an abortion — but neither character ever uses that word. The story's power comes entirely from what is not said. This section introduces the concept of subtext and establishes the fundamental question that will drive the rest of the chapter: how does a writer make meaning without stating it?

Hemingway's choice to omit the central word is not a literary trick. It is a portrait of real human behavior: the way people talk around enormous things in public places, using the language of small things because honesty is too costly, too exposed. The railway station bar setting — public, impermanent, between one place and another — creates the conditions for exactly this kind of evasive speech.

Key Concepts

  • Subtext is the engine of this story. What the characters mean and what they say are two different things, and Hemingway requires the reader to track the gap between them.
  • The story has almost no narrator. There are two people talking and a few descriptive sentences — and that is all. Meaning is carried by dialogue alone.
  • The omission of the word "abortion" is intentional and purposeful, not a censorship choice. Hemingway was writing in 1927 when the subject was not unmentionable; he chose silence because silence is what the characters would choose.
  • Listening is active work in this story. The reader must attend to rhythm, repetition, silence, and deflection — the same skills required to understand real conversations.
  • Hemingway's achievement is making the surface transparent: the depths show through without anyone having to name or point to them.

Important Terms

Subtext — What characters mean but do not say. In most fiction, subtext underlies dialogue; in this story, subtext IS the dialogue.

Narration — The narrator's voice that normally guides a reader through the story's meaning and emotional tone. This story nearly eliminates the narrator, forcing meaning onto the dialogue itself.

Omission — The deliberate exclusion of information a writer knows. In this story, the most important word — "abortion" — is omitted. Its absence is felt as a presence.

Implication — Meaning conveyed indirectly through what characters say and do, rather than through explicit statement. The story's entire argument is carried by implication.

Detailed Notes

The Central Omission

The word "abortion" never appears in "Hills Like White Elephants." The man refers to the operation as "awfully simple" and "not really an operation at all." These are euphemisms — language that replaces a difficult word with softer, easier language. The girl offers no equivalent euphemism; she mostly listens, drinks, and looks away.

Understanding why Hemingway withholds the word is essential. It is not because abortion was unwritable in 1927 — it was not. It is because two people sitting in a railway station bar with the train coming would not say it. The stakes are high enough that directness would crack the surface entirely. So they speak around it. Hemingway records what people actually do.

Subtext as Structure

In most fiction, there are two levels: what is said (the text) and what is meant (the subtext). The narration or context usually helps you connect them. In "Hills Like White Elephants," there is almost no narration, so the reader must connect the levels without a guide. The surface conversation is about drinks, the weather, the hills. The real conversation — never stated — is about whether a woman will end a pregnancy at the insistence of a man who has framed her decision as a small and simple thing.

How to Listen

The section tells you to listen the way you would listen to a real conversation: not for what people say, but for what they are trying not to say. In practice, this means:

  • Track who changes the subject and when
  • Notice what gets repeated and what gets dropped
  • Attend to who asks questions and who makes statements
  • Feel the rhythm of who has the next word

These are the same skills the story requires as those needed for reading human behavior in real life. That is part of Hemingway's achievement: his fiction teaches you to read the world more carefully.

The Transparent Surface

The section ends with a description of Hemingway's achievement: making the surface so transparent that the depths show through. The couple is talking about drinks. They are also negotiating the future of a pregnancy and, underneath that, the entire character of their relationship. All of this is accessible from the dialogue alone — but only if you know how to look.
