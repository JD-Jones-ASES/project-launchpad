Chapter 6, Section 3: The Tradition They Started

Overview

This section explores how the modern short story evolved into a vibrant tradition, propelled forward by writers who built upon the foundational work of Chekhov, Joyce, and Hemingway. It highlights six influential authors who either imitated, argued with, or responded creatively to the techniques and themes established by these predecessors, demonstrating the continuous development and rich diversity of the short story form.

Key Concepts

  • The Short Story as a Living Tradition: The modern short story is not static; it is a dynamic form continuously shaped by successive generations of writers who learn from and respond to their predecessors.
  • Forms of Influence: Writers are influenced in various ways, including direct imitation, engaging in "argument" with previous styles, or creating something entirely new as a response to what came before.
  • Legacy of the Masters: Chekhov, Joyce, and Hemingway established benchmarks that subsequent writers, such as O'Connor, Carver, Munro, Gallant, Frank O'Connor, and Lahiri, used as starting points for their own distinct literary contributions.
  • Theological Edge (O'Connor): The use of spiritual or religious themes to sharpen the exploration of human flaws and self-deception, leading to moments of violent or revelatory truth.
  • Iceberg Method Extension (Carver): Expanding on Hemingway's minimalist technique by focusing on working-class characters who suppress their problems, where silence and unstated emotions carry significant weight.
  • Novelistic Scope in Short Stories (Munro): Crafting short stories that possess the emotional depth, temporal span, and thematic complexity typically found in novels, often utilizing non-linear, memory-like narratives.
  • Displacement and Cultural Identity: A recurring theme in many successor writers, exploring the experiences of individuals living far from home, navigating multiple cultures, or grappling with their national and cultural heritage.

Important Terms

  • Living Tradition: A continuous and evolving practice or art form, like the modern short story, that is passed down and reinterpreted by new practitioners.
  • Theological Edge: A narrative approach that incorporates spiritual or religious insights to deepen the psychological or moral dimensions of characters and events.
  • Iceberg Method: A minimalist writing technique (associated with Hemingway) where much of the story's meaning is implied rather than explicitly stated, similar to how only a small portion of an iceberg is visible above water.
  • Displacement: The state of being moved or forced to move from one's home or country, often leading to feelings of alienation or being caught between cultures.
  • Cultural Between-ness: The experience of living between two or more cultures, often involving conflicting expectations, languages, and identities.
  • Domestic Ceremony: The rituals, routines, and significant events within family life that often reveal underlying character dynamics and cultural values.

Detailed Notes

The modern short story form did not end with Chekhov, Joyce, and Hemingway; rather, it became a vibrant, "living tradition." Subsequent writers learned from these masters, sometimes by emulating their styles, sometimes by challenging their conventions, and often by creating new forms that served as a direct response to the established tradition. This section introduces six notable writers who carried this tradition forward:

Flannery O'Connor (1925–1964)

  • Connection to Chekhov: Shares Chekhov's acute perception of human self-deception.
  • Distinctive Style: Relocates this insight to the rural American South, injecting it with a unique "theological edge."
  • Characters: Often proud, self-righteous, and certain of their worldview until a sudden, violent, or revelatory moment shatters their illusions.
  • Tone: Stories are characterized by a blend of humor and terror.
  • Recommended Readings: "A Good Man Is Hard to Find," "Everything That Rises Must Converge."

Raymond Carver (1938–1988)

  • Connection to Hemingway: Pushed Hemingway's "iceberg method" to new extremes.
  • Themes and Setting: Focused on the lives of working-class Americans, often struggling financially and emotionally.
  • Style: Early stories are extremely stripped-down, almost skeletal, yet achieve profound emotional impact. Characters frequently drink excessively, speak minimally, and inhabit spaces where unspoken silences hold deep meaning.
  • Recommended Readings: "Cathedral," "What We Talk About When We Talk About Love."

Alice Munro (1931–2024)

  • Connection to Chekhov: Frequently called the "Chekhov of the English language" due to her interest in the cumulative effect of ordinary experiences leading to wisdom.
  • Scope and Complexity: Her stories often possess the emotional and thematic complexity typically associated with novels.
  • Setting and Narrative: Primarily set in small-town Ontario, her narratives span decades and often circle through time like memory, jumping and doubling back to reframe past events through new understanding.
  • Recommended Readings: "The Bear Came Over the Mountain," the collection Runaway.

Mavis Gallant (1922–2014)

  • Connection to Joyce: Explores themes similar to Joyce, focusing on characters whose national and cultural identities are sources of both pride and confusion.
  • Background: A Canadian writer who lived most of her life in Paris.
  • Themes: Her stories, many published in The New Yorker, frequently explore displacement, exile, and the subtle cruelties among people living far from their homelands.
  • Prose: Known for its precision, wit, and quietly devastating emotional impact.
  • Recommended Readings: The collection The Selected Stories of Mavis Gallant, "The Ice Wagon Going Down the Street."

Frank O'Connor (1903–1966)

  • Connection to Joyce: An Irish successor to Joyce, writing with a warmer tone and less irony, but maintaining comparable precision.
  • Themes and Setting: Rooted in the everyday life of 20th-century Ireland, his stories often examine the lives of priests, schoolchildren, and families navigating the tension between religious doctrine and personal feelings.
  • Critical Contribution: His essay "The Lonely Voice" is considered a foundational critical study of the short story form.
  • Recommended Readings: "Guests of the Nation," "My Oedipus Complex."

Jhumpa Lahiri (b. 1967)

  • Connections to Joyce and Chekhov: Carries forward Joyce's meticulous attention to domestic ceremony and Chekhov's sensitivity to the subtle failures of human understanding.
  • Themes: Explores displacement and "cultural between-ness"—the experience of existing between two countries, languages, and sets of cultural expectations.
  • Focus: Her stories primarily depict the lives of Indian-American families.
  • Recommended Readings: The Pulitzer Prize-winning collection Interpreter of Maladies, the novella-length title story of The Namesake.

Review Questions

  1. How did the foundational work of Chekhov, Joyce, and Hemingway influence subsequent short story writers, and what different forms did this influence take?
  2. Compare and contrast Flannery O'Connor's exploration of human self-deception with that of Chekhov. What specific elements did O'Connor add or change?
  3. Explain how Raymond Carver extended Hemingway's "iceberg method." What kind of characters and settings did Carver use to achieve this effect, and what is the significance of silence in his stories?
  4. Alice Munro is called the "Chekhov of the English language." Based on the description, what specific narrative techniques or thematic interests justify this comparison, particularly concerning her approach to time and memory?
  5. Many of the writers discussed (Gallant, Lahiri) explore themes of displacement and cultural identity. How do their individual approaches to these themes reflect or diverge from similar interests seen in writers like Joyce?
