Study Guide: The Tradition They Started

Chapter 6: Why It Matters

Overview

The final section of the book introduces six writers who carried the modern short story tradition forward after Chekhov, Joyce, and Hemingway. It frames the tradition not as a fixed set of rules to imitate but as a living conversation — carried forward by imitation, argument, and radical departure alike. Each of the six writers is presented in relation to one or more of the founding three: Flannery O'Connor and Alice Munro extend Chekhov; Raymond Carver extends Hemingway; Mavis Gallant and Frank O'Connor extend Joyce; Jhumpa Lahiri draws on both Joyce and Chekhov. The section invites readers to enter this tradition as readers themselves.

Key Concepts

  • A living tradition: Literary traditions are not frozen. They are carried forward through imitation, argument, and radical departure — all of which constitute meaningful engagement with predecessors.
  • Flannery O'Connor: Extends Chekhov's insight into self-deception, relocated to the rural American South with a sharp theological edge. Stories are funny and terrifying in equal measure.
  • Raymond Carver: Extends Hemingway's iceberg method into working-class American life. His stripped-down stories carry enormous emotional weight despite — or because of — their apparent minimalism.
  • Alice Munro: Often called the Chekhov of the English language. Her stories have the scope of novels, spanning decades in a way that mirrors how memory works. Interested in the slow accumulation of ordinary experience into wisdom.
  • Mavis Gallant: A Canadian exile in Paris, she writes about displacement, exile, and the small cruelties of people far from home. Like Joyce, she treats national and cultural identity as a source of both pride and confusion.
  • Frank O'Connor: An Irish successor to Joyce, writing with more warmth and less irony. His critical essay "The Lonely Voice" is one of the best studies of the short story form.
  • Jhumpa Lahiri: Writes about displacement and cultural between-ness in Indian-American families. Carries forward Joyce's attention to domestic ceremony and Chekhov's sensitivity to quiet failures of mutual understanding.

Important Terms

Term Definition
Living tradition A literary tradition that grows and changes through engagement — including imitation, argument, and radical departure from predecessors.
Theological edge O'Connor's addition to Chekhov's insight: the violent or revelatory moment that breaks through a character's self-righteous certainty carries a spiritual or religious dimension absent from Chekhov.
Iceberg method Hemingway's technique of omission; Carver extends this into working-class settings where silence and avoidance are not aesthetic choices but survival strategies.
Displacement The experience of living between countries, cultures, or identities — a central subject for Gallant and Lahiri.
Cultural between-ness Lahiri's term (implicitly) for the experience of inhabiting two cultural worlds simultaneously, belonging fully to neither.
"The Lonely Voice" Frank O'Connor's critical essay on the short story form, considered one of the best studies of the genre.

Detailed Notes

Flannery O'Connor (1925–1964)

O'Connor shares Chekhov's gift for seeing exactly how people deceive themselves. But she makes two key changes:

  1. Setting: She relocates self-deception to the rural American South, with its specific social and religious landscape.
  2. Theology: She sharpens the moment of revelation with a theological edge Chekhov would not have recognized. Her characters are proud, self-righteous, and certain they understand the world — until a single violent or revelatory moment proves they do not.

Her stories are funny and terrifying in roughly equal measure.

Start with: "A Good Man Is Hard to Find" or "Everything That Rises Must Converge."

Raymond Carver (1938–1988)

Carver takes Hemingway's iceberg method and pushes it further. Where Hemingway's characters are in bars and hotels and on fishing trips, Carver's are working-class Americans in cramped rooms who can barely afford — emotionally and financially — to talk about their problems, and mostly do not try. His early stories are so stripped down they sometimes feel like outlines of stories. Yet they land with a weight that more elaborate fiction rarely achieves.

Start with: "Cathedral" or "What We Talk About When We Talk About Love."

Alice Munro (1931–2024)

Munro wrote stories with the scope and emotional complexity of novels. Set mostly in small-town Ontario, they span decades within a single narrative, circling through time the way memory does. She is often called the Chekhov of the English language — like Chekhov, she is interested in the slow accumulation of ordinary experience into something that feels, by the end, like wisdom.

Start with: "The Bear Came Over the Mountain" or the collection Runaway.

Mavis Gallant (1922–2014)

Gallant was Canadian but lived most of her life in Paris. Over five decades, she published stories — many in The New Yorker — about displacement, exile, and the small cruelties of people who live far from home. Like Joyce, she treats national and cultural identity as sources of both pride and confusion. Her prose is precise, witty, and quietly devastating.

Start with: The Selected Stories of Mavis Gallant or "The Ice Wagon Going Down the Street."

Frank O'Connor (1903–1966)

An Irish successor to Joyce, Frank O'Connor writes with more warmth and less irony than his predecessor — though with no less precision. His stories are rooted in the ordinary life of twentieth-century Ireland: priests, schoolchildren, families navigating the gap between what the Church teaches and what people actually feel. His critical essay The Lonely Voice remains one of the best studies of the short story ever written.

Start with: "Guests of the Nation" or "My Oedipus Complex."

Jhumpa Lahiri (b. 1967)

Lahiri writes about displacement and cultural between-ness — living between two countries, two languages, two sets of expectations. Her stories about Indian-American families carry forward both Joyce's attention to domestic ceremony and Chekhov's sensitivity to the quiet ways people fail to understand each other. Her debut collection won the Pulitzer Prize.

Start with: Interpreter of Maladies (Pulitzer Prize winner) or the novella-length title story of The Namesake.
