A Final Question: Understanding Open Endings

Overview

This section delves into the nature of story endings, specifically focusing on the prevalence and implications of "open endings" in modern short fiction. It argues that modern stories often conclude without neat resolutions, reflecting life's complexities. The section then challenges the reader to critically evaluate whether open endings are truly more honest or simply an artistic evasion, prompting reflection on the fundamental purpose of storytelling itself.

Key Concepts

  • Open Endings: A narrative technique where a story concludes without a clear resolution, leaving the characters' fates or the meaning of events ambiguous. This often reflects the idea that life itself doesn't offer neat conclusions.
  • Writer's Deliberate Choice: The lack of resolution in the example stories (Chekhov's "The Lady with the Dog," Joyce's "The Dead," Hemingway's "Hills Like White Elephants") is not accidental but a conscious artistic decision by the author.
  • Truthfulness in Modern Fiction: The modern short story often prioritizes portraying life's messiness and ambiguity, believing that open endings are a more truthful reflection of reality than tidy conclusions.
  • Open Endings as Evasion: A counter-argument suggesting that refusing to provide a resolution might be an evasion, a way for writers to appear sophisticated rather than to genuinely grapple with difficult conclusions.
  • Courage of Closed Endings: Providing a "closed ending" (a clear resolution) also requires courage, as it means committing to a specific meaning, outcome, and interpretation of events.
  • The Central Question: The core of the section is a genuine inquiry for the reader: Is an open ending the more honest choice, or is something valuable lost when a writer doesn't follow a story to its conclusion?
  • Purpose of Stories: How one answers the central question reveals personal beliefs about what stories are fundamentally for—whether they are meant to reflect reality, provide comfort, provoke thought, or offer meaning.

Important Terms

  • Open Ending: A narrative conclusion that intentionally leaves questions unanswered, conflicts unresolved, or character fates uncertain, mirroring the ambiguities of real life.
  • Resolution: The part of a story where the main conflict is settled, and the story's outcome is made clear.
  • Tidy Conclusion (Closed Ending): An ending that provides a clear, definitive resolution to all major plot points and character arcs, often bringing a sense of completeness or closure.
  • Evasion: The act of avoiding or escaping something, in this context, avoiding the responsibility or difficulty of providing a definitive conclusion to a story.
  • Verdict: A judgment or decision, often implying a definitive statement about right or wrong, or the meaning of events.

Detailed Notes

The Ambiguity of Endings

The section begins by highlighting a common characteristic of the three stories discussed in the book: their lack of clear resolution.

  • Gurov and Anna: Their story ends as they face the "most complicated and difficult part of their life together," with no insight into their future actions or decisions.
  • Gabriel Conroy: His realization about himself, experienced alone, remains internal and unspoken as snow falls across Ireland, leaving his future changed perspective ambiguous.
  • The Girl and the Man: Their tense conversation concludes with no decision made about their future, as the train has not arrived, leaving their relationship and choices in suspension.

These aren't accidental omissions; they are deliberate choices by the authors.

The Modern Short Story's Philosophy

The text posits that the modern short story often embraces open endings based on a particular philosophy:

  • Reflecting Reality: Modern writers frequently believe that life itself rarely provides neat conclusions or clear-cut resolutions. Therefore, fiction, to be truthful, should not pretend otherwise.
  • Honesty over Comfort: An open ending is seen as a more honest portrayal of human experience, even if it denies the reader the comfort of a definitive resolution or "verdict."

The Counter-Argument: Open Endings as Evasion

The section introduces a critical perspective on open endings, challenging their inherent "honesty":

  • Artificiality: It's argued that a refusal to resolve a story can be just as artificial as a overly neat conclusion. Both are artistic constructs.
  • Flattering Sophistication: Critics might suggest that open endings sometimes cater to a writer's desire to appear sophisticated or profound, rather than genuinely serving the story or the reader. It can be a way to avoid the hard work of crafting a meaningful resolution.

The Courage of Closed Endings

The discussion then turns to the alternative: the "closed ending."

  • Commitment to Meaning: A closed ending requires a different kind of courage—the courage to commit to a specific meaning, to declare "this is what happened, and this is what it means." This involves making definitive statements about character, plot, and theme.
  • Reader's Desire for Comfort: While open endings might flatter the writer's sophistication, closed endings often satisfy the reader's desire for comfort and completion.

The Central Question and Its Implications

The core of this section is a direct question posed to the reader:

  • "Do you agree that the open ending is the more honest choice? Or do you think something is lost when a writer refuses to follow a story to its conclusion?"
  • This is presented as a "genuine" question with "no right answer," emphasizing its value as a tool for personal reflection.
  • Beliefs about Stories: The way one answers this question reveals deeper beliefs about the fundamental purpose of stories—what function they serve in our lives and what we expect from them as readers.

The Unwritten Scene: A Practical Exercise

To further engage with this question, the section provides a "writing prompt" that encourages practical application:

  • Choose a Story: Select one of the three ambiguous stories from the book.
  • Write the Next Scene: Create the scene the original author chose not to write (e.g., Gurov and Anna's conversation, Gabriel and Gretta's morning after, the train ride for the couple in Hemingway's story).
  • Reflect and Analyze:
    • Does your continuation improve the story?
    • Does it "feel right," or does something feel lost compared to the original ending?
    • What does your attempt to extend the story reveal about why the original, open ending was effective or "right" in its own way? This exercise encourages a deeper understanding of the author's choices and the impact of narrative closure (or lack thereof).

Review Questions

  1. What are the key characteristics of an "open ending" as described in this section? Provide an example from one of the three stories mentioned (Gurov and Anna, Gabriel, or the couple in Hemingway's story).
  2. According to the text, why do modern short story writers often choose to employ open endings? What belief about life and truthfulness underpins this choice?
  3. Discuss the counter-argument presented: How can an open ending be seen as a form of "evasion" rather than honesty? What might a writer gain or lose by choosing this approach?
  4. The section suggests that a "closed ending" also requires courage. What kind of courage is needed for a writer to commit to a definitive conclusion, and what does this commitment entail?
  5. Reflect on the central question posed: "Do you agree that the open ending is the more honest choice? Or do you think something is lost when a writer refuses to follow a story to its conclusion?" How does your personal answer to this question reflect your broader beliefs about the purpose and function of stories?
