Study Guide: He Said, She Said

Chapter 5 — Ernest Hemingway, "Hills Like White Elephants" (1927)

Overview

This section moves from the story's imagery to its dialogue — specifically, to the question of power. Who controls this conversation? If you count the lines, mark the topics, and trace who steers the exchange back to the subject of the operation, a clear pattern emerges: the man dominates. But he does not dominate through argument. He dominates through repetition, calm assurance, and relentless return to the same point, even after the girl asks him to stop. Understanding how that dominance works — and how the girl resists it — is essential to understanding what the story is really about.

Key Concepts

  • The power imbalance between the man and the girl is visible in sentence rhythms, not in explicit statement.
  • The man's repeated reassurances function as a sales pitch, not genuine expressions of openness. He has already decided; he is managing her toward agreement.
  • The girl's resistance is indirect: she deflects to the landscape, the drinks, the possible life they could have — anything but engaging his argument on his terms.
  • The "seven pleases" moment is the emotional breaking point of the man's behavior in the conversation.
  • The man's calm is not the calm of openness; it is the calm of someone who has already decided and is waiting for the other person to catch up.

Important Terms

Power imbalance — A situation in which one person controls the terms, topics, and direction of an interaction while the other must respond. In this story, the man holds conversational power.

Indirection — The girl's primary form of resistance: deflecting, changing the subject, making observations the man cannot follow, rather than engaging in direct argument.

Repetition as pressure — The man's technique of returning again and again to the same point. The repetition is not carelessness; it is a strategy, whether conscious or not.

Euphemism — Softer language used in place of a harder word. The man's euphemisms ("awfully simple operation") are part of his strategy to minimize what he is asking.

Detailed Notes

Dialogue Analysis

The section invites a close reading of who speaks how. The man:

  • Initiates topics
  • Makes longer, more assured statements
  • Returns to the operation even after the girl asks him to stop
  • Frames his argument as consideration: "I don't want you to do it if you don't want to"

The girl:

  • Responds more than initiates
  • Makes shorter statements and asks questions
  • Tries repeatedly to shift the conversation
  • Eventually resorts to desperate repetition ("please please please")

This is not a conversation between equals. Hemingway encodes the asymmetry in the structure of the dialogue itself.

The Sales Pitch

The section describes the man's reassurances as having "the quality of a sales pitch." This is precise. A sales pitch:

  • Presents the product in the best possible light
  • Returns to key points after objections
  • Assures the customer that the decision is theirs while making refusal feel costly
  • Is delivered calmly to project confidence and safety

The man does all of these things. He says "I don't want you to do it if you don't want to" multiple times — but he never actually lets the subject drop. He claims to leave the decision open while his every return to the topic signals that she is expected to agree.

The Seven Pleases

"Please please please please please please please stop talking."

Seven repetitions. The section calls this "not a polite request" but rather "someone at the end of what she can bear, using the only tool she has left: sheer, desperate emphasis."

This is the moment when the girl's indirect resistance runs out. She cannot deflect anymore; she cannot change the subject; she cannot make him see what she sees. All she has left is the raw force of repetition — borrowed, perhaps, from the man's own technique, but here stripped of strategy and filled instead with desperation.

The Girl's Indirect Power

The section notes that the girl's power "comes from indirection." This is worth examining carefully. Indirection is not weakness — it is the only available mode for someone who lacks the structural power to argue directly and win. By looking away, by making observations the man cannot understand, by shifting the topic, the girl resists on her own terms. She refuses to fight the battle on his ground.

Whether this resistance is effective — whether it will change the outcome — is one of the things the story refuses to tell us.
