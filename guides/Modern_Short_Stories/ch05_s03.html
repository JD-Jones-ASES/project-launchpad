Study Guide: Ernest Hemingway's "Hills Like White Elephants"

Overview

Ernest Hemingway's "Hills Like White Elephants" is a minimalist short story told almost entirely through dialogue between an American man and a girl named Jig. Set at a Spanish train station, the story subtly reveals a tense conversation about an unspoken, life-altering decision—an abortion—that deeply affects their relationship and individual futures. Hemingway's "Iceberg Theory" of writing is on full display, with the profound emotional conflict largely hidden beneath the surface of seemingly simple words.

Key Concepts

  • Hemingway's "Iceberg Theory": This literary principle suggests that much of the story's meaning and emotional depth lies beneath the surface of the explicit text. Readers must infer the characters' true feelings, motivations, and the underlying conflict from their dialogue, gestures, and the setting.
  • The "White Elephant" Metaphor: This central metaphor refers to something expensive or troublesome that one wishes to be rid of. In the context of the story, it symbolizes the unwanted pregnancy and the subsequent abortion, which is costly in terms of emotional impact and the potential loss of their current relationship dynamics.
  • Unspoken Conflict (Abortion): The core of the story revolves around the man's desire for Jig to have an abortion, which he refers to euphemistically as an "awfully simple operation" or "just to let the air in." Jig's hesitation and internal struggle reveal the gravity of the decision for her.
  • Communication Breakdown: Despite constant talking, the couple fails to genuinely communicate their deepest fears and desires. They talk around the issue, leading to misunderstandings, frustration, and an inability to connect on an emotional level.
  • Contrast of Landscapes: The setting presents a stark contrast between the dry, barren hills on one side of the valley and the fertile fields of grain and trees on the other. This symbolizes the choices facing Jig: a barren, rootless life desired by the man, or a life of potential growth and permanence.
  • Rootless Existence: The couple's heavy bags bearing labels from various hotels highlight their transient, nomadic lifestyle. This suggests a lack of commitment, stability, and the inability or unwillingness to settle down, which complicates their decision about the pregnancy.
  • Gender Roles and Persuasion: The story subtly explores the power dynamics within the relationship, with the man trying to persuade Jig to his point of view, often dismissively or manipulatively, while Jig grapples with her autonomy and the implications for her own body and happiness.

Important Terms

  • Jig: The name of the young woman in the story. It is a diminutive name, possibly suggesting her perceived lack of agency or the man's infantilization of her.
  • The American: The man in the story, whose nationality highlights his cultural background and perhaps a sense of entitlement or detachment. He remains unnamed, emphasizing his archetype rather than a specific individual.
  • Ebro: A major river in northeastern Spain. The story is set in the valley of the Ebro, which features both barren hills and fertile fields, serving as a significant symbolic backdrop.
  • Barcelona/Madrid: Key Spanish cities that are the origin and destination of the train. The couple is at a junction point, literally and figuratively, between these major destinations, symbolizing their transient state and the decision they face.
  • White Elephant: An idiom referring to a possession that is troublesome or expensive to maintain but yields no benefit, or an unwanted gift. Its use in the story is a direct metaphor for the pregnancy.
  • Anis del Toro: An anise-flavored liqueur the couple tries. Its strong licorice taste and Jig's reaction ("Everything tastes of licorice... Especially all the things you’ve waited so long for, like absinthe") symbolize her disillusionment and the bitter taste of their experiences.
  • Absinthe: A potent, highly alcoholic anise-flavored spirit with a reputation for being intoxicating and potentially mind-altering. Jig's reference to it reinforces her sense of disillusionment and things not living up to their hype.
  • Bead Curtain: A curtain made of strings of bamboo beads that hangs across the doorway of the bar. It acts as a physical and symbolic barrier, a threshold between different spaces, and an object of Jig's contemplation, representing the veil over their true feelings and the passage between decisions.

Detailed Notes

I. Context and Hemingway's Style

  • Minimalist Narration: The story begins with a preface explicitly stating its lack of narration and reliance on dialogue. This immediately cues the reader to pay close attention to what is said and, more importantly, unsaid.
  • "Iceberg Theory": Hemingway believed that the true meaning and emotional impact of a story should not be explicitly stated but should reside beneath the surface, like an iceberg with only a small portion visible above water. This requires active interpretation from the reader, drawing inferences from dialogue, actions, and setting. The "real story" is the subtext.

II. Setting the Scene

  • Location: A remote train station in the Ebro valley, Spain. It's a junction, a place of transition between Barcelona and Madrid. This physical setting mirrors the couple's emotional state—they are at a crossroads, between destinations in every sense.
  • Atmosphere: Hot, dry, shadeless except for the building. This oppressive heat and barrenness contribute to the tension and discomfort between the characters. The bead curtain provides a slight separation and a visual focus point for Jig.
  • Characters: The "American" (man) and "Jig" (the girl). Their generic names emphasize their roles rather than unique identities, making their conflict feel universal. Their relationship is already established but clearly strained.

III. The Unspoken Conflict: The "White Elephant"

  • Jig's Observation: Jig describes the hills as "like white elephants." The man's dismissive response ("I've never seen one") immediately highlights a disconnect.
  • Symbolism of "White Elephant": This idiom perfectly encapsulates the central conflict: the unwanted pregnancy. It is a "costly but useless" thing in the man's eyes, an impediment to their preferred lifestyle. For Jig, it carries complex emotional weight.
  • Initial Tensions: The seemingly innocuous conversation about drinks and scenery quickly devolves into subtle arguments, revealing underlying resentments and unaddressed issues.

IV. Dialogue and Subtext: The "Operation"

  • The Anis del Toro: The couple tries a new drink, Anis del Toro. Jig's observation that "Everything tastes of licorice. Especially all the things you’ve waited so long for, like absinthe" reveals her disillusionment with life and perhaps with the promises of their relationship. The exotic and once-anticipated experiences now taste bitter and uniform.
  • Introduction of the "Operation": The man subtly introduces the topic, using euphemisms: "It’s really an awfully simple operation, Jig," "It’s not really an operation at all," "It’s just to let the air in." These phrases clearly point to an abortion, downplaying its significance.
  • The Man's Perspective: He views the abortion as a practical solution to "the only thing that bothers us," believing it will restore their past happiness and freedom. He tries to reassure Jig, emphasizing its simplicity and commonality, and framing it as something she wouldn't mind. He subtly manipulates her by repeatedly saying he doesn't want her to do it if she doesn't want to, while making it clear it's his preferred outcome.
  • Jig's Internal Struggle: Jig's dialogue reveals her deep ambivalence and fear. She questions the aftermath ("Then what will we do afterward?", "What makes you think so?"), worried that the operation will not restore their happiness but irrevocably change their relationship. Her poignant question, "And if I do it you’ll be happy and things will be like they were and you’ll love me?" shows her fear of losing his love and their shared life.
  • Jig's Resignation/Despair: Her statement, "Then I’ll do it. Because I don’t care about me," is a powerful expression of either self-sacrifice for the sake of the relationship or profound despair, hinting at a loss of self-worth.

V. Symbolism and Contrasts

  • Dry vs. Fertile Landscapes: Jig walks away from the table to observe the two sides of the valley. One side is "brown and dry," like the hills she earlier described, symbolizing barrenness and the path the man wants them to take. The other side, "fields of grain and trees along the banks of the Ebro," represents fertility, life, and the potential for a different future. Jig's realization, "And we could have all this... And we could have everything and every day we make it more impossible," signifies her understanding of what they are losing or actively destroying. Her sorrowful conclusion, "No, we can’t. It isn’t ours any more," suggests a deep sense of loss.
  • The Bags and Labels: The heavy bags, covered with labels from various hotels, symbolize their nomadic, rootless existence. They never settle, always moving from one place to another. This lifestyle is incompatible with raising a child and reinforces the man's desire to maintain their current, unencumbered way of life.
  • The Train: The approaching train represents an imminent decision and the irreversible movement forward. It creates a sense of urgency and forces the couple towards a resolution, whether genuine or superficial.

VI. Resolution (or Lack Thereof)

  • Jig's Plea for Silence: Her repeated "Would you please please please please please please please stop talking?" marks a breaking point, indicating the futility and pain of their circular conversation.
  • Superficial Resolution: The man carries the bags, drinks an Anis alone, and upon returning, asks, "Do you feel better?" Jig's response, "I feel fine... There’s nothing wrong with me. I feel fine," is delivered with a forced bright smile. This feigned contentment reveals that the emotional conflict is far from resolved. The story ends with a superficial peace, but the underlying tension and Jig's unspoken sorrow remain palpable. The ambiguity leaves the reader to ponder the true cost of their decision and the future of their relationship.

Review Questions

  1. Hemingway explicitly states that this story has "almost no narration" and is "almost entirely dialogue." How does this stylistic choice challenge the reader, and what are the advantages and disadvantages of such a narrative approach for conveying the story's themes?
  2. Analyze the idiom "white elephant." How does Jig's initial comparison of the hills to white elephants evolve throughout the story to symbolize the core conflict, and what does it reveal about the characters' differing perspectives on the situation?
  3. Discuss the role of euphemism in the story. How does the American man use language to downplay the significance of the "operation," and how does Jig's understanding of his language differ from his intention?
  4. Examine the symbolism of the two contrasting landscapes—the dry side of the valley versus the fertile side with grain fields and the Ebro river. What do these settings represent in terms of Jig's internal conflict and the couple's potential futures?
  5. The story concludes without explicitly stating what decision Jig makes or how their relationship truly resolves. Based on the final interactions and Jig's repeated assertion that "I feel fine," what do you infer about her decision and the long-term implications for the couple's happiness and relationship?
