Chapter 9, Section 1: Reading Scatter Plots

Overview

This section introduces scatter plots as a powerful tool for visualizing the relationship between two different variables for a collection of individual items. It teaches how to interpret the meaning of each data point, identify overall patterns (positive, negative, or no trend), distinguish scatter plots from line graphs, and read specific values from the plot, while also recognizing outliers.

Key Concepts

  • Purpose of a Scatter Plot: A scatter plot is used to display two measurements for individual items to see if there's a relationship between the variables.
  • Individual Data Points: Each dot on a scatter plot represents a single item (e.g., a person, an experiment) and shows its corresponding value for two different variables—one on the x-axis and one on the y-axis.
  • No Connected Dots: Unlike line graphs, the dots on a scatter plot are not connected. Each dot is an independent observation, and the order of dots does not imply a sequence or change over time.
  • Identifying Variables and Scales: Understanding the title, axis labels, and scales is crucial for interpreting what the scatter plot is representing.
  • Directions of Patterns (Trends):
    • Positive Trend: As the x-variable increases, the y-variable generally increases (dots go up from left to right).
    • Negative Trend: As the x-variable increases, the y-variable generally decreases (dots go down from left to right).
    • No Trend: The dots appear randomly scattered, indicating no clear relationship between the variables.
  • Reading Specific Values: To read the values for a specific dot, trace vertically down to the x-axis and horizontally left to the y-axis.
  • Outliers: An outlier is a data point that stands apart from the general pattern of the other dots, suggesting an unusual observation.

Important Terms

  • Scatter Plot (or Scatter Graph/Scatter Diagram): A graph that displays data as a collection of individual dots, where each dot represents an item's values for two different variables.
  • Variable: A characteristic, number, or quantity that can be measured or counted. In a scatter plot, two variables are typically measured for each item.
  • x-axis: The horizontal axis on a graph, typically used for the independent variable or the first measurement in a pair.
  • y-axis: The vertical axis on a graph, typically used for the dependent variable or the second measurement in a pair.
  • Positive Trend (Upward Pattern): A pattern in a scatter plot where, as the x-variable increases, the y-variable also tends to increase. The dots rise from the lower-left to the upper-right.
  • Negative Trend (Downward Pattern): A pattern in a scatter plot where, as the x-variable increases, the y-variable tends to decrease. The dots fall from the upper-left to the lower-right.
  • No Trend (No Pattern): A situation in a scatter plot where the dots are randomly scattered, indicating no clear relationship or direction between the two variables.
  • Outlier: A data point in a scatter plot that lies significantly far away from the other data points, not fitting the general pattern or trend.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Scatter Plots

Scatter plots are fundamental tools in data analysis, allowing us to visually explore potential relationships between two quantitative variables. For example, we can use them to investigate if more sleep leads to higher test scores or if taller athletes jump farther. Each plot starts with a group of "items" (people, games, countries) and measures two different things about each item.

What is a Scatter Plot?

A scatter plot displays data as a collection of individual dots. Each dot is unique and represents a single item, showing two specific values for that item:

  • One value is read on the horizontal (x) axis.
  • The other value is read on the vertical (y) axis.

Key Difference from a Line Graph

It's crucial to understand the distinction between a scatter plot and a line graph:

  • Line Graph: Connects dots to show how one variable changes over time. The order of the dots matters (e.g., Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday).
  • Scatter Plot: Shows if two variables are related to each other. The dots are NEVER connected because each dot is an independent observation, and the order typically does not matter or represent a sequence in time. Connecting the dots on a scatter plot creates a misleading zigzag.

What Each Dot Means

Every single dot on a scatter plot provides two pieces of information about one specific item.

  • Example: A dot at (65, 9) on a scatter plot with "Height (inches)" on the x-axis and "Shoe Size" on the y-axis means: one person in the dataset is 65 inches tall AND wears a size 9 shoe. It doesn't identify the person or imply causation, just that these two measurements coexist for that item.

How to Read a Scatter Plot: A Step-by-Step Guide

  1. Read the Title: Understand the overall question or relationship the graph is exploring (e.g., "Height vs. Shoe Size").
  2. Examine Axis Labels and Units: Identify what each axis measures (e.g., "Height (inches)" on the x-axis, "Shoe Size" on the y-axis). Pay attention to the units of measurement.
  3. Check the Scales: Observe the increments on each axis to understand the range and spacing of the data.
  4. Look for an Overall Pattern (Trend):
    • Positive Trend (Upward Pattern): Do the dots generally rise from the lower-left to the upper-right? This suggests that as the x-variable increases, the y-variable also tends to increase.
    • Negative Trend (Downward Pattern): Do the dots generally fall from the upper-left to the lower-right? This suggests that as the x-variable increases, the y-variable tends to decrease.
    • No Trend (No Pattern): Do the dots look like a random cloud with no discernible direction? This suggests little to no relationship between the variables.
  5. Identify Any Outliers: Look for dots that lie significantly far away from the main cluster or pattern of the other data points. An outlier represents an unusual observation.
  6. Read Specific Values: To find the exact measurements for any given dot, trace vertically down from the dot to the x-axis and horizontally across from the dot to the y-axis.

Directions of Patterns Explained

  • Upward Pattern (Positive Trend): Think of a staircase going up. If students who study more tend to get higher test scores, that would likely show a positive trend.
  • Downward Pattern (Negative Trend): Think of a slide going down. If people who exercise more tend to have lower heart rates, that might show a negative trend.
  • No Pattern (No Trend): Imagine throwing confetti in the air; it lands randomly. If the month a student was born has no impact on their favorite color, there would be no trend.

Worked Example: Height vs. Shoe Size Scatter Plot

Let's analyze a scatter plot showing the height (x-axis) and shoe size (y-axis) of 20 students.

  1. Labels: X-axis is "Height in inches" (approx. 58-72 inches). Y-axis is "Shoe size" (approx. 6-12).
  2. Overall Pattern: The dots generally move from the lower-left to the upper-right. This indicates a positive trend. It suggests that shorter students typically have smaller shoe sizes, and taller students tend to have larger shoe sizes.
  3. Specific Dots:
    • A dot at (60, 7) means one student is 60 inches tall and wears a size 7 shoe.
    • A dot at (70, 11) means one student is 70 inches tall and wears a size 11 shoe.
  4. Outliers: Most points follow the trend. If there were a student who was 72 inches tall but wore a size 6 shoe, that dot (72, 6) would be an outlier, as it doesn't fit the general pattern.
  5. Conclusion: In this dataset, there is an association between height and shoe size; taller height is associated with larger shoe size. (It's important to remember that association does not imply causation – a topic for a later section).

Remember: Dots Are Not Connected!

This is a common error. A scatter plot is a collection of distinct observations. Connecting them creates lines that imply a sequence or relationship that doesn't exist in this type of graph.

Review Questions

  3. Describe a real-world scenario where you would expect to see a negative trend in a scatter plot. Which variables would you plot on the x and y axes, and why?
  4. Why is it important to check the axis labels and scales before interpreting the overall pattern of a scatter plot? What could happen if you skipped this step?
  5. What is an outlier in the context of a scatter plot? Provide an example of an outlier in the "Height vs. Shoe Size" example, and explain how it deviates from the general pattern.
