SCALES AND HALF SYMBOLS: A STUDY GUIDE

Overview

This section explains the crucial role of the "scale" in creating effective pictographs. It defines what a scale is, provides guidelines for choosing an appropriate scale that makes data clear and easy to read, and highlights the problems that arise from selecting a poor scale, particularly when dealing with fractional symbols.

Key Concepts

  • Scale in Pictographs: The number of items that each full symbol in a pictograph represents. It's defined in the pictograph's key (e.g., "1 star = 5 votes" means the scale is 5).
  • Importance of Scale Choice: Choosing the right scale is vital for a pictograph to be clear, easy to read, and accurately represent data without being cluttered or confusing.
  • Criteria for a Good Scale:
    • Even Values: The data values should ideally be multiples of the scale to avoid needing fractional symbols.
    • Manageable Number of Symbols: Avoid scales that result in an excessively high number of symbols per row, which makes the graph unreadable.
    • Round Numbers: Scales like 2, 5, 10, 25, 50, or 100 are preferred because they simplify calculations and interpretation.
  • Problems with Poor Scales: A poorly chosen scale can lead to awkward fractional symbols (e.g., three-eighths of a symbol), making the pictograph hard to draw and interpret, or create rows with too many symbols.
  • Limitations of Fractional Symbols: While a half symbol can sometimes work, anything smaller (quarter, three-eighths, etc.) is generally too difficult to draw and accurately interpret, indicating a need to adjust the scale.

Important Terms

  • Scale : In a pictograph, the number of individual items that each single, complete symbol represents. It is always specified in the pictograph's key.
  • Key : The legend in a pictograph that explains what each symbol, or part of a symbol, stands for (e.g., "1 book = 10 students"). The key defines the scale.
  • Pictograph : A type of graph that uses images or symbols to represent data. Each symbol typically represents a certain quantity as defined by the scale.
  • Half Symbol : A symbol that is drawn as half of its full size to represent half of the quantity defined by the scale. Useful for showing values that are not exact multiples of the scale but can be divided by two.

Detailed Notes

The Importance of Scale in Pictographs

When creating a pictograph, the decision of scale is paramount. The scale dictates how many items each visual symbol represents. A well-chosen scale transforms raw data into an understandable and visually appealing chart, while a poor choice can lead to confusion, inaccuracies, or an overwhelming number of symbols.

Defining Scale

The scale is explicitly stated in the key of a pictograph. For instance, if a key says "1 star = 5 votes," then the scale is 5. This means every full star symbol on the graph counts as 5 votes. Understanding this relationship is fundamental to interpreting any pictograph correctly.

Golden Rules for Choosing a Good Scale

To create an effective pictograph, follow these three "golden rules" when selecting your scale:

  1. Make the Values Work Out Evenly (or Nearly So):

    • The best scales are those where your data values are multiples of the chosen scale.
    • Example: If you have data points like 4, 8, 12, and 16, a scale of 4 is ideal because each data point will be represented by a whole number of symbols (1, 2, 3, and 4 symbols, respectively). This avoids the need for difficult-to-draw and hard-to-read fractional symbols.

  2. Avoid Too Many Symbols Per Row:

    • A pictograph should be easy to scan and comprehend quickly. If your scale is too small relative to your data, you might end up with hundreds of symbols in a single row, making the graph cluttered and unreadable.
    • Example: If your highest data value is 200, and you choose a scale of 1, you'd need 200 individual symbols for that category. This is inefficient and hard to interpret. A scale of 10 or 20 would be much more appropriate, resulting in 20 or 10 symbols, respectively.

  3. Round Numbers Work Best:

    • For ease of calculation and quick interpretation, scales using round numbers are generally preferred.
    • Common and effective scales include 2, 5, 10, 25, 50, and 100. These numbers are easy to multiply and divide mentally, simplifying both the creation and reading of the pictograph.

The Impact of Scale: An Example

Consider the example of after-school activity sign-ups with data values of 10, 15, 20, and 25 students.

  • Scale A: 1 symbol = 5 students

    • Art Club (10 students): 2 symbols
    • Chess Club (15 students): 3 symbols
    • Drama Club (20 students): 4 symbols
    • Music Club (25 students): 5 symbols
    • Analysis: This scale works perfectly because all data values are multiples of 5, resulting in whole numbers of symbols. The chart is clean, concise, and easy to read.

  • Scale B: 1 symbol = 2 students

    • Art Club (10 students): 5 symbols
    • Chess Club (15 students): 7.5 symbols (7 full symbols and 1 half symbol)
    • Drama Club (20 students): 10 symbols
    • Music Club (25 students): 12.5 symbols (12 full symbols and 1 half symbol)
    • Analysis: While some values work out, others (like 15 and 25) require half symbols. This complicates the drawing and reading of the graph, and the rows become significantly longer, making it less clear than Scale A.

This comparison clearly demonstrates that Scale A is a much better fit for the given data due to its ability to represent all values with whole symbols and keep the graph compact.

When Pictographs Work Well (and When They Do Not)

Pictographs are excellent for presenting simple categorical data where the values are clear and easily represented by symbols. However, their effectiveness declines when complex values are involved:

  • Fractions of Symbols Are Tricky: While a half symbol (e.g., a symbol cut in half to represent 2.5 items if the scale is 5) can sometimes be acceptable, anything smaller than a half (like a quarter or three-eighths of a symbol) becomes nearly impossible to draw accurately and interpret correctly.
  • Sign of a Poor Scale: If you find yourself needing to represent tiny fractions of a symbol, it's a strong indicator that your chosen scale is inappropriate. In such cases, you should revisit your scale choice and select a different key value that allows your data to be represented by whole numbers or, at most, half symbols. The goal is always clarity and ease of understanding.
