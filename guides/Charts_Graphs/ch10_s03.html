Comparing Distributions with Box Plots

Overview

This section explores the powerful utility of side-by-side box plots for comparing two or more datasets. By placing multiple box plots on the same scale, we can quickly analyze and draw conclusions about differences in the center, spread, and extreme values of various groups, making it easier to answer comparative questions about data distributions.

Key Concepts

  • Side-by-Side Box Plots: This technique involves placing two or more box plots next to each other on a common numerical scale. This visual arrangement allows for a direct and immediate comparison of key features across different datasets.
  • Comparing Medians (Center): The median represents the middle value of a dataset. When comparing box plots, examining which group has a higher or lower median helps determine which group, on average, tends to have higher or lower values. The median is considered the fairest single-number summary of the center.
  • Comparing IQRs (Spread of Middle 50%): The Interquartile Range (IQR) is the width of the box in a box plot, representing the spread of the middle 50% of the data. A wider box indicates greater variability or spread in the central half of the data, while a narrower box suggests more consistency.
  • Comparing Whisker Lengths (Overall Range): The length of the whiskers indicates the overall spread of the data, excluding outliers. Longer whiskers suggest a wider overall range of values (though it's crucial not to assume most data is in the whiskers). Comparing whisker lengths helps understand which group has a broader span of values.
  • Identifying Outliers (Extreme Values): Outliers are individual data points that fall significantly outside the general pattern of the rest of the data, often plotted as individual dots. Comparing box plots allows you to see which groups, if any, contain these unusual or extreme values.
  • Appropriate Use of Side-by-Side Box Plots: They are best used when comparing two or more groups based on the same numerical measurement, when the goal is to understand differences in center, spread, and extreme values, and when there are too many data points (e.g., more than 20-25 per group) for a simple dot plot.
  • Limitations of Box Plots: While excellent for comparison, box plots do not show the detailed shape of a distribution (e.g., skewness, number of modes). For a more detailed view of the data's shape, histograms are more appropriate.

Important Terms

  • Box Plot: A graphical display that summarizes a dataset using its five-number summary: minimum, first quartile (Q1), median (Q2), third quartile (Q3), and maximum.
  • Side-by-Side Box Plots: Two or more box plots displayed together on the same numerical axis to facilitate direct comparison of their distributions.
  • Median: The middle value in an ordered dataset, dividing it into two equal halves. In a box plot, it's the line inside the box.
  • Interquartile Range (IQR): The difference between the third quartile (Q3) and the first quartile (Q1), representing the range of the middle 50% of the data. It is the length of the box in a box plot.
  • Whiskers: The lines extending from the box of a box plot, indicating the variability outside the middle 50% of the data, up to a certain maximum/minimum value within a calculated limit (often 1.5 * IQR from the quartiles).
  • Range: The difference between the maximum and minimum values in a dataset, providing a measure of the total spread.
  • Outlier: A data point that is significantly different from other data points in the same set, often indicating an error in data collection or a genuinely unusual event. In box plots, they are typically represented as individual points beyond the whiskers.
  • Distribution: Describes the pattern of values that a variable can take, showing how frequently each value or range of values occurs.

Detailed Notes

The Power of Side-by-Side Box Plots

Box plots are an incredibly efficient way to visualize the spread and center of numerical data. Their true strength shines when you need to compare two or more different groups or conditions. By placing these plots directly alongside each other on a shared scale, you can quickly answer comparative questions such as "Do students in Class A perform better than students in Class B?" or "Is there more variability in snowfall in one region compared to another?" This visual comparison is often much more intuitive and faster than scrutinizing multiple numerical summaries.

What to Compare in Side-by-Side Box Plots

When examining two or more box plots, focus on these four key aspects to draw comprehensive conclusions:

  1. Medians (Center):

    • Look at the line inside each box, which represents the median (the 50th percentile) of that dataset.
    • A higher median indicates that the typical value for that group is greater. For example, if the median test score for Class A is higher than for Class B, it suggests that Class A, on average, performed better. The median is favored because it's not as affected by extreme values as the mean.

  2. IQRs (Box Widths / Middle 50% Spread):

    • Observe the width of the box itself. This represents the Interquartile Range (IQR), which is the range of the middle 50% of the data (from the 25th to the 75th percentile).
    • A wider box signifies greater variability or spread among the central data points. This means the values within that group are more diverse.
    • A narrower box indicates less variability, meaning the central values are more consistent or clustered together.

  3. Whisker Lengths (Overall Range):

    • Examine the length of the whiskers extending from the boxes. These whiskers show the spread of the data beyond the middle 50%, up to the minimum and maximum values (excluding outliers).
    • Longer whiskers suggest a wider overall range of values for that group, indicating that the data extends further in either direction from the central tendency.
    • Common Misconception: Be careful not to assume that a long whisker means most of the data is out there. It simply means the data extends far. The density of data within a whisker is not easily discernible from a box plot.

  4. Outliers (Extreme Values):

    • Look for individual dots or asterisks plotted outside the whiskers. These represent outliers.
    • The presence and number of outliers can tell you if one group has more unusually high or low values compared to another. This can highlight interesting or exceptional cases within a dataset.

Worked Example: Sleep Hours on School Nights vs. Weekends

Let's apply these comparison points to the provided example of student sleep hours:

Scenario: A survey recorded sleep hours for 30 students on school nights and weekends, represented by side-by-side box plots.

  • Step 1: Compare Medians

    • School Nights Median = 7.5 hours
    • Weekend Median = 9.5 hours
    • Conclusion: Students typically sleep 2 hours more on weekends than on school nights. The center of the weekend sleep distribution is noticeably higher.

  • Step 2: Compare IQRs (Box Widths)

    • School Nights IQR = 8.0 - 6.5 = 1.5 hours
    • Weekend IQR = 10.5 - 8.0 = 2.5 hours
    • Conclusion: Weekend sleep hours are more variable. The wider box for weekend sleep indicates that the middle 50% of students have a greater spread in their sleep times compared to school nights. On school nights, students' sleep is more consistent.

  • Step 3: Compare Ranges (Whisker Lengths)

    • School Nights Range: 9.5 - 5.0 = 4.5 hours (max - min)
    • Weekend Range: 12.0 - 6.0 = 6.0 hours (max - min)
    • Conclusion: Weekend sleep has a wider overall range, meaning the difference between the minimum and maximum sleep hours is greater on weekends.

  • Step 4: Check for Outliers

    • In this specific example, neither box plot shows any outlier dots.
    • Conclusion: There are no unusually extreme sleep hours (very short or very long) reported in either group in this particular dataset.

Overall Conclusion: Students consistently sleep less and more consistently on school nights. On weekends, their sleep is longer on average but also much more varied, with some sleeping very late while others maintain a schedule closer to their school nights.

When to Use Side-by-Side Box Plots

Use side-by-side box plots when:

  • You need to compare two or more groups based on the same numerical measurement (e.g., test scores, heights, reaction times).
  • Your primary interest is in understanding differences in the center (median), spread (IQR, range), and the presence of extreme values (outliers) between groups.
  • You have a relatively large amount of data (more than 20-25 data points per group), making other plots like dot plots cluttered.

When NOT to Use Side-by-Side Box Plots

Avoid using side-by-side box plots if your goal is to understand the detailed shape of the distribution within a single group (e.g., if it's skewed, bimodal, or uniform). For such detailed shape analysis, histograms or density plots are more suitable.

Real-World Applications

Side-by-side box plots are widely used in many fields:

  • Medical Research: Comparing the effectiveness of two different drug treatments by looking at patient recovery times.
  • Ecology: Analyzing differences in animal sizes or plant growth across various habitats.
  • Sports Analytics: Comparing player performance distributions across different teams, seasons, or training regimens. For example, an analyst might show that one team has a higher median score but also more inconsistent performance (larger IQR) and more "bad games" (lower minimum) than another team.
