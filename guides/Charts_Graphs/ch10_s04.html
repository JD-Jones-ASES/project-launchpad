Histograms vs. Box Plots: A Comparative Study and Introduction to Stem-and-Leaf Plots

Overview

This section explores two fundamental data visualization tools, histograms and box plots, highlighting their unique strengths and weaknesses. It guides you in choosing the appropriate display based on the data and the question you're trying to answer. Additionally, it introduces stem-and-leaf plots as a simple, effective method for displaying small datasets, offering benefits of both detailed shape and individual value representation.

Key Concepts

  • Histograms for Shape: Histograms excel at revealing the detailed shape of a data distribution, showing where data clusters, gaps, and multiple peaks (bimodal distributions) occur.
  • Box Plots for Comparison: Box plots provide a compact summary of the five-number summary (minimum, Q1, median, Q3, maximum) and outliers, making them ideal for comparing the spread and central tendency of two or more groups side-by-side.
  • The Bin-Size Problem: The appearance and interpretation of a histogram can drastically change based on the chosen bin size. Too few bins hide detail, while too many create a noisy, spiky appearance.
  • Choosing the Right Display: Neither display is inherently "better"; the choice depends on the specific question being asked and what aspects of the data you want to emphasize or compare.
  • Stem-and-Leaf Plots: These plots offer a hybrid approach for small datasets, displaying both the overall shape of the distribution (like a histogram) and every individual data value (like a list).

Important Terms

  • Histogram: A graphical display of data using bars of different heights. Each bar groups numbers into ranges (bins), and taller bars show that more data falls into that range. It reveals the shape, spread, and central tendency of a dataset.
  • Box Plot (Box-and-Whisker Plot): A standardized way of displaying the distribution of data based on the five-number summary: minimum, first quartile (Q1), median, third quartile (Q3), and maximum. It also clearly marks outliers.
  • Bin Size: The width of the intervals or ranges used to group data in a histogram. An appropriate bin size is crucial for accurately representing the data's distribution.
  • Stem-and-Leaf Plot: A data display method that organizes numerical data by splitting each value into a "stem" (usually the leading digit or digits) and a "leaf" (usually the final digit).
  • Stem: In a stem-and-leaf plot, the leading digit(s) of a data value. All values with the same stem are grouped together in a row.
  • Leaf: In a stem-and-leaf plot, the final digit of a data value, listed horizontally next to its corresponding stem.

Detailed Notes

The Purpose of Data Displays

Histograms and box plots are both powerful tools for visualizing data distributions, but they serve different purposes. Understanding their strengths and weaknesses is essential for effective data literacy. There is no single "best" display; the most appropriate choice depends on the question you are trying to answer.

Histograms: Seeing the Shape

Histograms are particularly strong when you want to understand the detailed shape of a single dataset's distribution.

  • Strengths:

    • Reveals exact shape: You can see precisely where data points are concentrated, where they are sparse, and how the data is distributed (e.g., symmetrical, skewed left, skewed right).
    • Identifies bimodal distributions: Histograms are excellent at showing if a dataset has two distinct peaks, indicating two different groups within the data.
    • Spots gaps and clusters: Clear visual representation of any intervals where there is no data or where data is heavily clustered.

  • Weaknesses:

    • Difficulty with exact summary statistics: While you can estimate, it's not easy to pinpoint the exact median, quartiles (Q1, Q3), or other specific values directly from a histogram.
    • Challenging for group comparison: Comparing two or more datasets side-by-side using separate histograms can be cumbersome and difficult.
    • Sensitive to bin size: The visual appearance of a histogram can change dramatically depending on how the data is grouped into "bins."

The Bin-Size Problem

The choice of bin size is critical for histograms.

  • Too few bins: Can obscure important details, making the distribution look more uniform or symmetrical than it truly is. It hides variation.
  • Too many bins: Can make the histogram look spiky and noisy, highlighting random fluctuations rather than the underlying pattern of the data. It can make the data appear messier than it is.
  • Appropriate bin size: A moderate number of bins (often 5-15, depending on the data size) typically best represents the true shape of the distribution. Always critically evaluate a histogram's bin size before drawing conclusions.

Box Plots: Compact Comparison

Box plots offer a concise summary of a dataset, making them highly effective for comparing multiple groups.

  • Strengths:

    • Instant summary of key statistics: They immediately show the median, the interquartile range (IQR, which represents the middle 50% of the data), and the overall range (minimum to maximum), along with any outliers.
    • Easy group comparison: Their compact nature makes them ideal for placing multiple box plots side-by-side to compare central tendencies, spreads, and identify differences between groups.
    • Clear outlier identification: Individual data points identified as outliers are usually marked with distinct dots, making them easy to spot.

  • Weaknesses:

    • Hides distribution shape: Box plots do not show the detailed shape of the distribution. You cannot tell if a distribution is bimodal or has specific peaks and valleys from a box plot. Two very different underlying distributions can produce identical box plots.
    • Does not show data density: It's hard to tell how many data points are clustered within the box or whiskers.
    • Hides exact number of data points: A box plot doesn't directly tell you how many observations are in the dataset.

Comparing Histograms and Box Plots

Consider a dataset of class test scores shown as both a histogram and a box plot.

  • What a histogram reveals: The specific distribution of scores (e.g., if most scores are in the 70s, or if there's a cluster of low scores and another of high scores).
  • What a box plot reveals: The median score, the range of the middle 50% of scores, the overall range, and if there were any exceptionally low or high scores (outliers). It allows for quick comparison of these features if you had test scores from multiple classes.

Stem-and-Leaf Plots: The Best of Both Worlds

For smaller datasets, stem-and-leaf plots offer a unique combination of features.

  • Definition: A stem-and-leaf plot organizes data by splitting each value into a stem (typically the tens digit or higher) and a leaf (typically the ones digit). All values with the same stem are grouped together horizontally.

  • How to Read and Interpret:

    • Example: For scores like 55, 58, 62, 64, 67, 69, 70, 71, 73, 75, 75, 78, 82, 84, 86, 88, 89, 90, 93:
      Stem | Leaves
5    | 5 8
6    | 2 4 7 9
7    | 0 1 3 5 5 8
8    | 2 4 6 8 9
9    | 0 3
    • Each row represents a "stem" (e.g., the 50s, 60s, etc.). The "leaves" are the individual units digits within that range.
    • Reading 5 | 5 8 means the data includes 55 and 58.
    • Reading 7 | 0 1 3 5 5 8 means the data includes 70, 71, 73, 75, 75, and 78.

  • What they reveal:

    • Overall shape: By turning the plot on its side, you can see the distribution shape, similar to a histogram.
    • Individual values: Unlike a histogram, every single data point is visible.
    • Minimum and maximum: Easy to identify the lowest and highest values in the dataset.
    • Data density: You can see which ranges have the most data points (the longest rows of leaves).

Choosing Your Display

The "best" chart depends on your objective:

  • To show the precise shape of a single distribution: Use a histogram.
  • To quickly compare the central tendency and spread of multiple groups: Use box plots.
  • To show the shape and all individual values for a small dataset: Use a stem-and-leaf plot.

Review Questions

  1. Describe a scenario where a histogram would be a more appropriate data display than a box plot. Conversely, when would a box plot be preferred over a histogram? Provide specific reasons for each choice.
  2. Explain the "bin-size problem" in histograms. How can choosing too few or too many bins mislead someone interpreting the data? What steps should you take to critically evaluate a histogram's bin size?
  3. Imagine you are given a dataset of daily temperatures for a month. What specific insights could you gain from a histogram of this data that you could not easily discern from a box plot? What insights would a box plot provide that a histogram might obscure?
  4. A stem-and-leaf plot is described as having "the best of both worlds." What two types of information, typically found in separate displays, does a stem-and-leaf plot combine? For what type of dataset is it most effective?
  5. Why is it important for someone to understand the strengths and weaknesses of different data displays rather than thinking one display is "always better"? Discuss how this understanding contributes to overall data literacy.
