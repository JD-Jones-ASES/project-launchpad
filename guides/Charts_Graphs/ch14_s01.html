Choosing the Right Chart: The Chart Selection Flowchart

Overview

This section introduces a foundational framework for selecting the appropriate data visualization. It emphasizes that every chart type is designed to answer a specific question, and successful data storytelling begins by identifying one of five main purposes: comparison, composition, change over time, distribution, or relationship. By matching your data's purpose to the correct chart, you ensure clarity and avoid misinterpretation.

Key Concepts

  • Purpose-Driven Chart Selection: The most crucial step in choosing a chart is to first identify what question you are trying to answer or what story you want to tell with your data.
  • Five Main Data Visualization Purposes:
    • Comparison: Showing how different categories or groups measure against each other.
    • Composition: Illustrating how a whole breaks down into its constituent parts.
    • Change over Time: Displaying how a variable evolves or trends as time progresses.
    • Distribution: Revealing how values are spread out, clustered, or what their shape is within a dataset.
    • Relationship: Exploring how two or more variables interact or correlate with one another.
  • Chart as a Tool: Just like using the right tool for a job, selecting the correct chart ensures your data communicates clearly and honestly, preventing muddy or misleading messages.
  • Time on the X-Axis: For any chart that displays change over time, the horizontal (x-axis) must always represent time, ordered from earliest to most recent.

Important Terms

  • Comparison Chart: A visualization designed to display data so that different categories or groups can be easily measured and contrasted against each other.
  • Vertical Bar Chart: A standard comparison chart where bars extend vertically, ideal for 3-10 categories with short names.
  • Horizontal Bar Chart: A comparison chart where bars extend horizontally, best for categories with long names or when ranking items from highest to lowest.
  • Clustered Bar Chart: A comparison chart used to compare the same categories across multiple distinct groups (e.g., comparing sales across different regions for two different years).
  • Diverging Bar Chart: A comparison chart used when responses split in two opposing directions (e.g., "strongly agree" to "strongly disagree").
  • Pie Chart: A composition chart best for showing parts of a whole with six or fewer slices of clearly different sizes, where each slice represents a percentage of a 100% total.
  • Donut Chart: Similar to a pie chart, but with a hollow center, often used to display a key number or total within the middle.
  • Treemap: A composition chart for showing many categories or hierarchical data, using nested rectangles whose sizes represent their proportion of the whole.
  • Waffle Chart: A composition chart that uses a grid of cells (typically 10x10, where each cell is 1%) to visually represent exact percentages in a highly readable format.
  • Stacked Bar Chart: A composition chart used to compare the breakdown of parts across multiple different groups or categories.
  • Line Graph: A change over time chart that connects data points with lines, making trends (increases, decreases, plateaus) immediately visible, typically for one or a few series.
  • Area Chart: A change over time chart similar to a line graph, but with the region below the line filled in to emphasize the cumulative total or volume over time.
  • Stacked Area Chart: A change over time chart that shows how different parts of a total collectively change over time, with each category's area stacked upon the others.
  • Dot Plot: A distribution chart for small datasets (under 30 values) where each individual data point is shown.
  • Histogram: A distribution chart used for larger datasets of continuous data (like height or test scores), grouping values into "bins" to show the frequency distribution.
  • Box Plot: A compact distribution chart that summarizes the spread of data using five key values: minimum, first quartile (Q1), median, third quartile (Q3), and maximum, particularly useful for comparing distributions across groups.
  • Scatter Plot: A relationship chart that displays two variables, one on each axis, using individual dots. It helps identify positive trends, negative trends, or no trend (random scatter).
  • Bubble Chart: A relationship chart that extends the scatter plot by adding a third variable, represented by the size of each "bubble" or dot.

Detailed Notes

The Importance of Choosing the Right Chart

Just as you wouldn't use a screwdriver to hammer a nail, selecting the correct chart type is crucial for effective data communication. Each chart is designed to answer a specific question, and choosing the right one ensures your data tells a clear, honest story, rather than a muddy or misleading one. The core principle is to first identify what you are trying to show.

Five Questions, Five Purposes

All data visualizations fall into one of five main purposes:

  1. Comparison:

    • Goal: To show how different items, categories, or groups measure up against each other.
    • Common Misconception: Students often default to bar charts for everything. Remember, comparison is about distinct categories.
    • Chart Types:
      • Vertical Bar Chart: The standard for comparing 3-10 categories with short names. Bar height represents the value.
      • Horizontal Bar Chart: Ideal when category names are long (e.g., country names) or when you need to rank items from highest to lowest clearly.
      • Clustered Bar Chart: Use when you need to compare the same categories across multiple groups (e.g., boys vs. girls, or Year 1 vs. Year 2 performance).
      • Diverging Bar Chart: Effective for showing responses that split in two directions, such as survey results from "strongly agree" to "strongly disagree."

  2. Composition:

    • Goal: To illustrate how a whole breaks down into its constituent parts, often as percentages.
    • Common Misconception: Many think pie charts are always good for "parts." However, they have limitations.
    • Chart Types:
      • Pie Chart: Best for six or fewer slices, especially when showing distinct percentage differences. The total must sum to 100%.
      • Donut Chart: Functionally similar to a pie chart, but with a hollow center often used to display a central value or key information.
      • Treemap: Excellent for when you have many categories or a hierarchical breakdown (e.g., budget by department, then by sub-item within departments). Rectangles are used instead of slices.
      • Waffle Chart: Great for showing exact percentages in a grid format, where each cell (in a 10x10 grid) represents 1%.
      • Stacked Bar Chart: Useful for comparing the composition of multiple different wholes (e.g., showing the breakdown of sales by product category across several different stores).

  3. Change Over Time:

    • Goal: To show how a value or trend evolves as time passes.
    • Crucial Rule: The x-axis (horizontal) must always represent time (hours, days, months, years) arranged from earliest to most recent. Scrambling the time order destroys the story.
    • Chart Types:
      • Line Graph: The primary choice for tracking one or a few data series over time, making trends (ups, downs, stability) immediately obvious.
      • Area Chart: Similar to a line graph, but the area below the line is filled in, often used to emphasize the cumulative total or volume over time.
      • Stacked Area Chart: Used to show how different components contribute to a total which changes over time, visualizing how each part's contribution evolves.

  4. Distribution:

    • Goal: To understand the shape of your data—where values cluster, where there are gaps, what counts as typical, and what outliers look like.
    • Common Misconception: Students may confuse "distribution" (spread of data) with "comparison" (difference between values). A histogram shows spread; a bar chart shows direct comparison.
    • Chart Types:
      • Dot Plot: Best for small datasets (fewer than 30 values) where you want to see every individual data point.
      • Histogram: Ideal for larger datasets of continuous data (like heights, weights, or test scores) grouped into numerical "bins" to show frequency.
      • Box Plot: Provides a compact summary of the data's distribution (minimum, Q1, median, Q3, maximum), especially useful for comparing distributions across multiple groups.

  5. Relationship:

    • Goal: To see how two (or three) variables connect or correlate with each other.
    • Chart Types:
      • Scatter Plot: The go-to for showing the relationship between two variables. Each dot represents an item, with its position indicating its values on both the x and y axes. Look for positive trends (dots generally rise from left to right), negative trends (dots fall), or no trend (random scatter).
      • Bubble Chart: An extension of the scatter plot, allowing you to visualize a third variable by varying the size of the bubbles. For example, comparing x-value, y-value, and an additional measure like population size.

Worked Examples in Practice

  • Scenario 1: Class Survey (Favorite Sport)

    • Purpose: To compare how many students chose each sport. (Comparison)
    • Best Choice: A vertical bar chart. It clearly shows the count for each category (sport), making comparisons easy. A pie chart could also work as it's a composition, but bar charts are often better for direct comparison of sizes.

  • Scenario 2: Puppy's Weight Log

    • Purpose: To show how the puppy's weight changes over time. (Change over time)
    • Best Choice: A line graph. With weeks on the x-axis and weight on the y-axis, the connecting line instantly reveals growth trends.

Charts in Everyday Life

Once you understand these five purposes, you'll start to recognize them everywhere: sports statistics use bar charts for player comparisons, weather apps use line graphs for temperature forecasts, phone usage reports often show bar charts for daily screen time, and even doctors use scatter plots to track growth against norms. Recognizing these patterns helps you become a more data-literate individual.
