Understanding Misleading Pictographs

Overview

This section explores how pictographs, while seemingly simple, can be used to visually deceive viewers, primarily through a phenomenon called "area distortion." It explains why scaling up icons to represent larger numbers can lead to a drastic overestimation of differences, and it outlines other common tricks to watch out for. The section emphasizes the importance of critical thinking and introduces Otto Neurath's rule for creating honest pictographs.

Key Concepts

  • Area Distortion: Occurs when symbols in a pictograph are scaled up to represent larger values. Doubling a symbol's height (and thus its proportional width) results in an area that is four times larger, making differences appear much greater than they actually are. Your brain interprets the total area, not just the height or count.
  • Visual Deception: The general idea that charts, including pictographs, can be intentionally or unintentionally designed to mislead viewers by exaggerating or minimizing data differences.
  • The Honest Pictograph Rule (Otto Neurath's Rule): A fundamental principle stating that to show more of something, one should always use more symbols of the same size, never a bigger symbol. This rule directly prevents area and volume distortion.
  • Critical Evaluation: The necessity for viewers to carefully examine pictographs (and all data visualizations) for potential tricks, distortions, and missing information, rather than simply accepting them at face value. This includes checking the key and counting symbols.
  • Other Pictograph Tricks: Beyond area distortion, other methods to mislead include using 3D icons (which distort by volume even more severely), omitting a key, using inconsistent symbols, or employing confusing half symbols.

Important Terms

  • Pictograph: A type of graph that uses icons or pictures to represent quantities or data, making statistics visually engaging and accessible.
  • Area Distortion: The specific problem in pictographs where scaling up an icon proportionally (doubling height and width) causes its area to quadruple, making the represented value appear four times greater than intended.
  • ISOTYPE (International System of Typographic Picture Education): A system developed by Otto Neurath that uses simple, standardized, icon-based charts to communicate statistics clearly and honestly to a wide audience, following the rule of changing the number of symbols, not their size.
  • Key (or Legend): An essential component of a pictograph (and other charts) that explains what each symbol represents and its corresponding value. A missing or unclear key renders a chart uninterpretable or deceptive.

Detailed Notes

The Problem with Scaling Icons

  • The "Sneaky Trick": It seems logical to make a picture bigger to show a larger number. However, this is a common form of visual deception.
  • How it Misleads: When an icon is made larger to represent a higher value, it can make the difference between values appear much greater (or smaller) than it actually is.

The Area Problem Explained

  • The Scenario: If sales double from January (100 phones) to February (200 phones), making February's phone icon twice as tall creates a problem.
  • Proportional Scaling: When you double the height of a picture and keep it in proportion, its width also doubles.
  • The Result: A picture that is twice as tall AND twice as wide has an area that is four times the original.
  • Brain's Interpretation: Your brain primarily reads the area of the image, not just its height. So, even if the value is only twice as much, the icon looks four times bigger.
  • Definition of Area Distortion: This is formally defined as the phenomenon in a pictograph where scaling symbols up to show larger values leads to a misperception. Doubling a symbol's dimensions (height and width) quadruples its area, causing viewers to grossly overstate the actual differences.

Example: Phone Sales – Correct vs. Misleading

  • Honest Version: To show sales doubling from January to February (e.g., 4 icons for January, 8 for February, where 1 icon = 25 phones), the correct approach is to use more icons of the same size. This accurately conveys the 2:1 ratio visually.
  • Misleading Version: Using one small icon for January and one large icon for February, where the February icon is twice as tall (and thus four times the area), exaggerates the doubling of sales into a four-fold visual difference.

Watch Out: Big Icons Do Not Mean Big Numbers!

  • Rule of Thumb: Be highly skeptical whenever you see a pictograph where the icons are of different sizes.
  • Correct Practice: In a proper pictograph, only the number of symbols should vary, not their individual size.
  • Crucial Step: Always count the symbols and carefully check the key (legend) to understand the actual data. Never rely solely on the visual size of the icons.

Other Pictograph Tricks

Beyond area distortion, other methods can make pictographs misleading:

  • 3D Icons: Using three-dimensional objects amplifies distortion. Doubling the dimensions (height, width, and depth) of a 3D object results in eight times the volume, making differences appear far greater than they are.
  • Missing Key: Without a key or legend, it's impossible to know what each symbol represents, rendering the chart unreadable and potentially deceptive.
  • Inconsistent Symbols: Using different types of symbols for different categories or time periods within the same chart makes fair comparison impossible and confuses the viewer.
  • Misleading Half Symbols: Cutting a symbol in half horizontally can convey a different impression than cutting it vertically, leading to visual confusion or misinterpretation of partial values.

Otto Neurath and the ISOTYPE System

  • Pioneer in Data Visualization: Otto Neurath (1882–1945) was an Austrian philosopher and social scientist dedicated to making data accessible to everyone.
  • ISOTYPE Development: In the 1920s and 1930s, he created ISOTYPE (International System of Typographic Picture Education), a system of clear, standardized, icon-based charts.
  • Neurath's Key Rule: The core principle of ISOTYPE, and the key to honest pictographs, is: "always change the number of symbols, never their size."
  • Impact: Neurath's work emphasized clarity, honesty, and understandability without complex math, profoundly influencing modern graphic design, data visualization, and infographics.

The Honest Pictograph Rule (Quick Tip)

  • To create an honest and easily understandable pictograph, always adhere to Otto Neurath's rule: use more symbols of the same size to represent larger quantities, rather than bigger symbols.

Review Questions

  2. Imagine you are presented with a pictograph showing company profits increasing over three years. How would you determine if the pictograph is using area distortion to mislead the viewer? What specific elements would you look for?
  3. Besides area distortion, what other "tricks" or deceptive practices can make a pictograph misleading? Describe at least three and explain why they are problematic.
  4. Who was Otto Neurath, and what was his primary contribution to the field of data visualization? How does his "Honest Pictograph Rule" directly address the problem of area distortion?
