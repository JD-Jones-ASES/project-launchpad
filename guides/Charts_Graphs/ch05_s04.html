Chapter 5, Section 4: Diverging Bar Charts

Overview

Diverging bar charts are specialized visualizations designed to represent data that naturally splits into two opposing directions, such as agreement versus disagreement or positive versus negative values. By placing bars on both sides of a central line, these charts effectively illustrate the balance and distribution of responses across a spectrum, making it easy to compare opposing viewpoints or magnitudes. They are particularly useful for survey data and other scenarios with a clear midpoint.

Key Concepts

  • Data with a Natural Midpoint: Diverging bar charts are best suited for data that has a clear central point, such as "Neutral" in a survey, a zero value for financial changes, or a dividing line between two opposing categories.
  • Two-Directional Representation: Unlike standard bar charts, diverging charts display values extending in two directions from a central axis. Bars on one side represent one category (e.g., "Agree"), and bars on the other side represent its opposite (e.g., "Disagree").
  • Visualizing Balance and Distribution: The primary advantage is the ability to quickly see the proportion and balance between opposing values. Long bars on one side indicate a strong leaning, while bars of similar length on both sides suggest a split or controversial opinion.
  • Appropriate Use Cases: Ideal for Likert scale survey responses (e.g., Strongly Agree to Strongly Disagree), positive/negative changes, or any data where categories are intrinsically opposed and can be centered around a neutral point.

Important Terms

  • Diverging Bar Chart: A type of bar chart characterized by a central line (representing zero, neutral, or a midpoint) from which bars extend in two opposite directions. One direction represents one type of value (e.g., "Agree"), and the other represents its opposite (e.g., "Disagree").
  • Central Line/Midpoint: The vertical axis in the middle of a diverging bar chart, typically labeled 0, "Neutral," or serving as the division point between two opposing categories of data. It is crucial for interpreting the chart's meaning.
  • Likert Scale: A psychometric scale commonly used in surveys, where respondents rate their level of agreement or disagreement with a statement, typically ranging from "Strongly Disagree" to "Strongly Agree," often including a "Neutral" option. Diverging bar charts are excellent for visualizing Likert scale data.

Detailed Notes

What Makes a Bar Chart "Diverging"?

A diverging bar chart is distinguished by its central line, which acts as a pivot point. Instead of all bars originating from a single baseline, bars extend both to the left and to the right from this center. This design is specifically for data that inherently splits into two opposite directions. Examples include:

  • Opinion Data: Agree vs. Disagree, Positive vs. Negative sentiment.
  • Quantitative Data: Above Zero vs. Below Zero, Increase vs. Decrease.

The central line typically represents a neutral point, a zero value, or the dividing line between the two opposing categories.

When to Use Diverging Bar Charts

Diverging bar charts are most appropriate when your data naturally has a midpoint and you want to highlight the distribution and balance between two contrasting sets of values. They are particularly effective for:

  • Survey Results: Especially those using Likert scales (e.g., "Strongly Agree," "Agree," "Neutral," "Disagree," "Strongly Disagree").
  • Performance Metrics: Showing positive vs. negative changes over time or against a target.
  • Comparative Analysis: Visualizing two opposing aspects of a single subject.

Common Misconception: While diverging charts can show negative numbers, they are also widely used for opinion data where no values are truly negative. The "left" side simply represents one category (e.g., "Disagree"), and the "right" side represents its opposite (e.g., "Agree"). The key is the natural midpoint, not necessarily numerical negativity.

How to Read a Diverging Bar Chart

To effectively interpret a diverging bar chart, follow these steps:

  1. Locate the Center Line: Identify the vertical line in the middle of the chart. This is your reference point, often labeled 0, "Neutral," or indicating the split between the two opposing data types.
  2. Consult the Legend: Understand what each direction (left vs. right) and what different colors (if present, like for "Strongly Agree" vs. "Agree") represent. For example, "Left = Agree" and "Right = Disagree."
  3. Read Bar Lengths on Each Side: The length of a bar extending from the center line indicates the magnitude (percentage, count, etc.) for that specific category in that direction. Read values for both the left and right sides independently.
  4. Interpret the Balance: Compare the lengths of the bars on the left side to those on the right side for a given row (e.g., a specific survey question).
    • If bars extend significantly more to one side, it indicates a strong leaning or opinion in that direction.
    • If bars are roughly equal in length on both sides, it suggests a divided opinion or an even split.
    • The presence of a "Neutral" segment in the middle (often a different color) shows the proportion of respondents who did not lean either way.
  5. Compare Across Rows/Categories: Analyze different rows (e.g., different survey questions) to identify overall trends. Which questions received the most agreement? Which had the strongest disagreement? Which were the most controversial (most evenly split)?

Worked Example: Student Satisfaction Survey

Consider a survey where students rated statements about school lunch on a scale of "Strongly Agree," "Agree," "Neutral," "Disagree," and "Strongly Disagree." The chart uses dark blue (Strongly Agree) and light blue (Agree) bars extending left, a gray bar for Neutral in the center, and light red (Disagree) and dark red (Strongly Disagree) bars extending right.

Example Data for "The lunch menu has good variety":

  • Strongly Agree: 15%
  • Agree: 25%
  • Neutral: 20%
  • Disagree: 28%
  • Strongly Disagree: 12%

Interpreting the Balance:

  • Total "Agree" responses (left side): 15% (Strongly Agree) + 25% (Agree) = 40%
  • Total "Disagree" responses (right side): 28% (Disagree) + 12% (Strongly Disagree) = 40%
  • Neutral responses: 20%

In this case, the opinion is evenly split between agreement and disagreement, with a fifth of students remaining neutral. The bars on the left and right for this question would appear roughly equal in overall length, though composed of different segments.

Comparing Questions:

  • If another question, such as "Lunch line moves quickly," has most of its bar extending to the left (agree side), it means students generally agree with that statement.
  • Conversely, a question where the bar leans heavily to the right would indicate strong overall disagreement.

Application: Likert Scale Surveys

Diverging bar charts are perfectly suited for visualizing Likert scale data because the scale itself has a natural midpoint (Neutral) and extends in two opposite directions (Agree/Disagree). This makes it intuitive to see how opinions are distributed and balanced across a range of statements. When designing surveys, keep in mind that questions that yield a natural midpoint are excellent candidates for this chart type.

Review Questions

  1. What is the defining characteristic of a diverging bar chart, and in what types of data scenarios is it most effective compared to a standard bar chart?
  2. Imagine you are looking at a diverging bar chart of public opinion on a new policy. If the bars for one question showed a very long segment on the "Strongly Disagree" side and almost no segments on the "Agree" side, what would that tell you about public sentiment?
  3. Explain the five key steps you would take to accurately read and interpret any given diverging bar chart, from identifying the center line to comparing rows.
  4. A student believes that diverging bar charts can only be used for data with negative numbers. How would you correct this misconception, providing an example of a common use case where negative numbers are not involved?
  5. Consider the Student Satisfaction Survey example. If the bars for "Lunch line moves quickly" were mostly on the "Agree" side, and the bars for "The lunch menu has good variety" were evenly split between "Agree" and "Disagree" (as in the example), what immediate actions might a school principal consider based on this information?
