Study Guide: Understanding Treemaps

Overview

This section introduces treemaps as a powerful visualization tool for displaying hierarchical data and large datasets that pie charts cannot handle effectively. It explains how treemaps use nested rectangles where each rectangle's area is proportional to the value it represents, allowing for visual comparison of categories and sub-categories within a whole.

Key Concepts

  • Limitations of Pie Charts: Pie charts are only effective for showing parts of a whole when there are a small number of categories. They become unreadable with many slices and cannot display hierarchical relationships.
  • Treemap's Purpose: Treemaps solve the problems of showing many categories and hierarchical data simultaneously by displaying nested rectangles.
  • Area Proportionality: The fundamental principle of a treemap is that the area (width × height) of each rectangle directly corresponds to the value it represents. Larger areas mean larger values.
  • Hierarchical Data Visualization: Treemaps excel at showing data with categories inside categories (e.g., a school budget broken down by department, then by line item). Top-level categories are represented by large rectangles, which are then subdivided into smaller rectangles for sub-categories.
  • Visual Comparison: Instead of reading numbers off an axis, treemaps require comparison of the relative sizes (areas) of rectangles by eye to understand relationships and magnitudes within the dataset.
  • Comparison Within Groups: It is most valid to compare the sizes of rectangles within the same parent category. Comparing rectangles across different parent categories can be misleading if the parent totals are vastly different.

Important Terms

  • Treemap: A data visualization that displays hierarchical data as a set of nested rectangles, where each rectangle's area is proportional to the value it represents.
  • Hierarchy: A structure where items are organized into different levels, with larger groups containing smaller sub-groups, demonstrating relationships from broad categories to specific details.

Detailed Notes

The Need for Treemaps

  • Pie Chart Limitations: While useful for simple "parts of a whole" scenarios, pie charts become ineffective when:
    • There are too many categories (leading to a "confusing mess").
    • Data has a hierarchical structure (categories within categories), which pie charts cannot represent.

What is a Treemap?

  • Treemaps display data using a system of nested rectangles.
  • Key Principle: The area of each rectangle (width multiplied by height) is directly proportional to the numerical value it represents. This is crucial for understanding the data.
  • Use Case: Particularly useful for visualizing hierarchical data – data organized into levels where larger groups contain smaller sub-groups (e.g., a school district budget flowing down to individual classroom supplies). Treemaps can show two or more levels of this structure at once.

How a Treemap is Built (Conceptually)

  1. Start with a single large rectangle representing the entire dataset's total value (e.g., a whole budget).
  2. Divide this large rectangle into smaller, large pieces, with each piece's area proportional to the major top-level spending areas.
  3. Then, divide each of these large pieces further into even smaller rectangles, again with areas proportional to the sub-categories within that major area.
  4. The result is a complex, nested arrangement where bigger rectangles always signify bigger values.

How to Read a Treemap

  1. Read the Title: Understand what the entire treemap represents and its overall total.
  2. Identify Top-Level Groups: Look for the largest, usually bordered and color-coded sections. These represent the main categories.
  3. Identify Sub-Categories: Within each large rectangle, observe the smaller, subdivided rectangles. These are the sub-categories.
  4. Compare Areas Within a Group: To understand which sub-category is largest, compare the areas of the rectangles within the same parent group.
  5. Compare Large Groups: Observe which top-level group occupies the most space to identify the highest total among main categories.
  6. Check Labels and Values: Most treemaps provide labels and specific values for each rectangle for precise interpretation.
  7. Read the Color Legend: Color typically differentiates top-level groups, often using shades of a single color for sub-rectangles belonging to the same parent.

Worked Example: Westview Middle School Budget

  • Total Budget: $110,000.
  • Top-level Groups:
    • Academics (blue): $65,000 (59% of total) – Largest section.
    • Facilities (teal): $31,000 (28% of total) – Medium section.
    • Administration (orange): $14,000 (13% of total) – Smallest section.
  • Reading Within Academics:
    • "Teacher salaries" ($45,000) is the dominant sub-rectangle, making up nearly 70% of the Academics budget and 41% of the entire school budget.
    • "Books & supplies" ($12,000) and "Classroom technology" ($8,000) are significantly smaller within Academics.
  • Comparing Across Sections: "Teacher salaries" alone is larger than the entire "Facilities" budget and more than three times the "Administration" budget.
  • Conclusion: The school primarily allocates funds to teacher salaries, followed by building maintenance and then administrative functions.

Common Misconceptions and Important Reminders

  • Area, Not Height or Width: A crucial point is that the entire area (width × height) of a rectangle represents its value, not just its height or width. A tall, narrow rectangle can have the same value as a short, wide one if their areas are equal.
  • Comparing Across Different Parent Categories: Be cautious when comparing rectangles that belong to different top-level groups. Their sizes should be interpreted in context of their respective parent totals, which may vary significantly. Comparisons are most accurate within the same group.
  • Sorting is Not Required (but common): While many treemap tools sort rectangles by size automatically, the primary feature is proportional area, not the order of items.

Origin of Treemaps

  • Invented in 1990 by Ben Shneiderman to visualize file sizes on a computer hard drive, quickly identifying "space hogs."
  • Today, treemaps are widely used in various applications, from financial dashboards to media streaming and file management.

Review Questions

  1. Explain why a treemap is a more effective visualization tool than a pie chart when dealing with a large number of categories or hierarchical data.
  2. What is the fundamental principle that determines the size of each rectangle in a treemap? Why is it important to remember this principle when interpreting the data?
  3. Describe the steps you would take to interpret a treemap showing your city's annual budget, focusing on identifying top-level spending and then specific line items.
  4. Referring to the Westview Middle School budget example, how does the treemap effectively show that "Teacher salaries" is the largest single expenditure, not just within "Academics" but also relative to other main budget areas like "Facilities"?
  5. Imagine you need to visualize how a student spends their 24-hour day (sleep, school, activities, meals, free time). Would a treemap be a suitable visualization? Explain why or why not, and describe how you would construct it.
