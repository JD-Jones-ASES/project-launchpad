Chapter 10, Section 1: Histograms

Overview

This section introduces histograms as a powerful tool for visualizing the distribution of continuous numerical data. It distinguishes histograms from bar charts by emphasizing the use of continuous data, equal-width intervals (bins), and the critical absence of gaps between bars. Students will learn to interpret histogram shapes (symmetric, right-skewed, left-skewed) and extract key information about a dataset's center and spread.

Key Concepts

  • Histograms vs. Bar Charts: Histograms display continuous numerical data grouped into intervals (bins), while bar charts display categorical (discrete) data.
  • Continuous Data: Data that can take any value within a range (e.g., heights, temperatures). It's the type of data histograms are designed for.
  • No Gaps Between Bars: A defining feature of histograms. Since continuous data has no natural breaks, the bars representing consecutive intervals touch, signifying the flow of data across the range.
  • Bins (Intervals): Equal-width ranges into which continuous data is divided. Each bar on a histogram represents the frequency of data values falling within a specific bin.
  • Distribution: Describes how data values are spread out, showing where data clusters, is sparse, and its overall pattern. Histograms visually represent this distribution.
  • Shapes of Distribution:
    • Symmetric: The left side of the histogram is a mirror image of the right side, with the peak in the middle.
    • Right-Skewed: The bulk of the data is on the left, and there is a long "tail" stretching to the right. The skew refers to the direction of the tail.
    • Left-Skewed: The bulk of the data is on the right, and there is a long "tail" stretching to the left.
  • Reading a Histogram: Involves understanding axis labels, identifying bin intervals, reading bar heights (frequencies), describing the distribution's shape, and estimating the center and spread of the data.

Important Terms

Histogram: A type of bar chart used to display the distribution of a set of continuous numerical data. The x-axis is divided into equal-width intervals (bins), and the height of each bar shows how many data values fall within that interval. Bars in a histogram touch each other. Continuous Data: Numerical data that can take any value within a given range (e.g., height, weight, temperature, time). Bin (Interval): One of the equal-width groups or ranges into which continuous data is divided for a histogram. For example, 150–154 cm. Distribution: The way in which data values are spread out, including where they cluster, where they are sparse, and whether the overall pattern is symmetric or lopsided. Symmetric Distribution: A distribution where the left side is approximately a mirror image of the right side, with the peak usually in the middle. Right-Skewed Distribution: A distribution where the majority of the data is concentrated on the left side, and there is a long "tail" extending to the right. The term "skew" refers to the direction of this tail. Left-Skewed Distribution: A distribution where the majority of the data is concentrated on the right side, and there is a long "tail" extending to the left. The term "skew" refers to the direction of this tail.

Detailed Notes

What is a Histogram?

A histogram is a specialized type of bar chart designed specifically for continuous numerical data. Unlike a regular bar chart, which compares distinct categories (e.g., favorite colors), a histogram shows how a set of measurements (e.g., heights, test scores) are distributed across a range of values.

The Critical Difference: No Gaps!

The most crucial characteristic distinguishing a histogram from a bar chart is that there are no gaps between the bars in a histogram. This is because the data it represents is continuous.

  • Continuous data can take any value within a given range (e.g., 164 cm, 164.5 cm, 164.9 cm). There are no natural breaks between one value and the next in a continuous set.
  • Since there's no gap between, say, 164 cm and 165 cm, the bars representing these ranges (or intervals that include them) should also touch, showing a continuous flow of data.

Bins: Grouping Continuous Data

Because continuous data can have many possible values, we cannot create a bar for each individual measurement. Instead, we group the measurements into bins (also called intervals).

  • A bin is an equal-width range of values. For example, student heights might be grouped into bins like 150–154 cm, 155–159 cm, etc.
  • Each bar on the histogram represents one bin, and its height shows the frequency (how many data values) fall within that specific interval.

Reading the Shape of a Distribution

Histograms are excellent for visualizing the distribution of data, which describes how values are spread out. The shape of a histogram tells us a lot about the data:

  1. Symmetric Distribution:

    • The histogram looks roughly the same on both sides of its center.
    • The peak (tallest bar) is usually in the middle.
    • Most data values cluster around the center, and frequencies decrease towards the ends.
    • Example: Heights of a large, diverse group of adults.

  2. Right-Skewed Distribution:

    • The "bulk" or majority of the data is on the left side of the histogram.
    • There is a long "tail" stretching out to the right.
    • Misconception Alert: "Skewed right" means the tail is on the right, not that the peak leans right. The peak will actually be on the left.
    • Example: Household income (most people earn moderate amounts; a few earn very high salaries, pulling the tail to the right).

  3. Left-Skewed Distribution:

    • The "bulk" or majority of the data is on the right side of the histogram.
    • There is a long "tail" stretching out to the left.
    • Misconception Alert: "Skewed left" means the tail is on the left, not that the peak leans left. The peak will actually be on the right.
    • Example: Age at retirement (most people retire in their 60s or later; a few retire very early, pulling the tail to the left).

How to Read and Interpret a Histogram

Follow these steps to effectively read and understand a histogram:

  1. Read the Title and Axis Labels: Understand what data is being measured (x-axis) and what the height of the bars represents (y-axis, usually frequency or count).
  2. Identify the Bins: Note the range of values each bar covers. Ensure you understand the width of each bin.
  3. Read the Height of Each Bar: This tells you the frequency – how many data points fall within that specific bin.
  4. Describe the Shape: Determine if the distribution is symmetric, right-skewed, or left-skewed. Identify where the peak (tallest bar) is located.
  5. Identify Center and Spread:
    • Center: Which bin contains the most data (the peak)? This gives an idea of the typical value.
    • Spread: What is the overall range of the data (from the lowest value in the first bin to the highest value in the last bin)?
  6. Look for Gaps or Unusual Bars: Are there any bins with no data? Are any bars exceptionally tall or short compared to their neighbors? These might indicate outliers or unusual patterns.

Worked Example: Student Heights Histogram

Let's apply these steps to the provided histogram of fifty student heights (in 5 cm bins):

  1. Labels: X-axis: Height in cm. Y-axis: Number of students.
  2. Bin Intervals:
    • 150–154 cm
    • 155–159 cm
    • 160–164 cm
    • 165–169 cm
    • 170–174 cm
    • 175–179 cm
  3. Bar Heights (Frequencies):
    • 150–154 cm: 4 students
    • 155–159 cm: 8 students
    • 160–164 cm: 14 students (This is the tallest bar, indicating the most common height range)
    • 165–169 cm: 13 students
    • 170–174 cm: 8 students
    • 175–179 cm: 3 students
  4. Shape: The histogram is roughly symmetric. The tallest bar is near the center, and the frequencies decrease similarly on both sides.
  5. Center and Spread: The peak (center) is in the 160–164 cm range. The data spreads from 150 cm up to approximately 179 cm, covering a range of about 29 cm.
  6. Total Count: Summing the bar heights: 4 + 8 + 14 + 13 + 8 + 3 = 50 students, matching the given total.

Historical Note: The term "histogram" was coined by statistician Karl Pearson in 1891, combining Greek words for "mast" (histos) and "diagram" (gramma), visualizing the bars as ship masts.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental difference between a histogram and a bar chart, focusing on the type of data each displays and why this leads to a key visual distinction in their appearance.
  2. Why do histograms not have gaps between their bars? Connect your answer to the concept of continuous data.
  3. Describe the characteristics of a right-skewed distribution. Provide an example of a real-world dataset that would likely exhibit this shape, explaining your reasoning.
  4. You are given a histogram of student test scores ranging from 0 to 100. The highest bar is for the interval 80-89, and there's a long, short tail stretching towards the 0-9 interval. Is this distribution symmetric, right-skewed, or left-skewed? Justify your answer.
  5. Imagine you are presented with a histogram of daily temperatures in a city over a year. What steps would you take to interpret this histogram and understand the temperature patterns? What specific information would you look for?
