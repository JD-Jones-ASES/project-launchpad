Evaluating Infographics Critically

Overview

This section teaches you how to critically evaluate infographics, an essential skill because their appealing design can often hide misleading information. You will learn a five-question checklist to spot common flaws such as incorrect numbers, missing sources, disproportionate visuals, incomplete context, and underlying biases, empowering you to become a discerning data detective.

Key Concepts

  • Importance of Critical Evaluation: Infographics, despite their attractive design, can be as misleading as any other data visualization. Learning to read them critically protects you from false information.
  • The Critical Evaluation Checklist: A structured approach using five key questions to assess the reliability and accuracy of an infographic.
  • Verifying Data Sources: Always check if a source is cited and if it's trustworthy. A missing source is a major red flag, indicating that the information might be made up or untraceable.
  • Checking Numerical Accuracy: Percentages in a pie chart must add up to exactly 100%. Comparing numbers across different parts of an infographic helps ensure consistency.
  • Assessing Visual Proportionality: Visual elements (like icon size, bar height, or bubble area) must accurately reflect the data they represent. Disproportionate visuals can exaggerate or downplay information.
  • Identifying Missing Context: Look out for "cherry-picking," where an infographic only shows data that supports a particular viewpoint while omitting contradictory or important background information.
  • Understanding Bias and Agenda: Consider who created the infographic and what their motives might be. An organization promoting its own product or viewpoint may present technically accurate data in a way that serves their agenda, potentially hiding the "full truth."

Important Terms

  • Critical Evaluation: The process of carefully examining and judging the accuracy, reliability, and potential biases of information, especially in visual formats like infographics.
  • Data Source: The origin or provider of the data used in an infographic. It's crucial for verifying the information's credibility.
  • Proportionality: The principle that visual elements in an infographic (e.g., icons, bars, areas) should be sized in direct relation to the numerical values they represent.
  • Cherry-picking: The act of selectively presenting only the data or information that supports a particular argument or conclusion, while ignoring data that might contradict it.
  • Bias: A predisposition or inclination for or against something, which can influence how data is collected, presented, and interpreted, often leading to a skewed perspective.
  • Icon Size Distortion: A common infographic flaw where the size of an icon or visual element is not proportional to the data it represents, leading to an exaggerated or minimized visual impact.
  • Agenda: A plan or motive, often unstated, that influences the creation or presentation of an infographic, particularly in how data is selected and framed to persuade an audience.

Detailed Notes

The Importance of Critical Reading

Infographics are designed to be visually appealing and easy to understand, but this can make them more dangerous if they contain inaccuracies or biases. Their attractive nature can lower your guard, making it crucial to develop critical evaluation skills.

The Critical Evaluation Checklist: 5 Key Questions

Always ask these five questions when reading any infographic:

  1. Is the source cited?

    • Look for footnotes, captions, or small text indicating where the data came from.
    • If no source is provided, the numbers could be made up.
    • If a source is cited, evaluate its trustworthiness (Is it a reputable organization? Do they have expertise in the area?).

  2. Do the numbers add up?

    • For percentages, they must add up to exactly 100%.
    • If multiple charts or sections claim to show the same data, verify that their numbers match.
    • Always perform quick math checks yourself.

  3. Are the visuals proportional to the data?

    • Check if icons, bar heights, or bubble areas accurately reflect the values they represent.
    • For example, if one value is twice another, its corresponding visual should be twice as big (in area, not just height/width). Disproportionate visuals can mislead by over-emphasizing or under-representing data.

  4. Is important context missing?

    • Determine if the infographic presents the full picture or if it's "cherry-picking" data.
    • This might involve showing only favorable data points while ignoring less favorable ones, or presenting a trend without its historical context.

  5. What story is being told — and who benefits?

    • Identify the creator of the infographic (e.g., a company, a political group, a non-profit).
    • Consider their potential agenda or motivation. While bias doesn't automatically mean an infographic is wrong, it means you should scrutinize the information more carefully for selective presentation.

Worked Example: The Flawed Infographic (School Lunch Preferences)

Let's analyze a hypothetical infographic about "School Lunch Preferences at Westview Middle School" to apply our checklist.

The Infographic's Content:

  • Section 1 — “What Students Choose”:
    • Giant pizza slice icon: "Pizza: 45%"
    • Medium burger icon: "Burger: 35%"
    • Tiny salad icon: "Salad: 30%"
  • Section 2 — “The Numbers”: Pie chart with slices labeled: Pizza 45%, Burger 35%, Salad 30%.
  • Section 3 — “Conclusion”: Text stating: "Students overwhelmingly prefer hot food options. Our school should expand the pizza and burger menu."

Applying the Checklist (Identifying Flaws):

  1. Problem 1 — Numbers don't add to 100%:

    • $45% + 35% + 30% = 110%$. This is mathematically impossible for a single-choice survey where percentages should total 100%. This could mean the data was invented or misinterpreted (e.g., multiple choices allowed but presented as exclusive).

  2. Problem 2 — No source cited:

    • There is no information about who conducted the survey, when, or how many students participated. Without a source, the data is unverifiable and lacks credibility. The "conclusion" based on uncited data is therefore unreliable.

  3. Problem 3 — Icon size distortion:

    • The pizza icon is drawn significantly larger (about three times the height) than the salad icon. However, 45% is only 1.5 times larger than 30% (45/30 = 1.5). This visual exaggeration makes pizza appear far more dominant than the numbers actually suggest, misleading the reader. This is a common "pictograph" trick.

The Missing Source Trap

An infographic lacking a data source should immediately raise suspicion. Without a source, you cannot verify the information, and it suggests the designer may not want you to. Always prioritize infographics that cite real, credible sources.

Building Your Own Critical Eye: The "Who Benefits?" Question

As you practice critical evaluation, you'll become faster at spotting problems. A final, crucial question to always ask is: Who made this, and what do they gain if you believe it? Understanding the creator's motivation helps you understand if the data is being presented strategically to promote a certain view, even if technically accurate. It helps you distinguish between information that is simply true and information that is the whole truth.

Review Questions

  1. Why is it especially important to critically evaluate infographics, even though they are often designed to be clear and appealing?
  2. List and explain at least three of the five questions from the Critical Evaluation Checklist. Provide an example of a potential problem each question helps to identify.
  3. Imagine you see an infographic claiming "80% of students prefer chocolate milk," but the graphic shows a chocolate milk carton that is ten times larger than the regular milk carton, which is labeled "20%." What specific type of flaw is this, and how does it mislead the reader?
  4. Why is a missing data source a significant red flag when evaluating an infographic? What information should a good data source ideally include?
  5. Consider an infographic created by a company that sells bottled water, promoting the benefits of hydration. How might the "Who benefits?" question help you critically analyze this infographic, even if all the data presented is technically accurate?
