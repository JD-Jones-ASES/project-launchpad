Donut Charts: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces donut charts, a popular data visualization tool similar to pie charts but with a central hole. You will learn how donut charts differ from pie charts, understand their advantages (such as providing space for key information), and master the steps to accurately read and interpret data presented in them. The guide also covers common misconceptions and best practices for using donut charts effectively.

Key Concepts

  • Donut Chart Definition: A circular chart with a hole in the center, where the ring is divided into segments representing proportions of a whole, just like a pie chart.
  • Difference from Pie Charts: The primary distinction is the central hole, which is a design choice and does not represent missing data.
  • Purpose of the Center Hole: The hole is often used to display a key number, total, percentage, or label, providing immediate context or a summary metric for the chart.
  • Design Advantages: Many designers find donut charts visually cleaner, more modern, and less cluttered than filled pie charts, making them popular in dashboards and infographics.
  • Same Rules as Pie Charts: Donut charts follow the same principles as pie charts:
    • All segments must sum to 100% of the total.
    • They are best used with six or fewer categories for readability.
    • Avoid 3D effects, as they can distort perception of proportions.
  • Reading a Donut Chart: Involves interpreting the title, the central number/label, segment sizes, legends, and percentages to understand the data distribution.

Important Terms

  • Donut Chart: A circular data visualization chart with a central cutout, displaying proportional data in its segments.
  • Segment: Each colored section of a donut chart (or pie chart) that represents a specific category's proportion of the whole.
  • Legend: A key that identifies what each color or pattern in a chart represents, linking it to a specific data category.
  • Dashboard: A visual display of key performance indicators (KPIs) and data, often interactive, used to monitor and analyze information at a glance. Donut charts are frequently used in dashboards.
  • Infographic: A visual representation of information or data, designed to present complex information quickly and clearly. Donut charts are common components of infographics.

Detailed Notes

What is a Donut Chart?

A donut chart is essentially a pie chart's "close cousin." It's a circular chart that presents data as proportions of a whole, but with a significant difference: it has a hole in the middle. The outer ring is divided into colored segments, with each segment representing a category's contribution to the total. The sum of all segments must always equal 100%.

Why Choose a Donut Chart Over a Pie Chart?

Donut charts have gained popularity, especially in digital dashboards and infographics, for several reasons:

  1. Center Space for Key Information: The most compelling reason is the empty space in the middle. This "hole" is not empty data; it's a design feature designed to be functional. Designers often use it to display a crucial piece of information, such as:

    • Total Value: The sum of all the data represented by the segments (e.g., "Total Sales: $1,250").
    • Key Percentage: A significant percentage relevant to the chart, perhaps for the largest segment or a target (e.g., "65% Complete").
    • Label/Title: A descriptive label that immediately tells the reader what the chart represents (e.g., "Monthly Budget"). This central information helps readers quickly grasp the chart's main takeaway without needing to look at a legend or calculate sums.

  2. Cleaner Visual Appearance: Many designers find that donut charts look more modern, sleeker, and less visually cluttered than traditional, filled pie charts. The open center can make the chart feel lighter and integrate better into complex dashboard layouts.

  3. Same Data, Same Rules: Despite the visual difference, donut charts function identically to pie charts in terms of data representation. This means:

    • Segments Sum to 100%: Each segment represents a proportion, and collectively, they must add up to the total (100%).
    • Limited Categories: For optimal readability, it's best to use six or fewer categories. Too many segments can make the chart difficult to interpret, as small segments become hard to distinguish and compare.
    • Avoid 3D Effects: Just like with pie charts, adding a 3D effect to a donut chart can distort the perceived size of segments, making it harder to accurately compare proportions. Stick to 2D representations.

Common Misconceptions

  • The hole means missing data: It's important to remember that the hole in a donut chart is a purely aesthetic and functional design choice. It does not mean that some data is missing or excluded from the chart. All the data is contained within the ring's segments.
  • Donut charts are always better: While popular, donut charts aren't universally superior to pie charts. The choice depends on context. Donut charts shine when there's a valuable single number or label to display in the center. If no such key metric is needed, a pie chart might be perfectly adequate.

How to Read a Donut Chart

Reading a donut chart is similar to reading a pie chart, with an added step for the center:

  1. Read the Title: Understand what the entire chart represents (the "whole").
  2. Check the Center: Look for any number or label in the middle. What key information does it provide? This often gives immediate context or the total value.
  3. Read the Legend or Segment Labels: Identify what each colored segment represents. If percentages are labeled directly on segments, read those.
  4. Find Largest and Smallest Segments: Quickly identify which categories have the greatest and smallest proportions by visually comparing segment sizes.
  5. Verify Percentages: Mentally or explicitly confirm that all the segment percentages add up to 100%. This ensures you are looking at a complete data set.
  6. Compare Segments: Analyze the relative sizes of different segments to understand the distribution of data and draw conclusions.

Worked Example: Monthly Spending

Let's apply these steps to an example of a middle school student's monthly allowance. The center shows the total monthly allowance (e.g., $100). The segments represent spending categories: Food (35%), Entertainment (20%), Savings (20%), and Other (10%).

  • Reading the chart:

    • The total monthly allowance is stated in the center (e.g., $100).
    • Food (35%) is the largest segment, accounting for more than a third of the allowance.
    • Entertainment (20%) and Savings (20%) are equal in proportion, making them the next largest categories.
    • Other (10%) is the smallest segment represented.
    • The student allocates 20% of their allowance to savings. (Note: For a complete donut chart, all segment percentages must add up to 100%. If these were the only categories, they would sum to 85%, indicating there might be other unlisted categories or that this example focuses on key proportions rather than a complete breakdown.)

  • Using percentages to find dollar amounts (assuming a $100 total allowance):

    • Food: 35% of $100 = 0.35 * $100 = $35
    • Entertainment: 20% of $100 = 0.20 * $100 = $20
    • Savings: 20% of $100 = 0.20 * $100 = $20
    • Other: 10% of $100 = 0.10 * $100 = $10

A Bit of History

The original pie chart was invented over 200 years ago by William Playfair in 1801. He used it to illustrate land area proportions in the Turkish Empire. The donut chart, with its central hole, emerged much later, gaining widespread popularity with the advent of computer graphics software, which made creating such a "hole" much easier.
