Study Guide: Flow Diagrams and Sankey Charts

Overview

This section introduces flow diagrams, specifically focusing on Sankey charts, as powerful tools for visualizing how quantities move from one point to another. It emphasizes that the width of the "ribbons" or "arrows" in these charts is the key element representing the quantity of flow, rather than their length. The guide covers how to read and interpret Sankey charts, provides real-world examples, and highlights their historical significance.

Key Concepts

  • Visualizing Flow: Sankey charts are designed to show the movement or transfer of a quantity (like energy, money, people, or materials) from a source to one or more destinations.
  • Width Represents Quantity: The fundamental principle of a Sankey chart is that the width of the ribbons or arrows is directly proportional to the quantity of the flow it represents. A wider ribbon means a larger quantity.
  • Sources and Destinations: Charts typically show quantities originating from one or more "sources" (often on the left) and distributing to one or more "destinations" (often on the right).
  • Additivity of Flows: Just like a pie chart, the sum of all flows leaving a source (or arriving at a destination, if there's no loss) should equal the total quantity of the source.
  • Reading Labels: Good Sankey charts include labels with percentages or specific amounts on each flow, which are crucial for precise interpretation.
  • Aesthetic Order: The vertical order or position of ribbons is often for aesthetic clarity and does not necessarily indicate rank or importance; always refer to width and labels for data.
  • Applications: Sankey charts are widely used in various fields, including energy consumption, climate science (carbon footprints), business analytics (customer journeys), and supply chain management.
  • Historical Significance: Charles Joseph Minard pioneered this visualization technique with his famous map of Napoleon's 1812 Russian campaign, illustrating army size changes over geography and time.
  • Design Considerations: While powerful, Sankey charts can become cluttered and difficult to read if too many flows are represented simultaneously. Simplicity usually leads to clarity.

Important Terms

  • Sankey Chart (or Sankey Diagram/Flow Diagram): A type of chart that uses ribbons or arrows of varying width to visually represent how a quantity flows from one or more sources to one or more destinations. The width of each ribbon is proportional to the quantity it represents.
  • Flow Diagram: A general term for any chart that shows the movement or transfer of a quantity from one category or state to another. Sankey charts are a prominent type of flow diagram, but the term also encompasses process charts, decision trees, and supply chain diagrams.
  • Source: The origin point(s) of the quantity being tracked in a Sankey chart.
  • Destination: The end point(s) or categories where the quantity being tracked in a Sankey chart is distributed or consumed.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Flow Diagrams and Sankey Charts

  • Analogy: Think of a river splitting into smaller streams; the wider the stream, the more water it carries. Sankey charts apply this concept to quantities like energy, money, or materials.
  • Purpose: To visually represent the flow and distribution of a quantity.

Defining Sankey Chart and Flow Diagram

  • Sankey Chart:
    • Uses ribbons or arrows.
    • Width of the ribbon is directly proportional to the quantity flowing.
    • Shows movement from a source to one or more destinations.
  • Flow Diagram:
    • A broader category of charts that show movement or transfer.
    • Sankey charts are the most common type.
    • Other examples: process charts, decision trees, supply chain diagrams.

The Most Important Rule: Reading the Width

  • Crucial Point: Always read the width of the ribbon, not its length or position, to understand the data value.
  • A short, wide ribbon indicates a larger quantity than a long, thin one.
  • Always check the labels for specific percentages or amounts.

How to Read a Sankey Chart

  1. Identify Sources: Locate where the total quantity originates, typically on the left side of the chart. The total width here represents 100% of the initial flow.
  2. Identify Destinations: Find where the quantity ends up, typically on the right side. Each destination receives a portion of the total flow.
  3. Read Ribbon Widths: Visually compare the widths of the ribbons to quickly identify which destinations receive the largest or smallest shares of the flow.
  4. Read Labels: Pay close attention to the percentages or amounts labeled on each ribbon for precise data interpretation.
  5. Check for Additivity: Ensure that the sum of the flows leaving a source (or arriving at destinations, if applicable) equals the total initial quantity (e.g., 100%).

Worked Example: School Energy Usage

  • Scenario: A Sankey chart showing how electricity supplied to a school building is distributed among different uses.
  • Source: Electricity Input (100%).
  • Destinations & Shares:
    • Heating, Ventilation & Air Conditioning (HVAC): 35% (widest ribbon, highest usage).
    • Lighting: 30% (second widest).
    • Computers & Technology: 25%.
    • Other (cafeteria, elevators, etc.): 10% (thinnest ribbon, lowest usage).
  • Verification: 35% + 30% + 25% + 10% = 100%.
  • Key Insight: HVAC consumes over one-third of the school's electricity, suggesting that improvements in this area could significantly reduce energy costs.

Where Sankey Charts Are Used (Real-World Applications)

  • Energy: Visualizing national energy consumption, from sources (coal, oil) to end uses (electricity generation, transportation). (e.g., U.S. Energy Information Administration).
  • Climate Science: Showing carbon footprints, detailing greenhouse gas emissions from various sectors like transportation, agriculture, and industry.
  • Business: Tracking customer journeys on websites (flow from homepage to product pages to checkout) or analyzing where customers drop off.
  • Supply Chains: Illustrating the movement of raw materials through manufacturing processes to finished products.

Historical Context: Charles Joseph Minard

  • Pioneer: French civil engineer (1781–1870) who created one of the most celebrated data visualizations.
  • Napoleon's March: His famous map depicted Napoleon's disastrous 1812 campaign to Moscow.
    • Showed the army's march (beige) and retreat (black).
    • Ribbon Width: Represented the number of soldiers, dramatically shrinking from 422,000 to 10,000.
    • Integrated geography, time, direction, army size, and temperature data.
  • Legacy: Minard's map is considered a precursor to modern Sankey diagrams and a masterpiece of statistical graphics, influencing how we visualize flow data today.

Design Caution: Too Many Flows

  • The "Spaghetti Plate" Problem: Sankey charts are effective with a moderate number of flows (e.g., 3-8).
  • Issue: Too many flows (e.g., 20-30) lead to tangled, crossing ribbons, making the chart illegible.
  • Solution: Designers should simplify, group flows, or break the data into multiple, smaller charts for clarity.

Review Questions

  1. What is the primary purpose of a Sankey chart, and what is the most important visual element to pay attention to when interpreting one?
  2. Explain the difference between a "Sankey Chart" and a broader "Flow Diagram." Provide an example of each.
  3. Imagine a company uses a Sankey chart to show how its annual budget of $1,000,000 is spent. If the "Marketing" ribbon is half as wide as the "Operations" ribbon, and "Operations" accounts for 40% of the budget, how much money is spent on "Marketing"?
  4. Describe the steps you would take to accurately read and understand a Sankey chart, from identifying the source to checking the total flows.
  5. Why is Charles Joseph Minard's map of Napoleon's 1812 campaign considered an important historical example related to Sankey charts? What key aspects did his map illustrate effectively?
