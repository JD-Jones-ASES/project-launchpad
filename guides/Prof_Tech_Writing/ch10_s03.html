The Problem-Solution Structure: A Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental "problem-solution structure" as a highly effective organizational pattern for proposals, pitches, and other forms of persuasive communication. It explains why this structure resonates with decision-makers and details how to craft compelling problem statements and proposed solutions, ensuring a clear connection between the two. The section also covers the condensed version, the elevator pitch, and links the structure to persuasive writing in general.

Key Concepts

  • Problem-Solution Structure: A foundational organizational pattern used in proposals, advertisements, speeches, and business plans. It first identifies a problem, then presents a plan to solve it, mirroring how decision-makers evaluate proposals.
  • The Five Questions Every Proposal Must Answer: These are: What is the problem? Why does it matter? What do you propose to do? Why will your solution work? What will it cost and how long will it take? Leaving any unanswered risks rejection.
  • Strong Problem Statement: This critical opening identifies a specific problem, provides concrete evidence (data, surveys, reports) to prove it's real, and explains the significant consequences if the problem is not addressed. It aims to grab the reader's attention and establish urgency.
  • Strong Proposed Solution: This section clearly states the solution, breaks it into specific, actionable steps, explains how each step addresses the identified problem, assigns responsibility, and anticipates potential objections.
  • Connecting Problem to Solution: A crucial aspect of effective proposals where every element of the solution explicitly traces back to a specific aspect of the problem. Avoiding "disconnected solutions" ensures the reader understands how the proposed actions resolve the established issues.
  • Scope of Work: A defined boundary within a proposed solution that specifies exactly what tasks, deliverables, and areas a project will and will not include, preventing misunderstandings and managing expectations.
  • Elevator Pitch: A condensed, persuasive summary of a proposal, deliverable in about 60 seconds. It follows the problem-solution structure, answering what the problem is, what the solution is, and what is needed from the listener.

Important Terms

  • Problem-Solution Structure: An organizational pattern that first establishes a problem and then presents a plan to solve it.
  • Problem Statement: The initial part of a proposal that identifies a specific problem, provides evidence for its existence, and explains its consequences.
  • Proposed Solution: The section of a proposal that details the plan to address the identified problem, outlining specific actions, responsibilities, and anticipated outcomes.
  • Scope of Work: The specific tasks, deliverables, and boundaries of a proposed project, clearly defining what is included and excluded.
  • Elevator Pitch: A brief, persuasive summary of a proposal that can be delivered in approximately 60 seconds, condensing the problem-solution structure.

Detailed Notes

The Problem-Solution Structure: A Fundamental Approach

  • Core Idea: This structure is omnipresent in persuasive communication (e.g., ads, political speeches, business plans). It directly addresses an issue and offers a way forward.
  • How it Works: It aligns with the natural thought process of decision-makers. They first need to agree a problem exists, then understand its significance, and finally believe a proposed solution is viable and effective before committing resources.
  • Logical Progression: Each part of a proposal built on this structure answers the reader's next logical question.

The Five Questions Every Proposal Must Answer

For a proposal to be approved, it must thoroughly address these core questions:

  1. What is the problem? (Problem Statement): Clearly define the issue.
  2. Why does it matter? (Significance / Impact): Explain the consequences and why it needs attention.
  3. What do you propose to do? (Proposed Solution): Detail your plan.
  4. Why will your solution work? (Evidence / Qualifications): Provide proof, expertise, or reasons for effectiveness.
  5. What will it cost and how long will it take? (Budget / Timeline): Outline resources and time commitment.
  • Consequence of Omission: Leaving any of these questions unanswered provides the reader with a reason to reject the proposal.

Writing the Problem Statement

This is your opportunity to capture the reader's attention and convince them of the problem's reality and importance.

  • Three Key Elements of a Strong Problem Statement:
    1. Identifies the problem specifically: Avoid vague language. Use concrete details (e.g., "east parking lot floods," "12 student vehicles damaged per year").
    2. Provides evidence: Back up your claims with data, surveys, incident reports, expert opinions, or other verifiable facts. Don't rely on personal complaints.
    3. Explains the consequences: Articulate what will happen if the problem persists. Quantify impact in terms of money, time, safety, morale, or affected parties.
  • Example: Vague vs. Specific Problem Statement:
    • Vague: Relies on general feelings ("not very good," "unhappy").
    • Specific: Uses precise data (survey of 847 students, percentages for concerns, $48,000 lost revenue, outdated contract). Specific data allows decision-makers to act.
  • Writing Tip: Lead with Your Strongest Evidence: Start your problem statement with the most impactful data point (survey results, dollar figures, safety incidents) to immediately convey the severity of the problem.

Writing the Proposed Solution

Once the problem is established, the reader is ready for your answer—the heart of your proposal.

  • Characteristics of a Strong Proposed Solution:
    1. States the solution clearly in the first sentence: Get straight to the point.
    2. Breaks the solution into specific, actionable steps: Detail what will be done.
    3. Explains how each step addresses the problem: Create explicit links back to the issues.
    4. Identifies who will be responsible for each part: Assign roles and accountability.
    5. Anticipates and addresses potential objections: Proactively counter doubts.
  • Definition: Scope of Work: A crucial statement that defines the specific tasks, deliverables, and boundaries of the project. It clarifies what is included and not included, preventing misunderstandings and managing expectations.
    • Example: "This proposal covers X, but not Y or Z."
  • Example: Proposed Solution — Cafeteria Improvement Pilot:
    • Demonstrates how to structure a solution with clear components, timeframes, specific actions, and responsible parties.
    • Includes a clear Scope statement to manage expectations.

Connecting Problem to Solution

This is where the proposal's strength truly shines, showing a logical progression from issue to resolution.

  • Crucial Link: Every element of your solution must be directly traceable back to a specific aspect of the problem you identified.
  • Common Mistake: The Disconnected Solution: Describing a problem in detail but then proposing a solution that doesn't fully address all the aspects of that problem. This leaves the reader with lingering questions.
  • Fix: After drafting, review your solution against your problem statement. Use explicit connecting phrases (e.g., "To address the 31% of students who cited long wait times...") to make the link undeniable.

The Elevator Pitch: Your Proposal in 60 Seconds

When time is limited, an elevator pitch delivers your proposal quickly and persuasively.

  • Definition: A brief, compelling summary of your proposal, designed to be delivered in approximately 60 seconds (the length of a short elevator ride).
  • Structure: It follows the same problem-solution framework but in a highly compressed format.
  • Three Questions an Effective Elevator Pitch Answers:
    1. What is the problem?
    2. What is your solution?
    3. What do you need from the listener?
  • Example: After-School Tutoring Program: This example effectively includes a specific problem, a concrete solution, qualifications, a modest "ask," and a clear call to action, all within a concise statement.

Writing Connection: Problem-Solution in Persuasive Essays

The problem-solution structure is not just for proposals; it's a powerful framework for persuasive essays, often called "thesis-support."

  • Application:
    • Identify the problem (background/context for your argument).
    • Explain its significance (why your argument matters).
    • Propose your solution/thesis (your main claim).
    • Support your solution with evidence (why your claim is valid and will work).
  • Benefit: Using this framework makes arguments sharper, more logical, and ultimately more convincing.

  2. A strong problem statement does three specific things. Identify and explain each, providing a brief original example for how a student might apply it to a problem at their school.
  3. What is the "scope of work" within a proposed solution, and why is it crucial for managing expectations and preventing misunderstandings? Give an example of a clear scope statement for a hypothetical project.
  4. Describe the "disconnected solution" common mistake. How can a proposal writer ensure their solution is directly and explicitly linked to every aspect of the problem statement?
  5. Imagine you need to present an elevator pitch for a new "No-Plastic-Bottles" initiative for your school to the principal. What are the three key questions your pitch must answer, and what specific types of information would you include for each question to make your pitch effective in 60 seconds?
