Study Guide: Action Verbs and Results for Strong Resumes

Overview

This section highlights the critical difference between a weak and a strong resume: how experience is described. Strong resumes go beyond merely listing job duties; they use powerful action verbs and quantified results to articulate specific accomplishments and the impact of the applicant's work. The core principle is to "show, don't tell," making the applicant's contributions clear, concrete, and compelling to potential employers.

Key Concepts

  • Show, Don't Tell: Instead of stating what your job title was or what you were responsible for, a strong resume demonstrates what you did and what resulted from your actions. This principle transforms vague descriptions into impactful statements of accomplishment.
  • The Problem with Passive Descriptions: Phrases like "Responsible for," "Duties included," or "Helped with" are considered "resume deadwood." They describe a role or general responsibility rather than specific actions or achievements, failing to communicate value effectively.
  • Power Verbs for Resumes: These are strong, specific action verbs (e.g., "Led," "Designed," "Analyzed") that make your resume descriptions vivid and concrete. Using a variety of these verbs across different bullet points showcases a wider range of skills and responsibilities.
  • Quantifying Your Results: Numbers are essential for transforming vague claims into credible evidence. Attaching measurable terms (volume, frequency, money, percentages, time, scale) to your accomplishments makes them more impactful and easier for employers to grasp your contributions.
  • The Quantification Test: For every bullet point, ask "How many? How much? How often? How fast? Compared to what?" If you can answer, integrate that numerical data to strengthen your statement.
  • Turning Any Experience into Resume Language: Even seemingly informal experiences (babysitting, school clubs, volunteer work, family responsibilities) can be translated into professional resume language by focusing on action verbs and quantifiable results.
  • Vary Your Verbs: To avoid monotony and demonstrate a broader skill set, it's important not to start every bullet point with the same action verb.
  • Do Not Exaggerate or Fabricate: While presenting your experience in the best light is encouraged, lying or fabricating details on a resume can lead to serious consequences, including rejection or termination. Always be truthful, but focus on powerfully translating your real experiences.
  • Action Verbs in College Applications: The "show, don't tell" principle extends beyond resumes, making college essays more compelling by demonstrating traits through specific actions and results rather than just stating them.

Important Terms

  • Action Verb: A specific, concrete verb used to describe an applicant's direct involvement and what they did (e.g., organized, designed, led).
  • Quantify: To express a result or accomplishment using numbers, percentages, dollar amounts, or other measurable terms to provide concrete evidence of impact.
  • Resume Deadwood: Vague or passive phrases that take up space on a resume without communicating specific value or accomplishments (e.g., responsible for, duties included, helped with).
  • Passive Description: A statement that describes a general role or responsibility without detailing the specific actions taken or results achieved.
  • Active Accomplishment: A statement that clearly describes a specific action performed by the applicant and its measurable outcome or impact, usually starting with an action verb.
  • Power Verb: A strong, vivid action verb chosen to make resume descriptions more impactful, specific, and compelling.

Detailed Notes

The Core Difference: Action Verbs and Results

The most significant factor distinguishing a weak resume from a strong one is how an applicant's experience is presented. Weak resumes simply list job duties or responsibilities. Strong resumes, conversely, highlight what the applicant did and the impact or results of those actions.

Show, Don't Tell

Every bullet point on a strong resume should:

  1. Begin with a strong action verb that describes a specific, concrete action.
  2. Whenever possible, include a quantified result (using numbers, percentages, dollar amounts, etc.).
    • Example: Instead of "Responsible for cash register," write, "Processed an average of 85 transactions per shift with 99.8% accuracy."

The Problem with Passive Descriptions

Common resume phrases that detract from impact include:

  • "Responsible for customer service"
  • "Duties included stocking shelves"
  • "Helped with various tasks"
  • "Worked in the kitchen"
  • "Was in charge of social media" These phrases describe a role or position ("where you stood") rather than specific accomplishments ("what you did"). They are considered "resume deadwood" because they take up space without communicating value.

Before & After Example:

  • Passive Role: "Responsible for taking orders and handling the cash register at a busy restaurant."
  • Active Accomplishment: "Processed 60+ customer orders per shift and balanced a cash drawer averaging $1,200 daily with zero shortages over six months."

Power Verbs for Resumes

Strong action verbs make your resume specific and vivid. They are typically categorized by the type of work they describe:

Category Action Verbs
Leadership Led, directed, managed, supervised, coordinated, organized, delegated, mentored, facilitated, oversaw
Communication Presented, wrote, authored, edited, published, corresponded, negotiated, persuaded, promoted, reported
Problem-Solving Resolved, analyzed, diagnosed, improved, streamlined, redesigned, troubleshot, identified, addressed, corrected
Creation Created, designed, developed, built, launched, established, initiated, produced, implemented, introduced
Support Assisted, supported, helped, served, contributed, collaborated, maintained, provided, ensured, delivered
Analysis Researched, evaluated, assessed, calculated, measured, surveyed, tested, examined, reviewed, audited

Writing Tip: Vary Your Verbs: Avoid starting multiple bullet points with the same verb. Diversify your action verbs to demonstrate the full range of your skills and responsibilities. For example, instead of "Managed inventory, Managed schedule, Managed team," try "Tracked inventory levels, Coordinated weekly schedules for 8 employees, Led a team of 4 during weekend shifts."

Quantifying Your Results

Numbers are the most powerful tool on a resume because they transform vague claims into concrete, credible evidence. Always strive to include numbers whenever possible.

The Quantification Test

For every bullet point, ask yourself: "How many? How much? How often? How fast? Compared to what?" If you can answer any of these questions, incorporate that number into your statement.

Types of Numbers to Include:

  • Volume: How many customers, transactions, items, or tasks?
    • Example: "Served 40+ customers per shift."
  • Frequency: How often did something occur?
    • Example: "Prepared weekly inventory reports."
  • Money: Dollar amounts handled, saved, or earned.
    • Example: "Managed a cash drawer averaging $800 daily."
  • Percentages: Improvement, accuracy, or growth.
    • Example: "Increased social media followers by 45% in three months."
  • Time: Duration, speed, or consistency.
    • Example: "Maintained perfect attendance over 14 months."
  • Scale: Team size, event attendance, or scope of a project.
    • Example: "Organized a fundraiser attended by 150 community members."

Before & After Examples:

  • Vague vs. Quantified:
    • Vague: "Helped increase social media presence for the school newspaper."
    • Quantified: "Grew the school newspaper's Instagram following from 340 to 1,200 followers in four months by creating a weekly 'Behind the Headlines' story series."
  • Listing Duties vs. Showing Impact:
    • Duties: "Duties included tutoring students in math after school."
    • Impact: "Tutored 8 students weekly in Algebra I, helping 6 of 8 improve their grades by at least one letter grade within one semester."

Turning Any Experience into Resume Language

Many people, especially students, believe they lack "resume-worthy" experience. This is rarely true. School activities, volunteer work, personal projects, and even family responsibilities can be framed professionally using action verbs and quantified results.

Casual Description vs. Resume Language Example:

  • Casual: "I babysit my neighbor's kids a couple times a week. I make them snacks and help with homework and stuff."
  • Resume Language: "Provided after-school childcare for two children (ages 6 and 9) three days per week, including meal preparation, homework supervision, and activity coordination. Maintained a consistent 18-month schedule with zero cancellations."

Common Student Experiences and Professional Framing:

What You Did How to Write It
"I was on student council" Served on a 12-member student government body, voting on budget allocations totaling $15,000 annually
"I helped at my parents' restaurant" Assisted with front-of-house operations during peak hours, seating parties of 2–8 and managing a 20-minute average wait time
"I ran the class Instagram" Managed a school organization's social media account, creating 3+ posts per week and growing engagement by 60%
"I played varsity soccer" Committed 15+ hours weekly to varsity athletics, balancing practice, travel, and competition with a 3.5 GPA
"I did community service hours" Completed 80 hours of community service at the Springfield Food Bank, sorting and distributing donations to 200+ families monthly

Common Mistake: Exaggerating or Fabricating: It's crucial to differentiate between presenting your experience in the best possible light and outright lying. Exaggeration (e.g., saying you "led" when you only participated) and fabrication (listing a job you never held) are serious missteps that can lead to application rejection or even termination after hiring. Employers often verify claims. Focus on accurately translating your actual experiences with strong verbs and real numbers.

Writing Connection: Action Verbs in College Applications: The "show, don't tell" principle is equally vital for college application essays. Admissions officers seek essays that demonstrate qualities like "hard work" or "passion" through specific actions and results, rather than just stating them. For example, instead of writing "I am dedicated to community service," describe the specific volunteer tasks you performed and the tangible impact, like the number of families served.
