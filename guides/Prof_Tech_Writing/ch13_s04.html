Study Guide: Recommendation Reports

Overview

Recommendation reports are powerful documents that go beyond simply presenting data and analysis; they propose specific actions based on those insights. Essential for decision-making within any organization, these reports follow a logical structure, building from gathered evidence (findings) to interpretation (conclusions) and culminating in actionable steps (recommendations).

Key Concepts

  • Purpose of Recommendation Reports: Unlike analytical reports that explain data, recommendation reports tell the reader what to do with that data by proposing specific actions.
  • Logical Chain Structure: Recommendation reports are built like a staircase, where each section (findings, analysis, conclusions, recommendations) logically supports the next, ensuring coherence and credibility.
  • Comprehensive Report Components: They include all elements of an analytical report (data, analysis, conclusions) plus a final section dedicated to proposing specific actions.
  • Credible Methodology: A strong methodology section is crucial for establishing the trustworthiness of the report's findings by detailing how information was collected and analyzed, including any limitations.
  • Qualities of Effective Recommendations: Recommendations must be specific, supported by evidence, practical (considering feasibility and resources), and often prioritized if multiple actions are proposed.

Important Terms

  • Recommendation Report: A type of analytical report that not only presents data and analysis but also proposes specific actions based on the conclusions.
  • Executive Summary: A concise, one-page overview of the entire report, placed at the beginning, that includes the main recommendation. It is typically written last.
  • Introduction: The initial section that sets the stage for the report, outlining its purpose, scope, background, and methodology.
  • Methodology: The section that explains how the information was gathered and analyzed, including sources, collection methods, analysis techniques, and limitations.
  • Findings: The raw data, facts, observations, and objective information collected during the research phase, often organized by criteria or topic.
  • Analysis / Conclusions: The section where the findings are interpreted, explaining what the data means, identifying patterns, causes, and significance.
  • Recommendations: The core of the report, presenting specific, actionable steps or solutions derived directly from the conclusions, always with justification.
  • Appendices: Supplementary materials placed at the end of the report, such as raw data, surveys, detailed tables, or supporting documents.
  • Logical Progression/Chain: The sequential flow of ideas and information through the report, where each section builds upon and is supported by the preceding ones.
  • Specific (Recommendation): A recommendation that is precise, clear, and actionable, avoiding vague language.
  • Supported (Recommendation): A recommendation that is directly backed by evidence from the report's findings and conclusions.
  • Practical (Recommendation): A recommendation that is realistic and feasible to implement, considering factors like cost, time, and resources.
  • Prioritized (Recommendation): When multiple recommendations are given, they are ranked by importance or urgency.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Recommendation Reports

A recommendation report is a critical type of organizational document. While an analytical report explains what the data means, a recommendation report takes the crucial next step: it tells the reader what to do about it. These reports are instrumental in shaping decisions because they directly propose actions based on thorough analysis. They encompass all the elements of an analytical report—data, analysis, and conclusions—and add a final, action-oriented section: the recommendations. It is vital that these recommendations logically derive from the analysis; any disconnect signals a flaw in either the analysis or the proposed actions.

The Full Structure of a Recommendation Report

A recommendation report follows a carefully constructed "logical chain" or "staircase" where each part supports the next. Skipping a step can break the entire chain of credibility. The standard structure includes:

  1. Title Page: Contains the report title, author's name, date, and the recipient.
  2. Executive Summary: This is a one-page, high-level overview of the entire report, including the main recommendation. Although it appears first, it should be written last to accurately summarize all content.
  3. Introduction: Provides context for the report, detailing its purpose, scope (what it covers), relevant background information, and the methodology used to gather information.
  4. Findings: This section presents the objective data, facts, and observations collected during the research. It should be organized logically, perhaps by criteria or topic, to facilitate understanding.
  5. Analysis / Conclusions: Here, the findings are interpreted. This section explains what the data means, identifies patterns, explores causes, and discusses the significance of the observations.
  6. Recommendations: This is the action section. It proposes specific, justified actions that directly stem from the conclusions.
  7. Appendices: Contains any supporting materials that are too detailed or lengthy for the main body, such as raw survey data, interview transcripts, detailed tables, or extensive background documents.

This progression ensures that you first gather data (findings), then interpret that data (conclusions), and finally propose actions based on your interpretations (recommendations).

Writing the Methodology Section

The methodology section is essential for establishing the credibility and trustworthiness of your report. It allows readers to understand how your information was collected and analyzed, enabling them to judge the validity of your findings. A robust methodology section should answer the following questions:

  • What sources did you use? (e.g., surveys, interviews, published research, direct observation, experiments, existing reports)
  • How did you collect the data? (e.g., "An online survey sent to 150 employees," "Interviews with 8 department heads," "Analysis of three years of sales records")
  • How did you analyze the data? (e.g., "Calculated averages," "Compared percentages," "Looked for patterns over time," "Sorted by category")
  • What are the limitations? (Honest acknowledgment of factors that might affect the reliability or generalizability of the findings, e.g., low survey response rate, limited sample size).

Example: Weak vs. Strong Methodology

  • Weak: "We asked some students what they thought about the cafeteria and then looked at some data. Based on what people said, we came to our conclusions." (Lacks detail, subjective)
  • Strong: "We distributed an anonymous online survey to all 412 students in grades 9–12 during homeroom on October 15. The survey included 10 multiple-choice questions and 2 open-response questions about cafeteria food quality, variety, and pricing. We received 287 responses (69.7% response rate). We also analyzed the cafeteria's purchasing records for the past two academic years, provided by the food services director." (Detailed, quantifiable, objective)

Writing Recommendations

Recommendations are the core of a recommendation report – they are the calls to action. To be effective, each recommendation must possess four key qualities:

  1. Specific: Avoid vague language. Recommendations should be clear, precise, and actionable.
    • Vague: "Reduce paper use."
    • Specific: "Switch to double-sided default printing on all shared printers by January 15."
  2. Supported: Every recommendation must directly connect back to your findings and conclusions. If your data does not provide evidence for a recommendation, it should not be included.
  3. Practical: Consider the feasibility of implementation. This includes evaluating costs, necessary timelines, and available resources. Recommendations should be realistic.
    • Impractical: "Rebuild the entire school."
    • Practical: "Add two vegetarian options to the lunch menu within the current food budget."
  4. Prioritized: If you have multiple recommendations, rank them by importance, urgency, or logical sequence. This helps guide decision-makers on where to focus first.

Example: Unsupported vs. Supported Recommendation

  • Unsupported: "We recommend the school switch to Solar Vendor X for all electricity needs. Solar energy is good for the environment and everyone knows it saves money in the long run." (Lacks specific evidence from this report)
  • Supported: "Based on the 36% electricity cost increase over the past three years (Finding 2) and Solar Vendor X's guaranteed rate lock of $0.09/kWh for 10 years (Finding 5), we recommend the school enter a power purchase agreement with Solar Vendor X. At current usage levels, this would reduce annual electricity costs by approximately $18,400 (Conclusion 3), with no upfront installation cost." (Directly references findings and conclusions, provides quantifiable justification)

Model Recommendation Report (Excerpt Discussion)

The provided model report, "Recommendation Report: Selecting a Laptop for the 1:1 Student Device Program," illustrates the effective application of these principles.

  • Executive Summary: Clearly states the recommendation (Model A for 6-8, Model C for 9-12) and highlights the key criteria and projected cost savings, immediately informing the superintendent of the core message.
  • Introduction: Defines the purpose (evaluate laptops), background (1:1 initiative, budget), and a detailed methodology (vendor proposals, pilot tests, teacher surveys, TCO analysis). This strong methodology section builds trust.
  • Findings: Presents numbered, objective data points (2.1 Cost, 2.2 Durability, 2.3 Battery Life) for each model. Numbering findings (e.g., Finding 2) is a crucial "writing tip" as it allows for precise referencing in subsequent sections.
  • Conclusions: Interprets the findings. For example, it explains why Model A is ideal for general use but not for advanced courses due to its Chrome OS, and why Model C is a good alternative. It also dismisses Model B based on cost vs. advantage.
  • Recommendations: These are specific, numbered actions:
    1. Deploy Model A for grades 6-8 (specific model, grades, quantity, cost).
    2. Deploy Model C for grades 9-12 (specific model, grades, quantity, cost).
    3. Negotiate volume discount (specific action, target, timeline).
    4. Begin deployment in January, expand by August (specific timeline). Crucially, these recommendations are directly supported by the findings and conclusions (e.g., Model A's cost-effectiveness for general use, Model C's Windows compatibility for advanced courses). The total projected cost being under budget demonstrates practicality.

This model effectively shows how a logical chain from specific findings to clear conclusions leads to well-supported, practical, and specific recommendations. When reviewing such a report, it's beneficial to trace each recommendation back to its supporting evidence in the findings and conclusions.

Review Questions

  1. What is the primary difference between an analytical report and a recommendation report, and why is this distinction crucial for organizational decision-making?
  2. Explain the "staircase" analogy for a recommendation report's structure. How does each section build on the previous one, and what happens if a step is skipped?
  3. Why is a robust methodology section essential for the credibility of a recommendation report? What specific elements should it address to demonstrate trustworthiness?
  4. Describe the four key qualities of effective recommendations (Specific, Supported, Practical, Prioritized). Provide an example for each quality that illustrates its importance.
  5. Using the provided model report excerpt (School Laptop Program), trace the logical chain from a specific finding (e.g., 2.1 Cost or 2.3 Battery Life) to a conclusion, and then to a recommendation. How does the model demonstrate the principles of supported and practical recommendations?
