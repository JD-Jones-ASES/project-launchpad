Study Guide: Mastering Resume Formats

Overview

This section emphasizes the critical role of resume formatting and content in capturing a hiring manager's attention within seconds. It introduces three standard resume formats—chronological, functional, and combination—explaining when to use each. Furthermore, it details essential resume sections and what information to include (and exclude) to create a professional and impactful document.

Key Concepts

  • The "20-Second Rule": Hiring managers often spend very little time (around 20 seconds) reviewing a resume to decide if an applicant is worth further consideration. This means your resume must be scannable, organized, and immediately communicate your value.
  • Three Standard Resume Formats:
    • Chronological: The most common format, listing experience in reverse chronological order (most recent first). Best for steady work history and entry-level positions.
    • Functional: Organizes qualifications by skill categories rather than job titles or dates. Useful for career changers, those with employment gaps, or varied experiences.
    • Combination: Blends a skills summary at the top with a chronological work history, offering the benefits of both.
  • Choosing the Right Format: The ideal resume format depends on your individual experience, career goals, and the specific job you're targeting. For students, the chronological format is almost always the safest and most recommended choice.
  • Essential Resume Sections: Regardless of format, a resume typically includes Contact Information, an Objective or Summary, Education, Experience, Skills, and Activities/Leadership/Volunteer Work. Each section has specific content guidelines.
  • Professionalism in Content: Every element, from your email address to your bullet points, must convey professionalism. Generic statements, irrelevant information, and unprofessional contact details can negatively impact your application.
  • Resume vs. Curriculum Vitae (CV): A resume is a concise, targeted document (1-2 pages), while a CV is a comprehensive, multi-page academic document used primarily in academia and international markets. For most jobs in the U.S., a resume is required.
  • Specificity and Quantification: Effective resume content uses specific examples, action verbs, and numbers whenever possible to describe accomplishments, not just duties.

Important Terms

  • Resume: A one- or two-page document that summarizes your education, experience, skills, and qualifications for a specific job.
  • Curriculum Vitae (CV): A comprehensive document listing all academic and professional accomplishments; longer and more detailed than a resume, used mainly in academia and international job markets.
  • Chronological: A resume format organized by time order, typically listing the most recent experience first.
  • Functional: A resume format organized by skill categories rather than by time, emphasizing what you can do rather than when you did it.
  • Objective: A brief statement at the top of a resume describing the specific position or goal the applicant is seeking. Must be specific to be effective.
  • Summary: A short paragraph at the top of a resume highlighting key qualifications and strengths; more common than objectives in modern resumes.
  • Reference: A person who can speak to your qualifications, character, or work ethic when contacted by a potential employer. (Note: "References available upon request" should NOT be included on a resume.)
  • Reverse Chronological Order: A common organizational method, especially for experience and education sections, where the most recent entry is listed first, followed by previous entries in descending date order.

Detailed Notes

The Reality of Resume Review

  • Hiring managers review dozens, sometimes hundreds, of applications for a single job opening.
  • They spend very little time per resume (around 20 seconds) to decide if an applicant moves forward.
  • Your resume must be immediately organized, scannable, and relevant, clearly communicating your value without requiring the reader to hunt for information.

The Three Resume Formats

1. Chronological Resume

  • Description: The most common and widely accepted format. Lists your work experience in reverse chronological order (most recent first).
  • Best for:
    • Individuals with a steady work history in one field.
    • Situations where job titles and employers are important.
    • Most entry-level and mid-career applications.
    • High school students applying for jobs where any experience is relevant.
  • Standard Sections (in order):
    1. Contact Information
    2. Objective or Summary (optional but recommended for students)
    3. Education
    4. Experience
    5. Skills
    6. Activities and Leadership (optional, but highly recommended for students)

2. Functional Resume

  • Description: Organizes qualifications by skill category (e.g., "Customer Service," "Cash Handling") rather than specific job titles and dates.
  • Best for:
    • People changing careers where past job titles aren't directly relevant to the new direction.
    • Individuals with gaps in employment.
    • Students with lots of varied experience (volunteering, projects, part-time work) that doesn't fit neatly into a timeline.
  • Caution: Some hiring managers may view functional resumes with suspicion, assuming the applicant is trying to hide something. Use this format only when it genuinely highlights your qualifications better than a chronological layout.

3. Combination Resume

  • Description: Merges both approaches: a strong skills summary section at the top, followed by a chronological work history.
  • Best for:
    • Experienced professionals with strong, marketable skills and a solid work history.
    • Individuals whose skills are more impressive than their job titles might suggest.
    • Students with both relevant skills and some work experience.

Choosing the Right Resume Format

  • The best format depends on your experience and the job target.
  • When in doubt, use the chronological format. It is the safest choice for most situations, especially for a student's first resume.

Essential Resume Sections

1. Contact Information

  • Placement: Prominently at the top.
  • Content: Your full name, phone number, professional email address, and city/state.
  • What NOT to include: Full street address (outdated).
  • Common Mistake: Unprofessional email addresses (e.g., "sk8rboi2009@email.com"). Create a professional email address using your name (e.g., firstname.lastname@email.com).

2. Objective vs. Summary

  • Objective: States what you are looking for (e.g., "Seeking a part-time position at a veterinary clinic...").
  • Summary: States what you offer (e.g., "Reliable student with two years of pet-sitting experience...").
  • For Students: Either works.
  • Key: Be specific, not generic. A generic objective ("Seeking a challenging position in a dynamic company") tells the reader nothing. A specific one names the company, position, and relevant qualities.

3. Education

  • For high school students, this is often a strong section.
  • Content:
    • School name and location.
    • Expected graduation date (e.g., "Expected June 2027").
    • GPA if 3.0 or above (label if weighted/unweighted).
    • Relevant coursework, honors, or academic awards.

4. Experience

  • Each entry should include:
    • Job title (or role).
    • Organization name and location.
    • Dates of employment or involvement.
    • 2-4 bullet points describing accomplishments using strong action verbs (not just assigned duties).

5. Skills

  • List specific, relevant skills.
  • Content:
    • Software and technology skills (e.g., "Microsoft Excel," "Google Sheets," "Canva").
    • Languages (with proficiency level: conversational, fluent, native).
    • Certifications (e.g., CPR, food handler's permit).
    • Technical skills relevant to the job.
  • What NOT to include: Vague qualities like "hard worker."

6. Activities, Leadership, and Volunteer Work

  • Crucial for students with limited paid work experience.
  • Shows employers what you do with your time and responsibilities taken on.
  • Content: Club membership, team captaincy, volunteer roles, church activities, community service, personal projects.

What NOT to Include on a Resume

  • "References available upon request.": Employers assume this.
  • A photograph: Creates legal complications for U.S. employers (discrimination concerns).
  • Personal information: Age, marital status, religion, Social Security number.
  • Generic objective: If it doesn't add specific value, skip it.
  • Every job you've ever held: Include only what's relevant to the target position.
  • Irrelevant hobbies: Only include hobbies if they directly relate to the job (e.g., moderating a book review channel for a bookstore job).

Resume vs. Curriculum Vitae (CV)

  • Resume: Concise (1-2 pages), targeted for specific jobs, used for most non-academic positions in the U.S.
  • CV: Comprehensive (multiple pages), lists all academic/professional accomplishments, used mainly in academia, medicine, and international job markets.

Maria Santos Sample Resume Highlights

  • Specific Objective: Names the company, position, and relevant skills.
  • Action Verbs: Bullet points begin with strong action verbs (e.g., "Shelved," "Assisted," "Created," "Provided").
  • Quantification: Uses numbers to show impact (e.g., "200+ books," "10-15 patrons," "30%," "three families," "18 months").
  • Professional Presentation: Even seemingly informal experience like babysitting is presented professionally.
  • Specific Skills: Lists verifiable skills like "Microsoft Office Suite" and "Fluent in English and Spanish."

Review Questions

  1. Given the "20-second rule," explain why the organization, scannability, and immediate communication of value are so critical for a resume. What happens if a resume fails to meet these criteria?
  2. Describe the key characteristics of the chronological, functional, and combination resume formats. For what type of applicant and job situation is each format most suitable? Why is the chronological format often recommended as the safest choice for high school students?
  3. Imagine you are a high school student applying for your first part-time job as a camp counselor. What specific details would you include in your Education section, and how could you effectively use the "Activities, Leadership, and Volunteer Work" section to highlight relevant skills and experiences?
  4. Explain the difference between an "objective" and a "summary" on a resume. Provide an example of a specific and effective objective statement for a high school student applying for a library assistant position, using details that go beyond a generic statement.
  5. Identify at least five types of information that should never be included on a modern resume. For each item, briefly explain why it is excluded and what potential issues its inclusion might cause for the applicant or employer.
