Study Guide: Precision in Language

Overview

This section emphasizes the critical role of precision in professional and technical writing, extending beyond mere numerical accuracy to the careful selection of words. It highlights how vague language, often characterized by "weasel words," can lead to misunderstandings and costly errors. The section provides practical strategies for achieving clarity through specific terminology, accurate numerical data, appropriate levels of detail, and objective reporting.

Key Concepts

  • The Importance of Precision: In technical writing, precision is paramount for effective communication, preventing misinterpretation, saving time, and avoiding financial losses. It involves both exact measurements and clear word choices.
  • Eliminating Weasel Words: Weasel words are vague, hedging terms (e.g., "fairly," "somewhat") that lack specific information, forcing readers to guess. They indicate a writer's lack of commitment or knowledge.
  • The Weasel Word Test: A useful self-assessment tool: If two reasonable people could disagree about a sentence's meaning, it likely contains weasel words and needs revision for specificity.
  • Conventions for Numbers, Units, and Measurements: Technical writing requires strict adherence to conventions for presenting numerical data, including always using units, standard abbreviations, dual units for mixed audiences, specifying conditions for performance numbers, and maintaining consistent decimal precision.
  • The "Compared to What?" Test: Relative terms (e.g., "faster," "heavier") are meaningless without a clear reference point or baseline for comparison.
  • Choosing the Right Level of Detail: The appropriate level of precision and detail depends entirely on the document's purpose and its intended audience. Providing too much or too little detail can hinder communication.
  • Objective vs. Subjective Language: Technical writing relies on objective language, which describes measurable, verifiable facts. Subjective language, expressing personal opinions or interpretations, weakens technical documents and should generally be avoided.
  • Broader Impact of Precision: The discipline of using precise language benefits all forms of writing, making communication more credible, memorable, and persuasive, not just in technical contexts.

Important Terms

  • Weasel Words: Vague, hedging words that sound meaningful but convey no specific information, forcing the reader to guess (e.g., "fairly," "somewhat," "relatively," "pretty much").
  • Objective Language: Words and phrases that describe measurable, verifiable facts without the writer's opinion or interpretation. This is the foundation of technical writing.
  • Subjective Language: Words and phrases that express the writer's personal opinion, feelings, or interpretation. Generally avoided in technical writing.

Detailed Notes

The Foundation of Precision

Technical descriptions and specifications demand precision not only in numbers but also in the words chosen. Vague language leads to confusion, errors, and potential financial costs in professional and technical contexts.

Eliminating Weasel Words

  • Definition: Weasel words are vague, hedging terms that offer no specific information, forcing the reader to infer meaning.
  • Examples: Common weasel words include "fairly," "somewhat," "relatively," "pretty much," "approximately" (though "approximately" has specific acceptable uses).
  • The Weasel Word Test: To identify weasel words, ask: "Could two reasonable people disagree about what this means?" If the answer is yes, the sentence needs revision to be more specific.
    • Vague: "The battery lasts a long time." (How long?)
    • Vague: "The system is fairly reliable." (How reliable?)
    • Vague: "The case is somewhat heavy." (How heavy?)
  • Purpose: Weasel words often signal a writer's lack of specific knowledge or insufficient effort in research and articulation.
  • Exception: Words like "approximately" are acceptable when an exact number is genuinely unknown or unimportant (e.g., "approximately 200 employees"). However, in specifications or technical descriptions, exactness is usually required.
  • Impact of Revision: Replacing weasel words with specific details makes information verifiable, allowing readers to make informed decisions.

Numbers, Units, and Measurements

Technical writing relies heavily on accurate numerical information. Adhering to specific conventions ensures clarity and consistency:

  1. Always Include Units: A number without a unit is meaningless. Specify "12 inches," "12 volts," etc.
  2. Use Standard Abbreviations: After the first full mention, use consistent, standard abbreviations (e.g., in., mm, lb, kg, V, A, W, Hz).
  3. Provide Dual Units: For international audiences, include both US customary and metric units (e.g., "4.0 inches (101.6 mm)").
  4. Specify Conditions for Performance Numbers: Performance figures are only meaningful when the conditions under which they were measured are stated (e.g., "The motor produces 150 horsepower at 6,500 RPM").
  5. Use Consistent Decimal Precision: Maintain a consistent number of decimal places to signal consistent precision across related measurements (e.g., "12.0 inches" and "8.0 inches," not "8 inches").
  • Writing Tip: The “Compared to What?” Test:
    • Relative terms (e.g., "larger," "faster," "heavier") are uninformative without a clear point of reference.
    • Always provide context and specific values when making comparisons.
    • Vague: "The new model is 40% faster."
    • Clear: "The new model processes 1,400 transactions per second, a 40% increase over the previous model's 1,000 transactions per second."

Choosing the Right Level of Detail

The level of detail required in a technical document is determined by its purpose and its audience.

  • A consumer shopping for a product needs less technical detail than an engineer manufacturing that product.
  • Providing too much or too little detail indicates a failure to understand the document's objectives or the reader's needs.

Subjective vs. Objective Language

  • Objective Language: Describes measurable, verifiable facts without personal opinion or interpretation. This is the cornerstone of technical writing.
  • Subjective Language: Expresses the writer's personal opinions, feelings, or interpretations. While suitable for persuasive or marketing writing, it weakens technical documents by introducing bias and unverifiable claims.

Precision Improves All Your Writing

The habit of using specific and precise language extends beyond technical documents and enhances all forms of writing:

  • Science Reports: Vague observations become specific, quantifiable descriptions.
  • Math Proofs: General statements are replaced with precise mathematical conditions.
  • History Essays: Broad generalizations are replaced with specific facts and figures.
  • Application Essays: Generic statements of experience become concrete, quantifiable achievements. In any context, specific details are more credible, memorable, and ultimately more persuasive than vague generalizations.

  2. Define "weasel words" and describe the "Weasel Word Test." Provide two examples of sentences containing weasel words and revise them to be precise.
  3. List and explain at least four conventions for presenting numerical information clearly in technical writing. Why is each convention important?
