Study Guide: Specifications

Overview

This section defines what a specification is and how it differs from a technical description. It explores the precise language used in specifications, particularly the critical distinctions between "shall," "should," and "may," and outlines various types of specifications. Finally, it introduces the concept of tolerances, which define acceptable variations in measurements, and emphasizes the importance of making every requirement testable.

Key Concepts

  • Specifications vs. Descriptions: A technical description explains what something is (an existing object), while a specification defines what something must be, do, or withstand (a future object, guiding its design and construction).
  • Mandatory Language: Specifications use precise terms to indicate the importance of requirements:
    • Shall: Indicates a mandatory, non-negotiable requirement.
    • Should: Indicates a recommended requirement; non-compliance needs justification.
    • May: Indicates an optional feature or requirement.
  • Testable Requirements: Every requirement in a specification must be measurable and testable. If you can't describe how to prove a requirement is met, it's too vague.
  • Types of Specifications: Specifications categorize requirements based on what they define:
    • Performance: What the product must do (speed, durability).
    • Material: What the product must be made of.
    • Dimensional: The product's size and shape.
    • Quality: Standards for appearance and consistency.
  • Tolerances: Define the acceptable range of variation for a measurement (e.g., $10.0 \pm 0.1$ inches). Tighter tolerances (smaller variations) increase cost and production time, so they should only be specified when functionally necessary.

Important Terms

  • Specification: A document that defines the exact standards or requirements a product, system, or material must meet. It is a blueprint for something that will be created.
  • Shall: A keyword in specifications indicating a mandatory requirement that must be met. Failure to comply means the product does not meet the specification.
  • Should: A keyword in specifications indicating a recommended requirement that ought to be met. Non-compliance requires justification but isn't an automatic disqualification.
  • May: A keyword in specifications indicating an optional requirement or feature that can be included but is not mandatory.
  • Performance Specification: A type of specification that defines what a product must do, such as its speed, capacity, accuracy, or durability.
  • Material Specification: A type of specification that defines what a product must be made of, detailing the specific substances or alloys.
  • Dimensional Specification: A type of specification that defines a product's size and shape, including precise measurements and geometric features.
  • Quality Specification: A type of specification that defines standards for a product's appearance, finish, and consistency, ensuring visual and tactile qualities.
  • Tolerance: The acceptable range of variation from a nominal (target) measurement, typically expressed as a plus-or-minus value (e.g., $\pm 0.1$ mm).

Detailed Notes

What is a Specification?

A specification is a formal document that sets precise requirements for a product, system, or material. Unlike a technical description, which explains an existing item, a specification acts as a blueprint, guiding the design and construction of something that will be created. It answers the question, "What must this object be, do, or withstand to be acceptable?"

Specifications are fundamental in many professional fields:

  • Education: A school district specifies minimum dimensions for new desks.
  • Software: A software company specifies performance criteria (e.g., search speed) for new features.
  • Civil Engineering: A city specifies the compacted thickness of asphalt for road construction.

The Language of Specifications: Shall, Should, and May

The words "shall," "should," and "may" carry specific, legally and professionally binding meanings in specifications. Understanding their distinction is crucial for both writers and readers of technical documents.

  • Shall (Mandatory): This indicates an absolute requirement. The product must meet this condition without any flexibility. Failure to comply means the product is unacceptable.
    • Example: "The desk surface shall measure no less than 24 inches wide."
  • Should (Recommended): This indicates a recommended requirement. The product ought to meet this condition, and any deviation requires a strong justification. Non-compliance does not automatically disqualify the product, but it raises questions.
    • Example: "The case should be available in at least three colors."
  • May (Optional): This indicates an optional feature or requirement. The designer or builder has the choice to include it but is not obligated to.
    • Example: "The case may include an integrated kickstand."

This precise language transforms vague statements (e.g., "pretty strong," "a few colors") into measurable, testable criteria (e.g., "withstand a drop of 6 feet," "at least three colors").

Writing Tip: Make Every Requirement Testable

A specification is only valuable if its requirements can be objectively verified. When writing, always ask: "How would I prove this is true?" If you cannot define a test, the requirement is too vague and needs refinement.

  • Not Testable: "The screen shall be bright enough."
  • Testable: "The screen shall produce a minimum luminance of 500 nits at maximum brightness."

Types of Specifications

Specifications are typically categorized based on the aspect of the product they define:

  1. Performance Specifications: Describe what the product must do.
    • Examples: Speed, capacity, accuracy, durability, power consumption.
    • Requirement Example: "The printer shall produce a minimum of 30 pages per minute in monochrome mode."
  2. Material Specifications: Define what the product must be made of.
    • Examples: Specific alloys, plastics, fabrics, chemical compositions.
    • Requirement Example: "The frame shall be constructed from 6061-T6 aluminum alloy."
  3. Dimensional Specifications: Define the product's size and shape.
    • Examples: Length, width, height, diameter, angle, surface flatness.
    • Requirement Example: "The enclosure shall measure $12.0 \times 8.0 \times 3.5$ inches ($\pm 0.1$ inch)."
  4. Quality Specifications: Define standards for appearance, finish, and consistency.
    • Examples: Color matching, surface texture, absence of defects, purity.
    • Requirement Example: "The painted surface shall be free of runs, sags, and visible brush marks when viewed from a distance of 3 feet under standard office lighting."

A comprehensive specification document usually combines all these types, often organized by the product's components or systems.

Example: A Complete Product Specification (Custom Protective Phone Case)

This example illustrates how all the concepts — precise language, testable requirements, and different specification types — come together.

  • Purpose: Clearly states the product and its target device (iPhone 15).
  • Dimensional Requirements:
    • Uses "shall" for critical fit and protection (e.g., "snug fit," "extend at least 1.0 mm above the screen").
    • Includes specific measurements and tolerances (e.g., "no more than 0.5 mm lateral movement," "add no more than 3.0 mm total thickness," "tolerance of $\pm 0.3$ mm").
  • Material Requirements:
    • Uses "shall" for the main material and safety (e.g., "molded from TPU," "free of BPA, lead").
    • Uses "should" for a desirable but less critical material property (e.g., "resist yellowing").
  • Performance Requirements:
    • Uses "shall" for critical protection and functionality (e.g., "protect device from damage when dropped from 4 feet, tested per MIL-STD-810G," "maintain protective properties across a temperature range," "support wireless charging").
    • Note the use of an industry standard (MIL-STD-810G) for testability.
  • Appearance Requirements:
    • Uses "shall" for mandatory finishes (e.g., "available in matte black and clear").
    • Uses "should" for additional color options ("at least two additional colors").
    • Uses "may" for an optional customization feature (e.g., "may include a customizable area").
    • Includes a quality specification for finish (e.g., "free of flash, burrs, and mold marks").
  • Compliance:
    • Uses "shall" for mandatory regulatory adherence (e.g., "comply with REACH regulation").
    • Uses "should" for desirable market certifications (e.g., "carry CE marking").

Tolerances and Ranges

In manufacturing, perfect precision is unattainable and often unnecessary. Tolerances define the acceptable amount of variation from a target measurement.

  • Notation: A tolerance is usually written as a plus-or-minus value ($\pm$) after the nominal measurement.
    • Example: $10.0 \pm 0.1$ inches means the measurement must be between 9.9 and 10.1 inches.

Cost and Time Implications:

  • Tighter Tolerances (smaller variation): More expensive and time-consuming to achieve, requiring specialized equipment and processes. Often critical for high-precision components (e.g., jet engine parts).
  • Looser Tolerances (larger variation): Less expensive and faster to produce, suitable for components where exactness is not critical for function (e.g., a pen cap diameter).

Common Mistake: Specifying unnecessarily tight tolerances. Always evaluate the functional need. Overly tight tolerances lead to increased costs and production delays without adding real value to the product. Ask: "What is the loosest tolerance that still meets the functional need?"

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental difference between a technical description and a specification. Provide an example of each for a common household item.
  2. Imagine you are specifying a new student desk for a classroom. Write three requirements for the desk, using "shall," "should," and "may" at least once, and justify your choice of word for each requirement.
  3. Why is it crucial for every requirement in a specification to be "testable"? Provide an example of a vague requirement and how you would make it testable.
  4. Describe the four main types of specifications (Performance, Material, Dimensional, Quality). For a smartphone, give one specific example of a requirement for each type.
  5. What is a tolerance, and why is it important in a specification? Discuss the trade-offs between specifying tight versus loose tolerances in terms of cost and product functionality.
