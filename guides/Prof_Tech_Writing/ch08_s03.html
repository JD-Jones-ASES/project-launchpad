Meeting Minutes Study Guide

Overview

This section defines meeting minutes as the official written record of what transpired during a meeting, capturing decisions, assigned tasks, and essential information shared. Unlike a full transcript, minutes focus on outcomes rather than every spoken word, serving as proof of the meeting's productivity and a vital reference for future actions.

Key Concepts

  • Purpose of Meeting Minutes: Minutes are the official, written record of a meeting's outcomes. They document decisions made, tasks assigned (action items), and key information shared, serving as proof that the meeting achieved its objectives.
  • Minutes vs. Transcript: Minutes are not a word-for-word transcript. Their primary goal is to capture the results and outcomes of discussions, not the discussions themselves.
  • The Three Essential Captures: Effective minutes must record:
    1. Decisions made: What the group agreed to do, including vote results.
    2. Action items assigned: What tasks were given, to whom, and by when.
    3. Key information shared: Important updates or facts the group needs to remember.
  • Objective and Factual Reporting: Minutes should be written in past tense (describing what happened) and third person (as a neutral observer, not using "I" or "we"). They must remain factual, avoiding personal opinions, emotions, or characterizations of participants' attitudes.
  • Structured Format: Professional minutes follow a consistent format, making them easy to scan and reference. This includes a header block, sections for each agenda item, a consolidated action items summary, details for the next meeting, and adjournment time.
  • Efficient Notetaking Strategies: During a meeting, notetakers should use the agenda as a template, focus on listening for decisions and actions rather than full discussions, use abbreviations, ask for clarification when needed, and finalize and distribute minutes promptly (within 24 hours).

Important Terms

  • Meeting Minutes: The official written record detailing what was discussed, decided, and assigned during a meeting.
  • Agenda: A list of topics to be discussed during a meeting, serving as a plan.
  • Action Items: Specific tasks or assignments given to individuals, along with a deadline for completion, arising from a meeting.
  • Decision: A conclusion or resolution reached and agreed upon by the group during a meeting, often after discussion or a vote.
  • Key Information Shared: Critical updates, data, or facts presented during a meeting that are important for attendees to remember or act upon.
  • Notetaker: The person responsible for documenting the proceedings of a meeting, specifically creating the minutes.
  • Third Person: A grammatical perspective where the writer refers to others as "he," "she," "it," or "they," or by name, maintaining an objective distance.
  • Past Tense: A grammatical form used to describe actions or states that have already occurred at a definite time in the past.
  • Quorum: The minimum number of members required to be present at a meeting for valid business to be conducted or decisions to be made.
  • Motion: A formal proposal made by a member of a group during a meeting, requiring discussion and a vote.
  • Seconded: To formally agree with a motion proposed by another member, indicating support for it to be brought to a vote.

Detailed Notes

What are Meeting Minutes?

Meeting minutes are the authoritative, written account of a meeting. While an agenda outlines what will be discussed, minutes record what actually happened. They serve as concrete proof of accomplishments, outlining decisions, task assignments, and crucial information. The term "minutes" originates from the Latin minutia, meaning "small details," emphasizing the capture of important specifics that might otherwise be forgotten.

What to Capture (and What to Skip)

The primary goal of minutes is to capture outcomes, not a word-for-word transcript of the conversation.

The Three Things Minutes Must Capture:

  1. Decisions made: Clearly state what the group agreed upon, including vote results if applicable.
  2. Action items assigned: Detail who is responsible for what task and by when. (What/Who/When).
  3. Key information shared: Record any important facts, data, or updates presented.

Include in Minutes:

  • Decisions and vote results
  • Action items (What/Who/When)
  • Key facts and data shared
  • Topics tabled for future discussion
  • Motions made and seconded
  • Attendance (who was present)

Leave Out of Minutes:

  • Who argued for which side (focus on the decision, not the debate)
  • Off-topic conversations
  • Personal opinions and jokes
  • Repeated points or rephrased ideas
  • Small talk before the meeting
  • Word-for-word dialogue

Writing Tip: Past Tense, Third Person

  • Past Tense: Minutes describe events that have already occurred. For example, "The committee voted to approve..." not "The committee votes..."
  • Third Person: The notetaker acts as a neutral observer. Use names or "he/she/they" instead of "I" or "we." For example, "Marcus reported..." not "I reported..." This maintains professionalism and objectivity.

Format of Meeting Minutes

Professional minutes follow a standard, scannable format:

  1. Header Block: Contains essential meeting details, mirroring the agenda:

    • Meeting name (e.g., Westfield High School Environmental Club)
    • Type of meeting (e.g., Monthly Meeting Minutes)
    • Date, time, location
    • Facilitator, notetaker
    • List of attendees (present and absent, with excuses if known)

  2. Agenda Items: Each topic from the agenda should have its own section in the minutes. Under each topic, record:

    • A summary of relevant discussion points (briefly, focusing on facts).
    • Any decisions made.
    • Any action items assigned (What/Who/When).

  3. Action Items Summary: A consolidated list, usually at the end of the minutes, of all action items, their owners, and deadlines. This provides an easy reference for all participants to track responsibilities.

  4. Next Meeting Details: Date, time, and location of the subsequent meeting.

  5. Adjournment Time: The exact time the meeting concluded.

Structured minutes clearly separate discussion points from decisions and action items, making the outcomes immediately evident and actionable, even long after the meeting.

Taking Notes During the Meeting

Taking effective minutes live requires strategy due to the speed of conversation:

  1. Use the Agenda as a Template: Before the meeting, set up your document with the agenda items as headings. This provides a structure to fill in directly.
  2. Focus on Outcomes, Not Process: Listen for definitive statements like "So we are going to..." or "Let's decide that..." These are the moments to record decisions and actions. Avoid trying to capture every point of debate.
  3. Use Abbreviations and Clean Up Later: Employ shorthand (e.g., "AI" for Action Item, initials for names) during the meeting. The minutes will be polished and expanded after the meeting.
  4. Ask for Clarification: If a decision or action item is unclear, speak up. It's better to confirm details immediately than to record incorrect or vague information. Attendees will appreciate the thoroughness.
  5. Finish Within 24 Hours: Your memory is freshest immediately after the meeting. Draft, clean up, and distribute the minutes within one day to ensure accuracy.

Common Mistake: Attributing Opinions in Minutes Avoid recording personal feelings, judgments, or characterizations of participants (e.g., "Sarah argued strongly... and seemed frustrated"). Instead, report the factual content of what was said in a neutral way (e.g., "Sarah expressed concern about the budget increase, citing limited remaining funds."). Minutes should be objective and professional to prevent conflict.

Review Questions

  1. What is the fundamental difference between meeting minutes and a meeting transcript, and why is this distinction important for the notetaker?
  2. Identify and explain the "Three Things Minutes Must Capture." Provide an example of each.
  3. Explain why minutes should be written in past tense and third person. How does this style contribute to the professionalism and objectivity of the record?
  4. Describe at least three practical strategies a notetaker can use during a fast-paced meeting to ensure they capture all necessary information effectively.
  5. The textbook advises against attributing opinions or emotions in minutes. Provide an example of how an opinionated statement might be rephrased into a factual, neutral entry for meeting minutes.
