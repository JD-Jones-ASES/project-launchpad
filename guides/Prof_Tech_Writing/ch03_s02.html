Chapter 3, Section 2: Testing Your Instructions

Overview

This section emphasizes that even well-written instructions are unproven until someone unfamiliar with the task attempts to follow them. It introduces the "Fresh-Eyes Test" as the most effective method for identifying flaws in instructions, detailing how to conduct this usability test, what common problems it reveals, and how to revise effectively based on observations.

Key Concepts

  • The Fresh-Eyes Test: The crucial final step in writing instructions, involving having someone who has never performed the task follow your instructions without help, while you observe and take notes to identify areas of struggle and revise accordingly. It effectively uncovers gaps that are "invisible" to the writer.
  • Unconscious Skipping: Writers often omit seemingly "obvious" steps or details because their familiarity with the task causes their brains to fill in missing information automatically. The fresh-eyes test is designed to expose these overlooked details.
  • Usability Testing: A method used by professionals to determine how easy and effective something (like instructions) is to use. The fresh-eyes test is a form of usability testing.
  • The Importance of Silent Observation: During a fresh-eyes test, the observer must resist the urge to help or explain. Every hesitation, mistake, or question from the tester highlights a flaw in the instructions, not the tester, providing valuable revision data.
  • Common Problems Revealed by Testing: Testing frequently uncovers missing steps, assumed knowledge (undefined terms, unstated prerequisites), ambiguous language, steps in the wrong order, and the absence of result statements that confirm successful completion of a step.
  • Revision vs. Proofreading: Revision is a deeper process than proofreading. While proofreading fixes grammar and typos, revision focuses on improving the content itself—its clarity, completeness, organization, and overall effectiveness.
  • Iterative Testing and Revision: Professional technical writers understand that instructions are rarely perfect after one draft. They test, revise, and then test again multiple times, recognizing that each round of testing improves the quality and user-friendfriendliness of the instructions.

Important Terms

  • Fresh-Eyes Test: A method of testing instructions by having a novice user attempt to follow them without assistance, allowing the writer to observe difficulties and identify areas for revision.
  • Usability Testing: A general method for evaluating how easy and effective a product, system, or set of instructions is to use, typically by observing users interacting with it.
  • Imperative Mood: The grammatical mood used for commands and instructions (e.g., "Place," "Turn," "Remove"). (Mentioned in the introductory paragraph as a characteristic of good instructions).
  • Troubleshooting: The systematic process of identifying, diagnosing, and solving problems or errors, often included in instructions to help users resolve common issues.
  • Revision: The comprehensive process of reviewing and improving the content, clarity, organization, and completeness of a piece of writing, distinct from mere proofreading.
  • Result Statements: Brief descriptions within or after a step that inform the user what the outcome of that step should look like or feel like, helping them confirm they have performed the action correctly.
  • Ambiguous Language: Vague, unclear, or open-to-interpretation phrasing that can confuse readers, especially those unfamiliar with the task.
  • Assumed Knowledge: Information, terms, or skills that the writer presumes the reader already possesses but may not, leading to gaps in understanding.

Detailed Notes

The Necessity of Testing

No matter how carefully you draft your instructions, their true effectiveness can only be determined when someone who is unfamiliar with the task attempts to follow them. As the writer, your deep knowledge of the task makes you prone to "unconsciously skipping" details that seem obvious to you but are critical for a first-time user. These omissions are invisible to you because your brain automatically fills them in.

The Fresh-Eyes Test: Your Most Valuable Tool

The fresh-eyes test is the ultimate method for uncovering these hidden gaps and flaws. It's a form of usability testing, widely used by professionals to ensure their products and documentation are easy to use.

How to Conduct a Fresh-Eyes Test:

  1. Find an Unfamiliar Tester: Select someone who has absolutely no prior experience with the specific task your instructions describe. The less familiar they are, the more effective the test will be at revealing overlooked steps or assumptions.
  2. Provide Only Written Instructions: Hand the tester only the written instructions and the necessary materials. Crucially, do not offer any verbal explanations, demonstrations, or supplemental information before or during the test.
  3. Watch Silently: This is often the most challenging part. As the writer, you will instinctively want to jump in and help when your tester hesitates or makes a mistake. You must resist this urge. Every moment of confusion or error is a valuable data point that reveals a weakness in your instructions, not a flaw in the tester.
  4. Take Detailed Notes: Observe and record every instance where the tester:
    • Pauses, hesitates, or re-reads a step multiple times.
    • Performs actions out of the intended sequence.
    • Does something other than what you expected.
    • Asks a question (even if you cannot answer it during the test).
    • Makes an error or struggles to complete a step.
    • Skips a step entirely.
  5. Debrief the Tester: After the test, engage in a conversation with the tester. Ask them directly what parts were confusing, what they found helpful, and what suggestions they have for improvement. Their firsthand perspective is invaluable.
  6. Revise and Re-Test: Based on your observations and the tester's feedback, rewrite and refine your instructions. Professional technical writers often perform multiple rounds of testing and revision (three or more times) to achieve optimal clarity and effectiveness.

Classroom Activity: The Paper Airplane Test This hands-on exercise demonstrates the power of the fresh-eyes test. Students write instructions for making a specific paper airplane, then swap and follow their partner's instructions exactly as written, without asking questions. Comparing the resulting airplanes to the intended design immediately highlights where instructions were unclear, ambiguous, or incomplete, leading to a powerful revision exercise.

Common Problems Revealed by Testing

The fresh-eyes test consistently exposes specific types of flaws:

  • Missing Steps: This is the most frequent issue. Writers, due to their familiarity, inadvertently omit crucial steps that are essential for a novice.
  • Assumed Knowledge: Instructions may use technical jargon without defining it, or assume the reader possesses prior knowledge (e.g., how to "Open Terminal") that they do not.
  • Ambiguous Language: Vague phrasing like "Attach the piece to the side" leaves the reader guessing which piece and which side, leading to confusion and errors.
  • Wrong Order: Sometimes, a step is placed too early or too late in the sequence. Testing can reveal that gathering materials, for instance, should be a prerequisite rather than a mid-process step.
  • Missing Result Statements: Readers complete a step but have no way to verify if they did it correctly. They proceed, only to discover much later that an earlier step was performed incorrectly, wasting time and effort.

Revision: Improving Your Instructions

Revision is distinct from proofreading. Proofreading focuses on catching surface-level errors like typos and grammar. Revision, however, involves a deeper improvement of the content itself—its clarity, completeness, organization, and accessibility.

When revising after a fresh-eyes test, consider these critical questions:

  • Are all necessary steps included? Are any steps missing?
  • Does each step describe a single, clear action?
  • Are the steps logically ordered?
  • Are all technical terms or unfamiliar concepts clearly defined?
  • Are warnings and cautions positioned immediately before the potentially dangerous or critical steps they apply to?
  • Do important steps include result statements so the reader knows if they succeeded?
  • Could someone with absolutely no prior experience successfully follow these instructions?

Model Document: Professional Instructions for a Grilled Cheese Sandwich

The provided "Professional Recipe" for a grilled cheese sandwich serves as an excellent model. It demonstrates the application of all the principles discussed:

  • Clear Title and Purpose: States what the instructions accomplish and an estimated time.
  • Equipment and Ingredients (Prerequisites): Lists everything needed upfront.
  • Numbered Steps in Imperative Mood: Each step is a clear command.
  • Warnings/Cautions: A prominent "CAUTION" is placed before the first step, alerting the user to hot surfaces.
  • Notes: Explanatory notes clarify why certain actions are important (e.g., "Medium-low heat is important.").
  • Result Statement: Describes the desired final outcome (a "golden-brown grilled cheese sandwich...").
  • Troubleshooting Section: Addresses common problems and their solutions, helping users recover from mistakes.

By integrating these elements, the grilled cheese instructions achieve a level of clarity and robustness far superior to typical casual recipes.

Test, Revise, Repeat

The ultimate takeaway is that instruction writing is an iterative process. Professional technical writers understand that instructions are refined through repeated cycles of testing, revision, and re-testing. Each round of testing brings to light new insights, leading to progressively clearer and more effective instructions.
