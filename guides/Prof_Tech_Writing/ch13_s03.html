Study Guide: Findings vs. Conclusions vs. Recommendations

Overview

This section is crucial for writing effective analytical and recommendation reports, highlighting the critical distinction between findings, conclusions, and recommendations. It explains how these three elements form a logical chain, with each step building upon the previous one, and provides clear guidelines and tools to differentiate them correctly to strengthen your reports.

Key Concepts

  • The Three-Step Chain: Analytical and recommendation reports follow a logical sequence: Findings (what the data shows) lead to Conclusions (what the findings mean), which then inform Recommendations (what action should be taken). Each step is interdependent and essential for a coherent report.
  • Findings (Facts): These are objective, verifiable data points, observations, or measurements collected during research. They should be factual, specific (including numbers, dates, sources), and verifiable, presenting raw information without interpretation or opinion.
  • Conclusions (Interpretations): These explain the meaning of the findings, identify patterns, explain causes, and draw inferences. Conclusions must be logically supported by specific findings and clearly distinguished from them using phrases that signal interpretation.
  • Recommendations (Actions): These are specific, actionable steps proposed to address the issues or opportunities identified by the conclusions. They must be supported by the preceding conclusions, practical to implement, and prioritized.
  • The Sorting Test: A practical tool to ensure clarity, this test asks: Can it be verified with raw data? (Finding). Does it interpret the data's meaning? (Conclusion). Does it propose an action? (Recommendation). It helps prevent mixing these elements.
  • Benchmarks: A standard of comparison or a reference point that provides context for your data. For example, comparing your data to an industry average or historical performance.
  • Metrics: Specific, measurable values used to track performance, such as customer satisfaction scores, graduation rates, or cost per unit. Good metrics are specific, measurable, and relevant to the report's purpose.
  • Context for Numbers: Always provide context for numerical data, answering: What is it? How does it compare? Is it going up or down? This ensures the reader understands the significance of the numbers.

Important Terms

  • Finding: A specific data point, observation, or measurement collected during research; a verifiable fact.
  • Conclusion: An interpretation of findings, explaining what the data means, identifying patterns, or drawing inferences.
  • Recommendation: A proposed action or specific step that should be taken, based on the conclusions drawn from the findings.
  • The Sorting Test: A methodology to categorize statements in a report as findings, conclusions, or recommendations based on whether they are verifiable facts, interpretations, or proposed actions.
  • Benchmark: A standard or reference point used for comparison to give context to data.
  • Metric: A quantifiable measure used to track and assess performance or progress.

Detailed Notes

The Critical Distinction: Findings, Conclusions, and Recommendations

Many reports fall short because writers confuse findings, conclusions, and recommendations. While related, they are distinct components forming a logical chain essential for a strong, persuasive report. Mixing them weakens the entire message.

The Three-Step Logical Chain

Every effective analytical or recommendation report follows a clear progression:

  1. Findings: Answer "What did the data show?" (Facts)
  2. Conclusions: Answer "What do the findings mean?" (Interpretation)
  3. Recommendations: Answer "What should we do?" (Action)

This chain is interdependent. Without findings, conclusions are just opinions. Without conclusions, recommendations are merely guesses. And without context, findings are simply a data dump.

Findings: What the Data Shows

Findings are the bedrock of your report. They are objective facts derived directly from your collected data, observations, and measurements. They are verifiable, meaning another person examining the same data would report the same information.

Characteristics of Effective Findings:

  • Factual: No interpretation, opinion, or judgment. Present only the raw information.
  • Specific: Include precise numbers, dates, sources, and quantities. Avoid vague language.
  • Verifiable: The information can be confirmed by referring back to the original data or source.

Examples of Findings:

  • "Of the 845 students surveyed, 315 (37.3%) reported riding the school bus as their primary transportation."
  • "Ninth graders were the most likely to ride the school bus (112 of 209, or 53.6%), while twelfth graders were the least likely (41 of 224, or 18.3%)."
  • "Self-driving increased from 0% among ninth graders to 38.8% among twelfth graders."

Notice how these statements present data without attempting to explain why the numbers are as they are.

Conclusions: What the Findings Mean

Conclusions bridge the gap between raw data and actionable insights. They interpret findings, answering the "So what?" question. A conclusion identifies patterns, explains causes, and draws logical inferences from the data presented in the findings.

Characteristics of Effective Conclusions:

  • Supported: Every conclusion must be directly traceable to one or more specific findings. If you can't point to the data, it's an opinion, not a conclusion.
  • Logical: The interpretation must follow directly and reasonably from the data without overreaching or making unsupported leaps.
  • Clearly Distinguished: Use phrases like "This suggests," "These figures indicate," or "The data points to" to signal that you are moving from fact to interpretation.

Examples of Conclusions (based on the findings above):

  • "The steep decline in bus ridership from ninth grade (53.6%) to twelfth grade (18.3%) indicates that students shift away from school-provided transportation as they gain access to personal vehicles and peer carpools."
  • "The near-perfect inverse relationship between bus ridership and self-driving/carpooling across grade levels suggests that students view the bus as a last resort rather than a preferred option."

Finding Disguised as Conclusion vs. Real Conclusion:

  • Incorrect (Finding disguised as Conclusion): "138 students drive themselves to school." (This is a fact, a finding.)
  • Correct (Finding): "138 students (16.3%) drive themselves, with 63% of those drivers in twelfth grade."
  • Correct (Conclusion): "The concentration of self-drivers in twelfth grade indicates that the ability to drive—not a preference against the bus—is the primary factor in transportation choice, since self-driving becomes common only after students reach driving age."

Recommendations: What to Do About It

Recommendations are the action-oriented component of your report. They propose specific steps or solutions that the reader (e.g., decision-maker) should implement, based on the insights derived from the conclusions. Recommendations should be:

  • Specific: Clearly state what needs to be done.
  • Supported: Directly linked to the conclusions.
  • Practical: Feasible and realistic to implement.
  • Prioritized: If multiple recommendations, indicate their order of importance or urgency.

Examples of Recommendations (based on the conclusions above):

  • "To reduce parking congestion, the administration should promote the peer carpool program among eleventh and twelfth graders, targeting an increase from 114 to 170 carpooling students by offering priority parking spaces to registered carpool vehicles."
  • "Because bus ridership drops sharply after ninth grade, the district should consider reallocating one of the four bus routes serving this school, redirecting it to elementary schools where ridership demand is higher."

The Sorting Test: A Practical Tool for Clarity

To ensure you're correctly categorizing your statements, use this simple test for every sentence in your report:

  • Can I verify it with raw data? If yes, it's a Finding.
  • Does it interpret what the data means? If yes, it's a Conclusion.
  • Does it propose an action someone should take? If yes, it's a Recommendation.

If a single sentence attempts to do more than one of these things, split it. Place each distinct part in its appropriate section of the report.

Example: Mixed-Up vs. Properly Sorted Report Section:

  • Mixed-Up: "The cafeteria satisfaction survey found that students are unhappy with the food, so the school should hire a new food vendor. Only 34% of students rated the food 'good' or 'excellent,' which means the current vendor is failing. We recommend switching to Fresh Farms Catering immediately."
  • Properly Sorted:
    • Finding: "In the cafeteria satisfaction survey, 34% of students (n = 287) rated the food 'good' or 'excellent,' while 41% rated it 'poor' or 'very poor.'"
    • Conclusion: "With fewer than half of students satisfied and over 40% actively dissatisfied, the current food service is not meeting student expectations. The dissatisfaction is concentrated in entree variety (28% satisfaction) rather than side items (62% satisfaction)."
    • Recommendation: "The administration should issue a Request for Proposal (RFP) to at least three food service vendors by February 1, with a requirement that proposals include at least 8 rotating entree options per week, up from the current 4."

Connection to Other Academic Writing

Mastering the findings-conclusions-recommendations chain is not just for formal reports. It directly applies to other analytical papers:

  • Science Lab Reports:
    • Results section = Findings
    • Discussion section = Conclusions
    • Implications / Future Work = Recommendations
  • History Research Papers: Differentiating between historical evidence (findings) and your argument/interpretation of that evidence (conclusions) is key to a strong paper.

Benchmarks and Metrics

Two terms that enhance analytical reports:

  • Benchmark: A standard of comparison that provides context for your data. For example, comparing your school's 78% graduation rate to the state average of 85% or to your school's own 72% rate five years ago.
  • Metric: A specific, measurable value used to track performance. Examples include customer satisfaction scores, average response times, or employee retention rates. Effective metrics are specific, measurable, and relevant to the report's goals.

Writing Tip: Always Provide Context for Numbers

A number on its own is often meaningless. To make your data impactful, every number in your report should provide context by answering three questions:

  1. What is it? (e.g., "Our customer satisfaction score is 4.2.")
  2. How does it compare? (e.g., "The industry average is 3.8.")
  3. Is it going up or down? (e.g., "This is up from 3.6 last year.")

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental difference between a finding, a conclusion, and a recommendation. Why is it important not to confuse them in an analytical report?
  2. Imagine you conducted a survey on student satisfaction with school library hours. Provide one example of a finding, one example of a conclusion based on that finding, and one example of a recommendation based on that conclusion.
  3. Describe "The Sorting Test." How can applying this test improve the clarity and persuasiveness of your reports?
  4. How does the logical chain of findings, conclusions, and recommendations apply to writing a science lab report or a history research paper? Provide specific parallels.
  5. What is the significance of using benchmarks and metrics in an analytical report, and why is it crucial to provide context for any numerical data presented?
