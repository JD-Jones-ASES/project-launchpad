Study Guide: Evidence and Citations in Technical Documents

Overview

This section emphasizes the critical role of credible evidence and proper citations in white papers and technical proposals, transforming them from mere opinions or wish lists into persuasive, verifiable documents. It guides readers through finding, evaluating, using, and citing sources effectively, while also providing crucial advice on avoiding plagiarism.

Key Concepts

  • Primary vs. Secondary Sources: Primary sources offer original, firsthand evidence (e.g., raw data, interviews, experiments), carrying more weight. Secondary sources analyze or summarize primary sources (e.g., textbooks, review articles), useful for context.
  • Source Credibility Evaluation: Before using any source, assess its trustworthiness based on five criteria: Authority (expertise of author/organization), Currency (recency of publication), Accuracy (verifiability of claims), Purpose (reason for publication, potential bias), and Relevance (direct applicability to your point).
  • Paraphrasing vs. Quoting: Paraphrasing involves restating an idea in your own words while maintaining the original meaning and citing the source. Direct quoting uses the exact words in quotation marks. Paraphrasing is generally preferred in technical writing, with direct quotes reserved for specific instances where exact wording is critical.
  • Purposes of Citations: Citations serve to establish credibility by showing evidence support, allow readers to verify information independently, and ethically give credit to original researchers.
  • The Simple Rule for Avoiding Plagiarism: If an idea, fact, statistic, argument, or phrase did not originate in your own mind, it must be cited. The only exception is widely known "common knowledge." When in doubt, always cite.

Important Terms

  • Primary Source: Original, firsthand evidence, such as data you collected, experiments you ran, or official records.
  • Secondary Source: An analysis, interpretation, or summary of primary sources, like textbook chapters or review articles.
  • Paraphrase: To restate someone else's ideas in your own words while preserving the original meaning and giving credit to the source.
  • Quote Directly: To use the exact words from a source, enclosed in quotation marks, with proper attribution.
  • Citation: A reference within the text that tells the reader where a piece of information, data, or idea came from, typically including author and date.
  • Reference: An entry in a reference list or bibliography at the end of a document that provides full publication information for a source cited in the text.
  • Plagiarism: Presenting someone else's words, ideas, data, or creative work as your own, intentionally or accidentally, by failing to give proper credit.
  • Common Knowledge: Facts so widely known that they do not need attribution (e.g., "The sun rises in the east").

Detailed Notes

The Imperative of Evidence

  • Foundation of Argument: White papers and technical proposals require every significant claim to be supported by credible evidence. Without it, these documents are merely opinions or wish lists.
  • Section Focus: This section guides you through finding, evaluating, using, and citing evidence effectively to build strong arguments.

Primary vs. Secondary Sources

  • Defining Primary Sources: These are original, firsthand pieces of evidence.
    • Examples: Data you collected, interviews you conducted, surveys you administered, experiments you ran, official records, or statistics from the originating organization.
    • Weight: They carry more persuasive weight because they are closest to the original information.
  • Defining Secondary Sources: These sources analyze, interpret, or summarize primary sources.
    • Examples: Textbook chapters, review articles, news reports, encyclopedias.
    • Usefulness: They provide context, background information, or a broader perspective, but are less direct proof.
  • Writing Tip: Lead with Primary, Support with Secondary: Prioritize primary sources for your main arguments and specific findings. Use secondary sources to offer context, show larger patterns, or explain background information.

Evaluating Source Credibility

  • Importance: Not all sources are suitable; scrutinizing their credibility is essential before inclusion.
  • Five Key Criteria:
    1. Authority:
      • Question: Who created or published this? Are they recognized experts?
      • Example: A government energy report holds more authority than a personal blog post on electricity bills.
    2. Currency:
      • Question: When was this published? Is the information still relevant?
      • Consideration: In rapidly changing fields (e.g., technology), older data (even 10 years old) may be outdated. Always seek the most recent information.
    3. Accuracy:
      • Question: Can the claims be verified? Does the source cite its own sources?
      • Indicators: Look for specific data rather than vague assertions, and check for a clear methodology.
    4. Purpose:
      • Question: Why was this published? What is the author's intent?
      • Bias Awareness: Independent research is generally more trustworthy than marketing materials from a company selling related products. Be aware of potential biases.
    5. Relevance:
      • Question: Does this source directly support your specific point?
      • Specificity: A study on commercial office buildings may not be relevant to K-12 school buildings, even if both address energy efficiency.
  • Common Mistake: Citing Everything You Find: Including weak, irrelevant, or outdated sources detracts from your argument. Quality over quantity is crucial; a few strong sources are more persuasive than many mediocre ones.

Paraphrasing vs. Quoting

  • Paraphrasing:
    • Definition: Restating another's ideas in your own words while preserving the meaning and crediting the source.
    • Default in Technical Writing: This is the most common and preferred method in professional and technical documents. It demonstrates your understanding and integrates smoothly into your writing.
  • Direct Quoting:
    • Definition: Using the exact words from a source, enclosed in quotation marks.
    • Limited Use: Only quote directly when the precise wording is essential, such as for a legal definition, a famous statement, or highly specific technical language that would lose meaning if rephrased.
  • Poor vs. Effective Paraphrase Example: The example demonstrates how effective paraphrasing integrates data into the writer's voice, adds insight (e.g., calculating the 80% drop), and cites efficiently, rather than just stringing together quotes.

Citation Basics

  • What is a Citation? A citation is a reference that informs the reader of the source of information, data, or ideas.
  • Three Purposes of Citations:
    1. Credibility: They show that your claims are backed by verifiable evidence.
    2. Verification: They enable readers to locate and check your sources for themselves.
    3. Ethics: They acknowledge the original creators of the research or data, giving proper credit.
  • Citation Styles: Various styles exist (e.g., APA, MLA, Chicago, IEEE). Consistency in using one style throughout your document is more important than the specific style chosen.
  • Common Format (Author-Date):
    • Parenthetical: (Author & Author, Year) or (Organizational Author, Year)
    • Narrative: Author and Author (Year) found...
    • Multiple Sources: (Source 1, Year; Source 2, Year)

The Reference List

  • Definition: A complete list of all sources cited in your text, appearing at the end of the document.
  • Two-Way Correspondence: Every source cited in your text must have an entry in the reference list, and every entry in the reference list must be cited somewhere in the text. This ensures integrity and verifiability.
  • Model Document: The annotated reference list provides examples of how to format different types of sources (journal articles, technical reports, web resources) in a consistent style.

Avoiding Plagiarism

  • Definition: Presenting someone else's words, ideas, data, or creative work as your own, whether intentionally or accidentally, by failing to give proper credit.
  • Serious Consequences: Plagiarism is a severe professional and academic offense that can lead to career termination, legal action, and permanent damage to reputation.
  • Forms of Plagiarism:
    • Copying text word-for-word without quotation marks and a citation.
    • Paraphrasing someone's ideas without citing the source.
    • Using someone's data, charts, or images without attribution.
    • Submitting someone else's work as your own.
    • Self-plagiarism (reusing your own previously published work without disclosure).
  • The Simple Rule for Avoiding Plagiarism: If the information (idea, fact, statistic, argument, phrase) did not originate in your own mind, you must cite it. When in doubt, always cite.
  • Common Knowledge: The only exception to the citation rule. This refers to facts so widely known they require no attribution (e.g., "Water freezes at 32°F"). If unsure, cite it.

Writing Connection: Evidence in Research Papers

  • The skills learned in this section (strong sources, proper paraphrasing, consistent citation, avoiding plagiarism) are directly transferable and essential for academic research papers. The underlying principles remain the same, regardless of minor format variations between fields (e.g., MLA vs. APA).
