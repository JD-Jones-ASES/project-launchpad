Study Guide: The Memo Format

Overview

This section introduces the memorandum (memo) as a formal, structured document used for internal communication within an organization. It differentiates memos from emails by emphasizing their importance for announcements, requests, decisions, and record-keeping that require more formality and organization. The guide details the essential components of a memo, including the header block and a three-part body structure, to ensure clear and effective communication.

Key Concepts

  • Memo vs. Email: Memos are formal internal documents for structured communication on important matters, often for groups and filed for record. Emails are faster, more informal, and suited for daily, one-on-one, or routine communication.
  • Purpose of Memos: Memos serve to announce policies or changes, request action, summarize decisions, and create a formal record of important communications.
  • The Header Block: This is the essential formatted section at the top of every memo, containing the TO, FROM, DATE, and RE (Subject) fields, providing crucial context before the body is read.
  • Three-Part Memo Body: The main content of a memo follows a clear structure: an immediate purpose statement in the first paragraph, details in the middle paragraphs, and a clear action or closing in the final paragraph.
  • Specificity and Clarity: Memos demand precise language, especially in the RE line and the opening purpose statement, to ensure the reader quickly understands the message's core and can easily retrieve it later.

Important Terms

  • Memorandum (Memo): A formal written message used for internal communication within an organization, signaling that its content is organized, formatted, and important enough to be kept on file.
  • Header Block: The formatted section at the top of a memo containing the TO, FROM, DATE, and RE fields, which provide essential information about the memo's context.
  • RE Line (Regarding/Subject): The specific subject line of a memo, similar to an email's subject, used to clearly state the memo's topic and assist with filing and retrieval.
  • Purpose Statement: The first paragraph of a memo's body, which immediately states the reason for writing and the main point of the message.
  • Details (Memo Body): The middle paragraphs of a memo's body, providing the necessary information, context, explanations, and impact of the message.
  • Action or Closing (Memo Body): The final paragraph of a memo's body, which states any required actions, deadlines, next steps, or provides contact information for questions.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Memos

While professional emails are excellent for fast, informal daily communication, some internal messages within an organization require greater structure and formality. Memos are the standard format for these situations, indicating that the content is significant enough to be formally organized, formatted, and potentially archived.

What Is a Memo?

A memo is a structured internal document that signals the importance of its content. Its primary purposes include:

  • Announcing policies or changes: For example, a new company-wide policy or an update to an existing one.
  • Requesting action: Directing employees or departments to complete specific tasks or submit information by a deadline.
  • Summarizing decisions: Documenting the outcome of a meeting, project discussion, or committee review.
  • Creating a record: Establishing an official written account of events, timelines, or agreements for future reference.

When to Use a Memo Instead of an Email

Use a memo when the message meets one or more of these criteria:

  1. Directed to a group: Especially when the message impacts multiple individuals or departments.
  2. Important enough to be filed or referenced later: Content that needs to be officially archived.
  3. Announcing a policy, decision, or formal request: Topics requiring official communication and adherence.
  4. Needs the visual structure of a header block: The formal layout itself conveys importance and professionalism. For quick questions, routine updates, or one-on-one messages that don't require formal record-keeping, email remains the appropriate choice.

The Header Block

Every memo begins with a header block, a formatted section that immediately provides essential context:

  • TO: Identifies the intended audience. Be specific (e.g., "All Engineering Staff" instead of "Staff").
  • FROM: Identifies the sender. Include your title, as it conveys your authority for sending the message.
  • DATE: Specifies when the memo was written. Always use a complete, unambiguous format (e.g., "January 8, 2026," not "1/8/26").
  • RE (Regarding/Subject): States the memo's topic. This line functions exactly like an email's subject line. It must be specific and informative to help readers understand the content at a glance and to aid in filing and retrieval later. For example, "RE: New Parking Lot Hours Effective January 6" is much more useful than "RE: Parking."

Example of a Properly Formatted Header Block:

MEMORANDUM

TO: All Engineering Staff
FROM: Sarah Jenkins, Project Lead
DATE: January 8, 2026
RE: Updates to Code Review Process – Effective February 1, 2026

Key observations from this example:

  • TO: Clearly specifies "All Engineering Staff."
  • FROM: Includes sender's title, "Project Lead," establishing authority.
  • DATE: Uses a full, unambiguous date format.
  • RE: Is specific, indicating the topic and the effective date of the change.

The Body of a Memo

After the header block, the body of a memo typically follows a clear three-part structure:

  1. Purpose Statement (First Paragraph):

    • Immediately state the reason for writing the memo. Get straight to the point.
    • Answer the reader's question: "Why am I reading this?" within the first sentence or two.
    • Common Mistake: Avoid starting with lengthy background information or pleasantries. Present the core purpose first, then provide background if necessary.

  2. Details (Middle Paragraphs):

    • Provide all the necessary information, context, and explanations.
    • Cover "what" is changing, "why" it's happening, "how" it affects the reader, and "what" they need to know or do.
    • For longer memos, use bold headings within the body to organize information and help readers scan for specific details. Bulleted or numbered lists can also improve readability.

  3. Action or Closing (Final Paragraph):

    • Clearly state any required actions, deadlines, or next steps the reader must take.
    • If no immediate action is required, close by providing contact information (e.g., department, extension, email) for any questions readers may have.

A Complete Memo: Model Analysis

Let's examine a model memo that incorporates all these elements effectively:

MEMORANDUM

TO: All Employees, Riverside Manufacturing
FROM: David Chen, Human Resources Director
DATE: February 3, 2026
RE: New Flexible Work Schedule Policy — Effective March 1, 2026

Effective March 1, 2026, Riverside Manufacturing will implement a flexible
work schedule policy for all salaried employees. This memo explains the new
policy, who is eligible, and how to request a flexible schedule.

**What Is Changing**

Under the new policy, salaried employees may choose one of three schedule
options:
*   Standard: Monday–Friday, 8:00 AM – 5:00 PM (no change)
*   Early Start: Monday–Friday, 6:30 AM – 3:30 PM
*   Compressed: Monday–Thursday, 7:00 AM – 5:30 PM (Fridays off)

All options require 40 hours per week. Hourly employees are not eligible at
this time but will be considered in Phase 2 (targeted for July 2026).

**Why We Are Making This Change**

Employee surveys in October and December 2025 identified schedule flexibility
as the top-requested workplace improvement. Research from the Society for
Human Resource Management shows that flexible scheduling reduces turnover by
up to 20% and improves employee satisfaction scores.

**How to Request a Flexible Schedule**

1.  Complete the Flexible Schedule Request Form (available on the HR
    intranet page starting February 10).
2.  Submit the form to your direct supervisor for approval.
3.  Once approved, forward the signed form to HR at
    hr@riverside-mfg.com.
4.  New schedules take effect the first Monday after approval.

If you have questions about the policy or the request process, contact the
HR office at extension 2240 or hr@riverside-mfg.com.

Analysis of the Model Memo:

  • Header Block: Clear TO, FROM (with title), DATE, and highly specific RE line including the effective date.
  • Purpose Statement (Paragraph 1): Immediately announces the new policy and what the memo will cover, setting clear expectations.
  • Details (Paragraphs 2-4):
    • Uses bold subheadings ("What Is Changing," "Why We Are Making This Change," "How to Request a Flexible Schedule") to organize complex information.
    • Uses a bulleted list to clearly outline schedule options.
    • Provides specific dates and statistics (e.g., "October and December 2025," "up to 20%") for credibility.
    • Uses a numbered list for step-by-step instructions on how to request a schedule.
    • Explicitly states who is eligible and who is not, along with future considerations.
  • Action/Closing (Final Paragraph): Provides clear contact information for questions, fulfilling the closing requirement as no immediate action is universally required from the reader beyond understanding.

Opening a Memo: The Power of Directness

The "Before and After" example from the textbook highlights the critical importance of a direct purpose statement:

  • Before (Vague): "As you may be aware, the company has been considering various options for improving workplace satisfaction, and after extensive deliberation and consultation with multiple departments and external advisors, we have decided to implement some changes to how scheduling works around here." (42 words to say very little).
  • After (Direct): "Effective March 1, 2026, Riverside Manufacturing will implement a flexible work schedule policy for all salaried employees. This memo explains the new policy, who is eligible, and how to request a flexible schedule." (30 words, immediately informative).

The "After" version tells the reader exactly what is happening, when it's happening, and what the memo will cover, demonstrating the difference a clear purpose statement makes in effective communication.

  4. Review the sample memo about the "New Flexible Work Schedule Policy." Identify at least three specific techniques used within its body paragraphs to enhance clarity and readability.
