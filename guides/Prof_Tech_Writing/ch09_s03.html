Study Guide: Data and Evidence in Reports

Overview

This section emphasizes the critical role of data and evidence in creating useful and credible reports. It outlines "The Evidence Rule," which states that every significant claim must be supported by verifiable information. The section also covers various types of evidence, best practices for presenting numerical data using tables and figures, and the importance of attributing information to maintain credibility and avoid plagiarism.

Key Concepts

  • The Evidence Rule: Every significant claim made in a report must be supported by concrete data or evidence. If a claim cannot be proven with data, it should either be softened or the necessary data must be found. This rule ensures the report's credibility and factual basis.
  • Types of Evidence: Reports utilize different forms of evidence to support claims:
    • Quantitative Data: Numerical information (e.g., measurements, percentages, dollar amounts) used to show precise figures and statistical facts.
    • Qualitative Data: Descriptive information based on observations, interviews, or descriptions, providing context and insight into experiences or opinions.
    • Documentary Evidence: Existing written or recorded materials such as records, logs, emails, or photographs that serve as proof.
    • Expert Testimony: Statements or opinions provided by individuals who possess specialized knowledge or authority in a particular field.
  • Effective Data Presentation: Numerical data should not be buried in text.
    • Tables are best for presenting precise values, allowing readers to look up specific numbers and compare data across categories.
    • Figures (charts, graphs, diagrams) are ideal for visualizing trends, proportions, relationships, and telling a visual story with data.
  • Attribution and Credibility: Giving credit to the original source of information, data, or ideas is essential. Proper attribution allows readers to verify claims, strengthens the report's credibility, and protects the writer from accusations of plagiarism. Unsourced statistics can severely damage a report's trustworthiness.

Important Terms

  • Data: Raw facts, numbers, measurements, or observations collected for analysis. It is the raw material from which evidence is derived.
  • Evidence: Information that supports or proves a claim or finding. It can include numbers, dates, measurements, observations, or references to documented sources.
  • Table: A grid of rows and columns used to organize and present data, typically for precise values and comparisons.
  • Figure: Any visual element in a report that is not a table, such as charts, graphs, diagrams, or photographs. Used to illustrate trends, relationships, or conceptual information.
  • Attribution: The act of giving credit to the source of information, data, or ideas presented in a report. Usually done with brief in-text references.
  • Citation: A formal reference to a source, following a specific formatting style (e.g., APA, MLA). Citations allow readers to locate and verify the original source and protect against plagiarism.

Detailed Notes

The Foundation of a Useful Report: Evidence

The primary difference between a credible report and a mere opinion is the presence of evidence. Every claim made in an informational report must be backed by verifiable data. For example, stating "Sales have been pretty good lately" is an unsupported opinion, whereas "Q3 sales totaled $1.34 million, a 12% increase over Q2's $1.2 million" is a data-supported fact. Reports are built on facts and require substantial support for all assertions.

The Evidence Rule

This fundamental rule in report writing dictates that every significant claim requires support. Before including a statement, writers should ask themselves if they could provide data to prove it if challenged by a skeptical reader. If no data exists, the claim should either be removed or softened to reflect uncertainty. For instance, instead of an "unsupported claim" like "The new training program is working," a "supported claim" would be "Since the new training program launched on September 1, workplace safety incidents have decreased from an average of 4.2 per month (Jan–Aug) to 1.5 per month (Sep–Nov)."

Types of Evidence in Reports

Different situations call for different kinds of evidence:

  1. Quantitative Data: This includes all numerical information such as numbers, measurements, percentages, and dollar amounts. It's used for precise, measurable facts (e.g., "Response time averaged 3.2 minutes.").
  2. Qualitative Data: This type of evidence consists of observations, descriptions, and interview responses. It provides depth and context, often explaining why something is happening (e.g., "Three of five survey respondents described the new interface as 'confusing.'").
  3. Documentary Evidence: This refers to existing records, logs, emails, photographs, or any other documented sources. It's used to verify past events or existing conditions (e.g., "According to the maintenance log, the HVAC unit was last serviced on March 14, 2025.").
  4. Expert Testimony: Statements or opinions from qualified professionals or specialists who have expertise relevant to the report's subject matter (e.g., "The structural engineer confirmed that the crack does not affect the building's load-bearing capacity.").

When formulating claims, it's crucial to move from vague statements to specific, data-supported facts. For instance, instead of "Customer satisfaction has improved," provide details like "Customer satisfaction scores increased from 3.4 to 4.1 out of 5.0 between January and June 2025, based on 1,247 survey responses."

Tables and Figures for Data Presentation

When a report includes numerical data, it's often best presented visually rather than buried in paragraphs of text.

  • Tables are ideal when readers need to look up specific values, compare numbers across different categories, or require precise data.
  • Figures (such as charts, graphs, diagrams, or photographs) are best used to show trends, proportions, or relationships, helping to tell a visual story with the data.

Rules for Tables and Figures in Reports:

  1. Numbering: All tables and figures must be numbered sequentially (e.g., Table 1, Table 2; Figure 1, Figure 2). Tables and figures are numbered in separate sequences.
  2. Descriptive Titles: Each table and figure needs a clear, descriptive title that explains its content (e.g., "Table 3: Monthly Volunteer Hours, July–December 2025").
  3. In-text References: Every table and figure must be referenced within the report's text. Don't just place them without explanation (e.g., "As shown in Table 3, volunteer hours peaked in October.").
  4. Placement: Tables and figures should be placed close to the text that discusses them, ideally on the same or adjacent page.
  5. Clear Labels: All rows, columns, and axes must be clearly labeled, including units of measurement (e.g., dollars, hours, kilograms) in column headers, not in individual cells.

Presenting data effectively means transforming a wall of numbers into an easily digestible visual, complemented by text that highlights key takeaways and connects the data to objectives.

Attribution and Credibility

When incorporating data, information, or ideas from external sources, attribution is mandatory. This means giving credit to the original source. In professional reports, this is typically done through brief in-text references rather than formal footnotes. Examples include:

  • "According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, the unemployment rate in September 2025 was 3.8%."
  • "The 2024 Customer Experience Survey (conducted by Harris Associates) found that 73% of respondents preferred online support."

Citation refers to a formal reference to a source, following a specific format (like APA or MLA). Citations serve two crucial purposes:

  1. They enable readers to verify your claims by checking the original source.
  2. They protect you from accusations of presenting someone else's work as your own (plagiarism).

Common Mistake: A significant error is using precise numbers or statistics without providing their source. For example, stating "87% of employers value communication skills" without attribution undermines credibility. If a source cannot be found for specific data, it should not be included, as unsourced statistics do more harm than good to a report's trustworthiness.

Model Document: Incident Report - Chemistry Lab Spill

The provided Incident Report serves as an excellent example of a document heavily reliant on data and evidence. Key aspects demonstrated include:

  • Factual, Neutral Tone: The report objectively describes events without assigning blame (e.g., it doesn't call the student "careless").
  • Specific Details: It includes precise measurements, dates, and times (e.g., "10:22 AM on October 17, 2025," "500 mL beaker," "400 mL solution," "3-foot area," "12-minute interruption").
  • Documentation of Compliance: Mentioning that "Student A was wearing required safety goggles and a lab apron" confirms that proper precautions were in place.
  • Focus on Contributing Factors: The report identifies systemic issues (narrow bench, beaker placement) rather than solely blaming an individual, which helps in preventing future incidents.
  • Clear Recommendations: Based on the identified factors, actionable recommendations are provided.

This model report highlights how collecting and presenting detailed, objective evidence is crucial for accuracy, accountability, and problem-solving in real-world professional contexts.

Describe a scenario where you would choose to present some of this data in a table and another scenario where you would use a figure (like a line graph or pie chart). Justify your choices based on the principles discussed.
  4. Why is attribution crucial in professional reports? Besides avoiding plagiarism, how does giving credit to sources enhance the credibility and utility of your report for the reader?
