Study Guide: Process Descriptions vs. Instructions

Overview

This section clarifies the critical distinction between process descriptions and instructions in technical writing. While both might cover similar topics, instructions guide a reader through performing a task, whereas process descriptions explain how something works or happens, for a reader who primarily wants to understand. The key difference lies in the document's purpose and the audience's goal – to act or to comprehend.

Key Concepts

  • Purpose-Driven Documents: The primary difference between instructions and process descriptions is not the subject matter, but the purpose of the document and the audience's goal.
  • Instructions:
    • Purpose: To tell the reader how to do something.
    • Voice: Uses the imperative mood (commands) and speaks directly to the reader (second person).
    • Assumption: The reader will actively perform the task.
  • Process Descriptions:
    • Purpose: To explain how something works or how something happens.
    • Voice: Uses the indicative mood (states facts) and typically uses the third person.
    • Assumption: The reader wants to understand the process, not necessarily perform it.
  • Indicative Mood: A verb form used to state facts or describe what happens, common in process descriptions.
  • Imperative Mood: A verb form used to give commands, common in instructions.
  • Two Types of Process Descriptions:
    • Process Description (General): Explains a series of events or actions (e.g., how photosynthesis works).
    • Mechanism Description: Explains how an object, device, or system is built and how its parts interact (e.g., how a car engine converts fuel to motion).
  • The "Why" Test: A practical tip to determine document type: if the reader asks "How do I do this?", write instructions; if they ask "How does this work?" or "Why does this happen?", write a process description.

Important Terms

  • Instructions: Step-by-step directions that tell a reader how to perform a task. They use imperative mood and second person.
  • Process Description: A written explanation of how something happens or how a system works, intended for a reader who wants to understand the process rather than perform it.
  • Imperative Mood: A verb form used to give commands or make requests, speaking directly to the reader (e.g., "Press the button").
  • Indicative Mood: A verb form used to state facts or describe what happens in reality (e.g., "The pump circulates water").
  • Mechanism Description: A specific type of process description that explains how an object, device, or system is constructed and how its component parts work together.

Detailed Notes

1. The Fundamental Difference: Purpose and Audience Goal

The core distinction between instructions and process descriptions lies not in the topic, but in the purpose of the document and the audience's objective.

  • Instructions are action-oriented. Their goal is to enable the reader to successfully complete a task. They guide the reader through a series of steps they must take.
    • Characteristics: Use imperative verbs ("Turn," "Mix," "Attach"), address the reader directly (second person), and assume the reader will be performing the actions.
    • Example: "How to Change a Flat Tire."
  • Process Descriptions are understanding-oriented. Their goal is to explain how something functions, how a sequence of events unfolds, or how a system operates.
    • Characteristics: Use indicative verbs ("The engine starts," "Water circulates"), typically use third person, and assume the reader wants to gain knowledge, not necessarily to intervene or act.
    • Example: "How a Car Engine Converts Fuel to Motion."

Think of a biology lesson: students might get instructions for a lab experiment measuring photosynthesis, and a separate process description explaining the biochemical steps of photosynthesis. Same topic, different documents for different purposes.

2. Recognizing Which Document Type You Need

The simplest way to decide whether to write instructions or a process description is to ask: "Will my reader do this task, or do they just need to understand it?"

  • If the reader needs to act, write instructions.
  • If the reader needs to comprehend, write a process description.

3. Grammatical Moods: Indicative vs. Imperative

The choice of verb mood is a key indicator and differentiator:

  • Indicative Mood: This mood states facts or describes reality. It's the standard for factual statements.
    • Example: "The pump circulates water through the filter."
    • Usage: Primarily used in process descriptions because they describe what happens.
  • Imperative Mood: This mood is used to give commands, orders, or directions.
    • Example: "Circulate water through the filter."
    • Usage: Exclusively used in instructions because they tell the reader what to do.

4. Comparing a Practical Example (Coffee Machine)

Consider how the same information about a coffee machine changes:

Instructions (Imperative Mood):

  1. Fill the reservoir with cold water.
  2. Insert a coffee pod into the chamber.
  3. Press the brew button.
  4. Wait 60 seconds for brewing to complete.
  5. Remove your mug from the drip tray.
  • Focus: What the user needs to do. Short, direct commands.

Process Description (Indicative Mood): When a user presses the brew button, the machine's heating element raises the water temperature to 200°F in approximately 30 seconds. A pump then forces the heated water through the coffee pod at 15 PSI, extracting flavor compounds as it passes through the grounds. The brewed coffee drips into the mug below over a period of about 60 seconds. A sensor detects when the reservoir is empty and automatically shuts off the pump.

  • Focus: What the machine does. Detailed explanation of mechanisms, timing, and actions.

5. Two Main Types of Process Descriptions

Process descriptions aren't a monolithic category; they generally fall into two sub-types:

  • General Process Description:
    • Focus: Explains a series of events or actions, often sequential or cyclical. It answers "how does something happen?"
    • Examples: How a bill becomes a law, how the water cycle works, how the internet routes data packets, how a company hires new employees.
  • Mechanism Description:
    • Focus: Explains how an object, device, or system is constructed and how its various parts work together to achieve a function. It answers "how does something work?" or "how is this built?"
    • Examples: How a bicycle's gear system works, how a smartphone's touchscreen detects input, how a building's HVAC system regulates temperature. While mechanism descriptions often include elements of processes (e.g., "when the user shifts gears, the derailleur moves the chain"), their primary focus is on the components and their interaction. Both types, however, are fundamentally explanatory, not instructional.

6. The "Why" Test (Writing Tip)

To quickly differentiate:

  • If the reader's main question is "How do I do this?" — write instructions.
  • If their main question is "How does this work?" or "Why does this happen?" — write a process description. Always consider whether your reader needs to act or simply understand.
