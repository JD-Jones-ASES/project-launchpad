Study Guide: Writing Numbered Steps in Instructions

Overview

This section explains the foundational principles for writing effective numbered steps in instructions and procedures. It emphasizes using the imperative mood, limiting each step to a single action, and applying consistent formatting. The goal is to create clear, concise, and easy-to-follow instructions that make complex tasks manageable for any reader.

Key Concepts

  • Imperative Mood: Every step should be a direct command starting with a verb, telling the reader exactly what to do. This ensures clarity, conciseness, and an action-oriented tone.
  • One Action Per Step: Each numbered step should contain only one discrete action. Combining multiple actions can confuse readers and lead to skipped parts of a procedure.
  • Substeps: Use lettered or indented substeps for smaller actions that logically group under a single main step, enhancing organization without breaking the flow.
  • Numbered vs. Bulleted Lists: Use numbered lists when the order of steps is crucial (most instructions). Use bulleted lists only when items can be completed in any order (e.g., a list of materials).
  • Consistent Formatting: Maintain uniform formatting for text elements (e.g., bolding button names, italicizing menu options) throughout the instructions to reduce reader confusion.
  • Expected Results: Include the anticipated outcome after critical steps to help readers confirm they have successfully completed the action.

Important Terms

  • Imperative Mood: A grammatical mood used to express direct commands or requests. In instructions, steps written in this mood start with a verb and address the reader directly.
  • Substep: A smaller action or mini-step nested within a main numbered step, typically indicated by letters (a), (b), etc., or indentation.
  • Sequential Steps: Actions arranged in a specific order where the completion of one step is dependent on the completion of the previous one.
  • Parallel Steps: Actions or tasks that can be performed simultaneously, in any order, or independently of each other.
  • Run-On Step: A common mistake where multiple unrelated or distinct actions are combined into a single numbered step, causing confusion for the reader.

Detailed Notes

The Importance of Clear Numbered Steps

Numbered steps form the core of any instructions. Their clarity and organization directly determine the success or failure of a procedure. Well-structured steps simplify complex tasks, while vague or poorly ordered steps can make even simple tasks frustrating.

Using the Imperative Mood

The imperative mood is the natural and most effective voice for instructions. It is characterized by:

  • Beginning with a verb: Each step starts directly with an action verb (e.g., "Remove," "Press," "Click").
  • Direct address: It implicitly speaks directly to the reader ("You").
  • Clarity and Conciseness: It avoids unnecessary words and ambiguity, focusing solely on the action required.

Examples of Imperative Mood vs. Alternatives:

  • Correct (Imperative): "Remove the back panel."
  • Avoid (Passive): "The back panel should be removed." (Hides who performs the action)
  • Avoid (Wordy): "You will want to go ahead and remove the back panel at this point." (Buries the action in filler)
  • Avoid (Conditional): "The user may remove the back panel if desired." (Makes the step seem optional)

The imperative mood ensures that instructions are short, direct, and unambiguous, leaving no doubt about what the reader needs to do.

One Action Per Step

A fundamental rule for clear instructions is to limit each numbered step to a single, distinct action.

  • Why it matters: When multiple actions are packed into one step, readers can lose track, complete only part of the step, or become confused about whether they've finished.
  • Common Mistake: The Run-On Step: Do not combine steps to "save space." Readers follow instructions sequentially, completing one step before moving to the next. Combining "Remove the old filter" and "Rinse the housing" into a single step might lead a reader to skip the rinsing.

Example:

  • Instead of (Multiple Actions): "Open the lid, remove the old filter, rinse the filter housing with warm water, dry it thoroughly, insert the new filter, and close the lid."
  • Use (One Action Per Step):
    1. Open the lid.
    2. Remove the old filter.
    3. Rinse the filter housing with warm water.
    4. Dry the housing thoroughly with a clean cloth.
    5. Insert the new filter.
    6. Close the lid.

When to Use Substeps

While each main step should have one action, sometimes a single main goal involves several smaller, related actions. In these cases, substeps are appropriate.

  • Purpose: Substeps (e.g., (a), (b), (c)) are used when smaller actions logically belong together under a single umbrella task, and numbering them as separate main steps would break the context.
  • Formatting: Substeps are typically lettered or indented beneath the main step.
  • Depth Limit: Avoid nesting substeps more than one level deep. If you find yourself needing sub-sub-steps, it's an indication that the instructions should be broken into separate main sections or procedures.

Example:

  1. Connect the printer to your computer. (a) Plug one end of the USB cable into the printer's USB port. (b) Plug the other end into any available USB port on your computer. (c) Wait for the “New device detected” notification to appear on your screen.

Numbering and Formatting Guidelines

Numbering vs. Bullets:

  • Numbered Lists: Use for sequential steps where the order is critical. This is the default for most instructions.
  • Bulleted Lists: Use only for items where the order does not matter (e.g., a list of materials needed, or parallel tasks that can be done in any sequence).

Additional Formatting Guidelines:

  • Start with a Verb: Every step should begin with an action verb (e.g., "Click," "Open," "Remove," "Insert").
  • Keep Steps Short: Aim for one to two sentences per step. Lengthy explanations should be placed before the step or in a dedicated "Note" section.
  • Use Consistent Formatting: Apply the same formatting (e.g., bolding, italics) to similar elements (e.g., button names, menu options) throughout the entire set of instructions. Inconsistency can lead to confusion.
  • Include Expected Results: After critical steps, describe what the reader should see or experience. This confirms they have completed the step correctly (e.g., "The status light turns green," "A confirmation dialog appears").

Model Document: Setting Up a New Employee Email Account

This example demonstrates all the principles discussed:

  • Clear Title, Purpose, Prerequisites: Sets the stage for the user.
  • Numbered Steps: Used for the main procedure, indicating sequential actions.
  • Imperative Mood: Each step starts with a verb (e.g., "Open," "Enter," "Click," "Create").
  • Substeps: Used for the password requirements (a, b, c) under the main "Create a new password" step, as these are related sub-actions.
  • Expected Results: Included after key actions (e.g., "The system displays a 'Change Your Password' screen," "A confirmation message appears: 'Password updated successfully.'").
  • Result Statement and Troubleshooting: Provides closure and guidance for common issues.

  2. Why is it crucial to include only "one action per step" in instructions? What potential problems can arise if multiple actions are combined into a single numbered step?
  3. When is it appropriate to use substeps (e.g., (a), (b), (c)) instead of creating entirely new main numbered steps? What is the recommended limit for nesting substeps, and why?
