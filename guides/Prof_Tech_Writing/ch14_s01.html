Study Guide: Planning a Presentation

Overview

This section emphasizes that effective presentations begin long before opening a slide application. It highlights the critical importance of thoroughly planning by defining your purpose, understanding your audience, and identifying a single "key message" you want them to remember. It also introduces a structured approach to organizing content around key points and following a three-part presentation flow for maximum impact.

Key Concepts

  • The Three Foundational Questions: Before creating any slides, every presenter must answer: "What is my purpose?", "Who is my audience?", and "What is the one thing I want them to remember?" Skipping these leads to unfocused and ineffective presentations.
  • The "One Thing" Test (Key Message): This is the single most important idea your audience should retain. If your presentation attempts to convey too many ideas, listeners will remember none clearly. Every element of your presentation should support this one core message.
  • Defining Purpose: Presentations can aim to inform, persuade, or both. Clearly identifying your purpose guides content selection and delivery.
  • Understanding Your Audience: Knowing what your audience already knows, needs to know, and what will make them care is essential for tailoring your content and examples effectively.
  • Organizing by Key Points (Talking Points): Once the key message is established, the presentation should be built around 3-5 supporting "talking points." These brief phrases act as the skeleton of your presentation, each backed by evidence and examples.
  • The "So What?" Filter: For every piece of information or point you consider including, ask yourself: "So what? Why does my audience care about this?" If you can't answer it concisely, that point likely doesn't belong.
  • The Three-Part Presentation Structure: Most effective presentations follow an "Opening (10-15%), Body (75-80%), Closing (10-15%)" format. Each part has a distinct role, from hooking the audience and stating the key message, to presenting detailed points, and finally summarizing and delivering a call to action.

Important Terms

  • Presentation: A spoken communication, usually supported by visual aids, delivered to an audience for a specific purpose.
  • Slide deck: The collection of slides used as visual support during a presentation.
  • Key message: The single most important idea you want your audience to remember after your presentation.
  • Audience engagement: Techniques and strategies that capture and hold the audience's attention.
  • Call to action: A clear statement telling the audience what you want them to do next.
  • Talking points: Brief phrases or notes that remind a speaker of the main ideas to cover.
  • Template: A pre-designed slide layout that provides consistent formatting across a presentation.
  • Transition (presentation): A verbal or visual bridge that connects one section of a presentation to the next.
  • Visual hierarchy: The arrangement of elements on a slide so that the most important information stands out first.
  • Handout: A printed or digital document given to the audience summarizing the presentation's key points.
  • Speaker notes: Written prompts visible only to the presenter, used to guide delivery without reading slides.
  • Bullet point: A short phrase or sentence in a list, marked by a dot or symbol, used to organize information on slides.
  • Q&A: The question-and-answer period following a presentation, where the audience asks questions and the speaker responds.
  • Bridge (rhetorical): A technique for redirecting a conversation or question back to your key message.

Detailed Notes

The Importance of Planning

Before diving into slide design, font choices, or even rehearsing, the most crucial step in creating a strong presentation is thorough planning. Many failed presentations skip this vital stage, leading speakers to simply dump information onto slides without a clear focus, ultimately wasting both their time and the audience's. Effective planning ensures clarity, coherence, and impact.

Purpose, Audience, Key Message: The Three Pillars

These three questions form the foundation of any successful presentation:

  1. What is my purpose? Are you primarily trying to:

    • Inform? (e.g., a lab report presentation)
    • Persuade? (e.g., a proposal presentation)
    • Both? (e.g., a project update that informs stakeholders and persuades them the project is on track) Defining your purpose dictates the type of information you include and how you present it.

  2. Who is my audience? Tailoring your presentation to your audience is key. Consider:

    • What do they already know? Avoid stating the obvious or, conversely, using jargon they won't understand.
    • What do they need to know? Focus on information relevant to them.
    • What will make them care? Connect your topic to their interests, concerns, or goals. A cybersecurity presentation for IT students will differ greatly from one for school board members.

  3. What is the one thing (Key Message)? This is arguably the most critical question. Your key message is the single, most important idea you want your audience to remember even if they forget everything else.

    • The "One Thing" Test: You should be able to complete the sentence: "After my presentation, my audience will know that __________." If you can't articulate a single, clear phrase, your presentation lacks focus.
    • Why it's crucial: Audiences retain very little of what they hear. If you try to communicate too many ideas, none will stick. A single, well-supported idea is far more memorable. Every slide, example, and minute of your presentation must support this one key message; anything that doesn't should be cut.

Example of Focused vs. Unfocused Purpose:

  • Unfocused: "My presentation is about renewable energy, covering solar, wind, geothermal, hydroelectric, nuclear, fossil fuel history, climate change, and government policies." (Too broad, no clear takeaway.)
  • Focused: "My presentation argues that our school district should install solar panels on school rooftops, which would save $45,000 per year in energy costs and reduce our carbon footprint by 30%." (Clear key message, specific audience, measurable claim.)

Organizing by Key Points

Once your key message is defined, structure your presentation around three to five key points that directly support it. These act as the "pillars" of your argument.

  • Talking Points: These are brief phrases or notes that outline the main ideas you'll discuss. They are not full sentences or scripts, but rather the skeleton of your presentation. Each talking point should be supported by evidence, examples, or visuals.
  • Example (for the solar panel presentation):
    1. Current energy costs are unsustainable for the district.
    2. Solar panels would pay for themselves within seven years.
    3. Three neighboring districts have already switched and report savings.
    4. Installation can happen over the summer with no disruption to classes. Notice how each point directly contributes to persuading the school board about solar panels; irrelevant historical facts are omitted.
  • The "So What?" Filter: A powerful tool for content curation. For every potential point, ask: "So what? Why does my audience care about this?" If you cannot provide a compelling, one-sentence answer, that point likely doesn't belong and should be saved for the Q&A if asked.

Presentation Structure: The Three-Part Plan

Most effective presentations follow a clear, three-part structure:

  1. Opening (10–15% of your time): This is your chance to make a strong first impression.

    • Hook the audience: Grab their attention immediately (e.g., a surprising fact, a brief story, a compelling question, a bold statement). Avoid weak starts like "Um, so, my presentation is about..."
    • State your key message: Clearly articulate your "one thing" upfront.
    • Preview your main points: Give the audience a roadmap of what you will cover.
    • The audience decides whether to pay attention in the first 30 seconds.

  2. Body (75–80% of your time): This is where you deliver your main content.

    • Present your key points: Discuss each talking point one at a time.
    • Provide evidence and examples: Support your points with data, stories, demonstrations, or visuals.
    • Use transitions: Employ verbal or visual "bridges" to move smoothly and logically between key points, helping the audience follow your flow.

  3. Closing (10–15% of your time): End powerfully and memorably.

    • Summarize your key points: Briefly recap the main takeaways you covered.
    • Restate your key message: Reiterate your "one thing" to reinforce it.
    • Deliver your call to action: Clearly state what you want the audience to do, think, or remember as a result of your presentation.

Example Time Allocation for a 5-Minute Presentation:

  • Opening: 30–45 seconds
  • Body: 3 minutes 45 seconds – 4 minutes 15 seconds
  • Closing: 30–45 seconds

Activity: The 60-Second Pitch

A useful exercise to hone your planning skills is the "60-Second Pitch." Without slides, articulate your key message and perhaps one or two main supporting ideas in under a minute. This forces you to be concise, identify your "one thing," and deliver it clearly. If classmates can't state your key message back, it needs more focus.

Review Questions

  1. Why is it crucial to answer "What is my purpose? Who is my audience? What is the one thing?" before you even start creating slides for a presentation? What are the potential consequences of skipping this planning step?
  2. Explain the "One Thing" Test. Why is focusing on a single key message more effective than trying to convey multiple ideas, given how audiences typically retain information?
  3. Imagine you need to give a presentation about the benefits of online learning to two different audiences: high school students and school administrators. How would your approach to "Audience" differ for each, especially in terms of what they need to know and what will make them care?
  4. You've identified your key message for a presentation. Now, you have several facts and anecdotes you could include. Describe how you would apply the "So What?" Filter to decide which content makes it into your presentation's body and which might be saved for Q&A.
  5. Outline the three-part structure of an effective presentation (Opening, Body, Closing) and explain the specific goal of each section. Why is it important to dedicate different percentages of your time to each part?
