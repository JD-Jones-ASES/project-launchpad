Chapter 11, Section 1: What Makes an Animal? Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental characteristics that define an animal, distinguishing them from other living organisms like plants and fungi. It explores how animals move, obtain energy, and interact with their environment through senses. The section also touches upon the vast diversity of animal species and explains why humans are classified as animals.

Key Concepts

  • Defining Characteristics of Animals: Animals are unique among living things because they possess three key traits: they move on their own, they eat other organisms for energy, and they have senses to detect their surroundings.
  • Animals as Consumers: Unlike plants that produce their own food, animals must consume (eat) other living things to acquire the energy necessary for survival and growth.
  • Importance of Senses: Animal senses (sight, hearing, smell, taste, touch) are crucial for finding food, recognizing danger, and communicating with others.
  • Humans Are Animals: Based on these defining characteristics, humans are classified as animals, specifically belonging to the mammal group.
  • Animal Diversity and Classification: Scientists have identified millions of animal species, and they organize these diverse creatures into groups based on shared characteristics to better understand them.

Important Terms

Animal : A living thing that moves on its own, eats other organisms for energy, and has senses to detect the world around it. Consumer : An organism that obtains energy by eating other living things (plants, other animals, or both). Species : A group of living things that are very similar and can produce offspring together.

Detailed Notes

1. Introduction: What Makes an Animal Unique?

  • Animals are a fascinating and diverse group of living organisms on Earth.
  • While plants and fungi are also living, animals have specific traits that set them apart.

2. Three Key Characteristics of Animals

Scientists have identified three main characteristics that distinguish animals:

A. Animals Move on Their Own

  • Definition: Unlike plants, which are typically rooted in one place, animals are capable of locomotion – moving from one place to another independently.
  • Examples: Birds fly, snakes slither, fish swim, and even simpler animals like sea sponges can move water through their bodies.
  • Reasons for Movement: Animals move for essential survival activities, including:
    • Finding food sources.
    • Escaping from predators or other dangers.
    • Locating mates for reproduction.

B. Animals Eat Other Organisms for Energy (Consumers)

  • Definition: Animals cannot produce their own food using sunlight (like plants do through photosynthesis). Instead, they must obtain energy by consuming other living things.
  • Food Sources: Animals' diets can include plants (herbivores), other animals (carnivores), or both (omnivores).
  • Scientific Term: Animals are called consumers because they consume (eat) food to get the energy they need to live, grow, and reproduce.

C. Animals Have Senses

  • Definition: Animals possess specialized organs and systems that allow them to detect and interpret information from their surrounding environment.
  • Types of Senses: These include common senses like sight, hearing, smell, taste, and touch, though the specific senses can vary greatly among different animal species.
  • Purpose of Senses: Senses are vital for:
    • Locating food.
    • Detecting potential dangers or predators.
    • Communicating with other animals of their species.
  • Examples: Hawks use keen eyesight to spot prey from above; dogs have an exceptional sense of smell; even simple animals like earthworms can sense light and vibrations in their environment.

3. Are Humans Animals?

  • Yes, humans are animals! We meet all three defining criteria:
    • We move on our own (walk, run, etc.).
    • We eat other organisms for energy.
    • We have complex senses (sight, hearing, smell, taste, touch, etc.).
  • Classification: Humans belong to the group of animals known as mammals, alongside creatures like dogs, cats, and whales.

4. How Many Animals Are There?

  • Vast Diversity: Scientists have discovered and named approximately 1.5 million animal species to date.
  • Undiscovered Species: It is estimated that millions more animal species may exist but have not yet been discovered, particularly in hard-to-reach environments like the deep ocean and dense tropical rainforests.
  • Need for Organization: With such an enormous variety of life, scientists organize animals into groups based on their shared characteristics. This classification helps in understanding their relationships and evolutionary history.

5. Thinking About Exceptions: The Venus Flytrap

  • The Venus flytrap is a plant that appears to "move" (snapping shut) and "eat" (insects).
  • However, it is still classified as a plant because:
    • It does not move from place to place on its own.
    • It primarily gets its energy through photosynthesis, making its own food from sunlight. Catching insects provides supplementary nutrients, not its main energy source.
  • This example reinforces the importance of considering all three characteristics when defining an animal.

Review Questions

  1. Imagine you encounter an unknown living organism. What three key characteristics would you look for to determine if it is an animal?
  2. Explain the term "consumer" in the context of animals. How does an animal's energy acquisition differ fundamentally from that of a plant?
  3. Why are senses so important for animals? Provide specific examples of how different senses benefit animals in their daily lives.
  4. Based on the characteristics discussed, articulate why humans are definitively classified as animals, even though we often categorize ourselves separately.
  5. With millions of animal species already identified and potentially millions more undiscovered, why do scientists find it necessary to organize and group animals rather than just listing them individually?
