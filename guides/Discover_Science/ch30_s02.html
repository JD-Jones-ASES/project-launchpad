Cycle 6 Vocabulary Review Study Guide

Overview

This section provides a comprehensive review of key vocabulary terms from the four units within Cycle 6: Life Science (Life Cycles and Adaptation), Earth Science (Natural Resources and Conservation), Chemistry (Putting Atoms Together), and Physics (Electricity and Magnetism). Mastering these terms is crucial for understanding fundamental scientific concepts and for success on your final assessment, which may also include terms from earlier cycles.

Key Concepts

  • Life Cycles and Adaptation: Living things undergo distinct stages from birth to death (life cycles), often involving significant transformations like metamorphosis. Organisms possess adaptations (structural or behavioral) that enhance their survival in specific environments. Natural selection drives the evolution of these adaptations, with evidence found in fossils, and can lead to extinction if species cannot adapt.
  • Natural Resources and Conservation: Earth provides natural resources essential for human needs. These are categorized as renewable (replenish quickly, like sunlight and trees) or nonrenewable (form over millions of years and are finite, like fossil fuels and metals). Conservation efforts are vital for protecting these resources and safeguarding endangered species from extinction.
  • Atomic Structure and Chemical Bonding: Atoms combine through chemical bonds to form molecules and compounds. A molecule consists of two or more bonded atoms, while a compound specifically involves two or more different types of atoms. Chemical formulas concisely represent the atomic composition of these substances. A fundamental principle in chemistry is the conservation of matter, stating that atoms are rearranged but not created or destroyed in chemical reactions.
  • Electricity and Magnetism: Matter possesses electric charge (positive or negative). Static electricity involves a buildup of charge, while current electricity is a steady flow of charge through a conductor (materials that allow charge to flow easily). Circuits provide a complete path for current. Insulators prevent current flow. Magnets create magnetic fields and attract certain metals. Electromagnets are temporary magnets created by electric current. Generators convert motion into electricity, while electric motors convert electricity into motion.

Important Terms

Life Science — Life Cycles and Adaptation

  • Life cycle : The series of stages a living thing goes through from birth to death.
  • Germination : When a seed begins to sprout and grow into a new plant.
  • Metamorphosis : A dramatic change in body form during an organism's life cycle (e.g., tadpole to frog, caterpillar to butterfly).
  • Adaptation : A feature or behavior that helps an organism survive in its environment.
  • Structural adaptation : A physical feature that helps survival (e.g., thick fur, sharp claws, cactus spines).
  • Behavioral adaptation : An action or behavior that helps survival (e.g., migration, hibernation, camouflage).
  • Natural selection : The process by which organisms with helpful traits survive longer and have more offspring, so those traits become more common.
  • Fossil : Evidence of an organism that lived long ago, preserved in rock or other material.
  • Extinction : When every member of a species has died and no more exist.

Earth Science — Natural Resources and Conservation

  • Natural resource : Something from nature that people use to meet their needs.
  • Renewable resource : A resource that can be replaced or regrown within a human lifetime (e.g., sunlight, wind, trees, water).
  • Nonrenewable resource : A resource that takes millions of years to form and cannot be replaced once used up (e.g., coal, oil, natural gas, metals).
  • Fossil fuel : A fuel formed from the remains of ancient organisms buried and changed over millions of years (coal, oil, natural gas).
  • Conservation : The careful use and protection of natural resources.
  • Endangered species : A type of living thing that is in danger of becoming extinct.

Chemistry — Putting Atoms Together

  • Chemical bond : A force that holds atoms together in a molecule or compound.
  • Molecule : Two or more atoms bonded together.
  • Compound : A molecule made of two or more different types of atoms bonded together (e.g., H2O, CO2, NaCl).
  • Chemical formula : A shorthand way to show which atoms are in a molecule and how many of each (e.g., H2O means 2 hydrogen and 1 oxygen).
  • Conservation of matter : The principle that matter is never created or destroyed in a chemical reaction — atoms just rearrange.

Physics — Electricity and Magnetism

  • Electric charge : A property of matter; can be positive or negative.
  • Static electricity : A buildup of electric charge on an object.
  • Current electricity : A steady flow of electric charges through a conductor.
  • Circuit : A complete path through which electricity can flow.
  • Conductor : A material that allows electricity to flow through it easily (e.g., metals like copper and aluminum).
  • Insulator : A material that does not allow electricity to flow through it easily (e.g., rubber, plastic, wood).
  • Magnet : An object that produces a magnetic field and attracts certain metals (iron, nickel, cobalt).
  • Magnetic field : The invisible area around a magnet where its force can be felt.
  • Electromagnet : A magnet created by running electric current through a coil of wire.
  • Generator : A machine that converts motion into electricity using magnets and wire coils.
  • Electric motor : A machine that converts electricity into motion.

Detailed Notes

This section organizes the essential vocabulary by scientific discipline, providing a structured overview of the terms introduced in Cycle 6.

Life Science — Life Cycles and Adaptation (Unit 26)

  • Life Cycles: All organisms undergo a life cycle, a progression from birth through growth, reproduction, and death.
    • Germination marks the beginning of a plant's life cycle as a seed sprouts.
    • Metamorphosis is a dramatic change in form, seen in animals like butterflies (caterpillar to adult) and frogs (tadpole to adult).
  • Adaptation: Organisms possess adaptations—traits or behaviors that increase their chances of survival in their specific environment.
    • Structural adaptations are physical features, such as a polar bear's thick fur or a cactus's spines.
    • Behavioral adaptations are actions, like birds migrating south for winter or animals camouflaging themselves.
  • Evolutionary Processes:
    • Natural selection explains how helpful adaptations become more common over generations, as organisms with these traits are more likely to survive and reproduce.
    • Fossils provide direct evidence of past life and evolutionary changes.
    • Extinction occurs when all members of a species die out, often due to inability to adapt to environmental changes.

Earth Science — Natural Resources and Conservation (Unit 27)

  • Natural Resources: These are materials from nature that humans utilize.
    • Renewable resources can be replenished relatively quickly, such as sunlight, wind, trees, and fresh water.
    • Nonrenewable resources form over geological timescales (millions of years) and are finite; examples include coal, oil, natural gas, and metals.
    • Fossil fuels are a significant category of nonrenewable resources, formed from ancient organic matter.
  • Conservation: This refers to the careful management and protection of natural resources to ensure their availability for future generations.
  • Endangered Species: A species is classified as endangered when its population is so low that it faces a very high risk of extinction in the wild. Conservation efforts often focus on protecting these species and their habitats.

Chemistry — Putting Atoms Together (Unit 28)

  • Atomic Bonding: Atoms interact to form larger structures.
    • A chemical bond is the force that holds two or more atoms together.
    • A molecule is formed when two or more atoms bond together.
    • A compound is a specific type of molecule made up of two or more different types of atoms chemically bonded (e.g., water, H₂O, is a compound because it contains hydrogen and oxygen atoms).
  • Chemical Representation:
    • A chemical formula is a symbolic representation showing the types of atoms in a molecule and their respective numbers (e.g., CO₂ indicates one carbon atom and two oxygen atoms).
  • Fundamental Laws:
    • The conservation of matter (or mass) is a foundational principle stating that in any chemical reaction, atoms are rearranged but never created or destroyed. The total mass of reactants equals the total mass of products.

Physics — Electricity and Magnetism (Unit 29)

  • Electric Charge and Flow:
    • Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter, being either positive or negative.
    • Static electricity is the accumulation of electric charge on an object, often caused by friction.
    • Current electricity is the continuous flow of electric charges through a material.
    • A circuit is a closed loop or path that allows electric current to flow.
  • Materials and Electricity:
    • Conductors are materials that readily allow electric current to pass through them (e.g., most metals like copper).
    • Insulators are materials that resist the flow of electric current (e.g., rubber, plastic, wood).
  • Magnetism:
    • A magnet is an object that generates a magnetic field, an invisible region where its attractive or repulsive force can be detected. Magnets attract specific metals like iron, nickel, and cobalt.
    • An electromagnet is a temporary magnet created by passing an electric current through a coil of wire. The magnetic field can be turned on or off.
  • Energy Conversion:
    • A generator is a device that converts mechanical motion into electrical energy, often by moving wire coils through a magnetic field.
    • An electric motor performs the opposite function, converting electrical energy into mechanical motion, using the interaction between magnetic fields and electric currents.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast structural and behavioral adaptations, providing an example of each for an animal you might find in a desert environment. How does natural selection contribute to the prevalence of these adaptations?
  2. Explain the difference between renewable and nonrenewable resources. Give two examples of each and discuss why the conservation of nonrenewable resources is particularly critical.
  3. Describe the relationship between atoms, molecules, and compounds. How does a chemical formula help us understand the composition of a compound, and what does the principle of conservation of matter tell us about chemical reactions involving these substances?
  4. Trace the flow of electricity in a simple circuit, identifying the roles of conductors and insulators. How does an electromagnet differ from a permanent magnet, and what are some practical uses for electromagnets?
  5. Consider the overarching theme of cycles in science (e.g., life cycles, resource cycles, energy conversions in electricity). How do these cyclical processes demonstrate the interconnectedness of the different scientific disciplines reviewed in Cycle 6?
