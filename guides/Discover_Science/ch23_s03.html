Chapter 23, Section 3 Study Guide: Common Chemicals and Their Uses

Overview

This section explores several common chemicals found in everyday life, focusing on their chemical makeup, properties, and diverse applications. From basic necessities like salt and water to cleaning agents and baking ingredients, you'll learn how these substances are used in homes and industries, often due to their specific acidic, basic, or neutral properties. The section also includes an activity to help you identify acids and bases using a simple homemade indicator.

Key Concepts

  • Ubiquity of Chemicals: Many common household items are simple chemical compounds with a variety of practical uses.
  • Salt (Sodium Chloride - NaCl): An essential compound used for seasoning, food preservation (by drawing out water), melting ice (by lowering freezing point), and as a raw material for other chemicals.
  • Baking Soda (Sodium Bicarbonate - NaHCO₃): A mild base (pH ~9) used in baking (produces CO₂ gas with acid to make dough rise), cleaning (mild abrasive), deodorizing (absorbs smells), and as an antacid.
  • Vinegar (Acetic Acid): A mild acid (pH ~2-3, typically 5% acetic acid in water) used in cooking (tangy flavor), cleaning (dissolves minerals and grease), and food preservation (pickling).
  • Water (H₂O): The most crucial chemical on Earth, it is neutral (pH 7) and known as the universal solvent due to its ability to dissolve many substances. It's vital for biological processes and moves through the water cycle.
  • Chlorine: A powerful element used as a disinfectant in small, controlled amounts. It kills germs in swimming pools, drinking water, and is a key component in household bleach (sodium hypochlorite).
  • Chemical Safety: Strong chemicals like bleach require careful handling, good ventilation, and should never be mixed with other cleaners to avoid dangerous reactions.
  • pH Indicators: Substances that change color depending on whether they are in an acidic, basic, or neutral solution. Red cabbage juice is a natural pH indicator.
  • Acids and Bases in Biology: The balance of acids and bases is important in the body, as seen with toothpaste (a mild base) neutralizing acids produced by mouth bacteria to protect tooth enamel.

Important Terms

  • Sodium Chloride (NaCl): The chemical name for table salt.
  • Sodium Bicarbonate (NaHCO₃): The chemical name for baking soda.
  • Acetic Acid: The active ingredient in vinegar, a mild acid.
  • Universal Solvent: A term for water, indicating its ability to dissolve many different substances.
  • Chlorine: A chemical element (Cl) used as a powerful disinfectant.
  • Sodium Hypochlorite: The active compound in household bleach, derived from chlorine.
  • pH Indicator: A substance that changes color in the presence of an acid or a base, indicating the pH level.
  • Enamel: The hard, outer protective coating of teeth, which can be dissolved by acids.
  • Fluoride: A chemical added to toothpaste that strengthens tooth enamel.
  • Neutralize: To cause an acid and a base to react, forming water and a salt, thereby reducing their acidic or basic properties.
  • Abrasive: A substance capable of cleaning or polishing by rubbing or grinding.
  • Freezing Point: The temperature at which a liquid turns into a solid. Salt lowers the freezing point of water.
  • Antacid: A substance that neutralizes stomach acid, often used to relieve indigestion.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Common Chemicals

Many everyday substances around your home are simple chemicals with surprising versatility. Understanding their properties helps us appreciate their uses.

Salt — NaCl (Sodium Chloride)

  • Description: One of the oldest and most widely used chemicals. Each unit contains one sodium atom (Na) bonded to one chlorine atom (Cl).
  • Uses:
    • Seasoning Food: Enhances the flavor of many dishes.
    • Preserving Food: Historically used to prevent spoilage of meat and fish by drawing water out, inhibiting bacterial growth.
    • Melting Ice: Spread on roads in winter, salt lowers the freezing point of water, causing ice to melt even below 0°C.
    • Making Other Chemicals: A raw material for producing chlorine (for water purification) and sodium hydroxide (a strong base used in soap making).

Baking Soda — NaHCO₃ (Sodium Bicarbonate)

  • Description: A mild base with a pH of about 9.
  • Uses:
    • Baking: Reacts with acids (like vinegar or buttermilk) to produce carbon dioxide (CO₂) gas, which forms bubbles that make dough rise and create a fluffy texture.
    • Cleaning: Its mildly abrasive texture helps scrub stains from surfaces without scratching.
    • Deodorizing: Absorbs and neutralizes bad odors, often kept in refrigerators for this purpose.
    • Settling Stomachs: Dissolved in water, it acts as an antacid, neutralizing excess stomach acid.

Vinegar — Acetic Acid

  • Description: A solution of acetic acid in water, typically about 5% acetic acid for household use. It is a mild acid with a pH of about 2 to 3.
  • Uses:
    • Cooking: Adds a tangy flavor to foods like salad dressings, pickles, and sauces.
    • Cleaning: The acid can dissolve mineral deposits and cut through grease, making it effective for cleaning glass and countertops.
    • Preserving: Used in pickling to create an acidic environment that prevents the growth of harmful bacteria, preserving vegetables.

Water — H₂O

  • Description: The most critical chemical on Earth, composed of two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom per molecule. It is neutral, with a pH of 7.
  • Properties & Importance:
    • Universal Solvent: Water's unique molecular structure allows it to dissolve a vast number of other substances, crucial for many processes.
    • Biological Importance: Your body is about 60% water, and nearly all chemical reactions in your cells occur in a water solution.
    • Water Cycle: Continuously moves through the environment, carrying dissolved chemicals, as studied in Unit 12.

Chlorine

  • Description: A chemical element that, in its pure form, is a greenish-yellow gas with a strong smell. It is powerful and useful when used in controlled amounts.
  • Uses:
    • Swimming Pools: Small amounts of chlorine are added to kill germs, making the water safe for swimming.
    • Drinking Water: Water treatment plants add very tiny, safe amounts of chlorine to tap water to eliminate harmful bacteria.
    • Household Bleach: Bleach contains sodium hypochlorite, a compound made from chlorine, which is used to remove stains and kill germs on surfaces.
  • Safety Note: Bleach and other chlorine-based cleaners are strong. Always use them in well-ventilated areas, with adult supervision, and never mix them with vinegar, ammonia, or other cleaning products, as this can produce dangerous gases.

Trying It Yourself: Red Cabbage pH Indicator

This activity demonstrates how a natural substance can indicate the pH of other liquids.

  • Objective: To determine if household liquids are acids, bases, or neutral using red cabbage juice.
  • Materials: Chopped red cabbage, hot water, clear cups, various test liquids (lemon juice, vinegar, tap water, milk, baking soda solution, soap solution), spoons/droppers, safety goggles.
  • Procedure: Steep cabbage in hot water to create purple indicator juice. Add small amounts of the test liquids to separate cups of cabbage juice and observe color changes.
  • What Happens: Red cabbage juice changes color based on pH:
    • Red or Pink: Strong acid (pH 0-4)
    • Purple: Neutral (pH 5-7)
    • Blue or Green: Mild base (pH 8-10)
    • Yellow or Greenish-yellow: Strong base (pH 11-14)
  • Expected Results: Lemon juice and vinegar turn pink/red (acidic). Tap water stays purple (neutral). Milk might be slightly pinkish-purple (slightly acidic). Baking soda water turns blue/green (basic). Soap water turns green/greenish-yellow (basic).

Science Life: Toothpaste: A Base That Protects Your Teeth

  • Problem: Bacteria in your mouth produce acids from sugary foods, which attack and dissolve tooth enamel, leading to cavities.
  • Solution: Toothpaste is a mild base (pH 8-9). When you brush, it neutralizes these harmful acids, protecting your teeth.
  • Additional Protection: Toothpaste also contains fluoride, a chemical that strengthens tooth enamel, making it more resistant to acid attack. Brushing regularly therefore neutralizes acids and fortifies your enamel.

Review Questions

  1. Describe at least three different uses for common table salt (NaCl). How do its chemical properties relate to these uses?
  2. Baking soda (NaHCO₃) is described as a mild base with many uses. Explain how its basic property and/or its reaction with acid contribute to its effectiveness in baking and as an antacid.
  3. Water is often called the "universal solvent." What does this term mean, and why is this property so crucial for life on Earth and within our bodies?
  4. Chlorine is a powerful chemical with important applications in public health, but it also carries safety risks. Discuss its key uses for safety and the essential precautions that must be taken when using chlorine-based products.
  5. Imagine you're testing three unknown liquids with red cabbage juice. Liquid A turns the juice bright red, Liquid B makes it blue-green, and Liquid C causes no significant color change (remains purple). Based on the indicator chart, classify each liquid as an acid, base, or neutral, and estimate its pH range.
