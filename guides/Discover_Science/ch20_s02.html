Cycle 4 Vocabulary Review Study Guide

Overview

This study guide is designed to help you review and master the essential vocabulary from Cycle 4, encompassing Life Science (The Human Body), Earth Science (Weather and Climate), Chemistry (Chemical Changes), and Physics (Simple Machines). It provides a structured approach to understanding the key terms and concepts across these scientific disciplines, preparing you for upcoming assessments.

Key Concepts

  • Levels of Organization in the Human Body: The human body is organized from the smallest living unit (cells) to tissues, organs, and ultimately, complex body systems that work together to perform specific functions.
  • The Difference Between Weather and Climate: Weather describes the current atmospheric conditions at a specific time and place, while climate refers to the average weather patterns of an area over many years, often categorized into distinct climate zones.
  • Physical vs. Chemical Changes: Substances can undergo changes without altering their chemical identity (physical changes), or they can transform into entirely new substances through chemical reactions, where atoms rearrange to form new molecules.
  • The Nature of Work and Simple Machines: In physics, work involves using a force to move an object over a distance. Simple machines are fundamental tools that make work easier by changing the amount or direction of force required, and they can be combined to form more complex compound machines.

Important Terms

Life Science — The Human Body

  • Body system: A group of organs that work together to do a specific job for the body.
  • Cell: The smallest living unit of the body; the basic building block of all living things.
  • Tissue: A group of similar cells working together.
  • Organ: A body part made of different tissues that does a specific job (e.g., the heart, the stomach).
  • Skeletal system: The system of 206 bones that supports, protects, and helps move the body.
  • Joint: A place where two bones meet, allowing movement.
  • Muscular system: The system of more than 600 muscles that move the body.
  • Voluntary muscle: A muscle you can control on purpose (e.g., arm muscles).
  • Involuntary muscle: A muscle that works automatically without your thinking about it (e.g., the heart).
  • Digestive system: The system that breaks down food into nutrients the body can use.
  • Nutrition: Eating the right foods to keep your body healthy and strong.

Earth Science — Weather and Climate

  • Weather: The condition of the atmosphere at a particular time and place — temperature, wind, clouds, and precipitation.
  • Temperature: A measure of how hot or cold something is, measured in degrees Celsius (°C).
  • Wind: Moving air, caused by uneven heating of Earth's surface.
  • Precipitation: Water that falls from clouds as rain, snow, sleet, or hail.
  • Anemometer: An instrument that measures wind speed.
  • Rain gauge: An instrument that measures how much rain has fallen.
  • Meteorologist: A scientist who studies and forecasts weather.
  • Cumulus: Puffy, white clouds that usually mean fair weather.
  • Stratus: Flat, gray clouds that form in layers and may bring drizzle.
  • Cirrus: Thin, wispy clouds made of ice crystals high in the sky.
  • Climate: The average weather pattern of a place over many years.
  • Climate zone: A large area of Earth with a similar climate pattern (tropical, temperate, or polar).

Chemistry — Chemical Changes

  • Physical change: A change in which the substance stays the same, even though it may look different (e.g., cutting, melting, dissolving).
  • Chemical change: A change in which one or more new substances are formed (e.g., burning, rusting, cooking).
  • Chemical reaction: Another name for a chemical change — the process in which atoms rearrange to form new molecules.
  • Reactants: The substances you start with before a chemical change.
  • Products: The new substances formed after a chemical change.
  • Clues of chemical change: Signs that a chemical change may have occurred: color change, gas bubbles, heat or light, new smell, hard to reverse.
  • Conservation of matter: The idea that matter is never created or destroyed in a chemical reaction — the atoms just rearrange.

Physics — Simple Machines

  • Work: Using a force to move an object over a distance (Work = Force × Distance).
  • Simple machine: A device with few or no moving parts that makes work easier by changing the amount or direction of force.
  • Lever: A bar that turns on a fixed point (the fulcrum) to lift or move a load.
  • Fulcrum: The fixed point on which a lever turns.
  • Wheel and axle: A wheel attached to a rod (axle); turning the wheel turns the axle with greater force.
  • Pulley: A wheel with a rope or chain that changes the direction of a force.
  • Inclined plane: A flat surface set at an angle to the ground; a ramp.
  • Wedge: Two inclined planes joined together to form a sharp edge, used to split or cut.
  • Screw: An inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder, used to hold things together or lift.
  • Compound machine: A machine made of two or more simple machines working together (e.g., scissors, bicycle).

Detailed Notes

Life Science — The Human Body (Unit 16)

The human body is an incredibly complex and organized system. Understanding its structure begins with the smallest unit, the cell. Groups of similar cells working together form a tissue. Different tissues combine to create an organ, which performs a specific job (like the heart pumping blood or the stomach digesting food). Finally, multiple organs working together to accomplish a major bodily function form a body system.

Key body systems include:

  • Skeletal System: Provides support, protection for organs, and allows movement thanks to 206 bones and the joints where they meet.
  • Muscular System: Composed of over 600 muscles responsible for all body movements. Muscles can be voluntary, meaning you control them (like lifting your arm), or involuntary, meaning they work automatically (like your heart beating).
  • Digestive System: Breaks down food into nutrients that the body needs for energy, growth, and repair. Nutrition is vital for maintaining a healthy body.

Earth Science — Weather and Climate (Unit 17)

Weather describes the daily atmospheric conditions at a specific time and place. Its key components include:

  • Temperature: How hot or cold the air is.
  • Wind: Moving air, caused by differences in air pressure due to uneven heating of Earth's surface.
  • Clouds: Visible masses of water droplets or ice crystals. Different types indicate different weather:
    • Cumulus: Puffy, white, often associated with fair weather.
    • Stratus: Flat, gray layers, often bringing drizzle.
    • Cirrus: High, thin, wispy ice clouds.
  • Precipitation: Any form of water falling from clouds (rain, snow, sleet, hail).

Scientists use instruments to measure weather: an anemometer measures wind speed, and a rain gauge measures rainfall. A meteorologist is a scientist who studies and forecasts weather.

Unlike weather, climate is the average weather pattern of a place over a long period (many years). Earth is divided into climate zones (tropical, temperate, polar) which share similar long-term weather characteristics.

Chemistry — Chemical Changes (Unit 18)

Matter can change in two fundamental ways:

  • Physical Change: The substance's appearance changes, but its chemical identity remains the same. Examples include cutting paper, melting ice, or dissolving sugar in water. No new substances are formed.
  • Chemical Change (Chemical Reaction): The substance's chemical identity changes, resulting in one or more entirely new substances with different properties. This involves the rearrangement of atoms. Examples include burning wood, rusting iron, or cooking an egg.

In a chemical reaction, the original substances are called reactants, and the new substances formed are called products. There are several clues of chemical change that indicate a reaction has likely occurred, such as a change in color, the production of gas bubbles, the release or absorption of heat or light, or a new smell. A key principle in chemistry is the Conservation of Matter, which states that matter is neither created nor destroyed during a chemical reaction; atoms are merely rearranged.

Physics — Simple Machines (Unit 19)

In physics, work is done when a force causes an object to move over a distance (Work = Force × Distance). Simple machines are basic mechanical devices that make work easier by either reducing the amount of force needed, changing the direction of the force, or increasing the distance over which the force acts. They do not reduce the total amount of work done, but they make it more manageable.

There are six main types of simple machines:

  1. Lever: A rigid bar that pivots on a fixed point called a fulcrum to lift or move loads.
  2. Wheel and Axle: A wheel attached to a rod (axle); turning the larger wheel rotates the smaller axle with greater force, or vice versa.
  3. Pulley: A wheel with a rope or chain running over it, used to change the direction of a force or reduce the force needed to lift a load.
  4. Inclined Plane: A flat, sloped surface (a ramp) used to move objects to a higher or lower place with less force.
  5. Wedge: Two inclined planes joined back-to-back, forming a sharp edge used for splitting or cutting.
  6. Screw: An inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder, used to hold things together or lift.

When two or more simple machines work together, they form a compound machine, such as scissors (two levers and two wedges) or a bicycle (levers, wheels and axles, screws).

Review Questions

  1. Explain the hierarchical organization of the human body, starting from the cell and ending with a body system. Give an example of how one body system (e.g., skeletal or muscular) interacts with an organ or tissue.
  2. Imagine you are a meteorologist describing today's weather and then a climatologist describing the climate of your region. How would your descriptions differ, and what terms would you use for each role?
  3. Describe the key differences between a physical change and a chemical change. Provide an example of each and explain how you would identify it as either physical or chemical, including any "clues of chemical change."
  4. Define "work" in a scientific context. Choose three different simple machines and explain how each one helps to make work "easier" without actually changing the total amount of work performed.
  5. Thinking across all four units, identify one connection or relationship between a concept from Life Science and a concept from Earth Science, or Chemistry and Physics. For example, how might a concept from weather relate to the human body?
