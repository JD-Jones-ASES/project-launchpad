Chapter 12, Section 4: Fresh Water, Salt Water, and Taking Care of Water Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the crucial distinction between fresh water and salt water, revealing that only a tiny fraction of Earth's water is easily accessible fresh water. It highlights why consuming salt water is harmful and introduces desalination as an energy-intensive alternative. The section then emphasizes the vital importance of water conservation and provides practical strategies for protecting our limited fresh water resources.

Key Concepts

  • Fresh Water vs. Salt Water Distribution: While water is abundant on Earth, about 97% is undrinkable salt water in the oceans. Only 3% is fresh water, with most of it locked in glaciers and ice caps.
  • Scarcity of Accessible Fresh Water: A mere fraction of fresh water is readily available in lakes and rivers for human use, highlighting its extreme scarcity compared to global demand.
  • Consequences of Drinking Salt Water: Ocean water's high salt content actively dehydrates the body, making it unsuitable for drinking.
  • Desalination as an Alternative: Desalination is a process to remove salt from ocean water, but it is expensive and requires significant energy, making it a limited solution.
  • Water Conservation: The careful use and protection of natural resources like water are essential to ensure they last for future generations, preventing depletion faster than natural recycling can replenish them.
  • Practical Conservation Methods: Simple actions in daily life, such as taking shorter showers, fixing leaks, and watering plants efficiently, can significantly reduce water waste.
  • The Water Cycle and Climate: The "Water Cycle in a Bag" activity demonstrates evaporation and condensation, illustrating how water moves. Scientist Syukuro Manabe's work further emphasizes the deep connection between the water cycle, the atmosphere, and Earth's climate.

Important Terms

  • Fresh water: Water that contains very little dissolved salt, found in lakes, rivers, groundwater, and glaciers.
  • Salt water: Water that contains a large amount of dissolved salts, found mostly in the oceans.
  • Desalination: The process of removing salt and other minerals from salt water to make it drinkable.
  • Conservation: The careful use and protection of natural resources so they last for the future.

Detailed Notes

The Global Water Supply: Fresh vs. Salt

Earth is often called the "Blue Planet" due to its abundance of water, but not all of it is available for drinking or most human uses.

  • Salt Water Dominance: Approximately 97% of Earth's total water is salt water, primarily found in the oceans. This water is not suitable for drinking or agriculture due to its high salt content.
  • Fresh Water Scarcity: Only about 3% of Earth's water is fresh water. However, most of this fresh water is not easily accessible:
    • A large portion (roughly 2/3 of all fresh water) is frozen in glaciers and ice caps, often far from human populations.
    • Another significant amount is stored as groundwater, which is harder to access than surface water.
    • A very small percentage, less than 1% of all fresh water (or about 0.003% of Earth's total water), is found in easily accessible sources like lakes and rivers.

Visualizing Water Scarcity: Imagine all of Earth's water in a one-liter bottle:

  • 970 mL would be undrinkable salty ocean water.
  • 20 mL would be frozen in glaciers, inaccessible.
  • 7 mL would be hard-to-reach groundwater.
  • Only about 3 mL (less than a teaspoon) would be the fresh water in lakes and rivers that billions of people, animals, and plants depend on. This tiny amount must meet all needs for drinking, farming, and industry globally.

Why Can't We Drink Salt Water?

Ocean water contains a significant amount of dissolved salts, about 35 grams per liter (equivalent to seven teaspoons of salt).

  • Dehydration Effect: Drinking salt water makes you even thirstier. Your body has to use its own water to process and flush out the excess salt, leading to dehydration.
  • Desalination: This is a technological process used to remove salt and other minerals from salt water to make it drinkable.
    • While effective, desalination requires substantial energy and is very expensive.
    • Due to these limitations, it is not a widespread solution for global water supply, making the protection of existing fresh water even more critical.

Water Conservation: Protecting Our Precious Resource

Conservation means using natural resources carefully and protecting them so that they will be available for future generations.

  • Importance of Conservation:
    • Even though the water cycle naturally recycles water, it takes time.
    • If humans use fresh water faster than nature can replenish it, critical resources can be depleted. This can lead to rivers drying up, wells going empty, and agricultural failures, impacting ecosystems and human societies.
  • Practical Ways to Conserve Water: Everyone can contribute to water conservation through simple daily actions:
    1. Turn off the tap: While brushing teeth, you can save up to 8 liters per session.
    2. Take shorter showers: Cutting just two minutes off your shower can save approximately 18 liters.
    3. Fix leaks: A constantly dripping faucet can waste thousands of liters annually.
    4. Water plants wisely: Water gardens in the early morning or evening when temperatures are cooler to reduce water loss through evaporation.
    5. Use full loads: Only run dishwashers and washing machines when they are completely full to maximize efficiency.
    6. Reuse water: Water used for rinsing fruits and vegetables can be repurposed for watering houseplants.

The Water Cycle in Action: "Try It Yourself!"

The "Water Cycle in a Bag" activity provides a hands-on demonstration of key parts of the water cycle:

  • Materials: Resealable plastic bag, water, optional blue food coloring, clear tape, sunny window, marker.
  • Process: Water is sealed in a bag and taped to a sunny window. The sun's heat causes evaporation. The water vapor then rises and cools as it touches the cooler plastic surface, leading to condensation (formation of tiny water droplets). These droplets eventually become heavy enough to slide down the bag, mimicking precipitation and collection back into the "ocean."
  • Learning Outcome: This experiment visually explains how the sun's energy drives the evaporation of water, which then condenses to form clouds and eventually returns as precipitation.

Syukuro Manabe: Linking Water to Climate

  • Pioneering Climate Scientist: Syukuro Manabe (born 1931) is a Japanese-American scientist whose groundbreaking work revolutionized our understanding of Earth's climate system.
  • Early Climate Models: In the 1960s, Manabe developed one of the very first computer models of Earth's atmosphere.
  • Greenhouse Effect: His models demonstrated how atmospheric gases, particularly carbon dioxide (CO2), trap heat and contribute to warming the planet.
  • Water Cycle and Climate Connection: Manabe's research also highlighted the critical connection between the water cycle and climate. He showed that as the atmosphere warms, changes occur in evaporation rates, cloud formations, and global rainfall patterns.
  • Nobel Prize Recognition: For his fundamental contributions to climate modeling, Manabe was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2021. His work underscores the interconnectedness of the water cycle and the atmosphere, showing that changes in one inevitably impact the other.

Review Questions

  1. Describe the distribution of fresh water and salt water on Earth. How does the 1-liter bottle analogy help us understand the scarcity of accessible fresh water?
  2. Explain why humans cannot drink ocean water. What is desalination, and what are its main drawbacks?
  3. Why is water conservation so important, even though Earth has a natural water cycle? What happens if we use fresh water faster than it can be replenished?
  4. List and explain three practical ways you can conserve water in your daily life. How might these small actions collectively make a big difference?
  5. How did Syukuro Manabe's scientific work connect the water cycle to Earth's climate? What was the significance of his findings about a warming atmosphere?
