Chapter 16, Section 2: The Skeletal System

Overview

The skeletal system is the internal framework of bones that provides shape, support, and protection for your body. Composed of 206 bones in adults, it works with muscles to enable movement. Beyond its structural role, bones are living tissues that house bone marrow for blood cell production and require nutrients like calcium to stay strong.

Key Concepts

  • Skeletal System Definition: The complete framework of bones inside your body that provides shape, support, and enables movement.
  • Bone Count and Development: Adults have 206 bones, while babies are born with around 270. Many smaller bones fuse together as a person grows.
  • Three Main Jobs of the Skeleton:
    • Support: Holds up the body, giving it shape and allowing us to stand and sit upright.
    • Protection: Shields delicate internal organs (e.g., skull protects the brain, ribs protect heart and lungs, spine protects the spinal cord).
    • Movement: Bones act as levers that muscles pull on, facilitating motion.
  • Major Bones: Key bones include the skull, spine (vertebrae), ribs, pelvis, femur (longest and strongest), and tibia.
  • Joints: Places where two or more bones meet, allowing for various types of movement.
    • Hinge Joints: Allow bending and straightening (e.g., knees, elbows).
    • Ball-and-Socket Joints: Provide movement in many directions, including rotation (e.g., shoulders, hips).
  • Bones as Living Tissue: Bones are not lifeless; they contain blood vessels, receive nutrients, constantly rebuild themselves, and bone marrow inside them produces blood cells.
  • Bone Health: Bones need calcium (found in dairy, leafy greens) to remain strong and healthy.
  • Smallest Bone: The stirrup bone (stapes) in the ear is the smallest, crucial for hearing.

Important Terms

  • Skeletal System : The framework of bones inside your body.
  • Skeleton : The complete set of bones in your body that gives you shape and support.
  • Femur : The longest and strongest bone in the human body, located in the upper leg (thigh bone).
  • Stirrup bone (Stapes) : The smallest bone in the human body, located deep inside the ear, crucial for hearing.
  • Skull : The bony framework of the head, protecting the brain.
  • Spine (Backbone) : A column of 33 small bones (vertebrae) that protects the spinal cord and allows bending and twisting.
  • Vertebrae : The individual small bones that make up the spine.
  • Ribs : Twelve pairs of curved bones that form a cage around the heart and lungs.
  • Pelvis : The large bone at the hips that supports the upper body and connects to the legs.
  • Tibia : The shinbone, a major bone in the lower leg.
  • Joint : A place where two or more bones meet, allowing movement.
  • Hinge joint : A type of joint that allows movement in only one plane, like a door hinge (e.g., knee, elbow).
  • Ball-and-socket joint : A type of joint that allows for a wide range of motion in many directions, including rotation (e.g., shoulder, hip).
  • Bone marrow : A soft, spongy material found in the center of many bones that produces new blood cells for the body.
  • Calcium : An essential mineral that helps keep bones healthy and strong.

Detailed Notes

What Is the Skeletal System?

  • The skeletal system is the internal framework of bones that gives the body its structure.
  • An adult human has 206 bones.
  • At birth, humans have approximately 270 bones; as they grow, some of these smaller bones fuse together to form larger, stronger bones.
  • Bones come in various shapes and sizes.
    • Longest bone: The femur (thigh bone).
    • Smallest bone: The stirrup bone (stapes) in the ear, which is about 3 millimeters long (the size of a grain of rice) and plays a vital role in hearing.

Three Big Jobs of Your Skeleton

The skeleton performs three critical functions for the body:

  1. Support:
    • It holds the body upright and gives it its shape.
    • Without bones, a person would be unable to stand, sit, or hold their head up, similar to a building without a frame.
  2. Protection:
    • Bones act as shields for the body's soft and delicate internal organs.
    • Skull: Protects the brain.
    • Ribs: Form a cage around the heart and lungs.
    • Spine (backbone): Protects the spinal cord, which is the main nerve "highway" connecting the brain to the rest of the body.
  3. Movement:
    • Bones work in conjunction with muscles to enable movement.
    • Muscles pull on bones, and the bones act as levers, allowing the body to move.

A Look at Your Major Bones

Here are some of the most important bones in the human body:

  • Skull: Located in the head, it protects the brain and is made of several bones fused together.
  • Spine (Backbone): A flexible column made of 33 small bones called vertebrae. It protects the spinal cord and allows the body to bend and twist.
  • Ribs: Twelve pairs of bones that curve around the chest, forming a protective cage for the heart and lungs.
  • Pelvis: The large bone located at the hips. It supports the upper body and forms the connection point for the legs.
  • Femur: The thigh bone, found in the upper leg. It is the longest and strongest bone in the body.
  • Tibia: The shinbone, located in the lower leg.

Joints: Where Bones Meet

  • Bones themselves are hard and rigid and cannot bend. Movement is made possible by joints.
  • Joints are the places where two or more bones come together.
  • Different types of joints allow for different kinds of movement:
    • Hinge Joints: These joints work like a door hinge, allowing movement in primarily one direction (bending and straightening).
      • Examples: Knees and elbows.
    • Ball-and-Socket Joints: These joints allow for a much wider range of motion, enabling movement forward, backward, and around in a circle.
      • Examples: Shoulders and hips.

Bones Are Alive!

  • Contrary to popular belief, bones are not dry and lifeless; they are living tissues.
  • Inside bones, blood vessels carry essential nutrients and oxygen.
  • A soft material called bone marrow fills the center of many bones. Bone marrow has a crucial role: it produces new blood cells for the body.
  • Bones are constantly undergoing a process of rebuilding, where old bone tissue is replaced with new bone tissue.
  • Because bones are alive, they require proper nutrition to remain strong.
  • Calcium is a vital mineral for bone health. Foods rich in calcium include milk, cheese, yogurt, and leafy green vegetables.
