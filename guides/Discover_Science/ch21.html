What Is an Ecosystem? Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concept of an ecosystem, defining it as a dynamic system where all living and nonliving components interact and depend on one another. It differentiates between biotic (living) and abiotic (nonliving) factors, providing examples of each and explaining how they work together to sustain life in diverse environments, from a tiny rotting log to a vast ocean.

Key Concepts

  • Ecosystem Definition: An ecosystem is comprised of all the living (biotic) and nonliving (abiotic) things in a specific area, functioning together as a connected system where each part depends on others.
  • Biotic Factors: These are the living components of an ecosystem, including all organisms such as plants, animals, fungi, and bacteria. They interact with each other in various ways, like predation, competition, and mutualistic relationships.
  • Abiotic Factors: These are the nonliving physical and chemical components of an ecosystem, such as water, sunlight, air, soil, and temperature. Abiotic factors are crucial as they determine which types of living things can survive in a particular environment.
  • Interdependence: All parts of an ecosystem are interconnected. Living things rely on other living things and on nonliving things for survival, and in turn, living things can modify their nonliving surroundings.
  • Ecosystem Scale and Diversity: Ecosystems exist in a wide range of sizes, from a small rotting log to an entire ocean. While each ecosystem is unique in its specific components, they all share the basic principles of energy flow and interdependence among their parts.

Important Terms

  • Ecosystem: All the living things (biotic factors) and nonliving things (abiotic factors) in an area, working together as a system.
  • Biotic factor: Any living part of an ecosystem, including plants, animals, fungi, and bacteria.
  • Abiotic factor: Any nonliving part of an ecosystem, such as water, sunlight, air, soil, and temperature.

Detailed Notes

The Concept of an Ecosystem

An ecosystem is more than just a collection of plants and animals; it is a system where all parts work together in an interconnected way. Every living thing in an area relies on other living things and on the nonliving surroundings. For example, in a pond, frogs need water, fish need tiny creatures to eat, and plants need sunlight and soil. If one component is removed or drastically changed, it affects the entire system. Living things need nonliving things, and nonliving things are often changed by living things, highlighting the constant interaction.

Living Parts: Biotic Factors

Biotic factors are all the living components within an ecosystem. These include every organism, regardless of size, that plays a role in the system.

  • Examples of Biotic Factors:
    • Plants: Trees, grasses, wildflowers, and algae are producers that convert sunlight into food.
    • Animals: Insects, birds, fish, deer, and frogs represent consumers that eat other organisms.
    • Fungi: Mushrooms, mold, and yeast are decomposers, breaking down dead material.
    • Bacteria: Tiny, single-celled organisms found everywhere, playing vital roles as decomposers and in nutrient cycling.
  • Interactions Among Biotic Factors: Living things in an ecosystem constantly interact. These interactions can include:
    • Feeding: One organism consuming another (e.g., fish eating tiny creatures).
    • Competition: Organisms vying for the same limited resources like food, water, or space.
    • Cooperation/Mutualism: Organisms helping each other (e.g., a bee pollinating a flower, a bird dispersing seeds).

Nonliving Parts: Abiotic Factors

Abiotic factors are the nonliving components of an ecosystem. These factors are critical because they determine the physical conditions of an environment and thus influence which types of living things can survive and thrive there.

  • Examples of Abiotic Factors:
    • Water: Essential for all life processes. Its availability (or lack thereof, as in a desert) dictates the types of organisms present.
    • Sunlight: The primary energy source for most ecosystems. Plants use it for photosynthesis, setting the foundation for the food web.
    • Air: Provides gases necessary for life, such as oxygen for animal respiration and carbon dioxide for plant photosynthesis.
    • Soil: Offers physical support, water retention, and vital nutrients for plants. The quality and composition of soil greatly impact plant growth.
    • Temperature: Organisms have specific temperature ranges in which they can survive. Temperature influences metabolic rates and life cycles.

Ecosystems Are Everywhere

Ecosystems are incredibly diverse in size and location. They can be vast or incredibly small.

  • Scale of Ecosystems:
    • Large Ecosystems: Examples include vast oceans or expansive forests.
    • Small Ecosystems: A single rotting log, a puddle, or even the soil beneath your feet can be considered an ecosystem, complete with its own community of interacting biotic and abiotic factors.
  • Examples of Different Ecosystems:
    • Pond ecosystem: Includes water, mud, sunlight, frogs, fish, algae, and snails, all interacting.
    • Forest ecosystem: Involves trees, soil, rain, deer, birds, mushrooms, and decomposing leaves.
    • Desert ecosystem: Characterized by sand, rock, intense sunlight, very little rain, cacti, lizards, and scorpions.
    • Ocean ecosystem: Features salt water, sunlight (near the surface), whales, fish, coral, plankton, and seaweed.
    • Backyard ecosystem: Your own backyard with grass, soil, earthworms, robins, squirrels, and rain.
  • Universal Rules: Despite their unique characteristics, all ecosystems operate under fundamental principles: energy flows through them, and living things depend on each other and their nonliving surroundings.

The Hidden World Under Our Feet

The soil is a prime example of a thriving, often overlooked, ecosystem. A single teaspoon of healthy soil contains billions of bacteria, hundreds of thousands of fungi, and thousands of microscopic animals. These organisms are vital decomposers, breaking down dead organic material like leaves and turning it into rich nutrients that plants can absorb. Without these tiny workers, soil would be barren, and very few plants could survive.

Review Questions

  1. Define what an ecosystem is in your own words. Provide an example of an ecosystem and identify at least three biotic and three abiotic factors within it.
  2. Explain the difference between biotic and abiotic factors. Why are both essential for an ecosystem to function?
  3. Describe how biotic and abiotic factors interact within an ecosystem. Give specific examples of how a living thing might affect a nonliving thing, and vice-versa.
  4. Ecosystems can vary greatly in size. Discuss why a rotting log can be considered an ecosystem, even though it's much smaller than an ocean. What basic rules do both share?
  5. Imagine you are observing the area around your school. List at least four biotic factors and four abiotic factors you might find. How do these factors likely interact with each other in that specific area?
