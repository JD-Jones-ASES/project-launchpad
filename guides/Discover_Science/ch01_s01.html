What Makes Something Alive?

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental characteristics that define life. It identifies five universal traits — growth and development, reproduction, the need for energy, responsiveness to the environment, and being made of cells — that all living organisms share. Understanding these characteristics allows scientists to distinguish between living and non-living things.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of a Living Thing: A living thing is any entity that exhibits all five fundamental characteristics: it grows, reproduces, needs energy, responds to its environment, and is made of cells.
  • The Five Characteristics of Life: These are the universal traits present in every organism, from the smallest bacterium to the largest whale. If something lacks even one of these traits, it is not considered alive.
  • Growth and Development: All living things increase in size (grow) and undergo changes over their lifespan (develop).
  • Reproduction: Living things create new individuals of their own kind, ensuring the continuation of their species.
  • Need for Energy: Every living organism requires energy to fuel its life processes, such as growing, moving, and maintaining its body.
  • Response to Environment: Living things react to stimuli and changes in their surroundings, allowing them to adapt and survive.
  • Cellular Composition: All living things are composed of one or more cells, which are the basic structural and functional units of life.

Important Terms

  • Living Thing: An organism or entity that displays all five characteristics of life: growth and development, reproduction, energy utilization, response to environment, and cellular composition.
  • Reproduce: The biological process by which living organisms create new individuals of the same species.
  • Energy: The capacity to do work; living things acquire and use energy to power all their biological processes.
  • Environment: The surroundings or conditions in which a person, animal, or plant lives or operates; living things constantly interact with and respond to their environment.
  • Cell: The basic structural, functional, and biological unit of all known organisms. Cells are often microscopic and are the fundamental building blocks of life.
  • Develop: To change or grow over time, often becoming more complex or mature.

Detailed Notes

The question "What Makes Something Alive?" is central to biology. Scientists use a set of five shared characteristics to classify something as living. If an object possesses all five of these traits, it is considered alive; if even one trait is missing, it is not.

Here are the five defining characteristics of living things:

  1. Living Things Grow and Develop:

    • Growth refers to an increase in size. For example, a kitten grows larger as it becomes a cat, and a tiny seed grows into a mature tree. This increase in size is typically due to an increase in the number or size of cells within the organism.
    • Development refers to the changes an organism undergoes throughout its life cycle. A caterpillar develops into a butterfly, and a human baby develops from infancy through childhood to adulthood. These changes involve complex processes of maturation and specialization. All living things have a life cycle during which they grow and change over time.

  2. Living Things Reproduce:

    • Reproduction is the process by which living things create new individuals of their own kind. This ensures that life continues from one generation to the next.
    • There are different ways organisms reproduce. For example, animals like cats have kittens, and plants like apple trees produce seeds that can grow into new apple trees. Bacteria reproduce by dividing into two. The key is that they make more of themselves, preventing their species from dying out.

  3. Living Things Need Energy:

    • All living processes, such as growing, moving, thinking, and reproducing, require energy.
    • Organisms obtain energy in various ways. You get energy by eating food, which your body then breaks down. Plants, on the other hand, are producers; they use sunlight to create their own food through a process called photosynthesis.
    • Energy is vital for maintaining life and performing all the necessary functions that keep an organism alive and thriving.

  4. Living Things Respond to Their Environment:

    • Living things are not passive; they actively react to changes or stimuli in their surroundings, which is known as responding to their environment.
    • These responses can be immediate and obvious, like a dog turning its head when its name is called, or a plant's leaves curling when touched.
    • Responses can also be slower or less obvious, such as a plant growing towards a light source, or animals migrating in response to seasonal changes in temperature. This ability to respond helps organisms survive and adapt to their surroundings.

  5. Living Things Are Made of Cells:

    • Cells are the fundamental units of life. They are the tiny, self-contained building blocks from which all living organisms are constructed.
    • Most cells are microscopic, meaning they are too small to be seen without a microscope.
    • Some organisms, like bacteria, are unicellular (made of a single cell). Others, like humans, are multicellular, composed of trillions of specialized cells working together. The presence of cells, organized in a specific way, is a defining characteristic that distinguishes living matter from non-living matter.

Distinguishing Living from Non-Living: The section emphasizes that for something to be considered alive, it must possess all five of these characteristics. If even one is missing, it is not considered a living thing.

Consider the example of fire:

  • Fire grows (gets larger by consuming fuel).
  • It seems to "eat" (uses fuel, which can be seen as an energy source).
  • It responds to its environment (flickers in wind, spreads when fuel is abundant).
  • However, fire does not reproduce in the biological sense (it doesn't create new fires of its own kind with genetic material).
  • Crucially, fire is not made of cells. For these reasons, scientists classify fire as non-living.

By applying these five criteria, we can scientifically determine what is alive and what is not, whether it's a rock, a flower, a robot, or a mushroom.

Review Questions

  1. Why do scientists need a clear set of characteristics to define "living things"? What problems might arise if there were no such definition?
  2. Describe each of the five characteristics of living things. For each characteristic, provide an example different from those in the text.
  3. Explain the difference between growth and development. Why are both considered essential aspects of a living organism's life cycle?
  4. Imagine you find a strange object that moves on its own and changes shape. What specific questions would you ask or observations would you make to determine if it is a living thing?
  5. Based on the five characteristics, explain why a car, which "consumes" gasoline for energy, "moves" and "responds" to a driver, and can even be "reproduced" in a factory, is not considered alive.
