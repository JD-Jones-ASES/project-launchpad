Chapter 12, Section 1: Where Is Earth's Water? Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the concept of Earth's hydrosphere, which encompasses all the water on our planet. It explores the various forms and locations of water, from vast salty oceans to frozen glaciers, underground reserves, and even invisible atmospheric gases. The content emphasizes the uneven distribution of water, highlighting that while Earth is a "watery planet," most of its water is either salty or frozen, with a very small fraction being readily available liquid fresh water.

Key Concepts

  • The Hydrosphere: All the water on Earth, covering about 71% of its surface, is collectively known as the hydrosphere.
  • Diversity of Water: Earth's water exists in different forms—salty or fresh, liquid, solid (ice), or gas (water vapor).
  • Oceans as Major Reservoirs: Oceans hold approximately 97% of all Earth's water, making them the largest water reservoir. This water is salty due to dissolved minerals like sodium chloride.
  • Glaciers and Ice Caps - Fresh Water Storage: About 2% of Earth's water is locked up in glaciers and ice caps. These massive ice formations represent the largest store of fresh water on the planet.
  • Lakes, Rivers, and Streams: These surface bodies of water contain fresh water but make up less than 1% (specifically 0.03%) of Earth's total water, making them a tiny fraction despite being commonly seen.
  • Groundwater - Hidden Fresh Water: A significant amount of fresh water (0.7%) is found underground in spaces between rocks and soil, called groundwater, which is a crucial source for human use.
  • Water Vapor in the Atmosphere: A tiny fraction of water (0.001%) exists as an invisible gas called water vapor in the atmosphere, forming clouds and contributing to humidity.
  • Scarcity of Usable Fresh Water: Despite Earth being a watery planet, the vast majority of water is either salty or frozen, meaning only a very small percentage is readily available as liquid fresh water for direct human consumption and use.

Important Terms

  • Hydrosphere: All the water on Earth, including oceans, lakes, rivers, glaciers, groundwater, and water vapor in the atmosphere.
  • Ocean: A very large body of salt water that covers much of Earth's surface.
  • Salt water: Water that contains dissolved minerals, especially sodium chloride, giving it a salty taste; found primarily in oceans.
  • Glacier: A huge mass of ice that forms on land when snow builds up and packs together over many years, slowly turning into ice.
  • Fresh water: Water that is not salty; found in glaciers, ice caps, lakes, rivers, streams, and underground.
  • Lake: A large body of fresh water surrounded by land.
  • River: A long, flowing body of fresh water that moves toward an ocean, a lake, or another river.
  • Groundwater: Water found underground in the spaces between rocks and soil.
  • Water vapor: Water in the form of an invisible gas found in the air.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Earth's Water

  • A Watery Planet: Earth is unique for its abundance of water, with about 71% of its surface covered by it. From space, Earth appears mostly blue.
  • The Hydrosphere: Scientists refer to all of Earth's water, in all its forms and locations, as the hydrosphere.
  • Diversity of Water: Not all water is the same. It can be salty or fresh, liquid or frozen solid (ice), or an invisible gas (water vapor) in the air. This section explores where these different types of water are found.

Oceans — The Big Blue

  • Vastness of Oceans: Oceans are enormous bodies of salt water that dominate Earth's surface.
  • Dominant Water Source: Oceans contain approximately 97% of all the water on Earth. If you imagined 100 cups representing all Earth's water, 97 of them would be ocean water.
  • Salt Water Composition: Ocean water is salty due to dissolved minerals, primarily sodium chloride, which is the same chemical compound found in table salt.
  • Earth's Five Oceans: Earth has five major oceans: the Pacific, Atlantic, Indian, Southern, and Arctic.

Glaciers and Ice Caps

  • Definition of Glacier: A glacier is a massive body of ice that forms on land. It develops over hundreds or thousands of years as snow continually falls, accumulates, and compresses into solid ice.
  • Amount of Water: Glaciers and ice caps hold about 2% of Earth's total water.
  • Location: They are found in polar regions like Antarctica and Greenland, as well as on high mountain tops around the world.
  • Primary Fresh Water Reservoir: Despite being only 2% of the total water, glaciers store the majority of Earth's fresh water.
  • Environmental Impact: If all glaciers were to melt, there would be a dramatic rise in global sea levels.

Lakes, Rivers, and Streams

  • Fresh Water Bodies: Lakes, rivers, and streams are all bodies of fresh water, meaning they do not contain significant amounts of salt.
  • Definition of Lake: A lake is a large body of water that is completely surrounded by land.
  • Definition of River: A river is a long, flowing channel of water that moves across land, typically flowing towards an ocean, a lake, or another river.
  • Small Percentage: Despite being the most visible and often used sources of liquid fresh water, lakes and rivers hold less than 1% (specifically 0.03%) of all the water on Earth.

Underground Water

  • Formation of Groundwater: When rain falls, some of it soaks into the ground. This water trickles down through tiny pores and spaces in the soil and rock layers until it reaches a depth where all these spaces are completely filled with water.
  • Definition of Groundwater: This water found underground in the spaces between rocks and soil is called groundwater.
  • Vital Fresh Water Source: Groundwater is an extremely important source of fresh water for many people. Wells are dug to pump groundwater to the surface for drinking and other uses.
  • Springs: Springs occur when groundwater naturally flows out of the ground at the surface.
  • Amount: Groundwater accounts for about 0.7% of Earth's total water.

Water in the Air

  • Invisible Gas: Water is present in the air around us in the form of an invisible gas called water vapor.
  • Definition of Water Vapor: Water vapor is water that has heated up and changed into its gaseous state.
  • Humidity: While invisible, water vapor can be felt on humid days, making the air feel thick and sticky.
  • Cloud and Fog Formation: When water vapor cools down, it transforms into tiny liquid water droplets or ice crystals, which then become visible as clouds. Fog is essentially a cloud that forms very close to the ground.
  • Smallest Reservoir: The atmosphere holds the smallest fraction of Earth's water, approximately 0.001%.

Putting It All Together: Water Distribution Summary

Location Type Amount
Oceans Salt water 97%
Glaciers and ice Fresh water 2%
Groundwater Fresh water 0.7%
Lakes and rivers Fresh water 0.03%
Atmosphere Water vapor 0.001%

This summary table clearly shows that almost all of Earth's water is in the oceans (salty), and most of the fresh water is frozen in ice. The liquid fresh water in lakes and rivers, which we commonly use, represents only a tiny fraction of Earth's total water supply. This understanding highlights the importance of appreciating and conserving fresh water resources.

Review Questions

  1. What is the hydrosphere, and why is Earth often referred to as "the watery planet"? Provide examples of different forms and locations of water that are part of the hydrosphere.
  2. Compare and contrast the two largest reservoirs of water on Earth: oceans and glaciers. Discuss the type of water found in each, their approximate percentages of Earth's total water, and their significance.
  3. Explain why lakes, rivers, and streams, which are often the most visible sources of water for humans, represent such a small fraction of Earth's total water. How does this compare to the amount of groundwater?
  4. Describe the process by which groundwater forms and explain its importance as a water source. How do springs relate to groundwater?
  5. Based on the water distribution table, discuss why water conservation is a critical issue, even though Earth is mostly covered in water. Which types of water are most easily accessible for human use, and what percentage do they collectively represent?
