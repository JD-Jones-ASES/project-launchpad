Vocabulary Review: Cycle 1 Study Guide

Overview

This section serves as a crucial review of foundational scientific vocabulary covered in Cycle 1, organized by the major branches of science: Life Science, Earth Science, Chemistry, and Physics. It reinforces understanding of key terms related to living organisms, Earth's structure, the nature of matter, and the principles of forces and motion, providing a solid vocabulary base for future learning.

Key Concepts

  • Branches of Science: Science is categorized into different fields like Life Science (biology), Earth Science (geology, meteorology, oceanography), Chemistry, and Physics, each focusing on distinct aspects of the natural world.
  • Characteristics of Life: Living things share common characteristics, including growth, reproduction, energy needs, responsiveness to stimuli, and cellular composition, distinguishing them from nonliving or once-living entities.
  • Earth's Systems: Our planet is composed of interconnected systems, including the solid Earth (lithosphere), water bodies (hydrosphere), and the surrounding air (atmosphere), along with major geographical features like continents and oceans.
  • Nature of Matter: Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space, existing in various states (solid, liquid, gas) each with unique properties regarding shape and volume.
  • Forces and Motion: Objects in the universe are subject to forces, which are pushes or pulls that can cause changes in motion. Concepts like gravity and friction describe specific types of forces influencing movement.
  • Building Scientific Vocabulary: A strong understanding of scientific terminology is essential for comprehending complex concepts, engaging in scientific inquiry, and communicating effectively within the scientific community.

Detailed Notes

Life Science (Unit 1)

Life Science is the study of living things. This unit introduced us to the fundamental distinctions and characteristics that define life.

  • Living vs. Nonliving vs. Once-living: It's important to differentiate between these categories. A living thing displays a set of characteristics: it grows, reproduces, needs energy to survive, responds to changes in its surroundings, and is made up of cells. In contrast, a nonliving thing has never possessed these characteristics; examples include rocks, water, or air. A once-living thing was alive in the past but is no longer performing life processes, such as a fallen log or a fossil.
  • Reproduce: This vital characteristic means that living organisms can create new organisms of their own kind, ensuring the continuation of their species.
  • Habitat: Every living thing has a natural home or environment where it naturally lives and where it can find everything it needs to survive, such as food, water, shelter, and space.

Earth Science (Unit 2)

Earth Science focuses on the physical structure and processes of our planet. This unit highlighted the main components of Earth's systems and its major geographical features.

  • Earth's Spheres: We learned about three major interacting layers:
    • Lithosphere: This is Earth's solid, rocky outer shell, encompassing continents, ocean floors, and everything below them down to the upper mantle.
    • Hydrosphere: This term covers all the water found on Earth, including the vast oceans, lakes, rivers, ice caps, glaciers, and even water vapor in the atmosphere.
    • Atmosphere: This is the blanket of gases, commonly referred to as air, that surrounds Earth, protecting it from space and regulating temperature.
  • Major Geographical Features:
    • Continent: There are seven immense landmasses on Earth, each called a continent.
    • Ocean: The Earth is also dominated by five vast bodies of salt water, known as oceans. These large features cover most of Earth's surface.

Chemistry (Unit 3)

Chemistry is the study of matter and its properties. This unit laid the groundwork for understanding what everything around us is made of.

  • Matter, Mass, and Volume:
    • Matter: The most fundamental concept is that anything that has mass (the amount of material it contains) and takes up volume (the amount of space it occupies) is considered matter.
    • Mass: Measured in grams (g) or kilograms (kg), mass is a measure of an object's inertia, or its resistance to changes in motion.
    • Volume: Measured in milliliters (mL), liters (L), or cubic units, volume quantifies the space an object or substance fills.
  • States of Matter: Matter commonly exists in three primary states, each with distinct characteristics:
    • Solid: Solids have a fixed shape and a definite volume. Their particles are tightly packed and vibrate in fixed positions.
    • Liquid: Liquids have a definite volume but no fixed shape; they take the shape of their container. Their particles are close together but can slide past each other.
    • Gas: Gases have neither a definite shape nor a definite volume; they expand to fill any container they are in. Their particles are far apart and move randomly and rapidly.
  • Property: A property is an observable or measurable characteristic of matter. For example, color, hardness, density, boiling point, and flammability are all properties.

Physics (Unit 4)

Physics explores fundamental concepts like forces, energy, and motion. This unit introduced how objects interact and move.

  • Force: A force is simply a push or a pull. Forces are what cause objects to start moving, stop moving, speed up, slow down, or change direction.
  • Gravity: A ubiquitous force, gravity is the natural phenomenon by which all things with mass are brought toward one another. On Earth, it's the force that pulls objects down towards the planet's center.
  • Motion: When an object changes its position over a period of time, it is said to be in motion.
  • Speed: Speed is a measure of how fast an object is moving. It's calculated by dividing the distance an object travels by the amount of time it takes to travel that distance (Speed = Distance / Time).
  • Friction: Friction is a force that arises when two surfaces rub against each other. It always acts in the opposite direction of motion, tending to slow down or prevent movement.

  3. Compare and contrast the three states of matter (solid, liquid, and gas) by explaining their key differences in terms of "definite shape" and "definite volume." Provide an example of a "property" that could be used to identify each state.
  4. Explain how the forces of "gravity" and "friction" commonly influence the "motion" of everyday objects. Give a specific example where both forces are at play.
  5. Choose one word from each of the four branches of science (Life Science, Earth Science, Chemistry, Physics) and combine them into a single, scientifically accurate sentence. For example, "A living thing on the lithosphere made of matter can experience gravity." Now, create your own unique sentence.
