Study Guide: Chapter 23, Section 2 - Acids and Bases

Overview

This section introduces two fundamental types of chemicals: acids and bases. It describes their characteristic properties, provides numerous everyday examples, and explains how scientists use the pH scale to measure their strength. Finally, the section covers the important chemical reaction called neutralization, which occurs when acids and bases mix.

Key Concepts

  • Acids: Chemicals typically characterized by a sour taste, the ability to dissolve certain metals, and a pH below 7. They are common in foods like citrus fruits and vinegar, and also in your stomach.
  • Bases: Chemicals typically characterized by a slippery feel, a bitter taste, and a pH above 7. Examples include soap, baking soda, and antacid tablets.
  • The pH Scale: A numerical scale ranging from 0 to 14 used to measure how acidic or basic a substance is. A pH below 7 indicates an acid, a pH above 7 indicates a base, and a pH of 7 is neutral.
  • Neutral Substances: Substances that are neither acidic nor basic, having a pH of exactly 7. Pure water is a common example of a neutral substance.
  • Neutralization: A chemical reaction that occurs when an acid and a base are mixed. They react to cancel each other out, producing a substance closer to a neutral pH (pH 7). This reaction is important in many everyday applications.

Important Terms

  • Acid: A chemical substance that often tastes sour, dissolves some metals, and has a pH value below 7. Examples include lemon juice and vinegar.
  • Base: A chemical substance that often feels slippery, tastes bitter, and has a pH value above 7. Examples include soap and baking soda.
  • pH Scale: A standardized scale, typically ranging from 0 to 14, used to measure the acidity or alkalinity (basicity) of a substance. Lower numbers (0-6) indicate acidity, higher numbers (8-14) indicate basicity, and 7 is neutral.
  • Neutral: Describing a substance that is neither an acid nor a base, possessing a pH of 7. Pure water is a neutral substance.
  • Neutralization: A chemical reaction between an acid and a base that results in a solution becoming less acidic and less basic, moving closer to a neutral pH of 7.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Acids and Bases

Acids and bases are two major categories of chemical compounds that are fundamental to chemistry and found all around us. You encounter them daily in your food, cleaning products, and even within your own body.

  • Acids:

    • Taste: Often sour (e.g., lemons, vinegar). Important Safety Note: Never taste or touch unknown chemicals.
    • Effect on Metals: Can dissolve some metals.
    • pH: Have a pH value below 7. The lower the number (closer to 0), the stronger the acid.
    • Common Examples:
      • Lemon juice and orange juice: Contain citric acid, giving them their tart, sour taste.
      • Vinegar: Contains acetic acid, which provides its sharp smell and tangy flavor, useful in cooking and cleaning.
      • Soda pop: The fizziness comes from carbonic acid, formed when carbon dioxide gas dissolves in water.
      • Stomach acid: Your stomach produces strong hydrochloric acid to aid in digestion. Your stomach lining is specially adapted to protect itself from this acid.
      • Tomatoes: Are mildly acidic, contributing to their slightly tangy flavor.

  • Bases:

    • Feel: Often feel slippery to the touch (e.g., soap). Important Safety Note: Never taste or touch unknown chemicals.
    • Taste: Often bitter (e.g., unsweetened chocolate, baking soda).
    • pH: Have a pH value above 7. The higher the number (closer to 14), the stronger the base.
    • Common Examples:
      • Soap and shampoo: Most common soaps are mild bases, which is why they feel slippery.
      • Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate, NaHCO₃): A mild base when dissolved in water, used in baking, cleaning, and as an antacid.
      • Antacid tablets: Contain bases that help relieve an upset stomach by counteracting excess stomach acid.
      • Household ammonia: A strong-smelling base commonly found in glass cleaners. Always use in a well-ventilated area due to its fumes.
      • Toothpaste: Is typically a mild base designed to neutralize acids in your mouth produced by bacteria, which can cause tooth decay.

The pH Scale: Measuring Acidity and Basicity

To precisely describe how acidic or basic a substance is, scientists use the pH scale. This scale is a numerical range from 0 to 14.

  • Acidic Substances (pH 0-6):

    • Any substance with a pH less than 7 is considered an acid.
    • The closer the pH is to 0, the stronger the acid. For example, battery acid (pH 0-1) is a very strong acid, while coffee (pH ~5) is a weak acid.
    • Misconception Alert: A pH of 0 means it's the strongest acid, not the absence of acid.

  • Neutral Substances (pH 7):

    • A substance with a pH of exactly 7 is considered neutral. It is neither acidic nor basic.
    • Pure water is the most common example of a neutral substance.

  • Basic Substances (pH 8-14):

    • Any substance with a pH greater than 7 is considered a base (or alkaline).
    • The closer the pH is to 14, the stronger the base. For instance, drain cleaner (pH 13-14) is a very strong base, while baking soda solution (pH ~8-9) is a mild base.

Neutralization: When Acids Meet Bases

When an acid and a base are brought together, they undergo a special chemical reaction called neutralization. In this reaction:

  • The acid's properties are reduced, and the base's properties are reduced.
  • They effectively "cancel each other out," leading to a substance that is closer to a neutral pH (pH 7).
  • Neutralization reactions often produce water and a salt, although this section focuses on the pH change.

Examples of Neutralization in Action:

  • Antacid tablets: When you take an antacid for heartburn, the basic compounds in the tablet react with and neutralize the excess hydrochloric acid in your stomach, reducing discomfort.
  • Toothpaste: The mild bases in toothpaste neutralize the acids produced by bacteria in your mouth, which helps prevent cavities and protect your tooth enamel.
  • Vinegar and Baking Soda: When you mix vinegar (acetic acid) and baking soda (a base), they react to produce carbon dioxide gas, which causes the familiar fizzing and bubbling. This is a common example seen in science experiments.
  • Bee Stings: Bee stings are slightly acidic. Applying a paste made from baking soda (a base) can help neutralize the acid in the sting, reducing pain and swelling. This demonstrates the practical application of neutralization to relieve discomfort.

Neutralization is a vital chemical process used in many fields, including medicine, environmental science (e.g., treating acidic wastewater), and various industrial applications.

Review Questions

  1. Describe three characteristic properties each of acids and bases. Provide one everyday example for each, noting the specific chemical involved if possible.
  2. Explain the importance of the safety note provided in the text regarding tasting or touching unknown chemicals. What are the potential dangers, and what precautions should always be taken in a science setting?
  3. How does the pH scale work? Explain what the different ranges (0-6, 7, 8-14) signify, and give an example of a substance for each range.
  4. What is neutralization? Describe how this chemical reaction occurs and explain its significance in at least two different real-world scenarios, such as medicine or personal hygiene.
  5. Imagine you accidentally spill a strong acid in a lab (wearing proper safety gear, of course!). Why would it be important to use a base to clean it up rather than just water? Explain your reasoning using concepts from this section.
