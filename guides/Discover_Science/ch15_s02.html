Study Guide: Vocabulary Review - Cycle 3

Overview

This study guide is designed to help you review and solidify your understanding of key scientific vocabulary from Cycle 3, encompassing concepts from Life Science (The Animal Kingdom), Earth Science (Water, Water Everywhere), Chemistry (Atoms — The Building Blocks), and Physics (Energy Is Everywhere). Mastering these terms is crucial for building a strong foundation in various scientific disciplines and understanding how they connect.

Key Concepts

  • Animal Classification and Adaptation: Animals are broadly categorized based on the presence or absence of a backbone (vertebrates vs. invertebrates). Vertebrates are further divided into specific groups like fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals, each with unique characteristics and adaptations that help them survive in their environments, including how they regulate their body temperature (cold-blooded vs. warm-blooded).
  • The Water Cycle and Water Resources: Earth's water is in constant motion through the water cycle, a continuous process involving evaporation, condensation, precipitation, and collection. Understanding this cycle is essential, as is recognizing the critical difference between fresh water (a small percentage of Earth's total water, vital for life) and salt water, and the importance of water conservation.
  • Fundamental Building Blocks of Matter: All matter is composed of tiny particles called atoms. When atoms bond together, they form molecules. Substances made of only one type of atom are called elements, organized on the periodic table. When two or more different elements bond, they form a compound, represented by a chemical formula.
  • Forms and Transformation of Energy: Energy is the fundamental ability to do work and exists in various forms, such as kinetic (motion), potential (stored), heat, light, and sound. A core principle of physics is that energy cannot be created or destroyed, but it can change from one form to another through a process called energy transformation.

Important Terms

Life Science: The Animal Kingdom (Unit 11)

  • Vertebrate: An animal possessing a backbone, which is part of an internal skeleton. Examples include fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals.
  • Invertebrate: An animal that does not have a backbone. This diverse group includes insects, spiders, worms, jellyfish, and snails.
  • Fish: Cold-blooded aquatic vertebrates characterized by gills for breathing underwater and scales covering their bodies.
  • Amphibian: Cold-blooded vertebrates that typically begin their lives in water (often as larvae) and undergo metamorphosis to live on land as adults, though they usually return to water to breed (e.g., frogs, salamanders).
  • Reptile: Cold-blooded vertebrates with dry, scaly skin that typically lay their eggs on land (e.g., lizards, snakes, turtles).
  • Bird: Warm-blooded vertebrates distinguished by feathers, wings for flight (though some are flightless), beaks, and laying hard-shelled eggs.
  • Mammal: Warm-blooded vertebrates typically covered in hair or fur, known for feeding their young with milk produced by mammary glands.
  • Adaptation: A specific physical feature or a behavioral trait that has evolved to help an animal survive and thrive in its particular environment.
  • Cold-blooded: Describes an animal whose internal body temperature fluctuates and largely matches the temperature of its surrounding environment.
  • Warm-blooded: Describes an animal that can maintain a relatively stable internal body temperature, regardless of the temperature of its external surroundings.

Earth Science: Water, Water Everywhere (Unit 12)

  • Water cycle: The continuous, unending movement of water on, above, and below the surface of Earth, driven by solar energy, involving processes like evaporation, condensation, precipitation, and collection.
  • Evaporation: The phase change process where liquid water absorbs heat (usually from the sun) and transforms into water vapor (a gas), rising into the atmosphere.
  • Condensation: The phase change process where water vapor (gas) in the atmosphere cools down and changes back into tiny liquid water droplets or ice crystals, forming clouds, fog, or dew.
  • Precipitation: Any form of water—such as rain, snow, sleet, or hail—that falls from clouds and reaches Earth's surface.
  • Collection: The process where precipitated water gathers on Earth's surface, filling oceans, lakes, rivers, and seeping into the ground to become groundwater.
  • Fresh water: Water containing a very low concentration of dissolved salts. It is found in sources like rivers, lakes, glaciers, and underground aquifers, making up only about 3% of Earth's total water supply.
  • Salt water: Water that contains a significant amount of dissolved salts. It is predominantly found in oceans and seas, comprising about 97% of Earth's water.
  • Conservation: The careful management and protection of natural resources to ensure their continued availability and sustainability for current and future generations.

Chemistry: Atoms — The Building Blocks (Unit 13)

  • Atom: The smallest unit of an element that retains the chemical identity of that element. Atoms are submicroscopic and are the fundamental building blocks of all matter.
  • Molecule: A group of two or more atoms held together by chemical bonds. Molecules can consist of atoms of the same element (e.g., O2) or different elements (e.g., H2O).
  • Element: A pure substance consisting only of atoms that all have the same number of protons. Elements cannot be broken down into simpler substances by ordinary chemical means. Over 100 different elements are known, such as oxygen, carbon, and gold.
  • Periodic table: A systematic chart that organizes all known chemical elements based on their atomic number, electron configurations, and recurring chemical properties, allowing scientists to predict their behaviors.
  • Chemical formula: A shorthand notation using chemical symbols and numbers to represent the types and quantities of atoms present in a molecule or compound (e.g., H2O indicates two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom).
  • Compound: A substance formed when two or more different elements are chemically bonded together in a fixed ratio. Water (H2O) is a compound made of hydrogen and oxygen.

Physics: Energy Is Everywhere (Unit 14)

  • Energy: The capacity or ability to do work or cause change. A fundamental law of physics states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another.
  • Kinetic energy: The energy an object possesses due to its motion. Any object that is moving, from a rolling ball to a flowing river, has kinetic energy.
  • Potential energy: Stored energy that an object has due to its position, state, or chemical composition, which has the potential to be converted into other forms of energy. Examples include a stretched spring, a ball held at a height, or the chemical energy in food.
  • Heat energy: A form of energy associated with the random motion of atoms and molecules within a substance. Heat energy always transfers from warmer objects to cooler objects, causing temperature changes.
  • Light energy: A form of electromagnetic radiation that travels in waves and is visible to the human eye. The sun is Earth's primary natural source of light energy.
  • Sound energy: Energy produced by vibrations that travel as waves through a medium (like air, water, or solids) and can be detected by the ear.
  • Energy transformation: The process by which energy changes from one form to another. For example, a toaster converts electrical energy into heat energy, or a lamp converts electrical energy into light and heat energy.

Detailed Notes

Life Science: The Animal Kingdom (Unit 11)

This unit introduces the vast diversity of animal life, focusing on key characteristics used for classification. The most fundamental distinction is whether an animal has a backbone or not.

  • Vertebrates are animals with an internal skeleton that includes a backbone (or vertebral column). This group is further divided into five major classes:
    • Fish: Live exclusively in water, using gills to extract oxygen. They are cold-blooded and typically covered in scales.
    • Amphibians: Start life in water with gills (e.g., tadpoles) and often develop lungs to live on land as adults (e.g., frogs, salamanders). They have moist skin and are cold-blooded.
    • Reptiles: Have dry, scaly skin and lay leathery eggs on land. They are cold-blooded and breathe with lungs throughout their lives (e.g., snakes, turtles, lizards).
    • Birds: Unique for their feathers, wings (most can fly), and beaks. They are warm-blooded and lay hard-shelled eggs.
    • Mammals: Characterized by hair or fur, being warm-blooded, and producing milk to feed their young.
  • Invertebrates are animals without a backbone. This group is incredibly diverse and includes most animal species on Earth, such as insects, worms, spiders, jellyfish, and snails.
  • Adaptation: An organism's survival depends on its ability to adapt to its environment. Adaptations can be physical (like a bird's wings for flight) or behavioral (like migration to warmer climates).
  • Body Temperature Regulation:
    • Cold-blooded animals rely on external sources to regulate their body temperature, which fluctuates with their surroundings.
    • Warm-blooded animals can maintain a constant internal body temperature regardless of the external environment, often through internal metabolic processes.

Earth Science: Water, Water Everywhere (Unit 12)

This unit focuses on the crucial role of water on Earth and its continuous movement.

  • The Water Cycle: This is a fundamental concept illustrating how water continuously moves between the Earth's surface and atmosphere.
    • Evaporation: Driven by the sun's heat, liquid water turns into water vapor and rises into the atmosphere.
    • Condensation: As water vapor rises and cools, it changes back into tiny liquid droplets or ice crystals, forming clouds.
    • Precipitation: Water falls back to Earth from clouds in various forms like rain, snow, sleet, or hail.
    • Collection: The precipitated water gathers in oceans, lakes, rivers, and seeps into the ground to form groundwater, ready to evaporate again.
  • Types of Water:
    • Salt water: Makes up about 97% of Earth's water, found primarily in oceans. It is too salty for most direct human consumption or agricultural uses.
    • Fresh water: Represents only about 3% of Earth's water. Most of this is locked up in glaciers and ice caps, with smaller amounts in rivers, lakes, and underground. It is vital for life.
  • Conservation: Given the limited availability of usable fresh water, its careful use and protection (conservation) are essential for sustainable living.

Chemistry: Atoms — The Building Blocks (Unit 13)

This unit introduces the fundamental principles of chemistry, starting with the smallest units of matter.

  • Atom: The basic building block of all matter. Atoms are incredibly small and are the smallest particles that retain the properties of a specific element.
  • Element: A pure substance composed of only one type of atom. There are over 100 known elements (e.g., carbon, oxygen, hydrogen), each with unique properties. The Periodic Table is a chart that systematically arranges these elements.
  • Molecule: Formed when two or more atoms bond together. These atoms can be of the same element (e.g., O2, an oxygen molecule) or different elements.
  • Compound: A specific type of molecule formed when two or more different elements are chemically bonded together in a fixed ratio (e.g., water, H2O, is a compound of hydrogen and oxygen).
  • Chemical Formula: A shorthand notation used to represent the composition of molecules and compounds, showing the types of atoms and their quantities (e.g., H2O indicates two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom).

Physics: Energy Is Everywhere (Unit 14)

This unit explores the concept of energy, its various forms, and how it transforms.

  • Energy: Defined as the ability to do work or cause change. A crucial principle in physics is the law of conservation of energy: energy cannot be created or destroyed, only changed from one form to another.
  • Forms of Energy:
    • Kinetic energy: The energy an object possesses due to its motion. The faster an object moves, the more kinetic energy it has.
    • Potential energy: Stored energy that has the capacity to do work but is not currently in motion. This could be gravitational potential energy (an object held high), elastic potential energy (a stretched rubber band), or chemical potential energy (in food or fuel).
    • Heat energy: Associated with the internal motion of atoms and molecules; it is energy that flows from warmer objects to cooler ones.
    • Light energy: A form of electromagnetic energy that travels in waves and allows us to see. The sun is a primary source.
    • Sound energy: Produced by vibrations and travels in waves through a medium, allowing us to hear.
  • Energy Transformation: This is the process where energy changes from one form to another. For example, a burning candle transforms chemical potential energy into light and heat energy. A hydroelectric dam transforms the potential energy of water into kinetic energy, then into electrical energy.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast the characteristics of vertebrates and invertebrates. Provide at least two examples for each category.
  2. Describe the four main stages of the water cycle (evaporation, condensation, precipitation, and collection) and explain how the sun's energy drives this continuous process.
  3. Explain the relationship between atoms, elements, molecules, and compounds. How does a chemical formula help us understand the composition of a compound like water (H2O)?
  4. Differentiate between kinetic energy and potential energy. Give an example of an energy transformation you observe in your daily life, identifying at least two forms of energy involved.
  5. Imagine you are observing a pond ecosystem. How might you apply at least one vocabulary word from each of the four science branches (Life Science, Earth Science, Chemistry, and Physics) to describe what you see or understand about the pond? (e.g., an amphibian in the collection of fresh water, influenced by the elements in its environment, and moving with kinetic energy.)
