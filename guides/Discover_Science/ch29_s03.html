Study Guide: Magnets

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental properties of magnets, explaining that every magnet has two distinct poles where its force is strongest. It details how these poles interact—opposite poles attract, and like poles repel—and describes the invisible area of force around a magnet called a magnetic field. Finally, it identifies specific materials that are attracted to magnets and reveals how Earth itself acts as a giant magnet, which is the principle behind how a compass works.

Key Concepts

  • Magnetic Poles: Every magnet, regardless of its size or shape, has two ends called poles: a North pole and a South pole. These are the points where the magnetic force is strongest. Even if a magnet is broken, each new piece will still have both a North and a South pole.
  • Law of Magnetic Poles: This fundamental rule states that opposite magnetic poles attract each other (North pole and South pole), while like magnetic poles repel each other (North pole and North pole, or South pole and South pole). This interaction is similar to how electric charges behave.
  • Magnetic Fields: Magnets create an invisible region of force around them called a magnetic field. This field is responsible for a magnet's ability to attract or repel objects without direct contact. Magnetic fields can be visualized using iron filings, which arrange themselves in curved lines extending from one pole to the other.
  • Magnetic Materials: Only a select few materials are attracted to magnets. The primary magnetic materials are iron, nickel, and cobalt, as well as alloys (mixtures) that contain them (like steel, which is mostly iron). Many common metals, such as aluminum, copper, and gold, are not magnetic.
  • Earth as a Magnet: Our planet acts like a colossal magnet due to the movement of molten iron deep within its core, generating a vast magnetic field that surrounds Earth. This natural magnetic field is what allows compasses to function, as the compass needle (a small magnet) aligns itself with Earth's magnetic field, pointing roughly north.

Important Terms

  • Poles (of a magnet) : The two ends of a magnet where the magnetic force is strongest. Every magnet has a north pole and a south pole.
  • North pole (N) : One of the two distinct poles of a magnet, often labeled 'N'. It repels other north poles and attracts south poles.
  • South pole (S) : One of the two distinct poles of a magnet, often labeled 'S'. It repels other south poles and attracts north poles.
  • Magnetic field : The invisible area around a magnet where its force can attract or repel other magnets or magnetic materials.
  • Magnetic material : A material that is attracted to a magnet, such as iron, nickel, and cobalt.
  • Compass : An instrument containing a small, freely rotating magnet (the needle) that aligns itself with Earth's magnetic field, used for navigation.

Detailed Notes

Every Magnet Has Two Poles

Every magnet possesses two distinct regions of concentrated magnetic force, known as poles. These poles are usually located at the ends of a magnet. We label these as the north pole (N) and the south pole (S). An important characteristic of magnets is that poles always come in pairs. You can never find a magnet with only a north pole or only a south pole. Even if you were to break a bar magnet into smaller pieces, each new piece would immediately form its own north and south poles.

Attract and Repel — The Fundamental Rule

The way magnets interact with each other is governed by a simple, yet powerful, rule:

  • Opposite poles attract: If you bring a north pole close to a south pole, they will pull towards each other and snap together.
  • Like poles repel: If you bring two north poles together, or two south poles together, they will push away from each other.

This behavior is very similar to how positive and negative electric charges interact (opposites attract, likes repel), hinting at the deep connection between electricity and magnetism.

Magnetic Fields: The Invisible Force

You don't need to physically touch a magnet to feel its effects. If you hold a magnet near a small metal object like a paper clip, you'll notice the paper clip start to move before contact is made. This "action at a distance" is due to an invisible area of force surrounding every magnet called a magnetic field.

While magnetic fields cannot be seen directly, their presence and shape can be revealed. A common demonstration involves sprinkling iron filings (tiny bits of iron) onto a piece of paper placed over a magnet. The iron filings will align themselves along the lines of force, creating a visible pattern of curved lines that spread out from one pole and loop around to the other. These lines illustrate the shape and direction of the magnetic field.

What Is Magnetic?

It's a common misconception that all metals are magnetic. In reality, only a few specific materials are attracted to magnets. These are called magnetic materials. The primary elements that are magnetic are:

  • Iron: Very common in nails, paper clips, and is the main component of steel.
  • Nickel: Found in some coins and certain types of jewelry.
  • Cobalt: Used in some tools, specialized alloys, and batteries.

Materials made from these elements, or alloys (mixtures) containing them, will interact with a magnet. However, many other common metals, such as aluminum (used in cans), copper (used in pennies and wires), and gold (used in rings), are not magnetic and will not be attracted to a magnet.

Here's a quick comparison of some magnetic and non-magnetic items:

Magnetic

  • Iron nail
  • Steel paper clip
  • Nickel coin
  • Steel refrigerator door
  • Iron horseshoe

Not Magnetic

  • Aluminum can
  • Copper penny
  • Gold ring
  • Wooden table
  • Plastic bottle

Earth: A Giant Magnet

One of the most remarkable examples of magnetism is our own planet. Earth itself functions as an enormous magnet! This global magnetic field is generated deep within Earth's core, where vast currents of molten iron flow and churn.

This planetary magnetic field is crucial for the operation of a compass. A compass contains a small, lightweight magnet (the needle) that is carefully balanced so it can rotate freely. Because opposite poles attract, the north pole of the compass needle is pulled towards Earth's magnetic south pole. Interestingly, Earth's magnetic south pole is located near our geographic North Pole. This is why a compass needle consistently points roughly towards the geographic north, providing a reliable way for travelers and navigators to determine direction.

If you bring a strong bar magnet close to a compass, you'll notice the compass needle swings away from its usual north-pointing direction and aligns with the bar magnet's field instead. This demonstrates that the local magnetic field of the bar magnet is stronger than Earth's magnetic field at that close distance. As you move the bar magnet away, the compass needle will return to pointing north, re-aligning with Earth's broader, weaker field.

Review Questions

  1. Explain what magnetic poles are and why it's impossible to have a magnet with only one pole. What happens if you break a magnet in half?
  2. Describe the fundamental rule of how magnetic poles interact. Provide examples for both attraction and repulsion. How is this similar to the behavior of electric charges?
  3. What is a magnetic field, and why is it considered "invisible"? How can you demonstrate or visualize the presence and shape of a magnetic field?
  4. List the main materials that are attracted to magnets. Name at least three common metals that are not magnetic, and explain why this distinction is important.
  5. How does Earth act as a giant magnet, and what causes this phenomenon? Explain how this allows a compass to work, detailing the interaction between the compass needle and Earth's magnetic field.
