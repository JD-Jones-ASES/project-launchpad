Chapter 13, Section 3 Study Guide: Elements and the Periodic Table

Overview

This section introduces elements as the fundamental building blocks of all matter, defining them as pure substances made of only one kind of atom. It explains that scientists have identified about 118 elements, each with a unique chemical symbol. Finally, it describes the periodic table as an essential tool for organizing these elements based on their properties, highlighting the significance of the atomic number.

Key Concepts

  • Elements are Pure Substances: An element is a pure substance consisting of only one type of atom, and it cannot be broken down into simpler substances by ordinary chemical means. Examples include gold, iron, oxygen, and hydrogen.
  • Limited Number of Elements: Everything in the universe, from stars to living things, is composed of approximately 118 known elements. While this number seems small, these elements combine in countless ways to form millions of different substances.
  • Chemical Symbols: Each element is represented by a unique one- or two-letter symbol. The first letter is always capitalized, and the second (if present) is always lowercase. These symbols provide a universal way for scientists to refer to elements, sometimes deriving from ancient languages like Latin.
  • The Periodic Table Organizes Elements: The periodic table is a chart designed to organize all known elements based on their properties. It's an indispensable tool for scientists to understand and predict how elements will behave.
  • Atomic Number Identifies Elements: Each element has a unique atomic number, which represents the number of protons in the nucleus of its atoms. This number is used to arrange elements in order on the periodic table, from lightest to heaviest.
  • Elements Grouped by Properties: Elements with similar chemical behaviors or properties are grouped together in columns on the periodic table, making it easier to study their characteristics.

Important Terms

  • Element: A pure substance made of only one kind of atom. It cannot be broken down into simpler substances.
  • Chemical symbol: A one- or two-letter abbreviation for an element. The first letter is always capitalized.
  • Periodic table: A chart that organizes all known elements by their properties. Elements with similar properties are grouped together.
  • Atomic number: The number that identifies each element. It tells how many protons are in the center of each atom of that element.

Detailed Notes

What Are Elements?

  • Definition: An element is a pure substance that is made of only one specific kind of atom. This means all atoms in a sample of an element are identical.
  • Indivisible: Elements cannot be broken down into simpler substances using ordinary chemical methods. They are fundamental building blocks of matter.
  • Examples:
    • Gold (Au): Every atom in a pure gold piece is a gold atom.
    • Iron (Fe): Every atom in a pure iron piece is an iron atom.
    • Oxygen (O), Hydrogen (H), Carbon (C): Other common examples of elements.

How Many Elements Are There?

  • Total Discovered: Scientists have identified approximately 118 different elements.
  • Universal Building Blocks: Remarkably, these 118 elements combine in various ways to form everything in the entire universe—stars, planets, living organisms, water, and sand.
  • Common Elements: While there are 118 elements, you typically encounter only about 20-30 of them regularly in daily life.
  • Examples of Common Elements and Their Uses:
    • Hydrogen (H): A gas found in water; it's the most common element in the universe.
    • Oxygen (O): A gas essential for breathing, found in the air.
    • Carbon (C): A solid found in pencil lead (graphite) and all living things.
    • Nitrogen (N): A gas that makes up about 78% of the air.
    • Iron (Fe): A solid used in nails, bridges, and is found in your blood.
    • Gold (Au): A solid used in jewelry and electronics.
    • Aluminum (Al): A solid used in soda cans and foil.
    • Helium (He): A gas that makes balloons float, used in party balloons.

Element Symbols

  • Purpose: Each element has a unique chemical symbol, which is a short, standardized abbreviation for its name.
  • Rules for Symbols:
    • Symbols are usually one or two letters.
    • The first letter is always capitalized.
    • If there is a second letter, it is always lowercase.
  • Examples of Symbols:
    • Easy to Guess: H for Hydrogen, O for Oxygen, C for Carbon.
    • Tricky (Latin Origins): Some symbols come from older languages like Latin, reflecting the international nature of scientific discovery.
      • Fe for Iron (from ferrum)
      • Au for Gold (from aurum)

The Periodic Table

  • Purpose: The periodic table is a critical chart used by scientists to organize all 118 known elements. It's like a comprehensive directory for atoms.
  • Structure:
    • Each element has its own designated square on the chart.
    • Elements are arranged in a specific order, generally from the lightest (Hydrogen, atomic number 1) to the heaviest.
    • Elements are also grouped into columns based on similar chemical properties and behaviors. For instance, helium, neon, and argon are in the same column because they are all gases that are very unreactive.
  • Information in Each Square: While the full details are complex, at a basic level, each square on the periodic table typically shows:
    • The element's name.
    • The element's chemical symbol.
    • The atomic number.
  • Atomic Number:
    • This is a unique number for each element.
    • It tells you how many protons are in the center (nucleus) of every atom of that element.
    • The atomic number is what defines an element and determines its position on the periodic table.
  • No Need to Memorize (Yet!): You don't need to memorize the entire periodic table now, but it's important to understand its existence, purpose, and how scientists use it as a fundamental reference tool.

Elements in Everyday Life

  • Technology: Many common items, especially technology, contain a variety of elements. For example, a smartphone might contain gold (Au), silver (Ag), copper (Cu), aluminum (Al), lithium (Li) in the battery, and silicon (Si) in the computer chip. Coins often contain copper (Cu), nickel (Ni), and zinc (Zn).
  • Complexity from Simplicity: The fact that only about 118 elements can combine to form millions of different substances is similar to how the 26 letters of the alphabet can form thousands of words and countless sentences. Elements combine in endless ways to create the diverse world around us.

Review Questions

  1. What is an element, and how is it different from a general substance? Provide two examples of elements.
  2. Why do scientists use chemical symbols for elements instead of writing out their full names every time? What are the rules for writing a chemical symbol?
  3. What is the primary purpose of the periodic table, and how does it help scientists organize the elements?
  4. What three key pieces of information can you typically find in an element's square on the periodic table? Explain the significance of the atomic number.
  5. If there are only about 118 known elements, how is it possible for there to be millions of different substances and objects in the world?
