Vocabulary Review: Cycle 2 Study Guide

Overview

This study guide provides a comprehensive review of key vocabulary terms from Cycle 2 of your science curriculum, encompassing Units 6–9. It covers essential concepts from Life Science (The Plant Kingdom), Earth Science (Rocks and Minerals), Chemistry (Mixing and Separating), and Physics (Measuring Our World). The goal is to ensure you can confidently define each term and understand its significance within its scientific context.

Key Concepts

  • The Plant Kingdom: Plants are living organisms with specialized structures (roots, stem, leaves, flowers, fruit, seeds) that perform vital functions like anchoring, support, food production through photosynthesis, reproduction, and nutrient transport via internal tubes (xylem and phloem).
  • Rocks and Minerals: Earth's crust is composed of rocks, which are natural solids made of minerals. Rocks are continuously transformed through the rock cycle, changing between igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic types due to processes involving heat, pressure, and the cooling of molten rock (magma and lava).
  • Mixing and Separating: Substances can combine to form mixtures, where individual components retain their properties. Solutions are a special type of mixture where one substance (solute) completely dissolves in another (solvent). Various methods like filtering and evaporation can be used to separate components of mixtures based on their physical properties, and concentration describes the amount of solute present.
  • Measuring Our World: Physics provides tools and concepts for quantifying the world around us. Key measurements include length, mass, volume, and temperature, each with specific units and instruments (like graduated cylinders). Precision is crucial for accurate scientific observations and experiments.

Important Terms

  • Roots: The part of a plant that anchors it in the soil and absorbs water and nutrients.
  • Stem: The part of a plant that supports the leaves and flowers and carries water and nutrients between roots and leaves.
  • Leaves: The part of a plant that captures sunlight and makes food through photosynthesis.
  • Flowers: The part of a plant involved in reproduction; they attract pollinators and produce seeds.
  • Fruit: The part of a plant that surrounds and protects the seeds.
  • Seeds: Tiny packages containing a baby plant and stored food; they can grow into new plants.
  • Photosynthesis: The process by which plants use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to make sugar (food) and release oxygen.
  • Xylem: Tiny tubes inside a plant's stem that carry water upward from the roots to the leaves.
  • Phloem: Tiny tubes inside a plant's stem that carry sugar downward from the leaves to the rest of the plant.
  • Rock: A solid natural material made of one or more minerals.
  • Mineral: A natural, nonliving solid with a specific chemical makeup and crystal structure.
  • Igneous rock: Rock formed when melted rock (magma or lava) cools and hardens.
  • Sedimentary rock: Rock formed when tiny pieces of rock, sand, or once-living things are pressed and cemented together over time.
  • Metamorphic rock: Rock that has been changed by heat and pressure deep underground.
  • Rock cycle: The never-ending process by which rocks change from one type to another over very long periods of time.
  • Magma: Melted rock found underground.
  • Lava: Melted rock that has reached Earth's surface.
  • Mixture: A combination of two or more substances that are physically combined but not chemically changed. Each substance keeps its own properties.
  • Solution: A special type of mixture in which one substance dissolves completely in another.
  • Dissolve: To mix completely into a liquid so that the substance seems to disappear (but is still there).
  • Solute: The substance that dissolves in a solution (e.g., sugar in lemonade).
  • Solvent: The substance that does the dissolving (e.g., water in lemonade).
  • Filtering: Separating a mixture by pouring it through a material with tiny holes that catch larger particles.
  • Evaporation: The process of a liquid turning into a gas, often used to separate a dissolved solid from a liquid.
  • Concentration: How much solute is dissolved in a given amount of solvent; a concentrated solution has a lot of solute.
  • Measurement: Using numbers and units to describe the size, amount, or condition of something.
  • Precision: How close together repeated measurements are; careful measurements are more precise.
  • Length: How long something is, measured in centimeters (cm) or meters (m).
  • Mass: The amount of matter in an object, measured in grams (g) or kilograms (kg).
  • Volume: The amount of space something takes up, measured in milliliters (mL) or liters (L).
  • Graduated cylinder: A tall, narrow container used to measure the volume of liquids.
  • Temperature: How hot or cold something is, measured in degrees Celsius (°C).

Detailed Notes

Life Science: The Plant Kingdom (Unit 6)

This unit focuses on the structure and function of plants, highlighting how different parts contribute to the plant's survival and reproduction.

  • Roots: Anchor the plant firmly in the soil and are essential for absorbing water and dissolved nutrients from the soil.
  • Stem: Provides structural support for the plant, holding up the leaves and flowers. It also acts as a transport highway, carrying water and nutrients (through xylem) from the roots to the leaves, and carrying sugars (through phloem) made in the leaves to other parts of the plant.
  • Leaves: The primary sites for photosynthesis. Their broad, flat surface helps capture maximum sunlight.
  • Flowers: The reproductive organs of many plants. They often have bright colors or scents to attract pollinators, which help in the formation of seeds.
  • Fruit: Develops from the flower and encloses and protects the seeds, often aiding in seed dispersal.
  • Seeds: Contain a tiny embryonic plant and a food supply, allowing the potential for a new plant to grow.
  • Photosynthesis: The chemical process where plants convert light energy, water, and carbon dioxide into glucose (sugar, their food) and oxygen. This process is fundamental to life on Earth.
  • Xylem: Specialized vascular tissue that forms continuous tubes within the plant, conducting water and dissolved minerals from the roots upwards.
  • Phloem: Another type of vascular tissue that transports sugars produced during photosynthesis from the leaves to all other parts of the plant where they are needed for energy or storage.

Earth Science: Rocks and Minerals (Unit 7)

This unit introduces the fundamental components of Earth's crust and the dynamic processes that continually transform them.

  • Rock: A naturally occurring solid aggregate composed of one or more minerals or mineraloids. Rocks are the main building blocks of the Earth's solid surface.
  • Mineral: A naturally occurring, inorganic solid with a definite chemical composition and a characteristic crystal structure. Minerals are the basic components of rocks.
  • Igneous rock: Forms when molten rock cools and solidifies. This can happen underground (forming intrusive igneous rocks from magma) or on the Earth's surface (forming extrusive igneous rocks from lava).
  • Sedimentary rock: Forms from the accumulation and compaction of sediments (fragments of other rocks, minerals, or organic matter). These sediments are often transported by wind, water, or ice and then cemented together.
  • Metamorphic rock: Forms when existing rocks are subjected to intense heat, pressure, or chemical alteration deep within the Earth's crust. This process changes the rock's texture, mineralogy, or chemical composition.
  • Rock cycle: A geological concept that describes the continuous processes through which rocks are created, destroyed, and reformed over vast timescales. It illustrates the interrelationships between igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic rocks.
  • Magma: Molten rock material that originates beneath the Earth's surface. When it cools and solidifies, it forms intrusive igneous rocks.
  • Lava: Molten rock that has erupted onto the Earth's surface through volcanoes or fissures. When it cools and solidifies, it forms extrusive igneous rocks.

Chemistry: Mixing and Separating (Unit 8)

This unit explores how different substances combine and how they can be separated, focusing on mixtures and solutions.

  • Mixture: A substance containing two or more different pure substances which are physically combined but not chemically bonded. The individual substances retain their chemical identities and properties. Examples include sand and water, or air.
  • Solution: A homogeneous mixture where one substance (the solute) is uniformly dispersed or dissolved in another substance (the solvent). Solutions appear clear and do not separate over time. Examples include salt water or sugar water.
  • Dissolve: The process by which a solute mixes completely with a solvent to form a solution. The solute particles become surrounded by solvent particles and are no longer individually visible.
  • Solute: The substance that is present in a lesser amount and dissolves in a solvent to form a solution (e.g., salt in saltwater).
  • Solvent: The substance that is present in a greater amount and dissolves the solute to form a solution (e.g., water in saltwater). Water is often called the "universal solvent."
  • Filtering: A separation technique used for heterogeneous mixtures (where particles are large enough to be trapped). It involves passing a mixture through a porous material (like filter paper) that retains solid particles while allowing the liquid or gas to pass through.
  • Evaporation: A separation technique used for solutions where a solid is dissolved in a liquid. The liquid solvent is heated and turns into a gas, leaving the solid solute behind.
  • Concentration: A measure of the amount of solute present in a given amount of solvent or solution. A high concentration means a large amount of solute, while a low concentration means a small amount.

Physics: Measuring Our World (Unit 9)

This unit introduces fundamental physical measurements and the importance of accurate data in scientific inquiry.

  • Measurement: The process of assigning a numerical value to a physical quantity, typically by comparison with a standard unit. It allows us to quantify observations and make them objective.
  • Precision: Refers to the closeness of two or more measurements to each other. High precision indicates that repeated measurements under the same conditions yield very similar results, even if they are not necessarily close to the true value.
  • Length: A measure of the linear extent of an object from one end to the other. Common units include centimeters (cm) and meters (m).
  • Mass: A measure of the amount of matter in an object. It is a fundamental property of an object and is typically measured in grams (g) or kilograms (kg).
  • Volume: A measure of the amount of three-dimensional space an object or substance occupies. Common units include milliliters (mL) and liters (L).
  • Graduated cylinder: A cylindrical laboratory tool with markings (graduations) along the side, used for precisely measuring the volume of liquids.
  • Temperature: A physical quantity that expresses the degree of hotness or coldness of a substance. It is measured in degrees Celsius (°C), Kelvin (K), or Fahrenheit (°F).

Review Questions

  1. Explain the process of photosynthesis in a plant, making sure to use the terms leaves, xylem, and phloem to describe how necessary ingredients are obtained and products are distributed.
  2. Imagine you find a rock in your backyard. Describe how this rock might have formed through the rock cycle, explaining the characteristics of igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic rocks in your answer.
  3. You are making a lemonade solution. Identify the solute and solvent, and describe how you could separate the sugar from the water if you wanted to using a technique from this unit. What would happen to the concentration if you added more sugar without adding more water?
  4. Why is precision important when making scientific measurements of length, mass, and volume? Give an example of a situation where imprecise measurements could lead to problems.
  5. Consider a garden scenario. How might the terms roots, soil (as a mixture), and water (as a solvent) be connected when discussing how a plant gets its nutrients? Use at least three vocabulary words from different science branches to formulate your answer.
