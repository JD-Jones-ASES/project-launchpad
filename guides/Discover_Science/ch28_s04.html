Chapter 28, Section 4: States of Matter and Conservation of Matter Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the atomic-level behavior of matter in its three common states: solid, liquid, and gas, emphasizing how atomic movement changes with temperature. It then introduces the fundamental principle of the Conservation of Matter, explaining that matter cannot be created or destroyed, only rearranged, a crucial concept in chemistry.

Key Concepts

  • States of Matter (Atomic View): The physical state of matter (solid, liquid, gas) is determined by how fast its atoms or molecules are moving and how closely packed they are.
    • Solid: Atoms/molecules are tightly packed and vibrate in fixed positions, giving solids a definite shape.
    • Liquid: Atoms/molecules are close but can slide past each other, allowing liquids to flow and take the shape of their container.
    • Gas: Atoms/molecules move quickly and are far apart, spreading out to fill any container.
  • Heating and Cooling (Phase Changes): Temperature changes affect the energy and movement of atoms, leading to changes in the state of matter.
    • Heating: Adds energy, causing atoms to move faster, leading to melting (solid to liquid) and boiling (liquid to gas).
    • Cooling: Removes energy, causing atoms to slow down, leading to condensing (gas to liquid) and freezing (liquid to solid).
    • During phase changes, the atoms themselves remain the same; only their arrangement and movement change.
  • Conservation of Matter: This fundamental principle (also known as the Law of Conservation of Mass) states that matter cannot be created or destroyed in any chemical or physical change. The total amount of matter, or mass, remains constant because atoms are simply rearranged into new substances or configurations.
  • Importance of Conservation of Matter: This law allows scientists to predict the products and quantities in chemical reactions, as no atoms are lost or gained. It underpins our understanding of chemical changes.

Important Terms

  • Solid: A state of matter where atoms or molecules are packed closely together and vibrate in fixed positions, giving it a definite shape and volume.
  • Liquid: A state of matter where atoms or molecules are close together but can slide past one another, allowing it to flow and take the shape of its container while maintaining a definite volume.
  • Gas: A state of matter where atoms or molecules move quickly and are far apart, spreading out to fill any container and having no definite shape or volume.
  • Melting: The process by which a solid changes into a liquid due to the addition of heat energy.
  • Boiling: The process by which a liquid changes into a gas due to the addition of heat energy.
  • Condensing: The process by which a gas changes into a liquid due to the removal of heat energy (cooling).
  • Freezing: The process by which a liquid changes into a solid due to the removal of heat energy (cooling).
  • Conservation of Matter (Law of Conservation of Mass): The scientific principle that states matter cannot be created or destroyed, only rearranged, during any physical or chemical change. The total mass of substances involved remains constant.

Detailed Notes

States of Matter: An Atomic View

You've previously learned about the basic states of matter. Now, we're looking at what's happening at the atomic and molecular level within solids, liquids, and gases. The key difference among these states is how fast their atoms and molecules are moving.

  • Solids:
    • Atoms or molecules are packed very closely together.
    • They vibrate back and forth in place but do not move freely.
    • This tight arrangement and limited movement explain why solids hold their shape and have a definite volume.
    • Analogy: Students in a crowded line can wiggle but can't wander off.
  • Liquids:
    • Atoms or molecules are still close together, but they have enough energy to slide past one another.
    • This ability to move past each other allows liquids to flow and to take the shape of their container while maintaining a definite volume.
    • Analogy: Students walking slowly in a hallway can move around each other but stay fairly close.
  • Gases:
    • Atoms or molecules move very fast and are spread far apart.
    • They zoom in all directions, bouncing off each other and the container walls.
    • This rapid, free movement means gases will spread out to fill any container, having no definite shape or volume.
    • Analogy: Students running freely across a big field spread out and move in every direction.

Heating and Cooling (Phase Changes)

The speed and movement of atoms and molecules are directly linked to temperature.

  • Heating a substance:
    • You are adding energy to its atoms and molecules.
    • This added energy causes the atoms and molecules to move faster.
    • If you heat a solid enough, its atoms vibrate so much that they break free from their fixed positions and start sliding past each other – the solid melts into a liquid.
    • Continue adding heat, and the molecules in the liquid move even faster until they overcome the attractions between them and fly apart completely – the liquid boils into a gas.
  • Cooling a substance:
    • You are removing energy from its atoms and molecules.
    • This removal of energy causes the atoms and molecules to slow down.
    • Cool a gas enough, and its molecules slow down and come closer together – the gas condenses into a liquid.
    • Cool the liquid further, and its molecules slow down even more and settle into fixed positions – the liquid freezes into a solid.
  • Important Note: Throughout these changes, the atoms themselves remain the same. For example, when ice melts into water and then boils into steam, it's still H₂O molecules, just with different amounts of energy and movement. The water doesn't disappear when it turns to steam; the H₂O molecules are still present, just spread out as a gas.

Conservation of Matter

This is a cornerstone of chemistry:

  • Definition: The Conservation of Matter (also known as the Law of Conservation of Mass) states that matter cannot be created or destroyed. In any physical or chemical change or reaction, the total amount of matter (and thus total mass) remains the same. The atoms involved are simply rearranged, but none are lost or created.
  • Examples of Conservation of Matter:
    • Burning a log: While the wood seems to vanish, its atoms rearrange to form gases (like carbon dioxide and water vapor) and ash. If all these products were collected, their total mass would equal the original log's mass plus the oxygen consumed from the air.
    • Dissolving sugar in water: The sugar appears to disappear, but its atoms are still present, mixed within the water molecules. The total mass of the sugar water solution is exactly the sum of the mass of the water and the mass of the sugar.
    • Rusting iron: When iron rusts, it gains mass because oxygen atoms from the air chemically bond with the iron atoms. The "extra" mass comes from these added oxygen atoms; mass was not created from nothing.
    • Bead Activity Connection: If you performed an activity where beads were rearranged to form new "molecules," you likely found that the number of beads (and thus their total mass) remained constant before and after the rearrangement. This directly demonstrates conservation of matter.

Why Conservation of Matter Matters

  • Predicting Chemical Reactions: Because atoms are conserved, chemists can predict what products will form and in what quantities in a chemical reaction. If you know what goes into a reaction, you can determine what must come out, as no atoms are lost along the way.
  • Connection to Chemical Changes (Unit 18): When you observe a chemical change, like mixing vinegar and baking soda to produce bubbles (carbon dioxide gas), new molecules are formed. Even though the appearance changes dramatically, the total mass of all substances involved (reactants and products, including the gas) would be the same if measured in a closed system. This principle was also explored when learning about Antoine Lavoisier in Unit 18.

Famous Scientist: Linus Pauling

  • Linus Pauling (1901–1994) was a groundbreaking American chemist.
  • His most significant contribution was figuring out how and why atoms bond together, explaining that atoms share or transfer tiny particles (electrons) to form bonds and how these bonds dictate a molecule's shape.
  • He received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1954 for this work.
  • Remarkably, Pauling also won the Nobel Peace Prize in 1962 for his efforts to stop nuclear weapons testing, making him the only person in history to win two unshared Nobel Prizes.
  • Later in his life, he researched vitamin C's potential health benefits, a topic still debated, but his contributions to understanding chemical bonds are undeniable.
