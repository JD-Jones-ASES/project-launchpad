Three Types of Rocks Study Guide

Overview

Rocks are fundamental components of Earth's crust, and scientists categorize them into three main types—igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic—based on their distinct formation processes. Each type tells a unique geological story, shaped by intense heat, pressure, or the slow accumulation and cementation of materials over vast periods, resulting in varied appearances and compositions.

Key Concepts

  • Rock Classification by Formation: Rocks are grouped into igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic based how they formed, not just their current appearance.
  • Igneous Rocks: Formed from Melted Rock: These rocks originate from the cooling and hardening of molten rock.
    • Magma vs. Lava: Molten rock underground is called magma; molten rock that flows onto the Earth's surface is called lava.
    • Cooling Rate and Grain Size: Slow cooling (deep underground) results in large, visible mineral grains (e.g., granite), while fast cooling (on the surface) produces tiny, often microscopic grains (e.g., basalt).
  • Sedimentary Rocks: Formed from Layers of Sediment: These rocks are created when pieces of other rocks, sand, mud, or shells (called sediment) are deposited, compacted by weight, and then cemented together over time.
    • Fossils: Sedimentary rocks are the only type that can contain fossils, which are preserved remains or traces of ancient life.
  • Metamorphic Rocks: Formed by Transformation: These rocks are existing igneous, sedimentary, or even other metamorphic rocks that have been changed ("metamorphosed") by intense heat, pressure, or both, deep within the Earth.
    • Mineral Rearrangement: The heat and pressure cause the minerals within the original rock to rearrange, forming a new rock with different characteristics.
  • Distinct Characteristics: Each rock type has specific visual clues (e.g., crystals, layers, fossils, wavy bands) that help in identification.

Important Terms

  • Igneous rock: A type of rock that forms when hot, melted rock (magma or lava) cools and hardens.
  • Magma: Hot, melted rock that is found deep underground within the Earth.
  • Lava: Hot, melted rock that flows out of a volcano onto the Earth's surface.
  • Sedimentary rock: A type of rock that forms when small pieces of rock, sand, mud, or shells (sediment) are pressed and cemented together over time.
  • Sediment: Tiny bits of rock, sand, mud, or shells that settle and accumulate, often carried by wind or water.
  • Fossils: The preserved remains or traces of ancient living things, almost exclusively found in sedimentary rocks.
  • Metamorphic rock: A type of rock that has been changed from its original form by intense heat, pressure, or both deep inside the Earth.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Rock Classification

Scientists classify rocks into three main groups—igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic—based on their unique formation processes. Understanding how a rock forms helps us understand its properties and history.

I. Igneous Rocks: Born from Fire

Igneous rocks are fundamentally rocks that form from the cooling and solidification of molten rock. The word "igneous" itself comes from the Latin word for "fire," aptly describing their origin.

  • Molten Rock:
    • Magma: Melted rock that exists beneath the Earth's surface. It is incredibly hot, capable of melting solid rock.
    • Lava: Melted rock that has erupted and flowed onto the Earth's surface from a volcano.
  • Formation Process: When magma or lava loses heat, it eventually cools down and solidifies, forming igneous rock.
  • Two Main Kinds Based on Cooling Speed:
    • Granite (Intrusive Igneous):
      • Forms when magma cools slowly deep underground.
      • Slow cooling allows mineral grains sufficient time to grow large enough to be easily seen with the naked eye.
      • Often composed of visible specks of quartz, feldspar, and mica.
    • Basalt (Extrusive Igneous):
      • Forms when lava cools quickly on the Earth's surface.
      • Rapid cooling results in very tiny mineral grains, often too small to see without magnification.
      • Typically dark gray or black in color.
    • Mnemonic: Slow cooling = big grains. Fast cooling = tiny grains.

II. Sedimentary Rocks: Layer by Layer

Sedimentary rocks are formed from the accumulation, compaction, and cementation of sediment.

  • Formation Process:
    1. Erosion and Transport: Existing rocks are broken down into smaller pieces (sediment) by weathering and erosion. Rivers, wind, and glaciers transport these sediments.
    2. Deposition: As transport agents slow down, sediments settle and are deposited, often in layers, at the bottom of bodies of water or on land.
    3. Compaction: Over thousands or millions of years, new layers of sediment pile on top, and the immense weight of the overlying layers presses the bottom layers together, squeezing out water.
    4. Cementation: Minerals dissolved in groundwater seep through the compacted sediment and act like a natural glue, cementing the individual pieces together into solid rock.
  • Common Sedimentary Rocks:
    • Sandstone: Made primarily of tiny grains of sand pressed together. It often has a rough texture, similar to sandpaper.
    • Limestone: Frequently formed from the shells and skeletal remains of marine organisms (like coral or clams). It has a unique chemical property: it fizzes when a drop of vinegar (an acid) is applied.
    • Shale: Composed of very fine particles of mud. It characteristically splits into thin, flat layers.
  • Unique Feature: Fossils: Sedimentary rocks are the only type of rock that can contain fossils—the preserved remains or traces of ancient living things. This makes them crucial for studying Earth's past life.

III. Metamorphic Rocks: Changed by Heat and Pressure

Metamorphic rocks are rocks that have transformed from their original state due to intense heat, pressure, or chemical reactions deep within the Earth. The term "metamorphic" literally means "changed in form."

  • Formation Process:
    • Heat: Rocks can be heated by nearby magma chambers or by the general increase in temperature with depth inside the Earth.
    • Pressure: The enormous weight of overlying rocks, or the intense squeezing forces from tectonic plate movements, can apply immense pressure.
    • Rearrangement: This heat and pressure cause the minerals within the original "parent" rock to recrystallize or rearrange, creating a new rock with different mineral compositions, textures, and structures.
  • Common Metamorphic Rocks and Their Parent Rocks:
    • Marble: Forms when limestone (a sedimentary rock) is subjected to heat and pressure. Marble is typically smooth, often beautifully colored, and has been used by sculptors for centuries.
    • Slate: Forms when shale (a sedimentary rock) is compressed. Slate easily splits into smooth, flat sheets, which historically made it useful for writing boards.
    • Quartzite: Forms when sandstone (a sedimentary rock) is heated and pressed. Quartzite is known for being very hard and tough.

Comparing the Three Types of Rocks

Feature Igneous Sedimentary Metamorphic
How it forms Melted rock (magma/lava) cools and hardens Layers of sediment are pressed and cemented together Existing rock is changed by intense heat and pressure
Key clues May have crystals (large or tiny), or a glassy appearance May have visible layers, sediment grains, or fossils May have wavy bands, a shiny or folded look, or mineral alignment
Examples Granite, Basalt Sandstone, Limestone, Shale Marble, Slate, Quartzite

Spotting Rocks in the Real World

You can often identify different rock types in everyday settings. For instance:

  • Marble (metamorphic) might appear in smooth, swirling-colored building facades or countertops.
  • Sandstone (sedimentary) might be seen in buildings or pavers with a rough, sandy texture.
  • Granite (igneous) is common in countertops and building exteriors, recognizable by its visible, interlocked mineral specks.

Review Questions

  1. What is the primary factor that scientists use to classify rocks into igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic types? Why is this factor more important than just a rock's appearance?
  2. Explain the relationship between magma, lava, and igneous rocks. How does the rate of cooling affect the appearance of different igneous rocks like granite and basalt?
  3. Describe the sequential steps involved in the formation of a sedimentary rock, starting from the breakdown of existing rocks. What unique feature can only be found in sedimentary rocks, and what does it tell us about Earth's history?
  4. How do metamorphic rocks acquire their "changed form"? Provide examples of three metamorphic rocks mentioned in the text and identify the original rock from which each formed.
  5. Imagine you are a geologist examining an unknown rock sample. What specific visual clues would you look for to determine if it is igneous, sedimentary, or metamorphic? Give at least two distinct clues for each rock type.
