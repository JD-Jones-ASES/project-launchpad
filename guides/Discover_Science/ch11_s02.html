Vertebrates and Invertebrates: A Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental way scientists classify animals: based on whether they possess a backbone or not. It defines vertebrates as animals with an internal backbone and endoskeleton, and invertebrates as animals without a backbone, some of which may have an external exoskeleton or no skeleton at all. The section highlights the relative abundance of each group in the animal kingdom.

Key Concepts

  • Backbone as a Classifier: The presence or absence of a backbone (spine) is the primary characteristic used to divide the entire animal kingdom into two main groups.
  • Vertebrates: Animals that possess an internal backbone. They also have an endoskeleton (internal skeleton) made of bone or cartilage that provides support, protects organs, and aids movement. Despite being commonly recognized, vertebrates make up only about 5% of all animal species.
  • Invertebrates: Animals that do not have a backbone. This group is far more diverse and numerous, comprising about 95% of all animal species.
  • Exoskeleton: A hard outer covering found in some invertebrates (like insects and crabs) that provides support and protection. Because an exoskeleton does not grow, animals with one must molt (shed their old exoskeleton and grow a new, larger one) as they get bigger.
  • Endoskeleton Functions: Provides shape and support, protects soft internal organs, and works with muscles for movement.

Important Terms

  • Vertebrate: An animal that has a backbone (spine) inside its body.
  • Backbone (Spine): A column of small bones running along the back of an animal. It provides support for the body and protects the delicate spinal cord and nerves.
  • Endoskeleton: An internal skeleton, typically made of bone or cartilage, found inside an animal's body (characteristic of vertebrates).
  • Invertebrate: An animal that does not have a backbone.
  • Exoskeleton: A hard, protective outer covering that supports and protects the body of some invertebrates.
  • Molting: The process by which an animal sheds its old exoskeleton to grow a new, larger one, allowing for growth.

Detailed Notes

The Fundamental Divide: Backbone or Not?

The vast diversity of animal life can be initially categorized by a single, simple question: "Does it have a backbone?" This physical characteristic forms the basis for dividing the animal kingdom into two immense groups: vertebrates and invertebrates. Your own backbone, a bumpy line of bones running down your back, is the very structure that defines you as belonging to one of these groups.

Vertebrates: Animals with an Internal Skeleton

Vertebrates are animals distinguished by the presence of a backbone, also known as a spine, located inside their body. This backbone is comprised of small bones stacked on top of each other, forming a flexible column.

Functions of the Backbone:

  1. Support: It holds the body upright and provides the main structural support.
  2. Protection: It encases and protects the spinal cord, an essential bundle of nerves that transmits signals between the brain and the rest of the body.

The Endoskeleton: All vertebrates possess an endoskeleton, which is a skeleton located on the inside of their body. This internal framework is typically made of bones, though in some vertebrates (like sharks), it can be made of a more flexible material called cartilage.

Three Crucial Jobs of an Endoskeleton:

  1. Shape and Support: It gives the animal its characteristic shape and provides a rigid framework for the body.
  2. Organ Protection: It acts as a shield for soft internal organs. For instance, the skull protects the brain, and the rib cage safeguards the heart and lungs.
  3. Movement: It works in conjunction with muscles, providing points of attachment that allow the animal to move its limbs and body.

Vertebrate Abundance: Examples of vertebrates include familiar animals like dogs, eagles, frogs, sharks, and snakes—and humans! Despite their prominence in our daily lives and popular culture, vertebrates are actually a minority in the animal kingdom, making up only about 5% of all known animal species.

Invertebrates: Animals without a Backbone

In contrast to vertebrates, invertebrates are animals that do not possess a backbone. This group is incredibly diverse and represents the overwhelming majority of animal species on Earth.

Invertebrate Abundance: Invertebrates account for approximately 95% of all animal species. Within this group, insects alone constitute more than half of all known animal species, highlighting the sheer number of invertebrate forms. Examples of invertebrates include ants, butterflies, jellyfish, earthworms, snails, spiders, crabs, and octopuses.

Skeletal Structures in Invertebrates: Invertebrates exhibit a wider range of skeletal arrangements:

  • No Skeleton: Some invertebrates, such as earthworms and jellyfish, are entirely soft-bodied and lack any hard skeletal structure.
  • Exoskeleton: Many invertebrates, including insects, spiders, and crustaceans (like crabs), have a hard outer covering called an exoskeleton. This "suit of armor" provides both support and protection for the soft body parts inside.

The Process of Molting: A unique challenge for animals with an exoskeleton is that this hard covering does not grow. As the animal grows larger, it must shed its old, too-small exoskeleton and develop a new, larger one. This process is called molting. During molting, the animal is temporarily vulnerable as its new exoskeleton hardens.

Case Study: Turtles and Snails

Consider a turtle: it has a hard shell on the outside. Is it a vertebrate or an invertebrate? A turtle is a vertebrate. Its shell is actually made of modified bones that are fused to its backbone and part of its internal skeleton. So, beneath that hard outer layer, it still has a complete endoskeleton including a spine.

In contrast, a snail's shell is a completely external structure, separate from its soft body, and it has no backbone inside. Therefore, a snail is an invertebrate.

Further Exploration: Cartilage Skeletons

While most vertebrates have bony skeletons, some, like sharks and rays, have endoskeletons made entirely of cartilage, which is a flexible but strong material. Despite this difference, they are still classified as vertebrates because they possess an internal backbone (albeit cartilaginous) and an endoskeleton that performs the same functions as a bony one.

Review Questions

  1. What is the primary characteristic used to divide the animal kingdom into vertebrates and invertebrates? Why is this characteristic considered so fundamental?
  2. Describe the three essential functions of an endoskeleton in vertebrate animals. Provide an example for each function.
  3. Explain what an exoskeleton is and discuss why the process of molting is necessary for invertebrates that possess one.
  4. Give three examples of vertebrates and three examples of invertebrates. For each example, explain why it belongs to that specific group based on the definitions provided.
  5. A new species of animal is discovered that has a hard outer casing and grows by periodically shedding this casing. Could this animal be a vertebrate? Explain your reasoning, and then suggest what other information you would need to definitively classify it.
