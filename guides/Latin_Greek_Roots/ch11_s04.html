Study Guide: The CED / CEDE / CESS Word Family

Overview

This section explores the word family built on the Latin root ced/cede/cess (to go, to yield). This family demonstrates how a root with dual meaning — movement and concession — produces a rich variety of English words. The prefix attached to the root determines whether each word emphasizes going, yielding, or both.

Key Concepts

  • The Root ced / cede / cess: From Latin cedere (to go, to yield). Verb forms typically use cede or ceed; noun and adjective forms typically use cess.
  • Dual Meaning in Action: Some words emphasize movement (proceed, recede, exceed), others emphasize yielding (concede), and some blend both senses (succeed, access).
  • Present vs. Past Participle Stems: Verbs use the present stem (cede/ceed), while nouns and adjectives often use the past participle stem (cess): proceed/process, succeed/success.

Important Terms

Proceed: To move forward; to continue. Recede: To move back or withdraw; to become more distant. Concede: To admit that something is true; to surrender a point. Succeed: To achieve a goal; to come after another. Exceed: To go past a limit; to surpass. Access: The ability to approach or enter; a way in. Predecessor: A person who held a position before the current holder. Process: A series of steps or actions leading to a result.

Detailed Notes

Word-by-Word Analysis

  • Proceed

    • Etymology: pro- (forward) + ceed (to go)
    • Meaning: To move forward or continue with an action. Emphasizes forward movement.
    • Example: After the interruption, the speaker asked permission to proceed.

  • Recede

    • Etymology: re- (back) + cede (to go)
    • Meaning: To move back or withdraw; to become more distant. Often used for floodwaters, hairlines, or memories.
    • Example: The floodwaters began to recede after the storm passed.

  • Concede

    • Etymology: con- (completely) + cede (to yield)
    • Meaning: To admit that something is true or to surrender a point in an argument. Here the "yield" meaning dominates.
    • Example: After seeing the evidence, the candidate had to concede the election.

  • Succeed

    • Etymology: suc- (after, from below) + ceed (to go)
    • Meaning: To achieve a desired goal, or to come after another in a position or sequence. Literally, "to go after."
    • Example: She worked hard to succeed in her chosen field.

  • Exceed

    • Etymology: ex- (beyond) + ceed (to go)
    • Meaning: To go past a limit or boundary; to surpass what is expected.
    • Example: The results exceeded all expectations.

  • Access

    • Etymology: ac- (toward) + cess (a going)
    • Meaning: The ability to approach, enter, or use something; a way in. Uses the past participle cess form.
    • Example: Students have access to the library database from any computer.

  • Predecessor

    • Etymology: pre- (before) + de- (away) + cess (gone) + -or (one who)
    • Meaning: A person who held a position or office before the current holder. Literally, "one who went away before."
    • Example: The new principal honored the contributions of her predecessor.

  • Process

    • Etymology: pro- (forward) + cess (a going)
    • Meaning: A series of steps or actions leading to a result. Literally, "a going forward."
    • Example: The admissions process requires several forms and an interview.

Review Questions

  1. How do the words proceed and recede illustrate opposite directions of movement using the same root?
  2. Compare succeed and exceed. Both involve "going," but in what different ways? How do their prefixes create distinct meanings?
  3. Explain how concede demonstrates the "to yield" meaning of the root rather than the "to go" meaning.
  4. The words proceed (verb) and process (noun) share the same root and prefix. How does the shift from ceed to cess reflect the difference between a verb and a noun form?
  5. Break down the word predecessor into its component parts and explain how each part contributes to the overall meaning.
