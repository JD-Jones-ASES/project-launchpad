Study Guide: Chapter 16 — TEMPOR

Overview

Chapter 16 introduces the Latin root tempor / tempus, meaning "time." Students learn how the Romans' sense of time — the fleeting, precious, unstoppable passage of moments — gave English a family of words about duration, timing, improvisation, and even storms. The chapter shows how one root can describe everything from a musical beat to a violent thunderstorm.

Key Concepts

  • Root meaning: tempor/tempus = time (Latin tempus, genitive temporis)
  • English stem: tempor- (from the genitive form temporis)
  • Prefix patterns: con- (with/together), ex- (out of), tem- (base form)
  • Semantic range: These words describe how long things last (temporary, temporal), when they happen (contemporary), how prepared speakers are (extemporaneous), and how fast music moves (tempo)

Important Terms

  • temporary: lasting only for a limited time; not permanent (tempor + -ary)
  • contemporary: living or occurring at the same time; sharing the same era (con- + tempor + -ary)
  • temporal: relating to time; of the earthly, passing world (tempor + -al)
  • extemporaneous: done without preparation; spoken on the spot (ex- + tempor + -aneous)
  • tempo: the speed at which a piece of music is played (from Latin tempus via Italian)
  • tempest: a violent storm; originally any period of time or weather season (temp- + -est)

Detailed Notes

Time Ruling Roman Life

The Romans were acutely aware of time. The poet Virgil's famous line captures it: "Sed fugit interea, fugit irreparabile tempus" — "But meanwhile it flees: time flees irretrievably." From this consciousness came an entire vocabulary for measuring, experiencing, and describing the passage of time.

Temporary vs. Permanent

Roman soldiers built temporary camps (wooden fortifications they would abandon the next morning) as they marched. The word captures the Roman sense that some things are made for a moment and nothing more. The contrast with permanent structures — stone aqueducts, roads, temples — shows how deliberately the Romans thought about duration.

Contemporary: Sharing Time Together

A contemporary (con- with + tempor time) is someone who lives in the same time period as you. When historians call two events contemporary, they mean those events occurred in the same era. This word shows how the prefix con- (together, with) transforms the root from simple duration into shared experience.

Temporal: The Passing World

Roman philosophers distinguished between temporal pleasures (of this time-limited world) and eternal virtues. This philosophical contrast shaped Western thinking for centuries — the word temporal carries within it the idea that the earthly world is temporary, and that something more lasting might exist beyond it.

Extemporaneous: Out of Time to Prepare

An extemporaneous speech uses ex- (out of) + tempor (time) to describe speaking "out of" preparation time — on the spot, without a script. Public speakers who can think and speak extemporaneously are using the pressure of the moment itself as their guide.

Tempo: Time in Music

The musical term tempo entered English through Italian, which borrowed tempus directly from Latin. When a conductor adjusts the tempo, she controls how time flows through the music — fast or slow, urgent or gentle. Both temporary and tempo are, at heart, about measuring time.

Tempest: When Weather Defines a Season

The word tempest (violent storm) evolved from tempestas, which originally meant any period of time or season. Because bad weather was the most dramatic and memorable quality of any season, the word gradually narrowed until it came to mean only the most ferocious storms. This narrowing of meaning over time is called semantic narrowing — a common change in language history.

Review Questions

  1. How do temporary and contemporary both come from the same root but mean different things? What does the prefix change?
  2. Roman philosophers talked about temporal pleasures versus eternal virtues. What does this contrast tell us about how they thought about time?
  3. Why would a speech given extemporaneously be more challenging than a rehearsed speech? How does the root help explain what makes it difficult?
  4. The word tempest originally meant a period of time or season. How did it come to mean a violent storm? What does this tell us about how language changes?
  5. Tempo is a musical term borrowed from Italian, which borrowed it from Latin. How does controlling tempo mean controlling time itself in a piece of music?
