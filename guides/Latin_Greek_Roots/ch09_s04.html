Study Guide: Understanding the Word Family 'port'

Overview

This section explores a collection of English words derived from the fundamental Latin root "port," meaning "to carry." By understanding how this core meaning combines with various prefixes, you will gain insight into the nuanced definitions of words related to movement, transfer, communication, and entry.

Key Concepts

  • The Latin Root "port": The central idea behind all the words in this family is the concept of "carrying." Recognizing port as "to carry" is key to understanding the etymology and meaning of these words.
  • Impact of Prefixes: Prefixes like ex- (out), im- (in), trans- (across), de- (away), and re- (back) significantly alter the root's meaning, specifying the direction or manner of carrying, thus creating diverse vocabulary.
  • Etymological vs. Modern Meaning: While the literal "to carry" meaning often directly informs the modern definition, some words have evolved to more figurative or specialized senses, though the underlying concept of movement or transfer remains.
  • Vocabulary Building Strategy: Learning word families like port is an effective strategy for expanding vocabulary. By understanding a common root, you can often deduce the meaning of unfamiliar words and see connections between seemingly unrelated terms.

Important Terms

  • portable: (port-able) able to be carried — Easily carried or moved, especially by hand.
  • export: (ex-port) to carry out — To send goods or services to another country for sale.
  • import: (im-port) to carry in — To bring goods or services into a country from abroad for sale.
  • transport: (trans-port) to carry across — To take or carry people or goods from one place to another.
  • deport: (de-port) to carry away — To expel a foreign person from a country, typically for violating immigration laws or for committing a crime.
  • report: (re-port) to carry back — To give an account of something, often an event or situation; to convey information or news.
  • portal: (port-al) related to carrying — A doorway, gate, or entrance, especially a large and imposing one; a point of entry.
  • portfolio: (port-folio) carry + leaf — A flat, portable case for carrying loose papers or documents; a collection of creative work or investments presented together.

Detailed Notes

Let's explore each word in the "port" family, examining its etymology, literal meaning, and modern definition, along with an example to clarify its use.

  • portable

    • Etymology: port- (to carry) + -able (able to be)
    • Literal Meaning: "Able to be carried"
    • Modern Definition: Easily carried or moved, especially because of being of a light and manageable size.
    • Example: My new laptop is very portable, making it easy to work from anywhere.

  • export

    • Etymology: ex- (out) + port- (to carry)
    • Literal Meaning: "To carry out"
    • Modern Definition: To send goods or services produced in one country to another country for sale. This term is fundamental in economics and international trade.
    • Example: The country's economy relies heavily on its ability to export its agricultural products.

  • import

    • Etymology: im- (in) + port- (to carry)
    • Literal Meaning: "To carry in"
    • Modern Definition: To bring goods or services into a country from abroad for sale. This is the opposite process of exporting.
    • Example: Many electronic components used in manufacturing are imported from Asia.

  • transport

    • Etymology: trans- (across) + port- (to carry)
    • Literal Meaning: "To carry across"
    • Modern Definition: To take or carry people or goods from one place to another. This can refer to various methods like cars, trains, ships, or planes.
    • Example: The city is developing new public transport systems to reduce traffic congestion.

  • deport

    • Etymology: de- (away) + port- (to carry)
    • Literal Meaning: "To carry away"
    • Modern Definition: To expel a foreign person from a country, typically on grounds of illegal status or for committing a crime. This is a specific type of "carrying away" enforced by governmental authority.
    • Example: The government decided to deport the individual after their visa expired.

  • report

    • Etymology: re- (back) + port- (to carry)
    • Literal Meaning: "To carry back"
    • Modern Definition: To give an account of something observed, heard, or investigated; to convey information or news. The idea is to "carry back" information to someone else.
    • Example: The journalist had to report on the incident directly from the scene.

  • portal

    • Etymology: port- (to carry) + -al (related to)
    • Literal Meaning: "Related to carrying" (or, literally, "carrying place")
    • Modern Definition: A doorway, gate, or entrance, especially a large or grand one. Figuratively, it can also mean a point of entry or access to something, like a web portal which gives access to various online services. It is where one "carries through" or gains passage.
    • Example: The ancient temple featured an enormous stone portal leading to its main hall.

  • portfolio

    • Etymology: port- (to carry) + folio (leaf or sheet of paper)
    • Literal Meaning: "Carry leaf"
    • Modern Definition: A flat case used for carrying loose papers, documents, or drawings. It also refers to a collection of an artist's or writer's work, or a collection of financial investments, all conceptually "carried together."
    • Example: The art student carefully organized her best drawings in her portfolio for the college interview.

Review Questions

  1. Explain how the core meaning of the root "port" (to carry) is evident in both the literal and modern definitions of portable and transport.
  2. Compare and contrast export and import. How do their prefixes contribute to their opposite meanings, and why are both terms crucial in global economics?
  3. The word report literally means "to carry back." How does this literal meaning connect to its modern usage as giving an account of something or conveying information? Provide an example.
  4. Consider the words deport and portal. While deport refers to physically removing someone, portal refers to an entrance. How does the concept of "carrying" or "passage" link these two words despite their different applications?
  5. If you encountered a new word like "comportment," and you knew "com-" meant "with" or "together," how would your understanding of the "port" root help you infer its meaning? (Hint: Think about how one "carries" oneself.)
