Study Guide: The PORT Word Family

Overview

This section explores the word family built on the Latin root port (to carry). By examining how different prefixes and suffixes combine with this root, we see how a single meaning — "to carry" — generates a diverse family of English words used in everyday language, commerce, government, and professional life.

Key Concepts

  • The Root port: From Latin portare (to carry). This root remains consistent in form across all its English derivatives.
  • Prefix-Driven Meaning: Each word in the family takes its distinctive meaning from its prefix, which specifies the direction or manner of carrying.
  • Broad Application: Words from this family span domains including travel (transport), trade (export, import), government (deport), communication (report), architecture (portal), and professional life (portfolio).

Important Terms

Portable: Able to be carried or moved easily. Export: To send goods to another country for sale. Import: To bring goods into a country from abroad. Transport: To carry or move from one place to another. Deport: To expel someone from a country. Report: To give an account of something; to carry back information. Portal: A doorway or gate; an entry point. Portfolio: A case for carrying documents; a collection of work.

Detailed Notes

Word-by-Word Analysis

  • Portable

    • Etymology: port (to carry) + -able (able to be)
    • Meaning: Able to be carried or moved easily. Describes objects designed for convenient transport.
    • Example: A portable speaker can be taken anywhere.

  • Export

    • Etymology: ex- (out) + port (to carry)
    • Meaning: To send goods to another country for sale. The idea is carrying products out of one's own country.
    • Example: Brazil exports coffee to countries around the world.

  • Import

    • Etymology: im- (in) + port (to carry)
    • Meaning: To bring goods into a country from abroad. The idea is carrying products into one's country.
    • Example: The United States imports electronics from several Asian countries.

  • Transport

    • Etymology: trans- (across) + port (to carry)
    • Meaning: To carry or move from one place to another. Implies movement across a distance.
    • Example: Trains transport passengers and freight across the country.

  • Deport

    • Etymology: de- (away) + port (to carry)
    • Meaning: To expel someone from a country, typically by official order. Literally, to carry someone away.
    • Example: The government decided to deport the individual who had violated immigration law.

  • Report

    • Etymology: re- (back) + port (to carry)
    • Meaning: To give an account of something; to carry information back to someone. Can function as both a verb and a noun.
    • Example: The journalist filed a report from the scene of the event.

  • Portal

    • Etymology: port (to carry) + -al (related to)
    • Meaning: A doorway or gate; an entry point. Related to the idea of a place through which things or people are carried.
    • Example: The grand portal of the cathedral welcomed visitors from all over the world.

  • Portfolio

    • Etymology: port (to carry) + folio (leaf, page)
    • Meaning: A case for carrying documents or loose papers; also a collection of an artist's or professional's work samples.
    • Example: The architect presented her portfolio of completed building designs.

Review Questions

  1. How does the core meaning of port (to carry) manifest differently in export versus import? How do the prefixes create opposite meanings?
  2. Compare transport and deport. Both involve carrying, but how do their prefixes change the nature of the action?
  3. Explain how report embodies the "to carry" meaning of the root port, even though we usually think of reporting as a communication activity.
  4. How does the word portfolio combine the root port with another element to create a specialized meaning?
  5. Describe a real-world scenario that involves at least three words from the port word family.
