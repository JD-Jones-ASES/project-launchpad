Root Profile: leg, lect – The Roots of Reading, Choosing, and Law

Overview

This section explores the Latin root profile leg, lect (and its variant lig), which forms the basis for a significant portion of English vocabulary. We will examine its multiple core meanings—"to read," "to choose or gather," and "law"—and trace these meanings back to their distinct Latin origins, illustrating how they combine to create a rich array of derivative words in English.

Key Concepts

  • Polysemous Root: The root profile leg, lect is notable for having multiple distinct, yet sometimes related, core meanings: "to read," "to choose/gather," and "law." This versatility allows it to contribute to a wide range of English words.
  • Dual Latin Origins: While presented as a single root profile, its meanings stem from two distinct Latin sources: legere (meaning "to read," "to choose," "to gather") and lex, legis (meaning "law"). Understanding these separate origins helps clarify the various word families.
  • Variant Forms: Roots can appear in slightly altered forms within different words. For this root profile, leg is a common form, lect often appears as a past participle or in nouns/adjectives, and lig is a specific variant found in certain derivatives.
  • Foundation for English Vocabulary: This root profile is a fundamental building block, demonstrating how Latin roots contribute significantly to the complexity and richness of the English language.

Important Terms

  • Root: The basic form of a word from which other words are formed, typically carrying the primary meaning.
  • Derivative: A word formed from another word or root by adding prefixes, suffixes, or by slight changes to the root itself.
  • Latin legere: The Latin verb meaning "to read," "to choose," or "to gather." It is the direct source for the "read" and "choose/gather" meanings of our root profile.
  • Latin lectus: The past participle form of legere, often appearing in English derivatives related to "chosen" or "gathered."
  • Latin lex, legis: The Latin noun meaning "law." It is the direct source for the "law" meaning of our root profile.
  • Variant Form: A slightly altered spelling or pronunciation of a root that retains its core meaning but is adapted for specific derivative words (e.g., lig as a variant of leg).

Detailed Notes

The ROOT PROFILE: leg, lect

This root profile encompasses several forms and meanings, all originating from Latin. Understanding its components allows for a deeper grasp of numerous English words.

Root Forms

The primary forms observed in English derivatives are:

  • leg: This is a very common form, especially evident in words related to "law" or "reading."
  • lect: This form often derives from the Latin past participle lectus of legere. It is frequently seen in words related to "choosing" or "gathering."
  • lig: A variant form that appears in specific derivatives, often due to phonetic changes in its development through Latin and into English. For example, in words like diligent.

Core Meanings and Their Origins

The root profile leg, lect carries three primary meanings, each traceable to distinct Latin origins:

  1. To Read:

    • Origin: Directly from the Latin verb legere (to read).
    • Explanation: This meaning is straightforward and relates to the act of deciphering text or understanding written material.
    • Example Derivative:
      • legible: Derived from leg (read) + -ible (able to). Meaning "capable of being read; clear enough to be read." If handwriting is legible, it can be understood.

  2. To Choose, Gather:

    • Origin: Also from the Latin verb legere (to choose, to gather), illustrating its polysemous nature in Latin itself. The past participle form lectus is particularly influential here.
    • Explanation: This meaning involves the act of selecting items from a group, or bringing items together.
    • Example Derivatives:
      • select: From Latin se- (apart) + lect (chosen/gathered). Meaning "to choose (someone or something) from a group." When you select an item, you choose it out from others.
      • elect: From Latin e- (out) + lect (chosen). Meaning "to choose (someone) to hold public office or some other position by voting." To elect a leader is to choose them to represent a group.

  3. Law:

    • Origin: From the distinct Latin noun lex, legis (law). While sharing similar forms with legere, this is a separate etymological branch.
    • Explanation: This meaning relates to rules, statutes, and the legal system.
    • Example Derivative:
      • legal: Derived from leg (law) + -al (pertaining to). Meaning "of, relating to, or concerned with the law." Something that is legal conforms to the rules set by the law.

Understanding Variant Forms: lig

The variant form lig highlights how roots can evolve. In words like diligent, the root lig comes from a combination of the prefix dis- (apart) and the root legere (to choose/gather). A diligent person carefully "chooses apart" or attends to details, working thoroughly and industriously. This shows how phonetic changes over centuries led to leg becoming lig in certain contexts.

The Interconnectedness of Meanings

While legere (read/choose/gather) and lex (law) are distinct in their Latin origins, their similar forms leg and lect in English derivatives can sometimes create interesting connections or overlaps in meaning. For instance, a "lecture" (from lectus) is a speech read aloud or gathered information presented. "Legislation" (from leg in lex) involves the "reading" and "gathering" of laws. Understanding both specific origins and shared forms is key to mastering this root profile.
