Word Family: psych, path, hydr, and therm

Overview

This word family explores ten English words derived from the Greek roots psych (mind, soul), path (feeling, suffering), hydr (water), and therm (heat). These roots form the backbone of scientific, medical, and psychological vocabulary.

Word Breakdown

Psychology

Components: psych (mind) + -logy (study of)

Definition: The scientific study of the mind and behavior. Psychology examines how people think, feel, and act.

Psyche

Components: psych (mind, soul)

Definition: The human soul, mind, or spirit. In Greek mythology, Psyche was a mortal whose beauty rivaled Aphrodite's, symbolizing the soul's journey.

Empathy

Components: en-/em- (in) + path (feeling)

Definition: The ability to understand and share another's feelings. Empathy means feeling 'into' another person's experience.

Sympathy

Components: syn-/sym- (together, with) + path (feeling)

Definition: Feelings of pity and concern for another's suffering. Sympathy means feeling 'with' someone in their distress.

Pathology

Components: path (suffering, disease) + -logy (study of)

Definition: The study of the causes and effects of diseases. Pathology examines what causes suffering and illness.

Hydrogen

Components: hydr (water) + genes (born, producing)

Definition: A chemical element that produces water when burned. Literally 'water-producer,' named by Lavoisier in 1783.

Hydrant

Components: hydr (water) + -ant

Definition: An outdoor water pipe with a valve for firefighting. A hydrant provides access to the water supply.

Thermal

Components: therm (heat) + -al (relating to)

Definition: Relating to heat or warmth. Thermal energy, thermal underwear, and thermal springs all involve heat.

Thermostat

Components: therm (heat) + statos (standing, fixed)

Definition: A device that regulates temperature automatically. A thermostat keeps heat at a fixed ('standing') level.

Thermometer

Components: therm (heat) + metron (measure)

Definition: An instrument for measuring temperature. Literally 'heat-measurer.'

Patterns and Observations

Understanding how prefixes and suffixes modify the base root is the key to unlocking unfamiliar vocabulary. Notice how:

  • Prefixes change the direction or scope of the root meaning (e.g., em- means 'in,' sym- means 'together,' de- means 'away from').
  • Suffixes change the part of speech or specify the quality (e.g., -logy names a study, -al makes an adjective, -ant names a thing).
  • The same root can produce nouns, verbs, and adjectives depending on the suffix attached.

Review Questions

  2. Which prefixes in this word family change the root meaning most dramatically? Give examples.
  3. Can you think of additional English words not listed here that might share the same root? What do they mean?
  4. How does understanding the root and affix structure help you remember these words' meanings?
  5. Write a sentence using at least two words from this family that demonstrates their meanings.
