Understanding the Latin Root 'leg/lect': Gathering, Reading, and Choosing

Overview

This section explores the highly versatile Latin root legere, which originally meant "to gather." Over time, its meaning expanded to encompass "to read" (gathering words) and "to choose or select" (gathering people). This triple meaning explains the presence of leg/lect in a wide array of English words, including those related to literacy, elections, law, and even the fascinating evolution of the word "legend."

Key Concepts

  • The Versatile Root legere: The Latin verb legere is one of the most prolific roots in English, contributing to numerous words across different semantic fields.
  • Original Meaning: "To Gather": The fundamental sense of legere is akin to physically collecting items, like olives from the ground.
  • Expanded Meanings: "To Read" and "To Choose/Select": From gathering objects, the meaning evolved to gathering words (reading) and gathering people (choosing). This "triple meaning" is central to understanding its derivatives.
  • Influence on English Vocabulary: The three core meanings directly explain words like legible (readable), lecture (a reading aloud), select (to choose apart), and elect (to choose out).
  • Connection to Law (lex): The related Latin noun lex (law), meaning "chosen rules" or "gathered statutes," gives us words like legal.
  • Prefixes Modifying Meaning: Prefixes like se- (apart), e- (out), nec- (not), and col-/con- (together) combine with legere to create specific meanings, such as select, elect, neglect, and collect.
  • Evolution of "Legend": The word legend originated from Latin legenda, meaning "things to be read." Its meaning gradually shifted from important stories that needed to be preserved to extraordinary tales, often too grand to be entirely true.

Important Terms

  • legere: The Latin verb that forms the root leg/lect. Its core meanings are "to gather," "to read," and "to choose/select."
  • legible: (adj.) Clear enough to be read; able to be "gathered" or read easily.
  • lecture: (n.) A talk or discourse given to an audience, especially for instruction; historically, a "reading aloud" where knowledge is "gathered" and presented.
  • select: (v.) To carefully choose from a group; from Latin se- (apart) + legere (to choose) meaning "to choose apart."
  • elect: (v.) To choose (someone) to hold public office or some other position by voting; from Latin e- (out) + legere (to choose) meaning "to choose out."
  • lex: (n.) The Latin noun for "law," derived from the idea of "chosen rules" or "gathered statutes."
  • legal: (adj.) Relating to the law.
  • neglect: (v.) To fail to care for properly or attend to; from Latin nec- (not) + legere (to choose), meaning "not to choose" or "to fail to pick up."
  • collect: (v.) To gather together; from Latin col-/con- (together) + legere (to gather).
  • legenda: (n.) Latin for "things to be read," the origin of the English word "legend."
  • legend: (n.) An traditional story sometimes popularly regarded as historical but unauthenticated; a story so grand it seems almost too wonderful to be true; an extremely famous or notorious person.

Detailed Notes

The Root legere: A Foundation of Meaning

The Latin verb legere stands as one of the most adaptable and widely influential roots in the Latin language, leaving an indelible mark on English vocabulary. Its evolution in meaning is key to understanding a vast family of words.

  1. Original Meaning: "To Gather":

    • Initially, legere meant something concrete and physical: "to gather."
    • Imagine a Roman farmer meticulously gathering olives from the ground, one by one. This image captures the root's foundational sense of collecting individual items to form a whole.

  2. First Expansion: From Objects to Words ("To Read"):

    • The meaning gracefully expanded from gathering physical objects to gathering abstract ones, specifically words and meaning.
    • To read is, in essence, to gather meaning from the individual letters and words arranged on a page, piecing them together to comprehend a message.

  3. Second Expansion: From Words to People ("To Choose/Select"):

    • The expansion continued, moving from words to people.
    • To choose or select implies gathering the best or most suitable individuals from a larger group for a specific purpose or task. This involves picking out certain ones while leaving others.

The Triple Meaning in Action: Gathering, Reading, and Choosing

This triple meaning—gathering, reading, and choosing—serves as a powerful mnemonic for understanding the diverse applications of the leg/lect root in English.

  • Legible: Directly derived from the "reading" aspect. If handwriting is legible, it means it can be gathered (understood) or read easily without difficulty.
  • Lecture: This word also stems from the "reading" meaning. A lecture was historically a "reading aloud." A professor lectures by gathering knowledge, organizing it, and then orally presenting or "reading" it to students.
  • Select: This word beautifully illustrates the "choosing" meaning, enhanced by a prefix. Select combines the prefix se-, meaning "apart," with legere (to choose). Thus, to select something is literally to "choose apart"—to pick one item or person distinctly from many.
  • Elect: Similar to select, elect uses the prefix e- (or ex-), meaning "out," combined with legere (to choose). When voters elect a leader, they "choose out" the person they want to represent them from a field of candidates.

Further Reach: Law and Negligence

The influence of legere extends even beyond these direct applications into concepts like law and diligence.

  • Lex and Legal: The related Latin noun lex refers to "law." This connection arises from the idea of laws being "chosen rules" or "gathered statutes"—principles and regulations that society has collectively selected and compiled to govern itself. From lex we get the English adjective legal, meaning "relating to law."
  • Neglect: This word demonstrates how a negative prefix can invert the core meaning. Neglect comes from nec- (a variant of non-, meaning "not") combined with legere (to choose). To neglect something is to "not choose" it, to fail to pick it up, to disregard it, or to not attend to it properly.
  • Collect: This word brings us back to the most fundamental meaning of "to gather." Collect is formed from the prefix col- (a variant of con-, meaning "together") and legere (to gather). Therefore, to collect is to "gather together" various items or elements.

The Curious Case of 'Legend'

The word legend has a particularly interesting etymological journey that perfectly encapsulates the "reading" aspect of legere.

  • Original Latin legenda: In Latin, legenda literally meant "things to be read." These were stories, accounts, or narratives considered so important, significant, or instructive that they had to be gathered and read (often aloud) and subsequently passed on through generations.
  • Evolution of Meaning: Over centuries, the meaning of legend subtly shifted. It moved from simply being "a story worth reading" to signifying a story that is so grand, so extraordinary, or so captivating that it seems almost too wonderful or improbable to be entirely true.
  • Modern Usage: When someone refers to an athlete as a "legend" today, it highlights their exceptional status—their story is so remarkable that it is undoubtedly "worth talking about" and reading about for years to come.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the three primary meanings of the Latin root legere and provide an English example for each, demonstrating how the meaning is reflected.
  2. How does the concept of "gathering" apply to both the act of reading a book and the act of selecting candidates for an election?
  3. Trace the etymological path from the Latin lex to the English word legal. What does this connection reveal about the historical understanding of law?
  4. Describe the transformation of the word "legend" from its original Latin meaning ("things to be read") to its current usage. What does this shift tell us about how language and cultural values influence word meanings?
  5. Choose two words derived from legere (e.g., select, elect, neglect, collect) and explain how their specific prefixes contribute to their distinct meanings, using the core sense of legere.
