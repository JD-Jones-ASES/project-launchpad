Study Guide: Understanding the Latin Root ced/cede/cess

Overview

This section introduces the Latin root ced, cede, or cess, which carries the core meanings of "to go" or "to yield." Originating from the Latin verb cedere, understanding this root is fundamental to deciphering the meaning of a large number of English words derived from its various forms. Mastering this root enhances vocabulary, improves reading comprehension, and provides a powerful tool for linguistic analysis.

Key Concepts

  • Core Meaning: The primary meanings of the root ced/cede/cess are "to go" or "to yield." It also encompasses "to withdraw" from its original Latin form.
  • Latin Origin: The root stems from the Latin verb cedere, which means "to go, to yield, to withdraw."
  • Variant Forms: The root appears in English with three main variant forms:
    • ced: Often found within compound words.
    • cede: Typically used as a verb ending.
    • cess: Derived from the past participle cessus of the Latin verb cedere, often appearing in nouns and adjectives.
  • Word Building: Combining this root with various prefixes (like pro-, re-, con-, ad-) creates a wide array of English words with specific meanings related to movement or concession.

Important Terms

  • Root: The basic element of a word that carries the primary meaning, to which prefixes and suffixes can be added.
  • ced/cede/cess: The specific Latin root discussed in this section, meaning "to go" or "to yield."
  • cedere: The original Latin verb from which the root is derived, meaning "to go, to yield, to withdraw."
  • cessus: The past participle of cedere, which gives rise to the cess variant of the root in English.
  • Variant Forms: Different spellings or manifestations of the same root that arise due to historical linguistic changes or grammatical functions.
  • Derivatives: Words formed by adding prefixes, suffixes, or other roots to a base root.
  • Compound Words: Words formed from two or more existing words or roots.

Detailed Notes

The Latin Root ced/cede/cess: An Introduction

The root ced/cede/cess is a highly productive element in English vocabulary, forming the basis of hundreds of words. Its deep roots in Latin make it a valuable tool for understanding the historical development of the English language.

Origin and Core Meanings: The journey of ced/cede/cess into English begins with the Latin verb cedere. This verb had a nuanced meaning, primarily encompassing:

  1. "To go": Implies movement, progression, or retreat.
  2. "To yield": Suggests giving way, surrendering, or conceding.
  3. "To withdraw": A specific type of "going" or "yielding," often implying retreat or retirement.

These core meanings are crucial because they inform the meanings of all the English words derived from this root. When you encounter a word containing ced, cede, or cess, think about whether it relates to movement (going) or giving way (yielding).

Understanding the Variant Forms

The existence of three main variant forms (ced, cede, cess) can initially seem confusing, but each form has a specific context of use, making them predictable once understood.

  1. ced (in some compounds):

    • This form is typically found embedded within compound words, especially when the root is not at the very end of the word or when it combines with another element to form the stem.
    • Example: In words like antecedent, intercede, or secede, the ced form is maintained before a suffix or when combined with another prefix.
      • antecedent (ante- "before" + ced "go"): something that goes before.
      • intercede (inter- "between" + ced "go"): to go between, to mediate.

  2. cede (verb endings):

    • This is the most direct descendant of the Latin cedere in terms of its appearance as a verb in English. When the root forms the main part of a verb, it often takes the cede spelling.
    • Example: concede, recede, accede.
      • concede (con- "thoroughly" + cede "yield"): to admit, to grant, to yield.
      • recede (re- "back" + cede "go"): to go back, to withdraw.
      • accede (ad- "to" + cede "go/yield"): to agree to a demand, request, or treaty.

  3. cess (from past participle cessus):

    • This variant is particularly important because it derives from the past participle cessus of the Latin verb cedere. Past participles in Latin often formed the basis for nouns and adjectives, and this tradition continues in English.
    • The cess form is very common in nouns and adjectives, particularly when expressing the act or result of going or yielding.
    • Example: access, process, success, recession.
      • access (ad- "to" + cess "gone"): the means or opportunity to approach or enter. Here, cess refers to having "gone to."
      • process (pro- "forward" + cess "gone"): a series of actions or steps taken to achieve an end. Here, cess refers to "going forward."
      • success (sub- "under/up to" + cess "gone"): the accomplishment of an aim or purpose. Implies "having gone up to" a desired outcome.
      • recession (re- "back" + cess "gone"): a period of temporary economic decline, where the economy has "gone back."

Example Derivatives and Their Meanings

Let's explore some key English words formed from this root, demonstrating how prefixes combine with ced/cede/cess to create rich and specific meanings:

  • proceed (pro- "forward" + ced "to go"): To move forward; to begin or continue a course of action.
  • recede (re- "back" + cede "to go"): To go or move back or further away from a previous position; to gradually diminish.
  • concede (con- "thoroughly" + cede "to yield"): To admit that something is true or valid after first denying or resisting it; to surrender or grant.
  • access (ad- "to" + cess "gone"): The means or opportunity to approach or enter a place; the right or opportunity to use or benefit from something.
  • excess (ex- "out" + cess "gone"): An amount of something that is more than necessary, permitted, or desirable; an "over-going."
  • procession (pro- "forward" + cess "gone"): A number of people or vehicles moving forward in an orderly way, especially as part of a ceremony or demonstration.
  • secede (se- "apart" + cede "to go/yield"): To formally withdraw from a federation or body, especially a political one.
  • intercede (inter- "between" + cede "to go"): To intervene on behalf of another.

By breaking down these words into their prefix and root components, you can often deduce their meanings even if you haven't encountered them before.
