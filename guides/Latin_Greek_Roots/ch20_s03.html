Study Guide: The Root Story of Credibility

Overview

This section explores the deep roots of the English word family cred—related to belief and trust—tracing them back to the essential Roman value of fides (faith or trust) and the Latin verb credere (to believe or trust). It demonstrates how this single root has evolved to influence a wide spectrum of words in English, spanning everyday communication, religion, finance, education, and the legal system, all centered on the fundamental concept of trustworthiness.

Key Concepts

  • The Roman Foundation of Trust: The ancient Roman civilization placed immense value on trust, embodied by the goddess Fides and the Latin verb credere. This societal cornerstone formed the basis for many English words related to belief and reliability.
  • The Spectrum of Belief: The cred root gives rise to words like credible, incredible, and credulous, illustrating a continuum from deserving belief, to being beyond belief, to being too willing to believe.
  • Broad Applications of Credere: The concept of trust originating from credere permeates various aspects of life, including:
    • Everyday Language: Credibility in stories or statements.
    • Finance: Giving "credit" implies trust in future payment.
    • Academia: Receiving "credit" for completed work.
    • Religion: A "creed" is a statement of belief.
    • Professional Life: "Credentials" prove trustworthiness and qualification.
    • Social Standing: To "accredit" bestows trust, while to "discredit" destroys it, reflecting the Roman understanding of social reputation.
  • Trust in the Legal System: The legal system heavily relies on trust. Witnesses swear oaths (jur) with the expectation that courts will believe (cred) their testimony, highlighting the interconnectedness of these root concepts.

Important Terms

  • Fides: The Roman goddess of Faith or Trust; also the Latin word for faith or trust, a core Roman value.
  • Credere: The Latin verb meaning "to believe" or "to trust," the root of the English cred word family.
  • Credible: (adj.) Worthy of belief or trust; able to be believed.
  • Incredible: (adj.) Originally, "not believable"; today, often used loosely to mean "amazing" or "astonishing."
  • Credulous: (adj.) Too willing to believe; easily convinced without sufficient evidence.
  • Credit: (n.) Belief or trust that someone will pay money they owe; recognition for having done something (e.g., an assignment); honor or approval.
  • Creed: (n.) A set of beliefs or principles, especially a statement of religious faith. From Latin credo ("I believe").
  • Credential: (n.) A document or quality that proves a person is trustworthy, qualified, or authorized (e.g., academic degrees, professional licenses).
  • Accredit: (v.) To officially recognize someone or something (like a school) as being worthy of trust or meeting certain standards.
  • Discredit: (v.) To cause people to stop believing someone or something; to harm the reputation of.
  • Perjury: (n.) The offense of willfully telling an untruth in a court after having taken an oath or affirmation. (Related to jur, law/oath, and destroying cred).

Detailed Notes

I. The Roman Foundation of Trust: The Root Story

  • Central Roman Value: Roman civilization was fundamentally built on trust. A person's word, reputation, and fides (faith/trust) were paramount.
  • Goddess Fides: Romans even had a goddess, Fides, whose temple on the Capitoline Hill symbolized the sacredness of trust. Oaths were sworn by touching her sacred right hand.
  • The Verb Credere: The Latin verb credere, meaning "to believe" or "to trust," directly captured this essential Roman value. It is the linguistic origin for a vast family of English words.

II. The Credere Word Family: Everyday Applications

This group of words illustrates a spectrum of how we relate to belief and trust:

  • Credible:
    • Meaning: Worthy of belief; can be trusted.
    • Example: A credible witness tells a trustworthy story.
  • Incredible:
    • Original Meaning: Not believable; impossible to trust.
    • Modern Loose Meaning: Amazing or astonishing (often losing its original negative connotation of "not believable").
  • Credulous:
    • Meaning: Too willing to believe something, often without enough evidence; gullible.
    • Example: A credulous person might fall for a hoax.
  • Spectrum: These three words demonstrate a range from "deserving trust" (credible), to "beyond trust" (incredible), to "giving trust too easily" (credulous).

III. The Meaning of "Credit": Academic and Financial

The word "credit" itself showcases the root credere in practical, distinct contexts:

  • Academic Credit: When a teacher gives you credit for an assignment, they are literally stating, "I believe you did this work." This is trust in your effort and honesty.
  • Financial Credit: When a store or bank gives you credit, it means "I trust you to pay later." This is trust in your future ability and willingness to fulfill a financial obligation.
  • Shared Root: Both meanings stem from the core idea of belief and trust.

IV. Credere in Professional, Religious, and Social Contexts

The root's influence extends deeply into specialized areas:

  • Creed:
    • Origin: From Latin credo, meaning "I believe," which was the first word of ancient statements of faith (e.g., the Nicene Creed).
    • Meaning: A formal statement of religious or other belief.
  • Credential:
    • Meaning: A document (like a diploma, license, or certificate) that serves as proof that someone is trustworthy, qualified, or competent. It gives others a reason to believe in your abilities.
  • Accredit:
    • Meaning: To officially declare an institution (like a school or hospital) worthy of trust and meeting specific standards. This grants public confidence.
  • Discredit:
    • Meaning: To destroy the trust others have in a person, idea, or institution; to harm their reputation or credibility.
    • Roman Relevance: The Romans would have understood the severity of discrediting someone, as losing one's fides often meant losing one's place and standing in society.

V. Connecting Trust to Law: Cred and Jus/Jur

  • Interdependence: The legal system fundamentally relies on trust. Justice cannot function without a degree of belief in testimony and evidence.
  • Oaths and Testimony: Witnesses in court swear oaths (using the root jur, related to law and oaths) because the court must believe (cred) their testimony for legal processes to proceed fairly.
  • Contrasting Concepts:
    • A credible witness provides trustworthy testimony that the court can believe.
    • Someone who commits perjury (willfully giving false testimony under oath) actively destroys that trust, undermining the very foundation of the legal system. This demonstrates the critical importance of cred in the administration of jus/jur.

Review Questions

  1. How did the Roman concept of "Fides" influence the development of the Latin verb credere and its subsequent English descendants?
  2. Explain the spectrum of belief represented by "credible," "incredible," and "credulous." Provide a distinct example for each to illustrate its meaning.
  3. Discuss how the root credere forms the basis for both academic and financial meanings of "credit." What is the underlying shared principle that connects these seemingly different applications?
  4. Identify and describe at least three words from the credere family that relate to professional life, religion, or social reputation. How do they reflect the core meaning of trust or belief within their specific contexts?
  5. The "rootconnection" section links cred with jus/jur (law/oath). Explain this connection, using examples like witness testimony and perjury to illustrate the critical role of trust in the legal system.
