Study Guide: Chapter 7 — COGN/GNOSC (To Know and Recognize)

Overview

Chapter 7 introduces two roots that both mean "to know": the Latin cogn (from cognoscere) and the Greek gnosc / gnos (from gignoskein). This is the first chapter in the book to feature both a Latin and a Greek root for the same concept, demonstrating how English inherited a "double vocabulary" of knowing words from both ancient languages. Latin-derived words tend to appear in everyday contexts; Greek-derived words tend to appear in science and medicine.

Key Concepts

  • Root meaning and origin: "cogn" from Latin cognoscere (to get to know, recognize); "gnosc/gnos" from Greek gignoskein (to know, perceive); both share a common Proto-Indo-European ancestor
  • How the root appears: "cogn" in everyday words (cognition, recognize, cognizant, cognitive, incognito); "gnos" in scientific/medical words (diagnose, prognosis)
  • Important morpheme patterns: Prefixes again drive distinct meanings — re- (again), in- (not), dia- (through), pro- (before/ahead)

Important Terms

  • cognition: the mental process of knowing, thinking, and understanding (cogn + -ition)
  • recognize: to know again; to identify something previously known (re- + cogn + -ize)
  • cognizant: aware; having knowledge of something (cogn + -izant)
  • incognito: with one's identity hidden (in- + cognit + -o)
  • cognitive: relating to the process of thinking and knowing (cogn + -itive)
  • diagnose: to identify a disease by examining symptoms thoroughly (dia- + gnos)
  • prognosis: a prediction about the likely course of a disease (pro- + gnos + -is)

Detailed Notes

A Remarkable Double Inheritance

This chapter tells a story of two languages meeting in English. The Latin verb cognoscere and the Greek verb gignoskein both meant "to know." Deep in history, they share a common ancestor — an ancient Proto-Indo-European root meaning "to know." Both words traveled separately through centuries of history before arriving in English, giving us two distinct families of knowledge words.

The Latin Side: Everyday Knowing

Latin-derived cogn words show up in ordinary thinking and awareness:

  • cognition: the overall mental process of knowing — used in psychology and education
  • recognize: to know again — reconnecting a present perception to a past memory
  • cognizant: aware; in a state of knowing — "I am cognizant of the risks"
  • cognitive: relating to thinking processes — "cognitive skills," "cognitive science"
  • incognito: the prefix in- (not) + cognit (known) = unknown; with identity hidden. The word entered English through Italian, which preserved the full Latin form incognitus.

The Greek Side: Scientific Knowing

Greek-derived gnos words appear in medicine and science:

  • diagnose: dia- (through) + gnos (know) — to know through; a doctor reasons through all symptoms to name a disease
  • prognosis: pro- (before, ahead) + gnos (know) — knowing ahead; a prediction about how illness will develop

The key difference between these two words is time:

  • Diagnosis = What is wrong NOW (looking through present evidence)
  • Prognosis = What will happen NEXT (looking ahead)

The Perception-Knowledge Chain

Chapter 7 completes a three-step chain of human understanding introduced across Chapters 5–7:

  1. VIS/VID (Ch. 5): We see the world
  2. SENS/SENT (Ch. 6): We feel and perceive through all our senses
  3. COGN/GNOSC (Ch. 7): We know — we process what we perceived into understanding

Seeing evidence (vis), feeling something is wrong (sens), and knowing a diagnosis (cogn/gnos) — this is the chain from raw experience to knowledge.

Latin vs. Greek in English

This chapter offers your first clear example of a pattern that continues throughout Part VI: Latin roots tend to give English everyday vocabulary (words for daily thinking, law, and social life), while Greek roots tend to give English scientific and technical vocabulary (words for medicine, science, and philosophy). Chapter 7's cogn vs. gnos split is a perfect first illustration of this pattern.
