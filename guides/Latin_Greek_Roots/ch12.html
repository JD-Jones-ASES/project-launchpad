Study Guide: Chapter 12 — FER / LAT

Overview

Chapter 12 introduces the Latin root fer / lat, from ferre (to carry) and its past participle latus. This unusual pair — where the present and past forms come from completely different ancient words — gives English two families of words that look different but share the same core meaning: to carry, bear, or bring.

Key Concepts

  • Root meaning: fer/lat = to carry, to bear, to bring
  • Suppletive pair: ferre (present) and latus (past participle) come from two originally unrelated Latin words — linguists call this a suppletive pair
  • Two form families: fer words (transfer, prefer, fertile, different, suffer) and lat words (translate, relate, legislate)
  • Prefix patterns: trans- (across), pre- (before), dif-/dis- (apart), suf-/sub- (under), re- (back)

Important Terms

  • transfer: to move something from one place or person to another (trans- + fer)
  • prefer: to choose one thing over another; to carry it before others (pre- + fer)
  • fertile: able to bear growth; capable of producing abundant crops or offspring (fer + -tile)
  • different: not the same; distinct (dif- + fer + -ent: carried apart)
  • suffer: to bear pain or hardship; to carry a burden (suf-/sub- + fer)
  • translate: to carry meaning across from one language to another (trans- + lat)
  • relate: to tell or narrate; to show a connection between things (re- + lat)
  • legislate: to make or enact laws; to carry law (leg- + lat + -ate)

Detailed Notes

The Strange Verb Ferre

Latin's verb ferre (to carry) was irregular — its past participle was latus, from a completely different ancient root. This is called a suppletive pair: two unrelated forms that merged to serve as one verb. English has similar pairs: the past tense of go is went (originally from the verb wend), and good/better come from different roots entirely.

The Fer Family

Words using the present stem fer:

  • Transfer (trans- across): move something across
  • Prefer (pre- before): carry one choice before others
  • Fertile (able to bear): land or beings that can produce growth
  • Different (dif- apart): things that have been carried apart from each other
  • Suffer (sub- under): carry a burden beneath yourself

The Lat Family

Words using the past participle stem lat:

  • Translate (trans- across): carry meaning across languages
  • Relate (re- back): carry back; connect or narrate
  • Legislate (leg- law): carry law into effect

The Surprising Word: Different

The word different hides its root well. Breaking it down: dif- (dis- = apart) + fer (carry) + -ent (adjective ending). Things that differ have been "carried apart" — separated. This is a good example of why studying roots reveals hidden structure in ordinary words.

Fer and Lat Both Mean Across

Notice that both transfer (fer) and translate (lat) use the prefix trans- (across). This is possible because both roots carry the same core meaning. Transfer carries objects across space; translate carries meaning across languages. Two different word forms, one root concept.

Review Questions

  1. What is a suppletive pair, and why does Latin's ferre/latus qualify as one? Can you think of another example in English?
  2. How does the word different contain the root fer? What does it literally mean?
  3. Compare transfer and translate. Both use the prefix trans- (across). How are they similar and different?
  4. Why might the Romans have used the idea of "carrying" to describe both physical movement (transfer) and communication (relate)?
  5. The word fertile comes from fer meaning "to bear." How does this meaning connect to what fertile land or a fertile imagination actually does?
