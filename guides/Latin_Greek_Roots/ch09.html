Study Guide: Chapter 9 — PORT

Overview

Chapter 9 introduces the Latin root port, from portare, meaning "to carry." Students learn how the Roman Empire's vast road network made the concept of carrying central to Roman life — and how this root spread into dozens of English words about movement, trade, and communication.

Key Concepts

  • Root meaning: port = to carry (Latin portare)
  • How the root appears: port shows up consistently across its English derivatives (portable, transport, export, import, deport, report, portal, portfolio)
  • Prefix patterns: Each derivative is shaped by a prefix that modifies the direction or nature of the carrying: ex- (out), im- (in), trans- (across), re- (back), de- (away)

Important Terms

  • portable: able to be carried or moved easily (port + -able)
  • export: to send goods out to another country for sale (ex- + port)
  • import: to bring goods into a country from abroad (im- + port)
  • transport: to carry or move from one place to another (trans- + port)
  • deport: to expel someone from a country; to carry away (de- + port)
  • report: to give an account of something; to carry back information (re- + port)
  • portal: a doorway or gate; an entry point (port + -al)
  • portfolio: a case for carrying documents; a collection of work (port + folium, leaf/sheet)

Detailed Notes

The Roman Road Network and Portare

At the height of the Roman Empire, more than 250,000 miles of roads connected three continents. Along these roads, goods were transported (carried across), armies marched, and merchants traded. The Latin verb portare — to carry — sat at the heart of this empire of movement.

How the Prefix Changes the Meaning

The root port stays constant while prefixes change the direction of carrying:

  • ex-port: carry out to other countries
  • im-port: carry in from other countries
  • trans-port: carry across distances
  • de-port: carry away (in disgrace or expulsion)
  • re-port: carry back information to an audience

From Carrying to Communicating

The word report shows how a physical root can extend into abstract meaning. A reporter does not carry objects — but they do carry information, bringing news back from the field. This metaphorical extension is common: concrete physical actions (carrying) become abstract ones (communicating).

Port, Portal, and Portfolio

A portal is a gateway — a place where movement passes through, related to the idea of carrying people and goods. A portfolio originally meant a physical folder for carrying loose sheets of paper (folium = leaf/page). Today it means a collection of work, but the image of "carrying your papers" remains.

Roman Harbors: Portus

Roman harbors were called portus because they were the points where cargo was carried on and off ships. This same word became the English word port (a harbor), showing how strongly the idea of carrying was tied to the physical infrastructure of the empire.

Review Questions

  1. How does knowing the prefix help you figure out the meaning of a 'port' word? Give two examples.
  2. Why do you think a harbor was called a portus in Latin? What does carrying have to do with a harbor?
  3. The word report comes from re- (back) + port (carry). How does a journalist "carry back" information?
  4. Can you think of any modern word or technology that uses the root port or involves carrying that the Romans would have recognized as related to portare?
  5. Compare export and import. What does the change of prefix (ex- vs. im-) tell us about the direction goods are moving?
