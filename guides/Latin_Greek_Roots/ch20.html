Study Guide: Chapter 20 — CRED (To Believe, To Trust)

Overview

Chapter 20 introduces the Latin root cred, from credere meaning "to believe" or "to trust." This root reflects a core Roman value: that civilization depends on trust. From religion (creed) to finance (credit) to education (credential), the root cred touches almost every area of life. Students will explore how a spectrum of words — credible, incredible, credulous — all grow from the same root but carry very different meanings.

Key Concepts

  • Root cred — from Latin credere (to believe, to trust, to entrust); the foundation of English words about belief and trust
  • Prefix patterns: in- (not) + cred = incredible; dis- (away) + cred = discredit; ac- (toward) + cred = accredit
  • Suffix patterns: -ible (able to be), -ulous (tending to), -ential (relating to), -it (past participle)
  • Thematic connection: cred (to believe) connects to jus/jur (law) — legal systems depend on trustworthy witnesses
  • The spectrum: credible (worthy of trust) → incredible (beyond trust) → credulous (trusting too easily)

Important Terms

  • credible: cred + -ible — able to be believed; convincing
  • credential: cred + -ential — a document proving qualifications or trustworthiness
  • credit: from credere — trust given for future payment; recognition for work
  • creed: from credo ("I believe") — a statement of beliefs or principles
  • discredit: dis- + cred + -it — to damage the reputation or trust in someone
  • accredit: ac- + cred + -it — to officially recognize as meeting standards
  • incredible: in- + cred + -ible — originally not believable; now commonly used to mean extraordinary
  • credulous: cred + -ulous — too willing to believe; gullible

Detailed Notes

Roman Trust and the Goddess Fides

Roman civilization ran on trust (fides). A senator's word was his bond. The goddess Fides had her own temple on the Capitoline Hill, and Romans swore oaths by touching her sacred right hand. Into this culture of trust, the verb credere — to believe, to trust, to entrust — became one of the most important words in the Latin language.

The Credible Spectrum

Chapter 20 shows how one root produces a spectrum of words based on how trust is applied. Credible describes someone or something worthy of trust. Incredible originally meant "not believable" (in- = not), but today we often use it loosely to mean something so amazing it strains belief. Credulous describes a person who gives trust too easily — the opposite of healthy skepticism. Recognizing this spectrum helps students understand how the same root can produce words with very different implications.

Credo and Religious Language

The word credo ("I believe") opens several ancient statements of faith, including the Nicene Creed. From credo comes creed — any formal statement of principles. This shows how the root traveled from Roman civic life into religious practice, carrying the same core meaning of declared belief.

Credit in Finance and Education

The word credit appears in two very different contexts, but both trace back to credere. Financial credit means "I trust you to pay later." Academic credit means "I believe you completed this work." In both cases, the party giving credit is extending trust based on past behavior or reputation — exactly the Roman meaning of credere.

Discredit and Accredit

The prefixes dis- and ac- (ad-) point in opposite directions. To accredit (ac- = toward) moves toward trust — officially declaring something meets standards. To discredit (dis- = away) moves away from trust — destroying someone's reputation. These two words are near-antonyms built from the same root.

Review Questions

  1. How do credible, incredible, and credulous relate to each other? Create a sentence using each word correctly.
  2. Why does the word credit appear in both financial and academic contexts? What do both uses have in common?
  3. Explain the difference between accredit and discredit using their prefixes.
  4. What is a creed, and what Latin word does it come from? Can you think of a non-religious creed — a personal statement of what you believe?
  5. How does the root cred connect to the legal system concepts from Chapter 19 (jus/jur)? What role does trust play in a courtroom?
