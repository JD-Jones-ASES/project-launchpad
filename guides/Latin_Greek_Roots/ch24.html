Study Guide: Chapter 24 — PSYCH / PATH / HYDR / THERM (Mind; Feeling; Water; Heat)

Overview

Chapter 24 is the final chapter of Greek roots, introducing four roots at once: psych (mind, soul), path (feeling, suffering), hydr (water), and therm (heat). Each root names an invisible or fundamental force of nature — the workings of the mind, the depth of human emotion, the flow of water, and the power of heat. Together, they demonstrate how extensively Greek shaped English scientific vocabulary, from psychology and empathy to hydrogen and thermometers.

Key Concepts

  • Root psych — from Greek psyche (soul, mind, breath of life); appears in psychology, psyche
  • Root path — from Greek pathos (feeling, suffering); appears in empathy, sympathy, apathy, pathology
  • Root hydr — from Greek hydor (water); appears in hydrogen, hydrant, dehydrate
  • Root therm — from Greek therme (heat); appears in thermal, thermostat, thermometer
  • Prefix patterns with path: em- (in) + path = empathy; sym- (with) + path = sympathy; a- (without) + path = apathy
  • Greek vs. Latin parallels: path vs. sens/sent (feeling); hydr vs. aqua (water)

Important Terms

  • psychology: psych (mind) + -logy (study of) — the scientific study of the mind and behavior
  • psyche: from Greek psyche — the human soul, mind, or spirit
  • empathy: em- (in) + path (feeling) — the ability to feel into another person's experience
  • sympathy: sym- (with, together) + path (feeling) — feelings of pity and concern for another's suffering
  • apathy: a- (without) + path (feeling) — a complete absence of feeling or interest; indifference
  • pathology: path (suffering, disease) + -logy (study of) — the study of the causes and effects of diseases
  • hydrogen: hydr (water) + genes (producing) — an element that produces water when burned
  • hydrant: hydr (water) + -ant — an outdoor water pipe for firefighting
  • thermal: therm (heat) + -al — relating to heat or warmth
  • thermostat: therm (heat) + statos (standing) — a device that regulates temperature automatically
  • thermometer: therm (heat) + metron (measure) — an instrument for measuring temperature

Detailed Notes

Psyche: From Breath to Mind

The Greek psyche began as something physical: breath. When you breathe, you are alive; when you stop, you are not. Over time, psyche came to mean the animating principle — the soul — and then the inner mental and emotional life. The myth of Psyche (a mortal woman loved by the god Eros) gave the word poetic dimension. Today, psychology (psych + -logy) is the scientific study of the mind, while your psyche is your deepest inner self.

Pathos: The Spectrum of Feeling

Aristotle used pathos as one of the three rhetorical appeals — a speaker must engage the audience's emotions (pathos), establish personal credibility (ethos), and use logical argument (logos). This ancient meaning of "stirring feelings" lives on in the English word family. Empathy (em-, in + path) means placing yourself inside another's emotional experience. Sympathy (sym-, with + path) means feeling alongside someone. Apathy (a-, without + path) means feeling nothing at all — the complete absence of emotional engagement. Pathology (path + -logy) takes the meaning of "suffering" into medicine: the study of disease.

Hydr: Water in Science

The Greek hydor (water) appears whenever English needs to talk about water in a scientific or technical context. A hydrant is a water outlet (hydr + -ant). Hydrogen is the element that produces water when burned (hydr + genes, meaning "born" or "producing"). To dehydrate is to remove water from something (de-, away from + hydr). Compare this with Latin aqua, which gave everyday words: aquarium, aquatic, aqueduct. The pattern holds: Greek for science, Latin for daily life.

Therm: Heat and Measurement

The root therm (from therme, heat) appears in three interconnected scientific instruments and concepts. A thermometer measures heat (therm + metron, measure). A thermostat keeps heat standing at a set level (therm + statos, standing or fixed) — statos connects to the idea of stability or stasis. Thermal means relating to heat — thermal clothing traps body heat; thermal energy is heat energy. These words show how one root covers both the measurement of heat and its practical control.

Four Roots, One Theme

Chapter 24 groups these four roots because all four name invisible forces that shape human life. The mind (psych) is invisible but drives all behavior. Feeling (path) is invisible but defines human relationships. Water (hydr) is formless but essential to life. Heat (therm) is invisible but measurable and controllable. Together, these four roots gave English much of its vocabulary for describing the natural world — from the life of the mind to the temperature of the air.

Review Questions

  1. How did the original meaning of psyche (breath) evolve into the modern meaning (mind)? What does this tell us about how words change over time?
  2. Explain the difference between empathy, sympathy, and apathy using their prefixes.
  3. Why is hydrogen named after the Greek word for water? What happens chemically that justifies this name?
  4. What is the difference between a thermometer and a thermostat? How do their second roots (-metron and -statos) explain this difference?
  5. Aristotle used pathos as a rhetorical tool. How does a modern speaker or writer use pathos to persuade an audience? Give an example.
