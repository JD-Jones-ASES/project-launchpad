Study Guide: The AQUA / AQU Word Family

Overview

This section explores the word family built on the Latin root aqua/aqu (water). This compact but important family includes words from science, engineering, commerce, and everyday life. Several words in this family combine aqua with other Latin roots, demonstrating how compound etymology works.

Key Concepts

  • The Root aqua / aqu: From Latin aqua (water). This root anchors all words in the family to the concept of water.
  • Compound Roots: Many words combine aqua with another root: duct (to lead) in aqueduct, fer (to carry) in aquifer, marina (sea) in aquamarine.
  • Suffix Patterns: Suffixes define each word's function: -tic (relating to), -rium (place for), -ine (resembling), -ous (characterized by).

Important Terms

Aquatic: Relating to water; living or growing in water. Aqueduct: A channel or bridge-like structure for carrying water across a distance. Aquifer: An underground layer of rock or sand that holds and carries water. Aquarium: A glass tank or building where fish and water creatures are kept and displayed. Aquamarine: A blue-green color or gemstone resembling the color of sea water. Subaqueous: Existing, formed, or taking place under water.

Detailed Notes

Word-by-Word Analysis

  • Aquatic

    • Etymology: aqua (water) + -tic (relating to)
    • Meaning: Relating to water; living or growing in water. Used for plants, animals, and activities.
    • Example: Frogs are aquatic animals that spend much of their lives in water.

  • Aqueduct

    • Etymology: aque (water) + duct (to lead)
    • Meaning: A channel or bridge-like structure for carrying water across a distance. The Romans were famous for building these.
    • Example: The ancient Roman aqueduct still stands after two thousand years.

  • Aquifer

    • Etymology: aqui (water) + fer (to carry)
    • Meaning: An underground layer of rock or sand that holds and carries water. A critical source of groundwater.
    • Example: The Ogallala Aquifer provides water for much of the central United States.

  • Aquarium

    • Etymology: aqua (water) + -rium (place for)
    • Meaning: A glass tank or building where fish and water creatures are kept and displayed.
    • Example: The children loved watching the colorful fish at the aquarium.

  • Aquamarine

    • Etymology: aqua (water) + marine (of the sea)
    • Meaning: A blue-green color or gemstone resembling the color of sea water. Combines two water-related concepts.
    • Example: She wore a beautiful aquamarine pendant that matched the color of the ocean.

  • Subaqueous

    • Etymology: sub- (under) + aque (water) + -ous (characterized by)
    • Meaning: Existing, formed, or taking place under water. A technical term used in geology and marine science.
    • Example: The geologist studied the subaqueous rock formations on the lake floor.

Review Questions

  1. How does the root aqua connect all six words in this family? What shared concept do they express?
  2. Explain how aqueduct and aquifer each combine aqua with a different second root. What does each second root contribute?
  3. Compare aquatic and subaqueous. Both relate to water, but how does the prefix sub- change the meaning?
  4. How does the word aquamarine combine two water-related concepts? What does each element contribute to the color description?
  5. Why might the word aquarium use the suffix -rium (place for)? What other English words use this same suffix pattern?
