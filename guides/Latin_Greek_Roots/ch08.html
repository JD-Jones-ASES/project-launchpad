Study Guide: Chapter 8 — LEG/LECT (To Read, Choose, and Gather)

Overview

Chapter 8 introduces the Latin root leg / lect, which carries the remarkable triple meaning of "to gather," "to read," and "to choose." It comes from Latin legere (to read, choose, gather) and its past participle lectus. A related Latin noun, lex (law), connects this root to the idea of "chosen rules." This chapter shows how one root's expanding meaning — from picking up olives to reading books to electing representatives — created one of the most versatile word families in English.

Key Concepts

  • Root meaning and origin: "leg" comes from Latin legere (to read, choose, gather); "lect" comes from the past participle lectus; "lig" appears as a variant in some derivatives
  • How the root appears: As "leg" in words like legible and legal; as "lect" in words like lecture, select, elect, neglect, collect; in the noun lex (law) underlying legal
  • Important morpheme patterns: Prefixes direct the gathering/choosing action — se- (apart), e- (out), col-/con- (together), neg- (not)

Important Terms

  • legible: able to be read; clear enough to gather meaning from (leg + -ible)
  • lecture: a talk or presentation; originally a reading aloud (lect + -ure)
  • select: to choose carefully from a group (se- + lect)
  • legal: relating to the law (leg [law] + -al)
  • elect: to choose by voting (e- + lect)
  • neglect: to fail to care for; to not pick up or attend to (neg- + lect)
  • collect: to bring together; to gather items into a group (col- + lect)
  • legend: a traditional story; originally Latin for "things to be read" (leg + -end)

Detailed Notes

The Root's Triple Meaning

The original Latin verb legere meant "to gather" — picture a Roman farmer picking olives from the ground one by one. From gathering objects, the meaning expanded metaphorically to gathering words: to read is to gather meaning from a page. And from gathering words, it expanded further to gathering people: to choose or select is to pick the best candidates from a group. This three-step semantic journey explains why one root generates such different English words.

Word Family Analysis

Prefixes direct the triple meaning in specific ways:

"Choosing" words:

  • select (se- = apart): choose apart from others; single out carefully
  • elect (e- = out): choose out by voting; pick someone to represent the group
  • neglect (neg- = not): NOT choose; fail to pick up or attend to

"Gathering" words:

  • collect (col-/con- = together): gather together into one place
  • lecture (-ure = act of): the act of gathering and presenting knowledge aloud

"Reading" words:

  • legible: able to be read (the handwriting can be gathered by the eye)
  • legend: things to be read — stories gathered and passed down

The Legal Connection: Lex and Leg

The chapter introduces a related Latin noun lex, legis (law), which connects to legere through the idea of "chosen rules" — laws are the rules that a society has selected and gathered as binding. This is why the root leg appears in:

  • legal: relating to law (from lex)
  • illegal: not legal (not according to chosen rules)

Etymology Note: Legend

Latin legenda meant "things to be read." In the Middle Ages, collections of saints' lives and heroic tales were called legenda because they were stories meant to be read aloud to communities. Over centuries, "a story worth reading" gradually became "a story so extraordinary it seems almost too wonderful to be true." When someone today calls an athlete a "legend," the root simply says they are someone worth talking about — worth being read about.

Completing the Communication Cycle: SCRIB + LEG

Chapter 8's root forms the natural partner of Chapter 4's scrib/script:

  • scrib/script (Ch. 4): the scribe writes — information is fixed onto the page
  • leg/lect (Ch. 8): the reader gathers — meaning is retrieved from the page

Together these two roots capture the complete cycle of written communication: creating text and receiving it.

Review Questions

  1. What are the three related meanings of the Latin root legere, and how does one meaning grow out of the other? (Hint: start with "gathering.")
  2. Compare elect and select by analyzing their prefixes. In what situations would you use each word?
  3. How does the word neglect show what happens when the "choosing/gathering" meaning is negated?
  4. Explain how the word legend shifted from its original Latin meaning ("things to be read") to its modern meaning ("an extraordinary story").
  5. The chapter calls scrib/script (Ch. 4) and leg/lect (Ch. 8) "two sides of the same coin." What is that coin, and what makes these two roots natural partners?
