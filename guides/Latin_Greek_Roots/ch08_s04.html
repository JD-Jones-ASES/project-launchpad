Study Guide: Exploring the leg/lect Word Family

Overview

This section delves into the fascinating Latin root leg/lect, which carries meanings related to "reading," "gathering," "choosing," and even "law." By examining a family of words derived from this root, we uncover how prefixes and suffixes modify its core meaning, creating a rich vocabulary. The section also highlights the crucial connection between reading and writing, positioning leg/lect as an integral part of the cycle of written communication.

Key Concepts

  • The Root leg/lect: This versatile Latin root primarily means "to read," "to gather," or "to choose." Its form changes slightly between leg and lect depending on the word's construction.
  • Multiple Meanings: While "read" and "gather/choose" are central, the root also extends to mean "law" in specific contexts, demonstrating how roots can have distinct but often related semantic fields.
  • Impact of Prefixes: Prefixes like se-, e-, neg-, and col-/con- dramatically alter the meaning of the base root, creating words with opposing or nuanced definitions (e.g., select vs. collect).
  • Suffixes for Word Type: Suffixes like -ible, -ure, -al, and -end transform the root into different parts of speech (adjectives, nouns) and specify the nature of the concept (e.g., "able to be," "act of," "relating to," "thing to be").
  • Reading and Writing Connection: The section emphasizes the complementary relationship between the root leg/lect (reading/gathering words) and scrib/script (writing words), illustrating them as two fundamental components of communication.

Important Terms

  • legible: (adj.) Able to be read; clear enough to read.
  • lecture: (n.) A talk or presentation on a subject given to an audience; (v.) to deliver such a talk.
  • select: (v.) To choose carefully from a group.
  • legal: (adj.) Relating to the law.
  • elect: (v.) To choose by voting; to pick out.
  • neglect: (v.) To fail to care for properly; to not pay attention to or attend to.
  • collect: (v.) To bring together; to gather items into a group.
  • legend: (n.) A traditional story often considered historical but unverified; an extremely famous or notorious person; something "to be read."
  • leg/lect: (root) Latin root meaning "to read," "to gather," "to choose," or "law."
  • scrib/script: (root, from Chapter 4) Latin root meaning "to write."

Detailed Notes

The Latin root leg (which also appears as lect in various forms) is a powerful building block in English vocabulary. Its primary meanings revolve around "to read," "to gather," and "to choose." Understanding these core meanings is key to unlocking the definitions of many complex words.

Let's break down the words presented in this family:

  • legible: This word directly embodies the "read" meaning. It combines leg (read) with the suffix -ible (able to be). Therefore, legible means "able to be read," describing writing that is clear and decipherable. For example, a doctor's handwriting is often notoriously illegible (not legible).

  • lecture: Here, the lect form of the root is used, meaning "read" or "gather." The suffix -ure signifies "act of." Historically, a lecture was often a formal "reading aloud" of a text or prepared material. Today, it refers to "a talk or presentation on a subject," still rooted in the idea of presenting information that the audience "gathers" or "takes in."

  • select: This word focuses on the "choose" aspect of the root. The prefix se- means "apart" or "away." So, select literally means "to choose apart" from a group, implying a careful choice or discernment. When you select an item, you pick it out from many options.

  • legal: This word introduces a slightly different, though related, nuance of the leg root, meaning "law." The suffix -al means "relating to." Thus, legal refers to anything "relating to the law." This root connection for "law" often comes from the idea of "things read aloud" or "things gathered" as codified rules and regulations.

  • elect: Similar to select, elect uses the lect root to mean "choose." The prefix e- (a variant of ex-) means "out" or "from." So, elect means "to choose out," specifically by voting or by making a formal choice among candidates or options. We elect representatives to government.

  • neglect: This word combines the negative prefix neg-/nec- (meaning "not") with lect (choose, gather). To neglect means "to fail to care for" something, essentially "not choosing" to attend to it or "not gathering" the necessary effort or attention. This can apply to objects, duties, or even people.

  • collect: The prefix col-/con- means "together," and lect means "gather." Therefore, collect means "to bring together" or "to gather items into a group." When you collect stamps, you bring them together into an album. This is the opposite action of select (choosing apart) or elect (choosing out).

  • legend: This word stems from leg (read) and the suffix -end (thing to be). A legend is "a traditional story" or "an extraordinary person" – something "to be read" or passed down through generations. The idea of something being worthy of being read or remembered forms the core of its meaning.

Root Connection: leg/lect (read, gather) vs. scrib/script (write)

It's insightful to compare the leg/lect root with scrib/script (from Chapter 4), which means "to write." These two roots represent two sides of the same coin in the realm of written communication:

  • scrib/script describes the act of producing written words – the scribe writes the words onto the page.
  • leg/lect describes the act of interpreting and absorbing those words – the reader "gathers" the meaning from the page.

Together, these roots encapsulate the entire cycle of written communication, from creation to reception. They highlight that for communication to occur, words must first be written, and then they must be read (or gathered) by an audience.
