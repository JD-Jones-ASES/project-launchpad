Study Guide: Word Family leg/lect and the Cycle of Written Communication

Overview

This section introduces the Latin root leg/lect, which signifies "to read," "to choose," or "to gather." It establishes a crucial connection between leg/lect and the root scrib/script (from Chapter 4, meaning "to write"), highlighting how these two roots represent the complementary acts that complete the full cycle of written communication: writing and reading.

Key Concepts

  • The Root leg/lect: This Latin root primarily conveys the meanings "to read" and "to choose." It also stems from the Latin verb legere, which means "to gather." These meanings are interconnected, as reading involves gathering meaning from text, and choosing involves gathering selected items.
  • The Root scrib/script: Introduced in Chapter 4, this Latin root means "to write." It is fundamental to understanding the act of putting thoughts onto a page.
  • The Complementary Relationship: leg/lect and scrib/script are presented as "two sides of the same coin." scrib/script represents the act of creating written content, while leg/lect represents the act of interpreting or receiving that content.
  • The Cycle of Written Communication: Together, these roots encapsulate the entire process by which ideas are conveyed through written language. A writer uses scrib/script to produce words, and a reader uses leg/lect to comprehend them, thus completing the communication loop.

Important Terms

  • leg/lect: A Latin root meaning "to read," "to choose," or "to gather." It refers to the action of understanding or selecting information from written material.
  • legere: The Latin verb from which leg/lect is derived, specifically meaning "to gather." This original meaning helps to link "to read" (gathering information) and "to choose" (gathering specific items).
  • scrib/script: A Latin root (introduced in Chapter 4) meaning "to write." It refers to the act of forming letters, words, or texts.
  • Root: The fundamental part of a word that carries its primary meaning. Understanding roots helps in deciphering the meanings of many related words.
  • Word Family: A group of words that share a common root and often have related meanings, even if their spellings vary slightly.

Detailed Notes

The brief section on wordfamily focuses on the foundational Latin root leg/lect and its significant relationship to scrib/script. While concise, it offers a powerful insight into the mechanics of language and communication.

Understanding the Root leg/lect

The primary root explored is leg/lect. The text explicitly states its core meanings: "to read" and "to choose." A crucial piece of information is the direct connection to the Latin verb legere, meaning "to gather." This sheds light on how "to read" and "to choose" are related:

  • To Read: When you read, you are gathering information, meaning, and understanding from the written words on a page. The act of decoding symbols to construct comprehension is a form of gathering.
  • To Choose: Similarly, when you choose something, you are gathering specific items or options from a larger set. You are selecting or picking out what is desired.

Therefore, the underlying concept of "gathering" provides a unifying explanation for the seemingly distinct meanings of "to read" and "to choose" within the leg/lect root family.

The Connection to scrib/script

The study guide draws a direct parallel between leg/lect and the root scrib/script, which was introduced in Chapter 4 and means "to write." This comparison is not arbitrary; it is central to understanding the full scope of written communication.

  • scrib/script (to write): This root represents the encoding process. It is the act of a "scribe" or writer who physically forms letters, words, and sentences to convey a message. It is the creation of the written artifact.
  • leg/lect (to read, choose, gather): This root represents the decoding process. It is the act of the "reader" who interprets, understands, and "gathers" the meaning that the writer encoded.

The Entire Cycle of Written Communication

The most profound takeaway from this section is the concept of leg/lect and scrib/script as "two sides of the same coin" that capture "the entire cycle of written communication."

  1. Creation (Writing): The cycle begins with the writer using the principles represented by scrib/script to articulate thoughts and ideas into a written form. This involves drafting, composing, and inscribing.
  2. Reception (Reading): The cycle completes when a reader engages with the written text, employing the skills encapsulated by leg/lect. The reader "gathers" the message, interpreting the symbols and understanding the communication intended by the writer.

Without one, the other is incomplete. A written text ("script") is inert without a reader to "gather" its meaning. Conversely, the act of "reading" is meaningless without something written to be read. This symbiotic relationship underscores the fundamental nature of these two roots in human intellectual and cultural development. Understanding these roots not only helps in vocabulary building but also provides insight into the very structure of how we communicate knowledge across time and space.
