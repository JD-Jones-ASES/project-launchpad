Study Guide: The TEMPOR / TEMPUS Word Family

Overview

This section explores the word family built on the Latin root tempor/tempus (time). This family demonstrates how a single concept — time — branches into words about duration, cultural eras, spontaneity, music, and even violent weather. Each word connects back to the fundamental idea of time, modified by prefixes and suffixes.

Key Concepts

  • The Root tempor / tempus: From Latin tempus (time). This root anchors all words in the family to the concept of time.
  • Prefix Patterns: Prefixes specify the relationship to time: con- (with, same), ex- (out of).
  • Extended Meaning: The connection between tempus (time) and tempest (storm) reflects the ancient association between time and weather — a "time of bad weather."

Important Terms

Temporary: Lasting only for a limited time; not permanent. Contemporary: Living or occurring at the same time; modern. Temporal: Relating to time; worldly rather than spiritual. Extemporaneous: Done without preparation; spoken or performed on the spot. Tempo: The speed at which a piece of music is played. Tempest: A violent storm; a period of turbulent weather.

Detailed Notes

Word-by-Word Analysis

  • Temporary

    • Etymology: tempor (time) + -ary (relating to)
    • Meaning: Lasting only for a limited time; not permanent. Describes things with a defined duration.
    • Example: The company hired temporary workers for the holiday season.

  • Contemporary

    • Etymology: con- (with, together) + tempor (time) + -ary (relating to)
    • Meaning: Living or occurring at the same time as something else; also used to mean modern or current. Literally, "with the time."
    • Example: Shakespeare and Cervantes were contemporary writers, both living in the late 1500s.

  • Temporal

    • Etymology: tempor (time) + -al (of, relating to)
    • Meaning: Relating to time, especially as opposed to eternity. In religious contexts, temporal means worldly or earthly (concerns of this time), as contrasted with spiritual or eternal matters.
    • Example: The medieval Church distinguished between temporal power (kings) and spiritual power (the Pope).

  • Extemporaneous

    • Etymology: ex- (out of) + tempor (time) + -aneous (characterized by)
    • Meaning: Done without preparation; performed spontaneously. Literally, "out of the time" — done in the moment without advance planning.
    • Example: The senator gave an extemporaneous speech that impressed the audience with its wit and clarity.

  • Tempo

    • Etymology: tempo (time) — borrowed directly from Italian, from Latin tempus
    • Meaning: The speed at which a piece of music is played. Also used figuratively for the pace of any activity.
    • Example: The conductor increased the tempo during the finale.

  • Tempest

    • Etymology: temp (time) + -est — from Latin tempestas (a time, a season, a storm)
    • Meaning: A violent storm. The word evolved from meaning "a period of time" to "a period of weather" to specifically "a period of bad weather" — a storm.
    • Example: The sailors struggled to navigate through the fierce tempest.

Review Questions

  1. How does the root tempor connect the words temporary, contemporary, and temporal? What distinguishes each word's meaning?
  2. Explain the etymology of extemporaneous. How does "out of the time" lead to the meaning "without preparation"?
  3. Compare temporary and contemporary. How do the prefixes (or lack thereof) create different relationships to time?
  4. How did the word tempest evolve from a root meaning "time" to a word meaning "storm"? What intermediate meanings link these concepts?
  5. In what ways does the word temporal bridge the gap between time and worldly concerns? Why might "of time" and "worldly" be connected ideas?
