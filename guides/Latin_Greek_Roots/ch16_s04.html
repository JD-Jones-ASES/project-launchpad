Study Guide: The tempor Word Family

Overview

This section explores a family of English words derived from the Latin root tempor, meaning "time." Understanding this root helps clarify the meanings of words related to duration, simultaneity, worldly matters, lack of preparation, musical speed, and even periods of turbulent weather, all stemming from the fundamental concept of time.

Key Concepts

  • The Root tempor Means "Time": The core of this word family is the Latin root tempor, which signifies "time."
  • Modification by Prefixes and Suffixes: Various prefixes (like con-, ex-) and suffixes (-ary, -al, -aneous) attach to tempor to create new words with specific shades of meaning related to time.
  • Duration and Impermanence: Some words, like "temporary," emphasize a limited or non-permanent duration.
  • Simultaneity and Modernity: "Contemporary" highlights occurrences or existences that happen at the same time, or that belong to the current era.
  • Worldly vs. Spiritual: "Temporal" distinguishes between matters relating to earthly time and those that are spiritual or eternal.
  • Lack of Preparation: "Extemporaneous" denotes actions, particularly speech or performance, done without prior planning, literally "out of time."
  • Speed and Rhythm: "Tempo" directly refers to the speed or rate, especially in music, reflecting how time is measured in rhythm.
  • Period of Weather: "Tempest" connects to "time" by referring to a specific "time" or period characterized by violent, turbulent weather.

Important Terms

  • temporary (adj.): Lasting only for a limited time; not permanent.
    • Etymology: tempor- (time) + -ary (related to).
  • contemporary (adj.): Living or occurring at the same time; modern or of the present time.
    • Etymology: con- (with) + tempor- (time) + -ary (related to).
  • temporal (adj.): Relating to time; worldly or secular rather than spiritual or eternal.
    • Etymology: tempor- (time) + -al (of/relating to).
  • extemporaneous (adj.): Done or spoken without preparation; impromptu; on the spot.
    • Etymology: ex- (out of) + tempor- (time) + -aneous (related to).
  • tempo (n.): The speed at which a piece of music is played; the rate or pace of any activity.
    • Etymology: Directly from Latin tempus (time).
  • tempest (n.): A violent storm; a period of turbulent weather or emotion.
    • Etymology: Related to Latin tempus (time), referring to a "time of weather."

Detailed Notes

The Latin root tempor, meaning "time," provides the foundation for a variety of English words, each connecting to the concept of time in a distinct way. Understanding these connections can greatly enhance vocabulary and comprehension.

  1. temporary

    • Formed by combining tempor (time) with the suffix -ary (related to), "temporary" describes something that is related to a specific time or a limited duration. It implies impermanence, suggesting that something will only last for a period and then cease or change.
    • Example: A "temporary job" is one that lasts for a limited period, not indefinitely.

  2. contemporary

    • This word combines the prefix con- (meaning "with" or "together"), tempor (time), and the suffix -ary (related to). Thus, "contemporary" literally means "with the time." It can describe things or people existing at the same time as each other, or it can refer to what is modern or current – belonging to the present time.
    • Example: "Contemporary art" refers to art created in the present period, and "contemporary issues" are problems relevant to our current time.

  3. temporal

    • Using the root tempor (time) and the suffix -al (of or relating to), "temporal" primarily means "relating to time." It is often used to distinguish between things that exist within the bounds of time (and are thus worldly or earthly) versus things that are eternal or spiritual.
    • Example: "Temporal concerns" are worries about everyday life and the material world, as opposed to spiritual matters.

  4. extemporaneous

    • This word is built from the prefix ex- (meaning "out of"), tempor (time), and the suffix -aneous (related to). "Extemporaneous" literally means "out of the time" or "without time" for preparation. It refers to something done or said on the spur of the moment, without prior planning or rehearsal.
    • Example: An "extemporaneous speech" is one delivered without notes or detailed preparation, relying on impromptu thought.

  5. tempo

    • "Tempo" comes directly from the Latin word tempus, which means "time." In English, it is most commonly used in music to describe the speed or pace at which a piece should be played. By extension, it can also refer to the speed of any activity.
    • Example: The "fast tempo" of the song made it difficult to dance to, or "the tempo of modern life is very quick."

  6. tempest

    • While not as immediately obvious, "tempest" also traces its origin back to tempus (time), specifically referring to a "time" or period of weather. It denotes a violent storm, characterized by strong winds, rain, or hail, or metaphorically, a period of emotional or social turbulence.
    • Example: The ship was battered by a fierce "tempest" at sea, or "a tempest of emotions swept over him."

Review Questions

  1. What is the common Latin root shared by all the words in this family, and what is its fundamental meaning? How does this meaning tie into the diverse words derived from it?
  2. Explain the difference between "temporary" and "temporal." Provide an example sentence for each to illustrate their distinct uses.
  3. Imagine you are a speaker at an event and are suddenly asked to give an unplanned speech. Which word from this family best describes such a speech, and why is that particular word appropriate given its etymology?
  4. The word "contemporary" has two primary meanings related to time. Describe these two meanings and provide an example of how each might be used in a sentence.
  5. How do "tempo" and "tempest" relate to the root tempor (time), even though their modern meanings might not seem to be about "time" in the same way as "temporary" or "temporal"? Discuss the nuances in their connections.
