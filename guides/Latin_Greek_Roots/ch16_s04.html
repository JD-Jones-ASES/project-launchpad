Chapter 16, Section 4: The tempor- Word Family

Overview

This section explores the "word family" stemming from the Latin root tempor-. Understanding this root is crucial for deciphering the meanings of numerous English words related to time, duration, and pace. By examining how this core meaning is modified through prefixes and suffixes, you will gain a deeper insight into vocabulary and word formation.

Key Concepts

  • Word Roots: The fundamental building blocks of words, often derived from ancient languages like Latin or Greek. Understanding roots helps in inferring the meanings of unfamiliar words.
  • The tempor- Root: This Latin root primarily means "time" or "season." It is the base for many English words that describe aspects of time, such as duration, timing, or simultaneity.
  • Derivation through Affixes: Prefixes (added to the beginning of a root) and suffixes (added to the end) modify the core meaning of a root word, creating new words with specific nuances. For example, a suffix might turn a root into a noun, adjective, or verb, while prefixes can add meanings like "before," "after," or "together."
  • Semantic Nuances: Words derived from the same root can have distinct meanings, often reflecting different facets or applications of the root's core idea. For the tempor- family, this includes concepts of limited time, simultaneous time, lack of preparation time, or the speed of time.

Important Terms

  • tempor- (root): (Latin) Meaning "time," "season," or "period." It forms the core of many English words related to duration, timing, and the nature of existence within time.
  • Temporal:
    1. Relating to worldly affairs as opposed to spiritual affairs; secular.
    2. Relating to time or limited by time, as opposed to eternal or spiritual. Example: The temporal concerns of daily life often overshadow eternal spiritual questions.
  • Temporary: Lasting for only a limited period of time; not permanent. Example: The power outage was temporary, and electricity was restored within an hour.
  • Contemporary:
    1. Existing, occurring, or living at the same time.
    2. A person living at the same time as another. Example: Shakespeare was a contemporary of Queen Elizabeth I. Contemporary art often reflects modern societal trends.
  • Extemporaneous: Spoken or done without preparation; impromptu. Example: The politician delivered an impressive extemporaneous speech, even though he hadn't prepared notes.
  • Tempo:
    1. The speed at which a passage of music is or should be played.
    2. The rate or pace of any activity or movement. Example: The tempo of the song gradually increased, making it more exciting. The fast tempo of city life can be overwhelming.
  • Temporize: Avoid making a decision or committing oneself in order to gain time; postpone a decision. Example: The committee decided to temporize on the new policy, hoping for more data to emerge.
  • Temporality: The state of existing within or being subject to time. Often used in philosophical contexts. Example: Philosophers often debate the nature of human temporality and our experience of past, present, and future.

Detailed Notes

The Latin root tempor- (from tempus, temporis) is a prolific source of English vocabulary, universally connected to the concept of "time." Its pervasive influence highlights how deeply time permeates our language and understanding of the world.

The Origin and Core Meaning

The original Latin word tempus meant "time," "season," or "occasion." This broad meaning allows tempor- to manifest in English words describing various aspects of time:

  • Duration: How long something lasts.
  • Simultaneity: Events happening at the same time.
  • Timing: The opportune moment for an action.
  • Pace: The speed or rate at which something occurs.
  • Existence within Time: Philosophical concepts of being subject to the flow of time.

Categories of Derived Words

  1. Words of Duration and Permanence:

    • Temporary: This is perhaps the most direct derivative, explicitly stating something is "for a time" and thus not permanent. Its opposite, permanent, has a root meaning "to remain through."
    • Temporal: While it can refer to time in general, temporal often contrasts with eternal or spiritual. It refers to things of this world, bound by the limits of time and space, rather than endless existence or spiritual realms. Example: Monks often renounce temporal possessions to focus on spiritual growth.

  2. Words of Simultaneity and Coexistence:

    • Contemporary: The prefix con- (or com-, co-) means "with" or "together." Thus, contemporary literally means "with time," implying events or people existing or happening at the same time. It's often used to describe art, music, or literature of the present day, or people who lived during the same historical period. Example: Many contemporary issues, like climate change, require global cooperation.

  3. Words of Timing and Preparedness:

    • Extemporaneous: The prefix ex- means "out of" or "from." So, extemporaneous implies something done "out of time" or "without time" for preparation. It signifies spontaneity and improvisation. Example: He had to give an extemporaneous speech when the main speaker suddenly fell ill.
    • Temporize: This verb means to delay or postpone a decision or action, specifically to gain time. It suggests a strategic use of time to avoid immediate commitment. Example: Facing tough questions, the politician tried to temporize by changing the subject.

  4. Words of Pace and Rhythm:

    • Tempo: Directly from the Italian, which itself comes from Latin tempus. It refers to the speed or rate of something, particularly in music, but also in general activity. Example: The orchestra maintained a lively tempo throughout the piece.

  5. Philosophical and Abstract Concepts:

    • Temporality: This noun encapsulates the abstract concept of existing within time. It is a more academic term, used to discuss the nature of time itself and our relationship to it. Example: Understanding human temporality is central to existential philosophy.

Related Concepts and Distinctions

  • Tempus Fugit: This famous Latin phrase, meaning "time flies," uses the root tempus directly and serves as a reminder of the fleeting nature of time.
  • Distinction from Temper-: It's important not to confuse tempor- with the root temper- (from Latin temperare, meaning "to mix, moderate, regulate"). Words like temperament, temperature, and temperate derive from temper- and relate to balance, moderation, or a state of being, not directly to time. While temper can mean a state of mind (e.g., "bad temper"), this is a different etymological path.

By recognizing the tempor- root, you can unlock the meanings of a significant portion of English vocabulary, enhancing your comprehension and ability to navigate complex texts.
