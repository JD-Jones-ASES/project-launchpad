Study Guide: Foundations of Justice – An Epigraph from Justinian

Overview

This introductory section presents a foundational definition of justice, articulated by the Roman Emperor Justinian. It posits justice not merely as an outcome, but as an enduring, unwavering commitment (a "constant and perpetual will") to ensure that every individual receives what is rightfully theirs. This quote serves as a powerful philosophical cornerstone, setting the stage for deeper explorations into legal principles, ethics, and societal structure.

Key Concepts

  • Justice as a "Will": Justinian's definition emphasizes that justice is an internal disposition or commitment, rather than just a set of actions or an achieved state. It implies a moral intent and a consistent drive to act justly.
  • "Constant and Perpetual": This highlights the enduring and unchanging nature of the commitment to justice. It suggests that justice is not a fleeting act or a situational decision, but a fundamental, unwavering principle that must be upheld continually.
  • "Render to Each Person His Due Right": This core tenet of distributive justice signifies that justice involves fairly allocating what is owed or deserved to each individual. It raises questions about what constitutes a "due right" and how it is determined in different contexts.
  • Roman Legal Thought: The quote originates from Justinian's Institutiones, a key text in Roman law, indicating its historical significance as a cornerstone of Western legal tradition.

Important Terms

  • Justitia: Latin for "justice."
  • Voluntas: Latin for "will" or "desire." In this context, it refers to a deliberate and enduring commitment.
  • Jus suum cuique tribuendi: Latin phrase meaning "to render to each person his due right" or "to give everyone his due." This encapsulates the principle of distributive justice.
  • Epigraph: A short quotation or saying at the beginning of a book or chapter, intended to suggest its theme.
  • Justinian I (c. 482-565 AD): Byzantine Emperor who reigned from 527 to 565 AD. He is renowned for his ambitious military campaigns to reclaim lost Roman territories and, most notably, for his comprehensive reform and codification of Roman law, known as the Corpus Juris Civilis.
  • Institutiones: One of the four parts of Justinian's Corpus Juris Civilis. It was a textbook for law students, summarizing the fundamental principles of Roman private law, and served as an authoritative statement of law.

Detailed Notes

I. The Origin and Context of the Quote

The epigraph "Justitia est constans et perpetua voluntas jus suum cuique tribuendi" comes from the Institutiones of Justinian, specifically Book I, Title 1.

  • Justinian's Project: Emperor Justinian undertook the monumental task of compiling and revising the vast and often contradictory body of Roman law. This effort resulted in the Corpus Juris Civilis (Body of Civil Law), a collection that systematized Roman law and became the bedrock of civil law systems throughout Europe and beyond.
  • The Institutiones: This particular work was designed as an introductory textbook for students of law. Its purpose was to provide a clear and concise overview of the basic principles of Roman law. Beginning with a definition of justice underscored the philosophical and ethical foundations upon which the entire legal system was built.
  • Historical Significance: The Corpus Juris Civilis, including the Institutiones, was instrumental in preserving Roman legal wisdom after the fall of the Western Roman Empire and significantly influenced the development of European legal thought for centuries.

II. Dissecting the Definition of Justice

Justinian's definition is not merely descriptive but prescriptive, offering a profound insight into the ideal nature of justice.

  • "Justitia est..." (Justice is...): This declarative statement establishes the fundamental nature of what follows. Justice is presented as an inherent quality or principle.
  • "...constans et perpetua voluntas..." (a constant and perpetual will...):
    • "Voluntas" (Will): This is a crucial element. Justice is not presented as a static rule or a mere act, but as an active, conscious, and deliberate choice or intention. It implies a moral agent (individual or state) committed to upholding justice. This suggests that justice requires effort and intentionality.
    • "Constans et perpetua" (Constant and Perpetual): These adjectives emphasize the unwavering and enduring nature of this will. Justice should not be sporadic or dependent on convenience, but rather a consistent and unbroken commitment. It highlights the idea that justice must be applied universally and continuously, irrespective of changing circumstances or personal biases. It forms the bedrock of a stable legal and moral order.
  • "...jus suum cuique tribuendi." (to render to each person his due right.):
    • "Jus suum" (His due right): This refers to what an individual is legitimately owed or entitled to. This "right" can derive from various sources: natural law, positive law (statutes, decrees), custom, or moral principles. It encapsulates the idea of desert and entitlement.
    • "Cuique" (to each person): This emphasizes the individualistic aspect of justice. It implies that justice must consider the specific circumstances and entitlements of each person, rather than applying a blanket rule without discernment.
    • "Tribuendi" (to render, to give, to distribute): This verb signifies the act of giving or distributing what is due. It points to the active role of legal systems, judges, and individuals in ensuring that rights are recognized and upheld. This is the essence of distributive justice – the fair allocation of goods, burdens, and rights within a society.

III. Philosophical and Legal Implications

Justinian's definition has had far-reaching implications:

  • Foundation for Legal Systems: It provides an ethical and philosophical grounding for the creation and enforcement of laws. Laws are ideally designed to help realize this "constant and perpetual will" to ensure everyone receives their due.
  • Emphasis on Intent: By defining justice as a "will," it shifts focus from merely outward actions or results to the internal disposition and moral character of those administering justice. This highlights the importance of impartiality, integrity, and good faith in legal proceedings.
  • Challenge of Defining "Due Right": While powerful, the definition also presents a continuous challenge: what exactly constitutes "due right"? Societies, cultures, and legal traditions continuously grapple with defining and redefining rights in light of evolving ethical understanding, social changes, and technological advancements.
  • Ideal vs. Reality: The definition presents an ideal. The "constant and perpetual will" is something to aspire to, even if perfect justice is rarely achieved in practice. It serves as a guiding star for legal and ethical reformers.
  • Connection to Natural Law: This definition resonates strongly with natural law traditions, which posit that certain rights are inherent to human beings and can be discovered through reason, independent of specific human laws.

IV. Significance as an Epigraph

Placing this quote at the beginning of a chapter or book signals its foundational importance. It suggests that the subsequent material will explore how this ideal of justice is pursued, challenged, and manifested within legal frameworks, societal norms, or historical contexts. It prompts the reader to consider the deep philosophical underpinnings of the subject matter.

Review Questions

  1. According to Justinian's definition, what is the fundamental difference between an act of fairness and the concept of "justice" itself?
  2. Explain the significance of the phrases "constant and perpetual" in Justinian's definition of justice. How does this impact our understanding of the role of law in society?
  3. What challenges might a society face in consistently fulfilling the mandate "to render to each person his due right"? Provide examples.
  4. Considering that the Institutiones was a textbook for law students, why might Justinian have chosen to begin with this particular definition of justice? What message does it convey to aspiring legal professionals?
  5. Discuss the enduring relevance of Justinian's definition of justice in contemporary legal, ethical, or political debates. How does it still resonate with modern discussions about rights and fairness?
