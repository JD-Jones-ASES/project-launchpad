Study Guide: Chapter 19, Section 1: Epigraph - Justinian on Justice

Overview

This brief introductory section presents a foundational definition of justice from Justinian's Institutiones. It encapsulates the essence of justice as a consistent and unending commitment to ensuring that every individual receives what is rightfully theirs. This quote serves as a powerful philosophical anchor, likely setting the thematic stage for the discussions on law, rights, and ethical principles to follow in Chapter 19.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of Justice: Justice is explicitly defined as an active and sustained commitment, not merely a passive state or a fleeting emotion. It requires a conscious and ongoing will to act.
  • Constant and Perpetual Will (Constans et Perpetua Voluntas): This emphasizes the enduring, unwavering, and consistent nature required for true justice. It suggests that justice isn't a one-time act but a continuous endeavor, foundational to a stable legal and social order.
  • Rendering to Each Person His Due Right (Jus Suum Cuique Tribuendi): This core principle highlights the idea of fairness, equity, and individual entitlement. It implies a recognition of inherent rights or legitimate claims that individuals possess, which a just system is obligated to uphold and deliver. What constitutes "due right" is often debated but is central to legal philosophy.
  • Foundational Principle of Law: The epigraph, originating from Roman law, underscores how deeply this concept of justice has been embedded in Western legal thought for centuries, serving as a guiding star for lawmakers and legal practitioners.
  • Active vs. Passive Justice: By defining justice as a "will to render," Justinian emphasizes an active, deliberative, and executive component, rather than a mere theoretical understanding or passive observation of fairness.

Important Terms

  • Epigraph: A short quotation or saying at the beginning of a book, chapter, or document, intended to suggest its theme.
  • Justitia: Latin for "justice." In this context, it refers to the moral principle of fairness and righteousness, particularly as applied in law.
  • Constans et perpetua voluntas: Latin for "constant and perpetual will." This phrase highlights the unwavering and enduring commitment required for justice.
  • Jus suum cuique tribuendi: Latin for "to render to each person his due right" or "to give each his own right." This is the core action of justice according to Justinian, emphasizing equitable distribution and recognition of individual entitlements.
  • Justinian (Flavius Petrus Sabbatius Iustinianus Augustus, c. 482 – 565 AD): A Byzantine (Eastern Roman) Emperor who reigned from 527 to 565 AD. He is most famous for his monumental legal reforms, particularly the codification of Roman law.
  • Institutiones (Institutes): A textbook of Roman law written by Justinian's jurists (principally Tribonian, Theophilus, and Dorotheus) and published in 533 AD. It was designed for students and served as an introduction to Justinian's much larger legal compilation, the Corpus Juris Civilis.
  • Corpus Juris Civilis: "Body of Civil Law." The modern name for a collection of fundamental works in jurisprudence, issued from 529 to 534 by order of Justinian I. It comprises the Codex, Digesta (Pandects), Institutiones, and Novellae (Novels). It is considered the foundation of civil law systems throughout the world.

Detailed Notes

The Role of an Epigraph

An epigraph serves as more than just a decorative quote; it acts as a thematic compass, guiding the reader toward the central ideas or philosophical underpinnings of the text that follows. For Chapter 19, this specific epigraph from Justinian immediately signals that the subsequent content will delve into fundamental questions of justice, law, rights, and perhaps the very nature of a legal system's purpose. It grounds the chapter in a historical and philosophical tradition, indicating that the concepts explored are not new but have deep roots in legal thought.

Justinian: The Architect of Roman Law

Justinian I was one of the most significant figures in legal history. As the Byzantine Emperor, he inherited a vast, complex, and often contradictory body of Roman law, accumulated over centuries. Recognizing the need for clarity and systematization, he embarked on an ambitious project to codify and consolidate Roman law, resulting in the Corpus Juris Civilis (Body of Civil Law). This monumental work preserved classical Roman jurisprudence and became the bedrock upon which many modern legal systems, especially those in continental Europe (civil law systems), were built. His contributions underscore the enduring influence of Roman legal principles on contemporary legal thought.

The Institutiones: A Textbook for Legal Minds

The Institutiones (Institutes) was a crucial part of Justinian's legal compilation. Unlike the Digesta (which compiled fragments from classical jurists' writings) or the Codex (which contained imperial enactments), the Institutiones was specifically designed as a clear, systematic introductory textbook for law students. Its purpose was to provide a concise and understandable overview of Roman law's principles, enabling future lawyers and judges to grasp the fundamentals before diving into more complex legal texts. The fact that the definition of justice appears at the very beginning of this introductory text highlights its paramount importance as a foundational concept.

Deconstructing Justinian's Definition of Justice

The epigraph, "Justitia est constans et perpetua voluntas jus suum cuique tribuendi," offers a profound and active definition of justice: "Justice is the constant and perpetual will to render to each person his due right."

  1. "Justitia est..." (Justice is...): This declarative statement immediately posits justice as an essential, definable entity or principle.

  2. "constans et perpetua voluntas" (constant and perpetual will):

    • Will (Voluntas): This is critical. Justice isn't presented as a static rule or an abstract ideal, but as an active faculty of the mind. It requires conscious intent and decision. This implies agency and responsibility on the part of those administering or upholding justice. It's not enough for laws to exist; there must be a will to apply them justly.
    • Constant (Constans): Justice must be unwavering. It cannot be applied inconsistently, depending on the circumstances, the individuals involved, or the whims of the powerful. Consistency ensures predictability and reliability in the legal system.
    • Perpetual (Perpetua): Justice is timeless and unending. It's not a temporary measure but an eternal principle that must always be strived for. This speaks to its fundamental and enduring nature within any moral or legal framework.
    • Together, "constant and perpetual will" signifies an unyielding, unwavering, and active commitment to the principles of justice. It demands perseverance and integrity from all participants in the legal process.

  3. "jus suum cuique tribuendi" (to render to each person his due right):

    • To render (Tribuendi): This verb implies an act of giving, assigning, or distributing. It's an active process of dispensation.
    • To each person (Cuique): This emphasizes the universal application of justice. It applies to everyone, regardless of status, background, or power. It promotes equality before the law.
    • His due right (Jus suum): This is arguably the most complex part. What constitutes "due right"?
      • Positive Law: Rights granted by statutes, constitutions, or legal precedents.
      • Natural Law: Inherent rights believed to be universally recognizable, derived from human nature or a divine order, irrespective of specific laws.
      • Equity: Principles of fairness and justice used to temper the strict application of law, often to prevent harsh or unjust outcomes.
      • The phrase implies an objective standard by which entitlements are judged. A just system identifies what individuals are legitimately owed and then actively works to provide it. This can involve protecting existing rights, correcting wrongs, or ensuring fair distribution of resources or burdens.

The Enduring Legacy

Justinian's definition has had a profound and lasting impact on legal philosophy and practice. It provides a moral compass for legal systems, urging them to be not just efficient or orderly, but fundamentally fair and committed to the individual's legitimate claims. It challenges societies to continuously evaluate what constitutes "due right" in changing circumstances and to maintain the "constant and perpetual will" to uphold those rights for all. This makes it an ideal epigraph for a chapter likely exploring the intricacies and challenges of achieving justice in various contexts.

Review Questions

  1. What is the significance of the terms "constant" and "perpetual" in Justinian's definition of justice? How might a legal system falter if it lacks this "constant and perpetual will"?
  2. Discuss the meaning of "rendering to each person his due right." What are some different interpretations of what constitutes a "due right" in modern society? Provide examples.
  3. Why do you think the authors of this textbook chose an epigraph from Justinian's Institutiones to begin Chapter 19? What thematic purpose does it serve for the chapter?
  4. How does Justinian's definition emphasize the active nature of justice? What responsibilities does this definition place on individuals, institutions, and legal professionals?
  5. Consider a contemporary ethical or legal dilemma (e.g., resource distribution, criminal sentencing, intellectual property rights). How might Justinian's definition of justice guide a discussion or proposed resolution for this dilemma?
