Word Family: jus / jur / judic

Overview

This word family explores eight English words derived from the Latin roots jus (law, right), jur (to swear), and judic (judge). These words form the core of English legal and judicial vocabulary.

Word Breakdown

Justice

Components: just (right, law) + -ice (quality of)

Definition: Fairness; the quality of being just. Justice is the principle that people should be treated rightly and according to law.

Jury

Components: jur (to swear) + -y

Definition: A group sworn to judge a case fairly. Jurors take an oath (swear) to render an honest verdict.

Jurisdiction

Components: juris (of law) + dict (to say) + -ion

Definition: The authority to declare and apply the law. A jurisdiction defines the territory or domain where legal authority applies.

Judicial

Components: judic (judge) + -ial (relating to)

Definition: Relating to courts, judges, or the law. Judicial decisions are those made by judges in the exercise of their authority.

Adjudicate

Components: ad- (toward) + judic (judge) + -ate

Definition: To make a formal judgment on a dispute. An adjudicator acts as judge, moving 'toward' a verdict.

Perjury

Components: per- (wrongly) + jur (to swear) + -y

Definition: The crime of lying under oath. The prefix per- here means 'wrongly' or 'falsely,' so perjury is swearing falsely.

Prejudice

Components: pre- (before) + judic (judgment)

Definition: An opinion formed before knowing the facts. Literally a 'pre-judgment' -- reaching a conclusion before examining evidence.

Jurisprudence

Components: juris (of law) + prudence (wisdom)

Definition: The study and philosophy of law. Literally 'wisdom about the law,' jurisprudence examines legal theory and principles.

Patterns and Observations

Understanding how prefixes and suffixes modify the base root is the key to unlocking unfamiliar vocabulary. Notice how:

  • Prefixes change the direction or scope of the root meaning (e.g., per- means 'wrongly,' pre- means 'before,' ad- means 'toward').
  • Suffixes change the part of speech or specify the quality (e.g., -ice marks a quality, -ial makes an adjective, -ate makes a verb).
  • The same root can produce nouns, verbs, and adjectives depending on the suffix attached.

Review Questions

  2. Which prefixes in this word family change the root meaning most dramatically? Give examples.
  3. Can you think of additional English words not listed here that might share the same root? What do they mean?
  4. How does understanding the root and affix structure help you remember these words' meanings?
  5. Write a sentence using at least two words from this family that demonstrates their meanings.
