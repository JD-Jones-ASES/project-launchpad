Study Guide: Chapter 1 — VOC (Voice and Calling)

Overview

Chapter 1 introduces the Latin root voc / vox, which means "voice" or "to call." This root comes from the Latin verb vocare (to call) and the noun vox (voice), and it gave English a rich family of words all connected to the power of the human voice. Students will learn how this root appears in words about speaking, summoning, and identity.

Key Concepts

  • Root meaning and origin: "voc / vox" comes from Latin vocare (to call) and vox (voice), used in ancient Rome to describe everything from lawyers arguing in court to priests calling upon the gods
  • How the root appears: The root appears as "voc" in most words (vocal, vocation) and as "vok" in words that came through French (invoke, evoke, provoke, revoke)
  • Important morpheme patterns: Adding prefixes changes the direction of the calling — ad- (toward), in- (upon), e- (out), pro- (forth), re- (back)

Important Terms

  • vocal: relating to or produced by the voice (voc + -al)
  • advocate: one who speaks in support of another; to publicly support a cause (ad- + voc + -ate)
  • vocation: a person's calling or profession; the work one feels summoned to do (voc + -ation)
  • invoke: to call upon for help or as an authority (in- + vok)
  • vociferous: loud and forceful in expressing feelings (voc + ferre + -ous)
  • evoke: to call forth a feeling, memory, or image (e- + vok)
  • provoke: to call forth a reaction, often anger (pro- + vok)
  • revoke: to take back or withdraw a right or decision (re- + vok)

Detailed Notes

The Root in Ancient Rome

Two thousand years ago in the Roman Forum, the Latin verb vocare (to call) was central to public life. Lawyers raised their voices to defend citizens; priests invoked the names of gods; senators spoke to declare the law. From vocare and its cousin vox (voice), English inherited a large family of words all tied to the power of speaking and calling.

Word Family Analysis

The simplest descendant is vocal — anything relating to the voice. From there, prefixes transform the root's meaning by indicating the direction of the call:

  • invoke: in- (upon) — to call upon a rule or authority for support
  • evoke: e- (out) — to call out a memory or emotion
  • provoke: pro- (forth) — to call forth a reaction (often anger)
  • revoke: re- (back) — to call back; to take away a privilege

The word advocate (ad- + voc + -ate) literally means "one called toward" a cause — in Rome, the lawyer who stood beside you and spoke on your behalf. Your vocation is your life's calling — the profession you feel summoned to.

Morpheme Spotlight: Vociferous

The word vociferous is built from two roots: voc (voice) + ferre (to carry) + -ous. It literally means "carrying the voice" — someone who carries their voice loudly and forcefully. Knowing that ferre means "to carry" helps unlock other English words like transfer, ferry, and fertile.

False Cognate Warning: Avocado

The word avocado looks like it might share the voc root with advocate, but it does not. It entered English through Spanish aguacate, which came from the Nahuatl (Aztec) word ahuacatl. This is called a false cognate — a word that looks related but is not. Always check etymological sources carefully.

Connections to Other Roots

The voc root is the first in a group of communication roots covered in Chapters 1–5:

  • voc (Ch. 1): the voice that calls
  • dict (Ch. 2): the content of what is said
  • aud (Ch. 3): the act of hearing
  • scrib/script (Ch. 4): the act of writing
  • vis/vid (Ch. 5): the act of seeing

Review Questions

  1. What is the difference between evoke and provoke? How do their prefixes change the meaning of the root?
  2. In what ways did ancient Roman society depend on the power of the spoken voice? Name at least two examples from the chapter.
  3. Why is avocado considered a "false cognate" for the voc root family?
  4. Look at the word vociferous. Which two Latin roots are combined to make this word, and what does each one mean?
  5. If someone has a "vocation" as a teacher, what does that say about how they view their work, based on the meaning of the root?
