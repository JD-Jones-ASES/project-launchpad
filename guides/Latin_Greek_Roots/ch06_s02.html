Study Guide: The Roots sens and sent

Overview

This section explores the Latin root sentire, meaning "to feel" or "to perceive," but also carrying the deeper connotation of "to form a judgment" or "to arrive at an opinion through careful thought." We'll see how this dual meaning gives rise to a variety of English words, with the root sens often relating to physical sensation and sent to feelings, opinions, and agreement or disagreement, culminating in the surprisingly connected meanings of the word "sentence."

Key Concepts

  • The Latin Root sentire: The ultimate origin of both sens and sent roots. It meant both "to feel/perceive" (physically) and "to form a judgment/opinion" (intellectually/emotionally). The Romans saw these as interconnected.
  • Derivation of sens from sensus: The root sens comes from sensus, the past participle of sentire. Words derived from sens often relate to the physical senses.
  • Derivation of sent from sentire: The root sent comes directly from the verb stem of sentire. Words derived from sent often relate to feelings, thoughts, and opinions, particularly in social contexts of agreement or disagreement.
  • The Dual Nature of "Feeling": The Roman concept of sentire encompassed both immediate physical sensation and deeper, considered thought or judgment, which is reflected in the diverse English vocabulary.
  • The Etymology of "Sentence": This word is a unique example, deriving from Latin sententia ("a considered opinion"). Both its meanings in English—a judge's ruling and a grammatical complete thought—stem from this original idea of a fully formed judgment or expression.

Important Terms

  • sentire: (Latin verb) "to feel," "to perceive," and "to form a judgment/opinion."
  • sensus: (Latin past participle of sentire) The source of the sens root, particularly relating to physical perception.
  • Sensory: Relating to the physical senses (sight, hearing, touch, taste, smell).
  • Sensitive: Capable of feeling or perceiving things deeply, whether physical touch or emotional experiences.
  • Sentiment: A feeling or opinion, often an attitude toward a situation or idea.
  • Consent: To "feel together" (con- + sentire); to agree to something, sharing the same judgment or opinion.
  • Dissent: To "feel apart" (dis- + sentire); to differ in opinion or refuse to agree.
  • Assent: To "feel toward" (ad-/as- + sentire); to express agreement, often by nodding or indicating approval.
  • sententia: (Latin noun) "a considered opinion"; the direct origin of the English word "sentence."
  • Sentence (grammatical): A complete expression of a thought; a finished piece of meaning that is fully "felt out" or considered.
  • Sentence (judicial): The judgment or punishment delivered by a judge after weighing the facts of a case; the judge's considered opinion.

Detailed Notes

The Root Story: sentire – Perception and Judgment

The journey into the sens and sent roots begins with the Latin verb sentire. While its basic translation is "to feel" or "to perceive," the Romans understood it to encompass more than just physical sensation. For them, sentire also meant "to form a judgment" or "to arrive at an opinion through careful thought." This dual nature—combining raw perception with intellectual consideration—is key to understanding the rich family of English words derived from this root. When a Roman sentire something, they weren't just experiencing it, they were also processing and forming a conclusion about it.

The Root sens from sensus

The root sens comes specifically from sensus, which is the past participle of sentire. This branch of the family primarily relates to our physical abilities to perceive the world:

  • Your Five Senses: Sight, hearing, touch, taste, and smell are the fundamental ways your body perceives external stimuli. The connection here is direct and evident.
  • Sensory: Anything described as sensory directly relates to these five senses. For example, sensory input refers to the information gathered through sight, sound, etc.
  • Sensitive: A sensitive person or thing is one that feels or perceives things deeply. This can apply to physical sensations (e.g., sensitive skin reacting to touch) or emotional and experiential depth (e.g., being sensitive to another's feelings). The deep feeling aspect ties back to sentire's original meaning.

The Root sent from sentire's Verb Stem

The root sent comes directly from the verb stem of sentire. Words derived from sent often emphasize the intellectual, emotional, or opinion-based aspects of sentire:

  • Sentiment: This word refers to a feeling or an opinion. It's not just a fleeting emotion, but often a considered attitude or thought, reflecting the "judgment" aspect of sentire. For example, "public sentiment" refers to the general opinion of the populace.
  • Consent: This word literally means "to feel together." It's formed by combining the prefix con- (meaning "with" or "together") with sentire. When you consent to something, you are sharing the same judgment or opinion as another person or group; you are in agreement.
  • Dissent: In contrast, dissent means "to feel apart." The prefix dis- means "apart" or "away." To dissent is to hold a different opinion, to disagree, or to feel separately from the majority.
  • Assent: This word means "to feel toward" or "to agree to." The prefix as- (a form of ad-) means "to" or "toward." To assent is to express agreement or approval, often by indicating that one's feelings or thoughts are aligned with what is being proposed.

The Surprising Case of "Sentence"

Perhaps the most intriguing member of this word family is "sentence," precisely because it has two distinct meanings in English that seem unrelated at first glance: a grammatical unit and a judicial punishment. However, both meanings perfectly align with the core Latin logic of sentire:

  • Latin sententia: In Latin, sententia meant "a considered opinion" or "a thought that has been weighed and formed." It was the culmination of the sentire process.
  • A Judge's Sentence: When a judge delivers a sentence, she is pronouncing her considered opinion and final judgment after hearing all the facts and arguments of a case. It is her fully "felt out" decision regarding the accused.
  • A Grammatical Sentence: A grammatical sentence is a complete expression of a thought. It's not just a collection of words, but a finished piece of meaning, an idea that has been fully "felt out" or organized into a coherent statement. It's a fully formed thought, much like a considered opinion.

Both meanings of "sentence" demonstrate the profound connection between feeling, perceiving, and forming a complete, considered judgment or thought, showcasing the enduring logic of the Latin root.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the two main facets of the Latin word sentire (its original meanings) and provide examples of how each facet is reflected in modern English words derived from it.
  2. How does the root sens primarily relate to physical perception? Name two English words that use this root and briefly explain how they connect to the concept of the senses.
  3. Describe how the root sent is used in words like sentiment, consent, dissent, and assent. How do the prefixes con-, dis-, and as- modify the meaning of the root in these specific terms?
  4. The English word "sentence" has two common meanings: a judge's punishment and a complete grammatical thought. Trace both meanings back to their shared Latin origin (sententia) and explain the logical connection between them according to the text.
  5. Reflect on the Roman understanding of "feeling" (sentire) as encompassing both physical sensation and thoughtful judgment. How might this broader definition of "feeling" differ from how we commonly use the word "feel" in English today, and what insights does this difference provide?
