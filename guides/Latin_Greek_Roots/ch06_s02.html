Root Profile: sens, sent

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental Latin root forms "sens" and "sent," both derived from the verb sentire, meaning "to feel" or "to perceive." Understanding these roots provides a powerful tool for deciphering the meanings of numerous English words related to sensation, emotion, thought, and judgment.

Key Concepts

  • Core Meaning: The root forms sens and sent fundamentally mean "to feel" or "to perceive." This perception can be physical, emotional, or intellectual.
  • Latin Origin: Both forms originate from the Latin verb sentire (to feel, perceive, judge) and its past participle sensus.
  • Variant Form sens: This form primarily derives from the Latin past participle sensus. Words using sens often relate to physical sensation or the faculty of perception.
  • Variant Form sent: This form primarily derives from the stem of the Latin verb sentire. Words using sent often relate to feelings, opinions, or judgments.
  • Derivative Words: Many common English words, such as sense, sentence, consent, and sentiment, are direct descendants of these roots, reflecting their core meaning in various contexts.

Important Terms

  • Root: The basic element of a word that carries its primary meaning. In this section, sens and sent are the roots.
  • Etymology: The study of the origin and historical development of words. Here, we trace sens and sent back to Latin.
  • Latin: An ancient Italic language, widely influential in the development of English, especially for scientific, legal, and academic vocabulary.
  • Sentire (Latin verb): The Latin verb meaning "to feel, perceive, or judge," which is the ultimate source of both root forms.
  • Sensus (Latin past participle): The past participle form of sentire, meaning "felt" or "perceived," which directly gives rise to the sens variant.
  • Variant Form: A different spelling or pronunciation of the same root that carries the same or a very similar meaning. Sens and sent are variant forms of the same underlying root concept.
  • Derivative: A word that is formed from another word or root. For example, sentiment is a derivative of sent.

Detailed Notes

The roots sens and sent are foundational components in the English language, primarily linking to the concepts of "feeling" and "perception." Their rich etymology from Latin sentire allows for a wide range of derivative words that describe everything from physical touch to complex emotions and intellectual judgments.

Origin and Core Meaning

Both sens and sent trace their lineage back to the Latin verb sentire, which encompassed a broad spectrum of meanings:

  • To feel: This includes physical sensations (e.g., pain, touch) as well as emotional states (e.g., joy, sadness).
  • To perceive: This refers to becoming aware of something through the senses or intellectually understanding it.
  • To judge: This implies forming an opinion or making a decision based on perception or feeling.

The past participle of sentire was sensus, which literally meant "felt" or "perceived." It is from these two primary Latin forms—the verb stem and its past participle—that our English variants emerge.

The Variant Form: sens

The sens variant predominantly comes from the Latin past participle sensus. Words containing sens often emphasize:

  • Physical Sensation: The ability to feel through the bodily senses.
    • Sense: Any of the faculties by which humans and animals perceive external stimuli (sight, smell, hearing, touch, taste); also, a feeling or an awareness.
    • Sensory: Relating to sensation or the physical senses. (e.g., "sensory organs," "sensory input")
    • Sensible: Having or showing good sense or judgment; also, able to be perceived by the senses. (e.g., "a sensible decision," "a sensible temperature change")
    • Sensitive: Quick to perceive or feel; easily affected by external influences. (e.g., "sensitive skin," "a sensitive topic")
    • Nonsense: Language, behavior, or ideas that are absurd or have no meaning; the opposite of sense.

The Variant Form: sent

The sent variant typically derives from the verb stem of sentire. Words containing sent often lean towards:

  • Emotional Feeling or Opinion: States of mind, emotions, or beliefs.
    • Sentiment: A feeling or emotion; an attitude toward something. (e.g., "public sentiment," "a sentimental value")
    • Sentimental: Of or prompted by feelings of tenderness, sadness, or nostalgia.
    • Consent: To give permission or agreement; to feel or perceive together with others, leading to agreement. (from Latin con- meaning "with" + sentire)
    • Dissent: To hold or express opinions that are at variance with those previously, officially, or generally expressed. (from Latin dis- meaning "apart" + sentire)
    • Assent: To express approval or agreement, typically officially. (from Latin ad- meaning "to" + sentire)
    • Presentiment: An intuitive feeling about the future, especially one of an ominous nature; a feeling felt before. (from Latin prae- meaning "before" + sentire)
  • Judgment or Thought: The act of forming an opinion or expressing a thought.
    • Sentence: In a legal context, a judgment passed by a court; in grammar, a group of words that expresses a complete thought or idea (a perceived unit of meaning).
    • Sentient: Able to perceive or feel things; conscious. This word directly highlights the "perceive" aspect of the root, referring to beings capable of feeling and awareness.

By recognizing the underlying meaning of "to feel" or "to perceive" within sens and sent, one can often deduce the meaning of unfamiliar words, connecting them to familiar concepts of sensation, emotion, and judgment.
