Study Guide: Chapter 3 — AUD (To Hear and Listen)

Overview

Chapter 3 introduces the Latin root aud / audit, which means "to hear" or "to listen." It comes from the Latin verb audire (to hear). The Romans were masters of designing spaces — like their magnificent amphitheaters — so that sound could reach every listener. This chapter's root completes a three-part communication triangle with voc (to call) and dict (to say): one person uses a voice, says something, and another person hears it.

Key Concepts

  • Root meaning and origin: "aud" comes from Latin audire (to hear); audit is the past-participle form of the same root
  • How the root appears: As "aud" in most words (audible, audience, inaudible) and as "audit" in words derived from the past participle (auditorium, audition, audit)
  • Important morpheme patterns: The suffix -ible means "able to be" (audible = able to be heard); -orium marks a place (auditorium = a place for hearing); in- negates (inaudible = not able to be heard)

Important Terms

  • audience: a group of listeners or spectators (aud + -ience)
  • auditorium: a large hall designed for an audience to hear (audit + -orium)
  • audible: loud enough to be heard (aud + -ible)
  • audit: an official examination of records; originally a formal hearing of accounts (audit)
  • audition: a trial performance to demonstrate talent (audit + -ion)
  • inaudible: too quiet to be heard (in- + aud + -ible)

Detailed Notes

The Root in Ancient Rome

Roman amphitheaters were engineering marvels — their curved stone seating was designed so that even a whisper on the stage could be heard in the highest row. The Latin word audire (to hear) gave us audience, which at its core simply means a gathering of people who are listening. The Romans prized hearing as a civic virtue: citizens who listened well, witnesses who heard testimony, officials who heard accounts.

The Surprising History of "Audit"

The word audit today means a careful review of financial records — seemingly far removed from "hearing." But the connection is historical: in the Middle Ages, financial accounts were read aloud to officials who listened carefully for errors. The auditor literally "heard" the accounts. Even as the practice changed from oral to written review, the word preserved its ancient meaning of careful, attentive listening.

Word Family Analysis

All words in this family trace back to the act of hearing:

  • audience: the group gathered to hear something
  • audible / inaudible: whether a sound can or cannot be heard (the prefix in- negates the root)
  • auditorium: the physical space built for hearing (-orium = place for)
  • audition: a trial performance evaluated through listening (-ion = act of)
  • audit: a formal hearing or review (-it = Latin past participle form)

The Communication Triangle: VOC + DICT + AUD

This chapter completes a three-part model of human verbal communication introduced across Chapters 1–3:

  1. VOC (Ch. 1): A person uses their voice to call
  2. DICT (Ch. 2): That person says or declares something
  3. AUD (Ch. 3): Another person hears what was said

Together these roots capture the entire arc of a spoken exchange — from the voice that speaks to the ears that listen.

Suffix Focus: -ible and -orium

Two important suffixes appear in this chapter:

  • -ible means "able to be": audible = able to be heard; inaudible = not able to be heard (also in Ch. 1: vociferous uses -ous)
  • -orium means "a place for": auditorium = a place for hearing. You will see this suffix again in words like emporium (a place for trading).

Review Questions

  1. Why do you think Romans built their amphitheaters with curved, tiered seating? How does the meaning of the root aud connect to this architectural design?
  2. Explain why the word audit (a financial review) contains the root aud (to hear). What historical practice explains this connection?
  3. What is the difference between audible and inaudible? What role does the prefix in- play?
  4. How do the three roots voc, dict, and aud work together to describe a complete act of communication?
  5. If a singer fails her audition, what does that word tell us about how her performance was evaluated?
