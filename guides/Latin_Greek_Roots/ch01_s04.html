Study Guide: The VOC/VOK Word Family

Overview

This section explores the VOC/VOK word family, which originates from the Latin root meaning "voice" or "to call." By examining various prefixes and suffixes, we uncover how this core meaning expands to form words related to speaking, advocating, careers, and the act of calling upon or causing reactions.

Key Concepts

  • The Root VOC/VOK: The fundamental building block of this word family is the Latin root voc (which sometimes appears as vok), meaning "voice" or "to call." Understanding this core meaning is crucial for grasping the nuances of related words.
  • Impact of Prefixes: Prefixes attached to voc/vok significantly alter the word's meaning. For example, ad- (toward), in- (upon), e- (out), pro- (forth), and re- (back) direct the "calling" or "voicing" action in different ways.
  • Role of Suffixes: Suffixes further refine the meaning, often indicating the word's grammatical function (e.g., -al for relating to, -ate for a person or action, -ation for an act or state, -ous for full of).
  • Semantic Expansion: While rooted in "voice" or "to call," the words in this family develop diverse meanings, from physical sound production to abstract concepts like one's profession or causing an emotional response.

Important Terms

Vocal: Relating to or produced by the voice. Advocate: One who speaks in support of another; to support publicly. Vocation: A person's calling or profession, often implying a sense of purpose. Invoke: To call upon for help, inspiration, or as an authority. Vociferous: Characterized by loud and forceful expression of feelings or opinions. Evoke: To call forth a feeling, memory, or image, often from within. Provoke: To call forth a reaction or feeling, often an unwanted or angry one. Revoke: To take back or withdraw a right, decision, or privilege.

Detailed Notes

This section delves into each word from the VOC/VOK family, breaking down its etymology and providing a clear definition. Understanding the prefixes, roots, and suffixes will help solidify your grasp of these terms.

  • Vocal

    • Etymology: voc (voice) + -al (relating to)
    • Meaning: Directly related to the voice or speech. It can describe something produced by the voice (e.g., vocal music) or refer to the organs of speech (e.g., vocal cords).
    • Example: A vocal performance requires strong control over pitch and tone.

  • Advocate

    • Etymology: ad- (toward) + voc (to call) + -ate (suffix for a person or to make/cause)
    • Meaning: This word can function as both a noun and a verb. As a noun, an advocate is someone who speaks in support of another person, cause, or idea. As a verb, to advocate means to publicly support or recommend a particular cause or policy. The idea is "to call toward" or "to speak on behalf of."
    • Example: She became a passionate advocate for environmental protection, constantly speaking out against pollution.

  • Vocation

    • Etymology: voc (to call) + -ation (act of; state of being)
    • Meaning: A person's calling or profession, especially one for which they feel particularly suited or dedicated. It implies a strong sense of purpose or duty associated with one's work.
    • Example: Many teachers view their job not just as a career, but as a true vocation.

  • Invoke

    • Etymology: in- (upon) + vok (to call)
    • Meaning: To call upon someone or something for help, support, inspiration, or as an authority. It often suggests formally appealing to a higher power, a rule, or a principle.
    • Example: The speaker decided to invoke historical precedents to support their argument.

  • Vociferous

    • Etymology: voc (voice) + ferre (to carry) + -ous (full of; characterized by)
    • Meaning: Describing someone who is loud and forceful in expressing their feelings or opinions. It suggests carrying one's voice with great volume and intensity.
    • Example: The crowd became vociferous in their demands for the politician to address their concerns.

  • Evoke

    • Etymology: e- (out) + vok (to call)
    • Meaning: To call forth a feeling, memory, or image from someone. It suggests drawing something out from within a person or from the past.
    • Example: The old photograph managed to evoke powerful memories of her childhood.

  • Provoke

    • Etymology: pro- (forth) + vok (to call)
    • Meaning: To call forth a reaction, feeling, or action, often one that is unwelcome, negative, or angry. It implies stirring up a response.
    • Example: His rude comments were clearly intended to provoke an argument.

  • Revoke

    • Etymology: re- (back) + vok (to call)
    • Meaning: To take back or withdraw a right, privilege, or decision. It means to officially cancel something that was previously granted or decided.
    • Example: The driver's license was revoked after multiple traffic violations.

Review Questions

  1. How does the core meaning of voc/vok (voice/to call) manifest differently in words like vocal versus vocation? Provide an example for each.
  2. Compare and contrast the meanings of evoke and provoke. How do their respective prefixes (e- and pro-) contribute to these differences in meaning?
  3. Explain how an advocate embodies the "to call" aspect of the voc/vok root. Describe a situation where someone might need an advocate.
  4. If someone is described as being vociferous, what does that tell you about their communication style, and how does the etymology (voc + ferre) help you understand this?
  5. Describe a scenario where a decision or privilege might be revoked. How does the idea of "calling back" or withdrawing apply in your example?
