Study Guide: Root Profile - fer/lat

Overview

This section introduces the Latin root fer/lat, which means "to carry," "to bear," or "to bring." It highlights the fascinating linguistic phenomenon of a suppletive pair, where two seemingly distinct forms, fer (from the present tense) and lat (from the past participle), function together to convey the same core meaning. Understanding this root is crucial for deciphering the meanings of many English words.

Key Concepts

  • Core Meaning of fer/lat: The primary meaning of this Latin root is "to carry," "to bear," or "to bring." This core meaning is retained in many English words derived from it.
  • Latin Origin: The root originates from the Latin verb ferre (to carry) and its past participle latus (carried). This directly explains the two forms of the root we see in English.
  • Suppletive Pair: fer and lat constitute a suppletive pair. This means that two different original words (or different forms from different parts of a verb's conjugation) combine to form a complete paradigm (like present and past forms) for a single concept. In this case, fer acts as the "present stem" and lat as the "past participle stem."
  • Derivative Words: Many English words are "derivatives" of these roots, meaning they have evolved from the Latin originals and incorporate their core meaning. Examples like transfer, prefer, translate, and relate illustrate how the "carrying" or "bringing" sense is embedded in their definitions.

Important Terms

  • Root: The basic linguistic unit that carries the primary meaning of a word. Many words are built upon roots.
  • Etymology: The study of the origin of words and the way their meanings have changed throughout history. Understanding roots is a key part of etymology.
  • Latin: An ancient Italic language, originally spoken in Latium (including Rome), which heavily influenced English vocabulary, particularly in academic, scientific, and legal fields.
  • Suppletive Pair: In linguistics, a situation where different inflectional forms of a word (like present and past tenses, or singular and plural) come from different stems that were historically unrelated. They act as a pair to complete a paradigm. (e.g., "go" and "went" in English are a suppletive pair).
  • Present Stem: The part of a verb that is used to form present tense conjugations. For the root ferre, fer is the present stem.
  • Past Participle Stem: The part of a verb that is used to form past participles (and often perfect tenses). For the root ferre, lat (from latus) is the past participle stem.
  • Derivative: A word that is formed from another word or root, often by adding prefixes or suffixes, and usually retaining some aspect of the original word's meaning.

Detailed Notes

The root fer/lat is a prime example of how Latin has shaped English vocabulary. Its fundamental meaning of "to carry," "to bear," or "to bring" is a powerful tool for understanding a wide array of sophisticated English words.

The Root and Its Core Meaning: The core meaning of fer/lat is quite versatile:

  • To carry: Physically moving something from one place to another.
  • To bear: To support a weight, to endure, or to produce (as in bearing fruit).
  • To bring: To cause something to come to a place or state.

Language of Origin: Latin: This root's journey into English begins with Latin. The Latin verb ferre means "to carry" or "to bear." Its past participle is latus, meaning "carried" or "borne." This distinction between the present infinitive (ferre) and the past participle (latus) is crucial for understanding why we have two forms, fer and lat, operating as one root.

The Concept of a Suppletive Pair: The most distinctive feature of this root is its suppletive nature. A suppletive pair occurs when a word's different grammatical forms (like present and past) come from historically distinct stems. Think of the English verb "to go": its present tense is "go," but its past tense is "went"—these words come from entirely different Old English verbs. Similarly, in Latin, fer (from ferre) and lat (from latus) are treated as part of the same verb system despite their distinct origins.

  • fer serves as the present stem, used in words related to ongoing action or state.
  • lat serves as the past participle stem, often conveying a sense of having been carried, brought, or borne, or a completed action.

Example Derivatives and Their Meanings:

Let's break down the example derivatives provided and see how the meaning of fer/lat is integrated:

  1. Transfer:

    • trans- (prefix) meaning "across" or "beyond."
    • fer meaning "to carry" or "to bring."
    • Meaning: To carry or move something from one place to another; to convey across. (e.g., "transfer money," "transfer a student to another school").

  2. Prefer:

    • pre- (prefix) meaning "before" or "in front of."
    • fer meaning "to carry" or "to bring."
    • Meaning: Literally, to "carry before" or "bring before" others; hence, to like something better than another, to choose as more desirable. (e.g., "I prefer coffee to tea").

  3. Translate:

    • trans- (prefix) meaning "across" or "over."
    • lat (past participle stem) meaning "carried" or "borne."
    • Meaning: To "carry across" from one language to another; to express the meaning of words in a different language. It implies the information is carried over to a new form. (e.g., "translate a book from Latin to English").

  4. Relate:

    • re- (prefix) meaning "back" or "again."
    • lat (past participle stem) meaning "carried" or "borne."
    • Meaning: To "carry back" or "bring back" information or a connection; to show or establish a connection between things; to tell a story or give an account. (e.g., "How do these two concepts relate?", "She will relate her experiences").

Understanding the roots fer and lat and recognizing their suppletive relationship not only unlocks the meanings of these specific words but also provides a framework for understanding many other English words (e.g., refer, infer, defer, confer, suffer, differ, **elate, collate, dilatory, oblate). By knowing the basic building blocks, you can often deduce the meaning of unfamiliar words, significantly expanding your vocabulary and comprehension.
