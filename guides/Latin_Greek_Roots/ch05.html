Study Guide: Chapter 5 — VIS/VID (To See)

Overview

Chapter 5 introduces the Latin root vis / vid, which means "to see." It comes from Latin videre (to see) and its past participle visus. For the Romans, seeing was far more than a physical act — to see something clearly was to understand it and grasp its truth. This chapter explores how the root for seeing branched into words about vision, proof, oversight, and even a modern technology called "video." It also completes the communication chain begun in Chapters 1–4.

Key Concepts

  • Root meaning and origin: "vis" comes from the Latin past participle visus; "vid" comes from the verb stem videre (to see)
  • How the root appears: As "vis" in words like visible, vision, visual, supervise, revise; as "vid" in words like evident, video, provide
  • Important morpheme patterns: Prefixes create directional meanings — super- (over), re- (again), pro- (ahead), e- (out)

Important Terms

  • visible: able to be seen (vis + -ible)
  • vision: the ability to see; a mental image of the future (vis + -ion)
  • visual: relating to sight or seeing (vis + -ual)
  • evident: clearly seen; obvious; standing out so plainly it cannot be denied (e- + vid + -ent)
  • video: a recording of moving images; Latin for "I see" (vid + -eo)
  • supervise: to watch over and direct the work of others (super- + vis)
  • revise: to look at again in order to correct or improve (re- + vis)
  • provide: to see ahead and prepare; to supply what is needed (pro- + vid)

Detailed Notes

The Root in Roman Courts

In a Roman courtroom, the judge demanded evidentia — proof so clear it could be seen by everyone. The connection between seeing and knowing was deeply embedded in Roman thought: to see something plainly was to understand it beyond doubt. This is why the root vis/vid generated not just words about physical sight, but also words about proof and foresight.

Two Root Forms

This chapter introduces a root with two distinct forms from the same Latin verb videre:

  • vis: from the past participle visus — appears in visible, vision, visual, supervise, revise
  • vid: from the verb stem videre — appears in evident, video, provide

Both forms mean the same thing: to see.

Word Family Analysis

Prefixes direct the act of seeing in specific ways:

  • super- (over/above): supervise = to see over; to watch from above and direct
  • re- (again): revise = to see again; to look at one's work a second time to improve it
  • pro- (ahead/before): provide = to see ahead; to foresee needs and prepare for them
  • e- (out): evident = seen out clearly; so visible it stands out without question

Etymology Note: Video

The word video was borrowed directly from Latin in the 20th century. It is the first-person singular present tense of videre — literally "I see." So every time someone watches a video, the name itself is announcing in two-thousand-year-old Latin: I see!

Completing the Communication Chain

With this chapter, the major communication roots are complete:

  • voc (Ch. 1): the voice that calls — output
  • dict (Ch. 2): the words spoken — output
  • aud (Ch. 3): the ears that hear — input
  • scrib/script (Ch. 4): the hand that writes — output
  • vis/vid (Ch. 5): the eyes that see — input

The roots divide naturally into information-sending (voc, dict, scrib) and information-receiving (aud, vis) roots.

Review Questions

  1. Why did the Romans associate "seeing" with "knowing"? Use the word evident to support your answer.
  2. What does the word video literally mean in Latin, and why is that an interesting name for the technology?
  3. Explain the difference between revise and provide by analyzing their prefixes.
  4. A supervisor "sees over" others. How does this root-based meaning compare to the job of a supervisor in a school or workplace?
  5. How do the roots aud (Ch. 3) and vis (Ch. 5) function as "input" roots, as opposed to voc, dict, and scrib?
