Study Guide: Chapter 11 — CED / CEDE / CESS

Overview

Chapter 11 introduces the Latin root ced / cede / cess, from cedere, meaning "to go" or "to yield." Students learn how Roman political life — full of advancing arguments, retreating armies, and yielding senators — generated a versatile family of English words about movement and concession.

Key Concepts

  • Root meaning: ced/cede/cess = to go, to yield (Latin cedere)
  • Three spelling variants: ced (some compounds), cede (verb endings), cess (from past participle cessus)
  • Double meaning: The root captures both moving forward/back AND giving way/surrendering — two types of motion
  • Prefix patterns: Direction is controlled by prefixes: pro- (forward), re- (back), ex- (beyond), con- (together/intensifying), ac-/ad- (toward), suc- (after)

Important Terms

  • proceed: to go forward; to continue (pro- + ceed)
  • recede: to go back; to move away or withdraw (re- + cede)
  • concede: to admit something is true; to surrender a point (con- + cede)
  • succeed: to achieve a goal; to come after and accomplish (suc- + ceed)
  • exceed: to go beyond a limit; to surpass (ex- + ceed)
  • access: the ability to approach or enter; a way in (ac-/ad- + cess)
  • predecessor: a person who held a position before the current holder (prae- + de- + cess + -or)
  • process: a series of steps leading to a result; a going forward (pro- + cess)

Detailed Notes

Roman Politics: Advance and Retreat

In Rome, the verb cedere described both physical movement and political behavior. A general's army would cede (withdraw) after a defeat. A senator who lost a debate would cedere — step aside, give way. This dual meaning of movement and yielding gave English an unusually rich word family.

The Two Forms: -cede and -cess

This root appears in two main patterns:

  • -cede / -ceed words (from the present stem): proceed, recede, concede, succeed, exceed
  • -cess words (from the past participle cessus): access, process, predecessor, recess, excess

Noticing which form a word uses can help confirm its meaning.

The Prefix as a Compass

With this root, the prefix tells you which direction the "going" happens:

  • pro-ceed: go forward
  • re-cede: go back
  • ex-ceed: go beyond
  • ac-cess: go toward
  • suc-ceed: go after (achieve what comes next)
  • con-cede: yield completely

Predecessor vs. Successor

These two words are antonyms built from the same root with opposite prefixes:

  • Predecessor = prae- (before) + cess (go) → went before you
  • Successor = suc- (after) + cess (go) → comes after you

Process: Going Forward Step by Step

A process (pro- + cess) is not a single action but a going forward in stages — a systematic sequence of steps that advances toward a goal. This meaning perfectly captures the sense of movement embedded in the root.

Review Questions

  1. The root cedere means both "to go" and "to yield." Can you think of a situation in real life where going and yielding are connected?
  2. Using what you know about prefixes, explain the difference between proceed, recede, and exceed.
  3. What is the difference between a predecessor and a successor? How do their prefixes tell you which is which?
  4. How does the word access relate to the idea of going or moving? What does "a going toward" mean in practical terms?
  5. The chapter says "language, like life, is always in motion." What does the author mean by this, and how does the root ced/cede/cess illustrate it?
