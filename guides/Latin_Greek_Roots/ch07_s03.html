Study Guide: The Root Story of "Knowing"

Overview

This section explores the fascinating linguistic journey of how English acquired its rich vocabulary related to "knowing." It details a double inheritance from two ancient languages, Latin and and Greek, both of which developed words for "knowing" from a shared Indo-European ancestor. This dual heritage has resulted in distinct sets of English words—Latin-derived words for everyday and legal contexts, and Greek-derived words primarily for scientific and medical use.

Key Concepts

  • Double Inheritance of Knowledge Vocabulary: English has inherited words related to "knowing" from both Latin (e.g., cognition) and Greek (e.g., diagnosis), creating a rich and varied vocabulary.
  • Shared Indo-European Root: Both the Latin verb gnoscere and the Greek verb gignoskein originate from a common, ancient Indo-European root meaning "to know," highlighting a deep linguistic connection between these languages.
  • Latin Derivations (cogn-): Words derived from Latin cognoscere (meaning "to get to know" or "to recognize") often relate to general awareness, thought processes, and legal concepts in English (e.g., cognizant, recognize).
  • Greek Derivations (gnosis): Words derived from Greek gignoskein (meaning "to know" or "to perceive") are predominantly found in scientific and medical terminology in English (e.g., diagnosis, prognosis).
  • Distinct Usage Domains: Despite their shared ancient origin, the Latin and Greek derivatives for "knowing" have evolved into English words used in different specific contexts, demonstrating how ancient roots can diversify.

Important Terms

  • Cognition: The mental process of acquiring knowledge and understanding through thought, experience, and the senses. It is the process of knowing or thinking.
  • Cognizant: Having knowledge or being aware of something. To be "cognizant of" a situation means to be thoroughly aware of it.
  • Recognize: To identify someone or something previously known; to "know again." It involves connecting a present perception to a past memory.
  • Incognito: (From Latin in- "not" + cognitus "known") With one's true identity concealed or hidden. Traveling incognito means going "unknown."
  • Diagnosis: (From Greek dia- "through" + gnosis "knowing") The process of identifying the nature or cause of a disease or problem by examining symptoms and reasoning through them. Literally, a "knowing through."
  • Prognosis: (From Greek pro- "before" + gnosis "knowing") A forecast or prediction of the likely course of a disease or a situation, often predicting how an illness will progress. Literally, a "knowing before."
  • Indo-European Root: A hypothetical ancestral language from which many modern languages, including Latin, Greek, English, and others, are believed to have descended.
  • Latin cognoscere: The Latin verb meaning "to get to know" or "to recognize," formed from the prefix co- (together, thoroughly) and gnoscere (to know).
  • Greek gignoskein: The Greek verb meaning "to know" or "to perceive."
  • Etymology: The study of the origin and historical development of words, tracing them back to their earliest known forms and meanings.

Detailed Notes

The Double Heritage of "Knowing"

English is a language rich with vocabulary drawn from many sources, and the words we use for "knowing" offer a prime example of this diversity. This section highlights a unique "double inheritance" where English has acquired words for knowledge from two distinct ancient languages: Latin and Greek. While seemingly separate, these languages' words for "knowing" actually share a very ancient common ancestor.

The Root Story: An Ancient Connection

Deep in the history of language lies an Indo-European root that meant "to know." This single root branched out and evolved differently in various language families. In the Romance family, it led to the Latin verb gnoscere (to know), which combined with the prefix co- (together, thoroughly) to form cognoscere ("to get to know" or "to recognize"). Meanwhile, in the Hellenic family, it developed into the Greek verb gignoskein ("to know," "to perceive"). This common ancestry underscores a fundamental connection between these two powerful linguistic contributors to English.

The Latin Legacy: Everyday Knowing (cogn-)

From the Latin side of this heritage, English receives many words related to general awareness, thought processes, and legal recognition. These words often stem from the cogn- root:

  • Cognition: This noun refers to the broad process of knowing or thinking. When we talk about cognitive abilities, we're discussing the mental processes involved in understanding and learning.
  • Cognizant: An adjective meaning "aware" or "having come to know." If you are cognizant of the risks, you fully understand them.
  • Recognize: This verb literally means "to know again." It's formed from the Latin prefix re- (again) and cognoscere (to know). When you recognize a face, your mind connects it to a past memory.
  • Incognito: This word, which entered English from Italian but ultimately derives from Latin in- (not) + cognitus (known), means "unknown." To travel incognito is to hide one's true identity. It's one of the few English words that still fully preserves the original Latin root cognit.

Latin-derived words like these typically appear in everyday language, discussions about mental faculties, and legal contexts.

The Greek Legacy: Scientific and Medical Knowing (gnosis)

From the Greek side, English inherits words related to "knowing" that are primarily used in scientific and medical fields. These words often incorporate the gnosis root:

  • Diagnosis: Formed from the Greek prefix dia- (through) and gnosis (knowing), a diagnosis is literally a "knowing through." It describes the process by which a doctor examines symptoms and reasons through them to identify a specific disease or condition.
  • Prognosis: This term comes from the Greek prefix pro- (before) and gnosis (knowing), meaning a "knowing before." It refers to a prediction about the likely future course of an illness or condition, offering an outlook on how it will progress.

These Greek-derived terms are standard vocabulary in medicine and scientific research, emphasizing a more specialized, analytical form of knowing.

A Partnership in English

The coexistence of Latin-derived cogn- words and Greek-derived gnosis words in English demonstrates a fundamental aspect of the language's development: its ability to draw extensively from multiple ancient sources. While the Latin forms tend to populate general discourse and legal terminology, the Greek forms are reserved for more technical and precise scientific and medical applications. This section serves as a foundational example of how Latin and Greek roots work side-by-side in English, a partnership that will be further explored with many more Greek roots in later studies.
