Study Guide: The FER / LAT Word Family

Overview

This section explores the word family built on the Latin root fer/lat (to carry, to bear, to bring). Because this root is a suppletive pair — with the present stem fer and past participle stem lat coming from different original words — the family is unusually diverse. Words like transfer and translate, or prefer and relate, are actually etymological siblings despite their different appearances.

Key Concepts

  • The Root fer / lat: From Latin ferre (to carry). Present stem derivatives use fer; past participle derivatives use lat.
  • Two Faces of One Root: The fer branch includes transfer, prefer, fertile, different, and suffer. The lat branch includes translate, relate, and legislate.
  • Semantic Range: The meaning of "carrying" extends to physical transport (transfer), emotional endurance (suffer), biological productivity (fertile), intellectual distinction (different), and linguistic conversion (translate).

Important Terms

Transfer: To move something from one place or person to another. Prefer: To like one thing better than another; to choose first. Fertile: Capable of producing abundant growth or offspring. Different: Not the same; distinct from something else. Suffer: To experience pain or hardship; to bear a burden. Translate: To express meaning in another language. Relate: To tell or narrate; to show a connection between things. Legislate: To make or enact laws.

Detailed Notes

Word-by-Word Analysis

  • Transfer

    • Etymology: trans- (across) + fer (to carry)
    • Meaning: To move something from one place or person to another. The most literal use of the root — carrying something across.
    • Example: She decided to transfer to a different school.

  • Prefer

    • Etymology: pre- (before) + fer (to carry)
    • Meaning: To like one thing better than another. Literally, to carry one choice before (ahead of) another.
    • Example: I prefer tea to coffee.

  • Fertile

    • Etymology: fer (to bear) + -tile (able to)
    • Meaning: Capable of producing abundant growth or offspring. The "bearing" sense of the root — able to bear fruit or young.
    • Example: The valley's fertile soil supports a wide variety of crops.

  • Different

    • Etymology: dif- (apart) + fer (to carry) + -ent (adjective suffix)
    • Meaning: Not the same; distinct. Literally, "carried apart" — separated from something else.
    • Example: The twins look alike but have very different personalities.

  • Suffer

    • Etymology: suf- (under, from below) + fer (to carry)
    • Meaning: To experience pain or hardship. Literally, to carry a burden from underneath — to bear up under difficulty.
    • Example: The refugees suffered greatly during their journey.

  • Translate

    • Etymology: trans- (across) + lat (carried) + -e
    • Meaning: To express meaning in another language. Literally, to carry meaning across from one language to another.
    • Example: The diplomat was asked to translate the speech into three languages.

  • Relate

    • Etymology: re- (back) + lat (carried) + -e
    • Meaning: To tell or narrate; to show a connection between things. Literally, to carry back — to bring back a story or connection.
    • Example: The witness was asked to relate what she had seen.

  • Legislate

    • Etymology: leg(is)- (law) + lat (to carry) + -e
    • Meaning: To make or enact laws. Literally, to carry or propose law.
    • Example: Congress must legislate to address the issue.

Review Questions

  1. How do transfer (fer branch) and translate (lat branch) both express the idea of "carrying across," despite looking very different?
  2. Compare prefer and suffer. Both use the fer stem, but how do their prefixes create opposite emotional associations?
  3. Explain how the word different connects to the idea of "carrying." What is being "carried apart"?
  4. How does legislate combine two Latin roots? What does each root contribute to the meaning?
  5. Why is the concept of a suppletive pair important for vocabulary study? How does knowing about fer/lat help you recognize more words?
