Study Guide: Chapter 13 — TERR

Overview

Chapter 13 introduces the Latin root terr / terra, meaning "earth" or "land." Students learn how the Romans' deep connection to land — through conquest, agriculture, civic life, and burial — gave English a family of words about the ground beneath our feet, from territories to terracotta pots.

Key Concepts

  • Root meaning: terr/terra = earth, land, ground (Latin terra)
  • How the root appears: terr appears in territory, terrain, subterranean; terra appears in Mediterranean, terrace, terra firma, terracotta
  • Key prefix pattern: sub- (under) creates subterranean; medi- (middle) creates Mediterranean; in- (into) creates inter
  • Connected chapter: Chapter 14 (aqua) also uses the prefix sub- in the same pattern: subterranean (under earth) / subaqueous (under water)

Important Terms

  • territory: an area of land under the control of a ruler or state (terr + -itory)
  • terrain: a stretch of land, especially regarding its physical features (terr + -ain)
  • subterranean: existing or occurring beneath the earth's surface (sub- + terr + -anean)
  • Mediterranean: the sea in the middle of the lands (medi- + terr + -anean)
  • terrestrial: relating to the earth or land, as opposed to water or air (terr + -estrial)
  • terrace: a raised, level area of ground; an outdoor platform (terr + -ace)
  • inter: to bury a dead body in the earth (in- + terra)

Detailed Notes

Land and Power in Rome

Roman civilization was built on land. A citizen's wealth was measured in the terra he owned. When Roman generals conquered new regions, they surveyed the land immediately, turning it into territory — land belonging to the state. The word territory literally means "the land belonging to" a city or ruler.

Above and Below: Terrestrial and Subterranean

The Romans distinguished between the world above ground (terrestrial — of the earth's surface) and the world below (subterranean — under the earth). Beneath Rome ran tunnels, catacombs, and aqueduct channels carved through stone — a hidden, subterranean world parallel to the one above.

The Mediterranean: A Sea Defined by Its Surrounding Land

The Romans named their central sea not for its water, but for the land around it. Mare Mediterraneum = "the sea in the middle of the lands." This naming shows how central the concept of terra was to Roman thinking — even the sea was defined in terms of land.

From Burial to Terraces

The word inter (to bury) = in + terra: to put into the earth. The Romans placed their dead in the ground, returning them to the terra from which all life came. At the other end of earthly experience, a terrace is a piece of earth shaped for human use — leveled, raised, and opened to the sky.

Terracotta and Terra Firma

Two Latin phrases still in everyday English:

  • Terracotta (Italian terra cotta = baked earth): the reddish-brown clay material used for pots and sculptures — literally earth that has been fired
  • Terra firma (Latin = firm/solid ground): used to describe the stability of dry land, especially in contrast to the sea

Review Questions

  1. Roman soldiers created territories wherever they conquered. Based on the root meaning, what does a territory literally claim about the land?
  2. How does the word Mediterranean use the root terr to describe a sea rather than land?
  3. Compare the words subterranean and terrestrial. How do they describe opposite regions of the same earth?
  4. The word inter means to bury. What does this tell us about how the Romans thought about death and the earth?
  5. Can you think of any modern words or phrases that still use terra in their full Latin form (like terra firma or terracotta)?
