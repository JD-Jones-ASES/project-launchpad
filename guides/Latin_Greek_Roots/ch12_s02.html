Study Guide: The Latin Root fer/lat (To Carry, To Bear, To Bring)

Overview

This section introduces the Latin root fer/lat, which means "to carry," "to bear," or "to bring." It highlights a unique linguistic feature called suppletion, where the present stem fer and the past participle stem lat originate from different Latin words but function as a single root in English derivatives. Understanding this root is key to deciphering the meanings of many common English words.

Key Concepts

  • Core Meaning of fer/lat: The root primarily conveys the actions of moving something from one place to another, enduring something, or presenting information.
  • Latin Origin: The root is derived from the Latin verb ferre (to carry) and its past participle latus (carried).
  • Suppletive Pair: fer and lat constitute a suppletive pair, meaning their different forms (present vs. past participle) come from distinct, historically unrelated words. This is a common phenomenon in language evolution.
  • Variant Forms: fer is typically used for present-tense derivations, while lat is used for past participle derivations.
  • Formation of English Derivatives: This root combines with various prefixes and suffixes to form a vast array of English words, often retaining its core meaning of carrying, bearing, or bringing.

Important Terms

  • Root: The fundamental part of a word that carries its primary meaning.
  • Etymology: The study of the origin of words and the way their meanings have changed throughout history.
  • Latin: An ancient Italic language that was originally spoken in Latium, the region surrounding Rome. It is the language of origin for a significant portion of English vocabulary.
  • Suppletion/Suppletive Pair: A linguistic phenomenon where inflectional forms of a word (like different tenses or cases) are derived from different and historically unrelated stems. For example, "go" and "went" in English are a suppletive pair. In this case, fer and lat act as the present and past participle stems of what functions as a single root.
  • Present Stem (fer): The form of the root typically used to construct words describing an ongoing or present action of carrying, bearing, or bringing.
  • Past Participle Stem (lat): The form of the root typically used to construct words describing an action of carrying, bearing, or bringing that has been completed or is in a passive state.
  • Derivative: A word formed from another word or root, especially by the addition of a prefix or suffix.

Detailed Notes

The Latin Root fer/lat: Meaning and Origin

The root fer/lat is a powerful building block in English vocabulary, tracing its origins directly back to Latin. Its core meaning encompasses the actions "to carry," "to bear," and "to bring." This broad meaning allows it to contribute to words describing physical movement, enduring hardship, or conveying information.

  • Origin: The fer part comes from the Latin verb ferre, meaning "to carry" or "to bear." The lat part comes from the Latin past participle latus, meaning "carried" or "borne."

Understanding Suppletion: fer and lat as a Suppletive Pair

A key aspect of this root is its status as a suppletive pair. This means that fer and lat are variant forms of the same underlying concept ("to carry/bear"), but they are derived from different, historically unrelated words in Latin.

  • fer (Present Stem): This form is generally used when constructing words that imply an ongoing action or a state of carrying, bringing, or bearing.
  • lat (Past Participle Stem): This form is generally used when constructing words that imply something has been carried, been brought, or been borne, often suggesting a completed action or a passive state.

Think of it like the English verb "go," whose past tense is "went." "Go" and "went" do not share the same phonetic root, but they function as different forms of the same verb. Similarly, fer and lat provide different "tenses" or aspects for the action of carrying within derived English words.

Examples of Derivatives and Their Meanings

Understanding the core meaning and the suppletive nature of fer/lat helps unlock the meanings of many English words:

Derivatives from fer (Present Stem):

  • Transfer: (trans- meaning "across") + fer = to carry or move across. (e.g., transfer money, transfer a student to another school)
  • Prefer: (prae- meaning "before") + fer = to carry or place before others; to choose. (e.g., I prefer tea over coffee)
  • Refer: (re- meaning "back" or "again") + fer = to carry back to; to direct attention to; to consult. (e.g., refer to a dictionary, refer a patient to a specialist)
  • Confer: (con- meaning "together") + fer = to carry together; to consult or discuss; to grant a title or degree. (e.g., to confer with colleagues, to confer a degree)
  • Defer: (de- meaning "down" or "away") + fer = to carry down; to postpone; to yield to another's opinion. (e.g., to defer a payment, to defer to an elder's wisdom)
  • Infer: (in- meaning "into") + fer = to carry into; to deduce or conclude from evidence. (e.g., to infer meaning from clues)
  • Suffer: (sub- meaning "under") + fer = to bear or carry under; to undergo hardship or pain. (e.g., to suffer from an illness)

Derivatives from lat (Past Participle Stem):

  • Translate: (trans- meaning "across") + lat = to have carried across; to render from one language into another. (e.g., to translate a book)
  • Relate: (re- meaning "back" or "again") + lat = to have carried back; to tell a story; to connect or show a relationship between things. (e.g., to relate an experience, how are these ideas related?)
  • Elate: (e- or ex- meaning "out") + lat = to have carried out; to make someone ecstatically happy. (e.g., The good news elated him)
  • Collate: (con- meaning "together") + lat = to have carried together; to assemble or arrange in proper order. (e.g., to collate pages of a manuscript)
  • Dilatory: (dis- meaning "apart" or "away") + lat = tending to carry away; causing delay. (e.g., a dilatory tactic)

By understanding the root fer/lat and its fascinating suppletive nature, students can effectively expand their vocabulary and grasp the underlying logic behind many English words.

Review Questions

  4. Can you think of any other English words not listed in this section that contain fer or lat? How do their meanings connect to "to carry, to bear, or to bring"?
