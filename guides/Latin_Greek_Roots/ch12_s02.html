Study Guide: Understanding the Root "fer / lat"

Overview

This section profiles the Latin root fer/lat, derived from the verb ferre, meaning "to carry, to bear, to bring." What makes this root unique is that it is a suppletive pair — the present stem (fer) and the past participle stem (lat) come from different original words. This means two seemingly unrelated sets of English words actually share the same Latin verb. Recognizing both forms dramatically expands vocabulary comprehension.

Key Concepts

  • Core Meaning of fer / lat: To carry, to bear, to bring. This meaning covers physical carrying, enduring hardship, and producing results.
  • Suppletive Pair: The present stem fer (from ferre) and the past participle stem lat (from latus, meaning "carried") come from historically different words that merged in Latin. This is similar to English "go/went."
  • Latin Origin: From the Latin verb ferre (to carry), with its past participle latus (carried).
  • Two Families in One: Words built on fer (transfer, prefer, fertile) and words built on lat (translate, relate, legislate) all trace back to the same Latin verb.

Important Terms

  • fer (Latin root): The present stem meaning "to carry, to bear, to bring."
  • lat (Latin root): The past participle stem meaning "carried, borne, brought."
  • Suppletive pair: A linguistic phenomenon where different grammatical forms of the same word come from historically different roots (e.g., ferre/latus in Latin; go/went in English).

Detailed Notes

The Root fer — "To Carry" (Present Stem)

The fer form appears in many common English words where the action of carrying is ongoing or active:

  • transfer: to carry across
  • prefer: to carry before (to favor one over another)
  • fertile: able to bear (capable of producing)
  • suffer: to carry under (to endure hardship)
  • different: carried apart (not the same)

The Root lat — "Carried" (Past Participle Stem)

The lat form appears in words where the carrying has been completed or is described as a state:

  • translate: to carry across (to express in another language)
  • relate: to carry back (to tell or show connection)
  • legislate: to carry law (to make or enact laws)

Why This Matters

Students who learn only the fer form miss half the family. Recognizing that translate and transfer share the same root — both meaning "to carry across" — deepens understanding of both words and reveals hidden connections throughout English vocabulary.

Review Questions

  1. What is a suppletive pair, and how does the fer/lat root exemplify this concept?
  2. Why do transfer and translate have essentially the same etymological meaning despite looking so different?
  3. How does the word suffer connect to the idea of "carrying"? What does the prefix sub- contribute?
  4. Explain the relationship between fertile and the root fer. What is being "carried" or "borne"?
  5. Give an example of a suppletive pair in English (not Latin) and explain how it is similar to fer/lat.
