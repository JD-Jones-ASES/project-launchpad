Study Guide: Understanding the Root "ced / cede / cess"

Overview

This section profiles the Latin root ced/cede/cess, derived from the verb cedere, meaning "to go" or "to yield." This root is highly productive in English, forming the basis for words related to movement, progress, withdrawal, and concession. Its three variant forms reflect different grammatical stems of the original Latin verb, making it a valuable example of how Latin morphology shapes English vocabulary.

Key Concepts

  • Core Meaning of ced / cede / cess: The root carries a dual meaning: "to go" (indicating movement or progression) and "to yield" (indicating surrender or withdrawal). Both senses appear across its derivatives.
  • Three Variant Forms: ced appears in some compounds, cede appears in verb endings (from the Latin infinitive cedere), and cess comes from the past participle cessus.
  • Latin Origin: From the Latin verb cedere (to go, to yield, to withdraw), a fundamental verb of motion and concession.
  • Dual Semantic Threads: Some derivatives emphasize movement (proceed, recede), while others emphasize yielding (concede, access).

Important Terms

  • ced / cede (Latin root): To go, to yield; from the present stem of Latin cedere.
  • cess (variant): From the past participle cessus, meaning "having gone" or "having yielded."
  • Past participle stem: The form of a Latin verb used to build nouns, adjectives, and passive constructions — often different in spelling from the present stem.

Detailed Notes

The Root ced/cede/cess — "To Go, To Yield"

The Latin verb cedere could mean both "to go, to move" and "to yield, to give way." This duality persists in English: proceed emphasizes going forward, while concede emphasizes yielding a point.

Understanding the Three Variants

  • ced: A shortened form appearing in some compounds, often before consonants
  • cede: The full verb form, used when the root stands at the end of a word (re-cede, con-cede, pre-cede)
  • cess: The past participle form, typically appearing in nouns and adjectives (ac-cess, pro-cess, suc-cess)

Key Derivatives

  • proceed (pro-ceed): to go forward — to move ahead or continue
  • recede (re-cede): to go back — to withdraw or become more distant
  • concede (con-cede): to yield completely — to admit truth or surrender a point
  • access (ac-cess): a going toward — the ability to approach or enter

The "Go" vs. "Yield" Distinction

Understanding which sense applies helps with comprehension:

  • Movement words: proceed (go forward), recede (go back), exceed (go beyond)
  • Yielding words: concede (yield a point), accede (yield to a request)
  • Both senses: succeed can mean "go after" (follow) or imply that effort has yielded results

Review Questions

  1. What are the two core meanings of the Latin root ced/cede/cess, and how do these meanings appear in different English words?
  2. Explain the three variant forms of this root and give an example word for each variant.
  3. How does the word recede illustrate the "to go" meaning of the root?
  4. Compare proceed and concede. One emphasizes movement, the other yielding — explain how.
  5. The word successor contains the cess variant. How does the root meaning "to go" help explain what a successor is?
