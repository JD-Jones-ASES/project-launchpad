Chapter 6, Section 4: Exploring the Word Family of 'Sens/Sent'

Overview

This section introduces the Latin root sens/sent, which means "to feel" or "to perceive." It explores how this root not only encompasses all five physical senses but also extends into the realms of judgment, opinion, and intellectual understanding. By comparing sens/sent with other roots like vis/vid (to see), we gain a deeper appreciation for the breadth and nuance that sens/sent brings to a wide array of English words.

Key Concepts

  • The Root sens/sent: This Latin root is foundational to many English words and primarily conveys the meaning "to feel" or "to perceive." It signifies both physical sensations and mental processes of understanding.
  • Broad Scope of sens/sent: Unlike roots focused on a single sense, sens/sent is comprehensive. It relates to perception through all senses (touch, taste, smell, hearing, and even sight as part of a whole experience) and expands to cover emotional responses and intellectual discernment.
  • Contrast with vis/vid: While the root vis/vid (from Chapter 5) specifically denotes the act of "seeing," sens/sent offers a broader perspective on "taking in the world." It covers a more internal and often subjective experience of perceiving and understanding, extending beyond mere observation.
  • Extension to Judgment and Opinion: A critical aspect of sens/sent is its connection to making decisions and forming beliefs. The root helps us understand words related to forming rational judgments, expressing opinions, and arriving at conclusions based on gathered information or internal feelings.
  • Word Families and Etymology: Understanding roots like sens/sent is crucial for building vocabulary. Words derived from the same root form a "word family," sharing a common core meaning that helps in inferring the definitions of unfamiliar words and appreciating the intricate connections within the English language.

Important Terms

  • Root: The basic part of a word that carries its primary meaning.
  • Etymology: The study of the origin of words and how their meanings have changed throughout history.
  • Word Family: A group of words that share a common root and, therefore, a related meaning.
  • sens/sent: A Latin root meaning "to feel" or "to perceive"; also extends to "judgment" and "opinion."
  • vis/vid: A Latin root meaning "to see" (introduced in Chapter 5).
  • Perceive: To become aware of something through the senses or to understand or interpret something.
  • Judgment: The ability to make considered decisions or come to sensible conclusions; an opinion or conclusion reached after consideration.
  • Sensation: A physical feeling or perception resulting from something that happens to or comes into contact with the body.
  • Sentiment: A feeling or emotion; an attitude toward something.

Detailed Notes

The Root sens/sent: Feeling and Perceiving the World

The Latin root sens/sent is a powerful component in the English language, giving rise to numerous words that describe how we interact with and understand our surroundings. At its most fundamental, sens/sent means "to feel" or "to perceive." This encompasses both the passive reception of information through our senses and the active process of interpreting and understanding that information.

The Multifaceted Nature of sens/sent

The versatility of sens/sent allows it to appear in words related to different facets of human experience:

  1. Physical Sensation: This is the most direct application. Words in this category relate to the input we receive from our five senses. For example, a sensation is a physical feeling, and our sensory organs are responsible for processing these inputs. If something is sensory, it relates to our senses.
  2. Emotional Experience: The idea of "feeling" extends beyond physical touch to internal states. Words like sentiment describe a feeling or emotion, often linked to a particular thought or attitude. To resent someone means to feel bitterness or indignation towards them.
  3. Intellectual Perception and Judgment: This is where sens/sent truly broadens its scope. It moves beyond mere physical input to the processing and evaluation of information. When we talk about judgment, we're referring to the ability to make a discerning decision or form an opinion. If a decision is sensible, it shows good judgment and practicality. The capacity for feeling and perception, particularly conscious perception, can be described by words like sentient, meaning able to perceive or feel things.

Comparing sens/sent with vis/vid (Chapter 5)

A crucial way to understand the depth of sens/sent is to compare it with roots that have a more specific focus, such as vis/vid from Chapter 5, which means "to see."

  • vis/vid's focus on sight: Words like visual, vision, and invisible all revolve around the act of seeing or being seen. It's a precise interaction with the world through one specific sense.
  • sens/sent's comprehensive approach: In contrast, sens/sent covers a much wider spectrum. While sight is one of the senses, sens/sent encompasses all forms of perception. More significantly, it extends beyond simply receiving sensory input to the internal process of understanding, evaluating, and forming opinions.

The textbook's example perfectly illustrates this distinction: "A judge must both see the evidence [ch05] and feel her way to a sentence."

  • "See the evidence" directly relates to vis/vid – the physical act of observing facts.
  • "Feel her way to a sentence" brings in sens/sent – it's not about physical touch, but about carefully perceiving nuances, weighing facts, applying judgment, and forming a considered opinion or decision (the "sentence" here meaning the judgment or opinion, not just the punishment). This highlights sens/sent's role in abstract thought and ethical decision-making.

Building Vocabulary Through sens/sent

Although the original text did not provide a full list of words from the sens/sent family, understanding the root allows us to deduce the meaning of many words and to generate examples. By knowing that sens/sent means "to feel" or "to perceive," we can connect various words to this core idea:

  • Sense: A faculty by which the body perceives an external stimulus (e.g., sense of touch); also, practical intelligence or good judgment.
  • Sensible: (adj.) Having or showing good sense or judgment; (of a garment or shoe) practical and comfortable rather than fashionable.
  • Sensitive: (adj.) Quick to detect or respond to slight changes, signals, or influences; easily hurt or offended.
  • Sensation: (n.) A physical feeling or perception; a widespread reaction of interest and excitement.
  • Consent: (v.) To give permission for something to happen; (n.) Permission for something to happen. (literally, to "feel with" or agree)
  • Dissent: (v.) To hold or express opinions that are at variance with those previously, commonly, or officially expressed; (n.) The expression or holding of opinions contrary to those previously, commonly, or officially expressed. (literally, to "feel apart" or disagree)
  • Resent: (v.) To feel bitterness or indignation at (a circumstance, action, or person). (literally, to "feel again" in a negative way)
  • Sentiment: (n.) A feeling or opinion, especially one based on emotion rather than reason.
  • Sentient: (adj.) Able to perceive or feel things.
  • Presentiment: (n.) An intuitive feeling about the future, especially one of an unwelcome nature. (literally, a "feeling before")
  • Sensor: (n.) A device that detects or measures a physical property and records, indicates, or otherwise responds to it.
  • Sensory: (adj.) Relating to sensation or the physical senses.

By recognizing the sens/sent root in these words, you can quickly grasp their core meaning and appreciate how prefixes (like con-, dis-, re-, pre-) and suffixes (-ible, -itive, -ation, -ment, -ient, -or, -ory) modify the root's meaning to create a rich and diverse vocabulary.
