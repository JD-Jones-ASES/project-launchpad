Study Guide: Word Family - sens/sent

Overview

This section explores the word family stemming from the Latin root sens/sent, meaning "to feel" or "to perceive." It demonstrates how this core meaning expands through various prefixes and suffixes to create words related to our senses, emotions, agreement, and even judgment. The section also contrasts sens/sent with the root vis/vid (to see), highlighting the broader scope of sens/sent in encompassing all forms of perception and internal feeling.

Key Concepts

  • The Root sens/sent: This core root primarily means "to feel" or "to perceive." It's fundamental to understanding the entire word family.
  • Expansion of Meaning: The root's meaning extends beyond physical sensation to include judgment, opinion, and agreement.
  • Role of Prefixes and Suffixes: Prefixes like con- (together), dis- (apart), and as-/ad- (toward) significantly alter the root's meaning to indicate agreement, disagreement, or direction. Suffixes like -ory (relating to), -itive (having the quality of), and -ment (result of) specify the nature or outcome of the feeling/perception.
  • Distinction from vis/vid: While both roots deal with "taking in the world," vis/vid is specifically tied to sight. sens/sent, however, covers all five senses, as well as extending into abstract concepts like judgment and opinion, making it a much broader root.

Important Terms

  • sense : A faculty of perception (sight, hearing, touch, taste, smell); also, good judgment or reason.
  • sensory : Relating to the senses or sensation.
  • sensitive : Easily affected by feelings, emotions, or physical stimuli.
  • consent : To agree; to feel together with another person or party.
  • dissent : To disagree; to hold a different opinion from others.
  • assent : To agree to something; to express approval, often formally.
  • sentiment : A feeling, emotion, opinion, or attitude towards a situation or person.
  • sentence : A complete thought expressed in words; also, a judge's declared punishment for a convicted person.

Detailed Notes

The Latin root sens/sent is a powerful foundation for understanding a wide array of English words. Its core meaning, "to feel" or "to perceive," forms the basis for all the words in this family, but it's important to recognize how this meaning evolves and expands.

The Core Meaning and Its Evolution

Initially, sens/sent refers to direct physical perception, such as feeling cold or perceiving a sound. This is evident in words like sense (our faculties of perception) and sensory (relating to those perceptions). However, the root quickly branches out to encompass internal feelings (emotions), opinions, and even intellectual judgment. This expansion is crucial for understanding the diversity of this word family.

Exploring the sens/sent Word Family

  1. sense (sens + -e): This word directly represents the root's primary meaning. It refers to our five biological faculties for perceiving the world (sight, hearing, smell, taste, touch). Beyond that, "sense" also denotes good judgment or practical wisdom, showing the leap from physical perception to intellectual discernment. If you "make sense," you are perceiving things correctly and logically.

  2. sensory (sens + -ory): The suffix -ory means "relating to." So, sensory describes anything connected to our senses or the act of sensation. Think of "sensory input" (information from our senses) or "sensory organs" (eyes, ears, skin, etc.).

  3. sensitive (sens + -itive): The suffix -itive indicates "having the quality of." Thus, sensitive means having the quality of feeling or perceiving easily. This can apply physically (sensitive skin reacts easily to irritants) or emotionally (a sensitive person is easily affected by feelings).

  4. consent (con- + sent): Here, the prefix con- means "together" or "with." When you consent, you "feel together" or "agree with" another person's request or proposal. It implies a shared feeling or mutual agreement.

  5. dissent (dis- + sent): The prefix dis- means "apart" or "away." Therefore, to dissent means to "feel apart" or "feel differently" from others. It's the act of disagreeing or holding an opposing opinion.

  6. assent (as-/ad- + sent): The prefix as-/ad- means "toward." To assent is to "feel toward" an idea or proposal, signifying agreement or approval. While similar to consent, assent often implies a more formal or explicit expression of approval.

  7. sentiment (sent + -i- + -ment): The suffix -ment indicates the "result of" an action or state. Sentiment refers to a developed feeling, opinion, or attitude that results from one's perceptions or thoughts. It's often a more enduring or nuanced feeling than a simple emotion.

  8. sentence (sent + -ence): The suffix -ence signifies a "state of" or "quality." A sentence as a complete thought can be seen as a "state of feeling" or conveying a complete perception in words. The judicial meaning of sentence (a declared punishment) also connects to judgment, a higher form of perception and decision-making.

Root Connection: sens/sent vs. vis/vid

It's insightful to compare sens/sent with the root vis/vid from Chapter 5, which means "to see." Both roots are about "taking in the world," but their scope differs significantly:

  • vis/vid (to see): This root is narrow and specific to the sense of sight. Words like vision, visual, invisible, video all relate directly to what can be seen or observed with the eyes.
  • sens/sent (to feel, perceive): This root is much broader. It encompasses all five physical senses (sight, hearing, touch, taste, smell) and extends into abstract concepts like:
    • Emotional feeling: sentiment, sensitive.
    • Judgment and opinion: sense (good judgment), consent, dissent, assent, sentence (judicial).

The textbook provides an excellent example: "A judge must both see the evidence and feel her way to a sentence." This highlights the difference. The judge sees (vis/vid) the factual evidence presented in court. However, to arrive at a sentence (sens/sent), she must feel (sens/sent) her way through the nuances, consider various factors, and exercise her judgment and perception of fairness and justice. This demonstrates how sens/sent covers a deeper, more internal, and comprehensive form of understanding than vis/vid.

Review Questions

  1. What is the primary meaning of the root sens/sent? How does this meaning expand beyond basic physical perception into more abstract concepts?
  2. Choose three words from the sens/sent word family (e.g., consent, dissent, assent). Explain how their prefixes contribute to their specific meanings, building upon the root's core sense.
  3. How do the words sensory and sensitive differ in meaning, even though both relate to the root sens? What role do their respective suffixes play in these differences?
  4. Explain the key distinction between the roots sens/sent and vis/vid. Provide examples of words from each family to illustrate this difference in scope.
  5. Consider the two meanings of the word sentence (a complete thought; a judge's punishment). How can both meanings be traced back to the root sens/sent (to feel/perceive), especially in the context of judgment and expressing understanding?
