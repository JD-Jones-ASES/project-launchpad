Study Guide: Chapter 18, Section 2 - Narrative: The Root Story of Death

Overview

This section explores the pervasive presence of death in Roman culture and traces the etymological journey of the Latin roots mors and mori (death) into modern English. It highlights how these roots form a foundational vocabulary for concepts ranging from philosophy to finance, contrasting them with roots related to life, and revealing the surprising origins of common words like "mortgage" and "mortify."

Key Concepts

  • Death's Pervasive Presence in Roman Culture: The Romans had a constant awareness of mortality, evident in phrases like Memento mori (Remember that you will die) and Morituri te salutant (Those who are about to die salute you).
  • Latin Roots for Death: The Latin noun mors (genitive mortis) and the verb mori (to die) are the foundational roots for a significant family of English words related to death.
  • Contrast with Roots for Life: The roots mort and mor (death) are presented as a natural pair with vit and viv (life), illustrating how English uses both families to describe the human condition (e.g., "mortal beings" seeking "vital experiences").
  • Mortal vs. Immortal: The concept of mortality describes anything destined to die, while immortality (using the prefix im- meaning "not") refers to that which endures forever, such as gods or legends.
  • Etymological Evolution: Many words derived from mort have evolved in meaning over time. For example, "mortify" originally meant "to put to death" but softened to mean extreme humiliation.
  • Surprising Origins of Everyday Words: Words like "mortgage" have a profound and unexpected connection to death, originating from the Old French "death pledge."

Important Terms

  • Memento mori : A Latin phrase meaning "Remember that you will die," whispered to Roman generals during triumphs to remind them of their mortality.
  • Morituri te salutant : A Latin phrase meaning "Those who are about to die salute you," spoken by gladiators to the emperor.
  • mors (genitive mortis) : The Latin noun for "death."
  • mori : The Latin verb meaning "to die."
  • mort/mor : English root forms derived from Latin, meaning "death."
  • vit/viv : English root forms (from another chapter) meaning "life," used here to contrast with mort/mor.
  • Mortal : (adjective) Describing any creature or being that is destined to die; subject to death.
  • Immortal : (adjective) Not subject to death; living forever; enduring for all time (formed by adding the prefix im- meaning "not").
  • Mortuary : (noun) A place where dead bodies are kept before burial or cremation; a place dedicated to the care of the dead.
  • Mortify : (verb) In its root sense, "to put to death"; now, to cause someone to feel extreme embarrassment, shame, or humiliation.
  • Moribund : (adjective) In a dying state; on the verge of death; declining.
  • Post-mortem : (adjective/noun) Occurring, done, or observed after death; an examination conducted after death (from Latin post mortem).
  • Mortgage : (noun) A legal agreement by which a bank or other creditor lends money at interest in exchange for taking title to the debtor's property, with the condition that the conveyance of title becomes void upon the payment of the debt. Literally, a "death pledge" (mort + gage).

Detailed Notes

The Roman Perspective on Death

The concept of death was a fundamental aspect of Roman thought and culture. It wasn't something to be hidden or ignored, but rather a constant companion and a reminder of life's brevity.

  • Triumphs and Mortality: Even during moments of ultimate victory, like a general's triumph through Rome, a slave would famously whisper Memento mori ("Remember that you will die") as a humble reminder of human frailty amidst glory.
  • Gladiatorial Salutations: Gladiators, facing imminent death in the arena, acknowledged their fate with Morituri te salutant ("Those who are about to die salute you") when addressing the emperor.
  • Elaborate Rituals: The seriousness with which Romans regarded death led to the establishment of complex institutions like mortuaries for the care of the dead and lavish funeral processions through the city.

Latin Origins: Mors and Mori

The English language draws heavily from Latin for its vocabulary, especially for abstract and fundamental concepts.

  • The Latin noun for "death" is mors, with its genitive form being mortis.
  • The Latin verb "to die" is mori.
  • These Latin words form the core mort and mor roots that appear in many English words.

The Life-Death Connection

English, perhaps reflecting the human condition, often pairs concepts of life and death through related root families.

  • The roots mort and mor (death) are often contrasted with vit and viv (life).
  • This pairing helps us describe fundamental aspects of existence, such as "mortal beings" who inherently "seek vital experiences."

Direct Descendants of Mort and Mor

Many English words directly inherit the meaning of "death" from their Latin ancestors.

  • Mortal: The most common descendant, referring to any living being that is subject to death.
  • Immortal: Formed by adding the negative prefix im- (meaning "not"), this word describes those who will never die, such as gods, mythical figures, or things that endure indefinitely. This is analogous to how re- can change "vive" into "revive."
  • Mortuary: A place specifically dedicated to preparing and caring for the dead, reflecting the Roman practice of taking death seriously.

Evolution of Meaning: "Mortify"

Language is dynamic, and the meanings of words can shift significantly over centuries.

  • Root Sense: The word mortify originates from the Late Latin mortificare, which literally meant "to put to death."
  • Modern Meaning: Over time, its meaning softened from actual killing to a figurative "death" of self-esteem or dignity. To mortify someone now means to cause them extreme embarrassment or humiliation, making them feel as though they "could die of embarrassment."

Other Key Terms Related to Dying

  • Moribund: This adjective comes from the Latin moribundus, meaning "about to die." It describes something or someone in a dying state, or on the verge of collapse or extinction.
  • Post-mortem: This term combines the Latin post ("after") with mortem ("death"). A post-mortem examination is an autopsy, conducted after death to determine its cause. The phrase can also be used more generally to refer to any analysis or discussion that takes place after an event.

The "Death Pledge" of a Mortgage

One of the most surprising connections to the mort root is found in the financial term mortgage.

  • Etymology: The Old French word mortgage is a compound of mort (death) and gage (pledge).
  • Meaning: The "death pledge" refers to the idea that the pledge (the agreement) "dies" or ends when the debt is fully paid off. Alternatively, if the borrower fails to make payments, the property "dies" in the sense that it is forfeited to the lender. This etymology highlights a profound, albeit hidden, link between a common financial transaction and the concept of death.
