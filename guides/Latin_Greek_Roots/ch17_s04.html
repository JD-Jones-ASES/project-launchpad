Word Family: vit / viv

Overview

This word family explores eight English words derived from the Latin roots vit (life) and viv (to live). Each entry breaks down the word into its root and affixes, revealing how meaning is constructed from parts.

Word Breakdown

Vital

Components: vit (life) + -al (relating to)

Definition: Absolutely necessary; essential to life. Something vital is so important that life or success depends on it.

Vivid

Components: viv (to live) + -id (having the quality of)

Definition: Intensely bright, clear, or lifelike. A vivid description or color seems to pulse with life and energy.

Revive

Components: re- (again) + viv (to live)

Definition: To bring back to life or consciousness. The prefix re- adds the sense of returning or repeating the act of living.

Survive

Components: sur- (beyond, over) + viv (to live)

Definition: To continue living after a danger or hardship. The prefix sur- (from Latin super) means living beyond or outlasting a threat.

Viable

Components: vi (life) + -able (capable of)

Definition: Capable of living or working successfully. A viable plan is one that can 'live' -- that is, function and succeed.

Vitamin

Components: vit (life) + amine (chemical compound)

Definition: A nutrient essential for life and health. The word was coined in 1912, combining the life root with 'amine' (a type of organic compound).

Convivial

Components: con- (together) + viv (to live) + -ial (relating to)

Definition: Friendly, lively, and enjoyable. Literally 'living together,' suggesting the warmth of shared social life.

Vivacious

Components: viv (to live) + -acious (full of)

Definition: Attractively lively and animated. A vivacious person is full of life and infectious energy.

Patterns and Observations

Understanding how prefixes and suffixes modify the base root is the key to unlocking unfamiliar vocabulary. Notice how:

  • Prefixes change the direction or scope of the root meaning (e.g., re- means 'again,' sur- means 'beyond').
  • Suffixes change the part of speech or specify the quality (e.g., -al makes an adjective, -able means 'capable of,' -acious means 'full of').
  • The same root can produce nouns, verbs, and adjectives depending on the suffix attached.

Review Questions

  2. Which prefixes in this word family change the root meaning most dramatically? Give examples.
  3. Can you think of additional English words not listed here that might share the same root? What do they mean?
  4. How does understanding the root and affix structure help you remember these words' meanings?
  5. Write a sentence using at least two words from this family that demonstrates their meanings.
