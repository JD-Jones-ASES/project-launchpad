Study Guide: Chapter 17 — VIT / VIV (Life)

Overview

Chapter 17 introduces two closely related Latin roots: vit (from vita, "life") and viv (from vivere, "to live"). Together these roots form a large English word family covering everything from essential nutrients (vitamin) to personality traits (vivacious). Students will learn to recognize these roots in everyday and scientific vocabulary and understand how they contrast with the mort/mor family (death).

Key Concepts

  • Root vit — from Latin vita, meaning "life"; appears in vital, vitamin, viable
  • Root viv — from Latin vivere, meaning "to live"; appears in vivid, revive, survive, vivacious, convivial
  • Prefix patterns: re- (again) + viv = revive; sur- (beyond) + viv = survive; con- (together) + viv = convivial
  • Suffix patterns: -al (relating to), -id (having the quality of), -acious (full of), -able (capable of)
  • Thematic contrast: vit/viv (life) vs. mort/mor (death) — two root families covering the oldest contrast in human language

Important Terms

  • vital: vit (life) + -al — absolutely necessary; essential to life (a vital organ, a vital decision)
  • vivid: viv (to live) + -id — intensely bright, clear, or lifelike; so energetic it seems alive
  • revive: re- (again) + viv — to bring back to life or consciousness
  • survive: sur- (beyond, over) + viv — to continue living after a danger or hardship
  • viable: vi (life) + -able — capable of living or working successfully
  • vitamin: vit (life) + amine — a nutrient essential for life and health
  • convivial: con- (together) + viv + -ial — friendly, lively, and enjoyable; literally "living together"
  • vivacious: viv + -acious (full of) — attractively lively and animated

Detailed Notes

The Latin Sources

The Romans built two parallel words around life: vita (the noun, meaning "life") and vivere (the verb, meaning "to live"). Roman philosopher Cicero wrote of the vita activa (the active life of public service) and the vita contemplativa (the contemplative life of study). For the Romans, how one lived was the most important philosophical question.

The Vit Branch

Words using the vit form tend to describe life as a quality or concept. Vital means relating to life itself — so vital organs and vital decisions are those without which life cannot continue. Vitamin was coined in 1912 by Polish chemist Casimir Funk, who combined vita with amine (a type of chemical compound). He believed these substances were literally the chemicals of life. Viable describes anything capable of living or surviving on its own.

The Viv Branch

Words using the viv form tend to describe life as an action or energy. Vivid describes something so bright and intense it seems alive. Revive uses re- (again) to mean bringing something back to life — whether a patient, a tradition, or a plant. Survive uses sur- (beyond) to mean living past a danger. Vivacious uses -acious (full of) to describe a person bursting with liveliness. Convivial uses con- (together) to describe the Roman idea of convivium — a feast where people enjoy life together.

Connection to Chapter 18

The roots vit and viv have a natural opposite in mort and mor (death, Chapter 18). Just as re- turns viv into revive, im- turns mortal into immortal. Recognizing these paired root families helps students decode a wide range of English words about the human condition.

Review Questions

  1. What is the difference between vital and vivacious? How do their suffixes affect their meanings?
  2. How did the word vitamin get its name? What does this tell you about the importance of roots in scientific vocabulary?
  3. If you were to revive a fainted friend, what would you be doing? Break the word into its parts and explain.
  4. Why might the Romans have invented the word convivium (feast)? What does the etymology tell us about their values?
  5. Think of a situation where you might use the words survive and vital in the same sentence. What context would that be?
