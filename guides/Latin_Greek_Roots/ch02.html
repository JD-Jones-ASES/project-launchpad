Study Guide: Chapter 2 — DICT (To Say, Tell, and Declare)

Overview

Chapter 2 introduces the Latin root dict / dic, which means "to say," "to tell," or "to declare." It comes from the Latin verb dicere (to say or tell). In ancient Rome, the power to say something officially was the power to make law — from magistrates issuing decrees to juries delivering verdicts, the act of declaring was the foundation of Roman public authority. Students will learn how this root branches into many English words about speaking, declaring, and accusing.

Key Concepts

  • Root meaning and origin: "dict" comes from Latin dicere (to say, tell) and dictare (to dictate); in Roman society, official declarations shaped public life
  • How the root appears: As "dict" in most words (dictate, predict, verdict, contradict, diction, edict, indict) and as "dic" in words like abdicate
  • Important morpheme patterns: Prefixes change the direction or nature of the declaration — pre- (before), contra- (against), e- (out), in- (against), ab- (away)

Important Terms

  • dictate: to speak words for another to write down; to give orders (dict + -ate)
  • predict: to say what will happen before it occurs (pre- + dict)
  • verdict: a judgment or decision; literally "truly said" (vere + dict)
  • contradict: to say the opposite of what someone else has said (contra- + dict)
  • abdicate: to formally give up power or responsibility (ab- + dic)
  • diction: the choice and use of words in speech or writing (dict + -ion)
  • edict: an official order or decree (e- + dict)
  • indict: to formally accuse someone of a crime (in- + dict)

Detailed Notes

The Root in Ancient Rome

In ancient Rome, words were law. When a magistrate issued an edictum (official decree), his spoken words became binding rules. The Latin verb dicere — to say or tell — was at the heart of Roman public life. From it came dictare, meaning to speak aloud for someone else to record, which is where English gets the word dictate.

In an emergency, Rome could appoint a dictator — literally "one who speaks with authority" — to rule with absolute power for a limited time. The word preserved its Roman meaning for centuries before becoming the modern term for a tyrannical ruler.

Word Family Analysis

The root builds a network of declaration-related words:

  • edict: e- (out) + dict — to speak or declare outward; an official proclamation
  • predict: pre- (before) + dict — to say before an event; to forecast
  • contradict: contra- (against) + dict — to speak against; to disagree directly
  • indict: in- (against) + dict — to formally declare against; to officially charge with a crime
  • abdicate: ab- (away) + dic — to formally declare oneself away from power; to resign authority

The word verdict contains a bonus element: vere (truly) + dictum (said). It is literally "truly said" — the solemn, final word of a jury or judge.

Diction describes how a person says things — their word choice and style of expression. Good diction means choosing exactly the right words.

Comparing DICT and VOC

These two roots are close cousins in meaning, but each captures a different aspect of speech:

  • voc (Ch. 1): the voice itself — the act of calling, summoning, or using your voice
  • dict (Ch. 2): the content of speech — what is actually said, declared, or ordered

An advocate (voc) uses their voice on your behalf; a dictator (dict) tells you what must be done.

  4. How are abdicate and dictate related through their shared root? How are their meanings different?
