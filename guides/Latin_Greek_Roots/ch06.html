Study Guide: Chapter 6 — SENS/SENT (To Feel and Perceive)

Overview

Chapter 6 introduces the Latin root sens / sent, which means "to feel" or "to perceive." It comes from the Latin verb sentire (to feel, perceive, judge) and its past participle sensus. For the Romans, sentire carried a rich double meaning: physical sensation AND the forming of a judgment through careful thought. This chapter explores how one root gave English words for our five physical senses as well as words about opinion, agreement, and legal judgment.

Key Concepts

  • Root meaning and origin: "sens" comes from the Latin past participle sensus; "sent" comes from the verb stem sentire (to feel, perceive, judge)
  • How the root appears: As "sens" in words relating to physical perception (sense, sensory, sensitive) and as "sent" in words relating to opinion and agreement (consent, dissent, assent, sentiment, sentence)
  • Important morpheme patterns: The prefixes con- (together), dis- (apart), and as-/ad- (toward) create a trio of agreement/disagreement words

Important Terms

  • sense: a faculty of perception; also good judgment (sens)
  • sensory: relating to the senses or sensation (sens + -ory)
  • sensitive: easily affected by feelings or physical stimuli (sens + -itive)
  • consent: to agree; to feel together with another person (con- + sent)
  • dissent: to disagree; to hold a different opinion (dis- + sent)
  • assent: to agree or express approval (as-/ad- + sent)
  • sentiment: a feeling, opinion, or attitude (sent + -ment)
  • sentence: a complete thought in words; OR a judge's declared punishment (from Latin sententia)

Detailed Notes

The Root's Double Meaning

The Latin verb sentire meant both to physically feel and to mentally form a judgment. When you touch a hot stove, you feel it — that's the physical sense. When you weigh a decision carefully, you feel your way to a conclusion — that's the judgment sense. Both meanings live in English descendants of this root.

Word Family Analysis: Two Clusters

The word family divides into two clusters:

Physical perception cluster:

  • sense: any of the five faculties of physical perception; also "good sense" (judgment)
  • sensory: relating to the physical senses (-ory = relating to)
  • sensitive: easily moved by physical or emotional stimuli (-itive = having the quality of)

Opinion and agreement cluster (with 'sent'):

  • consent (con- = together): to feel together; to agree
  • dissent (dis- = apart): to feel apart; to disagree
  • assent (as-/ad- = toward): to feel toward; to nod in agreement
  • sentiment: the result of feeling; an emotional opinion

The Most Surprising Word: Sentence

The word sentence has two completely different modern meanings that trace back to a single Latin source:

  • Grammar: A grammatical sentence is a complete expression of thought — a "finished judgment" in words
  • Law: A judge's sentence is the opinion she pronounces after weighing the evidence

Both meanings come from the same Latin word sententia, which meant "a considered opinion" — what a person feels and concludes after careful thought. The judge delivers sententia; the grammarian calls a complete thought sententia.

Comparing SENS/SENT to VIS/VID

In Chapter 5, vis/vid covered the single sense of sight. In Chapter 6, sens/sent broadens the coverage to all five senses. But sens/sent goes further than vis/vid — it extends into the realm of opinion and judgment. A judge must both see the evidence (vis) and feel her way to a sentence (sent).

Agreement Words: A Prefix Pattern

One of the most useful patterns in this chapter is the trio of agreement words formed by different prefixes on sent:

  • con- (together) + sent = consent (agree)
  • dis- (apart) + sent = dissent (disagree)
  • as-/ad- (toward) + sent = assent (approve)

These three words share the same root but have opposite or distinct meanings depending solely on the prefix — a perfect demonstration of how prefixes drive meaning.

Review Questions

  1. What is the double meaning of the Latin verb sentire, and how does it explain why sentence can mean both a grammar term and a legal punishment?
  2. Explain the difference between consent, dissent, and assent by analyzing each word's prefix.
  3. How does the root sens/sent go beyond the coverage of vis/vid (Ch. 5)? What extra dimension does it add?
  4. A person described as "sensitive" is easily moved by feelings or physical stimuli. What do the root sens and the suffix -itive each contribute to this meaning?
  5. If a judge delivers a "sentence," and a teacher tells students to write a "sentence," how are these two uses of the word connected through Latin?
