Study Guide: Chapter 18, Section 2 - Narrative: The Root Story of Death

Overview

This section explores the Latin roots mort and mor, both meaning "death," and their significant impact on English vocabulary. It delves into how ancient Roman culture's constant awareness of death shaped expressions and customs, and how these roots, often paired with prefixes or other words, form a family of terms ranging from biological descriptions to financial agreements, revealing fascinating shifts in meaning over time.

Key Concepts

  • Latin Roots for Death: The roots mort (from mors, mortis) and mor (from mori) are fundamental to understanding many English words related to death.
  • Roman Awareness of Death: Roman society had a profound and ever-present awareness of mortality, evidenced by phrases like Memento mori ("Remember that you will die") and Morituri te salutant ("Those who are about to die salute you").
  • Life and Death Roots: The mort/mor root family forms a natural linguistic pair with vit/viv (life), highlighting the human condition as both "mortal beings" seeking "vital experiences."
  • Prefixes and Meaning: Prefixes like im- (not) create antonyms (e.g., mortal vs. immortal), and re- (again) can suggest revival (e.g., revive).
  • Evolution of Word Meanings: Some words derived from mort/mor have evolved from their literal meaning of "to put to death" to more figurative senses, such as mortify meaning extreme embarrassment rather than actual killing.
  • The "Death Pledge" of Mortgage: The word mortgage has a surprising etymology, literally meaning "death pledge," where the pledge "dies" upon repayment or forfeiture.

Important Terms

  • mors (genitive mortis) : Latin noun meaning "death."
  • mori : Latin verb meaning "to die."
  • Memento mori : Latin phrase meaning "Remember that you will die," a reminder of mortality.
  • Morituri te salutant : Latin phrase meaning "Those who are about to die salute you," famously used by gladiators addressing the emperor.
  • mortal : Adjective describing any creature that will eventually die; subject to death.
  • immortal : Adjective describing something that will never die or endure forever; not subject to death. Formed by adding the prefix im- (not).
  • mortuary : A place dedicated to the care of the dead; a funeral home.
  • mortify : In its root sense, to "put to death"; now commonly means to make someone feel extreme humiliation or embarrassment.
  • moribund : Adjective describing someone or something in a dying state, or nearing an end.
  • post-mortem : An examination conducted "after death," such as an autopsy. From the Latin post mortem.
  • mortgage : A legal agreement by which a bank or other creditor lends money at interest in exchange for taking title to the debtor's property, with the condition that the conveyance of title becomes void upon the payment of the debt. Literally, a "death pledge."

Detailed Notes

The Roman Perspective on Death

The concept of death was a pervasive and influential aspect of Roman culture. It wasn't something to be hidden or ignored, but rather a constant companion and motivator.

  • "Memento mori": This powerful phrase, often whispered by a slave to a triumphant general, served as a humble reminder that even in victory and glory, death awaited everyone. It underscored the transient nature of earthly achievements.
  • "Morituri te salutant": This iconic greeting from gladiators to the emperor before battle highlighted the stark reality of their profession, where death was an almost certain outcome.
  • These phrases demonstrate a cultural acceptance and even integration of mortality into daily life and significant public events.

The Roots mort and mor and Their English Descendants

The Latin noun mors (genitive mortis) and the verb mori (to die) are the foundational elements for a vast family of English words.

  • mort / mor (death) and vit / viv (life): These two root families are presented as natural linguistic counterparts. English uses them to describe the fundamental aspects of human existence: we are mortal beings (subject to death) who seek vital experiences (full of life). This pairing emphasizes the inherent duality of life and death.
  • Mortal and Immortal:
    • Mortal: Refers to any living being that is destined to die. It highlights the finite nature of life.
    • Immortal: Created by adding the negative prefix im- (meaning "not") to mortal. This describes gods, legendary figures, or anything perceived to last forever, never dying. The prefix re- (again, back) similarly helps form words like revive (bring back to life).
  • Roman Funeral Practices: The Romans' serious approach to death extended to elaborate customs, including the construction of mortuaries (places for the deceased) and grand funeral processions, reflecting their respect for the dead and belief in an afterlife.

Evolving Meanings and Specific Terms

Many words derived from mort/mor have retained their core meaning, while others have undergone fascinating shifts.

  • Mortify:
    • Originally from Late Latin mortificare, meaning "to put to death" or "to kill."
    • Over centuries, its meaning softened from literal killing to the intense feeling of wishing one could die from extreme embarrassment or humiliation. The feeling is so strong it's as if one were dying.
  • Moribund:
    • Derived from Latin moribundus, meaning "about to die."
    • This word describes a person or thing that is in a dying state, on the verge of extinction, or showing signs of terminal decline.
  • Post-mortem:
    • A direct borrowing from the Latin phrase post mortem, meaning "after death."
    • It is most commonly used to describe an examination (like an autopsy) conducted after a person's death to determine the cause or circumstances.

The Surprising Etymology of Mortgage

One of the most intriguing connections to the mort/mor root is found in the word mortgage.

  • "Death Pledge": The Old French word mortgage combines mort (death) with gage (pledge).
  • The "Death" Connection:
    • One interpretation is that the "pledge dies" when the debt is fully paid off, releasing the borrower from the agreement.
    • Alternatively, if the borrower fails to repay the debt, the property itself "dies" or is forfeited to the lender.
  • This etymology highlights how deeply embedded the concept of "death" is in language, even in unexpected financial terms.

Review Questions

  3. Describe the etymological journey of the word "mortify." How has its meaning changed from its original Latin sense to its common usage today?
  4. What is the literal meaning of "mortgage," and how does this "death pledge" concept relate to the actual process of securing and repaying a loan?
  5. Beyond the specific examples in the text, can you think of other English words that might logically derive from the root mort or mor, based on their meaning? (You may need to do some light research for this one!)
