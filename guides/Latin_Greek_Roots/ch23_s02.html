Chapter 23, Section 2: Narrative - The Roots of Sound and Time

Overview

This section explores two fundamental Greek roots that have significantly shaped English vocabulary: phon (sound, voice) and chron (time). It delves into their origins in ancient Greek culture, provides examples of English words derived from them, and highlights the subtle distinctions between these Greek roots and their Latin counterparts.

Key Concepts

  • Greek Root phon: Derived from the Greek word phone (voice, sound, tone), this root relates specifically to sound as a physical phenomenon, often in a technical or descriptive sense.
  • Words from phon: Common English words like telephone, microphone, symphony, and euphony illustrate the concept of sound, its transmission, amplification, or pleasing quality.
  • Distinction between phon and voc/vox: While both relate to voice, Greek phon focuses on sound as a physical occurrence, whereas Latin voc/vox (voice, to call) is used for words related to speaking and calling.
  • Greek Root chron: Originating from the Greek word chronos (time, duration, sequence), this root forms the basis for English words describing measurable, sequential, and ordered time.
  • Words from chron: Examples include chronology, synchronize, anachronism, and chronicle, all of which deal with time in terms of order, concurrence, or historical placement.
  • Distinction between chron and tempor: Greek chron tends to produce scientific and technical vocabulary related to time, while Latin tempor (time) gives us more general terms like temporary and contemporary.
  • Two Greek words for time: The Greeks had chronos (measurable, sequential time) which became a major English root, and kairos (the right or opportune moment), which survives in the field of rhetoric.

Important Terms

  • phon: Greek root meaning "sound, voice, tone."
  • telephone: A device for transmitting sound over long distances; literally "far-sound" (tele- "far" + phon "sound").
  • microphone: A device that picks up small sounds and makes them louder; literally "small-sound" (micro- "small" + phon "sound").
  • symphony: A harmonious blending of many instruments or voices; literally "sounding together" (syn- "together" + phon "sound").
  • euphony: The quality of being pleasing to the ear, especially through a harmonious combination of words; literally "good sound" (eu- "good" + phon "sound").
  • chron: Greek root meaning "time," specifically referring to duration and sequence.
  • chronology: An arrangement of events or dates in the order of their occurrence; a "time-account."
  • synchronize: To cause to occur or operate at the same time or rate; to make things happen "at the same time" (syn- "together" + chron "time").
  • anachronism: Something belonging to a period other than that in which it exists, especially a thing that is conspicuously old-fashioned; something "out of time" (ana- "against" + chron "time").
  • chronicle: A factual written account of important or historical events in the order of their occurrence; a record of events organized by time.
  • kairos: A Greek word referring to the opportune or propitious moment for action or decision, especially in rhetoric.

Detailed Notes

The Root Story: phon (Sound, Voice)

The ancient Greeks held a deep appreciation for sound and music, evident in their cultural practices. Festivals honoring Dionysus featured choruses singing in unison, trumpets announced the start of competitions at the Olympic Games, and actors in the theater used masks to amplify their voices. The Greek word phone encompassed all these elements of voice, sound, and tone. From phone, the English language inherited the root phon, which consistently appears in words related to sound.

  • Telephone: A prime example, telephone combines tele- (meaning "far") with phone (sound) to create "far-sound," accurately describing a device that transmits sound over distances.
  • Microphone: This word, from micro- (meaning "small") and phone (sound), refers to a "small-sound" device. It doesn't make sounds smaller but rather captures and amplifies small or faint sounds.
  • Symphony: Derived from syn- (meaning "together") and phone (sound), symphony literally means "sounding together," perfectly describing the harmonious blending of many musical instruments.
  • Euphony: Combining eu- (meaning "good") with phone (sound), euphony denotes "good sound" – the pleasing and smooth quality often found in beautifully flowing words or musical arrangements.

Root Connection: Greek phon vs. Latin voc/vox It's important to differentiate phon from the Latin root voc/vox (meaning "voice, to call"), which also relates to the human voice. The two roots carved out distinct areas in English:

  • Greek phon: Predominantly used for words concerning sound as a physical phenomenon, its production, and transmission (e.g., phonics, phonograph).
  • Latin voc/vox: Primarily used for words related to calling, speaking, and vocal expression (e.g., vocal, invoke, vocation).

The Root Story: chron (Time)

Beyond sound, the Greeks were profoundly fascinated by time. Their word chronos specifically referred to "time" in the sense of duration and sequential order. This concept of measurable, sequential time gave rise to numerous English words.

  • Chronology: A "time-account," a chronology is an ordered list of events, arranged precisely in the sequence they occurred.
  • Synchronize: Formed from syn- (meaning "together") and chronos (time), to synchronize means to make things happen "at the same time."
  • Anachronism: Combining ana- (meaning "against" or "back") with chronos (time), an anachronism is something "out of time"—an object, person, or idea that is historically misplaced, such as a modern wristwatch appearing in a historical drama.
  • Chronicle: An ancestor of modern historical writing, a chronicle is a detailed record of events organized strictly by the time of their occurrence.

Root Connection: Greek chron vs. Latin tempor Similar to the phon/voc distinction, Greek chron and Latin tempor (meaning "time") developed different applications in English:

  • Greek chron: Tends to be used for the scientific and technical vocabulary of time, dealing with precise measurement, order, and historical sequence (e.g., chronometer, chronograph).
  • Latin tempor: Gives us more general terms related to time periods and temporal states (e.g., temporary, contemporary, temporal).

Etymology Note: chronos vs. kairos The Greeks actually possessed two distinct words for time, each with a unique nuance:

  • Chronos: Refers to quantitative, measurable, and sequential time—the steady, ticking progression. This is the root that became a major contributor to English vocabulary.
  • Kairos: Refers to qualitative time, specifically "the right or opportune moment"—the perfect, critical moment for action or decision. While kairos did not become a root in English in the same way chronos did, it retains significance in rhetoric, where it describes the art of choosing the most effective moment to present an argument.
