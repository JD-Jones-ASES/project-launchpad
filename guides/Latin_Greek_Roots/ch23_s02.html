Chapter 23, Section 2: Narrative - Greek Roots phon and chron

Overview

This section explores two fundamental Greek roots, phon (sound, voice) and chron (time), and their significant influence on English vocabulary. It highlights how ancient Greek fascinations with music and the passage of time led to a rich array of words related to acoustics, communication, and temporal organization, while also differentiating these roots from similar Latin counterparts.

Key Concepts

  • The Greek Root phon (Sound, Voice): Derived from the Greek word phone, meaning voice, sound, or tone, this root is central to many English words related to auditory phenomena, communication, and music.
  • Examples of phon Words: Common words like telephone ("far-sound"), microphone ("small-sound"), symphony ("sounding together"), and euphony ("good sound") illustrate its application.
  • Distinction between phon and Latin voc/vox: While both relate to the human voice, phon typically describes sound as a physical phenomenon (e.g., microphone), whereas voc/vox relates to calling and speaking (e.g., vocal).
  • The Greek Root chronos (Time): Derived from the Greek word for "time" in the sense of duration and sequence, this root forms the basis of English words describing temporal order and events.
  • Examples of chronos Words: Words like chronology ("time-account"), synchronize ("at the same time"), anachronism ("out of time"), and chronicle (a time-organized record) demonstrate its usage.
  • Distinction between chron and Latin tempor: Similar to phon and voc/vox, chron provides much of our scientific and technical vocabulary for time (e.g., chronology), while tempor gives us words like temporary and contemporary.
  • Two Greek Words for Time: chronos vs. kairos: Chronos refers to measurable, sequential time, which became a major English root. Kairos, though not a major root, signifies the "right" or "opportune moment" and is significant in rhetoric.

Important Terms

phon: A Greek root meaning "sound" or "voice," derived from phone. telephone: A device for transmitting "far-sound" or voice over a distance (tele- "far" + phon "sound"). microphone: A device that picks up "small sounds" and amplifies them (micro- "small" + phon "sound"). symphony: A musical composition involving a harmonious "sounding together" of many instruments (syn- "together" + phon "sound"). euphony: The quality of "good sound" or pleasing, smooth-flowing words (eu- "good" + phon "sound"). voc/vox: A Latin root meaning "voice" or "to call," often associated with words about speaking (e.g., vocal, invoke). chronos: A Greek word and root meaning "time" in the sense of duration and sequence. chronology: An "time-account" or a list of events arranged in the order they occurred (chronos "time" + -logy "account/study of"). synchronize: To make things happen "at the same time" (syn- "together" + chronos "time"). anachronism: Something that is "out of time" or incongruous with its historical period (ana- "against/back" + chronos "time"). chronicle: A record of events organized by time, serving as an ancestor to modern history writing. tempor: A Latin root meaning "time," often seen in words like temporary and contemporary. kairos: A Greek word for "the right or opportune moment," particularly relevant in rhetoric for choosing the perfect time to make an argument.

Detailed Notes

The Root Story: Greek Fascination with Sound

The ancient Greeks held a deep appreciation for music and sound, evident in their cultural practices:

  • Festivals: Choruses sang in unison at festivals honoring Dionysus.
  • Olympic Games: Trumpets were used to signal the start of competitions.
  • Theater: Actors utilized masks to amplify their voices, emphasizing vocal projection.
  • The Greek word encompassing all these aspects—voice, sound, tone—was phone. This word became the source of the English root phon.

The Root phon and its English Descendants

The root phon consistently appears in English words related to sound:

  • Telephone: A classic example, literally meaning a "far-sound" device (from Greek tele-, "far," + phone, "sound").
  • Microphone: A device designed to pick up "small sounds" and make them louder (from Greek micro-, "small," + phone, "sound").
  • Symphony: Describes a "sounding together" or harmonious blending of many instruments (from Greek syn-, "together," + phone, "sound").
  • Euphony: Refers to "good sound," highlighting the pleasing quality of words that flow smoothly together (from Greek eu-, "good," + phone, "sound").

Root Connection: phon vs. voc/vox

It's important to differentiate phon from the Latin root voc/vox (voice, to call):

  • Both roots relate to the human voice.
  • Latin voc/vox: Primarily yielded words about calling and speaking (e.g., vocal, invoke).
  • Greek phon: Primarily yielded words about sound as a physical phenomenon (e.g., microphone, phonics).

The Root Story: Greek Fascination with Time

The Greeks were equally captivated by the concept of time.

  • Their word chronos referred to "time" in the sense of duration and sequence. This root is foundational to English vocabulary related to time.

The Root chronos and its English Descendants

The root chronos appears in many English words related to temporal order:

  • Chronology: A "time-account"—a list of events arranged in the order they occurred (from chronos, "time").
  • Synchronize: To make things happen "at the same time" (from Greek syn-, "together," + chronos, "time").
  • Anachronism: Something that is "out of time" or against the natural flow of time, such as a modern item appearing in an ancient setting (from Greek ana-, "against," + chronos, "time").
  • Chronicle: A record of events organized by time, serving as an early form of history writing.

Root Connection: chron vs. tempor

Similar to the sound roots, chron differs from the Latin root tempor (time):

  • Latin tempor: Gave us words like temporary (lasting for a limited time) and contemporary (existing at the same time).
  • Greek chron: Contributed much of our scientific and technical vocabulary of time, such as chronology and synchronize.

Etymology Note: chronos vs. kairos

The ancient Greeks actually had two distinct words for "time":

  • Chronos: Represented measurable, sequential time (e.g., seconds, minutes, years). This is the word that became a major English root.
  • Kairos: Referred to the "right" or "opportune moment." While kairos did not become a major English root for general vocabulary, it survives as a key concept in rhetoric, denoting the strategic timing for making an effective argument.

Review Questions

  2. Explain the primary distinction between the Greek root phon and the Latin root voc/vox. Give an example of an English word derived from each root that illustrates this difference.
  3. How did the ancient Greeks' fascination with time influence the English language? Discuss the meaning of the Greek root chronos and provide examples of English words formed from it.
  4. Compare and contrast the Greek root chron with the Latin root tempor. In what types of English words do we typically see each root?
  5. The Greeks had two words for time: chronos and kairos. Describe the difference in their meanings and explain which one became a major English root and in what context kairos is still used today.
