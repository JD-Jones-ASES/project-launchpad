The Story of tempor: A Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the Latin root tempus, meaning "time," and its profound influence on a family of English words. It illustrates how concepts of duration, simultaneity, impermanence, spontaneity, and even weather phenomena are all etymologically linked to the fundamental idea of time. Understanding this root helps us grasp the nuances and historical development of these common English terms.

Key Concepts

  • The Latin Root tempus: The foundational word meaning "time," from which all discussed English derivatives originate. Its stem temporis is particularly important for forming many of these words.
  • Etymology and Semantic Change: The process by which words evolve in meaning over time. The transformation of tempestas from "period of time" to "violent storm" is a prime example of how cultural observations can narrow a word's definition.
  • Impact of Prefixes: How Latin prefixes like con- (with, together) and ex- (out of) combine with the root temporis to create new meanings, such as contemporary and extemporaneous.
  • Concepts of Duration and Impermanence: Many words derived from tempus relate to how long things last, highlighting the transient nature of existence (e.g., temporary, temporal).
  • Contrast of the Temporal and the Eternal: The philosophical distinction between worldly, fleeting aspects of life (temporal) and spiritual or everlasting ones, a concept that shaped much of Western thought.
  • Spontaneity and Preparation: The idea of acting "out of time" or without prior arrangement, encapsulated by extemporaneous.

Important Terms

  • tempus (Latin): The original Latin word meaning "time."
  • Temporary: Lasting or existing for only a limited period; not permanent. From Latin temporarius, "of time."
  • Contemporary: Existing, occurring, or living at the same time. Also, a person living at the same time as another. Formed from Latin con- (with, together) + temporarius.
  • Temporal:
    1. Relating to time.
    2. Relating to worldly affairs as opposed to spiritual or eternal matters; transient and fleeting.
  • Extemporaneous: Done or spoken without preparation; impromptu. Derived from Latin ex temporis, literally "out of time."
  • Tempest: A violent windy storm. Originally from Latin tempestas, meaning "a period of time" or "season," its meaning evolved to refer specifically to severe weather.
  • Tempo (musical): The speed or pace at which a piece of music is meant to be performed. Directly borrowed from Italian, which in turn borrowed from Latin tempus.

Detailed Notes

The Latin word tempus forms the bedrock of an entire linguistic family in English, all revolving around the concept of "time." Its stem, temporis, serves as a powerful connector, revealing the hidden history within words we use every day.

Temporary: For a Time

The word temporary directly reflects its Latin origin temporarius, meaning "of time" or "for a time." It denotes something that is not meant to last indefinitely, highlighting the transient nature of many things. For instance, the Roman soldiers' temporary camps were designed for immediate use, only to be dismantled or abandoned as their march continued. This concept of existing only "for a time" is central to its meaning, contrasting sharply with permanence.

Contemporary: With the Time

Contemporary introduces the prefix con-, meaning "with" or "together." When combined with temporarius (of the time), it creates a word meaning "with the time"—that is, existing or occurring at the same time. Your contemporaries are the people who share your era, experiencing similar historical, social, and cultural moments. Likewise, when historians label two events as contemporary, they signify that these events unfolded during the same period, allowing for analysis of their potential interconnections or shared context.

Temporal: Of Time, and Earthly Things

The adjective temporal carries a dual meaning, both tied to time. Primarily, it describes anything related to time itself. However, it also commonly distinguishes between the earthly, passing world and the eternal or spiritual realm. Roman philosophers, for example, often contrasted "temporal pleasures" – those fleeting joys of this world – with "lasting virtues," which transcended earthly existence. This idea of the temporal as inherently fleeting and impermanent profoundly shaped Western philosophical and religious thought for centuries, emphasizing the transient nature of material life.

Extemporaneous: Out of Time, Without Preparation

One of the more dramatic derivatives is extemporaneous. This word combines the prefix ex- (out of) with temporis (the stem of tempus), literally meaning "out of the time." In practical terms, this signifies an action performed without preparation, "on the spot," or without prior planning. An extemporaneous speech, for instance, is not written or rehearsed but delivered spontaneously, requiring the speaker to think and articulate thoughts in the very moment, drawing words and ideas from the immediate pressure of time itself.

Tempest: A Time of Weather

Perhaps the most surprising member of this word family is tempest. To modern ears, a tempest unequivocally means a violent storm. However, its Latin ancestor, tempestas, originally held a much broader meaning: simply "a period of time" or "a season." The evolution of its meaning provides a fascinating insight into human experience. Because weather defines the character of each season, and memorable weather is often severe, tempestas gradually narrowed its focus to specifically denote bad weather, or a storm. Thus, a tempest is, etymologically speaking, merely a "time of weather" that happens to be ferocious and memorable.

Etymological Notes on Tempo

The musical term tempo provides another fascinating link to tempus. Directly borrowed into Italian, and then into English, tempo refers to the speed at which a piece of music is played. Both tempo and temporary share the same root because both fundamentally deal with the measurement of time. When a conductor adjusts the tempo, they are quite literally controlling how time flows through the musical composition, influencing its pace and character. The direct Italian borrowing explains why the word retains its distinct Italian pronunciation rather than a more Anglicized form.

Review Questions

  1. Explain how the original Latin meaning of tempus ("time") directly informs the definitions of both "temporary" and "contemporary." Provide an example for each.
  2. What is the core distinction Roman philosophers made when discussing "temporal pleasures" versus "lasting virtues"? How did this understanding of "temporal" influence Western thought?
  3. Describe what an "extemporaneous" speech entails. How does the etymological breakdown of the word (e.g., "out of the time") reflect the nature of such a speech?
  4. Trace the semantic evolution of the word "tempest" from its original Latin meaning to its modern usage. What factors contributed to this change in meaning?
  5. Despite their seemingly disparate contexts, how do the musical term "tempo" and the word "temporary" both illustrate the fundamental concept of measuring and experiencing time?
