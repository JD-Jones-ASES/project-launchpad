Understanding the Root 'tempor': Time and Its Derivatives

Overview

This section explores the rich family of English words derived from the Latin root tempus, meaning "time." It demonstrates how this fundamental concept influenced Roman life and continues to shape our language, giving rise to terms that describe duration, simultaneity, worldly existence, spontaneity, and even natural phenomena like storms, all fundamentally connected to the passage of time.

Key Concepts

  • The Primacy of Time: The Latin word tempus (time) was central to Roman life and forms the foundation for a wide array of English words.
  • Temporal Duration and Permanence: Words like temporary directly reflect the idea of something existing "for a time" and not lasting indefinitely.
  • Shared Eras and Simultaneity: The prefix con- (with, together) combines with temporarius to form contemporary, signifying events or people existing in the same period.
  • Worldly vs. Eternal: Temporal highlights the distinction between things related to earthly time and the fleeting world, as opposed to the eternal or spiritual.
  • Spontaneity and Lack of Preparation: Extemporaneous describes actions performed "out of the time," meaning without prior preparation, emphasizing immediate thought and speech.
  • Evolution of Meaning: The word tempest illustrates how a word's meaning can dramatically narrow over time, evolving from "a period of time" or "a season" to specifically mean a violent storm, influenced by memorable weather experiences.
  • Musical Time: The term tempo in music directly reflects the root tempus, controlling how time flows within a musical piece, and highlights direct linguistic borrowing.

Important Terms

  • tempor: The stem derived from the Latin word tempus, meaning "time."
  • temporary: (adj.) Lasting for only a limited period of time; not permanent. (Example: Roman soldiers' temporary camps.)
  • contemporary: (adj.) Existing, occurring, or living at the same period of time. (n.) A person living at the same time as another. (Example: Your peers are your contemporaries.)
  • temporal: (adj.) Relating to time; relating to worldly affairs as opposed to spiritual or eternal matters; transient. (Example: Temporal pleasures contrasted with lasting virtues.)
  • extemporaneous: (adj.) Done or spoken without preparation; impromptu. (Example: An extemporaneous speech.)
  • tempest: (n.) A violent storm. (Etymologically, a "time of weather.")
  • tempo: (n.) The speed at which a passage of music is or should be played. (From Italian, directly from Latin tempus.)

Detailed Notes

The Foundation: tempus and temporis

The section begins by establishing the profound importance of time (tempus) in Roman society, much as it is in ours. The Latin word tempus (meaning "time") provides the stem temporis, which is the base for a multitude of English words. These words describe various aspects of time: how long things last, when they happen, and how quickly they transpire.

Words of Duration: Temporary

The word temporary is one of the most direct derivatives, meaning "for a time." It signifies something that is not intended to be permanent or lasting. The example of Roman soldiers building temporary wooden camps illustrates this perfectly—structures designed for immediate use and then abandoned. This highlights the concept of limited duration inherent in the root.

Shared Time: Contemporary

The term contemporary illustrates how prefixes modify the base meaning. Con- means "with" or "together," so a contemporary is someone who lives "with the time" of another, or "of the time" (temporarius). This applies to people sharing the same historical era (your peers are your contemporaries) or to events occurring in the same period. Historians use this term to link events that happened simultaneously.

Earthly vs. Eternal: Temporal

Temporal describes anything related to time itself, but more specifically, it often refers to the earthly, transient world in contrast to the eternal or spiritual realm. Roman philosophers frequently debated the distinction between "temporal pleasures" (fleeting, worldly joys) and "lasting virtues" (enduring, moral principles). This concept of the temporal as impermanent and fleeting has significantly influenced Western thought and philosophy.

Without Preparation: Extemporaneous

One of the more dramatic and precise words from this root is extemporaneous. The prefix ex- means "out of" or "without," so something done extemporaneously is done "out of the time"—meaning without preparation or planning. An extemporaneous speech is delivered spontaneously, "on the spot," requiring the speaker to think and articulate their thoughts in the very moment, drawing words from the immediate pressure of time.

The Evolving Meaning: Tempest

The word tempest offers a fascinating example of semantic shift. To modern English speakers, a tempest is unequivocally a violent storm. However, its Latin ancestor, tempestas, originally held a broader meaning: simply "a period of time" or "a season." The text explains that because weather largely defined the character of each season, and because severe weather was particularly memorable, the meaning of tempestas gradually narrowed. It came to denote the most impactful kind of "time of weather"—a storm. Thus, a tempest is, etymologically speaking, a "time of weather" that happens to be ferocious.

Etymological Note: Tempo

The musical term tempo directly relates to the root tempor. Tempo refers to the speed at which a piece of music is played, a clear measurement of time's flow within the composition. When a conductor adjusts the tempo, they are literally controlling how time unfolds in the music. The reason tempo sounds more Italian than English is because Italian borrowed tempus directly from Latin, and the term then entered English from Italian musical vocabulary.

Review Questions

  1. How does the Latin root tempus (time) influence the modern English meanings of both temporary and contemporary? Provide an example for each.
  2. Explain the philosophical distinction Roman thinkers made using the word temporal. In what ways has this concept of the "temporal" as fleeting and impermanent shaped broader Western thought?
  3. What is the defining characteristic of an extemporaneous action, such as a speech? How does its etymology ("out of the time") perfectly capture this characteristic?
  4. Trace the semantic evolution of the word tempest from its original Latin meaning to its current usage. What factors contributed to this significant change in meaning?
  5. Discuss the connection between the musical term tempo and the Latin root tempor. Why do you think this specific word for musical speed was borrowed directly from Italian rather than being derived through common English word formation?
