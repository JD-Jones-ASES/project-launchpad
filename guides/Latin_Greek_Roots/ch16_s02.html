Study Guide: Understanding the Root "tempor / tempus"

Overview

This section profiles the Latin root tempor/tempus, derived from the Latin noun tempus (time), with its genitive form temporis (of time). This root connects English words related to time, duration, the present moment, and even weather. From temporary to contemporary, this root family reveals how the Latin concept of time shapes modern English vocabulary.

Key Concepts

  • Core Meaning of tempor / tempus: The root means "time," referring to duration, the current era, a particular period, or the passage of time.
  • Latin Origin: From the Latin noun tempus (time), genitive temporis (of time). This is one of the most fundamental Latin nouns.
  • Variant Forms: The root appears as tempor in most English derivatives and as temp in shortened compounds like tempo and tempest.
  • Broad Semantic Range: This root produces words about duration (temporary), cultural periods (contemporary), spiritual vs. worldly concerns (temporal), music (tempo), and even weather (tempest).

Important Terms

  • tempor (Latin root): Time; from Latin tempus, genitive temporis.
  • temp (variant): A shortened form appearing in some compounds.
  • Genitive form: The possessive or "of" form of a Latin noun — temporis means "of time."

Detailed Notes

The Root tempor — "Time"

The Latin word tempus was central to Roman thought, covering everything from brief moments to historical eras. In English, this root generates words about how long things last, what period they belong to, and how time relates to human activity.

Key Derivatives

  • temporary (tempor-ary): lasting for a time — not permanent
  • contemporary (con-tempor-ary): with the time — modern or living at the same time
  • temporal (tempor-al): of time — relating to time; worldly rather than spiritual
  • extemporaneous (ex-tempor-aneous): out of the time — done without preparation

Time and Its Metaphors

The root tempor reveals interesting connections:

  • Time as duration: temporary (limited time)
  • Time as era: contemporary (same time period)
  • Time as worldly life: temporal (earthly, as opposed to eternal)
  • Time as readiness: extemporaneous (out of the moment, impromptu)
  • Time as rhythm: tempo (the speed/timing of music)

  3. Compare temporary and contemporary. How do their prefixes create different relationships to time?
  4. Explain how temporal can mean both "relating to time" and "worldly." What connects these two senses?
  5. If you encountered the word extempore, how would the root tempor and prefix ex- help you guess its meaning?
