Study Guide: Exploring the 'cred' Word Family

Overview

This section delves into the "cred" word family, revealing how a single Latin root meaning "to believe" or "to trust" forms the basis of many related English words. Understanding this core meaning allows us to decipher the nuances of words like credible, incredible, credit, creed, and credential, which are used across various aspects of daily life, from finance to reputation to formal recognition.

Key Concepts

  • The Root "cred": The foundational meaning of "cred" is "to believe" or "to trust." This understanding is crucial for grasping the core idea behind all words in this family.
  • Affixes as Meaning Modifiers: Prefixes like dis- (away, apart), in- (not), and ac- (toward), and suffixes like -ible (able to be), -ential (relating to), and -ulous (tending to), significantly alter or refine the root's meaning, leading to a diverse set of words.
  • Trust and Belief as Central Themes: Every word in the "cred" family revolves around concepts of trust, belief, trustworthiness, or the act of believing.
  • Contextual Nuances: While sharing a common root, these words are used in distinct contexts, from describing a person's character (credulous) to formal documentation (credential) or official recognition (accredit).

Important Terms

  • accredit: (ac- toward + cred to trust + -it) To officially recognize someone or something as meeting a standard, often for quality or qualification.
  • credible: (cred to believe + -ible able to be) Able to be believed; convincing and trustworthy.
  • credential: (cred to trust + -ential relating to) A document or quality that proves a person's identity, qualifications, or trustworthiness.
  • credit: (cred to believe, trust + -it) The ability of a customer to obtain goods or services before payment, based on the trust that payment will be made in the future; also, public acknowledgment or praise for an achievement or contribution.
  • creed: (cred to believe, from credo, “I believe”) A statement of fundamental beliefs, principles, or aims, especially one of a religious nature.
  • credulous: (cred to believe + -ulous tending to) Having or showing too great a readiness to believe things; easily fooled or gullible.
  • discredit: (dis- away, apart + cred to believe + -it) To harm the good reputation of someone or something; to cause people to stop believing in someone or something.
  • incredible: (in- not + cred to believe + -ible able) Impossible or very difficult to believe; extraordinary or astonishing.
  • Root: The basic part of a word that carries its primary meaning.
  • Affix: A morpheme (a meaningful linguistic unit) that is attached to a word stem to form a new word or word form.

Detailed Notes

The Latin root "cred" is a powerful building block in the English language, consistently pointing to ideas of belief, trust, and reliability. By understanding this core, we can quickly grasp the essence of many seemingly different words.

  1. credible: This word describes something that is believable or convincing. When we say a witness's testimony is credible, we mean it seems trustworthy and likely to be true. News sources strive to be credible by presenting facts accurately. The opposite of credible is often incredible, highlighting the spectrum of belief.

  2. credential: A credential is essentially proof of trust or qualification. This could be a diploma, a license, a certification, or even a password that verifies your identity. Professionals often need specific credentials to practice their trade, demonstrating to clients and employers that they have the necessary training and expertise to be trusted.

  3. credit: The word credit has a few significant meanings, all stemming from trust.

    • Financial Trust: When you buy something "on credit", the seller trusts you to pay for it later. Banks extend credit (loans) based on their belief in your ability to repay.
    • Recognition/Belief in Achievement: We "give credit" to someone for an idea, a discovery, or hard work. This means we believe they are responsible for it and deserve recognition. For example, a student might give credit to their sources in a research paper.

  4. creed: A creed is a formal statement of beliefs, principles, or values. Historically, creeds are central to religious faiths, such as the Apostles' Creed. However, organizations, political parties, or even individuals can have a creed that outlines what they stand for or believe in. It’s a declaration of fundamental conviction.

  5. discredit: To discredit someone or something is to damage their reputation or cause others to lose trust or belief in them. If new evidence emerges that proves a scientific theory wrong, it discredits the theory. Spreading false rumors can discredit a person's character, making them seem less trustworthy or believable.

  6. accredit: This verb means to officially recognize that something meets certain standards, thereby instilling trust in it. Universities are accredited by educational bodies to ensure they offer quality education. Hospitals are accredited to confirm they meet health and safety standards. Accreditation is a formal stamp of approval, signifying trustworthiness and competence.

  7. incredible: The prefix "in-" means "not," so incredible literally means "not able to be believed."

    • Unbelievable (literally): Something can be incredible because it's so surprising or far-fetched that it's hard to accept as true (e.g., an incredible coincidence).
    • Extraordinary/Amazing: Often, incredible is used to express extreme admiration for something extraordinary or amazing, even if it's technically believable (e.g., "The view was incredible," meaning exceptionally beautiful).

  8. credulous: Someone who is credulous is excessively willing to believe things, often without sufficient evidence. This trait suggests a lack of critical thinking and makes a person vulnerable to being fooled or exploited. A credulous person might easily fall for scams or rumors, simply because they tend to trust what they hear without questioning.

In essence, the "cred" word family provides a rich vocabulary to discuss the many facets of belief, trust, and trustworthiness in society, from personal interactions to formal institutions.

Review Questions

  1. How does understanding the core meaning of the root "cred" (to believe, to trust) help in deciphering the meaning of unfamiliar words from this family? Provide specific examples from the given list.
  2. Explain the difference between something being "credible" and "incredible." Can something be considered "incredible" in a positive way, and if so, how does that relate to its root meaning?
  3. Discuss the multiple meanings of the word "credit." How does the concept of "trust" underpin each of these different uses (e.g., financial credit, giving credit for work)?
  4. Why is "accreditation" important for institutions like educational organizations or hospitals? How does it serve to build public trust, and what would be the implications if an institution lost its accreditation?
  5. Describe a scenario where a "credulous" person might encounter information that is not "credible." What might be the potential consequences for this person, and how could they avoid being "discredited" by their actions?
