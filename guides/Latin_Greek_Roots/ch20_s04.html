Word Family: cred

Overview

This word family explores eight English words derived from the Latin root cred (to believe, to trust). These words span religious belief, financial trust, reputation, and the human tendency toward (or away from) credulity.

Word Breakdown

Credible

Components: cred (to believe) + -ible (able to be)

Definition: Able to be believed; convincing. A credible source is one that can be trusted and believed.

Credential

Components: cred (to trust) + -ential (relating to)

Definition: A document proving qualifications or trustworthiness. Credentials establish that a person is worthy of trust or recognition.

Credit

Components: cred (to believe, trust) + -it

Definition: Trust given for future payment; recognition for work. In both senses, credit involves extending trust.

Creed

Components: from Latin credo, 'I believe'

Definition: A statement of beliefs or principles. A creed declares what a person or group holds to be true.

Discredit

Components: dis- (away, apart) + cred (to believe) + -it

Definition: To damage the reputation or trust in someone. The prefix dis- reverses or removes the trust associated with the root.

Accredit

Components: ac- (toward) + cred (to trust) + -it

Definition: To officially recognize as meeting standards. Accreditation moves a person or institution 'toward' trust and recognition.

Incredible

Components: in- (not) + cred (to believe) + -ible (able)

Definition: Impossible to believe; extraordinary. Literally 'not able to be believed,' though it often conveys amazement rather than disbelief.

Credulous

Components: cred (to believe) + -ulous (tending to)

Definition: Too willing to believe things; gullible. A credulous person is excessively inclined to trust what they are told.

Patterns and Observations

Understanding how prefixes and suffixes modify the base root is the key to unlocking unfamiliar vocabulary. Notice how:

  • Prefixes change the direction or scope of the root meaning (e.g., dis- means 'away,' in- means 'not,' ac- means 'toward').
  • Suffixes change the part of speech or specify the quality (e.g., -ible means 'able to be,' -ulous means 'tending to,' -ential means 'relating to').
  • The same root can produce nouns, verbs, and adjectives depending on the suffix attached.

Review Questions

  1. Choose any three words from this family and explain how their components combine to create meaning.
  2. Which prefixes in this word family change the root meaning most dramatically? Give examples.
  3. Can you think of additional English words not listed here that might share the same root? What do they mean?
  4. How does understanding the root and affix structure help you remember these words' meanings?
  5. Write a sentence using at least two words from this family that demonstrates their meanings.
