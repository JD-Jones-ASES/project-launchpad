Study Guide: Word Family - Cred

Overview

This section explores the "cred" word family, focusing on the Latin root cred, which means "believe" or "trust." Understanding this root provides a powerful tool for deciphering the meanings of many English words, helping to expand vocabulary and improve comprehension by recognizing common patterns and shared core concepts.

Key Concepts

  • The Root cred: The fundamental meaning of the Latin root cred is "to believe" or "to trust." This core meaning is central to all words in its family.
  • Vocabulary Expansion: Recognizing the root cred allows you to infer the meaning of unfamiliar words that contain it, even if you haven't encountered them before.
  • Nuance and Context: While all cred words relate to belief or trust, their specific meanings are refined by prefixes and suffixes, as well as the context in which they are used. This helps to differentiate words like "credible" (able to be believed) from "credulous" (too willing to believe).
  • Everyday Application: Words from the cred family are commonly used in daily conversation, news, academic discourse, and professional settings, making them essential for effective communication.

Important Terms

  • Credibility: (n.) The quality of being trusted and believed in.
  • Credible: (adj.) Able to be believed; convincing.
  • Credulous: (adj.) Having or showing too great a readiness to believe things; gullible.
  • Incredible: (adj.) Impossible to believe; astonishing or amazing.
  • Credit: (n.) The ability of a customer to obtain goods or services before payment, based on the trust that payment will be made in the future; (v.) To believe that someone or something is responsible for something.
  • Creed: (n.) A system of religious belief; a faith; a set of beliefs or aims that guide someone's actions.
  • Discredit: (v.) To harm the good reputation of someone or something; to cause an idea or evidence to be disbelieved.
  • Accredit: (v.) To give official authorization or approval to; to recognize someone or something as having a particular status or quality.
  • Credence: (n.) Belief in or acceptance of something as true.
  • Incredulity: (n.) The state of being unwilling or unable to believe something.

Detailed Notes

The word family originating from the Latin root cred is rich and varied, with each member retaining a connection to the core ideas of "belief" and "trust." By exploring these connections, we can gain a deeper understanding of English vocabulary.

The Foundation: cred = "believe," "trust"

The root cred comes from the Latin verb credere, meaning "to believe," "to trust," or "to entrust." This fundamental meaning is the cornerstone of all words in this family. Whether you are giving belief, receiving belief, or expressing a state of belief or disbelief, the idea of cred is always present.

Words About Being Believed or Believable

  • Credible (adj.): This word directly relates to the ability to be believed. If a story is "credible," it means it is plausible and trustworthy. A "credible witness" is one whose testimony can be believed.
    • Example: The journalist presented credible evidence to support her claims.
  • Credibility (n.): This is the noun form of credible, referring to the quality of being believable or trustworthy. Building one's credibility is essential in many professions, such as law or journalism.
    • Example: The politician's credibility was questioned after the scandal.
  • Credence (n.): This term refers to the belief in or acceptance of something as true. To "give credence" to a rumor means to believe it.
    • Example: The outlandish story was given little credence by the jury.

Words About Believing (or Not Believing) Too Much or Too Little

  • Credulous (adj.): This word describes someone who is too ready to believe things, often without sufficient evidence. A credulous person is easily fooled or tricked because they tend to accept things at face value. It carries a slightly negative connotation, implying gullibility.
    • Example: The credulous tourist fell for the obvious scam.
  • Incredulous (adj.): The prefix in- often means "not" or "opposite." Thus, "incredulous" means unwilling or unable to believe something. Someone who is incredulous is skeptical or surprised.
    • Example: She gave an incredulous gasp when she heard the news.
  • Incredible (adj.): Similar to incredulous, but typically refers to the thing being unbelievable, rather than the person's reaction. It can mean "impossible to believe," but it often used colloquially to mean "astonishing" or "amazing" in a positive sense.
    • Example (impossible to believe): The plot of the movie was utterly incredible.
    • Example (amazing): The athlete made an incredible comeback.
  • Incredulity (n.): The noun form of incredulous, signifying a state of disbelief or wonder.
    • Example: He shook his head in incredulity at the magic trick.

Words About Giving or Attributing Belief/Trust

  • Credit (n./v.): This is one of the most common words in the family.
    • As a noun, "credit" refers to the trust that allows someone to borrow money or buy goods with a promise of future payment (e.g., credit card). It also refers to recognition for an achievement (e.g., giving credit where it's due).
    • As a verb, "to credit" means to believe something (e.g., "I credit him with honesty") or to attribute something to someone (e.g., "She credited her success to hard work").
    • Example (noun - finance): It's important to maintain good credit.
    • Example (verb - attribute): The invention is credited to Thomas Edison.
  • Discredit (v.): The prefix dis- typically means "apart," "away," or "the opposite of." To "discredit" something means to harm its reputation, make it no longer believed, or cause it to lose trust.
    • Example: The defense lawyer tried to discredit the witness's testimony.
  • Accredit (v.): The prefix ac- (a form of ad-) means "to" or "towards." To "accredit" means to officially recognize or approve something as having a particular status or meeting certain standards. It's about giving formal belief or trust to an institution or individual.
    • Example: The university is accredited by a national board.

Words Related to Formal Belief Systems

  • Creed (n.): This word refers to a formal statement of religious belief, a set of principles, or a guiding philosophy. It's about a collective system of belief that members of a group hold in common.
    • Example: The organization's creed emphasized honesty and integrity.

Understanding Word Relationships

By observing these words, we can see how prefixes (in-, dis-, ac-) and suffixes (-ible, -ous, -ity) subtly shift the meaning of the core root cred.

  • cred + -ible = able to be believed
  • cred + -ulous = inclined to believe (often too easily)
  • in- + cred + -ible = not able to be believed (or astonishing)
  • dis- + credit = take away belief/trust

This systematic approach makes learning new vocabulary more efficient and logical.
