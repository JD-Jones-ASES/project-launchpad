Study Guide: Chapter 19 — JUS / JUR / JUDIC (Law, Right, Judge)

Overview

Chapter 19 explores three related Latin roots — jus/juris (law, right), jur (to swear), and judic (judge) — that together form the backbone of English legal vocabulary. These roots entered English through the Roman legal tradition, which gave Western civilization the idea that law should be written, public, and applied equally to all citizens. Students will learn how these roots combine with prefixes and suffixes to create words used in courtrooms and everyday life.

Key Concepts

  • Root jus / juris — from Latin jus, juris (law, right); appears in justice, jurisdiction, jurisprudence
  • Root jur — from Latin jurare (to swear an oath); appears in jury, perjury
  • Root judic — from Latin judex (a judge); built from jus + dicere (to say); appears in judicial, adjudicate, prejudice
  • Prefix patterns: pre- (before) + judic = prejudice; ad- (toward) + judic = adjudicate; per- (wrongly) + jur = perjury
  • Key compound: judex = jus (law) + dicere (to say) — a judge is "one who says what the law is"

Important Terms

  • justice: just (right, law) + -ice — fairness; the quality of being just
  • jury: jur (to swear) — a group of citizens sworn to judge a case fairly
  • jurisdiction: juris (of law) + dict (to say) + -ion — the authority to declare and apply the law within a territory
  • judicial: judic (judge) + -ial — relating to courts, judges, or the law
  • adjudicate: ad- (toward) + judic (judge) + -ate — to make a formal judgment on a dispute
  • perjury: per- (wrongly) + jur (to swear) — the crime of lying under oath
  • prejudice: pre- (before) + judic (judgment) — an opinion formed before knowing the facts
  • jurisprudence: juris (of law) + prudentia (wisdom) — the study and philosophy of law

Detailed Notes

The Twelve Tables and Roman Law

Around 450 BCE, Roman citizens demanded that their laws be written down. The result — twelve bronze tablets displayed in the Roman Forum — was a turning point in Western civilization. The idea that law (jus) should be public, knowable, and applied equally was revolutionary. This moment is the direct ancestor of constitutional law in modern democracies.

Three Roots, One Legal System

All three roots connect to the Roman legal tradition. Jus/juris refers to law as a concept and a body of rights. Jur refers to the act of swearing — essential in any legal system where witnesses must take oaths. Judic refers to the judge who interprets and declares the law. Together, they cover the three pillars of any legal proceeding: the law itself, the oath of truth, and the judgment.

Hidden Etymology: Judex = Jus + Dicere

One of the most elegant etymological discoveries in this chapter is that judex (judge) is itself a compound word. It combines jus (law) with dicere (to say), making a judge literally "one who says what the law is." This shows how Roman legal thinkers understood judging not as force or power, but as declaration and interpretation.

Prejudice: A Word Worth Examining

Prejudice combines pre- (before) + judicium (judgment). To prejudge someone is to form an opinion before the evidence is in. This is as much a logical error as a moral one — it violates the Roman principle that law (jus) should be applied fairly, based on facts rather than assumptions.

Jurisprudence

The word jurisprudence literally means "wisdom about the law" (juris + prudentia). Roman jurists who studied jurisprudentia were considered among the wisest citizens of the Republic. Today, jurisprudence is the branch of philosophy that studies the nature, purpose, and foundations of law.

Review Questions

  1. What were the Twelve Tables, and why did Roman citizens demand them? How does this relate to the root jus?
  2. How is judex a compound word? What does its etymology tell us about what judges are supposed to do?
  3. Explain the difference between perjury and prejudice. What do their prefixes (per- and pre-) contribute to their meanings?
  4. Why would losing trust be such a serious social punishment in Roman society? How does this connect to the root cred from Chapter 20?
  5. A teacher says, "The adjudicator will evaluate all science fair projects." Using your knowledge of roots, explain what an adjudicator does.
