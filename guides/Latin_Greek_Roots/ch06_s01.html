Study Guide: Perception and Being Affected – An Aristotelian Introduction

Overview

This section opens with a powerful epigraph from Aristotle's De Anima (On the Soul): "Sentire est pati," which translates to "To perceive is to be affected." This concise statement introduces a fundamental philosophical concept, suggesting that perception is not a passive process of simply receiving information, but rather an active engagement where the perceiver undergoes some form of change or influence. It sets a profound philosophical tone for understanding the nature of sensation and experience, likely foreshadowing deeper discussions on perception, consciousness, or the senses in the forthcoming chapter.

Key Concepts

  • Active Perception: The idea that perception is not merely a passive reception of sensory data, but an active process that involves the perceiver being influenced or changed by what is perceived.
  • The Affective Nature of Sensation: The core concept that to sense something is inherently to be acted upon, to experience an impact, or to undergo a modification. This "affection" is central to understanding how we interact with the world through our senses.
  • Aristotle's Philosophy of the Soul: The quote originates from De Anima, Aristotle's seminal work on the soul. For Aristotle, the soul (or psyche) is the animating principle of living beings, encompassing faculties like nutrition, growth, locomotion, sensation, and thought. Perception is one of the soul's primary functions.
  • Form without Matter: In Aristotle's view, when we perceive, our sense organs receive the "form" of the object without its "matter." For example, seeing a red apple means our eye is affected by the form of redness, not that we literally absorb part of the apple itself. This "affection" is the reception of the form.
  • Interconnectedness of Experience: The epigraph implies a deep connection between the external world and our internal state, highlighting how sensory input inherently shapes our experience and being.

Important Terms

  • Epigraph: A short quotation or saying at the beginning of a book, chapter, or section, intended to suggest its theme.
  • Sentire: (Latin verb) To perceive, to feel, to sense, to become aware of, to think.
  • Pati: (Latin verb) To suffer, to endure, to undergo, to experience, to be affected or acted upon.
  • Sentire est pati: (Latin phrase) "To perceive is to be affected" or "To sense is to suffer/endure."
  • Aristotle: (384–322 BC) Ancient Greek philosopher and polymath, student of Plato and teacher of Alexander the Great. His writings cover a vast range of subjects including physics, metaphysics, ethics, logic, rhetoric, poetics, biology, and politics.
  • De Anima: (Latin, meaning "On the Soul") Aristotle's treatise on the nature of living beings, specifically exploring the concept of the soul and its various faculties, including sensation, imagination, memory, and intellect.
  • Perception: The ability to see, hear, or become aware of something through the senses. In a broader philosophical sense, it refers to the process of acquiring, interpreting, selecting, and organizing sensory information.
  • Affection/Affectation: The process or state of being influenced, acted upon, or changed by something external. It can also refer to a feeling or emotion.

Detailed Notes

The Role of an Epigraph

An epigraph serves as a literary device placed at the beginning of a text, chapter, or section. Its purpose is multifaceted: it can set the tone, introduce a central theme, offer a summary, or hint at the philosophical underpinnings of the content that follows. In this case, the epigraph "Sentire est pati" from Aristotle's De Anima is strategically chosen to prime the reader for a deep dive into the nature of perception and sensation, signaling that the upcoming chapter will likely explore these concepts with philosophical depth. The source "from: sens" further reinforces that the focus will be on the senses.

Dissecting "Sentire est pati"

This short Latin phrase carries profound philosophical weight:

  • "Sentire" (To perceive, to feel, to sense): This verb points to the act of engaging with the world through our sensory faculties. It's about gathering information from our environment through sight, hearing, touch, taste, and smell.
  • "Pati" (To suffer, to endure, to be affected, to experience): This verb is crucial. While "suffer" or "endure" might suggest pain or hardship, in a philosophical context, "pati" more broadly means to be acted upon, to be susceptible to change, or to undergo an experience. It implies that perception is not a neutral, objective observation, but something that inherently impacts the perceiver.

Taken together, "Sentire est pati" argues that the very act of perceiving means that the perceiver is affected by the object perceived. Our senses are not passive receivers, but rather organs that are changed or modified by the incoming stimuli. This change constitutes the act of sensing.

Aristotle's Philosophy of Perception in De Anima

Aristotle's De Anima (On the Soul) is a foundational text in Western philosophy and an early systematic work in psychology. In it, Aristotle explores the nature of the "soul" (psychē), which for him is not separate from the body but rather its "form" or organizing principle. The soul is what gives a living thing its characteristic activities, including perception.

For Aristotle, sensation is the capacity of a living being to receive the form of a perceived object without its matter. To illustrate:

  • When we see a red object, our eye takes on the "form" of redness, but we don't physically absorb the red paint or pigment.
  • When we feel a hot object, our sense of touch is affected by the "form" of heat, becoming hot in a sensory way, but we don't literally take in the heat energy as matter.

This act of receiving the form is precisely what Aristotle means by "being affected" or "pati." The sense organ changes to become like the object it perceives, mirroring its quality. This "affection" is not a destruction of the sense organ, but a fulfillment of its potential to sense. The sense organ has the potential to become hot, cold, red, sweet, etc., and when it encounters an actual hot, cold, red, or sweet object, that potential is actualized, and the organ is "affected."

Implications and Relevance

  1. Subjectivity of Experience: The idea that perception involves being affected highlights the subjective nature of experience. Each individual's unique sensory apparatus and internal state will be affected in their own way, shaping their personal reality.
  2. Mind-Body Connection: Aristotle's view, though ancient, resonates with modern discussions about the mind-body problem. His insistence that the soul (and thus perception) is inseparable from the body underscores the intrinsic link between our physical being and our mental experiences.
  3. Foundation for Psychology: By emphasizing the interactive and transformative nature of perception, Aristotle laid groundwork for later psychological theories that explore how environmental stimuli impact our cognitive and emotional states. The phrase "from: sens" clearly indicates that the subsequent chapter will build upon this fundamental understanding of the senses.
  4. Beyond Passive Observation: The epigraph challenges the naive view that perception is simply a camera-like recording of external reality. Instead, it suggests an active, dynamic process where the perceiver is always transformed, even subtly, by what they encounter. This sets the stage for exploring how our senses shape us, not just inform us.

This short but profound epigraph thus serves as an invitation to consider perception not as a mere input mechanism, but as a fundamental mode of being, where to truly sense is to be open to influence, change, and the rich, complex experience of the world.

Review Questions

  1. What is the literal translation of "Sentire est pati," and how does the philosophical interpretation of "pati" extend beyond its common meaning of "to suffer"?
  2. According to the epigraph, what is the fundamental nature of perception, and how does this contrast with a view of perception as purely passive observation?
  3. Briefly explain Aristotle's concept of the "soul" (psychē) and how sensation fits into his broader understanding of living beings, particularly as it relates to receiving "form without matter."
  4. Why might this particular epigraph be chosen to introduce a chapter or section on "senses" or "perception"? What kind of philosophical framework does it establish for the reader?
  5. In what ways can the idea of "being affected" by our perceptions influence our understanding of subjective experience, emotions, or even learning? Provide an example.
