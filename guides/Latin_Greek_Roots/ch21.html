Study Guide: Chapter 21 — BIO / LOG (Life; Word, Reason, Study)

Overview

Chapter 21 marks the transition from Latin to Greek roots. It introduces two of the most productive Greek roots in the English language: bio (life, from Greek bios) and log/logos (word, reason, study). These roots joined together to form biology — literally "the study of life" — and appear across dozens of English words in science, philosophy, and everyday speech. Students will also learn how Latin life-roots (vit/viv) and Greek life-roots (bio) divide the vocabulary of "life" between everyday and scientific contexts.

Key Concepts

  • Root bio — from Greek bios (life, way of living); appears in biology, biography; produces scientific and academic vocabulary
  • Root log / logos — from Greek logos (word, reason, speech, account); as -logy means "the study of"; appears in logic, theology, eulogy, analogy, dialogue
  • Pattern: Latin (vit/viv) → everyday words; Greek (bio) → scientific terms. This is a key pattern throughout the book.
  • Prefix patterns: eu- (good) + log = eulogy; dia- (between) + logue = dialogue; ana- (according to) + log = analogy

Important Terms

  • biology: bio (life) + -logy (study of) — the scientific study of living organisms
  • biography: bio (life) + graphein (to write) — the written story of a person's life
  • ecology: oikos (house, habitat) + -logy — the study of organisms and their environments
  • logic: log (reason) + -ic — the science of correct reasoning
  • theology: theos (god) + -logy — the study of the nature of God and religion
  • eulogy: eu- (good) + log (word, speech) — a speech of good words praising someone, often at a funeral
  • analogy: ana- (according to) + log (reason, proportion) — a comparison showing how two things are alike
  • dialogue: dia- (between) + logue (speech) — a conversation between two or more people

Detailed Notes

Greek Roots: A Different Entry Point

Latin roots entered English through soldiers, senators, and the Roman Empire. Greek roots entered through scholars, scientists, and philosophers. This is why Greek roots tend to appear in academic and technical vocabulary, while Latin roots appear in everyday speech. Recognizing this pattern will help students decode unfamiliar words throughout their education.

Bio: Life as Science

The Greek root bios meant "life" or "way of living." When Greek thinkers wanted to name the science of life, they combined bios with logos to create biologia. A biography (bios + graphein, to write) is the written account of someone's life. These words are formal and scholarly — contrast them with the more everyday vital (Latin) or vivid (Latin).

Logos: The Most Versatile Greek Root

Few roots have been as productive as logos. It could mean "word," "speech," "reason," or "account." As -logy at the end of a word, it signals the name of a field of study. The pattern is remarkably consistent: biology, theology, ecology, zoology, geology, psychology all follow the same structure. Logos also appears in standalone words: logic is the art of reasoning; dialogue is speech between people; eulogy is good speech praising someone.

Eulogy and the Prefix eu-

The prefix eu- (good, well) appears in several important English words. A eulogy is literally "good words" — appropriate for a funeral speech celebrating someone's life. Students may also encounter euphony (good sound, Chapter 23) and euphemism (good speech, a polite replacement for a harsh word).

Analogy

An analogy compares two things by examining their proportional relationship. The ana- prefix means "according to" or "in proportion," and logos here means "reason." When you say "a captain is to a ship as a pilot is to a plane," you are drawing an analogy — showing a parallel structure of relationships.

Review Questions

  1. Why do Greek roots tend to appear in scientific and academic words while Latin roots appear in everyday speech?
  2. What does -logy mean, and what Greek word does it come from? Name four fields of study that use this suffix.
  3. Compare biography and biography. How are the roots bio and graph combined, and what does each contribute?
  4. The prefix eu- means "good." Using this knowledge, what do you think euphony means? What about a euphemism?
  5. How does an analogy work? Give an original example of an analogy and explain what the logos (reasoning) connection is.
