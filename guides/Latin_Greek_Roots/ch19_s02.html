Root Profile: jus / jur / judic (Law, Right; To Judge)

Overview

The Latin roots jus, jur, and judic come from jus, juris (law, right) and judex, judicis (judge). These roots form the backbone of English legal and judicial vocabulary.

Root Information

  • Root(s): jus / jur / judic
  • Meaning: law, right; to judge
  • Origin: Latin jus, juris (law, right), judex, judicis (judge)
  • Variants: just (in justice, adjust)
  • Key Examples: justice, jury, judicial, prejudice

Connections to Other Roots

This root family connects to dict (to say, to speak) through the compound judex (literally 'one who says the law'). It also relates to leg (law) from the legislative tradition.

Detailed Notes

Historical Context

Roman law (jus Romanum) is one of the most enduring legacies of ancient Rome. The distinction between jus (abstract right or law) and lex (specific statute) shaped Western legal thinking for millennia. The figure of the judex (judge) -- literally 'one who declares the right' -- embodied the Roman ideal of impartial justice.

How the Root Appears in English

  • jus-/just-: appears in words about fairness and rightness (justice, justify, adjust)
  • jur-: appears in words about swearing oaths and legal authority (jury, jurisdiction, perjury)
  • judic-: appears in words about the act of judging (judicial, adjudicate, prejudice)

Recognizing the Root in Unfamiliar Words

When you encounter an unfamiliar word containing jus, jur, or judic, consider whether its meaning relates to:

  • Fairness or moral rightness (justice, justify)
  • Sworn oaths or legal obligation (jury, perjury, abjure)
  • The act or authority of judging (judicial, adjudicate, jurisdiction)
  • Pre-formed judgments (prejudice, prejudge)
