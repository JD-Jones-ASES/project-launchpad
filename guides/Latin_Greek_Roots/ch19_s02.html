Study Guide: The Roots of Roman Law — Jus and Judic

Overview

This section explores the historical origins of Western legal thought, tracing it back to the Roman Republic's Twelve Tables. It then delves into the Latin roots jus (law, right) and judic (judge), demonstrating how a vast English legal vocabulary is derived from these ancient concepts and their combined forms.

Key Concepts

  • The Twelve Tables as a Turning Point: The demand by Roman citizens to have laws written down resulted in the Twelve Tables, establishing the principle that law (jus) should be public, knowable, and applied equally to all. This was a critical development for Western civilization.
  • The Root Jus: Derived from the Latin noun jus (meaning "law" or "right"), this root forms the basis for many English legal terms related to fairness, authority, and oaths.
  • The Root Judic: Stemming from judex (a judge), which itself combines jus and dicere ("to say"), this root signifies concepts related to judging, courts, and formal dispute resolution. A judge is fundamentally "one who says what the law is."
  • Word Building Through Root Combination: Latin roots often combine to create complex words, as seen in judex (jus + dicere) and jurisdiction (jus + dicere).
  • Prefixes Modifying Root Meanings: Prefixes like per- and pre- can alter the meaning of a root, sometimes giving a negative connotation, as in perjury (a corrupted oath) and prejudice (to judge before knowing the facts).
  • Jurisprudence as "Wisdom of Law": The term jurisprudence, the philosophy of law, combines juris (of law) with prudentia (wisdom), reflecting the deep thought and study involved in understanding legal principles.

Important Terms

  • Twelve Tables: The fundamental written laws of the Roman Republic, displayed publicly around 450 BCE, marking a shift towards public and knowable law.
  • Patrician: A member of a noble family in ancient Rome, who historically interpreted unwritten laws before the Twelve Tables.
  • Roman Forum: The central public space in ancient Rome where the Twelve Tables were displayed.
  • Jus: (Latin noun) Meaning "law" or "right"; the root for many English legal words.
  • Justice: The quality of being just or fair; derived from the Latin justitia.
  • Jury: A group of citizens who take an oath to judge a case honestly; related to jurare ("to swear").
  • Jurisdiction: The authority to "declare the law" within a certain territory; from jus (law) + dicere (to say).
  • Judex: (Latin noun) A judge; formed from jus (law) + dicere (to say), literally "one who says what the law is."
  • Judicial: Relating to courts or judges.
  • Adjudicate: To "judge toward" a decision, settling a dispute formally.
  • Perjury: The crime of lying under oath; per- (through, wrongly) + jurare (to swear).
  • Prejudice: To judge someone before knowing the facts; pre- (before) + judicium (judgment).
  • Jurisprudence: The philosophy or science of law; combines juris (of law) + prudentia (wisdom, foresight).

Detailed Notes

The Foundation of Public Law: The Twelve Tables

  • Historical Context: Around 450 BCE, Roman citizens, feeling that unwritten laws interpreted by patrician judges led to unfair treatment, demanded that the laws be codified.
  • Result: The creation of the Twelve Tables, twelve bronze tablets inscribed with fundamental laws, displayed in the Roman Forum.
  • Significance: This act was a pivotal moment in Western civilization. It established the principle that law (jus) should be:
    • Public: Known to all citizens.
    • Knowable: Clearly written and accessible.
    • Applied Equally: Not subject to arbitrary interpretation by a select few.

The Root Jus (Law, Right)

  • The Latin noun jus (genitive juris) is the source of a vast amount of English legal terminology.
  • Justice: Directly from justitia, meaning the quality of being just or fair.
  • Jury: A group of citizens who swear an oath (jurare means "to swear") to judge a case honestly.
  • Jurisdiction: The authority to "declare the law" within a specific geographical or legal sphere. This word is a combination of jus (law) and dicere (to say).

The Root Judic (Judge)

  • The root judic originates from judex, which is the Latin word for a judge.
  • Composition of Judex: Interestingly, judex itself is formed by combining jus (law) and dicere (to say). Therefore, a judge is literally "one who says what the law is." This highlights how Latin roots combine to form complex words.
  • Judicial: An adjective describing anything that relates to courts or judges.
  • Adjudicate: A verb meaning to formally settle a dispute or "judge toward" a decision.

Words with Negative Connotations

  • Perjury: This crime involves lying under oath. The prefix per- here means "through" in a negative sense, implying "wrongly" or even "to destruction," indicating a corrupted oath.
  • Prejudice: This term combines the prefix pre- (meaning "before") with judicium (meaning "judgment"). To prejudice someone is to form a judgment about them "before" knowing all the relevant facts.

The Philosophy of Law: Jurisprudence

  • Etymology: The word jurisprudence combines juris (from jus, meaning "of law") with prudentia (meaning "wisdom" or "foresight").
  • Meaning: It literally means "wisdom about the law" or the philosophy of law.
  • Historical Context: Roman jurists, who were scholars dedicated to the study of jurisprudentia, were highly regarded for their wisdom and understanding of legal principles.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the historical significance of the Twelve Tables in ancient Rome. How did their creation change the understanding and application of law?
  2. The root jus means "law" or "right." Discuss how the concept of jus as established by the Twelve Tables influenced the development of legal systems in Western civilization.
  3. Analyze the relationship between the roots jus and judic. How does the word judex (judge) demonstrate the combined meaning of these two roots?
  4. Examine how prefixes like per- and pre- alter the meaning of root words in perjury and prejudice. What do these examples teach us about the flexibility of Latin-based vocabulary?
  5. What is jurisprudence? Why were Roman jurists, who studied jurisprudentia, considered particularly wise citizens of the Republic?
