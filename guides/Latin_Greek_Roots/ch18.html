Study Guide: Chapter 18 — MORT / MOR (Death)

Overview

Chapter 18 explores the Latin roots mort and mor, both derived from the Latin words for death (mors/mortis) and dying (mori). These roots appear in English words ranging from everyday vocabulary (mortal, mortify) to legal and financial terms (mortgage, post-mortem). Students will learn how the Romans confronted death philosophically and how that attitude shaped the words we still use today.

Key Concepts

  • Root mort — from Latin mors, mortis (death); appears in mortal, immortal, mortify, mortgage, mortuary
  • Root mor — from Latin mori (to die); appears in moribund
  • Prefix patterns: im- (not) + mort = immortal; post- (after) + mort = post-mortem
  • Suffix patterns: -al (relating to), -ify (to make), -uary (place of), -bund (about to)
  • Thematic contrast: mort/mor (death) is the natural opposite of vit/viv (life) from Chapter 17

Important Terms

  • mortal: mort (death) + -al — subject to death; causing death; describes any creature that will eventually die
  • immortal: im- (not) + mort + -al — living forever; not subject to death; describes gods and legends
  • mortify: mort (death) + -ify (to make) — originally "to put to death"; now means to cause great embarrassment or shame
  • mortgage: mort (death) + gage (pledge) — a loan secured by property; the pledge "dies" when the debt is paid
  • moribund: mori (to die) + -bund (about to) — at the point of death; in terminal decline
  • post-mortem: post- (after) + mort (death) — an examination conducted after death
  • mortuary: mort (death) + -uary (place of) — a place where dead bodies are kept before burial or cremation

Detailed Notes

Roman Attitudes Toward Death

Death was a constant presence in Roman culture. Generals received a whispered reminder during their triumphs: Memento mori — "Remember that you will die." Gladiators greeted the emperor with Morituri te salutant — "Those who are about to die salute you." This philosophical acceptance of mortality shaped how the Romans talked about death, and those words passed into English.

The Mortal/Immortal Contrast

The most basic word from this root is mortal — any being that will eventually die. Adding the prefix im- (not) creates immortal, describing gods or legends that endure forever. This prefix pattern appears again in other chapters: just as im- makes mort into immortal, it also makes possible into impossible.

Mortify: A Word That Softened

Mortify originally meant "to put to death" in Late Latin (mortificare). Over centuries, the sense weakened from physical death to social death — the extreme humiliation of wishing you could disappear. This is a good example of how word meanings drift over time while roots remain constant.

Mortgage: Death in Finance

The word mortgage comes from Old French: mort (death) + gage (pledge). The pledge is called a death pledge because it either dies when the debt is fully paid, or the property dies (is forfeited) if the borrower cannot pay. This hidden etymology makes mortgage one of the most dramatic financial words in English.

Moribund and Post-Mortem

Moribund (about to die) is used both literally (a moribund patient) and figuratively (a moribund industry). Post-mortem (after death) is used in medical examinations to determine cause of death, but also in everyday language for any analysis conducted after something ends — a post-mortem review of a failed project, for instance.

Review Questions

  1. What does Memento mori mean, and why would Romans say this to a celebrating general?
  2. Explain the etymology of mortgage. Why is it called a "death pledge"?
  3. How has the meaning of mortify changed over time? What does the root still tell us about the original meaning?
  4. Compare mortal and moribund. How are they similar? How are they different?
  5. How does knowing the prefix im- (not) help you understand the word immortal? Can you think of two other words where im- means "not"?
