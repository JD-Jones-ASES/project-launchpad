Study Guide: Factoring II

Overview

This section significantly expands upon fundamental factoring techniques by introducing powerful new tools. It covers factoring sums and differences of cubes, recognizing and factoring polynomials in "quadratic form," and establishing the crucial connection between a polynomial's zeros and its factors through the Factor Theorem. Finally, the Rational Root Theorem provides a systematic approach for finding potential rational zeros of polynomials with integer coefficients.

Key Concepts

  • Difference of Squares (Review): The identity $a^2 - b^2 = (a+b)(a-b)$ is a fundamental pattern that can also appear with higher powers, requiring careful identification of $a$ and $b$.
  • Sum and Difference of Cubes: New factoring identities for $a^3 + b^3$ and $a^3 - b^3$. The resulting trinomial factors ($a^2 \mp ab + b^2$) do not factor further over the real numbers. The "SOAP" mnemonic helps recall the signs in the formulas.
  • Quadratic Form: A polynomial can be in quadratic form if it can be written as $au^2 + bu + c$, where $u$ is itself a power of $x$ (e.g., $x^2$, $x^3$). Factoring involves a substitution, factoring the resulting quadratic, and then substituting back.
  • Factor Theorem: A polynomial $P(x)$ has $(x - c)$ as a factor if and only if $P(c) = 0$. This theorem is the cornerstone for understanding the relationship between factors and zeros (roots) of polynomials, and it allows us to find factors once a zero is known.
  • Rational Root Theorem: For a polynomial with integer coefficients, this theorem provides a finite list of possible rational zeros ($p/q$). Here, $p$ must divide the constant term, and $q$ must divide the leading coefficient. This theorem helps narrow down the search for zeros when the Factor Theorem is applied.

Important Terms

  • Difference of Squares: A binomial in the form $a^2 - b^2$, which factors into $(a+b)(a-b)$.
  • Sum of Cubes: A binomial in the form $a^3 + b^3$, which factors into $(a+b)(a^2 - ab + b^2)$.
  • Difference of Cubes: A binomial in the form $a^3 - b^3$, which factors into $(a-b)(a^2 + ab + b^2)$.
  • Quadratic Form: A polynomial that can be expressed as $au^2 + bu + c$, where $u$ is an expression involving $x$ (often $x^n$ for some integer $n > 1$).
  • Factor Theorem: States that for a polynomial $P(x)$, $(x-c)$ is a factor if and only if $c$ is a root (or zero) of $P(x)$, meaning $P(c)=0$.
  • Zero of a Polynomial: A value $c$ for which the polynomial $P(c)$ evaluates to zero. If $c$ is a zero, then $(x-c)$ is a factor.
  • Rational Root Theorem: A theorem that provides a method for finding all possible rational roots (zeros) of a polynomial with integer coefficients by testing fractions $p/q$, where $p$ divides the constant term and $q$ divides the leading coefficient.
  • Synthetic Division: A shorthand method for dividing polynomials, especially useful when dividing by a linear factor of the form $(x-c)$. It is a key tool for applying the Factor Theorem efficiently.

Detailed Notes

Difference of Squares (Review)

Recall the fundamental difference of squares identity: $$ a^2 - b^2 = (a+b)(a-b) $$ This pattern is crucial and can appear "in disguise" when $a$ or $b$ involve higher powers or more complex expressions. For example, to factor $25x^4 - 16y^2$:

  1. Recognize $a^2 = 25x^4$, so $a = \sqrt{25x^4} = 5x^2$.
  2. Recognize $b^2 = 16y^2$, so $b = \sqrt{16y^2} = 4y$.
  3. Apply the formula: $(5x^2 + 4y)(5x^2 - 4y)$.

Sum and Difference of Cubes

These are two new, powerful factoring identities: $$ a^3 + b^3 = (a + b)(a^2 - ab + b^2) \ a^3 - b^3 = (a - b)(a^2 + ab + b^2) $$ A helpful mnemonic for the signs in the formulas is SOAP:

  • Same sign as the original binomial ($a \pm b$).
  • Opposite sign for the $ab$ term in the trinomial ($a^2 \mp ab + b^2$).
  • Always Positive for the last term in the trinomial ($+ b^2$).

It's important to note that the trinomial factors ($a^2 - ab + b^2$ and $a^2 + ab + b^2$) cannot be factored further over the real numbers.

Examples:

  • Factoring $x^3 + 27$:
    • Recognize $a = x$ and $b = 3$ (since $27 = 3^3$).
    • Apply the sum of cubes formula: $(x + 3)(x^2 - (x)(3) + 3^2) = (x + 3)(x^2 - 3x + 9)$.
  • Factoring $8x^3 - 125$:
    • Recognize $a = 2x$ (since $8x^3 = (2x)^3$) and $b = 5$ (since $125 = 5^3$).
    • Apply the difference of cubes formula: $(2x - 5)((2x)^2 + (2x)(5) + 5^2) = (2x - 5)(4x^2 + 10x + 25)$.
  • Factoring $54t^3 + 16$:
    • Always look for a Greatest Common Factor (GCF) first! The GCF is $2$.
    • $54t^3 + 16 = 2(27t^3 + 8)$.
    • Now, factor the expression inside the parentheses: $a = 3t$ (since $27t^3 = (3t)^3$) and $b = 2$ (since $8 = 2^3$).
    • Apply the sum of cubes formula: $2(3t + 2)((3t)^2 - (3t)(2) + 2^2) = 2(3t + 2)(9t^2 - 6t + 4)$.

Advanced Trinomial Factoring (Quadratic Form)

Some higher-degree polynomials can be factored by recognizing that they are in quadratic form. A polynomial is in quadratic form if it can be written as: $$ au^2 + bu + c $$ where $u$ itself is a power of $x$ (e.g., $u = x^2$, $u = x^3$).

Example: Factoring $x^4 - 5x^2 + 4$

  1. Identify $u$: Notice that $x^4 = (x^2)^2$. Let $u = x^2$.
  2. Substitute: The expression becomes $u^2 - 5u + 4$.
  3. Factor the quadratic: This factors into $(u - 1)(u - 4)$.
  4. Substitute back: Replace $u$ with $x^2$: $(x^2 - 1)(x^2 - 4)$.
  5. Factor completely: Each of these new factors is a difference of squares.
    • $x^2 - 1 = (x - 1)(x + 1)$
    • $x^2 - 4 = (x - 2)(x + 2)$
  6. Final factored form: $x^4 - 5x^2 + 4 = (x - 1)(x + 1)(x - 2)(x + 2)$.

The Factor Theorem

The Factor Theorem establishes a fundamental link between the zeros of a polynomial and its factors:

  • Let $P(x)$ be a polynomial. Then $(x - c)$ is a factor of $P(x)$ if and only if $P(c) = 0$.
  • Equivalently: $c$ is a zero of $P(x)$ if and only if $(x - c)$ divides $P(x)$ with a remainder of $0$.

This theorem is a direct consequence of the Remainder Theorem, which states that the remainder when dividing $P(x)$ by $(x-c)$ is $P(c)$.

Example: Using the Factor Theorem to factor $P(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 11x - 6$

  1. Test a value: We want to find a $c$ such that $P(c) = 0$. Let's try a simple integer like $x=2$. $$ P(2) = (2)^3 - 6(2)^2 + 11(2) - 6 = 8 - 24 + 22 - 6 = 0 $$
  2. Apply Factor Theorem: Since $P(2) = 0$, $(x - 2)$ is a factor of $P(x)$.
  3. Divide by the factor: Use synthetic division to find the other factor (the quotient).
       2 | 1  -6   11  -6
     |    2   -8   6
     ----------------
       1  -4    3   0
    The quotient is $x^2 - 4x + 3$.
  4. Factor the quotient: Factor the quadratic $x^2 - 4x + 3 = (x - 1)(x - 3)$.
  5. Complete factorization: $P(x) = (x - 1)(x - 2)(x - 3)$.

Example: Finding zeros using the Factor Theorem Given that $x = -3$ is a zero of $Q(x) = 2x^3 + 5x^2 - 4x - 3$, find all zeros of $Q(x)$.

  1. Apply Factor Theorem: Since $x = -3$ is a zero, $(x - (-3)) = (x + 3)$ is a factor.
  2. Divide by the factor: Use synthetic division with $c = -3$.
      -3 | 2   5  -4  -3
     |    -6   3   3
     ----------------
       2  -1  -1   0
    The quotient is $2x^2 - x - 1$.
  3. Factor the quotient: Factor the quadratic $2x^2 - x - 1 = (2x + 1)(x - 1)$.
  4. Find remaining zeros: Set each factor to zero:
    • $2x + 1 = 0 \Rightarrow x = -1/2$
    • $x - 1 = 0 \Rightarrow x = 1$
  5. All zeros: The zeros of $Q(x)$ are $x = -3$, $x = -1/2$, and $x = 1$.

The Rational Root Theorem

When factoring polynomials of degree 3 or higher, the Rational Root Theorem helps us find initial rational zeros to apply the Factor Theorem.

  • Let $P(x) = a_n x^n + a_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \dots + a_1 x + a_0$ be a polynomial with integer coefficients and $a_n \ne 0$.
  • If $p/q$ (in lowest terms) is a rational zero of $P(x)$, then:
    • $p$ must be a divisor of the constant term $a_0$.
    • $q$ must be a divisor of the leading coefficient $a_n$.

This theorem provides a list of candidates for rational zeros; it does not guarantee that any rational zeros exist.

Example: Finding all zeros of $P(x) = 2x^3 - 3x^2 - 8x - 3$

  1. List possible rational zeros $p/q$:

    • $p$ divides the constant term $a_0 = -3$: Possible $p$ values are $\pm 1, \pm 3$.
    • $q$ divides the leading coefficient $a_n = 2$: Possible $q$ values are $\pm 1, \pm 2$.
    • Possible rational zeros ($p/q$) are: $\pm \frac{1}{1}, \pm \frac{3}{1}, \pm \frac{1}{2}, \pm \frac{3}{2}$. This simplifies to: $\pm 1, \pm 3, \pm \frac{1}{2}, \pm \frac{3}{2}$.

  2. Test candidates: Start testing values from the list.

    • Try $P(1) = 2(1)^3 - 3(1)^2 - 8(1) - 3 = 2 - 3 - 8 - 3 = -12 \ne 0$.
    • Try $P(-1) = 2(-1)^3 - 3(-1)^2 - 8(-1) - 3 = -2 - 3 + 8 - 3 = 0$.
    • Since $P(-1) = 0$, $x = -1$ is a zero, and $(x + 1)$ is a factor.

  3. Divide by the factor: Use synthetic division with $c = -1$.

      -1 | 2  -3  -8  -3
     |    -2   5   3
     ----------------
       2  -5  -3   0

    The quotient is $2x^2 - 5x - 3$.

  4. Factor the quotient: Factor the quadratic $2x^2 - 5x - 3 = (2x + 1)(x - 3)$.

  5. Complete factorization and all zeros:

    • $P(x) = (x + 1)(2x + 1)(x - 3)$.
    • The zeros are $x = -1$, $x = -1/2$ (from $2x+1=0$), and $x = 3$ (from $x-3=0$).

Review Questions

  1. Explain how the Difference of Squares pattern, $a^2 - b^2 = (a+b)(a-b)$, can be applied to polynomials like $81x^6 - 49y^4$. Identify what $a$ and $b$ would represent in this specific case.
  2. Describe the two formulas for the sum and difference of cubes. How does the "SOAP" mnemonic help you remember the signs within these formulas? Why is it important to remember that the trinomial factor does not factor further over the real numbers?
  3. What does it mean for a polynomial to be in "quadratic form"? Outline the steps involved in factoring a polynomial like $x^6 - 7x^3 - 8$ using the quadratic form technique.
  4. State the Factor Theorem in your own words. If you find that $P(-5) = 0$ for a polynomial $P(x)$, what does this immediately tell you about a factor of $P(x)$? How would you then use synthetic division to find the other factors?
  5. Imagine you need to find all rational zeros of $P(x) = 4x^3 + 7x^2 - 14x - 6$. What are the first steps you would take using the Rational Root Theorem? List all the possible rational zeros you would need to test.
