Solving Inequalities in One Variable Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the concept of inequalities, which describe ranges of values rather than specific equality points. It covers the fundamental properties of inequalities, emphasizing the critical rule of reversing the inequality sign when multiplying or dividing by a negative number. The section also delves into solving compound inequalities (both "AND" and "OR" types) and applying these skills to real-world word problems.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of an Inequality: A mathematical statement comparing two expressions using symbols like less than ($<$), greater than ($>$), less than or equal to ($\le$), or greater than or equal to ($\ge$). Unlike equations, which usually have single-value solutions, inequalities typically have solution sets that are entire intervals of real numbers.
  • Properties of Inequality:
    • Addition/Subtraction Property: Adding or subtracting the same real number to both sides of an inequality does not change the direction of the inequality symbol.
    • Multiplication/Division by a Positive Number: Multiplying or dividing both sides of an inequality by a positive real number does not change the direction of the inequality symbol.
    • Multiplication/Division by a Negative Number: Multiplying or dividing both sides of an inequality by a negative real number requires reversing the direction of the inequality symbol. This is the most common source of error.
  • Solution Sets and Interval Notation: The solution to an inequality is often an infinite set of numbers. These are expressed efficiently using interval notation (e.g., $(a, b)$, $[a, \infty)$) and can be visualized on a number line with open or filled circles.
  • Compound Inequalities: These combine two or more inequalities.
    • "AND" Compound Inequality: Both conditions must be true simultaneously. The solution set is the intersection of the individual solution sets. These can often be written compactly as $a < x < b$.
    • "OR" Compound Inequality: At least one of the conditions must be true. The solution set is the union of the individual solution sets.
  • Inequalities in Word Problems: Real-world scenarios can be translated into inequalities by identifying key phrases such as "at most" ($\le$), "at least" ($\ge$), "no more than" ($\le$), and "no less than" ($\ge$).

Important Terms

  • Inequality: A mathematical statement asserting that two expressions are not equal or that one is larger/smaller than the other.
  • Solution Set: The collection of all real numbers that, when substituted into an inequality, make the inequality a true statement.
  • Interval Notation: A concise way to represent sets of real numbers over a continuous range, using parentheses () for exclusive endpoints (not included) and square brackets [] for inclusive endpoints (included).
  • Compound Inequality: A mathematical statement consisting of two or more inequalities joined by the words "and" or "or."
  • AND Compound Inequality: A compound inequality where the solution must satisfy all conditions simultaneously. The solution is the intersection of the individual solution sets.
  • OR Compound Inequality: A compound inequality where the solution must satisfy at least one of the conditions. The solution is the union of the individual solution sets.
  • Identity (Inequality): An inequality that is true for all possible real number values of the variable (e.g., $x+1 > x$). Its solution set is $(-\infty, \infty)$.
  • Contradiction (Inequality): An inequality that is never true for any real number value of the variable (e.g., $x+1 < x$). Its solution set is $\emptyset$ (the empty set).

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Solving Inequalities

Inequalities are used to describe situations where a quantity must fall within a range. They are solved using algebraic methods similar to equations, with one crucial difference. The solution to an inequality is typically an interval of real numbers, not a single value.

The Critical Rule: Reversing the Inequality Sign

The core principle distinguishing inequality solving from equation solving is: Whenever you multiply or divide both sides of an inequality by a negative number, you must reverse the direction of the inequality symbol.

  • Example: If you have $-2x < 6$, dividing by $-2$ means you must flip the sign: $x > -3$.
  • Common Error: Forgetting this rule is the most frequent mistake in solving inequalities.

Properties of Inequality

Let $a$, $b$, and $c$ be real numbers:

  • Addition/Subtraction: If $a < b$, then $a + c < b + c$ and $a - c < b - c$. (The inequality direction remains unchanged.)
  • Multiplication/Division by a Positive Number: If $a < b$ and $c > 0$, then $ac < bc$ and $\frac{a}{c} < \frac{b}{c}$. (The inequality direction remains unchanged.)
  • Multiplication/Division by a Negative Number: If $a < b$ and $c < 0$, then $ac > bc$ and $\frac{a}{c} > \frac{b}{c}$. (The inequality direction must be reversed!)

These properties apply to all inequality symbols ($>, \le, \ge$).

Steps for Solving Simple Inequalities

  1. Isolate the variable term: Use addition or subtraction to move constant terms to one side.
  2. Isolate the variable: Use multiplication or division to get the variable by itself.
    • Remember to flip the inequality sign if you multiply or divide by a negative number.
  3. Write the solution: Express the solution using interval notation and/or graph it on a number line.

Example 1: Simple Inequality (No Sign Flip) Solve $4x - 5 \ge 7$.

  1. Add 5 to both sides: $4x - 5 + 5 \ge 7 + 5 \implies 4x \ge 12$.
  2. Divide by 4 (a positive number, so no sign flip): $\frac{4x}{4} \ge \frac{12}{4} \implies x \ge 3$.
  3. Solution set: $[3, \infty)$. On a number line, a filled circle at 3 and shade to the right.

Example 2: Multi-Step Inequality (With Sign Flip) Solve $-3x + 2 > 11$.

  1. Subtract 2 from both sides: $-3x + 2 - 2 > 11 - 2 \implies -3x > 9$.
  2. Divide by $-3$ (a negative number, so FLIP THE SIGN): $\frac{-3x}{-3} < \frac{9}{-3} \implies x < -3$.
  3. Solution set: $(-\infty, -3)$. On a number line, an open circle at -3 and shade to the left.

Compound Inequalities

Compound inequalities involve two conditions connected by "and" or "or."

"AND" Compound Inequalities

  • Definition: Both inequalities must be true simultaneously. The solution is the intersection of the individual solutions.
  • Compact Form: Often written as $a < x < b$, meaning $a < x$ AND $x < b$.
  • Solving Strategy: Perform operations on all three parts of the inequality at the same time.

Example: Solve $-4 < 2x + 1 \le 9$.

  1. Subtract 1 from all three parts: $-4 - 1 < 2x + 1 - 1 \le 9 - 1 \implies -5 < 2x \le 8$.
  2. Divide all three parts by 2 (positive, no sign flip): $\frac{-5}{2} < \frac{2x}{2} \le \frac{8}{2} \implies -\frac{5}{2} < x \le 4$.
  3. Solution set: $(-\frac{5}{2}, 4]$. On a number line, an open circle at $-5/2$, a filled circle at 4, and shade between them.

"OR" Compound Inequalities

  • Definition: At least one of the inequalities must be true. The solution is the union of the individual solutions.
  • Solving Strategy: Solve each inequality separately, then combine their solution sets using the union symbol ($\cup$).

Example: Solve $2x + 5 < 1$ or $3x - 4 \ge 8$.

  1. Solve the left inequality: $2x + 5 < 1 \implies 2x < -4 \implies x < -2$. (Solution: $(-\infty, -2)$)
  2. Solve the right inequality: $3x - 4 \ge 8 \implies 3x \ge 12 \implies x \ge 4$. (Solution: $[4, \infty)$)
  3. Combine the solutions with "or" (union): $(-\infty, -2) \cup [4, \infty)$. On a number line, shade to the left of -2 (open circle) and to the right of 4 (filled circle).

Word Problems with Inequalities

Translate phrases into mathematical inequalities:

  • "at most" or "no more than" $\implies \le$
  • "at least" or "no less than" $\implies \ge$
  • "less than" $\implies <$
  • "greater than" $\implies >$

Example: The sum of two consecutive integers is at most 47. Find all such pairs.

  1. Define variables: Let the first integer be $n$. The next consecutive integer is $n+1$.
  2. Formulate the inequality: "The sum...is at most 47" translates to $n + (n+1) \le 47$.
  3. Solve the inequality: $2n + 1 \le 47$ $2n \le 46$ $n \le 23$.
  4. Interpret the solution: Since $n$ must be an integer and $n \le 23$, the possible values for $n$ are $..., 21, 22, 23$. The pairs are $(n, n+1)$: $..., (21, 22), (22, 23)$. (Check: $23+24 = 47 \le 47$. $24+25 = 49 > 47$, so $n=23$ is the largest possible first integer.)

Special Cases: Identities and Contradictions

Just like equations, inequalities can sometimes result in statements that are always true (identities) or never true (contradictions).

  • Identity: If solving leads to a true statement like $5 > 2$, the inequality is an identity, and its solution set is $(-\infty, \infty)$.
  • Contradiction: If solving leads to a false statement like $0 > 7$, the inequality is a contradiction, and its solution set is $\emptyset$ (the empty set).

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental difference between solving an equation and solving an inequality in terms of their solutions and the algebraic steps involved. When is the process different, and what is the key rule to remember?
  2. Describe the characteristics of an "AND" compound inequality versus an "OR" compound inequality. How does the solution set differ for each in terms of intersection and union? Provide a compact notation example for "AND" and an interval notation example for "OR."
  3. Solve the inequality $-4(x - 2) \le 3x + 15$ and express the solution in interval notation. Be sure to justify any instance where the inequality sign might need to be flipped.
  4. A student is taking a science class where grades are determined by the average of four quizzes. The first three quiz scores are 75, 82, and 70. What range of scores must the student get on the fourth quiz to achieve an average of at least 78 for all four quizzes?
  5. Consider the compound inequality $2x - 3 > 7$ or $5 - x \ge 2$. Solve this compound inequality and explain how to represent its solution on a number line and in interval notation.
