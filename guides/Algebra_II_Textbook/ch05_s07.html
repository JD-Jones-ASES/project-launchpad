Study Guide: Polynomial Inequalities

Overview

This section introduces the method for solving polynomial inequalities, which determine the values of x for which a polynomial expression is positive, negative, or zero. The core technique involves finding the polynomial's zeros (also called critical numbers) to divide the number line into intervals, then testing the sign of the polynomial within each interval using a "sign chart." This systematic approach efficiently identifies the solution set, often expressed in interval notation.

Key Concepts

  • Polynomial Inequality: An inequality involving a polynomial expression, such as $P(x) > 0$, $P(x) < 0$, $P(x) \ge 0$, or $P(x) \le 0$. The goal is to find all x-values that satisfy the inequality.
  • Zeros (Critical Numbers): The x-values where a polynomial $P(x) = 0$. These numbers are crucial because a polynomial can only change sign at its zeros. They partition the number line into distinct intervals.
  • Intervals: The sections of the number line created by the critical numbers. Within each interval, the polynomial maintains a consistent sign (either entirely positive or entirely negative).
  • Sign Chart Method (Interval Analysis Table): A systematic process for organizing information about the sign of a polynomial across different intervals. It involves:
    1. Getting zero on one side of the inequality.
    2. Factoring the polynomial.
    3. Finding the zeros (critical numbers).
    4. Dividing the number line into intervals using the zeros.
    5. Testing a value from each interval in the factored polynomial to determine its sign.
    6. Selecting the intervals that satisfy the original inequality.
  • Endpoint Inclusion/Exclusion:
    • For strict inequalities ($>$ or $<$), the critical numbers (zeros) are excluded from the solution set because the polynomial is zero at those points, not strictly positive or negative.
    • For non-strict inequalities ($\ge$ or $\le$), the critical numbers are included in the solution set because the polynomial can be zero as well as positive/negative.
  • Multiplicity of Zeros:
    • If a zero has an odd multiplicity (e.g., $(x-c)^1$, $(x-c)^3$), the polynomial changes sign at that zero.
    • If a zero has an even multiplicity (e.g., $(x-c)^2$, $(x-c)^4$), the polynomial does not change sign at that zero. The sign of the polynomial will be the same in the intervals on both sides of that zero.

Important Terms

  • Polynomial Inequality: An algebraic statement comparing a polynomial expression to zero or another polynomial using inequality symbols ($<, >, \le, \ge$).
  • Zeros of a Polynomial: The values of x for which the polynomial $P(x)$ equals zero. These are also known as roots.
  • Critical Numbers: In the context of polynomial inequalities, these are the zeros of the polynomial. They are "critical" because they mark the only points where the polynomial's sign can potentially change.
  • Sign Chart: A diagram or table used to determine the sign (positive or negative) of a polynomial expression over different intervals on the number line, based on its zeros.
  • Interval Notation: A way to write sets of real numbers using parentheses and brackets to denote intervals. Parentheses ( ) indicate excluded endpoints (for strict inequalities or infinity), while brackets [ ] indicate included endpoints (for non-strict inequalities).
  • Multiplicity: The number of times a particular factor appears in the factored form of a polynomial, which corresponds to the number of times its associated zero occurs.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Polynomial Inequalities

A polynomial inequality asks us to find all x-values that make a polynomial expression positive, negative, or equal to zero, depending on the inequality symbol. For example, $x^2 - x - 6 < 0$ asks for all x where the quadratic is negative. The fundamental principle is that a polynomial can only switch from positive to negative (or vice versa) by passing through zero. Thus, its zeros are the critical points where its sign might change.

The Sign Chart Method Explained

The sign chart method provides a structured way to solve polynomial inequalities.

  1. Standard Form: Always begin by rearranging the inequality so that one side is zero. This means you'll have $P(x) > 0$, $P(x) < 0$, $P(x) \ge 0$, or $P(x) \le 0$. (Example: $x^2 > 3x + 10$ becomes $x^2 - 3x - 10 > 0$).
  2. Factor Completely: Factor the polynomial $P(x)$ into its linear and irreducible quadratic factors. This step is crucial because the sign of the product of factors is easier to determine than the sign of the expanded polynomial. (Example: $x^2 - x - 6 = (x-3)(x+2)$).
  3. Find Zeros (Critical Numbers): Set each factor equal to zero and solve for x. These are your critical numbers. They are the points on the number line where the polynomial is zero. (Example: for $(x-3)(x+2)$, the zeros are $x=3$ and $x=-2$).
  4. Partition the Number Line into Intervals: Plot the critical numbers on a number line. These points divide the number line into several open intervals. (Example: Zeros $-2, 3$ create intervals $(-\infty, -2)$, $(-2, 3)$, $(3, \infty)$).
  5. Test Points and Build the Sign Chart:
    • Choose one test value from within each interval.
    • Substitute this test value into the factored form of $P(x)$. Alternatively, you can test the sign of each factor separately and then multiply the signs.
    • Record the sign of $P(x)$ (positive or negative) for each interval.
    • A sign chart visually represents these signs, often with a row for each factor and a final row for the product $P(x)$.
  6. Select Solution Intervals: Based on the original inequality:
    • If $P(x) > 0$, select intervals where $P(x)$ is positive.
    • If $P(x) < 0$, select intervals where $P(x)$ is negative.
    • If $P(x) \ge 0$, select intervals where $P(x)$ is positive and include the zeros themselves.
    • If $P(x) \le 0$, select intervals where $P(x)$ is negative and include the zeros themselves.
  7. Write Solution in Interval Notation: Combine the selected intervals using the union symbol ($\cup$). Remember to use parentheses for strict inequalities and infinity, and brackets for non-strict inequalities at the zeros.

Special Cases and Considerations

  • Repeated Factors (Even Multiplicity): If a factor like $(x-c)^k$ appears where $k$ is an even number, then the term $(x-c)^k$ will always be non-negative (positive or zero). This means the sign of the polynomial does not change at $x=c$. The sign will be the same on both sides of $c$. If the inequality is strict ($>$ or $<$), then the zero c itself must be excluded from the solution, even if the surrounding intervals are part of the solution.
    • Example: For $(x+1)^2(x-3) > 0$, the zero $x=-1$ has multiplicity 2. The term $(x+1)^2$ is always $\ge 0$. The sign of the overall polynomial is dictated by $(x-3)$, except exactly at $x=-1$ where the polynomial is 0.
  • Quartic Inequalities (and higher): For polynomials that look like quadratics in a higher power (e.g., $x^4 - 5x^2 + 4 \le 0$), a substitution can simplify factoring. Let $u = x^2$ (or the appropriate power), factor the resulting quadratic in u, then substitute back and factor further.
  • Applied Problems: Polynomial inequalities can model real-world situations, such as projectile motion or profit functions. Remember to consider the domain of the variable (e.g., time $t$ usually cannot be negative). The final answer must make sense in the context of the problem. Sometimes, an inequality may have no solution (e.g., $(t-2)^2 < 0$, which is impossible).

Example Walkthrough (from text): $x^2 - x - 6 < 0$

  1. Form: Already $P(x) < 0$.
  2. Factor: $(x-3)(x+2) < 0$.
  3. Zeros: $x=3, x=-2$.
  4. Intervals: $(-\infty, -2)$, $(-2, 3)$, $(3, \infty)$.
  5. Sign Chart (Conceptual):
    • Test $x=-3$ in $(-\infty, -2)$: $(-3-3)(-3+2) = (-6)(-1) = 6$ (Positive).
    • Test $x=0$ in $(-2, 3)$: $(0-3)(0+2) = (-3)(2) = -6$ (Negative).
    • Test $x=4$ in $(3, \infty)$: $(4-3)(4+2) = (1)(6) = 6$ (Positive).
  6. Select: We want $P(x) < 0$ (negative), so select $(-2, 3)$. Endpoints are excluded due to strict inequality.
  7. Solution: $(-2, 3)$.

Review Questions

  1. Explain why finding the zeros of a polynomial is the first critical step in solving a polynomial inequality. What role do these zeros play on the number line?
  2. Describe the difference in how you handle endpoints (the zeros) when solving a strict inequality ($P(x) > 0$) versus a non-strict inequality ($P(x) \ge 0$). Provide an example for each.
  3. When constructing a sign chart, what is the purpose of "testing a value from each interval"? Why can you be confident that the sign determined by this test value applies to the entire interval?
  4. Consider the polynomial $P(x) = (x-1)^3(x+2)^2$. How would the multiplicity of its zeros affect the signs of the polynomial in the intervals around $x=1$ and $x=-2$? If you were solving $P(x) > 0$, how would $x=-2$ be treated differently from $x=1$?
  5. An object's height is given by $h(t) = -16t^2 + 80t + 5$. For what time interval is the object at or below a height of $69$ feet? Outline the steps you would take to solve this problem, including how you would deal with the inequality sign and the domain of t.
