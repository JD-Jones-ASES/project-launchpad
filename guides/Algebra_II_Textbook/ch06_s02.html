Study Guide: Adding and Subtracting Rational Expressions

Overview

This section explains how to add and subtract rational expressions, emphasizing that the process is very similar to adding and subtracting ordinary fractions. The core idea involves finding a common denominator, specifically the Least Common Denominator (LCD), for all expressions. Once a common denominator is established, the numerators are combined, and the resulting expression is simplified by factoring and canceling common factors.

Key Concepts

  • Common Denominators are Essential: Just like with numerical fractions, rational expressions must have a common denominator before their numerators can be added or subtracted.
  • Least Common Denominator (LCD): For polynomial denominators, finding the LCD is crucial. It is the product of all distinct prime polynomial factors, each raised to the highest power it appears in any denominator.
  • Factoring is Fundamental: Factoring denominators completely is the first step in finding the LCD and is also necessary for simplifying the final rational expression by canceling common factors.
  • Steps for Same Denominators: When denominators are already the same, simply add or subtract the numerators and place the result over the common denominator. Always simplify the final expression.
  • Steps for Different Denominators:
    1. Find the LCD.
    2. Rewrite each rational expression as an equivalent expression with the LCD by multiplying its numerator and denominator by the appropriate "missing" factors.
    3. Add or subtract the numerators.
    4. Keep the LCD as the new denominator.
    5. Simplify the final rational expression.
  • Restrictions: It's important to identify values for the variable that would make any denominator (original or LCD) equal to zero, as these values are excluded from the domain of the expression.

Important Terms

  • Rational Expression: A fraction where the numerator and denominator are polynomials. (Though not explicitly defined in the text, it's the subject matter.)
  • Common Denominator: A polynomial that is a multiple of the denominators of two or more rational expressions, allowing them to be combined.
  • Least Common Denominator (LCD): The smallest polynomial (in terms of degree and coefficients) that is a common multiple of the denominators of two or more rational expressions. It is found by taking the product of all distinct prime factors from the denominators, each raised to its highest occurring power.
  • Prime Polynomial Factors: Polynomials that cannot be factored further into non-constant polynomials with integer coefficients (e.g., $x-2$, $x+1$, $x^2+1$).
  • Restrictions: Values of the variable for which a rational expression is undefined, typically because they make the denominator zero. These values must be excluded from the domain.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Adding and Subtracting Rational Expressions

The process of adding and subtracting rational expressions mirrors that of ordinary fractions. The key requirement is to have a common denominator. When dealing with polynomial denominators, this often means finding the Least Common Denominator (LCD). After combining, the result should always be simplified.

Case 1: Rational Expressions with the Same Denominator

If two rational expressions already share the same denominator, the process is straightforward:

  • Property:
    • $\frac{P}{Q} + \frac{R}{Q} = \frac{P + R}{Q}$
    • $\frac{P}{Q} - \frac{R}{Q} = \frac{P - R}{Q}$
    • (where $Q \ne 0$)
  • Procedure:
    1. Combine the numerators over the common denominator.
    2. Simplify the resulting rational expression by factoring the new numerator and denominator and canceling any common factors. Remember to state any restrictions (values that make the denominator zero).

Example (a): Add $\frac{3x}{x-2} + \frac{6}{x-2}$

  • Since denominators are the same, combine numerators: $\frac{3x + 6}{x-2}$
  • Factor the numerator: $\frac{3(x+2)}{x-2}$
  • (Cannot be simplified further by canceling factors.)
  • Restriction: $x \ne 2$

Example (b): Subtract $\frac{x^2}{x+3} - \frac{9}{x+3}$

  • Combine numerators: $\frac{x^2 - 9}{x+3}$
  • Factor the numerator (difference of squares): $\frac{(x-3)(x+3)}{x+3}$
  • Cancel the common factor $(x+3)$: $x-3$
  • Restriction: $x \ne -3$ (even though $(x+3)$ canceled, the original expression had this restriction).

Finding the Least Common Denominator (LCD)

The LCD is crucial when denominators are different.

  • Definition: The LCD of two or more rational expressions is the product of all distinct prime polynomial factors, each raised to the highest power in which it appears in any denominator.

  • Steps to find the LCD:

    1. Factor each denominator completely into its prime polynomial factors.
    2. List all distinct factors that appear in any of the factored denominators.
    3. For each distinct factor, determine the highest power to which it is raised in any of the factorizations.
    4. The LCD is the product of these distinct factors, each raised to its highest power.

Example (a): Find the LCD of $\frac{5}{6x^2}$ and $\frac{7}{4x}$

  • Factor denominators:
    • $6x^2 = 2 \cdot 3 \cdot x^2$
    • $4x = 2^2 \cdot x$
  • Distinct factors: $2, 3, x$
  • Highest powers: $2^2$ (from $4x$), $3^1$ (from $6x^2$), $x^2$ (from $6x^2$)
  • LCD $= 2^2 \cdot 3 \cdot x^2 = 4 \cdot 3 \cdot x^2 = 12x^2$

Example (b): Find the LCD of $\frac{3}{x^2-4}$ and $\frac{1}{x^2-4x+4}$

  • Factor denominators:
    • $x^2-4 = (x-2)(x+2)$
    • $x^2-4x+4 = (x-2)^2$
  • Distinct factors: $(x-2), (x+2)$
  • Highest powers: $(x-2)^2$ (from $x^2-4x+4$), $(x+2)^1$ (from $x^2-4$)
  • LCD $= (x-2)^2(x+2)$

Case 2: Adding and Subtracting Rational Expressions with Different Denominators

This is the most common scenario.

  • Procedure:

    1. Find the LCD of all the denominators using the steps outlined above.
    2. Rewrite each rational expression as an equivalent expression with the LCD as its new denominator. To do this, multiply the numerator AND denominator of each fraction by the factor(s) missing from its original denominator to make it the LCD.
    3. Add or subtract the numerators, placing the result over the common LCD. Be very careful with subtraction; distribute the negative sign to all terms in the subtracted numerator.
    4. Simplify the final result by factoring the new numerator and denominator and canceling any common factors. State restrictions.

  • Note: This procedure is identical to what is done with numeric fractions (e.g., $\frac{1}{4} + \frac{2}{3}$ requires LCD $=12$, giving $\frac{3}{12} + \frac{8}{12} = \frac{11}{12}$).

Example: Add $\frac{3}{x-1} + \frac{2}{x+4}$

  • LCD: Denominators $x-1$ and $x+4$ are prime and distinct, so LCD = $(x-1)(x+4)$.
  • Rewrite:
    • $\frac{3}{x-1} = \frac{3(x+4)}{(x-1)(x+4)}$
    • $\frac{2}{x+4} = \frac{2(x-1)}{(x-1)(x+4)}$
  • Combine numerators:
    • $\frac{3(x+4) + 2(x-1)}{(x-1)(x+4)} = \frac{3x+12 + 2x-2}{(x-1)(x+4)} = \frac{5x+10}{(x-1)(x+4)}$
  • Simplify: $\frac{5(x+2)}{(x-1)(x+4)}$
  • Restrictions: $x \ne 1, x \ne -4$

Example: Subtract $\frac{x}{x^2 - x - 2} - \frac{2}{x^2 - 4}$

  • Factor denominators:
    • $x^2 - x - 2 = (x-2)(x+1)$
    • $x^2 - 4 = (x-2)(x+2)$
  • LCD: $(x-2)(x+1)(x+2)$
  • Rewrite:
    • $\frac{x}{(x-2)(x+1)} = \frac{x(x+2)}{(x-2)(x+1)(x+2)}$
    • $\frac{2}{(x-2)(x+2)} = \frac{2(x+1)}{(x-2)(x+1)(x+2)}$
  • Combine numerators (remember to distribute the negative):
    • $\frac{x(x+2) - 2(x+1)}{(x-2)(x+1)(x+2)} = \frac{x^2+2x - (2x+2)}{(x-2)(x+1)(x+2)} = \frac{x^2+2x - 2x - 2}{(x-2)(x+1)(x+2)} = \frac{x^2 - 2}{(x-2)(x+1)(x+2)}$
  • Simplify: The numerator $x^2-2$ does not factor with rational coefficients to cancel with the denominator factors.
  • Restrictions: $x \ne 2, x \ne -1, x \ne -2$

Example: Simplify $\frac{1}{x} + \frac{2}{x+1} - \frac{3}{x^2+x}$

  • Factor denominators: $x$, $x+1$, $x^2+x = x(x+1)$
  • LCD: $x(x+1)$
  • Rewrite:
    • $\frac{1}{x} = \frac{1(x+1)}{x(x+1)}$
    • $\frac{2}{x+1} = \frac{2x}{x(x+1)}$
    • $\frac{3}{x(x+1)}$ (already has LCD)
  • Combine numerators:
    • $\frac{(x+1) + 2x - 3}{x(x+1)} = \frac{x+1+2x-3}{x(x+1)} = \frac{3x-2}{x(x+1)}$
  • Simplify: The numerator $3x-2$ does not factor to cancel.
  • Restrictions: $x \ne 0, x \ne -1$
