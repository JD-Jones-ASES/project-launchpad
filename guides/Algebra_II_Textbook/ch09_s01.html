Circles: An Introduction to Their Equations

Overview

This section introduces the geometric definition of a circle and derives its algebraic equation in standard form using the distance formula. It then explains how to convert a circle's equation from its general form to standard form by completing the square, enabling identification of the center and radius, and facilitating graphing.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of a Circle: A circle is the collection of all points in a plane that are equidistant (the radius) from a fixed central point.
  • Standard Form of a Circle: The algebraic equation $(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2$ represents a circle with center $(h, k)$ and radius $r$. This form is directly derived from the distance formula.
  • Center and Radius: In the standard form, $(h, k)$ is the coordinates of the center, and $r$ is the length of the radius (note that $r^2$ is on the right side of the equation).
  • Special Cases:
    • Center at the Origin: If the center is $(0, 0)$, the standard form simplifies to $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$.
    • Unit Circle: This is a special circle centered at the origin with a radius of $1$, so its equation is $x^2 + y^2 = 1$.
  • General Form of a Circle: An equation of the form $x^2 + y^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$ is the general form of a circle's equation. To find the center and radius from this form, you must convert it to standard form.
  • Completing the Square: This algebraic technique is used to convert the general form of a circle's equation into its standard form. It involves manipulating quadratic expressions ($x^2 + Bx$ and $y^2 + Cy$) into perfect square trinomials by adding a specific constant, $(B/2)^2$ or $(C/2)^2$, to both sides of the equation.
  • Interpreting $r^2$: After completing the square, the value on the right side of the standard form equation, $r^2$, determines the graph:
    • If $r^2 > 0$, the equation represents a circle.
    • If $r^2 = 0$, the equation represents a single point (the center).
    • If $r^2 < 0$, the equation has no real graph.
  • Leading Coefficients: If the $x^2$ and $y^2$ terms have coefficients other than 1 (e.g., $2x^2 + 2y^2$), you must divide every term in the equation by that coefficient before completing the square.

Important Terms

  • Circle: The set of all points $(x,y)$ in a plane that are a fixed distance (the radius) from a fixed point (the center).
  • Radius ($r$): The fixed distance from the center to any point on the circle. Must be greater than 0 ($r > 0$).
  • Center ($(h,k)$): The fixed point from which all points on the circle are equidistant.
  • Standard Form of a Circle: The equation $(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2$, where $(h,k)$ is the center and $r$ is the radius.
  • Unit Circle: A circle centered at the origin $(0,0)$ with a radius of $1$. Its equation is $x^2 + y^2 = 1$.
  • General Form of a Circle: An equation of a circle expressed as $x^2 + y^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$.
  • Completing the Square: An algebraic method used to rewrite a quadratic expression of the form $x^2 + bx$ as a perfect square trinomial $(x + b/2)^2$ by adding $(b/2)^2$.

Detailed Notes

Definition and Standard Form

A circle is fundamentally a geometric concept: it's the collection of all points in a flat plane that are exactly the same distance away from a central point. This fixed distance is called the radius ($r$), and the fixed point is the center ($(h,k)$).

We can translate this geometric definition into an algebraic equation using the distance formula. If $(x,y)$ is any point on the circle, and $(h,k)$ is the center, then the distance between $(x,y)$ and $(h,k)$ must be $r$. The distance formula is $\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2}$. Applying this to our circle: $r = \sqrt{(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2}$ To eliminate the square root, we square both sides of the equation, which leads directly to the Standard Form of a Circle: $(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2$

  • Here, $(h, k)$ represents the coordinates of the center of the circle.
  • $r$ represents the radius of the circle. Note that $r$ must be a positive value ($r > 0$).
  • If the center of the circle is at the origin $(0,0)$, the equation simplifies to $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$.
  • A special case is the unit circle, which is centered at the origin $(0,0)$ with a radius of $1$. Its equation is $x^2 + y^2 = 1$.

Examples: Writing the Equation of a Circle

Example 1: Write the standard-form equation of the circle with center $(-3, 5)$ and radius $4$. Solution: Substitute $h = -3$, $k = 5$, and $r = 4$ into the standard form $(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2$: $(x - (-3))^2 + (y - 5)^2 = 4^2$ $(x + 3)^2 + (y - 5)^2 = 16$

Examples: Finding Center and Radius

Example 2: Identify the center and radius of $(x - 2)^2 + (y + 7)^2 = 25$. Solution: To find the center and radius, we compare the given equation to the standard form $(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2$. $(x - 2)^2 + (y - (-7))^2 = 5^2$ By direct comparison:

  • $h = 2$
  • $k = -7$ (because $y - k$ becomes $y + 7$, so $k$ must be $-7$)
  • $r^2 = 25$, so $r = \sqrt{25} = 5$ (radius is always positive). Thus, the center is $(2, -7)$ and the radius is $5$.

Completing the Square to Find Standard Form

Sometimes, the equation of a circle is not given in standard form but rather in its general form: $x^2 + y^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$ To find the center and radius from this form, we use a technique called completing the square for both the $x$-terms and the $y$-terms.

Recall: Completing the Square To complete the square for an expression like $x^2 + bx$:

  1. Take half of the coefficient of $x$ (which is $b/2$).
  2. Square that result ($(b/2)^2$).
  3. Add this value to the expression to make it a perfect square trinomial, which can then be factored as $(x + b/2)^2$. Remember to add this same value to the other side of the equation to maintain equality.

Examples: Completing the Square

Example 3: Write $x^2 + y^2 - 6x + 2y - 6 = 0$ in standard form. Find the center and radius. Solution:

  1. Group $x$-terms and $y$-terms, and move the constant to the right side: $(x^2 - 6x) + (y^2 + 2y) = 6$
  2. Complete the square for the $x$-terms:
    • The coefficient of $x$ is $-6$.
    • Half of $-6$ is $-3$.
    • $(-3)^2 = 9$.
    • Add $9$ inside the $x$-group and to the right side of the equation.
  3. Complete the square for the $y$-terms:
    • The coefficient of $y$ is $2$.
    • Half of $2$ is $1$.
    • $(1)^2 = 1$.
    • Add $1$ inside the $y$-group and to the right side of the equation. The equation becomes: $(x^2 - 6x + 9) + (y^2 + 2y + 1) = 6 + 9 + 1$
  4. Factor the perfect square trinomials and simplify the right side: $(x - 3)^2 + (y + 1)^2 = 16$ This is now in standard form.
  • Center: $(3, -1)$
  • Radius: $r^2 = 16$, so $r = \sqrt{16} = 4$.

Example 4: Write $2x^2 + 2y^2 + 8x - 12y + 2 = 0$ in standard form. Find the center and radius. Solution:

  1. Divide by the leading coefficient: Since $x^2$ and $y^2$ have coefficients of $2$, divide every term in the equation by $2$ to make their coefficients $1$. $\frac{2x^2}{2} + \frac{2y^2}{2} + \frac{8x}{2} - \frac{12y}{2} + \frac{2}{2} = \frac{0}{2}$ $x^2 + y^2 + 4x - 6y + 1 = 0$
  2. Group $x$-terms and $y$-terms, and move the constant: $(x^2 + 4x) + (y^2 - 6y) = -1$
  3. Complete the square for $x$-terms:
    • Coefficient of $x$ is $4$. Half is $2$. $2^2 = 4$. Add $4$ to both sides.
  4. Complete the square for $y$-terms:
    • Coefficient of $y$ is $-6$. Half is $-3$. $(-3)^2 = 9$. Add $9$ to both sides. $(x^2 + 4x + 4) + (y^2 - 6y + 9) = -1 + 4 + 9$
  5. Factor and simplify: $(x + 2)^2 + (y - 3)^2 = 12$ This is now in standard form.
  • Center: $(-2, 3)$
  • Radius: $r^2 = 12$, so $r = \sqrt{12} = \sqrt{4 \cdot 3} = 2\sqrt{3}$. (Approximately $3.46$).

Graphing a Circle

Once a circle's equation is in standard form, graphing it is straightforward.

Example 5: Describe the graph of $(x - 1)^2 + (y + 4)^2 = 9$. Solution:

  1. Identify the center and radius:
    • Compare to $(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2$.
    • Center $(h, k)$ is $(1, -4)$.
    • Radius $r = \sqrt{9} = 3$.
  2. Sketch the graph:
    • Plot the center point $(1, -4)$.
    • From the center, move $r$ units (3 units) in four cardinal directions:
      • Right: $(1+3, -4) = (4, -4)$
      • Left: $(1-3, -4) = (-2, -4)$
      • Up: $(1, -4+3) = (1, -1)$
      • Down: $(1, -4-3) = (1, -7)$
    • Draw a smooth circle through these four points. The circle will be symmetric about the horizontal line $y = -4$ and the vertical line $x = 1$.

Important Note: After converting to standard form, the value of $r^2$ is crucial. If $r^2 > 0$, it's a circle. If $r^2 = 0$, the equation represents a single point (the center). If $r^2 < 0$, there is no real graph, as you cannot take the square root of a negative number to get a real radius. Always divide out any common coefficient before completing the square.

Checkpoint Problem

Write $x^2 + y^2 - 4x + 6y - 3 = 0$ in standard form. Find the center and radius. Answer:

  1. Group and move constant: $(x^2 - 4x) + (y^2 + 6y) = 3$
  2. Complete the square for $x$: $x^2 - 4x + 4$. Add $4$ to both sides.
  3. Complete the square for $y$: $y^2 + 6y + 9$. Add $9$ to both sides.
  4. Equation: $(x^2 - 4x + 4) + (y^2 + 6y + 9) = 3 + 4 + 9$
  5. Standard form: $(x - 2)^2 + (y + 3)^2 = 16$
  6. Center: $(2, -3)$, Radius: $r = \sqrt{16} = 4$.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words the relationship between the distance formula and the standard form equation of a circle.
  2. Given a circle with center $(-1, 8)$ and a point on the circle $(3, 5)$, how would you find the equation of the circle? What steps would you take?
  3. Describe the process of converting an equation from the general form $Ax^2 + Ay^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$ to the standard form $(x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 = r^2$. Why is it important that the coefficients of $x^2$ and $y^2$ are the same and non-zero?
  4. Consider the equation $(x + 5)^2 + (y - 1)^2 = -9$. What does this equation represent graphically, and why?
  5. If you are given the standard form equation of a circle, what key pieces of information does it immediately provide, and how do you use those to sketch its graph?
