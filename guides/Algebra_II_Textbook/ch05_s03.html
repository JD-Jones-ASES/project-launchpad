Factoring I: Foundations of Polynomial Factoring

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concept of factoring polynomials, which is the reverse process of multiplication. Mastering factoring is crucial for solving equations, simplifying rational expressions, and analyzing polynomial graphs. We explore key strategies including factoring out the greatest common factor (GCF), recognizing and factoring sums and differences of cubes, handling polynomials in quadratic form, and understanding the multiplicity of polynomial zeros, along with techniques for factoring trinomials.

Key Concepts

  • Factoring as Reverse Multiplication: The process of rewriting a polynomial as a product of simpler polynomials, much like decomposing a number into its prime factors.
  • Greatest Common Factor (GCF): The largest monomial that divides every term in a polynomial evenly. Factoring out the GCF is always the first step in any factoring problem.
  • Sum and Difference of Cubes: Special factoring patterns for binomials of the form $A^3 + B^3$ and $A^3 - B^3$, which expand into a binomial and a trinomial factor.
  • Polynomials in Quadratic Form: Polynomials that can be expressed as $au^2 + bu + c$ by making a suitable substitution for a variable expression, $u$.
  • Multiplicity of a Zero: If $(x-r)^k$ is the highest power of a factor that divides a polynomial $p(x)$, then $r$ is a zero of $p$ with multiplicity $k$. This indicates how many times a factor appears.
  • Factoring Monic Trinomials ($x^2 + bx + c$): To factor, find two numbers whose product is $c$ and whose sum is $b$.
  • Factoring Non-Monic Trinomials ($ax^2 + bx + c$ with $a \neq 1$): The AC Method involves finding two numbers whose product is $ac$ and whose sum is $b$, then rewriting the middle term and factoring by grouping.
  • General Factoring Strategy: Always start with the GCF, then consider the number of terms to apply specific methods (e.g., difference of squares, sum/difference of cubes for two terms; quadratic form, trinomial methods for three terms), and finally, verify by multiplying back out.

Important Terms

  • Factoring: The process of expressing a polynomial as a product of two or more polynomials.
  • Greatest Common Factor (GCF): The largest monomial (including constants and variables with their lowest powers) that divides each term of a polynomial.
  • Quadratic Form: A polynomial that can be rewritten as $au^2 + bu + c$, where $u$ is some expression involving a variable.
  • Zero of a Polynomial: A value $r$ such that when $x=r$ is substituted into the polynomial $p(x)$, the result is $p(r)=0$. Also known as a root of the polynomial.
  • Multiplicity of a Zero: The number of times a particular zero appears as a root of a polynomial, determined by the exponent of its corresponding factor in the polynomial's factored form.
  • Monic Trinomial: A trinomial where the leading coefficient (the coefficient of the term with the highest degree) is 1. For example, $x^2 + 5x + 6$.
  • AC Method: A technique used to factor trinomials of the form $ax^2 + bx + c$ (where $a \neq 1$) by splitting the middle term.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Factoring

Factoring is a fundamental algebraic skill, essentially the inverse operation of multiplication. When we factor a polynomial, we break it down into a product of simpler polynomials. This skill is critical for solving polynomial equations, simplifying complex rational expressions, and understanding the behavior of polynomial graphs. This section lays the groundwork by covering four essential factoring techniques.

Greatest Common Factor (GCF)

The Greatest Common Factor (GCF) is the largest monomial that divides every term in a polynomial evenly. It's crucial to always factor out the GCF as the very first step in any factoring problem.

Method to Factor out the GCF:

  1. Find the GCF of all terms:
    • Determine the highest common numerical factor (GCF of the coefficients).
    • For each variable common to all terms, take the lowest power of that variable present in any term.
  2. Rewrite each term: Express each term in the polynomial as a product of the GCF and the remaining factor.
  3. Apply the distributive property in reverse: Factor out the GCF from all terms.

Examples:

  • Factor $12x^3 - 8x^2 + 4x$:
    • GCF of coefficients $(12, 8, 4)$ is $4$.
    • Lowest power of $x$ (common to all terms) is $x^1$.
    • So, GCF $= 4x$.
    • Rewrite: $4x(3x^2) - 4x(2x) + 4x(1)$
    • Factor: $4x(3x^2 - 2x + 1)$.
  • Factor $6a^2b^3 - 9ab^2 + 3ab$:
    • GCF of coefficients $(6, 9, 3)$ is $3$.
    • Lowest power of $a$ is $a^1$.
    • Lowest power of $b$ is $b^1$.
    • So, GCF $= 3ab$.
    • Rewrite: $3ab(2ab^2) - 3ab(3b) + 3ab(1)$
    • Factor: $3ab(2ab^2 - 3b + 1)$.

Verification: Always multiply your factored expression back out to ensure it matches the original polynomial.

Sum and Difference of Cubes

These are two special factoring patterns used for binomials where both terms are perfect cubes.

Formulas for Sum and Difference of Cubes:

  • Sum of Cubes: $A^3 + B^3 = (A+B)(A^2 - AB + B^2)$
  • Difference of Cubes: $A^3 - B^3 = (A-B)(A^2 + AB + B^2)$

Important Note: The trinomial factor ($A^2 \pm AB + B^2$) in both formulas does not factor further over the real numbers (its discriminant is negative).

Memory Aid (SOAP): To remember the signs in the trinomial factor:

  • Same sign as the original binomial (e.g., $A^3 + B^3$ has + in $A+B$)
  • Opposite sign in the middle term of the trinomial (e.g., $A^3 + B^3$ has - in $A^2 - AB + B^2$)
  • Always Positive for the last term of the trinomial.

Examples:

  • Factor $8x^3 + 27$:
    • Recognize $8x^3 = (2x)^3$ and $27 = 3^3$. So, $A = 2x$ and $B = 3$.
    • Apply Sum of Cubes formula: $(2x+3)((2x)^2 - (2x)(3) + 3^2) = (2x+3)(4x^2 - 6x + 9)$.
  • Factor $x^3 - 64$:
    • Recognize $x^3 = x^3$ and $64 = 4^3$. So, $A = x$ and $B = 4$.
    • Apply Difference of Cubes formula: $(x-4)(x^2 + 4x + 16)$.

Polynomials in Quadratic Form

A polynomial is in quadratic form if it can be written as $au^2 + bu + c$, where $u$ represents some expression involving a variable. This allows us to use standard trinomial factoring techniques after a substitution.

Strategy:

  1. Identify a suitable expression to substitute for $u$ (e.g., if you have $x^4$ and $x^2$, let $u = x^2$).
  2. Factor the resulting quadratic expression in terms of $u$.
  3. Substitute the original expression back in for $u$.
  4. Check if any resulting factors can be factored further.

Examples:

  • Factor $x^4 - 5x^2 + 6$:
    • Let $u = x^2$. Then the expression becomes $u^2 - 5u + 6$.
    • Factor the quadratic in $u$: $(u-2)(u-3)$.
    • Substitute $u=x^2$ back in: $(x^2-2)(x^2-3)$.
    • These factors can be further factored over the reals using the difference of squares concept: $(x-\sqrt{2})(x+\sqrt{2})(x-\sqrt{3})(x+\sqrt{3})$.
  • Factor $x^6 - 7x^3 - 8$:
    • Let $u = x^3$. Then the expression becomes $u^2 - 7u - 8$.
    • Factor the quadratic in $u$: $(u-8)(u+1)$.
    • Substitute $u=x^3$ back in: $(x^3-8)(x^3+1)$.
    • Apply the Sum and Difference of Cubes formulas: $(x-2)(x^2+2x+4)(x+1)(x^2-x+1)$.

Multiplicity of Zeros

When a factor $(x-r)$ appears more than once in the complete factorization of a polynomial, its corresponding zero $r$ is said to have a multiplicity greater than 1.

Definition: If $(x-r)^k$ is the highest power of $(x-r)$ that divides the polynomial $p(x)$, then $r$ is a zero of $p$ with multiplicity $k$.

Example:

  • Find the zeros and their multiplicities for $p(x) = x^2(x-3)^3(x+1)$:
    • From the factor $x^2 = (x-0)^2$, we have $x=0$ as a zero with multiplicity 2.
    • From the factor $(x-3)^3$, we have $x=3$ as a zero with multiplicity 3.
    • From the factor $(x+1) = (x-(-1))^1$, we have $x=-1$ as a zero with multiplicity 1.

Factoring Trinomials: $x^2 + bx + c$ (Monic)

A monic trinomial has a leading coefficient of 1. To factor it, we look for two specific numbers.

Method to Factor a Monic Trinomial: To factor $x^2 + bx + c$, find two integers $p$ and $q$ such that:

  • Their product $p \cdot q = c$ (the constant term)
  • Their sum $p + q = b$ (the coefficient of the middle term) Then, the factored form is $(x+p)(x+q)$.

Examples:

  • Factor $x^2 + 7x + 12$:
    • Need $pq=12$ and $p+q=7$.
    • Pairs of factors for 12: $(1,12), (2,6), (3,4)$.
    • The pair $(3,4)$ sums to $7$.
    • Therefore, $x^2 + 7x + 12 = (x+3)(x+4)$.
  • Factor $x^2 - 5x - 14$:
    • Need $pq=-14$ and $p+q=-5$.
    • Pairs of factors for -14: $(1,-14), (-1,14), (2,-7), (-2,7)$.
    • The pair $(2,-7)$ sums to $-5$.
    • Therefore, $x^2 - 5x - 14 = (x+2)(x-7)$.

Factoring Trinomials: $ax^2 + bx + c$ with $a \neq 1$ (The AC Method)

When the leading coefficient $a$ is not 1, we use the AC Method (also known as factoring by grouping).

The AC Method Steps:

  1. Compute the product $ac$.
  2. Find two integers $m$ and $n$ such that their product $mn = ac$ and their sum $m+n = b$.
  3. Rewrite the middle term $bx$ as $mx + nx$. The polynomial becomes $ax^2 + mx + nx + c$.
  4. Factor by grouping: Group the first two terms and the last two terms, then factor out the GCF from each pair. If successful, you should have a common binomial factor.

Example:

  • Factor $6x^2 + 11x - 10$:
    • Here $a=6$, $b=11$, $c=-10$.
    • $ac = (6)(-10) = -60$.
    • Find $m, n$ such that $mn = -60$ and $m+n = 11$. The pair is $m=15, n=-4$.
    • Rewrite: $6x^2 + 15x - 4x - 10$.
    • Factor by grouping:
      • $3x(2x+5) - 2(2x+5)$
      • $(2x+5)(3x-2)$.
    • Check: $(2x+5)(3x-2) = 6x^2 - 4x + 15x - 10 = 6x^2 + 11x - 10$.

Summary: Strategy for Factoring Any Polynomial

Always follow these steps when attempting to factor a polynomial:

  1. GCF First: Always look for and factor out the Greatest Common Factor from all terms.
  2. Count the Terms:
    • Three terms (Trinomials):
      • Check for quadratic form ($au^2+bu+c$) and use substitution if applicable.
      • If leading coefficient is 1 ($x^2+bx+c$), find two numbers that multiply to $c$ and add to $b$.
      • If leading coefficient is not 1 ($ax^2+bx+c$), use the AC Method (factor by grouping).
    • Two terms (Binomials):
      • Look for Difference of Squares ($A^2 - B^2 = (A-B)(A+B)$).
      • Look for Sum or Difference of Cubes ($A^3 \pm B^3$ formulas).
  3. Check Each Factor: After factoring, examine each resulting factor to ensure that no factor can be factored further. Factoring completely means breaking it down into irreducible factors over the real numbers.
  4. Verify: Multiply your factored expression back out to confirm it equals the original polynomial.

Review Questions

  1. Explain why factoring out the Greatest Common Factor (GCF) should always be the first step when factoring any polynomial. Provide an example.
  2. Describe the key differences between factoring a sum of squares, a difference of squares, and a sum/difference of cubes. Give an example for each that is factorable over the real numbers.
  3. What does it mean for a polynomial to be in "quadratic form"? Demonstrate how to factor $x^4 - 10x^2 + 9$ using a substitution.
  4. Define the term "multiplicity of a zero" and explain its significance. For the polynomial $p(x) = (x-1)^2(x+2)(x-5)^4$, identify all zeros and their corresponding multiplicities.
