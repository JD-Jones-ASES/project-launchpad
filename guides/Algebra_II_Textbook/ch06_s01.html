Study Guide: Simplifying Rational Expressions

Overview

This section introduces rational expressions as fractions involving polynomials and details the crucial steps for simplifying them. It emphasizes the importance of identifying domain restrictions to prevent division by zero, which results in values excluded from the expression's domain. The core process involves factoring both the numerator and denominator to cancel common polynomial factors, while carefully tracking all initial domain restrictions.

Key Concepts

  • Rational Expression: An algebraic fraction where both the numerator and denominator are polynomials. It's the polynomial equivalent of an ordinary fraction.
  • Domain Restriction (Excluded Value): Any value of the variable that makes the denominator of a rational expression equal to zero, as division by zero is undefined. These values must be excluded from the domain.
  • Finding Domain Restrictions: To identify excluded values, factor the denominator completely, set each factor to zero, and solve for the variable.
  • Fundamental Property of Rational Expressions: Common factors (not terms) can be canceled from the numerator and denominator to simplify an expression. If $P, Q, R$ are polynomials and $Q \neq 0, R \neq 0$, then $PR/QR = P/Q$.
  • Simplifying Process: Involves factoring both the numerator and denominator completely, then canceling any identical factors.
  • Retaining Domain Restrictions: It is critical to state the domain restrictions from the original expression, even after factors have been canceled during simplification. These restrictions signify "holes" in the graph of the simplified function.
  • Opposite Factors: When a factor in the numerator is the opposite (negative) of a factor in the denominator (e.g., $3-x$ and $x-3$), factor out $-1$ from one of them to reveal a common factor that can be canceled.

Important Terms

  • Rational Expression: An expression of the form $\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$, where $P(x)$ and $Q(x)$ are polynomials and $Q(x) \neq 0$. Its domain is the set of all real numbers for which $Q(x) \neq 0$.
  • Domain Restriction (Excluded Value): Any value of the variable that causes the denominator of a rational expression to be zero. These values are not part of the expression's domain.

Detailed Notes

What is a Rational Expression?

A rational expression is essentially a fraction where the numerator and denominator are polynomials. For example, $\frac{x^2-4}{x-2}$ is a rational expression. Just like numerical fractions (e.g., $\frac{6}{9}$ simplifies to $\frac{2}{3}$), rational expressions can be simplified by canceling common factors.

Domain Restrictions

The most critical aspect of working with rational expressions is understanding domain restrictions. Division by zero is undefined. Therefore, any value of the variable that makes the denominator of a rational expression zero must be excluded from its domain. These are called domain restrictions or excluded values.

Steps to Find Domain Restrictions:

  1. Factor the denominator completely. This helps identify all potential factors that could become zero.
  2. Set each unique factor of the denominator equal to zero and solve. Each solution is an excluded value.
  3. State all values found as excluded from the domain. The domain includes all real numbers except these excluded values.

Examples:

  • For $\frac{3x}{x-5}$:
    • Denominator is $x-5$.
    • Set $x-5 = 0 \Rightarrow x=5$.
    • Domain: All real numbers except $x=5$, or $(-\infty, 5) \cup (5, \infty)$.
  • For $\frac{x+2}{x^2-x-6}$:
    • Factor the denominator: $x^2-x-6 = (x-3)(x+2)$.
    • Set factors to zero: $x-3=0 \Rightarrow x=3$; $x+2=0 \Rightarrow x=-2$.
    • Domain: All real numbers except $x=3$ and $x=-2$.
  • For $\frac{5}{x^2+4}$:
    • The denominator $x^2+4$ is always positive for any real $x$ (since $x^2 \ge 0$, then $x^2+4 \ge 4$).
    • It never equals zero.
    • Domain: All real numbers, or $(-\infty, \infty)$.

Simplifying by Canceling Common Factors

The basis for simplifying rational expressions is the Fundamental Property of Rational Expressions: If $P$, $Q$, and $R$ are polynomials with $Q \neq 0$ and $R \neq 0$, then $\frac{P \cdot R}{Q \cdot R} = \frac{P}{Q}$. This means you can cancel a common factor from both the numerator and the denominator.

Important Caution: You can only cancel factors, never terms. For example, $\frac{x+3}{x+7}$ cannot be simplified to $\frac{3}{7}$ because $x$ is a term, not a factor.

Steps for Simplifying a Rational Expression:

  1. Factor the numerator and denominator completely. This is often the most crucial step.
  2. Identify and cancel every common factor. These are identical polynomial factors found in both the numerator and denominator.
  3. State the domain restrictions that were present in the original expression. Even if a factor cancels out, the value it represents as zero is still excluded from the domain of the original expression.

Examples of Simplification:

  • Simplify $\frac{6x^3}{9x}$:

    • Factor: $\frac{3 \cdot 2 \cdot x \cdot x^2}{3 \cdot 3 \cdot x}$
    • Cancel common factors $(3x)$: $\frac{2x^2}{3}$
    • Restrictions (from original $9x \ne 0$): $x \ne 0$.
    • Result: $\frac{2x^2}{3}, x \ne 0$.

  • Simplify $\frac{x^2 - 9}{x^2 - x - 6}$:

    • Factor numerator: $(x-3)(x+3)$
    • Factor denominator: $(x-3)(x+2)$
    • Expression: $\frac{(x-3)(x+3)}{(x-3)(x+2)}$
    • Cancel common factor $(x-3)$: $\frac{x+3}{x+2}$
    • Restrictions (from original denominator factors $x-3$ and $x+2$): $x \ne 3, x \ne -2$.
    • Result: $\frac{x+3}{x+2}, x \ne 3, x \ne -2$.

  • Simplify $\frac{2x^2 + 5x - 3}{4x^2 - 1}$:

    • Factor numerator: $(2x-1)(x+3)$
    • Factor denominator: $(2x-1)(2x+1)$
    • Expression: $\frac{(2x-1)(x+3)}{(2x-1)(2x+1)}$
    • Cancel common factor $(2x-1)$: $\frac{x+3}{2x+1}$
    • Restrictions (from original denominator factors $2x-1$ and $2x+1$): $2x-1 \ne 0 \Rightarrow x \ne \frac{1}{2}$; $2x+1 \ne 0 \Rightarrow x \ne -\frac{1}{2}$.
    • Result: $\frac{x+3}{2x+1}, x \ne \frac{1}{2}, x \ne -\frac{1}{2}$.

Simplifying with Opposite Factors

Sometimes, a factor in the numerator is the opposite of a factor in the denominator (e.g., $3-x$ and $x-3$). Remember that $3-x = -(x-3)$. By factoring out $-1$ from one of these, you can reveal a common factor to cancel.

Example: Simplify $\frac{3-x}{x^2-9}$:

  • Factor denominator: $(x-3)(x+3)$
  • Rewrite numerator: $3-x = -(x-3)$
  • Expression: $\frac{-(x-3)}{(x-3)(x+3)}$
  • Cancel common factor $(x-3)$: $\frac{-1}{x+3}$
  • Restrictions (from original denominator factors $x-3$ and $x+3$): $x \ne 3, x \ne -3$.
  • Result: $\frac{-1}{x+3}, x \ne 3, x \ne -3$.

Simplifying Rational Functions and Graphical Implications

When simplifying a rational function, like $f(x) = \frac{x^3 - 4x}{x^2 + 2x}$, the process is the same. The simplified form of the function will generally look like a simpler polynomial or rational expression. However, it's crucial to remember that the original domain restrictions still apply.

Example: Simplify $f(x) = \frac{x^3 - 4x}{x^2 + 2x}$ and state its domain.

  • Factor numerator: $x(x^2 - 4) = x(x-2)(x+2)$
  • Factor denominator: $x(x+2)$
  • Original expression: $f(x) = \frac{x(x-2)(x+2)}{x(x+2)}$
  • Identify original domain restrictions: $x \ne 0$ and $x+2 \ne 0 \Rightarrow x \ne -2$.
  • Cancel common factors $x$ and $(x+2)$: $f(x) = x-2$
  • Result: $f(x) = x-2$, with domain restrictions $x \ne 0, x \ne -2$.

Graphically, the simplified expression $y = x-2$ is a straight line. However, because $x=0$ and $x=-2$ were excluded from the domain of the original function, the graph of $f(x)$ will have "holes" (single missing points) at $x=0$ and $x=-2$ on that line. These holes represent the values where the original expression was undefined.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words why it is crucial to identify domain restrictions before simplifying a rational expression. What happens if you forget to do this?
  2. Differentiate between canceling "terms" and canceling "factors" in a rational expression. Provide an example where canceling terms leads to an incorrect simplification.
  3. Describe the steps you would take to simplify a rational expression like $\frac{ax^2+bx+c}{dx^2+ex+f}$. What tools or techniques are most important for these steps?
  4. Consider the expression $\frac{x-y}{y-x}$. How would you simplify this, and what is the general rule for handling factors that are opposites of each other?
  5. If a rational function simplifies to a linear function, e.g., $f(x) = x+3$, but the original function had domain restrictions at $x=1$ and $x=-2$, how does this affect the graph of the function $f(x)$ compared to the simple line $y=x+3$?
