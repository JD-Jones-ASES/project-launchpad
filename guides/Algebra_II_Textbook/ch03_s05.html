Chapter 3, Section 5: Completing the Square

Overview

This section introduces the powerful algebraic technique of completing the square, which transforms any quadratic expression into a perfect square trinomial plus a constant. It serves as a method for solving quadratic equations and, crucially, as the derivation for the universal Quadratic Formula. The section also covers the discriminant, a key component of the Quadratic Formula that predicts the nature of a quadratic equation's solutions.

Key Concepts

  • Purpose of Completing the Square: This technique allows us to transform a quadratic expression x^2 + bx into a perfect square trinomial, (x + b/2)^2, by adding (b/2)^2. This makes quadratic equations solvable using the simpler square-root property.
  • Derivation of the Quadratic Formula: Completing the square is not just a solving method; it is the fundamental process used to derive the Quadratic Formula, which provides a universal solution for any quadratic equation in the form ax^2 + bx + c = 0.
  • Perfect Square Trinomial Pattern: A quadratic expression like x^2 + 2kx + k^2 is a perfect square trinomial, factoring as (x+k)^2. The constant term k^2 is always the square of half the linear coefficient (2k/2)^2.
  • Steps for Solving Equations: When solving x^2 + bx + c = 0 by completing the square, the process involves isolating the x^2 + bx terms, adding (b/2)^2 to both sides of the equation, factoring the left side into a perfect square, and then applying the square-root property.
  • Handling Non-Monic Quadratics: If the leading coefficient a in ax^2 + bx + c = 0 is not 1, the first step is to divide every term in the equation by a to make the x^2 term monic (coefficient of 1) before proceeding with completing the square.
  • The Discriminant (( \Delta )): Defined as b^2 - 4ac, the discriminant is the part of the Quadratic Formula under the radical. Its value indicates the nature of the quadratic equation's solutions:
    • Δ > 0: Two distinct real solutions.
    • Δ = 0: Exactly one real solution (a repeated root).
    • Δ < 0: Two complex (non-real) conjugate solutions.

Important Terms

  • Completing the Square: An algebraic technique used to rewrite a quadratic expression x^2 + bx as a perfect square trinomial (x + b/2)^2 by adding the term (b/2)^2.
  • Perfect Square Trinomial: A trinomial (polynomial with three terms) that is the square of a binomial, such as x^2 + 2kx + k^2 = (x+k)^2.
  • Square-Root Property: A method for solving equations of the form u^2 = d, which states that u = ±√d.
  • Quadratic Formula: A universal formula x = (-b ± √(b^2 - 4ac)) / (2a) that provides the solutions for any quadratic equation in the standard form ax^2 + bx + c = 0.
  • Discriminant (( \Delta )): The expression b^2 - 4ac within the Quadratic Formula, used to determine the number and type of solutions to a quadratic equation without fully solving it.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Completing the Square

While the quadratic formula is a universal solver, completing the square is a fundamental technique that not only solves quadratic equations but also forms the basis for deriving the Quadratic Formula itself. It transforms a quadratic expression into a perfect square trinomial, making it amenable to the simpler square-root method.

Completing the Square for Expressions

A perfect square trinomial follows a specific pattern: (x+k)^2 = x^2+2kx+k^2. Notice that the constant term k^2 is the square of half the linear coefficient (2k)/2 = k. We use this observation to complete the square for expressions of the form x^2 + bx.

Property: Completing the Square (Monic) To make x^2+bx a perfect square trinomial, you must add (b/2)^2. The result is x^2+bx + (b/2)^2 = (x + b/2)^2.

Example: What value must be added to x^2-6x to make it a perfect square trinomial? Here, b = -6. Half of b is b/2 = -6/2 = -3. The square of b/2 is (-3)^2 = 9. So, x^2-6x + 9 = (x-3)^2. The required value is 9.

Solving Equations by Completing the Square

To solve a quadratic equation ax^2+bx+c=0 using this method:

  1. If the leading coefficient a is not 1, divide all terms by a. This makes the x^2 term monic.
  2. Move the constant term c to the right side of the equation: x^2+bx = -c.
  3. Calculate (b/2)^2: Determine the value needed to complete the square on the left side.
  4. Add (b/2)^2 to both sides of the equation: This maintains the equality.
    • Caution: Always add (b/2)^2 to both sides. Adding it only to one side changes the original equation and leads to incorrect solutions.
  5. Write the left side as a perfect square: Factor x^2+bx+(b/2)^2 into (x + b/2)^2.
  6. Apply the square-root property: Take the square root of both sides, remembering to include ± on the right side: x + b/2 = ±√(right side).
  7. Solve for x: Isolate x to find the two solutions.

Example: Solving x^2+8x-3=0 by completing the square (Monic)

  1. Move the constant: x^2+8x = 3
  2. Calculate (b/2)^2: b=8, so (8/2)^2 = 4^2 = 16.
  3. Add 16 to both sides: x^2+8x+16 = 3+16
  4. Factor the left side: (x+4)^2 = 19
  5. Apply square-root property: x+4 = ±√19
  6. Solve for x: x = -4 ±√19
    • Solution set: {-4 + √19, -4 - √19}

Example: Solving 2x^2-5x+1=0 by completing the square (General)

  1. Divide by a (which is 2): x^2 - (5/2)x + 1/2 = 0
  2. Move the constant: x^2 - (5/2)x = -1/2
  3. Calculate (b/2)^2: b = -5/2, so b/2 = -5/4. (-5/4)^2 = 25/16.
  4. Add 25/16 to both sides: x^2 - (5/2)x + 25/16 = -1/2 + 25/16 x^2 - (5/2)x + 25/16 = -8/16 + 25/16 x^2 - (5/2)x + 25/16 = 17/16
  5. Factor the left side: (x - 5/4)^2 = 17/16
  6. Apply square-root property: x - 5/4 = ±√(17/16) which simplifies to x - 5/4 = ±√17 / 4
  7. Solve for x: x = 5/4 ±√17 / 4
    • Solution set: {(5 + √17)/4, (5 - √17)/4}

Deriving the Quadratic Formula

The power of completing the square is evident in its ability to derive the Quadratic Formula. By applying the steps to the general quadratic equation ax^2+bx+c=0 (where a ≠ 0), we arrive at the universal solution:

Derivation Steps:

  1. Divide by a: x^2 + (b/a)x + (c/a) = 0
  2. Move the constant: x^2 + (b/a)x = -c/a
  3. Add (b/(2a))^2 = b^2/(4a^2) to both sides: x^2 + (b/a)x + b^2/(4a^2) = -c/a + b^2/(4a^2) x^2 + (b/a)x + b^2/(4a^2) = b^2/(4a^2) - (4ac)/(4a^2) x^2 + (b/a)x + b^2/(4a^2) = (b^2 - 4ac)/(4a^2)
  4. Factor the left side: (x + b/(2a))^2 = (b^2 - 4ac)/(4a^2)
  5. Apply the square-root property: x + b/(2a) = ±√(b^2 - 4ac) / √(4a^2) x + b/(2a) = ±√(b^2 - 4ac) / (2a)
  6. Solve for x: x = -b/(2a) ±√(b^2 - 4ac) / (2a) x = (-b ± √(b^2 - 4ac)) / (2a)

Quadratic Formula: If ax^2+bx+c=0 with a ≠ 0, then x = (-b ± √(b^2 - 4ac)) / (2a).

The Discriminant (( \Delta ))

The expression b^2-4ac inside the radical of the Quadratic Formula is called the discriminant, often denoted by Δ. It determines the nature of the solutions without needing to complete the full calculation.

  • Δ > 0: The equation has two distinct real solutions. This means the parabola y=ax^2+bx+c intersects the x-axis at two different points.
  • Δ = 0: The equation has exactly one real solution (a repeated root). The parabola just "kisses" the x-axis at its vertex.
  • Δ < 0: The equation has two complex (non-real) conjugate solutions. The parabola does not intersect the x-axis at all; it either floats entirely above or entirely below it.

Note: When using the Quadratic Formula, it's a good practice to evaluate the discriminant first. If Δ < 0, remember to introduce the imaginary unit i (where √-1 = i) and write √Δ = i√|Δ|.

Example: Using the Discriminant Without solving, classify the roots of 3x^2-x+2=0. Here, a=3, b=-1, c=2. Δ = b^2 - 4ac = (-1)^2 - 4(3)(2) = 1 - 24 = -23. Since Δ = -23 < 0, the equation has two complex (non-real) conjugate solutions.

Example: Repeated Root Solve 4x^2+4x+1=0. Recognize that this is a perfect square trinomial: (2x+1)^2=0. So, 2x+1=0, which gives x=-1/2. This is one repeated real root. Alternatively, using the discriminant: Δ = b^2 - 4ac = (4)^2 - 4(4)(1) = 16 - 16 = 0. Since Δ = 0, it confirms there is one repeated real root.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental observation about perfect square trinomials that allows us to "complete the square." How is this property applied to an expression like x^2 + 12x?
  2. Outline the step-by-step process for solving a quadratic equation ax^2 + bx + c = 0 by completing the square, especially detailing how to handle cases where the leading coefficient a is not 1.
  3. Why is adding (b/2)^2 to both sides of an equation crucial when completing the square, rather than just one side? What would be the consequence of adding it to only one side?
  4. Describe the significance of the discriminant (Δ = b^2 - 4ac). How does its value (>0, =0, <0) inform us about the nature of the solutions to a quadratic equation, and what does this mean graphically?
  5. Discuss the relationship between completing the square and the Quadratic Formula. In what sense is completing the square more "fundamental" than the Quadratic Formula?
