Slopes of Lines Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the concept of slope, a fundamental measure that quantifies a line's steepness and direction. Understanding slope is crucial for writing linear equations, analyzing rates of change, and determining the relationship between parallel and perpendicular lines. It provides a numerical way to interpret how a line moves across a coordinate plane.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of Slope: The ratio of the vertical change (rise, $\Delta y$) to the horizontal change (run, $\Delta x$) between any two distinct points on a non-vertical line. It is denoted by $m$.
  • Slope Formula: For two points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$, the slope is calculated as $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$, provided $x_1 \neq x_2$. The order of subtraction for $x$ and $y$ must be consistent.
  • Interpreting Slope:
    • Positive Slope ($m > 0$): The line rises from left to right.
    • Negative Slope ($m < 0$): The line falls from left to right.
    • Zero Slope ($m = 0$): The line is horizontal.
    • Undefined Slope: The line is vertical (due to division by zero in the formula).
  • Collinear Points: Three or more points are collinear if they lie on the same straight line. This can be tested by verifying that the slope between any two pairs of points is identical.
  • Parallel Lines: Two distinct non-vertical lines are parallel if and only if they have equal slopes ($m_1 = m_2$). Vertical lines are also parallel to each other.
  • Perpendicular Lines: Two non-vertical lines are perpendicular if and only if their slopes are negative reciprocals of each other ($m_1 m_2 = -1$, or $m_2 = -\frac{1}{m_1}$). A vertical line and a horizontal line are also perpendicular.

Important Terms

  • Slope ($m$): A number that describes the steepness and direction of a line.
  • $\Delta y$ (Delta y): Represents the change in the $y$-coordinate, also known as the "rise."
  • $\Delta x$ (Delta x): Represents the change in the $x$-coordinate, also known as the "run."
  • Rise: The vertical change between two points on a line.
  • Run: The horizontal change between two points on a line.
  • Collinear Points: Points that lie on the same straight line.
  • Parallel Lines: Lines that lie in the same plane and never intersect; they have the same slope (or both are vertical).
  • Perpendicular Lines: Lines that intersect to form a right (90-degree) angle; their slopes are negative reciprocals of each other (or one is horizontal and the other is vertical).
  • Negative Reciprocal: For a slope $m$, its negative reciprocal is $-\frac{1}{m}$. The product of a number and its negative reciprocal is -1.

Detailed Notes

The Definition and Formula of Slope

The slope of a non-vertical line is a single number that quantifies its steepness and direction. It is defined as the ratio of the vertical change ($\Delta y$, or "rise") to the horizontal change ($\Delta x$, or "run") between any two distinct points on the line.

The formula for the slope $m$ between two points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ is: $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}}$ This formula requires $x_1 \neq x_2$ to avoid division by zero. It is crucial to maintain consistent order when subtracting coordinates: if you subtract $y_1$ from $y_2$, you must also subtract $x_1$ from $x_2$. You can choose either point as $(x_1, y_1)$, as swapping the labels will change the signs of both the numerator and denominator, leaving the ratio (slope) unchanged.

Example: Finding the Slope of a Line To find the slope of the line passing through $(-3, 4)$ and $(5, -2)$: Let $(x_1, y_1) = (-3, 4)$ and $(x_2, y_2) = (5, -2)$. $m = \frac{-2 - 4}{5 - (-3)} = \frac{-6}{5 + 3} = \frac{-6}{8} = -\frac{3}{4}$ A negative slope indicates the line falls from left to right.

Interpreting Slope

The sign and value of the slope provide important information about the line's orientation:

  • Positive slope ($m > 0$): The line rises as you move from left to right. Think of walking uphill.
  • Negative slope ($m < 0$): The line falls as you move from left to right. Think of walking downhill.
  • Zero slope ($m = 0$): The line is horizontal. This occurs when $\Delta y = 0$, meaning all $y$-coordinates are the same. The equation of such a line is $y = c$ (a constant).
    • Example: For the horizontal line $y = -3$, using points $(-2, -3)$ and $(4, -3)$: $m = \frac{-3 - (-3)}{4 - (-2)} = \frac{0}{6} = 0$.
  • Undefined slope: The line is vertical. This occurs when $\Delta x = 0$, meaning all $x$-coordinates are the same, leading to division by zero in the slope formula. The equation of such a line is $x = c$ (a constant).
    • Example: For the vertical line $x = 2$, using points $(2, 1)$ and $(2, 5)$: $m = \frac{5 - 1}{2 - 2} = \frac{4}{0}$, which is undefined.

Collinear Points

Three or more points are considered collinear if they all lie on the same straight line. To test for collinearity, calculate the slope between the first two points and then between the second and third points (or any two distinct pairs). If these slopes are identical, the points are collinear.

Example: Testing for Collinearity Determine whether $(1, 2)$, $(3, 5)$, and $(7, 11)$ are collinear.

  1. Slope between $(1, 2)$ and $(3, 5)$: $m_1 = \frac{5 - 2}{3 - 1} = \frac{3}{2}$
  2. Slope between $(3, 5)$ and $(7, 11)$: $m_2 = \frac{11 - 5}{7 - 3} = \frac{6}{4} = \frac{3}{2}$ Since $m_1 = m_2 = \frac{3}{2}$, the points are collinear.

Example: Non-Collinear Points Determine whether $(-2, 1)$, $(1, 4)$, and $(4, 8)$ are collinear.

  1. Slope from $(-2, 1)$ to $(1, 4)$: $m_1 = \frac{4 - 1}{1 - (-2)} = \frac{3}{3} = 1$
  2. Slope from $(1, 4)$ to $(4, 8)$: $m_2 = \frac{8 - 4}{4 - 1} = \frac{4}{3}$ Since $m_1 \neq m_2$, the three points are not collinear.

Parallel and Perpendicular Lines

Parallel Lines Two distinct non-vertical lines are parallel if and only if they have the exact same slope ($m_1 = m_2$). This means they have the same steepness and direction and will never intersect. Two vertical lines, both having an undefined slope, are also parallel.

Example: Slope of a Parallel Line If a line has a slope $m = \frac{3}{4}$, then any line parallel to it will also have a slope of $m_P = \frac{3}{4}$.

Perpendicular Lines Two non-vertical lines are perpendicular if and only if their slopes are negative reciprocals of each other. This means their product is -1 ($m_1 m_2 = -1$). Alternatively, if one slope is $m_1$, the perpendicular slope $m_2$ is $-\frac{1}{m_1}$. A special case involves a vertical line and a horizontal line, which are always perpendicular to each other.

Example: Slope of a Perpendicular Line If a line has a slope $m = \frac{3}{4}$, then a line perpendicular to it will have a slope $m_P = -\frac{1}{3/4} = -\frac{4}{3}$. To check: $\frac{3}{4} \times (-\frac{4}{3}) = -1$.

Checkpoint: The slope of line $L$ is $-5$. (a) The slope of a line parallel to $L$ is $-5$. (b) The slope of a line perpendicular to $L$ is $-\frac{1}{-5} = \frac{1}{5}$.

Review Questions

  1. What is the primary purpose of calculating a line's slope? How does it help us understand the line visually and algebraically?
  2. Describe the four types of slopes (positive, negative, zero, undefined) and sketch an example of each on a coordinate plane. What does each type tell you about the direction of the line?
  3. Given three points A, B, and C, explain the process for determining if they are collinear using the concept of slope. Provide an example where points are collinear and another where they are not.
  4. Explain the relationship between the slopes of two parallel lines and two perpendicular lines. If a line has a slope of $-\frac{2}{7}$, what would be the slope of a line parallel to it and a line perpendicular to it?
  5. Why is the slope of a horizontal line equal to zero, while the slope of a vertical line is undefined? Relate your explanation to the slope formula $m = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}$.
