Chapter 6, Section 3: Multiplying and Dividing Rational Expressions

Overview

This section explains how to multiply and divide rational expressions, emphasizing that the procedures are analogous to those used for ordinary fractions. The core strategy for both operations involves factoring all numerators and denominators completely, canceling common factors before performing multiplication, and being mindful of domain restrictions that arise throughout the process.

Key Concepts

  • Similarity to Fractions: Multiplying and dividing rational expressions follows the same fundamental rules as multiplying and dividing numerical fractions.
  • Factoring First: The most critical step for both multiplication and division is to factor all polynomials in the numerators and denominators completely before attempting to multiply or cancel. This simplifies the expressions and prevents dealing with large, complex polynomials.
  • Canceling Common Factors: After factoring, identify and cancel any factors that appear in both a numerator and a denominator. This must be done across all expressions involved in the product or before multiplying for division.
  • Multiplying Rational Expressions: To multiply $\frac{P}{Q}$ by $\frac{R}{S}$, multiply the numerators together and the denominators together: $\frac{P}{Q} \cdot \frac{R}{S} = \frac{PR}{QS}$.
  • Dividing Rational Expressions: To divide $\frac{P}{Q}$ by $\frac{R}{S}$, multiply the first rational expression by the reciprocal of the second: $\frac{P}{Q} \div \frac{R}{S} = \frac{P}{Q} \cdot \frac{S}{R} = \frac{PS}{QR}$. Remember to invert only the divisor (the second fraction).
  • Domain Restrictions: It's crucial to identify values that would make any denominator zero in the original expressions and in any intermediate steps (especially for division, where the divisor's numerator becomes a denominator after inverting). These values must be excluded from the domain.

Important Terms

  • Rational Expression: An expression that can be written as the ratio of two polynomials, where the denominator is not zero. (e.g., $\frac{x^2+1}{x-2}$)
  • Reciprocal: For a fraction $\frac{A}{B}$, its reciprocal is $\frac{B}{A}$. When dividing by a fraction, you multiply by its reciprocal.
  • Factor: To express a polynomial as a product of simpler polynomials (e.g., $x^2 - 4 = (x-2)(x+2)$).
  • Common Factors: Factors that appear in both the numerator and the denominator of a rational expression, which can be canceled out.
  • Domain Restrictions: Values for the variable(s) that would make any denominator in the expression equal to zero, and thus must be excluded from the domain of the expression.

Detailed Notes

Multiplying Rational Expressions

The process for multiplying rational expressions is straightforward once you remember the fundamental rules for multiplying fractions.

Property: If $\frac{P}{Q}$ and $\frac{R}{S}$ are rational expressions with $Q \neq 0$ and $S \neq 0$, then: $\frac{P}{Q} \cdot \frac{R}{S} = \frac{PR}{QS}$

Procedure:

  1. Factor all numerators and denominators completely.
  2. Cancel any common factors that appear in both a numerator and a denominator.
  3. Multiply the remaining numerators together and the remaining denominators together.
  4. State any domain restrictions (values that make any original denominator zero).

Example (a): Basic Multiplication Multiply and simplify: $\frac{3x^2}{5y} \cdot \frac{10y^3}{9x}$

  • Step 1: Factor (numbers can be broken down, variables are already factors) $\frac{3 \cdot x \cdot x}{5 \cdot y} \cdot \frac{2 \cdot 5 \cdot y \cdot y \cdot y}{3 \cdot 3 \cdot x}$
  • Step 2: Cancel Common Factors Notice $3$, $5$, $x$, and $y$ are common factors. $\frac{\cancel{3} \cdot \cancel{x} \cdot x}{\cancel{5} \cdot \cancel{y}} \cdot \frac{2 \cdot \cancel{5} \cdot \cancel{y} \cdot y \cdot y}{\cancel{3} \cdot 3 \cdot \cancel{x}}$
  • Step 3: Multiply Remaining Factors $\frac{x}{1} \cdot \frac{2 \cdot y \cdot y}{3} = \frac{2xy^2}{3}$
  • Domain Restrictions: From the original denominators, $5y \neq 0 \implies y \neq 0$ and $9x \neq 0 \implies x \neq 0$.

Example (b): Multiplying with Polynomials Multiply and simplify: $\frac{x^2 - 4}{x^2 + x} \cdot \frac{x^2 + 2x + 1}{x + 2}$

  • Step 1: Factor Everything Numerator 1: $x^2 - 4 = (x - 2)(x + 2)$ Denominator 1: $x^2 + x = x(x + 1)$ Numerator 2: $x^2 + 2x + 1 = (x + 1)^2 = (x + 1)(x + 1)$ Denominator 2: $x + 2$ (already factored)

    Rewrite the expression: $\frac{(x - 2)(x + 2)}{x(x + 1)} \cdot \frac{(x + 1)(x + 1)}{x + 2}$

  • Step 2: Cancel Common Factors Notice $(x+2)$ and $(x+1)$ are common factors. $\frac{(x - 2)\cancel{(x + 2)}}{x\cancel{(x + 1)}} \cdot \frac{\cancel{(x + 1)}(x + 1)}{\cancel{(x + 2)}}$

  • Step 3: Multiply Remaining Factors $\frac{x - 2}{x} \cdot \frac{x + 1}{1} = \frac{(x - 2)(x + 1)}{x}$

  • Domain Restrictions: From original denominators: $x^2 + x \neq 0 \implies x(x + 1) \neq 0 \implies x \neq 0, x \neq -1$ $x + 2 \neq 0 \implies x \neq -2$ So, $x \neq 0, x \neq -1, x \neq -2$.

Dividing Rational Expressions

Dividing rational expressions also mirrors the rule for dividing numerical fractions: "invert and multiply."

Property: To divide by a rational expression, multiply by its reciprocal: $\frac{P}{Q} \div \frac{R}{S} = \frac{P}{Q} \cdot \frac{S}{R} = \frac{PS}{QR}$ (provided $Q \neq 0, S \neq 0, R \neq 0$).

Procedure:

  1. Rewrite the division problem as a multiplication problem by inverting the second fraction (the divisor) and changing the operation to multiplication.
  2. Factor all numerators and denominators completely.
  3. Cancel any common factors.
  4. Multiply the remaining numerators and denominators.
  5. State any domain restrictions (values that make any original denominator zero, and values that make the numerator of the divisor zero, as it becomes a denominator).

Caution: Always invert the second fraction (the divisor), not the first. Flipping the wrong fraction is a common mistake.

Example (a): Basic Division Divide and simplify: $\frac{4x^3}{7y^2} \div \frac{8x}{21y}$

  • Step 1: Rewrite as Multiplication by Reciprocal $\frac{4x^3}{7y^2} \cdot \frac{21y}{8x}$
  • Step 2: Factor (break down numbers and variables) $\frac{4 \cdot x \cdot x \cdot x}{7 \cdot y \cdot y} \cdot \frac{3 \cdot 7 \cdot y}{2 \cdot 4 \cdot x}$
  • Step 3: Cancel Common Factors $\frac{\cancel{4} \cdot \cancel{x} \cdot x \cdot x}{\cancel{7} \cdot \cancel{y} \cdot y} \cdot \frac{3 \cdot \cancel{7} \cdot \cancel{y}}{2 \cdot \cancel{4} \cdot \cancel{x}}$
  • Step 4: Multiply Remaining Factors $\frac{x^2}{y} \cdot \frac{3}{2} = \frac{3x^2}{2y}$
  • Domain Restrictions: From original denominators: $7y^2 \neq 0 \implies y \neq 0$; $8x \neq 0 \implies x \neq 0$. From the inverted fraction's denominator (original divisor's numerator): $8x \neq 0 \implies x \neq 0$. Overall: $x \neq 0, y \neq 0$.

Example (b): Dividing with Polynomials Divide and simplify: $\frac{x^2 - 9}{x^2 - x} \div \frac{x^2 + x - 12}{x^2 - 1}$

  • Step 1: Rewrite as Multiplication by Reciprocal $\frac{x^2 - 9}{x^2 - x} \cdot \frac{x^2 - 1}{x^2 + x - 12}$

  • Step 2: Factor Everything $x^2 - 9 = (x - 3)(x + 3)$ $x^2 - x = x(x - 1)$ $x^2 - 1 = (x - 1)(x + 1)$ $x^2 + x - 12 = (x + 4)(x - 3)$

    Rewrite the expression: $\frac{(x - 3)(x + 3)}{x(x - 1)} \cdot \frac{(x - 1)(x + 1)}{(x + 4)(x - 3)}$

  • Step 3: Cancel Common Factors $\frac{\cancel{(x - 3)}(x + 3)}{x\cancel{(x - 1)}} \cdot \frac{\cancel{(x - 1)}(x + 1)}{(x + 4)\cancel{(x - 3)}}$

  • Step 4: Multiply Remaining Factors $\frac{x + 3}{x} \cdot \frac{x + 1}{x + 4} = \frac{(x + 3)(x + 1)}{x(x + 4)}$

  • Domain Restrictions: From original denominators: $x^2 - x \neq 0 \implies x(x - 1) \neq 0 \implies x \neq 0, x \neq 1$ $x^2 - 1 \neq 0 \implies (x - 1)(x + 1) \neq 0 \implies x \neq 1, x \neq -1$ From the numerator of the original divisor (which became a denominator): $x^2 + x - 12 \neq 0 \implies (x + 4)(x - 3) \neq 0 \implies x \neq -4, x \neq 3$ Overall: $x \neq 0, x \neq 1, x \neq -1, x \neq -4, x \neq 3$.

Example (c): Division Involving a Polynomial Simplify $\frac{x^2 + 2x - 8}{x + 1} \div (x - 2)$

  • Step 1: Rewrite as Multiplication by Reciprocal First, write the polynomial $(x-2)$ as a rational expression: $\frac{x-2}{1}$. Then, invert and multiply: $\frac{x^2 + 2x - 8}{x + 1} \cdot \frac{1}{x - 2}$

  • Step 2: Factor Everything Numerator 1: $x^2 + 2x - 8 = (x + 4)(x - 2)$ Denominator 1: $x + 1$ (already factored) Numerator 2: $1$ Denominator 2: $x - 2$ (already factored)

    Rewrite the expression: $\frac{(x + 4)(x - 2)}{x + 1} \cdot \frac{1}{x - 2}$

  • Step 3: Cancel Common Factors $\frac{(x + 4)\cancel{(x - 2)}}{x + 1} \cdot \frac{1}{\cancel{(x - 2)}}$

  • Step 4: Multiply Remaining Factors $\frac{x + 4}{x + 1} \cdot \frac{1}{1} = \frac{x + 4}{x + 1}$

  • Domain Restrictions: From original denominators: $x + 1 \neq 0 \implies x \neq -1$ From original divisor: $(x - 2) \neq 0 \implies x \neq 2$ Overall: $x \neq -1, x \neq 2$.
