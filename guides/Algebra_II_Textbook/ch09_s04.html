Hyperbolas: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces hyperbolas, a type of conic section defined by the constant difference of distances to two fixed points called foci. Unlike ellipses, hyperbolas consist of two separate, open branches that approach linear asymptotes. The study guide will cover their standard forms, key features like vertices and foci, methods for graphing, conversion from general equations, classification of all conic sections, and applications, including solving systems of equations.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of a Hyperbola: A hyperbola is the set of all points in a plane where the absolute difference of the distances from two fixed points (foci) is constant, $|d_1 - d_2| = 2a$.
  • Two Branches: Unlike other conic sections (except degenerate cases), hyperbolas consist of two distinct, unbounded curves.
  • Center, Foci, and Vertices:
    • The center is the midpoint of the segment joining the foci.
    • Foci are the two fixed points that define the hyperbola, located at a distance $c$ from the center.
    • Vertices are the points on each branch closest to the center, located a distance $a$ from the center along the transverse axis.
  • Transverse and Conjugate Axes:
    • The transverse axis passes through the foci and vertices and determines the orientation of the hyperbola (horizontal or vertical). Its length is $2a$.
    • The conjugate axis is perpendicular to the transverse axis, passes through the center, and has length $2b$.
  • Fundamental Relationship: For a hyperbola, the distances $a$, $b$, and $c$ are related by the equation $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$. This is a key distinction from ellipses ($c^2 = a^2 - b^2$).
  • Standard Forms:
    • Horizontal Transverse Axis: $\frac{(x-h)^2}{a^2} - \frac{(y-k)^2}{b^2} = 1$
    • Vertical Transverse Axis: $\frac{(y-k)^2}{a^2} - \frac{(x-h)^2}{b^2} = 1$
    • The term with the positive coefficient indicates the direction of the transverse axis.
  • Asymptotes: These are straight lines that the hyperbola branches approach but never touch as they extend outwards. They are crucial for sketching. Their slopes are $\pm b/a$ for horizontal hyperbolas and $\pm a/b$ for vertical hyperbolas, passing through the center $(h,k)$.
  • Central Rectangle: A rectangle centered at $(h,k)$ with dimensions $2a$ (along the transverse axis) by $2b$ (along the conjugate axis). Its diagonals define the asymptotes.
  • Eccentricity ($e$): For a hyperbola, $e = c/a$, and always $e > 1$. This value indicates how "open" the hyperbola's branches are.
  • Conjugate Hyperbolas: Two hyperbolas that share the same asymptotes but have their transverse and conjugate axes swapped. For example, $\frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$ and $\frac{y^2}{b^2} - \frac{x^2}{a^2} = 1$ are conjugates.
  • Degenerate Hyperbolas: Occur when the equation of a hyperbola simplifies to a pair of intersecting lines, usually passing through the center.
  • Classifying Conics by General Equation: For $Ax^2 + Cy^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$:
    • Circle: $A=C$ (and same sign)
    • Ellipse: $A \neq C$ (but both same sign)
    • Hyperbola: $A$ and $C$ have opposite signs
    • Parabola: Only one squared term ($A=0$ or $C=0$)
  • Linear-Quadratic Systems: Solving for the intersection points of a line and a conic section, typically using substitution to yield a quadratic equation whose solutions reveal 0, 1, or 2 intersection points.

Important Terms

  • Hyperbola: The set of all points where the absolute difference of the distances to two fixed points (foci) is constant.
  • Foci (plural of focus): The two fixed points used to define a hyperbola.
  • Center: The midpoint of the segment connecting the foci, and also the intersection point of the asymptotes.
  • Transverse Axis: The line segment connecting the two vertices of a hyperbola, passing through the foci and center. Its length is $2a$.
  • Conjugate Axis: The line segment perpendicular to the transverse axis, passing through the center. Its length is $2b$.
  • Vertices: The points where the hyperbola intersects its transverse axis. They are $a$ units from the center.
  • Asymptotes: Two lines that the branches of a hyperbola approach as they extend indefinitely. They intersect at the center of the hyperbola.
  • Eccentricity ($e$): A measure of how "open" a hyperbola is, defined as $c/a$. For hyperbolas, $e > 1$.
  • Central Rectangle: A rectangle used as a guide to sketch the asymptotes of a hyperbola. It is centered at $(h,k)$ with sides of length $2a$ and $2b$.
  • Conjugate Hyperbola: A hyperbola that shares the same asymptotes as another hyperbola but has its transverse and conjugate axes swapped.
  • Degenerate Hyperbola: A special case where the hyperbola's equation results in a pair of intersecting lines.
  • Linear-Quadratic System: A system of equations consisting of one linear equation and one conic section equation.
  • Secant Line: A line that intersects a conic section at two distinct points.
  • Tangent Line: A line that intersects a conic section at exactly one point.

Detailed Notes

1. Definition and Fundamental Properties of Hyperbolas

A hyperbola is geometrically defined as the collection of all points $(x,y)$ such that the absolute difference of their distances from two fixed points, called the foci ($F_1$ and $F_2$), is a constant value, typically denoted $2a$. Mathematically, this is $|d_1 - d_2| = 2a$. This definition contrasts with an ellipse, where the sum of distances is constant.

Hyperbolas are characterized by two distinct, open curves or "branches" that extend infinitely, moving away from each other and the center. They are formed when a plane intersects both halves (nappes) of a double cone.

Real-world applications include:

  • Cooling tower design: The cross-section of some cooling towers is hyperbolic.
  • Celestial mechanics: Paths of comets with enough speed to escape a star's gravity are hyperbolic.
  • Navigation systems: LORAN (Long Range Navigation) systems utilize the constant difference in signal arrival times from two transmitters to pinpoint a ship's hyperbolic location.

The center of a hyperbola is the midpoint of the segment connecting the foci.

2. Standard Forms of Hyperbolas

The standard form of a hyperbola reveals its key characteristics, including its center, orientation, and dimensions. Let $(h,k)$ be the center of the hyperbola. The relationship between $a$, $b$, and $c$ for a hyperbola is given by: $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$ (where $c$ is the distance from the center to each focus, $a$ is the distance from the center to each vertex, and $b$ is related to the width of the central rectangle).

There are two primary standard forms:

  1. Horizontal Transverse Axis: Branches open left and right. $\frac{(x-h)^2}{a^2} - \frac{(y-k)^2}{b^2} = 1$

    • Vertices: $(h \pm a, k)$
    • Foci: $(h \pm c, k)$
    • Asymptotes: $y - k = \pm \frac{b}{a}(x - h)$
    • Eccentricity: $e = \frac{c}{a} > 1$

  2. Vertical Transverse Axis: Branches open up and down. $\frac{(y-k)^2}{a^2} - \frac{(x-h)^2}{b^2} = 1$

    • Vertices: $(h, k \pm a)$
    • Foci: $(h, k \pm c)$
    • Asymptotes: $y - k = \pm \frac{a}{b}(x - h)$
    • Eccentricity: $e = \frac{c}{a} > 1$

Key Distinction for Orientation: The term with the positive coefficient in the standard form determines the orientation of the transverse axis.

  • If the $x$-term is positive, the hyperbola is horizontal.
  • If the $y$-term is positive, the hyperbola is vertical. Also, note that $a^2$ is always under the positive term, not necessarily the larger denominator (unlike ellipses).

Crucial Caution: Remember the relationship $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$ for hyperbolas. This is distinct from ellipses, where $c^2 = a^2 - b^2$. Mixing these up is a common error!

3. Graphing Hyperbolas Using the Central Rectangle and Asymptotes

The asymptotes are the most distinctive feature for sketching a hyperbola.

  1. Identify the Center: $(h,k)$.
  2. Determine $a$ and $b$: From the denominators $a^2$ and $b^2$.
  3. Construct the Central Rectangle:
    • Draw a rectangle centered at $(h,k)$.
    • For a horizontal hyperbola, the rectangle extends $a$ units left and right from the center, and $b$ units up and down.
    • For a vertical hyperbola, the rectangle extends $b$ units left and right from the center, and $a$ units up and down.
  4. Draw Asymptotes: Extend the diagonals of the central rectangle. These are the asymptote lines.
  5. Locate Vertices: Plot the vertices $a$ units along the transverse axis from the center.
  6. Sketch Branches: Draw the hyperbolic branches starting from the vertices and curving outwards, approaching the asymptotes but never touching them.
  7. Locate Foci: Calculate $c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$ and plot the foci $c$ units along the transverse axis from the center.

Example: For $x^2/9 - y^2/4 = 1$: Center $(0,0)$, $a=3, b=2$. Vertices: $(\pm 3, 0)$. Foci: $c^2 = 9+4=13 \implies c=\sqrt{13}$. Foci: $(\pm \sqrt{13}, 0)$. Asymptotes: $y = \pm (2/3)x$.

4. Converting General Form to Standard Form

To graph or identify features from an equation in general form $Ax^2 + Cy^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$, you must convert it to standard form by completing the square for both $x$ and $y$ terms.

Steps:

  1. Group $x$-terms and $y$-terms. Move the constant term to the right side of the equation.
  2. Factor out the coefficients of $x^2$ and $y^2$ from their respective groups.
  3. Complete the square for the $x$-terms and $y$-terms. Remember to add the same values to both sides of the equation (multiplied by the factored-out coefficient).
  4. Rewrite the perfect square trinomials as squared binomials.
  5. Divide the entire equation by the constant on the right side to make it equal to 1.
  6. Ensure the $x^2$ and $y^2$ terms have opposite signs to confirm it's a hyperbola.

Example: Convert $4x^2 - 9y^2 + 8x + 36y - 68 = 0$

  1. $4x^2 + 8x - 9y^2 + 36y = 68$
  2. $4(x^2 + 2x) - 9(y^2 - 4y) = 68$
  3. $4(x^2 + 2x + 1) - 9(y^2 - 4y + 4) = 68 + 4(1) - 9(4)$
  4. $4(x+1)^2 - 9(y-2)^2 = 68 + 4 - 36 = 36$
  5. $\frac{4(x+1)^2}{36} - \frac{9(y-2)^2}{36} = \frac{36}{36}$
  6. $\frac{(x+1)^2}{9} - \frac{(y-2)^2}{4} = 1$ From this standard form, we identify a horizontal hyperbola with center $(-1,2)$, $a=3$, $b=2$.

5. Classifying Conics by the General Equation

For the general quadratic equation $Ax^2 + Cy^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$:

  • Circle: $A=C$ (and both are non-zero and have the same sign).
  • Ellipse: $A \neq C$ (but both are non-zero and have the same sign).
  • Hyperbola: $A$ and $C$ have opposite signs.
  • Parabola: Either $A=0$ or $C=0$ (only one squared term).
  • Degenerate cases (a point, a line, or intersecting lines) are possible if the equation factors or has no real solutions.

6. Conjugate and Degenerate Hyperbolas

  • Conjugate Hyperbolas: Two hyperbolas are conjugates if they share the same asymptotes but their roles of $a$ and $b$ are swapped, meaning their transverse and conjugate axes are interchanged. If one hyperbola is $\frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$, its conjugate is $\frac{y^2}{b^2} - \frac{x^2}{a^2} = 1$.
  • Degenerate Hyperbolas: Occur when the constant term on the right side of the standard form equation is zero, or when the equation factors into two linear equations. For example, $x^2 - 4y^2 = 0$ factors into $(x - 2y)(x + 2y) = 0$, which represents two intersecting lines ($x=2y$ and $x=-2y$) passing through the origin.

7. Applications: LORAN Navigation

LORAN systems use hyperbolas for navigation. If a ship measures a constant difference in the arrival times of radio signals from two transmitters, it is located on a specific hyperbola where the transmitters act as the foci. By using a second pair of transmitters (creating another hyperbola), the ship's precise position can be determined as the intersection of these two hyperbolic curves.

8. Solving Linear-Quadratic Systems

A linear-quadratic system involves a linear equation (representing a line) and a quadratic equation (representing a conic section). The goal is to find the point(s) where the line intersects the conic.

Possible Intersections:

  • Two points: A secant line.
  • One point: A tangent line.
  • No points: The line misses the conic.

Algebraic Strategy:

  1. Solve the linear equation for one variable (e.g., $y$ in terms of $x$).
  2. Substitute this expression into the conic section equation.
  3. Simplify and solve the resulting quadratic equation.
    • Two real solutions indicate two intersection points.
    • One real solution (a repeated root, discriminant = 0) indicates a single tangent point.
    • No real solutions (negative discriminant) indicates no intersection points.
  4. Substitute the found variable values back into the linear equation to find the corresponding values of the other variable.

Example: Find intersections of $y = x+1$ and $x^2+y^2=25$. Substitute $y$: $x^2 + (x+1)^2 = 25 \implies x^2 + x^2 + 2x + 1 = 25 \implies 2x^2 + 2x - 24 = 0 \implies x^2 + x - 12 = 0$. Factoring gives $(x+4)(x-3)=0$, so $x=-4$ or $x=3$. If $x=-4$, $y=-4+1=-3$. Point: $(-4,-3)$. If $x=3$, $y=3+1=4$. Point: $(3,4)$. The line intersects the circle at two points.

Review Questions

  1. Describe the fundamental definition of a hyperbola and explain how it differs from the definition of an ellipse. Provide at least two real-world applications of hyperbolas.
  2. Given a hyperbola in standard form, explain how to determine its orientation (horizontal or vertical), identify its center, vertices, and foci. What is the key algebraic relationship between $a$, $b$, and $c$ for a hyperbola, and how is it different from that of an ellipse?
  3. What are asymptotes in the context of hyperbolas? Explain how the central rectangle is used to construct the asymptotes and sketch the graph of a hyperbola. Why are asymptotes important for visualizing hyperbolas?
  4. Consider the general equation $Ax^2 + Cy^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$. Explain the conditions on the coefficients $A$ and $C$ that classify this equation as a circle, ellipse, parabola, or hyperbola. Provide an example of a degenerate hyperbola and explain its graphical representation.
  5. You are given a linear equation and a hyperbola equation. Describe the algebraic strategy to find their intersection points. How can the number of real solutions from the resulting quadratic equation inform you about the geometric relationship between the line and the hyperbola?
