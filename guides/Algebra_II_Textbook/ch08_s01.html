Study Guide: Transformations I: Shifts and Reflections

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concept of graphical transformations, which allows us to quickly graph new functions by modifying known parent functions. We explore two primary types of transformations: shifts (moving the graph up, down, left, or right) and reflections (flipping the graph over the x-axis or y-axis). Understanding these operations simplifies the graphing process by relating complex functions back to simpler, familiar forms.

Key Concepts

  • Graphical Transformations: Operations that change the position or orientation of a graph without altering its fundamental shape. These include shifts and reflections.
  • Parent Function: The simplest form within a family of functions, serving as a base for transformations. Common examples include $f(x) = x^2$ (quadratic), $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$ (square root), $f(x) = |x|$ (absolute value), and $f(x) = x^3$ (cubic).
  • Vertical Shifts: Adding or subtracting a constant $k$ outside the function moves the graph up ($+k$) or down ($-k$). The shape remains the same.
  • Horizontal Shifts: Adding or subtracting a constant $h$ inside the function (to the input) moves the graph left ($-h$, often written as $f(x+h)$) or right ($+h$, often written as $f(x-h)$). The direction is counterintuitive to the sign.
  • Reflections over the x-axis: Placing a negative sign outside the function ($y = -f(x)$) flips the graph vertically across the x-axis.
  • Reflections over the y-axis: Placing a negative sign inside the function ($y = f(-x)$) flips the graph horizontally across the y-axis.
  • Combined Transformations: Multiple transformations can be applied to a single parent function. It's often helpful to interpret them sequentially, typically starting with changes to the input (horizontal shifts, y-axis reflections), then output (vertical shifts, x-axis reflections).

Important Terms

  • Parent Function: The most basic function of a type or family, from which other functions in the family can be derived through transformations.
  • Graphical Transformations: Arithmetic operations applied to a function's inputs or outputs that change the position or orientation of its graph, such as shifts and reflections.
  • Vertical Shift: A transformation that moves a graph up or down by adding or subtracting a constant to the function's output.
  • Horizontal Shift: A transformation that moves a graph left or right by adding or subtracting a constant to the function's input.
  • Reflection over the x-axis: A transformation that flips a graph across the horizontal x-axis, changing the sign of the y-coordinates of all points.
  • Reflection over the y-axis: A transformation that flips a graph across the vertical y-axis, changing the sign of the x-coordinates of all points.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Transformations

Graphical transformations provide an efficient way to sketch the graphs of many functions once the graph of a basic "parent" function is known. By applying simple arithmetic operations—adding, subtracting, or negating—to the input ($x$) or output ($f(x)$) of a parent function, we can shift or reflect its graph.

Parent Functions

A parent function is the simplest form of a function family. Knowing the basic shape and key features (like the vertex for parabolas or absolute value functions, or the starting point for square root functions) of these parent functions is crucial. Common parent functions discussed in this context include:

  • Quadratic: $f(x) = x^2$ (parabola with vertex at $(0,0)$)
  • Square Root: $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$ (starts at $(0,0)$ and increases to the right)
  • Absolute Value: $f(x) = |x|$ (V-shape with vertex at $(0,0)$)
  • Cubic: $f(x) = x^3$ (S-shape passing through $(0,0)$)

Vertical Shifts

A vertical shift moves the entire graph up or down without changing its shape. This occurs when a constant $k$ is added to or subtracted from the entire function output.

  • Rule: Let $k > 0$.
    • $y = f(x) + k$: Shifts the graph up $k$ units.
    • $y = f(x) - k$: Shifts the graph down $k$ units.
  • Effect on points: Every point $(x, y)$ on the original graph moves to $(x, y \pm k)$.
  • Example: For $f(x) = x^2$ (vertex at $(0,0)$):
    • $g(x) = x^2 + 3$: Shifts $f(x)$ up 3 units. The new vertex is at $(0, 3)$.
    • $h(x) = x^2 - 5$: Shifts $f(x)$ down 5 units. The new vertex is at $(0, -5)$.

Horizontal Shifts

A horizontal shift moves the entire graph left or right. This occurs when a constant $h$ is added to or subtracted from the input variable $x$ inside the function.

  • Rule: Let $h > 0$.
    • $y = f(x - h)$: Shifts the graph right $h$ units.
    • $y = f(x + h)$: Shifts the graph left $h$ units.
  • Counterintuitive Direction: Notice that $f(x-h)$ moves right, and $f(x+h)$ moves left. A useful trick is to ask: "What input value makes the expression inside the parentheses equal to zero?" This value indicates the new x-coordinate of the graph's key feature (e.g., vertex).
  • Effect on points: Every point $(x, y)$ on the original graph moves to $(x \mp h, y)$.
  • Example: For $f(x) = |x|$ (vertex at $(0,0)$):
    • $g(x) = |x - 4|$: Setting $x - 4 = 0$ gives $x = 4$. Shifts $f(x)$ right 4 units. The new vertex is at $(4, 0)$.
    • $h(x) = |x + 2|$: Setting $x + 2 = 0$ gives $x = -2$. Shifts $f(x)$ left 2 units. The new vertex is at $(-2, 0)$.
  • Checkpoint Example:
    • $y = (x-1)^2 + 7$: Parent $y=x^2$. Shifts right 1 and up 7. Vertex at $(1, 7)$.
    • $y = |x + 5| - 3$: Parent $y=|x|$. Shifts left 5 and down 3. Vertex at $(-5, -3)$.

Reflections

Reflections flip the graph across an axis. This occurs when a negative sign is applied to the function's output or input.

  • Reflection over the x-axis:
    • Rule: $y = -f(x)$
    • Effect: Flips the graph vertically across the x-axis.
    • Effect on points: Each point $(x, y)$ maps to $(x, -y)$.
  • Reflection over the y-axis:
    • Rule: $y = f(-x)$
    • Effect: Flips the graph horizontally across the y-axis.
    • Effect on points: Each point $(x, y)$ maps to $(-x, y)$.

Combined Shifts and Reflections

When multiple transformations are applied, they can be read sequentially. A general strategy is to consider transformations of the input first (horizontal shifts, y-axis reflections), then transformations of the output (vertical shifts, x-axis reflections).

  • Example 1: Starting from $f(x) = x^2$:

    • $g(x) = -(x - 2)^2 + 1$:
      1. $(x-2)^2$: Shift right 2 units.
      2. $-(x-2)^2$: Reflect over the x-axis (parabola opens downward).
      3. $-(x-2)^2 + 1$: Shift up 1 unit.
      • Result: Vertex at $(2, 1)$, opens downward.
    • $h(x) = -x^2 + 4$:
      1. $-x^2$: Reflect over the x-axis.
      2. $-x^2 + 4$: Shift up 4 units.
      • Result: Vertex at $(0, 4)$, opens downward.

  • Example 2: Describing $r(x) = -\sqrt{x} + 2$ relative to $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$:

    1. $-\sqrt{x}$: Reflect over the x-axis (graph now extends downward from the origin).
    2. $-\sqrt{x} + 2$: Shift up 2 units.
    • Result: Starting point moves from $(0, 0)$ to $(0, 2)$, and the graph decreases as $x$ increases.

  • Mapping Points Under Combined Transformations: If $(3, 5)$ lies on $y = f(x)$:

    • For $y = f(x - 4) + 2$: Shifts right 4, up 2.
      • $(x, y) \to (x+4, y+2)$.
      • $(3, 5) \to (3+4, 5+2) = (7, 7)$.
    • For $y = -f(x + 1) - 3$: Shifts left 1, reflects over x-axis, shifts down 3.
      • $(x, y) \to (x-1, -y-3)$.
      • $(3, 5) \to (3-1, -5-3) = (2, -8)$.
    • If $f(2) = 3$ and $g(x) = f(x+3) - 7$, find $g(-1)$:
      • Substitute $x = -1$ into $g(x)$:
      • $g(-1) = f(-1+3) - 7 = f(2) - 7$.
      • Since $f(2) = 3$, we have $g(-1) = 3 - 7 = -4$.

  • Checkpoint Example:

    • $p(x) = -(x+3)^2 - 1$ relative to $y = x^2$:
      1. $(x+3)^2$: Shift left 3.
      2. $-(x+3)^2$: Reflect over x-axis.
      3. $-(x+3)^2 - 1$: Shift down 1.
      • Result: Vertex at $(-3, -1)$, opens downward.

Review Questions

  1. What is a "parent function," and why is it a crucial concept for understanding graphical transformations? Provide an example of a parent function and explain its basic shape.
  2. Explain the key differences between a vertical shift and a horizontal shift. How can you tell from a function's equation whether it will shift vertically or horizontally, and in which direction? Provide an example for each.
  3. Describe the visual effect of reflecting a graph over the x-axis versus reflecting it over the y-axis. If a point $(a, b)$ is on the original graph, what would be its coordinates after each type of reflection?
  4. Consider the function $g(x) = -|x - 5| + 2$. Describe the sequence of transformations from the parent function $f(x) = |x|$ that would produce $g(x)$. What are the coordinates of the vertex of $g(x)$?
  5. Suppose the point $(-4, 9)$ lies on the graph of $y = f(x)$. Find the corresponding point on the graph of $h(x) = f(-x + 2) - 6$. Explain the steps you took to determine the new coordinates.
