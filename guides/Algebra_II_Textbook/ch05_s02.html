Study Guide: Multiplying Polynomials

Overview

This section focuses on the fundamental techniques for multiplying polynomials, building upon the distributive property. It covers methods for multiplying various types of polynomials, from monomials by polynomials to more complex expressions, and introduces special product patterns that streamline calculations and are crucial for future topics like factoring.

Key Concepts

  • Distributive Property as the Foundation: All polynomial multiplication relies on the distributive property, where each term of one polynomial is multiplied by every term of the other.
  • Monomial by Polynomial Multiplication: To multiply a single-term expression (monomial) by a multi-term expression (polynomial), distribute the monomial to each term within the polynomial.
  • Binomial by Binomial Multiplication: When multiplying two two-term expressions (binomials), apply the distributive property twice, multiplying each term of the first binomial by each term of the second. This results in four partial products before combining like terms.
  • Special Product Formulas: Certain binomial products appear frequently and have specific patterns that are efficient to memorize:
    • Square of a Sum: $(a + b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2$ (a perfect square trinomial).
    • Square of a Difference: $(a - b)^2 = a^2 - 2ab + b^2$ (a perfect square trinomial).
    • Difference of Squares: $(a + b)(a - b) = a^2 - b^2$.
  • Higher Powers of Binomials: To raise a binomial to a power greater than two, such as cubing, repeatedly apply the distributive property by multiplying the binomial by itself multiple times.
  • General Polynomial Multiplication: For any two polynomials, distribute each term of the first polynomial to every term of the second, then combine all resulting like terms. If one polynomial has $m$ terms and the other has $n$ terms, this process generates $mn$ partial products.

Important Terms

  • Monomial: A polynomial with only one term (e.g., $3x^2$, $5$).
  • Binomial: A polynomial with two terms (e.g., $x+2$, $3y-4$).
  • Polynomial: An algebraic expression consisting of one or more terms, where each term is a product of a constant and one or more variables raised to non-negative integer powers.
  • Distributive Property: An algebraic property that states $a(b+c) = ab + ac$. It is the core principle behind all polynomial multiplication.
  • Perfect Square Trinomial: A trinomial (polynomial with three terms) that results from squaring a binomial, such as $a^2 + 2ab + b^2$ or $a^2 - 2ab + b^2$.
  • Difference of Squares: A binomial that results from multiplying the sum and difference of the same two terms, having the form $a^2 - b^2$.
  • Partial Products: The individual products generated before combining like terms when multiplying polynomials.

Detailed Notes

The Foundation: Distributive Property

Multiplying polynomials is essentially an extension of the distributive property. It's crucial to understand that every term in one polynomial must multiply every term in the other polynomial. Mastering this process is vital, especially since factoring (the reverse of multiplication) relies heavily on these patterns.

Multiplying a Monomial by a Polynomial

To multiply a monomial (a single-term polynomial) by any polynomial, simply distribute the monomial to each term inside the polynomial.

Formula: $a(b_1 + b_2 + \dots + b_n) = ab_1 + ab_2 + \dots + ab_n$

Example: Expand $3x^2(2x^3 - 5x + 4)$.

  • Distribute $3x^2$ to each term: $3x^2(2x^3 - 5x + 4) = (3x^2)(2x^3) - (3x^2)(5x) + (3x^2)(4)$
  • Multiply the coefficients and add the exponents of the variables: $= 6x^5 - 15x^3 + 12x^2$

Multiplying Two Binomials

Multiplying two binomials involves applying the distributive property twice. Each term from the first binomial must multiply each term from the second binomial.

Formula: $(a + b)(c + d) = a(c + d) + b(c + d) = ac + ad + bc + bd$

Notice that this always produces four partial products before you combine any like terms.

Example: Multiply $(3x - 4)(2x + 5)$.

  • Distribute the first term ($3x$) of the first binomial to both terms of the second binomial, then do the same for the second term ($-4$). $(3x - 4)(2x + 5) = 3x(2x + 5) - 4(2x + 5)$
  • Perform the monomial-by-polynomial multiplication: $= (3x)(2x) + (3x)(5) - (4)(2x) - (4)(5)$ $= 6x^2 + 15x - 8x - 20$
  • Combine like terms ($15x - 8x$): $= 6x^2 + 7x - 20$

Checkpoint Practice: Use the distributive property to expand $(5x - 1)(x + 3)$.

  • $(5x - 1)(x + 3) = 5x(x + 3) - 1(x + 3)$
  • $= 5x^2 + 15x - x - 3$
  • $= 5x^2 + 14x - 3$

Special Products

Some binomial products appear so frequently that recognizing and memorizing their patterns can significantly speed up calculations.

Special Product Formulas: For any real numbers or expressions $a$ and $b$:

  1. Square of a Sum: $(a + b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2$ (results in a perfect square trinomial)
  2. Square of a Difference: $(a - b)^2 = a^2 - 2ab + b^2$ (results in a perfect square trinomial)
  3. Difference of Squares: $(a + b)(a - b) = a^2 - b^2$

Important Caution: A common error is assuming $(a+b)^2 = a^2 + b^2$. This is incorrect! The middle term, $2ab$, is essential and must not be omitted. Always remember to expand $(a+b)^2$ as $(a+b)(a+b)$ to see why the $2ab$ term appears.

Examples Applying Special Product Formulas: (a) Expand $(2x + 3)^2$ * Use the "square of a sum" formula: $(a+b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2$ * Here, $a = 2x$ and $b = 3$. * $(2x+3)^2 = (2x)^2 + 2(2x)(3) + (3)^2$ * $= 4x^2 + 12x + 9$

(b) Expand $(5x - 1)^2$ * Use the "square of a difference" formula: $(a-b)^2 = a^2 - 2ab + b^2$ * Here, $a = 5x$ and $b = 1$. * $(5x-1)^2 = (5x)^2 - 2(5x)(1) + (1)^2$ * $= 25x^2 - 10x + 1$

(c) Expand $(4x + 7)(4x - 7)$ * Use the "difference of squares" formula: $(a+b)(a-b) = a^2 - b^2$ * Here, $a = 4x$ and $b = 7$. * $(4x+7)(4x-7) = (4x)^2 - (7)^2$ * $= 16x^2 - 49$

Higher Powers of Binomials

To raise a binomial to a power higher than two (e.g., $(x+2)^3$), you do not need new formulas. Instead, you apply the distributive property repeatedly.

Example: Expand $(x + 2)^3$.

  • First, write the expression as a product of factors: $(x+2)(x+2)(x+2)$.
  • Multiply the first two factors using the "square of a sum" formula or general binomial multiplication: $(x+2)(x+2) = x^2 + 2(x)(2) + 2^2 = x^2 + 4x + 4$.
  • Now, multiply this result by the remaining factor $(x+2)$: $(x^2 + 4x + 4)(x+2)$
  • Distribute each term of the trinomial across the binomial: $= x^2(x+2) + 4x(x+2) + 4(x+2)$ $= (x^3 + 2x^2) + (4x^2 + 8x) + (4x + 8)$
  • Combine like terms: $= x^3 + (2x^2 + 4x^2) + (8x + 4x) + 8$ $= x^3 + 6x^2 + 12x + 8$

While this method works for any power, for very large powers, the Binomial Theorem (a more advanced topic) provides a shortcut.

Multiplying Any Two Polynomials

The general rule for multiplying any two polynomials is to distribute each term of the first polynomial to every term of the second polynomial. After performing all multiplications, combine any like terms.

Rule: For an $m$-term polynomial times an $n$-term polynomial, this process yields $mn$ partial products before combining.

Example: Multiply $(x^2 - 2x + 3)(x + 4)$.

  • Distribute each term of the trinomial $(x^2 - 2x + 3)$ across the binomial $(x + 4)$: $= x^2(x+4) - 2x(x+4) + 3(x+4)$
  • Perform the monomial-by-polynomial multiplications: $= (x^3 + 4x^2) - (2x^2 + 8x) + (3x + 12)$ $= x^3 + 4x^2 - 2x^2 - 8x + 3x + 12$
  • Combine like terms: $= x^3 + (4x^2 - 2x^2) + (-8x + 3x) + 12$ $= x^3 + 2x^2 - 5x + 12$

Checkpoint Practice: Expand $(2x^2 + x - 1)(x - 3)$.

  • $(2x^2 + x - 1)(x - 3) = 2x^2(x - 3) + x(x - 3) - 1(x - 3)$
  • $= (2x^3 - 6x^2) + (x^2 - 3x) - (x - 3)$
  • $= 2x^3 - 6x^2 + x^2 - 3x - x + 3$
  • $= 2x^3 - 5x^2 - 4x + 3$

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words why the distributive property is considered the fundamental principle for all polynomial multiplication. Provide an example comparing its application to multiplying a monomial by a polynomial versus multiplying two binomials.
  2. Describe the three special product formulas introduced in this section. When might it be more efficient to use these formulas instead of the general distributive property for binomial multiplication?
  3. A common mistake is claiming that $(x-y)^2 = x^2 - y^2$. Explain why this is incorrect and provide the correct expansion for $(x-y)^2$, identifying the missing term.
  4. If you are multiplying a polynomial with 4 terms by a polynomial with 3 terms, how many partial products will you get before combining like terms? Illustrate this with an example using general polynomial terms (e.g., $(a+b+c+d)(x+y+z)$).
  5. Outline the step-by-step process for expanding a binomial raised to a higher power, such as $(m+n)^4$, without using the Binomial Theorem. What is the first step, and how do subsequent steps build on it?
