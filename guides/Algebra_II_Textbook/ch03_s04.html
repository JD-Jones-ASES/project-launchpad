Study Guide: Solving Equations by Factoring

Overview

This section introduces a powerful method for solving polynomial equations: factoring. By combining factoring techniques with the Zero Product Property, we can transform complex equations into simpler, linear ones. The core idea is that if a product of factors equals zero, then at least one of those factors must be zero, allowing us to find the possible values for the variable.

Key Concepts

  • Zero Product Property: This fundamental property states that if the product of two or more real numbers is zero, then at least one of the individual numbers must be zero. Mathematically, if (ab = 0), then (a = 0) or (b = 0). This property is the basis for solving equations by factoring.
  • Standard Form of an Equation: Before applying the Zero Product Property, an equation must be written in standard form, meaning all terms are moved to one side of the equation, leaving zero on the other side (e.g., (ax^2+bx+c=0)). The Zero Product Property only applies when the product equals zero.
  • Steps for Solving by Factoring:
    1. Standardize: Write the equation in standard form, with all terms on one side and zero on the other.
    2. Factor Completely: Factor the non-zero side of the equation into its simplest factors. This may involve finding the Greatest Common Factor (GCF), using the AC method, recognizing special products like the difference of squares, or other factoring techniques.
    3. Set Factors to Zero: Apply the Zero Product Property by setting each individual factor equal to zero.
    4. Solve Each Equation: Solve each of the resulting simpler (usually linear) equations for the variable.
    5. Check Solutions: Verify each solution in the original equation to ensure accuracy and to catch any potential errors.
  • Solving Higher-Degree Equations: The factoring method is not limited to quadratic (degree 2) equations. If a polynomial of degree higher than two can be factored (e.g., by factoring out a GCF or using special product patterns), the Zero Product Property can still be applied to find its solutions.
  • Word Problems: When solving word problems that translate into quadratic equations, it's crucial to define variables, form the equation, solve by factoring, and then check the solutions against the original context of the problem (e.g., discarding negative lengths or non-integer values if the problem requires positive integers).

Important Terms

  • Zero Product Property: The mathematical rule that states if a product of factors is equal to zero, then at least one of those factors must be zero.
  • Standard Form (of a Quadratic Equation): The form (ax^2+bx+c=0), where (a, b, c) are real numbers and (a \neq 0). This is the required form for solving by factoring.
  • Factoring: The process of expressing a polynomial as a product of two or more simpler polynomials (its factors).
  • Greatest Common Factor (GCF): The largest factor that two or more terms share. Factoring out the GCF is often the first step in factoring a polynomial.
  • AC Method: A technique used to factor quadratic trinomials of the form (ax^2+bx+c) when (a \neq 1).
  • Difference of Squares: A special factoring pattern where (a^2 - b^2) factors into ((a-b)(a+b)).
  • Solution (or Root): A value of the variable that makes an equation true.
  • Consecutive Integers: Integers that follow each other in order (e.g., (n) and (n+1)).
  • Extraneous Solution: A solution obtained through valid algebraic steps but that does not satisfy the original equation or the conditions of the problem context.

Detailed Notes

The Zero Product Property: The Foundation

The ability to solve equations by factoring hinges entirely on the Zero Product Property. This property is straightforward: if you multiply any two numbers, (a) and (b), and their product is zero, then either (a) must be zero, or (b) must be zero (or both). This extends to any number of factors: if (a_1 a_2 \dots a_k = 0), then at least one (a_j) must be zero.

This property is incredibly useful because it transforms a complex polynomial equation into a series of much simpler linear equations.

Steps for Solving Equations by Factoring

To effectively use the Zero Product Property, follow these critical steps:

  1. Write the Equation in Standard Form: This is the most crucial first step. All terms must be moved to one side of the equation, leaving (0) on the other side. For a quadratic equation, this means the form (ax^2+bx+c=0). If the equation is not set to zero, the Zero Product Property does not apply. For instance, if ((x-3)(x-4)=12), you cannot say (x-3=12) or (x-4=12). You must first expand and move the (12) to the left side.

  2. Factor the Non-Zero Side Completely: Once the equation is in standard form, factor the polynomial expression completely. This might involve:

    • Factoring out a Greatest Common Factor (GCF).
    • Factoring a trinomial of the form (x^2+bx+c).
    • Using the AC Method for trinomials of the form (ax^2+bx+c) where (a \neq 1).
    • Recognizing and factoring special products like the difference of squares ((a^2-b^2)).

  3. Set Each Factor Equal to Zero and Solve: After factoring, you will have a product of factors equal to zero. Now, apply the Zero Product Property: set each individual factor (that contains a variable) equal to zero and solve the resulting simpler equations. These are often linear equations.

  4. Check All Solutions in the Original Equation: Always substitute your solutions back into the original, unfactored equation to ensure they are correct. This helps catch algebraic errors.

Examples of Standard Quadratic Equations

  • Monic Quadratic (coefficient of (x^2) is 1): To solve (x^2-7x+12=0):

    1. Already in standard form.
    2. Factor: We need two numbers that multiply to 12 and add to -7. These are -3 and -4. So, ((x-3)(x-4)=0).
    3. Set factors to zero: (x-3=0) or (x-4=0).
    4. Solve: (x=3) or (x=4).
    5. Check: ((3)^2-7(3)+12 = 9-21+12=0). ((4)^2-7(4)+12=16-28+12=0). Both are correct.

  • General Quadratic (coefficient of (x^2) is not 1): To solve (2x^2+5x-3=0):

    1. Already in standard form.
    2. Factor using the AC method: (ac = 2(-3) = -6). We need two numbers that multiply to -6 and add to 5 (these are 6 and -1). Rewrite: (2x^2+6x-x-3 = 2x(x+3)-1(x+3) = (2x-1)(x+3)=0).
    3. Set factors to zero: (2x-1=0) or (x+3=0).
    4. Solve: (2x=1 \Rightarrow x=1/2) or (x=-3).

Equations with Variables on Both Sides

If an equation has terms on both sides, always collect all terms onto one side to achieve standard form before attempting to factor. For example, to solve (3x^2 = 5x+2):

  1. Move terms to one side: (3x^2-5x-2=0).
  2. Factor using AC method ((ac = 3(-2) = -6); numbers adding to -5 are 1 and -6): (3x^2+x-6x-2 = x(3x+1)-2(3x+1) = (x-2)(3x+1)=0).
  3. Set factors to zero: (x-2=0) or (3x+1=0).
  4. Solve: (x=2) or (x=-1/3).

Higher-Degree Equations

The factoring method is applicable to polynomial equations of degree three or higher, provided they can be factored. For example, to solve (x^3-4x=0):

  1. Already in standard form.
  2. Factor out the GCF, (x): (x(x^2-4)=0).
  3. Recognize (x^2-4) as a difference of squares: (x(x-2)(x+2)=0).
  4. Set each factor to zero: (x=0) or (x-2=0) or (x+2=0).
  5. Solve: (x=0), (x=2), or (x=-2). A degree-three polynomial can have at most three real roots, and we found all three.

Word Problems

Solving word problems involves translating real-world scenarios into algebraic equations.

  1. Define Variables: Clearly state what your variable(s) represent.
  2. Formulate Equation: Translate the problem's relationships into an equation.
  3. Standard Form: Get the equation into standard form ((=0)).
  4. Solve by Factoring: Use the steps outlined above.
  5. Check in Context: This is especially important for word problems. A mathematical solution might not make sense in the real-world context (e.g., a negative length or a fractional number of people). Discard any solutions that are extraneous to the problem's conditions.

Example: Find two consecutive positive integers whose product is 72.

  1. Let the smaller integer be (n). The next consecutive integer is (n+1).
  2. Product is 72: (n(n+1)=72).
  3. Expand and standardize: (n^2+n=72 \Rightarrow n^2+n-72=0).
  4. Factor: ((n+9)(n-8)=0).
  5. Solve: (n=-9) or (n=8).
  6. Check in context: Since we need positive integers, (n=-9) is discarded. Thus, (n=8). The two integers are (8) and (n+1=9). Check: (8 \times 9 = 72).

Common Pitfalls

A very common error is applying the Zero Product Property when the product does not equal zero. For example, if you have (x(x-3)=10), it is incorrect to set (x=10) or (x-3=10). The correct approach is to first expand the expression and move the constant to the left side: (x^2-3x=10 \Rightarrow x^2-3x-10=0). Then factor: ((x-5)(x+2)=0), leading to (x=5) or (x=-2). Always ensure the equation is set to zero before factoring!
