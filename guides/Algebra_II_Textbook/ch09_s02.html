Study Guide: Parabolas as Conic Sections

Overview

This section reintroduces parabolas not just as graphs of quadratic functions, but as a fundamental conic section defined by a unique geometric property. It explores how parabolas are formed by points equidistant from a fixed point (the focus) and a fixed line (the directrix). Understanding this geometric definition reveals the practical applications of parabolas in reflective devices like satellite dishes and flashlights.

Key Concepts

  • Geometric Definition of a Parabola: A parabola is the set of all points in a plane that are the same distance from a specific fixed point (the focus) and a specific fixed line (the directrix).
  • Vertex: The midpoint exactly between the focus and the directrix. It is also the turning point of the parabola.
  • Axis of Symmetry: A line that passes through the vertex and the focus, dividing the parabola into two symmetrical halves.
  • Vertical Parabolas (Vertex Form): These parabolas have a vertical axis of symmetry and open either upward or downward. Their standard equation is $y = a(x - h)^2 + k$, where $(h, k)$ is the vertex.
    • Opens upward if $a > 0$.
    • Opens downward if $a < 0$.
    • Focus: $(h, k + \frac{1}{4a})$.
    • Directrix: $y = k - \frac{1}{4a}$.
  • Horizontal Parabolas (Vertex Form): These parabolas have a horizontal axis of symmetry and open either left or right. Their standard equation is $x = a(y - k)^2 + h$, where $(h, k)$ is the vertex.
    • Opens right if $a > 0$.
    • Opens left if $a < 0$.
    • Focus: $(h + \frac{1}{4a}, k)$.
    • Directrix: $x = h - \frac{1}{4a}$.
    • Note: Horizontal parabolas are not functions of $x$ as they fail the vertical line test.
  • Converting from General Form: Parabola equations given in general form (e.g., $Ax^2 + Bx + Cy + D = 0$ or $Ay^2 + By + Cx + D = 0$) can be converted to vertex form by completing the square for the squared variable.
  • Reflective Property of a Parabola: Any ray parallel to the axis of symmetry reflects off the parabolic surface and passes through the focus. Conversely, any ray emanating from the focus reflects parallel to the axis. This property is crucial for applications like satellite dishes, flashlights, and telescopes.

Important Terms

  • Parabola (Conic Definition): The set of all points in a plane equidistant from a fixed point (focus) and a fixed line (directrix).
  • Focus: The fixed point used in the geometric definition of a parabola. It always lies on the axis of symmetry, inside the parabola.
  • Directrix: The fixed line used in the geometric definition of a parabola. It is perpendicular to the axis of symmetry and lies outside the parabola.
  • Vertex: The turning point of the parabola, located exactly midway between the focus and the directrix.
  • Axis of Symmetry: The line passing through the vertex and the focus, which divides the parabola into two symmetrical halves.
  • Vertical Parabola: A parabola whose axis of symmetry is vertical, opening upward or downward.
  • Horizontal Parabola: A parabola whose axis of symmetry is horizontal, opening to the left or right.
  • General Form (of a Parabola): A second-degree equation with only one squared variable, such as $Ax^2 + Cx + Dy + Ey + F = 0$ (if $D \neq 0$) or $Ay^2 + By + Cx + D = 0$ (if $C \neq 0$).

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Parabolas

While we've previously encountered parabolas as graphs of quadratic functions, this section introduces them as one of the fundamental "conic sections"—shapes formed by intersecting a plane with a double cone. The key to understanding parabolas from this geometric perspective is their definition based on a focus (a special point) and a directrix (a special line). This definition explains why parabolas are so useful in real-world applications like optics and communication.

Vertical Parabolas

When the axis of symmetry is vertical, the parabola opens either upward or downward.

  • Vertex Form Equation: $y = a(x - h)^2 + k$
  • Vertex: The point $(h, k)$.
  • Opening Direction:
    • If $a > 0$, the parabola opens upward.
    • If $a < 0$, the parabola opens downward.
  • Focus: Located at $(h, k + \frac{1}{4a})$. The focus is always inside the curve.
  • Directrix: The horizontal line $y = k - \frac{1}{4a}$. The directrix is always outside the curve.

Example: For $y = 2(x - 1)^2 + 3$:

  • Here, $a = 2$, $h = 1$, $k = 3$.
  • Vertex: $(1, 3)$.
  • Focus: $(1, 3 + \frac{1}{4(2)}) = (1, 3 + \frac{1}{8}) = (1, \frac{25}{8})$.
  • Directrix: $y = 3 - \frac{1}{8} = \frac{23}{8}$. Since $a = 2 > 0$, the parabola opens upward.

Horizontal Parabolas

When the axis of symmetry is horizontal, the parabola opens either to the left or to the right.

  • Vertex Form Equation: $x = a(y - k)^2 + h$
  • Vertex: The point $(h, k)$.
  • Opening Direction:
    • If $a > 0$, the parabola opens right.
    • If $a < 0$, the parabola opens left.
  • Focus: Located at $(h + \frac{1}{4a}, k)$. The focus is always inside the curve.
  • Directrix: The vertical line $x = h - \frac{1}{4a}$. The directrix is always outside the curve.
  • Important Note: A horizontal parabola is not a function of $x$. Its graph fails the vertical line test. To graph it by plotting points, you might solve for $y$ to get two separate branches (e.g., $y = k \pm \sqrt{\frac{1}{a}(x-h)}$).

Example: For $x = -\frac{1}{2}(y + 3)^2 + 4$:

  • Here, $a = -\frac{1}{2}$, $h = 4$, $k = -3$.
  • Vertex: $(4, -3)$.
  • Focus: $(4 + \frac{1}{4(-\frac{1}{2})}, -3) = (4 - \frac{1}{2}, -3) = (\frac{7}{2}, -3)$.
  • Directrix: $x = 4 - (-\frac{1}{2}) = 4 + \frac{1}{2} = \frac{9}{2}$. Since $a = -\frac{1}{2} < 0$, the parabola opens to the left.

Converting from General Form

Parabolas can also be given in a "general form" equation, which is a second-degree equation where only one variable is squared. To identify the vertex, focus, and directrix, you must convert this general form into the vertex form by completing the square.

Steps for Completing the Square:

  1. Group the terms involving the squared variable.
  2. Factor out the coefficient of the squared term (if it's not 1).
  3. Take half of the coefficient of the linear term (the variable to the power of 1), square it, and add and subtract it inside the parentheses (or balance on the other side of the equation).
  4. Rewrite the perfect square trinomial as a squared binomial.
  5. Simplify the remaining constants.

Example (Vertical Parabola): Convert $y = x^2 - 8x + 11$ to vertex form. $y = (x^2 - 8x) + 11$ To complete the square for $x^2 - 8x$, take half of $-8$ (which is $-4$), and square it (which is $16$). $y = (x^2 - 8x + 16) + 11 - 16$ $y = (x - 4)^2 - 5$ Now it's in vertex form: $a = 1, h = 4, k = -5$.

  • Vertex: $(4, -5)$.
  • Focus: $(4, -5 + \frac{1}{4(1)}) = (4, -5 + \frac{1}{4}) = (4, -\frac{19}{4})$.
  • Directrix: $y = -5 - \frac{1}{4} = -\frac{21}{4}$.

Example (Horizontal Parabola): Convert $x = y^2 + 6y + 5$ to vertex form. $x = (y^2 + 6y) + 5$ To complete the square for $y^2 + 6y$, take half of $6$ (which is $3$), and square it (which is $9$). $x = (y^2 + 6y + 9) + 5 - 9$ $x = (y + 3)^2 - 4$ Now it's in vertex form: $a = 1, h = -4, k = -3$.

  • Vertex: $(-4, -3)$.
  • Focus: $(-4 + \frac{1}{4(1)}, -3) = (-4 + \frac{1}{4}, -3) = (-\frac{15}{4}, -3)$.
  • Directrix: $x = -4 - \frac{1}{4} = -\frac{17}{4}$.

Application: Parabolic Reflectors

The unique reflective property of parabolas makes them incredibly useful:

  • Rays Parallel to Axis: Any incoming ray that is parallel to the parabola's axis of symmetry will reflect off the parabolic surface and pass directly through the focus.
  • Rays from Focus: Conversely, any light or sound originating from the focus will reflect off the parabolic surface and travel outward in a beam parallel to the axis of symmetry.

This property is why:

  • Satellite dishes are parabolic: Incoming parallel radio waves (from satellites) converge at the receiver placed at the focus.
  • Flashlights and car headlights use parabolic reflectors: A light bulb placed at the focus creates a powerful, parallel beam of light.
  • Telescopes and solar collectors also utilize this property to collect and concentrate light or heat at a single point.

Example: Satellite Dish Design A satellite dish has a diameter of 6 ft and a depth of 1.5 ft. To find where the receiver (focus) should be placed, we can model the parabola.

  1. Place the vertex at the origin $(0,0)$ and let it open upward, so the equation is $y = ax^2$.
  2. The diameter is 6 ft, so the rim is 3 ft from the center along the x-axis. The depth is 1.5 ft, so a point on the rim is $(3, 1.5)$.
  3. Substitute this point into the equation: $1.5 = a(3)^2 \Rightarrow 1.5 = 9a \Rightarrow a = \frac{1.5}{9} = \frac{3/2}{9} = \frac{3}{18} = \frac{1}{6}$.
  4. The equation is $y = \frac{1}{6}x^2$.
  5. For a vertical parabola with vertex at $(0,0)$, the focus is at $(0, \frac{1}{4a})$.
  6. Focus: $(0, \frac{1}{4(1/6)}) = (0, \frac{1}{4/6}) = (0, \frac{6}{4}) = (0, \frac{3}{2})$. The receiver should be placed 1.5 ft (or $\frac{3}{2}$ ft) above the vertex, at the center of the dish.

Review Questions

  1. What is the fundamental geometric definition of a parabola, and how does it relate to its real-world applications in devices like satellite dishes or flashlights?
  2. Compare and contrast the vertex form equations for vertical and horizontal parabolas. How can you tell if a parabola opens left, right, up, or down by looking at its equation?
  3. Explain the process of converting a parabola's equation from general form to vertex form using the completing the square method. Why is the vertex form often more useful than the general form?
  4. Describe the reflective property of parabolas in detail. Provide at least two distinct examples of how this property is utilized in technology or nature.
  5. Given a parabola in vertex form, explain the steps to find its focus and directrix. Illustrate with an example for a vertical parabola like $y = -0.5(x+3)^2 + 2$ and a horizontal parabola like $x = 4(y-1)^2 - 5$.
