Logarithms: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces logarithms as the inverse of exponential functions. It defines logarithms, explains how to convert between logarithmic and exponential forms, and highlights special bases like common (base 10) and natural (base $e$) logarithms. Crucially, the section covers the fundamental properties of logarithms, the change-of-base formula for calculation, and how to determine the domain of logarithmic functions.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of a Logarithm: A logarithm answers the question, "What exponent on base $b$ gives $x$?" If $b^y = x$, then $y = \log_b(x)$. Logarithmic functions are the inverse of exponential functions.
  • Conditions for Logarithms: For $\log_b(x)$ to be defined, the base $b$ must be positive ($b > 0$) and not equal to 1 ($b \ne 1$). The argument $x$ must be strictly positive ($x > 0$).
  • Converting Forms: The ability to convert an equation between its logarithmic form ($y = \log_b(x)$) and its exponential form ($b^y = x$) is fundamental to understanding and solving logarithmic problems.
  • Common Logarithm: This is a logarithm with base 10, denoted as $\log(x)$. It's used widely in science and engineering.
  • Natural Logarithm: This is a logarithm with base $e$ (Euler's number, approximately 2.71828), denoted as $\ln(x)$. It's particularly important in calculus and natural growth/decay models.
  • Properties of Logarithms: These rules allow for the manipulation, simplification, expansion, and condensation of logarithmic expressions. They include the Product Rule, Quotient Rule, Power Rule, Inverse Properties, and special rules for the logarithm of 1 and the logarithm of the base.
  • Change-of-Base Formula: This formula allows you to evaluate logarithms with any base using a calculator's common or natural logarithm keys. It states $\log_b(x) = \frac{\log_a(x)}{\log_a(b)}$, where $a$ can be any valid base (typically 10 or $e$).
  • Domain of a Logarithmic Function: Because the argument of a logarithm must be strictly positive, the domain of a logarithmic function $f(x) = \log_b(g(x))$ is found by setting the argument $g(x) > 0$ and solving for $x$.

Important Terms

  • Logarithm: The inverse operation to exponentiation. It finds the exponent to which a base must be raised to produce a given number.
  • Base (of a logarithm): The number that is raised to a power in an exponential function, or the subscript in a logarithm (e.g., $b$ in $\log_b(x)$).
  • Argument (of a logarithm): The number or expression inside the logarithm (e.g., $x$ in $\log_b(x)$). This must always be positive.
  • Common Logarithm: A logarithm with base 10, typically written as $\log(x)$ without an explicit base.
  • Natural Logarithm: A logarithm with base $e$ (Euler's number), typically written as $\ln(x)$.
  • Euler's Number ($e$): An irrational and transcendental mathematical constant, approximately 2.71828, which is the base of the natural logarithm.
  • Product Rule: $\log_b(MN) = \log_b M + \log_b N$ (The logarithm of a product is the sum of the logarithms).
  • Quotient Rule: $\log_b\left(\frac{M}{N}\right) = \log_b M - \log_b N$ (The logarithm of a quotient is the difference of the logarithms).
  • Power Rule: $\log_b(M^p) = p \log_b M$ (The logarithm of a number raised to an exponent is the exponent times the logarithm of the number).
  • Inverse Properties: $\log_b(b^x) = x$ and $b^{\log_b x} = x$. These demonstrate the inverse relationship between exponential and logarithmic functions.
  • Change-of-Base Formula: A formula used to convert a logarithm from one base to another, often to evaluate it using a calculator: $\log_b(x) = \frac{\log_a(x)}{\log_a(b)}$.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Logarithms

Logarithms are essentially a different way to express exponential relationships. Since exponential functions like $f(x) = b^x$ (where $b > 0$ and $b \ne 1$) are one-to-one, they have inverse functions. This inverse is called a logarithm.

The formal definition states: For $b > 0$, $b \ne 1$, and $x > 0$, the logarithm base $b$ of $x$ is defined by: $\log_b(x) = y \iff b^y = x$.

This means that the expression $\log_b(x)$ asks: "To what power must we raise the base $b$ to get the value $x$?" The answer is $y$.

Example: Converting Between Logarithmic and Exponential Form

  • If we have $\log_2(8) = 3$, this is equivalent to $2^3 = 8$.
  • If we have $5^2 = 25$, this is equivalent to $\log_5(25) = 2$. Practice converting back and forth until it becomes second nature, as this is key to solving many logarithmic problems.

Common and Natural Logarithms

Two specific bases are used so frequently they have their own special notation:

  • Common Logarithm: This uses base 10. $\log(x) = \log_{10}(x)$. When you see $\log(x)$ without a base written, assume it's base 10.
  • Natural Logarithm: This uses Euler's number, $e \approx 2.71828$, as its base. $\ln(x) = \log_e(x)$. The natural logarithm is fundamental in calculus and various scientific applications.

Example: Evaluating Common and Natural Logs without a Calculator To evaluate these, think about the definition: what exponent gives the argument?

  • $\log(100)$: What exponent on 10 gives 100? $10^2 = 100$, so $\log(100) = 2$.
  • $\log(1/10)$: What exponent on 10 gives $1/10$? $10^{-1} = 1/10$, so $\log(1/10) = -1$.
  • $\ln(e^5)$: What exponent on $e$ gives $e^5$? $e^5 = e^5$, so $\ln(e^5) = 5$.
  • $\ln(1)$: What exponent on $e$ gives 1? Any base to the power of 0 is 1 ($e^0 = 1$), so $\ln(1) = 0$.

Properties of Logarithms

These properties are incredibly powerful tools for manipulating logarithmic expressions:

  • Product Rule: $\log_b(MN) = \log_b M + \log_b N$ (Multiplication inside the log becomes addition outside).
  • Quotient Rule: $\log_b\left(\frac{M}{N}\right) = \log_b M - \log_b N$ (Division inside the log becomes subtraction outside).
  • Power Rule: $\log_b(M^p) = p \log_b M$ (An exponent inside the log can be moved to the front as a multiplier).
  • Inverse Properties:
    • $\log_b(b^x) = x$ (A logarithm "undoes" an exponential with the same base).
    • $b^{\log_b x} = x$ (An exponential "undoes" a logarithm with the same base).
  • Log of 1: $\log_b 1 = 0$ (Any base raised to the power of 0 is 1).
  • Log of Base: $\log_b b = 1$ (Any base raised to the power of 1 is itself).

Example: Expanding and Condensing Logarithms These examples demonstrate how the properties are used:

  • Expand: $\log_3(9x^4/y)$
    1. Use Quotient Rule: $\log_3(9x^4) - \log_3 y$
    2. Use Product Rule: $\log_3 9 + \log_3 x^4 - \log_3 y$
    3. Simplify $\log_3 9$ ($3^2=9$): $2 + \log_3 x^4 - \log_3 y$
    4. Use Power Rule: $2 + 4\log_3 x - \log_3 y$
  • Condense: $3\ln x + \ln y - 2\ln z$
    1. Use Power Rule: $\ln x^3 + \ln y - \ln z^2$
    2. Use Product Rule (on addition): $\ln (x^3 y) - \ln z^2$
    3. Use Quotient Rule (on subtraction): $\ln\left(\frac{x^3 y}{z^2}\right)$

Change-of-Base Formula

Most calculators only have keys for common ($\log$) and natural ($\ln$) logarithms. To evaluate a logarithm with any other base, we use the change-of-base formula: $\log_b(x) = \frac{\log_a(x)}{\log_a(b)}$

You can choose any convenient base $a$, typically 10 or $e$. So, the formula is often seen as: $\log_b(x) = \frac{\log(x)}{\log(b)}$ (using common log) or $\log_b(x) = \frac{\ln(x)}{\ln(b)}$ (using natural log)

Example: Using the Change-of-Base Formula Evaluate $\log_7(200)$ using common logarithms. $\log_7(200) = \frac{\log(200)}{\log(7)}$ Using a calculator: $\log(200) \approx 2.3010$ and $\log(7) \approx 0.8451$. So, $\log_7(200) \approx \frac{2.3010}{0.8451} \approx 2.7237$. To check this, calculate $7^{2.7237}$, which should be approximately 200.

Domain of a Logarithmic Expression

A crucial restriction for logarithms is that the argument must always be strictly positive. This means for $\log_b(g(x))$, we must have $g(x) > 0$.

To find the domain of a logarithmic function, set its argument greater than zero and solve the inequality.

Example: Finding the Domain of a Logarithmic Function

  • Function: $f(x) = \ln(x - 4)$ The argument is $(x - 4)$. Set it greater than zero: $x - 4 > 0 \Rightarrow x > 4$. Domain: $(4, \infty)$.
  • Function: $g(x) = \log(9 - x^2)$ The argument is $(9 - x^2)$. Set it greater than zero: $9 - x^2 > 0$ Factor the expression: $(3 - x)(3 + x) > 0$. Use a sign chart or test points to determine where this product is positive. The critical points are $x=-3$ and $x=3$. For $x < -3$, e.g., $x=-4$: $(3 - (-4))(3 + (-4)) = (7)(-1) = -7 < 0$. For $-3 < x < 3$, e.g., $x=0$: $(3 - 0)(3 + 0) = (3)(3) = 9 > 0$. For $x > 3$, e.g., $x=4$: $(3 - 4)(3 + 4) = (-1)(7) = -7 < 0$. The product is positive when $-3 < x < 3$. Domain: $(-3, 3)$.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the relationship between exponential functions and logarithmic functions. How does understanding this relationship help you convert between exponential and logarithmic forms? Provide an example.
  2. Without using a calculator, evaluate $\log_5(125)$, $\log(0.01)$, and $\ln(e)$. Explain the reasoning for each answer based on the definition of a logarithm.
  3. Describe the purpose of the Product, Quotient, and Power Rules for logarithms. Give an example of how you would use each rule (a) to expand a single logarithm into multiple terms, and (b) to condense multiple logarithmic terms into a single logarithm.
  4. Why is the change-of-base formula necessary? If you wanted to evaluate $\log_8(100)$ using your calculator, how would you set up the expression using (a) common logarithms and (b) natural logarithms?
  5. What restriction applies to the argument of any logarithmic function? Explain why this restriction exists. Find the domain of the function $h(x) = \log_2(x^2 - 5x + 6)$ and justify your steps.
