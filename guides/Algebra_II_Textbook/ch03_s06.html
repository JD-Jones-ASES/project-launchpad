Quadratic Functions and Vertex Form Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces quadratic functions, whose graphs are parabolas. It details two primary forms of quadratic functions: standard form and vertex form, highlighting how each form reveals key features of the parabola, such as its vertex, axis of symmetry, and direction of opening. The guide also covers methods for converting between these forms, calculating the vertex, and systematically graphing parabolas.

Key Concepts

  • Quadratic Function: A polynomial function of degree two, written as (f(x) = ax^2+bx+c) (standard form) or (f(x) = a(x-h)^2+k) (vertex form), where (a \neq 0). Its graph is always a parabola.
  • Parabola: The U-shaped graph of a quadratic function.
  • Vertex: The turning point of a parabola, which represents either the maximum or minimum value of the function. In vertex form, it's ((h,k)).
  • Axis of Symmetry: A vertical line that divides the parabola into two mirror-image halves. It passes through the vertex. For vertex form, it's (x=h). For standard form, it's (x = -b/(2a)).
  • Direction of Opening: Determined by the sign of (a). If (a>0), the parabola opens upward (vertex is a minimum). If (a<0), it opens downward (vertex is a maximum).
  • Standard Form ((f(x) = ax^2+bx+c)): Useful for finding the y-intercept and applying the vertex shortcut formula.
  • Vertex Form ((f(x) = a(x-h)^2+k)): Directly reveals the vertex ((h,k)) and the axis of symmetry (x=h).
  • Converting Forms (Standard to Vertex): Achieved by completing the square. If (a \neq 1), factor (a) from the (x^2) and (x) terms first.
  • Vertex Shortcut Formula: For a quadratic function in standard form (f(x)=ax^2+bx+c), the (x)-coordinate of the vertex is given by (x = -b/(2a)). The (y)-coordinate is then (f(-b/(2a))).
  • Intercepts:
    • Y-intercept: The point where the parabola crosses the y-axis, found by calculating (f(0)).
    • X-intercepts (Zeros): The points where the parabola crosses the x-axis, found by solving (f(x)=0). There can be 0, 1, or 2 real x-intercepts, determined by the discriminant ((b^2-4ac)).
  • Steepness/Width: The absolute value of (a) affects the width of the parabola. If (|a|>1), the parabola is narrower than (y=x^2); if (|a|<1), it is wider.

Important Terms

  • Quadratic Function: A function of the form (f(x) = ax^2+bx+c) where (a \neq 0).
  • Parabola: The characteristic U-shaped graph of a quadratic function.
  • Vertex: The point on the parabola where it changes direction; either the lowest (minimum) or highest (maximum) point.
  • Axis of Symmetry: A vertical line that passes through the vertex and divides the parabola into two symmetrical halves.
  • Standard Form of a Quadratic Function: (f(x) = ax^2+bx+c), where (a, b, c) are constants and (a \neq 0).
  • Vertex Form of a Quadratic Function: (f(x) = a(x-h)^2+k), where ((h,k)) is the vertex.
  • x-intercepts (Zeros): The points where the graph of a function crosses or touches the x-axis; values of (x) for which (f(x)=0).
  • y-intercept: The point where the graph of a function crosses the y-axis; found by evaluating (f(0)).
  • Completing the Square: An algebraic technique used to rewrite a quadratic expression from standard form into vertex form.
  • Discriminant: The expression (b^2-4ac) from the quadratic formula, which indicates the number of real solutions (and thus x-intercepts) for a quadratic equation.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Quadratic Functions

Quadratic functions are polynomial functions of degree two. Their graphs are parabolas, which are fundamental shapes in mathematics and science. Understanding these graphs involves identifying key features: the vertex, axis of symmetry, and intercepts.

Forms of Quadratic Functions

There are two primary forms for expressing quadratic functions, each with distinct advantages for identifying graph features:

  1. Standard Form: (f(x) = ax^2+bx+c, \text{ where } a \neq 0).

    • The sign of (a) determines the parabola's direction:
      • If (a>0), the parabola opens upward, and the vertex is a minimum point.
      • If (a<0), the parabola opens downward, and the vertex is a maximum point.
    • The constant term (c) is the y-intercept, as (f(0) = a(0)^2 + b(0) + c = c).

  2. Vertex Form: (f(x) = a(x-h)^2+k).

    • This form directly gives the vertex of the parabola as the point ((h,k)).
    • The axis of symmetry is the vertical line (x=h).
    • The sign of (a) still determines the opening direction and whether the vertex is a minimum or maximum.

Symmetry of a Parabola

A parabola is perfectly symmetric about its axis of symmetry. This means that for any point ((h+d, y)) on the graph, there is a corresponding "mirror" point ((h-d, y)) equidistant from the axis of symmetry. This property is very helpful for graphing, as you can plot a point and its symmetric counterpart.

Converting Between Forms

1. Standard Form to Vertex Form (Completing the Square)

This method is used to transform (f(x) = ax^2+bx+c) into (f(x) = a(x-h)^2+k).

  • Case 1: When (a=1) (Monic Quadratic)

    • Take (f(x)=x^2-6x+5).
    • Focus on the (x^2-6x) terms. Take half of the coefficient of (x) ((-6/2 = -3)) and square it (((-3)^2 = 9)).
    • Add and subtract this value inside the function: (f(x) = (x^2-6x+9) - 9 + 5).
    • Factor the perfect square trinomial: (f(x) = (x-3)^2 - 4).
    • The vertex is ((3,-4)), and the axis of symmetry is (x=3).

  • Case 2: When (a \neq 1)

    • Take (f(x)=2x^2+8x-3).
    • Factor out (a) (which is 2) from only the first two terms: (f(x) = 2(x^2+4x) - 3).
    • Complete the square inside the parentheses: half of (4) is (2), (2^2=4).
    • Add and subtract this value inside the parentheses: (f(x) = 2(x^2+4x+4-4) - 3).
    • Distribute the (a) back to the subtracted term: (f(x) = 2[(x+2)^2 - 4] - 3).
    • Simplify: (f(x) = 2(x+2)^2 - 8 - 3 = 2(x+2)^2 - 11).
    • The vertex is ((-2,-11)), and the axis of symmetry is (x=-2).

2. Vertex Shortcut Formula (from Standard Form)

For (f(x)=ax^2+bx+c), the (x)-coordinate of the vertex can be directly calculated: (x_v = -b/(2a)). Once (x_v) is found, substitute it back into the original function to find the (y)-coordinate: (y_v = f(x_v)).

  • Example: For (f(x)=-3x^2+12x-5), (a=-3, b=12).
    • (x_v = -12/(2(-3)) = -12/(-6) = 2).
    • (y_v = f(2) = -3(2)^2 + 12(2) - 5 = -12 + 24 - 5 = 7).
    • The vertex is ((2,7)). Since (a<0), this is a maximum point.

Graphing Quadratic Functions

To sketch a comprehensive graph of a parabola, identify its key features:

  1. Vertex: The turning point ((h,k)).
    • Use vertex form if given, or the shortcut formula (x = -b/(2a)) then (f(x)) if in standard form.
  2. Axis of Symmetry: The vertical line (x=h) (or (x=-b/(2a))).
  3. Direction of Opening: Upward if (a>0), downward if (a<0).
  4. Y-intercept: Substitute (x=0) into the function to find (f(0)). This gives the point ((0, f(0))).
  5. X-intercepts (Zeros): Set (f(x)=0) and solve for (x).
    • These are the real solutions to the quadratic equation (ax^2+bx+c=0).
    • The discriminant ((b^2-4ac)) tells how many x-intercepts exist:
      • If (b^2-4ac > 0), there are two distinct real x-intercepts.
      • If (b^2-4ac = 0), there is exactly one real x-intercept (the vertex is on the x-axis).
      • If (b^2-4ac < 0), there are no real x-intercepts (the parabola does not cross the x-axis).
  6. Steepness: The value of (|a|) indicates how wide or narrow the parabola is compared to (y=x^2).
    • (|a|>1) makes it narrower.
    • (|a|<1) makes it wider.

Example: Finding the Equation from Vertex and a Point

If a parabola has vertex ((2,-3)) and passes through ((0,5)):

  • Start with vertex form: (f(x) = a(x-h)^2+k), so (f(x) = a(x-2)^2-3).
  • Substitute the given point ((0,5)) to solve for (a): (5 = a(0-2)^2-3) (5 = a(-2)^2-3) (5 = 4a-3) (8 = 4a \Rightarrow a=2).
  • The function is (f(x)=2(x-2)^2-3).

Example: Full Graph Analysis

For (f(x)=-x^2+4x+5):

  1. Vertex: (a=-1, b=4). (x_v = -4/(2(-1)) = 2). (y_v = f(2) = -(2)^2+4(2)+5 = -4+8+5 = 9). Vertex: ((2,9)). Axis of symmetry: (x=2). Since (a<0), it opens downward (maximum).
  2. Y-intercept: (f(0) = -0^2+4(0)+5 = 5). Point: ((0,5)).
  3. X-intercepts: Set (f(x)=0 \Rightarrow -x^2+4x+5=0). Multiply by -1: (x^2-4x-5=0). Factor: ((x-5)(x+1)=0). Solutions: (x=5) and (x=-1). Points: ((5,0)) and ((-1,0)).
  4. Symmetry Check: The x-intercepts (x=-1) and (x=5) are symmetric about (x=2) (their midpoint is ((-1+5)/2 = 2)). The y-intercept ((0,5)) has a symmetric point at ((4,5)) (since 0 is 2 units left of axis (x=2), 4 is 2 units right).

Common Pitfalls

Be careful with the signs in vertex form (f(x)=a(x-h)^2+k). The vertex is ((h,k)), not ((-h,k)). For example, in (f(x)=3(x-5)^2+2), (h=5), so the vertex is ((5,2)).
