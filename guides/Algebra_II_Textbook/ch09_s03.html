Ellipses: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces ellipses as a fundamental conic section, defining them as the set of all points where the sum of distances to two fixed points (foci) is constant. It covers the standard form of an ellipse equation, explaining its key features like the center, axes, vertices, foci, and eccentricity. The text also details how to extract these features from an equation, convert general quadratic equations into standard form, and applies these concepts to real-world phenomena like planetary orbits.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of an Ellipse: An ellipse is the set of all points in a plane for which the sum of the distances from two fixed points (called the foci) is constant. This property allows for a simple way to draw an ellipse using two thumbtacks and a taut string.
  • Geometric Origin and Applications: Ellipses are formed when a plane slices a cone at an angle that is not parallel to the base or a side. They are crucial for modeling planetary orbits (Kepler's laws), and are utilized in various designs such as "whispering galleries" and medical lithotripsy machines due to their reflective properties.
  • Center of an Ellipse: The midpoint of the segment connecting the two foci. This point serves as the reference for all other measurements.
  • Standard Form of an Ellipse Equation:
    • For a horizontal major axis: $\frac{(x-h)^2}{a^2} + \frac{(y-k)^2}{b^2} = 1$
    • For a vertical major axis: $\frac{(x-h)^2}{b^2} + \frac{(y-k)^2}{a^2} = 1$
    • In both forms, $(h,k)$ is the center, $a > b > 0$, and $a^2$ is always the larger denominator. The major axis orientation is determined by whether $a^2$ is under the $x$-term (horizontal) or $y$-term (vertical).
  • Key Measurements and Relationships:
    • Semi-major axis ($a$): Half the length of the major axis. Distance from the center to a vertex.
    • Semi-minor axis ($b$): Half the length of the minor axis. Distance from the center to a co-vertex.
    • Focal distance ($c$): Distance from the center to a focus.
    • Relationship: These three measurements are related by the equation $c^2 = a^2 - b^2$, which can also be written as $a^2 = b^2 + c^2$. This relationship visually forms a right triangle.
    • Vertices: The endpoints of the major axis, located at a distance of $a$ from the center along the major axis.
    • Co-vertices: The endpoints of the minor axis, located at a distance of $b$ from the center along the minor axis.
    • Foci: The two fixed points that define the ellipse, located at a distance of $c$ from the center along the major axis, always inside the ellipse.
  • Eccentricity ($e$): A measure of how "stretched out" or circular an ellipse is, defined as $e = \frac{c}{a}$.
    • For an ellipse, $0 < e < 1$.
    • As $e \to 0$, the ellipse becomes more circular (foci move towards the center, $c \to 0$, so $a \approx b$). A circle is the limiting case where $e=0$.
    • As $e \to 1$, the ellipse becomes more elongated (foci move towards the vertices, $c \to a$).
  • Converting General Form: Quadratic equations in general form ($Ax^2 + Cy^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$) can be converted to standard form by grouping terms, factoring out leading coefficients, and completing the square for both $x$ and $y$.

Important Terms

  • Ellipse: The set of all points in a plane for which the sum of the distances from two fixed points (the foci) is constant.
  • Foci (singular: Focus): The two fixed points that define an ellipse.
  • Center: The midpoint of the segment connecting the foci of an ellipse.
  • Semi-major axis ($a$): The distance from the center to a vertex of the ellipse. Half the length of the major axis.
  • Semi-minor axis ($b$): The distance from the center to a co-vertex of the ellipse. Half the length of the minor axis.
  • Major Axis: The longer axis of the ellipse, passing through the foci and vertices. Its length is $2a$.
  • Minor Axis: The shorter axis of the ellipse, perpendicular to the major axis and passing through the center and co-vertices. Its length is $2b$.
  • Vertices: The endpoints of the major axis.
  • Co-vertices: The endpoints of the minor axis.
  • Eccentricity ($e$): A value that describes the shape of an ellipse, calculated as $e = \frac{c}{a}$. It indicates how much the ellipse deviates from a perfect circle.
  • Perihelion: The point in an object's elliptical orbit where it is closest to the Sun (or other central body). For the Sun, this distance is $a-c$.
  • Aphelion: The point in an object's elliptical orbit where it is farthest from the Sun (or other central body). For the Sun, this distance is $a+c$.

Detailed Notes

Definition and Basic Properties

An ellipse is fundamentally defined by its foci. Imagine two thumbtacks (the foci) and a string tied between them. If you trace a curve with a pencil, keeping the string taut, you're drawing an ellipse. The length of the string represents the constant sum of distances. This elegant geometric property gives rise to many practical applications. For instance, in "whispering galleries," the elliptical shape allows sound waves generated at one focus to reflect off the walls and converge perfectly at the other focus, making whispers audible across large distances. Lithotripsy machines use this property to focus shock waves on kidney stones without harming surrounding tissue.

Standard Form of the Equation

The standard form provides a concise way to represent an ellipse and easily identify its key characteristics. An ellipse centered at $(h,k)$ has one of two standard forms:

  1. Horizontal Major Axis: $\frac{(x-h)^2}{a^2} + \frac{(y-k)^2}{b^2} = 1$
    • In this case, $a^2$ is the larger denominator and is under the $x$-term.
    • Vertices are at $(h \pm a, k)$.
    • Co-vertices are at $(h, k \pm b)$.
    • Foci are at $(h \pm c, k)$.
  2. Vertical Major Axis: $\frac{(x-h)^2}{b^2} + \frac{(y-k)^2}{a^2} = 1$
    • Here, $a^2$ is the larger denominator and is under the $y$-term.
    • Vertices are at $(h, k \pm a)$.
    • Co-vertices are at $(h \pm b, k)$.
    • Foci are at $(h, k \pm c)$.

Key Relationship: Regardless of the orientation, $a$ is always greater than $b$ ($a > b > 0$), and the relationship between $a$, $b$, and $c$ is given by $c^2 = a^2 - b^2$. This can be visualized as a right triangle where $a$ is the hypotenuse, and $b$ and $c$ are the legs. The foci are always located on the major axis, inside the ellipse.

Example 1: Identifying Parts of an Ellipse (Center at Origin)

For the ellipse $\frac{x^2}{25} + \frac{y^2}{9} = 1$:

  • Center: $(0,0)$
  • Comparing denominators, $25 > 9$, so $a^2=25 \Rightarrow a=5$ and $b^2=9 \Rightarrow b=3$. Since $a^2$ is under the $x^2$ term, the major axis is horizontal.
  • Focal distance: $c^2 = a^2 - b^2 = 25 - 9 = 16 \Rightarrow c=4$.
  • Vertices: $(0 \pm a, 0) = (\pm 5, 0)$.
  • Co-vertices: $(0, 0 \pm b) = (0, \pm 3)$.
  • Foci: $(0 \pm c, 0) = (\pm 4, 0)$.
  • Eccentricity: $e = \frac{c}{a} = \frac{4}{5} = 0.8$.

Example 2: Identifying Parts of an Ellipse (Shifted Center)

For the ellipse $\frac{(x+1)^2}{16} + \frac{(y-2)^2}{7} = 1$:

  • Center: $(-1, 2)$.
  • Comparing denominators, $16 > 7$, so $a^2=16 \Rightarrow a=4$ and $b^2=7 \Rightarrow b=\sqrt{7}$. Since $a^2$ is under the $(x+1)^2$ term, the major axis is horizontal.
  • Focal distance: $c^2 = a^2 - b^2 = 16 - 7 = 9 \Rightarrow c=3$.
  • Vertices: $(h \pm a, k) = (-1 \pm 4, 2)$, which gives $(3, 2)$ and $(-5, 2)$.
  • Co-vertices: $(h, k \pm b) = (-1, 2 \pm \sqrt{7})$.
  • Foci: $(h \pm c, k) = (-1 \pm 3, 2)$, which gives $(2, 2)$ and $(-4, 2)$.
  • Eccentricity: $e = \frac{c}{a} = \frac{3}{4} = 0.75$.

Completing the Square to Find Standard Form

When an ellipse equation is given in general form, such as $4x^2 + 9y^2 - 24x + 36y + 36 = 0$, you must complete the square to transform it into standard form.

Steps:

  1. Group terms: Group $x$-terms and $y$-terms together, and move the constant term to the right side of the equation. $4x^2 - 24x + 9y^2 + 36y = -36$
  2. Factor out coefficients: Factor out the coefficients of the squared terms ($A$ from $x$-terms, $C$ from $y$-terms). This ensures that the terms inside the parentheses are in the form $x^2 + Bx$ or $y^2 + Dy$. $4(x^2 - 6x) + 9(y^2 + 4y) = -36$
  3. Complete the square: For each grouped variable, take half of the coefficient of the linear term and square it. Add this value inside the parentheses. Remember to multiply this added value by the factored-out coefficient before adding it to the right side of the equation to maintain balance.
    • For $x$-group: Half of $-6$ is $-3$, $(-3)^2 = 9$. Add $9$ inside the parenthesis. Since $4$ was factored out, you're effectively adding $4 \times 9 = 36$ to the left side, so add $36$ to the right side.
    • For $y$-group: Half of $4$ is $2$, $(2)^2 = 4$. Add $4$ inside the parenthesis. Since $9$ was factored out, you're effectively adding $9 \times 4 = 36$ to the left side, so add $36$ to the right side. $4(x^2 - 6x + 9) + 9(y^2 + 4y + 4) = -36 + 36 + 36$
  4. Rewrite as squared terms: Express the parenthetical expressions as squared binomials. $4(x-3)^2 + 9(y+2)^2 = 36$
  5. Divide to make right side 1: Divide every term by the constant on the right side to make it equal to 1. $\frac{4(x-3)^2}{36} + \frac{9(y+2)^2}{36} = \frac{36}{36}$ $\frac{(x-3)^2}{9} + \frac{(y+2)^2}{4} = 1$
  6. Identify features: From this standard form, you can identify the center $(3, -2)$, $a^2=9 \Rightarrow a=3$, $b^2=4 \Rightarrow b=2$. Since $a^2$ is under the $x$-term, the major axis is horizontal. Then $c^2 = a^2 - b^2 = 9 - 4 = 5 \Rightarrow c=\sqrt{5}$.
    • Vertices: $(3 \pm 3, -2) = (6, -2)$ and $(0, -2)$.
    • Foci: $(3 \pm \sqrt{5}, -2)$.

Eccentricity and Planetary Orbits

Eccentricity is a key characteristic of an ellipse. A perfect circle has an eccentricity of $e=0$ (since $c=0$, meaning the foci coincide with the center, and $a=b$). As $e$ increases towards $1$, the ellipse becomes more elongated, meaning the foci move closer to the vertices.

Planetary orbits are elliptical, with the Sun located at one focus. Earth's orbit, for example, has a semi-major axis $a \approx 1.496 \times 10^8$ km and a small eccentricity $e \approx 0.0167$, making it nearly circular.

  • Calculating $c$: $c = ea = 0.0167 \times 1.496 \times 10^8 \approx 2.50 \times 10^6$ km.
  • Calculating $b$: $b = \sqrt{a^2 - c^2} = \sqrt{(1.496 \times 10^8)^2 - (2.50 \times 10^6)^2} \approx 1.495 \times 10^8$ km. Notice $b$ is very close to $a$, indicating a near-circular orbit.
  • Perihelion (closest point to Sun): Occurs when Earth is at a vertex closest to the focus (Sun). Distance is $a - c \approx 1.496 \times 10^8 - 0.0250 \times 10^8 = 1.471 \times 10^8$ km.
  • Aphelion (farthest point from Sun): Occurs when Earth is at a vertex farthest from the focus (Sun). Distance is $a + c \approx 1.496 \times 10^8 + 0.0250 \times 10^8 = 1.521 \times 10^8$ km.

Review Questions

  1. What is the fundamental definition of an ellipse, and how does the "string and thumbtacks" method visually demonstrate this definition?
  2. Explain how the standard form of an ellipse equation, $\frac{(x-h)^2}{A} + \frac{(y-k)^2}{B} = 1$, allows you to determine the center, orientation of the major axis, and lengths of the semi-major and semi-minor axes.
  3. Describe the concept of eccentricity for an ellipse. What does an eccentricity close to 0 imply about the shape of the ellipse, and what does an eccentricity close to 1 imply? Provide an example of each.
  4. Outline the step-by-step process for converting a quadratic equation in general form ($Ax^2 + Cy^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$) into the standard form of an ellipse, emphasizing the role of completing the square.
  5. Discuss two distinct real-world applications of ellipses (e.g., planetary orbits, architectural designs, medical technology). For each application, explain which geometric property of the ellipse is being utilized and why it is beneficial.
