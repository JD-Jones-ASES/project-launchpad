Chapter 7, Section 7: Growth, Decay, and Applications Study Guide

Overview

This section explores how exponential functions model phenomena that grow or decay at a rate proportional to their current size, such as population changes, radioactive decay, and continuously compounded interest. It introduces the continuous growth/decay model, demonstrates how to determine unknown rates or times using logarithms, and explains the utility of logarithmic scales like pH and the Richter scale for managing quantities that span vast ranges of magnitude.

Key Concepts

  • Exponential Growth/Decay Model ($A(t) = A_0 e^{kt}$): This fundamental formula describes quantities that change continuously at a rate proportional to their current amount. $A_0$ is the initial quantity, $t$ is time, and $k$ is the continuous growth/decay rate.
  • Growth Constant ($k$): The sign of $k$ determines the behavior:
    • If $k > 0$, the quantity experiences exponential growth, increasing over time.
    • If $k < 0$, the quantity experiences exponential decay, decreasing over time.
  • Determining the Rate Constant ($k$): In practical applications, $k$ is often unknown. It must be calculated using at least two known data points (e.g., initial amount and an amount at a specific time, or two amounts at two different times). Logarithms are crucial for solving for $k$.
  • Half-Life: A concept specific to exponential decay, it is the time required for a quantity to reduce to half its initial amount.
  • Doubling Time: An analogous concept for exponential growth, it is the time required for a quantity to double its initial amount.
  • Logarithmic Scales: Scales like pH and the Richter scale use logarithms to represent quantities that vary over many orders of magnitude. They compress very large or very small numbers into a more manageable range, making comparisons easier.

Important Terms

  • Exponential Growth: A pattern of increase in which the rate of growth is proportional to the current size of the quantity, leading to increasingly rapid growth over time.
  • Exponential Decay: A pattern of decrease in which the rate of decay is proportional to the current size of the quantity, leading to a gradual reduction toward zero.
  • Continuous Growth/Decay Model: The mathematical model $A(t) = A_0 e^{kt}$, representing growth or decay that occurs constantly, rather than at discrete intervals.
  • Growth Constant ($k$): The constant that determines the rate of continuous exponential growth or decay. A positive $k$ indicates growth, a negative $k$ indicates decay.
  • Initial Amount ($A_0$): The starting value of a quantity at time $t=0$.
  • Half-Life: The amount of time it takes for a quantity undergoing exponential decay to decrease to half of its original value.
  • Doubling Time: The amount of time it takes for a quantity undergoing exponential growth to double its original value.
  • pH Scale: A logarithmic scale used to specify the acidity or basicity of an aqueous solution. It is defined as $pH = -\log_{10}[H^+]$, where $[H^+]$ is the hydrogen ion concentration.
  • Richter Scale: A logarithmic scale used to quantify the magnitude of an earthquake. It is defined as $M = \log_{10}(I/I_0)$, where $I$ is the intensity of the earthquake and $I_0$ is a reference intensity.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Exponential Models

Exponential functions are powerful tools for modeling situations where quantities change at a rate directly proportional to their current amount. This means that the larger the quantity, the faster it grows (or decays). Examples include population dynamics, radioactive decay, compound interest, and light absorption. Visually, growth curves ascend rapidly without bound, while decay curves approach a horizontal asymptote (typically zero).

The Continuous Growth and Decay Model: $A(t) = A_0 e^{kt}$

This model is central to understanding continuous change:

  • $A(t)$: The amount of the quantity at time $t$.
  • $A_0$: The initial amount of the quantity (at $t=0$).
  • $e$: Euler's number, the base of the natural logarithm (approximately 2.71828).
  • $k$: The continuous growth or decay rate constant.
    • If $k > 0$, the quantity is growing exponentially.
    • If $k < 0$, the quantity is decaying exponentially.
  • $t$: Time.

Solving Problems with the Model: The first crucial step in most applications is to determine the value of the constant $k$. This usually requires at least one data point beyond the initial amount.

  • Example: Predicting Population Growth If $A_0$, $k$, and $t$ are known, you can directly calculate $A(t)$. For instance, if a city's population starts at $A_0 = 250,000$ and grows continuously at $k = 2.3%$ (or $0.023$) per year, after $t = 20$ years: $A(20) = 250,000 , e^{(0.023)(20)} \approx 396,025$.

  • Example: Finding $k$, Predicting Future Values, or Finding Time More often, $k$ is unknown and needs to be derived from data.

    1. Finding $k$ from two data points: If you know $A_0$ and $A(t)$ at a specific $t$, substitute these into the formula. Then, isolate $e^{kt}$, take the natural logarithm (ln) of both sides, and solve for $k$.
      • Light Absorption Example: $A_0 = 1200$, $A(3) = 600$. $600 = 1200 , e^{3k} \Rightarrow 0.5 = e^{3k} \Rightarrow \ln(0.5) = 3k \Rightarrow k = \ln(0.5)/3 \approx -0.2310$. The negative $k$ confirms decay.
    2. Predicting a future value: Once $k$ is known, plug in any future time $t$ to find $A(t)$.
      • Light Absorption Example: $A(8) = 1200 , e^{(-0.2310)(8)} \approx 189$ lm/m$^2$.
    3. Finding the time ($t$) to reach a specific value: Set $A(t)$ to the target value, use the known $A_0$ and $k$, and solve for $t$ using the natural logarithm.
      • Light Absorption Example: Find $t$ when $A(t) = 75$. $75 = 1200 , e^{-0.2310t} \Rightarrow 75/1200 = e^{-0.2310t} \Rightarrow 1/16 = e^{-0.2310t} \Rightarrow \ln(1/16) = -0.2310t \Rightarrow t = \ln(1/16)/(-0.2310) \approx 12$ meters.

Half-Life and Doubling Time

These are special cases of solving for $t$ in the continuous growth/decay model.

  • Half-Life: For decay, set $A(t) = A_0/2$.
    • Carbon-14 Example: If half-life is 5,730 years: $A_0/2 = A_0 , e^{k(5730)} \Rightarrow 0.5 = e^{5730k} \Rightarrow \ln(0.5) = 5730k \Rightarrow k = \ln(0.5)/5730 \approx -0.0001210$ yr$^{-1}$.
    • To find the age of a fossil with 30% of original C-14: $0.30 A_0 = A_0 , e^{kt} \Rightarrow 0.30 = e^{kt} \Rightarrow \ln(0.30) = kt \Rightarrow t = \ln(0.30)/k \approx 9,950$ years.
  • Doubling Time: For growth, set $A(t) = 2A_0$.
    • Investment Example: For 5% continuous interest ($k=0.05$): $2A_0 = A_0 , e^{0.05T} \Rightarrow 2 = e^{0.05T} \Rightarrow \ln(2) = 0.05T \Rightarrow T = \ln(2)/0.05 \approx 13.86$ years. Notice that in both half-life and doubling time calculations, the initial amount $A_0$ cancels out, meaning these times depend only on the rate $k$.

Logarithmic Scales

Logarithmic scales are used to compress a wide range of values into a more manageable, linear scale. This is especially useful when dealing with quantities that vary by many orders of magnitude.

  • pH (Acidity) Scale:

    • Measures the hydrogen ion concentration, $[H^+]$, in a solution.
    • Formula: $pH = -\log_{10}[H^+]$. (A lower pH indicates higher acidity.)
    • pH < 7: Acidic
    • pH = 7: Neutral
    • pH > 7: Basic (Alkaline)
    • Example: If $[H^+] = 1.6 \times 10^{-3}$ mol/L: $pH = -\log_{10}(1.6 \times 10^{-3}) = -(\log_{10}(1.6) + \log_{10}(10^{-3})) = -(0.2041 - 3) \approx 2.80$ (acidic).
    • To find $[H^+]$ from pH: $[H^+] = 10^{-pH}$.
      • Example: If pH $= 9.2$: $[H^+] = 10^{-9.2} \approx 6.31 \times 10^{-10}$ mol/L.

  • Richter Scale (Earthquake Magnitude):

    • Measures the magnitude of an earthquake based on its intensity $I$ relative to a reference intensity $I_0$.
    • Formula: $M = \log_{10}(I/I_0)$.
    • Since it's a base-10 logarithm, an increase of 1 on the Richter scale means the earthquake is 10 times more intense. An increase of 2 means it's $10^2 = 100$ times more intense, and so on.
    • Example: Comparing a 7.2 quake ($M_1$) to a 5.0 quake ($M_2$). $M_1 - M_2 = \log_{10}(I_1/I_0) - \log_{10}(I_2/I_0) = \log_{10}((I_1/I_0) / (I_2/I_0)) = \log_{10}(I_1/I_2)$. $7.2 - 5.0 = 2.2 = \log_{10}(I_1/I_2) \Rightarrow I_1/I_2 = 10^{2.2} \approx 158$. The 7.2 magnitude quake is approximately 158 times more intense than the 5.0 magnitude quake.

Review Questions

  1. Why are exponential functions particularly well-suited for modeling natural phenomena like population growth, radioactive decay, and continuously compounded interest? What common characteristic do these phenomena share?
  2. Explain the significance of the constant $k$ in the continuous growth/decay model, $A(t) = A_0 e^{kt}$. How does its value determine whether a quantity is growing or decaying, and what does its magnitude imply?
  3. Describe the general procedure for calculating the half-life of a decaying substance or the doubling time of a growing quantity. Why is it that the initial amount, $A_0$, does not influence the calculated half-life or doubling time?
  4. What is the main advantage of using a logarithmic scale (like pH or Richter) compared to a linear scale when measuring certain quantities? Provide an example from the text where a small difference on a logarithmic scale corresponds to a significantly larger difference in the actual measured quantity.
  5. Suppose you are tracking the spread of a chemical spill. You know the initial area affected ($A_0$) and the area after 48 hours ($A(48)$). Outline the steps you would take, using the continuous growth/decay model, to predict the area affected after one week.
