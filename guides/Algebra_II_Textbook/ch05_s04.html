Polynomial Division: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces two primary methods for dividing polynomials: polynomial long division and synthetic division. Polynomial long division is a general method applicable to any polynomial divisor, while synthetic division offers a streamlined shortcut specifically for division by linear factors of the form $(x - c)$. The section also presents the Division Algorithm for Polynomials, which defines the relationship between the dividend, divisor, quotient, and remainder, and introduces the Remainder Theorem, a powerful shortcut for evaluating polynomials.

Key Concepts

  • Polynomial Long Division: A systematic method for dividing any polynomial $P(x)$ by another polynomial $D(x)$, analogous to long division with integers. It provides a quotient $Q(x)$ and a remainder $R(x)$.
  • The Division Algorithm for Polynomials: A fundamental property stating that for any polynomials $P(x)$ (dividend) and $D(x)$ (divisor, $D(x) \neq 0$), there exist unique polynomials $Q(x)$ (quotient) and $R(x)$ (remainder) such that $P(x) = D(x) Q(x) + R(x)$. The remainder $R(x)$ is either $0$ or its degree is less than the degree of $D(x)$.
  • Placeholder Terms (Zero Coefficients): When setting up polynomial long division or synthetic division, it is crucial to include terms with a coefficient of zero for any missing powers of $x$ in the dividend (e.g., $0x^2$ if the $x^2$ term is absent) to maintain proper alignment during the division process.
  • Synthetic Division: A compact and efficient algorithm specifically designed for dividing a polynomial $P(x)$ by a linear factor of the form $(x - c)$. It uses only the coefficients of the polynomials, significantly simplifying the calculations.
  • The Remainder Theorem: A direct consequence of the Division Algorithm, stating that if a polynomial $P(x)$ is divided by a linear factor $(x - c)$, the remainder of this division is equal to $P(c)$. This provides a quick way to evaluate a polynomial at a specific value.

Important Terms

  • Dividend ($P(x)$): The polynomial being divided.
  • Divisor ($D(x)$): The polynomial by which another polynomial is divided.
  • Quotient ($Q(x)$): The result of polynomial division, excluding the remainder.
  • Remainder ($R(x)$): The polynomial left over after division, which has a degree less than that of the divisor (or is zero).
  • Linear Factor: A polynomial of the form $(x - c)$, where $c$ is a constant. Synthetic division is applicable only when the divisor is a linear factor.
  • Standard Form: Writing a polynomial with terms arranged in descending order of their exponents. It's essential to put polynomials in standard form before dividing.
  • Root/Zero: If the remainder of a polynomial division $P(x) \div (x-c)$ is $0$, then $(x-c)$ is a factor of $P(x)$, and $c$ is a root (or zero) of the polynomial $P(x)$.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Polynomial Division

Just like integers, polynomials can be divided to yield a quotient and a remainder. This operation is fundamental in algebra for factoring polynomials, finding roots, and simplifying rational expressions. This section covers two main techniques: polynomial long division (general) and synthetic division (specific to linear divisors).

Polynomial Long Division

Polynomial long division is a method similar to the long division you perform with numbers. It's a general technique that works for dividing any polynomial $P(x)$ by any non-zero polynomial $D(x)$.

The Division Algorithm for Polynomials: This algorithm formalizes the division process: If $P(x)$ and $D(x)$ are polynomials with $D(x) \neq 0$, then there exist unique polynomials $Q(x)$ (quotient) and $R(x)$ (remainder) such that: $P(x) = D(x) Q(x) + R(x)$ where either $R(x) = 0$ or the degree of $R(x)$ is less than the degree of $D(x)$. Equivalently, this can be written as: $\frac{P(x)}{D(x)} = Q(x) + \frac{R(x)}{D(x)}$.

Procedure for Polynomial Long Division:

  1. Setup: Write the dividend $P(x)$ and the divisor $D(x)$ in standard form. If any powers of $x$ are missing in $P(x)$, insert them with a coefficient of zero (e.g., $0x^2$) as placeholders. This is crucial for aligning terms correctly.
  2. Divide Leading Terms: Divide the leading term of the dividend by the leading term of the divisor. This gives the first term of the quotient.
  3. Multiply: Multiply the term you just found in the quotient by the entire divisor.
  4. Subtract: Subtract this product from the current dividend. Remember to distribute the negative sign to all terms being subtracted.
  5. Bring Down: Bring down the next term from the original dividend.
  6. Repeat: Continue steps 2-5 until the degree of the new dividend (the remainder) is less than the degree of the divisor.

Interpreting the Result:

  • The polynomial at the top is the quotient $Q(x)$.
  • The final polynomial at the bottom is the remainder $R(x)$.
  • If the remainder $R(x)$ is $0$, it means that the divisor $D(x)$ is a factor of the dividend $P(x)$.

Example Insights:

  • Example 1 ($2x^3 + 3x^2 - 11x - 6$ by $x + 3$): This example shows a case where the remainder is $0$, indicating that $(x+3)$ is a factor of the dividend.
  • Example 2 ($x^3 - 2x + 5$ by $x - 2$): This example highlights the importance of placeholders. The term $0x^2$ was inserted to ensure correct alignment during subtraction. The remainder here is a constant, $9$.

Synthetic Division

Synthetic division is a shortcut method specifically used for dividing a polynomial $P(x)$ by a linear divisor of the form $(x - c)$. It's much faster than long division because it only involves the coefficients.

Procedure for Synthetic Division (Dividing by $x-c$):

  1. Set up: Write the coefficients of the dividend $P(x)$ in a row. Remember to include zeros for any missing powers of $x$. Write the value $c$ (from the divisor $x-c$) to the left.
  2. Bring Down: Bring the first coefficient down below the line.
  3. Multiply: Multiply the number you just brought down by $c$. Write the product under the next coefficient.
  4. Add: Add the numbers in that column.
  5. Repeat: Continue multiplying the result of the addition by $c$ and adding to the next column, until all coefficients have been processed.
  6. Interpret Result:
    • The last number in the bottom row is the remainder $R$.
    • The other numbers in the bottom row are the coefficients of the quotient $Q(x)$. The degree of $Q(x)$ will be one less than the degree of the original polynomial $P(x)$.

Example Insight ($3x^4 - 5x^3 + 2x - 8$ by $(x - 2)$): The example demonstrates how to set up the coefficients (including $0$ for the missing $x^2$ term) and perform the multiply-and-add steps. The final row gives the coefficients of $Q(x)$ ($3x^3 + x^2 + 2x + 6$) and the remainder ($4$).

The Remainder Theorem

The Remainder Theorem provides a powerful connection between polynomial division and polynomial evaluation.

Statement: If a polynomial $P(x)$ is divided by a linear factor $(x - c)$, then the remainder of that division is equal to $P(c)$. In other words, $P(x) = (x-c)Q(x) + P(c)$.

Significance: This theorem means you can find the value of $P(c)$ simply by performing synthetic division of $P(x)$ by $(x-c)$ and looking at the remainder. This is often much quicker than direct substitution, especially for higher-degree polynomials.

Example Insight ($P(x) = x^4 - 3x^2 + 5x - 7$ divided by $(x - 3)$): The example shows how synthetic division with $c=3$ quickly yields a remainder of $62$. This value is then verified by direct substitution of $P(3)$, confirming that $P(3)=62$.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the key differences between polynomial long division and synthetic division. When is each method appropriate to use?
  2. State the Division Algorithm for Polynomials. What information does it provide about the relationship between the dividend, divisor, quotient, and remainder?
  3. Why is it crucial to use placeholder terms (e.g., $0x^2$) when performing both polynomial long division and synthetic division if the dividend is missing certain powers of $x$? Provide an example of how this could lead to an error if omitted.
  4. If, after performing polynomial division, the remainder is $0$, what does this tell you about the relationship between the divisor and the dividend? How does this relate to finding factors of a polynomial?
  5. Describe the Remainder Theorem in your own words. How can it be used as a shortcut, and when might you choose to use it instead of direct substitution to evaluate a polynomial $P(x)$ at a specific value $c$?
