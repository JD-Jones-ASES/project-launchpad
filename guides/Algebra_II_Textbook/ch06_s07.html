Study Guide: Rational Inequalities

Overview

This section introduces rational inequalities, which involve comparing rational expressions. The core method for solving them is similar to polynomial inequalities, relying on identifying critical values and using a sign chart. A key distinction is that values making the denominator zero (vertical asymptotes) are always excluded from the solution set, as the expression is undefined at these points.

Key Concepts

  • Rational Inequality Definition: An inequality that contains at least one rational expression, comparing it to a value (often zero) or another expression.
  • Strategy for Solving Rational Inequalities: A systematic five-step process:
    1. Rearrange: Manipulate the inequality to have zero on one side ($f(x)/g(x) \text{ } 0$).
    2. Simplify: Combine expressions into a single rational function, $p(x)/q(x)$.
    3. Find Critical Values: Determine the $x$-values where the numerator $p(x)$ equals zero and where the denominator $q(x)$ equals zero. These are the only points where the sign of the rational expression can change.
    4. Create a Sign Chart: Use the critical values to divide the number line into intervals. Test a value from each interval in the simplified rational expression to determine its sign (positive or negative).
    5. Write the Solution: Identify the intervals that satisfy the original inequality.
  • Critical Values from Denominator: Zeros of the denominator are always excluded from the solution set because the rational expression is undefined at these values. They represent vertical asymptotes.
  • Critical Values from Numerator: Zeros of the numerator can be included in the solution set if the inequality involves "or equal to" ($\leq$ or $\geq$).
  • Sign Chart Logic: The sign of a rational expression will be consistent within any interval defined by consecutive critical values.
  • Caution Against Multiplying by Denominator: Never multiply both sides of a rational inequality by a variable denominator without first knowing its sign (i.e., splitting into cases where the denominator is positive and negative). The sign-chart method avoids this complex splitting.

Important Terms

  • Rational Inequality: An inequality that contains one or more rational expressions (fractions where the numerator and denominator are polynomials).
  • Critical Values: The specific $x$-values that cause a rational expression to equal zero (from the numerator) or to be undefined (from the denominator). These values define the boundaries of the intervals on a sign chart.
  • Sign Chart: A number line diagram, divided into intervals by critical values, used to determine where an expression is positive, negative, or zero.

Detailed Notes

The Strategy for Rational Inequalities

Solving rational inequalities involves a systematic approach, mirroring polynomial inequalities but with an added consideration for values where the expression is undefined.

  1. Rearrange to get 0 on one side:

    • If you have $f(x) < g(x)$, subtract $g(x)$ from both sides to get $f(x) - g(x) < 0$.
    • This ensures you are comparing a single rational expression to zero, making sign analysis straightforward.

  2. Simplify into a single fraction:

    • Combine the terms on the non-zero side into one rational expression, $p(x)/q(x)$. This usually involves finding a common denominator.
    • Factor the numerator $p(x)$ and the denominator $q(x)$ if possible.

  3. Find Critical Values:

    • Zeros of the numerator: Set $p(x) = 0$ and solve for $x$. These values are where the expression might change sign from positive to negative or vice versa, and they can be part of the solution if the inequality includes "or equal to."
    • Zeros of the denominator: Set $q(x) = 0$ and solve for $x$. These values are where the expression is undefined (vertical asymptotes). They are always excluded from the solution set.

  4. Create a Sign Chart:

    • Draw a number line and mark all the critical values on it. These values divide the number line into several intervals.
    • Choose a test value from within each interval.
    • Substitute each test value into the simplified rational expression $p(x)/q(x)$ to determine its sign (+ or -) in that interval. It's often easier to determine the sign of each factor (numerator and denominator terms) separately and then combine them.

    Example Sign Chart Structure:

    Intervals:    (-inf, cv1)  (cv1, cv2)  (cv2, inf)
Factor 1:         -             +           +
Factor 2:         -             -           +
Expression:       +             -           +

  5. Write the Solution:

    • Identify the intervals where the rational expression has the desired sign (e.g., positive for $>0$, negative for $<0$).
    • For inequalities involving $\leq$ or $\geq$:
      • Include critical values from the numerator by using square brackets [ or ].
      • Always exclude critical values from the denominator by using parentheses ( or ).
    • For strict inequalities ($<$ or $>$):
      • Exclude all critical values using parentheses ( or ).
    • Use union symbol $\cup$ to combine multiple intervals.

Caution: Never Multiply by the Denominator

A common mistake is to multiply both sides of a rational inequality by the denominator to clear fractions. This is highly risky because the sign of the denominator can change depending on the value of $x$.

  • If the denominator is positive, the inequality direction remains the same.
  • If the denominator is negative, the inequality direction must be reversed.
  • Since the denominator's sign usually varies, you would have to split the problem into multiple cases, which is complicated. The sign-chart approach avoids this issue entirely by focusing on where the entire expression is positive or negative.

Examples

Example 1: Basic Rational Inequality Solve $ \frac{x-1}{x+2} > 0 $.

  • Steps 1 & 2: Already in the form $f(x) > 0$ and simplified.
  • Step 3 (Critical Values):
    • Numerator zero: $x-1=0 \implies x=1$
    • Denominator zero: $x+2=0 \implies x=-2$
  • Step 4 (Sign Chart):
    Intervals:  (-inf, -2)   (-2, 1)    (1, inf)
x-1:            -          -          +
x+2:            -          +          +
(x-1)/(x+2):    +          -          +
  • Step 5 (Solution): We want $> 0$ (positive). The expression is positive on $(- \infty, -2)$ and $(1, \infty)$. Neither critical value is included because it's a strict inequality and $x=-2$ makes the expression undefined. Solution: $ (- \infty, -2) \cup (1, \infty) $

Example 2: Rational Inequality Requiring Rearrangement Solve $ \frac{2}{x+1} \leq x $.

  • Step 1: Subtract $x$ to get $0$ on one side: $ \frac{2}{x+1} - x \leq 0 $.
  • Step 2: Combine into a single fraction: $ \frac{2}{x+1} - \frac{x(x+1)}{x+1} \leq 0 $ $ \frac{2 - (x^2 + x)}{x+1} \leq 0 $ $ \frac{-x^2 - x + 2}{x+1} \leq 0 $ Factor the numerator: $ \frac{-(x^2 + x - 2)}{x+1} \leq 0 \implies \frac{-(x+2)(x-1)}{x+1} \leq 0 $.
  • Step 3 (Critical Values):
    • Numerator zeros: $-(x+2)(x-1)=0 \implies x=-2, x=1$.
    • Denominator zero: $x+1=0 \implies x=-1$.
  • Step 4 (Sign Chart):
    Intervals:  (-inf, -2)  (-2, -1)   (-1, 1)    (1, inf)
-(x+2):         +          -          -          -
(x-1):          -          -          -          +
(x+1):          -          -          +          +
Expression:     -          +          -          +
  • Step 5 (Solution): We want $\leq 0$ (negative or zero). The expression is negative on $(- \infty, -2)$ and $(-1, 1)$. It is zero at $x=-2$ and $x=1$ (include these). It is undefined at $x=-1$ (exclude). Solution: $ (- \infty, -2] \cup (-1, 1] $

Example 3: Rational Inequality with Quadratic Factors Solve $ \frac{x^2-4}{x+1} \leq 0 $.

  • Steps 1 & 2: Already $f(x) \leq 0$; factor numerator: $ \frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x+1} \leq 0 $.
  • Step 3 (Critical Values):
    • Numerator zeros: $x-2=0 \implies x=2$; $x+2=0 \implies x=-2$.
    • Denominator zero: $x+1=0 \implies x=-1$.
  • Step 4 (Sign Chart):
    Intervals:  (-inf, -2)  (-2, -1)   (-1, 2)    (2, inf)
(x-2):          -          -          -          +
(x+2):          -          +          +          +
(x+1):          -          -          +          +
Expression:     -          +          -          +
  • Step 5 (Solution): We want $\leq 0$ (negative or zero). Negative on $(- \infty, -2)$ and $(-1, 2)$. Zero at $x=-2$ and $x=2$ (include). Undefined at $x=-1$ (exclude). Solution: $ (- \infty, -2] \cup (-1, 2] $

Example 4: Comparing Two Rational Expressions Solve $ \frac{1}{x} > \frac{1}{x+2} $.

  • Step 1: Subtract $ \frac{1}{x+2} $ from both sides: $ \frac{1}{x} - \frac{1}{x+2} > 0 $.
  • Step 2: Combine over common denominator $x(x+2)$: $ \frac{(x+2) - x}{x(x+2)} > 0 \implies \frac{2}{x(x+2)} > 0 $.
  • Step 3 (Critical Values):
    • Numerator is $2$ (never zero).
    • Denominator zeros: $x=0, x+2=0 \implies x=-2$.
  • Step 4 (Sign Chart):
    Intervals:  (-inf, -2)   (-2, 0)    (0, inf)
2:              +          +          +
x:              -          -          +
(x+2):          -          +          +
Expression:     +          -          +
  • Step 5 (Solution): We want $> 0$ (strictly positive). The expression is positive on $(- \infty, -2)$ and $(0, \infty)$. Both $x=-2$ and $x=0$ make the expression undefined, so they are excluded. Solution: $ (- \infty, -2) \cup (0, \infty) $

Checkpoint Answer

Solve $ \frac{x+3}{x-4} \leq 0 $. Answer: $[-3, 4)$.

Chapter Summary Reinforcement

Rational inequalities are a critical part of understanding rational expressions and functions. They leverage the concept of critical values and sign analysis on a number line, ensuring that both zeros of the expression and points of discontinuity (vertical asymptotes) are correctly handled to determine valid solution intervals.
