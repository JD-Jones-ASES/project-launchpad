Chapter 3, Section 1: The Complex Number System Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the complex number system, an extension of the real numbers necessary to solve certain polynomial equations like x^2 + 1 = 0 which have no real solutions. It defines the imaginary unit i as the square root of -1, establishes the standard form of a complex number a + bi, and covers fundamental arithmetic operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division), as well as concepts like powers of i, complex conjugates, and the modulus of a complex number, concluding with the geometric interpretation in the complex plane.

Key Concepts

  • Extension of Real Numbers: The real number system is insufficient to solve all polynomial equations (e.g., x^2 = -1). The complex number system (C) is a larger set that includes all real numbers and provides solutions to such equations.
  • Imaginary Unit (i): Defined as the number whose square is -1, i.e., i^2 = -1 or i = √(-1). For any positive real number b, √(-b) = i√b.
  • Complex Number Form: A complex number z is expressed in the form a + bi, where a and b are real numbers.
    • a is the real part (Re(z)).
    • b is the imaginary part (Im(z)).
    • If b = 0, the number is a real number.
    • If a = 0 and b ≠ 0, the number is a purely imaginary number.
  • Arithmetic Operations:
    • Addition/Subtraction: Combine real parts and imaginary parts separately: (a + bi) ± (c + di) = (a ± c) + (b ± d)i.
    • Multiplication: Use the distributive property (FOIL) and substitute i^2 = -1: (a + bi)(c + di) = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i.
  • Powers of i: The powers of i follow a repeating cycle of four values: i, -1, -i, 1. This pattern i^1 = i, i^2 = -1, i^3 = -i, i^4 = 1 can be used to simplify any integer power of i.
  • Complex Conjugate: For a complex number z = a + bi, its complex conjugate, denoted z̄, is a - bi. The product of a complex number and its conjugate z * z̄ = (a + bi)(a - bi) = a^2 + b^2, which is always a non-negative real number.
  • Division of Complex Numbers: To divide complex numbers, multiply both the numerator and the denominator by the conjugate of the denominator. This process eliminates the imaginary unit from the denominator.
  • Modulus (Absolute Value): The modulus of a complex number z = a + bi, denoted |z|, is √(a^2 + b^2). Geometrically, it represents the distance of the complex number from the origin in the complex plane.
  • Complex Plane: A two-dimensional plane used to represent complex numbers geometrically. The horizontal axis represents the real part, and the vertical axis represents the imaginary part.

Important Terms

  • Imaginary Unit (i): The number defined by i^2 = -1, or i = √(-1).
  • Complex Number: Any number that can be written in the form a + bi, where a and b are real numbers, and i is the imaginary unit. The set of all complex numbers is denoted by C.
  • Real Part (Re(z)): The real number a in the complex number z = a + bi.
  • Imaginary Part (Im(z)): The real number b in the complex number z = a + bi. (Note: the i is not part of the imaginary part itself, which is just the coefficient b).
  • Purely Imaginary Number: A complex number a + bi where a = 0 and b ≠ 0.
  • Complex Conjugate (z̄): For a complex number z = a + bi, its conjugate is a - bi.
  • Modulus (Absolute Value, |z|): For a complex number z = a + bi, its modulus is √(a^2 + b^2). It represents the distance from the origin in the complex plane.
  • Complex Plane: A Cartesian coordinate system used for graphing complex numbers, with the horizontal axis representing the real part and the vertical axis representing the imaginary part.

Detailed Notes

1. Introduction: Need for Complex Numbers

The real number system (R), which includes all rational and irrational numbers, allows us to solve many equations. However, some simple polynomial equations, like x^2 + 1 = 0, have no solutions within R. Rearranging this equation gives x^2 = -1. Since the square of any non-zero real number is positive, there is no real number x that satisfies this. To address this, the complex number system (C) was created as a larger number system where such equations can be solved.

2. The Imaginary Unit i

The foundation of the complex number system is the imaginary unit, denoted by i.

  • Definition: i^2 = -1. Equivalently, i = √(-1).
  • Property for Negative Radicands: For any real number b > 0, we can write √(-b) = i√b.
    • Example: √(-4) = i√4 = 2i; √(-9) = i√9 = 3i.

3. Defining Complex Numbers

  • Standard Form: A complex number is any number of the form a + bi, where a and b are real numbers (a, b ∈ R).

    • Real Part: a is called the real part of z, written Re(z) = a.
    • Imaginary Part: b is called the imaginary part of z, written Im(z) = b.
    • Special Cases:
      • If b = 0, the number a + 0i = a is a real number. This shows that R is a subset of C.
      • If a = 0 and b ≠ 0, the number 0 + bi = bi is a purely imaginary number.

  • Equality of Complex Numbers: Two complex numbers a + bi and c + di are equal if and only if their real parts are equal (a = c) and their imaginary parts are equal (b = d).

4. Arithmetic Operations with Complex Numbers

Complex numbers follow the ordinary rules of algebra, with the key replacement i^2 = -1.

  • Addition and Subtraction: To add or subtract complex numbers, simply combine their real parts and their imaginary parts separately. (a + bi) ± (c + di) = (a ± c) + (b ± d)i

    • Example (Addition): (3 + 5i) + (1 - 2i) = (3 + 1) + (5 - 2)i = 4 + 3i
    • Example (Subtraction): (4 - 7i) - (-2 + 3i) = (4 - 7i) + (2 - 3i) = (4 + 2) + (-7 - 3)i = 6 - 10i

  • Multiplication: To multiply complex numbers, use the distributive law (FOIL method) and substitute i^2 = -1 wherever it appears. (a + bi)(c + di) = ac + adi + bci + bdi^2 = ac + (ad + bc)i + bd(-1) = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i

    • Example: (3 + 2i)(1 - 4i) = 3(1) + 3(-4i) + 2i(1) + 2i(-4i) = 3 - 12i + 2i - 8i^2 = 3 - 10i - 8(-1) = 3 - 10i + 8 = 11 - 10i

5. Powers of i

Repeated multiplication of i generates a cyclical pattern:

  • i^1 = i
  • i^2 = -1
  • i^3 = i^2 * i = -1 * i = -i
  • i^4 = i^2 * i^2 = (-1)(-1) = 1
  • i^5 = i^4 * i = 1 * i = i (The pattern restarts)

The pattern i, -1, -i, 1 repeats every four steps. To find i^n for any positive integer n:

  1. Divide n by 4.
  2. The remainder determines the value:
    • Remainder 1: i^n = i
    • Remainder 2: i^n = -1
    • Remainder 3: i^n = -i
    • Remainder 0 (n is a multiple of 4): i^n = 1
  • Negative powers also follow this cycle (e.g., i^-1 = 1/i = i/i^2 = i/(-1) = -i).

6. Complex Conjugates and Division

  • Complex Conjugate: The complex conjugate of z = a + bi is z̄ = a - bi. It is formed by changing the sign of the imaginary part.

    • Product Property: The product of a complex number and its conjugate is always a non-negative real number: z * z̄ = (a + bi)(a - bi) = a^2 - (bi)^2 = a^2 - b^2i^2 = a^2 - b^2(-1) = a^2 + b^2. This property is crucial for division.

  • Division: To divide complex numbers, we utilize the complex conjugate to "rationalize" the denominator (make it a real number).

    1. Multiply both the numerator and the denominator by the complex conjugate of the denominator.
    2. Simplify the numerator and the denominator, remembering i^2 = -1.
    3. Express the result in the a + bi form.
    • Example: Write (2 + 3i) / (1 - 2i) in the form a + bi. The conjugate of the denominator (1 - 2i) is (1 + 2i). (2 + 3i) / (1 - 2i) = [(2 + 3i) * (1 + 2i)] / [(1 - 2i) * (1 + 2i)] Numerator: (2 + 3i)(1 + 2i) = 2 + 4i + 3i + 6i^2 = 2 + 7i - 6 = -4 + 7i Denominator: (1 - 2i)(1 + 2i) = 1^2 + 2^2 = 1 + 4 = 5 So, (2 + 3i) / (1 - 2i) = (-4 + 7i) / 5 = -4/5 + 7/5 i.

7. Absolute Value (Modulus) of a Complex Number

  • Definition: The modulus (or absolute value) of a complex number z = a + bi is denoted |z| and is calculated as |z| = √(a^2 + b^2).
  • Geometric Interpretation: |z| represents the distance from the origin (0, 0) to the point (a, b) representing z in the complex plane. This is directly derived from the Pythagorean theorem.
    • Notice that |z|^2 = a^2 + b^2 = z * z̄.
  • Example: Find |3 - 4i|. |3 - 4i| = √(3^2 + (-4)^2) = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5. Both 3 - 4i and its conjugate 3 + 4i have the same modulus.

8. The Complex Plane: Geometric Representation

Just as real numbers are represented on a number line, complex numbers are represented on a two-dimensional plane called the complex plane.

  • The horizontal axis is the real axis, representing the real part (a).
  • The vertical axis is the imaginary axis, representing the imaginary part (b).
  • A complex number a + bi corresponds to the point (a, b) in this plane. The modulus |z| is the distance from the origin (0,0) to the point (a,b), confirming its connection to the Pythagorean theorem.

9. Important Note on Radicals

A common error to avoid: √(-4) * √(-9) is NOT √((-4)*(-9)) = √36 = 6. The rule √(a) * √(b) = √(ab) only applies when both a and b are non-negative real numbers. The correct computation: √(-4) * √(-9) = (2i) * (3i) = 6i^2 = 6(-1) = -6. Always convert to the i form before multiplying negative square roots.

Review Questions

  1. Explain why the real number system is considered incomplete for solving all algebraic equations. Provide a specific example of an equation that necessitates the introduction of the complex number system.
  2. Define the imaginary unit i and describe its fundamental property. How does this property affect the outcomes of multiplying complex numbers or simplifying powers of i?
  3. Compare and contrast the arithmetic operations of addition, subtraction, and multiplication for complex numbers with those for binomials in real algebra. What is the key difference that arises due to the presence of i?
  4. What is the purpose of the complex conjugate in the context of complex number division? Illustrate with an example how it helps to express a quotient in the standard a + bi form.
  5. Describe the geometric interpretation of a complex number z = a + bi and its modulus |z| in the complex plane. How does this geometric representation connect to the algebraic definition of the modulus?
