Study Guide: Factoring Expressions

Overview

This section introduces factoring as the inverse operation of multiplication, essential for solving polynomial equations using the Zero Product Property. It outlines a systematic strategy for factoring any polynomial, starting with the Greatest Common Factor (GCF) and then applying different techniques based on the number of terms, including specific methods for trinomials (monic and non-monic) and special product patterns.

Key Concepts

  • Factoring as Reverse Multiplication: Factoring breaks down a polynomial into a product of simpler polynomials, much like division breaks down a number into its prime factors.
  • Importance of Factoring: It transforms polynomial equations into a product form, allowing the use of the Zero Product Property (if (AB=0), then (A=0) or (B=0)) to find solutions.
  • Systematic Factoring Strategy: Always begin by factoring out the Greatest Common Factor (GCF) from all terms. After that, count the remaining terms to determine the next factoring technique.
  • Factoring Trinomials ( (x^2+bx+c) ): For trinomials with a leading coefficient of 1, find two integers (p) and (q) that multiply to (c) and add to (b). The factored form is ((x+p)(x+q)).
  • The AC Method (for (ax^2+bx+c) with (a \neq 1)): This systematic procedure involves finding two numbers that multiply to (ac) and add to (b), then splitting the middle term and factoring by grouping.
  • Special Product Formulas:
    • Difference of Squares: (a^2 - b^2 = (a+b)(a-b))
    • Perfect Square Trinomials: (a^2+2ab+b^2 = (a+b)^2) and (a^2-2ab+b^2 = (a-b)^2)
  • Sum of Squares: Polynomials of the form (a^2+b^2) cannot be factored over the real numbers.
  • Always Check Your Work: Multiply your factors back together to ensure they equal the original polynomial. This catches most errors.

Important Terms

  • Factoring: The process of rewriting a polynomial as a product of simpler polynomials (its factors).
  • Greatest Common Factor (GCF) of a Polynomial: The largest monomial (including its coefficient and variable parts) that divides every term in a polynomial.
  • Zero Product Property: If the product of two or more factors is zero, then at least one of the factors must be zero. This property is fundamental for solving factored polynomial equations.
  • Trinomial: A polynomial with three terms.
  • AC Method: A systematic procedure for factoring trinomials of the form (ax^2+bx+c) where (a \neq 1). It involves finding two numbers whose product is (ac) and whose sum is (b), then splitting the middle term and factoring by grouping.
  • Difference of Squares: A binomial in the form (a^2-b^2), which always factors into ((a+b)(a-b)).
  • Perfect Square Trinomial: A trinomial that results from squaring a binomial, in the form (a^2+2ab+b^2) or (a^2-2ab+b^2). These factor into ((a+b)^2) or ((a-b)^2), respectively.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Factoring

Factoring is a fundamental skill in algebra, serving as the reverse process of multiplication. Just as we can multiply ((x+2)(x+3)) to get (x^2+5x+6), factoring allows us to take (x^2+5x+6) and rewrite it as ((x+2)(x+3)). This skill is crucial because it enables us to solve polynomial equations by applying the Zero Product Property.

The Systematic Factoring Strategy

When approaching any polynomial factoring problem, it's essential to follow a consistent set of steps:

  1. GCF First: Always begin by factoring out the Greatest Common Factor from all terms of the polynomial. This simplifies the remaining expression and often makes subsequent factoring steps easier.
  2. Count the Terms: After extracting the GCF, examine the number of terms in the remaining polynomial:
    • Two terms: Check if it's a difference of squares (a^2-b^2). (Remember, a sum of squares (a^2+b^2) is generally not factorable over real numbers).
    • Three terms:
      • First, check for a perfect square trinomial.
      • If not, try trinomial factoring. If the leading coefficient is 1 ((x^2+bx+c)), look for two numbers that multiply to (c) and add to (b).
      • If the leading coefficient is not 1 ((ax^2+bx+c) where (a \neq 1)), use the AC Method.
    • (Note: For four terms, the method is usually factoring by grouping, which is part of the AC method).
  3. Check Your Work: After factoring, always multiply your factors back together to verify that you obtain the original polynomial. This simple step can catch most factoring errors.

Step 1: Greatest Common Factor (GCF)

The GCF of a polynomial is the largest monomial (coefficient and variable parts) that evenly divides every term in the polynomial.

  • How to find the GCF:
    • Coefficients: Find the greatest common divisor of all the numerical coefficients.
    • Variables: For each variable present in all terms, choose the lowest power of that variable.
  • Factoring out the GCF: Use the distributive property in reverse.
    • Example 1: Factor (6x^3 - 9x^2 + 3x).
      • Coefficients: GCF of 6, 9, 3 is 3.
      • Variables: (x^3), (x^2), (x^1). The lowest power of (x) is (x^1).
      • GCF = (3x).
      • Factor: (3x(2x^2 - 3x + 1)).
      • Check: (3x(2x^2) - 3x(3x) + 3x(1) = 6x^3 - 9x^2 + 3x).
    • Example 2: Factor (12x^4y^2 - 8x^3y^3 + 4x^2y).
      • Coefficients: GCF of 12, 8, 4 is 4.
      • Variables: (x^4, x^3, x^2) (lowest (x^2)); (y^2, y^3, y^1) (lowest (y^1)).
      • GCF = (4x^2y).
      • Factor: (4x^2y(3x^2y - 2xy^2 + 1)).
      • Check: (4x^2y(3x^2y) - 4x^2y(2xy^2) + 4x^2y(1) = 12x^4y^2 - 8x^3y^3 + 4x^2y).

Factoring Trinomials with Leading Coefficient 1 ((x^2+bx+c))

For trinomials where the coefficient of the (x^2) term is 1, we are looking for two integers, (p) and (q), that satisfy two conditions:

  1. Their product is equal to the constant term (c): (pq = c).
  2. Their sum is equal to the coefficient of the middle term (b): (p+q = b). Once found, the trinomial factors as ((x+p)(x+q)).
  • Example: Factor (x^2-5x+6).
    • We need (p+q = -5) and (pq = 6).
    • Pairs of integers whose product is 6:
      • (1, 6) sum = 7
      • (-1, -6) sum = -7
      • (2, 3) sum = 5
      • (-2, -3) sum = -5
    • The pair (-2) and (-3) fits both conditions.
    • Factor: ((x-2)(x-3)).
    • Check: ((x-2)(x-3) = x^2 - 3x - 2x + 6 = x^2 - 5x + 6).

Factoring Trinomials with Leading Coefficient (a \neq 1) (The AC Method)

When the leading coefficient (a) is not 1, the AC method provides a systematic approach, often called "splitting the middle term."

Steps for the AC Method to factor (ax^2+bx+c):

  1. Compute (ac): Multiply the leading coefficient (a) by the constant term (c).
  2. Find two integers (m) and (n): Look for two integers (m) and (n) such that their product (mn = ac) and their sum (m+n = b). If no such integers exist, the trinomial is prime (cannot be factored further over integers).
  3. Rewrite the middle term: Split the middle term (bx) into (mx + nx). The trinomial becomes a four-term polynomial: (ax^2 + mx + nx + c).
  4. Factor by grouping: Group the first two terms and the last two terms. Factor out the GCF from each pair.
  5. Factor out the common binomial: The expressions in parentheses should now be identical. Factor out this common binomial.

  • Example 1: Factor (6x^2+7x-3).

    1. (ac = 6(-3) = -18).
    2. Find (m, n) such that (mn=-18) and (m+n=7). The pair (m=9, n=-2) works.
    3. Rewrite: (6x^2+9x-2x-3).
    4. Group and factor: (3x(2x+3) - 1(2x+3)). (Notice the (-1) factored out to make the binomial match).
    5. Factor out the common binomial: ((3x-1)(2x+3)).
    • Check: ((3x-1)(2x+3) = 6x^2+9x-2x-3 = 6x^2+7x-3).

  • Example 2 (Completing an earlier GCF example): Factor (2x^2-3x+1). (This was the trinomial left after factoring out (3x) from (6x^3-9x^2+3x)).

    1. (ac = 2(1) = 2).
    2. Find (m, n) such that (mn=2) and (m+n=-3). The pair (m=-1, n=-2) works.
    3. Rewrite: (2x^2-x-2x+1).
    4. Group and factor: (x(2x-1) - 1(2x-1)).
    5. Factor out the common binomial: ((x-1)(2x-1)).
    • The complete factoring of (6x^3-9x^2+3x) is (3x(x-1)(2x-1)).

Caution: Always remember to factor out the GCF first! If you don't, you'll work with larger numbers and might miss common factors, leading to an incomplete factorization.

Special Product Formulas

These patterns appear frequently and should be recognized for quicker factoring.

  1. Difference of Squares: (a^2 - b^2 = (a+b)(a-b))

    • This applies only when two perfect squares are being subtracted.
    • Example: Factor (4x^2-25).
      • Recognize (4x^2) as ((2x)^2) and (25) as (5^2).
      • Apply the formula: ((2x+5)(2x-5)).

  2. Perfect Square Trinomials:

    • (a^2+2ab+b^2 = (a+b)^2)
    • (a^2-2ab+b^2 = (a-b)^2)
    • To identify: Check if the first and last terms are perfect squares, and if the middle term is twice the product of the square roots of the first and last terms.
    • Example: Factor (x^2+10x+25).
      • (x^2) is ((x)^2), (25) is ((5)^2).
      • Is the middle term (2(x)(5))? Yes, (10x).
      • Apply the formula: ((x+5)^2).

Important Note: Sum of Squares Unlike the difference of squares, a sum of squares, (a^2+b^2), cannot be factored over the real numbers. For example, (x^2+4) is considered prime over the real numbers.
