Chapter 4, Section 2: Power Functions Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces power functions, which are fundamental polynomial functions of the form $f(x) = x^n$, where $n$ is a positive integer. We explore how the degree $n$ (whether it's even or odd) profoundly affects the function's graph, symmetry, domain, range, and end behavior. Understanding these characteristics is crucial for analyzing more complex functions later on.

Key Concepts

  • Power Function Definition: A function defined as $f(x) = x^n$, where $n$ is a positive integer, called the degree.
  • Even-Degree Power Functions:
    • Symmetry: Even functions, symmetric about the $y$-axis ($f(-x) = f(x)$).
    • Shape: U-shaped, opening upward, with a minimum at the origin $(0,0)$.
    • End Behavior: As $x \to \infty$, $f(x) \to \infty$; as $x \to -\infty$, $f(x) \to \infty$.
    • Effect of Degree: As $n$ increases, the graph becomes flatter near zero and steeper away from zero.
  • Odd-Degree Power Functions:
    • Symmetry: Odd functions, exhibiting $180^\circ$ rotational symmetry about the origin ($f(-x) = -f(x)$).
    • Shape: An S-shape that generally passes through $(-1,-1)$, $(0,0)$, and $(1,1)$.
    • End Behavior: As $x \to \infty$, $f(x) \to \infty$; as $x \to -\infty$, $f(x) \to -\infty$.
  • End Behavior: Describes the trend of the function's output ($f(x)$) as the input ($x$) becomes extremely large positive ($x \to \infty$) or extremely large negative ($x \to -\infty$).
  • Impact of Degree on Output: For $x > 1$, higher powers produce larger outputs ($x^4 > x^2$). For $0 < x < 1$, higher powers produce smaller outputs ($x^4 < x^2$).
  • Transformations: Power functions can be transformed (shifted, stretched, reflected) just like other basic functions. Vertical shifts can alter a function's symmetry.
  • Anchor Points: All power functions $y = x^n$ pass through $(0,0)$ and $(1,1)$. Even-degree functions also pass through $(-1,1)$, while odd-degree functions pass through $(-1,-1)$. These are useful for sketching.

Important Terms

  • Power Function: A function of the form $f(x) = x^n$, where $n$ is a positive integer.
  • Degree: The positive integer exponent $n$ in a power function $f(x) = x^n$.
  • Even Function: A function $f(x)$ where $f(-x) = f(x)$ for all $x$ in its domain. Its graph is symmetric about the $y$-axis.
  • Odd Function: A function $f(x)$ where $f(-x) = -f(x)$ for all $x$ in its domain. Its graph has $180^\circ$ rotational symmetry about the origin.
  • End Behavior: The behavior of the graph of a function as $x$ approaches positive infinity ($x \to \infty$) or negative infinity ($x \to -\infty$).
  • Symmetry about the y-axis: A graph can be folded along the y-axis, and the two halves match exactly. Characteristic of even functions.
  • Rotational Symmetry about the Origin: A graph looks the same after being rotated $180^\circ$ around the origin. Characteristic of odd functions.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Power Functions

A power function is a fundamental algebraic function defined by $f(x) = x^n$, where the exponent $n$ is a positive integer. This integer $n$ is also known as the degree of the power function. The behavior and graph of a power function are primarily determined by whether its degree $n$ is an even or an odd number.

Even-Degree Power Functions

When the degree $n$ is an even positive integer (e.g., $n=2, 4, 6, \dots$), the power function $f(x) = x^n$ exhibits specific characteristics:

  • Definition: $f(x) = x^n$, where $n$ is even.
  • Symmetry: These are even functions because $f(-x) = (-x)^n = x^n = f(x)$ for an even $n$. Their graphs are symmetric about the $y$-axis.
  • Shape: The graphs are U-shaped, similar to a parabola, and always open upward.
  • Vertex: The point $(0,0)$ is a minimum point on the graph, often called the vertex.
  • Domain: The domain for all even-degree power functions is all real numbers, written as $(-\infty, \infty)$.
  • Range: Since the functions open upward from $(0,0)$, the range is $[0, \infty)$.
  • End Behavior:
    • As $x$ increases without bound ($x \to \infty$), $f(x)$ also increases without bound ($f(x) \to \infty$).
    • As $x$ decreases without bound ($x \to -\infty$), $f(x)$ also increases without bound ($f(x) \to \infty$).
  • Effect of Degree $n$: As the even degree $n$ increases (e.g., comparing $x^2$ to $x^4$ to $x^6$):
    • The graph becomes flatter near zero (between -1 and 1).
    • The graph becomes steeper far from zero (for $|x|>1$).
  • Anchor Points: These graphs always pass through $(0,0)$, $(1,1)$, and $(-1,1)$.

Odd-Degree Power Functions

When the degree $n$ is an odd positive integer (e.g., $n=1, 3, 5, \dots$), the power function $f(x) = x^n$ has a different set of characteristics:

  • Definition: $f(x) = x^n$, where $n$ is odd.
  • Symmetry: These are odd functions because $f(-x) = (-x)^n = -x^n = -f(x)$ for an odd $n$. Their graphs have $180^\circ$ rotational symmetry about the origin.
  • Shape: The graphs typically have an S-shape, passing through the origin.
  • Domain: The domain for all odd-degree power functions is all real numbers, $(-\infty, \infty)$.
  • Range: The range is also all real numbers, $(-\infty, \infty)$, as they extend infinitely in both positive and negative $y$ directions.
  • Key Points: These graphs always pass through $(-1, -1)$, $(0, 0)$, and $(1, 1)$.
  • End Behavior:
    • As $x$ increases without bound ($x \to \infty$), $f(x)$ also increases without bound ($f(x) \to \infty$).
    • As $x$ decreases without bound ($x \to -\infty$), $f(x)$ decreases without bound ($f(x) \to -\infty$).
  • Effect of Degree $n$: Similar to even degrees, as the odd degree $n$ increases (e.g., comparing $x^3$ to $x^5$):
    • The graph becomes flatter near zero (between -1 and 1).
    • The graph becomes steeper far from zero (for $|x|>1$).

End Behavior

End behavior describes what happens to the function's output ($f(x)$) as the input ($x$) grows very large in the positive or negative direction.

  • Arrow Notation:
    • $x \to \infty$ means $x$ increases without bound (approaches positive infinity).
    • $x \to -\infty$ means $x$ decreases without bound (approaches negative infinity).
  • Summary for Power Functions ($f(x)=x^n$):
    • Even $n$: As $x \to \infty, f(x) \to \infty$; as $x \to -\infty, f(x) \to \infty$. (Both ends go up)
    • Odd $n$: As $x \to \infty, f(x) \to \infty$; as $x \to -\infty, f(x) \to -\infty$. (One end up, one end down)

Comparing Even and Odd Power Functions (Examples)

Let's consider $f(x)=x^2$ and $h(x)=x^4$ (even functions) versus $g(x)=x^3$ and $p(x)=x^5$ (odd functions).

  • Output Comparison:
    • For $x > 1$ (e.g., $x=2$): Higher powers result in larger outputs ($2^4=16 > 2^2=4$).
    • For $0 < x < 1$ (e.g., $x=0.5$): Higher powers result in smaller outputs ($0.5^4=0.0625 < 0.5^2=0.25$).
  • Symmetry and Vertical Shifts:
    • $f(x) = x^4$ is even (symmetric about $y$-axis).
    • $g(x) = x^5$ is odd (rotational symmetry about origin).
    • $h(x) = x^3 + 1$: A vertical shift of an odd function. $h(-x) = (-x)^3 + 1 = -x^3 + 1$. This is neither $h(x)$ nor $-h(x)$. Therefore, vertical shifts typically break the original even or odd symmetry.

Using Power Functions in Context

Power functions are used to model various real-world relationships. For instance, the volume of a sphere is $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$, which is a cubic (odd-degree) power function of the radius $r$ (ignoring the constant $\frac{4}{3}\pi$).

  • If the input (radius) is doubled, the output (volume) increases by a factor of $2^3 = 8$.
  • In general, for $f(x)=x^n$, if the input $x$ is multiplied by a factor $c$, the output $f(cx) = (cx)^n = c^n x^n = c^n f(x)$ is multiplied by $c^n$.

Transformations of Power Functions

Just like other basic functions, power functions can be transformed:

  1. Horizontal Shifts: $f(x) = (x-c)^n$ shifts the graph $c$ units to the right; $f(x) = (x+c)^n$ shifts it $c$ units to the left.
    • Example: $f(x) = (x - 3)^4$ is $y=x^4$ shifted right $3$ units.
  2. Vertical Shifts: $f(x) = x^n + c$ shifts the graph $c$ units up; $f(x) = x^n - c$ shifts it $c$ units down.
    • Example: $g(x) = -x^3 + 2$ involves an upward shift of $2$ units after reflection.
  3. Reflections:
    • $f(x) = -x^n$ reflects the graph across the $x$-axis.
    • $f(x) = (-x)^n$ reflects the graph across the $y$-axis (but for power functions, this often returns the original function for even $n$ or $-f(x)$ for odd $n$).
    • Example: $g(x) = -x^3 + 2$ involves a reflection over the $x$-axis.
  4. Vertical Stretches/Compressions: $f(x) = a \cdot x^n$
    • If $|a| > 1$, it's a vertical stretch.
    • If $0 < |a| < 1$, it's a vertical compression.
    • Example: $h(x) = 2(x + 1)^3 - 5$ involves a vertical stretch by a factor of $2$.

When describing transformations, apply reflections and stretches/compressions before shifts. The "vertex" or "inflection point" (like $(0,0)$ for $x^n$) of the basic power function will move according to the shifts. For instance, for $y = x^4$, $(0,0)$ is the minimum. For $y=x^3$, $(0,0)$ is an inflection point.

Review Questions

  1. Define a power function, state its general form, and explain what the "degree" of the function refers to.
  2. Compare and contrast the graphs and key properties (symmetry, domain, range, end behavior) of $f(x) = x^6$ and $g(x) = x^7$.
  3. How does an increase in the degree $n$ affect the graph of $f(x) = x^n$ for values of $x$ in the interval $(0,1)$ versus values of $x$ where $|x| > 1$? Provide an example.
  4. Consider the functions $f(x) = x^4$ and $h(x) = x^4 - 2$. Explain how the graph of $h(x)$ is related to $f(x)$ and discuss why $h(x)$ is still an even function, unlike the example $x^3+1$ being neither odd nor even.
  5. Describe the sequence of transformations required to obtain the graph of $k(x) = -3(x + 2)^5 + 1$ from the basic power function $y = x^5$. Identify the location of the transformed inflection point.
