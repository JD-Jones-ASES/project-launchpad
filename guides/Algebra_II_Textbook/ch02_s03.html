Study Guide: Linear Functions

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental ways to represent linear relationships through equations and graphs. You will learn three standard forms for linear equations – point-slope, slope-intercept, and standard form – and understand when to use each. The guide also covers how to write equations for lines given various pieces of information, including points, slopes, and relationships to parallel or perpendicular lines, and how to efficiently graph lines using their intercepts.

Key Concepts

  • Three Forms of Linear Equations: Lines can be expressed in Point-Slope form ($y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$), Slope-Intercept form ($y = mx + b$), or Standard form ($Ax + By = C$), each useful depending on the given information.
  • Writing Equations from Given Information:
    • Point and Slope: Use point-slope form directly.
    • Two Points: First calculate the slope, then use one point with the point-slope form.
    • Parallel Lines: Share the same slope.
    • Perpendicular Lines: Have slopes that are negative reciprocals of each other.
  • Graphing Using Intercepts: An efficient method for graphing linear equations (especially in standard form) by finding the points where the line crosses the x-axis (x-intercept) and y-axis (y-intercept).
  • Linear Functions: Defined as $f(x) = mx + b$, where $m$ is the slope and $b$ is the y-intercept. All linear functions (with $m \neq 0$) are one-to-one.

Important Terms

  • Point-Slope Form: The equation of a line with slope $m$ passing through the point $(x_1, y_1)$ is $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$.
  • Slope-Intercept Form: The equation of a line with slope $m$ and y-intercept $b$ is $y = mx + b$.
  • Standard Form: The standard form of a linear equation is $Ax + By = C$, where $A$, $B$, and $C$ are integers, $A \geq 0$, and the greatest common factor of $A$, $B$, and $C$ is 1.
  • x-intercept: The point where a line crosses the x-axis. Found by setting $y = 0$ in the equation and solving for $x$.
  • y-intercept: The point where a line crosses the y-axis. Found by setting $x = 0$ in the equation and solving for $y$.
  • Parallel Lines: Lines that have the same slope and never intersect.
  • Perpendicular Lines: Lines that intersect at a right angle (90 degrees). Their slopes are negative reciprocals of each other (e.g., if one slope is $m$, the perpendicular slope is $-1/m$).
  • Linear Function: A function that can be written in the form $f(x) = mx + b$, where $m$ and $b$ are constants. Its graph is a straight line.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Linear Equations

Once we can compute slopes, the next step is to use this information to write and graph equations of lines. This section focuses on providing the tools to achieve this effectively.

Forms of Linear Equations

There are three primary forms for writing the equation of a line. Each is useful in different situations:

  1. Point-Slope Form:

    • Formula: $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$
    • Use: This is the most direct form when you know the slope ($m$) and a single point $(x_1, y_1)$ that the line passes through. You simply substitute these values into the formula.

  2. Slope-Intercept Form:

    • Formula: $y = mx + b$
    • Use: Ideal when you know the slope ($m$) and the y-intercept ($b$). The y-intercept is a special point $(0, b)$. This form is essentially a specific application of point-slope form where $(x_1, y_1) = (0, b)$.
    • Conversion: Often, you'll write an equation in point-slope form and then rearrange it into slope-intercept form by isolating $y$.

  3. Standard Form:

    • Formula: $Ax + By = C$
    • Constraints: $A$, $B$, and $C$ must be integers. Additionally, $A$ should be non-negative ($A \geq 0$), and there should be no common factor (other than 1) among $A$, $B$, and $C$ (i.e., $(A, B, C) = 1$).
    • Use: Useful for certain types of problems or when a general form is required. It's also often the easiest form to graph using intercepts.
    • Conversion: To convert from slope-intercept or point-slope form, move the $x$-term to the left side of the equation and ensure all coefficients are integers and meet the other constraints.

Writing Equations of Lines

1. Given a Point and a Slope

  • Process: Substitute the given point $(x_1, y_1)$ and slope $m$ into the point-slope formula.
  • Example (from text): Line through $(-1, 3)$ with slope $m = 2$.
    • (a) Point-slope: $y - 3 = 2(x - (-1)) \Rightarrow y - 3 = 2(x + 1)$
    • (b) Slope-intercept: $y - 3 = 2x + 2 \Rightarrow y = 2x + 5$
    • (c) Standard form: $2x - y = -5$ (or $2x - y = -5$ if the $y$ term is moved to the left, which is $2x - y = -5$).

2. Given Two Points

  • Process:
    1. Find the slope ($m$): Use the slope formula: $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$.
    2. Use Point-Slope Form: Choose either of the given points $(x_1, y_1)$ and the calculated slope $m$ to write the equation in point-slope form.
    3. Simplify: Convert to slope-intercept or standard form as needed.
  • Example (from text): Line through $(2, -1)$ and $(5, 5)$.
    1. Slope: $m = \frac{5 - (-1)}{5 - 2} = \frac{6}{3} = 2$.
    2. Using $(2, -1)$ and $m=2$: $y - (-1) = 2(x - 2) \Rightarrow y + 1 = 2x - 4 \Rightarrow y = 2x - 5$.
    • Important Caution: When distributing the slope $m$ in point-slope form, especially if $m$ is negative, remember to distribute it to both terms inside the parentheses. Forgetting to distribute the negative sign is a common error (e.g., $-3(x-2) = -3x + 6$, not $-3x - 6$).

3. Lines Parallel to a Given Line

  • Concept: Parallel lines have the same slope.
  • Process:
    1. Identify the slope of the given line.
    2. Use this same slope along with the given point to write the new line's equation (typically using point-slope form, then converting).
  • Example (from text): Line parallel to $y = 3x - 7$ through $(0, 4)$.
    • Given slope is $m = 3$. The point $(0, 4)$ is the y-intercept, so $b = 4$.
    • Equation: $y = 3x + 4$.

4. Lines Perpendicular to a Given Line

  • Concept: Perpendicular lines have slopes that are negative reciprocals of each other. If $m_{given}$ is the slope of the given line, the perpendicular slope is $m_{\perp} = -\frac{1}{m_{given}}$.
  • Process:
    1. Find the slope of the given line (often by converting it to slope-intercept form).
    2. Calculate the negative reciprocal of this slope to find $m_{\perp}$.
    3. Use $m_{\perp}$ and the given point to write the new line's equation.
  • Example (from text): Line perpendicular to $2x - 3y = 6$ through $(4, -1)$.
    1. Find slope of $2x - 3y = 6$: $-3y = -2x + 6 \Rightarrow y = \frac{2}{3}x - 2$. So $m_{given} = \frac{2}{3}$.
    2. Perpendicular slope: $m_{\perp} = -\frac{3}{2}$.
    3. Using $(4, -1)$ and $m_{\perp} = -\frac{3}{2}$: $y - (-1) = -\frac{3}{2}(x - 4) \Rightarrow y + 1 = -\frac{3}{2}x + 6 \Rightarrow y = -\frac{3}{2}x + 5$.
  • Summary for Slopes: The slope is crucial because it completely defines the direction of a line. Knowing the slope and just one point on the line is sufficient to write its unique equation.

Graphing Lines Using Intercepts

This method is particularly efficient for lines in standard form ($Ax + By = C$).

  • x-intercept: The point where the line crosses the x-axis. At this point, the y-coordinate is always 0.
    • How to find: Set $y = 0$ in the equation and solve for $x$. The intercept is $(x, 0)$.
  • y-intercept: The point where the line crosses the y-axis. At this point, the x-coordinate is always 0.
    • How to find: Set $x = 0$ in the equation and solve for $y$. The intercept is $(0, y)$.
  • Process:
    1. Find the x-intercept.
    2. Find the y-intercept.
    3. Plot these two points.
    4. Draw a straight line through the two plotted points.
    • Check: You can verify the slope of the graphed line matches the slope derived from the equation.
  • Example (from text): Graph $2x - 3y = 6$ using intercepts.
    • x-intercept (set $y=0$): $2x - 3(0) = 6 \Rightarrow 2x = 6 \Rightarrow x = 3$. Point: $(3, 0)$.
    • y-intercept (set $x=0$): $2(0) - 3y = 6 \Rightarrow -3y = 6 \Rightarrow y = -2$. Point: $(0, -2)$.
    • Plot $(3, 0)$ and $(0, -2)$ and draw the line.
  • Special Case: If both intercepts are $(0, 0)$ (meaning the line passes through the origin), you'll need to find a second point by substituting any convenient non-zero value for $x$ and solving for $y$.

Linear Functions as Functions

  • Definition: A linear function is any function that can be written in the form $f(x) = mx + b$.
  • Graph: The graph of a linear function is always a straight line. The constant $m$ represents the slope, and $b$ represents the y-intercept.
  • One-to-one Property: Every linear function (as long as $m \neq 0$, meaning it's not a horizontal line) is a one-to-one function. This means that for every unique $x$-value, there is exactly one unique $y$-value, and no horizontal line will intersect the graph more than once.
