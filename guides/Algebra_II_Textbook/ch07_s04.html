Study Guide: Exponential Equations

Overview

Exponential equations are algebraic equations where the variable appears in the exponent. This section explores two primary strategies for solving these equations: using the property of having the same base on both sides, or taking the logarithm of both sides when bases cannot be easily matched. Mastering these techniques is crucial for solving various mathematical and real-world problems involving exponential growth and decay.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of an Exponential Equation: An equation where the unknown variable is located in the exponent of one or more terms.
  • Strategy 1: Same Base Property: If an exponential equation can be rewritten so that both sides have the same base ($b$), then their exponents must be equal. This property states: if $b^{f(x)} = b^{g(x)}$ (where $b > 0$ and $b \neq 1$), then $f(x) = g(x)$.
  • Strategy 2: Taking the Logarithm of Both Sides: When it's not feasible to express both sides of an exponential equation with the same base, logarithms are used. The general steps involve isolating the exponential term, taking the logarithm (either common $\log$ or natural $\ln$) of both sides, applying the Power Rule of Logarithms to bring the exponent down, and then solving the resulting algebraic equation.
  • Choice of Logarithm: Both common logarithm ($\log$, base 10) and natural logarithm ($\ln$, base $e$) can be used interchangeably to solve exponential equations. The result for the variable $x$ will be the same. The choice often depends on convention in a specific field (e.g., $\ln$ in science/calculus, $\log$ in engineering).
  • Quadratic Form Exponential Equations: Some exponential equations can be transformed into a quadratic form using substitution (e.g., letting $u = e^x$ transforms $e^{2x} - 5e^x + 6 = 0$ into $u^2 - 5u + 6 = 0$). After solving for $u$, you back-substitute to find $x$.
  • Applications: Exponential equations are widely used to model real-world phenomena such as population growth, radioactive decay, compound interest, and doubling time for investments.

Important Terms

  • Exponential Equation: An equation where the variable is found in the exponent.
  • Base: In an exponential expression $b^x$, $b$ is the base, the number being raised to a power.
  • Exponent: In an exponential expression $b^x$, $x$ is the exponent, indicating how many times the base is multiplied by itself.
  • Common Logarithm ($\log$): A logarithm with base 10, typically written without the base subscript ($\log x$ means $\log_{10} x$).
  • Natural Logarithm ($\ln$): A logarithm with base $e$ (Euler's number), typically written as $\ln x$ (meaning $\log_e x$).
  • Power Rule for Logarithms: A key property of logarithms stating that $\log_b(M^p) = p \log_b(M)$. This rule allows us to bring an exponent down as a multiplier.
  • Isolate: To manipulate an equation algebraically so that a specific term or variable is alone on one side of the equals sign.
  • Back-substitute: The process of replacing a temporary variable (like $u$ in a substitution method) with its original expression to solve for the primary variable.

Detailed Notes

What are Exponential Equations?

An exponential equation is an equation where the variable you are trying to solve for appears in the exponent. For example, $2^x = 16$ or $5^{2x-1} = 125$. The goal is to find the value(s) of the variable that make the equation true.

Strategy 1: Solving Using the Same Base Property

This method is applicable when both sides of the equation can be expressed with the same base.

  • The Property: If you have an equation where $b^{f(x)} = b^{g(x)}$ (and $b$ is a positive number not equal to 1), then you can conclude that their exponents must be equal: $f(x) = g(x)$.

  • Steps:

    1. Examine the bases: Look at the bases on both sides of the equation.
    2. Rewrite with a common base: If possible, rewrite one or both bases as powers of a common, smaller base. For example, $9$ can be written as $3^2$, and $27$ as $3^3$.
    3. Equate the exponents: Once both sides have the same base, set the exponents equal to each other.
    4. Solve for the variable: Solve the resulting algebraic equation (which is often linear).

  • Examples:

    • $2^{3x} = 2^{x+4}$
      • The bases are already the same (2).
      • Set exponents equal: $3x = x+4$.
      • Solve: $2x = 4 \implies x = 2$.
    • $9^x = 27$
      • Rewrite both sides with base 3: $9 = 3^2$ and $27 = 3^3$.
      • The equation becomes $(3^2)^x = 3^3$, which simplifies to $3^{2x} = 3^3$.
      • Set exponents equal: $2x = 3$.
      • Solve: $x = \frac{3}{2}$.
    • $4^{x-1} = 8^{x+2}$
      • Rewrite both sides with base 2: $4 = 2^2$ and $8 = 2^3$.
      • The equation becomes $(2^2)^{x-1} = (2^3)^{x+2}$, which simplifies to $2^{2(x-1)} = 2^{3(x+2)}$.
      • Apply exponent rules: $2^{2x-2} = 2^{3x+6}$.
      • Set exponents equal: $2x-2 = 3x+6$.
      • Solve: $-2-6 = 3x-2x \implies -8 = x$.

Strategy 2: Solving by Taking the Logarithm of Both Sides

This strategy is used when you cannot easily rewrite both sides of the equation with the same base.

  • Steps:

    1. Isolate the exponential expression: Get the term with the variable in the exponent by itself on one side of the equation. This means isolating $b^x$.
    2. Take the logarithm of both sides: Apply either the common logarithm ($\log$) or the natural logarithm ($\ln$) to both sides of the equation. It's important to apply the same logarithm to both sides.
    3. Apply the Power Rule: Use the logarithm property $\log_b(M^p) = p \log_b(M)$ to bring the exponent down as a coefficient. So, $\log(b^x)$ becomes $x \log(b)$.
    4. Solve for the variable: The equation will now be an algebraic equation (often linear) that can be solved for $x$. You'll typically get an answer involving logarithms, which can then be approximated using a calculator.

  • Choice of Logarithm ($\log$ vs. $\ln$):

    • Both $\log$ and $\ln$ will yield the same correct answer for $x$. For example, $x = \frac{\log 80}{\log 5}$ is numerically equal to $x = \frac{\ln 80}{\ln 5}$.
    • Natural logarithms ($\ln$) are generally preferred in higher-level mathematics (like calculus) and science. Common logarithms ($\log$) are often seen in engineering. Use whichever is more convenient or specified by your course.

  • Examples:

    • $5^x = 80$
      • The exponential term ($5^x$) is already isolated.
      • Take $\ln$ of both sides: $\ln(5^x) = \ln(80)$.
      • Apply Power Rule: $x \ln(5) = \ln(80)$.
      • Solve for $x$: $x = \frac{\ln(80)}{\ln(5)}$.
      • Approximate (to four decimal places): $x \approx \frac{4.3820}{1.6094} \approx 2.7228$.
    • $3e^{2x} - 1 = 74$
      • Isolate $e^{2x}$:
        • Add 1 to both sides: $3e^{2x} = 75$.
        • Divide by 3: $e^{2x} = 25$.
      • Take $\ln$ of both sides (since the base is $e$): $\ln(e^{2x}) = \ln(25)$.
      • Since $\ln(e^k) = k$: $2x = \ln(25)$.
      • Solve for $x$: $x = \frac{\ln(25)}{2}$.
      • Approximate: $x \approx \frac{3.2189}{2} \approx 1.6094$.

Solving Exponential Equations with Multiple Terms (Quadratic Form)

Sometimes, an exponential equation might resemble a quadratic equation.

  • Recognition: Look for terms where the exponent of one term is double the exponent of another, e.g., $e^{2x}$ and $e^x$.

  • Method:

    1. Substitute: Let $u$ equal the simpler exponential term (e.g., $u = e^x$). Then the term with the double exponent will be $u^2$ (e.g., $e^{2x} = (e^x)^2 = u^2$).
    2. Form a quadratic equation: Rewrite the original equation in terms of $u$. It should now be a standard quadratic equation.
    3. Solve for $u$: Solve the quadratic equation for $u$ using factoring, the quadratic formula, or completing the square.
    4. Back-substitute: Replace $u$ with its original exponential expression (e.g., $e^x$).
    5. Solve for $x$: Solve the resulting simple exponential equations for $x$, usually by taking logarithms.

  • Example: $e^{2x} - 5e^x + 6 = 0$

    • Let $u = e^x$. Then $e^{2x} = (e^x)^2 = u^2$.
    • Substitute: $u^2 - 5u + 6 = 0$.
    • Factor the quadratic: $(u-2)(u-3) = 0$.
    • Solve for $u$: $u = 2$ or $u = 3$.
    • Back-substitute:
      • $e^x = 2 \implies x = \ln(2) \approx 0.6931$.
      • $e^x = 3 \implies x = \ln(3) \approx 1.0986$.
    • The solution set is ${\ln 2, \ln 3}$.

Applications of Exponential Equations

Exponential equations are fundamental to modeling growth and decay in various real-world scenarios.

  • Example: Doubling-Time Application
    • An investment grows according to $A(t) = 1000 \cdot 2^{t/5}$ (dollars after $t$ years). In how many years will the investment reach $7,000?
    • Set up the equation: $1000 \cdot 2^{t/5} = 7000$.
    • Isolate the exponential term: Divide by 1000: $2^{t/5} = 7$.
    • Take logarithm of both sides: $\ln(2^{t/5}) = \ln(7)$.
    • Apply Power Rule: $\frac{t}{5} \ln(2) = \ln(7)$.
    • Solve for $t$: $t = 5 \cdot \frac{\ln(7)}{\ln(2)}$.
    • Approximate: $t \approx 5 \cdot \frac{1.9459}{0.6931} \approx 5 \cdot 2.8075 \approx 14.04$ years.

Review Questions

  1. Solve the equation $3^{2x-1} = 3^{x+5}$. Which strategy did you use and why was it appropriate?
  2. Solve the equation $16^x = 64^{x-1}$. Describe the steps you took to make the bases common.
  3. Solve the equation $7^x = 100$. Round your answer to four decimal places. Explain why the logarithm strategy was necessary here.
  4. Solve the equation $5 \cdot e^{3x+2} - 15 = 35$. Detail the steps involved in isolating the exponential term before taking the logarithm.
  5. An initial population of 500 bacteria doubles every 3 hours. The population can be modeled by $P(t) = 500 \cdot 2^{t/3}$. How long will it take for the population to reach 8,000 bacteria?
