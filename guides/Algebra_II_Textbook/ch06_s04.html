Graphing Rational Functions: Part 1 - Comprehensive Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces rational functions, defined as ratios of polynomials, and explores their unique graphical characteristics, such as breaks, gaps (holes), and asymptotic behavior. The primary goal is to equip students with a systematic procedure for identifying key features directly from the function's equation and using them to accurately sketch the graph.

Key Concepts

  • Rational Function: A function of the form $f(x) = p(x)/q(x)$, where $p(x)$ and $q(x)$ are polynomials and $q(x)$ is not the zero polynomial. These functions can have discontinuous graphs, unlike polynomials.
  • Vertical Asymptote (VA): A vertical line $x = a$ that the graph approaches indefinitely as $x$ gets closer to $a$. VAs occur at values of $x$ where the simplified denominator of the rational function is zero.
  • Horizontal Asymptote (HA): A horizontal line $y = L$ that the graph approaches as $x$ tends towards positive or negative infinity, describing the function's end behavior. Its existence and value depend on the comparison of the degrees of the numerator ($n$) and denominator ($d$).
  • Intercepts:
    • x-intercepts: Points where the graph crosses the x-axis ($y=0$). Found by setting the numerator of the simplified function equal to zero, provided these values do not make the denominator zero.
    • y-intercept: The point where the graph crosses the y-axis ($x=0$). Found by evaluating $f(0)$, if $0$ is in the function's domain.
  • Graphing Procedure: A systematic six-step process for sketching rational functions, involving simplification, identifying asymptotes and intercepts, using a sign chart, and drawing the final graph.

Important Terms

  • Rational Function: A function defined by a rational fraction, i.e., an algebraic fraction such that both the numerator and the denominator are polynomials.
  • Vertical Asymptote (VA): A vertical line $x=a$ that the graph of a function approaches as the input $x$ approaches $a$.
  • Horizontal Asymptote (HA): A horizontal line $y=L$ that the graph of a function approaches as the input $x$ approaches positive or negative infinity.
  • Degree of Numerator ($n$): The highest exponent of the variable in the polynomial in the numerator.
  • Degree of Denominator ($d$): The highest exponent of the variable in the polynomial in the denominator.
  • Leading Coefficient: The coefficient of the term with the highest degree in a polynomial.
  • Simplified Form: A rational function where any common factors between the numerator and denominator have been cancelled. Crucial for correctly identifying vertical asymptotes and holes.
  • x-intercept: A point $(x, 0)$ where a graph intersects the x-axis.
  • y-intercept: A point $(0, y)$ where a graph intersects the y-axis.
  • Sign Chart: A visual tool used to determine the sign (positive or negative) of a function over different intervals on the number line, helping to determine if the graph is above or below the x-axis.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Rational Functions

A rational function is defined as $f(x) = p(x)/q(x)$, where $p(x)$ and $q(x)$ are polynomials, and $q(x)$ is not the zero polynomial. Unlike polynomial functions, whose graphs are always smooth and continuous, rational functions can have discontinuities in the form of vertical asymptotes, horizontal asymptotes, and sometimes "holes" (removable discontinuities). This section focuses on identifying these features to accurately sketch the graph.

Key Features of a Rational Function

Vertical Asymptotes (VA)

A vertical line $x=a$ is a vertical asymptote if the function's output $f(x)$ approaches positive or negative infinity as $x$ approaches $a$ from either side.

  • How to find: Vertical asymptotes occur at the values of $x$ that make the simplified denominator of the rational function equal to zero. It is crucial to simplify the function (cancel any common factors between numerator and denominator) before setting the denominator to zero, as common factors lead to "holes" in the graph rather than vertical asymptotes.

Horizontal Asymptotes (HA)

A horizontal line $y=L$ is a horizontal asymptote if $f(x)$ approaches $L$ as $x$ approaches positive or negative infinity. It describes the function's end behavior.

  • How to find: Compare the degree of the numerator ($n$) to the degree of the denominator ($d$). Let $a_n$ be the leading coefficient of the numerator and $b_d$ be the leading coefficient of the denominator.
    • Case 1: $n < d$ (Degree of numerator is less than the degree of the denominator)
      • The horizontal asymptote is $y = 0$ (the x-axis).
    • Case 2: $n = d$ (Degree of numerator equals the degree of the denominator)
      • The horizontal asymptote is $y = a_n / b_d$ (the ratio of the leading coefficients).
    • Case 3: $n > d$ (Degree of numerator is greater than the degree of the denominator)
      • There is no horizontal asymptote. The function's value grows without bound as $|x| \to \infty$. (In Section 6-5, we will learn about slant or oblique asymptotes for this case.)
  • Intuition: For very large values of $|x|$, the highest-degree terms in the numerator and denominator dominate the behavior of the function, much like the leading term test for polynomials.

Intercepts

  • x-intercepts: To find where the graph crosses the x-axis (where $y=0$), set the numerator of the simplified rational function equal to zero and solve for $x$. It's important to verify that these values of $x$ do not make the denominator zero, as that would indicate a hole or an asymptote rather than an intercept.
  • y-intercept: To find where the graph crosses the y-axis (where $x=0$), evaluate the function at $x=0$, i.e., calculate $f(0)$. This is only possible if $0$ is in the function's domain.

Sketching Procedure for Rational Functions

To sketch the graph of a rational function $f(x) = p(x)/q(x)$, follow these steps:

  1. Simplify: Factor both the numerator and the denominator completely. Cancel any common factors. Note the $x$-values that were removed from the domain due to cancellation; these correspond to "holes" in the graph.
  2. Vertical Asymptotes: Set the simplified denominator equal to zero and solve for $x$. These are the equations of the vertical asymptotes.
  3. Horizontal Asymptote: Compare the degrees of the numerator ($n$) and denominator ($d$) of the simplified function using the rules described above ($n<d \implies y=0$; $n=d \implies y=a_n/b_d$; $n>d \implies$ no HA).
  4. Intercepts:
    • x-intercepts: Set the simplified numerator equal to zero and solve for $x$.
    • y-intercept: Evaluate $f(0)$ (if $0$ is in the domain).
  5. Sign Chart: Determine the critical values, which are the $x$-intercepts and the locations of the vertical asymptotes. Plot these values on a number line. Choose a test point in each interval created by these critical values and evaluate the function's sign (positive or negative) at that point. This indicates whether the graph is above or below the x-axis in that interval.
  6. Sketch:
    • Draw the vertical and horizontal asymptotes as dashed lines.
    • Plot the x-intercepts and y-intercepts.
    • Use the information from the sign chart and the behavior near the asymptotes to sketch the branches of the graph in each interval. Remember that the graph will approach the asymptotes but not cross them (except in rare cases for horizontal asymptotes, but not vertical ones).

Examples from the Text

Graphing a Basic Rational Function: $f(x) = \frac{2x}{x-3}$

  1. Simplify: No common factors.
  2. Vertical Asymptote: Set denominator to zero: $x-3=0 \implies x=3$. So, $x=3$ is the VA.
  3. Horizontal Asymptote: Degree of numerator ($n=1$) equals degree of denominator ($d=1$). Ratio of leading coefficients is $2/1 = 2$. So, $y=2$ is the HA.
  4. Intercepts:
    • x-intercept: Set numerator to zero: $2x=0 \implies x=0$. So, $(0,0)$ is the x-intercept.
    • y-intercept: Evaluate $f(0) = \frac{2(0)}{0-3} = 0$. So, $(0,0)$ is the y-intercept.
  5. Sign Chart: Critical values are $x=0$ (x-intercept) and $x=3$ (VA).
    • Test $x=-1$: $f(-1) = \frac{2(-1)}{-1-3} = \frac{-2}{-4} = \frac{1}{2} > 0$. (Graph above x-axis)
    • Test $x=1$: $f(1) = \frac{2(1)}{1-3} = \frac{2}{-2} = -1 < 0$. (Graph below x-axis)
    • Test $x=4$: $f(4) = \frac{2(4)}{4-3} = \frac{8}{1} = 8 > 0$. (Graph above x-axis)
  6. Sketch: Draw dashed lines $x=3$ and $y=2$. Plot $(0,0)$. Sketch branches consistent with the sign chart: above x-axis in $(-\infty, 0)$, below x-axis in $(0,3)$, above x-axis in $(3,\infty)$, approaching $y=2$ at the ends and $x=3$ vertically.

When the Numerator Has Higher Degree: $g(x) = \frac{x^2+1}{x-2}$

  1. Vertical Asymptote: Set denominator to zero: $x-2=0 \implies x=2$. So, $x=2$ is the VA.
  2. Horizontal Asymptote: Degree of numerator ($n=2$) is greater than degree of denominator ($d=1$). Therefore, there is no horizontal asymptote. (This function will have a slant asymptote, to be covered in Section 6-5).
  3. Intercepts:
    • x-intercept: Set numerator to zero: $x^2+1=0$. This equation has no real solutions, so there are no x-intercepts.
    • y-intercept: Evaluate $g(0) = \frac{0^2+1}{0-2} = \frac{1}{-2} = -\frac{1}{2}$. So, $(0, -1/2)$ is the y-intercept.

Writing a Rational Function from Given Features

Suppose we need a rational function with VA $x=2$, HA $y=3$, and x-intercept at $x=0$.

  1. Vertical Asymptote $x=2$: This means $(x-2)$ must be a factor in the denominator.
  2. Horizontal Asymptote $y=3$: This implies the degree of the numerator and denominator must be equal (e.g., both degree 1), and the ratio of their leading coefficients must be $3$.
  3. x-intercept $x=0$: This means the numerator must be zero when $x=0$, so $x$ must be a factor in the numerator.

Combining these:

  • Denominator has $(x-2)$.
  • Numerator has $x$.
  • To get HA $y=3$ with equal degrees, if the denominator is $(x-2)$ (leading coefficient 1), the numerator's leading coefficient needs to be $3$. So, the numerator could be $3x$. Thus, a possible function is $f(x) = \frac{3x}{x-2}$. We can check this:
  • VA: $x-2=0 \implies x=2$. (Correct)
  • HA: $n=1, d=1$, leading coefficient ratio $3/1=3$. (Correct)
  • x-intercept: $3x=0 \implies x=0$. (Correct)
  • y-intercept: $f(0) = 0/(0-2) = 0$.

Checkpoint Solution: $h(x) = \frac{3x-6}{x^2-4}$

  1. Simplify: Factor both numerator and denominator: $h(x) = \frac{3(x-2)}{(x-2)(x+2)}$ Cancel the common factor $(x-2)$: $h(x) = \frac{3}{x+2}$, for $x \neq 2$.
    • Important Note: Because $(x-2)$ was cancelled, there is a hole in the graph at $x=2$. To find the y-coordinate of the hole, substitute $x=2$ into the simplified function: $h(2) = \frac{3}{2+2} = \frac{3}{4}$. So, there's a hole at $(2, 3/4)$.
  2. Vertical Asymptote: Set the simplified denominator to zero: $x+2=0 \implies x=-2$. So, $x=-2$ is the VA.
  3. Horizontal Asymptote: For the simplified function $h(x) = \frac{3}{x+2}$, the degree of the numerator ($n=0$) is less than the degree of the denominator ($d=1$). So, $y=0$ (the x-axis) is the HA.
  4. Intercepts:
    • x-intercept: Set the simplified numerator to zero: $3=0$. This is never true, so there are no x-intercepts.
    • y-intercept: Evaluate $h(0)$ using the simplified function: $h(0) = \frac{3}{0+2} = \frac{3}{2}$. So, $(0, 3/2)$ is the y-intercept.

Review Questions

  1. What are the fundamental differences in graphical behavior between a polynomial function and a rational function? Provide examples of features unique to rational function graphs.
  2. Explain the significance of simplifying a rational function before identifying its vertical asymptotes. What kind of graphical feature arises if a factor is canceled from both the numerator and denominator?
  3. Describe the three rules for finding horizontal asymptotes, based on the degrees of the numerator and denominator. For each rule, briefly explain why that specific outcome occurs in terms of end behavior.
  4. You are given a rational function $f(x) = \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}$. How do you find its x-intercepts and y-intercept? What conditions must be met for an intercept to exist at a specific point?
  5. Imagine you have identified all asymptotes and intercepts for a rational function. How does creating a sign chart help you complete an accurate sketch of the graph, and what "critical values" do you use to define the intervals for the chart?
