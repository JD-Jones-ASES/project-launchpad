Chapter 4, Section 4: Function Arithmetic and Composition Study Guide

Overview

This section explores various ways to combine two or more functions to create new functions. It covers four fundamental arithmetic operations (sum, difference, product, and quotient) which combine functions directly, and introduces the more complex operation of function composition, where the output of one function becomes the input for another. Understanding these combinations is crucial for analyzing and modeling real-world phenomena involving multiple interconnected relationships.

Key Concepts

  • Arithmetic Combinations of Functions: Functions can be added, subtracted, multiplied, and divided similar to numbers.
    • Sum (f + g)(x) = f(x) + g(x): Adds the outputs of $f$ and $g$ for a given input $x$.
    • Difference (f - g)(x) = f(x) - g(x): Subtracts the output of $g$ from the output of $f$.
    • Product (fg)(x) = f(x)g(x): Multiplies the outputs of $f$ and $g$.
    • Quotient (f/g)(x) = f(x)/g(x): Divides the output of $f$ by the output of $g$.
  • Domain of Arithmetic Combinations: For sum, difference, and product, the domain is the intersection of the individual domains of $f$ and $g$ ($D_f \cap D_g$). For the quotient, it's also $D_f \cap D_g$, but with any $x$ values where $g(x) = 0$ explicitly excluded.
  • Composition of Functions (f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x)): A powerful operation where the output of the "inner" function $g(x)$ becomes the input for the "outer" function $f$. It's read as "$f$ composed with $g$" or "$f$ of $g$".
  • Evaluation of Composition (Inside Out): To evaluate $(f \circ g)(x)$, first calculate $g(x)$, then use that result as the input for $f$.
  • Non-Commutativity of Composition: In general, $(f \circ g)(x) \neq (g \circ f)(x)$. The order of composition matters.
  • Domain of a Composite Function: The domain of $(f \circ g)(x)$ consists of all $x$ values in the domain of $g$ such that the output $g(x)$ is within the domain of $f$. This is a two-step check: (1) $x$ must be valid for $g$, and (2) $g(x)$ must be valid for $f$.
  • Decomposition of Functions: The reverse process of composition, where a given complex function is broken down into two simpler functions $f$ and $g$ such that their composition $(f \circ g)(x)$ equals the original function.

Important Terms

  • Sum of Functions: The function $(f+g)(x)$ formed by adding the outputs of $f(x)$ and $g(x)$.
  • Difference of Functions: The function $(f-g)(x)$ formed by subtracting the output of $g(x)$ from $f(x)$.
  • Product of Functions: The function $(fg)(x)$ formed by multiplying the outputs of $f(x)$ and $g(x)$.
  • Quotient of Functions: The function $(f/g)(x)$ formed by dividing the output of $f(x)$ by $g(x)$, provided $g(x) \neq 0$.
  • Composition of Functions: An operation that combines two functions, say $f$ and $g$, to produce a new function $(f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x))$.
  • Inner Function: In a composition $(f \circ g)(x)$, $g(x)$ is the inner function, whose output is used as the input for the outer function.
  • Outer Function: In a composition $(f \circ g)(x)$, $f(x)$ is the outer function, which acts on the output of the inner function.
  • Domain of Composition: For $(f \circ g)(x)$, it's the set of all $x$ such that $x$ is in the domain of $g$ AND $g(x)$ is in the domain of $f$.

Detailed Notes

I. Function Arithmetic

Just as you can perform arithmetic operations on numbers, you can perform them on functions. When we define $(f+g)(x)$, for example, we are creating a new function whose rule is to add the outputs of $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ for any given $x$.

Definitions: Given two functions $f$ and $g$ with overlapping domains:

  1. Sum: $(f + g)(x) = f(x) + g(x)$
  2. Difference: $(f - g)(x) = f(x) - g(x)$
  3. Product: $(fg)(x) = f(x) g(x)$
  4. Quotient: $(f/g)(x) = f(x) / g(x)$, provided $g(x) \neq 0$.

Domains of Arithmetic Combinations:

  • For $(f+g)(x)$, $(f-g)(x)$, and $(fg)(x)$, the domain is the intersection of the domains of $f$ and $g$. That is, $D_f \cap D_g$.
  • For $(f/g)(x)$, the domain is $D_f \cap D_g$, with an additional restriction: any $x$ for which $g(x) = 0$ must be excluded.

Example: Forming and Evaluating Arithmetic Combinations Let $f(x) = 3x + 1$ and $g(x) = x^2 - 4$. Both $f$ and $g$ have a domain of $(-\infty, \infty)$. So, the initial intersection $D_f \cap D_g$ is $(-\infty, \infty)$.

  1. $(f + g)(x)$:
    • Formula: $(f + g)(x) = (3x + 1) + (x^2 - 4) = x^2 + 3x - 3$.
    • Evaluation at $x=2$: $(f+g)(2) = 2^2 + 3(2) - 3 = 4 + 6 - 3 = 7$.
  2. $(f - g)(x)$:
    • Formula: $(f - g)(x) = (3x + 1) - (x^2 - 4) = 3x + 1 - x^2 + 4 = -x^2 + 3x + 5$.
    • Evaluation at $x=2$: $(f-g)(2) = -(2^2) + 3(2) + 5 = -4 + 6 + 5 = 7$.
  3. $(fg)(x)$:
    • Formula: $(fg)(x) = (3x + 1)(x^2 - 4) = 3x^3 - 12x + x^2 - 4 = 3x^3 + x^2 - 12x - 4$.
    • Evaluation at $x=2$: $(fg)(2) = 3(2^3) + (2^2) - 12(2) - 4 = 3(8) + 4 - 24 - 4 = 24 + 4 - 24 - 4 = 0$.
    • Note: This makes sense because $g(2) = 2^2 - 4 = 0$, and anything multiplied by zero is zero.
  4. $(f/g)(x)$:
    • Formula: $(f/g)(x) = \frac{3x+1}{x^2-4}$.
    • Domain: We must exclude values of $x$ where $g(x) = x^2 - 4 = 0$. This occurs when $x^2 = 4$, so $x = 2$ or $x = -2$.
    • The domain is $(-\infty, -2) \cup (-2, 2) \cup (2, \infty)$.
    • Evaluation at $x=2$: $(f/g)(2)$ is undefined because the denominator becomes zero.

II. Composition of Functions

Composition is a fundamental operation where the output of one function becomes the input of another. Think of it as a chain reaction.

Definition: The composition of $f$ and $g$, written $(f \circ g)(x)$, is defined as: $$ (f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x)) $$ This is read as "$f$ composed with $g$" or "$f$ of $g$".

How to Evaluate Composition (The "Inside-Out" Rule): To find $f(g(x))$, you must:

  1. First, evaluate the inner function $g(x)$.
  2. Then, take the result of $g(x)$ and use it as the input for the outer function $f$.

Order Matters! (Non-Commutativity): It is critical to understand that $(f \circ g)(x)$ is generally not the same as $(g \circ f)(x)$. The order in which functions are composed changes the outcome.

Example: Evaluating Composite Functions at a Point Let $f(x) = x^2 + 1$ and $g(x) = 2x - 3$.

  1. $(f \circ g)(4)$:

    • Step 1 (Inner function): $g(4) = 2(4) - 3 = 8 - 3 = 5$.
    • Step 2 (Outer function): $f(g(4)) = f(5) = 5^2 + 1 = 25 + 1 = 26$.

  2. $(g \circ f)(4)$:

    • Step 1 (Inner function): $f(4) = 4^2 + 1 = 16 + 1 = 17$.
    • Step 2 (Outer function): $g(f(4)) = g(17) = 2(17) - 3 = 34 - 3 = 31$.
    • Note: As expected, $(f \circ g)(4) = 26 \neq (g \circ f)(4) = 31$.

  3. $(f \circ f)(2)$: (Composing a function with itself)

    • Step 1 (Inner function): $f(2) = 2^2 + 1 = 4 + 1 = 5$.
    • Step 2 (Outer function): $f(f(2)) = f(5) = 5^2 + 1 = 25 + 1 = 26$.

Example: Finding the Composition as a Formula Let $f(x) = 2x + 5$ and $g(x) = x^2 - 1$.

  1. $(f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x))$:

    • Replace the $x$ in the definition of $f(x)$ with the entire expression for $g(x)$: $$ f(g(x)) = f(x^2 - 1) $$
    • Substitute this into $f$'s rule: $$ 2(x^2 - 1) + 5 = 2x^2 - 2 + 5 = 2x^2 + 3 $$ So, $(f \circ g)(x) = 2x^2 + 3$.

  2. $(g \circ f)(x) = g(f(x))$:

    • Replace the $x$ in the definition of $g(x)$ with the entire expression for $f(x)$: $$ g(f(x)) = g(2x + 5) $$
    • Substitute this into $g$'s rule: $$ (2x + 5)^2 - 1 $$
    • Expand and simplify: $$ (4x^2 + 20x + 25) - 1 = 4x^2 + 20x + 24 $$ So, $(g \circ f)(x) = 4x^2 + 20x + 24$.

Decomposing a Composite Function: Sometimes, you're given a complex function and asked to find simpler functions $f$ and $g$ such that their composition equals the given function. The key is to identify an "inner" operation or expression and an "outer" operation.

Example: Decomposing a Composite Function Write each function as a composition $f \circ g$ (find $f$ and $g$ where $h(x) = f(g(x))$).

  1. $h(x) = (3x - 5)^4$

    • Inner expression: $3x - 5$. Let $g(x) = 3x - 5$.
    • Outer operation (what happens to $g(x)$): The result is raised to the 4th power. Let $f(x) = x^4$.
    • Check: $f(g(x)) = f(3x-5) = (3x-5)^4 = h(x)$. This works.

  2. $h(x) = \sqrt{x^2 + 7}$

    • Inner expression: $x^2 + 7$. Let $g(x) = x^2 + 7$.
    • Outer operation: The square root of the expression. Let $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$.
    • Check: $f(g(x)) = f(x^2+7) = \sqrt{x^2+7} = h(x)$. This works.

  3. $h(x) = \frac{1}{x+3}$

    • Inner expression: $x + 3$. Let $g(x) = x + 3$.
    • Outer operation: 1 divided by the expression. Let $f(x) = \frac{1}{x}$.
    • Check: $f(g(x)) = f(x+3) = \frac{1}{x+3} = h(x)$. This works.

Domain of a Composite Function: The domain of $(f \circ g)(x)$ requires two conditions to be met:

  1. $x$ must be in the domain of $g$. (You must be able to calculate $g(x)$).
  2. The output $g(x)$ must be in the domain of $f$. (You must be able to calculate $f(\text{result of } g(x))$).

Example: Domain of a Composite Function Let $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$ and $g(x) = x - 4$. Find $(f \circ g)(x)$ and state its domain.

  1. Form the composition: $$ (f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x)) = f(x - 4) = \sqrt{x - 4} $$

  2. Find the domain:

    • Condition 1: Domain of $g(x) = x - 4$ is all real numbers, $(-\infty, \infty)$.
    • Condition 2: The output of $g(x)$, which is $x-4$, must be in the domain of $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$. The domain of $\sqrt{x}$ requires its input to be non-negative. Therefore, we need $x - 4 \ge 0$. Solving for $x$, we get $x \ge 4$.
    • Combining these: Since $x \ge 4$ is a stricter condition than $x \in (-\infty, \infty)$, the domain of $(f \circ g)(x)$ is $[4, \infty)$.

Checkpoint Summary: If $f(x) = 4x - 1$ and $g(x) = x^2$:

  • $(f+g)(3) = f(3) + g(3) = (4(3)-1) + (3^2) = (12-1) + 9 = 11 + 9 = 20$.
  • $(f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x)) = f(x^2) = 4(x^2) - 1 = 4x^2 - 1$.
  • $(g \circ f)(x) = g(f(x)) = g(4x-1) = (4x-1)^2 = 16x^2 - 8x + 1$.
  • $(g \circ f)(-1) = g(f(-1)) = g(4(-1)-1) = g(-5) = (-5)^2 = 25$.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words the difference between $(f + g)(x)$ and $(f \circ g)(x)$. Provide an example where one is defined but the other is not for a specific $x$ value.
  2. Given $f(x) = \frac{1}{x-3}$ and $g(x) = x^2$. a) Find the domain of $(f+g)(x)$. b) Find the domain of $(f/g)(x)$. c) Find the domain of $(f \circ g)(x)$. d) Find the domain of $(g \circ f)(x)$.
  3. Why is it important to check both the domain of the inner function and the domain of the outer function (relative to the inner function's output) when determining the domain of a composite function?
  4. Decompose the function $h(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2 - 9}}$ into two simpler functions $f$ and $g$ such that $h(x) = (f \circ g)(x)$. Are there other possible decompositions?
  5. Consider two real-world scenarios that could be modeled by (a) an arithmetic combination of functions, and (b) a composition of functions. Describe the functions and what the combined/composite function would represent in each case.
