Graphing Rational Functions: Part 2 Study Guide

Overview

This section expands on the fundamentals of graphing rational functions by introducing two additional key features: holes (removable discontinuities) and oblique (slant) asymptotes. It also provides a comprehensive review of vertical and horizontal asymptotes, x-intercepts, and y-intercepts, culminating in a systematic approach for analyzing and sketching the graph of any rational function.

Key Concepts

  • Holes (Removable Discontinuities): These are single points where a rational function is undefined, but the graph doesn't exhibit unbounded behavior like an asymptote. They occur when a common factor cancels out from both the numerator and the denominator of the function.
  • Vertical Asymptotes (VA): Vertical lines that the graph approaches but never touches, where the function's output grows infinitely large or small. They are found at the zeros of the denominator after the function has been simplified to its lowest terms (i.e., after canceling any common factors).
  • Horizontal Asymptotes (HA): Horizontal lines that the graph approaches as x extends to positive or negative infinity. Their existence and location depend on the comparison of the degrees of the numerator and denominator polynomials.
  • Oblique (Slant) Asymptotes (OA): Non-horizontal, non-vertical linear asymptotes that occur when the degree of the numerator is exactly one greater than the degree of the denominator. They are found by performing polynomial long division.
  • x-Intercepts: The points where the graph crosses or touches the x-axis, meaning the function's output (y-value) is zero. These are found by setting the simplified numerator equal to zero.
  • y-Intercepts: The point where the graph crosses the y-axis, meaning the x-value is zero. This is found by evaluating $f(0)$.
  • Sign Analysis: A method used to determine where the graph lies above or below the x-axis within intervals defined by vertical asymptotes and x-intercepts. This helps in sketching the curve's behavior.

Important Terms

  • Rational Function: A function that can be written as the ratio of two polynomials, $f(x) = \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$, where $Q(x) \neq 0$. Its domain excludes all values of $x$ for which $Q(x) = 0$.
  • Vertical Asymptote: A vertical line $x = a$ that the graph of a function approaches as $|f(x)| \to \infty$ when $x \to a$. It arises from zeros of the denominator that do not cancel with factors in the numerator.
  • Hole (Removable Discontinuity): A single point of discontinuity in the graph of a rational function that occurs at $x = a$ when the factor $(x-a)$ cancels from both the numerator and the denominator. The function is undefined at this point, but the graph otherwise behaves normally around it.
  • Horizontal Asymptote: A horizontal line $y = b$ that the graph of a function approaches as $x \to \infty$ or $x \to -\infty$. The graph may cross a horizontal asymptote for finite values of $x$.
  • Oblique (Slant) Asymptote: A non-horizontal, non-vertical linear asymptote $y = mx + b$ that the graph of a rational function approaches as $x \to \infty$ or $x \to -\infty$. It occurs when the degree of the numerator is exactly one greater than the degree of the denominator.
  • Lowest Terms: A rational expression is in lowest terms when the numerator and denominator have no common factors other than 1 or -1. This is crucial for correctly identifying vertical asymptotes versus holes.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Advanced Graphing Features

Building on the understanding of vertical and horizontal asymptotes, this section introduces "holes" and "slant asymptotes" to provide a complete picture of graphing rational functions. A rational function, defined as $f(x) = \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$ where $P$ and $Q$ are polynomials and $Q(x) \neq 0$, has a domain that excludes all zeros of $Q(x)$.

Vertical Asymptotes (Review and Refinement)

A vertical asymptote (VA) is a vertical line $x=a$ where the function's values become infinitely large or small ($|f(x)| \to \infty$) as $x$ approaches $a$ from either side. Graphically, the curve rises or falls steeply near $x=a$ without actually touching the line.

Finding Vertical Asymptotes:

  1. Factor both the numerator $P(x)$ and the denominator $Q(x)$ completely.
  2. Simplify the rational function by canceling any common factors between $P(x)$ and $Q(x)$. This step is crucial for distinguishing between vertical asymptotes and holes.
  3. Set the remaining denominator factors equal to zero and solve for $x$. Each solution $x=a$ corresponds to a vertical asymptote.

Important Distinction: Vertical asymptotes arise from factors in the denominator that do not cancel with factors in the numerator. If a factor does cancel, it creates a hole, not a vertical asymptote.

Holes (Removable Discontinuities)

A hole in the graph of a rational function signifies a single missing point. This "removable discontinuity" occurs at $x=a$ when the factor $(x-a)$ cancels out from both the numerator and the denominator after factoring.

Finding Holes:

  1. Factor the numerator and denominator completely.
  2. Identify any common factors $(x-a)$ that cancel.
  3. For each canceled factor $(x-a)$, there is a hole at $x=a$.
  4. To find the $y$-coordinate of the hole, substitute $x=a$ into the simplified rational function (the expression after cancellation). The hole is located at $(a, y_{\text{hole}})$.

Example: Analyze $f(x) = \frac{x^2 - x - 2}{x^2 - 4}$.

  • Factor: Numerator: $(x-2)(x+1)$. Denominator: $(x-2)(x+2)$.
  • Function becomes: $f(x) = \frac{(x-2)(x+1)}{(x-2)(x+2)}$.
  • Cancellation: The factor $(x-2)$ cancels.
  • Hole: Since $(x-2)$ canceled, there's a hole at $x=2$.
    • Simplified form: $\frac{x+1}{x+2}$.
    • Evaluate at $x=2$: $\frac{2+1}{2+2} = \frac{3}{4}$.
    • Hole at $(2, \frac{3}{4})$.
  • Vertical Asymptote: The remaining denominator factor is $(x+2)$. Set $x+2=0$, so $x=-2$. This factor did not cancel, so there's a VA at $x=-2$.

x-Intercepts

The x-intercepts are points $(x, 0)$ where the graph crosses or touches the x-axis.

Finding x-Intercepts:

  1. Simplify the rational function to its lowest terms.
  2. Set the numerator of the simplified function equal to zero and solve for $x$.
  3. The solutions are the x-coordinates of the x-intercepts. Ensure these values are not domain restrictions (i.e., not values that would make the original denominator zero and result in a hole or VA).

Example: Find x-intercepts of $f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 5x + 6}{x - 1}$.

  • Factor numerator: $(x-2)(x-3)$.
  • Simplified form: $\frac{(x-2)(x-3)}{x-1}$ (no common factors, so no holes).
  • Set numerator to zero: $(x-2)(x-3) = 0$.
  • Solutions: $x=2$ and $x=3$.
  • x-intercepts: $(2,0)$ and $(3,0)$. (Neither $x=2$ nor $x=3$ makes the denominator $x-1$ zero, so they are valid intercepts.)

y-Intercepts

The y-intercept is the point $(0, y)$ where the graph crosses the y-axis.

Finding y-Intercepts:

  1. Evaluate $f(0)$ by substituting $x=0$ into the original function.
  2. If $f(0)$ is defined, the y-intercept is $(0, f(0))$.

Horizontal Asymptotes (Review)

A horizontal asymptote (HA) is a horizontal line $y=b$ that the graph approaches as $x \to \infty$ or $x \to -\infty$. Unlike vertical asymptotes, the graph can sometimes cross a horizontal asymptote for finite values of $x$. The rules for finding a horizontal asymptote depend on comparing the degrees of the numerator ($n$) and denominator ($m$) polynomials in $f(x) = \frac{a_n x^n + \dots}{b_m x^m + \dots}$.

Rules for Horizontal Asymptotes:

  1. If $n < m$ (degree of numerator is less than degree of denominator): The horizontal asymptote is $y = 0$ (the x-axis).
  2. If $n = m$ (degree of numerator is equal to degree of denominator): The horizontal asymptote is $y = \frac{a_n}{b_m}$ (the ratio of the leading coefficients).
  3. If $n > m$ (degree of numerator is greater than degree of denominator): There is no horizontal asymptote. Instead, if $n = m+1$, there will be an oblique asymptote.

Oblique (Slant) Asymptotes

An oblique asymptote (OA), also called a slant asymptote, is a non-horizontal linear asymptote. It occurs when the degree of the numerator polynomial is exactly one greater than the degree of the denominator polynomial ($n = m+1$).

Finding Oblique Asymptotes:

  1. Perform polynomial long division of the numerator $P(x)$ by the denominator $Q(x)$.
  2. The result of the division will be in the form $P(x)/Q(x) = (mx + b) + \frac{R(x)}{Q(x)}$.
  3. As $x \to \infty$ or $x \to -\infty$, the remainder term $\frac{R(x)}{Q(x)}$ approaches zero (since the degree of $R(x)$ is less than the degree of $Q(x)$).
  4. The oblique asymptote is the line $y = mx + b$, which is the non-remainder part of the long division result.

Example: Find the oblique asymptote of $f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 2}$.

  • The degree of the numerator (2) is one greater than the degree of the denominator (1). An oblique asymptote exists.
  • Perform polynomial long division:
        x + 2
  _________
x-2 | x^2 + 0x - 1
    -(x^2 - 2x)
    ---------
          2x - 1
        -(2x - 4)
        ---------
                3
  • So, $\frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 2} = x + 2 + \frac{3}{x - 2}$.
  • As $x \to \pm \infty$, $\frac{3}{x-2} \to 0$.
  • The oblique asymptote is $y = x + 2$.

Sign Analysis Between Asymptotes and x-Intercepts

To determine where the graph lies above or below the x-axis and to understand its behavior between critical points, perform a sign analysis.

Steps for Sign Analysis:

  1. Identify all vertical asymptotes and x-intercepts. These are the "critical points" that divide the real number line into intervals.
  2. Choose a test value within each interval.
  3. Substitute the test value into the simplified rational function $f(x)$ and determine the sign ($+$ or $-$) of the result.
  4. If $f(\text{test value}) > 0$, the graph is above the x-axis in that interval.
  5. If $f(\text{test value}) < 0$, the graph is below the x-axis in that interval.

Example: Sketch $f(x) = \frac{2x}{x^2 - 1}$.

  • Factor: $f(x) = \frac{2x}{(x-1)(x+1)}$. No cancellation, so no holes.
  • Domain: $x \neq 1, x \neq -1$.
  • Vertical Asymptotes: $x=1$ and $x=-1$.
  • Horizontal Asymptote: Degree of numerator (1) < Degree of denominator (2), so $y=0$.
  • x-intercept: Set numerator $2x=0 \implies x=0$. Point $(0,0)$.
  • y-intercept: $f(0) = \frac{2(0)}{0^2 - 1} = 0$. Point $(0,0)$.

Sign Chart: Critical points are $x=-1, 0, 1$.

Based on the sign chart and asymptotes:

  • The graph approaches $y=0$ from below as $x \to -\infty$. It then dives down along $x=-1$.
  • Between $x=-1$ and $x=0$, it comes from above $x=-1$, crosses the x-axis at $(0,0)$, and then approaches $x=1$ from below.
  • Between $x=0$ and $x=1$, it goes from above $x=-1$, crosses the x-axis at $(0,0)$, and then approaches $x=1$ from below. Correction: Between $x=-1$ and $x=0$, it is above the x-axis. Between $x=0$ and $x=1$, it is below the x-axis.

Review Questions

  2. Describe the conditions under which a rational function will have (a) a horizontal asymptote, (b) an oblique (slant) asymptote, and (c) neither. For each case, explain how to find the equation of the asymptote if it exists.
  3. Consider the function $f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 9}{x^2 - 2x - 3}$. Find all (a) domain restrictions, (b) vertical asymptotes, (c) holes (including their y-coordinates), (d) x-intercepts, and (e) the horizontal asymptote.
  4. Outline the complete step-by-step process you would follow to sketch the graph of a rational function, incorporating all features discussed in this section (domain, intercepts, asymptotes, holes, and sign analysis).

What does this tell you about the graph of the function in that interval? What if the adjacent interval $(5, 8)$ gives a negative result? What does this imply about the point $x=5$?
