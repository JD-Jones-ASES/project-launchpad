Study Guide: Transformations II: Stretches, Compressions, and Combined

Overview

This section expands on basic graph shifts by introducing scaling transformations: stretches and compressions. We'll learn how to make graphs taller/shorter (vertical scaling) or wider/narrower (horizontal scaling) by multiplying input or output values. Finally, we'll establish a crucial order for applying multiple transformations to a single function.

Key Concepts

  • Vertical Stretches and Compressions: These transformations alter the height of a graph.
    • Equation: $y = a \cdot f(x)$
    • Effect: If $a > 1$, the graph stretches vertically (becomes taller/narrower). If $0 < a < 1$, the graph compresses vertically (becomes shorter/wider).
    • Point Mapping: Each point $(x, y)$ on $f(x)$ becomes $(x, ay)$ on $a \cdot f(x)$. Points on the x-axis are unaffected.
  • Horizontal Stretches and Compressions: These transformations alter the width of a graph.
    • Equation: $y = f(b \cdot x)$
    • Counterintuitive Effect: If $b > 1$, the graph compresses horizontally by a factor of $1/b$ (becomes narrower). If $0 < b < 1$, the graph stretches horizontally by a factor of $1/b$ (becomes wider).
    • Point Mapping: Each point $(x, y)$ on $f(x)$ becomes $(x/b, y)$ on $f(bx)$. Points on the y-axis are unaffected.
  • Distinction Between Vertical and Horizontal Scaling: For some specific functions (like $f(x) = x^2$), a horizontal compression can produce the same result as a vertical stretch. However, this is not generally true for all functions, and it's vital to understand the difference.
  • Order of Transformations: When multiple transformations are applied to a function, they must be applied in a specific sequence, typically working from the "inside out" of the function notation.

Important Terms

  • Scaling Transformations: Operations that stretch or compress a graph, changing its size rather than just its position.
  • Vertical Stretch: A transformation where a graph becomes taller, achieved by multiplying the output $f(x)$ by a constant $a > 1$.
  • Vertical Compression: A transformation where a graph becomes shorter, achieved by multiplying the output $f(x)$ by a constant $0 < a < 1$.
  • Horizontal Stretch: A transformation where a graph becomes wider, achieved by multiplying the input $x$ by a constant $0 < b < 1$.
  • Horizontal Compression: A transformation where a graph becomes narrower, achieved by multiplying the input $x$ by a constant $b > 1$.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Scaling Transformations

In the previous section, we learned about shifting graphs horizontally and vertically. These transformations move every point the same distance without changing the graph's shape or size. Now, we're introducing scaling transformations (stretches and compressions), which do change the size of the graph by multiplying its input or output values.

Vertical Stretches and Compressions

When we multiply the entire function $f(x)$ by a positive constant $a$, we perform a vertical scaling.

  • The Rule: Given $y = f(x)$, the graph of $y = a \cdot f(x)$
    • Stretches vertically by a factor of $a$ when $a > 1$. The graph becomes taller and appears narrower.
    • Compresses vertically by a factor of $a$ when $0 < a < 1$. The graph becomes shorter and appears wider.
  • Effect on Points: Each point $(x, y)$ on the original graph $y=f(x)$ is transformed to $(x, ay)$ on the new graph $y=a \cdot f(x)$.
  • Points Unchanged: Any point on the $x$-axis (where $y=0$) remains fixed during a vertical stretch or compression because $a \cdot 0 = 0$.

Example: Vertical Scaling of a Parabola Consider $f(x) = x^2$.

  • For $g(x) = 3x^2$: Here $a = 3 > 1$. Every $y$-value of $f(x)$ is multiplied by 3. The point $(1, 1)$ on $f(x)$ becomes $(1, 3)$ on $g(x)$. The parabola stretches vertically, appearing narrower.
  • For $h(x) = \frac{1}{4}x^2$: Here $a = \frac{1}{4} < 1$. Every $y$-value of $f(x)$ is multiplied by $\frac{1}{4}$. The point $(2, 4)$ on $f(x)$ becomes $(2, 1)$ on $h(x)$. The parabola compresses vertically, appearing wider.
  • The vertex $(0,0)$ and the axis of symmetry $x=0$ are unaffected by these vertical scalings.

Horizontal Stretches and Compressions

When we multiply the input variable $x$ by a positive constant $b$ inside the function, we perform a horizontal scaling. This operation is often counterintuitive.

  • The Rule: Given $y = f(x)$, the graph of $y = f(b \cdot x)$
    • Compresses horizontally by a factor of $1/b$ when $b > 1$. The graph becomes narrower.
    • Stretches horizontally by a factor of $1/b$ when $0 < b < 1$. The graph becomes wider.
  • Effect on Points: Each point $(x, y)$ on the original graph $y=f(x)$ is transformed to $(x/b, y)$ on the new graph $y=f(bx)$.
  • Points Unchanged: Any point on the $y$-axis (where $x=0$) remains fixed because $0/b = 0$.
  • Mnemonic: Think of $f(2x)$ as the function "working twice as fast." To get the same $y$-output as $f(x)$, you only need half the $x$-input. This means the graph is compressed horizontally.

Order of Transformations

When multiple transformations (shifts, reflections, stretches, compressions) are applied, their order matters significantly. Follow this sequence:

  1. Horizontal Shifts and Horizontal Stretches/Compressions: These involve changes inside the function, affecting the $x$-values directly. (e.g., $f(bx+c)$ means $f(b(x+c/b))$. The shift $c/b$ comes before the scaling $b$.)
  2. Reflections over the y-axis: If $x$ is replaced by $-x$ inside the function ($f(-x)$).
  3. Vertical Stretches/Compressions: These involve multiplication outside the function (e.g., $a \cdot f(x)$).
  4. Reflections over the x-axis: If the entire function is multiplied by $-1$ (e.g., $-f(x)$).
  5. Vertical Shifts: These involve addition or subtraction outside the function (e.g., $f(x)+d$ or $f(x)-d$).

In practice: Handle all "inside" changes first (horizontal operations), then all "outside" changes (vertical operations), from scaling factors to shifts.

Example: Identifying All Transformations Starting with $f(x) = |x|$, let's analyze $g(x) = -2,|x + 1| - 3$. We rewrite $x+1$ as $x-(-1)$ to make the shift clear.

  1. Horizontal Shift: $x \rightarrow x+1$ means shifting the graph left by 1 unit. The vertex moves from $(0,0)$ to $(-1, 0)$.
  2. Vertical Stretch: Multiply the output by $2$. Each $y$-value doubles. The vertex remains at $(-1, 0)$. The V-shape becomes narrower.
  3. Reflection over x-axis: The leading negative sign (from $-2$) flips the graph downward. The V-shape now opens down.
  4. Vertical Shift: Subtract 3 from the entire function. The graph shifts down by 3 units. The vertex moves to $(-1, -3)$.

The vertex of $g(x)$ is $(-1, -3)$, and the graph opens downward with a slope of $\pm 2$ (steeper than the original $|x|$).

Example: Writing the Equation from a Description Start with $y = x^2$.

  1. Shift right 3: Replace $x$ with $(x-3)$. Equation becomes $y = (x-3)^2$.
  2. Compress vertically by $\frac{1}{2}$: Multiply the entire function by $\frac{1}{2}$. Equation becomes $y = \frac{1}{2}(x-3)^2$.
  3. Shift down 4: Subtract 4 from the entire function. Equation becomes $y = \frac{1}{2}(x-3)^2 - 4$.

So the final equation is $y = \frac{1}{2}(x-3)^2 - 4$.

Caution: Applying transformations in the wrong order can lead to an incorrect graph. For instance, shifting vertically before stretching vertically generally produces a different graph than stretching first (unless the stretch is centered at the axis of the shift).

Review Questions

  1. Describe the difference between a vertical stretch and a horizontal compression. Provide an example of each using a simple function like $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$.
  2. Given the function $h(x) = - \frac{1}{2} (x+4)^3 + 5$, list all transformations applied to the parent function $f(x) = x^3$ in the correct order. Identify the coordinates of the new "center" or "inflection point" of the graph.
  3. Explain why multiplying the input by a constant $b > 1$ (e.g., $f(2x)$) results in a horizontal compression, which can seem counterintuitive.
  4. The graph of $y = |x|$ is transformed as follows: stretched horizontally by a factor of 3, reflected over the x-axis, then shifted up by 2 units. Write the equation of the new transformed function.
  5. Is it always true that a horizontal stretch and a vertical compression can produce the same graph? If not, provide an example of a function where they are distinct, and explain why.
