Sequences: An Introduction to Ordered Lists of Numbers

Overview

This section introduces sequences as ordered lists of numbers that follow a specific rule, emphasizing their connection to functions where the domain is the set of positive integers. We delve into two fundamental types of sequences: arithmetic sequences, characterized by a constant difference between consecutive terms, and geometric sequences, distinguished by a constant ratio between consecutive terms. Understanding these sequence types and their respective formulas allows us to predict any term in the series and model various real-world scenarios.

Key Concepts

  • Sequence as a Function: A sequence is essentially a function whose input (domain) is the set of positive integers (1, 2, 3, ...), and whose output (range) is the ordered list of numbers. The $n$th term of a sequence, $a_n$, corresponds to the function value $a(n)$.
  • Arithmetic Sequence: An arithmetic sequence is an ordered list where the difference between any two consecutive terms is constant. This constant difference is known as the common difference ($d$).
  • Geometric Sequence: A geometric sequence is an ordered list where each term after the first is found by multiplying the previous one by a fixed, non-zero number. This fixed multiplier is called the common ratio ($r$).
  • $n$th-Term Formula for an Arithmetic Sequence: If the first term is $a_1$ and the common difference is $d$, the formula to find any term $a_n$ is $a_n = a_1 + (n - 1)d$. This formula highlights the linear growth of arithmetic sequences.
  • $n$th-Term Formula for a Geometric Sequence: If the first term is $a_1$ and the common ratio is $r$, the formula to find any term $a_n$ is $a_n = a_1 r^{n-1}$. This formula demonstrates the exponential growth or decay characteristic of geometric sequences.

Important Terms

  • Sequence: An ordered list of numbers, denoted by $a_1, a_2, a_3, \dots$, where each number is called a term.
  • Term ($a_n$): A specific number in a sequence. $a_n$ refers to the $n$th term in the sequence.
  • Arithmetic Sequence (or Arithmetic Progression): A sequence where the difference between any term and its preceding term is constant.
  • Common Difference ($d$): The constant value found by subtracting any term from its succeeding term in an arithmetic sequence ($d = a_{n+1} - a_n$).
  • Geometric Sequence: A sequence where the ratio of any term to its preceding term is constant.
  • Common Ratio ($r$): The constant non-zero value found by dividing any term by its preceding term in a geometric sequence ($r = \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}$).

Detailed Notes

What is a Sequence?

A sequence is fundamentally an ordered list of numbers. Each number in the list is called a term, and we use notation like $a_1$ for the first term, $a_2$ for the second, and $a_n$ for the $n$th term. From a functional perspective, a sequence is a special type of function where the domain consists only of positive integers (1, 2, 3, ...). So, $a_n$ is just another way of writing $a(n)$. This means that for each positive integer input $n$, there is a corresponding output term $a_n$.

Arithmetic Sequences

An arithmetic sequence is characterized by a consistent pattern: the difference between any two consecutive terms remains the same. This constant difference is crucial and is called the common difference, denoted by $d$.

  • Identifying an Arithmetic Sequence: To determine if a sequence is arithmetic, simply subtract each term from the term that immediately follows it. If all these differences are identical, then the sequence is arithmetic.
    • Example: For $3, 7, 11, 15, \dots$
      • $7 - 3 = 4$
      • $11 - 7 = 4$
      • $15 - 11 = 4$ Since the difference is consistently $4$, it's an arithmetic sequence with $d=4$.
  • The $n$th-Term Formula: The general formula for the $n$th term of an arithmetic sequence is given by: $$a_n = a_1 + (n - 1)d$$ Here, $a_1$ is the first term, $d$ is the common difference, and $n$ is the term number you want to find. This formula is powerful because it allows you to calculate any term directly without having to list all the preceding terms.
  • Connection to Linear Functions: Arithmetic sequences behave like linear functions. If you plot the term number $n$ against its value $a_n$ (i.e., points $(n, a_n)$), they will all lie on a straight line. The common difference $d$ acts as the slope of this line.
  • Using the Formula:
    • If $a_1 = 7$ and $d = -3$:
      • Formula for $a_n$: $a_n = 7 + (n-1)(-3) = 7 - 3n + 3 = 10 - 3n$.
      • To find $a_{10}$: $a_{10} = 10 - 3(10) = -20$.
      • To find which term equals $-50$: $10 - 3n = -50 \implies -3n = -60 \implies n = 20$. So, the 20th term is $-50$.

Geometric Sequences

A geometric sequence is defined by a constant multiplier between consecutive terms. This constant factor is called the common ratio, denoted by $r$.

  • Identifying a Geometric Sequence: To check if a sequence is geometric, divide each term by its preceding term. If all these ratios are the same, then the sequence is geometric. Remember, the common ratio $r$ cannot be zero.
    • Example: For $2, 6, 18, 54, \dots$
      • $6 / 2 = 3$
      • $18 / 6 = 3$
      • $54 / 18 = 3$ Since the ratio is consistently $3$, it's a geometric sequence with $r=3$.
  • The $n$th-Term Formula: The general formula for the $n$th term of a geometric sequence is given by: $$a_n = a_1 r^{n-1}$$ Here, $a_1$ is the first term, $r$ is the common ratio, and $n$ is the term number. This formula is vital for predicting terms in geometric progressions.
  • Connection to Exponential Functions: Geometric sequences mimic exponential functions. If you plot $(n, a_n)$, the points will lie on an exponential curve.
    • If $r > 1$, the terms grow rapidly (exponential growth).
    • If $0 < r < 1$, the terms shrink towards zero (exponential decay).
    • If $r < 0$, the terms will alternate in sign, while their absolute values either grow or shrink.
  • Using the Formula:
    • If $a_1 = 3$ and $r = 2$:
      • Formula for $a_n$: $a_n = 3 \cdot 2^{n-1}$.
      • To find $a_8$: $a_8 = 3 \cdot 2^{8-1} = 3 \cdot 2^7 = 3 \cdot 128 = 384$.
      • To find if $192$ is a term: $3 \cdot 2^{n-1} = 192 \implies 2^{n-1} = 64$. Since $64 = 2^6$, we have $n-1 = 6 \implies n = 7$. So, $192$ is the 7th term.

Finding the Formula from Two Terms

Sometimes you are given two non-consecutive terms of a sequence and need to find the common difference/ratio and the first term.

  • For Arithmetic Sequences: If $a_3 = 11$ and $a_7 = 27$:
    1. Find $d$: The difference in term numbers is $7 - 3 = 4$. So, $a_7 = a_3 + 4d$. $27 = 11 + 4d \implies 16 = 4d \implies d = 4$.
    2. Find $a_1$: Use the $n$th-term formula $a_n = a_1 + (n-1)d$ with either given term (e.g., $a_3=11$): $11 = a_1 + (3-1)(4) \implies 11 = a_1 + 8 \implies a_1 = 3$.
    3. Write the formula for $a_n$: $a_n = 3 + (n-1)(4) = 3 + 4n - 4 = 4n - 1$.

Real-World Application: Compound Interest as a Geometric Sequence

Compound interest is a classic example of a geometric sequence. When interest is compounded annually, the balance at the end of each year is found by multiplying the previous year's balance by a constant factor $(1 + \text{interest rate})$.

  • Example: $1,000 deposited at $6%$ annual interest compounded annually.
    • The common ratio $r = 1 + 0.06 = 1.06$. The initial deposit is $a_1 = 1,000$.
    • $a_1 = 1,000$
    • $a_2 = 1,000 \cdot 1.06 = 1,060$
    • $a_3 = 1,060 \cdot 1.06 \approx 1,123.60$
    • $a_4 = 1,123.60 \cdot 1.06 \approx 1,191.02$
    • To find the balance after $10$ years (which is $a_{10}$ using our term numbering, as $a_1$ is the initial deposit, and $a_2$ is the balance after 1 year): $a_{10} = a_1 \cdot r^{10-1} = 1,000 \cdot (1.06)^9 \approx 1,000 \cdot 1.689478 \approx $1,689.48$.

Review Questions

  1. Explain how a sequence can be viewed as a function. What is the specific characteristic of a sequence's domain when considered as a function?
  2. Describe the key difference between an arithmetic sequence and a geometric sequence. Provide an example for each type that clearly illustrates this difference.
  3. An arithmetic sequence has $a_2 = 8$ and $a_6 = 20$. Find the common difference, the first term, and write a formula for the $n$th term of this sequence.
  4. A geometric sequence starts with $a_1 = 100$ and has a common ratio of $r = 0.5$. Write the first four terms of the sequence. Will the terms ever become negative? Why or why not? What happens to the terms as $n$ gets very large?
  5. Consider a scenario where the number of bacteria in a colony doubles every hour. If you start with 50 bacteria, how can this situation be modeled using a sequence? Identify the type of sequence, its first term, common ratio/difference, and write a formula to find the number of bacteria after $n$ hours.
