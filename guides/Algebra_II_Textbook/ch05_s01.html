Chapter 5, Section 1: Polynomial Basics Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental building blocks of algebra: polynomials. It defines what a polynomial is, dissects its various components like terms, degrees, coefficients, and constant terms, and explains how to write them in standard form. Furthermore, it covers basic operations such as identifying parts, combining like terms, evaluating polynomials, and performing addition and subtraction of these expressions.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of a Polynomial: A polynomial in one variable $x$ is an expression consisting of terms of the form $a_k x^k$, where $a_k$ are real number coefficients and $k$ is a non-negative integer exponent. The exponents must be non-negative integers (no negative or fractional exponents).
  • Anatomy of a Polynomial:
    • Term: Each product $a_k x^k$ in the polynomial.
    • Degree of a Polynomial: The highest exponent of the variable in the polynomial.
    • Leading Term: The term that contains the highest exponent.
    • Leading Coefficient: The coefficient of the leading term.
    • Constant Term: The term without a variable factor (the $a_0$ term).
  • Standard Form: A polynomial is in standard form when its terms are arranged in descending order of their degrees, from the highest-degree term to the constant term.
  • Classification of Polynomials:
    • By Number of Terms: Monomial (one term), Binomial (two terms), Trinomial (three terms).
    • By Degree: Polynomials are also classified by their degree (e.g., linear for degree 1, quadratic for degree 2, etc., though specific names for higher degrees are not listed here).
  • Combining Like Terms: Like terms have identical variable parts (same variable raised to the same exponent). They can be combined by adding or subtracting their coefficients.
  • Evaluating a Polynomial: To find the value of a polynomial $P(x)$ at a specific number $a$, substitute $a$ for every $x$ in the expression and simplify the result. The notation for this is $P(a)$.
  • Adding and Subtracting Polynomials:
    • Addition: Group and combine like terms.
    • Subtraction: Distribute the negative sign to all terms of the subtracted polynomial, then group and combine like terms. The result should be written in standard form.

Important Terms

  • Polynomial: An algebraic expression consisting of one or more terms, where each term is a product of a number (coefficient) and one or more variables raised to non-negative integer powers.
  • Term: A single part of a polynomial, typically a product of a coefficient and a variable raised to an exponent (e.g., $5x^3$, $-2x$, $7$).
  • Coefficient: The numerical factor of a term in a polynomial (e.g., in $5x^3$, $5$ is the coefficient).
  • Degree of a Polynomial: The highest exponent of the variable present in any of its terms after the polynomial has been simplified.
  • Leading Term: The term in a polynomial (when written in standard form) that has the highest degree.
  • Leading Coefficient: The coefficient of the leading term.
  • Constant Term: The term in a polynomial that does not contain a variable (a term with a degree of zero).
  • Standard Form: The arrangement of a polynomial's terms in descending order of their degrees.
  • Monomial: A polynomial with exactly one term (e.g., $7x^2$).
  • Binomial: A polynomial with exactly two terms (e.g., $3x - 5$).
  • Trinomial: A polynomial with exactly three terms (e.g., $x^2 + 2x - 1$).
  • Like Terms: Terms that have the same variables raised to the same powers (e.g., $3x^2$ and $-7x^2$).
  • Evaluating a Polynomial: The process of substituting a specific numerical or algebraic value for the variable(s) in a polynomial and simplifying the resulting expression to find its value.

Detailed Notes

I. Defining and Understanding Polynomials

A polynomial is a fundamental algebraic expression. It's built from terms, each of which is a product of a real number (its coefficient) and a variable raised to a non-negative integer power. For example, in the general form $a_n x^n + a_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \dots + a_1 x + a_0$:

  • $n$ must be a non-negative integer ($0, 1, 2, \dots$). This means expressions with negative exponents (like $x^{-2}$) or fractional exponents (like $x^{1/2}$ or $\sqrt{x}$) are not polynomials.
  • The coefficients $a_k$ are real numbers.
  • Each $a_k x^k$ is a term.

II. Anatomy of a Polynomial

To fully understand polynomials, you need to identify their key parts:

  • Degree: This is the most important characteristic. It's simply the largest exponent on the variable.
  • Leading Term: This is the entire term that contains the highest exponent.
  • Leading Coefficient: This is the numerical part (coefficient) of the leading term.
  • Constant Term: This is the term that doesn't have a variable attached. It's essentially $a_0 x^0 = a_0 \cdot 1 = a_0$.

Example: For $P(x) = -4x^5 + 7x^3 - x + 9$

  • Degree: 5 (from the term $-4x^5$)
  • Leading Term: $-4x^5$
  • Leading Coefficient: $-4$
  • Constant Term: $9$
  • Coefficient of $x^3$: $7$ (from the term $7x^3$)

III. Standard Form and Classification

  • Standard Form: To write a polynomial in standard form, arrange its terms from the highest degree to the lowest degree. The constant term will always be last. This makes it easier to compare and work with polynomials.
    • Example: $3x^2 - 8 + 5x^4 - x$ becomes $5x^4 + 3x^2 - x - 8$ in standard form. Its degree is 4, and the leading coefficient is 5.
  • Classification by Number of Terms:
    • Monomial: One term (e.g., $5x^3$)
    • Binomial: Two terms (e.g., $x^2 - 4$)
    • Trinomial: Three terms (e.g., $2x^2 - 3x + 1$)
  • Classification by Degree: Although not fully detailed in the provided text, polynomials are also classified by their degree (e.g., a polynomial of degree 1 is linear, degree 2 is quadratic, degree 3 is cubic, etc.).

IV. Operations with Polynomials

A. Combining Like Terms

  • Definition: Like terms must have the exact same variable part, meaning the same variable raised to the same exponent.
  • Process: To combine like terms, simply add or subtract their coefficients while keeping the variable part unchanged.
  • Example (Addition): $(6x^3 - 2x^2 + 5x - 1) + (x^3 + 4x^2 - 3x + 7)$ Group like terms: $(6x^3 + x^3) + (-2x^2 + 4x^2) + (5x - 3x) + (-1 + 7)$ Combine: $7x^3 + 2x^2 + 2x + 6$
  • Important Note: The degree of a sum or difference of polynomials is at most the larger of their individual degrees. It can be lower if the leading terms cancel out (e.g., $(x^2+1) - (x^2-3) = 4$, where the degree drops from 2 to 0).

B. Evaluating a Polynomial

  • Process: To evaluate $P(x)$ at $x=a$, substitute the value $a$ for every $x$ in the polynomial and then simplify using the order of operations.

  • Notation: The result is written as $P(a)$.

  • Example (Numerical): Let $P(x) = 2x^3 - 5x^2 + x - 4$.

    • For $P(3)$: $P(3) = 2(3)^3 - 5(3)^2 + (3) - 4$ $P(3) = 2(27) - 5(9) + 3 - 4$ $P(3) = 54 - 45 + 3 - 4 = 8$
    • For $P(-2)$: $P(-2) = 2(-2)^3 - 5(-2)^2 + (-2) - 4$ $P(-2) = 2(-8) - 5(4) - 2 - 4$ $P(-2) = -16 - 20 - 2 - 4 = -42$

  • Caution with Negative Values: Always enclose negative numbers in parentheses when substituting them, especially before applying exponents. For example, $(-2)^2 = 4$, but $-2^2 = -4$.

  • Example (Variable Expression): Let $Q(x) = x^2 - 3x + 2$. Find $Q(a+1)$. $Q(a+1) = (a+1)^2 - 3(a+1) + 2$ $Q(a+1) = (a^2 + 2a + 1) - (3a + 3) + 2$ $Q(a+1) = a^2 + 2a + 1 - 3a - 3 + 2$ $Q(a+1) = a^2 - a$

C. Adding and Subtracting Polynomials

  • General Procedure:
    1. Distribute any negative signs (for subtraction).
    2. Identify and collect all like terms.
    3. Combine the coefficients of like terms.
    4. Write the final result in standard form.
  • Example (Subtraction): $(5x^3 - 2x^2 + 4) - (3x^3 + x^2 - 6x + 1)$ Distribute the negative sign: $5x^3 - 2x^2 + 4 - 3x^3 - x^2 + 6x - 1$ Group like terms: $(5x^3 - 3x^3) + (-2x^2 - x^2) + 6x + (4 - 1)$ Combine: $2x^3 - 3x^2 + 6x + 3$

Review Questions

  1. Describe the key characteristics that define a polynomial. Provide an example of an expression that is a polynomial and an example of an expression that is not a polynomial, explaining why.
  2. Given the polynomial $P(x) = 8x^7 - 5x^2 + 10 - x^3$:
    • Write the polynomial in standard form.
    • Identify its degree.
    • State the leading coefficient.
    • What is the constant term?
    • What is the coefficient of the $x^3$ term?
  3. Explain the difference between a monomial, a binomial, and a trinomial, providing an example for each.
  4. If $Q(x) = -x^2 + 4x - 6$, evaluate $Q(0)$, $Q(5)$, and $Q(-3)$. Explain any important considerations when substituting negative values.
  5. Perform the following operations, showing your steps and writing the result in standard form:
    • a) $(3y^4 + 2y^3 - 7y + 11) + (-y^4 + 5y^3 + y - 9)$
    • b) $(6a^2 - a + 8) - (2a^2 + 3a - 4)$
