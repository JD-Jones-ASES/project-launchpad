Rational Equations and Applications Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces rational equations, which are equations that contain one or more rational expressions. The primary strategy for solving these equations involves clearing denominators by multiplying by the Least Common Denominator (LCD), then solving the resulting polynomial equation. A critical final step is always checking for extraneous solutions—values that satisfy the transformed equation but make an original denominator zero, and thus must be rejected. The section also applies these problem-solving techniques to real-world scenarios like work-rate and distance-rate-time problems.

Key Concepts

  • Rational Equation: An equation where one or more terms are rational expressions (fractions with variables in the denominator).
  • Extraneous Solution: A value obtained during the solving process that appears to be a solution but, when substituted back into the original equation, causes a denominator to become zero. These values are not valid solutions and must be rejected.
  • Domain Restrictions: Values of the variable that would make any denominator in the original rational equation equal to zero. These values must be identified before solving and any potential solutions that match these restrictions must be discarded.
  • Clearing Denominators: The core strategy for solving rational equations involves multiplying every term on both sides of the equation by the LCD of all denominators. This transforms the rational equation into a simpler polynomial equation (linear or quadratic).
  • Work-Rate Formula: Used for problems where multiple entities work together to complete a task. If Worker A takes $t_A$ time units and Worker B takes $t_B$ time units to complete a job individually, working together they finish in time $T$ according to the formula: $1/t_A + 1/t_B = 1/T$.
  • Distance-Rate-Time Relationship: The fundamental formula is $Distance = Rate \times Time$, which can be rearranged to $Time = Distance / Rate$. This is often used in problems involving travel, especially when factors like current (for boats) or wind (for planes) affect the effective speed.

Important Terms

  • Rational Equation: An equation that includes at least one rational expression (a fraction where the numerator and/or denominator contains a variable).
  • Extraneous Solution: A solution derived from solving a transformed equation that does not satisfy the original equation because it makes a denominator equal to zero.
  • Least Common Denominator (LCD): The smallest multiple that all denominators in a set of rational expressions divide into evenly. Used to clear denominators in rational equations.
  • Work Rate: The fraction of a job that can be completed per unit of time. If a job takes $t$ hours to complete, the work rate is $1/t$ jobs per hour.

Detailed Notes

Understanding and Solving Rational Equations

A rational equation is any equation that contains one or more rational expressions. The fundamental approach to solving these equations is to eliminate the denominators, which simplifies the equation into a more familiar polynomial form (linear or quadratic).

Steps for Solving Rational Equations:

  1. Identify Domain Restrictions: Before doing anything else, determine all values of the variable that would make any denominator in the original equation equal to zero. These are the values your solutions cannot be.
  2. Find the LCD: Determine the Least Common Denominator (LCD) of all rational expressions in the equation.
  3. Clear Denominators: Multiply every single term on both sides of the equation by the LCD. This crucial step will eliminate all denominators, leaving you with a polynomial equation.
  4. Solve the Resulting Equation: The equation you now have will be either linear or quadratic. Solve it using appropriate methods (isolating the variable for linear equations, factoring or quadratic formula for quadratic equations).
  5. Check for Extraneous Solutions: This is the most critical step. Compare each solution you found in step 4 against the domain restrictions identified in step 1. If a solution matches any restriction (i.e., makes an original denominator zero), it is an extraneous solution and must be discarded. Only solutions that do not violate any restrictions are valid.

Example 1: Basic Rational Equation Solve $3/x + 1/2 = 5/x$.

  • Restriction: $x \neq 0$.
  • LCD: $2x$.
  • Multiply by LCD: $2x(3/x) + 2x(1/2) = 2x(5/x)$ $6 + x = 10$
  • Solve: $x = 4$.
  • Check: $x=4$ is not $0$, so it is a valid solution.

Example 2: Equation with an Extraneous Solution Solve $x/(x-3) = 3/(x-3) + 1$.

  • Restriction: $x \neq 3$.
  • LCD: $(x-3)$.
  • Multiply by LCD: $(x-3) [x/(x-3)] = (x-3) [3/(x-3)] + (x-3)(1)$ $x = 3 + (x-3)$ $x = x$
  • Solve: The equation $x=x$ is an identity, meaning it is true for all real numbers. However, we must remember our restriction.
  • Check: The solution set is all real numbers except $x=3$. If we tried to plug in $x=3$, it would make the denominators zero in the original equation.

Example 3: Rational Equation Leading to a Quadratic Solve $1/(x-2) + 1/(x+2) = 8/(x^2-4)$.

  • Factor Denominators: Note that $x^2-4 = (x-2)(x+2)$.
  • Restrictions: $x \neq 2$ and $x \neq -2$.
  • LCD: $(x-2)(x+2)$.
  • Multiply by LCD: $(x-2)(x+2) [1/(x-2)] + (x-2)(x+2) [1/(x+2)] = (x-2)(x+2) [8/((x-2)(x+2))]$ $(x+2) + (x-2) = 8$ $2x = 8$
  • Solve: $x = 4$.
  • Check: $x=4$ is not $2$ or $-2$, so it is a valid solution.

Important Caution: Always check your solutions in the original equation. An extraneous solution will satisfy the cleared polynomial equation but will cause a denominator to be zero in the original problem.

Applications of Rational Equations

Rational equations are powerful tools for solving various real-world problems.

Work and Rate Problems

These problems typically involve two or more individuals or machines working together to complete a task. The core idea is that if a person/machine takes $t$ units of time to complete a job, their work rate is $1/t$ (fraction of the job per unit of time). When working together, their rates add up.

Work-Rate Formula: $1/t_A + 1/t_B = 1/T$ Where:

  • $t_A$ = time for A to complete the job alone
  • $t_B$ = time for B to complete the job alone
  • $T$ = time for A and B to complete the job together

Example: Pipe Filling a Tank Pipe A fills a tank in 6 hours; Pipe B fills the same tank in 4 hours. How long does it take both pipes together to fill the tank?

  • Setup: Let $T$ be the time it takes them together. Pipe A's rate: $1/6$ tank/hour Pipe B's rate: $1/4$ tank/hour Together rate: $1/T$ tank/hour Equation: $1/6 + 1/4 = 1/T$
  • LCD: $12T$.
  • Multiply by LCD: $12T(1/6) + 12T(1/4) = 12T(1/T)$ $2T + 3T = 12$ $5T = 12$
  • Solve: $T = 12/5 = 2.4$ hours.
  • Check: The answer is positive and makes sense in context.

Distance-Rate-Time Problems

These problems often involve travel where speed is affected by external factors like current or wind, or when comparing different travel segments. The relationship $Time = Distance / Rate$ is key.

Example: Boat Traveling Upstream and Downstream A boat travels 30 miles upstream and 30 miles downstream in a total of 4 hours. The river current is 3 miles per hour. Find the boat's speed in still water.

  • Setup: Let $v$ = speed of the boat in still water (mi/h). Upstream speed (against current): $v - 3$ Downstream speed (with current): $v + 3$ Time upstream = $30/(v-3)$ Time downstream = $30/(v+3)$ Total time = 4 hours. Equation: $30/(v-3) + 30/(v+3) = 4$.
  • Restrictions: $v \neq 3$ and $v \neq -3$. Also, $v$ must be greater than $3$ for the boat to be able to move upstream.
  • LCD: $(v-3)(v+3)$.
  • Multiply by LCD: $30(v+3) + 30(v-3) = 4(v-3)(v+3)$ $30v + 90 + 30v - 90 = 4(v^2 - 9)$ $60v = 4v^2 - 36$
  • Solve (Quadratic Equation): $4v^2 - 60v - 36 = 0$ Divide by 4: $v^2 - 15v - 9 = 0$ Using the quadratic formula, $v = [-b \pm \sqrt{(b^2 - 4ac)}] / 2a$: $v = [15 \pm \sqrt{((-15)^2 - 4(1)(-9))}] / 2(1)$ $v = [15 \pm \sqrt{(225 + 36)}] / 2$ $v = [15 \pm \sqrt{261}] / 2$ $\sqrt{261} \approx 16.16$ $v \approx (15 \pm 16.16) / 2$ Possible solutions: $v \approx (15 + 16.16)/2 \approx 15.58$ or $v \approx (15 - 16.16)/2 \approx -0.58$.
  • Check: Since speed cannot be negative, we discard $v \approx -0.58$. The other solution, $v \approx 15.58$ mi/h, is greater than the current speed of 3 mi/h, so it is a valid and reasonable answer.

Checkpoint Review: A plumber can complete a job in 3 hours; an apprentice takes 6 hours. How long do they take working together?

  • Plumber's rate: $1/3$ job/hour
  • Apprentice's rate: $1/6$ job/hour
  • Together time: $T$
  • Equation: $1/3 + 1/6 = 1/T$
  • LCD: $6T$
  • Multiply by LCD: $6T(1/3) + 6T(1/6) = 6T(1/T)$ $2T + T = 6$ $3T = 6$ $T = 2$ hours.

Review Questions

  1. What is a rational equation? Describe the general strategy for solving rational equations, including the first and last critical steps.
  2. Explain the concept of an "extraneous solution" in the context of rational equations. Why is it essential to check for them, and where should you check your solutions?
  3. Outline the steps for solving a rational equation that, after clearing denominators, results in a quadratic equation. What methods can be used to solve the resulting quadratic?
  4. A painter can paint a room in 5 hours, while their helper can paint the same room in 8 hours. Set up and solve a rational equation to find out how long it would take them to paint the room together.
  5. A cyclist rides 24 miles against the wind and then 24 miles back with the wind. The total trip takes 4 hours. If the wind speed is 2 mph, what is the cyclist's speed in still air? Set up the rational equation and explain how you would solve it (you do not need to fully solve the quadratic).
