Applications of Quadratic Functions Study Guide

Overview

This section explores how quadratic functions model various real-world scenarios, particularly focusing on optimization problems—finding maximum or minimum values. We learn to apply the concept of a parabola's vertex to determine optimal conditions for projectile motion, maximizing area, revenue, or product, and solving general number and geometry problems, following a systematic five-step approach.

Key Concepts

  • Vertex as Maximum/Minimum: The vertex of a parabola represents the highest or lowest point on the graph. For quadratic functions f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c:
    • If a > 0 (parabola opens upward), the vertex is a minimum value.
    • If a < 0 (parabola opens downward), the vertex is a maximum value.
  • Optimization Strategy: A systematic approach to solving quadratic optimization problems:
    1. Define Variables: Clearly identify the quantities involved in the problem.
    2. Write the Quadratic Function: Express the quantity to be optimized (e.g., area, height, revenue, product) as a quadratic function of one variable, often using a constraint equation to eliminate another variable.
    3. Find the Vertex: Use the vertex formula x = -b / (2a) to find the input value that optimizes the function.
    4. State the Answer with Units: Calculate the corresponding output value and any other required dimensions or quantities, including appropriate units.
    5. Verify Reasonableness: Check if the answer makes sense in the context of the problem and falls within any feasible domain restrictions.
  • Projectile Motion Model: The height h of an object launched vertically at time t is given by h(t) = -16t^2 + v_0t + h_0 (for feet/second) or h(t) = -4.9t^2 + v_0t + h_0 (for meters/second), where v_0 is the initial velocity and h_0 is the initial height.
  • Revenue Function: In business, revenue R(x) is calculated as x * p(x), where x is the number of items sold and p(x) is the unit price (which can be a function of x). These functions often turn out to be quadratic.
  • Domain Restrictions: Always consider the practical limitations of the variables in real-world problems. For instance, time and dimensions cannot be negative. The optimal value must fall within the physically meaningful domain.

Important Terms

  • Vertex: The unique turning point of a parabola, which represents either the highest (maximum) or lowest (minimum) point on the graph of a quadratic function.
  • Optimization: The mathematical process of finding the most efficient or effective way to use a situation or resource; specifically, finding the maximum or minimum value of a function.
  • Projectile Motion: The path an object takes when thrown or launched into the air and subjected only to the force of gravity (ignoring air resistance). Its height over time is modeled by a quadratic function.
  • Initial Velocity (v_0): The speed and direction (upward or downward) of an object at the moment it begins its motion. A positive v_0 indicates an upward launch.
  • Initial Height (h_0): The vertical position of an object at the starting moment (t=0) of its motion.
  • Revenue Function (R(x)): A function that calculates the total income generated from selling x units of a product, typically given by the formula R(x) = x * p(x), where p(x) is the price per unit.
  • Constraint Equation: An equation that describes a limitation or condition within a problem, often used to relate two or more variables and simplify an optimization problem into a single-variable quadratic function.
  • Feasible Domain: The set of all possible input values for a variable that are realistic, logical, and relevant within the context of a real-world problem (e.g., positive lengths, non-negative time).

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Quadratic Applications

Quadratic functions are powerful tools for modeling situations where a quantity increases up to a peak (or decreases to a trough) and then changes direction. The most significant feature for these applications is the parabola's vertex, which precisely indicates this maximum or minimum point. Understanding how to find and interpret the vertex is key to solving optimization problems.

General Strategy for Optimization Problems

Solving real-world problems that involve maximizing or minimizing a quantity using quadratic functions follows a consistent approach:

  1. Define Variables: Clearly identify all unknown quantities relevant to the problem and assign appropriate variable names (e.g., w for width, t for time, x for quantity).
  2. Formulate the Quadratic Function:
    • Determine the quantity you need to optimize (e.g., area, height, revenue, product).
    • Write an initial equation for this quantity using your defined variables.
    • Look for a "constraint" or "given" condition within the problem that relates your variables. Use this constraint to express one variable in terms of another.
    • Substitute this expression into your initial equation to transform it into a quadratic function of a single variable, typically in the form f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c.
  3. Find the Vertex:
    • Once you have the quadratic function, use the vertex formula x_v = -b / (2a) to find the input value (e.g., x, w, t) that corresponds to the maximum or minimum.
    • Substitute this vertex input value back into your quadratic function to calculate the actual maximum or minimum output value (e.g., f(x_v), A(w_v), h(t_v)).
    • If other dimensions or quantities were involved, calculate them using the found vertex value and the constraint equation.
  4. Interpret and State the Answer:
    • Ensure your answer directly addresses the specific question asked in the problem.
    • Always include appropriate units (e.g., feet, meters, dollars, items, seconds) with your numerical answers.
  5. Check Reasonableness:
    • Critically evaluate your solution. Do the numbers make sense in the real-world context? (e.g., Can a dimension be negative? Is the time realistic?).
    • Ensure the optimal value falls within any implied or explicit domain restrictions of the problem.

Types of Applications

1. Maximizing Area

These problems commonly involve a fixed amount of material (like fencing) to enclose a shape.

  • Example (Fencing against a barn): A farmer has 200 ft of fencing for a rectangular pen against a barn.
    • Constraint: 2w + l = 200 (fencing for two widths and one length). This simplifies to l = 200 - 2w.
    • Area function: A = w * l. Substituting the constraint gives A(w) = w(200 - 2w) = -2w^2 + 200w.
    • This is a downward-opening parabola (a = -2), so the vertex yields the maximum area.
    • Vertex w = -200 / (2 * -2) = 50 ft.
    • Then, l = 200 - 2(50) = 100 ft.
    • Maximum Area = 50 * 100 = 5000 ft^2.
    • Insight: For a rectangular area with one side bounded, the optimal length parallel to the boundary is twice the width perpendicular to it.

2. Projectile Motion

This type of problem models the vertical movement of an object under gravity.

  • Model: h(t) = -16t^2 + v_0t + h_0 (when using feet for height and seconds for time).
    • Use h(t) = -4.9t^2 + v_0t + h_0 (when using meters for height and seconds for time).
    • v_0 is the initial velocity (positive for upward, zero for dropped, negative for downward).
    • h_0 is the initial height.
  • Maximum Height: This is found by calculating the vertex of the h(t) function. The t-coordinate of the vertex gives the time to reach maximum height, and the h(t) value at the vertex gives the maximum height itself.
  • Time to Hit the Ground: The object hits the ground when h(t) = 0. This involves solving a quadratic equation for t. Often, the quadratic formula t = [-b ± sqrt(b^2 - 4ac)] / (2a) is required. Always reject any negative time solutions, as time cannot be negative in this context.

3. Revenue and Profit Models

Businesses utilize quadratic functions to determine the optimal price or quantity to maximize revenue or profit.

  • Revenue Function: R(x) = x * p(x), where x is the quantity sold and p(x) is the unit price, which is often a linear demand function (e.g., p(x) = C - Mx).
  • Maximizing Revenue: Construct the R(x) function as a quadratic expression. Since the price typically decreases as quantity increases (p(x) has a negative slope), the x^2 term in R(x) will have a negative coefficient, meaning the parabola opens downward and has a maximum. Find the vertex to determine the quantity (x) that maximizes revenue and the corresponding maximum revenue R(x_vertex).

4. Number and Geometry Problems

These are versatile problems where various quantities can be optimized.

  • Example (Maximizing a Product): Find two positive numbers whose sum is S and whose product is maximum.
    • Let the numbers be x and S - x.
    • Product function: P(x) = x(S - x) = -x^2 + Sx.
    • The vertex occurs at x = -S / (2 * -1) = S/2. This implies that the two numbers are equal (S/2 and S - S/2 = S/2).
    • Insight: For a fixed sum, the product of two positive numbers is maximized when the numbers are as close to each other as possible (i.e., equal).

Important Considerations

  • Direction of Parabola: The coefficient a in ax^2 + bx + c is critical:
    • If a > 0, the parabola opens upward, and the vertex is a minimum.
    • If a < 0, the parabola opens downward, and the vertex is a maximum.
  • Domain Restrictions: Always verify that your calculated optimal values are physically or logically possible. For example, lengths, times, and quantities of items sold must typically be non-negative. If the vertex falls outside a specified or implied domain, the actual maximum or minimum value might occur at a boundary point of that feasible domain.
  • Units: Maintain consistency with units throughout the problem. Do not mix feet and meters in the same equation for projectile motion, for example.

Review Questions

  1. Describe the general five-step strategy for solving optimization problems involving quadratic functions. Why is finding the vertex a crucial step in this process, and how does the leading coefficient (a) guide your interpretation of the vertex?
  2. An object is launched straight upward from the ground (h_0 = 0) with an initial velocity of 80 ft/s.
    • a) Write the height function h(t) for this object.
    • b) When does the object reach its maximum height, and what is that maximum height?
    • c) When does the object hit the ground?
  3. A rectangular garden is to be bordered on three sides by 36 meters of fencing, with an existing wall forming the fourth side. What dimensions (length and width) will maximize the area of the garden, and what is the maximum area?
  4. A company sells x units of a product at a price p(x) = 150 - 0.25x dollars per unit.
    • a) Write the revenue function R(x) for the company.
    • b) How many units should the company sell to maximize its revenue?
    • c) What is the maximum revenue the company can achieve?
  5. Find two positive numbers whose sum is 24 such that the sum of their squares is minimized. (Hint: While the problems in the section focused on maximizing a product, you can also minimize sums of squares using quadratic functions.)
