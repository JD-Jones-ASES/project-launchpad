Relevance Fallacies: Attacking, Dodging, and Twisting - Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces students to relevance fallacies, which are faulty arguments that attempt to win a debate by introducing information or tactics that are irrelevant to the actual point being discussed. It details four common types: Ad Hominem (attacking the person), Straw Man (twisting an argument), Red Herring (changing the subject), and Tu Quoque (pointing out hypocrisy), providing examples for each to help identify these sneaky tricks in everyday arguments.

Key Concepts

  • Relevance Fallacies: A category of informal fallacies where the "evidence" or tactic used to support a conclusion is not logically connected to the conclusion itself; it's irrelevant to the actual issue at hand. These arguments try to win by distracting, attacking, or misrepresenting.
  • Ad Hominem (Attacking the Person): An argument that dismisses someone's position by attacking their character, motives, or other personal attributes, rather than addressing the substance of their argument. The quality of an argument is independent of the person making it.
  • Straw Man (Twisting Someone's Words): Occurs when someone misrepresents or exaggerates an opponent's argument to make it sound weaker or more extreme than it actually is, then attacks that distorted version, rather than the original argument.
  • Red Herring (Changing the Subject): A fallacy that introduces an unrelated topic to distract from the original issue being discussed. The new topic, while potentially interesting, serves only to divert attention and avoid engaging with the actual argument.
  • Tu Quoque ("You Also" or "You Do It Too!"): A specific type of ad hominem fallacy where one attempts to deflect criticism by pointing out that the person making the criticism is guilty of the same fault. It wrongly assumes that a hypocrite cannot make a valid argument.

Important Terms

  • Ad Hominem: Latin for “to the person.” A fallacy that attacks the person making an argument instead of addressing the argument itself.
  • Straw Man: A fallacy that misrepresents someone's argument to make it easier to attack, like knocking down a man made of straw instead of fighting a real opponent.
  • Red Herring: A fallacy that introduces an unrelated topic to distract from the original argument, named after the practice of using smoked fish to throw hunting dogs off a scent.
  • Sophists: A group of traveling teachers in ancient Greece (over 2,400 years ago) who taught people how to win arguments, often using clever but dishonest reasoning, regardless of the truth.
  • Sophistry: A term that still means "clever but dishonest reasoning" today, derived from the practices of the Sophists.
  • Tu Quoque: Latin for “you also.” A fallacy that deflects criticism by pointing out that the other person does the same thing, essentially saying, "you do it too!"

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Relevance Fallacies

  • What they are: Bad arguments that try to win by introducing information that has nothing to do with the actual point. The "evidence" is irrelevant and doesn't logically connect to the conclusion.
  • Common tactics: Making fun of someone, changing the subject, or twisting someone's words instead of responding to the actual argument.

Ad Hominem — Attacking the Person

  • Definition: An attack on the person making the argument, rather than on the argument itself. (Latin for "to the person").
  • Goal: To make you believe that because the person is "bad" (or flawed in some way), their argument must also be bad.
  • Core principle: An argument's validity depends on its logic and evidence, not on who is making it. Even a person with flaws can make a sound argument, and a respected person can make a weak one.
  • Examples:
    • School: Jake dismisses Maya's suggestion for more passing time by pointing out her late attendance record. Maya's punctuality is irrelevant to the merits of her proposal.
    • Sports: Deshawn rejects Carlos's advice to practice free throws more because Carlos himself missed free throws. Carlos's personal performance doesn't invalidate the team's low free-throw percentage or his suggestion.
  • Disguises of Ad Hominem:
    • Direct attack: Insulting the arguer ("You're just a kid — what do you know?").
    • Questioning motives: Suggesting ulterior motives ("You only say that because you want to be class president.").
    • Guilt by association: Dismissing an argument based on negative associations ("Your older brother got in trouble, so I don't trust your opinion either.").
  • Key takeaway: Always focus on the argument itself, not the arguer.

Straw Man — Twisting Someone's Words

  • Definition: Misrepresenting an opponent's argument to make it easier to attack. Instead of responding to the actual argument, one attacks a weaker, distorted version.
  • Sneakiness: This fallacy can be subtle, as the person using it might genuinely believe they are responding fairly. They often exaggerate or slightly alter the original argument.
  • Mechanism: Take the real argument, distort it (make it extreme, simplified, or completely different), then easily "defeat" the distorted version, pretending to have defeated the original.
  • Examples:
    • Family Debate: Aisha asks to stay up an hour later on weekends. Her dad twists this into "no bedtime at all" and easily rejects that extreme position.
    • Social Media: A post suggests more vegetarian options. The reply distorts this into "forcing everyone to stop eating meat," and attacks that fabricated claim.
  • Differentiation (from Red Herring): A straw man changes the argument itself by distorting it, whereas a red herring changes the entire topic.

Red Herring — Changing the Subject

  • Definition: Introducing an unrelated topic to distract from the original argument.
  • Origin: Named after the old trick of dragging a strongly scented smoked fish across a trail to confuse hunting dogs.
  • Effectiveness: The distracting topic might seem important or interesting, but if it doesn't address the original question, it's irrelevant and serves only to divert attention.
  • Example:
    • In Class: A student, asked about missing homework, discusses international homework policies instead. While an interesting topic, it avoids the original question about their missing assignment.
  • Historical Context (Did You Know):
    • Sophists: Ancient Greek teachers (over 2,400 years ago) who taught people how to win arguments, even when wrong, often using tricks like straw man and red herring.
    • Socrates: Argued against the Sophists, emphasizing that finding the truth is more important than merely winning.
    • Sophistry: Today, the word still refers to "clever but dishonest reasoning."

Tu Quoque — “You Do It Too!”

  • Definition: A fallacy that deflects criticism by pointing out that the other person is guilty of the same thing. (Latin for "you also").
  • Mechanism: It's a "you too" defense, an argument version of pointing a finger back.
  • Problem: The hypocrisy of the accuser does not make their argument or criticism invalid. The truth of a statement is independent of the speaker's actions.
  • Example:
    • At Home: You are criticized for phone use and respond by pointing out your mom's phone use. Even if your mom is also on her phone a lot, her argument about your screen time might still be valid.
  • Why it's tempting: It feels satisfying to expose hypocrisy.
  • Important reminder: Whether someone is a hypocrite or not, the real question is whether their argument is logical and makes sense. A doctor who smokes is still correct that smoking is unhealthy.

Review Questions

  1. Define relevance fallacies and explain why they are considered "bad arguments" in the context of logical reasoning. What is the fundamental problem with using irrelevant information?
  2. How can you distinguish between an ad hominem fallacy (attacking the person) and a situation where someone's character or past actions are genuinely relevant to their credibility in a specific argument (e.g., questioning an expert's opinion if they have a known conflict of interest)? Provide an example of each.
  3. Explain the difference between a straw man fallacy and a red herring fallacy. Why might someone confuse these two, and what is the key distinction to remember?
  4. Imagine your friend tells you, "You need to stop procrastinating on your schoolwork." You respond by saying, "But you procrastinated on your big history project last week!" Which fallacy are you using, and why does your friend's past behavior not invalidate their advice to you?
  5. In everyday conversations, online debates, or even political discourse, why do you think people often resort to relevance fallacies instead of addressing the core argument directly? What are some potential psychological reasons behind these "sneaky tricks"?
