Chapter 4, Section 2: Truth Tables

Overview

This section introduces truth tables as a fundamental tool in propositional logic to precisely determine the truth value of simple and compound statements. It explains how to construct truth tables for the four main logical connectives (NOT, AND, OR, IF THEN) and provides a method for evaluating more complex, compound statements using an order of operations. Finally, it explores related conditional statements: the converse, inverse, and contrapositive, highlighting their logical relationships with the original conditional.

Key Concepts

  • Truth Tables: Charts that systematically show every possible combination of truth values for propositions involved in a statement and determine the resulting truth value of the statement. They are essential for logical precision.
  • Logical Connectives: The basic operations used to combine or modify propositions.
    • NOT ($\sim$): A unary connective that reverses the truth value of a single proposition.
    • AND ($\land$): A binary connective that is true only when both propositions it connects are true; otherwise, it is false.
    • OR ($\lor$): A binary connective (inclusive OR) that is true if at least one of the propositions it connects is true; it is false only when both propositions are false.
    • IF THEN ($\to$): A binary connective (conditional) that is false only when the "if" part (antecedent) is true and the "then" part (consequent) is false. In all other cases, it is true, representing a "broken promise" scenario.
  • Order of Operations for Logic: A rule set for evaluating compound statements, similar to mathematical order of operations: Parentheses first, then NOT, then AND, then OR, then IF THEN.
  • Compound Statements: Logical statements formed by combining two or more simple propositions using connectives, which require step-by-step evaluation using truth tables.
  • Tautology: A statement that is always true, regardless of the truth values of its individual propositions. Modus Ponens, when written as a single formula, is an example of a tautology.
  • Related Conditional Statements: For a conditional $P \to Q$:
    • Converse ($Q \to P$): Switches the antecedent and consequent. Not logically equivalent to the original.
    • Inverse ($\sim P \to \sim Q$): Negates both the antecedent and consequent. Not logically equivalent to the original.
    • Contrapositive ($\sim Q \to \sim P$): Switches and negates both the antecedent and consequent. Logically equivalent to the original conditional.

Important Terms

  • Truth Table: A tabular display showing all possible truth values for a simple or compound logical statement, given every possible combination of truth values for its constituent simple propositions.
  • Proposition: A declarative sentence that is either true or false, but not both.
  • Connective: A logical operator (like AND, OR, NOT, IF THEN) that joins or modifies propositions to form more complex statements.
  • Conditional Statement ($P \to Q$): An "if-then" statement where $P$ is the antecedent (the "if" part) and $Q$ is the consequent (the "then" part).
  • Tautology: A compound statement that is always true, no matter what the truth values of its simple component propositions are.
  • Converse: For a conditional $P \to Q$, its converse is $Q \to P$.
  • Inverse: For a conditional $P \to Q$, its inverse is $\sim P \to \sim Q$.
  • Contrapositive: For a conditional $P \to Q$, its contrapositive is $\sim Q \to \sim P$.
  • Logically Equivalent: Two statements are logically equivalent if they always have the same truth value in every possible scenario (i.e., their truth tables are identical).
  • Boolean Algebra: A system of algebra developed by George Boole that uses symbols for logical operations (AND, OR, NOT) to represent logical statements mathematically, forming the basis for computer logic.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Truth Tables

A truth table is a powerful tool to make the meaning of logical connectives precise. It systematically lists every possible combination of "true" (T) and "false" (F) for the basic propositions in a statement and then shows the resulting truth value of the entire statement for each combination. For a statement with one proposition, there are 2 rows; for two propositions, there are $2^2 = 4$ rows; for three, $2^3 = 8$ rows, and so on.

Truth Table for NOT ($\sim$)

The NOT connective (also written as $\neg$) is the simplest, as it only involves one proposition. It flips the truth value.

$P$ $\sim P$
T F
F T

Truth Table for AND ($\land$)

The AND connective combines two propositions. For an "AND" statement to be true, both of its parts must be true. If even one part is false, the entire statement is false.

$P$ $Q$ $P \land Q$
T T T
T F F
F T F
F F F

Truth Table for OR ($\lor$)

The OR connective (inclusive OR) also combines two propositions. An "OR" statement is true if at least one of its parts is true. It is false only when both parts are false. This "inclusive" meaning means "either P, or Q, or both."

$P$ $Q$ $P \lor Q$
T T T
T F T
F T T
F F F

Truth Table for IF THEN ($\to$)

The conditional statement "$P \to Q$" (If $P$, then $Q$) is often the trickiest. Its truth table reflects the idea of a "broken promise." A conditional statement is only false in one specific scenario: when the "if" part ($P$, the antecedent) is true, but the "then" part ($Q$, the consequent) is false. In all other cases, the promise isn't broken, so the conditional is considered true.

Let's break down the "broken promise" analogy: "If you score 90+, then you get an A."

  • Row 1 (T $\to$ T): You score 95 (true P) and get an A (true Q). Promise kept! The conditional is True.
  • Row 2 (T $\to$ F): You score 95 (true P) but get a B (false Q). Promise broken! The conditional is False.
  • Row 3 (F $\to$ T): You score 80 (false P) but still get an A (true Q). The teacher was generous, but they didn't break the original promise (which only applied to 90+ scores). No promise was broken. The conditional is True.
  • Row 4 (F $\to$ F): You score 80 (false P) and get a B (false Q). Again, the promise didn't apply. No promise was broken. The conditional is True.
$P$ $Q$ $P \to Q$
T T T
T F F
F T T
F F T

Building Truth Tables for Compound Statements

When a statement involves multiple connectives, you build its truth table in stages, following a specific order of operations:

  1. Parentheses: Evaluate statements inside parentheses first, starting from the innermost.
  2. NOT ($\sim$): Evaluate any negations.
  3. AND ($\land$): Evaluate conjunctions.
  4. OR ($\lor$): Evaluate disjunctions.
  5. IF THEN ($\to$): Evaluate conditionals last.

Example: Truth table for $\sim P \land Q$ You need a helper column for $\sim P$ before you can combine it with $Q$ using AND.

$P$ $Q$ $\sim P$ $\sim P \land Q$
T T F F
T F F F
F T T T
F F T F

Example: Truth table for $P \land (P \to Q)$ First, evaluate the expression in parentheses $(P \to Q)$, then combine that result with $P$ using AND.

$P$ $Q$ $P \to Q$ $P \land (P \to Q)$
T T T T
T F F F
F T T F
F F T F

Notice that for $P \land (P \to Q)$, the result is only true in Row 1. If we consider the statement $[P \land (P \to Q)] \to Q$, its truth table would show 'T' in every row. A statement that is always true, regardless of the truth values of its components, is called a tautology. This specific tautology represents modus ponens, a fundamental rule of inference.

The Converse, Inverse, and Contrapositive

For any conditional statement $P \to Q$, there are three related conditional statements:

  • Original Conditional: $P \to Q$ ("If it rains, then the ground is wet.")
  • Converse: $Q \to P$ ("If the ground is wet, then it rains.")
    • The converse is not logically equivalent to the original. The ground could be wet for other reasons (e.g., a sprinkler).
  • Inverse: $\sim P \to \sim Q$ ("If it doesn't rain, then the ground isn't wet.")
    • The inverse is not logically equivalent to the original. The ground could be wet from a sprinkler even if it doesn't rain.
  • Contrapositive: $\sim Q \to \sim P$ ("If the ground isn't wet, then it didn't rain.")
    • The contrapositive is always logically equivalent to the original conditional. If the ground isn't wet, then it's impossible for it to have rained (because rain would have made it wet). Their truth tables will always match row for row.

Logic Legend: George Boole (1815–1864)

George Boole, a self-taught English mathematician, revolutionized logic by demonstrating that logical operations like AND, OR, and NOT could be expressed using mathematical equations. His system, detailed in his 1854 book, is known as Boolean algebra. This groundbreaking work forms the theoretical foundation for all modern digital computing and electronics, enabling the design of circuits and software that power our technological world.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the purpose of a truth table. How many rows would a truth table have for a statement with three distinct propositions ($P, Q, R$)?
  2. Describe the conditions under which an "AND" statement ($P \land Q$) is true, and the conditions under which an "OR" statement ($P \lor Q$) is true. Provide a simple example for each.
  3. The conditional statement "$P \to Q$" is often considered the trickiest. Using the "broken promise" analogy, explain why $P \to Q$ is true when $P$ is false, regardless of $Q$'s truth value.
  4. Given the statement "If I study hard, then I will pass the exam" ($S \to E$):
    • Write out its converse, inverse, and contrapositive in English.
    • Which of these three statements is logically equivalent to the original, and why?
  5. Construct a truth table for the compound statement $\sim (P \lor Q)$. What simple statement does this truth table match? (Hint: compare it to the truth tables for $\sim P$, $\sim Q$, and $\sim P \land \sim Q$, or $\sim P \lor \sim Q$).
