Understanding Arguments: Premises and Conclusions

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concept of an "argument" in logic, distinguishing it from common misunderstandings. It defines an argument as a set of statements where reasons (premises) are provided to support a claim (conclusion), and teaches how to identify these components, often with the help of signal words.

Key Concepts

  • Logical Argument vs. Disagreement: In logic, an argument is not a quarrel but a structured way of presenting reasons to support a claim. It's about convincing through rational support, not emotional conflict.
  • Premise: A statement offered as a reason, evidence, or justification for believing something else. Arguments can have one or many premises.
  • Conclusion: The main statement or claim that the premises are intended to support or prove. Every argument has exactly one conclusion.
  • Argument Structure: An argument is composed of at least one premise and exactly one conclusion. Think of premises as the "fuel" or "reasons," and the conclusion as the "destination" or "claim."
  • Signal Words: Specific words or phrases that often indicate whether a statement is a premise or a conclusion. They act like road signs, guiding you through the argument's structure.
  • Identifying Conclusions (without signals): If signal words are absent, ask yourself, "Which statement is the author trying to get me to believe?" That statement is the conclusion, and the others are premises.

Important Terms

  • Argument: In logic, a set of statements in which one or more statements (premises) are offered as reasons to believe another statement (the conclusion).
  • Premise: A statement provided as a reason or piece of evidence to support the conclusion of an argument.
  • Conclusion: The statement in an argument that the premises are intended to support or prove; it is the main claim being made.

Detailed Notes

What is an Argument (in Logic)?

Many people confuse "argument" with a fight or disagreement. However, in logic, an argument is a precise tool: it's a collection of statements where some statements (the reasons) are put forward to justify believing another statement (the claim). It's a structured way to persuade or explain why a particular belief is true.

The Parts of an Argument: Premises and Conclusions

Every logical argument has two essential types of statements:

  1. Premise: These are the foundational statements. They serve as the reasons, evidence, or support for the argument's main point. An argument can have one, two, three, or even more premises.
    • Example: In the argument "All dogs are animals. Buddy is a dog. Therefore, Buddy is an animal," the statements "All dogs are animals" and "Buddy is a dog" are the premises.
  2. Conclusion: This is the central claim or point that the argument is trying to establish. It's the statement that the premises are supposed to make you believe. Critically, every argument has exactly one conclusion.
    • Example: In the argument above, "Buddy is an animal" is the conclusion.

Think of an argument like a "little machine": you input reasons (premises), and it produces a claim (conclusion).

Structure of an Argument

A logical argument must always contain:

  • At least one premise.
  • Exactly one conclusion.

It's common for arguments to have multiple premises working together to support that single conclusion.

Identifying Premises and Conclusions: Signal Words

One of the most helpful ways to distinguish premises from conclusions is by looking for signal words. These words act like signposts, indicating whether a statement is a reason or the main claim.

  • Premise Signal Words: These words often introduce a reason or evidence.

    • because
    • since
    • Example from text: "You should trade me your chips because I gave you my cookie yesterday, and fair is fair." (Here, "because" signals that "I gave you my cookie yesterday" and "fair is fair" are premises.)
    • Example from text: "We should have pizza every Friday since it's the most popular food... and since students perform better..." (Here, "since" signals the reasons for having pizza every Friday.)

  • Conclusion Signal Words: These words often introduce the main point or the claim being argued for.

    • therefore
    • Example from text: "All dogs are animals. Buddy is a dog. Therefore, Buddy is an animal." (Here, "Therefore" signals that "Buddy is an animal" is the conclusion.)

Important Note: Not all arguments will explicitly use signal words. Sometimes, you need to infer the structure.

Identifying the Conclusion Without Signal Words

If you don't see any obvious signal words, a good strategy is to ask yourself: "Which statement is the author or speaker trying to convince me to believe?" That statement is almost always the conclusion. All other statements that support it are the premises.

Historical Context

The word "argument" originates from the Latin "argumentum," meaning "evidence" or "proof." This historical root reinforces the idea that an argument in logic is about providing reasons and proof, rather than a confrontation.

Practice Example Breakdown

Consider the student council debate: "We should have pizza every Friday since it's the most popular food in the cafeteria survey, and since students perform better when they're happy about lunch."

  • Conclusion: "We should have pizza every Friday." (This is the claim the speaker wants to persuade others to accept.)
  • Premise 1: "Pizza is the most popular food in the cafeteria survey." (Signaled by "since")
  • Premise 2: "Students perform better when they're happy about lunch." (Signaled by "since")

Review Questions

  1. What is the primary difference between how the word "argument" is used in everyday conversation versus its specific meaning in logic?
  2. Describe the two main components of a logical argument. How many of each must an argument contain?
  3. Explain the role of "signal words" in identifying the parts of an argument. Provide an example of a word that signals a premise and a word that signals a conclusion based on the section content.
  4. If an argument does not contain any obvious signal words, what mental trick can you use to reliably identify its conclusion?
  5. Construct a simple two-premise argument (like convincing a friend) and clearly label its premises and conclusion.
