What Logic Is (and Isn't): A Study Guide

Overview

This section defines logic as the study of good reasoning and distinguishes it from three things students commonly confuse it with: math, debate, and opinions. Logic is about the structure of reasoning — whether the reasons someone gives actually support their conclusion — and it applies to every topic imaginable.

Key Concepts

  • Logic Defined: The study of good reasoning. It gives us tools to figure out whether an argument makes sense — whether the reasons someone gives actually support what they're trying to prove.
  • Logic Is Not Math: Logic and math are cousins, but you don't need to be good at math to be good at logic. Only basic counting is needed for this book.
  • Logic Is Not Debate: Debate is about persuading people. Logic is about figuring out if reasoning actually works, whether or not anyone is persuaded. You can win a debate with a terrible argument if you're charming enough.
  • Logic Is Not About Opinions: Logic helps us see when an opinion is well-supported by reasons and when it's just a feeling. "I like chocolate ice cream" is an opinion; "Chocolate ice cream is the best because it won a taste test" is an argument.
  • The Big Idea: Logic is about the structure of reasoning, not any particular topic. It works everywhere — science, history, sports, video games, or lunch.
  • Logic as a Ruler: A ruler doesn't care what you're measuring; it just helps you measure accurately. Logic is like a ruler for your thinking.

Important People

Aristotle (c. 384–322 BC) : Greek philosopher who wrote the first books about logic around 350 BC. Logic is over 2,300 years old.

Important Terms

Logic : The study of good reasoning — the tools for figuring out whether an argument makes sense.

Detailed Notes

Three Things Logic Is NOT

  1. Not math — They are cousins (both involve careful thinking and rules), but logic requires only basic counting.
  2. Not debate — Debate is about persuasion. Logic evaluates whether reasoning works, regardless of charm.
  3. Not opinions — Opinions are personal preferences. Logic evaluates arguments, which use reasons to support claims.

Everyday Logic

You use logic every day: explaining why you deserve a later bedtime, figuring out who ate the last cookie based on clues, deciding whether a school rumor is probably true.

Historical Note

Aristotle wrote the first logic books around 350 BC — older than the Roman Empire, older than the Great Wall of China.
