Chapter 7, Section 2: Analogies Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces analogies as a powerful form of reasoning, not just a literary device. It explains how to construct an argument by analogy and, critically, how to distinguish between strong and weak analogies. The core idea is that an analogy's strength depends on the relevance and number of similarities compared to any important differences related to the conclusion.

Key Concepts

  • Reasoning by Analogy: A method of inductive reasoning where similarities between two things are used to infer further similarity. If Thing A and Thing B are alike in several known ways, and Thing A has property X, then Thing B probably also has property X.
  • Strength of an Analogy: Analogies can be strong or weak. A strong analogy provides a reasonable basis for its conclusion, while a weak one does not.
  • Relevant vs. Irrelevant Similarities: The key to evaluating an analogy. A relevant similarity is a shared feature that directly impacts the conclusion being drawn. An irrelevant similarity is a shared feature that has no bearing on the conclusion.
  • Evaluation Checklist: A systematic approach to determine an analogy's strength, considering the number of relevant similarities, the presence of important differences, and the boldness of the conclusion.

Important Terms

  • Analogy: A comparison between two things that are alike in some ways. In an argument by analogy, we reason that because two things are similar in known ways, they are probably similar in some further way.
  • Relevant Similarity: A way in which two things are alike that actually matters for the conclusion being drawn.
  • Irrelevant Similarity: A shared feature that has nothing to do with the conclusion.

Detailed Notes

What is an Analogy?

Analogies are comparisons used to explain something complex by relating it to something simpler or more familiar (e.g., "The heart is like a pump"). Beyond explanation, analogies are a fundamental form of reasoning, enabling us to make educated guesses or predictions based on observed similarities.

The Basic Pattern of an Argument by Analogy

An argument by analogy follows a clear inductive pattern:

  1. Premise 1: Thing A and Thing B are similar in ways 1, 2, and 3 (known similarities).
  2. Premise 2: Thing A also has property X.
  3. Conclusion: Therefore, Thing B probably has property X too.

Example: The New Restaurant If you loved an Italian restaurant for its great pasta, friendly staff, and cozy atmosphere (Thing A), and hear about a new Italian restaurant (Thing B) with great pasta and friendly staff (similarities 1 & 2), you might conclude it probably has a cozy atmosphere too (property X). This is a reasonable guess, though not guaranteed.

Strong vs. Weak Analogies

The core question when evaluating an analogy is: Are the similarities relevant to the conclusion?

  • Strong Analogy: When the similarities between the compared items are relevant and numerous, and there are few significant differences that undermine the conclusion.
  • Weak Analogy: When the similarities are irrelevant, superficial, or outweighed by important differences related to the conclusion.

Relevant vs. Irrelevant Similarities

  • Relevant Similarity: A similarity that logically connects to or supports the conclusion. It matters for the inference being made.
  • Irrelevant Similarity: A similarity that does not logically connect to the conclusion. It's a shared feature, but it doesn't help establish the likelihood of the inferred property.

Example: School Is Like a Factory — Or Is It? An argument states: "Schools are like factories. Both have schedules, supervisors, and rules. Factories produce products. Therefore, schools produce products (educated students)."

  • Similarities: Schedules, supervisors (teachers), rules. These are factual similarities.
  • Relevance Check: Do these similarities mean schools "produce products" in the same way factories do? Factories produce identical, interchangeable items. Schools foster the learning and growth of unique individuals. The purpose and nature of the "product" are vastly different.
  • Verdict: This analogy is weak because the shared features (schedules, rules) are superficial and irrelevant to the core claim about "producing products." The fundamental differences in purpose and process undermine the conclusion.

Checklist for Evaluating Analogies

To systematically assess the strength of an analogy, consider these points:

  1. Number of Relevant Similarities: The more ways the two things are alike that matter to the conclusion, the stronger the analogy.
  2. Important Differences: Are there significant differences between the two things that could negate the conclusion? Major differences in critical areas weaken the analogy. No analogy is perfect, but the differences shouldn't outweigh the relevant similarities.
  3. Boldness of the Conclusion: A modest conclusion ("it might have property X") requires less support than a sweeping, certain conclusion ("it definitely has property X"). Be wary of conclusions that claim certainty from an analogy.

Example: Video Game Analogy "Learning basketball is like learning a video game. Both require practice, have rules, get easier over time, and are fun with friends. If you got better at video games by daily practice, you'll improve at basketball similarly."

  • Evaluation: The similarities (practice, rules, gradual improvement) are highly relevant to the conclusion about getting better through consistent practice. The primary difference (physical vs. digital) doesn't diminish the connection between daily practice and skill acquisition. This is a fairly strong analogy for that specific conclusion.

Sherlock Holmes and Analogical Reasoning

Fictional detectives like Sherlock Holmes, while famous for "deduction," often employ inductive and analogical reasoning. Holmes constantly compared new cases or clues to previous ones ("This case reminds me of the affair of the Twisted Lip"), inferring similar explanations from similar evidence. This highlights how analogies are used to draw conclusions based on past patterns and resemblances, making them a practical tool for problem-solving.

Practical Application

Analogies are everywhere, from daily explanations to advertisements (e.g., a car compared to a cheetah to emphasize speed and power). It's crucial to critically evaluate the relevant similarities a comparison highlights and the important differences it might hide.

Review Questions

  1. What is an analogy, and how does it differ from a simple comparison? Provide an original example of an argument by analogy.
  2. Explain the difference between a relevant similarity and an irrelevant similarity in the context of an analogy. Why is this distinction crucial for evaluating an analogy's strength?
  3. Analyze the "School Is Like a Factory" analogy. What makes it weak, despite having some factual similarities?
  4. Imagine someone argues: "Working out is like charging your phone. Both require regular connection to a power source (food/electricity), both replenish energy, and both can be overcharged or undercharged. So, if you charge your phone daily, you should work out daily for optimal performance." Evaluate this analogy using the checklist provided in the text.
  5. Why is it important to consider "how bold the conclusion is" when evaluating an analogy? Give an example of a modest conclusion versus a bold one based on the same set of similarities.
