Syllogisms: Common Valid and Invalid Patterns Study Guide

Overview

This section delves into the core mechanics of syllogisms, teaching how to identify the three essential terms (major, minor, and middle) and differentiate between valid and invalid argument structures. You will learn to recognize classic valid patterns like Barbara and Celarent, and critically, how to spot common logical fallacies such as the undistributed middle, illicit major, and illicit minor, understanding that validity hinges purely on structure, not the truthfulness of the conclusion.

Key Concepts

  • Three Terms of a Syllogism: Every syllogism involves exactly three categories, each serving a specific role: the major term (predicate of the conclusion), the minor term (subject of the conclusion), and the middle term (the link appearing in both premises but not the conclusion). The middle term is crucial for connecting the other two.
  • Validity vs. Truth: A syllogism's validity refers to its logical structure, meaning if the premises are true, the conclusion must also be true. This is distinct from the actual truth of the premises or conclusion. A valid argument can have a false conclusion if one or more premises are false.
  • Common Valid Patterns: These are structures where the conclusion necessarily follows from the premises.
    • Barbara: The most famous valid pattern (All M are P, All S are M, Therefore All S are P).
    • Celarent: Another important valid pattern (No M are P, All S are M, Therefore No S are P).
  • Distribution of Terms: A term is distributed if the statement refers to every member of that category. Understanding distribution is key to identifying invalid patterns.
  • Common Invalid Patterns (Fallacies): These are structural flaws that prevent the conclusion from necessarily following, even if the premises seem true.
    • Undistributed Middle: Occurs when the middle term is not distributed in either premise, failing to make a proper connection between the major and minor terms.
    • Illicit Major/Minor: Occurs when the major or minor term is distributed in the conclusion but not distributed in its respective premise, meaning the conclusion makes a claim about a broader group than the premises justify.
  • Rules for Valid Syllogisms: A set of criteria that, if violated, indicate an invalid syllogism. These include requirements for the middle term's distribution and restrictions on negative or particular premises.
  • Venn Diagrams: A powerful visual tool for testing the validity of any syllogism by diagramming the premises and observing if the conclusion is thereby forced into existence.

Important Terms

  • Major Term: The predicate (second category) of the conclusion in a syllogism.
  • Minor Term: The subject (first category) of the conclusion in a syllogism.
  • Middle Term: The category that appears in both premises of a syllogism but not in the conclusion; it acts as the "glue" connecting the major and minor terms.
  • Valid Syllogism: An argument where, if the premises are true, the conclusion must logically be true. Validity is about structure, not the actual truth of statements.
  • Invalid Syllogism: An argument where the conclusion does not necessarily follow from the premises, even if the premises were true.
  • Distributed Term: A term in a categorical statement that refers to every single member of the category it represents.
  • Undistributed Term: A term in a categorical statement that refers only to some members of its category, or does not make a claim about the entire group.
  • Undistributed Middle (Fallacy): A common invalid pattern where the middle term is not distributed in at least one of the premises, preventing it from effectively connecting the major and minor terms.
  • Illicit Major (Fallacy): An invalid pattern where the major term is distributed in the conclusion but is not distributed in the major premise.
  • Illicit Minor (Fallacy): An invalid pattern where the minor term is distributed in the conclusion but is not distributed in the minor premise.
  • Barbara: A classic, universally valid syllogistic pattern: All M are P; All S are M; Therefore, All S are P.
  • Celarent: Another classic, universally valid syllogistic pattern: No M are P; All S are M; Therefore, No S are P.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Syllogisms

Having learned about the four forms of categorical statements (A, E, I, O) and how to represent them with Venn diagrams, we now apply these skills to evaluate the logical structure of syllogisms. A syllogism is a deductive argument consisting of two premises and a conclusion. Our goal is to determine if the conclusion is guaranteed by the premises.

The Three Terms

Every syllogism has exactly three unique categories, each playing a specific role:

  • Major Term (P): The predicate of the conclusion.
  • Minor Term (S): The subject of the conclusion.
  • Middle Term (M): The category that appears in both premises but not in the conclusion. It serves as the bridge connecting the major and minor terms.

Example:

  1. All humans are mortal.
  2. Socrates is a human.
  3. Therefore, Socrates is mortal.
    • Major Term (P): mortal (predicate of the conclusion)
    • Minor Term (S): Socrates (subject of the conclusion)
    • Middle Term (M): human (appears in both premises, connects Socrates and mortal)

The middle term is vital. If its connection between the major and minor terms is flawed, the syllogism will be invalid.

Common Valid Patterns

Medieval scholars named valid syllogistic patterns to aid memorization. The vowels in these names indicate the forms of the three statements (e.g., 'A' for "All," 'E' for "No," 'I' for "Some," 'O' for "Some...not").

Valid Pattern: Barbara

This is the most famous and fundamental valid pattern.

  • Pattern:
    1. All M are P. (All middle are major)
    2. All S are M. (All minor are middle)
    3. Therefore, all S are P. (All minor are major)
  • Example (Barbara in Action):
    1. All mammals (M) are warm-blooded (P).
    2. All dogs (S) are mammals (M).
    3. Therefore, all dogs (S) are warm-blooded (P).
  • Venn Diagram Test:
    1. Draw three overlapping circles for S, M, P.
    2. For "All M are P," shade the part of the M circle that is outside the P circle.
    3. For "All S are M," shade the part of the S circle that is outside the M circle.
    4. Observe the conclusion: Is the part of the S circle that is outside the P circle shaded? Yes. This shows the conclusion "All S are P" is forced by the premises, confirming validity.

Valid Pattern: Celarent

Another important valid pattern, with statements of forms E, A, E.

  • Pattern:
    1. No M are P. (No middle are major)
    2. All S are M. (All minor are middle)
    3. Therefore, no S are P. (No minor are major)
  • Example (Celarent in Action):
    1. No reptiles (M) are mammals (P).
    2. All snakes (S) are reptiles (M).
    3. Therefore, no snakes (S) are mammals (P).
  • Venn Diagram Test:
    1. Draw three overlapping circles for S, M, P.
    2. For "No M are P," shade the overlapping area between M and P.
    3. For "All S are M," shade the part of the S circle that is outside the M circle.
    4. Observe the conclusion: Is the overlapping area between S and P completely shaded? Yes. This shows "No S are P" is forced, confirming validity.

Invalid Patterns: The Role of Distribution

To identify invalid patterns, we need to understand distribution.

Definition: Distributed and Undistributed Terms

  • A term is distributed if the statement says something about every member of that category.
  • A term is undistributed if the statement only says something about some members or doesn't cover the whole group.

Quick Guide to Distribution:

  • "All A are B" (A-form): A is distributed (we talk about every A); B is undistributed (we only know some B are A).
  • "No A are B" (E-form): Both A and B are distributed (every A is excluded from B, and every B is excluded from A).
  • "Some A are B" (I-form): Neither A nor B is distributed.
  • "Some A are not B" (O-form): A is undistributed; B is distributed (we're saying A is completely outside of B for at least one A).

Invalid Pattern: The Undistributed Middle

This is the most common structural error.

  • Rule: The middle term must be distributed at least once in the premises. If it isn't, the syllogism is invalid.
  • Example (The Undistributed Middle Trap):
    1. All dogs are animals. (M is 'animals')
    2. All cats are animals. (M is 'animals')
    3. Therefore, all dogs are cats. (INVALID!)
  • Explanation:
    • In Premise 1 ("All dogs are animals"), "animals" is the predicate of an "All" statement, so it's undistributed. We only know some animals are dogs.
    • In Premise 2 ("All cats are animals"), "animals" is again the predicate of an "All" statement, so it's undistributed. We only know some animals are cats.
    • Since "animals" (the middle term) is never distributed, it fails to connect all dogs to all cats. Dogs and cats could both be inside the "animals" category without overlapping each other. The middle term fails to build a solid bridge.

Other Invalid Patterns

  • Illicit Major: The major term (P) is distributed in the conclusion but is not distributed in the major premise. You can't make a claim about all of something in the conclusion if your premises only referred to some of it.

    • Example:
      1. All dogs are animals.
      2. No cats are dogs.
      3. Therefore, no cats are animals. (INVALID!)
      • Explanation: The conclusion "no cats are animals" distributes "animals" (the major term). However, in Premise 1 ("All dogs are animals"), "animals" is the predicate of an "All" statement and is undistributed. We cannot conclude "no cats are all animals" if we only discussed "animals that are dogs" in the premise.

  • Illicit Minor: The minor term (S) is distributed in the conclusion but is not distributed in the minor premise. Similar to illicit major, you're making too strong a claim about the minor term in the conclusion.

    • Example:
      1. All basketball players are athletes.
      2. All basketball players are tall.
      3. Therefore, all athletes are tall. (INVALID!)
      • Explanation: The conclusion "all athletes are tall" distributes "athletes" (the minor term). However, in Premise 1 ("All basketball players are athletes"), "athletes" is the predicate of an "All" statement and is undistributed. We've only discussed "athletes who are basketball players," not all athletes.

Three Key Rules for Valid Syllogisms

A syllogism is invalid if any of these rules are broken:

  1. Distribution of the Middle Term: The middle term must be distributed in at least one premise. (Violating this causes the Undistributed Middle fallacy).
  2. Distribution of Major/Minor Terms: If a term (major or minor) is distributed in the conclusion, it must also be distributed in its respective premise. (Violating this causes Illicit Major or Illicit Minor fallacies).
  3. Negative Premises/Conclusion:
    • A valid syllogism cannot have two negative premises.
    • If one premise is negative, the conclusion must be negative.
    • If the conclusion is negative, one premise must be negative.
  4. Particular Premises/Conclusion:
    • A valid syllogism cannot have two particular (Some) premises.
    • If one premise is particular, the conclusion must be particular.

When in doubt, always draw a three-circle Venn diagram. It will visually reveal whether the conclusion is forced by the premises, providing a definitive answer to validity.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the role of the middle term in a syllogism. Why is its distribution in the premises so critical for an argument's validity, and what happens when it is not properly distributed?
  2. Describe what it means for a term to be "distributed" in a categorical statement. Provide an example for each of the four types of categorical statements (All A are B, No A are B, Some A are B, Some A are not B), identifying which terms are distributed and which are not.
  3. Compare and contrast the fallacies of "undistributed middle" and "illicit major." How are they similar in representing a logical flaw, and how do their specific errors differ? Provide a unique example for each.
  4. The textbook emphasizes that validity is about structure, not truth. Construct an example of a syllogism that is:
    • a) Valid, but has a false conclusion (due to a false premise).
    • b) Invalid, but has a true conclusion. Explain how each example demonstrates the distinction between validity and truth.
  5. Choose one of the valid patterns discussed (Barbara or Celarent) and one of the invalid patterns (Undistributed Middle, Illicit Major, or Illicit Minor). For each chosen pattern, create your own example. Then, for each example, briefly explain how a Venn diagram would visually confirm the validity or invalidity of the argument.
