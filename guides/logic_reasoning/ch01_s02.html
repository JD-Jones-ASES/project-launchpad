Identifying Arguments in Conversations, Stories, and News

Overview

This section teaches you how to identify arguments in everyday contexts, such as conversations, stories, and news articles. You'll learn to distinguish genuine arguments—which consist of a claim supported by reasons—from non-arguments like simple opinions, questions, commands, or descriptions. The goal is to equip you with the skills to "dig out" premises and conclusions even when they aren't explicitly labeled.

Key Concepts

  • Arguments vs. Non-Arguments: Not every statement or group of sentences is an argument. An argument must have a claim (conclusion) backed by reasons (premises).
  • Types of Non-Arguments: Statements that are not arguments include:
    • Descriptions: Simply state facts about something.
    • Questions: Seek information.
    • Commands: Give instructions.
    • Opinions without Reasons: State a personal belief or preference without providing any support for it.
  • Finding Premises and Conclusions: To identify an argument, look for a claim that someone is trying to convince you of (the conclusion) and the reasons they offer to support that claim (the premises).
  • Arguments in Everyday Life: Arguments are present in various forms of communication, including casual conversations, narratives (like stories), and news reports.
  • Lack of Signal Words: Arguments in real life rarely use explicit signal words like "premise" or "conclusion." You must infer the argumentative structure by asking: "Is a claim being made, and are reasons being offered to back it up?"
  • Aristotle, Father of Logic: An ancient Greek philosopher who was the first to systematically study argument structure, identifying premises and conclusions and setting rules for evaluating arguments.

Important Terms

  • Argument: A set of statements where one statement (the conclusion) is claimed to follow from, or be supported by, one or more other statements (the premises).
  • Premise: A statement offered as a reason or evidence in support of a conclusion.
  • Conclusion: The main claim or point that an argument is trying to establish; the statement that is being supported by the premises.
  • Non-Argument: A statement or group of statements that does not present a claim supported by reasons. This includes descriptions, questions, commands, and unsupported opinions.
  • Opinion (without reasons): A personal belief or preference stated without any supporting evidence or justification, and therefore not an argument.

Detailed Notes

The Challenge of Identifying Arguments

In real-world situations, arguments are rarely presented with clear labels. You won't hear someone say, "Here's my premise, and here's my conclusion." Instead, you need to actively listen, read, and analyze to find the underlying structure of an argument. This skill is crucial for understanding and evaluating information critically.

Not Every Statement is an Argument

Before you can identify arguments, it's essential to recognize what isn't an argument. Many sentences or groups of sentences simply serve other purposes.

  • Descriptions: They tell you about something without trying to convince you of a particular point. (e.g., "The sky is blue today.")
  • Questions: They ask for information. (e.g., "What time is it?")
  • Commands: They tell someone to do something. (e.g., "Close the door.")
  • Opinions Without Reasons: A common pitfall is mistaking every opinion for an argument. An opinion only becomes an argument when reasons are provided to support it. (e.g., "I love ice cream" is an opinion; "I love ice cream because it's sweet and refreshing" starts to become an argument.)

Example:

  • "It's raining outside, so you should bring an umbrella." (Argument) – Premise: It's raining. Conclusion: Bring an umbrella.
  • "I love pepperoni pizza." (Not an Argument) – Just an opinion without reasons.
  • "What time does the game start?" (Not an Argument) – A question.

Arguments in Everyday Conversations

Conversations are rich sources of arguments. People constantly try to persuade others, explain their choices, or justify their beliefs.

Example: The Movie Debate

  • Alex's Argument:
    • Premise 1: The new superhero movie got great reviews.
    • Premise 2: The last movie in the series was amazing.
    • Conclusion: We should watch the new superhero movie tonight.
    • Analysis: Alex provides specific reasons (reviews, previous movie's quality) to support his recommendation.
  • Jordan's Initial Statement: "I don't know. I'd rather watch a comedy."
    • Analysis: This is not an argument. Jordan states a preference but gives no reasons for it. If Jordan had said, "I'd rather watch a comedy because we've seen too many action movies lately," that would be an argument.

Arguments in Stories

Even fictional narratives can contain arguments, though sometimes they require a slight rephrasing to become clear. Characters might try to persuade others, rationalize their actions, or explain situations.

Example: The Big Bad Wolf

  • Original: "Then I'll huff, and I'll puff, and I'll blow your house in!"
    • Analysis: This is a threat, not an argument. It's a statement of intent, not a claim supported by reasons.
  • Rewritten (Argumentative): "You should let me in because it's cold outside, and since I promise not to eat you."
    • Premise 1: It's cold outside.
    • Premise 2: I promise not to eat you.
    • Conclusion: You should let me in.
    • Analysis: The wolf is now providing reasons (even if suspicious) for why the pig should let him in. This demonstrates how even simple statements can be reframed into an argumentative structure.

Arguments in the News

News articles, headlines, and reports often present arguments, though they might be embedded within factual reporting or presented as the views of others. Journalists might report on arguments made by politicians, community members, or experts.

Example: City Council Headline

  • "City Council Should Build New Park, Residents Say, Citing Lack of Green Space and Rising Childhood Obesity"
  • Residents' Argument:
    • Premise 1: There is a lack of green space.
    • Premise 2: Childhood obesity is rising.
    • Conclusion: The city council should build a new park.
    • Analysis: The headline clearly indicates that residents are making a recommendation (conclusion) and providing reasons ("citing lack of green space and rising childhood obesity") to support it.

The Father of Logic: Aristotle

The systematic study of arguments began with Aristotle (384–322 BC), an ancient Greek philosopher. He is known as the "Father of Logic" because he was the first to carefully analyze and formalize the structure of arguments. Aristotle identified that arguments consist of premises leading to a conclusion and developed rules to distinguish valid (good) arguments from invalid (bad) ones. His foundational work on logic shaped Western thought for over two millennia, making his insights relevant even today.

Review Questions

  1. What is the fundamental difference between an argument and a simple opinion, a question, or a command? Provide an example of each type of non-argument.
  2. Imagine someone says, "We should go to the beach today." How could you turn this statement into an argument by adding premises?
  3. Why is it important to recognize that arguments in everyday life often lack explicit "signal words" for premises and conclusions? How can you still identify them?
  4. Think about a recent news article or conversation you had. Can you identify an argument that was made? What were its premises and conclusion?
  5. Who was Aristotle, and what was his significant contribution to the study of arguments and logic? Why is he still considered important today?
