Study Guide: Comparing the Four Voices of Rhetoric

This study guide examines the rhetorical strategies of four distinct voices—Oliver Wendell Holmes, Smedley Butler, Woodrow Wilson, and Erich Maria Remarque—analyzing how their specific circumstances, audiences, and genres shaped their arguments regarding war.

Core Concepts of Rhetorical Analysis

Rhetorical Situation Analysis

Rhetorical situation analysis is the study of a writer’s audience, occasion, and purpose. It operates on the principle that no argument exists in a vacuum; every text is fundamentally shaped by the specific moment and context of its creation.

Audience Analysis

Audience analysis is the practice of identifying the specific individuals or groups a writer or speaker is addressing. Understanding the audience is critical because it dictates:

  • Evidence Selection: Which facts or data points the author chooses to emphasize.
  • Tone: The attitude or emotional quality of the writing.
  • Topic Avoidance: Which subjects the author chooses to ignore to maintain engagement or avoid alienating the listener.

Comparative Overview: The Four Voices

The following table synthesizes the rhetorical profiles of the four primary subjects.

Voice Medium/Genre Primary Audience Tone and Style
Oliver Wendell Holmes Oration (Speech) Sympathetic Harvard graduates and veterans. Inspirational; characterized by long, rolling sentences and classical allusions.
Smedley Butler Pamphlet Ordinary Americans unaware of corporate war profits. Provocative and shocking; uses short, punchy sentences and concrete dollar figures.
Woodrow Wilson Presidential Address Congress/Legislators and the historical record. Careful and legalistic; persuasive yet grave to reflect the weight of declaring war.
Erich Maria Remarque Novel The war generation and the younger generation. Experiential; focuses on making the incomprehensible comprehensible through narrative.

Key Rhetorical Patterns

1. The Influence of Genre on Argument

  • Holmes (Oration): Designed as a performance to inspire during ceremonial occasions.
  • Butler (Pamphlet): Designed as a weapon to provoke immediate outrage and action.
  • Wilson (Address): Designed as a formal document to persuade a specific legislative body while speaking to history.
  • Remarque (Novel): Eschews conventional argument in favor of immersive experience, letting the narrative perform the work of persuasion.

2. The Power of Silence

A careful rhetorical analysis identifies not just what is said, but what is omitted. These "silences" reveal the writer's priorities and the constraints of their rhetorical situation:

  • Holmes ignores the financial profiteering of war.
  • Butler refuses to concede that any war could be justified.
  • Wilson avoids discussing the physical horrors that constituents will face.
  • Remarque avoids offering a political opinion on the necessity of the war itself.

3. Persuasion vs. Testimony

A critical distinction exists between Remarque and the other three figures. Holmes, Butler, and Wilson provide explicit arguments utilizing a thesis and marshaled evidence to persuade. Remarque, however, acts as a witness. His "argument" is implicit; he provides testimony of "what it was like," leaving the judgment to the reader.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Define "rhetorical situation analysis" based on the text.
  2. How does the sentence structure of Smedley Butler differ from that of Oliver Wendell Holmes, and why?
  3. What was the primary goal of Boardman Robinson’s 1917 cartoon, "The Deserter"?
  4. According to the text, why does Woodrow Wilson utilize "legalistic phrasing"?
  5. In what way does Remarque use ethos, pathos, and logos differently than the other three writers?
  6. Why is "audience analysis" considered essential for evaluating a writer’s choices?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Impact of Audience on Tone: Compare and contrast how the perceived sympathy or skepticism of an audience influenced the tone of Smedley Butler and Oliver Wendell Holmes. How does a speaker’s approach change when they assume their listeners already share their values?
  2. The Rhetoric of Omission: Analyze the concept of "silence" in the works of these four authors. Select two authors and discuss what they chose not to say, explaining how these omissions served their specific rhetorical purposes.
  3. Bearing Witness vs. Making an Argument: Explore the distinction the text makes between Remarque’s testimony and the explicit arguments of Wilson, Butler, and Holmes. Is "bearing witness" through a novel a more or less effective rhetorical strategy for discussing war than a formal address or pamphlet? Support your answer using the concepts of implicit and explicit arguments.

Glossary of Key Terms

  • Audience Analysis: The process of identifying who a speaker is addressing to understand how that audience shapes the argument's tone and evidence.
  • Bearing Witness: A rhetorical mode focused on providing testimony of an experience rather than making an explicit, thesis-driven argument (associated with Remarque).
  • Genre: The category of a text (e.g., novel, pamphlet, oration) that dictates its structure and the nature of its argument.
  • Implicit Argument: An argument that is not stated directly but is understood through the depiction of experience or narrative.
  • Legalistic Phrasing: Careful, precise language often used in formal or government documents to convey gravity and persuasive weight (associated with Wilson).
  • Oration: A formal speech intended for a ceremonial occasion, often utilizing classical allusions and elevated style.
  • Rhetorical Situation Analysis: The study of how the specific circumstances—audience, occasion, and purpose—shape a piece of writing or speech.
  • The Deserter: A 1917 anti-war cartoon by Boardman Robinson depicting a firing squad aiming at Christ, published the same year Wilson asked for a declaration of war.
