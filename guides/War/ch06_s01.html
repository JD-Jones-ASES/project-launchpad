Study Guide: Rhetorical Analysis of the Four Voices

This study guide explores the comparative rhetorical analysis of four distinct historical and literary voices: Oliver Wendell Holmes, Smedley Butler, Woodrow Wilson, and Erich Maria Remarque. By examining their specific rhetorical situations—comprised of audience, occasion, and purpose—students can better understand how context shapes the construction of an argument.

Key Concepts

Rhetorical Situation Analysis

Rhetorical situation analysis is the study of a writer's audience, occasion, and purpose. It operates on the principle that no argument exists in a vacuum; rather, every text is a product of the specific moment and circumstances of its creation.

Audience Analysis

Audience analysis involves identifying the specific individuals or groups a speaker is addressing. A writer’s understanding of their audience—whether they are sympathetic, skeptical, or undecided—directly dictates several rhetorical choices, including:

  • The type of evidence emphasized.
  • The tone adopted (e.g., provocative, consoling, or legalistic).
  • The topics avoided or omitted.

Genre as a Tool of Argument

The format or "genre" of a text significantly influences its persuasive power and stylistic delivery.

  • Oration: A ceremonial performance intended to inspire.
  • Pamphlet: A provocative "weapon" designed to incite immediate action or outrage.
  • Presidential Address: A formal document that balances the need to persuade contemporary legislators with the awareness of historical record.
  • Novel: An immersive experience that uses narrative testimony rather than explicit thesis-driven argumentation.

Comparative Overview: The Four Voices

The following table summarizes the rhetorical profiles of the four primary subjects.

Speaker Genre Occasion / Context Audience Style & Strategy
Oliver Wendell Holmes Oration Harvard podium; ceremonial event. Harvard graduates and veterans (sympathetic). Long, rolling sentences; classical allusions; inspirational tone.
Smedley Butler Pamphlet Political activism; anti-corporate critique. Ordinary Americans (skeptical or unaware). Short, punchy sentences; concrete dollar figures; shocking and provocative.
Woodrow Wilson Presidential Address Address to a hushed Congress (1917). Politicians and legislators (responsible for voting). Legalistic, careful phrasing; grave and persuasive without being reckless.
Erich Maria Remarque Novel Post-war literature. The "lost generation" and the younger generation. Immersive experience; bearing witness; implicit argument via testimony.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does the audience's existing relationship with Oliver Wendell Holmes affect his rhetorical style?

    • Context: Holmes speaks to Harvard graduates and veterans who already share his values. This allows him to assume a baseline of sympathy and use an inspirational, ceremonial tone rather than a defensive one.

  2. What specific evidence does Smedley Butler utilize to convince his audience, and why?

    • Context: Butler uses "concrete dollar figures" to shock ordinary Americans into seeing the direct connection between corporate profits and the deaths of their sons.

  3. In what way does Woodrow Wilson's "legalistic phrasing" serve his rhetorical purpose?

    • Context: Wilson's phrasing reflects the "gravity" of his request for a declaration of war. He must be persuasive to politicians who must vote on the matter, ensuring he appears serious rather than reckless.

  4. What distinguishes Remarque’s approach to "argument" from the other three writers?

    • Context: Unlike the others, Remarque does not make an explicit argument with a thesis and marshaled evidence. He is "bearing witness," providing testimony of an experience and letting the reader judge the situation for themselves.

  5. What is the significance of "silence" or omission in a rhetorical text?

    • Context: Silences are revealing because they show what a writer chooses to avoid to maintain their argument—such as Wilson avoiding physical horrors or Butler refusing to concede that any war could be justified.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Rhetoric of Omission

Compare and contrast the "silences" in the works of Holmes, Butler, Wilson, and Remarque. How does what each author refuses to say—whether it be the physical horrors of war, the profit motives of conflict, or the political justifications for fighting—strengthen their specific rhetorical purpose?

2. Genre and Persuasion

Analyze how the chosen genre (oration, pamphlet, address, or novel) dictates the sentence structure and tone of the four voices. Argue whether a speaker's message is fundamentally inseparable from the medium they choose to deliver it, using the "punchy sentences" of Butler and the "rolling sentences" of Holmes as primary evidence.

3. Explicit Argument vs. Bearing Witness

Drawing on the distinction made between Wilson/Butler/Holmes and Remarque, evaluate the effectiveness of "implicit argument." Is a reader more likely to be moved by a document that "marshals evidence" (like Wilson's address) or a text that "places the reader inside an experience" (like Remarque's novel)?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Audience Analysis: The practice of identifying who a speaker is addressing and evaluating how those listeners shape the structure and tone of the argument.
  • Bearing Witness: A rhetorical mode, specifically attributed to Remarque, where the author provides testimony of an experience to allow the reader to form their own judgment rather than providing an explicit thesis.
  • Explicit Argument: A rhetorical approach that uses a clear thesis and marshals specific evidence to persuade an audience.
  • Implicit Argument: An argument that is not stated directly but is understood through narrative, testimony, or the presentation of an experience.
  • Legalistic Phrasing: The use of careful, precise, and formal language often found in presidential or official documents to convey gravity and avoid the appearance of recklessness.
  • Oration: A formal speech intended for a ceremonial occasion, often characterized by classical allusions and a performance-oriented style.
  • Rhetorical Situation Analysis: The study of the interconnected circumstances of a text’s creation, specifically its audience, occasion, and purpose.
  • The Deserter: A 1917 anti-war cartoon by Boardman Robinson depicting a firing squad taking aim at Christ, published the same year Wilson asked for a declaration of war.
