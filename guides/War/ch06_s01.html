Comparing the Four Voices Side by Side

Chapter 6 of Rhetoric Under the Microscope steps back from examining each writer on his own terms and asks a more demanding question: what can we learn by placing Holmes, Butler, Wilson, and Remarque side by side? The answer emerges in three patterns — patterns that are invisible when we read each voice in isolation but unmistakable in comparison.

Genre Shapes Argument

The genre each writer chooses is not merely a stylistic preference; it determines the kind of argument that is possible. Holmes delivers an oration — a ceremonial performance meant to inspire. His long, rolling sentences and classical allusions are suited to a Memorial Day podium, not a debate. Butler writes a pamphlet — a weapon designed to provoke outrage and action. His short, punchy sentences and concrete dollar figures target a reader who needs convincing, not consoling. Wilson delivers a presidential address — a document that must persuade legislators while simultaneously speaking to history. His careful, legalistic phrasing reflects the gravity of what he is asking. Remarque writes a novel — and notably does not argue in the conventional sense at all. He places the reader inside an experience and lets the experience do the work.

Audience Shapes Tone

Audience analysis is the practice of identifying who a writer or speaker is addressing and how that audience shapes the argument. Each of our four writers faces a different audience, and those audiences demand very different strategies. Holmes speaks to Harvard graduates, many of them veterans, who already share his values — he can assume sympathy and build on it. Butler speaks to ordinary Americans who may not yet see the connection between corporate profits and their sons' graves — he must shock them into attention. Wilson speaks to politicians who must cast a vote with enormous consequences — he must be persuasive without appearing reckless. Remarque writes for those who lived through the war and for those who did not — he must make the incomprehensible somehow comprehensible.

Silences Are as Revealing as Arguments

Perhaps the most important insight from comparative reading is this: what each writer chooses not to say is as telling as what he does say. Holmes does not discuss who profits from war. Butler does not concede that any war might be justified. Wilson does not dwell on the physical horrors his listeners' constituents will endure. Remarque does not offer a political opinion on whether the war should have been fought.

These silences reflect each writer's audience, purpose, and the limits of his position. Holmes' silence on profit protects his celebration of martial virtue from economic critique. Butler's refusal to concede any just war keeps his indictment total. Wilson's silence on bodily suffering prevents his address from becoming a deterrent rather than a call to arms. Remarque's silence on policy keeps his novel from becoming a manifesto.

Remarque: A Special Case

Remarque occupies a different category from the other three. Holmes, Butler, and Wilson are making explicit arguments — they have a thesis, they marshal evidence, they want to persuade. Remarque is bearing witness. His implicit argument, if we can call it that, is: this is what it was like; now you judge. He uses ethos, pathos, and logos — but in the service of testimony rather than persuasion. This distinction matters enormously when we analyze how the three classical appeals function differently in the service of witness versus advocacy.

Key Terms

  • Audience analysis: Identifying who is being addressed and how that shapes the argument's content, tone, and omissions.
  • Genre: The form of a text (oration, pamphlet, address, novel) that determines what kinds of arguments are possible within it.
  • Rhetorical silence: What a writer chooses not to say, which can reveal strategic choices as clearly as the arguments themselves.
  • Bearing witness: Remarque's mode — presenting experience and inviting judgment, rather than advancing a thesis.
