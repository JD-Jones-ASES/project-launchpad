# Study Guide: Woodrow Wilson and the Road to World War I

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the historical pressures and specific events that led the United States to abandon neutrality and enter World War I. It is designed to assist in understanding the complex transition from diplomatic negotiation to the declaration of war on April 2, 1917.

---

### Key Concepts and Historical Context

#### The Evolution of Submarine Warfare
By 1915, the Western Front had reached a stalemate. Germany utilized U-boats (submarines) to bypass the British Royal Navy’s blockade and strike at British supply lines. This introduced a significant conflict with international law, which required warships to identify themselves and allow for the evacuation of crews before sinking merchant vessels. Because submarines were vulnerable to gunfire when surfaced, Germany adopted a policy of **unrestricted submarine warfare**, attacking ships without warning. This policy directly threatened American merchant vessels and the safety of neutral citizens on the high seas.

#### The Sinking of the Lusitania and the Sussex Pledge
In May 1915, the sinking of the British liner *Lusitania* resulted in 1,198 deaths, including 128 Americans. While Germany argued the ship carried munitions—a claim later confirmed—the incident outraged the American public. Following the sinking of the *Sussex* in 1916, Germany issued the **Sussex Pledge**, promising to warn merchant ships before attacking. However, this was a temporary measure. By February 1, 1917, German military leaders resumed unrestricted attacks, calculating they could defeat Britain before American mobilization.

#### The British Blockade and Economic Neutrality
While Germany utilized submarines, Great Britain maintained a naval blockade of Germany. This blockade prevented food and materials from reaching German civilians, resulting in hundreds of thousands of deaths. President Wilson’s response to Britain was notably milder than his response to Germany. This disparity was driven by two factors:
1.  **Safety:** The British blockade did not kill American citizens.
2.  **Economics:** By 1917, American banks had lent over $2 billion to the Allies, and American industry was heavily invested in supplying Britain and France with war materiel.

#### The Zimmermann Telegram
In March 1917, the publication of a decoded telegram from German Foreign Secretary Arthur Zimmermann to the German ambassador in Mexico fundamentally changed American public opinion. The telegram proposed a military alliance where Mexico would attack the United States to recover lost territories (Texas, New Mexico, and Arizona) if the U.S. entered the war against Germany. This transformed the war from a distant maritime dispute into a direct territorial threat to the American homeland.

### Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

*   **The Illusion of Neutrality:** Analyze the argument that the United States was "neutral in name only" prior to 1917. Contrast the American diplomatic and economic responses to the British naval blockade versus German submarine warfare. 
*   **The Transition of the Threat:** Discuss how the Zimmermann Telegram shifted the American public's view of the war from an "abstract maritime dispute" to a "tangible and territorial" threat. Why was this shift necessary for Wilson to move toward a declaration of war?
*   **Wilson’s Grim Necessity:** Examine Woodrow Wilson’s personal approach to leadership and speechwriting. Based on the historical context provided, how did Wilson frame the decision to enter the war, and how did this differ from the perspectives of other contemporary figures like Holmes or Butler?
*   **The Escalation of Crisis:** Trace the timeline of events from the sinking of the *Lusitania* in 1915 to the speech on April 2, 1917. Identify the specific moments where diplomacy failed and explain why "armed neutrality" was eventually deemed insufficient.

### Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Belligerent** | A nation or party engaged in a war. |
| **Hammond Typewriter** | The specific portable typewriter Wilson used to personally type and revise his speeches. |
| **Lusitania** | A British ocean liner sunk by a German U-boat in 1915; the event served as a major catalyst for American anti-German sentiment. |
| **Stalemate** | A situation in which no progress can be made or no advancement is possible; describes the Western Front by 1915. |
| **Sussex Pledge** | A 1916 agreement where Germany promised to provide warnings before sinking merchant vessels. |
| **U-boat** | A German submarine (derived from *Unterseeboot*). |
| **Unrestricted Submarine Warfare** | A naval strategy where submarines sink vessels, including merchant and neutral ships, without warning. |
| **Zimmermann Telegram** | A coded message from Germany to Mexico proposing a military alliance against the U.S. in exchange for American territory. |
