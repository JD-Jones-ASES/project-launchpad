The Argument Unpacked

Beautiful prose can make even debatable ideas sound self-evident. This section steps back from Holmes' eloquence to examine the logical skeleton of his argument — the claims he makes, the evidence he offers, and, crucially, the things he leaves out. Understanding the argument is the first step toward evaluating it.

The Diagnosis: A Society Gone Soft

Holmes begins with a problem. He surveys Gilded Age America and finds it morally wanting. His critique has three targets:

Target Holmes' Complaint
Worship of wealth Vanderbilt has replaced the soldier as the measure of a man
Revolt against suffering The growing belief that pain is always a wrong that can and must be prevented
Scientific materialism Science has dissolved religious certainty without offering anything to replace it

This diagnosis is the foundation of everything that follows. Holmes must make his audience feel that something has been lost before he can argue for what should replace it.

The Prescription: Martial Virtue

Having diagnosed the disease, Holmes offers his cure. The virtues war cultivates — courage, obedience, and above all faith — are what a commercial, comfort-seeking society has lost and must recover.

The soldier's faith, Holmes' central concept, is defined precisely: the willingness to die for a cause one does not fully understand, in a plan one did not choose, under orders one cannot question. Notice what he is not saying:

  • He is not arguing that any particular war was just
  • He is not claiming that the cause must be right or the outcome favorable
  • His claim is more radical: the act of faith itself — regardless of cause or outcome — is the highest thing a human being can do

The Evidence: Two Sources

Holmes supports his claims with two principal types of evidence:

  1. Personal combat experience — Holmes describes in precise sensory detail what it feels like to stand under artillery fire, to advance toward rifle fire, to slip on a dead man's body in a dark wood. This is the rhetorical appeal of ethos: the authority of the witness. No one in his audience can say, "You don't know what you're talking about."

  2. Western literary and cultural tradition — Holmes invokes Beowulf, Tennyson, Swinburne, and Spenser to argue that the warrior ideal is not his personal opinion but the inheritance of civilization. The code of the gentleman, the ethic of honor, the songs of the sword all grow from the same root.

Holmes' Unstated Assumption

The argument's most vulnerable point is an assumption Holmes never states explicitly: that the virtues developed through war cannot be cultivated by any other means. If that assumption is wrong — if courage, sacrifice, and faith can be developed through other forms of risk and commitment — then the entire argument loses its force.

What Holmes Leaves Out

Every argument is shaped by what it omits. Holmes' speech is notable for four significant silences:

  • Who profits from war: The economic dimension — who pays, who benefits, who bears the cost — is entirely absent. This is precisely the question Smedley Butler will place at the center of his argument forty years later.
  • The politics of specific wars: Holmes treats war as a permanent feature of human existence, not as a political decision that can be evaluated on its merits.
  • The suffering of common soldiers: Holmes' battlefield descriptions emphasize courage and transcendence, not pain and futility. The soldier in his speech is a figure of nobility, not a victim.
  • The officer-enlisted gap: Holmes was a Harvard-educated officer from a wealthy family. His experience, while genuinely dangerous, was not identical to that of the working-class soldiers who made up the bulk of the army.

Key Concepts

  • Ethos: The rhetorical appeal based on the speaker's authority or credibility
  • Martial virtue: The qualities cultivated by combat — courage, obedience, faith — that Holmes argues are essential to civilization
  • Unstated assumption: A premise an argument depends on but does not openly defend
  • Smedley Butler: Future critic of war whose argument will focus on economic exploitation rather than spiritual transcendence
