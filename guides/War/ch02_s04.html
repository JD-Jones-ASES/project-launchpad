Study Guide: The Rhetoric of Smedley Butler

This study guide provides a comprehensive analysis of the rhetorical strategies employed by Smedley Butler in his 1935 work, War Is a Racket. It explores his unique voice, his use of evidence, and how his style contrasts with other military-themed rhetoric of the era.

Key Concepts and Analysis

1. The Vernacular Voice

Butler adopts a "vernacular" style, characterized by the ordinary, everyday language of common people rather than the formal prose of academics. His writing mimics a Marine sergeant addressing his men: short, blunt sentences and plain words. This is a deliberate rhetorical choice to signal that he speaks as a soldier and citizen rather than an intellectual.

  • The "Fist on the Table" Effect: By using short sentences without qualifications or literary allusions, Butler hammers his thesis home immediately. For example, his opening—"WAR is a racket. It always has been"—uses parallel superlatives (oldest, most profitable, most vicious) to overwhelm the reader with the extremity of his claim.
  • Contrast with Holmes: Unlike Oliver Wendell Holmes, who used ornate, periodic sentences to build toward meaning, Butler hits the reader with his conclusion first and dares them to argue.

2. Ethos: The Authority of the Participant

Butler’s credibility (ethos) is rooted in his thirty-three years of combat service and two Medals of Honor. He is not a critic from the outside; he is an insider who understands the machine of war better than almost anyone.

  • The Confession of Complicity: Butler explicitly admits that he was part of the system he now condemns. This admission strengthens his ethos because a critic who confesses their own involvement is harder to dismiss than one who has never participated.

3. Logos: Empirical Financial Data

While other writers appeal to abstract ideals like courage or faith, Butler uses logos in its most empirical form: ledger books. He names specific companies, cites profit figures, and calculates percentage increases.

  • Shifting the Burden of Proof: By presenting concrete data—such as the appearance of 21,000 new millionaires during the war—Butler forces his opponents to explain those figures away. The numbers create a presumption of truth that the reader must find an alternative explanation for.

4. Pathos: The Rhetoric of Outrage

Butler uses pathos (emotional appeal) driven by moral energy and physical specificity.

  • Anaphora: Butler uses the repetition of "How many of them" to create a rhythmic drumbeat of accusation against war profiteers.
  • Physical Specificity: His pathos is rooted in reality, not sentiment. He focuses on bodily experiences—digging trenches, ducking shells, and parrying bayonets—to highlight the disconnect between those who suffer in war and those who profit from it.

5. The Pamphlet as a Genre

Butler chose the pamphlet format, a tradition in American political life (e.g., Thomas Paine’s Common Sense). The genre requires brevity, readability for a mass audience, and a clear call to action. By choosing this form, Butler implies that the truth about war is simple enough to be stated plainly in a short document.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Define "vernacular" in the context of Butler’s writing.
    • Answer: It refers to the ordinary, everyday language of common people. Butler uses it to signal his identity as a soldier and citizen rather than an intellectual.
  2. How does Butler use "superlatives" to establish his thesis?
    • Answer: He stacks three superlatives—"oldest," "most profitable," and "most vicious"—in the opening of his pamphlet to emphasize the extremity of his claim that war is a racket.
  3. Why is Butler's admission of complicity considered a rhetorical strength?
    • Answer: It prevents him from being dismissed as an outsider or a "shirker." By admitting he was part of the system, his critique becomes more difficult to ignore.
  4. What is the primary difference between Butler's use of logos and Holmes' rhetorical appeals?
    • Answer: Butler appeals to empirical financial data and ledger books (logos), whereas Holmes appeals to abstract ideals like courage, faith, and sacrifice.
  5. How does "anaphora" function in Butler’s descriptions of war profiteers?
    • Answer: By repeating the phrase "How many of them," Butler creates a rhythmic, hammering effect that builds intensity and drives home the point that profiteers did not share the soldiers' physical suffering.
  6. What common misreading of Butler’s style does the text warn against?
    • Answer: Readers should not mistake his blunt style for simplicity or a lack of skill. His simplicity is a "weapon" used to make his argument harder to evade.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Contrast of Virtue and Greed: Compare and contrast the rhetorical styles and conclusions of Smedley Butler and Oliver Wendell Holmes. How do their specific language choices (diction, sentence structure, and appeals) reflect their diametrically opposed views on the nature of war?
  2. The Rhetoric of Complicity: Analyze how Butler establishes his authority (ethos) not just through his achievements, but through his confession of being part of the "racket." Why might this strategy be more effective for a mass audience than a standard academic critique?
  3. The Power of the Empirical: Evaluate Butler’s use of financial data. How does shifting the argument from philosophical principles to "ledger books" change the "burden of proof" for the reader? Is this a more or less effective form of persuasion in political writing?

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Anaphora The deliberate repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses or sentences to create a rhythmic, hammering effect.
Empirical Based on observation and measurement (such as financial data) rather than theory or pure logic.
Ethos The credibility or authority of a speaker; in Butler's case, derived from 33 years of military service.
Logos An appeal to logic or reason, often through the use of facts, figures, and data.
Pathos An appeal to emotion; Butler uses this to evoke outrage by describing the physical suffering of soldiers.
Periodic Sentence A long, winding sentence that builds toward its meaning through various clauses (a style used by Holmes, contrasted with Butler's short sentences).
Superlative The grammatical form expressing the highest degree of a quality (e.g., oldest, most profitable).
Vernacular The ordinary, everyday language of common people, as opposed to formal or academic prose.
