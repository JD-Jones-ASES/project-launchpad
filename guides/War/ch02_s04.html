Smedley Butler’s Rhetoric: A Study Guide on War Is a Racket

This study guide examines the rhetorical strategies employed by Smedley Butler in his 1935 work, War Is a Racket. It analyzes how Butler’s background as a decorated Marine Corps officer informs his writing style, his use of evidence, and his emotional appeals to a mass audience.

Key Concepts and Rhetorical Analysis

The Vernacular Voice

Smedley Butler utilizes the vernacular, which is the ordinary, everyday language of common people. Unlike the formal, elevated prose of academics like Oliver Wendell Holmes, Butler’s writing is characterized by:

  • Simplicity and Directness: Using short sentences and plain words to ensure his message is accessible to everyone, not just an elite audience.
  • Strategic Brevity: Stripping prose "to the bone" to create a "fist on the table" effect. For example, his thesis—"WAR is a racket"—is only four words long.
  • Avoidance of Ornamentation: Butler eschews literary allusions and subordinate clauses, choosing instead to hit the reader with conclusions immediately.

The Construction of Ethos

Butler’s ethos, or rhetorical credibility, is rooted in his thirty-three years of combat service and his status as a recipient of two Medals of Honor. This background makes his argument nearly impossible to dismiss as the product of cowardice or ignorance.

  • The Admission of Complicity: A unique aspect of Butler’s ethos is his confession that he was part of the "machine" he now condemns. By admitting he did not fully realize the nature of the racket until retirement, he positions himself as an insider-turned-critic, which strengthens his authority.

Empirical Logos

While other writers might appeal to abstract ideals like "courage" or "faith," Butler uses empirical logos. He relies on concrete measurement and observation, specifically financial data:

  • Specific Figures: He cites the tenfold increase in Du Pont’s earnings and the emergence of 21,000 new millionaires during the war.
  • Shifting the Burden of Proof: By presenting undeniable ledger-book data, Butler forces his opponents to explain away the massive profits rather than arguing over philosophical principles.

Pathos and the Rhetoric of Outrage

Butler’s emotional appeal (pathos) is not based on sentimentality but on "furious moral energy" and physical reality.

  • Anaphora: He uses the repetition of "How many of them" to create a rhythmic, hammering accusation against war profiteers.
  • Physical Specificity: He focuses on the bodily experiences of soldiers—shouldering rifles, digging trenches, and parrying bayonets—to contrast the lived suffering of the many with the financial gain of the few.

The Pamphlet as a Genre

Butler chose the pamphlet format, a short, polemical work aimed at a mass audience. Historically used for revolutionary texts like Common Sense, the pamphlet genre requires:

  • Readability: It must be understood by ordinary people.
  • Clear Call to Action: It moves the reader toward a specific conclusion or behavior.
  • Brevity: The short length implies that the truth is plain and does not require complex, hundreds-of-pages-long analysis.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does Butler’s sentence structure differ from that of Oliver Wendell Holmes?
  2. What are the three superlatives Butler uses to describe war in the opening of his pamphlet?
  3. Why is Butler’s admission of his own past participation in the "racket" considered a rhetorical strength?
  4. According to the text, what is the "Common Misreading" of Butler’s blunt writing style?
  5. Provide two specific examples of financial data Butler uses to support his logos-based arguments.
  6. What specific rhetorical device is used in the passage beginning with "How many of these war millionaires..."?
  7. How does Butler’s use of pathos differ from typical sentimental appeals?
  8. What does the choice of the "pamphlet" form suggest about Butler’s intended audience?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Weaponry of Simplicity

Butler’s style is described as "strategically simple." Write an essay analyzing how Butler uses plain language to prevent readers from "evading" his argument. Compare this to the potential risks of the "ornate, periodic sentences" used by writers like Holmes. How does language choice serve as a gatekeeper for different social classes?

2. The Credibility of the Critic

Analyze the role of ethos in War Is a Racket. Does Butler’s status as a high-ranking military officer make his critique more or less effective than a critique written by a civilian pacifist? Use the concept of "complicity" from the text to support your argument.

3. Abstract Virtue vs. Concrete Reality

Contrast the rhetorical foundations of Oliver Wendell Holmes and Smedley Butler. Holmes views war through the lens of human virtue, while Butler views it through the lens of human greed. Evaluate which approach—appealing to abstract ideals (faith, sacrifice) or empirical data (ledger books, profit margins)—is more persuasive in a political context.

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Anaphora The deliberate repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses or sentences to create a rhythmic, hammering effect.
Empirical Based on observation, measurement, and physical evidence rather than theory or pure logic.
Ethos The credibility or ethical appeal of a speaker, often based on their character, experience, or authority.
Logos An appeal to logic and reason, often through the use of data, facts, and statistics.
Pathos An appeal to the audience's emotions, such as outrage, pity, or fear.
Periodic Sentence A long, complex sentence that builds toward its main point at the end, often using multiple clauses.
Superlative The grammatical form expressing the highest degree of a quality (e.g., oldest, most vicious).
Vernacular The ordinary, everyday language or dialect spoken by the common people of a particular region or country.
