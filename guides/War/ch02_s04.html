Study Guide: Smedley Butler’s "The Argument Unpacked"

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the analysis of Smedley Butler’s 1935 work, War is a Racket. It focuses on the first two chapters, where Butler establishes his central thesis through a three-stage rhetorical structure: definition, evidence, and moral reckoning.

I. Key Concepts and Argument Structure

Butler’s argument is built sequentially to maximize rhetorical impact, moving from a categorical definition to specific financial data, and finally to the human consequences of war.

Stage One: The Definition of a "Racket"

Butler utilizes the term "racket"—a word heavily associated with 1930s organized crime—to frame war not as a necessary evil or a noble pursuit, but as a criminal scheme.

  • The Nature of a Racket: A scheme that benefits a small group of "insiders" while the majority of the people bear the costs.
  • Deception: A core component of a racket is that it is "not what it seems" to the public.
  • Categorical Claim: Butler does not argue that some wars are rackets; he makes a sweeping statement that war itself always has been one. This forces the audience into a binary position: they must either accept his framing or refute it entirely.

Stage Two: The Evidence (Logos)

In Chapter 2, Butler provides specific financial data from the World War (World War I) to support his claims. He uses "logos"—an appeal to logic and documented facts—to show that the conflict served as a massive wealth transfer from the public to private corporations.

Corporation Peacetime Annual Earnings Wartime Annual Earnings
Du Pont $6,000,000 $58,000,000
Bethlehem Steel $6,000,000 $49,000,000
United States Steel $105,000,000 $240,000,000

Key Data Point: At least 21,000 new millionaires and billionaires were created in the United States during the war. Butler refers to these as "blood gains" because they were derived from government contracts for materials consumed in a conflict that killed millions.

Stage Three: The Moral Reckoning

The final stage of the argument focuses on the Asymmetry of Cost and Profit. Butler highlights the stark divide between those who reap the rewards and those who pay the price.

  • The Profiteers: Bankers, industrialists, and arms manufacturers who remain safe at home.
  • The Payers: Soldiers and their families who bear the cost in "gravestones, mangled bodies, shattered minds, and broken hearts."
  • The Provocation: Butler points out that none of the war millionaires ever "shouldered a rifle" or "dug a trench."

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What was the cultural significance of the word "racket" when Butler wrote his book in 1935?
  2. According to Butler’s definition, who are the primary beneficiaries of the "war racket"?
  3. By approximately what factor did Du Pont’s earnings increase during the World War?
  4. How many new millionaires and billionaires were reportedly created in the U.S. during the World War?
  5. What rhetorical strategy (logos, ethos, or pathos) is primarily used when Butler cites specific dollar figures for steel and copper companies?
  6. What is the "asymmetry" Butler identifies regarding the costs of war?
  7. Identify one major question or "gap" that Butler leaves unaddressed in the first two chapters of his work.

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. Rhetorical Progression

Analyze why Butler chooses to present financial statistics (Stage Two) before discussing the human cost of war (Stage Three). How does this specific ordering of information affect the reader's moral response? Use evidence from the text regarding "blood gains" to support your answer.

2. The Limits of Generalization

Butler makes a categorical claim that "war is a racket." Evaluate the logical strengths and weaknesses of this absolute statement. Does his reliance on data from a single conflict (the World War) sufficiently support a universal claim about all wars, including wars of national defense or those against tyranny?

3. Profit vs. Purpose

Discuss the logical distinction between existence and purpose. Does the fact that corporations made enormous profits during the war prove that those profits were the primary reason the war was fought? How does Butler bridge this gap, and where might a critical reader find his evidence insufficient?

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Asymmetry of Cost and Profit: The concept that the risks and sacrifices of war are borne by one group (soldiers and taxpayers), while the financial rewards are reaped by a completely different group (insiders and industrialists).
  • Blood Gains: Butler’s term for wartime profits, emphasizing that financial wealth was generated through the suffering and death of others.
  • Logos: A rhetorical appeal based on logic, reason, and concrete evidence such as facts, statistics, and documented data.
  • Racket: Historically associated with organized crime; a criminal scheme that benefits insiders at the expense of the public while deceiving the majority about its true nature.
  • War Profiteering: The practice of making excessive or unreasonable profits from the sale of goods or services to a government during wartime, often carrying a negative moral connotation.
