The Argument Unpacked

Butler's first two chapters of War Is a Racket build a single devastating argument in three stages: define the crime, present the evidence, demand a moral reckoning. Understanding this structure is essential because Butler's rhetorical power depends on the sequence in which he delivers his punches. Each stage builds on the one before it.

Stage 1: Define the Crime

Butler opens with a categorical definition. War, he says, is a racket — not a noble enterprise, not a necessary evil, but a criminal scheme. He defines a racket as something that benefits a few insiders at the expense of the many, something that "is not what it seems to the majority of the people."

This is a bold rhetorical move. Butler does not say that certain wars have been fought for the wrong reasons. He makes a sweeping, universal claim: war itself is always a racket. This forces the reader to either accept his framing or argue against it — there is no middle ground. By positioning his argument as categorical rather than conditional, Butler places the burden on anyone who disagrees to disprove the universal claim.

Stage 2: Present the Evidence (Logos)

Having defined the crime, Butler turns detective. Chapter 2 catalogs the wartime profits of specific American companies during WWI, comparing peacetime earnings to wartime earnings. Du Pont's annual earnings rose from $6 million to $58 million — nearly tenfold. Bethlehem Steel's climbed from $6 million to $49 million. United States Steel rose from $105 million to $240 million. At least 21,000 new millionaires and billionaires were created during the war — what Butler calls "blood gains."

The power of this evidence lies in its specificity. Butler does not make vague claims about "the rich" getting richer. He names the companies, cites the dollar figures, and compares peacetime to wartime earnings so the reader can see the magnitude of the difference. This is logos at its most concrete — not abstract reasoning but documented facts arranged to make a specific case. The rhetorical effect is to shift the burden of proof: once Butler has laid out the numbers, anyone who wants to defend the war must explain those numbers away.

War profiteering is the term for this practice — making excessive profits from the sale of goods or services to a government during wartime. Butler's central charge is that war profiteering is not an abuse of the system but the system's actual purpose.

Stage 3: The Moral Reckoning (Pathos)

Butler does not stop with the numbers. Having shown who profits, he turns to who pays. The bill of war is measured in gravestones, mangled bodies, shattered minds, and broken homes. Butler deliberately places this accounting after the financial evidence, so the reader has already absorbed the obscenity of the profits before being confronted with the price.

The key concept here is the asymmetry of cost and profit: the people who profit from war are not the people who fight it. The industrialists, bankers, and arms dealers stay safely at home. The soldiers — and their families — bear the cost in suffering, disability, and death. Butler makes this asymmetry vivid with a series of rhetorical questions: How many of the war millionaires shouldered a rifle? How many dug a trench? How many were wounded or killed? The answer, in every case, is none.

What Butler Leaves Open

No analysis of an argument is complete without asking what it omits. Butler's categorical claim raises questions he does not fully address in Chapters 1–2:

  • Is every war a racket, or only some? His evidence comes almost entirely from WWI.
  • Can a necessary war be fought without enriching the wrong people?
  • Does the existence of enormous profit prove that profit was the purpose of the war? This is a crucial logical distinction — correlation is not causation — that Butler does not always make explicit.

These gaps do not invalidate Butler's argument. But a strong reader notes not only what a writer says, but what the writer assumes and where the evidence might support a different conclusion.

Key Terms

  • Logos — Rhetorical appeal to reason and evidence; Butler uses documented profit data.
  • Pathos — Rhetorical appeal to emotion; Butler uses catalogs of human suffering.
  • War profiteering — Making excessive profits from wartime government contracts.
  • Blood gains — Butler's term for wartime profits derived from the suffering and death of soldiers.
  • Asymmetry of cost and profit — The principle that those who profit from war are not those who fight and die in it.
