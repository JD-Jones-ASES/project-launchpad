# Analysis of Pathos: Rhetorical Emotional Appeals

This study guide examines the use of pathos—the rhetorical appeal to emotion—within the context of war-related discourse. Based on the analysis provided in "Rhetoric Under the Microscope," this document explores how different writers employ specific emotional registers to motivate audiences, the risks associated with these appeals, and the criteria for distinguishing between legitimate persuasion and emotional manipulation.

---

## I. Comparative Profiles of Pathos

The following table summarizes the distinct emotional strategies employed by four key writers to address the concept of war and sacrifice.

| Writer | Emotional Register | Primary Appeal | Core Objective | Identified Risk |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Holmes** | Elevation | Admiration and aspiration; the beauty of sacrifice. | To inspire reverence for the soldier’s faith and noble, transcendent acts. | Obscures the physical reality and "ugliness" of war (the hospital ward). |
| **Butler** | Outrage | Indignation at betrayal and economic injustice. | To incite fury and motivate political action/reform against war profiteering. | May oversimplify complex issues into a narrative of pure villainy. |
| **Wilson** | Solemnity | Duty and moral gravity; the "tragic hero" logic. | To convince the audience that war is a necessary, if fearful, moral burden. | Can make war seem inevitable, foreclosing the possibility of peace. |
| **Remarque** | Numbness | Unmediated experience and empathic horror. | To force the reader to "know" the raw, unbearable reality of the soldier. | Leaves the reader overwhelmed without a framework or call to action. |

## II. Detailed Rhetorical Strategies

### The Elevation of Holmes
Holmes utilizes the language of beauty and spiritual exaltation to lift the audience above ordinary concerns. By focusing on the "monument" rather than the "body," he encourages a hushed awe. This ceremonial style of oratory suggests that greatness is reachable through self-sacrifice, even when the specific reasons for that sacrifice remain uncalculated or unknown to the soldier.

### The Indignation of Butler
Butler’s architecture of pathos is built on the contrast between the suffering of the many and the profit of the few. He uses graphic descriptions of broken bodies not merely for sympathy, but as evidence of a "con game." His goal is movement—petitioning, voting, and protesting—because anger, unlike sadness, tends to produce action rather than contemplation.

### The Moral Weight of Wilson
Wilson’s appeal relies on the tension between reluctance and resolve. He acknowledges that war is "fearful," yet frames participation as a requirement of honor and justice. By positioning the decision as a moral obligation, Wilson makes inaction feel like a betrayal of fundamental values, effectively pressuring the audience to accept the burden of duty.

### The Raw Exposure of Remarque
Remarque avoids traditional rhetorical strategies like celebration or exhortation. Instead, he employs "showing" to depict visceral images, such as a man drowning in mud or a screaming, wounded horse. By stripping away layers of glory, profit, or necessity, he leaves the reader with a deep, inarticulate sadness that has no attached policy prescription.

## III. Evaluating Emotional Appeals

The distinction between persuasion and manipulation is a central challenge in rhetoric. The text provides three guiding principles for evaluating the legitimacy of pathos.

1.  **Evidence vs. Substitution:** Does the emotion accompany evidence or replace it? Pathos is legitimate when it reinforces logic (e.g., Butler using wounded veterans as evidence of economic costs). It is suspicious when it produces strong feelings without supporting facts.
2.  **Fairness of Representation:** Does the appeal represent the situation honestly? While writers may omit certain details, the reader must ask if these omissions (such as Holmes omitting physical suffering) are irrelevant to the point or intended to prevent the audience from seeing a truth that would undermine the argument.
3.  **Room for Dissent:** Does the appeal coerce the audience? If an appeal to duty or patriotism makes disagreement feel like treason or cowardice, it moves beyond persuasion into coercion.

### Persuasion vs. Manipulation
*   **Emotional Manipulation:** Occurs when a speaker uses emotion to bypass rational judgment, forcing the audience to stop thinking critically. This involves distorting facts or using emotion as a total substitute for evidence.
*   **Legitimate Persuasion:** Acknowledges that emotions are a necessary part of human reasoning. It uses pathos to illuminate a genuine truth and ensures the emotional reaction is proportional to the evidence provided.

## VI. Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Elevation:** An emotional register aimed at making an audience feel inspired or lifted toward noble, transcendent concerns; often used in ceremonial oratory.
*   **Empathic Horror:** The emotional effect produced by unmediated descriptions of suffering, intended to make the reader feel as though they are experiencing a nightmare.
*   **Emotional Manipulation:** The use of pathos to bypass critical thinking and rational judgment, often by substituting emotion for evidence.
*   **Outrage:** A specific emotional architecture designed to provoke anger at a perceived injustice or "con game."
*   **Pathos:** The rhetorical appeal to the audience’s emotions; the element of a speech or text that answers "Why should I care?"
*   **Solemn Resolve:** The emotional state Wilson seeks to induce, characterized by a heavy heart but a firm commitment to a necessary, albeit painful, duty.
*   **Tragic Hero Logic:** The emotional framework where an individual acknowledges the suffering their actions will cause but proceeds because they believe it is morally required.
*   **Unmediated Experience:** A rhetorical strategy of "showing" raw reality without imposing an explicit interpretation, argument, or call to action.
