Address to Congress, April 2, 1917

Woodrow Wilson delivered this address to a joint session of Congress on the evening of April 2, 1917. Four days later, Congress voted to declare war on Germany. It is one of the most carefully constructed speeches in American presidential history — a document that moves from specific grievance to universal principle, from maritime crisis to the survival of democracy itself. Reading it closely means attending both to what Wilson says and how he says it.

The Opening Grievance

Wilson begins not with a call to arms but with facts. Germany has announced it will use submarines to sink every vessel approaching the ports of Britain, Ireland, and Germany's other enemies — regardless of flag, cargo, or nationality. This is not a dispute Wilson invites Congress to interpret; it is a matter of documented record. Hospital ships, neutral vessels, ships carrying Belgian relief with Germany's own safe conduct passes — all have been sunk without warning or mercy.

Wilson's framing of this grievance is deliberate. He calls Germany's submarine campaign "a warfare against mankind" and "a war against all nations," because ships of every neutral country have been targeted indiscriminately. His concern, he emphasizes, is not the loss of property — "property can be paid for" — but "the wanton and wholesale destruction of the lives of noncombatants, men, women, and children."

The Failure of Armed Neutrality

Wilson had hoped arming American merchant ships would be sufficient to protect American rights without requiring war. He now concludes this policy is "impracticable." Submarines are "outlaws" — they cannot be dealt with as traditional surface warships can. They must be destroyed on sight before they show their own intention. Germany has even threatened to treat armed merchant guards as pirates. Armed neutrality, Wilson argues, would draw the United States into war without the rights or effectiveness of a recognized belligerent. The US will not choose the "path of submission."

The Call to War and Its Terms

Wilson asks Congress to declare that Germany's actions constitute war against the United States, and to formally accept the status of belligerent. He outlines specific requirements: cooperation with Allied governments and extension of financial credit to them; full equipment of the navy against submarines; an immediate addition of at least 500,000 men to the armed forces, to be raised by the principle of universal liability to service; and war financing through taxation, not just loans, to protect the American people from inflation.

These are the practical instruments of war. But Wilson insists the United States' motive will not be revenge or the assertion of national might — only "the vindication of right, of human right."

Democracy Against Autocracy

The speech's most significant turn comes when Wilson elevates the argument from the specific to the universal. America has no quarrel with the German people — they did not choose this war; their rulers did. Autocratic governments are inherently dangerous because they operate through secrecy, intrigue, and espionage. They cannot be trusted to keep the peace because their leaders do not answer to their citizens. "A steadfast concert for peace can never be maintained except by a partnership of democratic nations."

Wilson invokes the recent Russian Revolution — the fall of the Tsar — as evidence that the forces of democracy are advancing. Russia, newly "democratic at heart," is "a fit partner for a League of Honor." The world, Wilson declares, "must be made safe for democracy."

The Closing

Wilson closes with what would become one of the most famous passages in American oratory: "It is a fearful thing to lead this great peaceful people into war, into the most terrible and disastrous of all wars, civilization itself seeming to be in the balance. But the right is more precious than peace." He lists what America will fight for: democracy, the right of peoples to have a voice in their governments, the rights and liberties of small nations, and "a universal dominion of right" that will make the world "at last free."

Key Terms

Belligerent: A nation engaged in war. Wilson asked Congress to formally accept this status for the United States — a recognition that Germany's actions had already made America a target, whether it chose to fight or not.

League of Honor: Wilson's vision of an alliance of democratic nations committed to collective security — a precursor to his later proposal for the League of Nations.

Safe for Democracy: Wilson's famous phrase arguing that the war's purpose was to protect democratic nations from authoritarian aggression. It became both a rallying cry and, later, a target of sharp skepticism.

Universal Liability to Service: The principle that all citizens are subject to military conscription — the basis for Wilson's proposed army of 500,000 men.
