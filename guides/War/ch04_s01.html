From Diagnosis to Prescription

In War Is a Racket (1935), Smedley Butler moves in two distinct phases. The first two chapters diagnose the problem — war is a racket, a scheme run by a small number of insiders for private profit at the expense of the many who fight and die. The final three chapters shift to prescription: Butler proposes specific reforms and builds toward a passionate closing argument. Chapter 4 covers this transition, asking not only what Butler says but how he structures his case and why that structure matters.

The Diagnostic Foundation

Butler's diagnosis in Chapters 1–2 rests on corporate profit data. Du Pont's earnings jumped nearly tenfold during World War I. Bethlehem Steel's profits rose from six million to forty-nine million dollars per year. Some coal producers recorded gains of up to 7,856 percent on their capital. These numbers were meant to be damning — and for many readers, they were.

But a diagnosis, however accurate, is not a cure. Telling people that war is a racket does not tell them how to stop it. Butler understood this. The shift from Chapters 1–2 to Chapters 3–5 is the shift from "What is wrong?" to the harder question: "What do we do about it?"

The Human Cost (Chapter 3)

Before offering his reforms, Butler wants readers to feel what war costs the people who fight it. He takes readers on a tour of veterans' hospitals, describing men who were once the finest young Americans their communities could produce. He calls them "the living dead." He describes psychiatric wards where former soldiers are kept behind steel bars, their minds destroyed by combat trauma. Soldiers were paid thirty dollars a month — half of which was immediately deducted — then pressured into buying Liberty Bonds that bankers later repurchased at a discount of $84 or $86 on the hundred-dollar bond.

This placement is a deliberate rhetorical choice. By putting the human cost before his policy proposals, Butler ensures that readers evaluate his reforms as urgent responses to real suffering, not as abstract ideas. The question is no longer "Is this a good idea in theory?" but "How can we let this happen again?"

Three Proposals for Reform (Chapter 4)

Butler offers three concrete reforms, each targeting a different mechanism of the war racket:

1. Conscript capital before conscripting men. Before the government can draft a single young man, it must first conscript the nation's capital, industry, and labor — limiting everyone, from corporate executives to generals to politicians, to the same thirty dollars a month that soldiers earn. The first proposal removes the profit motive.

2. Hold a limited plebiscite. The decision to go to war should be made not by Congress but by those who will actually fight — young men of military age who pass a physical examination. Only those who would bear the consequences should have the power to authorize war. The second proposal transfers the decision from those who benefit to those who suffer.

3. Restrict the military to defense. Naval vessels should be limited by law to operating within two hundred miles of the American coastline. Aircraft should be restricted to five hundred miles. The army should never leave American territory. The third proposal makes offensive war physically impossible.

A plebiscite is a direct vote by citizens on a single major question. Butler's version is "limited" because he restricts the vote to men of military age and physical fitness — those who would be called to fight.

The Peroration (Chapter 5)

Chapter 5 is Butler's closing argument — his peroration. He drops the data and policy details to speak from the gut. He names Woodrow Wilson directly: Wilson was reelected in 1916 on the promise "He Kept Us Out of War," then asked Congress to declare war on Germany just five months after his second inauguration on April 2, 1917. Butler argues the reversal was driven by financial interests — the billions owed to American bankers by the Allied powers.

The pamphlet closes with four words in capital letters: "TO HELL WITH WAR!"

A Question of Structure

Butler's argumentative movement — evidence to policy to passion — is a classic structure in persuasive writing. But it raises important questions. Does the raw emotion of Chapter 5 strengthen the policy arguments of Chapter 4, or does it undercut them? Does the human cost described in Chapter 3 make Butler's proposals seem more reasonable — or does it cloud the reader's judgment with emotion? A writer who diagnoses well does not necessarily prescribe well. Evaluating Butler's reforms requires asking not only whether they are morally compelling but whether they would actually work.

Key Terms

Term Definition
Plebiscite A direct vote by citizens on a single major political question; Butler's version is restricted to men of military age and physical fitness
Peroration The closing section of a speech or argument, intended to summarize and move the audience to strong feeling or action
Prescriptive argument An argument that proposes specific solutions, as opposed to a diagnostic argument that identifies problems
Liberty Bonds Government securities sold to the public (including soldiers) to fund World War I; Butler describes them as tools of financial exploitation
The living dead Butler's phrase for veterans whose minds were destroyed by war, encountered during his tour of government hospitals
