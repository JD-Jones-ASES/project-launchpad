Study Guide: War Is a Racket by Smedley Butler (Chapters 3–5)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the final chapters of Smedley Butler’s 1935 work, War Is a Racket. It details the human and economic costs of warfare, Butler’s proposed reforms to end war profiteering, and his critique of the political and financial motivations behind military intervention.

I. Key Concepts and Themes

The Human Cost of War

Butler argues that while bankers and manufacturers reap financial rewards, the soldier pays the "biggest part of the bill." This cost is measured in several ways:

  • Mortality and Physical Injury: Butler notes that mortality among veterans is three times higher than those who stayed home. He describes "the living dead" in government hospitals—men destroyed physically and mentally.
  • Psychological Destruction: Soldiers are trained through mass psychology to regard murder as the "order of the day." However, they are discharged without support to perform an "about face" back to civilian life. Butler highlights the 1,800 veterans in "pens" at a Marion, Indiana hospital as evidence of total mental collapse.
  • Regimentation: Civilians are remolded into soldiers, losing their individuality and community roles while others profit from their absence.

The Economic Exploitation of Soldiers

Butler highlights a "crowning insolence" where soldiers not only risk their lives but also subsidize the war effort from their meager pay:

  • The $30 Monthly Wage: Out of this "munificent" sum, $15 was taken for dependents, and $6 was charged for accident insurance.
  • The Liberty Bond Manipulation: Soldiers were pressured into buying Liberty Bonds at $100 par value. When they returned from war and needed cash due to unemployment, bankers bought these bonds back at depressed prices ($84–$86) before stimulating a boom to sell them back at a profit.

The Three-Step Plan to Smash the "War Racket"

Butler proposes three concrete reforms to make war unprofitable and difficult to initiate:

  1. Conscription of Capital and Industry: Before men are drafted, the government must conscript capital, industry, and labor. All executives, bankers, politicians, and military officers would be restricted to a $30 monthly wage—the same as the soldier in the trenches.
  2. The Limited Plebiscite: Only those who would be eligible to fight and die (those of military age) should be allowed to vote on whether the nation goes to war. Butler argues that those who profit from war but never see combat should have no say in the declaration.
  3. Defensive Limitation of Military Forces: To ensure the military is for "defense only," the Navy should be legally restricted to within 200 miles of the coastline, and the Army should never leave territorial limits.

Political and Financial Motivations

Butler identifies the 1917 entry of the United States into World War I as a decision driven by money rather than ideals. He asserts that the Allies owed American bankers and manufacturers $5–$6 billion; if the Allies lost, that debt would never be repaid. He dismisses "safe for democracy" rhetoric as propaganda used to shroud secret financial negotiations.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to Butler, what was the actual monthly take-home pay for most soldiers after deductions for dependents and insurance?
    • Answer: Less than $9 per month.
  2. What specific reform does Butler suggest regarding the U.S. Navy to prevent offensive wars?
    • Answer: He proposes a law limiting all naval ships to within 200 miles of the U.S. coastline.
  3. How many veterans did Butler observe in "pens" at the government hospital in Marion, Indiana?
    • Answer: 1,800 men, with 500 kept in barracks with steel bars and wires.
  4. What was the "Napoleonic system" mentioned in Chapter 3?
    • Answer: The use of medals and decorations to encourage men to enlist for lower wages because they are "enamored of decorations."
  5. Who does Butler believe should be the only people allowed to vote in a war plebiscite?
    • Answer: Only those who would be called upon to bear arms and risk their lives.
  6. What reason does Butler give for the failure of disarmament conferences?
    • Answer: They are attended by professional soldiers and sailors who do not want to lose their jobs, and they are influenced by "sinister agents" of war profiteers.
  7. What does Butler claim was the "diplomatic language" used to hide the real reason for joining WWI?
    • Answer: The claim that it was a "war to make the world safe for democracy" and a "war to end all wars."
  8. According to the text, how did bankers profit from Liberty Bonds?
    • Answer: They depressed the price to buy them back from frightened or unemployed citizens at $84–$86, then sold them back at par or above when the market recovered.

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. Analysis of Propaganda: Butler claims that "even God was brought into" the war effort through the clergy. Analyze how Butler describes the use of propaganda (religious, idealistic, and social) to manufacture consent for conscription.
  2. The Economics of Conscription: Discuss Butler’s argument for the conscription of capital. Why does he believe that paying a CEO $30 a month would end the threat of war within thirty days?
  3. The Concept of "Defense Only": Butler suggests that the U.S. would not have entered the Spanish-American War if the "200-mile limit" had been in place. Evaluate his argument that military technology and movement should be legally restricted to territorial waters.
  4. The Soldier vs. The Profiteer: Contrast the physical and psychological toll on the soldier, as described by Butler in the hospitals he visited, with the financial gains of the "swivel-chair admirals" and "tycoons" he critiques.

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition Based on Source Context
Armistice The ceasefire agreement of November 11, 1918, which ended the fighting in World War I.
Conscript To legally compel individuals into military service; Butler proposes this should apply to capital and industry as well as men.
Liberty Bonds Government-issued securities sold to the public (including soldiers) to fund the war; Butler describes them as a tool for banker manipulation.
Munificent Extremely generous; used sarcastically by Butler to describe the soldier's meager $30 monthly salary.
Plebiscite A direct vote by the electorate on a specific question; Butler proposes a "limited" version restricted to those of military age.
Prize System A historical practice (ended after the Spanish-American War) where soldiers and sailors were paid bonuses or shares of captured enemy vessels.
Swivel-chair Admirals Butler’s derogatory term for naval officers who lobby for appropriations from offices in Washington rather than serving at sea.
War Racket Butler’s central metaphor for war, defined as a conflict where "a few profit—and the many pay."
