Study Guide: Rhetorical Analysis of Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr.’s "The Soldier's Faith"

This study guide provides an in-depth analysis of Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr.’s 1895 speech, "The Soldier's Faith." It examines Holmes’ use of language, the structure of his arguments, and the rhetorical tools employed to persuade his audience.

Key Concepts and Rhetorical Analysis

The Victorian Grand Style

Holmes utilizes the Victorian grand style, an oratorical tradition prevalent in the late nineteenth century. This style is not merely a method of communication but a deliberate artistic choice characterized by:

  • Complex Sentence Structure: The use of long, winding sentences with multiple subordinate clauses.
  • Elevated Diction: The use of sophisticated and formal language.
  • Classical and Literary Allusions: References to figures and works such as Beowulf, Tennyson, Swinburne, and Spenser to add weight and authority.
  • Emotional Intensity: A deliberate building of tension toward a climactic moment.

For Holmes, this style serves as an enactment of faith itself; the reader must follow a complex path without seeing the destination immediately, trusting that the journey is worthwhile.

The Periodic Sentence

A central feature of Holmes’ rhetoric is the periodic sentence. This structure delays the main clause until the very end, building suspense and momentum.

Feature Effect in "The Soldier's Faith"
Structure Stacks multiple conditional clauses (e.g., "if you have...") before the resolution.
Rhythm Creates a rhythmic pulse or "drumbeat" that raises emotional stakes.
Psychological Impact Forces the audience to experience a miniature version of the soldier's uncertainty and eventual resolution.

The Three Classical Appeals

Holmes weaves together the three pillars of rhetoric to create a persuasive and moving address:

  1. Ethos (Appealing to Authority/Character): Holmes establishes authority as a thrice-wounded veteran. His credibility is "written on his body." In the conclusion, his ethos shifts to an intimate claim of authority through grief and personal remembrance of fallen comrades.
  2. Pathos (Appealing to Emotion): Vivid sensory descriptions of combat—the "shrieking fragments" and the "spat of the bullets"—evoke terror and intensity. The closing elegy for an old colonel moves the audience through reverence and grief.
  3. Logos (Appealing to Reason): Holmes’ logic is centered on a specific worldview: life is a struggle, comfort leads to weakness, and human potential is best discovered through danger.

Detailed Passage Analysis

The Battlefield Passage

This passage is a single sentence exceeding two hundred words. It uses the "if you have" repetition to accumulate visceral sensory details:

  • Visual: The "puff of firing," the "blue line of fire."
  • Auditory: The "shrieking fragments," the "spat of bullets."
  • Tactile: Feeling your "foot slip upon a dead man's body."

The sentence concludes only after the audience has been fully immersed in the sensations of war: "you know that there is such a thing as the faith I spoke of."

The Central Creed

Holmes shifts from the "baroque complexity" of the battlefield to stark, plain sentences. By stating, "I do not know what is true," he strips away intellectual pretension. He defines the soldier's faith as "blindly accepted duty"—a faith performed without understanding the cause, the plan, or the tactics. This highlights faith as an act of pure will.

The Song of the Sword

The conclusion uses literary quotations to claim the authority of a thousand years of warrior culture. By ending with the image of a lonely funeral in an empty church, Holmes transforms the speech into an elegy, arguing that being "content with silence" is the final part of the soldier's faith.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What characterizes the "Victorian grand style" used by Holmes?

    • Answer: It is characterized by long, complex sentences, elevated diction, classical and literary allusions, and an emotional build-up toward climactic moments.

  2. How does the structure of a periodic sentence mirror the "faith" Holmes describes?

    • Answer: The structure requires the listener to follow a long, uncertain path of subordinate clauses before reaching the main idea, mimicking the soldier's willingness to move forward through uncertainty toward an unseen destination.

  3. What is the "vulnerability" identified in Holmes' central creed?

    • Answer: Because Holmes celebrates faith as "blind" and without the need for understanding, the argument could potentially be used to justify a wicked cause as easily as a just one.

  4. Why does Holmes use references to Beowulf and Spenser at the end of his speech?

    • Answer: To embed his argument within the tradition of Western civilization and warrior culture, claiming a historical and literary authority for his views.

  5. Distinguish between Holmes’ use of Ethos at the beginning versus the end of the speech.

    • Answer: Initially, his ethos comes from his status as a wounded veteran (physical authority). By the end, it is an "intimate ethos" based on grief and his role as a keeper of the memory of his fallen comrades.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Ethics of Blind Faith: Holmes argues that the noblest acts are those performed "in obedience to a blindly accepted duty." Analyze the implications of this statement. Is the value of an act tied to the cause it serves, or is the "pure will" of the individual the ultimate measure of virtue? Use Holmes' text to support your argument.

  2. Rhetoric vs. Truth: The text cautions that "a beautifully constructed argument is not necessarily a true one." Evaluate "The Soldier's Faith" based on this distinction. Does the "Victorian grand style" and the use of the periodic sentence provide a sound logical foundation for Holmes' claims, or does it merely use emotional momentum to bypass the audience's critical faculties?

  3. The Evolution of Oratory: Compare the Victorian grand style described in the text with modern forms of public address. How has the shift toward "shorter sentences and more direct expression" changed the way audiences perceive authority and emotional intensity in a speaker?

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Classical Allusion A reference to a well-known figure, event, or work from classical literature, mythology, or history used to add authority to an argument.
Elegy A poem or speech of mourning for the dead; an act of remembrance.
Ethos The rhetorical appeal to the character, credibility, or authority of the speaker.
Logos The rhetorical appeal to reason, logic, and a coherent worldview.
Oratory The art of formal, persuasive public speaking.
Pathos The rhetorical appeal to emotion, intended to make the audience feel the argument.
Periodic Sentence A sentence that delays its main clause until the end to build suspense and emphasis.
Victorian Grand Style An oratorical tradition marked by complex syntax, formal language, and deliberate emotional building.
