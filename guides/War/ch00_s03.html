Perspectives on the Purpose of War: A Comprehensive Study Guide

This study guide provides a structured overview of the four primary perspectives regarding the nature and purpose of war as presented in the source text. It examines the backgrounds and core arguments of Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr., Smedley Darlington Butler, Woodrow Wilson, and Erich Maria Remarque.

I. Profiles of the Four Voices

The following table summarizes the backgrounds and central arguments of the four writers examined in this study.

Figure Background & Experience Core Argument on War
Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. Civil War veteran (Union Army); US Supreme Court Jurist; known as "The Great Dissenter." War is a "crucible" that develops virtues—courage and self-sacrifice—that a commercial society cannot produce.
Smedley Darlington Butler Career Marine; two-time Medal of Honor recipient; exposed the "Business Plot" of 1933. War is a "racket" where insiders profit while soldiers suffer. The profit motive must be removed from warfare.
Woodrow Wilson Scholar and 28th US President; progressive reformer; champion of the League of Nations. War is a necessary tool to protect human rights and make the world "safe for democracy."
Erich Maria Remarque WWI German Army veteran; author of All Quiet on the Western Front; target of Nazi censorship. War is a physical and psychological horror that defies noble abstractions; it is best understood through the lived reality of the soldier.

II. Key Concepts and Thematic Analysis

The Crucible of Virtue vs. The Commercial Racket

A primary tension exists between the views of Holmes and Butler. Holmes argues from a philosophical standpoint that war serves a necessary social function by forging character and "the soldier's faith." Conversely, Butler views the institution of war through an economic lens, arguing that the "virtue" described by others is merely a cover for a scheme designed to generate private profit for wealthy industrialists.

Idealism vs. Reality

The text contrasts Woodrow Wilson’s rhetorical justification for war with Erich Maria Remarque’s visceral descriptions of it. Wilson frames war as a grand, democratic crusade for human rights, utilizing American power to achieve global stability. Remarque challenges such "abstractions" by focusing on the "senseless death," mud, and terror of trench warfare, suggesting that the reality of war contradicts the high-minded reasons given by politicians.

The Evolution of the Warrior

Both Butler and Remarque represent the "warrior turned critic." Butler moved from being the most decorated serviceman of his era to an antiwar activist who testified before Congress. Remarque moved from being a draftee to an international literary sensation whose work was so threatening to military-nationalist narratives that the Nazi regime revoked his citizenship and burned his books.

III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. Why was Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. nicknamed "The Great Dissenter"? Answer: He earned this title during his tenure on the U.S. Supreme Court (1902–1932) for his willingness to challenge the majority opinion.

2. According to Smedley Butler, who benefits from war and who pays the price? Answer: A small group of insiders and wealthy industrialists reap enormous profits (the "racket"), while ordinary soldiers pay with their bodies, minds, and lives.

3. What specific historical event in April 1917 marked a shift in Woodrow Wilson’s policy of neutrality? Answer: Wilson’s address to Congress requesting a declaration of war against Germany, concluding that America could no longer remain neutral if the world was to be made safe for democracy.

4. How does Erich Maria Remarque’s "argument" differ in style from the other three men? Answer: Remarque does not use traditional rhetoric or political arguments; instead, he "shows" the reality of war through the eyes of an ordinary soldier, letting the reader draw conclusions from the physical and psychological details.

5. What was the "Business Plot" of 1933? Answer: It was an alleged conspiracy by wealthy industrialists to overthrow President Franklin D. Roosevelt, which Smedley Butler exposed and testified about before Congress.

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Role of Abstraction in Warfare Woodrow Wilson justifies war through the abstractions of "human rights" and "democratic governance," while Erich Maria Remarque presents a "challenge to every abstraction about war." Analyze how these two different approaches—the political and the experiential—shape a citizen’s understanding of conflict. Which approach is more effective for evaluating the "necessity" of a war?

2. The Economic vs. Moral Dimensions of Sacrifice Compare Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr.’s belief in "the soldier's faith" as the highest expression of character with Smedley Butler’s claim that war is a "racket." Can both perspectives be true simultaneously? Discuss whether the personal virtues developed by individual soldiers (as Holmes argues) are invalidated if the war itself is being fought for private profit (as Butler argues).

3. The Evolution of Perspectives Smedley Butler and Erich Maria Remarque both experienced combat firsthand before becoming prominent critics of war. How does direct experience in the "crucible" of war, as Holmes calls it, lead to such different conclusions? Use the biographical details provided to explain why one veteran might celebrate the virtues of service while another exposes the "senseless death" of the trenches.

V. Glossary of Key Terms

  • Business Plot (1933): An alleged conspiracy by wealthy industrialists to overthrow the United States government, exposed by Smedley Butler.
  • Crucible: A severe test or trial. Holmes uses this term to describe how war "forges" human virtues like courage and devotion.
  • League of Nations: An international organization championed by Woodrow Wilson intended to promote democracy and international cooperation.
  • Racket: A fraudulent scheme or business. Butler uses this term to describe the military-industrial system as a way for insiders to make a profit.
  • The Soldier’s Faith: A term used by Holmes to describe the highest expression of human character, characterized by devotion to a cause greater than oneself.
  • Trench Warfare: A form of combat characterized by the "mud, terror, and senseless death" described by Remarque in his depictions of the Western Front.
  • War for Democracy: The central justification used by Woodrow Wilson to explain American intervention in World War I, asserting that American power is necessary to ensure democratic governance globally.
