Study Guide: Perspectives on War—Racket or Crusade?

This study guide explores the diverse arguments regarding the purpose and nature of war, as presented in Chapter 7, Section 2. It examines the perspectives of four distinct figures and provides tools for synthesizing these conflicting viewpoints into a coherent analysis.

Key Concepts and Theoretical Frameworks

The Four Perspectives on War

The text identifies four primary arguments concerning the function and impact of war. These perspectives represent a range of experiences, from political leadership and military service to literary depiction.

Author Core Argument Perspective Summary
Oliver Wendell Holmes War ennobles. War is a "crucible of character." The willingness to die for a cause greater than oneself represents the highest human virtue.
Smedley Butler War is a racket. War is an economic scheme organized by the powerful to enrich themselves while ordinary people pay with their lives.
Woodrow Wilson War is a moral necessity. War is sometimes required to defend democracy and civilization against autocracy and external threats.
Erich Maria Remarque War destroys. The reality of industrial warfare (shelling and death) mocks every abstraction or argument offered in its defense.

Levels of Abstraction (Framing)

Understanding how an argument is constructed requires identifying its "frame" or level of abstraction. Each frame reveals specific truths while concealing others:

  • Personal Experience (Holmes): Focuses on the impact of war on the individual soul and personal virtue.
  • Economic System (Butler): Focuses on the financial motivations—who pays for the war and who profits from it.
  • Political Necessity (Wilson): Focuses on the macro-level implications for democracy, security, and international order.

The Principles of Synthesis

Synthesis is defined as the process of combining multiple arguments, perspectives, or sources of evidence into a coherent analysis. Key principles include:

  • Simultaneous Truths: Acknowledging that multiple, seemingly contradictory viewpoints can be true at once (e.g., war can ennoble a soldier while simultaneously enriching a profiteer).
  • Critical Evaluation: Using rhetorical tools (ethos, pathos, and logos) to distinguish evidence from mere assertion.
  • Identifying Omissions: Asking "What has been left out?" to determine what a specific frame is concealing.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to Oliver Wendell Holmes, what is the primary benefit of war for the individual? Answer: Holmes believed war ennobles the individual, serving as a crucible that builds virtues—such as the willingness to sacrifice for a cause—that peace cannot produce.

2. On what grounds did Woodrow Wilson justify the United States' entry into the First World War? Answer: Wilson argued that war was a moral and political necessity to defend democracy and civilization against the threats of autocracy, specifically citing imperial Germany’s unrestricted submarine warfare and the Zimmermann Telegram.

3. What was Smedley Butler’s primary critique of the war enterprise? Answer: Butler characterized war as a "racket," a corrupt scheme where corporations and the powerful profit enormously while ordinary soldiers suffer poverty and death.

4. How does Erich Maria Remarque’s "argument" differ from those of Holmes, Butler, and Wilson? Answer: Remarque offers no formal argument or abstraction. Instead, he provides a visceral description of the reality of industrial warfare, suggesting that the experience of death and shelling renders abstract justifications meaningless.

5. What is the "Question of Framing" in the context of analyzing arguments about war? Answer: Framing refers to the level of abstraction a writer chooses (personal, economic, or political). Identifying the frame helps a reader see what the writer is highlighting and what they are intentionally or unintentionally leaving out.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Complexity of Simultaneous Truths The text suggests that Holmes, Butler, Wilson, and Remarque can all be "correct" at the same time. Write an essay exploring how war can simultaneously be a "crucible of character" and a "corrupt racket." Discuss how these competing realities complicate the process of making an "honest judgment" about the necessity of conflict.

2. The Role of Synthesis in Critical Reading Synthesis is described not as choosing a side, but as understanding how different positions relate to each other. Evaluate the tools of synthesis mentioned in the text (identifying ethos, pathos, logos, and omissions). How do these tools enable a reader to reach a "considered judgment" rather than a purely emotional one?

3. Abstraction vs. Reality Compare the political and philosophical abstractions of Woodrow Wilson and Oliver Wendell Holmes with the descriptive reality provided by Erich Maria Remarque. To what extent does the "reality of industrial warfare" invalidate the moral and character-based arguments for war? Or can these abstractions survive the lived experience of the soldier?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Abstraction: A conceptual or theoretical way of framing an issue, often removed from concrete, physical realities.
  • Autocracy: A system of government by one person with absolute power; the opposite of the democracy Wilson sought to defend.
  • Crucible: A situation of severe trial, or in Holmes's context, a place where different elements interact to produce something new and virtuous.
  • Ethos: A rhetorical appeal based on the character or credibility of the speaker.
  • Logos: A rhetorical appeal based on logic, reason, and evidence.
  • Pathos: A rhetorical appeal to the emotions of the audience.
  • Racket: A fraudulent or dishonest scheme or business, typically one that involves bribery or intimidation; the term Butler used to describe war.
  • Synthesis: The process of combining multiple perspectives or arguments into a single, coherent analysis that accounts for the strengths of each side.
  • Unrestricted Submarine Warfare: A type of naval warfare used by Germany that Wilson cited as a threat to international order and American security.
  • Zimmermann Telegram: A specific historical document cited by Wilson as evidence of a threat to the United States, justifying the necessity of war.
