# Study Guide: The Enduring Debate on War and Peace

This study guide explores the persistent rhetorical and philosophical arguments surrounding American military intervention from World War II to the present. It focuses on the recurring perspectives of four primary archetypes—Holmes, Butler, Wilson, and Remarque—and how their viewpoints manifest in different historical contexts.

---

### Core Concepts: The Four Perspectives

The debate over war in American history is not a series of isolated events but a continuous discourse involving four durable positions:

*   **Wilsonian Logic:** The belief that democracies must fight to preserve the international order, resist aggression, and promote self-determination or democracy.
*   **Holmesian Martial Virtue:** A vision of war as a site of idealism, purposeful military action, and individual or national virtue.
*   **Butler’s Skepticism:** The argument that war is often a "racket" driven by profit, benefiting industrialists and contractors while questioning the strategic or moral justifications for conflict.
*   **Remarque’s Realism:** An unflinching focus on the gap between patriotic rhetoric and the grim reality of the battlefield, emphasizing the lived experience of the soldier.

---

### Historical Applications

The following table summarizes how these four perspectives have appeared in major American conflicts since 1935:

| Conflict | Wilsonian / Holmesian Elements | Butler / Remarque Elements |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **World War II** | Seen as "the good war"; a clear defense of democracy against Nazi and Fascist threats; vindication of martial virtue. | Fortunes made by industrialists; injustices like Japanese American internment; a gap between rhetoric and battlefield reality. |
| **Korean War** | Framed as a defense of the free world against communist expansion; a test of international order. | Questions about the costs of global leadership; concerns over a military budget that stayed high even in peacetime. |
| **Vietnam War** | Initially justified as making Southeast Asia safe for self-determination and preserving American credibility. | The war was called a "racket"; the Pentagon Papers revealed official deception; writers like Tim O'Brien echoed Remarque’s honesty. |
| **Gulf War** | A coalition enforcing international law against aggression; a restoration of purposeful, decisive military action. | Skeptics noted the presence of Kuwaiti oil reserves, asking if the US would intervene without strategic resources. |
| **Iraq & Afghanistan** | Initial self-defense after 9/11; later justified as "spreading democracy" when WMDs were not found in Iraq. | Contractors made billions; a "revolving door" between defense firms and government; soldiers served multiple tours while the public was detached. |

### Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. Why is World War II considered the "fullest vindication" of Wilson’s logic?**
*Answer:* Because the threats posed by Nazi Germany, Imperial Japan, and Fascist Italy were so extreme that the argument for intervention—that democracies must fight to survive—seemed unanswerable.

**2. How did the outcome of the Korean War contribute to the rise of Butler-style skepticism?**
*Answer:* The war ended in a stalemate rather than a decisive victory, leading Americans to question if the costs of global leadership were worth paying and noting that the military budget did not shrink after the conflict.

**3. What role did the Pentagon Papers play in the rhetorical debate during the Vietnam War?**
*Answer:* The 1971 publication revealed that officials had systematically deceived the public regarding the war’s progress, reinforcing Butler’s argument that war is a racket and that the public is not being told the truth.

**4. How did the justification for the Iraq War change when no weapons of mass destruction (WMDs) were discovered?**
*Answer:* The justification shifted from a logic of necessary self-defense to a Wilsonian argument for spreading democracy.

**5. According to the text, what is the "historical legacy" of the four voices (Holmes, Butler, Wilson, and Remarque)?**
*Answer:* Their legacy is that all four remain active in American public discourse. No single voice has "won"; instead, they provide a permanent framework for debating every new conflict.

### Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Persistence of Rhetoric:** Analyze why the arguments used by Holmes, Butler, Wilson, and Remarque remain "remarkably stable" over nearly a century of conflict. Use examples from the Gulf War or the post-9/11 era to support your analysis.
2.  **The Reality Gap:** Discuss the recurring theme of the "gap between patriotic rhetoric and battlefield reality." How have writers from the generation of Remarque to the generation of the Vietnam War (such as Tim O'Brien or Michael Herr) addressed this gap?
3.  **The Economics of Conflict:** Using the arguments of Butler’s heirs, evaluate the impact of war production, defense contractors, and the "revolving door" on American military policy from World War II through the withdrawal from Afghanistan in 2021.
4.  **The Evolution of Wilsonianism:** Trace the application of Wilson’s logic from the defense of the international order in Korea to the "spreading of democracy" in Iraq. How have the circumstances of these conflicts challenged or reinforced the original framework?

### Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Butler's Heir:** A term used to describe modern skeptics who believe war primarily benefits industrial or political interests rather than the public good.
*   **Cold War Context:** The geopolitical environment following WWII where military interventions (like Korea) were framed as necessary to resist communist expansion and preserve the free world.
*   **Historical Legacy:** The lasting influence of an idea or argument on subsequent generations; measured by its ability to shape how people think about similar questions in the future.
*   **International Order:** The system of laws and norms among nations that Wilsonian logic seeks to defend through collective security and, if necessary, military force.
*   **Martial Virtue:** The Holmesian belief in the inherent moral or national value of military service and decisive, purposeful action.
*   **Pentagon Papers:** A set of documents published in 1971 that revealed systematic government deception regarding the Vietnam War, fueling public distrust.
*   **Revolving Door:** The movement of personnel between roles as government officials and roles in private defense firms, often cited by Butler-style skeptics as a conflict of interest.
*   **Stalemate:** A situation in which no further action or progress by opposing or competitive parties seems possible; specifically used to describe the conclusion of the Korean War.
