# Study Guide: The Political Clash—Wilson vs. Butler

This study guide examines the fundamental political disagreement between Woodrow Wilson and Smedley Butler regarding the justifications for war. While Wilson presents a vision of war as a necessary defense of democratic principles, Butler analyzes it as a mechanism for corporate profit.

---

## Key Concepts

### Wilson’s Political Argument: The Necessary War
Woodrow Wilson’s justification for entry into World War I was based on the belief that while war is a "fearful thing," certain conflicts are unavoidable and morally required.

*   **Particularity vs. Generality:** Wilson distinguished between "war-in-general" (which may be unjust) and a specific war fought for specific reasons.
*   **Immediate Provocations:** Wilson cited Germany’s policy of unrestricted submarine warfare and the Zimmermann Telegram—an attempt by Germany to recruit Mexico into a war against the United States—as direct threats to American lives and sovereignty.
*   **Moral Imperative:** Wilson argued that the autocratic governments of Central Europe threatened the survival of democracy. He famously claimed the war was necessary to make the world "safe for democracy."
*   **Defense of Principles:** Wilson believed a nation must be willing to fight for the principles upon which it was founded, or it would not deserve them.

### Butler’s Political Argument: The Profitable War
Smedley Butler viewed the rhetoric of democracy and patriotism as a facade for economic interests.

*   **Profit as the Primary Motive:** Butler argued that the real reason for war was to protect the investments of American banks and the profits of manufacturers who sold supplies to the Allied powers.
*   **Economic Disparity:** Butler highlighted the gap between the earnings of ordinary soldiers ($30 a month) and the massive profits of corporations like Du Pont, Bethlehem Steel, U.S. Steel, and Anaconda Copper.
*   **Rhetoric as "Window Dressing":** In Butler’s view, high-minded language about freedom is a tool used by those who profit from war to convince the public to support it.
*   **Structural Incentives:** Butler’s analysis focuses on "following the money," suggesting that those who benefit financially from war are the ones who lobby for its occurrence.

## The Timing Problem: Context and Hindsight

A critical element in comparing these two figures is the timing of their writings, which informs their perspectives:

*   **Wilson (1917):** Spoke in real time under extreme pressure. He did not know how the war would end or that the Treaty of Versailles would eventually fail to secure a lasting peace.
*   **Butler (1935):** Wrote with eighteen years of hindsight. He could see the economic data of the war's aftermath and the failure of the peace settlement to produce the democratic order Wilson had promised.

**Note on Analysis:** While hindsight gives Butler a retrospective advantage, it does not necessarily refute Wilson's sincerity. An argument may be reasonable at the time it is made, even if its predictions eventually prove incorrect.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  What two specific German actions did Wilson cite as immediate provocations for entering the war?
2.  How did Wilson distinguish his request for war in 1917 from "war-in-general"?
3.  What term did Butler use to describe Wilson’s language regarding democracy and self-determination?
4.  According to Butler, why were American banks specifically interested in an Allied victory?
5.  List three of the corporations Butler named as major profiteers during the war.
6.  How does the source context describe the difference between "sincerity" and "accuracy" in the context of Wilson’s arguments?
7.  What is the "Timing Problem" when comparing the arguments of Wilson and Butler?

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **Stated Reasons vs. Structural Incentives:** Compare Wilson’s reliance on stated moral principles with Butler’s reliance on economic data. Which method of analysis provides a more comprehensive understanding of why nations go to war? Use evidence from the text to support your conclusion.
2.  **The Ethics of Profit in Just War:** Wilson argued that the fact that some people profit from a just war does not make the war itself unjust. Butler argued that enormous profits make the "stated cause" inseparable from financial interests. Evaluate these two positions. Is it possible for a war to be both principled and profitable?
3.  **The Role of Hindsight:** Discuss how the passage of time influenced the rhetorical power of Butler’s argument. Does writing with the benefit of hindsight make an analysis more objective, or does it unfairly judge leaders for outcomes they could not have predicted?

## Glossary of Key Terms

*   **Autocratic:** Relating to a system of government where one person or a small group has absolute power; Wilson viewed this as a threat to democracy.
*   **Hindsight:** The understanding of a situation or event only after it has happened.
*   **Sovereignty:** The authority of a state to govern itself; Wilson argued Germany’s actions threatened American sovereignty.
*   **Unrestricted Submarine Warfare:** A naval policy where submarines sink vessels such as freighters and tankers without warning; a key provocation for Wilson.
*   **Window Dressing:** A misleading display or presentation designed to create a deceptively favorable impression of something.
*   **Zimmermann Telegram:** A historical document revealing Germany’s attempt to draw Mexico into a war against the United States.
