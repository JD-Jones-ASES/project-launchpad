Study Guide: The Voice from the Trenches — Erich Maria Remarque and the Reality of War

This study guide explores the transition from the abstract rhetoric of war to the concrete, lived experience of the ordinary soldier as depicted in Chapter 5, Section 5 of At the Table: Where the Arguments Meet. It focuses on Erich Maria Remarque’s seminal novel, All Quiet on the Western Front, and its role in challenging the political and philosophical frameworks used to justify or explain World War I.

Key Concepts and Historical Context

The Author: Erich Maria Remarque (1898–1970)

Erich Maria Remarque was a German veteran of World War I. Drafted at eighteen, he served on the Western Front and was wounded by shrapnel. His experiences informed his 1929 novel, All Quiet on the Western Front, which follows a young student named Paul Bäumer. The novel became an international sensation but was later banned and burned by the Nazis, who revoked Remarque's citizenship, forcing him into exile in Switzerland and eventually the United States.

The Western Front

The Western Front was a 440-mile line stretching from the English Channel to the Swiss border. It was characterized by:

  • Trench Warfare: Soldiers lived in earth-dug ditches, often infested with rats and flooded with mud.
  • No Man's Land: The devastated, crater-filled ground between opposing trenches.
  • Stagnation: For most of the war (1914–1918), the front barely moved. For example, the Battle of the Somme resulted in over one million casualties to move the line only six miles.

Fiction as Historical Evidence

While a novel is not a primary source in the same way a speech or pamphlet is, Remarque’s work is cited by historians for its "authenticity of physical and emotional detail." It captures truths about the war—such as the psychological erosion of the "Lost Generation"—that statistics and policy arguments cannot convey.

The Limits of Argument

The text posits that while orators like Holmes, Wilson, and Butler provide frameworks to make sense of war (virtue, democracy, or profit), the actual experience of combat is too chaotic and physical to be contained by these arguments. Every argument about war is, in some sense, incomplete because it cannot fully encompass the immediate reality of the trenches.

Summary of the Primary Source: All Quiet on the Western Front (Chapter VI)

The excerpt depicts Paul Bäumer and his comrades during a period of high tension and heavy bombardment. Key narrative elements include:

  • Omens of Death: The arrival of a double wall of brand-new coffins and the issuance of Edamer cheese and rum are recognized by the veterans as certain signs of a coming offensive and high casualties.
  • The Power of Chance: Survival in the trenches is described as a matter of "Chance." Paul notes that a soldier can be "smashed to atoms" in a bomb-proof shelter or survive ten hours of bombardment in the open. This randomness leads to a state of indifference.
  • The War on Rats: The trenches are infested with "corpse-rats"—repulsive, fat animals that steal the soldiers' bread. The men engage in a desperate, violent "war" against the rats to protect their meager rations.
  • Weaponry and Tactics: The soldiers discuss the impracticality of the bayonet, which often jams or breaks. Instead, they prefer sharpened spades for hand-to-hand combat, as they are more versatile and deadly.
  • Psychological Breakdown: The combination of claustrophobia and sustained bombardment leads to "shell-shock" and madness. Paul describes a recruit who has a fit and must be physically restrained and beaten to prevent him from running into certain death under fire.

Comparative Analysis: Remarque vs. The Orators

The document invites a comparison between Remarque’s "voice from the trenches" and the rhetorical frameworks of other major figures:

Figure Perspective/Framework Remarque’s Counter-Perspective
Oliver Wendell Holmes War as a "school of virtue" and a test of "soldier's faith." The experience is not heroic endurance but desperate, animalistic survival.
Woodrow Wilson War as a necessity to make the world "safe for democracy." The German soldiers suffer the same horrors, told the same lies about duty by their leaders.
Smedley Butler War as a "racket" where corporations profit at the expense of soldiers. To the man in the trench, economic profit is distant and irrelevant; life is measured in seconds.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why did the Nazis burn Remarque’s books and revoke his citizenship?
    • Answer: They viewed his "truth-telling" about the reality of war as a crime against the state and its nationalistic narrative.
  2. What specific items smell of "fir, and pine, and the forest" at the beginning of the excerpt, and what is their significance?
    • Answer: A high wall of new coffins; they signal that the military organization is prepared for massive casualties in the upcoming offensive.
  3. According to Paul, why is the sharpened spade a better weapon than the bayonet?
    • Answer: The spade is more versatile; it can be used for jabbing or striking with great weight, whereas bayonets often jam in the body or break off.
  4. How does the concept of "Chance" affect the soldiers' mindset?
    • Answer: It makes them indifferent. Because survival is random and cannot be determined by skill or caution, they simply "trust their luck."
  5. What is a "corpse-rat"?
    • Answer: A particularly large, repulsive rat found in the trenches, named for its habit of feeding on the dead.
  6. Why do the soldiers avoid each other's eyes as the bombardment begins?
    • Answer: They have "tired faces" and the shared knowledge of the impending horror, particularly the memory of previous bloodbaths like the Somme.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Breakdown of Rhetoric: Analyze how the physical realities described by Remarque (the rats, the mud, the "corpse-rats") undermine the abstract ideals of "honor" and "patriotism" promoted by authority figures like Paul’s schoolteacher.
  2. The Psychology of the Trench: Discuss the psychological effects of prolonged bombardment as depicted in the text. How do "Chance" and "claustrophobia" define the mental state of the soldiers, and how does this compare to the "soldier’s faith" described by Holmes?
  3. The Universal Soldier: Remarque was a German soldier, yet his novel was an international sensation. Based on the text, what elements of his description make the experience of the Western Front universal, regardless of which side a soldier fought on?
  4. Fiction as Truth: The source context suggests that Remarque's novel captures a "truth" that statistics cannot. Argue whether fiction can be a more effective tool for persuasion than political rhetoric in the context of war.

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Claustrophobia: An extreme fear of enclosed spaces; in the trenches, it often triggered psychological breakdowns during bombardments.
  • Dug-out: An underground shelter in a trench, used for protection from artillery fire.
  • Edamer Cheese: Edam cheese, traditionally sold in red wax spheres; its appearance in the trenches was a grim omen of heavy casualties.
  • Flame-throwers: Weapons projecting burning fuel, first used by Germany in 1915; among the most feared trench weapons.
  • No Man's Land: The devastated ground between opposing trench lines, filled with craters and barbed wire.
  • Parapet: The raised bank of earth at the front of a trench providing cover from enemy fire.
  • Skat: A popular German card game for three players, used by soldiers to pass the time and distract themselves.
  • The Somme: A 1916 battle resulting in over one million casualties; used in the text as a benchmark for sustained, horrific shelling.
  • Trench-mortars: Short-range artillery used to fire shells at high angles into enemy trenches.
