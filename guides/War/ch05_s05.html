The Argument That Won

Arguments do not exist in a vacuum. They succeed or fail not only on their logical merits but in the arena of history — where timing, power, audience, and circumstance determine which ideas take hold and which are pushed to the margins. The final section of Chapter 5 traces the historical reception of each writer's argument, asking which one "won" and what that tells us about the relationship between rhetoric and history.

Reception History: A Definition

Reception history: The study of how a text, speech, or idea was received, interpreted, and used by successive audiences over time. Reception history examines not just what a writer intended but how later generations understood, applied, or rejected the work.

Key insight: asking which argument "won" is asking a question about rhetoric and history — not necessarily about which argument was objectively true.

Wilson's Triumph

In both the short and long term, Wilson's argument dominated American foreign policy. The idea that the United States has a moral obligation to defend democracy abroad — that American power should serve American ideals — became the operating framework for American internationalism throughout the twentieth century.

Presidents who echoed Wilson's framework:

  • Franklin Roosevelt (1941): Declared WWII in language of moral necessity
  • Harry Truman (1950): Committed forces to Korea as a defense of freedom against communist aggression
  • John F. Kennedy (1961): Inaugural address — "pay any price, bear any burden" to defend liberty

The specific arguments changed; Wilson's underlying framework did not.

Butler's Exile and Revival

Butler's argument was marginalized for decades after he made it. When WWII began, his blanket condemnation of all war as a racket seemed inadequate. How could you call the war against Hitler a "racket"? The existence of a war that appeared clearly justified undercut Butler's central claim that war is always a scheme for profit.

But Butler's argument found a new and enormous audience during the Vietnam War in the 1960s and 1970s. That war looked to many Americans exactly like the kind of conflict Butler had described: unclear justification, costs borne disproportionately by the poor and working class, and record profits for defense contractors.

War Is a Racket was reprinted and widely distributed during the antiwar movement. Butler's face appeared on antiwar posters and websites. The central irony: Butler the Marine Corps general became a hero of the peace movement.

Holmes' Quiet Persistence

Holmes' martial idealism never became official government policy the way Wilson's internationalism did — but it never disappeared either. It persists in:

  • Military culture and memorial ceremonies
  • The rhetoric of political leaders praising the "greatest generation" or honoring soldiers' sacrifices
  • The broader American cultural belief that hardship and adversity build character

When politicians speak of what soldiers' sacrifice reveals about American character, they are often channeling Holmes' vision of war as a forge of virtue, whether they know it or not.

Remarque's Lasting Shift

Remarque made no political argument and shaped no foreign policy. But he accomplished something that may be more lasting: he changed how millions of people imagine war. Before Remarque, the dominant literary image of war was heroic — knights and cavalrymen, brave charges and glorious last stands. After Remarque, the dominant literary image shifted toward mud, terror, and waste. Every antiwar novel, film, and poem written since 1929 owes something to what he accomplished.

Rhetoric and Historical Timing

Key concept: The success or failure of a rhetorical argument depends not only on the quality of the argument itself but on whether it meets the needs of its audience at a particular historical moment.

Argument When It Dominated When It Faded
Wilson 1917; throughout Cold War When specific wars lose clear justification
Butler 1930s isolationism; Vietnam era When clearly necessary wars arise (WWII)
Holmes Postwar patriotism; times of national threat When war costs become visible
Remarque Always; gains power when abstraction needs grounding —

The debate among these four voices is not over. It is never over. Every new war reopens the same questions, and every generation must decide for itself which arguments hold up and which do not. The tools of rhetorical analysis — identifying ethos, pathos, and logos; detecting omissions and blind spots; distinguishing argument from testimony — are exactly the tools needed to navigate this debate whenever it surfaces again. And it will surface again.
