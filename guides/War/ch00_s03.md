# Study Guide: The World of 1895–1935

This study guide examines the historical context surrounding four major writers whose work spans the period from 1895 to 1935. It focuses on the political, military, and social shifts caused by the First World War and how these events shaped global perspectives on conflict.

---

## Key Historical Concepts

### The Alliance System and the Outbreak of War
In 1914, Europe was divided into two rigid alliance blocs. This system, intended to maintain a balance of power and prevent conflict, instead ensured that a regional dispute would escalate into a continental war. When a Serbian nationalist assassinated Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary on June 28, 1914, the obligations of these alliances were triggered:
*   **The Triple Entente:** Comprised of France, Russia, and Britain.
*   **The Central Powers:** Comprised of Germany, Austria-Hungary, and the Ottoman Empire.

### The Reality of Trench Warfare
The Western Front was defined by a 400-mile line of trenches stretching from the English Channel to the Swiss border. This form of warfare was characterized by:
*   **Attrition:** High casualty rates for minimal territorial gains. For example, the Battle of the Somme (1916) resulted in over one million casualties to move the front line only six miles.
*   **Technological Horror:** Soldiers faced constant artillery bombardment, poison gas, and the squalor of mud and barbed wire.
*   **Human Cost:** Approximately 10 million soldiers died in the war, with millions more wounded or killed by the 1918–1919 influenza pandemic.

### United States Neutrality and Entry
Initially, the United States remained neutral, with President Woodrow Wilson winning re-election in 1916 on the premise of keeping the nation out of the conflict. Two primary factors forced a change in policy:
1.  **Unrestricted Submarine Warfare:** Germany began sinking American merchant ships without warning in early 1917.
2.  **The Zimmermann Telegram:** A secret German proposal to Mexico, suggesting a military alliance against the U.S. in exchange for the return of Texas, New Mexico, and Arizona.

### Post-War Disillusionment
The end of the war did not bring lasting stability. The 1919 Treaty of Versailles forced Germany to accept sole responsibility and pay reparations. In the U.S., the Senate rejected the League of Nations. By the 1930s, a sense of "disillusionment" took hold, fueled by:
*   **Economic Instability:** The rise of fascism in Europe and economic hardship in the U.S.
*   **The Bonus Army (1932):** Impoverished veterans marched on Washington to demand early payment of promised wartime bonuses.
*   **Corporate Skepticism:** Figures like Smedley Butler began to argue that war was a "racket" designed to benefit wealthy industrialists rather than the soldiers who fought it.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

### 1. The Evolution of the American Perspective on War
The document notes that writers like Holmes viewed war through the lens of the Civil War, while later figures like Butler and Remarque viewed it through the trauma of the First World War. Analyze how the scale and nature of modern warfare changed the American public's moral and political justification for conflict between 1895 and 1935.

### 2. The Failure of the "System that Supposedly Prevented War"
The alliance system of 1914 was designed to provide security through mutual defense. Evaluate why this system failed to prevent the war and instead made a continental conflict "almost inevitable." Use specific examples of national mobilizations from the text to support your argument.

### 3. The Domestic Legacy of the Great War
While the war was fought in Europe, its aftermath had significant social and political consequences within the United States. Discuss the significance of the Bonus Army and the rejection of the League of Nations as evidence of the growing disillusionment and domestic tension in the post-war era.

## Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Archduke Franz Ferdinand** | The heir to the Austro-Hungarian throne whose assassination in 1914 triggered the start of WWI. |
| **Battle of the Somme** | A 1916 battle characterized by extreme casualties (over 1 million) and negligible territorial gains. |
| **Bonus Army** | A group of thousands of impoverished WWI veterans who marched on Washington in 1932 demanding early payment of service bonuses. |
| **Business Plot** | An alleged conspiracy by wealthy industrialists to overthrow President Franklin Roosevelt, testified about by Smedley Butler. |
| **Central Powers** | The wartime alliance of Germany, Austria-Hungary, and the Ottoman Empire. |
| **League of Nations** | An international organization proposed by Woodrow Wilson to maintain peace; it was realized on paper but rejected by the U.S. Senate. |
| **Smedley Butler** | A retired Marine general and author of *War Is a Racket* (1935) who criticized the motives behind American military involvement. |
| **Treaty of Versailles** | The 1919 peace treaty that ended WWI, forcing Germany to accept blame and pay reparations. |
| **Triple Entente** | The wartime alliance of France, Russia, and Britain. |
| **Unrestricted Submarine Warfare** | A German naval tactic of sinking merchant ships without warning, which contributed to the U.S. entry into the war. |
| **Zimmermann Telegram** | A coded message from Germany to Mexico proposing an alliance against the U.S.; its revelation caused American outrage. |
