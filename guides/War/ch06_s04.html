Logos: How Are Claims Supported?

Ethos establishes the right to speak. Pathos moves the audience to care. But logos — the logic and evidence that support a claim — is what gives an argument its backbone. Strip away the authority and the emotion, and what remains? Each of our four writers uses a fundamentally different logical method, and understanding those methods — and their vulnerabilities — is the heart of rhetorical analysis.

Holmes: Inductive Generalization

Holmes' logical method is inductive generalization: he begins with personal experience and generalizes outward to a philosophical principle. He fought in the Civil War, observed courage under fire, and concluded that war cultivates virtues — courage, discipline, self-sacrifice — that a comfortable commercial society cannot produce on its own.

The strength of this approach is its vividness. Holmes can point to real men he knew, real moments he witnessed, and real virtues he saw displayed under fire. His evidence carries the authority of lived experience.

The weakness is equally clear: his sample is narrow. He served in one war, in one army, in a handful of engagements. A skeptical reader might ask: Does war always cultivate virtue, or did Holmes happen to serve alongside men who were already virtuous? Do all wars produce the same effects as the Civil War — a conflict many Americans regarded as a moral crusade? And what about the men who came back not ennobled but broken?

Butler: Empirical Data

Butler's logical method is economic analysis supported by specific data. He does not philosophize about the nature of war; he follows the money. His central claim — that war is a racket — is supported by concrete dollar figures: the profits of specific corporations during World War I, the costs borne by taxpayers, the gap between what soldiers were paid and what munitions makers earned.

This is a fundamentally different kind of evidence from Holmes' personal testimony. Butler's numbers can, in principle, be checked. If Du Pont's profits really did increase by the percentages Butler claims, that is a verifiable fact. Butler's logos has the virtue of being testable in a way that Holmes' philosophical claims are not.

But Butler's logic has its own central vulnerability: the gap between evidence and conclusion. Butler proves that some corporations profited from war. He then concludes that profit is the reason wars are fought. This is a much larger claim than his evidence supports. Correlation does not prove causation. It is possible for war to produce profits without those profits being the cause of the war.

Wilson: Deductive Reasoning

Wilson's logical method is moral and geopolitical reasoning — essentially deductive. He begins with a set of principles (the rights of neutral nations, the sovereignty of democratic peoples, the duty of free governments to oppose autocracy) and argues that Germany's actions have violated those principles so thoroughly that war is the only remaining response.

His core deductive chain: (1) Nations have the right to trade freely and navigate the seas without interference. (2) Germany has systematically violated that right through unrestricted submarine warfare. (3) Diplomacy has failed to restore that right. (4) Therefore, armed force is the only means left to defend it.

The strength: this is a tightly constructed chain of reasoning, with each link examinable independently. The vulnerability: the argument depends on accepting Wilson's premises. Is free navigation an absolute right that can never be suspended in wartime? Had every diplomatic option truly been exhausted? And Wilson's second-level argument — that democracy itself is at stake — rests on predictions about German ambitions that could not be verified at the time.

Remarque: Experiential Testimony

Remarque offers no syllogisms, no profit figures, no philosophical principles. His "evidence" is the evidence of the human body: what it feels like to be shelled, to be gassed, to watch a friend die. His logical method, if we can call it that, is experiential testimony — the implicit claim that raw sensory data speaks for itself.

The strength: his evidence is impossible to argue with on its own terms. No one can claim that the experiences Remarque describes did not happen or that they do not matter.

The limitation: experiential testimony alone cannot answer a policy question. Each of the other writers can accept Remarque's evidence while drawing a completely different conclusion from it. Butler might say: "I agree that war is horrible — that is why I want to prevent it by eliminating the profit motive." Wilson might say: "I agree — and that is why I called it a fearful thing. But some evils are worse than war." Holmes might say: "I agree — and that horror is precisely what makes the soldier's faith so admirable." Remarque's testimony is powerful, but it does not, by itself, tell us what to do.

The Four Frameworks Compared

Author Method Strength Vulnerability
Holmes Inductive Vivid, concrete, authoritative Narrow sample; one war generalized to all
Butler Empirical Testable and verifiable Correlation mistaken for causation
Wilson Deductive Tightly constructed chain Depends on contested premises
Remarque Testimonial Irrefutable on its own terms Cannot answer "What should we do?"

Key Terms

  • Logos: The use of logic and evidence to support a claim; the backbone of an argument.
  • Inductive generalization: Reasoning from specific observations to a general principle.
  • Empirical analysis: Drawing conclusions from data that can, in principle, be independently verified.
  • Deductive reasoning: Drawing a conclusion from premises; if premises are true and the structure is valid, the conclusion follows.
  • Experiential testimony: Evidence based on first-hand sensory experience; powerful but unable to resolve policy disputes on its own.
  • Correlation vs. causation: The logical error of concluding that because two things occur together, one caused the other.
