# Study Guide: The Rhetoric of Smedley Butler’s *War Is a Racket* (Chapters 3–5)

This study guide examines the rhetorical strategies, thematic shifts, and core arguments presented in the final chapters of Smedley Butler’s 1935 work, *War Is a Racket*. It explores how Butler transitions from data-driven analysis to emotional appeal and direct policy demands.

---

## Key Concepts and Rhetorical Strategies

### Pathos in the Service of Logos
In Chapter 3, Butler shifts his rhetorical strategy from **logos** (appealing to reason through data and profit margins) to **pathos** (appealing to emotion). However, this is not a mere departure from logic; it is an extension of it. By juxtaposing the massive corporate profits detailed in earlier chapters with the "shattered minds" and "boys in pens" in veterans' hospitals, Butler creates a rhetorical bridge. The emotional response is the evidence: the contrast between who profits and who pays proves the corruption of the system.

### The Plain Style and the Imperative Mood
In Chapter 4, Butler adopts a "plain style" characterized by short, direct, and declarative sentences. Unlike the Victorian oratory of Oliver Wendell Holmes or the formal cadence of Woodrow Wilson, Butler writes like a commander giving orders. He frequently uses the **imperative mood** (e.g., "Let the officers and the directors be conscripted"), projecting an **ethos** of absolute confidence derived from his decades of military service.

### Certainty vs. Correctness
Butler’s rhetoric is marked by absolute certainty, often dismissing alternative views with phrases like "that and nothing else." While this makes his argument powerful, it introduces a logical risk. By presenting his proposals as the only possible solutions, he leaves no room for compromise or the acknowledgment of complex geopolitical realities.

### The Peroration
A peroration is the closing section of an argument intended to summarize main points and move the audience to action. Butler’s peroration in Chapter 5 abandons data and policy for raw emotion and accusation. He concludes with the four-word verdict: "TO HELL WITH WAR!"

### General vs. Specific Arguments
There is a fundamental tension between Butler’s and Woodrow Wilson’s arguments:
*   **Butler’s General Argument:** The entire institution of modern war is a racket that serves private profit.
*   **Wilson’s Specific Argument:** This particular war (WWI) is a necessary defense of democracy and civilization.
Butler argues that the existence of profit proves the absence of a legitimate cause, whereas Wilson’s framework allows for specific wars to be justified as "necessary," even if the broader system is flawed.

## Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Pathos** | An appeal to the audience's emotions, used by Butler to describe the human cost of war. |
| **Logos** | An appeal to reason through evidence, data, and logical structures. |
| **Ethos** | An appeal based on the character or authority of the speaker; Butler relies on his status as a decorated general. |
| **Peroration** | The final part of a speech or written argument, designed to inspire feeling and action. |
| **Imperative Mood** | A grammatical mood that forms a command or request (e.g., "Give capital... thirty days to think it over"). |
| **The Plain Style** | A direct, unadorned method of writing that avoids ornate language or classical allusions. |

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **How does Butler’s word choice in Chapter 3 change the way veterans are perceived?**
    *   *Answer:* He refers to them as "boys" rather than soldiers or men to emphasize their youth and vulnerability. He describes them as being "in pens," evoking the image of animals in cages rather than honored heroes.

2.  **What is the "rhetorical bridge" Butler builds between Chapter 2 and Chapter 3?**
    *   *Answer:* He connects the financial data of corporate profits (Logos) to the physical and mental suffering of veterans (Pathos). The juxtaposition functions as an argument: the human cost is the price paid for corporate gain.

3.  **How does Butler’s writing style in Chapter 4 differ from Woodrow Wilson’s?**
    *   *Answer:* Butler uses the "plain style"—short, blunt, and commanding sentences. Wilson uses a "stately cadence" of formal deliberation and presidential oratory.

4.  **According to Butler, what was the true motivation for America entering World War I?**
    *   *Answer:* He argues it was a "collection operation" for American bankers who had lent billions to the Allies; the war was necessary to ensure those loans were not lost.

5.  **What does the text identify as a potential weakness of Butler’s absolute confidence?**
    *   *Answer:* His certainty may outstrip his evidence. By claiming his way is the "only" way, he closes the door on alternative solutions or the recognition of legitimate security threats.

6.  **What is the significance of the closing line "TO HELL WITH WAR!"?**
    *   *Answer:* It is a blunt, profane capstone that carries the weight of Butler’s combat experience. It signals a move away from policy analysis toward a call for revolt.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Interplay of Emotion and Logic:** Analyze Butler’s claim that "Pathos is an extension of Logos." Is his use of emotional descriptions of veterans' hospitals a valid form of evidence, or does it distract from the policy proposals in Chapter 4? Use specific examples from the text to support your argument.

2.  **A Critique of the Single-Cause Explanation:** Butler reduces the cause of World War I to "money" and Allied debt. Contrast this with the "complex" view that acknowledges both financial interests and genuine security threats (like submarine warfare). Does Butler’s refusal to engage with Wilson’s specific arguments strengthen or weaken his overall thesis that "War is a racket"?

3.  **Comparative Rhetoric: Three Closings:** Compare the closings of Holmes, Wilson, and Butler. Holmes appeals to "nobility," Wilson to "divine guidance," and Butler to "anger." Which approach is most likely to move a modern audience to action, and why? Discuss the relationship between the tone of a peroration and the speaker’s ultimate goal.

4.  **Ethos and Authority:** Butler’s authority comes from his "two Medals of Honor" and "decades of combat." Explore how this identity allows him to use the "Plain Style" and the "Imperative Mood" effectively. Would his argument be as persuasive if written by a civilian or a politician? Why or why not?
