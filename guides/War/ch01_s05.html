Study Guide: "The Soldier's Faith" by Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. (1895)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the historical context, core arguments, and stylistic elements of Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr.’s 1895 address, "The Soldier's Faith." It is designed to facilitate a deep understanding of the transition from the Civil War era to the Gilded Age and the philosophical tensions Holmes identified during this period.

Key Concepts and Historical Context

The Post-War Transition

By 1895, thirty years had passed since the end of the American Civil War at Appomattox Courthouse. The nation was undergoing a demographic and cultural shift:

  • The Thinning Ranks: Civil War veterans were aging, and the generation that experienced the war firsthand was being replaced by a generation that had only known "the sound of commerce."
  • Memorial Day Origins: Originally known as "Decoration Day," the holiday began as a way to honor Union dead by decorating graves with flowers. By the 1890s, it had evolved into a national event featuring parades and speeches organized by groups like the Grand Army of the Republic.

The Gilded Age

The America of 1895 was defined by its status as the world’s largest industrial economy.

  • Industrial Transformation: The landscape was reshaped by steel mills, railroads, and factories.
  • The Titans of Industry: Enormous fortunes were amassed by individuals such as Andrew Carnegie, John D. Rockefeller, and Cornelius Vanderbilt.
  • Shift in Values: Holmes observed a national shift from "honor to profit" and from "duty to comfort." He expressed concern that a society focused entirely on material prosperity would lose the capacity for sacrifice and the willingness to risk everything for a cause larger than the individual.

The Purpose of the Speech

Delivered on May 30, 1895, to an audience of Harvard students, faculty, and veterans, the speech serves as a "carefully constructed case" for the virtues of war. Holmes argued that the qualities cultivated in battle—courage, faith, and the ability to act without guarantees—are essential to the health of a civilization.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. What was the original name of Memorial Day, and what was its initial purpose?

Answer: It was originally called Decoration Day, and its purpose was to honor the Union dead by decorating their graves with flowers.

2. Who were the three industrial "titans" mentioned as symbols of the Gilded Age’s wealth?

Answer: Andrew Carnegie, John D. Rockefeller, and Cornelius Vanderbilt.

3. What specific concern did Holmes have regarding the "generation of commerce"?

Answer: He feared that a society devoted entirely to material comfort and profit would lose its capacity for sacrifice and its willingness to take risks for ideals larger than self-interest.

4. In what setting and to what specific audience did Holmes deliver "The Soldier's Faith"?

Answer: He delivered the speech on May 30, 1895, at a Memorial Day gathering at Harvard. The audience included students, faculty, Boston civic leaders, and Civil War veterans.

5. How does the text define a "periodic sentence"?

Answer: A periodic sentence is one in which the main idea is not completed until the end. Subordinate clauses are stacked to create suspense and emphasis, leading to an emotional or forceful resolution.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Tension Between Commerce and Honor Analyze Holmes’ critique of the Gilded Age. How does he contrast the "sound of cannon" with the "sound of commerce," and why does he believe the virtues of a soldier are necessary even in a time of peace and prosperity? Use details from the text regarding the shift in national aspirations.

2. The Duality of Holmes’ Perspective The text notes that Holmes’ argument was controversial. Some saw him as a prophet of high ideals, while others believed he was romanticizing the horrors of war. Based on the summary of his arguments, construct a debate evaluating these two interpretations. Does Holmes provide a necessary moral instruction, or is his view of war dangerously nostalgic?

3. Style as Substance: The Periodic Sentence Holmes’ use of the periodic sentence is described as "creating an experience" rather than just making an argument. Discuss how the structure of a sentence—delaying the main point and building "like a wave breaking on shore"—reinforces Holmes’ themes of faith and "acting without guarantees."

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Appomattox Courthouse The site where Robert E. Lee surrendered in 1865, marking the end of the Civil War.
Decoration Day The original name for Memorial Day; a day established to honor fallen soldiers.
Gilded Age The late 19th-century era in the U.S. characterized by rapid industrial growth, the amassing of great fortunes, and a focus on commerce.
Grand Army of the Republic A veterans' organization that organized Memorial Day ceremonies and public remembrance events.
Periodic Sentence A stylistic device where the main clause or point is placed at the end of the sentence for emphasis and suspense.
The Soldier’s Faith Holmes’ term for the mindset of courage, sacrifice, and the willingness to act on conviction without the certainty of the outcome.
"Sound of Commerce" Holmes’ metaphor for the Gilded Age’s preoccupation with business, profit, and material gain.
