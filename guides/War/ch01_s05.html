The Rhetoric Examined

Holmes was not only a philosopher; he was a craftsman. "The Soldier's Faith" derives its power not just from its ideas but from the extraordinary skill with which those ideas are expressed. This section examines how Holmes wields the three classical appeals — ethos, pathos, and logos — and how his Victorian prose style becomes part of the argument itself.

The Victorian Grand Style

Holmes writes in the Victorian grand style of oratory: long, complex sentences, elevated diction, classical allusions, and a deliberate building of emotional intensity toward climactic moments. This style assumes an educated audience willing to follow a sentence through multiple subordinate clauses before arriving at the main idea.

The difficulty is deliberate. Holmes' long, winding sentences enact the faith he describes — the willingness to follow a path whose destination you cannot yet see, trusting the journey will prove worthwhile.

The Periodic Sentence

The periodic sentence is Holmes' signature technique.

Definition: A periodic sentence delays its main clause until the very end, building suspense through a series of subordinate clauses or phrases. The reader must hold the entire accumulating structure in mind until the sentence reaches its resolution. The effect is one of gathering momentum — like a wave that rises before finally breaking.

The famous battlefield passage is a single sentence exceeding two hundred words. Its structure:

  • Stacked conditional clauses: "if you have... if you have... if you have..."
  • Each clause adds a new sensory detail of combat, escalating the stakes
  • From watching the enemy to feeling the burst of shell to slipping on a dead man's body
  • The sentence does not resolve until the final clause: "you know that there is such a thing as the faith I spoke of."

The audience is forced to undergo a miniature version of the soldier's experience — moving forward through uncertainty toward a conclusion not yet visible.

The Three Classical Appeals

Appeal How Holmes Uses It
Ethos Speaks as a thrice-wounded veteran; authority is written on his body — no one can say he doesn't know what he's talking about
Pathos Battlefield descriptions, the funeral scene, and the songs of the sword create reverence, grief, and admiration
Logos Offers a coherent worldview: struggle is the fundamental fact of existence; combat virtues are the highest expression of human potential

What makes the speech so effective is not any single technique but how seamlessly these three appeals work together — each reinforcing the others.

Close Reading: The Central Creed

After the baroque complexity of the battlefield passage, Holmes suddenly shifts to plain, almost blunt sentences: "I do not know what is true. I do not know the meaning of the universe." The contrast is arresting. He strips away every intellectual pretension and presents himself as a man standing in the void — and from that void delivers his one certainty: the soldier's faith.

The key adjectives Holmes uses reveal how radical his claim is: blindly accepted duty, a cause he little understands, a plan of which he has no notion, tactics of which he does not see the use. The faith Holmes celebrates is faith without understanding — faith as an act of pure will.

The Argument's Vulnerability

This is where Holmes' rhetoric is both most powerful and most exposed. If the value of faith lies precisely in its blindness — in its independence from understanding, proof, or outcome — then what prevents it from serving a wicked cause as easily as a just one? Holmes offers no answer. The rhetorical momentum of the speech tends to carry readers past this question. Noticing it is an act of critical thinking.

A Caution About Rhetoric

A beautifully constructed argument is not necessarily a true one. Rhetoric is the art of persuasion, not the art of demonstration. As you reflect on the speech, the key question is: Am I persuaded because the argument is sound, or because the language is beautiful? Distinguishing between the two is one of the most important skills in reading any persuasive text.

Key Terms

  • Periodic sentence: A sentence whose main clause is withheld until the end, building suspense and momentum
  • Victorian grand style: Late nineteenth-century oratorical tradition marked by complexity, elevation, and literary allusion
  • Ethos / Pathos / Logos: The three classical appeals (credibility, emotion, logic)
  • Elegy: A mournful, meditative poem or speech honoring the dead
