# Study Guide: Woodrow Wilson’s Address to Congress (April 2, 1917)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of President Woodrow Wilson’s historic address to a joint session of Congress, which led to the United States' entry into World War I. The document outlines the progression of Wilson’s arguments from specific grievances against German naval policy to a broad vision for global democracy.

---

### Key Concepts and Themes

#### 1. The Failure of Armed Neutrality
Initially, the United States attempted to maintain its rights through "armed neutrality"—arming merchant ships for self-defense while remaining officially neutral. In this address, Wilson concludes that this policy is no longer sustainable. He argues that because submarines are "outlaws" that strike without warning, they cannot be defended against like traditional "privateers" or visible cruisers. He posits that armed neutrality is "ineffectual" and likely to draw the nation into war without the full rights or effectiveness of a recognized belligerent.

#### 2. Unrestricted Submarine Warfare as "Warfare Against Mankind"
The primary grievance cited is the Imperial German Government's decision to sink every vessel approaching the ports of Great Britain, Ireland, or the Mediterranean, regardless of flag, cargo, or intent. Wilson characterizes this as a "wanton and wholesale destruction of the lives of noncombatants," including women and children. He emphasizes that while property can be paid for, the lives of peaceful and innocent people cannot be.

#### 3. Democracy vs. Autocracy
Wilson draws a sharp distinction between the German people and their government. He argues that:
*   **The German People:** Are victims of their own government; they did not approve or have prior knowledge of the war.
*   **The German Government (Autocracy):** Is an "irresponsible government" that acts without the consent of its people. Wilson asserts that autocratic governments cannot be trusted to keep peace, as they operate through intrigue, spies, and "cunningly contrived plans of deception."

#### 4. The Goal: "A World Safe for Democracy"
Wilson’s most famous declaration is that "the world must be made safe for democracy." He argues that the United States has no selfish ends—no desire for conquest, dominion, or material compensation. Instead, the U.S. acts as a "champion of the rights of mankind," seeking to establish a "League of Honor" composed of democratic nations to ensure future peace and justice.

#### 5. Practical Requirements for War
Wilson outlines several immediate steps the U.S. must take to prepare for the conflict:
*   **Cooperation with Allies:** Extending liberal financial credit and material resources to nations already at war with Germany.
*   **Military Mobilization:** Adding at least 500,000 men to the armed forces based on the principle of "universal liability to service."
*   **Naval Preparation:** Equipping the navy specifically to deal with submarine threats.
*   **Economic Responsibility:** Funding the war through "well conceived taxation" rather than relying entirely on loans, to protect the public from the hardships of inflation.

### Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. On what date did Woodrow Wilson deliver this address to Congress?**
*Answer:* April 2, 1917.

**2. What did the Imperial German Government announce on February 1st that changed U.S. policy?**
*Answer:* They announced the use of submarines to sink all vessels approaching the ports of their enemies, disregarding previous promises to spare passenger boats and give warnings.

**3. Why did Wilson believe that submarines could not be dealt with under existing international law regarding "privateers"?**
*Answer:* Submarines are "outlaws" that attack from a submerged position without warning, making it impossible for merchant ships to defend themselves as they would against visible craft.

**4. What specific event regarding Mexico is cited as evidence of Germany's "hostile purpose"?**
*Answer:* An intercepted note to the German Minister at Mexico City, which revealed an attempt to stir up enemies against the United States.

**5. How did Wilson characterize the recent political changes in Russia?**
*Answer:* He described the Russian people as "democratic at heart" and noted that they had recently shaken off an autocracy, making Russia a "fit partner for a League of Honor."

**6. What was Wilson’s stance on the German people living within the United States?**
*Answer:* He described the millions of German-born Americans as "true and loyal," stating that they should be treated with friendship, though any "disloyalty" would be met with "stern repression."

### Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Evolution of Neutrality:** Analyze Wilson’s transition from "armed neutrality" to a formal declaration of war. Why did he believe that neutrality was "no longer feasible or desirable" in the context of modern autocratic aggression?
2.  **The Moral Architecture of the Speech:** Wilson frames the war not as a quest for territory but as a "vindication of right." Discuss how he uses the concept of "human right" versus "property right" to justify the gravity of leading the nation into a "terrible and disastrous" war.
3.  **Democracy as a Prerequisite for Peace:** Evaluate Wilson’s argument that a "steadfast concert for peace" can only be maintained by a "partnership of democratic nations." Does the text suggest that peace is possible with autocratic governments? Use his descriptions of "intrigue" and "inner circles" to support your answer.

### Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition Based on Source Context |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Armed Neutrality** | The policy of arming merchant ships for self-defense while maintaining a non-warring status; Wilson eventually deemed this "ineffectual" and "impracticable." |
| **Autocratic** | Relating to a government controlled by a single person or small group with absolute power, operating without the consent or knowledge of the people. |
| **Belligerent** | A nation or person engaged in active warfare. |
| **Count Tarnowski** | The Austro-Hungarian Ambassador whose credentials Wilson refused to accept after Austria-Hungary endorsed Germany’s submarine policy. |
| **Imperial German Government** | The government of the German Empire (1871–1918) headed by Kaiser Wilhelm II, which Wilson blamed for the war. |
| **League of Honor** | Wilson’s vision of a partnership of democratic nations committed to collective security and the interests of mankind. |
| **Privateer** | A privately owned ship authorized by a government to attack enemy vessels; an outdated concept Wilson argued did not apply to submarine warfare. |
| **Punctilio** | Strict or careful attention to the details of correct behavior or procedures. |
| **Safe for Democracy** | Wilson's central justification for the war, arguing that the conflict's purpose was to protect free peoples from authoritarian aggression. |
| **Universal Liability to Service** | The principle that all citizens are subject to being called for military service; the basis for Wilson's plan to raise an army of 500,000 men. |
