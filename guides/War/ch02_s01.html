Study Guide: Smedley Butler’s "War Is a Racket" (Chapters 1–2)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the first two chapters of Smedley Butler’s 1935 work, War Is a Racket. It outlines Butler’s central thesis, provides a detailed breakdown of the economic data he presents, and offers practice questions to test comprehension and encourage deeper analysis.

I. Executive Summary and Central Thesis

In the opening chapters of his pamphlet, Smedley Butler—a retired high-ranking military officer—asserts that war is a "racket": a scheme conducted for the benefit of a small "inside" group at the expense of the masses.

Core Arguments:

  • The Nature of the Racket: A racket is defined as something that is not what it seems to the public. War is the only racket where profits are measured in dollars and losses in human lives.
  • Economic Disparity: While the general public pays the bill through taxation, "blood gains," and physical suffering, a handful of industrialists, bankers, and speculators reap massive fortunes.
  • The Cycle of Conflict: Victorious nations acquire territory that is then exploited by the same few who profited from the war, while the public shoulders the ongoing economic instability.
  • The Human Toll: Butler contrasts the "bloodless" profits of the elite with the "horrible accounting" of the soldier: gravestones, mangled bodies, shattered minds, and broken homes.

II. Key Evidence: The Profits of War

Butler supports his claims by comparing the average annual profits of major corporations during the pre-war period (1910–1914) to their profits during the World War I period (1914–1918).

Sector-Specific Data (Senate Document No. 259)

Butler cites government reports indicating that profits under 25% were rare during the war:

  • Coal Companies: Made between 100% and 7,856% on their capital stock.
  • Chicago Meat Packers: Doubled and tripled their earnings.
  • Bankers: While their profits remained largely secret due to their status as partnerships, Butler identifies them as the primary beneficiaries of war financing.

III. Examples of Military Waste and Profiteering

Butler details several instances where the government purchased excessive or useless equipment, ensuring profits for manufacturers regardless of military necessity.

  • Footwear: The government purchased 35,000,000 pairs of hobnailed shoes for 4,000,000 soldiers. After the war, 25,000,000 pairs remained unused.
  • Cavalry Equipment: Hundreds of thousands of McClellan saddles were purchased for use overseas, despite the fact that there was no American cavalry in France.
  • Mosquito Netting: 20,000,000 nets were sold to the government for soldiers in France, where there were no mosquitoes. An additional 40,000,000 yards of netting were also sold.
  • Aviation: $1,000,000,000 was spent on airplane engines that never left the ground; not a single plane or motor ordered with that money reached a battle in France.
  • Nautical Waste: $635,000,000 was spent on wooden ships that were unable to float and eventually sank.
  • Miscellaneous: 6,000 buckboards (carriages) were purchased for colonels but never used; twelve dozen 48-inch wrenches were purchased for which only one nut in the country (at Niagara Falls) was large enough.

IV. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does Butler define a "racket"?
    • Answer: A scheme or enterprise carried on for private profit under the guise of public service, where the "inside" group understands the true nature of the operation while the public remains unaware.
  2. According to Butler’s research, how many new millionaires and billionaires were created in the United States during World War I?
    • Answer: At least 21,000.
  3. What was the total estimated cost of World War I to the United States, and what portion of that was estimated as pure profit?
    • Answer: The total cost was approximately $52,000,000,000, with $16,000,000,000 of that estimated as profit.
  4. What was George Washington’s advice that Butler claims the United States ignored?
    • Answer: His warning against "entangling alliances."
  5. Why does Butler argue that current (1935) plans to "limit profits" in wartime are insufficient?
    • Answer: Because the plans only suggest limiting financial gains but do nothing to limit the loss of life or the physical suffering of the soldiers.
  6. What specific statistic does Butler use to contrast the cost of trade in China versus the potential cost of a war to protect it?
    • Answer: Trade with China was worth $90,000,000 a year, but a war to protect it would cost tens of billions of dollars and hundreds of thousands of lives.

V. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Metaphor of the Racket: Analyze Butler’s use of the word "racket." Why is this term effective for his argument, and how does he use the comparison to organized crime (like bootlegging) to challenge the "patriotic" narrative of war?
  2. Human vs. Economic Accounting: Butler states that war is the only enterprise where "profits are reckoned in dollars and the losses in lives." Discuss how Butler uses specific financial data and rhetorical questions about the experiences of soldiers to highlight this disparity.
  3. The Critique of Patriotism: Butler describes various industries as "patriotic corporations" and "loyal citizens" while citing their massive profit increases. How does he use irony to expose what he perceives as the hypocrisy of war-time industrialism?
  4. The Geopolitical Context of 1935: Based on the text, what were the specific international tensions Butler observed in the mid-1930s (e.g., Mussolini, Hitler, the "Open Door" policy in China)? How did these events inform his urgency in writing the pamphlet?

VI. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Dollfuss (Engelbert): Chancellor of Austria who was assassinated by Austrian Nazis in 1934, an event Butler cites as a precursor to war.
  • Jugoslavia (Yugoslavia): A kingdom in southeastern Europe that was a source of tension in the 1930s following the assassination of King Alexander.
  • McClellan Saddles: A specific military saddle design; Butler uses them as an example of wasteful government spending for non-existent cavalry.
  • Nye Committee: A Senate committee that probed the munitions industry and its wartime profits, which Butler mentions as having "hardly scratched the surface."
  • Racket: Butler’s central metaphor; a scheme for private profit conducted under the guise of public service.
  • Rhetorical Question: A question asked to make a point rather than elicit an answer. Butler uses these frequently to challenge the reader's assumptions about who fights and who profits.
  • Temerity: Excessive boldness or audacity; Butler uses this to describe diplomats who claim war is not in the making despite 40,000,000 men being under arms.
