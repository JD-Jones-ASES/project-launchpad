The Man Behind the Pamphlet

To understand why War Is a Racket carries such extraordinary rhetorical force, you must first understand who wrote it. Smedley Darlington Butler (1881–1940) was not a pacifist, an academic, or an armchair critic. He was a Major General in the United States Marine Corps — at the time of his retirement in 1931, the most decorated Marine in the nation's history. His critique of war draws its power from the same source as his fame: thirty-three years of nearly continuous combat service.

Early Life and the Paradox of Service

Butler was born in 1881 in West Chester, Pennsylvania, into a prominent Quaker family. The Quaker pacifist tradition ran deep in the Butler household, which makes his choice of career all the more striking. At sixteen, he lied about his age to enlist in the Marine Corps during the Spanish-American War of 1898. He would spend the next thirty-three years in uniform, and almost all of that time in active combat.

The core irony of Butler's life is visible from the start: a boy raised in pacifism who spent his adult life as one of America's most effective warriors. This tension never left him. It is the engine of War Is a Racket.

The Service Record

Butler's career took him to every major theater of American military operations from 1898 to 1931:

  • Philippines — the insurrection that followed the Spanish-American War
  • China — the Boxer Rebellion, 1900
  • Honduras and Panama — 1903
  • Nicaragua — 1910
  • Veracruz, Mexico — 1914
  • Haiti — commanded the Gendarmerie d'Haïti during the American occupation
  • France (WWI) — commanded Camp Pontanezen, the largest American debarkation camp in Europe

His decorations included the Marine Corps Brevet Medal, the Medal of Honor twice, the Haitian Médaille Militaire, and the Distinguished Service Medal. He was one of the youngest major generals in Marine Corps history.

The Banana Wars

The military interventions that consumed most of Butler's career — in Central America, the Caribbean, and Mexico — are collectively known as the Banana Wars. These were not wars declared by Congress or fought for national survival. They were interventions ordered by presidents and the State Department, frequently at the behest of American business interests. Butler would later describe his role with brutal candor: he had been "a muscle man for Big Business."

This admission is essential context. Butler did not arrive at his critique of war from the outside. He enforced American commercial interests abroad with rifle and bayonet. He put down rebellions that threatened American-owned plantations and railroads. He knew exactly what he was denouncing — and he knew it from the inside.

Ethos: Authority from Scars

Butler's authority — his ethos — comes not from his education or eloquence but from his scars. When he says that war enriches a few at the expense of the many, he is not speculating. He is reporting what he saw over three decades of watching money flow from government contracts to private corporate hands.

This is what makes War Is a Racket so difficult to dismiss. When a career pacifist condemns war, critics can dismiss him as naive or uninformed. When the most decorated Marine of his generation does so, the objection is harder to raise. Butler had done the thing he was denouncing — and he knew it.

Key Terms

  • Ethos — A speaker's credibility, derived from character and experience. Butler's ethos comes from his combat record, not his education.
  • Banana Wars — U.S. military interventions in Latin America and the Caribbean (roughly 1898–1934), often conducted on behalf of American business interests.
  • Gendarmerie d'Haïti — Military police force established during the American occupation of Haiti, which Butler commanded.
  • Camp Pontanezen — The largest American debarkation camp in Europe during WWI, commanded by Butler in France.
