Study Guide: War Is a Racket by Smedley Butler (Chapters 1–2)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the first two chapters of Smedley Butler’s 1935 work, War Is a Racket. It details Butler’s central thesis, the economic data supporting his claims, and the specific instances of wartime profiteering he identifies.

Part 1: Key Concepts and Core Thesis

The Definition of a "Racket"

Butler defines a racket as an enterprise that is not what it seems to the majority of people. It is a scheme conducted for the benefit of a small "inside" group at the expense of the masses. He asserts that war is the oldest, most profitable, and most vicious racket in existence, uniquely international in scope.

The Central Argument

The document posits that war is the only enterprise where "profits are reckoned in dollars and the losses in lives." While the general public pays the bill through taxation, death, and social instability, a small group of "insiders"—including munitions makers, bankers, and shipbuilders—amass massive fortunes.

The Economic Shift

Butler tracks the United States' transition from a nation with little interest in international entanglements to one deeply involved in foreign affairs.

  • Pre-1898: The U.S. national debt was approximately $1 billion, with no territory outside North America.
  • Post-World War I: The national debt jumped to over $25 billion.
  • The Trade Balance: While the U.S. saw a favorable trade balance of $24 billion over twenty-five years, Butler argues this trade could have been achieved without the cost of war, which effectively left the nation "running a little behind."

Specific Instances of Waste and Fraud

Butler cites several examples where the government purchased excessive or useless supplies to benefit manufacturers:

  • Footwear: 35 million pairs of hobnailed shoes were purchased for 4 million soldiers. 25 million pairs remained after the war.
  • Saddles: Hundreds of thousands of McClellan saddles were purchased for the cavalry despite there being no American cavalry overseas.
  • Mosquito Netting: 20 million nets and 40 million additional yards of netting were sold to the government for use in France, where there were no mosquitoes. None reached France.
  • Aviation: $1 billion was spent on airplane engines that never left the ground; not a single plane or motor ordered with that money saw battle.
  • Shipbuilding: $635 million was spent on wooden ships that were unable to float and eventually sank.
  • Tools: 48-inch wrenches were purchased for which only one type of nut in the world existed (at Niagara Falls).

Part 3: Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How many new millionaires and billionaires were created in the United States during World War I, according to tax returns?
    • Answer: At least 21,000.
  2. What was the total cost of World War I to the United States, and how much of that was pure profit?
    • Answer: The war cost approximately $52 billion, of which $16 billion was estimated to be profit.
  3. According to Senate Document No. 259, what was the range of profits made by coal companies on their capital stock during the war?
    • Answer: Between 100 per cent and 7,856 per cent.
  4. Why does Butler claim the profits of bankers are more difficult to track than those of industrial corporations?
    • Answer: Because bankers operate as partnerships rather than incorporated organizations, they are not required to report to stockholders, keeping their profits secret.
  5. What was the "bill" paid by the general public for war, as described by Butler?
    • Answer: Gravestones, mangled bodies, shattered minds, broken hearts/homes, economic instability, depression, and generations of back-breaking taxation.
  6. What trade value with China does Butler cite, and how does he compare it to the potential cost of a war with Japan?
    • Answer: Trade with China was $90 million a year. Butler argues that a war to protect this trade (or $200 million in Philippine investments) would cost tens of billions of dollars and hundreds of thousands of lives.

Part 4: Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Rhetoric of Patriotism vs. Economic Reality: Analyze how Butler distinguishes between the public justification for war (patriotism, "saving the world for democracy") and the underlying financial motivations of the "inside" group. Use specific examples of corporate profit margins to support your analysis.
  2. The Human vs. Financial Ledger: Butler claims that in the war racket, "profits are reckoned in dollars and the losses in lives." Evaluate this statement by comparing the specific financial gains of companies like International Nickel or the du Ponts with the physical and mental consequences Butler lists for the soldiers.
  3. Critique of Government Oversight: Discuss Butler’s view of the government's role in the war racket. Address his examples of wasteful spending (such as the wooden ships and mosquito netting) and his skepticism toward the 1935 committee formed to "limit profits."
  4. Geopolitical Instability in 1935: Based on the text, describe the international climate Butler observed in Europe and the Orient. How does he use the actions of contemporary figures like Mussolini and Hitler to argue that another "racket" was in the making?

Part 5: Glossary of Important Terms

  • Dollfuss (Engelbert Dollfuss): The Chancellor of Austria who was assassinated by Austrian Nazis in 1934 during a failed coup attempt.
  • Du Ponts: The family and the chemical company (E. I. du Pont de Nemours) that served as major manufacturers of gunpowder and explosives during WWI.
  • Jugoslavia (Yugoslavia): A kingdom in southeastern Europe (1918–1941) comprising territories such as Serbia, Croatia, and Slovenia.
  • McClellan Saddles: A specific military saddle design used by the U.S. Army; Butler notes they were purchased in bulk despite a lack of cavalry in the WWI European theater.
  • Racket: Butler’s central metaphor; a scheme or enterprise carried on for private profit under the guise of public service.
  • Rhetorical Question: A question asked to make a point rather than to elicit an answer, used frequently by Butler to challenge the reader's assumptions.
  • Temerity: Excessive boldness or audacity; reckless daring. Butler uses this to describe diplomats who claim war is not in the making despite millions of men being under arms.
