Study Guide: The Reception History of War Arguments

This study guide explores the legacies of four pivotal arguments regarding war, as outlined in the text "The Argument That Won." It examines how these arguments—framed by Woodrow Wilson, Smedley Butler, Oliver Wendell Holmes, and Erich Maria Remarque—have been received, interpreted, and applied across different historical eras.

Key Concepts and Themes

Reception History and Historical Timing

The success of an argument is rarely determined by logic alone. Instead, it is governed by Reception History: the study of how texts and ideas are received and used by successive audiences over time. The primary factor in an argument's success is Historical Timing—whether an idea meets the specific needs or sentiments of an audience at a particular moment.

The Four Frameworks of War

The text identifies four distinct ways of conceptualizing war and its purpose:

Framework Key Proponent Central Argument Historical Legacy
Moral Internationalism Woodrow Wilson The U.S. has a moral obligation to defend democracy; military power serves moral ideals. Became the dominant framework for 20th-century U.S. foreign policy (FDR, Truman, JFK).
Economic Skepticism Smedley Butler War is a "racket" served by economic interests and profits disguised as patriotism. Marginalized during WWII but revived as a central pillar of the Vietnam-era antiwar movement.
Martial Idealism Oliver Wendell Holmes Military service and combat build character and reveal the best in human beings. Persists in military culture, memorial ceremonies, and the concept of hardship building virtue.
Realistic Testimony Erich Maria Remarque War is defined by mud, terror, and waste rather than glory or heroism. Shifted the dominant literary and cultural image of war from heroic to tragic.

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Internationalism: The principle or policy of cooperation among nations, specifically the belief that the United States should actively engage in world affairs rather than remain isolated.
  • Martial Idealism: The belief that war serves as a "forge of virtue," testing individuals and strengthening the moral fabric of society through sacrifice and discipline.
  • Reception History: The study of how a text, speech, or idea was interpreted and used by audiences over time, focusing on how later generations applied or rejected the work regardless of the author's original intent.
  • Rhetoric: The art of effective or persuasive speaking or writing, especially the exploitation of figures of speech and other compositional techniques.
  • The "Racket": A term used by Smedley Butler to describe war as a scheme for profit, where the costs are borne by the working class while corporations earn record profits.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why was Woodrow Wilson’s argument considered the "winner" of the 20th century? Wilson’s framework of moral internationalism became the operating standard for American foreign policy, influencing leaders like Franklin Roosevelt, Harry Truman, and John F. Kennedy to justify military action as a defense of freedom and democracy.

  2. How did the start of World War II affect the reception of Smedley Butler’s "War Is a Racket" argument? Butler's argument was marginalized in 1941. Because the war against Hitler was seen by most Americans as genuinely necessary and justified, Butler's claim that all war is merely a scheme for profit seemed inadequate and was largely ignored.

  3. In what ways does Oliver Wendell Holmes’ vision of war persist in modern American culture? His ideas survive in military culture, memorial services, and political rhetoric regarding the "greatest generation." It is also seen in the broader cultural belief that adversity and hardship are essential for building character.

  4. What was the "Quiet Power" of Remarque’s work? Remarque did not change policy, but he changed imagination. He shifted the cultural representation of war away from knights and heroic charges toward the grim reality of filth, fear, and wasted life.

  5. According to the text, why did Butler’s argument experience a surge in popularity during the Vietnam War? The Vietnam War mirrored Butler’s descriptions: it was a conflict with unclear justifications where the poor suffered the most while defense contractors profited, making his "who profits?" question relevant again.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Interplay of Rhetoric and History: Analyze the statement: "The same argument can be brilliant and ineffective, or crude and world-changing, depending on when and where it is made." Use the historical receptions of Wilson and Butler to support your analysis. How does historical context validate or invalidate a rhetorical position?

  2. The Evolution of the Image of War: Trace the shift from the "heroic" image of war to the "realistic" image as influenced by Erich Maria Remarque. Compare this to Holmes' "Martial Idealism." Can these two perspectives—war as a forge of virtue and war as a waste of life—coexist in a society’s consciousness?

  3. Moral vs. Economic Justifications: Compare and contrast Wilson’s "moral necessity" with Butler’s "economic racket." How have these two frameworks competed for dominance in American public discourse since 1917? Provide examples of how modern conflicts might reopen the debate between these two voices.

  4. The Tools of Rhetorical Analysis in Modern Citizenship: The text concludes by stating that tools like identifying ethos, pathos, and logos are necessary to navigate ongoing debates about war. Discuss how these tools help a citizen distinguish between "argument" and "testimony" when evaluating a government's call to military action.
