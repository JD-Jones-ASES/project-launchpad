The Political Clash

The philosophical debate between Holmes and Butler concerns what war does to human character. The political debate — between Wilson and Butler — concerns something more immediate and more urgent: whether a specific war is justified, even if war in general is corrupt. Wilson and Butler are not just arguing about abstract principles; they are arguing about the real decision made in April 1917 when the United States entered the First World War.

Wilson's Case: The Necessary War

Woodrow Wilson did not celebrate war. He openly called the decision to enter WWI a "fearful thing" and acknowledged it would cost American lives. His argument was not that war is good but that this war was unavoidable. Wilson cited three pillars:

  1. Unrestricted submarine warfare: Germany was threatening American lives and commerce on the high seas.
  2. The Zimmermann Telegram: A revealed German communication in which Germany tried to draw Mexico into a war against the United States — a direct threat to American sovereignty.
  3. The survival of democracy: The autocratic governments of Central Europe threatened democratic self-government worldwide.

Wilson's key distinction: he separated war-in-general (which may often be unjust) from this particular war (which he argued was different — a war to make the world "safe for democracy").

Butler's Counter: The Profitable War

Butler dismissed Wilson's rhetoric as window dressing for the real motive: profit. His argument rested on hard data:

  • American banks had loaned billions of dollars to Britain and France.
  • American manufacturers (Du Pont, Bethlehem Steel, U.S. Steel, Anaconda Copper) were selling weapons and supplies at enormous markups.
  • If the Allies lost, those loans would never be repaid and those profits would evaporate.
  • Soldiers earned $30/month; corporations earned hundreds of millions.

For Butler, the gap between Wilson's rhetoric and the economic reality was not incidental — it was the whole point. The rhetoric existed to disguise the reality.

The Timing Problem

A critical complication: Wilson spoke in 1917, making a decision in real time with incomplete information. Butler wrote in 1935, with 18 years of hindsight — knowing the war's outcome and knowing that the Treaty of Versailles had failed to produce the lasting democratic order Wilson promised.

Key distinction: Hindsight is not the same as refutation. An argument can be reasonable at the time it is made and still be wrong in its predictions. Wilson could not have known Versailles would sow the seeds of a second world war. Conversely, Butler's economic analysis may be correct about what actually motivated the war even if Wilson sincerely believed his own justification.

Key term:

  • Sincerity vs. accuracy: Wilson may have genuinely believed his moral case. That sincerity does not prove his justification was the true cause of American entry into the war.

Two Methods of Political Analysis

The deepest clash between Wilson and Butler is about what we should trust when a government asks citizens to go to war:

Wilson Butler
Trust stated reasons Follow the money
Nations can act on principle Structural incentives determine real motives
Judge the argument on its merits Identify who benefits

Key Concepts for Review

  • The Zimmermann Telegram (1917): German attempt to draw Mexico into war against the U.S.; key in Wilson's justification for American entry into WWI.
  • Unrestricted submarine warfare: Germany's policy of attacking all ships in British waters, including American civilian and commercial vessels.
  • Reception and context: Reading Wilson's 1917 speech and Butler's 1935 pamphlet side by side requires acknowledging that one was written under wartime pressure and the other with the benefit of hindsight.
  • Wilson's omission: Wilson's address to Congress never mentioned money — not loans, not profits, not trade. Butler regarded this as the most revealing silence in the debate.
