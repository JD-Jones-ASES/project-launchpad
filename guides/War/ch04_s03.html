The Argument Unpacked: Butler's Three Proposals

Smedley Butler's three reform proposals in War Is a Racket are not vague aspirations. They are specific policy recommendations, each targeting a different mechanism by which Butler believes war is perpetuated. This section examines each proposal on its own terms: What does it claim? What evidence supports it? What assumptions does it rest on? And what objections might a thoughtful critic raise?

Proposal 1: Conscript Capital Before Conscripting Men

Claim: Before the government drafts any young man into military service, it must first conscript the nation's capital, industry, and labor — limiting all incomes to thirty dollars per month, the same as soldiers earn.

Evidence: Corporate profits during World War I rose by hundreds or thousands of percent while soldiers earned thirty dollars a month, half of which was deducted. The profit motive drives the push for war.

Reasoning: If no one can profit from war, no one will lobby for it. Remove the financial incentive and you remove the pressure to fight.

Key assumption: War is primarily caused by the profit motive. If profits are eliminated, wars will not occur — or at least will be far less likely.

This is Butler's most radical proposal. The appeal is its simplicity and rough justice: Why should a munitions executive earn thousands while a soldier earns pennies? But critics raise serious questions. Could a modern industrial economy function if every worker, manager, and executive earned the same wage? Would factories produce the weapons an army needs if there were no financial incentive to do so? And what if some wars are driven by genuine threats — by aggression, invasion, or the need to defend allies? In such cases, capping profits might cripple the very industries needed for an effective defense.

Feasibility refers to whether a proposed plan can actually be carried out in practice. A proposal may be morally sound and logically consistent but still fail if it cannot survive political opposition, economic disruption, or constitutional limitations.

Proposal 2: A Limited Plebiscite

Claim: The decision to go to war should be made by a direct vote of those who will actually fight — young men of military age who are physically fit for service.

Evidence: In World War I, four million American men were sent to fight without being asked. Congress declared war; the soldiers had no say.

Reasoning: Those who bear the consequences of a decision should be the ones who make it. A seventy-six-year-old munitions executive who will never see combat should not have the same voice on war as a twenty-year-old who will be handed a rifle.

Key assumption: Young men facing the prospect of combat will vote against war in most cases, thereby preventing unnecessary conflicts.

Butler argues his restriction on the vote is fair because it mirrors existing restrictions (literacy tests, property requirements) and ensures the decision rests with those who have the most at stake. But several objections arise:

  • Constitutional: The U.S. Constitution grants Congress the power to declare war. A plebiscite would require a constitutional amendment — a high bar.
  • Speed: Wars sometimes begin with surprise attacks. Could a nation hold a national vote while enemy forces were advancing?
  • Assumption: History suggests young men do not always vote against war. In 1917, many were eager to fight; in 1941, after Pearl Harbor, the desire to go to war was nearly universal across all age groups.

A further scope limitation: Butler's plebiscite addresses whether to go to war, not how to conduct a war once it has begun. It would not apply to wars of self-defense triggered by an attack on American soil.

Proposal 3: Defense Only

Claim: American naval vessels should be limited by law to operating within two hundred miles of the coastline. Aircraft should be restricted to five hundred miles. The army should never leave American territory.

Evidence: The U.S.S. Maine was in Havana Harbor when it exploded in 1898, triggering the Spanish-American War. If the ship had been restricted to American waters, the war might never have happened.

Reasoning: A nation that cannot project military force beyond its own borders cannot start an offensive war. Restricting the military to defense removes the physical capability for aggression.

Key assumption: American security can be maintained without the ability to project power abroad. All American military engagements beyond the homeland are, by definition, unnecessary.

Of Butler's three proposals, this one has the clearest practical implications — and the most obvious vulnerabilities. Consider Wilson's argument: he claimed that unrestricted submarine warfare threatened American lives and commerce, and that German autocracy posed a threat to democratic civilization itself. If Wilson was right — if some threats originate far from American shores — then Butler's defensive limitation would leave the nation unable to respond until the threat arrived at its doorstep.

Butler vs. Wilson on Military Scope

Butler holds that the military should be restricted to home defense; any deployment beyond American territory serves corporate interests, not national security. Wilson holds that some threats to American security originate thousands of miles away, and a nation that cannot project power cannot defend the principles on which it was founded. Neutrality in the face of aggression, Wilson argues, is not peace — it is complicity. Butler's blanket restriction eliminates the case-by-case judgment that Wilson's position demands.

The Three Proposals as a Coherent System

Taken together, Butler's three reforms form an interlocking system. Proposal 1 removes the motive (profit). Proposal 2 transfers the decision (from those who benefit to those who suffer). Proposal 3 removes the capability (by restricting force to defense). Each reinforces the others: even if one fails, the remaining two would still constrain the war machine.

The strength of Butler's proposals is their specificity — he identifies exact mechanisms and proposes concrete steps to dismantle them. The weakness is the shared assumption underlying all three: that war is always a racket, driven by profit rather than by genuine threats. If even one war in American history was fought for legitimate reasons — the Revolution, the Civil War, World War II — then Butler's blanket system may be too rigid. The strongest counterargument to Butler is not that war is good, but that some wars are necessary, and a system designed to prevent all wars may also prevent the ones that should be fought.

Key Terms

Term Definition
Feasibility Whether a proposed plan can actually be carried out, given political, economic, and constitutional constraints
Limited plebiscite A direct vote on a specific question restricted to a defined group; Butler proposes limiting war votes to men of military age and physical fitness
Policy proposal A specific plan of action recommended to address a public problem, evaluated on both moral justification and feasibility
Profit motive The desire for financial gain; Butler identifies this as the primary driver of modern warfare
Power projection A military's ability to operate far from its home territory; Butler proposes eliminating this capability
