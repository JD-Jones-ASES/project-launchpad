Study Guide: Smedley Butler’s Three Proposals for Ending the War Racket

This study guide examines the specific policy recommendations proposed by Smedley Butler in War Is a Racket (1935). Butler’s arguments focus on dismantling the mechanisms of war by targeting profit motives, decision-making authority, and military capability.

Executive Summary of Butler’s System

Butler’s three proposals are designed as an interlocking system to attack the "war machine" from three directions simultaneously.

Goal Proposal Action
Remove Motive Conscript Capital Cap all wartime incomes (including corporate and political) at $30 per month.
Transfer Decision Limited Plebiscite Restrict the vote on declaring war to those of military age who will actually fight.
Remove Capability Defense Only Legally restrict the Navy and Army to domestic borders and immediate coastlines.

Analysis of Proposals

Proposal 1: Conscript Capital

The Claim: The government must conscript the nation’s capital, industry, and labor before drafting men into service.

  • Mechanism: All incomes—including those of corporate executives, bankers, politicians, and generals—would be limited to $30 per month, the same as a soldier’s pay.
  • Reasoning: War is driven by the profit motive. If no one can profit from conflict, the pressure to lobby for war disappears.
  • Criticisms:
    • Economic Feasibility: It is unclear if a modern industrial economy could function without financial incentives for production.
    • National Defense: If a war is driven by a genuine threat (e.g., Nazi aggression), capping profits might cripple the industries necessary for a defense.

Proposal 2: A Limited Plebiscite

The Claim: The decision to go to war should be made via a direct vote by those who will bear the physical burden of combat.

  • Mechanism: Only young men of military age and physical fitness would be eligible to vote on a declaration of war.
  • Reasoning: Those with the most at stake should make the decision, rather than older executives or politicians who will never see combat.
  • Criticisms:
    • Constitutionality: Article I, Section 8 grants the power to declare war to Congress; a plebiscite would require a constitutional amendment.
    • Practicality: National votes take time, which is a liability during a surprise attack.
    • Assumptions: History shows that young men are not always anti-war; many were eager to fight in 1917 and 1941.

Proposal 3: Defense Only

The Claim: The military must be legally restricted to home defense to prevent offensive aggression.

  • Mechanism: Naval vessels restricted to within 200 miles of the coastline; aircraft to 500 miles; the Army to remain on American territory.
  • Reasoning: If a nation cannot project force beyond its borders, it cannot engage in offensive wars or serve corporate interests abroad.
  • Criticisms:
    • Scope of Threats: This assumes no legitimate threat exists beyond the 200-mile limit.
    • The Wilsonian Counterargument: Some threats (like autocracy or unrestricted submarine warfare) originate far from home. A "defense only" policy may leave a nation unable to respond until a threat is on its doorstep.

Comparative Perspectives: Butler vs. Wilson

The fundamental disagreement between Smedley Butler and Woodrow Wilson centers on the definition of national security.

Feature Smedley Butler Woodrow Wilson
Military Scope Restricted strictly to home defense. Capable of projecting power abroad.
View of Intervention Always a "racket" for corporate gain. Sometimes a moral duty to defend principles.
Security Strategy Stay home to avoid unnecessary conflict. Engage with the world to stop threats early.
Nature of Peace Isolation and removal of profit. Active defense against aggression; neutrality can be complicity.

Glossary of Key Terms

  • Conscription of Capital: The policy of seizing or strictly limiting private wealth and industrial profits during wartime to ensure economic equality with soldiers.
  • Feasibility: The practical capability of a plan to be implemented, considering real-world constraints such as politics, economics, and human nature.
  • Limited Plebiscite: A direct vote on a specific issue (in this case, war) restricted to a specific segment of the population (those of military age).
  • Policy Proposal: A specific, concrete plan of action designed to address a public problem, detailing what should be done and by whom.
  • Profit Motive: The desire for financial gain, which Butler identifies as the primary driver of modern warfare.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What specific monthly income limit did Butler propose for all citizens during wartime?
  2. According to Butler, why did corporate profits during World War I justify the "Conscript Capital" proposal?
  3. Which specific group did Butler believe should be the only ones allowed to vote on a declaration of war?
  4. How does Proposal 2 (Limited Plebiscite) conflict with Article I, Section 8 of the U.S. Constitution?
  5. What are the specific geographic limits Butler proposed for the U.S. Navy and aircraft?
  6. Explain the "Logic of Butler’s System." How do the three proposals work together?
  7. What historical event in 1898 does Butler use as evidence that restricting ships to home waters could prevent war?
  8. Define "Feasibility" in the context of evaluating Butler’s recommendations.
  9. What is the primary criticism regarding Butler’s assumption about how young men would vote in a plebiscite?
  10. How did Woodrow Wilson’s view of "neutrality" differ from Butler’s view of "defense only"?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Feasibility of Economic Neutrality: Evaluate Butler’s "Conscript Capital" proposal. Is it possible for a modern industrial nation to mount a successful defense if all financial incentives for labor and industry are removed? Use the context of World War II to support your argument.
  2. Democratic Burden: Butler argues that only those who fight should decide on war. Analyze the tension between this "limited plebiscite" and the broader democratic principle that all citizens should have a say in major national decisions. Does Butler’s use of historical precedents like property requirements strengthen or weaken his moral case?
  3. The Geography of Security: Compare Butler’s "Defense Only" strategy with the interventionist arguments of Woodrow Wilson. In a modern globalized world, is it possible for a nation's security to be maintained within a 200-mile coastal limit, or are some threats inherently global?
  4. The Universal "Racket": Butler’s thesis assumes that war is almost exclusively driven by profit. Does this framework adequately explain wars of ideology or self-defense? Discuss whether Butler’s "interlocking system" is an effective preventative measure or a dangerous restriction on national survival.
