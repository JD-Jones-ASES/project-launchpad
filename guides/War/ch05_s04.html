Study Guide: The Political Clash – Wilson vs. Butler

This study guide examines the political disagreement between Woodrow Wilson and Smedley Butler regarding the justifications for war. While Wilson argues for the necessity of war based on moral principles and national defense, Butler contends that such justifications are merely masks for economic profit.

I. Overview of the Political Clash

The debate between Wilson and Butler centers on whether a specific war can be justified even if the broader system of warfare is flawed. This differs from the philosophical debate (such as that between Holmes and Butler), which focuses on the impact of war on human character. The Wilson-Butler clash focuses on immediate political and economic motivations.

Woodrow Wilson: The Necessary War

Wilson’s position, articulated in April 1917, is that war is a "fearful thing" that is sometimes unavoidable. His argument rests on three main pillars:

  • Immediate Provocations: Germany's policy of unrestricted submarine warfare and the Zimmermann Telegram (an attempt to draw Mexico into war against the U.S.) threatened American lives and sovereignty.
  • Moral Imperative: Wilson believed autocratic governments in Central Europe threatened the global survival of democracy. He famously argued the war was necessary to make the world "safe for democracy."
  • Particularism: Wilson distinguished between "war as a general category" (which might be unjust) and "particular wars" fought for specific, principled reasons.

Smedley Butler: The Profitable War

Writing in 1935, Butler viewed Wilson’s rhetoric as "window dressing" intended to hide the true motive for American entry into World War I: the protection of profit.

  • Economic Interests: American banks had loaned massive sums to the Allied powers (Britain and France). If the Allies lost, those loans would not be repaid.
  • Corporate Profiteering: Butler provided data showing that while soldiers earned only thirty dollars a month, major corporations saw massive profit increases.
  • The Mask of Patriotism: Butler argued that the language of freedom and democracy is a tool used by those who profit from war to convince ordinary citizens to support it.

II. Key Comparison: Principled vs. Profitable

The following table summarizes the core differences between the two perspectives:

Feature Woodrow Wilson (1917) Smedley Butler (1935)
Primary Motivation Defense of democratic principles and national sovereignty. Protection of Wall Street investments and corporate profits.
View of War A "fearful" but unavoidable necessity for a moral nation. A "racket" where rhetoric disguises economic reality.
Evidence Used Geopolitical threats (Zimmermann Telegram, submarine warfare). Economic data and corporate profit margins.
Timing/Context Real-time decision-making with incomplete information. Eighteen years of hindsight following the failure of the peace treaty.
Core Question Can a nation act on principle? Who benefits financially?

III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to Wilson, why was the survival of democracy at stake in 1917?
  2. What were the two specific "immediate provocations" Wilson cited as threats to American sovereignty?
  3. How does Butler define the term "window dressing" in the context of wartime rhetoric?
  4. Which specific corporations did Butler name as examples of entities that profited from the war?
  5. What is the "Timing Problem" when comparing the arguments of Wilson and Butler?
  6. According to the text, what is the distinction between "sincerity" and "accuracy" regarding Wilson's justifications?
  7. How did the pay of an average soldier compare to corporate profits during the war, according to Butler?

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Conflict of Interests: Analyze Wilson's claim that the "righteousness of a cause is independent of the financial interests that happen to align with it." Is it possible for a war to be both principled and profitable, or does the presence of profit inherently corrupt the stated mission?
  2. The Power of Hindsight: Discuss how Butler’s retrospective vantage point in 1935 influenced his critique of Wilson’s 1917 rhetoric. To what extent is it fair to judge a leader’s decision based on the eventual failure of a peace settlement (the Treaty of Versailles) that they could not have fully predicted?
  3. Stated Reasons vs. Structural Incentives: Evaluate the clash between Wilson’s focus on moral principles and Butler’s focus on economic structures. Which method of analysis provides a more comprehensive understanding of why nations go to war? Use evidence from the text to support your stance.

V. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Hindsight: The understanding of a situation or event only after it has happened. Butler used this to critique the outcomes of the war that Wilson could not have known in 1917.
  • Particular War: A concept used by Wilson to distinguish a specific conflict fought for justifiable reasons from the general, often unjust, category of "war-in-general."
  • Structural Incentives: The underlying economic or systemic factors (like profit or debt) that drive political decisions, regardless of the stated reasons.
  • Unrestricted Submarine Warfare: A German naval policy that Wilson cited as a direct threat to American lives and commerce, serving as a primary justification for entry into World War I.
  • Window Dressing: A misleading display designed to create a deceptively favorable impression. Butler used this term to describe Wilson’s appeals to democracy and self-determination.
  • Zimmermann Telegram: A historical provocation in which Germany attempted to recruit Mexico as an ally against the United States; cited by Wilson as a violation of sovereignty.

Entities Mentioned by Butler

  • Du Pont, Bethlehem Steel, U.S. Steel, Anaconda Copper: Specific corporations cited by Butler to demonstrate the staggering markups and hundreds of millions in profits earned by suppliers during the war.
  • Wall Street: Representing the financial interests and banks that had loaned money to the Allies, which Butler argued was the real reason the U.S. entered the conflict.
