The Voice from the Trenches

Holmes, Butler, and Wilson all argued about war from a distance — from a lectern, a desk, or a position of authority, years or decades after the fighting. This section introduces a fourth voice of a different kind: the voice of an ordinary soldier in the middle of the war itself. Erich Maria Remarque's novel All Quiet on the Western Front does something no argumentative text can — it puts the reader inside the experience of combat.

Remarque: Life and Context

Erich Maria Remarque (1898–1970) was born in Osnabruck, Germany. At eighteen, he was drafted into the German army and sent to the Western Front, where he was wounded by shrapnel. After the war he worked as a journalist and copywriter before publishing All Quiet on the Western Front in 1929. The novel was an immediate international sensation, selling millions of copies.

When the Nazis came to power in 1933, they burned Remarque's books and revoked his German citizenship. He emigrated first to Switzerland and then to the United States, where he became a citizen in 1947. His crime, in the Nazis' view: telling the truth about what war had done to his generation.

Paul Bäumer — the novel's narrator — is a young German student who enlists with his classmates after patriotic speeches from their schoolteacher, only to find that the reality of the trenches bears no resemblance to the glorious war they were promised.

The Western Front: Historical Context

  • A roughly 440-mile line from the English Channel to the Swiss border
  • For most of the war (1914–1918), the front barely moved
  • Soldiers lived in trenches — ditches dug in mud, infested with rats — separated from the enemy by "no man's land"
  • Attacks typically gained a few hundred yards at the cost of thousands of lives
  • The Battle of the Somme (1916): Over one million casualties on both sides; front line moved approximately six miles

Why Remarque Belongs Here

All Quiet on the Western Front is not a speech, pamphlet, or policy argument — it is a novel. So why include it alongside Holmes, Butler, and Wilson? Because Remarque provides something none of them can: the inside of combat. Holmes alludes to his Civil War service in dignified abstract language. Butler describes aftermath and broken bodies. Wilson acknowledges that war is "fearful" without describing the fear. Remarque puts you in the trench.

Note on fiction as evidence: A novel is not a primary source in the way a speech is. Remarque invented Paul Bäumer and the specific events. But historians and literary scholars frequently cite this novel not for factual accuracy in every detail but because it captures an experiential truth that statistics and policy arguments cannot convey.

What the Excerpt Reveals

The Chapter VI excerpt shows soldiers during an impending offensive bombardment:

  • Coffins stacked against a school — dark, gallows humor as psychological coping
  • Chance as the governing force: who lives and who dies is random, not a matter of courage or skill
  • Rats, hunger, and waiting: the texture of daily trench existence
  • The psychological breakdown of recruits under sustained bombardment — claustrophobia as a clinical response to confinement plus unrelenting artillery
  • The failure of food resupply when no one can move through the barrage

The Limits of Argument

Remarque's excerpt exposes a fundamental limitation shared by all three argumentative writers:

  • Holmes: The passage describes not heroic endurance but desperate survival. The soldiers are not choosing to be brave — they are trying not to die.
  • Wilson: The German soldiers suffer identically to Allied soldiers and were told the same things about duty and necessity by their own leaders.
  • Butler: For the men in the trench, the question of who profits is as distant as the question of who is morally right. Their world has shrunk to the shell crater and the next few seconds of life.

Key concept: Every argument about war is, in some sense, incomplete. The reader's challenge is to hold arguments about war and the reality of war in mind simultaneously — never losing sight of what the arguments are actually about.
