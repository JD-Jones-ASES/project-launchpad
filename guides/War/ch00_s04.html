Study Guide: The Nature and Purpose of War

This study guide examines the fundamental arguments regarding the purpose and justification of war. It explores the perspectives of historical figures who witnessed the consequences of conflict firsthand and provides tools for analyzing the rhetorical strategies used to define the meaning of war.

Key Concepts

The Magnitude of War

War is described as the most consequential action a nation can undertake. Its impact is felt across multiple dimensions of society:

  • Economic: It exhausts national treasuries.
  • Geopolitical: It alters international borders and causes the collapse of governments.
  • Human: It results in the deaths of millions.

The Four Perspectives on War

The central debate over war is categorized into four distinct philosophical and practical viewpoints, represented by specific historical figures:

  1. War as a School of Virtue: The belief that war brings out the highest human qualities, including courage, sacrifice, and loyalty to a cause greater than the individual.
  2. War as a Racket: The perspective that war is a calculated scheme designed by the powerful to generate profit for a few at the expense of those who fight.
  3. War as a Necessary Evil: The stance that war is not inherently good but is required to defend civilization against tyranny or to preserve freedom.
  4. War as an Unjustifiable Reality: The view that the sheer brutality and physical horror of the battlefield render any eloquent justification or abstract argument invalid.

The Role of Rhetoric

Understanding the debate over war requires the application of rhetoric, the ancient art of persuasion and argument. Readers are tasked with evaluating arguments on their own terms, identifying strengths and weaknesses, and determining which claims remain valid under scrutiny rather than simply choosing a favorite position.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to the text, what are the four primary consequences of war for a nation? Answer: War empties treasuries, reshapes borders, topples governments, and kills people by the millions.

2. How did Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr.’s personal experience influence his view on war? Answer: As a Civil War veteran who nearly died in battle, Holmes later characterized war as a "school of virtue," celebrating it for its ability to foster human excellence.

3. What is Smedley Butler’s central critique of war? Answer: Butler, a highly decorated Marine, argued that war is a "racket" run for the profit of a few powerful individuals.

4. Why does the text describe the debate over war as "worth having"? Answer: The debate is worth having because the answer is not obvious; intelligent, sincere, and experienced people have fundamentally disagreed about the meaning of war for as long as it has existed.

5. What was the irony of Woodrow Wilson’s presidency regarding war? Answer: Wilson campaigned on a platform of peace but ultimately led the United States into the bloodiest war the world had seen up to that point.

6. What was the objective of Erich Maria Remarque’s 1929 novel? Answer: Based on his experience in the trenches as a German soldier, Remarque's work aimed to strip away every justification for war by highlighting its brutal reality.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Tension Between Idealism and Reality: Compare the "school of virtue" perspective held by Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. with the reality-based perspective of Erich Maria Remarque. How does Remarque’s focus on the physical reality of the battlefield challenge Holmes’s celebration of courage and sacrifice?
  2. The Ethics of Necessity vs. Profit: Evaluate the arguments of Woodrow Wilson and Smedley Butler. Wilson frames war as a "necessary evil" in defense of civilization, while Butler frames it as a "racket" for profit. Discuss how these two views might interpret the same conflict differently.
  3. The Responsibility of the Reader/Analyst: The text asserts that the reader’s job is not to "pick a favorite" but to understand each argument through the lens of rhetoric. Discuss the importance of evaluating arguments based on their internal logic and evidence rather than personal bias, especially concerning a topic as consequential as war.

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Necessary Evil A perspective that war, while not desirable, is required to prevent a greater harm, such as the spread of tyranny.
Racket A scheme or fraudulent enterprise run for the profit of a few at the expense of many; the term used by Smedley Butler to describe war.
Rhetoric The ancient art of persuasion and argument used to make a case and influence an audience.
School of Virtue The concept that war serves as an environment where individuals learn and demonstrate noble qualities like loyalty and sacrifice.
Tyranny An oppressive form of government or rule that proponents of "necessary war" often claim to be fighting against.
Virtue High moral excellence or qualities—such as courage and loyalty—that some argue are cultivated through the experience of conflict.
