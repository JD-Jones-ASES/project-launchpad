Study Guide: The Perspectives and Justifications of War

This study guide examines the fundamental arguments regarding the purpose and justification of war as presented in "What Is War For?" It explores the diverse viewpoints of historical figures who experienced war firsthand and analyzes the rhetorical framework used to debate its necessity and morality.

I. Overview of the Central Question

War is described as the most consequential action a nation can take, resulting in the depletion of treasuries, the reshaping of national borders, the collapse of governments, and the loss of millions of lives. Because of these stakes, every generation engages in a debate regarding the true nature and purpose of war.

The core inquiry of this study is: What is war for? The text identifies four primary conceptual frameworks used to answer this:

  1. A Test of Character: War as a noble pursuit that fosters courage, sacrifice, and loyalty.
  2. A Profitable Enterprise: War as a "racket" designed to enrich the powerful at the expense of those who fight.
  3. A Necessary Evil: War as an unfortunate requirement to defend civilization against tyranny.
  4. An Unjustifiable Brutality: War as an experience so horrific that no eloquent argument can justify the resulting carnage.

II. Key Figures and Perspectives

The following individuals represent the primary voices in the historical debate over the meaning of war.

Figure Background Core Argument/Perspective
Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. (1895) Civil War veteran who nearly died in battle. Viewed war as a "school of virtue" that calls out the best human qualities.
Smedley Butler (1935) Highly decorated Marine of his era. Defined war as a "racket" run for the profit of a few powerful individuals.
Woodrow Wilson (1917) U.S. President who campaigned on a platform of peace. Argued for war as a necessary defense of freedom and civilization against tyranny.
Erich Maria Remarque (1929) German soldier and survivor of trench warfare. Authored a novel that stripped away justifications for war by focusing on its physical reality.

III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to the text, what are some of the material consequences of war on a national level? Answer: War empties national treasuries, alters international borders, and causes the downfall of governments.

2. How does the perspective of war as a "school of virtue" justify its existence? Answer: This view suggests that war is noble because it demands courage, sacrifice, and loyalty to a cause larger than the individual, thereby strengthening the "spine" of a society.

3. What is the primary criticism leveled by those who view war as a "racket"? Answer: They argue that war is a scheme orchestrated by the powerful to enrich themselves, while ordinary people bear the burden of the fighting and dying.

4. Why is Woodrow Wilson’s leadership during 1917 highlighted as a significant point of study? Answer: Wilson is a key figure because he originally campaigned on a peace platform but ultimately led the nation into one of the bloodiest conflicts in history, framing it as a necessary evil.

5. What specific skill set is required for a reader to effectively analyze these different arguments? Answer: The reader must employ the skills of rhetoric, which is defined as the ancient art of persuasion and argument.

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Tension Between Rhetoric and Reality Compare the arguments of Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. and Erich Maria Remarque. Holmes views war through the lens of virtue and character, while Remarque focuses on the physical reality of the battlefield. Discuss how the "reality of the bodies" challenges eloquent rhetorical justifications for conflict.

2. The Evolution of Justification Woodrow Wilson’s transition from a peace candidate to a war president illustrates the concept of war as a "necessary evil." Analyze the strengths and weaknesses of justifying war not as a "good" in itself, but as a defense against a greater threat, such as tyranny.

3. War as an Economic and Social Construct Smedley Butler argues that war is a "racket." Evaluate this claim in the context of the text's statement that war "empties treasuries" and "enriches the powerful." How does this perspective shift the focus of the debate from national morality to economic exploitation?

V. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Necessary Evil: A conceptualization of war where it is not considered inherently good, but is required to prevent a worse outcome, such as the destruction of civilization by tyranny.
  • Racket: A term used by Smedley Butler to describe war as a fraudulent scheme or business practiced by the powerful for profit.
  • Rhetoric: The ancient art of persuasion and argument; the tools used by the four figures to make their cases regarding war.
  • School of Virtue: A metaphorical description of war provided by Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr., suggesting that combat serves as an environment for learning and demonstrating high moral qualities like loyalty and sacrifice.
  • Tyranny: Cruel and oppressive government rule; cited by proponents of the "necessary evil" argument as a threat that justifies military intervention.
  • Trench Warfare: The specific type of brutal combat experienced by Erich Maria Remarque, which informed his perspective that war has no valid justification.
