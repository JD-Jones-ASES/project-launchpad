What Propaganda Looks Like

Having examined ethos, pathos, and logos across all four writers, we now confront the question that haunts every study of war rhetoric: when does persuasion become propaganda? This is not a question with a clean answer, but the tools to think about it clearly are within reach.

Defining Propaganda

Propaganda is communication designed primarily to influence attitudes and behavior rather than to inform or reason. It typically employs some combination of the following: selective presentation of facts, emotional manipulation, repetition of slogans, demonization of opponents, appeals to fear or group identity, and suppression of counterarguments. Propaganda differs from legitimate persuasion not in kind (both seek to change minds) but in degree and intent: propaganda subordinates truth to effect, while legitimate persuasion subordinates effect to truth.

The Blurry Line

The definition sounds clean, but in practice the line between persuasion and propaganda is maddeningly blurry. Every effective argument selects its evidence — no writer presents every fact. Every effective argument uses emotion. The question is not whether selection and emotion are present, but how much selection becomes manipulation and whether the emotional appeal is proportional to the evidence.

Two specific techniques are especially common in propaganda:

  • Straw man argument: Misrepresenting an opponent's position to make it easier to attack. Instead of engaging with what the opponent actually said, the arguer attacks a weaker or distorted version. Characterizing Wilson's argument as "Wilson wanted war because he enjoyed it" would be a straw man — Wilson explicitly called war "a fearful thing" and argued for it only as a last resort.

  • False dilemma: Presenting only two options when more actually exist. "Either we go to war or we surrender our freedoms" is a false dilemma if diplomatic pressure, economic sanctions, or multilateral negotiation remain available. False dilemmas create urgency and foreclose alternatives — though sometimes there really are only two options, and recognizing a genuine dilemma matters as much as detecting a false one.

Applying the Question to Our Four Writers

The following case studies do not deliver a verdict — they practice asking the right questions.

Holmes: He selectively presents war as a crucible of virtue while omitting its physical horrors. He uses emotional language approaching religious devotion. He presents no counterarguments. On the other hand, he is not trying to start a war or recruit soldiers. His purpose is philosophical and ceremonial — to honor the dead and argue that their sacrifice had meaning — for an audience that already shares his values. The question turns on whether his omissions distort the truth or reflect the appropriate focus of a Memorial Day address.

Butler: He uses vivid emotional imagery of wounded veterans, presents corporate profit data selectively, and does not seriously engage with the possibility that some wars might be justified. On the other hand, his central factual claims about wartime profits are largely accurate, his emotional appeals are grounded in real suffering, and he offers concrete policy proposals that can be evaluated on their merits. The question turns on whether his one-sidedness reflects honest conviction or calculated manipulation.

Wilson: This is the hardest case. His address was delivered for the explicit purpose of persuading Congress to declare war — with life-and-death consequences for millions. He uses the rhetoric of moral necessity to create enormous pressure for agreement. He frames the conflict as democracy versus autocracy, a simplification that omits the complicated imperial interests of the Allied powers. On the other hand, Germany's submarine warfare was real, the Zimmermann Telegram was real, and the threat to American lives was genuine. He was interpreting a real crisis, not fabricating one. The question turns on whether his moral framing illuminates or distorts the actual situation.

Remarque: He offers no explicit argument, makes no policy recommendations, and does not demonize any nation or leader. Yet the Nazi regime banned and burned his novel — they understood that his unflinching depiction of war's horror served a political purpose whether he intended it or not: it made war harder to glorify. If propaganda is defined by its effect rather than its intent, even a truthful witness account can function as propaganda. This should make us cautious about using the word too loosely.

Two Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: "Any argument I disagree with is propaganda." Propaganda is not a synonym for rejected opinion. It refers to specific techniques of manipulation. Calling an argument propaganda without identifying those techniques is name-calling, not analysis.

Mistake 2: "Propaganda must contain lies." The most effective propaganda often uses true facts. What makes it propaganda is the systematic selection, framing, and emotional packaging of those facts to produce a predetermined conclusion while suppressing alternative interpretations.

Five Diagnostic Questions

Rather than labeling any writer a "propagandist" — a label that ends conversation rather than starts it — apply these five questions:

  1. Does the writer acknowledge the strongest counterarguments?
  2. Does the writer present evidence that can be independently verified?
  3. Does the writer leave the audience free to disagree?
  4. Does the emotional intensity match the strength of the evidence?
  5. Does the writer simplify a complex situation into a binary choice?

Each of our four writers passes some tests and fails others. The line between persuasion and propaganda is not a wall but a spectrum, and thoughtful readers must learn to locate writers along that spectrum rather than simply sorting them into heroes and villains.

Key Terms

  • Propaganda: Communication designed to influence attitudes and behavior rather than to inform or reason; subordinates truth to effect.
  • False dilemma: Presenting only two options when more actually exist; creates urgency by foreclosing alternatives.
  • Straw man: Misrepresenting an opponent's position to make it easier to attack.
  • Persuasion vs. propaganda spectrum: The recognition that the distinction is a matter of degree and intent, not a clean binary division.
  • Zimmermann Telegram: The real German diplomatic communication offering Mexico an alliance against the United States — one of the genuine crises underlying Wilson's argument.
