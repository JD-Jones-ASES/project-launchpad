# Study Guide: Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. and “The Soldier’s Faith”

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the historical context, rhetorical style, and philosophical arguments presented in Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr.’s 1895 address, “The Soldier's Faith.”

---

### I. Historical Context: 1895 and the Gilded Age

The speech was delivered thirty years after the conclusion of the American Civil War (1865). By 1895, the United States had undergone a massive transformation from a nation at war with itself to the world’s leading industrial power.

| Feature | Civil War Era | Gilded Age (1895) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Focus** | Honor, duty, and national survival. | Profit, commerce, and industrial growth. |
| **Economic Landscape** | Agrarian and early industrial. | Steel mills, railroads, and massive factories. |
| **Key Influencers** | Soldiers and military leaders. | Industrialists like Carnegie, Rockefeller, and Vanderbilt. |
| **Social Priority** | Sacrifice for a cause. | Material comfort and professional security. |

#### Memorial Day (Decoration Day)
Originally known as Decoration Day, the holiday began shortly after the Civil War as a way to honor Union soldiers by decorating their graves. By the 1890s, it had evolved into a national event featuring:
*   **Parades and Speeches:** Organized by groups such as the Grand Army of the Republic.
*   **Harvard Tradition:** An annual address by a distinguished speaker, frequently a veteran, discussing the war's meaning and the duties of the living.

---

### II. Core Themes and Arguments

In “The Soldier's Faith,” Holmes presents a specific philosophical case regarding the role of struggle and sacrifice in society.

*   **The Indispensability of War Virtues:** Holmes argues that the virtues cultivated in war—courage, faith, and the willingness to act without guarantees—are necessary for a healthy civilization.
*   **Challenge to Materialism:** Holmes expresses concern that a society focused entirely on "the sound of commerce" and material comfort will lose its capacity for sacrifice.
*   **The Value of Risk:** He advocates for the willingness to risk everything for something larger than oneself, contrasting the lives of prosperous professionals with the "soldier’s faith."
*   **The "Experience" of the Speech:** Holmes does not just want to make a logical argument; he seeks to create an emotional experience for his audience through his rhetorical style.

---

### III. Rhetorical Style: The Periodic Sentence

Holmes utilized a specific literary device known as the **periodic sentence** to deliver his message.

*   **Definition:** A sentence where the main idea or central point is withheld until the very end.
*   **Structure:** Subordinate clauses and phrases are "stacked" or piled up, building suspense.
*   **Effect:** The sentence creates an emotional climax, arriving at its destination with significant force, much like a "wave breaking on shore."

### IV. Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Appomattox Courthouse** | The site where Robert E. Lee surrendered in 1865, marking the end of the Civil War. |
| **Decoration Day** | The original name for Memorial Day, focused on decorating the graves of the fallen. |
| **Gilded Age** | The era in late 19th-century America characterized by rapid industrialization, the accumulation of massive fortunes, and a shift toward commerce. |
| **Grand Army of the Republic** | A veterans' organization that organized Memorial Day ceremonies and public remembrance. |
| **Periodic Sentence** | A long, rolling sentence structure that delays the main point until the end to create emphasis and suspense. |
| **The Soldier's Faith** | The title of Holmes’ 1895 address; also refers to the mindset of acting with courage and sacrifice without the need for guarantees. |

### V. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **What significant anniversary coincided with Holmes’ speech?**
    The speech was delivered in 1895, thirty years after the Civil War ended at Appomattox Courthouse.
2.  **Who composed Holmes’ primary audience at Harvard?**
    The audience included the Harvard graduating class, faculty, Boston civic leaders, and surviving Civil War veterans.
3.  **Which three industrial titans does the text mention as symbols of the era’s wealth?**
    Andrew Carnegie, John D. Rockefeller, and Cornelius Vanderbilt.
4.  **How does the text describe the physical appearance of the veterans in the audience?**
    They were middle-aged or old men who carried the scars of battle beneath their formal dress.
5.  **What was Holmes' primary concern regarding the "young men" of 1895?**
    He was troubled that they would enter business or professions and live lives of comfort, never knowing the "sound of cannon" or the capacity for sacrifice.
6.  **According to the text, why is the speech considered controversial?**
    Some see it as a prophetic call to high ideals, while others believe Holmes romanticized the horror of war as a source of moral instruction.

### VI. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Conflict of Generations:** Analyze Holmes’ distinction between the generation that knew the "sound of cannon" and the generation that knew only the "sound of commerce." How does he use this contrast to critique Gilded Age society?
2.  **The Morality of War Virtues:** Holmes argues that war-time virtues are "indispensable to a healthy civilization." Evaluate this claim based on the text. Is he advocating for war itself, or for the character traits that war produces?
3.  **Style as Substance:** Explain the function of the "periodic sentence" in Holmes’ address. How does the structure of his language reflect his goal of "creating an experience" rather than just making a logical argument?
4.  **A Nation Transformed:** Describe the shift in American aspirations from the Civil War era to the 1890s as outlined in the source. How did the rise of the industrial economy change the "obligations of the living to the dead"?
