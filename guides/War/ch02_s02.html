Study Guide: Smedley Butler’s "War is a Racket" Analysis

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the analysis of Smedley Butler’s 1935 work, War is a Racket. It breaks down Butler’s three-stage argument, examines his use of evidence, and identifies the moral and logical frameworks he employs to challenge the nature of warfare.

I. Key Concepts and Argument Structure

Smedley Butler’s argument is delivered in three distinct stages, moving from a categorical definition to specific evidence, and finally to a moral conclusion.

Stage One: The Definition of a "Racket"

Butler utilizes the term "racket" to align war with organized crime. In the context of 1935, this term specifically evoked the imagery of Prohibition-era gangsters and bootleggers.

  • The Problem: War is not a noble enterprise or a necessary evil; it is a criminal scheme.
  • The Mechanism: A racket is an operation that benefits a small group of insiders at the expense of the masses.
  • The Deception: The true purpose of the enterprise is hidden from the majority of the public.

Stage Two: The Empirical Evidence (Logos)

Butler uses specific financial data from the World War to prove that the conflict served as a profit-generating engine for American industry. He transitions from a detective-like analysis to a presentation of "staggering" numbers.

Corporation Peacetime Annual Earnings Wartime Annual Earnings
Du Pont $6,000,000 $58,000,000
Bethlehem Steel $6,000,000 $49,000,000
United States Steel $105,000,000 $240,000,000

Key Finding: The war created at least 21,000 new millionaires and billionaires in the United States. Butler argues these were "blood gains" funded by taxpayers and paid for by the lives of millions.

Stage Three: The Moral Reckoning

The final stage of the argument focuses on the Asymmetry of Cost and Profit. Butler contrasts those who reap the financial rewards with those who bear the physical and emotional burden.

  • The Profiteers: Industrialists, bankers, and arms dealers who remain safe at home.
  • The Payers: Soldiers and their families who face death, disability, and "shattered minds."
  • The Rhetorical Challenge: Butler notes that none of the war millionaires ever "shouldered a rifle" or "dug a trench."

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why did Butler choose the word "racket" to describe war in 1935?
    • Answer: The term was closely associated with organized crime and gangsters of the Prohibition era, placing war in the same moral category as criminal schemes.
  2. According to Butler, who are the "insiders" who benefit from the war racket?
    • Answer: Industrialists, bankers, and arms manufacturers.
  3. What was the approximate increase in annual earnings for the Du Pont corporation during the war?
    • Answer: Earnings rose from $6,000,000 to $58,000,000, a nearly tenfold increase.
  4. How many new millionaires and billionaires were created in the U.S. during the World War according to Butler's data?
    • Answer: At least 21,000.
  5. What is the "Asymmetry of Cost and Profit" in Butler’s argument?
    • Answer: It is the fact that those who profit from war (industrialists/bankers) do not fight in it, while those who fight (soldiers) do not profit and instead suffer the physical and emotional costs.
  6. What does Butler mean by the term "blood gains"?
    • Answer: Profits made from government contracts for goods (shells, ships, steel) that are consumed in a conflict that kills millions.

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Ethics of Categorical Claims: Butler begins his argument with the absolute statement that "war is a racket," leaving no middle ground for "just" wars or wars of defense in his opening chapters. Analyze the rhetorical effectiveness of this strategy. Does this absolute framing strengthen his case, or does it risk alienating readers who believe some wars are necessary?
  2. Logos vs. Pathos in "The Argument Unpacked": Butler’s argument transitions from raw financial statistics (logos) to an emotional and moral accounting of human suffering (pathos). Discuss how the order of these stages contributes to the "cumulative force" of his message. Why is the financial evidence presented before the human cost?
  3. Correlation vs. Causation: Critique Butler’s argument regarding the purpose of war. While he successfully proves that corporations made enormous profits (correlation), does he definitively prove that these profits were the intended purpose of the war (causation)? Explore the logical gap between a system producing profit and a system being designed solely for that profit.
  4. The Scope of the Thesis: Examine the "Questions Butler Leaves Open." If war profiteering is an inherent part of the current system, is it possible to engage in a war of national defense or a war against tyranny without it becoming a "racket"? Use Butler’s definitions to argue whether his thesis applies to all conflicts or is limited to the World War.

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Asymmetry of Cost: The logical and moral gap between those who benefit financially from a conflict and those who pay the human price of that conflict.
  • Blood Gains: A phrase used by Butler to describe wartime profits derived from the destruction of life and the consumption of war materials.
  • Logos: A rhetorical appeal based on logic, reason, and evidence, such as the specific dollar figures and corporate earnings cited by Butler.
  • Racket: A criminal scheme that benefits a few insiders at the expense of many; traditionally associated with organized crime.
  • War Profiteering: The practice of making excessive or "unreasonable" profits from government contracts during wartime, often carrying the negative connotation of benefiting from human suffering.
