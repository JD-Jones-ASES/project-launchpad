Written in Anger

Smedley Butler did not become an antiwar crusader the day he retired. The transformation from celebrated warrior to outspoken critic unfolded over several years, shaped by specific events that convinced him the relationship between the military, the government, and big business was fundamentally corrupt. Understanding those events is essential to reading War Is a Racket correctly — because the pamphlet is not an abstract argument. It is a personal indictment, written in anger by a man who felt betrayed.

The America Butler Came Home To

Butler retired from the Marine Corps in 1931 — not to the confident, booming nation that had sent him abroad, but to a country shattered by the Great Depression. Millions were unemployed. Banks had failed. Factories sat idle. And the veterans who had fought the World War were demanding that the government honor a promise.

The Bonus Army, 1932

In 1924, Congress had passed the World War Adjusted Compensation Act, which promised World War I veterans a cash bonus — but the money would not be paid until 1945. By 1932, with the Depression at its worst, tens of thousands of veterans and their families marched on Washington to demand early payment. They called themselves the Bonus Expeditionary Force and set up a vast encampment of shacks and tents within sight of the Capitol.

President Herbert Hoover ordered the Army to clear the camp. On July 28, 1932, General Douglas MacArthur — with Major Dwight Eisenhower as his aide and Major George Patton commanding the cavalry — led infantry, cavalry, and six tanks against the unarmed veterans. Troops fired tear gas, bayoneted resisters, and burned the camp to the ground. Two veterans were shot and killed. An infant died from tear gas exposure. Hundreds were injured.

Butler had spoken publicly in support of the Bonus Marchers. He knew many of the men personally — they had served under him in France and the Caribbean. The spectacle of the United States Army attacking its own veterans confirmed something Butler had been suspecting for years.

The Business Plot, 1933

If the Bonus Army radicalized Butler, what came next pushed him even further. In 1933, Butler testified before the McCormack-Dickstein Committee that a group of wealthy businessmen and Wall Street financiers had approached him with a plan to overthrow President Franklin Roosevelt and install a fascist-style government. The conspirators had chosen Butler precisely because of his fame and influence among veterans — they believed he could mobilize 500,000 veterans into a private army to march on Washington.

Butler did exactly what the plotters did not expect: he reported the conspiracy to Congress. The committee confirmed his testimony was credible, but the affair was quickly buried. No one was prosecuted. The major newspapers — many owned by the same class of businessmen Butler accused — largely dismissed the story.

The European Context

Butler's testimony about the Business Plot must be understood against the backdrop of the early 1930s, when fascism was not a discredited ideology but an apparently successful one. Mussolini had ruled Italy since 1922. Hitler came to power in Germany in January 1933. Prominent American and British businessmen openly admired fascist governments. The idea of an American fascist movement was not as far-fetched in 1933 as it would seem a decade later.

From Warrior to Writer

By 1935, Butler had seen the full arc of American military power: he had fought the wars, watched profits flow to corporations while veterans begged for bonuses, seen the government attack those same veterans, and been recruited by the profiteering class to overthrow a democratically elected president. War Is a Racket is the result. It is a pamphlet — barely fifty pages — written in anger by a man who felt personally betrayed, aimed not at scholars but at the ordinary Americans whose sons would be called upon to fight the next war.

Key Terms

  • World War Adjusted Compensation Act (1924) — Law promising WWI veterans a cash bonus, not payable until 1945.
  • Bonus Expeditionary Force — The organization of WWI veterans who marched on Washington in 1932 to demand early bonus payment.
  • Business Plot — The alleged 1933 conspiracy by wealthy businessmen to install a fascist-style government using Butler as figurehead.
  • McCormack-Dickstein Committee — The congressional committee that investigated Butler's testimony; it confirmed the credibility of his claims.
  • Pamphlet — A short, unbound publication devoted to a single argument, rewarding brevity and urgency over scholarly qualification.
