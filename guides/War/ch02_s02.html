Study Guide: Smedley Butler’s "The Racket Revealed" (Chapters 1–2)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the analysis of Smedley Butler’s 1935 work, War is a Racket. It focuses on the structural unpacking of Butler’s argument, the specific evidence provided, and the moral conclusions drawn regarding the nature of warfare and profiteering.

Key Concepts and Argument Structure

Smedley Butler’s argument is delivered in a deliberate three-stage structure designed to maximize rhetorical impact: the definition of the problem, the presentation of evidence, and the final moral reckoning.

Stage One: The Definition of the "Racket"

Butler categorizes war not as a noble endeavor or a necessary evil, but as a criminal scheme. He utilizes the term "racket" specifically to evoke the organized crime associations of the Prohibition era (e.g., bootleggers and gangsters).

  • Definition of a Racket: An enterprise that benefits a small group of insiders at the expense of the many, while remaining "not what it seems to the majority of the people."
  • The Central Thesis: War operates exactly like organized crime. A small group of industrialists, bankers, and arms manufacturers reap enormous profits while the general public pays the cost in lives and taxes. The public is kept in the dark about the true purpose of the conflict.

Stage Two: The Evidence (Logos)

Butler uses specific, documented financial data from World War I to transition from a general claim to a concrete accusation. This appeal to logic and facts (logos) highlights the surge in corporate earnings during the war.

Corporation Peacetime Annual Earnings Wartime Annual Earnings
Du Pont $6,000,000 $58,000,000
Bethlehem Steel $6,000,000 $49,000,000
United States Steel $105,000,000 $240,000,000

Key Evidence Points:

  • Du Pont saw a nearly tenfold increase in earnings.
  • At least 21,000 new millionaires and billionaires were created in the U.S. during the World War.
  • Profits were derived from government contracts paid by taxpayers for goods consumed in a conflict that killed millions. Butler terms these "blood gains."

Stage Three: The Moral Reckoning

The final stage of the argument focuses on the Asymmetry of Cost and Profit. Having established who made the money, Butler identifies who paid the price.

  • The Cost: Measured in gravestones, mangled bodies, shattered minds, and broken hearts.
  • The Disconnect: Those who profit (bankers, industrialists) remain safely at home. Those who pay the price (soldiers and their families) shoulder the physical and emotional burden of combat.
  • The Rhetorical Challenge: Butler points out that none of the war millionaires were found in the trenches or among the casualties.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why did Butler choose the word "racket" to describe war in 1935?
  2. According to Butler, who are the "insiders" who benefit from the war racket?
  3. What specific financial evidence does Butler provide regarding the Du Pont corporation?
  4. How many new millionaires and billionaires were reportedly created in the U.S. during the World War?
  5. Define the term "blood gains" as used in the context of Butler’s argument.
  6. What is the "asymmetry" Butler identifies between those who profit from war and those who fight it?
  7. How does the placement of the human cost after the financial evidence serve Butler’s rhetorical purpose?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Ethics of Logos: Analyze Butler’s use of specific corporate earnings (such as Bethlehem Steel and Anaconda Copper) as a rhetorical strategy. Does the use of concrete numbers make his categorical claim—that all war is a racket—more or less persuasive?
  2. The Categorical Problem: Butler argues that "war is a racket" in an absolute sense. Explore the potential limitations of this argument. Does his focus on World War I profits provide enough evidence to support his claim that wars of national defense or wars against tyranny are also rackets?
  3. Correlation vs. Causation: Critique the logical link in Butler’s argument between the existence of profit and the purpose of war. Is the fact that corporations profit from a war sufficient proof that the war was initiated for the purpose of those profits?
  4. The Moral Reckoning: Discuss Butler’s comparison of war to organized crime. In what ways does this framing shift the reader's perspective from viewing war as a political or military issue to viewing it as a criminal and moral one?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Asymmetry of Cost: The disparity between the small group of individuals who reap financial rewards from war and the large group of individuals who suffer the physical and emotional consequences.
  • Blood Gains: Butler’s term for profits made by industries during wartime, highlighting that these financial successes are directly linked to human suffering and death.
  • Logos: A rhetorical appeal based on logic, reason, and concrete evidence, such as the statistics and financial records Butler cites in Chapter 2.
  • Racket: A criminal scheme, often associated with organized crime, where a few benefit by deceiving and exploiting the many.
  • War Profiteering: The practice of making excessive or unreasonable profits from government contracts during wartime, often carrying the negative connotation that the profit is made from the suffering of others.
