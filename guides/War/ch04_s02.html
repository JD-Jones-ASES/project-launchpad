Study Guide: From Diagnosis to Prescription – Smedley Butler’s War Is a Racket

This study guide examines Chapters 3 through 5 of Smedley Butler’s War Is a Racket. While the initial chapters of his work focus on diagnosing war as a "racket" driven by profit, these concluding chapters shift toward a prescriptive approach, offering specific reforms and a passionate call to action.

Key Concepts and Summary

The Strategic Shift: From Diagnosis to Prescription

In persuasive writing, identifying a problem (diagnosis) is distinct from proposing a solution (prescription). Butler’s work follows this structure:

  • Chapters 1–2 (Diagnosis): Butler provides evidence of war profiteering, citing massive corporate gains. For instance, Bethlehem Steel’s profits rose from six million to forty-nine million dollars annually, and some coal producers saw gains of 7,856 percent on capital.
  • Chapters 3–5 (Prescription): Butler moves from "What is wrong?" to "What do we do about it?" through a blend of emotional appeal, concrete policy, and rhetorical intensity.

The Human Cost (Chapter 3)

Before introducing policy, Butler focuses on the physical and psychological toll of war. He describes veterans in hospitals as the "living dead," highlighting those in psychiatric wards with destroyed minds.

Beyond physical injury, Butler details the financial exploitation of soldiers:

  • Wages: Soldiers were paid thirty dollars a month, with half often deducted immediately.
  • Liberty Bonds: Soldiers were pressured into buying government securities to fund the war, only to be forced to sell them back to bankers at a steep discount when they returned home unemployed.

The Three Proposals (Chapter 4)

Butler outlines three specific reforms designed to dismantle the "racket" of war by attacking the profit motive, the decision-making process, and the military’s physical capabilities.

Proposal Description Intended Goal
Conscription of Capital Before drafting men, the government must conscript industry and labor. All participants (executives, politicians, generals) are limited to $30 a month. To remove the profit motive from warfare.
Limited Plebiscite The decision to enter war is moved from Congress to a direct vote by those of military age who pass a physical exam. To ensure the decision is made only by those who will bear the consequences.
Defensive Restriction Naval vessels must stay within 200 miles of the coast; aircraft within 500 miles; the army must never leave American territory. To make offensive warfare physically and legally impossible.

The Peroration (Chapter 5)

The document concludes with a peroration—the most emotionally intense part of a speech. Butler focuses on the following:

  • The Critique of Woodrow Wilson: Butler points out that Wilson was reelected in 1916 on the slogan "He Kept Us Out of War," only to request a declaration of war five months later.
  • Financial Motives: Butler argues the true reason for U.S. involvement in WWI was to protect billions of dollars owed to American bankers by the Allied powers.
  • The Ultimatum: He concludes with the definitive declaration: "TO HELL WITH WAR!"

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How did Butler justify his claim that war is a "racket" in the diagnostic phase of his writing? Answer: He used corporate profit data, such as du Pont’s earnings jumping tenfold and coal producers recording gains of up to 7,856 percent.

  2. What term does Butler use to describe the veterans he encountered in hospitals? Answer: He refers to them as the "living dead."

  3. According to Butler, how were soldiers financially exploited regarding Liberty Bonds? Answer: Soldiers were pressured to buy them from their meager wages and later had to sell them back to bankers at a discount when they needed money after the war.

  4. In Butler's proposed plebiscite, who would be allowed to vote? Answer: Only young men of military age who have passed a physical examination.

  5. What are the specific mileage restrictions Butler proposes for the Navy and the Air Force? Answer: Naval vessels would be limited to 200 miles from the coastline, and aircraft to 500 miles.

  6. What historical reversal does Butler cite regarding Woodrow Wilson? Answer: Wilson won the 1916 election promising to keep the U.S. out of war but asked for a war declaration in April 1917, shortly after his second inauguration.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. Rhetorical Strategy and the Human Cost: Analyze why Butler chooses to open Chapter 3 with descriptions of veterans' hospitals and psychiatric wards before introducing his three policy proposals in Chapter 4. How does this emotional grounding affect the reader's reception of his technical reforms?

  2. Evaluating the Three Proposals: Critique Butler's three reforms (conscription of capital, limited plebiscite, and defensive restrictions). To what extent do these proposals address the "racket" he describes, and what might be the practical challenges of implementing them in a modern political system?

  3. Conflicting Narratives of War: Butler argues that the U.S. entered World War I to protect the interests of bankers, while Woodrow Wilson pointed to unrestricted submarine warfare and the Zimmermann Telegram. Compare these two perspectives. Is Butler’s economic focus more or less persuasive than Wilson’s security-based explanation?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Capital: The nation's industry, finance, and labor resources. Butler argued this should be conscripted alongside men.
  • Liberty Bonds: Government securities sold to the American public to finance World War I; Butler highlights them as a tool of soldier exploitation.
  • Limited Plebiscite: A direct vote on a specific issue (in this case, war) where the voting pool is restricted to a specific group—specifically those who would be eligible to fight.
  • Peroration: The concluding part of a speech or argument, intended to inspire enthusiasm or action through heightened emotion.
  • Prescription: In a persuasive context, the specific solution or plan of action proposed to fix a diagnosed problem.
  • The Living Dead: Butler’s evocative term for veterans whose bodies or minds were permanently broken by the trauma of combat.
