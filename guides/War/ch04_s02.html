War Is a Racket: Chapters 3–5 (Primary Source)

Smedley Butler's War Is a Racket (1935) is one of the most direct antiwar pamphlets in American history. In its final three chapters, Butler shifts from diagnosis to prescription — moving from evidence of who profits to an account of who pays, then to three specific reforms, and finally to a passionate closing demand. The text that follows is Butler's own voice, unfiltered, and reading it requires attention to where he relies on data, where he relies on emotion, and where he refuses to engage with arguments he does not want to answer.

Chapter 3: Who Pays the Bills?

Butler opens Chapter 3 with the Liberty Bond scheme. Citizens bought bonds at one hundred dollars; bankers depressed the price to $84–$86, bought them back from frightened holders, then stimulated a boom and resold them at par or above. Soldiers were among the most exploited: paid thirty dollars a month, half of which went to support dependents, six dollars more to accident insurance, and the remainder coerced into bond purchases. Most soldiers received no pay on payday at all.

The human cost Butler describes goes beyond money. He visited eighteen government hospitals and found approximately fifty thousand "destroyed men" — the pick of the nation eighteen years earlier, now the living dead. At the hospital in Marion, Indiana, 1,800 veterans were held in pens, five hundred of them in barracks surrounded by steel bars and wires. "These boys don't even look like human beings," Butler writes. "Physically, they are in good shape; mentally, they are gone."

Butler traces this destruction to a two-step process: soldiers were remolded through mass psychology to regard killing as normal, then discharged abruptly with no support for readjustment. The system built them up for war and abandoned them when the war ended.

The financial exploitation was layered. Before World War I, soldiers had bargained for their labor (the prize system paid bonuses and shares of captured vessels). After conscription replaced the prize system, soldiers lost the right to negotiate. The government replaced wages with medals — following Napoleon's insight that men hunger for decorations — and propaganda replaced both.

Chapter 4: How to Smash This Racket!

Butler is direct: war cannot be ended by disarmament conferences, peace parleys at Geneva, or well-meaning resolutions. It can be smashed only by taking the profit out of war. He offers three steps.

Step 1: Conscript capital and industry. One month before the government can conscript young men, it must conscript capital, industry, and labor. Every executive, banker, politician, general, and admiral is restricted to thirty dollars a month — the same as the soldier in the trenches. Why shouldn't they? They aren't running any risk of being killed.

Step 2: A limited plebiscite. The decision to go to war should belong to those who will fight it. A seventy-six-year-old munitions factory president who will never shoulder arms should not vote on whether the nation goes to war. Butler proposes annual physical examinations of men of military age — those who pass are the only voters in any war plebiscite.

Step 3: Restrict the military to defense. Naval vessels should be limited by law to within 200 miles of the coastline; aircraft to 500 miles for reconnaissance; the army should never leave American territory. Had the 200-mile law existed in 1898, the USS Maine would never have been in Havana Harbor. She would never have been blown up. There would have been no Spanish-American War.

Butler's "swivel-chair admirals" — his contemptuous name for Washington-based naval lobbyists — argue for large fleets in the name of defense while scheduling maneuvers two thousand to thirty-five hundred miles from the American coast. Butler's restriction would end this pretense.

Chapter 5: To Hell With War!

Butler opens the final chapter by acknowledging he is not naive: war is not a thing of the past, and people can be pushed into another one. Then he turns to Wilson. In 1916, Woodrow Wilson was reelected on the promise that he had kept America out of war. Five months later he asked Congress to declare war on Germany.

Butler's explanation: money. An Allied commission had told the President that without American intervention, the Allies would lose — and with them the five or six billion dollars owed to American bankers, munitions makers, and manufacturers. The war was a collection operation disguised as a crusade for democracy.

Disarmament conferences have failed and will fail, Butler argues. Professional soldiers don't want to disarm — no admiral wants to be without a ship, no general without a command. Lurking behind every conference are the agents of those who profit by war.

The pamphlet ends: "TO HELL WITH WAR!"

Key Terms and Definitions

Term Definition
Armistice The ceasefire of November 11, 1918, ending fighting on the Western Front in World War I
Conscript To compel into military service by law; Butler argues capital and industry should be conscripted before soldiers
Liberty Bonds Government securities sold to the public, including soldiers, to fund World War I; Butler exposes them as a tool for banker profit
Munificent Extremely generous; used sarcastically by Butler to describe the soldier's meager $30 monthly pay
Plebiscite A direct vote by eligible citizens on a specific question; Butler proposes limiting this to men of military age
Swivel-chair admirals Butler's contemptuous term for naval officers who lobby in Washington offices rather than serve at sea

Discussion Questions

  1. Butler says war can be "smashed effectively only by taking the profit out of war." Does his evidence support that profit is the primary cause of war, or could other factors be equally important?
  2. Butler's plebiscite would restrict the vote to men of military age and physical fitness. Is this restriction consistent with democratic principles? Who would it exclude?
  3. Butler accuses Wilson of going to war for financial reasons. Wilson gave different reasons — unrestricted submarine warfare and the Zimmermann Telegram. Is Butler's account more persuasive, less persuasive, or does it depend on what evidence you weigh?
  4. Does the closing line "TO HELL WITH WAR!" strengthen or weaken the policy proposals that precede it?
