# Rhetorical Ethos: Authority and Credibility in War Literature

This study guide explores the concept of **ethos**—the credibility, character, and authority a speaker brings to an argument—as analyzed in "Rhetoric Under the Microscope." By examining four distinct historical figures, this guide illustrates how personal background, professional experience, and social position shape the power and limitations of a persuasive message.

---

## Key Concepts and Theoretical Framework

### The Nature of Ethos
Ethos is the answer to the silent question asked by every audience: "Why should I listen to you?" It is the foundation upon which an argument is built. Rather than relying solely on logic or emotion, ethos draws its power from the speaker’s identity and the "right" they have earned to speak on a particular subject.

### The Four Models of Authority
The text identifies four distinct types of ethos used by writers and speakers to address the subject of war:

1.  **The Volunteer (Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr.):** Authority derived from chosen sacrifice.
2.  **The Insider (Smedley Butler):** Authority derived from professional expertise turned against the institution.
3.  **The Statesman (Woodrow Wilson):** Authority derived from positional power and public reluctance.
4.  **The Witness (Erich Maria Remarque):** Authority derived from representative, common experience.

---

## Detailed Profiles in Ethos

### Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr.: The Ethos of Sacrifice
Holmes’ authority is rooted in his status as a volunteer who suffered for his convictions. 
*   **Background:** A Harvard graduate and son of a famous writer, Holmes chose to enlist in the Twentieth Massachusetts Volunteer Infantry rather than avoiding the Civil War.
*   **The Physical Toll:** He was wounded three times: in the chest at Ball’s Bluff (1861), the neck at Antietam (1862), and the heel at Fredericksburg (1862).
*   **Rhetorical Function:** Holmes does not theorize from a distance; he speaks from the perspective of one who has "felt the bullet." His celebration of "martial virtue" is difficult to dismiss because he paid for his faith in blood.

### Smedley Butler: The Ethos of the Whistle-Blower
Butler’s authority comes from his position as a highly decorated career professional who eventually denounced the system he served.
*   **Background:** A Marine who served for over three decades across four continents (including the Philippines, China, Nicaragua, Haiti, Mexico, and France). He is one of the few individuals to receive the Medal of Honor twice.
*   **The Reversal:** Butler famously characterized war as a "racket." 
*   **Rhetorical Function:** His ethos is "devastating" because it is an internal betrayal. The audience expects a decorated general to defend the military; when he attacks it as a fraud, the surprise lends his words significant force.

### Woodrow Wilson: The Ethos of Reluctance
Wilson’s authority is not based on combat experience but on his high office and his perceived moral struggle.
*   **Background:** A scholar and university president who became President of the United States. He had no personal experience with weapons or combat.
*   **The Reluctant Warrior:** Wilson campaigned for re-election in 1916 on the slogan "He Kept Us Out of War." When he asked Congress for a declaration of war in 1917, he framed it as a reversal of his own inclinations, driven by "moral duty."
*   **Rhetorical Function:** By appearing as a man who exhausted every alternative to peace, he suggests that if even he is convinced war is necessary, the case must be undeniable.

### Erich Maria Remarque: The Ethos of Typicality
Remarque’s authority is derived from being an ordinary "witness" rather than an exceptional hero or leader.
*   **Background:** Conscripted (drafted) at eighteen into the Imperial German Army in 1916. He was wounded by shrapnel on the Western Front in 1917 and spent the rest of the war in a hospital.
*   **The Common Experience:** Unlike the volunteer Holmes or the professional Butler, Remarque represents the millions of young men swept up by forces beyond their control.
*   **Rhetorical Function:** He speaks for the "common experience." His ethos is that of the man who was there and survived to tell the truth that leaders and professionals often omit.

## Comparison: The Ethos Spectrum

| Figure | Type of Ethos | Primary Strength | Primary Weakness |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Holmes** | The Volunteer | High moral ground; cannot be accused of cowardice. | Experience was exceptional and elite, not typical of most soldiers. |
| **Butler** | The Insider | Deep internal knowledge of the "system." | Critics may question why he served for 30 years before speaking out. |
| **Wilson** | The Statesman | The massive weight and prestige of the Presidency. | No personal experience of the violence he is authorizing. |
| **Remarque**| The Witness | Speaks for the common, silent millions. | Offers testimony rather than a policy-based or logical argument. |

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Duality of Choice:** Compare and contrast the ethos of the "Volunteer" (Holmes) with the ethos of the "Witness" (Remarque). Does the act of choosing to fight grant a speaker more authority than one who was forced to fight? Analyze how "sacrifice" versus "typicality" impacts an audience's reception of their message.
2.  **The Power of the Insider:** Smedley Butler and Woodrow Wilson both occupy positions of high authority, yet they use their ethos in opposite ways. Analyze how Butler’s "insider betrayal" compares to Wilson’s "positional power." Which form of authority is more persuasive when arguing for or against an institutional change?
3.  **The Reluctant Warrior Archetype:** Explore the rhetorical effectiveness of Woodrow Wilson’s shift from a peace candidate to a war president. Is the "reluctant warrior" a more effective figure for mobilization than a leader who is eager for conflict? Use the text to support your argument regarding positional authority and moral duty.
4.  **The Spectrum of Experience:** The text argues that no form of ethos is inherently superior. Select a contemporary issue and describe four hypothetical speakers who would represent the Volunteer, the Insider, the Statesman, and the Witness. How would each speaker’s authority shape what they are "allowed" to say about that issue?

## Glossary of Key Terms

*   **Appeal to Authority:** An argument that draws persuasive force from a speaker’s credentials, experience, or position rather than logic or evidence alone.
*   **Conscript:** An individual who is drafted or legally required by the government to serve in the military, rather than enlisting voluntarily.
*   **Ethos:** The rhetorical appeal based on the character, credibility, and authority of the speaker.
*   **Martial Virtue:** The qualities or behaviors (such as courage and faith) associated with soldiers and military service.
*   **Position Authority:** Credibility derived from the office or rank one holds (e.g., the Presidency) rather than personal experience.
*   **Reluctant Warrior:** A rhetorical figure who claims to seek peace and only resorts to violence or war out of a profound sense of unavoidable duty.
*   **Representativeness:** The quality of being typical of a larger group; in rhetoric, the ability to speak for the "common" or "ordinary" person’s experience.
*   **The Insider:** A speaker whose authority comes from having been a high-ranking or long-term member of the institution they are now describing or criticizing.
