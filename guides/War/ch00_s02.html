Study Guide: The Four Voices on War

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the diverse perspectives on war as presented by four influential historical figures: Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr., Smedley Darlington Butler, Woodrow Wilson, and Erich Maria Remarque. It includes key concepts, biographical summaries, practice questions, and a glossary to assist in the critical evaluation of their arguments.

Core Perspectives and Key Concepts

The text presents four distinct arguments regarding the purpose and nature of war, ranging from a belief in its ability to forge human character to its condemnation as a profit-driven scheme.

1. Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr.: War as a Crucible of Virtue

  • Biographical Context: A Union Army veteran of the Civil War (wounded three times) and a long-serving U.S. Supreme Court Justice (1902–1932) known as "The Great Dissenter."
  • The Central Argument: War serves as a "crucible" that forges essential human virtues—courage, self-sacrifice, and devotion—that a commercial, comfortable society cannot produce.
  • Key Concept: The "soldier's faith" represents the highest expression of human character.

2. Smedley Darlington Butler: War as a Racket

  • Biographical Context: A highly decorated career Marine (two Medals of Honor) who became an antiwar activist after retirement. He exposed the "Business Plot" in 1933.
  • The Central Argument: War is a "racket" where a small group of insiders and wealthy industrialists reap enormous profits while soldiers pay the cost in lives and suffering.
  • Key Concept: The only way to stop war is to "take the profit out of war."

3. Thomas Woodrow Wilson: War for Democracy

  • Biographical Context: A scholar and progressive reformer who served as U.S. President during World War I. He was the architect of the League of Nations and a Nobel Peace Prize recipient.
  • The Central Argument: War is justified when it is fought for democracy and human rights rather than conquest. American power is necessary to make the world "safe for democratic governance."
  • Key Concept: International cooperation and the moral necessity of using power to secure global rights.

4. Erich Maria Remarque: War as Physical and Psychological Reality

  • Biographical Context: A German veteran of the Western Front and author of All Quiet on the Western Front. His work was later targeted and burned by the Nazi regime.
  • The Central Argument: Rather than using rhetoric or abstractions, Remarque shows the grim reality of trench warfare—mud, terror, and senseless death.
  • Key Concept: A challenge to "every abstraction about war," forcing the reader to confront the lived experience of the soldier.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Who is referred to as "The Great Dissenter," and why is this title relevant to his background?
  2. What specific events in Smedley Butler's post-retirement life signaled his transformation from a warrior to an antiwar activist?
  3. Contrast Woodrow Wilson’s 1916 election platform with his April 1917 address to Congress.
  4. How does Erich Maria Remarque's method of "arguing" about war differ from the other three writers?
  5. According to Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr., what is the "soldier’s faith"?
  6. What was the "Business Plot" of 1933, and which figure was responsible for exposing it?
  7. Why did the Nazi regime target Erich Maria Remarque’s work in 1933?
  8. What is the primary solution Smedley Butler proposes to end the "racket" of war?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Ethics of Abstraction vs. Reality: Compare Woodrow Wilson’s idealistic rhetoric regarding "democracy and human rights" with Erich Maria Remarque’s depiction of the "physical and psychological reality of trench warfare." Does Remarque’s focus on the individual soldier’s experience effectively undermine Wilson’s political justifications?
  2. Virtue vs. Exploitation: Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. views war as a crucible for human virtue, while Smedley Butler views it as a scheme for private profit. Evaluate these two opposing views. Can war simultaneously be a "racket" for the elite and a source of "highest character" for the soldier?
  3. The Evolution of the Warrior: Both Smedley Butler and Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. were decorated military men, yet they reached vastly different conclusions about the meaning of their service. Analyze how their post-war careers—one in law and the other in activism—might have shaped their final arguments on the nature of war.
  4. The Role of the Citizen-Evaluator: The text suggests that its purpose is to provide "the tools to evaluate... arguments for yourself." Using the four voices as a framework, discuss the challenges a citizen faces when trying to reach a conclusion on whether a war is "noble," "necessary," or "a racket."

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Business Plot (1933): An alleged conspiracy by wealthy industrialists to overthrow President Franklin D. Roosevelt, exposed by Smedley Butler.
  • Crucible: A severe test or situation in which different elements interact, leading to the creation of something new; used by Holmes to describe how war forges character.
  • League of Nations: An international organization championed by Woodrow Wilson intended to promote democracy and international cooperation.
  • Racket: A fraudulent scheme or business marked by the exploitation of others for profit; the term used by Butler to describe the military-industrial system.
  • The Great Dissenter: A nickname for Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr., reflecting his willingness to challenge majority opinions on the U.S. Supreme Court.
  • Trench Warfare: A form of combat characterized by the physical and psychological horrors of life in the trenches, as depicted by Erich Maria Remarque.
  • Western Front: The main theater of war during World War I in Western Europe, where Remarque served and where the events of All Quiet on the Western Front take place.
