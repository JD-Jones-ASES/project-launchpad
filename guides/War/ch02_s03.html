Study Guide: Smedley Butler and the Origins of "War Is a Racket"

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the life, military career, and rhetorical foundations of Major General Smedley Darlington Butler, as detailed in Chapter 2, Section 3 of The Racket Revealed.

Key Concepts and Biography

The Profile of Smedley Darlington Butler (1881–1940)

Smedley Butler was a Major General in the United States Marine Corps and, at the time of his retirement in 1931, the most decorated Marine in American history. His background presents a stark contrast between his upbringing and his professional life:

  • Family Heritage: Born into a prominent Quaker family in West Chester, Pennsylvania. The Quakers are traditionally pacifist.
  • Political Connections: His father, Thomas S. Butler, served in the U.S. House of Representatives for 31 years and chaired the House Naval Affairs Committee.
  • Early Enlistment: Despite his Quaker roots, Butler lied about his age at 16 to enlist in the Marine Corps during the Spanish-American War in 1898.

Military Record and Decorations

Butler's 33-year career spanned every major theater of American military operations between 1898 and 1931. He is one of only nineteen men in U.S. history to receive the Medal of Honor twice.

The Banana Wars

A defining element of Butler’s career was his participation in the "Banana Wars." These were not Congressionally declared wars fought for national survival, but rather military interventions in Central America, the Caribbean, and Mexico.

  • Primary Objective: To protect the commercial interests of American corporations, such as the United Fruit Company.
  • Authority: Ordered by U.S. Presidents and the State Department, often at the direct request of business interests.
  • Butler’s Perspective: He later characterized himself during this period as a "muscle man for Big Business," enforcing commercial interests with "rifle and bayonet."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. Why is Smedley Butler’s indictment of war considered difficult for critics to dismiss? Butler was not a pacifist intellectual or a career politician; he was one of the most accomplished combat soldiers in U.S. history. His arguments were based on three decades of firsthand experience in major military theaters.

2. What role did Butler’s father play in the United States government? Thomas S. Butler was a member of the U.S. House of Representatives for 31 years and served as the chairman of the House Naval Affairs Committee.

3. Define the Gendarmerie d'Haïti. It was the Haitian national police force, organized and commanded by U.S. Marine officers during the American occupation of Haiti (1915–1934).

4. What is the "great irony" of Butler’s life as described in the text? The irony lies in the fact that Butler spent thirty years actively enforcing the very same commercial and corporate interests that he would later denounce as a "racket."

5. How did Butler describe the source of his authority or "ethos" in writing War Is a Racket? His authority did not stem from formal education or eloquence, but from his "scars"—the firsthand knowledge gained from decades of observing money flow from government contracts to private corporations during wartime.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Evolution of Ethos: Analyze how Smedley Butler uses his personal history and military decorations to establish credibility (ethos). Why is the transition from "the most decorated Marine in history" to a critic of military intervention essential to the power of his message?

2. Commercial Interests and Military Intervention: Based on the text’s description of the "Banana Wars," discuss the relationship between private corporations, the State Department, and the U.S. Marine Corps in the early 20th century. How does Butler’s later description of himself as a "muscle man for Big Business" challenge the traditional narrative of military service?

3. The Conflict of Tradition and Action: Explore the contradiction between Butler’s Quaker pacifist upbringing and his 33-year career in combat. How might his family background and his father’s political position have influenced his perspective on the intersection of government policy and military force?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Banana Wars: A series of U.S. military interventions in Latin America and the Caribbean (approx. 1898–1934) aimed at protecting American corporate interests.
  • Camp Pontanezen: The largest American debarkation camp in Europe, located in France, which Butler commanded during World War I.
  • Ethos: A rhetorical appeal based on the character, credibility, or authority of the speaker.
  • Gendarmerie d'Haïti: The Haitian national police force managed by U.S. Marines during the occupation of Haiti.
  • Marine Corps Brevet Medal: One of the high-ranking military decorations awarded to Butler during his service.
  • Medal of Honor: The highest military decoration in the United States; Butler was a rare two-time recipient.
  • Quaker: A member of a Christian group (the Society of Friends) known for their deep tradition of pacifism.
  • The Racket: Butler’s term for war, suggesting it is a system that enriches a small group of profiteers at the expense of the many.
