War Is a Racket, Chapters 1–2

The first two chapters of War Is a Racket (1935) by Smedley Butler constitute one of the most direct antiwar arguments in American literature. Chapter 1 defines the problem; Chapter 2 produces the evidence. Together they build a case that has never been fully refuted — because Butler does not argue philosophically. He argues with ledger books.

Chapter 1: The Definition

Butler opens with six words: "WAR is a racket. It always has been." He defines a racket as something that benefits a small inside group at the expense of the many — something "not what it seems to the majority of the people." He then applies this definition to war with a series of rhetorical questions: How many of the 21,000 new millionaires created by the World War shouldered a rifle? How many dug a trench? How many were wounded or killed? In each case, the answer is none.

The human bill of war, Butler says, is not measured only in dollars. It is measured in "newly placed gravestones, mangled bodies, shattered minds, broken hearts and homes, economic instability, depression and all its attendant miseries." The same people who extract the profits leave the dying to others.

Chapter 2: Who Makes the Profits?

Chapter 2 is where the numbers appear. Butler documents the wartime profits of American corporations during the World War (1914–1918), comparing them to peacetime earnings (1910–1914):

Company Pre-War Annual Profit Wartime Annual Profit Increase
Du Pont $6,000,000 $58,000,000 ~950%
Bethlehem Steel $6,000,000 $49,000,000 ~800%
United States Steel $105,000,000 $240,000,000 ~128%
Anaconda Copper $10,000,000 $34,000,000 240%
International Nickel $4,000,000 $73,000,000 1,725%
Central Leather Co. $1,167,000 $15,000,000 1,100%

Butler also catalogs the waste: 35 million pairs of shoes purchased for 4 million soldiers (with 25 million left over after the war); thousands of McClellan cavalry saddles bought when there was no cavalry overseas; 20 million mosquito nets sent for soldiers in France, where there were no mosquitoes; $1,000,000,000 spent on airplane engines that never left the ground; $635,000,000 on wooden ships that sank.

The total cost of American participation in WWI: approximately $52,000,000,000 — of which $16,000,000,000 was profit, producing at least 21,000 new millionaires and billionaires.

The Geopolitical Warning

Writing in 1935, Butler observes the warning signs of another war: 40 million men under arms worldwide, Hitler rearming Germany, Mussolini proclaiming that "war alone brings up to its highest tension all human energy." He calculates the proposed justifications for war in Asia — protecting $90 million in China trade or less than $200 million in Philippine investments — against the cost of a war that "might well cost us tens of billions of dollars, hundreds of thousands of lives."

His conclusion: the same class of men who profited from the last war are preparing to profit from the next one.

Key Figures and Facts

  • 21,000 — new millionaires and billionaires created in the U.S. during WWI
  • $52,000,000,000 — total U.S. cost of WWI participation ($400 per American)
  • $16,000,000,000 — profits extracted from that total
  • Nye Committee — Senate committee probing munitions industry profits, cited by Butler as having "hardly scratched the surface"
  • Rhetorical question — a question asked not for an answer but to make a point; Butler uses them to confront the reader with obvious truths about who fights and who profits
