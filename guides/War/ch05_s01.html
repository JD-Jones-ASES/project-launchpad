The Philosophical Clash

Chapter 5 opens not with policy or politics but with the deepest possible disagreement: what does war do to the human soul? Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. and Smedley Butler are not merely arguing about whether a particular war was justified. They are arguing about the fundamental nature of war itself — and the fact that both men earned their views through direct combat experience makes their opposite conclusions all the more striking.

Holmes: War as a Forge of Character

Holmes argued that war is a crucible — a forge that burns away weakness and reveals what human beings are capable of at their best. The soldier who risks death for a cause he may not fully understand demonstrates the highest form of moral courage: faith without certainty, action without guarantee. For Holmes, a comfortable, commercial society focused on profit and material comfort could not produce such virtues on its own. It needed the pressure of war to keep its moral muscles from atrophying.

Key terms:

  • Ethos: Holmes' personal authority comes from his Civil War service. He was shot through the neck at Antietam and left for dead at Ball's Bluff. This direct experience gives his argument weight a civilian's version would lack.
  • Crucible / Forge: Holmes' central metaphor for what war does — it tests and reveals character under extreme pressure.
  • Gilded Age: The era of American history Holmes was responding to — a period of material prosperity and, in his view, moral softness.

Butler: War as a Machine of Destruction

Butler agreed that soldiers display extraordinary courage — he had earned two Medals of Honor in combat. But Butler argued that soldier courage is exploited, not honored, by the system that sends men to war. The real beneficiaries are not the men in the trenches but the corporations that sell the ammunition, the uniforms, and the canned goods. War does not build character; it destroys bodies, minds, and families while enriching a small class of profiteers who never hear a shot fired.

Key terms:

  • Racket: Butler's central claim — war is a scheme in which the few profit and the many pay.
  • Systemic exploitation: The war system uses genuine soldier courage for economic ends that benefit corporations, not the soldiers themselves.

Two Veterans, Two Verdicts

The most striking feature of this debate is that both men earned the right to their opinions through combat. Holmes served in the Civil War. Butler served as a Marine in conflicts spanning three decades. Neither can be dismissed as a coward or an armchair theorist. Yet they reached opposite conclusions about what their experience meant. This forces a direct confrontation with the question: can the same experience produce opposite lessons?

Can Both Be Right?

The section proposes that Holmes and Butler may not be as contradictory as they first appear — because they are analyzing war at different scales.

  • Holmes focuses on persons: For the individual soldier in the moment of decision, the virtues he describes are real. Courage is real. Self-sacrifice is real.
  • Butler focuses on systems: Whatever happens to the individual soldier's soul, the machinery of war serves economic interests. Corporations profit. Politicians gain power.

It is possible for an individual soldier to display genuine courage while the war he fights serves corrupt purposes. The question is whether celebrating the soldier's virtue — as Holmes does — distracts us from questioning the system — as Butler demands.

Key Concepts for Review

  • Individual vs. systemic analysis: The difference between asking "what does war do to a person?" and "what does war do to a society or economy?"
  • Ethos and lived experience: Both Holmes and Butler derive their authority from direct combat experience, making their disagreement philosophically honest even if irresolvable.
  • The distraction problem: Holmes argues that focusing on individual virtue is enough. Butler argues that celebrating courage without asking whose purposes it serves makes exploitation look noble. This tension — does honoring the soldier make it harder to question the war? — is one of the central questions of the chapter.
