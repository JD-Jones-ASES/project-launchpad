Study Guide: Woodrow Wilson’s Argument for War (1917)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of Woodrow Wilson’s 1917 address to Congress, as analyzed in Chapter 3, Section 2 of "The World Made Safe." It explores the architecture of Wilson’s moral argument, the principles of democracy versus autocracy, and the critical omissions within his rhetoric.

1. The Architecture of Wilson’s Argument

Wilson’s address is characterized not as a simple call to arms, but as a multi-layered moral case built on facts, universal principles, and national duty.

Layer 1: The Grievance (The Facts)

Wilson begins by establishing a record of German aggression. This is a deliberate rhetorical choice to present himself as a reasonable leader compelled to action by circumstances rather than emotion.

  • Key Facts: Resumption of unrestricted submarine warfare, sinking of neutral vessels, and the deaths of American noncombatants.
  • Core Claim: War is not a virtue or a racket, but a "grim duty" forced upon a peaceful nation because the German government left the United States no honorable alternative.

Layer 2: The Principle (Democracy vs. Autocracy)

Wilson elevates the conflict from a maritime dispute to a universal struggle for the survival of democratic government.

  • The Distinction: Wilson distinguishes between the German people and their government. He argues that the German people did not choose the war; it was thrust upon them by autocratic leaders who do not answer to their citizens.
  • The Goal: To make the world “safe for democracy” by establishing an international order where democratic nations can live without fear of autocratic aggression.

Layer 3: The Duty (America’s Role)

Wilson asserts that the United States has a unique responsibility because it was founded on principles of liberty, equality, and self-government.

  • Obligation: If democracy is threatened, America must respond because its founding principles impose a duty to defend those values globally.

2. Wilson’s Argument Map

The following table outlines the logical structure of Wilson’s case for entering the war:

Component Details
Claim The United States must enter the war against Germany.
Evidence 1. Germany’s resumption of unrestricted submarine warfare.
2. The Zimmermann Telegram revealing German hostility.
3. The inherently aggressive nature of autocratic governments.
Reasoning 1. Neutrality is impossible when attacked.
2. The conflict is a struggle between democracy and autocracy.
3. America must defend universal principles of self-government.
Conclusion War is a “fearful thing,” but submission to autocratic aggression is worse.

3. Critical Analysis: What Wilson Omitted

A complete understanding of Wilson’s argument requires examining the factors he chose to leave out. These omissions suggest that the argument operated in the realm of principle rather than economic or social reality.

  • The Cost of Victory: Wilson does not promise an easy or cheap victory. Unlike other thinkers who celebrate the experience of war, Wilson frames it as a "fearful" burden.
  • Economic Interests: Wilson ignores the influence of war profits. He does not mention munitions manufacturers, steel companies, or bankers who had lent billions to the Allies and stood to lose their investments if the Allies were defeated.
  • The British Blockade: While Wilson condemns German submarine warfare as a violation of neutral rights, he omits the fact that Britain also violated neutral rights by seizing American cargo and preventing food from reaching German civilians.
  • Domestic Inequality: Wilson speaks of fighting for democracy abroad while ignoring the millions of African Americans living under Jim Crow laws at home, effectively denying democracy to a significant portion of the American population.
  • Alternatives to War: Wilson presents the choice as binary (fight or submit) and does not engage with the possibility that continued neutrality might have saved more lives.

Note on Omissions: Identifying these gaps does not necessarily mean the argument is dishonest. Omissions can be strategic (hiding information) or practical (focusing on the most relevant points for the audience).

4. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why does Wilson lead his address with facts regarding submarine warfare rather than an emotional appeal?
  2. How does Wilson distinguish between the German people and the German government?
  3. According to "Democratic Peace Theory," why are republics less likely to engage in aggressive war?
  4. What was the significance of the Zimmermann Telegram in Wilson’s argument?
  5. In what way does Wilson’s view of war differ from that of Oliver Wendell Holmes or Smedley Butler?
  6. Why did Wilson protest the German submarine warfare more vigorously than the British naval blockade?

5. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Universalization of Conflict: Analyze how Wilson transforms a specific dispute over maritime rights into a "clash of civilizations." To what extent is this escalation necessary to justify American involvement in a European war?
  2. The Paradox of Democracy: Wilson argued that America had a duty to defend democracy abroad. Evaluate this claim in light of the domestic reality of Jim Crow laws mentioned in the text. Can a nation effectively fight for a principle it does not fully practice?
  3. Economic vs. Moral Motivation: Compare Wilson’s argument of "grim duty" and moral principle with Smedley Butler’s assertion that "war is a racket." Based on the omissions identified in the text, which perspective offers a more comprehensive view of the motivations for war?
  4. The Rhetoric of Neutrality: Discuss the role of "neutral rights" in Wilson’s argument. How does Wilson handle the violations of these rights by different belligerent nations, and what does this suggest about the strategic nature of his moral claims?

6. Glossary of Key Terms

  • Autocracy: A system of government where power is held by leaders (such as emperors or generals) who do not answer to their citizens, often resulting in aggressive foreign policy.
  • Democratic Peace Theory: A theory in political science, originally suggested by Immanuel Kant, which argues that democratic (republican) governments are naturally inclined toward peace because citizens are reluctant to vote for the costs and burdens of war.
  • Liberal Democracy: A system characterized by free elections, the rule of law, the protection of individual rights, and limits on government power.
  • Neutrality: The legal and political status of a nation that does not take part in a war between other nations. Wilson argued this state became impossible once American rights were directly attacked.
  • Self-Determination: The principle that peoples and nations have the right to choose their own form of government without external interference; a cornerstone of Wilson's postwar vision.
  • Unrestricted Submarine Warfare: A naval tactic where submarines sink merchant and passenger ships without warning, a primary factor cited by Wilson as a grievance against Germany.
