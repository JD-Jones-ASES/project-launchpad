The Rhetoric Examined: How Butler Argues

Butler's final three chapters are not just an argument — they are a performance. The shift from data-driven exposé in Chapters 1–2 to the raw emotion of Chapter 5 is deliberate. Understanding how Butler manages that shift is as important as evaluating what he says. This section examines the rhetorical strategies Butler uses in each chapter and asks whether those strategies strengthen or complicate his case.

Chapter 3: Pathos as Evidence

In Chapters 1–2, Butler relied heavily on logos — the appeal to reason through evidence and data. He cited specific corporations, specific profit margins, specific dollar amounts. Chapter 3 changes the register. The data gives way to description: veterans' hospitals, men behind steel bars, minds shattered by the war that was supposed to end all wars. This is pathos — the appeal to emotion — but Butler uses it in a distinctive way.

In the government hospital in Marion, Indiana, 1,800 of these boys are in pens! Five hundred of them in a barracks with steel bars and wires all around outside the buildings and on the porches. These already have been mentally destroyed. These boys don't even look like human beings. Oh, the looks on their faces!

Notice the word "boys." Butler does not call them soldiers, veterans, or men. He calls them boys — a word choice that emphasizes their youth and vulnerability. He describes them as being "in pens" — language that evokes animals in cages, not heroes honored for their service. The exclamation "Oh, the looks on their faces!" breaks the detached tone of policy argument and lets the reader hear Butler's own anguish.

But Butler is not merely being emotional. He is constructing a rhetorical bridge between the profit data of Chapters 1–2 and the policy proposals of Chapter 4. The logic runs: you have seen who profits; now see who pays. The juxtaposition is the argument. Butler does not need to say "this is unjust" — the contrast between corporate earnings and veterans locked in psychiatric wards says it for him. This is pathos in the service of logos: the emotional response the reader feels is itself the logical proof that the system is corrupt.

Chapter 4: The Plain Style

Chapter 4 is the most straightforward section of the pamphlet. Butler lays out his three proposals in plain, declarative prose. His sentences are short and direct. He uses the imperative mood: "Let the officers and the directors be conscripted." He anticipates objections and dismisses them with a soldier's bluntness: "Why shouldn't they? They aren't running any risk of being killed."

Give capital and industry and labor thirty days to think it over and you will find, by that time, there will be no war. That will smash the war racket — that and nothing else.

This is a marked contrast to Holmes, whose Victorian oratory builds in long, cresting periods, and to Wilson, whose presidential address moves with the stately cadence of formal deliberation. Butler writes like a man giving orders. He does not ask his readers to consider whether his proposal might have merit. He tells them flatly that it will work and that nothing else will.

This confidence is part of his ethos — the appeal based on the character and authority of the speaker. Butler speaks as a man who has seen war from the inside and knows how it operates. His two Medals of Honor and his decades of combat give him a platform no civilian critic could match.

But the same confidence can be a weakness. By presenting his proposals as the only possible solution ("that and nothing else"), Butler closes the door on alternative solutions, compromise, or the possibility that he might be wrong. Certainty is not the same as correctness. Strong writers acknowledge the limits of their own proposals.

Chapter 5: The Peroration

A peroration is the closing section of a speech or argument. In classical rhetoric, it had two purposes: to summarize the main points and to move the audience to action or strong feeling. The peroration is typically the most emotionally intense part of a speech, and it is the last thing the audience hears — which makes it the most likely to be remembered.

Chapter 5 is Butler's peroration, and it is dramatically different from everything that precedes it. The data is gone. The policy details are gone. What remains is anger, accusation, and a four-word verdict on the entire institution of war.

Butler opens by conceding a point: he is not naive enough to believe that war is a thing of the past. Then he turns to Wilson:

Looking back, Woodrow Wilson was re-elected president in 1916 on a platform that he had "kept us out of war" and on the implied promise that he would "keep us out of war." Yet, five months later he asked Congress to declare war on Germany.

This is a direct challenge to Wilson's account — but notice what Butler does not do. He does not engage with Wilson's specific arguments about submarine warfare or the rights of neutral nations. He does not address the Zimmermann Telegram. He does not consider that Wilson might have been genuinely torn between peace and war. Instead, he offers a single-cause explanation — money — and treats it as self-evidently sufficient. This is a powerful rhetorical move, but it is also a vulnerable one. By reducing Wilson's decision to a financial transaction, Butler avoids the harder question: What if the financial interests and the genuine security threat were both real?

Three Closings Compared

Holmes closes "The Soldier's Faith" with an elevated appeal to the beauty of sacrifice and the nobility of faith in a cause greater than oneself. His tone is reverential, even worshipful.

Wilson closes his war address with a solemn acknowledgment of the cost — "It is a fearful thing to lead this great peaceful people into war" — followed by an assertion that America has no choice and must accept God's guidance. His tone is grave but resolute.

Butler closes with "TO HELL WITH WAR!" His tone is raw, angry, and final.

Each closing reflects the writer's character and purpose. Holmes wants the audience to admire. Wilson wants the audience to accept. Butler wants the audience to revolt.

General vs. Specific Arguments

One of the most important features of War Is a Racket is how it handles Wilson's argument. Butler argues against war in general — he claims that the institution of war, as practiced by modern nations, always serves private profit at public expense. Wilson argues for a specific war — he claims that this particular conflict, at this particular moment, was necessary and just. These two arguments operate at different levels of generality and can logically coexist: it is possible that most wars are rackets and that some wars are necessary. Butler's framework does not allow for this. Wilson's does — but Wilson's framework also makes it easy to justify wars that perhaps should not be fought.

The strongest reading of Butler does not require accepting his claim that all wars are rackets. It requires only accepting his claim that the financial incentives built into the war system make it dangerously easy to dress up a profitable venture as a noble cause. Even if Wilson was sincere, Butler's structural critique stands: the system rewarded those who pushed for war and punished those who fought it.

Key Rhetorical Terms

Term Definition
Logos The appeal to reason through evidence, data, and logical argument
Pathos The appeal to the audience's emotions
Ethos The appeal based on the speaker's character, credibility, or authority
Peroration The closing section of a speech or argument; typically the most emotionally intense and most memorable part
Imperative mood A grammatical form that issues commands or requests (e.g., "Let the officers be conscripted")
Plain style Direct, unadorned writing that avoids ornate language and classical allusions
