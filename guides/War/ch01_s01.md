# Study Guide: The Soldier’s Faith (Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr., 1895)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr.’s 1895 Memorial Day address. It explores the central themes of the "soldier’s faith," the tension between commercial interests and military honor, and Holmes’s philosophical stance on the necessity of struggle.

---

## Key Concepts and Themes

### The Conflict Between Commerce and Honor
Holmes identifies a shift in societal values toward the end of the 19th century. He notes that "war is out of fashion" and that the "man of wealth" has replaced the soldier as the figure who commands the most attention. He characterizes the aspirations of his time as those of "commerce," where "philanthropists, labor reformers, and men of fashion" seek a life of comfort and safety without danger. Holmes argues that the "divine folly of honor" is superior to a world where everyone is merely "well fed and well housed."

### The Definition of "The Soldier’s Faith"
The core of Holmes's address is the "faith" that leads a soldier to "throw away his life in obedience to a blindly accepted duty." This faith does not require a full understanding of the cause, the campaign, or the tactics. Instead, it is an "unspeakable somewhat" that allows a person to face annihilation for a "blind belief." Holmes suggests that this willingness to die for something beyond oneself is the highest form of human faith.

### The Inevitability of Struggle
Holmes rejects the idea that suffering and war can—or should—be entirely eliminated. He refers to the "struggle for life" as the "order of the world." While he acknowledges that science has shaken established religion and reduced the world to a "network of vibrations," he maintains that "combat and pain still are the portion of man." He argues that humans need "high and dangerous action" to learn to believe in things that the "doubting minds" of civil life cannot prove.

### War as the Business of Youth
Holmes draws a distinction between the aging veterans in his audience (the "white shadows") and the young men who must take up the mantle. He admits that when he was young, he thought soldiers were old men, but realized in the midst of the Civil War that the army is made up of the very young. He asserts that "war is the business of youth and early middle age," calling on the new generation to be ready for the "bugles" and the "Song of the Sword."

### Acceptance and Oblivion
The conclusion of the speech shifts from the "war song" to a "song of the sword in its scabbard." Holmes speaks of the "soldier’s last word": a contented acceptance of silence and being forgotten. He uses the imagery of a soldier buried on a battlefield who is "content" even when told that the living have forgotten him, symbolizing the ultimate sacrifice where the duty itself—not the recognition—is the reward.

## Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Catholic Acceptance** | Universal or all-embracing acceptance (from the Greek *katholikos*). |
| **Cosmopolitanism** | Used dismissively by Holmes to mean a "rootless self-seeking" search for enjoyment at the least cost, replacing love of country. |
| **The Faith** | Holmes’s central claim: the willingness to die for a cause one does not fully understand as a representation of the highest human faith. |
| **Heidelberg** | A German university city known for student dueling fraternities where facial scars were marks of honor. |
| **Repine** | To feel or express discontent or dejection; to complain. |
| **Spherical Case-Shot** | A type of artillery ammunition (shrapnel) that exploded in the air to scatter metal balls. |
| **The 20th Massachusetts** | Known as the "Harvard Regiment," the Civil War unit in which Holmes served. |

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. **According to Holmes, why do the "poor and rich" alike sometimes view love of country as an "old wives’ tale"?**
   *   *Answer:* They replace it with interest in labor unions or "cosmopolitanism," seeking the most enjoyment for the least cost.

2. **How does Holmes describe the impact of science on the modern worldview?**
   *   *Answer:* He suggests science has shaken established religion and resolved the "thrilling world" into an "aimless web" of vibrations and the "pale irony of the void."

3. **What does Holmes mean by the phrase "the divine folly of honor"?**
   *   *Answer:* It refers to the pursuit of ideals and honor that may seem "senseless" or unachievable to a purely commercial or practical society, yet give life meaning.

4. **Why does Holmes "rejoice at every dangerous sport"?**
   *   *Answer:* Because he believes "high and dangerous action" breeds a race fit for "headship and command" and teaches faith in the worth of heroism.

5. **What was the "incommunicable experience" shared by Holmes and his fellow veterans?**
   *   *Answer:* The experience of war and having felt "the passion of life to its top."

6. **What is the significance of the "Song of the Sword" in the speech?**
   *   *Answer:* It represents the martial spirit—the "War-Thing"—which Holmes identifies as the "Will of God" and the "Father of Honor."

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. **The Ethics of Blind Obedience:** Holmes defines the "soldier's faith" as a willingness to die for a cause the soldier "little understands" and for tactics "of which he does not see the use." Analyze the moral implications of this argument. Is Holmes advocating for a dangerous lack of critical thinking, or is he describing a necessary psychological state for the preservation of a society?

2. **The Critique of Materialism:** Compare Holmes's description of a world focused on "commerce" and "comfort" with his description of the "soldier’s life." How does he use the figure of the soldier to critique what he perceives as the "individualist negations" and "wallowing ease" of 1895 American society?

3. **The Role of Tradition and Memory:** Holmes laments that "we do not save our traditions in this country," noting how regiments and flags vanished after the war. Discuss his views on how the lessons of the past should be transmitted to the "new generation." How does he reconcile the need for tradition with his final point that "the dead must be forgot"?

4. **Rhetorical Mastery:** Holmes is described as a "masterful writer" whose rhetoric can make "debatable ideas sound self-evident." Select one of the "powerful" or "troubling" passages you identified and analyze how Holmes uses rhythm, cultural references (such as *Beowulf* or Tennyson), and personal anecdote to persuade his audience of his worldview.
