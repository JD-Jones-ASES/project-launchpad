Study Guide: The Transformation of Smedley Butler and the Origins of War Is a Racket

This study guide examines the historical context, pivotal events, and ideological shifts that led Major General Smedley Butler to transition from a celebrated military leader to a vocal antiwar critic. It focuses on the economic and political turmoil of the early 1930s, specifically the Bonus Army incident and the alleged Business Plot.

Key Concepts and Historical Context

The Post-War Economic Climate

Following his retirement from the Marine Corps in 1931, Smedley Butler returned to an America gripped by the Great Depression. The era was defined by:

  • Economic Collapse: Widespread bank failures, idle factories, and millions of unemployed citizens.
  • Veteran Unrest: World War I veterans faced extreme poverty while waiting for government-promised compensation.

The Bonus Army (1932)

The World War Adjusted Compensation Act of 1924 had granted veterans certificates redeemable for a cash bonus, but these were not scheduled for payout until 1945. In 1932, the "Bonus Expeditionary Force"—tens of thousands of veterans and their families—marched on Washington, D.C., to demand early payment.

Event Aspect Details
Encampment A vast network of shacks and tents located within sight of the Capitol.
Government Response President Herbert Hoover ordered the U.S. Army to clear the camp on July 28, 1932.
Military Leadership Led by General Douglas MacArthur, assisted by Major Dwight Eisenhower and Major George Patton.
Tactics Used Infantry, cavalry, and six tanks used tear gas, bayonets, and fire to destroy the camp.
Casualties Two veterans shot and killed; one infant died from tear gas; hundreds injured.

The Business Plot (1933–1934)

In 1933, Butler was approached by wealthy businessmen and Wall Street financiers with a proposal for a "fascist-style" coup.

  • The Objective: To overthrow President Franklin D. Roosevelt and install a new government.
  • The Strategy: The conspirators wanted Butler to use his popularity to mobilize 500,000 veterans into a private army to seize control of Washington.
  • The Outcome: Butler reported the plot to Congress. The McCormack-Dickstein Committee investigated and found his testimony credible, though no prosecutions occurred, and major media outlets largely dismissed the story.

The Rise of Fascism

The Business Plot occurred during a time when fascism was viewed by some as a viable and efficient system. Mussolini had ruled Italy since 1922, and Hitler took power in Germany in 1933. Some American and British business interests admired fascist hostility toward labor unions and communism.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. What was the primary grievance of the Bonus Expeditionary Force?

Answer: They were demanding the early payment of cash bonuses promised to them by the World War Adjusted Compensation Act of 1924, which were not originally scheduled to be paid until 1945.

2. Which future famous American generals were involved in the forced removal of the Bonus Army?

Answer: General Douglas MacArthur led the operation, with Major Dwight Eisenhower serving as his aide and Major George Patton commanding the cavalry.

3. How did Smedley Butler respond to the Bonus Marchers while they were camped in Washington?

Answer: Butler visited the camp and spoke publicly in their support, identifying with them by stating, "well, you can tell them I am a radical, too."

4. Why did the conspirators of the Business Plot specifically target Smedley Butler to lead their movement?

Answer: They chose him because of his fame and his significant influence among veterans, believing he could mobilize a private army of 500,000 men.

5. What were the defining characteristics of a "pamphlet" as a literary genre in political history?

Answer: A pamphlet is a short, unbound publication (typically under 100 pages) devoted to a single cause. It prioritizes brevity, directness, and urgency over scholarly nuance or balance.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Radicalization of a Patriot: Analyze how the events of 1932 and 1933—specifically the military's treatment of the Bonus Army and the Business Plot—shattered Smedley Butler’s faith in the relationship between the government, the military, and big business.

  2. The Context of Fascism in the 1930s: Discuss why the idea of a "fascist-style government" was not considered far-fetched in 1933. Use the global political climate and the domestic interests of the American business class to support your argument.

  3. Tone and Intent in War Is a Racket: Butler's writing is described as being "written in anger" and "bearing witness." Explore how his personal experiences as a "celebrated warrior" inform the forceful and unapologetic tone of his 1935 pamphlet.

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Bonus Expeditionary Force: A group of tens of thousands of World War I veterans and their families who marched on Washington in 1932 to demand early payment of military bonuses.
  • The Business Plot: Also known as the "Wall Street Putsch," it was an alleged 1933 conspiracy by wealthy financiers to overthrow President Franklin Roosevelt and install a fascist government.
  • McCormack-Dickstein Committee: The congressional committee that investigated Smedley Butler’s allegations regarding the Business Plot and confirmed his testimony was credible.
  • Pamphlet: A short, unbound publication used for political rhetoric; examples include Thomas Paine’s Common Sense and Butler’s War Is a Racket.
  • World War Adjusted Compensation Act (1924): Legislation that granted veterans certificates for a cash bonus that was deferred for 21 years (until 1945), contributing to veteran resentment during the Great Depression.
