The Rhetoric Examined

If Oliver Wendell Holmes writes like a Harvard professor — long sentences, classical allusions, elevated diction — then Smedley Butler writes like a Marine sergeant addressing his troops: short sentences, plain words, and a tone that dares you to disagree. The difference is not accidental. Butler's rhetorical choices are as deliberate as Holmes', and they serve his argument just as effectively. This section examines those choices through the classical framework of ethos, logos, and pathos.

The Vernacular Voice

Vernacular refers to the ordinary, everyday language of common people, as opposed to the formal, elevated language of academic prose. A writer who uses the vernacular adopts the speech patterns, vocabulary, and rhythms of ordinary conversation. Butler's vernacular style is a rhetorical choice: it signals that he is speaking as a soldier and citizen, not as an intellectual or a statesman.

Consider his opening: "WAR is a racket. It always has been." Three short sentences. No qualifications. No subordinate clauses. No literary allusions. Butler states his thesis in six words and then hammers it with three parallel superlatives — oldest, most profitable, most vicious. The effect is like a fist on a table.

This contrasts sharply with Holmes' ornate, periodic sentences that build toward meaning through winding clauses. Holmes spoke to Harvard gentlemen. Butler speaks to everyone. His simplicity is strategic, not a limitation — plain language is harder to evade than elaborate prose.

Ethos: The Soldier's Authority

Butler's most powerful rhetorical tool is himself. His ethos — his credibility — does not come from education, social standing, or eloquence. It comes from thirty-three years of combat service and two Medals of Honor. When Butler says that war is a racket, the reader cannot dismiss him as a coward, a shirker, or someone who doesn't understand war. He understands better than almost anyone alive.

Butler makes this authority explicit: "For a great many years, as a soldier, I had a suspicion that war was a racket; not until I retired to civil life did I fully realize it." This is a confession as much as an accusation. Butler admits he was part of the machine he now condemns. A critic who has never participated in the thing he criticizes can be dismissed as an outsider. A critic who confesses his own complicity is much harder to ignore.

Logos: The Power of Specific Numbers

Butler's most distinctive rhetorical technique is his use of concrete financial data. Where Holmes appeals to abstract ideals — courage, faith, sacrifice — Butler appeals to ledger books. He names companies. He cites profit figures. He calculates percentage increases. This is logos in its most empirical form.

The rhetorical effect is to shift the burden of proof. Once Butler has laid out Du Pont's tenfold profit increase or the appearance of 21,000 new millionaires, anyone who wishes to defend the war must explain those figures away. The numbers do not lie. The numbers do not flatter. The numbers simply are — and they create a presumption that Butler's thesis is correct.

Pathos: The Rhetoric of Outrage

Butler does not rely on numbers alone. His pamphlet is driven by a furious moral energy that surfaces in his rhetorical questions, his catalogs of suffering, and his barely contained contempt for men who profit from war. The central technique is anaphora — the repetition of "How many of them..." at the beginning of six successive questions:

How many of these war millionaires shouldered a rifle? How many of them dug a trench? How many of them knew what it meant to go hungry in a rat-infested dug-out?...

This technique appears in the Psalms, in Cicero, in Lincoln's speeches. Butler uses it to create a drumbeat of accusation, each question driving the point deeper. Notice too the physical specificity: "shouldered a rifle," "dug a trench," "ducked shells," "parried a bayonet." These are not abstractions. They are bodily experiences — mud, hunger, fear, steel. Butler's pathos works because it is rooted in physical reality, not sentiment.

The Pamphlet as Genre

Butler chose the pamphlet form deliberately. A pamphlet must be brief, readable by ordinary people, and build toward a clear call to action. The tradition runs from Thomas Paine's Common Sense (1776) through the Federalist Papers to Butler's own work. The form signals Butler's intentions: he was not writing for scholars or policy experts. He was writing for the ordinary Americans whose sons would be called upon to fight the next war. The pamphlet's brevity is itself an argument — the truth about war, Butler implies, is not complicated. It does not require hundreds of pages.

The Holmes-Butler Contrast

Holmes and Butler draw their authority from the same source — personal military experience. Both appeal to their audiences' sense of what is right. But they arrive at diametrically opposed conclusions. Holmes sees in war the highest expression of human virtue. Butler sees in war the lowest expression of human greed. This contrast is the central tension of the entire debate between war's defenders and its critics.

Key Terms

  • Vernacular — Ordinary, everyday language as opposed to formal academic prose.
  • Ethos — Credibility; for Butler, derived from 33 years of combat service.
  • Logos — Appeal to reason and evidence; Butler uses documented profit data.
  • Pathos — Appeal to emotion; Butler uses physical specificity and anaphora.
  • Anaphora — The repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses to create rhythmic emphasis.
  • Superlative — The grammatical form expressing the highest degree (oldest, most profitable, most vicious).
