Study Guide: Smedley Butler and the Origins of War Is a Racket

This study guide examines the transformation of Smedley Butler from a decorated Marine Corps general to an outspoken antiwar critic. It details the social, economic, and political upheavals of the early 1930s that shaped his worldview and led to the publication of his 1935 pamphlet, War Is a Racket.

Key Historical Themes and Events

The Post-War Economic Climate

After retiring in 1931, Butler returned to a nation crippled by the Great Depression. The economic collapse was characterized by:

  • Widespread Poverty: Millions were unemployed, banks had failed, and industrial production had ceased.
  • Social Unrest: The contrast between the struggling working class and wealthy interests became a central point of tension in American life.

The Bonus Army Crisis (1932)

The World War Adjusted Compensation Act of 1924 had promised World War I veterans a cash bonus, but scheduled the payment for 1945. By 1932, desperate veterans formed the "Bonus Expeditionary Force" to demand early payment.

  • The Encampment: Thousands of veterans and their families set up a camp of tents and shacks near the Capitol in Washington, D.C.
  • The Military Response: President Herbert Hoover ordered the U.S. Army to clear the camp. On July 28, 1932, General Douglas MacArthur—assisted by Dwight Eisenhower and George Patton—led a force of infantry, cavalry, and six tanks against the unarmed veterans.
  • The Outcome: The Army used tear gas and bayonets, burning the camp to the ground. Casualties included two veterans shot dead and an infant who died from tear gas exposure.
  • Butler’s Involvement: Butler visited the camp and publicly supported the marchers, identifying himself as a "radical" in solidarity with the veterans he had once commanded.

The Business Plot (1933)

In 1933, Butler was approached by wealthy financiers and businessmen with a proposal to lead a coup against President Franklin Roosevelt.

  • The Objective: To install a fascist-style government in the United States.
  • The Strategy: The conspirators wanted Butler to use his popularity to mobilize 500,000 veterans into a private army to seize control of Washington.
  • The Investigation: Butler reported the plot to Congress. The McCormack-Dickstein Committee investigated his claims and found them credible, though no prosecutions followed, and the story was largely dismissed by the press at the time.

Global Context: The Rise of Fascism

The Business Plot occurred while fascism was ascending in Europe. Mussolini had ruled Italy since 1922, and Hitler took power in Germany in 1933. Many Western businessmen admired these regimes for their "efficiency" and their aggressive opposition to labor unions and communism.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. What was the primary grievance of the Bonus Expeditionary Force? The veterans were demanding early payment of cash bonuses promised to them by the World War Adjusted Compensation Act of 1924, which were not scheduled to be paid until 1945.

2. Which prominent military figures were involved in the dispersal of the Bonus Army? General Douglas MacArthur led the operation, with Major Dwight Eisenhower serving as his aide and Major George Patton commanding the cavalry.

3. Why did the "Business Plot" conspirators specifically target Smedley Butler to lead their movement? They chose him because of his immense fame and his influence among veterans, believing he could successfully mobilize a private army of 500,000 men.

4. How did the McCormack-Dickstein Committee respond to Butler’s testimony? The committee investigated the claims and confirmed in its final report that Butler’s testimony regarding the plot was credible, although no individuals were prosecuted.

5. How does the text describe the tone and length of the pamphlet War Is a Racket? The pamphlet is described as short (barely fifty pages) and written in anger. It avoids nuance and scholarly qualification in favor of a direct, forceful style intended to name crimes and criminals.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Radicalization of Smedley Butler Analyze the specific events between 1931 and 1935 that caused Butler’s transition from a "celebrated warrior" to an "antiwar crusader." How did the government’s treatment of veterans and the Business Plot influence his perception of the relationship between the military and big business?

2. The Influence of the Great Depression on Political Stability Discuss how the economic desperation of the 1930s created an environment where both the Bonus Army march and the Business Plot could occur. How did the domestic economic crisis reshape the way the military was used within American borders?

3. Media and Political Response to Domestic Threats Compare the public and institutional reactions to the Bonus Army and the Business Plot. Why might the Business Plot have been "buried" by major newspapers and the government despite a congressional committee finding the testimony credible?

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
World War Adjusted Compensation Act (1924) Legislation that granted World War I veterans certificates for a cash bonus, originally redeemable in 1945.
Bonus Expeditionary Force A group of tens of thousands of WWI veterans and their families who marched on Washington in 1932 to demand early bonus payments.
The Business Plot Also known as the Wall Street Putsch; an alleged 1933 conspiracy by wealthy businessmen to overthrow FDR and install a fascist government.
McCormack-Dickstein Committee The congressional committee that investigated Butler’s allegations regarding the Business Plot and confirmed their credibility.
Pamphlet A short, unbound publication (typically under 100 pages) focused on a single argument or cause; characterized by brevity and urgency.
Fascism A political ideology that gained prominence in the 1920s and 30s (notably under Mussolini and Hitler) characterized by authoritarianism and hostility to labor unions and communism.
