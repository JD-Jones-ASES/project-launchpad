Study Guide: Talking Past Each Other—Perspectives on War

This study guide explores the rhetorical dynamics of the debate surrounding war as presented in "At the Table: Where the Arguments Meet." It focuses on how different thinkers—Holmes, Butler, Wilson, and Remarque—approach the subject of war, the specific questions they address, and the critical information they omit.

Core Concept: Talking Past Each Other

In rhetorical analysis, "talking past each other" occurs when participants in a debate appear to be engaging one another but are actually addressing different questions, utilizing different assumptions, or operating within entirely different frameworks. Instead of a direct exchange of ideas, the result is a series of "parallel monologues" that fail to confront the opposition's strongest points.

Comparative Analysis of Arguments

The following table summarizes the focus of each writer and the specific "blind spots" or omissions within their arguments.

Writer Primary Focus / Question Key Omission (The Blind Spot)
Holmes Virtue: What does war reveal about the human spirit (courage, sacrifice, faith)? Beneficiaries: Fails to ask who benefits from sacrifice or if the cause is worthy of the suffering.
Butler Economics: Who profits from war, and who pays? Necessity: Fails to consider wars fought for genuine necessity, such as self-defense or stopping genocide.
Wilson Morality: What moral obligations justify the use of force for democracy? Finance: Completely ignores the economic dimension, including bank loans, trade, and industrial profits.
Remarque Experience: What does war actually feel like on a physical level? Justification: Refuses to engage in any philosophical or political argument for or against the war.

Detailed Perspectives and Critiques

Oliver Wendell Holmes

Holmes treats war as a "philosophical abstraction," viewing it as a forge for character and virtue. He insists that the reality of the soldier's courage is sufficient justification in itself.

  • The Critique: By focusing purely on the soldier’s internal character, Holmes ignores the "political and economic machinery" that creates wars. He fails to address whether courage exercised in the service of an exploitative "racket" can truly be called virtue.

Smedley Butler

Butler provides a precise critique of the economic motives behind conflict, famously asserting that "war is a racket."

  • The Critique: Butler’s framework is absolute; it cannot admit exceptions. By claiming profit is the only reason for war, he cannot account for provocations (like the Zimmermann Telegram) or the need to respond to genuine existential threats.

Woodrow Wilson

Wilson frames war as a "moral crusade" for self-determination and democracy.

  • The Critique: Wilson’s rhetoric is notable for its rhetorical omission. He makes no mention of the billions of dollars in loans or the financial catastrophe that would hit American interests if the Allies lost. Whether this was a deliberate choice to preserve "moral purity" or a genuine belief in its irrelevance, the omission leaves his argument incomplete.

Erich Maria Remarque

Remarque offers "testimony" rather than an argument. By describing the physical reality of gas attacks, shell craters, and corpses, he utilizes the argument from silence.

  • The Critique: Remarque’s refusal to argue is a challenge to all other speakers. He forces the others to reconcile their abstractions (virtue, democracy, profit) with the visceral reality of a nineteen-year-old soldier dying in the mud.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Define "Talking Past Each Other" as used in the text.
  2. What does the text identify as Holmes’s primary blind spot regarding the soldiers at Antietam?
  3. According to the text, why does Butler’s thesis—"war is always a racket"—potentially collapse if he admits exceptions?
  4. What specific economic factors did Woodrow Wilson omit from his address to Congress?
  5. How does Remarque’s novel act as a challenge to the arguments made by Wilson and Holmes?
  6. Explain the concept of "Rhetorical Omission" and provide an example from the source.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Ethics of Abstraction: Analyze how Holmes and Wilson use abstract concepts (virtue and democracy) to justify war. Contrast this with Remarque’s focus on physical reality. Does the use of abstraction strengthen a rhetorical case for war, or does it merely serve to hide uncomfortable truths?
  2. The Role of the Profit Motive: Evaluate the conflict between Butler’s economic analysis and Wilson’s moral crusade. Can a war be "morally necessary" if it also serves the interests of profiteers? Use the source text to explain how these two writers fail to engage with each other's perspectives.
  3. The Power of Silence: Discuss the "argument from silence" as demonstrated by Remarque. Why might a refusal to engage in traditional argumentation be a more powerful rhetorical tool than a direct rebuttal?

Glossary of Key Terms

  • Argument from Silence: A rhetorical technique where a speaker/writer refuses to engage with the standard justifications of an opponent, instead focusing on a specific reality (such as physical experience) to force the opponent to confront a different perspective.
  • Internal Coherence: A state where an argument makes sense within its own framework and answers its own questions effectively, even if it ignores external facts or opposing views.
  • Parallel Monologues: A characteristic of "talking past each other" where debaters speak at the same time but never truly engage with the substance of the other's claims.
  • Rhetorical Omission: The deliberate exclusion of relevant information from an argument, often used to avoid complicating a moral appeal or weakening a speaker's position.
  • Talking Past Each Other: A situation where debaters address different questions or use different assumptions, preventing a genuine engagement with the opposing side's strongest points.
  • Testimony: A form of expression that focuses on describing personal experience and physical reality rather than constructing a formal logical or political argument.
