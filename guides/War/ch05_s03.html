Talking Past Each Other

One of the most important rhetorical skills is not just recognizing what a speaker says, but recognizing what a speaker refuses to say. Every argument has blind spots — questions it does not ask, objections it does not address, evidence it does not consider. When Holmes, Butler, Wilson, and Remarque are placed side by side, the most revealing thing is not where they disagree but where they fail to engage each other's strongest points at all.

Definition: Talking Past Each Other

Talking past each other: A situation in which two or more people appear to be debating but are actually addressing different questions, using different assumptions, or operating within different frameworks. They deliver parallel monologues rather than genuinely engaging each other's arguments.

This is not a sign of poor thinking. It is a feature of all serious debate in which the deepest disagreements are about what matters most, not about facts. Each of these writers answers his own question brilliantly — the problem is that they are not all answering the same question.

What Holmes Ignores

Holmes celebrates the virtues of the soldier — courage, sacrifice, faith in something larger than oneself. What he never asks is: who benefits from that sacrifice? Holmes treats war as a philosophical abstraction — a test of character, a forge of virtue — without examining the political and economic machinery that produces wars. He does not ask whether the soldiers at Antietam died for a cause worthy of their courage, or whether their suffering served someone else's interests.

Butler's rejoinder: Courage exercised in the service of a racket is still courage — but calling it virtue without asking whose purposes it served is, at best, incomplete and, at worst, a way of making exploitation look noble.

What Butler Ignores

Butler exposes the economic machinery behind war with devastating precision. What he never seriously considers is the possibility that some wars might be genuinely necessary regardless of who profits. Butler writes as though the profit motive is the only reason nations go to war — and once you have identified the profiteers, you have fully explained the war.

But his framework has no room for: a war fought against an aggressor who genuinely threatens your homeland; a war fought to stop a genocide. Butler's thesis — "war is always a racket" — cannot admit exceptions without collapsing. This is the critical weakness that the Second World War would later expose.

What Wilson Ignores

Wilson frames the war as a moral crusade for democracy and self-determination. What he never addresses is the economic dimension. His address to Congress does not mention money. Not once — not a single reference to loans, profits, trade, or the financial interests that would be served by American intervention.

This omission is itself a rhetorical choice. Wilson may have omitted it because he genuinely believed it was irrelevant to the moral case. Or he may have omitted it because mentioning it would have undermined the purity of his moral appeal. Either way, a complete argument for war would need to address Butler's question — "who profits?" — even if only to explain why the profit motive does not invalidate the moral case.

What Remarque Refuses to Do

Remarque does not argue at all. He does not claim war is noble or corrupt, necessary or profitable. He simply describes what it is like to be in a trench. His novel is not an argument — it is testimony.

Yet Remarque's refusal to argue is itself a kind of argument: the argument from silence. By stripping away all justification and leaving only the physical reality of combat, Remarque implicitly challenges every other voice:

  • Holmes says war builds character. Remarque shows what war builds: shell craters and corpses.
  • Wilson says war defends democracy. Remarque shows what war defends: a few hundred yards of mud.
  • Butler says war enriches the few. Remarque does not care who profits — he cares about who dies.

Four Questions, One War

The four writers are not all asking the same question about war:

Writer Core Question
Holmes What does war reveal about the human spirit?
Butler Who profits from war, and who pays?
Wilson What moral obligations justify the use of force?
Remarque What does war actually feel like?

A complete reckoning with war would need to answer all four questions. No single writer in this book does that — and recognizing this is not a criticism of any one writer. It is a recognition that "What is war for?" is too large and too many-sided for any single argument to contain. That is precisely why all four voices belong at the table.
