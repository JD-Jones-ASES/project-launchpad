Study Guide: The Philosophical Clash — Perspectives on War

This study guide explores the philosophical disagreement regarding the nature and impact of war, focusing on the opposing viewpoints of Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. and Smedley Butler, with contextual reference to Woodrow Wilson and Erich Maria Remarque.

Core Philosophical Perspectives

Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr.: War as a Forge of Character

Holmes argues that war acts as a "crucible" for the human soul. His perspective is rooted in the belief that the hardships of battle produce moral qualities that a purely commercial or peaceful society cannot foster.

  • The Individual Experience: Holmes focuses on the internal transformation of the soldier.
  • Key Virtues: War cultivates courage, self-sacrifice, discipline, and "faith without certainty."
  • Societal Critique: Holmes believed that the Gilded Age's focus on profit and material comfort caused "moral muscles" to atrophy. He argued that civilization needs the pressure of war to remain worth defending.
  • Personal Ethos: Holmes’ authority stems from his direct experience as a soldier in the Civil War, having been wounded at Antietam and Ball’s Bluff.

Smedley Butler: War as a Machine of Destruction

Butler views war through a systemic lens, characterizing it as a "racket." While he acknowledges the bravery of individual soldiers, he argues that this bravery is exploited for the benefit of others.

  • The Systemic Function: Butler focuses on the economic and political machinery behind the conflict.
  • The Profit Motive: He argues that war primarily serves a small class of profiteers—corporations selling ammunition, uniforms, and goods—rather than the soldiers or the public interest.
  • The Human Cost: Beyond physical death, Butler emphasizes that war destroys minds and families while the beneficiaries remain safe from combat.
  • Personal Ethos: Butler was a career Marine and a two-time Medal of Honor recipient, giving him the authority of a high-ranking veteran who concluded the enterprise of war was fundamentally a "con."

Comparative Analysis: Holmes vs. Butler

The following table summarizes the primary points of contention between the two philosophical stances.

Primary Metaphor The Crucible (a test that transforms) The Racket (a scheme for profit)

Key Concepts and Synthesis

The "Question of Scale"

The disagreement between Holmes and Butler may be a matter of focus rather than a direct contradiction of facts.

  • Individual Scale: On the level of the individual, Holmes’ observations on courage and sacrifice can be true.
  • Systemic Scale: On the level of the institution, Butler’s observations on economic exploitation can also be true.

The Central Philosophical Tension

The text highlights a critical tension: Does celebrating the individual virtue of the soldier (as Holmes does) distract society from questioning the systemic corruption of the war itself (as Butler demands)? This tension is most intense in war because the stakes involve life and death rather than mere wages or working conditions.

Short-Answer Practice Quiz

  1. Who is the author of All Quiet on the Western Front, and what was his primary observation about war?
  2. How did Woodrow Wilson’s view on war differ from those of Holmes and Butler?
  3. Define the term "crucible" as Holmes used it to describe war.
  4. According to Holmes, what happens to a civilization that focuses solely on profit and material comfort?
  5. What specific military experiences provided Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. with his "ethos"?
  6. What did Smedley Butler identify as the "real beneficiaries" of war?
  7. How many Medals of Honor did Smedley Butler earn?
  8. In Butler’s view, what is the relationship between a soldier's courage and the economic interests of corporations?
  9. According to the text, why is the tension between individual virtue and systemic exploitation more "intense" in war than in other industries?

Essay Questions for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Authority of Experience: Both Holmes and Butler are highly decorated veterans who saw significant combat. Analyze how their identical "ethos" led them to such divergent conclusions. Is one man's experience more "valid" than the other's?
  2. The Softness of Peace: Critique Holmes’ argument that a peaceful, commercial society leads to moral atrophy. Is war the only "crucible" capable of fostering courage and self-sacrifice, or can these virtues be cultivated through other means?
  3. The Distraction of Virtue: Explore the synthesis provided in the text regarding the "Question of Scale." Discuss whether it is possible to honor the individual soldier’s courage (the Holmesian view) without inadvertently validating a corrupt system (the Butlerian view).
  4. The Racket vs. The Forge: Using the provided text, argue which lens—the individual/philosophical or the systemic/economic—is more critical for a society to adopt when deciding whether to go to war.

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Crucible: Literally, a container for melting metals at high heat; figuratively, a severe trial or test that transforms or purifies whatever passes through it.
  • Ethos: The characteristic spirit of a culture, era, or community; in rhetoric, it refers to the personal authority or credibility of a speaker.
  • Pacifist: A person who believes that war and violence are unjustifiable and refuses to participate in or support armed conflict.
  • Profiteer: A person or entity that makes an excessive or unfair profit, particularly during a time of national difficulty such as a war.
  • Racket: A fraudulent scheme or business, often one that involves intimidation or the exploitation of others for profit.
