Pathos: Where Does Each Writer Go for Emotion?

If ethos asks Why should I listen to you? then pathos asks Why should I care? Every effective writer reaches for emotion at some point — but each of our four writers reaches for a different emotion, drawn from a different well. Mapping those emotional appeals reveals not just how these writers try to move their audiences, but what each writer believes will motivate people to act.

Holmes: The Beauty of Sacrifice

Holmes does not appeal to pity or fear. His emotional register is elevation — the feeling of being lifted above the ordinary, of touching something noble and transcendent. He uses the language of beauty, faith, and spiritual exaltation. He invites his audience to admire the soldier who gives his life without knowing why — who acts on faith rather than calculation.

The emotion Holmes seeks is reverence: the hushed awe one feels before something sacred. The power of this appeal is real; most people respond to stories of courage and self-sacrifice. But it carries a risk. By making war beautiful, Holmes removes the ugliness — the shattered limbs, the dysentery, the screaming. His pathos is the pathos of the memorial, not the hospital ward: he shows the monument, not the body beneath it.

Butler: The Outrage of Betrayal

Butler's emotional register could not be more different. He seeks outrage — not sadness, not pity, but fury. His emotional architecture is simple: young men are sent to die, their bodies are broken, and while they suffer, a small class of industrialists and bankers grows rich on their suffering.

Butler does describe veterans who return missing limbs and futures. But these images serve a purpose beyond sympathy: they are evidence in an argument about who benefits. Butler's pathos is not an end in itself — it is a tool designed to make the audience so angry that they demand reform. The emotion he seeks is righteous anger. The risk: anger can oversimplify, turning a complex question into a story of pure villainy.

Wilson: The Weight of Duty

Wilson's emotional appeal is the most carefully controlled of the four. He does not celebrate war, and he does not rage against it. He asks his audience to feel the solemnity of a decision they cannot avoid. War, he tells Congress, is "a fearful thing." He does not pretend otherwise. But he insists that the alternative — standing aside while autocracy threatens free peoples — is worse.

Wilson's pathos follows the emotional logic of the tragic hero: the man who does what he must even though it will cause suffering. He wants his audience to feel that entering the war is not a choice made gladly but a burden accepted because honor and justice require it. The emotion he seeks is solemn resolve. The risk: the rhetoric of duty can make war seem inevitable, and it can make dissent feel like cowardice.

Remarque: The Numbness of Exposure

Remarque's emotional strategy is the strangest and, in some ways, the most powerful — because it is not a strategy at all. He does not tell the reader what to feel. He simply shows. A man drowns in mud. A horse screams with its intestines hanging out. A schoolboy bleeds to death in a shell crater.

The emotional effect is not a single nameable feeling like reverence or outrage. It is something closer to shock followed by a deep, inarticulate sadness — empathic horror: the feeling of being inside a nightmare that is not a dream. Remarque strips away every layer of meaning that the other writers impose on war and leaves only the raw experience. The reader is left holding an emotion with no argument attached to it, no policy prescription. Just: this happened, and it was unbearable. The risk: raw experience without argument can leave the reader overwhelmed but without a framework for response.

Evaluating Emotional Appeals

When is an emotional appeal legitimate, and when does it become manipulation? Emotional manipulation occurs when a speaker uses emotional appeals to bypass rational judgment — to make the audience feel so strongly that they stop thinking critically. Not all emotional appeals are manipulative; emotions are a legitimate part of human reasoning.

Three questions can guide evaluation:

  1. Does the emotional appeal accompany evidence or replace it? Butler's descriptions of wounded veterans are evidence for his economic argument. An appeal that produces strong feelings without supporting evidence should raise suspicion.

  2. Does the appeal fairly represent the situation? Holmes' celebration of martial virtue is emotionally compelling, but it omits the physical horror of the battlefield. Is the omission an appropriate focus or a distortion that undermines honest persuasion?

  3. Does the appeal leave room for disagreement? An emotional appeal that makes dissent feel like cowardice or treason is doing more than persuading — it is coercing.

Key Terms

  • Pathos: The rhetorical appeal to emotion; the element of argument that answers "Why should I care?"
  • Elevation: An emotional register aimed at inspiring reverence and aspiration toward something noble.
  • Empathic horror: The emotional state produced by Remarque's unmediated depictions — the feeling of being inside a nightmare.
  • Emotional manipulation: Using pathos to bypass rational judgment, substituting emotion for evidence.
  • Solemn resolve: The emotional state Wilson seeks — acknowledging suffering while insisting duty demands action.
  • Righteous anger: Butler's target emotion — fury at injustice that motivates political action rather than passive sympathy.
