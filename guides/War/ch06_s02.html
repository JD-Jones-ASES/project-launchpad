Ethos: Who Has the Right to Speak?

Every argument begins with a silent question the audience asks the speaker: Why should I listen to you? The answer to that question is ethos — the credibility, character, and authority a speaker brings to the podium. In the debate over war and its meaning, each of our four writers answers that question differently, and those different answers shape everything that follows.

Holmes: The Volunteer

Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. did not have to fight. He was a Harvard man, the son of one of the most famous writers in America. Instead, he enlisted as a lieutenant in the Twentieth Massachusetts Volunteer Infantry and was wounded three times — at Ball's Bluff, Antietam, and Fredericksburg. By 1895 he had carried those scars for thirty years.

This is the foundation of Holmes' ethos: the ethos of the volunteer — the man who walked toward danger when he could have walked away. His authority rests not on theory but on blood. When he speaks of the soldier's faith as "true and adorable," his audience finds the claim far more difficult to dismiss than it would be coming from someone who had never served. His strength: no one can accuse him of cowardice. His weakness: his experience was exceptional, not typical.

Butler: The Whistle-Blower

Smedley Butler's ethos operates on a different principle. Where Holmes is the volunteer who served and then reflected, Butler is the career professional who served and then rebelled. He fought in the Philippines, China, Nicaragua, Haiti, Mexico, and France — one of the most decorated warriors in American history. And then he called the entire enterprise a racket.

This is the ethos of the insider — the man who knows the institution from the inside and declares it corrupt. The power lies in the reversal: audiences expect a decorated general to defend the system that decorated him. When he attacks it instead, the surprise lends his words enormous force. His strength: he knows the system from within. His weakness: critics can ask why he served so long before speaking out.

Wilson: The Statesman

Woodrow Wilson is not a soldier. He is a scholar, a university president, a man of words. His authority does not come from personal experience of combat but from his office — he speaks as President of the United States, with the full weight of that position behind him.

But Wilson adds something distinctive: the ethos of reluctance. He campaigned for re-election on peace. He sought every alternative to war. He comes before Congress not eagerly but with profound moral duty. The rhetorical figure of the reluctant warrior is powerful: if even the man who tried hardest to avoid war concludes that war is necessary, the case must be strong indeed. His strength: the weight of the presidency. His weakness: he has never experienced what he is asking others to endure.

Remarque: The Witness

Remarque was not a volunteer like Holmes. He was drafted at eighteen into the Imperial German Army — an ordinary young man swept up by forces beyond his control, sent to the Western Front, wounded by shrapnel. He did not choose his war.

Does this give him more authority or less? The answer depends on what authority means. If ethos requires choice, Holmes has more. But if ethos requires typicality — the ability to speak for the common experience rather than the exceptional one — then Remarque has an authority that Holmes, Butler, and Wilson all lack. Most soldiers in most wars are not volunteers motivated by philosophy or career professionals seeking advancement. They are young men sent to fight by governments they did not choose. Remarque speaks for them. His is the ethos of the witness — the man who was there and survived to tell the truth. His strength: he speaks for the common soldier. His weakness: testimony alone cannot settle a policy question.

The Ethos Spectrum

No form of ethos is inherently superior to the others. Each carries real weight and real limitations. The critical reader's task is not to decide which authority is "best" but to understand how each writer's authority shapes the argument he is able to make — and prevents him from making.

Figure Ethos Type Strength Weakness
Holmes The Volunteer Cannot be accused of cowardice Experience was exceptional, not typical
Butler The Insider Knows the system from within Why serve so long before speaking out?

Remarque The Witness Speaks for the common soldier Testimony cannot answer policy questions

Key Terms

  • Ethos: The credibility, character, and authority a speaker brings to an argument.
  • Appeal to authority: Drawing persuasive force from credentials, experience, or position rather than logic alone — legitimate when relevant, problematic when it substitutes for evidence.
  • Ethos of the volunteer: Authority from chosen sacrifice.
  • Ethos of the insider: Authority from insider betrayal of an institution.
  • Ethos of the statesman: Authority from positional power combined with reluctance.
  • Ethos of the witness: Authority from representative, ordinary experience.
