Ethos and Authority in War Rhetoric: A Comprehensive Study Guide

This study guide examines the concept of ethos—the credibility, character, and authority of a speaker—as explored in "Rhetoric Under the Microscope." It focuses on four distinct types of authority presented by figures who have addressed the nature of war: Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr., Smedley Butler, Woodrow Wilson, and Erich Maria Remarque.

Key Concepts and Thematic Analysis

The Nature of Ethos

Every argument begins with a silent question from the audience: "Why should I listen to you?" Ethos is the answer to that question. It is not merely a list of credentials but the specific type of character a speaker brings to their argument, which shapes how their message is received.

Profiles in Authority

Figure Ethos Type Source of Authority Primary Rhetorical Strength
Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. The Volunteer Personal sacrifice and chosen service. Difficult to dismiss; he paid for his "soldier's faith" in blood.
Smedley Butler The Insider Professional expertise and subsequent betrayal of the system. The shock of a highly decorated general calling the system a "racket."
Woodrow Wilson The Statesman Positional power and moral reluctance. The weight of the Presidency; the "reluctant warrior" persona.
Erich Maria Remarque The Witness Representativeness and typicality of experience. Speaks for the millions of ordinary soldiers who lacked a voice.

1. Holmes: The Volunteer Who Bled

Holmes’ authority is rooted in chosen sacrifice. Despite being a "Harvard man" from a famous family who could have avoided combat, he enlisted and was wounded three times (at Ball's Bluff, Antietam, and Fredericksburg). His ethos is built on the fact that he is not a theorist; he has felt the physical consequences of war.

  • Limitation: Because his choice and background were exceptional, he may not represent the typical experience of a soldier.

2. Butler: The Professional Who Turned

Butler’s authority is the ethos of the whistle-blower. As a career Marine with two Medals of Honor and experience on four continents, he was the ultimate insider. When he turned against the institution of war, calling it a "racket," his words gained force through the reversal of audience expectations.

  • Limitation: Critics can question why he served for over three decades before deciding the system was corrupt.

3. Wilson: The Commander Who Agonized

Wilson’s authority is positional. As a scholar and politician, he lacked combat experience but commanded the weight of the Presidency. He utilized the ethos of reluctance, reminding the public that he campaigned on the slogan "He Kept Us Out of War." By framing the declaration of war as a painful moral duty, he made the necessity of the conflict seem more absolute.

  • Limitation: He asked others to endure experiences he never faced himself.

4. Remarque: The Conscript Who Witnessed

Remarque’s authority is based on ordinariness. Unlike the volunteer (Holmes) or the general (Butler), Remarque was an eighteen-year-old conscript swept up by forces beyond his control. He represents the common soldier who fought for reasons they barely understood.

  • Limitation: He offers testimony rather than policy arguments; witness alone cannot settle political questions.

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Appeal to Authority: A rhetorical argument that draws persuasive force from the speaker's credentials, experience, or position rather than from logic or evidence alone.
  • Conscript: An individual who is drafted into military service by the government rather than volunteering.
  • Ethos: The credibility, character, and authority a speaker establishes to convince an audience they are worth listening to.
  • Positional Authority: Credibility derived from the office or rank one holds (e.g., the Presidency) rather than personal experience.
  • Reluctant Warrior: A rhetorical figure who emphasizes their desire for peace to make their eventual call for war seem more morally justified and necessary.
  • Representativeness: The quality of an experience being typical of a large group, used by the "witness" to speak for the masses.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Where was Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. wounded during the Civil War?
    • Answer: He was wounded at Ball's Bluff (chest), Antietam (neck), and Fredericksburg (heel).
  2. What distinction does Smedley Butler hold that marks him as one of the most accomplished warriors in American history?
    • Answer: He received the Medal of Honor twice, a distinction held by only a handful of Marines.
  3. What slogan did Woodrow Wilson use in his 1916 re-election campaign?
    • Answer: "He Kept Us Out of War."
  4. According to the text, what is the primary difference between the ethos of Holmes and the ethos of Remarque?
    • Answer: Holmes’ ethos is based on choice and exceptional sacrifice (the Volunteer), while Remarque’s ethos is based on typicality and involuntary service (the Conscript/Witness).
  5. When does an "Appeal to Authority" become problematic or fallacious?
    • Answer: When the authority cited is irrelevant to the claim or when the appeal is used as a substitute for evidence that should be provided.
  6. Why is Smedley Butler’s attack on the "racket" of war considered "devastating"?
    • Answer: Because he was the system's most decorated servant; the audience expects him to defend the institution, so his betrayal carries enormous rhetorical force.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Spectrum of Experience: Compare the strengths and weaknesses of "The Volunteer" (Holmes) versus "The Witness" (Remarque). Does the fact that a soldier chose to fight give them more or less authority to speak on the morality of war than a soldier who was forced to fight?
  2. The Power of Reluctance: Analyze Woodrow Wilson’s use of the "reluctant warrior" persona. How does a speaker’s past commitment to peace affect their credibility when they eventually call for conflict? Use the shift from Wilson's 1916 campaign to his 1917 address to Congress as evidence.
  3. The Insider’s Reversal: Discuss the rhetorical impact of Smedley Butler’s transition from a highly decorated general to a critic of war. Why is the "whistle-blower" ethos uniquely persuasive, and what risks does the speaker take in adopting this stance?
  4. The Ethics of Authority: The text states that "the question is not who has the right to speak, but what each speaker’s particular authority allows—and prevents—him from saying." Choose two figures from the text and explain how their specific ethos limited the types of arguments they could effectively make.
