The Speech in Context

Wilson's decision to ask Congress for a declaration of war did not arrive suddenly. It was the product of nearly three years of escalating crises, failed diplomacy, and mounting pressure. To read the April 2 address intelligently, you need to understand the events that produced it — and the questions those events raise about American "neutrality."

The Submarine Crisis

The war in Europe had stalled into a grinding stalemate on the Western Front by 1915. Germany, unable to break through on land and blockaded at sea by the British Royal Navy, turned to a new weapon: the submarine. U-boats could strangle Britain's supply lines — but only by attacking without warning, which meant killing civilian sailors and passengers.

International law required warships to surface, identify themselves, and allow crews to evacuate before sinking merchant vessels. Submarines could not do this without becoming vulnerable to ramming or gunfire. Germany's policy of unrestricted submarine warfare therefore meant attacking any ship in the waters around Britain without warning — including neutral vessels.

The crisis broke into the open in May 1915, when a German submarine torpedoed the British ocean liner Lusitania off the coast of Ireland, killing 1,198 people, including 128 Americans. Wilson responded with diplomatic protests; Germany backed down temporarily. The Sussex Pledge of May 1916 committed Germany to warning before sinking merchant ships. But in February 1917, Germany's military commanders calculated they could knock Britain out of the war before America could mobilize, and resumed unrestricted attacks. Wilson severed diplomatic relations with Germany on February 3 — but still hesitated to seek a war declaration.

The Question of Even-Handedness

Germany was not the only belligerent interfering with neutral shipping. Britain maintained a naval blockade of Germany that prevented food and raw materials from reaching the civilian population — contributing to widespread hunger and, by some estimates, hundreds of thousands of civilian deaths by the war's end. Wilson protested the British blockade, but his protests were notably milder than his responses to German submarines.

The practical explanation is important: Britain's blockade did not kill American citizens, while German torpedoes did. But the deeper economic reality matters too. By 1917, American banks had lent the Allies over $2 billion, and American industry was supplying Britain and France with war materiel on a vast scale. The British blockade protected that trade; the German submarines threatened it. Critics — including Butler, whose War Is a Racket we will read in Chapter 4 — argued that American "neutrality" was neutral in name only.

The Zimmermann Telegram

In January 1917, German Foreign Secretary Arthur Zimmermann sent a coded telegram to the German ambassador in Mexico: if the United States entered the war against Germany, Mexico should attack the US — and Germany would help Mexico recover Texas, New Mexico, and Arizona. British intelligence intercepted and decoded the telegram and passed it to the American government. When it was published on March 1, 1917, American public opinion shifted dramatically toward war.

The Zimmermann Telegram transformed the debate. Before its publication, the submarine crisis was a maritime dispute — serious, but abstract for most Americans who lived far from the sea lanes. The Telegram made the threat territorial and personal. Germany was not merely sinking ships; it was plotting to invade American soil.

Wilson's Decision

By late March 1917, Wilson had exhausted his alternatives. Diplomacy had failed. Armed neutrality — arming merchant ships while remaining officially neutral — was insufficient: German submarines sank American vessels faster than the policy could protect them. Wilson had asked Congress for authority to arm merchant ships; a Senate filibuster blocked the bill ("a little group of willful men," Wilson called the obstructionists), and he armed the ships by executive order. But American sailors kept dying.

On the evening of April 2, Wilson rode to the Capitol in a light rain, carrying a speech he had typed himself. The galleries were packed. The Supreme Court justices sat in the front row. The members of Congress waited.

Key Dates

  • May 1915: Lusitania sunk — 1,198 dead, 128 Americans
  • May 1916: Sussex Pledge — Germany temporarily restricts submarine attacks
  • November 1916: Wilson re-elected on peace platform
  • January 1917: Zimmermann Telegram sent
  • February 1, 1917: Germany resumes unrestricted submarine warfare
  • February 3, 1917: Wilson severs diplomatic relations with Germany
  • March 1, 1917: Zimmermann Telegram published; opinion shifts dramatically
  • April 2, 1917: Wilson addresses Congress requesting declaration of war

Key Terms

Unrestricted Submarine Warfare: Germany's policy of attacking without warning any ship in the waters around Britain, including neutral vessels, in violation of international maritime law.

Sussex Pledge: Germany's May 1916 commitment to warn merchant ships before attacking — a temporary restriction on submarine warfare, abandoned in February 1917.

Zimmermann Telegram: The coded January 1917 message from Germany's Foreign Secretary proposing a military alliance with Mexico against the United States, offering to help Mexico recover Texas, New Mexico, and Arizona.

Armed Neutrality: Wilson's intermediate policy of arming American merchant ships for self-defense while remaining officially neutral — deemed insufficient by late March 1917.
