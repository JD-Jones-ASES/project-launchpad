Study Guide: Woodrow Wilson and the Argument for War (1917)

This study guide analyzes the rhetorical and moral architecture of Woodrow Wilson’s 1917 address to Congress. It explores the layers of Wilson’s argument for American entry into World War I, the philosophical underpinnings of his vision, and the significant omissions that shaped his case.

Key Concepts and Themes

1. The Necessity of War as a "Grim Duty"

Wilson’s core claim rejects the idea that war is either a virtue or a "racket." Instead, he presents the conflict as a "grim duty" forced upon a peaceful nation. He argues that the actions of the German government—specifically unrestricted submarine warfare—left the United States with no honorable alternative but to respond with force.

2. Democracy vs. Autocracy

Wilson elevates the conflict from a maritime dispute to a universal struggle for the survival of democratic government. He makes a sharp distinction between the German people and their government, asserting that:

  • Autocracies wage aggressive wars because leaders are not accountable to their citizens.
  • Democracies are naturally inclined toward peace because citizens, who bear the cost of war, are less likely to vote for it.

3. Making the World "Safe for Democracy"

Wilson’s primary goal is the establishment of an international order where democratic nations can coexist without the threat of autocratic aggression. This transforms the war from a mission of vengeance for sunken ships into a crusade for a new global system based on freedom and the consent of the governed.

Wilson’s Argument Map

The following table outlines the logical structure Wilson uses to justify American intervention.

Element Description
Claim The United States must enter the war against Germany.
Evidence 1. Resumption of unrestricted submarine warfare (killing citizens/sinking ships).
2. The Zimmermann Telegram revealing German hostility.
3. The inherently aggressive nature of autocratic governments.
Reasoning 1. Neutrality is impossible when a nation is under direct attack.
2. The conflict is a global struggle between democracy and autocracy.
3. America’s founding principles (liberty, equality) impose a duty to defend those principles globally.
Conclusion War is a “fearful thing,” but submitting to autocratic aggression is a worse alternative.

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Democratic Peace Theory: A theory in political science (dating back to Immanuel Kant’s Perpetual Peace) suggesting that republican or democratic governments are less likely to go to war because citizens must personally bear the costs of the conflict.
  • Liberal Democracy: A system of government defined by the rule of law, free elections, the protection of individual rights, and specific limits on the power of the government.
  • Self-Determination: The principle that all nations and peoples have the right to choose their own form of government without external interference.
  • Unrestricted Submarine Warfare: A naval strategy where submarines sink vessels (including neutral and merchant ships) without warning.

What Wilson Omitted

A critical analysis of Wilson’s argument requires looking at the facts and perspectives he chose to leave out:

  • The British Blockade: While Wilson condemned German submarine attacks on neutral rights, he did not acknowledge Britain’s own violations, such as seizing American cargo and preventing food from reaching German civilians.
  • Economic Interests: Wilson framed the war entirely in terms of principle, ignoring the massive financial investments American bankers and corporations had made in an Allied victory.
  • Domestic Inequality: Wilson’s call to defend democracy abroad did not address the lack of democracy at home, specifically the millions of African Americans living under Jim Crow laws.
  • The Cost of War: Unlike some of his contemporaries who celebrated war, Wilson acknowledged its weight but did not promise an easy or cheap victory.
  • Alternatives to Conflict: Wilson presented the situation as a binary choice (fight or submit), ignoring the argument that continued neutrality might save more lives than intervention.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why did Wilson begin his address with a list of facts regarding German submarine warfare?
    • Answer: To establish himself as a reasonable man driven to an extreme position by circumstances rather than emotion or an eagerness for war.
  2. How did Wilson distinguish between the German people and the German government?
    • Answer: He argued the German people did not choose the war; it was forced upon them by autocratic rulers who do not answer to their citizens.
  3. According to the text, why does Wilson believe America has a "special responsibility" to intervene?
    • Answer: Because the U.S. was founded on universal principles like liberty and equality, which impose an obligation to respond whenever democracy is threatened.
  4. What was Wilson’s ultimate goal for the postwar world?
    • Answer: To create an international order where the world is "safe for democracy" and nations govern themselves through the consent of the governed.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Rhetoric of Neutrality: Analyze Wilson’s shift from neutrality to intervention. Was his argument that "neutrality is no longer possible" a logical conclusion of German aggression, or a strategic pivot to justify a deeper ideological agenda? Use the "Argument Map" to support your analysis.
  2. The Democratic Peace Theory: Evaluate Wilson’s claim that democracies are inherently peaceful and autocracies are inherently aggressive. To what extent does this theory serve as a sufficient justification for entering a global conflict?
  3. The Ethics of Omission: Consider the "Common Misreading" caution provided in the text. Were Wilson's omissions regarding the British blockade and domestic inequality "strategic" or "practical"? Argue whether these omissions undermine the moral authority of his case for war.
  4. Principle vs. Profit: Contrast Wilson’s argument of "grim duty" and "universal principles" with the economic perspectives mentioned in the text (such as those of Smedley Butler). Can Wilson’s moral framework be reconciled with the reality of American financial interests in 1917?
