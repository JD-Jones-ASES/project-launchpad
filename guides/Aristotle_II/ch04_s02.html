Literary Greatness and the Defense of Poesy: Longinus and Sidney

This study guide examines the evolution of literary criticism following Aristotle, specifically focusing on Longinus’s theory of the sublime and Sir Philip Sidney’s definitive defense of poetry. These two figures expanded upon Aristotelian foundations to explore the transformative power of literature and its moral utility.

I. Longinus and the Theory of the Sublime

Longinus’s work, On the Sublime, serves as a completion of the analytical framework established by Aristotle. While Aristotle focused on the constituent elements of literary excellence (plot, character, diction), Longinus investigated the psychological impact of greatness on the reader.

The Nature of the Sublime (Hypsos)

  • Definition: Longinus uses the term hypsos to describe a quality of height, elevation, or sublimity in literature and oratory.
  • Effect: The sublime does not merely produce pleasure; it produces "transport"—a sense of being lifted above oneself and experiencing something greater than ordinary human measure. It is analogous to divine inspiration or ecstasy.
  • Attainability: Longinus argues that while much of the sublime depends on natural genius, it can be supported through study and art.

The Five Sources of the Sublime

Longinus identifies five principal sources of sublimity, organized in a hierarchy where the first two are natural endowments and the final three are cultivated through art.

Source Category Description
1. Grandeur of Thought Natural The capacity for great conceptions; the most important source.
2. Vigorous Passion Natural A spirited treatment of the passions; genuine emotion that sounds like "the voice of a god."
3. Artifice in Figures Art The effective use of figures of thought and figures of speech.
4. Dignified Expression Art Noble diction, including the proper choice of words and the use of metaphors.
5. Majesty of Structure Art The elevated composition and arrangement of the entire work.

The Sublime vs. The Pathetic

Longinus clarifies the relationship between the sublime and emotion (pathos):

  • Distinction: The sublime is not always emotional, and the emotional is not always sublime. Low passions like pity, grief, and fear are not "lofty."
  • Parenthyrsus: This is a rhetorical vice described as "false passion." It occurs when a writer strains for greatness but produces only "empty swelling" or bombast, leading to absurdity rather than transport.

II. Sir Philip Sidney’s The Defence of Poesy

Writing in Elizabethan England, Sir Philip Sidney sought to protect fiction against Platonic charges of corruption and Protestant skepticism regarding the moral effects of poetry.

The "Golden World" vs. The "Brazen World"

Sidney famously contrasts the world of nature with the world of the poet:

  • The Brazen World: Nature as it actually is—imperfect and constrained by reality.
  • The Golden World: The world created by the poet, where virtue is rewarded, excellence is portrayed at its height, and the moral order of things is made visible.

Poetry as the Monarch of Sciences

Sidney argues that poetry is superior to both philosophy and history in its ability to teach virtue:

  1. Philosophy: Teaches virtue through abstract, obscure definitions that are difficult to follow.
  2. History: Records virtue as it actually occurred, but is enslaved to the "truth" of what happened, even if it is not morally instructive.
  3. Poetry: Combines the two. It provides concrete, vivid images (like history) that represent universal truths (like philosophy). It moves the "will" to act virtuously by making virtue delightful.

The Response to Plato

Sidney addresses the charge that poets are liars by stating that "the poet nothing affirms, and therefore never lieth." Since the poet presents fiction as fiction and does not claim his stories are literally true, he cannot be guilty of deception. This aligns with Aristotle’s view that poetry imitates "things as they ought to be" rather than merely as they are.

III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to Longinus, what is the primary difference between the effect of the sublime and the effect of ordinary literary excellence?
  2. Which two sources of the sublime does Longinus categorize as "natural endowments"?
  3. What is the term for "false or misplaced passion" that results in bombast rather than sublimity?
  4. Why does Sidney refer to nature as a "brazen world"?
  5. How does Sidney justify the poet’s use of "wholesome things" hidden in "pleasant taste"?
  6. According to Sidney, why is a historian more "culpable" of lying than a poet?
  7. What does Longinus mean when he says that grandeur of thought without linguistic capacity remains "mute"?

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Synthesis of Art and Nature: Compare Longinus’s hierarchy of the five sources of the sublime with Aristotle’s ranking of the elements of tragedy. How does Longinus use the distinction between "natural endowment" and "art" to argue for the necessity of both genius and study in literature?
  2. The Moral Utility of Fiction: Analyze Sidney’s argument that poetry is the "monarch" of all human sciences. In what ways does Sidney suggest that the "golden world" of the poet is more effective at educating the human will than the factual accounts of the historian or the abstract theories of the philosopher?
  3. Transport and Transformation: Longinus focuses on the psychological state of "transport," while Sidney focuses on the "winning of the mind from wickedness to virtue." Discuss how these two perspectives shift the focus of literary criticism from the structure of a text to the effect it has on the audience.

V. Glossary of Key Terms

  • Astrophil and Stella: Sidney’s sequence of 108 sonnets and eleven songs (c. 1582), depicting a speaker's unsuccessful love for "Stella."
  • Brazen World: Sidney’s term for nature as it actually is—imperfect and brass-like.
  • Golden World: The poet’s creation; a world in which virtue is rewarded and excellence is portrayed at its highest, raising the reader toward a "golden age."
  • Hypsos: Height, elevation, or sublimity; the quality that overwhelms the reader and produces divine inspiration.
  • Parenthyrsus: False or misplaced passion; "empty swelling" or bombast that fails to achieve the sublime and falls into absurdity.
  • Pathos: Emotion or suffering; the emotional content of tragedy and a mode of persuasion.
  • Sublime: Derived from the Latin sublimis ("under the lintel"); refers to literary greatness so powerful it produces transport.
  • Transport: The sudden sense of being lifted above oneself, experiencing something greater than ordinary human measure.
