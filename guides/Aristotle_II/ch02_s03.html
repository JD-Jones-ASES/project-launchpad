Aristotle’s Poetics: The Art of Imitation Study Guide

This study guide explores Aristotle’s seminal work, the Poetics, composed around 335 BC. It examines his systematic approach to literary criticism, the fundamental concept of mimesis, and the historical evolution of poetic genres.

Key Concepts and Theoretical Framework

The Nature of Poetry as Mimesis

Aristotle identifies mimesis (imitation) as the foundational principle of all arts, including epic poetry, tragedy, comedy, choral hymns, and music. Unlike his predecessor Plato, who viewed imitation as a deceptive removal from reality, Aristotle treated it as a natural human instinct and a tool for learning.

According to Aristotle, art is not a "mere copying" but a creative representation of human action. He distinguishes different forms of art based on three specific dimensions:

  1. The Medium: The tools used to produce the imitation. These include rhythm, language, and "harmony" (melody).
    • Music (flute/lyre): Uses harmony and rhythm.
    • Dance: Uses rhythm alone to imitate character and emotion.
    • Prose/Verse: Uses language alone.
    • Tragedy/Comedy: Uses a combination of rhythm, tune, and metre.
  2. The Objects: The subjects being imitated, specifically "men in action."
    • Higher Type: Representing men as better than in real life (Tragedy, Epic).
    • Lower Type: Representing men as worse than in real life (Comedy, Satire).
    • True to Life: Representing men as they actually are.
  3. The Manner: The mode of presentation.
    • Narrative: The poet speaks in their own voice (Epic).
    • Dramatic: The poet makes characters speak and act directly (Tragedy, Comedy).

The Dual Origins of Poetry

Aristotle argues that poetry arose from two deep-seated human instincts:

  • The Instinct for Imitation: Humans are the most imitative of all creatures and learn their earliest lessons through imitation.
  • The Instinct for Rhythm and Harmony: A natural gift for melody and cadence that humans developed over time.

A central paradox in Aristotle’s theory is the pleasure derived from "painful" imitations. He notes that while we may view a dead body or a "low" animal with pain in reality, we find pleasure in a realistic artistic representation of them. This pleasure stems from the love of learning; by recognizing a representation, the viewer performs an act of understanding or inference.

The Evolution of Genres

The development of different poetic forms followed the "individual character" of the writers:

  • Serious Spirits: Focused on noble actions and good men, leading from hymns and praises to Epic poetry, and eventually to Tragedy.
  • Trivial Spirits: Focused on the actions of "meaner persons," leading from personal satires (lampoons) to Comedy.

The Role of Homer: Aristotle views Homer as the pivotal figure in both traditions. Homer is pre-eminent in the serious style for combining dramatic form with excellence of imitation in the Iliad and Odyssey. Simultaneously, he laid the groundwork for Comedy with his work Margites, which substituted the "ludicrous" for personal satire.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. Where and when did Aristotle compose the Poetics? Aristotle composed the Poetics around 335 BC in Athens, during the years he led his school, the Lyceum.

2. How did Aristotle’s view of poetry differ from Plato’s? Plato viewed poetry with suspicion as a dangerous imitation of appearances twice removed from reality. Aristotle approached it scientifically and philosophically, viewing it with curiosity and admiration as a natural human activity.

3. What are the three dimensions by which Aristotle distinguishes different arts? The three dimensions are the medium (how they imitate), the objects (what they imitate), and the manner or mode (how the imitation is presented).

4. According to Aristotle, what is the primary difference between Tragedy and Comedy? The difference lies in the objects of imitation: Comedy aims to represent men as "worse" than in actual life (lower types), while Tragedy aims to represent them as "better" (higher types).

5. Why do humans find pleasure in representations of objects that would be painful to see in person? The pleasure is derived from the act of learning. Contemplating a likeness allows people to infer and understand what an object is, and Aristotle asserts that learning gives the "liveliest pleasure" to humans.

6. What distinguishes Epic poetry from Tragedy in terms of "manner" and "time"? Epic poetry is narrative in form and has no specific limits of time. Tragedy is dramatic (characters acting) and endeavors to confine its action to a "single revolution of the sun" or slightly longer.

7. Who was the first Athenian writer to abandon personal lampooning in favor of generalized comic plots? Crates was the first Athenian writer to move away from the "iambic" or lampooning form to generalize themes and plots in comedy.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Biological and Psychological Roots of Art: Analyze Aristotle’s claim that poetry is an expression of what humans "most fundamentally are." How does his grounding of art in the instincts of imitation and rhythm challenge the idea that art is a "luxury" of civilization?
  2. Homer as the Universal Poet: Discuss Aristotle’s assessment of Homer. How does Aristotle justify placing Homer at the origin of both the tragic and comic traditions, and what specific qualities of Homeric epic does he believe transitioned into the "higher" form of drama?
  3. The Morality of Representation: Aristotle notes that the "objects of imitation" (men as better, worse, or equal) determine the moral tone of an artistic tradition. Explore how this classification system suggests that art's primary function is to illuminate human life and character rather than just provide entertainment.
  4. The Technical Evolution of Drama: Trace the transition from "lampooning" to Comedy and from "Epic" to Tragedy. Use Aristotle’s distinctions regarding metre, length, and dramatic form to explain why he considers drama a "larger and higher form of art" than epic or satire.

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Dithyramb A choral hymn sung in honor of Dionysus; one of the forms from which tragedy may have developed.
Harmony In the context of the Poetics, this refers to melody or musical tune used as a medium of imitation.
Iambic Measure Also known as the "lampooning measure"; the metre originally used by writers to satirize or attack individuals.
The Ludicrous A subdivision of the ugly, defined as a defect or ugliness that is not painful or destructive (e.g., a comic mask).
Lyceum The school founded by Aristotle in Athens near the sanctuary of Apollo Lykeios.
Mimesis The foundational concept of Aristotle’s theory; the creative representation or imitation of human action.
Peripatetics A nickname for Aristotle’s students ("walkers"), derived from their habit of discussing philosophy while strolling.
The Poetics Written c. 335 BC, it is the earliest surviving work of literary criticism in the Western tradition.
Single Revolution of the Sun The time limit (roughly 24 hours) that Aristotle observes Tragedy typically attempts to follow, unlike the Epic.
