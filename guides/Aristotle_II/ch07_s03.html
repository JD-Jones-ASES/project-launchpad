Comprehensive Study Guide: Aristotle’s Rhetoric, Book III

This study guide focuses on the third book of Aristotle’s Rhetoric, which marks a transition from the substance of persuasion (what to say) to its form (how to say it). The text examines the dual pillars of lexis (style) and taxis (arrangement), arguing that while logic is the foundation of any speech, the manner of delivery and the structure of the argument are essential for practical persuasion.

I. Key Concepts and Themes

The Necessity of Style (Lexis)

Aristotle posits that style is a practical necessity due to the "defects" of the audience. While an ideal audience would be moved by bare facts alone, the way a thing is said affects its intelligibility and the listener’s reception. Aristotle establishes a strict hierarchy: argument is primary, but style must serve that argument to ensure it is understood and persuasive.

The Virtues of Good Style

According to the text, a successful oratorical style must possess two primary qualities:

  1. Clarity: Speech fails if it does not convey a plain meaning. This is achieved by using current, ordinary words.
  2. Appropriateness (To Prepon): The style must match the subject matter, the speaker’s character, and the audience’s emotional state. It should avoid being too "mean" (lowly) or too "elevated" (poetic).

Metaphor and Liveliness (Energeia)

Metaphor is the most vital tool for the prose writer because it provides clarity, charm, and distinction. Aristotle emphasizes:

  • Proportional Metaphors: These are the most effective, as they create a "math-like" relationship between two things (e.g., "the spring has been taken out of the year" to describe fallen youth).
  • Actuality (Energeia): The highest achievement of style is making the audience "see" things in progress. This involves giving life to inanimate objects and representing things in a state of activity.

Prose Rhythm

Prose should be rhythmical but not metrical. If it is metrical (like poetry), it appears artificial and loses the audience's trust. Aristotle identifies the paean as the ideal rhythm for prose because it is the least obtrusive and does not fall into a predictable meter.

Arrangement (Taxis)

Aristotle rejects the complex structural systems of his contemporaries, stripping a speech down to its essential components:

  1. Statement: Stating the case.
  2. Proof: Arguing the case.
  3. Introduction/Epilogue: While often used, these are considered functional additions rather than logical necessities.

II. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Lexis Style; the study of how language is used to express ideas.
Taxis Arrangement; the organization and ordering of the different parts of a speech.
Energeia Vividness or "actuality"; the quality of making an audience see events as happening in the present moment.
To Prepon Appropriateness; the requirement that style match the subject, speaker, and audience.
Delivery The management of the voice (volume, pitch, and rhythm) to express emotions.
Metaphor The application of a word or idea to something it does not literally denote to provide new insight or "freshness."
Paean A specific prose rhythm with a 3:2 ratio; preferred by Aristotle for being rhythmical without being metrical.
Introduction The beginning of a speech (prologue or prelude) intended to show the aim of the discourse and prepare the audience.
Epilogue The closing section of a speech, designed to inspire the right emotions and summarize the argument.

III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why does Aristotle believe style is necessary even if the facts of an argument are sound? Answer: Because the way a thing is said affects its intelligibility and how it is received by the audience; audiences are often "defective" and require more than just bare facts to remain engaged and convinced.

  2. What are the three factors a speaker must bear in mind regarding delivery? Answer: Volume of sound, modulation of pitch, and rhythm.

  3. Why does Aristotle criticize the use of purely poetical language in prose? Answer: Poetic language often uses words not found in ordinary talk, which can make a speech feel artificial or deceptive. Prose should appear natural to be persuasive.

  4. According to Aristotle, what are the only two truly essential parts of a speech? Answer: The Statement of the case and the Proof (Argument).

  5. What is the "proportional metaphor," and why is it highly valued? Answer: It is a metaphor that relates four terms in a ratio (A is to B as C is to D). It is valued because it is "lively" and helps the audience seize new ideas promptly.

  6. What is the specific function of the paean rhythm at the end of a sentence? Answer: The paean that ends with a long syllable should be used at the close of a sentence to provide a sense of finality through the rhythm itself.

  7. What are the four functions of an epilogue? Answer: (1) Make the audience well-disposed to the speaker/ill-disposed to the opponent; (2) Magnify or minimize the facts; (3) Excite the required state of emotion; (4) Refresh the audience's memory.

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Ethics of Appearance: Aristotle notes that "the whole business of rhetoric being concerned with appearances, we must pay attention to the subject of delivery, unworthy though it is." Discuss the tension in Book III between the "purity" of logical proof and the "practical necessity" of style and delivery. Does Aristotle view style as a corruption or a completion of the rhetorical art?

  2. The Power of the Metaphor: Aristotle claims that metaphors cannot be taught and are a sign of natural talent. Analyze his requirement that metaphors be "fitting" and "beautiful." Using examples from the text (such as "the spring taken out of the year"), explain how a well-crafted metaphor functions as both an intellectual and an emotional tool of persuasion.

  3. Naturalness vs. Artificiality: A central theme in Book III is that "naturalness is persuasive, artificiality is the contrary." Explore Aristotle’s advice on how a writer should "disguise his art." How does the use of ordinary language, specific prose rhythms, and appropriate emotional tone contribute to the illusion of spontaneity?

  4. Structural Minimalism: Compare Aristotle's four-part speech structure (Introduction, Statement, Argument, Epilogue) with the more elaborate systems of his contemporaries (e.g., pre-narration, refutation, etc.). Why does Aristotle argue that these extra divisions are "pointless and silly"? How does his simplified structure reflect his belief in the primacy of the "Statement" and "Proof"?
