Study Guide: Style, Arrangement, and the Rhetorical Tradition

This study guide explores the evolution of rhetorical theory from the foundations laid by Aristotle to the expanded analyses of Longinus, the systematization of the Five Canons by Roman orators, and the eventual integration of these principles into medieval and Christian education.

I. Key Concepts and Themes

The Sublime and the Power of Composition

While Aristotle viewed style and arrangement as tools for effective argument, the author known as Longinus (Pseudo-Longinus) shifted the focus toward the "sublime" (hypsos). In his work On the Sublime, written roughly in the first century AD, Longinus identifies five sources of sublimity. The fifth source, composition, refers to the arrangement of words and the harmonious structure of a passage.

Longinus argues that composition is more than a mechanical arrangement; it is a kind of harmony that can "exalt the soul." He compares the effect of well-arranged prose to music, noting that just as a flute can kindle "orgiastic frenzy," a majestic and harmonious linguistic structure can gain "complete mastery over our minds."

Genius vs. Technical Art

A central theme in the study of rhetoric is the tension between following rules ("art") and natural "genius."

  • Art: Consists of the avoidance of error and technical correctness. It is exemplified by works like the Doryphorus of Polycletus, which is perfectly proportioned.
  • Genius: Characterized by high, though perhaps unequal, excellence. Longinus argues that while a writer who makes no slips receives "negative approbation," the sublime writer commands "positive reverence." Great masters like Homer, Demosthenes, and Plato may commit blunders, but these are infinitesimal compared to their moments of absolute perfection.

The Five Canons of Rhetoric

The transition from Aristotle’s two-part theory (What to say vs. How to say it) to the Roman five-part framework dominated Western education for two millennia. The Rhetorica ad Herennium (c. 86 BC) was the first text to organize these into a clear system.

Canon Latin Term Greek Equivalent Definition/Function
Invention Inventio Heuresis Finding and constructing arguments; covers Aristotle’s Books I and II.
Arrangement Dispositio Taxis Organizing the parts of the speech (Introduction, Statement, Proof, Epilogue).
Style Elocutio Lexis The language of the speech; includes metaphors and figures of speech.
Memory Memoria — Memorizing the speech, often using "memory palaces."
Delivery Pronuntiatio Hypokrisis Vocal and physical management (voice, gesture, expression).

The Ideal Orator

Cicero and Quintilian shifted the focus of rhetoric from the text to the speaker. They promoted the ideal of the vir bonus dicendi peritus—the "good man skilled in speaking." This philosophy suggests that rhetoric is inseparable from moral character; a truly skilled orator must also be a virtuous person.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Who is the traditional (though disputed) author of the treatise On the Sublime?
  2. What is the literal meaning of the term ekstasis as used by Longinus?
  3. According to Longinus, what happens to the grandeur of a passage if its rhythmic measure is "crippled by abridgment"?
  4. Which Greek orator does Longinus use to demonstrate the power of arranging the phrase "like a cloud" at the end of a sentence?
  5. What are the three components of the medieval trivium?
  6. Which Roman orator wrote De Oratore and served as consul in 63 BC?
  7. What is the primary goal of Quintilian’s twelve-book treatise, Institutio Oratoria?
  8. According to Augustine in De Doctrina Christiana, what are the three purposes of the Christian preacher (adapted from Cicero)?
  9. Why does Longinus prefer the "huge disproportioned Colossus" to the "Doryphorus of Polycletus" in the context of literature?
  10. Which canon of rhetoric was the largest and most extensively developed in the later tradition, generating elaborate taxonomies of figures?

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Musicality of Prose: Analyze Longinus’s argument that prose composition functions similarly to music. How does the arrangement of words create a "physiological effect" on the listener that transcends the literal meaning of the text? Use the example of Demosthenes' "like a cloud" to support your answer.
  2. Ethos and the Vir Bonus: Compare Aristotle's concept of ethos with the Roman ideal of the vir bonus dicendi peritus. How did Cicero and Quintilian expand the idea of "character" into a comprehensive moral and educational program?
  3. The Evolution of the Canons: Discuss the transition from Aristotle's original rhetorical structure to the Five Canons. Why were "Memory" and "Delivery" added as distinct categories, and how did this change the way rhetoric was taught and practiced in the Roman and Medieval periods?
  4. Rhetoric in Service of Truth: Explore Augustine’s justification for using secular rhetorical techniques in Christian preaching. How does he reconcile the "pleasure" of style (delectare) with the serious mission of proclaiming the Gospel?

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Delectare: To delight; one of Cicero’s three aims of the orator, corresponding to the pleasure produced by style.
  • Dispositio: The canon of Arrangement; the ordering of the parts of a speech.
  • Docere: To teach; the aim of the orator corresponding to logos or the transmission of information.
  • Ekstasis: Literally "standing outside oneself"; the experience of being overwhelmed or transported by the sublime.
  • Elocutio: The canon of Style; the choice and arrangement of words and figures.
  • Hypsos: The Greek term for the "sublime"—greatness or loftiness in writing that transports the audience.
  • Inventio: The canon of Invention; the process of finding and constructing arguments.
  • Memoria: The canon of Memory; the use of mnemonic systems to prepare for speech delivery.
  • Movere: To move; the aim of the orator corresponding to pathos or the emotional persuasion of the audience.
  • Philippics: A series of fourteen speeches by Cicero attacking Mark Antony, named after Demosthenes’ speeches against Philip II of Macedon.
  • Pronuntiatio: The canon of Delivery; the management of voice and gesture.
  • Trivium: The three arts of language in medieval education: grammar, logic, and rhetoric.
  • Vir bonus dicendi peritus: "A good man skilled in speaking"; the Roman ideal that rhetoric requires both technical skill and moral virtue.
