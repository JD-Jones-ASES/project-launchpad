# Aristotle and the Western Literary and Rhetorical Tradition: A Study Guide

This study guide explores the profound and enduring influence of Aristotle’s *Poetics* and *Rhetoric* on Western civilization. It traces the evolution of these ideas from ancient Rome through the Renaissance to modern storytelling and public discourse.

---

### Key Concepts and Historical Developments

#### 1. The Latin Inheritance: Horace and Quintilian
The Roman world did not merely translate Aristotle; it adapted his theories for practical and aesthetic use. 
*   **Horace:** In his *Ars Poetica*, Horace transformed Aristotelian theory into a set of guidelines for Roman tastes. He emphasized the dual purpose of poetry—to "delight and instruct" (*aut prodesse aut delectare*)—and established the principle *ut pictura poesis* ("as is painting, so is poetry"), suggesting that literature rewards close inspection similar to visual art.
*   **Quintilian:** In *Institutio Oratoria*, Quintilian defined the ideal orator as *vir bonus dicendi peritus*—a "good man skilled in speaking." This bridged the gap between technical craft and moral character, reflecting Aristotle’s emphasis on *ethos*.

#### 2. The Renaissance: Rules and Rebellion
The rediscovery of the *Poetics* in the 15th and 16th centuries led to a rigid systematization of literary "rules."
*   **The Three Unities:** Critics like Castelvetro derived the three unities (time, place, and action) from Aristotle. While French neoclassical dramatists like Racine embraced these constraints to create intense, concentrated tragedies, English playwrights like Shakespeare largely ignored the unities of time and place while maintaining a profound unity of action.
*   **Systematization:** Scholars such as Julius Caesar Scaliger sought to create complete systems for every genre, including tragedy, comedy, epic, and the newly emerging prose romance.

#### 3. The Christianization of Rhetoric
St. Augustine of Hippo played a pivotal role in preserving Aristotelian rhetoric by applying it to Christian preaching in *De Doctrina Christiana*. He argued that the tools of eloquence—*docere* (to teach), *delectare* (to delight), and *movere* (to move)—should be used to move audiences toward eternal truths rather than just temporal goods.

#### 4. Aristotle in the Modern Era
Aristotelian principles remain foundational in contemporary media:
*   **Film Structure:** The standard three-act structure (setup, confrontation, resolution) used in Hollywood is a direct descendant of Aristotle’s beginning-middle-end construct. Modern "character arcs" are essentially commercial adaptations of Aristotelian *catharsis*.
*   **Modern Persuasion:** Aristotle’s rhetorical triangle—*ethos* (credibility), *pathos* (emotion), and *logos* (logic)—is the primary framework for analyzing modern advertising, political campaigns, and social media interactions.

### Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. What was Horace’s primary contribution to the understanding of poetry’s purpose?**
Horace formulated the dual purpose of poetry as *aut prodesse aut delectare*: to both benefit (instruct) and delight the audience.

**2. Which three unities became the standard for judging drama during the Italian Renaissance?**
The unities of time, place, and action (often attributed to Castelvetro).

**3. How did William Shakespeare’s tragedies align with Aristotelian principles despite ignoring the unities of time and place?**
His tragedies featured heroes of genuine greatness undone by errors of judgment, with plots moving through reversal and recognition toward a catastrophic, yet inevitable, end.

**4. Name the five canons of rhetoric systematized by Cicero.**
*Inventio* (finding arguments), *dispositio* (arrangement), *elocutio* (style), *memoria* (memory), and *pronuntiatio* (delivery).

**5. What is the "trivium" in the medieval university curriculum, and what are its Aristotelian roots?**
The trivium consists of grammar, logic, and rhetoric. Logic stems from Aristotle’s *Organon*, grammar from the *Poetics* and *Rhetoric*, and rhetoric from the *Rhetoric* itself.

**6. According to the text, why is Aristotle’s lost book on comedy significant?**
It would have provided a penetrating analysis of comedy as the imitation of the "ridiculous" (the laughably absurd) rather than the wicked, potentially offering a theory of "comic catharsis."

**7. How does Syd Field’s *Screenplay* relate to Aristotle?**
It is a practical adaptation of Aristotelian story structure for cinema, emphasizing the three-act structure and the placement of reversal and recognition at moments of maximum tension.

**8. What was St. Augustine’s stance on the use of classical rhetoric in the church?**
He believed that preachers should use the resources of classical rhetoric to move audiences toward the Gospel, arguing that the power of speech should not be reserved for pagan orators.

### Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

**1. The Evolution of the "Ideal Orator":**
Compare and contrast the Aristotelian concept of *ethos* with Quintilian’s definition of the orator as *vir bonus dicendi peritus*. How does the requirement of moral goodness change the way we evaluate the "art" of persuasion? Use examples from the text, such as Cicero’s public career, to support your argument.

**2. Rigidity vs. Discovery in Literary Theory:**
Analyze the tension between the "rules" of the Renaissance neoclassical critics (such as the three unities) and the "truths" discovered by writers like Shakespeare. Does the survival of Aristotelian principles in Shakespeare’s work prove that Aristotle’s insights are "discovered" rather than "followed"?

**3. The Ethics of Persuasion in the Modern World:**
Aristotle suggested that a critical vocabulary (ethos, pathos, logos) makes a student harder to manipulate. Discuss how these three categories function in modern advertising or social media. Is rhetoric inherently neutral, or does its power impose a moral responsibility on the speaker, as Augustine and Quintilian suggested?

**4. The Mystery of Comedy:**
Using the fragments of information about Aristotle’s lost book and Umberto Eco’s *The Name of the Rose*, discuss the philosophical danger that laughter might have posed to medieval authorities. Why would the "imitation of the ridiculous" be seen as a threat to the "pious fear of God"?

### Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Ars Poetica** | "The Art of Poetry"; a verse letter by Horace (c. 19 BC) that became a foundational document of Latin literary theory. |
| **Aut prodesse aut delectare** | "To benefit or to delight"; Horace's formulation of the dual purpose of poetry, echoing Aristotle's claims on imitation and universal truths. |
| **Catharsis** | The purification or purgation of emotions (typically pity and fear) through art; in modern film, often referred to as a "character arc." |
| **Docere, delectare, movere** | "To teach, to delight, to move"; the three goals of the orator as identified by Cicero and later "baptized" by St. Augustine for Christian preaching. |
| **Ethos** | A rhetorical appeal based on the character, credibility, or authority of the speaker. |
| **Logos** | A rhetorical appeal based on logic, reason, evidence, and statistics. |
| **Mimesis** | The imitation of human action; the core principle of both drama and the modern novel. |
| **Pathos** | A rhetorical appeal based on the emotional response of the audience. |
| **Ut pictura poesis** | "As is painting, so is poetry"; Horace’s phrase used to argue that literature, like art, requires and rewards close inspection. |
| **Vir bonus dicendi peritus** | "A good man skilled in speaking"; Quintilian's definition of the ideal orator, bridging craft and morality. |
