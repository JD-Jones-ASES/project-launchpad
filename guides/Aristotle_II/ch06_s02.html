Study Guide: Emotion, Character, and Argument in the Rhetorical Tradition

This study guide explores the evolution of rhetorical theory concerning emotion (pathos) and its application from classical antiquity through the Renaissance to modern behavioral science. It focuses on how subsequent thinkers expanded Aristotle’s foundational mechanics of emotional appeal into theories of aesthetic transport, moral formation, and psychological framing.

I. Key Concepts and Theoretical Extensions

The Sublime and Authenticity (Longinus)

While Aristotle viewed pathos as one of three modes of persuasion serving the truth, Longinus shifted the focus toward "transport"—an overwhelming emotional effect that goes beyond mere persuasion.

  • The Five Sources of the Sublime: Longinus identifies five pillars that contribute to the sublime:
    1. Greatness of thought (megalophrosyne).
    2. Strong and inspired emotion (sphodra kai enthousiastike pathos).
    3. Figures of thought and speech.
    4. Noble diction.
    5. Dignified and elevated composition.
  • Nature vs. Art: The first two sources (thought and emotion) are considered natural gifts, while the latter three are products of art and craft.
  • Genuine vs. Manufactured Emotion: Longinus argues that for an emotional passage to be sublime, it must be authentic and arise from a greatness of subject. He cites Sappho’s Fragment 31 (describing physical symptoms of desire) and Homer’s heroes as examples where emotion is perfectly suited to the moment without straining for effect.

Literature and the Will (Philip Sidney)

Philip Sidney’s Defence of Poesy (c. 1580) argues for the moral superiority of poetry over philosophy and history based on its ability to move the listener's will toward virtuous action (praxis).

  • The Triple Duty of the Orator: Inherited from Cicero, these duties include:
    • Docere: To teach (inform about facts).
    • Delectare: To delight (win goodwill and hold attention).
    • Movere: To move (drive to action or change the will).
  • The Superiority of Poetry: Sidney posits that while the philosopher teaches through abstractions and the historian moves through concrete particulars that lack universality, the poet does both. By creating compelling, admirable heroes like Cyrus or Aeneas, the poet makes virtue attractive and addresses the "weakness of will"—the human tendency to know what is right but fail to act upon it.

Modern Psychology and Behavioral Economics

Aristotle’s observations on how emotional states affect judgment are reflected in contemporary research regarding human decision-making.

  • Cognitive Biases and Framing: Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky demonstrated that human reasoning is not purely logical. "Framing" an argument as a gain versus a loss (e.g., "200 people saved" vs. "400 people will die") fundamentally changes how people respond, even when the objective facts are identical.
  • Nudge Theory: Developed by Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein, this application of behavioral economics involves designing "choice environments" where the emotionally natural response aligns with the better choice (e.g., retirement savings or organ donation).

II. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Pathos Strong, authentic emotion; one of Aristotle's three modes of persuasion.
Parenthyrsus A term coined by Longinus for misplaced, excessive, or inappropriate passion that results in embarrassment rather than transport.
The Sublime An overwhelming rhetorical or aesthetic effect that transports the audience rather than merely persuading them.
Docere, Delectare, Movere The three Ciceronian duties: to teach, to delight, and to move.
Praxis Action or practice; distinguished from gnosis (knowledge). According to Sidney, praxis is the ultimate fruit of teaching.
Framing The deliberate shaping of the context in which information is presented to influence the emotional valence and judgment of the audience.
Nudge Theory The practice of designing choice environments to encourage specific, emotionally natural decisions without removing freedom of choice.

III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to Longinus, how does the "Sublime" differ from the "Pathetic"?
    • Answer: Longinus argues they are not the same; some passions are low (pity, fear) and lack sublimity, while some sublime passages (such as those describing the sons of Aloëus in Homer) lack pathos. However, he notes that appropriate genuine passion is highly conducive to sublimity.
  2. What is the danger of "manufactured" emotion in rhetoric?
    • Answer: Longinus warns that a writer who mistakes intensity for greatness or heaps on passion where the subject does not warrant it produces parenthyrsus. This leads to the reader’s contempt rather than elevation.
  3. Why does Sidney believe philosophy is less effective than poetry in promoting moral action?
    • Answer: Philosophy provides abstract "wordish" descriptions that reach the intellect but do not move the will. It requires an already-studious reader. Poetry, by contrast, uses vivid images of virtue that make the reader desire to emulate the characters.
  4. How do Kahneman and Tversky’s findings support Aristotle’s view of the audience?
    • Answer: Their research confirms that humans do not reason in purely logical terms and that emotional framing significantly affects judgment, mirroring Aristotle’s account of how the emotional state of a judge affects a verdict.
  5. What is the "test" for determining if an emotional appeal is honest or manipulative?
    • Answer: An appeal is honest if it illuminates a genuine feature of the situation (e.g., showing a victim’s family in a murder trial). It is manipulative if it exploits a psychological vulnerability to bypass rational scrutiny or substitute for argument.

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. Transport versus Persuasion: Longinus suggests that the highest form of rhetoric does not merely convince an audience but "transports" them. Compare this to Aristotle's more clinical "mechanics of pathos." Is "transport" a legitimate goal of ethical rhetoric, or does it inherently risk the "fine madness" Longinus describes?
  2. The Problem of the Will: Philip Sidney argues that the primary obstacle to a moral life is not a lack of knowledge (gnosis) but a weakness of the will. Analyze how Sidney’s theory of literature seeks to bridge the gap between knowing what is virtuous and performing virtuous acts (praxis).
  3. The Ethics of Choice Architecture: Discuss "Nudge Theory" in the context of Aristotelian rhetoric. Does the deliberate shaping of a choice environment to produce a "better" emotional response constitute "honest rhetoric" that illuminates the merits of a case, or is it a form of "sophisticated manipulation"?
  4. Authenticity in the Digital Age: Using Longinus’s concept of parenthyrsus, evaluate modern attempts at emotional appeal in social media or advertising. How does the distinction between "genuine" and "manufactured" emotion apply to content designed to go viral through high-intensity emotional triggers?
