# Study Guide: The Evolution of Mimesis—Averroes and Sir Philip Sidney

This study guide examines the historical reception and transformation of Aristotle’s concept of *mimesis* (imitation) as it traveled from ancient Greece to the medieval Islamic world and the English Renaissance. It focuses on the specific interpretations provided by Averroes of Cordoba and Sir Philip Sidney, highlighting how cultural context reshapes literary theory.

---

## Key Concepts and Thematic Overview

### The Cultural Translation of Mimesis
The Aristotelian *Poetics* was not a static text; it underwent significant transformation as it crossed cultural boundaries. Because Aristotle’s theories were rooted in the specific context of Athenian drama, later scholars had to reconcile his concepts with their own literary traditions.

### Averroes and the Cognitive Shift
In the 12th century, the scholar Averroes (Ibn Rushd) adapted the *Poetics* for a culture that lacked a tradition of theatrical performance. 
*   **Moral Framework:** Averroes reframed Aristotle’s categories of tragedy and comedy into a binary system of moral evaluation: praise (panegyric) and blame (satire).
*   **Muhakat:** He translated *mimesis* as *muhakat* (assimilation or likening), shifting the focus from dramatic enactment to a cognitive operation of making one thing resemble another.
*   **The Qasida Lens:** The dominant Arabic poetic form, the *qasida* (ode), replaced the Greek play as the primary model for poetic structure.

### Sir Philip Sidney and the Golden World
Writing in the 16th century, Sir Philip Sidney defended poetry against Puritan critiques by arguing for its moral and educational superiority.
*   **Improvement on Nature:** Sidney argued that while nature provides a "brazen world" (imperfect and corrupt), the poet creates a "golden world" that shows humanity at its best possibilities.
*   **The Superiority of Poetry:** Sidney positioned poetry as superior to both philosophy and history. It combines the universal truths of philosophy with the vivid, concrete examples of history, moving the reader toward virtue through "delight."
*   **The Truth of Fiction:** To counter the charge that poets are liars, Sidney argued that because poets do not affirm their fictions as literal facts, they cannot be said to lie.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

| Question | Answer Summary |
| :--- | :--- |
| **1. Who was Averroes, and what was his title among medieval European scholars?** | Averroes (ibn Rushd) was a 12th-century philosopher, physician, and Islamic legal scholar from Cordoba. He was known as “The Commentator.” |
| **2. What major cultural obstacle did Averroes face when interpreting the *Poetics*?** | 12th-century Arabic culture had no theater; therefore, Averroes had no cultural equivalents for terms like "tragedy" and "comedy." |
| **3. How did Averroes redefine tragedy and comedy?** | He identified tragedy with *panegyric* (praise poetry) and comedy with *satirical verse* (poetry of mockery). |
| **4. What does the term *muhakat* imply about poetry?** | It implies "assimilation" or "likening," suggesting poetry is a cognitive operation of structural correspondence rather than a dramatic performance. |
| **5. For what reason did Sir Philip Sidney write *The Defence of Poesy*?** | He wrote it as a response to Puritan attacks that claimed theater and poetry were immoral, idle, and dangerous. |
| **6. According to Sidney, how does the poet relate to nature?** | The poet is a "maker" (*poietes*) who creates a "golden world" superior to the "brazen world" produced by nature. |
| **7. Why does Sidney believe poetry is superior to philosophy?** | Philosophy is abstract and "leaves the heart cold," whereas poetry provides concrete, vivid examples that move the whole person. |
| **8. Why does Sidney believe poetry is superior to history?** | History is bound to the particular—to what actually happened—whereas poetry can depict what *ought* to happen or what best illuminates human nature. |
| **9. How does Sidney refute the claim that "poets are liars"?** | He states, "The poet nothing affirms, and therefore never lieth," arguing that poets present hypotheses rather than false assertions of fact. |
| **10. What historical role did Arabic scholars play in the survival of the *Poetics*?** | Their translations and commentaries kept the Aristotelian tradition alive and provided the pathway for the text's return to medieval Europe. |

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Malleability of Mimesis:** Compare and contrast the Greek, Arabic, and Renaissance definitions of *mimesis*. How does the shift from "dramatic enactment" to "cognitive assimilation" to "moral improvement" reflect the needs of each specific culture? Use Averroes’ use of the *qasida* and Sidney’s "golden world" as primary examples.

2.  **Poetry as Moral Instruction:** Both Averroes and Sidney view poetry as a tool for moral evaluation, yet they arrive at this conclusion through different means. Discuss how Averroes’ focus on "praise and blame" aligns with or differs from Sidney’s argument that poetry "teaches by delighting."

3.  **The Poet as "Maker":** Sidney and Aristotle both utilize the etymology of *poietes* (maker). Analyze Sidney's claim that the poet's "making" is superior to nature. Does this claim maintain Aristotelian fidelity, or does it represent a radical break from the idea that poetry should imitate reality?

4.  **The Tensions Between Discipline:** Sidney argues that poetry is the "queen of the sciences" by comparing it to history and philosophy. Evaluate his argument. In what ways does his defense rely on the distinction between "universal truths" and "particular facts"?

## Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Averroes (Ibn Rushd):** (1126–1198) A Cordoban philosopher and jurist whose commentaries on Aristotle influenced Jewish and Christian thought, including that of Thomas Aquinas.
*   **Brazen World:** Sidney’s term for the real world—imperfect, mixed, and full of corruption.
*   **Golden World:** Sidney’s term for the fictional world created by the poet, where virtue is rewarded and human potential is fully expressed.
*   **Madh (Panegyric):** The Arabic tradition of praise poetry, which Averroes equated with Aristotelian tragedy.
*   **Muhakat:** The Arabic translation of *mimesis*, meaning "assimilation" or "correspondence"; it emphasizes intellectual resemblance over dramatic action.
*   **Poietes:** The Greek word for "poet," literally meaning "maker."
*   **Qasida:** A classical Arabic ode structured in three parts; the dominant prestige form of Arabic poetry.
*   **Sir Philip Sidney:** (1554–1586) An Elizabethan poet and soldier who wrote *The Defence of Poesy*, defining the poet as a creator of moral ideals.
*   **Tragedy and Comedy (Averroes’ Definition):** Reframed as tools for moral instruction via the celebration of virtues (tragedy) and the mockery of failings (comedy).
