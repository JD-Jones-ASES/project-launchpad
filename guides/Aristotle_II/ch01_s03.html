Study Guide: The Literary Tradition of Aristotle’s Poetics and Rhetoric

This study guide explores the transmission, interpretation, and transformation of Aristotle’s literary and rhetorical ideas across different civilizations and eras. While Thomas Aquinas integrated Aristotle’s ethics and politics into a Christian framework, the reception of the Poetics and the Rhetoric involved a more varied and contested tradition involving Greek, Arabic, Italian, and English thinkers.

I. Key Figures in the Literary Tradition

Longinus (1st Century AD)

Traditional author of the treatise On the Sublime, a seminal work of ancient literary criticism.

  • Central Concept: The "sublime" (hypsos), which refers to the power of language to produce a "shock of recognition" and a sense of "transport" (ekstasis).
  • The Five Sources of the Sublime:
    1. Greatness of thought (Gift of nature)
    2. Strong and inspired emotion (Gift of nature)
    3. Certain figures of speech (Can be cultivated)
    4. Noble diction/metaphor (Can be cultivated)
    5. Dignified composition (Can be cultivated)

Averroes / Ibn Rushd (1126–1198)

A medieval Arabic commentator known as "The Commentator" in the Latin world.

  • Cross-Cultural Challenge: Arabic culture lacked a tradition of theatrical performance, meaning terms like "comedy" and "tragedy" had no direct equivalents.
  • Reinterpretation: Averroes adapted Aristotle’s ideas to the Arabic literary tradition by redefining:
    • Tragedy as praise poetry.
    • Comedy as satirical verse.

Lodovico Castelvetro (1505–1571)

An Italian Renaissance critic who authored Poetica d'Aristotele Vulgarizzata et Sposta.

  • From Descriptive to Prescriptive: He transformed Aristotle’s observations about drama into rigid, binding rules.
  • The Three Unities:
    Unity Description Aristotelian Basis
    Unity of Action A single plot without subplots. Prescribed by Aristotle.
    Unity of Time Action must occur within a single day. Hinted at by Aristotle.
    Unity of Place Action must occur in a single location. Invented by Castelvetro; never mentioned by Aristotle.

Sir Philip Sidney (1554–1586)

An Elizabethan poet and courtier who wrote The Defence of Poesy in response to Puritan attacks on fiction.

  • The "Golden World": Sidney argued that while historians record what happened and philosophers provide abstract precepts, poets create a "golden world" that embodies universal truths in engaging stories.
  • The Superiority of Poetry: He claimed poetry is the best medium for teaching and moving humans toward "the good" because it engages the mind, heart, and imagination simultaneously.

Augustine of Hippo (354–430)

A foundational figure in the Christian rhetorical tradition.

  • Integration: In De Doctrina Christiana, he argued that Christian preachers should utilize classical rhetorical tools (ethos, pathos, and logos) to proclaim the Gospel.
  • Legacy: This tradition links Aristotle and Cicero to medieval sermon literature and modern Christian oratory.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does Longinus distinguish "the sublime" from work that is merely "pleasant or persuasive"?
  2. Which two sources of the sublime does Longinus consider "gifts of nature" rather than skills that can be cultivated?
  3. What cultural obstacle did Averroes face when translating Aristotle’s Poetics?
  4. In Castelvetro’s "Three Unities," which unity was entirely his own invention?
  5. What was the primary purpose of Sir Philip Sidney’s The Defence of Poesy?
  6. According to Sidney, why is the poet superior to the historian?
  7. What classical text inspired Augustine of Hippo to turn his heart toward wisdom?
  8. How did French neoclassical writers like Corneille and Racine utilize Castelvetro’s rules?

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Evolution of Rules: Compare Aristotle’s original observations in the Poetics with Lodovico Castelvetro’s interpretations. Discuss how the shift from descriptive observation to prescriptive "binding requirements" influenced the development of European drama.
  2. Cross-Cultural Translation: Using Averroes as a primary example, analyze how great ideas are transformed when they move between civilizations. Is the "misreading" of a text (such as interpreting tragedy as praise poetry) a failure of translation, or a necessary part of intellectual adaptation?
  3. The Moral Utility of Literature: Contrast Sidney’s "golden world" with the abstract precepts of philosophy. How does Sidney’s argument for poetry as a vehicle for moral and spiritual formation reflect or expand upon Aristotle’s original views on art and emotion?
  4. The Rhetorical Bridge: Discuss the connection between classical Greek rhetoric and the Christian tradition. How did Augustine of Hippo’s adoption of ethos, pathos, and logos ensure the survival and relevance of Aristotelian ideas in a theological context?

IV. Glossary of Key Terms

  • Ekstasis (Transport): The sense of being lifted out of ordinary experience, which Longinus identifies as the primary effect of truly great literature.
  • Hypsos (The Sublime): Literally "height" or "elevation"; the quality of language that goes beyond mere competence to achieve overwhelming greatness.
  • Middle Commentary on the Poetics: The c. 1175 work by Averroes that reinterpreted Greek dramatic concepts for the Arabic-speaking world.
  • The Three Unities: A prescriptive dramatic theory requiring unity of action, time, and place; these rules dominated European drama from the 16th through the 18th century.
  • Poetica d'Aristotele Vulgarizzata et Sposta: The influential and controversial 1570 commentary by Lodovico Castelvetro.
  • De Doctrina Christiana (On Christian Teaching): Augustine’s work arguing for the use of classical rhetoric in Christian preaching.
  • The Defence of Poesy: Sir Philip Sidney’s philosophical defense of imaginative literature against moralistic and Puritan critiques.
