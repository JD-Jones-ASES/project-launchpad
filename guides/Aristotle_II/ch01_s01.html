Aristotle’s Ethics and Politics: A Comprehensive Study Guide

This study guide examines the foundational philosophical frameworks of Aristotle as presented in the Nicomachean Ethics and the Politics. It explores the interconnectedness of individual virtue and the political community, providing a basis for understanding Aristotle's broader views on human life, persuasion, and poetry.

Key Concepts and Theoretical Foundations

The Nicomachean Ethics: Individual Flourishing

The Nicomachean Ethics posits that every human action is directed toward "some good." Aristotle identifies the ultimate end of all human activity as eudaimonia, often translated as happiness or flourishing. Unlike a fleeting feeling, eudaimonia represents a kind of life defined by the active and excellent exercise of distinctly human capacities.

  • Function and Excellence: Aristotle argues that the "good" of any thing is found in performing its specific function excellently. For humans, this function is the active exercise of the soul in accordance with arete (virtue or excellence).
  • The Nature of Virtue: Virtue is not innate, nor is it a mechanical rule. It is a habit—a settled disposition developed through repeated practice. Characters are formed by what individuals repeatedly choose to do.
  • The Doctrine of the Mean: Virtue exists as a middle point between two vices: excess and deficiency.
    • Example (Courage): The mean between recklessness (excess) and cowardice (deficiency).
    • Example (Generosity): The mean between prodigality (excess) and miserliness (deficiency).
  • Phronimos: The "practically wise person" who possesses the discernment to find the right action in any given situation.
  • Social and Divine Virtues:
    • Justice: Considered the most complete virtue because it involves relations with others within a community.
    • Philia (Friendship): A high human good where a true friend is viewed as "another self" sharing in the pursuit of the good life.
    • Theoria (Contemplation): The highest form of happiness, involving intellectual contemplation of the rational and divine elements within humans.

The Politics: The Human Community

The Politics shifts the focus from the individual to the community, asking what kind of society makes the "good life" possible.

  • Man as a Political Animal: Aristotle declares man a politikon zōon. This suggests that humans are naturally suited for life in a polis (city-state), and that a fully human life is only possible within such an organized political community.
  • The Purpose of the Polis: The state does not exist merely for survival, but for the sake of the "good life" and the cultivation of virtue in its citizens.
  • Constitutional Classifications: Aristotle classifies governments based on whether they serve the common good (correct) or the interest of the rulers (deviant/corrupted).

  • Polity (Mixed Government): Aristotle’s preferred constitution, which balances elements of oligarchy and democracy and is ruled by a broad middle class to ensure stability.
  • The Role of Education: Education is vital to the state because citizens must be habituated to the specific constitution under which they live. The law and public instruction shape the characters of the people, linking individual virtue directly to the justice of the community.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does Aristotle define "eudaimonia," and how does it differ from a simple feeling?

    • Answer: Eudaimonia is defined as happiness or flourishing. It is not a feeling but an active exercise of the soul in accordance with excellence (arete) over the course of a life.

  2. According to Aristotle, how is human character formed?

    • Answer: Character is formed through habituation and practice. By repeatedly choosing and performing good acts, individuals develop the settled disposition of virtue.

  3. Explain the "Doctrine of the Mean" using the example of courage.

    • Answer: The Doctrine of the Mean states that every virtue is a middle point between an excess and a deficiency. Courage is the mean; recklessness is the vice of excess, and cowardice is the vice of deficiency.

  4. Why does Aristotle describe man as a "political animal" (politikon zōon)?

    • Answer: He means that humans are naturally designed to live in a polis. The political community is the necessary setting for a human to achieve a "fully human life" and the "good life."

  5. What distinguishes a "correct" constitution from a "deviant" one?

    • Answer: A correct constitution (monarchy, aristocracy, or polity) serves the common good of the whole community. A deviant constitution (tyranny, oligarchy, or democracy) serves only the private interests of the rulers.

  6. What is the "highest form of happiness" in the Nicomachean Ethics, and why?

    • Answer: The life of theoria (intellectual contemplation) is the highest happiness because the capacity for rational understanding is considered the most divine element in human beings.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Inseparability of Ethics and Politics: Aristotle argues that the "virtues that make individuals good are the same virtues that make communities just." Analyze how Aristotle uses the concept of habituation and education to link the internal character of a citizen to the external stability of the polis.

  2. The Concept of the Mean in Practical Wisdom: Discuss the role of the phronimos (the practically wise person). How does this individual use discernment to navigate between vices of excess and deficiency, and why is this discernment more effective than following mechanical rules?

  3. The Synthesis of Reason and Faith: While Aristotle focuses on what is achievable through "unaided human reason," Thomas Aquinas later expanded these ideas. Contrast Aristotle’s natural end of eudaimonia with Aquinas’s concept of the visio Dei (direct vision of God). How does the idea that "grace does not destroy nature but perfects it" change the interpretation of Aristotelian virtue?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Arete: Virtue or excellence. It refers to the full excellence of any kind of thing, not just moral goodness (e.g., courage, justice, wisdom).
  • Doctrine of the Mean: The principle that every virtue is a middle point between a vice of excess and a vice of deficiency.
  • Eudaimonia: Happiness or flourishing; the highest human good achieved through the active exercise of the soul’s capacities.
  • Philia: Friendship; characterized as one of the highest human goods where a friend is seen as "another self."
  • Phronimos: The practically wise person who possesses the discernment to find the "mean" or right action in various situations.
  • Polis: The city-state or political community; the basic unit of Greek political life intended to facilitate the good life.
  • Politikon Zōon: "Political animal"; the term used to describe the nature of humans as beings who must live in a community to be fully human.
  • Polity: Aristotle’s preferred "mixed government" involving rule by the many (specifically a broad middle class) for the common good.
  • Theoria: Intellectual contemplation; the highest activity of the human soul.
  • Visio Dei: The "direct vision of God"; identified by Thomas Aquinas as the supernatural ultimate end of human life, surpassing Aristotle’s natural eudaimonia.
