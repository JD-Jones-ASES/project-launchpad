# Study Guide: Tragedy and Its Elements—Aristotle, Castelvetro, and Longinus

This study guide examines the evolution of dramatic theory from ancient Greece through the Renaissance, focusing on the transformation of Aristotle’s descriptive observations into rigid prescriptions by Lodovico Castelvetro and the alternative aesthetic focus of Longinus on the "sublime."

---

## I. Key Concepts

### The Evolution of Aristotle’s *Poetics*
While Aristotle wrote the *Poetics* around 335 BC, its influence was modest in antiquity. It gained passionate intensity during the Renaissance when Italian humanist scholars recovered and debated the text. Two primary lines of reception emerged:
1.  **The Italian Renaissance Commentary Tradition:** This tradition transformed Aristotle’s observations about what Greek tragedies *tended* to do into binding rules for all drama.
2.  **The Ancient Greek Literary Criticism Tradition:** Represented by Longinus, this tradition prioritized the emotional and spiritual effect of tragedy over its structural rules.

### Castelvetro and the Three Unities
Lodovico Castelvetro (1505–1571) was a pivotal figure who codified the "Three Unities" in his 1570 commentary, *Poetica d'Aristotele Vulgarizzata et Sposta*. 

*   **Unity of Action:** The only unity actually prescribed by Aristotle. It requires a play to represent a single, whole action with a beginning, middle, and end. Subplots are permitted if they mirror or illuminate the main action.
*   **Unity of Time:** Aristotle observed that tragedies "endeavor" (using the Greek word *peiratai*, meaning "try" or "tend") to confine themselves to a single revolution of the sun. Castelvetro converted this tendency into a mandate, requiring plays to occur within 12 to 24 hours.
*   **Unity of Place:** Entirely absent from Aristotle’s text. Castelvetro invented this rule, arguing that because a theater audience remains in one place, they cannot believe the stage represents multiple locations.

**Verisimilitude:** The principle driving Castelvetro’s rules. It is the idea that art should appear true to life to maintain the theatrical illusion and prevent the audience from losing their suspension of disbelief.

### Neoclassicism vs. The English Tradition
*   **Neoclassicism:** A 17th- and 18th-century movement (primarily in France) that revived ancient standards. Dramatists like Pierre Corneille and Jean Racine followed the three unities strictly to achieve concentrated, inevitable plots.
*   **The English Tradition:** Dramatists like William Shakespeare generally ignored the unities of time and place. While observing unity of action, Shakespearean plays often spanned years and moved across continents (e.g., *The Winter’s Tale*). Although Philip Sidney defended the unities in *The Defence of Poesy*, the English stage largely rejected them.

### Longinus and the Sublime
Longinus, writing in the first century AD, shifted the focus from the *structure* of a work to its *effect* on the soul.

*   **Hypsos (The Sublime):** A quality of greatness that does not merely persuade but "transports" or produces ecstasy (*ekplexis*). It is described as a "lightning-flash" that illuminates an entire subject in a single moment.
*   **Aesthetic vs. Structural:** Where Aristotle provides a structural explanation of tragedy (catharsis through plot), Longinus provides an aesthetic experience of it (a shattering, overwhelming feeling of greatness).
*   **The Character of the Writer:** Longinus argued that sublimity stems from the power of forming great conceptions, meaning great writing is a reflection of the writer's mind and soul, not just technical skill.

## III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **Observation vs. Prescription:** Analyze the historical transformation of Aristotle’s descriptive observations into Castelvetro’s prescriptive rules. How did this shift change the development of European drama, particularly in the Neoclassical tradition?
2.  **The English Resistance:** Compare the dramatic philosophies of Jean Racine and William Shakespeare. Using the concepts of the Three Unities, discuss why Shakespeare’s "freedom" from these rules was considered a failure by Castelvetrans but a "magnificent" achievement by others.
3.  **Structuralism vs. Aesthetics:** Contrast Aristotle’s structural analysis of tragedy with Longinus’s focus on the sublime. In what ways do these two perspectives complement each other to provide a more complete understanding of great literature?
4.  **The Limits of Rules:** Longinus suggests that "genius" is the master of the sublime, yet he also argues for the necessity of "art" or "scientific method." Discuss the relationship between natural talent and technical rules in the creation of the sublime.

## IV. Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Catharsis:** Aristotle’s term for the purgation or purification of the emotions of pity and fear produced by tragedy.
*   **Ekplexis:** A state of transport or ecstasy produced by encounter with the sublime.
*   **Frigidity:** A fault in writing identified by Longinus, often resulting from over-elaboration or the pursuit of "childish absurdities" and novel thoughts.
*   **Hypsos:** The Greek term for "the sublime"; a quality of loftiness and excellence in language that overwhelms the reader.
*   **Neoclassicism:** A movement in 17th- and 18th-century literature seeking to revive the standards of ancient Greece and Rome, specifically the Three Unities in drama.
*   **Puerility:** A "pedantic habit of mind" that ends in frigidity; a failing of feeble minds aiming for brilliancy but resulting in affectation.
*   **The Three Unities:** A set of dramatic rules (Action, Time, and Place) derived and expanded from Aristotle by Renaissance commentators.
*   **Verisimilitude:** From the Latin *verisimilis* (“resembling truth”); the principle that a play should be believable and true to life to maintain the audience's illusion.
