Useful Fictions: Topic 6.1 — Corporate Persons

Overview

The legal fiction of corporate personhood is one of the most consequential inventions in the history of capitalism — and one of the clearest examples of how a useful fiction can outlive and overshoot its original purpose. Created to solve a practical coordination problem (how do you organize large-scale economic activity across time when the humans involved will die, retire, or change their minds?), the fiction of the corporation as a legal "person" became the financial engine of the industrial economy. Its gradual migration from commercial law into constitutional law — through a court reporter's administrative footnote — produced consequences that remain contested more than a century later. This section uses corporate personhood to introduce the key analytical concepts of legal fictions, limited liability, and the structural danger that useful fictions are modular: once created for one purpose, they can be imported into entirely different domains.

Key Concepts

  • Legal fiction: A fact assumed or created by a court or legislature for legal reasoning purposes, even though it does not correspond to empirical reality. Legal fictions are deliberate tools, not errors — false as biological claims but load-bearing in legal structure.
  • Corporate personhood: The doctrine that a corporation is a legal "person" capable of owning property, entering contracts, suing and being sued, and holding constitutional rights.
  • Limited liability: The rule that shareholders' losses are capped at their investment; corporate creditors cannot pursue shareholders' personal assets. This asymmetry (capped downside, uncapped upside) is described as the financial engine of modern capitalism.
  • The modularity problem: Useful fictions, once institutionalized, can migrate from the domain for which they were designed into adjacent domains — producing results their original architects would have found absurd.

Important Terms

  • Headnote: A court reporter's administrative summary preceding an opinion, with no legal force and not representing the court's holding.
  • Fourteenth Amendment: Ratified 1868 to protect citizenship rights of formerly enslaved people; later applied (via the Santa Clara headnote) to corporate persons.
  • Adverse possession: A legal fiction under which a long-trespassing squatter is "deemed" to own land.
  • Constructive notice: A legal fiction that presumes you know the contents of a public record whether you have read it or not.
  • Citizens United v. FEC (2010): Supreme Court case holding 5–4 that the First Amendment prohibits restrictions on corporate political expenditures.
  • Equal protection: Fourteenth Amendment guarantee that persons receive equal treatment under law — extended to corporations by the Santa Clara headnote doctrine.

Detailed Notes

The Santa Clara Headnote (1886)

  • Santa Clara County v. Southern Pacific Railroad was a dull tax dispute about railroad property assessment in California.
  • Before arguments, Chief Justice Waite made an oral prefatory remark: the Fourteenth Amendment's equal protection clause applies to corporations.
  • The case was decided on other grounds. Court reporter Bancroft Davis included Waite's remark as a headnote — a clerk's administrative note with no legal force.
  • Legal scholars still dispute whether Waite intended what Davis wrote, whether any real decision occurred, or whether the whole thing was a misunderstanding elevated to precedent.
  • The Fourteenth Amendment was ratified in 1868 to protect formerly enslaved people. Its application to railroad corporations through a non-binding note is, as the text puts it, "at minimum, an irony worth noting."

Why Corporations Exist: The Coordination Problem

  • Individual humans die, retire, change commitments, and have heirs with different goals. A business legally coextensive with its founders dissolves when they do.
  • Corporate personhood solves this by creating an artificial person that can own property, enter contracts, incur debts, and maintain perpetual legal identity across generations.
  • Shareholders bear only limited liability: if the corporation fails, creditors pursue corporate assets — not personal assets of shareholders.
  • The downside is capped; the upside is not. This asymmetry induces investors to commit capital to risky ventures at the scale required for industrial enterprise.
  • Without limited liability: no capital accumulation for railroads, pharmaceutical research, or airlines. The corporation is "as indispensable to the industrial economy as double-entry bookkeeping is to the firm."

The Metastasis of Corporate Rights

  • The Santa Clara headnote opened a door. If corporations are persons under the Fourteenth Amendment, they possess equal protection rights — making laws singling them out constitutionally suspect.
  • The doctrine expanded: corporations acquired Fourth Amendment search protections, partial Fifth Amendment protections, and First Amendment speech rights.
  • Citizens United v. FEC (2010): 5–4, Justice Kennedy majority held that restricting corporate political expenditures is unconstitutional censorship of protected speech. The identity of the speaker (biological or juridical) is irrelevant.
  • Justice Stevens' dissent: corporations "have no consciences, no beliefs, no feelings, no thoughts, no desires" and are not members of "We the People."
  • The text's structural diagnosis: this is the modularity problem. A fiction designed for commercial coordination was imported into constitutional law, where its premises (that the "person" has moral standing and democratic membership) do not hold.

The Paradox: Power and Danger in the Same Structure

  • Modern capitalism is unimaginable without corporate personhood and limited liability.
  • Yet the same structure externalizes costs onto workers, communities, and environments — entities with no recourse against a legally insulated entity.
  • The corporation "cannot go to prison. It cannot feel remorse. It is the person who isn't there, collecting the benefits of personhood while evading its full obligations — which is, of course, exactly what it was designed to do."
  • The tension in corporate personhood is the tension in all useful fictions: the structural feature that makes them powerful is the structural feature that makes them dangerous.
