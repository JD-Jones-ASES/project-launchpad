Useful Fictions: The Species Problem

Overview

This section uses the concept of "species" to extend the chapter's central argument — that human categories are sharp lines drawn across gradients that contain no such lines — into biology. Darwin, the man who explained how species form, concluded that "species" was a convenience label rather than a natural kind; over 160 years later, biologists still have no consensus definition, with at least 26 competing concepts in active use. The section surveys four major species definitions, each with serious practical limitations, then turns to ring species and hybridization as vivid demonstrations that speciation is a process unfolding across a gradient rather than an event that flips a binary switch. The conclusion: the species boundary is a line we draw across nature, necessary for science and law, but ours — not nature's.

Key Concepts

  • Darwin's doubt: Darwin spent 23 years preparing On the Origin of Species and emerged from that work convinced that "species" was "one arbitrarily given for the sake of convenience." He dismantled the theological view that God created each species separately with fixed boundaries — and thereby showed that no such boundaries exist.

  • The proliferation of species definitions: Kevin de Queiroz's 1997 review found at least 26 distinct definitions of "species" in active scientific use. All definitions agree that species represent real, distinguishable groupings in nature; they disagree about the criteria by which those groupings' edges are drawn.

  • Biological Species Concept (BSC): Ernst Mayr's 1942 definition — groups of interbreeding populations reproductively isolated from other groups. Clean but deeply limited: it excludes all asexually reproducing organisms (the majority of life on Earth), cannot apply to fossils, and treats reproductive isolation as a binary when it is actually a matter of degree.

  • Ring species: Populations that spread in a ring around a geographic barrier such that adjacent pairs can interbreed, but the two ends that eventually meet cannot. The Ensatina salamanders of California are the textbook case: "same species" at every adjacent step, "different species" at the junction. The category fails to close, showing that the species boundary is a line imposed on a connected gradient.

  • Speciation as process: The hybrid examples (pizzly/grolar bears; ligers) reinforce that populations diverge gradually — there is no moment at which they flip from same to different species, just as there is no moment at which adding one grain makes a heap. The species boundary is drawn, not found.

Important Terms

  • Species: A biological organizing fiction — a group of organisms classified together by one of many possible criteria. There is no universally accepted scientific definition.
  • Biological Species Concept (BSC): Mayr's 1942 definition based on interbreeding and reproductive isolation; the most widely cited but limited to sexual reproducers.
  • Phylogenetic Species Concept: Classification based on unique common ancestry and derived characteristics; more inclusive but produces species proliferation.
  • Morphological Species Concept: Classification by physical form; historically dominant but frequently contradicted by genetic evidence.
  • Ring species: Populations arranged in a geographic ring where adjacent members interbreed but endpoints do not — a natural demonstration of gradient speciation.
  • Reproductive isolation: The inability of two populations to interbreed and produce fertile offspring under natural conditions; the central criterion of the BSC.

Detailed Notes

Darwin's Doubt and the Irony of Evolution

  • Darwin waited 23 years to publish On the Origin of Species (1859), and by publication he called "species" a convenience label, not a natural kind.
  • He spent his career dismantling the theological notion that God created each species separately and eternally with fixed, clear boundaries.
  • The central irony: the man who best explained how species form also proved that the sharp boundaries the concept implies do not exist in nature.

Twenty-Six Definitions and Counting

  • De Queiroz's 1997 review identified at least 26 distinct species definitions; the count has not declined.
  • The four definitions covered in this section:
    • BSC (Mayr, 1942): Interbreeding + reproductive isolation. Fails for asexual organisms (all bacteria, many plants, numerous insects); cannot apply to fossils; reproductive isolation is a matter of degree, not a binary.
    • Phylogenetic Species Concept: Smallest group with unique common ancestor and unique derived characteristic. More inclusive of asexuals, but fractures formerly single species into many — chaos for legal protections, conservation priorities, and textbooks.
    • Ecological Species Concept: Organisms occupying the same ecological niche.
    • Morphological Species Concept: Classification by physical form (the historical standard). Problem: form and genetics frequently disagree — identical-looking beetles may be genetically isolated; dramatically different-looking subspecies may interbreed freely.

Ring Species: The Boundary That Isn't There

  • Ring species form when a population spreads in two arms around a geographic barrier; adjacent pairs can interbreed, but the two ends that eventually meet cannot.
  • Ensatina salamanders: Spread south from Pacific Northwest in two arms (coastal ranges; Sierra Nevada). Adjacent populations interbreed along each arm. But where the arms meet in southern California, the populations are too different to interbreed — they coexist without merging.
  • The troubling question: how many species? Every adjacent pair = same species; the endpoints = different species.
  • Herring gulls around the Arctic show a similar pattern.
  • Conclusion: ring species do not disprove species concepts; they show the category describes a real pattern that occasionally refuses to be neatly bounded.

The Hybrid Problem and Speciation as Process

  • When polar bears and grizzly bears hybridize in the wild (increasingly common with Arctic ice retreat), their fertile offspring (pizzly/grolar bears) suggest incomplete reproductive isolation.
  • Ligers (captive lion-tiger hybrids) exist but not in the wild; biologists say natural conditions, not zoo capacity, determine species status.
  • The deeper point: speciation is a process, not an event. Populations diverge gradually through accumulating genetic, morphological, and behavioral differences.
  • There is no moment at which two populations "flip" from same to different species — precisely parallel to the Sorites heap paradox.
  • The species boundary is a line we draw across the gradient of divergence. Like all such lines, it is necessary for science and law, and ultimately ours.

Review Questions

  1. What did Darwin mean when he called "species" a "term arbitrarily given for the sake of convenience"? How does evolutionary theory support his skepticism?
  2. Describe two limitations of the Biological Species Concept. What type of organism does it exclude entirely, and why?
  3. Explain the ring species phenomenon using the Ensatina salamander example. Why does the text say "the category fails to close"?
  4. How do hybrid examples (pizzly bears, ligers) support the claim that speciation is a process rather than an event?
