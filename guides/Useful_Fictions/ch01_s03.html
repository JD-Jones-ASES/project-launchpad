Blank Slates and Empty Lands: The Fiction of Emptiness as Power

Overview

The "fiction of emptiness" — the declaration that some territory (physical, mental, social, or historical) is blank, void, or unoccupied — is structurally identical to the mathematical invention of zero, but the stakes are incomparably higher. When a mathematician declares a set empty, the worst consequence is a wrong theorem. When a colonial administrator declares a continent empty, the consequences include dispossession and destruction of millions. This section traces the fiction of emptiness from John Locke's tabula rasa (the blank-slate mind) through the legal doctrine of terra nullius (blank land), to Year Zero politics (blank history). In every case, the pattern is the same: declaring something empty is never a neutral observation — it is always a claim about power, and always serves the interests of those doing the declaring.

Key Concepts

  • The Fiction of Emptiness: The political and philosophical move of declaring something blank or unoccupied in order to make it available for control or inscription. It is never descriptive; it is always performative — the act of declaring creates the vacancy it claims to observe.

  • Tabula Rasa (Blank Slate): Locke's theory that the newborn mind is entirely empty, inscribed only by experience. Liberating in its implication that no one is born to a fixed role, but dangerous in its implication that whoever controls the inscription process controls the mind.

  • Terra Nullius: The legal doctrine that land not occupied in ways European law recognized could be treated as belonging to nobody. Applied to Australia for 204 years (1788–1992); overturned by the Mabo decision, which called it "a fiction" — the court's own word.

  • Year Zero: The radical form of the fiction of emptiness applied to history itself — declaring that the past can be reset to nothing. Ranges from the relatively benign (the French Revolutionary calendar) to the genocidal (the Khmer Rouge's 1975 Cambodia).

  • Emptiness by Fiat: The underlying logic connecting all cases: the belief that naming something "nothing" is sufficient to make it nothing, regardless of what actually exists there. The text calls this the logic that "operates at every scale."

Important Terms

  • Tabula rasa: Latin for "blank slate" — Locke's term for the mind at birth, free of all innate content.
  • Innate ideas: The Cartesian doctrine that certain concepts (God, mathematics, logic) are present in the mind from birth, prior to experience.
  • Terra nullius: Latin for "land of no one" — a legal doctrine declaring land unowned and available for colonial claiming when not occupied in European legal terms.
  • Res nullius: Roman legal concept meaning "things belonging to nobody" — the origin of terra nullius.
  • Mission civilisatrice: The French imperial ideology of "civilizing" colonized peoples — premised on the blank-slate idea that non-European minds could be overwritten.
  • Native title: The legal recognition (post-Mabo) that Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples retained rights to land that was never legally extinguished.

Detailed Notes

Locke's Tabula Rasa

  • In An Essay Concerning Human Understanding (1689), Locke argued the newborn mind is an empty wax tablet inscribed entirely by sensation and reflection.
  • This opposed the Cartesian claim that some ideas (God, logic, mathematics) are innate.
  • Liberating implication: No hereditary rank; virtue and intelligence are made by environment; social contract government is possible.
  • Dangerous implication: "Whoever controls the inscription process controls the mind." The Enlightenment's pedagogical optimism and the totalitarian indoctrination impulse are children of the same parent concept.
  • The colonial mission civilisatrice directly applied this logic: minds inscribed by non-European cultures could be declared in need of correction and overwriting.

Terra Nullius and the Emptying of Continents

  • Legal doctrine derived from Roman res nullius; applied by British colonizers to land not occupied in European ways (not enclosed, not cultivated in European patterns, not subject to European-style sovereign authority).
  • Australia (1788): British declared the continent legally empty despite it being home to hundreds of distinct Aboriginal nations with 250+ languages and intricate land management systems.
  • The continent was declared empty not because the people were absent but because British law lacked the conceptual vocabulary to recognize their presence.
  • The doctrine persisted for 204 years.
  • Mabo v. Queensland (No. 2), 1992: High Court of Australia ruled terra nullius "a fiction" (the court's word) and recognized that native title had never been extinguished.
  • Significance: Courts rarely use the word "fiction" to describe their own prior legal foundations.

Year Zero and the Politics of Erasure

  • French Revolutionary Calendar (1793): A relatively benign version — history declared to begin at Year I; months renamed after natural phenomena; the old calendar displaced but not destroyed.
  • Khmer Rouge Year Zero (1975): The murderous extreme. Pol Pot declared Cambodian history erased: cities emptied, currency abolished, schools closed, books burned, educated people killed as carriers of the contaminated past. Result: one of the worst genocides of the twentieth century.
  • Both are "extreme cases of a logic that operates at every scale" — the belief that emptiness can be declared by fiat.

The Core Warning

  • The mathematician's zero enables calculation; the political zero causes harm.
  • Three questions to ask when someone declares something empty: Empty to whom? Empty by whose reckoning? Empty at what cost?
  • The pattern runs at every scale: from Locke's epistemology to colonial law to mass murder. Recognizing the structural identity of these moves is the section's essential lesson.
