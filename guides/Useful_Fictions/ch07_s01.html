All Maps Lie: The Geometry and Politics of Representation

Overview

This section uses cartography as a master case study for a claim that applies to all representations: every map is a lie — not a dishonest lie but a structural one, built into the act of simplification. Gerardus Mercator's 1569 projection, designed to make maritime navigation tractable by rendering rhumb lines as straight lines, introduced severe distortions of area near the poles. Africa is fourteen times larger than Greenland, but they appear comparable on Mercator's map. This is not a flaw in the projection — it is the projection doing exactly what it was built to do. The deeper point, grounded in Carl Friedrich Gauss's Theorema Egregium, is that no flat projection can preserve all geometric properties simultaneously. Every choice of projection is simultaneously a choice about what to misrepresent. The section closes by connecting this geometric necessity to Alfred Korzybski's warning that the implicit choices embedded in a representation travel invisibly when the representation changes contexts — doing unnoticed work on the perceptions of those who use it.

Key Concepts

  • Projection distortion: The unavoidable misrepresentation of at least one geometric property — area, shape, distance, or direction — when a sphere is mapped onto a flat plane.
  • Theorema Egregium: Gauss's theorem proving that a sphere and a flat plane have different Gaussian curvature, making all-property-preserving projection mathematically impossible.
  • The appropriate lie: The idea that the "correct" projection depends entirely on purpose; the right distortion is the one least harmful for the specific use case.
  • The 1:1 map paradox: A perfect representation is indistinguishable from the thing itself and therefore useless. Utility in maps — and in any model — derives from what is deliberately left out.
  • Korzybski's warning: Maps embed implicit choices about which truths to preserve; when used beyond their original purpose, those choices become invisible and do unnoticed work.

Important Terms

  • Rhumb line: A path that crosses all meridians at a constant angle (a constant compass bearing); appears as a straight line on the Mercator projection.
  • Conformal projection: Preserves local angles and shapes; the Mercator projection is conformal.
  • Equal-area projection: Preserves relative area of land masses; the Mollweide and Gall–Peters projections are examples.
  • Compromise projection: Sacrifices accuracy on all dimensions to minimize the worst distortions across all properties; the Robinson and Winkel Tripel projections are examples.
  • Gaussian curvature: A measure of intrinsic curvature; a sphere has positive Gaussian curvature everywhere, a flat plane has zero, which is why they cannot be mapped onto each other without distortion.
  • Gall–Peters projection: An equal-area projection published by James Gall in 1855 and popularized by Arno Peters in 1973 as a political corrective to Mercator.

Detailed Notes

The Mercator Projection: A Navigation Tool Misapplied

  • Gerardus Mercator (1569) solved a real navigational problem: sailors needed rhumb lines to appear as straight lines on a flat chart.
  • The mathematical transformation required to achieve this inflates features near the poles.
  • Greenland (836,000 sq mi) appears as large as Africa (11.7 million sq mi) — a factor-of-fourteen area distortion.
  • The projection was not designed for classroom use, but inertia placed it in schoolrooms for four centuries, training generations of students to misperceive continental scale.

The Theorema Egregium: Why All Maps Must Lie

  • Carl Friedrich Gauss proved in the 19th century that a sphere and a flat plane have fundamentally different curvature.
  • No smooth, bijective mapping between them can preserve area, shape, distance, and direction simultaneously.
  • Every projection therefore chooses which properties to preserve and which to sacrifice.
  • This is not a contingent technological limitation — it is a mathematical theorem.

Projection Types and Their Trade-offs

  • Conformal (Mercator): Preserves angles — useful for navigation; destroys area.
  • Equal-area (Mollweide, Gall–Peters): Preserves relative area — useful for size comparisons; distorts shapes.
  • Compromise (Robinson, Winkel Tripel): Sacrifices all dimensions partially to minimize the worst distortions; optimized for visual palatability.
  • Azimuthal Equidistant: Preserves distances from one center point; distortions increase as you move away from it.

The Peters Controversy: Projections as Political Arguments

  • Arno Peters (1973) promoted his equal-area projection as a correction to Mercator's colonial distortions.
  • Peters argued Mercator made the Global South look small, encoding an imperial center-periphery relationship into Western visual culture.
  • The American Cartographic Association (1989) condemned all rectangular world projections — Mercator and Peters alike.
  • Gall–Peters introduces compensating distortions: nations near the equator appear vertically stretched; polar nations are compressed.
  • Peters did not escape the Theorema Egregium; he chose different lies.

The 1:1 Map: Why Usefulness Requires Distortion

  • Jorge Luis Borges ("On Exactitude in Science"): cartographers of an empire build a map at 1:1 scale — it is useless, deteriorating in the desert.
  • Lewis Carroll independently made the same point: a map at one mile to the mile would cover the whole country and shut out sunlight.
  • The utility of a map derives precisely from its simplifications — the omissions and compressions that make it smaller and more manipulable than the territory.
  • A map that preserves everything is not a map; it is a second territory.

Korzybski's Warning: Implicit Choices Travel Across Contexts

  • The map-territory distinction is not merely the trivial observation that symbols are not things.
  • Every map embeds a choice about which truths to preserve and which to sacrifice.
  • When a representation designed for one purpose (navigation) is used for another (education), its implicit choices travel with it — invisible, unquestioned, doing their work.
  • The same structural point applies beyond cartography: economic models, diagnostic categories, and any representation that simplifies a complex reality all carry embedded choices that become dangerous when forgotten.
