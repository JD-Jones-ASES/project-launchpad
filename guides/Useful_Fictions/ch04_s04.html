Useful Fictions: Thresholds of Personhood

Overview

This section is both the final case study in Chapter 4 and the chapter's synthesis. It applies the same argument made for color, species, and geographic borders to the domain of human personhood and legal status: the law imposes sharp numeric thresholds on continuous biological, economic, and behavioral gradients that contain no such breaks. Three major threshold families are examined — the age of majority, the poverty line, and biological measures used in criminal law (BAC and IQ) — each showing how a line chosen for administrative convenience creates enormous categorical differences between people who are, by every physical measure, nearly identical. The section closes by articulating both why sharp lines are necessary (administrative systems require binary outputs; case-by-case discretion favors the privileged) and what is dangerous about forgetting they are drawn rather than found: they stop being questioned, updated, or contested — and their violence becomes invisible.

Key Concepts

  • Age of majority as fiction: Every nation draws a different line — South Korea at 19, Japan at 20 (revised to 18 in 2022), Iran at 9 (girls) and 15 (boys) — for the same underlying question about continuous human development. The prefrontal cortex (impulse control, long-term planning) develops into the mid-twenties. The threshold is a social and administrative choice, not a biological discovery.

  • The poverty line: Calculated in the 1960s by Mollie Orshansky as food budget × 3, adjusted only for inflation since. The 2023 line ($14,580 for a single individual) gates access to Medicaid, SNAP, CHIP, the EITC, housing, and school meals. It now measures something considerably less than contemporary poverty because housing, healthcare, and childcare have grown far faster than food costs.

  • Benefits cliff: The sharp eligibility cutoff means crossing the poverty threshold by one dollar can trigger loss of thousands of dollars in benefits. The person at $14,579 and the person at $14,581 are materially identical; the law treats them as belonging to different categories.

  • BAC and IQ thresholds in criminal law: The 0.08% BAC limit is not a scientific discovery — the physiological difference between 0.079% and 0.080% is undetectable. The 0.10% limit was changed to 0.08% in 2000 through federal funding pressure, not neuroscience. The IQ 70 cutoff for intellectual disability (from Atkins v. Virginia, 2002) produces the extreme case: a defendant with IQ 71 can be executed while one with IQ 69 cannot. The Flynn effect makes this worse — scores rise ~3 points per decade, tests are re-normed, so the threshold stays fixed while what is on either side keeps moving.

  • Necessity vs. violence of sharp lines: Sharp lines are necessary because administrative systems require binary outputs and because individualized assessment is inconsistent, expensive, and biased toward the privileged. The danger is not that sharp lines exist but that we forget they are drawn — which causes us to stop questioning whose interests they serve, who they misclassify, and when they need to be redrawn.

Important Terms

  • Age of majority: The legal threshold at which a person is recognized as an adult, varying across jurisdictions from 9 to 20.
  • Poverty line: An administrative income threshold determining eligibility for government assistance programs; originally Orshansky's food budget × 3.
  • Benefits cliff: The abrupt loss of substantial benefits when income crosses a threshold by a marginal amount.
  • BAC (blood alcohol concentration): The measure of alcohol in the bloodstream; the legal US driving threshold is 0.08%.
  • Flynn effect: The documented ~3-point-per-decade rise in average IQ scores across the industrialized world, requiring periodic test re-norming.
  • Atkins v. Virginia (2002): Supreme Court ruling that executing intellectually disabled defendants violates the Eighth Amendment; made IQ 70 a legally operative threshold.
  • Benefits cliff: The abrupt loss of substantial benefits when income crosses a threshold by a marginal amount.

Detailed Notes

Adulthood: The Sharp Line Across Human Development

  • The gap between the last day before and the first day of adulthood contains no measurable change in the person — yet legal rights and obligations change completely.
  • Different nations draw different lines: South Korea 19; Japan 20 → 18 (2022); Iran 9/15; UK 16 for military, 18 for voting.
  • The age of criminal responsibility varies from 7 (some US states, Nigeria) to 18 (Belgium, Brazil, Colombia).
  • Neuroscience does not contain a threshold: the prefrontal cortex, governing impulse control and long-term planning, develops through the mid-twenties.
  • Choosing a cutoff age is a social and moral decision, not a biological discovery.

The Poverty Line: An Administrative Convenience Masquerading as Fact

  • 2023 federal poverty line: $14,580/year for a single individual. $14,579 = "in poverty"; $14,581 = "not in poverty."
  • The line gates: Medicaid, SNAP, CHIP, EITC, subsidized housing, reduced-price school meals, and dozens more programs.
  • Mollie Orshansky (SSA, 1960s): food budget × 3 (because poor families spent ~1/3 of income on food).
  • Has been inflation-adjusted every year since — but never fundamentally recalculated.
  • Problem: housing, healthcare, and childcare have grown far faster than food costs. The contemporary line measures something considerably less than contemporary poverty.
  • Benefits cliff: in many states, crossing the poverty line by $1 triggers loss of thousands of dollars in benefits — a policy design problem, not a bug in the threshold itself, but illustrative of what binary eligibility creates.

BAC and IQ: Thresholds with Life-or-Death Consequences

  • BAC 0.08%:
    • 0.079% = legally sober; 0.080% = legally drunk.
    • The cognitive/physiological difference is not detectable by any performance test.
    • Alcohol impairment is real; the precise location of the legal threshold is not a scientific discovery.
    • The US limit changed from 0.10% to 0.08% in 2000 via federal highway funding pressure — not new neuroscience.
  • IQ 70 (death penalty):
    • Atkins v. Virginia (2002): executing intellectually disabled defendants violates the Eighth Amendment.
    • Cutoff: IQ below 70 + adaptive behavior limitations.
    • IQ 71 = can be executed; IQ 69 = cannot be executed.
    • Flynn effect: IQ scores rise ~3 pts/decade; tests are re-normed to keep the mean at 100.
    • Result: "score of 70" means different actual performance in different test generations. The line is fixed; what is on either side of it keeps moving.

The Necessity and Violence of Sharp Lines

  • Section structure: continuous reality / discrete language runs through all four sections of Chapter 4.
  • Why sharp lines cannot simply be replaced by nuanced continuous assessment:
    • Administrative systems require binary outputs (eligible or not).
    • Individualized expert assessment is inconsistent, expensive, and favors those with resources and better advocates.
    • A uniform sharp line has fairness properties that case-by-case discretion does not.
  • The danger: when we forget the line is drawn rather than found:
    • We stop asking: whose interests does it serve? Who is systematically misclassified? When does it need updating?
    • We enforce the line with certainty appropriate only to natural law.
    • The violence: insisting that the gradient contains a break it does not — treating nearly identical people as categorically different.

  2. Describe Mollie Orshansky's original calculation of the poverty line. Why does the section argue the contemporary line measures 'something considerably less than contemporary poverty'?
