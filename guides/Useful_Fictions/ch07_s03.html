Metaphor as Map: How Conceptual Metaphors Structure Thought

Overview

This section overturns the traditional view of metaphor as a decorative literary device. Drawing on George Lakoff and Mark Johnson's 1980 book Metaphors We Live By, it argues that metaphor is not ornament added to thought but the primary cognitive mechanism through which we understand abstract domains. We understand time, argument, the mind, and life not by direct perception but by mapping them onto concrete source domains we can perceive: money, warfare, containers, journeys. These mappings — conceptual metaphors — are pre-reflective and largely invisible. They function exactly like maps: enabling certain kinds of reasoning while hiding others, carrying implicit choices about what to preserve and what to distort, and doing their ideological work most powerfully when their constructed nature goes unnoticed. The section examines three central examples — ARGUMENT IS WAR, TIME IS MONEY, and LIFE IS A JOURNEY — and connects this cognitive linguistics back to Korzybski's map-territory warning and Chapter 6's narrative theory.

Key Concepts

  • Conceptual metaphor: A systematic mapping from a concrete source domain onto an abstract target domain, operating pre-reflectively and structuring how the target domain is understood, reasoned about, and acted within.
  • Source domain: The concrete, perceptible domain from which a conceptual metaphor borrows its structure (e.g., warfare, monetary exchange, journeys).
  • Target domain: The abstract domain being understood through the metaphor's mapping (e.g., argument, time, life).
  • Metaphor as cognitive infrastructure: Lakoff and Johnson's claim that conceptual metaphors are not decorative additions to completed thoughts but the foundational structures on which abstract thinking runs.
  • The hidden aspects problem: Every conceptual metaphor makes certain features of the target domain salient while hiding others that do not fit the source domain structure.

Important Terms

  • ARGUMENT IS WAR: The conceptual metaphor structuring English speakers' understanding of argument — attack positions, defend claims, demolish theories, win debates. Hides argument as collaborative inquiry.
  • TIME IS MONEY: The conceptual metaphor mapping monetary exchange onto temporal experience — spending, wasting, saving, budgeting time. Encodes industrial-economic assumptions about efficiency as the universal metric.
  • LIFE IS A JOURNEY: The conceptual metaphor structuring Western life narratives — destinations, paths, crossroads, arrival. Hides the retroactive, constructed nature of narrative coherence.
  • Sapir-Whorf hypothesis: The claim that vocabulary shapes perceptual categories; Lakoff and Johnson extend this from vocabulary to conceptual structure.
  • Pre-reflective: Operating below conscious awareness; conceptual metaphors are active before and without deliberate choice by the speaker.

Detailed Notes

The Traditional View Inverted

  • Common assumption: metaphor is a literary device — you have the idea first in literal form, then add a metaphor if you want to make it vivid.
  • Lakoff and Johnson (1980): this is precisely backwards. Metaphor is thought, not decoration.
  • We understand abstract domains (time, argument, morality, the mind) by mapping them onto concrete ones (space, physical conflict, cleanliness, containers).
  • The metaphors are cognitive infrastructure, not optional ornaments.

ARGUMENT IS WAR: Structure and Blind Spots

  • English vocabulary for argument is systematically military: attack positions, defend claims, shoot down objections, demolish theories, win or lose.
  • This is not poetic excess — it reflects a systematic mapping from warfare onto rational discourse.
  • What the mapping makes salient: opposition, defeat of the other side, winning.
  • What the mapping hides: argument as collaborative inquiry, as mutual refinement, as joint truth-seeking.
  • Counterfactual (ARGUMENT IS DANCE): coordination with a partner, finding a rhythm, arriving at synthesis neither could reach alone. A "good argument" would be elegant and mutually responsive; "winning" would be a category that doesn't apply.

TIME IS MONEY: Ideological Content of a Metaphor

  • English treats time as something you spend, waste, save, invest, budget, run out of, or squander.
  • The mapping presupposes: time is a finite resource; efficiency is the appropriate metric; idle time is waste.
  • Historical connection: medieval Venetian merchants began treating time as tradeable against money — renting labor by the hour, charging interest on delay. Double-entry bookkeeping made the metaphor material.
  • What the mapping hides: other cultures organize temporal experience around natural cycles, relational obligations, or contemplative states. The money framing is one cultural orientation among many, not a universal truth.
  • The mapping "makes time management feel like an obvious practical problem rather than a particular cultural orientation."

The Sapir-Whorf Extension: From Words to Mappings

  • Original Sapir-Whorf question: does having a word for a color category affect how you perceive color?
  • Lakoff and Johnson's extension: does the entire metaphorical mapping through which a domain is understood determine what can and cannot be thought about it?
  • Evidence: subjects primed with a crime-as-beast metaphor recommended more punitive policies than those primed with crime-as-virus, even with identical factual information.
  • Conclusion: "The metaphor was not describing a prior view; it was generating one."
  • The metaphor also affects memory and judgment, not just language production.

LIFE IS A JOURNEY: Agency, Narrative, and Retroactive Construction

  • The journey metaphor structures how most modern Westerners understand what a life should be: destination (goals), path (career stages), crossroads (decisions), arrival (fulfillment).
  • The metaphor implies agency: the traveler's choices determine where they end up.
  • What it hides: lives are recursive, cyclical, multidimensional — not linear progressions from start to end.
  • The map of a life is drawn after the territory has been lived. Narrative coherence is imposed on experience, not found within it.
  • Connection to Chapter 6: the "traveler" self is already a fiction. We draw the map as we go, then remember it as discovery — construction experienced as exploration.
