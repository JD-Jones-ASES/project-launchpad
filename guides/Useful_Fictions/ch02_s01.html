The Art of Leaving Things Out: Why All Models Are Wrong and Some Are Useful

Overview

This section opens Chapter 2 of Useful Fictions with the central puzzle of scientific modeling: why do deliberately false descriptions of reality generate genuine understanding? Using the famous "spherical cow" joke from physics — in which a physicist solves a dairy problem with a solution that only applies to spherical cows in a vacuum — the section argues that radical simplification is not a failure of scientific imagination but its most essential technique. Drawing on statistician George Box's dictum ("all models are wrong, but some are useful"), the section establishes the chapter's core thesis: the art of leaving things out is the method of science, and understanding begins with the purposeful lie.

Key Concepts

  • Box's Dictum: "All models are wrong, but some are useful." A precise technical claim: because all models omit things, they all fail to describe reality with perfect accuracy. The productive question is not whether a model is wrong but how wrong it is, and whether the ways it is wrong matter for the purpose at hand.
  • Model vs. Description: A description aims for completeness — trying to say everything true about a system. A model aims for leverage — capturing only what matters for a specific question in simple enough form to reason productively. The spherical cow is not a failed description; it is a successful model of a particular kind.
  • Strategic Omission: What you leave out of a model is the source of its power, not incidental to it. A model that included everything would be as complex as the thing it was trying to explain and therefore provide no explanatory advantage.
  • Deliberate Subtraction: The spherical cow is contrasted with the invention of zero (Chapter 1): zero was a deliberate addition to our conceptual toolkit; the spherical cow is a deliberate subtraction of features from a real entity. Both are purposeful intellectual constructions in service of understanding.
  • The Idealization Scaffold: Understanding begins with the simplest case (the spherical cow), then complications are reintroduced one at a time, with analysts watching at each step how conclusions change. The cow with legs comes after the spherical cow.

Important Terms

  • Model: A purposeful simplification of reality designed to answer a specific question. Succeeds by omitting what doesn't matter for that question.
  • Idealization: A deliberate departure from reality that isolates a causal relationship by removing complicating factors.
  • Explanatory Leverage: The ability to reason productively about a system by working with its simplified form. A perfectly complete description has no leverage because it is as complex as the thing being described.
  • Strategic Falseness: The deliberate use of claims known to be false (spherical cows, frictionless planes, ideal gases) as tools that generate genuine understanding — understanding the true description cannot always provide.
  • Failed Description: A description that aims for completeness but falls short. Distinct from a model, which never aimed for completeness in the first place.

Detailed Notes

The Spherical Cow and Its Lesson

  • The physicist in the joke proposes a solution that "works perfectly" but "only applies to spherical cows in a vacuum" — stripping away legs, ears, metabolic complexity, and behavioral variation.
  • The joke is funny because it is true: physicists really do routinely simplify objects to apparent absurdity, and this approach works.
  • The extraordinary claim: the stripped-down, impossible object occasionally tells you something the real, complicated object cannot.

Box's Dictum: Being Wrong Productively

  • George E. P. Box (1919–2013) argued that a model is by definition a simplification, and simplifications omit things. Omit anything and the model fails to describe reality with perfect accuracy — so all models are wrong.
  • Box was not being self-deprecating. He was shifting attention from the question "is this model true?" to "does the way this model is wrong matter for what I'm trying to do?"
  • This runs against a deep and largely unexamined intuition: that descriptions should be complete, that leaving things out is cheating. Box inverts the intuition: leaving things out is the method.

Models vs. Descriptions: The Road Map

  • A road map of a city deliberately omits tree locations, building colors, and traffic light schedules. This is not a failure — it is a design decision.
  • The map works precisely because it does not try to be the city. It answers one question: how do I get from here to there?
  • Borges's imagined perfect map — the size of the empire it depicted — would be complete and perfectly useless. A model must be simpler than the territory to provide any leverage.

Strategic Lies and Their Uses

  • A model is, in a sense, a lie: a spherical cow is not round, an ideal gas molecule has zero volume, a frictionless plane has no friction. Every claim is false as a description of physical reality.
  • These false claims are made deliberately because they are more useful than the true ones: they strip away irrelevant complexity, isolating the features that actually drive the system's behavior.
  • The history of science is substantially the history of strategically untrue descriptions that generated genuine understanding — understanding the true description could not provide.

The Idealization Scaffold

  • Fictional simplifications of science are techniques, not embarrassments. They are how you build a ladder from phenomena to understanding.
  • The process: understand the simple case (spherical cow), then the slightly less simple case, then gradually add back the complications that were strategically dropped — watching at each step how conclusions change.
  • "The spherical cow comes first. The cow with legs comes later. Understanding begins with the lie."

Review Questions

  1. Explain Box's dictum in your own words. Why does he consider it a precise technical claim rather than a mere expression of intellectual modesty?
  2. What is the difference between a failed description and a model? Use the road map example to illustrate your answer.
  3. Why would Borges's "perfect map" — a map the size of the empire it depicted — be useless? What does this reveal about the relationship between completeness and usefulness?
  4. The chapter says the spherical cow is "a deliberate subtraction" contrasted with zero as "a deliberate addition." What philosophical point does this contrast make about useful fictions in general?
  5. Identify a model you use in everyday life (a schedule, a budget, a simplified map). What does it leave out, and why are those omissions features rather than flaws?
