Occam's Dangerous Razor: When Simplicity Helps and When It Harms

Overview

This section takes up the most famous principle of scientific method — Occam's Razor, the preference for simpler explanations — and subjects it to the same critical analysis that the chapter applies to other useful fictions. The razor has genuine triumphs: the Copernican displacement of Ptolemaic epicycles, Lavoisier's oxygen theory over phlogiston, Einstein's relativity over the luminiferous ether. But it also has spectacular failures: the geological establishment used an implicit razor argument to reject Alfred Wegener's continental drift theory, preferring simpler mechanisms over accurate reality for half a century. Modern machine learning formalizes the tension mathematically as the bias-variance tradeoff, revealing that parsimony is not a goal but a constraint — an argument against gratuitous complexity, not a prescription for minimalism. The razor is not a self-applying algorithm; it is a question that requires judgment no principle can substitute for.

Key Concepts

  • Occam's Razor: Among competing explanations, prefer the simpler one. Do not multiply entities beyond necessity. Cuts unnecessary complexity. Originally a theological principle of William of Ockham (c. 1320); migrated into scientific practice during the Scientific Revolution.
  • Gratuitous vs. Necessary Complexity: The razor correctly eliminates gratuitous complexity — entities or mechanisms not demanded by evidence (Ptolemaic epicycles, phlogiston). It incorrectly eliminates necessary complexity — entities demanded by evidence that the simpler theory cannot explain (plate tectonics). The distinction requires judgment.
  • Bias-Variance Tradeoff: The mathematical formalization of parsimony. Too-simple models underfit (high bias); too-complex models overfit (high variance). The optimal model minimizes their sum — captures real signal without absorbing noise.
  • Underfitting (High Bias): A model too simple to capture genuine structure in the data; systematically wrong because its assumptions are too strong.
  • Overfitting (High Variance): A model too complex, memorizing noise in training data; performs perfectly on known cases and fails on new ones.

Important Terms

  • Occam's Razor: The epistemological principle that entities should not be multiplied beyond necessity — prefer simpler explanations when they account for the evidence equally well.
  • Epicycle: A circle riding on a circle, used in the Ptolemaic system to account for the apparent retrograde motion of planets. Added repeatedly as observations revealed discrepancies — the paradigm of gratuitous complexity.
  • Pangaea: Wegener's proposed single supercontinent that later split apart. Vindicated by plate tectonics in the 1960s, half a century after his death.
  • Plate Tectonics: The mechanism of large-scale crustal motion that vindicated Wegener's continental drift theory. What the geological establishment called gratuitous complexity turned out to be necessary reality.
  • Parsimony: The virtue of explaining a phenomenon with the fewest necessary entities or assumptions. Related to, but distinct from, minimalism: parsimony is about not adding entities that don't earn their predictive keep.

Detailed Notes

The Principle and Its Origins

  • William of Ockham (c. 1287–1347) was a fourteenth-century English friar. His Latin formulation — entia non sunt multiplicanda praeter necessitatem — was embedded in a theological argument, not a scientific method. It migrated into science during the Scientific Revolution.
  • Newton: nature does not "affect the pomp of superfluous causes." Einstein: everything should be made as simple as possible, but not simpler. Both express the razor's spirit without the theological baggage.

When the Razor Works Brilliantly: Three Cases

  • Copernicus over Ptolemy: The Ptolemaic system accumulated epicycles (circles on circles) to account for planetary retrograde motion. By the time Copernicus wrote, it was a "baroque clockwork" of epicycles, equants, and deferents — fitting almost any data because it had so many adjustable parameters. Copernicus's heliocentric model made retrograde motion a geometric consequence of Earth's own orbital motion: one conceptual move eliminated dozens of epicycles.
  • Oxygen over phlogiston: Phlogiston had no other observable properties and some formulations gave it negative weight. Lavoisier's oxygen theory required no such entity and fit the combustion data better.
  • Relativity over ether: The luminiferous ether required increasingly elaborate properties as experiments failed to detect it. Special relativity required no medium at all. The simpler story was the truer one.

When the Razor Fails Catastrophically: Continental Drift

  • Alfred Wegener (1880–1930) proposed Pangaea and continental drift with suggestive evidence: matching coastlines, identical fossils across the Atlantic, continuous geological formations.
  • He lacked a plausible mechanism. The geological establishment preferred the fixed-continent theory with sunken land bridges — fewer new mechanisms, therefore simpler.
  • The simpler theory was wrong. In the 1960s, paleomagnetism revealed seafloor spreading, and plate tectonics was discovered. Wegener was vindicated half a century after his death.
  • The diagnosis: The geological establishment misapplied parsimony. They preferred a simpler mechanism over a more accurate description of reality. Plate tectonics was not gratuitous complexity — it was necessary complexity, demanded by evidence the simpler theory could not explain.

The Mathematical Razor: Bias-Variance Tradeoff

  • Machine learning formalizes the razor. Two failure modes:
    • Underfitting (high bias): model too simple, assumptions too strong, misses genuine structure. Human analogy: "all politicians are corrupt" — stereotyping.
    • Overfitting (high variance): model too complex, memorizes noise, fails on new data. Human analogy: superstition, refusing to generalize from experience.
  • The optimal model minimizes the sum of bias and variance: not the simplest possible, not the most complex — the level of complexity that captures real signal without absorbing noise.
  • Implication: parsimony is not "simpler is always better." It is "do not add complexity that doesn't earn its predictive keep."

The Razor Properly Understood

  • Occam's Razor is not a self-applying algorithm. "Simpler" is not always obvious; the judgment of what counts as necessary versus gratuitous complexity depends on background assumptions that may themselves be wrong.
  • Every generation of scientists has made parsimony calls that embedded the assumptions of their time.
  • The razor is a question: "What does this added complexity actually explain that the simpler story cannot?" Answering it well requires exactly the kind of judgment no principle can substitute for.
  • Warning from the Specimen box: "The razor cuts in both directions. It can eliminate unnecessary complexity, or it can eliminate necessary reality."

Review Questions

  1. Trace Occam's Razor from its original theological context to its role in the Scientific Revolution. What changed about its application, and what stayed the same?
  2. What made the Ptolemaic system's epicycles "gratuitous complexity"? How did Copernicus's heliocentric model cut them away?
  3. Why is the geological establishment's rejection of continental drift described as a misapplication of parsimony? What distinguishes gratuitous from necessary complexity?
  4. Explain the bias-variance tradeoff. What are the two failure modes it describes, and what does it imply about "prefer simpler models" as a general principle?
  5. The text says the razor is "a question, not an algorithm." What is that question, and why does answering it require judgment that no principle can provide?
