The Scientific Method (Is Not a Method): Science as a Self-Revising Game

Overview

The laminated classroom poster showing six numbered steps of the scientific method is perhaps the most successful piece of institutional mythology in education. It is also not how science works. This section surveys four major philosophical accounts of scientific method — Bacon's inductivism, Popper's falsificationism, Kuhn's paradigm theory, and Feyerabend's epistemological anarchism — and argues that each demolishes the poster in a different way. What all four converge on is this: science is not best understood as a method but as a culture, a form of life in which rules are provisional, anomalies are prizes, and the highest virtue is to be proven wrong in a way that generates something new.

Key Concepts

  • Inductivism (Bacon): Scientific knowledge is built from accumulated observations; the general law emerges from the accumulation. Hume's problem of induction permanently undermined this.
  • Problem of Induction (Hume): No finite observations logically justify a universal conclusion — the black swan demonstrates the permanent gap.
  • Falsificationism (Popper): A hypothesis is scientific only if falsifiable. Science proceeds by attempting to refute, not confirm.
  • Paradigm (Kuhn): A shared framework defining what counts as a good question, answer, and problem. Normal science is puzzle-solving within the paradigm.
  • Paradigm Shift: Revolutionary reorganization after anomalies exceed what normal puzzle-solving can contain. After a shift, the questions — not just answers — change.
  • Epistemological Anarchism (Feyerabend): 'Anything goes' — every proposed rule has been violated at crucial moments by the scientists who made the key discoveries.

Important Terms

  • Novum Organum: Bacon's 1620 work proposing a systematic inductive method.
  • Problem of Induction: Hume's demonstration that no finite observations justify a universal conclusion — the permanent gap between 'some' and 'all.'
  • Falsifiable: A hypothesis is falsifiable if possible observations could show it false — Popper's criterion for science vs. pseudo-science.
  • Normal Science: Kuhn's term for routine puzzle-solving within a paradigm, where anomalies are set aside.
  • Anomaly: An observation the paradigm cannot explain; accumulated anomalies trigger crisis.
  • Epistemological Anarchism: Feyerabend's 'anything goes' — no proposed rule has escaped violation at key moments in scientific history.

Detailed Notes

Bacon, Hume, and the Failure of Induction

  • Bacon: science proceeds by patient observation; collect enough cases and the general law emerges. This is the model behind the poster.
  • Hume's counter (1739): ten thousand white swans do not logically justify the conclusion that all swans are white — one black swan refutes it. The gap between 'some' and 'all' is permanent and unbridgeable by adding observations.

Popper's Falsificationism — and Its Limits

  • Science does not prove, it fails to disprove. A hypothesis is scientific only if falsifiable — if it specifies what would count as evidence against it. This explains why null results are valuable and theories that explain everything are worthless.
  • The Vulcan problem: when Mercury's perihelion violated Newton, physicists posited an unseen planet rather than abandoning the framework. Real science maintains productive paradigms against single anomalies, violating strict falsificationism.

Kuhn's Paradigms and Feyerabend's Anarchy

  • Normal science: puzzle-solving within a paradigm. Anomalies are absorbed or set aside. Crisis develops when anomalies accumulate beyond containment.
  • Paradigm shift: the Copernican Revolution was not a logical event but a social one — the geocentric model was not definitively refuted; the heliocentric model offered more elegant puzzle-solving; Galileo made it rhetorically irresistible.
  • After a shift, questions and standards change — not just answers. The new paradigm is a different game with different rules.
  • Feyerabend: every proposed methodological rule has been violated at crucial moments by the scientists who made the discoveries. Galileo, Newton, Einstein each violated the rules of their time. The rules were reverse-engineered from the discoveries.

Science as Self-Revising Game

  • What distinguishes science from philosophy, theology, and law — all of which produce systematic knowledge — is that science regularly rewrites its own rules.
  • After each revolution, not just the answers but the questions and criteria for good answers change.
  • The culture of science: rules are provisional; anomalies are the most interesting things; the highest virtue is to be proven wrong in a way that generates something new.
  • The secret of science's power is not the poster. It is this culture — a form of life, as Wittgenstein would say — that accounts for rovers on Mars, sequenced genomes, and eradicated smallpox.
