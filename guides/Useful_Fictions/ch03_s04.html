The Invisible Hand: Karma, Fate, and History's Arc

Overview

This section examines teleological history — the belief that history has a direction, a destination, and a plot. It appears across virtually every civilization, from karma and Providence to Manifest Destiny and Marxist materialism. The section identifies the common psychological function these systems serve (making suffering bearable by embedding it in a larger story), their shared structure (an implicit mechanism driving history toward a goal), and their distinctive danger: when the destination is fixed and inevitable, any action that accelerates arrival can be justified. It closes with a genuinely difficult question: does the truth of teleological beliefs matter if they make people better equipped to act well?

Key Concepts

  • Teleological history: The belief that history has a direction, purpose, or destination — that events are not random noise but episodes in a story with a known or knowable ending. It appears across religious and secular traditions as one of civilization's most persistent organizing fictions.

  • The analgesic function (James): Psychologist William James observed the human mind's peculiar intolerance for meaninglessness. Teleological fictions convert unbearable pointless suffering into endurable suffering serving an unseen larger purpose — they function as psychological pain relief for the experience of unjust loss.

  • Historical inevitability and its danger: When a teleological system claims its destination is not just desirable but inevitable (inscribed in the logic of history or the will of God), it removes the moral brake from actions taken "in history's name." If the destination is fixed, any acceleration can be justified, however brutal.

  • Whig history: Herbert Butterfield's term for reading history backward from the present, tracing a triumphant narrative of Progress toward where we are now. It makes history useless as a guide because it can only see what confirmed the predetermined conclusion.

  • Productive and lethal in proportion to the same quality: The unconditional confidence that teleological fictions convey — certainty about where the story ends — is both what sustains resistance movements through defeat and what authorizes atrocities as necessary historical steps.

Important Terms

  • Teleology: Attribution of purpose or end-directedness to a process — in historical teleology, applied to the whole of human history.
  • Theodicy: The problem of reconciling a just God with unjust suffering. Teleological history is one response.
  • Karma: Hindu and Buddhist concept that actions have morally ordered consequences across lifetimes; implies agency, not pure fatalism.
  • Manifest Destiny: The doctrine that American expansion was divine fulfillment, not conquest.
  • Whig history: Butterfield's term for backward-reading history that traces a triumphant Progress narrative to the present.

Detailed Notes

The Opening Question

  • "The arc of the moral universe bends toward justice" — originated with Theodore Parker (1853), popularized by MLK Jr.
  • The text's challenge: How does anyone know the universe has an arc? No empirical mechanism is identified. The moral universe is under no obligation to go anywhere. The line is a wish in the grammatical form of a fact.

The Landscape of Teleological History

  • Providential history: God writes the story; all episodes — suffering and success alike — signify divine attention. Puritan settlers exemplified it.
  • Karma: morally ordered universe at the individual level; not pure fatalism because current choices shape future circumstances.
  • All teleological systems require a mechanism (God, karma, dialectical materialism) and serve the same functions: providing meaning, justifying suffering, motivating action, naturalizing or delegitimizing existing arrangements.

Three Case Studies

  • Manifest Destiny: expansion framed as fulfillment, not conquest. Dispossession of indigenous peoples and Mexican annexation became "the arc bending" — removed from ordinary ethical evaluation. Teleology provided "a conscience-salving narrative that made aggression feel like destiny."
  • Whig history (Butterfield, 1931): reading history backwards from the liberal democratic present; only confirming evidence is visible; makes history useless as a guide.
  • Marxist historical materialism: communist end-state not just desirable but inevitable, inscribed in economic logic. The danger: if the destination is fixed, any action that accelerates it can be justified, however brutal.

Why the Fiction Persists and Its Dual Edge

  • William James: the human mind has a peculiar intolerance for meaninglessness. The teleological fiction as analgesic: converts unbearable pointless evil into evil serving an unseen larger good.
  • The Holocaust test: the Jewish theological tradition intensified (not ended) its engagement with theodicy after Auschwitz. Almost nobody concluded the six million dead were "statistically predictable consequences of industrialized tribalism with no further meaning." "That conclusion is, perhaps, the accurate one. It is also almost unbearable."
  • Productive and lethal in proportion to the same quality — unconditional confidence about where the story ends: sustained the civil rights movement through decades of defeat; justified the Gulag as a necessary step toward an inevitable destination.
  • The "Think About It" challenge: if the arc claim is unfalsifiable, is it a description of the world or a declaration of how one wants to feel? Is there a meaningful difference between a universe that actually bends toward justice and one in which believers act more justly because they believe it does?
