Useful Fictions: Topic 6.3 — Gods, Spirits, and Ancestors

Overview

The oldest useful fictions are religious ones — and they may also be the most cognitively fundamental. This section examines gods not as metaphysical claims to be affirmed or denied, but as social technologies: what they do, how they work, and why they have persisted for millennia across virtually every human culture. Three analytical frameworks are brought to bear: Durkheim's sociological account (the sacred is the social — worship is society experiencing its own collective force as transcendent), Dennett's cognitive account (the intentional stance and hyperactive agent detection make god-like thinking a default mode of human cognition), and the Big God hypothesis (Norenzayan et al.) which explains how large-scale societies scaled cooperative enforcement beyond the reach of direct reputation networks. The central claim is that whether or not gods exist, they function — and their persistence is explained by that function, not by metaphysical facts we cannot settle.

Key Concepts

  • Gods as social technology: Gods perform identifiable social functions — moral enforcement, community bonding, personification of impersonal forces — that explain their persistence independently of their metaphysical status.
  • Collective effervescence (Durkheim): The state of heightened emotional intensity produced by shared ritual, in which individuals feel merged into something larger. Durkheim argues this force is the collective itself, experienced as divine.
  • The intentional stance (Dennett): The cognitive strategy of treating complex systems as if they had beliefs, desires, and intentions. Applied to impersonal forces, it generates gods.
  • Hyperactive agent detection: The evolved tendency of human brains to detect agents (entities with intentions) even where none exist, due to the asymmetric costs of false positives versus false negatives in predator detection.
  • The Big God hypothesis: The proposal that moralistic omniscient gods solved the cooperation problem of large-scale anonymous societies by providing credible universal surveillance of behavior — extending enforcement beyond what direct reputation networks can achieve.

Important Terms

  • Dunbar's number: The cognitive limit of roughly 150 close relationships the human brain can track; beyond this, direct reputation enforcement breaks down.
  • Totemic ritual: Sacred practices centered on totemic objects or animals, studied by Durkheim in Australian Aboriginal peoples as evidence of the social origin of the sacred.
  • Moral enforcement: The social process of rewarding cooperative behavior and punishing defection; the Big God hypothesis proposes omniscient gods extend this to the private mind.
  • Functional equivalent: An institution or mechanism that performs the same social role as another, regardless of its content or origin — courts and police as potential functional equivalents of divine oversight.
  • The sacred vs. the social (Durkheim): Durkheim's claim that these are the same experience — the awe and moral obligation directed toward the divine is the awe and moral obligation owed to the collective.

Detailed Notes

The Cognitive Roots of Religious Personification

  • The personification of impersonal forces (angry storm, generous river, pleased harvest goddess) is so ancient and universal it may be a feature of evolved human cognition rather than cultural invention.
  • Dennett's intentional stance: we explain complex systems — thermostats, computers, economies, hurricanes — by treating them as agents with beliefs, desires, and intentions. This same operation generates gods.
  • The Cross-Reference to Chapter 3 makes this explicit: whether the subject is the invisible hand of the market or the will of Zeus, the underlying mental operation is identical.
  • Hyperactive agent detection: the brain has specialized circuits for modeling agents. These circuits produce false positives readily (faces in clouds, malice in weather). The evolutionary asymmetry explains why: false negative costs are catastrophic (death), false positive costs are trivial (wasted seconds running). Selection favored brains that see agents everywhere.

Durkheim's Inversion: God as Society in Disguise

  • The Elementary Forms of Religious Life (1912): when people worship God, they are worshipping society. The sacred is the social.
  • Evidence: totemic rituals among Australian Aboriginal peoples produced collective effervescence — a real state of heightened intensity in which individuals felt merged into something larger. Durkheim did not dismiss this experience; he identified its source as the force of the collective.
  • Not a claim of manipulation: the experience is genuine. What Durkheim identifies is its structure — the experience of the sacred and the experience of moral obligation to the community are the same experience, accessed through ritual.
  • Implication: the gods and the nation are the same kind of fiction performing the same function at different scales. A soldier who dies for her country dies for the sacred thing the nation is — generated by the same collective mechanisms Durkheim found in Aboriginal ritual.

The Big God Hypothesis

  • Proposed by Ara Norenzayan and colleagues: the transition from local spirit religions to world religions was driven by the cooperation problem of large-scale societies.
  • Small societies: cooperation maintained through direct reciprocity and reputation. You cheat me; my network will know; you will be punished. Effective up to Dunbar's number (~150).
  • Beyond Dunbar's number: you cannot personally monitor ten thousand strangers. Direct reputation enforcement breaks down.
  • The Big God solution: a moralistic omniscient god who knows your thoughts, monitors all behavior, rewards virtue in the afterlife, and punishes vice — whether or not you are caught by neighbors — extends enforcement to the private mind. The psychological effect: cooperation among anonymous strangers at civilizational scale.
  • The Big God is "a bureaucratic technology: he maintains the ledger that human memory cannot hold."

Function vs. Metaphysics

  • Whether or not gods exist, they function. The function explains their persistence, cross-cultural universality, and resistance to debunking.
  • The sociological and evolutionary accounts do not prove gods do not exist. A real divine entity might work precisely through the cognitive and social mechanisms described.
  • The function is separable from the metaphysical claim — the functional analysis can proceed independently.
  • Open question: secular institutions (courts, police, tax authorities) are, on the Big God hypothesis, functional equivalents of divine oversight. Whether they are adequate substitutes is an empirical question — one that every secular society is currently running as an uncontrolled experiment.
  • "You cannot replace a social technology without providing a substitute." This is the chapter's warning to secular modernity.
