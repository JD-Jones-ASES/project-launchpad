Rules Without Rulers: Language, Practice, and Emergent Order

Overview

This section explores how rules operate without anyone having authored them. Drawing on Ludwig Wittgenstein's later philosophy — especially his concept of language games — the section argues that meaning is not a property of words in isolation but a function of how words are used within shared practices. Rules, including the rules of grammar, law, and social life, typically emerge from and are sustained by practice rather than being designed in advance. The section's central theme is the reversal: the game creates the rule, not the other way around.

Key Concepts

  • Language Games: Wittgenstein's term for structured human activities governed by rules that participants follow without necessarily articulating them. There is no single thing called "language" — only a plurality of practices, each with its own rules, context, and criteria for success.
  • Family Resemblance: The principle that many important concepts lack necessary and sufficient conditions and are instead held together by networks of overlapping similarities — like physical resemblances among family members.
  • Deep Grammar: The structural rules that allow native speakers to recognize and generate well-formed sentences without being able to state those rules; it operates below the threshold of conscious awareness.
  • Desire Paths: Dirt trails that emerge from accumulated pedestrian choices without any designer — a living demonstration that rules can precede rulers and that informal practice generates formal norms.
  • Following a Rule Is a Practice: Wittgenstein's key claim that rules and practices are not two separate things, one grounding the other. The rule is the pattern of the practice described at a level of abstraction.

Important Terms

  • Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus: Wittgenstein's early work (1921) — language as picture of the world.
  • Philosophical Investigations: Wittgenstein's late work (1953) — language as plurality of practices.
  • Form of Life: The shared background within which a language game's rules make sense. "To imagine a language means to imagine a form of life."
  • Prescriptive Grammar: Rules explicitly taught, as opposed to deep structural rules native speakers follow unconsciously.
  • Common Law: A legal system built from accumulated court decisions — the rule extracted from cases rather than applied to them.

Detailed Notes

The Two Wittgensteins

  • The early Wittgenstein (Tractatus): meaningful sentences have a logical form that mirrors the structure of facts; the limits of language are the limits of what can be thought.
  • The late Wittgenstein (Philosophical Investigations): there is an enormous variety of human practices, each with its own internal logic; none is reducible to any other; none is grounded in anything outside itself.
  • The shift is from a single unified logic underlying all language to a plurality of practices with no common foundation.

Language Games and Family Resemblance

  • Language games are not trivial or optional — they are the normal condition of language use.
  • Wittgenstein's challenge: find what all games have in common. His answer: nothing, because some games lack winning and losing, some lack competition, some lack skill, some lack fixed rules.
  • What holds the concept "game" together is not an essence but a family resemblance — overlapping similarities without any single defining feature.
  • Implication: most important concepts (art, justice, knowledge, good) are held together by family resemblance, not by definitions. This makes definitional debates irreducible to logic.

Grammar as Invisible Law and Desire Paths

  • Deep grammar operates below conscious awareness: speakers know that "Colorless green ideas sleep furiously" is grammatical without being able to say why. The rule is operating but was authored by no one.
  • Grammar is law without a legislator; custom is governance without a government.
  • Desire paths (dirt trails worn by pedestrian shortcuts) show the same principle: no one designed them, yet they function as rules. Some universities now read desire paths as information — the informal rule precedes the formal one.
  • This reversal appears in language evolution (languages precede grammarians), markets (commercial practice precedes contract law), and English common law (rule extracted from cases, not applied to them).

Resolving the Chicken-and-Egg Paradox

  • The paradox: you need rules to play the game, but you need the game to understand the rules.
  • Wittgenstein's resolution: "following a rule is a practice." The rule and the practice are not two separate things — the rule is the pattern of the practice at a level of abstraction.
  • Practical implication: to change the rules, change what people do. Once enough people practice differently, new rules write themselves. The rulebook follows the game.
  • Application to social change: changing practice (making racism costly, alternatives available, enforcement credible) eventually changes law and norm. The game changes when players play differently.
