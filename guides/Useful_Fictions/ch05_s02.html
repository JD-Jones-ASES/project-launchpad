Moral Bookkeeping: Accounting for Virtue

Overview

Long before there were banks, human cultures were keeping moral ledgers. The impulse to track, tally, and balance virtue and vice — measuring good deeds against sins and assigning consequences — is older than money itself. This section traces that impulse across three major systems: karma in Hindu and Buddhist cosmology, Catholic indulgences in medieval Europe, and Jeremy Bentham's secular felicific calculus in the Enlightenment. In each case, commercial accounting logic — credits, debits, balancing ledgers — was applied directly to the moral domain. The accounting metaphor was not decorating a pre-existing moral intuition; in these traditions, the ledger structure was the moral intuition.

Key Concepts

  • Karma as cosmic double-entry: Every action generates two ledger entries — the deed and its consequence — and the universe acts as auditor. The account must balance, if not in this life then across future rebirths.
  • Treasury of Merit: In medieval Catholic theology, a reserve of surplus spiritual merit accumulated by Christ and the saints, administered by the Church and available for transfer to sinners via indulgences.
  • Commodification of moral credit: By the late medieval period, indulgences were sold in tiered denominations for money. Johann Tetzel's marketing slogan made the transaction explicit: coin paid, soul released.
  • Luther's partial rejection: Luther attacked the institutional accounting system — denying the Church could transfer merit or sell salvation — but did not deny the underlying structure of moral accounting. He moved the auditor from Church to God.
  • Felicific calculus: Bentham's attempt to place ethics on a mathematical foundation by summing pleasures and subtracting pains across seven measurable dimensions. The result is ethics as arithmetic.
  • Utilitarian migration into policy: Despite its philosophical failures, utilitarianism migrated into economics and regulatory practice; the value of a statistical life (~$11 million in U.S. federal calculations) is Bentham's calculus applied institutionally.

Important Terms

  • Karma: Sanskrit for "action" or "deed"; the moral weight accumulated through actions across lifetimes in Hindu and Buddhist cosmology.
  • Samsara: The cycle of rebirth; karma shapes conditions within samsara, and the ultimate goal in many traditions is to exit it by exhausting the karmic account.
  • Indulgence: A papal certificate attesting the cancellation of a specified quantum of temporal punishment, by transferring merit from the Treasury of Merit to the purchaser.
  • Treasury of Merit: The theological reserve of surplus merit from Christ and the saints, administered by the Church.
  • Felicific calculus: Bentham's procedure for measuring moral worth by summing pleasures and subtracting pains across seven dimensions: intensity, duration, certainty, propinquity, fecundity, purity, and extent.
  • Utilitarian monster: The thought experiment showing that the calculus can justify intense suffering for one person if it increases small pleasures for a large enough crowd.
  • Value of a statistical life (VSL): A regulatory figure (~$11 million, U.S.) representing the aggregate willingness to pay for small reductions in mortality risk — the contemporary institutional form of utilitarian welfare calculation.

Detailed Notes

The Universal Moral Accounting Impulse

  • Before banks, there were temples. Before credit scores, karma. Moral bookkeeping precedes financial bookkeeping.
  • Across cultures and centuries, the cosmos was imagined as keeping precise records: virtue and sin accumulate, the ledger eventually closes, and debts are settled.
  • The accounting metaphor is not an analogy imported from commerce; in these systems it was the original conceptual structure for understanding moral causation.

Karma: The Universe as Auditor

  • Karma is among the most sophisticated moral accounting systems ever devised.
  • Operates across multiple lifetimes: every action generates good or bad karma shaping future rebirths.
  • Structure mirrors double-entry bookkeeping: every action generates the deed entry and its consequence entry; the sum must zero out.
  • Ultimate goal: exhaust the account entirely and exit samsara — a kind of moral bankruptcy leading to liberation.

Catholic Indulgences: Moral Credit at Scale

  • Medieval Catholic theology elaborated a moral accounting system with institutional ambition matching any bank's.
  • The Treasury of Merit held an unlimited reserve of saintly surplus grace, administered by the Pope.
  • Sinners with moral deficits could draw on the Treasury via an indulgence certificate.
  • Early indulgences rewarded acts of piety (Crusade, pilgrimage). By the late medieval period they were sold for money in tiered denominations.
  • Johann Tetzel's marketing phrase — "As soon as the coin in the coffer rings, the soul from purgatory springs" — made the commercial logic explicit.
  • Proceeds funded the rebuilding of St. Peter's Basilica in Rome and various bishops' campaigns.

Luther's Reformation: Same Ledger, Different Auditor

  • Luther's Ninety-Five Theses (1517) attacked the indulgence system's institutional mechanics.
  • What Luther denied: (1) the Church holds a transferable treasury of merit, (2) salvation can be purchased, (3) a certificate substitutes for genuine repentance.
  • What Luther did not deny: that moral life has a trackable structure, that virtue and sin accumulate, and that the account must eventually settle.
  • Luther moved the settlement mechanism from Church to God — the moral audit remained, only the auditor changed.
  • Legacy: the moral accounting intuition survived the Reformation intact, migrating into secular frameworks — "moral desert," "just deserts," and therapy culture's language of self-debt and self-compassion.

Bentham's Felicific Calculus: Secular Moral Arithmetic

  • Published 1789: Introduction to the Principles of Morals and Legislation.
  • Proposed measuring any action's moral worth by summing pleasures and subtracting pains, each measured on seven dimensions.
  • Ethics became arithmetic: the action with highest net utility is the right action.
  • The utilitarian monster problem: aggregating across persons allows a majority's small pleasures to outweigh one person's extreme suffering — seemingly justifying torture at scale.
  • Mill's patch: distinguish "higher" from "lower" pleasures. Philosophically dubious because it abandons the very commensurability the calculus required.
  • Core ontological failure: pleasure and pain have no common interpersonal unit. You cannot add your pleasure to mine. Interpersonal cardinal utility is assumed, never proven.

Utilitarianism's Migration into Policy

  • Despite its philosophical problems, the utilitarian impulse did not die — it became welfare economics and regulatory cost-benefit analysis.
  • The "value of a statistical life" (~$11 million in U.S. federal regulatory practice) is Bentham's calculus dressed in institutional authority.
  • We are still keeping the moral books. We just call it policy analysis.
