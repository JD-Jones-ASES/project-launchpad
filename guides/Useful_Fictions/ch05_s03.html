Measuring the Unmeasurable: Numbers That Govern Us

Overview

Society has a recurring need to take something genuinely complex — welfare, intelligence, health, creditworthiness — reduce it to a single number, and then govern by that number as if the reduction were lossless. This section traces that impulse through four case studies: GDP, IQ, BMI, and the FICO credit score. In each case, a statistical tool designed for a limited purpose was misappropriated for a broader one, acquired legal and institutional force, and began shaping the behavior it was supposed to describe. Charles Goodhart's formulation captures the underlying logic: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." The section closes with a call for measurement literacy — the ability to interrogate any governing number about what it was designed to measure, what it excludes, and who benefits from its cutoffs.

Key Concepts

  • GDP as welfare proxy: Simon Kuznets invented GNP in 1934 specifically to track Depression recovery, then warned Congress it should not be used to measure national welfare. Congress ignored him. GDP now serves as the headline measure of national wellbeing, despite excluding unpaid labor, environmental costs, inequality, and other welfare-relevant dimensions.
  • Goodhart's Law: When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. Actors optimize for the metric rather than the underlying reality, detaching the proxy from what it was meant to capture.
  • Campbell's Law: The broader formulation — the more a social indicator is used for social decisions, the more subject it will be to corruption pressures and the more it will distort the processes it monitors.
  • IQ misappropriation: Binet's screening tool was reinterpreted as measuring fixed, heritable intelligence. The Flynn effect (scores rising ~3 points/decade) proves scores are not stable attributes — the same raw score means different things in different eras.
  • BMI misappropriation: Quetelet's 1832 population-distribution index was adopted as individual clinical diagnosis in 1972, with WHO cutoffs standardized in 1998. It does not distinguish muscle from fat, does not account for fat distribution, and was calibrated on European-ancestry populations.
  • Credit scores creating the attribute: A FICO score that was supposed to measure pre-existing trustworthiness instead constructs trustworthiness by excluding those without credit histories from the systems that generate credit signals.
  • Measurement literacy: The practice of asking, of any governing number, what it was designed to measure, who designed it, what it excludes, who benefits from its cutoffs, and who bears the cost of misclassification.

Important Terms

  • GNP/GDP: Gross National Product / Gross Domestic Product — measures of total economic output; GNP based on residents' production, GDP based on production within borders.
  • Goodhart's Law: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." (Charles Goodhart)
  • Campbell's Law: The more a social indicator is used for high-stakes decisions, the more it corrupts the process it was meant to monitor. (Donald Campbell)
  • Flynn effect: The secular rise in average IQ scores (roughly 3 points per decade), requiring periodic re-norming and demonstrating that IQ is not a stable, fixed attribute.
  • BMI (Body Mass Index): Weight in kg divided by height in meters squared; originally a population-level epidemiological index, now used for individual clinical classification.
  • FICO score: A consumer credit score (300–850) predicting default risk, launched commercially in 1989 by Fair and Isaac.
  • Measurement literacy: The critical capacity to interrogate the design, purpose, exclusions, and incentive structure of any governing quantitative measure.
  • Sharp administrative lines: Discrete categorical boundaries (IQ 70, FICO 620, BMI 25) imposed on continuous distributions, creating arbitrarily different outcomes for statistically indistinguishable individuals on either side.

Detailed Notes

GDP: From Recovery Tool to National Scorecard

  • 1934: Kuznets delivers GNP to Congress; designed to understand and track Great Depression recovery.
  • Kuznets explicitly warned: "The welfare of a nation can scarcely be inferred from a measurement of national income."
  • What GDP excludes: unpaid household and care work (largely performed by women in 1934), environmental degradation, inequality of distribution, voluntary production, leisure, political freedom, social cohesion.
  • Perverse GDP incentives: natural disasters increase GDP (reconstruction); divorces increase GDP (legal fees); car accidents increase GDP (hospitals, body shops); factory pollution counted positively, cleanup counted positively again; weapons production in wartime increases GDP.
  • The U.S. adopted GDP (rather than GNP) as its headline measure in 1991. Kuznets's warning remains completely ignored.

Goodhart's Law and Campbell's Law

  • Goodhart (concise): "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."
  • Campbell (broader): High-stakes use of social indicators produces corruption pressures that distort the processes the indicators were meant to monitor.
  • The general pattern: (1) proxy is useful; (2) proxy becomes a target; (3) proxy becomes detached from original attribute while retaining institutional authority.
  • Instances identified in the text: school performance metrics (teaching to the test), academic citation counts, policing statistics, social media engagement scores, corporate earnings guidance.

IQ: The Score That Became an Identity

  • 1904–05: Paris school commission → Binet and Simon's intelligence test, designed to identify children needing extra support.
  • Binet explicitly warned against interpreting scores as measuring fixed, innate ability.
  • 1912: William Stern coins "intelligence quotient" (mental age ÷ chronological age).
  • 1916: Lewis Terman's Stanford-Binet popularizes IQ in the U.S.
  • Misuse: Terman and Goddard applied the test at Ellis Island and interpreted scores as evidence of fixed, heritable racial differences. Numerical precision (a score like "IQ: 78") made a test performance feel like a biological fact.
  • Flynn effect: Average scores rise ~3 points per decade. A score of 70 in 1970 is not the same as a score of 70 in 2020. Legal thresholds (e.g., intellectual disability at IQ 70) are therefore unstable across eras.

BMI: A Population Tool Applied to Individuals

  • 1832: Adolphe Quetelet's Quetelet Index — designed to describe statistical distributions of physical characteristics across large populations. Not a clinical diagnostic.
  • 1972: Ancel Keys proposes adopting it as a clinical obesity measure, renaming it BMI.
  • 1998: WHO standardizes cutoffs — underweight, normal, overweight, obese — now used for insurance premiums, clinical advice, and access to medical procedures.
  • Problems: Does not distinguish fat from muscle; muscular athletes score "overweight." Does not account for fat distribution, which better predicts health risk. Calibrated on European-ancestry populations. Fundamentally a population-level descriptor applied to individuals — a category error.

Credit Scores: Measuring and Making Trustworthiness

  • 1950s: Bill Fair and Earl Isaac develop statistical creditworthiness modeling.
  • 1989: FICO score launches commercially. Ranges 300–850; predicts default risk from five factor categories.
  • Key conceptual problem: A recent immigrant with a perfect payment record has a low score not because they are risky but because the measurement instrument has no data on them. The score excludes them from credit, preventing them from generating the data the score requires. The measure creates the attribute.
  • Sharp administrative lines: A score of 619 versus 620 can determine mortgage eligibility, even though these individuals are statistically indistinguishable in actual default risk.

Measurement Literacy

  • The text is not an argument against measurement — it is an argument for asking critical questions about any governing number.
  • The right questions: What was this designed to measure? Who designed it, and for what purpose? What does it exclude? Who benefits from the current cutoffs? Who bears the cost of misclassification?
  • Core reminder: "The number is not the thing. The map is not the territory. And the ledger, however precisely kept, is always an abstraction of a reality more complicated than any entry can capture."
