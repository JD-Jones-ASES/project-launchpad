Carbon Credits and the Commodification of Guilt

Overview

Can you buy your way out of environmental harm? This section examines what happens when accounting logic — the same impulse that generated moral bookkeeping, indulgences, and the felicific calculus — is applied to the atmosphere. The 1990 U.S. sulfur dioxide cap-and-trade program was a genuine success: a market mechanism reduced acid rain faster and more cheaply than projected. Emboldened by this, economists applied the same architecture to greenhouse gas emissions, creating carbon credits — social permission slips created by law, with no physical form, intended to make invisible pollution costs visible and priced. The section then traces the carbon credit's expansion into offsets (where additionality is nearly impossible to verify), ESG scores (where different rating agencies measuring the same company disagree profoundly), and the deeper philosophical question of whether all morally relevant values can be reduced to a common unit — commensurability. The section closes with a warning: the books may balance while the atmosphere continues to warm.

Key Concepts

  • Cap-and-trade: A regulatory mechanism that caps total permitted pollution while allowing individual actors to buy and sell emission permits, so that reductions happen where they are cheapest. A market in the right to pollute.
  • Carbon credit as useful fiction: A right to emit one ton of CO₂ did not exist before the law created it. It has no physical form and represents a collectively created permission slip — a pure instance of the book's central argument about invented abstractions that shape reality.
  • Carbon offset and additionality: An offset certifies that an equivalent emission was avoided or sequestered elsewhere. The core problem is additionality: how do you prove the reduction would not have occurred anyway, without the payment?
  • Goodhart's Law in offset markets: Once certified offsets became a commodity with a price, the incentive to produce certified reductions overwhelmed the incentive to produce actual reductions. A 2023 investigation found more than 90 percent of Verra's rainforest offset credits were phantom credits.
  • ESG scores: Corporate moral ratings on Environmental, Social, and Governance dimensions. Different agencies rating the same company correlate at only 0.61 (vs. 0.99 for bond agencies) — because ESG scores construct corporate virtue rather than describe a pre-existing attribute.
  • Commensurability: The philosophical thesis that all values can be compared on a single scale. Carbon markets, ESG scores, and the felicific calculus all presuppose commensurability. Elizabeth Anderson argues some values are incommensurable — different in kind, not just amount — so ranking them on a single scale necessarily misrepresents at least one.

Important Terms

  • Cap-and-trade: An emissions regulation system combining a declining total cap with a market allowing permit trading among covered entities.
  • EU ETS (European Union Emissions Trading System): The world's largest carbon market (launched 2005), covering ~40 percent of EU greenhouse gas emissions.
  • Carbon offset: A certificate representing emissions avoided or sequestered elsewhere, used to compensate for emissions produced by the buyer.
  • Additionality: The requirement that an offset project represent reductions that would not have occurred without the offset payment.
  • Phantom credits: Certified offsets not supported by actual emission reductions — the primary documented failure mode of the voluntary offset market.
  • ESG scores: Environmental, Social, and Governance ratings of corporations, assigned by competing agencies with low inter-agency correlation.
  • Commensurability: The thesis that all morally relevant values can be ranked on a single scale and traded off against each other.
  • Incommensurability: The condition in which two values are not just different in amount but different in kind — ranking them on a single scale necessarily distorts at least one.

Detailed Notes

The Sulfur Dioxide Success Story

  • 1990 Clean Air Act Amendments created the first major cap-and-trade system.
  • Each power plant received permits for a fixed annual tonnage of SO₂ (one permit = one ton/year).
  • Plants reducing emissions below allocation could sell surplus permits; those finding cleanup expensive could buy permits instead.
  • Total permit cap declined over time, ensuring aggregate emission reductions.
  • Acid rain across the northeastern U.S. declined dramatically; emissions fell faster and more cheaply than projected.
  • Economists took a lesson: market mechanisms solve environmental problems more efficiently than command-and-control regulation.

Cap-and-Trade and the Architecture of a Carbon Market

  • Cap-and-trade turns pollution into a commodity: the right to emit becomes a tradeable asset with a market price.
  • The cap ensures accounting adds up; the trade allocates permitted emissions to those who value them most.
  • EU ETS (2005): world's largest carbon market, covering power plants, industrial facilities, and aviation within the EEA; covers ~40 percent of EU greenhouse gas emissions.
  • The carbon credit is a pure useful fiction: it did not exist before the law created it, has no physical form, and represents a social permission slip. Nobody physically prevents uncredentialed emitters — the market creates the price where none existed.

Offsets: Where the Accounting Gets Complicated

  • An offset certifies that an equivalent ton of carbon was avoided or sequestered elsewhere (tree planting, forest preservation, efficient cookstoves).
  • Atmospheric logic: a ton of carbon not emitted in Brazil offsets a ton emitted in Ohio — the atmosphere is global.
  • Additionality problem: the reduction must represent something that would not have happened without the payment. A forest never at risk of logging does not offset anything — it is "counterfactual carbon."
  • 2023 investigation (Guardian, Zeit Online, SourceMaterial): more than 90 percent of Verra's rainforest offset credits — the dominant voluntary standard — were phantom credits with no underlying real reductions.
  • Goodhart's Law applies: once certification became a commodity, producing certified offsets became more lucrative than producing actual emission reductions.

ESG: Moral Credit Ratings for Corporations

  • ESG agencies (MSCI, Sustainalytics, Bloomberg, and dozens more) rate companies on Environmental, Social, and Governance performance.
  • $35 trillion in assets managed under ESG mandates as of 2020.
  • Structural problem: 2019 MIT Sloan study found inter-agency correlation of 0.61 for the same company's ESG score, compared to ~0.99 for bond credit agencies rating the same company.
  • Agencies measure different things and aggregate differently — the score does not describe a pre-existing attribute; it constructs one, and different constructors produce different artifacts.

Can Everything Be Made Commensurable?

  • Carbon markets, ESG scores, and the felicific calculus all rest on commensurability — the thesis that all morally relevant quantities can be compared on a single scale.
  • Elizabeth Anderson and others argue commensurability is false for important moral comparisons. Some values are incommensurable: not different in degree but in kind.
  • The Ohio-Indonesia example: atmospheric commensurability (a ton is a ton) may be true; moral commensurability (the total ethical situation is exhausted by atmospheric accounting) is almost certainly false.

The Layered Fiction and Its Limits

  • The carbon credit is a stack of abstractions: legal right → atmospheric permission → uncertain atmospheric reality (if offsets fraudulent) → intended moral outcome (stable climate) → via market mechanism → uncertain alignment of incentives with reality.
  • Each layer is a useful abstraction. The question is whether the full stack does the work it is supposed to do.
  • Closing warning: "The books may balance. The atmosphere does not care about the books." A system that allows rich-country institutions to feel virtuous through accounting while emissions continue is a fiction that may not be useful enough.
