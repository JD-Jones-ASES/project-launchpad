Useful Fictions: Borders, Real and Imagined

Overview

This section moves the chapter's argument about drawn lines through continuous reality from the natural sciences (color, species) to political geography. Borders are the most consequential version of the same structure: sharp lines imposed on continuous human populations, landscapes, and cultures that contain no natural joints. The section examines three major cases — the US-Canada 49th parallel, the Sykes-Picot Agreement dividing the Arab Middle East, and the Berlin Conference partitioning of Africa — as well as gerrymandering as a domestic American version of the same practice. Each case demonstrates that border-drawing serves the interests of whoever holds the pen, not the interests of the populations divided. The section ends with two complementary insights: borders become real through enforcement (the irony of the sharp line), and gerrymandering is paradoxically the most honest of all borders because it makes the human agency behind all boundary-drawing visible.

Key Concepts

  • The 49th parallel: Chosen by British and American negotiators in 1818 who had never seen most of the territory — picked because a line of latitude is geometrically clean and cartographically simple. The border cuts through the Blackfoot Confederacy and imposes sharp legal differences on a substantially continuous population.

  • Sykes-Picot Agreement (1916): Two diplomats, a map, and a pencil dividing the Arab territories of the collapsing Ottoman Empire between British and French spheres of influence. The line bore no relation to the region's actual human geography — splitting Sunni Arab populations, dividing Kurdish communities among four future nation-states, and allocating Mosul to Britain based on suspected oil deposits rather than community affinity.

  • Berlin Conference (1884-1885): Fourteen European nations gathered to manage the "Scramble for Africa" with no African representation. The result: 44 straight-line borders accounting for ~44% of Africa's total boundary length, cutting across more than 177 ethnic and linguistic groups. Post-independence African states kept these borders under the OAU principle of uti possidetis, calculating that continent-wide renegotiation would be worse than inherited dysfunction.

  • Gerrymandering: The manipulation of district boundaries for partisan advantage. Named for Massachusetts Governor Elbridge Gerry (1812). Modern computational gerrymandering optimizes district shapes to pack opposition into a few dominant districts and spread majority voters efficiently across competitive ones. The section argues gerrymandering is uniquely honest: unlike other borders, we can name who chose those lines and for what purpose.

  • Borders becoming real: The central irony of border enforcement — the more vigorously a border is enforced, the more real the differences it was supposed to merely describe eventually become. Communities stop visiting each other, trade routes shift, languages diverge.

Important Terms

  • 49th parallel: The US-Canada border west of the Great Lakes, a line of latitude chosen for geometric convenience in 1818.
  • Sykes-Picot Agreement: 1916 secret Anglo-French agreement dividing Arab Ottoman territories, basis for modern Middle Eastern state borders.
  • Berlin Conference: 1884-1885 European colonial congress partitioning Africa without African representation.
  • Uti possidetis: The OAU principle of inheriting colonial borders upon independence, to avoid the chaos of universal renegotiation.
  • Gerrymandering: Manipulation of district boundaries to produce partisan electoral advantage; named after Governor Elbridge Gerry.
  • Cartographic fiction: The section's framing of maps that do not merely record but actively create political reality.

Detailed Notes

The 49th Parallel: When Nothing Marks the Line

  • On the ground along the 49th parallel in Montana, there is no visible difference between the US and Canada.
  • The border was agreed in 1818 by British and American negotiators who had never seen most of the territory.
  • A line of latitude was chosen for geometric and cartographic convenience — no knowledge of terrain required.
  • Consequences: the line cuts through mountains, rivers, forests, and First Nations territories.
  • Most significantly: it slices the Blackfoot Confederacy into two jurisdictions with different laws, languages of government, and colonial histories.
  • This is what a border does — creates sharp difference where nature drew a gradient.

Sykes-Picot: Maximum Arbitrariness, Maximum Consequence

  • May 1916: British diplomat Mark Sykes and French diplomat François Georges-Picot drew a line from the Mediterranean coast near Acre eastward to the Persian frontier.
  • Neither man had spent significant time in the territory. The negotiation took place in London and Paris.
  • The line divided Arab territories into a French zone (modern Syria and Lebanon) and a British zone (modern Iraq and Jordan).
  • The line bore no relation to actual human geography:
    • Sunni Arab populations were split across it.
    • Kurdish communities were divided among four future nation-states — Turkey, Syria, Iraq, Iran — with no state of their own.
    • Mosul (mixed Arab, Kurdish, Assyrian) was given to Britain for its oil deposits, not community affinity.
    • The people who actually lived there were not consulted.
  • Sykes-Picot borders, modified at the 1919 Paris Peace Conference and codified in League of Nations Mandates, became the borders of modern Iraq, Syria, Lebanon, and Jordan — a source of chronic instability driven by borders designed for European imperial interests.

Berlin Conference: Africa in a Grid

  • 1884: "Scramble for Africa" led to a 14-nation European conference (plus the US as observer) in Berlin.
  • No African representative was present.
  • Over three months, participants drew boundaries across the continent — mostly straight lines along latitude and longitude.
  • Legacy: 44 straight-line borders account for ~44% of Africa's total boundary length, cutting across 177+ distinct ethnic and linguistic groups.
  • Examples of divided peoples: Ewe (British Gold Coast / German Togoland); Somali (five colonial jurisdictions); Maasai (two territories).
  • Post-independence: the OAU adopted uti possidetis — keep the colonial borders — calculating the alternative (renegotiating every border simultaneously) was worse than inherited dysfunction.

Gerrymandering: Borders Redrawn in Real Time

  • Gerrymandering takes the Sorites problem and weaponizes it: there is no natural answer to "which voters belong to which district?" because voters are a continuous population.
  • Named after Massachusetts Governor Elbridge Gerry, whose 1812 redistricting produced a salamander-shaped district.
  • Modern gerrymandering uses computational mapping to optimize with extraordinary accuracy — locking in legislative majorities for a decade.
  • The section argues gerrymandering is "more honest than most borders": we know someone chose those lines, and we can name them. Other borders disguise the same human agency as geography, nature, or history.
  • The pen that draws a border on a map does not describe the ground — it prescribes what will be enforced on it.

Review Questions

  1. Why was the 49th parallel chosen as the US-Canada border? What community does it divide, and how does this division illustrate the section's central argument?
  2. What made the Sykes-Picot Agreement unusual even by the standards of colonial border-drawing? List three specific consequences of the line it drew.
  3. Describe the Berlin Conference and its quantitative legacy in Africa's borders. Why did post-independence African states keep these borders despite their arbitrary origins?
  4. The section argues that gerrymandering is paradoxically "more honest" than other borders. Explain this argument.
  5. Explain the "central irony" of border enforcement described in the Specimen box. What does this irony suggest about how borders transition from legal fictions to social realities?
