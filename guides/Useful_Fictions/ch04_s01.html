Useful Fictions: Where One Color Ends and Another Begins

Overview

This section opens Chapter 4 ("The Sharp Line") by using color as an entry point into the book's central argument: that human categories are useful fictions — sharp lines drawn across gradients that contain no such lines. The electromagnetic spectrum is a continuous range of wavelengths with no natural color boundaries, yet every human language draws divisions across it, and no two languages draw them in the same places. The section examines cross-cultural evidence about color vocabulary (from Welsh and Russian to Japanese and Zulu), introduces the Berlin-Kay model of universal and language-specific aspects of color naming, surveys experimental evidence that language categories influence cognitive processing (the Russian blue experiment), and anchors the whole discussion in the ancient Sorites paradox — which reveals that the problem of sharp lines through continuous reality is not an accident of color science but a deep structural feature of how language works.

Key Concepts

  • The electromagnetic spectrum as a gradient: The spectrum runs from roughly 380 nm (violet) to 700 nm (red) as an unbroken continuum. There are no physical breakpoints at which one color ends and another begins. The physics of light is indifferent to the categories human languages impose on it.

  • Cross-linguistic color variation: Different languages carve the same continuous spectrum in different places. Welsh 'glas' covers both blue and green; Japanese 'ao' historically covered both until 'midori' was introduced for green after European contact; Russian has two separate basic terms for blue ('siniy' for dark blue, 'goluboy' for light blue) where English has one.

  • Berlin and Kay's universal and language-specific findings: Their 1969 cross-linguistic study of 98 languages found both universals and variation. Languages add color terms in a fixed sequence (black/white → red → yellow/green → blue → brown → others), and focal color exemplars are broadly consistent cross-culturally. But category edges — where one color ends and another begins — are fuzzy, negotiated, and genuinely language-dependent.

  • Linguistic relativity (Sapir-Whorf): The weak version of this hypothesis holds that language structure influences cognitive processing. The Winawer et al. experiment confirmed this for color: Russian speakers were measurably faster at matching blue squares when the task crossed their language's siniy/goluboy boundary. The language category creates a real perceptual discontinuity in cognition, not in the physics or the retina.

  • The Sorites paradox: Named from the Greek 'soros' (heap), this paradox shows that predicates like 'heap,' 'bald,' or 'blue' apply clearly at extremes but resist precise boundary-marking. One grain is not a heap; adding one never makes a heap; therefore no number of grains is a heap. The paradox reveals that language draws sharp lines through continuous reality, and that every such line is at some level arbitrary.

Important Terms

  • Electromagnetic spectrum: The continuum of light wavelengths; the physical substrate that color names are imposed upon.
  • Focal color: The best exemplar of a color category (the most prototypical red, blue, etc.); cross-culturally consistent according to Berlin and Kay.
  • Category edge: The fuzzy boundary between adjacent color categories; language-dependent and variable across cultures.
  • Sapir-Whorf hypothesis (linguistic relativity): The proposal that language structure influences thought and/or perception.
  • Sorites paradox: A logical puzzle about vague predicates that applies whenever there is no precise transition between "applies" and "does not apply."
  • siniy / goluboy: Russian basic color terms for dark blue and light blue respectively — evidence of a language dividing the blue range into two categories where English has one.

Detailed Notes

The Continuous Spectrum and Linguistic Division

  • The electromagnetic spectrum runs from ~380 nm (violet) to ~700 nm (red) with no natural gaps or fences.
  • Color names visible on a spectrum rendering (violet, blue, cyan, green, yellow, orange, red) are imposed by human language, not found in the physics.
  • Welsh 'glas,' Japanese 'ao,' and Russian 'siniy'/'goluboy' all illustrate that the same physical continuum can be carved very differently by different languages.
  • Japanese 'midori' (green) was introduced after contact with European color systems — showing that color vocabulary responds to cultural contact, not just biology.

Berlin and Kay: Universals and Variation

  • In 1969, Brent Berlin and Paul Kay analyzed color vocabularies in 98 languages and found results that 'upset both sides of the debate.'
  • Universal finding: Languages acquire basic color terms in a fixed sequence, with no language skipping ahead.
  • Universal finding: Focal colors (the most prototypical exemplars of each category) are broadly consistent across cultures.
  • Variable finding: Category edges — where one color ends and another begins — are fuzzy and language-dependent. Where English 'blue' ends and 'purple' begins is different for each language.
  • The Russian 'siniy'/'goluboy' split is highlighted as an anomaly: two basic terms where English has one, producing measurably faster discrimination of blue shades by Russian speakers.

Linguistic Relativity and the Russian Blue Experiment

  • Benjamin Lee Whorf's strong version of linguistic relativity (language determines thought) has been largely abandoned as obviously false.
  • The weak version (language influences cognitive processing) has real empirical support.
  • Jonathan Winawer and colleagues showed Russian and English speakers a center blue square flanked by two comparison squares and timed color matching.
  • When the pair crossed the Russian siniy/goluboy boundary: Russian speakers were measurably faster.
  • When both comparison squares were on the same side of the boundary: the advantage disappeared.
  • Conclusion: the language boundary creates a real perceptual discontinuity — but in cognitive processing, not in the physics or the retina.

The Sorites Paradox: Sharp Lines Through Gradients

  • The ancient Greek paradox of the heap (soros): one grain is not a heap; adding one grain to something that is not a heap cannot make it a heap; therefore no number of grains is ever a heap.
  • Classic variants: baldness (remove hairs one by one — when does someone become bald?); salary levels; color boundaries.
  • Key conclusion: the paradox does not show that heaps, baldness, or colors do not exist. It shows that language draws sharp lines through continuous reality and any such line is, at some level, arbitrary.
  • The drawn line is necessary for thought and communication. But the line is also ours — placed on the gradient, not found in it.
  • Color names that make matching faster are doing real cognitive work, even though the boundary they mark has no physical basis. The question is not whether to draw lines but whether we know we are drawing them.

Review Questions

  1. Explain how the Welsh word 'glas' and the Russian 'siniy/goluboy' distinction each illustrate different aspects of the relationship between language and the color spectrum.
  2. What did Berlin and Kay find to be universal about color naming, and what did they find to be language-specific?
  3. Describe the Winawer et al. experiment and explain what its results demonstrate about the weak version of linguistic relativity.
  4. State the Sorites paradox in your own words. What does it reveal about the relationship between language categories and continuous physical reality?
  5. The section argues that drawn color lines are 'neither useless nor interchangeable.' What reasoning supports this, and what caveat comes with it?
