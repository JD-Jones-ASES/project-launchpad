Frictionless Planes and Ideal Gases: The Physics of Impossible Objects

Overview

This section catalogues the "impossible zoo" — the collection of physically nonexistent objects that populate physics textbooks and drive daily scientific practice. Point particles, massless ropes, perfectly elastic collisions, infinite planes, and the ideal gas are not exotic curiosities but indispensable tools. None of them exist, not approximately, yet if they were removed from physics tomorrow, the field would largely collapse. Drawing on Boyle's discovery of the pressure-volume law and Newton's treatment of planets as point masses, the section argues that the usefulness of impossible objects is not mysterious: it follows directly from what scientific explanation is actually trying to do — not describe one specific real system, but identify the causal structure that holds across an entire class of phenomena.

Key Concepts

  • The Idealization Strategy: Strip a system down to its essential causal structure, understand that structure fully, and then build back up by adding the features that were stripped away. The frictionless plane exposes the angle-mass-acceleration relationship; friction is then added back as a correction for specific real cases.
  • Idealization vs. Approximation: An approximation gets the right answer, roughly. An idealization may give exactly the wrong answer for every real system while giving exactly the right structural insight into all of them. The ideal gas is not an approximation to real gases; it is a model of what gases would behave like if molecular volume and intermolecular forces did not exist.
  • Causal Structure: The underlying relationship between variables that holds even when surface details vary. Impossible objects isolate causal structure by removing the complicating details that obscure it.
  • The Accuracy-Illumination Trade-off: More accurate models are not necessarily more illuminating. The van der Waals equation is more accurate than the ideal gas law and less structurally revealing. Accuracy and understanding are distinct scientific virtues that can come apart.
  • The Pattern of Successful Idealizations: Idealizations succeed when the real system shares the idealization's key properties at a useful level of approximation. The art is knowing which properties to keep and which to discard — which requires knowing what question you are trying to answer.

Important Terms

  • Point Particle (Point Mass): An object with definite mass and location but zero spatial extent — no volume, shape, or surface. The foundation of Newtonian mechanics and classical electrodynamics.
  • Ideal Gas: A gas whose molecules have zero volume, exert no intermolecular forces, and collide perfectly elastically. Described by PV = nRT; the basis of thermodynamics despite being wrong about every real gas.
  • Boyle's Law: PV = k (at constant temperature) — pressure and volume are inversely proportional. Discovered by Robert Boyle in the 1660s using a J-shaped glass tube.
  • Van der Waals Equation: (P + an²/V²)(V − nb) = nRT — corrects the ideal gas law for molecular volume (b) and intermolecular forces (a). More accurate, less illuminating.
  • Causal Structure: The underlying relationship between variables that a model or idealization is designed to reveal, stripped of confounding surface complexity.

Detailed Notes

The Impossible Zoo

  • The impossible zoo contains objects that cannot exist in any physically real environment, yet live in introductory textbooks and in the equations used daily by scientists and engineers.
  • Members include: point particle (mass, no extent), massless rope (force transmission, no inertia), perfectly elastic collision (no energy loss), infinite plane (electric/gravitational field calculations), and the ideal gas.
  • These are not merely pedagogical simplifications for students — practicing scientists and engineers depend on them for daily work.

Why Impossible Objects Are Scientifically Useful

  • The goal of scientific explanation is not to describe a specific real system in all its particularity. The goal is to identify the causal structure underlying a class of phenomena — the relationship between variables that holds even when other details vary.
  • Friction is real and important, but also complicated (depends on materials, roughness, speed, wetness, vibration). A friction-inclusive model of one block on one ramp tells you almost nothing about any other block or ramp.
  • Removing friction entirely exposes the angle-mass-acceleration relationship present in every real case. That relationship is the causal structure the model was built to reveal.

The Ideal Gas: Boyle, Charles, and Productive Wrongness

  • Robert Boyle (1627–1691) discovered that pressure and volume of trapped air are inversely proportional at constant temperature: PV = k. Later extended to PV = nRT with the addition of temperature and the concept of moles.
  • The ideal gas law is wrong about every real gas: at high pressures, molecular volume matters; at low temperatures, intermolecular forces become significant; near liquefaction, deviations are practically important.
  • Yet it tells you why gases in general behave as they do — because molecules move, collide, and have kinetic energy proportional to temperature. Van der Waals's more accurate equation is also less illuminating: its correction terms tell you how each specific gas behaves, not why gases in general behave as they do.

The Point Mass and Newton's Theorem

  • The point mass — an object with mass but no spatial extent — is the most philosophically audacious item in the impossible zoo. You cannot touch a point (no surfaces), yet Newtonian mechanics is built on it.
  • Newton treated Earth and Moon as point masses in calculating gravitational attraction. This is wildly wrong about the Earth (which has an irregular density distribution, oceans, and a molten core) yet gives spacecraft-trajectory precision.
  • Newton's key theorem: a spherically symmetric mass distribution exerts exactly the same gravitational force on an external point as a point mass of the same total mass at its center. Real planets are approximately spherically symmetric. The idealization succeeds because the real object satisfies the key assumption well enough.

The General Pattern

  • Idealizations are not random fictions chosen for convenience. They are chosen because the real system shares the idealization's key properties at a useful level of approximation.
  • The idealization strategy is philosophically related to Platonism (appearances as copies of more perfect forms) but does not require it — a thoroughgoing empiricist can use impossible objects without committing to any metaphysical claims about ideal forms.

Review Questions

  1. What is the difference between an idealization and an approximation? Use the ideal gas law to illustrate why this distinction matters.
  2. Why does removing friction from a ramp model produce more generalizable understanding than including all friction factors? What does this reveal about the purpose of scientific models?
  3. The van der Waals equation is more accurate than the ideal gas law. Why does the text call it "less illuminating"? What does this suggest about accuracy and understanding as scientific virtues?
  4. How does Newton's theorem about spherically symmetric bodies explain why the point mass idealization succeeds for planetary mechanics? What general principle does this illustrate about choosing idealizations?
  5. Identify an impossible or idealized object used in a field you are familiar with (biology, economics, engineering, etc.). What causal structure does it isolate, and at what conditions does it fail?
