The Invisible Hand: Blind Watchmakers and Selfish Genes

Overview

This section examines how biology, like economics, generates powerful explanatory fictions by importing intentionality into processes that have none. Beginning with William Paley's watchmaker argument — a pre-Darwinian case for design in nature — it traces how Darwin's theory of natural selection performed what the text calls the "Great Inversion": replacing purposive explanations with mechanical ones. The section then explores why teleological language persists in biology despite being technically inaccurate, introduces Dennett's intentional stance as a justification for productive fictions, and analyzes Richard Dawkins' "selfish gene" as a masterclass in deploying — and potentially misusing — explanatory fictions. The central warning: the slide from "as if" to "actually" converts an analytical tool into a false worldview.

Key Concepts

  • The watchmaker argument: William Paley's 1802 analogy: a watch implies a watchmaker; a living organism is vastly more complex than any watch; therefore a designer. The argument is formally valid but fails because Darwin discovered a mechanical process that produces the appearance of design without a designer.

  • Darwin's Great Inversion: The replacement of purposive explanation (design, intention) with a mechanical one (differential reproduction over geological time). What Darwin did to Paley is what Torricelli did to Aristotle: a purposive cause made unnecessary by a discovered mechanism.

  • Teleological language: Language attributing purposes, goals, or intentions to biological processes. Technically inaccurate — evolution has no goals, white blood cells obey chemical gradients — but a persistent shorthand for longer mechanistic descriptions. Even Darwin used it constantly.

  • The intentional stance (Dennett): The strategy of treating a system as if it had beliefs, desires, and goals to predict its behavior. Effective without being literally true — we predict chess computers by imagining rational players, not by tracing electrons.

  • The selfish gene as productive fiction: Dawkins' framework treats genes "as if" selfish to generate predictions about altruism, parental investment, and kin conflict. The framework is analytically powerful despite genes having no preferences. The danger: taking the "as if" literally.

Important Terms

  • Teleology: Attribution of purpose or goal-directedness to a process. In biology, teleological language is technically inaccurate shorthand for mechanistic descriptions.
  • Natural selection: Differential reproduction over time — advantageous heritable variants become more common with no intention or foresight.
  • Intentional stance: Dennett's term for predicting system behavior by treating it as if it has beliefs and goals — effective without being literally true.
  • Selfish gene: Dawkins' analytical framework — genes treated "as if" selfish; a productive fiction generating real predictions.

Detailed Notes

Paley's Watchmaker: A Tight Argument with a Fatal Gap

  • Natural Theology (1802): a stone on a heath needs no explanation; a watch implies a watchmaker; a living organism is vastly more complex than any watch, therefore a designer.
  • Formally valid. Fails empirically: natural selection — undiscovered in 1802 — produces functional complexity without a designer. "His argument is tight." The defeat is scientific, not logical.

Darwin's Mechanism and the Grammar of Teleology

  • Natural selection: if organisms vary, variations are heritable, and some improve reproductive success, advantageous variants become more common — not because anyone intends it, but as a mechanical consequence of differential reproduction.
  • Darwin replaced Paley's purposive explanation exactly as Torricelli replaced Aristotle.
  • Yet biology is saturated with teleological sentences ("the eye is designed to see," "white blood cells seek pathogens"). This language is technically inaccurate but nearly impossible to avoid. "The eye evolved to see" is shorthand for a long mechanistic claim. The danger is forgetting it is shorthand.

Dennett's Intentional Stance

  • Treating a chess computer as a rational player trying to win predicts its moves better than tracing electron paths.
  • The intentional stance is effective but not truthful — it doesn't describe what's actually happening. This is the general justification for productive fictions: they work.

The Selfish Gene: Productive Fiction and Its Dangers

  • Dawkins' 1976 framework: treat genes as if they maximize their own replication. Predicts kin altruism, differential parental vs. sibling investment, parent-offspring conflicts.
  • Dawkins explicitly disclaimed literal selfishness. The fiction persists because it is "more useful than the accurate but cumbersome alternative" — "the gene whose frequency in a population increases because organisms carrying it have higher inclusive fitness."
  • The dangerous slide: from "genes behave as if selfish" to "selfishness is the fundamental fact of nature — cooperation is impossible." The territory contains vast cooperation the framework, taken too literally, cannot explain. Mistaking the map for the territory arrives somewhere its inventor never intended.
