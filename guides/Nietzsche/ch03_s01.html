Study Guide: Nietzsche’s Master and Slave Morality

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the analysis regarding Friedrich Nietzsche’s concepts of Master and Slave morality as presented in "Approaching the Arguments." It explores his genealogical method, the psychological origins of values, and the historical "slave revolt" in morality.

Key Concepts and Analysis

Genealogy vs. Advocacy

A central distinction in understanding Nietzsche is the difference between genealogy and advocacy.

  • Genealogy: This is a historical and psychological tracing of where values come from. It examines the needs, conditions, and human types that produced specific moral frameworks.
  • Advocacy: This is the endorsement or recommendation of a specific value system. While Nietzsche expresses admiration for the vitality of master morality, his primary project is to uncover the origins of these values. Proving where a value comes from is not the same as saying it should be kept or discarded.

Master Morality vs. Slave Morality

Nietzsche contrasts two primary moral frameworks based on the psychological drives behind them:

Feature Master Morality Slave Morality
Core Orientation Self-affirming: "I am good." Reactive: "He is evil, therefore I am good."
Primary Traits Vitality, honesty, creative power, vigorous. Humility, pity, self-denial.
Psychological Origin Aristocratic spirit. Ressentiment (resentment).
Contribution Beauty and vitality. Depth, "soul," and the interior life.

The "Genetic Fallacy"

Nietzsche’s work navigates the genetic fallacy, which is the logical error of judging the truth or value of a belief solely by its origin. For example, claiming a belief in equality is "false" just because it originated from the powerless is a genetic fallacy. Nietzsche uses genealogy not necessarily to refute values, but as a provocation—forcing individuals to question if they hold values because they are true or because those values serve a psychological need.

The Slave Revolt in Morality

Nietzsche identifies the historical origin of the "slave revolt in morality" in Judaism. This was not a military victory but a "revaluation of values" driven by a resolute will to power. This revolt overturned aristocratic frameworks and eventually led to Christianity. Nietzsche suggests this revolution was so successful that its results now seem "natural" or "obvious" to the modern world, making the historical shift nearly invisible.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why does Nietzsche distinguish genealogy from advocacy? Answer: To clarify that tracing the historical and psychological origins of a value (genealogy) is not the same as recommending or endorsing that value (advocacy).

  2. What does Nietzsche believe is the primary "gift" or contribution of slave morality to humanity? Answer: Slave morality produced "depth" and the "interior life," creating the "soul" and "conscience" that make human beings interesting.

  3. According to the text, what is the "genetic fallacy"? Answer: The error of judging the truth or value of a belief based solely on its origin or how it began.

  4. How does Nietzsche’s view of the Jewish role in morality differ from standard anti-Semitic interpretations? Answer: Nietzsche views the Jewish revaluation of values with "grudging, sometimes awestruck admiration," seeing it as a triumph of will and an extraordinary historical achievement rather than something contemptible.

  5. What is the fundamental difference in how Master and Slave moralities define "Good"? Answer: Master morality begins with a self-affirmation ("I am good"), whereas slave morality is reactive, defining itself as "good" only in opposition to an external "evil."

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Tension of Greatness: Nietzsche suggests that a world of pure master morality would be "shallow," while slave morality provides "depth." Analyze how the ongoing struggle between these two moralities—the tension between affirmation and negation—is necessary for human greatness according to the text.

  2. The Provocation of Origin: Discuss Nietzsche’s genealogical method as a tool for self-reflection. If our most "sacred" values (such as equality or pity) were proven to be rooted in the ressentiment of the powerless rather than rational truth, should that change our commitment to them? Use the text’s discussion of the "genetic fallacy" to support your argument.

  3. The Invisible Victory: Nietzsche claims the slave revolt in morality has been so successful for two thousand years that we can "no longer see" it. Explore the implications of a moral revolution that becomes so dominant it is mistaken for "natural" or "obvious" reality.

Glossary of Key Terms

  • Genealogy: The study of the origins and historical development of values, rituals, or beliefs.
  • Genetic Fallacy: The logical error of asserting that a belief is false or invalid simply because its source or origin is considered suspect or "all-too-human."
  • Internalization of Instinct: The process by which slave morality forced human instincts inward, creating the "soul" and the "interior life."
  • Master Morality: A value system rooted in the aristocratic spirit, characterized by self-affirmation, vitality, and creative power.
  • Ressentiment: A French term for resentment; the psychological state of the "slavish spirit" that defines itself by negating and labeling the powerful as "evil."
  • Revaluation of Values: The historical process, initiated during the "slave revolt," where traditional aristocratic values were overturned and replaced by a new moral framework.
  • Slave Morality: A reactive value system born from the needs of the powerless, emphasizing virtues like humility, pity, and self-denial.
