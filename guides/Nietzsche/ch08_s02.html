Study Guide: Connections and Implications in Nietzsche’s Critique of Christianity

This study guide explores the intellectual landscape surrounding Friedrich Nietzsche’s critique of Christianity as presented in The Antichrist. It examines the distinction between the historical figure of Jesus and the development of the Christian institution, parallels with other philosophical critiques, and the 20th-century theological movements influenced by Nietzsche’s assertions.

Key Concepts and Themes

The Historical Jesus vs. Church Doctrine

A central theme in Nietzsche’s work is the sharp distinction between the "historical Jesus" and the "Christ of church doctrine." Nietzsche argued that the original message of the founder was betrayed by the subsequent development of the Christian institution. While Nietzsche was not a formal historian, his analysis contributed to a long-standing scholarly debate regarding the gap between the actual life of Jesus and the religion that bears his name.

  • Nietzsche’s Portrayal: He depicted Jesus as a "Buddhist-anarchist-mystic." This portrait was less influenced by academic scholarship and more by literary figures like Tolstoy and Dostoevsky (specifically the protagonist of The Idiot).
  • The Scholarly Context: Nietzsche was aware of David Friedrich Strauss, who treated Gospel narratives as mythology. However, Nietzsche criticized Strauss for being "timid" and for attempting to replace Christianity with a "bland secular humanism" rather than facing the radical consequences of his own findings.

Kierkegaard and the Critique of "Christendom"

Søren Kierkegaard provides a compelling parallel to Nietzsche. Although they likely never read each other, they shared several core concerns:

Feature Søren Kierkegaard Friedrich Nietzsche
Target of Attack "Christendom" (The comfortable, state-sponsored institution). Christianity itself as a system of belief and morality.
View of the Church A "Sunday social club" that made faith too easy/soft. A betrayal of the original founder's intent by Paul.
Proposed Solution A "leap of faith" that is terrifying and irrational. Rejection of the system in favor of individual authenticity.
Commonalities Use of irony, pseudonyms, and a hatred for comfortable mediocrity. Focus on individual authenticity and indirect communication.

The "Death of God" Theology

In the 1960s, theologians such as Thomas Altizer and William Hamilton developed "Death of God" theology. This movement acknowledged Nietzsche’s diagnosis of the "death of God" not as a metaphysical statement about the existence of a deity, but as a "cultural reality." They argued that authentic faith must be lived without the traditional concept of a transcendent, supernatural being.

The Problem of Honest Faith

The text raises a persistent question for modern theology: Can religious faith be held honestly without self-deception or treating morality as a system of rewards? Various thinkers have offered responses:

  • Dietrich Bonhoeffer: Proposed a "religionless Christianity" while imprisoned by the Nazis.
  • Paul Tillich: Redefined God as the "ground of being."
  • Dostoevsky: Suggested faith is possible only through suffering.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why did Nietzsche criticize David Friedrich Strauss’s The Life of Jesus Critically Examined? Nietzsche despised Strauss not for his radical conclusions, but for his "timidity." He argued that Strauss demolished the foundations of Christianity but refused to face the consequences, instead proposing a comfortable secular humanism.

  2. How did Albert Schweitzer’s portrait of Jesus differ from Nietzsche’s? While Nietzsche saw Jesus as a mystic or "idiot" (in the Dostoevskian sense), Schweitzer argued in The Quest of the Historical Jesus (1906) that Jesus was an apocalyptic prophet whose message was centered on the imminent end of the world.

  3. What did Rudolf Bultmann believe about the historical Jesus? Bultmann contended that almost nothing could be known about the historical Jesus and that the focus of study should remain on the kerygma, or the existential meaning of the preached message.

  4. According to Kierkegaard, how had the institutional church made Christianity "impossible"? Kierkegaard believed the church made Christianity impossible by making it comfortable, bourgeois, and respectable, thereby removing the "leap of faith" and the radical demands inherent in the original Gospel.

  5. What is the core argument of the "Death of God" theologians of the 1960s? They argued that the transcendent God of traditional theism had died as a cultural reality, and that Christianity must adapt to a world where the old certainties of a supernatural being "up there" have collapsed.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Evolution of a Narrative: Compare and contrast Nietzsche’s literary interpretation of Jesus (the "Buddhist-anarchist-mystic") with the scholarly interpretations of E.P. Sanders, John Dominic Crossan, or N.T. Wright. How does the "gap" between history and doctrine serve Nietzsche’s broader philosophical goals?

  2. The Insider vs. The Outsider: Analyze the parallels between Nietzsche and Kierkegaard. How can two thinkers use similar methods (irony, attack on mediocrity) and identify similar betrayals of the "original message" yet arrive at diametrically opposed conclusions regarding the validity of faith?

  3. The Challenge of Modernity: Discuss the concept of "religionless Christianity" or God as the "ground of being" in the context of Nietzsche’s critique. To what extent do these 20th-century theological shifts successfully answer Nietzsche's challenge regarding the impossibility of "honest faith" in the modern world?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Christendom: A term used by Kierkegaard to describe the institutional, state-sponsored, and bourgeois version of Christianity that he believed betrayed the radical nature of the faith.
  • Death of God Theology: A mid-20th-century theological movement asserting that traditional theism is no longer culturally viable and that faith must be lived in a secularized context.
  • Ground of Being: Paul Tillich’s redefinition of God, moving away from a traditional "supernatural being" toward a more philosophical concept.
  • Historical Jesus: The figure of Jesus as he actually lived, as distinguished from the "Christ of faith" or the doctrines established by the church.
  • Kerygma: The preached message of the New Testament; Rudolf Bultmann argued this was the essential element of Christianity, regardless of historical facts about Jesus.
  • Leap of Faith: Kierkegaard’s concept of a necessary, irrational, and terrifying individual commitment to God that defies the comforts of institutional religion.
  • Religionless Christianity: A concept proposed by Dietrich Bonhoeffer regarding the practice of faith in a world where traditional religious structures have lost their meaning.
  • The "Idot": A reference to Dostoevsky's novel of the same name, used by Nietzsche to characterize Jesus as a figure of simple, mystical innocence.
