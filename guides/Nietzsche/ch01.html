Study Guide: Engaging with Nietzsche: His Style and Early Influences

Overview

This section introduces the unique and challenging way Friedrich Nietzsche writes philosophy, emphasizing that his unconventional style is a deliberate method designed to provoke thought and reveal the complex, contradictory nature of reality. It also provides an essential biographical sketch of Nietzsche's early life, tracing the personal and intellectual influences—from his religious family background and rigorous classical education to the profound impacts of Schopenhauer and Wagner—that shaped him into the philosopher he would become.

Key Concepts

  • Unconventional Philosophical Method: Nietzsche deliberately deviates from traditional philosophical argumentation, employing exaggeration, irony, and self-contradiction as tools rather than defects.
  • Aphoristic Style: The use of short, self-contained, numbered sections (aphorisms) that challenge the reader to connect ideas and tolerate tension, reflecting Nietzsche's belief that systematic philosophy is intellectually dishonest because reality is too messy for a single, logical structure.
  • Perspectivism: The idea that there is no single, absolute truth or perspective, and therefore philosophy must examine questions from multiple, often contradictory, angles to reveal different facets of understanding.
  • Role of Provocation: Nietzsche intentionally uses shocking or unsettling claims to challenge readers' unconscious assumptions, distinguishing between "doors" (provocations leading to deeper insight) and "walls" (genuine positions to engage with critically).
  • Reading for Questions: Emphasizes that Nietzsche's greatest contribution often lies in his profound questions rather than definitive answers, encouraging readers to focus on the enduring inquiries he poses.
  • Wiederkäuen (Rumination): Nietzsche's advice for deep, slow, and repetitive reading, akin to "chewing the cud," wherein a passage is revisited and contemplated multiple times to extract its full meaning.
  • Schopenhauer's Influence: Arthur Schopenhauer's philosophy, particularly his concept of the world as a "blind, striving Will" and the inherent suffering of existence, was a foundational "thunderbolt" for Nietzsche, despite his later rejection of Schopenhauer's pessimism in favor of life affirmation.
  • Wagner's Impact: Richard Wagner, the celebrated composer, profoundly influenced young Nietzsche, offering an initial vision of cultural redemption through art, though their friendship later soured.

Important Terms

  • Aphorism: A short, pithy, and often self-contained statement expressing a general truth or observation; Nietzsche's primary literary form.
  • Systematic Philosophy: A philosophical approach that aims to create a comprehensive, logically consistent, and interconnected framework to explain reality. Nietzsche rejected this.
  • Perspectival: The characteristic of being dependent on a particular perspective or viewpoint, implying that no single, objective view fully captures reality.
  • Provocation: In Nietzsche's context, an intentional statement or idea designed to disturb, challenge, or unsettle the reader's assumptions to stimulate genuine thought.
  • Wiederkäuen: A German term literally meaning "chewing the cud," used metaphorically by Nietzsche to describe the slow, deliberate, and repeated process of reading and contemplating his texts.
  • Classical Philology: The academic study of ancient Greek and Latin languages, literatures, and cultures, which was Nietzsche's initial academic profession.
  • Arthur Schopenhauer (1788–1860): A German philosopher whose work, The World as Will and Representation, profoundly influenced Nietzsche with its concept of a blind, irrational "Will" underlying existence.
  • Richard Wagner (1813–1883): A famous German composer and conductor, known for his operas and his vision of a comprehensive artwork (Gesamtkunstwerk), who became a significant friend and intellectual influence on Nietzsche.
  • The Will (Schopenhauerian): Schopenhauer's concept of a fundamental, irrational, and purposeless striving force that constitutes the underlying reality of the world and drives all phenomena, including human desires.

Detailed Notes

How to Read Nietzsche

Nietzsche's writing style is fundamentally different from that of traditional philosophers like Plato, Descartes, or Aristotle. This isn't a flaw but a deliberate philosophical method.

  1. Rejection of Traditional Argumentation:

    • He does not build careful, step-by-step demonstrations.
    • He avoids precise and consistent definitions of terms.
    • He frequently contradicts himself, exaggerates, insults, and employs deeply layered irony.
    • This "unconventional" style is a core part of his philosophy, reflecting his view of reality.

  2. The Aphoristic Style:

    • Many of his major works (Beyond Good and Evil, The Gay Science, The Antichrist) are composed of numbered aphorisms—short, self-contained sections.
    • Even On the Genealogy of Morals, a more continuous work, is sectioned.
    • Reasoning: Nietzsche believed that "systematic philosophy" (which attempts to capture the world in a logical, coherent structure) is a form of "intellectual dishonesty." Reality is inherently "messier, stranger, and more contradictory than any system can contain."
    • Aphorisms serve as individual "perspectives, provocations, a test" for the reader, who must actively connect them and be comfortable holding contradictory ideas in tension.

  3. Why He Contradicts Himself:

    • Contradictions are intentional and central to his "perspectival" philosophy.
    • If no single, absolute perspective on the world exists, then philosophy must explore questions from "multiple angles," each revealing something different.
    • Readers should not dismiss contradictions but interrogate what each perspective reveals and what Nietzsche is testing. The tension itself is often the point, showing the question's complexity.

  4. The Role of Provocation:

    • Nietzsche aims to "unsettle" and "make uncomfortable" his readers.
    • He uses shocking or offensive statements not for mere sensationalism, but because he believes genuine thinking requires challenging deep-seated, often unconscious, assumptions.
    • Readers should not always take his statements at face value; distinguish between genuine positions and dramatizations meant to expose something.
    • The "art of reading Nietzsche" involves discerning between "provocations that are doors" (leading to deeper insight) and "provocations that are walls" (serious positions that demand argument).

  5. Read for the Question, Not Just the Answer:

    • Nietzsche's primary gift lies in his profound and enduring questions, rather than elusive answers.
    • Examples: "Why do we value truth over untruth?" "Where do our moral judgments actually come from?" "What would it mean to take seriously the idea that God is dead?" "Can you affirm your life so completely that you would will its eternal repetition?"
    • These questions are meant to stay with the reader and stimulate ongoing thought.

  6. Nietzsche's Own Advice: Wiederkäuen (Rumination):

    • In On the Genealogy of Morals, he advises "Wiederkäuen" – literally, "chewing the cud."
    • This metaphor implies a slow, deep, and repeated engagement with the text, returning to passages multiple times.
    • He warns against quick reading and presuming understanding after a single read.
    • He calls for a reader who reads "slowly, deeply, with reservations, with doors left open, with delicate eyes and fingers" – like a "good philologist."
    • The textbook reinforces this, advising careful re-reading of Nietzsche's direct quotations, which are presented as "the main event."

Life and Times

  1. Early Life and Family (1844-1850s):

    • Born Friedrich Wilhelm Nietzsche on October 15, 1844, in Röcken, Prussian Saxony.
    • His father, Carl Ludwig Nietzsche, and both grandfathers were Lutheran pastors, creating an ironic contrast with his later critique of Christianity.
    • Father died when Friedrich was four (diagnosis: "softening of the brain"), leading to a lifelong dread of inherited neurological illness.
    • Raised in Naumburg in a household predominantly of women (mother, grandmother, aunts, younger sister Elisabeth).
    • A serious and intense child, already composing music and poetry; earned the nickname "the little pastor" for his ability to recite Bible verses.

  2. Schulpforta and Scholarship (1858-1864):

    • At 14, received a scholarship to Schulpforta, an elite German boarding school known for its rigorous classical curriculum.
    • The curriculum heavily emphasized Greek and Latin, profoundly shaping Nietzsche's prose style.
    • He developed a passion for classical philology (the study of ancient texts), which became his academic profession, though not his ultimate philosophical calling.
    • During this time, he also began to suffer from the debilitating migraines and digestive ailments that would plague him throughout his life, highlighting that much of his philosophy was written amidst physical pain.

  3. Leipzig: Schopenhauer's "Thunderbolt" (1864-1869):

    • Began studies at the University of Bonn, then transferred to Leipzig, where he excelled in classical philology under Friedrich Ritschl.
    • Defining Moment: In 1865, he discovered Arthur Schopenhauer's The World as Will and Representation in a secondhand bookshop.
    • This was a "philosophical conversion": Schopenhauer's worldview of a "blind, striving will"—purposeless, irrational, and indifferent to human happiness—struck Nietzsche with profound force.
    • Nietzsche was a devoted Schopenhauerian for several years, deeply influenced by his analysis of suffering, though he would eventually vehemently reject Schopenhauer's pessimistic conclusion of denying life.

  4. Basel: The Youngest Professor (1869-1879):

    • In 1869, at just 24, Nietzsche was appointed professor of classical philology at the University of Basel, Switzerland, without having formally completed his doctorate (it was granted retroactively).
    • He was the youngest person ever to hold such a chair.
    • While a conscientious teacher, his intellectual interests were already gravitating from pure scholarship towards philosophy.

  5. Wagner: Friendship and Fissure (1868-early 1870s):

    • In November 1868, Nietzsche met the renowned composer Richard Wagner.
    • Nietzsche (24) was intensely captivated by the older, charismatic Wagner (55) and his ambitious project, Der Ring des Nibelungen, which aimed for a redemption of German culture through art.
    • Their friendship, with Nietzsche frequently visiting Wagner at Tribschen, marked some of the happiest years of his life and significantly influenced his first book, The Birth of Tragedy (1872). This intense connection would later become a source of profound disillusionment.

Review Questions

  1. Nietzsche intentionally rejects conventional philosophical argumentation. Discuss at least three specific characteristics of his writing style (e.g., aphorisms, contradictions, provocation) and explain why he considers them methods rather than defects.
  2. What does Nietzsche mean by describing reality as "messier, stranger, and more contradictory than any system can contain"? How does this belief directly inform his choice of the aphoristic style?
  3. Explain the concept of "perspectivism" in Nietzsche's philosophy, particularly how it relates to his use of contradictions. As a reader, how should one approach seemingly contradictory claims in his work?
  4. Nietzsche advises readers to engage in "Wiederkäuen." Describe what this practice entails and discuss its implications for how one should approach complex philosophical texts, both Nietzsche's and others.
  5. Trace the early biographical influences on Nietzsche's intellectual development, specifically detailing how his family background, classical education, and initial encounters with Schopenhauer and Wagner shaped his philosophical trajectory, even when he later diverged from their views.
