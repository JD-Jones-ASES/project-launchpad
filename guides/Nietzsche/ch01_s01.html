The Life and Times of Friedrich Nietzsche: A Comprehensive Study Guide

This study guide provides a detailed overview of the life, intellectual development, and posthumous legacy of Friedrich Nietzsche (1844–1900). It covers his early influences, his rapid academic rise, his pivotal relationships, and the eventual distortion of his work by his sister.

I. Biographical Overview and Intellectual Development

Early Life and Family Background

Friedrich Wilhelm Nietzsche was born on October 15, 1844, in Röcken, Saxony. His upbringing was deeply rooted in Lutheran piety; both his father, Carl Ludwig Nietzsche, and both grandfathers were pastors.

  • Paternal Influence: Nietzsche's father died when Friedrich was four years old from "softening of the brain." This event haunted Nietzsche, leading to a lifelong fear that he had inherited a neurological weakness.
  • The Naumburg Household: Following his father's death, Nietzsche was raised in a household of women, including his mother, sister Elisabeth, grandmother, and two aunts.
  • Early Disposition: As a child, Nietzsche was serious and talented, composing music and writing poetry before age ten. His schoolmates nicknamed him "the little pastor" due to his ability to recite Bible verses from memory.

Academic Excellence and Health

Nietzsche’s formal education was marked by brilliance and physical suffering.

  • Schulpforta: At fourteen, he attended the prestigious Schulpforta boarding school, which emphasized a rigorous classical curriculum in Greek and Latin. This training permanently shaped his prose style.
  • The University Years: He studied classical philology at the University of Bonn and later Leipzig under Friedrich Ritschl.
  • Chronic Illness: During his student years, Nietzsche began suffering from migraines and digestive ailments. He produced most of his philosophical work while in significant physical pain.

The Basel Professorship

In 1869, at the unprecedented age of twenty-four, Nietzsche was appointed professor of classical philology at the University of Basel. He was awarded his doctorate retroactively by the University of Leipzig without a dissertation defense, based solely on his published work. He remains the youngest person ever to hold that chair.

II. Primary Intellectual Influences and Relationships

Arthur Schopenhauer

In 1865, Nietzsche discovered Schopenhauer’s The World as Will and Representation. This encounter was a "philosophical conversion."

  • The Will: Schopenhauer argued that reality is a blind, purposeless, irrational "Will" that drives nature.
  • Nietzsche’s Departure: While Nietzsche adopted Schopenhauer's analysis of suffering, he eventually rejected Schopenhauer's pessimistic conclusion (denying life) in favor of life-affirmation.

Richard Wagner

Nietzsche met the composer Richard Wagner in 1868. Their relationship was intense but ultimately collapsed.

  • Early Adulation: Nietzsche’s first book, The Birth of Tragedy (1872), was heavily influenced by Wagner’s artistic vision of redeeming German culture through art.
  • The Break: Nietzsche eventually grew repulsed by Wagner’s increasing German nationalism, anti-Semitism, megalomania, and his opera Parsifal, which Nietzsche viewed as a "capitulation to Christianity."

Lou Salomé

Introduced to Nietzsche in 1882 by Paul Rée, Lou Salomé was a Russian intellectual who Nietzsche hoped would become a philosophical collaborator.

  • The "Holy Trinity": Nietzsche, Rée, and Salomé formed an intellectual commune. A famous staged photograph from this era depicts Salomé holding a whip while Nietzsche and Rée are harnessed to a cart.
  • Rejection: Nietzsche proposed marriage multiple times; Salomé refused, preferring intellectual friendship. The relationship ended in late 1882, leaving Nietzsche devastated.

III. The Final Decade and Collapse

The Wandering Years (1879–1888)

Nietzsche resigned from Basel in 1879 due to ill health. For ten years, he lived as a wanderer in the Swiss Alps, Italy, and the French Riviera. Despite isolation and poor book sales (e.g., Beyond Good and Evil sold fewer than 200 copies in its first year), this was his most productive period, yielding:

  • The Gay Science
  • Thus Spoke Zarathustra
  • Beyond Good and Evil
  • On the Genealogy of Morals

The Collapse into Madness

On January 3, 1889, in Turin, Nietzsche suffered a mental breakdown, reportedly after witnessing a coachman beating a horse.

  • Wahnsinnsbriefe: Following his collapse, he sent "madness letters" to friends, signing them as "Dionysos" or "The Crucified."
  • Final Years: Nietzsche spent his last eleven years in a state of mental decline, cared for by his mother and sister. He died on August 25, 1900.

IV. Posthumous Legacy and the "Sister Problem"

The distortion of Nietzsche’s philosophy is largely attributed to his sister, Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche.

Action Impact on Nietzsche's Legacy
Control of Archive Elisabeth seized control of the Nietzsche Archive in Weimar after Friedrich's collapse.
Editing The Will to Power She compiled unpublished notes into a "systematic" book, altering passages to support her own nationalist and anti-Semitic views.
Political Alignment Elisabeth was married to Bernhard Förster, a prominent anti-Semite. She later aligned the Archive with National Socialism, welcoming Adolf Hitler in 1934.
Direct Contradiction Nietzsche himself despised anti-Semitism and called Bernhard Förster's "pure" German colony in Paraguay ("Nueva Germania") idiotic.

V. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What was the medical diagnosis for Nietzsche’s father’s death, and how did it affect Nietzsche?
  2. How did Nietzsche’s education at Schulpforta influence his later work?
  3. Why was Nietzsche’s appointment at the University of Basel considered "unprecedented"?
  4. Describe the concept of the "Will" according to Schopenhauer.
  5. What specific characteristics of Richard Wagner eventually led to Nietzsche’s break with him?
  6. Who was Paul Rée, and what was his role in Nietzsche's life?
  7. Identify the three primary locations where Nietzsche lived during his "wandering years."
  8. What are the "Wahnsinnsbriefe," and how were they signed?
  9. How did Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche manipulate the book The Will to Power?
  10. What was "Nueva Germania," and what was Nietzsche’s opinion of it?

VI. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Tension of Piety and Rebellion: Analyze how Nietzsche’s upbringing in a "steeped Protestant" household and his childhood moniker "the little pastor" contrast with his later philosophical "war on Christianity."
  2. Suffering as a Catalyst for Philosophy: Discuss the relationship between Nietzsche’s chronic physical ailments and his philosophical output. How does his rejection of Schopenhauer’s pessimism reflect his own struggle with health?
  3. The Salomé Influence: Examine the "holy trinity" relationship between Nietzsche, Salomé, and Rée. How does the staged photograph with the whip challenge or complicate the view of Nietzsche as a straightforward misogynist?
  4. The Distortion of Legacy: Evaluate the role of Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche in the 20th-century perception of Nietzsche. To what extent was the association between Nietzsche and National Socialism a deliberate construction rather than a reflection of his actual philosophy?

VII. Glossary of Key Terms and Figures

  • Bernhard Förster: Nietzsche’s brother-in-law; an anti-Semitic agitator who founded a failed "pure" Aryan colony in Paraguay.
  • Classical Philology: The study of ancient Greek and Latin texts; Nietzsche’s original academic profession.
  • Dionysos: One of the names Nietzsche used to sign his "madness letters" following his 1889 collapse.
  • Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche: Nietzsche’s sister; responsible for editing his work to fit nationalist agendas and for managing his literary estate.
  • Friedrich Ritschl: The professor at Leipzig who recognized Nietzsche’s brilliance and recommended him for the Basel professorship.
  • Lou Salomé: A brilliant Russian intellectual and author who rejected Nietzsche's marriage proposals but remained a significant intellectual figure of her generation.
  • Parsifal: Richard Wagner’s final opera, which Nietzsche viewed as a betrayal of his earlier artistic ideals and a return to Christian themes.
  • The Will to Power: A posthumous book compiled by Elisabeth from Nietzsche's notebooks; often incorrectly viewed as his definitive systematic work.
  • Wahnsinnsbriefe: The "madness letters" sent by Nietzsche in early 1889, marking the final stage of his mental health.
  • The World as Will and Representation: The seminal work by Arthur Schopenhauer that served as a major intellectual "thunderbolt" for the young Nietzsche.
