# Nietzsche’s Two Moralities: A Comprehensive Study Guide

This study guide examines Friedrich Nietzsche’s analysis of the historical and psychological origins of moral values, as presented in *Beyond Good and Evil* and *On the Genealogy of Morals*. It explores the fundamental distinction between Master Morality and Slave Morality, the mechanism of the "slave revolt in morality," and the correction of common historical misinterpretations regarding Nietzsche's work.

---

## I. The Core Framework: Two Types of Morality

Nietzsche argues that "morality" is not a singular, universal truth but rather a product of different human experiences. He identifies two primary types of value systems born from the lived realities of different social classes.

### Master Morality (*Herrenmoral*)
*   **Origin:** The ruling aristocratic class.
*   **Primary Distinction:** "Good" vs. "Bad."
*   **Core Values:** Strength, health, beauty, pride, self-affirmation, and a "pathos of distance."
*   **Direction of Valuation:** Spontaneous and active. It begins with a "yes" to oneself and one's own power. The "bad" is merely a secondary afterthought—a pale foil representing that which is common, weak, or contemptible.
*   **Key Traits:** The noble man regards himself as a "creator of values." He honors what he recognizes in himself and acts out of a "super-abundance of power" rather than pity.

### Slave Morality (*Sklavenmoral*)
*   **Origin:** The oppressed, the suffering, and the powerless.
*   **Primary Distinction:** "Good" vs. "Evil."
*   **Core Values:** Humility, pity, patience, diligence, friendliness, and utility.
*   **Direction of Valuation:** Reactive and negative. it begins with a "no" to the "other" (the master/oppressor). It defines the powerful as "evil" and only then, by negation, defines the self as "good."
*   **Key Traits:** Driven by *ressentiment*—a creative hatred that seeks imaginary revenge. It views the "good" man as the "safe" man: good-natured, easily deceived, and perhaps a little stupid.

## II. Key Concepts and Historical Processes

### The Slave Revolt in Morality (*Sklavenaufstand in der Moral*)
Nietzsche describes a long historical process in which slave morality conquered master morality in the West. He identifies the "priestly type" as the architect of this revolt. Lacking physical power, priests used "the cleverest revenge"—a radical transvaluation of values. By declaring the powerful "evil" and the wretched "blessed," they overturned the aristocratic equation (Good = Noble = Beautiful = Happy = Loved by Gods).

### The Pathos of Distance
This term describes the feeling of rank and difference that characterizes an aristocratic society. It is the psychological origin of the value judgment "good." The ruling class perceives itself as fundamentally different in kind from those beneath them. This distance creates the "longing for an ever new widening of distance within the soul itself" and the "self-surmounting of man."

### The Parable of the Lambs and Birds of Prey
Nietzsche uses this parable to illustrate the logic of slave morality:
*   **The Lambs:** Dislike the birds of prey for eating them and conclude that birds of prey are "evil." They further conclude that anyone who is the opposite of a bird of prey—namely, a lamb—is "good."
*   **The Birds of Prey:** Do not bear a grudge against the lambs; in fact, they like them because they are "tasty."
*   **The Insight:** It is absurd to demand that strength *not* express itself as strength. Slave morality depends on the "superstitious changeling" of the "subject" (or soul)—the belief that the strong man has the "option" to be weak, thereby making his strength a moral failing and the lamb's weakness a "merit."

### The "Blond Beast" (*Blonde Bestie*)
A frequently misunderstood metaphor, the "blond beast" refers to a **lion**—the golden-maned predator. It represents the untamed, predatory energy and "beast of prey" conscience at the core of all noble races, including Romans, Arabs, Germans, Japanese, and Homeric heroes. It is an animal metaphor for raw vitality, not a racial descriptor for Nordic people.

## III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **How does Nietzsche define the "pathos of distance"?**
    *   *Answer:* It is the feeling of rank and separation between the ruling class and the ruled. It is the emotional precondition for the creation of values, where the noble class arrogates the right to name things and define "good" based on their own superiority.
2.  **What is the etymological origin of the German word for "bad" (*schlecht*) according to Nietzsche?**
    *   *Answer:* It is identical to the word *schlicht* (plain or simple), originally used to denote the "plebeian" or "common" man in contrast to the aristocrat, without an initial sense of moral condemnation.
3.  **Why does Nietzsche call slave morality "reactive"?**
    *   *Answer:* Unlike master morality, which begins with a spontaneous self-affirmation ("I am good"), slave morality requires an external "evil" enemy to react against. It defines itself only by saying "no" to what is different from itself.
4.  **What role did the "priestly nation" of the Jews play in Nietzsche's genealogy?**
    *   *Answer:* Nietzsche credits them with initiating the "slave revolt in morality" through a radical transvaluation of values, replacing aristocratic values with the idea that the poor, sick, and lowly are the only "good" and "pious" ones.
5.  **What was Nietzsche’s stance on anti-Semitism?**
    *   *Answer:* Nietzsche was explicitly and vehemently opposed to anti-Semitism. He described it as a "folly" and an "obscuration of the German spirit," mocked anti-Semitic "bawlers," and broke personal ties with Richard Wagner and his own sister over their anti-Semitic views.

## IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Creativity of Ressentiment:** Nietzsche argues that while master morality is more vital, slave morality produced "depth" and the "human soul as we know it." Analyze how the "imaginary revenge" of the powerless leads to a more complex internal life than the "spontaneous" action of the aristocrat.
2.  **The Illusion of the "Subject":** Discuss Nietzsche's critique of the idea that a "doer" exists behind an "action." How does the belief in a free-choosing soul serve the interests of slave morality, and how does it relate to his parable of the lambs?
3.  **Exploitation as an Organic Function:** In *Beyond Good and Evil* 259, Nietzsche claims that exploitation "belongs to the nature of the living being as a primary organic function." Evaluate his argument that a society seeking to eliminate "injury, violence, and exploitation" is actually expressing a "Will to the Denial of life."
4.  **The History of a Misunderstanding:** Trace the distortion of Nietzsche’s "blond beast" concept. Using the source text, explain how the metaphor of the lion was co-opted for racial ideology and how Nietzsche's own writing refutes this appropriation.

## V. Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Ressentiment** | The festering, creative hatred of the powerless who, unable to act, take revenge through the imaginary revaluation of values. |
| **Sklavenaufstand** | "The slave revolt in morality"; the historical triumph of slave values (humility, pity) over master values (pride, strength). |
| **Genealogy** | Nietzsche’s method of tracing the historical and psychological origins of moral concepts to reveal the power dynamics and instincts behind them. |
| **Pathos der Distanz** | The "pathos of distance"; the feeling of rank and difference that separates higher from lower types, serving as the basis for value creation. |
| **Disintéressement** | French for "disinterestedness"; the idea of acting without self-interest, which master morality views with enmity and irony. |
| **Sipo Matador** | A climbing plant in Java used as a metaphor for an aristocracy; it encircles an oak to reach the light, supported by the tree it suppresses. |
| **Esprit de corps** | Collective loyalty and pride within a social class; the "group spirit" of the higher dominant race. |
| **Un bonhomme** | A "good man" in the context of slave morality; implies someone who is safe, good-natured, easily deceived, and "a little stupid." |
| **Gai saber** | "The gay science"; refers to the brilliant, ingenious culture of the Provençal poet-cavaliers to whom Nietzsche attributes the noble origin of "love as a passion." |
| **Res facta** | A "thing made"; something manufactured or artificial, used by Nietzsche to describe the modern concept of a "nation." |
