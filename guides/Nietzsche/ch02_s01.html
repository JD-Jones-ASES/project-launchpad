Study Guide: The Prejudices of Philosophers

This study guide examines Friedrich Nietzsche’s critique of the Western philosophical tradition as presented in the opening of Beyond Good and Evil. It explores his deconstruction of the "Will to Truth," his claim that philosophy is essentially a form of personal confession, and his introduction of the "Will to Power" as the fundamental driver of human thought.

I. Key Concepts and Themes

1. The Will to Truth (Wille zur Wahrheit)

Nietzsche challenges the unexamined assumption that truth is inherently more valuable than untruth, uncertainty, or ignorance. He characterizes the "Will to Truth" not as a disinterested pursuit of facts, but as a drive that requires questioning. He asks: Why do we want truth? This drive, he suggests, may be a manifestation of the "Will to Power"—a desire to master and categorize reality.

2. Philosophy as Autobiography

Nietzsche asserts that every great philosophy is an "involuntary and unconscious auto-biography" of its creator. Rather than being the product of "pure reason," a philosopher’s metaphysical assertions are grounded in their personal morality, temperament, and instinctive needs. The "impulse to knowledge" is merely an instrument used by other, more fundamental impulses.

3. Untruth as a Condition of Life

One of Nietzsche’s most radical claims is that certain "false" judgments are indispensable to human existence. He argues that without "logical fictions"—such as the belief in stable objects, the soul, or the ego—man could not live. To recognize that untruth is a necessity for life is to move "beyond good and evil" in a philosophical sense.

4. Perspectivism

Perspectivism is the doctrine that all knowledge is shaped by the specific perspective of the knower. There is no objective "view from nowhere." Every claim to truth reflects the interests, values, and physiological conditions of the individual making the claim.

5. The Will to Power (Wille zur Macht)

This is the fundamental drive Nietzsche identifies in all living things. It is not simply a desire for political or physical dominance, but the drive to grow, overcome resistance, and master one's environment. Nietzsche views even the most abstract philosophical systems as expressions of this "spiritual Will to Power."

II. Specific Philosophical Critiques

Nietzsche uses specific examples to illustrate how philosophers succumb to their own prejudices.

Target Core Critique Nietzsche's Argument
The Dogmatists Failed "courtship" of Truth. Dogmatists approach Truth with "clumsy importunity," assuming it can be won through rigid, heavy-handed logic rather than subtlety.
The Stoics Living "according to Nature." Nature is indifferent and purposeless. The Stoics projected their own values (order, rationality) onto Nature and then claimed to "discover" them there.
Descartes Cogito ergo sum ("I think, therefore I am"). Descartes assumes "I" is the cause of thought. Nietzsche argues "a thought comes when 'it' wishes," and the "I" is merely a grammatical habit or interpretation.
Metaphysicians Belief in Antitheses of Values. Metaphysicians assume truth and error are opposites. Nietzsche suggests they may be "essentially identical" or that "untruth" might have higher value for life.

III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to Nietzsche’s preface, why have dogmatic philosophers failed to "win" Truth?
  2. What does Nietzsche mean by the term "puerilism" in the context of philosophical edifices?
  3. How does Nietzsche redefine the relationship between a philosopher’s morality and their intellectual system?
  4. Why does Nietzsche refer to the Stoics as "extraordinary stage-players and self-deluders"?
  5. What are "frog perspectives," and what do they represent in Nietzsche's critique of metaphysics?
  6. According to Section 17, why is it a "perversion of the facts" to say that the subject "I" is the condition of the predicate "think"?
  7. What does Nietzsche identify as the "true vital germ" of every philosophy?
  8. In what way does Nietzsche suggest that "numbers" are a form of "counterfeiting" the world?

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Metaphor of Truth: Analyze Nietzsche’s opening metaphor: "Supposing that Truth is a woman—what then?" How does this image set the stage for his critique of dogmatism, and what does it suggest about his proposed "new order of philosophers"?
  2. The Value of Illusion: Nietzsche claims that "the renunciation of false opinions would be a renunciation of life." Discuss the implications of this statement. If "untruth" is a condition of life, what does this do to the traditional philosopher’s quest for absolute certainty?
  3. The Grammar of the Ego: Explore Nietzsche’s linguistic critique of Descartes. How does the structure of language (subject-verb-object) influence metaphysical beliefs about the "soul" or "ego"? Why does Nietzsche believe "grammar" is a form of "deception"?
  4. Will to Power as Methodology: Define "Will to Power" based on the text. How does Nietzsche use this concept to explain both the growth of a plant and the construction of a complex philosophical system like Platonism or Kantianism?

V. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Aphorism: A short, pithy statement expressing a general truth; in Nietzsche’s work, these are compressed arguments or thought experiments.
  • Causa Prima: Latin for "first cause"; the ultimate or original cause of everything.
  • Cogito ergo sum: "I think, therefore I am"; the foundational certainty of Cartesian philosophy that Nietzsche critiques as a nest of assumptions.
  • De omnibus dubitandum: "Everything is to be doubted"; the motto of Cartesian philosophy.
  • Dogmatism: The tendency to assert principles as undeniable truths without critically examining underlying assumptions.
  • Logos: A Greek term meaning "word," "reason," or "principle of order"; used by Stoics to describe the rational principle pervading the cosmos.
  • Perspectivism: The view that all knowledge and "truth" are filtered through the specific perspective and interests of the knower.
  • Puerilism: Childish or immature thinking.
  • Synthetic judgments a priori: Knowledge or reasoning independent of experience; Nietzsche refers to these as "logical fictions" necessary for life.
  • Tyronism: The state of being a beginner or novice; amateurishness.
  • Wille zur Wahrheit: "The will to truth"; the drive that compels philosophers to seek objective knowledge.
