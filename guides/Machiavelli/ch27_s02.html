Study Guide: Machiavelli's Exhortation to Liberate Italy (The Prince, Chapter XXVI)

Overview

In the final, emotionally charged chapter of The Prince, Niccolò Machiavelli abandons his analytical stance to make a passionate patriotic appeal. He argues that Italy's extreme suffering and political fragmentation create a unique and divinely favored opportunity for a new prince, specifically the Medici family, to unify and liberate the nation from foreign domination. Machiavelli outlines the necessity of native forces and a reformed military strategy, urging the chosen leader to seize this moment to restore Italy's ancient glory.

Key Concepts

  • Machiavelli's Patriotism and Purpose: This chapter reveals Machiavelli's underlying patriotic motive for writing The Prince. He shifts from objective analysis of power to a fervent call for Italian liberation, positioning his entire work as a guide for a potential liberator.
  • The Propitious Time: Italy's dire state—enslaved, oppressed, scattered, and ravaged by foreign powers—is presented as the perfect, even divinely ordained, moment for a new, virtuous leader to emerge and establish a new order. Extreme suffering often precedes great change.
  • Historical Parallels to Great Liberators: Machiavelli draws comparisons to Moses, Cyrus, and Theseus, legendary figures who led their people out of oppression. He argues that the circumstances in Italy are equally, if not more, ripe for a heroic leader to manifest their "virtue."
  • Virtue (Virtù) and Fortune: While fortune has brought Italy to this desperate state and prepared the ground, it is the "virtue" (skill, courage, decisiveness) of a human leader that must "do the rest" to achieve liberation. God has shown the way, but humans must act.
  • Divine Favor: Machiavelli suggests that God has clearly manifested extraordinary signs (like dividing the sea or raining manna for Moses) to demonstrate favor for the cause of Italian liberation, implying a moral and divine mandate for the prince who undertakes this task.
  • Need for a New Order and Laws: Italy's repeated military failures are attributed to a flawed "old order" and a lack of new, effective laws and ordinances. A new prince must establish these foundations to achieve lasting reverence and success.
  • Italian Individual Valor vs. Collective Weakness: Machiavelli observes that individual Italians possess great "strength, dexterity, and subtlety" in combat, but their armies consistently fail due to "insufficiency of leaders" and a lack of unified command and obedience.
  • Importance of Native Forces: A core tenet of Machiavelli's military philosophy is reiterated: a prince must rely on his "own forces" (citizen soldiers) rather than mercenaries, as they are the most faithful, true, and effective soldiers, fighting for their prince and country.
  • Critique of Foreign Infantries: Machiavelli analyzes the specific weaknesses of the formidable Swiss and Spanish infantry—Spaniards are vulnerable to cavalry, and Swiss to close-combat infantry. This critique paves the way for his proposal for a new Italian infantry that could overcome these defects and achieve superiority.
  • Call to the Medici Family: The "illustrious house" (the Medici family, particularly Lorenzo de' Medici) is directly addressed as the most hopeful candidate to lead this liberation, possessing the necessary "valour and fortune," favored by God and the Church.
  • Petrarch's Prophetic Verse: The chapter concludes with a quote from Petrarch, a famous Italian poet, which serves as a poetic rallying cry, asserting that the "ancient Roman valour" is not dead in Italian hearts and will rise against "fury" (foreign invaders).

Important Terms

  • Exhortation: A communication, speech, or discourse that strongly urges or encourages someone to do something. In this context, Machiavelli's fervent plea for action.
  • Propitious: Giving or indicating a good chance of success; favorable. Machiavelli sees the current state of Italy as a propitious time for a new prince.
  • Virtù (Machiavellian): A concept encompassing a prince's political skill, strength, courage, drive, and ability to act decisively and effectively to achieve goals and maintain the state. Distinct from conventional moral virtue.
  • Fortune: The unpredictable element of chance, luck, or fate. Machiavelli saw fortune as a powerful force but believed a virtuous prince could mitigate its effects.
  • Moses, Cyrus, Theseus: Biblical and historical figures cited by Machiavelli as examples of great leaders who liberated their people from oppression during times of extreme crisis.
  • Illustrious House: Refers to the powerful and prominent Medici family, who were the rulers of Florence and to whom The Prince was dedicated. Machiavelli saw them as having the potential to unite Italy.
  • Lombardy, Tuscany, Kingdom (of Naples): Regions of Italy that were suffering under foreign occupation or influence during Machiavelli's time, highlighting the widespread nature of the oppression.
  • Native Forces: An army composed of a state's own citizens, as opposed to mercenary (foreign hired) soldiers. Machiavelli believed these were essential for a strong, stable state.
  • Swiss Infantry: Renowned mercenary soldiers, famous for their pike formations, considered highly effective in Machiavelli's time.
  • Spanish Infantry: Another highly respected infantry force of the era, known for their discipline and use of swords and shields in close combat.
  • Il Taro, Allesandria, Capua, Genoa, Vaila, Bologna, Mestri: Locations of battles or sieges where Italian armies (often mercenary) suffered significant defeats, cited by Machiavelli as evidence of their military weakness.
  • Petrarch: Francesco Petrarch (1304–1374), a celebrated Italian scholar and poet of the Renaissance, whose verse "Virtue against fury shall advance the fight..." Machiavelli uses as a powerful concluding statement.

Detailed Notes

The Context and Shift in Tone

Chapter XXVI marks a dramatic shift in The Prince. After twenty-five chapters of dispassionate analysis on acquiring and maintaining states, Machiavelli reveals his true patriotic fervor. This final section is an "exhortation," a passionate appeal, demonstrating that his practical advice was ultimately intended to guide a leader towards the liberation and unification of Italy.

Italy's Desperate State: A Perfect Opportunity

Machiavelli begins by observing that the current times are uniquely "propitious" for a new prince. Italy is in an extreme state of suffering:

  • Enslaved, oppressed, scattered: Italy is under foreign dominion, fragmented into smaller, powerless states.
  • Without head, without order, beaten, despoiled, torn, overrun: A vivid description of the chaos, lack of leadership, and military defeats Italy has endured.
  • Yearning for a deliverer: The country "entreats God to send someone who shall deliver her" and is "ready and willing to follow a banner if only someone will raise it." Machiavelli compares this desperate situation to the circumstances that gave rise to great liberators like Moses (Hebrews enslaved), Cyrus (Persians oppressed by Medes), and Theseus (Athenians dispersed). He argues that Italy's condition is even worse, making the opportunity for a glorious leader even greater. A "spark" of hope was seen previously, but fortune turned against it, leaving Italy still "without life."

The Chosen Leader: The Illustrious House of Medici

Machiavelli directs his appeal to the "illustrious house" (understood to be the Medici family, particularly Lorenzo de' Medici). He highlights their advantages:

  • Valour and fortune: They possess inherent strength and favorable circumstances.
  • Favoured by God and the Church: Machiavelli suggests divine backing for their cause, emphasizing that God is on their side, much as he aided Moses.
  • Divine Intervention: Machiavelli lists miraculous events from biblical history (divided sea, cloud leading the way, rock pouring water, manna) as signs that God has prepared the way for Italian liberation, but insists that the prince must "do the rest," as "God is not willing to do everything, and thus take away our free will." Machiavelli asserts that the Medici's enterprise is no less just or easy than those of Moses, Cyrus, or Theseus. He states, "that war is just which is necessary, and arms are hallowed when there is no other hope but in them," providing a moral justification for their potential military action.

The Failures of the Past and the Need for a New Order

Machiavelli addresses why previous Italian attempts at liberation have failed:

  • "Old order of things was not good": The existing political and military structures were inadequate.
  • Lack of innovation: "None of us have known how to find a new one." A new prince, therefore, has an unparalleled opportunity to establish "new laws and new ordinances," which Machiavelli believes is the greatest source of honor and reverence for a ruler, especially one who rises newly to power.

Italian Military Potential and the Imperative of Native Forces

Machiavelli identifies a critical paradox in Italian military capability:

  • Individual prowess: Italians are superior in "strength, dexterity, and subtlety" in duels and hand-to-hand combat.
  • Collective weakness: Italian armies fail compared to foreign forces because of "insufficiency of the leaders" and a lack of unity and obedience among commanders. There has been no single leader distinguished enough to command universal respect. He cites examples of Italian armies giving "a poor account of itself" in numerous battles (Il Taro, Allesandria, Capua, etc.) over twenty years.

To remedy this, Machiavelli stresses the absolute necessity of:

  • "Your own forces": A national army composed of Italian citizens, loyal to their prince and their homeland, who are more faithful and effective than mercenaries.
  • A New Military Order: He proposes a new infantry that can overcome the specific weaknesses of the formidable Swiss and Spanish forces.
    • Spanish defect: Cannot resist cavalry.
    • Swiss defect: Afraid of infantry in close combat (as seen at Ravenna against German battalions, who adopted Swiss tactics). By combining strengths and avoiding these defects, a new Italian military order could be created, not necessarily creating "a new order of arms, but a variation upon the old," which would bring immense "reputation and power" to the new prince.

The Call to Action and Petrarch's Prophecy

Machiavelli concludes with an impassioned plea for the Medici to seize this moment:

  • Universal reception: The liberator would be received with immense "love," "thirst for revenge," "stubborn faith," "devotion," and "tears" across Italy, as "this barbarous dominion stinks."
  • No resistance: "What door would be closed to him? Who would refuse obedience to him? What envy would hinder him? What Italian would refuse him homage?" He urges the "illustrious house" to undertake this "just enterprise" with courage and hope. The chapter ends with a stirring quote from Petrarch: "Virtue against fury shall advance the fight... For the old Roman valour is not dead, Nor in th’ Italians’ brests extinguished." This serves as a powerful affirmation of Italy's latent strength and glorious past, awaiting a leader to ignite it.

Review Questions

  1. How does Machiavelli's tone and ultimate purpose shift in Chapter XXVI compared to the preceding chapters of The Prince? What does this reveal about his overall intent in writing the book?
  2. Explain why Machiavelli believes the current fragmented and oppressed state of Italy is actually the most "propitious time" for a new prince to emerge. What historical figures does he reference to support this idea?
  3. What are Machiavelli's main criticisms of Italian military performance during his time? How does he propose to rectify these weaknesses, and what specific advantages does he see in "native forces"?
  4. Discuss Machiavelli's view on the interplay between "virtue" and "fortune" in the context of liberating Italy. How does he incorporate the idea of divine favor into his argument, and what role does he assign to human agency?
  5. To whom does Machiavelli specifically direct his "exhortation," and what qualities or circumstances does he highlight as making them uniquely suited for the task of unifying and liberating Italy? How does the concluding quote from Petrarch reinforce his message?
