Fortune and Free Will: Navigating the Tides of Power in The Prince

Overview

This section addresses the crucial question of human agency versus fate, particularly in the realm of political power. Machiavelli grapples with the idea that if Fortune controls everything, human effort is pointless. He concludes that while Fortune governs roughly half of our actions, the other half is subject to our free will and foresight, emphasizing the need for adaptability and preparation to withstand unpredictable circumstances.

Key Concepts

  • The Power of Fortune: Machiavelli acknowledges the widespread belief that external forces (Fortune and God) govern world affairs, often unpredictably, and that human wisdom cannot direct them. He admits to being somewhat inclined to this view given the great changes of his time.
  • Free Will and Human Agency: Despite Fortune's power, Machiavelli asserts that humans retain free will over approximately half of their actions. This capacity allows for preparation and strategic responses to external forces, preventing the complete extinguishing of human effort.
  • The River Analogy: Fortune is likened to a raging river in flood, sweeping everything away. However, during calm times, people can build defenses (dikes, barriers) to channel its power and mitigate its destructive impact when it floods again. This illustrates that while Fortune's power is immense, it can be resisted and managed through foresight and "valour."
  • The "Spirit of the Times": Success for a prince depends on aligning one's actions and methods (caution, haste, force, skill) with the prevailing circumstances or "spirit of the times." Actions that are appropriate in one period may lead to ruin in another, even if the prince's character remains consistent.
  • The Challenge of Adaptability: Machiavelli notes that humans find it difficult to change their fundamental nature or methods, especially if past actions have led to success. This rigidity prevents them from adapting when the "spirit of the times" shifts, often leading to their downfall.
  • Impetuosity vs. Caution: While different approaches can succeed if they match the times, Machiavelli offers a personal conclusion: it is better to be adventurous (impetuous) than cautious. He metaphorically describes Fortune as a woman who is more easily mastered by those who are bold, violent, and audacious.

Important Terms

  • Fortune: In Machiavelli's context, Fortune refers to the unpredictable, uncontrollable forces of chance, fate, or external circumstances that influence human events and can elevate or depose rulers.
  • Free Will: The human capacity to make choices and direct one's own actions, even when faced with powerful external forces. Machiavelli argues it accounts for roughly half of human affairs.
  • Valour: (Implied by the text, though not explicitly named "virtù") Refers to the strength, courage, skill, and effective action a prince employs to resist or prepare for Fortune's challenges. It encompasses competence and decisive action.
  • Spirit of the Times: The prevailing conditions, trends, opportunities, or demands of a specific period or situation. A prince's success hinges on conforming their methods to this spirit.
  • Impetuous: Acting quickly, spontaneously, and sometimes rashly, driven by sudden emotion or instinct. Machiavelli contrasts this with cautious behavior.
  • Circumspect: Careful, cautious, and unwilling to take risks; characterized by prudence and forethought.

Detailed Notes

The Dilemma of Fortune and Free Will

Machiavelli begins by addressing a fundamental question that challenges the premise of his entire work: If Fortune (and God) truly govern all affairs, rendering human wisdom and effort useless, then what is the purpose of providing advice to princes? He acknowledges that many people hold this opinion, especially in his turbulent era, where rapid and unpredictable changes were common. While admitting a degree of inclination towards this fatalistic view, Machiavelli firmly rejects it as a complete explanation. He asserts that to prevent the "extinguishing of our free will," he believes Fortune controls approximately half of human actions, leaving the other half, or slightly less, to human direction.

The River Analogy: Resisting Fortune's Power

To illustrate this division, Machiavelli employs his famous river analogy. He compares Fortune to a raging river that, when in flood, overflows its banks, sweeping away trees, buildings, and soil with irresistible force. No one can withstand its violence. However, he points out that this destructive power does not prevent people, during periods of fair weather, from making provisions: building defenses, barriers, and canals. These preparations allow them to mitigate the river's destructive potential when it floods again, channeling its waters and reducing its danger.

Applying this analogy to politics, Machiavelli concludes that Fortune "shows her power where valour has not prepared to resist her." Her forces are directed where "barriers and defences have not been raised to constrain her." This highlights the critical role of human foresight, preparation, and "valour" (often translated as "virtù" in other contexts of Machiavelli's work) in managing and resisting the unpredictable forces of Fortune.

Italy as a Case Study

Machiavelli uses the current state of Italy as a prime example of a nation suffering from a lack of preparedness. He describes Italy as "an open country without barriers and without any defence," making it susceptible to invasions and political instability. In contrast, he points to Germany, Spain, and France as nations that have been defended by "proper valour." He suggests that if Italy had similar defenses, it either would not have experienced the significant changes it has endured, or the invasions would not have occurred at all. This illustrates his point that human agency, specifically strategic preparation and military strength, can effectively counter the impact of Fortune.

The Challenge of Individual Adaptation

Moving from the general to the particular, Machiavelli observes that a prince can experience immense success one day and total ruin the next, without any apparent change in their character or disposition. This, he argues, is because a prince who relies entirely on Fortune will inevitably be ruined when Fortune changes.

The key to success, according to Machiavelli, lies in directing one's actions "according to the spirit of the times." He notes that men pursue common goals like glory and riches through diverse methods: caution, haste, force, skill, patience, or their opposites. While all these methods can lead to success, their effectiveness is entirely dependent on whether they "conform in their methods to the spirit of the times." This explains why two cautious men might have different outcomes, or why a cautious man and an impetuous man might both succeed – their success stems from the alignment of their chosen method with the prevailing circumstances.

The core difficulty, however, is that men are rarely "sufficiently circumspect to know how to accommodate himself to the change." This inability to adapt stems from two main factors:

  1. Natural Inclination: Individuals are naturally inclined to act in certain ways and find it difficult to deviate from their inherent disposition.
  2. Past Success: Having always prospered by acting in a particular way, a prince becomes convinced that this method is always effective and is reluctant to abandon it, even when circumstances demand a different approach.

Consequently, a cautious man who has always succeeded through caution will be ruined when the times demand adventurousness, because he cannot change his established ways. Had he changed his conduct, Fortune would not have changed his fate.

Pope Julius II: An Example of Successful Impetuosity

Machiavelli provides Pope Julius II as a historical example of a leader whose impetuous actions perfectly aligned with the "spirit of the times." Julius II conducted all his affairs with boldness and energy, and the circumstances converged to ensure his success. Machiavelli specifically cites his first enterprise against Bologna. Despite opposition from Venice and Spain, and ongoing discussions with France, Julius II personally led the expedition with his characteristic audacity. This decisive move paralyzed Spain and Venice (the former hoping to regain Naples, the latter out of fear) and compelled the King of France to ally with him, seeking to humble Venice.

Machiavelli argues that Julius II's impetuous action achieved what no other pontiff, acting with conventional human wisdom and cautious planning, could have done. Had he waited in Rome to meticulously arrange everything, like a typical pontiff, he would have faced endless excuses from France and a thousand fears from others, ultimately failing. Julius II's success was due to his nature fitting the times. Machiavelli suggests that if circumstances had later required caution, Julius II's rigid impetuosity would have led to his ruin, but his short life prevented this particular misalignment.

Conclusion: Fortune as a Woman

Machiavelli concludes his discussion by observing the fundamental disjunction: Fortune is "changeful," while humankind tends to be "steadfast in their ways." Success occurs when these two align, and failure when they fall out of sync.

In a highly controversial and memorable passage, Machiavelli offers his personal advice on how to deal with Fortune: "For my part I consider that it is better to be adventurous than cautious, because fortune is a woman, and if you wish to keep her under it is necessary to beat and ill-use her." He elaborates that Fortune "allows herself to be mastered by the adventurous rather than by those who go to work more coldly." He likens her to a "lover of young men" because they are "less cautious, more violent, and with more audacity command her." This vivid metaphor underscores Machiavelli's belief that Fortune favors the bold, the decisive, and those willing to take risks rather than those who are overly hesitant or calculating.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the central dilemma Machiavelli addresses regarding Fortune and human free will. How does he resolve this dilemma, and why is this resolution important for his overall philosophy?
  2. Describe Machiavelli's famous river analogy. What do the raging river and the "defences and barriers" symbolize, and what core message about human agency does this analogy convey?
  3. Why does Machiavelli use Italy as an example in his discussion of Fortune? What lesson does he draw from Italy's situation compared to other European powers like Germany, Spain, and France?
  4. According to Machiavelli, why do some princes succeed while others fail, even if their character or disposition hasn't changed? What is the critical factor he identifies for consistent success, and what makes it so challenging for people to achieve?
  5. Discuss Machiavelli's final conclusion about dealing with Fortune, particularly his controversial metaphor of Fortune as a woman. What does this metaphor suggest about the qualities he believes are most effective for a prince in managing unpredictable events?
