# Study Guide: Principality by Wickedness and the Ethics of Power

This study guide examines the acquisition of political power through "wickedness" as outlined in Chapter VIII of Machiavelli’s work. It explores the distinction between mere effectiveness and the higher standard of *virtù*, the strategic application of cruelty, and the enduring philosophical dilemma known as the "dirty hands" problem.

---

### Core Concepts and Analysis

#### 1. The Category of Wicked Success
Machiavelli identifies a third path to power that exists outside of *virtù* (one’s own arms) and *fortuna* (the arms of others): principalities acquired through outright crime or wickedness. While these methods can lead to the successful acquisition and retention of a state, they occupy a unique and problematic space in political theory.

#### 2. Virtù vs. Wickedness: The Standard of Glory
A central theme is the distinction between achieving "empire" and achieving "glory." 
*   **Effectiveness:** A prince like Agathocles of Syracuse can be durably effective through terror and mass murder.
*   **Virtù:** This quality requires more than just survival; it requires the ability to earn genuine admiration. 
*   **The Distinction:** Machiavelli argues that while wickedness can gain a leader a state, it cannot gain them glory. Therefore, a "wicked" prince cannot be ranked alongside foundational figures like Moses or Cyrus because their actions—betraying friends, killing citizens, and being without religion—preclude them from being models of respect.

#### 3. The Utility of Cruelty
One of Machiavelli’s most famous and pragmatic distinctions involves how violence is applied during the transition of power:

| Type of Cruelty | Characteristics | Outcome |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Well-Used** | Committed all at once at the beginning; used only for security; followed by a shift to benevolence. | Stability; psychological safety for the prince; gradual distribution of benefits to subjects. |
| **Badly-Used** | Starts small and escalates over time; requires constant repetition of violence. | Accumulation of resentment; multiplication of enemies; a precarious and unstable state. |

#### 4. The "Dirty Hands" Problem
This concept, formalized by 20th-century philosophers like Michael Walzer and Jean-Paul Sartre, has its roots in Machiavelli's analysis. It describes the moral dilemma where a political leader may be forced to perform morally impermissible acts to achieve a necessary political outcome (such as security or stability). Machiavelli does not offer a moral justification but observes that the most successful "dirty hands" are those that commit violence swiftly to allow normal life to resume.

### Case Studies in Wickedness

*   **Agathocles of Syracuse (361–289 BC):** The son of a potter who rose to become the tyrant of Sicily. He seized power by murdering the entire assembly of Syracuse’s leading citizens and soldiers in a single afternoon. Despite his brutal methods, he held power for decades, defeated external threats (the Carthaginians), and died in old age still in control.
*   **Oliverotto da Fermo:** A contemporary example who used a pre-arranged signal at a dinner party to murder his uncle, Giovanni Fogliani, and other leading citizens of Fermo. Unlike Agathocles, he ruled for only a year before being strangled by Cesare Borgia, who demonstrated superior *virtù* by planning several steps ahead.

### Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **Why does Machiavelli refuse to categorize Agathocles as having *virtù*?**
    *   *Answer:* Because *virtù* involves a standard of political achievement that earns genuine admiration and glory. Agathocles’s methods—betraying friends and murdering fellow citizens—may have gained him power, but they lacked the qualities of a founder worthy of respect.

2.  **According to the text, what is the primary benefit of "well-used" cruelty?**
    *   *Answer:* It creates security by decisively breaking resistance all at once, which then allows the prince to shift to a stable regime that provides benefits to the citizens over time.

3.  **What was the fate of Oliverotto da Fermo, and who was responsible?**
    *   *Answer:* Oliverotto was strangled after ruling for approximately one year; he was eliminated by Cesare Borgia.

4.  **How does the "badly-used" cruelty model affect a prince’s long-term security?**
    *   *Answer:* It makes the position precarious because the prince never achieves psychological safety. Constant, low-level oppression causes resentment to accumulate and enemies to multiply.

5.  **What is the "paradoxical implication" of Machiavelli’s view on violence?**
    *   *Answer:* That a prince who commits all necessary cruelties at once will be remembered better and achieve more durability than one who commits smaller, repeated cruelties.

### Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Ethics of Realism:** Compare and contrast the pursuit of "empire" versus "glory." In Machiavelli’s framework, is it possible for a leader to be effective without being "virtuous" in the traditional sense? Does Machiavelli’s distinction between Agathocles and Moses suggest a hidden moral core in his writing?

2.  **The Temporal Nature of Violence:** Analyze the argument that the timing of cruelty is more important than the cruelty itself. Discuss whether "well-used" cruelty is a viable political strategy in the modern era, or if the "accumulation of resentment" is an inevitable consequence of any political violence.

3.  **The "Dirty Hands" Dilemma:** Drawing on the concepts of Michael Walzer and Jean-Paul Sartre mentioned in the text, discuss the burden of leadership. Is a prince who refuses to "get his hands dirty" acting morally, or is he failing his duty to provide security and stability for his state?

### Glossary of Key Terms

*   **Badly-Used Cruelty:** Violence that starts small and increases over time, preventing stability and breeding constant resentment.
*   **Dirty Hands:** A philosophical problem where a leader must do something morally wrong to achieve a greater political good or security.
*   **Fortuna:** The acquisition of power through luck or the assistance/arms of others.
*   **Glory:** A level of political achievement that earns lasting respect and admiration; distinct from mere power or empire.
*   **Virtù:** A quality of political genius and leadership that includes effectiveness, planning, and the ability to inspire admiration.
*   **Well-Used Cruelty:** Violent acts committed all at once at the start of a reign to secure power, followed by a transition to benevolent governance.
*   **Wickedness:** The acquisition of power through outright crime, treachery, or mass murder.
