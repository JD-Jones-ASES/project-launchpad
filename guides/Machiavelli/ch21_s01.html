Study Guide: Machiavelli on Fortresses and State Strategy

This study guide examines the practical strategies a prince must consider regarding the internal security and military organization of a state. It focuses on the nuances of arming subjects, the dangers of internal division, and the ultimate utility of physical fortifications compared to popular support.

Core Strategies and Verdicts

The text analyzes four primary questions facing a prince. Each strategy is evaluated based on its long-term impact on the stability and defense of the state.

1. The Arming of Subjects

Machiavelli argues that it is generally wise for a prince to arm his subjects. Disarming them is considered a serious mistake for several reasons:

  • Trust and Resentment: Disarming people signals a lack of trust, which breeds resentment and humiliation among the populace.
  • Dependence on Outsiders: A prince who disarms his subjects must rely on hired mercenaries or foreign auxiliaries, neither of whom are fully reliable.
  • Mutual Dependence: Arming citizens creates a bond where the people become partners in the state's defense. A prince leading his own armed citizens is more formidable and secure.

The Transition Qualification: When acquiring new territory, a prince may temporarily disarm the new inhabitants because their loyalty is not yet established. However, the long-term goal should always be to incorporate these new subjects into the military order to avoid permanent resentment.

2. The Dangers of Factionalism

While some rulers believe that fostering factions (dividing subjects or districts against one another) helps maintain control, Machiavelli views this as a dangerous weakness.

  • Ineffectiveness in War: Factions may be manageable during peacetime, but they cause a state to collapse during an external invasion.
  • The Invitation to Conquest: In a divided city, one faction will invariably side with an invader to defeat their internal rivals.
  • Foundation of Security: A prince’s security rests on unity. A divided people cannot provide a stable tax base or the collective strength needed to resist a long war.

3. The Utility of Fortresses

The value of a fortress is not absolute; it depends entirely on the prince's relationship with the people.

  • When Useful: Fortresses are useful only if a prince fears his own subjects more than foreign enemies, providing a temporary refuge to organize a response.
  • The Limitation: If the people hate the prince, no fortress can save him. Physical walls are vulnerable to being scaled, undermined, or starved out.
  • The Ultimate Defense: The "best fortress" is not a physical structure but the loyalty of the people. If the populace is loyal, they become an army for a loved prince; if they are hostile, a fortress becomes merely a prison for a hated prince.

Historical Illustrations

The text provides two contrasting examples to demonstrate the importance of popular support over physical fortifications:

Entity Location Outcome Lesson
Guidobaldo da Montefeltro Urbino Lost his state to Pope Julius II. His fortress was useless because he lacked popular support.
The Bentivoglio Family Bologna Maintained power for generations without a great fortress. Because the people genuinely supported them, they resisted enemies effectively.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to the text, what does a prince signal to his people when he chooses to disarm them?
  2. Why are mercenaries and foreign auxiliaries considered inferior to an armed citizenry?
  3. Under what specific condition does Machiavelli suggest a prince might need to disarm a portion of his subjects?
  4. What is the primary reason that factional divisions lead to a state's collapse during an invasion?
  5. According to Machiavelli, what is the "best fortress" a prince can possess?
  6. Why was Guidobaldo da Montefeltro unable to hold Urbino despite having a fortress?
  7. How does the example of the Bentivoglio family support Machiavelli’s argument regarding popular support?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Interdependence of Military and Social Order: Analyze Machiavelli’s argument that a prince's military strength is directly tied to his relationship with his subjects. How does the act of arming the citizenry function as both a military strategy and a political tool?
  2. Physical vs. Psychological Fortification: Compare and contrast the effectiveness of physical fortresses with the "fortress" of popular loyalty. Based on the text, why does Machiavelli believe that military hardware is merely a supplement to, rather than a substitute for, the goodwill of the people?
  3. The Tension Between Fear and Hatred: In earlier chapters, Machiavelli suggests a prince should be feared. However, in his analysis of fortresses, he states the prince must not be hated. Discuss the distinction between being feared and being hated, and explain how a prince might use "hard measures" or "severity" without losing the popular support necessary for the state's defense.

Glossary of Key Terms

  • Auxiliaries: Foreign troops borrowed from an ally to assist a prince in war; regarded by Machiavelli as untrustworthy.
  • Civil Principality: A state where the prince has ascended to power with the favor of his fellow citizens.
  • Faction: A small, organized, dissenting group within a larger one, often based on family or district loyalties, which Machiavelli argues weakens the state.
  • Fortress: A military stronghold or physical fortification; Machiavelli argues its utility is secondary to popular loyalty.
  • Mercenaries: Soldiers who fight for pay rather than for loyalty to a state or prince.
  • Mutual Dependence: The relationship created when a prince arms his subjects, making them partners in the defense of the state.
