Study Guide: Machiavelli’s New Principalities and the Rise of the Armed Prophet

This study guide explores the themes, historical examples, and political theories presented in Chapter VI of Niccolò Machiavelli’s The Prince. It focuses on the acquisition of new states through one’s own ability and arms rather than through fortune.

Key Concepts and Analysis

1. The Principle of Imitation

Machiavelli argues that because men almost always walk in paths beaten by others, a wise ruler should follow the examples of "supreme" men. He uses the Metaphor of the Archer: A clever archer, aiming for a distant mark, aims much higher than the target—not to hit that height, but to ensure the arrow reaches the desired distance. Similarly, a prince should imitate the greatest historical figures so that even if his ability does not equal theirs, it will "savour of it."

2. Ability (Virtù) vs. Fortune (Fortuna)

The transition from a private citizen to a prince requires either ability or fortune. However, Machiavelli emphasizes that those who rely least on fortune are the most successful.

  • Opportunity: For the greatest founders, fortune provided nothing more than the "opportunity" (the material).
  • Ability: Their "powers of mind" (ability) allowed them to recognize the opportunity and mould it into the form they desired. Without the opportunity, their ability would have been wasted; without their ability, the opportunity would have passed unnoticed.

3. The Peril of Innovation

Machiavelli identifies the "introduction of a new order of things" as the most difficult and dangerous task a leader can undertake. Resistance arises from two sources:

  • Active Enemies: Those who benefited under the old laws and conditions.
  • Lukewarm Defenders: Those who might benefit from the new order but are hesitant due to fear of opponents and a general "incredulity" toward things they have not experienced for a long time.

4. Armed vs. Unarmed Prophets

A central tenet of Machiavelli’s philosophy is that "all armed prophets have conquered, and the unarmed ones have been destroyed."

  • The Nature of the People: The populace is variable. It is easy to persuade them of something, but difficult to "fix them in that persuasion."
  • The Use of Force: A leader must be able to rely on themselves and use force. When the people no longer believe, the leader must have the means to make them believe by force to ensure the "new order" survives.

Glossary of Key Terms and Figures

Term/Figure Definition and Significance
Fortuna (Fortune) Unpredictable forces, luck, or timing. Machiavelli views it as the provider of opportunity but a weak foundation for a state.
Ability (Virtù) The skill, strength, and power of mind required to recognize opportunity and maintain a state.
Moses The leader of the Israelites; described as an "executor of the will of God" who found his people enslaved in Egypt.
Cyrus the Great Founder of the Persian Empire; he capitalized on the discontent of the Persians and the "effeminate" state of the Medes.
Romulus Legendary founder of Rome; he had to be abandoned at birth to eventually establish the Roman fatherland.
Theseus Legendary founder of Athens; he could only show his ability because the Athenians were dispersed.
Hiero the Syracusan A "lesser example" who rose from private citizen to Prince of Syracuse. He abolished old military and alliance systems to build new foundations.
Fra Girolamo Savonarola A Dominican friar who ruled Florence but failed as an "unarmed prophet" because he could not maintain the people's belief.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. Why does Machiavelli believe it is easier to maintain a state acquired through ability than one acquired through fortune?

  • Answer: Those who acquire a state through ability face great difficulties in the "ascent" (the acquisition), but once they have established new rules and exterminated those who envied them, they remain powerful, secure, and respected.

2. According to the text, what was the specific "opportunity" provided to Romulus?

  • Answer: It was necessary for Romulus to be abandoned at birth and choose not to remain in Alba so that he would be driven to found Rome.

3. What are the two reasons Machiavelli gives for the "lukewarmness" of those who might support a new leader?

  • Answer: First, fear of opponents who have the existing laws on their side; second, the "incredulity of men," who do not truly believe in new things until they have had long experience with them.

4. What happened to Girolamo Savonarola when the multitude stopped believing in him?

  • Answer: He was "ruined" because he was an unarmed prophet; he had no means of keeping the believers steadfast or forcing the unbelievers to believe.

5. In the metaphor of the archer, what is the purpose of "taking aim much higher than the mark"?

  • Answer: The purpose is not to reach that height, but to use the high aim to ensure the arrow reaches the actual desired target, which is otherwise too far distant for the bow's strength.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Synergy of Material and Form

Machiavelli writes that historical founders were "brought the material to mould into the form which seemed best to them." Using the examples of Moses, Cyrus, Romulus, and Theseus, analyze how specific "adverse" conditions (slavery, dispersion, oppression) served as the necessary "material" for political greatness. To what extent is a leader's success dependent on the "material" they inherit?

2. The Ethics of the "Armed Prophet"

Examine Machiavelli’s argument that force is a requirement for political stability. Discuss the contrast between the "supreme" examples (who used force to establish lasting orders) and Savonarola (who failed without it). In Machiavelli’s view, is the use of force a moral failing or a structural necessity of governance?

3. The Difficulty of Innovation

Analyze the quote: "There is nothing more difficult to take in hand... than to take the lead in the introduction of a new order of things." Based on the text, explain why the "innovator" is inherently at a disadvantage compared to the "partisan" of the old system. How does a prince overcome the "incredulity of men"?
