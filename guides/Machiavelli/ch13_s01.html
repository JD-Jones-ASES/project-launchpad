Study Guide: Machiavelli on Military Power and Mercenaries

This study guide explores the military philosophy of Niccolò Machiavelli as presented in Chapter XII of The Prince. It examines his critique of the mercenary system in Renaissance Italy and his advocacy for citizen-based military forces as the only viable foundation for a stable state.

Key Concepts

The Military Foundation of Political Power

Machiavelli asserts that military force is the material foundation of all political power. He argues that while all principalities require "good laws" and "good arms," the two are inextricably linked:

  • The Primacy of Force: Good laws cannot exist without good arms. Military strength is the prior condition that allows laws and political skill to have meaning.
  • The Defense of the State: Regardless of how a prince acquires a state or what governing methods they use, the ability to defend the territory is the ultimate measure of political worth.

The Structural Critique of Mercenaries

Machiavelli’s opposition to mercenary forces—professional soldiers hired for pay—is rooted in structural analysis rather than just personal dislike. He identifies several fatal flaws in the mercenary system:

  • Motivation and Loyalty: Mercenaries are motivated solely by wages. Because they lack a personal stake in the state’s survival, their loyalty evaporates when the money runs out or when they face a more lucrative offer.
  • Economic Drain: In peacetime, mercenaries deplete the state's treasury. In wartime, they often fail at crucial moments.
  • Incentives for Inefficiency: Mercenary captains (condottieri) have a financial incentive to prolong wars to keep their contracts active. They also seek to minimize their own casualties because dead soldiers represent a loss of capital.
  • Political Danger: Strong mercenary leaders may use their power to seize control of the state (as seen with Francesco Sforza), while weak leaders simply lose the state to its enemies.

The Citizen Army Ideal

As a solution to the instability of hired soldiers, Machiavelli proposes the "citizen army." This concept posits that a state is best defended by its own people.

  • Personal Stake: Citizens who own property and have families in the city they defend will fight with a commitment that paid foreigners cannot match.
  • Incorruptibility: Because victory ensures their own freedom and defeat means their subjugation, citizen soldiers are harder to bribe or buy off.
  • Historical Implementation: Machiavelli practiced this theory by organizing a citizen militia in Florence between 1505 and 1506 to reduce the city’s reliance on hired captains.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to Machiavelli, what is the relationship between "good laws" and "good arms"?

Answer: Machiavelli believes that good laws cannot exist without good arms. He views military strength as the foundational requirement for any state; without the force to back them up, laws are empty and unenforceable.

2. Why did the condottieri system in Italy prove to be ineffective?

Answer: The system was inherently perverse because the captains had no interest in decisive victories. They fought wars that were often bloodless and inconclusive to ensure they stayed on the payroll, leading to a collapse when faced with genuine national armies.

3. What happened when France invaded Italy in 1494?

Answer: The invasion exposed the fragility of the Italian mercenary system. The condottieri, who were accustomed to low-stakes warfare and minimal casualties, were unable to resist a real national army motivated by military objectives, leading to a near-immediate collapse of the system.

4. How does the example of Francesco Sforza support Machiavelli’s argument?

Answer: Sforza demonstrates the political risk of hiring skilled mercenaries. Rather than simply defending his employer, he used his military power to serve his own ambitions and eventually seized power for himself.

5. How has Machiavelli's "citizen army" concept influenced modern political thought?

Answer: His ideas influenced the republican tradition, specifically the debates among the American Founders regarding standing armies versus citizen militias. The U.S. Constitution's Second Amendment reflects Machiavelli’s suspicion of professional armies divorced from the citizenry.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Structural Incentives of Warfare

Machiavelli argues that mercenaries "have every incentive to prolong wars and minimize their own casualties." Analyze how the economic structure of mercenary contracts in the 15th century dictated the tactical behavior of the condottieri. Contrast this with the incentives driving a citizen militia.

2. Force as the Foundation of Law

"Law without force is empty; force can survive without fully developed law." Discuss the implications of this statement for a Renaissance prince. Does Machiavelli suggest that military power is the only thing that matters, or is he establishing a hierarchy of necessity?

3. Machiavelli and Modern Private Military Companies (PMCs)

Using Machiavelli’s critique of the condottieri, evaluate the role of modern private military contractors. Do PMCs share the same structural flaws that Machiavelli identified (e.g., lack of intrinsic stake, misalignment of interests with the state)? How might Machiavelli diagnose the risks of modern states outsourcing their defense?

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Citizen Army A military force composed of a state's own residents/property owners who fight out of a personal stake in the outcome of the conflict.
Condottieri Professional military commanders in Renaissance Italy who led hired companies of soldiers under a "condotta" or contract.
Francesco Sforza A prominent mercenary captain who used his military command to eventually seize power and become Duke of Milan.
Good Arms The reliable military force Machiavelli identifies as the essential foundation of any successful principality.
Italian Wars A series of conflicts involving France, Spain, and Italian city-states that served as the backdrop for Machiavelli’s military observations.
Mercenaries Professional soldiers who fight for pay rather than for loyalty to a nation or cause; described by Machiavelli as "useless and dangerous."
Secretary of the Florentine Republic The political office held by Machiavelli (1505–1506) during which he attempted to replace mercenaries with a citizen militia.
