Political Realism in the 20th and 21st Centuries: A Study Guide

Overview

This section traces the evolution of political realism from its Machiavellian origins to its dominance in 20th and 21st-century international relations. It highlights how thinkers like Hans Morgenthau, Henry Kissinger, Kenneth Waltz, and John Mearsheimer adapted Machiavelli's insights, emphasizing that states, driven by the pursuit of power and survival in an anarchic international system, prioritize their national interest over ideology or morality. The section concludes by examining realism's enduring relevance in contemporary global challenges, from great-power competition to humanitarian intervention.

Key Concepts

  • International Relations Realism: A dominant theoretical framework for understanding global politics that applies Machiavelli's insights about power, self-interest, and survival from individual princes to nation-states within an anarchic international system.
  • Pursuit of Power: The fundamental drive in international politics, where states are primarily motivated by the desire to survive and maximize their power relative to other states, rather than by ideology, morality, or international law.
  • National Interest Defined in Terms of Power: Hans Morgenthau's core concept, asserting that a state's foreign policy decisions and objectives are, and should be, based on calculations of its power and security in relation to other states.
  • Anarchic International System: The fundamental structural condition of international relations, characterized by the absence of a global government or overarching authority to enforce agreements and provide security among states. This necessitates self-reliance and competition.
  • Balance of Power: A key realist strategy to maintain international stability by ensuring that no single state or bloc of states becomes overwhelmingly powerful, thereby preventing hegemonic dominance and catastrophic wars.
  • Containment: A Cold War foreign policy strategy, notably advocated by George Kennan, based on a realist assessment of Soviet behavior and interests. It aimed to prevent the expansion of Soviet power without direct military confrontation, managing a difficult reality rather than attempting to transform it by force.
  • Structural Realism (Neorealism): A more formal version of realism developed by Kenneth Waltz, which argues that the behavior of states is primarily determined by the overarching structure of the international system (specifically, its anarchic nature), rather than by their internal characteristics or ideologies.
  • Offensive Realism: A branch of structural realism, championed by John Mearsheimer, that posits states have a structural incentive to maximize their power relative to rivals. This is because the absence of a world government means ultimate security can only be guaranteed by possessing overwhelming power, leading to an inherently competitive and tragic international environment.
  • Machiavellian Echo: The recurring theme that, like Machiavelli's prince who must build his own military foundation and cannot rely on others for security, modern states must build their own power and cannot rely solely on international institutions or moral agreements.

Important Terms

  • International Relations Realism: The dominant theoretical perspective in international politics for much of the 20th century, holding that states are the primary actors, driven by self-interest and the pursuit of power in an anarchic international system.
  • Anarchic International System: The condition of international politics where there is no central authority or world government capable of enforcing laws or agreements between sovereign states.
  • National Interest Defined in Terms of Power: Hans Morgenthau's foundational idea that a state's foreign policy should be guided by its own survival and accumulation of power, rather than by moral or ideological considerations.
  • League of Nations: An intergovernmental organization founded after World War I, based on idealist assumptions that states could resolve disputes through international institutions and moral appeals, which ultimately failed to prevent World War II.
  • Cold War: A period of geopolitical tension between the United States and the Soviet Union and their respective allies, characterized by deterrence, proxy conflicts, and ideological competition rather than direct large-scale warfare, often analyzed through a realist lens.
  • Deterrence: A strategy in which a state threatens to punish another state if it undertakes certain undesirable actions, aiming to prevent those actions by making the costs outweigh the benefits.
  • Proxy Conflicts: Wars or armed struggles instigated and supported by larger powers, who often provide funding, weapons, and training, but do not directly engage their own forces.
  • Containment Strategy: The American foreign policy strategy during the Cold War to prevent the spread of communism, primarily through economic, political, and limited military means, rather than direct confrontation.
  • Structural Realism (Neorealism): A school of thought in international relations that focuses on the effects of the international system's structure (anarchy) on state behavior, rather than on internal state characteristics.
  • Offensive Realism: A theory positing that states are perpetually searching for opportunities to gain power over their rivals, with the ultimate goal of becoming the hegemon (dominant power) in the international system, as this is the only reliable way to ensure their security.
  • Humanitarian Intervention: The use of military force by one state or a group of states in the territory of another state without its consent, primarily to prevent or stop widespread violations of human rights or mass atrocities.
  • Responsibility to Protect (R2P): A global political commitment, endorsed by the United Nations, to prevent four mass atrocity crimes: genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity.
  • Hybrid Warfare: A military strategy that blends conventional warfare, irregular warfare, and cyber warfare, along with other influencing methods such as fake news, diplomacy, and foreign electoral intervention.
  • Information Operations: The collection and dissemination of information and use of information systems and resources to affect adversary and potential adversary decision-making and to protect friendly decision-making.

Detailed Notes

From Political Theory to International Relations

  • Evolution of Realism: The Machiavellian tradition, initially focused on individual princes and domestic elites, significantly evolved in the 20th century.
  • New Context: Two World Wars, the rise of totalitarianism, and the Cold War between the United States and the Soviet Union necessitated new analytical frameworks.
  • Shift in Application: Political thinkers began applying Machiavellian insights to the relations between nation-states within an anarchic international system.
  • Dominant Framework: This tradition, known as international relations realism, became the prevailing approach to understanding world politics in the 20th century and maintains substantial influence today.

Hans Morgenthau: Politics Among Nations

  • Founding Text: Hans Morgenthau's Politics Among Nations (1948) is considered the seminal work of modern realist international relations theory.
  • Core Argument: Pursuit of Power: Morgenthau argued that the fundamental driver in international politics is the pursuit of power.
  • State Motivation: States, like Machiavelli's princes, are primarily motivated by survival and the desire to maximize their power relative to others, not by ideology, morality, or international law.
  • "National Interest Defined in Terms of Power": This is Morgenthau's central concept, essentially the Machiavellian formula translated for modern nation-states. Statesmen must understand power as it actually works, not as idealists suggest it should work.
  • Historical Influence: Morgenthau's framework was profoundly shaped by the failure of idealist policies in the 1930s (e.g., the League of Nations' inability to stop Hitler), reinforcing the Machiavellian lesson that actions matter more than stated intentions or international law.

Realism in Practice: Kissinger and the Cold War

  • Henry Kissinger: A prominent practitioner of Machiavellian realism, serving as National Security Advisor and Secretary of State under Nixon and Ford.
  • Subordinating Ideology: Kissinger's work, including Diplomacy (1994), argued that effective statecraft requires subordinating ideological preferences to pragmatic calculations of power and national interest. He warned against "moral crusades" that could undermine strategic interests.
  • Cold War Analysis: Kissinger viewed the Cold War as a Machiavellian contest between two great powers, managing their competition through a blend of deterrence, diplomacy, and proxy conflicts. The goal was stability and a stable balance of power, not ideological victory.
  • George Kennan: The American diplomat whose "Long Telegram" (1946) and "X Article" (1947) laid the intellectual foundation for the containment strategy. Kennan's analysis of Soviet behavior was realist, focusing on a structural assessment of Soviet interests and vulnerabilities rather than moral condemnation. The policy recommendation was to manage reality rather than forcefully transform it.

Structural Realism: Waltz and Mearsheimer

  • Neorealism: Later in the 20th century, a more formal approach known as structural realism or neorealism emerged.
  • Kenneth Waltz's Theory of International Politics (1979):
    • Argued that state behavior is primarily determined by the structure of the international system, specifically its anarchic nature (absence of world government).
    • This anarchy forces states to rely on themselves for security, compelling them to act like Machiavelli's princes: maintain military capacity, be suspicious of rivals, and prioritize survival, regardless of their internal character or ideology.
  • John Mearsheimer's The Tragedy of Great Power Politics (2001):
    • Developed offensive realism, asserting that states in an anarchic system have a structural incentive to maximize their power relative to rivals.
    • This isn't due to inherent aggression but because maximal power is seen as the most reliable guarantee of survival in an insecure system.
    • This leads to the "tragic pattern of great-power competition" observed throughout history.
  • Machiavellian Echo: Both Waltz and Mearsheimer underscore the Machiavellian principle that states, like princes, cannot rely on external guarantees but must build their own power for security.

Realism Today: Ongoing Debates

  • Enduring Relevance: Machiavellian realism continues to be highly relevant in 21st-century political debates.
  • Humanitarian Intervention: Debates over "responsibility to protect" (R2P) and military intervention to stop atrocities often pit realists against idealists. Realists caution that such interventions often lead to unintended consequences, exhaust resources, and destabilize regions without achieving desired moral outcomes.
  • Great-Power Competition: The growing rivalry between the United States and China strongly supports realist predictions. Realists like Mearsheimer argued that China's rise would inevitably lead to great-power competition due to the anarchic structure of the international system, irrespective of domestic politics or stated intentions.
  • Hybrid Warfare and Information Operations: Modern forms of competition—cyber attacks, disinformation, economic coercion, proxy forces—are new tools for state competition. These strategies, often requiring cunning and indirect influence ("the fox"), perfectly align with Machiavellian logic, showing how the essential character of political competition endures.

The Enduring Question

  • Morality vs. Political Power: Machiavelli's central question about the relationship between morality and political power remains unresolved.
  • Controversial Claim: While the conflict between politics and ethics is broadly accepted, Machiavelli's assertion that political necessity sometimes mandates actions condemned by conventional morality remains deeply controversial.
  • Generational Challenge: Every generation grapples with whether political demands are fundamentally different from personal morality, or if effective leadership can be reconciled with justice and virtue. The continued debate, five centuries later, is a testament to the enduring power of Machiavelli's insights.

Review Questions

  1. How did the historical context of the 20th century (e.g., world wars, Cold War) influence the development of international relations realism, shifting its focus from individual rulers to nation-states?
  2. Explain Hans Morgenthau's core concept of "national interest defined in terms of power." How does this idea reflect Machiavellian thinking, and what historical events did Morgenthau believe validated this approach?
  3. Discuss how Henry Kissinger and George Kennan applied realist principles to American foreign policy during the Cold War. What were their primary objectives, and how did their strategies (e.g., balance of power, containment) exemplify realist thought?
  4. Compare and contrast Kenneth Waltz's structural realism with John Mearsheimer's offensive realism. What central assumption do both theories share about the international system, and how does this lead to different implications for state behavior?
  5. Identify and elaborate on two contemporary global issues where Machiavellian realism continues to offer valuable insights. What specific arguments do realists typically make regarding these issues, and what tensions arise when contrasted with idealist perspectives?
