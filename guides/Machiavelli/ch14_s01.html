Study Guide: Auxiliaries and the Principle of Military Self-Reliance

This study guide examines the military and political theories presented in Machiavelli’s analysis of auxiliary forces. It explores the hierarchy of military strength, the specific dangers posed by borrowed troops, and the broader political philosophy of self-reliance.

I. Key Concepts and Analysis

The Hierarchy of Military Forces

The text establishes a clear hierarchy of military reliability and safety for a prince. This ranking descends from the most secure to the most dangerous:

  1. Own Arms: A prince's own troops, loyal directly to him and essential for true security.
  2. Mixed Forces: A combination of a prince's own troops supplemented by hired or borrowed men.
  3. Mercenaries and Auxiliaries: Positioned at the bottom of the hierarchy due to their unreliability and external loyalties.

The Specific Danger of Auxiliaries

While both mercenaries and auxiliaries are viewed as inferior to a state's own troops, the text argues that auxiliaries are the more dangerous of the two.

  • Definition: Auxiliaries are complete military units borrowed from another ruler, operating under the command of that ruler's captains.
  • Loyalty: Unlike mercenaries, who fight for money and have no master, auxiliaries are loyal to the prince who dispatched them.
  • The "Win-Win" Trap: If auxiliaries win a battle, the victory belongs to their original ruler, not the prince who borrowed them. This increases the prestige and power of a foreign ruler while the host prince becomes a dependent.
  • The Risk of Loss: If auxiliaries lose, the host prince is defeated and weakened, leaving them vulnerable to the "benefactor" who may seize the opportunity to take control.

Historical Case Studies

  • The Western Roman Empire: The empire began supplementing its citizen legions with barbarian (Germanic and Gothic) auxiliary units. Over time, these auxiliaries became the backbone of the military. When Rome lost the will to maintain its own legions, the auxiliary commanders became the true rulers, leading to the empire’s fall.
  • Pope Julius II: To drive the French out of Italy, Julius II borrowed Spanish troops. Although successful in his immediate goal, the victory established Spain as the dominant military power in Italy for the following century.

Political Self-Reliance

The military arguments serve as a foundation for a broader political philosophy:

  • Fundamental Lever of Power: Military security is the primary lever of political power. To outsource it is to surrender sovereignty.
  • Conditional Goodwill: Relying on the help of others subjects a prince to the conditional and changing goodwill of benefactors.
  • Modern Equivalents: In contemporary contexts, "own arms" extends beyond soldiers to include an independent industrial base, domestic food supplies, and control over critical technology and energy.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What is the primary difference in loyalty between a mercenary and an auxiliary soldier?
  2. According to the text, why is a victory achieved with auxiliary troops considered a "loss" for the prince who borrowed them?
  3. What was the long-term consequence of Rome's reliance on barbarian auxiliaries?
  4. How did Pope Julius II's use of Spanish auxiliaries affect the long-term political landscape of Italy?
  5. What is the "Hierarchy of Military Forces" described in the text, from most desirable to least desirable?
  6. Why does the text describe hiring auxiliaries as "inviting a rival into your home"?
  7. Beyond military troops, what modern resources would fall under Machiavelli's principle of "own arms"?

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Anatomy of Dependency: Analyze the relationship between a prince and his "benefactor" when using auxiliary troops. Explain how the short-term success of a military campaign can lead to long-term political subjugation.
  2. The Roman Precedent: Using the example of the Western Roman Empire provided in the text, discuss the transition from "citizen legions" to "auxiliary dominance." How does this historical example support the argument that military dependency is a precursor to political collapse?
  3. Modern Strategic Autonomy: Apply the logic of military self-reliance to modern international relations. Discuss the text's assertion that a nation-state must maintain an independent industrial and technological base to be a "genuine partner" rather than a "dependent client" in alliances like NATO.
  4. The Philosophy of Control: Explore the deeper political philosophy of self-reliance mentioned in the text. Why does the author argue that goodwill is an insufficient foundation for a state’s survival?

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Auxiliaries Military units borrowed from another ruler that arrive as a complete unit under their own commander; loyal to their original sovereign.
Mercenaries Soldiers hired to fight for money rather than loyalty to a state or cause; they generally have no master beyond their own captain.
Own Arms A state’s or prince's independent military force composed of its own citizens or subjects; the highest tier in the military hierarchy.
Mixed Forces A military composition that combines a prince's own troops with either mercenary or auxiliary supplements.
Self-Reliance The political principle that a ruler must control the essential factors of survival—military, industrial, and technological—to maintain sovereignty.
Conditional Goodwill The concept that the help or support of foreign powers is never guaranteed and changes based on their own strategic priorities.
Sovereignty The fundamental power of a state to govern itself and make independent decisions, which is compromised when dependent on foreign military force.
