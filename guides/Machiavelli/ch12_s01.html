Study Guide: Machiavelli on Ecclesiastical Principalities

This study guide examines the unique political status of the Church during the Renaissance as analyzed in Machiavelli’s The Prince. It focuses on the transition of the papacy from a spiritual authority to a dominant military and political force.

Key Concepts and Themes

The Exception to Political Rules

Machiavelli identifies ecclesiastical principalities as the sole exception to his established rules of statecraft. While they may be acquired through ability (virtù) or fortune, they are maintained by "ancient ordinances of religion." These religious foundations are so powerful that they sustain the state regardless of how the prince lives or governs. Consequently, these are the only states where a ruler can be incompetent, corrupt, or absent without losing power.

The Irony of Divine Maintenance

Machiavelli claims these states are "exalted and maintained by God" and suggests that analyzing them would be the act of a "presumptuous and rash man." However, this reticence is identified as a form of irony. By claiming the subject is beyond human analysis, Machiavelli highlights the unique—and perhaps enviable—nature of religious legitimacy while immediately proceeding to analyze the very human and political means by which these states were actually built.

The Transformation of Papal Power

The Papal States evolved from a collection of loosely controlled territories into a unified, powerful political entity through the actions of two specific popes:

  • Alexander VI: Utilized the military force and treachery of his son, Cesare Borgia, to consolidate power and expand the Church's borders.
  • Julius II: Inherited the gains of Alexander VI and expanded them further. He is noted for his personal leadership in military campaigns and his ability to project the Church’s power against international rivals like France and Venice.

Religion as Political Legitimacy

Machiavelli treats religion not as a spiritual truth but as a political phenomenon. Religious authority provides a "sacred canopy" that grants a prince legitimacy that military force or popular support alone cannot achieve. In this framework, the people’s obedience is rooted in reverence for the institution rather than the performance or character of the individual ruler.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to Machiavelli, what sustains ecclesiastical principalities regardless of the prince's behavior?

Answer: They are sustained by the ancient ordinances of religion, which are all-powerful and maintain the state regardless of the prince’s conduct.

2. How does Machiavelli’s tone change when he discusses the role of God in maintaining these states?

Answer: His tone becomes ironic. He claims it would be "presumptuous" to analyze states upheld by God, yet he immediately begins a historical and political analysis of how those states were actually built through force and strategy.

3. Which two popes are credited with turning the Papal States into a major political and military power?

Answer: Pope Alexander VI and Pope Julius II.

4. What role did Cesare Borgia play in the rise of the Papal States?

Answer: As the son of Pope Alexander VI, he used political violence, cunning, and treacy to expand and consolidate Church territories into a unified state.

5. Why was Julius II known as the "Warrior Pope"?

Answer: He earned the nickname (il Papa Terribile) because he personally led military campaigns, such as the siege of Mirandola in 1511, where he directed artillery fire and commanded troops in armor.

6. In Machiavelli’s view, why is a prince who holds power through religious authority "enviable"?

Answer: Because the legitimacy of their rule is not conditional on their performance; they do not need to be just or competent to maintain the obedience of their subjects.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Paradox of Ecclesiastical Stability: Analyze why Machiavelli considers ecclesiastical principalities the "exception to every rule." Compare the stability of these states to the hereditary and new principalities discussed in other sections of The Prince.
  2. Military Papacy and Secular Power: Discuss the transition of the papacy under Julius II. How did his use of military force and personal leadership in the field redefine the role of the Pope in Renaissance Italy? Use the siege of Mirandola as a key example.
  3. The Political Utility of Religion: Based on the text, evaluate Machiavelli’s argument that religion functions as a source of political legitimacy. How does he distinguish between religious belief and religion as a "source of popular allegiance"?
  4. Julius II and the Concept of Fortune: Machiavelli views Julius II as a ruler whose "impetuosity matched the demands of his moment." Explore how Julius’s aggressive personality allowed him to succeed where a more cautious ruler might have failed, particularly in his dealings with France and Venice.

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Ancient Ordinances of Religion: The established religious traditions and laws that provide the foundation and stability for ecclesiastical states, independent of the ruler's actions.
  • Civil Religion: A concept anticipated by Machiavelli where rulers utilize religious symbolism, ceremonies, and institutions to legitimize their political authority.
  • Ecclesiastical Principality: A state ruled by a religious leader (such as the Pope) where political power is intertwined with religious authority.
  • Il Papa Terribile: The nickname for Pope Julius II, reflecting his formidable personality and his aggressive, military-led approach to the papacy.
  • Mirandola (1511): The site of a military siege where Pope Julius II personally commanded troops, symbolizing the papacy's peak as a military power.
  • Papal States: The territories in central Italy under the direct sovereign rule of the Pope.
  • Vicar of Christ: The theological title for the Pope, which Machiavelli uses ironically to suggest the gap between spiritual claims and the political realities of ruling a state.

Biographical Note: Pope Julius II (1443–1513)

Born Giuliano della Rovere, he served as pope from 1503 to 1513. Beyond his military exploits, such as driving the French from the Italian peninsula and expelling Cesare Borgia, he was a major patron of the arts. He commissioned Michelangelo to paint the Sistine Chapel ceiling and design St. Peter's Basilica. Machiavelli highlights him as a primary example of a ruler whose impetuous nature perfectly suited the political crises of his time.
