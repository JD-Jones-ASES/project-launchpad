# Machiavelli’s Study of Mixed Principalities: A Comprehensive Study Guide

This study guide examines the complexities of governing annexed territories, as detailed in Chapter III of *The Prince*. It focuses on Machiavelli’s analysis of "mixed principalities"—newly acquired territories added to an existing state—and utilizes the successes of the Romans and the failures of King Louis XII of France to illustrate the principles of effective statecraft.

---

## I. Key Concepts and Principles

### The Nature of Mixed Principalities
A mixed principality is not entirely new but is an addition to an established state. Machiavelli identifies several inherent difficulties in these acquisitions:
*   **The Deception of Change:** Subjects often willingly change their rulers in the hope of improving their condition. However, they are frequently deceived, finding through experience that their situation has worsened.
*   **Necessary Offense:** A new prince is forced to burden new subjects with the presence of his military and other hardships incidental to the acquisition.
*   **The Dilemma of Support:** A prince gains enemies among those he injured during the takeover, yet he cannot fully satisfy the friends who helped him gain power, nor can he take strong measures against those friends due to his initial obligation to them.

### Strategies for Maintaining New Acquisitions
Machiavelli categorizes acquisitions into two types, each requiring different strategies:

**1. Territories of the Same Country and Language**
These are relatively easy to hold, especially if they are not accustomed to self-government. To secure them, a prince must:
*   Extinguish the family of the former prince.
*   Maintain the existing laws and taxes.

**2. Territories with Different Languages, Customs, or Laws**
These require "good fortune and great energy" to maintain. Recommended strategies include:
*   **Personal Residence:** The prince should go and reside in the new territory. This allows him to see disorders as they arise and remedy them quickly. It also prevents officials from pillaging the country and gives subjects direct recourse to the prince.
*   **Establishing Colonies:** The prince should send colonies to "key" locations. Machiavelli argues colonies are superior to military garrisons because they are:
    *   **Inexpensive:** They cost the prince little to nothing.
    *   **More Faithful:** They are more loyal than soldiers.
    *   **Less Injurious:** They only offend a poor, scattered minority whose land is taken, making them unable to seek revenge.
*   **Avoiding Garrisons:** Maintaining a military presence is costly, consumes all revenue from the state, and exasperates the entire population, turning the acquisition into a loss.

### Balance of Power in Foreign Provinces
A prince in a foreign province must observe three rules regarding neighboring powers:
1.  Make himself the head and defender of less powerful neighbors.
2.  Weaken the more powerful neighbors.
3.  Ensure no foreign power as strong as himself gains a footing in the region.

### The Importance of Foresight
Machiavelli compares political foresight to the diagnosis of "hectic fever" by physicians:
*   **Early Stage:** The malady is easy to cure but difficult to detect.
*   **Late Stage:** Having gone undetected and untreated, the malady is easy to detect but difficult to cure.
Prudent princes must address troubles as soon as they are foreseen. Machiavelli asserts that war is not to be avoided but only deferred to the advantage of others.

## II. Case Studies: Success and Failure

### The Roman Model (Success)
The Romans applied the principles of prudent statecraft in the territories they annexed, such as Greece:
*   They established colonies.
*   They maintained friendly relations with minor powers (the Achaeans and Ætolians) without allowing them to increase their strength.
*   They humbled major powers (the kingdom of Macedonia and Antiochus).
*   They never deferred war to avoid immediate trouble, preferring to fight Philip and Antiochus in Greece rather than eventually having to fight them in Italy.

### Louis XII of France (Failure)
King Louis XII’s intervention in Italy serves as a case study in failed statecraft. Machiavelli identifies five primary errors made by Louis, plus a final catastrophic sixth:
1.  **Destroyed minor powers:** Instead of protecting weak neighbors, he allowed them to be diminished.
2.  **Increased the strength of a major power:** He assisted Pope Alexander in occupying the Romagna, aggrandizing the Church's temporal power.
3.  **Brought in a powerful foreigner:** He divided the kingdom of Naples with the King of Spain.
4.  **Did not reside in the territory:** He failed to establish a personal presence in Italy.
5.  **Did not send colonies:** He relied on other means rather than efficient colonization.
6.  **Humbled the Venetians:** After making the Church and Spain powerful, he attempted to take the Venetians' dominions, which removed the only power that could have kept others from attacking Lombardy.

## IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Ethics of Power:** Machiavelli states that the wish to acquire is "very natural and common," and those who succeed are praised rather than blamed. Analyze this perspective in the context of his advice to either "well treat or crush" men. How does his focus on political necessity challenge traditional moral frameworks?
2.  **The Hectic Fever Analogy:** Discuss Machiavelli’s concept of political foresight. Use his analogy of the "hectic fever" to explain why he believes a prince should never "enjoy the benefits of time" but rather rely on his own "valour and prudence."
3.  **Anatomy of a Failure:** Detail the strategic errors of Louis XII in Italy. Contrast these errors with the actions of the Romans in Greece to argue whether Louis’s failure was, as Machiavelli claims, "reasonable and quite natural" rather than a "miracle."

## V. Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Alexander (the Great):** King of Macedon (356–323 BC) used by Machiavelli as a model for how centralized governments are hard to conquer but easy to hold once the ruling family is extinguished.
*   **Barons:** Hereditary nobility in France; Machiavelli identifies them as the reason France is easy to conquer (via bribery) but hard to hold (due to their constant rebellions).
*   **Cesare Borgia (Duke Valentino):** The son of Pope Alexander VI; described by Machiavelli as bold, ruthless, and strategically brilliant, despite being eventually undone by bad fortune.
*   **Composite State:** A state composed of multiple parts, such as an ancient state that has annexed new territories.
*   **France:** Machiavelli’s model of a decentralized state governed by a king and barons.
*   **Mixed Principality:** A principality that is not entirely new but is added as a "member" to an existing state.
*   **Pisa:** A Florentine subject city that rebelled in 1494; its prolonged resistance was a major focus of Machiavelli’s military and political career.
*   **Principality:** A state ruled by a single prince or sovereign, distinguished from a republic.
*   **Romagna:** A region in north-central Italy seized by Cesare Borgia between 1499 and 1503.
*   **Turk (The Ottoman Empire):** Machiavelli’s model of a highly centralized state; difficult to conquer because of its unity, but easy to hold once the central ruler is defeated.
