Machiavelli’s Classification of States and Principalities: A Study Guide

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the foundational concepts presented in the opening of Niccolò Machiavelli’s The Prince. It explores the taxonomy of governance, the methods of state acquisition, and the historical examples Machiavelli uses to illustrate his political theories.

I. Key Concepts and Structural Overview

Machiavelli’s analysis begins by categorizing all forms of human governance and further refining those categories based on their history, the nature of their acquisition, and the previous status of their citizens.

1. The Fundamental Division of States

According to the text, all states and powers that have exercised authority over men fall into one of two primary categories:

  • Republics: States governed by a form of representative or collective rule.
  • Principalities: States governed by a single ruler or prince.

2. Taxonomy of Principalities

Machiavelli focuses his initial analysis on principalities, which he divides into two main types based on the duration of the ruling family’s power:

  • Hereditary Principalities: States where the prince’s family has been established as the ruling power for a long period.
  • New Principalities: States that have recently come under the control of a prince.

3. Sub-categories of New Principalities

New principalities are further distinguished by how they were formed or integrated:

  • Entirely New: A state created or seized where no prior dynastic rule existed for that prince (e.g., Francesco Sforza in Milan).
  • Annexed Members: Territory added to an existing hereditary state (e.g., the Kingdom of Naples being annexed to the King of Spain).

4. Variables of Acquisition

The text identifies several critical variables that determine the nature of a dominion and how it is acquired:

  • Prior Condition of the State: Dominions are either accustomed to living under the rule of a prince or are accustomed to living in freedom.
  • Means of Acquisition: A prince may acquire a state through:
    • His own arms.
    • The arms of others.
    • Fortune (luck or external circumstances).
    • Ability (personal skill or virtù).

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to the opening lines of The Prince, what are the only two types of states that hold rule over men? Answer: Republics and principalities.

2. How does Machiavelli define a hereditary principality? Answer: A state in which the ruling family has been established for a long period of time.

3. Provide an example from the text of an "entirely new" principality. Answer: Milan under Francesco Sforza.

4. What historical example is used to illustrate a member annexed to a hereditary state? Answer: The Kingdom of Naples annexed to the Kingdom of Spain.

5. What are the four specific means by which a prince might acquire a dominion? Answer: Through his own arms, the arms of others, fortune, or ability.

6. What are the two types of social/political backgrounds Machiavelli identifies as being common to newly acquired dominions? Answer: They are either accustomed to living under the rule of a prince or they are accustomed to living in freedom.

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Dichotomy of Fortune and Ability: Compare and contrast the acquisition of power through "fortune" versus "ability" as presented in the text. Using Francesco Sforza as a reference point, analyze why Machiavelli considers the method of acquisition a defining characteristic of a prince's rule.

2. The Complexity of the "New" Prince: Machiavelli distinguishes between states that are entirely new and those that are annexed to a hereditary state. Discuss the potential challenges a prince might face when annexing a territory like Naples compared to establishing an entirely new rule in a place like Milan.

3. The Influence of Political History on Governance: Machiavelli notes that acquired dominions are either "accustomed to live under a prince, or to live in freedom." Explore how the prior political state of a population might influence a new prince's strategy for maintaining control.

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Ability (Virtù) The individual skill, talent, or strength of a prince used to acquire or maintain power; contrasted with fortune.
Annexed Member A territory or kingdom that is added to an existing state rather than being a standalone new principality.
Dominion A territory or state over which sovereign authority is exercised.
Fortune External circumstances, luck, or chance that can lead to the rise or fall of a prince.
Francesco Sforza The Duke of Milan (1401–1466) who rose from a condottiere (mercenary leader) to a duke through his own ability; cited by Machiavelli as an example of an "entirely new" prince.
Hereditary Pertaining to a system of rule where power is passed down through a long-established family line.
Principality A state or territory ruled by a prince.
Republic A state where the government is not a monarchy or principality, often involving a degree of public participation or freedom.
