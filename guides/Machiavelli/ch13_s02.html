Study Guide: The Military Foundations of the State and the Critique of Mercenary Forces

This study guide examines the transition in political theory from the classification of principalities to the practical military foundations required to sustain them. Based on the analysis of historical and contemporary Italian military failures, this document explores the necessity of nationalized military forces and the inherent dangers of relying on mercenary or auxiliary troops.

I. The Core Foundations of a State

The stability of any state, whether it is new, old, or composite, rests upon two primary pillars. Without these foundations, a state is destined for ruin.

  • Good Laws: The legal and civic framework that governs the conduct of citizens and the administration of the state.
  • Good Arms: The military means of offense and defense.

The Interdependency of Laws and Arms There is a direct correlation between military strength and legal integrity. A state cannot possess good laws if it is not well-armed; conversely, where a state is well-armed, good laws are likely to follow. Consequently, the study of statecraft must prioritize the nature and quality of a prince's "arms."

II. Classification of Military Forces

A prince may defend his state using four types of military forces:

  1. Own Arms: Forces comprised of the prince's own subjects or citizens.
  2. Mercenaries: Hired foreign soldiers.
  3. Auxiliaries: Soldiers lent by an ally.
  4. Mixed: A combination of the above.

The Critique of Mercenary Forces

Hired soldiers are characterized as "useless and dangerous." Reliance on them ensures that a state will never be firm or safe.

Characteristic Impact on the State
Lack of Discipline They are disunited, ambitious, and unfaithful.
Inconsistent Bravery They appear valiant before friends but are cowardly before enemies.
Lack of Devotion They have no fear of God and no fidelity to men.
Economic Motivation Their only incentive is a "trifle of stipend," which is insufficient to make them willing to die for the prince.
Peacetime Parasitism In times of peace, the state is robbed by the mercenaries; in war, it is robbed by the enemy.

III. The Dilemma of Mercenary Captains

The quality of a mercenary leader presents a no-win scenario for a prince:

  • The Capable Captain: If the captain is skillful, he cannot be trusted. He will inevitably seek his own greatness, either by oppressing his employer or by pursuing objectives contrary to the prince’s intentions.
  • The Unskilled Captain: if the captain lacks skill, he will lead the state to ruin through incompetence.

The Solution: Direct Command To avoid these outcomes, a prince must act as his own captain in person. In the case of a republic, the state must send its own citizens. If a citizen-captain is unsatisfactory, he must be recalled; if he is worthy, he must be bound by laws to ensure he does not exceed his command.

IV. Historical Case Studies

The following examples illustrate the consequences of military choices:

Successes of Nationalized Arms

  • Rome and Sparta: Both remained free and armed for many centuries by using their own citizens.
  • The Switzers: Highly armed and entirely free.
  • Early Venice: The Venetians acted safely and gloriously as long as they fought at sea with their own gentlemen and plebeians.

Failures of Mercenary Reliance

  • Carthage: Oppressed by their own mercenary soldiers after the first war with Rome.
  • Thebes: After the death of Epaminondas, they made Philip of Macedon their captain; after victory, he stripped them of their liberty.
  • Milan: Enlisted Francesco Sforza against the Venetians. After defeating the enemy at Caravaggio, Sforza betrayed Milan and allied with the Venetians to crush his former masters.
  • Naples: Queen Johanna was forced to surrender her kingdom to the King of Aragon after being abandoned by her mercenary captain, Sforza (father of Francesco).
  • Venice (Land Expansion): When the Venetians moved to land-based warfare, they adopted the Italian custom of hiring mercenaries. Under the captain Carmignuola, they realized his lack of passion for the war but could not dismiss him without losing their gains; they were eventually forced to murder him to secure themselves.

V. The Decline of Italy

The "ruin of Italy" is attributed to its long-standing reliance on mercenary forces. This transition occurred due to several historical shifts:

  1. Rise of the Church: The Pope acquired more temporal power.
  2. Fragmentation: Italy divided into more states as cities rebelled against nobles (favored by the Emperor) with the support of the Church.
  3. Civic Inexperience: Because these new states were ruled by priests or citizens unaccustomed to military life, they began to enlist foreigners.
  4. The Professionalization of Cowardice: Mercenary captains, such as Alberigo da Conio, developed military rules to reduce fatigue and danger. They avoided killing in battle, did not attack at night, and refused to campaign in winter. They prioritized cavalry over infantry because cavalry provided more prestige and was easier for a private citizen to maintain.

VI. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What are the "chief foundations" of all states according to the text?
    • Answer: Good laws and good arms.
  2. Why does Machiavelli argue that mercenaries are unwilling to die for a prince?
    • Answer: Because their only motivation is a small salary (stipend), which is not enough to inspire the ultimate sacrifice of their lives.
  3. What happened to the Florentines regarding their captain Pagolo Vitelli?
    • Answer: He was a prudent man of great renown; the Florentines realized that if he had taken Pisa, they would have had to obey him to keep his loyalty, as they had no means to resist him.
  4. Who was the King of France who seized Italy "with chalk in hand"?
    • Answer: King Charles.
  5. What was the primary reason mercenary captains prioritized cavalry over infantry?
    • Answer: They were private individuals without territory; they could not support many infantrymen, and a small number of infantry gave them no authority. Cavalry allowed them to be maintained and honored with a moderate force.
  6. How did the Venetians’ military success change when they moved from sea to land?
    • Answer: At sea, they were valiant with their own men, but on land, they followed the Italian custom of using mercenaries, leading to slow gains and sudden, massive losses (such as at Vaila).
  7. What specific "military rules" did mercenary captains invent to avoid danger?
    • Answer: Not killing in the fray, taking prisoners and liberating them without ransom, avoiding night attacks, and not campaigning in the winter.

VII. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Interdependence of Law and Military Might: Analyze the claim that "where they are well armed they have good laws." Is this a purely cynical view of power, or does it suggest a deeper sociological requirement for state stability?
  2. The Psychological Failure of the Mercenary: Discuss why a "trifle of stipend" is insufficient for maintaining a military force. Contrast this with the motivations Machiavelli implies are present in a "citizen-soldier" or a prince's own arms.
  3. The Historical Evolution of Italian Vulnerability: Trace the transition of Italy from a region of potential power to one of "slavery and contempt." Focus on the roles of the Church, the decline of the nobility, and the rise of mercenary schools like that of Alberigo da Conio.

VIII. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Alberigo da Conio: A Romagnian captain who first gave renown to mercenary soldiery; the teacher of Braccio and Sforza.
  • Auxiliaries: Military forces borrowed from another ruler or state to assist in war.
  • Bracceschi: A mercenary faction often in opposition to the forces of Sforza.
  • Carmignuola: A valiant captain for the Venetians who was murdered by them after he became "lukewarm" in his efforts during land-based expansion.
  • Francesco Sforza: A mercenary captain who betrayed his Milanese employers to become the Duke of Milan.
  • Giovanni Acuto: An English mercenary captain (John Hawkwood) whose fidelity to the Florentines was never truly tested because he did not achieve a decisive victory.
  • Mercenary: A professional soldier hired to serve in a foreign army, motivated by pay rather than political or national loyalty.
  • Stipend: The small, fixed salary paid to mercenary soldiers.
  • Temporal Power: The political and worldly authority held by the Church, as opposed to its spiritual authority.
  • Vaila: The site of a battle where the Venetians lost in one day what had taken eight hundred years of effort to acquire.
