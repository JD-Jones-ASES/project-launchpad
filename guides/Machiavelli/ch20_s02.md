# Machiavelli’s The Prince: Avoiding Contempt and Hatred (Study Guide)

This study guide examines the principles outlined in Chapter XIX of Niccolò Machiavelli’s *The Prince*. The text focuses on the critical necessity for a ruler to avoid the hatred and contempt of his subjects to ensure the security of his state and protection against conspiracies.

---

## Core Themes and Key Concepts

### 1. The Survival of the Prince
The central thesis is that a prince who is hated cannot survive, and one who is despised invites the very conspiracies that will destroy him. Success is defined by the fulfillment of two primary objectives:
*   **Avoiding Hatred:** This is achieved primarily by abstaining from the property and women of subjects.
*   **Avoiding Contempt:** This is achieved by projecting an image of greatness, courage, gravity, and fortitude.

### 2. The Two Fears
A prince must guard against two distinct threats:
*   **Internal Threats:** Conspiracies by subjects. The best defense is the goodwill of the people. If the people are satisfied, a conspirator fears the "sequel to the crime," knowing the populace will turn against them.
*   **External Threats:** Foreign powers. The defense against these includes being well-armed and having reliable allies.

### 3. The Management of "Humours"
Machiavelli identifies different "humours" or social groups that a prince must balance:
*   **The People:** Generally love peace and unaspiring princes.
*   **The Nobles:** Possess ambition that must be curbed.
*   **The Soldiers (Roman Context):** Historically loved warlike, cruel, and rapacious princes who allowed them to vent their greed upon the people.

### 4. Institutional Buffers: The Case of France
Machiavelli praises the French "parliament" as a prudent institution. It acts as an arbiter that "beats down the great and favours the lesser" without the king taking direct blame. This allows the king to "leave affairs of reproach to the management of others, and keep those of grace in his own hands."

## Historical Laboratory: The Roman Emperors

Machiavelli uses the Roman Emperors from Marcus Aurelius to Maximinus to illustrate how various traits lead to either success or ruin.

| Emperor | Outcome | Machiavelli’s Analysis |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Marcus Aurelius** | Lived and died honored. | Succeeded by hereditary title; possessed virtues that kept both the people and soldiers in their places. Neither hated nor despised. |
| **Pertinax** | Overthrown and murdered. | A "new prince" who tried to force an "honest life" on soldiers accustomed to licentiousness. Despised for his old age. |
| **Alexander** | Murdered by the army. | Extremely good and just, but despised as "effeminate" and for being governed by his mother. |
| **Severus** | Reigned successfully. | A "new prince" who combined the qualities of the **lion and the fox**. His supreme renown and valour awed the people and satisfied the soldiers. |
| **Antoninus Caracalla** | Murdered by a centurion. | Admired by soldiers for being warlike, but his extreme ferocity and "unheard of" cruelties made him hated by the whole world. |
| **Commodus** | Conspired against and killed. | Inherited the empire but fell into contempt by descending to the theatre to fight gladiators; hated for his rapacity and cruelty. |
| **Maximinus** | Murdered by his own army. | Despised for his low birth (a sheep-keeper) and hated for his barbarity and the cruelties of his prefects. |

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. According to the text, what are the two specific actions that most often make a prince hated?**
*Answer: Being rapacious and being a violator of the property and women of his subjects.*

**2. List four qualities that make a prince "contemptible" in the eyes of his subjects.**
*Answer: Being considered fickle, frivolous, effeminate, mean-spirited, or irresolute.*

**3. Why is it difficult for a conspirator to succeed if the prince has "popular goodwill"?**
*Answer: Because a conspirator expects to please the people by removing the prince. If the people love the prince, the conspirator faces infinite difficulties and fears the "sequel to the crime," as they will have the people as an enemy after the act.*

**4. What was the "third difficulty" Roman emperors faced that modern princes (of Machiavelli’s time) generally do not?**
*Answer: The cruelty and avarice of the soldiers. Roman emperors had to satisfy the soldiers' desire for a cruel and rapacious leader, which often conflicted with the people’s desire for peace.*

**5. How did Septimius Severus use the "fox" to deal with his rivals Niger and Albinus?**
*Answer: He used treachery to deceive Albinus by offering to share the title of Caesar with him while he dealt with Niger. Once Niger was killed, he falsely accused Albinus of treachery to justify killing him and taking his state.*

**6. Why does Machiavelli exempt "the Turk" and "the Soldan" from the rule of satisfying the people over the soldiers?**
*Answer: Because their kingdoms depend entirely on the strength and friendship of their standing armies (infantry and cavalry) rather than the general populace.*

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Ethics of Stability:** Machiavelli notes that "hatred is acquired as much by good works as by bad ones" and that a prince is often "forced to do evil" to maintain his state. Using the examples of Pertinax and Alexander, discuss the conflict between personal morality and political survival.
2.  **The Lion and the Fox:** Analyze the career of Septimius Severus. Why does Machiavelli consider him the ultimate example for a "new prince," and how did he successfully navigate the "two opposing humours" of the people and the military?
3.  **Institutional Design:** Examine the example of the French Parliament. How does the creation of an "arbiter" protect the prince from "reproach"? Can this principle be applied to modern governance?
4.  **Hereditary vs. New Princes:** Compare the reign of Marcus Aurelius with that of his son Commodus. Why was Marcus successful in maintaining the state while his son, despite having the same hereditary right, failed?

## Glossary of Key Terms

*   **Contemptible:** To be viewed as weak, fickle, or frivolous; a state that invites conspiracy and rebellion.
*   **Effeminate:** A term used by Machiavelli to describe a prince perceived as soft or easily influenced (e.g., Alexander being governed by his mother), leading to contempt.
*   **Fox and Lion:** A metaphor for the two natures a prince must imitate: the fox to recognize snares (cunning) and the lion to frighten wolves (force/valour).
*   **Humours:** The differing temperaments and desires of social classes (the people, the nobles, and the soldiers).
*   **Malcontent:** A dissatisfied subject. Machiavelli warns that a conspirator must rely on malcontents, who are often unreliable because they can benefit from denouncing the conspiracy.
*   **Rapacious:** Characterized by greed and the forceful taking of property; a primary cause of being hated.
*   **Reproach:** Blame or criticism directed at a prince, often for favoring one social class over another.
*   **Valour:** Great courage and skill, especially in battle; for Severus, this quality was so great that it protected him from the hatred of the people.
