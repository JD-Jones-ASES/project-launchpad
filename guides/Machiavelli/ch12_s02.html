Study Guide: Ecclesiastical Principalities

This study guide examines Chapter XI of Machiavelli’s The Prince, focusing on the unique nature of ecclesiastical principalities, their historical weakness within Italy, and their eventual rise to significant temporal power.

Overview of Ecclesiastical Principalities

Ecclesiastical principalities represent a unique category of statehood in Machiavelli’s political analysis. Unlike hereditary, mixed, new, or civic principalities, these states are governed by religious institutions and follow a distinct set of rules regarding acquisition and maintenance.

Unique Characteristics

According to the source context, ecclesiastical principalities are defined by several paradoxical traits:

  • Acquisition: They are acquired through either capacity (ability) or good fortune.
  • Maintenance: Once acquired, they require neither capacity nor fortune to be maintained. They are sustained by "ancient ordinances of religion" that are so powerful they preserve the state regardless of how the prince lives or behaves.
  • Defense and Governance: These are the only princes who possess states but do not defend them, and have subjects but do not rule them.
  • Security: Despite being unguarded, these states are not seized. Despite being unruled, the subjects do not seek to alienate themselves from the prince. Machiavelli characterizes these principalities as the only ones that are truly "secure and happy."
  • Divine Foundation: Machiavelli attributes their stability to being "exalted and maintained by God," placing them beyond the reach of standard human political logic.

Historical Evolution of Church Temporal Power

The text outlines the transition of the Church from a weak political entity to a dominant temporal power in Italy.

The Pre-Expansion Era

Before the intervention of King Charles of France, Italy was divided among five main powers: the Pope, the Venetians, the King of Naples, the Duke of Milan, and the Florentines. During this time, the Pope's temporal power was held in low esteem for several reasons:

Factor Impact on Papal Power
The Venetians Acted as a check on Papal expansion; required a union of all other powers to restrain.
Roman Barons The Orsini and Colonnesi factions kept the Pontiff weak through constant internal disorder and armed conflict.
Short Papal Reigns The average ten-year lifespan of a Pope was insufficient to permanently suppress rival factions.
Foreign Policy A primary anxiety for Italian potentates was ensuring no foreigner entered Italy under arms.

The Rise of Temporal Greatness

The transformation of the Church into a formidable power was driven by specific historical figures:

  1. Alexander VI: He was the first to demonstrate how a Pope could prevail using both money and arms. Utilizing Duke Valentino as his instrument and taking advantage of the French entry into Italy, he successfully expanded the Church's influence. Although his primary goal was to aggrandize the Duke, his actions ultimately enriched the Church.
  2. Julius II: He inherited a strong Church, the secured Romagna region, and suppressed factions. He furthered this power by finding new ways to accumulate money and successfully pursuing territorial gains (such as Bologna) and the removal of foreign (French) influence. Notably, Julius II acted to strengthen the Church itself rather than any private individual.
  3. Leo X: Inheriting a highly powerful pontificate, Leo X is expected to maintain its greatness through his "goodness and infinite other virtues."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What sustains an ecclesiastical principality once it has been acquired? It is sustained by ancient religious ordinances that are so powerful they maintain the state regardless of the prince’s conduct.
  2. How does Machiavelli describe the subjects of an ecclesiastical prince? They are subjects who are not ruled, yet they neither care about this lack of governance nor have the desire or ability to alienate themselves from the prince.
  3. Why was the temporal power of the Pope "little esteemed" in Italy before Alexander VI? Due to the constant internal strife caused by the Orsini and Colonnesi factions and the short average lifespan of Popes, which prevented long-term suppression of rivals.
  4. What role did Duke Valentino play in the Church’s history? He was the "instrumentality" through which Alexander VI used money and arms to expand territorial control and aggrandize the Church.
  5. What was the primary difference between the motivations of Alexander VI and Julius II? Alexander VI intended to aggrandize Duke Valentino (a private person), whereas Julius II performed his enterprises to strengthen the Church itself.
  6. How did Julius II prevent the Orsini and Colonnesi factions from regaining power? He kept them within the bounds he found them and, crucially, denied them their own cardinals, who were the primary source of factional disorder and ambition.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Paradox of the Unruled State: Analyze Machiavelli’s claim that ecclesiastical principalities are "secure and happy." Why does he argue that these states can exist without defense or active governance, and how does this contrast with his advice for other types of princes?
  2. The Shift from Spiritual to Temporal Power: Using the examples of Alexander VI and Julius II, discuss the methods by which the Papacy transformed from a weak Italian entity into a major European power that could make a King of France "tremble."
  3. The Role of Factionalism in State Weakness: Examine the impact of the Orsini and Colonnesi families on the Roman pontificate. How does Machiavelli use this historical example to illustrate the broader dangers of internal factions and the importance of a leader's "wisdom" and "fortune"?

Glossary of Key Terms and Figures

  • Alexander VI: The Pope who first demonstrated the effectiveness of using money and arms to expand the Church's temporal power.
  • Capacity (Virtù): One of the two methods (alongside fortune) by which a principality is initially acquired.
  • Duke Valentino: The agent and instrument used by Alexander VI to carry out military and territorial expansions.
  • Ecclesiastical Principality: A state held by the Church, unique because it is maintained by religious tradition rather than military or political effort.
  • Julius II: A successor to Alexander VI who further strengthened the Church, gained Bologna, and worked to drive the French out of Italy.
  • Leo X: The Pope at the time of the text's conclusion, noted for inheriting a powerful pontificate and expected to enhance it through virtue.
  • Orsini and Colonnesi: The two primary rival factions of Roman barons whose constant conflict historically kept the Papacy weak.
  • Temporal Power: The secular, political, and military power of the Church, as opposed to its spiritual authority.
  • Venetians: A major Italian power that, along with the Roman barons, served to restrain the territorial ambitions of the Pope prior to the reign of Alexander VI.
