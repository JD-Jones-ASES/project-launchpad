Study Guide: Cesare Borgia and the New Principality

This study guide examines Chapter VII of Niccolò Machiavelli’s The Prince, focusing on the rise and fall of Cesare Borgia. It analyzes the distinctions between power acquired through fortune versus ability and details the specific strategies employed by Borgia to consolidate his state.

Key Concepts and Analysis

1. Fortune vs. Ability (Virtù)

The document establishes a fundamental distinction between two ways of becoming a prince:

  • By Ability: Princes like Francesco Sforza rise through their own skill and "proper means." While the ascent is difficult and filled with "a thousand anxieties," maintaining power is relatively easy because they have laid deep foundations.
  • By Fortune: Those who rise by the "arms and fortune of others" (such as Cesare Borgia) find the ascent effortless—they "fly" to the top. However, they face extreme difficulty remaining there because they lack loyal roots, fixed "foundations," and the knowledge of how to command.

2. The Case of Cesare Borgia (Duke Valentino)

Machiavelli presents Borgia as the primary example of a prince who rose by fortune (his father, Pope Alexander VI’s, influence) but used extraordinary ability to attempt to secure his position. Despite his eventual failure, Machiavelli asserts that Borgia’s actions serve as the "best precepts" for any new prince.

3. Strategies for Consolidating Power

The document outlines the specific steps Borgia took to move from dependence on others to self-reliance:

  • Eliminating Rival Factions: He weakened the Orsini and Colonna parties in Rome by winning over their gentlemen with high pay and government offices.
  • The Use of Fraud: At Sinigalia, he used "wiles" and deception to lure the leaders of the Orsini into a trap, where he had them exterminated.
  • Establishing Order: To pacify the chaotic Romagna region, he appointed a "swift and cruel" governor, Messer Ramiro d’Orco.
  • Managing Public Perception: Once order was restored, Borgia sought to distance himself from the necessary cruelties of his governor. He had Ramiro d’Orco executed and left in a public square, a spectacle that left the people "satisfied and dismayed" while shifting the blame for past severity onto the minister.

4. The Four-Part Plan for Succession

Foreseeing the death of his father, Borgia intended to secure his future against a potentially hostile new Pope through four measures:

  1. Exterminating the families of the lords he had dispossessed to remove any pretext for the Church to reclaim lands.
  2. Winning over the Roman gentlemen to curb the Pope’s power.
  3. Gaining control over the College of Cardinals.
  4. Acquiring enough territory (such as Tuscany, Pisa, Lucca, and Siena) to resist any initial attacks.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to the text, why do princes who rise by fortune have difficulty maintaining their states?

They lack the knowledge of how to command (having lived as private citizens), they do not have loyal or faithful forces, and their states lack "foundations and correspondencies" to withstand political "storms."

2. How did Cesare Borgia handle the threat posed by the Orsini and French forces early in his campaign?

After noticing their hesitation and disloyalty during the attacks on Bologna and Urbino, he decided to "depend no more upon the arms and the luck of others" and began weakening their factions through bribery, office-granting, and eventually, the trap at Sinigalia.

3. What was the purpose of the "court of judgment" established in Romagna?

Borgia set up a court under an "excellent president" with advocates for every city to replace the excessive authority of Ramiro d’Orco, ensuring the government did not become "odious" to the people.

4. What two factors ultimately frustrated Borgia's designs and led to his ruin?

The "extraordinary and extreme malignity of fortune," specifically the death of his father (Alexander VI) occurring while Borgia himself was "sick unto death," and his "bad choice" in the election of Pope Julius II.

5. Why does Machiavelli blame Borgia for the election of Pope Julius II?

Borgia should have ensured a Spaniard became Pope or consented to the election of the Duke of Rouen. Instead, he allowed the election of Julius II (San Pietro ad Vincula), a man he had previously injured. Machiavelli notes that those who believe "new benefits will cause great personages to forget old injuries" are deceived.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Ethics of Stability: Analyze Borgia’s treatment of Ramiro d’Orco. Is Machiavelli suggesting that a leader’s primary moral duty is the "peace and unity" of the state, regardless of the cruelty used to achieve it? Use the text's description of the Romagna to support your argument.
  2. The Limits of Human Agency: Machiavelli argues that Borgia did everything an able man should do, yet he still failed. To what extent does the text suggest that "Ability" (Virtù) can overcome "Fortune"? Is politics ultimately a matter of skill, or are some events beyond even the most capable leader’s control?
  3. Foundations of Power: Compare and contrast the "foundations" laid by Francesco Sforza and Cesare Borgia. Why is the timing of when these foundations are laid (before or after becoming a prince) so critical to the longevity of a state?

Glossary of Key Terms and Figures

Term/Figure Description
Alexander VI The Pope and father of Cesare Borgia; he sought to aggrandize his son by using the power of the Church and French alliances.
Cesare Borgia Also known as Duke Valentino; the central example of a prince who rose by fortune but acted with great ability to secure his state.
Francesco Sforza The Duke of Milan who rose from a private citizen to power through his own great ability and "proper means."
Ramiro d’Orco A "swift and cruel" minister appointed by Borgia to pacify the Romagna; he was later executed by Borgia to appease the populace.
Sinigalia The location where Borgia successfully trapped and exterminated the leaders of the Orsini family.
Julius II Formerly San Pietro ad Vincula; the Pope whose election Borgia failed to prevent, leading to his ultimate downfall.
The Romagna A region initially characterized by "robbery, quarrels, and every kind of violence" under weak masters, which Borgia successfully brought under "peace and obedience."
The Orsini/Colonna Powerful Roman noble families and rival factions that Borgia had to dismantle to secure his own independent power.
Fortune External circumstances, luck, or the "goodwill" of others; characterized by Machiavelli as "inconstant and unstable."
