Study Guide: Machiavelli on Avoiding Contempt and Hatred

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the political theories and historical analyses found in Chapter 20, Section 1, regarding the maintenance of power and the avoidance of two fatal princely errors: being hated and being despised.

Key Concepts and Themes

The Two Fatal Errors

Machiavelli identifies two primary dangers that can lead to a prince's downfall. While a prince is encouraged to be feared rather than loved, he must avoid the following:

  • Hatred: This arises primarily from interfering with the property or the women of subjects. Violating these boundaries turns passive resentment into active fury, leading to conspiracies or vulnerability during external shocks.
  • Contempt: This is a more insidious danger that occurs when a prince is perceived as irresolute, effeminate, cowardly, or weak. A prince who cannot enforce decrees or who retreats from opposition invites challenges from both domestic factions and foreign powers.

The Tension of Governance

Navigating the space between these two dangers requires virtù and prudence. The decisive actions and severity required to avoid being despised (contempt) can, if taken to an extreme, lead to being hated. Conversely, the restraint required to avoid being hated can be misread as weakness, leading to contempt.

The Roman Empire as a "Political Laboratory"

Machiavelli uses the history of Roman emperors as a dataset to determine which political strategies lead to stability or ruin. He focuses particularly on "soldier-emperors," whose power relied on the loyalty of the legions and the balance between military and popular support.

Historical Case Studies: Success and Failure

The Models of Success: Marcus Aurelius vs. Septimius Severus

Machiavelli highlights two emperors who succeeded through diametrically opposed methods, proving that the correct strategy depends on the prince's circumstances.

Emperor Tenure Method of Success Machiavelli’s Assessment
Marcus Aurelius AD 161–180 Legitimate succession, philosophical restraint, and military competence. He was both loved and respected. Because he inherited a stable state, he could afford to be virtuous without needing cruelty.
Septimius Severus AD 193–211 Military coup, ruthless cunning, and the "fox-and-lion" method. He was never despised because he was highly capable and decisive. He used cunning to deceive and force to crush rivals.

The Models of Failure

The following emperors illustrate how either hatred or contempt leads to a loss of power:

  • Commodus (180–192): The son of Marcus Aurelius, he was hated for his personal cruelty and despised for behavior seen as beneath imperial dignity, such as performing as a gladiator.
  • Caracalla (211–217): The son of Severus, he was brilliant in battle but recklessly cruel in peace (e.g., massacring Alexandria), leading to his murder by his own guard.
  • Maximinus (235–238): A soldier of humble origins who was despised by the Senate and the people because he was seen as an outsider without a legitimate claim to dignity.
  • Pertinax (193): Attempted to restore order and discipline but was killed by soldiers who were unwilling to submit to his authority.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to Machiavelli, what are the two main ways a prince earns the hatred of his subjects?
  2. What specific traits or behaviors cause a prince to be viewed with contempt?
  3. Why was Marcus Aurelius able to succeed by being "virtuous" while other emperors were not?
  4. Explain the "fox-and-lion" method as exemplified by Septimius Severus.
  5. Why did the Senate and the people of Rome despise the emperor Maximinus?
  6. What was the fatal error committed by the emperor Caracalla?
  7. Why does Machiavelli consider "contempt" to be more dangerous than being feared?
  8. What is the "Universal Rule" Machiavelli concludes with regarding a prince's security?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Context of Success: Compare and contrast the reigns of Marcus Aurelius and Septimius Severus. Analyze how their differing paths to power (legitimate succession vs. military coup) dictated the methods they used to maintain control.
  2. The Paradox of Firmness: Discuss the tension between avoiding hatred and avoiding contempt. How can a prince exercise the "decisive action and severity" necessary to prevent contempt without crossing the line into the "greed and violation" that causes hatred?
  3. The Weight of Dignity: Using the examples of Commodus and Maximinus, argue the importance of "imperial dignity" in Machiavelli’s analysis. To what extent is a prince’s power dependent on the perception of his status and origins rather than just his actions?
  4. The Political Laboratory: Evaluate Machiavelli's use of Roman history. Why does he prioritize the "soldier-emperors" of the second and third centuries AD, and what does this suggest about his view of the ultimate source of a prince's power?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Contempt: A state of being despised; in a political context, it is the result of a leader appearing weak, indecisive, or cowardly.
  • Fox-and-Lion Method: A strategy of governance involving the use of both cunning/deception (the fox) and overwhelming force (the lion).
  • Hatred: Active fury or resentment from subjects, typically triggered by the prince seizing property or violating the honor of their families.
  • Legitimacy: The quality of having a recognized right to rule, often through birth or legal succession, which can protect a prince from the need to use cruelty.
  • Prudence: The practical wisdom to navigate political tensions and choose the correct course of action based on current circumstances.
  • Soldier-Emperors: Roman rulers whose power was primarily derived from the support and loyalty of the military legions rather than traditional civic institutions.
  • Virtù: The skill, energy, and strength of character required for a prince to maintain his state and achieve great things, often distinct from traditional moral virtue.
