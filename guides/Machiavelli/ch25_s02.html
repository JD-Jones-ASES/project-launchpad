Why Italian Princes Lost Their States: A Machiavellian Perspective

Overview

This section from Chapter 25, Section 2, delves into Niccolò Machiavelli's scathing assessment of why Italian princes in his time lost their states to foreign invaders. Machiavelli argues that these rulers were not victims of bad fortune, but rather lost their power due to their own lack of wisdom, preparedness, and effective governance, failing to secure their people, nobles, and military strength.

Key Concepts

  • Self-Blame for Loss of State: Machiavelli firmly asserts that Italian princes lost their states not due to external fortune, but solely because of their own shortcomings, including a lack of wisdom and preparedness. He observed these failures firsthand and delivered a harsh verdict.
  • The Scrutiny of New Princes: New princes are observed more closely than hereditary ones. However, their ability to establish and secure their state through good laws, arms, allies, and example earns them greater loyalty and a "double glory," binding men tighter than ancient lineage.
  • The "Double Disgrace" of Hereditary Princes: Princes born into power who lose their states through lack of wisdom face a "double disgrace" because they failed to maintain what was handed to them, demonstrating a profound inability to govern.
  • Common Defects Leading to Loss: Machiavelli identifies critical failures among the lost Italian states:
    • Deficiency in Arms: A weak or unreliable military force, often due to reliance on mercenaries.
    • Hostile Populace or Insecure Nobility: Either the people were against them, or they failed to secure the loyalty and support of their nobles, even if the people were friendly.
  • The Importance of Proactive Governance: Successful rulers, exemplified by Philip of Macedon, understood how to be warlike and how to attract the people and secure the nobles, allowing them to endure against powerful enemies and retain their core kingdom.
  • Against Complacency and Reliance on Fortune: Princes are criticized for their "sloth" – failing to prepare for difficult times during periods of calm. They reacted to crises by thinking of flight and hoping for public disgust with conquerors to restore them, a strategy Machiavelli deems unreliable and insecure.
  • Self-Reliance and Valour: True security and lasting dominion come only from a prince's own strength, wisdom, and "valour" (courage and capability), not from wishing for external forces or others to restore them.

Important Terms

  • Machiavelli (Niccolò Machiavelli): An influential Italian diplomat, philosopher, and writer from the Renaissance period. He is considered one of the founders of modern political philosophy, and his most famous work, The Prince, provides practical advice for rulers.
  • Italian Princes: The various rulers of the numerous independent city-states and principalities that made up the fragmented Italian peninsula during Machiavelli's lifetime, many of whom struggled to maintain their power against foreign invasion.
  • The Prince: Machiavelli's seminal 16th-century political treatise, offering a pragmatic analysis of how rulers can acquire, maintain, and expand political power. Chapter XXIV, specifically, examines the reasons for the downfall of Italian princes.
  • Hereditary Prince: A ruler who inherits their position, typically through family lineage, enjoying a form of legitimacy derived from tradition and "ancient blood."
  • New Prince: A ruler who has recently come to power, often through conquest, skill, or other non-hereditary means. Machiavelli argues they are more closely observed but can forge stronger loyalties if competent.
  • Principality: A territory or state ruled by a prince.
  • Defect in Arms: A significant weakness in a state's military forces, which Machiavelli frequently criticizes as a fundamental cause of state weakness, often advocating for citizen militias over mercenary armies.
  • Nobles: The aristocracy or upper class within a state, often holding considerable land, wealth, and influence. Securing their loyalty and cooperation is crucial for a prince to maintain stability and power.
  • Philip of Macedon: An ancient Greek king of Macedon, specifically the one conquered by the Roman general Titus Quintius (not Alexander the Great's father). Machiavelli cites him as an example of a ruler who, despite facing formidable enemies, managed to retain his kingdom through his personal capabilities and shrewd governance.
  • Sloth: In this context, it refers to laziness, idleness, or complacency on the part of a ruler, particularly a failure to prepare for future crises during times of peace and stability. Machiavelli attributes the princes' downfall to this flaw.
  • Fortune: In Machiavelli's writings, "fortune" refers to luck, fate, or the unpredictable external circumstances that can affect a ruler. However, he argues that princes should not blame fortune for their own failures, as they have agency through "virtù" (skill/valour).
  • Valour: Courage, bravery, and personal strength, particularly in a military or political context. Machiavelli emphasizes that a prince's own valour, self-reliance, and decisive action are the only truly reliable sources of security.

Detailed Notes

Machiavelli's Harsh Verdict on Italian Princes

  • Direct Observation: Machiavelli's analysis stems from his real-world experience observing the diplomatic failures and military losses of Italian princes during his own career.
  • Unsparing Judgment: He delivers a severe verdict, asserting that these rulers have no one to blame but themselves for losing their states to foreign invaders. Their downfall was not a matter of fate but of personal and political inadequacy.

The Prince, Chapter XXIV: Key Insights

  1. Establishing and Losing Power: New vs. Hereditary Princes:

    • New Princes and Greater Scrutiny: Newly established princes are under intense observation by their subjects. This scrutiny, however, can be an advantage.
    • Binding Loyalty: If a new prince demonstrates capability, they can quickly gain greater loyalty and support than those who inherit power through "ancient blood." People are more influenced by current benefits than historical ties.
    • "Double Glory": A new prince who successfully establishes and strengthens a principality with good laws, strong arms, reliable allies, and a good personal example achieves immense glory.
    • "Double Disgrace": Conversely, a prince born into power who loses his state due to a lack of wisdom faces a "double disgrace" – failing to maintain what was freely given to him.

  2. Common Defects of Italian Princes Who Lost States:

    • Fundamental Flaw: Deficiency in Arms: Machiavelli highlights a recurring theme: the lack of a strong, reliable military. This often implies relying on unreliable mercenaries rather than native forces.
    • Failure in Internal Governance (People and Nobles):
      • Hostile Populace: Some princes lost their states because their people were against them, indicating poor leadership or oppressive rule.
      • Insecure Nobles: Even if the populace was friendly, some princes failed to secure the loyalty and support of the powerful nobility. Machiavelli implies that control over both groups is essential.
    • Consequence of These Defects: Machiavelli unequivocally states that states with sufficient military power cannot be lost if these internal defects (hostile people, unsecured nobles, weak arms) are absent. This emphasizes that internal strength and good governance are paramount.

  3. The Counter-Example of Philip of Macedon:

    • Not Alexander's Father: Machiavelli clarifies he refers to the Philip who was defeated by the Roman Titus Quintius.
    • Against Overwhelming Odds: This Philip faced powerful adversaries (the great Romans and Greeks) and had relatively little territory.
    • Sustained Rule through Skill: Despite these disadvantages, he "sustained the war against his enemies for many years" because he was "warlike" (competent in military matters) and knew how to "attract the people and secure the nobles."
    • Retention of Kingdom: Even though he lost some cities, he successfully retained his kingdom, demonstrating that capable leadership can preserve a state even when facing superior external threats.

  4. Blaming "Sloth" Not "Fortune": The Ultimate Lesson:

    • Princes' Excuse: Italian princes habitually blamed fortune for their losses, seeking to deflect responsibility.
    • Machiavelli's Rebuke: "Sloth": Machiavelli dismisses this, attributing their downfall to their own "sloth" – their laziness, complacency, and lack of foresight.
    • Common Human Defect: He identifies a universal human flaw: "not to make any provision in the calm against the tempest," meaning people fail to prepare for difficult times during periods of peace.
    • Poor Crisis Management: When "bad times came," these princes reacted by thinking of "flight" rather than "defending themselves."
    • False Hope of Recall: They harbored the naive hope that their people, disgusted by the conquerors, would eventually recall them to power.
    • Machiavelli's Dismissal of This Hope:
      • He views this strategy as a last resort, and "very bad" to neglect all other expedients for it.
      • Reliance on others for deliverance is unreliable; it "either does not happen, or, if it does, it will not be for your security."
      • True Security and Valour: Machiavelli's core message is that lasting, reliable security and freedom ("deliverance") must "depend upon yourself and your valour." A prince must be self-reliant and possess the strength and ability to defend his state.

Review Questions

  1. According to Machiavelli, who is ultimately to blame for the loss of states by Italian princes, and why does he dismiss "fortune" as the primary cause?
  2. Compare and contrast Machiavelli's view on the security and reputation of a "new prince" versus an "hereditary prince." What constitutes a "double glory" or a "double disgrace"?
  3. What two main "common defects" does Machiavelli identify among Italian princes who lost their states? How do these defects relate to a state's ability to maintain power, even with a strong army?
  4. How does Machiavelli use the example of Philip of Macedon to support his arguments? What specific qualities did Philip demonstrate that were lacking in the Italian princes?
  5. Machiavelli criticizes princes for their "sloth" and for hoping to be recalled by their people after fleeing. Explain why he considers this approach dangerous and ineffective, and what alternative he proposes for true security.
