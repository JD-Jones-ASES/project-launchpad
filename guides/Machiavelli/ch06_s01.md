# Study Guide: Governing Conquered Free Cities

This study guide examines the strategies and philosophical underpinnings of Chapter V of Machiavelli’s *The Prince*, which focuses on the methods a ruler may use to maintain control over a city or principality that previously lived under its own laws and traditions of republican freedom.

---

## Key Concepts and Strategies

### The Three Options for Rule
Machiavelli identifies three distinct methods for a prince who has conquered a city accustomed to self-governance. Each option carries specific risks and requirements:

| Strategy | Description | Practical Outcome |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Destruction** | Dismantling the city’s institutions and dispersing its population. | **Most Secure:** Removes the organizational capacity for resistance and rebellion. |
| **Personal Residency** | The prince moves to the conquered city to govern in person. | **Demanding but Workable:** Allows for immediate response to issues and relationship building, but only effective while the prince remains. |
| **Indirect Rule** | Allowing the city to keep its laws while extracting tribute and installing a friendly local oligarchy. | **Inherently Weak:** The memory of past freedom eventually leads to rebellion against the collaborators. |

### The Security of Destruction
Machiavelli argues that destruction is the most reliable strategy for a conqueror. He posits that a city that has been destroyed cannot rebel because its citizens lack the structure to organize. He uses the historical example of **Rome and Carthage** to illustrate this. Rome initially attempted to allow Carthage to survive under controlled conditions, but Carthage eventually rebelled. Only after Rome razed the city entirely was the "Carthaginian problem" solved.

### The Failure of Indirect Rule
The attempt to govern through a friendly local oligarchy (a small group of collaborators) is considered the most precarious method. Machiavelli cites **Sparta** as a historical failure of this model; Sparta installed friendly governments in conquered Greek cities, yet every one of those cities eventually rebelled. The primary cause of failure is the "memory of liberty," which serves as a rallying cry for the population and can even lead the oligarchs themselves to turn against the prince.

### The Memory of Liberty
Perhaps the most significant insight in this chapter is the endurance of political culture. Machiavelli argues that the memory of self-governance is a "politically explosive" force. It outlasts suppression, the dismantling of institutions, and even the passing of generations. As long as the idea of former freedom persists, a conquered city remains a threat to its new ruler.

### Political Realism
Machiavelli’s method is defined by **realism**, which prioritizes "what actually works" over what is "just or merciful." By separating moral judgment from political analysis, he explores the brutal realities of power. His willingness to suggest the destruction of a city as an effective—though not necessarily "good"—strategy exemplifies this analytical approach.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

### 1. The Conflict Between Realism and Morality
Machiavelli’s analysis of Chapter V focuses entirely on effectiveness rather than justice. Discuss how his recommendation to destroy free cities challenges traditional moral frameworks. Is the "honest" reporting of how power functions more valuable than the promotion of political ideals? Support your argument using the text's definition of political realism.

### 2. The Persistence of Political Identity
Evaluate Machiavelli’s claim that the "memory of liberty" is an indestructible force. Does history support the idea that a population's tradition of self-governance is the greatest obstacle to conquest? Consider whether modern examples of nation-building or colonization confirm or refute his observation that institutions can be torn down, but ideas persist.

### 3. Evaluating the Three Options
Compare and contrast the three strategies provided by Machiavelli. While he argues that destruction is the most secure, he acknowledges that personal residency and indirect rule are options. Analyze the specific vulnerabilities of personal residency and why it fails to provide a permanent solution compared to the total dismantling of the city.

## Glossary of Key Terms

*   **Indirect Rule:** A governing strategy where the conqueror allows a city to maintain its own laws and customs but requires the payment of tribute and the installation of a local governing body loyal to the conqueror.
*   **Memory of Liberty:** The enduring cultural and political recognition of a city's past self-governance, which Machiavelli identifies as a primary driver of rebellion in conquered territories.
*   **Oligarchy:** A small group of people having control of a country, organization, or institution; in this context, local collaborators installed by a prince to govern on his behalf.
*   **Political Realism:** A political philosophy or method characterized by the prioritization of practical, effective power dynamics over moral or ethical considerations.
*   **Principality:** A state or territory ruled by a prince or monarch.
*   **Republican Freedom:** A tradition of self-governance where a city or state is ruled by its own laws and institutions rather than a foreign or absolute monarch.
*   **Tribute:** A wealth or tax paid periodically by one state or ruler to another as a sign of dependence or to ensure protection.
