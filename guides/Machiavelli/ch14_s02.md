# Study Guide: Machiavelli on Auxiliaries and National Defense

This study guide explores the principles of military self-reliance as outlined in the analysis of "Auxiliaries and One's Own Arms." It examines the inherent dangers of relying on external military force and the necessity of a prince establishing his own independent military power.

---

## Key Concepts and Core Themes

### 1. The Nature of Auxiliaries
Auxiliaries are defined as troops provided by another ruler to aid and defend a prince. While these forces may be competent in their own right, they are considered "useless" and "disadvantageous" to the ruler who requests them. The fundamental risks include:
*   **The Double Bind:** If the auxiliaries lose the battle, the prince is ruined. If they win the battle, the prince becomes their prisoner or remains under their control.
*   **Unified Obedience:** Unlike mercenaries, auxiliaries are a united force that owes its total obedience to someone else (their original ruler), making them a ready-made source of ruin.
*   **The Danger of Valor:** Machiavelli argues that while the greatest danger in mercenaries is their cowardice (*dastardy*), the greatest danger in auxiliaries is their courage (*valour*).

### 2. The Hierarchy of Military Forces
The text ranks different types of military arrangements based on their reliability and the security they provide to a state:
*   **One’s Own Forces (Highest Reliability):** Composed of a prince’s own subjects, citizens, or dependents. This is the only way to achieve "real victory" and long-term security.
*   **Mixed Forces:** A combination of national and mercenary/auxiliary forces. While superior to purely mercenary or auxiliary armies, they are significantly inferior to a fully national force.
*   **Mercenaries:** Paid soldiers who are not unified and require time to turn against a prince.
*   **Auxiliaries (Lowest Reliability):** Unified foreign forces that are dangerous even when they are successful.

### 3. The Illusion of Mixed Success
The text warns that rulers often fail to recognize the hidden "poison" in arrangements that appear beneficial at first. A ruler who cannot identify an evil until it is fully realized is not "truly wise." The dependency on foreign arms (like the French reliance on Swiss infantry) diminishes the value and reputation of a prince's own soldiers.

## Historical Case Studies

The following table summarizes the historical examples provided to illustrate the dangers of external arms:

| Figure/State | External Force Used | Outcome |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Pope Julius II** | Spanish Auxiliaries | He was saved only by "good fortune" when the Switzers drove out the victors, preventing him from becoming a prisoner of his allies. |
| **Florentines** | French Auxiliaries | Attempted to take Pisa and faced more danger than at any other point in their history. |
| **Emperor of Constantinople** | Turkish Auxiliaries | Once the war ended, the Turks refused to leave, beginning the "servitude of Greece." |
| **Cesare Borgia** | French/Orsini/Vitelli | He moved from auxiliaries to mercenaries, found both unfaithful, and finally established his own forces, which led to his highest reputation. |
| **Hiero of Syracuse** | Mercenaries (*condottieri*) | Found them useless and dangerous; had them destroyed so he could fight with his own forces. |
| **King Louis XI of France** | Swiss Mercenaries | By enlisting Switzers and abolishing French infantry, he made his own army dependent on foreign help and weakened the kingdom. |
| **Roman Empire** | Goths | The enlisting of the Goths marked the beginning of the empire's decline and the transfer of Roman "valour" to others. |

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Paradox of Victory:** Analyze Machiavelli's claim that a victory gained with the arms of others is not a "real victory." Use specific examples from the text to explain why winning with auxiliaries can be as dangerous as losing.
2.  **The Wisdom of Foresight:** The text compares political evils to "hectic fevers" that are hard to diagnose early but easy to cure, and later easy to diagnose but impossible to cure. Discuss the role of the "wise prince" in identifying military "poison" before it destroys the state.
3.  **The Evolution of Cesare Borgia:** Trace the transformation of Cesare Borgia’s military strategy. How does his journey from relying on French auxiliaries to his own national forces serve as a template for Machiavelli’s ideal ruler?
4.  **The Price of Dependency:** Using the examples of France and the Roman Empire, discuss how relying on foreign valor (the Switzers or the Goths) inevitably leads to the erosion of a nation's own strength and "reputation."

## Glossary of Terms

*   **Auxiliaries:** Foreign troops borrowed from another ruler to assist in a conflict; described as the most dangerous form of military assistance.
*   **Condottieri:** Professional mercenary military leaders in Renaissance Italy hired by city-states to lead their wars.
*   **Dastardy:** Cowardice or lack of spirit; identified as the primary risk when using mercenary forces.
*   **Mixed Forces:** An army composed of both national soldiers and hired mercenaries or auxiliaries.
*   **One's Own Forces:** A military comprised of a prince’s own subjects, citizens, or dependents; the only basis for true security and power.
*   **Orsini and Vitelli:** Mercenary leaders employed and eventually destroyed by Cesare Borgia when he found them unfaithful.
*   **Principality:** A state or territory ruled by a prince.
*   **Switzers:** Swiss mercenary soldiers, known for their infantry, upon whom the French became dangerously dependent.
*   **Valour:** Great courage or bravery, particularly in battle; cited as the quality that makes auxiliaries dangerous to the prince who employs them.
