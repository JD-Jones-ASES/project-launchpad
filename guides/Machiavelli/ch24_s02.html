Machiavelli on Avoiding Flatterers: A Study Guide

This study guide examines the principles outlined in Chapter XXIII of Niccolò Machiavelli’s The Prince. The text addresses the critical challenge of how a sovereign can secure honest advice in a court environment naturally prone to flattery and deception.

I. Key Concepts and Principles

The Problem of Flattery and Self-Complacency

Machiavelli identifies flattery as a "pest" that is difficult for princes to avoid. This difficulty arises from two main factors:

  • Human Nature: Individuals are often self-complacent regarding their own affairs and easily deceived by their own perspectives.
  • The Risk of Contempt: If a prince allows anyone to tell him the truth at any time, he risks losing the respect and majesty of his position. Conversely, if he ignores the truth to protect his ego, he falls victim to flatterers.

The "Third Course" of Advisement

To balance the need for truth with the need for authority, Machiavelli proposes a specific structural solution:

  1. Selective Liberty: The prince should choose a small group of wise men for his state.
  2. Restricted Truth-Telling: These advisors are granted the liberty to speak the truth only on subjects the prince specifically inquires about.
  3. Active Inquiry: The prince must question these advisors on everything, listen to their opinions patiently, and then form his own independent conclusions.
  4. Steadfastness: Once a resolution is reached, the prince must be "steadfast" and "pursue the thing resolved on." Changing one's mind due to varying opinions leads to a loss of reputation and contempt.

The Role of Sovereign Wisdom

A central axiom of Machiavelli’s thought is that "good counsels... are born of the wisdom of the prince, and not the wisdom of the prince from good counsels."

  • Recognition of Advice: An unwise prince cannot recognize good advice unless he has surrendered his power entirely to a single prudent individual.
  • The Danger of a Single Advisor: While a single prudent advisor might govern well for a time, they are likely to eventually seize the state from the prince.
  • Synthesizing Diverse Opinions: If an inexperienced prince takes counsel from many, he will receive conflicting advice. Without his own wisdom, he will be unable to unite these opinions or see through the self-interests of his counselors.

The Example of Emperor Maximilian I

Machiavelli uses the Holy Roman Emperor Maximilian I as a cautionary example of an "irresolute" prince.

  • Secrecy vs. Counsel: Maximilian was secretive and did not communicate his designs or seek opinions.
  • The Consequence: When his plans were put into effect and became known, they were immediately obstructed by those around him. Being "pliant," the Emperor would change his plans, leading to inconsistency where "those things he does one day he undoes the next."

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to the text, why is it dangerous for a prince to let everyone speak the truth to him?

Answer: When every person is permitted to tell the truth to the prince at any time, the respect for the prince’s authority abates (diminishes).

2. Under what specific conditions should a prince's advisors be allowed to speak the truth?

Answer: Advisors should only speak the truth regarding matters the prince has specifically asked about, and at no other time.

3. What happens to a prince who is "pliant" and frequently changes his mind based on varying opinions?

Answer: He falls into contempt and his resolutions cannot be relied upon by others.

4. What is Machiavelli’s "axiom" regarding a prince’s wisdom and his advisors?

Answer: A prince who is not wise himself will never take good advice, because good counsel is a product of the prince's own wisdom, not the source of it.

5. Why is it problematic for an unwise prince to take counsel from multiple advisors?

Answer: The prince will receive "united counsels" (conflicting advice) and will lack the ability to control the advisors or understand their individual self-interests.

6. How should a prince react if he discovers an advisor has not told him the truth?

Answer: He should let his anger be felt to discourage future dishonesty.

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Balance of Authority and Information: Discuss the tension Machiavelli identifies between maintaining "majesty" and seeking "truth." How does his proposed system of selective advisors attempt to resolve this tension, and what are the potential risks if the balance shifts too far in either direction?
  2. The Constraint of Human Nature: Machiavelli asserts that "men will always prove untrue to you unless they are kept honest by constraint." Analyze how this view of human nature informs his specific rules for ministerial conduct and the prince's behavior toward his court.
  3. Wisdom as a Prerequisite for Power: Critically examine Machiavelli’s argument that a prince cannot become wise simply by hiring wise advisors. In his view, what is the specific relationship between the character of the ruler and the quality of the governance?
  4. A Study in Failure: Maximilian I: Using the example of Emperor Maximilian, explain the paradox of a prince who is "secretive" yet ends up being "pliant." Why does Machiavelli argue that a lack of early consultation leads to a loss of control in the execution of plans?

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition in Context
Axiom A self-evident truth or established principle that serves as a foundation for further reasoning.
Contempt A state of being despised or viewed as lacking authority; the opposite of being respected and feared.
Constraint The use of systems, rules, or punishments to ensure that individuals (like advisors) remain honest and loyal.
Flatterers Courtiers who provide insincere praise or deceptive advice to win favor, often leading a prince to ruin.
Irresolute Lacking in firmness or purpose; hesitant; characterized by a tendency to change one's mind frequently.
Pliant Easily influenced or swayed by the opinions of others; lacking the steadfastness required of a ruler.
Self-complacent Being overly satisfied with one's own affairs or judgment, which leads to being easily deceived by flattery.
Third Course The strategic middle ground Machiavelli suggests for a prince to obtain truth from a limited group of wise men without losing authority.
