Study Guide: Machiavelli's Exhortation to Liberate Italy (The Prince, Chapter XXVI)

Overview

Chapter XXVI, titled "An Exhortation to Liberate Italy," marks a dramatic and passionate shift in Machiavelli's tone and purpose. After twenty-five chapters of cool, detached political analysis, he transforms into a fervent advocate, revealing that The Prince is not merely a handbook for rulers, but a desperate plea for a new leader to unite and free Italy from foreign domination. This final chapter recontextualizes all of Machiavelli's earlier, often ruthless, advice as a strategic blueprint for achieving national liberation.

Key Concepts

  • The Tone Shift: From Chapter 1 to 25, Machiavelli writes as a detached, systematic, and unsentimental analyst of power politics. In Chapter XXVI, his voice becomes passionately engaged, prophetic, and appeals directly for Italy's liberation, highlighting his deep personal stakes.
  • Italy's Suffering as Opportunity: Machiavelli argues that Italy's profound suffering in 1513—being "enslaved," "oppressed," "scattered," and "despoiled"—creates a "propitious" moment for a new prince to emerge. This mirrors the conditions faced by great founders like Moses, Cyrus, and Romulus, whose peoples' distress opened the door for their leadership.
  • The Prince as a Plea for National Liberation: The ultimate purpose of The Prince is revealed to be a passionate call for Italian national unity and liberation from foreign invaders. All the advice given throughout the book, from building one's own army to securing popular support, is presented as necessary medicine for a "sick state" in crisis.
  • Moses as an Archetypal Liberator: Machiavelli returns to Moses not just as an "armed prophet" (as earlier in the book), but as a symbol of a divinely appointed leader who can lead a suffering people out of bondage. He daringly suggests that a new Italian prince, like the Medici, could become Italy's "Moses."
  • Complicating the Cynical Reading: The emotional intensity and patriotic appeal of Chapter XXVI strongly refute interpretations of The Prince as a purely amoral guide for tyrants. It demonstrates that Machiavelli possessed profound moral and political stakes, caring deeply about Italy's freedom rather than just observing power dynamics neutrally.
  • Ruthlessness in Service of Liberation: While Machiavelli's earlier advice on cruelty, deception, and pragmatic morality remains, this chapter re-frames it. The controversial means are not ends in themselves, but tools recommended for the higher, noble goal of establishing political conditions for a free and unified Italy.

Important Terms

  • The Prince: Niccolò Machiavelli's influential 16th-century political treatise, offering advice to rulers on how to acquire, maintain, and wield political power.
  • Chapter XXVI (The Exhortation): The final chapter of The Prince, which dramatically shifts in tone to a passionate appeal for the liberation and unification of Italy.
  • Virtù: A key Machiavellian concept referring to a prince's strength, skill, courage, foresight, and ability to act decisively and effectively in the political sphere, often against the dictates of fortune. In this context, it refers to the qualities needed by a liberator.
  • Fortune: Refers to luck, fate, or external circumstances that are beyond a prince's direct control. Machiavelli discusses how a prince can attempt to manage or mitigate the effects of fortune through virtù.
  • Propitious: Favorable, opportune; indicating a good chance of success. Machiavelli describes the state of Italy as "propitious" for a new prince.
  • Armed Prophet: A leader who combines religious or ideological vision with the military capacity to enforce that vision. Moses is presented as the primary example.
  • Allegorical Figure: A symbolic representation of an abstract concept, often personified (e.g., a figure representing a nation, as in Philipp Veit's painting).
  • Dirty Hands Problem: A concept in political philosophy where leaders may be required to commit morally questionable or "dirty" acts to achieve a greater good for the state or community. Machiavelli's advice in The Prince is a classic example of this dilemma.

Detailed Notes

The Tone Shift and Its Significance

  • Contrast with Earlier Chapters: For the first twenty-five chapters, Machiavelli maintains a "detached analyst" persona. His prose is cool, systematic, unsentimental, and focused on practical strategies for gaining and maintaining power. He discusses conquering states, breaking promises, using cruelty, and managing fortune with a clinical precision.
  • Shift in Chapter XXVI: This final chapter abruptly changes character. The analytical voice gives way to an impassioned appeal, case studies are replaced by prophecy, and the professional detachment transforms into the desperate voice of a man deeply concerned for his country's freedom.
  • Understanding the True Purpose: This dramatic shift is crucial for understanding The Prince as a whole. It suggests that the entire preceding discussion has been leading to this exhortation, revealing Machiavelli's ultimate motivation.

Italy's Suffering as Opportunity for Liberation

  • A "Propitious" Moment: Machiavelli opens Chapter XXVI by declaring that the current moment is uniquely favorable for a new prince to emerge and liberate Italy.
  • Revisiting Great Founders: He reinforces this argument by recalling the examples of Moses, Cyrus, and Theseus (and Romulus from earlier in Chapter VI). These leaders succeeded because they found their respective peoples in states of extreme suffering or vulnerability, which prepared them to embrace a liberator.
    • Moses found the Israelites enslaved.
    • Cyrus found the Persians oppressed by Medes.
    • Romulus was abandoned, forcing him to rise to greatness.
  • Italy's Analogous Condition (1513): Machiavelli draws a devastating parallel, describing Italy as: "more enslaved than the Hebrews, more oppressed than the Persians, more scattered than the Athenians; without head, without order, beaten, despoiled, torn, overrun; and she has endured every kind of desolation."
  • Hunger for a Liberator: This profound suffering, paradoxically, creates the ideal conditions for a new prince. The people are stripped of complacency and genuinely ready to follow someone who can provide unity, order, and independence.

The True Core of The Prince: A Plea for Italian National Liberation

  • Beyond Tyranny: Chapter XXVI profoundly re-frames The Prince. It clarifies that the book is not, at its deepest level, merely a manual for amoral power or a defense of tyranny for its own sake.
  • A "Medicine for a Sick State": Instead, it is revealed as a "plea for Italian national liberation." Every piece of advice previously offered—from building an indigenous army and securing popular support to avoiding hatred and acting boldly—is ultimately directed towards an Italian prince who could unify the fragmented peninsula and expel the foreign powers devastating it since 1494.
  • Hope for a Virtuous Leader: Machiavelli expresses hope that a leader, perhaps from the powerful Medici family, will possess the virtù necessary to enact this plan and free Italy.

The Return of Moses: A Poetic Vision

  • Moses as a Type: Machiavelli’s repeated reference to Moses is significant. While earlier he was an example of an "armed prophet" (a strategic model), in Chapter XXVI, Moses becomes a type or archetypal figure whose story directly mirrors Italy’s predicament.
  • The Medici as Italy's Moses: The comparison is bold: Machiavelli suggests that whoever possesses sufficient virtù could be Italy's Moses, leading a suffering people out of bondage into freedom.
  • Elevated Language: This section sees Machiavelli's writing become genuinely elevated and poetic, culminating in a quote from Petrarch's patriotic poem, which calls for Italy's liberation and evokes the "ancient valor" still present in Italian hearts.

Complicating the "Cynical Reading"

  • Evidence Against Amoral Power: Many historical and modern readers interpret The Prince as a celebration of cynical, amoral power politics. However, the emotional intensity and passionate patriotism of Chapter XXVI provide the strongest counter-argument against this view.
  • Machiavelli's Stakes: A truly detached analyst would not end a work with such a fervent appeal. Machiavelli clearly cares deeply about Italy's fate; he is not neutral but actively "hoping" for its liberation.
  • Ruthlessness for a Noble Cause: This doesn't mean Machiavelli retracts his earlier advice on cruelty, deception, or prioritizing political necessity over conventional morality. Rather, it contextualizes it: the ruthlessness is presented as a necessary means to achieve the ultimate goal of Italian freedom, not as an end in itself or for personal tyrannical gain. It's a pragmatic approach to achieve a highly moral and patriotic objective.

Unfulfilled Vision and the "Dirty Hands" Problem

  • Historical Outcome: Despite Machiavelli's passionate plea, his advice was not taken by the Medici or any other Italian prince of his time. Italy remained divided and largely under foreign domination for centuries, only achieving national unification in 1861.
  • Ethical Dilemma: This historical context encourages reflection on the "dirty hands" problem: Does the nobility of the goal (national liberation) justify or explain the morally ambiguous means (deception, cruelty) Machiavelli recommends? Students are challenged to consider whether the character of the means changes depending on the character of the end.

Review Questions

  1. How does Machiavelli's tone shift dramatically from the opening chapters to Chapter XXVI, and what does this transformation reveal about his true motivations for writing The Prince?
  2. Explain Machiavelli's argument that Italy's severe suffering in 1513 created a "propitious" opportunity for a new prince. What historical figures does he invoke to support this claim, and how do their stories parallel Italy's situation?
  3. In what ways does Chapter XXVI challenge the common interpretation of The Prince as an amoral guide for tyrants? What does the "Exhortation" tell us about Machiavelli's own moral and political investment?
  4. Discuss the significance of Machiavelli's return to the figure of Moses in the final chapter. How does Moses's role here differ from his earlier mention as an "armed prophet," and what vision does he represent for Italy?
  5. Consider Machiavelli's advice in The Prince in the context of the "dirty hands" problem in political philosophy. Does the ultimate goal of Italian liberation, as presented in Chapter XXVI, justify or at least help explain the ruthless and often morally questionable means he recommends throughout the book?
