Study Guide: Cesare Borgia and the New Prince

This study guide explores the political career of Cesare Borgia as analyzed in Chapter VII of Machiavelli’s political discourse. It focuses on the challenges faced by princes who rise to power through the fortune and military strength of others and the methodical strategies Borgia employed to mitigate those vulnerabilities.

Executive Summary: The Model of the New Prince

The text identifies Cesare Borgia (c. 1475–1507), also known as Duke Valentino, as the primary example of a "new prince" who ascended to power via the arms and fortune of another—specifically his father, Pope Alexander VI. While Machiavelli is generally skeptical of such rulers because their foundations are often insecure and dependent on the goodwill of others, he considers Borgia an extraordinary exception.

Borgia is characterized as a methodical, ruthless, and highly capable leader who recognized the scale of his opportunity and worked systematically to build a lasting foundation for his state. Despite his eventual political collapse, Machiavelli maintains that Borgia’s failure was the result of extraordinary bad luck (fortuna) rather than a lack of ability.

Key Strategic Actions

Machiavelli admires Borgia for his long-range strategy to ensure survival after his father’s death. His approach consisted of three primary pillars:

  1. Neutralizing Rivals: He systematically weakened the families allied to major powers in the Romagna, specifically the Orsini (allied with France) and the Colonna (allied with Spain).
  2. Influencing the Papacy: He worked to ensure that any future pope would either be a supporter or too weak to pose a threat to his interests.
  3. Military Independence: He transitioned away from reliance on hired commanders and his father’s resources to build a military force that owed its primary loyalty to him.

Case Study: The Remirro de Orco Episode

The pacification of the Romagna serves as a primary example of Borgia's "brilliant political management" and his understanding of public perception.

  • The Problem: The Romagna was a territory of petty lordships characterized by chaos, private wars, and cruelty.
  • The Instrument: Borgia appointed Remirro de Orco, a "cruel and capable man," as governor. Remirro used brutal efficiency to restore order and pacify the region.
  • The Dilemma: While order was restored, the population grew to hate Remirro for his cruelty.
  • The Resolution: To satisfy the people and deflect the blame for the necessary violence, Borgia had Remirro arrested and executed. His body was displayed in the public square of Cesena, cut in two.
  • The Result: Machiavelli notes the spectacle left the people "satisfied and stupefied." It effectively removed the instrument of cruelty while allowing Borgia to claim credit for restoring both order and justice.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. Why does Machiavelli believe princes who rise through "others' fortune" generally face insecure foundations? Answer: They are dependent on the continued goodwill and arms of those who helped them rise, rather than their own ability and established power bases.

2. How did Rodrigo Borgia (Pope Alexander VI) use his position to benefit his son? Answer: He used the resources of the Church—including money, political influence, and the threat of spiritual sanctions—to carve out a state for Cesare in central Italy.

3. What were the two main families Borgia targeted to reduce potential rivals in the Romagna? Answer: The Orsini and the Colonna families.

4. According to the text, what was the "one mistake" Borgia made that led to his downfall? Answer: He allowed the election of Julius II to the papacy, an enemy he had hoped to prevent from taking office.

5. How does Machiavelli describe the reaction of the people to the execution of Remirro de Orco? Answer: He describes them as being "at once satisfied and stupefied."

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Role of Fortuna vs. Virtue: Based on the text, analyze the tension between Borgia’s personal "ability" and the role of "fortune." To what extent can a leader control their own destiny when their power is founded on the resources of others? Use Borgia’s illness and the death of Alexander VI as evidence for your argument.

2. The Ethics of Political Perception: Examine the Remirro de Orco episode as a theory of political perception. Machiavelli suggests that the "impression" of justice is as important as the reality of order. Is Borgia’s manipulation of Remirro an act of political genius or a corruption of the very order he sought to establish?

3. The Strategy of Independence: The text outlines three steps Borgia took to build a foundation. Discuss why the creation of a personal military force is often considered the most critical of these steps for a "new prince." How does this relate to Machiavelli’s skepticism of "others' arms"?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Alexander VI (Rodrigo Borgia): The Spanish-born Pope (1492–1503) who used the wealth and influence of the Holy See to advance his family’s interests, specifically those of his son, Cesare.
  • Cesare Borgia (Duke Valentino): The illegitimate son of Alexander VI; a cardinal turned military commander who conquered the Romagna and is used by Machiavelli as the model of a capable new prince.
  • Fortuna: A term used to describe luck or the unpredictable concatenation of events (such as Borgia's ill-timed illness) that can undermine even the most methodical political planning.
  • Julius II: The successor to the papacy after Alexander VI; an enemy of Borgia whose election Borgia failed to prevent, leading to his ultimate political collapse.
  • Remirro de Orco: The cruel governor appointed by Borgia to pacify the Romagna; he was eventually executed by Borgia to shift the blame for the region's harsh pacification away from the Prince.
  • The Romagna: A territory in central Italy consisting of petty lordships that Borgia conquered and attempted to consolidate into a singular state.
  • Satisfied and Stupefied: The phrase Machiavelli uses to describe the public’s psychological state following the spectacle of Remirro de Orco’s execution—combining a sense of justice served with a fear of the Prince’s power.
