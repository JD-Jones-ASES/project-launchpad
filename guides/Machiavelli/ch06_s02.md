# Study Guide: Governing Conquered Free Cities

This study guide explores the strategies and challenges associated with governing states that were previously accustomed to living under their own laws and in freedom, as outlined in Chapter V of Niccolò Machiavelli’s *The Prince*.

---

## I. Core Concepts and Strategies

When a prince acquires a state that has historically lived in freedom, Machiavelli identifies three distinct courses of action to maintain control. Each carries different levels of risk and reliability.

### The Three Methods of Control
1.  **Destruction (Ruin):** To physically or structurally dismantle the city. This is characterized as the only "safe" way to ensure a formerly free state does not rebel.
2.  **Personal Residence:** The prince moves to the conquered territory to reside there in person. This allows for immediate response to unrest and a more direct exercise of authority.
3.  **Indirect Rule via Oligarchy:** Permitting the city to continue living under its own laws while demanding a tribute and establishing an internal government (an oligarchy) composed of local citizens who are loyal to the prince.

### The Rationale for Indirect Rule
The third method relies on the idea that an oligarchy created by the prince cannot stand without his "friendship and interest." Because the local governors owe their power entirely to the prince, they will do their utmost to support him. Machiavelli suggests that a city accustomed to freedom is held more easily through its own citizens than by any other means, provided this loyalty is maintained.

---

## II. The Vitality of Republics vs. Principalities

A central theme of the text is the psychological and political difference between states accustomed to a monarch and those accustomed to liberty.

| Feature | States Accustomed to a Prince | Republics (Free Cities) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ease of Conquest** | Easier to secure once the ruling family is exterminated. | Difficult to hold without extreme measures. |
| **Self-Governance** | They do not know how to govern themselves and cannot agree on a new leader. | Possess a "rallying point" in the memory of ancient privileges. |
| **Response to Change** | Slow to take up arms. | High vitality, greater hatred, and a desire for vengeance. |
| **Memory of Liberty** | Not applicable. | Never forgotten, regardless of time or benefits received. |

---

## III. Historical Case Studies

Machiavelli uses classical examples to illustrate why certain methods fail while others succeed.

*   **The Spartans (Athens and Thebes):** The Spartans attempted to hold Athens and Thebes by establishing oligarchies. Despite this indirect rule, they eventually lost both cities.
*   **The Romans (Capua, Carthage, and Numantia):** The Romans chose to "dismantle" these cities. By ruining them, they successfully maintained control and did not lose the territories.
*   **The Romans (Greece):** Initially, the Romans attempted to hold Greece by following the Spartan model—permitting the Greeks to live under their own laws and maintain their freedom. This failed, and the Romans were eventually compelled to dismantle many cities in the country to retain their hold.
*   **The Florentines (Pisa):** Pisa serves as a modern (to Machiavelli) example of the enduring memory of liberty. After being held for one hundred years by the Florentines, Pisa rebelled at the first opportunity, proving that time does not erase the desire for freedom.

## V. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Necessity of Destruction:** Machiavelli argues that "the safest way is to destroy them or to reside there." Analyze the evidence provided in the text regarding the Romans and Spartans. Does the text suggest that any amount of "benefits" or "time" can reconcile a free people to a new prince?
2.  **The Psychology of Rebellion:** Compare and contrast the "vitality" of a republic with the "obedience" of a principality. How do these inherent characteristics dictate the military and political strategies a conquering prince must employ?
3.  **The Role of Oligarchy:** Evaluate the effectiveness of using a "government of the few" to manage a conquered territory. Based on the text, what are the strengths and inherent weaknesses of this strategy compared to the strategy of "ruining" the city?

## VI. Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Bondage:** A state of being held under the control of another; used to describe Pisa’s hundred-year subjection to Florence.
*   **Dismantle:** To pull down or destroy the structures (physical or political) of a city to prevent it from being used as a base for rebellion.
*   **Free City:** A state accustomed to living under its own laws and exercising self-governance.
*   **Oligarchy:** A small group of people having control of a country, organization, or institution. In this context, it is a local government created by a prince to maintain his interests.
*   **Principality:** A state or territory ruled by a prince or monarch.
*   **Republic:** A state where power is held by the people or their representatives, characterized by "vitality" and a deep-seated memory of liberty.
*   **Tribute:** Periodic payment made by one state to another as a sign of dependence or to ensure protection.
*   **Watchword of Liberty:** The symbolic name or concept of "freedom" used as a rallying cry for rebellion in formerly free states.
