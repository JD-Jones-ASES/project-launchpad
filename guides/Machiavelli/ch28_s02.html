The Italian Realist School: Elite Theory After Machiavelli

Overview

This section introduces the Italian Realist School, a group of 19th and early 20th-century political thinkers—Gaetano Mosca, Vilfredo Pareto, and Robert Michels—who systematically developed Machiavelli's core insights into a theory of political power. They argued that power is invariably exercised by a small, organized minority over a large, disorganized majority, regardless of a society's formal constitutional structure. Their work transformed Machiavelli's practical observations into a general academic theory of elite rule.

Key Concepts

  • Italian Realist School (Elite Theory School): A group of Italian social and political thinkers (Mosca, Pareto, Michels) who systematized Machiavelli's ideas, arguing that all societies are governed by a small, organized elite. They focus on how power actually works, not how it should work.
  • The Ruling Class (Mosca): Gaetano Mosca's theory that every society, despite its formal constitution, is always governed by a small, organized minority he called the "political class" or "ruling class." This is due to the inherent organizational advantages of small, cohesive groups over large, disorganized majorities.
  • Circulation of Elites (Pareto): Vilfredo Pareto's dynamic theory that ruling elites inevitably decay and are replaced by new elites in a continuous cycle throughout history. He famously described history as a "graveyard of aristocracies."
  • Foxes (Pareto): One of two elite types identified by Pareto, derived from Machiavelli. Foxes are clever, manipulative, and rely on persuasion, cunning, and coalition-building. They excel in stable conditions but become too soft when firmness is needed.
  • Lions (Pareto): The other elite type identified by Pareto. Lions are powerful, direct, and rely on force, tradition, and authority. They are effective in times of crisis but tend to become rigid and oppressive over time.
  • Iron Law of Oligarchy (Michels): Robert Michels's theory stating that even organizations explicitly committed to democracy and equality will inevitably develop internal hierarchies and concentrate power in a small leadership group. This is due to structural necessities of organization, leaders' expertise, and their incentives to maintain power.

Important Terms

  • Italian Realist School: The collective name for Gaetano Mosca, Vilfredo Pareto, and Robert Michels, who developed elite theory.
  • Elite Theory School: Another name for the Italian Realist School, emphasizing their core argument that all societies are ruled by elites.
  • Political Class/Ruling Class: Gaetano Mosca's term for the small, organized minority that governs every society, irrespective of its constitutional form.
  • Circulation of Elites: Vilfredo Pareto's concept describing the continuous cycle of ruling elites decaying and being replaced by new ones.
  • Foxes: According to Pareto, an elite type characterized by cunning, manipulation, and reliance on persuasion.
  • Lions: According to Pareto, an elite type characterized by force, directness, and reliance on authority.
  • Iron Law of Oligarchy: Robert Michels's principle asserting that "Who says organization, says oligarchy," meaning large organizations will always tend to centralize power in a few hands.
  • Machiavellian Realism: A commitment to studying politics as it actually operates, rather than as it ideally should, acknowledging power dynamics and human nature.

Detailed Notes

Machiavelli's Intellectual Descendants: The Italian Realist School

  • Foundation: Three centuries after Niccolò Machiavelli, Italian thinkers Gaetano Mosca, Vilfredo Pareto, and Robert Michels built upon his core insights.
  • Shared Commitment: Like Machiavelli, they focused on studying politics "as it actually works," not as idealists claim it should.
  • Core Conclusion: Power is consistently held and exercised by a small, organized minority over a larger, disorganized majority.
  • Significance: Their work marked the first systematic, academic development of Machiavelli's ideas, transforming his practical advice for princes into a general theory of political power.

Gaetano Mosca: The Ruling Class

  • Biography: Italian political scientist and lawyer (1858–1941) who taught at the Universities of Turin and Rome. He served in the Italian parliament and Senate, opposing Fascism.
  • Central Argument: In his major work, The Ruling Class (1896, revised 1923), Mosca argued that every society, regardless of its formal structure (democracy, oligarchy, monarchy, republic), is governed by a minority. He termed this group the "political class" or "ruling class."
  • Reason for Minority Rule: Organizational advantage. Small, organized, and coherent groups can coordinate actions and achieve goals more effectively than large, diffuse populations lacking clear leadership or a shared plan. This is an observation of political function, not a moral judgment.
  • Debt to Machiavelli:
    • Insistence on starting with observed political reality.
    • Skepticism of claims that government represents the "general will."
    • Belief that competition for political power is a permanent feature of social life.
  • Distinction from Machiavelli: While Machiavelli focused on individual princes, Mosca developed a theory about the structural conditions that make elite rule inevitable in any society.

Vilfredo Pareto: The Circulation of Elites

  • Biography: Italian economist and sociologist (1848–1923), trained as an engineer, became a professor of economics. Known for the "Pareto principle" (80/20 rule) in economics.
  • Acceptance of Elite Rule: Pareto accepted Mosca's premise that every society is governed by an elite.
  • Dynamic Theory: He added a dynamic element with his theory of the "circulation of elites." History is not static; it's a "graveyard of aristocracies," where ruling elites continuously decay and are replaced by new ones.
  • Machiavelli's Foxes and Lions: Pareto famously applied Machiavelli's distinction between foxes and lions to characterize elite types:
    • Foxes: Clever, manipulative elites who use persuasion, cunning, and coalition-building. They are adept at maintaining power in stable times but can become too soft or accommodating when decisive action is required.
    • Lions: Powerful, direct elites who rely on force, tradition, and authority. They are effective in crises but tend to become rigid and oppressive over time.
  • Cycles of History: Pareto argued that history is a cycle of these elite types replacing each other. When a fox elite becomes too timid, a lion elite (through revolution, coup, or superior organization) displaces it. When a lion elite becomes too rigid, a fox elite finds ways to outmaneuver it.
  • Debt to Machiavelli: Pareto explicitly borrowed and generalized Machiavelli's metaphor for an individual prince into a comprehensive theory of political history.

Robert Michels and the Iron Law of Oligarchy

  • Biography: German-born sociologist and political scientist (1876–1936) who spent much of his career in Italy and Switzerland. Initially a socialist, he became disillusioned by the internal politics of socialist parties.
  • Extension of Elite Theory: Michels extended the elite theory argument to organizations explicitly committed to democracy and equality, such as socialist parties, labor unions, and democratic associations.
  • The Iron Law of Oligarchy: His major work, Political Parties (1911), developed this law: "Who says organization, says oligarchy." Even democratic organizations inevitably develop internal hierarchies and concentrate power in a small leadership group.
  • Reasons for the Law:
    • Structural Necessity: Large organizations require leaders for coordination of complex activities.
    • Expertise: Leaders develop specialized knowledge, skills, and contacts that ordinary members lack.
    • Incentives: Leaders have stronger personal incentives to maintain their positions than members have to challenge them.
    • Organizational Survival: Over time, leaders develop an interest in the organization's survival, which may sometimes conflict with its original stated goals.
  • Machiavellian Lesson: Power always concentrates. The crucial questions are which elite governs, how, and with what accountability.
  • Implications: The Iron Law does not mean democracy is impossible or resistance is futile. Instead, it highlights that the concentration of power must be consciously resisted through institutional design, regular elections, and fostering civic participation.

The Common Thread

Mosca, Pareto, and Michels share three fundamental commitments that connect them directly to Machiavelli:

  1. Political Realism: They prioritize studying political reality—how power actually operates—over political ideals or how it should work.
  2. Concentration of Power: They all conclude that power inevitably concentrates in the hands of organized minorities, regardless of constitutional forms, stated principles, or popular desires.
  3. Permanent Competition: They recognize that the competition for political power is an inherent and permanent feature of social life that cannot be eliminated.

These thinkers transformed Machiavelli's practical advice for acquiring and holding power (from the perspective of the prince) into a scientific study of the structural laws governing the distribution and exercise of political power in any society.

Review Questions

  1. What is the core insight shared by Gaetano Mosca, Vilfredo Pareto, and Robert Michels regarding political power, and how does this directly connect them to Machiavelli's legacy?
  2. Explain Gaetano Mosca's concept of the "political class" or "ruling class." What are his primary reasons for asserting that minority rule is an inevitable feature of all societies?
  3. Describe Vilfredo Pareto's theory of the "circulation of elites." How does he use Machiavelli's distinction between "foxes" and "lions" to illustrate the dynamic nature of elite replacement?
  4. What is Robert Michels's "iron law of oligarchy," and why did his personal experiences with socialist parties lead him to formulate this theory? How does this law challenge the ideals of democratic organizations?
  5. Collectively, how do the ideas of Mosca, Pareto, and Michels transform Machiavelli's original writings from a practical guide for individual princes into a more general, academic theory of political power? What are the implications of their shared conclusions for modern democratic thought?
