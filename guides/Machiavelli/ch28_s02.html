CHAPTER 28, SECTION 2: LOOKING BACK - Study Guide

Overview

This section serves as a comprehensive review of Niccolò Machiavelli's The Prince, examining its structure, core arguments, and lasting influence. It distills Machiavelli's insights into political realism, the role of human agency versus circumstance, the importance of popular support, and effective leadership qualities. The section also critically assesses the limitations and eventual legacy of Machiavelli's work, placing it within the broader history of political thought.

Key Concepts

  • The Arc of The Prince: The book is structured into four main movements: analyzing types of principalities (Ch. I-XI), establishing military foundations (Ch. XII-XIV), exploring the prince's personal qualities (Ch. XV-XXIII), and concluding with an application to Italy and a call for liberation (Ch. XXIV-XXVI).
  • Political Realism: Machiavelli's foundational approach to studying politics "as it actually is," focusing on "the effectual truth of the matter" rather than idealized moral or philosophical prescriptions. This empirical method influenced centuries of political analysis.
  • Virtù and Fortuna: A central tension in Machiavelli's thought, representing the interplay between human skill, agency, and preparedness (virtù) against the unpredictable forces of fate, luck, or circumstance (fortuna). A leader with virtù maximizes their chances but cannot guarantee outcomes.
  • The People as the Prince's Foundation: A consistently emphasized, yet often overlooked, argument in The Prince. Machiavelli stressed that popular support and citizen-soldiers are a prince's most secure resource, offering better protection than nobles or mercenaries and anticipating democratic ideas.
  • The Fox and the Lion: A famous metaphor suggesting that effective political leaders must possess both the cunning and strategic intelligence of a fox to navigate difficult situations and the strength and decisiveness of a lion to act forcefully when necessary.
  • Machiavelli's Enduring Legacy: His most powerful contribution is the tradition of political realism, which insists on understanding politics empirically rather than normatively. This legacy continues to shape political philosophy, international relations theory, and statecraft five centuries later.

Important Terms

  • Congress of Vienna (1814–1815): A historical conference that reshaped Europe after Napoleon, cited as an example of the "balance-of-power politics" that Machiavelli analyzed.
  • Principalities: The various types of states (hereditary, mixed, acquired by force or fortune, etc.) that The Prince analyzes in terms of how they are acquired and maintained.
  • Mercenaries and Auxiliaries: Foreign or hired soldiers; Machiavelli considered them useless or dangerous, advocating instead for national armies composed of citizens.
  • Citizen-soldiers: An army composed of a prince's own citizens, loyal to their state, which Machiavelli argued was the most secure military foundation.
  • Effectual Truth: Machiavelli's term for the actual, practical reality of how politics functions, distinct from theoretical ideals or moral prescriptions.
  • Virtù: In Machiavelli's context, refers to a leader's skill, energy, courage, foresight, and ability to adapt to circumstances; not necessarily moral virtue.
  • Fortuna: The unpredictable force of fortune, chance, or fate that influences political events, often beyond human control.
  • Political Realism: The school of thought, founded by Machiavelli, that analyzes political behavior and phenomena as they are, rather than as they morally ought to be.
  • The Discourses on Livy: Another significant work by Machiavelli, which presents a more favorable view of republican government and free citizens, offering a contrast to the crisis-driven focus of The Prince.

Detailed Notes

The Arc of The Prince

  • The Prince is a short but dense work, structured logically to build its argument for Italian liberation.
  • Chapters I-XI: Types of Principalities:
    • Begins by categorizing different kinds of states (principalities) and examining how they are acquired and maintained.
    • Covers hereditary states (easiest to keep), mixed states (more complex), and states acquired from previously free cities (very difficult to hold without destruction).
    • Also discusses principalities acquired by one's own arms, by fortune, by wickedness, or through civil support. These chapters "establish the map" for the rest of the book.
  • Chapters XII-XIV: Military Foundations:
    • Emphasizes the critical importance of military power as the basis of all political authority.
    • Strongly condemns the use of mercenaries and auxiliaries, deeming them unreliable and dangerous.
    • Argues that a prince's own army, composed of his citizens, is the only truly secure military foundation, essential for genuine sovereignty.
  • Chapters XV-XXIII: The Prince's Personal Qualities:
    • This is the most famous and controversial section, detailing the qualities a prince needs to maintain power.
    • Key arguments include: the necessity of learning "how not to be good," the advantage of being feared over loved (though not hated), the flexibility to break promises when necessary, and the importance of appearances over reality.
    • Stresses that a prince's advisors should have interests aligned with his own.
    • Crucially, these chapters also highlight the people as the prince's most vital political resource. Machiavelli repeatedly warns that being hated or despised by one's subjects poses the greatest danger, even more than external enemies.
  • Chapters XXIV-XXVI: Application and Conclusion:
    • Chapter XXIV critiques Italy's failures, offering a verdict on why Italian rulers had lost their states.
    • Chapter XXV delves into the philosophical question of fortune (fate) versus free will, discussing how much human agency can counteract unpredictable circumstances.
    • Chapter XXVI is the passionate conclusion, revealing the underlying purpose of the entire book: a plea for a new prince to rise and liberate Italy from foreign domination.

The Most Enduring Insights

  • Political Realism: Machiavelli pioneered the study of politics as it is, not as it should be. His focus on the "effectual truth" — the practical realities of power acquisition and loss — marked a significant shift from idealized political theory and established a tradition that continues to this day.
  • Virtù and Fortuna: The dynamic interplay between human skill, boldness, and adaptability (virtù) and uncontrollable external circumstances (fortuna) is a constant feature of political and human life. Machiavelli's honest, somewhat tragic view acknowledges that even the most capable leader cannot guarantee success, but preparedness gives the best chance.
  • The People as the Prince's Foundation: Machiavelli consistently argued that popular support is a prince's most secure asset. He believed that being hated by the people was the greatest danger, and that citizen-soldiers provided the strongest military force. This emphasis on the common people as the ultimate political resource foreshadowed democratic political theory.
  • The Fox and the Lion: This metaphor encapsulates the dual nature of effective leadership. A prince must possess the cunning and intelligence of a fox to outwit adversaries and understand complex situations, combined with the strength and decisive action of a lion to enforce will and overcome obstacles, even when morally difficult.

What Machiavelli Got Wrong

  • Advice Unheeded by the Medici: Machiavelli's specific advice for the Medici family to liberate Italy was not taken, and Italy remained divided for centuries. While Italian unification eventually occurred, it wasn't a direct result of The Prince's blueprint for a heroic new prince.
  • Gendered Language: Machiavelli's problematic use of gendered metaphors, such as describing fortune as a woman who must be "beaten and ill-used," reflects outdated and unacceptable attitudes towards women. This language is not essential to his political point (that boldness often succeeds over caution) and distracts from the argument.
  • Distorted View of His Thought: Focusing solely on The Prince can give an incomplete picture of Machiavelli's political philosophy. In his Discourses on Livy, he championed republican government and the self-governance of free citizens as the strongest and most durable form of political organization. The Prince was intended as a guide for crisis, not a universal ideal state blueprint.

Machiavelli's Enduring Legacy

  • Despite his personal failures and the immediate lack of impact of his advice, Machiavelli profoundly reshaped political thought.
  • His most significant legacy is the tradition of political realism, which advocates for understanding politics as it truly is, rather than as it ideally should be. This empirical approach has influenced political philosophy, international relations theory, and statecraft for five centuries, continuing to shape contemporary discussions.

Review Questions

  1. Describe the four main movements of The Prince. How do these sections collectively build Machiavelli's argument and culminate in his final exhortation?
  2. Explain Machiavelli's concept of "political realism" and how it differs from a normative approach to politics. Provide examples from the text of how he applies this principle.
  3. Why did Machiavelli place such strong emphasis on "the people as the prince's foundation"? How does this insight challenge common perceptions of Machiavelli and anticipate later democratic political theories?
  4. Discuss the significance of the "virtù and fortuna" dynamic in Machiavelli's political philosophy. How does a prince, according to Machiavelli, best navigate the tension between human agency and external circumstances?
  5. What are two key criticisms or "things Machiavelli got wrong" according to the text? How do these criticisms, particularly regarding his Discourses on Livy, provide a more nuanced understanding of his overall political thought?
