Governance and Conquest: A Study of State Structures in Machiavelli’s The Prince

This study guide examines the analysis provided in Chapter IV of The Prince, which explores why some conquered territories are easily maintained while others are prone to constant rebellion. The text focuses on the internal structure of states—specifically the distinction between centralized monarchies and decentralized systems involving hereditary nobility—using the historical examples of Alexander the Great, the Ottoman Empire (the Turk), and the Kingdom of France.

Key Concepts and Core Theory

The Two Methods of Governance

The text identifies two primary ways a principality is governed. The internal structure of these governments dictates the difficulty of both seizing and holding the state.

  1. The Prince and His Servants (Centralized)

    • Structure: A single lord rules with the assistance of ministers who serve by his favor and permission.
    • Authority: The prince is held in the highest consideration; no one else is recognized as a superior. Obedience to others is only as officials of the prince.
    • Loyalty: There is no "natural affection" for the ministers; they are bondmen and slaves of the prince.
    • Examples: The Turk (Ottoman Empire) and the Kingdom of Darius III.

  2. The Prince and the Barons (Decentralized)

    • Structure: A prince rules alongside an ancient body of lords (barons) who hold their status through "antiquity of blood" rather than the prince’s grace.
    • Authority: Barons have their own states, subjects, and hereditary prerogatives that the king cannot easily take away.
    • Loyalty: Subjects recognize the barons as their lords and hold a "natural affection" for them.
    • Example: The Kingdom of France.

The Mechanics of Conquest and Retention

State Type Ease of Seizure Ease of Retention Reason for Difficulty/Ease
Centralized (The Turk/Darius) High Difficulty Great Ease Hard to corrupt ministers or incite internal revolt; once the ruling family is exterminated, no other figures hold credit with the people.
Decentralized (France) Great Ease High Difficulty Easy to enter by gaining the support of malcontent barons; hard to hold because remaining lords lead new rebellions and cannot be satisfied or fully exterminated.

Historical Case Studies

Alexander the Great and the Kingdom of Darius

The text addresses the mystery of why Alexander’s empire did not collapse into rebellion after his sudden death.

  • The Structure of Darius's State: It was similar to that of the Turk—highly centralized.
  • The Conquest: Alexander had to defeat Darius in the field and then kill him to secure the state.
  • The Result: Because the state was centralized, it remained secure after Darius's death. The only difficulties Alexander’s successors faced were those they provoked among themselves due to their own ambitions.

The Romans in Spain, France, and Greece

In contrast to Alexander's experience, the Romans faced frequent rebellions in Western Europe.

  • The Cause of Rebellion: These regions were filled with many small principalities (similar to the French model).
  • The Duration of Insecurity: As long as the "memory" of the old principalities endured, Roman possession remained insecure.
  • Final Security: The Romans only became secure possessors through the "long continuance" of their empire, which eventually caused the memory of the former lords to pass away.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to the text, why is a state like that of "the Turk" difficult to seize?

    • Answer: Because the ministers are all slaves and bondmen of the prince, they are difficult to corrupt. Furthermore, even if they were corrupted, they cannot carry the people with them, as the people do not recognize them as independent lords.

  2. What must a conqueror do to hold a centralized state once the ruler’s army has been routed in the field?

    • Answer: The conqueror must exterminate the family of the prince. Once the ruling bloodline is gone, there is no one left with enough credit among the people to lead a movement.

  3. Why is it easy to enter a kingdom like France?

    • Answer: One can easily enter by winning over a baron of the kingdom, as there are always "malcontents" and those who desire change. These lords can open the way for the invader.

  4. Why is a state governed by barons difficult to keep even after the prince’s family is exterminated?

    • Answer: Because the remaining lords (barons) make themselves the heads of "fresh movements." It is impossible to either satisfy them all or exterminate them all, leading to the loss of the state whenever an opportunity arises.

  5. What factor determines the ease or difficulty of holding a conquered state, according to the text's conclusion?

    • Answer: It is not the "little or abundance of ability in the conqueror," but rather the "want of uniformity in the subject state."

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Role of Legitimacy and Affection: Compare and contrast the sources of authority for a "minister" versus a "baron." How does the "natural affection" of subjects for their local lords create a barrier for an outside conqueror that a centralized bureaucracy does not?
  2. The Persistence of Memory: Analyze the text’s claim regarding the Roman occupation of Spain, France, and Greece. How does the passage of time and the "long continuance" of an empire eventually solve the problems inherent in governing a decentralized territory?
  3. The Paradox of Alexander’s Successors: Discuss the irony presented in the text regarding Alexander the Great's empire. Why did the empire's internal structure make it "secure and at their ease," and what does Machiavelli suggest was the actual source of the "tumults" that eventually occurred?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Antiquity of Blood: The status of nobility derived from long-standing hereditary lineage rather than being granted by a current ruler.
  • Barons: Lords who possess their own subjects and states, holding their dignity through inheritance rather than the prince's favor.
  • Bondmen/Slaves: Terms used to describe the ministers of a centralized prince (like the Turk) to highlight their total dependence on the ruler’s will and lack of independent authority.
  • Darius: Darius III, the last Achaemenid king of Persia, whose empire's centralized structure allowed it to be held by Alexander the Great once Darius was defeated and killed.
  • Malcontents: Dissatisfied members of the nobility who are willing to assist a foreign invader in hopes of changing the current political order.
  • Sanjaks: Administrative divisions within the Ottoman Empire (the Turk) used by the prince to manage his territory through appointed administrators.
  • Uniformity in the Subject State: The degree to which a state is centrally organized under a single authority; high uniformity makes a state harder to enter but easier to keep.
