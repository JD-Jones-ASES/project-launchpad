Study Guide: Principality Through Wickedness

This study guide examines the methods by which a private citizen may ascend to the position of prince through "wicked or nefarious ways." Based on the analysis of historical and contemporary figures, this text explores the distinction between attaining an empire and attaining glory, the strategic application of cruelty, and the necessity of stability in governance.

Key Concepts and Themes

The Two Paths to Power

The text identifies two primary ways a private citizen can become a prince without relying entirely on fortune or genius:

  1. Wickedness: Ascending through treachery, murder, and cruelty.
  2. Civil Favour: Becoming a prince through the support of fellow citizens.

Empire vs. Glory

A central theme is the distinction between achieving political power and achieving historical greatness. While "wicked" methods—such as slaying fellow citizens, deceiving friends, and being without faith or religion—can successfully gain a ruler an empire, they cannot earn that ruler "glory." True virtù (excellence) is not attributed to those who succeed solely through barbarous cruelty and infinite wickedness.

The Strategic Use of Cruelty

The text argues that the long-term success of a wicked ruler depends on whether severities are "properly" or "badly" used:

  • Properly Used Cruelty: These are injuries applied at a single stroke, deemed necessary for one’s security, and not persisted in. They are eventually turned toward the advantage of the subjects.
  • Badly Employed Cruelty: These are injuries that may be few at first but multiply over time rather than decreasing. Rulers who follow this path find it impossible to maintain their position.

Benefit and Injury Timing

To maintain a stable state, a usurper must adhere to a specific timeline for negative and positive actions:

  • Injuries: Should be done all at once so they are "tasted less" and therefore offend less.
  • Benefits: Should be granted "little by little" so that their "flavour" lasts longer and the subjects remain grateful.

Case Studies in Wickedness

The following table summarizes the two primary examples used to illustrate the rise to power through wickedness.

Feature Agathocles of Syracuse (Ancient) Oliverotto da Fermo (Modern)
Origins Son of a potter; rose from a "low and abject position." Orphan; raised by his uncle, Giovanni Fogliani.
Rise to Power Rose through military ranks to become Praetor of Syracuse. Trained under Pagolo and Vitellozzo Vitelli; became a top soldier.
Method of Seizure Assembled the senate and people; had soldiers kill all senators and the rich. Invited his uncle and city chiefs to a banquet; had them slaughtered in a private room.
Outcome Successfully defended Syracuse from Carthaginians; held the princedom without civil commotion. Seized Fermo and became formidable to neighbors; strengthened himself with new ordinances.
End of Reign Lived long and secure; never conspired against by his own citizens. Overreached by Cesare Borgia; strangled one year after his crime.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why does the text claim that Agathocles' success cannot be attributed to fortune?

    • Answer: Because he attained pre-eminence step by step through the military profession, facing thousands of troubles and perils, rather than through the favour of others.

  2. According to the text, what specific actions prevent a ruler from being "celebrated among the most excellent men"?

    • Answer: Slaying fellow citizens, deceiving friends, being without faith, mercy, and religion, and exhibiting "barbarous cruelty and inhumanity."

  3. How did Oliverotto da Fermo justify his return to Fermo with a hundred horsemen?

    • Answer: He claimed he wanted to see his patrimony and show the citizens he had not spent his time in vain, but had laboured to acquire honour.

  4. What is the consequence for a prince who, out of "timidity or evil advice," fails to perform all necessary injuries at once?

    • Answer: He is compelled to "keep the knife in his hand" and can never rely on his subjects, as they cannot attach themselves to him due to continued wrongs.

  5. Why should a prince avoid changing his conduct (becoming either harsh or mild) during "troubled times"?

    • Answer: Harsh measures will be too late, and mild ones will be seen as "forced" from the prince, resulting in no obligation or gratitude from the people.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Paradox of Agathocles: Discuss the tension between Agathocles’ "greatness of mind" and his "infinite wickedness." If a ruler possesses the courage to overcome extreme hardships and the ability to defend their state, why does the text still withhold the title of "genius" or "excellent"?

  2. The Utility of Cruelty: Analyze the argument that cruelty can be "properly used." Is the text suggesting a moral defense of cruelty, or is it strictly a pragmatic observation about political survival? Support your answer with the text's guidelines on the timing of injuries and benefits.

  3. The Failure of Oliverotto: Compare the long-term success of Agathocles with the rapid downfall of Oliverotto da Fermo. Based on the text's principles, was Oliverotto’s failure due to the nature of his wickedness, or his inability to avoid being "overreached" by a more powerful figure like Cesare Borgia?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Agathocles: A Sicilian who rose from the humble origins of a potter's son to become the King of Syracuse through military merit and extreme cruelty.
  • Amilcar the Carthaginian: The commander with whom Agathocles reached an understanding before seizing power in Syracuse.
  • Cesare Borgia: The figure who eventually "overreached" and executed Oliverotto da Fermo at Sinigaglia.
  • Giovanni Fogliani: The uncle and guardian of Oliverotto da Fermo who was murdered by his nephew during a banquet.
  • Glory: The lasting historical prestige and moral standing that the text argues cannot be achieved through wicked methods, even if those methods result in an "empire."
  • Oliverotto da Fermo: A professional soldier who seized the city of Fermo by murdering his uncle and the city's leading citizens.
  • Praetor: The high military and administrative rank Agathocles reached in Syracuse before seizing the princedom.
  • Properly Used Severities: Cruel acts performed "at one blow" for the sake of security and then transitioned into benefits for the subjects.
  • Virtù (implied as "Genius" or "Talent"): The quality of mind and body that allows a ruler to overcome fortune, which the text distinguishes from mere "wickedness."
