Study Guide: Measuring the Strength of Principalities

This study guide examines the core concepts presented in Chapter X of Machiavelli’s The Prince, focusing on the methods of evaluating a state's military and defensive capabilities. It analyzes the distinction between self-sufficient and dependent rulers and provides a framework for understanding the strategic importance of fortification and public morale.

Key Concepts

1. The Metric of Princely Strength

Machiavelli establishes a fundamental binary for assessing the power of a prince:

  • Self-Sufficient Princes: Those who possess enough men or money to assemble a sufficient army and engage any aggressor in open battle.
  • Dependent Princes: Those who cannot face an enemy in the field and are forced to retreat behind city walls for defense.

2. The Strategy of Defensive Fortification

For the dependent prince, the survival of the state rests on two pillars:

  • Physical Preparation: Fortifying the town with ditches, walls, and sufficient artillery.
  • Resource Management: Maintaining public depots with a one-year supply of "eating, drinking, and firing" (food, water, and fuel).

3. The German Model of Independent Cities

Machiavelli highlights the cities of Germany as exemplars of defensive strength. These cities remain "absolutely free" because:

  • They yield to the emperor only when it suits them.
  • Their fortifications make assault appear "tedious and difficult."
  • They provide work for the community in industries that represent the "life and strength of the city."
  • They hold military exercises in high repute and maintain strict ordinances to uphold them.

4. The Psychology of the Siege

A critical component of defense is the management of the citizenry’s morale. Machiavelli argues that a wise prince can maintain the loyalty of his subjects during a siege by:

  • Tempering Emotions: Offering hope that the hardship will be brief while simultaneously instilling fear of the enemy’s cruelty.
  • Managing Dissent: Adroitly handling subjects who become too bold or disruptive.
  • Reciprocal Obligation: Leveraging the "nature of men" to be bound by benefits they confer as much as those they receive. Once a citizen’s property is burned in defense of the prince, the citizen feels the prince is now under obligation to them, strengthening their bond.

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Artillery Large-caliber guns or weapons used in the defense or assault of fortified positions.
Ditches Trenches dug around a city or fortification to serve as a defensive barrier against attackers.
Field, The An open area of land where battles are fought, as opposed to defending from within a city.
Odious Extremely unpleasant or deserving of hatred; Machiavelli warns that a prince must not make himself odious to his people if he wishes to remain secure.
Ordinances Authoritative orders or decrees; in the context of German cities, these are laws used to uphold military exercises and public order.
Principalities Territories or states ruled by a prince.
Provisions Supplies of food, drink, and fuel necessary for sustaining a population during a long-term siege.
Public Depots State-managed storage facilities used to keep essential supplies for the community.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to Machiavelli, what is the primary indicator that a prince can support himself with his own resources?
  2. What is the specific advice Machiavelli gives to princes who are forced to defend themselves "by sheltering behind walls"?
  3. Identify three specific defensive features that make German cities difficult to attack.
  4. How do German cities keep their populations "quiet and without loss to the state" regarding employment?
  5. What two conflicting emotions must a prince manipulate in his subjects during a siege?
  6. Why does Machiavelli believe that the burning of the citizens' property outside the city walls might actually help the prince in the long run?
  7. What is Machiavelli’s observation regarding the "nature of men" and the concept of "benefits"?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Interdependence of Military and Civil Management: Analyze Machiavelli’s argument that a prince’s safety depends as much on his "management of the other concerns of his subjects" as it does on his walls and ditches. How does the relationship between the ruler and the ruled affect the military viability of a state?
  2. The German Model as a Case Study: Discuss why Machiavelli uses the German cities as an ideal example of defensive strength. In what ways do these cities balance autonomy, military preparedness, and economic stability?
  3. The Paradox of Hardship and Loyalty: Explore the psychological strategy Machiavelli proposes for a prince under siege. Critically examine his claim that people become more loyal to a leader after they have suffered personal loss (such as the destruction of their property) in that leader's defense.
  4. Self-Sufficiency vs. Dependency in Statecraft: Compare the strategic options available to a prince who can "join battle" versus one who must "fortify his town." Which position does Machiavelli view as more sustainable, and why is the reputation of the prince a deciding factor in both scenarios?
