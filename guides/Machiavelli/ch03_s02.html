Study Guide: Hereditary Principalities in Machiavelli’s The Prince

This study guide examines the analysis of hereditary states as presented in Chapter II of Niccolò Machiavelli’s The Prince. It focuses on the mechanisms of political stability, the advantages of established lineages, and the psychological relationship between a prince and his subjects.

Key Concepts and Analysis

The Ease of Hereditary Rule

Machiavelli asserts that hereditary principalities—those where the family of the prince has long held power—are significantly easier to maintain than new principalities. The primary reason for this stability is the power of custom. A prince of "average powers" can maintain his position simply by following two rules:

  1. Preservation of Tradition: Not transgressing the customs established by ancestors.
  2. Prudent Adaptability: Dealing with new circumstances with reasonable caution and skill.

Resilience Against External Threats

Hereditary states possess an inherent resilience. Because the governing structure is deeply rooted in the history of the land, it takes an "extraordinary and excessive force" to deprive a prince of his state. Even if a hereditary prince is successfully deposed by a usurper, Machiavelli argues he is likely to regain his state the moment the usurper encounters any misfortune or sinister event.

The Prince-Subject Relationship

The text highlights a psychological advantage inherent to long-standing dynasties:

  • Reduced Necessity to Offend: Unlike a new ruler who must often use force or disruptive changes to establish authority, a hereditary prince has less reason to harm his subjects.
  • Natural Disposition: Because the prince has fewer reasons to "offend," he is more likely to be loved. Subjects are naturally well-disposed toward a family that has ruled them for generations.
  • The Erasure of Change: Long-standing rule erases the "memories and motives" that lead to revolution. In Machiavelli's view, change acts as a catalyst; one change provides the "toothing" (a structural starting point) for the next. In an ancient dynasty, these starting points for rebellion have been smoothed over by time.

Historical Case Study: The Duke of Ferrara

Machiavelli cites the Duke of Ferrara as a primary example of the strength of established rule. The Duke was able to withstand significant military pressure from the Venetians in 1484 and Pope Julius II in 1510. Machiavelli attributes this survival not necessarily to the Duke's individual brilliance, but to the fact that his rule was "long established in his dominions."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to the text, why are hereditary states easier to hold than new ones?

  • Answer: They are accustomed to the prince's family, and the ruler only needs to maintain ancestral customs and respond prudently to new events to stay in power.

2. What level of ability is required for a hereditary prince to keep his state?

  • Answer: A prince of "average powers" is sufficient to maintain the state, provided he does not encounter an extraordinary external force.

3. What happens if a hereditary prince is deposed by a usurper?

  • Answer: The hereditary prince is likely to regain his state as soon as the usurper faces any "sinister" setback.

4. Why is a hereditary prince generally more "loved" by his subjects?

  • Answer: He has less "cause and less necessity to offend" them, leading to a natural disposition of support from the populace.

5. What does Machiavelli mean by the "toothing" for change?

  • Answer: He means that one change or innovation creates the foundation and motivation for further changes. In an old principality, the memory of these changes is lost, making the state more stable.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Power of Political Continuity: Analyze Machiavelli’s argument regarding the role of "long-established custom" in governance. To what extent does he suggest that the stability of a state relies on the habits of the people rather than the genius of the leader?
  2. The Usurper’s Dilemma: Based on the text, compare the position of a hereditary prince with that of a usurper. Why does Machiavelli believe the usurper’s position is inherently fragile when faced with a displaced hereditary ruler?
  3. The Duke of Ferrara Case Study: Evaluate the historical examples provided (the attacks of 1484 and 1510). How do these specific events support Machiavelli’s broader claim that "antiquity and duration of rule" provide a shield against external aggression?

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Hereditary Principality A state ruled by a prince whose family has been established in power for a long period.
Customs of Ancestors The traditional laws, social orders, and practices established by previous generations of a ruling family.
Usurper An individual who takes power by force or without legal right, displacing the established ruler.
Extraordinary Force A significant and unusual external power or event required to unseat a well-established prince.
Toothing The structural "leftovers" or foundations created by a change that allow subsequent changes to take hold; a catalyst for instability.
Offend In this context, to take actions that harm, irritate, or disadvantage the subjects of a state.
Prudence The quality of acting with care and foresight; the ability of a prince to handle circumstances as they arise.
