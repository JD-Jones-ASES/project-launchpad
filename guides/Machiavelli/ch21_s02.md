# A Study Guide to Machiavelli’s Strategies for State Security

This study guide examines the strategies and practicalities of maintaining power as outlined in Chapter XX of Niccolò Machiavelli’s *The Prince*. The text analyzes various methods rulers use to secure their states, including the arming or disarming of subjects, the utility of factions, the management of former enemies, and the construction of fortresses.

---

## Core Themes and Analysis

### The Arming and Disarming of Subjects
Machiavelli argues that the decision to arm or disarm subjects depends entirely on the status of the prince’s rule:
*   **New Princes in New States:** A new prince should never disarm his subjects. Instead, he should arm them. This process converts distrusted individuals into faithful adherents and maintains the loyalty of those already faithful. Disarming subjects is viewed as an offense, signaling cowardice or a lack of trust, which breeds hatred toward the prince. Furthermore, an unarmed prince must rely on mercenaries, who are insufficient against powerful enemies and distrusted subjects.
*   **Acquisition of New Provinces:** When a prince adds a new province to his existing state, he must disarm the inhabitants of the new province, with the exception of those who helped him acquire it. Over time, even those allies should be rendered "soft and effeminate" to ensure all armed force resides with the prince’s own soldiers from his original state.

### The Fallacy of Factions
While historical figures often claimed that internal factions (such as those in Pistoia or Pisa) helped maintain control, Machiavelli rejects this in a modern context.
*   **Weakness of Division:** Factions are only useful in times of peace to manage subjects easily. In times of war, they are ruinous. A divided city will fall quickly because the weaker party will always align with invading forces, leaving the remaining faction unable to resist.
*   **Sign of Weakness:** The reliance on internal disputes to maintain control is a sign of a weak prince. A vigorous principality will not permit such divisions.

### Turning Enemies into Assets
Machiavelli suggests that overcoming opposition is a primary method for a prince to gain renown.
*   **The "Ladder" of Renown:** Fortune often provides a new prince with enemies so that he may overcome them, using their opposition as a "ladder" to reach greater heights of reputation.
*   **Fidelity of Former Enemies:** Princes often find more loyalty in those who were initially suspicious or hostile than in those who were immediately trusted. Former enemies who need the prince's assistance are easily won over because they feel a necessity to "cancel by deeds" the prince's negative impression of them.
*   **The Danger of Discontented Allies:** Rulers should be wary of those who helped them seize power solely because they were discontented with the previous government. Such individuals are difficult to satisfy and may not have a natural affection for the new prince.

### The Utility of Fortresses
The value of fortresses is conditional rather than absolute.
*   **Internal vs. External Threats:** A prince who fears his own people more than foreigners should build fortresses. Conversely, a prince who fears foreigners more than his people should forgo them.
*   **The Best Fortress:** Machiavelli concludes that the best possible fortress is **not to be hated by the people**. If the people hate the prince, no physical fortress will save him, as foreigners will always come to the aid of a rebellious populace.
*   **Historical Precedents:**
    *   **Nicolo Vitelli and Guido Ubaldo:** Both chose to destroy fortresses in their territories to make it harder to lose their states.
    *   **The Countess of Forli:** Fortresses initially helped her survive a popular attack, but they failed her later when the people were allied with a powerful foreigner (Cesare Borgia).

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. According to the text, why is it dangerous for a new prince to disarm his subjects?**
> Disarming subjects offends them by showing distrust, which breeds hatred. It also forces the prince to rely on mercenaries, who are unreliable and insufficient for defense.

**2. Why are factions considered a "weakness" in a prince?**
> Factions are only manageable in peace. In war, they cause a state to fall quickly because the weaker internal party will assist outside enemies.

**3. Why might a former enemy be more faithful to a prince than a longtime supporter?**
> Former enemies often need the prince's support to maintain their own positions and feel a strong need to prove their loyalty through deeds to erase the prince's initial bad impression of them.

**4. Under what specific condition should a prince build a fortress?**
> A prince should build a fortress only if he has more to fear from his own people than from foreign invaders.

**5. What happened to the Countess of Forli's defensive strategy when Cesare Borgia attacked?**
> Her fortresses were of little value because the people, who were her enemy, allied themselves with the foreign attacker (Borgia).

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Paradox of Loyalty:** Compare and contrast the reliability of "discontented allies" who help a prince come to power versus "former enemies" who are reconciled to the prince’s rule. Use Machiavelli's arguments to explain why the latter may be more beneficial to the state’s stability.
2.  **The Psychological Fortress:** Machiavelli argues that "the best possible fortress is—not to be hated by the people." Analyze this statement in the context of state security. Is Machiavelli arguing that popular support is a more effective military deterrent than physical fortifications?
3.  **Fortune and the "Ladder" of Enemies:** Machiavelli suggests that a wise prince might "foster some animosity against himself" to increase his renown. Discuss the risks and rewards of this strategy. How does this align with his view that a prince becomes great by overcoming obstacles?

## Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Adherents** | Loyal followers or supporters of a leader. |
| **Effeminate** | A term used by Machiavelli to describe the softening or weakening of subjects to make them less likely to revolt. |
| **Factions** | Internal groups or parties (such as the Guelph and Ghibelline) that are often in conflict with one another within a city or state. |
| **Fortress** | A military stronghold designed to serve as a "bridle and bit" to control subjects and as a refuge from initial attacks. |
| **Mercenaries** | Hired soldiers who serve for pay rather than loyalty; described by Machiavelli as insufficient for true defense. |
| **New Prince** | A ruler who has recently come to power, as opposed to an hereditary prince who inherits a long-established state. |
| **Renown** | Fame or reputation gained through the overcoming of difficulties and enemies. |
| **Tributary Towns** | Towns or cities that are subject to a larger state and often pay for protection or as a sign of submission. |
