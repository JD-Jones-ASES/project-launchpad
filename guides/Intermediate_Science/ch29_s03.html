Electromagnetic Induction: Study Guide

Overview

This section explores electromagnetic induction, the groundbreaking discovery by Michael Faraday and Joseph Henry that a changing magnetic field can generate an electric voltage and current. This fundamental principle is the basis for technologies like electrical generators, transformers, and wireless chargers. It details Faraday's Law, which governs the magnitude of the induced voltage, and Lenz's Law, which determines its direction based on energy conservation.

Key Concepts

  • Electromagnetic Induction: The phenomenon where a changing magnetic field through a conductor induces a voltage and, if the circuit is closed, an electric current. A steady magnetic field does not induce a voltage.
  • Faraday's Law of Induction: States that the magnitude of the induced voltage in a coil is directly proportional to the rate at which the magnetic field (or magnetic flux) through the coil changes, and also to the number of turns in the coil.
  • Lenz's Law: Dictates that the direction of an induced current is always such that it creates a magnetic field that opposes the change in the original magnetic field that produced it. This is a direct consequence of the conservation of energy.
  • Generators: Devices that convert mechanical energy into electrical energy by continuously rotating a coil of wire within a magnetic field, thereby inducing an alternating voltage and current.
  • Transformers: Devices that use electromagnetic induction to efficiently change the voltage of an alternating current (AC). They consist of two coils around a shared iron core, where a changing current in one coil induces a changing magnetic field, which in turn induces a voltage in the other coil.
  • Power Transmission: The process of efficiently delivering electrical power over long distances. Transformers are crucial here, as step-up transformers increase voltage (and decrease current) for transmission to minimize heat loss ($P_{lost} = I^2R$), while step-down transformers reduce voltage for safe use at destinations.

Important Terms

  • Electromagnetic Induction: The process of producing a voltage and current in a conductor by exposing it to a changing magnetic field.
  • Faraday's Law of Induction: The scientific law stating that the induced voltage in a circuit is proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux through the circuit and the number of turns.
  • Lenz's Law: A principle of electromagnetism stating that the direction of an induced current is such that it opposes the cause producing it (the change in magnetic field).
  • Generator: A machine that converts mechanical energy into electrical energy, typically by rotating a conductor in a magnetic field to induce a current.
  • Alternating Current (AC): An electric current that periodically reverses direction, produced by generators.
  • Transformer: An electrical device that changes the voltage of an alternating current by electromagnetic induction, using primary and secondary coils wound around an iron core.
  • Primary Coil ($N_p$): The coil in a transformer that is connected to the input AC voltage.
  • Secondary Coil ($N_s$): The coil in a transformer where the induced output voltage is produced.
  • Step-up Transformer: A transformer designed to increase voltage, having more turns in the secondary coil than in the primary coil ($N_s > N_p$).
  • Step-down Transformer: A transformer designed to decrease voltage, having fewer turns in the secondary coil than in the primary coil ($N_s < N_p$).
  • Power Loss ($P_{lost} = I^2R$): The energy dissipated as heat in a conductor due to its resistance when current flows through it.

Detailed Notes

The Discovery of Electromagnetic Induction

Before 1831, it was known that electric currents create magnetic fields (Oersted, 1820). The reverse question—can a magnetic field create an electric current?—remained unanswered until Michael Faraday (England) and Joseph Henry (America) independently discovered the principle of electromagnetic induction. Their key finding was that a magnetic field can indeed generate a voltage and current, but only if the magnetic field is changing. A steady magnetic field produces no effect.

Faraday's Law of Induction

Faraday's experiments showed that he could induce a current in a coil of wire by:

  1. Moving a magnet into or out of the coil.
  2. Moving a coil toward or away from a stationary magnet.
  3. Changing the current in a nearby coil, which alters the magnetic field passing through the first coil.

In all these scenarios, the crucial element is change. The magnetic field passing through the coil must be increasing, decreasing, or rotating. A static magnet within a static coil will not induce any voltage.

Faraday's Law of Induction quantifies this phenomenon: The induced voltage in a coil is proportional to the rate at which the magnetic field through the coil changes. The magnitude of the induced voltage is determined by three main factors:

  • Strength of the magnetic field: A stronger magnetic field undergoing change will induce a greater voltage.
  • Number of turns in the coil: A coil with more turns will intercept more of the changing magnetic field, resulting in a higher induced voltage.
  • Speed of change: The faster the magnetic field changes (e.g., moving a magnet more quickly), the greater the induced voltage.

Lenz's Law

While Faraday's Law tells us the magnitude of the induced voltage, Lenz's Law tells us its direction: The direction of an induced current is always such that it opposes the change in magnetic field that produced it.

This law is a fundamental consequence of the conservation of energy. When you push a magnet's north pole into a coil, the induced current creates its own magnetic field that makes the coil's near end act as a north pole, repelling the incoming magnet. You must exert work against this repulsive force. This work is not lost; it is converted into the electrical energy generated in the circuit. If the induced current instead aided the change, the magnet would accelerate into the coil on its own, generating energy from nothing, which would violate the conservation of energy. Lenz's Law ensures that this fundamental principle is upheld.

Generators

A generator is a practical application of Faraday's Law, designed to convert mechanical energy into electrical energy. Its core components are a coil of wire rotating within a magnetic field. As the coil spins, the magnetic field passing through it continuously changes (increasing, decreasing, reversing direction). This continuous change induces a voltage in the coil, which in turn produces an electric current. Because the direction of the induced voltage alternates as the coil rotates, generators typically produce alternating current (AC).

Most electricity used globally comes from generators in power plants. These generators are powered by turbines—large spinning machines driven by steam (from burning fossil fuels or nuclear fission), falling water (hydroelectric power), or wind. The mechanical energy of the spinning turbine is efficiently converted into electrical energy via electromagnetic induction.

Transformers

A transformer is a device that utilizes electromagnetic induction to change the voltage of an alternating current. It consists of two coils—a primary coil and a secondary coil—wound around a shared iron core.

When alternating current (AC) flows through the primary coil, it creates a continuously changing magnetic field within the iron core. This changing magnetic field then extends through the secondary coil, inducing a voltage in it according to Faraday's Law. It is crucial that the current is alternating; direct current (DC) produces a steady magnetic field, which cannot induce a voltage.

The relationship between the voltages and the number of turns in the coils is given by the transformer equation: $$ \frac{V_p}{V_s} = \frac{N_p}{N_s} $$ Where:

  • $V_p$ = primary voltage
  • $V_s$ = secondary voltage
  • $N_p$ = number of turns in the primary coil
  • $N_s$ = number of turns in the secondary coil

Transformers can be:

  • Step-up transformer: If $N_s > N_p$, the secondary coil has more turns than the primary, so $V_s > V_p$. The voltage is increased.
  • Step-down transformer: If $N_s < N_p$, the secondary coil has fewer turns than the primary, so $V_s < V_p$. The voltage is decreased.

Proportional Reasoning and Energy Conservation in Transformers: The transformer equation is a proportion. We can rearrange it to solve for unknown values, for example: $$ V_s = V_p \frac{N_s}{N_p} \quad \text{or} \quad N_s = N_p \frac{V_s}{V_p} $$ Ideally, transformers conserve energy. This means that the power in the primary coil equals the power in the secondary coil ($P_p = P_s$). Since power $P = IV$, we have: $$ V_p I_p = V_s I_s \quad \text{(for an ideal transformer)} $$ This implies a crucial relationship: if a transformer steps up the voltage, it proportionally steps down the current, and vice versa. Power is conserved.

Worked Example: Transformer Calculations Problem: A step-down transformer in a phone charger receives 120 V AC from the wall outlet. The primary coil has 600 turns and the secondary coil has 25 turns. (a) What is the output voltage? (b) If the charger draws 0.5 A from the wall, what current does the secondary coil deliver to the phone (assuming an ideal transformer)?

Solution: (a) Using the transformer equation $V_s = V_p \frac{N_s}{N_p}$: $V_s = 120,V \times \frac{25}{600} = 120,V \times 0.04167 = 5.0,V$

(b) For an ideal transformer, $V_p I_p = V_s I_s$. We need to find $I_s$: $I_s = \frac{V_p I_p}{V_s} = \frac{120,V \times 0.5,A}{5.0,V} = \frac{60,W}{5.0,V} = 12,A$ Check: The voltage decreased by a factor of 24 ($120/5 = 24$), so the current increased by the same factor ($0.5 \times 24 = 12$). Power in = $120 \times 0.5 = 60,W$; power out = $5.0 \times 12 = 60,W$. Energy is conserved.

Power Transmission

Transformers are vital for the efficient transmission of electrical power over long distances. When current flows through wires, some energy is inevitably lost as heat due to the wire's resistance. This power loss ($P_{lost}$) is given by the formula: $$ P_{lost} = I^2 R $$ This equation highlights that power loss is proportional to the square of the current ($I$). Therefore, even a small reduction in current can lead to a significant reduction in energy loss.

To minimize these losses during transmission, power companies employ step-up transformers at power plants. These transformers increase the voltage to hundreds of thousands of volts, which proportionally decreases the current (since power $P=IV$ must remain constant). This dramatically reduces $I^2R$ losses along the transmission lines. Near homes and businesses, step-down transformers then reduce the voltage back to safe and usable levels (e.g., 120 V or 240 V).

Clarification: It's important not to confuse $P = IV$ (total power delivered) with $P = I^2R$ (power lost due to resistance). $P=IV$ describes the power carried by the entire transmission system, while $P=I^2R$ describes only the power wasted as heat in the resistive wires themselves. By increasing voltage, we decrease current for the same total power $P=IV$, which in turn reduces the lost power $P=I^2R$.

Worked Example: Power Transmission Losses Problem: A power plant generates 500 kW of power. The transmission lines have a total resistance of $R = 10,\Omega$. Compare the power lost as heat when the power is transmitted at (a) 10,000 V versus (b) 500,000 V.

Solution: First, find the current ($I = P/V$) in each case.

(a) At 10,000 V: $I = \frac{500,000,W}{10,000,V} = 50,A$ $P_{lost} = I^2 R = (50,A)^2 \times 10,\Omega = 2,500 \times 10 = 25,000,W = 25,kW$ Percentage lost: $\frac{25,kW}{500,kW} = 5%$ of total power.

(b) At 500,000 V: $I = \frac{500,000,W}{500,000,V} = 1,A$ $P_{lost} = I^2 R = (1,A)^2 \times 10,\Omega = 1 \times 10 = 10,W$ Percentage lost: $\frac{10,W}{500,000,W} = 0.002%$ of total power.

Conclusion: Transmitting at 50 times higher voltage (from 10,000 V to 500,000 V) reduces the current by 50 times. This reduces the power loss by a factor of $50^2 = 2,500$ times, demonstrating why high-voltage transmission lines are essential for efficient power delivery.

Review Questions

  1. What is electromagnetic induction, and what is the fundamental condition that must be met for a voltage or current to be induced in a conductor? Provide examples of how this condition can be achieved.
  2. Explain Faraday's Law of Induction. What three factors determine the magnitude of the induced voltage, and how does each factor influence the output?
  3. How does Lenz's Law relate to the conservation of energy? Describe a scenario where an induced current opposes the change in magnetic field, and explain what would happen if it aided the change instead.
  4. Describe the basic components and working principle of an electrical generator. What type of energy conversion does it perform, and what kind of current does it typically produce?
  5. Explain the purpose of transformers in power transmission. Differentiate between step-up and step-down transformers, and explain why both are crucial for efficient and safe electrical power delivery to homes and businesses.
