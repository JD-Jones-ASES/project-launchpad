Mechanisms of Evolution Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the fundamental processes that drive evolutionary change in populations. While natural selection is a powerful force, evolution also occurs through genetic drift, gene flow, mutation, and sexual selection. Understanding these mechanisms requires a grasp of how genetic variation within a population's gene pool changes over time, which scientists track by measuring allele frequencies.

Key Concepts

  • Evolution as Change in Allele Frequencies: Evolution is fundamentally defined as a change in the relative proportions of specific alleles within a population's gene pool across generations, not changes within an individual organism.
  • Gene Pool and Allele Frequency: The gene pool is the total collection of all alleles for all genes in a population. Allele frequency measures how common a particular allele is within this gene pool.
  • Natural Selection Patterns: Natural selection, which favors individuals with advantageous traits, can operate in three main ways:
    • Directional Selection: Favors one extreme phenotype.
    • Stabilizing Selection: Favors intermediate phenotypes, reducing variation.
    • Disruptive Selection: Favors both extreme phenotypes over the intermediate.
  • Genetic Drift: A random, chance-driven change in allele frequencies, most impactful in small populations. It includes the bottleneck effect (reduction in population size due to a disaster) and the founder effect (a small group establishing a new population).
  • Gene Flow: The movement of alleles between populations, typically through migration or dispersal. It tends to make populations more genetically similar and can introduce new variation.
  • Mutation: The ultimate source of all new genetic variation, creating new alleles through random changes in DNA. Most mutations are neutral or harmful, but some can be beneficial.
  • Sexual Selection: A specific form of natural selection where individuals with traits that enhance their success in attracting mates reproduce more successfully, even if those traits reduce survival.
  • Hardy-Weinberg Principle: A theoretical model describing the conditions under which allele frequencies would not change in a population (i.e., no evolution). It serves as a null model to detect when evolution is occurring and to identify the mechanisms responsible.

Important Terms

  • Population: A group of interbreeding organisms of the same species living in the same geographic area.
  • Gene pool: The complete set of all alleles for all genes present in a population. It represents the total genetic diversity available.
  • Allele frequency: The relative proportion of a specific allele compared to all other alleles for that gene in a population. Changes in allele frequency indicate evolution.
  • Directional selection: A pattern of natural selection where individuals at one extreme of a phenotypic range have greater reproductive success.
  • Stabilizing selection: A pattern of natural selection where intermediate phenotypes are favored, reducing genetic variation.
  • Disruptive selection: A pattern of natural selection where individuals at both extremes of a phenotypic range have greater reproductive success than intermediate phenotypes.
  • Genetic drift: Random fluctuations in allele frequencies in a gene pool, especially prominent in small populations, due to chance events rather than natural selection.
  • Bottleneck effect: A type of genetic drift where a significant portion of a population is suddenly wiped out by a random event (e.g., natural disaster), leading to a gene pool in the survivors that may not be representative of the original population.
  • Founder effect: A type of genetic drift that occurs when a small group of individuals colonizes a new habitat, and the new population's gene pool is a random, possibly unrepresentative, subset of the original population's gene pool.
  • Gene flow: The transfer of alleles from one population to another, usually through the migration of individuals or the dispersal of gametes (e.g., pollen).
  • Mutation: A random, permanent change in the DNA sequence. It is the ultimate source of new alleles and genetic variation.
  • Sexual selection: A form of natural selection in which individuals with certain inherited characteristics are more likely than others to obtain mates.
  • Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium: A theoretical state in which a population's allele and genotype frequencies remain constant across generations, implying no evolution is occurring.

Detailed Notes

The Basis of Evolution: Populations and Gene Pools

Evolution operates at the level of populations, not individuals. An individual organism cannot evolve new traits during its lifetime; rather, a population's genetic makeup changes over generations. The gene pool encompasses all the alleles for every gene present within a specific population. To track evolutionary changes, scientists monitor allele frequency, which is the proportion of a particular allele within the gene pool. Evolution, at its core, is defined as a change in these allele frequencies over time.

Mechanisms Driving Evolutionary Change

  1. Natural Selection Revisited Natural selection causes adaptive evolution by favoring individuals whose traits enhance their survival and reproduction in a given environment. This leads to an increase in the frequency of advantageous alleles in the gene pool. Natural selection acts in three main patterns:

    • Directional Selection: Favors individuals at one extreme of the phenotypic range. For instance, if larger beak sizes are more efficient for cracking seeds during a drought, the population's average beak size will increase over time.
    • Stabilizing Selection: Favors intermediate phenotypes and works against extreme variations. A classic example is human birth weight; babies of average weight tend to have higher survival rates than those who are very small or very large.
    • Disruptive Selection: Favors individuals at both extremes of the phenotypic range over intermediate forms. In a finch population, if very large beaks are good for cracking hard nuts and very small beaks are good for picking small seeds, but medium beaks are inefficient for both, then both extremes will be favored.

  2. Genetic Drift Genetic drift refers to random fluctuations in allele frequencies due to chance events, particularly significant in small populations. Unlike natural selection, it does not involve alleles conferring an advantage or disadvantage.

    • Example: In a small beetle population, a random event like a storm might kill a disproportionate number of beetles of a certain color, altering allele frequencies purely by chance.
    • Bottleneck Effect: Occurs when a population undergoes a drastic reduction in size due to a disaster (e.g., fire, flood, disease). The gene pool of the surviving, smaller population may be very different and have much lower genetic diversity than the original population. The northern elephant seal, which recovered from a near-extinction event, exhibits very low genetic diversity today due to a historical bottleneck.
    • Founder Effect: Occurs when a small group of individuals breaks away from a larger population to colonize a new area. The genetic makeup of this new "founder" population may not be representative of the original population's diversity. Rare alleles in the founders can become common in the new, isolated population.

  3. Gene Flow Gene flow is the transfer of alleles between populations, typically through the migration of individuals or the dispersal of gametes (like pollen or seeds).

    • Effects:
      • Introduces new alleles into a population, increasing genetic variation.
      • Can counteract the effects of genetic drift by homogenizing allele frequencies between populations.
      • Reduces genetic differences between populations, preventing them from diverging significantly.
    • If gene flow is restricted (e.g., by geographic barriers), populations can evolve independently, potentially leading to speciation.

  4. Mutation Mutations are random changes in an organism's DNA sequence and are the ultimate source of all new alleles and genetic variation within a population.

    • While most mutations are neutral (no effect) or harmful, some can be beneficial, providing new raw material for natural selection to act upon. Without mutation, evolution as we know it would cease, as there would be no new genetic diversity.

  5. Sexual Selection Sexual selection is a specialized form of natural selection where individuals with certain traits are more successful at attracting mates, thus increasing their reproductive output. These traits might not necessarily enhance survival and can even be detrimental to it.

    • Example: A peacock's elaborate tail makes it more visible to predators, but females prefer males with larger, more colorful tails, leading to the evolution of this seemingly disadvantageous trait due to increased mating success.
    • Other examples include bright bird plumage, deer antlers used in male-male combat, and complex courtship displays.

The Hardy-Weinberg Principle: A Null Model for Evolution

The Hardy-Weinberg principle, developed by G. H. Hardy and Wilhelm Weinberg, describes a hypothetical scenario where a population's allele frequencies would not change from generation to generation – a state known as Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium.

  • Conditions for Equilibrium (No Evolution):
    1. No natural selection (all genotypes have equal fitness).
    2. No genetic drift (the population is infinitely large).
    3. No gene flow (no immigration or emigration).
    4. No mutation (no new alleles are created).
    5. Random mating (no sexual selection or mate preference).
  • Utility: No real-world population perfectly meets all five conditions, meaning all natural populations are always evolving. Therefore, the Hardy-Weinberg principle serves as a crucial null model or baseline. If scientists observe changes in allele frequencies in a population, they know evolution is occurring and can then investigate which of the evolutionary mechanisms (natural selection, genetic drift, gene flow, mutation, or non-random mating) is responsible for the deviation from equilibrium. This principle is analogous to Newton's first law of motion, describing what happens when no forces act on a system, helping to identify when forces are at play.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental difference between how evolution affects an individual versus how it affects a population. Provide an example.
  2. Describe the three main patterns of natural selection (directional, stabilizing, disruptive). For each, give an example different from those in the text.
  3. Compare and contrast the bottleneck effect and the founder effect. How are they similar, and how do they differ in their origin and impact on genetic diversity?
  4. Discuss the critical role of mutation and gene flow in maintaining and introducing genetic variation within and between populations. What would be the long-term consequences if both were absent?
  5. Despite its unrealistic conditions, how is the Hardy-Weinberg principle a useful tool for evolutionary biologists? Explain its purpose as a "null model."
