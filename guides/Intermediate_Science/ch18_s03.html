Study Guide: Chapter 18, Section 3 - Balancing Equations

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental principle of the law of conservation of mass in chemistry, which dictates that atoms are rearranged but not created or destroyed in a chemical reaction. Consequently, chemical equations must be "balanced," meaning the number of atoms for each element is identical on both sides of the reaction arrow. It provides a systematic method for balancing equations using coefficients and clarifies the crucial difference between coefficients and subscripts.

Key Concepts

  • Law of Conservation of Mass: Established by Antoine Lavoisier, this law states that in any chemical reaction, atoms are neither created nor destroyed. They are simply rearranged into new substances.
  • Balanced Chemical Equations: A chemical equation is balanced when the number of atoms of each element is the same on both the reactant (left) and product (right) sides of the reaction arrow, reflecting the law of conservation of mass.
  • Coefficients vs. Subscripts:
    • Subscripts (small numbers within a formula, e.g., H₂O) indicate the number of atoms of each element within one molecule or formula unit. They define the identity of the substance and must never be changed when balancing an equation.
    • Coefficients (full-sized numbers before a formula, e.g., 2H₂O) indicate the number of molecules or formula units of that substance involved in the reaction. Adjusting coefficients is the only correct way to balance an equation.
  • Balancing by Inspection: This is a systematic method for balancing equations by trial and error, involving adjusting coefficients. A reliable approach includes starting with the most complex molecule, balancing one element at a time, saving single-element substances for last, and checking the final count.
  • Stoichiometric Ratios: The coefficients in a balanced chemical equation represent the fixed molar (and molecular) ratios in which reactants are consumed and products are formed. These ratios are crucial for understanding quantitative relationships in chemistry.

Important Terms

  • Law of Conservation of Mass: The scientific principle stating that mass in an isolated system is neither created nor destroyed by chemical reactions or physical transformations.
  • Balanced Chemical Equation: A chemical equation in which the number of atoms for each element is the same on both the reactant and product sides.
  • Coefficient: A number placed in front of a chemical formula in an equation to indicate how many molecules or formula units of that substance are involved in the reaction.
  • Subscript: A small number written after a chemical symbol in a formula, indicating the number of atoms of that element in one molecule or formula unit.
  • Balancing by Inspection: A method for balancing chemical equations by systematically adjusting coefficients through trial and error until the number of atoms of each element is equal on both sides.
  • Stoichiometric Ratio: The ratio of the coefficients of reactants and products in a balanced chemical equation, representing the fixed proportions in which substances react and are produced.

Detailed Notes

The Law of Conservation of Mass

The foundation of balancing chemical equations rests on the Law of Conservation of Mass, first established by Antoine Lavoisier in 1789. This law is fundamental because it states that atoms are not created or destroyed during a chemical reaction. Instead, they are merely rearranged to form new substances. This means that every single atom present at the start of a reaction (as a reactant) must be accounted for and present at the end of the reaction (as part of a product).

Why Equations Must Be Balanced

Because atoms are conserved, a chemical equation must accurately reflect this conservation. A balanced chemical equation shows the same number of atoms of each element on both sides of the reaction arrow, ensuring that the mass is conserved. An unbalanced equation is chemically incorrect because it implies that atoms are either appearing or disappearing.

Understanding Coefficients and Subscripts

A common source of confusion for students is distinguishing between coefficients and subscripts, but their difference is critical for correct balancing:

  • Subscripts: These are the small numbers written within a chemical formula (e.g., the '2' in H₂O or O₂).

    • Function: Subscripts tell you the exact number of atoms of each element present in one molecule or one formula unit of a compound. For example, H₂O means one molecule of water contains two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom.
    • Immutability: You must never change a subscript to balance an equation. Changing a subscript alters the chemical identity of the substance itself. H₂O (water) is a completely different substance from H₂O₂ (hydrogen peroxide), even though both contain hydrogen and oxygen. Modifying a subscript would mean you are no longer describing the original reaction.

  • Coefficients: These are the full-sized numbers placed in front of a chemical formula (e.g., the '2' in 2H₂O).

    • Function: Coefficients indicate how many molecules or formula units of that substance are participating in the reaction. For example, 2H₂O means two molecules of water.
    • Adjustability: Adjusting coefficients is the only correct method for balancing a chemical equation. By changing the number of whole molecules involved, you can equalize the atom count on both sides without changing the nature of the substances themselves.

The Process of Balancing by Inspection

"Balancing by inspection" is a systematic approach to balancing equations by adjusting coefficients. Follow these steps for a reliable method:

  1. Write the Unbalanced Equation: Ensure all chemical formulas are correctly written for reactants and products.
  2. Count Atoms: Tally the number of atoms of each element on both the reactant and product sides of the equation.
  3. Balance Complex Molecules First: Identify the most complex molecule (the one with the most different elements or atoms) and begin balancing elements within it. Balance one element at a time.
  4. Save Single-Element Substances for Last: If an element appears by itself as a reactant or product (e.g., O₂, Fe), balance it last. Its coefficient can be adjusted without affecting the counts of other elements you've already balanced.
  5. Check Your Work: After adjusting all coefficients, recount every atom of every element on both sides to ensure they are equal.
  6. Simplify Coefficients: If all coefficients can be divided by a common whole number to yield a smaller whole-number ratio, do so. Coefficients should always be the smallest possible whole numbers.

Worked Example: Balancing Propane Combustion

Let's apply the "balancing by inspection" method to the combustion of propane:

C₃H₈(g) + O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + H₂O(g) (unbalanced)

  1. Count Atoms (Unbalanced):

    • Reactants: C = 3, H = 8, O = 2
    • Products: C = 1, H = 2, O = 3 (1 from CO₂ + 2 from H₂O)

  2. Balance Carbon (C):

    • There are 3 C atoms on the left (in C₃H₈).
    • There is 1 C atom on the right (in CO₂).
    • Place a coefficient of 3 in front of CO₂: C₃H₈ + O₂ → 3CO₂ + H₂O

  3. Balance Hydrogen (H):

    • There are 8 H atoms on the left (in C₃H₈).
    • There are 2 H atoms on the right (in H₂O).
    • To get 8 H on the right, we need 4 molecules of H₂O (4 × 2 = 8).
    • Place a coefficient of 4 in front of H₂O: C₃H₈ + O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O

  4. Balance Oxygen (O) (Last, as O₂ is a single-element substance):

    • Count O atoms on the right (products):
      • From 3CO₂: 3 molecules × 2 O/molecule = 6 O atoms
      • From 4H₂O: 4 molecules × 1 O/molecule = 4 O atoms
      • Total O on product side = 6 + 4 = 10 O atoms.
    • Count O atoms on the left (reactants):
      • From O₂: Each molecule has 2 O atoms.
      • To get 10 O atoms, we need 10/2 = 5 molecules of O₂.
    • Place a coefficient of 5 in front of O₂: C₃H₈(g) + 5O₂(g) → 3CO₂(g) + 4H₂O(g)

  5. Check All Atoms:

    • Reactants: C = 3, H = 8, O = 10 (5 × 2)
    • Products: C = 3, H = 8 (4 × 2), O = 10 (3 × 2 + 4 × 1)
    • All atoms are balanced!

The final balanced equation is: C₃H₈(g) + 5O₂(g) → 3CO₂(g) + 4H₂O(g)

Stoichiometric Ratios

The coefficients in a balanced equation are more than just numbers to make the atoms match; they represent the stoichiometric ratios in which substances react and form. These ratios are constant and fundamental to chemical reactions.

For the balanced propane combustion equation: 1 C₃H₈(g) + 5 O₂(g) → 3 CO₂(g) + 4 H₂O(g)

This tells us:

  • For every 1 molecule of propane (C₃H₈) that reacts, 5 molecules of oxygen (O₂) are consumed.
  • The reaction produces 3 molecules of carbon dioxide (CO₂) and 4 molecules of water (H₂O) for every 1 molecule of propane.
  • These ratios are proportional. If you double the amount of propane, you would need double the oxygen and would produce double the carbon dioxide and water.

In future chemistry studies, these stoichiometric ratios will be extended to moles, allowing for calculations of exact masses of reactants and products. For now, understand that coefficients indicate the fixed proportions in which substances combine and are produced.

A Common Mistake: Changing Subscripts

As discussed, changing subscripts is a fundamental error when balancing. If a student attempts to balance H₂ + O₂ → H₂O by writing H₂ + O₂ → H₂O₂, they might mistakenly think the atoms are balanced (2 H, 2 O on both sides). However, H₂O (water) and H₂O₂ (hydrogen peroxide) are entirely different chemical substances. Changing the subscript changes the product itself. The correct approach is to balance the original substances using coefficients: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the Law of Conservation of Mass and how it directly applies to the necessity of balancing chemical equations.
  2. Differentiate clearly between a coefficient and a subscript in a chemical equation. Why is it acceptable to change a coefficient but never a subscript when balancing?
  3. Outline the systematic steps involved in balancing a chemical equation by inspection. Why is it often helpful to balance single-element substances (like O₂) last?
  4. Consider the balanced equation for the formation of ammonia: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) → 2NH₃(g). Describe the stoichiometric ratios involved in this reaction. What does this tell you about the relative amounts of reactants consumed and products formed?
  5. A student balances the reaction between hydrogen and oxygen to form water by writing 2H + O → H₂O. What fundamental error has the student made, and what would be the correct balanced equation for this reaction?
