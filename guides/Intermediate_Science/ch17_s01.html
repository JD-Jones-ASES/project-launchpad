Chapter 17, Section 1: Ocean Structure Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the dramatic and diverse landscape of the ocean floor, revealing how tectonic forces sculpt features comparable to those on land. It also delves into the fundamental characteristics of ocean water, including its salinity and the distinct temperature layers that define its vertical structure, explaining how these properties vary across different ocean regions.

Key Concepts

  • Dynamic Ocean Floor: The ocean floor is not flat but features a complex topography of plains, mountains, and deep trenches, all shaped by ongoing plate tectonic processes.
  • Major Seafloor Features: Key structures include the continental shelf, continental slope, vast abyssal plains, mid-ocean ridges (underwater mountain ranges formed at divergent plate boundaries), deep ocean trenches (formed at convergent plate boundaries), and seamounts (underwater volcanoes).
  • Ocean Salinity: Ocean water contains dissolved salts, primarily sodium chloride. Salinity, the total amount of dissolved salts, averages 35 parts per thousand (ppt) but varies globally due to factors like evaporation, precipitation, river input, and the freezing/melting of ice.
  • Temperature Stratification: The ocean typically has three distinct temperature layers: a warm surface layer, a thermocline (a zone of rapid temperature decrease with depth), and a cold, stable deep zone. The presence and strength of the thermocline vary significantly with latitude.

Important Terms

  • Continental shelf: The gently sloping, shallow underwater extension of a continent, extending from the shoreline to the shelf break.
  • Abyssal plain: The vast, flat region of the deep ocean floor, typically 3000–6000m below the surface, covered by thick sediment.
  • Salinity: The total amount of dissolved salts in water, typically expressed in parts per thousand (ppt) or grams of salt per kilogram of water.
  • Thermocline: The layer of ocean water in which temperature decreases rapidly with increasing depth, separating warm surface water from cold deep water.

Detailed Notes

The Diverse Landscape of the Ocean Floor

If the oceans were drained, we would discover an underwater landscape as varied and dramatic as Earth's land surface, featuring vast plains, towering mountain ranges, and incredibly deep trenches. This complex topography is a direct result of the same tectonic forces that shape continents, as studied in Unit 7.

Ocean Basins and Seafloor Features

The global ocean is divided into five major basins:

  • Pacific Ocean: The largest and deepest basin.
  • Atlantic Ocean
  • Indian Ocean
  • Southern (Antarctic) Ocean
  • Arctic Ocean

These basins are not simple bowls but contain distinct features:

Continental Margins

  • Continental shelf:
    • A gently sloping, shallow extension of the continent under water.
    • Relatively shallow, up to about 200m deep.
    • Extends from the shoreline to the shelf break, where the slope dramatically steepens.
    • Biologically the most productive part of the ocean due to sunlight reaching the bottom, supporting photosynthesis.
  • Continental slope:
    • Beyond the shelf, this plunges steeply downward toward the deep ocean floor.

Deep Ocean Floor Features

  • Abyssal plain:
    • Vast, flat regions of the deep ocean floor.
    • Typically 3000–6000m below the surface.
    • Among the flattest features on Earth, covered by a thick layer of fine sediment.
  • Mid-ocean ridges:
    • Underwater mountain chains.
    • Formed at divergent plate boundaries, where new oceanic crust is created by volcanic activity.
    • Example: The Mid-Atlantic Ridge is over 16000km long, making it the longest mountain range on Earth.
  • Ocean trenches:
    • Narrow, extremely deep valleys.
    • Formed at convergent plate boundaries where one tectonic plate subducts beneath another.
    • Example: The Mariana Trench in the Pacific Ocean is nearly 11000m deep, the deepest point in the ocean.
  • Seamounts:
    • Underwater volcanic mountains that rise from the ocean floor.
    • Do not reach the surface.
    • If a seamount breaks the surface, it forms a volcanic island.

Plate Tectonics Connection: The formation of mid-ocean ridges at divergent boundaries and ocean trenches at convergent boundaries highlights how seafloor features are direct results of plate tectonics, mirroring the forces that build mountains on land.

Ocean Water: Salinity

Ocean water is a complex solution, not just pure H2O. It contains dissolved salts, gases, and other substances.

  • Most abundant dissolved substance: Sodium chloride (NaCl), common table salt.
  • Salinity: The total amount of dissolved salts in water.
    • Typically expressed in parts per thousand (ppt) or grams of salt per kilogram of water.
    • Average ocean salinity is about 35 ppt (3.5%).

Factors Affecting Salinity

Salinity varies from place to place due to several factors:

  • Evaporation: Removes pure water but leaves salts behind, thus increasing salinity.
    • Example: The Red Sea has salinity above 40 ppt due to intense evaporation and limited freshwater input.
  • Precipitation and River Input: Adds freshwater, thereby decreasing salinity.
    • Example: Near the mouth of the Amazon River, surface salinity is well below average.
  • Melting and Freezing of Ice:
    • Melting ice adds freshwater, decreasing salinity.
    • When seawater freezes, salts are largely excluded from the ice crystal structure, increasing the salinity of the remaining unfrozen water.

Temperature Layers and the Thermocline

The ocean's temperature is not uniform with depth; it varies in a characteristic pattern, forming distinct layers:

  1. Surface layer (0–200m):
    • Warmed by sunlight and mixed by wind and waves.
    • Temperature varies with latitude and season but is relatively uniform within this layer due to mixing.
  2. Thermocline (200–1000m):
    • A critical zone where temperature rapidly decreases with increasing depth.
    • Acts as a significant barrier, separating the warm surface water from the cold deep water below.
  3. Deep zone (below 1000m):
    • Cold, dark, and remarkably uniform in temperature.
    • Typically ranges from 1–4°C, largely regardless of geographical location.

The thermocline is most pronounced (strongest) in tropical and temperate oceans, where the surface water is significantly warmer than the deep water. In polar regions, where surface water is already very cold, there is little temperature difference with depth, making the thermocline weak or entirely absent.

Think Deeper Answer: If a submarine descends from the surface to 2000m in the tropical Pacific, the water temperature would remain relatively stable (and warm, e.g., 25-28°C) through the surface layer (0-200m). The most dramatic change would occur in the thermocline (200-1000m), where the temperature would rapidly decrease (e.g., to 4-5°C). Below 1000m in the deep zone, the temperature would continue to decrease slowly to about 1-2°C and then remain nearly constant.

Review Questions

  1. Describe three major features found on the deep ocean floor (beyond the continental shelf and slope) and explain how plate tectonics contributes to their formation.
  2. Imagine you are comparing ocean water samples from the Red Sea, the mouth of the Amazon River, and the open Pacific Ocean. How would their average salinities likely compare, and what factors explain these differences?
  3. Explain the concept of the thermocline. In which oceanic regions would you expect to find a strong thermocline, and where would it likely be weak or absent? Why?
  4. Why is the continental shelf considered the most biologically productive part of the ocean, despite being a relatively small area compared to the vast abyssal plains?
  5. If Mount Everest (approximately 8,848m tall) were placed in the Mariana Trench (approximately 11,000m deep), what does this comparison reveal about the scale of ocean trenches? Relate this to the tectonic processes involved in their formation.
