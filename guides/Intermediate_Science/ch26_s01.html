Chapter 26, Section 1: Ecosystem Dynamics

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concepts of ecosystem structure, exploring how living organisms interact with each other and their nonliving environment. It details the flow of energy through trophic levels, explaining the significant energy loss at each step, and highlights how matter (nutrients) is continuously cycled within an ecosystem. Key processes like photosynthesis, decomposition, and specific nutrient cycles (carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus) are examined, alongside the critical roles of producers, consumers, and decomposers.

Key Concepts

  • Ecosystem Structure: An ecosystem comprises a community of living organisms and their abiotic (nonliving) environment. A community includes all populations of different species in an area, with each species occupying a specific habitat and playing a unique niche.
  • Energy Flow: Energy primarily enters ecosystems as sunlight, captured by producers (autotrophs) through photosynthesis. This energy then moves through different trophic levels as organisms consume one another.
  • The 10% Rule: On average, only about 10% of the energy from one trophic level is transferred to the next. The remaining 90% is used for the organism's life processes (respiration, growth, movement) or lost as heat, leading to energy pyramids and explaining why top predators are rare.
  • Food Webs: Represent the complex, interconnected feeding relationships within an ecosystem, showing that most organisms eat and are eaten by multiple species, unlike simplified food chains. Keystone species have a disproportionately large impact on food web stability.
  • Productivity and Biomass: Gross Primary Productivity (GPP) is the total energy captured by producers. Net Primary Productivity (NPP) is the energy remaining after producers use some for their own respiration (NPP = GPP - Respiration), representing the energy available to consumers. Biomass also decreases at higher trophic levels, mirroring the energy pyramid.
  • Nutrient Cycling: Unlike energy, matter (nutrients) is continuously recycled through biogeochemical cycles. Important cycles include:
    • Carbon Cycle: Movement of carbon between atmosphere, organisms, oceans, and Earth's crust (e.g., photosynthesis, respiration, decomposition, combustion).
    • Nitrogen Cycle: Conversion of atmospheric nitrogen (N2) into usable forms by nitrogen fixation (bacteria), followed by nitrification, assimilation, ammonification, and denitrification.
    • Phosphorus Cycle: Movement of phosphorus through rocks, soil, water, and organisms, notably lacking a significant atmospheric phase. Phosphorus is often a limiting nutrient.
  • Energy Flows, Matter Cycles: A fundamental ecological principle stating that energy moves one-way through an ecosystem and dissipates as heat, requiring continuous input from the sun, while matter is conserved and reused repeatedly.

Important Terms

  • Ecosystem: A community of living organisms interacting with their nonliving environment (soil, water, air, sunlight, temperature).
  • Community: All the populations of different species living and interacting in the same area at the same time.
  • Population: All the individuals of one species living in a defined area at a given time.
  • Habitat: The physical environment in which a species lives, including its climate, soil type, water availability, and shelter.
  • Niche: The role a species plays in its ecosystem, including what it eats, what eats it, when it's active, how it reproduces, and how it interacts with other species and its physical environment.
  • Producers (Autotrophs): Organisms, such as plants, algae, and cyanobacteria, that convert light energy into chemical energy through photosynthesis.
  • Trophic level: A step in the transfer of energy through an ecosystem, defined by an organism's feeding relationship.
  • Primary consumers (Herbivores): Organisms that feed directly on producers, forming the second trophic level.
  • Secondary consumers: Carnivores or omnivores that feed on primary consumers, forming the third trophic level.
  • Tertiary consumers: Predators that feed on secondary consumers, forming the fourth trophic level.
  • Decomposers: Organisms, such as bacteria and fungi, that break down dead organic matter and waste, returning nutrients to the soil, water, and atmosphere.
  • 10% rule: The generalization that roughly 10% of the energy at one trophic level is passed on to the next trophic level; the remaining 90% is used for life processes or lost as heat.
  • Food chain: A linear sequence showing how energy passes from one organism to the next through feeding relationships.
  • Food web: A network of interconnected food chains that shows the complex feeding relationships within an ecosystem.
  • Keystone species: A species whose impact on its ecosystem is disproportionately large relative to its abundance; its removal causes significant changes in community structure.
  • Biomass: The total mass of living organic matter in a given area or trophic level.
  • Gross primary productivity (GPP): The total amount of energy or biomass produced by all the producers in an ecosystem through photosynthesis over a given time period.
  • Net primary productivity (NPP): The energy or biomass remaining after producers have used some of the gross primary productivity for their own cellular respiration; NPP = GPP - respiration.
  • Carbon cycle: The biogeochemical cycle through which carbon moves between the atmosphere, living organisms, the ocean, and Earth's crust.
  • Nitrogen cycle: The biogeochemical cycle that describes how nitrogen is converted into various chemical forms as it circulates through the atmosphere, terrestrial, and marine ecosystems.
  • Nitrogen fixation: The process by which certain bacteria convert atmospheric N2 into ammonia (NH3) or ammonium (NH4^+), making nitrogen available to plants.
  • Nitrification: Soil bacteria convert ammonium (NH4^+) to nitrites (NO2^-) and then to nitrates (NO3^-).
  • Assimilation: Plants incorporate nitrates into amino acids and nucleic acids; animals obtain nitrogen by eating plants or other animals.
  • Ammonification: Decomposers break down dead organisms and waste, releasing ammonium back into the soil.
  • Denitrification: Certain bacteria convert nitrates back into N2, returning nitrogen gas to the atmosphere.
  • Phosphorus cycle: The biogeochemical cycle in which phosphorus moves between rocks, soil, water, and living organisms; it does not pass through the atmosphere in significant amounts.
  • Limiting nutrient: A nutrient whose scarcity limits the growth of producers in an ecosystem.

Detailed Notes

Ecosystems, Communities, and Habitats

  • An ecosystem is a complex system involving the interaction of all living (biotic) organisms and their nonliving (abiotic) environment (e.g., soil, water, air, sunlight, temperature).
  • Within an ecosystem, a community encompasses all the different species' populations interacting in the same area.
  • A population consists of all individuals of a single species in a defined area.
  • Each population lives in a habitat, its physical environment.
  • A species' niche is its complete role within the ecosystem, including what it eats, when it's active, how it reproduces, and all its interactions.
  • Abiotic components include chemical substances like dissolved O2, CO2, nitrates, and phosphates, which are crucial for nutrient cycles.

Energy Flow Through Trophic Levels

  • Energy Input: Most ecosystems receive energy from sunlight.
  • Producers (Autotrophs): Organisms like plants and algae capture solar energy through photosynthesis, converting CO2 and H2O into glucose (chemical energy) and O2. They form the first trophic level.
    • Equation: $6CO_2 + 6H_2O \xrightarrow{light} C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2$
  • Consumers:
    • Primary consumers (Herbivores): Eat producers (second trophic level).
    • Secondary consumers: Eat primary consumers (third trophic level).
    • Tertiary consumers: Eat secondary consumers (fourth trophic level).
    • Apex predators: At the top of many food chains, with no natural predators.
  • Decomposers: Bacteria and fungi break down dead organic matter and waste from all trophic levels, recycling essential inorganic nutrients back into the ecosystem for producers to reuse. Without them, nutrient cycles would halt.

The 10% Rule and Energy Pyramids

  • Energy Loss: Organisms use most of the energy they consume for their own life processes (cellular respiration, movement, growth, reproduction), releasing heat into the environment. This energy is lost and cannot be recaptured.
  • The 10% Rule: Only about 10% of the energy from one trophic level is transferred to the next.
  • Implications: This rule explains why there are fewer organisms and less biomass at higher trophic levels. For example, a vast amount of plant energy is needed to support a small population of apex predators.
    • Example: If producers capture 200 units of energy, primary consumers get 20 units, secondary consumers get 2 units, and tertiary consumers get only 0.2 units.

Food Webs

  • Food Chains vs. Food Webs: A food chain is a linear representation of energy flow. A food web is a more realistic, complex network of interconnected food chains, showing multiple feeding relationships.
  • Keystone Species: These species have a disproportionately large impact on their ecosystem. Their removal can cause significant ecosystem changes, such as the collapse of kelp forests when sea otter populations declined.

Biomass and Productivity

  • Biomass Pyramid: Similar to energy, the total biomass (mass of living organic matter) decreases at higher trophic levels.
  • Gross Primary Productivity (GPP): The total energy or biomass producers create through photosynthesis.
  • Net Primary Productivity (NPP): The energy or biomass remaining after producers use some of the GPP for their own cellular respiration. NPP is the energy available to consumers:
    • $NPP = GPP - Respiration$
  • Global Productivity: Tropical rainforests and coral reefs have very high NPP, while deserts and open oceans have low NPP per unit area.

Nutrient Cycling

  • Matter is Recycled: Unlike energy, matter (atoms, chemical elements) is conserved and cycled repeatedly through ecosystems.
  • The Carbon Cycle:
    • Photosynthesis: Removes CO2 from atmosphere, converts it to organic molecules.
    • Respiration: All organisms release CO2 back to atmosphere.
    • Decomposition: Releases carbon from dead organisms into soil and atmosphere.
    • Combustion: Burning fossil fuels and biomass releases CO2.
    • Ocean Absorption: Oceans absorb atmospheric CO2; marine organisms use it for shells.
    • Geological Processes: Carbon stored in rock (limestone, fossil fuels) over long periods; volcanism returns some to atmosphere.
  • The Nitrogen Cycle: Nitrogen (N2) is abundant in the atmosphere but unusable by most organisms due to its strong triple bond.
    • Nitrogen Fixation: Bacteria (e.g., in legume root nodules) convert N2 into ammonia (NH3) or ammonium (NH4^+), making it usable. Lightning also fixes some nitrogen.
    • Nitrification: Soil bacteria convert ammonium to nitrites (NO2^-) and then to nitrates (NO3^-), which plants absorb.
    • Assimilation: Plants incorporate nitrates into proteins and nucleic acids; animals get nitrogen by eating plants/animals.
    • Ammonification: Decomposers release ammonium from dead organisms and waste.
    • Denitrification: Bacteria convert nitrates back to N2 gas, returning it to the atmosphere.
  • The Phosphorus Cycle:
    • No Atmospheric Phase: Unlike carbon and nitrogen, phosphorus does not have a significant gaseous phase in its cycle.
    • Weathering: Phosphate-bearing rocks release phosphorus into soil and water.
    • Uptake: Plants absorb dissolved phosphorus from soil/water.
    • Food Webs: Phosphorus moves through consumers.
    • Decomposition: Returns phosphorus to soil and water.
    • Geological Deposition: Over long timescales, phosphorus is deposited in marine sediments and eventually uplifted into new rock.
    • Importance: Essential for DNA, RNA, ATP, and cell membranes.
    • Limiting Nutrient: Phosphorus is often a limiting nutrient in freshwater ecosystems, meaning its scarcity restricts producer growth.

Energy Flows, Matter Cycles

  • Key Distinction: Energy flows in one direction through an ecosystem (from sun to producers to consumers, dissipating as heat). Matter cycles (atoms are reused continually).
  • Laws of Thermodynamics/Conservation of Mass: This principle aligns with these fundamental scientific laws. Energy degrades at each transfer, requiring continuous input, while matter is neither created nor destroyed, only transformed and relocated.

Review Questions

  1. Describe how the concepts of ecosystem, community, population, habitat, and niche are interconnected, providing a hierarchical view of biological organization.
  2. Explain the "10% rule" of energy transfer in an ecosystem. How does this rule influence the structure of food webs and the typical biomass distribution among trophic levels?
  3. Compare and contrast the roles of producers, consumers (primary, secondary, tertiary), and decomposers in an ecosystem's energy flow and nutrient cycling.
  4. Outline the major steps in the carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus cycles. What is a key difference in the way phosphorus cycles compared to carbon and nitrogen, and why is phosphorus often considered a "limiting nutrient"?
  5. What is the fundamental difference between how energy and matter move through an ecosystem? How do the laws of thermodynamics and the law of conservation of mass relate to these ecological principles?
