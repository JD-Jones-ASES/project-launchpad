Chapter 12, Section 1: Atmosphere Structure Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces Earth's atmosphere, a crucial layer of gases held by gravity that makes life possible. It details the atmosphere's primary gaseous composition, emphasizing nitrogen and oxygen, and highlights the significant roles of trace gases like water vapor and carbon dioxide. Furthermore, the section describes the four main atmospheric layers—troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, and thermosphere—based on temperature changes with altitude, and explains the concept of atmospheric pressure and its variation.

Key Concepts

  • The Atmosphere's Essential Role: Earth's atmosphere is a thin blanket of gases vital for life, providing breathable air, regulating temperature, protecting from harmful radiation, and driving weather and climate.
  • Atmospheric Composition: The atmosphere is predominantly made up of Nitrogen (78%) and Oxygen (21%). The remaining 1% includes crucial trace gases like argon, carbon dioxide, methane, and varying amounts of water vapor and ozone, many of which influence Earth's temperature through the greenhouse effect.
  • Temperature-Based Layers: Scientists divide the atmosphere into four distinct layers (troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, thermosphere) based on how temperature changes with increasing altitude.
  • The Troposphere: This is the lowest layer, where nearly all weather occurs, containing most of the atmosphere's mass and water vapor. Temperature decreases with altitude here.
  • The Stratosphere and Ozone Layer: Above the troposphere, the stratosphere sees temperature increase with altitude due to the ozone layer. The ozone layer absorbs harmful ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the Sun, shielding life on Earth.
  • Upper Atmospheric Layers: The mesosphere is characterized by decreasing temperatures and where most meteors burn up. The thermosphere, the outermost layer, has extremely low density but very high temperatures due to intense solar radiation absorption, and is where auroras occur.
  • Atmospheric Pressure: This is the weight of the air above a given point, which decreases significantly with increasing altitude because there is less air overhead.

Important Terms

  • Atmosphere: The layer of gases surrounding Earth, held in place by gravity, which provides air, regulates temperature, protects against radiation, and drives weather.
  • Troposphere: The lowest layer of the atmosphere (surface to ~12 km), where nearly all weather occurs, and temperature decreases with altitude.
  • Tropopause: The upper boundary of the troposphere, where temperature stops decreasing.
  • Stratosphere: The second layer of the atmosphere (12 km to 50 km), characterized by increasing temperatures with altitude due to the ozone layer.
  • Ozone layer: A region within the stratosphere (15-35 km) with a high concentration of ozone (O3) molecules that absorb most of the Sun's harmful ultraviolet (UV) radiation.
  • Mesosphere: The layer above the stratosphere (50 km to 85 km), where temperature decreases with altitude, reaching the coldest temperatures in the atmosphere. Most meteors burn up here.
  • Thermosphere: The outermost layer of the atmosphere (85 km to 600 km or beyond), where temperatures can be extremely high due to absorption of solar radiation, despite very low air density. Auroras occur here.
  • Atmospheric pressure: The force exerted by the weight of the air above a given point on Earth's surface, which decreases with increasing altitude.

Detailed Notes

The Atmosphere: Earth's Gaseous Blanket

  • The atmosphere is a thin layer of gas surrounding Earth, held in place by gravity.
  • It's crucial for life, providing breathable air, regulating temperature, protecting from harmful radiation, and driving weather and climate.
  • Air density decreases rapidly with altitude; at 100 km, it's almost a vacuum.

Composition of the Atmosphere

  • Major Components:
    • Nitrogen (N2): ~78% by volume. Relatively unreactive but essential for the nitrogen cycle.
    • Oxygen (O2): ~21% by volume. Crucial for aerobic respiration; produced by photosynthetic organisms over billions of years.
  • Trace Gases (remaining ~1%):
    • Argon (Ar): ~0.93%
    • Carbon Dioxide (CO2): ~0.04%. A significant greenhouse gas.
    • Neon, Helium, Methane (CH4), Ozone (O3): Present in trace amounts.
    • Water Vapor (H2O): Highly variable concentration (0% to 4%).
      • Most abundant greenhouse gas.
      • Plays a central role in weather, forming clouds and precipitation.
    • Trace gases like CO2, CH4, and water vapor have a substantial influence on Earth's temperature via the greenhouse effect.

Layers of the Atmosphere

Scientists classify atmospheric layers based on temperature changes with altitude.

  1. Troposphere

    • Altitude: Earth's surface to ~12 km.
    • Characteristics:
      • Where all weather occurs.
      • Contains ~75% of the atmosphere's total mass and nearly all its water vapor.
      • Temperature: Decreases steadily with altitude (approx. 6.5°C per kilometer). Mountaintops are colder.
      • Tropopause: The boundary at the top of the troposphere where temperature stops decreasing.

  2. Stratosphere

    • Altitude: From ~12 km to ~50 km.
    • Characteristics:
      • Temperature: Increases with altitude (opposite of troposphere).
      • Ozone Layer: A critical region within the stratosphere (15-35 km) rich in ozone (O3).
        • Absorbs harmful ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the Sun, causing the temperature increase.
        • Shields life on Earth's surface from UV radiation, preventing sunburn, skin cancer, and damage to ecosystems.

  3. Mesosphere

    • Altitude: From ~50 km to ~85 km.
    • Characteristics:
      • Temperature: Decreases again with altitude, reaching the coldest temperatures in the atmosphere (as low as -90°C).
      • Where most meteors burn up, visible as "shooting stars."

  4. Thermosphere

    • Altitude: From ~85 km to ~600 km or beyond.
    • Characteristics:
      • Temperature: Can exceed 1000°C due to absorption of intense solar radiation by the few molecules present.
      • Density: Extremely low air density; you wouldn't feel warm despite high molecular kinetic energy.
      • Auroras: Where the aurora borealis (Northern Lights) and aurora australis (Southern Lights) occur as charged particles interact with atmospheric gases.

Atmospheric Pressure

  • Definition: The weight of the air above any given point on Earth's surface, pulled down by gravity.
  • Average Sea Level Pressure: Approximately 101.3 kPa, or 1 atm, or 760 mmHg.
  • Variation with Altitude:
    • Pressure decreases significantly with increasing altitude because there is less air above you.
    • Air is "thinner" at higher elevations (e.g., on mountaintops, requiring supplemental oxygen for climbers).
    • Roughly half of the atmosphere's mass is below 5.5 km, and 99% is below 30 km.

Think Deeper Discussion Point

  • Why flying in the lower stratosphere (10-12 km) might be smoother than the troposphere:
    • The troposphere is where nearly all weather occurs, driven by convection and temperature changes, leading to turbulence, clouds, and storms.
    • The lower stratosphere is above most of the weather systems. The temperature inversion (temperature increasing with altitude) in the stratosphere creates a stable layer, suppressing vertical air movement and thus reducing turbulence.

Review Questions

  1. Explain why Earth's atmosphere, despite being a "thin blanket of gas," is absolutely essential for supporting life.
  2. List the two most abundant gases in Earth's atmosphere and describe their respective roles. Why are trace gases like water vapor and carbon dioxide also considered extremely important?
  3. Describe how temperature generally changes with increasing altitude in each of the four main atmospheric layers (troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, thermosphere). What causes the temperature pattern in the stratosphere specifically?
  4. What is the ozone layer, where is it located, and why is it critically important for life on Earth's surface?
  5. Define atmospheric pressure and explain how it changes as you ascend from sea level to the top of a tall mountain. What are the practical implications of this change for phenomena like mountain climbing?
