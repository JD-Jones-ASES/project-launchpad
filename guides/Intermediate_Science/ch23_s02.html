Chapter 23, Section 2: Solubility Curves Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces solubility curves, which are graphical representations showing how the maximum amount of a solute that can dissolve in a given solvent changes with temperature. It teaches essential skills for interpreting these curves, including identifying saturated, unsaturated, and supersaturated solutions, and predicting precipitation. The section also differentiates between the solubility behavior of solids and gases, highlighting the impact of temperature and pressure on gas solubility.

Key Concepts

  • Solubility Curves: Graphs that illustrate the relationship between temperature and the maximum amount of solute (typically in grams) that can dissolve in a specific amount of solvent (usually 100 g of water). They are crucial for understanding and predicting dissolution and crystallization.
  • Reading Solubility Curves: Involves locating temperature on the x-axis and reading solubility on the y-axis, determining if a solution is saturated, unsaturated, or supersaturated based on its position relative to the curve, and comparing how different substances' solubilities change with temperature.
  • Types of Solutions:
    • Saturated: Contains the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve at a specific temperature. Any point on the curve.
    • Unsaturated: Contains less than the maximum amount of solute, meaning more could still dissolve. Any point below the curve.
    • Supersaturated: Contains more solute than a saturated solution at the same temperature. These solutions are unstable and are represented by points above the curve.
  • Predicting Precipitation/Crystallization: By cooling a hot, saturated solution, the solubility of the solute decreases. If the amount of dissolved solute exceeds the new, lower solubility limit, the excess solute will crystallize out of the solution, forming a solid precipitate.
  • Solubility of Gases: Unlike most solids, the solubility of gases in liquids generally decreases as temperature increases and increases as pressure increases (Henry's Law). These principles explain phenomena like fizzing soda and dissolved oxygen in lakes.

Important Terms

  • Solubility: The maximum amount of a solute that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature. It's commonly expressed in grams of solute per 100 grams of water.
  • Solubility Curve: A graph depicting the relationship between temperature and the maximum solubility of a substance in a particular solvent.
  • Saturated Solution: A solution that holds the maximum possible amount of dissolved solute for a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature. No more solute can dissolve under those conditions.
  • Unsaturated Solution: A solution that contains less than the maximum amount of solute that can be dissolved at a given temperature, meaning more solute could still be added and dissolved.
  • Supersaturated Solution: An unstable solution that contains more dissolved solute than a saturated solution at the same temperature. These solutions are typically formed by carefully cooling a saturated solution and are prone to rapid crystallization if disturbed or seeded.
  • Crystallization: The process by which solid crystals form from a solution, often occurring when a solution becomes supersaturated or when solvent evaporates.
  • Precipitation: The formation of a solid (precipitate) from a solution, often due to a chemical reaction or a decrease in solubility.
  • Henry's Law: A principle stating that the solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the partial pressure of that gas above the liquid.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Solubility Curves

A solubility curve is a powerful visual tool in chemistry that illustrates how the solubility of a substance changes with temperature. This relationship is crucial for many applications, from industrial processes to biological systems.

  • Axes:
    • The horizontal axis (x-axis) always represents Temperature (usually in degrees Celsius, °C).
    • The vertical axis (y-axis) represents Solubility, typically measured in grams of solute per 100 grams of water (g/100 g H₂O).
  • Interpreting the Curves:
    • Each distinct line on a solubility curve graph represents a different chemical substance.
    • For most solids, solubility increases as temperature increases, so their curves generally trend upwards.
    • The steepness of a curve indicates how much the solubility of that substance is affected by temperature changes.
      • Steep curves (e.g., KNO₃) show a dramatic increase in solubility with rising temperature.
      • Flat curves (e.g., NaCl) indicate that solubility changes very little with temperature.

Key Skills for Reading Solubility Curves

  1. Finding Solubility at a Given Temperature:

    • Locate the desired temperature on the horizontal axis.
    • Draw a vertical line upwards from that temperature until it intersects the curve for the specific substance.
    • From the intersection point, draw a horizontal line across to the vertical axis.
    • The value on the vertical axis is the maximum amount of that solute that can dissolve in 100 g of water at that temperature.

  2. Determining Saturation:

    • On the curve: Any point directly on a substance's solubility curve represents a saturated solution. This means the solution contains the maximum amount of solute dissolved at that specific temperature. No more solute can dissolve unless the temperature is changed.
    • Below the curve: Any point below the curve indicates an unsaturated solution. At this temperature, more solute could still be dissolved into the solvent before it reaches saturation.
    • Above the curve: Any point above the curve signifies a supersaturated solution. These solutions contain more dissolved solute than is normally possible at that temperature. They are unstable and can be formed by carefully cooling a saturated solution. Disturbing a supersaturated solution, or adding a "seed crystal," will usually cause the excess solute to rapidly crystallize out of solution.

Predicting Precipitation and Crystallization

Solubility curves are invaluable for predicting what will happen when a solution is cooled, particularly regarding crystallization.

  • Process:

    1. Dissolve a substantial amount of solute in hot water, creating a saturated or nearly saturated solution at a high temperature.
    2. Carefully cool the solution. As the temperature decreases, the solubility of most solids decreases (as indicated by the curve dropping).
    3. If the amount of solute that remains dissolved is greater than the new, lower solubility limit at the cooler temperature, the excess solute will come out of solution. This process is called crystallization or precipitation, where solid crystals form and settle out of the liquid.

  • Calculating Mass Crystallized: The amount of solid that crystallizes can be calculated using the formula: [ \text{Mass crystallized} = \text{Mass dissolved at } T_{\text{high}} - \text{Solubility at } T_{\text{low}} ] Example: If 90 g of KNO₃ is dissolved in 100 g of water at 60°C (where solubility is ~110 g/100 g H₂O), and the solution is then cooled to 20°C (where solubility is ~32 g/100 g H₂O): [ \text{Mass crystallized} = 90 , \text{g} - 32 , \text{g} = 58 , \text{g of KNO}_3 ] This means 58 g of KNO₃ would crystallize, leaving 32 g still dissolved in the 100 g of water.

Solubility of Gases

The solubility of gases in liquids exhibits behaviors that are distinct from those of solids:

  • Effect of Temperature: Gases become less soluble as temperature increases. This is because higher temperatures give gas molecules more kinetic energy, making it easier for them to escape from the liquid phase.
    • Examples: A warm can of soda goes flat faster because CO₂ escapes more readily. Warm lakes have less dissolved oxygen, impacting aquatic life.
  • Effect of Pressure (Henry's Law): Gas solubility increases as the partial pressure of the gas above the liquid increases. The higher pressure forces more gas molecules into the solution.
    • Examples: Carbonated beverages are bottled under high pressure to force CO₂ into solution. When the bottle is opened, pressure drops, and CO₂ bubbles out. Divers experience increased nitrogen solubility in their blood at depth due to higher pressure.

Earth Science Connection: Ocean Acidification and Climate Change

The principles of gas solubility have significant implications for Earth's climate and ecosystems.

  • CO₂ and Ocean Warming: As global temperatures rise, the solubility of CO₂ in ocean water decreases. This means warmer oceans absorb less CO₂ from the atmosphere, which could accelerate the greenhouse effect by leaving more CO₂ in the atmosphere.
  • Ocean Acidification: The CO₂ that does dissolve in ocean water reacts to form carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), which lowers the ocean's pH. This process, known as ocean acidification, has profound negative consequences for marine life, especially organisms that build shells or skeletons from calcium carbonate.

Think Deeper: Supersaturation Scenario

Consider a student who dissolves 170 g of KNO₃ in 100 g of water at 80°C (where solubility is ~170 g/100 g H₂O). The solution is then very carefully cooled to 40°C without disturbance (solubility at 40°C is ~65 g/100 g H₂O).

  • (a) Is the cooled solution unsaturated, saturated, or supersaturated?
    • The solution is supersaturated. At 40°C, only 65 g of KNO₃ should be dissolved in 100 g of water to be saturated. However, the solution still contains 170 g of dissolved KNO₃. It holds significantly more solute than its saturation point at that temperature.
  • (b) What would happen if the student dropped a single crystal of KNO₃ into the solution?
    • Dropping a seed crystal would provide nucleation sites for the excess solute. This would trigger rapid crystallization of the excess KNO₃. The solution would quickly revert to a saturated state, with the excess solute forming solid crystals.
  • (c) How much solid KNO₃ would form?
    • Mass crystallized = Mass dissolved at 80°C - Solubility at 40°C
    • Mass crystallized = 170 g - 65 g = 105 g of KNO₃
    • 105 g of KNO₃ would form as solid crystals, leaving 65 g still dissolved in the solution.
