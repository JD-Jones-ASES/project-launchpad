Study Guide: Minerals and Rocks Revisited

Overview

This section revisits the fundamental components of Earth's crust: minerals and rocks. It provides a precise definition of minerals, details the criteria for their classification, and explains the key physical properties used for identification. Furthermore, it delves into the formation processes of the three main rock types—igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic—and illustrates their continuous transformation through the dynamic rock cycle, driven by Earth's internal and external energies.

Key Concepts

  • Minerals as Earth's Building Blocks: Minerals are naturally occurring, inorganic solids with a definite chemical composition and an orderly internal arrangement of atoms (crystal structure). They are the fundamental components of rocks.
  • Mineral Identification: Geologists identify minerals based on distinct physical properties such as hardness (measured by the Mohs scale), luster (how light reflects off the surface), streak (color of the powdered mineral), cleavage (tendency to break along flat planes), fracture (irregular breakage), specific gravity (density), and other unique characteristics (e.g., magnetism, acid reaction).
  • Rocks are Aggregates of Minerals: Rocks are naturally occurring solids composed of one or more minerals, or in some cases, non-mineral materials. There are three primary types, each formed by distinct geological processes.
  • Igneous Rock Formation: Formed from the cooling and solidification of molten rock (magma underground or lava at the surface). Cooling rate influences crystal size, leading to coarse-grained (slow cooling) or fine-grained (rapid cooling) textures.
  • Sedimentary Rock Formation: Formed at or near Earth's surface from the accumulation and cementation of sediments (clastic) or the precipitation of minerals from solution or accumulation of organic material (chemical/biochemical). They often contain fossils.
  • Metamorphic Rock Formation: Formed when pre-existing rocks are transformed by intense heat, pressure, or chemically active fluids deep within the Earth, without completely melting. This causes recrystallization and rearrangement of minerals.
  • The Rock Cycle: A continuous, never-ending process where rocks are created, transformed, destroyed, and re-created over geologic time. Any rock type can change into another, driven by Earth's internal heat (volcanism, tectonics, metamorphism) and solar energy (weathering, erosion, water cycle).

Important Terms

  • Mineral: A naturally occurring, inorganic solid with a definite chemical composition and an orderly internal arrangement of atoms (a crystal structure).
  • Crystal: A solid in which atoms are arranged in a regular, repeating three-dimensional pattern called a crystal lattice, often reflected in its external shape.
  • Mohs hardness scale: A scale from 1 (softest, talc) to 10 (hardest, diamond) used to rank the scratch resistance of minerals.
  • Luster: The way a mineral's surface reflects light, described as either metallic (shiny like metal) or nonmetallic (glassy, waxy, pearly, silky, earthy, or dull).
  • Streak: The color of the powder left behind when a mineral is rubbed across an unglazed porcelain plate. It is often a more reliable identification property than surface color.
  • Cleavage: The tendency of a mineral to break along specific flat planes, reflecting weaknesses in its internal crystal structure.
  • Fracture: The way a mineral breaks when it does not exhibit cleavage, resulting in rough or irregular surfaces.
  • Specific gravity: A measure of the density of a mineral compared to the density of water.
  • Igneous rock: Rock formed from the cooling and solidification of magma (underground) or lava (at the surface).
  • Magma: Molten rock found beneath Earth's surface.
  • Lava: Molten rock that has erupted onto Earth's surface.
  • Coarse-grained (phaneritic): Igneous rock texture with large, visible mineral crystals, indicating slow cooling underground.
  • Fine-grained (aphanitic): Igneous rock texture with tiny, microscopic mineral crystals, indicating rapid cooling at or near the surface.
  • Sedimentary rock: Rock formed from the accumulation and cementation of sediments (fragments of other rocks, mineral grains, or organic material) or from the precipitation of minerals from solution.
  • Clastic sedimentary rocks: Sedimentary rocks made from fragments (clasts) of pre-existing rocks.
  • Chemical and biochemical sedimentary rocks: Sedimentary rocks formed from minerals precipitating out of water or from the accumulation of organic matter/shells.
  • Fossils: Preserved remains or traces of ancient organisms, commonly found in sedimentary rocks.
  • Metamorphic rock: Rock that has been transformed from a pre-existing rock by heat, pressure, or chemical processes without fully melting.
  • Rock cycle: The continuous process by which rocks are created, transformed, destroyed, and re-created over geologic time.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Minerals and Rocks

  • Rocks appear simple but hold a history of geological processes.
  • They form deep underground, on ancient seabeds, or through intense heat and pressure.
  • Every rock records its formation processes.
  • This section explores Earth's crustal materials and their transformations.

Minerals: The Building Blocks

  • Definition: A mineral is a naturally occurring, inorganic solid with a definite chemical composition and an orderly internal arrangement of atoms (a crystal structure).
  • Four Criteria for a Mineral:
    1. Naturally occurring: Not manufactured by humans.
    2. Inorganic: Generally not produced by living organisms (with few exceptions like calcite in shells).
    3. Solid: Maintains a fixed shape and volume.
    4. Definite chemical composition and characteristic crystal structure: Each mineral has a specific chemical formula and a unique, repeating atomic arrangement.
  • Diversity: Over 5,000 known minerals, but only about 20-30 are common "rock-forming minerals."
  • Common Rock-Forming Minerals: Quartz (SiO2), feldspar, mica, calcite (CaCO3), and olivine.
  • Crystal Structure: The orderly, repeating 3D pattern of atoms within a mineral.
    • When minerals have space to grow, they develop characteristic external crystal shapes (e.g., quartz prisms, halite cubes, calcite rhombuses).
    • Even without ideal external shapes (due to crowded growth), the internal atomic order remains.

Identifying Minerals

Geologists use observable physical properties for identification:

  • Hardness: A mineral's resistance to scratching.
    • Mohs hardness scale: Ranks 10 reference minerals from 1 (talc, softest) to 10 (diamond, hardest).
    • A harder mineral scratches a softer one.
    • Reference tools: Fingernail (2.5), copper penny (3.5), steel nail (5.5), glass plate (5.5-6).
  • Luster: How a mineral's surface reflects light.
    • Metallic: Shiny, like polished metal (e.g., pyrite, galena).
    • Nonmetallic: Various descriptions, including glassy (quartz), waxy, pearly, silky, earthy, or dull.
  • Streak: The color of the mineral's powder when rubbed on an unglazed porcelain plate (streak plate).
    • More reliable than surface color, which can vary due to weathering or impurities (e.g., hematite's variable surface color but consistent reddish-brown streak).
    • Only works for minerals softer than the streak plate (hardness ~7).
  • Other Useful Properties:
    • Cleavage: Tendency to break along flat, smooth planes due to atomic arrangement.
    • Fracture: Breaking with rough, irregular, or curved surfaces.
    • Specific gravity: A measure of density (ratio of mineral density to water density).
    • Special properties: Magnetism (e.g., magnetite), reaction with acid (e.g., calcite fizzes in HCl), fluorescence (glows under UV light).

Rocks and the Rock Cycle

  • Definition: A rock is a naturally occurring solid made of one or more minerals (or sometimes non-mineral materials like volcanic glass or organic matter).

Igneous Rocks

  • Formation: Form from the cooling and solidification of molten rock.
    • Magma: Molten rock underground.
    • Lava: Molten rock at the Earth's surface.
  • Cooling Rate and Texture:
    • Slow cooling (underground): Allows large mineral crystals to grow, forming coarse-grained (phaneritic) rocks (e.g., granite).
    • Rapid cooling (at surface/lava): Results in tiny crystals, forming fine-grained (aphanitic) rocks (e.g., basalt).
    • Very rapid cooling (extreme cases): No crystals form, creating volcanic glass (e.g., obsidian).

Sedimentary Rocks

  • Formation: At or near Earth's surface through two main processes:
    • Clastic sedimentary rocks: Formed from fragments (clasts) of other rocks that undergo weathering, erosion, transport, deposition, and then compaction and cementation.
      • Examples: Sandstone (sand-sized grains), shale (clay-sized particles), conglomerate (rounded pebbles/cobbles).
    • Chemical and biochemical sedimentary rocks: Form when minerals precipitate from water (e.g., rock salt from evaporating seawater) or when organisms accumulate (e.g., limestone from marine shells).
  • Importance: Sedimentary rocks frequently contain fossils, providing evidence of ancient life.

Metamorphic Rocks

  • Formation: Form when existing rocks (igneous, sedimentary, or other metamorphic rocks) are transformed by intense heat, pressure, or chemically active fluids deep within the Earth.
  • Process: The original rock's minerals recrystallize or rearrange without the rock fully melting.
  • Examples of Metamorphism:
    • Limestone becomes marble.
    • Shale becomes slate, then phyllite, schist, and eventually gneiss with increasing metamorphism.
    • Sandstone becomes quartzite.

The Rock Cycle: A Never-Ending Process

  • Definition: The continuous process by which rocks are created, transformed, destroyed, and re-created over geologic time.
  • Interconnections: Any rock type can be converted into any other:
    • Igneous $\rightarrow$ Sedimentary: Weathering and erosion break down igneous rock into sediments, which are then deposited and lithified.
    • Sedimentary $\rightarrow$ Metamorphic: Burial and tectonic forces subject sedimentary rock to heat and pressure.
    • Metamorphic $\rightarrow$ Igneous: If metamorphic rock is pushed deep enough to melt, the resulting magma cools to form new igneous rock.
    • Any type $\rightarrow$ Sedimentary: Any rock exposed at the surface can be weathered, eroded, and turned into sediment.
    • Any type $\rightarrow$ Metamorphic: Any rock subjected to sufficient heat and pressure (without melting) becomes metamorphic.
  • Driving Forces:
    • Internal Heat: Drives volcanism, mountain building, and metamorphism (e.g., tectonic plate movement, magma generation).
    • Solar Energy: Drives weathering, erosion, and the water cycle at the surface.
  • Central Theme: The rock cycle highlights the fundamental connection between Earth's internal and surface processes.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the four fundamental criteria that must be met for a substance to be classified as a mineral. Why are "definite chemical composition" and an "orderly internal arrangement of atoms" considered the most defining characteristics?
  2. Imagine you are a geologist in the field with only a fingernail, a steel nail, and a streak plate. Describe how you would use these tools, along with observation of luster, to help identify an unknown mineral sample.
  3. Compare and contrast the formation processes of the three main rock types: igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic. For each type, provide one specific example of a rock and describe its characteristic texture or composition based on its formation.
  4. Trace a complete pathway through the rock cycle, starting with a granite mountain. Explain the processes involved in transforming the granite into a sedimentary rock, then into a metamorphic rock, and finally back into an igneous rock.
  5. The rock cycle is described as being driven by two main energy sources. Identify these sources and explain how each contributes to at least two distinct processes within the rock cycle.
