Chapter 1, Section 2: Cell Structure Study Guide

Overview

This section provides a foundational tour of the cellular world, introducing the two major categories of cells—prokaryotic and eukaryotic—based on their internal organization. It then highlights key differences between plant and animal cells, both of which are eukaryotic. The core of the section is a detailed exploration of the various membrane-bound organelles within eukaryotic cells, describing their structure and specialized functions as components of a complex, miniature "cellular city."

Key Concepts

  • Two Fundamental Cell Designs: All cells are classified as either prokaryotic (simpler, no nucleus or membrane-bound organelles) or eukaryotic (larger, complex, with a nucleus and various membrane-bound organelles).
  • Prokaryotic Cells: These are typically smaller and simpler, lacking a true nucleus (DNA in a nucleoid region) and internal membranes. Bacteria and archaea are examples, thriving in diverse environments.
  • Eukaryotic Cells: These are larger and more complex, characterized by a membrane-bound nucleus containing DNA and numerous other specialized membrane-bound compartments called organelles. Plants, animals, fungi, and protists are all eukaryotes.
  • Differences Between Plant and Animal Cells: While sharing many organelles, plant cells have a rigid cell wall, chloroplasts for photosynthesis, and a large central vacuole. Animal cells lack these but possess centrioles for cell division. Both plant and animal cells contain mitochondria for energy.
  • Organelle Specialization: Each organelle within a eukaryotic cell has a distinct structure and performs a specific function, contributing to the overall survival and activity of the cell.
  • Endosymbiotic Theory: This theory explains the origin of mitochondria and chloroplasts, suggesting they evolved from free-living bacteria that were engulfed by ancestral eukaryotic cells, forming a symbiotic relationship.

Important Terms

Prokaryotic cell : A cell that lacks a membrane-bound nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles. Prokaryotes include bacteria and archaea. Eukaryotic cell : A cell that contains a membrane-bound nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles such as mitochondria and the endoplasmic reticulum. All plants, animals, fungi, and protists are eukaryotes. Nucleus : The large, membrane-bound organelle that contains the cell's DNA and controls cell activities; often called the "control center" of the cell. Nuclear envelope : The double membrane surrounding the nucleus. Nuclear pores : Tiny openings in the nuclear envelope that regulate the passage of molecules between the nucleus and cytoplasm. Chromatin : The tangled mass of DNA and associated proteins found within the nucleus when the cell is not dividing. Nucleolus : A dense region within the nucleus where the components of ribosomes are assembled. Ribosome : A tiny structure, found either floating freely in the cytoplasm or attached to the endoplasmic reticulum, that assembles amino acids into proteins based on instructions from DNA. Endoplasmic reticulum (ER) : An extensive network of folded membranes extending from the nuclear envelope through the cytoplasm. Rough ER : Part of the ER studded with ribosomes, involved in processing and folding proteins, often destined for export. Smooth ER : Part of the ER that lacks ribosomes, involved in synthesizing lipids, detoxifying chemicals, and storing calcium ions. Golgi apparatus : A stack of flattened membrane sacs that modifies, sorts, and packages proteins and lipids for transport to their final destinations inside or outside the cell. Vesicles : Small membrane bubbles that transport proteins and lipids between the ER, Golgi apparatus, and other destinations. Mitochondrion (plural: mitochondria) : A double-membrane organelle that converts chemical energy from food molecules into ATP, the cell's primary energy currency, through the process of cellular respiration. Cellular respiration : The process carried out in mitochondria that breaks down glucose and other organic molecules to release energy, stored as ATP. ATP (adenosine triphosphate) : The cell's primary energy currency, used to power almost every energy-requiring process. Cristae : Deep folds in the inner membrane of mitochondria that increase the surface area for cellular respiration reactions. Endosymbiotic theory : The theory proposing that mitochondria and chloroplasts were once free-living bacteria that were engulfed by a larger cell billions of years ago, becoming interdependent organelles. Chloroplast : A double-membrane organelle found in plant cells and some protists that captures light energy and converts it to chemical energy through photosynthesis. Photosynthesis : The process carried out in chloroplasts that converts light energy, water, and carbon dioxide into glucose and oxygen. Thylakoids : Stacks of flattened membrane discs inside chloroplasts that contain chlorophyll. Chlorophyll : The green pigment within thylakoids that absorbs light energy for photosynthesis. Stroma : The fluid surrounding the thylakoids inside a chloroplast. Vacuole : A membrane-bound sac used for storage. Central vacuole : A large vacuole typically found in plant cells that stores water, salts, and waste, and maintains cell pressure (turgor pressure). Cell sap : The solution filling the central vacuole in plant cells. Turgor pressure : The pressure exerted by the central vacuole against the cell wall, keeping the plant cell firm. Lysosome : A membrane-bound organelle containing powerful digestive enzymes that break down worn-out organelles, food particles, and foreign invaders. Cytoskeleton : A dynamic network of protein filaments and tubules that provides structural support, maintains cell shape, and enables cell movement and the transport of materials within the cell. Microfilaments : Thin protein fibers, one of the three main components of the cytoskeleton.

Detailed Notes

Introduction: The Cellular City

  • Cells are not empty but "bustling miniature cities" filled with specialized structures.
  • Each structure has a specific job, working together to maintain cell life.

Two Fundamental Cell Designs

All cells fall into one of two categories:

Prokaryotic Cells

  • Definition: Lack a membrane-bound nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles.
  • Examples: Bacteria and archaea.
  • Characteristics:
    • Generally smaller and simpler.
    • No nucleus; DNA floats in the cytoplasm in a region called the nucleoid.
    • Lack elaborate internal membrane systems.
    • Despite simplicity, extremely abundant and successful organisms.

Eukaryotic Cells

  • Definition: Contain a membrane-bound nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles.
  • Examples: All plants, animals, fungi, and protists.
  • Characteristics:
    • Larger and far more complex than prokaryotic cells.
    • Defining feature: A nucleus enclosed by a double membrane, which protects and organizes DNA.
    • Contain various membrane-bound compartments called organelles, each with a specialized function.

Plant Cells vs. Animal Cells

Both are eukaryotic but have key differences:

  • Cell Wall:
    • Plant Cells: Surrounded by a rigid cell wall made of cellulose, providing a fixed, boxy shape.
    • Animal Cells: Lack a cell wall; have only a flexible cell membrane, allowing varied shapes.
  • Chloroplasts:
    • Plant Cells: Contain chloroplasts for photosynthesis (converting light energy into sugar).
    • Animal Cells: Do not have chloroplasts; obtain energy by eating food.
  • Central Vacuole:
    • Plant Cells: Typically have one large central vacuole that stores water, salts, and waste, contributing to turgor pressure.
    • Animal Cells: May have several small vacuoles, but no single large central one.
  • Centrioles:
    • Animal Cells: Contain centrioles, structures involved in organizing cell division.
    • Plant Cells: Most lack centrioles.
  • Common Misconception: Plant cells do have mitochondria, in addition to chloroplasts. Chloroplasts make sugar from light, and mitochondria break down that sugar to release usable energy (ATP), just like in animal cells.

A Tour of the Organelles

The Nucleus — Command Center

  • Structure: Usually the largest organelle. Surrounded by a double membrane called the nuclear envelope, which has nuclear pores for molecular transport.
  • Contents: Contains long strands of DNA (genetic instructions). When not dividing, DNA is spread out as chromatin. Also contains the nucleolus, where ribosome components are assembled.
  • Function: Controls cell activities and stores genetic material.

Ribosomes — Protein Factories

  • Structure: Tiny, non-membrane-bound structures, visible only with electron microscopes.
  • Function: Read genetic messages (from DNA) and use them to build proteins. Proteins are essential for enzymes, structure, hormones, etc.
  • Location: Can float freely in the cytoplasm or be attached to the endoplasmic reticulum.

Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER) — Highway and Factory

  • Structure: An extensive network of folded membranes winding through the cytoplasm.
  • Two Types:
    • Rough ER: Covered with ribosomes (appears bumpy). Proteins made by these ribosomes enter the ER for folding, modification (e.g., with sugars), and packaging, often for export out of the cell.
    • Smooth ER: Lacks ribosomes. Functions include synthesizing lipids, breaking down toxic substances (e.g., liver cells have abundant smooth ER), and storing calcium ions.

Golgi Apparatus — Packaging and Shipping

  • Structure: A stack of flattened membrane sacs.
  • Function: Receives proteins and lipids from the ER in small membrane bubbles called vesicles. It further modifies these molecules (adds "address labels"), sorts them, and packages them into new vesicles for transport to their final destinations, either inside or outside the cell.

Mitochondria — Power Plants

  • Structure: Enclosed by two membranes. The outer membrane is smooth, while the inner membrane is deeply folded into structures called cristae, which increase surface area. Contain their own DNA and ribosomes.
  • Function: Known as the "powerhouses" of the cell. They break down glucose and other organic molecules through cellular respiration to release energy stored in ATP (adenosine triphosphate), the cell's energy currency.
  • Abundance: Active cells (muscle, nerve) have thousands; less active cells have fewer.
  • Endosymbiotic Theory: Their unique DNA and ribosomes support the theory that mitochondria were once free-living bacteria engulfed by a larger cell.

Chloroplasts — Solar Energy Converters

  • Location: Found only in plant cells and some protists.
  • Structure: Double-membrane organelle with its own DNA. Inside, stacks of flattened membrane discs called thylakoids contain the green pigment chlorophyll. The fluid surrounding the thylakoids is the stroma.
  • Function: Sites of photosynthesis, converting light energy, water, and carbon dioxide into glucose and oxygen.
  • Endosymbiotic Theory: Like mitochondria, their structure and DNA support the endosymbiotic theory.

Vacuoles — Storage Compartments

  • Structure: Membrane-enclosed sacs.
  • Plant Cells: Possess a large central vacuole (up to 90% of cell volume) filled with cell sap. It stores water, salts, and waste, and maintains turgor pressure by pushing against the cell wall, keeping the cell firm. Wilting occurs when water is lost from vacuoles.
  • Animal Cells: Contain smaller vacuoles for temporary storage or digesting engulfed materials.

Lysosomes — Recycling Centers

  • Structure: Small, spherical, membrane-bound organelles filled with powerful digestive enzymes.
  • Function: Break down old or damaged organelles for recycling, digest food particles, and destroy invading bacteria or viruses. Abundant in white blood cells for immune response.
  • Importance of Membrane: The membrane prevents enzymes from leaking and digesting the cell itself.

Cytoskeleton — Internal Scaffolding

  • Structure: A dynamic network of protein fibers crisscrossing the cytoplasm.
  • Components: Has three main components, including microfilaments (thin fibers).
  • Function: Provides structural support, maintains cell shape, enables cell movement, and facilitates the transport of materials within the cell.

Review Questions

  1. Describe the fundamental differences between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells, providing examples of organisms for each type.
  2. Compare and contrast plant and animal cells, highlighting at least three key structural differences and explaining the functional significance of each.
  3. Imagine you are observing a cell under a microscope and see a large central vacuole and chloroplasts. Is this cell prokaryotic or eukaryotic? Is it a plant or animal cell? Justify your answer with details about the observed organelles.
  4. Explain the main functions of the nucleus, ribosomes, endoplasmic reticulum (both rough and smooth), and Golgi apparatus, illustrating how these organelles work together in the production and distribution of proteins.
  5. Mitochondria and chloroplasts are both involved in energy transformation. Describe their individual roles in obtaining and utilizing energy within a cell, and explain how the endosymbiotic theory accounts for some of their unique characteristics.
