Chapter 27, Section 3: Evidence and Impacts of Climate Change

Overview

This section comprehensively outlines the robust scientific evidence for current, human-caused climate change, drawing from multiple independent observations. It details how rising global temperatures are impacting Earth's cryosphere (ice sheets, glaciers) and oceans (sea level rise, acidification), and how these changes exacerbate extreme weather events. Finally, the section explores the far-reaching consequences of climate change on ecosystems, agriculture, human health, and infrastructure, emphasizing issues of environmental justice.

Key Concepts

  • Multiple Lines of Evidence: The case for human-caused climate change is built on thousands of independent observations that consistently point to a warming planet and related changes.
  • Global Temperature Rise: Earth's average surface temperature has risen by approximately 1.1°C since the pre-industrial era, with an accelerated warming trend since the 1970s, and night-time temperatures rising faster than daytime.
  • Arctic Amplification: The Arctic region is warming significantly faster (2-3 times) than the global average, primarily due to the ice-albedo feedback loop.
  • Ice Mass Loss: Both Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets, as well as mountain glaciers worldwide, are losing mass at accelerating rates, threatening global water supplies and contributing to sea level rise.
  • Sea Level Rise Mechanisms: Global mean sea level is rising primarily due to two factors: thermal expansion of warming ocean water and the addition of water from melting land ice.
  • Ocean Acidification: The ocean absorbs a significant portion of atmospheric CO2, which reacts with seawater to form carbonic acid, lowering the ocean's pH and making it more acidic. This process is detrimental to marine organisms that build calcium carbonate shells and skeletons.
  • Extreme Weather Intensification: Climate change increases the frequency, intensity, or duration of many extreme weather events, including heat waves, heavy precipitation, droughts, and wildfires, and can intensify tropical cyclones.
  • Ecosystem Impacts: Species are shifting ranges, seasonal biological events are disrupted (phenological mismatch), and critical ecosystems like coral reefs face severe threats from warming and acidification (coral bleaching).
  • Positive Feedback Loops: Processes like the ice-albedo feedback and permafrost thaw accelerate warming by causing a change that amplifies the initial warming.
  • Human System Vulnerabilities: Climate change poses significant threats to agriculture, water resources, human health, infrastructure, and can lead to population displacement, disproportionately affecting vulnerable communities and raising environmental justice concerns.

Important Terms

  • Arctic amplification: The observed trend in which the Arctic region warms at a rate two to three times faster than the global average, driven primarily by the ice-albedo feedback.
  • Ice-albedo feedback: A positive feedback loop where melting ice (which is highly reflective) exposes darker land or ocean water (which absorbs more solar radiation), leading to further warming and more ice melt.
  • Temperature anomaly: The difference between a measured temperature and a reference average (baseline), usually the 1951–1980 or 1850–1900 average. Positive anomalies indicate warmer-than-average conditions.
  • Sea level rise: The increase in the average level of the ocean surface, caused primarily by thermal expansion of warming seawater and the addition of water from melting glaciers and ice sheets.
  • Thermal expansion: The increase in volume of a substance (like ocean water) as it absorbs thermal energy and its particles spread farther apart.
  • Carbonic acid (H2CO3): A weak acid formed when carbon dioxide dissolves in water; in the ocean, it dissociates to release hydrogen ions (H+), lowering the pH.
  • Ocean acidification: The decrease in ocean pH caused by the absorption of excess atmospheric CO2, which reacts with seawater to form carbonic acid.
  • Coral bleaching: A phenomenon where corals expel the symbiotic algae (zooxanthellae) that provide them with food and color, typically due to elevated water temperatures, which can lead to coral death if prolonged.
  • Phenological mismatch: A disruption in the timing of ecological events (e.g., flowering, migration, insect emergence) due to changes in climate, which can negatively impact food webs and species interactions.
  • Positive feedback loop: A process in which an initial change triggers a response that amplifies the original change (e.g., permafrost thaw releasing greenhouse gases, leading to more warming).
  • Environmental justice: The fair distribution of environmental benefits and burdens, recognizing that climate change impacts disproportionately affect vulnerable populations.

Detailed Notes

The Overwhelming Evidence for Climate Change

The scientific consensus on human-caused climate change is based not on a single study, but on thousands of independent observations collected globally over many decades. All these lines of evidence converge, forming a robust scientific case.

Temperature Records

  • Global Warming Trend: Instrumental temperature records, dating back to 1850, analyzed by multiple independent agencies (NASA, NOAA, Met Office, Japan Meteorological Agency) confirm that Earth's average surface temperature has risen by approximately 1.1°C since the pre-industrial era (1850–1900 baseline).

  • Accelerated Warming: The warming has significantly accelerated since the 1970s. The ten warmest years on record have all occurred since 2010.

  • Night-time Warming: Night-time temperatures are rising faster than daytime temperatures, consistent with the greenhouse effect, which traps heat that would otherwise radiate away at night.

  • Arctic Amplification: The Arctic region is warming at a rate two to three times faster than the global average. This is primarily due to the ice-albedo feedback: as reflective ice melts, it exposes darker ocean water, which absorbs more solar radiation, leading to further warming and more ice melt.

  • Temperature Anomalies: Scientists use temperature anomalies (differences from a reference average) rather than absolute temperatures to easily compare data globally and identify trends.

    • Worked Example 1: Calculating Rate of Temperature Change
      • Given: Temperature anomaly in 1970 was +0.2°C, and in 2020 it was +1.1°C (relative to 1850–1900 baseline).
      • Change in temperature = 1.1°C - 0.2°C = 0.9°C
      • Time interval = 2020 - 1970 = 50 years
      • Average rate of warming = 0.9°C / 50 years = 0.018°C/year, or 0.18°C per decade.

Ice Sheets and Glaciers

  • Mass Loss from Ice Sheets: The Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets, which hold enough water to raise global sea level by about 65 meters, are both losing mass at accelerating rates. Greenland lost ~280 gigatons (Gt) of ice per year from 2002-2020, and Antarctica lost ~150 Gt/year during the same period.
  • Glacier Retreat: Mountain glaciers worldwide are rapidly retreating. For example, Glacier National Park in Montana has seen its glacier count drop from 150 in 1850 to fewer than 25 today. This loss threatens freshwater supplies for billions of people who rely on glaciers for agriculture, drinking water, and hydroelectric power.

Sea Level Rise

  • Global Mean Sea Level Increase: Since 1900, global mean sea level rise has been approximately 20 cm. The rate has more than doubled since the 1990s, currently at about 3.6 mm per year.
  • Contributing Processes:
    1. Thermal expansion: As ocean water warms, its molecules move faster and spread out, increasing the water's volume. This is currently the largest contributor to sea level rise. Even a small fractional expansion is significant due to the ocean's immense volume.
    2. Melting land ice: Water from melting glaciers and ice sheets flows into the ocean, adding to its total volume. (Note: Melting sea ice, already floating, does not significantly contribute to sea level rise.)
  • Impacts: Even modest sea level rise increases the frequency and severity of coastal flooding during storms, particularly affecting low-lying island nations, river deltas, and coastal cities.

Ocean Acidification

  • CO2 Absorption: The ocean absorbs about 25% of human-emitted CO2, mitigating atmospheric buildup but altering ocean chemistry.
  • Chemical Reaction: When CO2 dissolves in seawater, it reacts with water to form carbonic acid (H2CO3): CO2 + H2O → H2CO3 → H+ + HCO3-
  • pH Drop: The release of hydrogen ions (H+) lowers the ocean's pH, a process known as ocean acidification. Since the Industrial Revolution, ocean pH has dropped from ~8.2 to ~8.1, representing a 26% increase in acidity due to the logarithmic nature of the pH scale. This is the fastest change in ocean chemistry in at least 50 million years.
  • Impacts on Marine Life: Ocean acidification is particularly harmful to organisms that build shells or skeletons from calcium carbonate (CaCO3), including:
    • Corals: The foundation of reef ecosystems, which support about 25% of all marine species.
    • Shellfish: Commercially important species like oysters, mussels, and clams.
    • Plankton: Microscopic organisms (e.g., coccolithophores, pteropods) that form the base of many ocean food chains.
    • More acidic water makes it harder for these organisms to form CaCO3 shells and can even cause existing shells to dissolve.

Extreme Weather and Ecosystem Impacts

  • Extreme Weather Events: Climate change doesn't create extreme weather, but it makes many events more frequent, intense, or longer-lasting:
    • Heat waves: More frequent and intense due to warmer average temperatures.
    • Heavy precipitation: Increasing because a warmer atmosphere holds more water vapor, leading to more intense rainfall and flooding.
    • Droughts: More severe in some regions as higher temperatures increase evaporation rates.
    • Tropical cyclones: May become more intense with stronger winds and heavier rainfall, fueled by warmer ocean surface temperatures.
    • Wildfires: Increasing in many regions due to hotter, drier conditions creating more flammable landscapes.
  • Ecosystem Responses:
    • Species range shifts: Species are migrating toward the poles and higher elevations to track suitable temperatures.
    • Phenological mismatch: Spring events (e.g., flowering, bird migration) are occurring earlier, disrupting ecological timing and interactions.
    • Coral bleaching: Elevated water temperatures cause corals to expel their symbiotic algae, leading to bleaching and potential death if temperatures remain high.
    • Arctic ecosystem changes: Thawing permafrost releases stored CO2 and methane (CH4), creating a positive feedback loop that accelerates warming. The ice-albedo feedback also plays a major role in Arctic warming.

Impacts on Human Systems

Climate change affects nearly all aspects of human society:

  • Agriculture: Changing precipitation patterns, increased heat stress, and more frequent droughts and floods threaten global crop yields, despite some regions potentially benefiting from longer growing seasons.
  • Water Resources: Retreating glaciers threaten freshwater supplies. Altered precipitation patterns impact river flows. Sea level rise leads to saltwater intrusion into coastal aquifers.
  • Human Health: Heat waves cause illness and death. Changing ranges of disease vectors (like mosquitoes) expand the spread of diseases (e.g., malaria, dengue). Air quality worsens due to wildfire smoke and ground-level ozone.
  • Infrastructure: Coastal flooding damages buildings, roads, and bridges. Thawing permafrost destabilizes foundations in Arctic regions. Extreme weather events overload drainage and power systems.
  • Displacement: Sea level rise and extreme weather can force millions to relocate, creating climate refugees, with low-lying island nations facing existential threats.
  • Environmental Justice: The impacts of climate change are not equally distributed. Developing nations, indigenous populations, and low-income communities often face the greatest risks due to fewer resources for adaptation, highlighting critical issues of environmental justice.

Review Questions

  1. Describe at least three distinct lines of evidence, beyond just global average temperature, that confirm Earth's climate is changing. How do these different observations strengthen the overall scientific case?
  2. Explain the two primary physical processes responsible for global sea level rise. Which process is currently the largest contributor, and why doesn't the melting of Arctic sea ice significantly contribute to global sea level rise?
  3. What is ocean acidification, and what is its chemical basis? Discuss at least three types of marine organisms that are particularly vulnerable to ocean acidification and explain why.
  4. How does climate change influence extreme weather events such as heat waves, heavy precipitation, and wildfires? Provide an example of how a positive feedback loop (like the ice-albedo effect or permafrost thaw) can accelerate climate change impacts.
  5. Beyond environmental impacts, how does climate change affect human systems such as agriculture, human health, and infrastructure? Discuss the concept of environmental justice in the context of climate change impacts.
