Chapter 8, Section 1: Organizing the Elements

Overview

This section explores the historical development of the periodic table, tracing early attempts to organize the growing number of known elements. It highlights Dmitri Mendeleev's groundbreaking work, including his successful predictions of undiscovered elements, and explains how Henry Moseley's discovery of atomic number provided the fundamental principle for the modern periodic table. Finally, it describes the basic structure of the modern table, including periods, groups, and the classification of elements into metals, nonmetals, and metalloids.

Key Concepts

  • Early Attempts at Organization: Before the modern periodic table, chemists like Johann Döbereiner (triads), Alexandre-Émile Béguyer de Chancourtois (spiral arrangement), and John Newlands (Law of Octaves) recognized patterns in element properties, hinting at a hidden order.
  • Mendeleev's Periodic Table: Dmitri Mendeleev created the first widely accepted periodic table in 1869 by arranging elements primarily by increasing atomic mass and grouping those with similar chemical properties. His genius lay in leaving gaps for undiscovered elements and accurately predicting their properties, which were later confirmed by the discovery of elements like gallium and germanium.
  • Atomic Number as the Organizing Principle: While Mendeleev used atomic mass, this led to minor inconsistencies (e.g., tellurium and iodine). Henry Moseley, in 1913, discovered that the true organizing principle is the atomic number (the number of protons in an atom's nucleus). Ordering by atomic number resolved previous anomalies and established the periodic law.
  • Structure of the Modern Periodic Table: The modern periodic table arranges 118 known elements by increasing atomic number. Horizontal rows are called periods (elements in the same period have the same number of electron energy levels), and vertical columns are called groups or families (elements in the same group have similar chemical properties due to having the same number of valence electrons).
  • Classification of Elements: Elements are broadly categorized into three types based on their properties:
    • Metals: Located on the left and center, typically shiny, good conductors of heat and electricity, malleable, and ductile.
    • Nonmetals: Located on the upper right, generally poor conductors, brittle as solids, and diverse in appearance.
    • Metalloids (Semimetals): Located along the "staircase" boundary, possessing properties intermediate between metals and nonmetals (e.g., semiconductors).

Important Terms

  • Periodic table: A chart that organizes all known elements by increasing atomic number, arranging them in rows (periods) and columns (groups) so that elements with similar properties fall in the same column.
  • Triads: Groups of three elements identified by Johann Döbereiner, where the middle element's atomic mass was roughly the average of the other two, and all three shared similar chemical properties.
  • Law of Octaves: Proposed by John Newlands, stating that every eighth element in his list shared similar properties, analogous to notes in a musical scale.
  • Dmitri Mendeleev: The Russian chemist who created the first widely accepted periodic table by arranging elements by atomic mass and successfully predicting the existence and properties of undiscovered elements.
  • Eka-aluminum/Eka-silicon: Temporary names (using the Sanskrit prefix "eka" meaning "one") used by Mendeleev for hypothetical, undiscovered elements for which he left gaps in his table and predicted properties. These were later identified as gallium and germanium, respectively.
  • Henry Moseley: The English physicist who, in 1913, discovered that atomic number, not atomic mass, is the fundamental organizing principle of the periodic table.
  • Atomic number: The number of protons in the nucleus of an atom, which uniquely identifies an element and determines its position in the modern periodic table.
  • Period: A horizontal row in the periodic table. Elements in the same period have the same number of electron energy levels.
  • Group (or Family): A vertical column in the periodic table. Elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons and similar chemical properties.
  • Metals: Elements typically found on the left and center of the periodic table, characterized by being shiny, good conductors of heat and electricity, malleable, and ductile.
  • Nonmetals: Elements typically found on the upper right side of the periodic table, generally poor conductors, brittle as solids, and diverse in physical state and appearance.
  • Metalloids (Semimetals): Elements located along the staircase-shaped boundary between metals and nonmetals, exhibiting properties intermediate between the two categories.

Detailed Notes

The Need for Organization

  • By the 1860s, over sixty elements were known, displaying a chaotic variety of properties (metals, gases, brittle solids).
  • Scientists suspected a fundamental order existed beneath this apparent chaos.

Early Attempts at Organization

  • Johann Döbereiner (1829):
    • Grouped elements into "triads"—sets of three elements with similar properties.
    • The middle element's atomic mass was approximately the average of the other two (e.g., lithium, sodium, potassium).
    • This hinted at patterns but covered only a few elements.
  • Alexandre-Émile Béguyer de Chancourtois (1862):
    • Arranged elements on a spiral wrapped around a cylinder.
    • Elements with similar properties lined up vertically.
  • John Newlands (1864):
    • Proposed the "Law of Octaves," noting that every eighth element shared similar properties, like notes in a musical scale.
    • Initially ridiculed, but his core idea of repeating properties at regular intervals was correct.

Mendeleev's Breakthrough

  • Dmitri Mendeleev (1869):
    • Arranged all 63 known elements by increasing atomic mass.
    • Organized them into a table so that elements with similar chemical properties appeared in the same column (group).
    • His key insight: Leaving gaps for undiscovered elements.
    • He predicted the properties of these missing elements (e.g., atomic mass, density, melting point, chemical behavior), giving them temporary names like eka-aluminum and eka-silicon.
  • Confirmation of Predictions:
    • Gallium (discovered 1875) matched Mendeleev's predictions for eka-aluminum almost exactly.
    • Germanium (discovered 1886) matched his predictions for eka-silicon with similar precision.
    • These successful predictions convinced the scientific community of the table's validity as a reflection of natural order.
    • Example (Mendeleev's Prediction vs. Reality for Germanium/Eka-silicon):
      • Predicted eka-silicon: Atomic mass ~72, density ~5.5 g/cm$^3$, oxide formula XO$_2$.
      • Discovered Germanium: Atomic mass 72.6, density 5.32 g/cm$^3$, oxide formula GeO$_2$.

Moseley and Atomic Number

  • Problem with Atomic Mass Ordering:
    • Mendeleev's table, based on atomic mass, had a few inconsistencies. For example, tellurium (127.6 amu) had to be placed before iodine (126.9 amu) to align with their chemical properties. Mendeleev prioritized chemical properties but couldn't explain why the mass order was sometimes incorrect.
  • Henry Moseley's Discovery (1913):
    • Bombarded elements with high-energy electrons and measured emitted X-ray frequencies.
    • Discovered that X-ray frequency increased in a regular, stepwise pattern corresponding to the number of protons in the nucleus—the atomic number.
    • Moseley established that atomic number is the true organizing principle of the periodic table, not atomic mass.
    • This resolved anomalies like the tellurium-iodine problem: tellurium (atomic number 52) comes before iodine (atomic number 53).

The Modern Periodic Table

  • Ordering Principle: Arranges all 118 known elements in order of increasing atomic number.
  • Periods (Horizontal Rows):
    • Seven periods.
    • Elements in the same period have the same number of electron energy levels.
    • Lengths vary: Period 1 (2 elements), Periods 2-3 (8 elements each), Periods 4-5 (18 elements each, including transition metals), Periods 6-7 (longest, including lanthanides and actinides).
  • Groups (or Families) (Vertical Columns):
    • Eighteen groups.
    • Elements in the same group share similar chemical properties because they have the same number of valence electrons.
  • Lanthanides and Actinides: Two series of elements usually displayed separately at the bottom to prevent the table from becoming too wide.

Categories of Elements

  • Metals:
    • Location: Left side and center of the table.
    • Properties: Typically shiny, good conductors of heat and electricity, malleable (can be hammered into sheets), ductile (can be drawn into wires). Most elements are metals.
  • Nonmetals:
    • Location: Upper right portion of the table.
    • Properties: Generally poor conductors, brittle as solids, diverse in appearance (gases, solids, one liquid like bromine at room temperature).
  • Metalloids (Semimetals):
    • Location: Along the staircase-shaped boundary between metals and nonmetals.
    • Properties: Intermediate between metals and nonmetals (e.g., silicon is a semiconductor, conducting electricity under certain conditions).

Atomic Mass vs. Atomic Number (Think Deeper Explanation)

  • Mendeleev's atomic mass arrangement worked mostly because atomic mass generally increases with atomic number.
  • However, atomic mass is the sum of protons and neutrons. Isotopes (atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons) mean that the average atomic mass of an element can be influenced by the relative abundance of its isotopes.
  • It is possible for an element with fewer protons (and thus a lower atomic number) to have a higher average atomic mass than the subsequent element, due to a higher abundance of heavier isotopes.
  • Atomic number, being simply the count of protons, always increases by exactly one from one element to the next, making it a perfectly reliable and unambiguous ordering criterion for fundamental chemical identity and periodic properties.

Review Questions

  1. Describe the limitations of early attempts to organize the elements (Döbereiner's triads, Newlands' Law of Octaves) and how Mendeleev's approach marked a significant breakthrough.
  2. What was the most significant aspect of Mendeleev's periodic table that convinced the scientific community of its validity? Provide specific examples.
  3. Explain the problem that arose when arranging elements strictly by atomic mass, using the example of tellurium and iodine. How did Henry Moseley's work resolve this issue?
  4. Distinguish between periods and groups in the modern periodic table, explaining what properties elements in each category share.
  5. Compare and contrast the general properties of metals, nonmetals, and metalloids. Where are these categories typically found on the periodic table?
