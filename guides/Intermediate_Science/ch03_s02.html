Study Guide: Subatomic Particles

Overview

This section dives into the fundamental building blocks of atoms: protons, neutrons, and electrons. It explains their distinct properties—mass, charge, and location—and clarifies how these particles define an element's identity (atomic number), its mass (mass number), and its chemical behavior (electrons). The section also introduces variations of atoms, such as isotopes (atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons) and ions (charged atoms due to gaining or losing electrons).

Key Concepts

  • Three Subatomic Particles: Atoms are composed of protons, neutrons, and electrons, each with unique characteristics that determine an atom's identity and behavior.
  • Location and Mass Distribution: Protons and neutrons reside in the tiny, dense nucleus, accounting for almost all of an atom's mass. Electrons occupy the much larger electron cloud around the nucleus but contribute very little to the atom's mass.
  • Charge Balance: Protons carry a +1 charge, electrons a -1 charge, and neutrons have no charge. In a neutral atom, the number of protons equals the number of electrons, resulting in a net zero charge.
  • Atomic Number (Z): The Element's Identity: The number of protons in an atom's nucleus (atomic number, $Z$) uniquely identifies an element. Changing the number of protons changes the element.
  • Mass Number (A): Nucleon Count: The mass number ($A$) represents the total count of protons and neutrons (nucleons) in an atom's nucleus. It is calculated as $A = Z + N$.
  • Isotopes: Variations of an Element: Atoms of the same element can have different numbers of neutrons, making them isotopes. Isotopes have the same atomic number but different mass numbers. While their masses differ, they exhibit nearly identical chemical properties.
  • Ions: Charged Atoms: Atoms can gain or lose electrons to become electrically charged particles called ions. Losing electrons creates positively charged cations, while gaining electrons forms negatively charged anions. The number of protons remains unchanged during ion formation.

Important Terms

  • Proton: A subatomic particle found in the nucleus with a positive electric charge (+1) and a relative mass of approximately 1 atomic mass unit (amu). Its number defines the element.
  • Neutron: A subatomic particle found in the nucleus with no electric charge (neutral) and a relative mass of approximately 1 amu.
  • Electron: A subatomic particle with a negative electric charge (-1) and an extremely small mass (about $1/1,836$ amu). Electrons are located in energy levels outside the nucleus and determine chemical behavior.
  • Nucleons: A collective term for protons and neutrons, which are both found in the nucleus.
  • Atomic Number ($Z$): The number of protons in the nucleus of an atom, uniquely identifying an element.
  • Mass Number ($A$): The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus of an atom. Calculated as $A = Z + N$.
  • Isotope: Atoms of the same element (same number of protons) but with different numbers of neutrons, leading to different mass numbers.
  • Ion: An atom that has gained or lost one or more electrons, resulting in a net electric charge.
  • Cation: A positively charged ion formed when an atom loses one or more electrons.
  • Anion: A negatively charged ion formed when an atom gains one or more electrons.
  • Scientific Notation: A method for expressing very large or very small numbers as a coefficient (between 1 and 10) multiplied by a power of 10.

Detailed Notes

The Three Subatomic Particles

All atoms are composed of three fundamental subatomic particles: protons, neutrons, and electrons. Their properties are summarized below:

Particle Location Charge Relative Mass (amu)
Proton Nucleus +1 ~1
Neutron Nucleus 0 ~1
Electron Electron Cloud -1 ~1/1,836
  • Location: Protons and neutrons are found within the tiny, dense atomic nucleus. Electrons are found in the much larger space surrounding the nucleus, often described as energy levels or shells.
  • Mass: Protons and neutrons have nearly identical masses, approximately 1 atomic mass unit (amu). Electrons are significantly lighter, about 1/1,836th the mass of a proton or neutron. This means that almost all of an atom's mass is concentrated in its nucleus.
  • Charge: Protons carry a single unit of positive charge (+1). Electrons carry a single unit of negative charge (-1). Neutrons carry no charge (they are electrically neutral).
  • Nucleons: Protons and neutrons, because they are both located in the nucleus, are collectively referred to as nucleons.
  • Neutral Atoms: In a neutral atom, the number of protons is equal to the number of electrons, causing their charges to cancel out, resulting in an overall charge of zero.

Atomic Number: The Identity of an Element

  • The atomic number ($Z$) is the defining characteristic of an element. It is the number of protons in an atom's nucleus.
  • Every atom of a specific element always has the same number of protons. For example, all carbon atoms have 6 protons ($Z=6$), all oxygen atoms have 8 protons ($Z=8$), and all gold atoms have 79 protons ($Z=79$).
  • The elements on the periodic table are arranged in order of increasing atomic number.

Mass Number: Counting Nucleons

  • The mass number ($A$) represents the total count of protons and neutrons in an atom's nucleus.

  • The formula for mass number is: $A = Z + N$, where $Z$ is the number of protons (atomic number) and $N$ is the number of neutrons.

  • To find the number of neutrons, you can rearrange the formula: $N = A - Z$.

  • Notation: Atoms are often represented using shorthand notation: $^A_Z \text{X}$, where X is the element symbol, A is the mass number (superscript), and Z is the atomic number (subscript). For example, Carbon-12 is written as $^12_6C$. Sometimes, the atomic number (Z) is omitted as the element symbol already implies it (e.g., $^12C$).

    • Worked Example: An atom written as $^27_{13}Al$.
      • Protons ($Z$): 13
      • Neutrons ($N$): $A - Z = 27 - 13 = 14$

Isotopes: Same Element, Different Mass

  • Isotopes are atoms of the same element that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.
  • Since the number of protons is the same, isotopes have the same atomic number ($Z$). However, because they have different numbers of neutrons, they have different mass numbers ($A$).
  • Chemical Behavior: Isotopes of an element exhibit nearly identical chemical behavior because chemical reactions primarily involve electrons, and isotopes of the same element have the same number of electrons (in a neutral atom).
  • Mass Differences: The difference in neutron count leads to a difference in atomic mass. Some isotopes can be radioactive (unstable nuclei).
  • Atomic Mass on Periodic Table: The atomic mass listed on the periodic table for each element is a weighted average of the masses of all its naturally occurring isotopes, reflecting their relative abundance in nature. This is why atomic masses are usually not whole numbers.

Ions: Gaining or Losing Electrons

  • Ions are atoms that have gained or lost one or more electrons, resulting in a net electric charge.
  • Cations: If an atom loses one or more electrons, it will have more protons than electrons, resulting in a net positive charge. Positively charged ions are called cations (e.g., $Na^+$).
  • Anions: If an atom gains one or more electrons, it will have more electrons than protons, resulting in a net negative charge. Negatively charged ions are called anions (e.g., $Cl^-$).
  • Element Identity: When an atom forms an ion, only the number of electrons changes; the number of protons (and thus the element's identity) remains constant.
  • Worked Example: Particles in a $^56_{26}Fe^{3+}$ ion.
    • Protons ($Z$): 26
    • Neutrons ($N$): $A - Z = 56 - 26 = 30$
    • Electrons: A neutral Fe atom has 26 electrons. A $3+$ charge means 3 electrons have been lost. So, electrons $= 26 - 3 = 23$.

Scientific Notation

  • Scientific notation is used to express very large or very small numbers concisely.
  • It involves writing a number as a coefficient between 1 and 10 multiplied by a power of 10 (e.g., $1.67 \times 10^{-27}$ kg for proton mass).
  • Move the decimal point to get a coefficient between 1 and 10. The exponent ($n$) is the number of places the decimal was moved. Negative $n$ for numbers less than 1, positive $n$ for numbers greater than 10.

The "Empty Space" of an Atom

  • Despite feeling solid, atoms are mostly empty space. The nucleus is extremely small relative to the electron cloud (e.g., a marble in a football stadium).
  • Solid objects feel solid due to the strong electromagnetic repulsion between the electron clouds of neighboring atoms, not because the atoms are physically "full."

Review Questions

  1. Describe the key differences in location, charge, and relative mass among protons, neutrons, and electrons. How do these properties contribute to the overall structure and mass of an atom?
  2. Explain the concept of atomic number ($Z$) and mass number ($A$). How is each used to define an atom, and what is the relationship between them? Provide an example using standard atomic notation.
  3. What are isotopes? How do isotopes of the same element differ, and what characteristic do they share? Why do isotopes generally exhibit the same chemical behavior?
  4. Define what an ion is and differentiate between a cation and an anion. Explain what specific subatomic particle is gained or lost when an atom becomes an ion, and why the element's identity does not change.
  5. Consider a neutral atom with an atomic number of 17 and a mass number of 35. Determine the number of protons, neutrons, and electrons in this atom. If this atom then forms an ion with a -1 charge, how many electrons would it have?
