Mastering Speed and Velocity

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concepts of speed and velocity, distinguishing between these two critical measures of motion. You will learn how to calculate average speed and velocity, understand the difference between average and instantaneous values, and interpret position-time and velocity-time graphs. By the end, you will be able to describe an object's motion precisely using the language of physics.

Key Concepts

  • Speed vs. Velocity: Speed is a scalar quantity indicating how fast an object is moving (distance over time), while velocity is a vector quantity indicating both how fast and in what direction an object is moving (displacement over time).
  • Average Speed: Calculated by dividing the total distance traveled by the total time taken. It smooths out variations in speed over a trip.
  • Instantaneous Speed: The speed of an object at a single, specific moment in time, like what a speedometer displays.
  • Average Velocity: Calculated by dividing the total displacement by the total time taken. It accounts for the object's starting and ending positions, including direction.
  • Formulas for Motion:
    • Speed: $v = d/t$ (distance/time)
    • Velocity: $v_{avg} = \Delta x / \Delta t$ (displacement/time)
  • Position-Time Graphs: The slope of a position-time graph represents the object's velocity. A steeper slope indicates faster motion, a positive slope indicates motion in the positive direction, and a negative slope indicates motion in the negative direction.
  • Velocity-Time Graphs: These graphs show velocity on the vertical axis and time on the horizontal axis. The area under a velocity-time graph represents the object's displacement. Different lines indicate constant velocity, changing velocity (acceleration), or changes in direction.
  • Rearranging Formulas: Algebraic skills are crucial for solving for any variable in a physics equation, such as $d = vt$ or $t = d/v$.

Important Terms

  • Speed: The rate at which an object covers distance. It is a scalar quantity (magnitude only, no direction) and is calculated as distance divided by time.
  • Average speed: The total distance traveled divided by the total time taken for the journey.
  • Instantaneous speed: The speed of an object at a specific moment in time.
  • Velocity: The rate at which an object changes its position. It is a vector quantity, possessing both magnitude (speed) and direction. Calculated as displacement divided by time.
  • Displacement ($\Delta x$): The change in an object's position, measured from its starting point to its ending point, including direction.
  • Scalar quantity: A physical quantity that has magnitude but no direction (e.g., speed, distance, time, temperature).
  • Vector quantity: A physical quantity that has both magnitude and direction (e.g., velocity, displacement, force).
  • Position-time graph: A graph that plots an object's position on the vertical axis against time on the horizontal axis. Its slope represents velocity.
  • Velocity-time graph: A graph that plots an object's velocity on the vertical axis against time on the horizontal axis. The area under its curve represents displacement.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Speed and Velocity

In physics, describing "how fast" something is moving requires precision. This section clarifies the difference between speed and velocity, two closely related but distinct concepts. Speed focuses purely on the rate of movement, while velocity adds the crucial element of direction.

Understanding Speed

Speed quantifies how much distance an object covers in a given amount of time. It is a scalar quantity, meaning it only has magnitude (a numerical value) and no associated direction.

  • Formula: The fundamental formula for speed is: $v = \frac{d}{t}$ where:
    • $v$ is speed
    • $d$ is distance
    • $t$ is time
  • Units: The International System of Units (SI) unit for speed is meters per second ($\text{m/s}$). Other common units include kilometers per hour ($\text{km/h}$) and miles per hour ($\text{mph}$).

Average Speed

When you calculate speed using the total distance of a trip and the total time taken, you are finding the average speed. This value averages out any changes in speed that occurred during the journey. For example, a car traveling 240 km in 3 hours has an average speed of 80 km/h, even if it stopped or changed speed along the way.

Instantaneous Speed

Instantaneous speed refers to an object's speed at a particular, single moment in time. A car's speedometer displays its instantaneous speed. On a position-time graph, the instantaneous speed at any point is represented by the slope of the line at that specific point (or the slope of the tangent line for a curved path).

Calculating Average Speed (Worked Example Revisit)

Problem: A car travels 120 km along a highway in 1.5 hours. What is the car's average speed?

  1. Known Values:
    • Distance ($d$) = 120 km
    • Time ($t$) = 1.5 h
  2. Formula: $v = d/t$
  3. Substitute and Solve: $v = \frac{120 \text{ km}}{1.5 \text{ h}} = 80 \text{ km/h}$ Answer: The car's average speed is 80 km/h.

Rearranging Formulas

The formula $v = d/t$ is versatile and can be rearranged to solve for any of its variables:

  • Solving for distance ($d$): If you know speed and time, multiply both sides by $t$: $d = v \times t$
    • Unit Check: $\text{meters} = (\text{meters}/\text{second}) \times \text{seconds}$. The seconds cancel, leaving meters, which is a unit of distance.
  • Solving for time ($t$): If you know distance and speed, divide both sides by $v$: $t = d/v$
    • Unit Check: $\text{seconds} = \text{meters} / (\text{meters}/\text{second})$. The meters cancel, leaving seconds, which is a unit of time.

Practice: A cyclist rides at 8 m/s for 250 seconds. How far does she travel? $d = v \times t = (8 \text{ m/s}) \times (250 \text{ s}) = 2,000 \text{ m} = 2.0 \text{ km}$

Understanding Velocity

Velocity is a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude (speed) and direction. It describes not only how fast an object is moving but also which way it is moving. For example, a car moving north at 80 km/h has a different velocity than a car moving south at 80 km/h, even though their speeds are identical.

  • Formula for Average Velocity: Velocity is calculated using displacement ($\Delta x$) rather than distance: $v_{avg} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{x_f - x_i}{t_f - t_i}$ where:
    • $v_{avg}$ is average velocity
    • $\Delta x$ is the change in position (displacement)
    • $\Delta t$ is the change in time
    • $x_f$ is final position, $x_i$ is initial position
    • $t_f$ is final time, $t_i$ is initial time
  • Positive and Negative Velocity: Because displacement can be positive or negative (indicating direction relative to an origin), velocity can also be positive or negative. A positive velocity typically means motion in the chosen positive direction, and a negative velocity means motion in the negative direction.
  • Round Trip Example: If you walk 100 m to a cafeteria and 100 m back, your total distance is 200 m. If this takes 200 seconds, your average speed is $200 \text{ m} / 200 \text{ s} = 1.0 \text{ m/s}$. However, your displacement is 0 m (you ended up where you started). Therefore, your average velocity for the round trip is $0 \text{ m} / 200 \text{ s} = 0 \text{ m/s}$.

Graphical Representations of Motion

The Slope of a Position-Time Graph

A position-time graph plots an object's position on the vertical axis against time on the horizontal axis. The slope of the line on a position-time graph directly represents the object's velocity.

  • Slope = Rise / Run:
    • "Rise" = change in position ($\Delta x = x_f - x_i$)
    • "Run" = change in time ($\Delta t = t_f - t_i$)
    • Therefore: $slope = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = v_{avg}$
  • Interpreting Slope:
    • Steeper slope: Indicates a faster velocity.
    • Positive slope (line going up): Indicates motion in the positive direction.
    • Negative slope (line going down): Indicates motion in the negative direction.
    • Zero slope (horizontal line): Indicates the object is at rest (zero velocity).

Velocity-Time Graphs

A velocity-time graph plots velocity on the vertical axis and time on the horizontal axis, offering another perspective on motion.

  • Interpreting Lines:
    • Horizontal line: The object is moving at a constant velocity (steady speed in a constant direction).
    • Line above the time axis: The object is moving in the positive direction.
    • Line below the time axis: The object is moving in the negative direction.
    • Line crossing the time axis: The object changes direction at that moment (velocity becomes zero, then changes sign).
    • Sloping line: The velocity is changing, meaning the object is accelerating (covered in the next section).
  • Area Under the Curve: A crucial feature of velocity-time graphs is that the area between the curve and the time axis equals the object's displacement during that time interval. For constant velocity, this area forms a rectangle, and its area is simply $v \times t$, which is equal to displacement ($\Delta x = v \Delta t$).

Science Connection: Speed in the Animal Kingdom

The natural world showcases a vast range of speeds. The cheetah, for instance, reaches approximately 30 m/s (108 km/h), while the peregrine falcon dives at over 90 m/s (320 km/h). In contrast, a three-toed sloth moves at a mere 0.07 m/s. These varied speeds are often directly linked to an organism's survival strategies, whether for hunting, escaping, or conserving energy.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the key difference between speed and velocity. Provide an example where two objects have the same speed but different velocities.
  2. Describe the difference between average speed and instantaneous speed. When would knowing an object's instantaneous speed be more useful than its average speed?
  3. A runner completes a 400-meter lap on a track in 60 seconds. What is their average speed for the lap? What is their average velocity? Explain your reasoning for both.
  4. How can you determine an object's velocity from a position-time graph? What does a horizontal line, a steeply positive line, and a negative sloping line each indicate on such a graph?
  5. What information can be gained from a velocity-time graph? Specifically, what does a horizontal line above the time axis tell you, and what does the area under the curve represent?
