Study Guide: Waves and Tides

Overview

This section explores the fundamental characteristics of ocean waves, detailing their measurable properties and how they transfer energy. It also delves into the fascinating phenomenon of tides, explaining their primary cause – the gravitational interaction between Earth, the Moon, and the Sun – and distinguishing between different types of tides based on celestial alignment.

Key Concepts

  • Waves Transfer Energy, Not Water: Ocean waves are disturbances that move energy through the water medium, while the water itself primarily moves in circular orbits, returning to its approximate starting point.
  • Wave Anatomy: Waves have distinct measurable parts, including crests, troughs, wavelength, wave height, amplitude, frequency, and period, each describing a different aspect of the wave's form or motion.
  • Wave Speed Equation: The speed of any wave ($v$) can be calculated using its frequency ($f$) and wavelength ($λ$) with the formula: $v = fλ$.
  • Deep vs. Shallow Water Waves: Wave behavior changes significantly as water depth decreases. In deep water, speed depends on wavelength. In shallow water, waves slow down, shorten, grow taller, and eventually break.
  • Tidal Causes: Tides are the regular, periodic rise and fall of the ocean, caused predominantly by the gravitational pull of the Moon and, to a lesser extent, the Sun, which create "tidal bulges" on opposite sides of Earth.
  • Spring Tides: Occur when the Sun, Moon, and Earth are aligned (new moon and full moon), combining their gravitational forces to produce the greatest tidal range (extra-high high tides and extra-low low tides).
  • Neap Tides: Occur when the Sun and Moon are at right angles to Earth (first and third quarter moons), causing their gravitational pulls to partially cancel, resulting in the smallest tidal range (lower high tides and higher low tides).

Important Terms

  • Wave: A disturbance that transfers energy through a medium (like water) without permanently displacing the medium itself.
  • Crest: The highest point of a wave.
  • Trough: The lowest point of a wave.
  • Wavelength ($\lambda$): The distance from one crest to the next, or one trough to the next.
  • Wave height: The vertical distance from a wave's trough to its crest.
  • Amplitude: Half the wave height; the distance from the undisturbed water level (rest position) to either a crest or a trough. Relates to the energy carried by the wave.
  • Frequency ($f$): The number of waves that pass a fixed point per unit time, measured in hertz (Hz).
  • Period ($T$): The time it takes for one complete wave to pass a fixed point, in seconds. It is the reciprocal of frequency ($T = 1/f$).
  • Wave Speed Equation: The mathematical relationship $v = fλ$, where $v$ is wave speed, $f$ is frequency, and $λ$ is wavelength.
  • Deep-Water Wave: A wave traveling in water where the depth is greater than half its wavelength. Its speed depends primarily on its wavelength.
  • Shallow-Water Wave: A wave traveling in water where the depth is less than half its wavelength. Its speed is affected by the bottom, causing it to slow down, shorten, and increase in height.
  • Breaking Wave: A wave that becomes too steep in shallow water and curls over, crashing onto the shore.
  • Tide: The regular, periodic rise and fall of the ocean surface caused primarily by the gravitational pull of the Moon and, to a lesser extent, the Sun.
  • Tidal Bulge: A bulge of ocean water created on the sides of Earth facing toward and away from the Moon, due to gravitational forces.
  • Spring Tides: Tides with the greatest tidal range, occurring when the Sun, Moon, and Earth are aligned (during new moon and full moon).
  • Neap Tides: Tides with the smallest tidal range, occurring when the Sun and Moon are at right angles relative to Earth (during first and third quarter moons).

Detailed Notes

What are Waves?

Ocean waves are not moving masses of water traveling across the ocean. Instead, they are disturbances that transfer energy through the water. While a wave passes, water particles themselves move in small circular paths, returning to their original positions. This is why a floating object bobs up and down with the waves but doesn't travel forward unless pushed by wind or current. Most ocean waves are generated by wind blowing over the water surface.

Wave Anatomy and Properties

Understanding the parts of a wave allows for precise measurement and description:

  • Crest: The highest point reached by the wave.
  • Trough: The lowest point of the wave.
  • Wavelength ($\lambda$): The horizontal distance between two consecutive crests or two consecutive troughs. It is measured in meters (m).
  • Wave Height: The vertical distance from the trough to the crest of a wave.
  • Amplitude: Half of the wave height. It is the vertical distance from the undisturbed water level (the "rest position") to either a crest or a trough. A larger amplitude indicates more energy carried by the wave.
  • Frequency ($f$): The number of complete waves that pass a fixed point in a given unit of time, typically one second. It is measured in hertz (Hz), where 1 Hz = 1 wave per second.
  • Period ($T$): The time it takes for one complete wave to pass a fixed point. It is measured in seconds (s) and is the reciprocal of frequency ($T = 1/f$).

Wave Speed

The speed at which a wave travels can be calculated using a fundamental equation that applies to all types of waves: Wave Speed Equation: $v = fλ$ Where:

  • $v$ = wave speed (in meters per second, m/s)
  • $f$ = frequency (in hertz, Hz)
  • $λ$ = wavelength (in meters, m)

Example Calculation: If an ocean wave has a wavelength of 12 meters and a frequency of 0.25 Hz: $v = (0.25 \text{ Hz}) \times (12 \text{ m}) = 3.0 \text{ m/s}$ This means the wave is traveling at a speed of 3.0 meters per second.

Deep-Water and Shallow-Water Waves

The behavior of a wave changes dramatically depending on the depth of the water it is traveling through:

  • Deep-Water Waves: Occur when the water depth is greater than half the wave's wavelength. In these conditions, waves travel freely, and their speed is primarily determined by their wavelength (longer waves travel faster).
  • Shallow-Water Waves: As a wave approaches the shore, the water depth becomes less than half its wavelength. At this point, the wave "feels" the bottom, leading to several changes:
    1. Slowing Down: The wave's speed decreases due to friction with the seabed.
    2. Decreased Wavelength: As the wave slows, the waves behind it start to catch up, causing the wavelength to decrease (the waves bunch together).
    3. Increased Wave Height: The energy of the wave is compressed into a smaller area, causing the wave height to increase significantly.
    4. Breaking: Eventually, the wave becomes too steep and unstable, causing it to curl over and crash onto the shore, forming what we call a breaking wave. This process explains why waves typically break parallel to the shoreline, regardless of their initial direction in the open ocean.

Tides

Tides are the predictable, rhythmic rise and fall of the ocean's surface observed daily along most coastlines. They are primarily caused by the gravitational attraction between Earth and the Moon, and to a lesser extent, the Sun.

  • How Tides Form:

    • The Moon's gravity pulls on the ocean waters closest to it, creating a tidal bulge on the side of Earth facing the Moon.
    • On the opposite side of Earth, a second tidal bulge forms. This occurs because the Moon's gravity pulls the solid Earth slightly away from the water on the far side, leaving the water to bulge outwards.
    • As Earth rotates throughout the day, different locations pass through these two tidal bulges, experiencing high tide, and then through the areas between the bulges, experiencing low tide. Most coastlines typically experience two high tides and two low tides each day.

  • Types of Tides: The alignment of the Sun, Moon, and Earth influences the magnitude of the tides:

    • Spring Tides: These occur when the Sun, Moon, and Earth are aligned in a straight line (during new moon and full moon phases). In this configuration, the gravitational pulls of the Sun and Moon combine, resulting in exceptionally high high tides and exceptionally low low tides. This creates the greatest tidal range.
    • Neap Tides: These occur when the Sun and Moon are at right angles relative to Earth (during the first quarter and third quarter moon phases). Here, the Sun's gravity partially counteracts the Moon's effect, leading to lower high tides and higher low tides. This results in the smallest tidal range.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental difference between how energy is transferred by an ocean wave versus how water itself moves in the ocean.
  2. Describe all the measurable properties of an ocean wave (crest, trough, wavelength, height, amplitude, frequency, period). How are amplitude and energy related?
  3. An ocean wave has a frequency of 0.1 Hz and a wavelength of 20 meters. Using the wave speed equation, calculate its speed. Show your work.
  4. Trace the journey of an ocean wave from deep water to the shoreline, explaining the changes it undergoes as it transitions from a deep-water wave to a shallow-water wave, and finally breaks.
  5. Compare and contrast spring tides and neap tides. What celestial alignments cause each, and how do they differ in terms of tidal range?
