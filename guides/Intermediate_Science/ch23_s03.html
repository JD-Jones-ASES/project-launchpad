Chapter 23, Section 3: Acids and Bases Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental chemical concepts of acids and bases, compounds essential to understanding many natural and industrial processes. It covers their characteristic properties, the pH scale used to quantify their strength, the difference between strong and weak acids/bases, and the role of indicators. Finally, it explains neutralization reactions, where acids and bases combine to form salt and water, with examples from everyday life and biology.

Key Concepts

  • Acids and Bases (Arrhenius Definition): Acids produce hydrogen ions (H+) in water, while bases produce hydroxide ions (OH-) in water. This fundamental definition helps categorize these compounds.
  • Characteristic Properties: Acids typically taste sour, react with metals to produce hydrogen gas, and turn blue litmus red. Bases taste bitter, feel slippery, and turn red litmus blue. Both react in neutralization reactions.
  • The pH Scale: A numerical scale (0-14) that measures the acidity or basicity of a solution. A pH of 7 is neutral, below 7 is acidic, and above 7 is basic. Each whole number change represents a tenfold change in H+ ion concentration.
  • Strong vs. Weak Acids and Bases: Strong acids and bases completely dissociate (break apart into ions) in water, leading to a high concentration of H+ or OH- ions. Weak acids and bases only partially dissociate, resulting in lower ion concentrations.
  • Indicators: Substances that change color at specific pH ranges, allowing for visual determination of a solution's acidity or basicity. Common examples include litmus, phenolphthalein, and universal indicator.
  • Neutralization Reactions: A chemical reaction between an acid and a base that produces a salt and water. The H+ ions from the acid combine with OH- ions from the base to form H2O, and the remaining ions form an ionic compound (salt).

Important Terms

  • Acid: A substance that produces hydrogen ions (H+) when dissolved in water, increasing the concentration of H+ ions in solution (Arrhenius definition).
  • Base: A substance that produces hydroxide ions (OH-) when dissolved in water, increasing the concentration of OH- ions in solution (Arrhenius definition).
  • pH: A numerical scale from 0 to 14 that measures the acidity or basicity of a solution, based on the hydrogen ion concentration.
  • Strong Acid: An acid that completely dissociates into ions when dissolved in water.
  • Weak Acid: An acid that only partially dissociates into ions when dissolved in water.
  • Strong Base: A base that completely dissociates or reacts to produce hydroxide ions when dissolved in water.
  • Weak Base: A base that only partially dissociates or reacts to produce hydroxide ions when dissolved in water.
  • Indicator: A substance that changes color depending on the pH of the solution it is in, used to determine if a solution is acidic, basic, or neutral.
  • Neutralization: A chemical reaction between an acid and a base that produces a salt and water, typically resulting in a solution with a pH near 7.
  • Salt: An ionic compound formed from the cation of a base and the anion of an acid during a neutralization reaction.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Acids and Bases

Acids and bases are two fundamental classes of chemical compounds that play crucial roles in chemistry, biology, environmental science, and daily life. Their distinct properties are evident in common observations like lemon juice stinging a cut (acidic) or soap feeling slippery (basic).

Properties of Acids

An acid is defined by the Arrhenius theory as a substance that increases the concentration of hydrogen ions (H+) when dissolved in water.

  • Taste: Sour (e.g., lemons, vinegar). Note: Never taste unknown chemicals in a lab.
  • Reactivity: React with active metals to produce hydrogen gas (H₂).
  • Litmus Paper: Turn blue litmus paper red.
  • Neutralization: React with bases in neutralization reactions.
  • Common Examples: Hydrochloric acid (HCl), sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄), nitric acid (HNO₃), acetic acid (CH₃COOH - found in vinegar), citric acid (found in citrus fruits).

Properties of Bases

A base is defined by the Arrhenius theory as a substance that increases the concentration of hydroxide ions (OH-) when dissolved in water.

  • Taste: Bitter (e.g., unsweetened chocolate, tonic water). Note: Never taste unknown chemicals in a lab.
  • Feel: Slippery (e.g., soap).
  • Litmus Paper: Turn red litmus paper blue.
  • Neutralization: React with acids in neutralization reactions.
  • Common Examples: Sodium hydroxide (NaOH - lye), potassium hydroxide (KOH), calcium hydroxide (Ca(OH)₂), ammonia (NH₃), magnesium hydroxide (Mg(OH)₂ - in milk of magnesia).

The pH Scale

The pH scale is a numerical measure from 0 to 14 used to express the acidity or basicity of a solution.

  • Neutral: A pH of 7 is considered neutral (e.g., pure water at 25°C).
  • Acidic: Values below 7 indicate an acidic solution. The lower the pH, the stronger the acid (higher H+ concentration).
  • Basic (Alkaline): Values above 7 indicate a basic solution. The higher the pH, the stronger the base (higher OH- concentration, lower H+ concentration).
  • Logarithmic Nature: Each whole number change on the pH scale represents a tenfold change in the hydrogen ion concentration. For example, a solution with a pH of 3 is ten times more acidic than a solution with a pH of 4, and 100 times more acidic than a solution with a pH of 5.

Strong and Weak Acids and Bases

The "strength" of an acid or base refers to its extent of dissociation (or ionization) in water, not its concentration.

  • Strong Acid: A strong acid completely dissociates (breaks apart into its constituent ions) when dissolved in water. For example, every molecule of hydrochloric acid (HCl) separates into H+ and Cl- ions in solution. Common strong acids include HCl (hydrochloric acid), HNO₃ (nitric acid), and H₂SO₄ (sulfuric acid).
  • Weak Acid: A weak acid only partially dissociates in water. Most of its molecules remain intact, and only a small fraction produce H+ ions. Acetic acid (CH₃COOH), found in vinegar, is a common weak acid.
  • Strong Base: Similar to strong acids, strong bases completely dissociate or react in water to produce OH- ions. Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) is a classic example of a strong base.
  • Weak Base: A weak base only partially reacts with water to produce OH- ions. Ammonia (NH₃) is a common weak base.

Indicators

An indicator is a chemical substance that changes color depending on the pH of the solution it is in. They are invaluable tools for quickly determining if a solution is acidic, basic, or neutral.

  • Litmus Paper:
    • Red litmus paper turns blue in the presence of a base.
    • Blue litmus paper turns red in the presence of an acid.
  • Phenolphthalein:
    • Remains colorless in acidic solutions.
    • Turns pink/magenta in basic solutions (typically at pH > 8.2).
  • Universal Indicator: A mixture of several indicators that changes through a broad range of colors, corresponding to different pH values across the entire 1-14 scale (e.g., red for strong acid, yellow for weak acid, green for neutral, blue for weak base, purple for strong base).

Neutralization Reactions

Neutralization is a specific type of chemical reaction that occurs between an acid and a base.

  • Products: The reaction typically produces a salt (an ionic compound) and water (H₂O).
  • General Equation: acid + base → salt + water
  • Mechanism: In a neutralization reaction, the hydrogen ions (H+) from the acid combine with the hydroxide ions (OH-) from the base to form water (H₂O). The remaining ions (the anion from the acid and the cation from the base) combine to form an ionic compound called a salt.
  • pH of Resulting Solution: In a complete neutralization between a strong acid and a strong base, the resulting solution will have a pH near 7 (neutral).

Worked Example: Neutralization of Hydrochloric Acid and Sodium Hydroxide

Problem: Write a balanced equation for the neutralization of hydrochloric acid (HCl) with sodium hydroxide (NaOH). Identify the salt produced.

Solution:

  1. Reactants: Hydrochloric acid (HCl) and Sodium hydroxide (NaOH).
  2. Expected Products: Water (H₂O) and a salt formed from the remaining ions (Na+ from NaOH and Cl- from HCl).
  3. Unbalanced Equation: HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)
  4. Balance Check:
    • Hydrogen (H): 1 (from HCl) + 1 (from NaOH) = 2 on reactant side. 2 (from H₂O) on product side. (Balanced)
    • Chlorine (Cl): 1 on reactant side. 1 (from NaCl) on product side. (Balanced)
    • Sodium (Na): 1 on reactant side. 1 (from NaCl) on product side. (Balanced)
    • Oxygen (O): 1 on reactant side. 1 (from H₂O) on product side. (Balanced) The equation is already balanced.
  5. Identify the Salt: The salt produced is sodium chloride (NaCl), which is common table salt.

Answer: HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l). The salt produced is sodium chloride (NaCl). This reaction exemplifies how a strong acid and a strong base produce a neutral salt solution.

Real-World Applications of Neutralization

  • Antacids: Antacid tablets contain bases like calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) or magnesium hydroxide (Mg(OH)₂) to neutralize excess hydrochloric acid (HCl) in the stomach, providing relief from heartburn.
  • Stomach Acid: Your stomach maintains a highly acidic environment (pH 1-2) using HCl to aid digestion and kill bacteria. A protective mucus layer prevents the acid from digesting the stomach itself.
  • Blood pH Regulation: The human body's blood pH is tightly regulated around 7.4 by buffer systems. Even small deviations can be life-threatening, highlighting the critical importance of maintaining pH balance in biological systems.

Review Questions

  1. Describe the key properties that distinguish an acid from a base, providing at least three characteristics for each. How do they generally react with litmus paper?
  2. Explain the concept of the pH scale. What does a pH value of 3 indicate compared to a pH value of 6? How does the hydrogen ion concentration change between these two values?
  3. Differentiate between a strong acid and a weak acid, and between a strong base and a weak base. Use the concept of dissociation in your explanation and provide an example for each category.
  4. What is the purpose of a chemical indicator, and how does it work? Describe the color changes you would expect if you tested a basic solution with phenolphthalein and then with blue litmus paper.
  5. Describe what happens in a neutralization reaction. Write the general word equation for a neutralization reaction, and then provide a specific balanced chemical equation for the neutralization of nitric acid (HNO₃) with potassium hydroxide (KOH), identifying the salt produced.
