Chapter 21, Section 2: Darwin's Theory of Natural Selection

Overview

This section introduces Charles Darwin's groundbreaking theory of natural selection, explaining how species change over time. It details his key observations during the HMS Beagle voyage, outlines the four core principles and one conclusion of natural selection, defines evolutionary fitness, and provides real-world examples of the theory in action, such as peppered moths, antibiotic resistance, and Darwin's finches.

Key Concepts

  • Darwin's Breakthrough: Charles Darwin did not originate the idea of species changing, but he provided the mechanism—natural selection—to explain how they change.
  • Observations from the HMS Beagle: Darwin's five-year voyage, particularly to the Galápagos Islands, revealed patterns:
    • Variation within species: Individuals of the same species are not identical.
    • Adaptation to local conditions: Traits match the specific environment.
    • Similarities to mainland species: Island organisms resembled mainland counterparts, suggesting common ancestry and change over time.
  • Mechanism of Natural Selection: The theory is built on four observations and a conclusion:
    1. Variation: Individuals in a population possess diverse, heritable traits.
    2. Overproduction: More offspring are produced than can survive.
    3. Competition: Organisms vie for limited resources and face environmental challenges.
    4. Differential Survival and Reproduction: Individuals with advantageous heritable traits are more likely to survive and reproduce.
    5. Conclusion: Adaptation: Over generations, advantageous traits become more common in the population, leading to better adaptation to the environment.
  • Evolutionary Fitness: Defined as an organism's ability to survive and reproduce in its specific environment. It is measured by the number of surviving offspring an individual produces, not just physical strength or health. Fitness is always relative to a particular environment.
  • Natural Selection in Action: The process is observable today through examples like:
    • Peppered Moths: Industrial pollution caused a shift from light to dark moths due to camouflage advantage, and then back again.
    • Antibiotic Resistance: Bacteria rapidly evolve resistance to antibiotics due to differential survival of resistant strains.
    • Darwin's Finches: Droughts led to an increase in average beak size in finch populations, as larger-beaked birds could access harder seeds.

Important Terms

  • Natural Selection: The process by which organisms with traits better suited to their environment tend to survive and reproduce more successfully than organisms without those traits. Over many generations, it causes populations to become better adapted.
  • Adaptation: A heritable trait that increases an organism's ability to survive and reproduce in its specific environment. Adaptations arise through natural selection.
  • Fitness (Evolutionary): An organism's ability to survive and reproduce in its specific environment. The fittest organism is the one that produces the most surviving offspring, relative to its environment.
  • Heritable: Capable of being passed from parent to offspring through genetic material (DNA).
  • Overproduction: The phenomenon where most species produce more offspring than can possibly survive due to limited resources and environmental challenges.
  • Competition: The struggle among organisms for limited resources (food, water, shelter, mates) and survival against predators, disease, and harsh conditions.
  • Differential Survival and Reproduction: The concept that individuals with traits that provide an advantage in a particular environment are more likely to survive and produce more offspring than those without such traits.
  • Galápagos Islands: A group of islands off the coast of South America, famous for the unique species Darwin observed, which greatly influenced his theory.
  • HMS Beagle: The British survey ship on which Charles Darwin served as naturalist during a five-year voyage (1831-1836).
  • On the Origin of Species: Charles Darwin's seminal book, published in 1859, which presented his theory of evolution by natural selection with extensive evidence.
  • Charles Darwin (1809–1882): British naturalist who developed the theory of evolution by natural selection.
  • Alfred Russel Wallace (1823–1913): A British naturalist who independently conceived a theory of evolution by natural selection around the same time as Darwin, prompting Darwin to publish his work.

Detailed Notes

The Genesis of Darwin's Theory

  • Background: In 1831, 22-year-old Charles Darwin embarked on a five-year voyage aboard HMS Beagle. While others had speculated about species changing, Darwin's unique contribution was explaining the mechanism of change: natural selection.
  • Observations from the Galápagos: His time on the Galápagos Islands was particularly influential. He noted several patterns:
    • Variation: Individuals of the same species, even on different islands, showed distinct differences (e.g., Galápagos tortoises with varied shell shapes, finches with diverse beak sizes and shapes).
    • Adaptation: These variations seemed perfectly suited to their local environments. Finches with strong, thick beaks lived where hard seeds were prevalent; those with long, thin beaks lived where insects were extracted from bark.
    • Similarities to Mainland Species: Organisms on the Galápagos Islands resembled species found on the nearby South American mainland but were clearly different species. This suggested that mainland ancestors had arrived on the islands and then gradually changed over time, adapting to new conditions.
  • Development of the Theory: After returning to England, Darwin spent over two decades meticulously gathering evidence from diverse sources (pigeon breeding, barnacle anatomy, correspondence with other naturalists) before publishing his groundbreaking book, On the Origin of Species, in 1859.

The Mechanism of Natural Selection

Darwin's theory is built on a logical sequence of observations and a powerful conclusion:

  1. Observation 1: Variation

    • Every individual in a population is unique; no two are exactly alike.
    • These differences include physical traits (size, color, beak shape) and physiological characteristics (speed, disease resistance).
    • Crucially, much of this variation is heritable, meaning it can be passed down from parents to offspring through genes.

  2. Observation 2: Overproduction

    • Most species produce significantly more offspring than can possibly survive to adulthood.
    • Examples: an oak tree produces thousands of acorns, a salmon lays thousands of eggs. If all survived, populations would explode.

  3. Observation 3: Competition

    • Due to overproduction and limited resources (food, water, shelter, mates), organisms must compete for survival.
    • Competition can be direct (fighting for territory) or indirect (consuming the same food source).
    • Environmental pressures like predators, diseases, and harsh weather also eliminate many individuals.

  4. Observation 4: Differential Survival and Reproduction

    • Because of variation, some individuals possess heritable traits that give them an advantage in their specific environment.
    • These "advantageous" individuals are more likely to survive the competition and environmental challenges.
    • Critically, they are also more likely to reproduce and pass on their beneficial genes to the next generation. Individuals without these advantageous traits are less likely to survive and reproduce.

  5. Conclusion: Adaptation

    • Over many generations, the advantageous heritable traits become more common in the population because the organisms carrying them leave more offspring.
    • This gradual process leads to the population becoming better suited, or adapted, to its specific environment.

Evolutionary Fitness

  • Beyond "Strong and Healthy": In biology, fitness has a very specific meaning, different from its everyday usage.
  • Reproductive Success: Evolutionary fitness refers to an organism's ability to survive and reproduce in its specific environment. The "fittest" organism is not necessarily the strongest or fastest, but the one that produces the most viable, surviving offspring that also reproduce.
  • Environment-Dependent: Fitness is always relative to a particular environment. A moth whose coloring blends with tree bark has high fitness in a forest with predatory birds. If the environment changes (e.g., bark darkens due to pollution), that color may no longer be advantageous, and its fitness would decrease, while a darker moth's fitness would increase.

Natural Selection in Action: Real-World Examples

Natural selection is not just a historical theory; it's an ongoing process observed by scientists:

  • Peppered Moths:

    • Before the Industrial Revolution, most peppered moths in England were light-colored, camouflaging them against pale, lichen-covered trees.
    • Industrial pollution darkened tree bark by killing lichens and depositing soot. Darker moths, previously rare, now had better camouflage and higher survival rates.
    • The moth population dramatically shifted, with dark forms becoming dominant in polluted areas.
    • After pollution controls cleaned the air, light-colored moths regained their advantage, and their population increased again. This is a classic example of rapid evolution driven by environmental change.

  • Antibiotic Resistance:

    • When bacteria are exposed to an antibiotic, most individuals die.
    • However, if even a few bacteria in the population happen to possess a gene conferring resistance to that antibiotic, they survive.
    • These resistant bacteria then reproduce rapidly, quickly leading to an entire population of antibiotic-resistant bacteria within days.
    • This rapid evolution poses a significant public health threat.

  • Darwin's Finches (Grant's Research):

    • Researchers Peter and Rosemary Grant spent over 40 years studying finches on the Galápagos Islands.
    • During a severe drought, food became scarce, and only large, hard seeds remained.
    • Finches with larger, stronger beaks were better able to crack these seeds, thus having a survival advantage.
    • In the next generation, the average beak size in the finch population had noticeably increased – a direct, observable instance of evolution through natural selection within a single generation.

Charles Darwin: The Man Behind the Theory

  • Born into a wealthy family in 1809, Darwin initially studied medicine and theology but was more drawn to natural history.
  • His five-year voyage on HMS Beagle, especially his observations in South America and the Galápagos, provided the empirical foundation for his theory.
  • He spent decades after his voyage meticulously collecting evidence and refining his ideas, corresponding with scientists globally.
  • He was finally prompted to publish in 1858 when Alfred Russel Wallace independently formulated a similar theory.
  • On the Origin of Species, published in 1859, presented overwhelming evidence for natural selection and was profoundly impactful, despite initial controversy.
  • Darwin's work, characterized by careful observation and evidence-based reasoning, revolutionized biology and established evolution by natural selection as the unifying theory of life sciences.

Think Deeper: Why can bacterial populations evolve so much faster than populations of large organisms like elephants or oak trees?

The rapid evolution of antibiotic resistance in bacteria is often called "evolution in fast forward" due to several key factors:

  1. Extremely Short Generation Times: Bacteria reproduce incredibly quickly, often completing a new generation every 20-30 minutes. In contrast, large organisms like elephants or oak trees have generation times of years or even decades. More generations in a shorter period mean more opportunities for selection to act and for advantageous traits to spread through the population.
  2. Enormous Population Sizes: Bacterial populations are vast, numbering in the billions or even trillions in a single infection or culture. With such massive numbers, the probability of a beneficial mutation (like an antibiotic resistance gene) arising in at least one individual is much higher, even if the mutation rate per individual is low.
  3. High Mutation Rates (for some): While not explicitly stated for all bacteria in the text, many bacteria can have relatively high mutation rates, further increasing the chance of beneficial mutations.
  4. Horizontal Gene Transfer (Bonus Context): While beyond the scope of this section, bacteria also have the ability to acquire genes, including resistance genes, from other bacteria through processes like conjugation, transformation, and transduction, rather than just vertical inheritance from parent to offspring. This accelerates the spread of advantageous traits.

These factors combine to allow bacteria to adapt and evolve much more rapidly to environmental pressures, such as the introduction of antibiotics, compared to organisms with longer life cycles and smaller populations.

Review Questions

  1. Describe the key observations Charles Darwin made during his voyage on the HMS Beagle that laid the groundwork for his theory of natural selection.
  2. Explain the four core observations and the single conclusion that form the basis of Darwin's theory of natural selection. How do these elements connect to drive evolutionary change?
  3. How does the evolutionary definition of "fitness" differ from its common usage in everyday language? Why is this distinction crucial for understanding natural selection?
  4. Choose one of the real-world examples (peppered moths, antibiotic resistance, or Darwin's finches) discussed in the text. Explain how this example demonstrates natural selection in action, identifying the variation, selection pressure, and resulting adaptation.
