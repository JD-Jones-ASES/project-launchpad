Series and Parallel Circuits

Overview

This section explores the two fundamental ways components can be connected in an electrical circuit: in series or in parallel. Understanding these arrangements is crucial because the connection type dramatically influences how current flows, how voltage distributes, and how the total resistance of the circuit is calculated. We will learn the specific rules governing current, voltage, and resistance for each type, how to read circuit diagrams, and how to analyze more complex "combination" circuits.

Key Concepts

  • Circuit Diagrams: Standardized graphical representations using schematic symbols to depict circuit components and their connections, allowing for clear and universal understanding among physicists and engineers.
  • Series Circuits: An arrangement where components are connected in a single, unbroken loop, creating only one path for current.
    • Current Rule: Current is the same through every component and equals the total current from the source ($I_{total} = I_1 = I_2 = ...$).
    • Voltage Rule: The total voltage supplied by the battery divides among the components, with voltage drops adding up to the total ($V_{total} = V_1 + V_2 + ...$).
    • Resistance Rule: The total equivalent resistance is the sum of individual resistances ($R_T = R_1 + R_2 + ...$). Adding more resistors in series increases total resistance.
    • Breakage: If any component in a series circuit breaks, the entire circuit is interrupted, and current stops.
  • Parallel Circuits: An arrangement where components are connected across the same two points, creating multiple separate paths (branches) for current to flow.
    • Voltage Rule: The voltage across each branch is the same and equals the total voltage supplied by the battery ($V_{total} = V_1 = V_2 = ...$).
    • Current Rule: The total current from the battery splits among the branches, with individual branch currents adding up to the total ($I_{total} = I_1 + I_2 + ...$).
    • Resistance Rule: The reciprocal of the total equivalent resistance is the sum of the reciprocals of individual resistances ($1/R_T = 1/R_1 + 1/R_2 + ...$). Adding more resistors in parallel decreases total resistance.
    • Breakage: If a component in one branch of a parallel circuit breaks, current can still flow through the other branches.
  • Combination Circuits: Circuits that contain both series and parallel arrangements of components. These are analyzed by systematically simplifying parallel and series sections until a single equivalent resistance is found.
  • Real-World Applications: Series and parallel circuits are found in everyday technology, such as household wiring (parallel for independence and full voltage) and even biological systems like the nervous system (parallel for redundancy).

Important Terms

  • Series Circuit: A circuit arrangement where components are connected end-to-end, forming a single path for current.
  • Parallel Circuit: A circuit arrangement where components are connected across the same two points, creating multiple alternative paths for current.
  • Circuit Diagram (Schematic Diagram): A simplified, standardized drawing that uses symbols to represent electrical components and their connections in a circuit.
  • Schematic Symbol: A graphical icon used in circuit diagrams to represent a specific electrical component (e.g., resistor, battery, wire).
  • Equivalent Resistance ($R_T$): The single resistance that could replace all resistors in a circuit (or part of a circuit) without changing the total current or voltage in the rest of the circuit.
  • Ohm's Law: The fundamental relationship between voltage, current, and resistance in a circuit: $V = IR$. This law is used extensively in analyzing both series and parallel circuits.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Circuit Connections

While a simple circuit might have just one resistor and a battery, most real-world electrical systems are far more complex, containing many components. The way these components are interconnected, either one after another or side by side, fundamentally alters how electricity behaves within the circuit. These two basic connection methods are called series and parallel.

Circuit Diagrams

Before you can analyze circuits, you must be able to "read" them. Electrical circuits are represented using circuit diagrams, which are standardized drawings that use special schematic symbols instead of realistic pictures of components. These symbols make it easy for anyone trained in electronics or physics to understand a circuit's design. Common symbols include those for resistors, batteries, and wires.

Series Circuits

A series circuit is characterized by having all its components connected in a single, continuous loop. This means there is only one pathway for the electric current to flow from the battery, through each component, and back to the battery.

Key Characteristics and Rules for Series Circuits:

  1. Current is the same everywhere: Because there's only one path, every unit of charge (coulomb) that leaves the battery must pass through every single component in the circuit.

    • Rule: $I_{total} = I_1 = I_2 = ...$ (where $I_1, I_2$, etc., are currents through individual components).

  2. Voltages add up to the battery voltage: As current passes through each resistor, there is a "voltage drop" across it, meaning some electrical potential energy is used. The sum of these individual voltage drops across all resistors must equal the total voltage supplied by the battery.

    • Rule: $V_{total} = V_1 + V_2 + ...$ (where $V_1, V_2$, etc., are voltage drops across individual components).

  3. Total resistance equals the sum of individual resistances: Adding resistors in series effectively makes the path for current longer and harder to traverse. Therefore, the total equivalent resistance ($R_T$) of a series circuit is simply the sum of all individual resistances.

    • Rule: $R_T = R_1 + R_2 + ...$
    • Effect of Adding Resistors: Adding more resistors in series always increases the total resistance. According to Ohm's Law ($I = V/R$), if the total resistance increases and the battery voltage remains constant, the total current in the circuit will decrease.
    • Consequence of Breakage: If any component in a series circuit breaks (e.g., a light bulb burns out and its filament breaks), the single path for current is interrupted, and current stops flowing through the entire circuit. This is why old strings of Christmas lights often went entirely dark when just one bulb failed.

Worked Example: Series Circuit Let's analyze the provided example:

  • Problem: Two resistors, $R_1 = 10 \Omega$ and $R_2 = 20 \Omega$, are connected in series to a $12V$ battery. Calculate (a) the total resistance, (b) the current in the circuit, and (c) the voltage drop across each resistor.

  • Solution:

    • (a) Total Resistance: Using the series resistance rule: $R_T = R_1 + R_2 = 10 \Omega + 20 \Omega = 30 \Omega$

    • (b) Current in the Circuit: Using Ohm's Law ($I = V/R$) with the total voltage and total resistance: $I = V_{total} / R_T = 12V / 30 \Omega = 0.40A$ Since current is the same everywhere in a series circuit, this 0.40A flows through both $R_1$ and $R_2$.

    • (c) Voltage Drop Across Each Resistor: Using Ohm's Law for each individual resistor, along with the common current: $V_1 = I \cdot R_1 = (0.40A)(10 \Omega) = 4.0V$ $V_2 = I \cdot R_2 = (0.40A)(20 \Omega) = 8.0V$ Check: $V_1 + V_2 = 4.0V + 8.0V = 12.0V$, which correctly equals the battery voltage. Notice that the larger resistor ($R_2$) has a larger voltage drop, meaning voltage divides proportionally to resistance in a series circuit.

Parallel Circuits

A parallel circuit features components connected across the same two points, creating multiple distinct pathways or "branches" for current to flow.

Key Characteristics and Rules for Parallel Circuits:

  1. Voltage is the same across each branch: Because each branch is connected directly across the battery's terminals, every component in a parallel branch experiences the full battery voltage.

    • Rule: $V_{total} = V_1 = V_2 = ...$ (where $V_1, V_2$, etc., are voltages across individual branches).

  2. Currents add up to the total current: The total current leaving the battery splits at junctions to flow through the different parallel branches. The sum of the currents in each branch must equal the total current supplied by the battery.

    • Rule: $I_{total} = I_1 + I_2 + ...$ (where $I_1, I_2$, etc., are currents through individual branches).

  3. Total resistance is found using the reciprocal formula: Adding more parallel paths provides more avenues for current to flow, effectively making it easier for charge to move through the circuit. This means adding more resistors in parallel actually decreases the total equivalent resistance ($R_T$).

    • Rule: $1/R_T = 1/R_1 + 1/R_2 + ...$
    • Effect of Adding Resistors: The total resistance of a parallel circuit is always less than the smallest individual resistance. Adding more resistors in parallel always decreases the total resistance, and thus increases the total current drawn from the source (for a fixed voltage).
    • Consequence of Breakage: If a component in one branch of a parallel circuit breaks, current simply stops flowing through that specific branch. The other branches remain connected to the voltage source, and current continues to flow through them independently.

Worked Example: Parallel Circuit Let's analyze the provided example:

  • Problem: Two resistors, $R_1 = 20 \Omega$ and $R_2 = 30 \Omega$, are connected in parallel to a $12V$ battery. Calculate (a) the total resistance, (b) the total current from the battery, and (c) the current through each resistor.

  • Solution:

    • (a) Total Resistance: Using the parallel resistance reciprocal formula: $1/R_T = 1/R_1 + 1/R_2 = 1/20 \Omega + 1/30 \Omega$ To add fractions, find a common denominator (60): $1/R_T = 3/60 \Omega + 2/60 \Omega = 5/60 \Omega$ Now, take the reciprocal to find $R_T$: $R_T = 60/5 \Omega = 12 \Omega$ Notice that $12 \Omega$ is less than both $20 \Omega$ and $30 \Omega$, as expected for a parallel circuit.

    • (b) Total Current: Using Ohm's Law ($I = V/R$) with the total voltage and total resistance: $I_T = V_{total} / R_T = 12V / 12 \Omega = 1.0A$

    • (c) Current Through Each Resistor: Using Ohm's Law for each individual resistor, along with the common voltage (the full battery voltage): $I_1 = V_{total} / R_1 = 12V / 20 \Omega = 0.60A$ $I_2 = V_{total} / R_2 = 12V / 30 \Omega = 0.40A$ Check: $I_1 + I_2 = 0.60A + 0.40A = 1.0A$, which correctly equals the total current $I_T$. Notice that the branch with lower resistance ($R_1$) carries more current, meaning current divides inversely proportionally to resistance in a parallel circuit.

Comparing Series and Parallel Circuits

Feature Series Circuit Parallel Circuit
Current (I) Same everywhere ($I_T = I_1 = I_2$) Adds up ($I_T = I_1 + I_2 + ...$)
Voltage (V) Adds up ($V_T = V_1 + V_2 + ...$) Same across each branch ($V_T = V_1 = V_2$)
Resistance (R) Adds up ($R_T = R_1 + R_2 + ...$) Reciprocals add ($1/R_T = 1/R_1 + 1/R_2 + ...$)
Adding Resistors Increases $R_T$, Decreases $I_T$ Decreases $R_T$, Increases $I_T$
Effect of Breakage Whole circuit stops Only affected branch stops, others continue
Voltage Division Proportional to resistance Not applicable (voltage is same)
Current Division Not applicable (current is same) Inversely proportional to resistance

Combination Circuits

Many practical circuits are not purely series or purely parallel; instead, they are combination circuits that incorporate both arrangements. To analyze these more complex circuits, you must simplify them systematically:

  1. Identify and Simplify Parallel Sections: Look for resistors connected in parallel. Replace each parallel group with a single equivalent resistance using the reciprocal formula ($1/R_{eq} = 1/R_1 + 1/R_2 + ...$).
  2. Identify and Simplify Series Sections: After simplifying parallel sections, you will likely find new resistors (including the equivalent resistances you just calculated) connected in series. Replace each series group with a single equivalent resistance by adding them up ($R_{eq} = R_1 + R_2 + ...$).
  3. Repeat: Continue steps 1 and 2 until the entire circuit is reduced to a single total equivalent resistance connected across the battery.
  4. Calculate Total Current: Use Ohm's Law ($I_T = V_{battery} / R_{total}$) to find the total current leaving the battery.
  5. Work Backward: To find individual currents and voltages, progressively expand the simplified circuit back to its original form. Use the series and parallel rules at each step to determine the current through and voltage across each original component.

Circuits in the Body (Science Connection)

Your nervous system provides an excellent biological example of a complex electrical circuit. Nerve impulses, which are electrical signals, travel along neurons. The brain acts as a central processing unit, receiving signals from sensory neurons (input) and sending commands via motor neurons (output). The branching nature of the nervous system is analogous to a parallel circuit. This parallel arrangement offers redundancy: if one nerve pathway is damaged, other pathways can often still transmit signals, allowing for potential recovery of function after injury.

Household Wiring (Think Deeper)

Why is household wiring typically parallel, not series? If your house were wired in series:

  • Interdependence: Turning off or unplugging any single appliance (like a light) would break the entire circuit, causing all other lights and appliances on that circuit to stop working.
  • Voltage Division: Each appliance would receive only a fraction of the total household voltage (typically 120V in North America). Appliances are designed to operate at the full voltage, so they would not function properly, if at all. For example, a 120V light bulb would be very dim or not light up at all if it only received 20V.
  • Adding Appliances: Adding more appliances in series would increase the total resistance, further reducing the current and voltage available to each appliance.

In contrast, parallel wiring ensures:

  1. Independent Operation: Each appliance forms its own branch, receiving the full 120V. You can turn one light off without affecting others.
  2. Full Voltage: Every appliance receives the voltage it needs to operate correctly.
  3. Consistent Performance: Adding or removing appliances does not change the voltage supplied to other devices.

The main trade-off of parallel wiring is that adding more devices increases the total current drawn from the main supply. This is why household circuits have fuses or circuit breakers – safety devices that automatically open the circuit if the current becomes dangerously high, preventing overheating and potential fires.
