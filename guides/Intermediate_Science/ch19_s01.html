Chapter 19, Section 1: Wave Properties Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concept of a wave as a disturbance that transfers energy without transporting matter. It differentiates between transverse and longitudinal waves based on the motion of the medium's particles relative to the wave direction. Key quantitative properties like wavelength, frequency, amplitude, and period are defined, along with their relationships, culminating in the wave speed equation. Finally, the section explores how a wave's speed and wavelength are affected by the medium it travels through.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of a Wave: A wave is a disturbance that moves through a medium, transferring energy from one place to another without permanently moving the matter of the medium itself. The medium's particles oscillate around their rest positions.
  • Transverse Waves: Waves where the particles of the medium vibrate perpendicular (at right angles) to the direction the wave travels. Examples include waves on a rope, water surface waves, and all electromagnetic waves. They feature crests (highest points) and troughs (lowest points).
  • Longitudinal Waves: Waves where the particles of the medium vibrate parallel (in the same direction) to the direction the wave travels. Sound waves are the primary example. They consist of compressions (regions where particles are close together and pressure is high) and rarefactions (regions where particles are spread apart and pressure is low).
  • Wave Anatomy (Fundamental Quantities): All waves are described by:
    • Wavelength ($\lambda$): The distance between two consecutive corresponding points on a wave.
    • Frequency ($f$): The number of complete wave cycles passing a point per unit time.
    • Amplitude: The maximum displacement of a particle from its rest position, related to the wave's energy.
    • Period ($T$): The time required for one complete wave cycle to pass a point.
  • Relationship between Period and Frequency: Period and frequency are reciprocals: $T = 1/f$ and $f = 1/T$.
  • Wave Speed Equation: The speed ($v$) of a wave is calculated by multiplying its frequency ($f$) by its wavelength ($\lambda$): $v = f\lambda$.
  • The Medium's Influence: The speed of a wave is determined by the properties of the medium it travels through. When a wave changes medium, its speed and wavelength change, but its frequency remains constant because it is determined by the wave's source.

Important Terms

  • Wave: A disturbance that transfers energy from one place to another without transporting matter.
  • Medium: The material through which a wave travels.
  • Transverse Wave: A wave in which the particles of the medium vibrate perpendicular to the direction the wave travels.
  • Crest: The highest point of a transverse wave.
  • Trough: The lowest point of a transverse wave.
  • Rest Position: The equilibrium position of the medium's particles when no wave is passing through.
  • Longitudinal Wave: A wave in which the particles of the medium vibrate parallel to the direction the wave travels.
  • Compression: A region in a longitudinal wave where particles are pushed close together, resulting in higher pressure.
  • Rarefaction: A region in a longitudinal wave where particles are spread apart, resulting in lower pressure.
  • Wavelength ($\lambda$): The distance between two consecutive corresponding points on a wave (e.g., crest to crest, compression to compression), measured in meters (m).
  • Frequency ($f$): The number of complete wave cycles that pass a given point per unit time, measured in hertz (Hz). ($1 \text{ Hz} = 1 \text{ cycle/second}$)
  • Amplitude: The maximum displacement of a particle from its rest position, indicating the energy carried by the wave.
  • Period ($T$): The time required for one complete wave cycle to pass a given point, measured in seconds (s).
  • Hertz (Hz): The SI unit of frequency, equal to one cycle per second.

Detailed Notes

What is a Wave?

A wave is a fundamental phenomenon in physics, defined as a traveling disturbance that transports energy from one location to another. Crucially, a wave accomplishes this without transporting matter. If you watch a wave on a rope or in water, the individual parts of the rope or water move up and down (or back and forth) but return to their original positions; they do not travel along with the wave itself. The material the wave travels through is called the medium, and its particles simply oscillate in place as the wave's energy moves forward.

Types of Waves

Transverse Waves

In a transverse wave, the particles of the medium vibrate perpendicular (at right angles) to the direction the wave travels.

  • Examples: Shaking a rope up and down to send a pulse horizontally, ripples on the surface of water, and all forms of electromagnetic waves (like light).
  • Anatomy:
    • Crest: The highest point of the wave, representing maximum upward displacement from the rest position.
    • Trough: The lowest point of the wave, representing maximum downward displacement from the rest position.
    • Rest Position: The horizontal line representing the undisturbed state of the medium.

Longitudinal Waves

In a longitudinal wave, the particles of the medium vibrate parallel (in the same direction) to the direction the wave travels.

  • Examples: Pushing and pulling on one end of a Slinky along its length, and sound waves traveling through air or other media.
  • Anatomy:
    • Compressions: Regions where the particles of the medium are bunched together, resulting in higher density and pressure than normal.
    • Rarefactions: Regions where the particles of the medium are spread apart, resulting in lower density and pressure than normal.
    • The wavelength of a longitudinal wave is the distance from one compression to the next compression (or from one rarefaction to the next rarefaction).

Wave Anatomy: Quantitative Descriptions

All waves, regardless of their type, can be characterized by four fundamental quantities:

  1. Wavelength ($\lambda$)

    • Definition: The distance between any two consecutive corresponding points on a wave. For transverse waves, this could be crest-to-crest or trough-to-trough. For longitudinal waves, it's compression-to-compression or rarefaction-to-rarefaction.
    • Units: Measured in meters (m).

  2. Frequency ($f$)

    • Definition: The number of complete wave cycles that pass a given point per unit of time.
    • Units: Measured in hertz (Hz), where $1 \text{ Hz}$ equals 1 cycle per second ($1 \text{ s}^{-1}$).

  3. Amplitude

    • Definition: The maximum displacement of a particle in the medium from its rest (equilibrium) position. For a transverse wave, it's the height from the rest position to a crest (or depth to a trough).
    • Relationship to Energy: A wave's amplitude is directly related to the amount of energy it carries. Greater amplitude means more energy.

  4. Period ($T$)

    • Definition: The time it takes for one complete wave cycle to pass a given point.
    • Units: Measured in seconds (s).
    • Relationship to Frequency: Period and frequency are inversely proportional (reciprocals) of each other: $$T = \frac{1}{f} \quad \text{or equivalently} \quad f = \frac{1}{T}$$ For example, if a wave has a frequency of $500 \text{ Hz}$ (500 cycles per second), its period is $1/500 \text{ s} = 0.002 \text{ s}$ (or $2 \text{ milliseconds}$).

Wave Speed

The speed at which a wave travels ($v$) can be calculated using its frequency ($f$) and wavelength ($\lambda$):

$$v = f \lambda$$

  • Where:
    • $v$ is the wave speed in meters per second (m/s).
    • $f$ is the frequency in hertz (Hz).
    • $\lambda$ is the wavelength in meters (m).

This equation makes intuitive sense: if each wave cycle is $\lambda$ meters long, and $f$ cycles pass per second, then in one second the wave covers a total distance of $f \times \lambda$ meters.

Worked Example: Calculating Wave Speed

  • Problem: A wave on a string has a wavelength of $0.50 \text{ m}$ and a frequency of $20 \text{ Hz}$. Calculate the wave speed.
  • Solution:
    • Known values: $\lambda = 0.50 \text{ m}$, $f = 20 \text{ Hz}$.
    • Apply the wave speed equation: $$v = f \lambda = (20 \text{ Hz})(0.50 \text{ m}) = 10 \text{ m/s}$$
    • The wave travels along the string at $10 \text{ m/s}$.

The Medium Matters

The speed of a wave is not constant; it fundamentally depends on the medium through which it propagates.

  • Examples: Sound travels faster in water than in air, and even faster in solids like steel. A wave on a thick, heavy rope will move slower than on a thin, light string under the same tension.
  • Key Principle: When a wave passes from one medium to another (e.g., light moving from air to water, or sound moving from water to steel), its speed ($v$) changes, and consequently, its wavelength ($\lambda$) also changes. However, the frequency ($f$) of the wave remains constant. This is because the frequency is determined by the source that created the wave and does not change as the wave enters a new medium. This principle is crucial for understanding phenomena like refraction.

Review Questions

  1. Explain what a wave is, focusing on how it transfers energy and what it does not transport. Provide a real-world example to illustrate this concept.
  2. Distinguish between transverse waves and longitudinal waves. For each type, describe the motion of the medium's particles relative to the wave direction and give two distinct examples.
  3. Define the four fundamental quantities used to describe a wave: wavelength, frequency, amplitude, and period. Explain how period and frequency are mathematically related.
  4. How does the medium a wave travels through affect its speed and wavelength? If a sound wave travels from air into water, which of its properties (speed, frequency, wavelength, amplitude) would change, and which would remain constant? Why?
