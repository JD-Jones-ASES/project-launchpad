Seafloor Spreading: A Study Guide

Overview

This section details how new discoveries about the ocean floor in the mid-20th century provided the crucial mechanism for continental drift. It introduces Harry Hess's seafloor spreading hypothesis, explaining how new oceanic crust is created at mid-ocean ridges and pushed outward. The section then outlines the three key lines of evidence—magnetic striping, the age of the ocean floor, and sediment thickness—that confirmed seafloor spreading and laid the foundation for the unifying theory of plate tectonics.

Key Concepts

  • Continental Drift's Missing Mechanism: Before seafloor spreading, geologists lacked an explanation for how continents moved, despite evidence suggesting they did.
  • Discovery of Mid-Ocean Ridges: Advanced sonar technology revealed complex underwater topography, including vast, globe-encircling mountain ranges called mid-ocean ridges, which became central to understanding crustal movement.
  • Hess's Seafloor Spreading Hypothesis: Proposed that the ocean floor is continually created at mid-ocean ridges as magma rises and solidifies, then moves away from the ridge crest like a conveyor belt, carrying continents with it.
  • Mantle Convection as the Driver: Seafloor spreading connected the observed movement of the surface to the slow circulation of rock deep within the Earth, known as mantle convection.
  • Evidence for Seafloor Spreading: Three main lines of evidence confirmed the hypothesis:
    • Magnetic Striping: Symmetrical patterns of alternating magnetic polarity in ocean floor rocks parallel to mid-ocean ridges.
    • Age of the Ocean Floor: Oceanic crust is youngest at mid-ocean ridges and progressively older farther away.
    • Sediment Thickness: Sediment layers are thinnest near mid-ocean ridges and thicken with increasing distance.
  • Foundation of Plate Tectonics: Seafloor spreading provided the essential mechanism that, combined with continental drift and other geological data, led to the comprehensive theory of plate tectonics, unifying various Earth processes.

Important Terms

  • Mid-ocean ridge: A long, underwater mountain range that forms along a divergent plate boundary where tectonic plates are moving apart. New oceanic crust is created at mid-ocean ridges as magma rises from the mantle and solidifies.
  • Rift: A narrow valley found at the crest of a mid-ocean ridge, indicating where the ridge is actively splitting apart.
  • Seafloor spreading: The process by which new oceanic crust is created at mid-ocean ridges as magma rises from the mantle, solidifies, and pushes older crust outward in both directions. This process was proposed by Harry Hess in 1962.
  • Basalt: A dark, dense, fine-grained volcanic rock that is the primary component of oceanic crust, formed from solidified magma at mid-ocean ridges.
  • Paleomagnetism: The study of the record of Earth's past magnetic field preserved in rocks. Paleomagnetic data from the ocean floor provided critical evidence for seafloor spreading.
  • Plate Tectonics: The comprehensive scientific theory that explains the large-scale movements of Earth's lithosphere (crust and upper mantle), driven by mantle convection, accounting for continental drift, seafloor spreading, earthquakes, volcanoes, and mountain formation.
  • Sonar: (Sound Navigation and Ranging) A technology that uses sound waves to map underwater features by measuring the time it takes for sound pulses to travel to the seafloor and back.

Detailed Notes

The Unsolved Mystery of Continental Drift

  • For decades after Alfred Wegener proposed continental drift, the idea remained controversial because no one could explain how continents moved.
  • In the 1950s and 1960s, groundbreaking discoveries about the ocean floor provided the missing mechanism.

Mapping the Ocean Floor: A New World Revealed

  • Technological Advancement: After World War II, improved sonar technology allowed scientists to create detailed maps of the ocean floor.
  • Unexpected Discoveries: The ocean floor was far from flat; it featured:
    • Towering underwater mountain chains.
    • Deep ocean trenches.
    • Vast abyssal plains.
  • Mid-Ocean Ridges: The most significant discovery was a global network of underwater mountain ranges called mid-ocean ridges.
    • Example: The Mid-Atlantic Ridge stretches over 16,000 kilometers down the center of the Atlantic Ocean.
    • At the crest of these ridges, scientists found a narrow valley, or rift, indicating a splitting apart of the ocean floor.

Harry Hess's Seafloor Spreading Hypothesis (1962)

  • The Idea: American geologist Harry Hess proposed that the ocean floor is not permanent but is constantly being created and destroyed through a process he named seafloor spreading.
  • How Seafloor Spreading Works:
    1. Magma Rises: Hot mantle material rises towards the surface beneath a mid-ocean ridge.
    2. Melting and Eruption: As it nears the surface, the rock partially melts, and the resulting magma pushes up through the rift at the ridge crest.
    3. New Crust Formation: When magma meets the cold ocean water, it rapidly solidifies into new oceanic crust, primarily composed of a dark, dense rock called basalt.
    4. Outward Movement: As more magma rises, the newly formed crust is continuously pushed outward, away from the ridge, in both directions. This is analogous to two conveyor belts moving away from the center.
    5. Continents as Passengers: Continents are embedded within these larger plates of crust and are passively carried along as the seafloor spreads.
  • Solving Wegener's Problem: Hess's hypothesis provided the crucial mechanism that Wegener's continental drift lacked. It showed that continents don't "plow" through oceanic crust but rather ride on top of moving plates.
  • Connection to Mantle Convection: Seafloor spreading linked the movement of the Earth's surface to the internal heat engine of mantle convection, which drives the slow circulation of rock deep within the Earth.

Evidence for Seafloor Spreading

Hess's hypothesis was quickly supported by three key lines of evidence:

  1. Magnetic Striping:

    • Earth's Magnetic Field Reversals: Earth's magnetic field periodically reverses, swapping the north and south magnetic poles.
    • Magnetic Record in Rocks: As magma erupts and cools at mid-ocean ridges, iron-rich minerals in the basalt align with Earth's magnetic field at that specific time, "freezing" the magnetic polarity into the rock.
    • Alternating Stripes: When the magnetic field reverses, the next batch of solidifying rock records the opposite polarity. This creates a pattern of alternating stripes of "normal" (current) and "reversed" polarity on the ocean floor.
    • Symmetry as Proof: In the early 1960s, scientists discovered these magnetic stripes using magnetometers. Crucially, the patterns were symmetrical on both sides of the mid-ocean ridges, providing strong confirmation that new crust was forming at the center and moving outward evenly.
    • Paleomagnetism: The study of this preserved magnetic record in rocks is called paleomagnetism.

  2. Age of the Ocean Floor:

    • Prediction: If new crust forms at the ridge and moves outward, the youngest oceanic crust should be found at the ridge crest, and the age should increase with distance from the ridge.
    • Confirmation: Scientists drilled into the ocean floor and dated rock samples at various distances from mid-ocean ridges. The results perfectly matched the prediction: crust near the ridges is very young (a few million years), while crust near continental margins is much older (up to about 200 million years).
    • Limited Age of Oceanic Crust: No oceanic crust older than approximately 200 million years has been found, which contrasts sharply with continental rocks that can be over 4 billion years old. This implies that old oceanic crust is recycled.

  3. Sediment Thickness:

    • Prediction: Following the age pattern, areas with younger crust would have had less time for sediment to accumulate, so sediment should be thinnest near the mid-ocean ridges and progressively thicker farther away.
    • Confirmation: Deep-sea drilling confirmed this: sediment layers are extremely thin or absent near mid-ocean ridges but can be hundreds of meters thick near continental margins.

From Hypothesis to Theory: Plate Tectonics

  • Overwhelming Evidence: The evidence for seafloor spreading was undeniable, providing the long-sought mechanism for continental drift.
  • Unified Theory: By the mid-1960s, scientists combined continental drift, seafloor spreading, and new data on earthquake locations and plate boundaries into a comprehensive theory: plate tectonics.
  • Impact on Earth Science: Plate tectonics transformed earth science into a unified, predictive framework, much like other major scientific theories. It explains nearly all geological processes, including earthquakes, volcanoes, mountain building, and ocean formation.
  • The Fate of Old Oceanic Crust: The absence of very old oceanic crust (beyond ~200 million years) is because it is continually recycled back into the mantle through processes like subduction (covered in Section 3). Continental crust, being less dense, resists subduction and therefore persists for billions of years at the surface.

Review Questions

  1. Before Harry Hess proposed seafloor spreading, what was the major weakness in Alfred Wegener's continental drift hypothesis, and how did discoveries about the ocean floor begin to address this weakness?
  2. Describe the process of seafloor spreading, explaining what occurs at a mid-ocean ridge. How does this continuous creation of new crust impact the movement of continents?
  3. Explain the significance of magnetic striping patterns found on the ocean floor as evidence for seafloor spreading. Why is the symmetrical nature of these stripes particularly convincing?
  4. The oldest oceanic crust on Earth is about 200 million years old, while some continental rocks are over 4 billion years old. Using your understanding of seafloor spreading, explain this significant age difference and what happens to old oceanic crust.
  5. How did the overwhelming evidence for seafloor spreading transform the understanding of Earth's dynamics and contribute to the development of the comprehensive theory of plate tectonics? What fundamental questions did it help answer?
