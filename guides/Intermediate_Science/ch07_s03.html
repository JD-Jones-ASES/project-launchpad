Chapter 7, Section 3: Plate Boundaries Study Guide

Overview

Earth's outer shell is broken into numerous tectonic plates that are constantly in motion. The boundaries where these plates meet are the most geologically active regions, responsible for earthquakes, volcanoes, mountains, and trenches. This section explains the mechanical layers of Earth involved in plate tectonics, details the three fundamental types of plate boundaries (divergent, convergent, and transform) and their associated geological features, and discusses the driving forces behind plate movement.

Key Concepts

  • Tectonic Plates: The rigid pieces of Earth's lithosphere that are in slow, constant motion across the planet's surface.
  • Lithosphere and Asthenosphere: Earth's outer layers are defined by their mechanical behavior. The lithosphere is the rigid, brittle outer shell (crust plus uppermost mantle) that breaks into plates. Beneath it lies the asthenosphere, a partially molten, plastic layer of the upper mantle that flows slowly, allowing the lithospheric plates to move.
  • Divergent Boundaries: Occur where two plates move apart. New crust is created as magma rises from the mantle to fill the gap. These boundaries form mid-ocean ridges in oceans and rift valleys on continents.
  • Convergent Boundaries: Occur where two plates move toward each other. The outcome depends on the type of crust involved:
    • Subduction: When an oceanic plate collides with another plate (either oceanic or continental), the denser oceanic plate sinks beneath the other into the mantle. This process creates deep ocean trenches, volcanic island arcs (oceanic-oceanic), or volcanic mountain ranges (oceanic-continental), along with frequent earthquakes.
    • Continental Collision: When two continental plates collide, neither subducts due to their buoyancy. Instead, the crust crumples, folds, and thickens, forming towering mountain ranges.
  • Transform Boundaries: Occur where two plates slide horizontally past each other. Crust is neither created nor destroyed, but the grinding motion along faults generates frequent and powerful earthquakes.
  • Driving Forces of Plate Tectonics: Plate movement is powered by heat from Earth's interior and involves three main mechanisms: mantle convection (dragging plates), ridge push (gravity sliding plates away from elevated ridges), and slab pull (dense subducting plates pulling the rest of the plate). Slab pull is considered the most dominant force.

Important Terms

  • Tectonic plates: Rigid pieces of Earth's lithosphere that move slowly over the asthenosphere.
  • Lithosphere: The rigid outer layer of Earth, consisting of the crust (oceanic or continental) and the uppermost portion of the mantle. It is broken into tectonic plates.
  • Asthenosphere: The partially molten, plastically deforming layer of the upper mantle beneath the lithosphere, allowing tectonic plates to move.
  • Divergent boundary: A plate boundary where two tectonic plates are moving apart from each other, leading to the creation of new crust.
  • Mid-ocean ridge: An underwater mountain range formed at a divergent boundary in an ocean basin where new oceanic crust is generated.
  • Rift valley: A long, narrow depression formed at a divergent boundary beneath a continent where the continental crust is stretching and thinning.
  • Convergent boundary: A plate boundary where two tectonic plates are moving toward each other, resulting in subduction or mountain building.
  • Subduction: The process by which a denser tectonic plate (usually oceanic) is forced beneath a less dense plate at a convergent boundary, descending into the mantle.
  • Island arc: A chain of volcanic islands formed above a subducting oceanic plate at an oceanic-oceanic convergent boundary.
  • Transform boundary: A plate boundary where two tectonic plates slide horizontally past each other, neither creating nor destroying crust.
  • Fault: A fracture or zone of fractures in the Earth's crust where blocks of rock move relative to each other, especially common at transform boundaries.
  • Mantle convection: The slow circulation of hot mantle rock that rises, spreads, cools, and sinks, exerting a drag on the base of tectonic plates.
  • Ridge push: A gravitational force that causes elevated material at mid-ocean ridges to slide downward and outward, pushing the plate away from the ridge.
  • Slab pull: The force exerted by a cold, dense subducting plate sinking into the mantle under its own weight, pulling the rest of the plate behind it.

Detailed Notes

Plate Boundaries: An Introduction

  • Earth's outer shell is not solid but fractured into approximately fifteen major tectonic plates and many smaller ones.
  • These plates are in constant, slow motion.
  • The zones where these plates interact are called plate boundaries, which are the most geologically active regions on Earth.
  • Plate boundaries are the sites of earthquakes, volcanoes, mountain ranges, and ocean trenches.
  • Understanding these interactions is central to plate tectonic theory.

The Lithosphere and the Asthenosphere

  • Earth's outer layers can be divided based on their mechanical behavior (how they respond to stress), distinct from their chemical composition (crust, mantle, core).
  • Lithosphere:
    • The rigid, brittle outer shell of Earth.
    • Includes all of the crust (oceanic and continental) and the uppermost, coolest part of the mantle.
    • When stressed, it cracks and breaks, causing earthquakes.
    • Thickness varies: ~10 km under mid-ocean ridges to over 200 km under old continents.
  • Asthenosphere:
    • Located beneath the lithosphere in the upper mantle.
    • Hot enough to behave plastically, meaning it deforms and flows slowly over time, even though it's mostly solid.
    • Allows lithospheric plates to "float" on top and be carried along by slow convection currents within the mantle.

Three Types of Plate Boundaries

Tectonic plates interact in three fundamental ways, each creating distinct geological features:

  1. Divergent Boundaries:

    • Definition: Plates move apart from each other.
    • Process: Hot mantle material rises to fill the gap, partially melts, and solidifies to form new crust. Therefore, new lithosphere is created here.
    • Examples and Features:
      • Mid-ocean ridges: Form when divergence occurs under an ocean. These are underwater mountain ranges where new oceanic crust is continuously produced, pushing plates apart at 1 to 15 cm/year (e.g., Mid-Atlantic Ridge, East Pacific Rise).
      • Rift valleys: Form when divergence occurs under a continent. These are long, narrow depressions where continental crust is stretched and thinned (e.g., East African Rift Valley). If rifting continues for millions of years, the continent can split, flood, and form a new ocean basin (like the Atlantic Ocean).

  2. Convergent Boundaries:

    • Definition: Plates move toward each other (collide).
    • Outcomes: Depend on the type of crust involved in the collision.
    • Three Scenarios:
      • Oceanic–Continental Convergence:
        • Process: Denser oceanic plate is forced beneath the lighter continental plate in a process called subduction. The oceanic plate sinks into the mantle, where it is recycled.
        • Features: Deep ocean trenches (e.g., Mariana Trench), a chain of volcanic mountains on the continental plate (as water from the subducting plate triggers mantle melting), and frequent earthquakes.
        • Example: Andes Mountains in South America, formed as the Nazca Plate subducts beneath the South American Plate.
      • Oceanic–Oceanic Convergence:
        • Process: The older, cooler, and therefore denser oceanic plate subducts beneath the younger, warmer one.
        • Features: Similar to oceanic-continental convergence – a deep trench at the boundary and a chain of volcanic islands called an island arc on the overriding plate.
        • Examples: Japan, the Philippines, and the Aleutian Islands of Alaska.
      • Continental–Continental Convergence:
        • Process: Neither continental plate can subduct because both are too buoyant (low density). Instead, the crust crumples, folds, and thickens, pushing rock upward.
        • Features: Formation of towering mountain ranges.
        • Example: The Himalayan Mountains, still rising today as the Indian Plate collides with the Eurasian Plate over millions of years.

  3. Transform Boundaries:

    • Definition: Plates slide horizontally past each other.
    • Process: No crust is created or destroyed. The plates grind against each other along a large fracture called a fault.
    • Features: Because surfaces are rough, stress builds up until the rock suddenly breaks and slips, releasing energy as an earthquake.
    • Example: The San Andreas Fault in California, where the Pacific Plate slides northwestward past the North American Plate.

What Drives the Plates?

Several mechanisms, ultimately powered by heat from Earth's interior, drive plate motion:

  • Mantle convection: Hot mantle rock rises, spreads laterally beneath the lithosphere, cools, and sinks. This slow circulation creates a dragging force on the bottom of the plates.
  • Ridge push: At mid-ocean ridges, newly formed crust is elevated because the underlying rock is hot and expanded. Gravity causes this elevated material to slide downward and outward away from the ridge, pushing the plate.
  • Slab pull: At subduction zones, the cold, dense edge of a subducting plate sinks into the mantle under its own weight, effectively pulling the rest of the plate behind it. Most geophysicists consider slab pull to be the dominant force driving plate motion today.
  • Combined Forces: All three mechanisms work together, with their relative importance varying by plate. For instance, the Pacific Plate, surrounded by subduction zones, moves quickly (6–10 cm/year), while the African Plate, with fewer subducting edges, moves more slowly (about 2 cm/year).

Plate Tectonics and Life Science

  • Plate tectonics significantly influences the history of life on Earth.
  • When continents drift apart, populations of organisms become geographically isolated.
  • Over millions of years, these isolated populations evolve independently, leading to the formation of new, unique species.
  • Example: Australia's distinctive wildlife (kangaroos, koalas) resulted from the continent's isolation for tens of millions of years, allowing its marsupial mammals to evolve separately.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental difference between the lithosphere and the asthenosphere in terms of their mechanical properties and how this difference is crucial for the movement of tectonic plates.
  2. Describe the three primary types of plate boundaries (divergent, convergent, transform), including the relative motion of plates at each. For each boundary type, provide a real-world geological example and describe two characteristic geological features.
  3. Discuss the three possible scenarios for convergent plate boundaries: oceanic-continental, oceanic-oceanic, and continental-continental. Explain why the geological outcomes differ in each case, focusing on the role of crustal density and buoyancy.
  4. Identify and describe the three main forces that geophysicists believe drive the motion of tectonic plates. Which of these forces is generally considered the most significant, and why?
  5. Imagine a continent undergoing rifting at a divergent boundary. Describe the initial geological features that would form. If this rifting continues for tens of millions of years, what long-term geological changes would you expect to see in the region?
