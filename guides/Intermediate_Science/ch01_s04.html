Chapter 1, Section 4: Cell Division

Overview

This section explains cell division, the fundamental process by which one cell splits into two. It covers why cells divide, the stages of the cell cycle (interphase and mitotic phase), the detailed steps of mitosis and cytokinesis, and what happens when this carefully controlled process malfunctions, leading to cancer.

Key Concepts

  • Purpose of Cell Division: Cells divide to maintain an efficient surface-area-to-volume ratio, replace damaged/dead cells, and enable growth and development. Each division must copy DNA and divide cytoplasm/organelles.
  • The Cell Cycle: The ordered sequence of events from a cell's formation to its division. It consists of two main stages: interphase (growth and DNA replication) and the mitotic phase (nuclear and cytoplasmic division).
  • Interphase: The longest stage where the cell grows, performs normal functions, and prepares for division. It has three sub-phases:
    • G$_1$ phase: Cell grows, produces proteins/organelles, carries out functions.
    • S phase: DNA synthesis occurs; each chromosome replicates into two identical sister chromatids joined at a centromere.
    • G$_2$ phase: Cell continues to grow, produces proteins for division, and checks DNA replication.
  • Chromosomes: Compact structures of DNA tightly coiled around histone proteins. They organize genetic material for division. Human cells have 46 chromosomes.
  • Mitosis (Nuclear Division): The process where the nucleus divides into two genetically identical daughter nuclei, ensuring each new cell gets a complete DNA set. It has four phases (PMAT):
    • Prophase: Chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes (sister chromatids), mitotic spindle forms, nuclear envelope breaks down, nucleolus disappears.
    • Metaphase: Chromosomes line up at the metaphase plate, and spindle fibers attach to centromeres.
    • Anaphase: Sister chromatids separate and are pulled to opposite poles of the cell, becoming individual chromosomes.
    • Telophase: Chromosomes uncoil, new nuclear envelopes form around each set, nucleoli reappear, spindle disassembles, resulting in two distinct nuclei.
  • Cytokinesis (Cytoplasmic Division): Follows mitosis, dividing the cytoplasm and organelles to form two separate daughter cells.
    • Animal Cells: A cleavage furrow forms by contracting microfilaments, pinching the cell in two.
    • Plant Cells: A cell plate forms from Golgi vesicles, growing outward to create a new cell wall between the daughter cells.
  • Cancer: Occurs when cell cycle control mechanisms fail due to mutations, leading to uncontrolled cell growth and division. Malignant tumors can metastasize (spread) throughout the body, making cancer life-threatening. Carcinogens are environmental factors that damage DNA and can cause mutations.

Important Terms

  • Cell division: The process by which one cell splits into two, essential for growth, repair, and reproduction.
  • Cell cycle: The ordered sequence of events that a cell goes through from its formation until it divides into two new daughter cells.
  • Interphase: The longest phase of the cell cycle, during which the cell grows, carries out its normal functions, and copies its DNA in preparation for division.
  • G$_1$ phase (first gap): Sub-phase of interphase where the cell grows, produces new proteins and organelles, and performs normal functions.
  • S phase (synthesis): Sub-phase of interphase where the cell copies (replicates) all of its DNA.
  • G$_2$ phase (second gap): Sub-phase of interphase where the cell continues to grow and produces specific proteins needed for cell division.
  • Sister chromatids: Two identical copies of a chromosome, joined together by a centromere, formed during S phase.
  • Centromere: The constricted region where sister chromatids are joined.
  • Chromosome: A structure made of DNA tightly coiled around proteins called histones, carrying genetic information.
  • DNA (Deoxyribonucleic acid): A long, double helix molecule that carries the genetic instructions for building proteins and controlling cell functions.
  • Histones: Proteins around which DNA coils tightly to form chromosomes.
  • Mitosis: The process by which a cell's nucleus divides into two genetically identical nuclei.
  • Mitotic spindle: A structure made of microtubules that forms during prophase and helps separate chromosomes during mitosis.
  • Centrioles: Small organelles in animal cells from which the mitotic spindle grows.
  • Nuclear envelope: The double membrane surrounding the nucleus, which breaks down during prophase and reforms during telophase.
  • Nucleolus: A dense structure within the nucleus, involved in ribosome synthesis, which disappears in prophase and reappears in telophase.
  • Metaphase plate: An imaginary line along the middle of the cell where chromosomes align during metaphase.
  • Cytokinesis: The division of the cytoplasm following mitosis, resulting in two separate daughter cells.
  • Cleavage furrow: An inward pinch in animal cells formed by contracting microfilaments during cytokinesis.
  • Cell plate: A structure formed from Golgi vesicles in plant cells that grows outward to become a new cell wall during cytokinesis.
  • Daughter cells: The two genetically identical cells produced from one parent cell after cell division.
  • Cancer: A group of diseases characterized by the uncontrolled growth and division of abnormal cells due to failures in cell cycle regulation.
  • Tumor: A mass of abnormal cells.
  • Benign tumor: A non-cancerous tumor that stays in one place and does not invade surrounding tissues.
  • Malignant tumor: A cancerous tumor that can invade nearby tissues and spread to other parts of the body.
  • Metastasis: The process by which cancer cells spread from their original site to other parts of the body.
  • Apoptosis: Programmed cell death, a self-destruct mechanism triggered by the cell when problems like DNA damage cannot be fixed.
  • Carcinogens: Environmental factors (substances or agents) that damage DNA and can cause cancer.

Detailed Notes

The Importance of Cell Division

  • Growth and Development: From a single fertilized egg to trillions of cells.
  • Repair and Replacement: Replaces damaged cells (e.g., skin cuts) and worn-out cells (e.g., blood cells).
  • Surface-Area-to-Volume Ratio: As a cell grows, its volume increases much faster than its surface area. A large cell cannot transport nutrients in or waste out efficiently enough, so dividing into smaller cells restores a favorable ratio.
  • Key Tasks: Every cell division must:
    1. Copy all DNA for a complete genetic set.
    2. Divide cytoplasm and organelles for functional new cells.

The Cell Cycle

  • Definition: The entire life sequence of a cell from its formation to its division.
  • Two Main Stages:
    1. Interphase: Growth and preparation for division.
    2. Mitotic Phase: Actual cell division (mitosis and cytokinesis).

Interphase: The Preparation Stage

  • Longest phase (approx. 90% of a cell's life).
  • Cell is very active, performing specialized functions, growing, and preparing.
  • Three Sub-phases:
    • G$_1$ phase (First Gap):
      • Cell grows in size.
      • Produces new proteins and organelles.
      • Carries out normal metabolic functions.
      • Longest sub-phase.
      • Checkpoint: Cell assesses conditions for division before proceeding.
    • S phase (Synthesis):
      • DNA replication occurs: entire genome is copied.
      • Before S phase: Each chromosome is a single DNA molecule.
      • After S phase: Each chromosome consists of two identical sister chromatids joined at a centromere.
    • G$_2$ phase (Second Gap):
      • Cell continues to grow.
      • Produces proteins specifically needed for cell division (e.g., tubulin for microtubules).
      • Checkpoint: Ensures DNA replication was completed correctly.

Chromosomes: Packages of DNA

  • DNA: Genetic instructions (double helix) in the nucleus.
  • Chromatin: Loosely coiled DNA and proteins (histones) during most of interphase.
  • Chromosomes: Highly condensed structures of DNA wrapped tightly around histones. This condensation makes DNA manageable for division.
  • Chromosome Number: Varies by species (e.g., human body cells have 46, or 23 pairs). Number doesn't equate to complexity.

Mitosis: Dividing the Nucleus

  • Ensures two genetically identical daughter nuclei, each with an exact copy of the parent cell's DNA.
  • Divided into four phases (PMAT):
    1. Prophase:
      • Chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes (each with two sister chromatids).
      • Mitotic spindle (microtubules) begins to form from centrioles (in animal cells) at opposite poles.
      • Nuclear envelope breaks down.
      • Nucleolus disappears.
    2. Metaphase:
      • Chromosomes line up along the metaphase plate (equator of the cell).
      • Spindle fibers attach to the centromere of each chromosome from opposite poles.
      • Checkpoint: Verifies proper attachment of all chromosomes to spindle fibers.
    3. Anaphase:
      • Shortest, most dramatic phase.
      • Centromeres split, separating sister chromatids.
      • Spindle fibers pull individual chromosomes toward opposite poles.
      • By the end, each pole has a complete, identical set of chromosomes.
    4. Telophase:
      • Reverse of prophase.
      • Chromosomes arrive at poles and begin to uncoil back into chromatin.
      • New nuclear envelope forms around each set of chromosomes, creating two distinct nuclei.
      • Nucleoli reappear.
      • Mitotic spindle disassembles.

Cytokinesis: Dividing the Cytoplasm

  • Usually begins during telophase.
  • Completes the creation of two separate daughter cells.
  • In Animal Cells:
    • Cleavage furrow forms: a ring of microfilaments beneath the cell membrane contracts, pinching the cell inward until it divides.
  • In Plant Cells:
    • Rigid cell wall prevents pinching.
    • Cell plate forms: vesicles from the Golgi apparatus fuse in the middle of the cell, growing outward to become a new cell wall and separating the cells.
  • Result: Two genetically identical daughter cells, each with a full set of chromosomes, DNA, and a share of organelles/cytoplasm.

When Cell Division Goes Wrong: Cancer

  • Cell Cycle Regulation: Controlled by molecular signals and checkpoints (checking for DNA damage, replication completion, spindle attachment).
  • Apoptosis: Programmed cell death if problems are irreparable.
  • Cancer Cause: Mutations in genes that regulate the cell cycle cause cells to ignore stop signals and divide uncontrollably.
  • Tumors: Masses of abnormal cells.
    • Benign Tumor: Stays in one place, not invasive.
    • Malignant Tumor: Can invade surrounding tissues and metastasize (spread via blood/lymph) to other parts of the body, making it life-threatening.
  • Nature of Cancer: Not one disease, but over 200, each arising from different mutations.
  • Carcinogens: Environmental factors (e.g., tobacco smoke, UV radiation, certain chemicals, viruses) that damage DNA and contribute to mutations.
  • Cancer Treatments: Many target the cell cycle (e.g., chemotherapy stopping spindle formation, radiation damaging DNA of rapidly dividing cells).

Review Questions

  1. Explain why cells divide instead of simply growing larger. What key ratio is involved, and why is it important for cell function?
  2. Describe the main stages of the cell cycle and briefly explain the primary activities that occur in each of the three sub-phases of interphase.
  3. Outline the four phases of mitosis (PMAT) in order, describing the key events that occur in each phase. How does mitosis ensure that daughter cells are genetically identical to the parent cell?
  4. Compare and contrast cytokinesis in animal cells versus plant cells. What structural differences in these cell types necessitate different mechanisms for cytoplasmic division?
  5. What is cancer, and how is it related to the cell cycle? Explain the difference between a benign and a malignant tumor, and why metastasis is a critical factor in the severity of cancer.
