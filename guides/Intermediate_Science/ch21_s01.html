Evidence for Evolution: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the compelling evidence for evolution drawn from various scientific disciplines. From the preserved remains of ancient life to the intricate details of our DNA, multiple lines of inquiry converge to demonstrate that all living species on Earth share a common ancestry and have changed significantly over vast spans of time.

Key Concepts

  • Evolution: The fundamental concept that explains the change in the inherited characteristics of populations over successive generations. It accounts for both the immense diversity of life and the deep similarities shared by all organisms, stemming from a common ancestor.
  • Common Ancestry: The idea that all living organisms on Earth are descended from shared ancestors over billions of years. This forms the unifying principle behind the shared characteristics observed across different species.
  • The Fossil Record: A chronological archive of life on Earth, made up of preserved remains or traces of past organisms. It directly shows how life forms have changed and diversified over millions of years, revealing patterns like increasing complexity, transitional forms, and widespread extinction.
  • Homologous Structures: Body parts in different species that are similar in underlying structure because they were inherited from a common ancestor, even if they now serve different functions. They are strong indicators of evolutionary relationships.
  • Analogous Structures & Convergent Evolution: Analogous structures perform similar functions (e.g., wings for flight) but evolved independently in unrelated organisms. This occurs through convergent evolution, where different species facing similar environmental challenges evolve similar features.
  • Vestigial Structures: Body structures that are reduced in size or function compared to their counterparts in ancestral species. They are remnants of evolutionary history and provide evidence of change over time.
  • Embryology: The study of early development, which reveals remarkable similarities among the embryos of different vertebrate species, suggesting a shared developmental program inherited from a common ancestor.
  • Molecular Biology: The study of life at the molecular level, providing evidence through the universality of the genetic code and the comparison of DNA and protein sequences. Greater similarity indicates a more recent common ancestor.
  • Biogeography: The study of the geographic distribution of species. It demonstrates how species distributions are influenced by evolutionary history, showing patterns like island species resembling nearby mainland species and isolated continents fostering unique forms of life.

Important Terms

  • Evolution: The change in the inherited characteristics of a population over successive generations.
  • Fossil: The preserved remains or traces of an organism that lived in the past.
  • Fossil Record: The total collection of fossils that have been discovered, providing a chronological archive of life's history on Earth.
  • Homologous Structure: A body part that is similar in structure across different species because it was inherited from a common ancestor, even though its function may differ.
  • Analogous Structure: A body part that performs a similar function in different species but has a different evolutionary origin and internal structure, resulting from convergent evolution.
  • Convergent Evolution: The process by which unrelated species evolve similar features or traits because they have to adapt to similar environmental challenges or ecological niches.
  • Vestigial Structure: A body structure that is reduced in size or function compared to the same structure in an ancestral species, providing evidence of evolutionary change.
  • Radiometric Dating: A method used by geologists to determine the absolute age of fossils and the rocks that contain them, based on the decay of radioactive isotopes.
  • Biogeography: The study of where organisms live and why, examining the geographic distribution of species as evidence for evolutionary processes.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Evidence for Evolution

  • Evolution explains that all living species are connected by a shared family history, similar to how family members share resemblances across generations.
  • Evidence for evolution comes from many independent scientific fields, including paleontology (fossils), anatomy, molecular biology (DNA), and biogeography.
  • All evidence consistently points to the conclusion that life on Earth has changed over time, and all species alive today descended from earlier forms.
  • Definition of Evolution: The change in the inherited characteristics of a population over successive generations. It explains both the vast diversity of life and the fundamental similarities shared by all organisms.
  • Shared features among living things (like being made of cells, using energy, growth, reproduction) are not coincidental but are results of common ancestry and descent over billions of years.

The Fossil Record

  • Fossils: Any preserved evidence of a past organism. This includes mineralized bones, shells, impressions (molds and casts), preserved footprints, and organisms trapped in amber or ice.
  • Fossil Record: The collective body of fossils forms a chronological archive of life on Earth, providing direct evidence of how life has changed.
  • Key Patterns Revealed by the Fossil Record:
    • Increasing Complexity Over Time: Older (deeper) rock layers contain simpler, often single-celled organisms. More complex organisms (fish, amphibians, reptiles, mammals) appear in progressively younger (shallower) layers.
    • Transitional Fossils: These fossils show intermediate characteristics between different groups, bridging evolutionary gaps. Tiktaalik, for example, exhibits features of both fish (scales, fins) and land-dwelling amphibians (flat skull, neck, limb-like fin bones), representing a crucial transition from water to land.
    • Extinction is Common: The fossil record documents that over 99% of all species that have ever lived are now extinct, illustrating that species thrive for millions of years and then disappear.
  • Earth Science Connection: Radiometric Dating: Geologists use methods like carbon-14 dating (for younger fossils, up to ~50,000 years) and potassium-argon dating (for much older rocks/fossils, millions to billions of years) to determine the absolute age of fossils. These chemistry-based methods are crucial for establishing the timeline of life.

Comparative Anatomy

  • Homologous Structures: These are body parts that share a similar basic structure across different species because they were inherited from a common ancestor, even if they have evolved to perform different functions.
    • Example: The forelimbs of humans, cats, whales, and bats all share the same underlying bone structure (humerus, radius, ulna, wrist bones, digits), despite being used for grasping, running, swimming, and flying, respectively.
    • This similarity strongly indicates that these species descended from a common ancestor with that basic limb structure, which was then modified by natural selection over time.
  • Analogous Structures and Convergent Evolution:
    • Analogous Structures: Structures that perform similar functions but evolved independently in unrelated organisms and have different underlying structures.
      • Example: The wings of a bird and the wings of a butterfly both allow for flight, but they have entirely different internal structures and developmental origins.
    • Convergent Evolution: This is the evolutionary process where unrelated species develop similar features because they face similar environmental pressures or occupy similar ecological niches. Analogous structures are a result of convergent evolution.
  • Vestigial Structures: These are structures that have become reduced in size or function over evolutionary time, often to the point of being non-functional. They are remnants of structures that were functional in ancestral species.
    • Examples: The human appendix, the tiny leg bones found inside some snakes, and the non-functional eyes of cave-dwelling fish.
    • Vestigial structures provide strong evidence that organisms have changed over time, retaining features from their ancestors that are no longer necessary or fully functional in their current environment.

Embryology

  • The early embryonic stages of different vertebrate species (fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, mammals) show remarkable similarities.
  • Examples: All vertebrate embryos develop pharyngeal pouches (which become gills in fish but ear structures and glands in mammals) and tails (which shrink or disappear in humans before birth).
  • These shared embryonic features suggest that vertebrates inherited a common developmental program from a shared ancestor.

Molecular Biology

  • Perhaps the most compelling evidence comes from the molecular level.
  • Universal Genetic Code: All living organisms use the same genetic code – the same four nucleotide bases (A, T, C, G) and the same system of codons to build proteins. This universality is a powerful indicator that all life descended from a single common ancestor.
  • DNA and Protein Sequence Comparisons: Scientists compare the DNA sequences (genes) and protein sequences across different species.
    • The more similar the sequences are, the more recently the two species shared a common ancestor.
    • Examples: Human and chimpanzee DNA differ by only about 1.2%, indicating a very recent common ancestor. Humans and yeast share roughly 30% of their genes, reflecting a much more ancient divergence, but still remarkable evidence of common ancestry.

Biogeography

  • Biogeography: The study of where organisms live and why, examining the geographic distribution of species.
  • Evidence Patterns:
    • Island Species: Species found on islands often resemble species on the nearest mainland but have evolved distinct adaptations suited to their island environments. For example, the Galápagos finches are similar to South American finches but have diverse beak shapes adapted to different food sources on different islands.
    • Isolated Continents: Continents that have been isolated for long periods develop unique species. Australia's marsupials (kangaroos, koalas) evolved in isolation, filling ecological roles occupied by placental mammals on other continents.
    • Similar Environments, Unrelated Species: Similar environments in geographically separate regions can lead to the evolution of similar-looking but unrelated species (another example of convergent evolution). For instance, cacti in the Americas and euphorbia in Africa both thrive in deserts and have similar adaptations to conserve water, despite being distantly related.

Review Questions

  1. Explain how the fossil record provides strong evidence for evolution. Be sure to describe at least two specific patterns or types of fossils found in the record that support this claim.
  2. Differentiate between homologous and analogous structures, providing a clear example for each. How do both types of structures contribute to our understanding of evolutionary processes?
  3. Describe how molecular biology, specifically the comparison of DNA and protein sequences, serves as compelling evidence for common ancestry among all living organisms.
  4. What are vestigial structures, and what can their presence tell us about an organism's evolutionary history? Provide an example of a vestigial structure in humans or another animal.
  5. How does the study of biogeography support the theory of evolution? Use examples of island species or organisms on isolated continents to illustrate your answer.
