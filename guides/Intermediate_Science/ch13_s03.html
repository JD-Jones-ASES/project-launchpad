Covalent Bonds Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces covalent bonds, which form when two nonmetal atoms share electrons to achieve stable electron configurations. It explores different types of covalent bonds (single, double, triple), how to represent them using Lewis structures, and the concepts of bond polarity influenced by electronegativity. Finally, it provides an introduction to the VSEPR model for predicting molecular shapes and how shape impacts overall molecular polarity.

Key Concepts

  • Covalent Bonding: A type of chemical bond formed between two nonmetal atoms that have similar electronegativities, leading them to share one or more pairs of electrons rather than transferring them. This sharing allows both atoms to achieve a stable electron configuration (like a filled outer shell).
  • Electron Sharing for Stability: Atoms share electrons to satisfy the "duet rule" (for hydrogen, needing 2 valence electrons) or the "octet rule" (for most other atoms, needing 8 valence electrons), effectively "seeing" a full outer shell.
  • Types of Covalent Bonds: Covalent bonds can be classified by the number of shared electron pairs:
    • Single bond: One shared pair (2 electrons).
    • Double bond: Two shared pairs (4 electrons).
    • Triple bond: Three shared pairs (6 electrons).
    • Triple bonds are generally shorter and stronger than double bonds, which are in turn shorter and stronger than single bonds.
  • Lewis Structures: A visual representation of a molecule that shows all valence electrons, with lines representing bonding pairs and dots representing non-bonding (lone) pairs. They are crucial for understanding electron distribution and predicting bonding patterns.
  • Polarity of Covalent Bonds:
    • Electronegativity: The measure of an atom's ability to attract shared electrons in a bond. It increases across a period and up a group.
    • Nonpolar Covalent Bond: Forms when electrons are shared equally or nearly equally between atoms with similar electronegativities (e.g., between identical atoms).
    • Polar Covalent Bond: Forms when electrons are shared unequally between atoms with different electronegativities, creating partial positive (δ+) and partial negative (δ-) charges on the atoms.
  • Molecular Shape (VSEPR Model): The Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) model predicts the three-dimensional arrangement of atoms in a molecule. It states that electron pairs (both bonding and lone pairs) around a central atom will repel each other and arrange themselves as far apart as possible to minimize repulsion. Molecular shape is critical because it influences a molecule's overall polarity and its interactions.

Important Terms

  • Covalent bond: A bond formed when two atoms share one or more pairs of electrons, typically between two nonmetals with similar electronegativities.
  • Lewis structure: A diagram that shows all valence electrons in a molecule, using dots for lone pairs and lines for bonding pairs. Also called an electron dot structure.
  • Polar covalent bond: A covalent bond in which electrons are shared unequally because the atoms have different electronegativities, resulting in partial positive and negative charges on the bonded atoms.
  • Nonpolar covalent bond: A covalent bond in which electrons are shared equally or nearly equally because the atoms have similar electronegativities.
  • Electronegativity: A measure of how strongly an atom in a bond attracts the shared electrons toward itself.
  • VSEPR model (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion): A model that predicts molecular shapes based on the principle that electron pairs around a central atom repel each other and arrange themselves as far apart as possible.

Detailed Notes

Covalent Bonds: Sharing is Caring

Unlike ionic bonds where electrons are transferred, covalent bonds involve the sharing of electrons. This occurs when two nonmetal atoms with similar electronegativities meet, meaning neither atom is strong enough to completely pull electrons away from the other. By sharing electrons, both atoms can effectively "see" a full outer electron shell, achieving greater stability. The shared electrons are simultaneously attracted to both nuclei, which holds the atoms together.

Electron Sharing and the Octet/Duet Rule

  • Hydrogen Example: A single hydrogen atom has one electron and needs two to fill its first energy level (the duet rule). When two hydrogen atoms bond, they each contribute one electron to a shared pair. This shared pair counts for both atoms, giving each hydrogen atom two electrons and a stable configuration (H-H or H$_2$).
  • Chlorine Example: A chlorine atom has seven valence electrons and needs one more to complete its octet. Two chlorine atoms can each contribute one electron to a shared pair, allowing both to achieve a stable octet (Cl-Cl or Cl$_2$).

Single, Double, and Triple Bonds

Atoms can share more than one pair of electrons:

  • Single Bond: Involves one shared pair of electrons (2 electrons total). Represented by a single line (e.g., H-H in H$_2$).
  • Double Bond: Involves two shared pairs of electrons (4 electrons total). Represented by a double line (e.g., O=O in O$_2$). Each oxygen atom has 6 valence electrons; sharing two pairs gives each atom an octet.
  • Triple Bond: Involves three shared pairs of electrons (6 electrons total). Represented by a triple line (e.g., N#N in N$_2$). Each nitrogen atom has 5 valence electrons; sharing three pairs completes both octets.

Bond Strength and Length: Generally, the more electron pairs shared, the stronger and shorter the bond. A triple bond is the strongest and shortest, followed by a double bond, then a single bond. This explains why nitrogen gas (N$_2$) is extremely stable due to its strong triple bond.

Lewis Structures

A Lewis structure (or electron dot structure) is a diagram that visually represents all valence electrons in a molecule. Bonding pairs are shown as lines between atoms, and non-bonding electrons (lone pairs) are shown as dots.

Steps to Draw a Lewis Structure:

  1. Count Total Valence Electrons: Sum the valence electrons from all atoms in the molecule.
  2. Place Central Atom: Put the least electronegative atom in the center. (Hydrogen is always an outer atom).
  3. Connect Outer Atoms: Draw single bonds (lines) from the central atom to each outer atom. Each single bond uses 2 electrons.
  4. Distribute Remaining Electrons: Place remaining electrons as lone pairs on the outer atoms first, making sure they achieve octets (or duets for hydrogen). Then, place any leftover electrons as lone pairs on the central atom to complete its octet.
  5. Form Multiple Bonds (if needed): If the central atom still lacks an octet after distributing all electrons, convert one or more lone pairs from an outer atom into double or triple bonds with the central atom.

Worked Example: Drawing the Lewis Structure for Water (H$_2$O)

  1. Count valence electrons: Oxygen has 6, each hydrogen has 1. Total: 6 + 1 + 1 = 8 valence electrons.
  2. Place central atom: Oxygen is in the center (hydrogen is always outer).
  3. Connect atoms: Connect each hydrogen to oxygen with a single bond. This uses 2 * 2 = 4 electrons.
  4. Distribute remaining electrons: 8 - 4 = 4 electrons remain. Place these as two lone pairs on the oxygen atom, completing its octet.
  5. Check: Oxygen now has 8 electrons (2 bonding pairs + 2 lone pairs). Each hydrogen has 2 electrons (1 bonding pair). All atoms are stable. The Lewis structure shows two O-H bonds and two lone pairs on oxygen.

Polar and Nonpolar Covalent Bonds

Not all electron sharing is equal.

  • Electronegativity: This property measures an atom's attraction for shared electrons in a bond. Fluorine is the most electronegative element, and electronegativity generally increases from left to right across a period and from bottom to top within a group on the periodic table.
  • Nonpolar Covalent Bond: Forms when two atoms with very similar (or identical) electronegativities share electrons equally. Examples include bonds between identical atoms (H-H, Cl-Cl) or between atoms with very small electronegativity differences (like C-H).
  • Polar Covalent Bond: Forms when two atoms with different electronegativities share electrons unequally. The more electronegative atom pulls the shared electrons closer to itself, developing a partial negative charge (δ-), while the less electronegative atom develops a partial positive charge (δ+). For example, in water, oxygen is more electronegative than hydrogen, so the O-H bonds are polar, with oxygen having a δ- charge and hydrogens having δ+ charges.

Electronegativity Difference and Bond Type:

  • Difference ~0: Nonpolar Covalent
  • Small to Moderate Difference: Polar Covalent
  • Large Difference: Ionic

Bonding exists on a continuum, from perfectly equal sharing (nonpolar covalent) to complete electron transfer (ionic), with polar covalent bonds in between.

Molecular Shape: Introduction to VSEPR

A molecule's three-dimensional shape is crucial, influencing its polarity, biological activity, and interactions with other molecules. The VSEPR model (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion) is a simple theory that predicts molecular shapes based on the idea that electron pairs (both bonding pairs and lone pairs) around a central atom will repel each other and arrange themselves as far apart as possible to minimize these repulsions.

Key Molecular Shapes and Electron Groups:

  • Linear: 2 electron groups around the central atom (e.g., CO$_2$).
  • Trigonal Planar: 3 electron groups around the central atom (e.g., BF$_3$).
  • Tetrahedral: 4 electron groups around the central atom (e.g., CH$_4$).
  • Bent: 4 electron groups around the central atom, but some are lone pairs (e.g., H$_2$O, which has 2 bonding pairs and 2 lone pairs, forcing a bent shape).
  • Trigonal Pyramidal: 4 electron groups around the central atom, but one is a lone pair (e.g., NH$_3$, which has 3 bonding pairs and 1 lone pair).

Important Note on Lone Pairs: Lone pairs count as electron groups and exert more repulsion than bonding pairs, significantly affecting the molecular geometry. For example, water (H$_2$O) has four electron groups around its central oxygen atom (two bonding pairs with hydrogen and two lone pairs). These four groups arrange tetrahedrally, but because two are lone pairs, the resulting molecular shape (the arrangement of atoms) is bent, not linear. This bent shape is why water is a polar molecule, as its partial charges do not cancel out.

Gilbert N. Lewis (1875–1946)

Gilbert N. Lewis was a pioneering American physical chemist who, in 1916, proposed that chemical bonds form by atoms sharing electron pairs. This fundamental insight laid the groundwork for modern covalent bonding theory. His simple Lewis dot structures are still indispensable tools in chemistry. Lewis also made significant contributions to thermodynamics and acid-base theory, though famously, he was never awarded the Nobel Prize despite numerous nominations.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental principle behind the formation of a covalent bond, contrasting it with how an ionic bond is formed. What types of atoms typically form covalent bonds?
  2. Differentiate between single, double, and triple covalent bonds in terms of the number of shared electrons, relative bond strength, and bond length. Provide a chemical example for each type of bond.
  3. Outline the systematic steps involved in drawing a Lewis structure for a molecule. As an example, draw the Lewis structure for Ammonia (NH$_3$).
  4. Define electronegativity and explain how the difference in electronegativity between two bonded atoms determines whether a covalent bond is nonpolar or polar. Give a real-world example of each bond type.
  5. Why is molecular shape important in chemistry? Using the VSEPR model, explain why the carbon dioxide (CO$_2$) molecule is nonpolar despite having polar C=O bonds, while the water (H$_2$O) molecule is polar even though it also has polar O-H bonds.
