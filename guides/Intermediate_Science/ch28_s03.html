Biochemistry: The Molecules of Life

Overview

Biochemistry is the study of the chemical processes and substances that occur within living organisms. This section introduces the four major classes of carbon-based organic molecules — carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids — which are essential for life. It explores how these crucial molecules are built from smaller units and broken down, and details their diverse structures and functions in cells, from energy storage and structural support to genetic information and metabolic catalysis.

Key Concepts

  • Biochemistry: The study of the chemical composition and processes of living organisms, bridging chemistry and biology.
  • Monomers and Polymers: Many large biological molecules (polymers) are built from repeating smaller units (monomers).
  • Dehydration Synthesis (Condensation Reaction): A chemical reaction that joins two monomers by removing a molecule of water, forming a new covalent bond. This process builds polymers.
  • Hydrolysis: A chemical reaction that breaks a covalent bond between two monomers by adding a molecule of water. This process disassembles polymers.
  • Carbohydrates: Organic compounds (C, H, O in a 1:2:1 ratio) that serve as primary energy sources and structural components.
    • Monosaccharides: Simple sugars like glucose, fructose, galactose (monomers).
    • Disaccharides: Two monosaccharides joined (e.g., sucrose, lactose).
    • Polysaccharides: Long chains of monosaccharides (e.g., starch, glycogen, cellulose) with different functions based on their glucose linkage.
  • Lipids: A diverse group of nonpolar, water-insoluble (hydrophobic) organic compounds. They function in long-term energy storage, membrane structure, and signaling.
    • Fats and Oils: Composed of glycerol and three fatty acid chains. Can be saturated (solid at room temperature) or unsaturated (liquid at room temperature).
    • Phospholipids: Key components of cell membranes, featuring a hydrophilic head and two hydrophobic tails.
  • Proteins: Highly diverse polymers of amino acids, performing a vast array of functions including enzymes, structural support, transport, and immunity.
    • Amino Acids: The 20 different monomers of proteins, each with a unique R-group determining its properties.
    • Peptide Bonds: Covalent bonds linking amino acids in a polypeptide chain.
    • 3D Shape and Function: The specific sequence of amino acids dictates a protein's unique three-dimensional shape, which is critical for its function.
    • Denaturation: The loss of a protein's specific 3D shape, leading to a loss of function.
  • Nucleic Acids: Polymers of nucleotide monomers responsible for storing and transmitting genetic information.
    • Nucleotides: Composed of a five-carbon sugar, a phosphate group, and a nitrogen-containing base.
    • DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid): Stores hereditary instructions, forms a double helix.
    • RNA (Ribonucleic Acid): Helps carry out genetic instructions (e.g., protein synthesis).

Important Terms

Biochemistry: The branch of chemistry that studies the structure, function, and interactions of the molecules found in living organisms.

Dehydration Synthesis (Condensation Reaction): A chemical reaction that joins two monomers by removing a water molecule, forming a new covalent bond.

Hydrolysis: A chemical reaction that breaks a covalent bond between two monomers by adding a water molecule. It is the reverse of dehydration synthesis.

Carbohydrate: An organic compound composed of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, typically in a ratio of 1:2:1 (C$_n$(H$_2$O)$_n$). Includes sugars, starches, and cellulose, serving as energy sources and structural materials.

Monosaccharide: A simple sugar, the basic monomer unit of carbohydrates (e.g., glucose).

Disaccharide: A carbohydrate formed from two monosaccharides joined by dehydration synthesis (e.g., sucrose).

Polysaccharide: A complex carbohydrate formed from long chains of monosaccharides (e.g., starch, glycogen, cellulose).

Lipid: A diverse group of organic compounds that are largely nonpolar and therefore insoluble in water. Includes fats, oils, phospholipids, and steroids, serving as long-term energy storage, structural components, and chemical messengers.

Hydrophobic: "Water-fearing"; describes substances that do not mix with water.

Glycerol: A small three-carbon alcohol molecule that forms the backbone of fats and oils.

Fatty Acid: A long hydrocarbon chain with a carboxyl group at one end, which combines with glycerol to form fats and oils.

Saturated Fat: A fat whose fatty acid chains contain only single bonds between carbon atoms, making them typically solid at room temperature.

Unsaturated Fat: A fat whose fatty acid chains contain one or more double bonds between carbon atoms, creating kinks and making them typically liquid at room temperature.

Phospholipid: A type of lipid that is the primary structural component of cell membranes, possessing a hydrophilic "head" and two hydrophobic "tails."

Protein: A polymer made of amino acid monomers linked by peptide bonds. Proteins perform a vast array of functions in organisms.

Amino Acid: The monomer unit of proteins, characterized by a central carbon bonded to an amino group, a carboxyl group, a hydrogen atom, and a variable R group (side chain).

Peptide Bond: A covalent bond formed through dehydration synthesis between the amino group of one amino acid and the carboxyl group of another.

Polypeptide: A chain of amino acids linked by peptide bonds. A protein typically consists of one or more polypeptides folded into a specific 3D shape.

Denaturation: The process by which a protein loses its specific three-dimensional shape, often due to changes in temperature or pH, resulting in a loss of biological function.

Nucleic Acid: A polymer of nucleotide monomers that stores and transmits genetic information.

Nucleotide: The monomer unit of nucleic acids, composed of a five-carbon sugar, a phosphate group, and a nitrogen-containing base.

DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid): A type of nucleic acid that stores the hereditary instructions for the development and function of all known living organisms.

RNA (Ribonucleic Acid): A type of nucleic acid that helps carry out the genetic instructions stored in DNA, guiding protein synthesis.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Biochemistry

Every living cell is a complex chemical factory. Biochemistry is the branch of chemistry that studies the molecules found in living organisms, focusing on their structure, function, and interactions. It explains how raw materials from food are broken down and reassembled into the specific molecules needed for life, such as muscle proteins, cell membranes, DNA, and energy carriers. The field centers around four main classes of carbon-based molecules: carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids.

Building and Breaking Polymers

Three of the four major biomolecule classes—carbohydrates, proteins, and nucleic acids—are polymers, large molecules built from repeating smaller units called monomers. Cells use two main types of chemical reactions to manage these polymers:

  • Dehydration Synthesis (or Condensation Reaction): This reaction builds polymers. Two monomers are joined together by forming a new covalent bond, and a molecule of water (H$_2$O) is removed. One monomer contributes an -OH group, and the other contributes an -H, which combine to form H$_2$O.
  • Hydrolysis: This reaction breaks down polymers. A water molecule is added to a covalent bond between two monomers, splitting them apart. Water supplies the -OH and -H to restore the original functional groups on each monomer. Hydrolysis is the reverse of dehydration synthesis.

Your digestive system relies on hydrolysis to break down the polymers in food into monomers that can be absorbed. Subsequently, your cells use dehydration synthesis to reassemble these monomers into the specific polymers your body requires. Enzymes, which are themselves proteins, catalyze these crucial reactions.

Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are organic compounds primarily composed of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, typically in a 1:2:1 ratio (general formula C$_n$(H$_2$O)$_n$). They are the primary energy source for most organisms and also serve structural roles.

  • Monosaccharides (Simple Sugars): These are the carbohydrate monomers.
    • Glucose (C$6$H${12}$O$_6$) is the most vital monosaccharide, serving as the main fuel for cellular respiration and a product of photosynthesis.
    • Fructose (fruit sugar) and galactose (milk sugar component) are isomers of glucose, meaning they have the same chemical formula but different structures.
  • Disaccharides: Formed when two monosaccharides join via dehydration synthesis.
    • Sucrose (table sugar) = glucose + fructose.
    • Lactose (milk sugar) = glucose + galactose.
    • Maltose (malt sugar) = glucose + glucose.
  • Polysaccharides: Long chains of hundreds or thousands of monosaccharide (usually glucose) monomers. Despite being made of the same monomer, their different properties arise from how the glucose units are linked. "Structure determines function."
    • Starch: The primary energy storage molecule in plants (found in potatoes, rice, bread).
    • Glycogen: The energy storage molecule in animals, stored mainly in the liver and muscles.
    • Cellulose: The main structural component of plant cell walls. It is the most abundant organic compound on Earth. Humans cannot digest cellulose (lacking the necessary enzyme), but it is important as dietary fiber.

Lipids

Lipids are a diverse group of organic compounds characterized by their insolubility in water (they are hydrophobic). Unlike carbohydrates, proteins, and nucleic acids, lipids are not true polymers built from identical repeating monomers.

  • Fats and Oils: The most common lipids. They are built from one glycerol molecule (a three-carbon alcohol) bonded to three fatty acid chains (long hydrocarbon chains).
    • Saturated fats: Have fatty acid chains with only single bonds between carbons. They are typically solid at room temperature (e.g., butter).
    • Unsaturated fats: Have one or more double bonds in their fatty acid chains, which create kinks that prevent tight packing, making them liquid at room temperature (e.g., olive oil).
    • Lipids are excellent for long-term energy storage, providing about 9 calories per gram, roughly twice as much as carbohydrates (4 calories per gram).
  • Phospholipids: The primary structural component of cell membranes. Each phospholipid has a hydrophilic (water-loving) phosphate "head" and two hydrophobic (water-fearing) fatty acid "tails." In water, phospholipids spontaneously arrange into a phospholipid bilayer, forming the basis of cell membranes.
  • Other lipids include steroids, which function as hormones (e.g., cholesterol, testosterone, estrogen).

Proteins

Proteins are arguably the most diverse class of biomolecules, performing a vast array of functions in living organisms. Your body contains tens of thousands of different proteins, each with a unique shape and function.

  • Amino Acids: The monomers of proteins. There are 20 different amino acids used by cells. Each amino acid shares a common core structure: a central carbon bonded to an amino group (-NH$_2$), a carboxyl group (-COOH), a hydrogen atom, and a unique R group (side chain). The R group gives each amino acid its distinct chemical properties.
  • Peptide Bonds: Amino acids link together via peptide bonds, which are covalent bonds formed by dehydration synthesis between the amino group of one amino acid and the carboxyl group of the next. A chain of amino acids is called a polypeptide.
  • Structure Determines Function: The specific sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide determines how it folds into a precise three-dimensional shape, and this unique shape is absolutely critical for the protein's function.
  • Denaturation: If a protein loses its specific three-dimensional shape, a process called denaturation, it typically loses its function. For example, cooking an egg denatures the albumin protein, changing the liquid egg white into a solid.
  • Key Protein Categories:
    • Enzymes: Biological catalysts that speed up chemical reactions (e.g., amylase, pepsin).
    • Structural Proteins: Provide physical support (e.g., collagen in skin, keratin in hair).
    • Transport Proteins: Carry molecules throughout the body (e.g., hemoglobin transports oxygen).
    • Antibodies: Immune system proteins that identify and neutralize foreign invaders.

Worked Example: Counting Atoms in a Molecular Formula

Problem: The amino acid glycine has the molecular formula C$_2$H$_5$NO$_2$. How many total atoms are in one molecule of glycine? If three glycine molecules join by dehydration synthesis, what is the molecular formula of the resulting tripeptide?

Solution:

  1. Count atoms in glycine (C$_2$H$_5$NO$_2$):
    • Carbon: 2
    • Hydrogen: 5
    • Nitrogen: 1
    • Oxygen: 2
    • Total atoms: $2 + 5 + 1 + 2 = 10$ atoms.
  2. When three glycines join: Two peptide bonds are formed (one between the first and second, and one between the second and third). Each peptide bond formation releases one H$_2$O molecule. Therefore, 2 water molecules are removed.
  3. Calculate the tripeptide formula:
    • Start with three glycine molecules: $3 \times \text{C}_2\text{H}_5\text{NO}_2 = \text{C}6\text{H}{15}\text{N}_3\text{O}_6$.
    • Subtract the atoms from 2 H$_2$O molecules ($2 \times \text{H}_2\text{O} = \text{H}_4\text{O}_2$): $\text{C}6\text{H}{15}\text{N}_3\text{O}_6 - \text{H}_4\text{O}_2 = \text{C}6\text{H}{(15-4)}\text{N}3\text{O}{(6-2)} = \text{C}6\text{H}{11}\text{N}_3\text{O}_4$.

Answer: One glycine molecule contains 10 atoms. The tripeptide has the molecular formula C$6$H${11}$N$_3$O$_4$.

Nucleic Acids

Nucleic acids are polymers of nucleotide monomers that are essential for storing and transmitting genetic information. The two main types are DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) and RNA (ribonucleic acid).

  • Nucleotide Structure: Each nucleotide monomer has three parts:
    1. A five-carbon sugar (deoxyribose in DNA, ribose in RNA).
    2. A phosphate group (PO$_4^{3-}$).
    3. A nitrogen-containing base (Adenine, Thymine, Guanine, Cytosine in DNA; Adenine, Uracil, Guanine, Cytosine in RNA).
  • Polymer Formation: Nucleotides link together via dehydration synthesis. The sugar of one nucleotide bonds with the phosphate of the next, forming the sugar-phosphate backbone of the nucleic acid strand.
  • DNA Double Helix: In DNA, the bases project inward and form specific pairs with bases on a complementary strand (Adenine with Thymine, Guanine with Cytosine) through hydrogen bonds, creating the famous double helix structure.

Investigation: Testing Foods for Biomolecules

This investigation provides a practical way to identify the presence of starch, simple sugars, proteins, and lipids in various food samples using chemical indicator tests:

  • Starch Test (Iodine): Iodine solution turns dark blue-black in the presence of starch.
  • Simple Sugar Test (Benedict's Solution): Benedict's solution, when heated in a water bath, changes color from blue to green, yellow, orange, or red in the presence of simple sugars (the warmer the color, the more sugar).
  • Protein Test (Biuret Reagent): Biuret reagent changes from blue to purple or violet in the presence of protein.
  • Lipid Test (Brown Paper): Rubbing a food sample on brown paper and letting it dry will leave a translucent (greasy) spot if lipids are present.

Safety Note: Always wear safety goggles and gloves when handling chemical reagents. Iodine stains skin and clothing. Benedict's solution must be heated carefully in a water bath.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast dehydration synthesis and hydrolysis. Explain how your digestive system uses one process and your body's cells use the other to manage biomolecules from food.
  2. Lipids are often described as "not true polymers," unlike carbohydrates, proteins, and nucleic acids. Explain why this distinction is made, referring to their basic structural organization.
  3. The text states, "Structure determines function." Using proteins as an example, explain how the sequence of amino acids and the resulting three-dimensional shape are critical for a protein's specific role, and what happens if this shape is lost.
  4. Imagine you are eating a bowl of oatmeal with milk and a handful of nuts. What major biomolecules would you expect to find in each food item, and how would your body process them for energy or building blocks?
  5. Polysaccharides like starch, glycogen, and cellulose are all made entirely of glucose monomers. Despite this, they have vastly different functions. Explain how this is possible, relating your answer to their structural differences.
