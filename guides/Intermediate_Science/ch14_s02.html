Study Guide: Chapter 14, Section 2 - Energy

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concept of energy as the ability to cause change and explains its close relationship with work, where work is the mechanism for energy transfer. It focuses primarily on two main forms of mechanical energy: kinetic energy (energy of motion) and gravitational potential energy (stored energy due to height), providing their respective formulas and practical applications. The section also briefly touches upon other forms of potential energy like elastic and chemical energy, highlighting their relevance in various contexts.

Key Concepts

  • Energy as the Ability to Cause Change: Energy is the fundamental capacity to do work or cause a change in an object or system.
  • Energy and Work Relationship: Doing work on an object transfers energy to it. Energy is measured in joules (J), the same unit as work, and is a scalar quantity (has magnitude but no direction).
  • Kinetic Energy (KE): The energy an object possesses due to its motion. It depends on an object's mass ($m$) and the square of its speed ($v$), given by the formula $KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$. Doubling the speed quadruples the kinetic energy.
  • Potential Energy (PE): Energy stored in an object because of its position, condition, or configuration. It has the potential to be converted into kinetic energy.
  • Gravitational Potential Energy (GPE): A specific type of potential energy stored in an object due to its height ($h$) above a chosen reference point. It depends on the object's mass ($m$), the acceleration due to gravity ($g$), and its height, calculated by $PE = mgh$. The choice of reference level for height is arbitrary.
  • Elastic Potential Energy: Energy stored in an object when it is deformed (stretched or compressed), like in a spring or rubber band. This stored energy can convert to kinetic energy upon release.
  • Chemical Energy: A form of potential energy stored in the bonds between atoms within molecules. It's crucial in biological processes like photosynthesis and cellular respiration, where energy is released or absorbed during bond changes.
  • Falling Objects and Energy: While heavier objects have more gravitational potential energy and thus convert to more kinetic energy upon impact, all objects fall at the same rate (ignoring air resistance) because the greater gravitational force on a more massive object is precisely balanced by its greater inertia, resulting in the same acceleration due $a = g$.

Important Terms

  • Energy: The ability to cause change or do work.
  • Kinetic Energy (KE): The energy an object possesses because of its motion.
  • Potential Energy (PE): Energy stored in an object because of its position, condition, or configuration.
  • Gravitational Potential Energy (GPE): The energy an object possesses because of its height above a reference point.
  • Joule (J): The standard unit of measurement for both energy and work.
  • Scalar Quantity: A physical quantity that has magnitude but no direction (e.g., energy, mass, speed).
  • Elastic Potential Energy: Stored energy resulting from the deformation (stretching or compressing) of an elastic object.
  • Chemical Energy: Energy stored in the bonds between atoms within a molecule.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Energy

  • In physics, work is the mechanism by which energy is transferred. When work is done on an object, energy is transferred to it.
  • Definition of Energy: Energy is the ability to cause change.
  • Relationship to Work: Energy is closely connected to work; doing work on an object changes its energy.
  • Properties of Energy:
    • It is a scalar quantity, meaning it has magnitude but no direction.
    • Its standard unit of measurement is the joule (J), which is the same unit used for work.
  • There are many forms of energy (thermal, chemical, electrical, nuclear, radiant), but this section focuses on kinetic energy and gravitational potential energy.

Kinetic Energy (KE)

  • Definition: Kinetic energy is the energy an object possesses because of its motion. Any object that is moving has kinetic energy.

  • Factors Affecting KE:

    1. Mass ($m$): The more massive an object, the more kinetic energy it has.
    2. Speed ($v$): The faster an object moves, the more kinetic energy it has. Speed has a squared relationship with KE.

  • Formula: The formula for kinetic energy is: $$ KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 $$ Where:

    • $m$ = mass in kilograms (kg)
    • $v$ = speed in meters per second (m/s)
    • The resulting kinetic energy (KE) is in joules (J).

  • Impact of Speed Squared: Kinetic energy is directly proportional to the square of the speed. This means if you double an object's speed, its kinetic energy quadruples ($2^2 = 4$). This principle is critical for understanding safety implications, such as why higher highway speeds drastically increase the energy involved in collisions.

  • Example: Calculating Kinetic Energy

    • Problem: A 0.145 kg baseball is pitched at 40 m/s. Calculate its kinetic energy.
    • Given:
      • $m = 0.145 \text{ kg}$
      • $v = 40 \text{ m/s}$
    • Solution:
      1. Apply the formula: $KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$
      2. Substitute values: $KE = \frac{1}{2}(0.145 \text{ kg})(40 \text{ m/s})^2$
      3. Calculate $v^2$: $(40 \text{ m/s})^2 = 1600 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2$
      4. Complete calculation: $KE = \frac{1}{2}(0.145 \text{ kg})(1600 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2) = 116 \text{ J}$
    • Result: The baseball has 116 J of kinetic energy.

Gravitational Potential Energy (GPE)

  • Definition: Gravitational potential energy is the energy stored in an object because of its height above a reference point.

  • How it's Stored: When you lift an object, you do work against gravity. This work is stored as gravitational potential energy.

  • Conversion: If the object is allowed to fall, this stored potential energy converts back into kinetic energy.

  • Factors Affecting GPE:

    1. Mass ($m$): More massive objects have more GPE.
    2. Acceleration due to Gravity ($g$): On Earth, this is approximately $9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$.
    3. Height ($h$): The greater the height above the reference point, the more GPE an object possesses.

  • Formula: The formula for gravitational potential energy is: $$ PE = mgh $$ Where:

    • $m$ = mass in kilograms (kg)
    • $g$ = acceleration due to gravity ($9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$)
    • $h$ = height above the chosen reference level in meters (m)
    • The resulting potential energy (PE) is in joules (J).

  • Reference Level: The choice of the reference level (where $h=0$) is arbitrary. It could be the floor, the ground, sea level, or any convenient point. What is significant is the change in height between two positions.

  • Example: Calculating Gravitational Potential Energy

    • Problem: A 2.0 kg book is placed on a shelf 1.5 m above the floor. Calculate its gravitational potential energy relative to the floor.
    • Given:
      • $m = 2.0 \text{ kg}$
      • $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$
      • $h = 1.5 \text{ m}$
    • Solution:
      1. Apply the formula: $PE = mgh$
      2. Substitute values: $PE = (2.0 \text{ kg})(9.8 \text{ m/s}^2)(1.5 \text{ m})$
      3. Complete calculation: $PE = 29.4 \text{ J}$
    • Result: The book has 29.4 J of gravitational potential energy relative to the floor. If it falls, this energy converts to 29.4 J of kinetic energy (ignoring air resistance).

Elastic Potential Energy

  • Concept: Gravitational potential energy is not the only type of stored energy. Elastic potential energy is stored in objects that are deformed (compressed or stretched).
  • Examples: A compressed spring, a stretched rubber band, or a drawn bow all store elastic potential energy.
  • Conversion: When released, this stored elastic energy converts into kinetic energy.
  • Factors: The amount of elastic potential energy depends on how much the object is deformed and the stiffness of the material. A qualitative understanding is sufficient for now: more deformation means more stored energy. (The quantitative formula $PE_e = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$ will be covered in higher-level physics).

Life Science Connection: Chemical Energy

  • Chemical Energy as Potential Energy: Chemical energy is a form of potential energy stored in the bonds between atoms within molecules.
  • Biological Relevance:
    • Photosynthesis: Converts light energy into chemical energy, storing it in glucose molecules.
    • Cellular Respiration: Releases the chemical energy stored in glucose to produce ATP (adenosine triphosphate), which powers cellular processes.
  • Energy Transformation: The principles of energy transformation learned in physics apply directly to how energy is used and converted within living cells. Energy is released or absorbed when chemical bonds are broken or formed.

Deeper Thinking: Mass, PE, KE, and Falling Objects

  • Problem: Two objects, a 1 kg ball and a 5 kg brick, are at the same height. Which has more gravitational potential energy? If both are dropped, which hits the ground with more kinetic energy? Does the heavier object fall faster?
  • Gravitational Potential Energy: The 5 kg brick has more gravitational potential energy ($PE = mgh$) because it has a greater mass ($m$) while $g$ and $h$ are the same for both.
  • Kinetic Energy on Impact: By the principle of conservation of energy (discussed further in Section 3), all potential energy converts to kinetic energy upon impact (ignoring air resistance). Therefore, the brick, having more initial potential energy, will hit the ground with more kinetic energy.
  • Falling Speed: Both objects fall at the same rate ($a = g \approx 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$), assuming air resistance is negligible. This is because according to Newton's Second Law ($F = ma$), the greater gravitational force ($F_g = mg$) on the heavier brick is exactly compensated by its greater inertia (resistance to change in motion, or mass $m$). So, the acceleration ($a = F_g/m = mg/m = g$) is the same for both.
  • Conclusion: They hit the ground at the same speed, but the brick has more kinetic energy because its mass ($m$) is larger, and $KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$.

  3. How does an object's speed affect its kinetic energy? If a car doubles its speed, how much more kinetic energy does it have, and why is this understanding crucial for highway safety?
