Chapter 14, Section 3: Conservation of Energy Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces one of the most fundamental laws in physics: the Law of Conservation of Energy. It explains how energy transforms between different forms, particularly focusing on the conservation of mechanical energy (kinetic and potential energy) in ideal systems. The section also addresses real-world scenarios where friction converts mechanical energy into thermal energy, influencing the efficiency of machines and processes.

Key Concepts

  • Law of Conservation of Energy: A foundational principle stating that energy can never be created or destroyed. Instead, it only transforms from one form to another (e.g., potential to kinetic) or transfers between objects. The total energy within an isolated system always remains constant.
  • Mechanical Energy: Defined as the sum of an object's kinetic energy (energy of motion) and potential energy (stored energy, often due to position or state). In the absence of non-conservative forces like friction, the total mechanical energy of a system is conserved.
  • Conservation of Mechanical Energy Equation: For systems where friction and air resistance are negligible, the initial sum of kinetic and potential energy equals the final sum: $KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f$. This principle allows for calculating speeds or heights without needing to know the forces involved directly.
  • Thermal Energy and Friction: In real-world systems, friction always converts some mechanical energy into thermal energy (heat). While mechanical energy decreases due to friction, the total energy of the system (including thermal energy) is still conserved, meaning no energy is actually lost or destroyed.
  • Efficiency: A measure of how effectively a machine or process converts input energy into useful output energy. It is calculated as the ratio of useful energy output to total energy input, typically expressed as a percentage. No real machine is 100% efficient due to energy losses, primarily as thermal energy from friction.

Important Terms

  • Conservation of Energy: The law stating that energy cannot be created or destroyed; it can only be transformed from one form to another or transferred from one object to another. The total energy of an isolated system remains constant.
  • Mechanical Energy: The sum of the kinetic energy ($KE$) and potential energy ($PE$) in a system, expressed as $E = KE + PE$.
  • Isolated System: A system that does not exchange matter or energy with its surroundings. The total energy within such a system remains constant.
  • Thermal Energy ($E_{thermal}$): Energy associated with the random motion of atoms and molecules; it is often generated by friction and represents a "lost" form of mechanical energy, making it unavailable for useful work. Also commonly referred to as heat.
  • Efficiency: The ratio of useful energy output to total energy input, expressed as a percentage. It quantifies how much of the input energy is converted into the desired form of output energy.

Detailed Notes

The Law of Conservation of Energy

The concept of energy conservation is introduced through the simple example of a ball dropped from a height. At its peak, the ball possesses maximum gravitational potential energy and zero kinetic energy. As it falls, its potential energy converts into kinetic energy, increasing its speed. Just before impact, all its initial potential energy has transformed into kinetic energy. The fundamental principle governing this is the Law of Conservation of Energy:

  • Energy can neither be created nor destroyed.
  • It can only change from one form to another (e.g., chemical to electrical, potential to kinetic).
  • It can be transferred from one object to another.
  • Crucially, the total energy of an isolated system remains constant. This law is a cornerstone of physics, applicable across all scales and connecting various scientific disciplines.

Mechanical Energy Conservation

In many physics problems, we focus on mechanical energy, which is the sum of an object's kinetic energy ($KE$) and its potential energy ($PE$): $E = KE + PE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + mgh$ where:

  • $m$ is mass
  • $v$ is speed
  • $g$ is the acceleration due to gravity
  • $h$ is height

In an ideal system—one where friction, air resistance, and other non-conservative forces are negligible—mechanical energy is conserved. This means that the total mechanical energy at any initial point ($i$) is equal to the total mechanical energy at any final point ($f$): $KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f$

This equation is extremely powerful because it allows you to determine unknown speeds or heights within a system by tracking energy transformations, without needing to analyze the complex forces involved.

Worked Example: Energy Conservation

Problem: A $0.50kg$ ball is dropped from rest at a height of $10m$. Ignoring air resistance, what is the ball's speed just before it hits the ground?

Solution:

  1. Identify Known Values:

    • Mass, $m = 0.50kg$
    • Acceleration due to gravity, $g = 9.8m/s^2$
    • Initial height, $h_i = 10m$
    • Initial speed, $v_i = 0m/s$ (dropped from rest)
    • Final height, $h_f = 0m$ (ground level)
    • Final speed, $v_f = ?$

  2. Apply Conservation of Mechanical Energy: $KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f$

    Since the ball starts at rest ($v_i = 0$), its initial kinetic energy ($KE_i$) is 0. Since it ends at ground level ($h_f = 0$), its final potential energy ($PE_f$) is 0.

    The equation simplifies to: $0 + mgh_i = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 + 0$

  3. Solve for $v_f$: Notice that the mass ($m$) cancels out from both sides of the equation: $gh_i = \frac{1}{2}v_f^2$

    Rearrange to solve for $v_f^2$: $v_f^2 = 2gh_i$

    Substitute the known values: $v_f^2 = 2(9.8m/s^2)(10m) = 196m^2/s^2$

    Take the square root to find $v_f$: $v_f = \sqrt{196} = 14m/s$

    The ball hits the ground at a speed of $14m/s$. This result highlights that, without air resistance, the final speed of a falling object is independent of its mass, consistent with Galileo's observations.

Thermal Energy and Friction

In the real world, "ideal systems" are rare. Friction is always present and plays a significant role in energy transformations. When friction acts, it does work on objects, converting some of their mechanical energy into thermal energy (heat).

  • Example: A sled sliding to a stop on snow. The kinetic energy of the sled isn't destroyed; it's converted into thermal energy, causing a slight warming of the sled runners and the snow surface. The initial kinetic energy lost by the sled is precisely equal to the thermal energy gained by the sled and snow.

Even with friction, the Law of Conservation of Energy still holds true. The total energy of the system (including mechanical and thermal forms) remains constant. The equation for energy conservation when friction is present becomes: $KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f + E_{thermal}$ where $E_{thermal}$ represents the thermal energy generated by friction.

Efficiency

Because some energy is inevitably converted to thermal energy by friction (and other resistive forces) in any real process or machine, no real machine can be 100% efficient. Efficiency is a measure of how much of the total energy input is converted into useful energy output.

The formula for efficiency is: $\text{Efficiency} = \frac{\text{useful energy output}}{\text{total energy input}} \times 100%$

  • Example: If you put $100J$ of energy into pushing a crate up a ramp, and the crate only gains $75J$ of gravitational potential energy (with the remaining $25J$ lost to friction as thermal energy), the efficiency is: $\text{Efficiency} = \frac{75J}{100J} \times 100% = 75%$

Life Science Connection

The concept of efficiency is not limited to physics. In biology:

  • During cellular respiration, not all of a glucose molecule's chemical energy becomes usable ATP; about 36-38 ATP molecules are produced, with the rest "lost" as thermal energy.
  • In food chains, only about 10% of the energy from one trophic level is transferred to the next. The remaining 90% is "lost" as thermal energy through metabolic processes. In both cases, "lost" does not mean destroyed, but rather converted into a less useful form of energy for the specific biological process.

Think Deeper: Roller Coaster Scenario

Consider a roller coaster car starting from rest at the top of the first hill.

  • If no energy were lost to friction, the car could never reach a height greater than the first hill. By the conservation of mechanical energy, its total mechanical energy (all potential energy at the start) is fixed. To gain more potential energy (reach a higher point), it would need to increase its total mechanical energy, which is impossible without an external energy input.
  • If the car had a motor, the motor would provide an external energy source, adding energy to the system. This added energy could increase the car's total mechanical energy, allowing it to reach a height greater than the first hill. This explains why the first hill on a real roller coaster is always the tallest – the motor does the work to lift the car to that initial height, and from there, the ride relies on energy transformations (and losses to friction).

Review Questions

  1. Explain the Law of Conservation of Energy in your own words. How does it differ from the conservation of mechanical energy?
  2. Describe the energy transformations that occur when a bungee jumper leaps from a tall bridge. Identify the points where potential energy is maximized, kinetic energy is maximized, and elastic potential energy is maximized.
  3. A $2.0kg$ bowling ball is dropped from a height of $5.0m$. Ignoring air resistance, calculate its speed just before it hits the ground. Show your work using the conservation of mechanical energy equation.
  4. Why is no real machine 100% efficient? What happens to the "lost" energy, and how does this relate to the overall Law of Conservation of Energy? Provide an example.
  5. How do biological processes, like cellular respiration or energy transfer in food chains, demonstrate the principle of energy conservation and the concept of efficiency? Explain what is meant by energy being "lost" in these contexts.
