Population Ecology Study Guide

Overview

Population ecology is the study of how populations change in size and distribution over time, driven by various environmental factors. This section explores different population growth patterns, the limiting factors that constrain growth, the dynamic interactions between species like predators and prey, and the scientific methods used to estimate population sizes in the wild.

Key Concepts

  • Exponential Growth: A rapid population increase where each generation is larger than the last by a fixed proportion, leading to a J-shaped curve when resources are unlimited.
  • Logistic Growth: A more realistic growth pattern where initial exponential growth slows as resources become scarce, eventually stabilizing around the environment's carrying capacity, producing an S-shaped curve.
  • Carrying Capacity ($K$): The maximum population size that a particular environment can sustainably support indefinitely, given its available resources.
  • Limiting Factors: Environmental conditions that restrict population growth, preventing it from increasing beyond a certain size. They are categorized as either density-dependent or density-independent.
  • Density-Dependent Factors: Limiting factors whose impact on a population intensifies as the population density increases (e.g., competition, predation, disease).
  • Density-Independent Factors: Limiting factors that affect a population regardless of its density (e.g., natural disasters, severe weather, human habitat destruction).
  • Predator-Prey Dynamics: The cyclical fluctuations in the population sizes of predator and prey species, where changes in one population directly influence the other, often with a time lag.
  • Population Sampling Methods: Techniques used by ecologists to estimate population sizes when counting every individual is impractical, including quadrat sampling, mark-recapture, and transect sampling.

Important Terms

  • Exponential growth: A pattern of population increase in which the population grows by a constant percentage in each time period, producing a J-shaped curve; it occurs when resources are unlimited and there are no constraints on reproduction.
  • Logistic growth: A pattern of population increase in which growth is initially exponential but slows as the population approaches the carrying capacity of the environment, producing an S-shaped curve.
  • Carrying capacity ($K$): The maximum population size that an environment can sustain indefinitely, given the available food, water, shelter, and other resources.
  • Limiting factor: Any environmental factor that restricts the growth, abundance, or distribution of a population; it prevents the population from increasing beyond a certain size.
  • Density-dependent factors: Limiting factors that become more severe as the population grows denser.
  • Density-independent factors: Limiting factors that affect populations regardless of their size.
  • Predator-prey dynamics: The cyclical fluctuation of predator and prey population sizes; as prey increase, predators increase; as predators increase, prey decrease; as prey decrease, predators decrease; and the cycle repeats.
  • Quadrat: A defined area, usually square, used to sample and count organisms in a specific location; multiple quadrats are placed randomly across a habitat and results are averaged to estimate population density.
  • Mark-recapture: A sampling method for mobile animals where individuals are captured, marked, released, and then a second sample is taken to estimate total population size based on the proportion of marked individuals recaptured.
  • Transect sampling: A sampling method involving walking along a defined line through a habitat and recording organisms within a set distance on either side, useful for studying distribution across environmental gradients.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Population Ecology

Population ecology explores the fascinating question of what controls the size and distribution of populations. From bacteria in a flask to large animal populations, understanding the factors that limit growth is crucial for managing species and ecosystems.

Population Growth Models

1. Exponential Growth

  • Definition: Occurs when a population has unlimited resources, no predators, and no disease. Each generation grows by a fixed proportion, leading to increasingly faster growth.
  • Curve Shape: Produces a characteristic J-shaped curve on a graph, starting slow and then rising steeply.
  • Mathematical Model: $N_t = N_0 r^t$
    • $N_t$: Population at time $t$
    • $N_0$: Initial population
    • $r$: Growth factor per time period
  • Real-World Examples: Can occur for short periods when a species colonizes a new habitat, such as bacteria in fresh broth, rabbits introduced to Australia, or algal blooms.

2. Logistic Growth

  • Definition: A more common pattern where initial rapid growth slows down as resources become scarce, predators increase, or disease spreads.
  • Curve Shape: Produces an S-shaped curve. Growth begins exponentially, then decelerates, and finally stabilizes.
  • Carrying Capacity ($K$): The population eventually levels off near the environment's carrying capacity, which is the maximum number of individuals an environment can sustainably support. At $K$, birth rates roughly equal death rates.
  • Fluctuations: Populations rarely stay exactly at $K$; they often fluctuate around it due to changing environmental conditions.
  • Mathematical Concept: The underlying equation incorporates the idea that as the population approaches $K$, its growth rate decreases toward zero. This S-shaped curve is a common pattern observed across many scientific disciplines.

Limiting Factors

Limiting factors are environmental constraints that prevent a population from growing indefinitely.

1. Density-Dependent Factors

  • Definition: These factors become more intense and their impact increases as the population density rises.
  • Examples:
    • Competition: For limited resources like food, water, space, or mates.
    • Predation: More prey in an area can attract and sustain more predators.
    • Disease: Pathogens spread more easily and rapidly in crowded populations.
    • Parasitism: Parasites find hosts more readily in dense populations.
    • Accumulation of Waste: Metabolic waste can become toxic at high densities (e.g., bacteria in a closed container).

2. Density-Independent Factors

  • Definition: These factors affect populations regardless of their size or density.
  • Examples:
    • Natural Disasters: Floods, fires, hurricanes, volcanic eruptions.
    • Severe Weather: Droughts, extreme cold, heat waves.
    • Human Activities: Habitat destruction, pollution, pesticide application.
  • Interaction: In reality, most populations are influenced by a combination of both density-dependent and density-independent factors. For instance, a fire (density-independent) might reduce a population, and then competition (density-dependent) would regulate its recovery.

Predator-Prey Dynamics

  • Definition: The classic ecological interaction where the population sizes of predators and their prey oscillate in a cyclical pattern.
  • Example: Snowshoe Hare and Canada Lynx:
    • When hare (prey) populations are high, lynx (predator) populations increase due to abundant food, but with a time lag.
    • As lynx numbers peak, they consume more hares, causing the hare population to crash.
    • With fewer hares, lynx begin to starve, and their population declines.
    • With fewer lynx hunting, the hare population recovers, restarting the cycle.
  • Key Principle: These oscillations highlight that populations within a community are interconnected through food webs, and changes in one species can have cascading effects on others.

Population Sampling Methods

Directly counting every individual in a population is usually impossible, so ecologists use various sampling techniques to estimate population size and density.

1. Quadrat Sampling

  • Method: A square frame (quadrat) of a known area is placed randomly at multiple locations within a habitat. All organisms within the quadrat are counted.
  • Calculation: The average density (organisms per unit area) from multiple quadrats is used to estimate the total population.
  • Suitability: Best for stationary organisms like plants or slow-moving animals.

2. Mark-Recapture

  • Method:
    1. An initial sample of mobile animals is captured, marked (e.g., with a tag or paint), and released.
    2. After a period, a second sample is captured.
    3. The number of marked individuals in the second capture is recorded.
  • Formula (Lincoln-Petersen): $N = (M \times C) / R$
    • $N$: Estimated total population size
    • $M$: Number of individuals originally marked and released
    • $C$: Total number of individuals captured in the second sample
    • $R$: Number of recaptured (marked) individuals in the second sample
  • Assumptions: Assumes marked and unmarked individuals mix randomly, marks don't affect survival or fall off, and there's no significant immigration/emigration or births/deaths between samples.
  • Example: If 40 frogs are marked, and later 50 are captured with 8 marked, $N = (40 \times 50) / 8 = 250$ frogs.

3. Transect Sampling

  • Method: Involves walking along a straight line (transect) through a habitat and recording all organisms observed within a specified distance on either side of the line.
  • Purpose: Especially useful for studying how species distribution or abundance changes across an environmental gradient (e.g., moving from a forest edge into a meadow).

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast exponential and logistic population growth models. Include a description of their characteristic curves, the environmental conditions under which each occurs, and why logistic growth is generally considered more realistic.
  2. Define carrying capacity ($K$). Explain how both density-dependent and density-independent limiting factors interact to influence the carrying capacity of an environment for a given population.
  3. Describe the classic predator-prey dynamics, using the snowshoe hare and Canada lynx as an example. Explain the cyclical nature of their population fluctuations and why there is typically a time lag between the peak of the prey population and the peak of the predator population.
  4. You are tasked with estimating the population size of two different organisms: dandelions in a field and deer in a forest. Which sampling method (quadrat or mark-recapture) would be more appropriate for each, and why? What are the key assumptions that must be met for the mark-recapture method to provide an accurate estimate?
  5. Consider a newly introduced invasive species that is experiencing rapid exponential growth in a new habitat. Predict at least three specific density-dependent limiting factors that will likely begin to exert pressure on this population as it grows. How might these factors eventually cause the population growth to shift towards a logistic model?
