Study Guide: Mitigation and Adaptation to Climate Change

Overview

This section explores the two fundamental approaches to addressing climate change: mitigation and adaptation. Mitigation focuses on reducing greenhouse gas emissions to prevent further warming, primarily through transitioning to renewable energy, improving energy efficiency, and capturing carbon. Adaptation involves adjusting to the inevitable impacts of climate change that are already underway or locked in, through strategies like coastal defenses and resilient agriculture. Both strategies are crucial for building a sustainable and resilient future.

Key Concepts

  • Mitigation: Actions aimed at reducing or preventing greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions to limit the extent of future climate change. It addresses the root causes of global warming.
  • Adaptation: Strategies developed to prepare for and adjust to the current and projected impacts of climate change, thereby reducing harm and taking advantage of potential new opportunities.
  • Renewable Energy: Energy derived from naturally replenished sources (solar, wind, hydroelectric, geothermal, biomass) that produce little to no GHG emissions during operation, making them critical for decarbonizing energy systems.
  • Energy Efficiency: Using less energy to achieve the same task or outcome. It's often the cheapest and quickest way to reduce energy consumption and emissions.
  • Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS): Technologies that prevent CO2 emissions from entering the atmosphere by capturing them from industrial sources or directly from the air, then storing them permanently underground.
  • International Agreements: Global frameworks and treaties (like the UNFCCC and Paris Agreement) designed to foster international cooperation and coordinated action to address climate change, recognizing its global nature.
  • Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC): A UN body that reviews and synthesizes global scientific literature on climate change to provide comprehensive assessments informing policy.

Important Terms

  • Mitigation: Actions to reduce or prevent greenhouse gas emissions to limit future climate change.
  • Adaptation: Actions taken to prepare for and adjust to the current and future effects of climate change.
  • Renewable Energy: Energy from sources naturally replenished on human timescales (solar, wind, hydro, geothermal, biomass).
  • Solar energy: Electricity generated from sunlight using photovoltaic cells or concentrated solar thermal systems.
  • Wind energy: Electricity generated from the kinetic energy of moving air using turbines.
  • Hydroelectric energy: Electricity generated by channeling flowing water through turbines, often using dams.
  • Geothermal energy: Electricity or heat derived from the Earth's internal heat.
  • Nuclear energy: Electricity generated from nuclear fission, producing very low operational greenhouse gas emissions, though not renewable.
  • Energy efficiency: Using less energy to accomplish the same task (e.g., LED lighting, insulation, efficient vehicles).
  • Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS): Technologies to capture CO2 from industrial sources or the atmosphere and store it underground.
  • Point-source capture: Capturing CO2 from the exhaust of specific industrial facilities.
  • Direct air capture (DAC): Extracting CO2 directly from ambient air using chemical processes.
  • United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC): The primary international treaty on climate change, established in 1992.
  • Kyoto Protocol: An international treaty (1997) that set binding emission reduction targets for developed countries.
  • Paris Agreement: A landmark international agreement (2015) where nearly all nations pledged to limit global warming to well below 2°C, pursuing 1.5°C.
  • Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC): The leading international body for assessing climate change science.

Detailed Notes

The challenge of climate change requires a dual approach: mitigation to reduce its causes and adaptation to cope with its unavoidable impacts. Some warming is already "locked in" due to past emissions, making adaptation essential. However, robust mitigation efforts will significantly ease the burden of future adaptation.

Mitigation: Reducing Greenhouse Gas Emissions

Mitigation strategies primarily focus on reducing the amount of greenhouse gases entering the atmosphere and enhancing natural carbon sinks.

Renewable Energy

Replacing fossil fuels, which account for about 75% of global GHG emissions, with renewable energy sources is the single most critical step in mitigation.

  • Solar Energy: Utilizes photovoltaic (PV) cells or concentrating solar thermal systems to convert sunlight into electricity or heat. Its cost has significantly decreased, making it economically competitive. Challenges include intermittency (only works when the sun shines) and the need for energy storage solutions (e.g., batteries).
  • Wind Energy: Harnesses the kinetic energy of moving air with turbines to generate electricity. Wind power costs have also fallen sharply, with offshore wind farms offering substantial generation capacity. Like solar, it's intermittent. Other concerns include visual/noise impacts and effects on wildlife, though these are generally minor compared to other human impacts.
  • Hydroelectric Energy: Generates electricity by channeling flowing water through turbines, typically using dams. It's a reliable and dispatchable source, meaning it can be turned on or off as needed, complementing intermittent sources like solar and wind. However, dams can have significant environmental impacts on river ecosystems, fish migration, and downstream water flow, and suitable locations are limited.
  • Geothermal Energy: Taps into the Earth's internal heat. It provides reliable, baseload electricity with minimal emissions, especially in geologically active regions. Enhanced geothermal systems are expanding its potential to more areas.
  • Nuclear Energy: While not renewable (it uses uranium fuel), nuclear power produces electricity with very low operational GHG emissions and accounts for about 10% of global electricity. Debates exist regarding its role due to concerns about nuclear waste management, high construction costs, and accident risks.

Physics Connection: All these technologies are rooted in physics. Solar panels use the photoelectric effect to convert light into electricity. Wind turbines convert the kinetic energy of air into electrical energy via electromagnetic induction. Hydroelectric dams transform the gravitational potential energy of water into kinetic and then electrical energy.

Energy Efficiency

The most cost-effective approach is often to reduce energy consumption in the first place. Energy efficiency means accomplishing the same task using less energy.

  • Examples:
    • LED lighting: Uses 75% less electricity than incandescent bulbs.
    • Building insulation: Reduces heating and cooling needs by preventing heat transfer.
    • Efficient vehicles: Electric vehicles (EVs) convert 85-90% of electrical energy to motion, far more efficient than internal combustion engines (20-35%).
    • Industrial processes: Redesigning to recover waste heat and minimize material waste.

Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)

CCS technologies aim to prevent CO2 from reaching the atmosphere.

  • Point-source capture: Capturing CO2 emissions directly from large industrial sources like power plants or factories before they enter the atmosphere. The CO2 is then compressed and injected into deep underground geological formations for permanent storage.
  • Direct air capture (DAC): A newer technology that chemically extracts CO2 directly from ambient air. It's currently expensive but undergoing rapid development.
  • Nature-based carbon capture: Reforestation (planting new forests), afforestation (creating forests where none existed), protecting existing forests, and restoring wetlands and mangroves all naturally sequester significant amounts of carbon in biomass and soil.

Other Mitigation Strategies

  • Reducing methane emissions: Implementing gas capture at landfills, using feed supplements for livestock, and repairing leaks in natural gas infrastructure.
  • Sustainable agriculture: Reducing nitrogen fertilizer use (to lower N2O emissions), improving rice paddy management (to lower CH4), and practicing no-till farming (to increase soil carbon).
  • Reducing deforestation: Protecting vital carbon sinks and biodiversity hotspots, especially tropical forests.
  • Electrification: Shifting heating, cooking, and transportation from fossil fuels to electricity, provided that electricity is generated from clean, renewable sources.

Adaptation: Preparing for Climate Impacts

Even with aggressive mitigation, some degree of climate change is unavoidable due to past emissions. Adaptation strategies are essential to help communities and ecosystems cope with these inevitable changes.

Adaptation Strategies

  • Coastal defenses: Constructing sea walls, levees, and storm surge barriers. Restoring coastal wetlands also provides natural protection by absorbing wave energy and reducing flooding. In highly vulnerable areas, "managed retreat" (relocating communities) may be considered.
  • Water management: Implementing more efficient irrigation techniques, harvesting rainwater, developing desalination plants, and recharging aquifers to ensure water supply in regions facing increased drought.
  • Agricultural adaptation: Developing and deploying heat-tolerant and drought-resistant crop varieties (using genetic principles), adjusting planting schedules, and diversifying crops to enhance food security.
  • Heat action plans: Urban strategies like creating cooling centers, expanding urban tree canopy (to reduce the urban heat island effect), and designing buildings for passive cooling to protect populations during extreme heat events.
  • Early warning systems: Improving weather forecasting and disaster preparedness mechanisms for extreme events such as floods, hurricanes, and heat waves, allowing communities to react more effectively.
  • Ecosystem-based adaptation: Protecting and restoring natural ecosystems like mangroves, coral reefs, forests, and wetlands, which provide crucial services like buffering against storms, regulating water flow, and supporting biodiversity.

International Agreements

Climate change is a global challenge that transcends national borders, requiring extensive international cooperation.

  • United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC, 1992): Established the foundational international framework for climate change negotiations.
  • Kyoto Protocol (1997): Set legally binding emission reduction targets primarily for developed countries. Its effectiveness was limited as some major emitters did not ratify it or withdrew.
  • Paris Agreement (2015): A landmark agreement adopted by nearly every nation, aiming to limit global warming to "well below 2°C" above pre-industrial levels, with an ambitious target of 1.5°C. Each country submits its own Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) for emission reductions and reports on progress.
  • Role of IPCC: The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change plays a vital role by synthesizing the latest climate science from around the world into comprehensive assessment reports that inform international policy negotiations.

Individual Action vs. Systemic Change

The discussion around individual action versus systemic change highlights the complex nature of climate solutions. While individual choices can drive awareness and demand, large-scale systemic changes—driven by policy, technology, and industry—are essential for meeting climate targets. Often, individual actions can catalyze or reinforce broader systemic shifts.

Investigation: Analyzing Real Climate Data

To understand the scientific basis of climate change, it's crucial to analyze real-world data. Activities involving graphing global temperature anomalies and atmospheric CO2 concentrations over time reveal the clear trends of warming and increasing CO2 levels, and allow for calculation of rates of change to quantify these shifts.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental difference between climate change mitigation and adaptation. Provide at least two distinct examples for each approach.
  2. Compare and contrast two major renewable energy sources (e.g., solar vs. wind, or hydro vs. geothermal) regarding their advantages, limitations, and potential contributions to reducing greenhouse gas emissions.
  3. Describe how energy efficiency and carbon capture and storage (CCS) strategies contribute to mitigating climate change. Discuss different methods for each.
  4. Outline at least three distinct adaptation strategies discussed in this section. Explain how local governments or communities might prioritize and implement these strategies based on their specific vulnerabilities.
  5. Why is international cooperation essential for addressing climate change? Discuss the significance of agreements like the Paris Agreement in fostering global action, and explain the role of bodies like the IPCC.
