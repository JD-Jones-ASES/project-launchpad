Chapter 14, Section 4: Power

Overview

This section introduces the concept of power, defining it as the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred. It explains how to calculate power using work and time, or force and velocity, and discusses its various units, including watts and horsepower. The section also delves into the broader implications of energy transfer and efficiency in real-world systems, drawing connections to conservation laws and everyday applications.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of Power: Power measures how quickly work is performed or how rapidly energy is transferred or used. It's not about the total amount of work done, but the speed at which it's done.
  • Formulas for Power:
    • P = W/t: Power (P) is equal to the work (W) done divided by the time (t) taken. This is the fundamental definition.
    • P = Fv: Power (P) can also be calculated as the force (F) applied multiplied by the speed (v) of the object in the direction of the force. This is useful when time is not explicitly known.
  • Units of Power: The standard international (SI) unit for power is the watt (W), equal to one joule per second (1 J/s). Other common units include kilowatts (kW) for larger power ratings and horsepower (hp) for engines.
  • Energy vs. Power: While closely related, power is the rate of energy transfer, while energy is the total amount transferred or used. The kilowatt-hour (kWh) is an important unit of energy used in electricity billing, not power.
  • Noether's Theorem: This fundamental theorem in physics establishes a profound link between symmetries in the laws of physics and conserved quantities. For example, the timelessness of physical laws implies the conservation of energy.
  • Energy Conservation and Efficiency: In real-world systems, energy is always conserved, but useful energy conversions are rarely 100% efficient. Some energy is inevitably "lost" (transformed into less useful forms, like heat due to friction) during conversion, as seen in roller coasters or regenerative braking.

Important Terms

  • Power: The rate at which work is done or energy is transferred; measures how quickly energy is used or delivered.
  • Watt (W): The SI unit of power, equivalent to one joule of work done per second (1W = 1 J/s). Named after James Watt.
  • Kilowatt (kW): A unit of power equal to 1000 watts. Commonly used for larger appliances and motors.
  • Horsepower (hp): An older unit of power, still used for engines, approximately equal to 746 watts (1 hp ≈ 746W).
  • Kilowatt-hour (kWh): A unit of energy (not power), typically used on electricity bills. $1 \text{ kWh} = 3.6 \times 10^6 \text{ J}$.
  • Noether's Theorem: A mathematical theorem stating that every continuous symmetry in the laws of physics corresponds to a conserved quantity (e.g., time symmetry for energy conservation, spatial symmetry for momentum conservation).
  • Regenerative Braking: A braking system in electric vehicles where the electric motor acts as a generator, converting the car's kinetic energy back into electrical energy to be stored in the battery, rather than dissipating it as heat through friction.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Power

Power is a crucial concept in physics because it quantifies the rate at which work is done. Imagine two people pushing identical boxes across a floor. If they both apply the same force over the same distance, they do the same amount of work. However, if one person pushes the box twice as fast, that person is exerting twice the power. Power highlights the efficiency or speed of energy transfer.

Calculating Power

There are two primary formulas to calculate power:

  1. Power as Work over Time: The most fundamental definition of power is: $P = \frac{W}{t}$ Where:

    • $P$ is power, measured in watts (W).
    • $W$ is the work done, measured in joules (J).
    • $t$ is the time taken, measured in seconds (s). This formula is used when you know the total work done and the time it took to do it.

  2. Power as Force times Velocity: Since work ($W$) is defined as force ($F$) multiplied by displacement ($d$) (when force and displacement are in the same direction), we can substitute $W = Fd$ into the first power formula: $P = \frac{Fd}{t}$ Recognizing that displacement over time ($\frac{d}{t}$) is equal to speed ($v$), we get: $P = Fv$ Where:

    • $P$ is power in watts (W).
    • $F$ is the force applied in newtons (N).
    • $v$ is the speed (or velocity component in the direction of the force) in meters per second (m/s). This formula is particularly useful when you know the force being applied and the constant speed at which an object is moving.

Units of Power

  • Watt (W): The SI unit, $1 \text{ W} = 1 \text{ J/s}$. This means that a device with a power rating of 100 W uses or delivers 100 joules of energy every second.
  • Kilowatt (kW): A larger unit, $1 \text{ kW} = 1000 \text{ W}$. Electric motors, large appliances, and car engines often have ratings in kilowatts.
  • Horsepower (hp): An older, non-SI unit still used for engines (e.g., in cars). $1 \text{ hp} \approx 746 \text{ W}$.
  • Kilowatt-hour (kWh): Crucially, the kilowatt-hour is a unit of energy, not power. It represents the energy consumed by a 1-kilowatt device operating for one hour. $1 \text{ kWh} = (1000 \text{ W})(3600 \text{ s}) = 3,600,000 \text{ J} = 3.6 \text{ MJ}$. Electricity bills charge based on the total energy consumed in kilowatt-hours, reflecting how much energy your appliances use over time, not their instantaneous power output.

Example: Calculating Power Output

Let's consider a student climbing stairs:

  • Problem: A 60 kg student climbs a 4.0 m staircase in 6.0 s. What is the student's power output?
  • Solution:
    1. Calculate Work Done: The work done is against gravity, so the force is the student's weight ($mg$). $W = Fd = mgd = (60 \text{ kg})(9.8 \text{ m/s}^2)(4.0 \text{ m}) = 2352 \text{ J}$
    2. Calculate Power: $P = \frac{W}{t} = \frac{2352 \text{ J}}{6.0 \text{ s}} = 392 \text{ W}$ The student's power output is approximately 390 W, which is about half a horsepower.

Power in Everyday Life

Power ratings are ubiquitous. Your home appliances (microwaves, light bulbs, refrigerators) all have power ratings, typically in watts or kilowatts, indicating how quickly they consume electrical energy. Your electricity bill calculates energy consumption in kilowatt-hours (kWh) because it's a measure of total energy used over time, determining the cost.

Emmy Noether and the Conservation of Energy

The concept of energy conservation is one of the most fundamental principles in physics. Emmy Noether, a brilliant German mathematician, provided a deeper understanding of why conservation laws exist through Noether's Theorem. She proved that every continuous symmetry in the laws of physics corresponds to a conserved quantity.

  • Time Symmetry: If the laws of physics remain the same over time (they don't change from moment to moment), then energy is conserved.
  • Spatial Symmetry: If the laws of physics are the same at every location in space, then momentum is conserved.
  • Rotational Symmetry: If the laws of physics are the same regardless of orientation, then angular momentum is conserved. Noether's work shows that energy conservation is not just an observation but a profound mathematical consequence of the universe's consistent behavior over time. Despite her immense contributions, she faced significant discrimination due to her gender and Jewish heritage.

Investigation: Roller Coaster Energy

This investigation explores the practical application of energy conservation and the reality of energy losses in a physical system.

  • Goal: To observe how well the conservation of energy predicts a marble's behavior on a track and to quantify energy losses.
  • Procedure: By releasing a marble from a certain height and observing the maximum height it reaches on a subsequent hill, students can compare the ideal (frictionless) prediction with actual results.
  • Key Findings:
    • The marble will never reach its initial starting height on a second hill.
    • This discrepancy is due to friction and other energy dissipation mechanisms (like air resistance and sound), which convert some of the mechanical energy into heat.
    • The "efficiency" of the track, calculated as the ratio of the maximum height reached to the starting height, will typically be between 70-85% for a well-built track.
    • Higher starting heights might lead to slightly lower efficiency because increased speed often results in greater friction losses.
  • Connection: This demonstrates that while total energy is always conserved (transformed into other forms like heat), the useful mechanical energy is not 100% conserved due to inefficiencies.

Think Deeper: Regenerative Braking

Electric cars offer an excellent real-world example of energy conversion and efficiency.

  • How it Works: In conventional cars, braking converts kinetic energy into thermal energy via friction, which is wasted as heat. Electric cars with regenerative braking convert a significant portion of the car's kinetic energy back into electrical energy by running the motor in reverse, acting as a generator. This electrical energy is then stored in the battery.
  • Conservation of Energy: Regenerative braking directly applies the law of conservation of energy by transforming kinetic energy into a more useful form (electrical energy) instead of dissipating it entirely as heat.
  • Why Not 100% Efficient? Even regenerative braking cannot recover 100% of the kinetic energy. This is due to:
    • Internal resistance: The electric motor and wiring have electrical resistance, leading to some energy loss as heat.
    • Mechanical friction: There's still some friction in the drivetrain and other moving parts.
    • Other losses: Small amounts of energy are lost as sound and heat from air resistance.
    • Second Law of Thermodynamics: A fundamental principle states that perfect energy conversion is impossible; some energy will always be converted into less usable forms (like increased entropy) in any real-world process. Typical regenerative braking systems recover about 60-70% of the kinetic energy.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the difference between work and power. Provide an example where two individuals do the same amount of work but generate different amounts of power.
  2. Describe the two main formulas for calculating power ($P = W/t$ and $P = Fv$). In what situations would you typically use each formula?
  3. Discuss Emmy Noether's Theorem and its significance in understanding the conservation of energy. How does the "timelessness" of physics laws relate to energy conservation?
  4. In the Roller Coaster Energy investigation, why does the marble never reach its original starting height on a subsequent hill, even though the law of conservation of energy states that energy cannot be created or destroyed? Where does the "lost" energy go?
  5. An electric kettle has a power rating of 2200 W. If you boil water for 5 minutes, how much energy (in Joules and then in kilowatt-hours) did the kettle use? (Assume the kettle operates at its rated power for the entire duration.)
