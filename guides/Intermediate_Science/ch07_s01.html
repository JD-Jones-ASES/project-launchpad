Chapter 7, Section 1: Continental Drift Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces Alfred Wegener's revolutionary hypothesis of continental drift, which proposed that Earth's continents were once joined in a single supercontinent called Pangaea and have slowly moved to their current positions. It details the four main lines of evidence Wegener presented to support his idea but also explains why the scientific community initially rejected his hypothesis due to the lack of a plausible mechanism for continental movement. Despite its early rejection, Wegener's core idea was later vindicated and became a cornerstone of modern Earth science.

Key Concepts

  • Continental Drift Hypothesis: The idea proposed by Alfred Wegener in 1912 that Earth's continents are not fixed but have slowly moved across the planet's surface over hundreds of millions of years.
  • Pangaea: The single supercontinent ("all lands") that Wegener theorized all continents were once united in. According to his hypothesis, Pangaea began to break apart roughly 200 million years ago.
  • Evidence for Continental Fit: The striking observation that the coastlines of continents, particularly South America and Africa, fit together like pieces of a jigsaw puzzle, suggesting they were once joined. Modern analysis of continental shelves enhances this fit.
  • Fossil Distribution Evidence: The discovery of identical fossils of ancient land-dwelling plants (like Glossopteris) and freshwater animals (like Mesosaurus) on continents now separated by vast oceans, implying these landmasses were once connected.
  • Rock and Mountain Matches Evidence: The alignment of ancient mountain ranges and distinct rock formations across different continents (e.g., Appalachians in North America and Caledonian Mountains in Europe) when they are reassembled, indicating they were once part of a continuous geological structure.
  • Glacial Evidence: The presence of features indicating ancient glaciation (scratches, till deposits, polished bedrock) in regions that are currently tropical or subtropical. This evidence makes sense if these continents were formerly united near the South Pole.
  • Rejection Due to Lack of Mechanism: Despite compelling evidence, Wegener's hypothesis was largely rejected because he could not provide a physically plausible explanation for how continents could move through solid oceanic crust. His proposed forces (tidal, centrifugal) were deemed too weak.
  • Importance of Mechanism in Science: A crucial principle in science where a hypothesis, even with strong observational evidence, requires a credible, testable, and physically plausible explanation for how the observed phenomena occur to gain widespread acceptance.

Important Terms

  • Continental Drift: The hypothesis, proposed by Alfred Wegener in 1912, that Earth's continents were once joined in a single landmass (Pangaea) and have since moved slowly to their current positions.
  • Pangaea: From the Greek for "all lands," the name given by Alfred Wegener to the supercontinent he proposed existed roughly 200 million years ago, from which all present-day continents originated.

Detailed Notes

Continental Drift: An Introduction

  • The striking visual fit between the coastlines of South America and Africa has been observed by mapmakers since the 1600s.
  • In the early 20th century, German scientist Alfred Wegener proposed a revolutionary hypothesis: these continents were once connected and have since moved apart.

Wegener's Hypothesis of Continental Drift

  • Proposed: In 1912, Alfred Wegener introduced his continental drift hypothesis.
  • Core Idea: Earth's continents are not stationary but have slowly moved across the planet's surface over hundreds of millions of years.
  • Pangaea: Wegener suggested that all continents were once united in a single supercontinent he named Pangaea (meaning "all lands").
  • Timeline: He theorized that Pangaea began breaking apart approximately 200 million years ago, with the continental fragments drifting to their current locations.
  • Innovator: While others noticed the continental fit, Wegener was the first to systematically compile a wide range of evidence from geology, paleontology, and climatology to support his hypothesis.

Evidence for Continental Drift

Wegener's evidence was categorized into four main types:

  1. Continental Fit:

    • The most visually persuasive evidence was the near-perfect fit of continental coastlines separated by oceans.
    • The classic example is the eastern edge of South America and the western edge of Africa.
    • Wegener also demonstrated how other landmasses, like North America, Europe, and Greenland, could be assembled into a coherent whole.
    • Modern computer analyses, using the edges of the continental shelves (which are underwater extensions of the continents) rather than just the visible coastlines, show an even more precise fit.

  2. Fossil Distribution:

    • Identical fossils of ancient plants and animals were found on continents now thousands of kilometers apart, separated by vast oceans.
    • Mesosaurus: Fossils of this small freshwater reptile (lived ~270 million years ago) are found exclusively in southern Africa and eastern South America. As a freshwater animal, it could not have swum across the salty Atlantic Ocean, implying the continents were connected.
    • Glossopteris: Fossils of this fern have been discovered in South America, Africa, India, Antarctica, and Australia. The widespread distribution of this land-dwelling plant across widely separated continents suggests they were once part of a continuous landmass.

  3. Rock and Mountain Matches:

    • When the continents are reassembled into Pangaea, specific mountain ranges and rock formations on currently opposite sides of the Atlantic Ocean align perfectly.
    • Example: The Appalachian Mountains in eastern North America show remarkable alignment and geological similarity (rock types, ages, structural features) with the Caledonian Mountains in Scotland and Scandinavia. Geologists believe they were once a single, continuous mountain chain that was later separated by continental movement.

  4. Glacial Evidence:

    • Wegener found evidence of widespread ancient glaciation in regions that are currently tropical or subtropical, including southern Africa, India, South America, and Australia.
    • This evidence included scratched and grooved rocks, deposits of glacial till (sediment left by glaciers), and polished bedrock surfaces.
    • Such features indicate that these areas were once covered by massive ice sheets, which is impossible in their current warm, equatorial locations.
    • However, if these continents were united near the South Pole as part of Pangaea, the glacial evidence makes perfect sense. The direction of glacial scratches, when continents were reassembled, even converged toward a common center of ice accumulation.

Why the Hypothesis Was Rejected

  • Lack of Mechanism: The primary reason the scientific community largely rejected Wegener's hypothesis was his inability to explain how continents moved.
  • Plausibility Issue: Scientists questioned what force could possibly be strong enough to push enormous landmasses through the solid rock of the ocean floor.
  • Wegener's Proposed Mechanisms: He suggested that continents plowed through the oceanic crust like icebreakers, driven by tidal forces and the centrifugal effect of Earth's rotation.
  • Physicists' Rebuttal: Physicists quickly demonstrated that these proposed forces were far too weak to move continents, discrediting Wegener's mechanism.
  • Nature of Science: This episode highlights a crucial scientific principle: a hypothesis, no matter how strong its observational evidence, often requires a credible, testable, and physically plausible explanation for how something happens to be accepted. Wegener showed that continents appeared to have moved, but not how.

Alfred Wegener (1880–1930)

  • Background: A German meteorologist, geophysicist, and polar explorer, originally trained as an astronomer.
  • Contribution: He dedicated years to gathering multidisciplinary evidence for continental drift, publishing his ideas in "The Origin of Continents and Oceans" (1915).
  • Opposition: Faced strong criticism and rejection from the geological establishment, particularly in North America and Britain.
  • Persistence: Despite the opposition, Wegener continued to refine and defend his hypothesis until his death.
  • Tragic End: He died in 1930 during an expedition on the Greenland ice sheet at age fifty, never seeing his core idea accepted.
  • Vindication: Decades after his death, in the 1960s, the discovery of seafloor spreading provided the missing mechanism, leading to the development of the theory of plate tectonics, which transformed Wegener's rejected hypothesis into the foundation of modern Earth science.
  • Scientific Legacy: Wegener's story is a powerful testament to the importance of persistence in science and how initial scientific consensus can evolve with new evidence and mechanisms.

Review Questions

  1. What was Alfred Wegener's hypothesis of continental drift, and what did he name the single supercontinent he proposed?
  2. Describe at least three distinct lines of evidence Wegener used to support his hypothesis. For each, explain how the evidence suggested that continents had once been connected.
  3. Despite the compelling evidence he presented, why did the scientific community largely reject Wegener's hypothesis of continental drift for several decades?
  4. Explain the significance of a "mechanism" in scientific inquiry, using Wegener's experience with continental drift as a primary example.
  5. How does Alfred Wegener's personal story illustrate the dynamic and sometimes challenging nature of scientific progress? What was the ultimate fate of his core idea?
