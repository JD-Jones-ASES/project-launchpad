CHAPTER 21, SECTION 4: Speciation and the Tree of Life

Overview

This section explores the dynamic processes through which new species arise (speciation) and old ones disappear (extinction), shaping the diversity of life on Earth. It delves into the mechanisms of speciation, particularly allopatric speciation, and introduces adaptive radiation as a pattern of diversification. Finally, it explains how scientists represent evolutionary relationships using cladograms and how modern classification reflects these deep evolutionary connections.

Key Concepts

  • Biological Species Concept: Defines a species as a group of organisms that can naturally interbreed and produce fertile offspring. This concept is central to understanding when populations become distinct species.
  • Speciation: The evolutionary process where one species splits into two or more distinct species. It typically involves reproductive isolation, preventing gene flow between populations.
  • Geographic Isolation and Allopatric Speciation: The most common way new species form, where a physical barrier separates a population, leading to independent evolution and eventual reproductive isolation.
  • Adaptive Radiation: A rapid evolutionary event where a single ancestral species diversifies into many new species, each adapting to a different ecological niche. This often occurs in new environments or after mass extinctions.
  • Extinction: The complete disappearance of a species. While a natural process (background extinction), mass extinction events significantly reduce biodiversity and are often followed by periods of adaptive radiation.
  • Phylogenetics and Cladograms: Scientists use cladograms (a type of phylogenetic tree) to visualize and hypothesize the evolutionary relationships among organisms, based on shared derived characteristics and common ancestry.
  • Modern Classification: Biological classification systems (Domain to Species) are now primarily based on evolutionary relationships revealed by DNA analysis and cladistics, rather than just physical similarities.
  • Natural Selection Simulation: An activity demonstrating how environmental pressures (like background color) can lead to differential survival and reproduction, mimicking the process of natural selection and contributing to evolutionary change.

Important Terms

  • Biological species concept: A definition of species as a group of organisms that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring under natural conditions.
  • Speciation: The process by which one species splits into two or more distinct species.
  • Reproductive isolation: The inability of different species to interbreed, or to produce fertile offspring, often due to genetic differences, behavioral differences, or physical barriers.
  • Geographic isolation: A physical barrier (like a mountain range or ocean) that divides a population into two or more groups, preventing gene flow.
  • Allopatric speciation: A type of speciation that occurs when biological populations become geographically isolated from each other to an extent that prevents or interferes with gene flow.
  • Adaptive radiation: The rapid diversification of a single ancestral species into many new species, each adapted to a different ecological niche.
  • Extinction: The complete disappearance of a species; occurs when the last individual of a species dies.
  • Background extinction: The normal, ongoing loss of species due to environmental change, competition, and other factors, occurring at a relatively constant rate over long periods.
  • Mass extinction events: Episodes in which a large percentage of species disappear in a geologically short time, often caused by catastrophic global events.
  • Phylogenetic tree (or phylogeny): A diagram that shows the evolutionary relationships among a group of organisms, resembling a "family tree."
  • Cladogram: A simplified branching diagram that shows the evolutionary relationships among a group of organisms based on shared derived characteristics, with each branching point representing a common ancestor.
  • Node: A branching point on a cladogram, representing a common ancestor shared by the groups above it.
  • Shared derived characters: Traits that evolved in an ancestor and are present in its descendants but absent in more distantly related groups, used to construct cladograms.

Detailed Notes

What Defines a Species?

The most widely accepted definition, the biological species concept, states that a species is a group of organisms capable of interbreeding in nature and producing fertile offspring. If offspring are sterile (like a mule, the offspring of a horse and a donkey), the parent organisms are considered separate species. This concept highlights the importance of gene flow within a species and its absence between different species.

The Process of Speciation

Speciation is the fundamental process that drives biodiversity, involving one species dividing into two or more distinct species. This occurs when populations become reproductively isolated, meaning they can no longer interbreed, and subsequently evolve independently.

Geographic Isolation and Allopatric Speciation

The most common pathway to speciation is allopatric speciation, which begins with geographic isolation.

  1. Physical Barrier: A physical barrier (e.g., a new river, mountain range, or even a highway) divides an existing population into separate groups.
  2. Cessation of Gene Flow: Once separated, the isolated populations can no longer exchange genes.
  3. Independent Evolution: Each isolated group experiences different environmental conditions, leading to different natural selection pressures. They also undergo different random genetic drift.
  4. Accumulation of Differences: Over vast periods, these differences accumulate in their gene pools, leading to significant genetic divergence.
  5. Reproductive Isolation: Eventually, the genetic differences become so profound that even if the physical barrier is removed, the two groups can no longer interbreed and produce fertile offspring. They have become separate species.
  • Example: The Galápagos finches are a classic example, where an ancestral finch population colonized different islands, leading to isolation, adaptation to local food sources, and the evolution of many distinct finch species.

Adaptive Radiation

Adaptive radiation is a rapid form of speciation where a single ancestral species diversifies into many new species, each occupying a unique ecological niche. This often happens under specific conditions:

  • New Environments: Colonization of a new area with many available resources and niches (e.g., an island chain).
  • Mass Extinctions: Following a mass extinction event, many ecological niches become vacant, allowing surviving species to rapidly diversify and fill these roles.
  • Examples: The Galápagos finches, Hawaiian honeycreepers, and the immense diversification of mammals after the extinction of non-avian dinosaurs are prime examples.

Extinction: The End of a Lineage

Extinction is a natural process where a species ceases to exist. While over 99% of all species that have ever lived are now extinct, the rate of extinction varies:

  • Background Extinction: This is the normal, ongoing rate of species loss due to regular environmental changes, competition, predation, and other biological factors.
  • Mass Extinction Events: These are rare, catastrophic periods where a large percentage of Earth's species disappear in a geologically short time. The fossil record indicates five major mass extinctions, with the most recent being 66 million years ago, caused by an asteroid impact, which led to the demise of the non-avian dinosaurs.
  • Pattern: Mass extinctions are typically followed by periods of rapid adaptive radiation as surviving species evolve to fill the newly available ecological niches.

Phylogenetics and Cladograms: Mapping Evolutionary History

Scientists organize the vast diversity of life into an evolutionary "family tree" called a phylogenetic tree (or phylogeny). A simplified diagram used to analyze these relationships is a cladogram.

  • Cladogram Definition: A branching diagram that illustrates the evolutionary relationships among a group of organisms, primarily based on shared derived characteristics (traits that evolved in a common ancestor and are present in its descendants).
  • Reading a Cladogram:
    • Tips: Represent individual species or groups of organisms that exist today.
    • Nodes: Represent a common ancestor shared by all the groups that branch off from that point. The closer a node is to two tips, the more recently those groups shared a common ancestor, meaning they are more closely related.
    • Branches: Show the evolutionary lineages extending from common ancestors to descendant species.

Classification Review

The established hierarchy of biological classification, from broadest to most specific, is: Domain $$ Kingdom $$ Phylum $$ Class $$ Order $$ Family $$ Genus $$ Species

  • Mnemonic: Dear King Philip Came Over For Good Spaghetti. Modern classification, or cladistics, heavily relies on evolutionary relationships revealed by genetic evidence (DNA analysis) and shared derived characters, rather than just superficial physical appearance. This explains why seemingly similar organisms (like sharks and dolphins) might be distantly related, while outwardly different organisms (like birds and crocodiles) can be close relatives due to their shared evolutionary history.

Investigation: Simulating Natural Selection

This hands-on activity provides a simplified model of natural selection in action.

  • Purpose: To observe how environmental conditions (like background color) can influence the survival rates of different phenotypes (prey colors) within a population.
  • Mechanism: Predators (students) hunt for "prey" (colored paper squares) on a specific background (green fabric/grass). Prey that are camouflaged blend in better and are harder to find, thus having a higher "survival rate."
  • Key Principles Demonstrated:
    1. Variation: The prey population has different color variations.
    2. Competition/Predation: Predators create a selective pressure.
    3. Differential Survival: Some colors survive better than others due to camouflage.
    4. Reproduction: Survivors "reproduce," passing on their advantageous traits.
    5. Change in Population: Over generations, the population composition shifts, with camouflaged colors becoming more prevalent, illustrating evolution by natural selection.
  • Expected Results: On a green background, green and brown prey typically have the highest survival rates, leading to an increase in their proportion over generations, while less camouflaged colors (red, blue, yellow) decline or go "extinct."
  • Limitations: This simulation simplifies many aspects of real natural selection (e.g., no genetic inheritance mechanism shown, predator behavior is simplified, environmental factors are static). However, it effectively conveys the core principles.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the biological species concept and provide an example that illustrates its definition. Why is the ability to produce fertile offspring crucial?
  2. Describe the process of allopatric speciation, detailing the sequence of events from geographic isolation to the formation of distinct species. Use the Galápagos finches as an example.
  3. What is adaptive radiation, and under what circumstances is it most likely to occur? How do mass extinction events relate to adaptive radiation?
  4. How do scientists use cladograms to represent evolutionary relationships? Explain the significance of nodes and shared derived characters in interpreting a cladogram.
  5. Referring to the "Simulating Natural Selection" investigation, explain how the results demonstrate the four key steps of natural selection. What would be the predicted outcome if the experiment were conducted on a brown background instead of a green one, and why?
