Acceleration Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces acceleration as the precise physics term for how quickly an object's velocity changes. It defines acceleration as a vector quantity, explains its calculation using initial and final velocities over a time interval, and clarifies how the sign of acceleration relates to an object speeding up or slowing down. Furthermore, it details how acceleration is represented by the slope of a velocity-time graph.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of Acceleration: Acceleration is the rate at which an object's velocity changes over time. It's a fundamental concept in kinematics, describing how motion itself changes.
  • Acceleration as a Vector Quantity: Like velocity, acceleration has both magnitude (how much velocity changes) and direction. Its direction is crucial for understanding whether an object is speeding up, slowing down, or changing direction.
  • Calculating Acceleration: Acceleration ($a$) is calculated as the change in velocity ($\Delta v$) divided by the time interval ($\Delta t$) over which the change occurs: $a = \Delta v / \Delta t = (v_f - v_i) / (t_f - t_i)$.
  • Units of Acceleration: The SI unit for acceleration is meters per second squared ($m/s^2$). This means that the velocity changes by a certain number of meters per second during each second.
  • Positive and Negative Acceleration:
    • Positive acceleration means velocity is becoming more positive (speeding up in the positive direction or slowing down while moving in the negative direction towards zero).
    • Negative acceleration means velocity is becoming more negative (slowing down while moving in the positive direction or speeding up in the negative direction).
  • Relationship between Velocity and Acceleration Signs: An object speeds up when its velocity and acceleration have the same sign. An object slows down when its velocity and acceleration have opposite signs.
  • Deceleration vs. Negative Acceleration: While "deceleration" is commonly used to mean slowing down, in physics, it's more precise to refer to acceleration with a sign opposite to the velocity's direction. Negative acceleration doesn't always mean slowing down.
  • Acceleration on a Velocity-Time Graph: The slope of a velocity-time ($v$-$t$) graph represents the acceleration of an object. A straight line on a $v$-$t$ graph indicates uniform (constant) acceleration.
  • Uniform Acceleration: This refers to acceleration that remains constant over time. It simplifies motion problems significantly and is a common assumption in introductory physics.

Important Terms

  • Acceleration: The rate at which an object's velocity changes over time. It is a vector quantity, measured in meters per second squared ($m/s^2$).
  • Deceleration: A common term for acceleration that opposes the direction of motion, causing an object to slow down. In physics, it's generally understood as acceleration with a sign opposite to the velocity.
  • Uniform acceleration: Acceleration that remains constant in both magnitude and direction over a given time interval. On a velocity-time graph, this is represented by a straight line.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Acceleration

In everyday life, we encounter acceleration constantly—a car speeding up, a ball slowing down as it reaches the peak of its flight, or a roller coaster changing speed dramatically. In physics, acceleration is given a precise definition: it's the measure of how quickly an object's velocity changes. Since velocity includes both speed and direction, a change in either constitutes acceleration.

Defining Acceleration Mathematically

Acceleration ($a$) is defined as the change in velocity ($\Delta v$) divided by the time interval ($\Delta t$) during which that change occurs. The formula for average acceleration is: $$a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v_f - v_i}{t_f - t_i}$$ Where:

  • $v_f$ is the final velocity.
  • $v_i$ is the initial velocity.
  • $t_f$ is the final time.
  • $t_i$ is the initial time, so $\Delta t = t_f - t_i$.

The SI unit for acceleration is meters per second squared ($m/s^2$). This unit signifies that the velocity, measured in $m/s$, changes by a certain amount each second. For example, an acceleration of $2,m/s^2$ means the object's velocity increases by $2,m/s$ every second.

Example: If a car goes from $0,m/s$ to $20,m/s$ in $10,s$: $$a = \frac{20,m/s - 0,m/s}{10,s} = 2,m/s^2$$ This means the car's velocity increases by $2,m/s$ every second. After 1 second, it's at $2,m/s$; after 2 seconds, $4,m/s$, and so on.

Positive and Negative Acceleration

Since velocity is a vector quantity (having both magnitude and direction), and acceleration describes the change in velocity, acceleration is also a vector. Its sign (positive or negative) is crucial and depends on the chosen positive direction of motion.

  • Positive Acceleration:
    • If an object is moving in the positive direction, positive acceleration means it is speeding up. (e.g., a car accelerating forward).
    • If an object is moving in the negative direction, positive acceleration means it is slowing down as its velocity becomes less negative (e.g., a car moving backward and applying brakes).
  • Negative Acceleration:
    • If an object is moving in the positive direction, negative acceleration means it is slowing down. (e.g., a car applying brakes while moving forward).
    • If an object is moving in the negative direction, negative acceleration means it is speeding up in the negative direction. (e.g., a car moving backward and pressing the gas pedal).

Important Rule: An object speeds up when its velocity and acceleration have the same sign. An object slows down when its velocity and acceleration have opposite signs.

The term "deceleration" is often used to describe slowing down. In physics, however, we typically describe this as "negative acceleration" when the chosen positive direction is the direction of initial motion. Be careful not to equate negative acceleration solely with slowing down, as shown in the examples above.

Calculating Acceleration (Worked Example)

Let's review the provided example:

Problem: A bicycle rider traveling at $4,m/s$ begins pedaling harder and reaches a velocity of $12,m/s$ in $5,s$. What is the rider's acceleration?

Solution:

  1. Identify known values:
    • Initial velocity ($v_i$): $4,m/s$
    • Final velocity ($v_f$): $12,m/s$
    • Time interval ($\Delta t$): $5,s$
  2. Write the formula: $$a = \frac{v_f - v_i}{\Delta t}$$
  3. Substitute and solve: $$a = \frac{12,m/s - 4,m/s}{5,s} = \frac{8,m/s}{5,s} = 1.6,m/s^2$$ Answer: The rider's acceleration is $1.6,m/s^2$. This means the rider's velocity increases by $1.6,m/s$ every second.

Acceleration on a Velocity-Time Graph

Just as the slope of a position-time graph gives velocity, the slope of a velocity-time ($v$-$t$) graph gives acceleration. $$slope\ of\ v\text{-}t\ graph = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = a$$ Interpreting $v$-$t$ graphs for acceleration:

  • Positive slope: Indicates positive acceleration (velocity is increasing in the positive direction, or decreasing in the negative direction towards zero).
  • Negative slope: Indicates negative acceleration (velocity is decreasing in the positive direction, or increasing in the negative direction).
  • Zero slope (horizontal line): Indicates zero acceleration, meaning the velocity is constant.
  • Steeper slope: Represents a greater magnitude of acceleration, meaning the velocity is changing more rapidly.

When the slope of a $v$-$t$ graph is a straight line (constant slope), the object is undergoing uniform acceleration. This is a common assumption in many physics problems because it simplifies calculations, leading to the kinematic equations that will be explored in the next section.

Think Deeper: Consider a ball thrown straight up. At the very top of its arc, its instantaneous velocity is zero. Does it also have zero acceleration at that moment? No, it does not have zero acceleration. Throughout its entire flight (after leaving the hand and before landing), the ball is under the influence of gravity. Gravity causes a constant downward acceleration, approximately $9.8,m/s^2$. Even at the peak, where the velocity is momentarily zero, gravity is still acting on the ball, constantly trying to pull it downwards and change its velocity. If the acceleration were zero, the ball would hover there indefinitely.

Review Questions

  1. Define acceleration in your own words. Why is it considered a vector quantity?
  2. Explain what the unit $m/s^2$ means in the context of acceleration. If an object has an acceleration of $-3,m/s^2$, what does that imply about its motion if it is initially moving in the positive direction? What if it's initially moving in the negative direction?
  3. A car accelerates from $10,m/s$ to $30,m/s$ in $5,s$. Calculate its acceleration. If it then brakes, slowing down from $30,m/s$ to $15,m/s$ in $3,s$, what is its acceleration during braking?
  4. Describe how you would interpret the acceleration of an object from its velocity-time graph. What does a horizontal line mean? What does a steeply sloping line mean compared to a gently sloping line?
  5. Imagine a train pulling into a station. It is moving forward but slowing down. Describe the sign of its velocity and the sign of its acceleration, assuming forward is the positive direction. Justify your answer using the rule about speeding up and slowing down.
