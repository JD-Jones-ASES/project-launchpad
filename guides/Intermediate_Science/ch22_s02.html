Chapter 22, Section 2: The Moon

Overview

This section explores Earth's nearest celestial neighbor, the Moon, detailing its appearance, gravitational influence, and origin. We will learn how lunar phases are created by the Moon's orbit around Earth, the mechanics behind solar and lunar eclipses, and how the Moon's gravity drives Earth's ocean tides. Finally, the section touches upon the leading scientific theory for the Moon's formation.

Key Concepts

  • Lunar Phases: The Moon doesn't produce its own light; its varying appearance (phases) is due to different amounts of its sunlit side facing Earth as it orbits. The cycle takes about 29.5 days.
  • Eclipses: These events occur when one celestial body passes into the shadow of another. Solar eclipses happen during a New Moon when the Moon blocks the Sun, casting a shadow on Earth. Lunar eclipses occur during a Full Moon when Earth's shadow falls on the Moon. The tilt of the Moon's orbit prevents eclipses every month.
  • Tidal Forces: The Moon's gravity creates bulges in Earth's oceans on both the near and far sides, leading to two high and two low tides daily. The Sun also contributes to tides, creating stronger "spring tides" when aligned with the Moon and Earth, and weaker "neap tides" when at right angles.
  • Giant Impact Hypothesis: The leading theory for the Moon's formation suggests that a Mars-sized body collided with early Earth, ejecting debris that coalesced to form the Moon.

Important Terms

  • Lunar phase: The changing appearance of the Moon as seen from Earth, caused by the varying angle of sunlight illuminating the Moon's surface during its orbit.
  • Eclipse: An event in which one celestial body passes into the shadow of another, temporarily blocking sunlight.
  • Solar eclipse: An eclipse that occurs when the Moon passes directly between the Sun and Earth, casting a shadow on Earth's surface.
  • Lunar eclipse: An eclipse that occurs when Earth passes between the Sun and Moon, and Earth's shadow falls on the Moon.
  • Umbra: The darkest, central part of a shadow, where light is completely blocked. (Specifically mentioned for the Moon's shadow during a total solar eclipse).
  • Tidal force: The gravitational force exerted by one body on another that varies across the second body's diameter, causing it to stretch or deform.
  • Spring tides: Exceptionally high and low tides that occur when the Sun, Moon, and Earth are aligned (during New Moon and Full Moon), combining their tidal forces.
  • Neap tides: Smaller tides that occur when the Sun and Moon are at right angles relative to Earth (during First and Third Quarter), causing their tidal forces to work against each other.
  • Giant impact hypothesis: The leading scientific theory for the Moon's origin, proposing that the Moon formed from debris ejected after a Mars-sized body collided with early Earth.

Detailed Notes

The Moon: Earth's Celestial Neighbor

  • The Moon is Earth's closest celestial neighbor, orbiting at an average distance of about 384,400 kilometers.
  • It significantly influences Earth through its gravitational pull (tides), its phases (calendars), and its role in eclipses.

Lunar Phases

  • Nature of Lunar Light: The Moon does not generate its own light; it reflects sunlight.
  • Cause of Phases: As the Moon orbits Earth, the angle at which we view the sunlit portion of its surface changes. This cycle of changing appearances is called lunar phases.
  • Orbital Period: The Moon completes one full orbit around Earth, and thus one cycle of phases, approximately every 29.5 days.
  • Eight Major Phases (in order):
    1. New Moon: The Moon is directly between the Sun and Earth. The sunlit side faces away from us, making the Moon invisible or nearly so.
    2. Waxing Crescent: A thin sliver of the sunlit side appears on the right (in the Northern Hemisphere) and grows larger. "Waxing" means increasing.
    3. First Quarter: Half of the Moon's face is illuminated (the right half). It has completed one-quarter of its orbit.
    4. Waxing Gibbous: More than half of the Moon is illuminated, and the illuminated area continues to grow.
    5. Full Moon: The entire face visible from Earth is illuminated. Earth is positioned between the Sun and the Moon.
    6. Waning Gibbous: The illuminated area begins to shrink from the right side. "Waning" means decreasing.
    7. Third Quarter: Half of the Moon's face is illuminated (the left half).
    8. Waning Crescent: A thin sliver remains illuminated on the left side before the cycle returns to New Moon.
  • Important Clarification: Lunar phases are not caused by Earth's shadow. Earth's shadow only causes a lunar eclipse. Phases are a result of the changing geometry of the Sun, Moon, and Earth.

Eclipses

  • Definition: An eclipse occurs when one celestial body passes into the shadow of another, blocking light.
  • Solar Eclipses:
    • Timing: Occur during a New Moon.
    • Mechanism: The Moon passes directly between the Sun and Earth, casting its shadow onto Earth's surface.
    • Total Solar Eclipse: The Moon completely covers the Sun's disk, allowing the Sun's outer atmosphere (corona) to become visible.
    • Rarity: Total solar eclipses are rare at any specific location because the Moon's darkest shadow (umbra) is very narrow (about 270 km wide).
  • Lunar Eclipses:
    • Timing: Occur during a Full Moon.
    • Mechanism: Earth passes directly between the Sun and Moon, casting Earth's shadow onto the Moon.
    • Visibility: Visible from the entire night side of Earth, making them more commonly observed than solar eclipses.
    • Coloration: During a total lunar eclipse, the Moon often appears reddish because Earth's atmosphere refracts red light into the shadow zone.
  • Why Not Every Month?
    • The Moon's orbit is tilted approximately 5 degrees relative to Earth's orbit around the Sun.
    • Most months, the Moon passes slightly above or below the Sun (during New Moon) or above or below Earth's shadow (during Full Moon).
    • Eclipses only happen when the Sun, Moon, and Earth are precisely aligned.

Tidal Forces

  • Definition: Tidal forces are gravitational forces that vary across a body's diameter, causing it to stretch or bulge.
  • Moon's Influence:
    • The Moon's gravity pulls on the ocean water closest to it, creating a "tidal bulge" on the near side of Earth.
    • A second bulge forms on the opposite side of Earth because the Moon's gravity pulls Earth's solid body away from the water on the far side.
    • As Earth rotates, most coastlines experience two high tides and two low tides each day.
  • Sun's Influence:
    • The Sun also exerts tidal forces, but its effect is about half that of the Moon due to its much greater distance.
  • Spring Tides:
    • Occur during New Moon and Full Moon, when the Sun, Moon, and Earth are aligned.
    • The gravitational pulls of the Sun and Moon combine, resulting in extra-high high tides and extra-low low tides.
  • Neap Tides:
    • Occur during First Quarter and Third Quarter phases, when the Sun and Moon are at right angles relative to Earth.
    • Their gravitational forces work against each other, leading to smaller tidal ranges (lower high tides and higher low tides).

Formation of the Moon

  • Giant Impact Hypothesis: This is the leading scientific theory for the Moon's origin.
    • Approximately 4.5 billion years ago, a Mars-sized body (dubbed "Theia") is thought to have collided with the early Earth.
    • The immense energy from this impact ejected a vast cloud of molten rock and debris into orbit around Earth.
    • This material gradually coalesced and cooled to form the Moon.
  • Supporting Evidence:
    • Composition: The Moon's composition is similar to Earth's mantle (the layer below the crust).
    • Iron Core: The Moon has a relatively small iron core, consistent with Theia's and Earth's cores merging during the impact, and only mantle material being ejected.
    • Volatile Elements: The Moon's scarcity of volatile (easily vaporized) elements suggests the extreme heat of a collision boiled them away.
