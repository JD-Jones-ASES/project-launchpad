Chapter 14, Section 1: Work Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the precise definition of "work" in physics, which differs significantly from its everyday usage. It establishes the mathematical formula for calculating work, emphasizing that work is done only when a force causes an object to move a certain distance in the direction of the force. The section also explains how the angle between the force and displacement affects the amount of work done and introduces the trigonometric cosine function for this purpose, finally categorizing work into positive, negative, and zero based on the energy transfer involved.

Key Concepts

  • Physics Definition of Work: In physics, work is done only when a force causes an object to undergo a displacement. It is the product of the force component parallel to the displacement and the magnitude of the displacement. If there's no movement or no force component in the direction of movement, no work is done.
  • The Work Equation ($W = Fd$): When a constant force acts directly in the direction of an object's motion over a distance $d$, the work done ($W$) is the product of the force ($F$) and the distance.
  • General Work Equation ($W = Fd \cos \theta$): When a force acts at an angle ($\theta$) to the direction of an object's displacement, only the component of the force parallel to the displacement does work. The cosine function ($\cos \theta$) accounts for this component, making it the general formula for work.
  • Role of Cosine Function: The cosine function (specifically $\cos \theta$) is used to find the component of a force that acts along the direction of motion. This is crucial when the force is not perfectly aligned with the displacement.
  • Positive Work ($W > 0$): Occurs when a force has a component in the direction of motion, transferring energy to the object and typically increasing its kinetic energy.
  • Negative Work ($W < 0$): Occurs when a force has a component opposite to the direction of motion, transferring energy away from the object and typically decreasing its kinetic energy.
  • Zero Work ($W = 0$): Occurs when a force is perpendicular to the direction of motion, or when there is no displacement, meaning no energy is transferred to or from the object.

Important Terms

  • Work (Physics Definition): The product of the force applied to an object and the displacement of the object in the direction of the force. Work is done only when a force causes an object to move.
  • Joule (J): The SI unit of work and energy. One joule is equal to the work done when a force of one newton moves an object one meter in the direction of the force ($1 J = 1 N \cdot m$).
  • Cosine ($\cos \theta$): A trigonometric function defined in a right triangle as the ratio of the length of the side adjacent to a given angle ($\theta$) to the length of the hypotenuse. In the context of work, it gives the fraction of the force that acts in the direction of motion.
  • Hypotenuse: In a right triangle, the longest side, always opposite the right ($90^\circ$) angle. In the work formula, it can represent the total applied force.
  • Adjacent Side: In a right triangle, the side next to a given angle ($\theta$) that is not the hypotenuse. In the work formula, it represents the component of force acting in the direction of motion.
  • Opposite Side: In a right triangle, the side directly across from a given angle ($\theta$). (Not directly used in the work formula discussed here, but part of the general trigonometric context).

Detailed Notes

1. Defining Work in Physics

  • Everyday vs. Physics: In everyday language, "work" can mean any effort (mental or physical), like studying or holding a heavy bag. However, in physics, work has a very specific definition.
  • Physics Definition: Work is done only when a force causes an object to move. If you push against a wall and it doesn't move, you do no physics work on the wall, even if you exert effort.
  • Key Requirement: For work to be done, there must be both a force and a displacement in the direction of that force (or a component of that force).
  • Units: The SI unit of work is the joule (J).
    • One joule (J) is defined as one newton-meter ($1 J = 1 N \cdot m$).
    • It can also be expressed as $1 J = 1 kg \cdot m^2/s^2$.

2. The Work Equation

  • Simple Case (Force and Displacement in Same Direction):
    • When a constant force ($F$) acts on an object that moves a distance ($d$) in the exact direction of the force, the work done ($W$) is simply: [ W = Fd ]
  • General Case (Force at an Angle to Displacement):
    • Often, the force is not perfectly aligned with the direction of motion (e.g., pulling a suitcase handle at an angle).
    • Only the part of the force that acts along the direction of motion does work.
    • To account for this, the general work equation is used: [ W = Fd \cos \theta ] where $\theta$ (theta) is the angle between the force vector and the displacement vector.
    • The term $\cos \theta$ gives the fraction of the force that acts in the direction of motion.

3. Introduction to Trigonometry (The Cosine Function)

  • Purpose: This section introduces the cosine function, which is essential for understanding the general work equation.
  • Right Triangles: Trigonometry deals with the relationships between angles and sides of triangles, particularly right triangles (triangles with one $90^\circ$ angle).
    • Hypotenuse: The longest side, opposite the right angle. (Think of the total force $F$).
    • Adjacent Side: The side next to the angle $\theta$ (but not the hypotenuse). (Think of the effective force component in the direction of motion).
    • Opposite Side: The side across from the angle $\theta$.
  • Definition of Cosine: The cosine function for an angle $\theta$ in a right triangle is defined as: [ \cos \theta = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}} ]
  • Applying to Work: In the work formula, you can visualize the applied force ($F$) as the hypotenuse and the component of force along the direction of motion as the adjacent side. So, $F \cos \theta$ gives you the effective force in the direction of motion.
  • Key Cosine Values to Remember:
    • $\cos 0^\circ = 1$ (Force is entirely in the direction of motion)
    • $\cos 90^\circ = 0$ (Force is perpendicular to the direction of motion)
    • $\cos 180^\circ = -1$ (Force is entirely opposite to the direction of motion)

4. Positive, Negative, and Zero Work

The sign of the work indicates whether energy is being transferred to or from an object.

  • Positive Work ($W > 0$)

    • Condition: The force has a component in the direction of motion. The angle $\theta$ is between $0^\circ$ and $90^\circ$ (excluding $90^\circ$).
    • Energy Transfer: Energy is transferred to the object, typically increasing its kinetic energy.
    • Example: Pushing a box across the floor in the direction it moves ($\theta = 0^\circ$, $\cos 0^\circ = 1$). The pushing force does positive work.

  • Negative Work ($W < 0$)

    • Condition: The force has a component opposite to the direction of motion. The angle $\theta$ is between $90^\circ$ and $180^\circ$ (excluding $90^\circ$).
    • Energy Transfer: Energy is transferred from the object, typically decreasing its kinetic energy.
    • Example: Friction acting on a sliding box ($\theta = 180^\circ$, $\cos 180^\circ = -1$). The friction force does negative work.

  • Zero Work ($W = 0$)

    • Condition 1: The force is perpendicular to the direction of motion. The angle $\theta = 90^\circ$ ($\cos 90^\circ = 0$).
      • Energy Transfer: No energy is transferred to or from the object by this force.
      • Example: The normal force on a box sliding across a flat floor is perpendicular to the motion, so it does zero work. A waiter carrying a tray horizontally exerts an upward force (perpendicular to horizontal motion), so the waiter does zero physics work on the tray.
    • Condition 2: There is no displacement ($d = 0$), regardless of the applied force.
      • Energy Transfer: No energy is transferred.
      • Example: Holding a heavy box motionless. Although you exert force and feel effort, if the box doesn't move, you do zero physics work on it. (Your muscles are doing metabolic work, but no mechanical work on the object).

5. Calculating Work (Worked Example)

  • Problem: A student pulls a sled with a force of $50 N$ along a rope that makes a $60^\circ$ angle with the horizontal ground. The sled moves $10 m$ along the ground. How much work does the student do?
  • Solution Steps:
    1. Identify Knowns: $F = 50 N$, $d = 10 m$, $\theta = 60^\circ$.
    2. Apply Work Equation: $W = Fd \cos \theta$
    3. Substitute Values: $W = (50 N)(10 m)(\cos 60^\circ)$
    4. Recall Cosine Value: From the key values, $\cos 60^\circ = 0.5$.
    5. Calculate: $W = (50 N)(10 m)(0.5) = 250 J$.
  • Interpretation: Only the horizontal component of the force ($50 N \times 0.5 = 25 N$) does work. This horizontal component is multiplied by the distance moved to get the total work done.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the key difference between the everyday understanding of "work" and the precise definition used in physics. Provide an example of an activity that is considered "work" in daily life but not in physics.
  2. A student pushes a heavy textbook across a table. Identify two different forces acting on the textbook during this motion, and for each force, state whether it does positive, negative, or zero work on the textbook. Justify your answers using the work equation.
  3. Describe a scenario where a significant force is applied to an object, but zero physics work is done on that object. Explain your reasoning in terms of the work equation and the angle between force and displacement.
  4. If a force of $200 N$ is applied to an object, causing it to move $5 m$, what is the maximum possible work that could be done, and what is the minimum (most negative) possible work that could be done by this force? Explain the conditions under which these maximum and minimum values occur.
  5. Why is the cosine function, $\cos \theta$, essential in the general work equation $W = Fd \cos \theta$? What would happen if we simply used $W = Fd$ when the force and displacement are not aligned?
