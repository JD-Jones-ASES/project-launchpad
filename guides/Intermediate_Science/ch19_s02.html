Study Guide: Chapter 19, Section 2 - Sound Waves

Overview

This section explores the fundamental nature of sound as a longitudinal mechanical wave, explaining how it is generated by vibrations and travels through a medium via compressions and rarefactions. It delves into key characteristics like the speed of sound, and how wave properties such as frequency and amplitude relate to perceived qualities like pitch and loudness. Finally, it covers the intriguing phenomena of the Doppler effect and resonance, highlighting their applications in everyday life and technology.

Key Concepts

  • Sound as a Mechanical Wave: Sound is produced by vibrations and requires a material medium (solid, liquid, or gas) to propagate. It cannot travel through a vacuum.
  • Longitudinal Waves: Sound travels as a longitudinal wave, meaning the particles of the medium vibrate parallel to the direction of wave propagation, creating alternating regions of high pressure (compressions) and low pressure (rarefactions).
  • Speed of Sound: The speed of sound depends on the medium's properties (density and stiffness) and its temperature. It travels faster in denser, stiffer materials and at higher temperatures.
  • Pitch and Frequency: Pitch, the perceived highness or lowness of a sound, is determined by the wave's frequency. Higher frequency means higher pitch.
  • Loudness and Amplitude: Loudness, the perceived intensity of a sound, is related to the wave's amplitude (the maximum pressure variation). Greater amplitude means a louder sound and more energy carried by the wave.
  • Decibel Scale: Sound intensity is measured logarithmically in decibels (dB), where a 10 dB increase represents a tenfold increase in intensity.
  • The Doppler Effect: This is the apparent change in a wave's frequency (and thus sound's pitch) when the source and observer are in relative motion. The pitch increases as the source approaches and decreases as it moves away.
  • Resonance: A dramatic increase in a wave's amplitude when a vibrating force is applied at an object's natural frequency. This principle is crucial in the design of musical instruments.

Important Terms

  • Sound Wave: A longitudinal mechanical wave caused by vibrations, propagating through a medium.
  • Mechanical Wave: A wave that requires a medium through which to travel, transferring energy by the vibration of particles.
  • Compression: A region in a longitudinal wave where the particles of the medium are crowded together, corresponding to a region of higher pressure.
  • Rarefaction: A region in a longitudinal wave where the particles of the medium are spread apart, corresponding to a region of lower pressure.
  • Vacuum: A space entirely devoid of matter. Sound cannot travel through a vacuum.
  • Pitch: The perceived highness or lowness of a sound, primarily determined by its frequency.
  • Frequency: The number of wave cycles (or vibrations) that pass a point per unit of time, measured in Hertz (Hz).
  • Infrasound: Sound waves with frequencies below the range of human hearing (typically below 20 Hz).
  • Ultrasound: Sound waves with frequencies above the range of human hearing (typically above 20,000 Hz).
  • Loudness: The subjective perception of the intensity of a sound, related to the amplitude of the sound wave.
  • Amplitude: The maximum displacement or distance moved by a point on a vibrating body or wave measured from its equilibrium position; for sound, it's the maximum pressure variation from normal.
  • Decibel (dB): A logarithmic unit used to measure sound intensity.
  • Doppler Effect: The perceived change in the frequency of a wave when its source and observer are moving relative to each other.
  • Redshift: A phenomenon in astronomy where light from a source moving away from an observer appears to have longer (redder) wavelengths due to the Doppler effect.
  • Resonance: The phenomenon where an object vibrates with increased amplitude when a periodic force is applied at its natural frequency.

Detailed Notes

The Nature of Sound Waves

  • Sound is produced by vibrations. For example, a vibrating tuning fork pushes and pulls air molecules, creating a disturbance.
  • This disturbance travels outward as a longitudinal wave, meaning the particles of the medium vibrate parallel to the direction the wave travels.
  • These vibrations create alternating regions of high pressure (where molecules are pushed together, called compressions) and low pressure (where molecules are spread apart, called rarefactions).
  • When these compressions and rarefactions reach your ear, your brain interprets them as sound.

How Sound Travels: A Mechanical Wave

  • Sound is a mechanical wave, meaning it requires a medium (like air, water, or solids) to transmit energy.
  • The vibration of one particle transmits energy to its neighbor, causing a chain reaction of compressions and rarefactions.
  • Individual particles only vibrate a small distance back and forth; the wave itself propagates over large distances.
  • Sound cannot travel through a vacuum because there are no particles to transmit the vibrations. This is why sound cannot be heard in outer space. Spacecraft use electromagnetic waves (like radio waves) for communication.

Speed of Sound

  • The speed at which sound travels depends on two main factors:
    1. The Medium: Sound travels faster through denser and stiffer materials. This is because particles in these materials are closer together and can transmit vibrations more efficiently.
    2. Temperature: Sound travels faster in warmer mediums.
  • In air at 20°C, the speed of sound is approximately 343 meters per second (m/s), or about 1 kilometer every three seconds.
  • Comparing Sound and Light: Light travels incredibly fast (about 3 x 10^8 m/s), nearly a million times faster than sound. This is why you see lightning before you hear thunder.
    • Think Deeper: If you see a lightning flash and count 9 seconds before hearing thunder, the distance to the lightning strike can be estimated using the speed of sound.
      • Distance = Speed × Time
      • Distance = 343 m/s × 9 s = 3087 meters (or approximately 3.1 kilometers).
      • The travel time of light over this distance is negligible compared to the 9 seconds for sound.

Pitch and Frequency

  • Pitch is the perceived highness or lowness of a sound.
  • Pitch is directly determined by the frequency of the sound wave.
    • Higher frequency = Higher pitch (e.g., a piccolo).
    • Lower frequency = Lower pitch (e.g., a bass drum).
  • The human ear can typically detect frequencies ranging from about 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz.
  • Infrasound: Frequencies below 20 Hz (e.g., used by elephants and whales for long-distance communication).
  • Ultrasound: Frequencies above 20,000 Hz (e.g., used in medical imaging, and by bats and dolphins for echolocation).

Loudness, Amplitude, and Decibels

  • Loudness is the perceived intensity of a sound.
  • Loudness is related to the amplitude of the sound wave, which is the maximum pressure variation from normal.
    • A louder sound has a greater amplitude, meaning larger compressions and more extreme rarefactions.
    • Waves with greater amplitude carry more energy.
  • Decibel (dB) Scale: Sound intensity is measured using the decibel scale, which is logarithmic.
    • Every increase of 10 dB represents a tenfold increase in sound intensity.
    • Example: A 60 dB conversation is ten times more intense than a 50 dB quiet room. A 70 dB vacuum cleaner is ten times more intense than the conversation.
  • Safety Note: Prolonged exposure to sounds above 85 dB can cause permanent hearing damage. Always use ear protection in loud environments.

The Doppler Effect

  • The Doppler effect is the apparent change in the perceived frequency (and thus pitch) of a wave when the source and the observer are moving relative to each other.
  • Approaching Source: When a sound source moves toward an observer, each successive wave compression is emitted from a position closer to the observer. This causes the compressions to arrive more frequently, resulting in an increased perceived frequency (higher pitch).
  • Receding Source: When a sound source moves away from an observer, each successive wave compression is emitted from a position farther away. This causes the compressions to arrive less frequently, resulting in a decreased perceived frequency (lower pitch).
  • Example: The changing pitch of a fire truck siren as it approaches and then passes you.
  • Not Limited to Sound: The Doppler effect also applies to light waves. Astronomers observe redshift (light shifted toward longer, redder wavelengths) from galaxies moving away from Earth, providing evidence for the expansion of the universe.

Resonance and Musical Instruments

  • Resonance is a dramatic increase in amplitude that occurs when a vibrating force is applied at an object's natural frequency.
  • Every object has one or more natural frequencies at which it vibrates most easily.
  • Example: Pushing a child on a swing at the correct timing (matching its natural frequency) makes the swing's amplitude grow larger.
  • Applications:
    • A singer can shatter a wine glass by hitting a note at the glass's natural frequency.
    • A tuning fork held near a specially designed hollow box will produce a much louder sound if the box resonates at the fork's frequency.
  • Musical Instruments: Instruments are designed to utilize resonance to amplify and create specific sounds.
    • A guitar string vibrates at a certain frequency, and the hollow body of the guitar resonates to amplify that sound.
    • A flute player changes the effective length of the resonating air column by opening and closing holes. Shorter columns resonate at higher frequencies (higher pitch), and longer columns at lower frequencies (lower pitch).

Sci-Connection: Ultrasound in Medicine

  • Medical ultrasound uses high-frequency sound waves (above 20,000 Hz) to create images of internal body structures.
  • A probe sends ultrasound pulses into the body.
  • When these waves encounter boundaries between different tissues (e.g., muscle and bone), some of the wave energy is reflected back as echoes.
  • A computer analyzes the time delay and intensity of these echoes to construct a real-time image.
  • Ultrasound is a safe imaging technique because it does not use ionizing radiation, making it ideal for monitoring fetal development, examining organs, and detecting blood flow issues.
