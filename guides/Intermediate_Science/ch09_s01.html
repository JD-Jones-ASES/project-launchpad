CHAPTER 9, SECTION 1: WHAT IS A FORCE? STUDY GUIDE

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concept of force, explaining it as the cause of changes in an object's motion or shape. It categorizes forces into contact and non-contact types, detailing common examples of each. The section also covers how multiple forces combine to form a "net force" and introduces the crucial tool of free-body diagrams for visualizing and analyzing forces.

Key Concepts

  • Forces Cause Motion Changes: A force is a push or pull that can make an object start moving, stop, change direction, speed up, slow down, or even deform its shape. Without a force, an object's motion will not change.
  • Force is a Vector Quantity: Forces have both magnitude (strength) and direction. This means they must be added and subtracted considering their direction, and their SI unit is the newton (N).
  • Contact vs. Non-Contact Forces: Forces are broadly classified based on whether objects must physically touch (contact forces like friction, applied force, normal force, tension, spring force, air resistance) or can act over a distance (non-contact/field forces like gravity, magnetic force, electric force).
  • Net Force Determines Acceleration: When multiple forces act on an object, their combined effect is called the net force (vector sum). If the net force is zero (balanced forces), the object is in equilibrium and does not accelerate (it stays at rest or moves at a constant velocity). If the net force is not zero (unbalanced forces), the object will accelerate in the direction of the net force.
  • Free-Body Diagrams (FBDs) are Essential Tools: FBDs simplify complex situations by representing an object as a point or box and drawing arrows to show all the forces acting on it, indicating their magnitude and direction. They are fundamental for analyzing and solving problems involving forces.

Important Terms

  • Force: A push or a pull exerted on an object that can cause a change in its motion (start, stop, change direction, speed up, slow down) or shape. It is a vector quantity measured in newtons (N).
  • Newton (N): The SI unit of force, named after Isaac Newton.
  • Contact Forces: Forces that require physical contact between objects to be exerted, such as applied force, friction, normal force, tension, spring force, and air resistance.
  • Non-Contact Forces (Field Forces): Forces that act over a distance without the objects physically touching, such as gravitational force, magnetic force, and electric force.
  • Applied Force: A direct push or pull exerted by a person or machine on an object.
  • Friction: A force that opposes the sliding or rolling motion of one surface over another. It acts parallel to the contact surface and opposite to the direction of motion.
  • Normal Force: The support force exerted by a surface on an object resting on it. It always acts perpendicular ("normal") to the surface.
  • Tension: The pulling force transmitted through a string, rope, cable, or chain when it is pulled taut.
  • Spring Force: The push or pull exerted by a compressed or stretched spring, proportional to its deformation.
  • Air Resistance (Drag): A friction-like force exerted by air on a moving object, opposing its motion.
  • Gravitational Force (Gravity): The attractive force between any two objects that have mass. Near Earth's surface, it pulls objects downward.
  • Net Force (Resultant Force): The vector sum of all individual forces acting on an object. It determines if an object will accelerate and in which direction.
  • Equilibrium: A state where the net force on an object is zero. An object in equilibrium either remains at rest or moves at a constant velocity (zero acceleration).
  • Free-Body Diagram (FBD): A simplified drawing used to analyze forces, depicting an object as a dot or box with labeled arrows representing all the forces acting on it, showing their magnitude and direction.

Detailed Notes

1. What is a Force?

In physics, a force is the fundamental concept used to explain why an object's motion changes. While we can describe how an object moves (its velocity, acceleration), force answers why that motion changes.

  • Definition: A force is simply a push or a pull.
  • Effects of Force: Forces can:
    • Start an object moving.
    • Stop an object moving.
    • Change an object's direction of motion.
    • Speed up or slow down an object.
    • Deform or change the shape of an object.
  • Vector Quantity: Forces possess both magnitude (strength) and direction, making them vector quantities, similar to velocity and acceleration.
  • Unit: The SI unit for force is the newton (N), named in honor of Sir Isaac Newton.

2. Categories of Forces

Forces are categorized based on whether physical contact is required between the interacting objects.

A. Contact Forces

These forces require physical contact between objects.

  • Applied Force: A direct push or pull exerted by a person, animal, or machine on an object. (e.g., kicking a ball, pushing a cart).
  • Friction: A force that resists the relative motion (or attempted motion) between two surfaces in contact. It always acts parallel to the contact surfaces and in the direction opposite to the motion. (e.g., a car's tires gripping the road, rubbing your hands together).
  • Normal Force: This is a support force exerted by a surface on an object resting on it. The term "normal" means perpendicular in mathematics, so the normal force always acts perpendicular to the surface. (e.g., a table pushing up on a book, the ground pushing up on your feet).
  • Tension: The pulling force transmitted through a flexible connector like a string, rope, cable, or chain when it is pulled taut. This force acts along the length of the connector. (e.g., a rope pulling a sled, a cable holding up an elevator).
  • Spring Force: The restoring force exerted by a spring when it is compressed or stretched. This force always tries to return the spring to its equilibrium (natural) length, and its strength increases with the amount of deformation.
  • Air Resistance (Drag): A type of fluid friction exerted by air on an object moving through it. It always opposes the object's motion. (e.g., a skydiver falling, a car driving at high speed).

B. Non-Contact Forces (Field Forces)

These forces act over a distance without the need for physical contact.

  • Gravitational Force (Gravity): The attractive force that exists between any two objects that have mass. The more massive the objects and the closer they are, the stronger the gravitational force. Near Earth's surface, gravity pulls all objects downward towards the Earth's center. (e.g., an apple falling from a tree, the Earth orbiting the Sun).
  • Magnetic Force: The push or pull that occurs between magnets or between a magnet and a magnetic material (like iron).
  • Electric Force: The push or pull between electrically charged objects.

3. Net Force

Often, more than one force acts on an object simultaneously. The net force (or resultant force) is the combined effect of all these individual forces.

  • Vector Sum: To find the net force, individual forces must be added as vectors, meaning their directions must be taken into account.

    • Forces acting in the same direction add up.
    • Forces acting in opposite directions partially or fully cancel out.
    • Example: If you push a box with 10 N to the right, and friction exerts 3 N to the left, the net force is 10 N - 3 N = 7 N to the right.
    • Example: A book weighing 5 N (gravity downward) on a table experiences a 5 N normal force upward. The net force is 5 N (up) - 5 N (down) = 0 N.

  • Balanced vs. Unbalanced Forces:

    • Balanced Forces / Equilibrium: When the net force acting on an object is zero, the forces are considered balanced, and the object is in equilibrium. An object in equilibrium will not accelerate; it will either remain at rest or continue moving at a constant velocity (constant speed in a straight line).
    • Unbalanced Forces: When the net force acting on an object is not zero, the forces are unbalanced, and the object will accelerate in the direction of the net force.

4. Free-Body Diagrams (FBDs)

A free-body diagram (FBD) is an indispensable problem-solving tool in physics. It simplifies a physical situation by isolating the object of interest and visually representing all the forces acting upon it.

  • Purpose: FBDs help visualize forces, ensuring all relevant forces are considered and their directions are correctly identified, which is crucial for applying physical laws.

  • Components:

    • The object is represented as a simple dot or small box.
    • Each force acting on the object is represented by an arrow.
    • The tail of the arrow usually originates from the center of the object.
    • The length of the arrow can qualitatively (or quantitatively, if magnitudes are known) indicate the force's magnitude.
    • The direction of the arrow indicates the direction of the force.
    • Each arrow is labeled with the name of the force (e.g., $F_{gravity}$, $F_{normal}$, $F_{friction}$).

  • Steps to Draw an FBD:

    1. Isolate the Object: Draw the object of interest as a simple dot or small box.
    2. Identify Forces: Think about every force acting on the object from its surroundings. (Do not include forces the object exerts on other things).
    3. Draw Arrows: From the center of your object, draw an arrow for each identified force.
      • Ensure the arrow points in the correct direction.
      • Label each arrow with the force's name and its known magnitude (if applicable).
    4. Review: Double-check that all relevant forces have been included (e.g., gravity, normal force, friction, tension, applied forces, air resistance).

  • Application Example (Skydiver): Consider a skydiver falling. Initially, gravity pulls her down, and she accelerates. As her speed increases, air resistance (drag) also increases, pushing upward.

    • At terminal velocity, the skydiver's speed becomes constant. This means her acceleration is zero.
    • According to the concept of net force, if acceleration is zero, the net force must also be zero (balanced forces).
    • Therefore, at terminal velocity, the downward force of gravity is exactly balanced by the upward force of air resistance. An FBD would show two arrows of equal length, one pointing down (gravity) and one pointing up (air resistance).

Review Questions

  1. What is a force, and what are its fundamental characteristics (e.g., type of quantity, unit, effects)?
  2. Differentiate between contact forces and non-contact forces. Provide at least three distinct examples for each category, explaining how they are exerted.
  3. Explain the concept of "net force." How does the net force determine whether an object is in equilibrium or will accelerate? Use examples to illustrate both scenarios.
  4. Describe the purpose and importance of a free-body diagram in physics. Outline the key steps one should follow to accurately draw an FBD for an object.
  5. Imagine a book sitting motionless on a desk. Identify all the forces acting on the book. Draw a simple free-body diagram for the book and explain how these forces result in zero net force.
