Chapter 12, Section 3 Study Guide: Wind and Pressure Systems

Overview

This section explains that wind is fundamentally caused by differences in atmospheric pressure, which arise from the Sun's uneven heating of Earth's surface. It introduces the Coriolis effect, a crucial force that deflects global wind patterns due to Earth's rotation, leading to predictable wind belts and jet streams. The section also covers local wind phenomena like sea and land breezes, demonstrating how differential heating drives atmospheric circulation on various scales.

Key Concepts

  • Wind as Air Movement: Wind is the movement of air from areas of high pressure to areas of low pressure.
  • Unequal Heating: The Sun heats Earth's surface unevenly, with the equator receiving more direct sunlight than the poles.
  • Pressure Differences: Unequal heating leads to warm, less dense air rising (creating low pressure) and cool, denser air sinking (creating high pressure), establishing a pressure gradient.
  • Coriolis Effect: Earth's rotation deflects moving objects, including wind, to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere, influencing global wind patterns. It does not create wind but alters its path.
  • Global Wind Belts: The combination of pressure differences and the Coriolis effect creates three major wind belts in each hemisphere: Trade Winds, Westerlies, and Polar Easterlies.
  • Jet Streams: Narrow, fast-moving currents of air in the upper troposphere that steer large-scale weather systems and affect air travel.
  • Local Winds: Smaller-scale wind systems, such as sea breezes and land breezes, are generated by localized differential heating of land and water.

Important Terms

  • Wind: The movement of air caused by differences in atmospheric pressure.
  • Atmospheric Pressure: The force exerted by the weight of air molecules on a surface.
  • High Pressure: An area where air is sinking and is denser, often associated with clear skies.
  • Low Pressure: An area where air is rising and is less dense, often associated with cloudiness and precipitation.
  • Coriolis Effect: The apparent deflection of moving objects (like wind and ocean currents) caused by Earth's rotation. Objects deflect right in the Northern Hemisphere and left in the Southern Hemisphere.
  • Trade Winds: Steady easterly winds blowing from approximately 30° latitude toward the equator in both hemispheres, historically important for sailing.
  • Westerlies: Prevailing winds that blow from west to east between 30° and 60° latitude in both hemispheres, influencing weather patterns across much of North America.
  • Polar Easterlies: Cold, dry winds blowing from the east near the poles (60°–90° latitude).
  • Polar Front: The boundary zone where the cold polar easterlies meet the warmer westerlies, often a region of intense storm activity.
  • Jet Stream: A narrow, fast-moving band of air in the upper troposphere (160–400 km/h), crucial for steering weather systems.
  • Polar Jet Stream: The stronger jet stream found near 60° latitude, influencing mid-latitude weather.
  • Subtropical Jet Stream: A weaker jet stream found near 30° latitude.
  • Sea Breeze: A local wind that blows from the sea toward the land during the day due to the land heating faster than the ocean.
  • Land Breeze: A local wind that blows from the land toward the sea at night due to the land cooling faster than the ocean.
  • Valley Breeze: An upslope wind occurring during the day as air warms over mountain slopes.
  • Mountain Breeze: A downslope wind occurring at night as cool, dense air drains down mountain slopes.

Detailed Notes

The Fundamentals of Wind

  • Wind is not random; it is a direct consequence of atmospheric pressure differences.
  • These pressure differences are primarily created by the unequal heating of Earth's surface by the Sun.
  • Understanding pressure and wind is essential for comprehending weather patterns.

Unequal Heating and Pressure Differences

  • Why Heating is Unequal:
    • Equatorial Regions: Receive more direct sunlight (nearly perpendicular angle), concentrating solar energy over a smaller area, leading to warmer temperatures.
    • Polar Regions: Receive sunlight at a low angle, spreading energy over a larger area, and sunlight travels through a thicker layer of atmosphere, resulting in cooler temperatures.
  • How Pressure Differences Form:
    • At the Equator: Warm air, being less dense, rises, creating a region of low pressure at the surface.
    • At the Poles: Cool air, being denser, sinks, creating regions of high pressure at the surface.
  • Air Movement (Wind): Air naturally moves from areas of high pressure to areas of low pressure. This planetary-scale movement drives global wind systems.

The Coriolis Effect

  • Earth's Rotation and Deflection: If Earth didn't rotate, winds would blow in straight lines from poles to the equator. However, Earth's rotation deflects moving air.
  • Definition: The apparent deflection of moving objects (including wind and ocean currents) caused by Earth's rotation.
  • Direction of Deflection:
    • Northern Hemisphere: Moving objects (like wind) are deflected to the right of their direction of travel.
    • Southern Hemisphere: Moving objects are deflected to the left of their direction of travel.
  • Effect on Wind: The Coriolis effect does not create wind; pressure differences do that. It only curves the wind's path.
  • Strength of Effect: Strongest at the poles, weakest (nearly zero) at the equator. It has no noticeable impact on small-scale phenomena like bathtub drains.

Global Wind Patterns

The interplay of unequal heating, rising/sinking air, and the Coriolis effect creates three primary wind belts in each hemisphere:

  1. Trade Winds (0°–30° Latitude):

    • Formation: Warm air rises at the equator, moves poleward aloft, cools, and sinks around 30° latitude. Surface air then flows back toward the equator.
    • Direction: Deflected by the Coriolis effect, they blow from the northeast in the Northern Hemisphere (Northeast Trades) and from the southeast in the Southern Hemisphere (Southeast Trades).
    • Historical Significance: Known for their reliability, they were crucial for sailing during the Age of Exploration.

  2. Westerlies (30°–60° Latitude):

    • Formation: Surface air from the sinking zone at 30° latitude also flows poleward.
    • Direction: Deflected to the right in the Northern Hemisphere, they blow predominantly from the southwest (Prevailing Westerlies).
    • Impact: Most of the continental United States lies within this belt, which is why weather systems generally move from west to east across the country.

  3. Polar Easterlies (60°–90° Latitude):

    • Formation: Cold, dense air at the poles sinks and flows toward 60° latitude.
    • Direction: Deflected by the Coriolis effect, these winds blow from the east.
    • Polar Front: Where the cold polar easterlies meet the warmer westerlies, a boundary called the polar front forms, which is a zone of frequent storm activity.

The Jet Stream

  • Definition: Narrow bands of very fast-moving wind (160–400 km/h) in the upper troposphere, near the tropopause. They "snake" around the planet.
  • Types:
    • Polar Jet Stream: The most important one, flowing roughly along the boundary between the westerlies and polar easterlies (around 60° latitude).
    • Subtropical Jet Stream: A weaker jet stream found near 30° latitude.
  • Significance:
    • Steers Weather Systems: Major storms and fronts tend to follow the path of the jet stream.
    • Air Travel: Airplanes flying with the jet stream (west to east) experience faster travel and lower fuel consumption; flying against it slows travel.
    • Temperature Changes: Dips or bulges in the jet stream can pull cold polar air into temperate regions or push warm tropical air poleward, causing sudden temperature shifts.

Local Winds: Sea Breezes and Land Breezes

These are smaller-scale winds driven by localized differential heating.

  • Sea Breeze (Daytime):

    • Mechanism: Land heats up faster than the ocean during the day. Warm air over the land rises (creating low pressure), and cooler air from over the ocean flows inland to replace it.
    • Experience: Coastal residents feel a refreshing afternoon breeze blowing from the sea toward the land.

  • Land Breeze (Nighttime):

    • Mechanism: Land cools faster than the ocean at night. Now the warmer air (and thus lower pressure) is over the water, causing it to rise. Cooler, denser air from the land flows out to sea.

Other Local Winds

  • Mountain and Valley Breezes: Operate on the same principle of differential heating and convection.
    • Valley Breeze (Daytime): Sun-warmed air flows up mountain slopes.
    • Mountain Breeze (Nighttime): Cool, dense air drains downhill.

Review Questions

  1. Explain how the Sun's unequal heating of Earth's surface ultimately leads to the creation of wind. Describe the role of pressure differences in this process.
  2. What is the Coriolis effect, and how does it influence the direction of wind in the Northern and Southern Hemispheres? Why is its strength variable across different latitudes?
  3. Describe the characteristics of the three major global wind belts (Trade Winds, Westerlies, and Polar Easterlies). How do they form, and what are their typical directions and latitudes?
  4. What is a jet stream, and why is the polar jet stream particularly significant for weather patterns and human activities like air travel?
  5. Compare and contrast sea breezes and land breezes. Explain the daily cycle of heating and cooling that drives these local wind phenomena.
