Cellular Respiration: Harvesting Energy from Food

Overview

Cellular respiration is the fundamental biological process by which cells break down glucose and other organic molecules to release energy, primarily in the form of ATP. This multi-stage chemical process, occurring in the cytoplasm and mitochondria, allows organisms to power their metabolic activities, with oxygen playing a crucial role in maximizing energy yield.

Key Concepts

  • Energy Extraction from Glucose: Cells break down glucose (a simple sugar derived from food) through a series of chemical reactions to produce ATP, the cell's main energy currency.
  • Cellular Respiration vs. Breathing: While breathing is the physical act of moving air (supplying O2, removing CO2), cellular respiration is the chemical process within cells that uses O2 and produces CO2. They are related but distinct.
  • Overall Equation: The simplified chemical equation is C6H12O6 + 6O2 → 6CO2 + 6H2O + Energy (ATP), essentially the reverse of photosynthesis.
  • Three Main Stages (Aerobic Respiration): When oxygen is present, cellular respiration proceeds through glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain, yielding a large amount of ATP.
  • Glycolysis (Stage 1): The initial breakdown of glucose into two pyruvate molecules. It occurs in the cytoplasm, does not require oxygen (anaerobic), and produces a small net gain of ATP and electron carriers (NADH).
  • Krebs Cycle (Stage 2): Processes pyruvate derivatives (acetyl-CoA) in the mitochondrial matrix, releasing carbon dioxide, a small amount of ATP, and more electron carriers (NADH and FADH2). It requires oxygen indirectly.
  • Electron Transport Chain (Stage 3): Located in the inner mitochondrial membrane, this is where the vast majority of ATP is produced. High-energy electrons from NADH and FADH2 power a series of protein pumps to create an H+ gradient, which then drives ATP synthesis. Oxygen is the final electron acceptor.
  • Fermentation (Anaerobic Respiration): When oxygen is absent, cells can use fermentation to regenerate electron carriers needed for glycolysis to continue. This process is much less efficient than aerobic respiration, producing only 2 ATP per glucose, and results in byproducts like lactic acid or ethanol.

Important Terms

  • Cellular Respiration: The process by which cells break down glucose and other organic molecules in the presence of oxygen to produce ATP, carbon dioxide, and water.
  • ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate): The primary energy currency of the cell.
  • Glycolysis: The first stage of cellular respiration, occurring in the cytoplasm, where one molecule of glucose is split into two molecules of pyruvate. It is an anaerobic process.
  • Pyruvate: A three-carbon molecule formed from the breakdown of glucose during glycolysis.
  • NADH/FADH2: Electron carriers that transport high-energy electrons to the electron transport chain.
  • Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle): The second stage of aerobic cellular respiration, occurring in the mitochondrial matrix, which completes the breakdown of pyruvate (via acetyl-CoA) and produces CO2, ATP, NADH, and FADH2.
  • Acetyl-CoA: A two-carbon molecule formed from pyruvate, which enters the Krebs cycle.
  • Electron Transport Chain (ETC): The third and final stage of aerobic cellular respiration, located in the inner mitochondrial membrane, where NADH and FADH2 donate electrons to a series of proteins, leading to the production of the majority of ATP.
  • ATP Synthase: An enzyme embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane that uses the flow of hydrogen ions (H+) to generate ATP.
  • Aerobic Respiration: Cellular respiration that requires oxygen.
  • Anaerobic Respiration: Cellular respiration that does not require oxygen (e.g., glycolysis, fermentation).
  • Fermentation: An anaerobic pathway that allows glycolysis to continue when oxygen is not available by recycling electron carriers (NADH to NAD+). It produces far less ATP than aerobic respiration.
  • Lactic Acid Fermentation: A type of fermentation where pyruvate is converted into lactic acid (occurs in muscle cells during intense exercise and in some bacteria).
  • Alcoholic Fermentation: A type of fermentation where pyruvate is converted into ethanol and carbon dioxide (performed by yeast).

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Cellular Respiration

  • Purpose: To extract energy from food molecules (like glucose) and store it in ATP for cellular activities.
  • Mechanism: A series of carefully controlled chemical reactions.
  • Distinction from Breathing:
    • Breathing: A mechanical process (ventilation) involving the lungs, supplying oxygen and removing carbon dioxide.
    • Cellular Respiration: A chemical process occurring inside individual cells, using oxygen and producing carbon dioxide. Breathing supports cellular respiration by providing its necessary inputs and removing its outputs.

Overall Equation for Cellular Respiration

  • Equation: C6H12O6 (glucose) + 6O2 (oxygen) → 6CO2 (carbon dioxide) + 6H2O (water) + Energy (≈36-38 ATP)
  • This equation summarizes the complete breakdown of one glucose molecule in the presence of oxygen.
  • Three Main Stages: Glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain.

Stage 1: Glycolysis

  • Definition: The initial breakdown of glucose.
  • Location: Cytoplasm of the cell (outside any organelle).
  • Oxygen Requirement: Anaerobic (does not require oxygen).
  • Evolutionary Significance: Oldest metabolic pathway, found in virtually all living organisms.
  • Key Steps/Outputs for one Glucose Molecule:
    • Input: 1 glucose molecule (6 carbons) + 2 ATP (energy investment).
    • Output: 2 pyruvate molecules (3 carbons each), 4 ATP (net gain of 2 ATP), and 2 NADH (electron carriers).
  • Next Steps for Pyruvate:
    • If oxygen is available: Pyruvate moves into the mitochondria for the Krebs cycle.
    • If oxygen is absent: The cell undergoes fermentation.

Stage 2: The Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle)

  • Definition: Completes the breakdown of pyruvate derivatives.
  • Location: Matrix (inner fluid) of the mitochondrion.
  • Oxygen Requirement: Aerobic (requires oxygen indirectly, as NADH/FADH2 will need oxygen in the ETC).
  • Conversion to Acetyl-CoA: Before entering the Krebs cycle, each pyruvate molecule is converted into a two-carbon molecule called acetyl-CoA. This step also releases 1 CO2 and 1 NADH per pyruvate.
  • Key Outputs for one turn of the Krebs Cycle (processing one acetyl-CoA):
    • 2 CO2 molecules released.
    • 3 NADH produced.
    • 1 FADH2 produced.
    • 1 ATP produced directly.
  • Total per Glucose: Since each glucose yields two pyruvates (and thus two acetyl-CoA molecules), the Krebs cycle turns twice for every glucose molecule, doubling all the above outputs.

Stage 3: The Electron Transport Chain (ETC)

  • Definition: The final and most productive stage of aerobic respiration.
  • Location: Inner membrane of the mitochondria.
  • ATP Yield: Produces the vast majority of ATP (approximately 32-34 ATP per glucose).
  • Mechanism (Simplified):
    1. Electron Delivery: NADH and FADH2 (produced in glycolysis and the Krebs cycle) deliver their high-energy electrons to a series of protein complexes (the ETC) embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane.
    2. Energy Release and Proton Pumping: As electrons pass from one protein to the next, they release energy. This energy is used to pump hydrogen ions (H+) from the mitochondrial matrix into the intermembrane space, creating a high concentration of H+ there (a proton gradient).
    3. ATP Synthesis: The H+ ions flow back down their concentration gradient, through a specialized enzyme called ATP synthase. This flow of H+ ions powers ATP synthase, which rotates like a turbine to generate ATP from ADP and phosphate.
    4. Oxygen's Role: At the very end of the ETC, oxygen acts as the final electron acceptor. It combines with electrons and H+ ions to form water (H2O). Without oxygen, the electrons have nowhere to go, the ETC backs up, and ATP production through this pathway stops.

ATP Summary (Approximate Yield per Glucose)

  • Glycolysis: 2 ATP (net)
  • Krebs Cycle: 2 ATP (1 ATP per turn x 2 turns)
  • Electron Transport Chain: 32-34 ATP
  • Total Aerobic Respiration: Approximately 36-38 ATP
  • Efficiency: The ETC accounts for about 90% of the total ATP yield from glucose.

Fermentation: Respiration Without Oxygen

  • Definition: An anaerobic pathway that allows glycolysis to continue when oxygen is unavailable.
  • Purpose: To recycle NADH back into NAD+ so that glycolysis can keep producing its small amount of ATP.
  • Conditions: Occurs when the ETC and Krebs cycle cannot operate due to lack of oxygen.
  • ATP Yield: Only 2 ATP per glucose (from glycolysis alone), making it much less efficient than aerobic respiration.
  • Types of Fermentation:
    1. Lactic Acid Fermentation:
      • Process: Pyruvate is directly converted into lactic acid.
      • Examples: Human muscle cells during intense exercise (when oxygen supply is insufficient), and certain bacteria used to make yogurt and cheese.
      • Effect: Lactic acid buildup in muscles contributes to fatigue and a burning sensation.
    2. Alcoholic Fermentation:
      • Process: Pyruvate is converted into ethanol (ethyl alcohol) and carbon dioxide (CO2).
      • Examples: Yeast cells.
      • Applications: Used in baking (CO2 makes bread rise) and brewing.
  • Industrial Importance: Fermentation has been used for millennia for food preservation and production (bread, beer, cheese, soy sauce, kimchi) and is now used in industrial production of antibiotics, biofuels, and vitamins.

Review Questions

  1. Describe the key differences between breathing and cellular respiration. How are these two processes linked in the human body?
  2. Imagine a cell has just completed glycolysis. What are the possible fates of the pyruvate molecules produced, and what determines which pathway they will follow?
  3. Explain the crucial role of oxygen in aerobic cellular respiration. What would happen to ATP production if oxygen were suddenly unavailable to a cell that normally performs aerobic respiration?
  4. Compare and contrast the amount of ATP produced by glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain from one molecule of glucose. Which stage is the most significant for overall energy production?
  5. Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of fermentation compared to aerobic cellular respiration. In what specific situations might a cell rely on fermentation, and why?
