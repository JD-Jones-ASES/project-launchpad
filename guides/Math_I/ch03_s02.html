Chapter 3, Section 2: Equivalent Fractions and Simplifying Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concept of equivalent fractions, which are different fractions that represent the same quantity. It teaches two crucial skills: building equivalent fractions by multiplying the numerator and denominator by the same number, and simplifying fractions to their "simplest form" (lowest terms) by dividing them by their greatest common factor (GCF). Mastering these skills is essential for nearly all operations involving fractions.

Key Concepts

  • Equivalent Fractions: Fractions that name the same amount or represent the same part of a whole. For example, $1/2$ and $3/6$ are equivalent.
  • Simplest Form (Lowest Terms): A fraction is in simplest form when its numerator and denominator share no common factor other than $1$. This means their greatest common factor (GCF) is $1$. For example, $3/5$ is in simplest form, but $4/10$ is not.
  • Building Equivalent Fractions: To create an equivalent fraction, multiply both the numerator and the denominator by the same non-zero number. This operation does not change the value of the fraction.
  • Simplifying Fractions: To simplify a fraction, divide both the numerator and the denominator by their greatest common factor (GCF). This process reduces the fraction to its unique simplest form.
  • Determining Equivalence: Two fractions are equivalent if they simplify to the same simplest form, or if their cross products (numerator of the first multiplied by denominator of the second, and vice-versa) are equal.

Important Terms

  • Equivalent Fractions: Two or more fractions that represent the exact same quantity or proportion, even if they have different numerators and denominators.
    • Example: $1/2$ and $2/4$ are equivalent.
  • Simplest Form (Lowest Terms): The form of a fraction where the numerator and denominator have no common factors other than 1.
    • Example: $3/5$ is in simplest form, while $6/10$ is not.
  • Greatest Common Factor (GCF): The largest positive integer that divides exactly into two or more numbers without leaving a remainder. Used to simplify fractions.
  • Numerator: The top number in a fraction, representing the number of parts being considered.
  • Denominator: The bottom number in a fraction, representing the total number of equal parts the whole is divided into.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Equivalent Fractions

Equivalent fractions are different ways to express the same amount. Imagine a pizza: eating $2$ out of $4$ slices ($2/4$) is the same amount as eating $1$ out of $2$ slices ($1/2$). This core idea is crucial for adding, subtracting, and comparing fractions.

  • Examples of Equivalent Fractions:
    • $1/2$ and $3/6$ both represent half of a whole.
    • $2/3$ and $8/12$ both represent two-thirds of a whole.

What is Simplest Form?

A fraction is considered to be in its simplest form when its numerator and denominator share no common factors other than $1$. This means that the Greatest Common Factor (GCF) of the numerator and denominator is $1$.

  • Examples:
    • $3/5$ is in simplest form because the only common factor of $3$ and $5$ is $1$.
    • $4/10$ is not in simplest form because $4$ and $10$ share a common factor of $2$.

Building Equivalent Fractions

To create a fraction that is equivalent to a given fraction, you can multiply both its numerator and its denominator by the same non-zero number. This is based on the property: [ \frac{a}{b} = \frac{a \times n}{b \times n}, \quad n \neq 0. ] This property works because multiplying both parts of the fraction by $n/n$ is essentially multiplying by $1$, which doesn't change the value.

  • Example: To find three fractions equivalent to $3/5$:
    • Multiply by $2/2$: $3/5 = (3 \times 2) / (5 \times 2) = 6/10$.
    • Multiply by $3/3$: $3/5 = (3 \times 3) / (5 \times 3) = 9/15$.
    • Multiply by $4/4$: $3/5 = (3 \times 4) / (5 \times 4) = 12/20$. So, $6/10$, $9/15$, and $12/20$ are all equivalent to $3/5$.

Simplifying Fractions

Simplifying a fraction means writing it in its simplest form. This is done by dividing both the numerator and the denominator by their Greatest Common Factor (GCF). [ \frac{a}{b} = \frac{a \div (a,b)}{b \div (a,b)}. ]

  • Example: Simplify $24/36$.

    1. Find the GCF of $24$ and $36$.
      • Factors of $24$: $1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, \mathbf{12}, 24$.
      • Factors of $36$: $1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 9, \mathbf{12}, 18, 36$.
      • The GCF is $12$.
    2. Divide both numerator and denominator by the GCF.
      • $24/36 = (24 \div 12) / (36 \div 12) = 2/3$. Therefore, the simplest form of $24/36$ is $2/3$.

  • Checkpoint Example: Simplify $18/27$.

    • The GCF of $18$ and $27$ is $9$.
    • $18/27 = (18 \div 9) / (27 \div 9) = 2/3$.

Determining If Two Fractions Are Equivalent

There are two primary methods to check if two fractions are equivalent:

  1. Simplify Both Fractions: If both fractions simplify to the same simplest form, then they are equivalent.

    • Example: Are $4/6$ and $10/15$ equivalent?
      • $4/6 = (4 \div 2) / (6 \div 2) = 2/3$.
      • $10/15 = (10 \div 5) / (15 \div 5) = 2/3$. Since both simplify to $2/3$, they are equivalent.

  2. Cross-Multiplication: Multiply the numerator of the first fraction by the denominator of the second, and vice-versa. If the two products are equal, the fractions are equivalent.

    • Example: Are $4/6$ and $10/15$ equivalent?
      • Cross-multiply: $4 \times 15 = 60$.
      • Cross-multiply: $6 \times 10 = 60$. Since $60 = 60$, the fractions are equivalent.

Real-World Application (Survey Results Example)

Imagine a class of $30$ students where $18$ chose pizza as their favorite lunch. To express this as a fraction in simplest form:

  1. Form the fraction: $18/30$.
  2. Find the GCF of $18$ and $30$. The factors of $18$ are $1, 2, 3, \mathbf{6}, 9, 18$. The factors of $30$ are $1, 2, 3, 5, \mathbf{6}, 10, 15, 30$. The GCF is $6$.
  3. Simplify the fraction: $18/30 = (18 \div 6) / (30 \div 6) = 3/5$. So, $3/5$ of the students chose pizza.

Important Considerations

  • There are infinitely many equivalent fractions for any given fraction (by multiplying by $2/2, 3/3, 4/4$, and so on). However, there is only one unique simplest form for any fraction.
  • Caution: When building or simplifying equivalent fractions, you must multiply or divide both the numerator and the denominator by the same non-zero number. Changing only one part of the fraction, or using different numbers, will create a different fraction, not an equivalent one.

Summary of Key Operations

  • To build an equivalent fraction: Multiply the numerator and denominator by the same nonzero number.
  • To simplify a fraction to its simplest form: Divide the numerator and denominator by their GCF.
  • To determine if two fractions are equivalent: Simplify both to their simplest form and compare, or use cross-multiplication.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words what an "equivalent fraction" is and why it's useful to be able to create them. Provide two examples of equivalent fractions not mentioned in the text.
  2. Describe the condition for a fraction to be in "simplest form." Why is it important for fractions to be expressed in their simplest form?
  3. A recipe calls for $3/4$ cup of flour. If you want to double the recipe, what equivalent fraction would represent the amount of flour needed? Show your work using the method for building equivalent fractions.
  4. You have $20$ apples, and $12$ of them are red. Write the fraction representing the red apples in simplest form. Explain each step of your simplification process.
  5. Imagine you are comparing two fractions: $6/8$ and $9/12$. Use both the simplification method and the cross-multiplication method to determine if they are equivalent. Which method do you find easier for this particular pair of fractions, and why?
