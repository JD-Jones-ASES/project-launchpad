Equivalent Fractions and Simplifying: A Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concept of equivalent fractions, which are different fractions that represent the same amount or part of a whole. It covers essential skills such as building equivalent fractions, simplifying fractions to their simplest form using the greatest common factor (GCF), and determining if two fractions are equivalent through various methods. Mastering these skills is crucial for all subsequent work with fractions.

Key Concepts

  • Equivalent Fractions: Fractions that name the same amount or represent the same part of a whole. For example, $\frac{1}{2}$ and $\frac{2}{4}$ are equivalent.
  • Simplest Form (or Lowest Terms): A fraction is in simplest form when its numerator and denominator share no common factors other than 1. This means their Greatest Common Factor (GCF) is 1.
  • Building Equivalent Fractions: You can create an equivalent fraction by multiplying both the numerator and the denominator by the same non-zero number. This operation does not change the value of the fraction.
  • Simplifying Fractions: To simplify a fraction, divide both the numerator and the denominator by their Greatest Common Factor (GCF). This process reduces the fraction to its simplest form.
  • Determining If Two Fractions Are Equivalent:
    • Method 1 (Simplification): Simplify both fractions to their simplest form. If they simplify to the same fraction, they are equivalent.
    • Method 2 (Cross-Multiplication): Multiply the numerator of the first fraction by the denominator of the second, and the denominator of the first by the numerator of the second. If these two products (cross products) are equal, the fractions are equivalent.

Important Terms

  • Equivalent Fractions: Fractions that represent the same value or quantity, even though they have different numerators and denominators.
  • Simplest Form (or Lowest Terms): The form of a fraction where the numerator and denominator have no common factors other than 1.
  • Numerator: The top number in a fraction, representing the number of parts being considered.
  • Denominator: The bottom number in a fraction, representing the total number of equal parts in the whole.
  • Common Factor: A number that divides exactly into two or more other numbers.
  • Greatest Common Factor (GCF): The largest number that is a common factor of two or more numbers.
  • Cross-Multiply: A method used to compare or determine the equivalence of two fractions by multiplying the numerator of one fraction by the denominator of the other.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Equivalent Fractions

Fractions are powerful tools for representing parts of a whole. An important aspect of working with fractions is understanding that the same amount can be represented in many different ways. For instance, if you eat 2 slices from a pizza cut into 4 slices ($\frac{2}{4}$), you've eaten the same amount as if you ate 1 slice from a pizza cut into 2 slices ($\frac{1}{2}$). These are equivalent fractions. They look different but have the same value.

Simplest Form

While there are infinitely many equivalent fractions for any given value, there is only one simplest form (also called lowest terms) for that value. A fraction is in simplest form when its numerator and denominator have no common factors other than 1. This means their Greatest Common Factor (GCF) is 1. For example, $\frac{3}{5}$ is in simplest form because the only common factor of 3 and 5 is 1. However, $\frac{4}{10}$ is not in simplest form because 4 and 10 share a common factor of 2.

Building Equivalent Fractions

To create an equivalent fraction without changing its value, you must perform the same operation on both the numerator and the denominator. The rule for building equivalent fractions states: If you multiply the numerator and the denominator of a fraction by the same non-zero number, the resulting fraction will be equivalent to the original one. Mathematically, this is expressed as: $$ \frac{a}{b} = \frac{a \times n}{b \times n}, \text{ where } n \neq 0 $$ Example: To find fractions equivalent to $\frac{3}{5}$:

  • Multiply by 2: $\frac{3 \times 2}{5 \times 2} = \frac{6}{10}$
  • Multiply by 3: $\frac{3 \times 3}{5 \times 3} = \frac{9}{15}$
  • Multiply by 4: $\frac{3 \times 4}{5 \times 4} = \frac{12}{20}$ So, $\frac{3}{5}$, $\frac{6}{10}$, $\frac{9}{15}$, and $\frac{12}{20}$ are all equivalent fractions.

Simplifying Fractions

Simplifying a fraction means rewriting it in its simplest form. This is achieved by dividing both the numerator and the denominator by their Greatest Common Factor (GCF). The rule for simplifying fractions states: If you divide the numerator and the denominator of a fraction by their Greatest Common Factor, the resulting fraction will be in simplest form. Mathematically, this is expressed as: $$ \frac{a}{b} = \frac{a \div (GCF \text{ of } a,b)}{b \div (GCF \text{ of } a,b)} $$ Example: To simplify $\frac{24}{36}$:

  1. Find the GCF of 24 and 36.
    • Factors of 24: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24
    • Factors of 36: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 9, 12, 18, 36
    • The GCF of 24 and 36 is 12.
  2. Divide both the numerator and denominator by the GCF:
    • $\frac{24 \div 12}{36 \div 12} = \frac{2}{3}$ So, the simplest form of $\frac{24}{36}$ is $\frac{2}{3}$.

Determining If Two Fractions Are Equivalent

There are two main methods to check if two fractions are equivalent:

  1. Simplify Both Fractions:

    • Simplify each fraction to its simplest form.
    • If both fractions simplify to the exact same simplest form, then they are equivalent. Example: Are $\frac{4}{6}$ and $\frac{10}{15}$ equivalent?
    • Simplify $\frac{4}{6}$: GCF of 4 and 6 is 2. $\frac{4 \div 2}{6 \div 2} = \frac{2}{3}$
    • Simplify $\frac{10}{15}$: GCF of 10 and 15 is 5. $\frac{10 \div 5}{15 \div 5} = \frac{2}{3}$ Since both simplify to $\frac{2}{3}$, they are equivalent.

  2. Cross-Multiplication:

    • Multiply the numerator of the first fraction by the denominator of the second fraction.
    • Multiply the denominator of the first fraction by the numerator of the second fraction.
    • If the two products are equal, the fractions are equivalent. Example: Are $\frac{4}{6}$ and $\frac{10}{15}$ equivalent?
    • Cross-multiply: $4 \times 15 = 60$
    • Cross-multiply: $6 \times 10 = 60$ Since $60 = 60$, the cross products are equal, so the fractions are equivalent.

Important Considerations

  • Always change both parts: When building or simplifying fractions, remember to always multiply or divide both the numerator and the denominator by the same non-zero number. Changing only one part will result in a completely different fraction, not an equivalent one.
  • Real-world application: These skills are used in many contexts, such as representing survey results, scaling recipes, or comparing quantities. For example, if 18 out of 30 students chose pizza, the fraction is $\frac{18}{30}$, which simplifies to $\frac{3}{5}$.

Review Questions

  1. What does it mean for two fractions to be "equivalent"? Provide two examples of equivalent fractions and explain why they are considered equivalent.
  2. Describe the two primary methods for determining if two fractions are equivalent. In what scenarios might one method be more efficient or easier to use than the other?
  3. Explain the concept of a fraction being in "simplest form." Why is it often desirable to express a fraction in its simplest form, and what role does the Greatest Common Factor (GCF) play in achieving this?
  4. Imagine you are given the fraction $\frac{5}{8}$. a. Write two fractions equivalent to $\frac{5}{8}$ using the method of building equivalent fractions. b. If you needed to simplify a fraction like $\frac{40}{64}$, how would you apply the concepts learned in this section?
  5. A student correctly calculated that 12 out of 16 people preferred apples over oranges. They then tried to simplify the fraction $\frac{12}{16}$ by dividing the numerator by 4 but forgot to divide the denominator by 4, resulting in $\frac{3}{16}$. Explain the mistake this student made and why their resulting fraction is not equivalent to the original one. What is the correct simplest form?
