Multiplying Decimals Study Guide

Overview

This section explains the process of multiplying decimals. The core idea is to multiply numbers as if they were whole numbers, then correctly place the decimal point in the product by counting the total number of decimal places in the original factors. It also covers a special shortcut for multiplying decimals by powers of ten and demonstrates how to apply these skills in word problems.

Key Concepts

  • Decimal Point Placement: Unlike addition and subtraction, decimal points are not lined up when multiplying. Instead, they are placed in the final product based on a count of decimal places in the factors.
  • Counting Decimal Places: The total number of decimal places in the product is equal to the sum of the decimal places in all the numbers being multiplied (the factors).
  • Multiplying by Powers of Ten: When multiplying a decimal by 10, 100, 1,000, etc., you can easily find the product by shifting the decimal point to the right. The number of places to shift corresponds to the number of zeros in the power of ten.
  • Rounding for Money Problems: When solving word problems involving money, it is standard practice to round the final answer to the nearest cent (the hundredths place) unless instructed otherwise.
  • Estimation as a Check: Always estimate your answer to ensure the calculated product is reasonable and helps catch potential errors in decimal point placement.

Important Terms

  • Product: The result obtained when two or more numbers are multiplied together.
  • Factor: Each of the numbers that are multiplied together to get a product. For example, in the expression $0.3 \times 1.2 = 0.36$, both $0.3$ and $1.2$ are factors.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Decimal Multiplication

When multiplying decimals, the procedure differs significantly from adding or subtracting decimals. You do not align the decimal points vertically. Instead, the focus is on treating the numbers as whole numbers initially and then determining the decimal point's position in the final answer. This section covers multiplying decimals by other decimals, by whole numbers, and by specific powers of ten.

The General Rule for Multiplying Decimals

To accurately multiply any two or more decimal numbers, follow these steps:

  1. Multiply as Whole Numbers: Begin by multiplying the numbers as if there were no decimal points. Ignore the decimals temporarily and perform the multiplication just as you would with whole numbers.
  2. Count Decimal Places in Factors: Go back to the original factors and count the total number of digits to the right of the decimal point in all of them combined.
    • For example, in $2.4 \times 0.13$:
      • $2.4$ has 1 decimal place (the digit '4').
      • $0.13$ has 2 decimal places (the digits '1' and '3').
      • The total number of decimal places is $1 + 2 = 3$.
  3. Place the Decimal Point in the Product: Starting from the rightmost digit of your whole-number product, count left the total number of decimal places you found in step 2. Place the decimal point there. If you need more places than you have digits, add leading zeros.

Example: Multiplying a Decimal by a Decimal

Let's find the product of $2.4 \times 0.13$.

  1. Multiply as whole numbers: $24 \times 13 = 312$.
  2. Count decimal places: $2.4$ has 1 decimal place; $0.13$ has 2 decimal places. Total = $1 + 2 = 3$ decimal places.
  3. Place the decimal point: Starting from the right of 312, count 3 places to the left. $312 \rightarrow 0.312$. Therefore, $2.4 \times 0.13 = 0.312$.

Example: Multiplying a Decimal by a Whole Number

Let's find the product of $6 \times 3.75$.

  1. Multiply as whole numbers: $6 \times 375 = 2250$.
  2. Count decimal places: $6$ has 0 decimal places; $3.75$ has 2 decimal places. Total = $0 + 2 = 2$ decimal places.
  3. Place the decimal point: Starting from the right of 2250, count 2 places to the left. $2250 \rightarrow 22.50$. This can be simplified to $22.5$. Therefore, $6 \times 3.75 = 22.5$.

Multiplying by Powers of 10

There's a convenient shortcut when multiplying decimals by powers of 10 (like 10, 100, 1,000, and so on). Instead of the full multiplication process, you simply move the decimal point to the right.

  • Multiply by 10: Move the decimal point 1 place to the right. (10 has one zero)
  • Multiply by 100: Move the decimal point 2 places to the right. (100 has two zeros)
  • Multiply by 1,000: Move the decimal point 3 places to the right. (1,000 has three zeros)
  • And so on for higher powers of ten.

Example: Multiplying by 10, 100, and 1,000

Find each product for $3.045$:

  1. $3.045 \times 10$: Move decimal 1 place right $\rightarrow 30.45$.
  2. $3.045 \times 100$: Move decimal 2 places right $\rightarrow 304.5$.
  3. $3.045 \times 1,000$: Move decimal 3 places right $\rightarrow 3,045$.

Applying Decimal Multiplication in Word Problems

Word problems often require multiplying decimals, especially in contexts like calculating costs or measurements. A structured approach helps ensure accuracy.

Example: Unit Price Word Problem

Apples cost $1.29 per pound. How much do $3.5$ pounds of apples cost?

  1. Read: Understand what is being asked. We need the total cost of 3.5 lbs of apples at $1.29/lb.
  2. Identify: Recognize the operation needed. Total cost = price per pound $\times$ number of pounds.
  3. Write: Set up the calculation. Total cost = $1.29 \times 3.5$.
  4. Solve:
    • Multiply as whole numbers: $129 \times 35 = 4515$.
    • Count decimal places: $1.29$ has 2 places; $3.5$ has 1 place. Total = $2 + 1 = 3$ places.
    • Place decimal point: $4515 \rightarrow 4.515$.
    • Round to the nearest cent: For money problems, round to two decimal places. $4.515$ rounded to the nearest cent is $4.52$.
    • The cost is $4.52.
  5. Check: Estimate the answer. $1.30 \times 3.5 = 4.55$. Our answer of $4.52$ is very close, indicating it's reasonable.

Important Note for Money Problems: Unless specified otherwise, always round your final answer to the nearest cent (two decimal places, the hundredths place) when dealing with currency.

Key Reminders and Cautions

  • Do NOT line up decimal points when multiplying. This rule applies only to addition and subtraction of decimals.
  • Always perform an estimation of your product to verify that your decimal point placement is correct and that your answer is reasonable.
  • If your whole-number product has fewer digits than the required number of decimal places, add leading zeros to the left of your number before placing the decimal point (e.g., $0.2 \times 0.3 = 0.06$).

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental difference in how decimal points are handled when multiplying decimals compared to adding or subtracting them. Why is this distinction important?
  2. Describe the step-by-step process for multiplying two decimals, such as $1.8 \times 0.25$. Make sure to explain how to determine the correct position of the decimal point in the final product.
  3. Without performing the full multiplication, explain how you would quickly calculate the product of $5.678 \times 100$. Provide another example using $0.032 \times 1,000$.
  4. A recipe calls for $0.75$ cups of sugar, and you want to make $2.5$ times the recipe. If you calculate the total sugar needed as $187.5$ cups, what mistake have you likely made, and what should the correct answer be?
  5. When should you round your answer to the nearest hundredth (cent) in a decimal multiplication problem? Why is this convention important in real-world applications?
