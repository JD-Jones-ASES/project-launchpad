Study Guide: Multiplying Decimals

Overview

This section explains how to multiply decimal numbers, including multiplying decimals by other decimals, by whole numbers, and by powers of ten. The core principle involves multiplying the numbers as if they were whole numbers and then precisely placing the decimal point in the final product based on the total number of decimal places in the original factors.

Key Concepts

  • Decimal Multiplication Rule: To multiply decimals, first multiply them as if they were whole numbers, ignoring the decimal points. Then, count the total number of decimal places in all the numbers being multiplied (the factors). Finally, place the decimal point in the product so that it has the same total number of decimal places.
  • Counting Decimal Places: The number of decimal places in the final product is the sum of the decimal places in each of the factors. For example, if you multiply a number with 1 decimal place by a number with 2 decimal places, the product will have 1 + 2 = 3 decimal places.
  • Multiplying by Powers of 10: When multiplying a decimal by a power of 10 (like 10, 100, 1,000, etc.), simply move the decimal point to the right. The number of places you move it corresponds to the number of zeros in the power of ten (e.g., 1 zero for 10, 2 zeros for 100, 3 zeros for 1,000).
  • Real-World Application (Money): In problems involving money, it is standard practice to round the final answer to the nearest cent (the hundredths place) unless specified otherwise.
  • Estimation: Using estimation to check your answer is a valuable strategy to ensure your calculated product is reasonable and that the decimal point is placed correctly.

Important Terms

  • Product: The result obtained when two or more numbers are multiplied together.
    • Example: In $0.3 \times 1.2 = 0.36$, the product is $0.36$.
  • Factor: Each number that is being multiplied in a multiplication problem.
    • Example: In $0.3 \times 1.2 = 0.36$, the factors are $0.3$ and $1.2$.

Detailed Notes

The Fundamental Rule for Multiplying Decimals

Unlike addition and subtraction of decimals, where you must align the decimal points, when multiplying decimals, you treat them initially as whole numbers. The placement of the decimal point comes at the very end.

Steps to Multiply Decimals:

  1. Multiply as Whole Numbers: Ignore the decimal points and multiply the numbers as if they were whole numbers.
  2. Count Total Decimal Places: Go back to the original numbers (the factors) and count the total number of digits that are to the right of the decimal point in all the factors combined.
  3. Place the Decimal Point: Starting from the right end of your whole-number product, move the decimal point to the left the same number of places you counted in Step 2. If you need more places, add leading zeros.

Example 1: Multiplying a Decimal by a Decimal Let's find $2.4 \times 0.13$.

  • Step 1 (Multiply as whole numbers): $24 \times 13 = 312$.
  • Step 2 (Count decimal places):
    • $2.4$ has 1 decimal place.
    • $0.13$ has 2 decimal places.
    • Total decimal places: $1 + 2 = 3$ places.
  • Step 3 (Place the decimal point): Take our product $312$ and move the decimal point 3 places to the left from the right. This gives us $0.312$.
    • So, $2.4 \times 0.13 = 0.312$.

Example 2: Multiplying a Decimal by a Whole Number Let's find $6 \times 3.75$.

  • Step 1 (Multiply as whole numbers): $6 \times 375 = 2250$.
  • Step 2 (Count decimal places):
    • $6$ has 0 decimal places (it's a whole number).
    • $3.75$ has 2 decimal places.
    • Total decimal places: $0 + 2 = 2$ places.
  • Step 3 (Place the decimal point): Take our product $2250$ and move the decimal point 2 places to the left. This gives us $22.50$, which simplifies to $22.5$.
    • So, $6 \times 3.75 = 22.50 = 22.5$.

Important Caution: Remember, do not line up the decimal points when multiplying! This is only done for addition and subtraction.

Multiplying by Powers of Ten

Multiplying by powers of ten (like 10, 100, 1,000, 10,000, etc.) is a special case that involves a shortcut:

Rule: When you multiply a decimal by a power of 10, move the decimal point to the right by the number of zeros in the power of ten.

  • Multiply by $10$: move the decimal point 1 place right (because 10 has one zero).
  • Multiply by $100$: move the decimal point 2 places right (because 100 has two zeros).
  • Multiply by $1,000$: move the decimal point 3 places right (because 1,000 has three zeros).

Example: Multiplying by 10, 100, and 1,000 Find each product for $3.045$:

  • $3.045 \times 10$: Move the decimal point 1 place right. $3.045 \rightarrow 30.45$.
  • $3.045 \times 100$: Move the decimal point 2 places right. $3.045 \rightarrow 304.5$.
  • $3.045 \times 1,000$: Move the decimal point 3 places right. $3.045 \rightarrow 3045$. (You can write it as $3,045.0$ or simply $3,045$).

Real-World Application: Word Problems

Decimal multiplication is frequently used in everyday situations, especially when dealing with money or measurements.

Example: Unit Price Word Problem Apples cost $1.29 per pound. How much do $3.5$ pounds of apples cost?

  1. Read and Understand: We need to find the total cost of $3.5$ pounds of apples when each pound costs $1.29.
  2. Identify Operation: Total cost = price per pound $\times$ number of pounds.
  3. Write the Equation: Total cost = $1.29 \times 3.5$.
  4. Solve:
    • Multiply as whole numbers: $129 \times 35 = 4515$.
    • Count decimal places: $1.29$ has 2 places; $3.5$ has 1 place. Total = $2 + 1 = 3$ decimal places.
    • Place the decimal point: Move the decimal 3 places left in $4515 \rightarrow 4.515$.
    • Round to the nearest cent (hundredths place) for money: $4.515$ rounds to $4.52$.
    • So, the apples cost $4.52.
  5. Check: Estimate: $1.30 \times 3.5 = (1.3 \times 3) + (1.3 \times 0.5) = 3.9 + 0.65 = 4.55$. Our answer of $4.52$ is very close to the estimate, making it reasonable.

Note on Money: Always round monetary answers to the nearest hundredth (two decimal places) unless specific instructions dictate otherwise. This is because our currency typically uses cents, which are hundredths of a dollar.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the step-by-step process for multiplying two decimals. How is the placement of the decimal point determined in the final product?
  2. What is the key difference in how you handle decimal points when multiplying decimals compared to adding or subtracting them? Provide an example to illustrate.
  3. If you are multiplying $0.05$ by $0.25$, how many decimal places will be in your final product? Explain how you arrived at that number.
  4. Describe the shortcut for multiplying a decimal by $100$. Provide an example of $7.892 \times 100$ and explain why this shortcut works.
  5. Consider a scenario where you buy $2.5$ yards of fabric that costs $8.75 per yard.
    • Set up the multiplication problem to find the total cost.
    • Perform the multiplication.
    • Explain how you would round your answer to the nearest cent, if necessary, and why estimation is a good final check for this type of problem.
