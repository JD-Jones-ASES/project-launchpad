Study Guide: Finding a Percent of a Number

Overview

This section teaches a fundamental skill: how to calculate a specific percentage of a given number. This technique is widely applicable in daily life for tasks like determining discounts, calculating tips, or understanding statistics. The core method involves converting the percent into a decimal (or fraction) and then multiplying it by the number.

Key Concepts

  • Converting Percent to Decimal: Before any calculation, a percent must be converted into its decimal equivalent. This is done by dividing the percent by 100, which is equivalent to moving the decimal point two places to the left.
  • The Meaning of "Of": In percentage problems, the word "of" almost always signifies multiplication. When you see "what is X% of Y," it means X% multiplied by Y.
  • Formula: The relationship can be expressed as: Percent × Whole = Part. Here, 'percent' is in decimal form, 'whole' is the original number, and 'part' is the resulting percentage amount.
  • Percents Greater Than 100%: If the percent you are finding is greater than 100% (e.g., 150%), the resulting "part" will be larger than the original "whole" number.
  • Common Mistake: A frequent error is to multiply by the percent number directly (e.g., using "25" instead of "0.25" for 25%). Always convert the percent to a decimal first.

Important Terms

  • Percent: A ratio or a fraction out of 100. It literally means "per hundred." (e.g., 25% means 25 out of 100).
  • Whole: The original, total amount or number that you are taking a percentage of.
  • Part: The amount that results from taking a certain percent of the whole. This is the value you are usually trying to find.
  • Decimal: A number system that uses powers of 10 and a decimal point to represent fractional parts. It's the standard format for performing calculations with percents.
  • Discount: A reduction in price, often expressed as a percentage off the original price.

Detailed Notes

The Fundamental Rule: Finding a Percent of a Number

Finding a percent of a number is a straightforward two-step process that you'll use frequently. This method is essential for understanding financial calculations, statistics, and many other real-world scenarios.

Steps to Find a Percent of a Number:

  1. Convert the Percent to a Decimal (or Fraction): This is the most critical first step.

    • To convert a percent to a decimal, divide the percent by 100. This is the same as moving the decimal point two places to the left.
      • Example: 25% = 25 / 100 = 0.25
      • Example: 8.5% = 8.5 / 100 = 0.085
      • Example: 150% = 150 / 100 = 1.50
    • (Alternatively, you could convert to a fraction, like 25% = 25/100 = 1/4, but decimal conversion is generally more versatile for multiplication.)

  2. Multiply the Decimal by the Number: Once you have the decimal equivalent of the percent, multiply it by the "whole" number you are working with.

    • The formula is: Decimal Percent × Whole Number = Part

The Significance of "Of"

A common phrase in percent problems is "what is X% of Y?" The word "of" in this context is a strong indicator that you should perform a multiplication operation. Whenever you encounter "of" in a percent problem, think "multiply."

Examples Walkthrough

Let's look at various scenarios and how to apply the rule:

Example 1: Basic Calculation

  • Question: What is 25% of 80?
  • Solution:
    1. Convert the percent: 25% = 0.25
    2. Multiply: 0.25 * 80 = 20
  • Answer: 25% of 80 is 20.

Example 2: Percent with a Decimal

  • Question: Find 8.5% of 200.
  • Solution:
    1. Convert the percent: 8.5% = 0.085 (Remember to move the decimal two places, even if it adds a leading zero).
    2. Multiply: 0.085 * 200 = 17
  • Answer: 8.5% of 200 is 17.

Example 3: Percent Greater Than 100%

  • Question: Find 150% of 40.
  • Solution:
    1. Convert the percent: 150% = 1.50
    2. Multiply: 1.50 * 40 = 60
  • Note: Since 150% is greater than 100%, the resulting "part" (60) is larger than the original "whole" number (40). This makes sense because you're finding more than the entire quantity.
  • Answer: 150% of 40 is 60.

Example 4: Real-World Application (Sale Discount)

  • Question: A jacket that regularly costs $60 is on sale for 30% off. How much is the discount?
  • Solution (Using a 5-step problem-solving process):
    1. Read: Understand the problem. We need to find the amount of the discount, which is 30% of the original price, $60.
    2. Identify:
      • Percent = 30%
      • Whole (original price) = $60
      • We need to find the "part" (the discount amount).
    3. Write: Set up the calculation. Convert the percent to a decimal: 30% = 0.30.
      • Discount = 0.30 * $60
    4. Solve:
      • 0.30 * $60 = $18
      • The discount is $18.
    5. Check: Does the answer make sense? $18 is about one-third of $60, and 30% is close to one-third. If the jacket costs $60 and the discount is $18, the sale price would be $60 - $18 = $42. The calculation seems reasonable.
  • Answer: The discount is $18.

Caution: Avoid a Common Mistake!

It is absolutely crucial to always convert the percent to a decimal before multiplying. A very common error is to multiply the number written as a percent directly. For example, multiplying 25 * 80 instead of 0.25 * 80 would yield 2,000, which is incorrect. Remember: 25% is not the same as 25 in calculations.

Summary of Finding a Percent of a Number

  • Convert the percent to a decimal (divide by 100).
  • Multiply: decimal × whole = part.
  • If the percent is greater than 100%, the part will be greater than the whole.
  • The word "of" indicates multiplication.
