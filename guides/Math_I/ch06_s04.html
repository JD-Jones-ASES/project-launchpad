Study Guide: Scale Drawings and Maps

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concepts of scale drawings, which are essential tools used by professionals to represent large objects or distances in a proportionally smaller format. You will learn what a "scale" and "scale factor" are, and how to apply ratios and proportions to calculate either real-world measurements from a drawing or drawing measurements from actual values.

Key Concepts

  • Scale Drawings: These are drawings that represent real objects or areas either larger or smaller than their actual size, but always maintaining accurate proportions. Maps, blueprints, and model kits are common examples.
  • Scale: This is a crucial ratio that directly compares a measurement on the drawing to the corresponding actual measurement in the real world. It's typically expressed as drawing length : actual length (e.g., 1 cm : 50 km).
  • Scale Factor: The scale factor is the scale expressed as a single number or fraction. It represents the ratio of any length in the scale drawing to the corresponding actual length, often with both measurements in the same units (e.g., a scale of 1:200 means a scale factor of 1/200).
  • Proportional Reasoning: The primary mathematical technique for solving problems involving scale drawings. Proportions are used to find an unknown measurement (either actual or drawing) when the scale and one measurement are known.
  • Unit Awareness: It is vital to consistently check and use the correct units. Scales often involve different units for the drawing and actual measurements (e.g., inches on a blueprint representing feet in reality), and these must be managed carefully when setting up proportions.

Important Terms

  • Scale: A ratio that compares a length in a drawing or model to the corresponding actual length. It establishes the relationship between the dimensions on the representation and the real-world dimensions.
  • Scale Factor: The ratio of any length in the scale drawing to the corresponding actual length, expressed as a single numerical value (often a fraction), usually when both measurements are in the same units.
  • Scale Drawing: A drawing or model that precisely uses a specific scale to represent an object as larger or smaller than its actual size, ensuring all dimensions are proportionally accurate.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Scale Drawings and Maps

Architects, engineers, and mapmakers frequently work with objects or areas that are too large to draw at their actual size. Scale drawings provide a practical solution by representing these large dimensions on a smaller piece of paper while maintaining accurate proportions. The scale is the key component that defines how measurements on the drawing relate to real-world measurements. Since a scale is a ratio, all the principles of ratios and proportions apply when working with scale drawings.

Understanding Scale and Scale Factor

Scale

A scale is a ratio that compares a length on a drawing or model to the corresponding actual length. It's often written in the format drawing length : actual length.

  • Example: A map scale of "$1 cm : 50 km$" means that every 1 centimeter you measure on the map represents an actual distance of 50 kilometers in real life.
  • Location: On maps, the scale is typically displayed in a legend or a corner box.
  • Units: Always check the units involved in the scale. The measurement on the drawing and the actual measurement may use different units (e.g., inches and feet). It is critical not to mix these units when setting up your calculations.

Scale Factor

The scale factor is simply the scale expressed as a single number or fraction. It's the ratio of any length in the scale drawing to the corresponding actual length.

  • Example: If a scale is given as 1 : 200, the scale factor is 1/200. This means that every dimension on the drawing is 1/200th of its actual size.

Working with Scale Drawings: Using Proportions

The most common way to solve problems involving scale drawings is by setting up and solving proportions. A proportion equates two ratios.

1. Finding Actual Distance from a Scale Drawing

When you know the distance on a map or drawing and the scale, you can find the actual distance.

  • Method:

    1. Write the given scale as a ratio (e.g., 1 cm / 25 km).
    2. Set up a proportion. Place the known drawing distance in the numerator of the second ratio and the unknown actual distance (d) in the denominator. Ensure that corresponding units are in the same positions in both ratios (e.g., drawing units in both numerators, actual units in both denominators).
    3. Cross-multiply and solve for the unknown variable d.

  • Example: On a map with a scale of 1 cm : 25 km, two cities are 3.5 cm apart. What is the actual distance?

    1 cm     3.5 cm
----- = --------
25 km    d km

    Cross-multiply: 1 * d = 25 * 3.5 d = 87.5 km The actual distance between the cities is 87.5 km.

2. Finding Drawing Distance from Actual Distance

If you know the real-world distance and the scale, you can determine how far apart those points would be on the drawing.

  • Method:

    1. Write the given scale as a ratio.
    2. Set up a proportion. Place the unknown drawing distance (d) in the numerator of the second ratio and the known actual distance in the denominator.
    3. Cross-multiply and solve for d.

  • Example: A map uses the scale 1 cm : 10 km. The actual distance between two landmarks is 45 km. How far apart are they on the map?

    1 cm     d cm
----- = --------
10 km    45 km

    Cross-multiply: 10 * d = 1 * 45 10d = 45 d = 45 / 10 d = 4.5 cm The landmarks are 4.5 cm apart on the map.

3. Applying Scale to Multi-Dimensional Problems (e.g., Blueprints)

When dealing with objects that have multiple dimensions (like a room with length and width), you apply the proportion method to each dimension separately.

  • Example: An architect's blueprint uses the scale 1 in. : 8 ft. On the blueprint, a living room measures 2.5 inches by 3 inches. What are the actual dimensions?
    • For Width:
      1 in.     2.5 in.
----- = ---------
8 ft      w ft
      1 * w = 8 * 2.5 w = 20 ft
    • For Length:
      1 in.     3 in.
----- = ---------
8 ft      l ft
      1 * l = 8 * 3 l = 24 ft The actual living room is 20 ft by 24 ft.

Important Reminders

  • Unit Mismatch: Always double-check the units in the scale! If the scale says 1 in. : 5 ft, it means 1 inch on the drawing corresponds to 5 feet in reality. Do not inadvertently treat them as the same unit when setting up your proportion, and make sure your final answer has the correct units.
  • Consistency in Proportion Setup: When setting up your ratios, ensure that the drawing measurement is consistently in the numerator (or denominator) and the actual measurement is consistently in the other position across both sides of the proportion.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words the difference between a "scale" (e.g., 1 cm : 50 km) and a "scale factor" (e.g., 1/5000000). When would you typically use one versus the other?
  2. Imagine you are an urban planner designing a new park. Describe why using a scale drawing is essential for your work, outlining at least three specific benefits.
  3. A model airplane is built with a scale of 1 : 72. If the actual airplane has a wingspan of 36 meters, describe step-by-step how you would calculate the wingspan of the model in centimeters.
  4. Why is it crucial to pay close attention to the units specified in a scale (e.g., 1 inch : 4 feet vs. 1 inch : 4 inches) when setting up a proportion? What kind of error could result from ignoring unit differences?
  5. If you have a map and its scale, what information do you need to find the actual distance between two points? Conversely, what information do you need to find the distance on the map if you know the actual distance?
