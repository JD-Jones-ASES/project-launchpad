Understanding Unit Rates: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the concept of rates, which are special ratios comparing two quantities measured in different units. It then focuses on unit rates, a powerful tool that simplifies comparisons by expressing a rate with a denominator of one. You will learn how to calculate unit rates and apply them to solve practical problems like finding the better buy or calculating speed.

Key Concepts

  • Rates Compare Different Units: Unlike simple ratios that compare quantities of the same type, a rate compares two quantities that have different units (e.g., miles and hours, dollars and pounds).
  • Unit Rates Simplify Comparisons: A unit rate is a specific type of rate where the second quantity (the denominator) is exactly 1. This "per 1 unit" format makes it easy to understand and compare different rates directly.
  • Calculating Unit Rates: To find a unit rate, you divide the first quantity by the second quantity. This operation transforms the second quantity into 1 while adjusting the first quantity proportionally.
  • "Per" Means Unit Rate: The word "per" in phrases like "miles per hour" or "cost per item" is a strong indicator that you are dealing with a unit rate. It signifies "for each" or "for every 1."
  • Practical Applications: Unit rates are incredibly useful for everyday tasks such as determining the best value when shopping ("better buy" problems), calculating speeds, and scaling quantities in word problems.
  • Consistent Units for Comparison: When comparing two different unit rates, it is crucial that the units in their denominators are the same (e.g., both "per hour" or both "per minute") to ensure a fair and accurate comparison.

Important Terms

  • Ratio: A comparison of two quantities. Ratios can compare parts to parts, parts to whole, or whole to whole. (Implied background knowledge)
  • Rate: A ratio that compares two quantities measured in different units.
    • Example: 120 miles in 2 hours, $7.50 for 3 pounds.
  • Unit Rate: A rate in which the second quantity (the denominator) is 1. Unit rates express "how much per one."
    • Example: 60 miles per 1 hour (60 mph), $2.50 per 1 pound.
  • Per: A word meaning "for each" or "for every 1." It signals that a unit rate is being used or discussed.
  • Better Buy: The item or option that offers more quantity or value for the same amount of money, or the same quantity for less money, often determined by comparing unit prices.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Rates and Unit Rates

A ratio is a general comparison between two numbers. For instance, if you have 3 red apples and 5 green apples, the ratio of red to green is 3:5.

A rate is a specific type of ratio that compares two quantities with different units. These different units are what make it a rate.

  • For example: "120 miles in 2 hours" is a rate because it compares miles (a unit of distance) to hours (a unit of time). "$7.50 for 3 pounds" is another rate, comparing dollars (a unit of currency) to pounds (a unit of weight).

A unit rate is an even more specific type of rate. It's a rate where the second quantity, often in the denominator when written as a fraction, is exactly 1. This standardized "per 1 unit" format makes unit rates incredibly useful for making comparisons and calculations.

  • For example: Instead of "120 miles in 2 hours," the unit rate would be "60 miles per 1 hour" (60 mph). Instead of "$7.50 for 3 pounds," the unit rate is "$2.50 per 1 pound."

The word "per" is a key indicator of a unit rate. When you see "miles per hour" or "cost per item," you are looking at a unit rate, which tells you the quantity for each single unit of the second item.

Finding a Unit Rate

To transform any rate into a unit rate, you need to make the second quantity (the denominator) equal to 1. You achieve this by dividing both quantities by the original value of the second quantity.

Formula for Finding a Unit Rate: If you have a rate of a units for b units, the unit rate is found by: $$ \frac{a \text{ units}}{b \text{ units}} = \frac{a \div b}{b \div b} = \frac{a/b}{1} \text{ unit} $$ The result will be (a/b) units per 1 of the second unit.

Example 1: Finding a Unit Price A pack of 8 juice boxes costs $6.00. What is the unit price (cost per box)? To find the cost per 1 box, divide both quantities by 8: $$ \frac{$6.00}{8 \text{ boxes}} = \frac{$6.00 \div 8}{8 \div 8} = \frac{$0.75}{1 \text{ box}} $$ The unit price is $0.75 per box.

Example 2: Speed as a Unit Rate A car travels 195 miles in 3 hours. What is the car's speed as a unit rate? To find miles per 1 hour, divide both quantities by 3: $$ \frac{195 \text{ miles}}{3 \text{ hours}} = \frac{195 \div 3}{3 \div 3} = \frac{65 \text{ miles}}{1 \text{ hour}} $$ The car's speed is 65 miles per hour (65 mph).

Checkpoint Practice: If a printer prints 90 pages in 6 minutes, the unit rate is: $$ \frac{90 \text{ pages}}{6 \text{ minutes}} = \frac{90 \div 6}{6 \div 6} = \frac{15 \text{ pages}}{1 \text{ minute}} $$ So, the unit rate is 15 pages per minute.

Everyday Uses of Unit Rates

  1. Comparing Prices (Finding the "Better Buy"): Unit rates are extremely helpful when you need to decide which product offers the best value. By calculating the unit price (cost per item, per ounce, per pound, etc.) for each option, you can easily compare them. The item with the lower unit price is the "better buy."

    Example: Better Buy Comparison Brand A sells 12 pencils for $3.60. Brand B sells 8 pencils for $2.80. Which is the better buy?

    • Brand A Unit Price: $3.60 / 12 pencils = $0.30 per pencil.
    • Brand B Unit Price: $2.80 / 8 pencils = $0.35 per pencil. Since $0.30 is less than $0.35, Brand A is the better buy because each pencil costs less.

    Checkpoint Practice: Store X sells 6 notebooks for $8.40. Store Y sells 4 notebooks for $5.00.

    • Store X Unit Price: $8.40 / 6 notebooks = $1.40 per notebook.
    • Store Y Unit Price: $5.00 / 4 notebooks = $1.25 per notebook. Store Y has the better price ($1.25 per notebook).

  2. Scaling Quantities (Word Problems): Once you find a unit rate, you can use it to predict or calculate quantities for different amounts.

    Example: Unit Rate Word Problem Maria types 450 words in 10 minutes. At the same rate, how many words can she type in 25 minutes?

    • Step 1: Find the unit rate. $$ \frac{450 \text{ words}}{10 \text{ minutes}} = \frac{450 \div 10}{10 \div 10} = \frac{45 \text{ words}}{1 \text{ minute}} $$ Maria types 45 words per minute.
    • Step 2: Use the unit rate to find the total for the new amount. Multiply the unit rate by the new number of minutes: $$ 45 \text{ words/minute} \times 25 \text{ minutes} = 1,125 \text{ words} $$ Maria can type 1,125 words in 25 minutes.

Important Considerations for Unit Rates

Caution: Consistent Units are Key for Comparison! When comparing two different unit rates, always ensure that the units in their denominators are identical. For instance, if you're comparing speeds, make sure both are expressed in "miles per hour" or both in "miles per minute." Comparing "miles per hour" with "miles per minute" directly without converting one to the other will lead to an incorrect comparison. You would first need to convert one of the rates so that both have the same time unit in the denominator.
