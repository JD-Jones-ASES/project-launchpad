Mastering Unit Rates: Comparing Quantities with Different Units

Overview

This section introduces the concept of rates, which are ratios comparing quantities measured in different units. It then focuses on unit rates, a special type of rate where the second quantity (denominator) is 1, making comparisons like prices and speeds much easier. You will learn how to calculate unit rates and apply them to solve practical problems such as finding the "better buy" or determining total amounts based on a given rate.

Key Concepts

  • Rates Compare Different Units: A rate is a ratio that compares two quantities with different units, such as miles and hours, or dollars and pounds. This distinguishes them from simple ratios that might compare quantities of the same unit (e.g., pencils to pencils).
  • Unit Rates Simplify Comparison: A unit rate is a specific type of rate where the second quantity (the denominator when written as a fraction) is 1. This "per 1 unit" format makes it very easy to compare different rates directly, like comparing the cost per item or speed per hour.
  • Finding a Unit Rate through Division: To calculate a unit rate, you divide both quantities in the rate by the value of the second quantity (the denominator). This process transforms the denominator into 1.
  • The Meaning of "Per": The word "per" often indicates a unit rate, meaning "for each" or "for every 1." For example, "miles per hour" means miles for every 1 hour.
  • Applications of Unit Rates: Unit rates are incredibly useful for everyday problems, including:
    • Comparing Prices: Determining which product offers a "better buy" by finding the cost per single item or unit of weight.
    • Calculating Speed: Expressing distance traveled per unit of time (e.g., miles per hour).
    • Solving Proportional Problems: Using a unit rate to find a total quantity for a different amount of the first unit (e.g., how many words typed in a different duration).
  • Consistency in Comparisons: When comparing multiple unit rates, it is crucial that the unit in the denominator is the same for all rates being compared to ensure an accurate comparison.

Important Terms

  • Rate: A ratio that compares two quantities measured in different units.
    • Examples: 120 miles in 2 hours, $7.50 for 3 pounds.
  • Unit Rate: A rate in which the second quantity (the denominator) is 1. It expresses how much of the first quantity corresponds to one unit of the second quantity.
    • Examples: 60 miles per 1 hour (60 mph), $2.50 per 1 pound.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Rates

A rate is a fundamental concept in mathematics that allows us to compare two different types of measurements. Unlike a simple ratio, which might compare apples to apples, a rate compares apples to oranges—meaning, quantities with different units. For instance, if you drive 120 miles in 2 hours, you're comparing distance (miles) to time (hours), which is a rate. Similarly, if you pay $7.50 for 3 pounds of fruit, you're comparing cost (dollars) to weight (pounds), another example of a rate.

What is a Unit Rate?

While rates compare different units, a unit rate takes this concept a step further by standardizing the comparison. A unit rate is simply a rate where the second quantity, often referred to as the denominator when written as a fraction, is exactly 1. This standardization makes unit rates incredibly useful for making direct comparisons.

Think about comparing speeds: saying one car travels 195 miles in 3 hours and another travels 140 miles in 2 hours doesn't immediately tell you which is faster. However, if you convert these to unit rates, like 65 miles per 1 hour and 70 miles per 1 hour, the comparison becomes instant and clear.

The word "per" is a strong indicator that you are dealing with a unit rate. "Miles per hour" means "miles for every 1 hour." "Price per item" means "price for every 1 item."

Finding a Unit Rate

The process for finding a unit rate is straightforward: To find a unit rate, you take the given rate (expressed as a fraction) and divide both the numerator and the denominator by the value of the denominator. This ensures that the denominator becomes 1.

Mathematically, if you have a rate of a units / b units, you find the unit rate by: a units / b units = (a / b) / (b / b) = (a / b) / 1

The result (a / b) then represents the amount of the first quantity for every 1 unit of the second quantity.

Example: Finding a Unit Price Let's say a pack of 8 juice boxes costs $6.00. To find the unit price (cost per box):

  1. Set up the rate: $6.00 / 8 boxes
  2. Divide both quantities by 8: ($6.00 / 8) / (8 boxes / 8) = $0.75 / 1 box The unit price is $0.75 per box.

Example: Speed as a Unit Rate If a car travels 195 miles in 3 hours, to find its speed as a unit rate:

  1. Set up the rate: 195 miles / 3 hours
  2. Divide both quantities by 3: (195 miles / 3) / (3 hours / 3) = 65 miles / 1 hour The car's speed is 65 miles per hour (65 mph).

Checkpoint: If a printer prints 90 pages in 6 minutes, its unit rate is (90 pages / 6 minutes) = 15 pages per minute.

Applications: Comparing and Solving Problems

Unit rates are incredibly practical for making informed decisions and solving everyday problems.

Example: Better Buy Comparison Imagine you're buying pencils:

  • Brand A: 12 pencils for $3.60
  • Brand B: 8 pencils for $2.80

To determine which is the better buy, you find the unit price (cost per pencil) for each:

  • Brand A: $3.60 / 12 pencils = $0.30 per pencil
  • Brand B: $2.80 / 8 pencils = $0.35 per pencil

Since $0.30 is less than $0.35, Brand A offers the better price per pencil.

Checkpoint: If Store X sells 6 notebooks for $8.40 ($1.40/notebook) and Store Y sells 4 notebooks for $5.00 ($1.25/notebook), Store Y has the better price.

Example: Unit Rate Word Problem Maria types 450 words in 10 minutes. How many words can she type in 25 minutes at the same rate?

  1. Find the unit rate: 450 words / 10 minutes = 45 words per minute
  2. Use the unit rate to find the total: 45 words/minute * 25 minutes = 1,125 words

Maria can type 1,125 words in 25 minutes.

Important Caution

When you are comparing unit rates, always ensure that the units in the denominator are the same for all rates you are comparing. For example, you cannot directly compare "miles per hour" with "miles per minute" without first converting one of them so that both rates have the same time unit in the denominator. Failing to do so will lead to an incorrect comparison.

In summary, unit rates simplify comparisons by standardizing the second quantity to 1. By dividing the first quantity by the second quantity, you can easily find the unit rate and use it to solve a variety of real-world problems.
