The Distributive Property: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Overview

The Distributive Property is a fundamental concept in mathematics that allows us to simplify calculations involving multiplication combined with addition or subtraction. It states that multiplying a number by a sum or difference is the same as multiplying that number by each part of the sum or difference individually, then adding or subtracting the results. This property is incredibly useful for mental math, simplifying expressions, and solving various problems, including those involving area.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of the Distributive Property: This property connects multiplication with addition or subtraction. It allows you to "distribute" a factor across terms inside parentheses.
    • Over Addition: For any numbers $a$, $b$, and $c$, $a(b + c) = ab + ac$.
    • Over Subtraction: For any numbers $a$, $b$, and $c$, $a(b - c) = ab - ac$.
  • Factoring (Reverse Distribution): The Distributive Property can also be used in reverse to combine terms that share a common factor. If you have an expression like $ab + ac$, you can factor out the common factor $a$ to get $a(b + c)$. This simplifies calculations.
  • Mental Math Strategy: The property is very effective for mental calculations by rewriting one of the factors as a sum or difference of numbers that are easier to multiply (e.g., $53 = 50 + 3$ or $97 = 100 - 3$).
  • Uniqueness: It is the only property that involves two different operations (multiplication and addition/subtraction) at the same time.
  • Limitation: The Distributive Property only applies to multiplication over addition or subtraction. It does not work with division inside the parentheses.

Important Terms

  • Distributive Property: A property stating that multiplying a number by a sum or difference gives the same result as multiplying that number by each term in the sum or difference and then adding or subtracting the products. Formally: $a(b+c) = ab + ac$ and $a(b-c) = ab - ac$.
  • Factor: A number that is multiplied by another number or expression. In $a(b+c)$, $a$ is a factor. In $ab + ac$, $a$ is a common factor.
  • Term: A single number, a variable, or the product of numbers and variables. Terms are typically separated by addition or subtraction signs in an expression (e.g., in $ab + ac$, $ab$ and $ac$ are terms).
  • Mental Math: Performing mathematical calculations in your head without the aid of a calculator or written tools, often by simplifying numbers or using properties like the Distributive Property.
  • Reverse Distribution (Factoring): The process of applying the Distributive Property backwards to identify and extract a common factor from multiple terms. For example, changing $ab + ac$ to $a(b + c)$.

Detailed Notes

Understanding the Core Idea

The Distributive Property provides a powerful way to handle multiplication when one of the numbers can be broken down into a sum or difference. For instance, if you need to calculate $6 \times 53$, it's not immediately obvious how to do this mentally. However, by thinking of $53$ as $50 + 3$, the problem becomes $6 \times (50 + 3)$. The Distributive Property allows you to "distribute" the multiplication by $6$ to both the $50$ and the $3$.

Formal Definition of the Distributive Property

For any numbers $a$, $b$, and $c$:

  • Over Addition: $a(b + c) = ab + ac$
  • Over Subtraction: $a(b - c) = ab - ac$

It's also important to note that the factor can be on the right side of the parentheses: $(b + c)a = ba + ca$. The result is the same due to the Commutative Property of Multiplication.

Key Insight: The Distributive Property is unique because it's the only property that links multiplication with addition or subtraction. This interaction makes it incredibly versatile.

Applying the Distributive Property for Calculation

The most common application is to simplify multiplication.

Example 1: Multiplication using Addition Let's find $6 \times 53$ using the Distributive Property.

  1. Rewrite $53$ as a sum: $53 = 50 + 3$.
  2. Substitute into the original expression: $6 \times (50 + 3)$.
  3. Apply the Distributive Property: $6 \times 50 + 6 \times 3$.
  4. Perform the multiplications: $300 + 18$.
  5. Perform the addition: $318$. So, $6 \times 53 = 318$.

Example 2: Multiplication using Subtraction Sometimes it's easier to think of a number as a difference, especially when it's close to a round number. Let's find $8 \times 97$.

  1. Rewrite $97$ as a difference: $97 = 100 - 3$.
  2. Substitute: $8 \times (100 - 3)$.
  3. Apply the Distributive Property: $8 \times 100 - 8 \times 3$.
  4. Perform the multiplications: $800 - 24$.
  5. Perform the subtraction: $776$. So, $8 \times 97 = 776$.

Factoring with the Distributive Property (Reverse Application)

The Distributive Property works both ways. If you see an expression where a common factor is multiplied by different terms that are being added or subtracted, you can "factor out" that common factor.

Example: Combining Terms with a Common Factor Find $7 \times 48 + 7 \times 52$.

  1. Notice that $7$ is a common factor in both terms ($7 \times 48$ and $7 \times 52$).
  2. Use the Distributive Property in reverse (factor out $7$): $7 \times (48 + 52)$.
  3. Perform the addition inside the parentheses: $7 \times 100$.
  4. Perform the multiplication: $700$. So, $7 \times 48 + 7 \times 52 = 700$. This is much simpler than calculating $7 \times 48$ and $7 \times 52$ separately and then adding.

Real-World Application: Finding Area

The Distributive Property can be used in geometric problems, such as finding the total area of a composite rectangle.

Example: Garden Area Imagine a rectangular garden 12 m long, divided into a vegetable section (8 m wide) and a flower section (5 m wide).

  1. Identify: The total width of the garden is the sum of the widths of its sections: $8 + 5 = 13$ m. The length is 12 m.
  2. Write: The total area can be expressed as $12 \times (8 + 5)$ square meters.
  3. Solve using Distributive Property: $12 \times (8 + 5) = (12 \times 8) + (12 \times 5)$ $= 96 + 60$ $= 156$
  4. Check: Calculate the total width first: $8 + 5 = 13$. Then multiply by the length: $12 \times 13 = 156$. The total area is 156 square meters.

Caution: When the Distributive Property Does NOT Apply

It is crucial to remember that the Distributive Property only works for multiplication over addition or subtraction. It does not work if there is division inside the parentheses.

Incorrect Example: $4 \times (12 \div 3)$ is NOT equal to $(4 \times 12) \div (4 \times 3)$. Let's check: $4 \times (12 \div 3) = 4 \times 4 = 16$. $(4 \times 12) \div (4 \times 3) = 48 \div 12 = 4$. Since $16 \ne 4$, the distributive property cannot be used with division in this way.

Summary of Uses

  • Breaking down hard multiplication: Rewrite one factor as a sum or difference of easier numbers.
  • Combining terms: Use factoring (reverse distribution) to simplify expressions with a common factor.
  • Mental calculations: Especially powerful for numbers near tens, hundreds, or thousands.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the Distributive Property in your own words. Provide an example of how you would use it to calculate $7 \times 62$ mentally, showing each step.
  2. Describe the difference between applying the Distributive Property (e.g., $5(10+2)$) and using it in reverse (factoring, e.g., $5 \times 10 + 5 \times 2$). Why might you choose one method over the other?
  3. The textbook states that the Distributive Property "is the only property that involves two different operations at the same time." What does this mean, and how does it make the property especially useful?
  4. Consider the expression $9 \times 95$. How can you rewrite $95$ using either addition or subtraction to make the multiplication easier with the Distributive Property? Show both possible ways and explain which one you find more efficient for mental calculation.
  5. A common mistake is to try to use the Distributive Property with division inside parentheses, for example, assuming $6 \times (18 \div 3) = (6 \times 18) \div (6 \times 3)$. Explain why this is incorrect using numerical evidence.
