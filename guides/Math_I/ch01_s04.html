The Distributive Property Study Guide

Overview

The Distributive Property is a fundamental concept in mathematics that allows you to simplify calculations by "distributing" a multiplication across an addition or subtraction within parentheses. It is a powerful tool for mental math, simplifying expressions, and solving real-world problems by breaking down complex operations into easier steps.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of the Distributive Property: For any numbers $a$, $b$, and $c$, the property states:

    • $a(b + c) = ab + ac$ (distributing over addition)
    • $a(b - c) = ab - ac$ (distributing over subtraction) This means you multiply the number outside the parentheses by each term inside the parentheses. The factor can also be on the right side: $(b + c)a = ba + ca$.

  • Connecting Operations: The Distributive Property is unique because it is the only property that connects two different operations (multiplication with addition or subtraction) at the same time.

  • Mental Math Strategy: This property is incredibly useful for performing multiplication mentally. You can rewrite one of the factors as a sum or difference of numbers that are easier to work with (often involving multiples of 10 or 100). For example, $6 \times 53$ becomes $6 \times (50 + 3)$.

  • Factoring (Reverse Distribution): The Distributive Property can also be applied in reverse. If you have an expression where multiple terms share a common factor, you can "factor out" that common factor. For example, $ab + ac = a(b + c)$. This simplifies expressions by grouping terms.

  • Limitations: It is crucial to remember that the Distributive Property only works with multiplication over addition or subtraction. It does not apply to division within parentheses.

Important Terms

  • Distributive Property: A mathematical property that states multiplying a number by a sum or difference is the same as multiplying the number by each term in the sum or difference and then adding or subtracting the products.
  • Factor: A number or expression that is multiplied by another number or expression. In the expression $a(b+c)$, $a$ is a factor, and $(b+c)$ is also a factor.
  • Term: A single number, variable, or the product of numbers and variables, separated by addition or subtraction signs in an expression. For example, in $ab + ac$, $ab$ and $ac$ are terms.
  • Common Factor: A factor that is shared by two or more terms in an expression. For instance, in $7 \times 48 + 7 \times 52$, the number 7 is a common factor.

Detailed Notes

The Distributive Property is a powerful tool for manipulating numbers and expressions. It allows us to relate multiplication to addition and subtraction.

The Fundamental Property: The core of the Distributive Property is captured by these formulas:

  • $a(b + c) = ab + ac$
  • $a(b - c) = ab - ac$

This means if you have a number $a$ multiplied by a sum $(b+c)$, you can get the same result by multiplying $a$ by $b$ and $a$ by $c$ separately, and then adding those products. The same logic applies to subtraction.

1. Applying the Distributive Property for Easier Multiplication (Distributing over Addition) Let's say you want to calculate $6 \times 53$. Multiplying 6 by 53 directly in your head can be tricky. The Distributive Property helps by breaking down 53 into simpler parts: $50 + 3$.

  • Example: Find $6 \times 53$.
    • Rewrite $53$ as $50 + 3$.
    • $6 \times 53 = 6 \times (50 + 3)$
    • Now, distribute the 6 to both 50 and 3: $ = (6 \times 50) + (6 \times 3)$
    • Perform the easier multiplications: $ = 300 + 18$
    • Add the results: $ = 318$

2. Applying the Distributive Property with Subtraction Sometimes it's easier to think of a number as a difference from a round number.

  • Example: Find $8 \times 97$.

    • Rewrite $97$ as $100 - 3$.
    • $8 \times 97 = 8 \times (100 - 3)$
    • Distribute the 8 to both 100 and 3: $ = (8 \times 100) - (8 \times 3)$
    • Perform the multiplications: $ = 800 - 24$
    • Subtract the results: $ = 776$

  • Checkpoint: Use the distributive property to find $9 \times 98$.

    • Answer: $9(100-2) = (9 \times 100) - (9 \times 2) = 900 - 18 = 882$.

3. Factoring using the Distributive Property (Reverse Application) The Distributive Property is not just for breaking apart multiplications; it can also be used to combine terms that share a common factor. This is often called "factoring."

  • Example: Find $7 \times 48 + 7 \times 52$.

    • Notice that both terms ($7 \times 48$ and $7 \times 52$) share a common factor of 7.
    • Using the Distributive Property in reverse, we can factor out the 7: $7 \times 48 + 7 \times 52 = 7 \times (48 + 52)$
    • Perform the addition inside the parentheses: $ = 7 \times 100$
    • Multiply: $ = 700$

  • Checkpoint: Find $5 \times 36 + 5 \times 64$.

    • Answer: $5(36 + 64) = 5 \times 100 = 500$.

4. Real-World Application: Finding Area The Distributive Property can be very useful in geometry, especially when dealing with areas of combined shapes.

  • Example: A rectangular garden is 12 m long. It is divided into a vegetable section 8 m wide and a flower section 5 m wide. Find the total area.
    • The total width of the garden is the sum of the widths of its sections: $8 \text{ m} + 5 \text{ m}$.
    • The total area can be found by multiplying the length by the total width: $Area = 12 \times (8 + 5)$
    • Applying the Distributive Property: $Area = (12 \times 8) + (12 \times 5)$
    • Calculate the area of each section: $ = 96 + 60$
    • Add the areas: $ = 156 \text{ m}^2$
    • (Check: $12 \times 13 = 156 \text{ m}^2$)

Important Caution: The Distributive Property does not work with division inside the parentheses. For example, $4 \times (12 \div 3)$ is not the same as $(4 \times 12) \div (4 \times 3)$.

  • $4 \times (12 \div 3) = 4 \times 4 = 16$
  • $(4 \times 12) \div (4 \times 3) = 48 \div 12 = 4$ Since $16 \neq 4$, the property does not apply to division. It is strictly for multiplication over addition or subtraction.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the Distributive Property in your own words. Provide an example of how $a(b+c)$ works using specific numbers.
  2. Describe how you would use the Distributive Property to mentally calculate $7 \times 49$. Show each step of your calculation.
  3. What does it mean to "factor" an expression using the Distributive Property? Illustrate this with the expression $11 \times 15 + 11 \times 85$.
  4. The Distributive Property is noted for connecting two different operations. What makes this significant compared to properties like the Commutative Property of Addition or the Associative Property of Multiplication?
  5. Provide an example that demonstrates why the Distributive Property does not work with division inside the parentheses. Explain the error in applying it to division.
