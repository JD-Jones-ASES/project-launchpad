Study Guide: Perimeter and Area of Polygons

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concepts of perimeter and area for various common polygons. You will learn to distinguish between these two measurements—perimeter as the distance around a shape and area as the surface it covers—and apply specific formulas to calculate them for rectangles, squares, triangles, parallelogms, and trapezoids. Understanding the importance of perpendicular height and correct units is also emphasized.

Key Concepts

  • Perimeter: The total distance around the boundary of a two-dimensional shape. It's found by adding the lengths of all its sides. For regular polygons (where all sides are equal), a simpler formula can be used.
  • Area: The measure of the amount of surface enclosed within a two-dimensional shape. It quantifies how much space the figure occupies on a flat plane.
  • Perpendicular Height (h): In area formulas for shapes like triangles, parallelograms, and trapezoids, the "height" must always be measured perpendicularly (at a 90-degree angle) from the base to the opposite side or vertex. It is not necessarily one of the slanted sides.
  • Units of Measurement: Perimeter is measured in linear units (e.g., cm, m, ft), reflecting a one-dimensional distance. Area is measured in square units (e.g., cm², m², ft²), reflecting a two-dimensional surface. Always include the correct unit in your final answer.
  • Polygon Area Formulas:
    • Rectangle: A = lw (length × width)
    • Parallelogram: A = bh (base × perpendicular height)
    • Triangle: A = 1/2 bh (half × base × perpendicular height)
    • Trapezoid: A = 1/2 (b₁ + b₂)h (half × sum of parallel bases × perpendicular height)

Important Terms

Perimeter: The total distance around the outside edge of a polygon or other two-dimensional shape. Area: The amount of two-dimensional space a shape covers or encloses. Polygon: A closed two-dimensional figure made up of straight line segments. Regular Polygon: A polygon in which all sides are equal in length and all interior angles are equal in measure. Length (l): Typically refers to the longer side of a rectangle or the general dimension of an object. Width (w): Typically refers to the shorter side of a rectangle or the general dimension of an object. Base (b): A side of a polygon chosen as the reference for measuring height, particularly in triangles, parallelograms, and trapezoids. Height (h): The perpendicular distance from the base to the opposite side or vertex. Perpendicular Height: A specific type of height measurement that forms a 90-degree angle with the base. Essential for accurate area calculations. Bases of a Trapezoid (b₁, b₂): The two parallel sides of a trapezoid.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Perimeter and Area

The section begins by clearly differentiating between perimeter and area.

  • Perimeter answers the question, "How far is it around this figure?" Think of a fence around a yard.
  • Area answers the question, "How much surface does this figure cover?" Think of how much grass is inside the yard.

Calculating Perimeter

The general rule for the perimeter of any polygon is to sum the lengths of all its sides.

  • Formula for Regular Polygons: If a polygon has n equal sides, and each side has a length s, then its perimeter P can be found using: P = ns

  • Perimeter of a Rectangle: For a rectangle with length l and width w, the perimeter can be calculated as: P = 2l + 2w This is because a rectangle has two lengths and two widths.

    Example: Perimeter of a Rectangle To find the perimeter of a rectangle with length 12 cm and width 7 cm: P = 2(12 cm) + 2(7 cm) P = 24 cm + 14 cm P = 38 cm

    Real-World Application: Fencing a Yard A rectangular yard is 20 m long and 12 m wide. To find how much fencing is needed, we calculate its perimeter:

    1. Read: The problem asks for the distance around the yard.
    2. Identify: This is a perimeter calculation for a rectangle.
    3. Write: The formula is P = 2l + 2w.
    4. Solve: Substitute the values: l = 20 m, w = 12 m. P = 2(20 m) + 2(12 m) P = 40 m + 24 m P = 64 m
    5. Check: Summing all sides: 20 + 12 + 20 + 12 = 64 m.

Calculating Area

Area formulas are specific to different polygon shapes. A crucial point for all area calculations involving height is that the height (h) must be measured perpendicular to the base. This means it forms a 90-degree angle with the base, and it's not always one of the polygon's sides.

Here are the key area formulas:

  • Rectangle: The area A of a rectangle with length l and width w is: A = lw

  • Parallelogram: The area A of a parallelogram with base b and perpendicular height h is: A = bh

    Checkpoint: Area of a Parallelogram Find the area of a parallelogram with base 9 m and height 4 m. A = bh A = (9 m)(4 m) A = 36 m²

  • Triangle: The area A of a triangle with base b and perpendicular height h is: A = 1/2 bh

    Example: Area of a Triangle Find the area of a triangle with base 10 in. and height 6 in. A = 1/2 bh A = 1/2 (10 in.)(6 in.) A = 1/2 (60 in.²) A = 30 in.²

    Checkpoint: Area of a Triangle Find the area of a triangle with base 14 cm and height 5 cm. A = 1/2 (14 cm)(5 cm) A = 1/2 (70 cm²) A = 35 cm²

  • Trapezoid: The area A of a trapezoid with parallel bases b₁ and b₂ and perpendicular height h is: A = 1/2 (b₁ + b₂)h

    Example: Area of a Trapezoid A trapezoid has parallel sides (bases) of 6 ft and 10 ft, and a height of 5 ft. A = 1/2 (b₁ + b₂)h A = 1/2 (6 ft + 10 ft)(5 ft) A = 1/2 (16 ft)(5 ft) A = 1/2 (80 ft²) A = 40 ft²

Important Considerations: Units

A common mistake is using the wrong units or forgetting to include them.

  • Perimeter is a one-dimensional measurement, so its units are linear (e.g., centimeters, feet, meters).
  • Area is a two-dimensional measurement, so its units are square units (e.g., square centimeters, square feet, square meters, denoted as cm², ft², m²). Always pay attention to the units given in the problem and use the correct units in your final answer.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental difference between "perimeter" and "area." Provide a real-world example where you would need to calculate each.
  2. A triangular garden plot has a base of 18 feet and a height of 7 feet. If you want to put decorative edging around the plot, and the three sides measure 18 ft, 8 ft, and 10 ft, what is the length of edging needed? What is the area of the garden plot for planting?
  3. Why is it crucial that the "height" used in the area formulas for triangles, parallelograms, and trapezoids is the perpendicular height? What could happen if a slant height were used instead?
  4. A classroom floor is a rectangle measuring 10 meters long and 8 meters wide. If you wanted to buy baseboards to go around the edges of the room, what calculation would you perform? If you wanted to buy carpet to cover the entire floor, what calculation would you perform? Explain the units you would expect for each answer.
  5. A trapezoidal window pane has parallel bases of 24 inches and 30 inches, and its height is 15 inches. Calculate the area of the window pane. If you wanted to apply weather stripping around the entire window, what other information would you need, and what calculation would you perform?
