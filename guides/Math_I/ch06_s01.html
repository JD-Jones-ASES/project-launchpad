Ratios and Equivalent Ratios Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concept of a ratio, which is used to compare two quantities by division. You will learn the three common ways to write ratios and understand the critical importance of the order of the terms. Furthermore, the section explains how to find equivalent ratios by scaling up or down, and how to simplify ratios to their simplest form.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of a Ratio: A ratio is a comparison of two quantities using division. It helps us understand the relationship between different amounts.
  • Forms of a Ratio: Ratios can be expressed in three distinct ways:
    • Word form: "$a$ to $b$"
    • Colon form: "$a : b$"
    • Fraction form: "$\frac{a}{b}$"
  • Terms of a Ratio: The quantities $a$ and $b$ in a ratio are called its terms.
  • Order Matters: The sequence of the terms in a ratio is crucial. "Red to Blue" is different from "Blue to Red" unless the quantities are identical.
  • Ratios vs. Fractions: While a ratio can be written as a fraction, it's not always the same as a fraction. A fraction typically represents a part of a whole, whereas a ratio compares any two quantities, which do not necessarily need to be parts of the same whole.
  • Equivalent Ratios: These are ratios that represent the same comparison or relationship, even if the numbers themselves are different. For example, $2:3$ and $4:6$ are equivalent.
  • Finding Equivalent Ratios: You can generate an equivalent ratio by multiplying or dividing both terms of the ratio by the same non-zero number.
    • $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{a \times n}{b \times n}$
    • $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{a \div n}{b \div n}$ (where $n \neq 0$)
  • Simplest Form: A ratio is in simplest form when its two terms share no common factor other than 1. To simplify a ratio, divide both terms by their Greatest Common Factor (GCF).

Important Terms

  • Ratio: A comparison of two quantities by division.
  • Terms of the Ratio: The individual quantities (represented by $a$ and $b$) being compared in a ratio.
  • Equivalent Ratios: Ratios that express the same comparison or relationship.
  • Simplest Form: The representation of a ratio where its terms have no common factor other than 1.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Ratios

A ratio is a fundamental mathematical concept used to compare two quantities. You encounter ratios frequently in everyday life, from scaling ingredients in a recipe to understanding odds in sports.

How to Write Ratios: There are three standard ways to write a ratio comparing quantity $a$ to quantity $b$:

  1. Word form: $a$ to $b$
  2. Colon form: $a : b$
  3. Fraction form: $\frac{a}{b}$

The quantities $a$ and $b$ are referred to as the terms of the ratio.

The Importance of Order: It is crucial to remember that the order of the terms in a ratio matters. The ratio of $a$ to $b$ is generally not the same as the ratio of $b$ to $a$. For example, if you have 5 red marbles and 8 blue marbles:

  • The ratio of red marbles to blue marbles is $5:8$.
  • The ratio of blue marbles to red marbles is $8:5$. These represent different comparisons.

Ratios vs. Fractions: While a ratio can be written in fraction form ($\frac{a}{b}$), it's important to understand that a ratio is not always the same as a fraction. A fraction typically represents a part of a whole (e.g., $\frac{1}{2}$ of a pizza), where the numerator is part of the denominator. A ratio, however, compares any two quantities, which do not necessarily have to be parts of the same whole (e.g., comparing red marbles to blue marbles).

Example: Writing Ratios A bag contains 5 red marbles and 8 blue marbles. Write the ratio of red marbles to blue marbles in three forms.

  • Word form: 5 to 8
  • Colon form: 5 : 8
  • Fraction form: $\frac{5}{8}$

Equivalent Ratios

Equivalent ratios are ratios that represent the exact same comparison, even if the numbers used are different. For instance, comparing 2 apples to 3 bananas is the same relationship as comparing 4 apples to 6 bananas.

Finding Equivalent Ratios: You can find an equivalent ratio by either multiplying or dividing both terms of the ratio by the same non-zero number. This is similar to finding equivalent fractions.

Property: If you have a ratio $\frac{a}{b}$, then:

  • $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{a \times n}{b \times n}$
  • $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{a \div n}{b \div n}$ (where $n$ is any non-zero number)

Example: Finding Equivalent Ratios Write three ratios equivalent to $3 : 5$. To find equivalent ratios, we can multiply both terms by the same number.

  • Multiply by 2: $3 \times 2 : 5 \times 2 = 6 : 10$
  • Multiply by 3: $3 \times 3 : 5 \times 3 = 9 : 15$
  • Multiply by 4: $3 \times 4 : 5 \times 4 = 12 : 20$ So, three equivalent ratios are $6:10$, $9:15$, and $12:20$.

Simplifying Ratios

A ratio is in its simplest form when the two terms have no common factor other than 1. To simplify a ratio, you divide both terms by their Greatest Common Factor (GCF).

Example: Simplifying a Ratio Write the ratio $18 : 24$ in simplest form.

  1. Find the GCF of 18 and 24.
    • Factors of 18: 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18
    • Factors of 24: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24
    • The GCF is 6.
  2. Divide both terms by the GCF (6):
    • $18 \div 6 = 3$
    • $24 \div 6 = 4$ The simplest form of the ratio $18 : 24$ is $3 : 4$.

Applying Ratios in Problems

Ratios are very useful for solving real-world problems involving proportional relationships.

Example: Recipe Ratios A pancake recipe uses 3 cups of flour and 2 cups of milk. If you use 9 cups of flour, how many cups of milk do you need to keep the same ratio?

  1. Identify the original ratio: Flour to Milk = $3 : 2$.
  2. Identify the new information: You are using 9 cups of flour.
  3. Find the multiplier: How many times larger is the new amount of flour compared to the original? $9 \div 3 = 3$. So, the recipe is being scaled up by a factor of 3.
  4. Apply the multiplier to the other term: Multiply the original amount of milk by the same factor (3).
    • Milk needed = $2 \text{ cups} \times 3 = 6 \text{ cups}$ You need 6 cups of milk.
  5. Check: The new ratio is $9 : 6$. Simplify this ratio by dividing both terms by 3: $9 \div 3 : 6 \div 3 = 3 : 2$. This matches the original ratio, confirming the answer.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words what a ratio is and provide an example of how ratios are used in everyday life.
  2. Describe the three different forms in which a ratio can be written. Why is it important to understand that a ratio written as a fraction is not always the same as a fraction representing part of a whole?
  3. Why does the order of the terms in a ratio matter? Give an example to illustrate your explanation.
  4. You have a ratio of $4 : 9$.
    • Write two ratios equivalent to $4 : 9$.
    • If you wanted to simplify a ratio like $28 : 63$, what mathematical operation would you use, and what would be the first step?
  5. A juice mixture calls for 5 parts orange juice concentrate to 2 parts water. If you want to make a larger batch using 15 parts of orange juice concentrate, how many parts of water will you need to maintain the correct ratio? Show your work.
