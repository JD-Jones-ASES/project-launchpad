Chapter 6, Section 1: Ratios and Equivalent Ratios Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concept of a ratio, which is a comparison of two quantities by division. You will learn the three common ways to write ratios, understand the importance of the order of terms, and discover how to find equivalent ratios by multiplying or dividing both terms by the same non-zero number. The section also covers how to simplify ratios to their simplest form.

Key Concepts

  • Ratio Definition: A ratio is a mathematical tool used to compare two quantities by division. It helps us understand the relationship between different amounts.
  • Forms of Ratios: Ratios can be expressed in three primary ways: word form (e.g., "a to b"), colon form (e.g., "a : b"), and fraction form (e.g., "a/b").
  • Order of Terms: The sequence of the quantities in a ratio is crucial. "a to b" is generally different from "b to a" unless both quantities are equal.
  • Equivalent Ratios: These are ratios that represent the same comparison or relationship, even if the numbers look different (e.g., 2:3 and 4:6).
  • Finding Equivalent Ratios: To create an equivalent ratio, you must multiply or divide both terms of the original ratio by the same non-zero number. This maintains the proportional relationship.
  • Simplest Form: A ratio is in simplest form when its two terms have no common factors other than 1. To simplify a ratio, divide both terms by their Greatest Common Factor (GCF).
  • Ratio vs. Fraction: While ratios can be written as fractions, they are not always the same. A fraction typically represents a part of a whole, whereas a ratio compares any two quantities, which may or may not be parts of the same whole.

Important Terms

  • Ratio: A comparison of two quantities by division.
  • Terms of a Ratio: The two quantities ($a$ and $b$) being compared in a ratio $a$ to $b$, $a:b$, or $a/b$.
  • Equivalent Ratios: Ratios that express the same comparison or relationship between quantities.
  • Simplest Form (of a ratio): A ratio where the two terms have no common factors other than 1.
  • Greatest Common Factor (GCF): The largest positive integer that divides evenly into two or more numbers. (Used when simplifying ratios).

Detailed Notes

What is a Ratio?

A ratio is a way to compare two quantities using division. It's a fundamental concept in mathematics that helps us understand relationships between numbers in various real-world situations, such as recipes, sports statistics, map scales, and scientific measurements.

How to Write Ratios

There are three standard forms for writing ratios. If you are comparing quantity '$a$' to quantity '$b$':

  1. Word Form: Expressed using the word "to".
    • Example: "$a$ to $b$"
    • From the example: 5 red marbles to 8 blue marbles is "5 to 8".
  2. Colon Form: Expressed using a colon.
    • Example: "$a : b$"
    • From the example: 5 red marbles to 8 blue marbles is "5 : 8".
  3. Fraction Form: Expressed as a fraction.
    • Example: "$a/b$"
    • From the example: 5 red marbles to 8 blue marbles is "5/8".

The quantities $a$ and $b$ are called the terms of the ratio.

The Importance of Order

The order in which the quantities are listed in a ratio is extremely important.

  • The ratio of $a$ to $b$ ($a:b$) is generally not the same as the ratio of $b$ to $a$ ($b:a$), unless $a$ and $b$ happen to be equal.
  • Caution: Always pay attention to the wording. "The ratio of boys to girls" is different from "the ratio of girls to boys."

Ratios vs. Fractions

It's important to note the distinction:

  • While a ratio can be written in fraction form, a ratio is not always the same as a fraction.
  • A fraction typically names part of a whole (e.g., 1/2 of a pizza).
  • A ratio compares any two quantities, which do not necessarily have to be parts of the same whole (e.g., 5 marbles to 8 marbles, or 3 cups of flour to 2 cups of milk).

Understanding Equivalent Ratios

Equivalent ratios are ratios that represent the same comparison or relationship between quantities. Even though the numbers in the ratios might be different, their proportional relationship is identical.

  • For example, $2:3$ and $4:6$ are equivalent ratios. If you have 2 apples for every 3 oranges, it's the same relationship as having 4 apples for every 6 oranges.

Finding Equivalent Ratios

You can find an equivalent ratio using two methods:

  1. Multiplication: Multiply both terms of the ratio by the same non-zero number.

    • If you have a ratio $a:b$, an equivalent ratio can be found by $(a \times n) : (b \times n)$, where $n$ is any non-zero number.
    • Example: To find ratios equivalent to $3:5$, multiply both terms by 2, 3, 4, etc.
      • $3 \times 2 : 5 \times 2 = 6 : 10$
      • $3 \times 3 : 5 \times 3 = 9 : 15$
      • $3 \times 4 : 5 \times 4 = 12 : 20$
      • So, $3:5$, $6:10$, $9:15$, and $12:20$ are all equivalent ratios.

  2. Division: Divide both terms of the ratio by the same non-zero number. This is often used to simplify ratios.

    • If you have a ratio $a:b$, an equivalent ratio can be found by $(a \div n) : (b \div n)$, where $n$ is any common non-zero factor of $a$ and $b$.

Simplifying Ratios

A ratio is in its simplest form when the two terms have no common factor other than 1. To simplify a ratio:

  1. Find the Greatest Common Factor (GCF) of the two terms in the ratio.
  2. Divide both terms of the ratio by their GCF.
    • Example: Simplify the ratio $18 : 24$.
      • The factors of 18 are 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18.
      • The factors of 24 are 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24.
      • The GCF of 18 and 24 is 6.
      • Divide both terms by 6: $18 \div 6 = 3$ and $24 \div 6 = 4$.
      • The simplest form of $18:24$ is $3:4$.

Applying Ratios in Real-World Problems (Recipe Ratios)

Ratios are very useful for scaling recipes or other proportional situations.

  • Example: A pancake recipe uses 3 cups of flour to 2 cups of milk (ratio 3:2). If you use 9 cups of flour, how much milk is needed?
    1. Identify the original ratio: Flour to Milk = $3:2$.
    2. Identify the new known quantity: 9 cups of flour.
    3. Find the multiplier: How many times was the flour increased? $9 \div 3 = 3$.
    4. Apply the multiplier to the other term: Multiply the milk quantity by the same multiplier: $2 \times 3 = 6$ cups of milk.
    5. The new ratio is $9:6$, which is equivalent to $3:2$.

Review Questions

  1. What is a ratio, and what are the three ways to write it? Provide an example for each form using quantities of your choice.
  2. Explain why the order of terms is crucial when writing a ratio. Give an example where changing the order significantly alters the meaning.
  3. Describe the difference between a ratio and a fraction, even though ratios can be written in fraction form.
  4. What does it mean for two ratios to be equivalent? Describe two methods you can use to find ratios equivalent to $4:5$.
  5. How do you simplify a ratio to its simplest form? Walk through the process of simplifying the ratio $30:45$.
