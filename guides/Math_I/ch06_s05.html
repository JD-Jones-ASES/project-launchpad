Applications of Proportional Reasoning Study Guide

Overview

This section explores how to apply ratios and proportions to solve practical problems across various fields. You will learn to use proportional reasoning for unit conversions, scaling recipes, working with similar geometric figures, and solving percent problems. The core method remains consistent: set up a proportion, cross-multiply, and solve for the unknown quantity.

Key Concepts

  • Proportional Reasoning: The ability to understand and work with relationships between quantities that change together at a constant rate. It involves setting up and solving proportions.
  • Conversion Factor: A ratio of two equivalent measurements that equals 1 (e.g., $12 \text{ in.} / 1 \text{ ft}$). These are used to convert quantities from one unit to another by setting up a proportion.
  • Recipe Scaling: Adjusting ingredient quantities in a recipe proportionally to change the number of servings.
  • Similar Figures: Two figures that have the same shape but may differ in size. Their corresponding angles are equal, and their corresponding sides are proportional. This proportionality allows us to find unknown side lengths.
  • Percent as a Proportion: Any percent problem can be solved by setting it up as a proportion where part/whole = percent/100. The word "percent" literally means "per hundred."
  • Multi-Step Proportional Problems: Many real-world problems require solving multiple proportions or combining proportional reasoning with other calculations to reach a final answer. Careful tracking of variables and steps is crucial.

Important Terms

  • Proportion: An equation stating that two ratios are equal. The fundamental tool for proportional reasoning.
  • Ratio: A comparison of two quantities by division.
  • Conversion Factor: A ratio of two equivalent measurements used to convert units, always equal to 1.
  • Similar Figures: Geometric figures that have identical shapes but potentially different sizes.
  • Corresponding Angles: Angles in the same relative position in similar figures; they are equal.
  • Corresponding Sides: Sides in the same relative position in similar figures; their lengths are proportional.
  • Scale Factor: The ratio of the lengths of two corresponding sides of similar figures.
  • Percent: A ratio out of 100, represented by the symbol %. For example, 10% means 10 out of 100.

Detailed Notes

The Foundation: Setting Up and Solving Proportions

The fundamental strategy for all applications of proportional reasoning is to:

  1. Set up a proportion: Create two equivalent ratios, with one of the values unknown (represented by a variable like $x$). Ensure corresponding units or quantities are aligned in the same positions in both ratios (e.g., dollars/euros = dollars/euros or cups/cookies = cups/cookies).
  2. Cross-multiply: Multiply the numerator of one ratio by the denominator of the other, and set this equal to the product of the remaining numerator and denominator.
  3. Solve: Isolate the variable to find its value.

1. Unit Conversion

Unit conversion involves changing a measurement from one unit to another (e.g., dollars to euros, inches to feet).

  • Using a Conversion Factor: A conversion factor is a ratio where the numerator and denominator represent the same amount but in different units (e.g., $12 \text{ in.} / 1 \text{ ft}$). Because the numerator and denominator are equivalent, a conversion factor has a value of 1.
  • Method: Set up the proportion as known unit / desired unit = given quantity / x.
    • Example (Currency Conversion):
      • Given exchange rate: $1 \text{ US dollar} = 0.85 \text{ euros}$.
      • Question: How many euros for $40$?
      • Proportion: $\frac{1 \text{ dollar}}{0.85 \text{ euros}} = \frac{40 \text{ dollars}}{x \text{ euros}}$
      • Cross-multiply: $1 \cdot x = 0.85 \cdot 40$
      • Solve: $x = 34$. You receive 34 euros.

2. Recipe Scaling

Scaling a recipe means adjusting the quantities of ingredients to make a different number of servings. This is a direct application of unit conversion, where "servings" or "number of items" acts as one of the units.

  • Method: Set up a proportion comparing ingredient quantity to servings.
    • Example (Cookie Recipe):
      • Recipe: $1.5 \text{ cups of sugar}$ for $24 \text{ cookies}$.
      • Goal: Make $60 \text{ cookies}$. How much sugar ($s$)?
      • Proportion: $\frac{1.5 \text{ cups}}{24 \text{ cookies}} = \frac{s \text{ cups}}{60 \text{ cookies}}$
      • Cross-multiply: $24s = 1.5 \cdot 60$
      • Simplify: $24s = 90$
      • Solve: $s = \frac{90}{24} = 3.75$. You need $3.75$ cups of sugar.

3. Similar Figures

Two figures are similar if they have the same shape but not necessarily the same size.

  • Key Properties:
    • Corresponding angles are equal.
    • Corresponding sides are proportional. This means the ratio of any pair of corresponding sides is constant (this constant is the scale factor).
  • Method:
    1. Identify corresponding sides.
    2. Set up proportions comparing corresponding sides. For example, $\frac{\text{Side A1}}{\text{Side A2}} = \frac{\text{Side B1}}{\text{Side B2}}$, where A1 and B1 are corresponding sides from one figure, and A2 and B2 are their corresponding sides from the second figure.
  • Example (Similar Triangles):
    • Triangle ABC is similar to Triangle DEF.
    • Given: $AB = 6 \text{ cm}$, $BC = 8 \text{ cm}$, $AC = 10 \text{ cm}$. $DE = 9 \text{ cm}$.
    • Find $EF$ and $DF$.
    • Identify corresponding sides: $AB \leftrightarrow DE$, $BC \leftrightarrow EF$, $AC \leftrightarrow DF$.
    • Find Scale Factor (optional but useful): $\frac{DE}{AB} = \frac{9}{6} = \frac{3}{2}$.
    • Find $EF$:
      • Proportion: $\frac{EF}{BC} = \frac{DE}{AB}$ or $\frac{EF}{8} = \frac{9}{6}$
      • Cross-multiply: $6 \cdot EF = 8 \cdot 9$
      • Simplify: $6 \cdot EF = 72$
      • Solve: $EF = \frac{72}{6} = 12 \text{ cm}$.
    • Find $DF$:
      • Proportion: $\frac{DF}{AC} = \frac{DE}{AB}$ or $\frac{DF}{10} = \frac{9}{6}$
      • Cross-multiply: $6 \cdot DF = 10 \cdot 9$
      • Simplify: $6 \cdot DF = 90$
      • Solve: $DF = \frac{90}{6} = 15 \text{ cm}$.

4. Percent Problems

"Percent" means "per hundred." This makes it easy to write percents as a ratio and set up proportions.

  • Method: Use the proportion $\frac{\text{part}}{\text{whole}} = \frac{\text{percent}}{100}$.
    • part: The quantity representing a portion of the whole.
    • whole: The total quantity.
    • percent: The number preceding the % sign (e.g., for 10%, use 10).

5. Multi-Step Proportional Reasoning

Many real-world problems require combining multiple steps, often involving more than one proportion or additional calculations.

  • Strategy:

    1. Read and Understand: Clearly identify what is being asked.
    2. Identify Knowns and Unknowns: List all given information and what needs to be found.
    3. Plan the Steps: Outline the sequence of calculations. You might need to find an intermediate value using one proportion before you can solve the main problem.
    4. Execute: Solve each step carefully, setting up proportions as needed.
    5. Check: Does your answer make sense in the context of the problem?

  • Example (Trail Mix with Discount):

    • Price: $4.50 \text{ per pound}$. Budget: $20.00$. Discount: $10% \text{ off}$. Max whole pounds?
    • Step 1: Calculate the discount amount per pound.
      • Proportion: $\frac{d}{4.50} = \frac{10}{100}$ (where $d$ is the discount)
      • Cross-multiply: $100d = 4.50 \cdot 10 = 45$
      • Solve: $d = \frac{45}{100} = 0.45$. So, $0.45 \text{ discount per pound}$.
    • Step 2: Calculate the discounted price per pound.
      • Discounted price = Original price - Discount = $4.50 - 0.45 = 4.05 \text{ per pound}$.
    • Step 3: Calculate the number of pounds you can buy.
      • Number of pounds = $\frac{\text{Budget}}{\text{Discounted price per pound}} = \frac{20.00}{4.05} \approx 4.938$
    • Step 4: Determine the maximum whole pounds.
      • Since you can only buy whole pounds, you can buy $4 \text{ pounds}$.
    • Step 5: Check.
      • $4 \text{ pounds} \times 4.05/\text{pound} = 16.20$ (within budget)
      • $5 \text{ pounds} \times 4.05/\text{pound} = 20.25$ (over budget)
      • The answer $4 \text{ pounds}$ is correct.

Caution: Always clearly label your variables and quantities to avoid mistakes in setting up proportions, especially in multi-step problems. Take the time to check your answer against the problem's context.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words what a "conversion factor" is and how it is used in proportional reasoning to change units. Provide an example not discussed in the text.
  2. Imagine you have a recipe for 8 servings that requires 3/4 cup of milk. You want to adjust the recipe to serve 20 people. Describe the steps you would take to determine how much milk is needed.
  3. Two rectangular windows are similar. The smaller window is 2 feet wide and 3 feet tall. If the larger window is 5 feet wide, what is its height? Explain your reasoning using the properties of similar figures.
  4. A student correctly answered 42 out of 50 questions on a test. Use a proportion to determine what percentage of the questions the student answered correctly.
  5. Describe a real-world scenario (different from the examples in the text) where you might need to use multi-step proportional reasoning to solve a problem. Outline the steps you would follow.
