Applications of Proportional Reasoning

Overview

This section explores three practical applications of proportional reasoning: unit conversion, scaling recipes, and solving problems involving similar figures and percentages. You'll learn how to set up and solve proportions to tackle real-world scenarios, reinforcing the fundamental skills of cross-multiplication and variable solving.

Key Concepts

  • Proportional Reasoning: The ability to understand and solve problems involving ratios and proportions, a fundamental skill for various real-world applications.
  • Unit Conversion: Changing a quantity from one unit of measure to another using a conversion factor.
  • Conversion Factor: A ratio of two equivalent measurements that equals 1, used to convert between units (e.g., 12 inches / 1 foot).
  • Recipe Scaling: Adjusting ingredient quantities in a recipe proportionally to change the number of servings.
  • Similar Figures: Geometric figures that have the same shape but may differ in size. Their corresponding angles are equal, and their corresponding sides are proportional.
  • Percent Problems: Problems involving parts of a whole that can be solved by setting up a proportion where part / whole = percent / 100.
  • Multi-Step Proportional Reasoning: Solving problems that require applying proportional reasoning in multiple sequential steps, often combining different types of applications.

Important Terms

  • Conversion Factor: A ratio of two equivalent measurements (e.g., 12 inches per 1 foot) that equals 1, used to convert a quantity from one unit to another.
  • Similar Figures: Two figures that have the same shape but not necessarily the same size. Their corresponding angles are equal, and their corresponding sides are proportional.
  • Percent: Meaning "per hundred," a way to express a part of a whole as a fraction of 100.

Detailed Notes

Proportional reasoning is a highly practical skill used across many aspects of daily life. This section demonstrates how to apply the principles of ratios and proportions—setting up a proportion, cross-multiplying, and solving for an unknown—to a variety of common situations.

Unit Conversion

Unit conversion involves changing a measurement from one unit to another (e.g., dollars to euros, inches to feet).

  • Conversion Factor: The key to unit conversion is using a conversion factor. This is a ratio of two equivalent measurements, such as $\frac{12 \text{ in.}}{1 \text{ ft}}$ or $\frac{1 \text{ km}}{1,000 \text{ m}}$. Because the two measurements are equal, any conversion factor essentially equals 1.
  • Solving with a Conversion Factor: To convert units, you set up a proportion: [ \frac{\text{known unit}}{\text{desired unit}} = \frac{\text{given quantity}}{x} ] Then, you cross-multiply and solve for $x$.
  • Example: Currency Conversion
    • If 1 US dollar = 0.85 euros, to find how many euros you get for $40: [ \frac{1 \text{ dollar}}{0.85 \text{ euros}} = \frac{40 \text{ dollars}}{x \text{ euros}} ]
    • Cross-multiplying gives $1 \cdot x = 0.85 \cdot 40$, which simplifies to $x = 34$. So, $40 is 34 euros.

Recipe Scaling

Proportions are ideal for scaling recipes up or down to change the number of servings.

  • Process: Set up a proportion comparing the ingredient amount to the number of servings (or items made) for the original recipe and the desired recipe. [ \frac{\text{original ingredient amount}}{\text{original servings}} = \frac{\text{new ingredient amount}}{\text{new servings}} ]
  • Example: Cookie Recipe
    • A recipe for 24 cookies calls for 1.5 cups of sugar. To make 60 cookies, you need to find the new sugar amount ($s$): [ \frac{1.5 \text{ cups}}{24 \text{ cookies}} = \frac{s \text{ cups}}{60 \text{ cookies}} ]
    • Cross-multiplying yields $24s = 1.5 \cdot 60$, which is $24s = 90$.
    • Solving for $s$: $s = 90 / 24 = 3.75$ cups of sugar.

Similar Figures

In geometry, proportions are used to analyze similar figures.

  • Definition: Two figures are similar if they have the exact same shape but not necessarily the same size.
  • Key Properties:
    • Corresponding angles are equal.
    • Corresponding sides are proportional. This means the ratios of the lengths of corresponding sides are equal.
  • Example: Similar Triangles
    • If triangle ABC is similar to triangle DEF, and AB corresponds to DE, BC to EF, and AC to DF, then their side ratios are equal.
    • Given ABC sides (6 cm, 8 cm, 10 cm) and DE = 9 cm.
    • First, find the scale factor by comparing known corresponding sides: $\frac{DE}{AB} = \frac{9}{6} = \frac{3}{2}$.
    • Then, use this scale factor to find unknown sides:
      • For $EF$ (corresponds to $BC$): $\frac{EF}{BC} = \frac{3}{2} \Rightarrow \frac{EF}{8} = \frac{3}{2} \Rightarrow EF = \frac{3 \cdot 8}{2} = 12$ cm.
      • For $DF$ (corresponds to $AC$): $\frac{DF}{AC} = \frac{3}{2} \Rightarrow \frac{DF}{10} = \frac{3}{2} \Rightarrow DF = \frac{3 \cdot 10}{2} = 15$ cm.

Percent Problems

Percentages are another common application solved using proportions.

  • Percent as a Proportion: Any percent problem can be structured as a proportion: [ \frac{\text{part}}{\text{whole}} = \frac{\text{percent}}{100} ] The "percent" value is the number before the $%$ sign (e.g., for 10%, use 10). The word "percent" literally means "per hundred," which explains why $\frac{p}{100}$ is used.

Multi-Step Proportional Reasoning

Some problems require combining several applications of proportional reasoning.

  • Strategy: Break down the problem into smaller, manageable steps. Solve each step using proportions.
  • Example: Trail Mix Purchase
    • Problem: Buy trail mix at $4.50/lb with a 10% coupon, budget $20.00. Max whole pounds?
    • Step 1: Calculate the discount per pound. [ \frac{d}{$4.50} = \frac{10}{100} ]
      • $100d = 45 \Rightarrow d = $0.45$ (discount amount).
    • Step 2: Find the discounted price per pound.
      • $$4.50 - $0.45 = $4.05$ per pound.
    • Step 3: Determine how many pounds can be bought.
      • Divide the total budget by the discounted price per pound: $$20.00 / $4.05 \approx 4.94$ pounds.
    • Step 4: Answer the question (whole pounds).
      • Since you can only buy whole pounds, the maximum is 4 pounds.
    • Check: Verify that 4 pounds ($4 \cdot $4.05 = $16.20$) is within budget, but 5 pounds ($5 \cdot $4.05 = $20.25$) exceeds it.
  • Caution: In multi-step problems, carefully label your variables and quantities. Always double-check your setup and calculations to avoid errors.

Summary of Proportional Reasoning Applications

  • Unit Conversion: Use a conversion factor (a ratio equal to 1) to change between units.
  • Recipe Scaling: Set up proportions between ingredients and servings to adjust recipe quantities.
  • Similar Figures: Use the proportionality of corresponding sides to find unknown lengths in similar shapes.
  • Percent Problems: Express as $\frac{\text{part}}{\text{whole}} = \frac{\text{percent}}{100}$ to solve for unknown values.
  • Multi-Step Problems: Solve complex problems by breaking them into sequential proportional reasoning steps.

Review Questions

  1. Explain what a "conversion factor" is and how it is used in unit conversion. Provide an example not discussed in the text, such as converting meters to centimeters or hours to minutes.
  2. You are adapting a recipe that makes 18 servings and requires 3 cups of flour. If you only want to make 6 servings, how much flour should you use? Show your proportional setup and solution.
  3. Describe the two main properties of similar figures. If two triangles are similar and the sides of the first triangle are 4 cm, 6 cm, and 8 cm, and the shortest side of the second triangle is 10 cm, how would you find the lengths of the other two sides of the second triangle?
  4. How can any percent problem be written as a proportion? Write the general form ($\frac{\text{part}}{\text{whole}} = \frac{\text{percent}}{100}$) and explain what "part" and "whole" represent in this context.
  5. Imagine you want to buy an item that costs $75.00. There's a 20% discount, and you also have to pay 5% sales tax on the discounted price. Outline the steps you would take, using proportional reasoning, to find the final price you would pay.
