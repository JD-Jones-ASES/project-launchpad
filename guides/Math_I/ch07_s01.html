Understanding Percents: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concept of percents, explaining their meaning as a ratio "per hundred." It covers how to visualize percents using a 10x10 grid model, where each small square represents one percent. Furthermore, the guide details the process of converting fractions into percents, particularly by finding equivalent fractions with a denominator of 100, and clarifies that percents can extend beyond 100%.

Key Concepts

  • What a Percent Is: A percent is a special type of ratio that compares a number to 100. The word "percent" originates from the Latin phrase "per centum," meaning "per hundred."
  • The Percent Symbol (%): This symbol is used to denote "out of 100." For example, 45% means 45 out of 100, or the fraction 45/100.
  • Percent Grid Model: This is a visual aid, typically a 10x10 square grid divided into 100 equal small squares. Each small square in the grid represents 1%, making it easy to shade or identify a given percentage.
    • 100% represents the entire quantity (all 100 squares shaded).
    • 0% represents none of the quantity (0 squares shaded).
    • 50% represents half of the quantity (50 squares shaded).
  • Converting Fractions to Percents: To express a fraction as a percent, the primary method involves rewriting it as an equivalent fraction with a denominator of 100. Once the denominator is 100, the numerator directly becomes the percent (e.g., n/100 = n%).
  • Percents Beyond 100%: Percents are not limited to 0% to 100%. It is possible to have percents greater than 100% when comparing a quantity that is larger than the original whole, such as illustrating growth or increase.

Important Terms

  • Percent: A ratio that compares a number to 100. It literally means "per hundred" or "out of 100."
  • Percent Grid: A 10x10 grid, composed of 100 equal small squares, used as a visual model to represent percents. Each small square corresponds to 1%.
  • Equivalent Fraction: Fractions that have different numerators and denominators but represent the same value. For example, 3/4 and 75/100 are equivalent fractions.

Detailed Notes

1. What are Percents?

Percents are a fundamental concept in mathematics with widespread applications in our daily lives, from understanding discounts to interpreting statistics. Understanding what a percent represents is the first step.

  • Definition: A percent is essentially a ratio that expresses how many parts there are out of a total of one hundred parts.
  • Origin and Symbol: The term "percent" comes from the Latin phrase "per centum," which literally translates to "per hundred." The symbol used to denote a percent is '%'.
  • Meaning of the Symbol: When you see the '%' symbol, it means "out of 100." For instance, if you hear "45%," it means "45 out of 100." This can be easily written as the fraction 45/100.
  • Everyday Examples: Percents are encountered daily in various contexts:
    • Sales: "25% off" means you save 25 cents for every 100 cents (or 25 dollars for every 100 dollars) of the original price.
    • Test Scores: "85% correct" means you answered 85 questions correctly out of every 100 possible questions (or an equivalent fraction).
    • Battery Levels: "10% battery remaining" means 10 parts out of 100 total battery capacity are left.

2. Modeling Percents with Grids

Visualizing percents can greatly aid understanding. The percent grid model provides an excellent way to see what a percent means in a concrete way.

  • The Percent Grid: This is a powerful visual tool for understanding percents. It typically consists of a large square divided into 10 columns and 10 rows, resulting in 100 smaller, equal squares.
  • Value of Each Square: In a percent grid, each individual small square represents 1% of the whole grid. This makes it intuitive to represent any percentage.
  • Representing Different Percents:
    • To show 45%: You would shade 45 of the 100 small squares.
    • To show 100%: You would shade all 100 of the small squares, representing the entire quantity.
    • To show 0%: You would shade none of the squares, representing no part of the quantity.
  • Key Equivalents to Remember:
    • 100% signifies the entire amount or all of something.
    • 50% signifies half of something.
    • 0% signifies none of something.
  • Example (Shading): If you are asked to shade a 10x10 grid to represent 45%, you simply count and shade 45 squares out of the 100 available squares.
  • Example (Reading from a Model): If you see a 10x10 grid where 72 squares are shaded, you can immediately determine that 72% of the grid is shaded because each square represents 1%.

3. Converting Fractions to Percents

Often, information is given in fractional form, and we need to express it as a percent. This section details how to convert fractions into percents, especially when dealing with a denominator of 100 or when needing to find an equivalent fraction.

  • Direct Conversion (Denominator of 100): If a fraction already has 100 as its denominator, the conversion is straightforward: the numerator becomes the percent. For example, if you have n/100, that directly equals n%.
  • Finding an Equivalent Fraction (Denominator Not 100): When the denominator of a fraction is not 100, the strategy is to find an equivalent fraction that does have a denominator of 100.
    • Method: To achieve this, multiply both the numerator and the denominator of the original fraction by the same number (which will make the denominator equal to 100).
  • Example 1 (Simple Fraction Conversion): Let's convert the fraction 3/4 into a percent.
    • We need the denominator to be 100. Since 4 * 25 = 100, we multiply both the numerator and the denominator by 25:
    • (3 * 25) / (4 * 25) = 75/100.
    • Now that the denominator is 100, the numerator is the percent: 75%. So, 3/4 = 75%.
  • Example 2 (Real-World Application - Test Score): Maria answered 18 out of 20 questions correctly on a quiz. To find her score as a percent:
    • Step 1 (Identify Fraction): Maria's score is represented by the fraction 18/20.
    • Step 2 (Find Equivalent Fraction): To get a denominator of 100, we multiply 20 by 5. So, we must also multiply the numerator by 5:
      • (18 * 5) / (20 * 5) = 90/100.
    • Step 3 (Convert to Percent): With a denominator of 100, the fraction 90/100 equals 90%. Maria scored 90% on her quiz.
    • Step 4 (Check): To verify, calculate 90% of 20: 0.90 * 20 = 18, which matches the number of correct answers.

4. Percents Greater Than 100%

While we often think of 100% as "the whole," percents can actually exceed 100%. This is important for understanding growth, increase, or situations where a quantity is compared to a smaller original amount.

  • Concept: A percent greater than 100% indicates that a quantity is larger than the original whole or baseline amount. It represents more than one complete unit or item.
  • Example (Plant Growth): Imagine a plant that initially measured 20 cm in height and then grew to 50 cm. To express the new height as a percent of the original height:
    • Fraction: The new height compared to the original is 50/20.
    • Convert to Percent: To get a denominator of 100, multiply both numerator and denominator by 5:
      • (50 * 5) / (20 * 5) = 250/100.
    • Result: The new height is 250% of the original height. This means the plant is two and a half times its original height.
  • Summary: This highlights that percents are flexible and can represent any part of a whole, the whole itself, or even amounts larger than the whole.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words what the term "percent" means and how its Latin origin "per centum" relates to its mathematical definition. Provide an example of how you might write a percentage as a fraction.
  2. Describe how a 10x10 percent grid can be used to model and understand percents. What does each small square represent, and how would you use the grid to show, for example, 60%?
  3. If you answered 21 questions correctly out of a total of 25 questions on a history test, how would you convert this score into a percentage? Show all the steps involved in the conversion.
  4. Why is it possible for a percent to be greater than 100%? Provide a real-world scenario (different from the plant growth example) where you might encounter a percentage exceeding 100% and explain what it signifies.
  5. Think about everyday situations where you encounter percents. List three different examples (e.g., in news, shopping, or sports) and briefly explain what each specific percentage signifies in that particular context.
