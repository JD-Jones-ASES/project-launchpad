Study Guide: Multiplying and Dividing Integers

Overview

This section extends the operations of multiplication and division from whole numbers to integers, which include negative numbers. The primary focus is on understanding and applying the sign rules to determine whether the product or quotient of integers will be positive or negative, along with special rules for operations involving zero.

Key Concepts

  • Sign Rule for Same Signs (Multiplication & Division): When multiplying or dividing two integers with the same sign (both positive or both negative), the result is always positive.
  • Sign Rule for Different Signs (Multiplication & Division): When multiplying or dividing two integers with different signs (one positive and one negative), the result is always negative.
  • Multiplication by Zero: The product of any integer and zero is always zero.
  • Division by Zero: Division by zero is undefined; it is an operation that has no valid mathematical result.
  • Zero Divided by a Nonzero Number: When zero is divided by any nonzero integer, the quotient is zero.
  • Multiplying Multiple Integers: For a product involving three or more factors, count the number of negative factors. An even count results in a positive product, while an odd count results in a negative product.

Important Terms

  • Integers: The set of whole numbers and their opposites (e.g., ..., -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, ...).
  • Product: The result of a multiplication operation.
  • Factors: The numbers that are multiplied together to get a product.
  • Quotient: The result of a division operation.
  • Undefined: A term used to describe a mathematical expression or operation that does not have a meaningful or valid numerical result, such as division by zero.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Multiplying and Dividing Integers

You are familiar with multiplying and dividing positive whole numbers. This section builds on that knowledge by introducing how to perform these operations when negative numbers (integers) are involved. The main challenge is determining the correct sign of the answer.

Multiplying Integers

1. Multiplying Integers with the Same Signs When both integers in a multiplication problem have the same sign (both positive or both negative), their product is always positive.

  • Positive times Positive = Positive
    • Example: (+) * (+) = +
    • Numerical Example: 4 * 5 = 20
  • Negative times Negative = Positive
    • Example: (-) * (-) = +
    • Numerical Example: (-3) * (-6) = 18

Example:

  • (-4) * (-9): Both factors are negative (same sign), so the product is positive. Result: 36.
  • 6 * 7: Both factors are positive (same sign), so the product is positive. Result: 42.

2. Multiplying Integers with Different Signs When the two integers in a multiplication problem have different signs (one positive and one negative), their product is always negative.

  • Positive times Negative = Negative
    • Example: (+) * (-) = -
    • Numerical Example: 7 * (-2) = -14
  • Negative times Positive = Negative
    • Example: (-) * (+) = -
    • Numerical Example: (-5) * 3 = -15

Example:

  • (-8) * 5: Different signs, so the product is negative. Result: -40.
  • 3 * (-11): Different signs, so the product is negative. Result: -33.

3. Multiplying by Zero Any integer multiplied by zero always results in zero.

  • For any integer a: a * 0 = 0
    • Example: 5 * 0 = 0, (-10) * 0 = 0

Understanding the Negative Times Negative Rule (Pattern) Consider the pattern: 3 * (-1) = -3 2 * (-1) = -2 1 * (-1) = -1 0 * (-1) = 0 Notice that as the first factor decreases by 1, the product increases by 1. Following this pattern: (-1) * (-1) should be 0 + 1, which is 1. This pattern helps to intuitively understand why a negative number multiplied by a negative number results in a positive number.

Dividing Integers

1. Sign Rules for Division The rules for determining the sign of a quotient are exactly the same as for multiplication:

  • Same Signs: If the dividend and divisor have the same sign, the quotient is positive.
    • Positive divided by Positive = Positive (+/+ = +)
    • Negative divided by Negative = Positive (-/- = +)
  • Different Signs: If the dividend and divisor have different signs, the quotient is negative.
    • Positive divided by Negative = Negative (+/- = -)
    • Negative divided by Positive = Negative (-/+ = -)

Example:

  • (-36) / (-4): Same signs (both negative), so the quotient is positive. Result: 9.
  • 42 / (-7): Different signs, so the quotient is negative. Result: -6.
  • -24 / 8: Different signs, so the quotient is negative. Result: -3.

2. Division by Zero It is critically important to remember that division by zero is undefined. You cannot divide any number by zero.

  • Example: 5 / 0 has no answer. This is not zero; it is simply not a defined mathematical operation.

3. Zero Divided by a Nonzero Number If zero is the dividend (the number being divided) and the divisor is any nonzero integer, the quotient is always zero.

  • Example: 0 / (-5) = 0

Multiplying Three or More Factors

When multiplying more than two integers, you don't need to multiply them in pairs and track signs. Instead, you can count the number of negative factors:

  • Even number of negative factors: The product will be positive.
    • Example: (-2) * (-3) * 1 * (-4) * (-1) (4 negative factors) = Positive result
  • Odd number of negative factors: The product will be negative.
    • Example: (-2) * (-3) * (-1) (3 negative factors) = -6

Real-World Application

Integer operations are useful in various situations. For instance, if a stock price drops by $3 each day for 5 days, the total change can be represented as 5 * (-3) = -15. This indicates a total drop of $15.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words the rule for multiplying two integers with the same sign. Provide an example.
  2. What is the rule for dividing two integers with different signs? Give a numerical example to illustrate your point.
  3. Calculate the product of (-9) * 7 and the quotient of (-60) / (-12). Explain how you determined the sign for each.
  4. Why is 10 / 0 considered "undefined," while 0 / 10 equals 0? What is the fundamental difference between these two operations?
  5. Consider the product (-1) * 2 * (-3) * (-4). Without calculating the final value, determine whether the product will be positive or negative, and explain your reasoning.
