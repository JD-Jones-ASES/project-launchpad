Multiplying Mixed Numbers Study Guide

Overview

This section focuses on the essential method for multiplying mixed numbers: converting them into improper fractions before performing the multiplication. It outlines a clear, step-by-step process that includes cross-canceling for simplification and converting the final answer back to a mixed number when appropriate.

Key Concepts

  • Convert to Improper Fractions First: The foundational rule for multiplying mixed numbers is to always convert each mixed number into an improper fraction before you begin the multiplication process.
  • Do Not Multiply Parts Separately: A common mistake is to multiply the whole number parts and the fraction parts of mixed numbers independently. This yields an incorrect result. For example, $2,12 \times 3,13 \neq 6,16$.
  • Cross Cancel Common Factors: Before multiplying the numerators and denominators, look for any common factors between any numerator and any denominator. Dividing these out (cross-canceling) simplifies the numbers involved and makes the final multiplication easier.
  • Standard Fraction Multiplication: After converting and cross-canceling, multiply the remaining numerators together to get the new numerator, and multiply the remaining denominators together to get the new denominator.
  • Simplify and Convert Back: The product will often be an improper fraction. You must simplify this fraction to its lowest terms and, if the context requires it or if it's an improper fraction, convert it back into a mixed number.
  • Whole Numbers as Fractions: When multiplying a whole number by a mixed number, remember to write the whole number as a fraction with a denominator of 1 (e.g., $6 = 61$) before converting the mixed number and proceeding with multiplication.

Important Terms

  • Mixed Number: A number consisting of a whole number and a proper fraction (e.g., $2,13$).
  • Improper Fraction: A fraction where the numerator is greater than or equal to the denominator (e.g., $94$).
  • Numerator: The top number in a fraction, indicating how many parts of the whole are being considered.
  • Denominator: The bottom number in a fraction, indicating the total number of equal parts that make up one whole.
  • Cross Cancel: A technique used in multiplying fractions where common factors are divided out from a numerator and a denominator before the actual multiplication, simplifying the calculation.
  • Proper Fraction: A fraction where the numerator is smaller than the denominator (e.g., $23$).

Detailed Notes

Multiplying mixed numbers requires a specific approach to ensure accuracy. Unlike addition and subtraction of mixed numbers, where you might sometimes work with the whole and fractional parts separately, multiplication always requires conversion to improper fractions first.

Here's the detailed process for multiplying mixed numbers:

The Method for Multiplying Mixed Numbers

  1. Convert to Improper Fractions: The very first step is to transform every mixed number into an improper fraction.

    • To do this, multiply the whole number by the denominator of the fraction, then add the numerator. Place this result over the original denominator.
    • Example: $2,14 = (2 \times 4) + 14 = 8 + 14 = 94$.

  2. Handle Whole Numbers (if present): If you have a whole number, write it as a fraction with a denominator of 1.

    • Example: $6 = 61$.

  3. Cross Cancel Common Factors: Before multiplying, look for any numbers in the numerators and denominators that share common factors. Divide these common factors out. This step makes the numbers smaller and the multiplication easier.

    • You can cross cancel diagonally (a numerator with an opposite denominator) or vertically (a numerator with the denominator directly below it, if it's a whole number written as a fraction).

  4. Multiply Numerators and Denominators: After cross-canceling, multiply the remaining numbers in the numerators together. This product will be the new numerator. Then, multiply the remaining numbers in the denominators together to get the new denominator.

  5. Simplify and Convert Back: The result of your multiplication will likely be an improper fraction.

    • Simplify: Reduce the fraction to its lowest terms by dividing both the numerator and denominator by their greatest common factor.
    • Convert to a Mixed Number: If the final answer is an improper fraction, convert it back to a mixed number by dividing the numerator by the denominator. The quotient becomes the whole number, the remainder becomes the new numerator, and the denominator stays the same.

Caution: A Common Mistake

It is crucial not to multiply the whole number parts and the fraction parts of mixed numbers separately. For instance, $2,12 \times 3,13$ is not $6,16$. This approach will always lead to an incorrect answer.

Examples Walkthrough

Example 1: Mixed Number Times a Fraction

Problem: Multiply $2,14 \times 23$

Solution:

  1. Convert $2,14$ to an improper fraction: $2,14 = (2 \times 4) + 14 = 8 + 14 = 94$
  2. Multiply: Now we have $94 \times 23$.
  3. Cross Cancel:
    • $9$ and $3$ share a common factor of $3$ ($9 \div 3 = 3$, $3 \div 3 = 1$).
    • $2$ and $4$ share a common factor of $2$ ($2 \div 2 = 1$, $4 \div 2 = 2$). [ \frac{3}{\cancel{9}_4} \times \frac{\cancel{2}^1}{3} = \frac{3 \times 1}{2 \times 1} = \frac{3}{2} ]
  4. Simplify/Convert: Convert the improper fraction $32$ to a mixed number: $3 \div 2 = 1$ with a remainder of $1$. Result: $1,12$

Example 2: Mixed Number Times Mixed Number

Problem: Multiply $1,23 \times 2,14$

Solution:

  1. Convert both mixed numbers to improper fractions:
    • $1,23 = (1 \times 3) + 23 = 3 + 23 = 53$
    • $2,14 = (2 \times 4) + 14 = 8 + 14 = 94$
  2. Multiply: Now we have $53 \times 94$.
  3. Cross Cancel:
    • $3$ and $9$ share a common factor of $3$ ($3 \div 3 = 1$, $9 \div 3 = 3$). [ \frac{5}{\cancel{3}_1} \times \frac{\cancel{9}^3}{4} = \frac{5 \times 3}{1 \times 4} = \frac{15}{4} ]
  4. Simplify/Convert: Convert $154$ to a mixed number: $15 \div 4 = 3$ with a remainder of $3$. Result: $3,34$

Example 3: Whole Number Times a Mixed Number

Problem: Multiply $6 \times 2,13$

Solution:

  1. Write whole number as a fraction: $6 = 61$.
  2. Convert mixed number to an improper fraction:
    • $2,13 = (2 \times 3) + 13 = 6 + 13 = 73$
  3. Multiply: Now we have $61 \times 73$.
  4. Cross Cancel:
    • $6$ and $3$ share a common factor of $3$ ($6 \div 3 = 2$, $3 \div 3 = 1$). [ \frac{\cancel{6}^2}{1} \times \frac{7}{\cancel{3}_1} = \frac{2 \times 7}{1 \times 1} = \frac{14}{1} ]
  5. Simplify: $141$ simplifies to $14$. Result: $14$

Real-World Application: Recipe Scaling

Problem: A cookie recipe calls for $1,34$ cups of flour. You want to make $2,12$ times the recipe. How much flour do you need?

Solution:

  1. Identify: We need to multiply the amount of flour by the scaling factor.
  2. Write: $1,34 \times 2,12$
  3. Convert to improper fractions:
    • $1,34 = (1 \times 4) + 34 = 74$
    • $2,12 = (2 \times 2) + 12 = 52$
  4. Multiply: $74 \times 52 = 358$ (no cross-canceling possible here).
  5. Convert $358$ to a mixed number: $35 \div 8 = 4$ with a remainder of $3$. Result: $4,38$ cups of flour.

Summary of Multiplying Mixed Numbers

  • Always convert mixed numbers to improper fractions before multiplying.
  • Cross cancel common factors to keep numbers small and simplify calculations.
  • Multiply numerators and denominators.
  • Simplify the resulting improper fraction and convert it back to a mixed number when appropriate for the final answer.
