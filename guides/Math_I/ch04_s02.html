Multiplying Mixed Numbers Study Guide

Overview

This section explains the correct method for multiplying mixed numbers. Unlike multiplying whole numbers and fractions separately, the essential first step is to convert all mixed numbers into improper fractions. Once converted, you can apply the standard fraction multiplication rules, including cross-cancellation, to find the product, which is then simplified and often converted back into a mixed number.

Key Concepts

  • Convert to Improper Fractions First: The fundamental rule for multiplying mixed numbers is to convert each mixed number into an improper fraction before performing any multiplication. This ensures the entire value of each number is correctly accounted for.
  • Do Not Multiply Separately: It is a common error to try multiplying the whole number parts and the fraction parts of mixed numbers independently. This method is incorrect and will lead to an incorrect answer (e.g., $2\frac{1}{2} \times 3\frac{1}{3} \neq 6\frac{1}{6}$).
  • Cross-Cancellation: Before multiplying the numerators and denominators, look for common factors between any numerator and any denominator. Cross-canceling these factors simplifies the numbers involved, making the multiplication easier and often leading to a simplified product without extra steps later.
  • Standard Fraction Multiplication: After converting to improper fractions and cross-canceling, multiply the remaining numerators together and the remaining denominators together.
  • Simplify and Convert Back: The product of mixed numbers should typically be simplified to its lowest terms. If the result is an improper fraction, convert it back into a mixed number for the final answer, especially in word problems or when a mixed number format is more intuitive.
  • Whole Numbers as Fractions: When multiplying a whole number by a mixed number, remember to write the whole number as a fraction with a denominator of 1 (e.g., $6 = \frac{6}{1}$) to facilitate multiplication with other fractions.

Important Terms

  • Mixed Number: A number consisting of a whole number and a fraction (e.g., $2\frac{1}{4}$).
  • Improper Fraction: A fraction where the numerator is greater than or equal to the denominator (e.g., $\frac{9}{4}$).
  • Cross-Cancellation: A technique used in fraction multiplication where common factors are divided out from a numerator and a denominator before multiplying, simplifying the calculation.
  • Numerator: The top number in a fraction, indicating how many parts are being considered.
  • Denominator: The bottom number in a fraction, indicating the total number of equal parts in the whole.
  • Simplify (a fraction): To reduce a fraction to its lowest terms by dividing both the numerator and the denominator by their greatest common factor.

Detailed Notes

The Core Method for Multiplying Mixed Numbers

Multiplying mixed numbers follows a specific four-step process to ensure accuracy. This method avoids common pitfalls and simplifies the calculation.

  1. Convert Mixed Numbers to Improper Fractions: This is the crucial first step. To convert a mixed number like $A\frac{B}{C}$ to an improper fraction:

    • Multiply the whole number part ($A$) by the denominator ($C$).
    • Add the numerator ($B$) to this product.
    • Place this sum over the original denominator ($C$).
    • Example: $2\frac{1}{4} = \frac{(2 \times 4) + 1}{4} = \frac{8 + 1}{4} = \frac{9}{4}$.

  2. Cross-Cancel Common Factors: Before you multiply, look for any numerator and any denominator that share a common factor. Divide both by this common factor. This step reduces the size of the numbers you'll be multiplying, making the calculation easier and often simplifying the final answer.

    • Example: In $\frac{9}{4} \times \frac{2}{3}$, you can cross-cancel:
      • $9$ (numerator) and $3$ (denominator) share a common factor of $3$. Divide $9 \div 3 = 3$ and $3 \div 3 = 1$.
      • $2$ (numerator) and $4$ (denominator) share a common factor of $2$. Divide $2 \div 2 = 1$ and $4 \div 2 = 2$.
      • The expression becomes $\frac{3}{2} \times \frac{1}{1}$.

  3. Multiply Numerators and Denominators: After converting and cross-canceling, multiply the new numerators together to get the numerator of the product, and multiply the new denominators together to get the denominator of the product.

    • Example: From $\frac{3}{2} \times \frac{1}{1}$, multiply: $(3 \times 1) = 3$ (new numerator) and $(2 \times 1) = 2$ (new denominator). The product is $\frac{3}{2}$.

  4. Simplify or Convert Back to a Mixed Number: If your final fraction is improper (numerator is greater than or equal to the denominator), convert it back into a mixed number. Always ensure the fractional part is in its simplest form.

    • Example: $\frac{3}{2}$ converts to $1\frac{1}{2}$ (since $3 \div 2 = 1$ with a remainder of $1$).

Important Caution

Never multiply the whole number parts and the fractional parts of mixed numbers separately. This is a common mistake and will lead to an incorrect answer. For instance, $2\frac{1}{2} \times 3\frac{1}{3}$ is NOT $6\frac{1}{6}$. You must convert to improper fractions first.

Examples from the Text

  • Mixed Number Times a Fraction:

    • Problem: $2\frac{1}{4} \times \frac{2}{3}$
    • Convert: $2\frac{1}{4} = \frac{(2 \times 4) + 1}{4} = \frac{9}{4}$.
    • Multiply with cross-cancellation: $\frac{9}{4} \times \frac{2}{3} \rightarrow \frac{\cancel{9}^3}{\cancel{4}^2} \times \frac{\cancel{2}^1}{\cancel{3}^1} = \frac{3 \times 1}{2 \times 1} = \frac{3}{2}$.
    • Convert to mixed number: $\frac{3}{2} = 1\frac{1}{2}$.

  • Mixed Number Times Mixed Number:

    • Problem: $1\frac{2}{3} \times 2\frac{1}{4}$
    • Convert both: $1\frac{2}{3} = \frac{(1 \times 3) + 2}{3} = \frac{5}{3}$ and $2\frac{1}{4} = \frac{(2 \times 4) + 1}{4} = \frac{9}{4}$.
    • Multiply with cross-cancellation: $\frac{5}{3} \times \frac{9}{4} \rightarrow \frac{5}{\cancel{3}^1} \times \frac{\cancel{9}^3}{4} = \frac{5 \times 3}{1 \times 4} = \frac{15}{4}$.
    • Convert to mixed number: $\frac{15}{4} = 3\frac{3}{4}$.

  • Whole Number Times a Mixed Number:

    • Problem: $6 \times 2\frac{1}{3}$
    • Write whole number as fraction and convert mixed number: $6 = \frac{6}{1}$ and $2\frac{1}{3} = \frac{(2 \times 3) + 1}{3} = \frac{7}{3}$.
    • Multiply with cross-cancellation: $\frac{6}{1} \times \frac{7}{3} \rightarrow \frac{\cancel{6}^2}{1} \times \frac{7}{\cancel{3}^1} = \frac{2 \times 7}{1 \times 1} = \frac{14}{1}$.
    • Simplify: $\frac{14}{1} = 14$.
    • Note: Cross-canceling with whole numbers can make the problem significantly easier, sometimes even resulting in a whole number answer.

Real-World Application: Recipe Scaling

  • Problem: A cookie recipe needs $1\frac{3}{4}$ cups of flour. You want to make $2\frac{1}{2}$ times the recipe. How much flour is needed?
  • Identify the operation: Multiply the amount of flour by the scaling factor.
  • Expression: $1\frac{3}{4} \times 2\frac{1}{2}$.
  • Convert to improper fractions: $1\frac{3}{4} = \frac{7}{4}$ and $2\frac{1}{2} = \frac{5}{2}$.
  • Multiply: $\frac{7}{4} \times \frac{5}{2} = \frac{35}{8}$.
  • Convert to mixed number: $\frac{35}{8} = 4\frac{3}{8}$.
  • Answer: You need $4\frac{3}{8}$ cups of flour.
