Study Guide: Decimal Place Value and Comparing Decimals

Overview

This section introduces decimals as an extension of the whole-number place value system to represent values less than one. It covers how to read, write, and identify the place value of digits in decimals up to the thousandths place. Furthermore, it provides a systematic method for comparing two or more decimals by understanding the importance of aligning decimal points and annexing zeros.

Key Concepts

  • Decimal Place Value System: Decimals extend the familiar base-ten place value system to numbers smaller than one. Each place to the right of the decimal point represents a fraction with a denominator that is a power of ten (tenths, hundredths, thousandths, etc.).
  • Three Forms of a Decimal: Decimals can be expressed in three distinct ways:
    • Standard Form: The numerical representation (e.g., 4.037).
    • Expanded Form: Breaking down the number into the sum of its place values (e.g., 4 + 0.0 + 0.03 + 0.007).
    • Word Form: Writing the decimal using words, with "and" marking the decimal point (e.g., four and thirty-seven thousandths).
  • Comparing Decimals: To accurately compare decimals, a structured approach is necessary. This involves aligning the decimal points, using annexing zeros to ensure both numbers have the same number of decimal places, and then comparing digits from left to right.
  • Annexing Zeros: This technique involves adding zeros to the rightmost end of a decimal without altering its value (e.g., 3.5 is equivalent to 3.50 or 3.500). It is a crucial step for fair comparison between decimals with differing numbers of decimal places.

Important Terms

Decimal : A number that uses a decimal point to represent values less than one. The digits to the right of the decimal point indicate fractional parts (tenths, hundredths, thousandths, etc.).

Decimal Place Value : The value assigned to a digit's position to the right of the decimal point. Each place is one-tenth the value of the place immediately to its left.

  • Tenths: The first digit to the right of the decimal point, representing $1/10$ or $0.1$.
  • Hundredths: The second digit to the right of the decimal point, representing $1/100$ or $0.01$.
  • Thousandths: The third digit to the right of the decimal point, representing $1/1000$ or $0.001$.

Annex Zeros : The process of writing extra zeros at the end of a decimal without changing its numerical value. This technique is often used to standardize the number of decimal places when comparing or performing operations on decimals. For example, $3.5 = 3.50 = 3.500$.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Decimal Place Value

Just like whole numbers, decimals operate on a place value system where each position is ten times the value of the position to its right. Decimals extend this concept to values less than one. A decimal point serves as the separator between the whole-number part and the fractional part of a number.

The place value names for digits to the right of the decimal point all end in "-ths":

  • Tenths: This is the first place to the right of the decimal point. It represents fractions with a denominator of 10. For example, $0.1$ is one-tenth.
  • Hundredths: This is the second place to the right of the decimal point. It represents fractions with a denominator of 100. For example, $0.01$ is one-hundredth.
  • Thousandths: This is the third place to the right of the decimal point. It represents fractions with a denominator of 1000. For example, $0.001$ is one-thousandth.

It's important to differentiate between whole-number places and decimal places. For instance, "tens" refers to a whole-number place, while "tenths" refers to a decimal place.

Identifying Decimal Place Value

To identify the place value of a specific digit in a decimal:

  1. Locate the decimal point.
  2. Move to the right, counting the positions from the decimal point.
    • First position: tenths
    • Second position: hundredths
    • Third position: thousandths

Example: In $12.583$, to find the place value of the digit 8:

  • $5$ is in the tenths place.
  • $8$ is in the hundredths place.
  • $3$ is in the thousandths place. Therefore, the place value of 8 is hundredths, representing $8/100$ or $0.08$.

Three Forms of a Decimal

Decimals can be expressed in three primary ways:

  1. Standard Form: This is the common numerical way to write a decimal.

    • Example: $4.037$

  2. Expanded Form: This shows the decimal as the sum of the values of its individual digits.

    • Example: For $4.037$, the expanded form is $4 + 0.0 + 0.03 + 0.007$. Notice that the digit 0 in the tenths place contributes a value of $0.0$.

  3. Word Form: This is how you would read the decimal aloud.

    • The word "and" is used to mark the decimal point, separating the whole-number part from the fractional part.
    • The fractional part is read as a whole number, and then the place value of its last digit is stated.
    • Example: For $4.037$, the word form is "four and thirty-seven thousandths."
    • Example: For $0.269$, the word form is "two hundred sixty-nine thousandths." (Since there is no whole-number part, you don't say "zero and.")

Checkpoint Example: To write $3.04$ in expanded form and word form:

  • Expanded form: $3 + 0.04$
  • Word form: three and four hundredths

Comparing Decimals

Comparing decimals allows us to determine which decimal is greater than, less than, or equal to another. Follow these steps:

  1. Line up the decimal points: This is crucial for comparing digits that have the same place value.
  2. Annex zeros: Add zeros to the end of the decimal with fewer digits to the right of the decimal point so that both numbers have the same number of decimal places. This ensures you are comparing equivalent parts. Remember, annexing zeros (e.g., $3.5 = 3.50$) does not change the value of the decimal.
  3. Compare digits from left to right: Starting from the leftmost digit (the largest place value), compare the digits in each corresponding place. The first pair of digits that are different will determine which decimal is larger.

Caution: Never compare digits that are in different place values. Always align decimal points and annex zeros first.

Examples of Comparing Decimals

Example 1: Compare $0.45$ and $0.437$.

  1. Line up decimal points: $0.45$ $0.437$
  2. Annex a zero to $0.45$ to make it $0.450$: $0.450$ $0.437$
  3. Compare from left to right:
    • Ones place: $0 = 0$ (same)
    • Tenths place: $4 = 4$ (same)
    • Hundredths place: $5 > 3$. Since $5 > 3$ in the hundredths place, $0.450$ is greater than $0.437$. Therefore, $0.45 > 0.437$.

Example 2: Comparing Measurements Two metal rods measure $2.75$ cm and $2.7$ cm. Which rod is longer?

  1. Identify the numbers to compare: $2.75$ and $2.7$.
  2. Annex a zero to $2.7$ to make it $2.70$.
  3. Compare $2.75$ and $2.70$ from left to right:
    • Ones place: $2 = 2$ (same)
    • Tenths place: $7 = 7$ (same)
    • Hundredths place: $5 > 0$. Since $5 > 0$ in the hundredths place, $2.75$ is greater than $2.70$. Therefore, the rod measuring $2.75$ cm is longer.

Checkpoint Example: Order from least to greatest: $0.6$, $0.59$, $0.605$.

  1. Line up decimal points and annex zeros to match the longest decimal (three decimal places): $0.600$ $0.590$ $0.605$
  2. Compare from left to right:
    • The whole number part is $0$ for all.
    • Comparing tenths: $0.590$ has $5$ in the tenths place, while $0.600$ and $0.605$ have $6$. So $0.590$ is the smallest.
    • Now compare $0.600$ and $0.605$.
      • Tenths: $6 = 6$
      • Hundredths: $0 = 0$
      • Thousandths: $0 < 5$. So $0.600$ is smaller than $0.605$.
  3. Order: $0.59 < 0.6 < 0.605$.
