Chapter 8, Section 2: Evaluating Expressions Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concept of evaluating algebraic expressions. You will learn how to find the numerical value of an expression by substituting specific numbers for its variables. The process emphasizes the critical role of the order of operations to ensure accurate calculations after substitution.

Key Concepts

  • Evaluating an Expression: This is the process of finding the numerical value of an algebraic expression. It involves replacing each variable in the expression with a given number and then simplifying the resulting arithmetic expression using the order of operations.
  • Substitution: The act of replacing a variable (a letter representing an unknown number) with a specific numerical value. Proper substitution, especially using parentheses, is crucial to avoid errors.
  • Order of Operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS): After substituting values for variables, you will have an arithmetic expression. The order of operations (Parentheses/Brackets, Exponents/Orders, Multiplication and Division (from left to right), Addition and Subtraction (from left to right)) must be strictly followed to arrive at the correct final value.
  • Using Parentheses for Substitution: When substituting a numerical value for a variable, particularly a negative number or when the variable is raised to a power, always enclose the substituted value in parentheses. This ensures that the entire value, including any negative signs, is operated on correctly. For example, $2(-3)^2$ means $2 \times (-3) \times (-3)$, which is $2 \times 9 = 18$, not $2 \times -3^2$, which would imply $2 \times -(3 \times 3) = 2 \times -9 = -18$.
  • Evaluating Formulas: Formulas are essentially algebraic expressions that represent relationships between different quantities (e.g., $A = lw$ for the area of a rectangle). Evaluating a formula means substituting known values for the variables in the formula to find the value of the unknown quantity.
  • Problem-Solving Steps: When evaluating expressions in real-world scenarios, it's helpful to follow a structured approach: Read the problem, Identify the known values for the variables, Write down the expression/formula, Solve by substituting and calculating, and finally, Check your answer.

Important Terms

  • Evaluate: To find the numerical value of an algebraic expression by replacing each variable with a given number and then simplifying the resulting arithmetic expression using the order of operations.
  • Algebraic Expression: A mathematical phrase that contains numbers, variables (letters), and operation symbols (like addition, subtraction, multiplication, division). It does not contain an equals sign.
  • Variable: A symbol, usually a letter (like $x$, $y$, $a$, $n$, $l$, $w$, $h$, $t$), that represents an unknown number or a quantity that can change.
  • Substitute: To replace a variable in an expression or formula with a specific numerical value.
  • Order of Operations: A set of rules that dictates the sequence in which mathematical operations should be performed to ensure a consistent and correct result. Commonly remembered by acronyms like PEMDAS (Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication and Division, Addition and Subtraction).

Detailed Notes

What is Evaluating an Expression?

Evaluating an expression is a core skill in algebra. It allows us to find a specific numerical answer for an expression when we know the values of its variables. This is like taking a recipe and, knowing the exact amount of each ingredient, calculating the final yield.

Steps to Evaluate an Expression

To ensure accuracy, follow these steps systematically:

  1. Write the original expression. This helps you keep track of the problem and avoids errors from prematurely changing the expression.
  2. Replace every variable with the given value. Crucially, use parentheses around the substituted value, especially if it's negative or if the variable is involved in an exponentiation.
  3. Simplify using the order of operations. Apply PEMDAS/BODMAS to compute the final numerical value.

Examples of Evaluating Expressions

1. Evaluating a Linear Expression Let's evaluate $3x + 5$ when $x = 4$.

  • Original expression: $3x + 5$
  • Substitute $x=4$: $3(4) + 5$
  • Simplify using order of operations (multiplication before addition): $12 + 5 = 17$ The value is $17$.

2. Evaluating with a Negative Value and Exponents Evaluate $2n^2 - 5$ when $n = -3$.

  • Original expression: $2n^2 - 5$
  • Substitute $n=-3$ (using parentheses): $2(-3)^2 - 5$
  • Simplify using order of operations (exponents before multiplication, then subtraction):
    • First, calculate the exponent: $(-3)^2 = (-3) \times (-3) = 9$. (Remember, $(-3)^2 = 9$, not $-9$. The parentheses indicate that the entire value of $-3$ is squared.)
    • Now the expression is: $2(9) - 5$
    • Next, multiplication: $18 - 5$
    • Finally, subtraction: $13$ The value is $13$.

Important Note on Parentheses: Always use parentheses when you substitute a negative number or when a variable that is being substituted for is raised to a power. This prevents ambiguity and ensures that the operation applies to the entire value. For instance, $2 -3^2$ could be misinterpreted, but $2(-3)^2$ clearly indicates that $-3$ is squared, not just $3$.

3. Evaluating a Formula Formulas are simply expressions that represent relationships. The area of a rectangle is given by $A = lw$, where $l$ is the length and $w$ is the width. Find the area when $l = 9$ and $w = 4$.

  • Original formula: $A = lw$
  • Substitute $l=9$ and $w=4$: $A = (9)(4)$
  • Simplify: $A = 36$ The area is $36$ square units.

4. Real-World Application: Earning Tips A pizza delivery driver earns $$8.50$ per hour plus tips. Her total pay for one shift is given by $8.50h + t$, where $h$ is the number of hours and $t$ is the total tips. Find her pay when she works $6$ hours and receives $$24.75$ in tips.

  • Step 1: Read. The problem asks for the total pay given hourly wage, hours worked, and tips.
  • Step 2: Identify. We are given $h = 6$ (hours) and $t = 24.75$ (total tips).
  • Step 3: Write. The expression for total pay is $8.50h + t$.
  • Step 4: Solve. Substitute the given values into the expression: $8.50(6) + 24.75$
    • Perform multiplication first: $51.00 + 24.75$
    • Then, perform addition: $75.75$
  • Step 5: Check. $6$ hours at $$8.50$ is $6 \times 8.50 = $51$. Adding $$24.75$ in tips gives $51 + 24.75 = $75.75$. The answer is consistent. The driver's total pay is $$75.75$.

Summary of Evaluating Expressions

  • Always substitute the given value for each variable.
  • Crucially, use parentheses around the substituted value. This is especially important for negative numbers or when the variable is part of an exponent.
  • Follow the order of operations strictly to simplify the expression to its final numerical value.
  • Remember that formulas are just specific types of algebraic expressions that you evaluate in the same way.

Review Questions

  1. In your own words, what does it mean to "evaluate an expression"? Why is this process important in algebra and in practical applications?
  2. Explain why it is critical to use parentheses when substituting a negative number into an expression, especially when that variable is being squared. Provide an example to illustrate the difference between correct and incorrect substitution.
  3. Describe the step-by-step process you would follow to evaluate the expression $3x^2 - 4y + 7$ when $x = -5$ and $y = 2$.
  4. The formula for the perimeter of a rectangle is $P = 2l + 2w$, where $P$ is the perimeter, $l$ is the length, and $w$ is the width. If a rectangular garden has a length of $15.5$ feet and a width of $8$ feet, what is its perimeter? Show your work.
  5. Why is the order of operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS) so fundamental to evaluating expressions correctly? What errors could arise if the order of operations is not strictly followed?
