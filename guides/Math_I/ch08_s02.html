Evaluating Expressions: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental process of evaluating algebraic expressions. To evaluate an expression means to find its numerical value by substituting given numbers for each variable and then performing the mathematical operations according to the order of operations. This skill is crucial for solving algebraic problems and applying mathematical formulas in real-world contexts.

Key Concepts

  • What is Evaluating an Expression? It is the process of finding the numerical value of an algebraic expression by replacing its variables with specific numerical values.
  • Substitution: The first step in evaluating is to replace each variable in the expression with its assigned number.
  • Order of Operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS): After substitution, you must follow the correct order of operations (Parentheses/Brackets, Exponents/Orders, Multiplication and Division (from left to right), Addition and Subtraction (from left to right)) to simplify the resulting numerical expression.
  • Importance of Parentheses: When substituting a numerical value, especially a negative number, it is critical to enclose it in parentheses. This ensures that operations like squaring (as in $(-3)^2$) apply to the entire substituted value, including its sign, preventing common errors.
  • Evaluating Formulas: Formulas are a type of algebraic expression that represent relationships between different quantities. The process of evaluating a formula is identical to evaluating any other algebraic expression – substitute the given values for the variables and simplify.

Important Terms

  • Evaluate: To find the numerical value of an algebraic expression by substituting specific numbers for the variables and then simplifying using the order of operations.
  • Algebraic Expression: A mathematical phrase that contains numbers, variables, and operation symbols (e.g., $3x + 5$, $A = lw$).
  • Variable: A symbol (usually a letter) used to represent an unknown numerical value in an algebraic expression.
  • Substitute: To replace a variable in an expression with a specific numerical value.
  • Order of Operations: The set of rules that dictate the sequence in which mathematical operations should be performed to ensure a consistent result.

Detailed Notes

The Process of Evaluating an Expression

Evaluating an algebraic expression is a straightforward process involving three main steps:

  1. Write the Original Expression: Always start by writing down the expression as it is given. This helps in tracking your work and reducing errors.
  2. Replace Variables with Given Values (Use Parentheses!): For every variable in the expression, substitute the numerical value provided. It is extremely important to use parentheses around the number you are substituting, especially when dealing with negative numbers or when the variable is part of an exponent or multiplication.
  3. Simplify Using the Order of Operations: Once all variables have been replaced with numbers, you will have a numerical expression. Use the order of operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS) to simplify this expression down to a single numerical value.

Examples of Evaluating Expressions

1. Evaluating a Linear Expression Let's evaluate $3x + 5$ when $x = 4$.

  • Step 1 (Write Original): $3x + 5$
  • Step 2 (Substitute): Replace $x$ with $4$. Using parentheses: $3(4) + 5$
  • Step 3 (Simplify): Follow order of operations.
    • Multiplication first: $3 \times 4 = 12$
    • Then addition: $12 + 5 = 17$ The value of the expression is $17$.

2. Evaluating with a Negative Value Consider the expression $2n^2 - 5$ when $n = -3$.

  • Step 1 (Write Original): $2n^2 - 5$
  • Step 2 (Substitute): Replace $n$ with $-3$. Crucially, use parentheses: $2(-3)^2 - 5$
  • Step 3 (Simplify):
    • Exponents first: $(-3)^2 = (-3) \times (-3) = 9$. (Note: If you wrote $2 -3^2 - 5$, it would mean $2 - (3^2) - 5 = 2 - 9 - 5$, which is different. Parentheses make it clear you are squaring the entire negative value.)
    • The expression becomes: $2(9) - 5$
    • Multiplication next: $2 \times 9 = 18$
    • The expression becomes: $18 - 5$
    • Subtraction last: $18 - 5 = 13$ The value of the expression is $13$.

Checkpoint Practice: Evaluate $4a - 7$ when $a = 5$.

  • Substitute: $4(5) - 7$
  • Multiply: $20 - 7$
  • Subtract: $13$

3. Evaluating a Formula Formulas are simply algebraic expressions that describe a specific relationship. The process of evaluation remains the same. Find the area of a rectangle given by $A = lw$ when length $l = 9$ and width $w = 4$.

  • Step 1 (Write Original): $A = lw$
  • Step 2 (Substitute): Replace $l$ with $9$ and $w$ with $4$: $A = (9)(4)$
  • Step 3 (Simplify): $A = 36$ The area is $36$ square units.

4. Real-World Application: Earning Tips A pizza delivery driver's total pay is given by $8.50h + t$, where $h$ is hours worked and $t$ is total tips. Find her pay when she works $6$ hours and receives $$24.75$ in tips.

  • Identify Values: $h = 6$, $t = 24.75$
  • Write Expression: $8.50h + t$
  • Substitute: $8.50(6) + 24.75$
  • Simplify:
    • Multiplication: $8.50 \times 6 = 51.00$
    • Addition: $51.00 + 24.75 = 75.75$ Her total pay is $$75.75$.

Checkpoint Practice: Evaluate $x + y^2$ when $x = 10$ and $y = 6$.

  • Substitute: $10 + (6)^2$
  • Exponents: $10 + 36$
  • Addition: $46$

Summary of Evaluating Expressions

To reiterate the key points for successful evaluation:

  • Substitute Carefully: Replace each variable with its given numerical value.
  • Use Parentheses: Always enclose substituted values, especially negative numbers or values that are being raised to a power, in parentheses. This avoids ambiguity and common errors. For instance, $2(-3)^2$ is correct, while $2 -3^2$ can be misinterpreted.
  • Follow Order of Operations: After substitution, strictly adhere to PEMDAS/BODMAS to simplify the resulting numerical expression.
  • Formulas are Expressions: The method for evaluating formulas is exactly the same as for any other algebraic expression.

  3. Why is it crucial to use parentheses when substituting a negative number into an expression, especially when the number is being squared or raised to another power? Provide an example to illustrate your answer.
