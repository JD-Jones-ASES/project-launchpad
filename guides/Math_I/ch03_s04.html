Study Guide: Comparing and Ordering Fractions

Overview

This section focuses on essential techniques for comparing and ordering fractions, especially when they have different denominators. You will learn two reliable methods—using a common denominator and cross multiplication—to accurately determine which fraction is larger, smaller, or if they are equal, allowing you to confidently arrange any set of fractions.

Key Concepts

  • Comparing Fractions with Common Denominators: To compare fractions effectively, the first step is to find their Least Common Denominator (LCD). This involves rewriting each fraction as an equivalent fraction that shares this LCD. Once the denominators are the same, the comparison becomes straightforward: the fraction with the larger numerator is the greater fraction.
  • Comparing Fractions by Cross Multiplication: This is a direct method for comparing two fractions, $\frac{a}{b}$ and $\frac{c}{d}$. You multiply the numerator of the first by the denominator of the second ($a \times d$) and the denominator of the first by the numerator of the second ($b \times c$). The comparison of these two products ($ad$ vs. $bc$) reveals the relationship between the original fractions.
  • Least Common Denominator (LCD): The LCD of a set of fractions is the Least Common Multiple (LCM) of their denominators. It is the smallest positive number that all of the original denominators can divide into evenly, serving as the common "base" for comparison.
  • Ordering Multiple Fractions: When tasked with ordering three or more fractions (e.g., from least to greatest), the most efficient approach is to convert all of them to equivalent fractions using their common LCD. This allows for a direct comparison of their numerators to establish the correct order.
  • Comparing Mixed Numbers: For mixed numbers, begin by comparing their whole number parts. If these parts are different, the mixed number with the larger whole number is the greater one. If the whole parts are identical, then you proceed to compare only their fractional parts, using either the common denominator or cross multiplication method.
  • Crucial Caution: It is a significant mistake to compare the numerators of fractions before they share a common denominator. This shortcut often leads to incorrect comparisons because the "parts" being compared are not of the same size. Always ensure a common denominator or use cross multiplication.

Important Terms

  • Common Denominator: A denominator that is shared by two or more fractions, allowing for direct comparison of their numerators.
  • Least Common Denominator (LCD): The smallest common denominator for a set of fractions; it is the Least Common Multiple (LCM) of their denominators.
  • Cross Multiplication: A method to compare two fractions ($\frac{a}{b}$ and $\frac{c}{d}$) by comparing the products $a \times d$ and $b \times c$.
  • Numerator: The top number in a fraction, representing the number of parts taken or considered from the whole.
  • Denominator: The bottom number in a fraction, indicating the total number of equal parts into which the whole is divided.
  • Least Common Multiple (LCM): The smallest positive integer that is a multiple of two or more given integers. Used to find the LCD.

Detailed Notes

The Challenge of Comparing Fractions

It's easy to compare numbers like $38$ and $512$ if they were whole numbers. However, when they're fractions like $\frac{3}{8}$ and $\frac{5}{12}$, it's much harder to tell which is larger at a glance because the denominators (the sizes of the parts) are different. This section provides two reliable strategies to overcome this challenge.

Method 1: Comparing Fractions with Common Denominators

This method provides a foundational understanding of how to compare fractions by making sure they are discussing the same "sized pieces."

Step-by-Step Process:

  1. Find the Least Common Denominator (LCD): This is the smallest number that all denominators can divide into evenly. It is found by calculating the Least Common Multiple (LCM) of the denominators.
  2. Rewrite Each Fraction: Convert each original fraction into an equivalent fraction that has the LCD as its new denominator. Remember to multiply both the numerator and the denominator by the same number to maintain the fraction's value.
  3. Compare the Numerators: Once all fractions share the same denominator, you can simply compare their numerators. The fraction with the larger numerator is the greater fraction.

Example: Comparing $\frac{3}{8}$ and $\frac{5}{12}$

  • Step 1: Find the LCD of $8$ and $12$. The LCM of $8$ and $12$ is $24$. So, LCD $= 24$.
  • Step 2: Rewrite fractions with the LCD.
    • For $\frac{3}{8}$: Since $8 \times 3 = 24$, we multiply the numerator by $3$: $\frac{3 \times 3}{8 \times 3} = \frac{9}{24}$.
    • For $\frac{5}{12}$: Since $12 \times 2 = 24$, we multiply the numerator by $2$: $\frac{5 \times 2}{12 \times 2} = \frac{10}{24}$.
  • Step 3: Compare numerators. We compare $\frac{9}{24}$ and $\frac{10}{24}$. Since $9 < 10$, it follows that $\frac{9}{24} < \frac{10}{24}$.
  • Conclusion: Therefore, $\frac{3}{8} < \frac{5}{12}$.

Method 2: Comparing Fractions by Cross Multiplication

This method offers a quicker way to compare two fractions without explicitly finding the LCD. It works by creating two products that indirectly tell you which fraction is larger.

Step-by-Step Process (for comparing $\frac{a}{b}$ and $\frac{c}{d}$ where $b, d > 0$):

  1. Calculate Cross Products: Multiply the numerator of the first fraction ($a$) by the denominator of the second fraction ($d$), resulting in $a \times d$. Then, multiply the denominator of the first fraction ($b$) by the numerator of the second fraction ($c$), resulting in $b \times c$.
  2. Compare the Products:
    • If $a \times d > b \times c$, then $\frac{a}{b} > \frac{c}{d}$.
    • If $a \times d < b \times c$, then $\frac{a}{b} < \frac{c}{d}$.
    • If $a \times d = b \times c$, then $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}$.

Example: Compare $\frac{4}{7}$ and $\frac{3}{5}$ using cross multiplication.

  • Step 1: Compute cross products.
    • $4 \times 5 = 20$
    • $7 \times 3 = 21$
  • Step 2: Compare the products. Since $20 < 21$.
  • Conclusion: Therefore, $\frac{4}{7} < \frac{3}{5}$.

Ordering Three or More Fractions

When you need to arrange multiple fractions, the common denominator method is generally the most straightforward way.

Example: Order $\frac{2}{3}$, $\frac{5}{9}$, and $\frac{7}{12}$ from least to greatest.

  • Step 1: Find the LCD of $3, 9,$ and $12$. The LCM of $3, 9,$ and $12$ is $36$. So, LCD $= 36$.
  • Step 2: Rewrite each fraction with the LCD of $36$.
    • $\frac{2}{3} = \frac{2 \times 12}{3 \times 12} = \frac{24}{36}$
    • $\frac{5}{9} = \frac{5 \times 4}{9 \times 4} = \frac{20}{36}$
    • $\frac{7}{12} = \frac{7 \times 3}{12 \times 3} = \frac{21}{36}$
  • Step 3: Order the numerators. The numerators are $24, 20, 21$. Ordering them: $20 < 21 < 24$.
  • Conclusion: From least to greatest, the fractions are: $\frac{5}{9} < \frac{7}{12} < \frac{2}{3}$.

Comparing Mixed Numbers

When dealing with mixed numbers, you first compare the whole number parts.

Steps:

  1. Compare Whole Parts: If the whole numbers are different, the mixed number with the larger whole number is greater.
  2. Compare Fraction Parts: If the whole numbers are the same, then you must compare their fractional parts using either the common denominator method or cross multiplication.

Example: Compare $3\frac{2}{5}$ and $3\frac{3}{8}$

  • Step 1: Compare whole parts. Both mixed numbers have a whole part of $3$. Since they are equal, we proceed to compare the fractional parts.
  • Step 2: Compare fraction parts ($\frac{2}{5}$ and $\frac{3}{8}$).
    • Find the LCD of $5$ and $8$. The LCD is $40$.
    • Rewrite fractions: $\frac{2}{5} = \frac{2 \times 8}{5 \times 8} = \frac{16}{40}$
    • Rewrite fractions: $\frac{3}{8} = \frac{3 \times 5}{8 \times 5} = \frac{15}{40}$
    • Compare numerators: Since $16 > 15$, we have $\frac{16}{40} > \frac{15}{40}$.
  • Conclusion: Therefore, $3\frac{2}{5} > 3\frac{3}{8}$.

Real-World Application: Ribbon Lengths

Here's how these methods are applied in practical scenarios.

Scenario: Sara has $\frac{5}{6}$ yard of ribbon. Tom has $\frac{7}{9}$ yard of ribbon. Who has more ribbon?

Solution (using cross multiplication):

  1. Read: The goal is to compare $\frac{5}{6}$ and $\frac{7}{9}$.
  2. Identify: Cross multiplication is a suitable method for comparing two fractions.
  3. Write: Set up the cross products: $5 \times 9$ and $6 \times 7$.
  4. Solve:
    • $5 \times 9 = 45$
    • $6 \times 7 = 42$
    • Since $45 > 42$, it means $\frac{5}{6} > \frac{7}{9}$.
  5. Check (optional, using common denominators): The LCD of $6$ and $9$ is $18$. $\frac{5}{6} = \frac{15}{18}$ and $\frac{7}{9} = \frac{14}{18}$. Since $15 > 14$, the comparison is correct.
  • Answer: Sara has more ribbon.

Common Mistake to Avoid

A frequent error is to compare fractions with different denominators simply by looking at their numerators. For instance, assuming $\frac{3}{8}$ is greater than $\frac{2}{5}$ just because $3 > 2$ is incorrect. Always remember that fractions must refer to parts of the same size (i.e., have a common denominator) or be compared using cross multiplication to get an accurate result.

Review Questions

  1. Explain why directly comparing numerators of fractions like $\frac{4}{9}$ and $\frac{3}{7}$ is unreliable. Describe, step-by-step, how you would use the common denominator method to compare these two fractions.
  2. Compare $\frac{7}{10}$ and $\frac{9}{13}$ using the cross multiplication method. Clearly show the products you calculate and state your conclusion.
  3. You have three different lengths of fabric: $\frac{5}{8}$ yard, $\frac{2}{3}$ yard, and $\frac{13}{24}$ yard. Order these lengths from shortest to longest.
  4. A recipe calls for $1 \frac{3}{4}$ cups of sugar, while another variation suggests $1 \frac{5}{6}$ cups. Which version of the recipe uses more sugar? Show how you determined your answer.
  5. When is it more advantageous to use the common denominator method versus cross multiplication when comparing fractions? Are there situations where one method is impossible or significantly harder than the other?
