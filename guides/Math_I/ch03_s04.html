Comparing and Ordering Fractions: A Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces essential methods for comparing and ordering fractions, particularly when they have different denominators. It covers two primary techniques: rewriting fractions with a common denominator and using cross multiplication. Mastering these methods ensures you can accurately determine which fraction is larger, smaller, or if they are equal, even for complex cases involving multiple fractions or mixed numbers.

Key Concepts

  • Comparing Fractions with Common Denominators: To compare two fractions, first find their Least Common Denominator (LCD). Then, rewrite each fraction as an equivalent fraction with the LCD. Finally, compare the numerators—the fraction with the larger numerator is the larger fraction. This method is especially useful for ordering three or more fractions.
  • Comparing Fractions by Cross Multiplication: This method is efficient for comparing two fractions, say $a/b$ and $c/d$ (where $b, d > 0$). You multiply the numerator of the first fraction by the denominator of the second ($a \times d$) and compare it to the product of the denominator of the first fraction and the numerator of the second ($b \times c$).
    • If $a \times d > b \times c$, then $a/b > c/d$.
    • If $a \times d < b \times c$, then $a/b < c/d$.
    • If $a \times d = b \times c$, then $a/b = c/d$.
  • Least Common Denominator (LCD): The LCD of two or more fractions is the Least Common Multiple (LCM) of their denominators. Using the LCD helps ensure you are comparing fractions with the smallest possible common denominator, making calculations simpler.
  • Ordering Multiple Fractions: When ordering three or more fractions, the most reliable method is to find the LCD for all of them, rewrite each fraction with that LCD, and then order them based on their numerators.
  • Comparing Mixed Numbers: If mixed numbers have different whole number parts, the one with the larger whole number part is greater. If their whole number parts are the same, you only need to compare their fractional parts using either the common denominator method or cross multiplication.
  • Common Mistake: A frequent error is comparing numerators of fractions without first finding a common denominator. For example, assuming $3/8 > 2/5$ just because $3 > 2$ is incorrect. Always use a proper comparison method.

Important Terms

  • Least Common Denominator (LCD): The smallest positive common multiple of the denominators of two or more fractions. It is used to create equivalent fractions that can be easily compared.
  • Least Common Multiple (LCM): The smallest positive integer that is a multiple of two or more integers. The LCD is the LCM of the denominators.
  • Common Denominator: A common multiple of the denominators of two or more fractions. While any common denominator works for comparison, the LCD is usually preferred for simplicity.
  • Cross Multiplication: A method used to compare two fractions or solve proportions by multiplying the numerator of one fraction by the denominator of the other.
  • Numerator: The top number in a fraction, representing the number of parts being considered out of the whole.
  • Denominator: The bottom number in a fraction, representing the total number of equal parts into which the whole is divided.

Detailed Notes

The Challenge of Comparing Fractions

When fractions have different denominators, it's impossible to compare them directly by just looking at their numerators or denominators. For example, comparing $3/8$ and $5/12$ by sight is difficult because the "pieces" (eighths vs. twelfths) are of different sizes. To make an accurate comparison, we need a way to relate them to a common reference point.

Method 1: Using Common Denominators

This is a fundamental method for comparing and ordering fractions.

  1. Find the Least Common Denominator (LCD): The LCD is the smallest number that all denominators divide into evenly. This is the same as finding the Least Common Multiple (LCM) of the denominators. For example, to compare $3/8$ and $5/12$, the LCM of 8 and 12 is 24, so the LCD is 24.
  2. Rewrite Each Fraction with the LCD: Convert each original fraction into an equivalent fraction that has the LCD as its new denominator. To do this, multiply both the numerator and the denominator by the same factor that makes the original denominator equal to the LCD.
    • For $3/8$: Since $8 \times 3 = 24$, multiply the numerator by 3: $3 \times 3 = 9$. So, $3/8 = 9/24$.
    • For $5/12$: Since $12 \times 2 = 24$, multiply the numerator by 2: $5 \times 2 = 10$. So, $5/12 = 10/24$.
  3. Compare the Numerators: Once the fractions have the same denominator, you can directly compare their numerators. The fraction with the larger numerator is the larger fraction.
    • Comparing $9/24$ and $10/24$: Since $9 < 10$, it follows that $9/24 < 10/24$.
    • Therefore, $3/8 < 5/12$.

Ordering Multiple Fractions

The common denominator method is ideal for ordering three or more fractions. You simply find the LCD for all the fractions, convert them all to equivalent fractions with that LCD, and then arrange them based on the size of their numerators.

  • Example: Order $2/3$, $5/9$, and $7/12$.
    • LCD(3, 9, 12) = 36.
    • $2/3 = (2 \times 12) / (3 \times 12) = 24/36$.
    • $5/9 = (5 \times 4) / (9 \times 4) = 20/36$.
    • $7/12 = (7 \times 3) / (12 \times 3) = 21/36$.
    • Ordering numerators: $20 < 21 < 24$.
    • Therefore, the order from least to greatest is $5/9 < 7/12 < 2/3$.

Comparing Mixed Numbers

When comparing mixed numbers, first look at their whole number parts:

  • If the whole number parts are different, the mixed number with the larger whole number part is greater. For example, $4 \ 1/2 > 3 \ 7/8$.
  • If the whole number parts are the same, you only need to compare their fractional parts.
    • Example: Compare $3 \ 2/5$ and $3 \ 3/8$.
      • The whole parts are both 3, so we compare $2/5$ and $3/8$.
      • LCD(5, 8) = 40.
      • $2/5 = (2 \times 8) / (5 \times 8) = 16/40$.
      • $3/8 = (3 \times 5) / (8 \times 5) = 15/40$.
      • Since $16 > 15$, we have $16/40 > 15/40$, which means $2/5 > 3/8$.
      • Therefore, $3 \ 2/5 > 3 \ 3/8$.

Method 2: Cross Multiplication

This method is a quick way to compare two fractions without explicitly finding the LCD.

  1. Multiply Diagonally: For fractions $a/b$ and $c/d$:
    • Multiply the numerator of the first fraction by the denominator of the second: $a \times d$.
    • Multiply the denominator of the first fraction by the numerator of the second: $b \times c$.
  2. Compare the Products:
    • If $a \times d > b \times c$, then $a/b > c/d$.
    • If $a \times d < b \times c$, then $a/b < c/d$.
    • If $a \times d = b \times c$, then $a/b = c/d$.

  • Example: Compare $4/7$ and $3/5$.

    • Cross multiply: $4 \times 5 = 20$ (from $4/7$) and $7 \times 3 = 21$ (from $3/5$).
    • Compare the products: $20 < 21$.
    • Therefore, $4/7 < 3/5$.

  • Example (Word Problem): Sara has $5/6$ yard, Tom has $7/9$ yard. Who has more?

    • Compare $5/6$ and $7/9$.
    • Cross multiply: $5 \times 9 = 45$ (for Sara) and $6 \times 7 = 42$ (for Tom).
    • Since $45 > 42$, $5/6 > 7/9$.
    • Sara has more ribbon.

Important Considerations

  • Choosing a Method: For comparing just two fractions, cross multiplication can be faster as it avoids finding the LCD. However, when ordering three or more fractions, the common denominator method is usually clearer and less prone to errors.
  • Avoiding Common Mistakes: The most crucial point is never to compare fractions with different denominators just by looking at their numerators (or denominators). Always transform them into a comparable form using one of the methods described.
