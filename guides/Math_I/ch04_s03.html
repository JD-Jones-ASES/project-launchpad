Dividing Fractions: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Overview

This section explains the fundamental concept of dividing fractions, establishing that division by a fraction is equivalent to multiplication by its reciprocal. It introduces the definition of a reciprocal and outlines the "keep, change, flip" method as a practical strategy for performing fraction division, covering scenarios involving fractions, whole numbers, and real-world applications.

Key Concepts

  • Reciprocal (Multiplicative Inverse): The reciprocal of a fraction $a/b$ is $b/a$ (where $a \neq 0$ and $b \neq 0$). When a number is multiplied by its reciprocal, the product is always $1$. Whole numbers can also have reciprocals by first writing them as fractions (e.g., the reciprocal of $5$ is $1/5$). Zero does not have a reciprocal.
  • Dividing Fractions Rule: To divide one fraction by another, you "keep" the first fraction, "change" the division operation to multiplication, and "flip" (take the reciprocal of) the second fraction. This rule is formally stated as $a/b \div c/d = a/b \times d/c$.
  • Handling Whole Numbers: When a division problem involves a whole number, it should first be written as a fraction over $1$ (e.g., $4 = 4/1$) before applying the division rule.
  • Cross-Cancellation: After changing division to multiplication and finding the reciprocal, always look for opportunities to cross-cancel common factors between numerators and denominators to simplify calculations before multiplying.

Important Terms

  • Reciprocal: (Also called multiplicative inverse) For a fraction $a/b$, its reciprocal is $b/a$. The product of a number and its reciprocal is always $1$.
  • Multiplicative Inverse: Another term for reciprocal.

Detailed Notes

The Fundamental Principle of Dividing Fractions

The core idea behind dividing fractions is simple yet powerful: dividing by a number is the same as multiplying by its reciprocal. This principle simplifies what might seem like a complex operation into a familiar one (multiplication).

Understanding the Reciprocal

Before we can divide fractions, we must understand what a reciprocal is.

  • Definition: The reciprocal (or multiplicative inverse) of a fraction $a/b$ is $b/a$. Essentially, you "flip" the fraction. This applies as long as $a \neq 0$ and $b \neq 0$.
  • Examples:
    • The reciprocal of $2/3$ is $3/2$.
    • The reciprocal of $7/4$ is $4/7$.
    • To find the reciprocal of a whole number, first write it as a fraction over $1$. For example, $5 = 5/1$, so its reciprocal is $1/5$.
  • Property: A number multiplied by its reciprocal always equals $1$. For instance, $(2/3) \times (3/2) = (2 \times 3) / (3 \times 2) = 6/6 = 1$.
  • Important Caution: Zero does not have a reciprocal. This is because you cannot flip $0/1$ to get $1/0$, as division by zero is undefined.

The Rule for Dividing Fractions: "Keep, Change, Flip"

The property for dividing fractions can be remembered using the popular mnemonic "Keep, Change, Flip" (KCF).

Property: Dividing Fractions For any fractions $a/b$ and $c/d$ (where $b \neq 0$, $c \neq 0$, and $d \neq 0$): $a/b \div c/d = a/b \times d/c$

Steps for Dividing Fractions:

  1. Keep the first fraction exactly as it is.
  2. Change the division sign ($\div$) to a multiplication sign ($\times$).
  3. Flip the second fraction (find its reciprocal).
  4. Multiply the resulting fractions: multiply the numerators together and the denominators together.
  5. Simplify your answer, if possible, by reducing to lowest terms. It's often helpful to cross-cancel common factors before multiplying to make the numbers smaller and simpler to work with.

Examples of Dividing Fractions

1. Fraction Divided by a Fraction

Problem: Divide $3/5 \div 2/7$

Solution:

  1. Keep the first fraction: $3/5$
  2. Change division to multiplication: $\times$
  3. Flip the second fraction ($2/7$ becomes $7/2$): $3/5 \times 7/2$
  4. Multiply: $(3 \times 7) / (5 \times 2) = 21/10$
  5. Simplify (convert to mixed number if preferred): $21/10 = 2 \text{ and } 1/10$

2. Whole Number Divided by a Fraction

Problem: Divide $4 \div 2/3$

Solution:

  1. Write the whole number as a fraction: $4 = 4/1$. The problem becomes: $4/1 \div 2/3$
  2. Keep the first fraction: $4/1$
  3. Change division to multiplication: $\times$
  4. Flip the second fraction ($2/3$ becomes $3/2$): $4/1 \times 3/2$
  5. Cross-cancel (4 and 2 share a common factor of 2): $(^2\cancel{4}/1) \times (3/\cancel{2}_1)$
  6. Multiply: $(2 \times 3) / (1 \times 1) = 6/1 = 6$

3. Fraction Divided by a Whole Number

Problem: Divide $5/8 \div 3$

Solution:

  1. Write the whole number as a fraction: $3 = 3/1$. The problem becomes: $5/8 \div 3/1$
  2. Keep the first fraction: $5/8$
  3. Change division to multiplication: $\times$
  4. Flip the second fraction ($3/1$ becomes $1/3$): $5/8 \times 1/3$
  5. Multiply: $(5 \times 1) / (8 \times 3) = 5/24$

Caution: Remember to always flip the second fraction (the divisor), not the first fraction (the dividend).

Real-World Applications

Dividing fractions is useful for solving problems where you need to split a total quantity into smaller, equal-sized fractional parts.

Example: Cutting Rope A piece of rope is $3/4$ meter long. It is cut into pieces that are each $1/8$ meter long. How many pieces can be cut?

Solution Strategy: To find out how many smaller pieces fit into a larger piece, you divide the total length by the length of each small piece.

  1. Set up the division: $3/4 \div 1/8$
  2. Apply "Keep, Change, Flip": $3/4 \times 8/1$
  3. Cross-cancel (4 and 8 share a common factor of 4): $(3/\cancel{4}_1) \times (^\cancel{8}_2/1)$
  4. Multiply: $(3 \times 2) / (1 \times 1) = 6/1 = 6$

Answer: 6 pieces of rope can be cut. Check: If you have 6 pieces each $1/8$ meter long, their total length is $6 \times 1/8 = 6/8 = 3/4$ meter, which matches the original length.
