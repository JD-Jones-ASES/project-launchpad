Study Guide: Dividing Fractions

Overview

This section explains how to divide fractions by introducing the concept of reciprocals. The core principle is that dividing by a fraction is equivalent to multiplying by its reciprocal, a rule often remembered as "keep, change, flip." We'll cover various scenarios, including dividing a fraction by another fraction, a whole number by a fraction, and a fraction by a whole number, along with practical applications.

Key Concepts

  • Reciprocal (Multiplicative Inverse): The reciprocal of a fraction $a/b$ is $b/a$ (where $a \neq 0$ and $b \neq 0$). Multiplying a number by its reciprocal always results in 1. Zero does not have a reciprocal.
  • Dividing Fractions Rule: To divide one fraction by another, you "keep" the first fraction, "change" the division operation to multiplication, and "flip" the second fraction (use its reciprocal). Symbolically: $a/b \div c/d = a/b \times d/c$.
  • Whole Numbers as Fractions: When performing operations with whole numbers, it's helpful to write them as fractions by placing them over 1 (e.g., $5 = 5/1$).
  • Cross Canceling: This technique simplifies calculations by dividing out common factors from a numerator and a denominator before multiplying, making the final multiplication easier and often preventing the need for later simplification. It's applied after the division has been converted to multiplication.

Important Terms

  • Reciprocal (Multiplicative Inverse): The number which, when multiplied by a given number, results in 1. For a fraction $a/b$, its reciprocal is $b/a$.
  • Keep, Change, Flip: A mnemonic (memory aid) used to remember the rule for dividing fractions: keep the first fraction, change the division sign to a multiplication sign, and flip the second fraction (take its reciprocal).
  • Cross Canceling: A simplification strategy used in multiplication of fractions where a numerator from one fraction and a denominator from another fraction (or the same fraction) share a common factor, which can be divided out before multiplying.

Detailed Notes

The Foundation: Reciprocals

The central idea behind dividing fractions is understanding and using reciprocals.

  • What is a Reciprocal? The reciprocal of a fraction $a/b$ is $b/a$. Essentially, you "flip" the fraction.
    • Example: The reciprocal of $2/3$ is $3/2$.
    • Example: The reciprocal of $7/4$ is $4/7$.
    • Example: For a whole number like $5$, first write it as a fraction $5/1$. Its reciprocal is $1/5$.
  • The Multiplicative Inverse Property: A number multiplied by its reciprocal always equals $1$. For instance, $(a/b) \times (b/a) = 1$. This property is why reciprocals are so useful in division.
  • Important Caution: Zero does not have a reciprocal. You cannot flip $0/1$ to get $1/0$ because division by zero is undefined.

The Rule for Dividing Fractions: "Keep, Change, Flip"

To divide any fraction $a/b$ by another fraction $c/d$ (where $b, c, d \neq 0$), follow these steps:

  1. Keep the first fraction as it is ($a/b$).
  2. Change the division sign ($\div$) to a multiplication sign ($\times$).
  3. Flip the second fraction (take its reciprocal, $d/c$). The rule is expressed as: $a/b \div c/d = a/b \times d/c$.

Examples in Detail

1. Fraction Divided by a Fraction

Problem: Divide $3/5 \div 2/7$

Solution:

  1. Keep $3/5$.
  2. Change $\div$ to $\times$.
  3. Flip $2/7$ to $7/2$. The expression becomes: $3/5 \times 7/2$
  4. Multiply the numerators and the denominators: $(3 \times 7) / (5 \times 2) = 21/10$
  5. Convert the improper fraction to a mixed number if required: $21/10 = 2 \frac{1}{10}$.

2. Whole Number Divided by a Fraction

Problem: Divide $4 \div 2/3$

Solution:

  1. Write the whole number $4$ as a fraction: $4/1$.
  2. Keep $4/1$.
  3. Change $\div$ to $\times$.
  4. Flip $2/3$ to $3/2$. The expression becomes: $4/1 \times 3/2$
  5. Cross cancel: Notice that $4$ (numerator) and $2$ (denominator) share a common factor of $2$. Divide $4$ by $2$ to get $2$. Divide $2$ by $2$ to get $1$. The expression is now: $2/1 \times 3/1$
  6. Multiply: $(2 \times 3) / (1 \times 1) = 6/1 = 6$.

3. Fraction Divided by a Whole Number

Problem: Divide $5/8 \div 3$

Solution:

  1. Write the whole number $3$ as a fraction: $3/1$.
  2. Keep $5/8$.
  3. Change $\div$ to $\times$.
  4. Flip $3/1$ to $1/3$. The expression becomes: $5/8 \times 1/3$
  5. Multiply: $(5 \times 1) / (8 \times 3) = 5/24$.
  • Important Caution: Remember to flip the second fraction (the divisor), even if it's a whole number. Do not flip the first fraction.

Real-World Applications (Word Problems)

Example: Cutting Rope A piece of rope is $3/4$ meter long. It is cut into pieces that are each $1/8$ meter long. How many pieces can be cut?

Steps:

  1. Read: We need to find out how many times $1/8$ meter fits into $3/4$ meter. This indicates division.
  2. Identify: Divide the total length of the rope by the length of each small piece.
  3. Write: $3/4 \div 1/8$
  4. Solve:
    • Keep $3/4$.
    • Change $\div$ to $\times$.
    • Flip $1/8$ to $8/1$.
    • The expression is $3/4 \times 8/1$.
    • Cross cancel: $4$ and $8$ have a common factor of $4$. $4 \div 4 = 1$, $8 \div 4 = 2$.
    • The expression becomes $3/1 \times 2/1$.
    • Multiply: $(3 \times 2) / (1 \times 1) = 6/1 = 6$.
  5. Check: If you have $6$ pieces, and each is $1/8$ m long, then $6 \times 1/8 = 6/8 = 3/4$ m. This matches the original length. Answer: 6 pieces can be cut.

Checkpoint Example: Cutting a Board A board is $5/6$ foot long. It is cut into pieces that are each $1/3$ foot long. How many pieces can be cut?

Solution:

  1. Set up the division: $5/6 \div 1/3$.
  2. Apply "keep, change, flip": $5/6 \times 3/1$.
  3. Cross cancel: $6$ and $3$ share a common factor of $3$. $3 \div 3 = 1$, $6 \div 3 = 2$.
  4. The expression becomes $5/2 \times 1/1$.
  5. Multiply: $(5 \times 1) / (2 \times 1) = 5/2$.
  6. Convert to a mixed number: $5/2 = 2 \frac{1}{2}$. Answer Interpretation: You can cut $2$ full pieces, with $1/2$ of a $1/3$-foot piece remaining. So, $2$ full pieces.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words why dividing by a fraction is the same as multiplying by its reciprocal. Provide an example.
  2. What is the reciprocal of $9/5$? What about the reciprocal of $12$? Why does zero not have a reciprocal?
  3. Calculate the following divisions, showing all steps, including cross-canceling where possible: a) $7/10 \div 14/5$ b) $9 \div 3/4$ c) $2/3 \div 6$
  4. A baker has $15/2$ cups of flour. If each cake recipe requires $3/4$ cup of flour, how many cakes can the baker make?
  5. A student correctly solved $5/8 \div 3$ by writing $5/8 \times 1/3$. Another student incorrectly tried to solve $5/8 \div 3$ by writing $8/5 \times 3/1$. What was the second student's mistake, and why is it important to follow the "keep, change, flip" rule precisely?
