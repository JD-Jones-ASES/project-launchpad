Study Guide: Converting Between Fractions and Decimals

Overview

This section explores the fundamental relationship between fractions and decimals, demonstrating that they are simply different ways to represent the same numerical values. You will learn the methods for converting fractions into decimals, distinguishing between terminating and repeating decimals, and converting decimals back into fractions, always simplifying to the lowest terms.

Key Concepts

  • Fractions and Decimals Represent the Same Values: Any value that can be expressed as a fraction can also be expressed as a decimal, and vice-versa, for terminating and repeating decimals.
  • Converting a Fraction to a Decimal: To change a fraction to its decimal form, perform division: divide the numerator by the denominator.
  • Terminating Decimals: These are decimals that have a finite number of digits after the decimal point, meaning the division process ends with a remainder of zero.
  • Repeating Decimals: These are decimals where one or more digits after the decimal point repeat endlessly. A bar notation is used above the repeating digit(s) to indicate this.
  • Converting a Terminating Decimal to a Fraction: This involves using the decimal's place value to determine the denominator (a power of 10) and then simplifying the resulting fraction to its lowest terms.
  • Rational Numbers: Every fraction, and consequently every terminating or repeating decimal, represents a rational number, which means it can be written as a ratio of two integers.

Important Terms

  • Terminating Decimal: A decimal that ends; it has a finite number of digits after the decimal point.
    • Example: $3/8 = 0.375$
  • Repeating Decimal: A decimal in which one or more digits repeat endlessly after the decimal point. A bar is placed over the repeating digits to denote this.
    • Example: $1/3 = 0.333... = 0.\overline{3}$
  • Numerator: The top number in a fraction, representing the number of parts of the whole.
  • Denominator: The bottom number in a fraction, representing the total number of equal parts that make up the whole.
  • Place Value: The value of a digit based on its position in a number (e.g., in 0.45, the 4 is in the tenths place, and the 5 is in the hundredths place).
  • Lowest Terms (Simplify): A fraction is in lowest terms when its numerator and denominator have no common factors other than 1.

Detailed Notes

The Relationship Between Fractions and Decimals

Fractions and decimals are two different notations used to express parts of a whole or ratios. They are interchangeable for rational numbers. Understanding how to convert between them is crucial for solving problems in various contexts, from cooking to advanced mathematics.

Converting a Fraction to a Decimal

The most straightforward way to convert any fraction ($\frac{a}{b}$) to a decimal is by performing division: divide the numerator ($a$) by the denominator ($b$). $$ \frac{a}{b} = a \div b $$ The result of this division will be one of two types of decimals:

1. Terminating Decimals

A terminating decimal is a decimal that ends. This occurs when the division process results in a remainder of zero.

  • Process: Divide the numerator by the denominator. If the division eventually yields a remainder of 0, the decimal terminates.
  • Example: Convert $\frac{3}{8}$ to a decimal.
      0.375
8 | 3.000
    -2 4
    ----
      60
     -56
     ----
       40
      -40
      ----
        0
    Since the division ends with a remainder of 0, $\frac{3}{8} = 0.375$.

2. Repeating Decimals

A repeating decimal is a decimal in which one or more digits repeat infinitely. This happens when the division process never yields a remainder of zero, and a pattern of remainders (and thus digits in the quotient) repeats.

  • Process: Divide the numerator by the denominator. If a remainder repeats, the corresponding digit(s) in the quotient will also repeat.
  • Notation: A bar is placed over the digit(s) that repeat.
  • Example: Convert $\frac{1}{3}$ to a decimal.
      0.333...
3 | 1.000
    -9
    ----
     10
     -9
     ----
      10
      -9
      ----
       1
    The digit '3' repeats endlessly. So, $\frac{1}{3} = 0.333... = 0.\overline{3}$.
  • Common Repeating Decimals to Memorize:
    • $\frac{1}{3} = 0.\overline{3}$
    • $\frac{2}{3} = 0.\overline{6}$
    • $\frac{1}{6} = 0.1\overline{6}$
    • $\frac{1}{9} = 0.\overline{1}$
    • $\frac{1}{11} = 0.\overline{09}$

Converting a Terminating Decimal to a Fraction

To convert a terminating decimal back into a fraction, follow these steps:

  1. Read the Decimal using Place Value: This step is crucial as it tells you the denominator of your initial fraction. For example, "0.45" is read as "forty-five hundredths," which immediately suggests a denominator of 100.
  2. Write the Fraction: Write the digits after the decimal point as the numerator, and the place value determined in step 1 as the denominator (which will be a power of 10 like 10, 100, 1000, etc.).
  3. Simplify to Lowest Terms: Find the greatest common factor (GCF) of the numerator and the denominator, and divide both by it to reduce the fraction to its simplest form.

  • Example: Write $0.45$ as a fraction in lowest terms.

    1. Read $0.45$ as "forty-five hundredths."
    2. Write as a fraction: $\frac{45}{100}$.
    3. Simplify: The GCF of 45 and 100 is 5. $$ \frac{45 \div 5}{100 \div 5} = \frac{9}{20} $$ Therefore, $0.45 = \frac{9}{20}$.

  • Checkpoint Example: Write $0.6$ as a fraction in lowest terms.

    1. Read $0.6$ as "six tenths."
    2. Write as a fraction: $\frac{6}{10}$.
    3. Simplify: The GCF of 6 and 10 is 2. $$ \frac{6 \div 2}{10 \div 2} = \frac{3}{5} $$ So, $0.6 = \frac{3}{5}$.

Real-World Application

Converting between fractions and decimals is a practical skill. For example, a recipe might call for a fractional amount of an ingredient, but your measuring tools might be marked in decimals, or vice-versa.

  • Example: A recipe calls for $\frac{2}{5}$ cup of sugar. Your measuring cup is marked in decimals. How much sugar do you measure?
    1. Goal: Convert $\frac{2}{5}$ to a decimal.
    2. Method: Divide the numerator by the denominator.
    3. Calculation: $2 \div 5 = 0.4$.
    4. Answer: You would measure $0.4$ cup of sugar.
    5. Check: Convert $0.4$ back to a fraction: $0.4 = \frac{4}{10} = \frac{2}{5}$. The conversion is correct.

Important Considerations

  • While every terminating or repeating decimal can be written as a "nice" fraction (a rational number), not all decimals are rational. Non-terminating, non-repeating decimals (like $\pi \approx 3.14159...$ or $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.41421...$) are called irrational numbers and cannot be expressed as a simple fraction of two integers. This section primarily deals with rational numbers.
