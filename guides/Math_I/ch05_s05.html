Converting Between Fractions and Decimals: A Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the fundamental relationship between fractions and decimals, demonstrating that they are simply different ways to represent the same numerical values. You will learn the systematic methods for converting fractions into decimals (which can be either terminating or repeating) and for converting decimals back into simplified fractions. Mastering these conversions is crucial for understanding rational numbers and their applications in various contexts.

Key Concepts

  • Equivalence of Fractions and Decimals: Fractions and decimals are two different notations that can represent the exact same quantity or value.
  • Terminating Decimals: These are decimals that have a finite number of digits after the decimal point, meaning their division process ends with a remainder of zero.
  • Repeating Decimals: These are decimals where one or more digits after the decimal point repeat endlessly in a pattern. A bar notation is used to indicate the repeating digits.
  • Converting Fractions to Decimals: To transform a fraction into its decimal equivalent, you perform division: the numerator is divided by the denominator. The result will be either a terminating or a repeating decimal.
  • Converting Decimals to Fractions: To convert a terminating decimal into a fraction, you use its place value to determine the denominator (a power of 10). The resulting fraction is then simplified to its lowest terms.
  • Rational Numbers: Every fraction (and therefore every terminating or repeating decimal) represents a rational number, meaning it can be expressed as a ratio of two integers.

Important Terms

  • Terminating Decimal: A decimal that has a finite number of digits after the decimal point. The division process ends with a zero remainder.
    • Example: $3/8 = 0.375$
  • Repeating Decimal: A decimal in which one or more digits repeat endlessly after the decimal point. A bar ($\bar{\phantom{0}}$) is placed over the repeating digit(s).
    • Example: $1/3 = 0.333... = 0.\bar{3}$
  • Numerator: The top number in a fraction, representing the number of parts being considered.
  • Denominator: The bottom number in a fraction, indicating the total number of equal parts into which the whole is divided.
  • Lowest Terms (Simplifying a Fraction): A fraction is in lowest terms when its numerator and denominator have no common factors other than 1. This often involves dividing both by their Greatest Common Factor (GCF).
  • Place Value: The value of a digit based on its position in a number (e.g., in 0.45, the 4 is in the tenths place, and the 5 is in the hundredths place).

Detailed Notes

Understanding Fractions and Decimals

Fractions and decimals are two fundamental ways to express parts of a whole or ratios. This section clarifies how to move between these two representations. Critically, every fraction can be written as either a terminating decimal or a repeating decimal, confirming that they are all rational numbers.

Converting a Fraction to a Decimal

The core method for converting any fraction ($a/b$) to a decimal is straightforward: divide the numerator ($a$) by the denominator ($b$). $$ \frac{a}{b} = a \div b $$

1. Terminating Decimals: When you divide and the remainder eventually becomes zero, the decimal is terminating.

  • Example: Convert $3/8$ to a decimal.

    • $1/6 = 0.1\bar{6}$

    • $1/11 = 0.\overline{09}$

3. Real-World Application: Converting fractions to decimals is useful for practical tasks, like using a decimal-marked measuring cup for a recipe given in fractions.

  • Example: A recipe calls for $2/5$ cup of sugar. How much do you measure with a decimal cup?
    • Divide $2 \div 5$.
    • $2 \div 5 = 0.4$
    • You would measure $0.4$ cup of sugar.
    • Check: $0.4 = 4/10 = 2/5$.

Converting a Decimal to a Fraction

To convert a terminating decimal into a fraction, follow these steps:

  1. Read the Decimal Using Place Value: This step is crucial for determining the denominator. For example, $0.45$ is read as "forty-five hundredths," which indicates the denominator will be $100$. $0.6$ is "six tenths," so the denominator is $10$.
  2. Write as a Fraction: Use the digits after the decimal point as the numerator, and the corresponding power of 10 (tenths, hundredths, thousandths, etc.) as the denominator.
    • $0.45 = 45/100$
    • $0.6 = 6/10$
  3. Simplify to Lowest Terms: Divide both the numerator and the denominator by their Greatest Common Factor (GCF).
    • Example: Convert $0.45$ to a fraction in lowest terms.
      • Read $0.45$ as "forty-five hundredths."
      • Write as a fraction: $45/100$.
      • Find the GCF of 45 and 100. Both are divisible by 5.
      • $45 \div 5 = 9$
      • $100 \div 5 = 20$
      • Simplify: $45/100 = 9/20$.
      • So, $0.45 = 9/20$.

Important Considerations

  • Rational Numbers: Remember that every terminating or repeating decimal can be written as a fraction, meaning they are all rational numbers. While some fractions might result in long repeating decimals, they still represent a precise fractional value.

Summary of Conversions

  • Fraction to Decimal: Divide the numerator by the denominator. The result will be either a decimal that ends (terminating) or one with digits that repeat forever (repeating, indicated by a bar).
  • Decimal to Fraction: Use place value to write the decimal as a fraction with a power of 10 in the denominator, then simplify the fraction to its lowest terms.
