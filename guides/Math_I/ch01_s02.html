Study Guide: Order of Operations

Overview

This section introduces the Order of Operations, a set of agreed-upon rules that dictate the sequence in which mathematical operations are performed in a numerical expression. By following these rules, universally known by the acronym PEMDAS, everyone can arrive at the same correct answer when evaluating expressions containing multiple operations and grouping symbols.

Key Concepts

  • Numerical Expression: A combination of numbers and mathematical operations (like addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division) that represents a single value. The goal of the Order of Operations is to evaluate these expressions consistently.
  • Grouping Symbols: Special symbols that indicate which operations must be performed first, overriding the standard order. Common grouping symbols include parentheses (), brackets [], and the fraction bar (which groups the entire numerator and the entire denominator separately).
  • Order of Operations (PEMDAS): A universally accepted sequence for evaluating numerical expressions:
    • Parentheses (and other grouping symbols) – always work from the innermost group outward.
    • Exponents – evaluate any powers.
    • Multiplication and Division – perform these operations from left to right as they appear.
    • Addition and Subtraction – perform these operations from left to right as they appear.
  • Left-to-Right Rule: For operations at the same level (Multiplication and Division, or Addition and Subtraction), always perform them in the order they appear from left to right.
  • Innermost Grouping First: When an expression contains nested grouping symbols (e.g., parentheses inside brackets), always evaluate the operations within the innermost symbols first and work your way outward.

Important Terms

  • Numerical Expression: A mathematical phrase that combines numbers and operation symbols to name a single value. For example, $10 - 2 \times 3$.
  • Grouping Symbols: Symbols used in mathematics to indicate which part of an expression should be evaluated first. Examples include parentheses (), brackets [], and the fraction bar.
  • Order of Operations: The specific set of rules that defines the sequence in which mathematical operations should be performed to ensure consistent evaluation of numerical expressions. This order is commonly remembered by the acronym PEMDAS.

Detailed Notes

The Need for Order

Imagine trying to solve $7 + 3 \times 5$. If you add first ($7+3=10$, then $10 \times 5 = 50$), you get 50. But if you multiply first ($3 \times 5 = 15$, then $7+15=22$), you get 22. To avoid such ambiguity and ensure everyone arrives at the same answer, mathematicians developed a fixed Order of Operations.

Numerical Expressions

A numerical expression is simply a mathematical statement involving numbers and operations, which represents a single value. Our goal is to find that single value.

Grouping Symbols

Grouping symbols are crucial because they allow us to change the natural order of operations. The most common ones are:

  • Parentheses: ()
  • Brackets: []
  • Fraction Bar: The line that separates the numerator from the denominator in a fraction acts as a grouping symbol for both the entire numerator and the entire denominator. You must evaluate the expressions above and below the bar separately before performing the division. For example, in $\frac{10+6}{4}$, you first calculate $10+6=16$ and then $16 \div 4 = 4$.

The Order of Operations (PEMDAS)

The widely accepted order for evaluating numerical expressions is remembered by the acronym PEMDAS:

  1. P — Parentheses (and other grouping symbols): Always start by performing all operations inside grouping symbols. If there are nested grouping symbols (e.g., parentheses inside brackets), work from the innermost group outward.
  2. E — Exponents: After handling grouping symbols, evaluate any exponents (powers). (This section doesn't have examples with exponents, but it's the next step in the full PEMDAS rule.)
  3. M/D — Multiplication and Division: Next, perform all multiplication and division operations. These two operations are at the same level of priority. When you have both in an expression, you must work from left to right as they appear.
  4. A/S — Addition and Subtraction: Finally, perform all addition and subtraction operations. These are also at the same level of priority. When you have both, you must work from left to right as they appear.

Applying the Order of Operations: Examples

Let's look at the examples from the textbook section to solidify understanding:

Example 1: Two Operations Evaluate $7 + 3 \times 5$.

  • According to PEMDAS, Multiplication comes before Addition.
  • First, multiply: $3 \times 5 = 15$.
  • Then, add: $7 + 15 = 22$.
  • So, $7 + 3 \times 5 = 22$.

Example 2: Left-to-Right Rule Evaluate $24 \div 6 \times 2$.

  • Division and Multiplication are at the same level.
  • Apply the left-to-right rule: perform the division first because it appears first from the left.
  • First, divide: $24 \div 6 = 4$.
  • Then, multiply: $4 \times 2 = 8$.
  • So, $24 \div 6 \times 2 = 8$. (A common mistake is to multiply $6 \times 2$ first, which would give the wrong answer of 2).

Example 3: Parentheses Evaluate $4(9 - 3) + 10$.

  • Parentheses come first.
  • First, perform the operation inside the parentheses: $9 - 3 = 6$. The expression becomes $4(6) + 10$.
  • Next, Multiplication comes before Addition: $4 \times 6 = 24$. The expression becomes $24 + 10$.
  • Finally, Add: $24 + 10 = 34$.
  • So, $4(9 - 3) + 10 = 34$.

Example 4: Nested Grouping Symbols Evaluate $2 [14 - (2 + 3)]$.

  • Work from the innermost grouping symbol outward. The innermost symbol is the parentheses (2 + 3).
  • First, calculate inside the innermost parentheses: $2 + 3 = 5$. The expression becomes $2 [14 - 5]$.
  • Next, evaluate inside the brackets: $14 - 5 = 9$. The expression becomes $2[9]$.
  • Finally, Multiply: $2 \times 9 = 18$.
  • So, $2 [14 - (2 + 3)] = 18$.

Important Note about Fraction Bars: Remember that a fraction bar acts as a grouping symbol. Evaluate the entire numerator and the entire denominator separately before performing the division implied by the fraction bar. For example, in $\frac{10 + 6}{4}$, you would first evaluate $10 + 6$ to get $16$, and then divide $16$ by $4$ to get $4$.

Common Mistake: A frequent error is to perform addition or subtraction before multiplication or division, especially when no grouping symbols are present to force that order. Always remember that multiplication and division have higher priority than addition and subtraction.

Order of Operations Summary

  • Use PEMDAS: Parentheses (and other grouping symbols), Exponents, Multiplication/Division (left to right), Addition/Subtraction (left to right).
  • Always work from the innermost grouping symbols outward.
  • A fraction bar implies grouping for both the numerator and the denominator.
  • When operations are at the same level (M/D or A/S), always work from left to right.

Review Questions

  1. Explain why mathematicians needed to agree on a fixed "Order of Operations." What problem does it solve?
  2. Describe the role of grouping symbols in a numerical expression. How does a fraction bar function as a grouping symbol?
  3. In the acronym PEMDAS, which operations are considered to be at the same level of priority? How do you decide which one to perform first when they appear together in an expression?
  4. Evaluate the expression $50 - 5 \times (3 + 4)$ step-by-step, explaining the reason for each operation you perform based on the Order of Operations.
  5. Consider the expression $18 \div 3 \times 2$. A student calculated it as $18 \div (3 \times 2) = 18 \div 6 = 3$. Is this correct? If not, explain the mistake and show the correct way to evaluate the expression.
