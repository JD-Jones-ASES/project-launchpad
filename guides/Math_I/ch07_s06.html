Study Guide: Applications of Tax, Tip, Discount, and Simple Interest

Overview

This section explores common real-world applications of percents in financial situations. It teaches you how to calculate sales tax on purchases, tips for services, discounts on items, and simple interest earned or paid on money. Mastering these calculations will equip you with essential skills for managing personal finances and making informed consumer decisions.

Key Concepts

  • Sales Tax: An additional percentage of money charged by the government on goods and services purchased. This amount is added to the original price to determine the total cost.
  • Tip (Gratuity): An extra amount of money given for a service (like at a restaurant), calculated as a percentage of the service bill. This is added to the bill to find the total payment.
  • Discount: A reduction in the original price of an item, typically expressed as a percentage off. The discount amount is subtracted from the original price to find the new, lower "sale price."
  • Simple Interest: Money earned or paid on an initial amount (called the principal) over a period of time. It is calculated using a specific formula that considers the principal, the annual interest rate, and the time in years.

Important Terms

  • Sales Tax: An amount added to the price of goods or services, collected by the government.
  • Tax Rate: The percentage used to calculate the sales tax.
  • Tip (Gratuity): An extra payment voluntarily given for a service, usually calculated as a percentage of the bill.
  • Tip Rate: The percentage used to calculate the tip amount.
  • Discount: A reduction in the original price of an item.
  • Discount Rate: The percentage used to calculate the discount amount.
  • Original Price: The initial price of an item before any discount is applied.
  • Sale Price: The price of an item after a discount has been applied.
  • Simple Interest: Interest calculated only on the initial principal amount.
  • Principal (P): The original amount of money deposited, invested, or borrowed.
  • Interest (I): The amount of money earned or paid on the principal.
  • Annual Interest Rate (r): The percentage at which interest is earned or charged per year, expressed as a decimal in calculations.
  • Time (t): The duration for which the principal is invested or borrowed, expressed in years.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Financial Applications of Percents

Percents are fundamental in many daily financial transactions. Understanding how to calculate tax, tip, discount, and interest is crucial for budgeting, shopping, and managing savings or loans. In all these applications, the percentage must be converted to a decimal before being used in calculations.

1. Sales Tax

Sales tax is an unavoidable part of many purchases. It's an amount added to the price of an item that goes to the government.

  • Formula for Tax Amount: tax amount = tax rate * price
  • Formula for Total Cost: total cost = price + tax amount

Example: Calculating Sales Tax A book costs $24.00. The sales tax rate is 7%. What is the total cost including tax?

  1. Convert the tax rate to a decimal: 7% = 0.07
  2. Calculate the tax amount: tax = 0.07 * $24.00 = $1.68
  3. Add the tax to the original price: total cost = $24.00 + $1.68 = $25.68

Checkpoint: If a pair of shoes costs $45.00 and the sales tax rate is 6%, the tax is 0.06 * $45.00 = $2.70. The total cost is $45.00 + $2.70 = $47.70.

2. Tip (Gratuity)

When you receive a service, such as at a restaurant, it's customary to leave a tip. This is an extra amount of money given to the service provider.

  • Formula for Tip Amount: tip amount = tip rate * bill
  • Formula for Total Amount Paid: total = bill + tip amount

Example: Calculating a Tip A family's restaurant bill is $52.00. They want to leave a 15% tip. How much is the tip, and what is the total amount paid?

  1. Convert the tip rate to a decimal: 15% = 0.15
  2. Calculate the tip amount: tip = 0.15 * $52.00 = $7.80
  3. Add the tip to the bill: total = $52.00 + $7.80 = $59.80

Checkpoint: For a lunch bill of $30.00 with a 20% tip, the tip is 0.20 * $30.00 = $6.00. The total paid is $30.00 + $6.00 = $36.00.

3. Discount

A discount is a reduction in the original price of an item, often advertised as a percentage off.

  • Formula for Discount Amount: discount amount = discount rate * original price
  • Formula for Sale Price: sale price = original price - discount amount

Example: Calculating a Discount A backpack has an original price of $55.00 and is on sale for 20% off. What is the sale price?

  1. Convert the discount rate to a decimal: 20% = 0.20
  2. Calculate the discount amount: discount = 0.20 * $55.00 = $11.00
  3. Subtract the discount from the original price: sale price = $55.00 - $11.00 = $44.00

Shortcut for Discounts: Instead of calculating the discount and then subtracting, you can directly calculate the sale price. If an item is 20% off, you are paying 100% - 20% = 80% of the original price.

  • Shortcut Formula: sale price = (1 - discount rate) * original price
  • Using the backpack example: sale price = (1 - 0.20) * $55.00 = 0.80 * $55.00 = $44.00.

4. Simple Interest

Simple interest is a straightforward way to calculate interest on an original amount of money (the principal). It applies to both money earned (e.g., in a savings account) and money paid (e.g., on a loan).

  • Formula for Simple Interest: I = Prt Where:
    • I = Interest earned or paid
    • P = Principal (the initial amount of money)
    • r = Annual interest rate (MUST be written as a decimal)
    • t = Time (MUST be in years)

Important Cautions for Simple Interest:

  • Always convert the interest rate r from a percentage to a decimal before using it in the formula (e.g., 4% = 0.04).
  • Ensure that the time t is expressed in years. If a problem provides time in months, divide the number of months by 12 to convert it to years (e.g., 6 months = 6/12 = 0.5 years).

Example: Calculating Simple Interest Rosa deposits $500 in a savings account that earns 4% simple interest per year. How much interest does she earn after 3 years? What is the total amount in the account?

  1. Identify the variables:
    • P = $500
    • r = 4% = 0.04 (as a decimal)
    • t = 3 years
  2. Apply the formula I = Prt: I = $500 * 0.04 * 3
  3. Calculate the interest: I = $60
  4. Calculate the total amount in the account: Total amount = Principal + Interest = $500 + $60 = $560

Checkpoint: If James borrows $1,000 at 5% simple interest for 2 years, the interest he owes is I = $1,000 * 0.05 * 2 = $100.

Summary of Formulas:

  • Sales Tax:
    • tax = rate * price
    • total = price + tax
  • Tip:
    • tip = rate * bill
    • total = bill + tip
  • Discount:
    • discount = rate * original price
    • sale price = original price - discount
  • Simple Interest:
    • I = Prt (where P is principal, r is annual rate as decimal, t is time in years)
