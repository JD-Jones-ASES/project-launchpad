Divisibility, Factors, and Prime Factorization Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces fundamental concepts in number theory, focusing on how numbers relate to each other through division. You will learn about divisibility rules, how to find all factors of a number, distinguish between prime and composite numbers, and perform prime factorization using factor trees. These skills are essential for understanding number properties and for future topics like finding the least common multiple (LCM).

Key Concepts

  • Divisibility: The property of one number being able to be divided by another number with no remainder.
  • Factors: Numbers that divide evenly into another number. Every number has 1 and itself as factors.
  • Prime Numbers: Whole numbers greater than 1 that have exactly two factors: 1 and themselves. Examples: 2, 3, 5, 7.
  • Composite Numbers: Whole numbers greater than 1 that have more than two factors. Examples: 4, 6, 8, 9.
  • Prime Factorization: Expressing a composite number as a product of its prime factors. This factorization is unique for every number greater than 1.
  • Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic: States that every whole number greater than 1 is either prime or can be written as a product of primes in exactly one unique way (disregarding the order of factors).
  • Least Common Multiple (LCM): The smallest positive number that is a multiple of two or more given numbers. Prime factorization is a systematic way to find the LCM.

Important Terms

  • Divisibility: A whole number $a$ is divisible by a nonzero whole number $b$ if $a \div b$ results in a remainder of $0$.
  • Factor: A number that divides evenly into another whole number, leaving no remainder.
  • Prime Number: A whole number greater than $1$ that has only two distinct factors: $1$ and itself.
  • Composite Number: A whole number greater than $1$ that has more than two distinct factors.
  • Prime Factorization: The process of writing a whole number as a product of only prime numbers.
  • Factor Tree: A diagram used to visually break down a number into its prime factors.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Divisibility and Factors

  • Divisibility means one number can be divided by another without any remainder. For instance, 12 is divisible by 3 because $12 \div 3 = 4$ with a remainder of 0.

  • Factors are the numbers that divide evenly into a given number. For 12, its factors are 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, and 12.

  • Divisibility Rules offer quick mental checks to see if a number is divisible by common small numbers:

    • By 2: If the last digit of the number is an even number (0, 2, 4, 6, 8).
    • By 3: If the sum of the digits of the number is divisible by 3.
    • By 5: If the last digit of the number is 0 or 5.
    • By 9: If the sum of the digits of the number is divisible by 9.
    • By 10: If the last digit of the number is 0.

    Example: To test if 1,374 is divisible by 2, 3, 5, 9, or 10:

    • By 2? Yes, because its last digit (4) is even.
    • By 3? Yes, because the sum of its digits (1+3+7+4=15) is divisible by 3.
    • By 5? No, because its last digit (4) is not 0 or 5.
    • By 9? No, because the sum of its digits (15) is not divisible by 9.
    • By 10? No, because its last digit (4) is not 0.

Prime and Composite Numbers

  • Prime Numbers: A whole number greater than 1 with exactly two factors: 1 and itself. Examples: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19...
    • The number 2 is unique because it is the only even prime number. All other even numbers are divisible by 2, 1, and themselves, making them composite.
  • Composite Numbers: A whole number greater than 1 that has more than two factors. Examples: 4 (factors: 1, 2, 4), 6 (factors: 1, 2, 3, 6), 9 (factors: 1, 3, 9).
  • Special Cases: The numbers 0 and 1 are neither prime nor composite.
    • 0 has infinitely many factors, violating the "exactly two factors" rule for primes and "more than two factors" for composites.
    • 1 has only one factor (itself), which doesn't fit the definition of prime (exactly two factors) or composite (more than two factors). If 1 were considered prime, the unique prime factorization of numbers would be broken (e.g., $6 = 2 \times 3 = 1 \times 2 \times 3$).

Finding All Factors of a Number

To find all factors of a number, systematically test whole numbers starting from 1. For each number that divides evenly, you find a pair of factors. Stop when the factors start repeating (when the number you are testing becomes greater than or equal to its pair).

  • Example: Finding all factors of 36
    • $36 = 1 \times 36$
    • $36 = 2 \times 18$
    • $36 = 3 \times 12$
    • $36 = 4 \times 9$
    • $36 = 6 \times 6$
    • The factors of 36 are: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 9, 12, 18, 36.

Prime Factorization

  • Prime factorization is writing a whole number as a product of only prime numbers. The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic guarantees that every whole number greater than 1 has a unique prime factorization.
  • Factor Trees are a visual tool to find prime factorization:
    1. Start with the number at the top.
    2. Break it down into any two factors.
    3. Continue breaking down any composite factors until all branches end in prime numbers.
    4. The prime factors at the ends of the branches are the prime factorization. Write them in ascending order, using exponents for repeated factors.
  • Example: Prime factorization of 60
    • Start with 60. You could choose $60 = 6 \times 10$.
    • Break down 6: $6 = 2 \times 3$. Both 2 and 3 are prime.
    • Break down 10: $10 = 2 \times 5$. Both 2 and 5 are prime.
    • The prime factors are 2, 3, 2, 5.
    • Arranging in order: $60 = 2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 5 = 2^2 \times 3 \times 5$.

Finding the LCM Using Prime Factorization

  1. Find the prime factorization of each number.
  2. Identify all unique prime factors that appear in any of the factorizations.
  3. For each unique prime factor, take the highest power (greatest exponent) of that prime factor that appears in any of the factorizations.
  4. Multiply these highest powers together to get the LCM.
  • Example: Find the LCM of 12 and 18
    1. Prime factorizations:
      • $12 = 2^2 \times 3$
      • $18 = 2 \times 3^2$
    2. Unique prime factors are 2 and 3.
    3. Highest power of 2: $2^2$ (from 12)
    4. Highest power of 3: $3^2$ (from 18)
    5. Multiply them: $LCM = 2^2 \times 3^2 = 4 \times 9 = 36$.
    • Check: $36 \div 12 = 3$ and $36 \div 18 = 2$. Both are whole numbers, confirming 36 is a common multiple. No smaller positive integer is a common multiple.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words the difference between a prime number and a composite number, providing two examples of each. Why are 0 and 1 considered neither?
  2. Describe the utility of divisibility rules. How can knowing whether a number is divisible by 2, 3, 5, 9, or 10 help you beyond simple division?
  3. Using a factor tree, find the prime factorization of 72. Explain each step you take.
  4. The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic states that every whole number greater than 1 has a unique prime factorization. Why is this uniqueness important in mathematics, particularly when finding the Least Common Multiple (LCM)?
  5. Consider two numbers: 45 and 75. First, determine if each is divisible by 2, 3, 5, 9, and 10 using the divisibility rules. Then, find the Least Common Multiple (LCM) of 45 and 75 using their prime factorizations.
