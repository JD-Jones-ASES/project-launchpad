Study Guide: Divisibility, Factors, and Prime Factorization

Overview

This section introduces the foundational concepts of divisibility, factors, and prime factorization, which are essential for understanding whole numbers. You will learn quick methods (divisibility rules) to determine if one number divides evenly into another, how to find all factors of a number, and how to break down any composite number into its unique prime building blocks using a factor tree. These concepts are crucial for further mathematical operations, such as finding the Least Common Multiple (LCM).

Key Concepts

  • Divisibility Rules: These are shortcuts that allow you to quickly determine if a number can be divided evenly by another number (like 2, 3, 5, 9, or 10) without performing long division. They are based on specific properties of the digits in a number.
  • Factors: A factor is a number that divides another number evenly, leaving no remainder. Understanding factors is key to breaking down numbers and simplifying expressions.
  • Prime Numbers: These are the fundamental building blocks of all other whole numbers (greater than 1). A prime number has only two distinct factors: 1 and itself.
  • Composite Numbers: These are whole numbers greater than 1 that have more than two factors. They can be "composed" by multiplying prime numbers.
  • Prime Factorization: This is the process of expressing a composite number as a product of its prime factors. The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic guarantees that every composite number has exactly one unique prime factorization, regardless of how you start the process.
  • Factor Tree: A visual tool used to systematically find the prime factorization of a number by repeatedly breaking it down into pairs of factors until all branches end in prime numbers.

Important Terms

Divisibility: A whole number $a$ is divisible by a nonzero whole number $b$ if $a \div b$ results in a remainder of $0$. We also say "$b$ divides $a$" or "$a$ is a multiple of $b$."

Factor: A number that divides another whole number evenly (with a remainder of $0$). For example, $1, 2, 3, 4, 6,$ and $12$ are factors of $12$.

Prime Number: A whole number greater than $1$ whose only factors are $1$ and itself. Examples include $2, 3, 5, 7, 11, \ldots$.

Composite Number: A whole number greater than $1$ that has more than two factors. Examples include $4, 6, 8, 9, 10, \ldots$.

Prime Factorization: The unique way of writing a whole number greater than $1$ as a product of its prime numbers. For example, the prime factorization of $12$ is $2 \times 2 \times 3$ or $2^2 \times 3$.

Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic: This foundational theorem states that every whole number greater than $1$ is either prime or can be written as a product of prime numbers in exactly one unique way, regardless of the order of the factors.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Divisibility

Divisibility is a fundamental concept in number theory. When we say a number is divisible by another, it means that when you divide the first number by the second, you get a whole number as a quotient and no remainder.

Divisibility Rules

Divisibility rules are mental shortcuts that can save time when checking for factors. Here are the common rules covered:

  • Divisibility by 2: A number is divisible by 2 if its last digit is an even number ($0, 2, 4, 6, 8$).
  • Divisibility by 3: A number is divisible by 3 if the sum of its digits is divisible by 3.
  • Divisibility by 5: A number is divisible by 5 if its last digit is $0$ or $5$.
  • Divisibility by 9: A number is divisible by 9 if the sum of its digits is divisible by 9.
  • Divisibility by 10: A number is divisible by 10 if its last digit is $0$.

Example: Testing Divisibility of 1,374 Let's apply these rules to determine if $1,374$ is divisible by $2, 3, 5, 9,$ or $10$.

  • By 2? The last digit is $4$, which is an even number. Yes.
  • By 3? Sum of digits: $1+3+7+4 = 15$. Since $15$ is divisible by $3$ ($15 \div 3 = 5$), Yes.
  • By 5? The last digit is $4$, not $0$ or $5$. No.
  • By 9? Sum of digits is $15$. Since $15$ is not divisible by $9$, No.
  • By 10? The last digit is $4$, not $0$. No. So, $1,374$ is divisible by $2$ and $3$ only.

Checkpoint: To test $2,340$:

  • By 2? Last digit is $0$ (even). Yes.
  • By 3? Sum of digits: $2+3+4+0 = 9$. $9$ is divisible by $3$. Yes.
  • By 5? Last digit is $0$. Yes.
  • By 9? Sum of digits: $9$. $9$ is divisible by $9$. Yes.
  • By 10? Last digit is $0$. Yes. $2,340$ is divisible by $2, 3, 5, 9,$ and $10$.

Factors, Prime, and Composite Numbers

Understanding factors leads us to classify numbers as prime or composite.

  • Prime Numbers: These are the bedrock of multiplication. A prime number has exactly two factors: $1$ and itself. Examples are $2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19, \ldots$. The number $2$ is unique as it's the only even prime number. All other even numbers are divisible by $2$ and thus have more than two factors.
  • Composite Numbers: These numbers have more than two factors. Examples are $4$ (factors: $1, 2, 4$), $6$ (factors: $1, 2, 3, 6$), $8$ (factors: $1, 2, 4, 8$).
  • Special Cases: The numbers $0$ and $1$ are neither prime nor composite.
    • $1$ is not prime because it only has one factor (itself), not two distinct factors. If $1$ were prime, prime factorization wouldn't be unique (e.g., $6 = 2 \times 3 = 1 \times 2 \times 3$).
    • $0$ is not considered prime or composite in this context.

Example: Finding All Factors of 36 To find all factors, systematically check each whole number starting from 1 up to the square root of the number. When you find a factor, you also find its "partner" (the number you multiply it by to get the original number).

  • $36 = 1 \times 36$
  • $36 = 2 \times 18$
  • $36 = 3 \times 12$
  • $36 = 4 \times 9$
  • $36 = 6 \times 6$ We stop at $6 \times 6$ because the factors begin to repeat ($9 \times 4$, $12 \times 3$, etc.). The factors of $36$ are $1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 9, 12, 18, 36$.

Prime Factorization

Prime factorization is the process of breaking down a composite number into a product of its prime factors.

  • Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic: This powerful theorem guarantees that every whole number greater than $1$ has exactly one unique prime factorization (ignoring the order in which the prime factors are written). This uniqueness makes prime factorization a critical tool in many mathematical areas.

  • Using a Factor Tree: A factor tree is a visual way to find the prime factorization. You start with the number at the top and branch down, breaking it into any two factors. You continue branching until all the numbers at the ends of the branches are prime.

Example: Prime Factorization of 60

  1. Start with 60. Choose any two factors, for example, $6$ and $10$.
        60
   /  \
  6   10
  2. Neither $6$ nor $10$ are prime, so continue branching.
    • For $6$: $2 \times 3$. Both $2$ and $3$ are prime.
    • For $10$: $2 \times 5$. Both $2$ and $5$ are prime.
        60
   /  \
  6   10
 / \  / \
2  3 2  5
  3. The prime factors at the bottom are $2, 3, 2, 5$.
  4. Write them in order, using exponents for repeated factors: $60 = 2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 5 = 2^2 \times 3 \times 5$.

Checkpoint: Write the prime factorization of $90$. $90 = 9 \times 10 = (3 \times 3) \times (2 \times 5) = 2 \times 3^2 \times 5$.

Application: Finding the LCM Using Prime Factorization

Prime factorization is extremely useful for finding the Least Common Multiple (LCM) of two or more numbers. The LCM is the smallest positive number that is a multiple of all the given numbers.

Example: Finding the LCM of 12 and 18 Step 1: Read. We need to find the smallest number that is a multiple of both $12$ and $18$.

Step 2: Identify. Find the prime factorization of each number.

  • $12 = 2 \times 2 \times 3 = 2^2 \times 3$
  • $18 = 2 \times 3 \times 3 = 2 \times 3^2$

Step 3: Write. For each unique prime factor (2 and 3 in this case), take the highest power that appears in any of the factorizations.

  • For prime factor 2: The powers are $2^2$ (from 12) and $2^1$ (from 18). The highest power is $2^2$.
  • For prime factor 3: The powers are $3^1$ (from 12) and $3^2$ (from 18). The highest power is $3^2$.

Step 4: Solve. Multiply these highest powers together. $LCM = 2^2 \times 3^2 = 4 \times 9 = 36$.

Step 5: Check.

  • Is $36$ a multiple of $12$? $36 \div 12 = 3$. Yes.
  • Is $36$ a multiple of $18$? $36 \div 18 = 2$. Yes. $36$ is the least common multiple.

Review Questions

  1. Describe in your own words what it means for one number to be "divisible" by another. Provide an example where a number is divisible, and another where it is not.
  2. Explain the difference between a prime number and a composite number. Why are the numbers $0$ and $1$ neither prime nor composite?
  3. Without performing long division, determine if the number $4,590$ is divisible by $2, 3, 5, 9,$ and $10$. Justify your answers using the divisibility rules.
  4. Find all the factors of $48$. Explain the systematic approach you used to ensure you found every factor.
  5. What is the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic, and why is it important for prime factorization? Use a factor tree to find the prime factorization of $72$ and show how it aligns with this theorem.
