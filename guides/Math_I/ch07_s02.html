Fractions, Decimals, and Percents: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Overview

This section explains the fundamental relationship between fractions, decimals, and percents as different ways to express the same numerical value. It provides clear, step-by-step methods for converting numbers seamlessly between these three forms, which is a crucial skill for many mathematical and real-world applications. Mastering these conversions will enhance your understanding of quantity representation.

Key Concepts

  • Interchangeable Forms: Fractions, decimals, and percents are simply different notations for the same number or proportion.
  • Percent to Decimal Conversion: To convert a percent to a decimal, divide the number by 100, which is equivalent to moving the decimal point two places to the left.
  • Decimal to Percent Conversion: To convert a decimal to a percent, multiply the number by 100 and attach the percent symbol (%), which is equivalent to moving the decimal point two places to the right.
  • Percent to Fraction Conversion: To convert a percent to a fraction, write the percent value over a denominator of 100 and then simplify the resulting fraction to its lowest terms.
  • Fraction to Percent Conversion: To convert a fraction to a percent, first divide the numerator by the denominator to obtain a decimal, and then convert that decimal to a percent.
  • Percents Greater Than 100%: A percent value exceeding 100% signifies a quantity larger than one whole. When converted, it will result in a decimal greater than 1 or a mixed number.
  • Percents Less Than 1%: A percent value less than 1% (e.g., 0.5%) represents a very small quantity. When converted to a decimal, it will require careful movement of the decimal point to correctly reflect its small value (e.g., 0.5% becomes 0.005).

Important Terms

  • Fraction: A way to represent a part of a whole, written as a ratio of two numbers (numerator over denominator). Example: $\frac{3}{5}$.
  • Decimal: A number that includes a decimal point, representing whole numbers and fractional parts (tenths, hundredths, thousandths, etc.). Example: $0.6$.
  • Percent: A ratio or fraction expressed as a number out of 100. The word "percent" means "per hundred" or "out of one hundred." Represented by the symbol %. Example: $60%$.
  • Numerator: The top number in a fraction, representing the number of parts being considered.
  • Denominator: The bottom number in a fraction, representing the total number of equal parts that make up the whole.
  • Simplify: To reduce a fraction to its lowest terms by dividing both the numerator and the denominator by their greatest common divisor.
  • Mixed Number: A number consisting of an integer (whole number) and a proper fraction. Example: $1\frac{1}{4}$.

Detailed Notes

The Relationship Between Fractions, Decimals, and Percents

Fractions, decimals, and percents are simply different ways of writing the same value. Think of them as different languages to express a quantity or a part of a whole. For instance, half of something can be written as $\frac{1}{2}$ (fraction), $0.5$ (decimal), or $50%$ (percent). Being able to convert between these forms is a fundamental mathematical skill.

Converting Between Percents and Decimals

This is one of the most common and straightforward conversions. The key is understanding that "percent" literally means "per hundred," so converting involves either multiplying or dividing by 100.

  • Percent to Decimal:

    • Rule: Divide the percent value by $100$. This is equivalent to moving the decimal point two places to the left.
    • Why?: You're expressing a value "out of 100" as a simple number.
    • Example 1: Convert $35%$ to a decimal.
      • $35% = \frac{35}{100} = 0.35$
      • (Think: Start with $35.0$, move decimal two places left $\rightarrow 0.35$)
    • Example 2: Convert $125%$ to a decimal.
      • $125% = \frac{125}{100} = 1.25$
      • (Think: Start with $125.0$, move decimal two places left $\rightarrow 1.25$)
    • Caution for small percents: When converting a percent less than $1%$, remember to move the decimal point two places left. For example, $0.5% = 0.005$, not $0.5$. (You need to add a leading zero.)

  • Decimal to Percent:

    • Rule: Multiply the decimal value by $100$ and then attach the percent symbol (%). This is equivalent to moving the decimal point two places to the right.
    • Why?: You're expressing the number as a value "per hundred."
    • Example 1: Convert $0.08$ to a percent.
      • $0.08 \times 100 = 8$
      • Attach % symbol: $8%$
      • (Think: Start with $0.08$, move decimal two places right $\rightarrow 8.0 \rightarrow 8%$)
    • Example 2: Convert $0.455$ to a percent.
      • $0.455 \times 100 = 45.5$
      • Attach % symbol: $45.5%$
      • (Think: Start with $0.455$, move decimal two places right $\rightarrow 45.5%$)
    • Real-world application (Nutrition Label): A cereal box states $0.15$ of the recommended daily amount of iron. To express this as a percent:
      • $0.15 \times 100 = 15$
      • Result: $15%$

Converting Between Percents and Fractions

  • Percent to Fraction:

    • Rule: Write the percent value as the numerator of a fraction with a denominator of $100$. Then, simplify the fraction to its lowest terms.
    • Why?: A percent is "per hundred," so it directly translates to a fraction with a denominator of 100.
    • Example 1: Convert $60%$ to a fraction.
      • $60% = \frac{60}{100}$
      • Simplify: Divide both numerator and denominator by 20.
      • $\frac{60 \div 20}{100 \div 20} = \frac{3}{5}$
    • Example 2: Convert $125%$ to a fraction (or mixed number).
      • $125% = \frac{125}{100}$
      • Simplify: Divide both by 25.
      • $\frac{125 \div 25}{100 \div 25} = \frac{5}{4}$
      • Convert to mixed number: $1\frac{1}{4}$

  • Fraction to Percent:

    • Rule: First, divide the numerator by the denominator to convert the fraction into a decimal. Then, convert that decimal to a percent (by multiplying by 100 and adding the % symbol).
    • Why?: This two-step process leverages your knowledge of fraction-to-decimal and decimal-to-percent conversions.
    • Example 1: Convert $\frac{3}{5}$ to a percent.
      • Step 1: Divide 3 by 5 to get the decimal: $3 \div 5 = 0.6$.
      • Step 2: Convert $0.6$ to a percent: $0.6 \times 100 = 60%$.
    • Example 2: Convert $\frac{7}{8}$ to a percent.
      • Step 1: Divide 7 by 8: $7 \div 8 = 0.875$.
      • Step 2: Convert $0.875$ to a percent: $0.875 \times 100 = 87.5%$.

Percents Greater Than 100%

When a percent is greater than $100%$, it means the quantity is more than one whole.

  • Decimal: Will be greater than 1. (e.g., $150% = 1.50$)
  • Fraction: Will be an improper fraction that can be written as a mixed number. (e.g., $150% = \frac{150}{100} = \frac{3}{2} = 1\frac{1}{2}$)

Summary of Conversions

Here's a quick summary of the conversion methods:

  • Percent to Decimal: Divide by 100 (move decimal two places left).
  • Decimal to Percent: Multiply by 100 (move decimal two places right) and attach %.
  • Percent to Fraction: Write over 100 and simplify.
  • Fraction to Percent: Divide numerator by denominator (to get decimal), then convert the decimal to a percent.

Remember: A percent greater than $100%$ corresponds to a number greater than $1$.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words why fractions, decimals, and percents are considered different ways to write the same number. Provide an example to illustrate your explanation.
  2. Describe the shortcut method for converting a decimal to a percent and a percent to a decimal. Why does this shortcut work in terms of multiplication or division by 100?
  3. A recipe calls for $\frac{3}{4}$ of a cup of sugar. If you wanted to tell a friend the percentage of a cup to use, what would you say? Show the steps for your conversion.
  4. Consider the values $0.2%$, $2$, and $\frac{2}{5}$. Convert all three to decimals, then order them from least to greatest. What does $0.2%$ represent in terms of its size?
  5. Imagine you are explaining to a younger student why $150%$ is equivalent to $1.5$ or $1\frac{1}{2}$. How would you use real-world examples (like doubling a recipe or a sale) to help them understand a percent greater than 100%?
