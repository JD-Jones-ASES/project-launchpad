Order of Operations with Fractions Study Guide

Overview

This section extends the familiar order of operations (PEMDAS) to include calculations involving fractions and mixed numbers. It emphasizes that the same hierarchical rules apply, even when expressions incorporate grouping symbols like parentheses, exponents, and fraction bars, along with all four basic fraction operations. Mastering this ensures consistent and accurate evaluation of complex fraction expressions.

Key Concepts

  • PEMDAS for Fractions: The established order of operations (Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication/Division, Addition/Subtraction) applies universally to expressions containing fractions and mixed numbers, just as it does for whole numbers and integers.
  • Fraction Bar as a Grouping Symbol: A fraction bar implicitly groups the entire numerator and the entire denominator. This means you must completely simplify the expression above the bar and the expression below the bar before performing the division represented by the fraction bar.
  • Mixed Numbers in Operations: When performing multiplication, division, addition, or subtraction with mixed numbers, especially in multi-step problems, it is often necessary and recommended to convert them into improper fractions first.
  • Multiplication and Division (Left to Right): After resolving parentheses and exponents, perform all multiplication and division operations from the leftmost to the rightmost occurrence in the expression.
  • Addition and Subtraction (Left to Right): Finally, after all other operations are complete, perform all addition and subtraction operations from the leftmost to the rightmost occurrence.
  • Simplifying at Each Step: To keep calculations manageable and reduce the chance of error, it is good practice to simplify fractions to their lowest terms whenever possible during intermediate steps.

Important Terms

  • Order of Operations (PEMDAS): An acronym (Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication, Division, Addition, Subtraction) representing the sequence in which mathematical operations should be performed to ensure a single, consistent result.
  • Fractions: Numbers that represent a part of a whole, expressed as a ratio of two integers, a numerator over a denominator (e.g., $1/2$).
  • Mixed Numbers: Numbers consisting of an integer and a proper fraction (e.g., $2 \frac{1}{2}$).
  • Grouping Symbols: Mathematical symbols used to indicate that the operations within them should be performed first. Common examples include parentheses (), brackets [], braces {}, and fraction bars.
  • Complex Fraction: A fraction where the numerator, denominator, or both contain fractions. It represents division between the simplified numerator and denominator.
  • Numerator: The top number in a fraction, representing the number of parts being considered.
  • Denominator: The bottom number in a fraction, representing the total number of equal parts into which the whole is divided.
  • Improper Fraction: A fraction where the numerator is greater than or equal to the denominator (e.g., $5/2$).

Detailed Notes

The Universal Rule of PEMDAS

The foundational principle for evaluating mathematical expressions remains PEMDAS, regardless of whether you are working with whole numbers, integers, decimals, or fractions. This means:

  1. Parentheses (and other grouping symbols like brackets, braces, and fraction bars)
  2. Exponents
  3. Multiplication and Division (from left to right)
  4. Addition and Subtraction (from left to right)

Understanding the Fraction Bar as a Grouping Symbol

One crucial point when working with fractions is that a fraction bar acts as an implicit grouping symbol. For any expression in the form $\frac{A}{B}$, you must treat 'A' (the entire numerator) as one group and 'B' (the entire denominator) as another group. This implies that all operations within the numerator must be completed, and all operations within the denominator must be completed before you perform the final division of the numerator by the denominator.

Example Walkthroughs

1. Multi-Step Computation ($1/2 + 3/4 \cdot 2/3$)

  • Problem: Evaluate $1/2 + 3/4 \cdot 2/3$.
  • PEMDAS Step: Multiplication comes before addition.
  • Step 1: Multiply.
    • $3/4 \cdot 2/3 = (3 \cdot 2) / (4 \cdot 3) = 6/12$
    • Simplify the product: $6/12 = 1/2$.
  • Step 2: Add.
    • Now the expression is $1/2 + 1/2$.
    • $1/2 + 1/2 = 2/2 = 1$.
  • Result: The evaluated expression is $1$.

2. Parentheses with Fractions ($(5/6 - 1/3) \cdot 3/5$)

  • Problem: Evaluate $(5/6 - 1/3) \cdot 3/5$.
  • PEMDAS Step: Parentheses first.
  • Step 1: Subtract inside parentheses.
    • Find a common denominator for $5/6 - 1/3$. The common denominator is $6$.
    • Convert $1/3$ to $2/6$.
    • $5/6 - 2/6 = 3/6$.
    • Simplify the result: $3/6 = 1/2$.
  • Step 2: Multiply.
    • Now the expression is $1/2 \cdot 3/5$.
    • $1/2 \cdot 3/5 = (1 \cdot 3) / (2 \cdot 5) = 3/10$.
  • Result: The evaluated expression is $3/10$.

3. Complex Fraction ($\frac{3/4 + 1/2}{5/6 - 1/3}$)

  • Problem: Evaluate the complex fraction $\frac{3/4 + 1/2}{5/6 - 1/3}$.
  • PEMDAS Step: The fraction bar acts as a grouping symbol, so simplify the numerator and denominator separately first.
  • Step 1: Simplify the Numerator.
    • $3/4 + 1/2$. Find a common denominator ($4$).
    • $3/4 + 2/4 = 5/4$.
  • Step 2: Simplify the Denominator.
    • $5/6 - 1/3$. Find a common denominator ($6$).
    • $5/6 - 2/6 = 3/6$.
    • Simplify the result: $3/6 = 1/2$.
  • Step 3: Divide the simplified Numerator by the simplified Denominator.
    • Now the expression is $\frac{5/4}{1/2}$. This means $5/4 \div 1/2$.
    • To divide fractions, multiply by the reciprocal of the divisor: $5/4 \cdot 2/1$.
    • $(5 \cdot 2) / (4 \cdot 1) = 10/4$.
    • Simplify the result: $10/4 = 5/2 = 2 \frac{1}{2}$.
  • Caution: Never attempt to cross-cancel between the numerator and denominator of a complex fraction before each part (top and bottom) has been simplified to a single fraction.

4. Multi-Step Word Problem (Mia's Fruit Bags)

  • Problem: Mia bought $2 \frac{1}{2}$ pounds of apples and $1 \frac{1}{4}$ pounds of grapes. She split the total fruit equally among $\frac{1}{2}$-pound bags. How many bags did she fill?
  • Step 1: Understand the Goal. Find the total weight of fruit, then divide that total by the weight per bag.
  • Step 2: Identify Operations. First, add the weights of the apples and grapes. Then, divide the sum by the bag size.
  • Step 3: Write the Expression. $(2 \frac{1}{2} + 1 \frac{1}{4}) \div \frac{1}{2}$.
  • Step 4: Solve using PEMDAS.
    • Convert Mixed Numbers to Improper Fractions: $2 \frac{1}{2} = 5/2$, $1 \frac{1}{4} = 5/4$.
    • Add inside Parentheses (P): $5/2 + 5/4$. Find a common denominator ($4$).
      • $10/4 + 5/4 = 15/4$.
    • Divide (D): Now the expression is $15/4 \div 1/2$.
      • Multiply by the reciprocal: $15/4 \cdot 2/1$.
      • $(15 \cdot 2) / (4 \cdot 1) = 30/4$.
      • Simplify the result: $30/4 = 15/2 = 7 \frac{1}{2}$.
  • Step 5: Check and State Answer. Mia can fill $7$ full bags, with $\frac{1}{2}$ a bag's worth (or $\frac{1}{4}$ pound) of fruit left over. The question asks "How many bags did she fill?", implying full bags. So, she filled 7 full bags.

Summary of Best Practices

  • Always follow PEMDAS rigorously.
  • Treat fraction bars as implicit grouping symbols.
  • Convert mixed numbers to improper fractions before performing multiplication or division, and often for addition/subtraction.
  • Work left-to-right for operations of the same precedence (M/D, A/S).
  • Simplify fractions as you go to avoid large numbers and make calculations easier.
