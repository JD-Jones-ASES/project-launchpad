Order of Operations with Fractions Study Guide

Overview

This section extends the familiar order of operations (PEMDAS) to include expressions involving fractions and mixed numbers. It emphasizes that the same rules apply when working with fractional values, paying special attention to how fraction bars act as grouping symbols and the process for simplifying complex fractions.

Key Concepts

  • PEMDAS for Fractions: The established order of operations (Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication/Division, Addition/Subtraction) applies identically when calculations involve fractions or mixed numbers.
  • Fraction Bar as a Grouping Symbol: A fraction bar implicitly groups the entire numerator and the entire denominator. You must simplify the numerator to a single value and the denominator to a single value before performing the division represented by the fraction bar.
  • Simplifying Complex Fractions: When faced with a complex fraction (a fraction where the numerator or denominator, or both, contain fractions), treat the numerator and denominator as separate expressions that need to be simplified first. After both are single fractions, then perform the division.
  • Mixed Numbers: For most operations, especially multiplication and division, it is often necessary and easier to convert mixed numbers into improper fractions before applying the order of operations.
  • Left-to-Right Rule: For operations at the same level (Multiplication/Division or Addition/Subtraction), always perform them from left to right within the expression.
  • Step-by-Step Simplification: It is good practice to simplify expressions at each step to keep the numbers manageable and reduce the chances of errors.

Important Terms

  • Order of Operations (PEMDAS): An acronym (Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication and Division, Addition and Subtraction) dictating the sequence in which mathematical operations should be performed to ensure a single, correct result.
  • Fractions: Numbers representing parts of a whole, written as a numerator over a denominator.
  • Mixed Numbers: Numbers composed of a whole number and a fraction (e.g., $2,12$).
  • Grouping Symbols: Symbols like parentheses ( ), brackets [ ], braces { }, and fraction bars that indicate which parts of an expression should be evaluated first.
  • Complex Fraction: A fraction in which the numerator, denominator, or both contain fractions themselves.
  • Numerator: The top number in a fraction, representing the number of parts being considered.
  • Denominator: The bottom number in a fraction, representing the total number of equal parts in the whole.

Detailed Notes

The Universal Rule: PEMDAS

The foundational principle for evaluating expressions, PEMDAS, does not change when you introduce fractions. Whether you're working with whole numbers, integers, or fractions, the hierarchy of operations remains the same:

  1. Parentheses (and all other grouping symbols, including fraction bars)
  2. Exponents
  3. Multiplication and Division (from left to right)
  4. Addition and Subtraction (from left to right)

Grouping Symbols: Parentheses and Fraction Bars

  • Parentheses: Always evaluate the operations inside parentheses first.

    • Example: To evaluate $(5/6 - 1/3) \times 3/5$:
      • First, calculate inside the parentheses: $5/6 - 1/3 = 5/6 - 2/6 = 3/6 = 1/2$.
      • Then, perform the multiplication: $1/2 \times 3/5 = 3/10$.

  • Fraction Bars as Grouping Symbols: This is a crucial point for fractions. A fraction bar implicitly tells you to treat the entire numerator as one grouped expression and the entire denominator as another. You must simplify each of these separately until they are single fractional values before you perform the final division.

    • Example: For an expression like $\frac{3/4 + 1/2}{5/6 - 1/3}$:
      • Step 1: Simplify the Numerator. $3/4 + 1/2 = 3/4 + 2/4 = 5/4$.
      • Step 2: Simplify the Denominator. $5/6 - 1/3 = 5/6 - 2/6 = 3/6 = 1/2$.
      • Step 3: Perform the Division. Now you have $\frac{5/4}{1/2}$, which means $5/4 \div 1/2$.
      • $5/4 \times 2/1 = 10/4 = 5/2 = 2,1/2$.
    • Caution: Do not attempt to cross-cancel terms between the numerator and denominator of a complex fraction before each has been simplified to a single fraction. Simplify each part completely, then divide.

Exponents

Although not explicitly demonstrated with an example in this section, remember that if an expression included a fraction raised to a power (e.g., $(2/3)^2$), you would evaluate the exponent after any parentheses but before multiplication, division, addition, or subtraction.

Multiplication and Division (Left to Right)

These operations are performed after parentheses and exponents, from left to right as they appear in the expression.

  • Example: To evaluate $1/2 + 3/4 \times 2/3$:
    • Multiplication comes before addition. First, multiply: $3/4 \times 2/3 = 6/12 = 1/2$.
    • Then, add: $1/2 + 1/2 = 2/2 = 1$.

Addition and Subtraction (Left to Right)

These operations are performed last, from left to right.

  • Example: (Continuing from the previous example, if the expression was $1/2 + 1/2$) The addition is the final step.

Working with Mixed Numbers

When an expression involves mixed numbers, it is generally best practice to convert them to improper fractions before performing multiplication, division, addition, or subtraction, especially if multiple operations are involved.

  • Word Problem Example: Mia bought $2,1/2$ pounds of apples and $1,1/4$ pounds of grapes. She split the total fruit equally among $1/2$-pound bags. How many bags did she fill?
    • Step 1: Formulate the expression. $(2,1/2 + 1,1/4) \div 1/2$.
    • Step 2: Convert mixed numbers to improper fractions. $2,1/2 = 5/2$, $1,1/4 = 5/4$.
    • Step 3: Add inside the parentheses. $5/2 + 5/4 = 10/4 + 5/4 = 15/4$.
    • Step 4: Divide. $15/4 \div 1/2 = 15/4 \times 2/1 = 30/4 = 15/2 = 7,1/2$.
    • Answer: She filled 7 full bags (with $1/4$ pound, or half a bag, remaining).

General Tips

  • Simplify as you go: Reduce fractions to their lowest terms at each step when possible to keep numbers smaller and computations easier.
  • Common Denominators: Remember to find common denominators for addition and subtraction of fractions.

Review Questions

  1. Explain why the order of operations (PEMDAS) is important when working with fractions, giving an example where a different order would lead to an incorrect answer.
  2. Evaluate the expression: $\frac{7}{8} - \frac{1}{4} \times \frac{2}{3}$
  3. Simplify the complex fraction: $\frac{\frac{2}{5} + \frac{1}{10}}{\frac{3}{4} - \frac{1}{2}}$
  4. A recipe calls for $1,1/3$ cups of flour for one batch of cookies. If you want to make $2,1/2$ batches, and then use $1/4$ of the total flour for decorating, how much flour (in cups) is left for the cookies themselves? Write out the expression and solve.
  5. What is the role of a fraction bar in terms of grouping symbols, and why is it critical to understand this role when simplifying expressions?
