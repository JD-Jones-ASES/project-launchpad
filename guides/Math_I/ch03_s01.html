Greatest Common Factor and Least Common Multiple Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces two fundamental concepts: the Greatest Common Factor (GCF) and the Least Common Multiple (LCM). These powerful tools are essential for working with fractions, specifically for simplifying them and finding common denominators, which are crucial steps in adding and subtracting fractions. You will learn how to define and calculate both GCF and LCM using various methods, including listing factors/multiples and prime factorization.

Key Concepts

  • Factors and Multiples: Understanding factors (numbers that divide evenly) and multiples (products of a number and any whole number) is foundational to GCF and LCM.
  • Greatest Common Factor (GCF): The largest factor shared by two or more numbers. It is used when you need to divide numbers into the largest possible equal parts, such as simplifying fractions.
    • Methods for GCF:
      1. Listing Factors: List all factors for each number and identify the largest one they share.
      2. Prime Factorization: Break numbers down into their prime factors. The GCF is found by taking the smallest exponent for each prime factor that the numbers share.
  • Least Common Multiple (LCM): The smallest positive multiple shared by two or more numbers. It is used when you need to find a common "meeting point" for numbers, such as finding a common denominator for fractions or solving scheduling problems.
    • Methods for LCM:
      1. Listing Multiples: List several multiples for each number and find the smallest positive one they share.
      2. Prime Factorization: Break numbers down into their prime factors. The LCM is found by taking the largest exponent for every prime factor present in any of the numbers.
  • Applications: GCF is primarily used for simplifying fractions, while LCM is critical for finding common denominators when adding or subtracting fractions, and for solving real-world problems involving events that repeat at different intervals.
  • Relationship between GCF and LCM: For any two whole numbers a and b, their GCF multiplied by their LCM is equal to the product of the numbers themselves: GCF(a,b) * LCM(a,b) = a * b.
  • Important Distinction: GCF is always less than or equal to the smallest of the given numbers. LCM is always greater than or equal to the largest of the given numbers.

Important Terms

  • Factor: A whole number that divides another whole number evenly, with no remainder.
    • Example: The factors of 12 are 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12.
  • Greatest Common Factor (GCF): The largest factor that two or more whole numbers share. It is sometimes denoted as (a,b) in the textbook, but more commonly as GCF(a,b) or gcd(a,b).
    • Example: The GCF of 12 and 18, GCF(12,18), is 6.
  • Multiple: The product of a whole number and any other whole number (including 0).
    • Example: The multiples of 4 are 0, 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, ...
  • Least Common Multiple (LCM): The smallest positive number that is a multiple of two or more whole numbers. It is sometimes denoted as (a,b) in the textbook, but more commonly as LCM(a,b) or lcm(a,b).
    • Example: The LCM of 4 and 6, LCM(4,6), is 12.
  • Prime Factorization: The process of expressing a composite number as a product of its prime factors. This method is highly efficient for finding GCF and LCM, especially for larger numbers.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to GCF and LCM

The concepts of Greatest Common Factor and Least Common Multiple are fundamental building blocks for understanding and manipulating fractions. Whether you're simplifying a fraction to its lowest terms or preparing to add or subtract fractions with different denominators, GCF and LCM provide the necessary tools.

Understanding Factors and How to Find the GCF

A factor is a number that can divide another number completely without leaving a remainder. For instance, if you can divide 12 by 3 and get 4 with no remainder, then 3 is a factor of 12.

The Greatest Common Factor (GCF) is the largest factor that two or more numbers share.

Method 1: Finding the GCF by Listing Factors

This method is straightforward for smaller numbers.

  1. List all factors for each number.
  2. Identify and circle the factors that appear in every list. These are the common factors.
  3. The greatest circled factor is the GCF.
  • Example: Find GCF(24, 36)
    • Factors of 24: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24
    • Factors of 36: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 9, 12, 18, 36
    • Common factors: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12
    • The greatest common factor is 12.

Method 2: Finding the GCF by Prime Factorization

This method is efficient, especially for larger numbers.

  1. Write the prime factorization of each number. This means expressing each number as a product of prime numbers raised to their respective powers.
  2. Identify every prime factor that appears in all factorizations. These are the shared prime factors.
  3. For each shared prime factor, take the smallest exponent that appears in any of the factorizations.
  4. Multiply these prime factors (with their chosen exponents) together. The product is the GCF.
  • Example: Find GCF(60, 90)
    • Prime factorization of 60: 60 = 2^2 * 3^1 * 5^1
    • Prime factorization of 90: 90 = 2^1 * 3^2 * 5^1
    • Shared prime factors are 2, 3, and 5.
    • Smallest exponent for 2: 2^1 (from 90)
    • Smallest exponent for 3: 3^1 (from 60)
    • Smallest exponent for 5: 5^1 (from both)
    • GCF(60, 90) = 2^1 * 3^1 * 5^1 = 2 * 3 * 5 = 30.

Understanding Multiples and How to Find the LCM

A multiple of a whole number is the result of multiplying that number by any whole number (0, 1, 2, 3, ...). For example, multiples of 4 are 0, 4, 8, 12, 16, etc.

The Least Common Multiple (LCM) is the smallest positive number that is a multiple of two or more numbers.

Method 1: Finding the LCM by Listing Multiples

This method is suitable for smaller numbers.

  1. List several positive multiples of each number.
  2. Find the smallest number that appears in every list.
  3. That number is the LCM.
  • Example: Find LCM(8, 12)
    • Multiples of 8: 8, 16, 24, 32, 40, 48, ...
    • Multiples of 12: 12, 24, 36, 48, ...
    • The smallest positive number appearing in both lists is 24.
    • So, LCM(8, 12) = 24.

Method 2: Finding the LCM by Prime Factorization

This method is highly effective for any size numbers.

  1. Write the prime factorization of each number.
  2. For each prime factor that appears in any of the factorizations (even if not shared by all numbers), take the largest exponent that appears for that prime factor.
  3. Multiply these prime factors (with their chosen exponents) together. The product is the LCM.
  • Example: Find LCM(12, 18) (from Checkpoint)
    • Prime factorization of 12: 12 = 2^2 * 3^1
    • Prime factorization of 18: 18 = 2^1 * 3^2
    • Prime factors involved are 2 and 3.
    • Largest exponent for 2: 2^2 (from 12)
    • Largest exponent for 3: 3^2 (from 18)
    • LCM(12, 18) = 2^2 * 3^2 = 4 * 9 = 36.

Applications of GCF and LCM

  • GCF applications: Used when you need to divide or simplify. The most common use in fractions is to simplify fractions by dividing both the numerator and denominator by their GCF.

  • LCM applications: Used when you need to combine or find a common meeting point. The most common use in fractions is to find the Least Common Denominator (LCD) when adding or subtracting fractions, which is simply the LCM of the denominators.

  • Example: Scheduling Problem (LCM)

    • Two buses leave at 8:00 a.m. Bus A returns every 20 minutes, Bus B every 30 minutes. When will they next be at the station together?
    • This is an LCM problem because we're looking for the first time (smallest common multiple) both events align.
    • Find LCM(20, 30).
      • 20 = 2^2 * 5
      • 30 = 2 * 3 * 5
      • Largest exponents: 2^2 (from 20), 3^1 (from 30), 5^1 (from both).
      • LCM(20, 30) = 2^2 * 3 * 5 = 4 * 3 * 5 = 60.
    • They will both be at the station in 60 minutes.
    • 60 minutes after 8:00 a.m. is 9:00 a.m.

Important Distinctions and Relationships

  • Do not confuse GCF and LCM!
    • The GCF is always less than or equal to the smallest of the numbers. It's about finding what they share in terms of division.
    • The LCM is always greater than or equal to the largest of the numbers. It's about finding a common point where their multiples meet.
  • Relationship Formula: For any two numbers a and b, the product of their GCF and LCM is equal to the product of the numbers themselves: GCF(a,b) * LCM(a,b) = a * b
    • Example: For 12 and 18: GCF(12,18) = 6, LCM(12,18) = 36.
      • 6 * 36 = 216
      • 12 * 18 = 216
      • The relationship holds true!

Review Questions

  1. What is the primary purpose of understanding GCF and LCM in the context of working with fractions? Give an example for each.
  2. Explain the difference between a "factor" and a "multiple." Provide three factors and three multiples for the number 15.
  3. Describe the two main methods for finding the GCF of two numbers. Which method would you prefer for very large numbers, and why?
  4. You are given two numbers, 10 and 15.
    • Find their GCF using the listing factors method.
    • Find their LCM using the listing multiples method.
    • Verify the relationship GCF(a,b) * LCM(a,b) = a * b using these numbers.
  5. Imagine a scenario where you have two different-sized pieces of fabric, one 42 inches long and the other 63 inches long. You want to cut both into the largest possible equal-length strips without any waste. Would you use GCF or LCM to solve this problem? Explain your choice and find the length of the strips.
