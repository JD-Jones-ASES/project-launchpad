Greatest Common Factor and Least Common Multiple Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces two essential tools for working with fractions: the Greatest Common Factor (GCF) and the Least Common Multiple (LCM). Understanding factors and multiples allows us to simplify fractions and find common denominators, which are critical operations for adding, subtracting, and comparing fractions.

Key Concepts

  • Factor: A whole number that divides another whole number evenly, leaving no remainder.
  • Multiple: The product of a whole number and any other whole number (0, 1, 2, 3, ...).
  • Greatest Common Factor (GCF): The largest factor that two or more whole numbers share. It's used primarily for simplifying fractions.
    • Method 1: Listing Factors: List all factors for each number, identify common ones, and pick the greatest.
    • Method 2: Prime Factorization: Write each number as a product of its prime factors. For shared prime factors, take the smallest exponent from their occurrences and multiply them together.
  • Least Common Multiple (LCM): The smallest positive number that is a multiple of two or more whole numbers. It's primarily used for finding common denominators when adding or subtracting fractions.
    • Method 1: Listing Multiples: List several multiples for each number, then find the smallest positive multiple that appears in all lists.
    • Method 2: Prime Factorization: Write each number as a product of its prime factors. For every prime factor that appears in any of the factorizations, take the largest exponent from its occurrences and multiply them together.
  • Relationship between GCF and LCM: For any two numbers, a and b, the product of their GCF and LCM is equal to the product of the numbers themselves: GCF(a,b) * LCM(a,b) = a * b.
  • Distinguishing GCF and LCM: The GCF is always less than or equal to the smallest of the numbers, while the LCM is always greater than or equal to the largest of the numbers.

Important Terms

  • Factor: A whole number that divides another whole number without leaving a remainder.
    • Example: The factors of 12 are 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12.
  • Greatest Common Factor (GCF): The largest number that is a factor of two or more given numbers. Often denoted as GCF(a,b) or simply (a,b) in some contexts.
    • Example: The GCF of 12 and 18 is 6.
  • Multiple: The result of multiplying a given whole number by another whole number (e.g., 0, 1, 2, 3, ...).
    • Example: Multiples of 4 are 0, 4, 8, 12, 16, ...
  • Least Common Multiple (LCM): The smallest positive number that is a multiple of two or more given numbers. Often denoted as LCM(a,b) or simply (a,b) in some contexts.
    • Example: The LCM of 4 and 6 is 12.
  • Prime Factorization: The process of breaking down a composite number into its prime number components. This method is highly efficient for finding GCF and LCM of larger numbers.

Detailed Notes

The Importance of GCF and LCM

The concepts of Greatest Common Factor (GCF) and Least Common Multiple (LCM) are foundational for working with fractions.

  • GCF is crucial for simplifying fractions (dividing both numerator and denominator by their greatest common factor).
  • LCM is essential for finding the least common denominator (LCD) when adding, subtracting, or comparing fractions with different denominators.

Understanding Factors and Multiples

  • Factors: When we talk about factors, we're looking for numbers that can divide a given number exactly.
    • Example: To find factors of 12, think of all pairs of numbers that multiply to 12: 1x12, 2x6, 3x4. So, the factors are 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12.
  • Multiples: Multiples are the results of multiplication. You can think of them as skipping counting.
    • Example: Multiples of 4 are 4x0=0, 4x1=4, 4x2=8, 4x3=12, and so on.

Finding the Greatest Common Factor (GCF)

The GCF is the largest number that divides into two or more numbers evenly.

  1. Method: Listing Factors

    • Steps:
      1. List all factors of each number.
      2. Identify and circle the factors that appear in every list (these are the common factors).
      3. The greatest (largest) of these circled factors is the GCF.
    • Example: Find GCF(24, 36)
      • Factors of 24: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24
      • Factors of 36: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 9, 12, 18, 36
      • Common factors: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12
      • The GCF(24, 36) is 12.

  2. Method: Prime Factorization

    • Steps:
      1. Write the prime factorization of each number (express each number as a product of prime numbers).
      2. Identify all prime factors that are common to all factorizations.
      3. For each common prime factor, take the one with the smallest exponent from its occurrences.
      4. Multiply these selected prime factors together. The product is the GCF.
    • Example: Find GCF(60, 90)
      • Prime factorization of 60: 2^2 * 3^1 * 5^1
      • Prime factorization of 90: 2^1 * 3^2 * 5^1
      • Common prime factors are 2, 3, and 5.
      • Smallest exponent for 2: 2^1
      • Smallest exponent for 3: 3^1
      • Smallest exponent for 5: 5^1
      • GCF(60, 90) = 2 * 3 * 5 = 30.

Finding the Least Common Multiple (LCM)

The LCM is the smallest positive number that is a multiple of two or more numbers.

  1. Method: Listing Multiples

    • Steps:
      1. List several positive multiples of each number.
      2. Find the smallest number that appears in every list. This is the LCM.
    • Example: Find LCM(8, 12)
      • Multiples of 8: 8, 16, 24, 32, 40, 48, ...
      • Multiples of 12: 12, 24, 36, 48, ...
      • The smallest positive number in both lists is 24.
      • The LCM(8, 12) is 24.

  2. Method: Prime Factorization

    • Steps:

      1. Write the prime factorization of each number.
      2. For every unique prime factor that appears in any of the factorizations (even if not shared by all numbers), take the one with the largest exponent.
      3. Multiply these selected prime factors together. The product is the LCM.

    • Example: Find LCM(20, 30) (as seen in the scheduling problem)

      • Prime factorization of 20: 2^2 * 5^1
      • Prime factorization of 30: 2^1 * 3^1 * 5^1
      • Unique prime factors are 2, 3, and 5.
      • Largest exponent for 2: 2^2
      • Largest exponent for 3: 3^1 (only appears in 30)
      • Largest exponent for 5: 5^1
      • LCM(20, 30) = 2^2 * 3 * 5 = 4 * 3 * 5 = 60.

    • Example: Find LCM(12, 18) (from checkpoint)

      • Prime factorization of 12: 2^2 * 3^1
      • Prime factorization of 18: 2^1 * 3^2
      • Unique prime factors are 2 and 3.
      • Largest exponent for 2: 2^2
      • Largest exponent for 3: 3^2
      • LCM(12, 18) = 2^2 * 3^2 = 4 * 9 = 36.

Real-World Application (Scheduling Problem)

LCM is often used in problems where events repeat at different intervals and you need to find when they will next occur simultaneously.

  • Problem: Bus A returns every 20 minutes, Bus B every 30 minutes. Both leave at 8:00 a.m. When will they next be at the station together?
  • Solution: This is an LCM problem because we're looking for the smallest common time in the future.
    • LCM(20, 30) = 60 minutes.
    • They will both be at the station again in 60 minutes, which is 1 hour after 8:00 a.m., so at 9:00 a.m.

Important Considerations and Relationship

  • GCF vs. LCM:
    • GCF is for dividing into equal parts (e.g., simplifying fractions, dividing objects into largest possible equal groups). The GCF is always less than or equal to the smallest of the original numbers.
    • LCM is for combining different-sized parts or finding when events align (e.g., finding a common denominator, scheduling). The LCM is always greater than or equal to the largest of the original numbers.
  • Relationship Formula: For any two positive integers a and b: GCF(a, b) * LCM(a, b) = a * b This formula can be a useful way to check your answers or find one value if the other is known.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words why GCF is useful for simplifying fractions and why LCM is useful for adding fractions.
  2. Describe the two main methods for finding the GCF of two numbers. Which method do you find more efficient for larger numbers and why?
  3. Describe the two main methods for finding the LCM of two numbers. Which method do you find more efficient for larger numbers and why?
  4. Given the numbers 15 and 25, find their Greatest Common Factor (GCF) using both the listing factors method and the prime factorization method.
  5. Given the numbers 6 and 9, find their Least Common Multiple (LCM) using both the listing multiples method and the prime factorization method. How does your answer relate to the "GCF(a,b) * LCM(a,b) = a * b" property?
