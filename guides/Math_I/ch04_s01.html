Multiplying Fractions Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental process of multiplying fractions. Unlike adding or subtracting fractions, finding a common denominator is not necessary. The core method involves multiplying numerators together and denominators together, with a valuable shortcut called "cross canceling" that simplifies calculations before the final multiplication.

Key Concepts

  • Multiplying Fractions Rule: To multiply two fractions, multiply their numerators to get the new numerator, and multiply their denominators to get the new denominator. The formula is $\frac{a}{b} \times \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a \times c}{b \times d}$.
  • No Common Denominator Needed: A significant advantage of multiplying fractions is that you do not need to find a common denominator, simplifying the initial setup compared to addition or subtraction.
  • Cross Canceling: This is a powerful shortcut where you can divide a numerator and an opposite denominator by their greatest common factor before multiplying. This keeps the numbers smaller throughout the calculation, making multiplication easier and often eliminating the need for extensive simplification at the end.
  • Multiplying with Whole Numbers: When multiplying a whole number by a fraction, always write the whole number as a fraction over 1 (e.g., $5 = \frac{5}{1}$). This allows you to apply the standard fraction multiplication rule.
  • Simplifying the Answer: After multiplying, always simplify the resulting fraction to its simplest form by dividing both the numerator and denominator by their greatest common factor.

Important Terms

  • Cross Canceling: A shortcut used before multiplying fractions where a numerator from one fraction and a denominator from the other fraction (diagonally opposite) are divided by a common factor. This process helps reduce the numbers involved, making subsequent multiplication and final simplification easier.

Detailed Notes

The Basics of Multiplying Fractions

Multiplying fractions is generally considered simpler than adding or subtracting them because you don't need to find a common denominator. The process is straightforward:

  1. Multiply the Numerators: The top numbers of the fractions.
  2. Multiply the Denominators: The bottom numbers of the fractions.
  3. Simplify: Reduce the resulting fraction to its simplest form if possible.

Property of Multiplying Fractions: For any fractions $\frac{a}{b}$ and $\frac{c}{d}$ (where $b \neq 0$ and $d \neq 0$): $$ \frac{a}{b} \times \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a \times c}{b \times d} $$

Example: Basic Fraction Multiplication To multiply $\frac{2}{3} \times \frac{4}{5}$: $$ \frac{2}{3} \times \frac{4}{5} = \frac{2 \times 4}{3 \times 5} = \frac{8}{15} $$ Since $8$ and $15$ have no common factors other than $1$, the fraction $\frac{8}{15}$ is already in its simplest form.

Commutative Property: Remember that the commutative property of multiplication applies. You can multiply the numerators and denominators in any order, and the factors within the numerators or denominators can also be multiplied in any order.

Using Cross Canceling for Simpler Calculations

Cross canceling is an extremely useful shortcut that can save you a lot of effort when multiplying fractions.

What is Cross Canceling? Before you multiply the numerators straight across and the denominators straight across, you look for common factors between any numerator and any denominator that are diagonally opposite each other. If you find a common factor, you divide both the numerator and the denominator by that factor.

Benefits of Cross Canceling:

  • Smaller Numbers: By dividing out common factors early, you work with smaller numbers, which makes the multiplication step much easier.
  • Less Simplification Later: Often, after cross canceling, the resulting fraction is already in simplest form, or requires very little further simplification.

Example: Cross Cancel Before Multiplying Multiply $\frac{3}{8} \times \frac{4}{9}$ using cross canceling.

  1. Identify Common Factors Diagonally:

    • Look at $3$ (numerator) and $9$ (opposite denominator). Both share a common factor of $3$.
    • Look at $4$ (numerator) and $8$ (opposite denominator). Both share a common factor of $4$.

  2. Perform the Canceling:

    • Divide $3$ by $3$ to get $1$. Divide $9$ by $3$ to get $3$.
    • Divide $4$ by $4$ to get $1$. Divide $8$ by $4$ to get $2$.

    The expression now looks like this (mentally or by lightly crossing out and writing the new numbers): $$ \frac{\cancel{3}^{1}}{\cancel{8}{2}} \times \frac{\cancel{4}^{1}}{\cancel{9}{3}} $$

  3. Multiply the New Numbers: $$ \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1 \times 1}{2 \times 3} = \frac{1}{6} $$ The answer $\frac{1}{6}$ is in simplest form.

Multiplying Whole Numbers by Fractions

When a whole number is involved in a multiplication problem with a fraction, you must first convert the whole number into a fraction.

Steps:

  1. Convert Whole Number: Write the whole number as a fraction by placing it over $1$ (e.g., $5 = \frac{5}{1}$).
  2. Multiply Fractions: Now you have two fractions, so follow the standard multiplication rules, including using cross canceling if applicable.
  3. Simplify: Ensure your final answer is in simplest form, converting improper fractions to mixed numbers if appropriate.

Caution: When converting a whole number to a fraction, always place the whole number in the numerator over $1$. Do not put the whole number in the denominator by mistake (e.g., $5 \neq \frac{1}{5}$).

Example: Whole Number Times a Fraction Multiply $5 \times \frac{2}{3}$.

  1. Convert $5$ to a fraction: $5 = \frac{5}{1}$.
  2. Multiply: $$ \frac{5}{1} \times \frac{2}{3} = \frac{5 \times 2}{1 \times 3} = \frac{10}{3} $$
  3. Simplify: $\frac{10}{3}$ is an improper fraction. Convert it to a mixed number: $10 \div 3 = 3$ with a remainder of $1$. So, $\frac{10}{3} = 3\frac{1}{3}$.

Applying Fraction Multiplication to Word Problems

Fraction multiplication is used in various real-world scenarios, such as calculating parts of a whole, areas, or distances.

Example: Garden Area A rectangular garden measures $\frac{3}{4}$ yard long and $\frac{2}{3}$ yard wide. What is the area of the garden?

  1. Identify Formula: The area of a rectangle is length $\times$ width.
  2. Set Up: $A = \frac{3}{4} \times \frac{2}{3}$.
  3. Solve (using cross canceling):
    • Cancel the $3$ in the numerator with the $3$ in the denominator. Both become $1$.
    • Cancel the $2$ in the numerator with the $4$ in the denominator. $2$ becomes $1$, $4$ becomes $2$. $$ A = \frac{\cancel{3}^{1}}{\cancel{4}{2}} \times \frac{\cancel{2}^{1}}{\cancel{3}{1}} = \frac{1 \times 1}{2 \times 1} = \frac{1}{2} $$
  4. Units: The area is $\frac{1}{2}$ square yard.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental rule for multiplying fractions. How does it differ from the process of adding or subtracting fractions?
  2. What is "cross canceling," and why is it a useful technique when multiplying fractions? Provide an example where cross canceling would simplify the problem.
  3. Describe the steps you would take to multiply a whole number by a fraction. What is a common mistake to avoid in this process?
  4. If you multiply two proper fractions (fractions where the numerator is smaller than the denominator), will the product always be smaller than both original fractions? Explain why or why not.
  5. A recipe calls for $\frac{3}{4}$ cup of flour. If you want to make only $\frac{1}{2}$ of the recipe, how much flour do you need? Show your work using fraction multiplication.
