Multiplying Fractions Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental process of multiplying fractions, highlighting its simplicity compared to addition or subtraction as it doesn't require a common denominator. It covers the core multiplication rule, the beneficial shortcut of cross canceling, and how to approach problems involving whole numbers or real-world scenarios.

Key Concepts

  • Multiplication Rule for Fractions: To multiply two fractions, you simply multiply their numerators together and multiply their denominators together. The result is a new fraction formed by these products.
  • Cross Canceling: This is a powerful shortcut used before multiplying. If a numerator in one fraction and a denominator in the other fraction share a common factor, you can divide both by that factor. This reduces the numbers you have to multiply, making the calculation easier and often eliminating the need to simplify at the end.
  • Multiplying by Whole Numbers: When multiplying a fraction by a whole number, always rewrite the whole number as a fraction by placing it over 1 (e.g., 5 becomes 5/1). This allows you to apply the standard fraction multiplication rule.
  • Simplifying the Answer: After multiplying, always ensure your final answer is in simplest form. Cross canceling helps with this, but if you don't cross cancel, you'll need to divide the numerator and denominator by their greatest common factor at the end.

Important Terms

  • Cross Canceling: A method used to simplify fractions before multiplying by dividing a numerator from one fraction and a denominator from the other fraction by a common factor.
  • Numerator: The top number in a fraction, representing the number of parts being considered.
  • Denominator: The bottom number in a fraction, representing the total number of equal parts into which the whole is divided.
  • Simplest Form: A fraction is in simplest form (or lowest terms) when its numerator and denominator have no common factors other than 1.
  • Common Factor: A number that divides exactly into two or more other numbers.

Detailed Notes

The Basic Rule for Multiplying Fractions

Multiplying fractions is straightforward because you don't need to find a common denominator, unlike with addition or subtraction. The rule is simple: To multiply fractions a/b and c/d (where b ≠ 0 and d ≠ 0): Multiply the numerators: a × c Multiply the denominators: b × d The resulting fraction is (a × c) / (b × d).

Example: To multiply 2/3 * 4/5:

  1. Multiply the numerators: 2 × 4 = 8
  2. Multiply the denominators: 3 × 5 = 15
  3. The product is 8/15. Since 8 and 15 share no common factors other than 1, 8/15 is already in simplest form.

Commutative Property Note: Remember that the order in which you multiply numbers doesn't change the result. This applies to the numerators and denominators as well, meaning (a/b) * (c/d) is the same as (c/d) * (a/b).

The Power of Cross Canceling

Cross canceling is an extremely useful shortcut that makes multiplying fractions much easier, especially with larger numbers. It involves simplifying before you multiply.

How to Cross Cancel:

  1. Look at the numerator of one fraction and the denominator of the other fraction.
  2. Identify if they share a common factor (a number that divides evenly into both).
  3. Divide both the numerator and the denominator by that common factor.
  4. Repeat this process with the other numerator and denominator pair if possible.

Example: To multiply 3/8 * 4/9 using cross canceling:

  1. Look at 3 (numerator) and 9 (denominator): Both are divisible by 3.
    • 3 ÷ 3 = 1
    • 9 ÷ 3 = 3
    • (The fractions now conceptually look like 1/8 * 4/3)
  2. Look at 4 (numerator) and 8 (denominator): Both are divisible by 4.
    • 4 ÷ 4 = 1
    • 8 ÷ 4 = 2
    • (The fractions now conceptually look like 1/2 * 1/3)
  3. Now, multiply the new, simplified numerators and denominators:
    • 1 × 1 = 1
    • 2 × 3 = 6
    • The product is 1/6. This method keeps your numbers small and often results in an answer that is already in simplest form.

Checkpoint Example: Multiply 5/6 * 3/10 using cross canceling.

  • 5 and 10 share a common factor of 5. (5÷5=1, 10÷5=2)
  • 3 and 6 share a common factor of 3. (3÷3=1, 6÷3=2)
  • After canceling, you have 1/2 * 1/2.
  • Multiplying gives (1×1) / (2×2) = 1/4.

Handling Whole Numbers

When you need to multiply a whole number by a fraction, the first step is always to convert the whole number into a fraction.

How to Convert a Whole Number to a Fraction: Place the whole number over 1. For example, 5 becomes 5/1. This doesn't change the value of the number, but it allows you to apply the fraction multiplication rule.

Caution: Never put the whole number in the denominator by mistake. This is a common error!

Example: To multiply 5 * 2/3:

  1. Rewrite 5 as a fraction: 5/1.
  2. Now you have 5/1 * 2/3.
  3. Multiply the numerators: 5 × 2 = 10.
  4. Multiply the denominators: 1 × 3 = 3.
  5. The product is 10/3.
  6. If needed, convert the improper fraction to a mixed number: 10 ÷ 3 = 3 with a remainder of 1, so 3 1/3.

Applying Fraction Multiplication: Word Problems

Fraction multiplication is frequently used to solve real-world problems, especially when you need to find a "fraction of a fraction" or a "fraction of a whole number."

Example: Garden Area A rectangular garden is 3/4 yard long and 2/3 yard wide. What is its area?

  1. Identify: Area of a rectangle = length × width.
  2. Write: A = 3/4 * 2/3.
  3. Solve:
    • Cross cancel 3 with 3 (3÷3=1).
    • Cross cancel 2 with 4 (2÷2=1, 4÷2=2).
    • The problem becomes 1/2 * 1/1.
    • Multiply: (1×1) / (2×1) = 1/2.
  4. Answer: The area of the garden is 1/2 square yard.

Checkpoint Example: A ribbon is 7/8 meter long. You use 2/7 of it. How many meters of ribbon did you use?

  • This is asking for 2/7 of 7/8. So, 2/7 * 7/8.
  • Cross cancel 7 with 7 (7÷7=1).
  • Cross cancel 2 with 8 (2÷2=1, 8÷2=4).
  • Multiply: 1/1 * 1/4 = 1/4.
  • You used 1/4 meter of ribbon.

Review Questions

  1. Explain why multiplying fractions is generally considered simpler than adding or subtracting them.
  2. Describe the process of cross canceling. Why is it a useful shortcut when multiplying fractions? Provide an example where cross canceling would be beneficial.
  3. You are asked to multiply a whole number, 6, by the fraction 3/4. What is the very first step you should take, and why is it important?
  4. A recipe calls for 1/2 cup of sugar, but you only want to make 2/3 of the recipe. How much sugar do you need? Show your work using fraction multiplication.
  5. If you multiply two fractions, a/b and c/d, and do not use cross canceling, what must you remember to do as a final step to ensure your answer is complete?
