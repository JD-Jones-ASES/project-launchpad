Study Guide: Variables and Algebraic Expressions

Overview

This section introduces the foundational concepts of algebra, moving beyond simple numbers to incorporate letters that represent changing values, known as variables. You will learn the essential vocabulary of algebraic expressions, including terms, coefficients, and constants, and understand the critical difference between an expression and an equation, preparing you to write basic algebraic expressions from word problems.

Key Concepts

  • Variables: Letters or symbols (e.g., $x, y, n, t$) that represent unknown or changeable numerical values. They are "variable" because the value they stand for can vary.
  • Algebraic Expression: A combination of numbers, variables, and mathematical operations (such as addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division). It does not contain an equal sign.
  • Term: A single number, a single variable, or a product of numbers and variables within an algebraic expression. Terms are separated from each other by addition ($+$) or subtraction ($-$) signs.
  • Coefficient: The numerical factor that is multiplied by a variable in a term. If a variable appears alone (e.g., $x$), its coefficient is understood to be $1$.
  • Constant: A term in an algebraic expression that does not contain any variables. Its value is fixed and does not change.
  • Expression vs. Equation: An expression is a mathematical phrase without an equal sign (e.g., $2x + 7$). An equation is a mathematical sentence that states two expressions are equal, and therefore contains an equal sign (e.g., $2x + 7 = 15$).
  • Writing Algebraic Expressions: The process of translating verbal descriptions or real-world scenarios into symbolic mathematical statements using variables, numbers, and operations.

Important Terms

  • Variable: A letter or symbol (like $x$, $y$, $n$, or $t$) used to represent an unknown or changeable numerical value.
  • Algebraic Expression: A mathematical phrase consisting of numbers, variables, and operation symbols, but no equal sign.
  • Term: A component of an algebraic expression, which can be a single number, a single variable, or a product of numbers and variables. Terms are separated by $+$ or $-$ signs.
  • Coefficient: The numerical part of a term that is multiplied by a variable. For example, in $5x$, the coefficient is $5$. If a variable has no number in front of it, the coefficient is $1$.
  • Constant: A term in an algebraic expression that is a number by itself, without any variables. Its value is fixed.
  • Equation: A mathematical statement showing that two expressions are equal, always containing an equal sign.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Algebra

In arithmetic, you primarily work with specific numbers. Algebra expands on this by introducing variables, which are letters or symbols that stand for numbers whose values can change or are unknown. This allows us to write more general mathematical statements and solve a wider range of problems.

Understanding Variables and Algebraic Expressions

  • Variables: Think of a variable as a placeholder for a number. For instance, if you don't know how many apples are in a basket, you might use $a$ to represent that unknown quantity. Common variables include $x$, $y$, $n$, and $t$.
  • Algebraic Expressions: When you combine these variables with numbers and mathematical operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division), you create an algebraic expression.
    • Examples:
      • $3x + 5$ (3 times some number $x$, plus 5)
      • $n4 - 2$ (some number $n$ times 4, minus 2) - Conventionally written as $4n - 2$
      • $7y$ (7 times some number $y$)

Anatomy of an Algebraic Expression: Terms, Coefficients, and Constants

Let's break down the components of an expression using an example: $5x + 3$.

  • Terms: These are the individual parts of an expression separated by $+$ or $-$ signs.
    • In $5x + 3$, the terms are $5x$ and $3$.
  • Coefficient: This is the numerical factor multiplied by a variable in a term.
    • In the term $5x$, the coefficient is $5$.
    • If a variable stands alone, like $x$ or $y$, its coefficient is understood to be $1$ (e.g., $x$ is the same as $1x$).
  • Constant: This is a term that consists only of a number, without any variables. Its value remains unchanged.
    • In $5x + 3$, the constant is $3$.

Example: Identifying Parts of an Expression For the expression $4n + 9$:

  1. Terms: The terms are $4n$ and $9$ (separated by the $+$ sign).
  2. Coefficient: The coefficient of the variable term $4n$ is $4$.
  3. Constant: The constant term is $9$.

Expression vs. Equation: A Key Distinction

It's crucial to understand the difference between an algebraic expression and an equation:

  • Algebraic Expression: This is like a mathematical "phrase." It describes a value but doesn't state that it's equal to anything.
    • Example: $2x + 7$
  • Equation: This is like a mathematical "sentence." It states that two expressions are equal to each other, and therefore always contains an equal sign ($=$).
    • Example: $2x + 7 = 15$

Example: Expression or Equation?

  1. $3y - 8$: This has no equal sign, so it is an expression.
  2. $3y - 8 = 10$: This has an equal sign, so it is an equation.

Checkpoint: Is $7 + 2x$ an expression or an equation?

  • Answer: It is an expression (because it has no equal sign).

Writing Algebraic Expressions

A fundamental skill in algebra is translating real-world situations or verbal descriptions into algebraic expressions.

Example: Writing an Algebraic Expression (Simple Subtraction) A store has $250$ items in stock. After returning $r$ defective items, write an expression for the number of items remaining.

  • Solution: You start with $250$ items and subtract the $r$ items returned.
    • Expression: $250 - r$

Example: Earning Money (Multiplication and Addition) Maria earns $$2$ for every hour she helps at home, plus a $$3$ weekly allowance. Write an expression for the total amount she earns in a week if she works $h$ hours.

  1. Read: Understand the components: $$2$ per hour, $$3$ flat allowance.
  2. Identify: Let $h$ represent the number of hours worked.
    • Earnings from hours: $$2$ multiplied by the number of hours, which is $2h$.
    • Flat allowance: $$3$.
  3. Write: Combine these parts using the appropriate operation.
    • Expression: $2h + 3$
  4. Check: (Optional but helpful) If $h = 5$ hours, total earnings would be $2(5) + 3 = 10 + 3 = 13$. This makes sense.

Checkpoint: Write an expression for the cost of $n$ notebooks at $$1.50$ each plus a $$2$ binder.

  • Answer: $1.50n + 2$

Important Conventions and Cautions

  • Order of Factors: When a number is multiplied by a variable, the convention is to write the number first (the coefficient), followed by the variable. So, write $5x$, not $x5$. While $x5$ has the same value, $5x$ is the standard and preferred notation.
  • Multiplication vs. Addition: A common mistake is to confuse multiplication and addition. Placing a number directly next to a variable (e.g., $5x$) implies multiplication ($5 \times x$). It does NOT mean addition ($5 + x$).

Review Questions

  1. Explain, in your own words, what a "variable" is and why it is a fundamental concept in algebra. Provide an example of how a variable helps represent a real-world situation.
  2. Differentiate between an "algebraic expression" and a "term." Give an example of an expression and then identify its individual terms.
  3. For the expression $10a^2 - 3b + 15$:
    • Identify all the terms.
    • State the coefficient of the term with the variable $b$.
    • Identify the constant term.
  4. Why is the presence or absence of an equal sign a critical factor in determining whether a mathematical statement is an "expression" or an "equation"? Provide one example of each.
  5. Translate the following verbal descriptions into algebraic expressions:
    • a) The cost of $m$ magazines if each magazine costs $$4.99$.
    • b) The number of remaining cookies if you started with $24$ and ate $c$ cookies.
    • c) Maria's age, if she is $5$ years older than twice her brother's age, $b$.
