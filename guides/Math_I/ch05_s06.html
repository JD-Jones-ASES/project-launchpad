Comparing and Ordering Rational Numbers: A Study Guide

Overview

This section teaches you how to compare and order rational numbers, which can appear in various forms like fractions, decimals, and negative numbers. The core strategy involves converting all numbers to the same form, typically decimals, before making comparisons. Special attention is given to understanding how negative numbers are ordered on a number line.

Key Concepts

  • Rational Number Definition: A rational number is any number that can be expressed as a fraction $\frac{a}{b}$, where $a$ and $b$ are integers and $b \neq 0$. This broad category includes fractions, terminating decimals, repeating decimals, integers, and their negative counterparts.
  • Unified Form for Comparison: To effectively compare rational numbers presented in different formats (e.g., a fraction and a decimal), it is crucial to convert them all into a single, consistent form. Decimals are often the preferred choice due to their ease of comparison.
  • Comparing Positive Numbers: Once all positive rational numbers are in decimal form, you compare them by aligning their decimal points and comparing digits from left to right, starting with the largest place value.
  • Comparing Negative Numbers: When comparing negative rational numbers, remember their position on a number line. A number farther to the left on the number line is considered smaller (lesser). This means that for negative numbers, the one with the greater absolute value is actually the smaller number.
  • Efficiency of Decimal Conversion: Converting numbers to decimals is generally the fastest and most straightforward method for comparison because it allows for direct, digit-by-digit assessment after aligning decimal points.

Important Terms

  • Rational Number: Any number that can be written as a fraction $\frac{a}{b}$, where $a$ and $b$ are integers and $b \neq 0$. This includes fractions, terminating decimals, repeating decimals, integers, and their negatives.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Rational Numbers

Before comparing, it's essential to understand what a rational number is. A rational number is a number that can be expressed as a fraction $\frac{a}{b}$, where $a$ and $b$ are integers and $b$ is not zero. This definition encompasses:

  • Fractions: Like $\frac{1}{2}$, $\frac{3}{4}$, etc.
  • Terminating Decimals: Decimals that end, like $0.5$, $0.25$, $0.75$. These can be written as fractions (e.g., $0.5 = \frac{1}{2}$).
  • Repeating Decimals: Decimals that have a pattern of digits that repeats infinitely, like $0.333...$ or $0.\overline{3}$ (which is $\frac{1}{3}$).
  • Integers: Whole numbers, including zero and negative whole numbers, like $-3, 0, 5$. These can be written as fractions (e.g., $5 = \frac{5}{1}$).
  • Negatives of all the above: Such as $-\frac{1}{2}$, $-0.75$, $-0.\overline{3}$, $-5$.

General Strategy for Comparing and Ordering

The fundamental principle for comparing and ordering rational numbers that are in different forms is to convert all numbers to the same form.

  1. Choose a Consistent Form: The most common and often easiest form to convert to is a decimal.
  2. Convert: Transform all fractions into their decimal equivalents. Decimals themselves do not need conversion.
  3. Compare:
    • For positive numbers: Compare them as you would any other decimals, lining up decimal points and comparing digit by digit from left to right.
    • For negative numbers: Remember that on a number line, numbers further to the left are smaller. This means that a negative number with a larger absolute value (further from zero) is actually the smaller number. For instance, $-0.5$ is less than $-0.3$ because $-0.5$ is farther to the left on the number line.
    • Mixed positive and negative: All negative numbers are less than zero, and all positive numbers are greater than zero.

Why Decimals are Preferred

Converting to decimals is often the fastest and most efficient method because:

  • You can easily line up decimal points.
  • Comparing digits by place value (tenths, hundredths, thousandths, etc.) becomes straightforward.

Examples Walkthrough

Example 1: Comparing a Fraction and a Decimal Compare $\frac{5}{8}$ and $0.62$.

  1. Convert: Convert the fraction $\frac{5}{8}$ to a decimal. $5 \div 8 = 0.625$.
  2. Compare: Now compare $0.625$ and $0.62$.
    • Tenths place: $6 = 6$.
    • Hundredths place: $2 = 2$.
    • Thousandths place: $5 > 0$. (Imagine $0.62$ as $0.620$). Therefore, $0.625 > 0.62$, which means $\frac{5}{8} > 0.62$.

Example 2: Ordering a Mixed Set of Positive Rational Numbers Order from least to greatest: $\frac{3}{4}$, $0.7$, $\frac{4}{5}$.

  1. Convert: Convert each number to a decimal.
    • $\frac{3}{4} = 0.75$
    • $0.7 = 0.70$ (adding a zero helps with comparison)
    • $\frac{4}{5} = 0.80$
  2. Compare and Order: Compare the decimals: $0.70 < 0.75 < 0.80$. Therefore, the order from least to greatest is $0.7 < \frac{3}{4} < \frac{4}{5}$.

Example 3: Comparing Negative Rational Numbers Compare $-0.4$ and $-\frac{3}{8}$.

  1. Convert: Convert $-\frac{3}{8}$ to a decimal. $\frac{3}{8} = 0.375$, so $-\frac{3}{8} = -0.375$.
  2. Compare: Now compare $-0.4$ and $-0.375$.
    • Think about a number line: $-0.4$ is equivalent to $-0.400$.
    • $-0.400$ is farther to the left on the number line than $-0.375$.
    • Alternatively, the absolute value of $-0.4$ is $0.4$, and the absolute value of $-0.375$ is $0.375$. Since $0.4 > 0.375$, the negative number with the larger absolute value ($-0.4$) is the lesser number. Therefore, $-0.4 < -\frac{3}{8}$.

Real-World Application: Choosing the Better Deal

This involves comparing prices given in different forms. Problem: Store A sells juice for $\frac{3}{4}$ of a dollar per bottle. Store B sells the same juice for $0.72 per bottle. Which store has the lower price?

  1. Read: Understand the task: compare two prices given as a fraction and a decimal.
  2. Identify: The strategy is to convert both prices to the same form (decimals) and then compare.
  3. Write: Convert $\frac{3}{4}$ to a decimal: $\frac{3}{4} = 0.75$. So, Store A sells juice for $0.75.
  4. Solve: Compare $0.75$ (Store A) and $0.72$ (Store B). Since $0.72 < 0.75$, Store B has the lower price.
  5. Check: Confirm that $0.72 is indeed less than $0.75. Conclusion: Store B ($0.72) has the lower price.

Caution with Negative Numbers

It is critical to remember that for negative numbers, a larger absolute value corresponds to a smaller number. For example:

  • $|-0.5| = 0.5$ and $|-0.3| = 0.3$.
  • Even though $0.5 > 0.3$, when they are negative, $-0.5 < -0.3$. This is because $-0.5$ is farther away from zero in the negative direction, making it a smaller value.

Summary of Comparing and Ordering Rational Numbers

  • Always convert all numbers to the same form (decimals are usually best) before comparing.
  • For positive rational numbers, compare them digit by digit after aligning decimal points.
  • For negative rational numbers, the number with the greater absolute value is the lesser number (it's farther left on the number line).
  • Visualizing numbers on a number line can be very helpful, especially when dealing with both positive and negative rational numbers.
