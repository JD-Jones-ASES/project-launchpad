Comparing and Ordering Rational Numbers Study Guide

Overview

This section teaches you how to compare and order rational numbers that might be presented in different forms, such as fractions, decimals, and negative numbers. The core strategy involves converting all numbers to a common format, usually decimals, to make direct comparison easier. You'll learn specific techniques for comparing positive numbers, negative numbers, and how to apply these skills in practical situations.

Key Concepts

  • Rational Numbers: Numbers that can be expressed as a fraction $a/b$, where $a$ and $b$ are integers and $b$ is not zero. This broad category includes fractions, terminating decimals, repeating decimals, integers, and their negative counterparts.
  • Standard Strategy for Comparison: To compare or order rational numbers in different forms, the most effective method is to convert them all into the same form.
  • Preferred Form (Decimals): Converting all numbers to decimals is often the fastest and easiest way to compare them because decimal points can be aligned, and digits compared place by place.
  • Comparing Positive Numbers: Once converted to decimals, positive rational numbers are compared just like whole numbers, from left to right, digit by digit.
  • Comparing Negative Numbers: When comparing negative rational numbers, remember that numbers farther to the left on a number line are smaller (less). This means a negative number with a larger absolute value is actually a smaller number (e.g., $-0.5 < -0.3$).
  • Number Line Visualization: A number line is a helpful tool to visualize the relative order of both positive and negative rational numbers.

Important Terms

  • Rational Number: Any number that can be written as a fraction $a/b$, where $a$ and $b$ are integers and $b$ is not equal to 0. This includes fractions, terminating decimals, repeating decimals, integers, and their negative values.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Comparing Rational Numbers

You've previously learned to compare numbers within specific categories, like comparing one fraction to another fraction, or one decimal to another decimal. This section expands on that by teaching you how to compare and order numbers that come in various forms all at once. This includes fractions, decimals, integers, and their negative equivalents. The key to tackling such mixed sets is to transform them into a single, consistent format before making any comparisons.

What is a Rational Number?

Before we dive into comparing, let's firmly define what a rational number is. A rational number is any number that can be expressed as a quotient (or fraction) of two integers, $a/b$, where $b$ is not zero. This definition is very inclusive:

  • Fractions: Like $1/2$, $3/4$, or $-5/8$.
  • Terminating Decimals: Decimals that end, like $0.5$ (which is $1/2$) or $0.75$ (which is $3/4$).
  • Repeating Decimals: Decimals that have a pattern of digits that repeats infinitely, like $0.333...$ (which is $1/3$) or $0.142857142857...$ (which is $1/7$).
  • Integers: Whole numbers and their opposites, like $3$, $-7$, or $0$. These can be written as fractions (e.g., $3 = 3/1$).

The Strategy: Converting to a Common Form

The fundamental principle for comparing rational numbers in different forms is simple: convert all numbers to the same form. While you could convert everything to fractions, it is generally much faster and easier to convert everything to decimals.

Why decimals are preferred:

  • You can easily line up the decimal points.
  • You can compare digits place by place, starting from the leftmost digit.
  • It eliminates the need for finding common denominators as you would with fractions.

Steps for Comparing Rational Numbers:

  1. Convert: Transform all numbers into their decimal form.
  2. Compare Positives: If all numbers are positive, compare them directly by lining up the decimal points and comparing digits from left to right.
  3. Compare Negatives (and Mixed): If there are negative numbers involved, remember that numbers further to the left on the number line are smaller. This means a larger absolute value for a negative number implies a smaller number overall.

Examples of Comparing and Ordering

Example 1: Comparing a Fraction and a Decimal Let's compare $5/8$ and $0.62$.

  1. Convert: Change $5/8$ to a decimal. Divide 5 by 8: $5 \div 8 = 0.625$.
  2. Compare: Now we compare $0.625$ and $0.62$. To do this, it helps to make them have the same number of decimal places by adding a zero to $0.62$: $0.620$.
    • Tenths place: $6 = 6$
    • Hundredths place: $2 = 2$
    • Thousandths place: $5 > 0$ Therefore, $0.625 > 0.620$, which means $5/8 > 0.62$.

Example 2: Ordering a Mixed Set of Rational Numbers Order the following from least to greatest: $3/4$, $0.7$, $4/5$.

  1. Convert:
    • $3/4 = 0.75$
    • $0.7$ (already a decimal, can write as $0.70$ for consistency)
    • $4/5 = 0.80$
  2. Compare and Order: Now we compare $0.70$, $0.75$, and $0.80$. Clearly, $0.70 < 0.75 < 0.80$. So, in their original forms: $0.7 < 3/4 < 4/5$.

(Checkpoint Answer: For $2/3$, $0.65$, $7/10$, convert: $2/3 \approx 0.666...$, $0.65$, $7/10 = 0.70$. So, $0.65 < 2/3 < 7/10$.)

Example 3: Comparing Negative Rational Numbers Compare $-0.4$ and $-3/8$.

  1. Convert: Change $-3/8$ to a decimal. We know $3/8 = 0.375$, so $-3/8 = -0.375$.
  2. Compare: Now we compare $-0.4$ and $-0.375$. It helps to think about the number line.
    • $-0.4$ can be written as $-0.400$.
    • Imagine a number line. As you move to the left, numbers become smaller.
    • $-0.400$ is further to the left than $-0.375$. Therefore, $-0.4 < -0.375$, which means $-0.4 < -3/8$.

Important Caution about Negative Numbers: When dealing with negative numbers, the number with the larger absolute value is actually the lesser (smaller) number. For instance, $|-0.5| = 0.5$ and $|-0.3| = 0.3$. Since $0.5 > 0.3$, it means $-0.5 < -0.3$. Always visualize or think in terms of the number line.

(Checkpoint Answer: For $-1/2$ and $-0.45$, convert $-1/2 = -0.5$. Comparing $-0.5$ and $-0.45$: $-0.5$ is further left on the number line than $-0.45$. So, $-1/2 < -0.45$.)

Example 4: Real-World Application - Choosing the Better Deal Store A sells juice for $3/4$ of a dollar per bottle. Store B sells the same juice for $0.72 per bottle. Which store has the lower price?

  1. Read and Identify: The goal is to compare two prices in different forms ($3/4$ and $0.72) to find the lower price.
  2. Convert: Change $3/4$ to a decimal: $3 \div 4 = 0.75$.
  3. Solve: Now compare $0.75$ and $0.72$. Since $0.72 < 0.75$, Store B has the lower price.
  4. Check: $0.72 is indeed less than $0.75, so Store B is cheaper. Answer: Store B ($0.72) has the lower price.

Summary for Comparing and Ordering Rational Numbers

  • Convert to Decimals: Always convert all rational numbers to decimal form first. This is usually the easiest approach.
  • Compare Positive Decimals: Line up decimal points and compare digits from left to right, just like whole numbers.
  • Compare Negative Decimals: Be extra careful!
    • Numbers further to the left on the number line are smaller.
    • A negative number with a greater absolute value is actually the lesser number.
  • Use a Number Line: A number line can be a powerful visual aid for understanding the relative positions and order of both positive and negative rational numbers.

Review Questions

  1. What is a rational number? Give three examples that are in different forms (e.g., a fraction, a terminating decimal, and an integer).
  2. Explain the primary strategy for comparing and ordering rational numbers that are in different forms. Why is converting to decimals often the most efficient method?
  3. Order the following rational numbers from least to greatest, showing your work: $1/2$, $0.55$, $3/5$, $0.48$.
  4. Compare $-7/10$ and $-0.75$. Explain your reasoning, especially how you handle the negative signs.
  5. A recipe calls for $0.3$ cups of milk, but you only have a measuring cup marked in fractions. You estimate $1/4$ cup. Is your estimate more or less than what the recipe requires? Explain using rational number comparison.
