Chapter 7, Section 5: Percent Increase and Decrease Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the concepts of percent increase and percent decrease, which are used to describe how much a quantity has changed relative to its original value. You will learn the universal formula to calculate any percent change and practice applying it to real-world scenarios like price adjustments or population shifts. Additionally, the section covers how to determine a new quantity after a specified percent increase or decrease.

Key Concepts

  • Percent Increase: This occurs when a quantity goes up. The increase is expressed as a percentage of the original amount. For example, a price rising from $40 to $50 is a percent increase.
  • Percent Decrease: This occurs when a quantity goes down. The decrease is expressed as a percentage of the original amount. For example, a population falling from 8,000 to 7,200 is a percent decrease.
  • Percent Change Formula: The core formula for calculating both percent increase and decrease is: percent change = (amount of change / original amount) * 100% The "amount of change" is calculated as new value - original value.
  • Original Amount as Denominator: It is crucial to always use the original amount (the starting value) in the denominator of the percent change formula. This ensures the change is compared to the initial state.
  • Finding the New Amount:
    • After an Increase: The new amount is found by adding the percentage increase (as a decimal of the original) to the original amount: new amount = original + (percent * original).
    • After a Decrease: The new amount is found by subtracting the percentage decrease (as a decimal of the original) from the original amount: new amount = original - (percent * original).

Important Terms

  • Percent Increase: A relative measure of how much a quantity has grown, expressed as a percentage of its initial value.
  • Percent Decrease: A relative measure of how much a quantity has shrunk, expressed as a percentage of its initial value.
  • Amount of Change: The absolute difference between the new value and the original value (new value - original value). This value is positive for an increase and negative for a decrease (though often used as an absolute value in the percent change formula, with the type of change indicated by context).
  • Original Amount: The starting value or initial quantity before any change occurred. This is always the reference point (denominator) for calculating percent change.
  • New Amount: The final value or quantity after the change has occurred.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Percent Increase and Decrease

Percent increase and decrease are everyday calculations that help us understand the magnitude of change in various situations. Whether it's the cost of groceries, the number of people in a city, or even your test scores, expressing change as a percentage of the original amount provides a standardized way to compare changes.

The Universal Percent Change Formula

The fundamental formula for calculating any percent change (whether an increase or a decrease) is:

[ \text{percent change} = \frac{\text{amount of change}}{\text{original amount}} \times 100% ]

Let's break down the components:

  • Amount of Change: This is simply the difference between the new value and the original value. You calculate it as new value - original value.
    • If the new value is greater than the original value, the amount of change will be positive, indicating an increase.
    • If the new value is less than the original value, the amount of change will be negative. However, for the formula, we often use the absolute value of the change and then determine if it's an increase or decrease based on whether the quantity went up or down.
  • Original Amount: This is the starting value from which the change occurred. It is always the denominator in the formula. This is a critical point; you compare the change to where you began, not where you ended.

Example: Percent Increase in Price

  • Problem: The price of a video game rose from $40 to $50. Find the percent increase.
  • Solution:
    1. Find the amount of change: $50 (new) - $40 (original) = $10.
    2. Apply the formula: [ \text{percent increase} = \frac{$10}{$40} \times 100% = 0.25 \times 100% = 25% ] The price increased by 25%.

Example: Percent Decrease in Population

  • Problem: A town's population fell from 8,000 to 7,200. Find the percent decrease.
  • Solution:
    1. Find the amount of change: 8,000 (original) - 7,200 (new) = 800. (Note: It's 8,000 - 7,200, or |7,200 - 8,000| = 800. The amount of change is 800, and since the population fell, it's a decrease.)
    2. Apply the formula: [ \text{percent decrease} = \frac{800}{8,000} \times 100% = 0.10 \times 100% = 10% ] The population decreased by 10%.

Checkpoint Review:

  • Problem: A shirt was $25 and is now $20. Find the percent decrease.
  • Solution:
    1. Amount of change: $25 - $20 = $5.
    2. Percent decrease: $\frac{$5}{$25} \times 100% = 0.20 \times 100% = 20%$. The shirt price decreased by 20%.

Finding the New Amount After a Percent Change

Sometimes, you know the original amount and the percentage of change, and you need to find the final, new amount.

  • After an Increase: new amount = original amount + (percent increase as decimal * original amount)
  • After a Decrease: new amount = original amount - (percent decrease as decimal * original amount)

Example: Finding the New Amount (Increase)

  • Problem: A bicycle costs $120. The price increases by 15%. What is the new price?
  • Solution:
    1. Calculate the amount of increase: 15% as a decimal is 0.15. 0.15 * $120 = $18.
    2. Add the increase to the original price: $120 + $18 = $138. The new price of the bicycle is $138.

Checkpoint Review:

  • Problem: A $200 tablet is marked down 25%. What is the new price?
  • Solution:
    1. Calculate the amount of decrease (markdown): 25% as a decimal is 0.25. 0.25 * $200 = $50.
    2. Subtract the decrease from the original price: $200 - $50 = $150. The new price of the tablet is $150.

Step-by-Step Problem Solving: School Enrollment

Let's walk through a complete problem-solving process.

  • Problem: A school had 600 students last year. This year there are 648 students. What is the percent increase in enrollment?

  • Step 1: Read. Understand the situation. Enrollment went from 600 students to 648 students. We need to find the percent increase.

  • Step 2: Identify.

    • Original amount = 600 students.
    • New amount = 648 students.
    • We are looking for the percent increase.

  • Step 3: Write. Set up the formula. First, find the amount of change.

    • Amount of change = 648 - 600 = 48.
    • Now, apply the percent change formula: [ \text{percent increase} = \frac{48}{600} \times 100% ]

  • Step 4: Solve. [ \frac{48}{600} \times 100% = 0.08 \times 100% = 8% ] The percent increase in enrollment is 8%.

  • Step 5: Check. Does this answer make sense?

    • Calculate 8% of the original 600 students: 0.08 * 600 = 48.
    • Add this increase to the original: 600 + 48 = 648.
    • This matches the new amount, so our answer is correct.

Important Reminder

Always use the original amount as the denominator when calculating percent change. Using the new amount as the denominator will result in a different, and incorrect, percentage. The comparison must be against the starting point.

Summary of Percent Increase and Decrease

  • To find percent change: percent change = (amount of change / original amount) * 100%.
  • Determine if it's an increase or decrease based on whether the new amount is greater or less than the original.
  • To find a new amount after a percentage change:
    • For an increase: new amount = original + (percent * original).
    • For a decrease: new amount = original - (percent * original).

  2. Last month, a store sold 300 units of a product. This month, they sold 375 units. What is the percent increase in sales?
  3. A jacket originally priced at $80 is on sale for 20% off. What is the new price of the jacket?
  4. An investment of $5,000 grew by 12% in one year. What is the new value of the investment?
  5. Explain why it is critical to always use the original amount in the denominator when calculating percent increase or decrease. Provide an example to illustrate your explanation.
