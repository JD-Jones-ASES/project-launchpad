Study Guide: Percent Increase and Decrease

Overview

This section explains how to calculate and understand percent increases and decreases, which are essential for describing how quantities change over time relative to their starting value. You will learn the fundamental formula for calculating percent change, differentiate between increases and decreases, and apply these concepts to find new amounts after a percentage change. The core idea is always to compare the amount of change to the original quantity.

Key Concepts

  • Percent Change: A general term describing how much a quantity has changed, expressed as a percentage of the original amount. It can be either an increase or a decrease.
  • Percent Increase: Represents how much a quantity has grown, calculated as the amount of increase divided by the original amount, then multiplied by 100%.
  • Percent Decrease: Represents how much a quantity has shrunk, calculated as the amount of decrease divided by the original amount, then multiplied by 100%.
  • Percent Change Formula: The universal formula is (Amount of Change / Original Amount) * 100%. The "Amount of Change" is found by subtracting the original value from the new value (new value - original value).
  • Original Amount as Denominator: It is crucial to always use the starting (original) amount as the denominator in the percent change formula. This ensures the change is compared to where the quantity began.
  • Finding the New Amount: When you know the original amount and the percent change, you can find the new amount by either adding (for an increase) or subtracting (for a decrease) the calculated change from the original amount.

Important Terms

  • Percent Increase: A relative measure of how much a quantity has grown, expressed as a percentage of its initial value.
  • Percent Decrease: A relative measure of how much a quantity has shrunk, expressed as a percentage of its initial value.
  • Amount of Change: The absolute difference between the new value and the original value (new value - original value). It's the numerical quantity by which something has gone up or down.
  • Original Amount: The starting value or initial quantity before any change occurred. This is always the reference point (the denominator) for calculating percent change.
  • New Amount: The final value or quantity after a change (increase or decrease) has occurred.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Percent Increase and Decrease

Many real-world situations involve quantities that change: prices, populations, scores, etc. To describe these changes meaningfully, we often express them as a percent of the original quantity. This allows for a standardized comparison regardless of the actual numerical size of the quantities involved.

  • Percent Increase: Occurs when a quantity goes up. For example, if the price of an item rises.
  • Percent Decrease: Occurs when a quantity goes down. For example, if a population declines.

Both are calculated relative to the starting point.

The Percent Change Formula

The fundamental formula for calculating any percent change (whether an increase or a decrease) is:

$$ \text{percent change} = \frac{\text{amount of change}}{\text{original amount}} \times 100% $$

Let's break down the components of this formula:

  1. Amount of Change: This is the absolute difference between the new value and the original value.

    • Amount of change = new value - original value
    • If the new value is greater than the original value, the result will be positive, indicating an increase.
    • If the new value is less than the original value, the result will be negative (or you can calculate the absolute difference, and recognize it's a decrease). It's generally easier to take (larger value - smaller value) to get the amount of change and then determine if it's an increase or decrease based on the context.

  2. Original Amount: This is the starting value of the quantity before any change happened. It is crucial to always use the original amount as the denominator in the formula. This ensures that the percentage change accurately reflects the change relative to the initial state, not the final state.

Let's look at some examples:

Example 1: Percent Increase in Price

  • Problem: The price of a video game rose from $40 to $50. Find the percent increase.
  • Solution:
    1. Identify Original and New: Original price = $40, New price = $50.
    2. Calculate Amount of Change: Since the new price is greater, it's an increase. Amount of change = New Value - Original Value = $50 - $40 = $10.
    3. Apply the Formula: percent increase = (Amount of Change / Original Amount) * 100% percent increase = ($10 / $40) * 100% percent increase = 0.25 * 100% percent increase = 25%
  • Answer: The price increased by 25%.

Example 2: Percent Decrease in Population

  • Problem: A town's population fell from 8,000 to 7,200. Find the percent decrease.
  • Solution:
    1. Identify Original and New: Original population = 8,000, New population = 7,200.
    2. Calculate Amount of Change: Since the new population is less, it's a decrease. Amount of change = Original Value - New Value = 8,000 - 7,200 = 800.
    3. Apply the Formula: percent decrease = (Amount of Change / Original Amount) * 100% percent decrease = (800 / 8,000) * 100% percent decrease = 0.10 * 100% percent decrease = 10%
  • Answer: The population decreased by 10%.

Self-Check: A shirt was $25 and is now $20. Find the percent decrease.

  • Solution:
    • Amount of change = $25 - $20 = $5.
    • Percent decrease = ($5 / $25) * 100% = 0.20 * 100% = 20%.

Finding the New Amount After a Percent Change

Sometimes, instead of finding the percent change, you'll be given an original amount and a percentage change, and asked to find the new amount.

  • After an increase: new amount = original amount + (percent of original amount) This can also be written as new amount = original amount * (1 + percent as a decimal).

  • After a decrease: new amount = original amount - (percent of original amount) This can also be written as new amount = original amount * (1 - percent as a decimal).

Example 3: Finding the New Amount (Increase)

  • Problem: A bicycle costs $120. The price increases by 15%. What is the new price?
  • Solution:
    1. Calculate the amount of increase: Convert the percentage to a decimal. 15% of $120 = 0.15 * $120 = $18.
    2. Add the increase to the original price: New price = Original price + Amount of increase New price = $120 + $18 = $138.
  • Answer: The new price is $138.

Self-Check: A $200 tablet is marked down 25%. What is the new price?

  • Solution:
    • Amount of markdown (decrease) = 25% of $200 = 0.25 * $200 = $50.
    • New price = Original price - Amount of markdown = $200 - $50 = $150.

Step-by-Step Problem Solving: School Enrollment Example

Let's walk through a more comprehensive problem-solving example, demonstrating how to apply these concepts systematically.

Problem: A school had 600 students last year. This year there are 648 students. What is the percent increase in enrollment?

  • Step 1: Read. Understand the problem. We are given two enrollment figures (last year and this year) and need to find the percent increase.
  • Step 2: Identify.
    • Original amount (last year's enrollment) = 600 students.
    • New amount (this year's enrollment) = 648 students.
    • We need to find the percent increase.
  • Step 3: Write. Set up the formula.
    • First, find the amount of change: 648 - 600.
    • Then, apply the percent change formula: percent increase = (648 - 600) / 600 * 100%.
  • Step 4: Solve. Perform the calculations.
    • Amount of change = 648 - 600 = 48.
    • percent increase = (48 / 600) * 100%
    • percent increase = 0.08 * 100%
    • percent increase = 8%.
  • Step 5: Check. Does the answer make sense? If there was an 8% increase on 600 students, the increase would be 0.08 * 600 = 48. Adding this to the original enrollment: 600 + 48 = 648. This matches the new enrollment, so the answer is correct.

Answer: There was an 8% increase in enrollment.

Important Reminder

Always use the original amount as the denominator when calculating percent change. Using the new amount will lead to an incorrect percentage. For instance, if you calculated (48 / 648) * 100% in the last example, you would get approximately 7.4%, which is different and wrong because it compares the change to the new total, not the original.

Summary of Percent Increase and Decrease

  • The general formula for percent change is: $$ \text{percent change} = \frac{\text{amount of change}}{\text{original amount}} \times 100% $$
  • If the new amount is greater than the original, it's a percent increase.
  • If the new amount is less than the original, it's a percent decrease.
  • When finding a new amount after a percentage change:
    • For an increase: New Amount = Original Amount + (Percent * Original Amount)
    • For a decrease: New Amount = Original Amount - (Percent * Original Amount)
