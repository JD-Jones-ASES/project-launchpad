Adding and Subtracting Mixed Numbers: A Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental methods for adding and subtracting mixed numbers. The core principle involves treating the whole-number and fractional parts separately, with a critical focus on finding common denominators. Special attention is given to the "borrowing" or "regrouping" technique, which is essential for subtraction when the fractional part being subtracted is larger than the initial fractional part.

Key Concepts

  • Separating Whole and Fraction Parts: When adding or subtracting mixed numbers, the whole number portions and the fraction portions are handled distinctly.
  • Least Common Denominator (LCD): Before fractions can be added or subtracted, they must have a common denominator. The LCD is the smallest common multiple of the denominators, making calculations simpler.
  • Borrowing (Regrouping): In subtraction, if the fraction being subtracted is larger than the fraction it's being subtracted from, you must "borrow" 1 from the whole number part of the minuend (the number you are subtracting from) and add its fractional equivalent (e.g., $d/d$) to its fraction part. This is analogous to regrouping in whole-number subtraction.
  • Converting Improper Fractions: After adding mixed numbers, if the resulting fraction part is improper (numerator greater than or equal to the denominator), it must be converted to a mixed number, and the extra whole number added to the whole number part of the sum.
  • Simplifying Fractions: The final step in both addition and subtraction is to simplify the resulting fraction to its lowest terms, if possible.
  • Alternative Method: Mixed numbers can also be converted to improper fractions first, then added or subtracted, and finally converted back to a mixed number. This method bypasses borrowing but can involve larger numbers.

Important Terms

  • Mixed Number: A number consisting of an integer (whole number) and a proper fraction, for example, $2\frac{2}{3}$.
  • Least Common Denominator (LCD): The smallest common multiple of the denominators of two or more fractions. It is used to create equivalent fractions with a common denominator for addition and subtraction.
  • Improper Fraction: A fraction in which the numerator is greater than or equal to the denominator, for example, $\frac{17}{12}$.
  • Borrowing (Regrouping): A technique used in mixed number subtraction where 1 is taken from the whole number part and rewritten as a fraction with the common denominator (e.g., $1 = \frac{d}{d}$), then added to the existing fraction part.
  • Simplify: To reduce a fraction to its lowest terms by dividing both the numerator and denominator by their greatest common factor.

Detailed Notes

General Approach to Mixed Number Operations

The core idea for adding and subtracting mixed numbers is to apply what you already know about fractions. You will manage the whole number parts and the fractional parts separately. Common denominators are always required for the fractional parts.

Adding Mixed Numbers

Here are the steps to add mixed numbers:

  1. Find the LCD: If the fraction parts have different denominators, find their Least Common Denominator (LCD).
  2. Rewrite Fractions: Rewrite the fractions as equivalent fractions using the LCD.
  3. Add Fraction Parts: Add the numerators of the fraction parts, keeping the common denominator.
  4. Add Whole Number Parts: Add the whole number parts together.
  5. Combine and Adjust: Combine the new whole number and fraction. If the resulting fraction part is an improper fraction (numerator is greater than or equal to the denominator), convert it to a mixed number. Add the whole number from this conversion to the existing whole number part of your sum.
  6. Simplify: Simplify the final fraction part to its lowest terms if possible.

Example: Adding Mixed Numbers Let's add $2\frac{2}{3} + 4\frac{3}{4}$.

  1. Find the LCD: The denominators are 3 and 4. The LCD of 3 and 4 is 12.
  2. Rewrite Fractions: $2\frac{2}{3} = 2\frac{8}{12}$ (since $\frac{2}{3} = \frac{2 \times 4}{3 \times 4} = \frac{8}{12}$) $4\frac{3}{4} = 4\frac{9}{12}$ (since $\frac{3}{4} = \frac{3 \times 3}{4 \times 3} = \frac{9}{12}$)
  3. Add Fraction Parts: $\frac{8}{12} + \frac{9}{12} = \frac{17}{12}$.
  4. Add Whole Numbers: $2 + 4 = 6$.
  5. Combine and Adjust: We have $6\frac{17}{12}$. Since $\frac{17}{12}$ is an improper fraction, convert it: $\frac{17}{12} = 1\frac{5}{12}$. Now, add this extra whole number (1) to our existing whole number (6): $6 + 1\frac{5}{12} = 7\frac{5}{12}$.
  6. Simplify: The fraction $\frac{5}{12}$ is already in simplest form. The final answer is $7\frac{5}{12}$.

Subtracting Mixed Numbers

Subtracting mixed numbers follows similar initial steps but introduces a crucial step: borrowing.

  1. Find the LCD: If the fraction parts have different denominators, find their LCD.
  2. Rewrite Fractions: Rewrite the mixed numbers with their equivalent fractions using the LCD.
  3. Check for Borrowing: Compare the numerators of the fractional parts. If the numerator of the fraction you are subtracting from is smaller than the numerator of the fraction you are subtracting, you must "borrow."
    • How to Borrow: Take 1 from the whole number part of the first mixed number. Rewrite this '1' as a fraction with the common denominator (e.g., $1 = \frac{d}{d}$). Add this fractional equivalent to the existing fraction part of the first mixed number. For example, $5\frac{1}{4} = 4\frac{1}{4} + \frac{4}{4} = 4\frac{5}{4}$.
  4. Subtract Fraction Parts: Subtract the numerators of the fraction parts.
  5. Subtract Whole Number Parts: Subtract the whole number parts.
  6. Simplify: Simplify the final fraction part to its lowest terms if possible.

Example: Subtracting Mixed Numbers with Borrowing Let's subtract $5\frac{1}{6} - 2\frac{3}{4}$.

  1. Find the LCD: The denominators are 6 and 4. The LCD of 6 and 4 is 12.
  2. Rewrite Fractions: $5\frac{1}{6} = 5\frac{2}{12}$ $2\frac{3}{4} = 2\frac{9}{12}$
  3. Check for Borrowing: We need to subtract $\frac{9}{12}$ from $\frac{2}{12}$. Since $\frac{2}{12} < \frac{9}{12}$, we must borrow.
    • Borrowing Step: Take 1 from the whole number 5, making it 4. Add this 1 as $\frac{12}{12}$ to the fraction part $\frac{2}{12}$: $5\frac{2}{12} = 4\frac{2}{12} + \frac{12}{12} = 4\frac{14}{12}$. Now the problem becomes $4\frac{14}{12} - 2\frac{9}{12}$.
  4. Subtract Fraction Parts: $\frac{14}{12} - \frac{9}{12} = \frac{5}{12}$.
  5. Subtract Whole Numbers: $4 - 2 = 2$.
  6. Combine and Simplify: The result is $2\frac{5}{12}$. The fraction $\frac{5}{12}$ is already in simplest form. The final answer is $2\frac{5}{12}$.

Example: Subtracting a Mixed Number from a Whole Number Subtract $8 - 3\frac{5}{8}$.

To do this, we need to rewrite the whole number 8 as a mixed number by borrowing 1 from it: $8 = 7 + 1 = 7 + \frac{8}{8} = 7\frac{8}{8}$. Now the problem is $7\frac{8}{8} - 3\frac{5}{8}$.

  1. Subtract Fraction Parts: $\frac{8}{8} - \frac{5}{8} = \frac{3}{8}$.
  2. Subtract Whole Numbers: $7 - 3 = 4$. The result is $4\frac{3}{8}$.

Important Caution on Borrowing: When you borrow, ensure you add the correct fractional equivalent of 1. If your common denominator is $d$, you are adding $\frac{d}{d}$ to your fraction part, not just $\frac{1}{d}$. For instance, if the LCD is 12, you add $\frac{12}{12}$.

Solving Word Problems with Mixed Numbers

Real-world problems often involve mixed numbers. Use the same systematic approach:

Example: Measurement Problem A board is $8\frac{1}{4}$ feet long. A carpenter cuts off a piece that is $3\frac{5}{6}$ feet long. How long is the remaining piece?

  1. Read: Understand the question: find the remaining length after a piece is cut.
  2. Identify: This is a subtraction problem.
  3. Write: The calculation needed is $8\frac{1}{4} - 3\frac{5}{6}$.
  4. Solve:
    • Find the LCD of 4 and 6, which is 12.
    • Rewrite the mixed numbers: $8\frac{1}{4} = 8\frac{3}{12}$ $3\frac{5}{6} = 3\frac{10}{12}$
    • Since $\frac{3}{12} < \frac{10}{12}$, borrow from 8: $8\frac{3}{12} = 7\frac{3}{12} + \frac{12}{12} = 7\frac{15}{12}$.
    • Now subtract: $7\frac{15}{12} - 3\frac{10}{12}$.
    • Subtract fractions: $\frac{15}{12} - \frac{10}{12} = \frac{5}{12}$.
    • Subtract whole numbers: $7 - 3 = 4$.
    • The result is $4\frac{5}{12}$.
  5. Check: Add the result back to the subtracted amount: $4\frac{5}{12} + 3\frac{10}{12} = 7\frac{15}{12} = 7 + 1\frac{3}{12} = 8\frac{3}{12} = 8\frac{1}{4}$. This matches the original length, so the answer is correct.

The remaining piece is $4\frac{5}{12}$ feet long.

Alternative Method: Using Improper Fractions

An alternative approach to adding and subtracting mixed numbers is to convert both mixed numbers into improper fractions first. Then, perform the addition or subtraction as you would with any fractions (find LCD, add/subtract numerators), and finally, convert the result back to a mixed number. This method avoids the borrowing step, which some find confusing, but it can involve working with larger numbers.

Review Questions

  1. Why is finding a common denominator an essential first step when adding or subtracting the fractional parts of mixed numbers?
  2. Explain the process of "borrowing" when subtracting mixed numbers. When is it necessary, and how is it similar to regrouping with whole numbers in standard subtraction? Provide an example.
  3. Describe the complete set of steps involved in adding two mixed numbers, including what to do if the fractional part of the sum is an improper fraction.
  4. Compare and contrast the two main methods for adding/subtracting mixed numbers (handling parts separately vs. converting to improper fractions). What are the potential advantages and disadvantages of each?
