Adding and Subtracting Mixed Numbers Study Guide

Overview

This section focuses on the essential skills of adding and subtracting mixed numbers. The core strategy involves treating the whole number parts and the fraction parts separately. Special attention is given to finding common denominators, converting improper fractions, and a crucial step called "borrowing" or "regrouping" when subtracting, which is similar to regrouping in whole-number subtraction.

Key Concepts

  • Separating Whole and Fraction Parts: The primary approach for both addition and subtraction of mixed numbers is to operate on the whole number components and the fraction components independently.
  • Least Common Denominator (LCD): Before adding or subtracting the fraction parts, they must have a common denominator. The LCD is the most efficient choice.
  • Converting Improper Fractions (Addition): If, after adding the fraction parts, the result is an improper fraction (numerator greater than or equal to the denominator), it must be converted to a mixed number, and its whole number part is added to the total whole number sum.
  • Borrowing (Regrouping) (Subtraction): When the fraction being subtracted is larger than the fraction it's being subtracted from, you must "borrow" 1 from the whole number part of the first mixed number. This borrowed 1 is then rewritten as an equivalent fraction (e.g., 12/12 if the LCD is 12) and added to the existing fraction part.
  • Simplifying Fractions: The final step in both addition and subtraction is to simplify the resulting fraction part to its lowest terms.
  • Alternative Method: Mixed numbers can be converted into improper fractions, added or subtracted, and then the result can be converted back into a mixed number.

Important Terms

  • Mixed Number: A number consisting of a whole number and a proper fraction (e.g., 2 1/2).
  • Least Common Denominator (LCD): The smallest common multiple of the denominators of two or more fractions; essential for adding or subtracting fractions.
  • Improper Fraction: A fraction where the numerator is greater than or equal to the denominator (e.g., 7/4).
  • Borrowing (Regrouping): A technique used in subtraction, particularly with mixed numbers, where 1 whole unit is taken from the whole number part and added to the fraction part, often by rewriting 1 as a fraction with a common denominator (e.g., 1 = 4/4).
  • Simplify: To reduce a fraction to its lowest terms by dividing both the numerator and denominator by their greatest common factor.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Adding and Subtracting Mixed Numbers

Working with mixed numbers involves combining or separating their whole number and fractional components. The most common strategy is to treat these parts separately, ensuring that fractions have a common denominator before performing any operations.

Adding Mixed Numbers

Adding mixed numbers follows a clear sequence of steps:

  1. Find the LCD: If the fraction parts have different denominators, find their Least Common Denominator (LCD).
  2. Rewrite Fractions: Rewrite both mixed numbers with equivalent fractions using the LCD.
  3. Add Fraction Parts: Add the rewritten fraction parts together.
  4. Add Whole Number Parts: Add the whole number parts together.
  5. Handle Improper Fractions: If the sum of the fraction parts is an improper fraction, convert it to a mixed number. Add the whole number part of this converted fraction to the total sum of the whole numbers.
  6. Simplify: Simplify the final fraction part if possible.

Example: Adding Mixed Numbers Add $2,2/3 + 4,3/4$.

  • Step 1: Find the LCD. The denominators are 3 and 4. The LCD of (3, 4) is 12.
  • Step 2: Rewrite the fractions. $2,2/3 = 2,(2 \times 4)/(3 \times 4) = 2,8/12$ $4,3/4 = 4,(3 \times 3)/(4 \times 3) = 4,9/12$
  • Step 3: Add the fraction parts. $8/12 + 9/12 = 17/12$
  • Step 4: Add the whole numbers. $2 + 4 = 6$
  • Step 5: Combine and handle improper fraction. Initially, we have $6,17/12$. Since $17/12$ is an improper fraction, convert it: $17/12 = 1,5/12$. Add this extra whole number (1) to the whole number sum (6): $6 + 1,5/12 = 7,5/12$.
  • Step 6: Simplify. The fraction $5/12$ cannot be simplified further. The result is $7,5/12$.

Checkpoint: Add $3,1/2 + 1,2/5$.

  • LCD is 10.
  • $3,1/2 = 3,5/10$
  • $1,2/5 = 1,4/10$
  • Add fractions: $5/10 + 4/10 = 9/10$
  • Add whole numbers: $3 + 1 = 4$
  • Combine: $4,9/10$. (No improper fraction, already simplified.)

Subtracting Mixed Numbers

Subtracting mixed numbers has an extra consideration: borrowing.

  1. Find the LCD: If the fraction parts have different denominators, find their LCD.
  2. Rewrite Fractions: Rewrite both mixed numbers with equivalent fractions using the LCD.
  3. Check for Borrowing: Compare the fraction part of the first mixed number (the one you're subtracting from) with the fraction part of the second mixed number. If the first fraction is smaller, you must borrow.
  4. Borrow (if needed): To borrow, take 1 from the whole number part of the first mixed number. Rewrite this 1 as a fraction with the common denominator (e.g., $1 = 12/12$ if the LCD is 12) and add it to the existing fraction part.
    • Important Note: When borrowing, you add a fraction equivalent to 1 (like $d/d$) to your existing fraction, not just $1/d$. For example, $5,1/4 = 4,1/4 + 4/4 = 4,5/4$.
  5. Subtract Fraction Parts: Subtract the fraction parts.
  6. Subtract Whole Number Parts: Subtract the whole number parts.
  7. Simplify: Simplify the final fraction part if possible.

Example: Subtracting Mixed Numbers with Borrowing Subtract $5,1/6 - 2,3/4$.

  • Step 1: Find the LCD. The denominators are 6 and 4. The LCD of (6, 4) is 12.
  • Step 2: Rewrite the fractions. $5,1/6 = 5,(1 \times 2)/(6 \times 2) = 5,2/12$ $2,3/4 = 2,(3 \times 3)/(4 \times 3) = 2,9/12$
  • Step 3: Check for borrowing. Compare $2/12$ and $9/12$. Since $2/12 < 9/12$, we must borrow.
  • Step 4: Borrow. Take 1 from the whole number 5 and add it to $2/12$: $5,2/12 = (5-1) + (2/12 + 12/12) = 4,14/12$. Now the problem becomes: $4,14/12 - 2,9/12$.
  • Step 5: Subtract the fraction parts. $14/12 - 9/12 = 5/12$.
  • Step 6: Subtract the whole numbers. $4 - 2 = 2$.
  • Step 7: Simplify. The fraction $5/12$ cannot be simplified further. The result is $2,5/12$.

Checkpoint: Subtract $7,1/4 - 3,2/3$.

  • LCD is 12.
  • $7,1/4 = 7,3/12$
  • $3,2/3 = 3,8/12$
  • Since $3/12 < 8/12$, borrow: $7,3/12 = 6,(3/12 + 12/12) = 6,15/12$.
  • Now subtract: $6,15/12 - 3,8/12$.
  • Subtract fractions: $15/12 - 8/12 = 7/12$.
  • Subtract whole numbers: $6 - 3 = 3$.
  • Result: $3,7/12$.

Subtracting a Mixed Number from a Whole Number

When subtracting a mixed number from a whole number, you'll always need to "borrow" from the whole number.

Example: Subtracting a Mixed Number from a Whole Number Subtract $8 - 3,5/8$.

  • Rewrite the whole number 8 by borrowing 1: $8 = 7,8/8$.
  • Now subtract: $7,8/8 - 3,5/8$.
  • Subtract fractions: $8/8 - 5/8 = 3/8$.
  • Subtract whole numbers: $7 - 3 = 4$.
  • Result: $4,3/8$.

Real-World Application: Measurement Problem

A board is $8,1/4$ feet long. A carpenter cuts off a piece that is $3,5/6$ feet long. How long is the remaining piece?

  • Step 1: Read. The problem asks for the remaining length after a piece is cut from a board.
  • Step 2: Identify. This is a subtraction problem: total length - cut piece = remaining length.
  • Step 3: Write. $8,1/4 - 3,5/6$.
  • Step 4: Solve.
    • Find the LCD of 4 and 6, which is 12.
    • Rewrite the mixed numbers: $8,1/4 = 8,3/12$ $3,5/6 = 3,10/12$
    • Since $3/12 < 10/12$, we must borrow.
    • Borrow from 8: $8,3/12 = 7,(3/12 + 12/12) = 7,15/12$.
    • Perform subtraction: $7,15/12 - 3,10/12$.
    • Subtract fractions: $15/12 - 10/12 = 5/12$.
    • Subtract whole numbers: $7 - 3 = 4$.
    • Result: $4,5/12$.
  • Step 5: Check. Add the result to the subtracted amount: $4,5/12 + 3,10/12 = 7,15/12 = 7 + 1,3/12 = 8,3/12 = 8,1/4$. The check confirms the answer.

The remaining piece is $4,5/12$ feet long.

Alternative Method: Using Improper Fractions

An alternative approach for both addition and subtraction of mixed numbers is to first convert all mixed numbers to improper fractions, then perform the operation, and finally convert the result back to a mixed number. This method can sometimes simplify the borrowing step in subtraction for some learners, but it might involve working with larger numbers.

Review Questions

  1. Why is finding a common denominator a crucial first step when adding or subtracting mixed numbers with different fraction parts?
  2. Explain the concept of "borrowing" (or regrouping) in mixed number subtraction in your own words. Provide a simple example of how $6,1/3$ would be rewritten if you needed to borrow from the whole number.
  3. After adding two mixed numbers, you get a result like $5,11/8$. What steps should you take next to present the answer in its simplest form?
  4. Describe an alternative method for adding or subtracting mixed numbers that doesn't involve handling whole and fraction parts separately throughout the entire process. What might be a potential advantage and disadvantage of using this alternative method?
  5. A painter needs $3,1/2$ gallons of paint for one room and $2,2/3$ gallons for another. If they start with $7$ gallons of paint, how much paint will they have left after painting both rooms? Show your work.
