Study Guide: Exponents and Powers

Overview

This section introduces exponential notation as a concise way to represent repeated multiplication. You will learn how to identify the base and exponent in an expression, read and write numbers in exponential form, evaluate expressions containing exponents, and apply the rules of exponents within the order of operations.

Key Concepts

  • Exponential Notation: A shorthand method for expressing repeated multiplication of the same number by itself. Instead of writing b × b × b, we write b^3.
  • Base and Exponent: In an expression b^n, b is the base (the number being multiplied), and n is the exponent (the number of times the base is used as a factor).
  • Special Powers:
    • b^2 is read as "b squared" (e.g., area of a square).
    • b^3 is read as "b cubed" (e.g., volume of a cube).
    • b^1 = b: Any number raised to the power of 1 is the number itself.
  • Zero Exponent Rule: For any nonzero number b, b^0 = 1. This is a fundamental rule in mathematics.
  • Order of Operations: Exponents are evaluated after operations inside parentheses but before multiplication, division, addition, and subtraction. This hierarchy is crucial for accurate calculations.
  • Powers of 10: Expressions like 10^n are especially useful for representing very large or very small numbers (as seen in scientific notation), where 10^n is a 1 followed by n zeros.

Important Terms

  • Exponent (or Power): The number n in b^n that indicates how many times the base b is used as a factor in multiplication.
  • Base: The number b in b^n that is being multiplied repeatedly.
  • Squared: An exponent of 2 (e.g., b^2). It literally means the base multiplied by itself twice.
  • Cubed: An exponent of 3 (e.g., b^3). It literally means the base multiplied by itself three times.
  • Zero Exponent: The rule that states any nonzero number raised to the power of 0 equals 1.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Exponents

When you need to multiply the same number by itself many times, such as 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2, writing it out can be long and tedious. Exponential notation provides a concise way to express such repeated multiplication.

Understanding the Parts of an Exponential Expression

An exponential expression is written in the form b^n.

  • Base (b): This is the number that is being multiplied.
  • Exponent (n): This number, written as a smaller superscript, tells you how many times to use the base as a factor in the multiplication. It is also sometimes called the "power."

We read b^n as "b to the nth power."

Special Powers and Their Meanings

Some exponents have special names:

  • b^2: Read as "b squared." For example, 5^2 means 5 × 5. This term comes from the formula for the area of a square, which is side × side.
  • b^3: Read as "b cubed." For example, 4^3 means 4 × 4 × 4. This term comes from the formula for the volume of a cube, which is edge × edge × edge.
  • b^1: Any number raised to the power of 1 is simply the number itself. For example, 7^1 = 7.

The Zero Exponent Rule

A special rule applies to the zero exponent:

  • For any nonzero number b, b^0 = 1.
    • Example: 15^0 = 1, 99^0 = 1.
    • Note: The expression 0^0 is undefined and is typically not encountered in this context.

Writing in Exponential Form

To write a product in exponential form, identify the number being multiplied (the base) and count how many times it appears (the exponent).

  • Example 1: Write 7 × 7 × 7 × 7 in exponential form.

    • The base is 7.
    • It appears 4 times.
    • So, 7^4.

  • Example 2: Write 10 × 10 × 10 in exponential form.

    • The base is 10.
    • It appears 3 times.
    • So, 10^3.

Evaluating Powers

To evaluate an expression with an exponent, you perform the repeated multiplication.

  • Example 1: Evaluate 3^4.

    • 3^4 means 3 × 3 × 3 × 3.
    • 3 × 3 = 9
    • 9 × 3 = 27
    • 27 × 3 = 81.
    • So, 3^4 = 81.

  • Example 2: Evaluate 10^5.

    • 10^5 means 10 × 10 × 10 × 10 × 10.
    • This is 100,000.

  • Example 3: Evaluate 15^0.

    • According to the zero exponent rule, 15^0 = 1.

Exponents and the Order of Operations

When an expression contains exponents, they must be evaluated according to the standard order of operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS):

  1. Parentheses (or Brackets)
  2. Exponents (or Orders)
  3. Multiplication and Division (from left to right)
  4. Addition and Subtraction (from left to right)
  • Example: Evaluate 3 + 2^4 × 5.
    1. Exponents first: 2^4 = 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 = 16. The expression becomes 3 + 16 × 5.
    2. Multiplication next: 16 × 5 = 80. The expression becomes 3 + 80.
    3. Addition last: 3 + 80 = 83. The final answer is 83.

Powers of 10 in Science and Large Numbers

Powers of 10 are extremely useful for writing and understanding very large or very small numbers, especially in scientific contexts.

  • 10^n is always a 1 followed by n zeros.

    • 10^1 = 10
    • 10^2 = 100
    • 10^3 = 1,000
    • 10^6 = 1,000,000

  • Example: The distance from Earth to the Sun is approximately 93 × 10^6 miles. Write this in standard form.

    • 10^6 = 1,000,000.
    • So, 93 × 1,000,000 = 93,000,000 miles.

Important Caution!

Remember that b^n does not mean b × n. The exponent tells you how many times to use the base as a factor.

  • For instance, 2^3 means 2 × 2 × 2 = 8, not 2 × 3 = 6. Always expand the exponential expression into repeated multiplication to avoid this common error.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words why exponential notation is useful. Provide an example of a calculation that would be tedious without exponents.
  2. Identify the base and exponent in the expression 6^5. Then, write the expression as repeated multiplication and evaluate its value.
  3. Describe the special rules for exponents b^1 and b^0. Why do you think these rules are defined this way?
  4. Evaluate the expression 7 + (2 + 3)^2 - 4^0. Show each step of the calculation, explaining which operation you perform at each stage according to the order of operations.
  5. How are powers of 10 useful in representing large numbers? Give an example of a real-world scenario where 10^n would be a practical way to express a quantity.
