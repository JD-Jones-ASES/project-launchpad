Study Guide: Dividing Decimals

Overview

This section explains the process of dividing numbers that involve decimals, building on your knowledge of long division with whole numbers. The core principle involves manipulating the decimal point to simplify the division, particularly by transforming the divisor into a whole number. You will learn how to divide decimals by whole numbers, by other decimals, and by powers of ten.

Key Concepts

  • Making the Divisor a Whole Number: The fundamental strategy for dividing by a decimal is to convert the divisor into a whole number by shifting its decimal point. To maintain the value of the quotient, the decimal point in the dividend must be shifted the same number of places.
  • Placement of the Decimal Point in the Quotient: When dividing by a whole number, the decimal point in the quotient is placed directly above the decimal point in the dividend. After adjusting the decimal points when dividing by a decimal, the same rule applies.
  • Dividing by Powers of Ten: There is a simple shortcut for dividing by 10, 100, or 1,000: move the decimal point to the left by one place for each zero in the power of ten.
  • Checking Your Work: Division can always be checked by multiplying the quotient by the divisor; the result should equal the original dividend.
  • Annexing Zeros: Sometimes, you may need to add zeros to the end of the dividend (after the decimal point) to continue the division process and get a more precise quotient.

Important Terms

  • Quotient: The answer or result obtained from a division problem. For example, in $6.4 \div 2 = 3.2$, the quotient is $3.2$.
  • Dividend: The number that is being divided in a division problem. In $6.4 \div 2$, the dividend is $6.4$.
  • Divisor: The number by which you are dividing in a division problem. In $6.4 \div 2$, the divisor is $2$.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Dividing Decimals

Dividing with decimals uses the familiar long division method. The main goal is to simplify the process by handling the decimal points correctly. This section covers three main scenarios: dividing a decimal by a whole number, dividing a decimal by another decimal, and dividing by powers of ten.

Terminology Review

Before diving into the mechanics, let's quickly review the terms:

  • In the expression $A \div B = C$:
    • $A$ is the dividend (the number being divided).
    • $B$ is the divisor (the number you divide by).
    • $C$ is the quotient (the answer).

Dividing a Decimal by a Whole Number

This is the most straightforward type of decimal division.

Steps:

  1. Place the Decimal Point: Immediately place the decimal point in the quotient directly above the decimal point in the dividend. This ensures your answer has the correct place value.
  2. Divide as with Whole Numbers: Perform long division just as you would with whole numbers, ignoring the decimal point initially during the calculation itself, but remembering its position for the final answer.
  3. Annex Zeros (if needed): If your division doesn't result in a remainder of zero, you can add zeros to the end of the dividend (after the decimal point) and continue dividing to get a more precise answer or to terminate the division.

Example: Find $14.76 \div 4$.

  • Set up the long division.
  • Place the decimal point in the quotient directly above the decimal point in $14.76$.
  • Divide $14$ by $4$, which is $3$ with a remainder of $2$. Write $3$ in the quotient.
  • Bring down the $7$ to make $27$. Divide $27$ by $4$, which is $6$ with a remainder of $3$. Write $6$ in the quotient.
  • Bring down the $6$ to make $36$. Divide $36$ by $4$, which is $9$ with a remainder of $0$. Write $9$ in the quotient.
  • The result is $3.69$.
  • Check: $3.69 \times 4 = 14.76$.

Dividing by a Decimal

This method requires an extra initial step to make the divisor a whole number.

Steps:

  1. Move Decimal in Divisor: Shift the decimal point in the divisor to the right until it becomes a whole number. Count how many places you moved it.
  2. Move Decimal in Dividend: Move the decimal point in the dividend the exact same number of places to the right. You may need to annex zeros to the dividend if there aren't enough digits.
  3. Place Decimal in Quotient: Place the decimal point in the quotient directly above the new position of the decimal point in the dividend.
  4. Divide as Usual: Perform long division using the adjusted numbers, treating the divisor as a whole number.

Why this works: When you move the decimal point in both the divisor and dividend by the same number of places, you are essentially multiplying both numbers by the same power of ten (e.g., 10, 100, 1,000). This operation does not change the value of the quotient. For instance, $7.56 \div 0.6$ is equivalent to $(7.56 \times 10) \div (0.6 \times 10)$, which simplifies to $75.6 \div 6$.

Example: Find $7.56 \div 0.6$.

  • The divisor is $0.6$. Move its decimal point one place to the right to make it $6$.
  • Move the decimal point in the dividend $7.56$ one place to the right to make it $75.6$.
  • Now, the problem is $75.6 \div 6$.
  • Place the decimal point in the quotient above $75.6$.
  • Divide $75.6$ by $6$:
    • $7 \div 6 = 1$ (remainder $1$).
    • Bring down $5$ to make $15$. $15 \div 6 = 2$ (remainder $3$).
    • Bring down $6$ to make $36$. $36 \div 6 = 6$ (remainder $0$).
  • The result is $12.6$.
  • Check: $12.6 \times 0.6 = 7.56$.

Important Caution: Always remember to move the decimal point in both the divisor and the dividend. Forgetting to move it in the dividend will lead to an incorrect answer.

Dividing by Powers of 10

Dividing by $10, 100, 1,000$, and so on, has a simple shortcut.

Rule: When you divide a decimal by a power of $10$, move the decimal point to the left by one place for each zero in the power of ten.

  • Divide by $10$ (one zero): Move the decimal point $1$ place to the left.
  • Divide by $100$ (two zeros): Move the decimal point $2$ places to the left.
  • Divide by $1,000$ (three zeros): Move the decimal point $3$ places to the left.
  • You may need to annex leading zeros to the number if there aren't enough digits to shift the decimal point.

Examples:

  1. $45.2 \div 10$: Move decimal $1$ place left $\rightarrow 4.52$.
  2. $45.2 \div 100$: Move decimal $2$ places left $\rightarrow 0.452$.
  3. $45.2 \div 1,000$: Move decimal $3$ places left $\rightarrow 0.0452$.

Real-World Application: Splitting Costs

Decimal division is essential for many everyday calculations, like splitting bills or finding unit prices.

Example: Four friends share a restaurant bill of $53.80 equally. How much does each person pay?

  1. Understand the problem: You need to divide the total cost by the number of people.
  2. Identify the operation: This is a division problem: Total Bill $\div$ Number of Friends.
  3. Write the equation: Share $= 53.80 \div 4$.
  4. Solve: Perform the division: $53.80 \div 4 = 13.45$.
  5. Check: $13.45 \times 4 = 53.80$. Each person pays $13.45.

Summary of Dividing Decimals

  • Dividing by a Whole Number: Place the decimal point in the quotient directly above the decimal point in the dividend, then divide as usual.
  • Dividing by a Decimal: Move the decimal point in both the divisor and dividend to the right until the divisor is a whole number. Then, place the decimal point in the quotient and divide.
  • Dividing by Powers of 10: Move the decimal point to the left by one place for each zero in the power of ten.
  • Always Check Your Answer: Multiply your quotient by the original divisor to confirm it equals the original dividend.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the primary strategy used when dividing a decimal by another decimal, and justify why this strategy doesn't change the value of the quotient.
  2. Describe the key difference in setting up a division problem when you are dividing a decimal by a whole number compared to dividing a decimal by a decimal.
  3. A student is trying to calculate $9.3 \div 0.3$. They correctly move the decimal in $0.3$ to make it $3$, but forget to move it in $9.3$. What will be the consequence of this error on their answer?
  4. You have a length of fabric that is $25.5$ meters long. If you need to cut it into pieces that are each $10$ times smaller than $25.5$ meters for a project, what would be the length of each piece, and how would you quickly calculate it?
  5. After performing a complex decimal division, what is the most reliable way to verify if your calculated quotient is correct? Explain why this method works.
