Study Guide: Dividing Decimals

Overview

This section focuses on the essential techniques for dividing decimal numbers. You'll learn how to perform long division when the dividend is a decimal and the divisor is a whole number, as well as how to handle division when both the dividend and divisor are decimals. A special shortcut for dividing by powers of ten is also covered, emphasizing the critical step of making the divisor a whole number to simplify calculations.

Key Concepts

  • Dividing by a Whole Number: When dividing a decimal by a whole number, the decimal point in the quotient is placed directly above the decimal point in the dividend, and then division proceeds like with whole numbers. Annexing zeros in the dividend may be necessary to complete the division.
  • Dividing by a Decimal: To divide by a decimal, the crucial first step is to transform the divisor into a whole number. This is achieved by moving the decimal point to the right. The decimal point in the dividend must then be moved the same number of places to the right to maintain the value of the quotient.
  • Dividing by Powers of 10 (10, 100, 1,000, etc.): A simple shortcut exists for division by powers of 10. The decimal point in the dividend is moved to the left by one place for each zero in the power of ten (e.g., one place for 10, two places for 100, three places for 1,000).
  • Checking Your Answer: Always verify your division by multiplying the quotient by the divisor. The result should equal the original dividend.
  • Annexing Zeros: Sometimes, when dividing, you might need to add zeros to the end of the dividend (after the decimal point) to continue the division process and get a more precise quotient.

Important Terms

  • Quotient: The answer or result obtained from a division problem.
    • Example: In $6.4 \div 2 = 3.2$, the quotient is $3.2$.
  • Dividend: The number that is being divided in a division problem. It's the total amount being split.
    • Example: In $6.4 \div 2$, the dividend is $6.4$.
  • Divisor: The number by which another number (the dividend) is divided. It's the number of parts you are splitting the dividend into.
    • Example: In $6.4 \div 2$, the divisor is $2$.

Detailed Notes

I. The Core Idea of Dividing Decimals

Division with decimals uses the familiar long-division process. The primary challenge, and key to accurate results, is correctly handling the decimal point. The most important rule to remember is to make the divisor a whole number before performing the actual division.

II. Dividing a Decimal by a Whole Number

When your divisor is already a whole number, the process is straightforward:

  1. Place the Decimal Point: Locate the decimal point in the dividend. Place the decimal point for your quotient directly above it. This ensures that the place values in your quotient align correctly with the dividend.
  2. Divide as with Whole Numbers: Proceed with the standard long-division steps, ignoring the decimal points for the moment, but making sure your digits in the quotient are aligned under the correct digits in the dividend.
  3. Annex Zeros (if needed): If your division doesn't result in a remainder of zero, you can add zeros to the end of the decimal dividend (without changing its value) and continue dividing to get a more precise answer or to see if the decimal terminates.

Example: Find $14.76 \div 4$.

  • Solution:
    • Place the decimal point in the quotient directly above the decimal point in $14.76$.
    • Divide $14$ by $4$ (gives $3$ with a remainder of $2$).
    • Bring down the $7$ to make $27$. Divide $27$ by $4$ (gives $6$ with a remainder of $3$).
    • Bring down the $6$ to make $36$. Divide $36$ by $4$ (gives $9$ with a remainder of $0$).
    • The result is $3.69$.
  • Check: $3.69 \times 4 = 14.76$.

III. Dividing by a Decimal

This is where the rule of making the divisor a whole number becomes critical:

  1. Move Decimal in Divisor: Count the number of decimal places in the divisor. Move the decimal point to the right until the divisor becomes a whole number.
  2. Move Decimal in Dividend: Move the decimal point in the dividend the same number of places to the right as you did for the divisor. If you run out of digits, add zeros as placeholders.
  3. Place Decimal in Quotient and Divide: Once both decimal points have been moved, place the decimal point in the quotient directly above the new position of the decimal point in the dividend. Then, divide as you would with a whole number divisor.

Caution: Forgetting to move the decimal point in the dividend after moving it in the divisor is a common error and will lead to an incorrect answer. You must move it the same number of places in both!

Example: Find $7.56 \div 0.6$.

  • Solution:
    • The divisor is $0.6$. It has one decimal place. Move the decimal point one place to the right to make it $6$.
    • Now, move the decimal point in the dividend $7.56$ one place to the right, making it $75.6$.
    • The problem now becomes $75.6 \div 6$.
    • Place the decimal point in the quotient above the decimal point in $75.6$.
    • Divide $75.6$ by $6$:
      • $7 \div 6 = 1$ (remainder $1$)
      • Bring down $5$ to make $15$. $15 \div 6 = 2$ (remainder $3$)
      • Bring down $6$ to make $36$. $36 \div 6 = 6$ (remainder $0$)
    • The quotient is $12.6$.
  • Check: $12.6 \times 0.6 = 7.56$.

Checkpoint: Find $3.225 \div 0.25$.

  • Answer: $12.9$ (Move decimal two places right in both: $322.5 \div 25$)

IV. Dividing by Powers of 10

Powers of 10 include $10, 100, 1,000$, and so on. Dividing by these numbers has a simple shortcut:

  • Move Decimal Point to the Left: For each zero in the power of ten, move the decimal point in the dividend one place to the left.
    • Dividing by $\mathbf{10}$ (one zero): Move the decimal point 1 place left.
    • Dividing by $\mathbf{100}$ (two zeros): Move the decimal point 2 places left.
    • Dividing by $\mathbf{1,000}$ (three zeros): Move the decimal point 3 places left.
    • If you need to move the decimal point past existing digits, add leading zeros as placeholders.

Example: Find each quotient.

  • a) $45.2 \div 10$
    • Move the decimal point 1 place left: $4.52$.
  • b) $45.2 \div 100$
    • Move the decimal point 2 places left: $0.452$.
  • c) $45.2 \div 1,000$
    • Move the decimal point 3 places left: $0.0452$.

Checkpoint: Find $8.3 \div 100$.

  • Answer: $0.083$ (Move decimal point 2 places left, adding a leading zero).

V. Real-World Application

Dividing decimals is essential for many everyday tasks, such as splitting costs, calculating averages, or determining unit rates.

Example: Splitting a Cost Four friends share a restaurant bill of $53.80 equally. How much does each person pay?

  • Solution: This is a division problem: $53.80 \div 4$.
  • Using the rules for dividing a decimal by a whole number:
    • Place the decimal point in the quotient above the decimal point in $53.80$.
    • Divide $53.80$ by $4$, which results in $13.45$.
  • Answer: Each person pays $13.45.
  • Check: $13.45 \times 4 = 53.80$.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental difference in how you set up a long division problem when the divisor is a whole number compared to when it is a decimal. Why is this difference important?
  2. Describe the steps involved in dividing $15.75 \div 2.5$. What is the common mistake to avoid in this type of problem?
  3. Without performing the full calculation, how would you determine the placement of the decimal point in the quotient of $248.6 \div 1,000$? Explain your reasoning.
  4. Imagine you have a ribbon that is $12.5$ meters long, and you want to cut it into pieces that are each $0.5$ meters long. How many pieces can you cut? Show how you would set up and solve this problem.
  5. A group of three students equally share the cost of a research tool that costs $47.55. How much does each student pay? Outline the steps you would take to find the answer and verify your solution.
