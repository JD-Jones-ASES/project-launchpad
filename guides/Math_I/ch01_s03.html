Study Guide: Properties of Addition and Multiplication

Overview

This section introduces fundamental properties of addition and multiplication that simplify calculations involving whole numbers. These properties allow numbers to be rearranged (commutative property) or regrouped (associative property) without changing the final sum or product. Additionally, special numbers called identities (0 for addition, 1 for multiplication) and the unique effect of multiplying by zero are explored.

Key Concepts

  • Commutative Property: For both addition and multiplication, the order in which you perform the operation does not change the result. This property is useful for rearranging numbers to make mental math easier.
  • Associative Property: For both addition and multiplication, the way you group numbers (using parentheses) does not change the result. This property helps in regrouping numbers to form "round" or easy-to-calculate pairs.
  • Identity Property of Addition: Adding zero to any number results in that same number. Zero is known as the additive identity.
  • Identity Property of Multiplication: Multiplying any number by one results in that same number. One is known as the multiplicative identity.
  • Zero Property of Multiplication: The product of any number and zero is always zero.
  • Mental Math Application: These properties are powerful tools for performing calculations more efficiently in your head by strategically rearranging and regrouping numbers.
  • Limitations: It is crucial to remember that these commutative and associative properties do not apply to subtraction or division.

Important Terms

  • Commutative Property of Addition: The property stating that changing the order of addends does not change the sum (e.g., $a + b = b + a$).
  • Commutative Property of Multiplication: The property stating that changing the order of factors does not change the product (e.g., $a \cdot b = b \cdot a$).
  • Associative Property of Addition: The property stating that changing the grouping of addends does not change the sum (e.g., $(a + b) + c = a + (b + c)$).
  • Associative Property of Multiplication: The property stating that changing the grouping of factors does not change the product (e.g., $(a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c)$).
  • Identity Property of Addition: The property stating that the sum of any number and $0$ is that number.
  • Additive Identity: The number $0$, because adding it to any number leaves the number unchanged.
  • Identity Property of Multiplication: The property stating that the product of any number and $1$ is that number.
  • Multiplicative Identity: The number $1$, because multiplying it by any number leaves the number unchanged.
  • Zero Property of Multiplication: The property stating that the product of any number and $0$ is $0$.
  • Addends: The numbers that are added together in an addition problem.
  • Factors: The numbers that are multiplied together in a multiplication problem.
  • Sum: The result of an addition problem.
  • Product: The result of a multiplication problem.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Properties

The properties of addition and multiplication are fundamental rules that govern how numbers behave under these operations. They provide flexibility in how we approach calculations, often making complex problems simpler to solve mentally or on paper.

Commutative Properties

These properties deal with the order of numbers.

  • Commutative Property of Addition:
    • Definition: The order of the addends does not affect the sum.
    • Formula: $a + b = b + a$
    • Example: $3 + 5 = 8$ and $5 + 3 = 8$.
    • Use: Allows you to rearrange numbers in an addition problem to group compatible numbers together (e.g., numbers that add up to 10 or 100).
  • Commutative Property of Multiplication:
    • Definition: The order of the factors does not affect the product.
    • Formula: $a \cdot b = b \cdot a$
    • Example: $4 \cdot 7 = 28$ and $7 \cdot 4 = 28$.
    • Use: Allows you to rearrange numbers in a multiplication problem to pair numbers that multiply easily (e.g., $25 \cdot 4 = 100$).

Associative Properties

These properties deal with the grouping of numbers, typically indicated by parentheses.

  • Associative Property of Addition:
    • Definition: The way addends are grouped does not affect the sum.
    • Formula: $(a + b) + c = a + (b + c)$
    • Example: $(2 + 3) + 4 = 5 + 4 = 9$ and $2 + (3 + 4) = 2 + 7 = 9$.
    • Use: Allows you to choose which pair of numbers to add first when you have three or more numbers. This is often used with the commutative property to create easy sums.
  • Associative Property of Multiplication:
    • Definition: The way factors are grouped does not affect the product.
    • Formula: $(a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c)$
    • Example: $(2 \cdot 5) \cdot 3 = 10 \cdot 3 = 30$ and $2 \cdot (5 \cdot 3) = 2 \cdot 15 = 30$.
    • Use: Allows you to choose which pair of numbers to multiply first when you have three or more numbers. This is especially helpful for mental math to find products that are multiples of 10 or 100.

Identity Properties

These properties highlight special numbers that leave the original number unchanged.

  • Identity Property of Addition:
    • Definition: The sum of any number and $0$ is that number.
    • Formula: $a + 0 = a$
    • Example: $47 + 0 = 47$.
    • Additive Identity: The number $0$.
  • Identity Property of Multiplication:
    • Definition: The product of any number and $1$ is that number.
    • Formula: $a \cdot 1 = a$
    • Example: $86 \cdot 1 = 86$.
    • Multiplicative Identity: The number $1$.

Zero Property of Multiplication

This property describes the unique behavior of zero in multiplication.

  • Definition: The product of any number and $0$ is always $0$.
  • Formula: $a \cdot 0 = 0$
  • Example: $5 \cdot 0 = 0$.

Using Properties for Mental Math and Problem Solving

The primary benefit of understanding these properties is to simplify calculations.

Example 1: Mental Math with Multiplication To find $25 \cdot 37 \cdot 4$:

  1. Rearrange (Commutative Property): $25 \cdot 4 \cdot 37$ (Changed order of $37$ and $4$)
  2. Regroup (Associative Property): $(25 \cdot 4) \cdot 37$ (Grouped $25$ and $4$)
  3. Calculate: $100 \cdot 37 = 3,700$ (Easy multiplication by 100)

Example 2: Mental Addition To find $46 + 85 + 54$:

  1. Rearrange (Commutative Property): $46 + 54 + 85$ (Moved $54$ next to $46$)
  2. Regroup (Associative Property): $(46 + 54) + 85$ (Grouped $46$ and $54$)
  3. Calculate: $100 + 85 = 185$ (Easy addition to 100)

Example 3: Real-World Problem (Ticket Sales) If a theater sold $230$ tickets on Friday, $370$ on Saturday, and $150$ on Sunday, how many tickets were sold in all?

  • Initial setup: $230 + 370 + 150$
  • Using properties (implicitly): Often, we naturally group numbers that are easy to add. $(230 + 370) + 150$.
  • Solve: $600 + 150 = 750$ tickets.

Important Distinctions and Cautions

  • Commutative vs. Associative:
    • Commutative: Changes the order of the numbers. Example: $a+b$ becomes $b+a$.
    • Associative: Changes the grouping (parentheses) of numbers, but the numbers stay in the same relative order. Example: $(a+b)+c$ becomes $a+(b+c)$.
  • Limitations on Subtraction and Division:
    • Subtraction is NOT commutative: $7 - 3 \ne 3 - 7$ (as $4 \ne -4$).
    • Subtraction is NOT associative: $(10 - 5) - 2 \ne 10 - (5 - 2)$ (as $5 - 2 = 3$ and $10 - 3 = 7$, so $3 \ne 7$).
    • Division is NOT commutative: $12 \div 4 \ne 4 \div 12$ (as $3 \ne 1/3$).
    • Division is NOT associative: $(24 \div 4) \div 2 \ne 24 \div (4 \div 2)$ (as $6 \div 2 = 3$ and $24 \div 2 = 12$, so $3 \ne 12$).
    • These properties apply only to addition and multiplication.

Review Questions

  2. Explain the role of the number zero in the Identity Property of Addition and the Zero Property of Multiplication. How are these roles different?
  3. You are asked to multiply $18 \cdot 50 \cdot 2$. Which properties would you use to perform this calculation mentally, and what steps would you take?
  4. Why is it important to understand that the Commutative and Associative Properties do not apply to subtraction and division? Give an example for subtraction that demonstrates this non-applicability.
