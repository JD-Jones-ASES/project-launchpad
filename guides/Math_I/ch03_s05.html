Chapter 3, Section 5: Adding and Subtracting Fractions

Overview

This section explains the fundamental rules for adding and subtracting fractions. The core principle is that fractions must have a common denominator before their numerators can be combined. It covers procedures for both fractions with "like" (same) denominators and "unlike" (different) denominators, emphasizing the importance of finding the Least Common Denominator (LCD) and simplifying the final answer.

Key Concepts

  • Common Denominators are Essential: You can only add or subtract fractions if they represent pieces of the same size, which means they must have the same denominator. Think of the denominator as the "name" of the pieces.
  • Adding and Subtracting Fractions with Like Denominators: When fractions already share the same denominator, you simply add or subtract their numerators and keep the common denominator.
  • Adding and Subtracting Fractions with Unlike Denominators: If denominators are different, the process requires four steps: find the Least Common Denominator (LCD), rewrite each fraction with the LCD, add or subtract the new numerators, and then simplify the result.
  • Least Common Denominator (LCD): The smallest common multiple of the denominators of two or more fractions. It's crucial for rewriting fractions so they can be combined.
  • Simplifying Fractions: After adding or subtracting, always reduce the resulting fraction to its simplest form, where the numerator and denominator have no common factors other than 1.
  • Do Not Add or Subtract Denominators: A common mistake is to add or subtract the denominators as well as the numerators. Always remember that the denominator stays the same once a common one is found.

Important Terms

  • Numerator: The top number in a fraction, representing the number of parts being considered.
  • Denominator: The bottom number in a fraction, representing the total number of equal parts into which the whole has been divided.
  • Like Denominators: Denominators that are the same.
  • Unlike Denominators: Denominators that are different.
  • Least Common Denominator (LCD): The smallest number that is a multiple of two or more denominators. It is used to create equivalent fractions with the same denominator.
  • Simplify (a fraction): To reduce a fraction to its lowest terms by dividing both the numerator and the denominator by their greatest common factor.
  • Improper Fraction: A fraction where the numerator is greater than or equal to the denominator (e.g., 7/5).
  • Mixed Number: A number consisting of a whole number and a proper fraction (e.g., 1 and 2/5).

Detailed Notes

The Fundamental Rule: Common Denominators

Adding and subtracting fractions works much like adding and subtracting whole numbers, but with a critical initial step: the fractions involved must have the same denominator. Imagine trying to add apples and oranges – you can't get a total number of "applanges." Similarly, you can't directly add 1/3 and 1/4 because they represent pieces of different sizes. The denominator tells you the size or "name" of the pieces.

Adding and Subtracting Fractions with Like Denominators

This is the simplest case. If the fractions already share the same denominator:

  1. Add or Subtract the Numerators: Perform the indicated operation (addition or subtraction) on the top numbers (numerators).
  2. Keep the Denominator: The denominator remains unchanged.
  3. Simplify: Always reduce the resulting fraction to its simplest form, if possible.

General Formulas:

  • For addition: a/c + b/c = (a + b)/c (where c is not 0)
  • For subtraction: a/c - b/c = (a - b)/c (where c is not 0)

Examples:

  • Addition: 2/7 + 3/7
    • Since the denominators are both 7, add the numerators: 2 + 3 = 5.
    • Keep the denominator: 5/7.
    • Result: 5/7 (already in simplest form).
  • Subtraction: 5/9 - 2/9
    • Denominators are both 9, subtract numerators: 5 - 2 = 3.
    • Keep the denominator: 3/9.
    • Simplify: Divide both numerator and denominator by 3: 3 ÷ 3 / 9 ÷ 3 = 1/3.
    • Result: 1/3.

Adding and Subtracting Fractions with Unlike Denominators

When fractions have different denominators, you cannot combine them directly. You must first convert them into equivalent fractions that do have a common denominator. This is done using the Least Common Denominator (LCD).

Here are the four steps:

  1. Find the Least Common Denominator (LCD): The LCD is the smallest common multiple of all the denominators. For example, the LCD of 3 and 4 is 12. This becomes your new common denominator.
  2. Rewrite Each Fraction with the LCD: For each fraction, determine what factor you need to multiply its original denominator by to get the LCD. Then, multiply both the numerator and the denominator by that same factor. This creates an equivalent fraction with the LCD.
  3. Add or Subtract the Numerators; Keep the LCD: Once all fractions have the LCD, perform the addition or subtraction operation on the numerators, keeping the common denominator the same.
  4. Simplify the Result if Possible: Always reduce your final answer to its simplest form.

Examples:

  • Adding Fractions with Unlike Denominators: 2/3 + 1/4

    • Step 1: Find the LCD. The multiples of 3 are 3, 6, 9, 12, 15... The multiples of 4 are 4, 8, 12, 16... The LCD of 3 and 4 is 12.
    • Step 2: Rewrite each fraction with the LCD.
      • For 2/3: To get 12 in the denominator, multiply 3 by 4. So, multiply numerator and denominator by 4: (2 * 4) / (3 * 4) = 8/12.
      • For 1/4: To get 12 in the denominator, multiply 4 by 3. So, multiply numerator and denominator by 3: (1 * 3) / (4 * 3) = 3/12.
    • Step 3: Add the numerators; keep the LCD. Now you have 8/12 + 3/12. Add the numerators: 8 + 3 = 11. Keep the denominator: 11/12.
    • Step 4: Simplify. 11/12 is already in simplest form because 11 and 12 have no common factors other than 1.
    • Result: 11/12.

  • Subtracting Fractions with Unlike Denominators: 5/6 - 3/8

    • Step 1: Find the LCD. The multiples of 6 are 6, 12, 18, 24, 30... The multiples of 8 are 8, 16, 24, 32... The LCD of 6 and 8 is 24.
    • Step 2: Rewrite each fraction with the LCD.
      • For 5/6: Multiply by 4/4: (5 * 4) / (6 * 4) = 20/24.
      • For 3/8: Multiply by 3/3: (3 * 3) / (8 * 3) = 9/24.
    • Step 3: Subtract the numerators; keep the LCD. Now you have 20/24 - 9/24. Subtract the numerators: 20 - 9 = 11. Keep the denominator: 11/24.
    • Step 4: Simplify. 11/24 is already in simplest form.
    • Result: 11/24.

Applying Fraction Operations to Word Problems

Fraction addition and subtraction are common in everyday situations. For example, recipes often involve fractions.

Recipe Problem: A muffin recipe calls for 2/3 cup of white flour and 1/4 cup of wheat flour. How much flour is needed in all?

  1. Read and Identify: The problem asks for the total amount of flour, indicating addition: 2/3 + 1/4.
  2. Solve using the steps for unlike denominators:
    • LCD of 3 and 4 is 12.
    • Rewrite 2/3 as (2 * 4) / (3 * 4) = 8/12.
    • Rewrite 1/4 as (1 * 3) / (4 * 3) = 3/12.
    • Add: 8/12 + 3/12 = 11/12.
  3. Check and State Answer: 11/12 is in simplest form. The recipe needs 11/12 cup of flour in all.

Important Considerations and Common Mistakes

  • Always Simplify: Make it a habit to simplify your final fraction. For example, 4/10 should always be simplified to 2/5.
  • Improper Fractions and Mixed Numbers: Depending on the context of the problem, you may need to convert an improper fraction result (where the numerator is larger than or equal to the denominator, e.g., 7/5) into a mixed number (e.g., 1 and 2/5).
  • NEVER Add or Subtract Denominators: This is a crucial mistake to avoid. 1/3 + 1/4 is not 2/7. You must find a common denominator first. Remember, the denominator indicates the size of the pieces; changing it would mean you're no longer talking about the same-sized pieces.
