Chapter 3, Section 5: Adding and Subtracting Fractions Study Guide

Overview

This section explains how to add and subtract fractions, emphasizing the crucial rule that fractions must share a common denominator before they can be combined. It covers procedures for both fractions already having the same denominator and those with different denominators, guiding students through finding the least common denominator (LCD) and simplifying results.

Key Concepts

  • Common Denominators are Essential: You can only add or subtract fractions if they represent pieces of the same size, meaning they must have the same denominator. Think of the denominator as the "name" of the fraction's pieces.
  • Adding/Subtracting with Like Denominators: When fractions already share the same denominator, simply add or subtract their numerators and keep the denominator the same.
  • Adding/Subtracting with Unlike Denominators: If fractions have different denominators, you must first convert them into equivalent fractions that do share a common denominator. The most efficient way to do this is by finding the Least Common Denominator (LCD).
  • Four-Step Process for Unlike Denominators: The procedure involves: 1) finding the LCD, 2) rewriting each fraction with the LCD, 3) adding or subtracting the new numerators (keeping the LCD), and 4) simplifying the result.
  • Simplifying the Result: After adding or subtracting, always check if the resulting fraction can be simplified (reduced to its lowest terms) or converted to a mixed number if it's an improper fraction and the context requires it.
  • Never Add or Subtract Denominators: A critical rule to remember is that denominators are never added or subtracted. Doing so changes the fundamental size of the pieces, leading to an incorrect answer.

Important Terms

  • Fraction: A number representing a part of a whole, written as a numerator over a denominator (e.g., 2/3).
  • Numerator: The top number in a fraction, indicating how many parts of the whole are being considered.
  • Denominator: The bottom number in a fraction, indicating the total number of equal parts into which the whole has been divided.
  • Like Denominators: Denominators that are the same.
  • Unlike Denominators: Denominators that are different.
  • Least Common Denominator (LCD): The smallest common multiple of the denominators of two or more fractions. It's the smallest number that all denominators divide into evenly.
  • Equivalent Fractions: Fractions that represent the same value, even though they have different numerators and denominators (e.g., 1/2 and 2/4).
  • Simplify (Reduce) a Fraction: To express a fraction in its lowest terms, where the numerator and denominator have no common factors other than 1.
  • Improper Fraction: A fraction where the numerator is greater than or equal to the denominator (e.g., 7/5).
  • Mixed Number: A number consisting of a whole number and a proper fraction (e.g., 1 2/5).

Detailed Notes

The Core Principle: Same-Sized Pieces

Adding and subtracting fractions works similarly to adding and subtracting whole numbers, but with one critical prerequisite: the fractions must refer to pieces of the same size. Imagine you have two slices of a pizza cut into 8 pieces and three slices of a pizza cut into 8 pieces. You can easily see you have a total of five slices (5/8 of the pizza). However, if one pizza is cut into 8 pieces and another into 6 pieces, you can't simply add the "number of slices" directly because the slices are different sizes. This is why fractions need a common denominator – it ensures all pieces being combined or separated are of equal size.

Adding and Subtracting Fractions with Like Denominators

When fractions already share the same denominator (they are "like" fractions), the process is straightforward:

  1. Add or subtract the numerators.
  2. Keep the denominator the same.
  3. Simplify the result if possible.

Mathematical Rule:

  • For addition: a/c + b/c = (a + b)/c (where c is not 0)
  • For subtraction: a/c - b/c = (a - b)/c (where c is not 0)

Example: Add 3/10 + 1/10.

  • The denominators are already the same (10).
  • Add the numerators: 3 + 1 = 4.
  • Keep the denominator: 4/10.
  • Simplify: 4/10 can be divided by 2 (common factor of 4 and 10) to get 2/5.
    • Result: 2/5

Adding and Subtracting Fractions with Unlike Denominators

When fractions have different denominators (they are "unlike" fractions), you must first convert them into equivalent fractions that share a common denominator. This is typically done using the Least Common Denominator (LCD).

Here's the four-step process:

  1. Find the Least Common Denominator (LCD): The LCD is the smallest number that is a multiple of all the denominators involved. It's the least common multiple (LCM) of the denominators. Using the LCD makes calculations easier and often results in a fraction that is already simplified or requires less simplification.
  2. Rewrite Each Fraction with the LCD: Convert each original fraction into an equivalent fraction that has the LCD as its new denominator. To do this, determine what you need to multiply the original denominator by to get the LCD, then multiply the numerator by the same number.
  3. Add or Subtract the Numerators: Once all fractions have the same (LCD) denominator, add or subtract their numerators, keeping the LCD as the denominator for the sum or difference.
  4. Simplify the Result: Always reduce the final fraction to its lowest terms. If the result is an improper fraction, you might convert it to a mixed number depending on the context of the problem.

Example 1: Adding Fractions with Unlike Denominators Add 2/3 + 1/4.

  • Step 1: Find the LCD.

    • Multiples of 3: 3, 6, 9, 12, 15...
    • Multiples of 4: 4, 8, 12, 16...
    • The LCD of 3 and 4 is 12.

  • Step 2: Rewrite each fraction with the LCD.

    • For 2/3: To get a denominator of 12, multiply 3 by 4. So, multiply the numerator by 4 as well: (2 * 4) / (3 * 4) = 8/12.
    • For 1/4: To get a denominator of 12, multiply 4 by 3. So, multiply the numerator by 3 as well: (1 * 3) / (4 * 3) = 3/12.

  • Step 3: Add the numerators (keep the LCD).

    • Now you have 8/12 + 3/12.
    • Add the numerators: 8 + 3 = 11.
    • Keep the denominator: 11/12.

  • Step 4: Simplify the result.

    • 11/12 is already in simplest form because 11 and 12 have no common factors other than 1.
    • Result: 11/12

Example 2: Subtracting Fractions with Unlike Denominators Subtract 5/6 - 3/8.

  • Step 1: Find the LCD.

    • Multiples of 6: 6, 12, 18, 24, 30...
    • Multiples of 8: 8, 16, 24, 32...
    • The LCD of 6 and 8 is 24.

  • Step 2: Rewrite each fraction with the LCD.

    • For 5/6: (5 * 4) / (6 * 4) = 20/24.
    • For 3/8: (3 * 3) / (8 * 3) = 9/24.

  • Step 3: Subtract the numerators (keep the LCD).

    • Now you have 20/24 - 9/24.
    • Subtract the numerators: 20 - 9 = 11.
    • Keep the denominator: 11/24.

  • Step 4: Simplify the result.

    • 11/24 is already in simplest form.
    • Result: 11/24

Applying Fractions to Word Problems

When solving word problems involving adding or subtracting fractions, follow these general problem-solving steps:

  1. Read: Understand the problem and what it's asking.
  2. Identify: Determine the mathematical operation needed (addition or subtraction of fractions).
  3. Write: Set up the equation or expression using the given fractions.
  4. Solve: Apply the appropriate steps for adding or subtracting fractions (find LCD if necessary, rewrite, combine, simplify).
  5. Check: Review your answer to ensure it makes sense in the context of the problem and is in simplest form.

Example: Recipe Problem A muffin recipe calls for 2/3 cup of white flour and 1/4 cup of wheat flour. How much flour is needed in all?

  • Read: The problem asks for the total amount of flour.
  • Identify: This is an addition problem: 2/3 + 1/4.
  • Write: 2/3 + 1/4
  • Solve:
    • LCD of 3 and 4 is 12.
    • Rewrite: 2/3 = 8/12 and 1/4 = 3/12.
    • Add: 8/12 + 3/12 = 11/12.
  • Check: 11/12 is in simplest form and is a reasonable amount.
  • Answer: The recipe needs 11/12 cup of flour in all.

Important Considerations and Common Mistakes

  • Always Simplify: Make it a habit to simplify every fraction result to its lowest terms. This makes answers clearer and easier to work with.
  • Improper Fractions and Mixed Numbers: Depending on the context of the problem, an improper fraction (like 7/5) might need to be converted to a mixed number (like 1 2/5). For example, in a recipe or measurement context, a mixed number is often more practical.
  • Do NOT Add or Subtract Denominators! This is a very common mistake. Remember, denominators represent the type or size of the pieces. Adding or subtracting them would fundamentally change what the fraction represents. 1/3 + 1/4 is definitely not 2/7. You must find a common denominator first to correctly combine the "pieces."

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental principle that must be satisfied before you can add or subtract two fractions. Why is this principle so important?
  2. Outline the complete, step-by-step process for adding or subtracting two fractions that have unlike denominators.
  3. What is the purpose of finding the Least Common Denominator (LCD) when adding or subtracting fractions with unlike denominators? Why is it generally preferred over just finding any common denominator?
  4. A common mistake is to add the denominators when adding fractions (e.g., claiming 1/2 + 1/3 = 2/5). Explain, using a real-world analogy (like pizza slices or candy bars), why this approach is incorrect.
  5. Describe a situation or type of problem where it would be more appropriate to express your final answer as a mixed number rather than an improper fraction. Provide a simple example of such a scenario.
