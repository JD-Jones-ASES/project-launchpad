Chapter 6, Section 3: Proportions and Cross Multiplication

Overview

This section introduces proportions as equations stating the equality of two ratios. It teaches how to verify if two ratios form a proportion using the Cross Products Property and, most importantly, how to solve for an unknown value in a proportion using cross multiplication. The concepts are then applied to practical problems, including those involving rates and similar figures.

Key Concepts

  • Proportion: An equation that declares two ratios are equivalent. For example, $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}$ is read as "$a$ is to $b$ as $c$ is to $d$."
  • Cross Products Property: A fundamental property of proportions stating that if $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}$ (where $b \neq 0$ and $d \neq 0$), then the product of the "cross" terms are equal: $a \cdot d = b \cdot c$. This property also works in reverse.
  • Determining Proportions: To check if two ratios form a proportion, calculate their cross products. If the cross products are equal, the ratios form a proportion.
  • Solving Proportions: To find an unknown value in a proportion, use the Cross Products Property to set up an equation, then solve for the variable.
  • Setting Up Proportions from Context: When dealing with word problems, it's crucial to set up the ratios consistently. Ensure that corresponding units or quantities are in the same position (e.g., both numerators represent bottles, and both denominators represent minutes).
  • Applications: Proportions are widely used to solve problems involving rates, scaling (like in similar figures), and other real-world scenarios where quantities are related proportionally.

Important Terms

  • Proportion: An equation that states two ratios are equal, typically written in the form $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}$.
  • Cross Products Property: A property of proportions that states if $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}$, then $a \cdot d = b \cdot c$. This provides a method for verifying proportions and solving for unknown values.

Detailed Notes

What is a Proportion?

A proportion is essentially an equation where two ratios are set equal to each other. For example, if you have the ratio of apples to bananas as $1:2$, and another ratio of apples to bananas as $2:4$, these two ratios are equivalent, and therefore, they form a proportion: $\frac{1}{2} = \frac{2}{4}$. Proportions are powerful because they allow us to find missing values when we know a relationship between quantities.

The Cross Products Property

The most important tool for working with proportions is the Cross Products Property.

  • Statement: If you have a proportion $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}$ (with $b \neq 0$ and $d \neq 0$), then the product of the "cross" terms are equal: $a \cdot d = b \cdot c$.
  • How it works: Imagine drawing an 'X' across the equals sign in the proportion. You multiply the top-left with the bottom-right ($a \cdot d$) and the bottom-left with the top-right ($b \cdot c$). These two products will always be equal if it's a true proportion.
  • Shortcut: Cross multiplication is a quicker way to clear denominators than multiplying both sides of the equation by a common denominator or by both denominators ($bd$). It's important to remember it only works when you have a single fraction on each side of the equals sign.

Determining Whether Two Ratios Form a Proportion

To check if two ratios are indeed proportional, you can use the Cross Products Property.

  • Method: Calculate the cross product for each pair. If the resulting products are equal, then the ratios form a proportion.
  • Example: Do $\frac{3}{4}$ and $\frac{9}{12}$ form a proportion?
    • First cross product: $3 \times 12 = 36$
    • Second cross product: $4 \times 9 = 36$
    • Since $36 = 36$, the cross products are equal, so $\frac{3}{4} = \frac{9}{12}$ is a proportion.
  • Non-Example: Do $\frac{5}{8}$ and $\frac{15}{20}$ form a proportion?
    • First cross product: $5 \times 20 = 100$
    • Second cross product: $8 \times 15 = 120$
    • Since $100 \neq 120$, these ratios do not form a proportion.

Solving a Proportion by Cross Multiplication

When one of the values in a proportion is unknown (represented by a variable), you can use cross multiplication to solve for it.

  • Steps:
    1. Set up the proportion equation.
    2. Apply the Cross Products Property to create an algebraic equation.
    3. Solve the resulting equation for the variable.
  • Example: Solve $\frac{n}{6} = \frac{10}{15}$.
    1. Cross multiply: $n \times 15 = 6 \times 10$
    2. Simplify: $15n = 60$
    3. Divide both sides by $15$: $n = \frac{60}{15}$
    4. Solution: $n = 4$.
  • Example: Solve $\frac{8}{n} = \frac{12}{9}$.
    1. Cross multiply: $8 \times 9 = n \times 12$
    2. Simplify: $72 = 12n$
    3. Divide both sides by $12$: $n = \frac{72}{12}$
    4. Solution: $n = 6$.

Setting Up a Proportion from Context

Word problems often require you to set up the proportion yourself before solving. The most critical rule is to maintain consistency in how you set up your ratios.

  • Consistency Rule: Whatever units or categories you place in the numerator of the first ratio must also be in the numerator of the second ratio. Similarly, for the denominators.
    • If you put (bottles)/(minutes) for the first ratio, the second ratio must also be (bottles)/(minutes).
    • You can also set it up as (bottles 1)/(bottles 2) = (minutes 1)/(minutes 2), as long as the corresponding items are aligned.
  • Example (Rates): A machine fills $5$ bottles every $2$ minutes. How many bottles does it fill in $14$ minutes?
    • Set up: $\frac{5 \text{ bottles}}{2 \text{ min}} = \frac{n \text{ bottles}}{14 \text{ min}}$
    • Cross multiply: $5 \times 14 = 2 \times n$
    • Solve: $70 = 2n \Rightarrow n = 35$. The machine fills 35 bottles.

Proportions and Similar Figures

Proportions are fundamental to understanding similar figures (figures that have the same shape but different sizes). In similar figures, corresponding sides are proportional.

  • Key Idea: The ratio of any two corresponding sides in similar figures will be equal.
  • Example (Similar Rectangles): A small rectangle is $4$ cm wide and $6$ cm long. A larger, similar rectangle is $10$ cm wide. What is its length?
    1. Identify Corresponding Sides: Small width = 4 cm, Large width = 10 cm. Small length = 6 cm, Large length = $L$ cm (unknown).
    2. Set Up Proportion: You can set it up as (small width)/(large width) = (small length)/(large length). $\frac{4 \text{ cm (small width)}}{10 \text{ cm (large width)}} = \frac{6 \text{ cm (small length)}}{L \text{ cm (large length)}}$
    3. Cross Multiply: $4 \times L = 10 \times 6$
    4. Solve: $4L = 60 \Rightarrow L = \frac{60}{4} \Rightarrow L = 15$. The larger rectangle's length is 15 cm.
    5. Check: $\frac{4}{10} = \frac{2}{5}$ and $\frac{6}{15} = \frac{2}{5}$. Since the ratios are equal, the answer is correct.

Summary of Proportions and Cross Multiplication:

  • A proportion equates two ratios: $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}$.
  • The Cross Products Property states that if $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}$, then $a \cdot d = b \cdot c$.
  • To solve a proportion for an unknown, use cross multiplication, then isolate the variable.
  • Always ensure consistent unit placement when setting up proportions from word problems to avoid errors.

Review Questions

  1. Define what a proportion is in your own words. How does the Cross Products Property help us understand and work with proportions?
  2. Describe the step-by-step process you would use to determine if the ratios $\frac{7}{12}$ and $\frac{21}{36}$ form a proportion. Show your work.
  3. Explain how cross multiplication is used to solve for an unknown variable in a proportion. Illustrate with an example of your own invention, like solving $\frac{x}{7} = \frac{15}{21}$.
  4. A recipe calls for $3$ cups of flour for every $2$ cups of sugar. If you want to use $9$ cups of flour, how much sugar do you need? Set up the proportion and solve, explaining the importance of consistent unit placement.
  5. Two triangles are similar. The first triangle has sides of $5$ inches, $12$ inches, and $13$ inches. The longest side of the second triangle is $39$ inches. What are the lengths of the other two sides of the second triangle? Explain how proportions are used here.
