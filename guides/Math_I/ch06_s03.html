Proportions and Cross Multiplication Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces proportions as equations stating the equality of two ratios. It teaches how to determine if two ratios form a proportion using the Cross Products Property, and how to use cross multiplication as a powerful tool to solve for unknown values in proportional relationships. The content also covers setting up proportions from real-world contexts and when dealing with similar figures.

Key Concepts

  • Proportion: An equation that states two ratios are equal. For example, a/b = c/d.
  • Cross Products Property: If two ratios form a proportion (a/b = c/d), then their cross products are equal (a * d = b * c). This property is fundamental for verifying proportions and solving for unknowns. It also works in reverse: if a * d = b * c, then a/b = c/d.
  • Cross Multiplication: A shortcut derived from the Cross Products Property, used to solve proportions where there is a single fraction on each side of an equals sign. It involves multiplying the numerator of one ratio by the denominator of the other.
  • Solving Proportions: The process typically involves applying the Cross Products Property (cross multiplying) to convert the proportion into a simpler algebraic equation, then solving for the unknown variable using inverse operations (usually division).
  • Setting Up Proportions from Context: When dealing with word problems or similar figures, it is crucial to set up the proportion correctly. This means ensuring that corresponding quantities (e.g., units, sides of figures) are in the same relative positions (both numerators or both denominators) in both ratios.

Important Terms

  • Proportion: An equation that states that two ratios are equal. Written as a/b = c/d (where b ≠ 0 and d ≠ 0).
  • Cross Products Property: The mathematical rule that states for a proportion a/b = c/d, the product of the numerator of the first ratio and the denominator of the second ratio (a * d) is equal to the product of the denominator of the first ratio and the numerator of the second ratio (b * c).
  • Cross Multiplication: The technique of applying the Cross Products Property to solve an equation involving two equal ratios. It simplifies a/b = c/d into a * d = b * c.
  • Similar Figures: Figures that have the same shape but not necessarily the same size. Their corresponding angles are equal, and their corresponding sides are proportional.

Detailed Notes

What is a Proportion?

A proportion is essentially an equation where two ratios are set equal to each other. For instance, a/b = c/d is a proportion. We read this as "a is to b as c is to d." The values b and d cannot be zero, as division by zero is undefined. Proportions are incredibly useful for solving problems where quantities change at a constant rate or maintain a consistent relationship.

The Cross Products Property

This property is the cornerstone of working with proportions. Statement: If a/b = c/d (with b ≠ 0 and d ≠ 0), then their cross products are equal: a * d = b * c. How it works: Imagine multiplying both sides of a/b = c/d by b * d (the product of the denominators). (b * d) * (a/b) = (b * d) * (c/d) d * a = b * c or a * d = b * c. This property also works in reverse: if a * d = b * c, then a/b = c/d. This means you can use cross products to verify if two ratios form a proportion.

Cross Multiplication as a Shortcut

Cross multiplication is the practical application of the Cross Products Property. It's a quick way to clear the denominators in a proportion, simplifying it into a linear equation. Important Note: This method only works when you have a single fraction (ratio) on each side of the equals sign.

Determining Whether Two Ratios Form a Proportion

To check if two ratios are proportional, calculate their cross products. If the cross products are equal, the ratios form a proportion.

Example: Do 3/4 and 9/12 form a proportion?

  1. Set them up as a potential proportion: 3/4 = 9/12
  2. Find the cross products:
    • 3 * 12 = 36
    • 4 * 9 = 36
  3. Since 36 = 36, the cross products are equal. Therefore, 3/4 and 9/12 form a proportion.

Solving a Proportion by Cross Multiplication

To solve for an unknown variable in a proportion:

  1. Set up the equation with the unknown.
  2. Apply cross multiplication to get rid of the denominators.
  3. Solve the resulting linear equation for the variable.

Example: Solve n/6 = 10/15

  1. Cross multiply: n * 15 = 6 * 10
  2. Simplify: 15n = 60
  3. Divide both sides by 15: n = 60 / 15
  4. Solve: n = 4

Setting Up a Proportion from Context

When solving word problems, the most critical step is correctly setting up the proportion. You must maintain consistency in how you arrange your units or corresponding quantities.

Rule: If the first ratio has (Unit A / Unit B), the second ratio must also have (Unit A / Unit B).

Example: A machine fills 5 bottles every 2 minutes. At the same rate, how many bottles does it fill in 14 minutes?

  1. Identify the units: bottles and minutes.
  2. Choose a consistent setup, e.g., (bottles / minutes).
  3. Set up the first ratio: 5 bottles / 2 minutes
  4. Set up the second ratio with the unknown n for bottles: n bottles / 14 minutes
  5. Form the proportion: 5/2 = n/14
  6. Cross multiply: 5 * 14 = 2 * n
  7. Simplify: 70 = 2n
  8. Solve: n = 70 / 2 = 35 The machine fills 35 bottles in 14 minutes.

Proportions and Similar Figures

Similar figures have corresponding sides that are proportional. This means you can set up proportions to find unknown side lengths.

Example: Two rectangles are similar. The smaller has a width of 4 cm and a length of 6 cm. If the larger has a width of 10 cm, what is its length?

  1. Identify corresponding sides:
    • Small width: 4 cm, Large width: 10 cm
    • Small length: 6 cm, Large length: x cm (unknown)
  2. Set up the proportion, ensuring consistency (e.g., small width / large width = small length / large length): 4/10 = 6/x
  3. Cross multiply: 4 * x = 10 * 6
  4. Simplify: 4x = 60
  5. Solve: x = 60 / 4 = 15 The length of the larger rectangle is 15 cm. Self-Check: 4/10 = 2/5 and 6/15 = 2/5, so the proportion is correct.

Caution: Always double-check that corresponding parts or units are in the same positions in both ratios of your proportion. Mixing them up will lead to incorrect answers.

Review Questions

  1. What is a proportion, and how is it fundamentally different from a single ratio? Provide an example of a proportion.
  2. Explain the Cross Products Property in your own words. Why is it a useful tool for both checking and solving proportions?
  3. Describe the step-by-step process for solving a proportion for an unknown variable using cross multiplication.
  4. When setting up a proportion from a word problem (like the bottles-per-minute example), why is it crucial to keep the units in consistent positions within both ratios? What might happen if you don't?
  5. How are proportions used in geometry when dealing with similar figures? If you have two similar triangles, how would you set up a proportion to find an unknown side length?
