Adding and Subtracting Decimals Study Guide

Overview

This section explains the fundamental rules for adding and subtracting decimals. The core principle involves aligning the decimal points to ensure digits of the same place value are combined accurately. We will cover the steps for performing these operations, including how to handle whole numbers and real-world scenarios like calculating with money.

Key Concepts

  • Aligning Decimal Points: When adding or subtracting decimals, the most crucial first step is to write the numbers vertically and line up their decimal points. This ensures that you are adding or subtracting digits in the correct place value (ones with ones, tenths with tenths, etc.).
  • Annexing Zeros: To make calculations easier and prevent errors, add zeros to the end of a decimal number (after the last non-zero digit) so that all numbers involved in the operation have the same number of decimal places. This doesn't change the value of the number.
  • Adding/Subtracting as Whole Numbers: Once the decimal points are aligned and zeros are annexed, you can perform the addition or subtraction just as you would with whole numbers, carrying over or borrowing as necessary.
  • Placing the Decimal Point in the Answer: The decimal point in your final answer will be placed directly below the aligned decimal points in the numbers you added or subtracted.
  • Handling Whole Numbers: When a whole number is part of a decimal operation, imagine a decimal point at the end of the whole number (e.g., 12 becomes 12.00) and then proceed with aligning and annexing zeros.
  • Real-World Applications: These skills are vital for practical situations like managing money, calculating distances, and understanding measurements.
  • Estimation: It's good practice to estimate your answer before or after performing the exact calculation to check if your result is reasonable.

Important Terms

  • Sum: The result obtained when two or more numbers are added together.
  • Difference: The result obtained when one number is subtracted from another number.
  • Annex Zeros: To add zeros to the end of a decimal number to ensure all numbers have the same number of decimal places for easier calculation.

Detailed Notes

The Foundation: Like Whole Numbers, But with a Twist

Adding and subtracting decimals is very similar to adding and subtracting whole numbers. The main difference, and the most critical step, is correctly aligning the decimal points.

The Rule for Adding and Subtracting Decimals

Follow these steps for accurate decimal operations:

  1. Write Vertically and Line Up Decimal Points: Arrange the numbers one above the other so that their decimal points are in a straight vertical line. This ensures that digits representing the same place value (ones, tens, tenths, hundredths, etc.) are positioned directly above or below each other.
  2. Annex Zeros: If the numbers have a different number of decimal places, add zeros to the end of the number(s) with fewer decimal places until all numbers have the same quantity of digits after the decimal point. This makes the columns complete and reduces the chance of errors. For example, to add 3.45 and 12.7, you'd rewrite 12.7 as 12.70.
  3. Add or Subtract as with Whole Numbers: Once the decimal points are aligned and zeros are annexed, perform the operation (addition or subtraction) column by column, starting from the rightmost digit, just like you would with whole numbers. Remember to carry over in addition and borrow in subtraction when necessary.
  4. Place the Decimal Point: In your final answer, place the decimal point directly below the line formed by the decimal points of the numbers you were adding or subtracting.

Special Considerations

  • Whole Numbers and Decimals: When a problem involves both whole numbers and decimals, treat the whole number as if it has a decimal point at its end. For example, the number 12 can be written as 12.0, 12.00, 12.000, and so on, depending on how many decimal places are needed for alignment.

  • Adding Multiple Decimals: The same rules apply when adding more than two decimal numbers. Line up all the decimal points, annex zeros to ensure all numbers have the same number of decimal places, and then add column by column.

    • Example: Adding Multiple Decimals To find $4.2 + 0.85 + 13.007$:
        4.200  (annexed two zeros)
  0.850  (annexed one zero)
+ 13.007
-------
  18.057

  • Subtracting with Borrowing: Subtracting decimals often requires borrowing, just like with whole numbers. When a digit in the top number is smaller than the digit below it in the same column, you must borrow from the digit to its left. Annexing zeros is especially helpful here to ensure you have enough digits to borrow from.

    • Example: Subtracting Decimals with Borrowing To find $20.3 - 8.47$:
        20.30  (annexed one zero)
-  8.47
-------
  11.83
      In the hundredths column, you can't subtract 7 from 0, so you borrow from the 3 in the tenths column, making it 2 and the 0 become 10. Then 10 - 7 = 3. In the tenths column, you can't subtract 4 from 2, so you borrow from the 0 in the ones column (which itself needs to borrow from the 2 in the tens column), making it 12. Then 12 - 4 = 8. Continue this process.

Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

  • Misaligning Decimal Points: This is the most common mistake. Always line up the decimal points, not the last digits. Forgetting this will lead to incorrect place value calculations.
    • Incorrect:
        3.45
+ 12.7
------- (Misaligned, will lead to wrong answer)
    • Correct:
        3.45
+ 12.70 (aligned and zero annexed)
-------

Solving Word Problems

When tackling word problems involving decimals, especially those with money, follow a structured approach:

  1. Read: Understand what the problem is asking.

  2. Identify: Determine what operations (addition, subtraction) are needed to find the solution.

  3. Write: Set up the problem as a mathematical expression or equation.

  4. Solve: Perform the calculations carefully, applying the rules for adding and subtracting decimals.

  5. Check: Does your answer make sense in the context of the problem? You can often do a quick estimation to verify.

    • Example: Making Change (Money Word Problem) Mia buys a notebook for $3.79 and a pen for $1.54. She pays with a $10 bill. How much change does she receive?
      • Step 1 & 2 (Identify): Need to find the total cost of items (addition) and then subtract that total from the amount paid ($10.00).
      • Step 3 (Write): Change = $10.00 - ($3.79 + $1.54)
      • Step 4 (Solve):
        • First, add the costs: $3.79 + $1.54 = $5.33
        • Then, subtract from $10.00: $10.00 - $5.33 = $4.67
      • Step 5 (Check): $5.33 + $4.67 = $10.00. The answer is reasonable.
      • Answer: Mia receives $4.67 in change.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words why lining up the decimal points is the most important first step when adding or subtracting decimals. What kind of error could occur if you don't?
  2. Describe the process of "annexing zeros" when working with decimals. Provide an example of a subtraction problem where annexing zeros is critical for finding the correct answer.
  3. How would you handle a problem that asks you to subtract a decimal number from a whole number, such as $25 - 8.35$? Explain the steps you would take.
  4. You are adding $5.6$, $0.23$, and $14$. Demonstrate how you would set up this problem vertically to ensure accurate addition, including any necessary steps like annexing zeros.
  5. Imagine you have $50.00. You buy an item for $18.75 and another for $6.20. Write out the steps, including the calculations, to determine how much money you have left.
