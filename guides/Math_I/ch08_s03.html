Study Guide: Translating Words to Algebraic Expressions

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental skill of converting everyday language into mathematical expressions and equations. It emphasizes recognizing keywords that signal specific arithmetic operations and understanding how the order of words, especially with phrases like "less than," impacts the algebraic representation. By mastering this translation, you can set up and solve real-world problems using the power of algebra.

Key Concepts

  • Algebra as a Language: Just like translating between spoken languages, algebra allows us to translate word phrases into symbolic mathematical expressions.
  • Keywords for Operations: Specific words or phrases consistently indicate addition, subtraction, multiplication, or division. Identifying these is crucial for correct translation.
  • Order Matters in Subtraction: Phrases like "less than" and "subtracted from" require a reversal of the numbers or variables in the algebraic expression compared to their order in the word phrase (e.g., "5 less than n" translates to n - 5).
  • Translating Multiple Operations: Complex word phrases can be broken down into smaller parts, translated individually, and then combined into a single algebraic expression.
  • Forming Equations: An algebraic expression can be set equal to a known value to create an equation, which can then be solved to find an unknown quantity.
  • Problem-Solving Strategy: A structured approach (Read, Identify, Write, Solve, Check) helps in converting word problems into solvable algebraic equations.

Important Terms

  • Algebraic Expression: A mathematical phrase that contains numbers, variables, and operation symbols, but no equality sign. Examples: n + 15, 6n, 2n + 7.
  • Variable: A symbol, usually a letter (like n or t), used to represent an unknown number or quantity in an expression or equation.
  • Equation: A mathematical statement that shows two expressions are equal. It contains an equality sign (=). Example: 8.50t = 51.

Detailed Notes

The Language of Algebra

Algebra provides a precise way to describe relationships between quantities using symbols. The first step in algebra is learning how to translate common word phrases into their symbolic algebraic forms.

Keywords for Operations

It's essential to recognize the words that signal each of the basic arithmetic operations:

  • Addition (+):

    • Keywords: "more than," "increased by," "sum of," "added to."
    • Example: "15 more than a number n."
      • Translation: n + 15
    • Example: "7 more than..."
      • Translation: + 7 (as part of a larger expression)

  • *Multiplication (×, , or implied by juxtaposition):

    • Keywords: "times," "product of," "multiplied by," "of," "twice," "triple," "per."
    • Example: "6 times a number n."
      • Translation: 6n
    • Example: "twice a number n."
      • Translation: 2n
    • Example: "triple a number n."
      • Translation: 3n
    • Example: "$8.50$ per hour (t hours)."
      • Translation: 8.50t

  • Division (÷, /):

    • Keywords: "divided by," "quotient of," "ratio of," "per."
    • Example: "a number n divided by 4."
      • Translation: n / 4 (or n ÷ 4 or n/4 often written as a fraction n/4)

  • Subtraction (-):

    • Keywords: "less than," "subtracted from," "difference between," "decreased by."
    • Crucial Note on Order: For "less than" and "subtracted from," the order of the numbers or variables is reversed in the algebraic expression.
      • Example: "$5$ less than n."
        • Translation: n - 5 (NOT 5 - n)
      • Example: "$10$ less than triple a number n."
        • Translation: 3n - 10
      • Caution: Read carefully! "4 less than n" is n - 4. However, "n less than 4" is 4 - n.

Translating Expressions with Multiple Operations

Many word phrases involve more than one operation. To translate these, break the phrase down into smaller, manageable parts.

  • Example: "7 more than twice a number n."
    1. First, translate "twice a number n": 2n
    2. Then, translate "7 more than" that result: + 7
    3. Combine them: 2n + 7

Setting Up an Equation from a Word Problem

Often, after translating a phrase into an expression, you'll need to use that expression to form an equation and solve a problem. Follow these steps:

Example: Rosa's Earnings Problem Rosa earns $8.50 per hour. She earned $51 last week. Write an expression for her earnings if she works t hours, then write an equation to find how many hours she worked.

  1. Step 1: Read. Understand the problem. Rosa's hourly wage is $8.50, and total earnings were $51.
  2. Step 2: Identify.
    • Define a variable: Let t = number of hours Rosa worked.
    • Identify keywords: "Per hour" signals multiplication.
  3. Step 3: Write.
    • Expression for earnings: Since she earns $8.50 per hour, for t hours, her earnings are 8.50t.
    • Equation: We know her total earnings were $51. So, set the expression equal to the total: 8.50t = 51.
  4. Step 4: Solve.
    • 8.50t = 51
    • To find t, divide both sides by 8.50: t = 51 / 8.50
    • t = 6
  5. Step 5: Check.
    • Substitute t = 6 back into the original equation: 8.50 * 6 = 51
    • 51 = 51. The solution is correct.

Conclusion: Rosa worked 6 hours.

Summary of Translating Words to Expressions

  • Always identify the keywords that indicate each operation (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division).
  • Translate the phrase piece by piece, building up the expression.
  • Be especially careful with "less than" and "subtracted from" phrases, as they reverse the order of the terms in the expression.
  • Once you have an expression, you can often use it to form an equation by setting it equal to a known value given in the problem.
