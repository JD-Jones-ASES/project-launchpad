Thomson's Violinist: A Study Guide

Overview

Thomson's Violinist is a pivotal thought experiment in applied ethics, introduced by Judith Jarvis Thomson in 1971. Its primary purpose is to differentiate between an individual's right to life and the notion that this right automatically implies an obligation for others to provide bodily support to sustain that life, especially at significant personal cost. The scenario highlights the importance of bodily autonomy and the distinction between actively causing harm and declining to offer extraordinary aid.

Key Concepts

  • Thomson's Violinist Thought Experiment: A hypothetical scenario where an individual is involuntarily connected to a famous violinist with a fatal kidney ailment for nine months, acting as a life-support system. It aims to explore the limits of moral obligation and the nature of rights.
  • Right to Life vs. Right to Use Another's Body: Thomson argues that even if one grants another person a full right to life, this does not automatically extend to a right to use another person's body or resources to sustain that life, especially without consent or at significant personal cost.
  • Killing vs. Declining to Sustain: A fundamental distinction made by Thomson. Disconnecting from the violinist (declining to sustain) is not morally equivalent to actively killing them, particularly when the connection was involuntary and demands extraordinary sacrifice.
  • Bodily Autonomy: The right of an individual to control their own body and make decisions about their personal health and bodily integrity. Thomson's argument strongly defends this right against claims that it can be overridden by another's need for life support.
  • Good Samaritan vs. Minimally Decent Samaritan: Thomson distinguishes between an act of extraordinary generosity (Good Samaritan) and fulfilling minimal moral obligations (Minimally Decent Samaritan). Staying connected to the violinist for nine months is presented as a supererogatory act, going beyond what is morally required.
  • Supererogation: Acts that are morally praiseworthy but not morally required; they go "above and beyond the call of duty." Thomson's argument suggests that sustaining the violinist is supererogatory, not obligatory.

Important Terms

  • Thomson's Violinist: The specific thought experiment designed by Judith Jarvis Thomson to explore the relationship between the right to life and bodily autonomy.
  • Right to Life: The fundamental entitlement of a person not to be killed without justification.
  • Right to Use Another's Body: The contentious claim that an individual's right to life implies an obligation for others to provide their body (or bodily resources) to sustain that life. Thomson argues against this expansive interpretation.
  • Bodily Autonomy: The principle that individuals have the right to control their own bodies and make decisions regarding their physical being without coercion.
  • Supererogation: (From Latin supererogare, "to pay beyond what is due.") A morally praiseworthy act that is not morally required; it goes beyond the call of duty.
  • Good Samaritan: In Thomson's framework, someone who goes far beyond what duty requires, performing acts of extraordinary generosity.
  • Minimally Decent Samaritan: Someone who performs the bare minimum that morality demands, fulfilling basic obligations.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Thomson's Violinist

Judith Jarvis Thomson's "A Defense of Abortion" (1971) introduced the Violinist thought experiment. Its core aim is to dissect the complex relationship between a person's "right to life" and the extent to which that right can impose obligations on others, particularly regarding their bodies.

The Scenario

The thought experiment posits a scenario where you awaken to find yourself surgically connected to a famous, unconscious violinist. You learn that the Society of Music Lovers kidnapped you because the violinist has a fatal kidney ailment, and your blood type is the only match. If you remain connected for nine months, the violinist will recover. If you disconnect, he will die. The central question is: Are you morally obligated to remain connected?

Thomson's Core Argument

Thomson contends that even if we grant the violinist a full and undeniable "right to life," it does not automatically follow that he has a right to use your body to sustain that life. Your right to decide what happens to your own body (bodily autonomy) is paramount and is not overridden by the violinist's right to life. The situation is not one you created or volunteered for; you were kidnapped. While staying connected might be a generous and admirable act, generosity is distinct from moral obligation.

Structure of the Argument

Thomson's argument is carefully constructed:

  1. She does not deny the violinist is a person with rights.
  2. She does not deny that disconnecting will lead to his death.
  3. Her crucial point lies in distinguishing between killing someone and declining to sustain someone at significant personal cost. The involuntary nature of the connection and the immense personal sacrifice required are central to this distinction.

Rights, Obligations, and the Limits of Duty

The thought experiment draws a sharp line between two distinct claims:

  • Claim 1: A has a right to life. This is generally accepted and means others may not kill A without justification.
  • Claim 2: A has a right to whatever is needed to sustain A's life. This is a much stronger and more controversial claim, implying that others are obligated to provide resources, labor, or even bodily support.

Thomson argues that accepting Claim 1 does not necessitate accepting Claim 2. If needing something automatically created a right to commandeer another's body, the concept of "right to life" would expand beyond reasonable limits, which Thomson finds untenable.

The Distinction Between Active Killing and Withholding Aid

The Violinist scenario serves as a powerful illustration of the moral difference between actively causing harm (killing) and simply failing to provide extraordinary aid (withholding aid). Not helping the violinist, while it may result in his death, is not the same as actively killing him. This distinction is a recurring theme in moral philosophy.

The Good Samaritan and the Minimally Decent Samaritan

Thomson further refines moral obligations by introducing the concepts of the Good Samaritan and the Minimally Decent Samaritan:

  • Good Samaritan: Someone who goes above and beyond what is morally required, performing acts of extraordinary selflessness.
  • Minimally Decent Samaritan: Someone who fulfills the basic, minimal requirements of morality. Staying connected to the violinist for nine months is presented as an act of supererogation – an extraordinary act of generosity well beyond minimal decency. Thomson acknowledges that the line between supererogation and obligation is complex and depends on factors like the cost to the helper, the severity of the need, and the relationship between the parties. Moral obligations are not always absolute; they exist on a spectrum.

Real-World Applications

The principles highlighted by Thomson's Violinist thought experiment resonate in real-world ethical and legal debates:

  • The state generally cannot compel individuals to donate blood or organs, even if it could save lives.
  • Parents are not legally required to donate a kidney to a dying child, despite the close relationship and potential for enormous benefit. These examples demonstrate a legal and moral distinction between harming someone and failing to rescue them, paralleling Thomson's argument.

Review Questions

  1. How does Thomson's Violinist thought experiment challenge the common understanding of the "right to life"? What specific philosophical point is she trying to make?
  2. Explain the key distinction Thomson makes between "killing someone" and "declining to sustain someone at significant personal cost." Why is this distinction crucial to her argument?
  3. What is the difference between a "Good Samaritan" and a "Minimally Decent Samaritan" according to Thomson? How does this distinction relate to moral obligation in the Violinist scenario?
  4. Discuss the concept of bodily autonomy as it relates to the Violinist scenario. How does Thomson argue that it limits the scope of another person's right to life?
  5. Based on Thomson's argument, provide one real-world ethical dilemma (either from the text or your own thinking) where the principles from the Violinist thought experiment might be applied. How would Thomson's framework help analyze that dilemma?
