The Monty Hall Problem — Study Guide

Key Concepts

  • The Monty Hall Problem: A probability puzzle in which switching doors after the host reveals a goat doubles your winning probability from 1/3 to 2/3
  • Conditional Probability: Probability updated by new information — the host's action changes the probability distribution over the remaining doors
  • Information Asymmetry: The host knows where the car is; his constrained, non-random action transfers information to the remaining door
  • The 50/50 Illusion: The intuition that two remaining doors are equally likely is wrong because it ignores how the host's constrained behavior concentrates probability
  • Probability Concentration: The 2/3 probability that the car is not behind your original door concentrates entirely on the door the host did not open
  • Knowledgeable Agent Effect: Whenever a knowledgeable agent filters options, the surviving options are not equally likely

Important Terms

  • Monty Hall: American television host of Let's Make a Deal (1963–1986), after whom the problem is named; he did not invent the puzzle
  • Marilyn vos Savant: American columnist who published the correct answer (always switch) in her 1990 Parade magazine column, receiving approximately 10,000 letters — many from professional mathematicians — insisting she was wrong; she was right
  • Conditional probability: The probability of an event given that another event has already occurred; P(A|B) = P(A and B) / P(B); the foundation of Bayesian reasoning
  • Constrained action: The host cannot open the door you chose, cannot open the door hiding the car, and must open a goat door — this constraint makes his action informative
  • Probability mass: The total probability assigned to all possibilities; the 2/3 mass originally distributed between the two unchosen doors moves entirely to the single remaining unchosen door after the host acts
  • 100-door version: A clarifying variant — you pick one of 100 doors, Monty opens 98 goat doors, one remains; should you switch? Intuitively yes (99/100 vs. 1/100); the same logic applies to three doors

Detailed Notes

The Monty Hall Problem is based on the television game show Let's Make a Deal, hosted by Monty Hall from 1963 to 1986. It became famous in 1990 when Marilyn vos Savant published the correct answer in her Parade magazine column and received approximately 10,000 letters — many from holders of Ph.D.s in mathematics — insisting she was wrong. She was right, and the controversy itself is one of the most instructive demonstrations in the history of probability education.

The setup: three doors, one hiding a car and two hiding goats. You choose a door — say, Door 1. The host, who knows exactly what is behind every door, opens a different door — say, Door 3 — revealing a goat. He then asks: would you like to switch to Door 2? Most people say it makes no difference — there are two doors left, so it must be 50/50. This answer is wrong. You should always switch. Switching gives a 2/3 probability of winning; staying gives only 1/3.

The three-case analysis makes the result clear. When you initially pick Door 1, there are exactly three equally likely scenarios. In Case 1 (probability 1/3), the car is behind Door 1; the host can open either Door 2 or Door 3; if you switch, you get a goat — switching loses. In Case 2 (probability 1/3), the car is behind Door 2; the host must open Door 3 (the only goat door available); if you switch to Door 2, you get the car — switching wins. In Case 3 (probability 1/3), the car is behind Door 3; the host must open Door 2; switching to Door 3 wins. Switching wins in two of three equally likely cases. Staying wins in only one. The 50/50 intuition is simply wrong.

The crucial detail is the host's constraint. Monty always opens a goat door — he never reveals the car, never opens the door you chose. This means his action is not random. When you first chose Door 1, there was a 1/3 chance you were right and a 2/3 chance the car was behind one of the other two doors. After Monty opens a goat door, that 2/3 probability does not split evenly between the two remaining doors. Because Monty was forced to avoid the car, all 2/3 of it concentrates on the door he left closed. Your original door retains its original 1/3. Switching captures the 2/3. The 100-door version makes this transparent: your original pick had 1/100 chance; after 98 doors are opened, the remaining door has 99/100. Of course you switch. The same logic, less obviously, governs three doors.

Review Questions

  1. State the Monty Hall Problem and give the correct answer. Why does most people's intuition say otherwise?
  2. Walk through all three cases (car behind Door 1, 2, or 3) to demonstrate that switching wins in 2/3 of cases.
  3. Why does the host's action provide information? What makes his behavior "constrained," and why does constraint matter?
  4. Explain the probability concentration concept: where does the 2/3 go after the host opens a door?
  5. Suppose the host randomly opened a door (sometimes accidentally revealing the car). Would switching still give you a 2/3 advantage? What changes?
  6. Use the 100-door version to explain the correct answer to a skeptic. Why does this version make the intuition clearer?
  7. The text says the Monty Hall controversy is "itself instructive." What does the controversy reveal about how even trained mathematicians can reason poorly about conditional probability?
  8. Give an example from medicine or hiring where the Monty Hall logic applies — where a knowledgeable agent's filtering action changes probabilities in a non-obvious way.
