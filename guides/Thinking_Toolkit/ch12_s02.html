Rawls's Veil of Ignorance: Designing a Just Society

Overview

This section explores John Rawls's influential thought experiment, the "veil of ignorance," which proposes a method for designing a just society. By imagining rational individuals behind a veil that conceals their personal characteristics and social standing, Rawls argues they would agree upon two core principles of justice: equal basic liberties for all and the difference principle, which permits social and economic inequalities only if they benefit the least advantaged members of society.

Key Concepts

  • Rawls's Veil of Ignorance: A hypothetical condition where individuals design the rules of society without knowing their own race, sex, intelligence, wealth, or social position. This thought experiment is designed to ensure impartiality in the creation of a just social contract.
  • Impartiality: The state of being unbiased and fair. The veil of ignorance achieves impartiality by removing any knowledge that could lead individuals to design rules that favor their own self-interest.
  • Two Principles of Justice: Rawls argued that rational people behind the veil would choose these principles:
    1. Equal Basic Liberties: Each person should have an equal right to the most extensive system of basic liberties compatible with a similar system of liberty for all (e.g., freedom of speech, religion, assembly, right to vote).
    2. Difference Principle: Social and economic inequalities are permissible only if they are arranged so that they are both (a) to the greatest benefit of the least advantaged, and (b) attached to offices and positions open to all under conditions of fair equality of opportunity.
  • Maximin Strategy: A decision-making strategy, emphasized by Rawls, where one chooses the option that maximizes the minimum possible outcome. Behind the veil, a rational person would insure against the worst possible social position by making that position as good as it can be.
  • Justice as Fairness: Rawls's overarching concept, which posits that a just society is one whose fundamental rules would be accepted by individuals who are unaware of their own place within it. It focuses on designing institutions that no one could reasonably reject.

Important Terms

  • A Theory of Justice (1971): John Rawls's landmark work of political philosophy, introducing the veil of ignorance and his theory of justice.
  • Veil of Ignorance: A hypothetical device in Rawls's philosophy that prevents individuals from knowing their personal characteristics or social status when designing societal rules, thereby ensuring impartiality.
  • Difference Principle: The second principle of justice in Rawls's theory, stating that social and economic inequalities are only justified if they benefit the least advantaged members of society.
  • Maximin: A strategy for making decisions under uncertainty, focusing on maximizing the worst possible outcome (the minimum gain). Rawls argued this is the rational choice behind the veil of ignorance due to the high stakes involved.
  • Impartiality: The quality of being unbiased and objective, free from favoritism or self-interest. The veil of ignorance is a tool to achieve this.
  • Risk Aversion: The tendency to prefer a certain outcome with a lower expected value over an uncertain outcome with a higher expected value. Rawls suggests rational agents behind the veil would exhibit extreme risk aversion.
  • Robert Nozick: A philosopher who critiqued Rawls's theory, arguing against redistributive justice and emphasizing individual rights and entitlements based on just acquisition and transfer.

Detailed Notes

John Rawls and "A Theory of Justice"

  • Published in 1971, A Theory of Justice by John Rawls is considered a pivotal work in 20th-century political philosophy.
  • It introduces a method for determining principles of justice that would govern a fair society.

The Veil of Ignorance: A Thought Experiment

  • The Core Question: How would you design a just society if you didn't know your own place in it?
  • The Hypothetical Condition: Imagine a group of rational people gathered behind a "veil of ignorance."
  • What the Veil Conceals: Individuals do not know their:
    • Race, sex, intelligence, physical ability
    • Wealth, religious beliefs, or social position
  • The Task: From behind this veil, the group must agree on the fundamental rules and principles that will govern their society.
  • The Reveal: Only after these rules are chosen is the veil lifted, and each person discovers their specific identity and position.
  • Purpose of the Veil: It serves as a device to ensure impartiality. Without knowing your own future, you cannot rig the rules in your favor, forcing you to design principles that are acceptable regardless of where you end up.

What Would You Choose? Rawls's Principles of Justice

Rawls argued that rational individuals behind the veil would converge on two fundamental principles:

  1. Equal Basic Liberties:

    • Each person should have an equal claim to a fully adequate scheme of equal basic rights and liberties.
    • Examples include freedom of speech, freedom of religion, freedom of assembly, and the right to vote.
    • These liberties are considered fundamental and take priority.

  2. Social and Economic Inequalities (The Difference Principle):

    • These inequalities are permissible, but only under specific conditions.
    • The Difference Principle Defined: Inequalities are acceptable only if they make the worst-off group in society better off than they would be under a system of strict equality.
    • Reasoning: Risk Aversion and Maximin:
      • Rawls's argument for the difference principle is rooted in risk aversion.
      • Behind the veil, you face the risk of being the least advantaged person in society.
      • A rational planner, uncertain of their future position, would choose rules that "insure against the worst outcome" by making the worst possible position as good as possible.
      • This strategy is called maximin (maximize the minimum outcome).
    • Simplified Example:
      • Society A: Top 10% earn $500,000, Bottom 10% earn $5,000.
      • Society B: Top 10% earn $200,000, Bottom 10% earn $30,000.
      • Rawls argues you'd choose Society B because the minimum outcome ($30,000) is significantly better than in Society A ($5,000), ensuring a "livable life" even if you are the worst off.

Objections and Limits to Rawls's Framework

While highly influential, Rawls's theory has faced significant critiques:

  • Robert Nozick's Entitlement Theory:
    • In Anarchy, State, and Utopia (1974), Nozick argued Rawls focuses too much on the distribution of goods (patterned justice) and not enough on how those goods were acquired.
    • If wealth is acquired through voluntary exchange and honest labor, redistributing it, even for the benefit of the worst-off, may violate individual rights.
  • Risk Tolerance:
    • The maximin strategy assumes extreme risk aversion.
    • Some rational people might prefer a riskier society with higher potential rewards, even if it means a greater chance of being worse off (a "gambler's choice").
    • Rawls's counter: The stakes (your entire life) are too high for gambling.
  • Knowledge and Motivation:
    • Critics question whether individuals stripped of so much information can make meaningful choices.
    • How do you define "basic liberty" or weigh different values without any personal context?
  • Cultural Blindness:
    • Some argue that Rawls's framework inherently assumes a Western, liberal set of values.
    • Would people from genuinely diverse cultural backgrounds, behind a truly neutral veil, converge on the same principles?

Enduring Value of the Veil of Ignorance

  • Despite objections, the veil of ignorance remains a powerful tool in political philosophy.
  • It forces a valuable discipline: before endorsing any social arrangement, ask yourself if you would accept it if you did not know what position you would occupy within that society.
  • It promotes "Justice as Fairness" by removing self-interest and compelling individuals to consider the well-being of all, especially the most vulnerable.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the core concept of Rawls's "veil of ignorance." What information does it obscure, and what is its primary purpose in the thought experiment?
  2. What are the two principles of justice that Rawls argues rational people would choose behind the veil of ignorance? Describe each principle briefly.
  3. How does the "difference principle" relate to the "maximin strategy"? Provide an example to illustrate why Rawls believes individuals would choose this principle.
  4. Discuss two significant objections raised against Rawls's theory of justice as derived from the veil of ignorance. How might a proponent of Rawls's theory respond to these objections?
  5. Reflect on the "think about it" prompt: If you were behind the veil of ignorance, would you choose a society with strict income equality, a meritocracy, or a society with a strong safety net but large inequalities? What does your choice reveal about your personal theory of justice and what you value most?
