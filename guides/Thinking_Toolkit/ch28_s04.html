The Gambler's Fallacy and the Hot Hand — Study Guide

Key Concepts

  • Gambler's Fallacy: The false belief that past outcomes in a random, independent process influence future outcomes — that a streak of one outcome makes the opposite more likely
  • Independence: Events are independent when the outcome of one does not affect the probability of future events; coin flips and roulette spins are independent
  • Law of Large Numbers: The true principle that long-run proportions converge to theoretical probabilities — operates by dilution, not correction
  • Hot Hand: The belief that recent athletic success elevates the probability of further success — potentially real in skill contexts, unlike random processes
  • Two Opposite Errors: The Gambler's Fallacy assumes streaks will reverse; the Hot Hand belief assumes they will continue — both errors stem from failing to assess independence
  • The Key Question: Are the events independent? This determines which analysis applies

Important Terms

  • Gambler's Fallacy: The erroneous belief that past independent random outcomes affect future probabilities; also called the Monte Carlo Fallacy
  • Monte Carlo incident (1913): Black appeared at a roulette wheel an extraordinary number of consecutive times; gamblers lost millions betting on red, believing the streak must end
  • Law of Large Numbers: As trials increase, the observed proportion of outcomes converges to the theoretical probability; this works by diluting past imbalances, not correcting them
  • Hot hand: The belief that athletes who have recently made multiple successful shots are more likely to make the next; originally dismissed as a fallacy, now considered partially real
  • Thomas Gilovich, Robert Vallone, Amos Tversky: Authors of the influential 1985 study concluding the hot hand was an illusion — shots were essentially independent
  • Joshua Miller and Adam Sanjurjo: Economists whose 2018 reanalysis identified a subtle statistical bias in the original study; their correction found evidence of a small real hot-hand effect
  • Independence: Two events are independent if the outcome of one does not change the probability of the other

Detailed Notes

The Gambler's Fallacy is one of the most common and costly errors in probabilistic reasoning. After a roulette wheel lands on red six times in a row, most people feel that black is "due" — that the universe owes a correction. This intuition is strong and completely wrong. The roulette wheel has no memory. Each spin is independent of every previous spin, and the probability of red on the next spin is precisely the same as it was before the streak began.

The fallacy arises from confusing two different statements. The first is true: in a long sequence of fair coin flips, the proportion of heads will converge to 50%. This is the law of large numbers. The second is false: after a run of heads, tails must "catch up." The law of large numbers does not work by correcting past imbalances. If you flip 100 consecutive heads, the next 10,000 flips will be approximately 50/50 — not because the coin compensates, but because 100 heads out of 10,100 total flips is negligibly close to 50%. The imbalance is diluted, not corrected. The Monte Carlo incident of 1913 illustrated the cost: when black appeared an extraordinary number of consecutive times, gamblers lost millions betting on red. The streak ended eventually — but not because it was overdue; it ended because all streaks end randomly.

The hot hand presents what looks like the opposite error. The belief that a basketball player who has made several consecutive shots has an elevated probability of making the next was, for decades, dismissed as a fallacy. Gilovich, Vallone, and Tversky's influential 1985 study analyzed basketball shooting data and concluded that shots were essentially independent — fans were pattern-matching on random noise. But in 2018, economists Joshua Miller and Adam Sanjurjo identified a subtle statistical bias in this analysis. When you identify sequences of consecutive hits and measure the subsequent success rate, a mathematical artifact deflates the measured rate — making a genuine hot-hand effect appear smaller than it is or invisible. After correcting for this bias, Miller and Sanjurjo found evidence of a small but real hot-hand effect. The researchers who debunked the hot hand may themselves have committed a statistical error.

The lesson is not that streaks always predict the future or that they never do. The key question is whether the events are independent. Coin flips and roulette spins are independent — no streak has predictive power. Basketball shots may not be independent — a player's confidence, fatigue, defensive pressure, and shot selection all change during a streak, potentially introducing genuine dependencies. The Gambler's Fallacy and the exaggerated Hot Hand belief are opposite errors rooted in the same failure: not asking whether the process is genuinely random before applying intuitions about streaks.

Review Questions

  1. State the Gambler's Fallacy. Give an example of how it can lead to poor decisions in gambling or investing.
  2. What is the law of large numbers? Explain carefully why it does not imply that the Gambler's Fallacy is correct.
  3. Describe the Monte Carlo incident of 1913. What error did gamblers make, and what was the result?
  4. What did the 1985 Gilovich-Vallone-Tversky study conclude about the hot hand? What did Miller and Sanjurjo's 2018 reanalysis find?
  5. How do the Gambler's Fallacy and the Hot Hand represent opposite errors about the same underlying issue?
  6. Why might a hot hand effect be real in athletic performance but not in roulette? What differs between these contexts?
  7. The text says "the key question is always: are the events independent?" Give one example of clearly independent events and one where independence is genuinely uncertain.
  8. How can awareness of the Gambler's Fallacy protect investors? Give a specific scenario where the fallacy could lead to a costly mistake.
