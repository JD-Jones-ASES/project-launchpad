Goodhart's Law: When Measures Become Targets

Overview

Goodhart's Law, famously summarized as "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure," describes a fundamental challenge in managing complex systems. It highlights how human behavior adapts to optimize a given metric when incentives are attached to it, often distorting the original reality the measure was intended to represent and leading to unintended, negative outcomes. This principle impacts virtually every field where numerical targets are used for evaluation and policy.

Key Concepts

  • Goodhart's Law: The core principle stating that once a statistical measure is used as a target for policy or management, people will inevitably begin to manipulate their behavior to achieve that target, causing the measure to lose its accuracy and reliability as an indicator of the underlying reality.
  • Measure Corruption: The process by which a measure, once employed as a target, becomes a distorted or unreliable representation of the true state it was meant to reflect. This corruption occurs because individuals or groups actively optimize for the number itself, rather than the intended real-world outcome.
  • Optimizing for the Number, Not Reality: The fundamental shift in focus that occurs when Goodhart's Law takes effect. Instead of genuinely improving the underlying situation (e.g., student learning, patient health), efforts are redirected towards making the specific metric look good, even if the actual reality deteriorates.
  • Perverse Incentives and Unintended Consequences: The negative and often counterproductive outcomes that arise when a measure becomes a target. By incentivizing specific numbers, systems can inadvertently encourage undesirable behaviors or a neglect of broader, more important goals.
  • The "Game" of Targets: When a measure becomes a target, the system often transforms into a "game" where participants actively seek ways to achieve the numerical goal, sometimes through superficial compliance or even manipulation, rather than genuine improvement.

Important Terms

  • Goodhart's Law: An economic and management principle formulated by Charles Goodhart, stating that "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."
  • Measure: A quantitative indicator or metric used to assess a particular aspect of a system or reality (e.g., crime rate, test score, average handle time).
  • Target: A specific goal or benchmark set for a measure, often with rewards or punishments attached, intended to drive improvement or achieve a desired outcome.
  • Statistical Regularities: Observable patterns or relationships in data that are consistent and predictable, often forming the basis for initial measures before they are used as targets.
  • Optimizing for the Number: The behavioral strategy of adjusting actions primarily to improve a specific numerical metric, often at the expense of other considerations or the actual underlying goal.
  • Key Performance Indicators (KPIs): Quantifiable measures used to evaluate the success of an organization, employee, or specific activity in meeting objectives. They are particularly susceptible to Goodhart's Law.
  • The Cobra Effect: A related phenomenon where an attempted solution to a problem unintentionally makes the problem worse, often due to poorly designed incentives.
  • Campbell's Law: A sociological principle similar to Goodhart's Law, stating that "the more any quantitative social indicator is used for social decision-making, the more subject it will be to corruption pressures and the more apt it will be to distort and corrupt the social processes it is intended to monitor."

Detailed Notes

The Origin and Generalization of Goodhart's Law

  • Original Formulation (1975): British economist Charles Goodhart first articulated this principle in the context of monetary policy in the United Kingdom. He observed that "statistical regularities collapse once they are used for policy purposes." This meant that predictable patterns in economic data would change and become unreliable once central banks tried to manipulate them to achieve specific policy goals.
  • Generalized Form: Anthropologist Marilyn Strathern later generalized Goodhart's insight into the widely recognized, more concise form: "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." This broader phrasing highlights its applicability across diverse fields, not just economics.

How Goodhart's Law Works: The Mechanism

  • Initial Utility of Measures: A good measure initially works because it accurately captures some aspect of reality, and nobody is actively trying to manipulate it. It serves as an objective indicator.
  • The Shift with Incentives: The moment rewards or punishments (incentives) become attached to achieving a specific number, human behavior changes. Individuals or organizations begin to optimize their actions primarily for that number.
  • Focus on the Number, Not Reality: Instead of genuinely improving the underlying reality that the measure was designed to represent, efforts are redirected towards manipulating or "gaming" the measure itself. The measure transforms from a diagnostic tool into a target, and the target, in turn, becomes a game.
  • Corruption of the Measure: As people optimize for the target, the measure loses its original value as an honest indicator. It no longer accurately reflects the reality it was intended to monitor.

Real-World Illustrations of Goodhart's Law

1. Hospital Administration and Patient Outcomes:

  • Measure: Average length of patient stay in the hospital.
  • Initial Observation: Shorter stays correlate with better patient outcomes (healthier patients recover faster).
  • Target Imposed: Reduce the average length of stay.
  • Outcome: While the average length of stay indeed drops, patient outcomes worsen. Patients are discharged prematurely, only to return sicker, proving that optimizing for the "days in hospital" metric did not equate to improving patient health.

2. Standardized Testing in Education:

  • Measure: Student performance on standardized tests.
  • Target Imposed: Improve test scores, often tied to school funding, teacher evaluations, and administrator careers.
  • Outcome: The entire educational system reorganizes around test preparation. Teachers "teach to the test," drilling specific formats and content. Curricula narrow, with subjects like art and music cut to make room for more test prep. Some extreme cases even involve outright changing answers on tests. Scores may rise, but genuine, broad-based learning may not improve, or might even decline.

3. Policing and Crime Statistics:

  • Measure: Reported crime statistics (e.g., crime rates).
  • Target Imposed: Reduce crime rates or increase arrest numbers.
  • Outcome: Police departments face pressure to make crime statistics look better on paper. This can lead to reclassifying serious crimes as minor ones (e.g., assault as a "disturbance"), or officers being pressured to avoid taking reports altogether, meaning crimes never enter the official statistics. The "numbers" improve, but the actual safety of the streets remains unchanged or worsens.

4. Corporate Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) in Call Centers:

  • Measure: "Average Handle Time" (AHT) – the average duration an agent spends on a customer call.
  • Target Imposed: Reduce AHT to improve efficiency.
  • Outcome: Agents, under pressure, learn to rush callers, transfer difficult cases unnecessarily, or even hang up on customers. The AHT metric improves, but customer satisfaction plummets because service quality is sacrificed for the numerical target.

5. Wells Fargo Account Scandal (2016):

  • Measure: Number of new accounts opened per customer.
  • Target Imposed: Employees were given aggressive targets for opening new accounts.
  • Outcome: Under intense pressure, employees fraudulently opened millions of accounts that customers never requested. The metric looked outstanding, but the reality was widespread fraud, leading to billions in fines and severe reputational damage. The measure had completely consumed the institution.

The Deeper Lesson and the Antidote

  • Measurement is Essential: The core lesson of Goodhart's Law is not that measurement is bad or should be abandoned. Measurement is crucial for understanding and managing systems.
  • The Danger of Targeting a Single Measure: The problem arises when a measure becomes a target, especially when significant weight and incentives are attached to a single metric. The more pressure placed on a single measure, the more aggressively it will be gamed.
  • The Antidote: To mitigate the effects of Goodhart's Law, systems should:
    • Use Multiple Measures: Rely on a diverse set of indicators to get a more holistic view. No single measure can capture all the nuances of a complex reality.
    • Rotate Measures: Periodically change or update the specific measures used as targets, preventing entrenched gaming strategies.
    • Hold Measures Loosely: Understand that measures are imperfect representations. Don't become overly fixated on a single number.
    • Never Lose Sight of Underlying Reality: The most critical aspect is to always remember what the numbers are supposed to represent. Continuously evaluate whether the numbers are genuinely reflecting desired real-world improvements or just being gamed.

Charles Goodhart (b. 1936)

  • A British economist and former member of the Bank of England's Monetary Policy Committee.
  • His 1975 paper on monetary policy introduced the observation that statistical relationships used for policy control tend to break down once they are explicitly relied upon for control. This insight has since gained universal recognition as a principle applicable to all forms of measurement and management.

Connections to Related Principles

Goodhart's Law is closely related to several other important concepts in management and social science:

  • Campbell's Law: A sociological principle emphasizing the corruption of social indicators when used for decision-making.
  • The Cobra Effect: Describes how incentive structures designed to solve a problem can unexpectedly make the problem worse.
  • The Streetlight Effect: Refers to the tendency to look for solutions or measure what is easy to see or quantify, rather than what is truly important or relevant.
  • Friedman's Teaspoons: Highlights the confusion between means and ends, where a tool or process becomes the goal itself.
