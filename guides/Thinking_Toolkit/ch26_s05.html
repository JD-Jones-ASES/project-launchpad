The St. Petersburg Paradox — Study Guide

Key Concepts

  • Expected Value: The probability-weighted average of all possible monetary payoffs — the St. Petersburg game has infinite expected value
  • Expected Utility: What rational decision-makers actually maximize — the subjective value (utility) of outcomes, not their raw dollar amounts
  • Diminishing Marginal Utility: Each additional dollar is worth less than the previous one; the utility of money increases with wealth but at a decreasing rate
  • The Core Paradox: A game with infinite expected monetary value is worth only a modest finite amount to any real person — standard expected-value theory fails
  • Logarithmic Utility: Daniel Bernoulli's proposed solution — utility is proportional to the logarithm of wealth, producing finite expected utility for the St. Petersburg game
  • Risk Aversion: The preference for a certain outcome over a gamble with the same expected value, explained by diminishing marginal utility

Important Terms

  • Nicolaus Bernoulli: Swiss mathematician (1687–1759) who proposed the St. Petersburg game in 1713 in a letter to Pierre de Montmort
  • Daniel Bernoulli: Swiss mathematician (1700–1782) who analyzed the paradox and proposed the expected utility resolution in a 1738 paper to the Imperial Academy of Sciences in St. Petersburg, giving the paradox its name
  • Expected value: The sum of each possible outcome multiplied by its probability; for the St. Petersburg game, this sum diverges to infinity
  • Expected utility: The sum of the utility of each outcome multiplied by its probability; finite under logarithmic utility even when expected monetary value is infinite
  • Diminishing marginal utility: The economic principle that each additional unit of wealth produces less additional utility than the previous unit; explains why people are risk-averse
  • Risk aversion: Preference for a certain outcome over a gamble with equal or even higher expected monetary value; rational under expected utility theory
  • Tail risk: Low-probability, high-magnitude events; St. Petersburg-like payoff structures appear in financial markets and contributed to the 2008 financial crisis

Detailed Notes

The St. Petersburg Paradox was proposed by Nicolaus Bernoulli in 1713 and given its decisive analysis by his cousin Daniel Bernoulli in 1738. The game is simple: a fair coin is flipped repeatedly until tails appears. If the first tails occurs on flip n, the player wins 2^n dollars. So tails on flip 1 pays $2, on flip 2 pays $4, on flip 10 pays $1,024, on flip 20 pays over one million dollars, and on flip 30 pays over one billion. Most people say they would pay $5–$25 to play.

When one calculates the expected value — the standard decision-theoretic guide to how much a rational person should pay — the result is alarming. Each flip n contributes a probability of (1/2)^n multiplied by a payoff of 2^n, which equals exactly $1. Since there are infinitely many possible flips, the expected value is the sum 1 + 1 + 1 + ... = infinity. Standard decision theory says a rational agent should be willing to pay any finite sum to play a game with infinite expected value. Yet no rational person would mortgage their house for a single play of this coin-flipping game. Something fundamental has gone wrong.

Daniel Bernoulli's resolution, proposed in 1738, was to distinguish between expected monetary value and expected utility. What rational agents actually maximize is not the dollar amount of outcomes but the utility — the subjective satisfaction or usefulness — those dollars provide. And the key insight is that utility does not scale linearly with money. The difference between having nothing and having $1,000 is life-changing. The difference between having $1,000,000 and having $1,001,000 is barely noticeable. Utility increases with wealth, but at a decreasing rate. Bernoulli proposed that utility is proportional to the logarithm of wealth. Under this logarithmic function, the expected utility of the St. Petersburg game is finite — a modest number that matches what people actually report they would pay.

This was a profound foundational contribution to economics and decision theory. The distinction between objective monetary value and subjective utility explains why people buy insurance even when it has negative expected monetary value (the utility cost of ruin far exceeds the cost of the premium), why people prefer a certain $50 over a 50% chance of $100 despite equal expected values, and why financial institutions focused solely on expected monetary value can expose themselves to catastrophic tail risks. The St. Petersburg Paradox was one of the first formal demonstrations that expected value alone is an insufficient guide to rational decision-making — a lesson with ramifications throughout modern economics, finance, and behavioral science.

Review Questions

  1. Describe the St. Petersburg game. Calculate the expected value step by step and explain why it is infinite.
  2. Why does the infinite expected value fail to predict how much a rational person would actually pay to play the game?
  3. Explain diminishing marginal utility. Use a concrete example with specific dollar amounts to illustrate why the 1,000th dollar is worth less than the first.
  4. How did Daniel Bernoulli resolve the paradox? What function did he propose for utility, and how does it produce a finite expected utility?
  5. Explain why risk aversion is rational under expected utility theory, even though it seems irrational under expected value theory.
  6. How does the paradox explain why people buy insurance even when insurance policies have negative expected monetary value?
  7. What does "tail risk" mean in finance, and how does the St. Petersburg Paradox illuminate why ignoring it can be catastrophic?
  8. Distinguish between the contributions of Nicolaus Bernoulli and Daniel Bernoulli to this paradox. What did each contribute?
