The Motte and Bailey — Study Guide

Key Concepts

  • Motte and Bailey: A rhetorical pattern in which a speaker defends a bold, controversial claim (the bailey) by retreating to a modest, nearly indisputable claim (the motte) when challenged, then returns to the bold claim once pressure relents.
  • The Bailey: The desirable but indefensible position — a strong, controversial claim that attracts attention and advances the speaker's argument but cannot withstand scrutiny.
  • The Motte: The safe, defensible fallback position — a modest claim that is almost impossible to dispute but that does not actually support the bold claim the speaker was making.
  • Rhetorical Retreat: The move from bailey to motte when challenged, which feels like a response to the objection but actually avoids answering it.
  • Unconscious Deployment: The motte and bailey is often used without deliberate intent; the speaker may genuinely believe they have answered the objection by retreating to defensible ground.
  • The Remedy: Hold the speaker to their original claim. Acknowledge the motte, then ask directly whether they still maintain the stronger bailey and, if so, invite them to defend it on its own terms.

Important Terms

  • Motte: In medieval castle architecture, the fortified stone tower on a raised hill — cramped and austere but nearly impregnable. In the rhetorical sense, the modest, defensible claim that serves as a retreat when the stronger position is challenged.
  • Bailey: In medieval castle architecture, the broad outer courtyard where daily life occurs — spacious and desirable but harder to defend. In the rhetorical sense, the bold, controversial claim the speaker actually wants to advance.
  • Epistemic Standing: The degree to which a claim has genuine authority or reliability as a source of knowledge — the target of the bailey claim in the science-as-belief-system example.
  • Rhetorical Maneuver: A strategic move in argument that creates the appearance of engaging with an objection while actually evading it.
  • Straw Man: A related fallacy in which a distorted or weakened version of an opponent's argument is attacked instead of the actual argument; shares with the motte and bailey the quality of avoiding genuine engagement.
  • Moving the Goalposts: Another related fallacy in which the criteria for a successful argument are shifted after the original criteria have been met.

Detailed Notes

The motte and bailey was named by philosopher Nicholas Shackel in a 2005 paper, drawing on an architectural metaphor from medieval castle design. A motte-and-bailey castle consisted of two areas: the bailey, a broad outer courtyard where daily life took place and which was difficult to defend, and the motte, a stone tower on a hilltop at the center of the complex that was nearly impregnable. When attackers arrived, defenders retreated from the bailey to the motte. When the attackers withdrew, the defenders returned to the comfortable but vulnerable bailey.

The rhetorical pattern mirrors this dynamic precisely. A speaker advances a bold, controversial claim — the bailey — that is interesting and useful to their argument but difficult to defend under scrutiny. When challenged, the speaker retreats to a much weaker, almost universally acceptable claim — the motte — which technically addresses the challenge but is not actually what was originally asserted. Once the challenger accepts the motte and moves on, the speaker quietly reoccupies the bailey, continuing to use the strong claim as though it had been defended. The bailey gets the benefits: attention, persuasive force, and rhetorical momentum. The motte provides the cover when defense is needed.

A clear example: a speaker claims "science is just another belief system" (bailey — asserting that scientific knowledge has no special epistemic standing). When challenged, they retreat to "all I mean is that scientists can make mistakes" (motte — true, modest, and impossible to dispute). But the original claim asserted far more than that scientists are fallible. Once the challenger accepts the concession and moves on, the speaker continues treating science as "just another belief system." The motte and bailey is particularly insidious because it is often unconscious. The speaker retreats to the motte, genuinely feels vindicated because the motte is defensible, and returns to the bailey without recognizing that their original strong claim was never actually defended.

The remedy requires discipline. When a speaker retreats to a weaker position, the correct response is to acknowledge the motte explicitly and then ask whether the speaker still maintains the stronger claim: "I agree that scientists can make mistakes. But that is not what you originally said. Do you still hold that science has no special epistemic standing compared to other belief systems? If so, please defend that directly." This forces a choice: either defend the bailey on its own merits, or explicitly abandon it. Simply accepting the motte and moving on allows the speaker to have it both ways — advancing a strong claim while retreating to a weak one whenever defense is required.

Review Questions

  1. Describe the motte and bailey using the original architectural metaphor. How does the structure of a medieval castle map onto the rhetorical pattern?
  2. What is the difference between the motte and the bailey in argumentative terms? Give an original example of each.
  3. Walk through the "science as belief system" example. What is the bailey? What is the motte? How does the retreat evade the original challenge?
  4. Why is the motte and bailey described as "particularly insidious" when it is unconscious? What makes it harder to identify in that form?
  5. What is the prescribed remedy for a motte and bailey? Why is it important to explicitly acknowledge the motte before pressing for a defense of the bailey?
  6. How does the motte and bailey differ from directly lying? In what sense does it allow a speaker to make a strong claim without ever being accountable for it?
  7. Find an example of a motte and bailey in political rhetoric, advertising, or everyday argument. Identify the bailey (strong claim) and the motte (retreat position).
  8. Compare the motte and bailey to the straw man fallacy. How are they similar? How are they different in terms of what each distorts?
