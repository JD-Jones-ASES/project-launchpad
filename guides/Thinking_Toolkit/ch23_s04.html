Comprehensive Study Guide: Pre-Mortem Analysis

The pre-mortem, developed by psychologist Gary Klein, is a technique that asks teams to imagine, before a project begins, that it has already failed spectacularly — and then explain why. This simple inversion of the usual planning question transforms group dynamics, overcomes the social pressure to suppress dissent, harnesses our natural skill at constructing post-hoc explanations, and surfaces risks that conventional risk assessment consistently misses.

Key Concepts

  • Pre-mortem — A planning technique that asks a team to imagine a project has already failed and to explain the reasons for failure, conducted before execution begins
  • Post-mortem — The traditional analysis conducted after failure; the pre-mortem asks the same questions beforehand
  • Optimism bias — The tendency to focus on reasons a plan will succeed and downplay reasons it might fail; one of the primary biases the pre-mortem is designed to counteract
  • Reframing dissent — The pre-mortem's mechanism: instead of asking people to criticize the plan (socially risky), it asks them to explain a failure that has already occurred (a creative exercise)

Important Terms

  • Gary Klein: American psychologist specializing in naturalistic decision-making; developed the pre-mortem as a practical planning tool in 2007
  • Hindsight bias: Our tendency to view past events as having been predictable; the pre-mortem harnesses this cognitive tendency (our skill at constructing plausible narratives for known outcomes) and redirects it toward the future
  • Groupthink: The suppression of dissent in group settings; the pre-mortem is specifically designed to overcome groupthink by changing the social framing
  • Independent generation: A critical step in the pre-mortem procedure — each team member writes down failure reasons independently before discussion, to prevent groupthink from shaping the results
  • Red teaming: The military equivalent — assigning a group to argue against a plan and identify vulnerabilities; used before the 2011 operation that found Osama bin Laden

Detailed Notes

Why Conventional Risk Assessment Fails

In a typical planning meeting, the leader presents a plan and asks for potential problems. The result is underwhelming. Team members are reluctant to challenge the plan — especially if the leader is enthusiastic. Pointing out risks feels unsupportive, pessimistic, even disloyal. Social pressure suppresses dissent. The risks that get mentioned are the obvious, surface-level ones. The deeper, more uncomfortable risks remain unspoken.

How the Pre-Mortem Changes This

The pre-mortem changes the social dynamics by changing the frame. Instead of asking people to criticize the plan, it asks them to explain a failure that has already occurred. Instead of putting dissenters in opposition to the leader, it puts everyone on the same team — the team trying to figure out why things went wrong.

The technique works because of a quirk of human psychology: we are much better at explaining why something happened than at predicting whether it will happen. A team asked "What could go wrong?" generates a short, generic list. The same team asked "It went wrong — why?" generates a long, specific, and uncomfortably realistic list.

The Procedure

  1. Gather the project team after the plan has been developed but before execution begins
  2. Ask each member to imagine the project has been completed and has failed badly
  3. Each member independently writes down the reasons for the failure (no discussion yet — independent thinking prevents groupthink)
  4. Go around the room, each person sharing one reason; continue until all reasons are captured
  5. Review the list; identify the most plausible and most damaging failure modes
  6. Modify the plan to address them

What the Pre-Mortem Does Not Guarantee

The pre-mortem does not guarantee success. It does not guarantee the real failure mode will be identified. But it systematically counteracts the optimism bias that makes planning so dangerous, gives pessimists permission to speak, and surfaces risks that everyone knows about but nobody wants to say aloud.

The Military Parallel

Before the 2011 operation targeting Osama bin Laden, CIA analysts conducted a structured exercise in which a separate team was asked to identify every way the intelligence assessment could be wrong and every way the mission could fail. This is red teaming — the military version of the pre-mortem. Both work by making failure a legitimate subject of analysis rather than a taboo topic.

Review Questions

  1. What is the fundamental difference between a post-mortem and a pre-mortem? Why is the pre-mortem potentially more valuable?
  2. Explain the social dynamics problem in conventional risk assessment that the pre-mortem is designed to solve. What makes it hard for team members to raise uncomfortable risks?
  3. Walk through the five steps of the pre-mortem procedure. Why is the step of independent writing especially important?
  4. The pre-mortem "harnesses the same cognitive abilities that produce hindsight bias." What does this mean? How is hindsight bias usually a problem, and how does the pre-mortem turn it into an asset?
  5. Think of a project you are involved in or have recently completed. Apply the pre-mortem: imagine it has failed completely one year from now. Write down three plausible reasons why. Were any of these risks you had not previously considered?
