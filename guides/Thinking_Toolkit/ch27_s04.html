P vs. NP — Study Guide

Key Concepts

  • P (Polynomial Time): The class of problems solvable efficiently — in time proportional to a polynomial function of the input size
  • NP (Nondeterministic Polynomial Time): The class of problems whose solutions can be verified efficiently, even if finding a solution may be hard
  • The P vs. NP Question: Does the ability to quickly verify a solution guarantee the ability to quickly find one? This remains unsolved.
  • NP-Completeness: The "hardest" problems in NP — if any one NP-complete problem can be solved efficiently, all problems in NP can be
  • Cryptographic Dependence: Modern encryption security rests on the assumption that P ≠ NP — that certain verification-easy/solution-hard problems are genuinely hard
  • Practical Response: Because P likely does not equal NP, engineers use approximation algorithms and heuristics for NP-complete problems

Important Terms

  • Stephen Cook: Canadian-American mathematician and computer scientist who proved in 1971 that NP-complete problems exist, founding the modern theory of computational complexity
  • Leonid Levin: Soviet-American mathematician who independently proved similar results in 1973
  • Polynomial time: An algorithm whose runtime is bounded by n^k for some constant k; considered "efficient" in computer science
  • Exponential time: An algorithm whose runtime grows as 2^n or n! — impractical for large inputs, often the only known approach for NP-hard problems
  • NP-complete: A problem in NP to which every other NP problem can be reduced in polynomial time; the hardest class within NP
  • Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP): Find the shortest route visiting every city exactly once; NP-complete; no known polynomial-time algorithm
  • Approximation algorithm: An algorithm guaranteed to find a solution within a known factor of optimal; used when exact solutions to NP-complete problems are too slow
  • Millennium Prize Problem: One of seven mathematical problems identified by the Clay Mathematics Institute in 2000, each offering $1 million for a solution; P vs. NP is one of them

Detailed Notes

The P vs. NP question, formalized by Stephen Cook in 1971 and independently by Leonid Levin in 1973, is among the deepest unsolved problems in all of science and has carried a $1 million prize from the Clay Mathematics Institute since 2000. It asks whether there is a fundamental difference between problems that are easy to solve and problems that are easy to check.

The class P consists of problems solvable in polynomial time — sorting a list, finding the shortest path in a graph, searching a database. As input size grows, the runtime grows manageably (proportional to n, n², n³, etc.). The class NP consists of problems whose solutions can be verified in polynomial time. Given a proposed solution to a jigsaw puzzle, you can quickly verify whether the pieces fit. Given a proposed route that visits every city exactly once, you can quickly check whether the route works and whether its total length is under a given limit. Finding the solution — assembling the puzzle or discovering the optimal route — appears to require checking an astronomical number of possibilities. Every P problem is automatically in NP (if you can solve it quickly, you can certainly verify a solution quickly). The question is whether the reverse holds: is every problem in NP also in P?

If P = NP, the consequences would be staggering. Most modern cryptography would collapse. Encryption schemes like RSA rely on problems that are easy in one direction (multiplying large prime numbers) but apparently hard in reverse (factoring the product). If P = NP, the hard direction becomes easy, and most current encryption breaks. Mathematical proof-finding would be transformed: verifying a mathematical proof is in NP, so if P = NP, finding proofs would become as fast as checking them. Optimization problems throughout science, engineering, and logistics — many of which are NP-complete — would become trivially solvable. Most computer scientists believe P ≠ NP, but no one has proven it despite decades of intense effort.

Cook's 1971 theorem established that there exist NP-complete problems — problems that are simultaneously in NP and to which every other NP problem can be reduced in polynomial time. If any single NP-complete problem can be solved in polynomial time, then P = NP and all NP problems become tractable. Hundreds of practically important problems have been proven NP-complete: graph coloring, circuit satisfiability, protein folding, crew scheduling, and the Traveling Salesman Problem. The fact that no polynomial-time algorithm has been found for any of them, despite decades of effort, constitutes the strongest (though not conclusive) evidence that P ≠ NP. In practice, engineers respond to NP-completeness with approximation algorithms (guaranteed near-optimal solutions) and heuristics (fast practical methods without optimality guarantees) — GPS routing, airline scheduling, and chip design all use such approaches.

Review Questions

  1. What is the difference between a P problem and an NP problem? Give one example of each.
  2. Why does every P problem automatically qualify as an NP problem? Explain the logical relationship.
  3. What would happen to modern cryptography if P = NP? Why does encryption security depend on P ≠ NP?
  4. What is an NP-complete problem? Why are NP-complete problems significant for the P vs. NP question?
  5. Explain Cook's theorem. What did it establish about the structure of NP?
  6. Give three examples of NP-complete problems from the text. What do they have in common?
  7. Most experts believe P ≠ NP but cannot prove it. What is the strongest evidence (though not proof) supporting this belief?
  8. How do engineers solve NP-complete problems in practice without a polynomial-time algorithm? Give a specific example.
