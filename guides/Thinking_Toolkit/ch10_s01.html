Study Guide: The Euthyphro Dilemma

Overview

The Euthyphro Dilemma, stemming from Plato's dialogue, presents a fundamental challenge to understanding the nature of morality and its relationship to divine authority. It asks whether something is good because God commands it, or if God commands it because it is inherently good. This question forces a critical examination of whether morality is grounded in arbitrary divine will or an independent, objective standard.

Key Concepts

  • The Euthyphro Dilemma: The central philosophical problem, originally posed by Socrates to Euthyphro, that questions the source and nature of "piety" or "goodness" in relation to divine authority. It forces a choice between two uncomfortable options concerning morality's foundation.
  • Divine Command Theory (Option 1): The view that an action is good because God commands it. This position grounds morality in divine will, making it absolute and beyond human dispute. However, it risks making morality arbitrary, as goodness is defined solely by divine fiat, potentially allowing even cruelty to be considered good if commanded.
  • Morality Independent of God (Option 2): The view that God commands an action because it is inherently good. This option upholds the idea that goodness is an objective, real quality that exists independently. However, it implies that morality is a standard that even God recognizes and follows, seemingly limiting divine sovereignty and making God a "reporter" rather than the "author" of ethics.
  • Divine Nature Theory: A common attempt to resolve the Euthyphro Dilemma. This theory proposes that goodness does not come from arbitrary divine commands nor an external standard, but rather flows necessarily from God's own inherent nature. God is good, and therefore God's commands reflect this essential goodness.

Important Terms

  • Euthyphro Dilemma: A philosophical quandary, originating from Plato's dialogue Euthyphro, which explores whether moral acts are good because they are commanded by God, or commanded by God because they are good.
  • Socrates: An influential ancient Greek philosopher (c. 470–399 BC) known for his method of questioning (Socratic method) to probe fundamental concepts and expose contradictions.
  • Euthyphro: A character in Plato's dialogue who is a self-proclaimed expert on piety and prosecuting his own father for manslaughter, believing it to be the pious thing to do.
  • Piety: In the context of the Euthyphro dialogue, this term refers to righteousness, holiness, or actions deemed morally right, particularly in relation to religious duty and the gods.
  • Divine Command Theory: An ethical theory that posits an action's moral status (right or wrong) is determined by God's commands.
  • Arbitrary: Based on random choice or personal whim, rather than any reason or system; lacking intrinsic reason or justification.
  • Divine Sovereignty: The concept of God's supreme power, authority, and independence over all things, including the creation and definition of morality.
  • Divine Nature Theory: A theological and philosophical response to the Euthyphro Dilemma suggesting that moral goodness is an intrinsic attribute of God's character, meaning God's commands necessarily reflect God's inherently good nature.

Detailed Notes

The Context of the Dilemma

The Euthyphro Dilemma originates from Plato's dialogue Euthyphro, set around 399–395 BC. Socrates encounters Euthyphro outside an Athenian court. Socrates is about to face trial for impiety, while Euthyphro is confident in his understanding of piety, as he is prosecuting his own father for the death of a laborer. Socrates, using his characteristic ironic style, asks Euthyphro to explain the true nature of piety, leading to one of philosophy's most profound questions.

The Core Question: Original and Modern Forms

Socrates' original question to Euthyphro was: "Is the pious loved by the gods because it is pious, or is it pious because it is loved by the gods?"

In modern ethical and theological discussions, this question is typically rephrased concerning a monotheistic God and the concept of "goodness": "Is something good because God commands it, or does God command it because it is good?" This is not a mere semantic trick; the two options lead to vastly different understandings of morality.

Option 1: Something is good because God commands it (Divine Command Theory)

  • The Position: This option asserts that goodness is not an inherent quality of actions but is created by God's will. Whatever God commands, by definition, becomes good.
  • Advantages:
    • Moral Grounding: Provides a stable, absolute, and universally authoritative foundation for morality, directly linked to divine authority.
    • Divine Sovereignty: Upholds God's ultimate power and control, as God is the sole originator of moral principles.
  • Disadvantages/Costs:
    • Arbitrariness: If goodness is solely based on command, then morality becomes arbitrary. If God commanded cruelty, then cruelty would, by this definition, be good. This clashes with our intuitive understanding of goodness.
    • Lack of Intrinsic Value: Actions don't possess inherent goodness; "good" becomes merely a label applied by divine fiat, rather than a quality that actions embody. It raises the question of why we would call it "good" rather than simply "commanded."

Option 2: God commands it because it is good (Morality Independent of God)

  • The Position: This option argues that certain actions are inherently good or bad, and God, recognizing this pre-existing moral reality, commands us to do the good and avoid the bad.
  • Advantages:
    • Objective Goodness: Preserves the intuition that goodness is a real, objective quality that exists independently of anyone's (even God's) will. Honesty, for instance, is genuinely good.
    • Rational Morality: Allows for a basis for understanding morality that is not just arbitrary command but rooted in inherent qualities or reasons.
  • Disadvantages/Costs:
    • Limits Divine Sovereignty: If goodness exists independently, then God is not the ultimate author of morality but rather an extremely reliable reporter or recognizer of it. This appears to place a standard above God, which can be uncomfortable for those who believe in God's absolute sovereignty.
    • External Standard: Implies that God is subject to an external moral standard, which can be seen as diminishing God's supreme authority.

The Dilemma's Challenge

The Euthyphro Dilemma creates a profound challenge for anyone who wishes to reconcile two desires:

  1. Objective Morality: The belief that moral truths are real and not just matters of opinion.
  2. Divine Grounding: The belief that morality ultimately comes from God.

The dilemma forces a choice: either God is the source of morality, but morality becomes arbitrary, or morality is independent of God, but then God is not its ultimate foundation.

The Divine Nature Theory: A Proposed Resolution

One of the most influential attempts to resolve the dilemma is the Divine Nature Theory.

  • Core Argument: This theory suggests that goodness doesn't come from God's arbitrary commands, nor is it an external standard that God observes. Instead, goodness flows inherently from God's own nature. God is good. Therefore, God's commands are not arbitrary, but necessary reflections of God's essentially good character.
  • Goal: To "thread the needle" between the two horns of the dilemma, allowing morality to be both divinely grounded and objectively non-arbitrary.
  • Continuing Debate: Whether this response truly resolves the dilemma remains a live question in philosophy and theology. Critics might ask if "God's nature is good" simply pushes the question back: "Is God's nature good because of what it is, or is 'good' just a label for whatever God's nature happens to be?"

The Enduring Significance

Regardless of how one attempts to resolve it, the Euthyphro Dilemma achieves crucial clarity. It compels individuals, particularly those within religious ethics, to articulate precisely:

  • What they mean when they say something is "good."
  • What role, if any, divine authority plays in that moral judgment. It demands that religious ethics be expressed with precision and a clear understanding of its foundational principles.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the original formulation of the Euthyphro Dilemma by Socrates and how it is typically rephrased in modern discussions about God and morality.
  2. What are the main implications and potential difficulties (the "costs") of accepting the position that something is good because God commands it (Divine Command Theory)?
  3. What are the main implications and potential difficulties (the "costs") of accepting the position that God commands something because it is inherently good? How does this impact the concept of divine sovereignty?
  4. Describe the Divine Nature Theory as a proposed resolution to the Euthyphro Dilemma. How does it attempt to reconcile the ideas of divine grounding and objective morality?
  5. Why is the Euthyphro Dilemma considered a foundational and enduring problem in philosophy and theology, even if there is no universally accepted resolution? What does it force us to clarify?
