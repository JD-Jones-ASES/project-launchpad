Sturgeon's Law: Navigating Quality in a Noisy World

Overview

Sturgeon's Law, coined by science fiction author Theodore Sturgeon, posits that ninety percent of everything is mediocre or "crud," but this is a universal base rate, not a specific criticism of any single field. The law serves as a crucial calibration tool, helping individuals set realistic expectations and wisely allocate their attention to find the valuable ten percent, while also defending against rhetorical tricks that dismiss entire categories based on their worst examples. In the internet age, its relevance has grown as the challenge shifts from scarcity of good content to the need for effective curation.

Key Concepts

  • Sturgeon's Law (The 90/10 Principle): The core observation that approximately ninety percent of content, products, or ideas in any given category is of low quality or unremarkable, while only ten percent is truly excellent. This is presented as a universal baseline, not a crisis.
  • Calibration Tool: The law helps to adjust one's expectations realistically. Instead of expecting universal excellence, it encourages understanding that mediocrity is the norm, thus preventing constant disappointment and guiding attention towards finding quality.
  • Defense Against Rhetorical Tricks: It provides a counter-argument to the tactic of dismissing an entire field or category by cherry-picking its worst examples (e.g., bad modern art, flawed startups). It reminds us that bad examples do not negate the existence of good ones.
  • Base-Rate Reasoning: Sturgeon's Law is fundamentally about understanding base rates. It cautions against two types of errors: condemning a field by its worst examples and praising it by its best, as both ignore the underlying distribution of quality.
  • Signal in a Noisy World: In an era of content explosion (especially online), the law highlights that while the absolute quantity of low-quality material (noise) increases, so does the absolute quantity of high-quality material (signal). The challenge is no longer scarcity but developing effective filters and curation skills to find the signal amidst the noise.

Important Terms

  • Sturgeon's Law: An observation stating that "ninety percent of everything is crud," used to defend against dismissing an entire category based on its worst examples and to set realistic expectations about quality distribution.
  • Base Rate: The natural or underlying prevalence of something within a population or category. In Sturgeon's Law, the "ninety percent crud" is presented as a universal base rate for quality.
  • Signal-to-Noise Ratio: A measure of the proportion of useful or relevant information (signal) compared to irrelevant or unwanted information (noise). The internet age has significantly lowered this ratio due to the sheer volume of content.
  • Curation: The process of selecting, organizing, and presenting content or information. In the context of Sturgeon's Law, it refers to the essential skill of filtering through vast amounts of material to identify the high-quality ten percent.
  • Replication Crisis: A methodological crisis in fields like psychology, where a significant percentage of published research findings could not be reproduced. Sturgeon's Law helps calibrate understanding of this crisis, suggesting it points to a need for better filtering mechanisms rather than invalidating the entire field.

Detailed Notes

The Origin and Core Idea of Sturgeon's Law

  • Theodore Sturgeon (1918–1985): An American science fiction and horror author.
  • Context: Coined in 1957, originally "Sturgeon's Revelation," as a defense for the science fiction genre. Critics routinely dismissed science fiction as worthless, pointing to its lowest common denominator (pulp magazines, bad plots, wooden dialogue).
  • Sturgeon's Retort: While agreeing that much science fiction was bad, he asked "compared to what?" His famous reply: "Ninety percent of science fiction is crud, but ninety percent of everything is crud."
  • Universal Principle: This observation extends beyond science fiction to virtually every domain: movies, books, music, academic papers, business plans, speeches, and even restaurant meals. Mediocrity is the "base rate" and a normal state, not an indication of a crisis unique to one field.

Applications of Sturgeon's Law

  1. A Calibration Tool:

    • Sets Expectations: It helps individuals establish realistic expectations. If one expects every book in a bookstore or every piece of content encountered online to be excellent, perpetual disappointment is inevitable.
    • Allocates Attention: By understanding that most material is forgettable, one can wisely invest energy in actively searching for the exceptional "ten percent" rather than lamenting the poor quality of the majority.

  2. A Defense Against Rhetorical Tricks:

    • Dismissal by Worst Examples: A common rhetorical tactic is to condemn an entire category or field by selectively highlighting its poorest examples.
      • Examples: "Modern art is nonsense – look at this blank canvas." "Philosophy is useless – look at this paper about holes." "Start-ups are fraudulent – look at Theranos."
    • Sturgeon's Counter: The law reminds us that the existence of bad examples (from the 90%) tells us nothing conclusive about the existence or quality of good examples (from the 10%) within the same category.

  3. Avoiding Failures of Base-Rate Reasoning (Reverse Application):

    • Praising by Best Examples: Just as one shouldn't condemn a field by its worst, one also shouldn't redeem the mediocre majority by pointing to its brilliant outliers.
      • Examples: The existence of Shakespeare doesn't mean most plays are good. A few rigorous nutrition studies don't make the entire field of nutrition science generally reliable.
    • Conclusion: Both errors—condemning by the worst and praising by the best—are failures to understand the underlying base rate distribution of quality.

Sturgeon's Law in the Internet Age

  • Increased Relevance: The proliferation of content online (social media, self-publishing, open-access journals) has democratized production dramatically.
  • Content Explosion: This has led to an enormous increase in the absolute quantity of both "crud" and excellence. The percentage (90/10) may remain similar.
  • Shift in Challenge: The primary challenge has moved from scarcity (not enough good material) to curation (too much material, requiring effective methods to find the good stuff).
  • Signal in a Noisy World: Recognizing that 90% is mediocre is normal. The practical skill now is not to lament the "noise" but to develop strong filters such as taste, expertise, reliance on trusted curators, and critical judgment to identify the valuable "signal."

Real-World Example: The Replication Crisis

  • Context: In the 2010s, systematic attempts to replicate published psychology studies found that 60–70% of results could not be reproduced, leading to a "crisis of confidence."
  • Sturgeon's Law's Role: While not excusing institutional failures, the law offers calibration. A large volume of unreliable research does not mean psychology offers no insights. Instead, it highlights that filtering mechanisms (peer review, replication, meta-analysis) within the field need to be significantly improved to better separate reliable signal from unreliable noise.

Connections to Other Concepts

Sturgeon's Law is related to several cognitive biases:

  • Base Rate Neglect: The tendency to ignore prior probabilities or general information in favor of specific, vivid information.
  • Confirmation Bias: The tendency to search for, interpret, favor, and recall information in a way that confirms one's pre-existing beliefs or hypotheses.
  • The Availability Heuristic: The mental shortcut where people rely on immediate examples that come to mind. Vivid bad examples might seem more prevalent than quiet good ones.

Review Questions

  1. What is the core principle of Sturgeon's Law, and what was its original context when Theodore Sturgeon first articulated it?
  2. How does Sturgeon's Law function as a "calibration tool" for our expectations? Provide an example from your own experience or from the text that illustrates this function.
  3. Explain how Sturgeon's Law helps individuals defend against common rhetorical tricks used to dismiss an entire category. Give one example of such a trick and how Sturgeon's Law would counter it.
  4. Why has Sturgeon's Law become more relevant in the age of the internet? Describe how the primary challenge related to content has shifted.
  5. Discuss the concept of "signal in a noisy world" in relation to Sturgeon's Law. What practical skills or strategies are encouraged by this concept to navigate vast amounts of information?
