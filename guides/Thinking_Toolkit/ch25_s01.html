The Liar Paradox — Study Guide

Key Concepts

  • Self-Reference: A statement that refers to itself, creating circular logical dependencies that resist stable truth-value assignment
  • The Liar Sentence: "This sentence is false" — the core paradox that cannot be consistently assigned a truth value
  • Logical Oscillation: When a statement's truth value flips endlessly between true and false without settling on either
  • Tarski's Hierarchy: The proposed solution that a language cannot contain its own truth predicate — truth must be defined in a higher metalanguage
  • Paraconsistent Logic: Logical systems designed to tolerate localized contradictions without the system collapsing entirely
  • Dialethism: The philosophical position that some contradictions are genuinely true, championed by Graham Priest
  • Ex Falso Quodlibet: The classical logic principle that a single contradiction allows proving anything at all

Important Terms

  • Liar Paradox: The logical puzzle arising from the sentence "This sentence is false," which cannot be consistently assigned either truth value
  • Epimenides Paradox: A weaker variant in which the Cretan philosopher Epimenides said "All Cretans are liars" — not strictly paradoxical because he could simply be lying while some other Cretans tell the truth
  • Eubulides of Miletus: 4th-century BC logician credited with formulating the Liar Paradox; attributed to him and discussed for over 2,300 years
  • Alfred Tarski: Polish logician who proposed in the 1930s that a language cannot consistently contain its own truth predicate, requiring a metalanguage hierarchy
  • Metalanguage: A language used to discuss another language (the object language); in Tarski's framework, truth predicates belong to metalanguages, not object languages
  • Paraconsistent logic: Logical systems that block ex falso quodlibet, allowing localized contradictions to coexist with otherwise sound reasoning
  • Dialethism: The view associated with Graham Priest that some sentences are genuinely both true and false simultaneously

Detailed Notes

The Liar Paradox is one of the oldest unsolved problems in logic, attributed to Eubulides of Miletus in the 4th century BC and discussed continuously for over 2,300 years. It arises from the simple sentence "This sentence is false." If the sentence is true, then what it says must hold — but it says it is false, making it false. If it is false, then its claim (that it is false) fails, which means it must be true. The sentence oscillates endlessly between the two values, unable to settle on either. This is not a riddle with a clever answer; it is a genuine logical problem that exposes deep tensions in our foundational concepts of truth, meaning, and language.

The paradox matters because any logical or formal system that permits self-referential statements must grapple with it or risk incoherence. Most significantly, Kurt Godel used a version of the Liar's self-referential structure to prove his incompleteness theorems — among the most important results in 20th-century mathematics. The Liar also surfaces in everyday language in diluted forms. Paired contradictory statements like "The following statement is true. The preceding statement is false." create a two-sentence version of the same loop. The Epimenides version, while famous, is weaker: a Cretan claiming all Cretans are liars could simply be lying himself while other Cretans tell the truth, so the statement does not generate the same tight contradiction as the pure Liar.

Several resolution strategies have been proposed. Alfred Tarski argued in the 1930s that a language cannot consistently contain its own truth predicate. To evaluate whether a sentence is true, one must use a higher-level metalanguage that talks about the original object language. The Liar arises by trying to collapse these levels. Tarski's solution is rigorous and widely accepted in formal logic, but it does not fully explain how natural languages like English function despite routinely using their own truth predicates (we say "that's true" all the time). Paraconsistent logicians take a different approach, building systems that accept the Liar as both true and false in a localized region without the entire system collapsing. In classical logic, one contradiction implies everything (ex falso quodlibet); paraconsistent logics block this explosive step.

The most radical response is dialethism, associated with philosopher Graham Priest, which holds that the Liar sentence really is both true and false simultaneously — and that this is not a defect but a genuine feature of reality. Most logicians find this deeply counterintuitive, but Priest argues it is the most honest response to a problem that has resisted every other solution for millennia. A fourth approach simply dismisses the Liar as meaningless — as a grammatically correct string of words that fails to express a genuine proposition. This has the appeal of simplicity, but critics note that the sentence appears perfectly meaningful, is grammatically well-formed, and uses familiar words in a familiar pattern. Declaring it meaningless feels more like avoiding the problem than solving it.

Review Questions

  1. State the Liar Paradox in your own words and explain why the sentence cannot be consistently assigned either truth value.
  2. What is the Epimenides Paradox, and why is it considered a weaker form of the Liar Paradox rather than a strict paradox?
  3. Explain Tarski's hierarchy of languages as a solution to the Liar Paradox. What does it accomplish, and what does it leave unexplained?
  4. What is ex falso quodlibet, and why does it make even a single contradiction dangerous in classical logic?
  5. How does paraconsistent logic respond differently to the Liar than classical logic does?
  6. What is dialethism? Why do most logicians reject it, and what is the argument in its favor?
  7. Why does the text say the Liar Paradox is "not a toy problem"? What major mathematical results does it foreshadow?
  8. Consider the variation "This sentence is neither true nor false." Does adding a third truth value resolve the paradox, or does it generate a new version?
