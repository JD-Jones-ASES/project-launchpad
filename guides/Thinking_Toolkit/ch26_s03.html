Hilbert's Grand Hotel — Study Guide

Key Concepts

  • Hilbert's Hotel: A thought experiment demonstrating that an infinite hotel, even when completely full, can accommodate additional guests through rearrangement
  • Infinity vs. Large Finite Numbers: Infinite sets behave qualitatively differently from very large finite sets — the whole can equal one of its proper parts
  • One-to-One Correspondence: The mathematical definition of equal "size" (cardinality) for infinite sets — two sets have the same cardinality if their elements can be paired exactly
  • Countable Infinity: The cardinality of the natural numbers (aleph-null, ℵ₀) — the natural numbers, even numbers, odd numbers, integers, and rational numbers all share this cardinality
  • Uncountable Infinity: A strictly larger infinity — the real numbers are uncountably infinite, proved by Cantor's diagonal argument
  • Hierarchy of Infinities: Cantor's discovery that there is not just one infinity but a tower of strictly increasing infinite cardinalities

Important Terms

  • David Hilbert: German mathematician (1862–1943) who proposed the Hotel thought experiment in a 1924 lecture to illustrate Cantor's mathematics of infinity
  • Georg Cantor: German mathematician who developed the mathematics of infinite sets in the 1870s–1880s; defined cardinality by one-to-one correspondence and proved different sizes of infinity exist
  • Cardinality: The mathematical "size" of a set; for infinite sets, defined by whether a one-to-one correspondence can be established with another set
  • Aleph-null (ℵ₀): The cardinality of the natural numbers — the smallest infinite cardinal; any set in one-to-one correspondence with the natural numbers has this cardinality
  • Cantor's diagonal argument: The proof that real numbers between 0 and 1 cannot be listed — for any proposed list, one can construct a real number differing from the nth entry at the nth decimal place
  • Uncountably infinite: A set with strictly greater cardinality than the natural numbers; the real numbers are uncountably infinite
  • Countably infinite: A set in one-to-one correspondence with the natural numbers; the even numbers, the integers, and the rationals are all countably infinite

Detailed Notes

David Hilbert proposed his Grand Hotel thought experiment in a 1924 lecture to make vivid the counterintuitive properties of infinite sets that Georg Cantor had been studying for decades. Imagine a hotel with infinitely many rooms, numbered 1, 2, 3, 4, and so on without end. Every room is occupied. A new traveler arrives. In a finite hotel, this is impossible — every room is taken. But the manager of Hilbert's Hotel announces that every guest in room n should move to room n+1. Every guest moves; room 1 opens; the new guest checks in. The hotel went from full to full-plus-one without adding rooms or displacing anyone.

The story becomes more dramatic. Suppose an infinitely long bus arrives carrying infinitely many new passengers. The manager asks all current guests to move from room n to room 2n. Guest in room 1 goes to room 2, room 2 to room 4, room 3 to room 6, and so on. Now all even-numbered rooms are occupied by existing guests, and all odd-numbered rooms — infinitely many of them — stand empty, ready to receive the bus passengers. The hotel absorbed an entire infinite bus without displacing a single existing guest.

The mathematical key behind all of this is Georg Cantor's definition of cardinality. Two sets have the same size if their elements can be placed in one-to-one correspondence — each element of one set paired with exactly one element of the other, nothing left over on either side. By this definition, the natural numbers and the even numbers have the same cardinality, even though the even numbers are a proper subset of the natural numbers. Moving guests from room n to room 2n is just establishing this correspondence. For finite sets, a whole is always greater than any of its proper parts. For infinite sets, this rule breaks down entirely. This is what makes Hilbert's Hotel possible.

One of Cantor's most stunning discoveries was that not all infinities are equal. While the natural numbers, the integers, and even the rational numbers all have the same cardinality ℵ₀, the real numbers constitute a strictly larger infinity. Cantor proved this with his diagonal argument: however one tries to list all real numbers between 0 and 1, one can always construct a new real number not on the list by choosing its nth digit to differ from the nth digit of the nth listed number. The real numbers are uncountably infinite — not just bigger than the countable sets but of an entirely different order of size. There is not one infinity but a hierarchy of infinities, each strictly larger than the one below.

Review Questions

  1. Explain how Hilbert's Hotel accommodates one new guest when already full. What instruction does the manager give, and why does it work?
  2. How does the hotel accommodate an infinitely long bus of new guests? Walk through the manager's instruction step by step.
  3. What is cardinality, and how does one-to-one correspondence define it? Give an example of two infinite sets with the same cardinality.
  4. What is the difference between a countably infinite and an uncountably infinite set? Give one example of each.
  5. Explain Cantor's diagonal argument. Why does it show that the real numbers cannot be listed?
  6. The text poses a puzzle: if infinitely many odd-room guests check out, infinitely many guests remain. If guests in rooms greater than 5 check out, only 5 guests remain. Both are "infinity minus infinity." What does this reveal about arithmetic with infinite sets?
  7. What practical fields does the text cite as depending on the mathematics of cardinality and countability?
  8. Why does the text say infinity "is not just a very large number but a fundamentally different kind of quantity"?
