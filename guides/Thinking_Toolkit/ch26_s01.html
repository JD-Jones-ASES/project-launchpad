The Banach-Tarski Paradox — Study Guide

Key Concepts

  • Banach-Tarski Paradox: A proven mathematical theorem that a solid sphere can be decomposed into a finite number of pieces and reassembled into two spheres each identical in size to the original
  • Non-Measurable Sets: The "pieces" in the decomposition — mathematical objects so wildly scattered that no consistent notion of volume applies to them; not physically constructible
  • Axiom of Choice: The set-theoretic axiom whose non-constructive power guarantees the existence of non-measurable sets, making the theorem possible
  • Volume Intuition Failure: The paradox reveals that our assumption "the volume of pieces sums to the volume of the whole" fails for non-measurable sets
  • Mathematical vs. Physical Reality: The theorem describes a feature of abstract mathematical space, not a recipe for duplicating physical objects (which are made of atoms)
  • Rigorous Theorem: This is not a trick or approximation — it is proven with the same certainty as the Pythagorean theorem

Important Terms

  • Stefan Banach: Polish mathematician (1892–1945), one of the most important analysts of the 20th century; proved the theorem with Tarski in 1924
  • Alfred Tarski: Polish logician and mathematician (1901–1983); co-proved the Banach-Tarski theorem and also developed Tarski's hierarchy of languages for the Liar Paradox
  • Non-measurable set: A set of points so wildly scattered and interleaved that no consistent notion of volume can be assigned to it; exists only in abstract mathematical space, not in physical reality
  • Axiom of Choice: States that given any collection of non-empty sets, exactly one element can be selected from each, even with no explicit selection rule; enables non-constructive existence proofs
  • Measurable set: A set to which a well-defined, consistent volume (or area, or length) can be assigned; all ordinary geometric figures are measurable
  • Decomposition: The act of dividing a mathematical object into pieces; in Banach-Tarski, five non-measurable pieces suffice
  • Rotation and translation: The only operations used to reassemble the Banach-Tarski pieces — no stretching, squishing, or filling of gaps

Detailed Notes

The Banach-Tarski Paradox, proved by Stefan Banach and Alfred Tarski in 1924, is one of the most startling results in all of mathematics. It states that a solid sphere can be cut into a finite number of pieces — five will do — and those pieces can be rearranged using only rotations and translations (no stretching or gaps) to form two solid spheres, each the same size as the original. One sphere becomes two. This is proven with the same logical certainty as any theorem in geometry. It is not a thought experiment, a magic trick, or a physicist's approximation.

The key to understanding how this is possible lies in the nature of the pieces. They are not solid chunks or thin slices that one could cut with a knife. They are non-measurable sets — collections of points so wildly scattered and interleaved throughout the sphere that no consistent notion of volume can be assigned to them. Our intuition about volume rests on the assumption that every piece of a solid has a definite volume and that volumes add up: cut a sphere into pieces, and the total volume of the pieces equals the volume of the sphere. For non-measurable sets, this assumption fails completely. These pieces have no volume; the concept does not apply to them. When they are rearranged to form two spheres, no actual "volume" has been created because the pieces never had any volume to begin with.

This is why the paradox does not violate physics. Physical matter is composed of atoms — it is discrete, not infinitely divisible. One cannot physically decompose a basketball into non-measurable sets. The theorem says something profound about the mathematics of continuous, abstract space — the kind of space described by real numbers, with infinitely many points and no smallest granularity. The paradox reveals a limit on our geometric intuitions: they are reliable for ordinary measurable objects but break down when applied to the full universe of mathematical subsets of continuous space.

The theorem depends crucially on the Axiom of Choice. This axiom states that given any collection of non-empty sets, one can select one element from each — even when the collection is infinite and there is no explicit rule for choosing. The Axiom of Choice is highly non-constructive: it guarantees existence without providing any method of construction. Without it, non-measurable sets cannot be shown to exist, and the Banach-Tarski decomposition is impossible. Most mathematicians accept the Axiom of Choice because its consequences throughout mathematics are too useful to abandon, but the Banach-Tarski Paradox stands as the most dramatic illustration of how counterintuitive the Axiom's existence guarantees can be.

Review Questions

  1. State the Banach-Tarski Paradox. What does it claim, and why does it sound impossible?
  2. What are non-measurable sets? Why are they crucial to the theorem, and why cannot they be physically constructed?
  3. Why does the Banach-Tarski Paradox not violate conservation of mass or any law of physics?
  4. Explain the role of the Axiom of Choice in the theorem. What does the Axiom of Choice say, and why does the theorem depend on it?
  5. What specific assumption about volume does the paradox show is false? Under what conditions does this assumption hold, and when does it break down?
  6. If the Axiom of Choice were rejected, what would happen to the Banach-Tarski Paradox? Why do most mathematicians nonetheless accept the Axiom of Choice?
  7. The text says this is "not a flaw in mathematics." What does it reveal instead?
  8. Compare the Banach-Tarski Paradox to Hilbert's Hotel. What common theme about infinity do they share?
