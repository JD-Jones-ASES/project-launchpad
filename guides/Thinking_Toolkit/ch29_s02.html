The Paradigm Shift — Study Guide

Key Concepts

  • Normal Science: The everyday work of researchers puzzle-solving within an accepted paradigm — extending and refining the framework, not questioning its foundations
  • Paradigm: The shared assumptions, methods, exemplary problems, and standards that define a scientific field and determine what counts as legitimate work
  • Anomalies: Observations the paradigm cannot explain; initially dismissed as errors, but accumulate over time until they trigger a crisis
  • Scientific Revolution: The wholesale replacement of one paradigm by another — a transformation of foundational assumptions, methods, vocabulary, and even the meanings of key terms
  • Science Is Not Smoothly Cumulative: Progress alternates between long periods of normal science and disruptive episodes of revolution; it does not simply add facts to facts
  • Incommensurability: Scientists working in different paradigms are in an important sense working in different conceptual worlds

Important Terms

  • Thomas Kuhn: American historian and philosopher of science (1922–1996); The Structure of Scientific Revolutions (1962) is one of the most cited academic books of the 20th century
  • Paradigm: In Kuhn's precise usage, the shared framework of assumptions, methods, standards, and exemplary solved problems that defines what counts as science in a given field at a given time
  • Normal science: Puzzle-solving within an established paradigm; most scientific work at any time is normal science; it extends the paradigm without questioning it
  • Anomaly: An observation that resists accommodation within the current paradigm; initially set aside as error or "one for later," but accumulates toward a crisis
  • Crisis: The phase in which anomalies have accumulated enough that the paradigm's inadequacy becomes undeniable; precedes revolution
  • Paradigm shift: A revolution in which one paradigm is replaced by another; changes not just theories but the questions asked, methods used, and meanings of key terms
  • Incommensurability: Kuhn's claim that paradigms before and after a revolution are not fully translatable — the same words can mean different things in different paradigms
  • Copernican Revolution: Kuhn's central example; the shift from Ptolemaic geocentrism to Copernican heliocentrism took decades, was genuinely contested, and transformed the meaning of key concepts

Detailed Notes

Thomas Kuhn's The Structure of Scientific Revolutions (1962) proposed a revisionary account of scientific progress. Against the dominant view that science advances by steadily accumulating new facts and refining old theories, Kuhn argued that scientific progress is episodic, alternating between two fundamentally different modes of activity.

The first is normal science — the day-to-day work of researchers operating within an accepted paradigm. A paradigm defines what questions are worth asking, what methods are legitimate, and what counts as a satisfactory answer. Normal science is puzzle-solving: the paradigm provides the rules, and scientists work within them to extend and refine the framework. A Newtonian physicist calculating planetary orbits, a Darwinian biologist tracing evolutionary descent, a plate-tectonics geologist mapping fault zones — all are doing normal science. Most scientific work at any given time has this character. It is productive, cumulative, and relatively uncontroversial within the field.

Over time, anomalies accumulate — observations that the paradigm cannot accommodate without increasingly awkward ad hoc adjustments. At first, scientists set these aside as experimental errors or unimportant puzzles to address later. But if enough anomalies pile up, and if a competing framework emerges that explains both the old successes and the new puzzles more elegantly, the stage is set for revolution. In Kuhn's account, this transition is not a calm, rational process of weighing evidence on both sides. It is messy, contentious, and often generational. Defenders of the old paradigm do not simply concede when presented with evidence; they fight, often with legitimate objections. The new paradigm does not merely add information — it changes the questions deemed worth asking, the methods considered valid, and sometimes the very meaning of key terms.

The Copernican Revolution is Kuhn's central historical example. For over a thousand years, the Ptolemaic model placed Earth at the center of the universe, with its system of epicycles predicting planetary positions accurately enough for navigation. Copernicus proposed instead that the Sun was at the center. His initial model was actually less accurate than Ptolemy's because he retained circular orbits. It took Kepler's ellipses and Galileo's telescopic observations to tip the balance — a process spanning decades in which the old paradigm's defenders had genuine objections. When chemistry replaced alchemy, "element" changed its meaning. When Einstein replaced Newton, "mass" and "time" changed theirs. Scientists on opposite sides of such a shift inhabit, in an important sense, different conceptual worlds. The term "paradigm shift" has been so overused in popular discourse that it now means little more than "big change." In Kuhn's precise sense, there have been perhaps a dozen genuine paradigm shifts in the history of science.

Review Questions

  1. What is "normal science" in Kuhn's account? What role does a paradigm play in defining the work of normal science?
  2. Define "paradigm" as Kuhn uses the term. What does a paradigm include beyond just theories and data?
  3. What are anomalies, and how do they eventually lead to scientific revolution? Why are they not immediately taken as refutations of the paradigm?
  4. Describe the Copernican Revolution as Kuhn presents it. Why was the transition not a single decisive moment but a decades-long contest?
  5. What does Kuhn mean by saying scientists on opposite sides of a paradigm shift are "working in different worlds"? Give an example from the text of a concept that changed meaning across a shift.
  6. The text warns that "paradigm shift" has been diluted by overuse. What is the correct Kuhnian meaning? Give an example of something that is genuinely NOT a paradigm shift despite being described as one.
  7. Kuhn argued that paradigm transitions are often generational rather than purely rational. What does he mean, and is this a criticism of science or just an accurate description of how it works?
  8. How does Kuhn's account of scientific progress challenge the popular image of science as a steady, cumulative accumulation of knowledge?
