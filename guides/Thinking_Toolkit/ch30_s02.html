Occam's Razor — Study Guide

Key Concepts

  • Occam's Razor: The principle of parsimony — when multiple explanations account for the same evidence, prefer the one that requires the fewest assumptions.
  • Parsimony: The quality of an explanation that introduces no more entities, causes, or assumptions than are necessary to account for the observed evidence.
  • William of Ockham: The 14th-century Franciscan friar and logician whose relentless application of parsimony throughout his philosophical work permanently associated the principle with his name.
  • Razor as Heuristic: Occam's Razor is a guide for choosing among explanations, not a guarantee of truth; nature is not obligated to be simple, and complexity should be added when evidence demands it.
  • Scientific Application: Major scientific advances — Copernicus, Newton, Darwin — have often involved replacing more complex explanatory systems with simpler, more unified ones.
  • Limits of Simplicity: Quantum mechanics and climate science demonstrate that the most accurate explanations are sometimes highly complex; simplicity is a starting point, not a destination.

Important Terms

  • Principle of Parsimony: The methodological guideline to prefer explanations that invoke the minimum necessary number of assumptions, entities, or causal mechanisms.
  • Epicycle: In the Ptolemaic (Earth-centered) astronomical system, a small circle in whose circumference a planet was held to move and whose center moved along a larger circle — one of many such complications added to account for observed planetary motions. Copernicus's heliocentric model eliminated the need for most of them.
  • Natural Selection: Darwin's explanatory mechanism for the diversity of life — replacing the need for a separate creation event for each species with a single, unified process.
  • Nominalism: The philosophical position, associated with Ockham, that universals (abstract categories) are names rather than real entities existing independently of the mind.
  • Underdetermination: The philosophical problem that a given set of evidence is often compatible with more than one theory, making purely evidence-based selection among theories impossible without additional criteria such as simplicity.
  • Heuristic: A practical rule of thumb that is useful and reliable in most cases but does not guarantee correct conclusions in all cases.

Detailed Notes

Occam's Razor is one of the oldest and most durable principles in intellectual history. Its core claim is deceptively simple: when two explanations account for the same evidence, prefer the simpler one — the one that requires fewer assumptions, fewer entities, and fewer causal mechanisms. The principle is associated with William of Ockham, a Franciscan friar from 14th-century England who applied it throughout his logical and philosophical work with such consistency that it became permanently linked to his name. Ockham himself was building on a tradition that includes Aristotle, but his relentless application of parsimony made the principle recognizably his.

The razor's practical value is illustrated by the hoofbeats example: if you hear hoofbeats outside your window, you could hypothesize that a horse is passing by or that a zebra has escaped from a zoo. Both explanations are consistent with the evidence, but the horse hypothesis requires fewer assumptions — no escaped zoo animal, no evaded capture, no wandering into an unfamiliar neighborhood. Occam's Razor says to prefer the horse. This is not because zebras are impossible but because the simpler explanation is the appropriate starting point for reasoning. You add the zebra only if the horse explanation fails to account for new evidence.

In the history of science, the razor has been repeatedly productive. Copernicus's heliocentric model was simpler than Ptolemy's Earth-centered system, which required an ever-growing number of epicycles to account for observed planetary motions. Newton's law of universal gravitation unified terrestrial and celestial mechanics under a single principle, replacing a complex array of separate explanations. Darwin's theory of evolution by natural selection replaced the need for separate creation events for each species with one mechanism operating across all of life. In each case, the simpler explanation proved not only more intellectually satisfying but empirically more powerful — making better predictions with fewer auxiliary assumptions.

However, Occam's Razor is a heuristic, not a law. The history of science also contains cases where the simpler explanation was wrong. Quantum mechanics is vastly more complex than classical mechanics, yet it is also more accurate. Climate systems involve thousands of interacting variables, and any "simple" account of them is almost certainly incomplete. The razor does not say the simplest explanation is always correct; it says simplicity is the right starting point, and that complexity should be added incrementally as the evidence demands it. Using the razor to dismiss a complex explanation without examining the evidence it is designed to account for is itself a reasoning error. The razor cuts toward simplicity — but evidence, not preference, must have the final word.

Review Questions

  1. State Occam's Razor in your own words. What does "entities should not be multiplied beyond necessity" mean in practice?
  2. Who was William of Ockham, and why is the principle named after him rather than Aristotle, who expressed a similar idea earlier?
  3. Walk through the hoofbeats example. Why does Occam's Razor favor the horse hypothesis? What would it take to make the zebra hypothesis preferable?
  4. How did Copernicus, Newton, and Darwin each apply the principle of parsimony in their scientific work? What did each simplify?
  5. The section includes a "Caution" that Occam's Razor is a heuristic, not a law of nature. Give two examples from science where the simpler explanation was not the correct one.
  6. What is the difference between using Occam's Razor as a starting point and using it as a final verdict? Why does this distinction matter?
  7. How does Occam's Razor relate to the concept of underdetermination — the idea that evidence alone cannot select among all possible explanations?
  8. Apply Occam's Razor to a real-world scenario: two competing explanations for a phenomenon you find interesting. Which does the razor favor, and does the evidence support that preference?
