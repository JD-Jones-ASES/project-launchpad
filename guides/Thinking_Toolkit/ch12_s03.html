Chapter 12, Section 3 Study Guide: Parfit's Repugnant Conclusion

Overview

This section introduces Derek Parfit's "Repugnant Conclusion," a major unsolved problem in moral philosophy, particularly population ethics. It illustrates how applying total utilitarianism (maximizing overall happiness) can lead to a counter-intuitive outcome: a world with a vast population living barely worthwhile lives might be considered "better" than a world with fewer people living wonderfully fulfilling lives. The section explores why this conclusion feels wrong and examines several proposed philosophical "escape routes" designed to avoid it, each with its own significant challenges.

Key Concepts

  • The Repugnant Conclusion: This is the central paradox, stating that for any population of very happy people, there exists a much larger population whose lives are barely worth living that, by total utilitarian standards, contains more total happiness and is thus considered better. It highlights a profound conflict between our intuitive moral judgments and the logical implications of maximizing total welfare.
  • Total Utilitarianism: A moral theory that dictates actions or policies should aim to maximize the sum total of happiness or welfare across all individuals. The Repugnant Conclusion arises directly from the arithmetic of total utilitarianism, where a large number of small positive values can outweigh a smaller number of large positive values.
  • Population Ethics: This branch of moral philosophy deals with the moral issues surrounding population size, composition, and the welfare of future generations. The Repugnant Conclusion is one of its most challenging problems, revealing the difficulty in aggregating welfare when population numbers vary.
  • Escape Routes: These are alternative moral theories or modifications to utilitarianism designed specifically to avoid the Repugnant Conclusion. Each attempts to address the core problem but often introduces new, equally troubling implications. The main ones discussed are Average Utilitarianism, Critical-Level Theories, and Person-Affecting Views.

Important Terms

  • Repugnant Conclusion: A philosophical paradox, formulated by Derek Parfit, which demonstrates that total utilitarianism implies that a vast population living lives barely worth living is preferable to a smaller population living wonderfully happy lives, due to the sheer quantity of total happiness.
  • Total Utilitarianism: A consequentialist ethical theory which asserts that the best action is the one that maximizes the sum total of happiness or well-being across all individuals affected.
  • Average Utilitarianism: An alternative to total utilitarianism that proposes maximizing the average happiness per person, rather than the total. This approach avoids the Repugnant Conclusion but introduces other counter-intuitive outcomes.
  • Critical-Level Theories: Philosophical theories in population ethics that argue that only lives above a certain "critical threshold" of welfare contribute positively to overall value. Lives below this threshold do not add value, thus avoiding the Repugnant Conclusion.
  • Person-Affecting Views: Ethical perspectives that hold that an action can only be right or wrong if it makes specific individuals better or worse off. These views struggle to evaluate scenarios involving different populations where no specific individual is made worse off.
  • Non-Identity Problem: A philosophical puzzle, also introduced by Derek Parfit, which questions whether we can harm future people by bringing them into existence if different choices would lead to different people being born, and those new people would be worse off. It challenges person-affecting views when evaluating population choices.
  • Population Ethics: The subfield of moral philosophy concerned with ethical questions about the number, identity, and quality of lives in a population, especially concerning future generations and decisions that affect who will exist.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Parfit's Repugnant Conclusion

The philosopher Derek Parfit, in his seminal 1984 work Reasons and Persons, identified what he considered one of the most significant unsolved problems in moral philosophy: the Repugnant Conclusion. This paradox highlights a deep tension between our common-sense moral intuitions and the logical consequences of certain ethical frameworks, particularly total utilitarianism.

The Thought Experiment: World A vs. World Z

To illustrate the Repugnant Conclusion, Parfit presented a compelling thought experiment:

  • World A: Imagine a world inhabited by ten billion people. Each of these individuals lives an exceptionally wonderful life—full of health, creativity, fulfillment, and happiness, reaching the highest possible human quality of life.
  • World Z: Now imagine a vastly different world, containing one hundred trillion people. In World Z, each person lives a life that is barely worth living. These lives are not miserable; there is no suffering, but they are characterized by dull routines, marginal nutrition, and minimal joy. Yet, by the slimmest margin, each individual's life is still preferable to nonexistence.

The Utilitarian Calculation: The problem arises when we apply the principles of total utilitarianism, which measures value by the sum total of happiness or welfare.

  • Even though each individual in World A experiences a vastly superior quality of life compared to individuals in World Z, the sheer number of people in World Z (one hundred trillion) means that their combined, tiny amounts of happiness result in a greater total happiness than the combined, very high happiness of the ten billion people in World A.
  • According to total utilitarianism, World Z, despite the low individual quality of life, is therefore a better world than World A.

The Repugnance: This conclusion is deeply counter-intuitive to almost everyone. It feels "repugnant" to suggest that a world where everyone lives a barely worthwhile life could be morally superior to a world where everyone lives a wonderful, flourishing life. The paradox lies in the stark contrast between this intuitive rejection and the seemingly unassailable arithmetic that produces it.

Why the Repugnant Conclusion Matters

The Repugnant Conclusion is not merely an abstract philosophical puzzle. Its implications extend to real-world policy, governmental decisions, and our understanding of societal progress.

  • Development Strategies: Consider a government choosing between two development strategies:
    • Strategy A: Leads to a small, prosperous population where individuals enjoy a high standard of living.
    • Strategy B: Leads to a much larger population, but with a lower average standard of living for each individual.
  • If total welfare is the sole criterion, Strategy B would be preferred if its larger population, even with lower individual happiness, produced a greater aggregate sum of welfare. This implies that maximizing total welfare might necessitate sacrificing individual quality of life for the sake of sheer numbers, a conclusion many find morally objectionable.
  • The difficulty lies in articulating why this feels wrong without resorting to arbitrary preferences or creating new problems.

Proposed Solutions and Their Difficulties (Escape Routes)

Philosophers have sought various "escape routes" from the Repugnant Conclusion, each attempting to modify or replace total utilitarianism. However, each alternative comes with its own set of challenges:

  1. Average Utilitarianism:

    • How it avoids RC: Instead of maximizing total happiness, this view aims to maximize the average happiness per person. In the World A vs. World Z scenario, World A (with very happy people) would clearly be superior, thus avoiding the Repugnant Conclusion.
    • Problems: Average utilitarianism has its own significant drawbacks:
      • It could imply that adding a very happy person to an already highly happy population is wrong if that person's happiness, while high, is below the current average.
      • It might endorse a tiny population of extremely ecstatic individuals over a much larger population of very happy (but slightly less ecstatic) people, which also feels counter-intuitive.

  2. Critical-Level Theories:

    • How it avoids RC: These theories propose that a life only contributes to overall value if its welfare exceeds a certain "critical threshold." Lives below this threshold do not add positive value to the total. Therefore, simply piling up vast numbers of barely-worth-living lives would not outweigh fewer excellent lives because those barely-worth-living lives wouldn't "count."
    • Problems: The main challenge is setting this "critical threshold." Any specific level appears arbitrary and difficult to justify without philosophical basis, leading to questions about where the line should be drawn.

  3. Person-Affecting Views:

    • How it avoids RC: This approach suggests that we should only consider the welfare of people who actually exist or who will exist as a direct result of our choices. It avoids comparing abstract populations of different sizes, focusing instead on whether our decisions make specific people better or worse off.
    • Problems: Person-affecting views struggle significantly with the Non-Identity Problem. If different policies result in entirely different sets of people being born, it becomes difficult to argue that one policy "harms" anyone, because the "harmed" individuals would not have existed under the alternative policy. This undermines the ability of person-affecting views to guide population choices effectively.

Parfit's Enduring Struggle

Derek Parfit himself spent decades searching for a theory that could avoid the Repugnant Conclusion without generating other equally troubling results. He was never fully satisfied with any proposed solution. In Reasons and Persons, he concluded that population ethics remains one of the most difficult and important areas of moral philosophy precisely because our fundamental intuitions pull in incompatible directions, and no single theory seems capable of capturing all of them without leading to paradoxes.

Connections to Other Philosophical Problems

The Repugnant Conclusion is deeply intertwined with other challenging problems in moral philosophy:

  • The Utility Monster: Another paradox (discussed in Section [related section]) that explores problems with aggregating welfare, where an entity with an insatiable capacity for pleasure could theoretically justify the suffering of many if its happiness contributes more to the total.
  • The Non-Identity Problem: (Discussed in Section [related section]) directly challenges person-affecting views by questioning how we can morally evaluate actions that determine the very identity and existence of future people.
  • The Trolley Problem: (Discussed in Chapter [related chapter]) represents the broader tension in ethics between focusing on the consequences of actions (like maximizing total welfare) and respecting individual rights or intuitions about harm.

These connections underscore that the challenge of the Repugnant Conclusion is not isolated but reflects fundamental difficulties in constructing a coherent and intuitively acceptable moral theory, especially when dealing with the scope and quantity of human lives.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words what the Repugnant Conclusion is and how it arises from the principles of total utilitarianism.
  2. Describe the thought experiment involving World A and World Z. Why does World Z, despite its low individual quality of life, produce a greater total happiness according to total utilitarian standards?
  3. Why does the Repugnant Conclusion matter for real-world policy and development decisions, beyond being a philosophical puzzle? Provide an example.
  4. Outline the three "escape routes" (Average Utilitarianism, Critical-Level Theories, Person-Affecting Views) proposed to avoid the Repugnant Conclusion. For each, explain how it attempts to solve the problem and what new difficulties or counter-intuitive results it introduces.
  5. Based on Parfit's own conclusions, why does population ethics remain such a difficult and important field of study in moral philosophy? What does it reveal about our moral intuitions?
