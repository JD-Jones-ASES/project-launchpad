The Pre-Mortem (Decision Tool) — Study Guide

Key Concepts

  • Pre-Mortem: A structured technique, developed by Gary Klein, in which a team imagines that a project has already failed completely — before it begins — and then generates explanations for why it failed.
  • Prospective Hindsight: The cognitive mechanism that makes pre-mortems effective; imagining that failure has already happened makes potential failure modes feel concrete and specific rather than abstract and hypothetical.
  • Optimism Bias Counteraction: Once a group has committed to a plan, members suppress concerns and focus on reasons for success. The pre-mortem reframes failure analysis as a constructive contribution, giving people permission to voice concerns.
  • Groupthink Counteraction: In team settings, dissent is socially costly. The pre-mortem makes raising concerns the expected behavior — reframing the pessimist as the realist who is helping stress-test the plan.
  • 30% Improvement: Klein's research found that pre-mortems increase the ability to identify reasons for failure by roughly 30% compared to simply asking "what could go wrong?"
  • Personal Application: The technique works equally well for individual decisions — imagining regret after a job acceptance or a major purchase surfaces concerns that optimism and excitement would otherwise suppress.

Important Terms

  • Pre-Mortem: A prospective failure analysis conducted before a project begins, in which participants assume the project has failed and generate explanations for why.
  • Post-Mortem: A retrospective analysis of what went wrong after a project has already failed; the pre-mortem performs the same function before execution, when corrections are still possible.
  • Optimism Bias: The systematic tendency to overestimate the probability of positive outcomes and underestimate the probability of negative ones — especially pronounced once a group has committed to a plan.
  • Groupthink: The tendency of cohesive groups to suppress dissent and converge prematurely on consensus, reducing the quality of decision-making by excluding critical perspectives.
  • Prospective Hindsight: The technique of imagining that a future event has already occurred in order to reason more concretely about how it came to pass — the cognitive mechanism underlying pre-mortem effectiveness.
  • Failure Mode: A specific way in which a system, plan, or decision can go wrong; pre-mortems are designed to surface failure modes before they materialize.
  • Mitigating Action: A step taken in response to an identified failure mode to prevent it, reduce its likelihood, or limit its impact if it occurs.

Detailed Notes

Gary Klein, a researcher in naturalistic decision-making, developed the pre-mortem as a structured tool for counteracting the overconfidence that tends to accompany group commitment to a plan. A post-mortem examines what went wrong after failure has occurred; a pre-mortem does something more valuable — it examines what could go wrong before execution begins, when there is still time to prevent or prepare for failures. The technique's elegance lies in a simple reframing: instead of asking "what could go wrong?" (which invites abstract speculation), it asks "the project has already failed — explain why" (which invites concrete, specific reasoning).

The procedure has four steps. First, the team assumes failure: imagine that you are one year in the future and the project has failed completely and spectacularly, not partially or in a recoverable way. Second, each person independently writes down all the reasons for the failure — what went wrong, what risks materialized, what assumptions proved false, what was not anticipated. Third, the explanations are compiled and prioritized: identify the most plausible, the most frequently mentioned, and the most catastrophic. Fourth, for each high-priority failure mode, ask what can be done now to prevent it, reduce its likelihood, or limit its impact if it occurs.

The pre-mortem works because it counteracts two of the most powerful forces that degrade group decision quality. The first is optimism bias: once a group has committed to a plan, the social and psychological dynamics of the group push members toward focusing on reasons for success and suppressing concerns. Asking people to explain a hypothetical failure gives them explicit permission to raise those concerns — and makes doing so a contribution to the plan's success rather than an act of disloyalty or negativity. The second is groupthink: in most team settings, the person who raises concerns about an enthusiastically endorsed plan takes on social costs. The pre-mortem reframes dissent by making failure analysis the task everyone is expected to perform, transforming the "pessimist" into a valued contributor. Klein's research found that this reframing increases the identification of failure modes by roughly 30% compared to simply asking the team what might go wrong.

The technique is equally applicable to individual decisions. Before accepting a job offer, imagine that one year later you deeply regret having taken it. Why? Was it the culture, the commute, the nature of the work, the opportunity cost of what you gave up? Before committing to a major purchase, imagine that six months later you wish you had not made it. Why? These personal pre-mortems surface concerns that optimism and excitement — and the sunk-cost commitment to having already made the choice — would otherwise suppress. The best time to discover a fatal flaw in any plan is before you execute it, when changing course is still possible.

Review Questions

  1. What is the difference between a post-mortem and a pre-mortem? Why is the pre-mortem more useful, even though a post-mortem analyzes an actual failure?
  2. Describe the four-step pre-mortem procedure. Why is each step necessary?
  3. What is prospective hindsight, and how does imagining that failure has already occurred make the pre-mortem more effective than simply asking "what could go wrong?"
  4. How does the pre-mortem counteract optimism bias? What specifically does it do that gives people permission to raise concerns they might otherwise suppress?
  5. How does the pre-mortem counteract groupthink? How does it change the social cost of expressing dissent?
  6. Klein found that pre-mortems improve failure identification by roughly 30%. Why is the reframing of the question — from "what could go wrong" to "this has already failed, explain why" — responsible for this improvement?
  7. Describe a personal pre-mortem for a significant decision: choosing a major, accepting a job offer, or making a large purchase. What failure modes does it surface?
  8. The section mentions pre-mortems being used in software development, medicine, and event planning. Choose one of these domains and describe specifically what a pre-mortem might reveal that conventional planning would miss.
