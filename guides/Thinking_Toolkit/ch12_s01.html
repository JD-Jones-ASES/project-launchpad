The Non-Identity Problem: A Study Guide

Overview

The Non-Identity Problem, introduced by Derek Parfit, is a profound philosophical puzzle that challenges our fundamental understanding of harm, moral obligation, and our responsibilities to future generations. It arises when our long-term decisions not only affect the conditions of future lives but also determine the very identity of the people who will live them, making it difficult to argue that any specific individual has been "harmed" by choices that led to their existence, however degraded.

Key Concepts

  • The Non-Identity Problem: A philosophical puzzle where a choice impacts both the quality of life for future generations and the specific identities of the individuals who will exist, making it difficult to claim that the individuals born under a "worse" policy have been harmed, as their only alternative was non-existence.
  • Identity of Future Generations: The crucial insight that long-term decisions (like environmental policy, medical advances, or personal timing of childbirth) significantly alter who meets whom, who reproduces, and when, thereby changing the specific people who will be born.
  • Harm vs. Non-existence: The core dilemma that individuals born into degraded conditions due to past policy choices cannot be said to be "harmed" by those choices in a traditional sense, because without those very policies, they would not have existed at all. As long as their lives are "marginally worth living," their existence is not worse than non-existence.
  • Person-Centered vs. Impersonal Value: The problem highlights a tension in ethical theories. Traditional "person-centered" theories require an identifiable victim who is made worse off. The non-identity problem suggests that we might need to consider "impersonal value"—whether the overall state of the world is better or worse, regardless of who inhabits it—even if this leads to other puzzles like the Repugnant Conclusion.
  • Real-World Relevance: The Non-Identity Problem is not abstract; it directly applies to critical contemporary issues such as climate change policy, genetic engineering, and long-term resource management, where decisions impact future populations profoundly.

Important Terms

  • Non-Identity Problem: A philosophical challenge concerning moral obligations to future people, where the identity of those people is contingent on the very decisions being evaluated.
  • Derek Parfit: A prominent British philosopher, author of Reasons and Persons (1984), who articulated the Non-Identity Problem and considered it one of the most important and difficult challenges in ethics.
  • Future Generations: The people who will live in the future, often centuries from now, whose well-being and existence are affected by current decisions.
  • Harm: In a traditional moral sense, an action that makes a specific individual worse off than they would have been otherwise. The Non-Identity Problem complicates this definition when the alternative to a "harmful" action is non-existence for the individual.
  • Impersonal Value: A concept in ethics that suggests what matters is the overall quality of outcomes or states of the world, rather than the welfare or rights of specific individuals.
  • Repugnant Conclusion: A separate but related puzzle (also by Parfit) that arises if one prioritizes total impersonal value, suggesting that a vast population of barely happy people might be morally preferable to a smaller population of very happy ones.
  • Contractualism: An ethical framework (e.g., T. M. Scanlon's) which posits that an action is wrong if it violates principles that no one could reasonably reject. The Non-Identity Problem challenges its application to future generations whose existence is contingent on current choices.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to the Non-Identity Problem

The Non-Identity Problem, introduced by Derek Parfit in Reasons and Persons (1984), is a central puzzle in ethics concerning our obligations to future generations. It arises from the observation that many long-term decisions not only affect the quality of future lives but also determine which specific individuals will come into existence.

The Core Scenario

Consider a government choosing between two energy policies:

  • Policy A (Clean & Expensive): Preserves the environment, leading to a high quality of life for future generations.
  • Policy B (Cheap & Dirty): Causes significant environmental degradation, lowering the quality of life for people born in the next century.

Intuitively, most people believe Policy B is wrong, feeling we owe future generations a livable planet. However, Parfit's puzzle complicates this intuition.

The Problem: No Identifiable Victim

The critical insight is that the choice between Policy A and Policy B will change everything about the future, including who meets whom, who has children, and when those children are conceived. This means:

  • The people born under Policy A would be different people from those born under Policy B.
  • People living in the degraded world of Policy B cannot complain that they have been harmed by Policy B. Their only alternative was not a better life, but non-existence.
  • As long as their lives under Policy B are at least "marginally worth living" (i.e., not so terrible that non-existence would have been preferable), it's hard to claim they were made "worse off" by being born under Policy B.

So, if no specific individual is "harmed," to whom do we owe an obligation?

The Structure of the Puzzle

The Non-Identity Problem manifests whenever a decision simultaneously impacts:

  1. The conditions of future lives: The quality, resources, and environment available to them.
  2. The identity of the people who will live them: Which specific individuals will be born.

This applies to nearly all long-term decisions:

  • Environmental Policies: Influence economic conditions, migration, social interactions, and thus who meets and has children with whom.
  • Medical Advances: Enable individuals to survive and reproduce who otherwise wouldn't, altering the genetic makeup of future populations.
  • Personal Choices: Even decisions like when to have children or where to live can affect the specific individual conceived.

The puzzle isn't a lack of moral intuition (we strongly feel degrading the environment is wrong), but that our standard moral concepts—like harm, rights, and obligations—typically require an identifiable victim who is made worse off. The Non-Identity Problem appears to eliminate such a victim.

Why It Is So Hard to Solve: Proposed Approaches and Their Challenges

Philosophers have explored several avenues to address the Non-Identity Problem, but each faces significant difficulties:

  • Impersonal Value: This approach suggests that what matters is whether the overall state of the world is better or worse, regardless of the identity of its inhabitants. Policy A produces a better world than Policy B.
    • Challenge: This perspective can lead to the "Repugnant Conclusion" (another Parfit puzzle), where a vast population of barely-happy people might be deemed morally superior to a smaller population of very happy people, simply because the total amount of "value" is greater.
  • Rights-Based Approaches: This posits that future people have a right to a certain quality of life, irrespective of whether they would have existed under alternative policies.
    • Challenge: It's difficult to define what this "right" entails when the very existence of the right-holder depends on the policy in question. Can someone have a right to something if they wouldn't exist to exercise that right otherwise?
  • Contractualism (T. M. Scanlon): This framework suggests an action is wrong if it violates principles that no one could reasonably reject. One might argue that no one could reasonably reject a principle requiring a habitable planet.
    • Challenge: The concept of "no one" becomes circular when it must include people who do not yet exist and whose existence is itself dependent on the choices being made. How can non-existent people "reasonably reject" anything?

Parfit's View and Real-World Significance

Parfit himself acknowledged the Non-Identity Problem as one of the most important and difficult challenges in ethics. It is not merely an academic exercise; its implications are profound for:

  • Climate Change Policy: Aggressive emissions reductions would result in different individuals being born in 2100 compared to a high-emissions scenario. The future inhabitants of a degraded world cannot claim harm, yet most believe we have obligations to them. The problem forces us to articulate the grounding of these obligations beyond individual harm.
  • Genetic Engineering: Decisions about altering human embryos or gene editing can affect who is born and with what characteristics.
  • Long-Term Resource Management: Policies on resource depletion will determine the conditions for future generations and, indirectly, who they are.

The puzzle forces us to confront the limitations of person-centered moral theories and consider whether ethical frameworks need to incorporate impersonal values or find new ways to articulate duties to future generations that do not rely on the concept of direct harm to identifiable individuals.

Review Questions

  1. Explain Derek Parfit's Non-Identity Problem in your own words. Why does the choice between Policy A and Policy B in the example complicate our understanding of harm?
  2. According to Parfit, why can the people born under Policy B (the dirty policy) not claim that they have been "harmed" by that policy? What is the crucial alternative that they face?
  3. Describe two different types of long-term decisions, beyond environmental policy, where the Non-Identity Problem could arise. How do these decisions affect both the conditions and the identity of future people?
  4. Briefly discuss two of the proposed solutions to the Non-Identity Problem (e.g., impersonal value, rights-based approaches, contractualism). What are the main challenges or criticisms associated with each of these solutions?
  5. Why is the Non-Identity Problem considered highly relevant to current discussions about climate change? What does it force us to reconsider about our ethical obligations to future generations in this context?
