Simpson's Paradox — Study Guide

Key Concepts

  • Simpson's Paradox: A trend that appears in every subgroup can reverse (or disappear) when the subgroups are combined — aggregation can produce a picture that is the opposite of the truth
  • Confounding Variable: An unmeasured variable correlated with both the grouping variable and the outcome; the hidden engine of Simpson's Paradox
  • Aggregation Distortion: Combining groups with different baseline rates, without controlling for the confounder, generates a misleading overall statistic
  • Disaggregation as Solution: Always examining subgroups separately before drawing conclusions from combined data is the primary safeguard
  • Berkeley Case Study: Women applied disproportionately to harder departments; the resulting aggregate appeared to show gender discrimination where none existed at the department level

Important Terms

  • Edward H. Simpson: British statistician who described the paradox formally in a 1951 paper; Karl Pearson (1899) and Udny Yule (1903) noted related phenomena earlier
  • Simpson's Paradox: The statistical phenomenon in which a relationship present within each subgroup reverses when the subgroups are combined into an aggregate
  • Confounding variable: A variable that is correlated with both the grouping variable (e.g., sex) and the outcome variable (e.g., admission rate), but is not accounted for in the analysis
  • Lurking variable: An alternative term for confounding variable — a variable "lurking" in the background that drives the apparent relationship in the aggregated data
  • UC Berkeley admissions study (1973): The famous real-world example: overall data suggested women were admitted at lower rates, but departmental analysis showed no discrimination — the confounder was which departments women applied to
  • Randomization: A technique in clinical trials that balances confounders across treatment groups, preventing Simpson's Paradox from distorting results
  • Stratified analysis: Dividing data into subgroups defined by the confounder and examining each separately, rather than collapsing to an aggregate

Detailed Notes

Simpson's Paradox takes its name from Edward H. Simpson's 1951 paper, though the phenomenon was noted earlier by Pearson (1899) and Yule (1903). It describes a counterintuitive statistical phenomenon: a trend or association that appears in every subgroup can reverse or vanish when the subgroups are combined. The most celebrated real-world example comes from a 1973 study of graduate admissions at the University of California, Berkeley.

In 1973, Berkeley appeared to show significant gender bias in admissions: approximately 44% of male applicants were admitted overall, compared to only about 35% of female applicants. The gap was statistically significant and seemed to confirm a pattern of systematic discrimination. However, when researchers examined the data department by department, a startling reversal emerged. In most individual departments, women were admitted at equal or even higher rates than men. There was no departmental-level discrimination. The aggregate gap was entirely an artifact of how the data was combined.

The explanation involves a confounding variable: which departments applicants chose. Women disproportionately applied to smaller, more competitive departments with low admission rates for everyone — humanities, social sciences, and similar programs. Men disproportionately applied to larger departments — engineering and the natural sciences — which happened to accept a higher proportion of all applicants because they had more available spots. The overall comparison mixed two very different applicant pools without accounting for this difference. When you compare men and women only within the same department (controlling for the confounder), the discrimination signal disappears.

The paradox operates whenever a lurking variable is (1) correlated with the grouping variable (here, sex and department choice are correlated) and (2) correlated with the outcome (department choice and admission rate are correlated). Hiding this variable by aggregation produces a misleading picture. The paradox appears across many domains: in sports (a batter can have a higher season average in every individual year but a lower career average than a rival), in medicine (a treatment can appear better overall but worse for every subgroup), and in public policy (a policy can appear to improve aggregate outcomes while making every demographic group worse off). In medicine, the stakes are life-or-death: a drug appearing superior in aggregate data might be inferior for every patient subgroup — and the only safeguard is randomized trials with stratified analysis.

Review Questions

  1. State Simpson's Paradox. What is the counterintuitive thing it demonstrates about subgroups and aggregates?
  2. Explain the Berkeley admissions example in detail. What did the overall data show? What did the departmental data show? What was the confounding variable?
  3. How does a confounding variable produce Simpson's Paradox? What two correlations must it have to cause the reversal?
  4. Describe a medical scenario where Simpson's Paradox could lead to the wrong treatment decision, and explain how randomization and stratification prevent this.
  5. Give an example from sports (the text mentions batting averages) where Simpson's Paradox could appear.
  6. What practical steps should a researcher or analyst take to detect or prevent Simpson's Paradox?
  7. The text poses a "Think About It" question: a city has two hospitals; Hospital A has a higher overall survival rate. What confounding variable might explain why this aggregate comparison is misleading?
  8. Why is the practical lesson of Simpson's Paradox especially important in medical research? What institutional practices does it motivate?
