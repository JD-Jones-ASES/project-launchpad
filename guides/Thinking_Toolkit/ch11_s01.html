Study Guide: The Drowning Child Argument

Overview

This section introduces Peter Singer's influential "Drowning Child" argument, which explores our moral obligations to help others, especially those in distant need. It presents a thought experiment where saving a nearby child seems obvious, then challenges us to consider if the same moral imperative applies to children suffering from preventable causes worldwide, despite psychological differences that might make us feel less obligated. The section delves into the demanding implications of Singer's view and highlights the tension between impartiality and partiality in ethics.

Key Concepts

  • The Drowning Child Thought Experiment: A scenario where one can easily save a child from drowning at minor personal cost (e.g., ruining shoes, being late), leading to the obvious conclusion that saving the child is morally required.
  • Singer's Core Argument: If it is in our power to prevent something very bad from happening, without thereby sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance, we ought to do it.
  • Moral Irrelevance of Distance: Singer argues that geographical distance or the fact that victims are "statistics" rather than "identifiable individuals" does not change our moral obligation to help.
  • Psychological Barriers vs. Moral Justification: Common psychological reasons for feeling less obligated to distant suffering (e.g., identifiable victim effect, diffusion of responsibility, uncertainty) explain why we feel differently but do not, according to Singer, justify a difference in moral obligation.
  • Demandingness of Singer's Conclusion: Taken to its logical extreme, Singer's principle suggests that individuals in wealthy countries should donate most of their disposable income to prevent suffering until their own sacrifice is "comparable" to the benefit provided.
  • Impartiality vs. Partiality: The argument reveals a fundamental tension in moral philosophy: whether morality requires us to treat everyone's interests equally (impartiality) or allows us to prioritize those closest to us (partiality).
  • Effective Altruism: Singer's work provides a philosophical foundation for this movement, which advocates for using evidence and reason to determine the most effective ways to improve the world.

Important Terms

  • Peter Singer (b. 1946): Australian moral philosopher, renowned for his work in animal ethics and global poverty. His "Famine, Affluence, and Morality" (1972) paper introduced the Drowning Child argument and significantly influenced modern ethics and the effective altruism movement.
  • Drowning Child Argument: A thought experiment proposed by Peter Singer to illustrate the moral imperative to prevent suffering, even when the victims are distant, provided the cost to the helper is not morally significant.
  • Identifiable Victim Effect: A psychological phenomenon where people respond more strongly and offer more help to specific, identifiable individuals in need than to anonymous, statistical victims.
  • Diffusion of Responsibility: A socio-psychological phenomenon where individuals feel less personal responsibility for taking action when others are present, assuming someone else will step in.
  • Impartiality: The moral principle of treating all individuals and their interests equally, without bias or preference for certain people or groups.
  • Partiality: The moral stance that allows or requires giving special consideration or priority to the interests of certain individuals (e.g., family, friends, fellow citizens) over others.

Detailed Notes

The Drowning Child Scenario and Its Core Implication

  • Initial Thought Experiment: You encounter a child drowning in a shallow pond. You can easily save them, but it would ruin your expensive shoes and make you late for a meeting.
  • Obvious Moral Answer: The overwhelming consensus is that you must save the child. The child's life vastly outweighs the cost of shoes or being late. To do otherwise would be seen as morally monstrous.
  • Singer's Extension: Philosopher Peter Singer uses this clear intuition as a starting point. He argues that if you accept the obligation to save the nearby child, you are logically committed to a more demanding conclusion regarding distant suffering.
  • The Analogy to Distant Suffering: Thousands of children die daily from preventable causes (malaria, malnutrition, contaminated water) in distant parts of the world. You can save some of these children by donating money to effective charities, at a cost (disposable income) far less than a child's life.

Peter Singer's Core Argument

  • The Principle: Singer's argument, paraphrased, states: "If it is in our power to prevent something very bad from happening, without thereby sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance, we ought to do it."
  • Moral Calculus: The argument asserts that the moral weight of a child's life remains the same whether they are at your feet or in a distant country. Distance does not alter the moral obligation.

Why Distance Seems to Matter (Psychological Differences)

Most people feel a stronger obligation to the nearby child. Several psychological factors contribute to this:

  1. Identifiability: The drowning child is a specific, visible individual, triggering a stronger emotional response. Distant children are often abstract statistics, making it harder to connect emotionally (the "identifiable victim effect").
  2. Proximity and Certainty: You can directly see and save the drowning child, knowing your action will have an immediate, certain outcome. Donating to charity involves uncertainty about the money's effectiveness or its reach.
  3. Diffusion of Responsibility: You are the sole rescuer for the nearby child. For global poverty, millions could help, leading to a diminished sense of individual obligation because responsibility is shared.
  4. Effort and Directness: Saving the nearby child is a single, concrete, heroic act. Addressing global poverty requires sustained, abstract, and often less tangible commitment.

Singer's Rebuttal: Psychological vs. Moral Relevance

  • Singer acknowledges these psychological differences but firmly argues they are not morally relevant.
  • Our feelings, developed in smaller, localized communities, may not have adapted to a globalized world where our choices affect distant strangers.
  • The fact that we feel less obligated does not mean we are less obligated. Moral obligations should stem from principles, not just evolved intuitions.

The Demandingness of Singer's View

  • Logical Conclusion: Following Singer's principle strictly, one should continue giving until the marginal cost to oneself equals the marginal benefit to others. For many in wealthy nations, this would mean donating a very substantial portion, even the vast majority, of one's income, leading to significant personal impoverishment.
  • Moderated Position: In later works like The Life You Can Save (2009), Singer softened his practical recommendation, proposing a sliding scale of giving (e.g., 1% for modest earners, rising for the wealthy). While the philosophical principle remains demanding, the practical suggestion aims for something more achievable by most people.
  • Common Objections: Many readers push back on the extreme demandingness:
    • Is morality truly this demanding?
    • Do I have an obligation to impoverish myself for strangers?
    • Don't I have a right to live my own life, pursue personal projects, and prioritize my own family?
  • Impartiality vs. Partiality: These objections highlight a core tension in moral philosophy:
    • Impartiality: The idea that everyone's interests should count equally.
    • Partiality: The idea that we are allowed, or even obligated, to give special weight to those closest to us (family, friends).
    • The Drowning Child argument doesn't resolve this tension but powerfully reveals it.

Peter Singer: A Brief Biography

  • Key Figure: Peter Singer is an Australian moral philosopher and one of the most influential and controversial ethicists of the modern era.
  • Major Contributions:
    • Animal Liberation (1975) launched the modern animal rights movement.
    • His work on global poverty (e.g., "Famine, Affluence, and Morality") provided the philosophical foundation for the effective altruism movement.
  • Ethical Stance: Singer is known for his commitment to following arguments to their logical conclusions, even when they lead to unpopular or highly demanding ethical positions.

Review Questions

  1. Explain Peter Singer's core argument in "Famine, Affluence, and Morality." How does the initial Drowning Child scenario set up this argument, and what is its ultimate conclusion regarding our obligations to the global poor?
  2. Identify and describe three psychological reasons why people might feel a stronger obligation to save a nearby drowning child than to help a distant child dying from preventable causes.
  3. How does Singer address the psychological differences between nearby and distant suffering? Why does he argue that these differences are not morally relevant?
  4. Discuss the "demandingness" objection to Singer's argument. What is the logical conclusion of his principle, and how has Singer's practical recommendation evolved over time?
  5. What is the fundamental tension between "impartiality" and "partiality" that Singer's Drowning Child argument brings to light? How does this tension relate to the question of how demanding morality can reasonably be?
