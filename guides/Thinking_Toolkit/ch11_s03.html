Study Guide: The Trolley Problem

Overview

The Trolley Problem is a foundational thought experiment in modern moral philosophy, developed by Philippa Foot and expanded by Judith Jarvis Thomson. It presents a series of dilemmas designed to probe our moral intuitions about life-or-death tradeoffs, revealing the complex interplay between different ethical frameworks like consequentialism and deontology, and highlighting how factors such as personal involvement, intent, and rights influence our judgments.

Key Concepts

  • Trolley Problem (Original): A scenario where one must choose between diverting a runaway trolley to kill one person and saving five, or doing nothing and allowing five to die. Most people choose to divert.
  • Doctrine of Double Effect: A moral principle stating that it is permissible to perform an action that has both a good intended effect and a foreseen, but unintended, bad side effect, provided the good effect is proportionate and the bad effect is not directly intended as a means to the good.
  • Footbridge Variant: A variation where one must actively push a large man off a footbridge to stop a trolley and save five, resulting in his death. Most people recoil, despite the identical arithmetic outcome.
  • Transplant Surgeon Variant: A scenario where a surgeon could kill one healthy person to harvest organs and save five dying patients. This highlights the concept of individual rights.
  • Consequentialism: An ethical theory that judges the morality of an action based solely on its outcomes or consequences. The "best outcome" (e.g., saving more lives) is considered the most moral.
  • Deontology: An ethical theory that judges the morality of an action based on rules, duties, and obligations, rather than consequences. It emphasizes that certain actions are inherently right or wrong, regardless of their outcomes, and that individuals have inherent rights and dignity.
  • Moral Intuition: Immediate, often emotional, moral judgments that guide our responses to ethical dilemmas, which the trolley problem reveals can be inconsistent across similar outcomes.
  • Means vs. Side Effect: A distinction crucial to the Doctrine of Double Effect, where using someone's death as the means to achieve a good outcome is generally seen as less permissible than their death being a side effect of an action aimed at good.
  • Personal vs. Impersonal Force: The idea that direct physical contact or personal involvement in causing harm (e.g., pushing someone) elicits a stronger emotional and negative moral response than indirect or mechanical actions (e.g., pulling a lever).
  • Rights and Dignity: The concept, central to deontological ethics, that individuals possess inherent value and rights, and should not be treated merely as tools or means to an end, even for the greater good.

Important Terms

Trolley Problem : A family of thought experiments in ethics, first introduced by Philippa Foot, that explores moral dilemmas involving sacrificing one life to save multiple lives.

Doctrine of Double Effect : A moral principle, rooted in Catholic theology, that evaluates actions with both good and bad outcomes, distinguishing between intended harm and foreseen but unintended harm.

Consequentialism : The ethical framework that asserts the rightness or wrongness of an action is determined by its outcomes or consequences.

Deontology : The ethical framework, derived from the Greek "deon" (duty), that argues some actions are intrinsically right or wrong, based on rules, duties, or rights, independent of their consequences.

Philippa Foot (1920–2010) : A British moral philosopher best known for introducing the original Trolley Problem and for her work in reviving virtue ethics.

Judith Jarvis Thomson (1929–2020) : An American philosopher who significantly extended the Trolley Problem with variants like the Footbridge Case and developed other influential thought experiments concerning bodily autonomy.

Moral Intuition : An immediate, non-inferential moral judgment or feeling that arises quickly when presented with an ethical situation.

Virtue Ethics : A moral philosophy that focuses on the character of the moral agent rather than the rules or consequences of actions, emphasizing virtues like honesty, compassion, and courage.

Thought Experiment : A hypothetical scenario used to explore philosophical questions, test theories, or illustrate complex ideas by reasoning about a situation that cannot be easily studied in reality.

Detailed Notes

The Origin and Purpose of the Trolley Problem

The Trolley Problem was first introduced by British philosopher Philippa Foot in 1967 in her paper, "The Problem of Abortion and the Doctrine of Double Effect." It was later sharpened and extended by American philosopher Judith Jarvis Thomson in the 1980s. Its primary goal is not to offer a definitive solution, but to expose the complexities and potential inconsistencies in our moral reasoning, making abstract philosophical positions like consequentialism and deontology tangible and "gut-level."

The Original Trolley Problem

Scenario: A runaway trolley is headed towards five workers. You can pull a lever to divert it onto a side track, where it will hit and kill one worker instead.

Common Response: Most people quickly agree that pulling the lever is the correct action.

Rationale: The decision seems straightforward based on simple arithmetic: saving five lives at the cost of one is a better outcome. The action is seen as redirecting an existing danger rather than creating new harm, and the one worker's death is a tragic side effect. This aligns with consequentialist thinking, where the greatest good (saving the most lives) determines the right action.

Connection to Doctrine of Double Effect: Foot used this scenario to explore the doctrine of double effect, a principle distinguishing between harm one intends and harm one merely foresees as a side effect. In the original trolley problem, saving five is the intended effect, and the one death is a foreseen but unintended consequence, making the action permissible under this doctrine.

The Footbridge Variant

Scenario: You are on a footbridge over the tracks. The same trolley is heading towards the same five workers. Next to you is a very large man. Pushing him off the bridge onto the tracks will stop the trolley, saving the five workers, but killing him.

Common Response: Most people recoil from this option, despite the identical arithmetic (one death for five saved).

Why the Divergence in Intuition? Several explanations have been proposed for why this scenario feels fundamentally different and less permissible:

  • Means vs. Side Effect: In the footbridge case, the large man's death is not a side effect but the means by which the five are saved. You are actively using him as a tool, which is prohibited by the doctrine of double effect and deontological principles.
  • Personal vs. Impersonal Force: Pushing a human being off a bridge involves direct physical contact and feels viscerally like killing, activating emotional brain regions. Pulling a lever feels more mechanical and impersonal, engaging more calculating reasoning.
  • The Contact Principle: Research suggests people are more reluctant to cause harm when it involves direct physical contact with the victim, highlighting that our moral intuitions can track features of the action (how harm is caused) rather than just the outcome (how many die).

The Transplant Surgeon Variant

Scenario: A surgeon has five patients, each needing a different organ to survive. A healthy person comes in for a routine checkup. The surgeon could kill this healthy person, harvest their organs, and save all five patients.

Common Response: Almost no one agrees the surgeon should proceed.

Why Not? The Role of Rights: This variant pushes the limits of pure consequentialism. While the outcome (one death, five lives saved) is arithmetically identical to the trolley problems, our strong rejection of the surgeon's action points to the importance of rights. The healthy person has a fundamental right not to be killed, which outweighs the utilitarian benefit of saving five other lives. This scenario strongly supports deontological ethics, where certain actions are inherently wrong, regardless of their consequences.

Why Our Intuitions Diverge: Key Factors

The trolley problem isn't one puzzle but a family of puzzles, each designed to isolate different moral variables that influence our judgments:

  1. Doing vs. Allowing: We tend to judge actively causing harm more harshly than passively allowing harm to occur, even if the outcome is the same. Pulling a lever feels closer to "allowing" the trolley to take a different path, while pushing someone is unmistakably "doing."
  2. Intended vs. Foreseen Harm: The doctrine of double effect highlights this: foreseen harm (a side effect) is often seen as more permissible than intended harm (a means to an end).
  3. Personal vs. Impersonal: Neuroscience research by figures like Joshua Greene suggests that "personal" dilemmas (direct physical harm) engage emotional brain regions, while "impersonal" dilemmas (lever-pulling) engage more rational, utilitarian reasoning.
  4. Rights and Dignity: Deontological ethics, particularly Kantian philosophy, posits that individuals have inherent dignity and rights and should never be used merely as means to others' ends. Violating these rights, even for a greater good, is seen as morally wrong.

The Trolley Problem in the Real World

Far from being a mere academic exercise, the trolley problem's structure mirrors real-world dilemmas:

  • Autonomous Vehicles: How should self-driving cars be programmed to react in unavoidable accident scenarios? Should they prioritize the lives of occupants, other road users, or minimize overall harm?
  • Medical Triage: During crises (e.g., pandemics), decisions about allocating scarce resources like ventilators involve implicit trolley problem logic: which patients should receive life-saving care when not everyone can?
  • Military Decisions: The concept of "collateral damage" in warfare directly invokes the doctrine of double effect: is it permissible to bomb a legitimate military target if civilian deaths are a foreseen but unintended consequence?
  • Public Policy: Decisions about speed limits, pollution regulations, and drug approval processes involve implicit tradeoffs between public safety (lives saved) and other factors (economic cost, individual freedom), even if not explicitly framed as trolley problems.

Key Philosophers: Philippa Foot and Judith Jarvis Thomson

  • Philippa Foot: A British moral philosopher influential in the revival of virtue ethics, which emphasizes character and human flourishing. Her introduction of the trolley problem profoundly impacted modern ethical debate.
  • Judith Jarvis Thomson: An American philosopher renowned for her vivid thought experiments, including the footbridge variant of the trolley problem and the "violinist analogy" in debates about abortion. Her work demonstrated the power of hypotheticals to shift philosophical conversations.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the core difference between the original Trolley Problem and the Footbridge Variant. What moral principle or intuition does this difference highlight?
  2. How does the "Doctrine of Double Effect" apply to the original Trolley Problem? In which variants does it seem to break down, and why?
  3. The Transplant Surgeon variant pushes us to consider something beyond pure consequentialism. What "something" is it, and why does it make the action feel profoundly wrong despite the arithmetic?
  4. Identify and explain at least three factors that philosophers and psychologists have identified as influencing our moral intuitions in trolley-like dilemmas.
  5. Beyond philosophical discussion, provide two real-world examples where the core dilemmas of the Trolley Problem might manifest. How do these real-world scenarios echo the hypothetical situations?
