Falsifiability — Popper's Criterion — Study Guide

Key Concepts

  • Falsifiability: A claim is scientific if and only if it makes predictions specific enough that some conceivable observation could prove it wrong
  • Demarcation Criterion: Popper's answer to the question separating science from non-science — not truth, utility, or careful observation, but exposure to potential refutation
  • A Theory That Explains Everything Explains Nothing: When a theory can accommodate any possible outcome, it makes no real prediction and provides no genuine scientific knowledge
  • Risky Predictions as Scientific Virtue: Good scientific theories make specific, testable predictions that could fail — this exposure to falsification is what makes them informative
  • Unfalsifiable Does Not Mean Worthless: Moral principles and metaphysical claims may be unfalsifiable but still meaningful — they are simply not playing the scientific game
  • Contrast Cases: Einstein's general relativity (falsifiable, confirmed but could have failed) vs. Freudian theory (explains any outcome, immune to falsification)

Important Terms

  • Karl Popper: Austrian-British philosopher (1902–1994); born in Vienna, fled Nazism; settled at the London School of Economics; published The Logic of Scientific Discovery (1934) and The Open Society and Its Enemies (1945)
  • Falsifiability: The property of a claim that it makes predictions specific enough to be potentially refuted by some conceivable observation
  • Demarcation problem: The philosophical question of what distinguishes scientific from non-scientific claims; Popper's answer is falsifiability
  • Ad hoc modification: An adjustment made to a theory specifically to protect it from a falsifying observation; a sign the theory is being immunized rather than tested
  • General relativity: Einstein's 1915 theory predicting that gravity bends starlight by a specific amount; confirmed by Eddington's 1919 solar eclipse expedition, but could have been refuted
  • Freudian psychoanalysis: Popper's example of an unfalsifiable framework — any patient outcome (improvement or worsening) can be accommodated within the theory
  • Corroboration: Popper's preferred term for a theory's track record of surviving genuine tests — not "confirmation" (which implies proof) but evidence the theory has not yet been falsified

Detailed Notes

Karl Popper developed the falsifiability criterion in the 1920s and 1930s while observing how different intellectual movements responded to evidence. He noticed that Marxist historians and Freudian psychoanalysts possessed a remarkable ability to explain any possible outcome after the fact. If a revolution occurred, Marxism had predicted it. If a revolution failed to materialize, Marxism explained why the conditions were not yet ripe. If a patient improved in therapy, Freud's theory was vindicated. If the patient worsened, the theory explained that too — the patient was "resisting." No possible outcome could embarrass these theories. Popper concluded this was precisely their intellectual weakness: a theory that explains everything predicts nothing. Having no risk of failure means carrying no genuine information.

The contrast Popper drew was with Einstein's general relativity. In 1915, Einstein made a specific, quantitative prediction: gravity would bend starlight passing near the sun by approximately 1.75 arcseconds. This was a testable, risky prediction — specific enough to fail. Arthur Eddington's 1919 solar eclipse expedition confirmed the bending to within experimental error. But the crucial point for Popper is that the prediction could have failed. If the bending had not been observed, or had been a different amount, general relativity would have faced serious difficulty. Einstein himself acknowledged he would have had to abandon the theory. That willingness to be proven wrong — that genuine exposure to falsification — is what makes a prediction scientifically meaningful.

Popper's criterion: a claim is scientific if and only if it is falsifiable — if some conceivable observation would count against it. "All swans are white" is falsifiable because one black swan refutes it. "Everything happens for a reason" is not falsifiable because no observation can ever count against it — whatever happens, a believer can always assert the reason was there. Note the critical distinction: falsifiability does not mean a claim is false. It means the claim is the kind of claim that could in principle be shown to be false. Furthermore, Popper was explicit that unfalsifiable claims are not necessarily worthless. "Be kind to others" is unfalsifiable — no experiment can disprove it — but it is a perfectly good moral principle. His point was narrower: unfalsifiable claims are not scientific claims. They may be important, meaningful, even true. They are simply playing by different rules.

The practical applications are extensive. Medical drug trials are designed so the hypothesis can be falsified: if the treatment group does no better than the placebo group, the hypothesis fails. Without this structure, every anecdote becomes "evidence" and ineffective treatments persist indefinitely. Many conspiracy theories have the opposite structure: absence of evidence proves the conspiracy is well-hidden; contradictory evidence proves the conspirators planted it. Every possible outcome confirms the theory, which is why it predicts nothing. Good strategic planning specifies in advance what outcomes would signal that a strategy has failed — without this prior commitment, any result can be recruited to claim the strategy is working.

Review Questions

  1. State Popper's falsifiability criterion in your own words. What property must a claim have to count as scientific?
  2. Why does Popper argue that "a theory that explains everything explains nothing"? Use Freudian psychoanalysis as an example.
  3. Explain why Einstein's general relativity is, for Popper, a paradigm example of good science. What specific prediction did it make, and why did the prediction matter?
  4. Distinguish between a claim being falsifiable and a claim being false. Can a falsifiable claim turn out to be true?
  5. What does Popper mean when he says unfalsifiable claims are "not scientific, but may still be important"? Give an example of a meaningful unfalsifiable claim.
  6. Why are many conspiracy theories structured to be unfalsifiable? How does this immunize them from refutation — and why does that undermine rather than support them?
  7. Describe how a business strategy can be designed to be falsifiable. What would that require that vague strategies lack?
  8. How does medical drug testing embody Popper's criterion? What is the role of the placebo group in making the hypothesis falsifiable?
