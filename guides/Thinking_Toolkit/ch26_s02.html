Zeno's Paradoxes Revisited — Study Guide

Key Concepts

  • Convergent Infinite Series: An infinite sum whose partial sums approach a definite finite limit; the series 1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 + ... converges to exactly 1
  • Resolution of Zeno: The mathematical proof that infinitely many tasks can be completed in finite time, provided each successive task takes sufficiently less time than the last
  • Geometric Series: A series where each term is a constant multiple (the ratio r) of the previous term; converges to a finite sum whenever |r| < 1
  • False Assumption in Zeno: Zeno assumed "infinitely many parts" implies "infinitely much total time" — the convergent series proves this is wrong
  • Partial Sums: The sums of the first n terms of a series; their limiting value as n grows is the sum of the infinite series
  • Divergence: Not all infinite series converge — the harmonic series grows without bound even though its terms approach zero

Important Terms

  • Zeno's Dichotomy Paradox: To cross a room of length 1, you must first cross 1/2, then 1/4, then 1/8 — producing infinitely many required sub-journeys
  • Convergent series: An infinite series whose partial sums approach a finite limit; the series "sums to" that limit in the precise mathematical sense
  • Divergent series: An infinite series whose partial sums grow without bound; the harmonic series 1 + 1/2 + 1/3 + 1/4 + ... is a divergent series despite its terms approaching zero
  • Geometric series: A series of the form a + ar + ar² + ar³ + ...; converges to a/(1-r) whenever |r| < 1
  • Limit: The value that a sequence or series approaches as its terms continue indefinitely; the concept that provides the rigorous definition of a convergent sum
  • Calculus: The mathematical framework (developed in the 17th century by Newton and Leibniz) that introduced limits and provided the tools to resolve Zeno's paradoxes
  • Harmonic series: The series 1 + 1/2 + 1/3 + 1/4 + ...; diverges even though terms decrease, because they do not decrease fast enough

Detailed Notes

Zeno's paradoxes were introduced in an earlier chapter. This section revisits them with the mathematical tools needed to explain why motion is possible despite the apparent contradiction. In Chapter 1, Zeno argued that to cross a room you must first cross half, then half of the remainder, then half of that — an infinite sequence of sub-journeys. How can anyone complete infinitely many tasks? The ancient Greeks had no satisfying mathematical answer; they could sense motion was real but lacked the tools to explain why the paradox fails. Those tools arrived in the 17th century with the development of calculus.

The core resolution is a single profound idea: an infinite sum can have a finite total. The distances Zeno's traveler must cross — 1/2, 1/4, 1/8, 1/16, and so on — form a geometric series with first term a = 1/2 and ratio r = 1/2. Computing the partial sums makes the convergence visible: after one term, S₁ = 0.5; after two, S₂ = 0.75; after three, S₃ = 0.875; after four, S₄ = 0.9375. Each partial sum is larger than the last but never exceeds 1, and the formula for the partial sum — S_n = 1 − (1/2)^n — approaches 1 as n grows without bound. The infinite sum equals exactly 1, not approximately 1 but exactly. Completing all infinitely many sub-journeys crosses exactly the entire room.

What this means for Zeno is decisive. His argument assumed that completing infinitely many tasks must take infinitely long. The geometric series proves this assumption wrong. When Achilles chases the tortoise, he does cover infinitely many sub-distances. But each sub-distance is smaller than the last in a geometric pattern, and the total distance is finite. The time intervals for each sub-journey also form a convergent series. Achilles completes all infinitely many intervals in finite total time. Zeno was right that the journey can be divided into infinitely many parts; he was wrong that infinitely many parts require infinite time. The hidden false assumption is that "infinitely many" implies "infinitely much" — a feeling that is natural and completely wrong.

An important caveat distinguishes convergent from divergent series. The harmonic series — 1 + 1/2 + 1/3 + 1/4 + ... — has terms that get smaller and smaller, approaching zero, yet its sum grows without bound. It is divergent. This shows that terms merely getting small is not enough to guarantee a finite sum. Convergence requires that the terms get small fast enough. The geometric series with ratio r = 1/2 passes this test; the harmonic series does not. This distinction matters throughout mathematics and physics: whether an infinite process leads to a finite or infinite total depends on the rate at which successive terms decrease.

Review Questions

  1. State Zeno's dichotomy paradox. Why does it appear to make motion impossible?
  2. What is a convergent series? Write out the first five partial sums of 1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 + ... and show what they are converging toward.
  3. What is a geometric series, and when does it converge? What is the formula for the sum of an infinite geometric series with first term a and ratio r?
  4. What was the false assumption in Zeno's reasoning? How does the convergent series expose it?
  5. Explain how the Achilles and the Tortoise version of Zeno's paradox is resolved by the same mathematical idea.
  6. What is the harmonic series? Why does it diverge even though its terms approach zero? How does this differ from the geometric series in Zeno's paradox?
  7. Why did the ancient Greeks lack the mathematical tools to resolve Zeno's paradoxes? What intellectual development was required?
  8. The text says the infinite sum equals exactly 1, "not approximately 1." Why is this precision — the word "exactly" — philosophically important for resolving Zeno?
