Underdetermination — Study Guide

Key Concepts

  • Underdetermination: For any body of evidence, multiple theories can fit the data equally well — evidence alone never fully determines which theory is correct
  • Duhem's Insight: No hypothesis is ever tested in isolation; every experiment relies on auxiliary background assumptions, and an anomalous result may indicate a failed auxiliary rather than a failed main hypothesis
  • Quine's Extension: Our entire web of beliefs faces experience as a whole; any belief can in principle be maintained in the face of contrary evidence by adjusting other beliefs in the network
  • Duhem-Quine Thesis: The combined claim that evidence underdetermines theory and that hypotheses can always be protected by modifying auxiliary assumptions
  • Theoretical Virtues: The non-evidential criteria scientists use to choose among empirically equivalent theories: simplicity, elegance, unifying power, fruitfulness
  • Science Is Not Arbitrary: Underdetermination does not make theory choice random — theoretical virtues have an excellent practical track record

Important Terms

  • Underdetermination: The thesis that for any body of evidence, more than one theory is compatible with all of it; empirical evidence alone cannot uniquely identify the correct theory
  • Pierre Duhem: French physicist and philosopher (1861–1916); argued in 1906 that hypotheses cannot be tested in isolation but only as part of a larger theoretical system
  • W. V. O. Quine: American philosopher (1908–2000); extended Duhem's insight in "Two Dogmas of Empiricism" (1951) to argue that any belief can be retained given sufficient adjustments elsewhere in one's web of beliefs
  • Duhem-Quine thesis: The joint thesis combining Duhem's holism about testing with Quine's holism about belief revision
  • Auxiliary hypothesis: A background assumption supporting an experimental test — about instrument calibration, absence of confounders, the validity of a measurement technique; anomalous results may indicate a failed auxiliary rather than a failed main hypothesis
  • Theoretical virtue: A property of theories valued in theory selection beyond mere empirical fit: simplicity, elegance, unifying power, predictive fruitfulness, internal consistency
  • Ptolemaic model: The geocentric model with epicycles; accurately predicted planetary positions and could not be definitively refuted by early modern astronomical data alone
  • Copernican model: The heliocentric model; initially less accurate than Ptolemy's but ultimately preferred for its greater simplicity, elegance, and unifying power

Detailed Notes

Underdetermination poses a philosophically uncomfortable fact about scientific reasoning: the evidence never fully determines which theory is correct. For any body of data, multiple theories can fit the observations equally well. The choice of which theory to accept cannot be made by evidence alone — something else must be brought to bear.

The Copernican Revolution provides the most celebrated historical illustration. Both the Ptolemaic and Copernican models competed to describe planetary motion for decades before the heliocentric model achieved consensus. Both predicted planetary positions in the sky with roughly comparable accuracy. An astronomer in 1550, armed with the best available observational data, could not choose definitively between them on purely empirical grounds. The Ptolemaic system, with enough epicycles, could be adjusted to match almost any observation. The Copernican system was simpler in some respects but initially less accurate because Copernicus retained circular orbits. What eventually tipped the balance was not a single crucial observation but a combination of factors: Kepler's discovery that elliptical orbits made the heliocentric model far more elegant and predictive; Galileo's telescopic evidence (phases of Venus, Jupiter's moons) that was difficult, though not strictly impossible, to accommodate in the Ptolemaic framework; and above all, the fact that the Copernican model opened the door to Newton's universal gravitation, which unified terrestrial and celestial mechanics in a way Ptolemy never could. Theory choice depended on theoretical virtues — simplicity, elegance, unifying power, fruitfulness — not just on evidence.

Pierre Duhem articulated a related insight in 1906: no hypothesis can be tested in isolation. Every experiment rests on a web of auxiliary assumptions — that the instruments are calibrated correctly, that confounding factors have been excluded, that the measurement techniques are valid. When an experiment produces an unexpected result, something in this web is wrong, but the anomaly alone cannot identify which part. The main hypothesis might be incorrect — or an auxiliary assumption might have failed. A scientist can always protect the main hypothesis by blaming an auxiliary, and this protection is sometimes legitimate. W. V. O. Quine extended this point in 1951, arguing that our entire system of beliefs faces experience as a whole. Any individual belief can be maintained in the face of any adverse evidence, provided one is willing to make compensating adjustments elsewhere in the network. In principle, no single observation is logically sufficient to compel the abandonment of any specific theory.

Does underdetermination make science arbitrary? The text's answer is no. Scientists do not freely choose whatever theories they prefer regardless of evidence. Instead, they systematically apply theoretical virtues to select among empirically equivalent options. Simpler theories are preferred over complex ones; more unified theories over fragmented ones; theories that generate fruitful new predictions over those that merely accommodate known data. These virtues are not mere aesthetic preferences — they have a remarkable track record of guiding inquiry toward theories that prove productive and accurate. Science succeeds not by logically deducing the truth from data but through the disciplined management of underdetermination via the careful application of criteria whose reliability has been demonstrated over centuries.

Review Questions

  1. Define underdetermination. What does it say about the relationship between evidence and theory?
  2. Explain the Copernican example. What does it show about theory choice in the 16th and 17th centuries?
  3. What were the factors that eventually led scientists to prefer the Copernican over the Ptolemaic model? Were these factors purely evidential?
  4. Explain Duhem's point about auxiliary hypotheses. Why does an anomalous experimental result not straightforwardly falsify the main hypothesis?
  5. What is the Duhem-Quine thesis in its strongest form? What does it imply about the logical relationship between evidence and theory?
  6. What are theoretical virtues? Name at least four and explain why each is valued in theory selection.
  7. Does underdetermination make science arbitrary? What is the text's answer, and do you find it convincing?
  8. Give a real-world example of underdetermination outside astronomy — from medicine, history, or data science — and explain how theoretical virtues help resolve or manage it.
