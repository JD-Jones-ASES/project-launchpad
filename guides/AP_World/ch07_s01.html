Study Guide: Collapse of the Land-Based Empires

AP World History — Topic 7.1

Key Concepts

  • Simultaneous Imperial Collapse: The Ottoman, Qing, and Romanov empires all ceased to exist in their traditional forms between 1911 and 1924 — not coincidentally, but because all three faced the same structural pressures: nationalist movements among subject peoples, the economic demands of industrial warfare, and the mismatch between multi-ethnic dynastic legitimacy and a world reorganizing around ethnically defined nation-states.

  • Turkification and the Armenian Genocide: The Ottoman CUP's response to multi-ethnic crisis was coercive Turkification — imposing Turkish identity on minorities. World War I provided the cover for this policy to escalate into the systematic deportation and massacre of Armenians (1915–1923), recognized as the first genocide of the twentieth century and a template for state-organized mass murder.

  • Unequal Treaties and Chinese Nationalism: The unequal treaty system imposed on the Qing following military defeats between 1842 and 1911 — ceding territory, opening treaty ports, granting extraterritoriality, and imposing indemnities — functionally semi-colonized China. The humiliation of this system became the central grievance driving Chinese nationalism and the 1911 revolution.

  • Power Vacuum and Warlordism: The collapse of the Qing did not produce a stable successor state. The Republic of China fragmented almost immediately, and China entered the Warlord Era (1916–1928), a decade of regional military competition that left a power vacuum unfilled until 1949 — shaping every Chinese crisis in between.

  • WWI as the Killing Blow for the Romanovs: Russia mobilized over 12 million men but lacked the industrial base to supply them. Five million casualties, bread shortages in Petrograd, and the Provisional Government's refusal to exit the war produced two revolutions in 1917, replacing the Romanov dynasty with the world's first communist state.

  • Nationalism vs. Dynastic Legitimacy: The deepest structural cause of all three collapses was the collision between the political force of nationalism (peoples demanding self-determination) and the cultural force of dynastic legitimacy (centuries of tradition justifying multi-ethnic rule). When nationalism became the dominant framework, multi-ethnic empires lost their justification.

Vocabulary Review

  • Turkification: The CUP policy of coercively imposing Turkish language, culture, and identity on non-Turkish minorities within the Ottoman Empire, providing the ideological framework for the Armenian Genocide.

  • Armenian Genocide: The systematic deportation and massacre of Ottoman Armenians by the CUP during WWI (1915–1923), killing an estimated 600,000 to 1.5 million people — the first genocide of the twentieth century.

  • Unequal treaty: A treaty imposed after military defeat requiring the weaker state to make major concessions — territory, ports, extraterritoriality, indemnities — without equivalent benefits. China signed dozens between 1842 and 1911.

  • Extraterritoriality: The right of foreign nationals in China to be subject to their home country's laws rather than Chinese law, a humiliating symbol of Qing loss of sovereignty embedded in unequal treaties.

  • Revolution of 1911 (Xinhai Revolution): The military uprising beginning at Wuchang that ended 267 years of Qing rule and more than two thousand years of imperial governance when Emperor Pu Yi abdicated on 12 February 1912.

  • Warlord Era (1916–1928): The decade of Chinese political fragmentation following Yuan Shikai's death, in which regional military commanders controlled territories while the nominal Beijing government exercised little real authority.

  • October Manifesto (1905): Tsar Nicholas II's concession following the Revolution of 1905, promising a constitutional assembly (Duma) — partial, quickly backtracked, but revealing Romanov vulnerability.

  • Bolshevik Revolution (October 1917): The Bolshevik seizure of power in Petrograd under Lenin, promising "Peace, Land, Bread" and transforming Russia into the world's first communist state.

  • Treaty of Sèvres (1920): The post-WWI settlement that partitioned Anatolia among multiple powers; repudiated by the Turkish nationalist movement under Mustafa Kemal.

  • Self-determination: The principle that peoples sharing a common culture and identity have the right to govern themselves — the dominant nationalist framework of the early twentieth century that made multi-ethnic dynastic empires untenable.

  • CUP (Committee of Union and Progress): The Young Turk organization that governed the Ottoman Empire after 1908 and bore primary responsibility for Turkification and the Armenian Genocide.

  • Romanov dynasty: Russia's imperial ruling family from 1613 to 1917, governing the world's largest territorial empire until the February Revolution forced Nicholas II's abdication.

Key Figures and Events

  • Mustafa Kemal (Atatürk): Led the Turkish nationalist resistance to the Treaty of Sèvres, expelled Greek forces from Anatolia (1919–1922), founded the Republic of Turkey in October 1923, and abolished the sultanate and caliphate.
  • Pu Yi: Last Qing emperor, abdicated 12 February 1912 as a child — ending over two thousand years of Chinese imperial governance.
  • Yuan Shikai: Seized the Chinese presidency after the 1911 revolution, attempted to restore the monarchy (1915–1916), died before succeeding, leaving the Warlord Era power vacuum.
  • Vladimir Lenin: Led the Bolshevik October 1917 revolution with the promise of "Peace, Land, Bread"; began transforming Russia into the world's first communist state.
  • Russo-Japanese War (1904–1905): First major defeat of a European great power by an Asian nation in the modern era; triggered the Russian Revolution of 1905 and revealed Romanov vulnerability.
  • The Armenian Genocide (1915–1923): CUP-organized systematic deportation and massacre of Armenians during WWI — 600,000 to 1.5 million dead; established a grim template for state-organized ethnic destruction.
  • Wuchang Uprising (October 1911): The military revolt that triggered province-by-province declarations of independence from Qing authority, rapidly toppling the dynasty.

Themes

Political: The collapse of the Ottoman, Qing, and Romanov empires represents the most decisive political transformation of the early twentieth century — the replacement of multi-ethnic dynastic empires by ethnically defined nation-states. Each empire's collapse transferred political authority from hereditary dynasties claiming divine right or tradition to nationalist governments claiming to represent "the people." This shift reshaped borders, created new minority vulnerabilities, and established the nation-state as the default form of political organization for the remainder of the century. The power vacuums left by collapse — China's Warlord Era, the post-Ottoman Arab mandate system, the Russian Civil War — directly fueled the instability that produced the conflicts of the 1930s and 1940s.

Cultural: The dynastic legitimacy that had sustained these empires for centuries — the Romanovs since 1613, the Qing since 1644, the Ottomans for over six centuries — proved unable to compete with nationalism as a cultural framework for political belonging. When peoples began to define themselves as Armenians, Chinese, Ukrainians, Poles, or Arabs rather than as subjects of a dynasty, the cultural glue of empire dissolved. The Armenian Genocide illustrates the most extreme form of this cultural conflict: the CUP's attempt to forcibly homogenize the empire's population through Turkification, eliminating those who could not or would not assimilate.

Compare and Contrast

The Qing and Romanov collapses share a surface similarity — both dynasties fell to revolutionary pressure — but differ in important ways. The Qing's crisis was rooted in decades of external humiliation: the unequal treaty system imposed by Britain, France, Russia, Japan, and the United States after repeated military defeats functionally semi-colonized China, stripping it of territory, legal sovereignty, and economic resources. The resulting nationalist movement was explicitly anti-dynastic and anti-imperial. The Romanov collapse, by contrast, was detonated primarily by the immediate catastrophe of World War I — the military failure to supply a 12-million-man army, the resulting five million casualties, and bread shortages in Petrograd — on top of pre-existing pressures. In both cases, the dynasties failed to provide either external security or domestic welfare, but the Qing's failure was measured in decades of external humiliation while the Romanovs' was measured in three years of catastrophic industrial war.

Practice Prompt

Short Answer Question (SAQ)

"The near-simultaneous collapse of the Ottoman, Qing, and Romanov empires between 1911 and 1924 was not coincidental but the product of shared structural pressures."

(A) Identify ONE specific structural pressure shared by all three empires that contributed to their collapse, and briefly explain how it weakened each.

(B) Explain how World War I served as a proximate cause of collapse for ONE of the three empires (your choice). Be specific about the mechanisms involved.

(C) Explain how the collapse of one of the three empires contributed to political instability or conflict in the decades that followed (1920s–1940s). Use specific evidence.
