Study Guide: The Non-Aligned Movement

AP World History — Unit 8, Section 3

Key Concepts

  • Non-alignment as active diplomacy: The Non-Aligned Movement is frequently misunderstood as political passivity or neutrality. In reality, it was an active strategy through which newly independent states maximized sovereignty and leverage by playing the superpowers against each other. Egypt obtained Soviet dam funding after American withdrawal; India received aid from both superpowers; Yugoslavia extracted Western concessions by distancing itself from Moscow.

  • Bandung Conference (1955) as founding moment: The first gathering of twenty-nine African and Asian nations without European colonial powers or superpowers present. Bandung's political significance lay not only in its stated principles but in its very composition: it asserted that peoples subjected to colonialism had "the right and the capacity to organize their own international order."

  • Three architects of non-alignment: Nehru (India), Nasser (Egypt), and Tito (Yugoslavia) each demonstrated non-alignment's possibilities in different ways — intellectual articulation, strategic action during a crisis, and defiance of superpower control respectively. Their diversity (a democratic socialist, a nationalist, a Communist) illustrated that non-alignment was a strategic framework, not an ideology.

  • The limits of non-alignment: Non-alignment had real limits: many nominally non-aligned states accepted superpower aid under pressure; regional conflicts among NAM members undermined Third World solidarity; the NIEO economic demands were rejected by industrialized powers; and the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan (1979) produced no unified NAM response.

  • NIEO: extending non-alignment to economic sovereignty: The Group of 77's New International Economic Order demands (early 1970s) attempted to translate political non-alignment into economic sovereignty — demanding fairer trade, debt relief, and technology transfer. Its rejection by industrialized powers illustrated that formal political independence did not automatically transform colonial economic structures.

  • The alignment/non-alignment distinction as tendency, not rigid category: Formally aligned states (France under de Gaulle, Romania in the Warsaw Pact) exercised independence; nominally non-aligned states accepted superpower aid. The categories describe tendencies, not absolute positions.

Vocabulary Review

Non-Aligned Movement (NAM) — Formal diplomatic movement launched at the Belgrade Conference (1961) of states refusing Cold War bloc membership; grounded in anti-colonialism and the right to self-determination; active diplomatic strategy, not passive neutrality.

Bandung Conference (1955) — First large gathering of twenty-nine African and Asian postcolonial states; produced the Bandung Communiqué asserting sovereignty and opposing colonialism; founding event of Third World solidarity.

Panchsheel — Nehru's five principles of peaceful coexistence: mutual sovereignty respect, non-aggression, non-interference, equality, and peaceful coexistence; the NAM's philosophical foundation.

Suez Crisis (1956) — Egyptian nationalization of the Suez Canal; Anglo-French-Israeli invasion; both superpowers opposed the invasion; Nasser emerged victorious and a global hero of anti-colonial nationalism — the paradigm case of non-alignment as active strategy.

Third World — Cold War-era term for postcolonial nations outside both superpower blocs; carried a political meaning of postcolonial solidarity asserting a rightful place in the international order.

Group of 77 — Coalition of developing nations (originally 77) that used NAM solidarity to push for the New International Economic Order; represents the extension of non-aligned political goals into the economic domain.

New International Economic Order (NIEO) — Developing nations' 1970s demands for fairer trade terms, debt relief, and technology transfer; rejected by industrialized powers; articulated the principle of economic sovereignty.

Uti possidetis — Legal principle endorsed by the Organization of African Unity (1964) that colonial-era borders would be maintained at independence; prevented territorial reorganization but also meant colonial administrative lines determined postcolonial state boundaries.

Belgrade Conference (1961) — Formal founding conference of the Non-Aligned Movement, organized by Tito, Nehru, Nasser, Sukarno, and Nkrumah.

Key Figures and Events

  • Nehru — India's first prime minister; intellectual architect of panchsheel and non-alignment; argued newly independent nations had an obligation to remain outside Cold War blocs and serve as mediators.
  • Nasser — Egyptian leader whose handling of the Suez Crisis demonstrated non-alignment's concrete strategic power; nationalized the Suez Canal, survived foreign invasion, and became a hero of Arab nationalism.
  • Tito — Yugoslav Communist who defied Stalin in 1948, charted an independent socialist path, and organized the Belgrade Conference; demonstrated that non-alignment was possible even for a Communist European state.
  • Sukarno — Indonesian president who convened the Bandung Conference; positioned Indonesia as a Third World leader.
  • Bandung Conference (April 1955) — Landmark assertion of postcolonial international agency; twenty-nine African and Asian nations, representing more than half the world's population, met without European or superpower presence.
  • Soviet invasion of Afghanistan (1979) — Tested the NAM's cohesion; the NAM could not produce a unified response, illustrating that even a major Soviet action against a country associated with non-aligned movements could not generate bloc solidarity.

Themes

The Political theme is central to this section: the Non-Aligned Movement represents one of the most significant attempts by newly independent states to carve out political space in a bipolar world dominated by two superpowers. The movement demonstrates that agency in international relations is not reserved for great powers — smaller and newly independent states can shape outcomes through skillful diplomacy, coalition-building, and strategic exploitation of great-power rivalries. The NAM's use of the UN General Assembly to push for decolonization and condemn apartheid shows how multilateral institutions can amplify the power of states that would be individually weak.

The Economic theme emerges through the NIEO, which attempted to translate political independence into economic sovereignty. The demands for fairer trade terms, debt relief, and technology transfer reveal that leaders of newly independent states understood that formal political independence was insufficient without structural economic transformation. Colonial economic relationships — extractive, commodity-focused, technology-depriving — could persist long after the colonial flag was lowered. The NIEO's failure also illustrates a key pattern in twentieth-century history: political decolonization moved faster than economic decolonization, leaving many newly independent states structurally embedded in economic relationships inherited from the colonial era.

Compare and Contrast

A productive AP comparison places the Non-Aligned Movement's strategy alongside formal Cold War alignment as competing approaches to the central challenge facing newly independent states: how to protect sovereignty and advance development in a world dominated by two superpowers. Aligned states gained military protection, economic aid, and political patronage — but accepted constraints on their foreign policy and risked being drawn into superpower conflicts for reasons unrelated to their own interests. Non-aligned states accepted vulnerability in exchange for flexibility: they could receive aid from both superpowers, maintain genuinely independent foreign policies, and build Third World coalitions. The comparison reveals that both strategies involved real tradeoffs, and that "non-alignment" was not simply the absence of alignment but an active foreign policy choice with its own strategic logic and limitations.

Practice Prompt

Short-Answer Question (SAQ)

Use the following passage and your knowledge of the period to answer parts A, B, and C.

"Non-aligned leaders played the superpowers against each other: Egypt's Nasser obtained Soviet funding for the Aswan Dam after American financing was withdrawn; India received aid from both superpowers while maintaining its independence; Yugoslavia's Tito extracted economic and security concessions from the West by maintaining his distance from Moscow." — Source text, Section 8.3

A. Explain ONE specific historical development that made it possible for newly independent states to pursue a non-aligned strategy in the 1950s and 1960s — that is, what structural conditions of the Cold War gave these states leverage?

B. Explain ONE specific limitation of the Non-Aligned Movement as a strategy for protecting the sovereignty of newly independent states, using evidence from the section.

C. Explain how the Non-Aligned Movement's demands for a New International Economic Order (NIEO) represented a continuation and extension of the same anti-colonial principles that motivated the Bandung Conference.
