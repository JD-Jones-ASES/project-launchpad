Study Guide: AP World History — Topic 5.3: The French Revolution

Key Concepts

  • Structural inequality as the root cause: The Ancien Régime's three-estate system left the fiscal burden of France's war debts on the Third Estate (97% of the population) while the clergy and nobility paid little or no direct taxes — a structural injustice that made fiscal crisis into political crisis.
  • From fiscal dispute to constitutional revolution: The Estates-General's 1789 meeting escalated through the Tennis Court Oath, the Bastille's fall, and food shortages into a full constitutional revolution. Each step radicalized the next.
  • Distinct revolutionary phases: The Revolution moved through constitutional monarchy (1791), republic (1792), the Reign of Terror (1793–1794), Thermidor, and the Directory before Napoleon's coup — each phase more radical (or more conservative) than the last.
  • The paradox of revolutionary violence: The Terror demonstrated that Enlightenment ideals of liberty, when translated into political power under existential pressure (foreign invasion, internal rebellion), could produce outcomes — mass executions — their authors had not intended. This paradox haunts all subsequent revolutions.
  • Napoleon as heir and betrayer: Napoleon preserved the Revolution's legal achievements (Code Napoléon) and spread them through conquest while abolishing political freedoms the Revolution had created. His 1802 reversal of slavery abolition directly triggered the Haitian Revolution's final phase.
  • Atlantic resonance of the Declaration of the Rights of Man: The Declaration's universalist language was read across the Atlantic world, including in Haiti, providing vocabulary that enslaved people and colonized peoples could claim — even though French revolutionaries had not intended it for them.

Vocabulary Review

  • Ancien Régime: The pre-revolutionary French social and political order of absolute monarchy supported by privileged estates, with the tax burden falling on the Third Estate.
  • Estates-General: France's feudal representative body divided into clergy, nobility, and everyone else; convened in 1789 for the first time since 1614 to resolve the fiscal crisis, triggering the Revolution instead.
  • Third Estate: Approximately 97% of France's population — peasants, artisans, workers, bourgeoisie — who bore the tax burden and whose National Assembly became the engine of revolution.
  • Tennis Court Oath (June 20, 1789): The National Assembly's pledge not to disband until France had a constitution, transforming a tax dispute into a constitutional revolution by asserting popular sovereignty.
  • sans-culottes: Radical urban working poor of Paris — artisans, shopkeepers, wage laborers — who demanded price controls, popular democracy, and anti-counter-revolutionary action, driving the Terror from below.
  • Reign of Terror (1793–1794): The revolutionary government's program of emergency mass executions (~17,000 officially) to defend the Republic against foreign invasion and internal rebellion, led by Robespierre and the Jacobins.
  • Committee of Public Safety: The emergency governing body that dominated France during the Terror, arguing existential threats justified revolutionary violence.
  • Thermidor: The July 1794 reaction that ended the Terror; Robespierre was arrested and executed by fellow revolutionaries, followed by a more conservative Directory.
  • Code Napoléon (1804): Napoleon's civil law code synthesizing Enlightenment principles of legal equality, property rights, and abolition of feudal distinctions; model for legal systems across Europe and Latin America.
  • Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen (1789): The National Assembly's proclamation of natural rights — liberty, property, security, resistance to oppression — whose universalist language resonated globally.

Key Figures and Events

  • Louis XVI: Absolute monarch who convened the Estates-General to solve the fiscal crisis; his Flight to Varennes (1791) destroyed trust; guillotined January 1793 for treason.
  • Maximilien Robespierre (1758–1794): Jacobin leader who dominated the Terror, arguing revolutionary violence was necessary for survival; arrested and guillotined in Thermidor.
  • The sans-culottes: Radical urban poor whose pressure from below drove the Revolution's most extreme phase, demanding popular democracy and economic controls.
  • Napoleon Bonaparte (1769–1821): Military general who overthrew the Directory (1799); Emperor (1804); codified Enlightenment legal principles in the Code Napoléon while abolishing political freedoms.
  • Storming of the Bastille (July 14, 1789): The moment the Revolution moved from the Assembly hall into the streets; symbol of popular action against royal despotism.
  • Vendée uprising: Royalist peasant rebellion in western France, one of the counter-revolutionary threats that justified the Terror's emergency measures.

Themes

The French Revolution embodies the Political SPICE-T theme most dramatically: it abolished absolute monarchy and the feudal social order, executed the king, declared popular sovereignty, and created modern political vocabulary (left and right, liberty/equality/fraternity, revolution itself). Its universalist language in the Declaration of the Rights of Man had global Cultural resonance — read in Haiti, translated into dozens of languages, cited by revolutionaries for a century. The Revolution also demonstrated the Economic transformation associated with abolishing feudalism: the Code Napoléon's emphasis on legal equality and enforceable contracts created the legal infrastructure for capitalist property relations across France and wherever Napoleon's empire reached.

The Revolution's most important historical contribution may be its demonstration of the paradox inherent in all subsequent revolutionary politics: movements dedicated to liberty can justify mass violence in liberty's defense. The Terror's guillotine — symbolically egalitarian, actually murderous — captures this paradox. That paradox recurs in the Haitian, Russian, and Chinese revolutions, making the French case not simply a European story but a template for understanding revolutionary radicalization globally.

Compare and Contrast

The French Revolution was far more socially radical than the American Revolution but less racially radical than the Haitian. Where the American Revolution's elite founders protected existing social hierarchies (especially slavery) during independence, the French Revolution erupted from within French society, attacking the monarchy, aristocracy, and Church simultaneously — abolishing feudal privileges, trying and executing the king, and eventually (in 1794) abolishing slavery. Yet French revolutionary leaders' initial equivocation about whether the Declaration of the Rights of Man applied to enslaved Africans in Saint-Domingue, and Napoleon's 1802 reversal of abolition, showed that even the most radical European revolution still hesitated at the full implications of universal rights. The Haitian Revolution went furthest.

Practice Prompt

Answer all three parts of the following SAQ:

A. Explain how the structural inequalities of the Ancien Régime's estate system contributed to the fiscal crisis that triggered the French Revolution in 1789.

B. Explain the conditions that led to the Reign of Terror (1793–1794). How does the Terror complicate the claim that the French Revolution was guided by Enlightenment ideals of liberty?

C. Explain how Napoleon Bonaparte was simultaneously the heir and the betrayer of the French Revolution. Use at least TWO specific examples in your response.
