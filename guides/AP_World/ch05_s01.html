Study Guide: AP World History — Topic 5.1: The Enlightenment

Key Concepts

  • Natural rights and popular sovereignty: Locke argued that rights to life, liberty, and property belonged to all humans by nature, preceding and constraining any government. This idea relocated sovereignty from kings to the people and justified revolution when rulers violated those rights.
  • The social contract: Locke and Rousseau both held that legitimate government rested on the consent of the governed — a voluntary arrangement that the people could revoke if rulers became tyrannical. This principle demolished the foundation of divine-right monarchy.
  • Separation of powers: Montesquieu's comparative analysis of governments led him to advocate dividing authority among legislative, executive, and judicial branches, preventing dangerous concentrations of power. The U.S. Constitution directly adopted this model.
  • Limits of Enlightenment universalism: Most male philosophes applied "universal" principles selectively, excluding women, enslaved people, and colonial subjects. Wollstonecraft exposed this contradiction through Enlightenment logic itself; Haitian revolutionaries did so through armed struggle.
  • Enlightenment as Atlantic vocabulary: The movement's political language — rights, liberty, social contract — was adopted by revolutionary actors across the Atlantic world, from colonial Philadelphia to Paris to Saint-Domingue, demonstrating its genuine transnational reach.
  • Scientific Revolution as precursor: The Enlightenment grew from the Scientific Revolution's demonstration that reason and observation could uncover universal laws. If Newton explained planetary motion, Enlightenment thinkers asked whether reason could identify principles of good governance.
  • Free-market economics as Enlightenment application: Adam Smith applied Enlightenment rationalism to economics, arguing that free competition rather than mercantilist state control would optimize resource allocation — providing the intellectual foundation for industrial capitalism.

Vocabulary Review

  • Enlightenment (siècle des Lumières): The intellectual movement (c. 1650–1800) arguing that human reason, not religion or tradition, was the proper basis for understanding nature and organizing society and government.
  • Natural rights: Rights inherent to all humans by virtue of their humanity, not granted by rulers. Locke specified life, liberty, and property as the core inalienable natural rights.
  • Social contract: The philosophical framework holding that legitimate government rests on the consent of the governed; if government violates the contract, the people may alter or abolish it.
  • Philosophes: French Enlightenment writers — Voltaire, Diderot, d'Alembert, Montesquieu — who popularized Enlightenment ideas through satire, encyclopedias, and comparative analysis.
  • Encyclopédie: The massive collaborative work (1751–1772) edited by Diderot and d'Alembert, systematizing Enlightenment knowledge across all fields for a wide literate audience.
  • Separation of powers: Montesquieu's principle dividing governmental authority among legislative, executive, and judicial branches to prevent tyrannical concentration.
  • General will: Rousseau's concept of the collective interest of all citizens in a community, which legitimate government must serve rather than the private interests of individuals or factions.
  • Laissez-faire: The economic doctrine, associated with Adam Smith, that free markets should operate with minimal government interference, with competition and self-interest guiding efficient resource allocation.
  • Glorious Revolution (1688–1689): The English political crisis that removed James II and established parliamentary supremacy; the context in which Locke wrote his Two Treatises.
  • Deism: The Enlightenment-era belief that God created the universe and its laws but did not intervene thereafter — compatible with scientific inquiry and independent of revealed religion.
  • Invisible hand: Adam Smith's metaphor for the self-regulating mechanism of free markets, whereby individual self-interest in competitive markets produces socially efficient outcomes without government direction.

Key Figures and Events

  • John Locke (1632–1704): Author of Two Treatises of Government; argued natural rights constrained government and justified resistance to tyranny. The most influential political framework for Atlantic revolutions.
  • Voltaire (1694–1778): French philosophe who used satire and letters to attack religious superstition, judicial torture, and clerical privilege, making Enlightenment critique socially engaged.
  • Montesquieu (1689–1755): Comparative political analyst who advocated separation of powers in The Spirit of the Laws; his framework was adopted in the U.S. Constitution and other revolutionary constitutions.
  • Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778): The Enlightenment's most radical voice; argued property was the root of inequality and legitimate governance required expressing the general will. His Social Contract (1762) was the most politically incendiary Enlightenment text.
  • Mary Wollstonecraft (1759–1797): Applied Enlightenment logic to gender in A Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1792), arguing that equal rationality implied equal rights — a founding text of modern feminism.
  • Adam Smith (1723–1790): Applied Enlightenment rationalism to economics in The Wealth of Nations (1776), arguing that free markets were superior to mercantilist direction.
  • Scientific Revolution: The seventeenth-century revolution in natural philosophy (Newton, Galileo, Descartes) that demonstrated reason and observation could uncover universal laws, inspiring Enlightenment political thought.

Themes

The Enlightenment sits at the intersection of multiple SPICE-T themes. Politically, it constituted the most significant transformation of political theory in the early modern period: by relocating sovereignty from hereditary rulers to the people and grounding legitimacy in consent rather than divine right, Enlightenment thinkers provided both the vocabulary and the justification for revolution. The social contract idea directly underwrote the American Declaration of Independence, the French Declaration of the Rights of Man, and the Haitian rebels' extension of those rights to enslaved people. Culturally and intellectually, the Enlightenment represented a decisive shift toward secular reasoning as the arbiter of social and political questions — embodied in the Encyclopédie's project of freely sharing systematic knowledge. The movement also revealed internal tensions: its universalist language coexisted with the exclusion of women, enslaved people, and colonial subjects, contradictions that would fuel the next century of political conflict.

Economically, Adam Smith's application of Enlightenment principles to markets generated laissez-faire theory that would shape industrial capitalism. Smith's critique of mercantilism provided the intellectual framework that Atlantic commercial interests used to argue for free trade — with enormous consequences for colonial relationships and the organization of global trade in the nineteenth century.

Compare and Contrast

Locke and Rousseau both argued for social contract theory but diverged significantly in their implications. Locke accepted existing property arrangements as natural and argued for government as the protector of individual property rights — a conservative interpretation that suited propertied colonial elites like American revolutionaries. Rousseau, by contrast, treated private property as the origin of social inequality and the corruption of natural human goodness, arguing that the general will of the entire community must supersede individual property interests. This made Rousseau's ideas more radical and more double-edged: French revolutionaries' Jacobins drew on Rousseau to justify collective action against aristocratic property, while Wollstonecraft and Haitian rebels could draw on both thinkers' universalist premises to argue that the exclusions in male-authored Enlightenment theory were self-contradictions rather than principled positions.

Answer all three parts of the following SAQ:

A. Identify ONE specific Enlightenment concept (from any thinker discussed in this section) and explain how that concept challenged the political legitimacy of absolute monarchy.

B. Explain why Mary Wollstonecraft's A Vindication of the Rights of Woman represented a challenge from within Enlightenment thought rather than a rejection of it.
