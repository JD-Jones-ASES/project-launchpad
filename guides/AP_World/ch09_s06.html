Study Guide: Cultural Syncretism and Anti-Globalization Resistance (AP World History — Topic 9.6)

Key Concepts

  • Syncretism vs. homogenization: Globalization did not produce cultural uniformity. Instead, it generated simultaneous intensification of cultural exchange (syncretism) and cultural resistance. K-pop, Brazilian funk, and Bollywood are examples of creative hybrid production; French film quotas, Islamic revivalism, and economic nationalism are examples of resistance.
  • Métissage, not McDonaldization: The French concept of métissage (mixture) better describes contemporary global culture than the 'McDonaldization' thesis. Cultural flows are multi-directional: Nigerian Nollywood, Korean K-drama, Mexican telenovelas, Chinese TikTok, and Brazilian funk all reshaped global culture from non-Western origins.
  • Religious revivalism as the most globally consequential form of counter-globalization: Across the Muslim world, sub-Saharan Africa, India, Myanmar, and Sri Lanka, religious and civilizational movements grew dramatically from the 1970s through the 2000s — using modern media and political institutions to articulate critiques of Western secular modernity.
  • Economic nationalism in wealthy nations: Deindustrialization from trade integration (especially China's WTO accession) devastated manufacturing workers in the U.S., UK, and France — workers who benefited as consumers but suffered as workers. Political entrepreneurs channeled this into Brexit, Trump's America First, and similar movements in the 2010s.
  • The unity beneath diversity of globalization critics: Critics came from radically different positions (labor, environment, religious revivalism, economic nationalism, Global South farmers) but shared the conviction that globalization served a small transnational elite at the expense of most people.
  • Syncretism has historical precedent: Cultural blending — Swahili culture, Latin American Catholicism, Jazz — long predates contemporary globalization. What changed is the speed and scale, as global media dramatically accelerated cultural contact.

Vocabulary Review

  • cultural syncretism: The creative blending of elements from two or more distinct cultural traditions to produce new hybrid forms; does not imply either tradition's disappearance.
  • McDonaldization: The argument (and fear) that globalization would make world culture uniformly American, displacing local traditions; the text argues the evidence is 'real but partial.'
  • métissage: French concept meaning 'mixture'; invoked as a more accurate description of global culture than McDonaldization — emphasizing hybridity over homogenization.
  • Islamic revivalism: The broad growth of Islamic religious and political movements from the 1970s onward, taking democratic (Turkey's AKP), theocratic (Iran), and violent (al-Qaeda) forms, but sharing rejection of Westernization as the path to modernity.
  • velayat-e faqih: 'Guardianship of the jurist' — the Khomeinist doctrine governing Iran's Islamic Republic, holding that senior clergy hold political authority.
  • Hindutva: The ideology that India is essentially a Hindu civilization; grew from 1920s fringe to mainstream under the BJP (Bharatiya Janata Party) and Narendra Modi in the 2010s.
  • economic nationalism: Political movements prioritizing national economic interests — protecting manufacturing, restricting immigration, challenging trade agreements — that grew in wealthy nations in the 2010s as reactions to deindustrialization.
  • deindustrialization: The long-term decline of manufacturing employment in wealthy nations, accelerated by trade integration and China's WTO accession; the structural basis for 2010s economic-nationalist politics.

Key Figures and Events

  • K-pop: Korean popular music that borrowed from American hip-hop while projecting Korean identity and achieving global audiences — the source's primary example of culturally syncretic production.
  • Nollywood (Nigeria): The world's second-largest film industry by output; evidence that major global cultural production can emerge from sub-Saharan Africa.
  • Iranian Revolution (1979): Overthrew a U.S.-backed monarchy; established an Islamic Republic under velayat-e faqih; demonstrated that an alternative path to modernity was possible outside Western liberal frameworks.
  • BJP / Narendra Modi (2014, 2019): Electoral victories of India's Hindu nationalist party rooted in Hindutva ideology — the culmination of a decades-long civilizational-nationalist movement.
  • Brexit vote (2016): UK referendum vote to leave the EU; drew on sovereignty critiques and working-class economic displacement; part of the wave of 2010s economic nationalism.
  • Trump's America First: U.S. political movement channeling manufacturing-community discontent into protectionist and nationalist politics; parallel to Brexit and France's National Rally.

Themes

The dominant SPICE-T theme is Cultural (C) — the entire section analyzes how cultures respond to intensified global contact: through creative hybridization (syncretism) or defensive assertion (revivalism, nationalism). The section demonstrates that the Cultural theme is not passive — cultures are active agents, both absorbing and resisting outside influences in ways that produce entirely new forms (K-pop, Brazilian funk) or reassert older identities in modern forms (Hindutva using social media, Islamic revivalism using satellite television).

The Political and Economic themes are equally present: economic nationalism connects the macro-economic disruption of deindustrialization to the political movements that channeled it; religious and civilizational nationalisms challenge the political authority of secular and Western-dominated international institutions. What unites these disparate responses is a shared conviction about who globalization serves — making anti-globalization as much a Political and Economic critique as a Cultural one.

Compare and Contrast

Compare religious revivalism (Islamic revivalism, Hindutva) with economic nationalism (Brexit, Trump, National Rally) as two forms of anti-globalization resistance. Both grew in the same 2000s–2010s window and drew on critiques of globalization as a project of unaccountable elites. But they differed in location, ideology, and target: religious revivalism primarily emerged in non-Western societies as a cultural and civilizational assertion against Western secular hegemony; economic nationalism primarily emerged in wealthy Western nations as a working-class economic response to deindustrialization. Religious revivalists used modern media to resist secular modernity's values; economic nationalists used democratic electoral politics to resist trade policies. Both drew on authentic grievances and both used the institutions of the globalized world (media, politics) to challenge it.

Practice Prompt

Short-Answer Question (SAQ):

(A) Briefly explain the concept of cultural syncretism, using ONE specific contemporary example from outside the United States or Western Europe as evidence.

(B) Briefly explain ONE way in which religious revivalism in the late twentieth century can be understood as a form of resistance to cultural globalization, using a specific example from outside the United States.

(C) Briefly explain how the economic disruption caused by trade integration in the early twenty-first century contributed to the rise of economic nationalist political movements in wealthy industrialized nations.
