Study Guide: AP World History — Topic 5.4: The Haitian Revolution

Key Concepts

  • The most radical Atlantic Revolution: The Haitian Revolution was the only one in which enslaved people themselves seized power, abolished slavery, and founded an independent nation — taking Enlightenment universalism at its literal word where the American and French revolutions had hedged or equivocated.
  • Saint-Domingue's explosive social structure: The colony's extraordinary wealth (40% of Europe's sugar, more than half its coffee) rested on three overlapping tensions: grand/petit blancs vs. gens de couleur over political rights; white colonists vs. free people of color; and the enslaved majority (90% of the population) against the entire free population.
  • Staged radicalization: The revolution moved through distinct phases — the gens de couleur uprising (1790), the enslaved Africans' uprising (1791), Toussaint's consolidation and military campaigns (1791–1801), Napoleon's attempt at reconquest (1801–1803), and final independence (1804) — each more radical than the last.
  • Toussaint L'Ouverture's strategic genius: His ability to negotiate shifting alliances, build a disciplined army, repel British invasion, and unify competing factions under his authority was what made independence possible. His arrest by Napoleon radicalized rather than ended the revolution.
  • Terror and the slave-owning world: Haiti's success was more threatening to Atlantic slaveholding societies than any other revolution because it proved enslaved people were capable of sustained, organized revolutionary action — and that slavery could be overthrown from below.
  • Atlantic consequences: The Haitian Revolution triggered the Louisiana Purchase (1803), inspired subsequent slave resistance (German Coast Uprising, 1811), and made Haiti a symbol of freedom for the enslaved and terror for slaveholders across the Americas.

Vocabulary Review

  • Saint-Domingue: The French colony on the western third of Hispaniola; the Atlantic world's most productive colony, later renamed Haiti after independence.
  • grand blancs / petit blancs: Wealthy white planters and small white merchants/artisans in Saint-Domingue; at the top of the racial hierarchy but divided by class.
  • gens de couleur: Free people of color — free Black and mixed-race individuals who occupied a legally ambiguous middle position; some were property-owners, yet they faced racial humiliation laws restricting political participation.
  • Bois Caïman ceremony: The August 14, 1791 Vodou gathering that served as the founding moment of the Haitian Revolution, days before the northern province erupted in uprising.
  • Toussaint L'Ouverture (c. 1743–1803): The revolution's most brilliant military-political leader; built the disciplined army that made independence possible; arrested under a flag of truce by Napoleon and died in French captivity.
  • Jean-Jacques Dessalines (1758–1806): Led the final phase of the revolution after Toussaint's arrest; declared Haitian independence January 1, 1804.
  • Louisiana Purchase (1803): Napoleon's sale of Louisiana to the United States — a direct consequence of the Haitian Revolution making a Caribbean-centered French empire impossible.
  • manumission: The formal act of freeing an enslaved person. Some gens de couleur in Saint-Domingue were free by manumission or by birth to a free mother.

Key Figures and Events

  • Vincent Ogé (d. 1791): Led the 1790 uprising of free people of color demanding political rights; captured, tortured, and executed — his fate demonstrated that the colonial order would not yield rights voluntarily.
  • Bois Caïman ceremony (August 14, 1791): The founding moment of the revolution; enslaved leaders' gathering in a Vodou ceremony before the August 22–23 uprising.
  • Toussaint L'Ouverture (c. 1743–1803): Most significant military and political leader; repelled British invasion; negotiated alliances; promulgated the 1801 constitution; died in French captivity.
  • Napoleon's reversal of abolition (1802): Reinstated slavery in French Caribbean colonies; directly precipitated the revolution's final phase by eliminating any possibility of compromise.
  • Jean-Jacques Dessalines and Henri Christophe: Led Haitian forces to final victory after Toussaint's arrest; defeated a French army decimated by yellow fever as much as by battle.
  • Haitian independence (January 1, 1804): Dessalines declared the first free Black republic; named it "Haiti" (the indigenous Taino name), deliberately rejecting the French colonial name.

Themes

The Haitian Revolution is the most important test case for the SPICE-T theme of Social transformation in the Atlantic revolutionary era. Where the American and French revolutions preserved or only partially dismantled existing social hierarchies, the Haitian Revolution abolished the foundational hierarchy of chattel slavery itself — replacing a society built entirely on enslaved labor with a free republic governed by formerly enslaved people. This was the most radical social transformation of the Atlantic world in this era.

Politically, the revolution demonstrated something that slaveholding societies desperately needed to deny: that enslaved people were political agents capable of organized, sustained, militarily successful revolutionary action. The existence of the Haitian republic was therefore not merely a political fact but an existential ideological challenge to every slave society in the Americas. The United States' refusal to recognize Haiti diplomatically until 1862 was not diplomatic oversight but deliberate suppression of a dangerous precedent.

Compare and Contrast

Place the American, French, and Haitian revolutions side by side on the question of slavery. The American Revolution explicitly protected chattel slavery — Three-Fifths Compromise, slave trade protected until 1808, fugitive slave laws — in its constitutional settlement. The French Revolution's National Convention abolished slavery in 1794 but Napoleon reinstated it in 1802, demonstrating that metropolitan French politics could override colonial freedom. Only the Haitian Revolution achieved permanent, unconditional abolition, and only there were the formerly enslaved themselves the political agents of that change. The comparison demonstrates that "universal rights" was not a settled principle in the Atlantic revolutionary era but a contested claim whose meaning was determined by political struggle — and that the Haitian Revolution pushed that meaning furthest.

Practice Prompt

Answer all three parts of the following SAQ:

A. Explain why the Haitian Revolution is described as the most radical of the Atlantic Revolutions. What specific outcome made it more radical than the American or French revolutions?

B. Explain how Napoleon Bonaparte's policies toward Saint-Domingue contributed to both the Haitian Revolution's final phase and the Louisiana Purchase.

C. Explain why the Haitian Revolution terrified slave-owning societies across the Atlantic world. What did Haiti's success prove that those societies had a stake in denying?
