Study Guide: AP World History — Topic 5.7: Global Spread and Competing Ideologies

Key Concepts

  • Industrialization spread through multiple pathways: Belgium, France, the German states, the United States, Japan, and Russia all industrialized by 1900, but through distinct mechanisms — market-driven (U.S.), state-directed (Germany, Japan, Russia), or combinations. Each path was shaped by local conditions and position in the global economy. There was no single "British model" simply replicated.
  • The three competing ideologies as responses to the same cause: Classical liberalism, socialism, and Marxist communism were all responses to industrial capitalism's disruption of traditional social arrangements. Their conflict defined political history from the Russian Revolution (1917) to the Cold War (1991).
  • Classical liberalism's internal tension: The ideology of the industrial bourgeoisie combined economic liberalism (free markets, minimal government, opposition to labor regulations) with political liberalism (civil liberties, constitutional government). This tension — supporting rights while opposing the protections that would make those rights meaningful for workers — became a defining fault line of modern politics.
  • Marx and Engels' historical materialism: "Scientific socialism" argued that history was driven by class struggle over productive resources; that capitalism was historically specific with internal contradictions (bourgeoisie/proletariat exploitation); and that those contradictions would inevitably produce revolution. The Manifesto's opening — "A spectre is haunting Europe" — reflected communism's perceived threat to established elites.
  • Japan as defensive industrialization: The Meiji government industrialized explicitly to resist Western imperialism that had forced unequal treaties on China and Japan. Japan's 1905 defeat of Russia demonstrated that state-directed industrialization could produce the military-industrial capacity to resist European domination — the first Asian nation to defeat a major European power.
  • Russia as unstable late industrializer: Russian industrialization (1890s) combined a small industrial working class beside an enormous peasant majority, autocratic government with limited capacity to manage social conflict, and dependence on French and Belgian capital. These conditions produced the social tensions that exploded in 1905 and 1917.

Vocabulary Review

  • Bourgeoisie: In Marxist analysis, the class owning the means of production; more broadly, the urban middle and upper-middle class whose political ideology was classical liberalism.
  • Classical liberalism: The ideology of free markets, private property, and minimal government interference, associated with Adam Smith, Ricardo, and Mill; the industrial bourgeoisie's political program.
  • Socialism: The ideology that means of production should be collectively owned to distribute industrial productivity's benefits equitably; the industrial working class's ideological response to capitalism.
  • Historical materialism (scientific socialism): Marx and Engels' theory that history is driven by class struggle over productive resources, and that capitalism's contradictions would inevitably produce revolution.
  • Communist Manifesto (1848): Marx and Engels' pamphlet arguing for proletarian revolution against the bourgeoisie; published during the Revolutions of 1848.
  • Utopian socialists: Robert Owen, Charles Fourier, Henri de Saint-Simon — early socialists who experimented with cooperative alternatives to capitalism rather than developing systematic revolutionary theory.
  • Zaibatsu: Large Japanese industrial conglomerates emerging from the privatization of Meiji government-built model factories.
  • Second industrial revolution: The science-based industrialization of chemicals, electrical engineering, and steel associated with Germany in the later nineteenth century.
  • Laissez-faire: The economic doctrine of minimal government intervention in markets; the practical policy preference of classical liberalism.

Key Figures and Events

  • Karl Marx (1818–1883) and Friedrich Engels (1820–1895): Co-authors of the Communist Manifesto (1848) and founders of scientific socialism / historical materialism; most systematic and influential socialist theorists.
  • Robert Owen, Charles Fourier, Henri de Saint-Simon: Utopian socialists who experimented with cooperative alternatives to capitalism before Marx systematized socialist theory.
  • Meiji Restoration (1868): Japan's political revolution that launched state-directed industrialization to resist Western imperialism; within a generation produced the capacity to defeat Russia (1905).
  • Germany after unification (1871): State-directed industrialization produced the "second industrial revolution" in chemicals and electrical engineering; Social Democratic Party became Europe's largest socialist party by 1914.
  • Russia in the 1890s: Late industrialization under autocratic tsarist rule produced structural social tensions that exploded in revolution (1905, 1917).
  • Revolutions of 1848: The year of European revolutionary upheaval across France, Prussia, Austria-Hungary, and Italy — the context in which Marx and Engels published the Communist Manifesto.

Themes

This section crystallizes the SPICE-T theme of Political and Economic ideology as a global response to industrialization. The three competing ideologies — classical liberalism, socialism, Marxist communism — were not abstract philosophical positions but programs shaped by class interest and material conditions. Classical liberalism served the industrial bourgeoisie: free markets maximized capital accumulation. Socialism served the industrial working class: collective ownership would distribute industrial productivity's benefits. Marxist communism provided a systematic theory — rooted in Enlightenment-era concepts of historical progress and Ricardo's economics — predicting capitalism's inevitable collapse.

Economically, the global spread of industrialization restructured the world into industrializing cores and raw-material-supplying peripheries. The mechanisms varied: market capitalism drove U.S. industrialization; state direction drove Germany, Japan, and Russia. But everywhere industrialization went, it generated the same social conflict between bourgeoisie and proletariat — making local variation in industrial path less important than the shared ideological challenge of managing that conflict.

The Technology theme connects sections 5.6 and 5.7: Britain's technological innovations (steam, textiles) were transmitted globally, adapted to different economic contexts (German science-based industry, Japanese state-managed factories), and generated both industrial capacity and ideological responses. Technology alone did not determine who industrialized or when — the social and political framework mattered as much as the machines.

Compare and Contrast

Compare the Japanese and Russian paths to industrialization. Both were late industrializers responding to external pressures; both used state direction rather than market-driven investment; both depended on foreign expertise and capital. But their outcomes diverged dramatically. Japan's Meiji government had the institutional capacity and social cohesion to direct industrialization effectively, absorbing foreign models selectively and generating both industrial output and military power. Russia's industrialization, by contrast, operated under an autocratic tsarist government with limited administrative capacity to manage social conflicts, producing a small industrial working class alongside an enormous peasant majority in explosive combination. Japan's industrialization produced national power (defeated Russia in 1905); Russia's produced revolution (1905, 1917). The comparison demonstrates that state capacity and social structure shaped industrialization's political outcomes as much as the economic inputs.

Practice Prompt

Answer all three parts of the following SAQ:

A. Explain how Japan's Meiji industrialization program differed from Britain's path to industrialization in both its motivations and its methods.

B. Based on the excerpt from The Communist Manifesto, explain how Marx and Engels placed capitalism within a broader theory of historical development. What is the relationship between capitalism and earlier systems of exploitation in their argument?

C. Explain the internal tension within classical liberalism between its economic and political principles. How does this tension illuminate the competing interests of the industrial bourgeoisie and the industrial proletariat?
