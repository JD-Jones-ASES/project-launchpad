Study Guide: World War I — Causes (MAIN)

AP World History — Topic 7.2

Key Concepts

  • MAIN Framework: Historians organize the structural causes of World War I under four headings — Militarism, Alliances, Imperialism, and Nationalism. Each reinforced the others, creating a system so tightly wound that a single assassination could detonate a world war. On the AP exam, demonstrating how these causes interacted with each other earns higher marks than treating them as independent factors.

  • The Cult of the Offensive: Pre-1914 militarism was not only an arms race; it produced military planning cultures that glorified offensive action and removed diplomatic "pause options." The Schlieffen Plan required Germany to proceed to full mobilization once conflict began, meaning military timetables drove the July 1914 crisis rather than diplomatic deliberation.

  • Alliance System as Escalation Machine: The interlocking alliances (Triple Alliance: Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy; Triple Entente: France, Russia, Britain) rewarded commitment and punished hesitation. Each power that honored its alliance automatically triggered a counter-response, transforming an Austro-Serbian bilateral dispute into a European — and then global — war within six weeks.

  • WWI's Global Character: This is the section's most anti-Eurocentric argument. WWI was not a European war with colonial auxiliaries; it was a world war in which imperial systems mobilized the populations of every inhabited continent — 1.5+ million Indian troops, ~600,000 West African soldiers, 140,000 Chinese laborers, and Dominion troops from Australia, New Zealand, Canada, and South Africa.

  • Nationalism's Destabilizing Force: Nationalism threatened every multi-ethnic empire in early-twentieth-century Europe. In Austria-Hungary, the Sarajevo assassination was not an isolated act but the culmination of years of South Slavic nationalist agitation. In Germany, pan-German nationalism (Einkreisung, the demand for a "place in the sun") intensified the sense that war was necessary and should be fought sooner rather than later.

  • Imperialism as Both Context and Cause: Imperial competition created prewar flashpoints (Moroccan Crises, Balkan Wars) and a general climate of great-power rivalry. It also determined the war's global character: colonial empires meant the war was immediately fought in Africa, Asia, and across multiple oceans.

Vocabulary Review

  • Schlieffen Plan: Germany's strategic plan requiring a rapid offensive through neutral Belgium to defeat France before turning east against Russia — with no pause option once mobilization began.

  • Triple Alliance: The mutual defense pact among Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Italy (Italy ultimately switched sides in 1915).

  • Triple Entente: The looser arrangement of mutual support among France, Russia, and Britain.

  • July Crisis: The six-week escalation from the 28 June 1914 assassination of Franz Ferdinand to European-wide war on 4 August 1914, driven by alliance commitments.

  • Militarism: The glorification of military power and belief in warfare as a legitimate tool of national policy; produced the prewar arms race and offensive-oriented military planning cultures.

  • Self-determination: The principle that peoples sharing a common culture and identity have the right to govern themselves; articulated by Wilson in 1918 but applied selectively, primarily to white Europeans.

  • Einkreisung: The German nationalist sense of "encirclement" by France and Russia, used to argue that war was both inevitable and better fought sooner rather than later.

  • Tirailleurs sénégalais: West African soldiers recruited by France — approximately 600,000 — who fought in France and across Africa, representing one of the largest non-European contributions to WWI.

  • Chinese Labour Corps: 140,000 Chinese laborers recruited from Shandong province to work behind Allied lines on the Western Front, kept segregated and largely invisible in Allied records.

  • Moroccan Crises (1905–1906, 1911): Two Franco-German confrontations over Morocco that created flashpoints of imperial rivalry contributing to the prewar atmosphere of great-power competition.

  • Pan-German nationalism: The movement arguing that Germany deserved colonial possessions and international prestige commensurate with its industrial power — a "place in the sun" denied by Britain and France.

Key Figures and Events

  • Gavrilo Princip: Bosnian Serb nationalist whose assassination of Franz Ferdinand on 28 June 1914 served as the immediate trigger of WWI — representing nationalism's culminating act of opposition to dynastic empire.
  • Franz Ferdinand: Heir to the Austro-Hungarian throne; his assassination in Sarajevo triggered the July Crisis.
  • HMS Dreadnought (1906): British battleship that rendered all existing battleships obsolete and launched a new round of Anglo-German naval competition.
  • Balkan Wars (1912–1913): Reshuffled Balkan territory, emboldened Serbia, and humiliated Austria-Hungary — raising the temperature of the crisis that would explode in 1914.
  • East Africa Campaign: A WWI theater in modern Tanzania, Mozambique, and Zambia where German and British forces fought across African territory, killing an estimated 200,000 African civilians and soldiers.
  • Indian troops in WWI: Over 1.5 million Indian soldiers served in British armies across multiple theaters, representing the single largest non-European contribution to Allied forces.

Themes

Political: The alliance system represents a Political theme case study in how formal international institutions can constrain state behavior. Rather than allowing Austria-Hungary and Serbia to resolve their dispute bilaterally, the alliance system created automatic escalation: each power's decision to honor its commitments triggered counter-responses. The lesson — that rigid multilateral commitments can transform local conflicts into global catastrophes — was debated throughout the twentieth century and shaped the design of postwar international security architecture (the UN Security Council's veto mechanism is partly a response to this problem).

Economic + Social: Imperialism as a cause of WWI has both Economic and Social dimensions. Economically, competition for colonial markets and raw materials drove rivalry among industrial great powers. Socially, the mobilization of colonial peoples — Indian soldiers, West African troops, Chinese laborers — for a European war challenged the racial hierarchies that underpinned colonial systems, raising expectations of political recognition that went unmet at Versailles and fueled subsequent anti-colonial movements.

Compare and Contrast

Nationalism as a cause of WWI operated differently in Germany than in Austria-Hungary. In Austria-Hungary, nationalism was a centrifugal force — multiple ethnic groups (Czechs, Slovaks, Croats, Bosniaks, Poles) demanded autonomy or independence from within the empire, and Serbia was seen as an external sponsor of this internal dissolution. In Germany, nationalism was a centripetal and expansionist force — pan-German nationalists argued from a position of industrial strength that Germany was being denied the international prestige it deserved and that war against France and Russia was both necessary and better fought immediately. One was nationalist pressure from below, threatening to tear an empire apart; the other was nationalist pressure from above, driving an industrial state toward aggressive expansion. Both ultimately pointed toward war, but through opposite mechanisms.

Practice Prompt

Short Answer Question (SAQ)

"The structural conditions created by Militarism, Alliances, Imperialism, and Nationalism — not the assassination of Franz Ferdinand — were the real causes of World War I."

(A) Identify and briefly describe ONE specific way in which any two of the four MAIN causes interacted with each other to make the escalation of the July Crisis more likely.

(B) Explain ONE piece of evidence that World War I was a genuinely global war from its earliest stages, and explain what this evidence reveals about the relationship between European great powers and their colonial empires.

(C) Evaluate the argument that the alliance system was the single most important cause of WWI by explaining both what the argument gets right AND one important limitation or caveat.
