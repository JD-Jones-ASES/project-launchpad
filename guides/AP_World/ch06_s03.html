Study Guide: State Expansion in Asia

AP World History — Unit 6, Section 3

Key Concepts

  • Asia's imperialism took varied forms: India came under direct British Crown administration (1858); China experienced semi-colonialism through unequal treaties without formal annexation; France assembled Indochina by military conquest; Russia expanded across Central Asia. The varied outcomes reflect differences in state capacity, geography, and available responses.
  • Industrial military asymmetry was the common thread: Steam gunboats, breech-loading rifles, and artillery gave European and American forces decisive advantages across the continent. Diplomatic resistance alone could not overcome this gap — a truth China, India, and Vietnam all experienced.
  • Colonial economics caused structural harm: Britain's dismantling of India's textile industry (25% to 2% of world manufacturing, 1750–1900) and cash-crop revenue systems that stripped farmers of food security demonstrate that colonial economic policy caused harm independent of — and in addition to — military conquest.
  • The ideology-practice gap recurs: Queen Victoria's 1858 proclamation promised equal protection and religious freedom; the Raj continued to export grain during famines killing millions. The gap between humanitarian language and exploitative practice, seen in the Congo, appears here too.
  • Late Victorian famines as structural violence: Mike Davis's concept of "late Victorian holocausts" captures how colonial economic policies — not monsoon failure alone — killed millions by eliminating farmers' subsistence buffers and maintaining grain exports during famine.
  • Administrative models were strategic choices: The British Raj combined direct rule (presidencies) and indirect rule (562 princely states) as cost-calculation, not as a uniform policy. Both models extracted resources and constrained sovereignty; the difference was administrative cost and degree of cultural disruption.
  • French Indochina built the conditions for Ho Chi Minh: Corvée labor, abolition of Confucian examinations, Catholic missionary support, and linguistic imposition created the structural grievances that fueled continuous armed resistance (1885, 1887, 1888+) and ultimately the twentieth-century independence movement.

Vocabulary Review

  • British Raj: Direct British Crown rule over India, 1858–1947; governed through a Viceroy and the Indian Civil Service, whose senior positions were almost exclusively British.
  • British East India Company (EIC): Royal-chartered trading company (1600–1858) that acquired governing, taxing, and military functions across India before being abolished after the 1857 Rebellion.
  • paramountcy: System requiring nominally independent Indian princes to defer to Britain on external matters; fiction of sovereignty masking genuine British control.
  • direct rule: Colonial administration replacing existing political structures with European administrators and law codes (used in Bengal, Bombay, and Madras presidencies).
  • indirect rule: Maintaining existing political structures (Indian princes, Malay sultans) while directing policy through European "advisors." Cheaper and less culturally disruptive than direct rule.
  • deindustrialization: The collapse of India's textile manufacturing under deliberate British trade policy; India fell from ~25% to ~2% of world manufacturing between 1750 and 1900.
  • ryotwari / zamindari: Colonial Indian revenue systems imposing cash taxation on subsistence farmers, forcing cash-crop production and eliminating food-security buffers.
  • late Victorian holocausts: Mike Davis's term for famines that killed millions across colonial Asia and Africa — produced not by natural disaster alone but by colonial economic structures that made subsistence farmers vulnerable.
  • corvée: Forced, unpaid labor imposed on colonial subjects; used in French Indochina for infrastructure construction.
  • unequal treaties: Agreements imposed on China after military defeats in the Opium Wars, stripping sovereignty over port cities, courts (extraterritoriality), and tariff policy without Chinese consent.
  • extraterritoriality: The right of foreign nationals in China to be tried in their own nations' consular courts rather than Chinese courts — one of the unequal treaty provisions that stripped Chinese judicial sovereignty.
  • Kaiser-i-Hind: "Empress of India" — the additional title granted to Queen Victoria in 1877 by the Royal Titles Act, formalizing Britain's self-understanding of India as an imperial rather than commercial possession.

Key Figures and Events

  • Queen Victoria (Empress of India, 1877–1901): Never visited India but presided over the Raj's consolidation; proclaimed Empress at the Delhi Durbar (1877); issued promises of equal protection that the colonial administration honored selectively.
  • Benjamin Disraeli: British Prime Minister who secured the Royal Titles Act (1876), motivated partly by his belief that imperial grandeur strengthened Conservative politics domestically.
  • Mike Davis: Historian whose concept of "late Victorian holocausts" reframes colonial-era famines as products of policy-induced structural vulnerability, not purely natural disaster.
  • Great Famine of 1876–79: Killed an estimated 5.5 million people in southern India while the Raj continued grain exports — the definitive evidence for Davis's structural-violence argument.
  • Delhi Durbar (1877, 1903, 1911): Ceremonial performances of British imperial sovereignty directed at both Indian elites and European audiences, staging submission and power.
  • Opium Wars (1839–42, 1856–60): British military victories over China that resulted in unequal treaties — cession of Hong Kong, opening of treaty ports, extraterritoriality, and legally enforced opium imports.
  • Union of Indochina (1887): The administrative consolidation of French colonial Vietnam, Cambodia, and Tonkin/Annam, completing France's conquest of the region begun with the seizure of Saigon in 1859.

Themes

Economic (E) consequences of colonialism in Asia are the section's central concern. The Raj's deindustrialization of India — from 25% to 2% of world manufacturing in 150 years — represents one of the most dramatic economic transformations in modern history. This was not a natural market process but a deliberate policy outcome: import flooding and export taxation dismantled an existing competitive industry. The cash-crop revenue systems compounded this by reorganizing agriculture around export production rather than subsistence, eliminating farmers' ability to survive climatic shocks. The result was catastrophic famine — and continued grain export during those famines — that killed millions. This is the colonial economic logic at its most starkly documented.

Political (P) themes appear in the contrast between direct and indirect rule as administrative strategies. Both extracted resources and constrained sovereignty; the choice between them reflected British cost-benefit calculations, not a spectrum from benign to malevolent governance. Cultural (C) imposition — the Delhi Durbar's performance of submission, French abolition of Confucian examinations, Catholic missions in Indochina — operated alongside economic extraction as parallel dimensions of colonial control. Social (S) disruption appears in the famines' mass mortality, the displacement of Indian weavers, and the corvée labor systems. All five SPICE-T dimensions are active in this section, and they interconnect: economic restructuring enabled Cultural imposition, Political control enabled economic extraction, and Technology (T) — steam gunboats, military asymmetry — made all of it possible.

Compare and Contrast

British colonial India and French Indochina share a common structure: both combined economic extraction (deindustrialization/cash crops in India; corvée labor/plantation agriculture in Indochina) with cultural imposition (English-medium education, racial hierarchy in India; French-medium education, abolition of Confucian examinations in Indochina) and generated sustained local resistance. Both invoked the civilizing mission to justify their presence. The key difference was administrative: Britain combined direct and indirect rule across a vast subcontinent; France consolidated more uniform direct administration in a smaller territory. The outcomes for resistance also differed in timing: India's major rebellion came early (1857), while Indochina's continuous resistance built across decades toward the twentieth-century independence movement. Both cases illustrate that the specifics of administrative form mattered less than the underlying structure of extraction and cultural erasure in shaping the intensity and persistence of anti-colonial resistance.

Practice Prompt

(AP Short-Answer Question Format)

"India had been one of the world's largest textile producers before British colonization. Whereas India had accounted for roughly 25 percent of world manufacturing in 1750, it accounted for roughly 2 percent by 1900."

Using the passage above and your knowledge of world history:

(A) Briefly explain ONE specific British colonial policy that contributed to India's deindustrialization between 1750 and 1900.

(B) Briefly explain ONE way in which the Raj's agricultural revenue systems contributed to the catastrophic famines of the late nineteenth century.

(C) Briefly explain ONE similarity between British economic policy in India and French colonial policy in Indochina in the nineteenth century.
