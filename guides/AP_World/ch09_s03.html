Study Guide: Global Institutions and Economic Integration (AP World History — Topic 9.3)

Key Concepts

  • Deliberate design of post-1945 globalization: Economic integration after 1945 was not a natural process — it was the result of deliberate policy choices made at Bretton Woods (1944) and through the GATT/WTO framework. The institutions that governed globalization were designed primarily by and for wealthy nations, and their rules reflected those interests.
  • The Bretton Woods system and its evolution: The 1944 Bretton Woods conference created the IMF (short-term stabilization lending), World Bank (reconstruction and development finance), and a dollar-gold fixed-exchange system. After 1971, the IMF evolved toward structural adjustment programs that critics argued dismantled social safety nets while serving Western creditors.
  • Who benefits from trade integration: Countries that integrated into global manufacturing exports (South Korea, Taiwan, China) generally prospered; those locked into raw-material exports faced volatile commodity prices and deteriorating terms of trade. This divergence reveals that how a country participates in globalization, not just whether it participates, determines outcomes.
  • The UN's structural limitations: The Security Council's five permanent vetoes were designed to reflect 1945 power realities, permanently excluding Africa, Latin America, and South Asia from top-tier governance — a design critics argue has never been adequately reformed.
  • Regional integration: possibilities and tensions: The EU raised living standards, ended warfare among core members, and created a major economic pole — but the Greek austerity crisis revealed that integration can transfer democratic sovereignty upward to institutions accountable to financial markets rather than citizens.
  • Backlash and economic nationalism: China's WTO integration lifted hundreds of millions out of poverty in China while displacing manufacturing in wealthy nations — generating the political backlash that fueled Brexit, Trump, and economic-nationalist movements in the 2010s.

Vocabulary Review

  • Bretton Woods system: The 1944 international monetary architecture — currencies pegged to the dollar, dollar to gold — creating exchange stability. Collapsed in 1971 when Nixon suspended dollar–gold convertibility.
  • structural adjustment: IMF/World Bank loan conditions (1980s–1990s) requiring privatization, spending cuts, trade liberalization, and deregulation of borrowing nations' economies; criticized for harming social services and sovereign autonomy.
  • GATT: General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (1947); multilateral framework for reducing trade barriers; replaced by the WTO in 1995.
  • WTO (World Trade Organization): Replaced GATT in 1995; added formal dispute-resolution authority; 164 members by 2024 representing over 98% of world trade.
  • terms of trade: The ratio of a country's export prices to its import prices; raw-material exporters historically face deteriorating terms of trade relative to manufactured-goods exporters.
  • supranational governance: Governance by institutions that transcend and have authority over individual nation-states — exemplified by the EU's parliament, court, and executive commission.
  • eurozone: The subset of EU member states that adopted the euro as a common currency, creating shared monetary policy but divergent fiscal situations exposed by the 2008 financial crisis.
  • Brexit: The UK's 2020 departure from the EU following a 2016 referendum — partly driven by sovereignty critiques and economic-nationalist sentiment.
  • economic nationalism: Political movements prioritizing national economic interests over global integration — exemplified in the 2010s by Brexit, Trump's America First, and similar movements in France and Poland.

Key Figures and Events

  • Bretton Woods Conference (1944): 44 Allied nations designed the post-war monetary system; created the IMF and World Bank; reflected the power of the United States as dominant wartime and post-war economy.
  • Nixon's suspension of dollar–gold convertibility (1971): Collapsed the fixed-exchange system; the IMF survived and evolved toward structural adjustment.
  • China's WTO accession (2001): The most consequential single event in post-1990 trade expansion — exponential manufacturing export growth lifted hundreds of millions out of Chinese poverty while displacing manufacturing jobs in wealthy nations.
  • Greek austerity crisis (post-2008): EU and IMF creditors imposed spending cuts; became a symbol of sovereignty transferred from democratic governments to financial institutions.
  • European Coal and Steel Community (1952) → Treaty of Rome (1957) → euro (1999) → Brexit (2020): The arc of EU integration — from coal-and-steel cooperation to supranational governance to internal fracture.

Themes

The central SPICE-T themes of this section are Political and Economic. Politically, the post-1945 world saw an unprecedented proliferation of multilateral and supranational institutions — the UN, IMF, World Bank, WTO, and EU — that pooled or constrained national sovereignty in exchange for stability and prosperity. The tension between national sovereignty and international institutional authority is the defining political tension of the era. The UN's veto structure, the EU's supranational governance, and the IMF's conditionality all represent different points on this spectrum.

Economically, the section shows that globalization is not a neutral force — it was designed by specific actors for specific interests, and its benefits and costs were unevenly distributed. The contrast between manufactured-export nations (South Korea, China) and raw-material exporters illustrates how the form of participation in the global economy determines whether a nation prospers or is disadvantaged. The backlash of the 2010s — Brexit, economic nationalism — is the political expression of the economic disruption that decades of trade integration created.

Compare and Contrast

Compare the IMF's original Bretton Woods mandate with its structural adjustment programs of the 1980s–1990s. Originally, the IMF was a relatively neutral short-term lender — providing stabilization loans without requiring fundamental domestic reform. Under structural adjustment, the IMF became an enforcer of a specific economic model: privatization, deregulation, and trade liberalization. This shift was widely criticized in the Global South as an intrusion on sovereignty that prioritized creditors over citizens. The contrast parallels a broader pattern visible throughout the section: institutions designed in 1945 for one set of purposes (stabilization, reconstruction) were repurposed in later decades to advance different interests (market liberalization), without restructuring their governance to include the nations most affected.

Practice Prompt

Short-Answer Question (SAQ):

(A) Briefly explain ONE structural feature of the United Nations Security Council that reflects the power dynamics of 1945 rather than the contemporary global order.

(B) Briefly explain ONE way in which the IMF's structural adjustment programs of the 1980s–1990s differed from its original Bretton Woods mandate.

(C) Briefly explain ONE reason why China's admission to the WTO in 2001 generated political backlash in wealthy industrialized nations despite also producing economic benefits.
