Study Guide: World War II — Conduct and Resolution

AP World History — Topic 7.5

Key Concepts

  • Blitzkrieg and the European Theater: Germany's "lightning war" doctrine of coordinated tanks, motorized infantry, and air support overwhelmed opponents organized for static defense. It conquered Poland in five weeks and France in six — stunning the world. But Blitzkrieg depended on speed; its advantages diminished in the Soviet Union's vast territory, where the war eventually became a grinding, attritional conflict Germany could not win.

  • Eastern Front as the Decisive European Theater: The Soviet-German war was WWII's decisive European theater. From the Battle of Stalingrad (turning point, 1942–1943) onward, the Red Army drove Germany back. The Soviet Union suffered approximately 27 million dead — the largest national toll of any belligerent. D-Day (June 1944) opened a second front but did not determine the outcome, which was decided primarily on Soviet soil.

  • The Pacific War's Independent Origins: The Pacific war had roots distinct from the European conflict. Japan had been militarily expanding since 1931 (Manchuria) and invaded China fully in 1937 — killing an estimated 20 million Chinese before Pearl Harbor (1941). The Pacific war was not simply a theater of the "European" WWII but a separate, earlier-starting conflict that merged with the European war after Pearl Harbor.

  • Japan's Co-Prosperity Sphere Contradiction: Japan framed its Southeast Asian empire as liberation from Western colonialism — yet in practice substituted brutal Japanese colonial control for European colonial control, extracting resources and compelling forced labor. The ideology was anti-colonial; the practice was colonial.

  • Pacific War as Decolonization Catalyst: Japan's rapid defeats of European colonial powers in Southeast Asia permanently shattered the myth of European invincibility that had sustained colonial rule. Even though Japanese occupation was brutal, the political lesson — that Europeans could be defeated by non-Europeans — was irreversible. This accelerated the decolonization movements that reshaped Asia, Africa, and the Middle East in the 1950s–60s.

  • The Postwar Order: The United Nations (with Security Council P5 veto structure), the Bretton Woods system (US dollar hegemony, IMF, World Bank), and the Cold War division of Germany and Japan into occupation zones defined the postwar international framework. The Grand Alliance between the US and USSR shattered almost immediately after the common enemy was defeated.

Vocabulary Review

  • Blitzkrieg: "Lightning war" — rapid combined-arms attacks (tanks, motorized infantry, close air support) that overwhelm static defenses through speed and shock.

  • Operation Barbarossa: Germany's 22 June 1941 invasion of the Soviet Union — the largest military invasion in history; over 3 million German soldiers along a front from Baltic to Black Sea.

  • Battle of Stalingrad (1942–1943): Turning point of the European war; Germany's Sixth Army (330,000 men) destroyed by Soviet Operation Uranus encirclement.

  • Pearl Harbor (7 December 1941): Japan's attack that brought the United States into both the Pacific and European wars simultaneously.

  • Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere: Japan's ideological framing for its Asian empire — Asian liberation from Western imperialism — that in practice substituted Japanese colonial exploitation.

  • Island-hopping: American Pacific strategy of capturing or bypassing Japanese island bases while destroying Japan's naval power and imposing economic blockade.

  • Battle of Midway (June 1942): US naval victory that destroyed Japanese naval power in the Central Pacific — the Pacific war's turning point.

  • Bretton Woods system: Post-WWII economic framework; US dollar as world reserve currency; IMF and World Bank established.

  • United Nations: International security organization founded 1945; Security Council with five permanent veto-holding members (P5).

  • Cold War: The postwar geopolitical rivalry between the United States and Soviet Union; began by 1946–1947 as the Grand Alliance shattered.

  • Decolonization catalyst: The role of the Pacific war in permanently undermining European colonial legitimacy by demonstrating Europeans could be defeated by non-European forces.

Key Figures and Events

  • Battle of Stalingrad (July 1942–February 1943): The European war's turning point; Germany's Sixth Army destroyed; Red Army goes on permanent offensive.
  • Nanjing Massacre (1937–1938): 200,000–300,000 Chinese civilians and POWs killed — evidence the Pacific war was already catastrophically underway before Pearl Harbor.
  • D-Day, Normandy (6 June 1944): Allied amphibious invasion opening a second European front; significant but not decisive in the overall outcome.
  • Hiroshima (6 August 1945) and Nagasaki (9 August 1945): Atomic bombings; 70,000–80,000+ immediate dead each; Japan surrendered 15 August 1945.
  • Harry S. Truman: US President who authorized the use of atomic weapons in August 1945.
  • General MacArthur: US commander who oversaw Japan's postwar democratization and demilitarization under American occupation.

Themes

Political: WWII's political consequences were world-historical in scale. The Pacific war's destruction of European colonial legitimacy accelerated decolonization in Asia, Africa, and the Middle East. In Europe, Germany's division into occupation zones embedded the emerging Cold War into physical geography. The creation of the United Nations and the Bretton Woods system attempted to build a rules-based international order — but the Cold War's emergence immediately challenged the premise that the major powers could cooperate within it.

Economic: The Bretton Woods system is the WWII period's major Economic theme outcome. By establishing the US dollar as the world's reserve currency and creating multilateral financial institutions (IMF, World Bank), it institutionalized US economic hegemony and the liberal international economic order that would define postwar globalization. The wartime destruction of European and Japanese industrial capacity also created the economic conditions for American-led reconstruction programs (Marshall Plan) that tied Western economies to US leadership.

Compare and Contrast

The European and Pacific theaters of WWII shared some features — industrial-scale killing, strategic bombing of civilians, eventual Allied victory — but differed in origin, character, and consequence. The European theater began in a clearly defined timeline (September 1939, German invasion of Poland) following the interwar fascist rise; it was resolved through combined Soviet and Anglo-American military pressure, and its key legacy was the Cold War division of Europe. The Pacific theater began earlier (Japanese expansion from 1931) and had distinct ideological content: Japan's anti-colonial framing of its empire — however hypocritical in practice — made the Pacific war's postwar consequence different. Japan's defeats of European powers permanently transformed the legitimacy calculus of colonial rule, producing decolonization movements that the European theater, focused on restoring the prewar territorial order, did not directly challenge.

Practice Prompt

Short Answer Question (SAQ)

"World War II was not primarily a European war: its Pacific theater began earlier, killed more people, and had more transformative global consequences."

(A) Identify ONE specific piece of evidence that the Pacific war's origins preceded the September 1939 European start date, and explain what this reveals about the war's character as a global event.

(B) Explain the causal mechanism by which Japan's military victories in Southeast Asia served as a 'decolonization catalyst.' Be specific about what changed and why.

(C) Explain why historians consider the Eastern Front — not D-Day — the decisive theater of the European war. Use specific evidence to support your argument.
