Study Guide: AP World History — Topic 5.2: The American Revolution

Key Concepts

  • Constitutional argument, not just economic complaint: The colonial cry of "no taxation without representation" was a claim about the constitutional right of British subjects to be taxed only by legislatures representing them — Parliament in London could not legitimately tax colonists because it did not represent them.
  • Enlightenment ideas as revolutionary justification: Colonists drew directly on Lockean natural rights and social contract theory to frame British policies as violations of the social contract, making resistance not just permissible but obligatory. This was the first major translation of Enlightenment philosophy into a successful revolutionary program.
  • Radicalism and limits of the revolutionary settlement: The new republic was radical in creating constitutional government based on Enlightenment principles; it was conservative in protecting chattel slavery, excluding women and enslaved people from equality, and enabling westward indigenous dispossession.
  • Diverse coalitions with divergent interests: The Revolution involved merchants, small farmers, artisans, Black Loyalists and Patriots, and indigenous peoples — each with distinct stakes in the conflict. Indigenous peoples nearly universally supported Britain, correctly calculating that independence would accelerate their dispossession.
  • Contradiction between universalist language and exclusionary practice: The Declaration's claim that "all men are created equal" was a universalist statement with deliberate exclusions. This contradiction — proclaimed equality coexisting with constitutional protection of slavery — became the defining tension of Atlantic revolutionary politics.

Vocabulary Review

  • Mercantilism: The economic doctrine that national wealth depended on accumulating precious metals and that colonies existed to supply raw materials and purchase finished goods from the mother country, with colonial trade and manufacturing restricted accordingly.
  • No taxation without representation: The colonial constitutional principle that British subjects could only be taxed by legislative bodies that represented them; Parliament in London was not such a body.
  • Continental Congress: The colonial governing body convened in Philadelphia that coordinated resistance and eventually declared independence; appointed Jefferson's committee to draft the Declaration.
  • Declaration of Independence (1776): Jefferson's document translating Lockean natural rights into American political language, listing 27 grievances against George III and declaring revolution justified when government violates the social contract.
  • Unalienable rights: Rights that cannot legitimately be taken away by any government; Jefferson named "Life, Liberty and the pursuit of Happiness" as unalienable, substituting Locke's "property."
  • Loyalists: The approximately 20% of colonists who remained loyal to Britain; tens of thousands fled to Canada and the Caribbean after American victory.
  • Black Loyalists: Enslaved people who fought for the British in exchange for freedom — making a rational calculation that British promises of emancipation were more reliable than Patriot rhetoric of liberty.
  • Three-Fifths Compromise: The constitutional provision counting enslaved people as three-fifths of a person for congressional apportionment, giving slave states disproportionate political power and embedding slavery in the republic's political structure.
  • Treaty of Paris (1783): Ended the Revolutionary War; British recognition of independence opened the trans-Appalachian interior to American expansion and intensified indigenous dispossession.
  • Bill of Rights (1791): First ten amendments to the U.S. Constitution, protecting individual liberties; became a model for constitutional protection of rights globally.

Key Figures and Events

  • John Locke: Provided the intellectual framework colonists used — natural rights, social contract, right to resist tyranny — to justify revolution.
  • Thomas Jefferson: Principal drafter of the Declaration of Independence; translated Lockean theory into American political language; a slaveholder embodying the revolution's central contradiction.
  • Lexington and Concord (April 1775): The first armed clashes of the Revolution, marking the transition from constitutional argument to military conflict.
  • Declaration of Independence (July 4, 1776): The philosophical manifesto and political document proclaiming independence based on natural rights and the social contract.
  • U.S. Constitution (1787): The world's first modern written constitution, institutionalizing Enlightenment principles of limited government while embedding protections for slavery.
  • Indigenous peoples in the Revolution: Nearly universally supported Britain, correctly predicting that American independence would accelerate westward expansion into their territories — a prediction the subsequent century confirmed.
  • Black Patriots and Loyalists: Enslaved people who made divergent strategic choices — some fighting for Britain in exchange for freedom, others fighting for Patriots in hopes that liberty rhetoric would extend to them.

Themes

The American Revolution sits at the intersection of Political and Economic SPICE-T themes. Politically, it produced the world's first modern written constitution and the first republic explicitly grounded in Enlightenment principles of separation of powers, individual rights, and popular sovereignty. This made the American republic a model widely admired and imitated in Europe and Latin America. But politically, the revolution was also deeply conservative: the Constitution embedded protections for chattel slavery through the Three-Fifths Compromise, the slave trade protection, and fugitive slave provisions. The Declaration's proclaimed equality was deliberately not extended to approximately 700,000 enslaved people. Economically, independence broke the mercantilist grip on American trade and opened Atlantic commerce, but the plantation economy — entirely dependent on enslaved labor — was protected, not challenged. The new republic's westward expansion intensified both the dispossession of indigenous peoples and the geographical spread of chattel slavery — making the economic continuities as significant as the political changes.

Compare and Contrast

Compare the American Revolution to the Haitian Revolution (1791–1804) that followed. Both drew on Enlightenment language of natural rights and the social contract; both justified political change as the people's right when government violated the social contract. But the differences are more revealing than the similarities. The American Revolution was led by property-owning colonial elites seeking independence from a distant power without dismantling the social hierarchy of slavery that sustained their wealth. The Haitian Revolution was led by enslaved people who took Enlightenment rhetoric literally, extending it universally to demand freedom from slavery — the very institution American founders had protected. Where the American revolutionary settlement entrenched slavery constitutionally, the Haitian Revolution abolished it unconditionally. The comparison reveals that the meaning of "universal rights" was contested, not settled, by the American founding.

Practice Prompt

Answer all three parts of the following SAQ:

A. Identify ONE specific British policy and explain how colonists used ONE specific Enlightenment concept to argue that the policy violated their rights.

B. Explain why the Three-Fifths Compromise represents a limitation of the American revolutionary settlement. How does it demonstrate the tension between the Declaration's promise and the Constitution's provisions?

C. Explain why indigenous peoples' decision to support the British during the American Revolution was a strategically rational choice, based on their assessment of what American independence would mean for their territories.
