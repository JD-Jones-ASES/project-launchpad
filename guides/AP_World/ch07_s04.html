Study Guide: The Interwar Period

AP World History — Topic 7.4

Key Concepts

  • The Great Depression as Political Catalyst: The Depression did not merely cause economic suffering; it destabilized liberal democratic politics across the industrial world. In Germany, 30 percent unemployment discredited the Weimar Republic and capitalism simultaneously, driving voters toward extreme alternatives — both the Communist Party and the Nazi Party gained at Weimar democracy's expense. Understanding the economic-to-political mechanism is essential for explaining the rise of fascism.

  • Fascism as a Political Movement: Fascism combined extreme nationalism, authoritarian single-party rule, state economic control, glorification of violence, and the identification of an internal "enemy" whose elimination was framed as national regeneration. It was explicitly anti-communist and anti-liberal, rejecting both Marxist class struggle and Enlightenment individualism. Italy (1922) was its first laboratory; Germany (1933) its most extreme expression.

  • Stalinism and Soviet Terror: Stalin transformed the Soviet Union through forced collectivization (beginning 1928) and Five-Year Plans. Collectivization produced the Holodomor — a deliberate famine killing 3.5–5 million Ukrainians. The Great Terror (1936–1938) executed ~750,000 and devastated the Red Army's officer corps, leaving the USSR militarily vulnerable when Germany invaded in 1941.

  • Two Paths to Totalitarianism: Fascism arrived from the extreme nationalist right; Stalinism from the communist left. Both produced single-party rule, cult of the leader, terror-based suppression of dissent, propaganda mobilization, and internal "enemy" identification. Comparing them analytically — not equating them — is essential AP World History thinking.

  • Appeasement and Its Failure: Britain and France pursued appeasement not from cowardice but from genuine military unpreparedness, public war-trauma, and the unavailability of the Soviet Union as a partner (due to Western anti-communism). Munich (1938) conceded the Sudetenland; six months later Hitler occupied the rest of Czechoslovakia, proving appeasement could not satisfy expansionist ambitions.

  • Hitler's Systematic Dismantling of Versailles: From 1933 onward Hitler rearmed Germany, remilitarized the Rhineland (1936), annexed Austria (Anschluss, 1938), seized the Sudetenland (1938), and occupied Czechoslovakia (1939) — each step testing Allied resolve and finding it absent until the invasion of Poland.

Vocabulary Review

  • Fascism: Authoritarian nationalist ideology combining single-party rule, state economic control, violence glorification, and an identified internal enemy; explicitly anti-communist and anti-liberal.

  • Great Depression: Global economic catastrophe beginning 1929; global industrial output fell 38%, international trade 65%; transmitted internationally by the gold standard.

  • Weimar Republic: Germany's post-WWI democratic government; overwhelmed by Depression unemployment, the stab-in-the-back myth, and reparations burdens; replaced by Nazi dictatorship by 1933.

  • Stab-in-the-back myth: The false claim that Germany was betrayed rather than militarily defeated in 1918; delegitimized Weimar democracy by associating its founders with national humiliation.

  • Collectivization: Soviet forced consolidation of peasant farms into collective farms; grain seizures funded industrialization; resistance met with deportation or execution as "kulaks."

  • Holodomor: Ukrainian famine (1932–1933) caused by Soviet grain seizures; 3.5–5 million dead; classified as genocide by Ukrainian scholars and many governments.

  • Five-Year Plans: Soviet industrialization programs beginning 1928; dramatically increased steel, coal, and electricity production; achieved through forced labor and suppression of independent economic life.

  • Gulag: Soviet forced-labor camp system housing millions of political prisoners; subsidized industrial development.

  • Great Terror (1936–1938): Stalin's purge of the Soviet leadership; ~750,000 executed; devastated the Red Army's officer corps.

  • Appeasement: Britain and France's policy of conceding Hitler's territorial demands to avoid war; epitomized at Munich (1938); failed to satisfy Hitler's expansionist ambitions.

  • Anschluss: Germany's annexation of Austria, March 1938 — part of Hitler's systematic dismantling of Versailles.

  • Enabling Act (1933): Legislation converting Hitler's chancellorship into a dictatorship by granting emergency powers to govern without the Reichstag.

Key Figures and Events

  • Adolf Hitler: Appointed German Chancellor 30 January 1933; converted to dictatorship through the Enabling Act; systematically dismantled Versailles through rearmament, remilitarization, Anschluss, and territorial annexations.
  • Joseph Stalin: Consolidated control of the Soviet Union by late 1920s; transformed it through collectivization and Five-Year Plans at 6–20 million lives' cost; conducted the Great Terror (1936–38).
  • Benito Mussolini: First fascist leader, in power in Italy from 1922; provided the ideological template that Hitler adapted.
  • Holodomor (1932–1933): Ukrainian famine produced by Soviet grain seizures during collectivization; 3.5–5 million dead; genocide debate centers on intentionality.
  • Munich Conference (September 1938): Britain and France concede Sudetenland to Hitler; defining moment of appeasement.
  • German invasion of Poland (1 September 1939): Triggered British and French declarations of war; beginning of WWII in Europe.

Themes

Political: The interwar period demonstrates the fragility of democratic political institutions under economic stress. When liberal democratic governments failed to provide economic security during the Depression, citizens turned to authoritarian alternatives that promised national regeneration through strong leadership and the elimination of internal enemies. Both fascism and Stalinism showed that modern industrial states could efficiently organize mass political violence — a capability the twentieth century would deploy repeatedly.

Economic: The Great Depression represents the Economic theme's most catastrophic moment in the interwar period. The gold standard's transmission of deflation internationally, the 38 percent fall in industrial output, and governments' constrained ability to respond demonstrated the interconnectedness of industrial economies and the inadequacy of laissez-faire policy in a systemic crisis. The Depression's most important consequence was not economic but political: it made totalitarian alternatives to liberal capitalism seem viable to millions of voters.

Compare and Contrast

Fascism and Stalinism both produced totalitarian states, but from opposite ideological directions and with different economic foundations. Fascism preserved nominal private property while directing the economy through state command — it was anti-Marxist in its economics, relying on nationalist mobilization rather than class analysis. Stalinism eliminated private property entirely through collectivization and nationalization, applying Marxist theory to create a command economy. Yet both systems used terror to enforce compliance, both identified internal enemies whose persecution unified the body politic, and both subordinated individual economic and political life to the state's goals. For AP World History purposes, recognizing their structural parallels while maintaining analytical clarity about their ideological and economic differences is essential.

Practice Prompt

Short Answer Question (SAQ)

"The economic catastrophe of the Great Depression was the necessary precondition for the political catastrophes of the 1930s."

(A) Explain ONE specific way in which the Great Depression contributed to the political rise of extremist movements in Germany. Be specific about the economic and political mechanisms involved.

(B) Compare Stalin's collectivization policy with fascist economic policy: identify ONE significant similarity AND one significant difference in how each system organized its economy.

(C) Explain why Britain and France practiced appeasement toward Hitler rather than confronting German territorial expansion earlier. Identify at least TWO factors that shaped their decision-making.
