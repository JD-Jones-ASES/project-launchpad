Study Guide: Migration and Indentured Servitude

AP World History — Unit 6, Section 5

Key Concepts

  • The nineteenth century was a world in motion: Between 1815 and 1914, an estimated 50–55 million people migrated across oceanic distances — the largest voluntary (and involuntary) population movement in human history to that point. Three overlapping streams: European emigration, indentured labor migration, and internal colonial displacement.
  • All three migration streams were consequences of the same structural process: The global reorganization of labor by industrialization and imperialism. Colonial economies created poverty and desperation in sending regions; industrial and plantation economies created demand in receiving regions. Migration linked these structural conditions.
  • Push-pull analysis is essential: Large-scale migration reflects the interaction of push factors (conditions driving emigration: famine, poverty, persecution) and pull factors (conditions attracting migrants: land, wages, transport accessibility). Both must be analyzed; neither alone is sufficient.
  • Indentured servitude was the post-abolition labor solution: After formal abolition of the slave trade (1807) and slavery (1833, British Empire), planters faced a labor crisis. Indentured servitude — legally binding five-year contracts with Indian and Chinese workers — was the colonial system response. Historians debate whether this constituted "a new system of slavery."
  • Continuity and change across unfree labor systems: The abolition of slavery represents formal legal change; the persistence of plantation labor under coercive contracts represents structural continuity. AP World History asks students to analyze both — the legal framework changed but material conditions remained similar enough to sustain the historical debate.
  • Diaspora communities became political actors: Migrations produced diaspora communities that maintained homeland ties and organized transnational political movements — Irish-American funding of the Fenian Brotherhood and Easter Rising; Indian diaspora organizing under Gandhi; Chinese diaspora funding of Sun Yat-sen's republican movement. The migrations that imperialism created ultimately challenged empire.
  • Receiving societies showed structural tension: Economic demand for immigrant labor coexisted with cultural anti-immigrant nativism — the Chinese Exclusion Act (1882) and immigration quotas reflect the same societies that absorbed and economically depended on immigrant labor.

Vocabulary Review

  • push-pull factors: Framework for migration analysis: push factors motivate departure; pull factors attract to destination. Both are required for large-scale migration.
  • Great European Emigration: The movement of ~50 million Europeans to the Americas and Australasia between 1845–1914, driven by famine, poverty, persecution, and industrial displacement.
  • Ireland's Great Famine (1845–52): Killed ~1 million Irish; drove ~1 million to emigrate in five years; a defining push factor of nineteenth-century Irish emigration.
  • indentured servitude: A labor system using legally binding contracts for fixed periods (typically five years) with Indian and Chinese workers, developed as the post-abolition solution to plantation labor shortages. Workers could be imprisoned for breach of contract.
  • arkattis: Labor recruiters who enlisted workers for indenture contracts, frequently misrepresenting conditions at the destination.
  • Hugh Tinker: British historian who described nineteenth-century indentured labor as "a new system of slavery" — the central historiographical position in the debate about continuity across unfree labor systems.
  • Sanderson Committee (1909): British government inquiry hearing testimony from indentured laborers in Fiji, Natal, and the Caribbean; a primary source for the indenture system's conditions.
  • diaspora: A community living outside its ancestral homeland while maintaining cultural, linguistic, religious, or political ties to it; nineteenth-century imperialism created Irish, Indian, Chinese, and Jewish diasporas, among others.
  • nativism: Anti-immigrant sentiment in receiving societies — cultural anxiety about national identity coexisting with economic dependence on immigrant labor.
  • chain migration: Migration facilitated by existing family or community networks; explains why specific immigrant communities clustered in specific occupations and cities.
  • Chinese Exclusion Act (1882): U.S. legislation prohibiting Chinese laborer immigration — the defining example of receiving-society nativism targeting a specific racial group.
  • coolie: A derogatory contemporary term for Chinese indentured workers; now considered offensive.

Key Figures and Events

  • Ireland's Great Famine (1845–52): Created the Irish-American diaspora that later funded and organized Irish nationalism; one of the defining push factors in the Great European Emigration.
  • Abolition of slavery (1833, British Empire; 1865, USA; 1888, Brazil): Created the plantation labor crisis that generated the indentured servitude system; the pivotal policy change that restructured global labor migration.
  • Sanderson Committee testimony (1909): Primary source evidence of indentured conditions in Fiji, Natal, and the Caribbean; central to the "new system of slavery" historical debate.
  • Mohandas Gandhi in South Africa: Gandhi's political career began organizing the Indian indentured community in South Africa — connecting indentured labor history directly to the twentieth-century independence movement.
  • Sun Yat-sen and Chinese diaspora networks: Diaspora financial support and political organizing for the Chinese republican movement — demonstrating how migration created transnational political networks challenging empire.
  • Fenian Brotherhood and Irish-American nationalism: Irish-American organizations funding Irish resistance; the Easter Rising (1916) was partly organized from diaspora networks — showing how the Great Famine's emigrant generation built political capacity over decades.
  • Chinese Exclusion Act (1882): U.S. legislation reflecting the structural tension between immigrant labor demand and nativist cultural anxiety; the clearest example of racial targeting in nineteenth-century immigration restriction.

Themes

Social (S) and Economic (E) themes dominate this section's SPICE-T analysis. Economically, the nineteenth-century migrations were a response to the same structural conditions: industrial capitalism's creation of labor surpluses in some regions (displaced artisans in England, impoverished farmers in Ireland and India) and labor demand in others (American industry, Caribbean plantations, South African mines). The fall in transatlantic shipping costs from steam technology made the supply of labor across oceanic distances economically feasible — a Technology (T) → Economic (E) connection. Socially, the migrations created new communities, new cultural hybridities, and new political identities on a global scale. The diaspora communities formed in this period — Indian in the Caribbean and Indian Ocean world, Chinese in Southeast Asia and the Pacific, Jewish in Western Europe and the Americas, Irish in Britain and North America — reshaped both sending and receiving societies.

The indentured labor system connects this section directly to the slavery debates that preceded it. Continuity and change is the analytical frame: the formal legal structure changed (contract replacing property), but material conditions were similar enough that a leading historian described it as "a new system of slavery." AP World History asks students to analyze this continuity seriously — not to conclude that nothing changed, but to examine what the changes were (legal rights, time limits, contract terms) against what persisted (coercive enforcement, plantation labor, subsistence conditions, limited recourse). The colonial economic systems that created indentured labor also generated the diaspora communities that eventually organized against empire — the migrations that imperialism produced were among the forces that ultimately challenged it.

Compare and Contrast

European voluntary emigration and Indian indentured labor migration share the same macro-structural cause — the global reorganization of labor under industrialization and imperialism — but differ significantly in their mechanisms, degrees of coercion, and historical consequences. European emigrants experienced genuine push factors (famine, poverty, persecution) but retained agency in choosing destinations, timing, and occupations. Indian indentured workers were recruited by agents who misrepresented conditions, signed contracts that were legally binding under criminal penalty, arrived to find conditions radically worse than promised, and could not leave until their five-year term expired — if they could afford return passage at all. Both created diaspora communities; both communities became political actors. But the experiences of emigration differed fundamentally in the coercive architecture surrounding them. The comparison also illuminates the receiving society: European immigrants faced nativism and discrimination while building political power (Irish police forces, immigrant political machines); Indian and Chinese workers faced more severe legal discrimination (Chinese Exclusion Act, formal indenture contracts) while having fewer institutional paths to political inclusion.

Practice Prompt

(AP Short-Answer Question Format)

"The formal abolition of the Atlantic slave trade (1807, Britain; 1808, United States) and of slavery itself (1833, British Empire; 1865, United States; 1888, Brazil) left British, French, Dutch, and American plantation agriculture facing a labor crisis. Planters and colonial governments developed the indentured servitude system as their solution."

Using the passage above and your knowledge of world history:

(A) Briefly explain ONE specific way in which indentured servitude in the nineteenth century can be characterized as a continuation of unfree labor systems.

(B) Briefly explain ONE specific way in which indentured servitude represented a change from the Atlantic slave trade in terms of legal structure or worker status.

(C) Briefly explain how ONE nineteenth-century diaspora community created by migration later acted as a political force challenging imperial or colonial systems.
