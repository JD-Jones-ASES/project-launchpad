Study Guide: The "New Imperialism"

AP World History — Unit 6, Section 1

Key Concepts

  • New Imperialism (c. 1870–1914): The rapid, state-directed wave of European colonial expansion that brought roughly 10 million square miles and 150 million additional people under colonial control in less than five decades. Distinguished from earlier colonialism by its speed, ideological self-confidence, and explicit backing by industrial-era military force.
  • Economic roots of imperialism: Industrial capitalism generated three overlapping imperatives — raw materials to feed factories, new markets to absorb surplus output, and investment outlets for accumulated capital. By the 1890s British capital abroad exceeded domestic investment, making empire partly a financial necessity for ruling elites.
  • Social Darwinism: The misapplication of evolutionary biology to human races and civilizations, used to argue that European dominance was a natural and inevitable result of racial "fitness." Had no legitimate scientific basis; served to legitimate conquest to domestic audiences who also valued Christian ethics and Enlightenment ideals.
  • Civilizing mission: The ideological claim that Europeans had a duty to "uplift" peoples they deemed less advanced. Simultaneously a sincere belief for some and a convenient rationalization for others — always functioning, regardless of sincerity, to justify forced cultural transformation and resource extraction.
  • The dual-engine structure of imperialism: The economic track (raw material extraction, labor reorganization, market integration) and the cultural track (Social Darwinism, civilizing mission) operated together — ideology made extraction seem natural, and extraction made ideology necessary. Neither can be understood without the other.
  • Primary source analysis as AP skill: Kipling's "White Man's Burden" must be read for historical context (February 1899, Philippine annexation), intended audience (American readers), purpose (imperial advocacy), and point of view (colonized peoples erased as subjects). H. T. Johnson's counter-poem provides a contrasting perspective that centers colonial violence.
  • Critique from within the colonizing world: J. A. Hobson's Imperialism: A Study (1902) challenged the claim that empire benefited Britain broadly — profits flowed to a narrow financial class while ordinary citizens bore the tax cost. This was a contemporary argument, not a retrospective one.

Vocabulary Review

  • New Imperialism: The aggressive, state-backed wave of European territorial acquisition between roughly 1870 and 1914, enabled by industrial-era military technologies and justified by Social Darwinism and the civilizing mission.
  • Social Darwinism: Misapplication of evolutionary "survival of the fittest" logic to human races; used to claim European superiority was scientifically inevitable. Coined by Herbert Spencer; Darwin never endorsed it.
  • civilizing mission (mission civilisatrice): Ideological claim that colonizers had a duty to uplift "less advanced" peoples through European language, Christianity, law, and education — in practice, a justification for conquest and cultural erasure.
  • investment imperialism: Export of surplus European capital into colonial infrastructure (railways, harbors, telegraphs) that returned profit to investors while also serving military control.
  • raw material extraction: The seizure and reorganization of colonial economies to supply European factories with rubber, cotton, tin, palm oil, and precious metals.
  • Maxim gun: Rapid-fire machine gun (500 rounds/minute) invented 1884; gave colonial forces decisive military advantage over armies relying on traditional weapons; a key enabler of the Scramble for Africa.
  • Herbert Spencer: British philosopher who coined "survival of the fittest" and applied it to social groups; intellectual father of Social Darwinism.
  • J. A. Hobson: British critic of empire who argued in Imperialism: A Study (1902) that imperial profits concentrated among financiers while society paid the costs.
  • terra nullius: Legal fiction claiming land without European-style property documents was "empty" and available for seizure, used to delegitimize existing African and Asian sovereignty.
  • push-pull factors: Framework for migration analysis: push factors drive people out of a place; pull factors attract them to a destination. Also applicable to imperial capital flows — surplus capital was "pushed" abroad when domestic investment returns fell.
  • surplus capital: Accumulated investment funds exceeding domestic opportunities; a structural driver of imperial expansion as European financiers sought colonial infrastructure projects with reliable returns.
  • Suez Canal (1869): Canal cutting ~7,000 miles off the Britain–India route; transformed imperial logistics and prompted British occupation of Egypt (1882) to protect access.

Key Figures and Events

  • Rudyard Kipling: British author who published "The White Man's Burden" (February 1899) in the American magazine McClure's, urging the United States to accept colonial responsibility for the Philippines — a clear act of imperial advocacy at a specific political moment.
  • H. T. Johnson: African American minister and AME Church Review editor whose "The Black Man's Burden" (1899) directly countered Kipling by centering the violence of colonial conquest rather than its supposed burdens on colonizers.
  • J. A. Hobson: British economist whose 1902 critique of empire identified surplus capital as the structural driver and argued that ordinary Britons bore costs while financiers reaped profits.
  • Herbert Spencer: Philosopher who coined "survival of the fittest" (1864) and applied it to human civilization, providing the intellectual architecture for Social Darwinism.
  • Hiram Maxim: American-born inventor whose machine gun (demonstrated 1884) fired 500 rounds per minute — the defining military technology of the Scramble for Africa.
  • Suez Canal opening (1869): Transformed the logistics of British imperial power in Asia; triggered Britain's occupation of Egypt in 1882.
  • Treaty of Paris (December 1898): Transferred the Philippines from Spain to the United States following the Spanish-American War; the specific political event that prompted Kipling's poem.

Themes

The New Imperialism is one of the richest SPICE-T intersections in AP World History. Economically (E), the section demonstrates how industrial capitalism's structural surpluses — of goods, capital, and production — created material imperatives for overseas expansion that went beyond simple greed. The reorganization of colonial economies around European needs (dismantling subsistence agriculture, forcing cash-crop labor) represents a fundamental transformation of global economic geography. Culturally (C), the section shows how ideology is never merely decorative: Social Darwinism and the civilizing mission performed real work, making conquest appear not only permissible but morally obligatory to European publics who also valued Christian ethics. The Kipling–Johnson pairing is a masterclass in how two authors can represent the same events from diametrically opposed vantage points — and why point of view analysis matters.

Technology (T) intersects powerfully with Political (P): the Maxim gun, steam-powered gunboats, and telegraph lines were not merely weapons but systems that restructured the political geography of three continents within four decades. Without industrial military technology, the pace and scale of conquest — 10 million square miles in 44 years — would have been impossible. The section also raises the Social (S) dimension through the forced reorganization of labor: subsistence farmers across Africa and Asia were pushed into cash-crop and plantation labor systems that served European markets at the expense of local food security and community stability.

Compare and Contrast

The New Imperialism can be compared to the earlier phase of European overseas expansion (1450–1750) to clarify what was genuinely new. Both phases involved resource extraction, the Atlantic slave trade, and the imposition of European authority on distant peoples. However, the New Imperialism was qualitatively different in three ways: first, the pace was far faster — 10 million square miles in 44 years versus centuries of gradual penetration; second, the technologies (Maxim gun, steam gunboat, telegraph) enabled direct territorial administration rather than coastal-enclave trading; and third, the ideological architecture — Social Darwinism and the civilizing mission — was elaborated specifically to justify conquest to audiences that also subscribed to Enlightenment ideals of human equality. The earlier phase relied more on mercantilist economic logic; the New Imperialism combined industrial capitalism's structural needs with nationalist interstate competition in a volatile mixture.

Practice Prompt

(AP Short-Answer Question Format)

"Take up the White Man's burden — / Send forth the best ye breed — / Go bind your sons to exile / To serve your captives' need." — Rudyard Kipling, "The White Man's Burden," 1899

Using the excerpt above and your knowledge of world history:

(A) Briefly explain ONE specific historical circumstance that helps explain why Kipling addressed this poem to an American audience in February 1899.

(B) Briefly explain ONE way in which H. T. Johnson's "The Black Man's Burden" (1899) challenges the argument Kipling makes in the excerpt above.

(C) Briefly explain ONE economic motive for European imperialism in the late nineteenth century that is NOT reflected in Kipling's framing of colonialism as a "burden" for the colonizer.
