Study Guide: Decolonization — Negotiation versus Armed Struggle

AP World History — Unit 8, Section 4

Key Concepts

  • Decolonization as the century's most consequential transformation: Between 1945 and 1980, more than eighty new nations emerged from colonial rule across Asia, Africa, the Middle East, and the Caribbean. This added more than a billion people to the community of self-governing peoples and reshaped every major international institution. It was not a single event or smooth process — it ranged from negotiated transfers to bloody wars of national liberation lasting years or decades.

  • Structural variables determine the path: The source identifies five variables that shaped whether independence came through negotiation or armed struggle: (1) presence or absence of a large settler population; (2) economic value to the colonial metropole; (3) independence movement's military strength; (4) democratic pressure against colonial war in the metropole; and (5) Cold War pressures. India had all five favoring compromise; Algeria had them favoring resistance.

  • Gandhi and satyagraha: Gandhi transformed Indian nationalism from an elite political movement into a genuine mass campaign through satyagraha — nonviolent resistance grounded in the inseparability of means and ends. His philosophical commitment: a free India could not be built through violence that replicated colonial coercion. The Salt March (1930) demonstrated the strategic genius of choosing civil disobedience accessible to the poorest Indian.

  • Algeria: settler colonialism and armed struggle: Where India had indirect rule over a large indigenous population, Algeria was a settler colony: one million pieds-noirs legally dominant over nine million Algerians, with France treating Algeria as an integral part of the republic itself. This structure gave France strong internal incentives to resist independence that did not exist in India, making an eight-year war (1954–62) the result.

  • Decolonization as cultural transformation: Both Gandhi and Fanon recognized that colonialism operated at the cultural and psychological level — not just the political — and that independence required transformation of how colonized peoples understood themselves. They agreed on the diagnosis but offered opposite prescriptions: Gandhi's nonviolent means, Fanon's liberating violence.

  • Year of Africa (1960): Seventeen African nations became independent in 1960 alone, most through negotiation. The explanation: European colonial powers calculated that the cost of holding colonies by force exceeded economic benefit — a calculation accelerated by the Algerian War's political damage to France.

Vocabulary Review

Satyagraha — Gandhi's philosophy of nonviolent resistance combining "truth" and "force"; the practitioner accepts suffering to convert opponents rather than destroy them; active and disciplined, not passive.

Salt March (1930) — Gandhi's 240-mile walk to harvest sea salt illegally; strategically chose a commodity universal, monopolized, and accessible to the poorest; demonstrated colonial brutality to international press.

Partition (1947) — Division of the Indian subcontinent into India and Pakistan at independence; triggered ~14 million refugees and 200,000–2 million deaths in communal violence.

Pieds-noirs — European settler population of approximately one million in Algeria; their legal dominance over the indigenous population and political stakes in continued colonial rule were the key structural variable explaining why Algeria required armed struggle.

FLN (Front de Libération Nationale) — Algerian independence organization that launched armed revolt November 1, 1954; led the eight-year War of Independence through guerrilla warfare.

Evian Accords (1962) — Agreement ending the Algerian War; 91% of Algerians voted for independence in the subsequent referendum; one million pieds-noirs fled to France.

Mau Mau Uprising (1952–60) — Kikuyu guerrilla revolt against British settler dispossession in Kenya; suppressed through mass detention, torture, and collective punishment; Kenya independent 1963.

Year of Africa (1960) — Seventeen African nations achieved independence in 1960 alone, mostly through negotiation as colonial powers calculated that holding colonies by force exceeded their worth.

Frantz Fanon — Martinican psychiatrist and FLN member; argued in The Wretched of the Earth (1961) that anti-colonial violence was necessary to reverse colonialism's psychological damage.

Kwame Nkrumah — Leader of Ghana's independence (1957), first sub-Saharan African nation independent; pan-Africanist, co-founder of OAU.

Key Figures and Events

  • Gandhi — Transformed Indian independence from elite politics to mass movement; satyagraha philosophy; Salt March (1930); Quit India Movement (1942); assassinated January 1948; global symbol of nonviolent resistance whose methods inspired Martin Luther King Jr. and anti-apartheid campaigns.
  • Indian independence and Partition (August 1947) — Largest nonviolent independence transition in history, accompanied by catastrophic communal violence; demonstrates that negotiated independence did not guarantee peaceful transition when colonial policies had sharpened communal divisions.
  • FLN and the Algerian War (1954–62) — Paradigm case of armed anti-colonial struggle against a settler colony; exposed France's human rights contradiction; cost hundreds of thousands to 1.5 million Algerian lives.
  • Kwame Nkrumah — Ghana independent 1957; first sub-Saharan African independence; pan-Africanist who argued for continental African unity.
  • Frantz Fanon — The Wretched of the Earth (1961); argued decolonization required cultural and psychological transformation; offered the most influential theoretical justification for revolutionary violence.
  • Dien Bien Phu (1954) — French defeat in Vietnam that demonstrated European colonial armies could be defeated and accelerated the FLN's decision to launch the Algerian revolt months later.

Themes

The Political theme is foundational to this section: decolonization was the mass political transformation of the twentieth century, converting colonial subjects into sovereign citizens. But the source insists decolonization was not only a transfer of formal sovereignty but also a Cultural and psychological process. Gandhi's philosophy of satyagraha and Fanon's theory of liberating violence both address the cultural dimension: colonialism had shaped how colonized peoples understood themselves, and independence required reclaiming that self-understanding. Gandhi insisted that nonviolent means were necessary because colonial coercion could not be the foundation of a free culture. Fanon argued that anti-colonial violence was precisely what restored colonized peoples' dignity and agency — both are SPICE-T Political + Cultural arguments about the relationship between political action and cultural transformation.

The Social theme appears in the staggering human costs: Partition's 200,000–2 million dead, the Algerian War's 300,000–1.5 million dead, the Mau Mau suppression's thousands of detained and tortured. These numbers reflect the social cost of colonial systems unwilling to permit peaceful transition and of post-colonial demographic reorganization that colonial administrations had long deferred. The Economic theme connects to the structural question: colonial economies built on resource extraction shaped the independence paths available — colonies with large European economic investments (like Algeria's pieds-noirs) faced more violent transitions than colonies where the economic stake was in trade rather than settlement.

Compare and Contrast

A central AP comparison in this section: India (negotiated independence) vs. Algeria (armed struggle). The key structural variable is the presence of a large settler population. In India, Britain governed through indirect rule over a large indigenous population; there was no significant British settler population with permanent economic stakes in resisting independence. In Algeria, one million pieds-noirs had established legal dominance over nine million Algerians; France treated Algeria as part of the republic itself. This structural difference — not the relative strength of independence movements or the relative democratic values of Britain and France — explains the divergent paths. This comparison exemplifies the AP skill of identifying structural causes rather than attributing historical outcomes to individual agency or national character.

Practice Prompt

Short-Answer Question (SAQ)

Use your knowledge of the period 1900–present to answer parts A, B, and C.

A. Briefly explain Gandhi's concept of satyagraha and explain ONE specific way it shaped the tactics of the Indian independence movement.

B. Explain ONE structural difference between India and Algeria that helps account for why India achieved independence through negotiation while Algeria required eight years of armed struggle.

C. Explain ONE way in which the outcome of either the Indian independence movement or the Algerian War had consequences beyond the borders of the country immediately involved — that is, one way in which it influenced decolonization or resistance movements elsewhere.
