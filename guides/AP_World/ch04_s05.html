Study Guide: Mercantilism and Joint-Stock Companies

AP World History — Unit 4: Transoceanic Interconnections, 1450–1750

Key Concepts

  • Mercantilism's zero-sum logic: Mercantilist theory held that global wealth was finite and competitive: one nation's gain was another's loss. National wealth meant accumulating bullion (gold and silver) and running a favorable trade balance — exporting more than importing. Colonies served this framework by supplying raw materials and serving as captive markets for manufactured goods, forbidden from trading with rival powers.

  • The price revolution — mercantilism's paradox: The influx of American silver caused severe inflation in Spain, making Spanish goods more expensive relative to those of northern European rivals. Spain then imported manufactured goods from the Dutch Republic, England, and France — sending the silver it had accumulated outward. By the mid-seventeenth century, Spain was deeply in debt to northern European banking houses. Mercantilist bullion accumulation was self-defeating because silver functioned as money: more money chasing the same goods raised prices rather than increasing real wealth.

  • The joint-stock company as institutional innovation: The joint-stock company solved three problems simultaneously: (1) capital accumulation — spreading risk across investors; (2) governance — delegating sovereign powers to a corporate entity; (3) alignment — linking private profit to state geopolitical interests. The VOC and EIC were not simply trading companies; they were hybrid state-corporate entities that governed millions of people.

  • Violence as a corporate tool: The Banda Islands massacre (1621) illustrates that joint-stock companies combined the capitalist profit motive with state-level military violence. The VOC was empowered to negotiate treaties, maintain armies and navies, and govern territories — making it functionally a sovereign entity, not merely a commercial firm.

  • Tordesillas as a precedent: The Treaty of Tordesillas (1494) established that European powers could negotiate the division of the non-European world among themselves without indigenous consent — a legal logic that the source explicitly connects to the Berlin Conference of 1884–1885.

Vocabulary Review

  • mercantilism: Economic theory (c. 1500–1750) holding that national wealth = accumulated bullion + favorable trade balance; colonies exist to supply raw materials and buy manufactured goods.

  • bullion: Gold and silver in bulk form; the target of mercantilist accumulation.

  • balance of trade: Difference between exports and imports; mercantilist states sought to export more than they imported to retain bullion.

  • price revolution: Severe European inflation caused by American silver inflows; paradoxically enriched Spain's commercial rivals by diffusing silver through import payments.

  • joint-stock company: Corporate entity pooling investor capital; received crown charter with monopoly rights; the primary institutional vehicle of European colonial expansion.

  • VOC (Dutch East India Company): Founded 1602; charter covered all Dutch trade east of the Cape of Good Hope; empowered to wage war, negotiate treaties, and govern territories; peak power in the mid-seventeenth century.

  • EIC (English East India Company): Founded 1600; eventually governed much of the Indian subcontinent.

  • Virginia Company: Founded 1606; established Jamestown (1607) — first permanent English North American settlement.

  • Jan Pieterszoon Coen (1587–1629): VOC Governor-General who ordered the Banda Islands massacre to enforce the nutmeg monopoly.

  • Treaty of Tordesillas (1494): Papal-brokered division of the non-European world between Spain and Portugal; ignored by rivals; precedent for European self-dealing over non-European territories.

  • quinto real: Spanish crown's 20% tax on colonial precious metals; primary mercantilist revenue mechanism, but self-defeating through price revolution dynamics.

Key Figures and Events

  • VOC (1602): Founded as the first company to issue publicly traded shares; operated more ships than any European government at its peak; governed territories from Cape of Good Hope to Japan.

  • Jan Pieterszoon Coen (1621): Banda Islands massacre — population reduced from ~15,000 to <600 to enforce nutmeg monopoly; illustrates corporate use of state-level violence.

  • Treaty of Tordesillas (1494): Drew imaginary line dividing non-European world; Spain west, Portugal east; ignored by Dutch, English, French; precedent for the Berlin Conference.

  • Jamestown (1607): First permanent English North American settlement; established by the Virginia Company under joint-stock charter.

  • Adam Smith, Wealth of Nations (1776): The intellectual critique that replaced mercantilist theory with free-trade liberalism.

  • Casa de Contratación (est. 1503): Spanish agency enforcing mercantilist colonial trade controls; extracted quinto real.

Themes

Economic Theory and Policy (SPICE-T: E) Mercantilism was the dominant economic theory of this period, and its assumptions shaped colonial policy at every level — from port controls and licensed shipping to the quinto real and the prohibition of inter-colonial trade with rivals. Understanding mercantilism is essential for AP World History because it explains why colonial policy was structured as it was: not out of simple greed, but from a coherent (if flawed) theory about how national wealth operated. The price revolution revealed the theory's fundamental flaw: bullion was not stored wealth but circulating money, and more money in circulation raised prices rather than making a nation richer.

Political Power and Corporate Organization (SPICE-T: P + T) The joint-stock company represents a technological and institutional innovation in the organization of power — a new form of political-economic entity that blended state authority with private capital. The VOC and EIC governed millions of people and shaped the global economy for two centuries using powers (war, treaty, jurisdiction) that had previously belonged exclusively to sovereigns. This form of governance — private corporate entities holding public power — is both historically specific to this period and historically consequential for understanding modern capitalism and colonialism.

Compare and Contrast

Compare Spanish and Dutch approaches to colonial economic organization. Both the Spanish empire and the Dutch VOC extracted resources from non-European peoples through violence and monopoly control. The key institutional difference is in organizational form: Spanish colonialism was a state enterprise, with the crown directly controlling trade through the Casa de Contratación, extracting revenue through the quinto real, and using state labor institutions (encomienda, mita) to mobilize indigenous workers. Dutch colonialism through the VOC was organized as a private corporate enterprise chartered by the state: shareholders bore the costs and received the profits, and the VOC used its own military force rather than the Dutch state's. Both models became templates for later European colonialism — the Spanish model for continental empire-building, the Dutch-English joint-stock model for commercial maritime empire.

Practice Prompt

Short Answer Question (SAQ)

Use the following claim to answer parts A, B, and C:

"The joint-stock company was the most consequential institutional innovation of the early modern period because it solved three interlocking problems that no state alone could solve."

Part A: Briefly explain ONE specific problem that the joint-stock company solved, and identify a specific company as evidence.

Part B: Briefly explain ONE specific example of how a joint-stock company exercised sovereign powers (war, treaty, or jurisdiction) that would normally belong to a state — and what this reveals about the nature of early modern European colonialism.

Part C: Briefly explain ONE way in which mercantilist economic theory shaped the policies of either the Spanish empire or a joint-stock company in the period 1500–1750.
