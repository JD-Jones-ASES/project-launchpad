Study Guide: AP World History — Topic 5.5: Latin American Independence

Key Concepts

  • The casta system as the structural precondition: Spanish America's rigid racial hierarchy — peninsulares at top, then creoles, mestizos, mulatos, indigenous peoples, enslaved Africans — created the social tensions that independence movements navigated. Creole exclusion from top offices generated elite grievances; mestizo and indigenous exclusion from any power generated popular mobilization.
  • Napoleon's invasion as catalyst: The 1808 invasion of Spain created a legitimacy vacuum — if the Spanish king was a French puppet, to whom did colonial loyalty belong? Juntas forming in the name of Ferdinand VII became the institutional vehicle through which independence gradually emerged.
  • Creole nationalism and its limits: Independence was primarily led by creole elites who wanted political independence without social revolution. They replaced peninsulares at the top of colonial structures without redistributing land, wealth, or power — making formal independence coincide with social continuity.
  • Popular participation without popular outcomes: Mestizo and indigenous peoples (and in some cases formerly enslaved people) participated in independence wars and sometimes provided decisive military force — but were largely excluded from political settlements. Indigenous tribute was abolished in form but replaced by other coercion.
  • Divergent paths within Latin American independence: Mexico's independence came through popular mobilization (Hidalgo) followed by conservative co-optation (Iturbide). South America saw Bolívar's ambitious campaigns in the north and San Martín's in the south. Brazil's transition was uniquely peaceful and monarchical. These varied outcomes demonstrate that "Latin American independence" was not one event but a series of overlapping struggles.
  • Failure of pan-American unity: Bolívar's dream of a unified Hispanic American federation — outlined in his Jamaica Letter — collapsed by 1830. Regional interests, social divisions, and caudillo politics prevailed over federal vision.

Vocabulary Review

  • Casta system: Spain's colonial racial hierarchy, encoding privilege and exclusion based on birth, ancestry, and racial classification.
  • Peninsulares: Spanish-born colonists favored for the highest colonial offices; their monopoly on top positions was the creoles' central grievance.
  • Creole (criollo): Person of Spanish descent born in the Americas; educated, wealthy, politically organized — the natural leaders of independence movements — but socially conservative.
  • Mestizo/mulato: People of mixed European-indigenous or European-African ancestry; 80–90% of Spanish America's population with mestizos and indigenous peoples; often provided military force without gaining political power.
  • Junta: Colonial governing council formed after 1808 in the name of Ferdinand VII; some evolved into independence movements.
  • Caudillo: Military strongman who dominated nineteenth-century Latin American politics; the typical form of political power in republics that could not sustain stable democratic governance.
  • Creole nationalism: Independence led by creoles who preserved colonial social hierarchies after removing Spanish-born officials from power.
  • Grito de Dolores: Hidalgo's 1810 call to arms that launched Mexico's popular independence movement, mobilizing mestizo and indigenous peasants.
  • Jamaica Letter (1815): Bolívar's analysis of the prospects and challenges of South American independence; honestly acknowledged the region's social divisions and the limits of formal independence.

Key Figures and Events

  • Simón Bolívar (1783–1830): "El Libertador"; Venezuelan creole who led campaigns across five present-day countries; Jamaica Letter (1815) articulated the most sophisticated vision of South American independence and federation.
  • Father Miguel Hidalgo (1753–1811): Mexican priest whose Grito de Dolores (1810) mobilized popular forces; executed in 1811, demonstrating the limits of popular revolution within a creole-dominated independence movement.
  • José María Morelos (1765–1815): Hidalgo's successor; continued popular mobilization; also captured and executed.
  • Agustín de Iturbide (1783–1824): Royalist creole officer who co-opted Mexico's independence through conservative compromise; briefly Emperor Agustín I — epitomizing creole nationalism.
  • José de San Martín (1778–1850): Led the Andes crossing (1817); liberated Chile; coordinated with Bolívar on Peru.
  • Pedro I of Brazil (1798–1834): Declared Brazilian independence as Emperor (1822) — a monarchical, relatively peaceful transition, the most conservative Latin American independence outcome.

Themes

Latin American independence sits at the intersection of Political and Social SPICE-T themes in ways that highlight contradictions. Politically, the movements produced over a dozen new republics with written constitutions modeled on Enlightenment principles — separation of powers, natural rights, elected government. This represented a formal transformation from colonial rule. But in practice, the political theme was continuity: power passed from Spanish-born peninsulares to American-born creoles without redistributing land or dismantling racial hierarchy. The new republics' instability — dominated by caudillos and ravaged by civil wars — revealed how shallow constitutional formalism was when economic and social structures remained unchanged.

Socially, the independence wars involved all of Spanish America's social groups in ways that challenged the colonial order momentarily — Hidalgo's army, Bolívar's multiracial forces, Haitian-connected anti-slavery commitments — yet the post-independence settlements largely restored the creole class to dominance. Indigenous tribute technically ended but was replaced by coerced labor. The social promises of independence were deferred, not fulfilled — a pattern that defined Latin American politics for the next century.

Compare and Contrast

Compare the outcomes of North American and Latin American independence. Both drew on Enlightenment political philosophy and produced written constitutions claiming natural rights and popular sovereignty. But the U.S. case achieved greater political stability over the nineteenth century (despite the slavery contradiction that required a civil war to resolve), while most Latin American republics experienced chronic instability, caudillo politics, and fragmentation. The difference lies partly in social structure: the U.S. had a large middle class of smallholder farmers; Latin America's society was polarized between creole landowning elites and a mass of mestizo, indigenous, and formerly enslaved people excluded from political participation. The presence of much larger non-elite populations (80–90% vs. smaller proportions in the U.S. case) made governance more contested and social demands more explosive.

Practice Prompt

Answer all three parts of the following SAQ:

A. Explain how Napoleon's invasion of Spain in 1808 created the conditions for Latin American independence movements. What was the 'legitimacy vacuum' it produced?

B. Explain why the concept of 'creole nationalism' helps explain the outcomes of Latin American independence. Why did independence not produce social equality for mestizo, indigenous, and formerly enslaved populations?

C. Using the example of Mexico, explain how the independence movement's initial popular character (Hidalgo's Grito de Dolores) was transformed into a conservative creole outcome (Iturbide's compromise of 1821).
