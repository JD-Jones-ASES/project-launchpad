Study Guide: The Atlantic Slave Trade and the Middle Passage

AP World History — Unit 4: Transoceanic Interconnections, 1450–1750

Key Concepts

  • Qualitative difference from earlier slavery: The Atlantic slave trade was not simply a larger version of earlier slaveries. It introduced hereditary, racialized, permanent chattel status on an industrial scale — characteristics absent from most earlier systems, which were based on debt, war captivity, or criminal punishment. The racial coding of slavery was new and consequential.

  • Labor crisis as proximate cause: The demographic collapse of indigenous American populations (Section 4.2) created an acute labor shortage for European planters. Sugar, tobacco, cotton, and silver required vast, year-round workforces. African enslaved labor was chosen for interconnected reasons: geographic accessibility of West Africa, existing African slave-supply chains through political elites, and partial immunity to tropical diseases.

  • The triangular trade circuit: European manufactured goods (textiles, firearms, iron goods) → West African coast; enslaved people → Americas (Middle Passage); sugar, tobacco, and cotton → Europe. Understanding the three legs is essential for explaining the economic logic of the entire system.

  • The Middle Passage as defining trauma: The six-to-eight-week Atlantic crossing was characterized by extreme overcrowding, disease, dehydration, and violence; mortality ranged from 10–20%. Survivors arrived stripped of languages, names, and kinship ties. The Middle Passage was not an incidental feature of the trade but its constitutive horror.

  • Transformation of West Africa: The slave trade was as consequential for West Africa as for the Americas. It incentivized political fragmentation and wars of capture; states that participated received firearms and military advantage over those that did not. Regions like Dahomey, Asante, and Ndongo reorganized their political economies around slave raiding. Africa's demographic loss was through forced emigration, concentrated in the most productive age cohort.

  • African diaspora cultural creativity: Under conditions of extreme violence and deprivation, enslaved Africans created hybrid cultural forms — Creole languages, syncretic religions (Candomblé, Vodou), music, martial arts (Capoeira) — that were neither purely African nor purely European. This creativity was a form of cultural resistance and identity preservation.

Vocabulary Review

  • Middle Passage: The forced Atlantic crossing from West Africa to the Americas; mortality 10–20%; survivors stripped of cultural identity markers.

  • triangular trade: The three-leg Atlantic circuit: manufactured goods → West Africa → enslaved people → Americas → plantation products → Europe.

  • chattel slavery: Legal ownership of persons as movable property; the enslaved can be bought, sold, and inherited. Characterized the Atlantic system and distinguished it from most earlier forms of servitude.

  • hereditary racial status: The defining innovation of Atlantic slavery: enslaved status transmitted from parent to child regardless of the child's personal circumstances, linked permanently to African descent.

  • Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database: Digital scholarly resource (slavevoyages.org) documenting ~36,000 voyages and 12.5 million enslaved people transported.

  • Bartolomé de las Casas (1484–1566): Dominican friar, former encomendero turned indigenous advocate; authored Brevísima relación (1542) documenting Spanish violence against indigenous Americans.

  • Brevísima relación de la destrucción de las Indias (1542): Las Casas's account of colonial atrocities on Hispaniola and elsewhere, addressed to King Charles I; a primary source requiring careful contextual analysis.

  • Candomblé / Vodou: Syncretic Afro-diasporic religions blending West African spiritual traditions with Catholic iconography; developed in Brazil and Haiti respectively.

  • Creole languages: Hybrid linguistic systems combining European grammar with African vocabulary and phonology; emerged throughout the Caribbean and coastal South America.

  • Capoeira: An Afro-Brazilian martial art combining fighting and dance; developed among enslaved Africans in Brazil as a culturally coded form of resistance and identity.

Key Figures and Events

  • Bartolomé de las Casas (1484–1566): Former encomendero whose religious conversion led him to advocate for indigenous peoples before the Spanish crown; his Brevísima relación (1542) remains the most influential colonial-era critique.

  • Portuguese Atlantic islands (Madeira, Azores, São Tomé): Sites of the earliest articulation of African enslaved labor with sugar monoculture — the model exported to Brazil and the Caribbean.

  • Dahomey, Asante, Ndongo: West and Central African states whose political economies were reorganized by participation in the Atlantic slave trade as suppliers; each was transformed by the trade's incentive structures.

  • Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database: The primary quantitative source for scale estimates: ~36,000 voyages, 12.5 million transported, 10.7 million survivors.

  • New Laws of 1542: Spanish crown legislation attempting to restrict the encomienda system (discussed further in Section 4.4); one factor that increased demand for enslaved African labor as indigenous labor became legally constrained.

Themes

Social Hierarchies and Race (SPICE-T: S) The Atlantic slave trade created a new social architecture built on hereditary racial status — the idea that African descent determined permanent, inheritable unfreedom. This was not a natural category; it was an administrative and legal construct that emerged from the specific economic demands of plantation agriculture and the demographics of colonial labor shortage. The racial coding of labor that the Atlantic system introduced left legal and social legacies that structured the politics of the United States, Brazil, and the Caribbean for centuries after emancipation.

Economic Structures and Transformation (SPICE-T: E) The slave trade was an economic system before it was a moral crisis (though it was always both). The triangular circuit organized the first truly Atlantic economy: European manufactured goods, African labor, and American plantation products moved in a circuit that generated profit at every node. West African states and merchants were themselves economic actors within this system, not passive victims — participation was driven by competition for the military advantage that firearms provided. Understanding the economic logic does not diminish the moral horror; it explains why the system persisted for four centuries despite sustained critique from figures like Las Casas.

Compare and Contrast

Compare the demographic consequences of the Atlantic slave trade for West Africa and for the Americas. Both regions experienced significant demographic harm, but through different mechanisms. The Americas experienced explosive epidemic disease mortality — a biological event that preceded and enabled colonial control. West Africa experienced demographic drain through forced emigration: the loss was concentrated in the most economically productive age cohort (young adults), with men disproportionately exported to Atlantic markets, disrupting agricultural production and social organization. Unlike the Americas, West Africa was not devastated by introduced diseases; it was structurally reorganized by the trade's political incentives. In both regions, the slave trade is not merely a background condition — it is the central organizing fact of the period.

Short Answer Question (SAQ)

Use the following excerpt from Bartolomé de las Casas (1542) to answer parts A, B, and C:

"The reason why the Christians have killed and destroyed such an infinite number of souls is that they have been moved by their wish for gold and their desire to enrich themselves in a very short time."

Part A: Briefly explain ONE way in which Las Casas's position as a former encomendero shapes what he reports in this excerpt and what he omits.

Part B: Briefly explain ONE historically significant cause of indigenous population decline in the Americas that Las Casas's explanation does not address.

Part C: Briefly explain ONE way in which the demographic collapse of indigenous American populations shaped the development of the Atlantic slave trade in the sixteenth century.
