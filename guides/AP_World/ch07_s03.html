Study Guide: World War I — Total War and Aftermath

AP World History — Topic 7.3

Key Concepts

  • Total War: WWI introduced a new kind of conflict in which the distinction between soldier and civilian nearly dissolved. Factory workers, farmers, and financiers were as essential as soldiers; governments conscripted armies, seized control of industries, imposed rationing, and targeted enemy economies through naval blockade. Understanding total war is essential for analyzing both WWI and WWII.

  • The Western Front Stalemate: The Schlieffen Plan failed at the First Battle of the Marne (September 1914), and both sides dug into trenches stretching 700 km from Belgium to Switzerland. The stalemate persisted for three and a half years because defensive weapons (machine gun, barbed wire, artillery) had advanced far beyond offensive tactics' capacity to overcome them — producing catastrophic battles like the Somme and Verdun for minimal territorial gain.

  • Mobilization Beyond Europe: The Western Front narrative obscures WWI's global reach. The Eastern Front, Gallipoli, the East Africa campaign, and colonial theaters all brought devastating violence to non-European populations. The East Africa campaign alone killed approximately 200,000 Africans; Gallipoli became a defining national experience for Australia and New Zealand.

  • The Broken Promise of Versailles: The Versailles settlement embodied a fundamental contradiction. It proclaimed self-determination while applying it selectively — primarily to white European nations — while maintaining or expanding European colonial control over Asia, Africa, and the Middle East. This created two sets of grievances: nationalist resentment in Germany (exploited by Hitler) and anti-colonial resentment among colonized peoples (fueling decolonization movements).

  • Wilson's Fourteen Points: Widely circulated as Allied propaganda in 1918, the Fourteen Points raised expectations — in Germany, among Arab nationalists, among Korean delegates, among Vietnamese petitioners — that the actual settlement betrayed. The gap between Point V's promise of impartial colonial adjustment and Versailles' actual mandate system is one of the AP exam's most important causal arguments.

  • Social Consequences of Total War: Total war opened previously closed economic sectors to women across all belligerent nations. The link between women's wartime industrial labor and the expansion of voting rights (Britain, 1918/1928) illustrates how military mobilization can drive social and political transformation beyond the battlefield.

Vocabulary Review

  • Total war: Warfare requiring mobilization of a state's entire economy, society, and political apparatus; civilians become participants and targets.

  • Western Front: The 700-km trench system from Belgium to Switzerland; the site of WWI's bloodiest battles and longest stalemate.

  • Battle of the Somme: July–November 1916; 57,470 British casualties on day one; ~1.2 million total casualties; epitomizes industrial warfare's human cost.

  • Battle of Verdun: February–December 1916; ~700,000 Franco-German casualties; "Ils ne passeront pas" — symbol of defensive attrition warfare.

  • Fourteen Points: Wilson's January 1918 framework for a just peace: self-determination, open diplomacy, freedom of the seas, League of Nations.

  • War guilt clause (Article 231): Versailles clause assigning sole responsibility to Germany; legal basis for reparations; fueled German nationalist resentment.

  • Reparations: Germany's financial liability set at 132 billion gold marks in 1921 — a source of economic instability and nationalist grievance in the Weimar Republic.

  • Sykes-Picot Agreement: Secret Franco-British division of former Ottoman Arab territories into mandates, betraying Arab independence promises and creating artificial borders.

  • League of Nations: Wilson's proposed collective-security organization; created but weakened by US non-membership; failed to contain 1930s aggression.

  • Gallipoli (1915): Failed Allied amphibious campaign to knock the Ottomans out of the war; 500,000+ casualties; defining experience for Australia and New Zealand (ANZAC).

  • Mandate system: The League of Nations framework through which Britain and France administered former Ottoman and German colonial territories — colonialism by another name.

  • Korean Samil Movement (1919): Korean independence movement triggered by Versailles' failure to apply Point V's self-determination promise to colonial Korea.

Key Figures and Events

  • Woodrow Wilson: US president who articulated the Fourteen Points; arrived at Paris with a vision of a just peace; the resulting treaty disappointed German and colonial expectations alike.
  • Georges Clemenceau: French PM who demanded harsh terms against Germany; shaped the punitive elements of Versailles.
  • Ho Chi Minh: Vietnamese nationalist who petitioned the Paris Peace Conference for self-determination; was ignored; later led the Vietnamese independence movement.
  • Paul von Lettow-Vorbeck: German commander in East Africa whose guerrilla campaign with African Askari soldiers tied down far larger Allied forces until after the Armistice.
  • First Battle of the Marne (September 1914): Halted the Schlieffen Plan and forced both sides into the trench stalemate.
  • Arab Revolt and Hussein-McMahon Correspondence: Arab nationalists assisted Britain against the Ottomans in exchange for promises of independence; those promises were betrayed by Sykes-Picot.

Themes

Political: The Versailles settlement was politically contradictory in ways that shaped the next quarter century of global history. The selective application of self-determination — applied to Europeans while denied to colonized peoples — established two distinct political fault lines: in Europe, German nationalist resentment of a "diktat" peace; globally, anti-colonial nationalist movements among peoples who had been promised and denied self-determination. Both fault lines contributed to the conflicts of 1939–1945.

Social: Total war's mobilization of women into industrial workforces illustrates how military-driven economic change can produce social and political transformation. The suffrage connection is particularly significant: women's political rights advanced not through abstract arguments about equality but through the concrete, visible reality of women's wartime economic contribution. This dynamic — military mobilization as a driver of social change — recurs throughout the twentieth century.

Compare and Contrast

The treatment of European and non-European peoples at the 1919 Paris Peace Conference reveals the racial hierarchy embedded in the "self-determination" principle. In Europe, new states were carved from the former Habsburg, Ottoman, and Romanov empires for Poles, Czechs, Slovaks, Serbs, and others who met the ethnic-nationalist criteria. But in Asia and Africa, the same principle was quietly shelved: Arab nationalists who had fought alongside the Allies received mandates instead of independence; Korean delegates were dismissed; Vietnamese petitioners were ignored. The one case of consistent application was Europe; the one case of consistent non-application was the non-European world. This contrast is central to understanding why the post-WWI settlement generated anti-colonial movements that would reshape the world from the 1940s onward.

Practice Prompt

Short Answer Question (SAQ)

Use the following excerpt from Wilson's Fourteen Points (1918), Point V: "A free, open-minded, and absolutely impartial adjustment of all colonial claims, based upon a strict observance of the principle that in determining all such questions of sovereignty the interests of the populations concerned must have equal weight with the equitable claims of the government whose title is to be determined."

(A) Briefly explain ONE specific way in which the Versailles settlement contradicted the promise of Point V for a non-European people. Be specific.

(B) Explain ONE reason why the selective application of self-determination created long-term political instability in either the Middle East or East Asia.

(C) Explain how the concept of total war — specifically its economic mobilization dimension — contributed to one social or political change in a belligerent country during or after WWI.
