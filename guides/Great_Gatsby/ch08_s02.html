Study Guide: Chapter 7: Literary Analysis

Key Concepts

  • Chapter 7 functions as both the novel's climax (Plaza Hotel confrontation) and its peripeteia — the moment of tragic reversal
  • The oppressive summer heat operates as both an atmospheric device and a symbol of mounting pressure and moral collapse
  • The confrontation between Gatsby and Tom is fundamentally about class, not love
  • Tom wins the confrontation through the "gravitational weight" of his social position and inherited legitimacy, not superior virtue
  • Tom and Gatsby function as foils: both are morally compromised, but Tom's old money insulates him from consequences
  • The theme of carelessness — introduced here, made explicit by Nick later — governs the chapter's final movement
  • Dramatic irony: Gatsby believes he is protecting Daisy, but Nick sees through the window that she has already returned to Tom
  • Fitzgerald's use of the Plaza Hotel setting (Wedding March drifting up, whiskey sweating on the table) reinforces the sense of a world about to crack

Summary

Chapter 7 is the structural and thematic center of the novel, engineered with extraordinary narrative control. Every detail of the Plaza Hotel scene — the stifling heat, the Wedding March rising from the ballroom below, the characters' physical discomfort — contributes to a sense of imminent collapse. Fitzgerald uses this pressure to expose what the Gatsby-Daisy-Tom triangle is really about: not romantic rivalry but class warfare.

Gatsby's fatal mistake is believing that Daisy can simply erase five years of history by declaring she never loved Tom. But Tom wins not through greater love or virtue — he wins because his social position carries an institutional weight that Gatsby's self-invented identity cannot match. When Tom exposes Gatsby's bootlegging and his association with Meyer Wolfsheim, he destroys the fiction of respectability that Gatsby has carefully constructed. Old money, the novel argues, has a capacity to absorb scandal and maintain legitimacy that new money — however lavish — can never acquire.

The chapter's final movement extends this analysis through the theme of carelessness. Daisy kills Myrtle while driving Gatsby's car, then retreats into the safety of her marriage. Tom deflects Wilson's grief and rage toward Gatsby. Neither Buchanan faces any consequence. Meanwhile, Gatsby stands outside in the moonlight, prepared to take the blame for a crime he did not commit, guarding a woman who is already sitting at the kitchen table with her husband. The dramatic irony is crushing: Gatsby thinks he is protecting a love worth protecting. Nick, peering through the window, sees only two people conspiring.

Key Terms

  • Climax: The moment of highest dramatic tension; in Chapter 7, the Plaza Hotel confrontation
  • Peripeteia: The tragic reversal of fortune; the moment when Gatsby's dream becomes irretrievable
  • Foil: A character who contrasts with another to highlight qualities; Tom serves as Gatsby's foil — both morally compromised, but differently positioned by class
  • Dramatic irony: When the audience (or narrator) understands something a character does not; Nick sees that Gatsby's vigil protects nothing
  • Carelessness: Nick's moral verdict on the Buchanans — their wealth insulates them from consequences, enabling destruction without responsibility
  • Old money vs. new money: The distinction between inherited wealth (East Egg, Tom) and recently acquired wealth (West Egg, Gatsby) — a class barrier Gatsby cannot cross regardless of his fortune

Review Questions

  1. How does Fitzgerald use the summer heat as more than just weather in Chapter 7?
  2. Why does Tom ultimately win the Plaza Hotel confrontation, even though his character is no more admirable than Gatsby's?
  3. In what sense is the confrontation between Tom and Gatsby really a contest about class rather than love?
  4. How does the concept of carelessness, as applied to Tom and Daisy, explain their behavior in the aftermath of Myrtle's death?
  5. What is the dramatic irony of Gatsby's vigil, and how does it relate to the novel's broader theme of illusion versus reality?
