Study Guide: The True History of Jay Gatsby

Overview

This section peels back the layers of mystery surrounding Jay Gatsby, revealing his humble origins as James Gatz from North Dakota. It details his profound transformation at age seventeen when he encountered the millionaire Dan Cody, who became his mentor and introduced him to the world of wealth and sophistication. Although Gatsby was ultimately cheated out of an inheritance, his time with Cody provided him with an invaluable education in self-presentation and ambition, foundational to the persona of the enigmatic Jay Gatsby.

Key Concepts

  • Self-Invention and the "Platonic Conception": Gatsby's radical act of rejecting his birth name, parents, and working-class background to create an idealized version of himself—Jay Gatsby. This concept emphasizes the power of ambition and imagination in shaping identity, aligning with a quintessential American ideal of starting anew.
  • The American Dream and Social Mobility: James Gatz's relentless pursuit of a grander future, leaving his humble origins behind to seek wealth and status. His journey with Dan Cody represents an attempt to shortcut or learn the ropes of the upper class, embodying a yearning for upward mobility often associated with the American Dream.
  • Mentorship and Education Beyond Institutions: Dan Cody served as Gatsby's pivotal mentor, offering him an "education" not found in traditional colleges. Gatsby gained practical knowledge of high society, business, and the lifestyle of the wealthy, which proved more valuable than any monetary inheritance.
  • The Impact of the Past on Identity: While Gatsby attempts to completely shed his past, his origins as James Gatz deeply inform his motivations and the construction of his new identity. The desire to escape poverty and insignify is a powerful engine for his ambition.
  • Narrative Structure and Reader Understanding: Nick Carraway's deliberate choice to reveal Gatsby's true history out of chronological order. By placing this backstory here, Nick aims to clear up misconceptions and provide the reader with a deeper, more accurate understanding of Gatsby's character and motivations before the novel's climactic events.

Important Terms

  • James Gatz: Gatsby's birth name, reflecting his humble beginnings in North Dakota.
  • Dan Cody: A self-made millionaire from the Nevada silver fields, Yukon gold rush, and Montana copper. He served as Gatsby's mentor and employer for five years, educating him in the ways of the wealthy.
  • Lake Superior: The body of water where seventeen-year-old James Gatz first encountered Dan Cody's yacht, marking the beginning of his transformation into Jay Gatsby.
  • St. Olaf College: The Minnesota college James Gatz briefly attended for two weeks, leaving because he felt it was indifferent to his "destiny" and he despised the janitorial work required to pay his way.
  • Ella Kaye: The woman who, through legal maneuvering, managed to claim Dan Cody's inheritance, preventing Gatsby from receiving the $25,000 Cody had left him.
  • "Platonic Conception of Himself": A phrase used by Fitzgerald to describe Gatsby's profound act of self-creation, where he rejected his actual origins to embrace an idealized, self-imagined identity.
  • Tuolomee: The name of Dan Cody's yacht, aboard which Gatsby spent five years learning about wealth and high society.

Detailed Notes

The Unveiling of James Gatz

  • True Identity: Jay Gatsby's real name was James Gatz, born to "unsuccessful farm people" in North Dakota.
  • Early Life: Before meeting Dan Cody, James Gatz led a humble existence along the south shore of Lake Superior, working as a clam-digger and salmon-fisher. He took on any labor to secure food and shelter.
  • Aspiration and Discontent: Gatz harbored grand visions for his future, which clashed with his current circumstances. He briefly attended St. Olaf College in Minnesota but left after just two weeks. He found the college "dismayed at its ferocious indifference to the drums of his destiny" and scorned the janitor's work he had to do. He was actively searching for a path to the extraordinary future he envisioned for himself.
  • The Moment of Transformation: At the age of seventeen, Gatz seized an opportunity on Lake Superior. He rowed out to Dan Cody's yacht to warn him about dangerous winds. This precise moment marked the symbolic "beginning of his career" and his decision to construct a new identity: Jay Gatsby.

Dan Cody: Mentor and Gateway to Wealth

  • Who Was Dan Cody? Cody was a robust, fifty-year-old millionaire who amassed his fortune from the Nevada silver fields, the Yukon gold rush, and Montana copper. His wealth was built on pioneering, rough-and-tumble endeavors.
  • Gatsby's Role on the Yacht: For five years, Gatsby served aboard Cody's yacht, the Tuolomee, in various capacities:
    • Steward and Mate: Performing ship duties.
    • Skipper and Secretary: Managing the vessel and Cody's affairs.
    • "Jailor": Protecting the often-drunken Cody from his own excesses and ensuring his safety.
  • Gatsby's Education: Life aboard Cody's yacht provided Gatsby with an invaluable, practical education. He observed and absorbed the manners, appearances, and lifestyle of the wealthy. This experience was crucial in shaping the sophisticated persona he would later adopt as Jay Gatsby. He learned about luxury, the world of finance (though not directly involved), and the social etiquette of the upper class.

The Lost Inheritance and Its True Value

  • Cody's Will: Dan Cody had intended to leave Gatsby a legacy of twenty-five thousand dollars, recognizing his dedication and potential.
  • Ella Kaye's Interference: Gatsby never received this money. A woman named Ella Kaye used a complex "legal device" (which Gatsby never fully understood) to claim what remained of Cody's millions, effectively disinheriting Gatsby.
  • The "Singularly Appropriate Education": Despite losing the money, Gatsby gained something far more significant: his "singularly appropriate education." This encompassed the knowledge, bearing, ambition, and understanding of the wealthy world that allowed him to meticulously construct and maintain the elaborate persona of Jay Gatsby. This mentorship provided him with the foundation for his future endeavors and the image he presented to the world.

Nick's Narrative Choices

  • Non-Chronological Revelation: Nick Carraway explicitly states that he learned this backstory "very much later" but chose to "put it down here" to clarify misconceptions about Gatsby.
  • Effect on the Reader: By revealing Gatsby's true origins at this point in the novel, Nick provides crucial context for Gatsby's immense wealth, his elaborate parties, and his relentless pursuit of Daisy. It transforms Gatsby from a mysterious, perhaps even shady, figure into a determined, self-made man driven by a profound, if misguided, idealism. This allows the reader to understand the depth of Gatsby's aspirations and the carefully constructed nature of his entire existence.

Review Questions

  1. How does Gatsby's decision to change his name from James Gatz to Jay Gatsby exemplify the American idea of "self-invention"? What does this act reveal about his character?
  2. In what ways was Dan Cody's mentorship more valuable to Gatsby than the $25,000 inheritance he lost? Discuss the specific lessons Gatsby learned.
  3. Fitzgerald writes that Gatsby “sprang from his Platonic conception of himself.” What does this phrase mean in the context of Gatsby's origins, and how does it contrast with his actual family background?
  4. Why might Nick Carraway choose to reveal Gatsby's true history and origins at this particular point in the narrative, rather than earlier or later? What impact does this have on the reader's perception of Gatsby?
  5. How do Gatsby's experiences with Dan Cody and his eventual disinheritance foreshadow or explain his later pursuit of wealth and his methods for acquiring it?
