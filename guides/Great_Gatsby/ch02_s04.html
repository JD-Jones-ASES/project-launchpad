Chapter 2, Section 4: CH02_TEXT_B Study Guide

Overview

This section details a raucous and increasingly chaotic party held in Tom and Myrtle Wilson's New York City apartment, attended by Nick Carraway, Tom Buchanan, Myrtle, her sister Catherine, and Mr. and Mrs. McKee. The gathering quickly devolves into a drunken display of social climbing, gossip, marital dissatisfaction, and ultimately, shocking violence, providing Nick with a disturbing glimpse into the moral decay beneath the glittering surface of 1920s society.

Key Concepts

  • Social Climbing and Materialism: Myrtle Wilson's character exemplifies a desperate desire to ascend the social ladder. She rejects her humble origins and her husband, George Wilson, for the perceived glamour and wealth offered by Tom Buchanan. Her elaborate shopping list and disdain for the "lower orders" highlight her materialistic aspirations and superficial understanding of class.
  • Adultery and Marital Dissatisfaction: The core of the section is the illicit affair between Tom and Myrtle, and the deep unhappiness both feel in their respective marriages. Catherine's candid observations about Tom and Myrtle's disdain for their spouses, combined with Myrtle's regret about marrying George, underscore the theme of broken vows and the pursuit of superficial gratification outside of marriage.
  • The Brutality of Wealth and Power: Tom Buchanan's character is portrayed as arrogant and physically imposing. His casual infidelity and ultimate act of striking Myrtle with brutal force for merely mentioning Daisy's name reveal a dark side to his privileged power and a disregard for human dignity, highlighting the destructive potential of unchecked wealth.
  • Nick's Role as Observer and Outsider: Nick Carraway narrates the events, frequently expressing his discomfort and moral unease. He feels "within and without," simultaneously drawn to and repelled by the "inexhaustible variety of life" presented by the party. His detachment allows him to critically observe the moral failings of the group, yet he struggles to fully extricate himself.
  • Deception and Superficiality: The characters engage in various forms of deception, from Catherine's fabricated story about Daisy's Catholicism to justify Tom and Myrtle's affair, to Myrtle's attempts to portray herself as sophisticated. The entire atmosphere of the party, with its forced gaiety and pretentious conversations, underscores a pervasive superficiality that masks deeper unhappiness and moral emptiness.

Important Terms

  • Mrs. Eberhardt: A fictional character mentioned by Mrs. McKee as a foot doctor who makes house calls. Her mention highlights the mundane details that contrast with the extravagant aspirations of the characters.
  • Montauk Point—The Gulls / Montauk Point—The Sea: Titles of two of Mr. McKee's pretentious photographic "studies" of Long Island. These titles reflect his attempt to present himself as an artist of significance, despite his seemingly unremarkable talent.
  • Kaiser Wilhelm: The last German Emperor and King of Prussia, notorious for his role in World War I. Catherine's rumor that Gatsby is his "nephew or cousin" and that this is the source of his money, hints at the speculative and often unsavory origins attributed to new wealth, especially in the context of the anti-German sentiment post-WWI.
  • Catholic (as a reason for divorce): Catherine falsely claims that Daisy Buchanan is Catholic and therefore does not believe in divorce, preventing Tom from marrying Myrtle. This lie highlights the characters' willingness to invent elaborate justifications for their immoral behavior.
  • Monte Carlo: A luxurious resort city in Monaco, known for its casinos. Catherine's brief and ill-fated trip there underscores the characters' superficial pursuit of excitement and wealth, often leading to disappointment or loss.
  • "Kyke": An offensive and derogatory term for a Jewish person, used by Mrs. McKee when recounting a past suitor. Her use of this term exposes her prejudiced and class-conscious mindset, reinforcing the underlying snobbery and bigotry present in the social circles depicted.
  • "You can't live forever": Myrtle Wilson's desperate mantra, repeated when she recounts meeting Tom. It serves as her justification for embracing the illicit affair and chasing immediate pleasure and material gains, reflecting a carpe diem attitude that ignores moral consequences.
  • TOWN TATTLE: A fictional gossip magazine, representative of the tabloid culture of 1920s New York that fed on scandal and celebrity. Myrtle attempts to use a copy of it to cover her bleeding nose after Tom assaults her, symbolizing the public's appetite for sensationalism and the characters' desperate attempts to cover up their messy lives.

Detailed Notes

The Setting and Initial Impressions

The party takes place in a small, crowded apartment in New York City, which serves as a secret rendezvous point for Tom and Myrtle's affair. The apartment's confined space and artificial atmosphere contrast sharply with the expansive, opulent homes of West Egg and East Egg. Nick is immediately struck by the pretentiousness and superficiality of the other guests, Mr. and Mrs. McKee. Conversation begins with trivialities, quickly revealing the characters' self-absorption and social aspirations.

Character Dynamics and Emerging Tensions

  • Myrtle Wilson's Transformation: Myrtle, in her "adorable" dress, attempts to project an air of sophistication and disdain for her humble origins. She rejects compliments while fishing for more, expresses contempt for "the lower orders," and dreams of an extravagant life. Her narration of meeting Tom reveals her attraction to his wealth and social standing, seeing him as an escape from her marriage to George Wilson.
  • The McKees' Artistic and Social Ambitions: Mr. McKee is a photographer who takes himself seriously, boasting about his "studies" of Montauk Point. His wife, Mrs. McKee, is flighty and gossipy, constantly trying to elevate her husband's status and assert her own. Her story about almost marrying a "kyke" reveals her deep-seated prejudices and concern with social class.
  • Catherine's Candid Cynicism: Myrtle's sister, Catherine, is a source of gossip and cynical observations. She bluntly tells Nick that neither Tom nor Myrtle can "stand the person they're married to" and suggests they should divorce and marry each other. Her elaborate lie about Daisy being Catholic (to explain why Tom can't divorce her) highlights the characters' casual disregard for truth and their willingness to invent narratives to suit their desires. Her anecdote about losing money in Monte Carlo further illustrates the reckless pursuit of pleasure among the group.
  • Tom Buchanan's Dominance: Tom maintains his aggressive, dominant demeanor throughout the party. He dismisses Mr. McKee's artistic endeavors and rudely orders Myrtle to fetch more ice and mineral water. His control over the situation is evident until the climax of the scene.
  • Nick's Growing Discomfort: As the alcohol flows and the party grows increasingly chaotic, Nick becomes more uncomfortable. He tries to leave multiple times but is "entangled" in arguments, pulled back into the fray. His internal monologue ("I was within and without, simultaneously enchanted and repelled by the inexhaustible variety of life") captures his dual role as an observer and a participant, highlighting his moral ambiguity and the allure of this corrupt world.

Myrtle's Marital Regrets and the Lie

Myrtle reveals the superficial reasons she married George Wilson, believing he was a "gentleman" with "breeding," only to discover he borrowed a suit for their wedding. This deeply embarrassed her and solidified her disdain for him, painting a picture of her desperate longing for a higher social status, even at the cost of genuine affection. Her fierce denial of ever being "crazy about him" contradicts Catherine's recollection, further exposing the volatile and unreliable nature of her emotions and memories.

The Climax: Tom's Brutal Act

The party reaches its shocking peak when Myrtle, emboldened by alcohol and her newfound assertiveness, repeatedly taunts Tom by shouting "Daisy!" Tom, enraged by this blatant transgression of unspoken boundaries, suddenly and violently breaks her nose with his open hand. This act reveals the underlying brutality in Tom's character and the harsh consequences of challenging his control, especially regarding his public image and family life.

The Aftermath and Nick's Escape

The party descends into immediate chaos, with women scolding and consoling Myrtle amidst her pain. Mr. McKee, waking from his drunken doze, observes the scene in a daze before leaving. Nick, taking his hat, follows him out, both metaphorically and literally distancing himself from the moral squalor. His final descent in the elevator with McKee and his subsequent semi-conscious state in Pennsylvania Station emphasize his desire to escape the overwhelming and disturbing events, leaving him reflective and weary. His scattered thoughts about McKee's photographic subjects underscore the fragmented and distorted reality he has just witnessed.

Review Questions

  1. How does the atmosphere of the New York City apartment party differ from the parties at Gatsby's mansion or the gatherings at the Buchanans' home? What does this contrast suggest about the social circles depicted?
  2. Analyze Myrtle Wilson's motivations for her affair with Tom Buchanan and her feelings towards her husband, George Wilson. To what extent is she a victim of her circumstances, and to what extent is she an active participant in her own choices?
  3. Discuss the role of deception and illusion in this section. Provide specific examples (e.g., Catherine's lie about Daisy, Myrtle's aspirations) and explain how they contribute to the portrayal of the characters and their moral landscape.
  4. How does Tom Buchanan's violence towards Myrtle reveal aspects of his character and the power dynamics within the affair? What is the significance of the specific trigger for his violent outburst?
  5. Reflect on Nick Carraway's internal struggle and his feeling of being "within and without" during the party. How does his perspective shape the reader's understanding of the events, and what does this suggest about his own moral compass?
