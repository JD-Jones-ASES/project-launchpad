Chapter 3, Section 3: Gatsby's Party - A Study Guide

Overview

This section vividly introduces Gatsby's legendary, lavish parties through Nick Carraway's eyes, detailing their extravagant scale, the eclectic and often uninvited guests, and the air of careless revelry. Nick, uniquely invited, navigates this chaotic social landscape, encountering Jordan Baker and overhearing the many wild rumors that circulate about his enigmatic host, Gatsby, whom he has yet to meet. The section culminates in a telling discovery within Gatsby's library, hinting at the constructed nature of his persona.

Key Concepts

  • Lavish Extravagance: Gatsby's parties are presented as spectacles of immense wealth and excess, featuring large orchestras, endless food and drink, and a multitude of servants. This highlights the opulent and sometimes wasteful nature of the Jazz Age elite.
  • Anonymous Guest Culture: Most guests are uninvited, arriving simply because others do, treating Gatsby's estate like an amusement park. They often don't know Gatsby, and introductions among guests are fleeting, emphasizing a superficial and transient social environment.
  • The Elusive Host: Despite hosting these grand affairs, Gatsby himself remains largely unseen and unheard by many of his guests, fueling the mystique and wild speculation about his identity and past.
  • Nick's Outsider Perspective: Nick is initially uncomfortable and out of place amidst the revelry. His unique invitation and attempts to find Gatsby set him apart from the anonymous crowd, positioning him as an observer who seeks genuine connection or understanding.
  • Rumors and Speculation: A significant part of Gatsby's public image is built on conflicting rumors about his past—from being a murderer to a German spy or an American soldier. These rumors underscore the lack of concrete information about him and the public's fascination with his mysterious wealth.
  • Appearance vs. Reality: The discovery of "real" books in Gatsby's library by the "Owl-Eyed" man is a pivotal moment, suggesting that even Gatsby's elaborate "sets" might contain elements of genuine substance, or at least a meticulous effort to appear genuine.
  • East Egg vs. West Egg Social Dynamics: The brief interaction with the "dignified homogeneity" of the East Egg party illustrates the condescension and guarded nature of old money towards the "spectroscopic gayety" of West Egg's new wealth.

Important Terms

  • Cataracts: (Figurative) Large waterfalls or sudden downpours of water; here used figuratively to describe an overwhelming flow of foam from motor-boats, suggesting the powerful and uncontrolled nature of Gatsby's activities.
  • Innuendo: An indirect or subtle reference, especially one made in a derogatory or suggestive way; an insinuation. Used to describe the casual, often gossipy, conversations at the party.
  • Prodigality: Reckless extravagance or wastefulness in the use of resources; lavish generosity. Describes the unrestrained laughter and spending at Gatsby's parties.
  • Erroneous: Wrong; incorrect; based on false information or faulty reasoning. Refers to the inaccurate news circulating about a dancer at the party.
  • Credulity: A tendency to be too ready to believe that something is real or true; gullibility. Highlighted when guests switch between believing conflicting rumors about Gatsby.
  • Spectroscopic: (Figurative) Relating to the spectroscope, an instrument used to analyze light by separating it into its component wavelengths. Here used figuratively to suggest intense, precise scrutiny, indicating how East Egg observes West Egg's vibrant parties.
  • Belasco: David Belasco (1853–1931), a famous American theatrical producer known for his elaborate, hyper-realistic stage sets. The comparison to Gatsby's library suggests his careful construction of an image or a "set."

Detailed Notes

The Spectacle of Gatsby's Parties

  • Setting: Held at Gatsby's mansion in West Egg, described with "blue gardens" and guests coming and going "like moths."
  • Activities: Guests engage in diving from a raft, sunbathing, aquaplaning behind motor-boats. On weekends, a Rolls-Royce acts as an omnibus to and from the city, and a station wagon meets trains, indicating an open-door policy.
  • Post-Party Cleanup: Eight servants, including an extra gardener, spend all day Monday "repairing the ravages of the night before," demonstrating the sheer scale of the mess and Gatsby's resources.
  • Food and Drink:
    • Five crates of oranges and lemons arrive weekly, processed by a machine that extracts juice from 200 oranges in half an hour.
    • Fortnightly, a corps of caterers arrives with canvas, lights, and buffet tables laden with "glistening hors-d'oeuvre," "spiced baked hams," "salads of harlequin designs," and "pastry pigs and turkeys."
    • A fully stocked bar with a brass rail offers gins, liquors, and forgotten cordials.
  • Music and Atmosphere:
    • A large orchestra plays "yellow cocktail music."
    • The air is filled with "chatter and laughter," "casual innuendo," and fleeting introductions.
    • Groups of people constantly change and dissolve, with "confident girls" weaving through the crowd.
    • A spontaneous dance by a woman believed to be Gilda Gray's understudy from the Follies marks the official "beginning" of the party.

The Nature of Gatsby's Guests

  • Uninvited Access: Most guests were not formally invited; they "went there" after hearing about the parties, ending up at Gatsby's door through various means.
  • Anonymous Behavior: Guests are described as conducting themselves "according to the rules of behavior associated with amusement parks," implying a lack of formality or personal responsibility. Many never meet Gatsby.
  • Motivations: Some guests, particularly young Englishmen, are perceived as "selling something" (bonds, insurance, automobiles), drawn by the "easy money in the vicinity."
  • Fleeting Connections: Introductions are "forgotten on the spot," and women meet "who never knew each other's names," emphasizing the superficiality of the interactions.

Nick Carraway's Experience

  • Unique Invitation: Nick is one of the few genuinely invited guests, receiving a formal note from Gatsby via chauffeur.
  • Initial Discomfort: He feels "ill at ease" and "purposeless and alone" amidst the unfamiliar crowd, highlighting his difference from the other guests.
  • Search for Gatsby: Nick attempts to find his host but is met with confused stares and denials of knowledge, underscoring Gatsby's elusive nature.
  • Encounter with Jordan Baker: Nick finds refuge by attaching himself to Jordan, whom he met previously. They wander through the party, observing the scene.
  • Sitting with Strangers: They join a table with two "girls in yellow" and three anonymous men, illustrating the casual and often impersonal social dynamics.

Rumors and Speculation about Gatsby

  • Conflicting Stories: While sitting with Jordan and the girls in yellow, Nick overhears various theories about Gatsby:
    • He "killed a man once." (This creates a thrill among listeners).
    • He "was a German spy during the war." (Supported by a man claiming to have grown up with him in Germany).
    • He "was in the American army during the war." (A counter-argument).
  • Romantic Speculation: The conflicting and often sensational rumors testify to "the romantic speculation he inspired," suggesting his character is a projection of others' imaginations rather than a known entity.

Social Dynamics: East Egg vs. West Egg

  • Jordan's Party: Jordan and Nick briefly join a party consisting of three married couples and Jordan's escort (a "persistent undergraduate") described as having "dignified homogeneity."
  • East Egg Condescension: This group is characterized as representing the "staid nobility of the country-side—East Egg condescending to West Egg, and carefully on guard against its spectroscopic gayety." This highlights the social tension and judgment between old money (East Egg) and new money (West Egg).
  • Jordan's Disinterest: Jordan finds this group "much too polite" and quickly decides to leave, indicating her preference for the more unrestrained West Egg atmosphere.

The Library and the Owl-Eyed Man

  • Search for Gatsby: Jordan and Nick search for Gatsby, eventually entering a "high Gothic library" "panelled with carved English oak."
  • Owl-Eyed Man's Discovery: They find a "stout, middle-aged man" with large spectacles (dubbed "Owl-Eyes") sitting drunk amidst the bookshelves. He excitedly reveals that the books are "absolutely real—have pages and everything," expressing surprise as he expected "nice durable cardboard."
  • Belasco Allusion: Owl-Eyes compares Gatsby to "Belasco," a theatrical producer known for hyper-realistic stage sets, implying Gatsby's entire world might be an elaborate, carefully constructed illusion. The reality of the books challenges this expectation.

Review Questions

  1. Describe the atmosphere and scale of Gatsby's parties. What details does Fitzgerald use to convey the sense of extravagance and chaos?
  2. How do most guests typically attend Gatsby's parties, and what does their behavior (or lack thereof) reveal about the social norms of West Egg society?
  3. What are some of the rumors circulating about Gatsby's past? What is the significance of these conflicting stories, and what do they tell us about Gatsby's public image?
  4. Compare and contrast Nick Carraway's experience at the party with that of the other guests. How does his initial discomfort and search for Gatsby highlight his unique perspective?
  5. Explain the interaction with the "Owl-Eyed" man in the library and his discovery about Gatsby's books. What deeper meaning or commentary does this scene offer regarding Gatsby's character and the themes of appearance versus reality?
