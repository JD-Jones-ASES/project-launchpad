Study Guide: The Great Gatsby - Chapter 7, Section 1: CH07_ANALYSIS

Overview

Chapter 7 marks the dramatic climax and catastrophic turning point of The Great Gatsby. It details the heated confrontation between Gatsby and Tom over Daisy, Gatsby's ultimate defeat in his attempt to reclaim the past, and the tragic death of Myrtle Wilson. The chapter culminates in the stark revelation of the Buchanans' callous indifference and the ultimate collapse of Gatsby's romantic dream.

Key Concepts

  • Climax and Peripeteia: The Plaza Hotel confrontation is the novel's structural climax, where all the major tensions erupt. It also marks Gatsby's peripeteia, a sudden reversal of fortune, as his dream of winning Daisy's exclusive love and erasing the past irrevocably collapses.
  • Class Conflict and Old vs. New Money: The underlying struggle between Gatsby and Tom is revealed to be more about their social standing and legitimacy than merely their love for Daisy. Tom, representing established old money, effectively discredits Gatsby's self-invented identity and illicit wealth, proving the insuperable barrier of class.
  • Symbolism of Heat and Atmosphere: The oppressive summer heat serves as a powerful atmospheric device, mirroring the escalating tension, discomfort, and inevitable breakdown of relationships and illusions within the chapter.
  • Carelessness of the Wealthy: The chapter vividly illustrates the destructive carelessness of the aristocratic class, particularly through Tom and Daisy's actions. Daisy's fatal hit-and-run and the Buchanans' subsequent, seamless retreat into their shared world highlight their capacity to inflict harm without consequence or remorse.
  • The Death of Gatsby's Dream: Gatsby's desperate attempt to reconstruct the past with Daisy is shattered. His final vigil outside her house, where he believes he is protecting her, is tragically ironic, as Nick observes her "conspiring" with Tom, signifying that Gatsby is "watching over nothing."

Important Terms

  • Trimalchio: A character from Petronius's Roman novel Satyricon, a former slave who amassed immense wealth and threw extravagant, vulgar parties. Fitzgerald initially considered this title for The Great Gatsby, underscoring Gatsby's nouveau riche excess and superficiality.
  • Dilatory: Slow to act or respond; intended to cause delay. Used to describe the new butler's grudging manner, symbolizing the shift from Gatsby's open, lively parties to a more closed-off, tense atmosphere.
  • Caravansary: An inn with a central courtyard for travelers in Eastern countries. Nick uses this metaphorically to describe Gatsby's house, which, once a hub of transient, anonymous visitors, has now "fallen in like a card house," reflecting its diminished vibrancy and purpose.
  • Portentous: Of great significance; ominous or foreboding. The term is used twice: once for the "portentous chords" of Mendelssohn's Wedding March, hinting at the impending marital strife, and again for the road ahead, suggesting impending disaster.
  • Libertine: A person who is morally or sexually unrestrained. Nick uses this to describe Tom Buchanan's past behavior, highlighting his hypocrisy when he later condemns Gatsby's affair.
  • Prig: A self-righteously moralistic person. Paired with "libertine" to emphasize Tom's double standard; he transitions from a morally loose individual to one who condemns Gatsby's perceived immorality.
  • Magnanimous: Generous or forgiving, especially toward a rival or less powerful person. Tom's dismissal of Gatsby with "magnanimous scorn" is a performative display of condescension, asserting his victory and superiority.
  • Peripeteia: A sudden and often tragic reversal of fortune or change in circumstances in a dramatic plot. The confrontation at the Plaza Hotel serves as Gatsby's peripeteia, where his hopes for Daisy are dashed, leading to his ultimate downfall.
  • Contingency: An unforeseen event or circumstance that might occur. Tom is described as being pulled back from a "theoretical abyss" by "the immediate contingency" of Gatsby's direct threat to his marriage, forcing him to act decisively.
  • Expostulation: Earnest protest, reasoning, or remonstrance. The crowd at the accident scene closes up with a "running murmur of expostulation" as Tom pushes through, indicating their shock and vocal disapproval.

Detailed Notes

The Shifting Landscape at Gatsby's Mansion

  • Gatsby's Seclusion: Chapter 7 opens with a significant change at Gatsby's mansion. To facilitate Daisy's visits discreetly, Gatsby dismisses his former servants and replaces them with shady individuals connected to Meyer Wolfsheim. This signals a shift from his extravagant public persona to a more private, yet increasingly dangerous, reality.
  • A "Caravansary" No More: Nick notes that Gatsby's house, once a bustling "caravansary" of transient guests, has now "fallen in like a card house," reflecting the collapse of his carefully constructed public image and his increasing isolation.

The Hottest Day and Rising Tension

  • Atmosphere of Oppression: The entire chapter is set on the hottest day of the summer, a detail that perfectly encapsulates the escalating tension and impending explosion of suppressed emotions. The oppressive heat mirrors the characters' internal turmoil and the pressure building within their relationships.

The Tense Lunch at the Buchanans'

  • Daisy's Open Admiration: During a stifling lunch at the Buchanan's house, Daisy openly expresses her admiration for Gatsby, declaring, "You always look so cool." This seemingly innocent remark, laden with affection, immediately reveals the affair to Tom, who observes the exchange with growing suspicion.
  • Tom's Dominance: Tom, asserting his control, insists that the group drive into New York City. He symbolically swaps cars with Gatsby, driving Gatsby's yellow car himself while Gatsby, Daisy, and Nick follow in Tom's blue coupé. This gesture is a calculated display of power and contempt, challenging Gatsby's masculinity and ownership.

The Plaza Hotel Confrontation: Climax and Catastrophe

  • The Confrontation Erupts: At the Plaza Hotel, the simmering tensions explode. Gatsby, desperate to reclaim the past, demands that Daisy tell Tom she never loved him. This is his ultimate test, believing that if Daisy disavows her love for Tom, their five years apart can be erased.
  • Daisy's Betrayal and Falters: Daisy falters under the immense pressure, admitting, "I did love him once—but I loved you too." Her inability to fully commit to Gatsby and renounce Tom reveals the depth of her indecision and ultimately, her ties to the comfort and security of her old-money world.
  • Tom's Attack and Exposure: Sensing Daisy's hesitation, Tom seizes the advantage. He brutally attacks Gatsby's background, exposing his bootlegging operations and his association with criminals like Wolfsheim. Tom's weapon is the truth about Gatsby's illegitimate wealth, which directly challenges Gatsby's carefully constructed image of respectability.
  • Class as the Deciding Factor: Tom wins the confrontation not through superior virtue or love, but through the sheer "gravitational weight" of his social position and inherited wealth. Gatsby's self-invented identity and new money cannot withstand the institutional power and legitimacy of Tom's old-money world. Daisy's courage completely disintegrates, confirming the collapse of Gatsby's dream (peripeteia).

Myrtle Wilson's Death

  • A Calculated Act of Contempt: In a final act of dominance and contempt, Tom sends Daisy home with Gatsby in Gatsby's yellow car, confident that Gatsby has been defeated.
  • The Fatal Accident: On the return trip from New York, Gatsby's yellow car strikes and kills Myrtle Wilson, who had rushed into the road believing the car was Tom's. Nick later learns that Daisy was the driver.
  • Carelessness and Escape: This event epitomizes the theme of carelessness. Daisy, a symbol of the wealthy elite, causes a tragic death but immediately retreats into the safety of her marriage and social position, avoiding any immediate consequences.

Gatsby's Vigil and the Buchanans' Indifference

  • Gatsby's Illusory Protection: The chapter concludes with Gatsby standing vigil outside the Buchanan house in the moonlight, prepared to take the blame for the accident. He believes he is protecting Daisy, still clinging to the remnants of his dream.
  • The "Unmistakable Air of Natural Intimacy": Nick, observing through the pantry window, sees Tom and Daisy sitting together at the kitchen table, "conspiring," reconciled, and sharing an "unmistakable air of natural intimacy." This image devastatingly reveals their indifference to the destruction they have caused and their unbreakable bond forged in shared privilege and carelessness.
  • "Watching Over Nothing": Gatsby is, as Nick realizes, "watching over nothing"—guarding a dream that Daisy has already abandoned, a past that cannot be recovered, and a woman who has returned to the secure, if morally bankrupt, world of her husband. His vigilance is ultimately futile.

Review Questions

  1. How does the pervasive motif of "the hottest day" throughout Chapter 7 contribute to both the literal and metaphorical atmosphere of the narrative? Discuss specific instances where the heat seems to heighten the tension or symbolize an underlying conflict.
  2. Analyze the Plaza Hotel confrontation as the novel's climax. What specific demands does Gatsby make of Daisy, and why is her inability to fulfill them so devastating for his dream?
  3. Beyond the superficial conflict over Daisy, what deeper societal struggle between Tom and Gatsby is revealed during their confrontation? How does Tom ultimately "win" this struggle, and what does this suggest about the power of old money versus new money in the novel's world?
  4. Discuss the theme of "carelessness" as depicted in Chapter 7. How do the actions of Tom and Daisy, particularly after Myrtle's death, illustrate their deep-seated irresponsibility and indifference to the consequences of their behavior?
  5. Examine the chapter's final scene, with Gatsby standing vigil outside the Buchanan house and Nick observing Tom and Daisy inside. What is the dramatic irony of this moment, and what does Nick mean when he suggests Gatsby is "watching over nothing"?
