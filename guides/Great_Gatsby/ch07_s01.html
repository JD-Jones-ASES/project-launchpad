Study Guide: Chapter 6: The Novel

Key Concepts

  • Nick reveals Gatsby's true identity: born James Gatz to poor farm people in North Dakota
  • At age seventeen, James Gatz reinvented himself as "Jay Gatsby" when he encountered millionaire Dan Cody's yacht on Lake Superior
  • Gatsby served Cody for five years as steward, mate, secretary, and jailor — absorbing the manners and ambitions of wealth
  • Ella Kaye cheated Gatsby out of his $25,000 inheritance when Cody died
  • Tom Buchanan visits Gatsby's estate with Mr. Sloane and a woman; Gatsby is treated with condescension and not-so-subtle exclusion
  • Tom and Daisy attend one of Gatsby's parties; Daisy is appalled by West Egg's vulgarity, Tom grows suspicious of Gatsby's background
  • After the party, Gatsby reveals his impossible demand: Daisy must tell Tom she never loved him so they can return to Louisville and relive the past
  • Nick warns that one cannot repeat the past; Gatsby responds incredulously, "Can't repeat the past? Why of course you can!"

Summary

Chapter 6 opens with a reporter arriving at Gatsby's door, drawn by the spreading legends about the mysterious host — underground pipelines to Canada, a house that moves secretly along the shore. Nick uses this moment to deliver the truth. James Gatz, the real man behind the myth, grew up the son of "shiftless and unsuccessful farm people" in North Dakota. At seventeen, seeing Dan Cody's yacht drop anchor on Lake Superior, he rowed out to warn Cody of dangerous shallows — and in that moment, James Gatz ceased to exist and Jay Gatsby was born. Cody took the ambitious young man under his wing, outfitted him, and employed him for five years as everything from steward to jailor. When Cody died, his companion Ella Kaye used a legal device to cut Gatsby out of the promised inheritance.

The chapter's second half shows Gatsby increasingly at odds with the social world he is trying to enter. Tom Buchanan visits with Mr. Sloane and a woman; their polite invitation to dinner is a deliberate snub — they ride off before Gatsby can follow. The following Saturday, Tom accompanies Daisy to one of Gatsby's parties, and the evening is suffused with unpleasantness. Daisy is repelled by West Egg's raw vulgarity; Tom grows suspicious and contemptuous. After the guests leave, Gatsby tells Nick his true demand of Daisy: she must erase four years of marriage by telling Tom she never loved him, so they can return to Louisville and start again from the moment before Gatsby left for the war. Nick warns that the past cannot be repeated. Gatsby looks at him with incredulous certainty and insists it can.

Key Terms

  • James Gatz: Gatsby's real birth name; he legally changed it to Jay Gatsby at age seventeen
  • Dan Cody: A wealthy copper magnate and yachtsman who became young James Gatz's mentor; his name evokes the rough self-made wealth of the American frontier
  • Ella Kaye: A journalist and Dan Cody's companion who, through a legal maneuver, inherited Cody's millions after his death, depriving Gatsby of his $25,000 legacy
  • laudable: Deserving praise; used to describe the reporter's initiative in seeking Gatsby out
  • shiftless: Lacking ambition or initiative; how Fitzgerald describes Gatsby's parents to emphasize how completely he rejected his origins

Review Questions

  1. Why does Nick choose to reveal Gatsby's true biography at this point in the novel rather than earlier?
  2. How does the encounter with Dan Cody shape Gatsby's understanding of wealth, self-presentation, and ambition?
  3. Describe the visit by Tom Buchanan, Mr. Sloane, and the woman. How does Tom treat Gatsby, and what does this reveal about the social divide between old money and new money?
  4. What specifically appalls Daisy about Gatsby's party, and why does her reaction matter to Gatsby's plans?
  5. Gatsby insists he can repeat the past. What does he mean, and what does this belief reveal about the nature of his obsession with Daisy?
