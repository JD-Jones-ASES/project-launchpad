Study Guide: The Aftermath of Gatsby's Death

Overview

This section details the immediate, somber aftermath of Jay Gatsby's death, focusing on Nick Carraway's desperate and ultimately futile attempts to arrange a funeral and find mourners. It starkly reveals the indifference and self-interest of those who had benefited from Gatsby's hospitality and wealth, contrasting sharply with Nick's growing sense of loyalty and responsibility. The section also introduces Gatsby's father, Henry C. Gatz, whose arrival brings a poignant and often misunderstood perspective on his son's life.

Key Concepts

  • Isolation in Death: Gatsby, despite his lavish parties and numerous acquaintances, dies completely alone, abandoned by almost everyone he knew. His death underscores the superficiality of the relationships he cultivated.
  • Hypocrisy and Self-Interest: Characters like Daisy, Tom, Meyer Wolfshiem, and Klipspringer quickly distance themselves from Gatsby and his death, prioritizing their own reputations, convenience, or even trivial possessions over any genuine grief or loyalty.
  • Fabrication of Truth: The media, fueled by sensationalism, and characters like Catherine (Myrtle's sister) intentionally distort the facts surrounding the deaths of Gatsby and Wilson to simplify the narrative and protect reputations.
  • Nick's Loyalty and Disillusionment: Nick Carraway, initially confused, becomes Gatsby's sole advocate and takes on the responsibility for his funeral. This experience further deepens his disillusionment with the wealthy elite and the moral emptiness of West Egg and East Egg.
  • The Unveiling of "Jimmy" Gatz: Gatsby's father arrives, providing a glimpse into Gatsby's humble origins and his relentless ambition. Mr. Gatz's pride in his son's material success, despite his ignorance of its source, highlights a different kind of American Dream.

Important Terms

  • Pasquinade: (As used in the text) A satire or lampoon, often public and abusive. Nick initially feared Wilson's story would be turned into a racy one, ridiculing Gatsby.
  • Adventitious Authority: Authority or influence that is accidental or acquired incidentally, rather than inherent or by right. Used to describe the detective's voice that shaped the early, false newspaper reports.
  • Deranged by Grief: The official explanation for George Wilson's actions, concocted by Catherine's testimony to simplify the murder-suicide case and protect reputations, particularly Myrtle's.
  • Superfluous: Unnecessary; more than what is needed or useful. Nick's request for Wolfshiem to come seemed superfluous because he assumed Wolfshiem would naturally respond to the news.
  • Ulster: A long, loose overcoat made of heavy material, often with a cape. What Gatsby's father, Henry C. Gatz, was wearing upon his arrival.
  • Henry C. Gatz: Jay Gatsby's elderly father, who travels from Minnesota for his son's funeral. He embodies a simple, hardworking American identity and harbors immense pride in his son's perceived success, unaware of its criminal origins.
  • Klipspringer: Known as "the boarder," he was a frequent guest who practically lived at Gatsby's mansion. He calls Nick after Gatsby's death, but only to retrieve a pair of tennis shoes, demonstrating extreme self-absorption and indifference.
  • Meyer Wolfshiem: Gatsby's business associate and mentor. Despite his initial expressions of shock, he refuses to attend Gatsby's funeral, citing "important business" and a desire to avoid entanglement, showcasing his pragmatic and ruthless nature.
  • Slagle: A mysterious caller who telephones Nick after Gatsby's death, revealing a detail about "Young Parke" being caught with bonds, hinting at the illicit nature of Gatsby's business dealings.
  • The Swastika Holding Company: The name of Wolfshiem's business office, where Nick attempts to find him. The name's later historical connotations add an unsettling layer to Wolfshiem's character.

Detailed Notes

The Immediate Aftermath and Public Reaction

  • Chaos and Sensationalism: The days following Gatsby's death are marked by a constant presence of police, photographers, and newspaper reporters. The public, represented by curious children, treats the scene as a spectacle.
  • Distorted Truth: Newspaper reports are described as "grotesque, circumstantial, eager, and untrue," setting a false narrative.
  • Catherine's Deception: At the inquest, Myrtle Wilson's sister, Catherine, falsely testifies that Myrtle was happy with George and had never known Gatsby. Her lies are driven by a desire to preserve her sister's reputation and simplify the tragic event, successfully reducing Wilson to a man "deranged by grief."

Nick's Burden and Disillusionment

  • Sole Responsibility: Nick feels "responsible" for Gatsby's affairs because "no one else was interested." He alone shoulders the burden of arranging the funeral and finding mourners.
  • Daisy and Tom's Escape: Nick instinctively calls Daisy, only to discover that she and Tom have "gone away early that afternoon, and taken baggage with them," leaving no forwarding address. Their abrupt departure signifies their complete abandonment of Gatsby and their refusal to face the consequences of their actions.
  • Gatsby's Haunting Plea: Nick imagines Gatsby's voice, pleading, "Look here, old sport, you've got to get somebody for me. You've got to try hard. I can't go through this alone." This reinforces Nick's sense of duty and Gatsby's profound isolation.

The Indifference of Acquaintances

  • Wolfshiem's Evasion: Nick attempts to contact Meyer Wolfshiem, initially by phone and then by sending a letter. Wolfshiem sends a letter back expressing shock but firmly states he "cannot come down now as I am tied up in some very important business and cannot get mixed up in this thing now." He asks to be informed about the funeral but makes it clear he wants no direct involvement.
  • Klipspringer's Selfishness: When Nick finally reaches Klipspringer, the "boarder," he expresses no condolences or interest in the funeral. His sole concern is a pair of tennis shoes he left at Gatsby's mansion, which he asks the butler to send to him. Nick hangs up in disgust, symbolizing the depth of superficiality among Gatsby's former guests.
  • Further Rejection: Other attempts to find mourners are met with indifference or even scorn, leading Nick to feel "a certain shame for Gatsby."

The Arrival of Henry C. Gatz

  • A Telegram from Minnesota: A telegram from "Henry C. Gatz" arrives, announcing his immediate departure and requesting a postponement of the funeral.
  • Gatsby's Humble Origins: Henry C. Gatz is revealed to be Gatsby's elderly father, a "solemn old man, very helpless and dismayed." His arrival provides a stark contrast to the grand illusion Gatsby maintained.
  • Fatherly Pride and Misunderstanding: Mr. Gatz is deeply grieved but also immensely proud of "Jimmy's" achievements. He views the mansion as a testament to his son's "brain power" and ambition, imagining Gatsby as a future "great man" like James J. Hill, completely unaware of the illicit sources of Gatsby's wealth. He insists Jimmy preferred the East, where he "rose up to his position." This highlights the difference between Gatsby's self-invented identity and his father's simple, admiring view.

Nick's Final Attempts to Secure Wolfshiem's Presence

  • The Swastika Holding Company: On the morning of the funeral, Nick travels to New York to confront Wolfshiem. He finds Wolfshiem's office, "The Swastika Holding Company," with a hostile receptionist who claims Wolfshiem is in Chicago.
  • Wolfshiem's Evasive Confrontation: Despite the receptionist's denial, Nick deduces Wolfshiem is there. Wolfshiem emerges, feigning solemnity and offering a cigar, but deftly steers the conversation away from his attendance at the funeral, beginning to recount his past association with Gatsby in a way that further distances himself from the present tragedy. His refusal to acknowledge Gatsby publicly underscores the criminal underworld's code of self-preservation.

Review Questions

  1. How do the reactions of Daisy, Tom, Meyer Wolfshiem, and Klipspringer to Gatsby's death collectively illustrate the theme of isolation and the superficiality of the relationships Gatsby cultivated?
  2. Discuss the significance of Catherine's testimony at the inquest. What does her decision to lie reveal about her character and the prevailing societal values concerning reputation?
  3. What is Nick Carraway's emotional journey throughout this section? How does his growing sense of responsibility and his disillusionment with Gatsby's "friends" shape his character?
  4. Analyze the character of Henry C. Gatz. What does his arrival and his perspective on "Jimmy" reveal about Gatsby's past, his ambitions, and the nature of the American Dream in the novel?
  5. Consider the role of truth versus fabrication in this section, from the newspaper reports to Catherine's testimony. How does the manipulation of truth contribute to the tragedy and further emphasize the moral decay of society?
