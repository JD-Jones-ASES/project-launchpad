Study Guide: The Novel and Its Place in Literature

Key Concepts

  • The Great Gatsby was published by Charles Scribner's Sons on April 10, 1925; Fitzgerald considered it his best work
  • Initial reception was mixed and sales were modest — roughly 20,000 copies in the first year; the novel went out of print during Fitzgerald's lifetime
  • The novel was revived during World War II when the Council on Books in Wartime distributed approximately 155,000 copies to American soldiers through the Armed Services Editions
  • By the 1960s the novel had entered the American literary canon; today it sells approximately 500,000 copies per year
  • The novel participates in multiple literary traditions simultaneously: modernism, social realism, classical tragedy, social satire, and romance
  • Its central subject — the American Dream — gives it lasting resonance; Fitzgerald both celebrates and interrogates the belief that anyone can rise from humble origins through determination
  • Key modernist features include an unreliable first-person narrator, non-chronological storytelling, and dense symbolic patterns (the green light, the eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg, the valley of ashes)

Summary

When The Great Gatsby appeared in April 1925, critical response was respectful but not enthusiastic, and sales were far below those of This Side of Paradise. Fitzgerald was disappointed. The novel went out of print in his lifetime, and at his death in 1940 it appeared destined for obscurity. Its resurrection came unexpectedly during World War II, when the Council on Books in Wartime selected it for the Armed Services Editions — lightweight paperbacks distributed free to American servicemen overseas. Roughly 155,000 soldiers encountered the novel for the first time in this format. After the war, scholars and critics began to reassess it, and by the 1960s it had secured its place as one of the essential works of American literature. It now sells approximately 500,000 copies per year.

The novel's durability reflects both its formal craft and its subject matter. At fewer than 50,000 words it is compressed and precisely constructed, with symbolic patterns that reward close attention. Its central subject — the American Dream, the belief that anyone can rise from humble origins to wealth and success through hard work and determination — remains a live question in American culture. Fitzgerald neither simply endorses nor simply condemns this dream; his ambivalence toward it gives the novel a complexity that sustains repeated reading.

The novel draws on several literary traditions at once. As a work of modernism, it employs an unreliable narrator (Nick Carraway), non-chronological presentation of Gatsby's history, and dense symbolism. As social realism, it offers precise observations of class, geography, and social behavior in Long Island and New York City in the early 1920s. Gatsby himself can be read as a classical tragic hero — a figure of exceptional qualities destroyed by a fatal flaw (his inability to accept that the past is past). The novel also works as social satire (exposing the hollowness of the wealthy) and as a romance quest (Gatsby's pursuit of Daisy parallels a hero's pursuit of an unreachable ideal, with the green light functioning as a kind of grail symbol).

Key Terms

  • Armed Services Editions: Lightweight paperbacks distributed free to American soldiers during World War II; the edition that revived The Great Gatsby's readership
  • American Dream: The belief that anyone can achieve prosperity and success through hard work and determination, regardless of birth or social standing; the novel's central subject
  • Modernism: A literary movement of the 1910s–1930s characterized by formal experimentation, unreliable narration, subjective experience, and disillusionment with traditional values
  • Unreliable narrator: A narrator whose account cannot be taken entirely at face value; Nick Carraway is one of the most discussed examples in American literature
  • Tragic hero: A figure of exceptional qualities destroyed by a fatal flaw; Gatsby's flaw is his insistence that time can be reversed and the idealized past recovered
  • Romance (literary): A quest narrative in which a hero pursues an ideal; Gatsby's pursuit of Daisy follows this structure, with the green light as a grail symbol
  • Symbolism: The use of concrete objects or images to represent abstract ideas; key symbols include the green light, the eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg, and the valley of ashes

Review Questions

  1. Why did The Great Gatsby fall into obscurity after its publication, and what event triggered its revival?
  2. What two factors does the source material identify as explaining the novel's extraordinary durability?
  3. What features of The Great Gatsby place it in the modernist literary tradition?
  4. In what sense can Gatsby be read as a classical tragic hero? What is his fatal flaw?
  5. How does the novel function as a romance quest, and what object serves as the grail symbol?
