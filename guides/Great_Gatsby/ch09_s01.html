Study Guide: Chapter 8: The Novel

Key Concepts

  • Nick cannot sleep after Myrtle's death and visits Gatsby at dawn
  • Gatsby reveals the full story of his romance with Daisy in Louisville in 1917 — a penniless young officer courting a wealthy girl under false pretenses
  • Gatsby insists Daisy never truly loved Tom, dismissing the Plaza confrontation as a misunderstanding
  • Nick urges Gatsby to flee but Gatsby refuses to leave until he knows what Daisy will do
  • Nick tells Gatsby: "You're worth the whole damn bunch put together" — his only genuine compliment
  • George Wilson, grief-stricken and mentally unraveling overnight at the garage, confuses the billboard eyes of Dr. T. J. Eckleburg with the eyes of God
  • Wilson traces the yellow car to Gatsby's estate and shoots Gatsby in his pool, then kills himself
  • Gatsby had never used the pool all summer; he is using it for the first time on the day he dies
  • Daisy never calls; the butler waits for a phone message that never arrives

Summary

Unable to sleep after the previous night's catastrophe, Nick crosses the lawn to find Gatsby still awake, heavy with dejection. The two men wander the enormous, dusty house in the dark, smoking and waiting for a dawn that feels like an ending. It is in this hollow, quiet space that Gatsby finally tells Nick the full story of his love for Daisy — how in 1917, a penniless young officer named James Gatz entered a world of wealth and beauty he had no right to touch, fell genuinely in love, went to war, and returned to find Daisy married to Tom Buchanan. Gatsby still refuses to believe Daisy ever truly loved Tom. Nick urges him to leave town — the car can be traced — but Gatsby cannot abandon his vigil.

Nick reluctantly leaves for work, pausing at the hedge to call back the one honest compliment he ever gives: "You're worth the whole damn bunch put together." The chapter then shifts to the valley of ashes, where George Wilson has spent the night in a state of deepening grief and paranoia. Michaelis tends to him through the dark hours while Wilson mutters about the yellow car and about God. At one point Wilson stands at the garage window and, staring at the faded billboard of Dr. T. J. Eckleburg, declares that God sees everything.

By morning Wilson has disappeared. He traces the yellow car across several towns on foot, arriving at Gatsby's estate at midday. At two o'clock, Gatsby puts on his bathing suit and gets into the pool, waiting for a phone call from Daisy that never comes. Wilson shoots him there. When Nick arrives after work, he finds the laden mattress drifting in the pool, a thin red circle tracing outward in the water. Wilson's body lies a short distance away in the grass.

Key Terms

  • Dan Cody: The wealthy copper magnate who took in the young James Gatz, gave him his first taste of wealth, and shaped the Gatsby persona; revealed in Chapter 6, referenced here as context for Gatsby's origins
  • Humidor: A container for storing cigars or tobacco at constant humidity; one of the objects Nick and Gatsby search for in the dark, dusty house — suggesting the mansion has already lost its vitality
  • Pneumatic mattress: The air-filled pool float Gatsby uses on the day of his death; an object of summer leisure that becomes a deathbed
  • Holocaust: Used in the chapter's final sentence in its original sense — a complete destruction; "the holocaust was complete" refers to the total ruin of Gatsby, Wilson, and Myrtle
  • Grail: Nick compares Gatsby's pursuit of Daisy to a medieval knight's quest for the Holy Grail — a sacred, all-consuming mission that gives his life meaning but may be unattainable

Review Questions

  1. What does Gatsby's account of his 1917 romance with Daisy reveal about the origins and nature of his obsession?
  2. Why does Gatsby refuse Nick's advice to leave town after Myrtle's death?
  3. How does George Wilson come to believe that Gatsby killed Myrtle, and what role does Tom Buchanan play in this?
  4. What is the significance of the pool in Chapter 8 — why does Gatsby use it for the first time on the day he dies?
  5. What does Nick's compliment ("You're worth the whole damn bunch put together") reveal about his conflicted feelings toward Gatsby?
