Study Guide: Chapter 9, Section 1: CH09_ANALYSIS - After Gatsby

Overview

Chapter 9 details the immediate aftermath of Jay Gatsby's death, focusing on Nick Carraway's profound disillusionment with the superficiality and moral decay of the East. Gatsby's neglected funeral underscores the emptiness of his social connections, while Nick's final reflections expand Gatsby's personal tragedy into a poignant meditation on the historical promise and ultimate elusiveness of the American Dream.

Key Concepts

  • The Emptiness of Wealth and Social Connections: Gatsby's lavish parties attracted hordes, yet his funeral is attended by only a handful, revealing the superficial and transactional nature of his relationships and the moral indifference of the wealthy elite.
  • The Disillusionment with the East: Nick's experiences culminate in a profound rejection of the East's recklessness, moral decay, and "careless" wealth, leading him to conclude that it distorts dreams and destroys lives.
  • The American Dream as a Recurring Fantasy: Fitzgerald posits that Gatsby's romantic longing for Daisy is a modern iteration of the original American Dream, represented by the wonder of the Dutch sailors discovering the "new world." Both dreams, however, are shown to be projections onto an idealized reality, ultimately unattainable.
  • The Inescapability of the Past: The novel's famous closing metaphor—boats beating against the current, borne ceaselessly into the past—suggests that humanity is perpetually drawn back to idealized moments, making true progress or the recapture of a lost dream impossible.
  • Gatsby's Enduring Idealism: Despite his corrupt means, Gatsby's "capacity for wonder" and the intensity of his dream are presented as his greatest, almost heroic, qualities, connecting him to the aspirational spirit of America itself.

Important Terms

  • Adventitious: Coming from an external source rather than being inherent; accidental. Used to describe the false authority that the detective's manner lent to the newspaper reports.
  • Pasquinade: A satire or lampoon, especially one posted in a public place. Nick fears the inquest testimony will be sensationalized.
  • Surmise: A supposition or guess formed without sufficient evidence. Every "surmise" about Gatsby is directed to Nick after the death.
  • Superfluous: Unnecessary, more than is needed. Nick calls his written request to Wolfsheim "superfluous," expecting him to come without prompting.
  • Defiance: Open resistance; bold disobedience. Nick feels a "scornful solidarity" and defiance toward all who abandon Gatsby.
  • Ulster: A long, heavy overcoat originally from Ulster, Ireland. Henry C. Gatz wears one inappropriately in the warm September weather.
  • Elocution: The skill of clear and expressive speech. Listed in young Gatsby's self-improvement schedule.
  • Incessant: Never ceasing; constant. Used to describe the faint music and laughter Nick remembers from Gatsby's parties.
  • Incoherent: Lacking logical or meaningful connection. Nick calls Gatsby's abandoned mansion a "huge incoherent failure of a house."
  • Commensurate: Corresponding in size, extent, or degree; proportionate. Fitzgerald describes the continent as "something commensurate to his capacity for wonder."
  • Orgastic: Fitzgerald's coinage (often emended to "orgiastic"), suggesting ecstatic or rapturous intensity. Describes the "orgastic future" that forever recedes before us.
  • Provincial: Relating to a province; unsophisticated. Nick describes his moral discomfort with Tom as "provincial squeamishness."

Detailed Notes

The Aftermath of Gatsby's Death and the Neglected Funeral

  • Nick's Solitary Burden: Following Gatsby's death, Nick Carraway takes on the sole responsibility for arranging the funeral. This immediately highlights Gatsby's isolation despite his vast wealth and social connections.
  • Indifference of the "Friends": Nick encounters widespread refusal and indifference from those who once frequented Gatsby's extravagant parties.
    • Tom and Daisy Buchanan: They vanish without a word, demonstrating their profound carelessness and lack of remorse or loyalty.
    • Meyer Wolfsheim: Gatsby's business partner, who helped him build his fortune, declines to attend, citing a principle of not getting involved in a friend's troubles once they're gone. This underscores the purely transactional nature of their relationship.
    • Ewing Klipspringer (the "boarder"): Calls only to retrieve a pair of tennis shoes he left at Gatsby's mansion, further emphasizing the self-serving attitudes of those who exploited Gatsby's generosity.
  • Arrival of Henry C. Gatz: Gatsby's father arrives from Minnesota. He is a simple, proud man who cherishes his son's ambition and success, unaware of the criminal means through which it was achieved. He brings two poignant relics: a cracked photograph of Gatsby's mansion and a boyhood schedule of self-improvement written inside a copy of Hopalong Cassidy, revealing Gatsby's early drive for self-perfection.
  • The Meager Funeral: The funeral is a desolate affair, attended by only Nick, Mr. Gatz, the servants, the postman, and the "owl-eyed man" (who had been fascinated by Gatsby's real books). This sparse attendance dramatically contrasts with the throngs at Gatsby's parties, exposing the superficiality of his social world and the ultimate loneliness of his life.

Nick's Final Encounters and Departure

  • Break with Jordan Baker: Nick ends his relationship with Jordan, recognizing the fundamental incompatibility of their moral perspectives and Jordan's own careless cynicism.
  • Confrontation with Tom Buchanan: Nick encounters Tom on Fifth Avenue and confronts him about his role in Gatsby's death. Tom defiantly admits to telling George Wilson that Gatsby owned the yellow car, expressing no remorse and even claiming Gatsby deserved it. This encounter solidifies Nick's judgment of Tom and Daisy as "careless people" who "smashed up things and creatures and then retreated back into their money or their vast carelessness."
  • Return to the Midwest: Deeply disillusioned with the moral decay and corruption of the East, Nick prepares to return to the Midwest. His departure symbolizes his rejection of the false promises and destructive materialism he witnessed.

The American Dream: Gatsby and the Dutch Sailors

  • Expansion of Scope: In the novel's final pages, Fitzgerald elevates Gatsby's personal story into a profound meditation on the meaning of America itself and the nature of the American Dream.
  • The Dutch Sailors' Vision: On his last night, Nick sits on the beach, imagining the Dutch sailors who first encountered Long Island's shore. They experienced a moment of "wonder" at the "fresh, green breast of the new world," a feeling "commensurate to his [Gatsby's] capacity for wonder."
  • Connecting Gatsby's Dream to America's Promise: Fitzgerald explicitly links this historical moment of discovery to Gatsby's wonder upon seeing the green light at the end of Daisy's dock. The recurring color "green" symbolizes both the natural beauty of the new world and the elusive dream that draws people towards it. This connection suggests that Gatsby's personal longing for Daisy was a modern manifestation of the same fundamental desire that inspired European explorers.
  • The Nature of the Dream – Greatness and Doom:
    • Gatsby's Greatness: Lies in the "purity and intensity of his capacity to dream"—his unwavering belief in the possibility of recapturing an idealized past.
    • Gatsby's Doom: Stems from the flawed nature of his dream. Like the Dutch sailors who projected their desires onto land already inhabited, Gatsby projects an ideal onto Daisy, attempts to recapture an irrecoverable past, and believes wealth can buy what is inherently priceless.
  • The Universal Verdict: "Boats Beating Against the Current": The novel's iconic closing metaphor describes humanity as "boats beating against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past." This implies that the current represents time itself, and the "past" is where our dreams and idealized visions are perpetually located, making them forever just out of reach. We are all, in a sense, Gatsby—striving for something that recedes before us.
  • Literary Techniques: Fitzgerald employs extended metaphor and elegy. The passage mourns not only Gatsby but also a certain idea of American possibility—the hope that dreams can be realized.
  • Nick's Epiphany: The systematic stripping away of illusions (Tom's confession, Jordan's farewell, the empty mansion) leads Nick to an epiphany: the East (and perhaps America) has been "haunted" and "distorted" by the gap between its promise and its reality.
  • The Moral Education: Nick's return to the Midwest is not a triumph, but an admission of the painful truth he has learned: dreams can be corrupted, careless wealth destroys, and the past, however passionately pursued, cannot be recovered. The novel's final word, "past," serves as both its subject and its melancholic verdict.

Review Questions

  1. Analyze the significance of the differing attendance levels at Gatsby's parties versus his funeral. What does this contrast reveal about the nature of his relationships and the values of the society he inhabited?
  2. How does Nick's confrontation with Tom Buchanan on Fifth Avenue serve as a final catalyst for his disillusionment? What does Tom's confession and lack of remorse reveal about his character and the "careless" nature of the wealthy elite?
  3. Discuss how Fitzgerald uses the image of the Dutch sailors encountering the "fresh, green breast of the new world" to broaden the scope of Gatsby's personal tragedy into a commentary on the American Dream.
  4. Explain the meaning and impact of the novel's closing metaphor: "So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past." What universal truths about human nature, dreams, and time does it convey?
  5. What lessons does Nick Carraway ultimately learn from his time in the East, and why does he decide to return to the Midwest? How does his journey reflect the novel's overall message about idealism and reality?
