Study Guide: Chapter 9: The Novel

Key Concepts

  • Nick takes sole responsibility for Gatsby's funeral arrangements — no one else steps forward
  • Tom and Daisy have left without a forwarding address; Wolfsheim refuses to attend; Klipspringer calls only about abandoned tennis shoes
  • Gatsby's father, Henry C. Gatz, arrives from Minnesota — proud, awed by the mansion, and bearing two relics of his son's boyhood
  • The boyhood schedule in the back of a copy of Hopalong Cassidy reveals the origins of Gatsby's lifelong self-improvement project
  • The funeral is attended only by Nick, Mr. Gatz, the servants, the postman, and the owl-eyed man from the library
  • Owl-eyes — who marveled at Gatsby's real books in Chapter 3 — delivers the chapter's most honest eulogy: "The poor son-of-a-bitch"
  • Nick breaks with Jordan Baker; their final exchange turns on her accusation that Nick is a careless driver — an echo of the novel's central metaphor
  • Nick confronts Tom on Fifth Avenue and learns that Tom directed Wilson to Gatsby's house
  • On his last night, Nick sits on the beach and meditates on Dutch sailors seeing Long Island's shore — connecting Gatsby's dream to the original promise of the American continent
  • The novel ends: "So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past"

Summary

In the days following Gatsby's murder, Nick discovers that he is the only person willing to take responsibility for the arrangements. Daisy and Tom vanish without a word; Wolfsheim sends a letter declining to attend; Klipspringer, one of the most permanent party guests, calls not to offer condolences but to ask about a pair of tennis shoes he left behind. The contrast between the hundreds who once crowded Gatsby's parties and the handful who attend his funeral is devastating.

Gatsby's father, Henry C. Gatz, arrives from Minnesota — a trembling old man made proud by the sight of his son's mansion. He shows Nick a cracked photograph of the house that Jimmy had sent him, and then produces a far more revealing relic: a boyhood copy of Hopalong Cassidy with a self-improvement schedule written on the back flyleaf, dated September 12, 1906. The schedule — rising at six, practicing elocution, studying electricity, resolving to "be better to parents" — shows that James Gatz had been reinventing himself long before he became Jay Gatsby. The dream began in childhood.

At the funeral only a handful appear. The owl-eyed man, who had recognized that Gatsby's books were real, arrives in the rain and offers the chapter's most honest three-word eulogy. Afterward, Nick settles his unfinished business: a strained farewell with Jordan, who accuses him of careless driving — a loaded echo — and a confrontation with Tom on Fifth Avenue, where Tom admits he told Wilson that Gatsby owned the yellow car. Nick cannot forgive Tom but finds himself almost pitying him, recognizing that Tom did what he did out of self-interest, not malice, and that this distinction changes nothing. On his last night, sitting on the beach beside the dark water, Nick thinks of the Dutch sailors and the green light, and arrives at the novel's final, universal metaphor.

Key Terms

  • Henry C. Gatz: Gatsby's elderly father from Minnesota; his arrival with the boyhood schedule humanizes Gatsby and reveals the real James Gatz beneath the invented Jay Gatsby
  • Hopalong Cassidy: A popular fictional Western hero; the book in which Gatz wrote his self-improvement schedule — connecting Gatsby's self-invention to a distinctly American myth of the self-made man
  • Owl-eyes: The bespectacled man who discovered in Chapter 3 that Gatsby's library books were real (uncut); his presence at the nearly empty funeral and his blunt epithet mark him as one of the few honest witnesses to Gatsby's life and death
  • Orgastic future: Fitzgerald's coinage for the ecstatic, receding quality of the American Dream — a hope that is perpetually ahead of us, always just out of reach
  • Dutch sailors: The original European explorers who saw Long Island's unspoiled shore; Nick imagines their wonder as a parallel to Gatsby's capacity for dreaming — both projecting desire onto a new world

Review Questions

  1. What does the contrast between Gatsby's crowded parties and his nearly empty funeral reveal about the nature of the relationships he cultivated?
  2. What does the boyhood schedule in Hopalong Cassidy tell us about the origins of Gatsby's drive for self-improvement?
  3. Why is Owl-eyes present at the funeral when almost no one else is, and what is the significance of his brief epithet?
  4. What does Nick learn from Tom on Fifth Avenue, and how does this information complete the picture of how Gatsby died?
  5. How does the image of Dutch sailors seeing Long Island for the first time connect Gatsby's personal dream to a broader American story?
