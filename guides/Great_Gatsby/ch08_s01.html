Study Guide: Chapter 7: The Novel

Key Concepts

  • The climax of the novel occurs in the Plaza Hotel confrontation between Gatsby, Tom, and Daisy
  • Gatsby replaces his servants with Wolfsheim associates to prevent gossip about Daisy's visits
  • The oppressive summer heat intensifies the tension throughout the chapter
  • Tom forces a group trip to New York, swapping cars with Gatsby as an act of dominance
  • Daisy falters when Gatsby demands she declare she never loved Tom — she admits she loved both men
  • Tom exposes Gatsby's bootlegging and criminal associations, destroying his credibility
  • On the drive home, Daisy (driving Gatsby's yellow car) strikes and kills Myrtle Wilson
  • Tom directs Wilson's suspicion toward Gatsby and the yellow car
  • The chapter ends with Gatsby standing vigil outside the Buchanan house while Tom and Daisy sit together at the kitchen table, conspiring and reconciled

Summary

Chapter 7 is the longest and most pivotal chapter of the novel, containing both the climax and the catastrophe. After shutting down his parties and replacing his servants to conceal Daisy's visits, Gatsby joins Nick at a tense lunch at the Buchanans'. Daisy's open admiration of Gatsby reveals their affair to Tom. Tom insists the group drive into New York, and at the Plaza Hotel the long-brewing confrontation erupts. Gatsby demands that Daisy say she never loved Tom, but Daisy falters — she admits she loved them both. Tom attacks Gatsby's background and exposes his bootlegging operations, and Daisy's nerve fails completely.

Tom sends Daisy home with Gatsby in a calculated gesture of contempt. On the return drive through the valley of ashes, Gatsby's yellow car strikes and kills Myrtle Wilson, who had run into the road believing the car was Tom's. Nick later learns that Daisy was at the wheel. Tom, back at the Buchanan house, directs the grief-mad George Wilson's suspicion toward Gatsby and the yellow car, ensuring that neither Buchanan will bear any consequence for the death.

The chapter closes with one of the novel's most devastating images: Gatsby standing alone in the moonlight outside the Buchanan house, keeping a protective vigil over Daisy, while through the pantry window Nick can see Tom and Daisy sitting together at the kitchen table — reconciled, intimate, and utterly indifferent to the destruction surrounding them. Gatsby, as Nick perceives, is watching over nothing.

Key Terms

  • Trimalchio: A reference to a vulgar social climber in the ancient Roman novel Satyricon; Fitzgerald uses it to describe Gatsby's showy party-hosting phase, which ends abruptly before Chapter 7
  • Vigil: A period of watchful waiting; Gatsby's all-night surveillance outside the Buchanan house after Myrtle's death
  • Valley of ashes: The desolate industrial wasteland between West Egg and New York, home to the Wilsons; the site of Myrtle's death
  • Yellow car: Gatsby's ostentatious automobile, which becomes the instrument of Myrtle's death and the object of Wilson's revenge

Review Questions

  1. Why does Gatsby replace his household servants before Chapter 7 begins, and what does this reveal about his priorities?
  2. What does Daisy's response during the Plaza Hotel confrontation reveal about the limits of Gatsby's dream?
  3. How does Tom use knowledge of Gatsby's criminal connections as a weapon during the confrontation?
  4. Who was actually driving the car that struck Myrtle Wilson, and what are the consequences of this fact?
  5. What does the final image of Gatsby's vigil — contrasted with Tom and Daisy at the kitchen table — suggest about the relationship between Gatsby's dream and reality?
