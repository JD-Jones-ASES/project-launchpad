Study Guide: Gatsby and Daisy's Long-Awaited Reunion

Overview

This section details the highly anticipated, yet incredibly awkward, reunion between Jay Gatsby and Daisy Buchanan, orchestrated by Nick Carraway. It vividly portrays Gatsby's extreme nervousness and elaborate preparations, the initial strained interaction between the former lovers, and Nick's role as an uneasy facilitator and observer of their complex emotional landscape.

Key Concepts

  • Gatsby's Obsessive Preparations and Nervousness: Gatsby's actions leading up to the tea party, such as lighting his entire house, offering Nick a confidential business opportunity, and sending his gardener to mow Nick's lawn, underscore his frantic desire to create a perfect environment for Daisy. His pale face, sleeplessness, and fumbling behavior during the reunion reveal his profound anxiety and the immense significance he places on this meeting.
  • Nick's Role as Facilitator and Observer: Nick not only arranges the tea party but also serves as a crucial emotional buffer and confidant. He navigates Gatsby's wild mood swings and Daisy's initial coyness, providing an objective yet sympathetic lens through which the reader experiences the reunion. His house becomes the stage for this pivotal event.
  • Symbolism of the Defunct Mantelpiece Clock: The broken clock that Gatsby nearly knocks over symbolizes his desperate attempt to stop or even reverse time, specifically the five years that have passed since he last saw Daisy. His desire to "fix" the clock mirrors his deeper yearning to recapture a past moment and make it current again, highlighting the impossibility of truly turning back time.
  • The Power and Illusion of Daisy's Voice: Daisy's voice is consistently described with captivating, almost magical qualities (e.g., "exhilarating ripple," "wild tonic"). This emphasizes her allure for Gatsby and its almost hypnotic effect. However, her "clear artificial note" during the initial greeting suggests a layer of insincerity or social performance that tempers the raw emotion of the moment.
  • The Awkwardness of Recreating the Past: Despite Gatsby's elaborate efforts, the reunion is initially fraught with silence, embarrassment, and strained interactions. This highlights the difficulty, if not impossibility, of simply resuming a relationship exactly where it left off after a long separation, especially given the weight of Gatsby's five-year obsession.

Important Terms

  • Rout: (noun) A disorderly, tumultuous crowd of people; a wild party. Nick initially mistakes the blazing lights of Gatsby's house for another "wild rout."
  • Confidential sort of thing: Gatsby's vague offer to Nick of a clandestine "side line" business opportunity, implied to be illicit. This reveals Gatsby's willingness to use his wealth and connections to manipulate situations, even offering financial gain to Nick to secure his cooperation.
  • Reproachfully: (adverb) In a way that expresses disapproval or disappointment; addressing someone with blame or criticism. Gatsby looks "reproachfully" at Nick's Finnish maid, possibly seeing her as an obstacle or distraction from his preparations.
  • Defunct mantelpiece clock: A broken clock on Nick's mantel that Gatsby nearly knocks over during his nervous reunion with Daisy. This literally "stopped time" object symbolizes Gatsby's desire to reverse the five years since he last saw Daisy, representing his futile attempt to recapture the past.
  • Obstinate: (adjective) Stubbornly refusing to change one's opinion or chosen course of action, despite attempts to persuade. The story of the brewer who built Gatsby's house mentions that Americans were "obstinate about being peasantry," referring to their refusal to adopt straw-thatched roofs despite potential tax benefits.

Detailed Notes

I. Gatsby's Overwhelming Anticipation and Preparations

  • The Blazing House: Nick returns home late to find Gatsby's entire mansion "lit from tower to cellar," appearing like the "World's Fair." This excessive lighting symbolizes Gatsby's feverish state of anticipation and his desire to present an opulent stage for Daisy's arrival.
  • Late-Night Offers: Gatsby appears on Nick's lawn, almost absently suggesting a trip to Coney Island or a swim. His suppressed eagerness indicates his mind is elsewhere, consumed by the impending meeting.
  • Daisy's Invitation: Nick informs Gatsby he's invited Daisy for tea the next day. Gatsby feigns carelessness ("Oh, that's all right...I don't want to put you to any trouble") but quickly corrects Nick about the suitable day, revealing his thinly veiled desperation.
  • The Lawn Incident: Gatsby's sudden concern about getting "the grass cut" (specifically Nick's ragged lawn) highlights his meticulous attention to detail and his need for everything to be perfect for Daisy. He later sends his gardener to do it.
  • The Business Proposition: Gatsby attempts to offer Nick a "confidential sort of thing" – a side business that would yield "a nice bit of money." This is an awkward, tactless attempt to repay Nick for facilitating the reunion, revealing Gatsby's transactional view of relationships and his assumption that everyone can be bought or influenced. Nick, recognizing the ethical implications, politely declines.
  • Morning Preparations: The day of the tea is rainy. Gatsby sends a gardener to Nick's house, and later, a "greenhouse" full of flowers and innumerable receptacles, along with twelve lemon cakes.
  • Gatsby's Appearance and State: Gatsby arrives in a striking white flannel suit, silver shirt, and gold-colored tie, looking "pale" with "dark signs of sleeplessness." He is extremely nervous, questioning if "everything's all right" and appearing oblivious to his surroundings, even the newly mown grass.

II. Nick's Role and Daisy's Arrival

  • Setting the Stage: Nick calls Daisy from his office, inviting her to tea but pointedly warning her: "Don't bring Tom." Daisy's innocent "Who is Tom?" highlights her flirtatious nature or perhaps her tendency to disregard inconvenient realities.
  • A Rainy Day: The persistent rain throughout the section sets a somber, perhaps melancholic or cleansing, mood for the reunion.
  • Daisy's Entrance: Daisy arrives in a "large open car" with a chauffeur. Her "bright ecstatic smile" and "exhilarating ripple of her voice" captivate Nick. She teases him ("Are you in love with me... or why did I have to come alone?"), emphasizing her charm and awareness of her own power.

III. The Awkward Reunion

  • Gatsby's Disappearance and Re-entry: Upon Daisy's arrival, Gatsby, consumed by nerves, vanishes from the living room. Nick finds him "pale as death," glaring tragically from a puddle outside the front door. Gatsby re-enters with rigid, mechanical movements.
  • Initial Silence and Strained Greetings: The living room is deserted upon Daisy's entry, adding to the awkwardness. When Gatsby finally re-emerges, there is a "choking murmur" and an "artificial note" in Daisy's voice as she greets him, revealing their mutual discomfort.
  • The Defunct Clock Incident: Gatsby, leaning against the mantelpiece in a "strained counterfeit of perfect ease," nearly knocks over a broken clock. This clumsy act is a powerful symbol: he is trying to stop or reverse time, specifically the "Five years next November" that have passed since they last met.
  • "We haven't met for many years": Daisy's matter-of-fact statement, followed by Gatsby's automatic, precise correction, underscores the weight of the lost time and Gatsby's obsessive memory of their past.
  • Nick's Interventions: Nick tries to ease the tension by suggesting they make tea, but the maid (the "demoniac Finn") brings it in. He creates a pretext to leave the room, sensing their need for privacy.

IV. Gatsby's Emotional Breakdown and Nick's Observations

  • "A Terrible Mistake": As Nick tries to leave, Gatsby follows him into the kitchen, whispering, "Oh, God! This is a terrible mistake." His profound anxiety and fear of failure are palpable.
  • Nick's Reassurance and Scolding: Nick comforts Gatsby by telling him Daisy is "just as embarrassed" as he is, then scolds him for being "rude" and "acting like a little boy." This shows Nick's growing understanding of Gatsby's emotional fragility and his role as a grounding force.
  • Gatsby's "Unforgettable Reproach": Gatsby's look of "reproach" at Nick before returning to Daisy highlights his extreme vulnerability and his dependence on Nick for emotional support and guidance in this overwhelming moment.
  • The Brewer's House Backstory: While Gatsby and Daisy are alone, Nick observes Gatsby's mansion from outside. He recounts the story of the original owner, a brewer who built the house in a "period craze" but then went into decline. This backstory subtly parallels Gatsby's own potentially transient grandeur and the superficiality of his aspirations. The reference to Americans being "obstinate about being peasantry" reflects a cultural pride and resistance to class subjugation.

Review Questions

  1. How does Gatsby's elaborate lighting of his house and his various offers to Nick (Coney Island, swimming, business) reveal his deep-seated anxieties and expectations for his reunion with Daisy?
  2. Analyze the significance of the "defunct mantelpiece clock" incident. What does it symbolize about Gatsby's relationship with time and his hopes for the past?
  3. Describe the initial atmosphere of the tea party before Daisy's arrival and the immediate moments after she enters. How does Nick's narration convey the tension and awkwardness?
  4. What role does Nick Carraway play in this section, both as an active participant and as an observer? How does his presence influence the interactions between Gatsby and Daisy?
  5. Discuss Daisy's demeanor and dialogue upon her arrival and during her initial interaction with Gatsby. Does she seem genuinely surprised or emotionally guarded, and what might account for her reactions?
