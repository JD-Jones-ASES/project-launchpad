Chapter 6, Section 4: The Truth About Gatsby - Study Guide

Overview

This section describes a pivotal party at Gatsby's mansion, attended by Daisy and Tom, which serves as a critical turning point in Gatsby's pursuit of Daisy. It reveals Daisy's conflicted feelings about Gatsby's world, Tom's growing suspicions about Gatsby's wealth, and Gatsby's profound disappointment. The section culminates in Gatsby's desperate confession to Nick about his desire to entirely erase the past five years and his deeply romantic, yet deluded, belief that he can recreate his history with Daisy exactly as it once was, a desire illuminated by a flashback to their first kiss.

Key Concepts

  • The Illusion of the American Dream: Gatsby's parties, while glittering, are revealed to be superficial and vulgar through Daisy and Tom's eyes. Gatsby's dream of winning Daisy back is deeply flawed, based on a nostalgic ideal rather than present reality.
  • The Unrepeatable Past: Gatsby's central delusion is his fervent belief that he can "repeat the past" – erase his and Daisy's lives since their separation and return to their original romantic moment. Nick's famous line, "You can't repeat the past," directly confronts this impossible fantasy.
  • Social Class and Morality: The contrasting reactions of Daisy and Tom (representing old money/East Egg) to Gatsby's West Egg party highlight the social chasm. Daisy is "appalled" by the "raw vigor" and lack of decorum, viewing West Egg as crude and morally bankrupt, despite her momentary attraction to Gatsby. Tom's suspicions about Gatsby being a "bootlegger" underscore the old money's disdain and mistrust of the newly rich.
  • Gatsby's Romantic Obsession: The section vividly portrays the depth of Gatsby's unwavering devotion to Daisy. His entire existence, including his lavish parties and wealth, is a calculated effort to win her back. His "unutterable depression" when Daisy doesn't enjoy the party reveals the fragility of his self-worth tied to her approval.
  • The Incarnation of a Dream: The flashback to Gatsby's first kiss with Daisy is described as an "incarnation" – the moment his abstract dreams and "unutterable visions" became concrete and embodied in Daisy. This act inextricably linked his future to her, forever shaping his pursuit.

Important Terms

  • Bootlegger: A person who makes, distributes, or sells alcoholic beverages illegally. Tom's accusation of Gatsby being a bootlegger implies that Gatsby's wealth is illicitly gained, connecting him to the criminal underworld of Prohibition.
  • West Egg: The less fashionable, "new money" side of Long Island, where Gatsby and Nick live. It is characterized by ostentatious displays of wealth and a more transient, less refined social scene, which appalls Daisy.
  • East Egg: The fashionable, "old money" side of Long Island, where Tom and Daisy live. It represents established wealth, tradition, and a certain social decorum, though often accompanied by hypocrisy and moral decay.
  • "The Polo Player": Tom Buchanan's identity, as ironically given to him by Gatsby. Tom dislikes this label because he prefers to observe others rather than be defined or noticed, reflecting his arrogant sense of superiority and control.
  • Incarnation: The embodiment or personification of an abstract quality or idea. In the context of Gatsby's first kiss with Daisy, it signifies the moment his idealized vision of love and life became concrete and focused entirely on her.
  • Sentimental: Having or expressing tender, romantic, or nostalgic feelings. Gatsby's sentimentality is his profound, sometimes excessive, emotional attachment to his idealized past with Daisy, which blinds him to current realities.
  • "Can't repeat the past": Nick Carraway's famous line, a stark statement of reality that directly contradicts Gatsby's core belief and underscores the tragic impossibility of his dream.

Detailed Notes

The Party Experience: A Clash of Worlds

  • Arrival and Initial Impressions: Daisy and Tom arrive at twilight. Daisy initially expresses excitement ("These things excite me so"), while Nick offers a joking "green card" for a kiss, hinting at the superficiality.
  • Gatsby's Attempts to Impress: Gatsby proudly introduces Daisy and Tom to various celebrities (a movie star, her director, a small producer), trying to showcase the glamour of his world.
  • Tom's Disdain: Tom's "arrogant eyes" roam the crowd; he finds no one he knows and expresses contempt for the party-goers, viewing them as "oblivion." He resents being called "the polo player" by Gatsby, indicating his discomfort with being labeled or defined by others, especially by someone he perceives as inferior.
  • Daisy's Mixed Feelings: Daisy expresses some enjoyment ("I liked that man...with the sort of blue nose") and finds the movie star "lovely." She and Gatsby dance a "graceful, conservative fox-trot." They share a private moment on Nick's steps, which suggests a renewed intimacy.
  • The "Tipsy Table": Nick observes the vulgarity and drunken excess of the West Egg guests. The incident with Miss Baedeker being dunked in a pool and her violent, drunken outbursts highlight the lack of refinement and decorum, which ultimately "offends" Daisy.
  • Daisy's "Appalled" Reaction: Daisy is deeply "appalled by West Egg," seeing its "raw vigor" and the "too obtrusive fate" of its inhabitants. She recognizes its stark contrast to her refined East Egg world, finding something "awful in the very simplicity she failed to understand." Her approval of the elegant movie star and director, contrasted with her disgust for the general party, shows her selective judgment.

Tom's Growing Suspicion and Daisy's Defense

  • Questioning Gatsby's Identity: As they wait for their car, Tom abruptly demands, "Who is this Gatsby anyhow? Some big bootlegger?" This reveals his suspicion about Gatsby's sudden wealth and his prejudiced view of the "newly rich."
  • Daisy's Defense: Daisy quickly defends Gatsby, claiming he "owned some drug-stores, a lot of drug-stores. He built them up himself." This may be a partial truth or a fabricated story designed to legitimize Gatsby in Tom's eyes.
  • Tom's Determination: Tom's insistence ("I'd like to know who he is and what he does... And I think I'll make a point of finding out") foreshadows his later investigation and confrontation with Gatsby.
  • Daisy's Romantic Possibility: Despite her revulsion, Daisy's glance toward the "lighted top of the steps" and the "sad little waltz" suggests a lingering romantic possibility and a sense of something "totally absent from her world" in Gatsby's casual party.

Gatsby's Tragic Dream: To Repeat the Past

  • Gatsby's Disappointment: After the party, Gatsby is visibly distressed and tells Nick, "She didn't like it... She didn't have a good time." This reveals his immense emotional investment in Daisy's approval and his "unutterable depression."
  • The Grand Delusion: Gatsby reveals his ultimate desire: for Daisy to tell Tom she "never loved you," then to return to Louisville and marry "just as if it were five years ago." He wants to erase the entire intervening period. He dismisses the importance of the dance, focusing instead on this monumental, impossible reversal of time.
  • Nick's Reality Check: Nick offers his famous warning, "I wouldn't ask too much of her... You can't repeat the past."
  • Gatsby's Incredulous Response: Gatsby vehemently rejects Nick's advice, crying, "Can't repeat the past?... Why of course you can!" He believes he can "fix everything just the way it was before," revealing his complete detachment from reality. He is trying to recover "something, some idea of himself perhaps, that had gone into loving Daisy."

The Flashback: The Genesis of Gatsby's Dream

  • The Autumn Night: Nick narrates the story of Gatsby's first kiss with Daisy five years prior, on a cool autumn night.
  • The "Ladder" to a Dream: Gatsby perceived the sidewalk blocks as a "ladder" leading to a "secret place above the trees," a metaphorical ascent to his idealized future. He believed that if he climbed alone, he could "suck on the pap of life, gulp down the incomparable milk of wonder."
  • The Moment of Incarnation: When Gatsby kissed Daisy, his heart beat faster. He knew that by kissing her, he would "forever wed his unutterable visions to her perishable breath." His mind would "never romp again like the mind of God" – his boundless potential and imagination would now be irrevocably channeled into the singular pursuit of Daisy. At her lips' touch, "she blossomed for him like a flower and the incarnation was complete." This kiss bound Gatsby to his idealized vision of Daisy, making her the embodiment of his entire life's purpose and dream.
  • Nick's Uncommunicable Understanding: Nick feels a profound, almost mystical understanding of Gatsby's "appalling sentimentality," but the insight remains "uncommunicable forever."

Review Questions

  1. Describe Daisy's reaction to Gatsby's party. What specific details suggest her initial excitement, her moments of connection with Gatsby, and her ultimate disgust with West Egg?
  2. How does Tom's behavior at the party, particularly his comments about "the polo player" and his suspicions about Gatsby's wealth, reinforce his character as an "old money" elitist?
  3. Discuss Gatsby's deep disappointment after the party. What does his reaction reveal about the true nature of his feelings for Daisy and the fragility of his self-perception?
  4. Analyze Gatsby's insistence that he "can repeat the past." What aspects of his character and his American Dream does this belief highlight, and how does it contrast with Nick's more realistic perspective?
  5. Explain the significance of the flashback to Gatsby and Daisy's first kiss. How does this moment, described as an "incarnation," define Gatsby's life path and his pursuit of Daisy?
