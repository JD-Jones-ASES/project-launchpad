Chapter 2, Section 3 Study Guide: The Valley of Ashes

Overview

This section introduces the desolate and industrial "valley of ashes," a grim wasteland between West Egg and New York City. Here, Nick Carraway is forcibly introduced to Tom Buchanan's mistress, Myrtle Wilson, and her unsuspecting husband, George, who owns a struggling garage. The narrative follows Tom, Nick, and Myrtle to a small New York apartment where Myrtle attempts to embody a more sophisticated persona, highlighting themes of social class, moral decay, and the pursuit of superficial dreams.

Key Concepts

  • The Valley of Ashes as a Symbol of Moral and Social Decay: This desolate, dust-choked industrial wasteland represents the blight of unchecked industrialism and the moral decay beneath the glittering surface of the Jazz Age. It's a place where the American Dream has turned to dust, a literal dumping ground for the excesses of the wealthy.
  • Doctor T. J. Eckleburg as a Symbol of Absent Oversight: The gigantic, spectacled eyes on a faded billboard stare down over the valley. They symbolize a forgotten, indifferent "God" or moral authority, watching over a world devoid of spiritual guidance. They also represent the commercialism and superficiality that pervades society, a remnant of a capitalist venture.
  • Class Division and Social Aspirations: The section explicitly contrasts the opulence of West/East Egg with the working-class despair of the valley. Myrtle Wilson, despite her limited means, desperately attempts to ascend socially by having an affair with Tom and acquiring expensive, if tacky, possessions in her New York apartment.
  • Illusion vs. Reality: Myrtle’s transformation in New York, from a garage owner's wife to a vibrant, demanding woman in an overcrowded, gaudy apartment, exemplifies the pervasive theme of illusion. She creates a superficial persona fueled by materialism, but it is ultimately hollow and dependent on Tom's patronage.
  • Tom Buchanan's Dominance and Arrogance: Tom's forceful manner, his open disregard for his marriage, and his condescending attitude towards George Wilson underscore his privileged and entitled nature. He treats people, especially those of a lower class, as possessions or instruments for his pleasure.

Important Terms

  • Valley of Ashes: A desolate, industrial wasteland located between West Egg and New York City, characterized by gray dust and smoke. It symbolizes the waste, poverty, and moral decay resulting from the pursuit of wealth and industrial expansion.
  • Grotesque: Comically or repulsively ugly or distorted; incongruous or inappropriate to a shocking degree. Used to describe the "gardens" of ashes and the general appearance of the valley.
  • Ash-gray men: The workers in the valley of ashes, covered in industrial dust, making them appear ghostly and dehumanized. They represent the exploited working class.
  • Doctor T. J. Eckleburg: A faded billboard advertisement featuring enormous, bespectacled eyes that "brood" over the valley of ashes. It serves as a potent symbol of absent moral oversight, commercialism, or even a godlike, unseeing presence.
  • Supercilious: Behaving or looking as though one thinks one is superior to others. Used to describe Tom Buchanan's assumption that Nick had nothing better to do than accompany him.
  • Unprosperous: Not flourishing or successful, especially financially. Describes George Wilson's garage, reflecting his meager circumstances.
  • Anaemic: Lacking in color, spirit, or vitality. Used to describe George Wilson, highlighting his weak and lifeless demeanor.
  • Vitality: The state of being strong, active, and full of energy; liveliness. A key characteristic of Myrtle Wilson, contrasting sharply with her husband's spiritlessness.
  • Interposed: Placed or inserted between one thing and another; interjected. Describes Tom's action when he quickly stops Nick from leaving.
  • Regal: Resembling, or fit for, a monarch, especially in being magnificent or dignified. Used ironically to describe Mrs. Wilson's haughty glance around her neighborhood.
  • Haughtily: Arrogantly superior and disdainful. Describes Myrtle's manner as she enters the apartment building, trying to project an air of importance.
  • Tapestried furniture: Furniture upholstered with heavy fabric featuring woven designs. The apartment is crowded with such furniture, too large for the space, symbolizing Myrtle's attempt at grandeur that is ultimately ill-fitting and ostentatious.

Detailed Notes

The Setting: The Valley of Ashes

  • Location and Appearance: The motor road from West Egg to New York temporarily merges with the railroad to avoid this "desolate area." It is described as a "fantastic farm" where ashes grow like grotesque gardens, forming ridges and hills.
  • Symbolism of Ashes: The ashes represent waste, industrial decay, moral barrenness, and the crumbling remnants of failed dreams. It's the byproduct of the glittering wealth of New York and the Eggs.
  • The "Ash-gray Men": Workers in the valley are perpetually covered in dust, appearing as "men who move dimly and already crumbling." They are dehumanized by their labor, blending into their灰蒙蒙 surroundings.
  • Doctor T. J. Eckleburg's Eyes:
    • Description: A gigantic, faded billboard with blue, spectacled eyes (one yard high) overlooking the valley. They look out from "no face," but from "enormous yellow spectacles."
    • Origin: Placed there by an oculist (eye doctor) in Queens who either "sank down himself into eternal blindness, or forgot them and moved away."
    • Symbolism: These eyes are a powerful symbol throughout the novel. They represent a sense of watchful, yet indifferent, divine judgment, the superficiality of advertising, or the decay of spiritual values in a materialistic world. They "brood on over the solemn dumping ground," suggesting a silent witness to the characters' moral failures.

The Introduction of Myrtle and George Wilson

  • Tom's Affair: Nick recounts that Tom Buchanan's affair is widely known and resented by his acquaintances. Nick had been curious to meet her but had no desire to, yet Tom forces the encounter.
  • Meeting at the Train Halt: Trains stopping at the drawbridge near the valley provide the opportunity for Tom to meet Myrtle. Tom, possibly intoxicated, physically compels Nick to get off the train.
  • George B. Wilson's Garage:
    • Location: A small block of yellow brick, "contiguous to absolutely nothing," situated on the edge of the wasteland. It contains a garage, a for-rent shop, and an all-night restaurant.
    • Appearance: The garage interior is "unprosperous and bare," with only a "dust-covered wreck of a Ford" visible.
    • George Wilson: The proprietor, a "blond, spiritless man, anaemic, and faintly handsome." He appears weak and submissive, dependent on Tom for business (the promise of a car sale). A "damp gleam of hope" springs into his eyes upon seeing Tom, quickly dashed by Tom's coldness. He is veiled by the "white ashen dust" of his surroundings.
    • Relationship with Tom: Tom treats George with contempt and condescension, exercising power over him through a delayed car deal. George is clearly intimidated by Tom.

Myrtle Wilson's Introduction and Character

  • Physicality: She is in her "middle thirties," "faintly stout," but carries her "surplus flesh sensuously." She has "no facet or gleam of beauty" in her face but possesses an "immediately perceptible vitality."
  • Interaction with George: She "walk[s] through her husband as if he were a ghost," demonstrating her disdain and disregard for him. Her voice is "soft, coarse" when speaking to him, and she gives him orders.
  • Interaction with Tom: She shakes Tom's hand, looking him "flush in the eye," showing an assertiveness and confidence absent with her husband. They quickly arrange a rendezvous in New York.
  • Tom's Deception: Tom reveals that George believes Myrtle visits her sister in New York, adding, "He's so dumb he doesn't know he's alive." This highlights Tom's casual cruelty and George's oblivious nature.

The Journey to New York and the Apartment

  • Myrtle's Transformation:
    • Appearance: On the train to New York, Myrtle changes into a "brown figured muslin" dress that stretches tightly over her hips. This marks her transition from the dreary Valley of Ashes to a more aspirational (though still provincial) persona.
    • Purchases: At the station, she buys TOWN TATTLE. (a gossip magazine), a moving-picture magazine, cold cream, and perfume—all signs of her desire for glamour and superficial refinement.
    • The Dog: She insists on buying a "police dog" from a street vendor for her apartment, settling for an "Airedale" for ten dollars. Tom dismissively identifies it as a "bitch," highlighting his power and her eagerness to please.
    • Taxi: She deliberately chooses a "lavender-colored with gray upholstery" taxi, indicating her taste for showy displays.
  • The Apartment:
    • Location: 158th Street, "one slice in a long white cake of apartment-houses."
    • Interior: Small living-room, dining-room, bedroom, and bath. It is "crowded to the doors with a set of tapestried furniture entirely too large for it," suggesting an attempt at grandeur that is overwhelming and ill-fitting.
    • Decor: The "over-enlarged photograph" of a stout old lady (initially mistaken for a hen) and "old copies of TOWN TATTLE." and "small scandal magazines of Broadway" on the table reveal a tacky, gossip-filled, and superficial environment.
  • The Gathering:
    • Myrtle announces she will call her sister Catherine (described as "very beautiful") and the McKees.
    • Tom immediately retrieves whiskey from a locked bureau.
    • Nick gets drunk for the second time in his life, leading to a "dim, hazy cast" over subsequent events.
    • Myrtle, once in the apartment, becomes increasingly assertive and self-important, calling people and adopting an air of hostess. She and Nick are soon calling each other by their first names.

Review Questions

  1. How does the description of the Valley of Ashes serve as a symbolic commentary on the American Dream and the societal consequences of industrialization during the Jazz Age?
  2. Analyze the symbolism of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg's eyes. What different interpretations can be drawn from their presence in the Valley of Ashes?
  3. Compare and contrast George and Myrtle Wilson's characters and their respective relationships with Tom Buchanan. What do these relationships reveal about social class and power dynamics in the novel?
  4. Describe Myrtle Wilson's behavior and purchases once she arrives in New York City. What do these actions reveal about her aspirations, her perception of herself, and her attempts at social mobility?
  5. What is Nick Carraway's role in this section? How does his narration (especially his growing intoxication) affect the reader's perception of the events unfolding in Myrtle's apartment?
