Study Guide: F. Scott Fitzgerald (1896-1940)

Key Concepts

  • Fitzgerald was born in St. Paul, Minnesota, into a family caught between middle-class and upper-class worlds — his father's business failures and his mother's prosperous Irish immigrant background created a lifelong tension between social aspiration and financial insecurity
  • He attended the Newman School and Princeton University but never graduated, devoting himself to writing rather than academics
  • His courtship with Zelda Sayre — who broke off their engagement because he lacked financial prospects — directly shaped the backstory of Gatsby and Daisy in the novel
  • The publication of This Side of Paradise (1920) launched Fitzgerald and Zelda into Jazz Age celebrity; their life was glamorous but ultimately tragic, marked by Zelda's mental breakdowns and Fitzgerald's alcoholism
  • Fitzgerald can be identified with both Jay Gatsby (the Midwesterner who reinvented himself) and Nick Carraway (the moralist who sees corruption beneath the glamour)
  • At the time of his death in 1940, The Great Gatsby was out of print and Fitzgerald was considered a relic; his full literary vindication came posthumously

Summary

Francis Scott Key Fitzgerald was born in 1896 into a family that embodied the tension between social ambition and financial reality. His father came from a distinguished Maryland family but repeatedly failed in business; his mother was the daughter of a successful Irish immigrant grocer. This uneasy class position — never quite secure, never quite belonging — became one of the driving preoccupations of his fiction. He left Princeton without graduating and received a military commission in 1917, during which he met Zelda Sayre, whose rejection of him for lack of money would become the emotional core of The Great Gatsby.

When This Side of Paradise became a bestseller in 1920, Zelda agreed to marry him, and the couple became emblems of the Jazz Age. They lived extravagantly in New York, Long Island, and France, spending as fast as Fitzgerald earned. The decade took a heavy toll: Zelda suffered a series of mental breakdowns beginning in 1930, and Fitzgerald struggled with alcoholism. His follow-up novel, Tender Is the Night (1934), appeared as his reputation was fading. He spent his final years in Hollywood as a screenwriter and died of a heart attack in 1940 at age forty-four, his masterpiece out of print.

The parallels between Fitzgerald's life and Gatsby's story are real but should not be oversimplified. Like Gatsby, Fitzgerald was a Midwesterner who remade himself in the East and pursued a woman whose world seemed closed to him. But he was also Nick Carraway — the observer who perceived the corruption beneath the glamour even as he was drawn to it. The Great Gatsby draws on personal experience but transcends autobiography to become a meditation on American aspiration itself.

Key Terms

  • Jazz Age: The term Fitzgerald himself coined for the 1920s, a decade of cultural vitality, economic expansion, and social transformation
  • This Side of Paradise: Fitzgerald's debut novel (1920), whose bestseller success enabled his marriage to Zelda and launched his public celebrity
  • Tender Is the Night: Fitzgerald's 1934 novel about the disintegration of an American couple on the French Riviera, published as his reputation was declining
  • The Last Tycoon: Fitzgerald's final, unfinished novel, begun during his Hollywood screenwriting years

Review Questions

  1. What tension in Fitzgerald's family background became a central theme in his fiction?
  2. How did Zelda Sayre's initial rejection of Fitzgerald's marriage proposal influence the plot of The Great Gatsby?
  3. In what ways does Fitzgerald resemble Jay Gatsby? In what ways does he resemble Nick Carraway?
  4. What was the state of Fitzgerald's reputation at the time of his death in 1940, and why is this ironic given the novel's later status?
