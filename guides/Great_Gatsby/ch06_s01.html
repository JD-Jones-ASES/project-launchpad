Study Guide: Chapter 5: The Novel

Key Concepts

  • Gatsby arranges an elaborate reunion with Daisy at Nick's cottage: flowers, lawn mowing, fine clothes
  • The initial meeting is painfully awkward — Gatsby is pale and rigid, nearly knocks over a mantelpiece clock
  • After Nick retreats into the rain for half an hour, the atmosphere transforms: Gatsby is radiant, Daisy tearful but joyful
  • Gatsby tours Daisy through his mansion, revaluing every possession through her response to it
  • Daisy weeps over Gatsby's beautiful shirts — an enigmatic emotional climax
  • Gatsby points out the green light across the bay, recognizing that its symbolic power has diminished now that Daisy is physically present
  • As Nick departs, a flicker of bewilderment crosses Gatsby's face — a hint that the real Daisy may not match his five-year dream
  • Klipspringer, "the boarder," plays piano including the song "Ain't We Got Fun"

Summary

The night before the reunion, Gatsby haunts his blazing mansion in a state of nervous energy, then intercepts Nick to confirm the tea arrangement. Despite his apparent casualness, Gatsby sends over a gardener to cut Nick's lawn and has an entire greenhouse of flowers delivered the morning of Daisy's visit. He arrives early in an immaculate white flannel suit, silver shirt, and gold tie, pale and sleepless beneath his polished exterior.

When Daisy arrives in the pouring rain and Gatsby slips in through the front door, the meeting is agonizing: he is rigid against the mantelpiece, nearly knocking over a defunct clock, and speaks in strained half-sentences. After Nick retreats outside for half an hour, he returns to find an entirely different scene — Gatsby glowing, Daisy tear-streaked but joyful, all awkwardness dissolved.

Gatsby escorts them across to his mansion for a tour of its opulent rooms: Marie Antoinette salons, period bedrooms, a gold-bristled toilet set, and closets full of shirts in coral, apple-green, and lavender, imported from England each season. When Gatsby throws the shirts before Daisy, she buries her face in them and weeps — "such beautiful shirts." Standing at the window, Gatsby points out the green light at the end of Daisy's dock and notes that without the mist, he could see her home. He falls briefly silent, as though registering that the light's enchantment has faded. As Nick leaves, he catches a faint expression of bewilderment on Gatsby's face — a quiet recognition that Daisy "tumbled short of his dreams."

Key Terms

  • defunct mantelpiece clock: The broken clock at Nick's cottage that Gatsby nearly knocks over during the reunion; it symbolizes Gatsby's attempt to reverse or stop time
  • ecstatic: In a state of overwhelming happiness or rapture; used to describe Gatsby's transformed expression after the reunion succeeds
  • vestige: A trace or remnant of something disappearing; Nick uses it to describe the last traces of embarrassment vanishing between Gatsby and Daisy
  • inconceivable: Beyond what can be imagined; used to describe the pitch of intensity at which Gatsby has sustained his dream of Daisy
  • Klipspringer: Ewing Klipspringer, the "boarder" who has been living at Gatsby's mansion so long he seems to have no other home; plays piano at Gatsby's command

Review Questions

  1. Why does Gatsby make such elaborate preparations for what is officially just a simple tea visit? What do the flowers, the lawn, and the outfit reveal about his state of mind?
  2. Describe the mood of the initial meeting between Gatsby and Daisy. What specific details signal Gatsby's anxiety?
  3. What does the mantelpiece clock represent in this scene, and why is Gatsby's near-toppling of it significant?
  4. Why does Daisy weep over Gatsby's shirts? What might her tears suggest about what she is feeling?
  5. Gatsby points out the green light and then falls silent. What shift in his understanding does this moment represent?
