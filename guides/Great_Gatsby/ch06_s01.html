Study Guide: Chapter 6, Section 1 - The Truth About Gatsby

Overview

Chapter 6 pulls back the curtain on Jay Gatsby, revealing his humble origins as James Gatz and his self-reinvention through the mentorship of Dan Cody. The narrative then shifts to a pivotal Gatsby party, where Tom Buchanan's condescension and Daisy's discomfort expose the fragility of Gatsby's meticulously constructed world. The chapter culminates in Gatsby's desperate confession of his desire to literally repeat the past with Daisy, showcasing his profound belief in his own idealized reality.

Key Concepts

  • Gatsby's Self-Invention: Gatsby was born James Gatz, a poor farm boy. He meticulously created the persona of "Jay Gatsby" as a direct embodiment of his idealized self, shedding his past identity to pursue a grander vision of life.
  • The Influence of Dan Cody: Gatsby's five years serving as Dan Cody's protégé exposed him to the world of wealth and luxury, shaping his manners, aspirations, and understanding of the rich, despite being cheated out of his inheritance.
  • The Platonic Conception of Self: Gatsby "sprang from his Platonic conception of himself," meaning he created an ideal, perfect version of himself in his imagination (an ideal "Form") and spent his life trying to make the material world conform to this vision. His belief in repeating the past stems from this philosophical framework.
  • Contrast of Old Money vs. New Money: Tom Buchanan's contempt for Gatsby's party and his "new money" associates highlights the rigid social hierarchy and the disdain the established elite held for those who earned their wealth.
  • The Fragility of Gatsby's Dream: Daisy's revulsion at the vulgarity of West Egg and her inability to share Gatsby's idealized view of the past demonstrate that his carefully constructed dream relies on others accepting his reality, which they are unwilling or unable to do.
  • The Impossibility of Repeating the Past: Gatsby's adamant belief that "of course you can!" repeat the past clashes with Nick's more realistic perspective, underscoring the novel's central theme about the elusive nature of time and memory.

Important Terms

  • Notoriety: The state of being famous or well known, typically for something negative or disreputable.
  • Insidious: Proceeding in a gradual, subtle way but with harmful effects.
  • Shiftless: Lacking ambition or purpose; lazy.
  • Meretricious: Apparently attractive but having in reality no value or integrity; showily but falsely alluring.
  • Turgid: Swollen and distended; also, of language, pompously inflated.
  • Contingencies: Future events or circumstances that are possible but cannot be predicted with certainty.
  • Debauchee: A person given to excessive indulgence in sensual pleasures.
  • Ingratiate: To bring oneself into favor with someone by flattering or trying to please them.
  • Septic: Infected with harmful bacteria; figuratively, corrupted or poisoned.
  • Obtrusive: Noticeable in an unwelcome or intrusive way.
  • Dilatory: Slow to act; causing delay.
  • Incarnation: The embodiment of a quality or idea in physical form.

Detailed Notes

The Unveiling of James Gatz: Gatsby's True Origin

Chapter 6 marks a pivotal moment in The Great Gatsby as the narrator, Nick Carraway, finally uncovers the truth behind the enigmatic Jay Gatsby. We learn that Gatsby was born James Gatz to "shiftless and unsuccessful farm people" in North Dakota. At seventeen, Gatz underwent a profound transformation, not merely changing his name, but fundamentally reinventing his identity. This reinvention occurred on Lake Superior when he encountered the millionaire Dan Cody, a "pioneer debauchee" and a man who represented the lavish, uninhibited wealth Gatsby aspired to.

For five years, James Gatz served as Cody's protector and protégé, managing his affairs and absorbing the manners, aspirations, and superficial glamour of immense wealth. Though Gatz was ultimately cheated out of the inheritance Cody intended for him, this period was crucial in shaping the "Jay Gatsby" persona. Gatsby dedicated himself to "a vast, vulgar, and meretricious beauty," a showy but ultimately false allure that he believed defined true success.

The Platonic Conception of Self

Central to Gatsby's self-creation is the idea that he "sprang from his Platonic conception of himself." This refers to the philosophy of Plato, who posited that ideal, perfect "Forms" exist independently of the imperfect material world. Gatsby did not simply change his name; he conceived of an ideal "Jay Gatsby" within his imagination—a perfect, abstract version of himself—and then devoted his life to making his earthly existence conform to that perfect vision. His humble origins, his name James Gatz, and his poverty were all part of the "flawed world of appearances" that he believed could be discarded in favor of his self-created ideal.

The Clash of Worlds: Tom, Daisy, and the West Egg Party

The chapter's second half brings the contrasting worlds of East Egg (old money, established society) and West Egg (new money, Gatsby's lavish parties) into direct conflict. Tom Buchanan, representing the inherited privilege of old money, visits Gatsby's estate. His interactions with Gatsby are marked by casual condescension and thinly veiled contempt for Gatsby's ostentatious display of wealth.

When Tom and Daisy attend one of Gatsby's parties, the evening becomes a disaster from Gatsby's perspective. Daisy, unaccustomed to the unrestrained, often vulgar atmosphere of West Egg, is "appalled" by what she sees. What once amused Nick, observing the endless stream of partygoers, "turned septic on the air" when seen through Daisy's critical eyes. She is repelled by "the too obtrusive fate" that seems to govern the lives of the partygoers. Meanwhile, Tom, always suspicious and possessive, grows increasingly wary of Gatsby's mysterious background and his evident connection to Daisy.

Gatsby's Impossible Demand: Repeating the Past

After the guests depart, Gatsby, deeply disturbed by Daisy's reaction, confides in Nick his ultimate, almost impossible, demand. He insists that Daisy must tell Tom that she never loved him and then, crucially, return with Gatsby to Louisville to relive their past exactly as it was five years prior. This demand is not merely nostalgic; it is a direct consequence of Gatsby's Platonic worldview. For him, the ideal Form of their love, the perfect moment of their first kiss, is the true reality. The intervening five years of Daisy's marriage and motherhood are merely illusions that can be overridden.

Nick, with his pragmatic understanding of time, warns Gatsby that "You can't repeat the past." Gatsby's reply is immediate, "incredulous," and defining: "Can't repeat the past? Why of course you can!" This statement is not simple naïveté; it is the logical conclusion of his belief that the ideal is more real than the actual. If the ideal is true, then the actual (Daisy's current life) can be changed to match it.

Tom as a Foil and the Tragic Flaw

Fitzgerald uses Tom Buchanan as a powerful "foil" to Gatsby. Tom embodies everything Gatsby is not: he possesses old money rather than new, inherited status rather than self-made mythology, and crude physical dominance rather than romantic aspiration. Tom's disdain for Gatsby's party and his demand to know "Who is this Gatsby anyhow?" represent the harsh reality of social judgment pushing back against Gatsby's idealized self. Daisy's discomfort at the party further signals that reality is already winning. She cannot share Gatsby's vision; she sees the world through her own lens, which is shaped by her old-money upbringing.

The tragic flaw in Gatsby's grand self-conception is that it fundamentally depends on other people—especially Daisy—accepting and conforming to his version of reality. They, however, are under no obligation to do so, making his dream inherently vulnerable.

The Incarnation of a Dream

The chapter closes with Nick's lyrical description of Gatsby's first kiss with Daisy five years earlier in Louisville. This moment was when Gatsby permanently "wed his unutterable visions to her perishable breath." In that kiss, Gatsby's abstract, idealized dream of wealth and love took on concrete form. "The incarnation was complete," signifying the moment his vast, unexpressed aspirations became inextricably linked to Daisy, transforming her into the living embodiment of his life's pursuit. This beautiful, yet vulnerable, union sets the stage for the tragic unraveling of Gatsby's dream.

Review Questions

  1. How does Gatsby's past as James Gatz, particularly his time with Dan Cody, contradict or reinforce the image he projects as "Jay Gatsby"?
  2. Explain the concept of Gatsby's "Platonic conception of himself." How does this philosophical idea influence his actions and desires, especially his steadfast belief that he can "repeat the past"?
  3. Compare and contrast the reactions of Tom and Daisy Buchanan to Gatsby's party. What do their responses reveal about their respective characters, their social class, and their perception of Gatsby?
  4. Why is Gatsby's demand that Daisy tell Tom she never loved him so crucial to his overall dream? What does this reveal about the depth and nature of his idealized vision of their relationship?
  5. Nick describes Gatsby "wedding his unutterable visions to her perishable breath" during their first kiss. What does this metaphor suggest about the nature of Gatsby's love for Daisy and the inherent vulnerability of his dream?
