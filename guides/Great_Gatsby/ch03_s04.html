Study Guide: Chapter 3, Section 4 – Gatsby's Party

Overview

This section continues Nick Carraway's description of Gatsby's lavish party, delving into the chaotic atmosphere as the night progresses and Gatsby's mysterious nature becomes more apparent. It culminates in a bizarre car accident involving two of Gatsby's guests and a reflective shift by Nick to his own personal life and ambitions in New York. The scene highlights the moral decay and carelessness prevalent among the wealthy elite.

Key Concepts

  • Gatsby's Enigma and Isolation: Despite being the host of an enormous party, Gatsby remains a figure of mystery and isolation. He doesn't drink, observes his guests rather than participates fully in their revelry, and is sought out for private conversations, reinforcing his separateness from the boisterous crowd. His formal farewells and immediate phone call suggest a life beyond the party's immediate superficiality.
  • Decadence and Moral Decay: The party descends into drunken arguments, emotional breakdowns, and reckless behavior, particularly evident in the fighting couples and the egregious car accident. This illustrates the moral emptiness and irresponsibility of many of Gatsby's guests, who engage in self-indulgent excess without consequence or self-awareness.
  • Carelessness and Irresponsibility: The car accident involving Owl Eyes and another unnamed, drunken driver serves as a powerful symbol of the extreme carelessness that pervades the West Egg social scene. The drivers' complete lack of concern for the damage or the potential danger highlights a broader societal disregard for rules and consequences.
  • Jordan Baker's Secrecy and Urbane Detachment: Jordan is portrayed as elusive and private, evidenced by her comment on the "intimacy" of large parties and her secretive meeting with Gatsby. Her subsequent tantalizing remarks to Nick after her private conversation heighten the mystery surrounding Gatsby and her own guarded personality.
  • Nick's Role as Observer and Moral Compass: Nick's narration frequently positions him as an observer, noting the absurdity and excess around him. His provincial idealism is challenged by the scene, yet he maintains a degree of detachment. The section concludes with Nick shifting focus to his own disciplined, hardworking life, providing a stark contrast to the extravagance and moral laxity of the partygoers.
  • The "Jazz Age" Atmosphere: The mention of "Vladimir Tostoff's JAZZ HISTORY OF THE WORLD" and the ensuing boisterous, slightly uncontrolled party atmosphere perfectly encapsulate the wild, energetic, and often chaotic spirit of the 1920s Jazz Age.

Important Terms

  • Echolalia: (Figurative use) The repetitive or meaningless repetition of another person's spoken words, here used to describe the murmuring, indistinct chatter of the party guests.
  • Jovial condescension: A cheerful or hearty attitude that also implies a patronizing or superior air, as shown by the orchestra leader.
  • Fraternal hilarity: Friendly, boisterous, and unrestrained merriment among a group, typically men, but here applied to the mixed-gender party guests.
  • Puppyish, convivial: Playfully affectionate and friendly, describing the guests' increasingly uninhibited interactions.
  • Vinous sleep: A deep sleep induced by excessive consumption of wine or alcohol.
  • Rent asunder: Torn apart or violently separated, referring to the arguments breaking up Jordan's party and other couples.
  • Flank attacks: (Figurative use) Indirect or roundabout aggressive approaches; here, a wife's sudden, insistent confrontations with her husband.
  • Indignant: Feeling or showing anger or annoyance at what is perceived as unfair treatment.
  • Apparition: A ghost-like figure or phantom; here, used to describe the disoriented, drunken second driver emerging from the wrecked car.
  • Crescendo: The loudest point reached in a gradually increasing sound; here, the climax of the car horns' blaring.
  • Wafer of a moon: A very thin, crescent moon, suggesting a delicate, almost insubstantial presence in the sky.
  • Probity Trust: A fictional financial institution where Nick works, representing his commitment to a principled and trustworthy career. "Probity" means strong moral principles; honesty and decency.
  • "Old sport": Gatsby's characteristic form of address, meant to sound casually aristocratic and British. Its repeated use suggests Gatsby's carefully constructed persona and his effort to project old-money refinement.

Detailed Notes

The Winding Down of Gatsby's Party

  • Nick's Continuing Curiosity: Nick's initial curiosity about Gatsby's origins is piqued by the rumors ("I think he killed a man"). He finds Gatsby's sudden appearance and wealth incomprehensible given his own "provincial inexperience."
  • Jordan's Paradoxical View: Jordan changes the subject, noting that "large parties" are "intimate" because "at small parties there isn't any privacy." This reflects her urbane, detached perspective.
  • The "Jazz History of the World": The orchestra leader introduces a sensational modern jazz piece, emblematic of the era's innovative and perhaps chaotic cultural shifts.
  • Gatsby's Solitary Nature: As the music plays, Nick observes Gatsby standing alone, sober, on the steps, watching his guests. He appears "more correct" as the "fraternal hilarity" increases, highlighting his detachment and contrasting demeanor. No one interacts casually with Gatsby as they do with each other.
  • Gatsby's Private Request: Gatsby's butler suddenly appears to summon Jordan Baker for a private conversation with Gatsby. Jordan expresses surprise and follows, raising her eyebrows at Nick.
  • Party's Decline into Chaos: Nick wanders into a crowded room where a drunken chorus girl is weepily singing. He observes increasing discord among the guests, particularly fights between couples, some of whom are supposedly married. Jordan's own party from East Egg is "rent asunder by dissension."
  • Reluctance to Leave: Many guests resist leaving, including "deplorably sober men and their highly indignant wives" who complain about being the first to leave, even as the orchestra has already departed. The scene concludes with two wives being physically "lifted, kicking, into the night" by their husbands.

Jordan's Secret and Gatsby's Farewell

  • The Amazing Revelation: Jordan and Gatsby emerge from the library, and Jordan, in a hushed, excited tone, tells Nick she has heard "the most amazing thing" but swore not to tell. She tantalizes him before rushing off, leaving him with an invitation and a reference to her aunt, Mrs. Sigourney Howard.
  • Gatsby's Formal Farewell: Gatsby greets the last remaining guests, including Nick, with an eager yet formal manner. He uses his characteristic phrase "old sport" and mentions a hydroplane ride for the next morning.
  • The Mysterious Phone Call: Gatsby is interrupted by his butler, who informs him that "Philadelphia wants you on the 'phone, sir," hinting at his business dealings and ongoing engagements beyond the party. Nick feels a "pleasant significance" in being among the last to leave, perceiving it as a desired outcome for Gatsby.

The Car Accident: A Symbol of Carelessness

  • The Bizarre Wreck: Outside Gatsby's gates, a new coupe has crashed into a wall, losing a wheel. The scene is chaotic, with chauffeurs and horns blaring.
  • Owl Eyes' Ignorance: The first person to emerge from the wreck is Owl Eyes, the library patron, who is comically bewildered by the situation. He claims to know "nothing whatever about mechanics" and "very little about driving," disclaiming all responsibility.
  • The True Driver Revealed: Owl Eyes reveals that he wasn't driving, and a second, even more intoxicated and disoriented man emerges from the car, swaying and completely oblivious to the extent of the damage ("Wha's matter? Did we run outa gas?"). His attempts to "back out" the car, despite the missing wheel, highlight extreme incompetence and carelessness.
  • Symbolism: The accident serves as a stark symbol of the reckless and irresponsible behavior of the wealthy elite at Gatsby's parties, their detachment from consequences, and their reliance on others (like chauffeurs) to navigate the complexities of life.

Nick's Return to Reality and Personal Ambitions

  • Contrast to His Own Life: Nick shifts his narrative focus, clarifying that the party events were "merely casual events in a crowded summer" and less absorbing than his "personal affairs."
  • His Work and Routine: He describes his disciplined daily routine: working at the "Probity Trust" as a bond-salesman, lunching with colleagues, a brief affair in Jersey City, and studying investments at the Yale Club library.
  • Nick's Moral Grounding: This section firmly establishes Nick's character as hardworking, financially responsible, and earnest, providing a moral counterpoint to the superficial and dissolute world he observes in West Egg. He is actively building his life, unlike many of the idle rich.

Review Questions

  1. How does Gatsby's behavior at his own party, particularly his sobriety and isolation, contribute to his mysterious image in Nick's eyes?
  2. Analyze Jordan Baker's comment that "large parties... are so intimate. At small parties there isn't any privacy." What does this statement reveal about her character and her perception of social interactions?
  3. Describe the descent into chaos at Gatsby's party as the night progresses. What specific examples of behavior illustrate the moral decay and irresponsibility among the guests?
  4. Discuss the significance of the car accident involving Owl Eyes and the other drunken man. What does this incident symbolize about the values and conduct of the people attending Gatsby's parties?
  5. How does Nick Carraway's concluding reflection on his own life and daily routine serve as a contrast to the events and characters at Gatsby's party? What does this contrast suggest about Nick's perspective and his place in this new environment?
