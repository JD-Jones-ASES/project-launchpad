Study Guide: Chapter 2: The Novel

Key Concepts

  • The valley of ashes is a desolate industrial wasteland between West Egg and New York, where ash-gray workers move through clouds of dust
  • The eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg — a faded billboard advertisement for an oculist — brood over the valley like a godlike but indifferent witness
  • Tom Buchanan takes Nick to Wilson's garage to meet his mistress, Myrtle Wilson, the wife of the garage owner George Wilson
  • At the 158th Street apartment Tom keeps for the affair, a drunken party forms with Myrtle's sister Catherine and the McKees from downstairs
  • Myrtle transforms her personality when she changes into a cream-colored chiffon dress, adopting affected mannerisms and airs of upper-class hauteur
  • The party ends violently when Tom breaks Myrtle's nose after she repeatedly shouts Daisy's name

Summary

The chapter opens with a vivid description of the valley of ashes — a grotesque landscape of industrial waste halfway between West Egg and New York, overshadowed by the enormous painted eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg on a faded billboard. It is here that Tom Buchanan drags Nick off the train to meet his mistress, Myrtle Wilson, the wife of George Wilson, a pale and spiritless garage owner who blends into the ash-gray walls of his failing shop. Myrtle, by contrast, enters with an "immediately perceptible vitality," walking through her husband "as if he were a ghost." Tom arranges for Myrtle to follow them into New York while deceiving George about her destination.

In the city, Myrtle buys a puppy of dubious breed from a street vendor and insists on stopping at an apartment Tom keeps at 158th Street. A small, overcrowded party assembles: Myrtle's sister Catherine, who spreads the lie that Daisy is Catholic and cannot grant Tom a divorce, and the McKees from below — a photographer and his shrill wife. Myrtle changes into an elaborate cream-colored dress and her personality shifts with it, transforming her garage vitality into an affected hauteur as she holds court in the cramped rooms.

As the afternoon dissolves into a drunken evening, Myrtle begins shouting Daisy's name, asserting her right to say it. In a single brutal gesture, Tom breaks her nose. The party collapses. Nick departs in an alcoholic haze and ends the chapter half-asleep at Pennsylvania Station, waiting for an early morning train.

Key Terms

  • Valley of ashes: The desolate industrial wasteland between West Egg and New York, symbolizing the dark underside of wealth and prosperity
  • Eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg: A faded billboard featuring enormous bespectacled eyes that brood over the valley of ashes; functions as a symbol of moral oversight — or its absence
  • Grotesque: Comically or repulsively ugly or distorted; used to describe the ash formations that take the shapes of houses, chimneys, and men
  • Vitality: The state of being strong, active, and full of energy; used to describe Myrtle Wilson's forceful presence when she enters
  • Hauteur: An air of superiority and disdain; the manner Myrtle adopts after changing into her cream-colored dress
  • Contiguous: Sharing a common border; immediately adjacent — used to describe the garage's bleak surroundings

Review Questions

  1. How does Fitzgerald describe the valley of ashes, and where is it located in relation to the other key settings?
  2. What are the eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg, and what makes them an unsettling presence in the chapter?
  3. How does Tom treat George Wilson at the garage, and what does this reveal about the power dynamic between them?
  4. How does Myrtle's behavior change between the garage and the apartment, and what causes the change?
  5. What triggers the violent ending of the apartment party, and how does Tom respond?
