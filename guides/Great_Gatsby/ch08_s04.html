Study Guide: Chapter 8, Section 4: Gatsby's Vigil

Overview

This pivotal section opens with Nick's growing emotional detachment from the superficial world of the wealthy, highlighted by his cold interaction with Jordan. The narrative then shifts into a detailed flashback, recounting the immediate aftermath of Myrtle Wilson's death through the eyes of Michaelis, a neighbor. We witness George Wilson's spiraling grief, his mounting suspicion fueled by the discovery of a dog leash, and his eventual delusion that the eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg are the eyes of God watching all. The section culminates in Wilson's relentless hunt for the owner of the yellow car, leading him to Gatsby's estate, where Gatsby is found tragically waiting for a phone call from Daisy that never comes, signifying the final collapse of his lifelong dream.

Key Concepts

  • Nick's Detachment and Disillusionment: Nick's emotional withdrawal from Jordan and the wealthy East Egg/West Egg crowd signals his growing revulsion for their carelessness and moral bankruptcy, a stark contrast to his initial fascination.
  • George Wilson's Descent into Madness and Vengeance: Overwhelmed by grief and betrayal, Wilson succumbs to delusion, interpreting the billboard eyes as a divine presence. His isolation and single-minded focus on vengeance drive him to track down the perceived killer of his wife.
  • The Symbolism of the Dog Leash: This seemingly minor detail becomes a powerful symbol of Myrtle's secret life and infidelity, providing Wilson with what he believes is concrete evidence of her deception and, by extension, the identity of her lover.
  • The "Eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg" as God: Wilson's belief that the billboard eyes are an omniscient deity watching over the Valley of Ashes transforms a commercial advertisement into a profound, terrifying symbol of moral judgment and truth.
  • Gatsby's Fatal Hope and the Death of a Dream: Gatsby's final hours are marked by his desperate, futile wait for Daisy's call, clinging to the last vestiges of his dream. His death at this moment signifies the complete failure of his illusion and the crushing reality of his unattainable ideal.
  • Tragic Irony and Foreshadowing: The section is rich with dramatic irony, as the reader knows the truth about the yellow car and Daisy's involvement, while Wilson is tragically misled. Gatsby's vigil by the pool, awaiting a phone call that would never absolve him, underscores his tragic destiny.

Important Terms

  • Hempstead/Southampton: Locations mentioned by Jordan Baker, indicating her movements away from Daisy's house and further into the social circles of the wealthy elite.
  • Ashheaps (Valley of Ashes): The desolate, industrial wasteland between West Egg and New York City, a recurring symbol of moral decay and the neglected underbelly of American society, and the site of Myrtle's death.
  • Central: The telephone operator in the early 20th century, a common reference in the era before automated dialing.
  • Michaelis: A Greek neighbor of George Wilson who owns a coffee shop near the garage. He is the primary witness to Wilson's grief and breakdown in the hours following Myrtle's death, attempting to offer comfort and reason.
  • Catherine: Myrtle Wilson's sister, who arrives at the garage intoxicated and largely unhelpful in the immediate aftermath of the accident.
  • Doctor T. J. Eckleburg: The eyes on a billboard advertising an optometrist, located in the Valley of Ashes. George Wilson hallucinates these eyes as the all-seeing eyes of God.
  • Pneumatic mattress: An air-filled mattress that Gatsby uses in his pool, symbolizing his desire for leisure and comfort, yet becoming the site of his tragic demise.
  • Wolfshiem's proteges: Refers to people associated with Meyer Wolfshiem, Gatsby's business partner, hinting at the illicit and perhaps dangerous nature of Gatsby's past associations. The chauffeur is identified as one, which later explains his lack of surprise at the gunshots.
  • Port Roosevelt/Gad's Hill/West Egg: The geographical path of George Wilson's determined, yet desperate, journey as he hunts for the yellow car and eventually for Gatsby's house.

Detailed Notes

Nick's Disengagement and Busy Phone Lines

  • Jordan's Departure: Jordan informs Nick she has left Daisy's house, moving on to Hempstead and then Southampton. Her departure highlights her typical self-preservation and avoidance of messy situations.
  • Nick's Coldness: Nick is annoyed by Jordan's detached behavior and her accusation ("You weren't so nice to me last night"). He rejects her offer to come into town, signifying his growing emotional distance from her and the superficial world she represents. Their conversation ends abruptly, underscoring Nick's disillusionment.
  • Calling Gatsby: Nick immediately tries to call Gatsby, but the line is busy, being held for a long-distance call from Detroit – a subtle hint at Gatsby's ongoing, possibly illicit, business dealings.

The Aftermath at the Garage: Wilson's Grief and Growing Suspicion

  • Myrtle's Forgotten Tragedy: Nick reflects on how quickly a tragedy like Myrtle's death can become a spectacle, then distorted, and finally forgotten by the public, emphasizing the ephemeral nature of public memory and compassion.
  • Catherine's Incapacitation: Myrtle's sister, Catherine, arrives at the garage drunk and unable to comprehend the situation, representing another casualty of the night's events and the general moral decay.
  • Wilson's Despair: George Wilson is found rocking back and forth in his office, consumed by incoherent grief. Michaelis and other men stay with him, offering what comfort they can.
  • Shift in Wilson's Focus: Around three o'clock in the morning, Wilson's grief takes a dark turn. He grows quieter and begins to talk about the "yellow car," shifting from sorrow to a nascent desire for answers.
  • Revelation of Myrtle's Abuse: Wilson blurts out that Myrtle returned from the city with a bruised face and swollen nose a couple of months prior, suggesting a history of physical abuse or violent encounters, likely with her lover. Michaelis tries to distract him with questions about his marriage and children.
  • The Dog Leash: Wilson reveals he found a new, expensive dog leash, made of leather and braided silver, which Myrtle had tried to explain. Michaelis offers plausible explanations (Myrtle bought it), but Wilson's suspicion is already solidified, believing it to be evidence of her infidelity.
  • Wilson's Conviction: Fueled by the leash and his wife's previous injuries, Wilson becomes convinced that Myrtle was murdered by her lover. He dismisses the idea of it being an accident, stating, "He murdered her." He believes Myrtle ran out to speak to the driver of the yellow car, whom he sees as her killer.

"God Sees Everything": Wilson's Delusion

  • The Eyes of Eckleburg: In a moment of intense delusion, Wilson takes Michaelis to the window and points to the billboard eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg, proclaiming, "God knows what you've been doing... You may fool me, but you can't fool God!" He believes these eyes are literally the eyes of God watching over their sins.
  • Michaelis's Attempt at Reason: Michaelis, shocked, tries to assure Wilson it's just an advertisement, but Wilson remains fixed on his belief, nodding into the twilight. This scene dramatically highlights Wilson's complete mental breakdown and his quest for a divine justice.

Wilson's Hunt and Gatsby's Final Vigil

  • Wilson's Disappearance: After Michaelis leaves to get some sleep, Wilson vanishes from the garage, setting in motion his vengeful pursuit.
  • Tracing Wilson's Path: Police later trace Wilson's movements on foot to Port Roosevelt and Gad's Hill, where he bought food he didn't eat. He is described as "acting sort of crazy."
  • The Hunt for the Yellow Car: It is theorized that Wilson spent three hours going from garage to garage, inquiring about a yellow car. However, the narrative suggests he might have had an "easier, surer way of finding out," implying a more direct, perhaps violent, line of questioning.
  • Arrival at West Egg: By 2:30 PM, Wilson is in West Egg, where he asks for directions to Gatsby's house, indicating he has successfully identified Gatsby as the owner of the yellow car.
  • Gatsby's Last Moments: Gatsby puts on his bathing suit and takes his pneumatic mattress to the pool, instructing the butler to bring any phone calls to him there. He also curiously insists that his open car (the one Daisy drove) not be taken out, likely to conceal the damaged fender which would link it to Myrtle's death.
  • The Fading Dream: Nick reflects on Gatsby's wait for Daisy's call, which he believes Gatsby himself knows will never come. Gatsby's dream is described as having "lost the old warm world," a "grotesque thing," with the raw sunlight revealing a "new world, material without being real." Gatsby has paid a "high price for living too long with a single dream."
  • The Tragic Encounter: As Gatsby floats in the pool, Wilson, described as an "ashen, fantastic figure," approaches. The chauffeur hears shots, but doesn't initially react, accustomed to strange occurrences at Gatsby's house.
  • Discovery of the Bodies: Nick arrives to find panic. He, the butler, chauffeur, and gardener rush to the pool, where they discover Gatsby's body floating on the mattress. Moments later, the gardener finds Wilson's body in the grass nearby, confirming the double tragedy.

Review Questions

  1. How does Nick's conversation with Jordan at the beginning of the section reflect his changing perspective on the wealthy and his increasing disillusionment?
  2. Describe George Wilson's emotional and mental state in the hours following Myrtle's death. What specific details contribute to his transformation from grief-stricken husband to vengeful killer?
  3. Discuss the significance of the dog leash and the eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg in motivating Wilson's actions. How do these symbols contribute to his distorted understanding of reality and justice?
  4. Analyze Gatsby's actions and thoughts during his final hours by the pool. What does his waiting for Daisy's call, and Nick's interpretation of this wait, reveal about the nature of Gatsby's dream and its ultimate failure?
  5. How does this section serve as the tragic climax of the novel? What overarching themes, such as the destructive nature of the American Dream, the carelessness of the wealthy, or the pursuit of an unattainable past, are brought to their brutal conclusion here?
