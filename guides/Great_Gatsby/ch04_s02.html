Study Guide: Chapter 4, Part 1 – Gatsby's Guests and His Story

Overview

The first half of Chapter 4 reveals the superficial and often disreputable nature of Gatsby's party guests through Nick's detailed catalog. It then delves into Gatsby's carefully constructed autobiography, which Nick initially doubts but accepts after seeing physical evidence. The introduction of Meyer Wolfshiem, a known criminal, finally exposes the illicit origins of Gatsby's wealth and his connections to the underworld, challenging Nick's romanticized view of his mysterious neighbor.

Key Concepts

  • Gatsby's Constructed Identity: Gatsby presents a carefully curated image of himself as a wealthy, old-money gentleman with a distinguished past. His autobiographical narrative is formulaic, designed to evoke a specific impression, and is supported by seemingly authentic but strategically deployed physical evidence.
  • The Superficiality of Gatsby's Social World: Nick's list of party guests highlights the transient, self-serving, and often morally ambiguous individuals drawn to Gatsby's lavish parties. They attend for pleasure and opportunity, without genuine connection to their host, demonstrating the isolation inherent in Gatsby's pursuit of a particular lifestyle.
  • The Criminal Underworld and "New Money": The introduction of Meyer Wolfshiem, a notorious gambler who fixed the 1919 World Series, unveils the shady, criminal foundations of Gatsby's wealth. This connection underscores the darker, less reputable side of "new money" in the Jazz Age, contrasting sharply with the idealized vision Gatsby attempts to project.
  • Nick's Shifting Perception: Nick begins as a skeptical observer, doubting Gatsby's embellished story. However, he is temporarily swayed by tangible proof (the medal and photograph), only to have his skepticism fully validated by the unsettling encounter with Wolfshiem, marking a significant step in his understanding of Gatsby's true nature.
  • Illusion vs. Reality: The section constantly pits Gatsby's carefully crafted illusion against the harsh reality of his background and connections. His elegant parties and polished stories are revealed as a facade covering a less glamorous, and even dangerous, truth.

Important Terms

  • Meyer Wolfshiem: A "small, flat-nosed Jew" and notorious gambler who famously "fixed" the 1919 World Series. He is Gatsby's business associate and symbolizes the criminal underworld from which Gatsby's wealth originates.
  • Montenegro Medal (Order of Danilo): A genuine war decoration awarded to Gatsby by the government of Montenegro, which he uses as physical proof to support his claims of war heroism.
  • Oxford Photograph: A photograph showing Gatsby among young men at Oxford University, used as further physical evidence to validate his claims of an elite European education.
  • Rosy Rosenthal: A man murdered at the old Metropole, whose death Meyer Wolfshiem recounts with a strange mix of sentimentality and callousness, highlighting the violence inherent in his world.
  • 1919 World Series: The infamous baseball championship series that Meyer Wolfshiem allegedly "fixed" or manipulated, establishing his reputation as a powerful figure in organized crime.

Detailed Notes

Nick's Catalog of Gatsby's Party Guests

  • The Nature of the Guests: Nick meticulously records the names of those who attended Gatsby's parties on an old timetable. This guest list reveals a "cross-section of wealthy, shady, and reckless individuals" from both East and West Egg, representing diverse backgrounds and often questionable characters.
  • Patterns and Fates: Nick observes specific patterns among the guests. Many are associated with "new money," including film producers, gamblers, and promoters. A significant number come to "dark or violent ends," such as Snell going to the penitentiary, Henry L. Palmetto committing suicide, and Muldoon's brother strangling his wife.
  • Lack of Genuine Connection: The catalog underscores that these guests are drawn by Gatsby's lavish hospitality, but they care little about him personally. They arrive and depart without forming any real bonds or showing genuine interest in their host, highlighting the superficiality and isolation within Gatsby's social world.

Gatsby's Autobiographical Narrative

  • Gatsby's Claims: On their drive to New York, Gatsby shares a detailed, "polished" story of his life. He claims to be the son of "wealthy Midwesterners" (who are now deceased), educated at Oxford "as a family tradition." He describes a life as a "young rajah," traveling Europe, collecting jewels, and hunting after inheriting money. He also boasts of distinguished war service in the Argonne Forest, earning decorations from "every Allied government."
  • Nick's Doubt: Nick's skepticism is immediately triggered by inconsistencies and awkwardness in Gatsby's delivery.
    • Gatsby's geographical error: When asked "what part of the Middle West?" Gatsby responds "San Francisco," a detail that undermines the entire narrative.
    • Gatsby's hurried claim: The phrase "educated at Oxford" is "hurried" or "swallowed," suggesting it's a claim Gatsby is uncomfortable with or that has "bothered him before."
  • Formulaic Narrative (Pause and Consider): Fitzgerald makes Gatsby's story sound "formulaic"—resembling a narrative "skimming hastily through a dozen magazines"—to reveal how Gatsby has consciously constructed his identity. This conventional pattern of wealth, European education, and war heroism is an attempt to create a believable, respectable past that aligns with his aspirations, rather than reflecting genuine experience.

Physical Evidence: The Montenegro Medal and Oxford Photograph

  • The Objects: To counter Nick's skepticism, Gatsby produces two pieces of physical evidence: an authentic-looking Order of Danilo medal from Montenegro, inscribed with "Major Jay Gatsby," and a photograph from Oxford showing him among young men with cricket bats and spires.
  • Nick's Reaction Shift: Initially, Nick suspects Gatsby's story is fabricated, comparing him to a "turbaned 'character' leaking sawdust." However, the material evidence dramatically shifts Nick's perception. The authentic medal and the convincing photograph cause his skepticism to "collapse," leading him to believe "it was all true."
  • Power of Material Proof: Fitzgerald uses this rapid reversal to demonstrate how easily tangible proof can override rational doubt, even in the face of previous inconsistencies. Nick's mind jumps to romanticized images of "tiger skins" and "a chest of rubies," highlighting the power of physical evidence to create or reinforce an illusion.

Meyer Wolfshiem

  • Nick's Encounter and Description: Nick meets Meyer Wolfshiem at lunch. Wolfshiem is portrayed with distinctive physical features: "a small, flat-nosed Jew" with "two fine growths of hair" in his nostrils and "tiny eyes." His speech is marked by dialect, using terms like "gonnegtion" (connection) and "Oggsford" (Oxford).
  • Behavior and Accessories: Wolfshiem tells a sentimental story about the murder of a man named Rosy Rosenthal at the old Metropole, highlighting a strange mix of emotion and casual familiarity with violence. He then abruptly shifts to asking whether Nick is a potential "business gonnegtion." Most strikingly, his cuff buttons are made of "human molars."
  • Suggestive Details: These details—Wolfshiem's physical appearance, his dialect, his unsettling blend of sentimentality and business acumen regarding violence, and his macabre accessories—strongly suggest a man deeply immersed in the criminal underworld.
  • The 1919 World Series: Nick learns that Wolfshiem is the infamous gambler who "fixed" the 1919 World Series, solidifying his identity as a powerful figure in organized crime.
  • Juxtaposition (Pause and Consider): The rapid transition from Wolfshiem's sentimental story about murder to his immediate inquiry about a "business gonnegtion" reveals the amoral and cynical nature of the world Gatsby inhabits. It demonstrates that in this world, violence, death, and illicit dealings are closely intertwined with business, money, and power, complicating any romantic understanding of Gatsby's wealth.

Review Questions

  1. What specific details from Nick's catalog of Gatsby's party guests reveal the social climate and moral character of the people who frequented his gatherings? How do these details challenge the notion of the American Dream?
  2. Analyze Gatsby's autobiographical narrative to Nick. What claims does he make, and why does Nick initially doubt them? How does Fitzgerald use the "formulaic" nature of Gatsby's story to comment on constructed identities?
  3. Describe Nick's reaction to the Montenegro medal and the Oxford photograph. Why does his skepticism collapse so dramatically, and what does this reveal about the power of material evidence versus rational thought?
  4. Detail the key characteristics of Meyer Wolfshiem's appearance, speech, and behavior as observed by Nick. What do these elements collectively suggest about Wolfshiem's world and his relationship with Gatsby?
  5. Discuss the significance of Wolfshiem's sentimental anecdote about Rosy Rosenthal's murder followed by his abrupt shift to discussing "business gonnegtions." What does this transition imply about the values and morality prevalent in Gatsby's social and financial circles?
