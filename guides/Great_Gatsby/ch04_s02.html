Study Guide: Chapter 3: Literary Analysis

Key Concepts

  • Chapter 3 is organized around the theme of performance and self-invention: Gatsby's party is a theatrical production, and Gatsby himself is a performer constructing his identity in real time
  • The owl-eyed man's Belasco comparison crystallizes the chapter's central insight: Gatsby's world achieves verisimilitude without substance — the books are real but unread, the set is perfect but hollow
  • Gatsby's extraordinary smile is itself a performance: it offers "eternal reassurance" and understands the observer "just so far as you wanted to be understood" — the technique of an actor who knows his audience
  • Jordan Baker's dishonesty — the moved golf ball, the borrowed car — extends the theme of performance beyond Gatsby; her "cool, insolent smile" is its own carefully maintained facade
  • Nick's concluding self-description as "one of the few honest people that I have ever known" invites skepticism: is Nick's role as reliable moral observer itself a performance?
  • Gatsby stands apart from his guests throughout the party — sober, formal, increasingly "correct as the fraternal hilarity increased" — the host who never participates in his own spectacle

Summary

Fitzgerald organizes Chapter 3 around the concept of performance. Gatsby's party preparations are described with stage-manager precision — the orchestra's arrival, the caterers, the colored lights — and the guests perform their roles (dancing, gossiping, drinking) while the host remains offstage, invisible to the audience he has assembled. When the owl-eyed man in the library discovers the books are real but their pages are uncut, he calls Gatsby "a regular Belasco" — a producer of hyper-realistic theatrical sets. The comparison crystallizes the chapter's theme: Gatsby's world is a performance that achieves realism without substance.

When Gatsby finally appears, the performance continues at the individual level. His smile offers reassurance calibrated precisely to whoever receives it, concentrating on each person "with an irresistible prejudice in your favor." Nick, who notices this, also observes that Gatsby's "elaborate formality of speech just missed being absurd" and that he was "picking his words with care." Gatsby is constructing himself in real time, choosing each word and gesture as deliberately as he chose each book for his unread library.

Fitzgerald extends the performance theme beyond Gatsby. Jordan Baker's dishonesty — moving a golf ball in a tournament, lying about a borrowed car — is another mode of performance, maintaining an image of careless superiority. Nick's closing claim to be "one of the few honest people" he has known asks the reader to question whether Nick, too, performs a role: the reliable moral observer. The chapter raises the question of whether authenticity is possible in a world built entirely on surfaces.

Key Terms

  • Performance / self-invention: The central theme of Chapter 3; Gatsby and other characters construct identities through careful selection of words, gestures, dress, and setting
  • Verisimilitude: The appearance of being real or true; the books in Gatsby's library are real (verisimilitude achieved) but unread (substance absent)
  • Belasco, David: A Broadway producer famous for obsessively realistic stage sets; the comparison suggests Gatsby's home is an impressive but fundamentally theatrical production
  • Spectroscopic: Here used figuratively to suggest intense, precise scrutiny — describing the East Egg guests guarding themselves against West Egg's "gayety"
  • Subterfuge: A deceptive stratagem or trick; Nick concludes Jordan began "dealing in subterfuges when she was very young" to maintain her cool advantage
  • Cardinal virtue: A principal or fundamental virtue; Nick claims honesty as his own, though the novel has already complicated his reliability

Review Questions

  1. How does the owl-eyed man's Belasco comparison illuminate the central theme of performance in Chapter 3?
  2. How does Nick describe Gatsby's smile, and what does the description suggest about Gatsby's relationship to the people around him?
  3. What specific details reveal that Gatsby is "picking his words with care" and constructing himself deliberately during his introduction to Nick?
  4. How does Jordan Baker's dishonesty connect to the chapter's broader theme of performance and maintained appearances?
  5. Nick ends the chapter by declaring his own honesty as a cardinal virtue. What reasons does the reader have, by this point, to question that claim?
