Chapter 6, Section 3: The Truth About Gatsby

Overview

This section finally peels back the layers of mystery surrounding Jay Gatsby, revealing his true origins as James Gatz from North Dakota and detailing the pivotal role of Dan Cody in his self-reinvention. It explains how Gatsby meticulously crafted his idealized identity. The narrative then shifts to an awkward and telling encounter between Gatsby, Tom Buchanan, and two of Tom's upper-class acquaintances, highlighting the social tensions and Tom's growing suspicion of Gatsby.

Key Concepts

  • Gatsby's True Identity and Origin: We learn that Jay Gatsby was born James Gatz to poor, "shiftless" farming parents in North Dakota. He rejected this background and meticulously created his new persona.
  • The "Platonic Conception of Himself": Gatsby's identity is not inherited but self-created, an idealized vision he held of himself. He was driven by a powerful ambition to embody this grand, almost divine, self-image.
  • The Influence of Dan Cody: A wealthy, rough-hewn copper magnate and pioneer debauchee, Cody became Gatsby's mentor and employer for five years. Cody exposed Gatsby to the world of wealth, luxury, and the debauched lifestyle of the super-rich, shaping his ambitions and refining his outward persona.
  • Loss of Inheritance: Despite being named in Cody's will, Gatsby was legally swindled out of a $25,000 inheritance by Ella Kaye, Cody's opportunistic companion. This loss underscores the precariousness of Gatsby's position in the world of old money.
  • The Meeting of Worlds: Tom Buchanan, representing old money and established social circles, directly confronts Gatsby, a symbol of new wealth and self-made ambition. This encounter highlights the class divide and Tom's immediate disdain and suspicion of Gatsby.
  • Nick as Narrator and Clarifier: Nick Carraway explicitly takes a "halt" in the narrative to dispel the wild rumors surrounding Gatsby, presenting the factual account of his past as told to him by Gatsby himself.

Important Terms

  • Laudable initiative: Deserving praise and commendation; admirable drive or determination. (Used to describe the reporter's effort)
  • Notoriety: The state of being famous or well-known for some bad quality or deed. (Gatsby's widespread, but mysterious, fame)
  • James Gatz: Gatsby's birth name, revealing his humble origins.
  • Platonic conception of himself: A philosophical term referring to Plato's theory of ideal Forms. Gatsby's "Platonic conception" means he created an idealized, perfect version of himself in his mind and strived to embody it, rather than being defined by his birth.
  • Meretricious beauty: Alluring by a show of flashy, superficial attractions; tawdry. (Describes the kind of vulgar, showy beauty Gatsby was dedicated to serving)
  • Ineffable gaudiness: Too great or extreme to be expressed in words, combined with showiness or tasteless extravagance. (Refers to the overwhelming, dream-like splendor of Gatsby's internal world)
  • Dan Cody: The wealthy copper magnate and yachtsman who became Gatsby's mentor and employer, introducing him to the world of affluence and luxury.
  • Ella Kaye: A journalist and companion to Dan Cody who, through legal maneuver, inherited Cody's millions, thereby depriving Gatsby of his legacy.
  • Pioneer debauchee: A dissolute or licentious person from the frontier era. (Describes Dan Cody, symbolizing the rough, unrestrained wealth of the American frontier)
  • Ingratiate: To bring oneself into favor with someone by flattering or trying to please them. (Nick's attempt to please Jordan's aunt)

Detailed Notes

The Unraveling of Gatsby's Past

  • The Reporter's Curiosity: The section opens with a New York reporter seeking a statement from Gatsby, indicating that Gatsby's reputation has grown to the point of being "just short of being news."
  • Wild Rumors: Hundreds of guests spread fantastic and often absurd legends about Gatsby, such as an "underground pipe-line to Canada" or that he lived in a boat. These rumors served to increase his enigmatic notoriety.
  • Gatsby's True Identity: Nick, as narrator, clarifies that Gatsby's real name was James Gatz. He was born to poor, "shiftless" farmers in North Dakota. Gatsby never truly accepted them as his parents, instead shaping his own destiny.
  • Self-Creation and the Platonic Ideal: At age seventeen, Gatz changed his name to Jay Gatsby. This was not just a name change but the moment he embraced his "Platonic conception of himself" – an idealized, self-invented identity. He saw himself as a "son of God," destined to serve a "vast, vulgar, and meretricious beauty." He remained faithful to this self-created image throughout his life.
  • Early Life and Ambition: Before meeting Cody, Gatz worked as a clam-digger and salmon-fisher along Lake Superior. He experienced early encounters with women, which led to a certain contempt. His internal world was a "turbulent riot," filled with "grotesque and fantastic conceits" and "ineffable gaudiness," fueling his restless imagination and dreams of future glory.
  • Brief College Stint: An "instinct toward his future glory" led him to the Lutheran college of St. Olaf, but he left after two weeks, finding it indifferent to his grand destiny and disliking the janitor's work required to pay his way.

The Influence of Dan Cody

  • Cody's Background: Dan Cody was a fifty-year-old self-made millionaire from the Nevada silver fields and Yukon gold rushes, a "pioneer debauchee" whose wealth came from aggressive ventures. Though physically robust, he was becoming "soft-minded," making him vulnerable to opportunistic women.
  • Ella Kaye's Role: Ella Kaye, a journalist, had already exploited Cody's weakness, maneuvering him into taking to the sea in a yacht.
  • The Fateful Meeting: Gatz, resting on his oars near Lake Superior, saw Cody's yacht, the TUOLOMEE, anchor. He rowed out, warning Cody of an impending wind, and impressed the older man with his quick wit and ambition. Cody asked him questions that "elicited the brand new name" – Jay Gatsby.
  • Mentorship and Transformation: Cody took Gatsby under his wing, buying him clothes and bringing him along on his yacht. For five years, Gatsby served Cody in various capacities (steward, mate, secretary, even "jailor" to Cody's drunken excesses).
  • Gatsby's Education and Abstinence: During this time, Gatsby traveled the world with Cody, gaining an "singularly appropriate education" in the ways of wealth and the leisure class. He also learned to avoid liquor due to Cody's heavy drinking.
  • Cody's Death and Lost Inheritance: The partnership ended abruptly when Ella Kaye came aboard the yacht in Boston, and a week later, Cody died. Gatsby was supposed to inherit $25,000 from Cody, but Ella Kaye, through some legal maneuver, managed to secure the entire fortune, leaving Gatsby penniless but with invaluable experience.

Nick's Narrative Interruption and a Social Encounter

  • Clarifying the Past: Nick explicitly states he's providing this detailed account to "explod[e] those first wild rumors" about Gatsby's background, setting the record straight. He reveals Gatsby told him these details "much later" during a time of confusion.
  • A Hiatus in Nick's Association: Nick had been spending time in New York with Jordan Baker, away from Gatsby.
  • Tom Buchanan's Visit: Tom Buchanan, accompanied by a man named Mr. Sloane and a woman in a brown riding-habit, arrives unexpectedly at Gatsby's house on horseback.
  • Gatsby's Awkward Hospitality: Gatsby is clearly affected by Tom's presence but tries to be an overly gracious host, offering drinks and conversation. He senses that his guests only came for entertainment.
  • Social Discomfort: The interaction is stiff. Gatsby attempts to establish a connection with Tom, reminding him they've met, even mentioning knowing Daisy. Tom is gruffly polite but clearly doesn't remember and treats Gatsby as an inferior. Mr. Sloane is haughty, and the woman initially silent.
  • A Faux Invitation: After a few drinks, the woman suggests they "all come over to your next party, Mr. Gatsby." Gatsby, eager to engage with Tom's circle, immediately expresses delight.
  • The Snub: Mr. Sloane, however, clearly wants to leave and subtly scolds the woman. The woman, perhaps realizing her mistake or succumbing to Sloane's influence, then attempts to invite Gatsby and Nick to her supper, but Sloane makes it clear only she and Tom are expected. Gatsby, still hoping to go, offers to follow in his car since he doesn't have a horse.
  • Tom's Disdain: Tom observes Gatsby's eagerness with contempt, remarking, "My God, I believe the man's coming... Doesn't he know she doesn't want him?" He expresses old-fashioned disapproval of women "running around too much" and meeting "all kinds of crazy fish," clearly referring to Gatsby and his concern about Daisy's connection to him.
  • The Departure: Sloane and the woman quickly mount their horses, instructing Tom to tell Gatsby they couldn't wait, leaving Gatsby, Nick, and Tom behind.

Review Questions

  1. How does Gatsby's decision to change his name from James Gatz to Jay Gatsby at age seventeen signify a pivotal moment in his life? What does the narrator mean by Gatsby's "Platonic conception of himself"?
  2. Describe the character of Dan Cody and his background. In what ways did Cody act as a mentor to Gatsby, and what lasting impact did he have on Gatsby's life, despite Gatsby losing his inheritance?
  3. Analyze the significance of Gatsby's "turbulent riot" of a heart and his "universe of ineffable gaudiness" spinning in his brain. How do these descriptions illuminate the source of his ambition and his idealized vision of the world?
  4. Examine the social dynamics of the interaction between Gatsby, Tom Buchanan, Mr. Sloane, and the woman in the brown riding-habit. What does this encounter reveal about the class differences and unspoken social rules between old money and new money?
  5. Why does Nick choose this specific point in the narrative to reveal Gatsby's true history? What is the effect of interjecting this detailed backstory amidst the unfolding events of the present?
