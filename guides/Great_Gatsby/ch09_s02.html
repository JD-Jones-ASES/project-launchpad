Study Guide: Chapter 9, Section 2 - After Gatsby (Midcheck)

Overview

This section details the immediate aftermath of Jay Gatsby's death, focusing on Nick Carraway's desperate attempts to arrange a funeral and gather mourners. It starkly reveals the superficiality of Gatsby's social connections and explores the origins of his ambition through the arrival of his father and the discovery of personal artifacts. The section also highlights the deliberate obscuring of truth surrounding Myrtle Wilson's death.

Key Concepts

  • The Superficiality of Wealth and Popularity: Gatsby, despite his lavish parties and vast social network, is ultimately abandoned in death. His "friends" prove to be fair-weather acquaintances, prioritizing their own convenience over loyalty or respect.
  • Nick's Loyalty vs. Social Indifference: Nick Carraway emerges as the sole loyal figure, taking on the responsibility of Gatsby's funeral despite others' refusals. This contrasts his genuine care with the widespread apathy and self-interest of Gatsby's former associates.
  • Truth and Public Perception: Catherine's false testimony at the inquest demonstrates how truth can be manipulated to maintain public order and protect reputations, even at the cost of erasing complex realities. This deception contributes to a simplified, inaccurate narrative of events.
  • The Theme of Self-Invention: The arrival of Henry C. Gatz and the discovery of young Jimmy Gatz's childhood schedule and "General Resolves" reveal the foundational ambition and discipline that fueled Gatsby's transformation. It emphasizes his deliberate crafting of a new identity and pursuit of an idealized dream.
  • The Isolation of the American Dream: Gatsby's journey, from humble origins to immense wealth, ultimately ends in profound isolation. His meticulously constructed dream world, designed to win back Daisy, crumbles with his death, leaving him alone in the end.

Important Terms

  • Nick Carraway: The narrator, Gatsby's neighbor, and the only character who takes responsibility for Gatsby's funeral. He embodies genuine loyalty in a world of superficiality.
  • Jay Gatsby: The enigmatic millionaire, whose murder precipitates the events of this section. His death exposes the true nature of his relationships and the emptiness of his social world.
  • Henry C. Gatz: Gatsby's elderly, impoverished father from Minnesota. His arrival offers a poignant connection to Gatsby's humble origins and provides insight into his son's early ambitions.
  • Meyer Wolfsheim: Gatsby's business associate, who refuses to attend the funeral, citing his inability to "get mixed up in it." His refusal underscores the shady nature of his and Gatsby's business dealings.
  • Klipspringer: Known as "the boarder," a parasitic individual who frequently stayed at Gatsby's mansion. He calls Nick only to retrieve his tennis shoes, exemplifying the transactional and self-serving nature of Gatsby's party-goers.
  • Catherine: Myrtle Wilson's sister. Her false testimony at the inquest denies Myrtle's affair with Tom and her connection to Gatsby, helping to simplify the official narrative of the car accident.
  • Inquest: A legal inquiry, typically held to establish the facts surrounding a death. Catherine's testimony here is crucial in shaping the public understanding of the events.
  • Self-Invention: A core theme in the novel, exemplified by Jay Gatsby's transformation from Jimmy Gatz. This section reveals the early roots of this ambition through his childhood writings.
  • Hopalong Cassidy: A popular Western character. Young Jimmy Gatz's copy of a Hopalong Cassidy book contains his meticulous schedule and "General Resolves," illustrating his early drive for self-improvement and disciplined ambition.

Detailed Notes

Nick's Futile Attempts to Arrange a Funeral

  • Daisy and Tom's Disappearance: Immediately following Gatsby's death, Daisy and Tom Buchanan abandon their home, leaving no forwarding address. This act signifies their complete disengagement from the tragedy and their moral cowardice.
  • Wolfsheim's Refusal: Nick tries to contact Meyer Wolfsheim, Gatsby's business partner, by phone, letter, and finally in person. Wolfsheim consistently refuses to attend the funeral, stating he "cannot get mixed up in it." This highlights the predatory and self-preserving nature of his relationship with Gatsby, devoid of personal loyalty.
  • The Indifference of Party-Goers: Nick attempts to invite various acquaintances who frequented Gatsby's parties. Their responses range from outright declines to thinly veiled condemnations of Gatsby, demonstrating the superficiality of his vast social network. No one feels any obligation or genuine sorrow.
  • Klipspringer's Call: Klipspringer, "the boarder," calls Nick, not to offer condolences or confirm attendance, but to request that his tennis shoes be sent to him. When pressed about the funeral, he makes flimsy excuses, further illustrating the parasitic relationships that defined Gatsby's social world. Nick hangs up on him, disgusted.
  • Cumulative Effect: This sequence of refusals structurally emphasizes Gatsby's profound isolation. His wealth and "popularity" were entirely transactional, failing to garner any real affection or loyalty from those who benefited from his hospitality.

Catherine's Testimony at the Inquest

  • The Lie: Catherine, Myrtle Wilson's sister, swears under oath that Myrtle had never seen Gatsby and was completely happy with her husband, George. This testimony is entirely false.
  • Significance:
    • Simplifies the Narrative: Her lie allows the official story of the deaths to remain straightforward: Wilson was a "madman" "deranged by grief," preventing any deeper inquiry into the complex web of relationships and affairs.
    • Protects Reputations: It shields Myrtle's reputation and, more importantly, keeps Tom Buchanan's affair a secret, preventing a public scandal for the wealthy elite.
    • Erases Truth: Catherine's testimony deliberately conceals the true chain of events involving Daisy, Tom, Myrtle, George, and Gatsby, ultimately distorting how these tragic deaths are publicly understood.

Henry C. Gatz's Arrival

  • Physical Appearance: Gatsby's father, Henry C. Gatz, arrives from Minnesota. He is described as a "solemn old man, very helpless and dismayed," wearing a "long cheap ulster" unsuitable for the warm September weather. His eyes "leaked continuously with excitement," his hands trembled, and he spilled a glass of milk.
  • What Details Reveal: These physical details emphasize his poverty, advanced age, and profound emotional fragility. He presents a stark contrast to the opulent mansion and the polished image his son constructed. His humble appearance roots Gatsby firmly in his working-class past.
  • Emotional State and Identity: Henry C. Gatz is both heartbroken by his son's death and immensely proud of his achievements, though he still affectionately calls him "Jimmy." This dual perspective anchors Gatsby's extraordinary life to his very ordinary origins.

Artifacts and the Origins of Gatsby's Ambition

  • Cracked Photograph of the House: Henry C. Gatz carries a cracked photograph of Gatsby's mansion, which he proudly shows to Nick. This artifact symbolizes Gatsby's achievement and his father's awe at his son's success, making the dream tangible.
  • Hopalong Cassidy Book: Henry C. Gatz also possesses a childhood copy of a Hopalong Cassidy book, which belonged to young Jimmy Gatz.
    • Daily Schedule: Inside, Nick finds a meticulously planned daily schedule, filled with ambitions for self-improvement ("Rise from bed....Dumbbell exercise....Study electricity....Practice elocution....Study needed inventions").
    • "General Resolves": Accompanying the schedule are "General Resolves," such as "No smoking or chewing," "Be better to parents," "Save 5 dollars week," and "Read one improving book or magazine per week."
  • Development of Self-Invention: These artifacts are crucial for understanding the origins of Jay Gatsby. They reveal that his relentless ambition, discipline, and desire for self-improvement were present from his childhood. The schedule and resolves demonstrate a conscious, systematic effort to transform himself, laying the groundwork for the "Jay Gatsby" persona. They highlight his early belief in the power of self-discipline to transcend his circumstances and achieve greatness.

Review Questions

  1. Discuss the cumulative effect of Nick's repeated failed attempts to gather mourners for Gatsby's funeral. What argument does this pattern make about the relationship between wealth, popularity, and genuine loyalty in the novel's world?
  2. How does Catherine's false testimony at the inquest affect the public understanding of the tragic events, and what broader implications does it have for the theme of truth versus illusion in The Great Gatsby?
  3. Analyze the significance of Henry C. Gatz's physical description and emotional state upon his arrival. How does he serve as a foil to the image of "Jay Gatsby," and what does his presence add to our understanding of the protagonist?
  4. Explain how Fitzgerald uses the artifacts—the cracked photograph of Gatsby's house and the Hopalong Cassidy book with its schedule and "General Resolves"—to develop the theme of self-invention. What do these items reveal about the origins and nature of Gatsby's ambition?
  5. Consider Klipspringer's phone call to Nick. What does this seemingly minor interaction reveal about the nature of Gatsby's social world and the ultimate failure of his attempts to connect with people?
