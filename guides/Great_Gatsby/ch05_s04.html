Study Guide: Chapter 5, Section 4 – The Reunion's Climax

Overview

This section captures the emotional peak of Jay Gatsby and Daisy Buchanan's long-awaited reunion. After the initial awkwardness and rain, the sun emerges, mirroring Gatsby's radiant joy and Daisy's tearful relief. The narrative follows their tour of Gatsby's lavish mansion, a deliberate display of his wealth intended to impress Daisy, culminating in her overwhelming emotional reaction to his luxurious shirts. The section also delves into Gatsby's reflection on the diminished symbolism of the green light, introduces a figure from his past, and ends with an ironic musical interlude, leaving Gatsby with a hint of bewilderment.

Key Concepts

  • Emotional Release and Transformation: The end of the rain symbolizes a breakthrough in Gatsby and Daisy's reunion. Gatsby transforms from nervous to "glowing," radiating an intense well-being, while Daisy experiences a powerful, tearful release, suggesting the immense pent-up emotion finally breaking through.
  • Wealth as a Performance: Gatsby meticulously showcases his opulent mansion and possessions, especially the elaborate display of his shirts, not just as proof of his success, but as a deliberate performance designed to impress and win over Daisy. His wealth is a means to achieve his ultimate dream.
  • The Vanishing Significance of the Green Light: Once a powerful symbol of his distant, unattainable dream, Gatsby realizes the green light at the end of Daisy's dock has lost its "colossal significance" now that his dream is physically present. Its transformation from a potent symbol to "again a green light on a dock" marks a pivotal moment in his realization of the dream's reality.
  • The Unreality of the Achieved Dream: Despite the overwhelming joy, there's an undercurrent of unreality. Gatsby is dazed, almost "overwound," and Daisy's reaction is almost excessive. The dream, once a powerful intangible, struggles to exist in the tangible world, hinting at the potential emptiness or impossibility of fully replicating a five-year-old fantasy.
  • Gatsby's Enigmatic Past and Present: Gatsby's vague answers about how he earned his money ("panic of the war," "drug business and then I was in the oil business") and his suspicious phone call ("Detroit is his idea of a small town") reinforce the mystery surrounding his wealth and suggest illicit dealings. The introduction of Dan Cody's photograph offers a glimpse into his formative years.
  • Hollow Grandeur and Isolation: While Gatsby's mansion is immense and filled with luxury, Nick notes the strange silence and absence of real "stir of bright dresses." The presence of the "boarder," Mr. Klipspringer, highlights the superficiality and perhaps the loneliness of Gatsby's grand existence, needing to "keep it always full of interesting people" but surrounded by transient figures.
  • Ironic Juxtaposition through Music: Klipspringer's reluctant performance of songs like "The Love Nest" and "Ain't We Got Fun" creates a poignant irony. These songs, celebrating simple happiness despite poverty, are sung in a setting of unimaginable wealth, subtly undermining the idea that Gatsby's opulence brings genuine, uncomplicated joy.

Important Terms

  • Vestige (A trace or remnant): Used to describe how "every vestige of embarrassment was gone" from Gatsby and Daisy, indicating a complete shift in their emotional state.
  • Ecstatic (Feeling or expressing overwhelming happiness): Describes Gatsby's joyful reaction to the sun, illustrating his elation at the success of the reunion.
  • Inconceivable (Not capable of being imagined or grasped mentally; unbelievable): Refers to the "inconceivable pitch of intensity" of Gatsby's long-held dream, emphasizing the profound nature of his longing.
  • Colossal (Extremely large or great; immense): Gatsby uses this word to describe the previous "colossal significance" of the green light, highlighting how important it was to his dream.
  • Mr. Klipspringer (The "Boarder"): A peculiar character who seems to live at Gatsby's mansion indefinitely, discovered doing exercises. He represents the transient, often parasitic, nature of some of Gatsby's "guests."
  • Dan Cody: An elderly man in a yachting costume whose photograph is displayed in Gatsby's study. Gatsby reveals he was his "best friend years ago," hinting at a crucial mentor figure from his past.
  • The Green Light: A symbol Gatsby associated with Daisy and his dream of reuniting with her. In this section, its symbolic power diminishes upon the physical realization of his dream.
  • Marie Antoinette music-rooms/Restoration salons: Architectural and decorative styles Gatsby uses in his mansion, suggesting his attempt to mimic the grandeur and aristocratic past of old money, albeit in a somewhat theatrical or purchased way.
  • "The Love Nest" / "Ain't We Got Fun": Songs played by Klipspringer. Their lyrics, often about simple happiness or getting by, create an ironic contrast with Gatsby's extravagant lifestyle and the complex emotions of the reunion.

Detailed Notes

The Aftermath of the Rain and Emotional Shift

  • Sunlight Returns: After half an hour of rain, the sun shines again, creating a shift in atmosphere.
  • Nick's Observations: Nick senses a new silence within the house, different from the "murmur of voices" during the rain.
  • Gatsby and Daisy's Change: Upon entering, Nick finds Gatsby and Daisy looking at each other, "every vestige of embarrassment" gone. Daisy's face is tear-smeared, but Gatsby literally "glowed," radiating a profound new well-being.
  • Gatsby's Distraction: Gatsby is so consumed by Daisy's presence that he responds to Nick's comment about the rain with a delayed realization, then ecstatically shares the news with Daisy.
  • Daisy's Joy: Daisy's response, "I'm glad, Jay," is filled with "aching, grieving beauty," signaling deep, unexpected joy.

The Invitation to Gatsby's Mansion

  • Gatsby's Eagerness: Gatsby immediately insists that Daisy and Nick come to his house so he can "show her around."
  • Nick's Role: Nick questions if Gatsby truly wants him to come, but Gatsby confirms, perhaps needing Nick as a witness or emotional buffer.
  • Gatsby's Pride: On the lawn, Gatsby demands, "My house looks well, doesn't it?" proud of how it catches the light.

Gatsby's Mysterious Wealth

  • The Origin of Money: When Nick mentions Gatsby supposedly inherited his money, Gatsby automatically says he did, but then quickly corrects himself, claiming he "lost most of it in the big panic—the panic of the war."
  • Vague Business Dealings: Asked about his business, Gatsby gives evasive answers: "That's my affair," then "I've been in several things...drug business and then...oil business. But I'm not in either one now." He then shifts to ask Nick if he's considered his "proposal" from the other night, hinting at illegal activities.

The Tour of the Mansion

  • Daisy's Awe: Daisy is overwhelmed by the "huge place," questioning how Gatsby lives there alone. Gatsby insists he keeps it "always full of interesting people."
  • Opulent Interiors: They wander through elaborately decorated rooms (Marie Antoinette music-rooms, Restoration salons), giving Nick the feeling of concealed guests.
  • Mr. Klipspringer: They intrude upon a dishevelled man in pajamas doing liver exercises—Mr. Klipspringer, "the boarder," highlighting the strange inhabitants of Gatsby's world.
  • Gatsby's Apartment: They reach Gatsby's simpler bedroom and Adam study, where they drink Chartreuse.
  • Gatsby's Dazed State: Gatsby continuously looks at Daisy, seemingly revaluing his possessions through her eyes. He appears dazed, almost losing his balance, as if her presence makes everything else unreal.
  • The Gold Toilet Set: Daisy delights in a pure dull gold toilet set in Gatsby's bedroom, smoothing her hair with the brush.

The Shirt Scene: Emotional Climax

  • Gatsby's Laughter and Transformation: Gatsby begins to laugh hilariously, described as passing "visibly through two states and was entering upon a third," moving from embarrassment to "unreasoning joy" to "consumed with wonder." He is likened to an "overwound clock" running down.
  • The Display of Shirts: Gatsby opens huge cabinets filled with clothes and begins throwing "massed suits and dressing-gowns and ties," and "shirts, piled like bricks," one by one before them. They are made of expensive materials like sheer linen, thick silk, and fine flannel, in many colors and patterns.
  • Daisy's Emotional Outburst: Overwhelmed by the luxurious display, Daisy buries her head in the pile of shirts and begins to cry "stormily," sobbing, "They're such beautiful shirts...It makes me sad because I've never seen such—such beautiful shirts before." This moment symbolizes the culmination of her emotional response to Gatsby's wealth and devotion.

The Green Light and Dan Cody

  • Rain Returns: As they plan to tour the grounds, it begins to rain again.
  • Gatsby's Realization: Looking at the Sound, Gatsby points out that Daisy's home would be visible "if it wasn't for the mist." He then muses about her "green light that burns all night at the end of your dock," immediately realizing that its "colossal significance...had now vanished forever." The dream, now physically present, has lost its symbolic power.
  • Dan Cody's Photograph: Nick notices a large photograph of an elderly man in a yachting costume: Mr. Dan Cody, who Gatsby says was his "best friend years ago." A smaller picture of a young Gatsby in yachting attire is also present, which Daisy admires.
  • Daisy's Clippings: Gatsby quickly diverts attention by showing Daisy a collection of clippings about her.

The Phone Call and Klipspringer's Music

  • Mysterious Phone Call: Gatsby receives a brief, cryptic phone call where he discusses a business matter, mentioning "a SMALL town" and implying "Detroit is his idea of a small town," further hinting at illicit dealings.
  • Daisy's Whimsical Wish: Daisy spots a "pink and golden billow of foamy clouds" and wishes to put Gatsby in one and "push you around."
  • Klipspringer Summoned: Gatsby decides they will have Klipspringer play the piano. Klipspringer, embarrassed and reluctant, claims he was asleep and "hardly play[s] at all."
  • Ironic Songs: Gatsby insists, cutting him off. Klipspringer plays "THE LOVE NEST" and then "IN THE MORNING, IN THE EVENING, AIN'T WE GOT FUN," songs whose lyrics about finding joy despite poverty contrast sharply with Gatsby's lavish surroundings and the complex emotions unfolding.
  • Gatsby's Bewilderment: As Nick prepares to leave, he notices "the expression of bewilderment" return to Gatsby's face, suggesting a "faint doubt" about the quality of his present happiness, perhaps realizing the reality doesn't quite match his five-year dream.

Review Questions

  1. How does the shift in weather from rain to sun parallel the emotional transformation Gatsby and Daisy experience in the initial moments of this section? What does Gatsby's "glowing" and Daisy's tears signify?
  2. Gatsby takes great pains to show Daisy his elaborate mansion and, most notably, his collection of shirts. What do these displays reveal about Gatsby's understanding of how to win Daisy's affection, and why does Daisy react so intensely to the shirts?
  3. Explain Gatsby's reflection on the green light. What was its "colossal significance" to him previously, and why does he feel its meaning has "vanished forever" now that Daisy is physically present?
  4. The section introduces Mr. Klipspringer, "the boarder," and features his reluctant piano performance of songs like "Ain't We Got Fun." How does Klipspringer's presence and the choice of music contribute to the overall mood and themes of Gatsby's achieved dream?
  5. What do Gatsby's vague explanations about his wealth and his mysterious phone call suggest about the nature of his business and his overall character? How does this contrast with the image of sophisticated wealth he tries to project?
