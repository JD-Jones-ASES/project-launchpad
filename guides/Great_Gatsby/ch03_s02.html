Study Guide: Chapter 2: Literary Analysis

Key Concepts

  • Chapter 2 is a sustained study in class performance: characters use dress, manner, and speech to project identities that differ sharply from their actual circumstances
  • The valley of ashes functions as the novel's moral geography — the hidden cost of the wealth displayed in East Egg and West Egg
  • The motif of watching pervades the chapter: Eckleburg's eyes brood over the valley, Tom and Myrtle conduct their affair under Wilson's unknowing gaze, and Nick observes everything as an "enchanted and repelled" witness
  • Tom's violence — breaking Myrtle's nose — strips away the evening's social pretensions and reveals the brutality that underlies his authority
  • Myrtle's apartment performance exposes the hollowness of the class markers she imitates: the furniture is too large, the tapestry depicts Versailles, and she dismisses her expensive dress as "just a crazy old thing"
  • Nick describes himself as "within and without, simultaneously enchanted and repelled by the inexhaustible variety of life"

Summary

Fitzgerald constructs Chapter 2 around a series of deliberate juxtapositions. The most important is the contrast between Myrtle Wilson at the garage and Myrtle Wilson in the apartment. At the garage she carries her vitality naturally, entering with force while her husband fades into the walls. In the apartment, when she changes into cream-colored chiffon, her personality undergoes a "parallel transformation": vitality becomes hauteur, and her gestures grow "more violently affected moment by moment." The dress is not just a garment — it is a costume enabling a performance of class she cannot otherwise access. Every detail of the apartment scene then ironizes that performance: furniture too large for the rooms, tapestry depicting aristocratic Versailles, and Myrtle holding forth as if she commanded servants.

Fitzgerald does not mock Myrtle for aspiring upward. Instead, he exposes the hollowness of the class markers she imitates — because Tom's world is itself a performance, resting on inherited wealth and physical intimidation rather than merit. The chapter's dominant motif is watching. The eyes of Eckleburg — a faded advertisement whose creator "sank down into eternal blindness" — observe the moral wasteland below. Tom and Myrtle's affair happens directly under George Wilson's nose while he remains oblivious. And Nick watches everything, drawn into the party by ropes of drunken argument while also standing apart.

The climax arrives with economy: "Making a short deft movement, Tom Buchanan broke her nose with his open hand." The brevity of Fitzgerald's narration is the point. Tom's violence is casual, reflexive, and final — the reality beneath the performance, the power at the base of all the glittering pretension.

Key Terms

  • Class performance: The way characters use dress, speech, gesture, and setting to project social identities that differ from their actual circumstances
  • Motif: A recurring element — image, object, or idea — that develops thematic meaning across a work; the motif of watching connects Eckleburg's eyes, Wilson's unknowing gaze, and Nick's narration
  • Irony: A contrast between appearance and reality; the apartment's Versailles tapestry and oversized furniture ironize Myrtle's pretensions to refinement
  • Juxtaposition: Placing two contrasting elements side by side to highlight their differences; Fitzgerald juxtaposes the valley of ashes with the wealth of the Eggs, and Myrtle at the garage with Myrtle at the apartment
  • Strident: Loud and harsh; grating — used to describe the arguments that hold Nick in his chair as the evening degrades
  • Languid: Displaying a disinclination for physical exertion; slow and relaxed — used to describe Mrs. McKee

Review Questions

  1. What does the valley of ashes represent in the novel's moral geography, and how does Fitzgerald's imagery establish its symbolic meaning?
  2. How does the motif of watching — Eckleburg's eyes, Wilson's obliviousness, Nick's dual position — contribute to the chapter's themes?
  3. Describe the change in Myrtle's self-presentation between the garage and the apartment. What specific details does Fitzgerald use to show this shift?
  4. Identify two examples of irony in the apartment scene and explain what each reveals about Myrtle's performance of class.
  5. How does Fitzgerald's narration of Tom breaking Myrtle's nose — its brevity and matter-of-fact tone — contribute to the scene's effect?
