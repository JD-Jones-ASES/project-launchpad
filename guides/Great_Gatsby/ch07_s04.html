Study Guide: Chapter 7, Section 4: The Hottest Day – The Confrontation

Overview

This pivotal section details the dramatic climax of the central conflict between Tom Buchanan and Jay Gatsby, occurring during an intensely hot afternoon at the Plaza Hotel. Tom forces Gatsby to confront him directly about his affair with Daisy, leading to an emotional and brutal exchange that exposes Gatsby's illegal past and Daisy's inability to commit fully to him. The section concludes with Nick Carraway's reflective 30th birthday and a foreboding shift in perspective to George Wilson, foreshadowing a tragic turn of events.

Key Concepts

  • Climax of the Conflict: The long-simmering tension between Tom and Gatsby erupts into an open, verbal battle for Daisy's affection and loyalty. This scene represents the peak of their rivalry.
  • Daisy's Indecision and Weakness: Despite Gatsby's fervent pleas, Daisy falters under pressure, unable to fully renounce Tom or completely commit to Gatsby, revealing her deep-seated ambivalence and emotional fragility.
  • Tom's Hypocrisy and Social Conservatism: Tom, a known adulterer himself, uses moral outrage and racist rhetoric to attack Gatsby, revealing his deep-seated prejudices and his self-serving defense of traditional "family institutions." He leverages social class and perceived moral superiority.
  • Gatsby's Shattered Dream: Gatsby's romanticized vision of Daisy and their past love is brutally confronted with reality. His dream of recreating the past and winning Daisy back unequivocally is ultimately crushed by her admissions and Tom's accusations.
  • Exposure of Gatsby's Illegal Activities: Tom systematically reveals the true, illicit nature of Gatsby's wealth, exposing him as a bootlegger involved in selling grain alcohol through "drug-stores" with Meyer Wolfshiem. This destroys Gatsby's carefully constructed image.
  • Nick's Detachment and Maturation: Nick's 30th birthday marks a moment of significant introspection and disillusionment. He observes the tragic arguments with increasing detachment, symbolizing his growing weariness with the superficiality and moral decay of the wealthy elite.
  • Foreshadowing of Tragedy: The sudden shift to George Wilson, describing his physical and mental distress and his discovery of Myrtle's infidelity, serves as a dark premonition of the violent and tragic events to come.

Important Terms

  • Armistice: A formal agreement of warring parties to stop fighting; a truce. Gatsby mentions the opportunity given to officers after the Armistice of World War I to attend universities.
  • Mint Julep: A cocktail consisting primarily of bourbon, sugar, water, crushed or shaved ice, and fresh mint. Daisy suggests making one for Tom.
  • Libertine: A person, especially a man, who behaves without moral principles or a sense of responsibility, especially in sexual matters. Nick uses this term to describe Tom's past behavior.
  • Prig: A self-righteously moralistic person who behaves as if superior to others. Nick observes Tom's transition from "libertine to prig" during his moralizing outburst.
  • Bootlegger: A person who makes, distributes, or sells alcoholic liquor illegally. Tom exposes Gatsby as a bootlegger.
  • Grain Alcohol: Ethanol, typically produced by fermentation of grain, used here to refer to a component of illegal alcoholic beverages sold during Prohibition.
  • Kapiolani / Punch Bowl: Specific locations in Hawaii associated with romantic memories between Tom and Daisy, brought up by Tom to remind Daisy of their shared past.
  • Michaelis: The young Greek man who runs the coffee joint next to the ash-heaps. He becomes a witness to George Wilson's deteriorating state.
  • Ash-heaps (Valley of Ashes): A desolate, industrial area separating West Egg and New York City, symbolic of the moral and social decay hidden beneath the glittering facade of the Jazz Age.
  • Inquest: A judicial inquiry to ascertain the facts relating to an incident, such as a death. Michaelis is described as the principal witness at the inquest, hinting at an upcoming death investigation.

Detailed Notes

The Confrontation at the Plaza

  • Gatsby's Oxford Claim: The scene opens with Gatsby revealing he attended Oxford for five months after the Armistice, an opportunity for officers. Nick experiences a "renewal of complete faith" in him, but Tom remains skeptical.
  • Daisy's Attempt to De-escalate: Daisy tries to lighten the mood by offering to make mint juleps, but her comment "Then you won't seem so stupid to yourself" highlights the underlying tension.
  • Tom's Direct Challenge: Tom confronts Gatsby directly, asking, "What kind of a row are you trying to cause in my house anyhow?" Gatsby is "content" that the conflict is now "out in the open."
  • Daisy's Plea for Self-Control: Daisy desperately tries to intervene, accusing Tom of causing the "row."
  • Tom's Racist Outburst and Hypocrisy: Tom, enraged, calls Gatsby "Mr. Nobody from Nowhere" and then launches into a tirade against modern morality, culminating in a racist fear of "intermarriage between black and white." Nick notes Tom's complete transition "from libertine to prig."
  • Gatsby's Declaration: Gatsby, interrupted by Daisy's plea to leave, asserts directly to Tom: "Your wife doesn't love you. She's never loved you. She loves me."
  • Gatsby's Account of the Past: Gatsby explains Daisy married Tom because he was poor and she was "tired of waiting." He insists she "never loved any one except me!"
  • Tom's Denial: Tom "automatically" calls Gatsby "crazy!" and insists Daisy loved him then and loves him now, acknowledging he "go[es] off on a spree" but always returns.
  • Daisy's Indignation and Accusation: Daisy calls Tom "revolting" and reveals he "left Chicago" due to a "little spree," demonstrating his past infidelity.
  • Gatsby's Demand for Truth: Gatsby urges Daisy to tell Tom she "never loved him."
  • Daisy's Reluctance and Partial Confession: Daisy hesitates, her eyes falling on Nick and Jordan, before reluctantly admitting, "I never loved him."
  • Tom's Counter-Memories: Tom reminds Daisy of intimate shared moments from their past, like "Kapiolani" and carrying her from "the Punch Bowl," appealing to their shared history.
  • Daisy's Emotional Breakdown: Daisy cries out to Gatsby, "Oh, you want too much!" She confesses, "I love you now—isn't that enough? I can't help what's past. . . . I did love him once—but I loved you too." Gatsby is visibly wounded by "You loved me TOO?"
  • Tom's Final Blow: Tom savagely states, "Even that's a lie," asserting an unbreakable bond and "things between Daisy and me that you'll never know."
  • Daisy's Admission: Daisy admits, "Even alone I can't say I never loved Tom. It wouldn't be true." This shatters Gatsby's dream.

Tom's Victory and Gatsby's Exposure

  • Gatsby's Panic: Gatsby, with "a touch of panic," insists Daisy is leaving Tom and he's "not going to take care of her any more."
  • Tom's Laughter and Accusations: Tom laughs dismissively and begins his attack, calling Gatsby a "common swindler." He reveals he investigated Gatsby and his connection to Meyer Wolfshiem.
  • The Bootlegging Revelation: Tom exposes Gatsby's "drug-stores" business, explaining they "bought up a lot of side-street drug-stores here and in Chicago and sold grain alcohol over the counter." He connects this to Walter Chase, whom Gatsby supposedly "left him in the lurch."
  • Gatsby's Silence and Defeat: Gatsby is unable to effectively defend himself against these specific accusations. His dream is visibly "dead." Daisy, terrified, pulls further into herself.
  • Tom's Triumphant Dismissal: Tom, now in control, sends Daisy home with Gatsby in his car, sarcastically stating, "He won't annoy you. I think he realizes that his presumptuous little flirtation is over."

Nick's Birthday and Foreshadowing

  • Nick's 30th Birthday: As Tom wraps up the whiskey, Nick suddenly remembers it's his 30th birthday. This milestone marks a moment of profound reflection for him, envisioning a "portentous, menacing road of a new decade" and a "decade of loneliness."
  • Detachment: Nick feels detached from Tom's incessant chatter, describing his voice as "remote." He and Jordan are "content to let all their tragic arguments fade with the city lights."
  • Journey Toward Death: Nick's final thought as they drive on is "So we drove on toward death through the cooling twilight," a grim premonition.

Shift to George Wilson

  • Michaelis's Discovery: The narrative shifts abruptly to Michaelis finding George Wilson "sick in his office," pale and shaking.
  • Wilson's Breakdown and Confession: Wilson refuses to go to bed, then reveals he has "my wife locked in up there" and plans to move away with her "till the day after to-morrow."
  • Foreshadowing of Myrtle's Death: Wilson's calm explanation, his locking up Myrtle, and his plan to leave imply he has discovered her affair, setting the stage for the tragic accident to come. Michaelis's role as a principal witness at an "inquest" confirms an upcoming death investigation.

Review Questions

  1. Analyze the distinct strategies Tom and Gatsby employ in their confrontation at the Plaza Hotel. How do their arguments reflect their characters, social standing, and understanding of love and loyalty?
  2. Discuss Daisy's emotional state and actions during the climax of this section. To what extent is she responsible for the outcome, and what do her choices reveal about her character and the limitations placed upon her?
  3. How does Tom's character develop or become more fully revealed through his interaction with Gatsby? Consider his use of racism, his hypocrisy, and his ultimate "victory" in this scene.
  4. Examine Nick's perspective and his reaction to his 30th birthday. How does this personal milestone affect his narration and his growing disillusionment with the wealthy characters and their drama?
  5. What is the narrative significance of the abrupt shift to George Wilson at the end of this section? How does his breakdown foreshadow future events, and what thematic connections can be drawn between his situation and the conflict between Gatsby, Tom, and Daisy?
