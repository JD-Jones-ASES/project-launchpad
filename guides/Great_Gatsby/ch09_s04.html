Chapter 9, Section 4: After Gatsby — Study Guide

Overview

This section details the aftermath of Gatsby's death, focusing on the sparse attendance at his funeral, Nick's encounters with Gatsby's father (Mr. Gatz), and Nick's final disillusioned reflections on the East Coast. It culminates in Nick's departure from New York, but not before he confronts Jordan Baker and Tom Buchanan, revealing their moral failings and solidifying his decision to return to the honesty of the Midwest.

Key Concepts

  • The Hollow Nature of Gatsby's Connections: Despite Gatsby's immense wealth and the crowds he attracted, his funeral is attended by only a few, highlighting the superficiality of his relationships and the transactional nature of the Jazz Age society.
  • Gatsby's Early Ambition and Self-Invention: Through Mr. Gatz and his son's childhood schedule, we gain insight into the disciplined, idealistic young man James Gatz was, driven by a profound desire for self-improvement and success, which contrasts sharply with the corrupt means he later employed.
  • Nick's Disillusionment with the East: Nick articulates his profound discomfort and moral objection to the reckless and amoral lifestyle he witnessed in the East. He perceives the region as "distorted" and "haunted," ultimately leading to his decision to return to the values of the Midwest.
  • The Recklessness of the Wealthy Elite: Nick's final confrontations with Jordan and Tom underscore their irresponsibility and lack of empathy. Tom's unapologetic confession about leading Wilson to Gatsby, and Jordan's "bad driver" metaphor, symbolize the moral carelessness that defined their class.
  • The West as a Moral Sanctuary (for Nick): Nick identifies himself and the core characters as "Westerners" who were "unadaptable to Eastern life," suggesting that their origins provided a moral framework that clashed with the decadence of the East. His return West represents a search for integrity and a rejection of the moral decay he experienced.

Important Terms

  • Mr. Gatz: Jay Gatsby's proud, elderly father from the Midwest. He arrives for his son's funeral, bringing with him a photograph of Gatsby's mansion and Gatsby's boyhood "Hopalong Cassidy" schedule, which reveals Gatsby's early drive for self-improvement.
  • Owl-eyed man: A minor character first encountered marveling at Gatsby's uncut books in his library. He is the only one of Gatsby's partygoers to attend the funeral, serving as a poignant observer of the superficiality of Gatsby's connections.
  • Hopalong Cassidy schedule: A detailed, rigorous self-improvement schedule Gatsby (as "Jimmy Gatz") created in his youth. It outlines daily routines for physical exercise, study, work, and moral resolves, illustrating his extraordinary ambition and dedication to self-invention.
  • Middle West (Nick's definition): For Nick, the Middle West represents more than geography; it embodies a sense of shared identity, tradition, and perhaps a simpler, more authentic existence, contrasted with the moral complexity and decadence of the East.
  • Eastern life (Nick's perception): Portrayed by Nick as morally corrupt, superficial, "distorted," and ultimately "haunted." It is a place where wealth enables reckless behavior without consequence, leading to disillusionment for those with a moral compass.
  • "Bad driver" metaphor: Used by Jordan Baker in her final conversation with Nick. She suggests that Nick, by breaking up with her, proved to be another "bad driver," implying recklessness and a lack of consideration, mirroring the dangerous behavior prevalent among their peers.

Detailed Notes

I. The Funeral Arrangements and Wolfsheim's Refusal

  • Nick attempts to contact those who knew Gatsby, starting with Meyer Wolfsheim.
  • Wolfsheim refuses to get involved, citing a personal rule about not getting "mixed up" when a man is killed. He suggests showing friendship when a person is alive, not after they are dead, highlighting his pragmatic, self-serving nature and the transactional relationships prevalent in their world.
  • Nick realizes Wolfsheim is determined not to come and leaves his office.

II. Mr. Gatz's Arrival and Gatsby's Past

  • Nick returns to West Egg in a drizzle and finds Gatsby's father, Mr. Gatz, excitedly waiting.
  • Mr. Gatz is immensely proud of his son, carrying a worn photograph of Gatsby's house, which he points out with trembling fingers, seeking Nick's admiration.
  • He reveals Gatsby (whom he still calls "Jimmy") bought him a house two years prior and had always been generous.
  • Mr. Gatz then produces a ragged copy of a Hopalong Cassidy book. On the back fly-leaf is Gatsby's childhood "SCHEDULE" from September 12, 1906.
    • The schedule details a rigorous routine: waking early, dumbbell exercise, wall-scaling, studying electricity, work, baseball, elocution practice, and studying needed inventions.
    • It also includes "GENERAL RESOLVES": no wasting time, no smoking/chewing, bathing every other day, reading an improving book per week, saving money, and being better to parents.
  • Mr. Gatz emphasizes "Jimmy's" ambition and his dedication to improving his mind, illustrating Gatsby's lifelong drive for self-perfection and "getting ahead."

III. The Funeral Service

  • As the time for the funeral approaches, only Nick, Mr. Gatz, and a Lutheran minister are present.
  • Nick and Mr. Gatz anxiously wait for others, but no one comes.
  • The procession consists of only three cars: the hearse, the limousine with Nick, Mr. Gatz, and the minister, and Gatsby's station wagon carrying a few servants and the postman.
  • As they enter the cemetery, the "owl-eyed man" appears, soaking wet, having followed them. He is the only other "guest" from Gatsby's social circle.
  • Nick briefly tries to remember Gatsby but can only recall Daisy's absence (no message or flowers).
  • The owl-eyed man utters a poignant "Amen" to a murmured blessing, observing the pathetic turnout.
  • He remarks to Nick, "They used to go there by the hundreds," highlighting the stark contrast between Gatsby's lavish parties and his lonely burial. He concludes with, "The poor son-of-a-bitch."

IV. Nick's Final Reflections and Departure from the East

  • Nick shifts to a retrospective mood, reminiscing about returning West from school at Christmas, emphasizing a sense of shared identity and belonging that he felt in the "real snow" of his "Middle West."
  • He concludes that he, Tom, Gatsby, Daisy, and Jordan were all "Westerners" and shared a "deficiency in common which made us subtly unadaptable to Eastern life."
  • He describes the East, particularly West Egg, as having a "quality of distortion" for him, likening it to a night scene by El Greco, filled with conventional yet grotesque elements and moral decay (the drunken woman on a stretcher, ignored).
  • Feeling "haunted" by the East after Gatsby's death, Nick decides to move back home to the Midwest.

V. Final Confrontations

  • With Jordan Baker: Nick meets Jordan to clear things up. She is dressed for golf, seemingly unaffected.
    • She reveals she's engaged (which Nick doubts).
    • She accuses Nick of "throwing her over" on the telephone, expressing that she "felt a little dizzy for a while."
    • Jordan recalls an earlier conversation about "bad drivers," turning the metaphor on Nick: "Well, I met another bad driver, didn't I?" She implies he was not the "honest, straightforward person" she thought.
    • Nick responds with a weary acknowledgment of his age and desire for honesty: "I'm thirty... I'm five years too old to lie to myself and call it honor." He leaves, "angry, and half in love with her, and tremendously sorry."
  • With Tom Buchanan: Nick encounters Tom on Fifth Avenue. Tom initially tries to shake hands, but Nick refuses, stating, "You know what I think of you."
    • Nick confronts Tom about what he told Wilson the day of Gatsby's death.
    • Tom defiantly confesses he told Wilson who owned the yellow car. He justifies his actions by claiming Wilson was "crazy enough to kill me" and had a revolver. He adds that Gatsby "had it coming to him" for throwing "dust into your eyes just like he did in Daisy's" and for running over Myrtle.
    • Nick notes the "unutterable fact that it wasn't true" (Gatsby running over Myrtle).
    • Tom expresses his own "suffering" over Myrtle's death, revealing his complete lack of empathy for others and his self-serving narrative.
    • Nick walks away, seeing Tom as "careless" and "destroying things and creatures and then retreating back into their money or their vast carelessness."

Review Questions

  1. Why is Gatsby's funeral so poorly attended, despite the lavish parties he hosted? What does the turnout, particularly the presence of the "owl-eyed man" and Mr. Gatz, reveal about the nature of Gatsby's relationships and the society he inhabited?
  2. Analyze the significance of the "Hopalong Cassidy" schedule that Mr. Gatz shares with Nick. How does this artifact from Gatsby's youth deepen our understanding of his character and his lifelong pursuit of the American Dream?
  3. Discuss Nick's changing perceptions of the East and the West throughout the novel and specifically in this section. What does he mean by describing the East as "distorted" and "haunted," and why does he ultimately decide to leave?
  4. Examine Nick's final conversations with Jordan Baker and Tom Buchanan. What do these interactions reveal about their respective characters, their moral culpability, and Nick's own growth and disillusionment?
  5. How does the metaphor of "bad drivers" contribute to the themes of carelessness, responsibility, and moral decay in the novel? Who are the "bad drivers" in the story, and what are the consequences of their actions?
