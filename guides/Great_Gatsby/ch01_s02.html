Study Guide: The Jazz Age and the 1920s

Key Concepts

  • The Great Gatsby is set during the summer of 1922, in a decade Fitzgerald himself named "the Jazz Age"
  • World War I (1914–1918) left approximately seventeen million dead and shattered faith in progress, producing postwar disillusionment that runs through every major character in the novel
  • Prohibition (Eighteenth Amendment, ratified 1919) banned alcohol but created a vast underground economy of bootleggers, speakeasies, and organized crime — the likely source of Gatsby's fortune
  • The 1920s economic boom generated a new class of self-made wealthy Americans; the novel maps this social reality onto its geography: East Egg (old money) versus West Egg (new money)
  • The automobile transformed American life and appears in the novel as both a symbol of freedom and an instrument of destruction
  • The Great Migration, the rise of jazz, and the Nineteenth Amendment (women's suffrage, 1920) contributed to broader social change; "flappers" challenged Victorian conventions
  • Fitzgerald was both a participant in and a critic of the Jazz Age — attracted to its glamour but aware of its moral emptiness

Summary

The Great Gatsby cannot be fully understood without knowing the world in which it is set. The novel takes place in the summer of 1922, early in a decade of dramatic social and economic transformation. That transformation was shaped, first of all, by the aftermath of World War I: the deaths of roughly seventeen million people and the failure of the ideals — honor, duty, glory — that had justified the war produced a deep postwar disillusionment. This skepticism about inherited values runs beneath the surface of every character's story, from Gatsby's dubious military heroism to Tom Buchanan's restless aggression to Nick Carraway's quiet sense that the old certainties no longer hold.

Prohibition, launched by the Eighteenth Amendment in 1919, attempted to eliminate drinking but instead created an underground economy of bootleggers, speakeasies, and organized crime. The novel strongly implies that Gatsby's mysterious wealth is built on illegal alcohol distribution. Prohibition also produced a broader cultural hypocrisy: respectable citizens routinely broke the law, and the boundary between legitimate business and criminal enterprise blurred. Simultaneously, the 1920s economy expanded dramatically — the stock market rose, consumer credit spread, and new industries such as automobiles, radio, and motion pictures reshaped daily life.

The economic boom created a new class of self-made wealthy Americans, and the novel encodes the social tensions this produced in its geography. East Egg represents old, inherited wealth (the Buchanans); West Egg represents new, self-made wealth (Gatsby). The old-money conviction that new money can never purchase true social belonging is central to the novel's conflict. Alongside these economic shifts, the Great Migration, the rise of jazz, women's suffrage, and the emergence of the "flapper" culture all contributed to a loosening of Victorian social norms — a transformation the novel captures in characters like Jordan Baker.

Key Terms

  • Jazz Age: Fitzgerald's name for the 1920s, a decade of cultural energy, economic expansion, and loosened social conventions
  • Prohibition: The ban on manufacture, sale, and transportation of alcohol established by the Eighteenth Amendment (ratified 1919), which inadvertently created a large underground economy
  • Bootlegger: A person who illegally manufactures or distributes alcohol during Prohibition; the novel implies Gatsby built his fortune this way
  • Speakeasy: An illegal establishment selling alcohol during Prohibition
  • East Egg / West Egg: The novel's fictional Long Island locations representing old inherited wealth and new self-made wealth, respectively
  • Great Migration: The movement of African Americans from the rural South to northern cities during the early twentieth century
  • Flappers: Young women of the 1920s who challenged traditional expectations about dress, behavior, and independence
  • Nineteenth Amendment: Ratified in 1920, it granted women the right to vote in the United States

Review Questions

  1. How did World War I contribute to the atmosphere of disillusionment in The Great Gatsby?
  2. What were the unintended consequences of Prohibition, and how do they appear in the novel?
  3. What social distinction does the novel's geography — East Egg versus West Egg — represent?
  4. How did the automobile function as both a symbol and a plot element in 1920s American culture and in the novel?
  5. In what sense was Fitzgerald simultaneously an insider and a critic of the Jazz Age?
