Study Guide: Chapter 1, Section 2 - First Impressions and Introductions

Overview

This section introduces Nick Carraway, our narrator, as he settles into West Egg, Long Island, and provides his initial impressions of his wealthy neighbors. It details his first dinner with his cousin Daisy Buchanan and her husband Tom in East Egg, sketching vivid portraits of their personalities through their physical presence and surroundings. Crucially, the section also hints at Nick's complex narrative approach, where his stated desire to "reserve all judgments" immediately clashes with his keen and often critical observations.

Key Concepts

  • Nick Carraway's Narrative Perspective: Nick introduces himself as a tolerant individual who, following his father's advice, reserves judgment about others. However, his immediate and sharp observations of Gatsby, Tom, and Daisy demonstrate a contradiction, suggesting he is an observant and discerning, if not entirely objective, narrator. This gap between his stated philosophy and his actions is a key element for understanding his role in the story.
  • The Socioeconomic Divide of East Egg and West Egg: The stark contrast between Nick's "small eyesore" of a house, Gatsby's "colossal affair" next door (both in West Egg), and the grand Buchanan mansion in East Egg immediately establishes a visible economic and social hierarchy. West Egg represents "new money" and ostentatious display, while East Egg embodies "old money" with a more established, albeit equally extravagant, sense of wealth.
  • Indirect Characterization through Description: Fitzgerald heavily relies on physical descriptions of characters and their environments to reveal their personalities and social standing. Tom Buchanan's "cruel body" and "supercilious manner" immediately convey his dominance and arrogance, while Daisy's "thrilling" voice hints at her allure and potential for manipulation, even as it draws listeners in. The grandeur of the Buchanan house, too, indirectly characterizes their world of privilege and superficiality.
  • Early Glimpses of Discontent and Irony: Despite the outward glamour and wealth, there are immediate suggestions of underlying unhappiness and moral ambiguity, particularly within the Buchanan household. Nick's critical observations of Tom's aggressive nature and Daisy's performance-like charm introduce a subtle irony to their seemingly perfect lives.

Important Terms

  • Nick Carraway: The narrator of The Great Gatsby, a Yale-educated bond salesman from the Midwest who moves to West Egg, Long Island.
  • "Reserving Judgment": Nick's stated philosophy, learned from his father, which claims he avoids making quick evaluations of others. This is immediately challenged by his own narrative.
  • West Egg: The less fashionable of the two wealthy Long Island peninsulas, home to "new money," including Nick and Jay Gatsby.
  • East Egg: The more fashionable and established of the two wealthy Long Island peninsulas, home to "old money," including Daisy and Tom Buchanan.
  • Jay Gatsby: Nick's mysterious, immensely wealthy neighbor in West Egg, known for his lavish mansion and parties.
  • Daisy Buchanan: Nick's second cousin, a beautiful and charming woman living in East Egg with her husband, Tom. Her voice is particularly captivating.
  • Tom Buchanan: Daisy's immensely wealthy husband, a former Yale football star described as powerful, arrogant, and physically imposing.
  • Jordan Baker: A languid, cynical professional golfer whom Nick meets at the Buchanan's dinner party.
  • Supercilious: Behaving or looking as though one thinks one is superior to others; a term used to describe Tom Buchanan's manner.
  • Indirect Characterization: The process by which the personality of a fictitious character is revealed through the character's speech, actions, appearance, etc., rather than through direct statements by the narrator.

Detailed Notes

1. Nick Carraway: The Narrator's Introduction

  • Background: Nick introduces himself as a bond salesman from the Midwest, educated at Yale, who has come East in 1922 to work on Wall Street. He is renting a small, rather ordinary house in West Egg.
  • His Philosophy ("Reserving Judgment"): Nick explains his father's advice: "Whenever you feel like criticizing anyone...just remember that all the people in this world haven't had the advantages that you've had." This counsel led Nick to cultivate a habit of tolerance and "reserving all judgments," which he claims makes him a confidant for others.
  • The Contradiction: Immediately, Nick's narrative voice demonstrates a sharp, often critical, judgment of Gatsby, Tom, and Daisy. He admits his tolerance has limits, particularly after his experiences in the East. This early contradiction reveals his complexity as a narrator, suggesting he might be more opinionated and less objective than he presents himself.

2. West Egg and East Egg: A Glimpse of Two Worlds

  • Nick's Humble Abode: Nick describes his rented house in West Egg as a "small eyesore," costing eighty dollars a month, situated between two enormous estates.
  • Gatsby's Mansion: His immediate neighbor is the mysterious Jay Gatsby, whose mansion is a "colossal affair," a "factual imitation of some Hôtel de Ville in Normandy." It boasts a tower, a marble swimming pool, and over forty acres of land. This stark contrast between Nick's home and Gatsby's establishes the scale of wealth in West Egg.
  • The Drive to East Egg: Nick's journey to East Egg to visit his second cousin, Daisy Buchanan, and her husband, Tom, highlights the geographical and social separation of the two peninsulas.
  • The Buchanan Mansion: The East Egg estate of the Buchanans is also grand and imposing, though perhaps with a different architectural sensibility, suggesting old money's established elegance.

3. The Buchanans: Wealth, Power, and Underlying Tension

  • Tom Buchanan:
    • Physical Description: Nick emphasizes Tom's physical bulk and power. He is a "sturdy straw-haired man of thirty" with "two shining arrogant eyes." His body is described as "enormous power" and "capable of enormous leverage," with muscles shifting "under his thin coat." Nick explicitly calls it "a cruel body."
    • Character Implications: These details immediately suggest dominance, aggression, and a man who uses his physical presence to intimidate. His "supercilious manner" further highlights his arrogance and sense of superiority. His past as a Yale football star underscores his physical prowess and competitive nature.
  • Daisy Buchanan:
    • Voice as Characterization: Nick pays exceptional attention to Daisy's voice, describing it as "low" and "thrilling," "the kind of voice that the ear follows up and down, as if each speech is an arrangement of notes that will never be played again." It has "a singing compulsion, a whispered 'Listen,'" and carries "a promise that she had done gay, exciting things in the past."
    • Effect and Implication: Her voice is alluring and draws listeners in, creating a sense of intimacy and excitement. However, Nick's later comments suggest he questions the sincerity behind this performance, hinting that the "promise" might be unfulfilled or deceptive.
  • Their Dynamic: The initial interaction between Tom and Daisy, observed by Nick, suggests underlying tension or dissatisfaction despite their immense wealth and privileged lifestyle.

4. Other Introductions

  • Jordan Baker: Nick also meets Jordan Baker, a "languid" professional golfer, at the Buchanan's dinner party. Her presence adds another layer to the social circle Nick is entering.

5. Fitzgerald's Descriptive Techniques

  • Focus on Surfaces and Possessions: Fitzgerald uses detailed descriptions of the characters' bodies, clothing, and homes to reveal their inner selves and their place in society. The opulence of the mansions, Tom's aggressive physique, and Daisy's captivating voice all serve as indirect characterization.
  • Indirect Characterization: Rather than stating directly that Tom is arrogant or Daisy is alluring, Fitzgerald shows these qualities through carefully chosen descriptive details, inviting the reader to infer meaning. This technique allows for a deeper, more nuanced understanding of the characters and their world.

Review Questions

  1. Nick claims to "reserve all judgments," yet within the opening paragraphs he renders sharp judgments about Gatsby, Tom, and Daisy. What does this contradiction reveal about Nick as a narrator, and how might Fitzgerald intend the reader to interpret this gap between his stated philosophy and his actions?
  2. Compare and contrast Nick's modest house in West Egg with Gatsby's lavish mansion next door. What does this initial juxtaposition of residences suggest about the "new money" culture of West Egg and the aspirations of its inhabitants?
  3. Analyze Fitzgerald's extensive physical description of Tom Buchanan. How do specific details about his body, such as his "enormous power" and "cruel body," contribute to his indirect characterization and foreshadow his role in the narrative?
  4. Nick describes Daisy's voice with particular fascination. Based on his descriptions, what effect does her voice have on those who listen to her, and what deeper symbolic meaning might it carry about her character or the world she inhabits?
  5. How does Fitzgerald utilize descriptive details—focusing on surfaces, possessions, and physicality—as a primary form of indirect characterization for the Buchanans and their world? Provide specific examples from the text.
