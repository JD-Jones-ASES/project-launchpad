Study Guide: Chapter 4: The Novel

Key Concepts

  • Nick catalogs the vast and disreputable array of guests who attend Gatsby's parties
  • Gatsby drives Nick to New York and delivers a rehearsed, implausible autobiography
  • Physical evidence (a war medal, an Oxford photograph) momentarily convinces Nick of Gatsby's story
  • Meyer Wolfshiem is introduced as a gambler who fixed the 1919 World Series — a clue to the criminal origins of Gatsby's wealth
  • Jordan Baker reveals the backstory: Gatsby and Daisy fell in love in Louisville in 1917
  • Daisy nearly refused to marry Tom Buchanan on their wedding eve but ultimately went through with it
  • Gatsby has purchased his West Egg mansion specifically to be across the bay from Daisy
  • Gatsby's request of Nick: invite Daisy to tea so Gatsby can "come over" from next door

Summary

Chapter 4 opens with Nick compiling a roster of Gatsby's summer guests — a parade of names suggesting corruption, recklessness, and shadowy social standing. Gatsby then arrives at Nick's door in his spectacular cream-colored car and drives him to lunch in New York. Along the way, Gatsby delivers an elaborate autobiography: wealthy Midwestern family, an Oxford education, wartime heroism in the Argonne Forest, and the "Orderi di Danilo" medal from Montenegro. Nick finds the account unconvincing until Gatsby produces the physical evidence — the medal and an Oxford photograph — at which point his skepticism collapses entirely.

At lunch in a Forty-second Street cellar restaurant, Gatsby introduces Nick to Meyer Wolfshiem, a small-time sentimentalist whose cuff buttons are made from human molars. Wolfshiem nostalgically recounts the murder of Rosy Rosenthal at the old Metropole, and Gatsby later reveals that Wolfshiem is the man who fixed the 1919 World Series. When Tom Buchanan unexpectedly appears in the restaurant, Gatsby vanishes without greeting him.

That afternoon, Jordan Baker tells Nick the backstory Gatsby strategically omitted. In Louisville in 1917, Daisy Fay — the most popular girl in town — fell in love with Lieutenant Jay Gatsby, who was stationed at Camp Taylor. When Gatsby shipped overseas, Daisy eventually married Tom Buchanan in a lavish ceremony, though the night before the wedding she was found drunk in her room, clutching a letter and trying to return Tom's pearls. She married him anyway. Years later, Jordan reveals, Gatsby has engineered his entire situation — the mansion, the parties — just to get across the bay from Daisy and arrange a simple tea meeting through Nick.

Key Terms

  • knickerbocker: A member of the original Dutch settlers of New York; by extension, a person of established New York social standing
  • rajah: An Indian king or prince; used here to evoke a life of exotic luxury and extravagance
  • Montenegro: A small kingdom in southeastern Europe; Gatsby's medal from Montenegro is offered as proof of his wartime heroism
  • denizen: An inhabitant or occupant of a particular place; someone who frequents a specific location
  • Camp Taylor: A U.S. Army training camp near Louisville, Kentucky, where Gatsby was stationed in 1917

Review Questions

  1. What physical evidence does Gatsby produce to support his autobiography, and why does it persuade Nick even though his story sounds implausible?
  2. Who is Meyer Wolfshiem, and what does his association with Gatsby reveal about the source of Gatsby's wealth?
  3. Why does Gatsby disappear from the restaurant when Tom Buchanan appears?
  4. What was Daisy's behavior on the eve of her wedding to Tom, and what does it suggest about her feelings for Gatsby?
  5. Jordan reveals that Gatsby bought his mansion specifically to be across the bay from Daisy. How does this reframe everything Nick has observed about Gatsby's parties and lifestyle?
