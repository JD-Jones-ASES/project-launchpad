Study Guide: Chapter 4, Section 4 – Gatsby's Story

Overview

This section continues Nick Carraway's lunch with Jay Gatsby and Meyer Wolfshiem, revealing shocking details about Wolfshiem's criminal past and Gatsby's mysterious connections. The narrative then shifts to a flashback told by Jordan Baker, recounting Daisy Fay Buchanan's early life, her initial romantic encounter with a young military officer named Gatsby, her lavish marriage to Tom Buchanan, and the immediate signs of their troubled relationship, shedding light on the history that intertwines Gatsby and Daisy.

Key Concepts

  • Gatsby's Mysterious Business: Gatsby's interactions with Wolfshiem hint at the illicit nature of his wealth and connections, further establishing his enigmatic background.
  • Meyer Wolfshiem as a Criminal Figure: Wolfshiem is revealed as the man who fixed the 1919 World Series, solidifying his role as a prominent figure in the criminal underworld and illuminating the source of Gatsby's "business connections."
  • The Corrupting Influence of Wealth: The ease with which Wolfshiem discusses his criminal acts and the luxury he enjoys highlights the pervasive corruption that could exist beneath the glittering surface of the Jazz Age.
  • Daisy's Past and Entrapment: Jordan's story reveals Daisy's youthful romance with Gatsby, her family's intervention, and her subsequent marriage to Tom Buchanan, motivated by social expectations, wealth, and security, despite deep emotional conflict.
  • Early Signs of Marital Discord: The flashback shows that Daisy's marriage to Tom was fraught with emotional turmoil and infidelity from its very beginning, setting a precedent for their dysfunctional relationship.
  • The Unreliability of Reputation: Daisy's ability to maintain an "absolutely perfect reputation" despite moving in a "fast crowd" and her husband's blatant infidelity underscores the superficiality of social appearances.

Important Terms

  • Meyer Wolfshiem: A shadowy, Jewish gambler and business associate of Gatsby. He is characterized by his peculiar mannerisms, such as his cuff links made of human molars, and his infamous past as the man who fixed the 1919 World Series.
  • "Oggsford man": Wolfshiem's mispronunciation of "Oxford," used to refer to Gatsby's supposed attendance at Oxford University, implying a cultured, upper-class background.
  • World's Series (1919): The American baseball championship series infamously "fixed" by gamblers, including Meyer Wolfshiem. This historical event serves as a concrete link between Gatsby's associates and large-scale criminality.
  • Louisville: The city in Kentucky where Daisy Fay grew up and where she and Gatsby first met in 1917.
  • Camp Taylor: A U.S. Army training camp near Louisville, Kentucky, where Gatsby was stationed as a young military officer in 1917 before being shipped overseas.
  • Sauterne: A sweet white wine, in this context, used by Daisy to get drunk before her wedding to Tom.
  • Santa Barbara: A city in California where Tom and Daisy stayed after their wedding, and where Tom's first recorded act of infidelity occurred.

Detailed Notes

I. Nick's Lunch with Gatsby and Wolfshiem

  • Wolfshiem's Business Inquiry: The lunch begins with Meyer Wolfshiem, upon being introduced to Nick, mistakenly assuming Nick is a "business connection" Gatsby is looking for. Gatsby quickly corrects him, asserting Nick is "just a friend," indicating a separation between his social and business worlds.
  • Wolfshiem's Demeanor and Oddities:
    • Wolfshiem eats with a "ferocious delicacy" and his eyes constantly rove the room, suggesting a cautious, perhaps suspicious, nature.
    • He proudly displays his cuff buttons, which are made of "finest specimens of human molars," a grotesque detail highlighting his underworld connections and lack of conventional morality.
  • Gatsby's Apology and Nick's Frustration: Gatsby apologizes to Nick for having made him "angry" that morning and for communicating through Jordan Baker. Nick expresses his dislike for "mysteries" and demands to know frankly what Gatsby wants, resenting the indirect approach. Gatsby assures him "it's nothing underhand."
  • Gatsby's Abrupt Departure: Gatsby suddenly leaves the table to make a telephone call, leaving Nick alone with Wolfshiem.
  • Wolfshiem's Praise for Gatsby: Wolfshiem expresses high regard for Gatsby, calling him a "fine fellow," "handsome," and a "perfect gentleman." He also reinforces the idea of Gatsby being an "Oggsford man," confirming Gatsby's own previous claim to Nick.
  • Wolfshiem's History with Gatsby: Wolfshiem states he made Gatsby's acquaintance "just after the war" and immediately recognized him as a man of "fine breeding."
  • The Revelation of the World's Series Fix: Upon Gatsby's return, Nick asks about Wolfshiem. Gatsby, after a slight hesitation, coolly reveals that Meyer Wolfshiem is "the man who fixed the World's Series back in 1919."
    • Nick is profoundly "staggered" by this, realizing the immense personal agency behind such a historical scandal. He previously thought of it as an abstract event, not the deliberate action of one man playing "with the faith of fifty million people."
    • Gatsby explains Wolfshiem "just saw the opportunity" and is "a smart man" whom "they can't get."
  • Encounter with Tom Buchanan: Nick spots Tom Buchanan across the room and impulsively calls him over to introduce him to Gatsby.
    • Tom eagerly approaches, demanding to know where Nick has been and mentioning Daisy is "furious."
    • During the brief handshake between Tom and Gatsby, Gatsby displays a "strained, unfamiliar look of embarrassment," revealing his profound discomfort in Tom's presence.
    • Immediately after the introduction, Gatsby mysteriously disappears, leaving Nick to explain his absence.

II. Jordan Baker's Flashback: Daisy's Past

  • 1917, Louisville – Daisy's Youth: Jordan Baker begins a long flashback, recounting Daisy Fay's life in Louisville in 1917. Daisy was eighteen, popular, wealthy, lived in a grand house with large lawns and patriotic banners, and was constantly sought after by young officers from Camp Taylor. She dressed in white and drove a white roadster.
  • Daisy's First Encounter with Jay Gatsby: Jordan recalls seeing Daisy with a lieutenant she didn't recognize in her white roadster. This officer was Jay Gatsby. They were deeply engrossed in each other, and Jordan remembers the intense way Gatsby looked at Daisy, a look "every young girl wants to be looked at sometime." Jordan didn't connect this officer to the Gatsby she knew years later until Nick described him.
  • Daisy's Emotional Turmoil (Post-Gatsby's Departure):
    • Wild rumors circulated about Daisy. One winter night, her mother prevented her from running away to New York to say goodbye to a soldier going overseas (implied to be Gatsby), leading to weeks of estrangement from her family.
    • After this, Daisy stopped dating soldiers and only went out with "flat-footed, short-sighted young men" who couldn't get into the army.
  • Engagement and Marriage to Tom Buchanan (1919):
    • After the Armistice, Daisy regained her vivaciousness. She had a debut and was briefly engaged to a man from New Orleans.
    • In June, she married Tom Buchanan of Chicago in a lavish ceremony in Louisville, surrounded by wealth and "pomp and circumstance." Tom gifted her a string of pearls valued at $350,000.
  • Daisy's Pre-Wedding Breakdown: As bridesmaid, Jordan finds Daisy drunk and distraught half an hour before the bridal dinner.
    • Daisy clutches a bottle of Sauterne and a letter. She wants to call off the wedding, begging Jordan to return the pearls and declare that "Daisy's change' her mine!"
    • She cries uncontrollably and refuses to let go of the letter, taking it into the bathtub with her, where it disintegrates.
    • Despite this emotional collapse, Daisy is revived and marries Tom "without so much as a shiver" the next day.
  • Early Years of Marriage and Tom's Infidelity:
    • Tom and Daisy embark on a three-month honeymoon to the South Seas.
    • Jordan observes Daisy in Santa Barbara, deeply infatuated with Tom, displaying "unfathomable delight" in his presence.
    • A week later, Tom crashes his car on Ventura Road, revealing his infidelity as a chambermaid from the hotel is with him and suffers a broken arm, making the papers.
  • Later Life and Reputation:
    • Daisy gives birth to her daughter the following April, and they live in France for a year before returning to Chicago.
    • Daisy maintains an "absolutely perfect reputation" in Chicago, despite moving with a "fast crowd" of young, rich, and wild people. Jordan attributes this to Daisy's not drinking, allowing her to be discreet and control her image.
    • Jordan hints at a deeper, perhaps melancholic, quality in Daisy's voice, suggesting hidden desires or a capacity for "amour."
  • Daisy's Recognition of Gatsby: Six weeks prior, Daisy heard Gatsby's name from Jordan (when Jordan asked Nick if he knew Gatsby). Daisy's reaction—waking Jordan, asking "What Gatsby?" in a "strangest voice"—confirmed her realization that the wealthy man in West Egg was the same officer she had known in Louisville. Jordan then made the connection herself.

Review Questions

  1. How does Meyer Wolfshiem's character and his revelation about the 1919 World Series fix contribute to Nick's understanding of Gatsby's world and the source of his wealth?
  2. What is the significance of Gatsby's "strained, unfamiliar look of embarrassment" when he is introduced to Tom Buchanan? What does this moment foreshadow?
  3. Describe Daisy's emotional state on the day before her wedding to Tom Buchanan. What does her behavior, particularly with the letter and the pearls, reveal about her feelings and motivations at that time?
  4. How does Jordan Baker's account of Tom Buchanan's car accident in Santa Barbara, so early in his marriage to Daisy, establish a pattern for their relationship? What does this imply about their future?
  5. Jordan suggests that Daisy maintains an "absolutely perfect reputation" by not drinking and being able to "hold her tongue." What does this observation, along with the hint about Daisy's voice, tell us about Daisy's character and her approach to life in the wealthy social circles she inhabits?
