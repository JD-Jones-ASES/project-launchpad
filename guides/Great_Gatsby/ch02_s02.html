Study Guide: Chapter 1: Literary Analysis

Key Concepts

  • Nick Carraway is an unreliable narrator — he announces his objectivity while simultaneously demonstrating his subjectivity through constant interpretation and judgment
  • Fitzgerald's choice of Nick as narrator means readers receive all events filtered through one participant's biases and sympathies
  • Symbolic geography: East Egg (old wealth, inherited privilege) vs. West Egg (new wealth, lacking social pedigree) establishes the novel's central class tension
  • The green light at the end of Daisy's dock is the novel's most iconic symbol — a beacon of desire that is always visible but never reachable
  • Tom's enthusiasm for racist pseudoscience (a fictional book modeled on Lothrop Stoddard's real 1920 work) reveals his bigotry and the hollowness of his "civilization"
  • Daisy's cynicism — including her hope that her daughter will be "a beautiful little fool" — is presented as performed rather than genuinely felt

Summary

Fitzgerald's most consequential artistic decision in The Great Gatsby is his choice of narrator. Nick establishes his credentials as a reliable witness in the opening paragraphs — tolerant, non-judgmental, taught by his father to reserve criticism. Yet within sentences he is rendering verdicts on every character: Gatsby represented "everything for which I have an unaffected scorn," Tom has "a cruel body," and Nick detects "the basic insincerity" in Daisy's words. This contradiction is deliberate. Nick is a narrator who announces objectivity and then immediately demonstrates subjectivity, and the reader must learn to read through him rather than simply with him.

The chapter introduces the novel's symbolic geography. East Egg, with its "white palaces" and old money, represents inherited privilege and established social position. West Egg, where Nick and Gatsby live, houses the newly rich — those who have money but not the social pedigree East Egg demands. The "courtesy bay" between them is narrow but socially impassable. Fitzgerald uses this geography to raise the novel's central question: whether wealth alone can purchase social acceptance, or whether class in America is as rigid as the aristocracies the nation supposedly left behind.

The chapter closes with the green light — Gatsby's arms outstretched toward a small light at the end of Daisy's dock. This image establishes the novel's dominant symbol of longing: a goal that is always visible, always just across the water, and never truly within reach.

Key Terms

  • Unreliable narrator: A narrator whose credibility is compromised by bias, limited knowledge, or personal involvement — Nick is unreliable not because he lies, but because he selects, interprets, and arranges events
  • Symbolic geography: The use of physical settings as symbols — here, East Egg and West Egg embody old vs. new wealth and the class divisions of 1920s America
  • Green light: The recurring symbol of desire and unattainability first glimpsed at the end of Daisy's dock; associated with Gatsby's longing throughout the novel
  • Feigned: Simulated or pretended; not genuine — used of Nick's deliberate avoidance of unwanted confidences
  • Supercilious: Behaving as though one is superior to others; disdainfully proud — used to describe Tom Buchanan's manner

Review Questions

  1. What does Nick claim about himself in the novel's opening paragraphs, and in what ways does the chapter itself undercut that claim?
  2. What is an unreliable narrator, and what specific evidence from Chapter 1 suggests Nick fits this description?
  3. How does the East Egg / West Egg distinction function as a symbol of class in American society?
  4. What does Tom's discussion of "The Rise of the Colored Empires" reveal about his character and about the world Fitzgerald is depicting?
  5. How does the image of the green light at the end of the chapter establish a central theme of the novel?
