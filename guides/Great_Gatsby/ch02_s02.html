Chapter 2, Section 2: The Valley of Ashes - Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the desolate "valley of ashes," a stark industrial wasteland situated between the opulent West Egg and the vibrant New York City. Overseen by the faded, watchful eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg, this area serves as a backdrop for Tom Buchanan's clandestine meeting with his mistress, Myrtle Wilson, at her husband George Wilson's garage. The encounter reveals significant power imbalances, Tom's domineering character, George Wilson's quiet desperation, and Myrtle's vibrant, restless energy.

Key Concepts

  • The Valley of Ashes as a Symbol: This desolate, ash-covered industrial wasteland represents the moral and social decay hidden beneath the glittering surface of the Jazz Age. It's a place where the American Dream has turned to dust, a dumping ground for the industrial waste of the prosperous city, and metaphorically, for the forgotten lives of the working class. It stands in stark contrast to the wealth of East and West Egg.
  • The Eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg: A faded billboard featuring gigantic, spectacled eyes that "brood" over the valley. These eyes are an unsettling, watchful presence, often interpreted as a symbol of God's unseeing or indifferent judgment, the commercialization of spirituality, or the decay of idealism.
  • Power Dynamics and Class: The interaction between Tom Buchanan and George Wilson starkly illustrates the immense power imbalance between the wealthy elite and the struggling working class. Tom's condescending attitude and economic leverage highlight his sense of social superiority and his willingness to exploit others.
  • Contrasting Personalities: The section presents a vivid contrast between George Wilson, described as "spiritless" and "anaemic," and his wife Myrtle, who possesses "immediately perceptible vitality." This contrast emphasizes Myrtle's ambition and her desire to escape her dreary existence, as well as George's powerlessness.
  • Nick as Observer: Nick Carraway's role as the narrator and observer is crucial. He is a witness to Tom's moral transgressions and the harsh realities of the valley, allowing the reader to experience these events through his relatively objective, though sometimes judgmental, perspective.

Important Terms

  • Valley of Ashes: A desolate, industrial wasteland between West Egg and New York City, characterized by dust and ash, symbolizing poverty, industrial decay, and moral desolation.
  • Doctor T. J. Eckleburg: A faded billboard advertising an oculist, featuring giant, spectacled eyes. It acts as a symbolic, watchful presence over the valley, often interpreted as representing an absent God, indifferent judgment, or commercialism.
  • West Egg: The "new money" district of Long Island, where Nick and Gatsby reside, representing flashy wealth and a less established social standing.
  • East Egg: The "old money" district, home to Tom and Daisy Buchanan, signifying inherited wealth, established aristocracy, and often, moral complacency.
  • Condescending: Exhibiting an attitude of superiority; talking down to someone. Tom Buchanan's behavior towards George Wilson is highly condescending.
  • Domineering: Asserting one's will over another in an arrogant or arbitrary way. Tom's control over the conversation and his threats toward Wilson show his domineering nature.
  • Anaemic: Lacking in color, spirit, or vitality; sickly. Used to describe George Wilson, suggesting his physical and emotional depletion.
  • Vitality: The state of being strong and active; energy. Myrtle Wilson is described as having "immediately perceptible vitality," contrasting sharply with her husband.

Detailed Notes

The Valley of Ashes: A Desolate Landscape

  • Geographical Location: The valley of ashes is situated halfway between the wealthy communities of West Egg and the vibrant metropolis of New York City. The motor road and railroad tracks run alongside it, symbolizing a forgotten transition zone.
  • Description and Imagery: Fitzgerald describes it as a grotesque, desolate land where ashes form into ridges, hills, and fantastic shapes resembling houses, chimneys, and men. The air is thick with powdery ash, stirred by "gray, ash-covered men" who move like ghosts. This imagery establishes a stark, somber mood.
  • Symbolic Significance: This setting serves as a powerful symbol. It represents the industrial byproduct of American prosperity, a place where the forgotten poor reside, and a moral wasteland reflecting the underlying decay of the Jazz Age's superficial glamour. It stands in direct contrast to the bright, clean opulence of the Eggs.

The Eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg

  • Physical Description: A massive, faded billboard advertising an oculist. It features enormous blue eyes, "gigantic," with irises "a yard high," looking out from a pair of yellow spectacles over a nonexistent nose.
  • Origin Story: The sign was placed by an oculist who either went blind or moved away, leaving the advertisement to slowly decay. This backstory adds to its sense of abandonment and obsolescence.
  • Symbolic Interpretations:
    • Indifferent God: Many interpret the eyes as a symbolic representation of God, or a higher moral authority, silently observing the moral decay without intervention.
    • Commercialism: The eyes are a relic of a failed business venture, representing the pervasive commercialism that has replaced spiritual or moral guidance.
    • Faded Idealism: The decaying sign itself can symbolize the fading of the American Dream or the loss of moral clarity in the modern era.
    • Conscience: They act as an unsettling, watchful presence, perhaps a collective conscience for the characters who pass through the valley.

The Garage Visit: Tom, George, and Myrtle

Tom Buchanan's Behavior and Power Dynamics

  • Condescending and Domineering: Tom treats George Wilson with a mixture of false geniality and outright contempt. He slaps Wilson on the shoulder and makes empty promises about selling him a car.
  • Exerting Control: When Wilson attempts to press Tom about the car, Tom quickly becomes cold, threatening to sell it elsewhere. He controls the conversation, dismisses Wilson's concerns, and later calls Wilson "dumb" to Nick, showcasing his absolute social and economic superiority.
  • Abuse of Power: Tom uses his wealth and social standing to intimidate Wilson and exploit him, highlighting the vast class divisions and the ruthlessness of the upper class.

George Wilson's Characterization

  • Physical and Emotional State: George is described as "blond, spiritless," and "anaemic." He wipes his hands on waste, and a "damp gleam of hope" appears in his eyes when he sees Tom, suggesting economic desperation and a pathetic reliance on Tom's patronage.
  • Submissive and Powerless: He answers "unconvincingly" and his voice fades off under Tom's pressure. He is clearly intimidated by Tom and unable to assert himself, blending into the ash-gray walls of his garage.
  • Contrast to Myrtle: His lack of vitality and submissive nature provide a sharp contrast to his wife's vibrant entrance.

Myrtle Wilson's Entrance and Vitality

  • Striking Presence: Myrtle enters with "immediately perceptible vitality," a stark contrast to her husband's listlessness and the dreary surroundings. She carries herself sensuously and commands attention.
  • Assertiveness: She is forceful and assertive, described as walking through her husband "as if he were a ghost." Her presence is powerful, and she is clearly not content with her life in the valley of ashes.
  • Escape from Drabness: Her vibrancy suggests a yearning for a life beyond the valley, mirroring her affair with Tom as an attempt to escape her economic and social circumstances.

Tom's Orchestration and Nick's Observation

  • Tom's Manipulation: Tom orchestrates the entire visit, forcing Nick off the train, arranging Myrtle's departure to follow them into the city, and deceiving Wilson about their intentions. This reveals Tom's disregard for others' feelings and his manipulative nature.
  • Nick's Role: Nick serves as the detached, yet morally sensitive, observer. He is uncomfortable with Tom's behavior and the illicit nature of the affair, but he remains passive, witnessing the moral decay without intervening. His perspective allows the reader to critically evaluate the characters and their actions.
  • Shift in Register: The narrative shifts from Fitzgerald's sweeping, almost mythic language used to describe the valley of ashes to a more mundane, observational tone during the garage scene. This shift highlights the stark reality of the characters' lives within the symbolic landscape, grounding the abstract despair in concrete human interactions.

Review Questions

  1. How does Fitzgerald use vivid imagery and language to characterize the Valley of Ashes? What is its symbolic significance in the novel, particularly in contrast to West and East Egg?
  2. Discuss the role of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg's eyes. What possible interpretations can be drawn from their description and placement, and how do they contribute to the mood or themes of the section?
  3. Analyze the power dynamics between Tom Buchanan and George Wilson during their interaction at the garage. What do Tom's actions and words reveal about his character and his perception of others, especially those from a lower social class?
  4. Compare and contrast the initial descriptions of George Wilson and Myrtle Wilson. How does Myrtle's "vitality" stand out against her husband and the setting of the Valley of Ashes, and what might it signify about her aspirations?
  5. Consider Nick's role as an observer in this section. How does Tom's orchestration of the visit and Nick's reaction to it contribute to our understanding of the moral landscape and social attitudes depicted in The Great Gatsby?
