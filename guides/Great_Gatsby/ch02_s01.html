Study Guide: Chapter 2, Section 1 - The Valley of Ashes

Overview

Chapter 2 introduces the bleak "valley of ashes," a desolate industrial zone symbolizing moral decay and poverty, watched over by the faded billboard eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg. Here, Nick Carraway is reluctantly drawn into Tom Buchanan's illicit affair with Myrtle Wilson, leading to a raucous and ultimately violent party in their New York apartment that exposes the artificiality of social class and the brutal realities beneath the surface of the wealthy.

Key Concepts

  • The Valley of Ashes as a Symbolic Landscape: This desolate industrial wasteland between West Egg and New York City represents the moral and social decay hidden beneath the glamour of the Jazz Age. It is a place of poverty, forgotten dreams, and the literal refuse of industrialization, contrasting sharply with the opulent "Eggs."
  • The Eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg: A faded billboard featuring giant, spectacled eyes, these eyes "brood" over the valley of ashes, symbolizing an indifferent, all-seeing, yet ultimately powerless deity or a forgotten moral conscience in a materialistic world.
  • Class Performance and Identity: The chapter intensely explores how social class is performed through dress, mannerisms, and possessions. Myrtle Wilson dramatically transforms her personality and behavior when she changes into her cream-colored dress, illustrating how identity can be a "costume" adopted to project a higher social standing.
  • Irony and Artificiality: The apartment party is rife with irony, highlighting the superficiality of Myrtle's aspirations. Details like oversized furniture, a tapestry depicting Versailles, and Myrtle's exaggerated complaints and affected speech expose the hollowness of the class markers she attempts to imitate.
  • Violence as a Revealer of Truth: The chapter culminates in Tom's brutal act of breaking Myrtle's nose. This casual violence strips away the pretensions and artifice of the evening, revealing the underlying brutality of Tom's power, which stems from inherited wealth and physical intimidation, and the precariousness of Myrtle's aspirations.
  • The Motif of Observation and Detachment: Nick Carraway's role as an observer, "within and without, simultaneously enchanted and repelled," is central. This theme extends to the indifferent gaze of Eckleburg's eyes and George Wilson's unwitting ignorance of his wife's affair.

Important Terms

  • Contiguous: Sharing a boundary or edge; touching.
    • Usage: Used to describe the garage's relationship to the surrounding emptiness.
  • Anaemic: Lacking vitality or energy; pale and weak.
    • Usage: Applied to George Wilson's appearance and manner.
  • Hauteur: Disdainful pride; haughty manner.
    • Usage: Describes the transformation in Myrtle's personality when she changes into the cream-colored dress.
  • Proprietary: Relating to ownership; behaving as if one owns something.
    • Usage: Catherine enters the apartment with “proprietary haste.”
  • Ectoplasm: A supernatural substance supposedly emanating from a spiritualist medium; here used to describe the ghostly quality of McKee's enlarged photograph hovering on the wall.
  • Strident: Loud, harsh, and grating.
    • Usage: Describes the arguments that keep pulling Nick back into his chair as the evening wears on.
  • Supercilious: Behaving as though one is superior to others.
    • Usage: Applied to Tom's assumption that Nick has nothing better to do on a Sunday afternoon.
  • Crêpe de Chine: A lightweight, crinkled silk fabric.
    • Usage: Myrtle wears a spotted dress of dark blue crêpe de Chine at the garage.

Detailed Notes

The Valley of Ashes: A Desolate Landscape

  • Chapter 2 introduces the valley of ashes, a bleak, industrial wasteland located between the prosperous West Egg and bustling New York City.
  • It is characterized by desolation, representing the forgotten poor and the moral decay that underpins the era's economic prosperity.
  • Dominating this landscape are the enormous, faded billboard eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg, which seem to watch over the scene like an indifferent, antiquated deity.

Tom Buchanan and Myrtle Wilson's Affair

  • Tom Buchanan takes Nick to the valley of ashes to meet his mistress, Myrtle Wilson, at her husband George's failing garage.
  • George Wilson is portrayed as "anaemic," spiritless, and compliant, unaware of his wife's infidelity.
  • Myrtle Wilson is described as possessing an "intense vitality" and a palpable desire to escape her circumstances.

The Journey to New York and the Apartment Party

  • Tom, Nick, and Myrtle travel into the city. Along the way, Myrtle indulges in buying a dog and other "small luxuries," immediately engaging in consumerism as a means of aspiring to a higher class.
  • They arrive at a small apartment Tom maintains on 158th Street, specifically for the affair.
  • The party guests include Myrtle's sister, Catherine, and the downstairs neighbors, the McKees.
  • The atmosphere becomes increasingly drunken as the afternoon progresses into evening.

Myrtle's Transformation and Performance

  • Myrtle undergoes a dramatic transformation:
    • She changes from her modest "spotted dress of dark blue crêpe de Chine" into an elaborate "cream-colored chiffon."
    • Her personality shifts from "intense vitality" to "impressive hauteur," adopting affected mannerisms and a "high, mincing shout."
    • She "holds court" over the cramped rooms, attempting to project an image of upper-class gentility.
  • Fitzgerald uses this transformation to illustrate the idea of identity as a costume, which can be put on and taken off, suggesting the superficiality of social roles.

Self-Deception and Irony at the Party

  • The party reveals layers of self-deception:
    • Catherine falsely reports that Daisy is Catholic, preventing Tom from divorcing her, a lie Myrtle repeats.
    • Myrtle recounts her marriage to George Wilson, expressing disappointment and implying she married beneath her, fabricating a narrative of superiority.
  • The apartment scene is rich with irony:
    • The furniture is "entirely too large" for the small rooms, creating an illusion of grandeur that clashes with reality.
    • A tapestry depicting ladies in the gardens of Versailles hangs inappropriately in the modest setting.
    • Myrtle complains about the quality of service as if she were accustomed to a household staff, and dismisses her expensive dress as "just a crazy old thing," highlighting her artificiality.
    • Her story of hiring a woman to examine her feet is presented as a routine luxury, exposing the hollowness of her imitated class markers.

Class, Power, and Violence

  • The chapter functions as a study in class performance and the violence underlying social hierarchy.
  • Juxtapositions abound: the desolation of the valley of ashes vs. the wealth of the Eggs; George Wilson's spiritless compliance vs. Tom's aggressive authority; Myrtle at the garage vs. her "performance" in the apartment.
  • Tom's world is also one of performance, where his authority derives not from merit but from inherited wealth and physical intimidation.
  • The motif of watching pervades the chapter:
    • The eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg are a constant, brooding presence.
    • Tom and Myrtle conduct their affair under the unsuspecting gaze of George Wilson.
    • Nick consistently positions himself as an observer, "within and without, simultaneously enchanted and repelled."
  • The climax of the chapter is Tom's casual, brutal act of breaking Myrtle's nose with an open hand after she repeatedly invokes Daisy's name.
  • This violent act is not just for shock; it serves as a moment of clarity, stripping away the evening's pretensions. It reveals the reality beneath the performance: Tom's power is ultimately physical and absolute, and Myrtle's aspirations exist entirely at his sufferance.
  • Nick departs in an alcohol-induced haze, eventually falling asleep at Pennsylvania Station, reflecting the disorienting and disillusioning nature of the evening.

Review Questions

  1. How does Fitzgerald use the "valley of ashes" as a symbolic setting, and what specific aspects of the Jazz Age society does it represent in contrast to West Egg and New York City?
  2. Analyze Myrtle Wilson's transformation in the apartment scene. What does her change in dress and demeanor reveal about her aspirations and the nature of identity in this social context?
  3. Discuss the significance of the "eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg" in this chapter. What do they symbolize, and how do they relate to the various acts of watching and being watched throughout the section?
  4. Examine the role of irony in the description of Myrtle's apartment and her behavior during the party. How does Fitzgerald use these ironic details to critique the pursuit of social status?
  5. What does Tom Buchanan's violent act against Myrtle reveal about his character, his perception of women, and the underlying power dynamics of the era? How does this moment expose the "brutality that the novel's glittering surfaces work so hard to conceal"?
