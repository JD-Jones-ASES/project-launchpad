Study Guide: Chapter 4: Literary Analysis

Key Concepts

  • Identity in the novel is fundamentally performative — characters construct public selves that conceal private realities
  • Gatsby's autobiography follows the conventions of the self-made American hero, yet fails to convince precisely because it is too polished
  • Physical objects carry more persuasive power than narrative coherence in a culture obsessed with surfaces
  • Meyer Wolfshiem parallels Gatsby — both perform respectability while concealing criminal activity
  • Jordan Baker's account of Gatsby's Louisville romance provides the emotional core that Gatsby's own autobiography strategically omits
  • Three competing narrative layers structure the chapter: Gatsby's autobiography, Wolfshiem's reminiscences, and Jordan's backstory

Summary

Chapter 4 is organized around the theme of constructed identity. Gatsby's autobiography — wealthy family, Oxford education, wartime valor, European wandering — follows the conventions of the classic American self-made hero so precisely that it rings false. Nick compares the effect to a stuffed figure "leaking sawdust," a metaphor that exposes Gatsby's self-presentation as artificial construction rather than lived experience. When Gatsby produces material evidence — the medal, the photograph — Nick's resistance collapses. Fitzgerald illustrates a core theme: in a world obsessed with surfaces and possessions, physical objects carry more persuasive authority than narrative coherence.

The lunch scene with Wolfshiem adds a second layer of performed respectability over criminal reality. Wolfshiem's sentimental nostalgia for the murder of Rosy Rosenthal, his human-molar cuff buttons, and his attempt to recruit Nick as a "business gonnegtion" reveal that his cultivated manner is a performance identical in structure to Gatsby's. The revelation that Wolfshiem fixed the 1919 World Series forces the reader to reconsider everything Gatsby has said — if his wealth comes from criminal enterprise, his polished autobiography is not merely exaggerated but deliberately fabricated.

Jordan's narration in the chapter's second half provides a third perspective: the story of Gatsby and Daisy in Louisville. Filtered through memory and retold secondhand, this account supplies the emotional motive that Gatsby's autobiography concealed. Daisy's drunken attempt to return Tom's pearls on the wedding eve — her single moment of authentic self-expression — is then smothered by decorum. The chapter concludes that even Daisy performs the role expected of her. Identity throughout Chapter 4 is shown to be a role adopted in service of social ambition, romantic obsession, or survival.

Key Terms

  • performative identity: The idea that selfhood is constructed through outward acts and performances rather than being an authentic inner essence
  • sawdust: Nick's metaphor for Gatsby's autobiography — a conventionally stuffed figure that leaks its artificial filling under pressure
  • Orderi di Danilo: The Montenegrin military medal Gatsby produces as material proof of his wartime heroism
  • gonnegtion: Wolfshiem's pronunciation of "connection," referring to a criminal business partnership he attempts to offer Nick
  • somnambulatory abstraction: The trancelike, watchful distraction Wolfshiem lapses into in public — a behavioral sign of a man accustomed to surveillance

Review Questions

  1. Why does Nick describe Gatsby's autobiography as a figure "leaking sawdust"? What does this metaphor reveal about the nature of Gatsby's self-presentation?
  2. In what ways does Meyer Wolfshiem function as a parallel or double to Gatsby in this chapter?
  3. How does Fitzgerald use physical objects — the medal, the photograph, the molar cuff buttons — to comment on the relationship between material evidence and truth?
  4. Jordan Baker's narrative is filtered through memory and retold secondhand to Nick. How does this layered narration affect the reader's confidence in the story of Gatsby and Daisy?
  5. The chapter presents three distinct narrators within Nick's frame: Gatsby, Wolfshiem, and Jordan. How do their accounts of the past differ in purpose and reliability?
