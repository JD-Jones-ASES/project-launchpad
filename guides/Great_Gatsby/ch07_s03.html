Chapter 7, Section 3: The Hottest Day – Study Guide

Overview

This section marks a significant turning point in the novel, as Gatsby's elaborate parties cease and his clandestine affair with Daisy becomes more overt. The oppressive, sweltering summer heat serves as a constant backdrop, mirroring the escalating tension and impending confrontation between Gatsby, Daisy, and Tom. The narrative culminates in a pivotal luncheon at the Buchanan's, where simmering emotions finally boil over, leading to Daisy's shocking public declaration of love for Gatsby and Tom's stunned realization of the affair.

Key Concepts

  • The End of Gatsby's Trimalchio Persona: Gatsby abruptly stops his lavish parties, signaling a shift from his public pursuit of Daisy through grand displays to a more private, serious stage of their relationship.
  • Discretion and Secrecy: Gatsby dismisses his old servants to prevent gossip, underscoring the delicate and illicit nature of his renewed relationship with Daisy.
  • The Oppressive Heat as a Motif: The sweltering summer weather is a dominant presence, creating an atmosphere of discomfort, lethargy, and rising tension, mirroring the emotional climax unfolding between the characters.
  • The Confrontation at the Buchanan's: The luncheon serves as the setting for an open display of affection between Daisy and Gatsby, forcing Tom to confront the reality of their affair.
  • Daisy's Conflicted Nature: Daisy's actions—her public kiss, her comment about Gatsby looking "cool," and her struggle with the day's confusion—highlight her wavering loyalty and internal conflict between Gatsby and Tom.
  • Gatsby's Naivety Regarding Pammy: Gatsby's surprise at Daisy's daughter, Pammy, reveals his idealized vision of Daisy, which struggles to reconcile with her established life as a wife and mother.
  • "Her Voice Is Full of Money": Nick's profound realization about Daisy's voice encapsulates her ultimate allure and the deep-seated materialism that defines her and her world.
  • Tom's Assertiveness and Possessiveness: Tom's attempts to control the situation, question Gatsby, and demand to drive Gatsby's car demonstrate his fierce possessiveness over Daisy and his desire to reassert dominance.

Important Terms

  • Trimalchio: A character from Roman literature known for his extravagant feasts. Nick's reference to Gatsby's "career as Trimalchio" ending signifies the close of his period of ostentatious entertaining.
  • Caravansary: A large inn or hotel with a courtyard for travelers. Here, it metaphorically refers to Gatsby's bustling, party-filled house.
  • Dilatory: Slow to act or respond; intended to cause delay. Used to describe the new butler's grudging manner.
  • Harrowing: Acutely distressing or painful. Nick uses this to describe the intense scene Gatsby had previously outlined.
  • Shirtwaist: A woman's blouse, typically styled like a man's shirt.
  • Commutation ticket: A ticket for multiple journeys, often for daily travel between home and work.
  • Gin Rickey: A popular alcoholic cocktail, often consumed in hot weather, made with gin, lime juice, and soda water. Symbolizes the era's decadence and the characters' attempts to cope with the heat.
  • "Her voice is full of money": Nick Carraway's insightful observation about Daisy Buchanan, revealing that her enchanting vocal quality is imbued with the allure and security of wealth, signifying her deep connection to the upper class and its material comforts.
  • Dog-days: The hottest, most sultry days of summer, traditionally associated with the rising of the Dog Star, Sirius.

Detailed Notes

The End of the Parties and New Servants

  • Gatsby's Change: Gatsby's famous parties suddenly cease, ending his "career as Trimalchio." Nick observes cars turning into the drive and then sullenly leaving.
  • Nick's Visit: Nick goes to check on Gatsby, encountering an unfamiliar, rude butler who denies him access and claims not to know who "Carraway" is.
  • Gossip Spreads: Nick's Finnish maid confirms that Gatsby dismissed all his servants a week prior, replacing them with a new, suspicious crew described as "brothers and sisters" who used to run a hotel for Wolfsheim. They are explicitly hired to prevent gossip.
  • Daisy's Influence: Gatsby reveals the reason for the change: Daisy "comes over quite often—in the afternoons," and he needs discretion. The elaborate "caravansary" of his parties "fallen in like a card house at the disapproval in her eyes."

The Invitation to Lunch and the Oppressive Heat

  • Daisy's Request: Gatsby calls Nick at Daisy's request, inviting him to lunch at the Buchanan's house the next day. Daisy calls shortly after, sounding relieved that Nick is coming, suggesting "something was up."
  • The Sweltering Journey: The next day is described as "broiling," "certainly the warmest, of the summer." Nick's train journey is marked by intense heat, creating an atmosphere of discomfort and physical strain for everyone. This pervasive heat functions as a symbol of the emotional tension brewing.

Arrival at the Buchanan's

  • Answering the Door: Gatsby and Nick arrive at the Buchanan's house. The butler answers with a theatrical, slightly nonsensical line about being unable to "furnish" "the master's body" because it's "far too hot to touch," before clarifying that he'll "see."
  • Cool Sanctuary: The interior of the house, thanks to awnings, is "dark and cool." Daisy and Jordan are depicted languidly on a couch, like "silver idols," unable to move due to the heat.
  • Tom's Call: Tom is on the phone in the hall, his voice gruff and annoyed. Jordan whispers to Nick that it's "Tom's girl on the telephone," confirming his ongoing affair. Tom loudly concludes his call, asserting his independence.

Escalating Tensions and Daisy's Affection

  • Tom's Entrance: Tom enters, shaking Gatsby's hand with "well-concealed dislike," a clear sign of his underlying hostility despite his superficial politeness.
  • Daisy's Boldness: Daisy immediately asks for a cold drink. As Tom leaves the room, she gets up, pulls Gatsby's face down, and kisses him passionately on the mouth, murmuring, "You know I love you."
  • Jordan's Awareness: Jordan acknowledges the impropriety ("You forget there's a lady present") but Daisy dismisses it, even playfully suggesting Jordan kiss Nick. Daisy then "clogs" on the fireplace, a fleeting act of rebellion, before remembering the heat.

The Appearance of Pammy

  • A Child's Entrance: A nurse brings in Daisy and Tom's daughter, Pammy, described as a "little girl." Daisy lavishes affection on her, calling her "Bles-sed pre-cious" and showing her off.
  • Gatsby's Surprise: Gatsby takes Pammy's hand, but he gazes at her with "surprise." Nick notes, "I don't think he had ever really believed in its existence before." This highlights Gatsby's idealized and somewhat delusional vision of Daisy, which doesn't account for her life as a mother.
  • Pammy's Departure: Pammy is quickly taken away by the nurse as Tom returns with "four gin rickeys that clicked full of ice." The brevity of Pammy's appearance emphasizes her role as a symbol of Daisy's established life rather than a developed character.

Tom's Provocation and Daisy's Declaration

  • Drinks and Small Talk: The group drinks, Gatsby visibly tense. Tom attempts to engage in trivial conversation, mistakenly recalling facts about the sun, a sign of his general thoughtlessness.
  • Tom's Tour: Tom invites Gatsby to "have a look at the place," asserting his ownership and trying to draw Gatsby out onto his property. Gatsby points across the bay to his own house, asserting his proximity.
  • Luncheon and Discontent: They have lunch in the darkened dining room. Daisy expresses her ennui and confusion ("What'll we do with ourselves this afternoon? . . . and the next thirty years?" "everything's so confused").
  • Going to Town: Daisy impulsively suggests, "Let's all go to town!"
  • The Crucial Moment: Daisy stares at Gatsby and says, "You always look so cool." Tom, astounded, understands in that moment that Daisy has told Gatsby she loves him. His "mouth opened a little," and he looks between them "as if he had just recognized her as some one he knew a long time ago." Daisy then awkwardly references "the advertisement of the man."
  • Tom's Reaction: Tom quickly agrees to go to town, his eyes "still flashing between Gatsby and his wife," his temper cracking as he insists they leave.

Nick's Epiphany and the Car Swap

  • Gatsby's Constraint: While waiting for Daisy and Jordan to get ready, Gatsby tells Nick, "I can't say anything in his house, old sport."
  • Daisy's Voice: Nick remarks on Daisy's "indiscreet voice," hesitating before Gatsby finishes his thought: "Her voice is full of money."
  • Nick's Realization: This is a profound moment for Nick. He finally understands the "inexhaustible charm" of Daisy's voice, associating it with wealth, luxury, and the "golden girl" of a "white palace." This links Daisy's allure directly to her class and financial status.
  • The Car Arrangement: Gatsby suggests they all go in his car. Tom immediately demands to drive Gatsby's yellow car to town, offering his coupe in return. Gatsby reluctantly objects, mentioning low gas, but Tom insists, asserting his dominance over Gatsby's possessions.

Review Questions

  1. Why does Gatsby dismiss his original staff and hire new ones, described as Wolfsheim's people? What does this change reveal about Gatsby's priorities and the nature of his relationship with Daisy?
  2. How does the pervasive heat contribute to the atmosphere and rising tension throughout this section? Provide specific examples of how the heat affects characters and events.
  3. Describe the significance of Pammy Buchanan's brief appearance at the luncheon, particularly Gatsby's reaction to her. What does this reveal about Gatsby's perception of Daisy and her life?
  4. Explain Nick Carraway's profound realization that Daisy's "voice is full of money." How does this observation characterize Daisy and her ultimate appeal within the novel's social context?
  5. Analyze Tom Buchanan's behavior during the luncheon, from his phone call to his interaction with Gatsby and Daisy. What strategies does he employ to assert his dominance, and how does he react to the growing evidence of the affair?
