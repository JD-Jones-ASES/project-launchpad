Reading a Modern Novel: The Great Gatsby — Chapter 4, Section 3

Study Guide: Gatsby's Guests and Gatsby's Story

Overview

This section opens by detailing the diverse and often disreputable crowd that frequents Gatsby's extravagant parties, highlighting the superficiality and anonymity of his guests. It then shifts to a pivotal moment where Gatsby takes Nick Carraway into town and, prompted by Nick's curiosity and his own desire to set the record straight, reveals an elaborate, yet partially fabricated, account of his past, including claims of wealth, European travel, and heroic military service. The section concludes with the revelation that Gatsby has a specific, undisclosed request for Nick involving Jordan Baker.

Key Concepts

  • The Nature of Gatsby's Parties: Gatsby's parties are shown to be grand, chaotic, and filled with people who know little about their host. The guests are largely superficial, focused on enjoying the spectacle and Gatsby's resources, rather than forming genuine connections.
  • Anonymity and Rumors: Despite the multitude of attendees, very few truly know Gatsby. His mysterious background fuels wild rumors about his wealth, profession, and past, reflecting the gossip-driven culture of the Jazz Age.
  • Gatsby's Fabricated Persona: Gatsby presents a carefully constructed life story to Nick, replete with claims of old money, Oxford education, exotic travels, and war heroism. This narrative is a blend of truth and elaborate fiction designed to create an impressive, yet ultimately hollow, image.
  • Nick's Shifting Perception of Gatsby: Initially, Nick finds Gatsby rather uninteresting in conversation, seeing him as merely a wealthy neighbor. However, as Gatsby shares his "story" and provides physical proof (the medal and photograph), Nick's skepticism battles with a growing sense of wonder and even belief, though inconsistencies like "San Francisco in the Middle West" remain.
  • The Significance of Gatsby's Car: Gatsby's cream-colored car is described as monstrously luxurious and ostentatious, a physical embodiment of his immense wealth and a reflection of the era's materialism and excess.
  • Gatsby's Hidden Agenda: The elaborate storytelling and display of "proof" are ultimately revealed to be a calculated precursor to a "big request" Gatsby needs Nick to facilitate. This highlights Gatsby's manipulative tendencies and the underlying purpose behind his interactions.

Important Terms

  • Bootlegger: A person who makes, distributes, or sells alcoholic beverages illegally. This term is used in rumors about Gatsby, suggesting his wealth comes from illicit activities during Prohibition.
  • Knickerbockers: Loose-fitting breeches gathered at the knee. Clarence Endive is noted for wearing them, connecting him to a particular fashion trend or social class.
  • Rajah: An Indian king or prince; a term Gatsby uses to describe his opulent lifestyle in Europe, evoking images of exotic luxury.
  • Montenegro: A small country in southeastern Europe. Gatsby presents a war medal from Montenegro as proof of his decorated military service, selecting an obscure nation to lend a sense of exotic authenticity that is difficult to verify.
  • Argonne Forest: A significant battleground in northeastern France during World War I. Gatsby claims to have performed extraordinary feats of bravery there, cementing his image as a war hero.
  • Trinity Quad: A quadrangle at Trinity College, Oxford University. Gatsby's photograph from "Trinity Quad" is offered as evidence of his Oxford education, an important part of his fabricated background.
  • East Egg/West Egg: The two distinct wealthy communities on Long Island where the novel is set. East Egg represents old money and established aristocracy, while West Egg symbolizes new money and flashy extravagance. The guest list illustrates people coming from both, though Gatsby himself resides in West Egg.
  • "Old sport": Gatsby's distinctive catchphrase, an affected term of address he frequently uses when speaking to Nick and others, adding to his carefully constructed persona.

Detailed Notes

I. The Spectacle of Gatsby's Parties

  • Sunday Morning Scene: The section opens with a vivid image of the "world and its mistress" returning to Gatsby's house after a wild Saturday night, symbolizing the continuation of hedonism and revelry.
  • Whispers and Rumors: Young ladies at the party spread scandalous rumors about Gatsby, calling him a "bootlegger" and even a "man who had killed a man." These rumors underscore Gatsby's mysterious reputation and the lack of concrete knowledge about him.
  • Nick's Guest List: Nick documents the names of those who attended Gatsby's parties in the summer of 1922 on an old timetable. This list serves to:
    • Emphasize Quantity over Quality: The sheer number of names highlights the immense scale of Gatsby's hospitality.
    • Illustrate Social Anonymity: Despite their attendance, guests "paid him the subtle tribute of knowing nothing whatever about him."
    • Showcases the "New Rich" and Social Climbers: Many guests are connected to industries like movies or finance, representing the dynamic and often morally ambiguous landscape of the Jazz Age.
    • Reveals Disreputable Characters: Nick notes figures like Ripley Snell, who went to penitentiary, and G. Earl Muldoon, who strangled his wife, indicating the low moral standards of some attendees.
    • Highlights Fleeting Relationships: Characters like Klipspringer, "the boarder," are practically squatters, demonstrating the parasitic nature of some guests.
    • Presents Eccentricities: The "Blackbucks" who flip their noses, Edgar Beaver whose hair turned white, and Benny McClenahan with his ever-changing "identical" girls add to the colorful, almost grotesque, tapestry of Gatsby's social circle.

II. Gatsby's Car and His Restless Demeanor

  • Gatsby's First Visit to Nick: One July morning, Gatsby calls on Nick directly for the first time, using his "gorgeous car" to invite Nick to lunch.
  • The Car's Description: Gatsby's car is a "rich cream color, bright with nickel," of "monstrous length," and "terraced with a labyrinth of wind-shields." It is an embodiment of excessive wealth and ostentation, mirroring Gatsby's own public persona.
  • Gatsby's Restlessness: Gatsby's movements are described as "peculiarly American," marked by "resourcefulness" and "formless grace," but also a "restlessness" and impatience that breaks through his "punctilious manner." This hints at an inner turmoil or a constant drive beneath his polished exterior.
  • Nick's Initial Disappointment: Despite Gatsby's opulence, Nick has found him to have "little to say" in previous interactions, leading Nick to initially see him as merely "the proprietor of an elaborate road-house next door."

III. Gatsby's Fabricated Life Story

  • The Disconcerting Ride: During their drive, Gatsby unexpectedly asks Nick's "opinion of me, anyhow?" and announces he wants to tell Nick "God's truth" to counteract the "wrong idea... from all these stories."
  • Claims of Wealth and Education:
    • Gatsby states he is the "son of some wealthy people in the Middle West—all dead now."
    • He claims to have been "educated at Oxford" as a "family tradition."
    • Nick immediately notices Gatsby's hesitation on "Oxford," sparking suspicion.
    • A glaring inconsistency arises when Gatsby claims "San Francisco" is in the "Middle West," further deepening Nick's doubt.
    • He claims to have inherited a "good deal of money" after his family's sudden death.
  • The "Young Rajah" Lifestyle:
    • Gatsby describes living "like a young rajah in all the capitals of Europe—Paris, Venice, Rome."
    • His activities included "collecting jewels, chiefly rubies, hunting big game, painting a little," all while "trying to forget something very sad."
    • Nick finds these claims clichéd and unbelievable, evoking images of a "turbaned 'character.'"
  • War Heroism:
    • Gatsby credits the war as a "great relief," where he "tried very hard to die" but seemed to bear an "enchanted life."
    • He recounts a heroic tale as a first lieutenant in the Argonne Forest, leading two machine-gun detachments far forward and holding their ground against German divisions.
    • He claims promotion to major and decorations from "every Allied government—even Montenegro."
  • Providing "Proof":
    • The Montenegro Medal: Gatsby produces a medal, the "Orderi di Danilo," from "Montenegro, Nicolas Rex," inscribed with "Major Jay Gatsby, For Valour Extraordinary." Its authentic appearance momentarily sways Nick's incredulity.
    • The Oxford Photograph: Gatsby then shows a photograph from "Trinity Quad" at Oxford, depicting him among young men in blazers, one of whom he identifies as "the Earl of Dorcaster." This photo reinforces the Oxford claim.
  • Nick's Shift to Belief: With the physical evidence, Nick's "incredulity was submerged in fascination." He briefly imagines the exotic life Gatsby described, reflecting how Gatsby's calculated presentation can be convincing.

IV. Gatsby's True Purpose for Nick

  • The "Big Request": Gatsby explains that he shared his life story because he's about to make a "big request" and didn't want Nick to think he was "just some nobody."
  • The "Sad Thing": He reiterates his story of drifting to forget "something very sad that had happened."
  • Involving Jordan Baker: Gatsby reveals his request involves Miss Baker, stating he "happened to find out" that Nick is taking her to tea. He clarifies he is not in love with Jordan but that she has "kindly consented to speak to you about this matter."
  • Nick's Annoyance: Nick is "more annoyed than interested," resenting being used in Gatsby's plans and finding the whole situation "utterly fantastic."

Review Questions

  1. How do the descriptions of Gatsby's party guests and their behavior contribute to the overall atmosphere and themes of the novel? What do they reveal about the Jazz Age society Gatsby inhabits?
  2. Analyze Nick's changing perception of Gatsby during their car ride. What specific details or inconsistencies cause Nick to doubt Gatsby's story, and what elements ultimately lead him to a partial belief?
  3. Discuss the significance of the objects Gatsby uses as "proof" for his story (the medal and the photograph). How effective are these items in convincing Nick, and what do they suggest about Gatsby's methods of self-presentation?
  4. Gatsby claims to have lived "like a young rajah" and describes his war heroism. What might be the underlying psychological reasons for Gatsby to craft such an elaborate and partially false personal history?
  5. What is Gatsby's true motivation for sharing his elaborate life story with Nick, and how does this reveal his character and his plans? How does Nick react to this revelation, and what does it foreshadow for their relationship?
