Study Guide: Chapter 6: Literary Analysis

Key Concepts

  • Gatsby "sprang from his Platonic conception of himself" — a philosophical framework central to understanding his character
  • Platonic idealism: the belief that abstract ideal Forms are more real than the imperfect material world; Gatsby applies this to his own identity
  • Gatsby's cry — "Can't repeat the past? Why of course you can!" — is not naivety but the logical conclusion of his worldview
  • Tom Buchanan functions as a foil to Gatsby: old money vs. new, inherited status vs. self-made myth
  • Daisy's discomfort at the party signals that social reality is already pushing back against Gatsby's idealized vision
  • The chapter reveals the fatal flaw in Gatsby's grand conception: it depends on others — especially Daisy — accepting his version of reality
  • Fitzgerald uses the kiss scene to show the moment Gatsby "wed his unutterable visions to her perishable breath," making Daisy the vessel for an idea that transcends any real person

Summary

At the center of Chapter 6 lies one of the novel's most philosophically dense passages: Jay Gatsby "sprang from his Platonic conception of himself." The term Platonic refers not to friendship but to the philosophy of Plato, who argued that ideal Forms — perfect, unchanging abstractions — are more real than the imperfect material world we inhabit. Gatsby has created an ideal Form of himself, the perfect "Jay Gatsby," and has spent his life forcing the material world to conform to it. His parents, his poverty, his birth name — these belong to the degraded world of appearances and can be discarded. The name James Gatz is not merely changed; it is philosophically annihilated.

This framework gives Gatsby's demand of Daisy its internal logic. When he insists that Daisy tell Tom she never loved him and return with Gatsby to Louisville to live the past again, he is not being simply delusional. He is asserting that the ideal moment of their first kiss — when "the incarnation was complete" and Daisy "blossomed for him like a flower" — is the true reality, and the intervening five years of her marriage and changed life are the illusion. His incredulous "Why of course you can!" in response to Nick's warning is the voice of a man who genuinely believes ideal Forms override contingent facts.

Fitzgerald uses Tom Buchanan as a foil to expose the fatal weakness in this worldview. Tom is old money, inherited status, and physical dominance — the brute force of social reality. His contemptuous "Who is this Gatsby anyhow?" and Daisy's discomfort at the party demonstrate that the real world is not obligated to accept Gatsby's version of it. The tragedy Fitzgerald is constructing is not that Gatsby is wrong to dream but that he has confused an ideal with a person, and Daisy — real, imperfect, married, frightened — cannot be made to carry the weight of his five-year vision.

Key Terms

  • Platonic conception: Derived from Plato's philosophy of ideal Forms; Gatsby's "platonic conception of himself" is a perfect, abstract self-image to which he tries to make reality conform
  • incarnation: The embodiment of an idea or quality in physical form; when Gatsby kisses Daisy, the "incarnation was complete" — his ideal found its material vessel
  • foil: A character whose contrasting qualities highlight the traits of another; Tom Buchanan's inherited wealth, social certainty, and contempt for Gatsby's new-money world sharpen the reader's understanding of Gatsby's position
  • ineffable: Too great or extreme to be expressed in words; used to describe the "universe of ineffable gaudiness" spinning in young Gatz's imagination
  • appalling: Causing shock or dismay; also used in the sense of diminishing — Fitzgerald uses it to describe both Daisy's reaction to West Egg and the "appalling sentimentality" in Gatsby's account of the past
  • meretricious: Apparently attractive but having in reality no value or integrity; applied to the "vast, vulgar, and meretricious beauty" that Gatsby has devoted himself to serving

Review Questions

  1. What does it mean that Gatsby "sprang from his Platonic conception of himself"? How does this philosophical framework explain his behavior throughout the novel?
  2. Why is Gatsby's insistence that the past can be repeated not simply foolish, but the logical conclusion of his worldview?
  3. In what specific ways does Tom Buchanan function as a foil to Gatsby in this chapter? What does the contrast between them reveal?
  4. Daisy is "appalled" by West Egg and Gatsby's party. Why does her reaction represent a serious threat to Gatsby's plans?
  5. Nick describes the moment of Gatsby's first kiss with Daisy as the moment he "wed his unutterable visions to her perishable breath." What does this phrase suggest about the nature of Gatsby's love for Daisy?
