Chapter 7, Section 2: Rising Tensions on the Hottest Day

Overview

This section highlights a critical turning point in the novel, as Gatsby abandons his public persona to protect his burgeoning private affair with Daisy. Against a backdrop of oppressive summer heat, a tense lunch culminates in Tom's undeniable realization of Daisy's affection for Gatsby, prompting a fateful drive to New York. Along the way, a stop at Wilson's garage reveals a parallel discovery of infidelity, intensifying the already suffocating emotional atmosphere.

Key Concepts

  • Prioritizing Love over Performance: Gatsby's decision to dismiss his old servants and halt his extravagant parties signifies a profound shift. With Daisy now directly present in his life, the elaborate public displays designed to attract her are no longer necessary, revealing his ultimate focus on her.
  • Symbolism of Oppressive Heat: The extreme summer heat is more than mere setting; it functions as an atmospheric pressure cooker. It physically mirrors and amplifies the escalating emotional tension, suspicion, and unspoken conflicts building among Gatsby, Daisy, and Tom, foreshadowing an inevitable explosion.
  • The Reality of Pammy: Daisy's daughter serves as an undeniable, concrete embodiment of her past marriage and life with Tom. Her brief appearance shatters Gatsby's idealized fantasy of erasing the last five years and forces him to confront the unalterable realities of Daisy's existence.
  • Coded Declarations and Shattered Complacency: Daisy's seemingly innocuous remark to Gatsby about him "always look[ing] so cool" operates as a public, if subtle, declaration of her admiration and attraction. This coded message unequivocally alerts Tom to the affair, profoundly shocking him and forcing him to act.
  • Parallel Discoveries of Infidelity: Both Tom Buchanan and George Wilson discover (or confirm) that their respective partners are involved in secret affairs. This parallelism underscores themes of male possessiveness, betrayal, and the fragility of control over others, regardless of social standing.
  • "Voice Full of Money" Metaphor: Nick's sudden realization that Daisy's "voice is full of money" identifies her intrinsic connection to wealth and old money. This metaphor provides indirect characterization, suggesting that her charm, allure, and even her emotional resonance are inseparable from her vast material security.
  • Car-Swapping and Shifting Power Dynamics: The deliberate arrangement of characters in different cars—Tom driving Gatsby's car, and Gatsby driving Tom's—symbolizes the shifting power struggles, emotional alliances, and Tom's desperate need to assert control and dominance over Gatsby.

Important Terms

  • Indirect Characterization: The revelation of a character's personality through their actions, speech, thoughts, or what other characters say about them, rather than through direct authorial description. (e.g., Daisy's "voice full of money").
  • Symbolism: The use of objects, people, or ideas to represent something else, often a deeper meaning or abstract concept. (e.g., the oppressive heat symbolizing emotional tension).
  • Metaphor: A figure of speech that directly compares two unlike things without using "like" or "as," stating one thing is another. (e.g., Daisy's voice is full of money).
  • Parallelism: The use of similar or identical grammatical structures or thematic situations to show a connection or comparison between different elements. (e.g., Tom's and Wilson's discoveries of infidelity).
  • Power Dynamics: The interplay of power and influence among individuals or groups within a relationship or social setting. (e.g., how the car arrangements reflect who is trying to assert control).
  • Complacency: A feeling of uncritical satisfaction with oneself or one's achievements, often coupled with a lack of awareness of potential dangers or problems. (e.g., Tom's initial unawareness of Daisy's affair).
  • Fantasy vs. Reality: A central thematic conflict in the novel, particularly evident in Gatsby's idealized vision of Daisy colliding with the actualities of her life.

Detailed Notes

1. Gatsby's Shift in Priorities and the End of His Parties:

  • Protecting the Secret: Gatsby has dismissed all his original servants and replaced them with associates connected to Meyer Wolfsheim, who "wouldn't gossip." This radical change is directly aimed at protecting the secret of Daisy's regular afternoon visits to his mansion.
  • Abandoning the Performance: The lavish, public parties that Gatsby once hosted were primarily a means to attract Daisy's attention and showcase his wealth. Now that Daisy is coming to him directly, the "performance" is over. This change signifies that Gatsby has abandoned his earlier strategy; his ultimate goal of winning Daisy's love has become a tangible, private reality, rendering his elaborate public displays obsolete.

2. The Oppressive Heat as a Character and Catalyst:

  • Sensory Details: Fitzgerald devotes unusual attention to the oppressive heat, using specific details such as the train's straw seats "hover[ing] on the edge of combustion," a woman's newspaper dampening under her fingers, the conductor's repetitive chatter about the heat, and Nick's commutation ticket returning with "a dark stain" from the conductor's sweating hand.
  • Function as Symbolism: The stifling, inescapable heat functions as more than just setting. It acts as an atmospheric pressure cooker, mirroring and amplifying the emotional tension building among the characters. Just as the air is suffocating, so are the unspoken conflicts—Tom's suspicion, Gatsby's anticipation, and Daisy's anxiety—that are poised to explode in the impending confrontation.

3. Pammy's Impact: A Confrontation with Reality:

  • Gatsby's Reaction: When Daisy's young daughter, Pammy, is brought into the room, Gatsby reacts with genuine "surprise." Nick observes, "I don't think he had ever really believed in its existence before."
  • Significance: Pammy is concrete, living proof of Daisy's married life with Tom. Her existence directly contradicts Gatsby's fantasy of being able to erase the past five years and reclaim Daisy as she was before. The child's presence momentarily forces Gatsby to confront an undeniable reality that his idealized dream of Daisy cannot accommodate or undo.

4. Daisy's Coded Declaration and Tom's Awakening:

  • The "Cool" Remark: Daisy tells Gatsby that he "always look[s] so cool," a seemingly innocent comment made in a room sweltering with heat and unspoken tension. However, this remark functions as a public, if coded, declaration of her admiration and attraction to Gatsby.
  • Tom's Realization: Nick observes that with this comment, "she had told him that she loved him, and Tom Buchanan saw." Tom is "astounded," looking from Gatsby to Daisy "as if he had just recognized her as some one he knew a long time ago." This moment shatters Tom's complacency, confirming his suspicions and triggering his decision to force a confrontation in New York.

5. George Wilson's Parallel Discovery of Infidelity:

  • Wilson's Situation: At Wilson's garage, George Wilson reveals to Tom that he has discovered Myrtle has "some sort of life apart from him in another world." He has learned she is having an affair, although he doesn't know the identity of her lover, and he plans to take Myrtle away, saying he "got wised up to something funny."
  • The Parallel: Nick explicitly draws a direct parallel between Wilson's situation and Tom's: both men have just discovered (or are in the process of discovering) that the women they regard as their possessions are involved with other men. This highlights a shared theme of male possessiveness and the fragility of control over their partners, regardless of their social standing.

6. Nick's Insight: "Daisy's Voice is Full of Money":

  • The Metaphor's Meaning: Nick's sudden articulation of this long-sensed truth reveals the intrinsic connection between Daisy's allure and her inherited wealth. Her voice doesn't just sound pleasant; it resonates with the security, privilege, and alluring promise of old money, embodying the lifestyle it represents.
  • Indirect Characterization: This metaphor offers profound indirect characterization of Daisy. It suggests that her charm, beauty, and even her emotional resonance are inextricably linked to her vast material prosperity. She is the "golden girl," where desire, social status, and wealth are deeply intertwined.
  • Gatsby's Attraction: For Gatsby, who tirelessly pursues wealth as a means to an end, Daisy's "moneyed" voice symbolizes the ultimate prize. She embodies the pinnacle of the American Dream and the aristocratic world he desperately desires to enter and conquer, making his attraction both romantic and deeply aspirational.

7. Car-Swapping and Shifting Power Dynamics:

  • The Arrangement: The drive toward New York arranges the characters in telling combinations: Tom drives Gatsby's opulent yellow car with Nick and Jordan, while Daisy rides with Gatsby in Tom's more conservative coupé.
  • Dramatizing Power: Fitzgerald uses this car-swapping episode to dramatize the shifting power dynamics among the characters. Tom's insistence on driving Gatsby's car suggests his desperate need for control, his desire to symbolically appropriate Gatsby's possessions, and perhaps to assert dominance over Gatsby's identity in front of his wife.
  • Shifting Alliances: This physical separation of the lovers (Gatsby and Daisy) from the observers (Nick, Jordan, and an increasingly agitated Tom) underscores the emotional distances and alliances. It sets the stage for the direct confrontation, with Tom physically and symbolically separating himself from the intimate pairing he suspects.

Review Questions

  1. How does Gatsby's decision to dismiss his old servants and halt his lavish parties signify a profound change in his character and priorities now that Daisy is directly involved in his life? What does this reveal about his past motivations?
  2. Analyze how Fitzgerald uses the oppressive heat on this particular day. Beyond mere setting, what emotional or thematic purposes does the extreme weather serve in accelerating the narrative's tension?
  3. Discuss the significance of Pammy's brief appearance at the lunch. How does Gatsby's reaction to Daisy's daughter challenge his romanticized vision of the past and Daisy herself?
  4. Examine how Daisy's seemingly innocuous comment about Gatsby "always look[ing] so cool" functions as a critical turning point in the section. What does it reveal about her feelings, and what are its immediate repercussions for Tom?
  5. Compare and contrast George Wilson's discovery about Myrtle with Tom's realization about Daisy. What does this parallelism suggest about the nature of relationships, control, and male possessiveness in the novel?
