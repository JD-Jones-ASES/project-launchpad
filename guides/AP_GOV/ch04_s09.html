Study Guide: Topic 4.9 — Ideology and Economic Policy

Key Concepts

Supply-Side Economics Supply-side economics (also called "trickle-down economics") holds that economic growth is best achieved by reducing taxes on businesses and high-income earners, cutting regulation, and encouraging investment. The theory argues that when businesses have more capital, they expand production, hire workers, and create wealth that benefits everyone. Supply-side economics is most closely associated with conservative and Republican policy, particularly the Reagan-era tax cuts of 1981.

Keynesian Economics (Demand-Side) Keynesian economics holds that economic growth is driven primarily by consumer demand. When people have money to spend, businesses produce more to meet that demand. Keynesian theory supports government spending during recessions -- stimulus checks, infrastructure projects, unemployment benefits -- to boost demand when the private sector contracts. This approach is more closely associated with liberal and Democratic policy, tracing back to the New Deal.

Progressive Taxation A tax structure that imposes higher rates on higher incomes. The federal income tax is progressive: a person earning $500,000 pays a higher percentage than a person earning $50,000. Liberals favor progressive taxation because it reduces inequality and generates revenue for social programs.

Flat Tax A proposal to apply the same tax rate to all income levels. Advocates (mostly conservatives and libertarians) argue it is simpler, fairer, and less punitive toward success. Critics argue it places a heavier effective burden on low-income earners who must spend a larger share of income on necessities.

Regressive Taxation A tax structure that takes a larger percentage of income from lower earners than from higher earners. Sales taxes are regressive because everyone pays the same rate, but lower-income families spend a larger share of their income on taxable goods. The payroll tax (Social Security and Medicare) is also partially regressive because it applies only up to an income cap.

Economic Regulation Liberals support government regulation of the economy to protect consumers, workers, and the environment. Regulatory agencies (EPA, OSHA, CFPB) enforce standards that markets alone would not produce, correcting market failures like pollution and financial fraud. Conservatives argue regulation imposes costs on businesses, slows growth, and stifles innovation -- and favor deregulation.

Federal Deficit The annual difference between government spending and government revenue. When spending exceeds tax revenue, the government runs a deficit and must borrow. Both parties have contributed to deficits: Republican tax cuts reduce revenue; Democratic spending programs increase outlays.

National Debt The accumulated total of all past deficits (minus any surpluses). The national debt exceeded $34 trillion by 2024. Conservatives argue debt burdens future generations and crowds out private investment; liberals argue deficit spending is appropriate during recessions and that public investment produces economic returns.

Mandatory vs. Discretionary Spending Mandatory spending (Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, other entitlements) is legally required and makes up about 60% of the federal budget. Discretionary spending (defense, education, infrastructure) is set annually through the appropriations process. Because mandatory spending dominates the budget, cutting only discretionary programs cannot balance the budget.

Vocabulary Review

  • Supply-side economics: Economic theory holding that reducing taxes on businesses and wealthy individuals stimulates investment, growth, and job creation; associated with conservative/Republican policy.
  • Keynesian economics: Economic theory holding that consumer demand drives growth; government should spend during recessions to stimulate demand; associated with liberal/Democratic policy.
  • Progressive tax: Tax structure where higher earners pay a higher percentage; the federal income tax is the primary example.
  • Flat tax: Proposal to apply a single rate to all income levels regardless of amount earned.
  • Regressive tax: Tax structure where lower earners pay a higher share of income; sales taxes and the payroll tax (up to the cap) are examples.
  • Deregulation: Reducing or eliminating government rules that constrain private enterprise; a core conservative economic policy goal.
  • Federal deficit: The annual shortfall when government spending exceeds tax revenue.
  • National debt: The accumulated total of all past federal deficits.
  • Mandatory spending: Federal outlays required by existing law (Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid); not subject to annual appropriations cuts.
  • Discretionary spending: Federal outlays set annually through the congressional appropriations process (defense, education, non-defense programs).

Key Takeaways

  • Supply-side economics (Republican/conservative) focuses on cutting taxes and regulation to spur investment; Keynesian economics (Democrat/liberal) focuses on stimulating consumer demand through government spending.
  • The federal income tax is progressive; sales taxes and the payroll tax (up to the cap) are regressive.
  • Liberals favor regulation as a correction for market failures; conservatives favor deregulation and market competition.
  • Both parties have contributed to the national debt: tax cuts reduce revenue; spending programs increase outlays.
  • Mandatory spending (Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid) dominates the federal budget, making budget balancing politically and fiscally difficult.
  • Net interest on the debt is a growing fiscal concern for both parties as the debt grows.
