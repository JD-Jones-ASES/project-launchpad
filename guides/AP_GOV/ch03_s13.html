Study Guide: Topic 3.13 — Affirmative Action

Key Concepts

  • Affirmative action: Policies that take race, ethnicity, sex, or other factors into account to promote equal opportunity and address the effects of past discrimination. Implemented in employment, government contracting, and higher education admissions.

  • Origin: The term was first used by President Kennedy in Executive Order 10925 (1961), directing federal contractors to take "affirmative action" to ensure non-discrimination. President Johnson expanded these policies, arguing in his 1965 Howard University speech that true equality requires more than removing barriers — it requires addressing the accumulated disadvantages of past discrimination.

  • Justifications for affirmative action:

    • Remedial: Compensates for centuries of slavery, segregation, and systemic discrimination that created lasting structural disadvantages.
    • Diversity: Diverse educational and workplace environments produce better outcomes for all — broader perspectives, more creative problem-solving.
    • Representation: Institutions should reflect the demographics of the communities they serve.

  • Arguments against affirmative action:

    • Violates the principle of individual merit and equal treatment.
    • Constitutes "reverse discrimination" against individuals not in the targeted group.
    • May stigmatize the very groups it intends to help by implying they cannot succeed without assistance.
    • Race-neutral alternatives can achieve diversity without racial classifications.

  • Regents of the University of California v. Bakke (1978): Court struck down racial quotas as unconstitutional but held that race could be one factor among many in university admissions to achieve the compelling interest of student body diversity. Established the diversity rationale as a compelling state interest.

  • Grutter v. Bollinger (2003): Upheld the University of Michigan Law School's holistic admissions process as constitutional. Justice O'Connor predicted that affirmative action should no longer be necessary in 25 years. Diversity in higher education is a compelling state interest.

  • Gratz v. Bollinger (2003): Struck down Michigan's undergraduate admissions policy that automatically awarded points based on race as not narrowly tailored — it was too mechanical, too close to a quota.

  • Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard (2023): The Court held that race-conscious admissions programs at Harvard and UNC violated the Equal Protection Clause. Chief Justice Roberts: programs lacked measurable objectives and operated as racial balancing. Effectively ended the use of race as a factor in college admissions nationwide.

  • Current status after SFFA (2023): Universities may no longer consider race in admissions. Applicants may discuss in personal essays how their racial identity has shaped their experiences, but race cannot be a factor in selection. Race-neutral alternatives — socioeconomic preferences, geographic diversity, first-generation status — are being developed.

Vocabulary Review

  • Affirmative action: Policies that consider race, ethnicity, sex, or other characteristics to promote equal opportunity and address historical discrimination.
  • Racial quota: A fixed numerical requirement to admit or hire a specific number of individuals from a racial group; held unconstitutional in Regents of UC v. Bakke (1978).
  • Holistic review: An admissions process that considers the whole applicant — including race as one factor among many — rather than applying mechanical formulas.
  • Compelling state interest: Under strict scrutiny, the government's justification must be essential or critical. Student body diversity has been recognized as a compelling interest (Grutter, 2003) but that framework was overturned by SFFA (2023).
  • Narrowly tailored: Requirement that the law use the least restrictive means necessary to achieve the compelling interest; a holistic process is more narrowly tailored than a quota.
  • Remedial justification: The argument that affirmative action compensates for the accumulated disadvantages created by historical discrimination.
  • Diversity justification: The argument that diverse educational and workplace environments produce better outcomes for all participants.
  • Race-neutral alternatives: Admissions or hiring approaches — such as socioeconomic preferences or geographic diversity considerations — that may produce racial diversity without explicitly considering race.
  • Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard (2023): Supreme Court case ending race-conscious college admissions; held that Harvard's and UNC's programs violated the Equal Protection Clause.

Key Takeaways

  • Affirmative action policies have been debated since the 1960s as a means of addressing the persistent effects of historical discrimination.
  • The Supreme Court's approach evolved from permitting race as one factor among many (Bakke, 1978; Grutter, 2003) to prohibiting race in admissions entirely (SFFA, 2023).
  • The central constitutional question is whether using race to achieve diversity violates the Equal Protection Clause's requirement of equal treatment — a direct tension between two interpretations of equality.
  • After SFFA (2023), universities are developing race-neutral alternatives, though whether these can achieve comparable diversity remains an open empirical and legal question.
  • The affirmative action debate reflects a deeper disagreement about equality: Is equality achieved by treating every individual identically regardless of history? Or does true equality require addressing the structural consequences of past discrimination?

AP Exam Connection

Likely question types: Concept Application (scenario involving a college or workplace affirmative action policy), Argument Essay (arguing for or against affirmative action using constitutional principles and foundational documents), SCOTUS Comparison (comparing Grutter or SFFA to a non-required equal protection case).

Key constitutional question: Does the Equal Protection Clause permit racial classifications designed to benefit historically disadvantaged groups? The answer has shifted from yes (with limits) to effectively no after SFFA (2023).
