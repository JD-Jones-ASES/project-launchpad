Study Guide: Topic 2.5 — Checks on the Presidency

Key Concepts

  • Congressional checks on the president include the power of the purse, Senate confirmation, veto override, impeachment, and the ability to pass legislation limiting executive authority.
  • Judicial checks operate through judicial review of executive orders, regulations, and presidential actions—courts can declare them unconstitutional (e.g., Youngstown v. Sawyer, 1952).
  • The War Powers Resolution (1973) requires the president to notify Congress within 48 hours of deploying troops, and mandates withdrawal within 60 days without congressional authorization. It has been difficult to enforce.
  • The 22nd Amendment (1951) limits presidents to two terms, adopted after FDR's four election victories. It creates "lame duck" second-term dynamics.
  • Impeachment is the most dramatic congressional check. All three presidents impeached by the House (Johnson, Clinton, Trump twice) were acquitted by the Senate.

Vocabulary Review

Term Definition
Power of the purse Congress's constitutional authority to control federal spending through appropriations—no money can be spent without congressional approval
Confirmation power The Senate's authority to approve or reject presidential nominees for cabinet, judiciary, and other positions
Override Congress's power to overturn a presidential veto with a two-thirds vote in both chambers
Impeachment The formal process by which the House charges a federal official with "high Crimes and Misdemeanors" (simple majority); the Senate then conducts a trial (two-thirds to convict)
War Powers Resolution A 1973 law requiring the president to notify Congress within 48 hours of deploying troops and withdraw forces within 60 days without congressional authorization
22nd Amendment Ratified in 1951, limits the president to two terms; adopted after FDR's four consecutive elections
Judicial review The power of courts to declare executive actions (and laws) unconstitutional
Government shutdown The suspension of non-essential federal government operations when Congress and the president cannot agree on appropriations
Lame duck A second-term president who cannot seek reelection and thus has reduced political leverage

Important Details

Congressional Checks on the President

  1. Power of the Purse: Congress controls all spending through appropriations; no executive program can be funded without legislative approval; government shutdowns demonstrate this leverage
  2. Confirmation Power: Senate may reject nominees, delay hearings, or extract policy commitments; the threat of rejection shapes who presidents nominate
  3. Override Power: Two-thirds of both chambers can override a veto; rare because it requires a supermajority, but its possibility constrains the president
  4. Impeachment: House impeaches by simple majority; Senate convicts by two-thirds. Three presidents impeached; none removed:
    • Andrew Johnson (1868): Tenure of Office Act violation; acquitted by one vote
    • Bill Clinton (1998): Perjury and obstruction; acquitted on both counts
    • Donald Trump (2019): Abuse of power, obstruction; acquitted
    • Donald Trump (2021): Incitement of insurrection; acquitted (but 7 Republican senators voted to convict—most bipartisan)
  5. Legislative Power: Congress can pass laws limiting or redirecting executive authority

Judicial Checks

  • Youngstown Sheet & Tube v. Sawyer (1952): Court struck down Truman's seizure of steel mills during Korean War—no constitutional or statutory authority
  • United States v. Nixon (1974): Court ordered Nixon to release White House tapes; executive privilege is not absolute
  • Courts can strike down executive orders and administrative regulations as unconstitutional or exceeding statutory authority

War Powers Resolution Requirements

  1. Consult Congress before introducing forces into hostilities
  2. Notify Congress within 48 hours of deploying troops
  3. Withdraw forces within 60 days (with 30-day extension) without congressional authorization

Why WPR Has Been Difficult to Enforce:

  • Every president since Nixon has questioned its constitutionality
  • Presidents have deployed forces without explicit authorization (Grenada, Kosovo, Libya, Syria)
  • Congress has rarely forced the issue
  • Courts typically decline to resolve disputes between Congress and the president over war powers (political question doctrine)

22nd Amendment Dynamics

  • Adopted 1951 in response to FDR's four election victories (1932–1944)
  • George Washington established the two-term tradition in 1796
  • Second-term "lame duck" effect: allies and opponents anticipate the next election, reducing the president's leverage
  • Counter-effect: freed from reelection concerns, second-term presidents may be bolder

Review Questions

1. Identify three congressional checks on presidential power and explain how each constrains the executive. The power of the purse is perhaps Congress's most fundamental check: no federal money can be spent without congressional appropriation, meaning the president cannot fund any program or military operation without legislative approval. This gives Congress leverage in policy disputes that can lead to government shutdowns. The confirmation power allows the Senate to approve or reject presidential nominees for key positions; the prospect of Senate rejection shapes who the president nominates for cabinet and judicial posts. Impeachment is the most extreme check: the House can charge the president with high crimes and misdemeanors by simple majority, and the Senate can convict and remove by a two-thirds vote—though this has never happened to a president.

2. What are the key requirements of the War Powers Resolution, and why has it been difficult to enforce? The War Powers Resolution of 1973 requires the president to (1) consult Congress before deploying forces into hostilities, (2) notify Congress within 48 hours of a deployment, and (3) withdraw forces within 60 days (with a 30-day extension) unless Congress authorizes the military action. The Resolution has been difficult to enforce for several reasons: every president since Nixon has questioned its constitutionality, arguing it infringes on the Commander in Chief power; courts have generally declined to resolve the dispute as a political question; Congress has rarely been willing to politically confront a president during an active military operation; and presidents have found ways to characterize deployments as not triggering the Resolution (e.g., framing them as something other than "hostilities").

3. How does the 22nd Amendment function as a check on presidential power? The 22nd Amendment limits the president to two terms (or 10 years if completing a predecessor's term). As a structural check, it prevents any president from accumulating the political capital and institutional control that might come with longer tenure—directly addressing fears raised by FDR's unprecedented four terms. The amendment also creates a "lame duck" dynamic in second terms: the president cannot seek reelection, reducing their leverage with Congress, their own party, and foreign governments, who all know a new president is coming. However, some second-term presidents have also leveraged their freedom from reelection pressure to pursue bold or politically risky policies without electoral consequences.

4. Why has no president ever been convicted and removed by the Senate? The two-thirds conviction threshold makes Senate removal extremely difficult. Convicting and removing a president of one's own party is politically costly: senators risk intraparty backlash, loss of political relationships, and electoral consequences in states where the president remains popular. Johnson was acquitted by one vote; Clinton by margins comfortable enough that neither article received even a simple majority; Trump was acquitted twice. The pattern suggests that impeachment functions more as a political and symbolic accountability mechanism than as a reliable tool for removing a president. Removal has occurred only for lower-level federal judges who lacked the political protection of a partisan coalition.

5. Describe the judicial checks demonstrated in Youngstown v. Sawyer and United States v. Nixon. In Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer (1952), the Supreme Court struck down President Truman's seizure of steel mills during the Korean War, holding that neither Article II's Commander in Chief power nor inherent executive authority justified the seizure—only a statute authorizing it would have sufficed. In United States v. Nixon (1974), the Court recognized executive privilege as a constitutional principle but held it is not absolute when balanced against the judiciary's need for evidence in a criminal proceeding. Both cases established that the president operates within constitutional and legal limits that courts will enforce—the executive branch is not above judicial oversight.

Summary

The Constitution's system of checks and balances provides multiple mechanisms to constrain presidential power. Congressional tools include the power of the purse, confirmation, veto override, impeachment, and legislation. The judiciary exercises judicial review over executive actions. The War Powers Resolution represents Congress's statutory effort to reclaim war-making authority, though enforcement remains problematic. The 22nd Amendment imposes a structural two-term limit. While no president has ever been convicted and removed by the Senate, the pattern of checks—individually imperfect but collectively significant—reflects the Framers' concern about concentrations of executive power.
