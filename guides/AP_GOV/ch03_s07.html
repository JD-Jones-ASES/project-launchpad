Study Guide: Topic 3.7 — Selective Incorporation

Key Concepts

  • Selective incorporation is the doctrine by which the Supreme Court applies individual Bill of Rights provisions to state governments through the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause on a case-by-case basis.
  • The constitutional test asks whether a right is "fundamental to the American scheme of ordered liberty" or "deeply rooted in this Nation's history and tradition." If so, it is incorporated.
  • Incorporation began in the early twentieth century and has continued into the modern era (e.g., Timbs v. Indiana, 2019), demonstrating that the process is ongoing.
  • Three provisions remain largely unincorporated: the Third Amendment, the Fifth Amendment grand jury requirement, and the Seventh Amendment civil jury guarantee.
  • The Fourteenth Amendment's ratification in 1868 was the transformative constitutional development that created the legal foundation for incorporation.

Vocabulary Review

  • Selective incorporation: The Supreme Court doctrine that applies individual Bill of Rights provisions to state governments through the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause when those rights are found to be fundamental.
  • "Implicit in the concept of ordered liberty": One of the formulations used by the Supreme Court to determine whether a right is fundamental enough to warrant incorporation against the states.
  • "Deeply rooted in this Nation's history and tradition": The modern formulation of the incorporation test used in McDonald v. Chicago (2010) and other recent cases.
  • Due Process Clause (14th Amendment): The Fourteenth Amendment provision that "nor shall any State deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law" — the constitutional vehicle for incorporation.
  • Privileges or Immunities Clause: Another Fourteenth Amendment provision that some scholars argue should be the incorporation vehicle; rejected by most courts but endorsed by Justice Thomas in McDonald.
  • Gitlow v. New York (1925): The first major selective incorporation case, applying First Amendment free speech protections to state governments.
  • Mapp v. Ohio (1961): Applied the Fourth Amendment's exclusionary rule to state criminal proceedings.

Important Details

  • Incorporation is a case-by-case process: the Court does not incorporate entire amendments at once but evaluates individual rights within amendments. For example, different Fourth Amendment protections were incorporated in separate cases.
  • The process began with property rights (Chicago, Burlington & Quincy Railroad v. Chicago, 1897) and free speech (Gitlow v. New York, 1925), then expanded rapidly through the Warren Court era of the 1960s.
  • The Warren Court (1953–1969) dramatically accelerated incorporation, incorporating the Fourth Amendment exclusionary rule (Mapp, 1961), the right to counsel (Gideon, 1963), Miranda warnings (Miranda, 1966), and the right to jury trial (Duncan, 1968), among others.
  • Not incorporated: Third Amendment (quartering soldiers) has never been directly addressed by the Supreme Court; Fifth Amendment grand jury requirement means states may use prosecutorial information rather than grand juries; Seventh Amendment civil jury guarantee ($20 threshold) does not apply to state courts.
  • Timbs v. Indiana (2019) — the most recent major incorporation case — incorporated the Eighth Amendment's excessive fines clause, showing the doctrine's continued vitality.

Review Questions

1. What is the constitutional test the Supreme Court uses to determine whether a Bill of Rights provision should be incorporated against the states? The Supreme Court asks whether the specific right is "fundamental to the American scheme of ordered liberty" or "deeply rooted in this Nation's history and tradition." Both formulations test whether the right is of such fundamental importance that denying it to citizens in proceedings against state governments would be inconsistent with basic notions of justice and liberty. Rights meeting this standard are incorporated through the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause.

2. Why did the Fourteenth Amendment make selective incorporation possible? Before the Fourteenth Amendment (1868), the Bill of Rights restricted only the federal government, as confirmed by Barron v. Baltimore (1833). The Fourteenth Amendment, ratified to protect freed slaves, included a Due Process Clause prohibiting states from depriving persons of "life, liberty, or property" without due process. The word "liberty" provided the constitutional vehicle: the Supreme Court could interpret it to include specific rights enumerated in the Bill of Rights, making those rights applicable to state governments.

3. List five specific Bill of Rights provisions that have been incorporated and identify the case that incorporated each. (1) First Amendment free speech — Gitlow v. New York (1925); (2) Fourth Amendment exclusionary rule — Mapp v. Ohio (1961); (3) Sixth Amendment right to counsel — Gideon v. Wainwright (1963); (4) Fifth Amendment self-incrimination — Malloy v. Hogan (1964); (5) Second Amendment right to bear arms — McDonald v. City of Chicago (2010).

4. Identify two Bill of Rights provisions that have not been incorporated and explain why their unincorporated status matters. The Fifth Amendment grand jury requirement has not been incorporated, meaning states can charge defendants with felonies through prosecutorial information rather than grand jury indictment. The Seventh Amendment civil jury guarantee ($20 threshold) has not been incorporated, meaning states do not have to provide jury trials in civil cases under the federal standard. Their unincorporated status matters because it means significant procedural protections in federal criminal and civil proceedings do not automatically apply to state proceedings — states may use different, and potentially less protective, alternatives.

5. Why is the incorporation process described as "ongoing" rather than complete? New cases continue to reach the Supreme Court raising incorporation questions. Timbs v. Indiana (2019) incorporated the Eighth Amendment's excessive fines clause as recently as 2019. Moreover, rights that have been incorporated may raise new questions about their full scope when applied to state proceedings. And some unincorporated provisions — like the Third Amendment — could theoretically come before the Court in the future. The doctrine remains active because the Constitution is litigated continuously.

Summary

Selective incorporation is the Supreme Court's process of applying individual Bill of Rights provisions to state governments through the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause, evaluated case by case. A right is incorporated if it is fundamental to ordered liberty or deeply rooted in U.S. history and tradition. The process began in the late nineteenth century, accelerated dramatically during the Warren Court era, and continues today — as demonstrated by Timbs v. Indiana (2019). A few provisions remain unincorporated, but the practical effect of selective incorporation is to make the Bill of Rights a comprehensive protection for individual liberty at every level of American government.
