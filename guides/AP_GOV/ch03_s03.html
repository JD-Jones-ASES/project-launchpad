Study Guide: Topic 3.3 — Freedom of Religion

Key Concepts

  • The First Amendment contains two religion clauses that work together: the Establishment Clause (prohibiting government establishment of religion) and the Free Exercise Clause (protecting individual religious practice).
  • The Establishment Clause is evaluated using several tests: the Lemon test (1971), the endorsement test, and the coercion test; recent Court decisions have moved away from the Lemon test toward historical practice and coercion standards.
  • The Free Exercise Clause protects religious belief absolutely, but government may regulate religiously motivated conduct if the regulation is neutral and generally applicable (Employment Division v. Smith, 1990).
  • Engel v. Vitale (1962) established that government-composed prayer in public schools violates the Establishment Clause even if nondenominational and technically voluntary.
  • Wisconsin v. Yoder (1972) established that the Free Exercise Clause can require exemptions from otherwise valid laws when they substantially burden sincere religious practice and the government lacks a compelling interest.

Vocabulary Review

  • Establishment Clause: The First Amendment provision prohibiting Congress from making any law "respecting an establishment of religion." Requires government neutrality toward religion — neither promoting nor hindering it.
  • Free Exercise Clause: The First Amendment provision protecting individuals' right to practice their religion without government interference; belief is absolutely protected, but practice may be regulated by neutral and generally applicable laws.
  • Lemon test: Three-part Establishment Clause test from Lemon v. Kurtzman (1971): (1) the law must have a secular purpose, (2) its primary effect must neither advance nor inhibit religion, and (3) it must not create excessive government entanglement with religion.
  • Endorsement test: Justice O'Connor's Establishment Clause standard: does the government action send a message to non-adherents that they are outsiders, or to adherents that they are favored insiders?
  • Coercion test: Justice Kennedy's Establishment Clause standard: does the government coerce anyone to support or participate in religion?
  • Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA): Federal law passed in 1993 requiring government to demonstrate a compelling interest before substantially burdening religious exercise, enacted as a response to Employment Division v. Smith.
  • Neutrality principle: The requirement that government treat religious and non-religious activities equally — it may neither favor religion over non-religion nor the reverse.

Important Details

  • The two religion clauses can create tension: the Establishment Clause limits government involvement with religion, while the Free Exercise Clause may require government to accommodate religious practice. Courts must balance these competing demands.
  • In Engel v. Vitale (1962), the school prayer was composed by the New York State Board of Regents and was described as nondenominational. The Court held 6–1 that even voluntary, nondenominational prayer composed by the government violates the Establishment Clause because it is "no part of the business of government to compose official prayers."
  • In Wisconsin v. Yoder (1972), the Court balanced the state's interest in universal education against the Amish community's 300-year history of self-sufficient living. The unanimous decision found that forcing Amish children to attend high school served no compelling government interest sufficient to override their sincere religious beliefs.
  • In Employment Division v. Smith (1990), the Court held that the Free Exercise Clause does not exempt individuals from neutral, generally applicable laws — even if those laws burden their religious practice. Congress responded with RFRA to restore a higher level of protection.
  • Kennedy v. Bremerton School District (2022) signaled a shift: the Court moved away from the Lemon test and emphasized historical practices and the coercion standard in evaluating Establishment Clause claims.

Review Questions

1. What are the three prongs of the Lemon test, and what is the consequence of failing any one prong? The Lemon test asks: (1) Does the law have a secular legislative purpose? (2) Is the primary effect one that neither advances nor inhibits religion? (3) Does the law avoid excessive government entanglement with religion? If the government action fails any one of the three prongs, it violates the Establishment Clause and is unconstitutional. The test was the dominant Establishment Clause framework for decades, though recent decisions have moved toward other standards.

2. How do the Establishment Clause and the Free Exercise Clause work together and in tension? Together, the two clauses establish a zone of religious neutrality: government may neither promote religion (Establishment Clause) nor prohibit it (Free Exercise Clause). But they can pull in opposite directions. For example, if a government program exempts religious organizations from a regulation to protect religious practice (Free Exercise), critics may argue that the exemption constitutes a government benefit for religion (Establishment). Courts must navigate this tension case by case.

3. What did Engel v. Vitale establish about school-sponsored prayer? Engel held that it violates the Establishment Clause for government to compose and promote prayer for use in public schools, even if the prayer is nondenominational and participation is technically voluntary. Justice Black wrote that "it is no part of the business of government to compose official prayers." The coercive social pressure of the school environment makes voluntary participation illusory for young students.

4. How did the Court balance competing interests in Wisconsin v. Yoder? The Court weighed the state's interest in ensuring all children receive at least two years of high school education against the Amish community's sincere religious belief that high school attendance would expose their children to worldly values incompatible with their faith. The Court found the state's interest insufficient: the Amish community had a centuries-long record of law-abiding, productive living and provided vocational training for its youth. Compelling another year or two of formal schooling did not serve a compelling government interest that could override the religious burden.

5. After Employment Division v. Smith (1990), what protection does the Free Exercise Clause provide for religiously motivated conduct? After Smith, the Free Exercise Clause does not exempt individuals from neutral, generally applicable laws even if those laws burden their religious practice. If a law is neutral and does not target religion, it applies to everyone, including those whose religious beliefs conflict with it. Congress enacted RFRA in 1993 to restore greater protections by requiring government to demonstrate a compelling interest before substantially burdening religious exercise — a higher standard than the Constitution alone requires after Smith.

Summary

The First Amendment's two religion clauses establish a complex framework for government neutrality toward religion. The Establishment Clause prohibits government promotion of religion (including school-sponsored prayer under Engel v. Vitale), while the Free Exercise Clause protects the right to practice one's religion (including exemptions from neutral laws when sincere beliefs are substantially burdened, as in Wisconsin v. Yoder). The evolving doctrine — from the Lemon test to the coercion and endorsement tests — reflects ongoing debate about where the line between church and state should be drawn.
