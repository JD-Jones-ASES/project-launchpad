Study Guide: Topic 2.13 - Discretionary and Rulemaking Authority

Key Concepts

Congressional Delegation - Congress passes broad legislation and delegates authority to executive agencies to fill in the specifics through regulations. Reasons: technical expertise agencies possess, flexibility to update rules without new legislation, political cover for Congress, and the sheer volume of modern governance.

Discretionary Authority - the power of agencies to make decisions about how to implement and enforce laws within the boundaries set by Congress. Manifests in interpreting statutory language, setting enforcement priorities, and filling legislative gaps.

Administrative Procedure Act (APA) of 1946 - the law establishing the notice-and-comment rulemaking process. Requires agencies to follow transparent, participatory procedures when creating regulations.

Notice-and-Comment Rulemaking Process:

  1. Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM): Agency publishes proposed rule in the Federal Register.
  2. Public comment period: Interested parties submit written comments (typically 30-90 days). Agencies must consider all significant comments.
  3. Final rule: Agency publishes final rule with responses to significant comments.
  4. Effective date: Rule takes effect (typically 30 days after publication).

Federal Register - the official daily journal of the federal government publishing proposed and final regulations.

Regulations vs. Legislation - both have the force of law, but they differ: legislation is created by elected Congress (bicameralism + presentment); regulations are created by unelected agencies (notice-and-comment). Regulations can be updated through rulemaking without new legislation.

Intelligible Principle Standard - the constitutional test for delegation. Courts will uphold congressional delegation as long as Congress provides an intelligible principle to guide agency discretion. The Court has interpreted this very broadly.

Key Takeaways

  • Congress delegates authority because agencies have technical expertise, can update rules without legislation, and can absorb political costs for controversial decisions.
  • Bureaucratic discretion gives agencies significant policy-making power - regulations fill thousands of Federal Register pages annually.
  • The notice-and-comment process is a democratic check on agency power: ensures transparency, gives affected parties a voice, and creates a written record for judicial review.
  • Both regulations and legislation have the force of law - violations result in real penalties - but regulations are created by unelected bureaucrats through the rulemaking process.
  • Critics argue excessive delegation violates Article I's vesting of all legislative power in Congress; defenders argue delegation is necessary and constitutional with an intelligible principle.

AP Exam Connection

Topic 2.13 connects the bureaucracy to congressional power and the separation of powers. Expect FRQ prompts asking you to explain the notice-and-comment process or to compare regulations with legislation. The congressional delegation question - whether unelected bureaucrats should make binding rules - is a high-frequency AP exam theme about democratic accountability. Be able to explain both the justification for delegation (expertise, flexibility) and the criticism (unaccountable lawmaking).
