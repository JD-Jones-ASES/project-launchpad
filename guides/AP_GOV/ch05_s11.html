Study Guide: Topic 5.11 -- Campaign Finance

Key Concepts

  • The Federal Election Campaign Act (FECA) of 1971 (amended 1974) established the first comprehensive campaign finance framework: contribution limits, disclosure requirements, the Federal Election Commission (FEC), and voluntary public financing for presidential campaigns.
  • Buckley v. Valeo (1976) drew the foundational distinction that defines campaign finance law today: contribution limits (to candidates) are valid anti-corruption measures and may be upheld; expenditure limits (how much anyone can spend) are unconstitutional restrictions on political speech protected by the First Amendment.
  • McCain-Feingold / BCRA (2002) banned soft money contributions to national party committees and restricted electioneering communications, closing the major loophole through which unlimited party money had flowed since FECA.
  • Citizens United v. FEC (2010) struck down BCRA's restrictions on corporate independent expenditures, leading directly to the creation of Super PACs and an explosion in outside spending.
  • Small-dollar fundraising (ActBlue, WinRed) has emerged as a countertrend, reducing dependence on large donors but tending to reward ideologically polarizing candidates who generate online enthusiasm.

Vocabulary Review

  • Hard money: Campaign contributions made directly to candidates, subject to strict FEC limits and disclosure requirements under FECA.
  • Soft money: Funds donated to party organizations for "party-building activities" -- not subject to FECA's hard money limits. Used to run thinly veiled campaign ads. Banned by McCain-Feingold (BCRA) in 2002.
  • FECA (Federal Election Campaign Act): 1971 law (amended 1974) establishing the modern campaign finance framework: contribution limits, mandatory disclosure, the FEC, and voluntary public financing.
  • FEC (Federal Election Commission): The independent regulatory agency responsible for enforcing campaign finance law, overseeing disclosure, and administering public financing for presidential campaigns.
  • Buckley v. Valeo (1976): Supreme Court ruling that upheld contribution limits but struck down expenditure limits as unconstitutional restrictions on First Amendment speech. Foundational case for all subsequent campaign finance law.
  • BCRA / McCain-Feingold (2002): Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act that banned soft money to national parties and restricted electioneering communications within 30 days of a primary or 60 days of a general election.
  • Electioneering communication: Broadcast advertising that mentions a federal candidate within 30 days of a primary or 60 days of a general election -- regulated under BCRA, restrictions later struck down by Citizens United.
  • Small-dollar fundraising: Online fundraising from millions of individual small donors ($5-$25). Platforms: ActBlue (Democratic) and WinRed (Republican). Reduces dependence on large donors but rewards highly polarizing candidates.

Key Takeaways

  • The core tension in campaign finance law is between two values: preventing corruption and protecting free speech. Every major legal development reflects a different resolution of this tension.
  • Buckley's contribution/expenditure distinction created the framework that Citizens United built on 34 years later.
  • Soft money was the great loophole of the post-FECA era; BCRA closed it; Citizens United opened a new one through Super PACs.
  • Outside spending has grown from $100 million in 2000 to over $4 billion in 2024, with Super PACs and dark money groups driving the increase.
  • Small-dollar fundraising represents a genuine democratizing force in campaign finance but its polarizing incentive structure is a trade-off.

AP Exam Connection

  • Quantitative Analysis FRQ: the campaign spending data table (2000-2024) is a prime stimulus -- expect questions about trends, the sharpest increase, and what legal change explains the 2012 jump in outside spending.
  • Citizens United is a required SCOTUS case -- expect SCOTUS Comparison FRQs comparing it to a non-required campaign finance case.
  • Buckley v. Valeo is not a required case but is essential context for understanding Citizens United's legal logic.
  • Concept Application FRQ scenarios may describe various types of political spending and ask students to identify which legal framework applies and whether the spending can be regulated.
