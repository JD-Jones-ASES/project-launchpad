Study Guide: Topic 3.2 — Freedom of Speech

Key Concepts

  • The First Amendment's free speech guarantee is not absolute; the Supreme Court distinguishes between protected speech (which receives constitutional protection) and unprotected speech (narrow categories that fall outside First Amendment coverage).
  • Political speech and symbolic speech receive the highest level of protection; commercial speech receives intermediate protection.
  • Unprotected categories include true threats, incitement to imminent lawless action, obscenity (under the Miller test), fighting words, and defamation with actual malice for public figures.
  • Even protected speech may be subject to content-neutral time, place, and manner restrictions that are narrowly tailored and leave open alternative channels of communication.
  • Schenck v. United States (1919) introduced the "clear and present danger" test; Tinker v. Des Moines (1969) established that students retain First Amendment rights in public schools under the "substantial disruption" standard.

Vocabulary Review

  • Symbolic speech: Expression that communicates ideas through conduct rather than words (e.g., wearing armbands, burning flags, displaying signs). Protected under the First Amendment when it is clearly communicative.
  • Prior restraint: A government order that prevents speech or publication before it occurs. Carries a heavy presumption of unconstitutionality.
  • Clear and present danger test: The standard established in Schenck v. United States (1919): speech may be restricted if it poses a clear and present danger that it will produce the substantive evils Congress has the right to prevent.
  • Imminent lawless action standard: The updated and narrower test from Brandenburg v. Ohio (1969): speech is unprotected only if it is directed at producing imminent lawless action AND is likely to produce such action.
  • Fighting words: Words that by their very utterance tend to incite an immediate breach of the peace; an unprotected category established in Chaplinsky v. New Hampshire (1942).
  • Miller test: The three-part test for obscenity: (1) appeals to prurient interest, (2) depicts sexual conduct in a patently offensive way, and (3) lacks serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value.
  • Actual malice: The standard public figures must meet to win a defamation lawsuit: the statement was made knowing it was false, or with reckless disregard for its truth or falsity (New York Times v. Sullivan, 1964).
  • Substantial disruption test: The Tinker standard for limiting student speech in public schools: officials may restrict student expression only if it would cause substantial disruption of school activities or invade others' rights.
  • Time, place, and manner restrictions: Content-neutral regulations on when, where, or how speech may occur; constitutional if narrowly tailored and leaving open alternative communication channels.

Important Details

  • Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. wrote the Schenck opinion and the famous "shouting fire in a crowded theater" analogy to illustrate why context shapes the constitutional status of speech.
  • Schenck's "clear and present danger" test was narrowed by Brandenburg v. Ohio (1969), which set a higher bar: speech must be directed at imminent lawless action and likely to produce it — mere advocacy of illegal action is protected.
  • Tinker v. Des Moines (1969) involved black armbands worn to protest the Vietnam War; the students were suspended after the school adopted a preemptive ban. The Court held 7–2 that the symbolic protest was "closely akin to pure speech" and caused no substantial disruption.
  • Later cases limited Tinker: Bethel School District v. Fraser (1986) allowed schools to restrict lewd and vulgar speech; Hazelwood School District v. Kuhlmeier (1988) permitted schools to control school-sponsored expression with legitimate pedagogical concerns; Morse v. Frederick (2007) upheld restrictions on speech promoting drug use at a school-supervised event.
  • Commercial speech (advertising) receives intermediate protection: government may regulate misleading commercial speech or ads for illegal products, but truthful commercial speech about lawful products is generally protected.

Review Questions

1. What is the difference between political speech and commercial speech in terms of First Amendment protection? Political speech — expression about government, candidates, and public affairs — receives the highest level of First Amendment protection because it lies at the core of democratic self-governance. Commercial speech — advertising and business-related expression — receives only intermediate protection. The government may regulate misleading commercial speech or ads for illegal products but generally cannot restrict truthful advertising for lawful activities.

2. What are the three requirements that a time, place, and manner restriction must meet to be constitutional? A time, place, and manner restriction must be (1) content-neutral — it may not discriminate based on the message being communicated; (2) narrowly tailored to serve a significant government interest; and (3) leave open alternative channels of communication so that the speaker still has meaningful ways to express the message.

3. How did the "clear and present danger" test from Schenck differ from the "imminent lawless action" standard from Brandenburg? The Schenck "clear and present danger" test was relatively permissive, allowing restriction whenever speech created a danger of producing illegal results — even if those results were not imminent. The Brandenburg standard is more speech-protective: speech may only be restricted if it is (1) directed at producing imminent lawless action AND (2) likely to produce such action. Mere advocacy of illegal ideas, even in strong terms, is protected under Brandenburg.

4. Why did the Supreme Court protect the Tinker students' armband protest, and what test did the Court establish? The Court held that the black armbands were "closely akin to pure speech" and that the students' silent, passive protest caused no substantial disruption of school activities. The Court established the substantial disruption test: school officials may restrict student speech only if they can demonstrate that the expression would cause substantial disruption or invade the rights of others. Because school officials had only feared disruption rather than observed it, the ban was unconstitutional.

5. Identify three categories of unprotected speech and explain why each falls outside First Amendment protection. (1) True threats are unprotected because they communicate a serious intent to commit violence, which serves no legitimate expressive purpose and causes real harm to targets. (2) Incitement under Brandenburg is unprotected because speech designed to immediately produce illegal violence poses a direct threat to public order with no countervailing social value. (3) Obscenity under the Miller test is unprotected because material that appeals to prurient interest and lacks serious social value is deemed to have no constitutional value worth protecting.

Summary

The First Amendment's free speech clause protects a wide range of expression but not all speech. Political and symbolic speech receive the strongest protection, while narrow categories — true threats, incitement, obscenity, fighting words, and defamation — fall outside First Amendment coverage. Even protected speech can be regulated through content-neutral time, place, and manner restrictions. Two landmark cases define the field: Schenck v. United States (1919) established that context determines whether speech is protected, and Tinker v. Des Moines (1969) confirmed that students retain constitutional rights in public schools under the substantial disruption standard.
