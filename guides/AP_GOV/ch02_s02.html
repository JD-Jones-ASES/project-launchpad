Study Guide: Topic 2.2 — Structures, Powers, and Functions of Congress

Key Concepts

  • Enumerated powers (Article I, Section 8) are the explicit grants of congressional authority: taxing, borrowing, regulating interstate commerce, declaring war, coining money, and more. They define the outer boundary of federal legislative power.
  • Implied powers flow from the Necessary and Proper Clause, which allows Congress to enact any law "necessary and proper" to carry out its enumerated powers. McCulloch v. Maryland (1819) confirmed this broad reading.
  • The committee system is where most legislative work happens—hearings, markup, investigation. The four committee types (standing, select, joint, conference) divide legislative labor.
  • The filibuster allows any senator to speak indefinitely, blocking a vote. Ending a filibuster requires cloture: 60 votes (three-fifths of the Senate). The 60-vote threshold gives the minority party significant blocking power.
  • Congressional leadership differs between chambers: the Speaker of the House is the most powerful figure in Congress; the Senate Majority Leader controls the floor agenda.

Vocabulary Review

Term Definition
Enumerated powers Powers explicitly listed in the Constitution (Article I, Section 8)
Implied powers Powers not explicitly stated but reasonably inferred from enumerated powers via the Necessary and Proper Clause
Necessary and Proper Clause Article I, Section 8 provision (also called the Elastic Clause) authorizing Congress to make laws needed to execute its enumerated powers
Standing committee Permanent committee specializing in a policy area (e.g., House Ways and Means, Senate Judiciary)
Select committee Temporary committee created to investigate a specific issue
Joint committee Committee with members from both chambers; typically handles administrative or research functions
Conference committee Temporary joint committee that reconciles differences between House and Senate versions of a bill
Filibuster A Senate tactic allowing unlimited debate to delay or block a vote on legislation
Cloture The procedure to end a Senate filibuster; requires 60 votes (three-fifths of the full Senate)
Speaker of the House The presiding officer of the House, elected by the full House; the most powerful member of Congress

Important Details

Exclusive Powers by Chamber

House only:

  • Originate revenue bills (Art. I, Sec. 7)
  • Impeach federal officials (simple majority)
  • Choose president in contingent election (one vote per state delegation)

Senate only:

  • Advice and consent on appointments (Art. II, Sec. 2)
  • Ratify treaties (two-thirds vote)
  • Conduct impeachment trials (two-thirds to convict)

The Committee System

  • Standing committees are the legislative workhorses; most bills die in committee
  • Committee chairs set agendas and hearing schedules—enormous gatekeeping power
  • Conference committees are essential when House and Senate pass different versions of the same bill; both chambers must approve the conference report

Leadership Structure

  • House: Speaker → Majority Leader → Majority Whip → Minority Leader → Minority Whip
  • Senate: VP (President of Senate, tie-breaking only) → President Pro Tempore (ceremonial) → Majority Leader (most powerful) → Minority Leader
  • Speaker is second in presidential line of succession; President Pro Tempore is third

The Filibuster and Cloture

  • Filibuster reflects Senate tradition of extended debate and protecting minority rights
  • 60-vote cloture threshold effectively requires a supermajority for most legislation
  • "Nuclear option": In 2013, Senate lowered cloture threshold to simple majority for lower-court judicial nominees; in 2017, extended to Supreme Court nominees
  • Legislative filibuster still requires 60 votes

Review Questions

1. Distinguish between enumerated and implied powers, and identify the constitutional basis for each. Enumerated powers are those explicitly listed in Article I, Section 8 of the Constitution—taxing, borrowing, regulating interstate commerce, declaring war, coining money, etc. These represent the direct grants of congressional authority. Implied powers are those not explicitly stated but reasonably inferred from the enumerated powers. The constitutional basis for implied powers is the Necessary and Proper Clause (Article I, Section 8, final clause), which allows Congress to enact any law "necessary and proper" to carry out its enumerated powers. McCulloch v. Maryland (1819) established that Congress may use any means not prohibited by the Constitution to carry out its enumerated powers.

2. Explain the role of conference committees in the legislative process. Conference committees are temporary joint committees formed when the House and Senate pass different versions of the same bill. Members from both chambers meet to negotiate a compromise version. Once a conference report is agreed upon, both the full House and full Senate must vote to approve the compromise text—neither chamber can amend the conference report. If either chamber rejects the report, the conference process restarts. Conference committees are essential for resolving the differences that naturally arise when two distinct legislative bodies work on the same legislation.

3. Why does the filibuster give the minority party significant influence in the Senate? The filibuster allows any senator to speak on the floor for an unlimited time, preventing the Senate from moving to a vote on legislation. To end a filibuster and force a vote, the Senate must invoke cloture with 60 votes—three-fifths of the full Senate. In a chamber of 100 members, this means that 41 senators (well under half) can block almost any legislation they oppose. Because each party rarely holds 60 seats, this effectively gives the minority party veto power over most legislation, forcing the majority to negotiate bipartisan support or watch their priorities stall.

4. Compare the leadership structures of the House and Senate. The House is led by the Speaker, elected by the full chamber but effectively chosen by the majority party; the Speaker controls the legislative calendar, assigns bills to committees, and shapes party strategy. The Senate's most powerful figure is the Majority Leader, who controls the floor schedule. The Senate's constitutional presiding officers—the Vice President and President Pro Tempore—hold largely ceremonial roles. The Speaker's authority is more concentrated because House rules are stricter; the Senate's culture of individualism and the filibuster limit even the Majority Leader's control.

5. What is the "nuclear option" and what has been its impact? The "nuclear option" is the use of a simple majority vote to change Senate rules—specifically, to lower the cloture threshold from 60 votes to a simple majority for certain categories of nominations. Democrats used it in 2013 to lower the threshold for lower-court judicial nominees; Republicans extended it in 2017 to Supreme Court nominees. The impact has been a significant reduction in the minority party's ability to block judicial confirmations, leading to more partisan confirmation votes and accelerating the ideological reshaping of the federal judiciary.

Summary

Congress derives its legislative authority from enumerated powers in Article I, Section 8 and implied powers authorized by the Necessary and Proper Clause. The committee system divides legislative labor among specialized bodies, with most legislative work (and bill deaths) occurring at the committee level. Leadership structures differ between the chambers—the Speaker dominates the House while the Senate Majority Leader manages a more individualistic body constrained by the filibuster. The 60-vote cloture requirement for most legislation gives the Senate minority party substantial blocking power, shaping the dynamics of legislative bargaining and coalition-building.
