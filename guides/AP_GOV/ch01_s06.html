Study Guide: Topic 1.6 — Principles of American Government

Key Concepts

  • Separation of powers divides governmental authority horizontally among three distinct branches — legislative (makes laws), executive (enforces laws), judicial (interprets laws) — ensuring that no single institution can accumulate enough power to threaten individual liberty.
  • Checks and balances layer additional constraints on top of separation of powers, giving each branch specific constitutional tools to restrain the others. The logic, from Madison's Federalist No. 51, is that "ambition must be made to counteract ambition."
  • Federalism divides authority vertically between the national government and state governments, creating a second dimension of power distribution distinct from the horizontal separation among branches.
  • Judicial review, established in Marbury v. Madison (1803), is the judiciary's ultimate check on government power — the ability to declare laws and executive actions unconstitutional, enforcing the Constitution's limits on the other branches.
  • Limited government and republican government are the overarching principles: the Constitution grants only defined powers (limited government), and those powers are exercised through elected representatives accountable to the people (republican government).

Vocabulary Review

Separation of powers: The constitutional principle dividing governmental authority among three distinct branches — legislative, executive, and judicial — each with its own personnel, powers, and sphere of responsibility.

Checks and balances: A system of overlapping powers allowing each branch to restrain the others, preventing any single branch from accumulating unchecked authority. Examples: the presidential veto, congressional override, Senate confirmation, judicial review.

Veto: The president's power to reject legislation passed by Congress. Congress can override a veto with a two-thirds vote in both chambers.

Override: Congress's power to enact legislation despite a presidential veto, requiring a two-thirds vote in both the House and Senate.

Impeachment: The formal process of charging a sitting president, vice president, or federal judge with misconduct. The House impeaches (brings charges) by a simple majority; the Senate conducts the trial and requires a two-thirds vote to convict and remove.

Advice and consent: The Senate's constitutional role in confirming presidential appointments to the cabinet, federal judiciary, and other key positions, and ratifying treaties by a two-thirds vote.

Judicial review: The power of federal courts to examine the actions of the legislative and executive branches and declare them unconstitutional; established in Marbury v. Madison (1803), though not explicitly mentioned in the Constitution.

Federalism: The constitutional division of power between the national and state governments, each deriving authority from the Constitution and each operating directly on the people within its sphere.

Important Details

  • The concept of separation of powers came primarily from French philosopher Baron de Montesquieu, whose The Spirit of the Laws (1748) argued that concentrating legislative, executive, and judicial power in the same hands is the very definition of tyranny.
  • The three branches are completely independent in personnel: a member of Congress cannot simultaneously hold an executive or judicial position. This separation of persons is as important as the separation of powers.
  • Legislative checks on the Executive: override veto (2/3 vote), Senate confirms appointments, Senate ratifies treaties (2/3 vote), controls the budget (power of the purse), House can impeach the president.
  • Executive checks on the Legislature: veto legislation, call special sessions, use the "bully pulpit" to influence public opinion and pressure Congress, vice president casts tie-breaking Senate votes.
  • Judicial checks: courts can declare laws unconstitutional (judicial review), declare executive actions unconstitutional, issue injunctions blocking executive actions; the Chief Justice presides over presidential impeachment trials.
  • Executive checks on Judiciary: president nominates all federal judges including Supreme Court justices, can grant pardons for federal offenses.
  • Legislative checks on Judiciary: Senate confirms judges, Congress can propose constitutional amendments to override court decisions, Congress controls court size and jurisdiction, Congress can impeach judges.
  • Madison in Federalist No. 51: "If men were angels, no government would be necessary. If angels were to govern men, neither external nor internal controls on government would be necessary." The constitutional design accounts for human self-interest, harnessing it as a structural force rather than relying on individual virtue.
  • The Tenth Amendment (part of the Bill of Rights) makes limited government explicit: "The powers not delegated to the United States by the Constitution, nor prohibited by it to the States, are reserved to the States respectively, or to the people."
  • Article IV, Section 4 guarantees every state a "Republican Form of Government" — ensuring no state can establish a monarchy, dictatorship, or direct democracy inconsistent with representative governance.

Review Questions

1. Explain how the system of checks and balances prevents any single branch from becoming too powerful. Provide two specific examples.

Answer: Checks and balances prevent dominance by giving each branch constitutional tools to restrain the others — so that if any branch overreaches, the others can push back. Example 1: Congress can pass legislation, but the president can veto it. If Congress believes the veto was unwarranted, it can override the veto with a two-thirds vote in both chambers. This cycle means legislation requires at least some cooperation or consensus between the branches. Example 2: The president nominates all federal judges, potentially shaping the judiciary's ideological direction, but the Senate must confirm these nominees. Senate rejection (such as the 1987 rejection of Robert Bork) demonstrates that the executive cannot unilaterally control the judiciary. Together, these mechanisms ensure that no branch can act unilaterally on major questions without the at least tacit acceptance of the others.

2. What is the difference between separation of powers and federalism, and how do they work together to limit government power?

Answer: Separation of powers is a horizontal division of authority — it splits governmental power among three branches (legislative, executive, judicial) at the same level of government. No single branch can make, enforce, and interpret law. Federalism is a vertical division — it splits authority between two levels of government (national and state), each supreme within its own sphere. They work together to create what Madison called a "double security" for individual rights: the separation of powers prevents any single branch from accumulating total authority at the national level, while federalism prevents the national government itself from monopolizing all governmental power. Citizens can appeal to state governments when they distrust federal action, and vice versa. The overlapping network of horizontal and vertical divisions makes it extremely difficult for any faction — whether a single official, a branch, or even a level of government — to seize total control.

3. What is the significance of Madison's statement in Federalist No. 51 that "ambition must be made to counteract ambition"?

Answer: Madison's statement reflects a deeply realistic view of human nature. He acknowledges that government officials are driven by self-interest and ambition — they naturally seek to expand their own power and authority. Rather than hoping that officials will be virtuous enough to restrain themselves, Madison's constitutional design harnesses this human tendency as a structural force. By giving each branch both the incentive (self-preservation) and the constitutional tools (checks) to resist encroachments by the others, the system ensures that power checks power automatically — without relying on the personal virtue of any particular officeholder. If the president becomes too powerful, Congress's institutional self-interest motivates it to push back. If Congress overreaches, the president's institutional interests motivate a veto. The genius of the design is that it turns human weakness into a constitutional strength, making the system self-correcting.

4. Identify three specific checks that the Legislative branch has on the Executive branch.

Answer: The Constitution gives Congress multiple checks on presidential power. (1) Power of the purse: Congress controls the federal budget — it appropriates all federal spending. The president cannot spend money that Congress has not authorized. This is perhaps the most fundamental check, as it can effectively block any executive initiative that requires funding. (2) Override: Congress can overcome a presidential veto with a two-thirds vote in both chambers, ensuring that presidential disapproval is not the final word on legislation. (3) Impeachment: The House can impeach a president (bring formal charges) by a simple majority, and the Senate conducts a trial that can result in removal from office with a two-thirds vote. Historically, presidents Jackson (1868), Clinton (1998), and Trump (2019, 2021) were impeached by the House but not convicted by the Senate.

5. Why is judicial review considered so significant even though it is not explicitly mentioned in the Constitution?

Answer: Judicial review is arguably the most powerful check in the entire constitutional system, yet it appears nowhere in the Constitution's text. Chief Justice Marshall derived it in Marbury v. Madison (1803) through logical reasoning: a written constitution is the supreme law of the land; if any law conflicts with the Constitution, the Constitution must prevail; therefore, courts must have the authority to strike down unconstitutional laws — otherwise the Constitution's limits would be meaningless. The significance is enormous: without judicial review, constitutional limits on government would be merely aspirational. Congress and the president could ignore them without legal consequence. With judicial review, the Supreme Court serves as the ultimate arbiter of constitutional meaning, making the Constitution a legally enforceable document rather than a political statement. Every major constitutional battle — from desegregation to abortion rights to campaign finance — has ultimately been decided through the exercise of judicial review.

Summary

The Constitution's structural design rests on three overlapping principles that collectively limit governmental power while enabling effective governance. Separation of powers divides authority horizontally among three branches, each with distinct functions, personnel, and constitutional powers. Checks and balances add a web of overlapping constraints, giving each branch tools to restrain the others — turning human ambition into a structural safeguard as Madison explained in Federalist No. 51. Federalism provides a second dimension of power distribution, dividing authority vertically between national and state governments. Judicial review — established by Marbury v. Madison — makes all these constitutional limits legally enforceable by empowering courts to strike down government actions that violate the Constitution. Together, these principles create the "double security" for individual rights that the Founders designed.
