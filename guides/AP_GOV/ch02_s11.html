Study Guide: Topic 2.11 - Checks on the Judicial Branch

Key Concepts

Constitutional Amendment is the most definitive check on judicial power. Congress and the states can amend the Constitution to overrule a Court interpretation. Examples: 11th Amendment overruled Chisholm v. Georgia; 13th/14th Amendments overruled Dred Scott; 16th Amendment overruled Pollock; 26th Amendment overruled Oregon v. Mitchell. The amendment process is deliberately difficult.

Jurisdiction Stripping - Article III, Section 2 grants Congress the power to regulate the Supreme Court's appellate jurisdiction with such Exceptions as Congress shall make. Congress can remove entire categories of cases from federal court review.

Court Packing - the Constitution does not specify the number of Supreme Court justices. FDR's 1937 plan to add one justice for every sitting justice over age 70 was widely condemned and defeated in Congress. The Court's subsequent jurisprudential shift was called the switch in time that saved nine.

Impeachment of Judges - federal judges can be impeached by the House and removed by the Senate for high Crimes and Misdemeanors. Only Justice Samuel Chase was impeached (1804); he was acquitted, establishing the norm that judges should not be removed for their judicial opinions.

Statutory Override - when the Court interprets a federal statute (not the Constitution), Congress can pass new legislation changing the statutory language. Example: Civil Rights Act of 1991 restored employment discrimination protections narrowed by the Court in 1989.

Appointment Power - the president shapes the ideological direction of courts for decades by nominating judges whose philosophy aligns with presidential priorities.

Doctrines of Self-Restraint: standing (concrete personal injury required), mootness (controversy must still exist), political question doctrine (courts decline questions committed to other branches), stare decisis (follow precedent), case or controversy requirement (no advisory opinions).

Key Takeaways

  • The judicial branch is subject to checks from Congress, the president, and from its own doctrines of self-restraint.
  • Constitutional amendment is the definitive but rarely used check - its difficulty means most Court decisions stand unless Congress can change the underlying statute.
  • FDR's court-packing plan failed but established an important norm against using court size as a political weapon; the episode illustrates both the power and limits of political pressure on the judiciary.
  • The political question doctrine reflects the Court's own understanding that some questions should be resolved by elected branches, not courts.
  • Enforcement discretion means the executive effectively has a silent veto over how aggressively court decisions are implemented.

AP Exam Connection

Topic 2.11 is a key part of the checks and balances framework. Expect FRQ prompts asking you to identify specific checks on the judicial branch from both Congress and the president. The FDR court-packing plan is a frequently tested example. The political question doctrine appears in scenario-based questions. Know the difference between checks on constitutional interpretations (amendment, court packing) vs. statutory interpretations (statutory override).
