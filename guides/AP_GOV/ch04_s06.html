Study Guide: Evaluating Public Opinion Data

AP US Government and Politics — Unit 4, Topic 4.6

Key Concepts

  • Sampling error is the expected, measurable difference between a sample result and the true population value; it decreases with larger samples and is quantified by the margin of error.
  • Non-sampling error encompasses all other inaccuracies (coverage error, nonresponse error, measurement error, weighting error); it cannot be reduced by increasing sample size and is more dangerous than sampling error.
  • Critical poll evaluation requires examining the conducting organization, sample size, sampling method, timing, question wording, and population being measured.
  • Polling averages (aggregation) reduce the influence of any single outlier poll but cannot correct systematic biases shared by all polls in the average.
  • The AP Quantitative Analysis FRQ requires describing trends, drawing conclusions connected to political principles, and explaining limitations of data.

Vocabulary Review

Term Definition
Sampling error The expected difference between a sample result and the true population value; measurable, quantified by margin of error, decreases with larger samples
Non-sampling error All sources of polling inaccuracy other than sampling error; cannot be reduced by increasing sample size
Coverage error Non-sampling error that occurs when the sampling frame does not match the target population (e.g., landline-only polls miss cell-phone-only households)
Nonresponse error Non-sampling error from systematic differences between those who participate and those who don't; if disengaged citizens refuse polls, results overrepresent the politically active
Measurement error Non-sampling error from question wording, interviewer effects, or response bias
Weighting error Non-sampling error from incorrect assumptions in statistical adjustments made to raw data to match known demographics
Polling average / aggregation Combining results from multiple polls to produce a more stable, accurate estimate; reduces impact of single-poll outliers but cannot correct shared systematic biases

Important Details

Sampling Error vs. Non-Sampling Error: The Critical Distinction

Sampling Error Non-Sampling Error
Cause Surveying a subset, not the whole population Methodology flaws: wrong population, refusals, bad questions, wrong weights
Can it be reduced by larger sample? Yes No
Is it measurable? Yes — it's the margin of error Not easily quantifiable
Is it inevitable? Yes (always some sampling error) No — can be eliminated with good design
Which is more dangerous? Less dangerous More dangerous

Four Types of Non-Sampling Error

1. Coverage error — sampling frame ≠ target population

  • Example: A 2016 landline-only poll would miss the majority of adults under 35 who use only cell phones
  • Impact: Systematically overrepresents older, wealthier, more conservative demographics

2. Nonresponse error — refusals and unreachables differ from respondents

  • Example: If politically disengaged citizens refuse to answer surveys, poll results overrepresent the politically active and ideologically extreme
  • Impact: Poll appears to show more political engagement than actually exists in the population

3. Measurement error — question wording, interviewer effects, response bias

  • Same as covered in Topic 4.5; distorts the answers people give
  • Impact: Results don't reflect genuine views even from a well-selected sample

4. Weighting error — incorrect demographic adjustments

  • Pollsters adjust raw data to match known proportions (men/women, age groups, etc.)
  • If weighting assumptions are wrong (e.g., predicting turnout incorrectly), results are skewed
  • Impact: A poll may look balanced demographically but still produce biased results

Six Questions for Critical Poll Evaluation

  1. Who conducted it? (Reputable organization vs. campaign or advocacy group?)
  2. What is the sample size and margin of error? (Larger = more precise; subgroup results have larger margins)
  3. How was the sample selected? (Random sampling only; opt-in panels are not valid)
  4. When was it conducted? (Post-event spikes may be temporary; timing matters)
  5. What were the exact questions? (Wording should be neutral and unambiguous)
  6. Who is the population? ("All adults" vs. "registered voters" vs. "likely voters" produce different results)

Understanding Population Differences

Population Who's Included Likely Effect
All adults Every adult Most inclusive; includes non-voters
Registered voters Those registered More politically engaged than all adults
Likely voters Those likely to vote Most conservative of the three; older, more politically engaged; may undercount young voters

Polling Averages (Aggregation)

  • Combine multiple polls weighting by sample size, methodology quality, and historical track record
  • Advantage: Reduces impact of any single outlier; provides clearer trend picture
  • Limitation: If all polls share the same systematic bias (e.g., all undercount a demographic group), averaging amplifies rather than corrects it

AP Exam Skill: Quantitative Analysis FRQ

Required steps:

  1. Describe a trend or pattern ("From 2004 to 2020, support for X increased from Y% to Z%")
  2. Draw a conclusion connecting data to a political principle ("This trend suggests that Americans increasingly favor...")
  3. Explain a limitation or alternative explanation ("However, this poll does not account for...")

Review Questions

1. What is the key difference between sampling error and non-sampling error? Which is more dangerous, and why? Answer: Sampling error is the expected, inevitable difference between a sample result and the true population value, caused simply by surveying a subset rather than everyone. It is measurable (the margin of error) and decreases as sample size increases. Non-sampling error encompasses all other sources of inaccuracy—coverage error, nonresponse error, measurement error, and weighting error—and it cannot be reduced by increasing sample size. Non-sampling error is more dangerous because it is often invisible, unmeasurable, and systematic: it does not average out with a larger sample but instead produces consistently biased results that appear credible.

2. A political campaign conducts a poll of 600 people using an online opt-in panel. What type of error does this method risk, and why can't it be fixed by increasing the sample to 6,000? Answer: An online opt-in panel creates coverage error and nonresponse error—forms of non-sampling error. The sampling frame (people who choose to take an online poll) does not match the target population (all voters), because self-selecting participants systematically differ from non-participants: they are more politically engaged, more likely to use the internet frequently, and may have other demographic differences. Increasing the sample to 6,000 would reduce sampling error (the margin of error would decrease), but it would not fix the non-sampling error. All 6,000 respondents are still drawn from the same biased pool. The flaw is in who participates, not how many. A biased sample of 6,000 is still a biased sample.

3. Explain how "likely voter" models create potential weighting error in polls, especially in unusual election years. Answer: Pollsters who survey "likely voters" must decide who counts as a likely voter—typically by asking about past voting behavior, intention to vote, and interest in the election. They then statistically weight their sample to match predictions about the likely electorate's demographic composition. If those predictions are wrong—as can happen in years with unusual turnout patterns—the weights introduce error. For example, if a pollster assumes young voters will turn out at their historical average but a social movement dramatically increases youth turnout (as happened with some elections), the poll would undercount young voters' likely preferences and skew results. The weights are based on assumptions, and when those assumptions don't match reality, the adjustments produce rather than reduce error.

4. Using the two-poll comparison from the section (Poll A from a university research center; Poll B from an advocacy group), identify two methodological reasons Poll A is more reliable. Answer: First, Poll A used random digit dialing (reaching both cell and landline users), a probability-based sampling method that gives every adult an equal chance of selection. Poll B used an online opt-in panel, which is not random—only people who chose to participate are included, creating selection bias. Second, Poll A asked a neutral question ("Do you favor or oppose raising the federal minimum wage to $15 per hour?") that presents the policy without loaded language. Poll B asked a leading question framing the policy as helping "working families make ends meet"—sympathetic language that predicts higher support by activating compassion rather than measuring genuine opinion. Additionally, Poll A had a larger sample (1,500 vs. 600) and therefore a smaller margin of error (±2.5% vs. ±4%).

5. Explain why polling averages are more reliable than individual polls, but also identify one significant limitation of aggregation. Answer: Polling averages are more reliable because they combine results from multiple independent polls, reducing the influence of any single outlier. If one poll has an unusually high or low result due to chance, the average pulls toward the true value. Aggregation also weights polls by their sample size, methodology quality, and historical accuracy, giving more influence to more reliable polls. The significant limitation is that aggregation cannot correct systematic bias shared across all polls. If, for example, all polls consistently undercount a particular demographic group—say, rural voters without reliable phone or internet access—averaging them together will not fix the problem. Every poll in the average shares the same blind spot, so the average inherits the same systematic bias at greater apparent confidence.

Summary

Evaluating public opinion data requires distinguishing between sampling error (expected, measurable, reducible by larger samples) and non-sampling error (coverage, nonresponse, measurement, and weighting flaws that cannot be fixed by larger samples). Critical evaluation of any poll requires examining who conducted it, how the sample was selected, what questions were asked, when it was taken, and what population it claims to measure. Polling averages reduce the influence of single-poll outliers but cannot correct shared systematic biases. On the AP exam, quantitative analysis FRQs require describing trends, drawing conclusions tied to political principles, and identifying data limitations—skills directly applicable to real-world evaluation of the polls that fill political coverage.
