Study Guide: Topic 2.10 - The Court in Action

Key Concepts

Writ of Certiorari is a formal order directing a lower court to send the record of a case for review by the Supreme Court. The certiorari process is discretionary - the Court is not obligated to hear any case.

Rule of Four is the Supreme Court's internal practice requiring at least four justices to vote to hear a case before certiorari is granted. Ensures a substantial minority considers the legal question important.

Factors for Granting Certiorari: circuit split (conflicting circuit decisions), constitutional significance, government involvement (Solicitor General petitions granted at high rates), and numerous amicus curiae briefs signaling broad significance.

Oral Arguments are formal hearings in which attorneys present arguments and justices ask probing questions. Each side receives about 30 minutes. Not presentations - intense dialogues where justices probe reasoning through hypotheticals.

Conference is the private meeting of all nine justices after oral arguments. No clerks or recording devices. Chief Justice presents each case; justices vote in seniority order. Chief assigns majority opinion if in the majority.

Majority Opinion is the opinion joined by five or more justices; the binding legal rule lower courts must follow. Establishes precedent.

Concurring Opinion is written by a justice who agrees with the result but has different reasoning. Does not carry precedential force.

Dissenting Opinion is written by a justice who disagrees with the majority. No legal force but articulates alternatives and may lay groundwork for future reconsideration.

Plurality Opinion - when no single opinion commands a majority, the opinion joined by the most justices controls. Creates weaker precedent.

Stare Decisis (let the decision stand) is the principle that courts should follow precedents established by prior decisions. Promotes stability. Not absolute - the Court can overrule precedents when egregiously wrong.

Amicus Curiae are friend of the court briefs filed by non-parties to inform the Court about broader implications.

Key Takeaways

  • The Supreme Court receives 7,000-8,000 petitions per term but hears only about 70-80 cases - the ability to choose which questions to answer is itself a form of agenda-setting power.
  • Denial of certiorari does not mean the Court agrees with the lower court's decision - it simply declines to review it.
  • Oral arguments are intense dialogues, not presentations - justices probe reasoning through hypotheticals and pointed questions.
  • The assignment of opinion writing is a significant power: the opinion author shapes the legal reasoning and scope of the precedent.
  • Dissenting opinions have no legal force but serve important functions - articulating alternatives and sometimes laying groundwork for future reversal.
  • Stare decisis promotes stability but is not absolute; the Court has overruled major precedents including Plessy, Lochner, and Roe.

AP Exam Connection

Topic 2.10 is highly testable. Expect questions distinguishing the types of opinions and explaining the significance of each. The certiorari process and rule of four are common Concept Application FRQ topics. Stare decisis appears frequently connecting Marbury (Unit 1) to later overrulings like Brown overruling Plessy. Know how to explain why a dissenting opinion matters even though it has no legal force.
