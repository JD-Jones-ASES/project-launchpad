Study Guide: Topic 2.15 - Policy and the Branches of Government

Key Concepts

Public Policy is the set of government actions and deliberate inactions that address public problems. Develops through a complex, iterative process involving all three branches, interest groups, the media, and the public.

Five Stages of the Policy Process:

  1. Agenda setting: Problem identified and placed on the government's agenda. Influenced by media, interest groups, public opinion, focusing events, and political leaders.
  2. Policy formulation: Proposals developed through research, debate, and drafting by congressional committees, White House staff, agencies, think tanks, and interest groups.
  3. Policy adoption: Specific proposal enacted through legislation (bicameralism + presentment), executive action (executive orders, regulations), or judicial decisions.
  4. Policy implementation: Bureaucracy carries out adopted policy - interprets the law, writes regulations, allocates resources. Decisions here can significantly alter policy effects from congressional intent.
  5. Policy evaluation: Policy's effectiveness assessed by Congress (GAO, oversight hearings), executive (agency reviews), researchers, media, and interest groups. Findings may lead to revision, expansion, or termination.

Legislative Process Key Features:

  • Multiple veto points: A bill must survive committee review, floor votes in both chambers, conference committee, and presidential action. Bias toward the status quo makes major change difficult.
  • Committee gatekeeping: Most bills die in committee - never reaching a floor vote.
  • Conference committees: Reconcile differences when House and Senate pass different versions.
  • Presidential action: Sign, veto, or take no action. If Congress is in session and president takes no action for 10 days, the bill becomes law without signature.

Policy Feedback Loops - the cyclical nature of the policy process. Implementation and evaluation of existing policies influence agenda setting and formulation of new policies. The ACA (2010) generated new political debates; Social Security created a constituency making benefit reductions nearly impossible.

Divided Government - when different political parties control different branches or chambers. The post-WWII historical norm.

Gridlock - legislative paralysis due to partisan disagreement. May produce government shutdowns, continuing resolutions, and greater reliance on executive orders as substitutes for legislation.

Bipartisan Cooperation - divided government does not always produce gridlock. Some landmark legislation (Civil Rights Act 1964, Clean Air Act amendments 1990, welfare reform 1996) passed under divided government when bipartisan coalitions formed.

Key Takeaways

  • The policy process is neither linear nor controlled by a single actor - it is iterative, cyclical, and involves all three branches plus outside actors.
  • The legislative process has multiple veto points creating a structural bias toward the status quo - major change is difficult.
  • Policy implementation by the bureaucracy is often the most consequential stage - decisions about how to interpret and apply policy can significantly alter real-world effects.
  • Policy feedback loops ensure that policy-making is ongoing, not a one-time event - existing policies create constituencies and consequences that shape future policy debates.
  • Divided government is the historical norm in the post-WWII era; gridlock is frequent but not inevitable.
  • Executive orders and agency regulations serve as policy substitutes when Congress cannot legislate, expanding executive policy-making during periods of gridlock.
