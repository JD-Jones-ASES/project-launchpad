Study Guide: Topic 2.14 - Holding the Bureaucracy Accountable

Key Concepts

Congressional Oversight is Congress's most extensive mechanism for bureaucratic accountability, exercised through hearings and investigations (agency officials testify under oath; committees can subpoena documents), the power of the purse (control over the federal budget - most powerful tool), reauthorization (periodic renewal requiring review), and confirmation power (extracting commitments from nominees).

Government Accountability Office (GAO) is a nonpartisan congressional agency that audits federal programs, investigates waste and fraud, and evaluates whether agencies achieve their statutory objectives.

Presidential Control through appointment and removal (heads of executive departments serve at the president's pleasure), executive orders (directing agency priorities), OMB/OIRA regulatory review (centralized review of significant proposed regulations), and reorganization proposals.

OIRA (Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs) within the OMB reviews significant proposed regulations before publication, giving the White House a mechanism to ensure regulations align with presidential priorities.

Judicial Review of Agency Actions - under the APA, courts review whether agencies acted within statutory authority, followed required procedures, made non-arbitrary and capricious decisions, and interpreted their mandates reasonably.

Whistleblowers are government employees or contractors who report waste, fraud, abuse, or illegality. Protected by the Whistleblower Protection Act (1989, amended 2012). Serve as an important internal check on misconduct.

Inspectors General (IGs) are independent watchdogs embedded within federal agencies, established by the Inspector General Act of 1978. Conduct audits, investigations, and inspections. Report to both the agency head and Congress.

Key Takeaways

  • Bureaucratic accountability is addressed through multiple overlapping mechanisms: congressional oversight, presidential control, judicial review, whistleblowers, and inspectors general.
  • The power of the purse is Congress's most powerful oversight tool - the threat of budget cuts can shape agency behavior even without formal legislation.
  • Presidential control through appointment, executive orders, and OIRA gives the White House significant ability to direct agency priorities, but independent regulatory commissions are partially insulated.
  • Courts review agency actions under the APA for procedural compliance and rationality - the arbitrary and capricious standard allows courts to strike down poorly reasoned regulations.
  • Historical oversight hearings (Watergate, Iran-Contra, 9/11 Commission) demonstrate how congressional investigation can hold even the president accountable.

AP Exam Connection

Topic 2.14 connects checks and balances to the bureaucracy - a key AP exam theme. Expect FRQ prompts asking you to identify specific mechanisms Congress uses to oversee agencies, or to explain how multiple branches check bureaucratic power. The power of the purse is frequently tested. Know the difference between the GAO (legislative watchdog) and OIRA (executive watchdog).
