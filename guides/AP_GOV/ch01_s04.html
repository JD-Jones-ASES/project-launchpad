Study Guide: Topic 1.4 — Challenges of the Articles of Confederation

Key Concepts

  • The Articles of Confederation (1781) were America's first governing document — deliberately weak by design because the revolutionary generation distrusted centralized power after their experience with British rule.
  • Structural weaknesses of the Articles included a unicameral legislature with no executive branch and no judicial branch, meaning no one could enforce laws or resolve disputes between states.
  • Fiscal and regulatory failures — no power to tax, no power to regulate interstate commerce, no standing army — left the national government perpetually broke, commercially chaotic, and militarily helpless.
  • Shays' Rebellion (1786–1787) was the armed uprising that demonstrated the Articles' fatal weakness in dramatic fashion: the national government could not raise an army to suppress a domestic insurrection, shocking political leaders into demanding constitutional reform.
  • The road to Philadelphia began with the Annapolis Convention (1786) and culminated in the Constitutional Convention (1787), where delegates quickly abandoned the Articles and drafted an entirely new constitution.

Vocabulary Review

Articles of Confederation: America's first constitution, drafted in 1777 and ratified in 1781, which created a deliberately weak central government as a "firm league of friendship" among thirteen sovereign states rather than a unified nation.

Unicameral: Having only one legislative chamber. The Articles created a unicameral Congress of the Confederation in which each state had one vote regardless of population, meaning tiny Delaware had equal power to populous Virginia.

Shays' Rebellion: An armed uprising of farmers in western Massachusetts (1786–1787), led by former Continental Army captain Daniel Shays, who closed courts to prevent debt foreclosures. The national government's inability to respond exposed the Articles' fatal weaknesses.

Interstate commerce: Trade and commercial exchange that crosses state lines. Under the Articles, states could impose their own tariffs and trade restrictions on interstate goods, creating economic chaos and interstate rivalries that undermined national prosperity.

Annapolis Convention (1786): A meeting of delegates from five states called to discuss interstate commerce problems. Finding their numbers insufficient, delegates issued a call for a broader constitutional convention in Philadelphia.

Congress of the Confederation: The unicameral legislative body established by the Articles of Confederation; each state had one vote, and major legislation required nine of thirteen states.

Important Details

  • The Articles reflected the revolutionary generation's fear of tyrannical central government — they had just fought a war against the British Crown and were determined not to create a similar threat at home.
  • Under the Articles, Delaware (smallest state) had the same one vote in Congress as Virginia (most populous) — a structural imbalance that represented state sovereignty but produced governance problems.
  • The national government could not directly tax citizens — it could only "requisition" (request) funds from states, which routinely ignored or underpaid these requests. By 1786, the national treasury was essentially empty.
  • New York taxed goods from New Jersey and Connecticut. States along the Mississippi River fought over navigation rights. This commercial chaos illustrated why interstate commerce regulation was urgently needed.
  • Congress required nine of thirteen states to pass major legislation — a supermajority threshold that made basic governance difficult.
  • Amending the Articles required unanimous consent of all thirteen state legislatures — a single state could block any reform, however widely supported.
  • Shays' Rebellion: About 1,500 armed farmers closed courts from August 1786. In January 1787, they attempted to seize the federal arsenal at Springfield. Massachusetts suppressed the uprising with a privately funded militia — not a national army.
  • George Washington wrote that Shays' Rebellion proved there were "combustibles in every State, which a spark might set fire to" — expressing the fear that the entire republic might dissolve.
  • The Annapolis Convention (September 1786) was attended by delegates from only five states — too few to accomplish meaningful reform — but it issued the call that led to Philadelphia.
  • Congress officially authorized the Philadelphia Convention only to revise the Articles; delegates exceeded this mandate by drafting an entirely new constitution.

Review Questions

1. Identify three structural weaknesses of the Articles of Confederation and explain how each undermined effective governance.

Answer: First, no power to tax: Congress could only request funds from states, which routinely ignored these requisitions. The national government was perpetually broke, unable to pay war debts, fund an army, or maintain operations. Second, no executive branch: Without a chief executive, laws Congress passed depended entirely on voluntary state compliance. There was no mechanism to compel states to follow national policy. Third, unanimous consent for amendments: Any change to the Articles required all thirteen states to agree, meaning a single state could block reforms supported by the other twelve. In practice, this made the Articles virtually impossible to improve even when their deficiencies were obvious.

2. Why was Shays' Rebellion significant? How did it demonstrate the inadequacy of the Articles?

Answer: Shays' Rebellion (1786–1787) was a crisis that exposed the Articles' fatal weakness: the national government was completely powerless to respond to a domestic insurrection. When debt-crushed farmers in Massachusetts closed courts and attempted to seize a federal arsenal, Congress could not raise an army — it had no taxing power to fund one and no authority to compel states to contribute troops. Massachusetts had to suppress the rebellion with a privately funded militia. This demonstrated that the "national" government could not protect order even within a single state. The rebellion alarmed leaders nationwide, including George Washington, and provided the urgency needed to convene the Philadelphia Convention. It illustrated that a government that could not enforce its will — even in a domestic crisis — could not survive.

3. Why did the Articles require unanimous consent for amendments, and why did this prove to be a fatal flaw?

Answer: The unanimous consent requirement reflected the revolutionary generation's view that the thirteen states were sovereign entities voluntarily participating in a "league of friendship." Because each state was theoretically equal and sovereign, any change to the compact required all to agree. This was meant to protect smaller states from being overridden by larger ones. However, it proved fatal because practical governance requires the ability to adapt and reform. Any state with an interest in preserving the status quo — or simply a contentious legislature — could single-handedly block reforms that twelve others desperately needed. This produced a government that could identify its own deficiencies but was constitutionally incapable of fixing them. The framers of the new Constitution recognized this flaw and required only three-fourths of states for ratification of amendments.

4. What does Article II of the Articles of Confederation reveal about the fundamental nature of that government?

Answer: Article II states that "each state retains its sovereignty, freedom, and independence, and every power, jurisdiction, and right, which is not by this Confederation expressly delegated to the United States." This reveals that the Articles created not a national government but a confederacy of sovereign states. The word "expressly" sharply limited national power — only specifically listed powers belonged to the national government, nothing more. States retained their full sovereignty. This is the opposite of the Constitution's structure, where the Necessary and Proper Clause allows implied powers. The contrast explains why the Articles' government was so weak: it was deliberately designed to be an agent of the states, not a sovereign authority in its own right.

5. Why did the delegates at the Constitutional Convention exceed their mandate, and what does this reveal about the severity of the Articles' problems?

Answer: Congress had authorized the Philadelphia Convention only to "revise the Articles of Confederation." Yet within the first two weeks, delegates voted to abandon the Articles entirely and draft a new constitution — a decision that was, by any reading, revolutionary. Delegates exceeded their mandate because the problems with the Articles were so fundamental that revision was impossible; they required replacement. The core issues — no executive, no judiciary, no taxing power, no commerce regulation, unanimous amendment requirement — were not bugs that could be patched. They were features of a deliberately weak government that had proved incapable of governing. The delegates recognized that tinkering would not be enough. Their willingness to exceed their authority demonstrates both the severity of the crisis and the political courage required to address it.

Summary

The Articles of Confederation created America's first national government as a deliberately weak confederacy of sovereign states, reflecting the revolutionary generation's deep distrust of centralized power. Critical structural weaknesses — no power to tax, no executive to enforce laws, no judiciary to resolve disputes, and unanimous amendment requirements — made effective governance impossible. By 1786, the national treasury was empty, interstate commerce was chaotic, and the government was powerless to defend itself. Shays' Rebellion (1786–1787) proved the breaking point, demonstrating that the national government could not even suppress a domestic insurrection. The Annapolis Convention (1786) issued the call that led to Philadelphia, where fifty-five delegates did far more than revise the Articles — they replaced them entirely with a new constitutional framework that addressed the fundamental failures of America's first government.
