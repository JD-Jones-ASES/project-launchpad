Study Guide: Topic 3.1 — The Bill of Rights

Key Concepts

  • The Bill of Rights (Amendments 1–10) was added to the Constitution in 1791 as a compromise to win Anti-Federalist support for ratification, providing explicit protections for individual rights against federal government overreach.
  • Originally, the Bill of Rights restricted only the federal government; the Supreme Court ruled in Barron v. Baltimore (1833) that it did not apply to state governments.
  • The Fourteenth Amendment (1868), specifically its Due Process Clause, became the legal vehicle for applying Bill of Rights protections to state and local governments through a doctrine called selective incorporation.
  • Selective incorporation is a case-by-case process: the Supreme Court evaluates each right individually, incorporating it against the states only if it is deemed "fundamental to the American scheme of justice."
  • Not all Bill of Rights provisions have been incorporated; the Third Amendment, Fifth Amendment grand jury requirement, and Seventh Amendment civil jury guarantee remain unincorporated at the Supreme Court level.

Vocabulary Review

  • Bill of Rights: The first ten amendments to the U.S. Constitution, ratified December 15, 1791, establishing fundamental protections for individual liberties against federal government action.
  • Selective incorporation: The doctrine by which the Supreme Court applies individual Bill of Rights provisions to state governments through the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause, evaluating each right separately.
  • Total incorporation: The rejected theory (advocated by Justice Hugo Black) that the Fourteenth Amendment automatically applied all Bill of Rights protections to the states at once.
  • Due Process Clause: The provision of the Fourteenth Amendment stating that no state shall "deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law," which serves as the constitutional vehicle for selective incorporation.
  • Barron v. Baltimore (1833): The Supreme Court decision holding that the Bill of Rights applied only to the federal government, not to state governments.
  • Fundamental right: A right deemed so important to ordered liberty that it must be incorporated against the states; the standard the Supreme Court uses to evaluate whether to apply a Bill of Rights provision to state governments.
  • Anti-Federalists: Opponents of ratification of the Constitution who demanded a bill of rights as a condition for their support; their insistence led directly to the addition of the first ten amendments.

Important Details

  • Madison initially opposed a bill of rights as unnecessary since the federal government had only enumerated powers, but he drafted the amendments to secure ratification.
  • The Fourteenth Amendment (1868) was ratified following the Civil War to protect freed slaves; its Due Process Clause's word "liberty" became the constitutional hook for incorporation.
  • Key incorporation milestones: Gitlow v. New York (1925) incorporated free speech; Mapp v. Ohio (1961) incorporated the exclusionary rule; Gideon v. Wainwright (1963) incorporated the right to counsel; McDonald v. Chicago (2010) incorporated the Second Amendment right to bear arms.
  • Timbs v. Indiana (2019) incorporated the Eighth Amendment's excessive fines prohibition, demonstrating that incorporation continues into the modern era.
  • Without selective incorporation, states could theoretically establish official religions, censor the press, or deny criminal defendants attorneys — actions that now violate the federal Constitution as applied to the states.

Review Questions

1. Why did Anti-Federalists demand the addition of a Bill of Rights to the Constitution? Anti-Federalists feared that the new federal government was too powerful and lacked explicit protections for individual rights. They worried that without written guarantees, the government could restrict speech, religion, and other liberties. Several state ratifying conventions approved the Constitution only on the condition that amendments protecting individual rights would be promptly added.

2. What did Barron v. Baltimore (1833) decide, and why was it significant? Barron held that the Fifth Amendment's takings clause — and by extension the entire Bill of Rights — applied only to the federal government, not to state governments. This was significant because it meant that for most of the nineteenth century, states could restrict individual rights without violating the federal Constitution, leaving citizens without federal protection against state abuses.

3. How does the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause enable selective incorporation? The Due Process Clause states that no state may deprive any person of "life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." The Supreme Court interpreted "liberty" to include specific rights enumerated in the Bill of Rights. If a right is deemed fundamental, the clause makes it applicable to state and local governments, effectively incorporating it against the states.

4. Explain the difference between selective incorporation and total incorporation. Selective incorporation evaluates each Bill of Rights provision individually, applying it to the states only if it is "fundamental to the American scheme of justice." Total incorporation — the position advocated by Justice Hugo Black but never adopted by the Court — would apply all Bill of Rights protections to the states simultaneously through the Fourteenth Amendment. The Court has consistently chosen the selective approach.

5. Name two Bill of Rights provisions that remain unincorporated and explain why their status matters. The Fifth Amendment's grand jury indictment requirement and the Seventh Amendment's civil jury trial guarantee remain unincorporated. Their unincorporated status means that states are not required to provide grand juries for state felony indictments or jury trials in civil cases under the federal constitutional standard. States may choose to offer these protections but are not federally compelled to do so.

Summary

The Bill of Rights, ratified in 1791, established fundamental protections against federal government overreach but originally did not apply to state governments. Through the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause, the Supreme Court has gradually applied most of these protections to state and local governments through the doctrine of selective incorporation — a case-by-case process spanning more than a century. Not all provisions have been incorporated, but the practical effect of the doctrine is to make the Bill of Rights a comprehensive framework of individual liberty that constrains government at every level.
