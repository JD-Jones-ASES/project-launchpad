Study Guide: Changes in Ideology

AP US Government and Politics — Unit 4, Topic 4.3

Key Concepts

  • Realignment is a durable, large-scale shift in party coalitions, typically triggered by a critical election; the 1960s–1980s Southern realignment is the most consequential modern example.
  • The Southern realignment transformed both parties: Democrats became uniformly liberal, Republicans became uniformly conservative, ending the era of conservative Democrats and liberal Republicans.
  • Polarization is the growing ideological distance between the parties; its causes include partisan media, geographic sorting, primary elections that reward ideological purity, and gerrymandering.
  • Dealignment occurs when voters disengage from both parties and identify as independents; many "independents" are actually partisan leaners who consistently vote for one party.
  • Ideological change can be gradual (generational replacement) or sudden (triggered by crises like wars or economic collapses).

Vocabulary Review

Term Definition
Ideology A coherent set of beliefs about the proper role of government and the values that should guide policy
Realignment A significant, lasting shift in the electoral coalition of one or both major parties, often following a critical election
Critical election An election that reveals a new, durable pattern of party loyalty, often signaling a realignment
Southern Strategy Richard Nixon's 1968/1972 Republican appeal to racial conservatives and states' rights advocates in the South, accelerating white Southern migration to the GOP
New Deal coalition FDR's coalition of Southern whites, Northern labor unions, urban ethnic groups, African Americans, and progressives that gave Democrats dominance from the 1930s–1960s
Polarization The growing ideological distance between the two major parties and their supporters, reducing overlap and cross-partisan cooperation
Dealignment A process in which voters disengage from both parties and identify as independents rather than switching from one party to the other
Gerrymandering Drawing legislative district boundaries to favor one party; creates "safe seats" that reward ideological purity in primaries

Important Details

How Ideological Change Happens

Gradual change — New generations replace older ones with different formative experiences and priorities. Each generation's political views were shaped by different events, so as older cohorts age out and younger ones grow in, the overall ideological balance shifts.

Sudden change — Crises (wars, economic collapses, social upheavals) rapidly reshape what Americans expect from government. The Great Depression dramatically expanded public support for government intervention within a few years.

The Southern Realignment (1960s–1980s)

Before: The Solid South was reliably Democratic for nearly a century after the Civil War. Southern whites associated Republicans with Reconstruction.

Trigger: President Lyndon B. Johnson signed the Civil Rights Act (1964) and Voting Rights Act (1965), alienating many white Southern voters who felt the Democratic Party had abandoned them.

Republican strategy: Nixon's Southern Strategy explicitly appealed to racial conservatives and states' rights advocates through coded messaging. Over two decades, white Southern voters migrated to the GOP while African Americans consolidated behind Democrats.

Result: Both parties became more ideologically homogeneous:

  • Democrats became uniformly liberal
  • Republicans became uniformly conservative
  • Intra-party diversity (liberal Republicans, conservative Democrats) faded

Causes of Polarization

Cause How It Contributes to Polarization
Partisan media Cable news, talk radio, social media allow people to consume only information reinforcing existing views
Geographic sorting Americans increasingly live among ideological neighbors, reducing cross-partisan contact
Primary elections Primary voters are more ideologically extreme; candidates must adopt more partisan positions to win nomination
Gerrymandering Safe seats make the primary (not the general election) the real contest, rewarding ideological purity

Pew Research Polarization Data (1994–2017)

The ideological distance between median Democrat and median Republican more than doubled between 1994 and 2017, as both parties moved away from the center in opposite directions. In 1994 the gap was 0.07 on a –1 to +1 scale; by 2017 it had grown to 0.30.

Dealignment vs. Realignment

  • Dealignment: Voters disengage from party politics entirely, increasing self-identified "independents." Share of self-described independents has grown steadily.
  • Reality: Many "independents" are "leaners"—they consistently vote for the same party. True independents who split their votes are a smaller group.

Review Questions

1. Define realignment and explain what typically triggers it. Answer: Realignment is a significant, lasting shift in the electoral coalition of one or both major parties. It typically follows a critical election—a contest that reveals a new, durable pattern of party loyalty. Realignments are often triggered by major crises or policy changes that force voters to choose new party homes based on their values and interests.

2. How did the civil rights movement contribute to the Southern realignment? Answer: The Democratic Party had held white Southern voters as a core coalition partner since the Civil War era through the New Deal. When President Johnson signed the Civil Rights Act (1964) and Voting Rights Act (1965), many white Southern voters felt the party had abandoned their values and support for racial segregation. Republican strategists, particularly Nixon in 1968 and 1972, pursued the "Southern Strategy"—appealing to racial conservatives and states' rights advocates. Over the following decades, white Southern voters migrated to the Republican Party in large numbers, fundamentally transforming both parties.

3. Identify two causes of increased polarization and explain how each contributes to the trend. Answer: First, partisan media (cable news, talk radio, social media) allows citizens to consume only information that confirms their existing beliefs—a phenomenon called selective exposure. This reduces exposure to opposing viewpoints and makes people's views more extreme and entrenched. Second, primary elections reward ideological purity because primary voters tend to be more ideologically extreme than general election voters. Candidates who moderate their positions risk losing their primary to a more extreme challenger. Over time, this incentive system pushes party officeholders away from the center, increasing the gap between the parties.

4. What is the difference between realignment and dealignment? Answer: Realignment occurs when voters switch from one party to the other in a lasting way, reshaping party coalitions. Dealignment occurs when voters disengage from both parties and identify as independents rather than switching to the other side. Realignment changes which party people support; dealignment involves withdrawing from party identification altogether. In recent decades, dealignment has increased the share of self-identified independents, though many are really partisan leaners who consistently vote for the same party.

5. How does geographic sorting contribute to political polarization? Answer: Geographic sorting means Americans increasingly live in communities where most neighbors share their political views—liberal-leaning cities and suburbs, conservative-leaning rural areas and exurbs. This reduces daily cross-partisan interaction, making people less familiar with and more suspicious of the other side's views. When you rarely encounter people who think differently in everyday life, the opposing party can more easily become an abstract "enemy" rather than neighbors with different but understandable views. Geographic sorting also reinforces partisan media consumption and makes redistricting easier to produce ideologically homogeneous districts.

Summary

American political ideology is not static—it shifts in response to historical events, demographic change, and strategic political decisions. The most consequential modern ideological shift was the Southern realignment of the 1960s–1980s, triggered by civil rights legislation and accelerated by the Republican Southern Strategy, which converted the Solid South from Democratic to Republican and made both parties more internally uniform. Today, polarization—the growing ideological distance between parties—is driven by partisan media, geographic sorting, primary election incentives, and gerrymandering. Some voters respond to polarization through dealignment, identifying as independents, though many "independents" are actually consistent partisan leaners.
