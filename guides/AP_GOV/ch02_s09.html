Study Guide: Topic 2.9 - The Role of the Judicial Branch

Key Concepts

Judicial Review is the power of courts to declare laws and executive actions unconstitutional. Established in Marbury v. Madison (1803) when Chief Justice Marshall declared it is emphatically the province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is.

Judicial Activism describes a willingness to strike down laws, overrule precedent, and interpret the Constitution expansively. Activists argue courts must protect rights even against popular majorities. Not synonymous with liberal ideology.

Judicial Restraint describes a reluctance to overturn laws or deviate from precedent; deference to elected branches on policy questions. Not synonymous with conservative ideology.

Counter-Majoritarian Institution - the Supreme Court's unelected, life-tenured members can overrule decisions of democratically elected majorities. This raises the fundamental question: in a democracy, who should have the final say?

Legitimacy - the judiciary's power rests on public acceptance of its authority to make binding legal decisions. Unlike other branches, courts have no independent enforcement power.

Tyranny of the Majority - Alexis de Tocqueville's concept that democratic majorities can oppress minorities. The counter-majoritarian Court serves as a structural check on this tendency.

Key Takeaways

  • Judicial review, though not in the Constitution's text, is established by Marbury v. Madison and is the Court's most consequential power.
  • The Court exercises judicial review sparingly - the vast majority of laws are never challenged, and most challenged laws are upheld.
  • Judicial activism and judicial restraint are approaches to how aggressively courts exercise their power - not partisan labels.
  • The Court's counter-majoritarian role is both a strength (protecting minority rights) and a concern (unelected judges overruling democratic majorities).
  • The judiciary's power depends entirely on legitimacy - if the public or other branches refuse to comply, the Court cannot enforce its decisions.
  • Brown v. Board required federal troops to enforce: compliance is not automatic.

AP Exam Connection

Topic 2.9 revisits Marbury v. Madison in the context of judicial power. Expect FRQ prompts asking you to explain the counter-majoritarian nature of the Court - including both the defense (protection of minority rights) and the criticism (democratic accountability). The concept of legitimacy as the basis of judicial power (Federalist No. 78) is a high-frequency exam topic. Be prepared to provide historical examples of resistance to court decisions.
