Study Guide: Topic 3.6 — Balancing Liberty and Public Safety

Key Concepts

  • Civil liberties are not absolute; the government can restrict individual freedoms when it can demonstrate a sufficiently important justification, evaluated through varying levels of judicial scrutiny.
  • Strict scrutiny is the most rigorous standard: the government must show a compelling interest, a narrowly tailored restriction, and the least restrictive means available. It is very difficult to satisfy.
  • Throughout American history, national security crises have prompted the government to curtail civil liberties — sometimes later repudiated as unconstitutional or unjust (e.g., Japanese American internment).
  • The USA PATRIOT Act (2001) significantly expanded government surveillance authority after 9/11; the 2013 Snowden revelations exposed bulk metadata collection, sparking national debate; the USA FREEDOM Act (2015) curtailed some programs.
  • The government's police power enables restrictions on personal freedom for public health purposes, as upheld in Jacobson v. Massachusetts (1905), but remains subject to constitutional limits.

Vocabulary Review

  • Strict scrutiny: The highest level of judicial review, applied to restrictions on fundamental rights or classifications based on race/ethnicity. The government must show a compelling interest, a narrowly tailored law, and the least restrictive means available.
  • Intermediate scrutiny: The middle level of judicial review, applied to sex-based classifications; the government must show an important interest and a law substantially related to achieving it.
  • Rational basis review: The lowest level of judicial review, applied to most other laws; the government need only show a legitimate interest and a rational relationship.
  • Police power: The government's broad authority to regulate behavior for the health, safety, morals, and general welfare of the public.
  • USA PATRIOT Act (2001): Post-9/11 legislation expanding federal surveillance powers, including roving wiretaps, access to business records under Section 215, and National Security Letters.
  • Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court (FISA Court): A specialized federal court that reviews government applications for surveillance warrants in national security cases.
  • National Security Letters (NSLs): FBI demands for records from telecommunications companies and financial institutions that do not require judicial approval.
  • USA FREEDOM Act (2015): Legislation that ended bulk metadata collection under Section 215 and required more targeted government data requests.
  • Korematsu v. United States (1944): Controversial Supreme Court case upholding Japanese American internment during World War II; later repudiated by the Court.

Important Details

  • Strict scrutiny is sometimes described as "strict in theory, fatal in fact" because so few government actions survive its demanding requirements. When a court applies strict scrutiny, the government almost always loses.
  • Japanese American internment (Executive Order 9066, 1942) affected approximately 120,000 people. The Supreme Court upheld the internment in Korematsu (1944), applying what it claimed was strict scrutiny. The decision is now widely regarded as one of the Court's most serious errors, and the Trump v. Hawaii (2018) decision formally repudiated Korematsu.
  • Edward Snowden's 2013 revelations about NSA bulk data collection under Section 215 of the PATRIOT Act demonstrated the scale of surveillance: the government was collecting call metadata on millions of ordinary Americans without individualized suspicion.
  • Jacobson v. Massachusetts (1905) upheld a compulsory smallpox vaccination law, establishing the principle that individual liberty may be constrained "under the pressure of great dangers" when the safety of the general public demands it. The case has been cited in debates over pandemic-era restrictions.
  • The tension between liberty and security is not just legal — it is political: public opinion about acceptable levels of surveillance and restriction shifts significantly in the aftermath of major crises.

Review Questions

1. What must the government prove to satisfy strict scrutiny when restricting a fundamental right? To satisfy strict scrutiny, the government must prove three things: (1) the restriction serves a compelling government interest — an interest of the highest order, not merely convenience or preference; (2) the law is narrowly tailored to achieve that compelling interest — it targets the problem precisely and does not sweep up more liberty than necessary; and (3) the law uses the least restrictive means available — there is no less liberty-infringing alternative that would achieve the same goal.

2. Why is Japanese American internment during World War II considered a cautionary example of security-liberty tradeoffs? Executive Order 9066 (1942) forcibly relocated and imprisoned approximately 120,000 Japanese Americans — most of them U.S. citizens — based solely on their ancestry. No evidence was required of individual disloyalty. The Supreme Court upheld the policy in Korematsu (1944). Decades later, Congress apologized and paid reparations to survivors. The case illustrates how wartime fear can produce government actions that — when viewed with later reflection — represent serious violations of civil liberties and equal protection.

3. How did the USA PATRIOT Act expand government surveillance authority after 9/11, and what concerns did critics raise? The PATRIOT Act, enacted 45 days after the 9/11 attacks, granted sweeping new surveillance powers: Section 215 allowed the FBI to obtain "any tangible things" relevant to terrorism investigations from the FISA Court; National Security Letters permitted demands for records without judicial approval; roving wiretaps authorized surveillance across multiple devices. Critics argued these provisions violated the Fourth Amendment's protections against unreasonable searches because they enabled broad surveillance without individualized suspicion — collecting information on millions of ordinary Americans rather than specific suspects.

4. What did the 2013 Snowden revelations reveal, and how did Congress respond? Edward Snowden revealed that the NSA had been collecting metadata on millions of Americans' phone calls — who called whom, when, and for how long — under Section 215 of the PATRIOT Act, without individualized suspicion. This mass surveillance program applied to ordinary Americans, not just suspected terrorists. The revelations sparked a national debate about privacy, civil liberties, and government oversight. Congress responded by passing the USA FREEDOM Act (2015), which ended bulk metadata collection and required the government to make more targeted requests based on specific selection terms.

5. What principle did Jacobson v. Massachusetts (1905) establish about the relationship between individual liberty and public health? Jacobson established that individual liberty is not absolute and that the government's police power permits restrictions on personal freedom when the safety of the general public demands it. The Court upheld a state compulsory vaccination law, holding that liberty may be "subjected to such restraint as the safety of the general public may demand" under the pressure of great dangers. This principle has been foundational in subsequent public health law and was cited in debates over pandemic-related restrictions on businesses, gatherings, and movement.

Summary

The tension between civil liberties and public safety has been a recurring theme throughout American history. The government's power to restrict fundamental rights is constrained by the doctrine of strict scrutiny, which requires compelling justifications and narrowly tailored means. Historical episodes — from the Alien and Sedition Acts to Japanese internment to post-9/11 surveillance — illustrate how national security crises can lead to restrictions later viewed as excessive. The ongoing challenge for American democracy is determining how much liberty must be sacrificed in the name of security, a balance that courts, Congress, and public opinion continue to renegotiate.
