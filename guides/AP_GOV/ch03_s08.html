Study Guide: Topic 3.8 — Due Process and Rights of the Accused

Key Concepts

  • The Fourth, Fifth, and Sixth Amendments collectively protect individuals accused of crimes against government overreach — reflecting the Founders' deep suspicion of arbitrary government power.
  • The Fourth Amendment's exclusionary rule (extended to states in Mapp v. Ohio, 1961) prevents government from using illegally obtained evidence, deterring police misconduct.
  • The Fifth Amendment protects against self-incrimination (the basis for Miranda warnings), double jeopardy, and takings without just compensation.
  • The Sixth Amendment guarantees a speedy and public trial, an impartial jury, the right to confront witnesses, and — most significantly through Gideon v. Wainwright (1963) — the right to counsel for felony defendants.
  • Gideon v. Wainwright established that the right to an attorney is a fundamental right, creating the public defender system that exists throughout the United States today.

Vocabulary Review

  • Due process: The constitutional guarantee that government must follow fair procedures before depriving a person of life, liberty, or property.
  • Exclusionary rule: The doctrine that evidence obtained through an unconstitutional search or seizure is inadmissible in court; the "fruit of the poisonous tree." Established in Weeks v. United States (1914) for federal courts; extended to state courts in Mapp v. Ohio (1961).
  • Probable cause: The standard required for police to obtain a search or arrest warrant; "a reasonable basis to believe" that evidence of a crime will be found or that the suspect committed the crime.
  • Miranda warnings: Required police advisories, from Miranda v. Arizona (1966): the right to remain silent, that anything said can be used against the suspect, the right to an attorney, and that one will be appointed if the suspect cannot afford one.
  • Self-incrimination: The Fifth Amendment right to refuse to be a witness against oneself in a criminal case; the basis for the right to remain silent.
  • Double jeopardy: The Fifth Amendment prohibition on being tried twice for the same offense by the same sovereign after acquittal; does not prevent separate state and federal prosecutions.
  • Eminent domain: The government's power to take private property for public use; the Fifth Amendment requires "just compensation" for any taking.
  • Exclusionary rule exceptions: Circumstances where illegally obtained evidence may still be used: good-faith exception, inevitable discovery, independent source, and attenuation.
  • Gideon v. Wainwright (1963): The case establishing that states must provide free legal counsel to felony defendants who cannot afford attorneys.

Important Details

  • The Fourth Amendment requires warrants issued on probable cause, particularly describing the place to be searched and items to be seized. General warrants (common in colonial Britain) are prohibited.
  • Exceptions to the Fourth Amendment warrant requirement include: consent searches, searches incident to a lawful arrest, the automobile exception, plain view doctrine, and exigent circumstances (emergency requiring immediate action).
  • Carpenter v. United States (2018) extended Fourth Amendment protections to cell-site location information, requiring a warrant before government accesses historical location data — applying the Fourth Amendment to the digital age.
  • In Miranda v. Arizona (1966), the Court held that custodial interrogation without warnings was inherently coercive. The warnings were designed to make the right to remain silent a practical reality, not just a formal guarantee.
  • Clarence Earl Gideon handwrote his petition to the Supreme Court on prison stationery after being denied a court-appointed attorney. He was later retried with appointed counsel and acquitted — illustrating exactly why the right to counsel matters.
  • The "separate sovereigns" doctrine means that separate state and federal prosecutions for the same conduct do not violate double jeopardy because state and federal governments are distinct sovereigns.

Review Questions

1. What is the purpose of the exclusionary rule, and why does it protect Fourth Amendment rights? The exclusionary rule requires that evidence obtained through an unconstitutional search or seizure be excluded from trial. Its primary purpose is deterrence: if law enforcement knows that illegally seized evidence will be inadmissible, officers have a strong incentive to comply with Fourth Amendment requirements — obtaining proper warrants and respecting constitutional limits on searches. Without the exclusionary rule, there would be no effective remedy for Fourth Amendment violations in many cases.

2. What are the four Miranda warnings, and why did the Court require police to deliver them before custodial interrogation? The four Miranda warnings are: (1) you have the right to remain silent; (2) anything you say can and will be used against you in court; (3) you have the right to an attorney; and (4) if you cannot afford an attorney, one will be appointed for you. The Court required these warnings in Miranda v. Arizona (1966) because it found that custodial interrogation — being held in police custody while questioned — is inherently coercive, creating psychological pressure to speak even for those who wish to remain silent. The warnings make the Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination and the Sixth Amendment right to counsel practical rather than merely theoretical.

3. Explain the holding and significance of Gideon v. Wainwright (1963). Clarence Gideon was denied a court-appointed attorney for a felony trial because Florida provided counsel only in capital cases. He represented himself, was convicted, and petitioned the Supreme Court from prison. The Court held unanimously that the Sixth Amendment's right to counsel is a fundamental right incorporated against the states, and that states must provide free legal representation to felony defendants who cannot afford attorneys. Justice Black wrote that "lawyers in criminal courts are necessities, not luxuries." The decision created the foundation for the public defender system across the country.

4. What is the "fruit of the poisonous tree" doctrine? The "fruit of the poisonous tree" is an extension of the exclusionary rule: not only is directly obtained illegal evidence inadmissible, but also any derivative evidence discovered as a result of the illegal search or seizure. If police illegally search a home and find a note leading them to additional evidence elsewhere, that additional evidence — the "fruit" — is also excluded. The doctrine prevents police from benefiting indirectly from constitutional violations even when the directly obtained evidence is excluded.

5. How does the Fifth Amendment's double jeopardy protection work, and what is the "separate sovereigns" exception? The Fifth Amendment prohibits trying a defendant twice for the same offense by the same sovereign after an acquittal. Once acquitted at trial, the government cannot retry the defendant for the same crime. However, the "separate sovereigns" doctrine holds that state and federal governments are distinct sovereigns, each with independent authority to prosecute. If conduct violates both state and federal law, the defendant may be tried by both the state and the federal government without violating double jeopardy — because the two prosecutions are by different sovereigns.

Summary

The Fourth, Fifth, and Sixth Amendments collectively establish a framework of procedural protections for individuals accused of crimes, reflecting the Founders' determination to prevent the arbitrary abuses of the British colonial system. The Fourth Amendment's exclusionary rule deters unconstitutional searches; the Fifth Amendment protects against self-incrimination (Miranda warnings), double jeopardy, and uncompensated takings; and the Sixth Amendment guarantees fair trials, including the right to counsel established in Gideon v. Wainwright. Through selective incorporation, these protections now apply to state and local criminal proceedings, fundamentally shaping the American criminal justice system.
