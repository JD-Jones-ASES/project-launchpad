Study Guide: Topic 2.7 — Presidential Communication

Key Concepts

  • The State of the Union address is the president's premier platform for setting the national agenda, required by Article II, Section 3. It has evolved from a written report (Jefferson–Wilson) to an annual televised speech.
  • Presidential communication has evolved through four technological eras: print (1789–1920s), radio (1920s–1950s), television (1960s–2000s), and digital/social media (2008–present).
  • FDR's fireside chats revolutionized the president-public relationship by allowing the president to speak intimately to millions in their homes, demonstrating how communication technology shapes presidential power.
  • The rally-around-the-flag effect describes the temporary surge in presidential approval during international crises or major national events. It is typically short-lived.
  • Social media has fundamentally disrupted the gatekeeping function of traditional media, allowing presidents to bypass editorial filters and communicate directly with millions of followers—with both democratizing benefits and polarization risks.

Vocabulary Review

Term Definition
State of the Union An annual presidential address to Congress, required by Article II, Section 3, outlining the administration's legislative priorities and agenda
Bully pulpit The president's unique ability to command public attention and shape opinion; term coined by Theodore Roosevelt
Fireside chats FDR's radio addresses (1933–1944) that established an intimate, direct relationship between the president and the American public
Rally-around-the-flag effect The temporary increase in presidential approval that typically occurs during international crises or major national events
Going public A presidential strategy of directly appealing to the public to pressure Congress to support the president's agenda
White House Press Secretary The president's public spokesperson who conducts daily briefings and manages information flow to the media
Press conference A formal meeting at which the president takes questions from journalists; first formalized by Woodrow Wilson

Important Details

Four Eras of Presidential Communication

Era Dates Key Technology Key Example
Print era 1789–1920s Newspapers, editorials, letters Jackson cultivates partisan press
Radio era 1920s–1950s Radio broadcast FDR's fireside chats (1933–1944)
Television era 1960s–2000s Network/cable TV JFK's press conferences; Reagan "the Great Communicator"
Digital era 2008–present Social media Obama's digital strategy; Trump's Twitter presidency

The State of the Union

  • Constitutional requirement: Article II, Section 3 — president "shall from time to time give to the Congress Information of the State of the Union"
  • Jefferson and most early presidents sent written reports to Congress; Wilson revived the speech format in 1913
  • Modern function: outline legislative priorities, frame the political narrative, introduce major proposals
  • Audience includes Congress, Supreme Court justices, cabinet, military leaders—symbolic moment of national unity

FDR's Fireside Chats

  • 30 radio addresses from 1933–1944
  • Spoke in conversational, intimate language to millions of Americans in their homes
  • Calmed public fears during the Great Depression, built support for New Deal policies
  • First time the president could speak to the nation without media intermediaries

Rally-Around-the-Flag Effect: Examples

  • George H.W. Bush: 89% approval after 1991 Gulf War
  • George W. Bush: 90% approval after September 11, 2001
  • Both surges were temporary—Bush 43's approval eventually fell below 30%

Social Media: Benefits and Risks

  • Benefits: direct communication without media filters, democratizing access
  • Risks: misinformation, polarization, lack of vetting, diplomatic incidents from impulsive statements, market disruption
  • Trump used Twitter as primary governance communication tool
  • Biden used multiple platforms while maintaining more traditional approach to official statements

Review Questions

1. How did FDR's fireside chats change the relationship between the president and the American public? FDR's fireside chats (1933–1944) established an unprecedented direct relationship between the president and ordinary Americans. For the first time, the president could speak intimately and personally to millions of citizens in their own homes without going through the intermediaries of the press or other institutions. FDR's warm, conversational radio addresses—explaining New Deal programs, calming Depression fears, mobilizing support for the war effort—transformed the presidency from a distant institutional role into a personal presence in Americans' daily lives. This demonstrated that communication technology could fundamentally reshape the nature of presidential power.

2. What is the rally-around-the-flag effect, and why is it typically temporary? The rally-around-the-flag effect is the tendency of the public to increase support for the president during international crises or major national events. When the nation faces an external threat, people tend to set aside partisan differences and unify behind the commander in chief. George H.W. Bush reached 89% approval after the Gulf War; George W. Bush hit 90% after 9/11. The effect is temporary because crises are eventually evaluated on their management: as wars extend, costs mount, policy failures emerge, or normal partisan divisions reassert themselves, approval returns toward baseline or below. Bush 43's approval collapsed from its post-9/11 peak to below 30% by his second term's end.

3. Identify one advantage and one disadvantage of presidential use of social media for direct communication. Advantage: Social media allows the president to communicate directly with millions of citizens without editorial gatekeeping or media filtering. Presidents can shape their message, respond rapidly to events, and mobilize supporters without relying on potentially unfriendly media outlets to carry their message. This can be democratizing, allowing the public to receive information directly from the president rather than through intermediaries. Disadvantage: Social media communication lacks the vetting process that traditional White House communications undergo. A single post can move financial markets, provoke diplomatic incidents, or commit the government to positions that contradict official policy—as Trump's Twitter usage frequently illustrated. The speed and informality of social media also incentivize inflammatory statements over deliberate governance, potentially inflaming polarization.

4. What is "going public" and how does it relate to the bully pulpit? "Going public" is a presidential strategy described by political scientist Samuel Kernell in which the president directly appeals to the public to build pressure on Congress to support the president's legislative agenda. Rather than bargaining privately with legislators, the president uses public speeches, media appearances, and direct outreach to mobilize citizens who then pressure their own representatives. It relies on the bully pulpit—the president's unique ability to command national attention—as the mechanism. Going public became more viable as communication technology (radio, television, social media) allowed presidents to reach the public directly and instantaneously, bypassing congressional leaders.

5. How has the evolution of communication technology affected the relationship between the president and Congress? As communication technology has evolved from print to radio to television to social media, presidents have gained increasing capacity to appeal directly to the public, reducing their dependence on congressional cooperation. When presidents needed newspapers to communicate their positions, they were more dependent on editorial gatekeepers and the slow rhythms of political discourse. Radio and television gave presidents the ability to speak directly to millions, mobilizing public pressure on Congress. Social media has accelerated this process to real-time: a president can bypass Congress entirely and communicate directly to a national audience within seconds. This shift toward "going public" has changed the dynamics of legislative bargaining—presidents can now try to pressure Congress from outside rather than negotiating privately—though critics note that public pressure is not always more effective than private bargaining.

Summary

Presidential communication has evolved from written reports to Congress to an era of instant social media direct communication, with each technological revolution reshaping the president's relationship with the public and the capacity to exercise informal power. FDR's fireside chats demonstrated that radio could make the president a personal presence in Americans' homes; JFK and Reagan showed the centrality of television; Obama and Trump demonstrated the transformative power of social media. The rally-around-the-flag effect illustrates how national crises temporarily suspend partisan divisions, but these boosts are short-lived. The president's unique ability to command public attention—the bully pulpit—remains central to the informal exercise of presidential power.
