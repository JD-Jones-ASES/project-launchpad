Study Guide: Topic 1.5 — Ratification of the U.S. Constitution

Key Concepts

  • The Constitutional Convention (1787) brought together fifty-five delegates from twelve states (Rhode Island refused to attend) who quickly exceeded their mandate and drafted an entirely new constitution rather than revising the Articles of Confederation.
  • Major compromises at the Convention — the Great Compromise on representation, the Three-Fifths Compromise on enslaved persons, the Electoral College compromise, and the slave trade compromise — resolved fundamental disagreements between large and small states and between Northern and Southern states.
  • The Federalist vs. Anti-Federalist debate was the pivotal political argument over ratification: Federalists argued the new Constitution would create a strong, effective national government while protecting liberty; Anti-Federalists warned it would destroy state sovereignty and create a dangerous central authority without adequate protections for individual rights.
  • The Bill of Rights was the key political compromise that secured ratification — Anti-Federalists demanded explicit protections for individual liberties as the price of their support, and the promise to add amendments resolved the crucial debates in key states.
  • Marbury v. Madison (1803) established the doctrine of judicial review — the Supreme Court's power to declare laws unconstitutional — making the Constitution a legally enforceable document rather than a mere political statement.

Vocabulary Review

Virginia Plan: A proposal at the Constitutional Convention (drafted by James Madison) calling for a bicameral legislature with representation based on population; favored large states.

New Jersey Plan: A counterproposal by William Paterson calling for a unicameral legislature with equal representation for each state regardless of population; favored small states.

Great Compromise (Connecticut Compromise): The agreement that resolved the large-state/small-state dispute by creating a bicameral Congress: the House of Representatives with representation proportional to population, and the Senate with equal representation (two senators per state).

Three-Fifths Compromise: The agreement that counted enslaved persons as three-fifths of a person for purposes of apportioning House seats and direct taxes among states — a morally compromised formula that increased Southern states' political power.

Federalist: A supporter of the new Constitution who argued it would create a strong, effective national government while containing sufficient safeguards against tyranny. The Federalist Papers (Hamilton, Madison, Jay) were the primary intellectual defense.

Anti-Federalist: An opponent of the Constitution who feared it would destroy state sovereignty, create a tyrannical central government, and lacked explicit protections for individual rights. Demanded a Bill of Rights as the price of ratification.

Ratification: The process of formally approving the Constitution. Nine of thirteen states were required; once achieved (New Hampshire, 1788), the Constitution took effect.

Judicial review: The power of the courts to examine laws and government actions and declare them unconstitutional; established in Marbury v. Madison (1803); not explicitly mentioned in the Constitution.

Important Details

  • The convention was held in complete secrecy — sentries posted at doors, windows shut despite summer heat — allowing delegates to speak candidly, change their minds, and negotiate without political pressure from constituents or press.
  • Rhode Island refused to attend the Convention; it was the last state to ratify the Constitution (May 1790, barely 34–32).
  • George Washington presided over the Convention, lending it crucial legitimacy. Madison's detailed notes are our primary historical record of the proceedings.
  • The Virginia Plan proposed a bicameral legislature with representation based on population (favoring Virginia and other large states). The New Jersey Plan proposed a unicameral legislature with equal state representation (favoring small states like New Jersey and Delaware).
  • The Great Compromise (proposed by Roger Sherman of Connecticut) split the difference: proportional representation in the House, equal representation in the Senate. This is still the structure today.
  • The Three-Fifths Compromise (three-fifths of enslaved persons counted for House apportionment) increased the political power of Southern slave states without granting any rights to enslaved people themselves.
  • The Electoral College was a compromise that gave states a role in selecting the president while avoiding direct popular election.
  • Key Federalist arguments: the new Constitution would prevent the chaos exposed by the Articles; a large republic would protect against faction (Federalist No. 10); separation of powers and checks and balances would prevent tyranny (Federalist No. 51).
  • Key Anti-Federalist arguments (Brutus No. 1): a large republic would produce a distant, unresponsive government; the Constitution lacked a bill of rights protecting individual liberties; the national government would swallow state authority.
  • Marbury v. Madison (1803): Secretary of State James Madison refused to deliver William Marbury's judicial commission. Chief Justice Marshall held that while Marbury had a right to the commission, the section of the Judiciary Act of 1789 granting the Court original jurisdiction to issue writs of mandamus was unconstitutional. By ruling against himself (the Court's power), Marshall actually claimed a much larger power: the ability to strike down acts of Congress.

Review Questions

1. Compare the Virginia Plan and the New Jersey Plan. How did the Great Compromise resolve the dispute between large and small states?

Answer: The Virginia Plan (drafted by Madison) called for a bicameral legislature in which representation in both chambers was based on population — strongly favoring large states like Virginia. Smaller states would have far less influence than under the Articles. The New Jersey Plan (proposed by Paterson) countered with a unicameral legislature giving each state equal representation regardless of population — essentially preserving the Articles' structure. The dispute seemed irreconcilable until Roger Sherman proposed the Great Compromise: a bicameral Congress in which the House of Representatives would have proportional representation (population-based) and the Senate would have equal representation (two senators per state regardless of size). This satisfied large states on the House and small states on the Senate, and remains the structure of Congress today.

2. Explain two key arguments made by Federalists and two key arguments made by Anti-Federalists during the ratification debate.

Answer: Federalist arguments: (1) The new Constitution would solve the failures of the Articles — it would give the national government the powers needed to tax, regulate commerce, raise armies, and enforce laws, preventing the chaos and weakness that had plagued the 1780s. (2) Structural safeguards would prevent tyranny — Madison argued in Federalist No. 10 that a large republic with diverse interests would prevent any single faction from dominating, and in Federalist No. 51 that separation of powers and checks and balances would ensure "ambition counteracts ambition."

Anti-Federalist arguments: (1) The Constitution lacked a bill of rights — without explicit protections for freedom of speech, religion, due process, and other fundamental rights, the powerful national government could trample individual liberties; the Founders had fought a revolution against exactly this kind of power. (2) A large republic would be unresponsive — Brutus argued that in a nation covering such vast territory, citizens would know few of their rulers, making genuine accountability impossible and allowing elites to govern without meaningful popular oversight.

3. Why is Marbury v. Madison considered one of the most important Supreme Court decisions in American history?

Answer: Marbury v. Madison (1803) established judicial review — the power of the federal courts to declare acts of Congress or the executive branch unconstitutional. This power is not explicitly mentioned in the Constitution; Marshall derived it through careful reasoning about the nature of a written constitution. He argued that the Constitution is the supreme law of the land, and that if a law conflicts with the Constitution, courts must strike down the law — otherwise the Constitution's limits on government power would be meaningless. Without judicial review, the Constitution's protections would be merely aspirational; Congress and the president could violate them without legal consequence. With judicial review, the Supreme Court became the ultimate arbiter of constitutional meaning, fundamentally shaping the balance of power among the branches and making the Constitution a living, enforced legal document rather than a political statement.

4. What role did the promise of a Bill of Rights play in securing ratification of the Constitution?

Answer: The absence of a bill of rights was the Anti-Federalists' strongest argument against ratification, and several key states — including Massachusetts, Virginia, and New York — were reluctant to ratify without explicit protections for individual liberties. In Massachusetts, the compromise was reached by promising to add rights-protecting amendments immediately after ratification. Federalists, led by Madison, agreed to champion these amendments in the first Congress. This "Federalist promise" — ratify now, add a bill of rights later — broke the political deadlock in crucial states and secured ratification. Madison followed through, shepherding through Congress the ten amendments that became the Bill of Rights in 1791. Without this political compromise, several key states might have rejected or demanded drastic revisions to the Constitution.

5. Why did the Constitutional Convention conduct its proceedings in complete secrecy, and was this appropriate?

Answer: The Convention agreed to secrecy for practical political reasons: open deliberations would have subjected delegates to immediate public and political pressure, making genuine negotiation impossible. If every proposal were public, delegates would have had to grandstand for their home constituencies rather than seek workable compromises. The secrecy allowed delegates to propose radical ideas, test arguments, change their minds, and make politically painful concessions without immediately paying a political price. The Great Compromise, the Three-Fifths Compromise, and many other agreements might have been impossible under public scrutiny. Whether this was appropriate is debatable: it was arguably anti-democratic to draft a new constitution in secret without any public input. However, given the urgency of the national crisis and the practical need for genuine deliberation, most historians view the secrecy as a pragmatic necessity that enabled a more workable result than open proceedings might have produced.

Summary

The Constitutional Convention (1787) quickly exceeded its mandate of merely revising the Articles of Confederation, producing an entirely new governing framework through a series of hard-won compromises. The Virginia and New Jersey Plans were reconciled through the Great Compromise, creating a bicameral Congress that balanced large-state and small-state interests. The Three-Fifths Compromise and other arrangements secured Southern support at a severe moral cost. The subsequent Federalist-Anti-Federalist debate shaped American political thought: Federalists published brilliant defenses of the Constitution in The Federalist Papers while Anti-Federalists warned of centralized tyranny. The promise to add a Bill of Rights proved essential to ratification in key states, and Madison delivered on this promise in 1791. Marbury v. Madison (1803) completed the constitutional architecture by establishing judicial review — the mechanism that makes constitutional limits on government actually enforceable.
