Study Guide: Topic 5.8 — Electing a President

Key Concepts

  • The United States does not elect the president by national popular vote; instead, the Electoral College is an indirect system in which voters choose state electors who then cast the official presidential votes.
  • The winner-take-all system (used in 48 states and D.C.) concentrates campaign attention on swing states, effectively marginalizing voters in non-competitive states.
  • The primary process determines each party's presidential nominee through a sequence of state contests (primaries and caucuses) culminating in a national convention.
  • Open primaries tend to produce more moderate nominees because they allow any registered voter to participate; closed primaries restricted to party members may produce more ideologically extreme nominees.
  • The Electoral College's design—allocating electors by state congressional delegation—can produce a president who loses the national popular vote, as occurred in 2000 and 2016.

Vocabulary Review

  • Electoral College: The constitutional system for electing the president, in which each state receives electors equal to its total congressional representation; a candidate needs 270 of 538 electoral votes to win.
  • Winner-take-all (Electoral College): In 48 states and D.C., the candidate who wins the state's popular vote receives all of its electoral votes.
  • Swing state (battleground state): A state where the electoral outcome is genuinely competitive; receives disproportionate campaign attention because its outcome could go to either party.
  • Primary election: A state-level election to choose a party's presidential nominee.
  • Open primary: A primary in which any registered voter may participate regardless of party affiliation; produces more moderate nominees.
  • Closed primary: A primary in which only registered party members may vote; may produce more ideologically extreme nominees.
  • Caucus: A local meeting where party members discuss candidates and publicly declare preferences; attracts more ideologically committed participants than primaries.
  • National convention: The gathering where each party formally nominates its presidential and vice-presidential candidates and adopts its platform.
  • Delegate: A party member chosen to represent their state at the national convention; allocated based on primary/caucus results.
  • Superdelegate: Democratic Party leaders and elected officials who can support any candidate regardless of primary results; reforms have reduced their influence.
  • Twenty-Third Amendment (1961): Granted Washington, D.C. three electoral votes.

Important Details

How the Electoral College Works

  • Total electors: 538 (435 House + 100 Senate + 3 for D.C. under 23rd Amendment)
  • Majority needed: 270 electoral votes
  • If no majority: The election goes to the House of Representatives, where each state delegation casts one vote (not each member)
  • Winner-take-all: 48 states + D.C. → all electoral votes go to the state's popular vote winner
  • Exceptions: Maine and Nebraska use district-based systems (2 electoral votes to statewide winner, 1 per congressional district winner)

Why Swing States Get More Attention

In winner-take-all, reliably blue or red states are effectively predetermined outcomes. Campaigns allocate resources where votes are persuadable—competitive swing states (e.g., Pennsylvania, Wisconsin, Michigan, Arizona, Georgia, Nevada). Voters in safe states have less influence over the campaign's choices.

Arguments About the Electoral College

Pro-Electoral College Anti-Electoral College
Preserves federalism Can elect president who lost popular vote (2000, 2016)
Forces geographically broad coalitions Overrepresents small states

Clear winner, avoids national recounts Winner-take-all ignores large minorities in each state

The Primary Process

Closed primary: Only registered party members vote → tends to favor more ideologically extreme candidates preferred by party activists.

Open primary: Any registered voter participates → tends to produce more moderate nominees because independents and crossover voters participate.

Caucus: Public, in-person meetings; time-consuming; attract more committed, ideologically extreme participants. Iowa caucus historically had outsized influence.

Order matters: Early states (Iowa, New Hampshire) generate media momentum and "momentum" effects disproportionate to their size.

Superdelegates (Democrats): Party leaders and elected officials who are free to support any candidate; Democratic reforms after 2016 reduced their influence at the nominating convention.

Review Questions

1. Explain how the winner-take-all system in the Electoral College shapes presidential campaign strategy.

Answer: Because the Electoral College awards all of a state's electoral votes to the popular vote winner (in 48 states and D.C.), states that reliably vote for one party are effectively foregone conclusions. Campaigns have little incentive to spend resources in California (safe Democrat) or Wyoming (safe Republican). Instead, campaigns concentrate their money, time, and advertising on swing states—competitive states that could go to either party—because a narrow victory there awards the full electoral vote total. This means voters in non-competitive states receive far less candidate attention, and campaign decisions are driven by the geography of competitiveness, not raw population size.

2. What happens if no presidential candidate wins a majority of electoral votes?

Answer: If no candidate reaches the required 270 electoral votes, the election is decided by the House of Representatives in a "contingency election." Each state delegation in the House casts one vote regardless of state size—so Wyoming's single representative gets the same vote as California's 52-member delegation. This system strongly favors smaller states and has only been used twice in American history (1800 and 1824). The vice president is separately elected by the Senate in this scenario.

3. Compare open and closed primaries. Which type is more likely to produce moderate nominees?

Answer: In a closed primary, only registered party members may vote, meaning candidates must appeal primarily to the party's base—which tends to be more ideologically extreme (more liberal in Democratic primaries, more conservative in Republican primaries). In an open primary, any registered voter may participate regardless of party affiliation, meaning candidates must also appeal to independents and potential crossover voters from the other party who typically hold more moderate views. Open primaries are therefore more likely to produce moderate nominees because the broader electorate includes voters who are not firmly committed to either party's ideological agenda.

4. Identify one argument for and one argument against the Electoral College system.

Answer: FOR: The Electoral College preserves federalism by requiring candidates to build geographically broad coalitions across multiple states rather than simply maximizing votes in the most populous cities. It also protects the political relevance of smaller states that might otherwise be ignored in a pure popular vote system. AGAINST: The winner-take-all mechanism can produce a president who lost the national popular vote, as occurred in 2000 (Gore won popular vote; Bush won Electoral College) and 2016 (Clinton won popular vote; Trump won Electoral College). Critics argue this violates the democratic principle that the candidate preferred by most voters should win.

5. What role do superdelegates play in the Democratic primary process, and how has their role been reformed?

Answer: Superdelegates are Democratic Party leaders and elected officials (members of Congress, governors, DNC members) who attend the national convention and can support any candidate regardless of primary results—they are not bound by voters' choices. Before 2016, superdelegates could potentially override the will of primary voters, sparking controversy when many superdelegates initially committed to Hillary Clinton in 2016 before a single primary vote was cast. After 2016, Democratic Party rules were reformed to prevent superdelegates from voting on the first ballot at the convention unless no candidate has already secured a majority through pledged delegates, significantly reducing their influence.

Summary

Presidential elections are decided through the Electoral College, an indirect system that requires 270 of 538 electoral votes and focuses campaign attention on competitive swing states rather than the entire national electorate. The winner-take-all rule in most states can produce a president who lost the national popular vote. The primary process—through open primaries, closed primaries, or caucuses—determines nominees before the general election, with different rules producing candidates of different ideological profiles. Superdelegates in the Democratic Party have been reformed to reduce their ability to override voter preferences at the convention.
