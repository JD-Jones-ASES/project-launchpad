Study Guide: Topic 2.3 — Congressional Behavior

Key Concepts

  • Models of representation describe how members of Congress relate to their constituents: the trustee model relies on legislators' own judgment; the delegate model mirrors constituent preferences; the politico model blends both based on circumstances.
  • Party-line voting has increased dramatically with partisan polarization. Party leaders use committee assignments, campaign funds, whip operations, and agenda control to enforce discipline.
  • Logrolling, pork barrel spending, and earmarks are practices by which members trade votes and direct federal funds to their districts—generating both coalition-building benefits and public criticism.
  • Incumbency advantage is the overwhelming structural benefit enjoyed by sitting legislators: House incumbents win reelection at rates above 90% despite low congressional approval ratings.
  • The apparent paradox of low approval / high reelection rates reflects that voters often distinguish between Congress as an institution (disliked) and their own representative (liked).

Important Details

Three Models of Representation

Edmund Burke championed the trustee model, arguing legislators owe the people their "judgment" not their "opinion." The delegate model is the most democratically pure—representatives as conduits for popular will. In practice, most members function as politicos, acting as delegates on local issues where constituent opinion is intense and clear, and as trustees on complex policy questions where the public is less informed or engaged.

Party Influence Tools

  1. Whip operations: Vote counting and persuasion before key votes
  2. Committee assignments: Rewarding loyalty with desirable placements
  3. Campaign support: Party committees channel funds to allies
  4. Agenda control: Leaders decide what reaches the floor and when

Incumbency Advantages

  • Name recognition from prior campaigns and media coverage
  • Franking privilege: free mail to constituents
  • Credit claiming: pointing to specific accomplishments
  • Superior fundraising (donors favor incumbents with committee influence)
  • Casework: building personal loyalty by helping constituents with federal agencies
  • Gerrymandering: safe districts protect incumbents of the dominant party

Review Questions

1. Compare the trustee and delegate models of representation. Which does the politico model most closely resemble? The trustee model holds that representatives should exercise independent judgment even when it conflicts with constituent preferences—they are elected for their wisdom, not to be a rubber stamp for public opinion. The delegate model holds the opposite: representatives should faithfully reflect constituent preferences regardless of their own views. The politico model most closely resembles a contextual blend of both. It acknowledges that on some issues (local, highly salient) members act as delegates, while on others (complex, low public salience) they act as trustees. Most members of Congress function this way in practice.

2. Identify three factors that contribute to the incumbency advantage in congressional elections. First, name recognition: incumbents are already known to voters through media coverage, prior campaigns, and constituent service, while challengers must build visibility from scratch. Second, fundraising advantage: donors prefer to invest in candidates with existing committee positions and proven viability, so incumbents consistently raise more money than challengers. Third, casework and constituent service: by helping individuals navigate federal bureaucracy (Social Security, veterans' benefits, immigration cases), incumbents build personal loyalty that transcends partisan preferences and insulates them from broader congressional approval trends.

3. Define logrolling and explain why it can be both beneficial and problematic. Logrolling is the legislative practice of trading votes: "I'll vote for your bill if you vote for mine." It can be beneficial because it facilitates coalition-building in a diverse democracy where no single interest can command majority support alone—by linking bills together, members can assemble enough votes to pass legislation that serves a genuine public need. It can be problematic because it may produce legislation that bundles unrelated provisions together, makes it harder to evaluate bills on their individual merits, and can generate spending on projects that benefit narrow constituencies at the expense of the broader public interest.

4. Why do incumbents win reelection at high rates even when congressional approval ratings are very low? The paradox of "I hate Congress but love my congressman" reflects several structural advantages incumbents hold. Voters distinguish between Congress as an institution (broadly disliked for partisan dysfunction) and their own representative (who may be well-known, credited with securing local benefits, and helpful with casework). Additionally, gerrymandering creates safe districts where the dominant party's incumbent faces little genuine competition. Campaign finance advantages further insulate incumbents: they typically outspend challengers significantly. These structural factors operate independently of national sentiment about Congress as a whole.

5. How do party leaders use tools of discipline to maintain party cohesion on key votes? Party leaders employ several mechanisms. Whip operations count votes in advance and apply direct pressure to wavering members before key votes. Committee assignments are used as both rewards (loyal members get desirable, influential positions) and punishments (dissenters may lose committee seats). Party committees distribute campaign funds strategically, channeling money to loyal members and potentially withholding support from those who break ranks. Agenda control is perhaps the most subtle tool: by deciding what comes to the floor and when, leaders can protect members from having to cast difficult votes on issues that would split the caucus, or can force votes that expose opponents' vulnerabilities.

Summary

Congressional behavior is shaped by competing pressures: constituent preferences, party loyalty, personal judgment, and reelection incentives. Members generally operate as politicos—adapting their representational style to the nature of the issue. Increasing partisan polarization has strengthened party-line voting, with leaders using a range of tools to maintain caucus discipline. Despite low institutional approval ratings, incumbents enjoy enormous structural advantages—name recognition, fundraising, casework, and gerrymandering—that produce reelection rates consistently above 90% in the House.
