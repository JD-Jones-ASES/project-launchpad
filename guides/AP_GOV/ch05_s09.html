Study Guide: Topic 5.9 -- Congressional Elections

Key Concepts

  • Incumbency advantage is one of the most powerful forces in American elections: House incumbents win re-election more than 90% of the time in most cycles, driven by name recognition, the franking privilege, credit claiming, campaign finance advantages, and constituency service.
  • Midterm elections follow a consistent pattern: the president's party almost always loses seats in Congress, reflecting voter frustration, lower turnout among the president's base, and energized opposition -- averaging 26 House seat losses since World War II.
  • The coattail effect describes how a popular presidential candidate can boost down-ballot co-partisans in election years, though this effect has weakened as ticket-splitting has increased.
  • Gerrymandering (packing and cracking opposition voters) allows the majority party in state legislatures to draw congressional district maps that advantage their candidates. Courts have imposed limits on racial gerrymandering but not partisan gerrymandering.
  • Two required SCOTUS cases: Baker v. Carr (1962) established that redistricting disputes are justiciable (courts can hear them) and underpinned the "one person, one vote" standard; Shaw v. Reno (1993) held that districts drawn primarily on race are subject to strict scrutiny.

Vocabulary Review

  • Incumbency: The status of holding office; an incumbent is a current officeholder running for re-election.
  • Franking privilege: The right of members of Congress to send official mail to constituents at taxpayer expense, allowing year-round contact with voters.
  • Credit claiming: The practice of incumbents pointing to specific benefits delivered to their district (federal grants, military bases, infrastructure) to demonstrate their effectiveness.
  • Coattail effect: The tendency of a popular presidential candidate to boost the electoral performance of co-partisan congressional candidates in the same election year.
  • Midterm election: Elections held in the middle of a president's term (every two years); the president's party historically loses seats.
  • Redistricting: The redrawing of congressional district boundaries, conducted every ten years following the census by state legislatures in most states.
  • Gerrymandering: Drawing district boundaries to advantage a particular party or group. Two forms: "packing" (concentrating opposition voters into a few districts) and "cracking" (spreading opposition voters across many districts to dilute their influence).
  • Baker v. Carr (1962): Supreme Court ruling that redistricting disputes are justiciable (courts can hear and resolve them); established the basis for "one person, one vote."
  • Shaw v. Reno (1993): Supreme Court ruling that congressional districts drawn primarily on the basis of race are subject to strict scrutiny under the Equal Protection Clause.

Key Takeaways

  • House incumbency reelection rates typically exceed 90%, making congressional elections far less competitive than they appear.
  • The primary drivers of incumbency advantage are structural (name recognition, franking, credit claiming, campaign finance) rather than pure policy performance.
  • The midterm penalty for the president's party is one of the most consistent patterns in American politics, functioning as a referendum on presidential performance.
  • Gerrymandering illustrates how institutional rules can shape electoral outcomes independently of voter preferences.
  • Shaw v. Reno and Baker v. Carr are both Unit 5 required cases that connect redistricting to constitutional principles of equal protection and justiciability.

AP Exam Connection

  • Stimulus-based MC: expect a data table showing incumbency reelection rates across election cycles, asking for trend identification and explanation of 2010 anomaly.
  • Concept Application FRQ: scenarios involving redistricting, midterm patterns, or incumbency advantages are common.
  • SCOTUS Comparison: Shaw v. Reno (required) compared to a non-required racial gerrymandering case; Baker v. Carr (required) compared to a non-required redistricting case.
  • Quantitative Analysis: congressional approval rating vs. reelection rate data tests whether students can identify the paradox (low approval, high reelection).
