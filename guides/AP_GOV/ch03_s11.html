Study Guide: Topic 3.11 — Government Responses to Social Movements

Key Concepts

  • Social movements: Organized efforts by citizens demanding that the government live up to its constitutional promises. Use protest, litigation, legislation, and civil disobedience to pressure government action. Government responses have ranged from violent resistance to landmark legislation.

  • Civil Rights Movement: The most transformative social movement in modern American history (1950s–1960s). African Americans and allies used nonviolent protest, legal challenges, and political organizing to dismantle the Jim Crow system of legal segregation.

  • Key Civil Rights tactics:

    • Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955–56): Year-long boycott sparked by Rosa Parks; brought Dr. King to national prominence.
    • Sit-ins (1960): Greensboro students sat at whites-only lunch counters to protest segregation.
    • Freedom Rides (1961): Integrated groups tested federal desegregation orders on interstate buses.
    • March on Washington (1963): 250,000 gathered; King delivered "I Have a Dream" speech.
    • Selma to Montgomery marches (1965): "Bloody Sunday" attack on peaceful voting rights marchers generated national outrage.

  • Letter from Birmingham Jail (1963): Foundational document written by Dr. King from his jail cell responding to white clergymen. Distinguishes between just and unjust laws; justifies civil disobedience against unjust laws. Required AP Government foundational document.

  • Civil disobedience: The deliberate, nonviolent refusal to comply with laws considered unjust, with willingness to accept legal consequences. King grounded this practice in natural law tradition (St. Augustine, Aquinas): "An unjust law is no law at all."

  • Just vs. unjust laws (King's framework): A just law "squares with the moral law or the law of God" and "uplifts human personality." An unjust law "is a code that is out of harmony with the moral law" and "degrades human personality." Segregation is unjust because it "distorts the soul and damages the personality."

  • Civil Rights Act of 1964: Landmark legislation prohibiting discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin in employment and public accommodations; gave legislative force to the Equal Protection principles established in Brown.

  • Voting Rights Act of 1965: Prohibited discriminatory voting practices, including literacy tests; provided federal oversight of elections in jurisdictions with histories of discrimination; dramatically increased Black voter registration in the South.

  • Title IX (1972): Prohibited sex discrimination in federally funded education programs; transformed women's access to educational opportunities and athletics.

  • Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA, 1990): Prohibited discrimination against individuals with disabilities in employment, public accommodations, and government services; modeled on the Civil Rights Act.

Vocabulary Review

  • Civil disobedience: Deliberate, nonviolent refusal to obey laws considered unjust, with acceptance of legal consequences as a means of drawing public attention to the injustice.
  • Just law: King's framework: a law that upholds human dignity and squares with moral law; a law that a majority imposes on itself as well as the minority.
  • Unjust law: King's framework: a law that degrades human personality; a law imposed by a majority on a minority that the majority does not follow itself.
  • De jure segregation: Racial separation established or enforced by law or government policy (contrast with de facto segregation).
  • De facto segregation: Racial separation that exists in practice without legal mandate, often resulting from residential patterns and private choices.
  • Jim Crow laws: State and local laws in the post-Reconstruction South that mandated racial segregation in schools, transportation, restaurants, and other public spaces.
  • Nonviolent direct action: Protest strategy using peaceful civil disobedience, marches, boycotts, and sit-ins to expose injustice and pressure change; associated with the Civil Rights Movement.
  • Letter from Birmingham Jail: 1963 foundational document by Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. articulating the moral basis for civil disobedience and the urgency of civil rights reform.

Key Takeaways

  • The Civil Rights Movement succeeded by combining legal strategies (NAACP litigation), nonviolent protest tactics, and political pressure to force legislative action.
  • The Letter from Birmingham Jail is a foundational document for AP Government because it illustrates the tension between legal positivism (obey all laws) and natural law (disobey unjust laws), and the conditions under which civil disobedience may be justified in a democracy.
  • Government responses to social movements vary: from violent resistance (Bloody Sunday) to landmark legislation (Civil Rights Act, Voting Rights Act).
  • The civil rights framework has been extended to other groups: women (Title IX), people with disabilities (ADA), and LGBTQ+ Americans (Obergefell v. Hodges, 2015).
  • Achieving formal legal equality (de jure) does not automatically eliminate de facto inequality — a central tension in ongoing civil rights debates.

AP Exam Connection

Required foundational document: Letter from Birmingham Jail (1963) — tested on King's distinction between just and unjust laws, the justification for civil disobedience, the relationship to natural law philosophy, and the application to the Civil Rights Movement.

Likely question types: Concept Application (scenario involving civil disobedience or government response to a movement), Argument Essay (using Letter from Birmingham Jail to argue about civil disobedience or equality), SCOTUS Comparison (comparing Brown v. Board to a post-1954 civil rights case).

Key connection: Topic 3.11 directly applies the equal protection principles from Topic 3.10 to the political and social context of the Civil Rights Movement. It also connects to Topics 3.12 and 3.13 (balancing rights and affirmative action).
