Study Guide: Topic 3.10 — Equal Protection

Key Concepts

  • Equal Protection Clause: Fourteenth Amendment provision (1868) stating that no state shall deny to any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws. Originally enacted to protect formerly enslaved African Americans; expanded over time to address discrimination based on race, ethnicity, sex, and other classifications.

  • Civil rights vs. civil liberties: Civil liberties are protections of individuals from government interference; civil rights are guarantees of equal treatment by the government. The Equal Protection Clause is the primary constitutional foundation for civil rights.

  • Levels of scrutiny: The Supreme Court applies different levels of judicial scrutiny depending on the classification at issue:

    • Strict scrutiny: Applied to suspect classifications (race, ethnicity, national origin, religion) and laws burdening fundamental rights. Government must show a compelling interest and a narrowly tailored law. Very few laws survive.
    • Intermediate scrutiny: Applied to quasi-suspect classifications (sex, gender). Government must show an important interest substantially related to the law. Sometimes described as "heightened scrutiny."
    • Rational basis review: Applied to all other classifications (age, income, etc.). Government need only show a legitimate interest and a rational relationship. Most laws survive.

  • Strict scrutiny: Sometimes described as "strict in theory, fatal in fact" because the government so rarely satisfies its demanding requirements. The government must demonstrate both a compelling state interest and that the law is narrowly tailored to achieve that interest.

  • De jure discrimination: Discrimination "by law" — established or enforced by official government policy. Jim Crow laws requiring racial segregation are the classic example. The Equal Protection Clause directly addresses de jure discrimination.

  • De facto discrimination: Discrimination "in fact" — exists in practice without legal mandate. Results from economic patterns, private decisions, and historical inequities. More difficult to challenge constitutionally because it may not involve intentional government action.

  • Brown v. Board of Education (1954): Unanimous Supreme Court decision holding that racial segregation in public schools violates the Equal Protection Clause. Chief Justice Warren rejected the "separate but equal" doctrine from Plessy v. Ferguson (1896), reasoning that separate educational facilities are inherently unequal. Required AP Government case.

  • Plessy v. Ferguson (1896): Supreme Court case that established the "separate but equal" doctrine, permitting racial segregation if facilities were nominally equal. Overturned by Brown v. Board of Education (1954).

Vocabulary Review

  • Equal Protection Clause: Fourteenth Amendment provision prohibiting states from denying equal protection of the laws to any person within their jurisdiction.
  • Strict scrutiny: Highest level of judicial review; applied to suspect classifications and fundamental rights. Government must show a compelling interest and narrowly tailored law.
  • Intermediate scrutiny: Mid-level judicial review applied to sex/gender classifications; government must show an important interest substantially related to the law.
  • Rational basis review: Lowest level of judicial review; government need only show a legitimate interest rationally related to the law. Applied to most other classifications.
  • Suspect classification: A classification — such as race, ethnicity, or national origin — that triggers strict scrutiny because of its historical use to discriminate.
  • Compelling interest: The government's justification under strict scrutiny; must be more than important — it must be essential or critical to a government function.
  • Narrowly tailored: Requirement under strict scrutiny that the law use the least restrictive means to achieve the compelling interest.
  • De jure discrimination: Discrimination established or enforced by law or official government policy.
  • De facto discrimination: Discrimination that exists in practice without legal mandate, often resulting from historical inequities and structural factors.
  • Separate but equal: Doctrine from Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) holding that racially separate facilities did not violate equal protection if they were physically equal; overturned by Brown v. Board of Education (1954).

Key Takeaways

  • The Equal Protection Clause was ratified in 1868 to protect formerly enslaved African Americans but has been expanded to prohibit government discrimination based on race, ethnicity, sex, and other classifications.
  • The level of scrutiny determines the outcome: strict scrutiny is nearly always fatal to the government's action; rational basis review nearly always sustains it.
  • Brown v. Board of Education (1954) is one of the most important Supreme Court decisions in American history — it overturned Plessy's "separate but equal" doctrine and provided the legal foundation for the Civil Rights Movement.
  • The distinction between de jure and de facto discrimination explains why legal equality does not automatically produce equality in practice — a key tension in American civil rights history.
  • The Equal Protection Clause applies to state governments; the federal government is bound by similar principles through the Fifth Amendment's due process clause (as interpreted in Bolling v. Sharpe, 1954).

AP Exam Connection

Required case: Brown v. Board of Education (1954) — tested on the holding that racial segregation in public schools violates the Equal Protection Clause, the rejection of "separate but equal," Chief Justice Warren's reasoning about the psychological effects of segregation, and the significance of the unanimous decision.

Likely question types: SCOTUS Comparison (Brown v. Board vs. a non-required segregation or equal protection case), Concept Application (scenario involving government classification by race or sex), Quantitative Analysis (data on racial disparities in education or employment).
