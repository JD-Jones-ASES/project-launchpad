Study Guide: Topic 1.7 -- Relationship Between States and the Federal Government

Key Concepts

  • Enumerated (Expressed/Delegated) Powers: Powers explicitly granted to the national government by the Constitution, primarily in Article I, Section 8 -- including taxing, coining money, declaring war, and regulating interstate commerce.

  • Necessary and Proper Clause (Elastic Clause): Article I, Section 8, Clause 18 grants Congress authority to make all laws "necessary and proper" for executing its enumerated powers. Broadly interpreted in McCulloch v. Maryland (1819) as the constitutional basis for implied powers.

  • Reserved Powers: Powers retained by states under the Tenth Amendment -- all powers not delegated to the national government nor prohibited to the states. Includes education, police power, and regulating intrastate commerce.

  • Concurrent Powers: Powers exercised simultaneously by both national and state governments: taxing, borrowing money, establishing courts, building roads, and making and enforcing laws.

  • Supremacy Clause: Article VI, Clause 2 -- the Constitution, federal laws, and treaties are "the supreme Law of the Land." Applies only to valid federal law enacted within Congress's constitutional authority.

  • Full Faith and Credit Clause: Article IV, Section 1 -- each state must recognize the public acts, records, and judicial proceedings of every other state.

  • Privileges and Immunities Clause: Article IV, Section 2 -- states cannot discriminate against citizens of other states in fundamental rights such as travel, conducting business, and accessing courts.

  • Extradition: Article IV, Section 2 -- a state must return a person charged with a crime who has fled to another state upon the demanding state's request.

  • Interstate Compacts: Formal agreements between two or more states, requiring congressional consent (Art. I, Sec. 10). Example: Port Authority of New York and New Jersey.

  • Guarantee Clause: Article IV, Section 4 -- the United States guarantees every state a "Republican Form of Government."

  • Tenth Amendment: Reserves all powers not delegated to the United States or prohibited to states, to "the States respectively, or to the people." The Anti-Federalists' legacy; limits national government to enumerated and implied powers.

Vocabulary Review

Enumerated powers: Powers explicitly listed in the Constitution as belonging to the national government.

Implied powers: Powers not explicitly stated but inferred from the Necessary and Proper Clause; confirmed in McCulloch v. Maryland.

Reserved powers: Powers retained by state governments under the Tenth Amendment.

Concurrent powers: Powers shared by both the national and state governments simultaneously.

Supremacy Clause: Article VI provision making valid federal law supreme over conflicting state law.

Full Faith and Credit Clause: Requirement that states recognize the legal acts and court judgments of other states.

Privileges and Immunities Clause: Protection against state discrimination against out-of-state citizens in fundamental rights.

Extradition: Constitutional obligation of a state to return a fugitive criminal to the state where the crime was committed.

Interstate compact: A formal agreement between states, requiring congressional approval.

Police power: State authority to enact laws protecting the public health, safety, welfare, and morals of citizens -- a core reserved power.

Key Takeaways

  • The Constitution divides power along three lines: enumerated (national), reserved (state), and concurrent (both).
  • The Necessary and Proper Clause gives Congress implied powers beyond explicitly listed authorities -- the constitutional basis for a flexible, expandable federal government.
  • The Tenth Amendment embodies the Anti-Federalist principle that the national government holds only those powers granted to it; all others belong to the states or the people.
  • The Supremacy Clause does not give unlimited federal power -- it only makes valid federal law supreme. If Congress acts outside its constitutional authority, the Tenth Amendment reserves that area to the states.
  • Interstate relations are governed by specific constitutional provisions: Full Faith and Credit, Privileges and Immunities, and extradition requirements prevent states from becoming isolated legal islands.
  • The key distinction between the Articles of Confederation and the Constitution: the Articles used "expressly delegated," strictly limiting national power; the Tenth Amendment omitted "expressly," opening the door to implied powers.

AP Exam Connection

  • Stimulus-based MC questions frequently present scenarios where national and state authority conflict -- identify which level holds the power and which constitutional provision applies.
  • The Necessary and Proper Clause vs. the Tenth Amendment is a classic AP tension: know how each expands or limits federal power.
  • Concept Application FRQs may present a scenario (a state law conflicting with federal statute) and ask you to identify the constitutional clause and predict the outcome.
  • SCOTUS Comparison FRQs often feature McCulloch v. Maryland -- know facts, issue, holding (broad implied powers), and significance, and apply its reasoning to novel situations.
  • The layers of federalism diagram -- national/enumerated, state/reserved, shared/concurrent -- is a frequent source for quantitative analysis questions.
