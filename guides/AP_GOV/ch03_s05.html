Study Guide: Topic 3.5 — Second Amendment Rights

Key Concepts

  • The Second Amendment's two-clause structure has generated decades of debate: does it protect an individual right to bear arms, or a collective right tied to militia service?
  • District of Columbia v. Heller (2008) resolved the debate in favor of an individual right: the Second Amendment protects an individual's right to possess firearms for traditionally lawful purposes, including self-defense in the home.
  • McDonald v. City of Chicago (2010) incorporated the Second Amendment against state and local governments through the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause.
  • Both Heller and McDonald emphasized that the right is "not unlimited": regulations on felons, mentally ill individuals, and firearms in sensitive places remain presumptively lawful.
  • New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen (2022) established a new test requiring firearms regulations to be consistent with the historical tradition of firearm regulation.

Vocabulary Review

  • Collective right interpretation: The view that the Second Amendment protects only the right to bear arms in connection with service in a state militia — a right belonging to the states, not individuals.
  • Individual right interpretation: The view that the Second Amendment protects an individual's right to keep and bear arms for traditionally lawful purposes, including self-defense, regardless of militia service.
  • District of Columbia v. Heller (2008): The Supreme Court case holding for the first time that the Second Amendment protects an individual right to possess firearms unconnected with militia service.
  • McDonald v. City of Chicago (2010): The Supreme Court case incorporating the Second Amendment against state and local governments through the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause.
  • Selective incorporation: The doctrine by which the Supreme Court applies Bill of Rights provisions to state governments; McDonald completed the incorporation of the Second Amendment.
  • Bruen test: The standard from New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen (2022): firearms regulations must be consistent with the historical tradition of firearm regulation in the United States.
  • Red flag laws: State laws allowing courts to temporarily confiscate firearms from individuals deemed to pose a danger to themselves or others.

Important Details

  • The Second Amendment's prefatory clause ("A well regulated Militia, being necessary to the security of a free State") and operative clause ("the right of the people to keep and bear Arms, shall not be infringed") have been at the center of the interpretive debate. Heller held that the prefatory clause announces a purpose but does not limit the operative clause's protection of individual rights.
  • Otis McDonald, the plaintiff in McDonald v. Chicago, was a retired maintenance engineer in a high-crime Chicago neighborhood who wanted a handgun for home defense. His case provided the vehicle for incorporating the Second Amendment.
  • The McDonald plurality applied incorporation through the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment (the standard selective incorporation vehicle). Justice Thomas wrote separately, arguing incorporation should proceed through the Privileges or Immunities Clause instead.
  • Even after Heller and McDonald, the scope of permissible regulations remains contested. Courts have upheld or struck down assault weapons bans, high-capacity magazine restrictions, background check requirements, and carry restrictions.
  • Bruen (2022) struck down New York's "may issue" concealed carry licensing scheme and replaced the previous two-step interest-balancing test with a purely historical analysis.

Review Questions

1. Explain the individual right and collective right interpretations of the Second Amendment. The collective right interpretation holds that the Second Amendment protects the right to bear arms only in connection with service in a "well regulated Militia" — the right belongs to the states' authority to maintain militias, not to private individuals. The individual right interpretation holds that "the right of the people" protects an individual's right to keep and bear arms for traditionally lawful purposes including self-defense, regardless of any militia connection. Heller (2008) settled the Supreme Court debate in favor of the individual right interpretation.

2. What did Heller decide, and what limitations did the Court note? Heller held for the first time that the Second Amendment protects an individual right to possess firearms unconnected with militia service, and struck down Washington D.C.'s handgun ban. However, the Court was explicit that the right is "not unlimited" — regulations on felons and mentally ill individuals possessing weapons, restrictions on firearms in sensitive places like schools, conditions on commercial sales, and prohibitions on dangerous and unusual weapons all remain presumptively lawful.

3. How did McDonald v. Chicago extend Heller? Heller struck down a Washington D.C. handgun ban, but D.C. is a federal district, so the decision only applied against the federal government. McDonald extended Heller's holding to state and local governments by incorporating the Second Amendment through the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause. After McDonald, the individual right recognized in Heller constrains all levels of government.

4. Why is McDonald v. Chicago significant for the doctrine of selective incorporation more broadly? McDonald (2010) represents one of the most recent major applications of selective incorporation. It demonstrates that the process of applying Bill of Rights provisions to the states is ongoing — not a historical artifact but an active constitutional doctrine. McDonald also illustrates the standard criteria: the Court found that the right to keep and bear arms is "fundamental to our scheme of ordered liberty" and "deeply rooted in this Nation's history and tradition," satisfying the requirements for incorporation.

5. What new test did Bruen (2022) establish for evaluating Second Amendment challenges? Bruen rejected the previous two-step interest-balancing test used by lower courts and established a purely historical test: to justify a firearms regulation, the government must demonstrate that the regulation is consistent with the historical tradition of firearm regulation in the United States at the time the Second Amendment was ratified. If there is no historical analogue, the regulation is presumptively unconstitutional.

Summary

The Second Amendment's individual right to keep and bear arms was definitively established in District of Columbia v. Heller (2008) and extended to state and local governments in McDonald v. City of Chicago (2010), one of the most recent major selective incorporation decisions. While both cases affirmed that the right is not unlimited — leaving room for many firearms regulations — the scope of permissible restrictions continues to be contested. Bruen (2022) established a new historical test that has created significant uncertainty about which regulations can survive constitutional challenge.
