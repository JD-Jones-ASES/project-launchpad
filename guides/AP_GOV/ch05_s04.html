Study Guide: Topic 5.4 — How and Why Political Parties Change and Adapt

Key Concepts

  • American political history can be divided into distinct party eras separated by critical elections in which voter loyalties shift dramatically and a new dominant coalition emerges.
  • Three major realignments have reshaped the party system: the Civil War realignment (1860s), the New Deal realignment (1930s), and the Civil Rights realignment (1960s).
  • The most consequential recent realignment was the Civil Rights era: Democratic support for civil rights legislation drove white Southern voters into the Republican Party, completing a regional flip by the 1990s.
  • Dealignment—a trend in which voters move away from both major parties without strong attachment to either—has grown since the 1970s, with independents now representing the largest identification group.
  • Many self-identified independents still lean toward and vote reliably for one party; the growth of the "independent" label does not necessarily mean increased moderation.

Vocabulary Review

  • Party era: An extended period during which one party or coalition dominates national politics.
  • Critical election (realigning election): An election in which voter loyalties shift dramatically and a new dominant coalition emerges, defining a new party era.
  • Realignment: A major, durable shift in the partisan loyalties of a significant segment of voters, often triggered by a major political crisis or policy choice.
  • New Deal coalition: FDR's broad electoral coalition of Southern whites, African Americans, labor unions, urban ethnic voters, intellectuals, and the poor; gave Democrats congressional dominance from the 1930s through the mid-1990s.
  • Southern Strategy: Republican electoral strategy of appealing to white Southern racial anxieties to attract voters who were abandoning the Democratic Party after its embrace of civil rights legislation.
  • Dealignment: A trend in which voters move away from both major parties without forming a strong attachment to either; reflected in growth of independent identification.
  • Independent: A voter who does not identify strongly with either major party; the largest self-identified group in polling since the 1970s, though many independents "lean" toward one party.
  • Coalition: A broad alliance of different groups united behind a common political platform or candidate.

Important Details

The Three Major Realignments

1. Civil War Realignment (1860s)

  • Catalyst: Lincoln's election (1860) and the Civil War
  • The newly formed Republican Party captured Northern and Western voters; championed union, abolition, industrial development
  • Democrats became the party of the white South
  • Result: "Solid South"—Southern states voted reliably Democratic for nearly a century after Reconstruction

2. New Deal Realignment (1930s)

  • Catalyst: Great Depression and FDR's New Deal
  • Coalition assembled: Southern whites + African Americans + labor unions + urban ethnic voters + intellectuals + poor voters
  • Redefined Democrats as champions of active federal government and social safety net
  • Democrats controlled Congress for most of the next 60 years

3. Civil Rights Realignment (1960s)

  • Catalyst: Democratic Party's embrace of Civil Rights Act (1964) and VRA (1965)
  • White Southern voters began migrating to Republicans
  • Republican "Southern Strategy" accelerated this shift by appealing to white racial anxieties
  • By the 1990s: the South had become reliably Republican in presidential elections

Dealignment: Key Points

  • Since the 1970s, the proportion identifying as independent has grown steadily
  • More Americans now identify as independent than as either Democrat or Republican (Gallup)
  • BUT: Many "independents" lean toward one party and vote nearly as reliably as partisans
  • Dealignment does NOT necessarily mean moderation

Why Dealignment Occurs

  1. Declining trust in institutions
  2. Perception that neither party represents one's interests
  3. Higher education levels (correlated with issue-based voting)
  4. Generational shifts—younger voters resist fixed partisan labels

Review Questions

1. What is a critical election, and how is it different from a normal election?

Answer: A critical election (also called a realigning election) is one in which voter loyalties shift dramatically, producing a new dominant coalition that defines politics for years or decades. Unlike normal elections, which are won and lost within an existing partisan alignment, critical elections reorganize who votes for which party. The 1932 election (FDR's landslide) is the classic example: it shattered Republican dominance and created the New Deal coalition that shaped American politics for two generations.

2. Explain how the Democratic Party's support for civil rights legislation in the 1960s led to a major realignment.

Answer: For nearly a century, the Democratic Party had held together an internally contradictory coalition that included both Northern liberals and white Southern conservatives who depended on segregation. When President Johnson signed the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965—both championed by Northern Democrats over fierce Southern Democratic opposition—white Southern voters felt betrayed. Republican strategists (using the "Southern Strategy") appealed to these voters by emphasizing states' rights and racial resentment. The migration of white Southerners to the Republican Party and African Americans firmly to the Democratic Party fundamentally reshaped both coalitions, completing a regional flip that was fully visible by the 1990s.

3. Distinguish between realignment and dealignment. Which trend better characterizes current American party politics?

Answer: Realignment involves voters shifting from one party to another, replacing one dominant coalition with another. Dealignment involves voters moving away from both major parties without forming a strong attachment to either. Current trends better reflect dealignment: the proportion identifying as independent has grown from about 29% in 1972 to approximately 47% by 2024, while both party identifications have declined. However, many self-identified independents still lean consistently toward one party and vote for it reliably, suggesting that some of this "dealignment" is superficial—voters resist the label "Democrat" or "Republican" but still vote predictably along partisan lines.

4. What was the New Deal coalition, and why did it eventually fracture?

Answer: The New Deal coalition was FDR's broad electoral alliance assembled during the Great Depression, uniting Southern whites, African Americans, labor unions, urban ethnic immigrants, intellectuals, and low-income voters. It gave Democrats control of Congress for most of the period from the 1930s to the 1990s. The coalition fractured primarily because it contained internal contradictions: Southern whites who supported New Deal economic programs strongly opposed racial equality. When the national Democratic Party chose civil rights over Southern loyalty in the 1960s, the Southern white component of the coalition departed, leaving a more ideologically coherent but smaller coalition.

5. Why does the growth of independent identification not necessarily mean that American voters are becoming more moderate?

Answer: A common assumption is that independents are ideological moderates who are frustrated with both parties' extremism. In reality, research shows that many independents are "closet partisans" who lean toward one party and vote for it nearly as reliably as registered party members. Their independent identification reflects aversion to the party label rather than moderate ideology. Some independents are actually highly ideological—they just refuse to formally affiliate. Gallup and ANES data show that party identification numbers overstate political moderation if you assume all independents are centrists.

Summary

American political parties change through realignment—dramatic shifts in voter loyalties triggered by major crises or policy choices—and through dealignment, the gradual detachment of voters from both parties. Three major realignments have reshaped the system: the Civil War (1860s), the New Deal (1930s), and the Civil Rights era (1960s), the last of which produced the regional alignment we live with today. Since the 1970s, dealignment has grown, with independents now the largest self-identified group. But many independents still vote predictably for one party, making the "independent" label more culturally expressive than behaviorally significant.
