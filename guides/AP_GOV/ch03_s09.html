Study Guide: Topic 3.9 — Right to Privacy

Key Concepts

  • Right to privacy: Although the word "privacy" never appears in the Constitution, the Supreme Court has recognized a constitutional right to privacy derived from the "penumbras" and "emanations" of several Bill of Rights provisions, as articulated in Griswold v. Connecticut (1965).

  • Penumbra doctrine: Justice William O. Douglas reasoned in Griswold that several constitutional provisions — the First, Third, Fourth, Fifth, and Ninth Amendments — cast "penumbras" (implied zones) of privacy that together establish a constitutionally protected right to privacy.

  • Griswold v. Connecticut (1965): Landmark case striking down a Connecticut law banning contraceptives for married couples. Established the constitutional right to privacy and the penumbra doctrine. First application of privacy to protect intimate personal decisions from government interference.

  • Roe v. Wade (1973): Extended the right to privacy to include a woman's decision to terminate a pregnancy. Established a trimester framework balancing privacy rights against state interests. Although overturned by Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization (2022), Roe remains a required AP Government case because it illustrates the constitutional principle of unenumerated rights derived from the right to privacy.

  • Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization (2022): Overruled Roe and Planned Parenthood v. Casey (1992), holding that the Constitution does not confer a right to abortion and returning authority over abortion regulation to the states.

  • Substantive due process: A constitutional doctrine under the Fourteenth Amendment that protects certain fundamental rights — including privacy — from government interference, even if not explicitly enumerated in the Constitution. Used by Justices Harlan and White as an alternative basis for privacy rights.

  • Third-party doctrine: Traditional doctrine holding that individuals have no reasonable expectation of privacy in information voluntarily shared with third parties (e.g., banks, phone companies). The Supreme Court began limiting this doctrine in Carpenter v. United States (2018).

  • Riley v. California (2014): Supreme Court held unanimously that police must obtain a warrant before searching the digital contents of a cell phone seized during an arrest. The Court recognized that cell phones contain "the privacies of life."

  • Carpenter v. United States (2018): Extended Fourth Amendment warrant requirements to accessing historical cell-site location records, recognizing that digital location tracking reveals intimate details of a person's life.

Vocabulary Review

  • Penumbra: An implied right derived from the "emanations" of explicitly stated constitutional protections; the doctrinal basis for the right to privacy in Griswold v. Connecticut.
  • Unenumerated rights: Rights not explicitly listed in the Constitution but recognized by the Supreme Court as constitutionally protected; the Ninth Amendment reserves these rights to the people.
  • Substantive due process: Constitutional doctrine holding that the Due Process Clause protects fundamental liberties from government interference, not merely procedural fairness.
  • Trimester framework: The analytical structure established in Roe v. Wade dividing pregnancy into three stages, each with different levels of permissible government regulation.
  • Undue burden standard: The test from Planned Parenthood v. Casey (1992) that replaced the Roe trimester framework; prohibited abortion regulations that place a substantial obstacle in the path of a woman seeking an abortion before fetal viability.
  • Third-party doctrine: Rule that individuals voluntarily sharing information with third parties lose their Fourth Amendment expectation of privacy in that information.
  • Cell-site location information (CSLI): Records generated by cell phone towers that reveal the approximate location of a cell phone user over time; subject to Fourth Amendment warrant requirements after Carpenter (2018).

Key Takeaways

  • The right to privacy is not explicitly stated in the Constitution but is derived from the penumbras of the First, Third, Fourth, Fifth, and Ninth Amendments (Griswold, 1965).
  • Roe v. Wade (1973) applied this privacy right to reproductive decisions but was overruled by Dobbs (2022); Roe remains required AP content because it illustrates unenumerated constitutional rights.
  • The digital age has created new privacy challenges: Riley (2014) extended warrant requirements to cell phone searches; Carpenter (2018) extended them to digital location tracking.
  • The third-party doctrine — that sharing information with others destroys the expectation of privacy — is under increasing judicial pressure in the digital era.
  • Privacy rights are not absolute; they must be balanced against legitimate government interests such as law enforcement and national security.

AP Exam Connection

Required case: Roe v. Wade (1973) — tested on the constitutional principle of unenumerated rights derived from the right to privacy, the role of the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause, and the tension between individual liberty and government regulation. Students should know that Dobbs (2022) overturned Roe's holding but that the constitutional principles Roe illustrates remain on the AP exam.

Likely question types: SCOTUS Comparison (comparing Griswold or Roe to a non-required privacy case), Concept Application (scenario involving government surveillance or digital privacy), Argument Essay (privacy vs. security trade-offs).

Key connection: Topic 3.9 connects to Topic 3.8 (Fourth Amendment digital privacy cases), Topic 3.6 (balancing liberty and national security), and the broader concept of unenumerated rights under the Ninth Amendment.
