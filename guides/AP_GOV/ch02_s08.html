Study Guide: Topic 2.8 - The Judicial Branch

Key Concepts

Article III establishes the judicial branch, vesting the judicial Power of the United States in the Supreme Court and such inferior courts as Congress creates. It provides life tenure and salary protection to guarantee judicial independence.

Original Jurisdiction is the authority to hear a case for the first time. The Supreme Court's original jurisdiction is narrow: cases involving ambassadors and cases where a state is a party.

Appellate Jurisdiction is the authority to review decisions of lower courts. The Supreme Court's appellate jurisdiction is broad and largely discretionary.

Federal Court Hierarchy operates on three levels:

  • U.S. District Courts (94 districts): trial courts; facts established, juries deliberate. Most federal cases end here.
  • U.S. Courts of Appeals (13 circuits): review questions of law in three-judge panels; no new evidence accepted.
  • Supreme Court (9 justices): final arbiter of constitutional meaning via discretionary certiorari.

Senatorial Courtesy is the informal norm giving home-state senators significant influence over district court appointments.

Originalism is the judicial philosophy that the Constitution should be interpreted according to its meaning at ratification. Associated with Justice Antonin Scalia.

Living Constitution is the competing philosophy that constitutional meaning evolves with contemporary conditions. Associated with Justice William Brennan.

Federalist No. 78 is Hamilton's defense of the federal judiciary describing courts as the least dangerous branch because they possess neither FORCE nor WILL, but merely judgment. He argued for life tenure to insulate judges from political pressure and laid the groundwork for judicial review.

Key Takeaways

  • Article III creates only the Supreme Court; all other federal courts are created by Congress through legislation.
  • Life tenure and salary protection are structural guarantees of judicial independence.
  • The three-level hierarchy channels cases: most begin and end at district courts; appeals go to circuit courts; only a tiny fraction reach the Supreme Court.
  • The nomination and confirmation process has become intensely politicized, reflecting the stakes of lifetime appointments.
  • Originalism and the living constitution approach lead to different outcomes on contested constitutional questions.
  • Hamilton's Federalist No. 78 argues that judicial independence and judicial review are essential to protect constitutional limits even from democratic majorities.
