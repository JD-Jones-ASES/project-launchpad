Study Guide: Topic 5.1 — Voting Rights and Models of Voting Behavior

Key Concepts

  • The right to vote expanded gradually through constitutional amendments and legislation, moving from a narrow franchise restricted to propertied white men toward universal suffrage over two centuries.
  • A critical distinction in voting rights history is the gap between de jure rights (rights in law) and de facto rights (rights in practice); legal guarantees alone did not produce meaningful access without enforcement.
  • The Voting Rights Act of 1965 was the single most consequential piece of voting rights legislation, directly attacking the discriminatory mechanisms that had made the 15th Amendment ineffective for nearly a century.
  • Political scientists use four major models—rational choice, retrospective, prospective, and party identification—to explain why voters make the choices they do in the booth.
  • Party identification is empirically the single strongest predictor of vote choice, but it is less useful for explaining the behavior of independents and swing voters.

Vocabulary Review

  • Suffrage: The right to vote; also called the franchise.
  • Franchise: The legal right to vote; synonymous with suffrage.
  • Poll tax: A fee required to be paid before voting; abolished in federal elections by the 24th Amendment (1964).
  • Literacy test: A requirement that voters demonstrate reading ability, used primarily in the South to disenfranchise Black voters; banned by the Voting Rights Act of 1965.
  • Grandfather clause: A provision exempting individuals whose ancestors had voted from new voting restrictions; used to exempt white voters from literacy tests and poll taxes.
  • Voting Rights Act of 1965 (VRA): Landmark legislation banning literacy tests and establishing federal oversight (preclearance) of election procedures in jurisdictions with histories of discrimination.
  • Preclearance: The requirement under Section 5 of the VRA that certain states obtain federal approval before changing voting procedures.
  • Rational choice voting: A model of voting behavior in which voters calculate costs and benefits of each candidate's positions and vote for the one that best serves their self-interest.
  • Retrospective voting: Voting based on evaluation of past performance; asking "Am I better off than before?"
  • Prospective voting: Voting based on candidates' promises and future policy plans.
  • Party identification: A long-term psychological attachment to a political party, often formed early in life, that is the strongest predictor of vote choice.
  • De jure: By law; referring to rights or conditions established in formal legal codes.
  • De facto: In practice or reality; referring to actual conditions as distinct from legal requirements.

The Gap Between Law and Practice (15th Amendment)

The 15th Amendment (1870) theoretically guaranteed Black men the right to vote, but Southern states immediately responded with:

  • Poll taxes
  • Literacy tests (applied discriminatorily)
  • Grandfather clauses (exempting whites from new requirements)
  • Outright violence and intimidation

Black voter registration in Mississippi was just 6.7% in 1964. After the VRA, it rose to 59.8% by 1968.

The Voting Rights Act of 1965

  • Signed by President Lyndon B. Johnson
  • Banned literacy tests nationwide
  • Established federal oversight of jurisdictions with histories of discrimination
  • Section 5 required "preclearance" before changing voting procedures
  • Ratified in record time—26th Amendment passed in only 107 days

Four Models of Voting Behavior

  1. Rational Choice: Voters as rational cost-benefit calculators. Criticized for overestimating voter policy knowledge and underestimating identity/emotion.
  2. Retrospective: "Am I better off?" Rewards/punishes incumbents based on past performance. Presidential approval tracks closely with economic indicators.
  3. Prospective: Future-focused; evaluates candidates based on promises. Campaign promises, debates, and policy papers matter most.
  4. Party Identification: Psychological attachment to party formed through socialization. Most stable and most predictive factor in vote choice overall.

Review Questions

1. What barriers did Southern states use to prevent Black citizens from voting despite the 15th Amendment?

Answer: Southern states used poll taxes (fees to vote), literacy tests (applied selectively and impossibly to Black applicants), grandfather clauses (exempting those whose ancestors had voted—i.e., whites), and outright violence and intimidation. These barriers kept Black voter registration in the Deep South in the single digits for nearly a century after the 15th Amendment's ratification.

2. Why was the Voting Rights Act of 1965 necessary if the 15th Amendment already guaranteed the right to vote?

Answer: The 15th Amendment established a de jure right but state-level discriminatory practices made it meaningless de facto. The VRA attacked these practices directly by banning literacy tests, requiring federal preclearance of election law changes in discriminatory jurisdictions, and authorizing federal examiners to register voters. It converted the paper guarantee into a practical reality.

3. What does the rational choice model of voting assume, and what is the main criticism of that assumption?

Answer: Rational choice assumes voters are rational actors who compare candidates' policy positions and select the one that best serves their self-interest. The main criticism is that this model overestimates how much detailed policy knowledge voters actually have and underestimates the powerful roles of emotion, group identity, habit, and party loyalty in shaping decisions.

4. How does retrospective voting explain presidential approval ratings tracking economic conditions?

Answer: Retrospective voters ask "Am I better off now than I was before?" If the economy is strong, they reward the president and the incumbent party with approval and votes. If the economy has deteriorated, they punish the incumbent. Since economic conditions directly affect how well off people feel, economic indicators and presidential approval ratings move together.

5. Why is party identification considered the single strongest predictor of vote choice?

Answer: Party identification is a long-term psychological attachment formed through political socialization (family, community, peers) that tends to be remarkably stable over time. In most elections, voters simply support the candidate of their party. Because party ID is so durable and so widespread, it explains more variation in vote choice than any other single factor, including candidate qualities, campaign events, or issue positions.

Summary

The history of American voting rights is a story of gradual expansion from a narrow founding franchise to near-universal suffrage, punctuated by the persistent gap between legal guarantees and practical access. Constitutional amendments established the legal framework, but legislation—especially the Voting Rights Act of 1965—was essential to making those rights real. Political scientists explain how voters actually make decisions through four models: rational choice (policy calculation), retrospective (past performance), prospective (future promises), and party identification (psychological attachment), with the last proving the most consistently predictive of vote choice in most elections.
