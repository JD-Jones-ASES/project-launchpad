Study Guide: Topic 2.6 — Expansion of Presidential Power

Key Concepts

  • Presidential power has expanded dramatically since the founding. The early "Whig presidency" was restrained; modern presidents command an executive branch of over 2 million civilian employees and a multi-trillion-dollar budget.
  • Executive orders have become one of the most significant instruments of presidential authority—transforming civil rights, war powers, immigration, and environmental policy without congressional action.
  • National security and emergency powers have expanded most dramatically. Presidents have committed forces to major conflicts—Korea, Vietnam, Gulf War, Afghanistan, Iraq—without formal congressional declarations of war.
  • Signing statements allow presidents to signal interpretive intent or challenge provisions of legislation they sign, functioning as a quasi-line-item veto.
  • Congressional delegation has amplified presidential power by transferring policy-making authority to executive agencies, which the president oversees and directs.

Vocabulary Review

Term Definition
Whig presidency A pre-Civil War conception of the presidency as restrained and deferential to Congress on most domestic matters
Executive order A directive from the president managing operations of the federal government; derives from Article II; has the force of law
Emancipation Proclamation Lincoln's 1863 executive order declaring enslaved people in Confederate states free; issued as a war measure under Commander in Chief authority
Signing statement A written comment issued by the president when signing legislation, sometimes indicating how the president interprets or plans to enforce specific provisions
National Emergencies Act (1976) Law requiring presidents to formally declare emergencies, granting access to over 100 special statutory powers
Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF) Congressional authorization granted after September 11, 2001 that multiple presidents have used to justify broad military operations globally
Administrative state The vast network of executive departments, independent agencies, and regulatory commissions that exercise broad policy-making authority
Congressional delegation Congress's transfer of policy-making authority to executive agencies through broad legislative mandates

Important Details

Historical Arc of Presidential Power Expansion

  • Early republic: "Whig presidency" — restrained executive deferring to Congress (Monroe, Buchanan)
  • Civil War transformation: Lincoln claimed sweeping wartime powers (Emancipation Proclamation as executive order)
  • Progressive Era/New Deal: FDR greatly expanded executive power and the administrative state
  • Cold War: National security apparatus, undeclared wars, intelligence agencies
  • Modern era: Obama/Trump/Biden executive orders on immigration, environment, public health—often reversing predecessors' actions

Landmark Executive Orders

Order President Content
Emancipation Proclamation (1863) Lincoln Freed enslaved people in Confederate states; war measure under Commander in Chief power
Executive Order 9066 (1942) FDR Authorized internment of 120,000 Japanese Americans; upheld in Korematsu v. U.S. (1944); repudiated in Trump v. Hawaii (2018)
Executive Order 9981 (1948) Truman Desegregated the U.S. armed forces; major civil rights step without congressional action
Modern examples Obama/Trump/Biden Immigration enforcement, environmental regulation, travel restrictions—frequently reversing predecessors

Signing Statements and Their Controversy

  • George W. Bush challenged over 1,200 legislative provisions through signing statements
  • Critics: signing statements create a de facto line-item veto (the actual line-item veto was struck down in Clinton v. City of New York, 1998)
  • The American Bar Association has criticized the practice as a unilateral expansion of executive power

Congressional Delegation and the Administrative State

  • Congress passes broad laws directing agencies to act "in the public interest" or set "reasonable" standards
  • This transfers policy-making to unelected agency officials who serve under the president
  • Result: president shapes policy through appointments, executive orders, and regulatory review without new legislation
  • The Office of Management and Budget (OMB) reviews major regulations, giving the White House centralized control over agency rule-making

Review Questions

1. Identify two historical executive orders that significantly expanded presidential power, and explain the constitutional basis for each. Lincoln's Emancipation Proclamation (1863) was issued as a war measure under the Commander in Chief power—Lincoln argued that freeing enslaved people in Confederate states was a military necessity, as it deprived the Confederacy of labor and encouraged defections. It transformed the war's purpose without congressional action. FDR's Executive Order 9966 (1948), issued by Truman, desegregated the armed forces using the president's authority as Commander in Chief over military personnel, achieving a major civil rights reform Congress had refused to legislate. Both examples show how the Commander in Chief power has been used to justify sweeping domestic policy changes during wartime or national emergency conditions.

2. How has congressional delegation of authority contributed to the growth of executive power? Congressional delegation amplifies presidential power by transferring the substance of policy-making to executive agencies. When Congress passes broad laws directing agencies to set "safe" standards, regulate "in the public interest," or address "unreasonable" risks, it effectively delegates legislative power to unelected bureaucrats who serve under the president. The president can then shape policy by appointing agency heads who share presidential priorities, issuing executive orders directing agency priorities, and using the OMB to review and shape proposed regulations. This means the president can advance a policy agenda through the administrative state even when Congress declines to pass specific legislation. The result is an executive branch that writes thousands of pages of binding regulations annually without direct congressional action on each rule.

3. What are signing statements, and why are they controversial? Signing statements are written comments issued by the president when signing legislation into law. Sometimes ceremonial, they have been used increasingly by presidents to declare specific provisions unconstitutional, signal plans to interpret provisions in a particular way, or indicate that certain sections will not be fully enforced. They are controversial because critics argue they allow the president to unilaterally modify legislation—effectively creating a line-item veto that the Supreme Court struck down in Clinton v. City of New York (1998). The American Bar Association has criticized signing statements as a violation of the separation of powers, arguing that if a president finds a provision unconstitutional, the proper remedy is to veto the entire bill, not sign it with reservations.

4. Explain the significance of the Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF) passed after September 11, 2001. The AUMF authorized the president to use all necessary and appropriate force against those responsible for the 9/11 attacks. Presidents Bush, Obama, Trump, and Biden have all used this single authorization to justify military operations in numerous countries far beyond Afghanistan—the original target—including Yemen, Syria, Somalia, and others. The AUMF has become, in effect, a broad standing congressional authorization for the executive branch to conduct military operations globally, dramatically expanding war-making authority without requiring specific congressional approval for each operation. Critics argue the AUMF represents Congress's abdication of its constitutional war-declaring authority; defenders argue it provides a necessary legal basis for conducting counter-terrorism operations.

5. How does the president use the administrative state to exercise policy power? The administrative state—the network of executive departments, independent agencies, and regulatory commissions—gives the president enormous policy-making tools without requiring new legislation. The president shapes agency direction through appointments (choosing agency heads who share presidential priorities), executive orders (directing agencies to change enforcement priorities or interpret statutes differently), and regulatory review (OMB reviews major regulations, allowing the White House to approve, modify, or delay agency rules). When Congress refuses to act on a presidential priority, the president can often achieve similar policy goals through agency rule-making. When a new president takes office with different priorities, executive orders and agency rules can be reversed or replaced—as demonstrated by Obama, Trump, and Biden each reversing their predecessors' major executive actions on immigration and environmental policy.

Summary

Presidential power has expanded far beyond the Framers' original design, driven by national crises, congressional delegation, and the growth of the administrative state. Executive orders, national security claims, signing statements, and control over the regulatory bureaucracy give modern presidents policy-making tools that operate largely without direct congressional approval. Landmark executive orders—from Lincoln's Emancipation Proclamation to FDR's internment order to Truman's desegregation of the military—illustrate both the transformative potential and the serious risks of expansive executive power. Congressional delegation has become a primary mechanism of this expansion, as Congress transfers increasing policy detail to executive agencies that the president can direct.
