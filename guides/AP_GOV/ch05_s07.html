Study Guide: Topic 5.7 — Groups Influencing Policy Outcomes

Key Concepts

  • The modern campaign finance landscape features multiple types of organizations with different legal rules governing how much they can raise, how they can spend, and whether they must disclose their donors.
  • PACs (political action committees) and Super PACs are fundamentally different: PACs have strict contribution limits and give directly to candidates; Super PACs can raise and spend unlimited amounts on independent expenditures but cannot coordinate with candidates.
  • The rise of 501(c)(4) "social welfare" nonprofits has enabled "dark money"—unlimited political spending with no donor disclosure requirements—representing a serious transparency concern.
  • Citizens United v. FEC (2010) is a required Supreme Court case: it held that political speech does not lose First Amendment protection simply because its speaker is a corporation, striking down BCRA restrictions on corporate independent expenditures.
  • The practical consequence of Citizens United was the creation of Super PACs, which have dramatically increased outside spending in federal elections—from $338 million in 2008 to over $1 billion in 2012 and continuing to grow.

Vocabulary Review

  • Political Action Committee (PAC): An organization that raises money from members and donates directly to candidates; regulated by the FEC with strict contribution limits ($5,000 per candidate per election); must disclose donors.
  • Super PAC: An organization created after Citizens United that may raise and spend unlimited amounts on independent expenditures; cannot coordinate with campaigns; must disclose donors.
  • Independent expenditure: Spending on political communications (ads, mailers) that advocates for or against candidates but is not coordinated with the candidate's campaign.
  • 527 organization: A tax-exempt political group named for IRC Section 527; can raise unlimited funds for voter mobilization and issue advocacy; must disclose donors.
  • 501(c)(4) organization: A "social welfare" nonprofit that may engage in political activity as a non-primary purpose; does NOT have to disclose donors—hence "dark money."
  • Dark money: Political spending by organizations that do not disclose their donors; primarily associated with 501(c)(4) nonprofits; criticized for enabling anonymous influence over elections.
  • Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act (BCRA/McCain-Feingold): 2002 law that banned soft money to national parties and restricted "electioneering communications" within 30/60 days of elections; partially struck down by Citizens United.
  • Electioneering communication: A broadcast advertisement mentioning a candidate within 30 days of a primary or 60 days of a general election; regulated by BCRA, restrictions partially struck down by Citizens United.
  • Federal Election Commission (FEC): The federal agency that regulates campaign finance and enforces campaign finance laws.

Citizens United v. FEC (2010) — Required Case

Facts: Citizens United, a conservative nonprofit, produced Hillary: The Movie, a documentary critical of Senator Hillary Clinton during the 2008 primary. The FEC blocked its distribution under BCRA, which prohibited corporate-funded electioneering communications within 30 days of a primary.

Constitutional Issue: Does BCRA's prohibition on corporate-funded electioneering communications violate the First Amendment's free speech protection?

Holding: Yes (5-4). The Supreme Court struck down BCRA's restrictions on corporate independent expenditures as unconstitutional.

Reasoning: Justice Kennedy's majority held that political speech does not lose First Amendment protection because the speaker is a corporation rather than an individual. The government may not suppress political speech based on the speaker's corporate identity. The Court distinguished between direct contributions (which may be regulated to prevent corruption) and independent expenditures (which pose less corruption risk because they are not coordinated with candidates).

Dissent: Justice Stevens argued that corporations are not people, the Founders did not intend the First Amendment to protect corporate political spending, and unlimited corporate spending threatens democratic self-governance by drowning out ordinary citizens' voices.

Significance: Combined with SpeechNow.org v. FEC (D.C. Circuit, 2010), Citizens United directly created Super PACs. Outside spending in federal elections rose from $338 million (2008) to over $1 billion (2012) and has continued growing.

Key AP Distinction: Contributions vs. Expenditures

From Buckley v. Valeo (1976):

  • Contributions (direct donations to candidates) may be limited → anti-corruption justification
  • Expenditures (independent spending) may not be limited → protected First Amendment speech

This distinction is the constitutional foundation for why Super PACs are legal.

Review Questions

1. What is the fundamental legal difference between a PAC and a Super PAC?

Answer: A PAC is regulated by the FEC and subject to strict contribution limits ($5,000 per candidate per election). It can donate directly to candidates' campaigns. A Super PAC can raise and spend unlimited amounts on independent expenditures—ads, mailers, and other communications—but it cannot donate directly to candidates or coordinate with their campaigns. Both must disclose their donors. The key distinction: PACs = limited, direct giving; Super PACs = unlimited, independent spending.

2. What makes 501(c)(4) organizations "dark money" groups?

Answer: 501(c)(4) organizations are classified as "social welfare" nonprofits and may engage in political activity as long as it is not their "primary purpose." Unlike PACs, Super PACs, and 527s—all of which must disclose their donors—501(c)(4)s face no donor disclosure requirement. This anonymity enables wealthy individuals and corporations to spend unlimited amounts on political activity without public accountability, "darkening" the money because voters cannot identify who is funding the messages.

3. State the constitutional holding in Citizens United v. FEC and explain the reasoning behind the majority opinion.

Answer: The Court held 5-4 that BCRA's prohibition on corporate-funded electioneering communications violated the First Amendment. Justice Kennedy's majority reasoned that political speech does not lose constitutional protection simply because the speaker is a corporation rather than an individual. The First Amendment protects the content of speech, not just certain types of speakers. The majority also distinguished between direct campaign contributions (which can be regulated to prevent quid pro quo corruption) and independent expenditures (which, because they are not coordinated with candidates, pose a lesser corruption risk and thus cannot be restricted).

4. What was Justice Stevens's main argument in dissent in Citizens United?

Answer: Stevens argued that the majority's equation of corporations with individuals for First Amendment purposes was historically wrong—the Founders did not intend to extend constitutional protections of political speech to corporations. He further argued that corporations are not members of the democratic community in the same way citizens are, and that unlimited corporate spending poses a genuine threat to democratic self-governance by drowning out the voices of ordinary citizens who cannot match the spending power of large corporations. Stevens saw the decision as legalizing a form of corruption—purchasing influence over elections and policymakers.

5. What were the practical consequences of Citizens United combined with SpeechNow.org v. FEC?

Answer: Together, these 2010 decisions directly created the legal framework for Super PACs—organizations that can raise and spend unlimited amounts on independent expenditures without coordinating with candidates. Total outside spending in federal elections skyrocketed: from $338 million in 2008 (pre-Citizens United) to over $1 billion in 2012 (post-Citizens United) and continuing to climb. The decision also enabled the proliferation of dark money (501(c)(4) spending), further obscuring the sources of political influence.

Summary

The modern landscape of political money features PACs (limited, direct candidate donations; full disclosure), Super PACs (unlimited independent expenditures; full disclosure; no coordination), 527s (unlimited voter mobilization; disclosure required), and 501(c)(4) dark money groups (unlimited political activity; no disclosure). Citizens United v. FEC (2010) is the constitutional foundation of this system, holding that political speech does not lose First Amendment protection because the speaker is a corporation. The decision's practical consequence—combined with SpeechNow.org—was the creation of Super PACs and a dramatic expansion of outside spending, making Citizens United one of the most controversial decisions in modern constitutional law.
