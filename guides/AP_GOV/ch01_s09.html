Study Guide: Topic 1.9 — Federalism in Action

Key Concepts

  • Preemption: Federal law overrides conflicting state law under the Supremacy Clause. Congress may preempt state law explicitly (express preemption) or implicitly when federal regulation is so pervasive it occupies the field.

  • Concurrent Jurisdiction: Both national and state governments have authority over a policy area (e.g., environmental regulation, drug laws), creating potential for conflict when they impose different requirements.

  • Prosecutorial Discretion: The executive branch's authority to choose which laws to prioritize for enforcement. Used by the federal government to effectively tolerate state marijuana legalization despite federal prohibition.

  • Sanctuary Policies: State/local policies limiting cooperation with federal immigration enforcement. Reflect tension between federal immigration authority (enumerated power) and state/local law enforcement priorities.

  • Preemption in Arizona v. United States (2012): Supreme Court struck down key provisions of Arizona's SB 1070 (immigration law), ruling federal immigration law preempted most state enforcement mechanisms. Upheld only the provision allowing immigration status checks during lawful stops.

  • No Child Left Behind (NCLB, 2002): Used federal spending power to require states to implement standardized testing and sanctions. Critics: federal overreach into state education domain. Supporters: necessary for national equity.

  • Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA, 2015): Replaced NCLB; returned more authority to states on education accountability while maintaining federal testing requirements. Illustrates pendulum of federalism.

  • Environmental Federalism: EPA sets minimum national standards; states may exceed but not fall below them. California's Clean Air Act waiver allows stricter vehicle emissions standards -- a federalism laboratory in action.

  • COVID-19 and Federalism: Pandemic exposed tensions between federal guidance (CDC, Operation Warp Speed) and state police power (stay-at-home orders, mask mandates). NFIB v. OSHA (2022) struck down broad workplace vaccine mandate but upheld narrower mandate for healthcare workers receiving federal funds.

  • Thesis Writing: Claim + Evidence + Reasoning: AP FRQ argument structure. A claim is a defensible position, not a definition. Evidence must be specific (case law, document excerpts, examples). Reasoning explains why the evidence supports the claim.

Vocabulary Review

Preemption: Federal law displacing conflicting state law under the Supremacy Clause.

Concurrent jurisdiction: Shared authority of national and state governments over a policy area.

Prosecutorial discretion: Executive branch's choice about which laws to prioritize for enforcement.

Sanctuary policy: State or local policy limiting cooperation with federal immigration enforcement.

Cooperative federalism in practice: Federal-state collaboration in implementing shared regulatory programs (e.g., EPA minimum standards + state enforcement).

Police power: State authority to regulate public health, safety, welfare, and morals -- the constitutional basis for state COVID-19 responses (mask mandates, business closures).

Fiscal leverage: Federal government's use of spending conditions to influence state behavior in areas of state authority (education, Medicaid).

Laboratories of democracy: States as sites for policy experimentation; successful state innovations can spread nationally.

Key Takeaways

  • Federalism becomes concrete in five major contemporary policy disputes: immigration, marijuana legalization, education policy, environmental regulation, and COVID-19.
  • Immigration: Federal authority is clear (enumerated power to set naturalization rules), but states and localities bear practical costs, creating tension over enforcement cooperation.
  • Marijuana: The most dramatic federalism paradox -- 24 states have legalized recreational marijuana despite federal Schedule I classification. Federal prosecutorial discretion creates a de facto accommodation that does not resolve the constitutional conflict.
  • Education: Traditionally a state reserved power, but federal spending leverage (NCLB, Race to the Top) has drawn the federal government deeply into curriculum and accountability -- and ESSA pulled it back somewhat.
  • Environment: Cooperative federalism model -- EPA sets floor, states may exceed it. California's waiver is a unique exception enabling stricter state standards for vehicle emissions.
  • COVID-19: States used police power for the most dramatic exercises of reserved powers in modern history; federal coordination was real but nonbinding guidance. NFIB v. OSHA illustrated the constitutional limits of federal workplace regulation.
  • Strong AP argument structure: Claim (defensible position) + Evidence (specific case law or document) + Reasoning (explanation connecting evidence to claim).

AP Exam Connection

  • This section provides real-world applications of federalism concepts from Topics 1.6-1.8 -- the AP exam heavily tests the ability to apply abstract principles to contemporary scenarios.
  • Concept Application FRQs will present a federalism scenario (similar to Colorado marijuana or Arizona immigration) and ask you to identify which constitutional provision applies and explain the outcome.
  • Argument Essay FRQs on federalism may ask whether federalism enhances or hinders effective governance -- use examples from this section (COVID, marijuana, education) as evidence.
  • Know the structure of an AP argument: claim (not just a definition), specific evidence, reasoning that explains the connection. Practice writing a complete thesis with all three elements.
  • Arizona v. United States (2012) may appear in SCOTUS Comparison FRQs alongside McCulloch v. Maryland or U.S. v. Lopez to test understanding of Supremacy Clause application.
  • The pendulum of federalism in education (NCLB to ESSA) illustrates that federal-state power balance is politically contested and changes with administrations -- a recurring AP theme.
