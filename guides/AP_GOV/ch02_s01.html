Study Guide: Topic 2.1 — Congress: The Senate and House of Representatives

Key Concepts

  • Bicameralism was established by the Great Compromise of 1787, resolving the conflict between large states (favoring population-based representation) and small states (demanding equal representation). The result: a House apportioned by population and a Senate giving each state two votes.
  • Reapportionment and redistricting occur after each decennial census. Reapportionment reassigns House seats among states; redistricting redraws district boundaries within states.
  • Gerrymandering manipulates district lines to favor a political party or dilute a group's voting power. Two techniques are packing (concentrating one group's voters into as few districts as possible) and cracking (splitting a group across multiple districts so it cannot form a majority anywhere).
  • Baker v. Carr (1962) opened federal courts to redistricting challenges by ruling such cases justiciable (not political questions), laying the groundwork for "one person, one vote."
  • Shaw v. Reno (1993) held that race-conscious district lines drawn predominantly on racial grounds require strict scrutiny under the Equal Protection Clause, even when the goal is to increase minority representation.

Vocabulary Review

Term Definition
Bicameral legislature A lawmaking body divided into two chambers; the U.S. Congress consists of the House and the Senate
Reapportionment The redistribution of House seats among states following a decennial census based on population changes
Redistricting The redrawing of congressional (or state legislative) district boundaries, usually by state legislatures
Gerrymandering Drawing district lines to give one political party or group an unfair electoral advantage
Packing A gerrymandering technique that concentrates one group's voters into one or a few districts to limit their influence elsewhere
Cracking A gerrymandering technique that splits a cohesive voting group across multiple districts to prevent them from forming a majority
Malapportionment A condition in which legislative districts have unequal populations, diluting some voters' representation
Justiciable Capable of being decided by courts; presenting a legal question, not solely a political one
Great Compromise The 1787 agreement at the Constitutional Convention creating a bicameral Congress with population-based representation in the House and equal state representation in the Senate
Equal Protection Clause The Fourteenth Amendment provision prohibiting states from denying any person equal protection of the laws; basis for challenging racial gerrymandering and malapportionment

Baker v. Carr (1962)

  • Tennessee had not redistricted since 1901 despite massive urban population growth
  • Justice Brennan held reapportionment claims are justiciable under the 14th Amendment Equal Protection Clause
  • Led directly to Reynolds v. Sims (1964) and the "one person, one vote" rule
  • Shifted political power from rural to urban/suburban areas

Shaw v. Reno (1993)

  • North Carolina drew a district 160 miles long along I-85, shaped to create a second majority-minority district
  • Justice O'Connor held that districts "so bizarre" they are explainable only by race trigger strict scrutiny
  • States must narrowly tailor race-conscious lines to serve a compelling interest
  • Ongoing tension with Voting Rights Act requirements

Review Questions

1. Why did the Framers create a bicameral legislature? The Framers created a bicameral Congress for two main reasons. First, the Great Compromise resolved the conflict between large and small states by giving the House population-based seats (satisfying large states) and the Senate equal representation per state (protecting small states). Second, bicameralism creates an internal check within the legislature—both chambers must agree on any bill, encouraging deliberation and making hasty legislation difficult. Madison argued in Federalist No. 51 that dividing legislative power prevents dangerous concentrations of authority.

2. Define gerrymandering and distinguish between packing and cracking. Gerrymandering is the manipulation of district boundaries to benefit a particular political party or group. Packing concentrates a group's voters into as few districts as possible, wasting their votes on lopsided victories and limiting their influence in surrounding districts. Cracking does the opposite—it splits a concentrated group across multiple districts so the group cannot form a majority in any of them, effectively neutralizing their collective electoral power.

3. How did Baker v. Carr change the ability of federal courts to address redistricting disputes? Before Baker v. Carr, courts had dismissed redistricting challenges as "political questions" beyond judicial authority. Baker held that federal courts could hear such cases because they involve claims of individual rights under the Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause—not purely political matters committed to another branch. This opened the courthouse door to redistricting challenges and led to the "one person, one vote" standard, forcing states to draw districts of roughly equal population.

4. What standard did Shaw v. Reno establish for racial gerrymandering? Shaw v. Reno established that when a redistricting plan is so irregular in shape that it can be explained only by race, it is subject to strict scrutiny. Under strict scrutiny, the state must show that the racial classification is narrowly tailored to serve a compelling governmental interest. This standard applies even when the racial classification is intended to benefit minority voters (e.g., creating majority-minority districts under the Voting Rights Act).

5. Compare the special powers of the House and Senate. The House holds exclusive power to originate all revenue (tax) bills, reflecting the Framers' belief that the chamber closest to the people should control taxation. The House also has the sole power of impeachment—formally charging federal officials with "high crimes and misdemeanors" by simple majority vote. The Senate has exclusive power to confirm presidential appointments (cabinet, judges, ambassadors) and ratify treaties by a two-thirds vote. The Senate also conducts impeachment trials, requiring a two-thirds vote to convict and remove an official.

Summary

The U.S. Congress is a bicameral legislature created by the Great Compromise of 1787, balancing the interests of large and small states through population-based representation in the House and equal state representation in the Senate. Structural differences between the chambers—in size, terms, debate rules, and special powers—reflect distinct constitutional roles. Redistricting and gerrymandering are central issues in American democracy, with Baker v. Carr (1962) opening courts to apportionment challenges and Shaw v. Reno (1993) establishing that race-based district drawing requires strict judicial scrutiny.
