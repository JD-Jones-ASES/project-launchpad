Understanding Stocks: Ownership in Public Companies

Overview

This section introduces stocks as units of ownership in a corporation, explaining why companies issue them to raise capital for growth. It details what owning stock means for an investor, including rights to dividends and voting, and outlines the two main ways investors can profit from stocks: price appreciation and dividends. The section also covers how stocks are traded on exchanges, how market capitalization is determined, and how to calculate the total return on a stock investment, while emphasizing the inherent risks of picking individual stocks.

Key Concepts

  • Stocks as Ownership: A stock represents a fractional ownership stake in a company, making the buyer a shareholder. Companies issue stock to raise money for expansion without incurring debt.
  • Shareholder Rights: As a shareholder, you are entitled to a proportional share of any dividends paid, voting rights on significant company decisions, and a share of the company's assets if it is ever liquidated.
  • Two Ways to Profit:
    • Price Appreciation (Capital Gain): Selling a stock for a higher price than you paid for it.
    • Dividends: Regular cash payments distributed by some profitable companies to their shareholders.
  • Stock Exchanges: Organized marketplaces (like the NYSE or NASDAQ) where buyers and sellers trade stocks, providing transparency, regulation, and liquidity.
  • Market Capitalization (Market Cap): The total value of a company's outstanding shares, calculated by multiplying the current stock price by the number of shares outstanding. It indicates a company's size.
  • Calculating Stock Return: The total profit or loss from a stock investment, which includes both changes in stock price and any dividends received, expressed as a percentage of the initial investment.
  • Risk of Individual Stock Picking: Investing in individual stocks is highly risky, as even professional investors often fail to consistently beat market averages. Diversified funds are generally a more sensible option for most investors.

Important Terms

Stock (Share/Equity) : A unit of ownership in a corporation. Shareholder : An individual or entity that owns shares of stock in a company, making them a partial owner. Dividend : A cash payment distributed by some companies to their shareholders, typically quarterly, representing a share of the company's profits. Capital Gain : The profit earned when an investment (like a stock) is sold for more than its purchase price. Stock Exchange : An organized marketplace where stocks are bought and sold, providing a regulated environment for trading. Liquidity : The ease with which an asset (like a stock) can be converted into cash without affecting its market price, often facilitated by stock exchanges. Market Capitalization (Market Cap) : The total market value of a company's outstanding shares, calculated as Stock Price × Number of Shares Outstanding. Outstanding Shares : The total number of a company's shares that are currently held by all its shareholders, including institutional investors and restricted shares. Price Appreciation : An increase in the market value of an asset over time. Total Return : The overall gain or loss on an investment over a specific period, including both capital gains/losses and any income (like dividends) generated.

Detailed Notes

What Are Stocks?

Companies frequently require additional funds to expand their operations, whether it's building new facilities, hiring more staff, or developing new products. To secure this capital, they have two primary methods:

  1. Borrowing Money: This creates debt for the company, which must be repaid with interest.
  2. Issuing Stock: This involves selling ownership stakes to the public. Each unit of ownership is called a stock, share, or equity. When you purchase a stock, you become a shareholder, meaning you own a fraction of that company. The more shares you own, the larger your ownership stake.

What Does Owning Stock Mean?

As a shareholder, you are a partial owner of the company, and your rights are proportional to the number of shares you hold. For instance, if you own 100 shares of a company with 1,000,000 shares outstanding, you own 0.01% of that company. This ownership entitles you to:

  • Dividends: A proportional share of any dividends paid. A dividend is a cash payment, usually quarterly, distributed by some companies from their profits to shareholders. It's important to note that not all companies pay dividends; many choose to reinvest profits back into the business for growth.
  • Voting Rights: A vote on major company decisions, also proportional to the number of shares owned. This allows shareholders to influence the company's direction.
  • Asset Claim: A proportional share of the company's assets if the company is ever sold or liquidated.

Two Ways to Make Money from Stocks

Investors can generate income from stock investments in two distinct ways:

  1. Price Appreciation (Capital Gain): This occurs when the market price of the stock increases after you buy it. If you sell the stock for more than you paid, the profit earned is called a capital gain. For example, buying a stock at $30 and selling it at $45 yields a $15 capital gain per share.
  2. Dividends: If the company you own stock in pays dividends, you receive regular cash payments. These payments provide income without requiring you to sell your shares.

Stock Exchanges

Stocks are not bought directly from companies but are traded on specialized marketplaces called stock exchanges.

  • Definition: A stock exchange is an organized marketplace where stocks are bought and sold.
  • Function: Exchanges connect buyers and sellers, facilitating the trading process.
  • Benefits: They provide transparency (prices and trades are public), regulation (rules to ensure fair trading), and liquidity (making it easy to buy or sell shares quickly at current market prices during trading hours).
  • Historical Context: Stock markets have a rich history, with the Amsterdam Stock Exchange, founded in 1602 to trade shares of the Dutch East India Company, often cited as the world's first formal stock market.

Market Capitalization

Market capitalization (market cap) is a crucial metric that represents the total market value of all of a company's outstanding shares. It is calculated using a simple formula:

Market Cap = Stock Price × Number of Shares Outstanding

For example, a company with a stock price of $50 and 10 million shares outstanding would have a market cap of $500 million ($50 × 10,000,000). Market cap is often used to categorize companies by size (e.g., small-cap, mid-cap, large-cap).

Calculating Stock Return

When evaluating the performance of a stock investment, it's essential to calculate the total return, which accounts for both changes in the stock price and any dividends received. The formula is:

Return = (Sell Price - Buy Price + Dividends per Share) / Buy Price × 100%

Example Calculation: Suppose you buy one share of stock for $50. Over the next year, you receive $2.00 in dividends. You then sell the share for $65.

  1. Identify the values:

    • Buy Price = $50.00
    • Sell Price = $65.00
    • Dividends per Share = $2.00

  2. Plug into the formula: Return = (65 - 50 + 2) / 50 × 100% Return = (15 + 2) / 50 × 100% Return = 17 / 50 × 100% Return = 0.34 × 100% Return = 34%

Your total return on this stock was 34%, which includes both the $15 price gain and the $2.00 in dividend income.

Quick Check Example: You buy a stock at $80. You receive $1.50 in dividends. You sell it at $88.

Return = (88 - 80 + 1.50) / 80 × 100% Return = (8 + 1.50) / 80 × 100% Return = 9.50 / 80 × 100% Return = 0.11875 × 100% Return = 11.875%

Important Considerations: The Risk of Picking Individual Stocks

It is crucial for investors to understand that picking individual stocks is highly risky. Even experienced professionals who dedicate their careers to analyzing stocks often struggle to consistently outperform the overall market average over long periods. Concentrating a significant portion of your savings into a small number of individual stocks drastically increases your risk exposure. For most people, a far more sensible and less risky approach is to invest in diversified funds (such as mutual funds or index funds, which will be covered in later sections), as they spread investments across many companies, thereby reducing company-specific risk.

Review Questions

  1. What is a stock, and why do companies typically issue shares to the public?
  2. Describe the two primary ways an investor can make money from owning a stock. Provide a brief example for each.
  3. Explain the function of a stock exchange. What benefits do exchanges provide to both buyers and sellers of stocks?
  4. Define market capitalization and explain how it is calculated. Why is this metric important for understanding a company's position in the market?
  5. Using the formula for calculating stock return, illustrate how you would determine your total percentage return if you bought a stock for $25, received $0.75 in dividends, and later sold it for $30. Why is picking individual stocks considered risky, and what alternative is often recommended for most investors?
