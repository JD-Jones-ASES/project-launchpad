Chapter 14, Section 5: Checking and Monitoring Your Credit Score

Overview

You cannot improve what you cannot measure. Regularly checking your credit report and score is one of the most important financial habits you can build. Federal law makes much of this free — but knowing where to look and what to watch for is essential.

Your Free Annual Credit Report

  • Federal law (the Fair Credit Reporting Act) entitles every American to one free credit report per year from each of the three major bureaus
  • The only government-mandated website: AnnualCreditReport.com
  • Pulling all three at once gives a complete picture, but a smarter strategy is to stagger them:
Month Action
January Pull Equifax report
May Pull Experian report
September Pull TransUnion report

This gives you three free checks spread across the year rather than one bulk pull with nine months unmonitored.

What to Look for in Your Credit Report

When reviewing your report, check for these problems:

  • Accounts you did not open — possible identity theft or fraud
  • Incorrect payment history — on-time payments listed as late
  • Wrong account information — incorrect balances, limits, or account status
  • Outdated negative information — most negatives must be removed after 7 years; bankruptcies after 10 years
  • Duplicate accounts — same account listed more than once

If you find an error, you have the right to dispute it in writing with the bureau. The bureau must investigate and correct verified errors — usually within 30 days.

Hard Inquiries vs. Soft Inquiries

Type of Check Hard or Soft? Affects Score?
You check your own score Soft No
Employer background check Soft No
Pre-approved credit offers Soft No
Applying for a credit card Hard Yes (slightly)
Applying for a car loan Hard Yes (slightly)
Applying for an apartment Hard Yes (slightly)
Applying for a mortgage Hard Yes (slightly)

Key rule: Only credit applications trigger hard inquiries. Checking your own score never hurts your credit.

Free Score Monitoring Options

Your score is separate from your free annual report. Free options include:

  • Bank and credit union apps — many show your score in online portals
  • Credit card issuers — many provide free monthly score access to cardholders
  • Monitoring websites — may offer free scores in exchange for showing targeted offers

Important: Confirm whether a service shows a true FICO score or a different model — results can vary. FICO is the model most lenders actually use.

Watch Out: Fake Credit Monitoring Sites

Red flags of a fraudulent credit or repair site:

  • Asks for a credit card to access your "free" report
  • Offers to "fix" your credit for a fee
  • Promises to remove accurate negative information (this is illegal — no one can do this)
  • Uses a URL other than AnnualCreditReport.com

The only things that truly repair credit: time, on-time payments, and lower balances.

Recommended Annual Monitoring Routine

  1. Pull a free bureau report every four months (one bureau at a time)
  2. Check your score monthly through your bank or card app (soft inquiry — no score impact)
  3. Set calendar reminders for credit card due dates
  4. Immediately investigate any account or inquiry you do not recognize

Starting at 18: No Credit History Is Normal

Many young adults discover at 18 — when they need their first apartment or car loan — that they have no credit history at all. This is completely normal. The best time to start building credit is before you need it. Start early, and you may already have a solid credit foundation when you enter the working world.

Key Terms

  • Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) — federal law granting free annual credit report access and dispute rights
  • AnnualCreditReport.com — the only government-mandated free report website
  • Hard inquiry — credit check from a new credit application; slightly lowers your score
  • Soft inquiry — credit check that does not affect your score (self-checks, employer checks, pre-approvals)
  • FICO score — the credit scoring model most commonly used by lenders
  • Credit dispute — formal written challenge to inaccurate information on your report
