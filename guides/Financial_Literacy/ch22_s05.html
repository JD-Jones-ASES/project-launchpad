Chapter 22, Section 5: Life Insurance Overview

Overview

Life insurance is designed to replace your income and protect the people who depend on you financially — your dependents — if you die. It is not about protecting yourself; it is about protecting those who would suffer financially without your income.

Who Needs Life Insurance?

Ask yourself: Does anyone depend on my income to survive?

Situation Life Insurance Priority
Have a spouse, children, or other dependents Likely need life insurance
Single young adult with no dependents Not an immediate priority

For young adults with no dependents: The most important step is naming beneficiaries on your existing accounts (bank accounts, 401k, IRA). This takes five minutes, costs nothing, and is more urgent than buying a policy.

Term Life Insurance

Term life insurance provides coverage for a specific period of time (10, 20, or 30 years).

  • If you die during the term → your beneficiaries receive the death benefit
  • If you outlive the term → the policy ends with no payout

Key Features of Term Life

  • Lower cost — premiums are much lower than permanent insurance, especially when bought young and healthy
  • Pure protection — no investment component; you pay only for coverage
  • Simple — easy to understand and compare across providers
  • Best for: most people; a 20- or 30-year term policy in your 20s or 30s covers the years when dependents need your income most

Whole Life Insurance (Permanent Life Insurance)

Whole life insurance provides coverage for your entire lifetime, as long as premiums are paid. It also builds a cash value that can be borrowed against or withdrawn.

Key Features of Whole Life

  • Lifetime coverage — never expires with consistent premium payments
  • Cash value — grows slowly; accessible during your lifetime
  • Much higher cost — premiums can be 5-15 times higher than term for the same death benefit
  • Complexity — combines insurance and investment in one product

Warning: Whole life is frequently sold because agents earn higher commissions. For most people, the better approach is: buy term life and invest the premium difference in a retirement account. The cash value growth in whole life rarely justifies the higher cost.

How Much Life Insurance Do You Need?

A common rule of thumb: purchase coverage equal to 10-12 times your annual income.

Example: Person earns $50,000/year with two young children

  • $50,000 × 10 = $500,000
  • $50,000 × 12 = $600,000
  • Recommended coverage: $500,000 to $600,000 (20-year term policy)

Factors That Increase Coverage Needs

  • More dependents or a stay-at-home spouse
  • Large debts (mortgage, student loans)
  • High-cost special needs of a dependent

Buy Early: Premiums Rise with Age

Life insurance is cheapest when you are young and healthy. Locking in a low rate with a 20- or 30-year term policy in your 20s can save thousands of dollars compared to buying later. Every year you wait, the premium increases — underwriters assign more risk to older applicants.

Key Terms

  • Death benefit — the lump sum paid to beneficiaries upon the insured's death
  • Beneficiary — the person(s) named to receive the death benefit
  • Premium — regular payment to keep the policy active
  • Underwriting — insurer's process to evaluate your health risk and set your premium; younger and healthier = lower premium
  • Term life insurance — coverage for a specific time period; pure protection; no investment component
  • Whole life insurance (permanent) — lifetime coverage with a cash value component; 5-15x more expensive than term
  • Cash value — the savings-like component inside a whole life policy that grows slowly over time
