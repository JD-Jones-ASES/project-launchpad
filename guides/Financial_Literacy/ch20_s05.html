Chapter 20, Section 5: Choosing Between Accounts

Overview

You now know the three main retirement accounts: Traditional IRA, Roth IRA, and 401(k). Choosing between them depends primarily on one question: what will your tax rate be now vs. in retirement?

The Core Tax Trade-Off

Account Type When You Pay Tax Best For
Traditional IRA / 401(k) At withdrawal (in retirement) Current bracket higher than retirement bracket
Roth IRA Now (contributions are after-tax) Current bracket lower than retirement bracket

The fundamental bet: Traditional assumes you'll be in a lower bracket in retirement. Roth assumes your bracket will stay the same or rise.

Decision Framework

Your Situation Likely Better Choice Why
Low tax bracket (10-12%) now Roth Pay low taxes now; avoid higher taxes later
High bracket (24%+) now Traditional Reduce taxes now; probably lower bracket in retirement
Young worker starting out Roth Almost always in the lowest bracket early in career
Peak earning years (mid-career) Traditional or split High bracket now; likely lower in retirement
Want to leave money to heirs Roth No RMDs; heirs inherit tax-free growth

The Math: Roth vs. Traditional (Equal Tax Rates)

Assumptions: $6,000 pre-tax income, 22% tax rate now and in retirement, 8% annual return, 40 years.

Traditional IRA:

  • Contribute full $6,000 (pre-tax)
  • Future value at retirement: $6,000 × 259.056 = $1,554,336
  • Pay 22% tax at withdrawal: $1,554,336 × 0.78 = $1,212,382 after-tax

Roth IRA:

  • Pay 22% tax first: $6,000 × (1 - 0.22) = $4,680 contributed
  • Future value: $4,680 × 259.056 = $1,212,382 tax-free

Result: When tax rates are equal, both produce the exact same after-tax amount. The math is perfectly equivalent.

Why Roth Wins for Young Workers Anyway

Even if rates appear equal, Roth is typically better for young people because:

  1. Lowest bracket now — your income and rate will likely rise as your career advances
  2. No RMDs — Roth has no Required Minimum Distributions, giving more flexibility in retirement
  3. Protection against rising rates — if general tax rates rise over the next 40 years, Roth holders pay nothing extra

Account Features Comparison

Feature Traditional IRA Roth IRA 401(k)
Contribution limit (2024) $7,000 $7,000 $23,000
Tax on contribution Deductible Not deductible Pre-tax
Tax on growth Deferred Tax-free Deferred
Tax at withdrawal Taxed Tax-free Taxed
RMDs Yes (age 73) No Yes (age 73)
Employer match No No Possible
Best for High bracket now Low bracket now Everyone (match!)

Can You Have Both?

Yes. IRA and 401(k) limits are completely separate — contributing to one does not reduce what you can contribute to the other.

Recommended Priority Order:

  1. Contribute to 401(k) up to the full employer match — this is free money; always capture it first
  2. Max out a Roth IRA ($7,000) — best for most young workers
  3. Return to 401(k) with any remaining funds, up to the $23,000 limit

Key Terms

  • Traditional IRA — pre-tax contributions; taxed at withdrawal; RMDs at age 73
  • Roth IRA — after-tax contributions; tax-free withdrawals; no RMDs
  • 401(k) — employer-sponsored plan; pre-tax; possible employer match; RMDs at age 73
  • Required Minimum Distribution (RMD) — mandatory annual withdrawal from retirement accounts starting at age 73 (Traditional and 401k; not Roth)
  • Tax bracket — the marginal rate applied to income above a threshold; determines Roth vs. Traditional preference
  • Employer match — free money contributed by your employer to your 401(k) when you contribute; always capture the full match first
