Amortization: How Loan Payments Work

Overview

This section demystifies how loan payments are calculated and applied. It provides a step-by-step method to determine a fixed monthly loan payment using a specific formula and explains the concept of amortization, illustrating how each payment is split between covering interest and reducing the principal balance over the life of the loan.

Key Concepts

  • Monthly Payment Formula: A specific mathematical formula used to calculate the fixed monthly payment ($M$) required to fully pay off an amortized loan over its term.
  • Variables in the Formula: Understanding what each component represents: $P$ (principal), $r$ (monthly interest rate), and $n$ (total number of monthly payments).
  • Calculating Monthly Interest Rate ($r$): Derived by dividing the Annual Percentage Rate (APR) by 12 and expressing it as a decimal.
  • Calculating Total Number of Payments ($n$): Found by multiplying the loan term in years by 12.
  • Exponent Calculation: The use of $(1+r)^n$ in the formula, which requires a scientific calculator's $y^x$ (or equivalent) function.
  • Amortization Process: The gradual repayment of a loan over time through regular payments that cover both interest and a portion of the principal.
  • Shifting Payment Allocation: In early loan stages, a larger portion of the payment goes toward interest, with a smaller amount reducing the principal. As the principal shrinks, the interest portion decreases, and more of each payment goes toward the principal.
  • Benefit of Early Principal Payments: Making extra payments toward the principal, especially early in the loan term, can significantly reduce the total interest paid and shorten the loan duration because interest is always calculated on the remaining balance.

Important Terms

  • Amortization: The process of paying off a debt with a fixed repayment schedule in regular installments over a period of time, where each payment covers both interest and a portion of the principal.
  • Monthly Payment (M): The fixed amount of money paid by the borrower each month to the lender.
  • Principal (P): The initial amount of money borrowed from the lender, or the remaining balance of the loan.
  • Monthly Interest Rate (r): The interest rate applied to the loan's principal each month, calculated as the Annual Percentage Rate (APR) divided by 12, expressed as a decimal.
  • Total Number of Monthly Payments (n): The total count of payments that will be made over the entire life of the loan, calculated as the loan term in years multiplied by 12.
  • APR (Annual Percentage Rate): The yearly rate of interest charged on a loan, expressed as a percentage.
  • Interest: The cost of borrowing money, calculated as a percentage of the principal.
  • Loan Term: The duration over which a loan is to be repaid, typically expressed in years.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Amortization

Understanding amortization is crucial for anyone taking out a loan. It's not just about knowing your monthly payment; it's about comprehending how that payment is distributed between interest and principal, and how that distribution changes over time. This knowledge empowers you to make informed financial decisions.

The Monthly Payment Formula

The core of calculating an amortized loan payment is the following formula:

$M = P \frac{r(1+r)^n}{(1+r)^n - 1}$

Where:

  • $M$ = your fixed monthly payment.
  • $P$ = the principal amount you borrowed (the original loan amount).
  • $r$ = the monthly interest rate. To find this, you take the Annual Percentage Rate (APR), convert it to a decimal (e.g., 6% = 0.06), and then divide it by 12 (for 12 months in a year). So, $r = \frac{APR}{12}$.
  • $n$ = the total number of monthly payments you will make over the life of the loan. To find this, you multiply the loan term in years by 12. So, $n = \text{years} \times 12$.

Understanding Exponents on Your Calculator

The formula includes the term $(1+r)^n$. This means $(1+r)$ is multiplied by itself $n$ times. For example, if $r = 0.005$ and $n = 60$, you need to calculate $(1.005)^{60}$.

To do this on most scientific calculators:

  1. Enter the base number: 1.005
  2. Press the exponent key: This key is often labeled y^x, x^y, or ^.
  3. Enter the exponent: 60
  4. Press the equals key: =
  5. The result for $(1.005)^{60}$ is approximately 1.348850. Mastering this step is key to solving loan payment calculations quickly and accurately.

Worked Example: The $20,000 Car Loan

Let's apply the formula to a real-world scenario:

You borrow $20,000 for a car at an APR of 6% over 5 years.

Given:

  • $P = $20,000$
  • APR $= 6% = 0.06$
  • Term $= 5$ years

Step-by-Step Calculation:

  1. Find the monthly rate ($r$): $r = \frac{0.06}{12} = 0.005$

  2. Find the total number of payments ($n$): $n = 5 \text{ years} \times 12 \text{ months/year} = 60 \text{ payments}$

  3. Calculate $(1 + r)^n$: $(1 + 0.005)^{60} = (1.005)^{60} \approx 1.348850$ (using your calculator's exponent function)

  4. Calculate the numerator of the fraction ($r(1+r)^n$): $0.005 \times 1.348850 \approx 0.006744$

  5. Calculate the denominator of the fraction ($(1+r)^n - 1$): $1.348850 - 1 = 0.348850$

  6. Divide the numerator by the denominator (the fractional part of the formula): $\frac{0.006744}{0.348850} \approx 0.019333$

  7. Multiply by the principal ($P$) to find the monthly payment ($M$): $M = $20,000 \times 0.019333 \approx $386.66$ per month

So, your fixed monthly payment for this car loan would be $386.66.

What Is Amortization? (Deeper Dive)

Once you know your monthly payment, the concept of amortization explains what happens inside that payment. Amortization is the process where each regular payment you make toward a loan is split into two parts: one part goes to pay the interest accrued on the outstanding balance, and the other part goes to reduce the principal balance itself.

The key dynamic of amortization is the shifting balance between these two parts over the life of the loan:

  • Early in the loan term: A significant portion of your payment goes towards interest because the principal balance is still high. Only a smaller amount goes toward reducing the principal. For example, in Month 1 of the car loan, $100.00 (about 26%) of the $386.66 payment went to interest, and only $286.66 reduced the principal.
  • As the loan progresses: With each payment, the principal balance shrinks. Because interest is always calculated on the remaining principal balance, the interest charged in subsequent months becomes slightly smaller. This means that a slightly larger portion of your fixed monthly payment can then be allocated to reducing the principal.
  • Late in the loan term: This shift continues until, in the final months, most of your payment goes towards the principal, and only a small amount covers interest.

Money Tip: Make Extra Principal Payments Early

Because interest is calculated on the remaining principal balance, making extra payments toward the principal, especially early in the loan's life, can lead to substantial savings.

  • How it works: When you reduce the principal balance sooner, the base on which future interest is calculated becomes smaller faster. This snowballs over time, reducing the total amount of interest you pay over the loan term and potentially allowing you to pay off the loan much earlier.
  • Important Note: Always specify to your lender that any extra money you send is to be applied directly to the principal balance, not as a pre-payment for the next month's payment. If not specified, some lenders might just apply it as an early payment for the next month, which doesn't provide the same interest-saving benefits.

Review Questions

  1. In your own words, explain what amortization is and why understanding it is important for a borrower.
  2. List the variables ($M$, $P$, $r$, $n$) in the monthly loan payment formula and describe what each variable represents and how it is typically calculated.
  3. Describe how the proportion of interest versus principal within a fixed monthly loan payment changes from the beginning to the end of a loan term.
  4. Why is making an extra payment towards the principal balance more beneficial early in a loan's term compared to later? What crucial instruction should you give your lender when making an extra payment?
  5. If you were planning to borrow $15,000 for a 3-year loan with an 8% APR, what would be the values for 'r' (monthly interest rate) and 'n' (total number of payments) that you would use in the monthly payment formula?
