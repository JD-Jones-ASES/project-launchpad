Chapter 16, Section 5: Good Debt vs. Bad Debt

Overview

Not all debt is created equal. The difference between good debt and bad debt comes down to one question: does this borrowing create value greater than its cost? Understanding this distinction is one of the most valuable financial skills you can develop.

Good Debt: Investing in Your Future

Good debt is borrowing that is likely to increase your net worth or earning power over time — where the return on the investment is greater than the cost of the loan.

Example Why It Can Be Good Debt
Student loans (proportional) A degree can increase lifetime earnings far beyond the loan cost
Mortgage Builds equity, home may appreciate, housing costs are unavoidable
Business loan Funds income-generating activity greater than the loan cost

Key caveat for student loans: The amount borrowed must be proportional to the income boost. A $30,000 loan for a $500,000 lifetime earnings increase works. A $100,000 loan for a $35,000/year career may fail the test.

Bad Debt: Paying Interest on Things That Lose Value

Bad debt is borrowing for things that depreciate (lose value) or for pure consumption — where you pay interest with no lasting financial benefit.

Example Why It Is Bad Debt
Credit card balances (at 20%+ APR) Items consumed/lost value; interest keeps accumulating
Payday loans APRs of 400%+ can trap borrowers in a debt cycle
Luxury auto loans Car depreciates; high cost with no financial return
Long-term auto loans (7 years) Being "upside down" — you owe more than the car is worth

The Debt Decision Framework

Before borrowing, ask yourself:

  1. Does this create lasting value? (Education, home equity, income-producing asset)
  2. Is the cost reasonable relative to the benefit? ($100,000 loan for a $35,000/year career may not pass)
  3. Can I comfortably make the payments? (Never borrow beyond your budget)
  4. Is there a less expensive alternative? (Save up? Buy used? Take a smaller loan?)

Gray Area: Auto Loans

Cars always depreciate, making auto loans technically bad debt — but almost everyone needs a car. The key is to minimize the bad-debt impact:

  • Buy a reliable used car rather than a new or luxury vehicle
  • Borrow as little as possible at the lowest rate available
  • Choose the shortest loan term you can afford
  • Avoid 7-year loans — you may be "upside down" (owe more than the car is worth) for years

A reliable $10,000 used car with a 3-year loan beats a $40,000 new car with a 7-year loan in almost every financial scenario.

The High-APR Emergency Rule

If you carry a credit card balance at 20-25% APR, treat it as a financial emergency:

  • No investment reliably returns 20% per year
  • Paying off that debt is the best guaranteed return you can get
  • This takes priority over most other financial goals

Key Context: American Household Debt (2023)

  • Average total household debt (including mortgages): ~$104,215
  • Average non-mortgage consumer debt: ~$21,800

Understanding how to manage and minimize debt is one of the most valuable life skills you can develop.

Key Terms

  • Good debt — borrowing that creates value or income greater than the cost of the loan
  • Bad debt — borrowing for depreciating assets or consumption with no financial return
  • Depreciation — decrease in an asset's value over time
  • Payday loan — short-term, extremely high-APR loan; classic bad debt
  • Upside down (negative equity) — owing more on a loan than the asset is currently worth
  • APR (Annual Percentage Rate) — the yearly cost of borrowing, expressed as a percentage
