Types of Income: A Comprehensive Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concept that not all money received is treated the same, particularly for tax purposes. It categorizes income into two main types: "earned income," which comes from active work, and "unearned income," which stems from assets or investments. Understanding this distinction is crucial for comprehending how taxes are calculated, eligibility for certain benefits, and the varying tax rates that apply to different income sources.

Key Concepts

  • Income Diversity: Money received comes from various sources and is treated differently by law, especially for taxes.
  • Earned Income: Compensation received for performing work or services, requiring active effort. It is the most common type of income for students.
  • Unearned Income: Income received from sources other than work, primarily from assets or investments, and does not require active ongoing effort.
  • Tax Rate Differences: Most earned income is taxed at "ordinary income tax rates." However, certain types of unearned income (like long-term capital gains and qualified dividends) often receive preferential, lower tax rates, which can significantly impact an individual's overall tax burden.
  • Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC): A government benefit designed for lower-income workers, which specifically requires earned income for eligibility. Unearned income does not qualify for EITC.
  • Gifts and Inheritances: Generally, money received as a gift or inheritance is not considered taxable income to the recipient for federal tax purposes. However, the giver may face gift tax rules above certain limits, and any income generated by inherited assets after they are received becomes taxable to the new owner.
  • Importance of Record Keeping: It is vital to keep track of all income sources, both earned and unearned, as banks and brokerages report certain types of income to the IRS.

Important Terms

  • Earned Income: Compensation received for performing work or services; includes wages, salaries, tips, commissions, and net earnings from self-employment.
  • Wages: Hourly pay received from an employer for work performed.
  • Salary: A fixed annual amount paid to an employee, typically divided into regular paychecks, regardless of the exact hours worked.
  • Tips: Extra money received directly from customers for service rendered.
  • Commissions: A percentage of sales earned by an individual, common in sales professions.
  • Self-employment Income: Profit earned from operating one's own business, freelance work, or gig economy jobs.
  • Unearned Income: Income received from sources other than active work or employment, such as interest, dividends, rental income, and capital gains.
  • Interest: Money paid to an individual by a bank or financial institution for keeping money in savings accounts, certificates of deposit, or bonds.
  • Dividends: Payments made by a company to its shareholders from its profits.
  • Rental Income: Money received from tenants for the use of property owned by the landlord.
  • Capital Gains: Profit realized from selling an asset (like stocks, real estate, or collectibles) for more than its original purchase price.
  • Long-term Capital Gains: Profits from selling assets that have been held for more than one year, often taxed at preferential, lower rates.
  • Ordinary Income Tax Rates: The standard tax rates applied to most types of earned income and some unearned income, structured into various tax brackets.
  • Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC): A refundable tax credit for low-to-moderate-income working individuals and families, requiring earned income for eligibility.
  • APY (Annual Percentage Yield): The real rate of return earned on a savings deposit or investment, taking into account the effect of compounding interest.
  • Form 1099-INT: A tax form sent by banks to report interest income earned by an individual to both the taxpayer and the IRS.
  • Gifts: Money or property transferred from one person to another without expectation of repayment; generally not taxable income to the recipient.
  • Inheritances: Money or property received upon the death of another person; generally not taxable income to the recipient.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Income Categories

The Internal Revenue Service (IRS) and tax laws categorize income differently based on its source. This distinction is not merely an accounting formality; it significantly impacts how much tax you owe and what benefits you might be eligible for. The foundational step to managing your taxes is recognizing these income types.

Earned Income

Earned income is the most common and easily understood type of income. It is essentially any money you receive as a direct result of your work or services. If you put in effort, performed a job, and got paid, it's likely earned income.

  • Core Principle: Requires active effort on your part.
  • Examples:
    • Wages: What you earn from an hourly job, like working at a retail store, a fast-food restaurant, or a summer internship.
    • Salary: A fixed amount paid annually, typically broken down into regular paychecks, for a full-time position (e.g., a teacher's annual salary).
    • Tips: Extra money given by customers for good service, such as waiting tables or delivering food.
    • Commissions: A percentage of sales you make, often received by real estate agents or car salespeople.
    • Self-employment Income: Profits from running your own small business, doing freelance work (e.g., writing, graphic design), or engaging in gig economy jobs (e.g., mowing lawns, tutoring, selling crafts online).

For most high school students, any money earned from a job falls into the earned income category.

Unearned Income

Unearned income is money you receive not because you actively worked for it, but because you own an asset that generates income. Your money or property "works for you."

  • Core Principle: Generated from assets; does not require active, ongoing effort.
  • Examples:
    • Interest: Money a bank pays you for keeping funds in a savings account, certificate of deposit (CD), or from owning bonds.
      • Real-World Example: If you save $1,000 in a high-yield savings account earning 4.00% APY, you will earn $40 in interest ($1,000 * 0.04). This $40 is unearned income. Your bank will report this to you and the IRS on a Form 1099-INT.
    • Dividends: Payments made to stockholders from a company's profits as a return on their investment.
    • Rental Income: Money collected from tenants for the use of property you own (e.g., an apartment or house).
    • Capital Gains: The profit you make when you sell an asset (like stocks, real estate, or collectibles) for more than you originally paid for it.
    • Alimony (for agreements before 2019): Payments received under older divorce settlements.

Why the Distinction Matters for Taxes

The classification of income into "earned" and "unearned" has two major implications:

  1. Different Tax Rates:
    • Most earned income is subject to ordinary income tax rates, which are progressive, meaning higher income levels are taxed at higher percentages (you'll learn about tax brackets in Section 6.3).
    • Certain types of unearned income receive special, often lower, tax treatment. Notably, long-term capital gains (profits from selling assets held for over one year) and qualified dividends are taxed at preferential rates: 0%, 15%, or 20%, depending on your total income. For many middle-income investors, this rate is often 15%. This can be significantly lower than the ordinary income tax rates (which can go much higher, e.g., 22% or more). This preferential treatment is a key reason wealthy individuals who derive most of their income from investments may pay a lower effective tax rate than someone earning the same amount solely through wages.
  2. Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) Eligibility:
    • The EITC is a significant government benefit designed to help lower-income working individuals and families.
    • Crucially, only earned income qualifies an individual for the EITC. Unearned income sources like interest, dividends, or capital gains do not count towards EITC eligibility.

Gifts and Inheritances: Not Your Income (Generally)

A common question is whether money received as a gift or an inheritance counts as income. The general rule for federal tax purposes is no.

  • Recipient's View: If you receive a gift or an inheritance, you typically do not owe federal income tax on it.
  • Giver's View (Gifts): The person giving a gift may be subject to gift tax rules if the value of the gift exceeds certain annual limits (e.g., $18,000 per person in 2024). This tax is typically paid by the giver, not the recipient.
  • Inherited Assets: While the inheritance itself is not taxable income to the recipient, any income that the inherited assets generate after you receive them (such as interest from an inherited bank account or dividends from inherited stocks) does become your taxable income going forward.

The Importance of Good Record Keeping

It is absolutely essential to keep thorough records of all your income sources, not just those from a regular job. This includes any interest earned on savings accounts, payments for freelance work, or profits from selling assets.

  • Reporting Requirements: Financial institutions like banks and brokerages are legally required to report certain types of income (like interest and dividends) to both you and the IRS.
  • Accuracy: Your tax return needs to accurately reflect all income reported by these institutions to the IRS to avoid discrepancies and potential issues. Being organized ensures you report everything correctly.

Review Questions

  1. What is the fundamental difference between "earned income" and "unearned income"? Provide at least two distinct examples for each type of income.
  2. Explain two significant reasons why the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) and tax laws distinguish between earned and unearned income.
  3. Describe how long-term capital gains and qualified dividends are typically taxed differently from regular wages or salary. Why is this difference important, especially for investors?
  4. Imagine you receive a $2,000 cash gift from your grandparents for graduation and later inherit a stock portfolio worth $10,000. How would these events generally affect your personal federal income taxes? What if the inherited stock portfolio then pays you $100 in dividends?
  5. Why is it important to keep careful records of all your income sources, including small amounts of interest or freelance earnings, even if you don't think you'll owe taxes on them?
