Chapter 19, Section 4: 401(k) and Employer Match Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the 401(k) plan, an employer-sponsored retirement savings vehicle crucial for financial planning. It details how 401(k)s work, emphasizing the highly valuable employer match as a source of "free money." The section also explains vesting schedules and differentiates between Traditional and Roth 401(k) options, providing a strategic order for retirement savings.

Key Concepts

  • 401(k) Plan: An employer-sponsored retirement savings plan allowing employees to contribute a portion of their paycheck on a pre-tax basis. Contributions grow tax-deferred and are taxed upon withdrawal in retirement.
  • Pre-tax Contributions: Money contributed to a Traditional 401(k) is deducted from your paycheck before taxes are calculated, reducing your current taxable income.
  • Tax-Deferred Growth: Investments within a 401(k) grow without being taxed annually, allowing for faster compounding. Taxes are only paid when funds are withdrawn in retirement.
  • High Contribution Limits: 401(k) plans have significantly higher annual contribution limits (e.g., $23,000) compared to IRAs, making them a primary retirement savings tool.
  • Employer Match: A contribution made by your employer to your 401(k) account, often contingent on your own contributions. This is considered "free money" and offers an immediate, significant return on investment.
  • Vesting: The process by which employees gain full ownership of employer-contributed funds over time. If an employee leaves a company before being fully vested, they may forfeit some or all of the employer's contributions.
  • Traditional vs. Roth 401(k): Traditional 401(k) uses pre-tax contributions and tax-deferred growth, with withdrawals taxed in retirement. A Roth 401(k) uses after-tax contributions, but qualified withdrawals in retirement are tax-free.
  • Strategic Savings Order: A recommended approach to maximize retirement savings involves prioritizing the employer match, then a Roth IRA, and finally additional 401(k) contributions.

Important Terms

  • 401(k) Plan: An employer-sponsored retirement savings account that allows employees to contribute a portion of their gross salary on a pre-tax basis, with investments growing tax-deferred until retirement.
  • Employer Match: Contributions made by an employer to an employee's 401(k) plan, typically a percentage of the employee's own contributions up to a certain limit (e.g., 50% of the first 6% of salary).
  • Vesting: The legal right an employee earns over time to the employer-contributed funds in their retirement account. It dictates when those funds fully become the employee's property.
  • Cliff Vesting: A vesting schedule where an employee becomes 100% vested in employer contributions after a specific period of employment (e.g., 3 years), but owns nothing before that period.
  • Graded Vesting: A vesting schedule where an employee gradually gains ownership of employer contributions over a period of time (e.g., 20% per year for 5 years).
  • Roth 401(k): A type of 401(k) plan where contributions are made with after-tax dollars, and all qualified withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free.

Detailed Notes

Understanding the 401(k) Plan

  • What it is: An employer-sponsored retirement savings plan.
  • How it works:
    • Employees contribute a portion of their paycheck.
    • Contributions are typically made on a pre-tax basis, meaning they reduce your current taxable income.
    • The money grows tax-deferred, meaning you don't pay taxes on investment gains until withdrawal.
    • Withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income.
  • Origin: Named after Section 401(k) of the U.S. tax code.
  • Contribution Process:
    • You choose a percentage of each paycheck to contribute.
    • This money is deducted from your gross pay before income taxes are calculated.
    • Funds are invested in a selection of options provided by your employer, usually mutual funds and index funds.
  • Contribution Limits: 401(k) plans have high annual contribution limits (e.g., $23,000 in current year), making them a primary retirement savings vehicle for most working individuals, significantly higher than IRA limits (e.g., $7,000).

The Employer Match: Your "Free Money"

  • Most Valuable Feature: Many employers contribute money to your 401(k) in addition to your own contributions. This is often referred to as "free money" because it requires no extra effort beyond your own savings.
  • Mechanism:
    • The employer match is contingent on your contributions. You must contribute to receive it.
    • Common structures include matching a percentage of your contributions up to a certain percentage of your salary (e.g., 50% match on the first 6% of your salary, or 100% match on the first 3% of your salary).
  • Calculating an Employer Match Example:
    • Scenario: Annual salary $50,000; employer matches 50% on the first 6% of salary you contribute.
    • Step 1: Calculate your necessary contribution to get the full match.
      • 6% of $50,000 = $3,000
      • (This is the amount you need to contribute.)
    • Step 2: Calculate the employer's match.
      • 50% of your $3,000 contribution = $1,500
      • (This is the amount your employer contributes.)
    • Step 3: Find the total annual contribution to your retirement account.
      • Your contribution ($3,000) + Employer Match ($1,500) = $4,500
    • Instant Return: The employer match provides an immediate 50% return on your contributed funds ($1,500 / $3,000 = 50%) before any market gains.
  • Critical Rule: Always contribute at least enough to get the full employer match. Failing to do so is leaving free money on the table, potentially costing you tens of thousands of dollars over your career due to lost contributions and compound growth.

Vesting: When Employer Money Becomes Yours

  • The Catch: While your own 401(k) contributions are always 100% yours immediately, employer contributions may be subject to a vesting schedule.
  • Definition: Vesting is the process by which you earn full ownership of the funds your employer contributes.
  • Impact: If you leave the company before you are fully vested, you may forfeit some or all of the employer's contributions.
  • Common Vesting Schedules:
    • Cliff Vesting: You become 100% vested after a specific number of years of service (e.g., after 3 years). If you leave before this period, you receive none of the employer contributions.
    • Graded Vesting: You gradually gain ownership over time (e.g., 20% vested after 1 year, 40% after 2 years, reaching 100% after 5 years).
  • Important: Always check your employer's vesting schedule before making job changes to avoid losing valuable employer contributions.

Traditional vs. Roth 401(k)

  • Roth 401(k) Option: Many modern 401(k) plans offer a Roth option in addition to the traditional pre-tax option.
  • Key Differences:
    • Traditional 401(k): Contributions are pre-tax, grow tax-deferred, and withdrawals are taxed in retirement.
    • Roth 401(k): Contributions are made with after-tax dollars, but all qualified growth and withdrawals in retirement are tax-free.
  • Contribution Limit: The Roth 401(k) shares the same high contribution limit (e.g., $23,000) as the Traditional 401(k), making it a powerful tool for tax-free growth, especially for young workers.
  • Benefit for Young Workers: For those currently in lower tax brackets, contributing to a Roth 401(k) can be advantageous, as paying taxes now means avoiding potentially higher taxes on withdrawals in a higher income tax bracket during retirement.

Ideal Retirement Savings Order

For most young workers, a strategic approach to retirement savings is recommended:

  1. Contribute to your 401(k) at least up to the full employer match. This secures the "free money" first, offering an unbeatable immediate return.
  2. Open and max out a Roth IRA. This allows for additional tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement, with a lower contribution limit (e.g., $7,000/year).
  3. If you have additional funds, contribute more to your 401(k) up to its annual limit. This allows you to leverage the high contribution limits of the 401(k) beyond the employer match. This order maximizes free money and tax-free growth simultaneously, setting a strong foundation for retirement.

Review Questions

  1. Explain how a Traditional 401(k) reduces your current taxable income and how its growth is taxed over time.
  2. Describe the "employer match" feature of a 401(k). Why is it considered "free money," and what is the most important rule related to it?
  3. Imagine your salary is $60,000, and your employer offers a 100% match on the first 3% of your salary. How much money would your employer contribute to your 401(k) if you contributed enough to get the full match? What is the "instant return" on your contribution?
  4. What is vesting, and why is it an important consideration when evaluating employer-contributed funds in a 401(k)? Compare and contrast cliff vesting and graded vesting schedules.
  5. What is the key difference between a Traditional 401(k) and a Roth 401(k)? For whom might a Roth 401(k) be a better choice, and why?
