Compound Interest Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces compound interest, contrasting it with simple interest by explaining that with compound interest, you earn interest not only on your initial principal but also on the interest you've already accumulated. This "interest earning interest" phenomenon leads to significantly faster growth of savings over time, a concept often referred to as the power of compounding. The section details how to calculate compound interest year-by-year and provides a general formula to determine the future value of an investment based on principal, interest rate, compounding frequency, and time.

Key Concepts

  • Simple vs. Compound Interest:
    • Simple Interest: Interest is calculated solely on the original principal amount. Your principal grows, but the interest earned each period remains constant if the principal doesn't change.
    • Compound Interest: Interest is calculated on the original principal and on all accumulated interest from previous periods. This means your balance grows, and then that larger balance earns even more interest in the next period.
  • The Power of Compounding: Even small differences in interest calculation (simple vs. compound) lead to enormous discrepancies in total earnings over long periods. The effect accelerates over time as interest increasingly earns interest.
  • Compound Interest Formula: A mathematical model used to calculate the future value of an investment or loan with compound interest. The formula is $A = P(1 + r/n)^{nt}$.
  • Variables in the Compound Interest Formula: Each letter in the formula represents a specific value:
    • A: The final amount (principal + all interest).
    • P: The principal (the starting amount deposited or borrowed).
    • r: The annual interest rate, expressed as a decimal (e.g., 6% is 0.06).
    • n: The number of times interest is compounded per year (e.g., 1 for annually, 12 for monthly, 365 for daily).
    • t: The time in years.
  • Understanding Exponents: The term $(1 + r/n)^{nt}$ in the formula involves an exponent. This means the base number $(1 + r/n)$ is multiplied by itself $nt$ times, representing the total number of compounding periods over the investment's life.
  • Compounding Frequency's Impact: The more frequently interest is compounded (e.g., daily vs. annually), the more rapidly your money grows, even if the stated annual interest rate (APR) is the same. This is because interest begins to earn interest sooner.

Important Terms

  • Compound Interest: Interest calculated on both the original principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods. Each time interest is added, the new, larger balance becomes the basis for the next interest calculation.
  • Simple Interest: Interest calculated only on the original principal amount of a deposit or loan.
  • Principal (P): The initial amount of money deposited, invested, or borrowed.
  • Annual Interest Rate (r): The percentage rate at which interest is earned or charged per year, always expressed as a decimal in financial formulas.
  • Compounding Periods per Year (n): The number of times per year that interest is calculated and added to the principal balance. Common frequencies include annually (n=1), monthly (n=12), and daily (n=365).
  • Time in Years (t): The duration, in years, over which the principal is invested or borrowed.
  • Final Amount (A): The total value of the investment or loan at the end of the specified time period, including the original principal and all accumulated interest.
  • Exponent: A mathematical notation indicating the number of times a base number is to be multiplied by itself. For example, in $x^y$, $y$ is the exponent, meaning $x$ is multiplied by itself $y$ times.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Compound Interest

Compound interest is a fundamental concept in finance, crucial for understanding savings, investments, and loans. The core idea is simple: your interest earns interest. Unlike simple interest, where interest is only calculated on the initial principal, compound interest factors in all previously earned interest, adding it to your principal before the next interest calculation. This creates a snowball effect, accelerating the growth of your money.

Seeing It Year by Year

Let's illustrate the concept with an example: Imagine you deposit $1,000 into an account earning 6% annual interest, compounded annually.

  • Start: $1,000.00
  • Year 1: You earn 6% on $1,000.00.
    • $1,000.00 \times 1.06 = $1,060.00
    • Your balance is now $1,060.00.
  • Year 2: You earn 6% on the new balance of $1,060.00.
    • $1,060.00 \times 1.06 = $1,123.60
    • Notice you earned interest on the $60 interest from Year 1.
  • Year 3: You earn 6% on the $1,123.60.
    • $1,123.60 \times 1.06 = $1,191.02
    • At this point, the account has $1,191.02.

Comparison with Simple Interest: If this were simple interest, after 3 years at 6% on $1,000, you would have: $1,000 + ($1,000 \times 0.06 \times 3) = $1,000 + $180 = $1,180.00. With compound interest, you have $1,191.02, which is already $11.02 more. This difference might seem small initially, but the section emphasizes that "that gap grows every year." Over 30 years, a compound account could reach $5,743.49, while a simple interest account would only reach $2,800.00. This stark contrast highlights the significant "power of compounding."

The Compound Interest Formula

To calculate compound interest over any period without step-by-step calculations, we use a general formula:

$A = P(1 + r/n)^{nt}$

Where:

  • A = Final amount (principal + all interest)
  • P = Principal (starting amount)
  • r = Annual interest rate (as a decimal)
  • n = Number of compounding periods per year
    • Annual: $n = 1$
    • Monthly: $n = 12$
    • Daily: $n = 365$
  • t = Time in years

Understanding Exponents

The formula uses an exponent, $(1 + r/n)^{nt}$. An exponent tells you to multiply a number by itself a certain number of times. For example, $(1.06)^3$ means $1.06 \times 1.06 \times 1.06$.

  • $1.06 \times 1.06 = 1.1236$
  • $1.1236 \times 1.06 = 1.191016 \approx 1.1910$ On a calculator, you typically use the $y^x$ or ^ key to perform exponentiation.

Compound Interest at Different Frequencies

The frequency of compounding (n) significantly impacts the final amount. Let's compare a $1,000 deposit at 6% annual interest for 10 years under different compounding frequencies:

  • $P = $1,000$
  • $r = 0.06$
  • $t = 10$ years

  1. Annual Compounding ($n = 1$): $A = 1,000(1 + 0.06/1)^{1 \times 10}$ $A = 1,000(1.06)^{10}$ Since $(1.06)^{10} \approx 1.790847$ $A = 1,000 \times 1.790847 = $1,790.85$

  2. Monthly Compounding ($n = 12$): $A = 1,000(1 + 0.06/12)^{12 \times 10}$ $A = 1,000(1 + 0.005)^{120}$ $A = 1,000(1.005)^{120}$ Since $(1.005)^{120} \approx 1.819397$ $A = 1,000 \times 1.819397 = $1,819.40$

  3. Daily Compounding ($n = 365$): $A = 1,000(1 + 0.06/365)^{365 \times 10}$ $A = 1,000(1 + 0.00016438...)^{3650}$ Since $(1 + 0.06/365)^{3650} \approx 1.822029$ $A = 1,000 \times 1.822029 = $1,822.03$

Comparison Summary:

Compounding Frequency n Final Amount (A)
Annually 1 $1,790.85
Monthly 12 $1,819.40
Daily 365 $1,822.03

As the table shows, all scenarios use the same starting amount ($1,000) and rate (6%), but more frequent compounding leads to more money. This is because "interest starts earning interest sooner."

Practical Money Tip: When choosing a savings account, prioritize those that compound interest monthly or daily over annually. Even with the same stated Annual Percentage Rate (APR), more frequent compounding will make your money grow faster.

Review Questions

  1. What is the fundamental difference between simple and compound interest? Provide a simple example to illustrate why compound interest is generally more beneficial for an investor over the long term.
  2. Explain what is meant by "the power of compounding." How does the difference between simple and compound interest grow over time, and what causes this accelerated growth?
  3. Write out the compound interest formula ($A = P(1 + r/n)^{nt}$) and clearly define what each of the five variables (A, P, r, n, t) represents in the context of an investment.
  4. You deposit $2,500 into a savings account that offers an annual interest rate of 4.5%. Calculate the balance of your account after 7 years if the interest is compounded annually. Show your work using the compound interest formula.
  5. Why does compounding interest more frequently (e.g., monthly or daily, as opposed to annually) result in a higher final amount for the investor, even when the stated annual interest rate (APR) remains the same?
