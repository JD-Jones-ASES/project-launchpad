Study Guide: Interest Rates and APR

Overview

This section explains how credit card interest works, focusing on the Annual Percentage Rate (APR) and its role in calculating charges. It details both a simplified monthly interest calculation and the technically accurate daily periodic rate method, providing essential formulas and examples to understand the true cost of carrying a credit card balance.

Key Concepts

  • Carrying a Balance Incurs Interest: If you don't pay your full credit card statement balance each month, the credit card company will charge you interest on the amount you owe.
  • APR (Annual Percentage Rate): This is the yearly interest rate applied to any outstanding balance. It's crucial for determining how much extra you'll pay.
  • Interest Accrues Daily: Credit card companies technically calculate interest based on your "average daily balance" using a daily periodic rate, rather than just a simple monthly calculation.
  • Monthly Interest Calculation (Simplified): For most practical purposes and this course, monthly interest can be calculated by dividing the APR by 12 (to get a monthly rate) and multiplying it by your outstanding balance.
  • Daily Periodic Rate: This is the actual daily interest rate applied to your balance. It's calculated by dividing the APR by 365.
  • The High Cost of Debt: Credit card interest rates are significantly higher than typical savings account interest rates, making paying off high-interest debt a critical financial priority.

Important Terms

  • APR (Annual Percentage Rate): The annual interest rate charged on any credit card balance carried from month to month. Expressed as a percentage.
  • Balance: The total amount of money you owe on your credit card at a given time.
  • Monthly Interest: The amount of interest charged on your balance for one billing cycle, typically one month.
  • Daily Periodic Rate: The daily interest rate applied to your credit card balance, calculated by dividing the APR by 365.
  • Average Daily Balance: The method credit card companies use to calculate interest, tracking your balance each day of the billing cycle and applying the daily periodic rate to the average.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Interest Charges

When you use a credit card and do not pay the full statement balance by the due date, you are "carrying a balance." The credit card company charges you interest on this outstanding amount. Understanding this calculation is fundamental to managing your finances effectively.

What is APR?

The APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is the annual cost of borrowing money on your credit card, expressed as a percentage.

  • Credit card APRs are typically high, often ranging from 15% to 30% or more.
  • The average credit card APR has been above 20% in recent years, making credit card debt particularly expensive.

How Credit Card Interest is Calculated

Credit card companies technically calculate interest using your average daily balance. This means they track your balance every single day of the billing cycle and multiply it by a daily rate.

For this course, a simplified but accurate monthly calculation is used for easier understanding, which yields very similar results to the actual daily method.

Core Formula 1: Monthly Credit Card Interest

This formula helps you quickly estimate the monthly interest charge.

$$ Monthly\ Interest = Balance \times \frac{APR}{12} $$

Where:

  • Balance: The amount you owe at the beginning of the month.
  • APR: The Annual Percentage Rate, expressed as a decimal (e.g., 19.99% = 0.1999).
  • 12: Represents the 12 months in a year. Dividing the APR by 12 converts the annual rate into a monthly rate.

Example: Calculating Monthly Interest

  • Situation: You have a credit card with a $2,500 balance and an APR of 19.99%.
  • Step 1: Convert APR to a decimal.
    • 19.99% ÷ 100 = 0.1999
  • Step 2: Divide by 12 to get the monthly rate.
    • 0.1999 ÷ 12 = 0.016658 (rounded to 6 decimal places)
  • Step 3: Multiply by the balance.
    • Monthly Interest = $2,500 × 0.016658 = $41.65

Result: Carrying a $2,500 balance for one month at 19.99% APR will cost you $41.65 in interest for that month. If this balance remained constant for a year, you would pay approximately $41.65 × 12 = $499.80 in interest annually, just for borrowing that $2,500.

Core Formula 2: Daily Periodic Rate

This is the rate that credit card companies actually use to calculate interest on a daily basis.

$$ Daily\ Periodic\ Rate = \frac{APR}{365} $$

Where:

  • APR: The Annual Percentage Rate, expressed as a decimal.
  • 365: Represents the 365 days in a year. Dividing the APR by 365 converts the annual rate into a daily rate.

Example: Calculating the Daily Periodic Rate

  • Situation: Same card, 19.99% APR.
  • Step 1: Convert APR to decimal.
    • 19.99% ÷ 100 = 0.1999
  • Step 2: Divide by 365.
    • Daily Rate = 0.1999 ÷ 365 = 0.000548 (rounded)

Result: Each day, the credit card company multiplies your balance by 0.000548 (or about 0.0548%) to calculate that day's interest. For a $2,500 balance, this would be $2,500 × 0.000548 = $1.37 per day. Over 30 days, this would accrue $1.37 × 30 = $41.10, which is very close to the monthly calculation. The slight difference is due to rounding and variations in the number of days per month (30 vs. an average of 30.42).

The High Cost of Debt vs. Value of Savings

It's important to recognize that credit card APRs are significantly higher than what you might earn on a high-yield savings account.

  • Debt is expensive: $1,000 carried on a credit card at 22% APR costs you about $220 in interest per year.
  • Savings grow slowly (comparatively): The same $1,000 in a savings account at 4.5% APR would only earn you about $45 in interest per year. This stark difference highlights why paying off high-interest credit card debt is often considered one of the best "investments" you can make.

Review Questions

  2. If you have a $1,500 balance on a credit card with a 24% APR, what would be your estimated monthly interest charge using the simplified monthly calculation? Show your steps.
  3. Describe the difference between how credit card interest is technically calculated (average daily balance) versus the simplified monthly calculation presented in this section. Why is it useful to understand both?
  4. Calculate the daily periodic rate for a credit card with a 21% APR. If you had an average daily balance of $1,200, how much interest would you accrue in one day? (Round to 6 decimal places for the rate).
  5. Based on the "Did You Know" section, why is paying off credit card debt often considered a better financial move than trying to earn interest in a savings account, especially when you have high-interest debt?
