Chapter 7, Section 5: Filing Basics

Overview

Filing a tax return means reporting your income to the government and calculating whether you owe more taxes or are entitled to a refund. This section covers the key documents, deadlines, statuses, and strategies every filer needs to know.

Key Documents

Form W-2 (Wage and Tax Statement)

  • Provided by your employer by January 31 of the following year
  • Reports your total annual wages and all taxes withheld
  • Critical boxes:
    • Box 1 — Total wages subject to federal income tax
    • Box 2 — Federal income tax withheld (use this to calculate your refund or balance due)
    • Boxes 3/4 — Social Security wages and tax withheld
    • Boxes 5/6 — Medicare wages and tax withheld
    • Boxes 16/17 — State wages and state income tax withheld
  • If you have multiple jobs, you receive one W-2 per employer and combine all wages on your return

Form 1040 (U.S. Individual Income Tax Return)

  • The main federal tax return form, filed each spring
  • Walks through:
    1. Report all income sources
    2. Subtract adjustments → AGI (Adjusted Gross Income)
    3. Subtract standard or itemized deduction → Taxable Income
    4. Apply tax brackets → Tax Owed
    5. Subtract credits
    6. Compare to taxes withheld → Refund or Balance Due

Form W-4 (Employee's Withholding Certificate)

  • Filled out when starting a new job
  • Tells your employer how much to withhold from each paycheck
  • Update it if your situation changes (second job, marriage, new dependent)
  • Available at IRS.gov with a withholding estimator

Filing Status

Your filing status affects your standard deduction and tax bracket thresholds.

Status Who Qualifies
Single Unmarried individuals
Married Filing Jointly Married couples combining income (usually most advantageous)
Married Filing Separately Married couples on separate returns (rarely beneficial)
Head of Household Unmarried person paying >50% of costs for a home with a qualifying dependent; larger deduction than Single

Who Must File?

You must file a federal return if gross income exceeds your standard deduction:

Filing Status 2024 Threshold
Single (under 65) $14,600
Head of Household (under 65) $21,900
Married Filing Jointly (both under 65) $29,200

Always file even if below the threshold if:

  • Federal taxes were withheld from your paychecks (only way to get a refund)
  • You qualify for a refundable tax credit (e.g., Earned Income Tax Credit)

Tax Refund vs. Tax Due

  • Refund = withholding exceeded your actual tax owed → IRS returns the overpayment
  • Balance due = withholding was less than your actual tax → you pay the difference by April 15

Key insight: A refund is NOT a bonus — it is your own money returned. A large refund means you gave the government an interest-free loan all year. Ideal withholding closely matches your actual tax.

Free Filing Options

  • VITA (Volunteer Income Tax Assistance) — Free in-person help at community centers, libraries, and schools for eligible filers
  • IRS Free File — Free online filing tools at IRS.gov for qualifying income levels
  • E-filing — ~94% of returns are filed electronically; faster processing and refunds in ~2 weeks

Watch Out: Tax Scams

The IRS will NEVER:

  • Call demanding immediate payment
  • Threaten arrest for non-payment
  • Request payment by gift card, wire transfer, or cryptocurrency
  • Email requesting your Social Security number

Rule: The IRS communicates primarily by mail. Any call or email demanding urgent payment is a scam — hang up, do not click links, do not provide personal information.

Key Terms

  • Form W-2 — Employer document reporting wages and withheld taxes
  • Form 1040 — Main individual federal tax return
  • Form W-4 — Withholding certificate you submit to your employer
  • AGI (Adjusted Gross Income) — Total income minus certain adjustments
  • Taxable income — AGI minus your standard or itemized deduction
  • Filing status — Classification (Single, MFJ, MFS, HoH) that determines deduction amounts and bracket thresholds
  • VITA — Volunteer Income Tax Assistance; free in-person tax help
  • Tax refund — Return of overpaid withholding; not extra income
