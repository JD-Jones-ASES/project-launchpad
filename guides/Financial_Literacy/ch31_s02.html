Chapter 31, Section 2: Charitable Giving and Tax Benefits

Overview

If you donate money to a qualifying organization, you may be able to reduce your tax bill through a charitable deduction. However, most young donors do not receive this benefit — understanding why will help you make smarter decisions about when and how to give.

What Is a Charitable Deduction?

  • A charitable deduction is a reduction in your taxable income (not a direct reduction in taxes owed)
  • Allowed by the IRS when you donate money or property to a qualified tax-exempt organization
  • These are commonly called 501(c)(3) organizations — the IRS designation for tax-exempt nonprofits
  • Does not apply to:
    • Gifts to individuals
    • Political campaigns
    • Most non-501(c)(3) purposes

Standard Deduction vs. Itemizing

To claim a charitable deduction, you must itemize deductions on your tax return.

What Is Itemizing?

  • Listing all eligible deductions (mortgage interest, medical expenses, state and local taxes, charitable donations)
  • Comparing their total to the standard deduction
  • You take whichever is larger

2024 Standard Deduction Amounts

Filing Status Standard Deduction
Single $14,600
Married Filing Jointly $29,200

Why Most Young People Don't Get the Tax Benefit

  • Most young, single earners have no mortgage and few large deductions
  • Their total itemized deductions typically fall below $14,600
  • So they take the standard deduction — and charitable donations provide no additional tax benefit
  • Important: this does not make giving unwise — donations still help the organizations you support

When You Do Itemize: Calculating the Deduction

Formula

Tax savings = donation amount × marginal tax bracket rate

Example: 22% Bracket, $2,000 Donation

  • Step 1: Tax rate = 22% = 0.22
  • Step 2: Tax savings = $2,000 × 0.22 = $440
  • Effective out-of-pocket cost = $2,000 − $440 = $1,560
  • The IRS effectively subsidizes part of your charitable giving

How Tax Bracket Affects the Subsidy

Tax Bracket $2,000 Donation — Tax Savings Effective Cost
22% $440 $1,560
32% $640 $1,360

The higher your bracket, the larger the effective government subsidy on your giving.

Donor-Advised Funds (DAFs)

  • A donor-advised fund (DAF) is an account you fund with a large donation in one tax year
  • You receive an immediate deduction for the full amount in that year
  • You then distribute the money to charities over subsequent years at your discretion

The Bunching Strategy

  • Instead of giving $3,000/year for 3 years (each year likely below the standard deduction threshold):
  • Contribute $9,000 in a single year to a DAF (may exceed the standard deduction → generates a deduction)
  • Then distribute $3,000/year to charities over the next three years
  • Bunching = concentrating multiple years of donations into one year to unlock the deduction

The Money Tip: Giving Is a Values Decision

  • Just because you do not get a tax deduction does not mean giving is financially unwise
  • The tax benefit is a bonus for those who happen to itemize — not the primary reason to give
  • Most young donors give because of their values, not because of the tax math

Key Terms

  • Charitable deduction — an IRS-allowed reduction in taxable income for qualifying donations
  • 501(c)(3) organization — the IRS designation for a qualified tax-exempt nonprofit
  • Itemizing deductions — listing individual eligible deductions instead of taking the standard deduction
  • Standard deduction — a fixed IRS amount subtracted from income without itemizing ($14,600 single / $29,200 MFJ in 2024)
  • Donor-advised fund (DAF) — a charitable account that accepts a large lump contribution with immediate deduction, distributing to charities over time
  • Bunching — concentrating multiple years of donations into a single tax year to exceed the standard deduction threshold

Key Takeaways

  • A charitable deduction reduces taxable income, not taxes directly — savings depend on your bracket rate
  • Only donations to 501(c)(3) organizations qualify; individual gifts and political donations do not
  • You must itemize to claim the deduction; most young earners cannot exceed the standard deduction
  • Tax savings = donation × bracket rate (e.g., $2,000 × 22% = $440 savings)
  • Higher brackets receive a larger subsidy per dollar donated
  • DAFs and bunching are strategies to generate itemized deductions when giving in larger amounts
  • Tax law changes — always consult a professional for your specific situation
