Chapter 31, Section 4: Building Generosity into Your Budget

Overview

The most effective way to give consistently is to treat giving like any other budget category: planned, automatic, and non-negotiable. Giving that happens only when there is money "left over" rarely happens at all — because there is almost never money left over when you are not planning for it.

Start With What You Can

  • If money is tight, start small — but start
  • Even 1% of your income (one penny out of every dollar) is a meaningful beginning
  • The habit of generosity built early tends to grow over time as income and financial stability improve

Two Approaches to Giving

Approach Description Result
Approach A "I'll give when I have extra money." Rarely results in consistent giving
Approach B "I give $25/month, automatically, before I spend anything else." Giving happens every month, regardless of mood or impulse spending

Automatic Giving

  • Just as you automate savings contributions, you can automate giving
  • Most charities and community organizations accept recurring monthly donations
  • Set it up once → giving happens without ongoing decision-making
  • This is exactly the same psychology that makes automatic retirement contributions so powerful
  • Automation removes the temptation to skip a month

Evaluating Where to Give

Before donating to any organization, research the following:

  1. What does the organization do? Can you clearly explain their mission in one sentence?
  2. What percentage of donations goes to programs vs. overhead? Nonprofit watchdog websites publish this data. No single ratio is "right," but it enables comparison.
  3. Is it a qualified 501(c)(3)? Verify for free on the IRS website.
  4. Does it operate in an area or issue you care about?

Volunteering: Time Has Value Too

  • Not all generosity is financial — time, skills, and expertise have genuine value
  • Examples of high-value volunteering:
    • A skilled accountant helping a nonprofit file its annual return
    • A student tutoring younger kids
    • A person helping an elderly neighbor with groceries
  • Volunteering also builds:
    • Skills
    • Community connections
    • Sense of purpose

The Math of Consistent Small Giving

Example: $25/Month on a $2,000/Month Income

  • $25 ÷ $2,000 = 1.25% of income
  • Over 1 year: $25 × 12 = $300
  • Over 10 years (unadjusted): $25 × 120 = $3,000

As income grows and the giving habit grows with it — even at the same percentage — the annual and lifetime impact grows significantly.

Building a "Giving Portfolio"

Just as you diversify investments, you can diversify giving across different types of organizations:

Category Description Examples
Local Organizations serving your immediate community Food bank, shelter, mentorship programs
National or Global Causes addressing large-scale problems Disease research, disaster relief, environmental conservation
Personal Causes tied to your own experience, community, or values Any cause with personal significance
  • This is a framework, not a requirement
  • Some people give everything to one trusted organization; others spread widely — both are valid

The Bigger Picture

You have now studied the full arc of personal finance — earning, budgeting, saving, investing, borrowing, protecting, and planning. That knowledge is valuable, but it is a means, not an end.

  • Financial literacy without generosity is just accumulation
  • A full financial life includes intentional giving as part of the plan
  • Money enables: security, freedom, opportunity, and the ability to care for yourself and others

The Simple Rule

Give first. Save second. Live on the rest.

Whether you start at 1%, 5%, or 10% — putting giving first ensures it actually happens.

Key Terms

  • Automatic giving — setting up a recurring monthly donation so giving occurs without ongoing decisions
  • Overhead ratio — the percentage of a charity's donations that go to administrative costs vs. programs
  • 501(c)(3) — the IRS designation for a qualified tax-exempt nonprofit organization
  • Giving portfolio — distributing charitable donations across multiple types of organizations, analogous to investment diversification
  • Bunching (giving context) — covered in Section 2; concentrating giving into one year to maximize tax benefit

Key Takeaways

  • Planned, automatic giving is the only reliable way to give consistently — leftover money rarely materializes
  • Start with 1% if that is all you can manage; the habit matters more than the amount at the start
  • Automatic giving removes decision fatigue and functions like automated retirement savings
  • Research any organization before donating: mission clarity, overhead ratio, and 501(c)(3) status
  • Volunteering time and skills is a genuine form of generosity with compounding personal benefits
  • $25/month = $300/year = $3,000 over a decade — small consistent giving accumulates into something real
  • Diversifying giving across local, national/global, and personal causes mirrors good investment practice
  • The closing principle of the entire course: give first, save second, live on the rest
