Simple Interest: A Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the concept of simple interest, which is the most basic form of interest calculation. Simple interest is earned or charged only on the initial amount of money deposited or borrowed, known as the principal, and does not compound. It explains the key components—principal, interest rate, and time—and provides formulas for calculating both the interest earned and the total final amount.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of Simple Interest: Simple interest is calculated solely on the original principal amount. The interest earned (or paid) each period remains constant because it doesn't earn interest on previously accumulated interest.
  • Principal ($P$): This is the initial sum of money that is either deposited into an account or borrowed as a loan. It's the starting point for all interest calculations.
  • Interest Rate ($r$): The percentage charged or earned on the principal per year. It is crucial to convert this percentage to a decimal before using it in any formula (e.g., 6% becomes 0.06).
  • Time ($t$): The duration, measured in years, for which the money is deposited or borrowed. If time is given in months, it must be converted to a fraction of a year (e.g., 6 months = 0.5 years).
  • Simple Interest Formula ($I = Prt$): This formula calculates the total amount of interest earned or paid over the specified time period.
  • Total Amount/Final Balance Formula ($A = P + I$ or $A = P(1 + rt)$): This formula calculates the total value of the investment or loan at the end of the time period, including both the original principal and the accumulated simple interest.
  • Linear Growth: Simple interest results in a consistent increase in the total balance over time, as the same amount of interest is earned each period.

Important Terms

  • Principal ($P$): The original amount of money invested or borrowed.
  • Interest Rate ($r$): The annual percentage charged on the principal, always converted to a decimal for calculations.
  • Time ($t$): The duration for which the principal is invested or borrowed, expressed in years.
  • Interest ($I$): The monetary amount earned or paid for the use of the principal.
  • Final Amount ($A$): The total value of the principal plus the accumulated interest at the end of the term.
  • Simple Interest: A method of calculating interest where interest is only computed on the initial principal amount.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Simple Interest

Interest is essentially the cost of using money over a period of time. When you deposit money in a bank, the bank pays you for the use of your money. When you borrow money, you pay the lender for the use of theirs. Simple interest is the most straightforward way to calculate this cost. It's unique because the interest is only ever calculated on the original amount you started with, the principal. This means that any interest you earn does not itself earn further interest.

Key Variables in Simple Interest

To work with simple interest, you need to identify three main variables:

  1. Principal ($P$): This is the initial sum of money.
    • Example: If you deposit $1,000, your principal is $1,000. If you take out a $5,000 loan, your principal is $5,000.
  2. Interest Rate ($r$): This is given as an annual percentage.
    • Crucial Step: Always convert the percentage rate to a decimal before using it in any formula.
      • To convert: Divide the percentage by 100, or move the decimal point two places to the left.
      • Examples:
        • 6% = 6 / 100 = 0.06
        • 3.5% = 3.5 / 100 = 0.035
        • 12% = 12 / 100 = 0.12
  3. Time ($t$): This is the duration for which the money is invested or borrowed.
    • Crucial Step: Time must be expressed in years.
      • If the period is given in months, divide the number of months by 12 to convert it into years.
      • Example: 6 months = 6/12 = 0.5 years; 18 months = 18/12 = 1.5 years.

The Simple Interest Formulas

There are two primary formulas you'll use:

  1. To calculate the Interest Earned ($I$): [ I = P r t ] Where:

    • $I$ = The total amount of interest earned or paid (in dollars).
    • $P$ = The principal (original deposit or loan amount, in dollars).
    • $r$ = The annual interest rate as a decimal.
    • $t$ = The time in years.

  2. To calculate the Final Amount ($A$) (Principal + Interest): [ A = P + I ] Alternatively, you can substitute $I = Prt$ into the first formula: [ A = P(1 + rt) ] Where:

    • $A$ = The final amount, which is the original principal plus the interest earned.

Step-by-Step Examples

Let's walk through the examples provided to solidify your understanding.

Example 1: Calculating Simple Interest You deposit $1,000 in an account that pays 6% simple interest per year. How much interest do you earn after 10 years? What is your total balance?

  • Step 1: Identify the variables.
    • $P = $1,000$
    • $r = 6% = 0.06$ (converted to decimal)
    • $t = 10$ years
  • Step 2: Calculate the interest ($I$).
    • Using the formula $I = Prt$:
    • $I = $1,000 \times 0.06 \times 10 = $600.00$
  • Step 3: Calculate the total balance ($A$).
    • Using the formula $A = P + I$:
    • $A = $1,000 + $600 = $1,600.00$

Observation: Notice that you earn $600 in interest over 10 years. This means you earn $60 per year ($1,000 \times 0.06 = $60$). This consistent annual interest is a hallmark of simple interest.

Example 2: Simple Interest Over Different Periods A $500 savings bond pays 4% simple interest. How much is it worth after (a) 2 years and (b) 5 years?

  • Identify common variables:
    • $P = $500$
    • $r = 4% = 0.04$
  • (a) After 2 years:
    • $t = 2$ years
    • $I = Prt = $500 \times 0.04 \times 2 = $40.00$
    • $A = P + I = $500 + $40 = $540.00$
  • (b) After 5 years:
    • $t = 5$ years
    • $I = Prt = $500 \times 0.04 \times 5 = $100.00$
    • $A = P + I = $500 + $100 = $600.00$

Observation: In this example, the balance increases by $20 per year ($500 \times 0.04 = $20$). This demonstrates that simple interest grows linearly, meaning it increases by the same fixed amount for each unit of time.

Practical Applications of Simple Interest

While not common for most long-term savings accounts, simple interest is typically used for:

  • Short-term loans: Like personal loans that might last for a few months to a year or two.
  • Some types of savings bonds: Government bonds or specific investment vehicles.

It's important to be aware that most savings accounts and investments that aim for significant growth over time utilize compound interest, which will be covered in the next section. Compound interest allows interest to earn interest, leading to much faster growth.
