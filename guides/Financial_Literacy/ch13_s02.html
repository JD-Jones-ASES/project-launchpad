Chapter 13, Section 2: The Five Factors of Your FICO Score

Overview

Your FICO credit score is a crucial numerical representation of your creditworthiness, derived from five specific factors, each weighted according to its importance in predicting your ability to repay debt. Understanding these factors—Payment History, Amounts Owed/Credit Utilization, Length of Credit History, New Credit/Hard Inquiries, and Credit Mix—is essential for building, protecting, and improving your credit score over time.

Key Concepts

  • FICO Score Calculation: Credit scores are not arbitrary; they are calculated based on five weighted factors that predict repayment behavior.
  • Payment History (35%): The most significant factor, emphasizing the importance of paying bills on time. Late payments severely damage scores.
  • Amounts Owed / Credit Utilization (30%): The second most important factor, measuring how much of your available credit you are using. A low utilization ratio (ideally under 30%) is best for your score.
  • Length of Credit History (15%): Reflects the age of your credit accounts; longer histories generally indicate more experience managing credit.
  • New Credit / Hard Inquiries (10%): Shows how often you apply for new credit. Hard inquiries temporarily lower your score, while soft inquiries do not.
  • Credit Mix (10%): Demonstrates your ability to manage different types of credit, such as revolving accounts (credit cards) and installment loans (mortgages, auto loans).
  • Strategic Credit Management: Actions like setting up autopay, keeping utilization low, avoiding closing old accounts, and being mindful of new applications are critical for a healthy FICO score.

Important Terms

  • FICO Score: A numerical representation of your creditworthiness, calculated by Fair Isaac Corporation, used by lenders to assess risk.
  • Payment History: Records of whether you've made payments on your debts on time.
  • Late Payment: A payment made 30, 60, 90, or more days past its due date, which severely damages your credit score.
  • Amounts Owed / Credit Utilization: The amount of money you currently owe on your credit accounts compared to your total available credit.
  • Credit Utilization Ratio: A percentage calculated by dividing your total current balances by your total credit limits.
  • Length of Credit History: The average age of all your credit accounts, from the oldest to the newest.
  • New Credit / Hard Inquiries: The number of recent applications for new credit, which result in a "hard inquiry" on your credit report.
  • Hard Inquiry (Hard Pull): Occurs when a lender checks your credit report as part of a credit application, temporarily lowering your score by a small amount.
  • Soft Inquiry: Occurs when you check your own credit, an employer conducts a background check, or a company pre-screens you for an offer. Soft inquiries do not affect your credit score.
  • Credit Mix: The variety of credit accounts you have, including revolving credit and installment loans.
  • Revolving Credit: Credit that can be used repeatedly up to a certain limit, with balances changing month to month (e.g., credit cards, lines of credit).
  • Installment Loans: Loans with a fixed payment amount over a set period (e.g., auto loans, student loans, mortgages).

Detailed Notes

The Five Factors of Your FICO Score

Your FICO credit score is a carefully calculated number, not a random one. It is determined by five specific factors, each carrying a different weight based on its predictive power regarding your ability to repay debts. Understanding these factors is key to managing and improving your credit score.

Factor 1: Payment History (35%)

This is the single most important factor, accounting for more than a third of your total score. It directly answers whether you pay your bills on time.

  • On-Time Payments: Consistent on-time payments are crucial for building and maintaining a good score over time.
  • Late Payments: Even a single late payment (30 days or more past due) can significantly harm your score, potentially dropping it by 50–100 points.
  • Severity of Lateness: The longer a payment is late (e.g., 60 days, 90 days), the more severe the damage to your score.
  • Reporting Period: Late payments remain on your credit report for seven years, impacting your score for that duration.
  • Protection Strategy: Using autopay for at least the minimum payment on all accounts is highly recommended to prevent accidental missed due dates and safeguard your score.

Factor 2: Amounts Owed / Credit Utilization (30%)

This factor, the second most influential, measures your credit utilization ratio—how much of your available credit you are currently using.

  • Credit Utilization Ratio Formula: [ \text{Credit Utilization} = \frac{\text{Total Balances}}{\text{Total Credit Limits}} \times 100% ]
  • Example Calculation:
    • If you have a credit card with a $1,000 limit and a $400 balance:
      • Utilization = ($400 / $1,000) * 100% = 40%.
    • This is above the recommended threshold. To get below 30%, the balance would need to be under $300.
    • If your balance were $250:
      • Utilization = ($250 / $1,000) * 100% = 25%, which is a good range.
  • Recommended Ranges:
    • Under 30%: Generally recommended for a good score.
    • Under 10%: Even better for achieving the highest scores.
    • Over 30%: Begins to negatively impact your score.
    • Over 50%: Can significantly damage your score.
    • 0% (no balance): Excellent, but it's important to have at least some open accounts to demonstrate active credit management.
  • Warning: Do Not Close Old Credit Cards: Closing an old credit card can hurt your score by reducing your total available credit, which increases your utilization ratio even if your balances stay the same. It also shortens your average credit history. Keeping no-annual-fee cards open, even if unused, is usually beneficial.

Factor 3: Length of Credit History (15%)

This factor assesses how long your credit accounts have been open, signaling to lenders your track record in managing credit.

  • Key Metrics: The scoring model considers:
    • The age of your oldest active account.
    • The age of your newest active account.
    • The average age of all your accounts.
  • Impact on Young Individuals: People new to credit often have lower scores because they simply haven't had credit accounts open for very long. Time and consistent credit management are the only remedies for a short credit history.

Factor 4: New Credit / Hard Inquiries (10%)

This factor examines how often you apply for new credit.

  • Hard Inquiry: When you apply for new credit (like a credit card or loan), the lender performs a "hard inquiry" (or "hard pull") on your credit report.
    • Score Impact: A hard inquiry typically lowers your score by a small amount (5–10 points) temporarily.
    • Multiple Applications: Multiple hard inquiries in a short period can suggest financial desperation to lenders, potentially impacting your score more negatively.
    • Reporting Period: Hard inquiries remain on your credit report for two years but generally only affect your score for about one year.
  • Soft Inquiry: This occurs when you check your own credit, an employer performs a background check, or a credit card company pre-screens you for an offer. Soft inquiries do not affect your credit score.
  • Rate-Shopping Exception: When shopping for specific loans like auto loans or mortgages, multiple hard inquiries within a short window (typically 14–45 days) are often counted as a single inquiry. Credit bureaus recognize that consumers are comparing rates, not applying for excessive credit.

Factor 5: Credit Mix (10%)

Lenders prefer to see that you can responsibly manage different types of credit.

  • Types of Credit:
    • Revolving Credit: Accounts where the balance changes month to month, and you can borrow up to a limit, repay, and borrow again (e.g., credit cards, lines of credit).
    • Installment Loans: Loans with a fixed payment amount over a set period until the loan is paid off (e.g., auto loans, student loans, personal loans, mortgages).
  • Demonstrating Creditworthiness: While having only one type of credit isn't detrimental, managing both revolving and installment credit types demonstrates broader financial responsibility and can slightly boost your score.
  • Important Caveat: Never take out a loan solely to improve your credit mix. Only borrow money you genuinely need for a legitimate purpose.

Review Questions

  1. List the five factors of your FICO score from most to least important (by percentage).
  2. You have a credit card with a $2,000 limit and a $700 balance. What is your credit utilization ratio? Is it within the recommended range?
  3. Explain why closing an old credit card with a zero balance might actually harm your credit score.
  4. Distinguish between a "hard inquiry" and a "soft inquiry," and explain their respective impacts on your FICO score.
  5. Why is "Payment History" considered the single most important factor, and what are the immediate and long-term consequences of a single 30-day late payment?
