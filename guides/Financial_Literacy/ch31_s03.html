Chapter 31, Section 3: Tithing and Faith-Based Giving

Overview

Across many of the world's major religious and cultural traditions, giving a portion of one's income or resources to others is considered a spiritual practice or moral obligation. These traditions predate modern tax law by thousands of years and represent one of the most universal frameworks through which people make giving decisions.

Giving Traditions Across Faiths

Christianity — The Tithe

  • Tithe comes from the Old English word for "tenth"
  • Traditionally refers to giving 10% of income to the local church or religious community
  • Denominations vary in interpretation:
    • Some define it as 10% of gross income
    • Some as 10% of net income
    • Some treat it as a voluntary guideline rather than a strict rule

Judaism — Tzedakah

  • Tzedakah (צדקה) comes from the Hebrew root for "righteousness" or "justice"
  • Framed as a moral obligation, not an optional virtue
  • Traditional Jewish law suggests giving 10–20% of income to those in need
  • 10% is the minimum; 20% is the traditional upper ceiling

Islam — Zakat

  • Zakat is one of the Five Pillars of Islam
  • Applies to Muslims who meet a minimum wealth threshold called nisab
  • Rate: 2.5% of accumulated net assets
  • Calculated on total wealth held for a lunar year — not just annual income
  • Unlike income-based tithing, zakat is a wealth-based obligation

Buddhism and Other Traditions

  • Dana is the Buddhist concept of generosity as a spiritual path
  • Many indigenous and other traditions practice redistribution and communal care
  • Specific forms vary widely, but underlying values of generosity and care for others appear across all human cultures

The Financial Planning Benefit of Percentage-Based Giving

Whatever the motivation, percentage-based giving has a practical advantage: it scales automatically with income.

  • When income grows → giving grows proportionally
  • When income drops → giving decreases automatically
  • No recalculation or renegotiation needed — the percentage does the work

Scaling Example (10% Giving Rate)

Annual Income Annual Gift (10%) Monthly Gift
$20,000 $2,000 $167
$40,000 $4,000 $333
$60,000 $6,000 $500
$80,000 $8,000 $667
$100,000 $10,000 $833

Both a $20,000 earner and a $100,000 earner give the same fraction of their resources, even though the dollar amounts differ dramatically.

This Is a Personal Decision

  • The specific percentage, recipients, and motivation are entirely personal
  • Valid choices include:
    • 10% out of religious conviction
    • 1% because it is what you can manage right now
    • Volunteering time instead of money
    • Focusing on local vs. global causes
  • This section is presented as factual information about existing traditions — not as a recommendation for any particular practice

Did You Know: Etymology of Philanthropy

  • Philanthropy comes from ancient Greek:
    • philos = loving
    • anthropos = human
  • Literally: "love of humanity"
  • The practice of organized human generosity is as old as recorded history

Key Terms

  • Tithe — from Old English for "tenth"; giving 10% of income, associated with Christian tradition
  • Tzedakah — Hebrew charitable giving as a moral obligation rooted in justice/righteousness (10–20% of income)
  • Zakat — one of the Five Pillars of Islam; 2.5% of qualifying net assets above the nisab threshold
  • Nisab — the minimum wealth threshold that triggers the zakat obligation
  • Dana — the Buddhist concept of generosity as a spiritual path
  • Philanthropy — from Greek for "love of humanity"; organized giving for the public good
  • Percentage-based giving — committing a fixed percentage of income or assets to giving, regardless of dollar amount

Key Takeaways

  • Formal giving frameworks exist across every major world religion and many cultural traditions
  • The key difference in Islam's zakat: it is calculated on total accumulated wealth, not annual income
  • Tzedakah in Judaism is framed as justice, not charity — a duty rooted in righteousness
  • Percentage-based giving automatically adjusts as income changes — a practical financial planning benefit
  • No single percentage or tradition is prescribed; giving decisions are deeply personal
  • The universal theme across traditions is generosity and care for others
