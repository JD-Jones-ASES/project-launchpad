Business Applications: Borrowing for Business and Consumers Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the practical application of loan formulas to various real-world borrowing scenarios faced by both consumers and businesses. It details how to calculate and compare the true costs of auto loans, mortgages, credit card finance charges, and business equipment financing, emphasizing the importance of understanding total interest paid over the life of a loan.

Key Concepts

  • True Cost of Borrowing: Beyond just the monthly payment, it's crucial to understand the total amount of interest paid over the life of a loan.
  • Loan Comparison: Different loan options, varying in term length and interest rate, can have significantly different total interest costs, even if the principal is the same. Longer terms often lead to higher total interest, despite lower monthly payments.
  • Average Daily Balance Method: This is a common method used by credit card companies to calculate finance charges, based on the average balance maintained over a billing cycle.
  • Amortization Table: A detailed schedule that breaks down each loan payment into its principal and interest components, showing how the loan balance decreases over time. This is particularly useful for businesses for financial tracking and tax purposes.
  • Truth in Lending Act (TILA): A federal law designed to protect consumers in credit transactions by requiring clear disclosure of key loan terms, such as APR, total interest, and total cost.
  • Tax Implications for Businesses: Businesses can often deduct the interest paid on loans as a business expense, which can reduce their taxable income.

Important Terms

  • Principal (P): The initial amount of money borrowed.
  • Monthly Interest Rate (r): The annual interest rate divided by 12 (for monthly payments).
  • Number of Payments (n): The total number of monthly payments over the loan's term.
  • Monthly Payment (M): The fixed amount paid each month to repay a loan.
  • Total Paid: The sum of all monthly payments made over the loan's term ($M \times n$).
  • Total Interest: The difference between the Total Paid and the original Principal ($Total Paid - P$).
  • Annual Percentage Rate (APR): The annual cost of borrowing, expressed as a percentage, which includes the interest rate and other charges.
  • Down Payment: An initial upfront payment made when purchasing an item, reducing the amount that needs to be financed.
  • Amortization: The process of gradually paying off a debt over a period of time through a series of regular payments.
  • Amortization Table: A schedule that shows how each payment is applied to both the principal and interest, and the remaining loan balance.
  • Average Daily Balance: The average of the balances in a credit card account for each day of a billing cycle.
  • Finance Charge: The total cost of credit, including interest and other fees, applied to a credit card balance.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Borrowing

Borrowing is a fundamental part of modern financial life for individuals and companies. Whether it's for a car, a home, or new business equipment, understanding the complete financial implications – especially the total interest cost – is vital. This section demonstrates how to calculate these costs using the loan payment formula ($M = P \frac{r(1+r)^n}{(1+r)^n - 1}$) and other methods for various common scenarios.

Auto Loans: Comparing Options

When taking out an auto loan, borrowers often face choices regarding the loan term and associated interest rates. A shorter loan term typically means a higher monthly payment but a lower total interest paid over the life of the loan. Conversely, a longer loan term reduces the monthly payment but increases the overall interest cost.

Example: Jenna's Auto Loan Decision Jenna needs to finance $20,000 for a used car.

  • Option A: 48 months (4 years) at 5.4% annual interest.
    • Monthly rate ($r$) = 0.054 / 12 = 0.0045
    • Number of payments ($n$) = 48
    • Monthly Payment ($M_A$): Calculated as $464.03.
    • Total Paid: $464.03 \times 48 = $22,273.44.
    • Total Interest: $22,273.44 - $20,000.00 = $2,273.44.
  • Option B: 60 months (5 years) at 6.0% annual interest.
    • Monthly rate ($r$) = 0.06 / 12 = 0.005
    • Number of payments ($n$) = 60
    • Monthly Payment ($M_B$): Calculated as $386.66.
    • Total Paid: $386.66 \times 60 = $23,199.60.
    • Total Interest: $23,199.60 - $20,000.00 = $3,199.60.

Comparison: Option A (shorter term, slightly lower rate) has a higher monthly payment ($464.03 vs. $386.66) but saves $926.16 in total interest ($3,199.60 - $2,273.44) compared to Option B.

Mortgages: 15-Year vs. 30-Year

Mortgages are significant long-term loans. The choice between a 15-year and a 30-year mortgage dramatically impacts monthly payments and total interest paid. Shorter-term mortgages (like 15-year) typically offer lower interest rates and save a substantial amount in total interest, but require higher monthly payments.

Example: The Patel Family's Mortgage The Patels need to finance $224,000.

  • 15-Year Mortgage: 5.8% annual interest.
    • Monthly rate ($r$) = 0.058 / 12 = 0.004833
    • Number of payments ($n$) = 15 years * 12 months/year = 180
    • Monthly Payment ($M$): Calculated as $1,868.38.
    • Total Paid: $1,868.38 \times 180 = $336,308.40.
    • Total Interest: $336,308.40 - $224,000.00 = $112,308.40.
  • 30-Year Mortgage: 6.4% annual interest.
    • Monthly rate ($r$) = 0.064 / 12 = 0.005333
    • Number of payments ($n$) = 30 years * 12 months/year = 360
    • Monthly Payment ($M$): Calculated as $1,399.55.
    • Total Paid: $1,399.55 \times 360 = $503,838.00.
    • Total Interest: $503,838.00 - $224,000.00 = $279,838.00.

Comparison: The 15-year mortgage has a significantly higher monthly payment ($1,868.38 vs. $1,399.55), but saves an astonishing $167,529.60 in total interest ($279,838.00 - $112,308.40) over the loan's life.

Calculator Steps: Mortgage Payment (15-Year) To find $M = P \frac{r(1+r)^n}{(1+r)^n - 1}$:

  1. Calculate $(1+r)^n$: For $r = 0.004833$ and $n = 180$, $1.004833^{180} \approx 2.378414$. Store this value.
  2. Multiply by $r$: $0.004833 \times 2.378414 \approx 0.011497$.
  3. Subtract 1 from the stored value: $2.378414 - 1 \approx 1.378414$.
  4. Divide the result from step 2 by the result from step 3: $0.011497 / 1.378414 \approx 0.008341$.
  5. Multiply by the principal ($P$): $0.008341 \times 224,000 \approx $1,868.38.

Truth in Lending Act (TILA): This federal law ensures lenders provide clear disclosures (like APR, total interest, total cost) so borrowers can easily compare loan offers.

Credit Cards: Average Daily Balance

Credit card finance charges are often calculated using the average daily balance (ADB) method. This involves tracking the balance each day of the billing cycle, summing these daily balances, and then dividing by the number of days in the cycle to get the average.

Example: Marcus's Credit Card Statement Marcus has an APR of 21.99% for a 31-day billing cycle.

  1. Track Daily Balances:
    • Days 1-7 (7 days): $1,450.00
    • Days 8-17 (10 days): $1,450.00 (old balance) + $325.00 (charge) = $1,775.00
    • Days 18-24 (7 days): $1,775.00 (old balance) - $500.00 (payment) = $1,275.00
    • Days 25-31 (7 days): $1,275.00 (old balance) + $75.00 (charge) = $1,350.00
  2. Sum of Daily Balances:
    • $(7 \times 1,450) + (10 \times 1,775) + (7 \times 1,275) + (7 \times 1,350)$
    • $= 10,150 + 17,750 + 8,925 + 9,450 = 46,275$
  3. Average Daily Balance (ADB):
    • $46,275 / 31 \approx $1,492.74
  4. Finance Charge:
    • $ADB \times (APR / 365) \times (\text{number of days in cycle})$
    • $1,492.74 \times (0.2199 / 365) \times 31$
    • $1,492.74 \times 0.00060246 \times 31 \approx $27.88

Quick Practice: If Marcus's balance remained $1,450 for all 31 days, the finance charge would be $1,450 \times (0.2199 / 365) \times 31 \approx $27.08.

Business Equipment Loans and Amortization Tables

Businesses often take out loans for equipment. An amortization table is crucial for tracking these loans, detailing how each payment is split between interest and principal.

Example: Rivera Printing Equipment Loan Rivera Print Solutions finances $45,000 at 7.2% annual interest over 5 years (60 monthly payments).

  1. Find the Monthly Payment (M):
    • $P = $45,000.00$, $r = 0.072 / 12 = 0.006$, $n = 60$.
    • Monthly Payment ($M$): Calculated as $895.00.
  2. Build the Amortization Table (First 3 Payments):
    • Payment 1:
      • Interest = $45,000.00 \times 0.006 = $270.00
      • Principal Paid = $895.00 (payment) - $270.00 (interest) = $625.00
      • New Balance = $45,000.00 - $625.00 = $44,375.00
    • Payment 2:
      • Interest = $44,375.00 \times 0.006 = $266.25
      • Principal Paid = $895.00 - $266.25 = $628.75
      • New Balance = $44,375.00 - $628.75 = $43,746.25
    • Payment 3:
      • Interest = $43,746.25 \times 0.006 = $262.48
      • Principal Paid = $895.00 - $262.48 = $632.52
      • New Balance = $43,746.25 - $632.52 = $43,113.73

Summary After 3 Payments:

  • Total Paid: $895.00 \times 3 = $2,685.00
  • Total Interest Paid: $270.00 + $266.25 + $262.48 = $798.73
  • Total Principal Paid: $625.00 + $628.75 + $632.52 = $1,886.27

Calculator Steps: Amortization Row For each payment, use the current loan balance ($B$) and monthly rate ($r$):

  1. Interest: $B \times r$
  2. Principal: $M - Interest$ (where $M$ is the fixed monthly payment)
  3. New Balance: $B - Principal$ Repeat these steps using the new balance for the next payment.

Tax Implications: Businesses often deduct the interest portion of loan payments as a business expense, which reduces their taxable income. This is why accurately tracking interest and principal payments in an amortization table is crucial for accounting.

Quick Practice: Using Rivera's loan, for Payment 4 (Balance = $43,113.73, $r = 0.006$):

  • Interest = $43,113.73 \times 0.006 = $258.68
  • Principal = $895.00 - $258.68 = $636.32
  • New Balance = $43,113.73 - $636.32 = $42,477.41
