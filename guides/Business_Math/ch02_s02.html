Study Guide: Business Applications - Percents in Business

Overview

This section explores the practical application of percents in various business scenarios. It covers essential calculations like sales tax, tips, and commissions, as well as more complex concepts such as year-over-year growth and successive discounts. Understanding these applications is crucial for both consumers and business professionals.

Key Concepts

  • Sales Tax: A percentage added to the purchase price of goods or services, collected by sellers on behalf of the government, resulting in a total price higher than the tag price.
  • Tipping/Gratuity: A voluntary payment, typically a percentage of the pre-tax bill, given to service staff as an appreciation for their service.
  • Commissions: A form of payment where an individual's earnings are based directly on the amount or value of sales they make, often a percentage of total sales.
  • Year-Over-Year (YOY) Growth: A critical metric used by businesses to compare their performance (e.g., sales, revenue, expenses) for the same period in two consecutive years, indicating growth or decline.
  • Successive Discounts: Multiple discounts applied sequentially to an item, where each subsequent discount is calculated on the already reduced price, not the original price.
  • Finding Original Price: The process of calculating the initial cost of an item before any discounts were applied, given the discounted price and the discount rate.

Important Terms

  • Sales Tax: A percentage of a purchase price added by the store, collected for state or local government.
  • Tip (Gratuity): An additional payment made to service staff, usually a percentage of the pre-tax bill.
  • Commission: Earnings based on the volume or value of sales made, expressed as a percentage of total sales.
  • Commission Rate: The percentage used to calculate the commission earned on sales.
  • Year-Over-Year (YOY) Growth: A measure of change in a financial metric (like revenue) from one year to the next, comparing the same period.
  • Successive Discounts: A series of discounts applied sequentially, with each discount taken from the current reduced price.

Detailed Notes

This section emphasizes the widespread use of percents in business, from everyday consumer transactions to intricate financial analysis. Mastering these applications is essential for understanding common financial calculations and making informed decisions.

I. Calculating Sales Tax

Sales tax is a mandatory charge added to the price of most goods and services, collected by sellers on behalf of the government.

  • Definition: A percentage of the purchase price that goes to the state or local government. The price on the tag is typically before tax.
  • Total Cost: The amount you pay is the original price plus the sales tax.
  • Calculation Steps:
    1. Calculate Sales Tax Amount: Tax = Original Price × Tax Rate (as a decimal) Example: For a $7.25%$ tax rate, use 0.0725.
    2. Calculate Total Price: Total = Original Price + Sales Tax Amount
  • One-Step Method for Total Price: You can find the total price including tax by multiplying the original price by (1 + tax rate).
    • Example: If an item costs $$349.99$ with a $7.25%$ tax rate, the total is $349.99 × (1 + 0.0725) = $349.99 × 1.0725 = $375.36 (rounded).

II. Figuring Tips (Gratuity)

Tips are voluntary payments given to service staff, common in industries like dining, to show appreciation for good service.

  • Definition: An additional amount paid by a customer for a service, typically a percentage of the bill. Also known as gratuity.
  • Common Rates: Usually range from $15%$ to $20%$.
  • Basis for Tip: In the U.S., tips are generally calculated on the pre-tax amount of the bill.
  • Total Payment Calculation:
    1. Tip = Pre-tax Bill × Tip Rate (as a decimal)
    2. Sales Tax = Pre-tax Bill × Sales Tax Rate (as a decimal)
    3. Total Amount Paid = Pre-tax Bill + Tip + Sales Tax
  • Cultural Note: Tipping customs vary significantly across countries; in the U.S., servers often depend on tips as a major part of their income.

III. Computing Sales Commissions

Commissions are a form of performance-based pay, directly linked to a salesperson's sales volume.

  • Definition: Pay based on the amount of sales a person makes, expressed as a percent of total sales.
  • Types of Commission Structures:
    • Straight Commission: The salesperson earns only commission, with no fixed base salary. Commission = Total Sales × Commission Rate (as a decimal) Example: $3%$ commission on $$285,000$ sales = $285,000 × 0.03 = $8,550.
    • Base Salary Plus Commission: The salesperson earns a fixed base salary in addition to commission on their sales.
      1. Commission = Total Sales × Commission Rate (as a decimal)
      2. Total Pay = Base Salary + Commission Example: Base salary of $$1,800$ + $4.5%$ commission on $$38,400$ sales = ($38,400 × 0.045) + $1,800 = $1,728 + $1,800 = $3,528.

IV. Year-Over-Year (YOY) Growth

YOY growth is a vital business metric for tracking performance trends over time and assessing business health.

  • Definition: Compares a business metric (like sales, revenue, or expenses) for the same period in two consecutive years. It measures whether the business is growing or shrinking.
  • Calculation (Percent Change): Percent Change = (Current Year Value - Last Year Value) / Last Year Value × 100%
    • A positive percent change indicates growth (increase).
    • A negative percent change indicates shrinkage (decrease).
    • Example: Revenue of $$186,000$ last year, $$213,900$ this year: ($213,900 - $186,000) / $186,000 × 100% = $27,900 / $186,000 × 100% = 0.15 × 100% = 15% increase.

V. Successive Discounts

Retailers often use successive discounts (e.g., a sale price followed by an additional discount) to manage inventory or incentivize purchases.

  • Definition: Multiple discounts applied one after another. Crucially, each subsequent discount is applied to the already reduced price, not the original price.
  • Calculation Steps:
    1. Apply the first discount to the original price to find the first discounted price.
    2. Apply the second discount to the first discounted price to find the final price.
  • Important Note: Successive discounts are not additive. A $30%$ discount followed by a $15%$ discount is not equivalent to a single $45%$ discount. The combined discount will always be less than the sum of the individual percentages because the second discount is on a smaller base.
    • To find the final price directly after multiple discounts: Final Price = Original Price × (1 - Discount Rate 1) × (1 - Discount Rate 2) ...
    • Example: Original price $$220$, $30%$ off, then an additional $15%$ off: $220 × (1 - 0.30) × (1 - 0.15) = $220 × 0.70 × 0.85 = $154 × 0.85 = $130.90. This is equivalent to 1 - (0.70 × 0.85) = 1 - 0.595 = 0.405 or $40.5%$ off the original price.

VI. Finding the Original Price Before a Discount

Sometimes you know the discounted price and the discount rate, and you need to determine the item's initial cost.

  • Concept: If an item is $20%$ off, the customer pays $100% - 20% = 80%$ of the original price.
  • Formula: Let B be the original price. Discounted Price = (1 - Discount Rate) × Original Price To solve for Original Price: Original Price = Discounted Price / (1 - Discount Rate) (as a decimal)
  • One-Step Method: Divide the discounted price by (1 - discount rate).
    • Example: A customer paid $$68.00$ after a $20%$ discount. Original Price = $68.00 / (1 - 0.20) = $68.00 / 0.80 = $85.00.

Review Questions

  1. Explain why successive discounts of, for example, $20%$ and $10%$ are not equivalent to a single $30%$ discount. Provide an example to illustrate your explanation.
  2. Describe the difference between straight commission and a base salary plus commission structure. In what types of sales roles might each be more common?
  3. Why do businesses track year-over-year (YOY) growth? What insights can it provide that simply looking at current year sales alone might not?
  4. Marcus paid an $18%$ tip on his pre-tax bill of $$124.60$. If he had decided to tip $18%$ on the total bill after tax ($6.5%$ sales tax), how much more would his tip have been?
  5. A store is trying to clear out winter inventory. A pair of skis was originally priced at $$450.00$. They are first marked down by $25%$. For a special weekend sale, they offer an additional $10%$ off the sale price. What is the final price of the skis before tax? If the sales tax is $8%$, what is the total cost?
