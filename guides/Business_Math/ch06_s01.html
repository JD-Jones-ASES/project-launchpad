Chapter 6, Section 1: The Math: Markup, Markdown, and Break-Even

Overview

This section introduces essential business math concepts used in pricing products to cover costs and earn a profit. It details how to calculate markup, which is the amount added to an item's cost to determine its selling price, and markdown, a reduction from the original selling price. Finally, it explains break-even analysis, a crucial tool for determining the sales volume needed to cover all costs and avoid a loss.

Key Concepts

  • Markup: The amount added to an item's cost to reach its selling price. It can be calculated as a percentage of either the cost or the selling price. It's critical to know which base is being used as the formulas and outcomes differ significantly.
  • Markdown (Discount): A reduction from an item's original selling price, often used for sales, promotions, or to clear inventory. Markdowns can be single percentage reductions or successive, where each subsequent discount is applied to the already reduced price, not the original.
  • Fixed Costs: Business expenses that remain constant regardless of the production or sales volume (e.g., rent, insurance, salaries). These costs must be paid even if no units are sold.
  • Variable Costs: Expenses that change in direct proportion to the number of units produced or sold (e.g., raw materials, packaging per unit, sales commissions).
  • Revenue: The total income generated from selling products, calculated by multiplying the selling price per unit by the quantity sold.
  • Break-Even Point: The critical sales volume (in units) at which a business's total revenue exactly equals its total costs, resulting in neither a profit nor a loss. It signifies the minimum level of sales required to keep the business operational without financial loss.
  • Contribution Margin per Unit: The difference between the selling price per unit and the variable cost per unit. This amount from each sale contributes to covering fixed costs and, once fixed costs are covered, generating profit.

Important Terms

  • Markup: The amount added to the cost of an item to determine its selling price.
  • Markdown (Discount): A reduction from the original selling price.
  • Successive Markdowns: Occur when an item is discounted more than once, with each discount applied to the most recent price, not the original price.
  • Fixed Costs: Expenses that stay the same regardless of how many units are produced or sold (e.g., rent, insurance, salaries).
  • Variable Costs: Expenses that change with the number of units (e.g., materials, packaging, shipping per unit).
  • Revenue: The total income from selling a product.
  • Break-Even Point: The number of units at which total revenue equals total cost — the business is neither making a profit nor taking a loss.

Detailed Notes

The Fundamentals of Pricing and Profit

For any business selling products, setting the right price is paramount. Prices must be high enough to cover all expenses and allow for a profit. This section provides the mathematical tools to achieve this.

Markup

Markup is a core concept in retail pricing. It's the difference between what a business pays for an item and what it sells it for.

  • Basic Markup Equation:
    • Selling Price = Cost + Markup
    • Markup = Selling Price - Cost

Calculating Markup as a Percentage: Markup can be expressed in two primary ways, and understanding the base is critical.

  1. Percent Markup on Cost:

    • This calculates markup as a percentage of the cost of the item.
    • Formula: Percent markup on cost = (Markup / Cost) * 100%
    • To find Selling Price (given Cost and markup rate on cost): Selling Price = Cost * (1 + markup rate)
    • Example: A store buys a jacket for $45.00 and marks it up 60% on cost.
      • Markup Amount = $45.00 * 0.60 = $27.00
      • Selling Price = $45.00 + $27.00 = $72.00
      • (Alternatively: $45.00 * (1 + 0.60) = $45.00 * 1.60 = $72.00)

  2. Percent Markup on Selling Price:

    • This calculates markup as a percentage of the final selling price of the item.
    • Formula: Percent markup on selling price = (Markup / Selling Price) * 100%
    • To find Selling Price (given Cost and markup rate on selling price): Selling Price = Cost / (1 - markup rate)
    • Algebraic Explanation: If S is the selling price and r is the markup rate on selling price, then Markup = r * S. Since Cost = S - Markup, we can substitute to get Cost = S - (r * S) = S * (1 - r). Solving for S gives S = Cost / (1 - r).
    • Example: A retailer wants a 40% markup on selling price. If the cost is $36.00:
      • Selling Price = $36.00 / (1 - 0.40) = $36.00 / 0.60 = $60.00
      • Check: Markup = $60.00 - $36.00 = $24.00. Percent of selling price = $24.00 / $60.00 = 0.40 = 40%.

Finding the Cost from Selling Price: You can also use these formulas to work backward.

  • Example: A pair of shoes sells for $85.00 after a 70% markup on cost. What was the cost?
    • Selling Price = Cost * (1 + markup rate)
    • $85.00 = Cost * (1 + 0.70)
    • Cost = $85.00 / 1.70 = $50.00

Crucial Distinction: "Markup on cost" and "markup on selling price" are not interchangeable and will lead to different selling prices for the same item. Always confirm which base is being used.

Markdown (Discount)

A markdown is a temporary or permanent reduction from an item's original selling price, often used to stimulate sales or clear old inventory.

  • Basic Markdown Equation:
    • Sale Price = Original Price - Markdown Amount

Percent Markdown: Markdowns are typically expressed as a percentage of the original price.

  • Formula: Percent markdown = (Markdown Amount / Original Price) * 100%
  • To find Sale Price (given Original Price and markdown rate): Sale Price = Original Price * (1 - markdown rate)
  • Example: A $120.00 pair of boots is marked down 25%.
    • Sale Price = $120.00 * (1 - 0.25) = $120.00 * 0.75 = $90.00

Successive Markdowns: This occurs when an item receives multiple discounts. Each new discount is applied to the most recent reduced price, not the original price.

  • Formula (for two markdown rates, $d_1$ and $d_2$, as decimals): Final Price = Original Price * (1 - d_1) * (1 - d_2)
  • Important Note: Two successive markdowns are not equivalent to a single markdown equal to the sum of the individual rates. The cumulative discount is generally less than a single discount of the combined percentage.
  • Example: A $200.00 coat is first marked down 20%, then an additional 15% off the reduced price.
    • After first markdown: $200.00 * 0.80 = $160.00
    • After second markdown: $160.00 * 0.85 = $136.00
    • (In one step: $200.00 * 0.80 * 0.85 = $136.00)
    • Comparison: A single 35% markdown would yield $200.00 * (1 - 0.35) = $200.00 * 0.65 = $130.00. This is a larger discount ($6.00 more) than the successive markdowns.

Break-Even Analysis

Break-even analysis is a crucial financial planning tool that helps businesses determine the minimum sales volume required to cover all costs and avoid a loss.

Understanding Business Costs: Costs are generally categorized into two types:

  1. Fixed Costs: Expenses that remain constant regardless of the number of units produced or sold. Examples include rent, insurance premiums, salaries for administrative staff, and property taxes.
  2. Variable Costs: Expenses that change in direct proportion to the number of units produced or sold. Examples include raw materials, direct labor per unit, packaging costs per unit, and sales commissions.
  • Total Cost Formula: Total Cost = Fixed Costs + (Variable Cost per Unit * Quantity)

Revenue: Revenue is the total income generated from sales.

  • Revenue Formula: Revenue = Selling Price per Unit * Quantity

The Break-Even Point: The break-even point is the level of sales (in units) where total revenue exactly equals total costs. At this point, the business is neither making a profit nor taking a loss.

  • To calculate the break-even point, we set Revenue = Total Cost:
    • Price * Quantity = Fixed Costs + (Variable Cost * Quantity)
    • Solving for Quantity (Q):
      • Price * Q - Variable Cost * Q = Fixed Costs
      • Q * (Price - Variable Cost) = Fixed Costs
      • Break-Even Quantity (Q) Formula: Q = Fixed Costs / (Price per Unit - Variable Cost per Unit)
  • The denominator, (Price per Unit - Variable Cost per Unit), is called the contribution margin per unit. It represents the amount each unit sale contributes to covering fixed costs and, eventually, generating profit.
  • Example: A company has fixed costs of $12,000.00 per month. Each unit costs $8.00 to produce and sells for $20.00.
    • Q = $12,000 / ($20.00 - $8.00) = $12,000 / $12.00 = 1,000 units
    • The company must sell 1,000 units per month to break even.

Profit Formula: Once the break-even point is known, you can calculate the profit or loss at any given sales volume.

  • Profit Formula: Profit = Revenue - Total Cost
  • Substituting the cost and revenue formulas:
    • Profit = (Price * Quantity) - [Fixed Costs + (Variable Cost * Quantity)]
    • Simplified Profit Formula: Profit = (Price - Variable Cost) * Quantity - Fixed Costs
  • A positive profit indicates sales exceed the break-even point. A negative profit (loss) indicates sales are below the break-even point.
  • Example (using the company data from above):
    • (a) If 1,500 units are sold:
      • Profit = ($20 - $8) * 1,500 - $12,000 = $12 * 1,500 - $12,000 = $18,000 - $12,000 = $6,000 profit
    • (b) If 800 units are sold:
      • Profit = ($20 - $8) * 800 - $12,000 = $12 * 800 - $12,000 = $9,600 - $12,000 = -$2,400 loss

Review Questions

  1. Explain the difference between "markup on cost" and "markup on selling price." Why is it crucial for a business to know which method they are using? Provide an example where a 30% markup on cost and a 30% markup on selling price yield different selling prices for an item that costs $70.
  2. A department store is having a clearance sale. An item originally priced at $150 is first discounted by 20%, then an additional 10% is taken off the reduced price. What is the final sale price? Would the final price be higher or lower if the store offered a single markdown of 30% instead? Explain why successive markdowns are not the same as a single combined markdown.
  3. Define fixed costs and variable costs, and provide three unique examples of each for a small coffee shop. How do these cost categories relate to the total cost formula and the concept of "contribution margin"?
  4. What is the significance of the "break-even point" for a business? If a company calculates its break-even point to be 5,000 units, what actionable insights can they derive from this information regarding their operations, sales goals, and risk assessment?
  5. A company manufactures custom t-shirts. Their monthly fixed costs are $3,000. Each t-shirt costs $5 in materials and labor (variable cost) and sells for $15.
    • a) Calculate the break-even quantity in units.
    • b) If the company sells 400 t-shirts in a month, what is their profit or loss?
    • c) How many t-shirts do they need to sell to make a target profit of $1,500 in a month?
