Chapter 7, Section 1: The Math: Linear and Exponential Decrease - Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the concept of depreciation, the loss of an asset's value over time, and explores two fundamental mathematical models to calculate it: linear decrease and exponential decrease. Linear decrease involves a constant dollar amount loss each period, while exponential decrease involves a constant percentage loss of the current value. Additionally, the section covers how businesses use IRS-mandated depreciation tables, specifically the Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System (MACRS), for tax purposes.

Key Concepts

  • Depreciation: The process by which an asset's value decreases over time due to wear and tear, obsolescence, or market factors. Understanding depreciation is crucial for business accounting and tax planning.
  • Linear Decrease (Straight-Line Depreciation): A depreciation model where an asset loses the same, constant dollar amount of value in each equal time period. When graphed, the value over time forms a straight, downward-sloping line.
  • Exponential Decrease (Declining-Balance Depreciation): A depreciation model where an asset loses a fixed percentage of its current value in each equal time period. This means the dollar amount of depreciation gets smaller over time, even though the percentage rate remains constant.
  • MACRS Depreciation (Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System): A specific depreciation method mandated by the IRS for most business property. It uses predetermined tables of percentages, grouped by property class (e.g., 3-year, 5-year, 7-year), which are multiplied by the original cost to find each year's depreciation.
  • Book Value: The current value of an asset as recorded in a company's accounting records, calculated as the original cost minus the total accumulated depreciation taken to date.

Important Terms

  • Depreciation: The decrease in value of an asset over time.
  • Linear Decrease (Straight-Line Depreciation): A method of depreciation where the asset loses a constant dollar amount each period.
  • Exponential Decrease (Declining-Balance Depreciation): A method of depreciation where the asset loses a fixed percentage of its current value each period.
  • Original (Initial) Value ($V_0$): The cost of the asset when it was new.
  • Salvage Value ($V_{end}$): The estimated value of an asset at the end of its useful life, after all depreciation has been accounted for.
  • Depreciation Amount ($d$): The constant dollar amount an asset loses each period in linear depreciation.
  • Rate of Decrease ($r$): The constant percentage an asset loses each period in exponential depreciation (expressed as a decimal).
  • Decay Factor: In exponential decrease, the term $(1-r)$, which represents the percentage of the asset's value that remains after one period.
  • MACRS (Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System): The specific depreciation method required by the IRS for most business assets for tax purposes.
  • Property Classes: Categories defined by the IRS for MACRS, determining the number of years over which an asset can be depreciated (e.g., 5-year property, 7-year property).
  • Half-Year Convention: An IRS rule used in MACRS that assumes an asset is placed in service at the midpoint of the first year, resulting in an additional year of depreciation (e.g., a "5-year" property is depreciated over 6 calendar years).

Detailed Notes

Understanding Depreciation

When a business buys an asset (like a truck or computer), its value decreases over time. This loss of value is called depreciation. Calculating depreciation is essential for business accounting, financial reporting, and tax purposes. This section introduces two mathematical models for calculating depreciation and a common tax-based method.

Linear Decrease (Straight-Line Depreciation)

This model assumes an asset loses the same fixed dollar amount of value during each equal time period.

  • Definition: A quantity decreases linearly when it loses the same dollar amount in every equal time period.

  • Visual Representation: If you plot the value of the asset over time, you get a straight line that slopes downward.

  • Formula: $V(t) = V_0 - d t$

    • $V(t)$ = value after $t$ time periods
    • $V_0$ = original (initial) value
    • $d$ = amount of decrease per period (the depreciation amount)
    • $t$ = number of time periods

  • The value 'd': Represents the annual depreciation, which is the constant dollar amount lost each year. It is the negative slope of the line when value is plotted against time.

  • Example: Calculating Value Over Time

    • A machine costs $$20,000$ and loses $$2,500$ per year.
    • $V(1) = 20,000 - 2,500(1) = $17,500$
    • $V(2) = 20,000 - 2,500(2) = $15,000$
    • Notice the value drops by exactly $$2,500$ each year.

  • Finding the Annual Depreciation ($d$) If you know the original cost, the salvage value, and the total number of periods: $d = \frac{V_0 - V_{end}}{n}$

    • $V_0$ = original cost
    • $V_{end}$ = value at the end of its useful life (salvage value)
    • $n$ = number of time periods (e.g., years)

  • Example: Calculating Annual Depreciation

    • Equipment costs $$36,000$ and will be worth $$6,000$ after 10 years.
    • $d = \frac{36,000 - 6,000}{10} = \frac{30,000}{10} = $3,000$ per year.

Exponential Decrease (Declining-Balance Depreciation)

This model assumes an asset loses a fixed percentage of its current value during each equal time period.

  • Definition: A quantity decreases exponentially when it loses a fixed percent of its current value each period.

  • Key Characteristic: The dollar amount of the decrease gets smaller over time, even though the percent loss remains constant.

  • Formula: $V(t) = V_0 (1 - r)^t$

    • $V(t)$ = value after $t$ time periods
    • $V_0$ = original (initial) value
    • $r$ = rate of decrease per period (as a decimal)
    • $t$ = number of time periods

  • Decay Factor: The expression $(1 - r)$ is known as the decay factor. It represents the proportion of value remaining after one period.

  • Example: Calculating Value Over Time

    • A car costs $$30,000$ and loses 20% of its value each year ($r = 0.20$). The decay factor is $(1 - 0.20) = 0.80$.
    • $V(1) = 30,000(0.80)^1 = $24,000$ (Loss of $$6,000$)
    • $V(2) = 30,000(0.80)^2 = $19,200$ (Loss of $$4,800$ from previous year)
    • Notice the dollar amount of depreciation decreases each year.

Comparing Linear and Exponential Decrease

It's crucial to distinguish between these two patterns:

  • Linear Decrease: "Loses $$X$ per year" means a constant dollar amount is subtracted annually.
  • Exponential Decrease: "Loses Y% per year" means a constant percentage is applied to the current value, leading to a changing dollar amount of depreciation.

When comparing, an asset depreciating by a constant percent will generally retain more value over time than an asset depreciating by a constant dollar amount if the initial depreciation amounts are similar, because the dollar drops in exponential decrease diminish over time.

Reading Depreciation Tables (MACRS)

For tax purposes, many businesses use the Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System (MACRS), a depreciation method mandated by the IRS.

  • Purpose: MACRS provides a structured way to depreciate assets for tax deductions.

  • How it Works: The IRS categorizes assets into property classes (e.g., 3-year, 5-year, 7-year) and provides tables with specific percentages for each year of depreciation.

  • Half-Year Convention: A key feature of MACRS is that the IRS assumes assets are placed in service at the midpoint of the first year. This means a "5-year" property will actually have 6 years of depreciation (e.g., half a year in the first calendar year, full years for the next four, and another half a year in the sixth calendar year).

  • Using a MACRS Table:

    • Depreciation for any year: $Depreciation\ in\ year\ k = Original\ Cost \times MACRS\ percentage\ for\ year\ k$
    • Book Value: The remaining value at the end of any year is the original cost minus all depreciation taken so far.

  • Important Caution: Unlike the declining-balance (exponential) method, MACRS percentages are always multiplied by the original cost of the asset, not its current book value.

  • Example: Using a MACRS Table

    • A computer system costs $$8,000$ and is 5-year MACRS property.
    • From the 5-year MACRS table:
      • Year 1 percentage: 20.00%
      • Year 2 percentage: 32.00%
    • Year 1 depreciation: $8,000 \times 0.2000 = $1,600$
    • Year 2 depreciation: $8,000 \times 0.3200 = $2,560$
    • Total depreciation through year 2: $1,600 + 2,560 = $4,160$
    • Book value at end of year 2: $8,000 - 4,160 = $3,840$

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental difference between linear decrease and exponential decrease in terms of how the dollar amount of depreciation changes each period. Provide an example of a real-world asset that might be best modeled by each method.
  2. A company purchases office furniture for $$15,000$. It is expected to be useful for 5 years and have a salvage value of $$2,500$. Calculate the annual depreciation using the linear decrease method and determine the furniture's value at the end of 3 years.
  3. An IT server system costs $$25,000$ and loses 30% of its value each year. Calculate its value after 1 year, 2 years, and 3 years. How much dollar value did the server lose in its first year compared to its third year?
  4. Describe the primary purpose of MACRS depreciation tables for businesses. How does the calculation of annual depreciation using a MACRS table differ from the exponential decrease formula, especially regarding the base value used for multiplication?
  5. A piece of specialized manufacturing equipment costs $$40,000$ and is classified as 7-year MACRS property. Using the MACRS table provided in the textbook (Year 1: 14.29%, Year 2: 24.49%, Year 3: 17.49%), calculate:
    • The depreciation amount for Year 1.
    • The depreciation amount for Year 3.
    • The total accumulated depreciation at the end of Year 3.
    • The book value of the equipment at the end of Year 3.
