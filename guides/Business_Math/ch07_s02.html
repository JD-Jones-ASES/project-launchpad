Business Applications: Depreciating Business Assets Study Guide

Overview

This section explains the critical role of depreciation in business accounting and tax reporting, as assets naturally lose value over time. It introduces four common methods for calculating this loss: straight-line, declining-balance, units-of-production, and MACRS, each offering different advantages depending on the asset and financial strategy.

Key Concepts

  • Depreciation: The accounting process of allocating the cost of a tangible asset over its useful life. It reflects the asset's loss of value due to wear and tear, obsolescence, or age.
  • Tax Deduction: Depreciation reduces a business's taxable income, thereby lowering the amount of income tax owed.
  • Salvage Value: The estimated resale or trade-in value of an asset at the end of its useful life.
  • Useful Life: The estimated period over which an asset is expected to be productive for a business.
  • Depreciable Amount (or Depreciable Base): The portion of an asset's cost that can be depreciated. Calculated as: Cost - Salvage Value.
  • Book Value: The original cost of an asset minus its accumulated depreciation to date.
  • Straight-Line Depreciation: A method that spreads the depreciable amount evenly over the asset's useful life, resulting in the same depreciation expense each year. Simple and common for financial reporting.
  • Declining-Balance Depreciation (Accelerated Depreciation): A method that results in larger depreciation deductions in the early years of an asset's life and smaller deductions in later years. The double declining-balance (DDB) method applies a rate (double the straight-line rate) to the asset's current book value.
  • Units-of-Production Depreciation: A method that ties depreciation to the actual usage or output of an asset. Years with higher usage incur more depreciation.
  • MACRS (Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System): The depreciation method mandated by the IRS for federal income tax purposes in the United States. It's an accelerated method that assigns assets to specific recovery periods and uses predetermined percentages applied to the original cost, without considering salvage value. It generally allows for faster deductions to reduce taxes sooner.

Important Terms

  • Asset: An item of economic value owned by a business, expected to provide future benefit (e.g., vehicles, equipment, computers, furniture).
  • Depreciation: The systematic reduction in the recorded cost of a fixed asset over its useful life.
  • Salvage Value: The estimated residual value of an asset at the end of its useful life, after all depreciation has been expensed.
  • Useful Life: The estimated period (in years, units of production, or other measures) an asset is expected to be used by a business.
  • Depreciable Amount (Depreciable Base): The total amount of an asset's cost that will be expensed over its useful life (Cost - Salvage Value).
  • Book Value: The value of an asset as recorded in a company's accounting records, calculated as its original cost minus accumulated depreciation.
  • Straight-Line Rate: The percentage of an asset's depreciable amount that is expensed each year under the straight-line method (1 / Useful Life).
  • DDB Rate (Double Declining-Balance Rate): Twice the straight-line rate, used in the double declining-balance depreciation method.
  • Accelerated Depreciation: Any depreciation method that records more depreciation expense in the early years of an asset's life than in its later years.

Detailed Notes

I. Introduction to Business Depreciation

  • Businesses own assets that lose value over time.
  • Tracking this loss of value (depreciation) is crucial for:
    • Accounting: To accurately reflect the asset's value on financial statements.
    • Taxes: Depreciation is a tax deduction, reducing taxable income and the income tax a business owes.
  • Four primary depreciation methods are used:
    1. Straight-Line
    2. Declining-Balance
    3. Units-of-Production
    4. MACRS (Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System)

II. Straight-Line Depreciation

  • Concept: Distributes the cost of an asset evenly over its useful life. Simplest and most common for financial reporting.
  • Calculation Steps:
    1. Find the Depreciable Amount: Depreciable Amount = Cost - Salvage Value
    2. Calculate Annual Depreciation: Annual Depreciation = Depreciable Amount / Useful Life (in years)
  • Characteristics:
    • The same amount of depreciation is recognized each year.
    • Book value decreases by a constant amount annually until it reaches the salvage value.
  • Example (Rivera Floral Supply Van):
    • Cost: $28,000, Salvage Value: $3,000, Useful Life: 5 years
    • Depreciable Amount = $28,000 - $3,000 = $25,000
    • Annual Depreciation = $25,000 / 5 years = $5,000 per year
    • Depreciation Schedule: Book value drops by $5,000 each year from $28,000 to $3,000.

III. Declining-Balance Depreciation (Double Declining-Balance)

  • Concept: An accelerated depreciation method that records larger depreciation expenses in the early years of an asset's life. This allows businesses to take larger tax deductions sooner.
  • Calculation Steps (Double Declining-Balance - DDB):
    1. Find the DDB Rate: DDB Rate = 2 * (1 / Useful Life) (This is double the straight-line rate).
    2. Calculate Annual Depreciation: Depreciation = DDB Rate * Current Book Value
      • Important: Unlike straight-line, the DDB rate is applied to the declining book value each year, not the original depreciable amount.
  • Characteristics:
    • Depreciation expense decreases each year because the rate is applied to a continually smaller book value.
    • Does not typically consider salvage value in the initial calculation, but an asset cannot be depreciated below its salvage value. (The example in the text stated "assume no salvage value floor for now.")
  • Example (Greenfield Consulting Office Equipment):
    • Cost: $40,000, Useful Life: 10 years
    • Straight-Line Rate = 1/10 = 10%
    • DDB Rate = 2 * 10% = 20%
    • Year 1: $40,000 * 0.20 = $8,000 (Book Value: $32,000)
    • Year 2: $32,000 * 0.20 = $6,400 (Book Value: $25,600)
    • Year 3: $25,600 * 0.20 = $5,120 (Book Value: $20,480)
    • Year 4: $20,480 * 0.20 = $4,096 (Book Value: $16,384)

IV. Units-of-Production Depreciation

  • Concept: Depreciation expense is directly linked to the actual usage or output of an asset, making it suitable for assets whose wear and tear depend heavily on activity.
  • Calculation Steps:
    1. Find the Depreciation Rate Per Unit: Rate per Unit = (Cost - Salvage Value) / Total Estimated Units of Production
    2. Calculate Annual Depreciation: Annual Depreciation = Rate per Unit * Actual Units Produced in the Year
  • Characteristics:
    • Depreciation expense varies from year to year based on usage.
    • Matches the expense to the revenue-generating activity.
  • Examples of Use: Manufacturing equipment (copies produced), delivery trucks (miles driven), aircraft (flight hours).
  • Example (Apex Printing Press):
    • Cost: $120,000, Salvage Value: $10,000, Total Estimated Units: 5,500,000 copies
    • Depreciation Rate Per Unit = ($120,000 - $10,000) / 5,500,000 = $110,000 / 5,500,000 = $0.02 per copy
    • Year 1 Production: 825,000 copies
      • Depreciation Year 1 = 825,000 * $0.02 = $16,500
    • Year 2 Production: 1,100,000 copies
      • Depreciation Year 2 = 1,100,000 * $0.02 = $22,000

V. MACRS (Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System)

  • Concept: The mandated method for federal income tax purposes in the U.S. It's an accelerated method designed to allow businesses to recover asset costs faster for tax benefits.
  • Calculation Steps:
    1. Determine Asset Class/Recovery Period: IRS specifies useful lives for different types of assets (e.g., 3-year, 5-year, 7-year property).
    2. Use MACRS Percentage Tables: IRS tables provide fixed depreciation percentages for each year based on the asset's recovery period.
    3. Calculate Annual Depreciation: Annual Depreciation = Original Cost * MACRS Percentage for the Year
      • Important: Always multiply by the original cost, not the book value.
  • Characteristics:
    • No Salvage Value: MACRS assumes zero salvage value; assets are fully depreciated to $0.00.
    • Accelerated: Typically front-loads depreciation, providing larger tax deductions in early years.
    • Recovery Period: The number of years over which an asset's cost is recovered for tax purposes. Often, it's one year longer than the class life to account for the half-year convention.
    • Tax Benefit: Larger early deductions improve a business's cash flow by reducing current tax obligations.
  • Example (Summit Delivery Services Truck):
    • Cost: $45,000, 5-year MACRS property
    • Using IRS 5-year MACRS percentages (from a table):
      • Year 1 (20%): $45,000 * 0.20 = $9,000
      • Year 2 (32%): $45,000 * 0.32 = $14,400
      • Year 3 (19.2%): $45,000 * 0.192 = $8,640
      • Year 4 (11.52%): $45,000 * 0.1152 = $5,184
      • Year 5 (11.52%): $45,000 * 0.1152 = $5,184
      • Year 6 (5.76%): $45,000 * 0.0576 = $2,592
    • Total depreciation over 6 years = $45,000 (full cost).
  • Note: MACRS rates and rules are subject to change by tax law. Always refer to current IRS Publication 946 for the latest information.

Review Questions

  1. Explain why tracking the depreciation of business assets is important for both financial accounting and tax purposes.
  2. Compare and contrast straight-line depreciation with the double declining-balance method. What are the main advantages and disadvantages of each for a business?
  3. A construction company buys a new bulldozer for $150,000. It has an estimated salvage value of $20,000 and is expected to operate for 10,000 hours. If the bulldozer is used for 1,200 hours in its first year, how much depreciation would be recorded using the units-of-production method?
  4. Why does the MACRS depreciation schedule for a "5-year property" actually span 6 calendar years? What is the primary benefit of using MACRS for tax reporting?
  5. Imagine a company is choosing a depreciation method for a new machine. What factors might lead them to choose the units-of-production method over the straight-line method, and vice-versa?
