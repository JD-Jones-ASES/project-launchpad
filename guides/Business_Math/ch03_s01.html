Chapter 3, Section 1: The Math: Ratios, Rates, and Proportions

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental mathematical concepts of ratios, rates, and proportions, which are essential for comparing quantities. It defines each term, explains how to simplify ratios, calculate unit rates, solve proportions using cross-multiplication, and identify direct variation relationships, laying the groundwork for their application in business settings.

Key Concepts

  • Ratios: A comparison of two quantities using division. Ratios can be written as a/b, a:b, or "a to b". For simplification, the quantities must be in the same units, and the ratio is simplified by dividing both parts by their greatest common factor (GCF). The order of quantities in a ratio matters.
  • Rates: A special type of ratio that compares two quantities with different units. Examples include miles per hour or dollars per pound.
  • Unit Rates: A rate where the denominator is 1. To find a unit rate, divide the numerator by the denominator. Unit rates are particularly useful for comparing efficiency or value (e.g., price per ounce).
  • Proportions: An equation that states two ratios are equal (a/b = c/d). Proportions are used to solve for unknown quantities when a relationship between two ratios is known.
  • Cross-Multiplication: A key property for solving proportions. If a/b = c/d, then ad = bc. This allows you to eliminate denominators and solve for an unknown variable.
  • Direct Variation: A relationship between two quantities, x and y, where y is directly proportional to x (represented by y = kx). Here, k is the constant of proportionality. In direct variation, if one quantity doubles, the other also doubles; the ratio y/x remains constant, and its graph is a straight line passing through the origin.

Important Terms

  • Ratio: A comparison of two quantities using division.
  • Rate: A ratio that compares two quantities with different units.
  • Unit Rate: A rate with a denominator of 1.
  • Proportion: An equation stating that two ratios are equal.
  • Direct Variation: A relationship where two quantities x and y are related by an equation of the form y = kx, where k is a constant.
  • Constant of Proportionality (k): The constant value k in a direct variation relationship y = kx, representing the constant ratio y/x.

Detailed Notes

Ratios

A ratio is a way to compare two quantities by division.

  • Notation: Ratios can be written as a fraction (a/b), with a colon (a:b), or using words ("a to b").
  • Units: When simplifying a ratio, both quantities must be in the same units. If they are not, one must be converted to match the other before simplification.
  • Simplifying Ratios: To simplify a ratio, divide both parts by their Greatest Common Factor (GCF), similar to simplifying a fraction.
    • Example: Ratio of 18 girls to 12 boys.
      • Ratio: 18 : 12
      • GCF of 18 and 12 is 6.
      • Simplify: (18 ÷ 6) : (12 ÷ 6) = 3 : 2.
  • Unit Conversion Example: A board is 3 feet long and 8 inches wide. Ratio of length to width.
    • Convert 3 feet to inches: 3 ft * 12 in/ft = 36 in.
    • Ratio: 36 in : 8 in
    • Simplify: 36/8 = 9/2 or 9 : 2.
  • Order Matters: The order in which quantities are presented in a ratio is crucial. 3:2 (girls to boys) is different from 2:3 (boys to girls).

Rates and Unit Rates

  • Rates: A comparison of two quantities with different units.
    • Examples include miles per hour (distance/time), dollars per pound (cost/weight), heartbeats per minute (events/time).
  • Unit Rates: A special type of rate where the denominator is 1. This makes it easy to understand "how much per one unit" of the second quantity.
    • Finding a Unit Rate: Divide the numerator by the denominator.
    • Example: A truck travels 273 miles on 13 gallons. Find the unit rate in miles per gallon.
      • 273 miles / 13 gallons = 21 miles per gallon.
  • Comparing Unit Rates: Unit rates are excellent for comparing different options to find the best value or efficiency.
    • Example: Store A: 16-oz peanut butter for $4.48. Store B: 28-oz peanut butter for $7.28.
      • Store A unit rate: $4.48 / 16 oz = $0.28 per oz.
      • Store B unit rate: $7.28 / 28 oz = $0.26 per oz.
      • Store B offers the better deal because $0.26 is less than $0.28.

Proportions

A proportion is an equation that states two ratios are equal.

  • Notation: a/b = c/d (read as "a is to b as c is to d").
  • Cross-Multiplication Property: This is the fundamental tool for working with proportions. If a/b = c/d, then you can multiply diagonally to get ad = bc.
  • Solving Proportions: Use cross-multiplication to solve for an unknown variable.
    • Example: Solve for x: x/15 = 8/12.
      • Cross-multiply: x * 12 = 15 * 8
      • 12x = 120
      • x = 120 / 12 = 10.
    • Example with Decimals: Solve for n: 4.5/n = 9/24.
      • Cross-multiply: 4.5 * 24 = n * 9
      • 108 = 9n
      • n = 108 / 9 = 12.
  • Checking for Proportions: To determine if two ratios form a proportion, cross-multiply them. If the cross-products are equal, they form a proportion.
    • Example: Do 6/14 and 9/21 form a proportion?
      • Cross-multiply: 6 * 21 = 126 and 14 * 9 = 126.
      • Since 126 = 126, they form a proportion.
  • Setting Up Proportions from Word Problems: Ensure that the units on both sides of the proportion match in their respective positions. If the first ratio is "miles over hours," the second ratio must also be "miles over hours."

Direct Variation

Two quantities x and y are in direct variation if their relationship can be expressed as y = kx, where k is a non-zero constant called the constant of proportionality.

  • Key Characteristics:
    • The equation y = kx shows no constant added or subtracted.
    • The ratio y/x is constant for all pairs of x and y values. This constant value is k.
    • The graph of a direct variation is a straight line that passes through the origin (0,0).
    • If x doubles, y doubles; if x triples, y triples.
  • Identifying Direct Variation from a Table: Calculate the ratio y/x for each pair of values. If this ratio is constant, it's a direct variation.
    • Example:
      x y y/x
      2 7 3.5
      5 17.5 3.5
      8 28 3.5
      10 35 3.5
      Since y/x = 3.5 for all pairs, this is a direct variation with k = 3.5, and the equation is y = 3.5x.
  • Using Direct Variation to Find a Value:
    1. Use a known pair of (x, y) values to find the constant of proportionality k = y/x.
    2. Write the direct variation equation y = kx.
    3. Substitute the new x value into the equation to find the corresponding y.
    • Example: Cost C of gasoline varies directly with gallons g. 8 gallons cost $27.60. How much do 15 gallons cost?
      • Find k: k = C/g = $27.60 / 8 gallons = $3.45 per gallon.
      • Equation: C = 3.45g.
      • For 15 gallons: C = 3.45 * 15 = $51.75.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the difference between a ratio and a rate. Provide an example for each where the distinction is clear.
  2. Why is it important for quantities in a ratio to have the same units before simplification, but not for quantities in a rate?
  3. Describe the steps involved in solving a proportion for an unknown variable. What property makes this process possible?
  4. A grocery store offers two deals for apples: Deal A is 3 pounds for $5.25, and Deal B is 5 pounds for $8.50. Use unit rates to determine which deal offers a better price per pound.
  5. What are the three main ways to identify if a relationship between two variables, x and y, is a direct variation? If y varies directly with x, and y=15 when x=3, what would y be when x=7?
