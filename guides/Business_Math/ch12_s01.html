Chapter 12, Section 1: The Math: Exponential Growth and Annuities Study Guide

Overview

This section expands on the concept of compound interest by introducing annuities and present value, which are crucial for effective financial planning. It explores how money grows over time with regular contributions, how to estimate doubling times, and how to determine the current worth of future payments or income streams. Understanding these mathematical foundations is essential for individuals and businesses managing their finances.

Key Concepts

  • Exponential Growth (Compound Interest): The process where an initial amount of money grows over time by earning interest on both the principal and previously accumulated interest. This leads to an accelerating growth rate.
  • Rule of 72: A quick and easy formula to estimate the number of years required for an investment to double in value, given a fixed annual interest rate.
  • Annuities: A sequence of equal payments made or received at regular intervals over a specified period, often used for savings plans or loan repayments.
  • Future Value of an Annuity (FV): The total accumulated amount of money at a future date, resulting from a series of regular, equal payments (an annuity) that earn compound interest.
  • Present Value (PV): The current worth of a future sum of money or stream of cash flows, discounted back to the present using a specific interest rate. It answers how much you need today to achieve a future financial goal.
  • Sinking Fund: A dedicated fund created by making regular deposits over time to accumulate a specific amount of money by a future date, typically for a large expense or debt.

Important Terms

  • Exponential Growth: An increase in a quantity over time, where the rate of increase is proportional to the current amount, leading to faster growth as the quantity gets larger.
  • Growth Factor: In compound growth, the multiplier (1 + r) that indicates how much the principal grows by each period, where r is the growth rate as a decimal.
  • Rule of 72: An approximation method to find the doubling time of an investment by dividing 72 by the annual interest rate (expressed as a whole number percentage).
  • Annuity: A series of fixed payments or receipts occurring at regular intervals for a defined period.
  • Ordinary Annuity: An annuity where payments are made at the end of each period. This is the default assumption unless stated otherwise.
  • Annuity Due: An annuity where payments are made at the beginning of each period. These typically accumulate slightly more interest than ordinary annuities.
  • Future Value (FV): The value of an investment or an annuity at a specific point in the future, considering the effects of compound interest.
  • Present Value (PV): The current worth of a future sum of money or a series of future payments, discounted at a specific rate of return.
  • Time Value of Money: The economic principle that a dollar today is worth more than a dollar in the future because a dollar today can be invested and earn interest.
  • Sinking Fund: A financial mechanism where regular contributions are made into a fund to accumulate a target amount of money by a specific future date, often for planned expenditures or debt repayment.

Detailed Notes

The Math: Exponential Growth and Annuities

This section delves into fundamental financial math concepts that build upon basic compound interest. It explores how regular contributions (annuities) grow over time and how to determine the current worth of future amounts (present value), both critical for effective financial planning.

Exponential Growth Review

Exponential growth describes situations where an amount increases by a constant percentage over time, such as money earning compound interest.

  • Compound Growth Formula (Future Value of a Single Lump Sum): $A = P(1 + r)^t$
    • A = future value (the amount after t periods)
    • P = present value (the initial starting amount or principal)
    • r = growth rate per period (expressed as a decimal)
    • t = number of periods (e.g., years, months)
  • The term (1 + r) is known as the growth factor. If r is 5% (0.05), the growth factor is 1.05, meaning the amount increases by 5% each period.

Example: Compound Growth If a business revenue is $200,000 this year and grows by 4% annually for 6 years: $A = 200,000(1.04)^6 = 200,000 \times 1.265319 = $253,063.82$

The Rule of 72

The Rule of 72 is a simple estimation tool for calculating how long it takes for an investment to double in value.

  • Rule of 72 Formula: $Doubling\ time = \frac{72}{rate\ (as\ a\ percent)}$
  • Example: Using the Rule of 72 To double $5,000 at 8% annual interest: $Doubling\ time = \frac{72}{8} = 9\ years$ (Checking this with the compound growth formula: $5,000 (1.08)^9 \approx $9,995.03$, very close to $10,000.)

Quick Practice: At 3% interest, it takes $72 / 3 = 24$ years to double your money.

Future Value of an Annuity

While compound interest deals with a single lump sum, annuities involve a series of regular, equal deposits.

  • Annuity: A series of equal payments made at equal time intervals.

    • Ordinary Annuity: Payments are made at the end of each period. (This is the default.)
    • Annuity Due: Payments are made at the beginning of each period.

  • Future Value of an Ordinary Annuity Formula: $FV = PMT \left[ \frac{(1 + r)^n - 1}{r} \right]$

    • FV = future value (total accumulated amount)
    • PMT = payment amount per period
    • r = interest rate per period (as a decimal)
    • n = total number of payments (periods)

Example: Future Value of Monthly Deposits Alicia deposits $300 at the end of each month into an account earning 6% annual interest compounded monthly for 10 years.

  • PMT = $300

  • r = $0.06 / 12 = 0.005$

  • n = $10 \text{ years} \times 12 \text{ months/year} = 120$ $FV = 300 \left[ \frac{(1.005)^{120} - 1}{0.005} \right] = 300 \left[ \frac{1.819397 - 1}{0.005} \right] = 300 \left[ \frac{0.819397}{0.005} \right] = 300 \times 163.879 = $49,163.79$ Alicia deposited $300 \times 120 = $36,000$, earning an additional $13,163.79 in interest.

  • Future Value of an Annuity Due Formula: If payments are made at the beginning of each period, each payment earns one extra period of interest. $FV_{due} = FV_{ordinary} \times (1 + r)$

Example: Annuity Due vs. Ordinary Annuity Using Alicia's scenario, if she deposited at the beginning of each month: $FV_{due} = $49,163.79 \times (1.005) = $49,409.61$ The annuity due yields $245.82 more.

Quick Practice: Deposit $500/month (end of month) at 4.8% annual interest compounded monthly for 5 years.

  • r = $0.048 / 12 = 0.004$
  • n = $5 \text{ years} \times 12 \text{ months/year} = 60$ $FV = 500 \left[ \frac{(1.004)^{60} - 1}{0.004} \right] = 500 \left[ \frac{1.270489 - 1}{0.004} \right] = 500 \times 67.622 = $33,811.16$

Present Value

Present value determines what a future amount of money is worth today. This is crucial for evaluating investments and financial decisions.

  • Present Value: The amount of money you would need to invest today to have a specific amount in the future, given a particular interest rate.

  • Time Value of Money: The concept that money available at the present time is worth more than the identical sum in the future due to its potential earning capacity.

  • Present Value of a Lump Sum Formula: $PV = FV(1 + r)^{-n} \quad \text{or} \quad PV = \frac{FV}{(1 + r)^n}$

    • PV = present value
    • FV = future value
    • r = rate per period
    • n = number of periods

Example: Present Value of a Lump Sum A business receives $50,000 in 5 years. At a 7% annual return, its present value is: $PV = 50,000(1.07)^{-5} = 50,000 \times 0.712986 = $35,650.94$ This means $35,650.94 invested today at 7% would grow to $50,000 in 5 years.

  • Present Value of an Annuity Formula: The present value of receiving (or paying) equal amounts for n periods: $PV = PMT \left[ \frac{1 - (1 + r)^{-n}}{r} \right]$

Example: Present Value of an Annuity A rental property generates $2,000 per month for 15 years. At a discount rate of 6% compounded monthly, the present value of this income stream is:

  • PMT = $2,000
  • r = $0.06 / 12 = 0.005$
  • n = $15 \text{ years} \times 12 \text{ months/year} = 180$ $PV = 2,000 \left[ \frac{1 - (1.005)^{-180}}{0.005} \right] = 2,000 \left[ \frac{1 - 0.407497}{0.005} \right] = 2,000 \left[ \frac{0.592503}{0.005} \right] = 2,000 \times 118.501 = $237,001.28$

Sinking Funds

A sinking fund is a practical application of the future value of an annuity, where the goal is to determine the regular payment needed to reach a specific future amount.

  • Sinking Fund: A savings plan where you make regular deposits to accumulate a specific amount by a target date.
  • Sinking Fund Payment Formula: This is derived by rearranging the future value of an ordinary annuity formula to solve for PMT. $PMT = FV \left[ \frac{r}{(1 + r)^n - 1} \right]$

Example: Sinking Fund Calculation A company needs $100,000 in 5 years for equipment replacement. They deposit monthly into an account earning 4.8% annual interest compounded monthly.

  • FV = $100,000
  • r = $0.048 / 12 = 0.004$
  • n = $5 \text{ years} \times 12 \text{ months/year} = 60$ $PMT = 100,000 \left[ \frac{0.004}{(1.004)^{60} - 1} \right] = 100,000 \left[ \frac{0.004}{1.270489 - 1} \right] = 100,000 \left[ \frac{0.004}{0.270489} \right] = 100,000 \times 0.014786 = $1,478.64$ The company must deposit $1,478.64 per month. Over 60 months, they deposit $88,718.40, with the remaining $11,281.60 coming from interest.

Quick Practice: Save $20,000 in 3 years at 3.6% annual interest compounded monthly.

  • r = $0.036 / 12 = 0.003$
  • n = $3 \text{ years} \times 12 \text{ months/year} = 36$ $PMT = 20,000 \left[ \frac{0.003}{(1.003)^{36} - 1} \right] = 20,000 \left[ \frac{0.003}{1.113842 - 1} \right] = 20,000 \left[ \frac{0.003}{0.113842} \right] = 20,000 \times 0.026354 = $527.07$

Review Questions

  1. Explain the difference between the future value of a single lump sum and the future value of an ordinary annuity. Provide a real-world scenario where each formula would be appropriate.
  2. Describe the purpose of the Rule of 72. If you want to double your money in approximately 6 years, what annual interest rate would you need according to this rule?

  4. Why is the concept of "present value" important for businesses and individuals? Provide an example of a financial decision that would heavily rely on a present value calculation.
  5. Compare an ordinary annuity and an annuity due. In which scenario would an annuity due be preferable, and why does it typically result in a higher future value?
