The Math: Loan Calculations and Amortization

Overview

This section provides a foundational understanding of how loans are calculated and repaid over time. It covers the mathematical formulas for determining monthly payments on installment loans, calculating finance charges on credit cards using the average daily balance method, and constructing amortization tables to track loan repayment. Mastering these calculations is essential for making informed personal and business financial decisions.

Key Concepts

  • Installment Loans: Loans repaid through a fixed number of equal payments made at regular intervals, such as auto loans and mortgages.
  • Monthly Payment Formula: A specific formula used to calculate the consistent monthly payment required to repay an installment loan, considering the principal, monthly interest rate, and total number of payments.
  • Total Interest Paid: The total amount of money paid in interest over the life of an installment loan, calculated by subtracting the original principal from the total amount paid.
  • Credit Card Finance Charges (Average Daily Balance Method): A method used to calculate the interest charged on credit card balances, based on the average balance across all days in a billing cycle.
  • Amortization: The process of gradually paying off a loan over time with regular payments, where each payment is split between covering the accrued interest and reducing the principal balance. The proportion of each payment going to principal increases over the loan's life.

Important Terms

  • Installment Loan: A loan that is repaid over time with a set number of scheduled, equal payments.
  • Principal (P): The initial amount of money borrowed in a loan.
  • Monthly Interest Rate (r): The annual interest rate converted to a decimal and then divided by 12, used in monthly payment calculations.
  • Total Number of Payments (n): The total count of individual payments that will be made over the life of a loan.
  • Finance Charge: The interest charged on a credit card balance.
  • Average Daily Balance Method: A common method for calculating credit card finance charges, which involves summing daily balances, dividing by the number of days in the billing cycle, and then applying the daily interest rate.
  • Amortization: The systematic process of reducing the principal of a debt over a set period through regular payments that include both principal and interest.

Detailed Notes

Installment Loan Payments

Most large purchases are financed through installment loans, which require a consistent payment over a set period. Understanding the math behind these loans helps borrowers compare options effectively.

Monthly Payment Formula

The formula to calculate the monthly payment ($M$) for an installment loan is: [ M = P \frac{r(1+r)^n}{(1+r)^n - 1} ] Where:

  • $P$ = Principal (the amount borrowed)
  • $r$ = Monthly interest rate (annual rate divided by 12, as a decimal)
  • $n$ = Total number of payments (loan term in years multiplied by 12 for monthly payments)

Important Note on Interest Rate: Always convert the annual interest rate to a decimal first, then divide by 12 to get the monthly interest rate ($r$). For example, a 6% annual rate is $0.06 / 12 = 0.005$ monthly.

Example: Computing a Monthly Payment Let's find the monthly payment for a $15,000 loan at 6% annual interest for 4 years.

  1. Identify values:
    • $P = $15,000$
    • Annual rate = 6% = 0.06
    • $r = 0.06 / 12 = 0.005$
    • $n = 4 \text{ years} \times 12 \text{ months/year} = 48 \text{ payments}$
  2. Substitute into the formula: $M = 15,000 \frac{0.005(1.005)^{48}}{(1.005)^{48} - 1}$
  3. Calculate $(1.005)^{48}$: This term is approximately $1.270489$.
  4. Calculate numerator: $0.005 \times 1.270489 = 0.006352$
  5. Calculate denominator: $1.270489 - 1 = 0.270489$
  6. Complete the calculation: $M = 15,000 \frac{0.006352}{0.270489} = 15,000 \times 0.023485 = $352.28$ The monthly payment is $352.28.

Total Paid and Total Interest

Once the monthly payment ($M$) is known, you can calculate the total amount paid and the total interest.

  • Total amount paid = $M \times n$ (monthly payment times total number of payments)
  • Total interest paid = (Total amount paid) - $P$ (total paid minus the original principal)

Example: Finding Total Interest Using the previous loan example ($352.28 per month for 48 months):

  1. Total paid: $352.28 \times 48 = $16,909.44$
  2. Total interest: $16,909.44 - $15,000.00 = $1,909.44$ The borrower pays $1,909.44 in interest over the loan's life.

Quick Practice: A $20,000.00 loan has a monthly payment of $402.62 and runs for 60 months. Find the total interest paid.

  • Total paid = $402.62 \times 60 = $24,157.20$
  • Total interest = $24,157.20 - $20,000.00 = $4,157.20$

Credit Card Finance Charges

Unlike installment loans, credit cards allow for varying payments. Finance charges (interest) are typically calculated using the average daily balance method.

Average Daily Balance Method

To calculate a credit card's finance charge:

  1. Record daily balances: For each day in the billing cycle, note the account balance.
  2. Sum daily balances: Add up all the daily balances.
  3. Calculate average daily balance: Divide the sum of daily balances by the number of days in the billing cycle.
  4. Calculate finance charge: Multiply the average daily balance by the daily rate (Annual Percentage Rate (APR) / 365) and then by the number of days in the billing cycle. [ \text{Finance Charge} = \text{Average Daily Balance} \times \left(\frac{\text{APR}}{365}\right) \times \text{Days in Cycle} ]

Example: Average Daily Balance Calculation A credit card has an APR of 18%. The billing cycle is 30 days. The account starts with a $800 balance. On Day 11, a $200 purchase is made. On Day 21, a $300 payment is posted.

  1. Balance for each period:
    • Days 1-10 (10 days): $800.00
    • Days 11-20 (10 days): $800.00 + $200.00 = $1,000.00
    • Days 21-30 (10 days): $1,000.00 - $300.00 = $700.00
  2. Sum of daily balances: $(10 \times $800) + (10 \times $1,000) + (10 \times $700)$ $= $8,000 + $10,000 + $7,000 = $25,000$
  3. Average daily balance: $$25,000 / 30 = $833.33$
  4. Finance charge: $$833.33 \times (0.18 / 365) \times 30$ $= $833.33 \times 0.000493 \times 30 \approx $12.33$

Warning on Minimum Payments: Credit card minimum payments are often very low. Making only minimum payments means a significant portion of your payment goes to interest, causing the balance to decrease extremely slowly and leading to very high total interest paid over many years.

Amortization Tables

Amortization is the process of paying off a loan with equal payments, where each payment covers both interest and a portion of the principal. An amortization table details how each payment is applied.

Building One Row of an Amortization Table

For each payment period:

  1. Interest for the period: Remaining Balance $\times r$ (where $r$ is the monthly rate)
  2. Principal paid: Monthly Payment ($M$) - Interest for the period
  3. New balance: Old Balance - Principal Paid

Example: Building Amortization Table Rows A $10,000 loan at 9% annual interest has a monthly payment of $207.58. Monthly rate ($r$) = $0.09 / 12 = 0.0075$.

  • Payment 1:

    • Interest = $10,000.00 \times 0.0075 = $75.00$
    • Principal = $207.58 - $75.00 = $132.58$
    • New balance = $10,000.00 - $132.58 = $9,867.42$

  • Payment 2:

    • Interest = $9,867.42 \times 0.0075 = $74.01$
    • Principal = $207.58 - $74.01 = $133.57$
    • New balance = $9,867.42 - $133.57 = $9,733.85$

  • Payment 3:

    • Interest = $9,733.85 \times 0.0075 = $73.00$
    • Principal = $207.58 - $73.00 = $134.58$
    • New balance = $9,733.85 - $134.58 = $9,599.27$

Notice that as the loan balance decreases, the interest portion of each payment gets smaller, and consequently, the principal portion gets larger. This is a fundamental characteristic of amortization.

Quick Practice: Using the loan above, compute Payment 4. The remaining balance after Payment 3 is $9,599.27 and the monthly rate is $0.0075$.

  • Interest = $9,599.27 \times 0.0075 = $71.99$
  • Principal = $207.58 - $71.99 = $135.59$
  • New balance = $9,599.27 - $135.59 = $9,463.68$

How Amortization Works Over Time

In the early stages of a loan, a larger portion of each payment is allocated to interest because the principal balance is higher. As the principal is gradually reduced, the interest accruing on the smaller balance decreases, allowing a greater share of each subsequent payment to go towards paying down the principal. This means that accelerating payments (e.g., paying a little extra each month) can significantly reduce the total interest paid and shorten the loan term.

  1. Explain the key differences between an installment loan and a credit card regarding payment structure and how interest is charged.
  2. A loan is taken out for $25,000 at an annual interest rate of 5% for 5 years.
    • What is the monthly interest rate ($r$)?
    • What is the total number of payments ($n$)?
    • Without calculating the full monthly payment, describe the steps you would take to use the monthly payment formula to find $M$.
  3. Why is it crucial to convert the annual interest rate to a monthly rate ($r$) when using the installment loan payment formula? What error would occur if you used the annual rate directly?
