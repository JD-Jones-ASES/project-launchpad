The Math: Interest Formulas - Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental concepts and formulas behind interest calculations, a critical aspect of personal finance, banking, and business. It differentiates between simple and compound interest, explaining how each is calculated based on principal, interest rate, and time, and provides tools like the Annual Percentage Yield (APY) to compare different investment or loan options.

Key Concepts

  • Interest: The cost of borrowing money or the reward for lending it, typically expressed as a percentage of the principal over a period.
  • Principal (P): The initial amount of money deposited, invested, or borrowed.
  • Interest Rate (r): The annual percentage charged or earned on the principal, always converted to a decimal (e.g., 6% becomes 0.06) for use in formulas.
  • Time (t): The duration of the loan or investment, always expressed in years. Months are divided by 12, and days typically by 360 (Banker's Year) unless specified otherwise.
  • Simple Interest: Interest calculated only on the original principal, meaning the dollar amount of interest earned or owed remains constant each period.
  • Maturity Value (A): The total amount at the end of a simple interest loan or investment, calculated as principal plus all earned interest (A = P + I or A = P(1 + rt)).
  • Compound Interest: Interest calculated on the original principal and on all accumulated interest from previous periods, leading to faster growth due to "interest on interest."
  • Compounding Periods (n): The number of times per year interest is calculated and added to the principal. Common frequencies include annually (n=1), semiannually (n=2), quarterly (n=4), and monthly (n=12).
  • Future Value (A) with Compound Interest: The total amount after t years, including compound interest, calculated using the formula A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt).
  • Annual Percentage Yield (APY): Also known as the effective annual rate, this is the equivalent annual simple interest rate that accounts for compounding, allowing for a standardized comparison of different interest-bearing accounts.

Important Terms

  • Principal (P): The original amount of money deposited, invested, or borrowed.
  • Interest Rate (r): The annual percentage charged or earned, always used as a decimal in formulas.
  • Time (t): The duration of the financial transaction, always measured in years.
  • Banker's Year (Ordinary Interest): A convention for calculating time in days, where t = days/360.
  • Exact Interest: A method for calculating time in days, where t = days/365.
  • Simple Interest: A type of interest where earnings are calculated only on the initial principal amount.
  • Maturity Value (A): The total amount repaid or received at the end of a simple interest loan or investment, including the principal and all simple interest.
  • Compound Interest: A type of interest where earnings are calculated on both the initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods.
  • Compounding Periods (n): The number of times per year that interest is calculated and added to the principal.
  • Annual Percentage Yield (APY): The effective annual rate of return, taking into account the effect of compounding interest, used to compare different interest rates on a level playing field.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Interest

Interest is fundamentally the cost of using money over time. If you borrow, you pay interest; if you lend or deposit, you earn interest. This section lays the groundwork for understanding how this cost or reward is mathematically determined.

Key Variables in Interest Formulas

All interest calculations rely on three core variables:

  • Principal (P): This is the starting amount. Whether you're putting money into savings or taking out a loan, P is the initial sum.
  • Interest Rate (r): This is the percentage. Crucially, the annual rate (r) must always be converted to a decimal before being used in any formula. For example, 6% becomes 0.06. Using the percentage value directly will lead to incorrect results (100 times too large!).
  • Time (t): This is the duration. Time (t) must always be expressed in years.
    • If given in months, divide by 12: t = months / 12.
    • If given in days, there are two common methods:
      • Banker's Year (Ordinary Interest): t = days / 360. This is the default unless otherwise stated.
      • Exact Interest: t = days / 365.

Simple Interest

Simple interest is the most straightforward form of interest.

  • Definition: Interest is calculated only on the original principal amount. This means the dollar amount of interest earned or owed is the same for each period.

  • Simple Interest Formula: I = Prt Where:

    • I = interest earned (or owed)
    • P = principal
    • r = annual interest rate (as a decimal)
    • t = time (in years)

    Example: To find the simple interest on $2,000 at 5% for 3 years: I = 2,000 * 0.05 * 3 = $300.00

  • Maturity Value (Simple Interest): The maturity value (A), also known as future value for simple interest, is the total amount at the end of the loan or investment. It includes the original principal plus all the interest earned.

    • Formula 1: A = P + I
    • Formula 2 (derived by substituting I = Prt): A = P(1 + rt)

    Example: Marcus borrows $6,000 at 7% simple interest for 2 years. A = 6,000 * (1 + 0.07 * 2) = 6,000 * (1 + 0.14) = 6,000 * 1.14 = $6,840.00

  • Solving for P, r, or t: The simple interest formula (I = Prt) can be rearranged to solve for any of the variables if the other three are known:

    • P = I / (rt)
    • r = I / (Pt)
    • t = I / (Pr)

    Example (Finding Principal): An investment earned $540 simple interest at 6% over 3 years. P = 540 / (0.06 * 3) = 540 / 0.18 = $3,000.00

    Example (Finding Rate): A $5,000 loan for 2 years resulted in $800 simple interest. r = 800 / (5,000 * 2) = 800 / 10,000 = 0.08 = 8%

Compound Interest

Compound interest is more common and powerful than simple interest, especially over longer periods.

  • Definition: Interest is calculated on the original principal and on all previously accumulated interest. This "interest on interest" effect leads to exponential growth.

  • Compounding Periods (n): This refers to how many times per year the interest is calculated and added to the principal.

    • Annually: n = 1
    • Semiannually: n = 2
    • Quarterly: n = 4
    • Monthly: n = 12
    • Daily: n = 365

  • Compound Interest Formula (Future Value): A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt) Where:

    • A = future value (total amount after t years)
    • P = principal
    • r = annual interest rate (as a decimal)
    • n = number of compounding periods per year
    • t = time (in years)

    The total interest earned is I = A - P. Note: The exponent nt represents the total number of times interest is compounded over the life of the investment/loan.

    Example (Quarterly Compounding): Find the future value of $5,000 invested at 8% compounded quarterly for 3 years. P = 5,000, r = 0.08, n = 4, t = 3 A = 5,000 * (1 + 0.08/4)^(4*3) A = 5,000 * (1 + 0.02)^12 A = 5,000 * (1.02)^12 (Using calculator: (1.02)^12 is approximately 1.268242) A = 5,000 * 1.268242 = $6,341.21 Interest earned: I = 6,341.21 - 5,000.00 = $1,341.21

    Calculator Steps: When calculating (1 + r/n)^(nt), it's often best to calculate r/n first, then add 1, then use the exponent key (y^x or ^) for nt, and finally multiply by P.

  • Comparing Simple and Compound Interest: Compound interest always yields more than simple interest for the same principal, rate, and time, due to the "interest on interest" effect.

    Example: $1,000 at 10% for 3 years.

    • Simple Interest: I = 1,000 * 0.10 * 3 = $300.00, so A = $1,300.00
    • Compound Annually: A = 1,000 * (1 + 0.10)^3 = 1,000 * (1.10)^3 = 1,000 * 1.331 = $1,331.00. Interest I = $331.00. Compound interest earns $31.00 more.

Effective Annual Rate (APY)

  • Purpose: The APY, or Annual Percentage Yield, provides a standardized way to compare interest rates from different accounts or loans, especially when they have different compounding frequencies. It expresses the actual annual rate you would earn or pay, factoring in compounding.

  • APY Formula: APY = (1 + r/n)^n - 1 Where:

    • r = annual interest rate (as a decimal)
    • n = number of compounding periods per year

    Example: Bank A offers 5.9% compounded monthly; Bank B offers 6.0% compounded annually.

    • Bank A (monthly): r = 0.059, n = 12 APY = (1 + 0.059/12)^12 - 1 APY = (1.004917)^12 - 1 APY approx 1.06062 - 1 = 0.06062 = 6.062%
    • Bank B (annually): r = 0.06, n = 1 APY = (1 + 0.06/1)^1 - 1 = 1.06 - 1 = 0.06 = 6.0% Bank A offers a better return (6.062% vs. 6.0%).

  • Note: Banks are legally required to disclose the APY for savings products, making it the most reliable figure for direct comparison.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental difference between simple interest and compound interest. In what scenarios might one be preferred over the other?
  2. An investment advertisement boasts an "8% annual rate." What crucial piece of information is missing to understand the true return, and why is it important?
  3. Calculate the simple interest and the maturity value for a loan of $7,500 at 6% annual simple interest for 18 months.
  4. You are offered two investment options:
    • Option A: 5.5% compounded semiannually for 4 years.
    • Option B: 5.4% compounded monthly for 4 years. If you invest $10,000, which option will give you a higher future value, and by how much? Explain your reasoning.
  5. Why is the Annual Percentage Yield (APY) a more reliable metric than the stated annual interest rate (nominal rate) when comparing different savings accounts? Provide an example to support your answer.
