The Math: Understanding and Using Percents Study Guide

Overview

This section provides a foundational understanding of percents, explaining their origin as "per hundred" and their widespread use in daily life. It covers essential skills such as converting between percents, decimals, and fractions, using the percent equation to solve for unknown quantities (part, rate, or base), calculating percent increases and decreases, and accurately handling multiple successive percent changes.

Key Concepts

  • Definition of Percent: A ratio that compares a number to 100, symbolized by "$%$," meaning "out of 100."
  • Percent–Decimal Conversions:
    • Percent to Decimal: Divide the percent by 100 (move the decimal point two places to the left).
    • Decimal to Percent: Multiply the decimal by 100 (move the decimal point two places to the right).
  • Percent–Fraction Conversions:
    • Percent to Fraction: Write the percent as a fraction with a denominator of 100, then simplify.
    • Fraction to Percent: Divide the numerator by the denominator, then multiply the resulting decimal by 100.
  • The Percent Equation ($P = R \cdot B$): This fundamental equation relates the Part ($P$), the Rate ($R$ as a decimal), and the Base ($B$, the whole amount). It can be rearranged to solve for any unknown.
  • Percent Change: Measures how much a quantity has increased or decreased relative to its original value. The formula is $\frac{\text{New Value - Old Value}}{\text{Old Value}} \cdot 100%$. A positive result indicates an increase, and a negative result indicates a decrease.
  • Successive Percent Changes: When multiple percent changes are applied one after another, they cannot simply be added together. Each change applies to the new amount. To find the net effect, multiply the individual factors $(1 + r_1)(1 + r_2)...$, where $r$ is the percent change as a decimal (negative for decreases).

Important Terms

  • Percent: A ratio indicating parts per one hundred.
  • Part ($P$): The portion of the whole being considered in a percent problem.
  • Rate ($R$): The percent in decimal form; represents the proportional relationship between the part and the base.
  • Base ($B$): The whole or total amount; the quantity to which the part is compared.
  • Percent Change: The relative change in a quantity, expressed as a percentage of its original value.
  • Percent Increase: A positive percent change, indicating growth.
  • Percent Decrease: A negative percent change, indicating reduction.
  • Successive Percent Changes: Two or more percentage changes applied sequentially to an amount, where each subsequent change is based on the new, adjusted amount.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Percents

The concept of "percent" originates from the Latin "per centum," meaning "per hundred." It's a convenient way to express a proportion out of a total of 100, making comparisons easier. Percents are encountered daily in various contexts like sales discounts, tax calculations, interest rates, and academic scores. This section aims to equip you with the skills to confidently work with percents.

Converting Between Percents, Decimals, and Fractions

Percents and Decimals

Converting between percents and decimals is a fundamental skill:

  • Percent to Decimal: To convert a percent to its decimal form, you divide by 100. This is equivalent to moving the decimal point two places to the left.
    • Example: $68% = 68 \div 100 = 0.68$
    • Example: $4.5% = 4.5 \div 100 = 0.045$
    • Example: $150% = 150 \div 100 = 1.50$ (Note: Percents can be greater than 100%)
    • Example: $0.25% = 0.25 \div 100 = 0.0025$ (Be careful with small percents!)
  • Decimal to Percent: To convert a decimal to a percent, you multiply by 100. This is equivalent to moving the decimal point two places to the right and adding the percent symbol.
    • Example: $0.075 = 0.075 \times 100 = 7.5%$

Percents and Fractions

  • Percent to Fraction: To convert a percent to a fraction, write the percent value as the numerator over a denominator of 100, then simplify the fraction to its lowest terms.
    • Example: $75% = \frac{75}{100} = \frac{3}{4}$
  • Fraction to Percent: To convert a fraction to a percent, first divide the numerator by the denominator to get a decimal. Then, convert that decimal to a percent by multiplying by 100 (moving the decimal two places to the right).
    • Example: $\frac{3}{8} = 3 \div 8 = 0.375 = 37.5%$
    • Example: $\frac{5}{8} = 5 \div 8 = 0.625 = 62.5%$

Common Equivalents and Cautions

Memorizing frequently used conversions (e.g., $25% = 0.25 = \frac{1}{4}$, $50% = 0.5 = \frac{1}{2}$) can significantly speed up calculations. A crucial point of confusion for many students is distinguishing between very small percents and larger ones. For instance, $0.5%$ is $0.005$ (a tiny amount), while $50%$ is $0.50$ (half). Always remember the "divide by 100" rule for percent-to-decimal conversion.

The Percent Equation: $P = R \cdot B$

This equation is central to solving many percent-related problems.

  • $P$ represents the Part: The specific portion or amount you are interested in.
  • $R$ represents the Rate: The percent, but always expressed as a decimal in the equation.
  • $B$ represents the Base: The whole, total, or original amount.

The equation can be rearranged to solve for any of the three variables:

  1. To find the Part ($P$): Use the original formula: $P = R \cdot B$
    • Example: What is $15%$ of $$240.00$? Here, $R = 0.15$ and $B = 240$. $P = 0.15 \times 240 = $36.00$
  2. To find the Rate ($R$): Divide the Part by the Base: $R = \frac{P}{B}$. Remember to convert the resulting decimal to a percent.
    • Example: $$18.00$ is what percent of $$80.00$? Here, $P = 18$ and $B = 80$. $R = \frac{18}{80} = 0.225 = 22.5%$
  3. To find the Base ($B$): Divide the Part by the Rate: $B = \frac{P}{R}$.
    • Example: $$45.00$ is $30%$ of what amount? Here, $P = 45$ and $R = 0.30$. $B = \frac{45}{0.30} = $150.00$

Helpful Trick: Before calculating, ask yourself if the answer should logically be larger or smaller than the given numbers. For example, if you're finding $15%$ of a number, the part will be smaller than the base. If a small part (like $30%$) represents a given amount, the base must be much larger.

Calculating Percent Change

Percent change quantifies how much a value has increased or decreased relative to its starting point.

  • Formula: $\text{Percent Change} = \frac{\text{New Value - Old Value}}{\text{Old Value}} \times 100%$
  • Interpretation:
    • A positive result indicates a percent increase.
    • A negative result indicates a percent decrease.

Percent Increase Example

A store's monthly sales rose from $$42,000.00$ (Old Value) to $$49,350.00$ (New Value). $\text{Percent Change} = \frac{49,350 - 42,000}{42,000} \times 100% = \frac{7,350}{42,000} \times 100% = 0.175 \times 100% = 17.5%$ Sales increased by $17.5%$.

Percent Decrease Example

The price of a laptop dropped from $$899.99$ (Old Value) to $$719.99$ (New Value). $\text{Percent Change} = \frac{719.99 - 899.99}{899.99} \times 100% = \frac{-180.00}{899.99} \times 100% \approx -0.200 \times 100% \approx -20.0%$ The price decreased by $20.0%$.

Crucial Caution: Always divide by the original (old) value. This is the most common error in percent change problems. The "base" for the change is always the starting amount.

Handling Successive Percent Changes

Successive percent changes occur when a quantity undergoes multiple percentage adjustments one after another. A key takeaway is that you cannot simply add or subtract the individual percentages. Each subsequent change is applied to the new, adjusted amount.

Example: Multiple Discounts

A jacket costs $$80.00$. It gets a $25%$ discount, then an additional $10%$ off the sale price.

  1. First Discount (25% off):
    • Sale price = Original Price $\times (1 - \text{Discount Rate})$
    • Sale price = $$80.00 \times (1 - 0.25) = $80.00 \times 0.75 = $60.00$
  2. Second Discount (10% off the sale price):
    • Final price = Sale Price $\times (1 - \text{Additional Discount Rate})$
    • Final price = $$60.00 \times (1 - 0.10) = $60.00 \times 0.90 = $54.00$

Comparing this to a single $35%$ discount ($25% + 10%$): $$80.00 \times (1 - 0.35) = $80.00 \times 0.65 = $52.00$ The results are different ($$54.00$ vs. $$52.00$), proving that successive percents are not additive.

Net Effect of Successive Percent Changes

To find the overall single equivalent percent change, multiply the factors corresponding to each change. For an increase, the factor is $(1 + r)$; for a decrease, it's $(1 - r)$.

  • For the jacket example:
    • Factor for 25% off: $(1 - 0.25) = 0.75$
    • Factor for 10% off: $(1 - 0.10) = 0.90$
    • Net factor = $0.75 \times 0.90 = 0.675$ This means the final price is $67.5%$ of the original price. To find the total discount, calculate $1 - 0.675 = 0.325$, or $32.5%$. The total discount is $32.5%$, not $35%$.

Review Questions

  1. Explain in your own words why a percent is a useful way to express a number, and provide three real-world examples not mentioned in the text.
  2. Describe the key differences and common pitfalls when converting between percents, decimals, and fractions. Provide an example for converting $0.008$ to a percent and $\frac{1}{25}$ to a percent, explaining each step.
  3. You are given the values for the "part" and the "base" in a percent problem. How would you use the percent equation ($P = R \cdot B$) to find the "rate"? Illustrate with an example where $P = $60$ and $B = $250$.
  4. A company's revenue increased by $15%$ last quarter and then decreased by $5%$ this quarter. Calculate the overall percent change in revenue over these two quarters. Is this the same as a $10%$ increase? Explain your reasoning.
  5. What is the most common mistake students make when calculating percent change, and why is it important to avoid this error? Create a hypothetical scenario to demonstrate the impact of this mistake.
