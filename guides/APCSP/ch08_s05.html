AP CSP — Topic 8.5: Intellectual Property and Open-Source Licensing

Core Question

How does U.S. copyright law manage the tension between the economic incentive to create digital works (which depends on the ability to restrict copying) and the social benefit of wide distribution (which is maximized when copying is unrestricted), and how do Creative Commons and open-source licenses operate within that legal framework? Section 8.5 catalogs three layers: (1) the default copyright regime — automatic on fixation, life-plus-70 for individual works post-1978, with fair use as a four-factor defense; (2) the Creative Commons license system, which grants standing permissions through standardized terms (BY, SA, NC, ND combined into six primary licenses plus CC0); (3) the open-source software ecosystem, with permissive (MIT, BSD) and copyleft (GPL) licenses producing different economic equilibria. The textbook framing is descriptive: it explains how each mechanism works, what rights it grants, what obligations it imposes, and what its economic effects are.

Key Concepts

  • Intellectual property (IP): Legal rights granted to creators over their original works. For digital computing, copyright is the most relevant form.
  • Copyright (U.S.): Arises automatically on fixation in a tangible medium of expression. No registration, notice, or publication required for protection. Registration unlocks statutory damages in litigation.
  • Copyright duration: Individual works post-1978: life of author + 70 years. Works for hire: 95 years from publication or 120 years from creation, whichever is shorter. Works published pre-1928 are in U.S. public domain regardless.
  • Public domain: Works not subject to copyright (expired, U.S. federal-government-created, or explicitly dedicated). Free to use without permission.
  • Fair use: Statutory limitation on copyright (17 U.S.C. 107). A defense, not a category of automatically permitted uses; case-by-case evaluation against the four factors.
  • Four-factor test: (1) Purpose and character — transformative uses (commentary, parody, education, news) favor fair use; (2) Nature of original — factual receives narrower protection than creative; (3) Amount and substantiality — small fraction better, "heart" of work counts heavily; (4) Effect on market — substitution or licensing-revenue loss weighs strongly against.
  • Creative Commons (CC): Standardized, machine-readable licenses applied by creators to grant specific permissions in advance. Operate within copyright. Six primary types from combinations of BY, SA, NC, ND, plus CC0 (public-domain dedication).
  • Permissive open-source license: MIT, BSD — minimal conditions; allows proprietary derivative distribution and binary-only release.
  • Copyleft open-source license: GPL — derivatives distributed to others must be released under the same license, with source available. Coined by Richard Stallman.
  • Open Source Initiative: Maintains the ten-criterion Open Source Definition that a license must meet to be considered "open source."

Mechanisms and Mitigations

  • Mechanism: Copying digital works has near-zero marginal cost -> Mitigation: Copyright as a default exclusive right. The U.S. Copyright Act of 1976 grants automatic protection on fixation, providing creators with a baseline incentive structure regardless of whether they take any registration step.
  • Mechanism: Strict copyright would block transformative uses (commentary, parody, education, news) -> Mitigation: Fair-use four-factor test. Courts evaluate purpose, nature, amount, and market effect together for each specific use. Result: a flexible defense rather than a fixed category, decided case-by-case.
  • Mechanism: Creators wanting to grant standing permissions must draft custom licenses for each work -> Mitigation: Creative Commons standardized licenses. Six primary licenses (CC BY, BY-SA, BY-NC, BY-NC-SA, BY-ND, BY-NC-ND) plus CC0 give creators interoperable terms users can recognize and machines can parse.
  • Mechanism: Software users want both freedom to inspect/modify code and protection against losing those freedoms downstream -> Mitigation: Two open-source license families. Permissive (MIT, BSD) maximizes downstream freedom but allows proprietary forks. Copyleft (GPL) constrains downstream by requiring derivative source release, creating a feedback that returns improvements to the community.

Common Pitfalls

  • Believing copyright requires registration. U.S. copyright arises automatically on fixation; registration is needed only for statutory damages in litigation, not for protection itself.
  • Treating fair use as a category of automatically permitted uses. Fair use is a defense evaluated case-by-case against four factors; courts can rule against any specific use even when it resembles a prior fair-use case.
  • Assuming all open-source licenses behave the same way. Permissive licenses (MIT, BSD) allow proprietary derivative distribution; copyleft licenses (GPL) do not. Mixing them in a single product can create license-compatibility constraints.
  • Confusing CC ShareAlike with CC NonCommercial. ShareAlike requires the same license on derivatives but allows commercial use; NonCommercial blocks commercial use entirely. They are independent conditions and combine differently.
  • Treating the U.S. public-domain cutoff as identical to global rules. The textbook's pre-1928 cutoff is U.S.-specific; other jurisdictions have different durations, and Berne Convention rules complicate cross-border use.

Connect to the Exam

Section 8.5 is part of Big Idea 5. AP exam questions typically present a scenario — a song posted online, a library considered for a commercial product, an article quoted in a news piece — and ask which legal or licensing mechanism applies and what specific rule determines the outcome. Use the textbook's exact vocabulary: copyright arises on fixation; fair use is a four-factor defense; CC licenses grant standing permissions within copyright; permissive vs copyleft is the core open-source distinction. The exam tests mechanism knowledge, not policy advocacy. When asked whether a use is permitted, identify the rule (license terms, fair-use factors) and apply it to the specific facts.

Self-Check

  • A teacher photocopies one chapter of a 20-chapter novel for classroom discussion and analysis. Apply the four fair-use factors to this scenario and explain which factors weigh for or against the use.
  • A startup wants to embed a popular npm package in a closed-source SaaS product. The package is licensed MIT. Does the MIT license permit this use? Explain by citing the conditions the license imposes and what it does not require.
  • A researcher modifies a GPL-licensed database engine and deploys the modified version on the company's internal servers without distributing it externally. Does the GPL require source disclosure in this scenario? Explain why the GPL's distribution trigger matters.
  • A photographer applies CC BY-NC to their image library. A nonprofit news outlet wants to reprint one image with credit. Does the license permit it? What if a for-profit news outlet wants to do the same?
  • Explain in two sentences why the textbook describes copyleft as "using copyright law to enforce perpetual openness." Identify the specific legal mechanism that makes this strategy work.
