AP CSP — Topic 8.2: The Digital Divide — Infrastructure and Economics

Core Question

Why is broadband Internet not uniformly available across the population, and what technical and economic mechanisms drive the gap? Section 8.2 treats the digital divide as an outcome of infrastructure economics. Deploying a network has fixed and variable costs that depend on population density, terrain, and distance to the nearest backbone. Where deployment cost per connection exceeds expected revenue per connection, private investment will not occur without external incentives. The section catalogs the access technologies that span this cost-revenue space (fiber, cable, DSL, fixed wireless, LEO satellite), the four metrics by which broadband is measured (download speed, upload speed, latency, cost relative to income), and three categories of mitigation (federal infrastructure grants, municipal broadband, LEO satellite expansion). The framing is descriptive throughout: the textbook lays out the mechanisms and lets the policy questions remain separate.

Key Concepts

  • Digital divide: The gap in broadband access (availability, speed, latency, cost) between geographic and demographic groups.
  • Last-mile problem: Per-connection cost rises sharply in low-density areas because each connection spans more distance with fewer customers to share the expense, while monthly revenue per customer is similar or lower.
  • Backbone vs last-mile: Backbone (trunk fiber, exchange points) amortizes well across thousands of connections; last-mile (final segment to each premise) does not amortize and dominates rural deployment cost.
  • Broadband (FCC): At least 25 Mbps download / 3 Mbps upload before 2024; 100 / 20 Mbps after 2024 update.
  • Four connectivity metrics: (1) Download speed, (2) Upload speed, (3) Latency in milliseconds, (4) Monthly cost as a percentage of median household income (captures affordability separately from physical availability).
  • Latency floors: Real-time interactive use needs < 20 ms; geostationary satellite latency is 600-700 ms due to the 35,786 km orbital altitude; LEO satellite drops latency to 20-40 ms by orbiting at 550-1,200 km.
  • Fiber (FTTH): Highest bandwidth (symmetrical 1 Gbps+), lowest latency (< 5 ms); deployment requires per-premise digging, so it is cost-effective mainly in dense areas.
  • Cable (HFC), DSL, FWA, LEO satellite: Each technology has a distinct cost structure, coverage profile, and performance ceiling. DSL speed falls sharply with distance from the telephone exchange. FWA eliminates last-mile cabling cost wherever cellular coverage exists.
  • BEAD program: Federal grant program (Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, 2021) allocating $42.45 billion to fund deployment in unserved and underserved locations, allocated by formula based on each state's unserved percentage.
  • Municipal broadband: A city or county builds and operates a network (e.g., Chattanooga's city-owned gigabit fiber).

Mechanisms and Mitigations

  • Mechanism: Last-mile cost asymmetry between dense and sparse areas -> Mitigation: Federal infrastructure grants (BEAD). When per-connection cost in rural areas exceeds private return, formula-based federal funding subsidizes the gap, with conditions on speed thresholds and low-income subscription availability.
  • Mechanism: Private incumbents lack incentive to overbuild competitively in mid-density areas -> Mitigation: Municipal broadband. A city like Chattanooga finances the capital cost with public bonds and either retails service directly or leases to competing private providers (open-access model), expanding choice and capacity. Viability depends on financing rate, expected take-rate, and competitive response.
  • Mechanism: Terrestrial deployment is uneconomic for very low-density and remote premises -> Mitigation: LEO satellite expansion. Constellations like Starlink decouple service from terrestrial geography. As satellite counts and ground stations grow, per-subscriber cost and latency both decline, making real-time interactive applications viable in locations no terrestrial technology serves.
  • Mechanism: High latency from geostationary altitude blocks real-time interaction -> Mitigation: Lower orbital altitude (LEO). Satellites at 550-1,200 km cut round-trip light-travel time by roughly two orders of magnitude, dropping latency from 600-700 ms to 20-40 ms — within the range needed for video calls, online gaming, and remote machine control.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating the digital divide as a purely demographic phenomenon rather than an outcome of infrastructure economics. Density and terrain determine cost per connection, and revenue per connection determines whether private investment is viable.
  • Confusing geostationary and LEO satellite. Both are "satellite broadband," but the 35,786 km vs 550-1,200 km altitude difference produces a 30x latency gap that determines which applications are usable.
  • Forgetting that the FCC broadband definition changed in 2024 (25/3 -> 100/20 Mbps), so the same connection may count as broadband under the old rule and below threshold under the new one.
  • Citing "DSL is rural" without adding the distance-attenuation caveat: a premise 1 km from the exchange may receive 25 Mbps; a premise 4 km away may receive only 5-10 Mbps. The technology is widespread in copper-wired areas but speed-limited and distance-limited.
  • Treating municipal broadband as automatic. The textbook notes its viability depends on financing rate, take-rate, and incumbent response — it is one mitigation among several, not a universal answer.

Connect to the Exam

Section 8.2 is part of Big Idea 5 (Impact of Computing) and contributes to the AP exam's heaviest-weighted unit (21-26%). Expect questions that present a deployment scenario (a rural county, a remote farmstead, a multi-student household) and ask which technology fits, why per-connection cost varies with density, or how a specific mitigation (BEAD grant, municipal fiber, LEO satellite) addresses a specific mechanism. The exam tests the descriptive vocabulary — last-mile problem, latency, four-metric framework — not policy preference. When asked to compare technologies, lead with the mechanism (cost structure, propagation distance) rather than with a normative judgment about which should be chosen.

Self-Check

  • A community of 200 homes spread across 30 km of mountain road has no broadband. Compare two mitigation options (BEAD-funded fiber vs LEO satellite) by mechanism: which costs are reduced, which constraints remain, and which use cases each enables.
  • Geostationary satellite latency is 600-700 ms; LEO satellite latency is 20-40 ms. Explain the physical reason for the gap, and identify two applications where the difference is operationally significant.
  • A household has 5 Mbps DSL. List three contemporary applications it can support adequately, two it cannot, and explain the bandwidth arithmetic for each.
  • The 2024 FCC broadband definition raised the threshold from 25/3 Mbps to 100/20 Mbps. Identify two specific metrics whose population values change as a result, and explain why the cost-as-percentage-of-income metric is independent of this change.
  • A city is considering municipal broadband. Identify three factors the textbook says determine the economic viability of such a deployment, and explain why each matters.
