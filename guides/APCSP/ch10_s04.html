AP CSP — Topic 10.4: Multi-Select Strategies

Core Question

Why do AP CSP students consistently score lower on multi-select questions than on single-select questions, even when they understand the underlying material? The answer is structural: every other question type lets you eliminate wrong answers until one remains. Multi-select inverts the strategy — you must POSITIVELY identify exactly two correct answers from four options. There is no partial credit. A student who picks one right and one wrong scores zero, exactly the same as a student who picked both wrong. This section gives you a systematic strategy for these questions and a taxonomy of the four distractor patterns that make them difficult.

Key Concepts

  • Multi-Select Multiple Choice: Select EXACTLY two correct answers from four options. Eight such questions appear in Section I. Binary scoring: both correct = 1 point, anything else = 0 points.
  • No "all of the above" / "none of the above": Multi-select correct answers are always two specific options, never meta-options.
  • Why multi-select is hard: (1) The stem is often a synthesis question drawing on multiple Big Ideas simultaneously. (2) At least two of the four answers are designed to be plausible — they attract partial-knowledge students.
  • Plausible-but-Irrelevant distractor: Sounds true and may be true in general, but does not directly address the question. Most dangerous on narrowly-scoped questions.
  • Over-Broad distractor: True in general but false in the specific scenario. Often signaled by universal quantifiers — "always," "never," "all," "every."
  • Over-Narrow distractor: True for one case (one input, one example) but false as a general claim. Traps students who test only one example.
  • Trick-Vocabulary distractor: Uses correct technical terms in a sentence that is actually false. Most dangerous because it requires precise knowledge of what the terms mean. Example: "Symmetric encryption uses two different keys" (false — symmetric uses the same key).
  • Read-All-Four-First: Read all four answer choices before evaluating any of them. Pass 1 is categorization, not decision-making.
  • Five-step multi-select process: (1) Read the question stem; note the scope. (2) Read all four choices without evaluating. (3) Identify each option's distractor pattern. (4) Select the two that directly answer the question. (5) 90-second time cap; mark and move on if exceeded.
  • The "second correct answer" prompt: Once you find one correct answer, ask: "What would the second correct answer look like?" The two usually address the topic from different angles.
  • Synthesis pairings: BI 3 + BI 2 (algorithm + abstraction); BI 4 + BI 5 (network/security + impact); BI 1 + BI 3 (Create-PT purpose + algorithmic property).

Worked Examples / Code Walkthroughs

Example 1 — All four distractor types in one question

Stem: A student writes a procedure that finds the smallest value in a list:

PROCEDURE findMin(data)
  smallest <- data[1]
  i <- 2
  REPEAT UNTIL (i > LENGTH(data))
    IF (data[i] < smallest)
      smallest <- data[i]
    i <- i + 1
  RETURN(smallest)

Question: Which TWO of the following are correct?

(A) "The procedure always returns the smallest value in the list, regardless of initial ordering."

  • Pattern: Plausible-but-Irrelevant (or directly correct depending on framing). Actually CORRECT — algorithm scans every element and updates smallest whenever it finds a smaller one. Independent of initial order.

(B) "The procedure uses a comparison-based approach: it examines each element in the list at least once."

  • Pattern: Direct paraphrase of the algorithm. CORRECT — the loop runs from i=2 to LENGTH(data), and the comparison at index 1 is implicit in the initialization.

(C) "The procedure runs in O(log n) time because it only looks at elements smaller than the current minimum."

  • Pattern: Trick-Vocabulary. The phrase "looks at" is wrong (it looks at every element to compare), and O(log n) is the wrong complexity (it is O(n)). Wrong.

(D) "If the list contains exactly one element, the REPEAT UNTIL body never executes and the procedure correctly returns data[1]."

  • Pattern: Could look like Over-Narrow but is actually a true statement about a specific edge case that is also general (one-element list edge case is universal). Acceptable second correct answer in some framings.

The two strongest correct answers are (A) and (B) — direct claims about the procedure's behavior, not narrow special cases.

Example 2 — Over-Broad universal quantifier

Stem: "An algorithm searches for a target in an unsorted list of n integers."

Distractor: "The algorithm always finds the target in O(log n) time, regardless of input size."

Pattern: Over-Broad — uses "always" and the wrong complexity. The unsorted list forces linear search, which is O(n), not O(log n). The "always" universalizes a claim that is false in this specific scenario.

Example 3 — Trick-Vocabulary with correct AP terminology

Stem: A multi-select question about cybersecurity asks which statements are true.

Distractor: "Asymmetric encryption uses the same key for encryption and decryption."

Pattern: Trick-Vocabulary. "Asymmetric" is real AP vocabulary, but asymmetric encryption uses TWO DIFFERENT keys (public + private). Same-key is symmetric. The sentence uses correct words in wrong relationships.

Example 4 — Two correct answers from different angles

Stem: A network question about packet switching asks which TWO statements are correct.

Correct answer 1 (mechanism): "Each packet is routed independently and may take a different path to the destination."

Correct answer 2 (impact): "Packet switching allows the network to recover from a single link failure without dropping all traffic."

These two correct answers come from different Big Ideas (BI 4 mechanism + BI 4/5 impact). Recognize that AP often pairs mechanism with impact in the two correct answers.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating multi-select like single-select: Eliminating one wrong answer at a time and stopping when you "find a correct one" misses the requirement to identify TWO correct answers. Always seek the second.
  • Selecting based on first impression: Reading and judging answer 1 before reading answer 2 leads to premature commitment. Always read all four first.
  • Ignoring universal quantifiers: "Always," "never," "all," "every" are red flags for over-broad. Re-check against the specific scenario described in the stem.
  • Trusting trick-vocabulary because the words look right: A correct technical term in a sentence does not make the sentence true. Verify the relationship between terms, not just the presence.
  • Letting one multi-select consume the time of three others: 90-second cap, mark, and move on. The opportunity cost of obsessing over one hard multi-select is leaving easier questions unanswered.

Connect to the Exam

Eight multi-select questions × 1 point each = 8 points available. A student applying the four-pattern strategy reliably scores 5–7; one guessing scores about 1.3 (random pair selection from 6 possible 2-of-4 combinations gives 1/6 expected hit rate). The 4-point swing is among the largest single-strategy gains available in Section I. Multi-select also rewards synthesis: students who can name the two Big Ideas a question is testing will identify the two correct answers (one from each Big Idea) faster than students treating each option in isolation.

Self-Check

  • For each of the four distractor patterns, write your own one-sentence example based on a topic from earlier units (loops, lists, procedures, networking).
  • A multi-select question's two candidate correct answers seem to say opposite things. What is your next move?
  • A multi-select question on encryption includes the option: "Public-key cryptography uses a private key for encryption and a public key for decryption." Which distractor pattern is this and why?
  • Time yourself on a 90-second budget for a single multi-select question. If you exceed it, what is the discipline rule, and why does the rule serve your overall section score?
  • Identify the two Big Ideas at play in this stem: "Which statements are true about a packet-switched network's resilience to a single router failure?" Which Big Ideas do the two correct answers most likely come from?
