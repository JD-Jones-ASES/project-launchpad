AP CSP — Topic 9.4: The Personalized Project Reference (PPR)

Core Question

What two screenshots must you submit with your Create PT, and why is one specific formatting rule — no inline comments — the difference between a high-scoring artifact and a flagged one? The PPR is unusual because it has two scoring lives: it earns rubric points directly during Create PT grading, and it is the only reference material the student may consult while writing the in-exam Written Response. A bad PPR not only loses artifact points; it cripples the student's ability to cite line numbers under exam-day pressure. Section 9.4 specifies the exact required content of each screenshot, the absolute No-Comments Rule, and the formatting standards that make the PPR functional both as graded evidence and as an exam-day reference card.

Key Concepts

  • What the PPR Is: Two specific screenshots from the program source code, submitted with the Create PT and returned to the student during the in-exam Written Response.
  • Screenshot 1 — List Creation and Use: Shows where the list is initialized and where it is used (read, iterated, indexed, or passed). The two segments may be from different code locations and may be presented as two sub-images within the screenshot.
  • Screenshot 2 — Procedure Definition: Shows the complete student-developed procedure from def line to final return (or last line of body). Every line of the procedure body must be visible.
  • The No-Comments Rule: Inline # comments are absolutely prohibited in PPR screenshots. Even one comment line flags the screenshot as non-compliant, and the dependent rubric rows cannot earn points.
  • Why Comments Are Prohibited: Comments could narrate code in a way that circumvents the Written Response requirement. The College Board enforces a strict separation: code goes in the PPR, explanation goes in the Written Response.
  • Comment-Stripping Workflow: Take the PPR screenshot from a temporary copy with comments removed; submit the original (with comments) as the program code.
  • IDE Folding Alternative: Some IDEs can hide or fold comment lines temporarily, allowing screenshots from the stripped view without modifying the source file.
  • Docstring Caution: Docstrings are string expressions, not comments, but their PPR permission status has varied. The conservative choice is to remove or fold them like comments.
  • Formatting Standards: Minimum 10 pt font (12 pt preferred), line numbers visible, no identifying information.
  • Line-Number Citation in Written Response: High-scoring Written Responses cite the PPR by screenshot number and line number, converting vague claims into falsifiable evidence.

PT Rubric Alignment

The PPR primarily supports the Data Abstraction and Procedural Abstraction rubric rows. Screenshot 1 is the visual evidence the reader checks for the Data Abstraction row — without a visible list creation and use, the row is un-scoreable. Screenshot 2 is the visual evidence for the Procedural Abstraction row — without the complete procedure body visible, the reader cannot verify that the parameter is used meaningfully or that all three control structures (sequencing, selection, iteration) appear inside the body. The PPR also indirectly supports the Algorithm Implementation and Managing Complexity rows by providing line-number citation targets in the Written Response. The Written Response sections of Topic 9.5 will explicitly reward line-number citations to PPR content; without a clean, well-formatted PPR, those citations cannot be made under exam-day time pressure.

Common Pitfalls

  • Comments in the screenshot. The single most-flagged PPR mistake. Even a 1-line "# main loop" comment in Screenshot 2 disqualifies the artifact. Strip comments from a temporary copy or fold them in the IDE before screenshotting.
  • Procedure body cut off. Screenshot 2 shows the def header and the first 40 lines of a 55-line procedure; the return is invisible. The reader cannot verify the procedure ends with a meaningful return because they cannot see it. Shrink the font (down to 10 pt minimum) or break the procedure into smaller helper functions.
  • List creation without showing the use. Screenshot 1 captures study_records = [] but no iteration or indexing. The reader cannot tell whether the list is just declared or actually drives behavior. Always include both halves: creation/assignment and use.
  • Identifying information in the screenshot. A username in the IDE file path bar, the student's name in a comment, or a school logo on the desktop visible behind the IDE — any of these violate the identification prohibition. Crop carefully, hide unnecessary panels, and screenshot in a clean environment.
  • Font too small to read line numbers. Screenshots compressed to fit a long procedure can become unreadable for both the reader and the student in the exam. Aim for legibility at typical viewing distance; 10 pt is the absolute floor.

Connect to the Exam

The PPR is uniquely consequential because it operates twice. It is graded once during the Create PT scoring window, and it is consulted again on exam day during the in-exam Written Response. A high-scoring Written Response cites PPR line numbers explicitly: "As shown in Screenshot 2 of my PPR, lines 28-73 define compute_streak(records, target_subject). The procedure uses target_subject in the selection condition at line 49 (if subject == target_subject and minutes >= 30:)." This style is precise, falsifiable, and directly addresses rubric language about parameter use. The reader can verify the citation in seconds, making the rubric point hard to deny. Practice writing such citations during your draft Written Responses; under exam timer pressure, line-number lookup must be reflexive.

Self-Check

  • A student's compute_streak() function is 55 lines long. The PPR Screenshot 2 only shows lines 1-40; the return on line 55 is not visible. Why is this a problem, and how should the student fix it without violating any other rule?
  • A student's PPR Screenshot 2 shows the procedure with three # comment lines clearly visible. The student argues the comments are helpful and clarify the algorithm. Will the reader award the procedural-abstraction point? Explain the College Board's rationale.
  • Sketch a draft Screenshot 1 plan for the Study Streak Tracker: identify which lines you would capture for list creation and which lines you would capture for list use. Note any formatting choices (font size, presence of line numbers, sub-image layout).
  • Practice writing a single sentence of an in-exam Written Response that cites a specific line number from your own program's procedure. Use the format: "As shown in Screenshot 2 of my PPR, line N [does what the rubric requires]..." Verify that line N actually performs the cited operation.
  • Audit your IDE setup. If you took a screenshot of your procedure right now, would the file path bar reveal a username? Would any open tab or panel display identifying information? Sketch the panels you would need to hide before taking a clean PPR screenshot.
