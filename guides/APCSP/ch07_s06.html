AP CSP — Topic 7.6: Cybersecurity Foundations

Core Question

Every packet you send travels through routers you do not own, operated by organizations you have never heard of, in countries you may never visit. Cybersecurity is the discipline that makes the Internet usable despite this threat: it provides ways to encrypt data in transit, verify the identity of communicating parties, and protect users from the human-targeted attacks (phishing, social engineering) that no amount of cryptography can stop on its own. This section introduces symmetric and asymmetric encryption, the Diffie-Hellman key exchange, common threats and defenses, and the NIST Cybersecurity Framework — and closes with the principle that ties them together: defense in depth.

Key Concepts

  • Symmetric encryption: same key for encryption and decryption. Fast (AES-256 at multi-Gbps), but requires the parties to share the key in advance. Examples: AES, ChaCha20.
  • Caesar cipher: simplest symmetric cipher. Each letter shifts by a fixed amount, wrapping with mod 26. The textbook teaching example, completely insecure but pedagogically clean.
  • Asymmetric (public-key) encryption: a related key pair. The public key encrypts; the private key decrypts. Anyone can encrypt to a recipient with their public key without prior shared secret. 100-1000x slower than symmetric.
  • Key distribution problem: how do two parties agree on a shared symmetric key without an eavesdropper learning it? Public-key cryptography and Diffie-Hellman both solve it.
  • Diffie-Hellman key exchange: lets Alice and Bob compute a shared secret over an open channel. Eve sees every message and still cannot determine the secret because doing so would require solving the discrete logarithm problem.
  • One-way function: easy to compute in one direction, infeasible to invert. Modular exponentiation g^a mod p is the canonical example.
  • HTTPS / TLS: HTTP over TLS. TLS authenticates the server (via a certificate from a trusted authority) and encrypts the channel (via a symmetric session key). Defeats man-in-the-middle attacks.
  • Multi-factor authentication (MFA): two or more independent factors — something you know, have, are. Even a stolen password cannot complete login alone.
  • Common threats: malware, phishing, man-in-the-middle, denial of service, SQL injection.
  • Defense in depth: multiple overlapping layers — encryption + MFA + updates + awareness + network controls. No single defense is sufficient.

Worked Examples / Mechanisms

  • Caesar cipher example. Encrypt the letter T with shift 7. Step 1: convert T to its alphabet index (with A=0): T → 19. Step 2: add the shift: 19 + 7 = 26. Step 3: take mod 26: 26 mod 26 = 0. Step 4: convert back to a letter: 0 → A. The ciphertext character is A. The modulo is essential because shifts wrap from Z back to A; without it, T + 7 would overflow past Z.
  • Symmetric vs asymmetric — when to use each. Symmetric: encrypt the bulk of the data because it is fast (e.g., the body of a long file or a streaming video). Asymmetric: exchange the symmetric session key because it solves the key-distribution problem at the cost of being slower. HTTPS does both: TLS uses public-key cryptography during the handshake to establish a session key, then uses the symmetric key (via AES or ChaCha20) for everything that follows.
  • Diffie-Hellman conceptual walkthrough. Public values: large prime p and a base g. Alice picks private exponent a and sends A = g^a mod p. Bob picks private exponent b and sends B = g^b mod p. Each computes the shared secret independently: Alice computes B^a mod p = g^(ab) mod p, Bob computes A^b mod p = g^(ab) mod p. Eve sees g, p, A, B but cannot recover a or b without solving the discrete logarithm problem. The shared secret was never transmitted — it was computed.
  • HTTPS handshake (anti-MitM mechanism). (1) Browser opens TCP connection to the server. (2) Browser receives the server's certificate, chained to a trusted authority. The certificate proves "this is bank.com," not just "I have a key." (3) Browser performs key exchange (often Diffie-Hellman variant) to establish a session key. (4) All subsequent traffic uses the session key with symmetric encryption. An attacker between browser and server can see encrypted bytes but cannot read or modify them, and cannot impersonate the server because they lack a certificate from a trusted authority.
  • Phishing scenario. You receive an email apparently from your bank, asking you to click a link and enter your password to "verify" your account. The link goes to bank-secure-update.example.com, not bank.com. Defense one: never click email links to login sites; navigate to the bank's site directly. Defense two: enable MFA on your bank account, so even a captured password cannot unlock the account without the second factor.

Common Pitfalls

  • Believing public-key encryption replaces symmetric encryption. It does not. Public-key encryption is too slow for bulk data. The standard pattern is hybrid: public-key for key exchange, symmetric for the actual data.
  • Forgetting the modulo in Caesar cipher arithmetic. Wrapping is what handles letters near the end of the alphabet. T + 7 = 26 in raw arithmetic, but 26 mod 26 = 0 = A. Skipping the mod gives wrong results.
  • Treating MFA as redundant if you already have a strong password. Even strong passwords leak through phishing, server breaches, and reused-password attacks. MFA defends against the consequences of a compromised password.
  • Confusing HTTPS with VPNs or proxies. HTTPS protects the channel between your browser and a specific server. A VPN protects all your traffic from your local network to the VPN exit point. They solve different problems.
  • Treating all cybersecurity threats as encryption problems. Phishing, malware, and SQL injection have nothing to do with breaking encryption — they bypass it by attacking the human or the application logic. Defense in depth, not encryption alone, is required.

Connect to the Exam

Section 7.6 is a content-rich finale to Big Idea 4. AP CSP loves protocol-choice questions ("Which type of attack is this email? Name two defenses."), Caesar cipher arithmetic, and conceptual contrasts (symmetric vs asymmetric, threat vs defense). The Diffie-Hellman conceptual walkthrough may appear as a stimulus question — you do not need to do the math, but you should be able to describe it as: Alice and Bob each pick a private value, exchange public values, and arrive at a shared secret without ever transmitting it, exploiting a one-way function. For the cipher question, remember that A=0 and the modulo 26 handles the wrap.

Self-Check

  • Explain the key distribution problem of symmetric encryption. How does the Diffie-Hellman key exchange solve it without ever transmitting the shared secret over the channel?
  • Alice uses a Caesar cipher with shift 7 to encrypt the letter T. What is the ciphertext character? Show your calculation using modular arithmetic.
  • You receive an email from your bank asking you to click a link and enter your password. Identify the type of attack this likely is, and describe two defenses — one technical, one behavioral.
  • Compare symmetric and asymmetric encryption in terms of speed and use case. Why does HTTPS use both, and what role does each play in a TLS handshake?
  • Define defense in depth. Give one example of a system in which removing any single layer would still leave reasonable protection thanks to remaining layers, and one example in which removing a critical layer would expose the system completely.
