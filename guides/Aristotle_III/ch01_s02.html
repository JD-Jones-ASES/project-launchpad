Aristotle’s Metaphysics: A Comprehensive Study Guide

This study guide provides a detailed overview of Aristotle’s Metaphysics, exploring its accidental naming, its structural complexity, and its foundational role in Western philosophy. The guide covers the central inquiries of the text, its connection to Aristotle’s broader body of work, and key biographical details of the philosopher.

I. The Nature and Origin of the Metaphysics

The Accidental Title

The term "metaphysics" was not coined by Aristotle himself. Its origin is organizational rather than descriptive:

  • Etymology: Derived from the Greek ta meta ta physika, meaning "the things after the Physics."
  • Origin: Following Aristotle's death in 322 BC, editors organizing his manuscripts placed his treatises on fundamental reality after those regarding natural science (the Physics).
  • Meanings of "Meta": The prefix can mean "after," "beyond," or "about." All three interpretations have shaped how the field of metaphysics is understood today.

Aristotle’s Own Terminology

Aristotle referred to this area of study by two distinct names, reflecting its depth and its ultimate goal:

  1. Prote Philosophia (First Philosophy): This is the inquiry into "being as being," first causes, and the highest principles. It is "first" because it is the most fundamental study upon which all other sciences depend.
  2. Theologike (Theology): Aristotle used this term because the inquiry culminates in the study of the "Unmoved Mover"—the eternal, self-thinking intellect that serves as the first cause of all motion and being.

Composition and Structure

The Metaphysics is not a polished, systematic textbook but a collection of fourteen books, traditionally labeled with Greek letters (Alpha through Nu).

  • Nature of the Text: Compiled from lecture notes and research treatises written over several decades.
  • Internal Consistency: Because it was written across different stages of Aristotle’s career, some books repeat material or contain minor contradictions. It represents an ongoing inquiry rather than a finished system.
  • Translation: The standard scholarly English translation is by W. D. Ross (1924), noted for its philosophical precision.

II. The Core Books and Recurring Questions

While the work consists of fourteen books, five are considered the essential core of Aristotelian metaphysics:

Book Label Key Inquiry
Book I (Alpha) Survey of first causes through the history of philosophy; defines wisdom.
Book IV (Gamma) Defines "being as being" and defends the principle of non-contradiction.
Book VII (Zeta) Investigates the nature of substance (ousia).
Book IX (Theta) Explains the distinction between potentiality and actuality.
Book XII (Lambda) The "theological climax" arguing for the existence of the Unmoved Mover.

Central Philosophical Questions

The text is held together by a set of persistent inquiries:

  • What are the first causes and principles of everything that exists?
  • What does it mean for something simply to "be"?
  • What is substance (ousia)—the primary kind of being?
  • What is the relationship between potentiality and actuality?
  • Is there an eternal, unchanging, first principle of all motion?

III. Interdisciplinary Connections

Metaphysics serves as the foundation for Aristotle’s other major works, providing the technical framework for ethics, art, and logic.

  • Relationship to the Ethics:
    • The Ethics identifies happiness as the exercise of the soul's rational capacities. The Metaphysics defines the soul as the "form" of the body—the organizing principle that makes a living thing what it is.
    • The Ethics describes the highest human life as theoria (contemplation). The Metaphysics explains that this approximates the divine life, as the Unmoved Mover is pure thought thinking itself.
  • Relationship to the Poetics:
    • The Poetics uses the "four causes" (material, formal, efficient, and final) to analyze art.
    • Aristotle’s claim that the plot is the "soul" of a tragedy uses "soul" in the metaphysical sense: the formal cause and organizing principle of the work.
  • Relationship to the Rhetoric:
    • The Rhetoric assumes that reason can grasp reality. The Metaphysics (Book IV) defends the principle of non-contradiction, which is the necessary condition for all rational discourse and science.

IV. Biographical Context: Aristotle (384–322 BC)

  • Early Life: Born in Stagira; son of Nicomachus, physician to the Macedonian court.
  • The Academy: Entered Plato’s Academy at age seventeen, staying for twenty years as a student and teacher.
  • Royal Tutor: Served as the tutor to the young Alexander the Great in 343 BC.
  • The Lyceum: Founded his own school in Athens in 335 BC. It was known as the Peripatetic school because Aristotle reportedly taught while walking (peripatein).
  • Legacy: His work covered logic, biology, ethics, politics, and poetics, with the Metaphysics representing the culmination of his inquiry into the first principles of reality.

V. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What was the original meaning of the Greek phrase ta meta ta physika?
  2. Why did Aristotle refer to his inquiry as "first philosophy"?
  3. Which book of the Metaphysics is referred to as the "philosophical lexicon"?
  4. According to Aristotle, what is the "Unmoved Mover"?
  5. How does Aristotle’s metaphysical definition of the "soul" relate to his biological and ethical works?
  6. What is the significance of "little alpha" (Book II)?
  7. Define the relationship between the "formal cause" and the plot of a tragedy in the Poetics.
  8. Why is the principle of non-contradiction central to Book IV (Gamma)?

VI. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Accidental Discipline: Discuss how the organizational history of Aristotle’s manuscripts influenced the naming and subsequent interpretation of "metaphysics" in Western thought.
  2. The Foundation of Sciences: Analyze why Aristotle believed that "first philosophy" was the study upon which all other sciences depend. Use examples from his views on ethics and rhetoric.
  3. Potentiality and Actuality: Based on the recurring questions of the Metaphysics, explain why the distinction between potentiality and actuality is necessary for understanding the nature of being.
  4. The Divine Intellect: Explore the climax of the Metaphysics in Book XII. How does Aristotle’s concept of the Unmoved Mover bridge the gap between "first philosophy" and "theology"?

VII. Glossary of Key Terms

  • Lyceum: A gymnasium outside Athens where Aristotle founded his school; the origin of the term "Peripatetic school."
  • Meta: A Greek prefix meaning "after," "beyond," or "about."
  • Ousia: Frequently translated as "substance" or "being"; the central concept of the Metaphysics naming what makes a thing what it is.
  • Peripatein: Greek for "to walk about"; the source of the name for the Peripatetic school of philosophy.
  • Prote Philosophia: "First philosophy"; Aristotle’s term for the study of the most fundamental principles of reality.
  • Theologike: "Theology" or the "science of the divine"; the term Aristotle used for the branch of philosophy that studies the Unmoved Mover.
  • Theoria: Contemplation; identified in the Ethics as the highest human activity and in the Metaphysics as the nature of the divine mind.
  • Unmoved Mover: The first cause of all motion and being; an eternal, self-thinking intellect.
