# A Study Guide to Substance: Descartes’ Rejection of Forms and Aquinas’s Metaphysics of Being

This study guide examines a pivotal shift in Western philosophy: the transition from the Aristotelian framework of "substantial forms" to the modern mechanical view of René Descartes, and the contrasting medieval deepening of Aristotle by Thomas Aquinas. It covers the rejection of teleology in natural science and the development of the "real distinction" between essence and existence.

---

## I. Key Concepts and Themes

### The Cartesian Revolution: From Forms to Extension
René Descartes (1596–1650) fundamentally challenged the Aristotelian-Scholastic tradition that had dominated philosophy for two millennia.
*   **Rejection of Substantial Forms:** Scholastics used "substantial forms" to explain the nature and behavior of things (e.g., why fire rises or acorns become oaks). Descartes dismissed these as obscure and unscientific.
*   **Res Extensa:** Descartes redefined material substance by a single property: **extension** (length, breadth, and depth). In this view, the physical world is understood through geometry and the mechanics of motion rather than inherent essences.
*   **The Reduction of Causality:** Descartes eliminated **formal causes** (the "what" or pattern) and **final causes** (the "why" or purpose) from natural science. He recognized only the **efficient cause**—the mechanical push-and-pull of matter in motion.
*   **The Wax Argument:** Using a piece of wax, Descartes demonstrated that our knowledge of objects does not come from the senses (which perceive changing qualities like smell or color) but from the **intellect**, which perceives the unchanging underlying extension of the matter.

### The Thomistic Synthesis: Essence and Existence
Thomas Aquinas (c. 1225–1274) accepted Aristotle’s form-matter analysis but expanded it to include a deeper metaphysical distinction.
*   **Essence vs. Existence:** Aquinas distinguished between **essence** (*essentia*—what a thing is) and **existence** (*esse*—the act of being). For all created things, these two are distinct; knowing what a phoenix is does not tell you if it actually exists.
*   **Contingency and Participation:** Because a creature’s existence is not part of its essence, its existence must be received from an exterior agent. Creatures are "beings by participation."
*   **Ipsum Esse Subsistens:** This refers to God as "Subsistent Existence Itself." In God alone, essence and existence are identical. God does not *have* existence; God *is* existence.
*   **Divine Simplicity:** Aquinas argued that God is "altogether simple," meaning He is not composed of parts—neither quantitative parts, matter and form, nor essence and existence.

## III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Death of Teleology:** Compare the Aristotelian-Scholastic view of nature with the Cartesian view. Discuss the implications of removing "final causes" (purposes) from the study of the natural world. Does the elimination of purpose make the world more or less intelligible to the human mind?
2.  **Intellect vs. Sensation:** Analyze Descartes’ argument in *Meditation II* regarding the piece of wax. Why does he conclude that the wax is known by the "intuition of the mind" rather than the senses or the imagination? How does this support his broader project of rebuilding philosophy on "clear and distinct" ideas?
3.  **The Metaphysics of God:** Explain Aquinas’s concept of *Ipsum esse subsistens*. How does the distinction between essence and existence lead Aquinas to the conclusion that God is the "first efficient cause" and "altogether simple"? Contrast this with the way creatures receive existence.
4.  **The Modern Scientific Worldview:** The text suggests that modern scientists explaining an oak tree through genetic code are working within a Cartesian tradition. Evaluate this claim. In what ways does a mechanical/mathematical explanation differ from an explanation based on "substantial forms"?

## IV. Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Efficient Cause** | The agent or mechanical force that brings something about; the "push-and-pull" of matter. |
| **Esse** | The act of existing; existence itself as an act, distinguished from the essence of a thing. |
| **Essentia (Essence)** | The "whatness" of a thing; the definition or nature that makes a thing the kind of thing it is. |
| **Final Cause** | The purpose or end toward which a thing strives; inherent directions in nature. |
| **Formal Cause** | The pattern, structure, or "substantial form" that determines the essence of a substance. |
| **Ipsum Esse Subsistens** | "Subsistent Being Itself"; Aquinas’s term for God, whose essence is identical to His existence. |
| **Res Cogitans** | "Thinking thing"; Descartes’ term for the mind or mental substance. |
| **Res Extensa** | "Extended thing"; Descartes’ term for material substance, defined by its spatial dimensions. |
| **Substantial Form** | In Aristotelian philosophy, the principle that makes a material thing a specific kind of substance. |
| **Divine Simplicity** | The doctrine that God is not composed of any parts or any distinction between essence and existence. |
| **Contingency** | The state of a being whose existence is not necessary and depends on an external cause. |
