Study Guide: The Inheritors of Aristotle—Aquinas and Descartes

This study guide examines the profound influence of Aristotle's metaphysics on Western philosophy through the lens of its two most significant "inheritors": Thomas Aquinas and René Descartes. While both thinkers took Aristotle’s arguments seriously, they moved in opposite directions—Aquinas deepening and synthesizing Aristotle’s work with Christian theology, and Descartes challenging its foundations to build a new philosophy based on the sovereign thinking self.

I. Key Concepts and Comparisons

The divergent paths of Aquinas and Descartes define the central fork in the road for modern intellectual history.

Feature Thomas Aquinas (The Builder) René Descartes (The Challenger)
Philosophical Goal To synthesize reason (Aristotle) with faith (Scripture). To find absolute certainty through systematic doubt.
View of Aristotle Viewed him as "The Philosopher" to be embraced and perfected. Viewed his system as vague and unable to provide mathematical rigor.
Foundational Principle Gratia non tollit naturam, sed perficit (Grace perfects nature). Cogito, ergo sum (I think, therefore I am).
Nature of Reality A hierarchy of being, purpose, forms, and final causes. A mechanical world of two substances: mind and matter.
The Material World Populated by substances and potentiality. A vast machine governed by mathematical laws.

II. Thomas Aquinas: The Great Builder

The Recovery of Aristotle

During the Middle Ages, much of Aristotle's work—including the Physics, Metaphysics, and Ethics—was lost to the Latin West. It was recovered in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries via Latin translations of Arabic scholars (such as Averroes and Avicenna) and later from original Greek manuscripts. This recovery created an intellectual crisis, as scholars debated whether pagan philosophy could coexist with Christian faith.

The Thomistic Synthesis

Aquinas's primary achievement was the Summa Theologica, a synthesis that argued faith and reason are not in conflict.

  • The Unmoved Mover: Aquinas took Aristotle’s concept of the Unmoved Mover—an eternal intellect that moves the cosmos—and extended it. Through the lens of revelation, he argued this mover is also the God of Scripture who knows, loves, and redeems the world.
  • Grace and Nature: Aquinas maintained that reason can arrive at truths that faith then confirms and elevates. In his view, Aristotle’s conclusions were true but incomplete; revelation carried those conclusions to a higher fulfillment.

III. René Descartes: The Great Challenger

The Method of Systematic Doubt

Writing three and a half centuries after Aquinas, Descartes sought to rebuild knowledge from scratch. He was dissatisfied with the Aristotelian tradition, which he felt relied too heavily on deceptive sense perceptions and unverifiable concepts like "substantial forms."

  • The Meditations: In his Meditations on First Philosophy, Descartes systematically doubted everything—the senses, the physical world, and even mathematics—to see what remained certain.
  • The Cogito: He discovered that the act of doubting itself proved the existence of a thinker. This led to his foundational truth: Cogito, ergo sum ("I think, therefore I am").

Cartesian Dualism

Descartes reconstructed the world using only two kinds of substance:

  1. Res cogitans ("Thinking thing"): The mind or soul, defined purely by thought. It is distinct from the body and could theoretically exist without it.
  2. Res extensa ("Extended thing"): Physical matter, defined by spatial extension (length, breadth, depth). This view stripped nature of "inner purposes" or "forms," treating the physical world as a machine governed by mechanical and mathematical laws.

IV. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Who were the two Arabic scholars primarily responsible for transmitting Aristotle’s work back to Western Europe?
  2. What specific title did medieval scholars, including Aquinas, use to refer to Aristotle?
  3. Translate and explain the significance of the phrase gratia non tollit naturam, sed perficit.
  4. How does Aristotle’s "Unmoved Mover" differ from Aquinas’s conception of God?
  5. Why was Descartes dissatisfied with the Aristotelian philosophy he learned from the Jesuits?
  6. What is the "one certainty" that survives Descartes’ method of systematic doubt?
  7. In the Cartesian worldview, how is res extensa (the physical world) different from Aristotle's view of reality?
  8. What is the difference between a "Thomistic" idea and a "Cartesian" idea?

V. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Crisis of Integration: Analyze why the arrival of Aristotle’s full corpus created a "crisis" in medieval Christendom. How did Aquinas use the relationship between faith and reason to resolve this tension?
  2. The Shift to the Self: Contrast Aristotle’s foundation for knowledge (the world outside the mind) with Descartes’ foundation (the thinking self within). How does this shift change the requirements for "genuine knowledge"?
  3. Mechanism vs. Purpose: Compare the physical world described by Descartes (res extensa) with the world described by Aristotle. Discuss the implications of viewing nature as a "vast machine" versus a "hierarchy of forms pursuing ends."
  4. The Legacy of Inheritors: Evaluate the statement that Aquinas and Descartes define the "fork in the road" for modern thought. Which thinker’s approach do you find more persuasive in explaining the nature of reality?

VI. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Averroes (Ibn Rushd): An Arabic commentator from Córdoba whose works were vital in transmitting Aristotle to the medieval West; known as "The Commentator."
  • Avicenna (Ibn Sīnā): A Persian philosopher and physician whose Aristotelian-influenced works shaped the medieval European reception of philosophy.
  • Cartesian: Relating to the philosophy or methods of René Descartes.
  • Cogito, ergo sum: "I think, therefore I am"; the foundational certainty of Descartes' philosophy.
  • Meditations on First Philosophy: Descartes’ 1641 masterwork focused on systematic doubt and the rebuilding of knowledge.
  • Res extensa: "Extended thing"; Descartes’ term for physical matter, defined by its existence in space.
  • Res cogitans: "Thinking thing"; Descartes’ term for the mind or soul, defined purely by the activity of thought.
  • Summa Theologica: Aquinas’s comprehensive synthesis of Christian theology and Aristotelian philosophy.
  • Thomistic (or Thomist): Relating to the philosophy or theological system developed by Thomas Aquinas.
  • Unmoved Mover: Aristotle’s concept of an eternal intellect that moves the universe by being an object of desire; a concept later adapted by Aquinas.
