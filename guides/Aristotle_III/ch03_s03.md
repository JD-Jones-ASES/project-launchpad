# Study Guide: Aristotle on Being and the Principle of Non-Contradiction

This study guide explores the foundational concepts of Aristotelian metaphysics as presented in Book IV of the *Metaphysics*. It covers the definition of "first philosophy," the primary role of substance, and the logical principles that ground all meaningful thought and reality.

---

### Key Concepts and Themes

#### 1. The Science of Being as Being
Aristotle identifies a unique science, distinct from specialized disciplines like physics or mathematics, which investigates "being as being" (*on he on*). While special sciences "cut off" a part of reality to study specific attributes (e.g., motion or quantity), this "first philosophy" or "theology" examines the universal framework of all existing things. It seeks the first principles and highest causes that belong to things simply because they exist.

#### 2. The Unity of Being and the Primacy of Substance
Aristotle famously asserts that "being is said in many ways" (*to on legetai pollachos*). A quality, a relation, and a quantity are all "beings," but they are not beings in the same sense. However, these various senses are not merely ambiguous; they all refer to one central point: **Substance** (*ousia*). 
*   **The Primary Sense:** Substance is the fundamental kind of being upon which all other categories (qualities, accidents, processes) depend.
*   **The Focus of Philosophy:** Because all senses of being refer back to substance, the science of being as being is essentially the science of substance.

#### 3. The Principle of Non-Contradiction (PNC)
Aristotle identifies the PNC as the most certain of all principles and the starting point for all axioms. It states: **The same attribute cannot both belong and not belong to the same subject at the same time and in the same respect.**
*   **Indemonstrability:** The PNC cannot be proven through traditional demonstration because there is no more fundamental truth from which to derive it. Attempting to prove everything leads to an infinite regress.
*   **Negative Demonstration (Retorsion):** Aristotle defends the PNC by showing that anyone who speaks and intends to mean something definite has already assumed the principle. If a word means everything, it means nothing, and thought becomes impossible.

#### 4. The Law of Excluded Middle (LEM)
Complementary to the PNC, the LEM states that there can be no intermediate between contradictories. For any given predicate, one must either affirm or deny it of a subject.
*   **Truth and Falsity:** To say of what is that it is not, or of what is not that it is, is false. To say of what is that it is, or of what is not that it is not, is true.
*   **Metaphysical Implication:** The LEM ensures that reality is determinate. Without it, things would hover in an indeterminate zone, making all statements simultaneously true and false.

### Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **Who was Averroes, and why was he called "The Commentator"?**
2.  **How does "first philosophy" differ from "special sciences" like physics or biology?**
3.  **Explain the phrase "Being is said in many ways." How does Aristotle unify these meanings?**
4.  **What is the "Principle of Non-Contradiction"? State it in its formal version.**
5.  **Why does Aristotle argue that the Principle of Non-Contradiction cannot be proven by standard demonstration?**
6.  **What is "negative demonstration" or "retorsion" in the context of defending the PNC?**
7.  **According to Aristotle, what happens to human speech and thought if the PNC is denied?**
8.  **How does Aristotle use the example of a man walking to Megara to refute those who deny the PNC?**
9.  **What is the "Law of Excluded Middle"?**
10. **How did the Heracliteans, such as Cratylus, view the possibility of making true statements about the world?**
11. **Why does Aristotle criticize the view that knowledge is merely sensation?**
12. **In what way does the denial of the PNC "do away with substance and essence"?**
13. **According to Aristotle, what is the difference between an "essential" attribute and an "accidental" one?**
14. **How does Aristotle respond to the Protagorean view that "all appearances are true"?**
15. **What is the significance of "potentiality" in Aristotle’s response to Anaxagoras’s idea that "all is mixed in all"?**

### Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

*   **The Defender of Reason:** Analyze Aristotle’s defense of the Principle of Non-Contradiction. Why does he consider the "opponent" who refuses to speak to be no better than a "vegetable"? Discuss how the act of meaningful communication itself serves as a proof for the laws of logic.
*   **Substance as the Anchor of Reality:** Explain why Aristotle moves from the study of "being as being" to the study of "substance." How does the distinction between substance and accident prevent an infinite regress in predication?
*   **The Critique of Relativism:** Aristotle argues that those who deny the PNC and the LEM ultimately make all things relative to the perceiver. Evaluate his arguments against Protagoras and the "natural philosophers" who equated truth with sensible appearance.
*   **Unity and Diversity in Metaphysics:** Explore the tension between the "many ways" being is said and the "single science" Aristotle proposes. How does the analogy of "health" or "the medical art" help explain the unity of the science of being?

### Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Accident** | A property that belongs to a substance but is not part of its essence (e.g., being musical or white). |
| **Averroes (Ibn Rushd)** | A 12th-century Islamic philosopher known as "The Commentator" whose Latin translations reintroduced Aristotle's *Metaphysics* to the West. |
| **Being as Being (*on he on*)** | The study of the universal attributes of existing things, rather than the specific attributes studied by specialized sciences. |
| **Essence** | The defining nature of a thing; that which makes a thing what it is essentially (e.g., "two-footed animal" for a man). |
| **First Philosophy** | Also called "theology" or *prote philosophia*; the science that investigates the first principles and highest causes of reality. |
| **Law of Excluded Middle** | The principle that there is no intermediate between contradictories; a statement must be either true or false. |
| **Principle of Non-Contradiction** | The most certain axiom stating that a subject cannot both possess and not possess the same attribute at the same time and in the same respect. |
| **Retorsion** | A method of defense that turns an opponent's denial back on itself, showing the denial presupposes the very principle being denied. |
| **Substance (*ousia*)** | The primary sense of being; the independent reality to which all accidents and qualities must belong. |
| **The Philosopher** | The title of honor given to Aristotle by medieval scholars. |
