Study Guide: Aristotle's Unmoved Mover (Metaphysics Book XII)

Overview

This section delves into Aristotle's profound concept of the Unmoved Mover, primarily explored in Book XII (Lambda) of his Metaphysics. It presents the culmination of his philosophical inquiries into the first causes and principles of being, arguing for the existence of an eternal, immaterial, and purely actual substance that serves as the ultimate source of all motion and change in the cosmos, moving everything not by force but as an object of desire and thought.

Key Concepts

  • First Causes and Principles: Aristotle's ultimate goal in Metaphysics is to uncover the fundamental origins and foundational truths that explain all existence. Book XII is the culmination of this search, identifying the Unmoved Mover as the highest cause.
  • Being as Being and Substance: First philosophy, or metaphysics, studies existence qua existence. Substance is identified as the primary kind of being, that which can exist independently, and thus is the main subject of this inquiry.
  • Form as Primary Sense of Substance: For Aristotle, the form (or essence) of a thing is what truly makes it what it is, distinguishing it from its matter. This concept is crucial for understanding the immaterial nature of the Unmoved Mover.
  • Actuality is Prior to Potentiality: A core Aristotelian idea that asserts that a thing's fully realized state (actuality) is logically and often temporally prior to its capacity to become something (potentiality). This principle leads directly to the idea of a purely actual being.
  • Pure Actuality (Actus Purus): The concept that the First Mover must possess no potentiality whatsoever. If it had any unrealized capacity or matter, it would require something prior to actualize it, contradicting its status as first. Thus, it is a being that simply and eternally is what it is, without change or development.
  • Regress of Motion and the Unmoved Mover: Aristotle observes universal motion and change. Every moved thing is moved by something else. To avoid an infinite regress of movers, there must be a first cause of motion that is itself unmoved. This "Unmoved Mover" is eternal and immaterial.
  • Causation by Desire/Thought (Final Cause): The Unmoved Mover does not cause motion through physical force (efficient causation). Instead, it acts as a final cause, inspiring movement in the heavens and all nature by being supremely desirable and intelligible, much like a beloved object draws a lover or a goal inspires action.
  • Three Kinds of Substance:
    1. Perishable Substances: Things that come into being and pass away (e.g., plants, animals, human artifacts). These are the subject of physics.
    2. Eternal, Moving Substances: The heavenly bodies (planets, stars), which exhibit eternal, circular motion. These are also part of physics.
    3. Eternal, Unmoved Substance: The First Mover, which exists apart from matter and motion. This is the exclusive subject of metaphysics.
  • Divine Life as Thought Thinking Itself (Theoria): Because the Unmoved Mover is pure actuality and the highest possible being, its activity must be the highest possible activity: pure intellectual contemplation (theoria). Since the highest object of thought is the Mover itself, its life consists of eternal self-contemplation, "thought thinking itself."

Important Terms

  • Metaphysics (Book XII/Lambda): The specific book within Aristotle's Metaphysics where the concept of the Unmoved Mover is most fully developed. Scholars refer to it by the Greek letter Lambda.
  • First Philosophy: Aristotle's term for the highest science, which studies being as being and seeks the first causes and principles of all things; later known as metaphysics.
  • Being as Being: The study of existence in its most fundamental and universal sense, not just specific kinds of being.
  • Substance: That which underlies accidents and can exist independently; the primary kind of being. In Aristotle's view, form is the primary sense of substance.
  • Form: The essential nature or structure of a thing, what makes it the kind of thing it is, distinct from its matter.
  • Actuality: The state of being real, fully realized, or in act; the fulfillment of potentiality.
  • Potentiality: The capacity or power of something to become something else, to change or develop.
  • Pure Actuality (Actus Purus): A being that has no unrealized potential, no capacity for change, and no matter. This describes Aristotle's Unmoved Mover.
  • Unmoved Mover (First Mover): The eternal, immaterial, purely actual being that is the ultimate cause of all motion and change in the universe, without itself being moved.
  • Efficient Causation: The agent or force that brings something about (e.g., a sculptor making a statue).
  • Final Causation: The purpose or goal for which something is done; the end toward which a thing strives (e.g., health is the final cause of medicine). The Unmoved Mover acts as a final cause.
  • Theoria: Greek term for contemplation, intellectual inquiry, or theoretical understanding. Aristotle considers it the highest form of human activity and the sole activity of the divine.
  • Ptolemaic Cosmos: The geocentric model of the universe, with Earth at the center and celestial bodies moving in nested spheres around it. Aristotle's cosmology, though earlier, also featured eternal circular motion of the heavens.

Detailed Notes

The Significance of Metaphysics Book XII (Lambda)

  • Culmination of Aristotelian Thought: Book XII is considered the peak of Aristotle's Metaphysics, drawing together central arguments from previous books:
    • Book I: The search for first causes and principles.
    • Book IV: The primary subject of first philosophy is "being as being," with substance as the primary kind of being.
    • Book VII: Form is identified as the primary sense of substance.
    • Book IX: Actuality is established as prior to potentiality, highlighting the fundamentally active nature of being.
  • The Ultimate Consequence: These prior discoveries lead Aristotle to conclude that there must exist a purely actual substance—a being entirely without potentiality, matter, or unrealized capacity. This being eternally is what it is.

The Argument for the Existence of the Unmoved Mover

  • Observation of Motion and Change: The observable world is characterized by constant motion and change (things coming into being, passing away, moving, growing, decaying).
  • Principle of Causation: Every instance of motion requires a cause.
  • Avoidance of Infinite Regress: If every cause of motion is itself in motion, this would lead to an infinite regress of causes, which Aristotle deems impossible.
  • Conclusion: Therefore, there must be a first cause of all motion that is itself unmoved. This is an eternal, immaterial substance. It is the root cause holding everything in existence and order.

Three Kinds of Substance (Reading 6.1)

Aristotle categorizes substances to frame his argument:

  1. Perishable Substances:
    • Examples: Animals, plants, artifacts.
    • Characteristics: Come into being and pass away; have matter and form; subject to generation and corruption.
    • Subject of: Physics (as they involve movement and change).
  2. Eternal, Moving Substances:
    • Examples: The heavenly bodies (planets, stars).
    • Characteristics: Exhibit eternal, unchanging circular motion; generally considered divine by the ancients.
    • Subject of: Physics (as they imply movement).
  3. Eternal, Unmoved Substance:
    • Examples: The Unmoved Mover.
    • Characteristics: Not merely eternal in motion but itself unmoved; purely actual; immaterial; exists apart from all other substances.
    • Subject of: "Another science" (i.e., Metaphysics), because it requires principles distinct from those that govern moving substances.

The Nature of the First Mover (Reading 6.2)

  • Pure Actuality (Actus Purus):
    • Reasoning: If the First Mover had any potentiality (any unrealized capacity, any matter that could receive a different form), then something prior to it would be required to actualize that potential. This would contradict its status as the first mover.
    • Implications: Therefore, the First Mover must be pure actuality—without matter, without potentiality, without any composition or internal change. It is eternally perfect and complete.
  • Mode of Causation - Final Cause, Not Efficient Cause:
    • The First Mover does not move things by physical pushing or direct intervention (efficient causation).
    • Instead, it moves all things as the object of their love and their thought. It is supremely desirable (the "real good") and supremely intelligible.
    • The celestial spheres, and by extension all of nature, are drawn towards its perfection, striving to imitate its eternal, perfect activity. Circular motion, being eternal and complete, is seen as the closest imitation possible for moving substances.
  • Necessity and Goodness:
    • The First Mover exists by necessity, meaning it cannot be otherwise.
    • Its necessary mode of being is inherently good, making it a first principle.
    • It is the ultimate source on which "depend the heavens and the world of nature."

The Life of the Divine (Reading 6.3)

  • Pure Activity: As pure actuality, the First Mover's existence is one of continuous, eternal activity. It cannot sleep, rest, or wait.
  • Highest Possible Activity: Given its supreme status, its activity must be the highest possible activity. Aristotle identifies this as theoria (intellectual contemplation).
  • Thought Thinking Itself:
    • Object of Thought: What does the First Mover think about? If it thought about anything less perfect than itself, its activity would be diminished. If it thought about the imperfect, it would change.
    • Conclusion: The highest possible object of thought is the divine intellect itself. Therefore, the divine life consists of thought contemplating its own contemplation. It is its own thinking.
    • Implications: This means the Unmoved Mover does not actively govern the cosmos, respond to prayers, or create/sustain the world in an interventionist sense. Its influence is through being the ultimate perfection that inspires and draws all things towards itself.
  • God as Living, Eternal, Most Good: Aristotle describes God (the Unmoved Mover) as a living being whose life is the eternal actuality of thought, a continuous and eternal state of supreme goodness and pleasure.

Review Questions

  1. How does Aristotle's foundational work in previous books of the Metaphysics (e.g., on first causes, substance, form, and actuality) logically lead him to the concept of a purely actual substance?
  2. Explain Aristotle's argument for the existence of an Unmoved Mover, beginning with the observation of motion and addressing the problem of infinite regress.
  3. What are the three kinds of substance identified by Aristotle in Book XII, and how does the Unmoved Mover differentiate itself from the other two categories?
  4. Describe how the Unmoved Mover causes motion in the universe. Why is this distinct from typical physical (efficient) causation, and what does it imply about the Mover's relationship to the world?
  5. What is the nature of the Unmoved Mover's "life" and activity? Explain Aristotle's famous conclusion that it is "thought thinking itself," and what this implies about its divine existence.
