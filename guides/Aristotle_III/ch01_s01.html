A Guide to Reading Aristotle’s Metaphysics

This guide serves as a comprehensive framework for navigating Aristotle’s Metaphysics. It outlines the structure of the text, the philosophical methodology employed by Aristotle, and key strategies for interpreting the dense, abstract nature of the inquiry into the structure of reality.

Core Concepts and Philosophical Framework

The Nature of the Inquiry

Unlike the Ethics or the Poetics, the Metaphysics operates at a high level of abstraction. Rather than examining the "good life" or the mechanics of tragedy, Aristotle investigates the structure of reality itself. This involves moving from the visible world to its invisible ground, progressing from the many to the one, and from the changing to the changeless.

The Big Picture: Book-by-Book Focus

The Metaphysics is not a linear treatise but a collection of fourteen books that circle a set of related problems from different angles.

Book Primary Philosophical Questions
Book I What is wisdom? What are the four causes? How did earlier philosophers approach first principles?
Book IV What is being as such? What is the most certain of all principles?
Book VII What is substance? What makes a thing the kind of thing it is?
Book IX What is actuality? How do potentiality and actuality explain change and being?
Book XII What is the first cause of all motion? What is the divine intellect?

The Dialectical Method

Aristotle utilizes a dialectical method rather than simply asserting a thesis. This process involves:

  1. Surveying Endoxa: Reviewing reputable or received opinions from common understanding or earlier philosophers.
  2. Refining and Correcting: Working through the strengths and weaknesses of these positions.
  3. Establishing a Position: Demonstrating that his conclusion is superior to the alternatives.

The Role of Definitions

Aristotle is a precise definer of technical terms. In the Metaphysics, the argument often hinges on specific definitions established early in the text. Confusion in later passages often stems from a lost grasp of these foundational definitions, such as ousia (substance), dynamis (potentiality), and energeia (actuality).

Methodological Habits for Success

To successfully navigate the "tortuous" and dense prose of the Ross translation, students should adopt the following habits:

  • Track Questions, Not Just Pages: Identify the specific problem Aristotle is trying to solve in any given selection.
  • Consult Footnotes and Glossaries: Use these tools to clarify unfamiliar terms or archaic translation devices before proceeding.
  • Expect Repetition: Recognize that because the work was compiled from lecture notes and research treatises over decades, some arguments may appear more than once or from slightly different developmental stages.
  • Focus on Syntax: When a sentence is particularly dense, identify the main verb and the main subject to untangle the meaning.
  • Distinguish "Being" from "Existence": Understand that Aristotle’s inquiry into "being" is not merely about whether something exists or is alive, but what it means for something to be and what makes it the specific kind of thing it is.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does the Metaphysics differ from Aristotle’s other works like the Ethics?
  2. What is the "dialectical method," and why does it often feel like a "long detour"?
  3. What transition does the "overall map" of the Metaphysics follow regarding the visible and invisible worlds?
  4. Why is the W. D. Ross translation preferred for this study, despite its density?
  5. According to Book IX, what two concepts are used to explain change and being?
  6. What should a reader do if they feel confused three paragraphs into a passage?
  7. What are the primary questions addressed in Book XII?
  8. Why do some arguments in the Metaphysics appear more than once?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Architecture of Reality

Analyze Aristotle's progression from the "many to the one." How does the structure of the Metaphysics—moving from the history of philosophy in Book I to the first cause of motion in Book XII—reflect his goal of finding the "first principles of everything"?

2. Substance and Identity

In Book VII, Aristotle asks what makes a thing the "kind of thing it is." Contrast this inquiry with a modern, colloquial understanding of "being" as mere existence. How does Aristotle’s focus on substance (ousia) change the scope of metaphysical inquiry?

3. Potentiality versus Actuality

Using the example of the acorn and the tree, explain the relationship between dynamis and energeia. How does this distinction allow Aristotle to provide a rational account of change without abandoning the search for changeless principles?

4. The Value of the Dialectical Approach

Critique Aristotle's use of endoxa. Is starting with "received opinions" an effective way to reach objective truth about reality, or does it tether the philosopher too closely to the errors of the past?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Dynamis (Potentiality): The capacity or potential for a thing to become something else; for example, an acorn is potentially a tree.
  • Endoxa: Reputable or received opinions held by the majority or by the wise. These serve as the starting point for Aristotle's dialectical inquiries.
  • Energeia (Actuality): The state of being fully realized or "at work." It is the state toward which potentiality strives (e.g., the full-grown tree).
  • Metaphysics: The study of the structure of reality itself and the first principles of all things.
  • Ousia: Frequently translated as "substance"; the core of what makes a thing the specific kind of thing it is.
  • Ross Translation: A 1924 translation by W. D. Ross noted for its philosophical precision and fidelity to the original Greek.
