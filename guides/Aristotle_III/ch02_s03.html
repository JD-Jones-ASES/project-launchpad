Study Guide: Aristotle’s First Philosophy and the Nature of Wisdom

This study guide examines the foundational concepts of Aristotle’s Metaphysics, specifically focusing on the inherent human desire for knowledge, the hierarchy of understanding, the definition of wisdom as the study of first causes, and the cooperative nature of philosophical inquiry.

Key Concepts Overview

1. The Natural Desire to Know

Aristotle posits that human beings are defined by a constitutional drive toward understanding. This desire is not merely for practical utility but is an essential aspect of human nature. The delight taken in the senses—especially sight—serves as evidence of this, as humans value perception for its own sake, independent of its usefulness for action.

2. The Hierarchy of Knowledge

Aristotle traces a progression of cognitive development that distinguishes humans from other animals and identifies the transition from basic perception to the highest forms of wisdom:

  • Sensation: The most primitive form of knowledge, common to all animals.
  • Memory: Arises from sensation in some animals, allowing for intelligence and the ability to be taught (particularly in those that can hear).
  • Experience: Formed when several memories of the same thing produce a single capacity for judgment. Experience deals with individuals (e.g., knowing that a specific treatment helped Socrates).
  • Art (Techne): Arises when a universal judgment is produced from many experiences. Art deals with universals (e.g., knowing that a treatment helps all people of a certain constitution).
  • Science and Wisdom: The highest levels, focusing on the "why" and the first principles of all things.

3. The Definition of Wisdom

Wisdom (Sophos) is not a practical craft or trade. It is the most universal and difficult knowledge because it is furthest from the senses. Aristotle defines wisdom as the knowledge of first causes and principles. While the man of experience knows that a thing is so, the wise man knows why it is so.

4. The Four-Cause Framework

Aristotle argues that a complete explanation of any thing requires four distinct types of causes. This framework provides the metaphysical foundation for understanding both nature and human art.

Cause Type Definition
Material Cause Matter / Substratum That out of which a thing comes to be (e.g., the bronze of a statue).
Formal Cause Essence / Substance The definition or shape that makes a thing what it is (the ousia).
Efficient Cause Source of Change The agent or process that brings the thing into being (e.g., the sculptor).
Final Cause Purpose / Good The telos, or the end for which a thing exists (e.g., the reason the statue was made).

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why does Aristotle consider the sense of sight to be the most beloved of the senses? It is preferred because it brings many differences between things to light and is valued for itself, even when no action is intended.

  2. According to Aristotle, what is the primary difference between "experience" and "art"? Experience is knowledge of individuals and knowing that something is the case. Art is knowledge of universals and knowing the cause or the why.

  3. Why is the "man of art" considered wiser than the "man of experience"? The man of art is considered wiser because he knows the causes of what is done and can teach others, whereas the man of mere experience cannot teach the underlying principles.

  4. In what historical conditions did the theoretical sciences, such as mathematics, first arise? They arose in places where men had leisure, specifically in Egypt, where the priestly caste was permitted time for non-utilitarian study.

  5. List three characteristics Aristotle attributes to the "wise man." (1) He knows all things as far as possible (universal knowledge); (2) He can learn difficult things far from the senses; (3) He is capable of teaching causes.

  6. What is the "bat’s eyes" analogy used to describe in Reading 2.5? It describes the difficulty of human reason when faced with things that are most evident by nature; our minds are dazzled by the "blaze of day" (ultimate truths) not because the truth is obscure, but because our reason is limited.

  7. How does Aristotle view the contributions of his predecessors, such as Thales or Plato? He views them as contributors to the accumulation of truth who identified partial causes (e.g., Thales identified the material cause), serving as a necessary foundation for his own synthesis.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Paradox of "Useless" Knowledge: Aristotle suggests that wisdom is the most "useless" knowledge because it is not pursued for its results or utility, yet he considers it the most important and authoritative science. Explain how Aristotle justifies the pursuit of knowledge for its own sake and why he believes the "superior science" must not be ancillary to others.

  2. The Four Causes as a Complete Explanation: Using an example from either nature or human production (art), apply Aristotle's four causes to provide a "complete explanation" of that object. Discuss why omitting any one of the four causes results in an incomplete understanding of the object’s reality.

  3. Philosophy as a Cooperative Enterprise: Analyze Aristotle's reflection on the study of truth in Metaphysics Book II. How does his view of philosophical inquiry as a "union of all" across generations contrast with the image of the solitary thinker? Discuss the role of "inferior" or "superficial" views in the development of human thought according to the text.

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Art (Techne): A stage of knowledge where universal judgments are formed from multiple experiences; characterized by an understanding of causes and the ability to teach.
  • Efficient Cause: The source of change or the agent that produces an effect; the third of Aristotle's four causes.
  • Experience (Empeiria): Knowledge derived from multiple memories of the same thing, focused on individuals and the "that" rather than the "why."
  • Final Cause (Telos): The purpose, end, or "the good" for which a thing is done or exists; the fourth of Aristotle's four causes.
  • Formal Cause: The essence, definition, or substance (ousia) of a thing; the "why" reducible to the definition.
  • Material Cause: The physical matter or substratum from which a thing is composed.
  • Sensation: The basic faculty of perception animals are born with; the foundation from which memory and higher knowledge grow.
  • Substance (Ousia): The primary category of being; that which exists in itself rather than in another; often linked to the formal cause.
  • Universal: A judgment or principle that applies to an entire class of objects rather than a single individual.
  • Wisdom (Sophos): The highest form of knowledge; the science that investigates the first principles and first causes of all things.
