Study Guide: Looking Ahead: Descartes and Kant

Overview

This section provides a summary of René Descartes' Meditations on First Philosophy and introduces Immanuel Kant's revolutionary contributions to philosophy. It explores Descartes' journey from radical doubt to certainty about the thinking self, God, and the external world, establishing the rationalist tradition. It then presents Kant's "Copernican Revolution," which, in response to David Hume's skepticism, argues that the mind actively structures experience, fundamentally altering how we understand knowledge and metaphysics.

Key Concepts

  • Descartes' Method of Radical Doubt: A systematic process of questioning and stripping away all beliefs that can possibly be doubted to find an indubitable foundation for knowledge.
  • Cogito Ergo Sum ("I think, therefore I am"): Descartes' foundational certainty, asserting that the very act of doubting or thinking proves the existence of the self as a thinking entity.
  • Res Cogitans (Thinking Thing): Descartes' conclusion that the essential nature of the self is not the body, but a non-physical mind or thinking substance.
  • Wax Argument: An illustration used by Descartes to show that knowledge of the essential nature of things (like the extension of wax) comes from the intellect's grasp, not from mutable sensory perceptions.
  • Mind-Body Dualism: Descartes' theory that the mind (a thinking, non-extended substance) and the body (an extended, non-thinking substance) are distinct entities.
  • Rationalism: A philosophical tradition, exemplified by Descartes, Spinoza, and Leibniz, that holds pure reason, independent of sensory experience, as the primary source of genuine knowledge.
  • Kant's Copernican Revolution: Kant's fundamental shift in philosophy, comparing it to Copernicus's astronomical revolution. Instead of assuming the mind must conform to objects to gain knowledge, Kant proposed that objects of experience conform to the mind's way of experiencing them.
  • Forms of Intuition (Space and Time): For Kant, space and time are not features of the world "in itself" but are inherent structures or frameworks of the human mind through which all sensory data is received and organized.
  • Categories of Understanding: Innate mental concepts or structures (such as causation, substance, necessity) that the mind actively applies to the raw data of sensation to organize it into a coherent, intelligible world of objects and events.

Important Terms

  • Evil Genius (malin génie): In Descartes' Meditations, a hypothetical, all-powerful, and malicious deceiver whose sole purpose is to make the meditator believe false things, even about mathematics. It is a thought experiment to push doubt to its absolute maximum.
  • Cogito: Latin for "I think." Part of Descartes' famous phrase cogito ergo sum, signifying the undeniable existence of the thinking self.
  • Res Cogitans: Latin for "a thinking thing." Descartes' term for the essential nature of the self, distinct from the body.
  • Rationalist Tradition: A school of thought in 17th and 18th-century philosophy, primarily in continental Europe, emphasizing reason and innate ideas as the primary source of knowledge. Key figures include Descartes, Spinoza, and Leibniz.
  • Copernican Revolution (in Philosophy): Kant's term for his philosophical paradigm shift, where the mind is no longer seen as passively receiving knowledge but actively constructing the structure of experience.
  • Forms of Intuition: Kant's concept that space and time are not properties of objects themselves but are the fundamental, innate structures of our perception that organize all sensory input.
  • Categories of Understanding: Kant's set of fundamental concepts (e.g., causality, substance, unity, plurality) that the intellect imposes on phenomena to make coherent experience possible.

Detailed Notes

I. Immanuel Kant (1724–1804): A Pivotal Figure

  • Biography: Born and died in Königsberg, Prussia. Studied mathematics, natural science, and philosophy. Became a professor of logic and metaphysics, known for his extremely regular habits. He never traveled far from his birthplace.
  • Key Works: Critique of Pure Reason (1781, 1787), Critique of Practical Reason (1788), Critique of the Power of Judgment (1790), Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals (1785), Prolegomena to Any Future Metaphysics (1783).
  • Philosophical Significance: One of the most influential philosophers in Western history. He argued that the human mind is not a passive receiver of reality but actively shapes experience through innate structures (space, time, categories). This "Copernican Revolution" reshaped subsequent philosophy across multiple fields.
  • Catalyst for Kant's Work: Reading David Hume's work "awakened him from his dogmatic slumber." Hume's skepticism about the rational justification of causation, substance, and God prompted Kant's lifelong effort to construct a philosophical response.

II. Descartes: The Full Picture

  • The Meditations on First Philosophy:

    • A concise, approximately sixty-page book meant to be read continuously, as intended by Descartes.
    • Structured as six days of solitary philosophical reflection, similar to a spiritual retreat, but with radically new content.
    • The goal is to dismantle all dubitable beliefs to discover what, if anything, remains certain.

  • Meditation I: The Method of Radical Doubt

    • Initiates systematic doubt by pointing out that senses can deceive.
    • Introduces the extreme hypothesis of an evil genius—an all-powerful deceiver—to question even fundamental truths like mathematics.
    • Concludes that everything, including the external world, one's own body, and even geometrical truths, appears uncertain.

  • Meditation II: The Cogito and the Thinking Thing

    • Discovers the foundational certainty: cogito, ergo sum ("I think, therefore I am"). Even if deceived, the very act of doubting or thinking proves the existence of the "I" that is thinking.
    • Concludes that the meditator's essential nature is a res cogitans (a thinking thing), a mind rather than a body.
    • The Wax Argument demonstrates that true knowledge of the wax's essential nature (its extension, flexibility, movability) comes from the intellect, not the changing sensory properties. This establishes extension as the defining characteristic of physical matter.

  • Meditations III and V: Arguments for God's Existence

    • Meditation III: Argues that the idea of a perfect being in the meditator's mind must have a cause at least as perfect as the idea itself; therefore, an actually perfect being (God) must exist to be its cause.
    • Meditation V: Presents the ontological argument, asserting that existence is a perfection. A being lacking existence would lack a perfection, thus not be the most perfect being. Therefore, God, as the most perfect being, must necessarily exist.
    • Both arguments depart from Aristotelian forms, deriving God's existence from the contents and necessities of the thinking mind rather than from observations of the physical world.

  • Meditation IV: The Problem of Error

    • Addresses why, if a perfect and good God created humans, they are capable of error.
    • Descartes' solution: Error stems from the misuse of the will, which extends beyond the intellect. We err when we affirm or deny something we do not clearly and distinctly perceive.

  • Meditation VI: Mind-Body Distinction and the External World

    • Establishes a real distinction between mind and body as separate substances.
    • Argues for the existence of the external world: The meditator has a strong natural inclination to believe in an external world as the cause of sensations. A good God, who would not be a deceiver, would not have instilled such an inclination if it were unreliable. Therefore, the external world exists.
    • The Meditations conclude by restoring belief in the world, but now with a rational justification.

  • After the Meditations:

    • Discourse on Method (1637): An autobiographical account of Descartes' development of his philosophical method, published with scientific essays (Optics, Meteorology, Geometry).
    • Descartes' contributions included inventing analytic geometry, unifying algebra and geometry, demonstrating the practical power of his new philosophy for scientific inquiry.

  • The Rationalist Tradition:

    • Descartes' work initiated the rationalist tradition, emphasizing pure reason as the primary source of knowledge.
    • Baruch Spinoza (1632–1677): Carried Descartes' framework to a monistic conclusion, positing one substance (God or Nature) of which minds and bodies are attributes.
    • Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646–1716): Rejected Descartes' dualism and Spinoza's monism, proposing a universe of infinitely many individual substances called monads.
    • These three philosophers form the core of the rationalist tradition, which Kant would later challenge and transform.

III. Kant's Copernican Revolution

  • Challenging Aristotle: Kant directly challenged Aristotle's view that human reason can know the ultimate nature of things (substance, being, first cause). Kant argued this was not possible in the traditional way.

  • The Influence of David Hume (1711–1776):

    • Hume, a Scottish empiricist, argued that all knowledge comes from sense experience.
    • He famously demonstrated that fundamental beliefs like causation, a persisting self, or an external world cannot be rationally justified through experience (we only observe sequence, not necessity).
    • Hume's empiricist conclusions led to the destruction of traditional metaphysics, jolting Kant from his "dogmatic slumber."

  • Kant's Response: Critique of Pure Reason (1781):

    • Kant accepted Hume's point that causation is not derived from sense experience.
    • However, he drew a radically different conclusion: if causation isn't from experience, it must be a contribution of the mind to experience.
    • The mind is not a passive recipient; it actively structures the raw data of sensation into an ordered world.

  • The "Copernican Revolution" Analogy:

    • Just as Copernicus showed that the apparent motion of the sun is due to the earth's motion, Kant argued that the apparent order and necessity of the experienced world is not a feature of "things in themselves" but a contribution of the mind that experiences them.
    • Before Kant, knowledge was assumed to be the mind conforming to objects. Kant reversed this: objects of experience conform to the mind's way of experiencing.

  • The Mind's Structures of Experience:

    • Space and Time: These are not properties of reality as it is "in itself," but are the forms of intuition—the innate frameworks through which the mind receives and organizes all sensory data. We can only experience things spatially and temporally.
    • Categories of Understanding: These are innate concepts (like substance, causality, necessity) that the mind applies to the sensory input, actively organizing it into the coherent world of objects and events that we experience.

Review Questions

  1. Describe Descartes' method of radical doubt as introduced in Meditation I. What extreme hypothesis does he use to push doubt to its maximum, and what does it lead him to conclude about knowledge?
  2. Explain Descartes' "cogito, ergo sum" and its significance as his foundational certainty. How does the "wax argument" in Meditation II further clarify his understanding of what he is, essentially?
  3. Summarize Descartes' main arguments for the existence of God presented in Meditations III and V. How do these arguments differ in their starting point from Aristotelian approaches to God's existence?
  4. What was David Hume's central challenge to traditional metaphysics regarding beliefs like causation and substance, and why did it prompt Kant to develop his own philosophical system?
  5. In what ways does Kant argue that the human mind actively structures experience rather than passively receiving it? Explain the distinct roles of "space and time" (forms of intuition) and the "categories of understanding" in his philosophy.
