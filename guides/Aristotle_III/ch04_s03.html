Study Guide: Aristotle's Metaphysics, Book VII - Substance

Overview

Aristotle's Metaphysics Book VII (Zeta) grapples with the fundamental question: "What is being?" He argues that this question ultimately reduces to "What is substance?" Through rigorous analysis, Aristotle surveys several candidates for what constitutes a thing's substance—matter, essence, universal, and genus—ultimately concluding that substance is primarily form, the defining structure that makes a thing what it is.

Key Concepts

  • Substance (ousia) as Primary Being: Aristotle's central claim that substance is the most fundamental kind of being. All other categories of existence (quality, quantity, relation, etc.) depend on substance and exist only derivatively because they are properties or accidents of substances.
  • Four Candidates for Substance: Aristotle systematically examines and evaluates:
    1. Substratum: The underlying matter or stuff from which a thing is made.
    2. Essence (to ti en einai): What a thing is by its very nature, stripped of all incidental properties, captured in its definition.
    3. Universal: A general concept or characteristic shared by many individual things (e.g., "humanity").
    4. Genus: A broader category to which a thing belongs (e.g., "animal" for a human).
  • Form (eidos) as Primary Substance: After considering the candidates, Aristotle concludes that the defining structure or pattern of a thing—its form—is its primary substance. The matter is merely the material vehicle for that form.
  • Essence (to ti en einai): Literally "the what it was to be," essence represents the core nature of a thing. It is expressed in a proper definition that states what a thing truly and necessarily is, distinct from its accidental attributes or its physical components.
  • Form and Matter (hyle) in Coming-to-Be: Aristotle's analysis of how things come into existence reveals a crucial relationship: matter is the "out of which" a thing is made, and form is the "into which" or the goal that the craftsman or nature aims at.

Important Terms

  • Ousia: Greek term for "substance" or "being." Aristotle's technical term for the primary kind of being that exists in itself, not as a property of something else. Often translated as "essence" in some contexts, but "substance" is more common.
  • Eidos: Greek for "form." In Aristotle's philosophy, this refers to the defining structure, pattern, or essence that makes a thing what it is. It is not necessarily physical shape but the intelligible structure.
  • To ti en einai: Literally "the what it was to be," translated as "essence." It is the formula that captures what a thing truly and necessarily is, stripped of all incidental properties.
  • Hyle: Greek for "matter." The physical stuff or substratum from which a thing is made.
  • Substratum: The underlying material or subject that remains constant through change and upon which properties are predicated. One of Aristotle's candidates for substance.
  • Universal: A general concept, property, or characteristic that can be predicated of many individual things (e.g., "redness" can apply to many red objects).
  • Genus: A broader category or class under which more specific species fall (e.g., "mammal" is a genus for "dog").
  • Propter se: Latin for "by its very nature" or "in itself." Aristotle uses this to distinguish essential attributes from accidental ones. The essence of a thing is what it is propter se.

Detailed Notes

The Central Question of Being and Substance

Aristotle's Metaphysics, particularly Book VII (Zeta), undertakes one of philosophy's most profound inquiries: "What is being?" He emphatically states that this overarching question reduces to "What is substance (ousia)?" This implies that understanding existence fundamentally requires understanding what it means for something to be a substance. This isn't merely a technical debate; the answer profoundly impacts our understanding of identity, change, knowledge, and even ethics and theology. Thomas Aquinas, a towering medieval philosopher-theologian, recognized and built upon Aristotle's framework, aiming to reconcile reason and faith.

Aristotle's Method: Surveying Candidates for Substance

Aristotle proceeds with a characteristic rigor, systematically examining four main candidates for what constitutes a thing's substance:

  1. The Substratum: This refers to the underlying matter of which a thing is made. For example, the bronze of a statue.
  2. The Essence (to ti en einai): This is the "what it was to be" a thing, its intrinsic nature or definition. For a human, it might be "rational animal."
  3. The Universal: This is a general concept or property shared by many individuals. For instance, "humanity" as a concept shared by all humans.
  4. The Genus: This refers to the broader category to which a thing belongs, such as "animal" for a human.

After meticulous argument, Aristotle arrives at a pivotal conclusion: substance is primarily form (eidos). This means that what truly defines a thing and makes it what it is, is its inherent structure or organizing principle, not merely the material it's composed of. A bronze statue is primarily a human figure or deity, not just a lump of bronze.

Substance as the Root of All Categories

Aristotle argues that substance holds a unique and primary status among all categories of being. While things can have qualities (red), quantities (large), relations (to the left), or exist in time and place, these attributes only belong to them because there is, first and foremost, a thing—a substance—to bear those properties. "Being" applies primarily to substance and only derivatively to everything else.

The text from Metaphysics, Book VII, Part 1, emphasizes this point:

  • Priority in Definition: The definition of a thing's substance must be present in the definition of any other predicate applied to it.
  • Priority in Knowledge: We know something most fully when we know what it is (its substance), rather than just its qualities or quantities.
  • Priority in Time/Existence: Other categories cannot exist independently; only substance can. You can't have "whiteness" without something being white.

Therefore, the ancient and enduring question "what being is" is essentially the question "what is substance?"

The Concept of Essence (To Ti En Einai)

Having established substance as primary, Aristotle delves deeper into one of its most critical aspects: essence (to ti en einai). This phrase, literally "the what it was to be," is fundamental to his metaphysics. It signifies the intrinsic nature of a thing, what it is propter se (by its very nature), before any particular circumstances or accidental properties are considered.

The essence is not:

  • The thing itself as a composite of form and matter.
  • A mere description of surface properties.
  • A combination of attributes that aren't inherently one (e.g., "white surface" is not the essence of a surface, because "white" is added to "surface").

Instead, the essence is what we capture in a proper definition—a formula that expresses what a thing truly and necessarily is. For Aristotle, only substances have essences, and specifically, only those things whose formula constitutes a true definition. This typically applies to species within a genus, not to accidental compounds (like "white man") or individual attributes themselves. An essence implies that elements are not merely predicated of each other but are intrinsically united.

Form and Matter in the Process of "Coming-to-Be"

Aristotle further clarifies the relationship between form and matter (hyle) by analyzing how things come into existence, whether by nature (like a plant growing) or by art (like a craftsman making a statue).

  • Matter (hyle) is the raw material, the "out of which" something is made (e.g., bronze for a statue, bricks and timber for a house).
  • Form (eidos) is the blueprint, the structure, the "into which" or the goal that guides the process of creation (e.g., the human figure for the statue, the structure for sheltering humans for the house).

In any process of "coming-to-be," the craftsman or nature is always aiming to impose a particular form onto matter. The form is the actualizing principle, giving specific identity and purpose to the indeterminate matter. This concept is central to understanding how physical objects, and indeed all natural entities, acquire their specific identities and functions.

Review Questions

  1. What is the central philosophical question Aristotle addresses in Metaphysics Book VII, and why does he equate it with the question "What is substance?"
  2. Describe Aristotle's method for determining what substance is. List the four candidates he considers and briefly explain why he ultimately concludes that form is primary.
  3. Aristotle argues that substance is primary in "every sense" (definition, knowledge, time). Explain what he means by this, using examples from the text to illustrate how other categories of being are dependent on substance.
  4. Define Aristotle's concept of "essence" (to ti en einai). How does it differ from a thing's accidental properties or its raw matter? According to Aristotle, what kinds of things have essences?
  5. Using the examples of a bronze statue and a house, explain the relationship between form (eidos) and matter (hyle) in Aristotle's philosophy, particularly in the context of "coming-to-be."
