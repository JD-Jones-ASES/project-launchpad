Aristotle’s First Philosophy: Being and the Principles of Reality

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the fundamental metaphysical concepts presented in Aristotle’s Metaphysics, Book IV. It explores the nature of "being as being," the primacy of substance, and the foundational axioms of logic and reality: the Principle of Non-Contradiction and the Principle of Excluded Middle.

I. Core Concepts and Themes

The Science of Being as Being (on he on)

Aristotle identifies a unique discipline he calls "first philosophy" (prote philosophia) or "theology" (theologike). While other sciences are restricted to specific domains (e.g., biology studies living things, mathematics studies numbers), first philosophy investigates what is true of all things simply because they exist.

  • Definition: The study of things in the capacity of their being, rather than their specific attributes.
  • Scope: It is a universal inquiry that seeks the most fundamental truths, such as the four causes and the governing principles of reality.
  • Clarity vs. Vagueness: Unlike specific sciences that gain clarity by narrowing their focus, first philosophy achieves precision by identifying universal truths that apply to every being.

The Primacy of Substance (ousia)

Aristotle addresses the potential problem that "being" is said in many different ways (e.g., a person "is," a color "is," a number "is"). He resolves this through the concept of focal meaning (pros hen equivocity).

Category Description Relationship to Being
Substance What exists in itself (e.g., Socrates, a horse). The primary sense of being; the focal point.
Dependent Categories Qualities, quantities, and relations (e.g., pale, fast, three). Real only by belonging to or existing through a substance.

The Science of Substance: Because all other forms of being depend on substance for their existence, the science of being as being is fundamentally the science of substance.

The Principle of Non-Contradiction (PNC)

The PNC is described as the "most certain of all principles." It is not merely a rule of thought but a fundamental truth about the nature of reality.

  • The Formulation: The same attribute cannot at the same time belong and not belong to the same subject in the same respect.
  • The Defense by Retorsion: The PNC cannot be proven through deduction because it is the presupposition of all proof. Instead, Aristotle defends it by showing that any person attempting to deny it must use meaningful speech. Since a meaningful word must signify something definite (and exclude its opposite), the speaker implicitly relies on the PNC the moment they speak.

The Principle of Excluded Middle (PEM)

This principle is the companion to the PNC and establishes the "law of bivalence."

  • The Formulation: For any statement, there is no middle ground between affirmation and negation. A thing either is or is not; it cannot hover in an indeterminate zone.
  • Knowledge vs. Reality: The PEM is a claim about the way the world is, not what we know about it. Even if we are ignorant of a fact, the reality of that fact remains determinate.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does "first philosophy" differ from "physics" or "mathematics"? Physics and mathematics restrict their inquiry to specific types of things (natural bodies or abstract quantities), whereas first philosophy studies "being as being"—what is true of all things simply because they exist.

  2. Why does Aristotle describe "being" as having a "focal meaning"? Because "being" is not used in exactly the same way for everything (univocity) nor is it a random coincidence of names (synonymy). Instead, every sense of "being" refers back to one primary center: substance.

  3. What are the three crucial qualifications Aristotle adds to the Principle of Non-Contradiction? The attribute must belong and not belong to the subject: (1) at the same time, (2) in the same respect, and (3) in the same way.

  4. Why is it impossible to provide a direct proof for the Principle of Non-Contradiction? A proof requires premises more certain than the conclusion. There is nothing more certain than the PNC, and any attempt to prove it would either be circular or collapse because the truth of the premises would rely on the PNC itself.

  5. What is "retorsion" in the context of Aristotle’s arguments? It is a philosophical technique where an opponent’s claim is shown to be self-defeating because the very act of making the claim presupposes the truth of what is being denied.

  6. According to the Principle of Excluded Middle, what is the status of a statement that we do not know to be true or false? The statement is still either true or false. The principle concerns the determinacy of reality, not the extent of human knowledge.

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Interdependence of Language and Reality: Aristotle argues that the moment a denier speaks a word with a definite meaning, they have committed themselves to the Principle of Non-Contradiction. Discuss the relationship between the structure of language and the structure of reality in Aristotle’s thought. Does the necessity of the PNC in speech prove its necessity in the physical world?

  2. Substance as the Anchor of Being: Analyze Aristotle’s claim that "the science of being as being is fundamentally the science of substance." Explain how the focal meaning of substance prevents the study of being from becoming a "chaotic collection of unrelated concepts."

  3. Challenges to the Principles: Aristotle’s historical targets were Heraclitus and Protagoras. Contrast Aristotle’s "world-first" approach to certainty with the perspectives of those who believe that contradictions can coexist or that "man is the measure of all things." How does Aristotle’s Principle of Excluded Middle address the idea of an "indeterminate" reality?

  4. Metaphysical Foundations: Compare the search for certainty in Aristotle’s Metaphysics with later philosophical movements (such as the "mind-first" approach mentioned in the text). Why does Aristotle believe that the ground of certainty must be found in being itself rather than in the internal mechanics of human thought?

IV. Glossary of Key Terms

  • Being as Being (on he on): The study of things solely in their capacity as existing entities; the subject of metaphysics or "first philosophy."
  • Excluded Middle, Principle of: The axiom stating that every meaningful statement is either true or false, with no third possibility; the world is determinate.
  • Focal Meaning (pros hen equivocity): A linguistic and metaphysical structure where various related senses of a word all refer back to one primary sense.
  • Non-Contradiction, Principle of (PNC): The axiom that it is impossible for the same thing to both be and not be in the same way at the same time.
  • Ousia: The Greek term for "substance"; that which exists in itself and serves as the primary category of being.
  • Prote Philosophia: "First philosophy"; the study of the most fundamental principles and the highest beings.
  • Retorsion: A defensive argument strategy that demonstrates how a position's denial presupposes the very principle it seeks to reject.
  • Substance: The primary kind of being that does not exist in another; the entity upon which all qualities, quantities, and relations depend.
