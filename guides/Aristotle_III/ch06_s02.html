Descartes and Aquinas on the Existence of God

Overview

This section explores two distinct philosophical approaches to proving God's existence: René Descartes' "inside-out" method, which begins from the certainty of his own mind, and Thomas Aquinas's "outside-in" method, which builds upon Aristotle's observations of the natural world. While both philosophers aim to establish God's existence, their arguments reveal a fundamental shift in the foundations of knowledge between ancient and modern thought, with Aquinas integrating natural reason with religious revelation.

Key Concepts

  • Internal vs. External Foundations of Knowledge: This is the core contrast presented. Ancient philosophy, exemplified by Aristotle and Aquinas, often began with observations of the external world. Modern philosophy, spearheaded by Descartes, shifted its starting point to the internal realm of the human mind and its ideas.
  • Descartes' Trademark Argument: An argument for God's existence positing that the idea of a supremely perfect, infinite being (God) found within the human mind could not have originated from an imperfect, finite human. Therefore, it must have been "stamped" there by God Himself.
  • Descartes' Ontological Argument: A second argument claiming that the very concept of a supremely perfect being necessarily includes existence as one of its perfections. To conceive of a supremely perfect being that does not exist would be a contradiction, much like conceiving of a triangle without three angles.
  • Natural Theology: The branch of theology that seeks to prove God's existence and attributes using only natural reason and observation of the world, without reliance on special revelation (like scripture). Aquinas significantly developed this.
  • Aquinas's First Way (Argument from Motion): One of Aquinas's Five Ways to prove God's existence, starting from the observable fact that things are in motion. It argues that everything in motion must be moved by something else, and an infinite regress of movers is impossible, thus necessitating a "First Mover" which is God.
  • Aquinas's Second Way (Argument from Efficient Causation): Another of Aquinas's Five Ways, based on the principle that everything has an efficient cause. Since nothing can be its own cause, and an infinite regress of causes is impossible, there must be a "First Efficient Cause," which is God.
  • Aristotle's Unmoved Mover vs. Aquinas's God: Aristotle's Unmoved Mover is the cause of cosmic motion but doesn't interact with or know the world. Aquinas accepts this as a starting point but argues that the God of Christian faith (who creates, knows, and loves) is a fuller, more complete understanding of this First Mover, accessible through a combination of natural reason and divine revelation.

Important Terms

  • Meditations on First Philosophy: René Descartes' seminal philosophical work, in which he presents his arguments for God's existence and the nature of reality.
  • "I think, therefore I am" (Cogito, ergo sum): Descartes' foundational indubitable certainty, establishing his own existence as a thinking being. This serves as the starting point for his "inside-out" philosophical inquiry.
  • Trademark Argument: Descartes' argument that the idea of God within him serves as a "trademark" or "signature" of God, implying divine authorship.
  • Ontological Argument: An argument for the existence of God based on the concept of God's being, claiming that existence is inherent to the definition of a perfect being. (Originally formulated by Anselm of Canterbury).
  • Clear and Distinct Perception: A key Cartesian criterion for truth; ideas perceived "clearly" (present and manifest to the attentive mind) and "distinctly" (precisely separated from all other ideas) are considered true.
  • Perfection: In Descartes' ontological argument, a quality that makes something greater or more complete. Existence is considered a perfection.
  • Natural Theology: The attempt to gain knowledge of God through natural human reason and observation, without the aid of divine revelation.
  • Summa Theologica: Thomas Aquinas's monumental work of systematic theology, which includes his famous "Five Ways" for proving God's existence.
  • First Mover: The ultimate uncaused cause of all motion, a concept central to Aristotle's and Aquinas's arguments for God.
  • Efficient Cause: That which brings something into being or causes a change. In a chain of causes, it is the active agent.
  • Potentiality and Actuality: Aristotelian concepts where potentiality is the capacity to be something, and actuality is the state of being that something. Motion is the reduction from potentiality to actuality.
  • Infinite Regress: A chain of causes or explanations that never ends, with each item depending on a preceding one, without any ultimate first cause or explanation. Both Aristotle and Aquinas argue against the possibility of an infinite regress in certain contexts.
  • Revelation: The disclosure of divine truth to humans, usually through scripture or direct communication, which surpasses what can be known by natural reason alone.

Detailed Notes

Descartes: God from Within

Descartes' approach to proving God's existence represents a radical departure from ancient philosophical traditions, particularly Aristotle's. While Aristotle began with observations of the external world (motion, change) to infer a First Mover, Descartes starts from the internal certainty of his own mind. His foundational truth, "I think, therefore I am," establishes his existence as a thinking being. From this sole certainty, he endeavors to prove the existence of God and, subsequently, the external world. This "turn inward" is a defining characteristic of modern philosophy, placing the human mind and its structures at the center of philosophical inquiry, influencing later thinkers like Kant.

The Trademark Argument (Third Meditation)

  1. Starting Point: Having established his own existence as a thinking thing, Descartes examines the contents of his mind.
  2. Discovery of the Idea of God: He finds within himself an idea of a supremely perfect being—a being that is infinite, omnipotent (all-powerful), omniscient (all-knowing), and perfectly good.
  3. Principle of Causality: Descartes asserts that every idea must have a cause, and this cause must possess at least as much reality or perfection as the effect (the idea itself). A less perfect cause cannot produce a more perfect effect.
  4. Application: Descartes, being an imperfect and finite being, recognizes that he could not have generated the idea of infinite perfection from his own limited resources.
  5. Conclusion: Therefore, the idea of a supremely perfect being must have been implanted in his mind by a supremely perfect being—God. This is likened to a craftsman leaving a "trademark" or signature on their work, indicating its origin.
  6. God as Non-Deceiver: From this perfection, Descartes concludes that God, being supremely perfect and without defect, cannot be a deceiver, as fraud and deception stem from imperfection. This is crucial for Descartes to later trust his clear and distinct perceptions of the external world.

The Ontological Argument (Fifth Meditation)

  1. Concept of a Supremely Perfect Being: Descartes argues that the existence of God follows necessarily from the very idea or concept of God.
  2. Analogy of the Triangle: He uses the example of a triangle: just as having three angles is an essential and undeniable property of a triangle (it's part of its very definition), so too is existence an essential property of a supremely perfect being.
  3. Existence as a Perfection: For Descartes, existence is a "perfection"—a quality that makes something greater or more complete. A being that exists is more perfect than a being that merely could exist or is only an idea.
  4. Necessity of Existence: Therefore, a supremely perfect being must possess all perfections, including existence. To deny God's existence, according to this argument, is to contradict the very concept of a supremely perfect being. It's as illogical as saying "there is a triangle without three angles."
  5. Clear and Distinct Conception: Descartes links the certainty of God's existence through this argument to the clarity and distinctness with which we conceive of God. He believes that if our minds were not cluttered with prejudices, we would know God's existence more immediately than almost anything else.

Aquinas: The God Who Creates and Loves

Thomas Aquinas, in contrast to Descartes, stands firmly within the Aristotelian tradition, building upon its framework to articulate a comprehensive understanding of God. He regarded Aristotle's argument for the Unmoved Mover as a pinnacle of human reason.

Aristotle's God vs. The God of Christian Faith

Aquinas recognized the profound insights of Aristotle's philosophy but also identified its limitations when compared to the God revealed in Christian faith:

  • Aristotle's God: Identified as eternal, immaterial, pure actuality, and the ultimate cause of cosmic motion. However, this God does not know the world, does not create (as the universe is considered eternal), and does not love or interact with human beings.
  • Aquinas's Fuller God: Aquinas integrates Aristotle's philosophical God with the God of Abraham. This God not only is the First Mover but also the creator of all things (ex nihilo, from nothing), who knows the world, and loves individual human beings with providential care.

Natural Theology and Revelation

Aquinas systematically distinguished between what human reason alone can know about God (natural theology) and what requires divine revelation.

  • Natural Reason (Natural Theology): Can establish that God exists, that God is one, eternal, immaterial, omnipotent, and the first cause of all things. Aquinas's Five Ways are prime examples of natural theology.
  • Revelation: Is necessary to understand truths such as God creating freely from nothing, the doctrine of the Trinity, the Incarnation of Christ, or God's personal, providential love for individuals. These truths transcend human reason and are known only through faith.

Aquinas's Five Ways (Aristotelian Structure)

In the Summa Theologica, Aquinas presents five ways to prove God's existence, each starting from an observable feature of the world and reasoning back to a necessary first cause.

  1. The First Way (from Motion):
    • Observation: We perceive things in motion in the world.
    • Principle: Whatever is in motion is moved by another. Nothing can move itself because something cannot be both in potentiality (capable of moving) and in actuality (moving) at the same time in the same respect.
    • Rejection of Infinite Regress: A chain of movers cannot go on to infinity, because then there would be no first mover to initiate the entire chain. If there is no first mover, there are no subsequent movers.
    • Conclusion: Therefore, there must be a First Mover, itself unmoved by another, and this everyone understands to be God.
  2. The Second Way (from Efficient Causation):
    • Observation: We find an order of efficient causes in the world (e.g., a parent causes a child, fire causes wood to burn).
    • Principle: Nothing can be the efficient cause of itself, as it would have to exist prior to itself, which is impossible.
    • Rejection of Infinite Regress: An infinite series of efficient causes is impossible, because if there were no first cause, there would be no intermediate or ultimate causes either.
    • Conclusion: Therefore, it is necessary to posit a First Efficient Cause, which is God.

Aquinas used these Aristotelian arguments to establish God as the ultimate cause, extending the idea to mean that this First Cause not only initiates motion but also gives existence itself to all things.

Comparison and Consequences

The contrast between Aristotle/Aquinas and Descartes is profound. For the former, knowledge of God begins with the objective reality of the external world and moves inward through reason. For Descartes, it begins with the subjective reality of the mind and its ideas, moving outward. This "turn inward" radically reshaped philosophy, shifting its primary focus to the human mind – its capacities, limitations, and structures of representation. This intellectual revolution laid the groundwork for later philosophical developments, notably Kant's "Copernican Revolution," which emphasized the mind's active role in shaping our understanding of reality.

The teacher notes provide critical insights:

  • Anselm's Ontological Argument: Descartes' version of the ontological argument is a revival of one first formulated by Anselm of Canterbury.
  • Kant's Critique: A significant later critique by Immanuel Kant argues that "existence is not a predicate" (i.e., not a property like "blue" or "tall"). If existence isn't a property, then a perfect being doesn't necessarily gain anything by existing, undermining the premise that existence is a "perfection."
  • Persuasiveness: To a skeptic, Aristotle's/Aquinas's arguments start from widely accepted observations (motion) but require accepting the impossibility of infinite regress. Descartes' arguments begin from premises about ideas having proportionate causes, which are less immediately obvious. Both types of arguments have faced centuries of defense and attack.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast the fundamental starting points for proving God's existence in Aristotle's/Aquinas's philosophy versus Descartes' philosophy. What are the broader philosophical implications of this difference, particularly regarding the focus of philosophical inquiry?
  2. Describe Descartes' "Trademark Argument" from the Third Meditation. What specific idea does he identify in his mind, and how does the principle of causality lead him to conclude that God must exist?
  3. Explain Descartes' Ontological Argument from the Fifth Meditation. How does he use the analogy of a triangle to support his claim, and why does he consider denying God's existence a contradiction within this argument?
  4. According to Aquinas, what are the strengths of Aristotle's concept of the Unmoved Mover, and where does it fall short in representing the God of Christian faith? How does Aquinas use the concept of "natural theology" to bridge this gap?
  5. Detail Aquinas's First Way (from Motion) and Second Way (from Efficient Causation) for proving God's existence. What observable phenomena do they start from, what principles do they rely on (e.g., rejection of infinite regress), and how do they conclude in God?
