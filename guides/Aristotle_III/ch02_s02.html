Study Guide: First Philosophy and the Responses to Aristotelianism

This study guide explores the transition from the Aristotelian framework of first causes to the divergent philosophical responses of René Descartes and Thomas Aquinas. It examines the shift from a world-centered confidence in human reason to the internal certainty of the mind and the external necessity of divine revelation.

Key Concepts and Core Themes

The Aristotelian Foundation

Aristotle’s Metaphysics established a framework that dominated Western thought for nearly two millennia. His system was built upon two primary claims:

  1. Knowledge of First Causes: Wisdom is defined as the understanding of the primary causes of reality.
  2. The Four Causes: A complete explanation of everything in the universe can be found through the framework of the four causes (formal, material, efficient, and final).

Aristotle’s approach was one of confidence in the senses. He believed that "All men by nature desire to know," and that human cognitive capacities—beginning with sensation and ascending through experience and art—eventually reach wisdom.

The Cartesian Shift: Doubt and the Cogito

In 1641, René Descartes sought to dismantle the Aristotelian tradition, not because its claims were necessarily false, but because they lacked absolute certainty.

  • The Method of Doubt: Descartes’ procedure involved systematically rejecting any belief that could possibly be doubted. This was not for the sake of permanent skepticism, but to find an indubitable bedrock for knowledge.
  • The Evil Genius: To push doubt to its limit, Descartes hypothesized a "malicious genius" (evil genius) who might be manipulating his senses and even his mathematical reasoning.
  • The First Certainty: Descartes discovered that even if he is being deceived, he must exist to be deceived. This led to the fundamental principle: Cogito, ergo sum ("I think, therefore I am").
  • Consequences of the Shift: Descartes replaced Aristotle's "world-to-mind" ascent with a "mind-to-world" descent. He eventually rejected formal and final causes, focusing instead on mind, matter, and efficient (mechanical) causation.

The Thomistic Synthesis: Reason and Revelation

Thomas Aquinas accepted Aristotle’s confidence in natural reason but argued that it was incomplete. He sought to integrate Aristotelian philosophy with Christian theology.

  • Sacra Doctrina (Sacred Doctrine): Aquinas argued that while natural philosophy is valuable, a higher wisdom—revealed by God—is necessary for human salvation.
  • The Necessity of Revelation: Aquinas maintained that revelation is required because truths reached through unaided reason take too long to discover, are reached only by a few, and are often mixed with error.
  • The Five Ways: Aquinas utilized Aristotelian metaphysics (specifically the concepts of motion, causation, and potentiality) to provide five logical demonstrations for the existence of God.

The Five Ways of Thomas Aquinas

Aquinas’s arguments for the existence of God, known as the Quinque Viae, are summarized in the following table:

Way Argument Source Summary of Logic Conclusion
First Way Motion Whatever is in motion is put in motion by another; an infinite regress is impossible. There must be a First Mover (God).
Second Way Efficient Causation Every effect has an efficient cause; a thing cannot cause itself; an infinite regress is impossible. There must be a First Efficient Cause (God).
Third Way Possibility and Necessity Contingent things (possible not to be) cannot always exist. If everything were contingent, nothing would exist now. There must be a Necessary Being (God).
Fourth Way Gradation Things are more or less good/true/noble relative to a maximum. The maximum of a genus is the cause of all in that genus. There must be a Most Perfect Being (God).
Fifth Way Governance (Teleology) Unintelligent natural bodies act for an end. They must be directed by an intelligent being. There must be an Intelligent Designer (God).

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why did René Descartes find Aristotelian philosophy "dissatisfied" despite having mastered it? Descartes felt that the Aristotelian method of starting from common experience led to "learned disputation" rather than genuine certainty. He believed that for knowledge to be valid, it must be grounded in something that cannot be doubted.

  2. What is the purpose of Descartes’ "Evil Genius" hypothesis? It serves as the most extreme form of doubt, allowing Descartes to question not just his senses, but the reliability of mathematics and the entire fabric of human reason.

  3. According to the Meditations, what is the only truth that cannot be doubted? The existence of the thinker. Because one must think in order to doubt or be deceived, the statement "I am, I exist" is necessarily true whenever it is conceived.

  4. What is sacra doctrina, and why does Aquinas believe it is necessary? Sacra doctrina is sacred teaching or revelation. It is necessary for salvation because the "end" (God) surpasses the grasp of human reason, and truths about God would otherwise be reached by too few people and with too many errors.

  5. How does Aquinas's view of the "Unmoved Mover" differ from Aristotle’s? While Aristotle stops at a divine First Mover that does not know or love the world, Aquinas identifies this being as the God of Scripture—the creator, sustainer, and lover of humanity.

  6. What was the pedagogical consequence of Descartes’ rejection of formal and final causes? The loss of final causation (teleology) led to a focus on mechanical causation and, according to modern critics like Alasdair MacIntyre, created a crisis in moral philosophy where the concept of "virtue" became incoherent.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Internal vs. External Starting Point: Compare and contrast the starting points of knowledge for Aristotle and Descartes. How does beginning with the "mind" rather than the "natural world" change the resulting philosophical system? Use the concepts of "ascent" and "descent" in your analysis.

  2. The Role of Doubt in Certainty: Analyze Descartes’ Method of Doubt. Is it a successful strategy for establishing a foundation for science, or does it create an unbridgeable gap between the thinker and the external world? Refer to the Meditations and the "Evil Genius" in your response.

  3. The Compatibility of Reason and Faith: Examine Aquinas’s argument in the Summa Theologica regarding the relationship between philosophy and sacred doctrine. How does he use Aristotelian logic to defend the necessity of divine revelation? Is his synthesis a deepening of Aristotle or a departure from him?

  4. Critiquing the Five Ways: Choose two of Aquinas’s Five Ways and evaluate their reliance on Aristotelian metaphysics (such as potentiality and actuality). Do these arguments provide a "proof" of God that would satisfy a modern skeptic, or do they require the acceptance of Aristotelian premises first?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Arche: The principle, beginning, or first cause that Aristotle seeks in the Metaphysics.
  • Cogito, ergo sum: "I think, therefore I am." Descartes' first certainty, established in Meditation II, proving his existence through the act of thinking.
  • Efficient Cause: The agent or action that brings something into being; the only type of causation Descartes retained from the Aristotelian framework.
  • Evil Genius (malin génie): A hypothetical, supremely powerful deceiver used by Descartes to test the limits of doubt regarding mathematics and reason.
  • Final Cause: The purpose or "end" for which a thing exists. This was a core part of Aristotle’s system but was rejected by Descartes.
  • Method of Doubt: Descartes' procedure of systematically rejecting any belief that could possibly be doubted to find a secure starting point for knowledge.
  • Quinque Viae (Five Ways): The five logical demonstrations for the existence of God proposed by Thomas Aquinas in the Summa Theologica.
  • Sacra Doctrina: "Sacred doctrine"; knowledge of God received through divine revelation rather than natural reason alone.
  • Sed Contra: "On the contrary"; the section of a Summa article where Aquinas states the position he intends to defend, often using an authoritative text.
  • Respondeo: "I answer that"; the main body of Aquinas's answer in a Summa article, where he provides his detailed philosophical reasoning.
