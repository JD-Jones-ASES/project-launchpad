Chapter 6, Section 1: The Unmoved Mover - Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces Aristotle's concept of an "Unmoved Mover" as the ultimate explanation for motion and change in the universe. It begins by categorizing substance into three kinds – perishable, eternal moving, and eternal unmoved – and then systematically builds an argument for the existence of the eternal unmoved substance. The section details the unique way this First Mover causes motion (as a final cause, "as being loved") and describes its activity as "thought thinking itself," while clarifying what Aristotle's God is and is not, especially in contrast to later theological conceptions.

Key Concepts

  • Three Kinds of Substance: Aristotle categorizes all existing things into perishable (e.g., plants, animals), eternal moving (e.g., heavenly bodies), and eternal unmoved (the First Mover itself). This establishes a hierarchy of reality.
  • Eternity of Motion: A fundamental premise for Aristotle, arguing that motion and time have always existed and will always exist, a point of divergence from creationist theological views.
  • Everything in Motion is Moved by Something Else: Aristotle asserts that for anything to transition from potentiality to actuality (i.e., to move or change), it requires an external cause already in actuality.
  • No Infinite Regress: To avoid an endless chain of movers that ultimately explains nothing, Aristotle argues there must be a first cause of motion that is itself unmoved.
  • The Unmoved Mover (akineton kinoun): This is the eternal, immaterial, and purely actual ultimate cause of all cosmic motion, standing at the highest level of reality.
  • Moves as Being Loved (kinei hos eromenon): The Unmoved Mover does not cause motion through direct physical interaction (efficient causation) but as a final cause – it is the supreme object of desire and intellect towards which all things in the cosmos strive.
  • Thought Thinking Itself (Noesis Noeseos): The highest activity of the Unmoved Mover is pure intellectual contemplation. Because it is the highest being, its thought must be directed at the highest object, which is itself, making its activity self-contemplating thought.
  • Aristotle's God (Distinct from Abrahamic Deities): It's crucial to understand that Aristotle's Unmoved Mover is not a creator, personal, providential, or interested in human affairs. Instead, it is the supreme good, ultimate reality, and the first and final cause of cosmic order.

Important Terms

  • Perishable Substance: Things that come into being and pass away, such as animals, plants, humans, and artifacts.
  • Eternal Moving Substance: Bodies that are eternal and in continuous, perfect motion, such as the sun, moon, planets, and fixed stars in Aristotle's cosmology.
  • Eternal Unmoved Substance: The highest form of substance, which is eternal and the ultimate source of all motion, but is itself completely motionless. This is the Unmoved Mover.
  • Unmoved Mover (akineton kinoun): Aristotle's term for the ultimate cause of all motion in the universe; it is eternal, immaterial, and purely actual.
  • Potentiality: The capacity for something to become something else; what something could be.
  • Actuality: The state of being real or in existence; what something is.
  • Efficient Cause: The agent or force that directly brings something about (e.g., a sculptor making a statue).
  • Final Cause: The purpose or end toward which something strives; that for the sake of which something is done (e.g., health is the final cause of medicine).
  • Infinite Regress: A chain of causes or reasons that extends infinitely backward, without a first or ultimate starting point. Aristotle argues against this for motion.
  • Pure Actuality (actus purus): A being that has no potentiality whatsoever, existing only as fully realized activity. The Unmoved Mover is pure actuality.
  • Noesis Noeseos (Thought Thinking Itself): Aristotle's description of the Unmoved Mover's highest activity – the divine intellect contemplating its own perfect intelligibility, where the thinking and the object of thought are identical.
  • Theoria: Intellectual contemplation or theoretical activity, which Aristotle considers the highest and most divine form of activity.
  • Cosmological Argument: A type of argument for the existence of God that begins with observed features of the universe (like motion, causation, or contingency) and reasons back to a first cause or necessary being.

Detailed Notes

Three Kinds of Substance

Aristotle begins his inquiry into substance by identifying three fundamental types:

  1. Perishable Substance: These are the familiar objects of our everyday experience, like animals, plants, humans, and artifacts. They have a beginning and an end in time, undergoing processes of coming into being, existing, and passing away. They are the focus of natural science.
  2. Eternal Moving Substance: This category refers to the celestial bodies (sun, moon, planets, fixed stars) in Aristotle's geocentric cosmology. They are eternal, meaning they never come into or go out of existence, and their motion is continuous, perfect, and circular. They represent an intermediate level of reality, demonstrating eternal motion, which implies an eternal cause.
  3. Eternal Unmoved Substance: This is the highest form of substance, the First Mover. It is eternal like the heavenly bodies but crucially, it is not in motion itself. It is the ultimate, unmoved source and cause of all cosmic motion.

The Argument for an Unmoved Mover

Aristotle's argument for the existence of the Unmoved Mover is a rigorous philosophical proof built on several key premises:

  1. Empirical Observation: There is undeniably motion and change in the world.
  2. Premise 1: Motion is Eternal.
    • Aristotle argues in his Physics that time is eternal and without beginning.
    • Since motion is inseparable from time, motion must also be eternal; there has never been a moment without motion in the universe.
    • Note: This premise puts Aristotle in direct conflict with later Abrahamic (Jewish, Christian, Islamic) theological views that affirm a creation in time by God. Medieval philosophers like Aquinas debated this point, often concluding that faith, not reason, reveals a temporal beginning.
  3. Premise 2: Everything in Motion is Moved by Something Else.
    • Nothing moves itself from potentiality to actuality without an external, already actualized cause.
    • Examples: a stone doesn't throw itself; an acorn doesn't grow into an oak without sun, soil, and water. Change requires an external actualizer.
  4. Consequence and Conclusion: No Infinite Regress.
    • If motion is eternal, and every motion requires a prior mover, there must be an eternal cause of motion.
    • Aristotle rejects the idea of an infinite regress of movers (a chain without a first link). Such a regress would fail to provide any ultimate explanation for motion.
    • Therefore, there must be a first mover – a cause of eternal motion that is itself unmoved.
    • This First Mover must be eternal, immaterial, and pure actuality, with no potentiality, as any potentiality would require another mover to actualize it, making it not truly first. This argument is a precursor to later "cosmological arguments."

The Unmoved Mover: How it Causes Motion

Aristotle's Unmoved Mover is distinct in how it influences the universe:

  • Not an Efficient Cause: It does not physically push, pull, or interact with the world like an efficient cause. If it did, it would itself undergo change, violating its "unmoved" nature.
  • A Final Cause: It Moves as Being Loved (kinei hos eromenon): The Unmoved Mover causes motion by being the supreme object of desire and thought. Just as beauty draws a lover without the beauty itself moving, the Unmoved Mover draws the entire cosmos toward it. All things in the universe strive towards it as their ultimate end or fulfillment.
  • This vision portrays the cosmos not as a mechanical system but as one ordered by aspiration and attraction towards the highest good and reality. It aligns with Aristotle's metaphysical principle of the priority of actuality, where the fully realized end draws the potential towards it.

Thought Thinking Itself (Noesis Noeseos)

The activity of the Unmoved Mover is also unique:

  • Pure Activity: As an eternal and purely actual being, the Unmoved Mover cannot be passive. Its existence is activity, and this activity must be the highest possible.
  • Highest Activity is Thought (Theoria): Aristotle argues that intellectual contemplation (theoria) is the highest, most self-sufficient, continuous, and pleasurable activity, both for humans (as discussed in Nicomachean Ethics) and, by extension, for the divine.
  • Object of Divine Thought: What does this perfect intellect think? It must think the best possible object. Aristotle makes the profound claim that the best possible object is itself. The divine intellect is the highest intelligible reality. Therefore, its activity is self-contemplation.
  • Noesis Noeseos (Thinking that has Thinking as its Own Object): This means the divine intellect's activity is both the act of thinking and the object of that thought. The distinction between mind and object collapses for a perfect intellect; the intellect is the intelligible. This identity of intellect and intelligible is the source of its supreme pleasure and fulfillment.

What Aristotle's God Is and Is Not

It is crucial to differentiate Aristotle's Unmoved Mover from typical theological conceptions of God:

  • What it IS NOT:
    • Not a Creator: The universe is eternal; the Unmoved Mover does not bring it into existence from nothing.
    • Not Personal: It does not know individual humans, respond to prayers, or intervene in human affairs.
    • Not Providential: It does not guide history, punish the wicked, or reward the righteous. Its perfect self-contemplation leaves no room for attention to "lesser things."
  • What it IS:
    • The Supreme Good: The ultimate reality and the highest possible being.
    • First and Final Cause: The ultimate origin of motion and the ultimate end towards which all things strive.
    • Pure Actuality: Without any potentiality, entirely realized.
    • Pure Thought/Intelligibility: Its essence is self-contemplating intellect.
    • Object of Cosmic Desire: The whole of nature is drawn towards it.
    • Supremely Happy: Engaged continuously in the highest activity with the highest object, fully self-sufficient.
  • Connection to Human Happiness: Aristotle's Ethics argues that the contemplative life (theoria) is the highest human happiness because it is most like the divine life. Thus, human philosophers pursuing intellectual understanding are participating in the highest form of existence.

Review Questions

  1. Explain Aristotle's three kinds of substance. How do they form a hierarchy of reality, and what distinguishes each kind?
  2. Outline Aristotle's full argument for the existence of an Unmoved Mover, detailing each premise and how they lead to his conclusion. What is the significance of his argument against "infinite regress"?
  3. How does the Unmoved Mover cause motion in the universe? Distinguish between its role as a "final cause" and the concept of "efficient causation."
  4. Describe what Aristotle means by "Thought Thinking Itself" (Noesis Noeseos) when referring to the activity of the Unmoved Mover. Why is this activity considered the highest possible, and what is the philosophical implication of the divine intellect thinking itself?
  5. In what ways does Aristotle's Unmoved Mover differ significantly from common conceptions of God found in Jewish, Christian, and Islamic theology? Discuss what Aristotle's God is and is not.
