Aristotle’s Metaphysics: Substance and the Doctrine of Hylomorphism

This study guide examines the core metaphysical inquiries presented in Aristotle's Metaphysics, specifically focusing on the investigation of substance, the relationship between form and matter, and the application of these concepts across various fields of study.

Key Concepts and Analysis

The Central Inquiry: What is Being?

Aristotle identifies the fundamental question of metaphysics as the nature of "being." While being can be described in many ways (qualities, quantities, and categories), these are all dependent upon substance (ousia). Substance is being in the primary sense; everything else exists only because it belongs to or depends on a substance. Therefore, the study of "being as being" is essentially the investigation of substance.

The Search for the Principle of Substance

Aristotle’s investigation is not a search for a list of existing things (like trees or rocks), but a search for the principle or cause of substance—the specific factor that makes something a substance. He evaluates four primary candidates for this role:

  1. The Substratum (Subject): The underlying matter that persists through change.
  2. The Essence (to ti en einai): The formula stating what a thing truly and necessarily is.
  3. The Universal: General concepts shared by many (e.g., "human being").
  4. The Genus: Wider categories under which species fall (e.g., "animal").

Aristotle eliminates universals and genera because they are properties of many things rather than individual, particular substances. He further concludes that matter alone cannot be the primary substance because matter is indeterminate. Form (eidos) is the definitive answer, as it is the defining structure that makes a thing knowable and definable.

Hylomorphism: Form and Matter

The doctrine of hylomorphism posits that every physical thing is a composite (synolon) of form and matter.

Element Description Function
Matter (Hyle) The physical stuff or substratum. The "out of which" a thing is made; passive and indeterminate.
Form (Eidos) The defining structure or pattern. The "what it is to be"; provides identity, knowability, and definition.
Composite (Synolon) The "whole together." The actual existing individual thing (e.g., a specific bronze statue).

Aristotle vs. Plato on Form

A critical distinction in Aristotle's metaphysics is his rejection of Plato's Theory of Forms.

  • Plato: Forms are separate, eternal, and transcendent realities existing in a realm beyond the physical world.
  • Aristotle: Forms exist in things. The form of a horse is what makes a specific horse what it is, and this structure exists within the horse itself, not in a separate realm.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What is the relationship between substance and other categories of being, such as quality or quantity?

    • Answer: Substance is primary; all other categories depend on substance for their existence. A quality (like "red") or a quantity (like "large") must belong to a substance to exist.

  2. Why does Aristotle rule out "matter" as the primary sense of substance?

    • Answer: Matter is pure indeterminacy and potentiality. Substance must be definable, and since form is what provides definition and character, matter alone cannot be the primary substance.

  3. Explain the term synolon in the context of hylomorphism.

    • Answer: Synolon means "the whole together." It refers to the composite of form and matter that constitutes a particular physical substance, such as a house made of bricks (matter) according to a plan (form).

  4. How does Aristotle define "death" using the framework of hylomorphism?

    • Answer: Death is the separation of form from matter. The matter (the body) remains, but the form (the soul or animating principle) is gone.

  5. What does it mean to say that "knowledge is the possession of form without the matter"?

    • Answer: Based on De Anima, when we perceive or understand an object, our mind or senses grasp the defining structure (form) of the object without taking in its physical material (matter).

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Priority of Form: Aristotle argues that form is "prior" to matter. Using the examples of a house or a bronze statue provided in the text, argue why the defining structure (form) is more fundamental to a thing's identity than the material (matter) from which it is constructed.

  2. Hylomorphism across Disciplines: Analyze how the form-matter distinction serves as the "backbone" of Aristotle's broader philosophical enterprise. Discuss its application in at least two of the following fields: Biology, Psychology, Ethics, or Epistemology.

  3. The Aristotelian Critique of Plato: Contrast Aristotle’s "immanent" forms with Plato’s "transcendent" Forms. How does Aristotle’s insistence that forms must be in things change the way we understand the relationship between the physical world and true knowledge?

Glossary of Key Terms

  • Composite (Synolon): Literally "the whole together"; the individual entity formed by the combination of matter and form.
  • Essence (To ti en einai): The "what it is to be" a thing; the formula or definition that states what a thing truly and necessarily is.
  • Form (Eidos): The defining structure, pattern, or organizing principle that makes a thing the specific kind of thing it is. It is the primary sense of substance.
  • Hylomorphism: The philosophical doctrine that all physical substances are composed of both matter (hyle) and form (morphe).
  • Matter (Hyle): The physical stuff or substratum out of which things are made; it is passive, indeterminate, and represents pure potentiality.
  • Prime Matter: Matter in its strictest philosophical sense, considered as pure potentiality with no form or determinate character whatsoever.
  • Soul: In Aristotelian psychology, the soul is the "form of the body," serving as the organizing principle that makes a living body active and alive.
  • Substance (Ousia): The primary kind of being; the underlying reality upon which all other categories of being depend.
