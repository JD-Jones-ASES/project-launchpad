Study Guide: Aristotle's Solution to the Problem of Change

Overview

This section introduces Aristotle's profound philosophical innovation: the concepts of potentiality (dynamis) and actuality (energeia). It addresses the ancient Greek philosophical paradox of change, posed by Parmenides (who denied real change) and Heraclitus (who asserted constant change). Aristotle's framework provides a middle ground, explaining change as a transition from potential being to actual being, thereby making ordinary experience comprehensible and laying the foundation for his ethics and metaphysics.

Key Concepts

  • The Problem of Change: The philosophical dilemma arising from Parmenides' view (no real change, only appearance) and Heraclitus' view (everything is constant change, no stability).
  • Potentiality (dynamis): A real capacity, power, or undeveloped state of being that a thing possesses to become something else. It is not nothing, but an incomplete mode of being.
  • Actuality (energeia): The realization, fulfillment, or exercise of a potentiality. It is the complete state toward which potentiality is directed.
  • Rational Powers: Capacities unique to human beings that are two-directional (e.g., medical knowledge can heal or harm). These require deliberate choice for their exercise.
  • Irrational Powers: Capacities found in nature that are one-directional (e.g., fire heats, ice cools). They are exercised automatically under the right conditions.
  • Proairesis: Rational choice or deliberate decision, essential for directing rational powers. It is central to Aristotle's ethics as the distinguishing mark of virtuous action.
  • Entelecheia: A specific form of actuality meaning "having its end within itself," signifying the complete fulfillment of a capacity or the full realization of a thing's purpose (telos).
  • Analogy (as a way to define actuality): Since actuality is too fundamental to be directly defined, Aristotle explains it through comparative examples (e.g., waking is to sleeping) to evoke an immediate intellectual perception.
  • The Priority of Actuality: Aristotle's claim that actuality is superior to potentiality in three fundamental ways: definition, time, and substance (or telos).

Important Terms

  • Parmenides of Elea: An ancient Greek philosopher who argued that real change is impossible, as being cannot come from non-being.
  • Heraclitus of Ephesus: An ancient Greek philosopher who held the opposite view, that everything is constantly changing and nothing is stable.
  • Potentiality (dynamis): The capacity or power for something to be or become.
  • Actuality (energeia): The realization, completion, or exercise of a capacity.
  • Proairesis: Rational choice or deliberate decision; the act of choosing to exercise rational powers in a specific direction.
  • Entelecheia: The complete fulfillment or full realization of a thing's inherent potential or purpose, meaning "having its end within itself."
  • Telos: The end, goal, purpose, or ultimate aim of a thing. For Aristotle, everything in nature has a telos towards which it is ordered.

Detailed Notes

The Ancient Problem of Change

Greek philosophy prior to Aristotle struggled with how to reconcile the apparent stability of things with the undeniable fact of change.

  • Parmenides' View: Argued that true change is impossible. If something changes, it must either come from non-being (which is impossible) or already be (in which case it hasn't truly changed). Therefore, the world of change we perceive is an illusion.
  • Heraclitus' View: Contended the opposite, that everything is in a constant state of flux; "you cannot step into the same river twice." Nothing is ever truly stable.
  • The Paradox: Both thinkers had insights, but their extremes made ordinary experience—where things both change and persist—incomprehensible.

Aristotle's Solution: Potentiality and Actuality

Aristotle resolves this paradox by introducing a crucial "third category" between absolute being and non-being:

  • Potentiality (dynamis): This is not non-existence, but a real capacity or power to be or do something. An acorn genuinely has the capacity to become an oak in a way a stone does not. It is rooted in the thing's nature and form.
  • Actuality (energeia): This is the realization or exercise of that capacity. When the acorn grows into an oak, its potential is actualized.
  • Change: For Aristotle, change is simply the transition from potential being to actual being. Something doesn't come from nothing; it moves from a state of "being-potentially" to "being-actually."

Understanding Potentiality and Actuality

  • Potentiality (dynamis): The raw capacity. Examples: an acorn is potentially an oak; marble is potentially a statue; a child is potentially an adult. It's an incomplete, dependent mode of being.
  • Actuality (energeia): The fulfillment of that capacity. Examples: the oak, the statue, the adult. It is the complete and realized state.

Rational vs. Irrational Powers

Aristotle extends this analysis to human beings and their unique capacities:

  • Irrational Potentialities: These are one-directional. When conditions are right, they always act the same way. Examples: fire always heats, ice always cools, heavy objects always fall. There is no choice involved.
  • Rational Potentialities: These are two-directional. The possessor of such a power can choose to actualize it in opposite ways. Examples: A doctor's knowledge can heal or harm; a musician's skill can play beautifully or discordantly.
  • Proairesis (Rational Choice): Because rational powers are two-directional, something additional is required to determine their outcome: a deliberate decision or rational choice. This concept is fundamental to Aristotle's ethics, where virtue is seen as a stable disposition to make good choices (proairesis) about how to exercise one's rational powers.

Entelecheia: Full Realization

Aristotle uses two terms for actuality, highlighting different aspects:

  • Energeia: Refers to ongoing activity or the exercise of a capacity ("being at work").
  • Entelecheia: Refers to the complete fulfillment of a capacity, the state where a thing has fully realized its inherent end or purpose ("having its end within itself").
  • Both terms are necessary to capture the full scope of actuality: it is both the continuous activity of being and the ultimate completion of being. For instance, the soul actively organizes the body (energeia) and is simultaneously directed toward the full potential of the human being (entelecheia).

Actuality is Defined by Analogy

A striking feature of Aristotle's explanation is his admission that actuality cannot be defined directly.

  • Why not directly defined? Actuality is more fundamental than potentiality; potentiality is defined by reference to actuality. Actuality is a basic feature of existence.
  • Method of Analogy: Aristotle presents paired examples to illustrate the difference:
    • Waking is to sleeping
    • Seeing is to having eyes shut
    • The man who has used his knowledge is to the man who possesses knowledge but isn't using it
    • What is being built is to what can be built
  • Philosophical Significance: This method suggests that the most fundamental features of reality are not deduced but are directly experienced. The philosopher's role is to point to these experiences so the student can recognize what is already present.

The Priority of Actuality

Aristotle's boldest claim is that actuality is prior to potentiality in three distinct senses:

  1. Priority in Definition (Logical/Conceptual): You can only define a potentiality by specifying the actuality it is ordered toward. The acorn's potentiality is understood by reference to the oak (its actuality). The capacity to see is defined by the act of seeing.
  2. Priority in Time: While individual potentiality precedes individual actuality (the egg before the chicken), Aristotle argues that in a deeper sense, actuality is prior. The egg comes from an already-actual chicken; the species itself is eternally actual. Tracing the chain of generation backward leads to something always already actual.
  3. Priority in Substance (Teleological): This is the most fundamental sense. Actuality is the end (telos) toward which every potentiality is ordered. Everything in nature exists for the sake of its actuality (e.g., the acorn for the oak, the embryo for the adult). The end is always more real, complete, and fundamental than the means. Understanding a thing means understanding its telos.

Caution: Potentiality is Not Nothing

It's important to remember that while actuality is "prior," potentiality is not unreal or unimportant. It is a genuine, incomplete mode of being—an "incipient directedness" toward a specific actuality. Aristotle's claim emphasizes that potentiality is dependent on and directed toward actuality for its meaning and fulfillment. To truly understand any being, one must grasp not only what it is now, but also what it is for – its ultimate actuality or telos.

Connecting the Concepts

Aristotle's distinction between potentiality and actuality forms the core of his entire philosophy:

  • Biology: Explains growth, development, and generation (e.g., seed to plant, embryo to animal).
  • Ethics (Volume I): Virtue is the actualization of the soul's rational capacities, and eudaimonia (flourishing) is the full actualization of human nature. Proairesis is key to virtuous action.
  • Theology (Chapter 6): God is described as pure actuality (actus purus), the ultimate source of all actualization in the universe, the Unmoved Mover.

Review Questions

  1. How did Aristotle's concept of potentiality (dynamis) provide a resolution to the "problem of change" debated by Parmenides and Heraclitus?
  2. Explain the difference between "rational powers" and "irrational powers," providing an example of each. Why is proairesis exclusively associated with rational powers, and what ethical significance does this have?
  3. Aristotle states that actuality cannot be defined directly but must be understood through analogy. What does this method of explanation imply about the nature of fundamental reality and how we come to know it?
  4. Describe the three distinct senses in which Aristotle argues that "actuality is prior to potentiality." How do these different priorities contribute to a comprehensive understanding of reality?
  5. Consider the "thinkdeeper" prompt: If actuality is the telos toward which potentiality is ordered, how does this view influence our understanding of human education and personal development? Does it imply a specific, fully actualized version of yourself, and how does that idea make you feel?
