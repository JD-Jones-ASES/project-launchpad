# Study Guide: Being, Certainty, and the Nature of Truth

This study guide examines the fundamental shift in philosophical inquiry from the classical realism of Aristotle to the subjective turn of René Descartes, as well as Thomas Aquinas’s theological expansion of Aristotelian metaphysics. It focuses on the grounds of certainty, the nature of God as Being itself, and the definition of truth.

---

## I. Key Concepts and Themes

### 1. The Ground of Certainty: Aristotle vs. Descartes
The transition from ancient to modern philosophy is defined by a change in the starting point for knowledge.
*   **Aristotelian Realism:** Certainty is found in the **Principle of Non-Contradiction**. This is a metaphysical truth (about reality) and a logical truth (about thought). It assumes the world is structured in a way that reason can grasp.
*   **Cartesian Subjectivity:** Descartes seeks a foundation that cannot be undermined even by a "malicious demon." He finds this in the **cogito** ("I think, therefore I am"). Certainty moves from the structure of the world to the clarity of the human mind.

### 2. Descartes’ Criterion for Truth
For Descartes, the standard for any true proposition is that it must be perceived **"clearly and distinctly."** 
*   **The Turn to the Subject:** Because the senses can deceive, the mind must work from its own internal certainty outward to the world, rather than assuming the world exists as perceived.
*   **Metaphysical Doubt:** Descartes notes that until he can prove a non-deceiving God exists, he cannot be fully certain of external realities or even mathematical truths (like 2+3=5) if a creator might have designed him to be deceived.

### 3. Aquinas and the Metaphysics of God
Aquinas takes Aristotle’s concept of "substance" and applies it to God in a radical way.
*   **Ipsum Esse Subsistens:** God is not merely a being among others; God is "subsistent being itself." In creatures, essence (what they are) and existence (that they are) are separate. In God, **essence and existence are identical.**
*   **Divine Simplicity:** God has no composition. He is not a body (which is divisible and in potentiality), nor is He a composite of form and matter or substance and accidents. God is pure act.

### 4. The Order of Truth
Aquinas defines truth as ***adequatio intellectus et rei*** (the conformity of intellect and thing).
*   **Primary Truth:** Resides in the intellect.
*   **Secondary Truth:** Resides in things.
*   **The Flow of Truth:** A thing is "true" if it conforms to the intellect that designed it. Natural things are "true" because they conform to the **Divine Intellect**. Human knowledge is "true" when it conforms to natural things. Thus, God is Truth Itself.

## II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. How does Aristotle’s Principle of Non-Contradiction function as both a logical and a metaphysical principle?**
*Answer:* It is logical because it governs how we must think (the same attribute cannot belong and not belong to a subject); it is metaphysical because it describes reality (things cannot simultaneously be and not be in the same respect).

**2. What is the "malicious demon" in Descartes’ philosophy?**
*Answer:* It is a thought experiment imagining a powerful deceiver capable of making Descartes wrong about everything, even basic sensory perceptions and mathematical facts, used to establish the need for an indubitable foundation.

**3. According to Descartes' Third Meditation, what defines a "thing that thinks"?**
*Answer:* A thing that doubts, affirms, denies, knows a few things, is ignorant of many, wills, desires, imagines, and perceives.

**4. Why does Aquinas argue that God cannot be a body?**
*Answer:* Aquinas provides three reasons: 1) God is the First Mover and must be unmoved, whereas bodies move; 2) God is pure act without potentiality, whereas bodies are divisible and thus in potentiality; 3) God is the most noble being, and an animate body’s nobility comes from its soul, not its "body-ness."

**5. In Aquinas’s view, how is a "true stone" defined?**
*Answer:* A stone is called "true" when it possesses the nature proper to a stone according to the preconception or "species" in the divine intellect.

**6. What is the "turn to the subject"?**
*Answer:* It is the shift in philosophy, starting with Descartes, where the human mind and its internal structures become the primary object of investigation, rather than the external world.

## III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Inward vs. Outward Path:** Compare and contrast the "starting points" of Aristotle and Descartes. Explain the consequences of starting with the structure of *being* versus starting with the structure of *thought*. Which approach offers a more secure foundation for science?
2.  **The Identity of Essence and Existence:** Analyze Aquinas’s claim that in God, essence and existence are one. How does this distinguish God from all "created things," and how does this lead to the doctrine of Divine Simplicity?
3.  **The Hierarchy of Truth:** Discuss the definition of truth as *adequatio intellectus et rei*. Explain how truth "flows" from the Divine Intellect to the natural world and finally to the human mind. How does this framework reconcile the idea that truth is both in the mind and in the things themselves?

## IV. Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Adequatio intellectus et rei** | The conformity or "adequation" of the intellect and the thing; the classic definition of truth used by Aquinas. |
| **Clear and Distinct** | Descartes’ criterion for truth; an idea that is so self-evident to the mind that it cannot be doubted. |
| **Cogito, ergo sum** | "I think, therefore I am"; the foundational certainty Descartes identifies as the starting point for knowledge. |
| **Divine Simplicity** | The doctrine that God is not composed of parts (neither matter/form, substance/accident, nor essence/existence). |
| **Ipsum esse subsistens** | "Subsistent being itself"; the term Aquinas uses to describe God's nature, where His essence is His existence. |
| **Metaphysical Doubt** | A deep level of skepticism regarding the very nature of reality, such as the possibility of being deceived by a creator. |
| **Principle of Non-Contradiction** | The rule that "the same attribute cannot at the same time belong and not belong to the same subject in the same respect." |
| **Turn to the Subject** | The move in modern philosophy (post-Descartes) to make the human mind and its perceptions the central subject of inquiry. |
| **Virtual Quantity** | A term used by Aquinas to explain scriptural descriptions of God's "size" (height, depth) as metaphors for His power and knowledge rather than physical dimensions. |
