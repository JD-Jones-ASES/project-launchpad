Study Guide: Navigating Boccaccio’s Decameron

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the strategies, themes, and historical contexts necessary for a deep engagement with Giovanni Boccaccio’s The Decameron. Based on the introductory guidance in Chapter 1, Section 2, the following sections outline how to approach the text's complex narrative structure and moral landscape.

Key Concepts for Reading the Decameron

To fully appreciate the Decameron, readers must look beyond the surface of the tales. Boccaccio’s work is a sophisticated interplay of entertainment, satire, and structural design.

1. The Priority of Storytelling

The primary function of the Decameron is to entertain, surprise, and delight. Before engaging in deep literary or historical analysis, the reader should experience the tales as stories—appreciating the humor of the "tricks," the weight of the tragedies, and the cleverness of the characters.

2. The Role of the Narrator

The tales are told by the brigata, a specific group of narrators, each with distinct personalities and preferences.

  • Significance: The narrator’s identity shapes the story. For example, Dioneo is an irreverent joker, while Filomena focuses on fortune and adventure.
  • Reliability: Narrators are not always reliable. There is often a meaningful gap between what a narrator claims a story means and what the story actually conveys. Boccaccio uses narrators like characters in a play to add layers of meaning.

3. Pervasive Irony

Boccaccio rarely communicates his meaning directly. Irony is his primary mode of expression, manifesting in several ways:

  • A narrator may praise a character whom the reader is intended to condemn.
  • A stated "moral" may be the exact opposite of the tale's actual effect.
  • A character’s words may mean one thing to their internal audience and something else entirely to the reader.

4. Moral Complexity and Ambiguity

Unlike many modern stories that offer clear lessons, the Decameron resists simple morality.

  • Complex Characters: Characters like the monstrous yet brilliant Ser Ciappelletto or the patient yet disturbing Griselda defy easy categorization as "good" or "bad."
  • Medieval Perspective: Medieval audiences were more comfortable with moral ambiguity and were expected to exercise their own judgment rather than being handed an easy answer.

5. Architectural Structure

The Decameron is not a random collection of stories; its structure is highly deliberate.

  • Thematic Progression: Each "Day" has a theme.
  • The Arc: The overall collection moves from a focus on comedy and satire toward themes of nobility and virtue.
  • Internal Echoes: Tales within a specific Day often echo or contrast with one another to build a larger argument.

Content and Translation Notes

The Rigg Translation

The text utilizes the 1903 translation by J.M. Rigg.

  • Style: Faithful to the original Italian but written in Edwardian English.
  • Vocabulary: Includes archaic terms such as "whither" (where), "hath" (has), and "prithee" (I pray you).

Themes and Satire

  • Bawdiness: Many tales deal frankly with sexual desire and adultery. These are rooted in the fabliau tradition and are intended to illuminate human nature.
  • Anticlericalism: Boccaccio frequently satirizes the clergy (monks, friars, nuns), portraying them as hypocrites. This was a common medieval Christian tradition; the satire targets the gap between religious profession and actual behavior, rather than the faith itself.
  • The Beffa: A central motif in the book is the beffa, or practical joke. These stories celebrate cleverness while often punishing gullibility with comic cruelty.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Narrator’s Mask: Analyze the importance of the gap between the narrator’s interpretation and the actual content of a tale. How does Boccaccio use the brigata to create a layer of irony that would be missing if there were a single, omniscient narrator?
  2. The Ethics of Cleverness: Discuss the role of the beffa and the fabliau in the Decameron. Does Boccaccio suggest that cleverness is a virtue in itself, even when used for transgressive or cruel purposes? Use specific examples like the "clever wives" of Day VII or the trickster Ser Ciappelletto.
  3. Medieval vs. Modern Morality: Explore the concept of moral ambiguity in the Decameron. Why might a medieval audience find characters like Griselda or Ser Ciappelletto less "troubling" than a modern reader might? How does Boccaccio’s refusal to provide "easy answers" impact the reader's engagement with the text?

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Beffa A practical joke or elaborate trick; a central motif that celebrates cleverness and punishes gullibility.
Brigata The group of narrators who flee the plague and tell the stories that make up the Decameron.
Edwardian English The formal, somewhat stiff style of English used in the 1903 Rigg translation, characterized by archaic vocabulary.
Fabliau A long literary tradition of comic, often bawdy tales used to illuminate truths about human nature.
Irony A mode of expression where the intended meaning is different from, or opposite to, the literal meaning.
Satire The use of humor or exaggeration to critique hypocrisy, particularly within the clergy in Boccaccio's context.
