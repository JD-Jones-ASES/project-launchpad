Study Guide: Reading the Decameron

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the strategies, themes, and contextual considerations necessary for a deep engagement with Giovanni Boccaccio’s The Decameron. Based on the introductory guidance for the text, it explores the narrative structure, the use of irony, and the historical context of the work.

Key Reading Strategies and Concepts

1. Prioritize Narrative Enjoyment

Before engaging in literary or historical analysis, the reader is encouraged to approach the Decameron as a collection of stories crafted for entertainment. Boccaccio was a "supreme entertainer," and his tales are designed to evoke immediate emotional responses: laughter at tricks, empathy for tragedies, and admiration for cleverness.

2. Analyze the Narrator

Each story is told by a member of the brigata (the group of ten young people fleeing the plague). The identity of the narrator is crucial to the story's meaning:

  • Narrator Personalities: Different narrators favor different themes. For example, Dioneo is an irreverent joker, while Filomena prefers stories of fortune and adventure.
  • Reliability: Narrators are not always reliable. Boccaccio often creates a "gap" between what a narrator claims a story means and the actual implications of the narrative. This discrepancy is often where the most significant literary interest lies.

3. Identify Pervasive Irony

Boccaccio rarely communicates his meaning directly. Irony is a fundamental mode of expression in the Decameron, characterized by:

  • Contradiction: A narrator may praise a character who is clearly meant to be condemned.
  • False Morals: The "moral" stated at the end of a tale may be the exact opposite of the lesson the reader is intended to draw.
  • Subtext: If a situation appears too simple or "neat," it is likely a signal of Boccaccio’s ironic intent.

4. Embrace Moral Complexity

The Decameron resists simple "good versus bad" categorizations. Medieval literature often presented morally ambiguous situations and trusted the audience to exercise judgment.

  • Complex Characters: Figures like the wicked Ser Ciappelletto (a monster yet a brilliant performer) or the patient Griselda (admirable yet disturbing) exemplify this complexity.
  • Controversial Figures: The "clever wives" of Day VII are presented as both sympathetic and transgressive.

5. Recognize the Architecture

The structure of the Decameron is deliberate and non-accidental. Readers should observe:

  • Thematic Progression: The themes of the Days develop over time, moving from comedy and satire toward nobility and virtue.
  • Internal Echoes: Tales within a single Day often echo or contrast with one another.

Content and Context

The J.M. Rigg Translation

The tales are frequently presented in the 1903 translation by J.M. Rigg.

  • Style: It is faithful to the original Italian but utilizes formal Edwardian English.
  • Archaic Language: Readers will encounter words such as "whither" (where), "hath" (has), and "prithee" (I pray you).

Satire and Social Critique

The Decameron contains content that may be challenging for modern readers, including bawdy humor and anticlerical satire.

  • Bawdy Tales: These stories deal frankly with sexual desire and the "appetites of the body," following the tradition of the fabliau (comic tale).
  • Anticlericalism: Satire is directed at hypocrisy—the gap between religious profession and actual behavior—rather than at faith itself. Monks, friars, and nuns are often portrayed as hypocrites to illuminate truths about human nature.
  • The Beffa: A central motif is the beffa, or practical joke, which celebrates cleverness while punishing the gullible.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

Question Answer
Who are the members of the brigata? The ten narrators who tell the stories within the Decameron.
How does the storytelling style of Dioneo differ from Filomena? Dioneo is an irreverent joker, while Filomena favors tales of fortune and adventure.
What is a beffa? A practical joke or elaborate trick that celebrates cleverness and punishes gullibility.
Why is Ser Ciappelletto considered a morally complex character? He is described as both a "monster" and a "brilliant performer."
What is the primary target of Boccaccio’s anticlerical satire? Hypocrisy—specifically the gap between how the clergy profess to behave and how they actually behave.
What defines the tone of the J.M. Rigg translation? It is formal, Edwardian English, utilizing archaic vocabulary like "hath" and "prithee."
How does the overall "arc" of the Decameron move? It moves from comedy and satire toward themes of nobility and virtue.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Unreliable Narrator: Analyze the significance of the "gap" between a narrator’s stated moral and the actual events of a tale. How does this technique force the reader to engage more deeply with Boccaccio's irony?
  2. Medieval vs. Modern Morality: Discuss the concept of "moral complexity" in the Decameron. Why might medieval audiences have found moral ambiguity less troubling than modern readers, and how does Boccaccio use characters like Griselda to challenge simple judgments?
  3. The Function of Satire: Examine Boccaccio’s treatment of the clergy. Using figures like Fra Cipolla or Ser Ciappelletto, argue whether the text is an attack on religious institutions or a critique of individual human failings.
  4. Literary Architecture: Explore how the organization of the "Days" contributes to the meaning of the work. How do the shifting themes from Day I to Day X reflect a broader message about human nature and society?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Beffa: A central motif in the Decameron involving a practical joke or elaborate trick; it often involves comic cruelty and celebrates the cleverness of the trickster.
  • Brigata: The group of narrators who flee the plague and tell the stories that comprise the Decameron.
  • Edwardian English: The formal style of English used in the 1903 J.M. Rigg translation, characterized by archaic terms and a stiff, rhythmic prose.
  • Exemplum (Comic): A short story used to illustrate a moral point, though Boccaccio often subverts these to create irony.
  • Fabliau: A literary tradition of comic, often bawdy, tales that use humor to explore human nature and bodily appetites.
  • Irony: A mode of expression where the intended meaning is the opposite of the literal meaning; used by Boccaccio to critique characters or subvert stated morals.
  • Prithee: An archaic Edwardian English term meaning "I pray you."
  • Whither: An archaic term meaning "where."
