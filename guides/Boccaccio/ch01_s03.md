# Study Guide: The Life and Times of Giovanni Boccaccio

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the life of Giovanni Boccaccio (1313–1375), his literary contributions, and the historical context of the 14th century, specifically the impact of the Black Death on his masterpiece, the *Decameron*.

---

### Key Concepts and Biography

#### Early Life and the Neapolitan Influence
Born in 1313 as the illegitimate but acknowledged son of a prominent Florentine banker, Boccaccio was caught between the worlds of commerce and art. His formative years (1327–1340) were spent in **Angevin Naples**, where he was sent to learn the banking trade at the Bardi banking house. While he lacked interest in business, the brilliant culture of the Angevin court exposed him to French romances and classical Latin literature. This period introduced him to his muse, **Fiammetta**, and saw the composition of early works like *Filostrato* and *Filocolo*, which established his lifelong interest in the transformative power of love.

#### The Florentine Return and the Black Death
Boccaccio returned to Florence around 1340 due to his father's financial difficulties. He found the merchant-heavy atmosphere of Florence a sharp contrast to the glamour of Naples. However, it was in Florence that he witnessed the **Black Death of 1348**. The plague killed between 50,000 and 80,000 Florentines—more than half the population—and caused a total collapse of social, religious, and moral structures. This catastrophe served as the direct inspiration and framing device for the *Decameron*.

#### Literary Achievement and Humanism
The *Decameron*, composed between 1349 and 1353, is a collection of 100 tales told over ten days. It is celebrated for its range, covering everything from tragedy to bawdy farce in polished Italian prose. In 1350, Boccaccio began a transformative friendship with **Francesco Petrarch**. Under Petrarch's influence, Boccaccio moved toward **humanism**, focusing on classical scholarship and Latin works. Despite a late-life spiritual crisis triggered by religious anxiety, Boccaccio remained a dedicated scholar, giving the first public lectures on Dante’s *Divine Comedy* and compiling encyclopedic works on mythology.

### Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Architecture of Crisis:** Analyze how Boccaccio uses the physical and moral devastation of the 1348 plague as a "frame" for the *Decameron*. How does the chaos of the Black Death justify the storytelling retreat of his ten protagonists?
2.  **Naples vs. Florence:** Compare and contrast the cultural influences of the Angevin court in Naples with the merchant-driven environment of Florence. How did Boccaccio’s exposure to both worlds shape the "sacred and profane" nature of his writing?
3.  **The Paradox of Influence:** Discuss the relationship between Boccaccio and Petrarch. Consider how Petrarch’s preference for Latin and lyric poetry both encouraged Boccaccio’s humanist scholarship and potentially devalued his greatest contribution to Italian prose.
4.  **The Humanist Transition:** Define "Humanism" in the context of the 14th century as described in the text. How did Boccaccio embody the transition from a medieval storyteller to a Renaissance scholar through his public service and his lectures on Dante?

### Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Angevin Naples** | The Kingdom of Naples ruled by the French House of Anjou (1266–1435); a cultural crossroads of French, Italian, and classical traditions. |
| **Bardi** | A powerful Florentine banking family that financed kings and popes; Boccaccio’s father was connected to this house. |
| **Certaldo** | A Tuscan hill town southwest of Florence where Boccaccio was likely born and eventually died. |
| **Fabliau** | A short, humorous, and often bawdy verse tale popular in medieval France; Boccaccio adapted many of these into prose for the *Decameron*. |
| **Fiammetta** | Meaning "little flame," the name Boccaccio gave to his beloved and muse in several early works. |
| **Humanist** | A scholar devoted to the *studia humanitatis* (grammar, rhetoric, poetry, history, philosophy) through the study of classical Greek and Roman texts. |
| **Pestilence** | Specifically referring to the Black Death of 1348, caused by the bacterium *Yersinia pestis*. |
| **Prose** | Written or spoken language in its ordinary form, without metrical structure; Boccaccio is credited with elevating Italian prose to a high literary art. |
| **Tre Corone** | "Three Crowns"; the collective title for Dante, Petrarch, and Boccaccio, the foundational figures of early Italian literature. |
| **Yersinia pestis** | The modern scientific name for the bacterium responsible for the bubonic, pneumonic, and septicemic plague. |
