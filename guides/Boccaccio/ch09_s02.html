Study Guide: Boccaccio’s Decameron, Day VIII Introduction

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the introductory themes, characters, and structural shifts presented in the eighth day of storytelling in Boccaccio’s Decameron. It explores the transition from the lighthearted trickery of Day VII to the broader and occasionally darker scope of Day VIII.

I. Key Concepts and Themes

1. The Broadening Scope of the Beffa

Under the rule of Lauretta, the theme of Day VIII focuses on "tricks" (beffa). While Day VII was limited to tricks played by wives on their husbands, Day VIII expands the field to include:

  • Women practicing tricks on their husbands.
  • Husbands practicing tricks on their wives.
  • Tricks played between men.
  • Men practicing tricks on women (a significant addition).

This expansion breaks the symmetry established in the previous day, moving trickery beyond the domestic confines of the marriage bed and into a larger, more varied social stage.

2. Ethical Shifts: Leggera vs. Crudele

The Italian critical tradition identifies a shift in the tone of trickery during Day VIII. The document distinguishes between two types of tricks:

  • Beffa leggera (Light trick): Harmless pranks, often lighthearted, where the primary consequence is a bruised ego.
  • Beffa crudele (Cruel trick): Pranks that escalate into something genuinely cruel, where cleverness is utilized as a weapon rather than a game.

Boccaccio juxtaposes these two types to force the reader to confront the ethical boundaries of wit and deception.

3. Historical Realism and Urban Gossip

Unlike anonymous characters from distant lands, the characters introduced in Day VIII are recognizable Florentines from the world of Boccaccio's original audience. By using real people as the basis for his stories, Boccaccio elevates "urban gossip" to the level of high art.

II. Key Figures and Characters

Entity Role/Description
Lauretta The Queen of the eighth day; she sets the theme of universal trickery.
Calandrino Based on the historical Nozzo di Perino; he is portrayed as a gullible painter and the "eternal dupe."
Bruno and Buffalmacco Witty pranksters and painters who torment Calandrino. They were real historical figures mentioned in Vasari’s Lives of the Artists.
Neifile The narrator who begins the storytelling for Day VIII at the command of the Queen.

III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Who is the presiding ruler of Day VIII, and what theme does she establish? Answer: Lauretta is the queen; she establishes the theme of tricks played by women on husbands, husbands on wives, or one man upon another.

  2. How does the scope of Day VIII differ from Day VII? Answer: Day VII was exclusively about wives tricking husbands. Day VIII broadens the scope to include tricks between all genders and social configurations, specifically adding men tricking women.

  3. What are the two categories of tricks described in the Italian critical tradition? Answer: Beffa leggera (light trick) and beffa crudele (cruel trick).

  4. Which historical source mentions the real-life painters Bruno and Buffalmacco? Answer: Giorgio Vasari’s Lives of the Artists (1550).

  5. Describe the morning routine of the narrators before the storytelling begins on Day VIII. Answer: They rose at dawn, walked in a dewy meadow, heard Divine service at a nearby church at half tierce, returned to the palace for breakfast, sang and danced, and then rested until after the meridian.

  6. Where do the narrators sit when they begin their storytelling session? Answer: They sit by a "fair fountain."

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Evolution of Cleverness: Analyze how the concept of "cleverness" changes as the Decameron moves from Day VII to Day VIII. Consider the shift from cleverness as a tool for marital navigation to cleverness as a potentially "cruel" weapon.

  2. Historical Archetypes: Discuss Boccaccio’s decision to use real Florentine citizens, such as Calandrino, Bruno, and Buffalmacco, instead of anonymous or fictionalized characters. How does this "texture of urban gossip" affect the reader's engagement with the text?

  3. The Ethical Limits of the Beffa: Using the distinction between beffa leggera and beffa crudele, evaluate whether trickery in Day VIII is presented as a justifiable social skill or a moral failing.

V. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Beffa: An Italian term for a trick or a prank; the central thematic device of Day VIII.
  • Beffa Crudele: A "cruel trick" where cleverness becomes a harmful weapon.
  • Beffa Leggera: A "light trick" that is harmless or playful in nature.
  • Half Tierce: A time of day (approximately mid-morning) when the company attended Divine service.
  • Meridian: Midday; the narrators gathered to begin storytelling once the sun had passed this point.
  • The Mead: A meadow where the company walked in the morning dew.
  • Vasari’s "Lives of the Artists": A historical text (1550) that confirms the real-life existence of the characters Bruno and Buffalmacco.
