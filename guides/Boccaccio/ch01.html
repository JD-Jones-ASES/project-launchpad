Study Guide: Boccaccio, The Decameron, and the World of the Black Death

Overview

This section introduces Giovanni Boccaccio's literary masterpiece, The Decameron, set against the backdrop of the devastating Black Death of 1348. It explores the author's personal motivations, his dedication of the work to women, and his famous, vivid description of the plague's horrifying impact on Florence. The section also highlights the formation of the brigata, a group of ten young people who seek refuge and create order through storytelling amidst the societal collapse.

Key Concepts

  • The Black Death as a Catalyst: The catastrophic plague of 1348 is not merely a historical setting but the central event that compels the characters to retreat from society and initiate the storytelling framework of The Decameron.
  • Boccaccio's Dedication to Women: The author explicitly dedicates his work to "gentle ladies," especially those suffering from the pangs of love or social confinement, seeing them as more in need of solace and diversion than men, who have more avenues for relief.
  • Breakdown of Social and Moral Order: Boccaccio's account of the plague goes beyond physical symptoms, powerfully illustrating the collapse of familial bonds, civic duties, and religious practices, likening its impact to historical accounts like Thucydides' description of the Plague of Athens.
  • The Brigata as a Creative Response: In the face of widespread death and social chaos, a small company of ten young people (seven women and three men), known as the brigata, chooses to create a self-contained world of order, companionship, and meaning through the act of storytelling.
  • Storytelling as Solace and Instruction: The narratives within The Decameron are intended to provide both pleasure and entertainment (diversion) and practical wisdom and moral guidance (what to shun and what to pursue) for its audience, especially for women seeking relief from melancholy.
  • Combination of Clinical and Moral Observation: Boccaccio's description of the plague is distinctive for its blend of precise physical observations of the disease's symptoms with profound reflections on its ethical, psychological, and societal consequences.

Important Terms

  • Black Death / Mortal Pestilence: The devastating pandemic that swept through Europe in 1348, causing widespread death and societal upheaval. Boccaccio specifically refers to it as "the late mortal pestilence."
  • Proem: The introductory section of a literary work, in which Boccaccio dedicates The Decameron and explains his purpose.
  • Brigata: The Italian word for "company" or "squad," referring to the group of seven ladies and three young men who flee Florence and tell the hundred tales in The Decameron.
  • Gavoccioli: The specific term Boccaccio uses for the characteristic tumors or swellings (buboes) that appeared in the groin or armpits of plague victims, described as being the size of an apple or an egg.
  • Discourse: Conversation; a formal discussion or treatise on a subject. In the context of the Decameron, it refers to the exchange of stories.
  • Prudence: One of the four cardinal virtues; the ability to judge wisely and act accordingly. Boccaccio mentions it in contrast to uncontrollable desire.
  • Wont: Accustomed; in the habit of. Also used as a noun meaning "custom" or "habit."
  • Perchance: Perhaps; by chance.
  • Fortune: Fate or chance, often personified as a goddess (Fortuna) who arbitrarily raises and casts down mortals.
  • Wherefore: For what reason; why. (Not to be confused with "where.")
  • Commend: To entrust; to praise. "Commend me to" means "give my regards to."

Detailed Notes

Introduction to The Decameron and Its Context

  • Giovanni Boccaccio wrote The Decameron in the aftermath of the Black Death of 1348, one of Europe's greatest catastrophes.
  • The work consists of one hundred tales focusing on love, trickery, wit, and fortune, profoundly influencing Western literature.
  • The provided text covers the Proem (Boccaccio's dedication) and the Introduction to I (description of the plague and formation of the brigata).

Boccaccio's Description of the Black Death

  • Boccaccio's account is a renowned passage in medieval literature, often compared to:
    • Thucydides' account of the Plague of Athens (430 BC): Both authors emphasize the breakdown of social and moral order alongside physical symptoms. Boccaccio may have been indirectly familiar with Thucydides.
    • Daniel Defoe's A Journal of the Plague Year (1722).
  • Boccaccio's distinctive approach: It combines clinical observation (detailing symptoms and spread) with moral reflection (exploring the devastating impact on family, friendship, religion, and civic life).

"What to Watch For" - Key Themes

  • Shift from General to Particular: Boccaccio moves from the overall physical effects of the plague to its specific social and moral consequences.
  • Breakdown of Human Duties: He highlights the collapse of ordinary responsibilities: parents abandoning children, priests refusing the sick, and the dead left unburied.
  • The Brigata's Contrast and Moral Foundation: The assembly of the ten young people (the brigata) provides a stark contrast. Out of chaos, they choose to create order through companionship and storytelling. This creative response to destruction forms the moral core of The Decameron.

The Decameron, Proem (Boccaccio's Dedication)

  • Author's Personal Motivation: Boccaccio recounts his own experience of intense, unrequited love in his youth, which caused him great suffering. He found solace through a friend's "delectable discourse" and "commendable consolations," which he believes saved his life.
  • Gratitude and Purpose: His own suffering has now abated, leaving only pleasant memories. Driven by gratitude (a virtue he highly commends, unlike ingratitude), he resolves to offer solace to others who might need it.
  • Target Audience: Gentle Ladies:
    • Boccaccio believes women are most in need of comfort because they "harbour secret fires of love" but are constrained by social expectations and confined to their chambers.
    • This confinement prevents them from finding the same diversions available to men (e.g., hawking, hunting, fishing, riding, playing, trading) that help alleviate melancholy.
    • He aims to "compensate the injustice of Fortune" by providing support and diversion to "such of them as love."
  • Content and Purpose of the Stories:
    • He intends to recount "one hundred Novels or Fables or Parables or Stories," told over ten days by a company of seven ladies and three young men during the plague.
    • The stories will feature "love rudely crossed" and "felicitous issues" from both modern and ancient times.
    • Their purpose is dual: to provide pleasure from entertaining matters and good counsel, teaching readers "what to shun, and likewise what to pursue." Ultimately, they aim to banish melancholy through "diversion of mind."

The Decameron, Introduction (First Day)

  • Addressing the "Horrid Beginning": Boccaccio acknowledges that the description of the plague will be "heavy and distressful" and revive sorrowful memories.
  • Analogy of the Journey: He compares the "horrid beginning" to a "steep and rugged mountain" that must be climbed to reach a "plain most fair and delectable." The toil makes the destination more agreeable, just as misery is often followed by happiness.
  • Necessity of the Plague Description: He states that recounting the plague is essential to explain how the subsequent stories and events came to pass; it is a necessary prelude to the delights that follow.
  • The Plague in Florence (1348):
    • Date: "One thousand three hundred and forty-eight" (1348 AD).
    • Location: The "illustrious city of Florence," described as "the fairest of all the cities of Italy."
    • Origin and Spread: The plague originated "some years before in the East," spreading westward "without respite" and destroying "innumerable multitude of living beings."
    • Possible Causes: Boccaccio mentions common beliefs about its origin: "influence of the celestial bodies" or God's "just wrath by way of retribution for our iniquities."
    • Failed Human Efforts: All attempts to avert it failed, including:
      • "Human wisdom and forethought" (cleansing the city, refusing entrance to sick, health precautions).
      • "Humble supplications addressed to God" (public processions, devout prayers).
    • Symptoms in Florence: Began in early spring of 1348. Unlike the East where nosebleeds signaled death, in Florence, symptoms first appeared as "certain tumors in the groin or the armpits." These "gavoccioli" (buboes) could grow as large as an apple or an egg.

Review Questions

  1. How does Boccaccio frame the writing of The Decameron in relation to his personal experiences of love and the historical context of the Black Death? What connection does he draw between these two seemingly disparate elements?
  2. Why does Boccaccio specifically dedicate The Decameron to "gentle ladies" rather than men, and what justifications does he offer for this choice based on their social circumstances and emotional needs?
  3. Compare Boccaccio's description of the Black Death to historical accounts like Thucydides'. What makes Boccaccio's account distinctive, particularly regarding its focus beyond mere physical symptoms?
  4. Explain the "moral foundation" of The Decameron as described in the "What to Watch For" notebox. How does the contrast between the plague's destruction and the brigata's creative response illuminate this foundation?
  5. Beyond providing mere entertainment, what other significant purposes does Boccaccio intend his "Novels or Fables or Parables or Stories" to serve for his readers, especially for his target audience of women?
