# Study Guide: Lydia’s Triple Proof of Love (Decameron, Day VII, Novel 9)

This study guide provides a comprehensive analysis of the ninth tale told on the Seventh Day of Boccaccio’s *Decameron*. Narrated by Panfilo, this story represents the most elaborate and audacious example of the "beffa" (the trick), featuring a calculated campaign of deception that pushes the boundaries of logic and self-deception.

---

## I. Narrative Overview and Context

### The Setting and Tone
The tale is set in **Argos, Greece**, an ancient city of Achaia. This non-Italian setting provides an exotic distance from the Florentine audience, allowing Boccaccio to explore surreal comedic heights. Unlike other tales where tricks are born of sudden necessity, this narrative focuses on a **calculated campaign** of escalating daring.

### Core Themes
*   **The Beffa (The Trick):** The story is a supreme example of the "beffa," where a victim is led to believe something patently false, often despite the evidence of their own senses.
*   **Inversion of Courtly Love:** Boccaccio parodies romance literature. Typically, a lady sets tasks for a knight; here, the lady (Lydia) must perform impossible feats to prove her devotion to a social inferior (Pyrrhus).
*   **Self-Deception:** The story explores the psychology of a fool, demonstrating how trust and a desire to believe can lead a person to reject the testimony of their own eyes.
*   **Fortune vs. Reason:** Panfilo introduces the tale by noting that Lydia owed more to the "favour of Fortune" than to the "guidance of reason."

## II. Character Profiles

| Character | Role | Key Traits & Motivations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Lydia** | Wife of Nicostrato | Courageous, charming, and highly intelligent. She is "mightily enamoured" with Pyrrhus and willing to perform violent or shocking acts to prove her passion. |
| **Nicostrato** | Nobleman of Argos | Wealthy and distinguished, but verging on old age. He is "wise and wary" in business but a "fool" in his domestic life, showing an extraordinary capacity for self-deception. |
| **Pyrrhus** | Servant/Companion | A "gallant of no mean accomplishment" trusted by Nicostrato. Initially skeptical and cautious, he demands three "impossible" proofs of Lydia's love to ensure he is not being set up. |
| **Lusca** | Chambermaid | Lydia's loyal confidante and messenger. She is sharp-tongued (calling Pyrrhus a "blockhead") and instrumental in coordinating the deceptions. |

## III. The Three Proofs of Love
Pyrrhus demands three specific feats to be convinced of Lydia's sincerity. These tests follow a pattern of increasing outrageousness and physical stakes.

1.  **The Killing of the Sparrow-Hawk:**
    *   **The Act:** During a breakfast for several gentlemen, Lydia looses Nicostrato’s prized sparrow-hawk and dashes it against a wall.
    *   **The Deception:** She claims the bird has stolen her husband's time and attention, leaving her lonely. The guests find her "jealousy" charming, and Nicostrato’s anger turns to laughter.

2.  **The Tuft of the Beard:**
    *   **The Act:** While toying with Nicostrato’s hair in their chamber, Lydia nimbly plucks a tuft of hair directly from his chin.
    *   **The Deception:** She frames the act as a playful "tit-for-tat," claiming he pulled her hair harder than she pulled his beard.

3.  **The Extraction of the Tooth:**
    *   **The Act:** Lydia convinces Nicostrato he has a rotten tooth causing his breath to stink (using two young servants to feign disgust as evidence). She then wrenches a healthy tooth from his jaw with pincers.
    *   **The Deception:** She shows him a previously hidden, "horribly decayed" tooth to convince him the painful procedure was necessary for his health.

---

## IV. The Climax: The Enchanted Pear-Tree
The final deception is the most brazen, requiring Nicostrato to disbelieve his own eyesight.

*   **The Setup:** Lydia feigns illness and asks to be taken to the garden. She sits at the foot of a pear-tree and asks Pyrrhus to climb it and shake down fruit.
*   **The Vision:** From the tree, Pyrrhus "sees" Nicostrato and Lydia engaging in carnal intercourse and berates them for their indecency.
*   **The Experiment:** Nicostrato, confused, climbs the tree to see if it is "enchanted." While he is in the branches, Lydia and Pyrrhus actually engage in the act.
*   **The Resolution:** When Nicostrato shouts down in rage, the lovers pretend they are merely sitting apart. They convince him that the pear-tree produces "illusions of sight." To "vindicate" their honor, Lydia has Pyrrhus cut the tree down.

## V. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **Why does Lydia choose Pyrrhus as the object of her affection?**
2.  **What is Pyrrhus’s initial reaction to Lusca’s message about Lydia’s love?**
3.  **How does Lydia justify the killing of the sparrow-hawk to Nicostrato’s guests?**
4.  **What role do the two young servants (the carver and the cup-bearer) play in the "tooth" deception?**
5.  **What is Lydia’s ultimate argument for why the pear-tree must be cut down?**
6.  **According to Panfilo’s introduction, why should women not necessarily follow Lydia's example?**

## VI. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Evolution of the Beffa:** Compare the complexity of Lydia’s scheme to "simpler" tricks found elsewhere in the *Decameron* (such as Peronella’s improvisation in VII.2). How does the calculated, multi-stage nature of Lydia’s plan change the reader's perception of her character?
2.  **The Psychology of the Cuckold:** Analyze Nicostrato’s character. Is his deception a result of Lydia’s brilliance, or is it a result of his own inherent "foolishness"? Use the "tooth" and "pear-tree" episodes to support your argument.
3.  **Parody of Romance:** Explore how Boccaccio uses the "three tests" structure to mock the conventions of courtly love. How does inverting the roles of the lady and the servant serve his comedic and thematic purposes?

## VII. Glossary of Key Terms

*   **Beffa:** A complex trick or practical joke played on a victim, central to many tales in the *Decameron*.
*   **Chirurgeon:** An archaic term for a surgeon.
*   **Feign:** To pretend or give a false appearance of something (e.g., Lydia feigning sickness).
*   **Jesses:** Short leather straps fastened around the legs of a hawk or falcon.
*   **Lewd:** In the medieval context of the source, this referred to the ignorant or unlearned (of the common people), though the modern sexual connotation is also beginning to emerge.
*   **Samite:** A rich medieval silk fabric, often interwoven with gold or silver threads.
*   **Troth:** Faith, loyalty, or a pledge; used as a common oath ("By my troth").
