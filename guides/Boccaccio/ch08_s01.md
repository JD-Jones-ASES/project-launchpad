# Study Guide: Day the Seventh of Boccaccio’s Decameron

This study guide examines Day VII of the *Decameron*, a sequence of tales where trickery and deception take center stage. The following sections explore the central literary concepts, historical context, and narrative structures that define Boccaccio’s exploration of marital wit.

---

## Key Concepts and Themes

### The Beffa: Trickery as Art
The *beffa* is the organizing principle of Day VII. While deceptions appear throughout the *Decameron*, it is in this section that the elaborate practical joke becomes a test of *ingegno* (intelligence and resourcefulness). 

A successful *beffa* requires:
*   **A Trickster (*beffatore*):** One who exploits a target's weakness through superior wit or audacity.
*   **A Dupe (*beffato*):** One who is deceived, usually due to a specific flaw such as vanity, jealousy, or gullibility.
*   **Psychological Insight:** The trickster must understand the dupe’s desire to believe in order to maintain domestic order or avoid humiliation.

### Marriage and Social Reality
In fourteenth-century Florence, marriage was primarily a social and economic arrangement negotiated between families. Personal desire was secondary to lineage, property, and dowries. While the Church viewed marriage as a sacred obligation, Boccaccio highlights the gap between these ideals and human desire. In Day VII, wives use their wits as a weapon against the constraints of these arranged, often loveless, unions.

### Boccaccio vs. Dante
Boccaccio’s treatment of adultery differs significantly from his predecessor, Dante Alighieri. While Dante places adulterers in the second circle of Hell (*Inferno*, Canto V), Boccaccio treats adultery as a social fact. He focuses on the drama, danger, and contest of wits rather than moral condemnation.

### The Fabliau Tradition
The *beffa* has roots in the French *fabliaux* (short, humorous verse tales) and the *Roman de Renart*. Boccaccio enhances this tradition by adding psychological depth. While Chaucer’s *Canterbury Tales* (such as the *Miller's Tale*) also utilize the *fabliau* tradition, Boccaccio’s focus remains on the tales themselves rather than the specific social class or personality of the narrator.

## Summary of Day VII Tales

| Novel | Central Figures | The Trick/Deception |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **VII.1** | Gianni Lotteringhi & Wife | The wife uses a "ghost story" and signals to hide her lover from her simple-minded husband. |
| **VII.2** | Peronella & Stratico | Peronella hides her lover in a tub and convinces her husband the lover is a buyer inspecting it. |
| **VII.3** | Fra Rinaldo & Godmother | A friar pretends to perform a religious "cure" on a child to provide cover for an affair. |
| **VII.4** | Tofano & Monna Ghita | After being locked out, the wife fakes a suicide in a well to trick her husband into coming outside so she can lock *him* out. |
| **VII.5** | The Jealous Husband | A husband disguises himself as a priest to hear his wife's confession; she recognizes him and feeds him a false story. |
| **VII.6** | Isabella & Leonetto | Caught with two admirers, Isabella hides one under a chicken coop and uses her husband to "rescue" the other. |
| **VII.7** | Lodovico & Beatrice | Lodovico disguises himself as a servant to access Beatrice; a maid’s quick thinking saves them when discovered. |
| **VII.8** | The String Trick | A husband ties a string to his wife’s toe to monitor her; she switches the string to a maid and later shames him publicly. |
| **VII.9** | Lydia & Nicostrato | Lydia performs three escalating tests of deception, culminating in making her husband doubt his own eyesight. |
| **VII.10**| Tingoccio & Meuccio | A ghost returns from the afterlife to report that adultery with a *comare* (godmother) is not punished by God. |

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

*   **Intelligence and Agency:** Analyze the role of *ingegno* in Day VII. Is the cleverness of the wives a form of genuine empowerment, or is it merely a necessary survival tactic within a society that offers them no other power? Use specific examples from Peronella (VII.2) or Lydia (VII.9) to support your argument.
*   **The Psychology of the Dupe:** Explore why the husbands in these tales are so easily deceived. To what extent is their gullibility a result of their own flaws (jealousy, vanity) versus a psychological need to maintain the appearance of domestic order?
*   **The Morality of the Beffa:** Boccaccio famously refuses to moralize about the adultery depicted in Day VII. Compare this approach to Dante’s *Inferno*. What does Boccaccio’s neutral stance suggest about his view of human nature and social reality?
*   **Narrative Design and Self-Deception:** In Tale VII.9, Lydia’s tricks become increasingly outrageous. Discuss how Boccaccio uses this escalating structure to explore the human capacity for self-deception. Why is the most unbelievable test saved for last?

## Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Beffa:** An elaborate trick, practical joke, or deception used as a primary engine of comedy in the *Decameron*.
*   **Beffatore:** The trickster or person who carries out the *beffa*.
*   **Beffato:** The dupe or victim of the trick.
*   **Comare:** Literally "co-mother" or godmother. Sexual relations with a *comare* were considered "spiritual incest" by the medieval Church.
*   **Confraternita:** A lay religious society dedicated to prayer and charity. In Day VII, Boccaccio often uses membership in these as a marker of a character's foolish credulity.
*   **Fabliaux:** Short, humorous, often bawdy medieval French verse tales that served as a precursor to Boccaccio’s style.
*   **Ingegno:** Intelligence, wit, and resourcefulness; the essential quality required to successfully execute a *beffa*.
*   **Motto:** A witty remark or clever verbal retort; alongside the *beffa*, it is one of the two great engines of Boccaccian comedy.
