# Study Guide: Boccaccio’s Decameron, Proem and Introduction

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the opening sections of Giovanni Boccaccio’s *Decameron*. It covers the historical context of the Black Death in Florence, the moral and social breakdown caused by the pandemic, and the formation of the *brigata*—the group of ten young people who escape the city to seek order through storytelling.

---

## I. Key Concepts and Themes

### The Historical and Clinical Context of the Plague
The *Decameron* opens in 1348 during the arrival of the Black Death in Florence. Boccaccio’s account is noted for its blend of clinical observation and moral reflection. 
*   **Origin and Spread:** The pestilence began in the East and moved westward. In Florence, it persisted despite city-wide cleaning, the exclusion of the sick, and public religious supplications.
*   **Physical Symptoms:** The disease manifested as *gavoccioli* (tumors in the groin or armpits ranging from the size of an egg to an apple). Following these, black or livid spots appeared on the limbs. Death usually occurred within three days of the appearance of symptoms.
*   **Contagion:** The disease was highly virulent, spreading not just through speech or association but through contact with the clothes or belongings of the sick. Boccaccio recounts witnessing two hogs die almost instantly after tossing the rags of a deceased man in their mouths.

### Social and Moral Breakdown
The plague resulted in the total dissolution of both human and divine laws, as the ministers of law and religion were either dead or incapacitated. Boccaccio identifies four primary responses to the chaos:
1.  **The Temperate:** Those who lived secluded lives, eating delicate foods and drinking fine wines in moderation while avoiding all news of death.
2.  **The Hedonistic:** Those who believed the "sovereign remedy" was to drink freely, satisfy every appetite, and mock the disaster. They frequently occupied the abandoned homes of others.
3.  **The Middle Course:** Those who did not seclude themselves but walked abroad carrying fragrant herbs and spices to "comfort the brain" against the stench of death.
4.  **The Fugitives:** Those who believed no medicine was superior to flight, abandoning their homes, kin, and city entirely.

The ultimate tragedy described is the "dereliction of the sick," where the bonds of family and friendship were severed to the point that parents abandoned their own children.

### The Moral Foundation of the Brigata
The *brigata* (the company of ten) represents a creative response to social chaos. By retreating to the countryside, they do not merely seek to survive, but to restore order, dignity, and "sprightly" social interaction through structured living and storytelling.

## II. The Brigata and Their Household

The company consists of seven women and three men, all of noble birth and gentle manners, aged between 18 and 28.

### Members of the Company
| Name | Role/Identity |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Pampinea** | The eldest lady (28); elected Queen for the first day; initiator of the plan to leave Florence. |
| **Fiammetta** | The second lady; identified in notes as Maria, daughter of Robert, King of Naples. |
| **Filomena** | The third lady; noted for her excellent judgment and for suggesting the need for male guidance. |
| **Emilia** | The fourth lady. |
| **Lauretta** | The fifth lady. |
| **Neifile** | The sixth lady; initially hesitant to include the men due to potential scandal. |
| **Elisa** | The seventh and youngest lady. |
| **Pamfilo** | One of the three young men (aged 25+); described as debonair and chivalrous. |
| **Filostrato** | One of the three young men. |
| **Dioneo** | One of the three young men; noted for his unmatched courtesy and wit. |

### Servant Assignments
To maintain order at the country estate, Pampinea assigned specific roles to the group's servants:
*   **Parmeno (Dioneo's man):** Seneschal; in charge of the household and hall service.
*   **Sirisco (Pamfilo's man):** Treasurer and chancellor of the exchequer.
*   **Tindaro (Filostrato's man):** In charge of the private chambers of the three men.
*   **Misia & Licisca:** Kitchen maids.
*   **Chimera & Stratilia:** Ladies' chambermaids; responsible for cleanliness.

## III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **To whom does Boccaccio dedicate his work in the Proem?**
    *   *Answer:* He dedicates the work to "gentle ladies" who are in love, as he believes they have fewer diversions than men to help them cope with the "melancholy bred of amorous desire."
2.  **In what year did the plague reach Florence?**
    *   *Answer:* 1348.
3.  **What was the specific name for the tumors that appeared in the groin or armpits?**
    *   *Answer:* Gavoccioli.
4.  **Why did the brigata decide they needed to include men in their company?**
    *   *Answer:* Filomena and Elisa argued that women are "sensitive, perverse, suspicious, pusillanimous and timid," and seldom reach a "commendable conclusion" without the guidance and governance of men.
5.  **Where did the seven ladies first meet to discuss their plight?**
    *   *Answer:* The venerable church of Santa Maria Novella.
6.  **Who are the "becchini"?**
    *   *Answer:* Grave-diggers or corpse-carriers drawn from the "baser ranks" who performed burials for hire and often mocked the miseries of the citizens.
7.  **What is the "visible sign" of sovereignty worn by the ruler of the day?**
    *   *Answer:* A wreath made of bay-tree leaves.
8.  **According to the text, how many people are believed to have died in Florence between March and July?**
    *   *Answer:* Upwards of 100,000.

## IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Breakdown of Social Contract:** Analyze Boccaccio's description of how the plague affected domestic and civic duties. How does the abandonment of children by parents and the disuse of funeral rites signify a deeper collapse of Florentine society?
2.  **Gender and Solace:** Discuss Boccaccio's argument in the Proem regarding why women require the "solace" of his stories more than men. How does this perspective inform the structure and tone of the *Decameron*?
3.  **Nature vs. Reason:** Pampinea argues that "to fortify, preserve, and defend his life... is the dictate of natural reason." Explore how the *brigata* uses reason to distinguish themselves from those who either fled in "inhuman" neglect or stayed in "dissolute" hedonism.
4.  **The Symbolism of the Countryside:** Contrast Boccaccio's description of the "desolate walls" of Florence with the "verdant hills" and "ordered palace" of the retreat. How does the physical environment of the country estate reflect the moral goals of the *brigata*?

## V. Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Albeit:** Although; even though.
*   **Becchini:** A class of porters from the lower ranks who carried the dead to graves for hire.
*   **Canonical Hours:** The fixed times of daily prayer observed in monasteries (e.g., Matins, Vespers).
*   **Canzonets:** Short, light songs; some are sung by the ladies for their delectation.
*   **Courtesy:** In medieval usage, the refined, noble behavior expected of the upper class.
*   **Discourse:** Formal conversation or discussion on a subject.
*   **Fortune:** Fate or chance, often personified as the goddess Fortuna.
*   **Gavoccioli:** The physical tumors associated with the initial stages of the plague.
*   **Indulgence:** A remission of punishment for sin granted by the Church.
*   **Novella:** A short prose tale; the literary form Boccaccio perfected.
*   **Pestilence:** The plague, specifically the Black Death of 1348.
*   **Prudence:** One of the four cardinal virtues; the ability to judge wisely and act accordingly.
*   **Requital:** A return or repayment, whether of a favor or an injury.
*   **Signory:** Lordship or sovereignty; the authority held by the elected leader of the *brigata* for a single day.
*   **Tierce:** The third of the canonical hours, usually around 9:00 AM.
*   **Wont:** Accustomed; a habit or custom.
