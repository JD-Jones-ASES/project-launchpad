Day the Third: Cleverness Triumphant – A Study Guide for Boccaccio’s Decameron

This study guide examines the themes, characters, and narrative structures of Day III of Giovanni Boccaccio’s Decameron. It focuses on the transition from the influence of fortune to the triumph of human intelligence and explores the moral complexities inherent in the pursuit of desire.

Core Themes and Key Concepts

The Transition from Fortuna to Ingegno

Boccaccio structures the Decameron so that each day develops and complicates the themes of the previous one. In Day II, characters are "buffeted by fortuna" (fortune), often at the mercy of external circumstances. In Day III, the central theme shifts to ingegno—intelligence, wit, and resourcefulness. The characters of Day III do not wait for luck to change; they actively reshape their circumstances through the power of their minds.

Ingegno: The Supreme Virtue

In the world of the Decameron, ingegno is valued above birth, wealth, physical strength, and moral virtue. It is the quality that most reliably leads to success. This emphasis reflects a distinctly modern and Florentine value system rooted in a commercial city where wit and enterprise often outweighed noble blood.

Female Agency and Social Hierarchy

Day III is notable for its prominent female protagonists who act with determination. Unlike the passive women often found in medieval literary conventions, Boccaccio’s female characters are frequently the "architects of their own stories."

  • Gillette of Narbonne (III.9): A primary example of a woman using intelligence to succeed within a restrictive system. She does not rebel against the social hierarchy or the system of arranged marriage; instead, she masters the rules to turn them to her advantage.
  • The Wife of Bath Connection: Gillette finds a parallel in Geoffrey Chaucer’s Wife of Bath. Both women insist on their right to act and choose, representing some of the most compelling female voices in 14th-century European literature.

Moral Ambiguity and Satire

Boccaccio uses cleverness to explore various moral landscapes:

  • Anticlerical Satire: Several tales (III.3, III.4, III.8, III.10) target institutional hypocrisy, depicting members of the clergy as either gullible dupes or predatory tricksters.
  • The Limits of Cleverness: The text raises questions about whether ingegno is an unqualified good. It asks readers to consider if cleverness is admirable when used for deception or to serve desire rather than justice.

Summary of Tales: Day III

The following table summarizes the tales of Day III, highlighting how each protagonist employs ingegno.

Novel Primary Characters Narrative Summary
III.1 Masetto and the Nuns Masetto pretends to be deaf and mute to gain work at a convent. His deception allows him to fulfill the secret desires of the nuns, resulting in prosperity for all involved.
III.2 A Clever Groom and King Agilulf A horse-servant (groom) disguises himself as the King to visit the Queen at night. When discovered, his quick thinking in a dark dormitory saves his life.
III.3 A Lady, a Friar, and a Lover A married woman uses a naive friar as an unwitting messenger to communicate with her lover, convincing the friar he is helping her resist temptation.
III.4 Dom Felice and Fra Puccio A monk tricks the devout Fra Puccio into performing a grueling, stationary penance in the garden so the monk can visit Puccio’s wife inside.
III.5 Zima and Francesco’s Wife Zima is granted permission to speak to a woman on the condition she remains silent. His rhetoric is so persuasive that she is moved to respond, using words as an instrument of seduction.
III.6 Ricciardo and Catella Ricciardo tricks the faithful Catella into believing her husband is unfaithful, leading her to a darkened bathhouse where she mistakes Ricciardo for her husband.
III.7 Tedaldo and Ermellina Tedaldo, believed to be dead, returns to Florence disguised as a friar. He uses this deception to reclaim his lover and save an innocent man from execution.
III.8 Ferondo’s Purgatory An abbot drugs Ferondo into a death-like sleep and convinces him he is in Purgatory. While Ferondo is imprisoned, the abbot pursues Ferondo's wife.
III.9 Gillette of Narbonne Gillette uses the "bed trick"—deceiving her husband Beltramo into consummating their marriage without his knowledge—to fulfill the impossible conditions he set for her.
III.10 Alibech and Rustico A young woman seeking religious instruction is taught by the hermit Rustico to "put the Devil in Hell," a sustained sexual metaphor and satire of religious language.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does the role of "Fortuna" differ between Day II and Day III of the Decameron?
  2. What specific social class or setting does the concept of ingegno reflect, according to the text?
  3. In Novel III.1, why does the text suggest the nuns are not portrayed as victims?
  4. What is the "bed trick," and which two characters in Day III are associated with its use?
  5. How does Gillette of Narbonne demonstrate a "pure form" of ingegno in her dealings with the King and her husband?
  6. In Novel III.2, what is the specific meaning of the word "groom"?
  7. What is the primary instrument of ingegno used by Zima in Novel III.5?
  8. How does Boccaccio use the concept of "Purgatory" in Novel III.8?
  9. According to the text, what is the central challenge for students when analyzing the tales of Day III?
  10. In what way is the Wife of Bath described as being different from Gillette of Narbonne?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Ethics of Intelligence: Boccaccio presents ingegno as a supreme virtue, yet many characters use it to deceive, seduce, or manipulate. Compare the use of cleverness by Masetto (III.1) and Ricciardo (III.6). Is one more "moral" than the other, or does Boccaccio suggest that success is its own justification?
  2. Gender and Power: Analyze the role of women in Day III. How do characters like Gillette of Narbonne or the lady in Novel III.3 navigate the constraints of a patriarchal medieval society? Do these stories empower women, or do they merely show women participating in a corrupt system?
  3. Satire and the Clergy: Discuss Boccaccio's treatment of religious figures in Day III. Using examples from Novels III.3, III.4, III.8, or III.10, explain how Boccaccio uses humor and metaphor to critique the gap between religious vows and human nature.
  4. Mastering the System: Gillette of Narbonne succeeds not by breaking rules, but by understanding them better than those in power. Discuss the effectiveness of this strategy compared to more overt forms of rebellion. Use the comparison to Chaucer’s Wife of Bath to support your argument.

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Beffa: A trick or practical joke, often involving a complex deception (noted in the context of Novel III.8).
  • Bed Trick: A literary device where one person is substituted for another in a bed, typically in the dark, so that a character unknowingly has sexual relations with someone other than their intended partner.
  • Fortuna: The personification of luck or fate; the primary driving force of the tales in Day II of the Decameron.
  • Groom: In the medieval context of the Decameron, a servant who tends to horses, rather than a man about to be married.
  • Ingegno: An Italian term meaning intelligence, wit, resourcefulness, or ingenuity. It is the defining human quality celebrated in Day III.
  • Purgatory: In medieval Catholic theology, a state after death where souls are purified before entering Heaven. Boccaccio uses it as a comedic device in Novel III.8.
  • "Putting the Devil in Hell": Boccaccio’s most famous sexual metaphor, used in Novel III.10 to satirize religious language through sustained double entendre.
