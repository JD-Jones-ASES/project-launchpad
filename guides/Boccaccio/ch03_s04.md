# A Study Guide to The Decameron, Day II, Tale 7: The Adventures of Alatiel

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the seventh tale of the second day of Boccaccio’s *Decameron*. Narrated by Panfilo, this story is a sprawling epic of "Fortune’s Reversals," following the journey of Alatiel, the daughter of the Sultan of Babylon, as she is cast across the Mediterranean by the whims of fate.

---

## Core Themes and Narrative Analysis

### 1. The Power of Fortuna
The central theme of the tale is *fortuna* (fortune) at its most extreme. Alatiel possesses no control over her destiny; she is a passive figure to whom things happen rather than a protagonist who drives the plot. Despite being subjected to a series of kidnappings, murders, and shipwrecks, fortune eventually restores her to her original intended destination, illustrating the unpredictable and often circular nature of fate.

### 2. The Burden of Beauty
Alatiel’s extraordinary beauty is presented as a "poisoned chalice." While her appearance is her most notable trait, it is also the primary cause of her misfortunes, inciting lust, betrayal, and violence in every man she encounters. The narrator, Panfilo, uses her story as a cautionary example for women who are "unduly solicitous of beauty," suggesting that physical advantages often entail a "dolorous life."

### 3. Satire and Reputation
The tale concludes with a sharp narrative irony. After living with nine different men over the course of four years, Alatiel is restored to her father and successfully presented to her future husband as a virgin. Boccaccio explores the gap between official reputation and actual experience, suggesting that "truth" is often a construct of clever storytelling rather than historical fact.

### 4. Agency and Silence
A significant portion of Alatiel's journey is defined by her inability to communicate. Because she does not speak the languages of her captors, she is often treated as a beautiful object. It is only when she meets Antigono—someone who speaks her language and can help her construct a narrative—that she regains her social standing.

## Key Figures and Alatiel’s Journey

The following table outlines the sequence of men into whose hands Alatiel falls and the locations associated with her travels.

| Order | Figure | Location/Context | Fate of the Relationship |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1** | **Beminedab** | Babylon (Egypt) | Her father; sends her to marry the King of Algarve. |
| **2** | **Pericone da Visalgo** | Majorca | A lord who rescues her from a shipwreck and seduces her with wine. |
| **3** | **Marato** | Majorca/At sea | Pericone’s brother; murders Pericone and abducts Alatiel. |
| **4** | **Two Genoese Masters** | At sea | Ship captains who throw Marato overboard; one kills the other in a duel. |
| **5** | **Prince of the Morea** | Klarenza | Takes Alatiel after the wounded Genoese captain is captured. |
| **6** | **Duke of Athens** | Athens (Villa) | Murders the Prince and his chamberlain to claim Alatiel. |
| **7** | **Constantine** | Chios | Son of the Emperor; abducts Alatiel from the Duke’s villa. |
| **8** | **Osbech, King of Turks** | Smyrna | Captures Alatiel during a raid on Chios and marries her. |
| **9** | **Antioco** | Smyrna/Rhodes | Osbech’s servant; flees with Alatiel after Osbech’s death in battle. |
| **10** | **Cypriote Merchant** | Cyprus (Baffa) | Antioco’s friend; takes charge of Alatiel after Antioco dies of illness. |
| **11** | **Antigono** | Famagosta | A former servant of the Sultan who recognizes her and helps her return home. |

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. Who is the narrator of this tale, and what is the stated purpose of his story?**
*Answer:* The narrator is Panfilo. His purpose is to demonstrate that human desires—specifically for beauty and riches—are subject to the "chances and changes of fortune" and can lead to ruin rather than happiness.

**2. What does "Babylon" refer to in the context of this 14th-century narrative?**
*Answer:* In medieval usage, "Babylon" refers to Cairo or Egypt generally, not the ancient city in Mesopotamia.

**3. How does Pericone da Visalgo eventually overcome Alatiel's resistance to his advances?**
*Answer:* He uses wine ("enlisting Bacchus in the service of Venus"). Since Alatiel's law forbade wine, she found it palatable and drank until she lost her modesty and inhibitions.

**4. What happens to Marato after he abducts Alatiel?**
*Answer:* He is thrown into the sea by the two Genoese shipmasters who also desire Alatiel.

**5. Why does the Duke of Athens decide to murder the Prince of Morea?**
*Answer:* He is "smitten with a desire" and "poisoned" by love after seeing Alatiel’s beauty; he chooses to cast aside honor and justice to possess her.

**6. How does Alatiel’s lack of language skills affect her experience throughout the tale?**
*Answer:* It renders her passive and isolated, forcing her to rely on gestures and preventing her from revealing her true identity until she meets Antigono in Cyprus.

**7. Who is Antigono, and what role does he play in the resolution of the story?**
*Answer:* Antigono is a man of "ripe wisdom" who previously served the Sultan. He recognizes Alatiel in Baffa and devises the cover story that allows her to return to her father with her reputation intact.

**8. What is the "pious" lie Alatiel tells her father regarding her four-year absence?**
*Answer:* She claims she was rescued from the shipwreck by four men and taken to a convent where she lived a devout life among religious ladies, eventually being escorted home by French pilgrims.

**9. What is the significance of "St. Crescent-in-Hollow" (or "St. Crescent-in-hand") in the tale?**
*Answer:* These are ironic or euphemistic references used by the characters/narrator to refer to sexual encounters or physical anatomy within the context of the story's amorous adventures.

**10. How many men has Alatiel been with by the time she marries the King of Algarve?**
*Answer:* She has been with eight men in total.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. **The Nature of Passivity:** Analyze Alatiel's character. Is her passivity a sign of weakness, or is it a calculated survival strategy? Discuss how Boccaccio uses her lack of agency to highlight the overwhelming power of *Fortuna*.

2. **The Irony of the "Virgin" Bride:** The tale ends with Alatiel marrying the King of Algarve as a "virgin" despite her history. Explore what this conclusion suggests about the medieval concepts of honor, truth, and the value placed on a woman's reputation versus her actual experiences.

3. **Geography and Politics:** The tale spans the Mediterranean, from Egypt to Portugal, Greece, and Turkey. How does the setting of a "dynastic alliance within the Islamic world" (the marriage to the King of Algarve) frame the story? How do the various Christian and Muslim territories reflect the "expansive" world Boccaccio describes?

4. **Narrative Satire:** Examine the narrator's tone in the final sentence: *"Mouth, for kisses, was never the worse: like as the moon reneweth her course."* How does this proverb encapsulate the story’s satirical take on morality and the physical nature of beauty?

## Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition from Context |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Algarve** | The southernmost region of modern Portugal; in the 14th century, it was a Muslim-ruled territory. |
| **Consort** | A companion or spouse; also, the act of keeping company with another. |
| **Entreat** | To ask earnestly; to beg or implore someone for a favor or mercy. |
| **Fortuna** | Fortune; the personified force of luck and fate that dictates the reversals of human life. |
| **Klarenza** | A location in the Morea (modern-day Peloponnese, Greece) where the Prince resides. |
| **Morea** | The medieval name for the Peloponnese peninsula in Greece. |
| **Soldan** | A Sultan; specifically Beminedab, the Sultan of Babylon/Egypt. |
| **Wroth** | Angry; furious; full of intense wrath. |
