# Day Six of the Decameron: Wit, Eloquence, and the Art of the Retort

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the introductory sections and thematic foundations of Day Six of Giovanni Boccaccio’s *Decameron*. It examines the shift in tone from previous days, the specific linguistic themes decreed by the queen of the day, and the narrative disruptions that precede the formal storytelling.

---

### Key Concepts and Themes

#### 1. The Theme of the "Motto"
The theme for Day Six, as decreed by Queen Elissa, focuses on individuals who have "by some apt retort avoided loss, danger, or scorn." The Italian term for this witty or pointed remark is *motto*. Unlike earlier days that prioritized physical adventure or the whims of fortune, Day Six celebrates verbal dexterity and the ability to use language as a defensive or offensive tool.

#### 2. *Eloquenza* (Eloquence)
In the Italian literary tradition, *eloquenza* refers to the art of speaking well. For Boccaccio, this is not merely a decorative skill but a visible form of intelligence. Day Six is the shortest of the ten days, emphasizing precision and the power of a perfectly timed remark over lengthy narration.

#### 3. Meditation on the Craft of Storytelling
Day Six serves as a thematic center for the *Decameron*. Through the stories told—specifically the first tale involving Madonna Oretta—Boccaccio reflects on the craft of narration itself. This day acts as a commentary on what constitutes a "good" story versus one that is ruined by poor delivery.

#### 4. The Character of Elissa
Elissa, the queen of Day Six, is also known by the name Dido, the legendary queen of Carthage. Within the *brigata*, she is characterized as reserved and thoughtful. Her choice of theme reflects a preference for intellectual and verbal skill rather than the emotional intensity or physical action seen in previous days.

### The Events of Day Six: Morning and Introduction

The morning of Day Six follows the established routine of the *brigata*, yet it is interrupted by a rare moment of domestic friction among the servants.

| Activity | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Early Morning** | The company rises at dawn and saunters across the dewy meadow, conversing about the beauty and incidents of previous stories. |
| **Mid-Morning** | As the sun rises, they return to the palace, which has been prepared with fragrant herbs and flowers for breakfast. |
| **Leisure Time** | After eating, the company sings "dainty and jocund ditties," followed by sleep, chess, or dice. Dioneo and Lauretta sing of Troilus and Cressida. |
| **The Disturbance** | Before the storytelling begins, an uproar breaks out in the kitchen between the servants Licisca and Tindaro. |

#### The Dispute: Licisca vs. Tindaro
The conflict between the two servants serves as a prelude to the day’s focus on speech.
*   **The Issue:** Tindaro argues that women are simple and that their first sexual encounters are always by force and involve "effusion of blood." 
*   **Licisca’s Argument:** Licisca, described as "advanced in years" and "disposed to give herself airs," vehemently disagrees. She asserts that women are not "numskulls" who wait for their fathers to marry them off. She claims that no woman in her neighborhood was a virgin at the time of marriage and that wives frequently play tricks on their husbands.
*   **Resolution:** The queen asks Dioneo to judge the matter. Dioneo immediately sides with Licisca, declaring Tindaro a fool.

### Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **Who is the queen of Day Six, and what does her name signify?**
    *   *Answer:* The queen is Elissa, another name for Dido, the legendary queen of Carthage.

2.  **How does the length of Day Six compare to the other days in the *Decameron*?**
    *   *Answer:* It is the shortest of the ten days, containing the briefest tales.

3.  **What is the specific Italian term used to describe the witty retorts featured in the day’s stories?**
    *   *Answer:* *Motto.*

4.  **According to the text, why does Day Six stand at the center of the work both structurally and thematically?**
    *   *Answer:* Structurally, it is the sixth of ten days. Thematically, it is Boccaccio's meditation on the art and craft of storytelling itself.

5.  **Which two members of the *brigata* sing about Troilus and Cressida while the others sleep or play games?**
    *   *Answer:* Dioneo and Lauretta.

6.  **What specific threat does the queen use to finally silence Licisca during her argument?**
    *   *Answer:* She threatens to have her whipped and sent back to the kitchen.

### Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Power of the Word:** Analyze how Day Six shifts the focus from external "Fortune" to internal "Intelligence" through the use of *eloquenza*. How does the ability to speak well function as a form of power within the social hierarchy described in the text?

2.  **Licisca’s Subversion:** Examine the argument between Licisca and Tindaro. In what ways does Licisca’s "ready speech" and cynical view of marriage and virginity challenge the romanticized or idealized versions of love presented in earlier days of the *Decameron*?

3.  **Meta-Narrative and Craft:** Discuss the significance of Madonna Oretta’s story (as mentioned in the introduction) as a statement on the art of narration. Why would Boccaccio choose the midpoint of his work to discuss what "ruins" a story?

### Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Brigata:** The company of storytellers (seven women and three men) who have fled the plague.
*   **Eloquenza:** The art of speaking well; intelligence made visible through language.
*   **Motto:** A witty, pointed remark or apt retort delivered at the exactly right moment to avoid a negative outcome.
*   **Seneschal:** An official in a medieval or Renaissance household in charge of domestic arrangements and the supervision of servants.
*   **Varlet:** A servant or attendant; often used contemptuously to mean a rogue or knave.
*   **Vonted Order:** The usual or customary order in which the *brigata* took their places by the fountain.
