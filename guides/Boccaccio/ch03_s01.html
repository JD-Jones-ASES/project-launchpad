Study Guide: Fortune’s Reversals in Boccaccio’s Decameron (Day II)

This study guide explores the second day of Boccaccio’s Decameron, focusing on the theme of Fortuna (Fortune) and the various ways human intelligence and passivity interact with the unpredictable turns of fate.

Core Philosophical Concepts

Fortuna (Fortune)

In the medieval worldview, Fortuna was a quasi-divine, personified goddess who was capricious, irresistible, and morally indifferent. The concept, inherited from the classical tradition and thinkers like Boethius and Dante, describes a force that governs the rise and fall of human affairs through shipwrecks, unexpected encounters, and sudden reversals.

  • The Wheel of Fortune: Derived from Boethius’s Consolation of Philosophy, this image depicts mortals rising to prosperity only to be dashed down again. It serves as the primary organizing metaphor for all the tales in Day II.
  • Narrative Function: In the Decameron, Fortune acts as the "great plot-maker," creating the high-stakes situations that test a character's nature.

Ingegno (Intelligence/Wit)

While Day I focused on the power of speech, Day II introduces ingegno—resourcefulness and native wit—as the counter-force to Fortune.

  • Human Agency: Ingegno is the ability to think quickly, adapt to shifting circumstances, and turn misfortune into an advantage.
  • The Heroic Ideal: The heroes of Day II are not those who avoid Fortune’s wheel, but those who possess the intelligence to "ride it."

Analysis of Key Tales

Andreuccio da Perugia (II.5)

This tale is a prototype of the picaresque genre, following a protagonist through a dangerous, episodic journey across the underside of society. Set in Naples—a city of darkness and hidden opportunity—the story tracks Andreuccio’s transformation from a "naive provincial" to a "resourceful survivor."

The Three Stages of Transformation:

  1. The Trap: Andreuccio is lured and robbed by a prostitute claiming to be his sister. He loses his money but learns his first lesson in worldly duplicity.
  2. The Well: After falling into a well and being hauled out by watchmen, he begins to adapt and joins them in a more dangerous scheme.
  3. The Tomb: While robbing the Archbishop of Naples’ tomb, Andreuccio’s companions attempt to trap him inside. He uses their own trickery to escape with a precious ruby ring, successfully turning a life-threatening situation into a gain.

Alatiel (II.7)

In contrast to Andreuccio, Alatiel represents total passivity. A beautiful royal woman, she is passed between various men by force of circumstance and desire without ever exercising agency or speaking.

  • Satire and Irony: The tale concludes with Alatiel being returned to her father and married off "as a virgin." The closing proverb—"kissing-lips lose no flavor, but renew themselves like the moon"—suggests that in her social world, appearances alone determine reality.
  • Social Commentary: Boccaccio may use Alatiel’s extreme passivity to expose a system that reduces women to objects of exchange, valued only for beauty and perceived virginity.

Summary of Other Day II Tales

Tale Protagonist Summary of Fortune’s Reversal
II.1 Martellino A jester feigns a miracle but is nearly hanged when caught; he is saved by a second deception.
II.2 Rinaldo d'Asti A merchant is robbed and left in the cold, only to find unexpected shelter and comfort with a widow.
II.3 Three Brothers Noble brothers lose their inheritance but are restored to wealth after discovering a king's daughter in disguise.
II.4 Landolfo Rufolo A merchant turns to piracy, is shipwrecked twice, but finds a chest of gems that makes him the richest man in his region.
II.6 Madam Beritola A noblewoman loses her family during the Sicilian Vespers but is eventually reunited with her sons after years of exile.
II.8 Count of Antwerp A count is falsely accused and exiled, living as a servant for years before his innocence is proven and his status restored.
II.9 Bernabò of Genoa A husband is tricked into believing his wife, Zinevra, was unfaithful; she disguises herself as a man to eventually expose the liar.
II.10 Ricciardo An elderly judge loses his wife to a pirate; she refuses to return, preferring the pirate to her controlling husband.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What are the two simultaneous meanings of the Italian word fortuna?
  2. Which philosopher’s work is the source of the "Wheel of Fortune" metaphor?
  3. How does the character of Andreuccio change from the beginning of his night in Naples to the end?
  4. In tale II.9, how does Zinevra protect herself after her husband orders her death?
  5. What is the "crude and philosophically suggestive" proverb that concludes Alatiel’s tale?
  6. How does Naples function as more than just a setting in the tale of Andreuccio?
  7. What historical event serves as the backdrop for Madam Beritola’s story (II.6)?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. Agency vs. Passivity: Compare and contrast the journeys of Andreuccio (II.5) and Alatiel (II.7). How does Boccaccio use their differing responses to Fortune to define what makes a story "worth telling"?
  2. The Morality of Fortune: The text suggests that Fortune is "morally indifferent," rewarding and punishing without regard for a character's virtue. Using examples from Day II (such as Landolfo Rufolo or Martellino), argue whether Boccaccio’s world is entirely chaotic or if a subtle moral order exists.
  3. The Picaresque Hero: Define the picaresque genre based on the provided text. To what extent does Andreuccio fit the definition of a picaro, and how does his experience in the "underside of society" reflect the themes of the Decameron?
  4. Social Satire in Alatiel's Tale: Analyze the conclusion of tale II.7. Does Boccaccio intend for the reader to find the ending humorous, or is he using irony to critique a society that values "appearances" over "social reality"?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Boethius (c. 480–524): A Roman senator and philosopher whose Consolation of Philosophy introduced the metaphor of Fortune's wheel to medieval thought.
  • Fortuna: A Latin and Italian term meaning both "wealth" (fortune) and "fate" (chance); personified as a capricious goddess.
  • Ingegno: Intelligence, resourcefulness, or native wit; the supreme human quality required to adapt to Fortune.
  • Picaresque: A genre of fiction featuring a resourceful protagonist (a picaro) of low birth who lives by their wits while moving through a corrupt or dangerous world.
  • Sicilian Vespers (1282): A historical rebellion in Sicily against French Angevin rule, resulting in a massacre; used as a backdrop for tale II.6.
  • The Angevin Court: The royal court in Naples where Boccaccio lived from approximately 1327 to 1340, providing him with the intimate knowledge of the city displayed in his writing.
