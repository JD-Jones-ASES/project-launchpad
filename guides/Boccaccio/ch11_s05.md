# Study Guide: Boccaccio’s Tale of Messer Torello and Saladin (Decameron, X.9)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the ninth tale told on the tenth day of Boccaccio’s *Decameron*. Narrated by Panfilo, the king of the Day, this story represents the pinnacle of the day's theme: magnanimity and virtue. It serves as a profound meditation on the ideal of *cortesia*—refined, generous behavior—and its power to bridge the divides of religion, culture, and conflict.

---

## Key Concepts and Narrative Themes

### 1. The Ideal of *Cortesia* (Courtesy)
*Cortesia* is the central pillar of the narrative. It refers to the refined and generous behavior expected of the noble classes. In Boccaccio’s view, this quality is not restricted by geography or faith. Both the Christian knight, Messer Torello, and the Muslim Sultan, Saladin, embody this ideal. Torello’s hospitality is described as "lavish, spontaneous, and entirely without calculation," while Saladin’s eventual repayment is characterized by "royal magnificence."

### 2. The "Noble Pagan"
The portrayal of Saladin (Salah ad-Din) reflects a common trope in Western medieval literature where he is depicted as a figure of extraordinary chivalry. Despite being the Kurdish Muslim sultan who recaptured Jerusalem from the Crusaders, Boccaccio presents him as a moral equal—and at times a superior—to his Christian counterparts. This highlights a theme of cross-cultural respect, suggesting that true nobility transcends religious boundaries.

### 3. Friendship and Reciprocity
The story is structured in two parallel movements of hospitality:
*   **The First Movement:** Torello hosts Saladin (disguised as a merchant) in Pavia with unmatched grace, using a ruse to ensure the strangers accept his hospitality.
*   **The Second Movement:** Years later, Saladin recognizes a captured Torello in Alexandria. He repays the debt of honor by treating Torello not as a prisoner, but as an equal lord, eventually facilitating his magical return home.

### 4. The Supernatural Element
While the *Decameron* is generally grounded in realism, this tale utilizes a rare supernatural device: the magical transport of Torello on a flying bed. Borrowed from the romance tradition, this element underscores the extraordinary nature of Saladin’s power and his commitment to helping his friend.

### 5. Fidelity and Time
The plot hinges on a specific timeframe. Upon departing for the Crusades, Torello asks his wife to wait "a year and a month and a day" before remarrying if he does not return. This creates the narrative tension that necessitates the magical intervention, as Torello arrives home on the very day his wife is set to wed another.

## Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Cortesia** | The refined, generous, and noble behavior expected of the upper classes; the central theme of the tale. |
| **Christendom** | The collective community of Christian nations in medieval Europe, often contrasted with the Islamic world. |
| **Soldan** | A medieval variant of "Sultan"; specifically refers to Saladin as the "Soldan of Babylonia" (Egypt) in the text. |
| **Importune** | To press or urge someone persistently or urgently; used to describe the pressure on Torello's wife to remarry. |
| **Pavia** | A city in Lombardy, northern Italy, known for its university and as the home of Messer Torello. |
| **Necromancer** | A practitioner of magic; in this tale, one is employed by Saladin to transport Torello across the world in a single night. |
| **Vair** | A type of expensive squirrel fur used to line the robes of the nobility. |
| **Tierce** | A canonical hour of the day, approximately 9:00 AM. |
| **Falconer** | A person who hunts with, breeds, or trains hawks; Torello's skill in this area leads to his recognition by Saladin. |

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **What is the stated purpose of Panfilo telling this story?**
    *   *Answer:* Panfilo intends to incite the listeners to "take delight in doing good offices" by showing an instance of Saladin’s magnificence, hoping they might one day receive a similar reward for their virtue.

2.  **How does Messer Torello first encounter Saladin, and what ruse does he use?**
    *   *Answer:* He meets Saladin and his companions (disguised as merchants) between Milan and Pavia. To ensure they stay at his estate, he tells them it is too late to enter the city and has a servant lead them to his home by "devious roads" so they cannot decline his invitation.

3.  **What specific gifts does Torello’s wife give to the "merchants" before they depart Pavia?**
    *   *Answer:* She gives each of them two pairs of robes (one lined with silk, the other with vair), three vests of taffeta, and linen clothes.

4.  **How does Saladin eventually recognize Torello in Alexandria?**
    *   *Answer:* Saladin notices the specific way Torello’s mouth shapes when he smiles, which he had observed in Pavia. He confirms his suspicion by showing Torello the robes his wife had gifted the Sultan years earlier.

5.  **What is the significance of the "year and a month and a day" deadline?**
    *   *Answer:* It is the period Torello requested his wife wait for him before remarrying. The story’s climax occurs because this time limit is about to expire, leading to the necessity of Torello’s magical return.

6.  **What treasures does Saladin place on the bed with the sleeping Torello?**
    *   *Answer:* He includes a sumptuous crown for Torello's wife, a ring with a brilliant carbuncle, a valuable sword, a pearl pendant, and two vast basins of gold full of pistoles.

7.  **How does Torello reveal his identity to his wife at the wedding feast?**
    *   *Answer:* He drops the ring she gave him upon his departure into a cup of wine she has sent to him. When she drinks the wine and finds the ring, she recognizes him.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Universality of Virtue:** Analyze how Boccaccio uses the characters of Torello and Saladin to argue that nobility is a human trait rather than a religious or national one. How does the "noble pagan" archetype challenge medieval audience expectations?

2.  **The Role of Reciprocity:** Discuss the theme of "reward for good offices" in the tale. Is Torello’s initial hospitality truly "without calculation," and how does the narrative justify the seemingly miraculous nature of his eventual reward?

3.  **Gender and Fidelity:** Examine the role of Torello’s wife (Adalieta). Though the story focuses on the bond between the two men, how does her character embody the virtues of *cortesia* and fidelity? Consider her initial gifts to the strangers and her resistance to remarriage.

4.  **Symbolism of the Supernatural:** The flying bed is a departure from Boccaccio's typical realism. Evaluate why this specific element is used in this tale. Does it heighten the sense of Saladin's "magnificence," or does it serve a more practical narrative function regarding the constraints of time and distance?
